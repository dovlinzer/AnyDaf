# Sanhedrin 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Solomon's Declining Power

### Reish Lakish Overview

The daf opens with a teaching from Reish Lakish tracing the gradual diminishment of King Solomon's authority. The tradition he cites follows an arc of progressive contraction, beginning at the heights of cosmic dominion and ending in near-total loss.

### Heavenly to Earthly Rule

Reish Lakish teaches: *batichila malach Shlomo al elyonim* — in the beginning, Solomon reigned even over the celestial sphere, over the angels and demonic forces, as it is written, *vayeshev Shlomo al kisei Hashem* — Solomon sat upon the throne of God. Yet ultimately, *u-levasof malach al tachtonim* — he came to reign only in the temporal, physical world. The verse cited is *ki hu rodeh bechol ever hanahar mi-Tifsach ve-ad Azah* — he ruled over the entire *ever hanahar*, from Tifsach to Azah.

Rav and Shmuel each expound upon this same point while arriving at it differently. One holds that Tifsach and Azah were at opposite ends of the world — *sofa olam* — so that ruling from one to the other means ruling over everything between. The other holds that Tifsach and Azah were situated adjacent to one another, *bahadei hadadi havu yatevei*, and that ruling over both therefore implies ruling over all that lies in every direction around them — analogous to the formulation *me-Hodu ve-ad Kush*. Either way, the conclusion is that Solomon had once ruled over the entire world.

### Israel to Jerusalem

From there the decline continues. *U-levasof malach ela al Yisrael* — ultimately he reigned only over Israel, as the verse says, *ani Kohelet hayiti melech al Yisrael*. The identification of Kohelet with Solomon is assumed throughout. And then further still: *u-levasof malach ela al Yerushalayim* — he reigned only over Jerusalem, as it is written, *Divrei Kohelet ben David melech bi-Yerushalayim*.

### Throne to Staff

The contraction does not stop at geography. *U-levasof malach ela al mitato* — in the end, all Solomon had dominion over was his own bed, as it is written, *hinei mitato sheli-Shlomo*. And at the very last stage: *u-levasof malach ela al maklo* — he held authority over nothing but his staff, derived from the verse *ze hayah chelki mikol amali* — this is all I had from all my toil. The connection between *ze* and a staff is that *ze* implies something one can physically point to or hold. Rav and Shmuel dispute what exactly this refers to: one says *maklo*, his staff; the other says *gundo*, his cloak. In either interpretation, the king had been reduced to virtually nothing. This episode is connected in a note to the story of *Ashmedai*, the demon king, who assumed Solomon's form and expelled him from his throne — a narrative recalled from tractate *Gittin*.

### Restoration Question

The Gemara then raises the question *hadar lo hadar* — did Solomon ultimately recover his former stature? Rav and Shmuel again divide: one says *hadar*, he was restored; the other says *lo hadar*, he was not. According to the view that he was not restored: *malach ve-hediyot* — he was a king who became a commoner. According to the view that he was restored: *malach ve-hediyot u-malach* — king, commoner, and king again.

## Royal Spoils & Treasure

### Treasure Distribution

The Gemara proceeds to discuss the king's portion of war booty. A *baraita* teaches: *otzrot melachim la-melech* — the treasure houses of conquered kings go to the victorious king. *Ve-shear biza shebozezin, mechetza la-melech u-mechetza la-am* — and the remainder of all spoils taken are split equally between the king and the people. Being the winning king, in short, is extremely lucrative.

### Abaye's Question

Abaye raises a challenge to Rav Dimi (or, in another version of the text, to Rav Acha): *bishlama otzrot melachim la-melech, orcha de-milta hi* — it is understandable that the conquered king's treasury goes to the victor, for that is simply the way of things. But from where do we derive that even the remaining general spoils are split with the king getting half? The source, the Gemara answers, comes from a verse: *vayimshchu l'Hashem le-nagid u-le-Tzadok la-kohen* — they anointed Solomon as ruler and Tzadok as *kohen*.

### Solomon-Tzadok Parallel

From this juxtaposition, a *hekkesh* is drawn: the king (*nagid*) is compared to Tzadok the *kohen*. *Makish nagid le-Tzadok* — just as Tzadok the *kohen* receives half of something, so too the king receives half.

### Priestly Share Source

The Gemara then asks: *ve-Tzadok gufei mena lan* — but from where do we know that the *kohen* gets half? The answer comes from a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi: when the Torah speaks of the *lechem ha-panim* and says it shall be *le-Aharon u-le-vanav* — for Aaron and his sons — this is not simply referring collectively to the *kohanim*. Rather, Aaron himself receives half and his sons collectively receive the other half. This principle is then extended more broadly.

A parallel is noted from *Matot*, where the spoils of the war against Midyan were divided between the fighters and the rest of the nation, with the fighters contributing one out of five hundred to Elazar and the people contributing one out of fifty — far from a half-and-half split. No king existed at the time; the role was filled by Yehoshua. The proportions there are quite different, and the Gemara does not draw on that precedent for the present *halacha*.

## Royal Wives Limit

### Eighteen Wives Rule

The *mishnah* states: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ela shemoneh esrei* — the king may not have more than eighteen wives.

Before addressing the *machloket* between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon, the broader *parashah* of *Shoftim* is examined, as it provides the scriptural grounding for the entire discussion. The relevant passage in Devarim 17:14 begins with the people anticipating their entry into *Eretz Yisrael* and saying *asim alai melech ke-chol ha-goyim asher svivotai* — let me appoint a king like all the nations around me. This phrasing is notable because it echoes the complaint lodged against the people's request for a king elsewhere — that they asked *ke-chol ha-goyim*, which was seen as the wrong framing. Yet the Torah itself uses this formulation here, which leaves the question of whether kingship is ideal or merely permitted as a genuine *machloket*.

The passage continues: *som tasim alecha melech* — you shall appoint a king over yourself — which the Gemara reads as requiring that the king's authority be felt, that one hold him in genuine awe. *Mikerev achecha tasim alecha melech* — he must be from among your brethren; *lo tuchal latet alecha ish nochri asher lo achecha hu* — a foreigner cannot serve as king, which is the root of the problem with Hordus, who was a slave. The sages of that era were criticized for flattering him — being *machnif* — when they should not have been.

The passage then lists restrictions: *rak lo yarbeh lo susim* — he may not accumulate too many horses — and this prohibition extends to bringing the people back to Egypt to acquire them. The verse adds: *ve-lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-lo yasur levavo* — he should not have too many wives so that his heart not be led astray — *ve-chesef ve-zahav lo yarbeh lo me'od* — and he should not accumulate excessive silver and gold. The verse provides a reason for the horses restriction (not returning to Egypt), and a reason for the wives restriction (*ve-lo yasur levavo*), but states the money restriction without any stated rationale — presumably because the reason is self-evident.

The verse that anchors the number eighteen is the passage from Shmuel 2, chapter 12, where the prophet Nathan, rebuking David over Batsheva, conveys God's words: *va-etna lecha et beit adonecha ve-et neshei adonecha be-cheikecha* — I gave you the house of your master and the wives of your master — and adds: *ve-im m'at ve-osifa lach kahena ve-kahena* — and if that was too little, I would have given you more, *kahena ve-kahena*, here and here. Since David at that point had six wives — born to him in Chevron as enumerated in Shmuel 2, chapter 3 — each *kahena* is taken to equal six. Six plus six plus six equals eighteen.

### Yehudah: Conditional

Rabbi Yehudah holds that the king *may* have more than eighteen wives, provided that they do not turn his heart astray: *marbe hu lo u-vilvad she-lo yehu mesirot libo*. In his view, the prohibition is triggered not by the number but by the consequence.

### Shimon: No-Astray Rule

Rabbi Shimon takes the opposing position: *afilu achat u-mesira et libo harei zu lo yisana* — even one wife who will turn his heart astray, he may not marry. *Im ken lama ne'emar ve-lo yarbeh lo nashim* — if so, what does the Torah add by saying he should not *multiply* wives? *D'afilu ke-Avigayil* — even if they are all as righteous as Avigayil, having too many is still prohibited.

### Dispute Resolution

The Gemara asks: does this mean Rabbi Yehudah is the one who *darish ta'ama de-kra* — who interprets the law through its underlying reason — while Rabbi Shimon does not? This appears to invert the usual alignment, since Rabbi Shimon is famously associated with *derisha ta'ama de-kra* elsewhere. The classic example is the law of *lo tachbol beged almana* — one may not take a cloak as collateral from a widow. Rabbi Yehudah holds that this applies whether the widow is rich or poor — he does not look to the reason and applies the law categorically. Rabbi Shimon holds that a rich widow's cloak *may* be taken as collateral, since the reason for the prohibition — that she is poor and will be embarrassed going to retrieve it from her creditor — does not apply to a wealthy woman. Rabbi Shimon elaborates: if one does take a *mashkon* from a poor widow, she will inevitably return to retrieve it, and her neighbors will draw unseemly conclusions about why she is constantly visiting a man's home, damaging her reputation. For a rich widow, this dynamic does not arise. Thus Rabbi Shimon *is* the one who reasons from *ta'ama de-kra*, and Rabbi Yehudah is not.

How, then, do we reconcile the reversal apparent in the king's wives case? The answer given is: *shani hacha de-mefaresh ta'ama de-kra* — this case is different because the Torah itself explicitly states the reason. In the widow case, no reason is given, so Rabbi Shimon infers one. Here, the Torah openly states *ve-lo yasur levavo*. Given that explicit reason, Rabbi Yehudah naturally incorporates it into his ruling: so long as the wives will not turn the king's heart astray, the prohibition does not apply. Rabbi Shimon, for his part, says: precisely because I generally derive reasons myself, I know that if the Torah had intended to convey only this principle it could simply have written *lo yarbeh lo nashim* and remained silent — *ve-lishtok*. I would have inferred the reason myself. Since the Torah went out of its way to add *ve-lo yasur levavo*, it must be teaching something beyond what I would have derived on my own: namely, that even one wife who will lead him astray is forbidden. And since that is the lesson of the added phrase, the main prohibition of *lo yarbeh* must teach something distinct: *afilu ke-Avigayil* — even if all the wives are righteous, exceeding the number is still prohibited.

### Kahena Calculation

The derivation of eighteen from the *kahena ve-kahena* formula is that since David had six wives at the time of Nathan's rebuke, each *kahena* equals six. The *tanna de-didan* holds that the two instances of *kahena* each add six, bringing the total to 6 + 6 + 6 = 18. This is supported by a principle that the second *kahena* is linked to the first: *makish kaheina basra le-kaheina kamma, mah kaheina kamma sheis af kaheina basra sheis*.

### Higher Wife Counts

Alternative readings produce higher totals. If the second *kahena* is taken as doubling all that came before rather than adding a flat six, then 6 + 6 = 12, and *kahena* again doubles to yield 24. A *baraita* is cited in support: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-yoter me-esrim ve-arba* — he may not have more than twenty-four. Alternatively, taking the doubling principle further: 6 + 6 = 12, then a *kahena* equivalent to all of that is 12, so 12 + 12 = 24, and a final *kahena* equal to 24 produces 48. Another *baraita* is cited in support: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-yoter me-arba'im u-shemoneh* — he may not have more than forty-eight.

## Michal & David's Wives

### Michal as Eglah

Among the six wives listed in Shmuel 2, chapter 3 is a woman named *Eglah*, which is an unusual name. The Gemara, citing Rav, identifies this as Michal: *amar Rav, Egla zu Michal*. The epithet *egla* is a term of endearment — *she-havivah alav ke-egla*, she was beloved to him like a calf. A parallel is drawn to Shimshon's riddle: *lulei charashtem be-eglati lo metzasem chidosi* — had you not plowed with my *egla*, meaning extracted the answer through my wife, you would not have solved the riddle.

### Michal's Children Problem

This identification immediately raises a difficulty: *u-mi havu le-Michal banim?* Did Michal actually have children? The verse in Shmuel explicitly states: *u-le-Michal bat Shaul lo hayah lah yeled ad yom motah* — Michal the daughter of Shaul had no child until the day of her death. This verse follows the episode in which Michal rebuked David for dancing before the *aron*, saying *mah nichbad hayom melech Yisrael*, accusing him of demeaning himself by dancing before everyone *ke-higlot niglot achad ha-reikim* — like one of the empty commoners. David responded that he would humble himself even further and would be held in esteem by the *shfachot*. After this exchange, the verse records that Michal had no offspring until her dying day.

### Hebron vs Jerusalem

Two resolutions are offered. Rav Chisda proposes: *ad yom motah lo hayah lah, be-yom motah hayah lah* — she had no child *until* the day she died, meaning she gave birth on the very day of her death, presumably dying in childbirth from the son Yitream listed among David's Chevron children. The second resolution notes that the children enumerated in Shmuel 2:3 were born to David in *Chevron*, whereas the entire incident with Michal and the dancing before the *aron* took place in *Yerushalayim* — as the verse explicitly places the scene there. Therefore *mipnei baneha she-chashavu be-Chevron* — the children attributed to her were conceived in Chevron — and *ve-ilu ma'aseh de-Michal be-Yerushalayim havah* — the episode that resulted in her barrenness occurred only afterward in Yerushalayim. Rav Yehudah (or Rav Yosef) adds that she was punished for her words to the king: *shekaltah Michal de-mitrefah*. The fuller formulation reconciles the texts: *ad oto ma'aseh hayah lah, mi-kan ve-eilach lo hayah lah* — until that incident she had children; from that point on she did not.

This resolution is coherent: David reigned first in Chevron with a more limited kingdom, then consolidated power and moved to Yerushalayim. The children, including the one born of Michal (*Eglah*), predate the Yerushalayim episode.

### Merav-Michal Solution

The Gemara also addresses the verse in Shmuel 2, chapter 21 that refers to *chameshah bnei Michal bat Shaul* — five sons of Michal the daughter of Shaul — which seems to attribute even more children to her. One possible resolution, already noted on 19b, is: *Merav yaldah, Michal giddelah, be-chach nikreu al shemah* — Merav bore them, Michal raised them, and therefore they are called by her name. The word *yaldah* would normally imply she gave birth, but the Gemara is willing to entertain the principle that raising children can render them attributable to the one who raised them. This provides an alternative avenue of resolution independent of the Chevron-versus-Yerushalayim answer, though the Gemara employs both.

## Concubines & 400 Children

### Concubine Definition

The verse in Shmuel 2, chapter 5 states that David took additional *pilagshim* and *nashim* in Yerushalayim — *vayikach David od pilagshim ve-nashim mi-Yerushalayim*. The Gemara asks: *mai nashim mai pilagshim* — what distinguishes a wife from a *pilegesh*? Rav Yehudah citing Rav explains: *nashim be-ketubah u-ve-kiddushin* — wives have a *ketubah* and *kiddushin*; *pilagshim belo ketubah u-velo kiddushin* — concubines have neither. The relationship is essentially a sexual one, without the formal legal structure of marriage. Whether such an arrangement is normatively permissible, and under what conditions — whether it was specific to kings, to the biblical period, or to the very wealthy — is not addressed here; the Gemara only records what the distinction is.

### 400 Yefat Toar Children

Rav Yehudah citing Rav teaches: *arba me'ot yeladim noldu le-David ve-khullam bnei yefat to'ar hayu* — four hundred children were born to David, and all of them were sons of *yefat to'ar*. The *yefat to'ar* is the beautiful captive discussed in *Ki Tetzei*: a non-Jewish woman captured in war whom a soldier desires. David was a highly martial king who fought many wars, and he apparently desired women captured in those campaigns, with the result that four hundred children were born from such relationships. These children all had *beloriot* — the braided hairstyle associated with non-Jewish culture — and they would sit in golden chariots, *bekronot shel zahav*, and lead the troops. They were the powerful fighters of the house of David: *ba'alei egrofim shel beit David*.

### Blorit Hair Problem

This creates a serious halakhic problem. The *belorit* is not merely a foreign aesthetic; it falls squarely within the prohibition of *chukkot ha-goyim* and possibly even *avodah zarah*. The Rambam in *Hilchot Avodah Zarah*, chapter 11, *halacha* 1, lists among the prohibited acts: *lo yilbash malbush ha-miyuchad lahem* — one may not wear clothing distinctive to them; *ve-lo yigadel tzitzit kemo tzitzit rosham* — one may not grow hair in the style of their hair; *ve-lo yegalach min ha-tzdadim ve-yaniach ha-se'ar be-emtza, kemo she-hem osim* — one may not shave the sides and leave hair in the center as they do. And: *ve-zehu ha-nikra blorit* — this is what is called *blorit*. According to the Rambam, violating these prohibitions incurs *malkut*.

### Rambam's Close-to-Royalty

The Rambam himself provides a limited dispensation: *Yisrael she-hayah karov la-malchut ve-tzarich liyeshev lifnei malcheihem ve-yehei gnai im lo yidameh lahen* — a Jew who is close to the royal court and must appear before non-Jewish kings, for whom it would be demeaning not to resemble them, *harei zeh mutar lilbosh ke-malbushan u-le-galach ha-se'ar keneged panav ke-derech she-hem osim* — he is permitted to dress as they dress and to style his hair as they do.

### Kesef Mishneh Defense

The *Kesef Mishneh* cites the case of Avtulos bar Reuven, who was permitted to have a *komi* haircut — a non-Jewish style — because he was *karov la-malchut*, in proximity to royalty. From this the Rambam derived the broader dispensation for dress as well.

But the *Kesef Mishneh* presses further: if the Rambam rules that one incurs *malkut* for these acts, how could the *chachamim* permit them even for those close to royalty, given that such proximity does not ordinarily override a *lav* of the Torah? The answer offered by the *Kesef Mishneh* is: *mishum hatzalat Yisrael hu de-sharu* — it was permitted on grounds of saving Jewish lives. Those who served as emissaries or advisors to kings were in a position to protect the Jewish people, and thus the allowance falls under the principle of *pikuach nefesh*. The *Kesef Mishneh* then applies this reasoning directly to the four hundred children: *nami hatzalat Yisrael hu de-havu meva'atim et ha-oyvim* — this too was permitted for the sake of *hatzalat Yisrael*, because these men, placed at the forefront of battle looking entirely non-Jewish, would strike fear into the enemy. Seeing what appeared to be an enormous, non-Jewish-looking army advancing against them, the enemy would lose heart — even their own population appeared to oppose them. The *belorit* served a strategic military function of psychological intimidation, and on that basis the *Kesef Mishneh* justifies it.

An alternative formulation is also raised: *she-ha-Torah lo partah davar ela amrah uvechukoteihem lo telechu* — the Torah did not specify the precise content of this prohibition, only stating the general rule not to follow their customs. The Torah entrusted the details to the *chachamim*, and the *chachamim* determined that it does not apply to those close to royalty. Under this reading there is no true *Torah*-level prohibition being overridden; the *chachamim* have simply defined its scope.

## Amnon & Tamar Incident

### Tamar as Yefat Toar

Rav Yehudah citing Rav teaches: *Tamar bat yefat to'ar hayeta* — Tamar was the daughter of a *yefat to'ar*. This is derived from her own words during the assault, when she pleaded with Amnon: *ve-atah daber na el ha-melech ki lo yimna'eni mimecha* — speak to the king, for he will not withhold me from you. How could Tamar have suggested that the king would permit her marriage to Amnon, given that they were half-siblings sharing the same father? The answer is that she must have been a *bat yefat to'ar* — a daughter born of a captive woman — and thus there was a question whether her status as David's daughter was halakhically equivalent to a full sister. *Michdei bat achva me-nisuin hava, achva hi ve-i sharei lei?* — she was indeed his sister through their father. The very fact that she could plausibly argue the king might permit it indicates she knew there was a technical basis for such a position, and that basis is her being a *bat yefat to'ar*.

This raises a further question addressed in *Tosafot*. The *Yerushalmi* records a *machloket* between Rav and Shmuel: *Rav amar eina muteret ba-milchamah ela bi'ah rishonah* — Rav holds that relations with a *yefat to'ar* are permitted once, in the heat of battle, before the full conversion process. *Ve-Shmuel amar af lo bi'ah rishonah* — Shmuel holds that even the first act of relations is not permitted until the conversion ritual is complete. According to Shmuel's position, by the time a man could have legitimate relations with a *yefat to'ar*, she would already be Jewish — so how could Tamar have been born before her mother converted?

*Tosafot* offers two answers. First: *she-imah kvar hayeta uberet ke-she-ba'ah la-milchamah* — Tamar's mother may have already been pregnant with Tamar from her previous non-Jewish partner at the time she was captured. David then performed the *yefat to'ar* ritual and formally married her, but Tamar herself was conceived before that. Thus, even according to Shmuel, David never had relations with Tamar's mother before she was Jewish — he only did so after the conversion — but Tamar herself was conceived by a different father beforehand. Second: *mitoch she-gadlah be-cheiko shel David karu lah bat ha-melech* — since Tamar grew up in David's household, she was called the king's daughter, even if David was not her biological father. This reading can absorb the textual reference to her as David's daughter without requiring that David fathered her at all. Neither answer forecloses the other, and together they demonstrate that the *Tamar* narrative cannot serve as definitive proof in either direction regarding the *bi'ah rishonah* question.

### Yonadav's Evil Advice

The narrative returns to the incident itself. The text introduces Amnon's associate: *ve-le-Amnon rei ve-shmo Yonadav ben Shima achi David* — Amnon had a friend named Yonadav, the son of David's brother Shima, making him David's nephew. He is described as *ish chacham* — a clever man. Rav Yehudah citing Rav glosses: *ish chacham hayah le-rish'ah* — he was clever in wickedness, for he devised the scheme. Yonadav advised Amnon: *shechav al mitatecha ve-hitchala* — lie on your bed and feign illness. When people come to you, say that only Tamar's cooking can restore you. Rav Yehudah citing Rav adds that what Tamar made was *minei tigun* — fried foods. The reason the ruse required Tamar to cook in front of him specifically was that fried food must be served immediately; it loses its quality if it sits. He could therefore legitimately demand that it be prepared fresh in his presence, providing the pretext to clear everyone else from the room.

### Amnon's Hatred

After the assault, the verse states: *vayisna Amnon sin'ah gedolah me'od* — Amnon hated her with a very great hatred. The reversal of passion into revulsion is noted without extended commentary. The hatred he felt was likely intensified by the sight of Tamar, which confronted him constantly with his own transgression.

### Tamar's Pubic Hair Issue

The Gemara then addresses a strange textual issue. The phrase *mai Tamar* is read to suggest that Tamar did something involving a strand of hair tied in a knot, and this caused Amnon to become a *k'rut shofcha* — one whose generative capacity is severed or destroyed. The verse is read: *kashra lo nima ve-asta lo k'rut shofcha* — she tied a hair-strand around him and thereby rendered him sterile. It is understood here as her act of resistance — she managed to strike back at her attacker in some way, leaving him permanently incapacitated.

This raises an apparent contradiction with a statement of Rava, who teaches from the verse *vayeitzei shemech ba-goyim be-yofyeich* — your fame went out among the nations for your beauty — that Jewish women do not have hair in the underarm or the pubic area: *she-ein lahem li-vnot Yisrael lo sarchat ha-shachi ve-lo beit ha-erva*. If Jewish women lack pubic hair, how could Tamar have used one? The resolution proposed is that Jewish women of that era were scrupulous about shaving — they did grow hair naturally, but maintained careful grooming such that they were smooth. This is not a claim that Jewish women biologically lack body hair (*nishtana ha-teva* is not invoked), but rather a description of their customary practice. How then could Tamar have done this? *Ve-shani Tamar de-bat yefat to'ar havi* — Tamar, as the daughter of a *yefat to'ar*, was not in the same category. Alternatively, since she was an unmarried woman who was not having sexual relations, she would have had no occasion or reason to shave, and so she was in a different practical situation than married women.

### Yichud Decree on Women

The narrative concludes with a communal consequence. The verse describes Tamar placing ash on her head and rending her garment of many colors in mourning. A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha teaches: *gader gadol gadrah Tamar be-otah sha'ah* — Tamar erected a great fence through this incident. The reasoning: *amru bnot melachim kach, bnot hedyotot al achat kama ve-chama; im li-tzenu'ot kach, li-frutzot al achat kama ve-chama* — if this could happen to the modest daughter of a king, *kal ve-chomer* to anyone; if even a modest woman was vulnerable, certainly an immodest one would be.

Rav Yehudah teaching: *ba-otah sha'ah gazru al ha-yichud ve-al ha-penuyah* — at that time they decreed regarding *yichud* and regarding the unmarried woman. The Gemara clarifies: is *yichud* not already prohibited from the Torah? Yes — *yichud de-Oraita hu* — seclusion with a married woman is a Torah-level prohibition, derived from the verse *ki yesitcha achicha ben imecha*: the Torah specifies "the brother, the son of your mother" rather than simply "your brother" to convey that only a son may be secluded with his own mother; any other pairing involving the forbidden relations of the Torah falls under *yichud*. That derivation is attributed to Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak.

If *yichud* was already prohibited, what was new in the decree? The Gemara clarifies: *ela eima gazru al yichud de-penuyah* — the decree addressed *yichud* with an unmarried woman. Until that point, an unmarried man and an unmarried woman could be alone together, since neither was technically *arayot* to the other. After the incident with Tamar — which demonstrated the danger even in a royal, modest setting — the rabbis extended the prohibition to cover seclusion between unmarried men and women as well.

## Adoniyah's Rebellion

### Failed Coronation

The *mishnah* concludes with Adoniyah the son of Chagit, who declared *ani emloch* — I shall be king — and made an attempt to claim the throne. Rav Yehudah citing Rav interprets this: *melamed she-bikkesh le-holmo ve-lo holmato* — the text teaches that he sought to have the crown placed on his head, and it did not fit.

### Speedy Runners Enhanceme…

The verse records that Adoniyah prepared a chariot and cavalry and fifty runners to go before him. The Gemara asks: is that remarkable? Many princes have entourages. The answer is what distinguished these fifty men: *kulam ntulei tchol ve-chakukei kipot raglayim hayu* — they had all undergone spleen removal, and their foot-soles had been treated — literally, carved. The spleen removal was apparently understood to enhance running endurance. The treatment to the feet refers to the deadening of nerve endings: since runners of that era ran barefoot, they would be slowed or stopped by sharp thorns and rocks underfoot. By killing the nerve endings in the soles of their feet, these runners could traverse any terrain without pain, enabling them to travel greater distances at greater speed. These were deliberately engineered elite runners, prepared through what amounts to surgical intervention.
