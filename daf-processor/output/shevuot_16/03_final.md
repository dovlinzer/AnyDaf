# Shevuot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Procession Path Debate

### Rashi: Outside Walk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי פְּנִימִית נֶאֱכֶלֶת – מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתְיָא חִיצוֹנָה קַמַּהּ, וְקַדְּשָׁהּ לַהּ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָא מְיקַדְּשִׁי!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that the two thanks-offerings moved forward **one behind the other,** and that the inner one is the one that was in the rear, it is **due to this** that **the inner one is eaten, because the outer one came before it and consecrated** the additional area, so that it is now a sanctified place fit for the eating of the thanks-offering loaves. **But according to the one who says** that the two thanks-offerings moved forward **one alongside the other, the two of them together consecrated** the additional area.

The daf opens with a point left unaddressed in the previous session: the debate between Rashi and Tosfos regarding the exact path taken during the procession used to sanctify the expanded boundaries of Jerusalem. Rashi holds that both participants in the procession walk on the outside of the perimeter of the newly designated space — that is, along the exterior of the new boundary wall.

### Tosfos: Inside Walk

Tosfos raises a significant objection to Rashi's position. If both participants walk on the outside, two difficulties arise. First, it is somewhat peculiar to sanctify a space by walking around its exterior. Second — and more decisively — if both participants remain outside the perimeter throughout the procession, both *todah* breads should become invalid, since neither would have entered the sanctified space at any point. Tosfos therefore concludes that both participants walk on the inside of the perimeter.

### Single-File Sanctificati…

On Tosfos's reading, the procession proceeds along the interior edge of the new boundary wall. Walking in single file, the lead participant is constantly sanctifying the space ahead, which simultaneously renders invalid the bread that participant carries — the space was unsanctified a moment before, and becomes sanctified as the procession advances. The second participant, following immediately behind, moves through space that has just been sanctified. The Gemara ultimately rejects the notion that a single participant could accomplish the sanctification alone, insisting that the two must work in tandem, but Tosfos's construction explains the basic geometry of the procession.

### Wall Thickness Sanctity

A further complication for Tosfos arises from an established *halacha*: the thickness of the boundary wall itself also carries the sanctity of the interior space — so that someone standing within a gate embedded in the wall is considered to be within the sanctified zone. Since on Tosfos's reading the participants walk along the inner face of the wall, it is not immediately obvious how the act of walking there sanctifies the full depth of the wall's thickness. Tosfos acknowledges this difficulty but concludes that the *halacha* simply must be accepted as it stands, even without a fully satisfying geometric account.

## Sanctification w/ Korban…

### Todah for Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל פִּי נָבִיא נֶאֱכֶלֶת וְעַל פִּי נָבִיא נִשְׂרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Based on a ruling of a prophet** the loaves of one thanks-offering **were eaten, and based on a ruling of a prophet** the loaves of the other thanks-offering **were burned.**

The method for sanctifying Jerusalem's expanded boundaries involves the breads of a *todah* offering. The *todah* breads are consecrated items that may only be consumed within the walls of Jerusalem — and it is precisely this quality that makes them the instrument of sanctification: by bringing them into the newly expanded space, one demonstrates and enacts the boundary of the city.

### Mincha for Azarah

An analogous mechanism applies to the sanctification of the *Azarah*. There, the remnant of a *Mincha* offering — flour and water baked as *matzah*, since a *Mincha* is not *chametz* — serves as the instrument of sanctification, because a *Mincha* may only be consumed within the *Azarah* itself.

### Har HaBayis Limits

Tosfos raises a question regarding *Har HaBayis*, which carries the status of *Machaneh Leviyah* — the Levite camp. This encompasses the *Ezras Nashim* and the entire Temple Mount. The challenge is that there is no *korban* whose consumption is delimited precisely by the boundaries of *Har HaBayis*. The sanctity of *Har HaBayis* is defined not by where a particular *korban* may be eaten, but by which levels of *tumah* may or may not enter. Accordingly, there is no obvious candidate for a korban that could serve as the instrument for sanctifying *Har HaBayis* in the way *todah* breads serve for Jerusalem or *Mincha* remnants serve for the *Azarah*.

Tosfos acknowledges this gap and concludes with the principle that where it is impossible to apply the standard method, one simply does not apply it — *heicha d'lo efshar, lo efshar*. Presumably the procession would still take place, but without the symbolic use of a *korban* whose territorial limit coincides with the new boundary.

### Boundaries & Symbols

This leads to a broader reflection on the symbolic power of these boundary-marking acts. The use of the *todah* bread or *Mincha* flour is a way of saying: here is where this sanctified item reaches its limit; beyond this, the item may not go. It is a concrete, enacted declaration of a boundary. An interesting parallel — not one the Gemara itself draws, but suggestive of the same logic — is the lighting of *Shabbos* candles, which marks the transition from weekday to *Shabbos*, and *Havdalah* on the other end, which marks the return. These are acts that signify a threshold: after this, something changes.

## Components Required

### Rav Huna: All Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשֵׂית בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ כּוּ׳. אִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ״ תְּנַן, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: ״בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: And with regard to **any** addition to the Temple **that was not made with all these** ceremonial procedures, one who enters there while ritually impure is not liable. **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed about the text of the mishna. **Rav Huna says: We learned** in the mishna: **With all these,** which means that all of these procedures are indispensable, and if even one is missing, the consecration does not take effect. **Rav Naḥman says: We learned** in the mishna: **With one of all these;** i.e., any one of these procedures suffices for the consecration to take effect.

The Gemara now records a dispute between *Amoraim* regarding what components must be present for a valid sanctification. The Mishnah states *kol she'lo na'asah b'chol elu* — anything not done with all of these is invalid. Rav Huna reads this literally: *b'chol elu tnan* — the Mishnah means all of them together: *Kohen Gadol*, *Melech*, *Navi*, the full *Sanhedrin* of seventy-one, and the rest.

### Rav Nachman: One Suffices

Rav Nachman disagrees: *b'achas mikol elu tnan* — any one of these components is sufficient for a valid sanctification.

### First Sanctity Eternal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר ״בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ״ תְּנַן – קָסָבַר קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְעֶזְרָא זֵכֶר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further clarifies this disagreement. **Rav Huna says: We learned** in the mishna: **With all these.** The Gemara explains: **He maintains** that **the initial consecration** of the Temple in the days of David and Solomon **sanctified** it **for its time and sanctified** it **forever.** Therefore, the site of the Temple retained its original sanctity even during the days of the Second Temple. **And Ezra,** who reconsecrated the area, **did** so **merely as a commemoration** of the initial consecration.

Rav Huna's position rests on the following reasoning. The proof-text for this entire procedure derives from the time of Nechemya. Yet at that time there was no king, and — crucially — there were no *Urim V'Tummim*. The requirement for a *Kohen Gadol* with *Urim V'Tummim* could not have been met. If one were to say that all components are required, then the sanctification performed in the time of Nechemya was simply not valid — because not all the components were present. This creates no problem, however, because Rav Huna holds: *kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'kidsha l'asid lavo* — the original sanctification of Jerusalem and the *Beis HaMikdash*, which took place in the time of David and Shlomo, was valid not only for its time but for all future time. Nechemya's generation therefore needed to do nothing, since the sanctity had never lapsed. And what was performed in the time of Ezra — the procession, the ceremony — was merely a *zeicher*, a memorial, evoking what had been done in the past, significant nationally and symbolically but carrying no independent *halachic* force. One might wonder whether the *Urim V'Tummim* requirement means simply that the *Kohen Gadol* must be wearing his full regalia, or whether it requires actually consulting the *Urim V'Tummim* — i.e., seeking divine approval — and the question is a good one, though not fully resolved here.

An important practical consequence follows from Rav Huna's position: since all the required components are no longer available, it is impossible, from the time of the destruction of the first *Beis HaMikdash* onward, to extend the boundaries of Jerusalem. The mechanism described in the Mishnah for expanding the city's sanctified area would have been limited in its operation to the era of the first *Beis HaMikdash*.

## Nechemya & Ezra

### Nechemya's Components

With the framework of Rav Huna's position now clear — that the original sanctity endures and that what happened in Nechemya's time was only a symbolic memorial — the Gemara examines the historical situation. At the time of Nechemya, the returning community lacked both a recognized king and the *Urim V'Tummim*. According to Rav Huna, this simply means they did not perform a genuine resanctification, because none was needed.

### Ezra: Memorial or Real?

The ceremony conducted by Ezra is characterized, on Rav Huna's view, as *zeicher b'alma* — a purely commemorative act. The nation was returning to a destroyed Temple, rebuilding and re-entering a sacred space; it was appropriate to reenact the gestures of dedication in a way that was spiritually and communally meaningful. This is comparable in spirit to a *chanukas habayis* — a dedication ceremony — which may be symbolically significant even when it carries no independent *halachic* weight. According to Rav Huna, that is all that occurred at Ezra's time.

### Boundary Extension Rules

The consequence of this position is pointed: not only was no resanctification necessary, none was possible. The extension of the city's boundaries requires all of the listed components — *Melech*, *Navi*, *Urim V'Tummim*, the *Sanhedrin* of seventy-one, and the two *todah* offerings with song. In the absence of any of these, the boundary cannot be legitimately expanded, even through a ceremonial procession.

## Components Required…

### First Sanctity Temporary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר ״בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ״ תְּנַן – קָסָבַר: קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְעֶזְרָא קַדּוֹשֵׁי קַדֵּישׁ אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הֲווֹ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: We learned** in the mishna: **With one of all these.** The Gemara explains: **He maintains** that **the initial consecration** of the Temple **sanctified** it **for its time** only, **and did not sanctify** it **forever. And Ezra consecrated** the Temple and its courtyards, **even though there was no** king or ***Urim VeTummim*.** Consequently, it follows that there is no need for all of these procedures in order for the consecration to take effect.

Rav Nachman's position rests on the opposite premise: *kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'lo kidsha l'asid lavo* — the original sanctity was only for its time and did not endure beyond the destruction of the first *Beis HaMikdash*. When the Temple was destroyed, the sanctity lapsed. Resanctification was therefore genuinely necessary when the community returned, and Ezra accomplished a real resanctification — *v'Ezra kidesh l'sha'ata*. Because Rav Nachman holds that any single component suffices, the resanctification at Ezra's time was fully valid even without a king or *Urim V'Tummim*. This also raises the theoretical question of whether, in principle, such a sanctification — or even an extension of boundaries — might be possible today, depending on how one understands terms like *Melech*. Formally, however, any one of the qualifying components would in principle be enough.

## Rava's Challenge

### Mishnah Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: ״כֹּל שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשֵׂית בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ״! תָּנֵי: ״בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman:** The mishna explicitly states: **Any** addition **that was not made with all these** procedures. Rav Naḥman said to him: Emend the mishna and **teach** it as stating: **With one of all these** procedures.

Rava challenges Rav Nachman's reading of the Mishnah. The Mishnah's language is *b'chol elu* — with all of them. This would appear to favor Rav Huna's reading that all components are required.

### RN Responds

Rav Nachman replies: read it as *b'achas mikol elu* — with one of all of them, meaning any one. In effect, he inserts the word "one" into the Mishnah's formulation. Tosfos notes that the difficulty is perhaps not as severe as it appears, since the phrase as it stands can bear that reading without emendation. The Gemara then turns to Tannaic evidence to adjudicate between the two positions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי בִּיצְעִין הָיוּ בְּהַר הַמִּשְׁחָה, תַּחְתּוֹנָה וְעֶלְיוֹנָה. תַּחְתּוֹנָה – נִתְקַדְּשָׁה בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ; עֶלְיוֹנָה – לֹא נִתְקַדְּשָׁה בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ, אֶלָּא בְּעוֹלֵי גוֹלָה; שֶׁלֹּא בְּמֶלֶךְ וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof against the opinion of Rav Naḥman: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **Abba Shaul says: There were two ponds [*bitzin*] on the Mount of Olives [*Har HaMishḥa*], a lower** pond **and an upper** pond. **The lower** pond **was consecrated** during the time of the First Temple **with all** the procedures mentioned in the mishna, and it has the sanctity of Jerusalem for all purposes. By contrast, **the upper** pond **was not consecrated with all these** procedures, **but** rather it was consecrated **by those who returned from the exile** in Babylonia, **without a king and without** the ***Urim VeTummim*.**

The Gemara cites Abba Shaul: *shenei bitza'in hayu b'Har HaMishcha* — there were two swamps on the Mount of Olives. Rashi, on the word *bitza'in*, cites the old French *mariska* — itself not immediately illuminating — though the Aramaic *ach* (swamp) in the verse from this week's *parasha* (ותעמדנה באחו, *vata'amodna ba'achu*, Bereishis 41:2) confirms the meaning. The boundaries of Jerusalem had been extended at different times to incorporate different parts of the Mount of Olives, and these two swamps mark two successive boundary lines.

## Two Swamps on Har HaMish…

### Lower Swamp: Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּחְתּוֹנָה שֶׁהָיְתָה קְדוּשָּׁתָהּ גְּמוּרָה – עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ נִכְנָסִין לְשָׁם, וְאוֹכְלִין שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים אֲבָל לֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי; וַחֲבֵרִים אוֹכְלִים שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **the lower** pond, **whose consecration was complete, *amei ha'aretz*,** uneducated people who were not scrupulous in their observance of the mitzvot relating to tithes and to ritual purity, **would enter into there and** would **partake of offerings of lesser sanctity** that may be eaten in all of Jerusalem **there, but** they would **not** partake of **second tithe** there because they conducted themselves stringently concerning this matter. **And *ḥaverim*,** who were meticulous in their observance of those mitzvot, **would partake of** both **offerings of lesser sanctity and second tithe there.**

The lower swamp — *tachtona* — was incorporated into Jerusalem's boundaries with all the required components in the time of the first *Beis HaMikdash*. Its sanctification was therefore *kedusha gemurah*, complete and full.

### Upper Swamp: Incomplete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶלְיוֹנָה שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה קְדוּשָּׁתָהּ גְּמוּרָה – עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ הָיוּ נִכְנָסִין שָׁם, וְאוֹכְלִין שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים אֲבָל לֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי; וַחֲבֵרִים אֵין אוֹכְלִים שָׁם לֹא קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וְלֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא קִידְּשׁוּהָ? שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הָעִיר וְעַל הָעֲזָרוֹת אֶלָּא בְּמֶלֶךְ וְנָבִיא וְאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים, וּבְסַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד, וּבִשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת וּבְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: As for **the upper** pond, **whose consecration was incomplete, *amei ha'aretz* would enter into there and partake of offerings of lesser sanctity there, but** they would **not** partake of **second tithe** there. **And *ḥaverim* would partake of neither offerings of lesser sanctity nor second tithe there. And for what** reason **did they not consecrate** the upper pond? It was **because additions can be made to the city** of Jerusalem **or to the** Temple **courtyards only by** a special body comprising the **king, a prophet, the *Urim VeTummim*, and the Sanhedrin of seventy-one, and with two thanks-offerings and with** a special **song.**

The upper swamp — *elyona* — was incorporated only in the time of *olei hagola*, those who returned from exile in the era of Ezra. Crucially, that sanctification was *shelo b'melech v'shelo b'urim v'tumim* — without a king and without the *Urim V'Tummim*. Its sanctification was therefore incomplete.

### Different Laws

The practical consequences are stated explicitly. In the lower zone, whose sanctity was complete: *amei ha'aretz* — people who were not scrupulous in the laws of *tumah* and *tahara* — would enter and eat *kodashim kalim* there. The text as it stands says they would eat *kodashim kalim* but not *ma'aser sheni*. Rashi appears to have had a different *girsa* of the text, reading that they would eat both *kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* there. Under Rashi's reading, both *amei ha'aretz* and *chaveirim* — those who were punctilious — would eat both *kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* in the lower zone, which makes intuitive sense for a fully sanctified area.

In the upper zone, whose sanctity was incomplete: *amei ha'aretz* would enter and eat *kodashim kalim*, but not *ma'aser sheni*. And *chaveirim* would eat neither *kodashim kalim* nor *ma'aser sheni* there, because they were aware that this zone had not been properly sanctified and that consuming these items there was not genuinely permitted.

The seemingly paradoxical element here is that the *amei ha'aretz* distinguished in the upper zone between *kodashim kalim* on the one hand and *ma'aser sheni* on the other — despite the fact that *kodashim kalim* might appear to be the weightier matter. Rashi offers an explanation: perhaps the *amei ha'aretz* were familiar with the *derasha* on the verse *aser te'aser* — "you shall surely tithe" — which the Midrash interprets as *aser bishvil she'tis'asher*, "tithe so that you will become wealthy." Perhaps this made them especially particular to eat *ma'aser sheni* specifically within the ancient inner city, the walls of the original, fully sanctified Jerusalem. It remains somewhat puzzling that *korbanos* would be treated more leniently by the same people, since *korbanot* would normally appear more significant — but Rashi's suggestion is that it is a matter of popular sentiment and the particular weight that the *ma'aser sheni* *derasha* carried.

The theoretical implication worth noting is this: one might hypothesize — and the Gemara is not saying this, but it emerges as a possibility — that the sanctification of the second *Beis HaMikdash* era, lacking all the components, could not extend the *mikdash*-related sanctity, i.e., the sanctity relevant for *korbanot* and *kodashim*. Perhaps for some other dimension of Jerusalem's sanctity — such as *ma'aser sheni*, whose *halakha* is based on the verse *lifnei Hashem*, before God, rather than directly on proximity to the *Mikdash* — the picture might be different. This remains an interesting conceptual possibility, though not one the Gemara explicitly develops.

What is clear is that the sanctity that counts is the *kedusha rishona*, the first sanctity. The extension made in the era of Ezra and Nechemya does not carry full force.

### Why Symbolize?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָמָּה קִידְּשׁוּהָ? לָמָּה קִידְּשׁוּהָ?! הָא אָמְרַתְּ לֹא קִידְּשׁוּהָ! אֶלָּא לָמָה הִכְנִיסוּהָ? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁתּוֹרְפָּהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם הָיְתָה, וְנוֹחָה הִיא לִיכָּבֵשׁ מִשָּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why did they consecrate** the upper pond if they could not do so properly? This Gemara responds: **Why did they consecrate it? Didn't you say** that **they did not consecrate it? Rather,** the question should be asked as follows: Given that they could not consecrate the upper pond, **why did they bring it within** the walls of the city? The Gemara answers: **Because it was a weak point [*turpa*] of Jerusalem and it would have been easy to conquer** the city **from there,** it became necessary to include it within the wall.

If the upper zone could not be validly sanctified without all the required components, why did the returning community bother performing this ceremony at all? The Gemara answers: *mipnei she-turpa shel Yerushalayim hayta* — because it was the exposed flank of Jerusalem, a strategically vulnerable area from which the city could easily be attacked. By incorporating this territory into Jerusalem — building the wall out to that perimeter and performing the ceremony — the community was making a public statement, encouraging people to relate to this zone as genuinely part of Jerusalem, to own it emotionally and communally. Rashi explains that they built up the wall to the outer limit for this reason. Even if the ceremony lacked the *halachic* force of a genuine sanctification, it served a vital national and defensive purpose.

## Curtains (Klayim)

### RE: Curtains for Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: שָׁמַעְתִּי כְּשֶׁהָיוּ בּוֹנִים בַּהֵיכָל, עָשׂוּ קְלָעִים לַהֵיכָל וּקְלָעִים לָעֲזָרוֹת. אֶלָּא שֶׁבַּהֵיכָל בּוֹנִים מִבַּחוּץ, וּבָעֲזָרוֹת בּוֹנִים מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: This **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** as to whether the initial consecration of Jerusalem and the Temple sanctified them only for their time or forever. Abba Shaul maintains that the initial consecration lapsed, and therefore the reconsecration required all of the procedures mentioned in the mishna. Where is this dispute taught? **As it is taught** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 8:6): **Rabbi Eliezer says: I heard** that **when they were building the Sanctuary** in the Second Temple, **they fashioned** temporary **curtains for the Sanctuary and** temporary **curtains for the courtyards** to serve as partitions until the construction of the stone walls was completed. The difference was **only that in the Sanctuary,** the workers **built** the walls **outside** the curtains, without entering, **and in the courtyards,** the workers **built** the walls **within** the curtains.

The Gemara now introduces a *Baraisa* in which Rabbi Eliezer describes the practice during the construction of the second *Beis HaMikdash*: *asu kla'im laheichal v'kla'im la'azaros* — they erected curtains around the *Heichal* and curtains around the *Azaros*. The purpose was to maintain a sense of the sanctity and boundaries of these spaces even while construction was underway. Since only *Kohanim* were normally permitted entry into the *Heichal*, one would not want the builders to be gazing into or working casually within the space that was to become the most sanctified zone — even before the walls were fully built. The *kla'im* served as a screen, preserving the dignity and separateness of the space during reconstruction.

### Construction Order

A distinction is made in how the curtains functioned in each zone. For the *Heichal*, the builders worked on the outside of the curtains — *bonim mibachutz* — so that the actual construction activity never penetrated the curtained space. For the *Azaros*, the builders worked on the inside of the curtains — *bonim mibifnim* — since the *Azarah* carried a lesser degree of sanctity than the *Heichal*, and the curtains there served more to demarcate the space and prevent casual observation from passersby than to shield it from the construction workers themselves.

## Sanctity Endures

### RY: No Structure Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁמַּקְרִיבִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בַּיִת, אוֹכְלִין קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין קְלָעִים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין חוֹמָה; מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא. לָאו מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: לֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Yehoshua says: I heard that one sacrifices offerings** on the altar **even if there is no Temple,** and **one partakes of offerings of the most sacred order** in the Temple courtyard **even if there are no curtains, and** one partakes of **offerings of lesser sanctity and second-tithe** produce in Jerusalem **even if there is no wall** surrounding the city. This is **due to** the fact **that the initial consecration sanctified** the Temple and Jerusalem **for their time and** also **sanctified** them **forever.** The Gemara concludes: From the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua based his opinion on the principle that the initial consecration sanctified the Temple and Jerusalem forever, can one **not** learn **by inference** that **Rabbi Eliezer maintains** that **it did not sanctify** them **forever?** Apparently, this issue is the subject of a dispute between *tanna'im*.

Rabbi Yehoshua offers a striking teaching: *shamati shemakrivim af al pi she'ein bayis* — I have heard that *korbanot* may be brought even without a *Beis HaMikdash* standing. Furthermore, *ochlim kodshei kodashim af al pi she'ein kla'im* — one may eat *kodshei kodashim* even without the curtains that demarcate the *Azarah*. And *kodashim kalim u'ma'aser sheni af al pi she'ein chomah* — *kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* may be eaten in Jerusalem even without the city wall standing.

This principle underlies the position of those who seek to bring the *korban Pesach* today. The argument is that the physical structure of the *Beis HaMikdash* is not what generates the sanctity — it merely reflects and houses a sanctity that is inherently present. If the sanctity never left, then the space remains valid for the bringing of *korbanot*, notwithstanding the absence of the building.

### Presence Endures

The basis for Rabbi Yehoshua's position is the principle: *kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'kidsha l'asid lavo* — the original sanctification endures. The physical structure is not the source of the sanctity; the *Shechina* — the divine presence — continues to dwell in that place. The destruction of the building did not remove the sanctity, because the sanctity was never a property of the building as such, but of the place itself.

### Land vs. Mikdash

This raises a fascinating question discussed by both Tosfos and Rambam: is the question of whether *kedusha rishona* endures for the *Beis HaMikdash* the same question as whether the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* for agricultural purposes — *terumos*, *ma'asros*, and *shemittah* — also endured after the exile? Tosfos in several places considers these questions and treats them as potentially separable. One could, in principle, hold that the sanctity of the *Mikdash* endures while the sanctity of the land for agricultural purposes does not — or vice versa.

Rambam's ruling is precisely this asymmetry: the sanctity of the *Beis HaMikdash* endures, while the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* for *terumos* and *ma'asros* did not survive the exile. Rambam's explanation is fundamental: the sanctity of the *Mikdash* is defined by *hashras hashechina*, the indwelling of the divine presence. Once the divine presence has dwelt in a particular place, that presence does not simply depart because the physical structure was destroyed. The place remains sanctified by virtue of what dwelt there. The sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* for agricultural purposes, by contrast, is not defined in this way — it is linked rather to Jewish sovereignty or Jewish habitation of the land. When sovereignty was lost, that sanctity lapsed.

Tosfos also entertains the possibility of the reverse: the land's sanctity could endure while the *Mikdash*'s sanctity might not be assumed to endure in the same way. Both positions can be understood: the *Mikdash* sanctity, one might argue, depends on the physical structure being in place, since the structure was anointed and sanctified concretely; whereas the land itself remains the land regardless. But Rambam rules the other way, and the principle is that what defines the sanctity of the *Mikdash* is not the structure but the divine presence — and that presence, once established, remains.

## Curtains (Klayim)…

### Reconciliation Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וּמָר מַאי דִּשְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ קָאָמַר, וּמָר מַאי דִּשְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ קָאָמַר. וְכִי תֵּימָא: קְלָעִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמָה לִי? לִצְנִיעוּתָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: From where** do you draw this inference? **Perhaps everyone** maintains that **the initial consecration sanctified** the Temple and Jerusalem **for their time and** also **sanctified** them **forever. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **stated that** tradition, **which he heard** from his teachers, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua, **stated that** tradition, **which he heard** from his teachers, and there is no dispute between them. **And if you would say: Why do I** need **curtains** at all **according to Rabbi Eliezer?** The original sanctity remained when Jerusalem was not surrounded by walls, and similarly, the presence or absence of curtains is irrelevant to the sanctity of the Temple area. The Gemara answers: The curtains were established **merely for seclusion,** as it would have been unbecoming for the activity in this most sacred venue to be visible to all.

Ravina asks Rav Ashi whether the statements of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua necessarily reflect a dispute about *kedusha rishona*. The Gemara had suggested inferring from Rabbi Eliezer's emphasis on erecting *kla'im* that he holds the first sanctity did not endure — necessitating the physical demarcation of the space to re-establish it. Ravina challenges this inference: *dilma d'kholei alma kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'kidsha l'asid lavo* — perhaps everyone agrees that the first sanctity endured. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua may not be disagreeing at all; each is simply reporting what he heard. Rabbi Eliezer heard that they put up *kla'im*; Rabbi Yehoshua heard that *korbanot* could be brought even without a structure. These need not be contradictory positions. If you then ask why Rabbi Eliezer's tradition mentions the *kla'im* at all — since if the sanctity was already present the *kla'im* would not be needed to establish it — the answer is *l'tzni'uta b'alma*: they were hung for reasons of modesty and propriety, to shield the sacred space from casual view during construction, not to reconstitute its sanctity.

## First vs. Second Sanctity

### RYBRJY: Resanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לָמָּה מָנוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת אֵלּוּ? שֶׁכְּשֶׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה, מָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּם. אֲבָל רִאשׁוֹנוֹת בָּטְלוּ מִשֶּׁבָּטְלָה הָאָרֶץ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this matter is subject to a dispute between **these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the walled cities listed in the mishna in tractate *Arakhin* (32a): **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: Why did the Sages enumerate** specifically **these** nine cities as cities that were walled since the days of Joshua, son of Nun? In fact, there were many more. The reason is **that when the exiles ascended** to Eretz Yisrael from Babylonia in the time of Ezra, **they found these** cities **and consecrated them** as walled cities; **but the** sanctity of the **first** walled cities, enumerated in the book of Joshua, **was nullified when** settlement in **Eretz** Yisrael **was negated** and the Jewish people were exiled. The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **maintains: The initial consecration sanctified** the Temple and Jerusalem **for their time** only **and did not sanctify** them **forever.**

The Gemara returns to the question of which walled cities possess the sanctity of *batei arei chomah* — the status that determines laws such as the redemption of houses sold in walled cities, the removal of *metzora'im*, and others. The Mishnah lists a specific set of such cities, a notably short list. Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yose asks: why did the Sages list only these cities? He answers: because when *benei hagola* returned from exile, these were the cities they found and resanctified — *matzu elu v'kideshum*. The implication is that the first sanctity of the other walled cities — the ones not on this list — was nullified when the land was lost. *Aval rishonos batlu mishebatla ha'aretz* — but the original cities, their status was cancelled when they went into exile. This is a position that supports Rav Nachman, since it implies that resanctification was genuinely necessary and that what Ezra and Nechemya did was indeed a real sanctification, not merely a memorial.

### RYBRJY: First Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ורְמִינְהִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי אֵלּוּ בִּלְבַד הָיוּ?! וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״שִׁשִּׁים עִיר כׇּל חֶבֶל אַרְגֹּב מַמְלֶכֶת עוֹג בַּבָּשָׁן, כׇּל אֵלֶּה עָרִים בְּצֻרוֹת חוֹמָה גְבֹהָה״! אֶלָּא לָמָּה מָנוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת אֵלּוּ – שֶׁכְּשֶׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה מָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a different *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says: Were these** cities that are enumerated in tractate *Arakhin* **the only** walled cities? **But isn't it already stated: "Sixty cities, all the region of Argob, the kingdom of Og, king of Bashan, all these were cities fortified with high walls,** gates and bars" (Deuteronomy 3:4–5), indicating that there were many walled cities in the time of Joshua? **Rather, why** then **did the Sages enumerate** specifically **these** cities? It is due to the fact **that when the exiles ascended** from Babylonia **they found these and consecrated them** as walled cities.

However, the Gemara presents a second statement attributed to Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yose that is quite different. He asks: were these really the only walled cities? After all, the Torah itself tells us of *shishim ir kol chevel Argov mamlekhet Og babashan* — sixty cities in *Ever HaYarden* alone that were fortified with high walls, gates, and bars. Why is the Mishnah's list so short? He answers again: when *benei hagola* returned, they found these cities and *maneum* — they counted them, they listed them. The Gemara interrupts the quotation at the word *kideshum* (sanctified them), noting that the end of the statement is going to say that resanctification was unnecessary. So the correct reading is not that they sanctified these cities but that they found them and listed them. The broader principle follows: *aval lo elu bilvad* — the list is not exhaustive. It is an abbreviated record of what happened to be documented when the community returned. Any city for which there is a *mesorah* — a transmitted tradition — that it was surrounded by a wall from the time of Yehoshua bin Nun has all the relevant *mitzvos* applying to it, *mipnei shekedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'kidsha l'asid lavo* — because the first sanctity endured.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִידְּשׁוּם הַשְׁתָּא?! הָא אָמְרִינַן לְקַמַּן דְּלָא צְרִיכָא לְקַדּוֹשֵׁי! אֶלָּא מָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ וּמְנָאוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **They consecrated them?** If their sanctity remained, it should not have been necessary to consecrate them. **Now, don't we say later** in the same *baraita* **that it is not necessary to consecrate** them? The Gemara clarifies: **Rather,** this is what the *baraita* means to say: When the exiles ascended from Babylonia **they found these and enumerated them.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֵלּוּ בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא כׇּל שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה בְּיָדְךָ מָסוֹרֶת מֵאֲבוֹתֶיךָ שֶׁמּוּקֶּפֶת חוֹמָה מִימוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן – כׇּל מִצְוֹת אֵלּוּ נוֹהֲגוֹת בָּהּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא. קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי אַדְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And not only these, but** in **any** city with regard to **which you receive a tradition from your ancestors that it was surrounded by a wall from the days of Joshua, son of Nun, all these mitzvot** with regard to walled cities **are observed in it, due to** the fact **that the initial consecration sanctified** the Temple and Jerusalem **for their time and sanctified** them **forever.** The Gemara comments: This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei and** another statement **of Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei.**

These two statements are contradictory, and the Gemara notes the problem directly: *kashya d'Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosei ad'Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosei* — Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yosei contradicts himself. The Gemara resolves this either by attributing the two statements to different Tannaim who each transmitted a tradition in Rabbi Yishmael's name, or by noting that one of the statements may actually belong to Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose — a distinct Tanna — whose name was confused with the similar-sounding Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosei.

### REBRJ: Past Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי; אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר יוֹסֵי אַמְרַהּ. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹא חֹמָה״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עַכְשָׁיו וְהָיָה לוֹ קוֹדֶם לָכֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that there are **two *tanna'im*** who disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei.** Each transmitted Rabbi Yishmael's opinion in a different manner. And **if you wish, say** instead that one of the traditions is mistaken, as **one of the** *baraitot* **was stated by Rabbi Elazar bar Yosei,** and not his brother, Rabbi Yishmael. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar bar Yosei says** that the verse with regard to walled cities states: **"Which has [*lo*] a wall"** (Leviticus 25:30). The word *lo* is written with an *alef*, meaning no, and accordingly the verse would be stating to the contrary, that the city does not have a wall, but its vocalization is in the sense of its homonym, *lo* with a *vav*, meaning that it has a wall. This indicates that **even if it does not presently have** a wall **but it had** a wall **previously,** it retains its status as a walled city. It is Rabbi Elazar bar Yosei who maintains that the first consecration sanctified the Temple and Jerusalem forever.

Evidence for the attribution to Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose comes from an independent *Baraisa*. The verse regarding *batei arei chomah* uses the spelling *asher lo choma* — where the word *lo* is written with an *aleph*, meaning "without a wall," whereas the contextual meaning requires the word *lo* with a *vav*, meaning "that has a wall." Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose derives from this orthographic irregularity: *af al pi she'ein lo achshav v'hayah lo kodem lachen* — even if the city does not have a wall now, if it had one previously — from the time of Yehoshua bin Nun — it retains the relevant sanctity and all the associated *halachos* apply to it. This teaching is fully consistent with the view that *kedusha rishona* endures, and that a city's status is determined by its condition at the time of the original sanctification, not its current physical state.

The weight of Tannaic opinion, then, appears to fall on the side that the first sanctity — of the *Beis HaMikdash*, of Jerusalem, and of *batei arei chomah* — endures and that what was performed at the time of Ezra was either a *zeicher* with no independent *halachic* force, or a genuine resanctification that required only one component rather than all. The minority position, which supports Rav Nachman, holds that the first sanctity lapsed entirely and that Ezra's resanctification was real and legally operative — a position supported by at least one version of Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yose.

## Tumah B'Azarah

### Staying = Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא בָּעֲזָרָה וְנֶעֶלְמָה מִמֶּנּוּ טוּמְאָה וְכוּ׳. טוּמְאָה בַּעֲזָרָה מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״אֶת מִשְׁכַּן ה׳ טִמֵּא״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי אֶת מִקְדַּשׁ ה׳ טִמֵּא״; אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְטוּמְאָה שֶׁבַּחוּץ, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְטוּמְאָה שֶׁבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches about one who was ritually pure when he entered the Temple but **who became impure** while **in the** Temple **courtyard, and** afterward his **impurity was hidden from him.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that one is liable even if he contracted **impurity in the** Temple **courtyard** and failed to leave immediately by way of the shortest route, and not only if he entered the Temple courtyard when he was already impure? **Rabbi Elazar says: One verse states: "He has defiled the Tabernacle of the Lord"** (Numbers 19:13), **and one verse states: "For he has defiled the Temple of the Lord"** (Numbers 19:20). **If** this second verse **is not** needed for the **matter of** one who contracted **impurity outside** before he entered the Temple courtyard, as this situation is already referred to in the previous verse, **apply it** to the **matter of** one who contracted **impurity inside** the Temple courtyard.

The Gemara now transitions to a new topic that is closely related to the overall theme of *kedusha* and its *halachic* consequences. The Mishnah establishes that someone who becomes *tamei* while already inside the *Azarah* is liable — they must bring a *korban* — if they remain there long enough to perform a *hishtachavaya*, or if they actually prostrate themselves, or if they take a circuitous route out rather than the most direct path. The principle is that remaining in the *Mikdash* while *tamei* constitutes a violation equivalent in character to entering while *tamei*, even though the person did not enter in a state of *tumah* — they became *tamei* inside.

### Dual Verses Teaching

The Gemara asks: *tumah b'Azarah minalan* — what is the source that this prohibition applies, and specifically what is the source that it incurs *kares*? Since the *korban* brought for this violation is linked to the *kares* that would be incurred for deliberate transgression, the Gemara needs to establish that remaining while *tamei* is a *kares*-bearing offense, not merely a prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָאֵי מְיַתְּרִי?! הָא מִיצְרָךְ צְרִיכִי! דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִם נֶאֱמַר ״מִשְׁכָּן״, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״מִקְדָּשׁ״? וְאִם נֶאֱמַר ״מִקְדָּשׁ״, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״מִשְׁכָּן״? אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״מִשְׁכָּן״ וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״מִקְדָּשׁ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: עַל מִשְׁכָּן יְהֵא חַיָּיב – שֶׁהֲרֵי מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, וְעַל מִקְדָּשׁ לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב. וְאִם נֶאֱמַר ״מִקְדָּשׁ״ וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״מִשְׁכָּן״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: עַל מִקְדָּשׁ יְהֵא חַיָּיב – שֶׁהֲרֵי קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ קְדוּשַּׁת עוֹלָם, וְעַל מִשְׁכָּן לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב. לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״מִשְׁכָּן״, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״מִקְדָּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is it really **superfluous** to have both of these **verses,** one having been sufficient? **Aren't they** each **necessary** to teach a novel ruling? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar says: If it is stated: "Tabernacle," why does it state: "Temple"? And if it is stated: "Temple," why does it state: "Tabernacle"?** He explains: **Had** the verse **stated** only: **"Tabernacle," and not stated: "Temple," I would have said** that one is **liable for** entering **the Tabernacle** in a state of impurity, **since it was anointed with the anointing oil,** and therefore it carries greater sanctity, **but he is not liable for** entering **the Temple,** which was not anointed with the anointing oil. **And had** the verse **stated** only: **"Temple," and not stated: "Tabernacle," I would have said** that one is **liable for** entering **the Temple** in a state of impurity, **since its sanctity is an eternal sanctity, but he is not liable for** entering **the Tabernacle,** whose sanctity was only for its time. **For this reason, it is stated: "Tabernacle," and for this reason, it is stated: "Temple."**

Rabbi Elazar points to two verses. One states: *es mishkan Hashem tima v'nichrasah* — he defiled God's *Mishkan* and shall be cut off. The other states: *ki es mikdash Hashem tima v'nichrasah* — he defiled God's *Mikdash* and shall be cut off. One of these two verses is seemingly redundant with respect to the prohibition of entering while *tamei* — since that prohibition could be established by a single verse. The redundant verse is therefore available to extend the prohibition to a new case: remaining in the *Mikdash* while *tamei* even without having entered in that state.

### Must Say Both Terms

The Gemara challenges this: perhaps neither verse is redundant, because both are necessary — one for the *Mishkan* and one for the *Mikdash*, and neither can be inferred from the other. This is established through a *Baraisa* in which Rabbi Eliezer explains the indispensability of each term.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: מִכְּדִי מִשְׁכָּן אִיקְּרִי ״מִקְדָּשׁ״ וּמִקְדָּשׁ אִיקְּרִי ״מִשְׁכָּן״; נִכְתּוֹב אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״מִקְדָּשׁ״, אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״מִשְׁכָּן״! ״מִשְׁכָּן״ וּ״מִקְדָּשׁ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Elazar** did not base his derivation on a superfluous verse, but on the change in wording between the two verses. **This is what is difficult for him: Since the Tabernacle is** also **called Temple and the Temple is** also **called Tabernacle, then let** the verse **write either** in **both** verses: **Temple, or** in **both** verses: **Tabernacle; why do I** need both **"Tabernacle" and "Temple"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** i.e., that one is liable even for impurity contracted inside the grounds, and that the *halakha* applies both in the Tabernacle and in the Temple.

The Gemara therefore resolves the difficulty elegantly: there are two separate *derashos* operating simultaneously. The switch in terminology between *Mishkan* and *Mikdash* teaches that one cannot be inferred from the other — both must be stated explicitly. The redundancy — the fact that both verses speak of defilement and *kares* — teaches that even remaining without having entered incurs *kares*. Both *derashos* are independently valid and neither undermines the other.

## Sanctity Endures…

### When Both Terms Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִקְדָּשׁ אִיקְּרִי מִשְׁכָּן – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַתִּי מִשְׁכָּנִי בְּתוֹכְכֶם״; אֶלָּא מִשְׁכָּן דְּאִיקְּרִי מִקְדָּשׁ – מְנָלַן? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָסְעוּ הַקְּהָתִים נֹשְׂאֵי הַמִּקְדָּשׁ״ – הָהוּא בְּאָרוֹן כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וְעָשׂוּ לִי מִקְדָּשׁ וְשָׁכַנְתִּי בְּתוֹכָם״, וּכְתִיב: ״כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי מַרְאֶה אוֹתְךָ אֵת תַּבְנִית הַמִּשְׁכָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions its previous assumption: **Granted, the Temple is** also **called Tabernacle, as it is written: "And I will set My Tabernacle among you"** (Leviticus 26:11), where the reference is to the Temple, as the verse is referring to that which will transpire after the Jewish people have settled in their land. **But from where do we** derive **that the Tabernacle is** also **called Temple? If we say** that it is derived **from that which is written: "And the Kehatites, the bearers of the Temple, set forward"** (Numbers 10:21), **that** instance of the term Temple **is** not written with regard to the Tabernacle; rather, it is **written with regard to the Ark** and the other sacred vessels. **Rather,** it is derived **from here: "And let them make Me a Temple, that I may dwell among them"** (Exodus 25:8), where the reference is to the Tabernacle, as immediately afterward **it is written: "According to all that I show you, the pattern of the Tabernacle"** (Exodus 25:9).

Tosfos asks a broader structural question: why does the Gemara in some contexts need to derive that a particular *halacha* applies equally to both the *Mishkan* and the *Mikdash*, while in other contexts it is simply assumed that what applies to one applies to the other? Tosfos's answer is that the distinction turns on the nature of the *halacha* in question. When the *halacha* concerns the *kedusha* of the space itself — the degree of sanctity, the *kareis*-bearing prohibitions associated with entry — then one genuinely cannot assume that the *Mishkan* and the *Mikdash* are equivalent. The *Mishkan* was directly anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*, its physical beams consecrated in a concrete and immediate way. The *Mikdash*, by contrast, was sanctified through the enduring divine presence — *kedushas olam* in the sense that the sanctity attaches to the ground permanently. These are different modes of sanctification, and one cannot infer the degree of one from the degree of the other. When the question is about something more procedural — such as the rule that *korbanot* may only be brought when the gates are open — that is a functional matter, and since the same functions apply in both settings, what applies to one automatically applies to the other.

## Prostration & Time

### Inward Bow Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁהָה כְּדֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה כְּלַפֵּי פְנִים, אֲבָל הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה כְּלַפֵּי חוּץ – שָׁהָה אִין, לֹא שָׁהָה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **he bowed down, or he tarried** in the Temple courtyard **long enough to bow down,** or he went out by way of a longer route when he could have taken a shorter route, he is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. **Rava says: They taught** this **only when he bowed down** in the Temple courtyard **facing inward,** toward the Holy of Holies, as that alone is proper bowing. **But** if **he bowed down facing outward,** that is not considered bowing. When he bows facing outward, if **he tarried** in the Temple courtyard long enough to bow down, **yes,** he is liable, but if **he did not tarry** long enough to bow down, he is **not** liable.

Rava addresses a tension within the Mishnah. The Mishnah says one is liable if one performs a *hishtachavaya* or if one waits long enough to perform a *hishtachavaya*. If the time measure is defined by the duration of a *hishtachavaya*, then the act of *hishtachavaya* itself would always satisfy the time criterion — making the explicit mention of the act seemingly redundant. Rava resolves this by distinguishing between two types of *hishtachavaya* based on direction. *Lo shanu ella shehishtachavah klapei pnim* — the rule that one is liable for the act of *hishtachavaya* even without waiting the full time applies specifically when one bows down facing inward, toward the *kodesh*, which is the recognized and meaningful direction. That constitutes a formal *hishtachavaya* regardless of how quickly it is performed.

### Outward Bow Time Required

*Aval hishtachavah klapei chutz* — if one bows down facing outward, away from the *kodesh*, that is not recognized as a formal *hishtachavaya*. In that case, liability depends solely on the amount of time one remains: *she'eino ella shahah* — it is treated as mere staying. Paradoxically, bowing outward is disrespectful — yet because it is not halachically recognized as a *hishtachavaya*, a person who does so quickly and immediately exits may not be liable, having neither performed a recognized act nor waited the required time. The Gemara notes the two possible ways to attach Rava's statement to the Mishnah — either to the clause mentioning *hishtachavaya* or to the clause about waiting — but in either reading the substance of his ruling is the same.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא: אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁהָה בִּכְדֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה – מִכְּלַל דְּהִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה גּוּפַהּ בָּעֲיָא שְׁהִיָּיה; אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה כְּלַפֵּי חוּץ, אֲבָל כְּלַפֵּי פְנִים – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא שָׁהָה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה כְּלַפֵּי פְנִים, אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁהָה כְּדֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה בְּהָךְ הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה דִּכְלַפֵּי חוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who teach** the statement of Rava **with regard to the latter clause: Or** if **he tarried** in the Temple courtyard **long enough to bow down,** he is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. It may be said **by inference that bowing down itself entails tarrying,** and the mere act of bowing does not render one liable. With regard to this **Rava says: They taught** this **only when he bowed down facing outward.** In such a case, he is liable only if he tarried long enough to bow down. **But** if he bowed down **facing inward** toward the Holy of Holies, he is liable **even if he did not tarry** for that length of time. **And this is** what the mishna **is saying:** If **he bowed down facing inward, or** if he **tarried long enough to bow down while** he was **bowing down facing outward,** he is **liable.**

### Standard Time Unit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה דְּאִית בַּהּ שְׁהִיָּיה, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה דְּלֵית בַּהּ שְׁהִיָּיה? דְּלֵית בַּהּ שְׁהִיָּיה – זוֹ כְּרִיעָה בְּעָלְמָא הִיא, דְּאִית בָּהּ שְׁהִיָּיה – פִּישּׁוּט יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of bowing in which there is tarrying, and what are the circumstances of bowing in which there is no tarrying?** The Gemara answers: With regard to bowing **in which there is no tarrying, that is merely kneeling.** As for bowing **in which there is tarrying,** that is prostrating oneself while **spreading** one's **arms and legs** in total submission.

The Gemara asks: *v'kama shiur shehiya* — exactly how long is the required waiting time? This was disputed between Rav Yitzchak bar Nachmani and his colleague — identified in one version as Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi and in another as Rabbi Shimon bar Nachmani. One holds that the time is equivalent to the recitation of the following verse in full; the other holds it is only from the midpoint of the verse onward. The verse is from the dedication of the *Beis HaMikdash* (2 Divrei Hayamim 7:3): *v'chol b'nei Yisrael ro'im b'redes ha'esh u'chvod Hashem al habayis vayichru apayim artza al haritzpah vayishtachavu v'hodos l'Hashem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo* — "And all the children of Israel saw the fire descending and the glory of God upon the House, and they knelt with their faces to the ground on the pavement and they bowed down and gave thanks to God, for He is good, for His kindness endures forever." The full verse takes only a few seconds to recite. The dispute is whether the standard is the entire verse or only the second half, beginning with *vayichru*. In either case, the time involved is measured in seconds — two, three, four at most. If one remains in the *Azarah* while *tamei* for that brief duration, one has transgressed; if one bows down in the proper direction, liability attaches immediately regardless of duration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר שְׁהִיָּיה? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי וְחַד דְּעִימֵּיהּ, וּמַנּוּ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי; וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי וְחַד דְּעִימֵּיהּ, וּמַנּוּ – רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר נַחְמָנִי. חַד אָמַר: כְּמֵימְרֵיהּ דְּהַאי פְּסוּקָא, וְחַד אָמַר: כְּמִ״וַּיִכְרְעוּ״ לְסֵיפָא. ״וְכֹל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל רֹאִים בְּרֶדֶת הָאֵשׁ וּכְבוֹד ה׳ עַל הַבָּיִת, וַיִּכְרְעוּ אַפַּיִם אַרְצָה עַל הָרִצְפָה, וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ וְהוֹדוֹת לַה׳ כִּי טוֹב כִּי לְעוֹלָם חַסְדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the measure of** that **tarrying? Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani and one** other Sage **who was with him disagree about this. And who is** that other scholar? **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi. And some say** that it was **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi and one** other Sage **who was with him. And who is** that other scholar? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani. And some say** it was **Rabbi Shimon bar Naḥmani. One** Sage **says:** It is as long **as** the time it takes **to recite this** following **verse** in its entirety, **and one** Sage **says:** It is as long **as** the time it takes to recite **from** the term **"and they bowed"** in the middle of that verse **until the end.** What is the verse? **"And when all the children of Israel saw how the fire came down, and the glory of the Lord upon the House, and they bowed with their faces to the ground upon the pavement, and prostrated themselves, and praised the Lord, saying: For He is good, for His steadfast love endures forever"** (II Chronicles 7:3).

### Three Bow Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קִידָּה – עַל אַפַּיִם, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַתִּקֹּד בַּת שֶׁבַע אַפַּיִם אֶרֶץ״. כְּרִיעָה – עַל בִּרְכַּיִם, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מִכְּרֹעַ עַל בִּרְכָּיו״. הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה – זוֹ פִּישּׁוּט יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, וְכֵן אוֹמֵר: ״הֲבוֹא נָבוֹא אֲנִי וְאִמְּךָ וְאַחֶיךָ לְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֹת לְךָ אָרְצָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the different forms of bowing in greater detail. **The Sages taught:** The term ***kidda*** indicates falling **upon** one's **face, and so** the verse **states: "Then Bathsheba bowed [*vatikkod*] with her face to the ground"** (I Kings 1:31). ***Keria*** means descending **upon** one's **knees, and so** the verse **states** with regard to Solomon: "He rose from before the altar of the Lord, **from kneeling [*mikero'a*] upon his knees"** (I Kings 8:54). ***Hishtaḥava'a*, this** is prostrating oneself while **spreading **one's **arms and legs** in total submission, **and so** the verse **states** that Jacob asked, in response to Joseph's relating of his dream: **"Shall I and your mother and your brothers indeed come to bow down [*lehishtaḥavot*] to you to the ground"** (Genesis 37:10), i.e., spread out completely on the ground.

A *Baraisa* at the close of the daf delineates three distinct forms of bowing and prostration:

*Kidah* refers to prostration with the face pressed to the ground — *al apayim*. The verse cited is *vatikod Bas-sheva apayim aretz* — and Batsheva prostrated herself with her face to the ground.

*Kriah* refers to kneeling — *al birkayim*, a bowing that involves only going down on one's knees. The verse cited is *mikroa al birkav* — he arose from kneeling on his knees.

*Hishtachavaya* refers to the intermediate position: arms and legs outstretched — *pishut yadayim v'raglayim* — with the body fully extended, but not necessarily with the face pressed to the ground. The verse cited is *havo navo ani v'imcha v'achecha l'hishtachavot l'cha artzah* — shall we indeed come, I and your mother and your brothers, to bow down to you to the ground.

Tosfos notes that in some contexts *hishtachavaya* is described as including the face being directed toward the ground, but the essential definition is the fully outstretched position. These three forms can be visualized as progressive stages: first kneeling (*kriah*), then full prostration with arms extended (*hishtachavaya*), then placing the face against the ground (*kidah*) — a sequence visible in forms of prostration practiced to this day.
