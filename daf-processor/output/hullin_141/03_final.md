# Hullin 141 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hovering & Contact

### Rovetzet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיְתָה מְעוֹפֶפֶת, בִּזְמַן שֶׁכְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּחַ, אֵין כְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ. אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא אֶפְרוֹחַ אֶחָד אוֹ בֵּיצָה אַחַת – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״קַן״, קֵן מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the mother bird **was hovering** over the eggs or fledglings in the nest, **when its wings are touching** the eggs or fledglings **in the nest,** one is **obligated to send** away the mother. When its wings **are not touching** the eggs or fledglings **in the nest,** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother. Even if **there is only one fledgling or one egg,** one is **obligated to send** away the mother, **as it is stated:** "If a bird's **nest** happens before you" (Deuteronomy 22:6), indicating that one is obligated to send away the mother bird from the **nest in any case.**

The daf opens in the middle of the laws of *shiluach haken*, and the mishna before us addresses the precise physical conditions under which the obligation applies. The verse uses the word *rovetzet* — literally, crouching down — and from this the Talmud derives that the mother bird must be in a specific positional relationship to the eggs or chicks. *Rovetzet* implies not merely proximity but a definitive orientation: the bird must be directly over, not merely near, the nest.

### Wings Touching Nest

The mishna then addresses the case of a bird that is hovering (*me'ofefes*) rather than sitting. The rule is: *bizman she-knafeha nog'os baken* — when the bird's wings are actually touching the nest, one is obligated to send her away. If she is hovering high enough that her wings do not touch the nest, one is exempt. The operative criterion is thus physical contact between wing and nest, which serves as the legal surrogate for the crouching posture the verse describes.

### Single Chick/Egg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שָׁם אֶפְרוֹחִים מַפְרִיחִים, אוֹ בֵּיצִים מוּזָרוֹת – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהָאֵם רֹבֶצֶת עַל הָאֶפְרֹחִים אוֹ עַל הַבֵּיצִים״, מָה אֶפְרוֹחִים בְּנֵי קַיָּימָא, אַף בֵּיצִים בְּנֵי קַיָּימָא, יָצְאוּ מוּזָרוֹת. וּמָה הַבֵּיצִים צְרִיכִין לְאִמָּן, אַף הָאֶפְרוֹחִין צְרִיכִין לְאִמָּן, יָצְאוּ מַפְרִיחִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **there were fledglings** capable of **flying, or unfertilized eggs** from which a fledgling will not hatch, one is **exempt from sending** away the mother bird from the nest, **as it is stated** in the same verse: **"And the mother is resting upon the fledglings or upon the eggs."** From the juxtaposition of the fledglings and the eggs one derives: **Just as the fledglings are living, so too, the eggs** must **be** capable of producing **living** fledglings. This **excludes unfertilized** eggs, which cannot produce a living fledgling. **And** furthermore, **just as the eggs need their mothers** to hatch them, **so too, the fledglings** must be those that **need their mothers.** This **excludes** fledglings that are capable of **flying.**

The mishna further clarifies that even if there is only one chick or one egg present, one is still obligated. The verse says *kan* — a nest — *mikol makom*, from wherever it may be found, and this inclusive language absorbs even a single occupant. Although the verse uses the plural forms *efrochim* (chicks) and *beitzim* (eggs), the derivation from *kan* establishes that the plural is not a limiting condition.

### Fertilized & Dependent

The mishna proceeds to qualify what counts as a valid chick or egg for purposes of the obligation. If the chicks present are already able to fly (*mafrichim*), or if the eggs are unfertilized (*muzaros*), one is exempt. The textual basis is the verse's pairing of *efrochim* and *beitzim* — chicks and eggs — which the mishna reads as a reciprocal analogy. Just as chicks must be *bnei kayama* (viable, living creatures), so too eggs must be capable of producing life, excluding unfertilized eggs. And conversely, just as eggs require their mother's care, so too chicks must still need their mother — excluding those already capable of independent flight. It is worth noting that the term *kayama* is Aramaic rather than Hebrew, which is itself somewhat unusual, and there is also the relatively rare feature of a *drasha* being embedded within the mishna itself.

## Rovetzet Drasha

### Rovetzet vs. Yoshevet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״רוֹבֶצֶת״ – וְלֹא מְעוֹפֶפֶת. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ כְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״רוֹבֶצֶת״. מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? מִדְּלָא כְּתִיב ״יוֹשֶׁבֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna discusses the case of a mother bird that hovers over the nest. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse teaches that one is obligated to send away the mother bird only when it is at rest, as it states: "And the mother is **resting** upon the fledglings" (Deuteronomy 22:6), **and not** when it is **hovering.** One **might** have thought that one is exempt **even** if the mother is hovering and **its wings are touching the nest.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Resting,"** indicating that in fact one is obligated in the mitzva in such a case. The Gemara asks: **What is the** biblical **derivation,** i.e., how is this derived from the word "resting"? The Gemara responds: It is derived **from** the fact **that** the word **sitting is not written** in the verse. Although a bird whose wings are touching the nest is not sitting upon it, such a bird is considered to be resting upon it.

The Gemara takes up the question of why the Torah uses the word *rovetzet* (crouching) rather than *yoshevet* (sitting). The baraita states: *rovetzet*, not *me'ofefes* — hovering is excluded. One might therefore think that even if the bird is hovering very close to the nest with her wings touching it, she would be exempt. The baraita rejects this, invoking the very word *rovetzet* as the inclusive term.

The Gemara asks: where exactly do we see that *rovetzet* includes close hovering? The answer is *midelo ketiv yoshevet* — from the fact that the Torah did not say *yoshevet*, sitting, but chose instead *rovetzet*, crouching. When one crouches, one's body is lowered toward something without necessarily making contact with it from below. This implies a posture that can encompass hovering, provided it is sufficiently close. Sitting on the nest is certainly included; hovering with wings touching the nest is also included under *rovetzet*.

### Two Branches Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָיְתָה יוֹשֶׁבֶת בֵּין שְׁנֵי רוֹבְדֵי אִילָן – רוֹאִים, כֹּל שֶׁאִם תִּשָּׁמֵט נוֹפֶלֶת עֲלֵיהֶם – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּחַ, וְאִם לָאו – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If the mother bird **was sitting** above the eggs or fledglings **between two tree branches,** one **looks** at the following factor in order to determine whether the obligation to send away the mother applies: In **any** case **where if** the branches were to separate, the bird **would slip** between them and **fall upon** the eggs or fledglings, one is **obligated to send** away the mother bird. **And if** the bird would **not** fall upon them, but to the sides, one is **exempt** from the mitzva of sending away the mother bird.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav then presents a case: the mother bird is sitting on two parallel branches (*rovdei ilan*), with eggs directly below her. She is neither flying nor crouching directly on the eggs — she is perched on branches with an air gap between her body and the eggs below. The rule formulated is: *ro'in* — we observe — if, were you to remove the branches, she would fall directly onto the eggs, then she is considered *rovetzet* and the obligation applies. If not, she is exempt.

This is a notable ruling. It suggests that physical continuity — defined by the gravitational relationship between bird and eggs — serves as the legal test for *rovetzet*, even when there is no actual bodily contact. One might initially wonder whether this applies even if the bird is very high above the eggs, but the more natural reading of the rule is that the only question being resolved here is whether the intervening branches constitute a *hefsek* (division). Given what we have already established — that the bird's wings must be able to touch — it is reasonable to assume close proximity is still required, and that the novel ruling concerns the legal significance of the branches rather than the question of height.

### Between vs. On Top

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיְתָה יוֹשֶׁבֶת בֵּינֵיהֶן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ, עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּחַ, הָיְתָה מְעוֹפֶפֶת, אֲפִילּוּ כְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rav from that which was taught in a *baraita*: If the mother bird **was sitting among** the eggs or the fledglings, one is **exempt from sending** it away, as the verse states: "And the mother is resting upon the fledglings, or upon the eggs" (Deuteronomy 22:6), i.e., upon and not among. If it was sitting **above them,** one is **obligated to send** it away. If it **was hovering, even** if **its wings are touching the nest,** one is **exempt from sending** it away.

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a baraita. The baraita distinguishes three scenarios. If the bird is sitting *beineihen* — between the eggs — she is exempt, because she is not situated over them. If she is sitting *al gabeihen* — on top of them — she is obligated. And if she is *me'ofefes* — hovering — even if her wings touch the nest, she is exempt.

The last clause, of course, directly contradicts our mishna. Setting aside that conflict for a moment, the Gemara focuses on the first two clauses. A natural reading would be: just as *beineihen* means the bird is touching the eggs from the side, so *al gabeihen* (obligated) means the bird is touching from above. By parallel, one might conclude that sitting on branches without touching would be exempt. But the Gemara rejects this reading. The parallel between *beineihen* and *al gabeihen* should instead be read as follows: just as *beineihen* means the bird is not touching the tops of the eggs (she may be touching their sides, but that does not constitute being *over* them), so *al gabeihen* in the obligated case means the bird need not touch the tops of the eggs either — she need only be above them. And that, says the Gemara, is precisely the case of the parallel branches: she is over them without touching their tops, and she is obligated. This reading would support Rav Yehuda's ruling about the branches.

### Hovering Baraita Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן, דּוּמְיָא דְּבֵינֵיהֶן: מָה בֵּינֵיהֶן – דְּנָגְעָה בְּהוּ, אַף עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן – דְּנָגְעָה בְּהוּ, אֲבָל רוֹבְדֵי אִילָן – פָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** that the case of sitting **above them** is **similar to** the case of sitting **between them?** The Gemara elaborates: **Just as** in the case of sitting **between them** one is exempt **where she is touching them, so too,** in the case of sitting **above them** one is obligated **where she is touching them.** If so, conclude from this *baraita* that one is obligated only if the bird is touching the fledglings or eggs, **but** if it is sitting between two **tree branches** above the eggs or fledglings without touching them, one is **exempt** from sending her away.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן דּוּמְיָא דְּבֵינֵיהֶן: מָה בֵּינֵיהֶן – דְּלֹא נָגְעָה עֲלַיְיהוּ, אַף עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן – דְּלֹא נָגְעָה עֲלַיְיהוּ, וְהַיְינוּ רוֹבְדֵי אִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No;** actually, the case of sitting **above them** is **similar to** that of sitting **between them** in the following manner: **Just as** the case of sitting **between them** is one **where she is not touching them** from **above,** but from the side, **so too,** the case of sitting **above them** is one **where she is not touching them** from **above.** Accordingly, the *baraita* teaches that one is obligated to send away the bird if it is resting on branches above the nest, even though it is not touching the eggs or fledglings. **And that is** the case where the mother is sitting between two **tree branches.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ רוֹבְדֵי אִילָן פָּטוּר – אַדְּתָנֵי: הָיְתָה מְעוֹפֶפֶת, אֲפִילּוּ כְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ, לִיתְנֵי רוֹבְדֵי אִילָן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן מְעוֹפֶפֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This, too, stands to reason,** that one is obligated to send away a mother bird that is sitting between two tree branches above the nest. **As, if** it should **enter your mind** that one is **exempt** when the mother bird is sitting between two **tree branches,** then one may ask: **Rather than teaching** that if it **was hovering, even** if **its wings are touching the nest,** one is **exempt from sending** it away, **let** the *baraita* **teach** that one is exempt in the case of a mother bird that is sitting between two **tree branches, and all the more so** if the mother **is hovering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעוֹפֶפֶת אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ, דַּאֲפִילּוּ כְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: Perhaps one is exempt from sending the mother bird if it is resting between two tree branches. Nevertheless, it **was necessary** for the *tanna* to teach the case of **a hovering** bird, in order to teach **that even** if **its wings are touching the nest,** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother bird. Had the *tanna* taught the exemption in a case of a bird resting between two tree branches, one might have thought that this is because the bird's wings are not touching the nest.

However, the Gemara then notes a problem with this line of inference. If the case of parallel branches yields an obligation, as just argued, then the baraita's final clause — that hovering, even with wing-contact, is exempt — is a lesser novelty than the branch case. If sitting on branches exempts, hovering certainly would. Why does the baraita not cite the more striking case of branches and then derive the hovering case by *kal va-homer*?

The Gemara responds: *me'ofefet itztricha lei* — the baraita needed to address hovering specifically, because hovering has a feature the branch case lacks: there is an element of actual physical contact when the wings touch the nest. One might have thought that this contact is sufficient to create an obligation even if the sitting-on-branches case would not. The baraita therefore explicitly tells us that hovering with wing-contact is still exempt. This reasoning means the baraita neither confirms nor denies what the rule is for parallel branches — that case remains unresolved from this baraita.

The Gemara then attempts the same inference in reverse (*ika d'amri*, some try to support Rav Yehuda's ruling), reading the baraita's structure as a proof rather than a challenge, and arrives at the same impasse: the baraita does not explicitly address the branch case, and the same argument about why hovering had to be mentioned applies in the same way. The parallel branches case therefore remains open.

### Side vs. Top Touch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁכְּנָפֶיהָ נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקֵּן – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּחַ! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כִּי קָתָנֵי מַתְנִיתִין – בְּנוֹגֵעַ מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now questions the *baraita* itself: How can the *baraita* state that even if the hovering bird's wings are touching the nest, one is exempt from sending the bird away? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **When its wings are touching the nest,** one is **obligated to send** away the mother? **Rav Yehuda said: When** the case of the hovering mother bird **is taught** in **the *baraita*,** it is referring **to** a bird **touching** the nest **from the side.** In such a case, one is exempt because its wings are not touching the nest from above. By contrast, the mishna is referring to a case where the bird is hovering directly above the nest and touching the nest with its wings from above.

The Gemara returns to reconcile the baraita's claim — that hovering with wings touching is exempt — with our mishna's claim that it is obligated. Rabbi Yirmiya resolves the contradiction: our mishna, when it says the wings touch and one is obligated, refers to a case where the wings touch from above, *min hatzad* in the sense of the tops of the eggs or chicks. The baraita, when it says wings touching the nest and one is exempt, refers to touching only from the side.

This resolution requires a reinterpretation of the word *kan* (nest) in the mishna. At this stage, Rashi reads *min hatzad* as the wings touching the *tzideihen* — the sides — as opposed to touching *al gabeihen* — the tops. The distinction being drawn, then, is not merely between nest and eggs, but between side-contact and top-contact. Being beside the eggs — whether physically sitting between them or hovering with wings brushing their sides — does not constitute *rovetzet al*. Only if the bird is positioned above in such a way that her body or wings make contact with or pass over the tops of the eggs or chicks does she qualify as *rovetzet* for purposes of the obligation.

## Ta'amei Hamitzvos

### Rachamim Concern

The formalism of these distinctions — crouching versus hovering, touching from above versus from the side, parallel branches, wing-contact — prompts a natural question about the relationship between the technical structure of the law and the values that seem to underlie *shiluach haken*. After all, the verse about sending away the mother bird seems to many to be paradigmatically transparent in its moral logic. Yet the Gemara is emphatic about not reducing the *mitzvah* to a simple compassion rationale.

The Gemara in Berachot states explicitly: if someone says *al kan tzippor yagi'u rachamecha* — "Your mercies extend to the bird's nest" — in the context of prayer, *mashtakin oto*, they are silenced. One explanation given is *she-oseh gezeirosav shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu rachamim*, that this treats God's commandments as expressions of compassion when in fact they are decrees. This is a striking and counterintuitive position for a *mitzvah* that seems, on its face, to be obviously about compassion.

### Three Interpretations

The Rishonim offer three quite different accounts of the *mitzvah*'s purpose, and it is worth observing how divergent they are. The Rambam, who generally holds that *mitzvot* do have reasons and that we should try to understand them, takes the position that the silencing of those who invoke compassion reflects only an early Tannaitic view that *ta'amei hamitzvot* should not be sought — a view we do not rule according to. He maintains that *shiluach haken* is, in fact, about compassion for animals.

The Ramban, however, argues that the *mitzvah* is not about compassion for the bird as such. Sending the mother away while her eggs are taken is not obviously compassionate — it could be argued that swifter *shechita* would be less distressing. Rather, the Ramban holds that God cares about human beings, and this law is a *gezeirah* in the sense of a royal decree, because the act of taking both mother and offspring together breeds cruelty and callousness in the person who does it. The parallel to *oso v'es beno* — the prohibition of slaughtering a parent and offspring on the same day — reinforces this reading.

The Sefer HaChinuch offers a still different account, one less concerned with moral psychology and more with theology. He understands the *mitzvah* as reinforcing our recognition of *hashgacha* — divine providence and care for the world. Seeing that God does not wish the annihilation of an entire animal family cultivates in us a sense of *hashgacha pratis*, God's ongoing involvement in the details of creation.

### Formalism vs. Values

This diversity of explanations — compassion for animals, moral formation of human character, theological recognition of divine providence — makes the apparent transparency of the *mitzvah* considerably less self-evident. And it sharpens the question of why the Gemara itself seems so deliberately to keep *ta'amei hamitzvot* at arm's length, pursuing instead highly technical formal distinctions about the precise angle of wing-contact and the gravitational implications of parallel branches.

The concern is a genuine one that has been articulated in the Gemara in Sanhedrin: if one focuses too heavily on the purpose of a law, one risks concluding that the purpose can be satisfied without adhering to its particulars. The classic illustration is Shlomo HaMelech, who reasoned that he could multiply wives without going astray. If the purpose is what matters, the formal provisions become negotiable — and that is precisely what the halakhic tradition resists. There is a general principle of *darshinan ta'ama dikra* versus *lo darshinan ta'ama dikra* — whether one reasons from the purpose of a verse — and the conditions under which purpose may guide the application of law are carefully bounded.

What remains genuinely difficult, however, is not that the Gemara is formalistic — the Gemara is always formalistic, even in domains where no value-laden reading is remotely available. The challenge specific to *shiluach haken* is that the formal legal analysis does not appear to be in any serious conversation with the values that are presumably at stake. One can readily construct scenarios, as the Gemara itself nearly does, in which strict adherence to the formal parameters allows the underlying value to be entirely circumvented — capturing the mother only when she is not hovering, taking the eggs, and thereby destroying the family unit without transgressing a single provision. The Gemara does not seem troubled by this, which is at minimum an accurate description of where things stand.

## Multiple Sending Away

### Repeated Returns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׁלְּחָהּ וְחָזְרָה, אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה פְּעָמִים – חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁלֵּחַ תְּשַׁלַּח אֶת הָאֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one sent** away the mother bird **and it returned** to rest on the eggs, **even** if it returned **four or five times,** one is **obligated** to send it away again, **as it is stated: "You shall send [*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*] the mother"** (Deuteronomy 22:7). The doubled verb indicates that one must send away the mother bird multiple times if needed.

The mishna states: *shalcha ve-chazra afilu arba va-chamishah pe'amim chayav* — if you sent the mother away and she returned, even four or five times, you remain obligated to send her away again. The verse's formulation *shaleach tishalach* — the doubled language of sending — conveys an unlimited obligation that continues as long as the mother bird returns to the nest in a *rovetzet* configuration.

A student suggested that the scenario of her returning might envision a case where you have not yet taken the eggs — the bird was sent away but flew back before you had the chance to take them, recreating the situation of *ha'em rovetzet al ha'efrochim*. This reading is probably correct, since if you had already taken the eggs, the bird returning to an empty nest would not recreate the triggering condition. The mishna is thus addressing a case of multiple consecutive returns before the eggs are taken.

### Mother Choice Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: הֲרֵינִי נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאֵם וּמְשַׁלֵּחַ אֶת הַבָּנִים – חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁלֵּחַ תְּשַׁלַּח אֶת הָאֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** If one **said: I am hereby taking the mother and sending** away **the offspring,** he is still **obligated** to send away the mother even if he sent away the offspring, **as it is stated: "You shall send the mother."**

The mishna then addresses a scenario discussed previously: *amar hareni notel et ha'em u-meshalei'ach et habanim* — if someone declares, "I intend to take the mother and send away the chicks." The Torah's scheme, as articulated in the verse, is to send away the mother and take the chicks. One might ask whether it is permitted to reverse this — to take the mother and release the chicks — since after all you are not taking both together.

The mishna rules: even in that case, *chayav l'shalei'ach* — one is obligated. The positive commandment of *shaleach tishalach et ha'em* — send away the mother — applies and one may not simply take the mother in lieu of the chicks.

The Sefer HaChinuch initially speculated that taking only the mother, without the chicks, would not transgress the negative commandment *lo tikach ha'em al habanim*, since the negative is understood as prohibiting taking both together. However, based on the Gemara and the Rishonim, this is not the halakha. The word *al* in *lo tikach ha'em al habanim* is read not as "together with" but as "over" — do not take the mother who was hovering over the children. Therefore, taking the mother alone while she was *rovetzet* transgresses the negative as well as the positive.

Ironically, if one wishes to take the mother bird, the halakhically permissible approach is to take the eggs first and send away the mother, and then capture the mother after she has flown away and is no longer *rovetzet*. Alternatively, one can simply capture her when she is not hovering at all, since the *mitzvah* and its attendant prohibitions apply only in the *rovetzet* configuration.

### Return After Taking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶת הַבָּנִים וְהֶחְזִירָן לָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ חָזְרָה הָאֵם עֲלֵיהֶן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** If one sent away the mother and **took the offspring and** then **returned them** to the mother's nest, **and thereafter the mother returned** and rested **upon them,** one is **exempt from sending** away the mother bird.

The mishna addresses a further case: *natal et habanim ve-hechziran* — one took the eggs, and then, for whatever reason, returned them to the nest, after which the mother resumed hovering over them. In that case, *patur melishaleach* — one is exempt from sending her away. The reason is the principle discussed earlier concerning *mezuman*: once one has taken the eggs, they are in one's possession or domain. The verse says *ki yikare* — "if you happen upon" — implying a chance encounter, and the fact that one placed the eggs back does not recreate the original condition of a spontaneous encounter. The eggs have already been acquired, and the *mitzvah* does not apply again to this reconfigured scene.

## Purpose Override Question

### Shaleach Tishalach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״שַׁלֵּחַ״ – אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים, ״תְּשַׁלַּח״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת, לִדְבַר מִצְוָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תְּשַׁלַּח״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** Actually, the *halakha* that one must send away the mother multiple times is not derived from the phrase "*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*." Rather, the word **"*shalle'aḥ*"** indicates that one must send away the mother bird **even one hundred times,** and the word **"*teshallaḥ*"** teaches another matter: **I have** derived **only** the obligation to send away the mother bird in a case where one takes the eggs or the fledglings and wants to take the mother bird **for a discretionary purpose,** e.g., to eat it. In a case where one takes the eggs or the fledglings and needs the mother bird **for a matter** involving **a mitzva,** e.g., the purification of a leper, **from where** is the *halakha* that he must send away the mother derived? **The verse states: "*Teshallaḥ*,"** to teach that **in any case** one must send away the mother bird.

The Gemara then takes up the doubled language *shaleach tishalach* directly. A student posed: perhaps *shaleach* means sending once and *tishalach* means sending a second time — giving us a total of only two mandatory sendings, not an indefinite number. The Gemara rejects this. *Shaleach* itself already connotes an unlimited obligation — it does not mean "once." Rather, *tishalach* carries a different and important teaching: *ein li ela lidvar ha-reshut* — I know the obligation applies when one wishes to take the birds for an optional purpose. How do I know it applies even when one is taking the birds for a *mitzvah* purpose? *Talmud lomar tishalach mikol makom* — the doubled form tells us: in all circumstances.

### Metzora Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר רָבָא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״תְּשַׁלַּח״, הָא לָאו הָכִי, הָוֵה אָמֵינָא: לִדְבַר מִצְוָה לָא? עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה הוּא, וְאֵין עֲשֵׂה דּוֹחֶה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba, son of Rav Yosef bar Rava, said to Rav Kahana: But** according to this explanation, the only **reason** that one must send away the mother bird even when needed for a matter involving a mitzva is **that the Merciful One wrote: "*Teshallaḥ*"; but** if **not** for **this, I would say** that when the mother is needed **for a matter** involving **a mitzva,** one is **not** obligated to send her away. But this is difficult, as the sending of the mother bird **is** both **a positive mitzva and a prohibition,** as the verse states: "You shall not take the mother with the young; you shall send the mother" (Deuteronomy 22:6–7), **and** there is a principle that **a positive mitzva cannot override** both **a prohibition and a positive mitzva.**

The practical context for this ruling is the *mitzvah* of purifying a *metzora* (a person afflicted with the skin condition described in Leviticus). The purification rite requires two birds. If these happen to be the only birds available, and one of them is a mother bird currently *rovetzet* over her eggs, may one take the mother bird for this *mitzvah*?

The answer of the mishna is no. Even in service of a *dvar mitzvah*, *shaleach tishalach* — one must send her away. Rav Abba son of Rav Yosef bar Abba then explains to Rav Kahana why one might have thought otherwise: *assei v'lo ta'asei hu*, this is a case of a positive commandment together with a negative, and a single *asei* cannot override a *lav* combined with an *asei*. So why was a verse needed to tell us this? The obligation should have been obvious. The Gemara answers: the verse is needed for a more refined scenario.

### Rav Abba Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּעֲבַר וְשַׁקְלַהּ לְאֵם, דְּלָאו עַבְרֵיהּ, עֲשֵׂה הוּא דְּאִיכָּא, לֵיתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְלִידְחֵי עֲשֵׂה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana replied: **No,** the word "*teshallaḥ*" is **necessary** for a case **where one transgressed and took the mother** with the fledglings. In such a case, **he has** already **transgressed the prohibition,** and **there is** now only **a positive mitzva** to send away the mother. Perhaps one might say: **Let the positive mitzva** performed with the mother, e.g., the purification of the leper, **come and override the positive mitzva** of sending away the mother. Therefore, the word "*teshallaḥ*" **teaches us** that this is not so, and one is obligated to send away the mother.

The case that requires a verse is where one took the mother bird *al menat l'shalei'ach* — with the intent to send her away, as part of the act of *shiluach haken* itself. In that case, the act of taking has not constituted a transgression of *lo tikach ha'em al habanim*, because at the moment of taking, one's intent was permissible — it was part of the *mitzvah* process. Then one changes one's mind and considers using the bird for the *metzora* instead. At that point, one has not transgressed the *lav* (since the initial act was permissible and there is no new act of taking), but one does have an active *asei* — the obligation of *shileach tishalach*, send away the mother.

Now it is a question of one *asei* against another *asei*. The *asei* of purifying the *metzora* — restoring him to full participation in family and communal life — is a powerful and weighty commandment. Why then might one not invoke it to override the *asei* of *shiluach haken*? The Gemara invokes the principle that *gadol shalom shebein ish le-ishto* — great is domestic peace — and notes that a *metzora* is prohibited from *tashmish ha-mitah* while impure, as derived from *ve-yashav michutz le-ohalo shivat yamim*, where *ohalo* is interpreted as referring to his domestic life with his wife. Given the urgency of restoring this marital normalcy, one might have genuinely thought the *asei* of the *metzora*'s purification outweighs that of sending away the mother bird. The verse therefore teaches: *ka-mashma lan* — no. The obligation of *shiluach haken* holds even here.

### Lav HaNitak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּתָנֵי קִיְּימוֹ וְלֹא קִיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This works out well according to the one who teaches** that the criterion for determining whether one is flogged for violating a prohibition that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva is whether he **fulfilled** the mitzva immediately afterward **or did not fulfill it.** If he does not fulfill the mitzva immediately, he is flogged when he fails to do so. Consequently, if one does not send away the mother bird immediately after taking her with the fledglings, he has immediately violated the prohibition, and only the requirement to fulfill the positive mitzva remains.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּתָנֵי בִּטְּלוֹ וְלֹא בִּטְּלוֹ, כַּמָּה דְּלָא שַׁחְטַהּ – לָא עַבְרֵיהּ לְלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **But according to the one who teaches** that the criterion for determining whether one is flogged in that case is whether he **negated** the possibility of fulfilling the positive mitzva **or did not negate it,** and one is flogged only if he performed an action that renders it impossible to fulfill the mitzva, this explanation does not work out well. After all, **as long as one has not slaughtered** the mother bird, he **has not transgressed the prohibition,** since he may still send her away. Consequently, even if one takes the mother, as long as he has not slaughtered her, the prohibition remains in addition to the positive mitzva. If so, there is no need for the Torah to teach that the obligation to send away the mother bird applies in the case of a mitzva.

Before fully resolving the *metzora* reasoning, the Gemara pauses to address a structural question. The entire scenario was constructed around a case where the *lav* had been removed from the equation, leaving only an *asei* against an *asei*. The Gemara notes that this reasoning — *de-avar ve-shaklah*, one transgressed and already took the mother bird — presupposes a particular version of the *lav ha-nitak la-asei* principle.

According to the version that formulates the principle as *kiymo v'lo kiymo* — whether one has fulfilled the positive commandment or not — the transgressor has already committed the *lav* and now faces only the remaining *asei*. In that configuration, one *asei* faces another *asei*, and the *hava amina* (initial assumption) is coherent. But according to the version formulated as *bitlo v'lo bitlo* — whether one has actively negated the possibility of fulfilling the positive commandment — one does not fully transgress the *lav* until one also destroys the possibility of fulfilling the *asei*. Under that reading, *kama d'lo shachta* — as long as one has not killed the bird — *lo avar al lav*, one has not yet transgressed the negative. Therefore, taking the bird and then considering using it for the *metzora*'s purification would still mean facing an *asei* against a *lav* and an *asei* — an obvious prohibition that needs no verse to establish.

Similarly, according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *shaleach me-ikara mashma* — that the positive commandment of sending away was meant to be fulfilled at the outset, not as a post-hoc remedy — once one has taken the mother bird improperly, there is no remaining *asei* at all. In that case, there is nothing standing in the way of using the bird for the *metzora*, and the verse would have been unnecessary. The Gemara therefore concludes that Mar bar Rav Ashi's construction — *ke-gon she-netala al menat le-shalei'ach*, one who took the bird with intent to send it away and then changed their mind — is the only scenario that generates a genuine *hava amina* requiring a verse to resolve it.

## Kiymo vs. Bitlo

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹטֵל אָדָם אֵם עַל הַבָּנִים – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּחַ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְשַׁלֵּחַ וְאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ קוּם עֲשֵׂה – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who takes the mother** bird **with** its **fledglings, Rabbi Yehuda says: He is flogged** for taking the mother bird, **and** he **does not send** away the mother. **And the Rabbis say: He sends** away the mother **and is not flogged,** as **this is the principle:** With regard to **any prohibition that entails** a command to **arise** and **perform** a mitzva, **one is not flogged for its** violation.

The Gemara now turns to the underlying dispute about *lav ha-nitak la-asei* — a negative commandment connected to a positive commandment that is meant to serve as its remedy. The mishna states the general rule: *kol mitzvat lo ta'aseh she-yesh bah kum aseh, ein lokin aleha* — any negative transgression that has a positive commandment built in as a corrective does not incur lashes. This is the position of the Sages against Rabbi Yehuda, who rules *lokeh ve-eino meshalei'ach* — one incurs lashes and sending away accomplishes nothing.

### Two Versions

The Gemara in Makkot presents two distinct formulations of when lashes are avoided under this principle, and both are now relevant. The first formulation is *kiymo v'lo kiymo*: lashes are avoided if one fulfills the positive commandment, and incurred if one does not. This is relatively straightforward — send away the mother bird after having transgressed, and one is exempt from lashes; fail to do so, and lashes are incurred. The second formulation is *bitlo v'lo bitlo*: lashes are incurred only if one actively negates the positive commandment — for instance, by killing the bird or otherwise destroying the means of fulfillment. Merely failing to send her away, without actively destroying the possibility, does not trigger lashes under this version.

Between these two versions there are, of course, intermediate and edge cases. What if the bird dies on its own — not killed by the transgressor, but simply unavailable? Under *kiymo*, one has not fulfilled the *asei*, so lashes should follow; under *bitlo*, one has not actively negated it, so perhaps lashes do not follow. Rashi also entertains the possibility that under *kiymo*, the window for fulfilling the *asei* is very narrow — that the bird must be sent away *toch k'dei dibbur* (within a moment of transgression), and failure to act promptly means the chance to fix it has passed.

The Gemara's analysis here is anchored in the question of how these two formulations apply to the scenario of using the mother bird for the *metzora*. Under *kiymo*, at the moment after transgression, all that remains is an *asei*, and the *hava amina* that a stronger *asei* could override it is coherent. Under *bitlo*, as long as the bird is alive, one has technically not fully transgressed the *lav* — making the scenario structurally different and the *hava amina* less coherent.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לָאו שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Yehuda, one who takes the mother with the offspring is flogged. With regard to this, **Rabbi Abba bar Memel raises a dilemma:** Is **the reasoning** for the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda that he holds** that **one is flogged for** violation of **a prohibition that entails** fulfillment **of a positive mitzva?** Accordingly, one is flogged for transgressing the prohibition: "You shall not take the mother with the young," which entails the fulfillment of: "You shall send the mother."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא בְּעָלְמָא סָבַר, לָאו שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר ״שַׁלֵּחַ״ מֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The second possibility is now presented: **Or perhaps in general** Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that **one is not flogged for** violation of **a prohibition that entails** fulfillment **of a positive mitzva; and here, this is the reason** one is flogged for taking the mother: It is **because he holds** that when the verse states: "You shall send the mother [*shalle'aḥ teshallaḥ*]" (Deuteronomy 22:7), the word **"*shalle'aḥ*" indicates** that the mitzva to sending the mother bird applies only **at the outset,** i.e., when encountering the nest. Accordingly, this is not a case of a prohibition that entails the performance of a positive mitzva, but an independent prohibition, for which one is liable to receive lashes.

Having established the parameters, the Gemara asks whether Rabbi Yehuda's position in the mishna — that the transgressor gets lashes and sending away accomplishes nothing — reflects a general disagreement with the principle of *lav ha-nitak la-asei*, or whether he accepts the principle in general and simply interprets the specific verse of *shaleach* differently.

The question is posed as follows: *ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda mishum de-savar lav ha-nitak la-asei lokin alav* — is his reason that he holds, as a general rule, that even a *lav* connected to a corrective *asei* incurs lashes? Or: *be-alma savar lav ha-nitak la-asei ein lokin alav*, and here specifically, *mishum de-kasavar shaleach me-ikara mashma* — he agrees with the Sages in general, but holds that in this particular verse, *shaleach* means what you were supposed to do originally, not a remedy after the fact?

A baraita is then cited stating that Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *ganav* (thief) and *gazlan* (robber) are subject to *malkut* despite the positive commandment of *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeila* — returning the stolen object. Since returning the stolen object can only be done *after* the theft, it cannot be read as a prior obligation; it is clearly a corrective *asei*. Yet Rabbi Yehuda assigns lashes. This would suggest he holds the general rule that *lav ha-nitak la-asei* does incur lashes.

## Corrupt Baraita Issue

### Trustworthy Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: לָאו אָמֵינָא לְכוּ, כֹּל מַתְנִיתָא דְּלָא תַּנְיָא בֵּי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וּבֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא מְשַׁבַּשְׁתָּא הִיא, וְלָא תּוֹתְבוּ מִינַּהּ בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, דִּלְמָא אֵינָהּ בִּכְלַל מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to him: Didn't I** already **tell you** that **any *baraita* that is not taught** in the study **hall** of **Rabbi Ḥiyya or** the study **hall** of **Rabbi Oshaya,** who were precise in the formulation of their *baraitot*, **is corrupted, and you may not raise objections** based **upon it** in **the study hall?** Since this *baraita* was not taught in either of the aforementioned study halls, you may not cite it as proof. After all, **perhaps** the phrase: **Are not included among** those who are liable to receive **forty lashes** by Torah law, **was taught** by Rabbi Yehuda in the *baraita*, but the text was corrupted. It is therefore possible that according to Rabbi Yehuda, one is not flogged for violation of a prohibition that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva, and his reasoning of his ruling in the mishna is that the word "*shalle'aḥ*" indicates that the mitzva to send away the mother bird applies only at the outset.

Rabbi Zeira intervenes at this point with a methodological ruling of the first importance: *kol matnita de-lo tanya be-Rebbi Chiyya u-ve-Rebbi Oshaya meshabbeshta* — any baraita that was not edited and transmitted by Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Oshaya is a corrupted text (*meshabbeshta*), and one may not raise halakhic arguments from it in the *beit midrash*.

Rashi explains the basis for this principle. Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Oshaya — who are widely regarded as the editors or compilers of the Tosefta — subjected all the *baraitot* to rigorous textual review. They verified attributions and ensured that each Sage's statement was recorded as it was actually said: *dikdeku be-divrei kol chacham lomar ke-mo she-amar*.

The problem with other *baraitot* is the *tanna'im* — not the Tannaim in the sense of the Sages of the Mishnaic period, but the human books of the oral culture: the memorizers (*tanna'im* with a lowercase *t*) who were assigned to preserve and transmit texts. Saul Lieberman articulated the dynamic well: in an oral culture, publishing a text meant finding expert memorizers, transmitting the precise wording to them, and sending them to communities to repeat it. The ideal qualifications for such a person were a strong memory and minimal creative intelligence — because an intellectually engaged memorizer would tend to "correct" what he perceived as errors, inserting his own reasoning into the text.

The result, Rabbi Zeira warns, is that *baraitot* outside the Chiyya-Oshaya corpus may have been modified by their transmitters. The baraita cited about the *ganav* and *gazlan* — that Rabbi Yehuda holds they incur *malkut* — may simply be a corrupted text. Perhaps the original baraita said the opposite: that Rabbi Yehuda exempts them. The transmitter, knowing Rabbi Yehuda generally assigns lashes, may have inverted the ruling. One therefore cannot draw any inference from it.

## Reliable Proofs

### Sheaves Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: ״לֹא תָשׁוּב״ וְשָׁב, ״לֹא תְכַלֶּה״ וְכִלָּה – יֶשְׁנָן בִּכְלַל מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that **which is taught** in a *baraita* by **Rabbi Oshaya and Rabbi Ḥiyya:** It is written with regard to forgotten sheaves left for the poor: "When you reap your harvest in your field and forget a sheaf in the field, **you shall not return** to fetch it" (Deuteronomy 24:19). If one transgressed this prohibition **and** one **returned** to fetch it, he is liable to receive lashes. Likewise, it is written with regard to produce in the corner of the field [*pe'a*], which is given to the poor: "And when you reap the harvest of your land, **you shall not wholly reap** the corner of your field" (Leviticus 19:9). If one transgressed this prohibition **and reaped** the corner of his field, such individuals **are included among** those who are liable to receive **lashes** by Torah law. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר לָאו שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה – לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes the resolution: Since the mitzvot of leaving *pe'a* and forgotten sheaves are prohibitions that entail fulfillment of a positive mitzva, as the verse states with regard to both of them: "For the poor and for the stranger you shall leave them" (Leviticus 19:10), **conclude from** the *baraita* that **the reasoning** of the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda** in the mishna is **that he holds** that **one is flogged for** violation of **a prohibition that entails** fulfillment **of a positive mitzva.**

The Gemara continues seeking a reliable proof about Rabbi Yehuda's underlying position. A baraita from Rabbi Yoshia and Rabbi Chiyya — and significantly, Rabbi Chiyya is named, meaning this falls within the trusted corpus — is cited. The baraita addresses the case of *shich'cha* (the forgotten sheaf that must be left for the poor) and *peah* (the corner of the field that must be left unharvested). If the farmer returned and took the forgotten sheaf (*lo tashuv ve-shav*), or harvested the corner of the field (*lo techaleh ve-chileh*), Rabbi Yehuda rules *yeshnan bi-khlal makkos arba'im* — they incur forty lashes.

This seems to establish that Rabbi Yehuda holds the general rule: *lav ha-nitak la-asei* does incur lashes. After all, the verse says *le-ani ve-la-ger ta'azov osam* — leave it for the poor — which comes after the prohibition, suggesting a corrective *asei*. Yet Rabbi Yehuda assigns lashes.

The Gemara responds, however: *dilma hasam hainu ta'ama de-kasavar ta'azov me-ikara mashma* — perhaps there too, Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse as he reads ours. Just as he reads *shaleach* as "you should have sent her away from the outset," he reads *ta'azov* as "you should have left it for the poor from the outset." The positive commandment in both cases, on his reading, is a prescription for proper conduct before transgression, not a remedy afterward. The baraita therefore does not prove that he holds the general *lav ha-nitak la-asei* principle to always incur lashes; it may simply reflect his consistent hermeneutic of reading such positive formulations as original obligations rather than post-hoc correctives.

### Pesach Remnant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, ״לֹא תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר וְגוֹ׳ בָּאֵשׁ תִּשְׂרֹפוּ״ – בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן עֲשֵׂה אַחַר לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, לוֹמַר לָךְ שֶׁאֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which is taught in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the Paschal offering: **"You shall let nothing of it remain until the morning;** but that which remains of it until the morning **you shall burn in fire"** (Exodus 12:10). **The verse comes to position the positive mitzva** of burning the leftover meat **after** the **prohibition** against leaving over the meat, in order **to say that one is not flogged for its** violation; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר ״שַׁלֵּחַ״ מֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina continued: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that according to Rabbi Yehuda, one is not flogged for violation of a prohibition that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva, and that **the reasoning** of the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda** in the mishna is **that he holds** that the word **"*shalle'aḥ*" indicates** that the mitzva to send away the mother applies only **at the outset.** Consequently, the prohibition against taking the mother with the offspring is not one that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva, but an independent prohibition that is punishable by lashes. The Gemara concedes: **Conclude from** it that this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda.

Ravina then offers Rav Ashi a more decisive proof. A baraita from a reliable source states: *lo tosiru mimenu ad boker* — do not leave over meat from the *korban* until morning — followed by *ve-ha-nosar mimenu ad boker ba-esh tisrofu* — burn it in fire. The verse establishes a positive commandment (*asei*) following the negative: burn the *nosar* (remnant). Now, burning *nosar* can only be done *after* it has become *nosar*, i.e., after the transgression of leaving it over has occurred. There is no way to read this as "you should have burned it from the outset." *Ba ha-kasuv li-ten asei achar lo ta'aseh* — the verse comes to attach a positive after the negative — *lomar lach she-ein lokin alav* — to tell you that lashes are not incurred. *Divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — this is Rabbi Yehuda's position.

Here, then, is a baraita from the trusted corpus in which Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states that when a positive commandment follows a negative and is clearly meant as a corrective — not as an original instruction — one does not incur lashes. *Shema mina ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda mishum de-kasavar shaleach me-ikara mashma*: one may therefore conclude that Rabbi Yehuda's disagreement in the case of *shiluach haken* is not a general rejection of the *lav ha-nitak la-asei* principle. He accepts the principle. His specific dispute is over the reading of *shaleach* — which he interprets as an original obligation rather than a remedial one.

## Mishna Language Analysis

### Mishna Phrasing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אִידִי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: הַנּוֹטֵל אֵם עַל הַבָּנִים – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּחַ, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָאו שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו, ״לוֹקֶה וּמְשַׁלֵּחַ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Idi said to Rav Ashi:** The language of **the mishna is also precise, as it teaches:** With regard to **one who takes the mother with the offspring, Rabbi Yehuda says: He is flogged and there is no** longer a mitzva to **send** away the mother. **But if** it **enters your mind** to say that **the reasoning** of the ruling **of Rabbi Yehuda** in the mishna is that **one is flogged for** violation of **a prohibition that entails** fulfillment **of a positive mitzva,** then Rabbi Yehuda **should have** said: **He is flogged and sends** away the mother. Rather, conclude that according to Rabbi Yehuda, this is not a prohibition that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר בְּמַתְנִיתִין: אֵין נִפְטָר עַד דְּמַלְקִין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: One cannot infer anything from the language of the mishna, since **perhaps this** is what Rabbi Yehuda **is saying in the mishna:** There is still a mitzva to send away the mother, but **one is not absolved** of guilt for transgressing the prohibition against taking the mother **until** the court **flogs him.**

Rav Idi then suggests to Rav Ashi that the language of the mishna itself supports this conclusion. The mishna says: *ha-notel em al habanim, Rabbi Yehuda omer lokeh ve-eino meshalei'ach* — Rabbi Yehuda says one incurs lashes and does not send away. The phrase *eino meshalei'ach* — "and does not send away" — implies that sending away serves no purpose at all. This is consistent with his position that *shaleach* was an original instruction that has been violated; once one has taken the mother, there is nothing left to do. There is no residual *mitzvah* of sending away, so it is not merely that lashes are incurred despite the *asei* — rather, the *asei* no longer applies at all.

The Gemara, however, resists deriving this conclusion definitively from the mishna's phrasing. One might read *lokeh ve-eino meshalei'ach* differently: *ein niftar ad de-malkin leh* — it will not exempt him from lashes, but perhaps Rabbi Yehuda still holds that there is an obligation to send away, it simply will not mitigate the lashes. Under this reading, the mishna is saying: sending away will not help you, but it is not saying there is no *mitzvah* of sending away after the fact. The mishna's language is thus compatible with either position, and no decisive inference can be drawn from it alone.

What is nonetheless established — now from the *nosar* baraita — is the reliable conclusion that Rabbi Yehuda accepts the *lav ha-nitak la-asei* principle in its general form, and disputes only its application to *shaleach tishalach*, which he reads as an original rather than a remedial command. Whether the Sages agree that there is a positive *mitzvah* of *shileach* applicable even before any transgression, or whether they limit the *mitzvah* to a post-transgression remedy, remains a question to carry forward.

## Metzora Obligation

### Rav Yehuda's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לִזְכּוֹת בְּבֵיצִים שֶׁהָאֵם רוֹבֶצֶת עֲלֵיהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁלֵּחַ אֶת הָאֵם״ וַהֲדַר ״הַבָּנִים תִּקַּח לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And now that Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: It is prohibited to acquire eggs whose mother is resting upon them, as it is stated:** "You shall **send the mother," and** only **then: "The young you may take for yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:7), **you** may **even say: Even though** the eggs fully emerged and **fell into his courtyard,** he must still send away the mother bird.

The final mishna of the daf states: *shaleach tishalach et ha'em ve-et habanim tikach lach* — send away the mother and take the children, even for a *mitzvah* purpose. The Gemara explains that the scenario is taking the birds for the purification of a *metzora*. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav holds that the obligation of *shiluach haken* applies only if one actually wants the children — *im rotzeh ba-banim*. If one does not want the children at all, the *mitzvah* of sending away does not apply.

### Baraita Objection

The Gemara raises an objection from a baraita: *shaleach tishalach* — why the double language? To include even a case where one does not want the children. This seems to directly contradict Rav Yehuda's position.

### Tannaim Dispute

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: the baraita reflects the view of one Tanna, who holds the *mitzvah* is independent of whether one desires the children. Rav Yehuda follows a different Tanna, who holds the *mitzvah* is conditional on wanting the children. The dispute thus reflects a genuine *machlokes* between Tannaim on the essential question of whether *shiluach haken* is an independent obligation triggered by the *rovetzet* configuration alone, or whether it is inherently instrumental — applying only when one intends to benefit from the nest by taking what is in it.
