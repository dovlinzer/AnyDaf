# Taanit 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Statements

### Tzurba Merabbanan

The daf opens with a continuation of Rava's meteorological statements from the previous day, now broadening into observations about the character of Torah scholars. Rava begins with an agricultural metaphor: *hai tzurba merabbanan dami le-partzida de-tusei kala, de-keivan de-navat navat* — a young rabbinic student is like a tiny seed or sprout buried beneath a clump of earth. Once it begins to shoot upward, it continues to grow. The image captures something essential about the early career of a *talmid chacham*: the beginnings may be hidden and halting, but once growth commences, the momentum sustains itself.

Rava then turns from the agricultural to the temperamental. If a young *talmid chacham* is *de-ratach* — that is, given to flashes of anger or passion — one should be understanding, because *oraita hu de-kamratach lei*: it is the Torah within him that burns and may cause him to overreact. The verse is adduced in support: *halo ko devarai ka-esh neum Hashem* — "Are not My words like fire, says God?" The passion of Torah learning, in other words, can produce a kind of holy fire that occasionally manifests as excessive zeal.

### Hard as Iron

Rav Ashi moves in a more demanding direction. Whereas Rava's statement could be read as contextualizing — even excusing — passionate behavior, Rav Ashi appears to actively endorse a certain hardness of character: *kol talmid chacham she-eino kasheh ke-varzel, eino talmid chacham* — any *talmid chacham* who is not as hard as iron is not truly a *talmid chacham*. The proof text is *u-kefatish yifotzetz sela* — "like a hammer that shatters rock." The implication is that the pursuit of Torah requires an iron-willed single-mindedness, a refusal to be a pushover in one's convictions and focus.

### Builders/Boneha

Rav Abba responds to Rav Ashi: *atun me-hatam matnitu lah, anan me-hacha matninan lah* — you derive it from that verse; we derive it from a different one. The verse is *eretz asher avaneha varzel* — "a land whose rocks are iron" — with the interpretive reading: *al tikrei avaneha ela boneha*, do not read "its rocks" but "its builders." This is the well-known *derashah* applied elsewhere as well: *rav shalom bonayikh al tikrei vanayikh ela vonayikh*. The *boneha* formulation shifts the emphasis subtly — these are not merely hard individuals but builders, those who construct the world. The iron, then, is not mere stubbornness but the structural integrity of one who bears the weight of *Torah* and *avodah*. It is worth noting that a student raised the additional layer present in this *derashah*, connecting *boneha* to the *shoresh* of *binah* and understanding — a point acknowledged as a meaningful additional dimension of the classical reading.

### Pleasant Interaction

Ravina supplies the necessary counterweight. *Afilu hakhi, miba'ei lei le-inish lemeilef nafshiah be-nichusa* — even granted all of the above, a person must train himself to conduct his interpersonal affairs pleasantly. The verse cited is *ve-haser ka'as mi-libecha* — "remove anger from your heart." This juxtaposition with Rav Ashi's statement is deliberate. It is well and good to have passion and conviction and a strength of character that does not bend; but there is simultaneously a *derekh eretz* that must govern one's dealings with other people. The *talmid chacham* who is as hard as iron in his learning and convictions must also know how to *meilef nafshiah be-nichusa* — to school himself in pleasant interaction. The two qualities are not in tension but in balance, and the *sugya* here presents them as a unified portrait of the ideal scholar.

## Three Petitioners

### Eliezer & Rivka

The *Gemara* now shifts into an extended *aggadic* mode, introduced by Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *shlosha sha'alu shelo ke-hogen* — three people made requests inappropriately. Of these three, *u-lishnayim heshivuhu ke-hogen* — two received fitting responses despite the problematic framing of their requests, while *u-le-echad heshivuhu shelo ke-hogen* — one received a response that matched the carelessness of his petition.

The three are: Eliezer the servant of Avraham, Shaul ben Kish, and Yiftach the Gileadite.

Eliezer — called here *eved Avraham* because the Torah never names him in the episode with Rivka, though *Chazal* identify him as Eliezer — set up the following test: the young woman who responds to his request for water by also offering to water his camels will be the chosen bride for Yitzchak. The problem: *yakhol afilu chigeret afilu suma* — what if the woman who passes the test happens to be lame or blind, someone who would be entirely unsuitable for Yitzchak for other reasons? His criterion was too narrowly drawn, and a different woman might theoretically have fit it. Rashi and Tosafot grapple with why he could not have checked other matters first, with the suggestion that not every disability is immediately apparent. In any event, *heshivuhu ke-hogen* — he received the right result. *Ve-nizdamna lo Rivka* — Rivka herself appeared, the perfectly appropriate match.

### Saul & David

Shaul ben Kish made the proclamation that whoever slays Goliath will receive great wealth from the king and the hand of his daughter in marriage: *ha-ish asher yakenu ya'asrenu ha-melech osher gadol ve-et bito yiten lo*. The flaw in his framing: *yakhol afilu eved afilu mamzer* — what if the victor were a slave or a *mamzer*? The offer as stated makes no exception, and marrying one's daughter to such a person would be deeply problematic. Yet *heshivuhu ke-hogen* — the outcome was fitting. *Ve-nizdamen lo David* — it was David who stepped forward. Whatever the later complications in the relationship between Shaul and David, at the moment of the challenge, David was plainly the right answer.

### Yiftach's Daughter

Yiftach's case is the most striking. He vowed: *ve-haya ha-yotze asher yetzei mi-daltei beiti* — whatever emerges first from the doors of my house when I return from battle, I will offer as a sacrifice to God. The *Gemara* points out that he was insufficiently careful — *yakhol afilu davar tamei* — what if something non-kosher had emerged first? He had not thought through even that basic scenario. Of course, the graver problem — which the *Gemara* pointedly does not spell out at this stage — is that it could be, and was, a person. *Heshivuhu shelo ke-hogen* — he received an inappropriate response corresponding to his careless request. *Ve-nizdamna lo bito* — his own daughter was the first to come out.

The *Gemara* then connects this to the prophet's lament in Yirmeyahu: *hatzori ein b'Gil'ad im rofeh ein sham* — is there no balm in Gilead, is there no healer there? *Gil'ad* is Yiftach the Gileadite, and the *navi* is expressing distress that the situation was not remedied. This connects to the tradition cited by Rav Yosef that Pinchas was alive at the time and could have served as the authority to annul the vow — *matir ha-neder* — but both parties stood on their dignity: Yiftach said let Pinchas come to me, Pinchas said let Yiftach come to me. Neither moved, and the tragedy unfolded.

This raises a genuinely uncomfortable question about the narrative in *Sefer Shoftim*. If one reads the story of Yiftach without external commentary, there is nothing in the text that condemns what he did as religiously abhorrent. The daughter mourns that she will die a virgin; the tone is that of tragedy, not denunciation. And yet the *Gemara* here is working to establish that God did not want this and never sanctioned it — even while the narrative structure of the *Gemara* itself emphasizes that God could have arranged for something other than his daughter to emerge and chose not to. The tension is present and deliberate.

### Child Sacrifice

The *Gemara* now introduces a verse from Yirmeyahu bearing on the practice of child sacrifice more broadly: *asher lo tziviti ve-lo dibarti ve-lo altah al libi* — I did not command it, I did not speak of it, and it never even entered My mind. This verse is applied with striking precision to three cases.

*Asher lo tziviti* — that which I never commanded: this refers to the son of Mesha, king of Moav, who, with defeat imminent in battle against Israel, took his firstborn son and offered him as a sacrifice upon the wall. The disquieting detail in the biblical account is that after this sacrifice the Israelite siege broke off and Moav was not vanquished. The *Gemara* here does not ignore the implication. It does not claim the narrative is entirely comfortable. It simply asserts: God never wanted that. *Ve-lo dibarti* — that which I never spoke: this is Yiftach. God never instructed Yiftach to do what he did; it was Yiftach's own reckless vow. *Ve-lo altah al libi* — that which never entered My mind: this is Yitzchak son of Avraham. God said *ha'alehu l'olah*, bring him up as a sacrifice, but it never truly entered the divine mind that it would be carried out in that manner. Rashi, quoting the *Midrash*, notes that God's intention was not literal.

This is a remarkable passage. The *Gemara* is working through cases in *Tanakh* where child sacrifice — or something perilously close to it — appears without explicit divine condemnation in the immediate narrative. The verse in Yirmeyahu is deployed as a sweeping theological rejection: none of these episodes should be read as divine endorsement of child sacrifice in any form.

The *Midrash Bereishit Rabbah*'s account of the *Akeidah* is relevant here. When Avraham brought the ram as a substitute for Yitzchak, the *Midrash* has him say: let this be as though I am slaughtering Yitzchak; as he sprinkles the blood, let this be as Yitzchak's blood. The imagery is graphic, but the point is that the impulse does not simply disappear upon being redirected. The *Gemara* here, drawing on Yirmeyahu's categorical statement, insists that however these stories have been read, the divine word is unambiguous: *lo tziviti, ve-lo dibarti, ve-lo altah al libi*.

The section of the three petitioners, then, performs double work. It makes a point about the dangers of careless *she'eilah* — imprecise or poorly considered requests that leave open disastrous possibilities — and simultaneously uses the case of Yiftach to confront, through the lens of Yirmeyahu, the question of how certain morally troubling episodes in *Tanakh* are to be understood theologically.

## Israel's Request

### Dew Response

The *Gemara* transitions to a final *aggadic* passage that ties back directly to the themes of the *masechet*. Rav Brachia introduces an additional instance of an inappropriate request: *af Knesset Yisrael sha'alah shelo ke-hogen, ve-ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu heshivah ke-hogen* — even the *Knesset Yisrael* asked inappropriately, yet God responded appropriately.

The request: *ve-nei'dah nird'fah lada'at et Hashem, k'shachar nakhon motzao, ve-yavo ke-gashem lanu* — let us know God, let us pursue knowing Him; His rising is as certain as the morning, and He shall come to us like rain. The people are expressing their longing for divine closeness with the image of rain: reliable, sustaining, life-giving. And yet God's response is gently corrective: *biti, at sho'elet davar she-pe'amim mitbakeit ve-pe'amim eino mitbakeit* — My daughter, you are asking for something that is sometimes desirable and sometimes is not. Rain in the wrong season is a *klalah*, a curse, as the *masechet* has already established. *Aval ani eheyeh lakh davar ha-mitbakeit le-olam* — I will be for you something that is always desirable. *Shene'emar: ehyeh ke-tal le-Yisrael* — I will be like the dew to Israel. The dew, as was learned on the previous daf, never ceases, is never untimely, is always a blessing.

This exchange carries the major thematic statement of the *masechet* in miniature. Rain is not merely an agricultural phenomenon; it is an expression of *yedidut*, of closeness and favor between God and *Bnei Yisrael*. The people's longing for divine presence is expressed in meteorological terms, and God responds in kind — but with the superior image of dew, which is unconditional and constant where rain is contingent and seasonal.

### Seal on Heart

The *Gemara* records one further exchange. *Knesset Yisrael* requests: *simeini kha-chotam al libecha kha-chotam al zero'echa* — place me like a seal upon Your heart, like a seal upon Your arm. Again God responds that this is *she-pe'amim nireh ve-pe'amim eino nireh* — sometimes visible, sometimes not visible. A seal on the arm can be covered by a sleeve; closeness that depends on visibility is closeness that can be obscured.

God's counter-offer: *hen al kapayim chakotikh* — behold, I have engraved you upon My very palms. The palms are never covered; what is written there is always seen. The promise is not merely closeness but perpetual, unconcealed visibility. God's relationship to Israel is carved into His hands, not sealed upon a covered arm.

The pairing of these two exchanges — rain replaced by dew, a seal on the heart replaced by an engraving on the palms — reinforces the same theological movement: Israel reaches toward something genuine and beautiful, but God redirects each request toward something more permanent and more unconditional. This is the larger frame in which the entire discussion of *geshem*, *tal*, and their proper seasons operates: behind the *halachic* discussion of when to mention rain and when to request it lies the understanding that rain itself is a modality of divine relationship, and the texture of that relationship — conditional or unconditional, seasonal or constant — matters profoundly.

It should be noted *le-halakhah* that *Tosafot* cites a *Yerushalmi* supporting the practice of saying *morid ha-tal* throughout the year, including the summer months. This is not the practice of the *Rema* and most *Ashkenazim*, but it is the practice of *Sefardim* and certain strands of *Ashkenazi nusach*. The practical benefit is significant: if one says *morid ha-tal* habitually throughout the year, then forgetting to say *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* in the autumn immediately after *Sukkot* does not require *chazarah*, since *morid ha-tal* serves as an acceptable substitute and one has not omitted a *shevach* of *HaKadosh Baruch Hu* entirely. The logic depends on whether *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* is understood as a precursor to *she'eilah* or as an independent *shevach* of divine power over rain and meteorological sustenance. If the latter, then *morid ha-tal* belongs to the same category — acknowledging God's mastery over the water cycle — and can function as a *be-di'eved* substitute. This question — whether *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* is primarily a *ritztui she'eilah*, a lead-in to request, or an independent *shevach* — is precisely the conceptual axis around which the *sugya* that follows will turn.

## Mention vs. Ask

### Same Act?

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah*'s statement *ein sho'alin et ha-geshamim ela samuch la-geshamim* — one does not request rain except close to the rainy season. The *Gemara* initially assumes — *savruha* — that *she'eilah* and *hazkarah* are the same act and occur simultaneously: *she'eilah ve-hazkarah chad milta hi*. This assumption is plausible at least in the view of Rabbi Yehoshua, who does not maintain any gap between mentioning and asking. Rabbi Yehoshua's entire position is that one does not say *mashiv ha-ruach* until rain is genuinely wanted and seasonally appropriate — so one might reasonably infer that for him, mentioning and asking begin and end together.

*Man tanna?* — whose *Tanna* is this? Rava answers: it is Rabbi Yehoshua, *de-amar mish'at hanachato* — who holds that one begins at the time of putting down the *lulav*, which corresponds to *Shemini Atzeret*, meaning one would presumably start saying *ve-ten tal u-matar* as well from that point.

The *Gemara* then reads the *Mishnah* differently: *afilu teima Rabbi Eliezer* — it could even be Rabbi Eliezer. Even Rabbi Eliezer, who does say *mashiv ha-ruach* earlier during *Sukkot*, would agree that one asks for rain later. The mentioning comes early; the asking comes when the season is right: *she'eilah le-chud ve-hazkarah le-chud*. This is in fact what Rabbi Eliezer says explicitly in the *Mishnah*, but the *Gemara* is asking whether the same gap could exist, in a different configuration, for Rabbi Yehoshua as well.

The *Gemara* proceeds to the opposite formulation: *leima Rabbi Yehoshua hi* — maybe the *Mishnah* about asking only near the rainy season represents only Rabbi Yehoshua? If so, asking later would imply mentioning later — which is Rabbi Yehoshua's position. Rava responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Eliezer — she'eilah le-chud ve-hazkarah le-chud*. Even Rabbi Eliezer can hold that the asking is delayed relative to the mentioning. This seems obvious from the *Mishnah* itself, yet the *Gemara* is pressing on whether Rabbi Yehoshua accepts any gap at all between the two.

### Abiyi's Objection

The *Gemara* next sets two *Mishnayot* against each other. The upcoming *Mishnah* on 10a — *ad mosai sho'alin et ha-geshamim* — asks until when rain is requested. Rabbi Yehuda answers: until *Pesach* has passed. But the current *Mishnah* governs the transition of the *sheliach tzibur* at *musaf* of the first day of *Pesach*, stopping *mashiv ha-ruach* there. Rav Chisda attempts to resolve this: *kan lish'ol kan le-hazkir* — one *Mishnah* speaks of the cessation of asking, the other of the cessation of mentioning. Thus one could continue to ask for rain throughout *Pesach* while having already stopped saying *mashiv ha-ruach* from the first day.

Ulla objects strenuously. The suggestion that one would continue asking for rain while no longer mentioning *mashiv ha-ruach* is *kashya khe-chometz la-shinayim ukhe-ashan la-einayim* — as vinegar to the teeth and smoke to the eyes, a citation from *Mishlei*. The objection is logical: *uma be-makom she-eino sho'el mazkir* — in the period before asking is appropriate, one mentions; certainly then, *be-makom she-sho'el*, in the period of active asking, one should also be mentioning. How could asking continue without mentioning?

### Ulla's Solution

Ulla resolves the tension differently: *trei tanna'ei aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda* — there are simply two *Tanna'im* reporting Rabbi Yehuda's position, each representing a different version. The *Mishnah* that has the transition at *musaf* of the first day of *Pesach* holds that both *she'eilah* and *hazkarah* end there simultaneously. The other *Mishnah* holds that both continue through all of *Pesach*. There is no need to posit a gap between the two; it is simply a dispute about what Rabbi Yehuda said.

Rav Yosef attempts a more creative resolution. *Mai ad she-ya'avor ha-Pesach?* — what does "until *Pesach* has passed" mean? He proposes: *ad she-ya'avor sheliach tzibur rishon ha-yored be-yom tov rishon shel Pesach* — until the first *sheliach tzibur* who descends before the congregation on the first day of *Pesach* has finished. The word *she-ya'avor* is read not as "until *Pesach* has passed over" but as "until the prayer leader passes." This would mean both *Mishnayot* speak of the same moment — the *musaf* transition on the first day — and there is no gap.

### Raba's Pesach

Rava offers yet another reconciliation. *Mai ad she-ya'avor ha-Pesach* — until the time of the *shechitat ha-Pesach* has passed: *Pesach* in its more biblical sense refers not to the holiday of *Chag HaMatzot* but to the *korban Pesach* itself, offered on the afternoon of the fourteenth of Nisan. The *shechita* occurs in the afternoon; by extension, the morning before is when asking ceases.

The logic Rava builds upon this: *ma techilato mazkir af al pi she-eino sho'el, af sofo* — just as at the beginning of the season one begins mentioning before one begins asking, so too at the end, one continues mentioning even after one has ceased asking. The parallel is structurally elegant: mentioning brackets the asking on both sides, preceding it and following it.

Abaye challenges this. *Bishlama techilato mazkir* — I understand why mentioning precedes asking at the outset. *Hazkara nami ritztui she'eilah hi* — the mention of *mashiv ha-ruach* is a *ritztui she'eilah*, a preparatory appeasement, laying the groundwork for the petition. First one praises God for this capacity, then one asks God to exercise it. But *sofo* — at the end — *mai ritztui she'eilah ika?* — once one is done asking, what function does the mention serve? If *mashiv ha-ruach* is conceived entirely as a lead-in to *she'eilah*, then once the asking ends, the mentioning has no further purpose.

The *Gemara* again concludes: *ela mivarta ke-de-Ulla* — the clearest resolution remains Ulla's, that it is a dispute about the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Nevertheless, this extended back-and-forth has sharpened the central conceptual question considerably: is *mashiv ha-ruach* an independent *shevach* with its own standing in *Shemoneh Esrei*, or is it a *ritztui she'eilah*, meaningful only as a prelude to asking? The answer has practical consequences in both directions — for when it can precede asking and whether it can follow asking — and the *Gemara* has not resolved this cleanly but has laid out the terms.

A parallel use of *ritztui* is worth noting here. The same term appears in Rabbi Eliezer's language in the *baraita* on 2b: *elu arba'ah minim hallalu einam ba'im ela le-ratzot al ha-mayim* — the four species come only to appease regarding the water. The *lulav* and its companions are understood as a *ritztui* for water, an act that makes God, as it were, favorably disposed toward granting rain — before the explicit asking has even begun. This is the liturgical and sacrificial logic that underlies the whole structure: *ritztui* can precede *bakasha*, and indeed should. What the *Gemara* here is asking is whether *ritztui* can also trail behind *bakasha* once the asking is done, and Abaye's answer is that it cannot — at least not if we understand *hazkarah* purely in this *ritztui* capacity.

## Halachic Timeline

### Yochanan's Position

The *Gemara* now moves to the *psak halakhah*. Rav Assi cites Rabbi Yochanan: *halakhah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda. We are accustomed to this ruling: the transition to *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* occurs at *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret* and the cessation occurs at *musaf* of the first day of *Pesach*.

But what does Rabbi Yochanan actually hold about the relationship between *hazkarah* and *she'eilah*? Remarkably, the *Gemara* surfaces an explicit statement: *hitchil le-hazkir matchil lish'ol, posek milisho'el posek mile-hazkir* — as soon as one begins mentioning, one begins asking; when one stops asking, one stops mentioning. For Rabbi Yochanan, *mashiv ha-ruach* and *ve-ten tal u-matar* are completely co-extensive. They begin at exactly the same moment and end at exactly the same moment. This is the position that the *Gemara* had earlier been trying to avoid, the one that seemed too extreme — and it turns out to be Rabbi Yochanan's explicit view.

### Rav Assi Challenge

This generates an immediate problem. Rav Zeira raises it to Rav Assi: *u-mi amar Rabbi Yochanan hakhi?* — did Rabbi Yochanan really say that? *Ve-hatnan — be-shlosha be-Marcheshvan sho'alin et ha-geshamim* — the *Mishnah* (on 10a) states that one begins asking for rain on the third of *Marcheshvan*, with Rabban Gamliel saying the seventh, and *Rabbi Elazar* rules the *halakhah* follows Rabban Gamliel, meaning we start saying *ve-ten tal u-matar* on the seventh of *Marcheshvan* in *Eretz Yisrael*.

If Rabbi Yochanan holds that *mashiv ha-ruach* and *ve-ten tal u-matar* are perfectly co-extensive, how can he rule like Rabbi Yehuda — meaning the transition at *Shemini Atzeret* — and simultaneously be consistent with the ruling that *ve-ten tal u-matar* is delayed until the seventh of *Marcheshvan*? If they occur together, they should both transition at *Shemini Atzeret* or both begin at *Zayin Marcheshvan*. The apparent inconsistency is glaring.

Rav Assi's first deflection: *gabra a-gabra ka ramit* — you are citing one authority against another, Rabbi Yochanan against Rabbi Elazar; that is not an internal contradiction. But the *Gemara* presses further. *Ve-iba'it eima lo kashya — kan lish'ol kan le-hazkir* — perhaps even for Rabbi Yochanan one could distinguish: *she'eilah* begins at *Zayin Marcheshvan* and *hazkarah* begins at *Shemini Atzeret*. But the *Gemara* rejects this immediately by citing Rabbi Yochanan's own explicit statement — *hitchil le-hazkir matchil lish'ol* — which rules out any such gap. Rabbi Yochanan himself says they are simultaneous.

### Land Distinction

The resolution: *ela lo kashya — ha lan ve-ha lehu* — this is not difficult; one ruling is for us and one is for them. The *Mishnah* about *Zayin Marcheshvan* — which might seem to describe *Eretz Yisrael* practice — is actually describing *Bavel*. In Babylon the crops remain in the field and premature rain would damage them; hence one delays both *hazkarah* and *she'eilah* until *Zayin Marcheshvan*. Rabbi Yehuda's position, which is what Rabbi Yochanan rules as *halakhah*, applies to *Eretz Yisrael*, where rain is needed and wanted right after *Sukkot*.

The *Gemara* then refines further. *Mai shna le-didan? De-it lan pirei be-dabra* — what distinguishes us in Babylon? We have produce still in the fields. But then the obvious response: *le-didehu nami it lehu olei regalim* — in *Eretz Yisrael* they also had a reason to delay, namely the *olei regalim*, the pilgrims who came up for *Sukkot* and needed time to return home before rain would fall upon the roads. The *Mishnah* itself gives this as the reason for the delay.

The answer: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Yehuda bi-zman she-ein Beit HaMikdash kayam* — Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that we switch over immediately after *Sukkot* applies specifically to the time when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, because without a *Beit HaMikdash* there are no *olei regalim* and no reason to delay in *Eretz Yisrael*. The *Mishnah* about *Zayin Marcheshvan*, on the other hand, reflects the period when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood and the pilgrim traffic made the delay necessary. *Ve-lo kashya — kan bi-zman she-Beit HaMikdash kayam, kan bi-zman she-ein Beit HaMikdash kayam.* With this distinction, the two *Mishnayot* are reconciled within *Eretz Yisrael* itself across two historical periods, without needing to invoke *Bavel* at all.

### Two Days Practice

Finally, the *Gemara* addresses a practical question for diaspora communities that observe two days of *Shemini Atzeret* — the first day (*Shemini Atzeret*) and the second day (*Simchat Torah*). *Heichi avdinan?* — how do we handle the transition? Since the identity of the "true" *Shemini Atzeret* is uncertain across the two days, the question is which *musaf* triggers the permanent switch to *mashiv ha-ruach*.

Rav's position: one begins saying *mashiv ha-ruach* at *musaf* of the first day, then stops at *mincha*, then stops again through *maariv* and *shacharit* of the second day, and resumes again only at *musaf* of the second day — essentially replaying the transition on both days. Shmuel objects: *achar she-asito kodesh* — once you have said *mashiv ha-ruach* at *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret*, you have declared the day to be *Shemini Atzeret*; going back and not saying it would be to implicitly declare it still *Sukkot*, which is internally inconsistent. Therefore Shmuel rules: one begins at *musaf* and continues through *mincha* to close out the day fully as *Shemini Atzeret*, then stops through *maariv* and *shacharit* of the second day — effectively treating *Simchat Torah* as if it might be *Shemini Atzeret* — and resumes at *musaf* of the second day.

Rava's ruling is the one we follow: *keivan she-hitchil shuv ein posek* — once one has begun, one does not stop. From *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret* onward, one says *mashiv ha-ruach* continuously without interruption. This is the accepted *psak*.

What the *Gemara* has accomplished in this final stretch is to surface and then resolve the apparent paradox embedded in Rabbi Yochanan's position. He rules that *hazkarah* and *she'eilah* are perfectly co-extensive — and simultaneously rules like Rabbi Yehuda, which in context means the immediate post-*Sukkot* transition. The resolution through *Bavel* versus *Eretz Yisrael*, and then further through the *Beit HaMikdash* versus post-*Beit HaMikdash* distinction, elegantly untangles what appeared to be an internal contradiction. And throughout, the *Gemara* has been probing a question it has not fully resolved: whether *mashiv ha-ruach* is best understood as a *ritztui she'eilah* — a preparatory praise that exists in service of the petition — or as an independent *shevach* with its own standing. Rabbi Yochanan's position, requiring perfect co-extensiveness in both directions, implicitly favors the former reading, while the practice of saying *mashiv ha-ruach* from *Shemini Atzeret* without yet saying *ve-ten tal u-matar* — which is in fact how we rule — points in a more complex direction.
