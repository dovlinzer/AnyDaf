# Gittin 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Woman & Seven Children

### Seven Sons Refuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: זוֹ אִשָּׁה וְשִׁבְעָה בָּנֶיהָ – אַתְיוּהּ לְקַמָּא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּקֵיסָר, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״אָנֹכִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּקֵיסָר, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה לְךָ אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים עַל פָּנָי״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״זוֹבֵחַ לֵאלֹהִים יׇחֳרָם״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״לֹא תִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְאֵל אַחֵר״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״וְיָדַעְתָּ הַיּוֹם וַהֲשֵׁבוֹתָ אֶל לְבָבֶךָ, כִּי ה׳ הוּא הָאֱלֹהִים בַּשָּׁמַיִם מִמַּעַל וְעַל הָאָרֶץ מִתָּחַת, אֵין עוֹד״. אַפְּקוּהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאִידַּךְ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: פְּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״אֶת ה׳ הֶאֱמַרְתָּ וְגוֹ׳ וַה׳ הֶאֱמִירְךָ הַיּוֹם״, כְּבָר נִשְׁבַּעְנוּ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ מַעֲבִירִין אוֹתוֹ בְּאֵל אַחֵר, וְאַף הוּא נִשְׁבַּע לָנוּ שֶׁאֵין מַעֲבִיר אוֹתָנוּ בְּאוּמָּה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda said: This** verse applies to the **woman and her seven sons** who died as martyrs for the sake of the sanctification of God's name. The incident occurred as follows: **They brought** in **the first** of the woman's sons **before the emperor** and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "I am the Lord your God"** (Exodus 20:2). **They** immediately **took him out and killed him. And they** then **brought** in **another** son **before the emperor,** and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "You shall have no other gods beside Me"** (Exodus 20:3). And so **they took him out and killed him. They** then **brought in** yet **another** son before the emperor, and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "He that sacrifices to any god,** save to the Lord only, **he shall be utterly destroyed"** (Exodus 22:19). And so **they took him out and killed him. They** then **brought** in **another** son, and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "You shall not bow down to any other god"** (Exodus 34:14). And so **they took him out and killed him. They** then **brought** in yet **another** son, and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One"** (Deuteronomy 6:4). And so **they took him out and killed him. They** then **brought** in yet **another** son, and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "Know therefore this today, and consider it in your heart, that the Lord, He is God in heaven above and upon the earth beneath; there is no other"** (Deuteronomy 4:39). And so **they took him out and killed him. They** then **brought** in yet **another** son, and **said to him: Worship the idol. He said to them:** I cannot do so, as **it is written in the Torah: "You have avouched the Lord** this day to be your God…**and the Lord has avouched you this day** to be a people for His own possession" (Deuteronomy 26:17–18). **We already took an oath to the Holy One, Blessed be He, that we will not exchange Him for a different god, and He too has taken an oath to us that He will not exchange us for another nation.**

The Gemara continues from the middle of 57b, following the *aggadata* about young children who threw themselves into the sea to avoid a life of moral degradation — a passage the Tosafot cites as evidence that, in extremis, even taking one's own life may constitute an act of *kiddush Hashem*, departing from the standard halachic framework that ordinarily governs martyrdom. With that context in mind, the Gemara introduces another story in the same spirit.

The verse that frames the entire discussion is from Tehillim: *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom, nechshavnu k'tzon tivcha* — "For Your sake we are slaughtered all day long, we are counted as sheep for the slaughter." Rabbi Yehuda applies this verse specifically to the story of a woman and her seven children.

They were brought before Caesar one by one and commanded to worship idols. The first son refused, citing *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* — "I am the Lord your God." He was taken out and executed. The second quoted *Lo yihyeh lecha elohim acherim al panai* — "You shall have no other gods before Me" — and was likewise killed. The third answered with *Zoveach la'elohim yochoram* — "Whoever sacrifices to foreign gods shall be destroyed." The fourth cited *Lo tishtachaveh l'el acher* — "Do not bow to another god." The fifth declared *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad*. The sixth quoted *V'yadata hayom vahasheivota el levavecha ki Hashem hu ha'elokim bashamayim mima'al v'al ha'aretz mitachat ein od* — "You shall know today and take to heart that the Lord is God in the heavens above and on the earth below; there is no other." One notices here a progression: the earlier sons quoted the *lo ta'asehs*, the prohibitions against idolatry, while the later sons shifted to positive affirmations of faith.

### Seventh Son & Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר: אֶישְׁדֵּי לָךְ גּוּשְׁפַּנְקָא וּגְחֵין וְשִׁקְלֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֵימְרוּ: קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ הַרְמָנָא דְּמַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲבָל עֲלָךְ קֵיסָר, חֲבָל עֲלָךְ קֵיסָר; עַל כְּבוֹד עַצְמְךָ כָּךְ, עַל כְּבוֹד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה! אַפְּקוּהּ לְמִיקְטְלֵיהּ, אֲמַרָה לְהוּ אִימֵּיהּ: יַהֲבוּהּ נִיהֲלִי וְאֶינַשְּׁקֵיהּ פּוּרְתָּא. אָמְרָה לוֹ: בָּנַיי, לְכוּ וְאִמְרוּ לְאַבְרָהָם אֲבִיכֶם: אַתָּה עָקַדְתָּ מִזְבֵּחַ אֶחָד וַאֲנִי עָקַדְתִּי שִׁבְעָה מִזְבְּחוֹת! אַף הִיא עָלְתָה לַגַּג וְנָפְלָה וּמֵתָה. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שְׂמֵחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** It was the youngest brother who had said this, and the emperor pitied him. Seeking a way to spare the boy's life, **the emperor said to him: I will throw down my seal before you; bend over and pick it up, so that** people **will say** that **he has accepted the king's authority [*harmana*].** The boy **said to him: Woe [*ḥaval*] to you, Caesar, woe to you, Caesar.** If you think that **for the sake of your honor** I should fulfill your command and do **this,** then **for the sake of the honor of the Holy One, Blessed be He, all the more so** should I fulfill His command. **As they were taking him out to be killed, his mother said to them: Give him to me so that I may give him a small kiss. She said to him: My son, go and say to your father Abraham, You bound one** son to the **altar, but I bound seven altars. She too** in the end **went up to the roof, fell, and died. A Divine Voice emerged and said: "A joyful mother of children"** (Psalms 113:9), as she raised her children to be devoted in their service of God.

When the seventh child was brought before Caesar, the emperor, frustrated by this point, tried a different strategy. He would throw down his imperial coin — bearing his own image — and instruct the boy to pick it up, so that onlookers would think he was bowing in submission to the emperor's authority. This was, of course, the imperial cult: the veneration of Caesar as a god. The child's response was striking: *Chaval alecha keisar, chaval alecha keisar* — "Woe to you, Caesar, woe to you." If you are so insistent on your own honor, he said, how much more is God insistent upon His honor? How then could I even make it *appear* that I am betraying God's honor?

The child was taken out to be executed. At that moment, his mother asked the soldiers to bring him to her for a moment so she could embrace him. She gathered all seven of her children — apparently before any of them had been killed — and spoke to them. She said: Go and tell Abraham your forefather, *Atah akadata mizbe'ach echad, va'ani akadeti shivah mizbachot* — "You bound one altar, while I have bound seven altars." Then she too went up to the roof, fell, and died. A *bat kol* then declared: *Em habanim smeichah* — "The mother of children rejoices." They had sanctified God's name.

It is worth pausing over the precise wording. The text uses *mizbe'ach* — altar — rather than *zevach* — sacrifice — which seems grammatically awkward. One senses that the language is deliberately softened, shifting away from the stark statement that Isaac was literally offered as a slaughter. This connects to a *midrash* in which Abraham did not actually slaughter Isaac — it says *al tishlach yadcha el hana'ar*, "do not lay your hand upon the lad" — but that the fire on the altar consumed him nonetheless, and a miracle reconstituted him. The *midrash* about the *eifer shel Yitzchak munach lifnei Hashem* — the ashes of Isaac resting before God — plays on exactly this reading.

This imagery proved extraordinarily potent. During the Crusades, Jewish communities facing forced conversion invoked the *akeidah* as a model for taking their own lives, and in some cases the lives of their children, rather than allow forcible baptism. The Crusade chronicles record parents using language almost identical to this passage — "Abraham offered one sacrifice; I offer all my children." It raises profound halachic questions: not merely taking one's own life, but actively taking a child's life. And the anguish was compounded when, on at least one occasion, the threatened assault never materialized, and the tragedy had been carried out in vain. Here in the Talmudic story, the mother took her own life but did not herself kill her children — and the text's use of *mizbe'ach* rather than *zevach* perhaps reflects that same hesitation about stating the thing too directly.

### Jeremiah's Lament

The Gemara now applies the verse from Tehillim — *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom* — to several other contexts, moving through a series of interpretations that carry us from literal martyrdom into the world of *talmud Torah*.

## Martyrdom Interpretations

### Brit Milah & Martyrdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: זוֹ מִילָה שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בַּשְּׁמִינִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** concerning the verse: "For Your sake we are killed all the day long" (Psalms 44:23), that **this** is referring to **circumcision, which was given for the eighth** day, as the blood of our newborn sons is spilled for the sake of the covenant with God.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi reads *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom* as referring to *brit milah*, which was *nitnah bashmini* — given on the eighth day. During Roman persecutions, the Romans frequently prohibited circumcision, in part because they considered it a disfigurement of the body, and Jews were repeatedly prepared to risk their lives in order to perform the commandment. Rashi offers a somewhat different reading — that sometimes the surgical procedure itself could lead to complications and death — but it seems more compelling to read the verse as referring to the willingness to give one's life, or risk it, for the sake of *milah*.

### Torah Tent Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמַּרְאִין הִלְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה בְּעַצְמָן, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל מִילֵּי לִיחְזֵי אִינִישׁ בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, בַּר מִשְּׁחִיטָה וְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** This verse was stated in reference to **Torah scholars who demonstrate the *halakhot* of slaughter on themselves,** meaning that they demonstrate on their own bodies how ritual slaughter should be performed and occasionally injure themselves in the process. This is **as Rava says: A person may demonstrate anything using himself** to illustrate the act **except for slaughter and another matter,** a euphemism for sexual intercourse.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish offers a reading that is, on the face of it, startling. He says *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom* refers to *talmidei chachamim shemarim hilchot shechitah be'atzman* — Torah scholars who demonstrate the laws of *shechitah* on themselves. When learning *hilchot shechitah* and wanting to illustrate the proper movement of the knife on the animal's neck, one slips — and that is *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom*.

This is a jarring juxtaposition. From Roman persecution and the Caesar demanding emperor worship, we have suddenly shifted to the *beit midrash* and someone demonstrating slaughter techniques. The Gemara itself adds: *d'amar Rava, kol milei lachzei inish b'nafshei bar mishechitah* — one may demonstrate anything on oneself except *shechitah*. Do not try this at home. There is also mention of *tzara'at umar'ot nega'im* — one should not demonstrate the appearance of leprous afflictions on one's own body, presumably because of *ayin hara*.

Yet the shift itself is theologically significant. Recall the arc of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai: the entire camera of Jewish life pivoted from the *Beit HaMikdash* and Roman Caesar and martyrdom to *ten li Yavneh v'chachameha* — the world of Torah replacing that other world. Here too, a verse that once described literal death at Roman hands is now given new meaning in the *beit midrash*.

### Torah Devotion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמְּמִיתִין עַצְמָן עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, כִּדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּמִית עַצְמוֹ עֲלֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: These** people in the verse **are Torah scholars who kill themselves over the words of Torah, in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish. As Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: The words of the Torah endure only for one who kills himself over them, as it is stated: "This is the Torah, when a man dies in a tent"** (Numbers 19:14).

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak completes the transformation. He reads *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom* as referring to *talmidei chachamim shememitim atzmam al divrei Torah* — Torah scholars who kill themselves, figuratively, over words of Torah. The literal martyrdom of the *churban* era becomes the self-sacrifice of the scholar who devotes himself entirely to learning.

This is grounded in the dictum of Reish Lakish: *ein divrei Torah mitkaymin ela bemi shememit atzmo aleihem* — words of Torah endure only in one who is willing to die for them — as the verse says, *zot hatorah adam ki yamut b'ohel* — "This is the Torah: when a person dies in the tent." The figurative death in the tent of Torah replaces the literal death *ki alecha horagnu kol hayom*.

## Destruction of Beitar

### Tefillin at Beitar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה קְצוּצֵי תְפִילִּין נִמְצְאוּ בְּרָאשֵׁי הֲרוּגֵי בֵיתֵּר. רַבִּי יַנַּאי בְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר: שָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת שֶׁל אַרְבָּעִים אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אַרְבָּעִים קוּפּוֹת שֶׁל שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא דְּרֵישָׁא, הָא דִּדְרָעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **of phylactery boxes were found on the heads of those killed in Beitar. Rabbi Yannai, son of Rabbi Yishmael, says:** There were found **three large baskets** each holding **forty *se'a*** of phylactery boxes. And **it was taught in a *baraita*:** There were **forty large baskets** each holding **three *se'a*.** The Gemara notes: **And** these Sages **do not disagree: This** Sage is referring to phylacteries **of the head,** whereas **this** Sage is referring to phylacteries **of the arm,** for owing to the different manners in which they are fashioned, they are also different in size.

The Gemara now turns to the destruction of Beitar. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *arbaim se'ah ktzitzai tefillin nimtze'u berashei harugei Beitar* — forty *se'ah* of *tefillin batim* were found on the heads of those slaughtered at Beitar. Forty *se'ah* is the measure required for a *mikvah*, which one listener noted is perhaps symbolically resonant, though the precise import requires further thought.

Rabbi Yannai b'Rabbi Yishmael disputes the figure: it was not forty *se'ah*, but *shalosh kupot shel arbaim arbaim* — three containers each of forty *se'ah*, three times the amount. A *braita* teaches yet another version: *arbaim kupot shel shalosh shalosh* — forty containers of three *se'ah* each. The Gemara resolves that these two versions do not in fact contradict: *had'reisha had'dara* — one refers to *tefillin shel yad* and the other to *tefillin shel rosh*. Rashi explains that the *tefillin shel rosh*, which requires four compartments, cannot be made as small as the *shel yad*; anyone who has seen the tiny *tefillin* discovered at Masada will understand the difficulty of fitting four *batim* into a single very small housing, and so the *shel rosh* would necessarily be larger — roughly three times the size of the *shel yad*.

The image of all these slaughtered people still wearing their *tefillin* is arresting. People in those days wore *tefillin* throughout the day. The *tefillin* represents both the *mitzvah* and the *parshiyot* written within — a symbol of the *Torah* world. And so the image of the *tefillin* on the heads of the slain intersects the three themes woven throughout this passage: the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the destruction of the people, and the destruction of the world of *Torah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: אַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין מוֹחַ נִמְצְאוּ עַל אֶבֶן אַחַת. עוּלָּא אָמַר: תִּשְׁעַת קַבִּין. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא וְאִיתֵּימָא שֵׁילָא בַּר מָרִי: מַאי קְרָאָה? ״בַּת בָּבֶל הַשְּׁדוּדָה, אַשְׁרֵי שֶׁיְשַׁלֶּם לָךְ וְגוֹ׳, אַשְׁרֵי שֶׁיֹּאחֵז וְנִפֵּץ אֶת עוֹלָלַיִךְ אֶל הַסָּלַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says: Four *kav* of brains** from children whose skulls were smashed **were found on one stone. Ulla says: Nine *kav*. Rav Kahana said, and some say** that it was **Sheila bar Mari** who said: **What is the verse** from which it is derived? **"O daughter of Babylon, marked for devastation; happy is he who shall repay you** your recompense for what you have done to us. **Happy is he who shall seize and dash your little ones against the rock"** (Psalms 137:8–9).

Rabbi Assi adds: *arba'a kabbin mo'ach nimtze'u al even achat* — four *kav* of brains were found on a single stone, from the smashing of skulls. Ulla says the figure was nine *kabbin*.

Rav Kahana (and some say it was Shila bar Mari) asks: what is the *pasuk* for this? The verse cited is from Tehillim 137: *Bat Bavel hashidudah, ashrei she-yeshalem lach, ashrei she-yo'chez venipetz et olalayich el hasala* — "O devastated Daughter of Babylon, praised is the one who repays you what you have done to us; praised is the one who seizes your infants and dashes them against the rock." This is a deeply challenging verse, one that has generated much contemporary discussion given its apparent expression of bloodlust. Here it is cited as evidence of what the Jewish people suffered — if this is what is considered appropriate repayment, then by inference this is what was done to them.

### Children of Zion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּנֵי צִיּוֹן הַיְקָרִים הַמְסוּלָּאִים בַּפָּז״ – מַאי ״מְסוּלָּאִים בַּפָּז״? אִילֵּימָא דַּהֲווֹ מְחַפִּי בְּפִיזָּא, וְהָאָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא: תַּרְתֵּי מַתְקָלֵי אִיסְתֵּירֵי פִּיזָּא נְחוּת בְּעָלְמָא – חֲדָא בְּרוֹמִי, וַחֲדָא בְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא! אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיוּ מְגַנִּין אֶת הַפָּז בְּיוֹפְיָין. מֵעִיקָּרָא, חֲשִׁיבִי דְרוֹמָאֵי הֲווֹ נָקְטִי בִּלְיוֹנָא דְגוּשְׁפַּנְקָא, וּמְשַׁמְּשִׁי עַרְסַיְיהוּ; מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, מַיְיתוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָסְרִי בְּכַרְעֵי דְּפוּרַיְיהוּ, וּמְשַׁמְּשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"The precious sons of Zion, comparable to fine gold"** (Lamentations 4:2). **What** is the meaning of the expression **"comparable to fine gold"? If we say that** it means **they were covered in fine gold [*piza*],** this is difficult; **but didn't the school of Rabbi Sheila say: Two *istira* weights of fine gold came down into the world, one in Rome and one in all** the rest of **the world.** If so, it is certainly impossible to cover the inhabitants of Jerusalem with fine gold, as there is not enough of it in the entire world to do so. **Rather,** this means that they would be so attractive **that they would disgrace fine gold because of their beauty. Initially the noblemen of Rome would keep an image** imprinted **on a seal** by their beds **and engage in sexual intercourse** opposite that image, so that they would beget children of similar beauty. **From this** point **forward,** from the time of the Great Revolt, **they would bring Jewish children, tie them to the foot of their beds, and engage in sexual intercourse** across from them, because they were so handsome.

The Gemara turns to the verse in *Eichah*: *B'nei Tzion ha-yekarim ham'sula'im bapaz* — "The precious children of Zion, esteemed above gold." What does *m'sula'im bapaz* mean? Does it mean they were literally covered in gold jewelry?

The Gemara rejects this: *d'bei Rabbi Shila* reports that only two measures of the finest gold, *pizi*, descended into the world — one in Rome, and one distributed across the entire rest of the world. There was nowhere near enough for every child in Zion to be coated in gold. Rather, *m'sula'im bapaz* means *she-hayu meganim et ha-paz be-yofyam* — they shamed gold by the superiority of their beauty. Gold jewelry paled compared to how these children looked.

The Gemara continues: originally, prestigious Romans would keep an image of a handsome person — a seal or portrait — on the wall of their bedchambers during intercourse, believing that what one envisions at the moment of conception influences the appearance of the resulting child. This goes back to some degree to the story of Yaakov and the striped rods. After Israel was taken captive, the Romans would instead *maiti benei Yisrael ve-asrei be-charei de-puryeihu* — bring Israelite captives and tie them at the foot of their beds — in order to produce beautiful offspring.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ חַד לְחַבְרֵיהּ: הָא, הֵיכָא כְּתִיבָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״גַּם כׇּל חֳלִי וְכׇל מַכָּה אֲשֶׁר לֹא כָתוּב בְּסֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת״. אֲמַר: כַּמָּה מְרַחַיקְנָא מִדּוּכְתָּא פְּלָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִינְּגֵד פּוּסְתָּא וּפַלְגָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מְטַאי לְגַבֵּיהּ לָא אִיצְטְרִיכִי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that it once happened that they did this to two children, and **one** of them **said to the other: Where is this** affliction **written** in the Torah? The other **said to him:** As it is written: **"Also every sickness, and every plague, which is not written in the book of this Torah"** (Deuteronomy 28:61). The first one **said: How far am I** in my studies **from this,** i.e., how much more would I have had to learn in order to reach this verse? The other **said: Had you gone on one and a half columns [*pusta*],** you would have reached this. The first child **said to** the other: **Had I reached this** verse, **I would not have needed you,** as I would have known on my own that the verse was speaking about this.

One Roman asks his friend: where is this terrible humiliation actually prophesied? The friend responds with the verse *gam kol choli ve-chol maka asher lo katuv be-sefer ha-Torah ha-zot* — "also every illness and every plague that is not written in this book of Torah" will befall you. Even the unimaginable, the unprecedented degradations, were encompassed in that catch-all phrase. The first man says: how far was that verse from where I was up to in my learning? His friend answers: about a page and a half. The man replies: had I gotten there myself, I wouldn't have needed to ask you. A strange little exchange — but it perhaps lightens the devastating material slightly.

### Beitar Synagogues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״עֵינִי עוֹלְלָה לְנַפְשִׁי מִכֹּל בְּנוֹת עִירִי״? אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת הָיוּ בִּכְרַךְ בֵּיתֵּר, וּבְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת הָיוּ בָּהּ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת מְלַמְּדֵי תִּינוֹקוֹת, וְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הָיוּ לְפָנָיו אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן, וּכְשֶׁהָיָה אוֹיֵב נִכְנָס לְשָׁם הָיוּ דּוֹקְרִין אוֹתָן בְּחוֹטְרֵיהֶן. וּכְשֶׁגָּבַר אוֹיֵב וּלְכָדוּם, כְּרָכוּם בְּסִפְרֵיהֶם וְהִצִּיתוּם בָּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "My eye affects my soul because of all the daughters of my city"** (Lamentations 3:51)? **There were four hundred synagogues in the city of Beitar, and in each and every one of them there were four hundred schoolteachers, and each and every one** of these teachers **had four hundred schoolchildren. And when the enemy entered there,** these schoolchildren **stabbed them with their pens [*beḥotreihen*]. And when the enemy prevailed and caught them, they wrapped** the children **in their scrolls and lit them on fire.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel: what does the verse *einai olela le-nafshi mi-kol benot iri* mean — "my eye afflicts my soul, more than all the daughters of my city"? *Arba mea batei kneisiyot hayu be-krach Beitar* — there were four hundred synagogues in the city of Beitar. Each synagogue had four hundred schoolteachers of young children. Each of those teachers had four hundred students. These are clearly hyperbolic numbers, but they evoke a vast and thriving world of Jewish education. When the enemy entered the city, they pierced the children with their staffs. When the enemy ultimately prevailed and captured the people, *karchum be-sifreihem ve-hitzitum ba-esh* — they wrapped the children and teachers in their books and set them ablaze. This is the same fate we associate with Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon, but here it is depicted as happening to an entire civilization of Torah scholars and students. One cannot read this without thinking of later destructions.

## Rabbi Yehoshua Rescues

### Ransom from Prison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה שֶׁהָלַךְ לִכְרַךְ גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּרוֹמִי, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: תִּינוֹק אֶחָד יֵשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים, יְפֵה עֵינַיִם וְטוֹב רוֹאִי וּקְווּצּוֹתָיו סְדוּרוֹת לוֹ תַּלְתַּלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** another *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Horayot* 2:5) relating to the fate of the Jewish children: There was **an incident involving Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya who** once **went to the great city of Rome,** where **they said to him: There is a child in prison** with **beautiful eyes** and **an attractive appearance, and his curly hair is arranged in locks.**

The Gemara presents the story of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya, who traveled to the great city of Rome. He was told: *tinok echad yesh be-veit ha-asurim* — there is one child in the prison, *yafeh einayim ve-tov ro'i ve-kvutzotav sdurot lo taltalim* — beautiful of eyes, handsome of appearance, his locks arranged in layers. Coming just after the discussion of the beauty of the *Benei Tzion*, the echo is unmistakable.

### Child's Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלַךְ וְעָמַד עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאֲסוּרִים, אָמַר: ״מִי נָתַן לִמְשִׁיסָּה יַעֲקֹב וְיִשְׂרָאֵל לְבוֹזְזִים״? עָנָה אוֹתוֹ תִּינוֹק וְאָמַר: ״הֲלֹא ה׳ זוּ חָטָאנוּ לוֹ וְלֹא אָבוּ בִדְרָכָיו הָלוֹךְ וְלֹא שָׁמְעוּ בְּתוֹרָתוֹ״. אָמַר: מוּבְטְחַנִי בּוֹ שֶׁמּוֹרֶה הוֹרָאָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, הָעֲבוֹדָה! שֶׁאֵינִי זָז מִכָּאן עַד שֶׁאֶפְדֶּנּוּ בְּכׇל מָמוֹן שֶׁפּוֹסְקִין עָלָיו. אָמְרוּ: לֹא זָז מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁפְּדָאוֹ בְּמָמוֹן הַרְבֵּה, וְלֹא הָיוּ יָמִים מוּעָטִין עַד שֶׁהוֹרָה הוֹרָאָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. וּמַנּוּ? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן אֱלִישָׁע.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **went and stood by the entrance to the prison. He said,** as if speaking to himself: **"Who gave Jacob for a spoil, and Israel to the robbers?"** (Isaiah 42:24). **That child answered by reciting** the continuation of the verse: **"Did not the Lord, He against Whom we have sinned, and in Whose ways they would not walk, neither were they obedient to His law?"** Rabbi Yehoshua **said: I am certain that,** if given the opportunity, **this** child will **issue** halakhic **rulings in Israel,** as he is already exceedingly wise. He said: I take an oath **by the Temple service that I will not move from here until I ransom him for whatever** sum of **money they set for him. They said** that **he did not move from there until he ransomed him for a great** sum of **money, and not** even **a few days** had passed when this child then **issued** halakhic **rulings in Israel. And who was** this child? This was **Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha.**

Rabbi Yehoshua went and stood at the entrance of the prison. He quoted the first half of a verse from Yeshayahu: *Mi natan limshisah Ya'akov ve'Yisrael le'vozezim* — "Who gave Jacob over to plunder and Israel to spoilers?" The child, from inside, responded with the second half of the verse: *Halo Hashem zo chatanu lo, velo avu bidrachav haloch, velo shamu betorato* — "Is it not the Lord, against Whom we sinned, in Whose ways they would not walk, and Whose Torah they did not heed?"

The child knew the end of the verse. Rabbi Yehoshua declared: *Muvtachni bo she-moreh hora'ah be'Yisrael* — I am confident that this child will rule on halachic matters in Israel. And he took an oath: *she'eini zaz mikan ad she'efdenu bechol mamon sheposkin alav* — I will not leave here until I redeem him, no matter the price demanded.

This raises an immediate question in light of the earlier *Gemara* about *tikkun olam* — the rabbinic enactment not to redeem captives for more than their worth, lest it encourage further taking of captives. Tosafot addresses this directly: *ki itcha sakanat nefashot podin shvuyim yoter mikdei dameihen* — when there is a genuine threat to life, one redeems captives for any sum. The earlier limitation applies only where life is not at risk. This is an important qualification of the earlier *Gemara* that had not been stated there explicitly.

Tosafot offers a second explanation: *mishum de'mufla be'chochmah hayah* — because this child was so obviously exceptional in his Torah knowledge, one might make an exception for him. This raises a perennial tension: on one hand, we resist distinguishing between lives based on social worth; on the other hand, *Mishnah Horayot* establishes that a *talmid chacham* takes precedence over all others.

### Rabbi Yishmael Identity

The Gemara reports: he did not leave until he redeemed him for a great sum of money, and it was not long before that child ruled on halachic matters in Israel. *U-manu* — and who was he? *Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha.*

## Rabbi Yishmael's Children

### Captors' Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּבְנוֹ וּבְבִתּוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן אֱלִישָׁע שֶׁנִּשְׁבּוּ לִשְׁנֵי אֲדוֹנִים. לְיָמִים נִזְדַּוְּוגוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד, זֶה אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ לִי עֶבֶד שֶׁאֵין כְּיוֹפְיוֹ בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ לִי שִׁפְחָה שֶׁאֵין בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ כְּיוֹפְיָהּ. אָמְרוּ: בּוֹא וְנַשִּׂיאֵם זֶה לָזֶה, וּנְחַלֵּק בַּוְּולָדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving the son and the daughter of Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha** the High Priest, **who were taken captive** and sold into slavery **to two** different **masters. After some time** the two masters **met in a certain place. This** master **said: I have a male slave whose beauty is unmatched in all of the world, and that** master **said: I have a female slave whose beauty is unmatched in all of the world. They said: Come, let us marry** these two slaves **to one another and divide the children** born to them between us, as they will certainly be very beautiful.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: there is a story about the son and daughter of Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha — himself a *kohen* of distinguished lineage — who were taken captive by two separate masters. We have just been told how beautiful these children were; their beauty made them desirable. *Liyamim nizdavgu sheneihem bemakom echad* — after some time, the two masters happened to meet. One said: I have a male slave of unparalleled beauty. The other said: I have a female slave more beautiful than any in the world. They agreed: let us have them marry one another, and we will divide the children between us. They placed them together in a room, treating them as commodities whose offspring would be split between owners.

### Siblings' Reunion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִכְנִיסוּם לְחֶדֶר. זֶה יָשַׁב בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית זֶה, וְזוֹ יָשְׁבָה בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית זֶה, זֶה אוֹמֵר: אֲנִי, כֹּהֵן בֶּן כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים, אֶשָּׂא שִׁפְחָה?! וְזֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: אֲנִי, כֹּהֶנֶת בַּת כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים, אֶנָּשֵׂא לְעֶבֶד?! וּבָכוּ כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, הִכִּירוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה, וְנָפְלוּ זֶה עַל זֶה וְגָעוּ בִּבְכִיָּה עַד שֶׁיָּצְאָה נִשְׁמָתָן. וַעֲלֵיהֶן קוֹנֵן יִרְמְיָה: ״עַל אֵלֶּה אֲנִי בוֹכִיָּה, עֵינִי עֵינִי יוֹרְדָה מַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** They secluded them in a room. **This** one, the son, **sat in one corner, and that** one, the daughter, **sat in the other corner. He said: I am a priest** and **the descendant of High Priests. Shall I marry a female slave? And she said: I am the daughter of a priest** and **the descendant of High Priests. Shall I be married to a male slave? And they wept all** through the **night. When dawn arrived they recognized each other** and saw that they were brother and sister. **They fell on each other and burst into tears until their souls departed** due to their great distress. **And with regard to them** and others like them, **Jeremiah lamented: "For these things I weep; my eye, my eye runs down with water"** (Lamentations 1:16).

Each sat in a separate corner of the room. He said to himself: I am a *kohen*, descended from *kohanim gedolim* — shall I marry a slave woman? She said to herself: I am a *kohenet*, the daughter of *kohanim gedolim* — shall I marry a slave? They wept through the entire night, presumably unable to see one another in the dark. When dawn broke and they could finally see each other, *hikiru zeh et zeh* — they recognized one another as brother and sister. *Ve-nafalu zeh al zeh ve-ga'u bi-vchiyah* — they fell upon each other and cried out, *ad she-yatzta nishmatan* — until their souls departed.

There is something worth noting about their initial reasoning. Neither knew the other person was a Jew. They refused on the grounds of their priestly status — they could not marry a slave, even having themselves become slaves. It is this instinctive clinging to identity and dignity, before any recognition of kinship, that makes the reunion all the more devastating.

On them, Yirmeyahu composed his lament: *al eileh ani bochiya, eini eini yordah mayim* — "For these I weep; my eye, my eye runs down with water."

## Tzafnat Bat Pnu'el

### Tzafnat's Assault

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת, וְצָפְנַת בַּת פְּנִיאֵל שְׁמָהּ; ״צָפְנַת״ – שֶׁהַכֹּל צוֹפִין בְּיוֹפְיָהּ, ״בַּת פְּנִיאֵל״ – בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים; שֶׁנִּתְעַלֵּל בָּהּ שַׁבַּאי כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. לְמָחָר הִלְבִּישָׁהּ שִׁבְעָה חֲלוּקִים, וְהוֹצִיאָהּ לְמוֹכְרָהּ. בָּא אָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְכוֹעָר בְּיוֹתֵר, אָמַר לוֹ: הַרְאֵנִי אֶת יוֹפְיָהּ. אָמַר לוֹ: רֵיקָא! אִם אַתָּה רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח – קַח, שֶׁאֵין כְּיוֹפְיָהּ בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי כֵן. הִפְשִׁיטָהּ שִׁשָּׁה חֲלוּקִים, וּשְׁבִיעִי קְרָעַתּוּ וְנִתְפַּלְּשָׁה בָּאֵפֶר, אָמְרָה לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אִם עָלֵינוּ לֹא חַסְתָּ, עַל קְדוּשַּׁת שִׁמְךָ (הַגִּבּוֹר) [הַגָּדוֹל] לָמָּה לֹא תָּחוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** There was **an incident involving a certain woman named Tzafenat bat Peniel.** And why was she called this? She was called **Tzafenat** because **they would all gaze [*tzofin*] at her beauty,** and she was called **bat Peniel** because she was **the daughter [*bat*] of the High Priest who served in the innermost sanctum [*lifnai velefnim*]** of the Temple. And it happened that she was taken captive and **her captor abused** and raped **her all night. The next day he dressed her in seven garments and took her out to sell her. A certain man who was especially ugly came** and **said to** the man who was selling her: **Show me her beauty. He said to him: Good-for-nothing, if you wish to buy** her then **buy** her, **for there is no beauty like hers in all of the world. The potential buyer said to the seller: Even so, I wish to see for myself. He removed** the **six** outermost **garments, and she** herself **tore the seventh, and rolled in ashes. She said before** God: **Master of the Universe,** even **if You have shown no pity to us,** and have allowed us to be disgraced in this way, **why have You not shown pity to the sanctity of Your mighty name** by which we are called?

Reish Lakish relates the story of a woman named *Tzafnat bat Pnu'el*. What does *Tzafnat* mean? *She-hakol tzofim be-yofyah* — everyone would gaze upon her beauty. *Pnu'el* means *bito shel kohen gadol she-shimesh lifnai ve-lifnim* — the daughter of a *kohen gadol* who served in the innermost chambers, the *lifnai ve-lifnim*, meaning the Holy of Holies. She was taken captive, and her captor abused her sexually the entire night. In the morning, he dressed her in seven garments and brought her out to sell her.

An exceedingly ugly man came and demanded to see her beauty before purchasing her. The captor said: if you want to buy her, buy her. The man insisted on seeing her. Six of her garments were stripped off. The seventh she herself tore away, rolled herself in the dust, and cried out to God.

### Challenge to God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָלֶיהָ קוֹנֵן יִרְמְיָה: ״בַּת עַמִּי חִגְרִי שָׂק וְהִתְפַּלְּשִׁי בָאֵפֶר, אֵבֶל יָחִיד עֲשִׂי לָךְ מִסְפַּד תַּמְרוּרִים, כִּי פִתְאוֹם יָבֹא הַשּׁוֹדֵד עָלֵינוּ״ – ״עָלַיִךְ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״עָלֵינוּ״; כִּבְיָכוֹל, עָלַי וְעָלַיִךְ בָּא שׁוֹדֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to her** and others like her, **Jeremiah lamented: "O daughter of My people, gird yourself with sackcloth and roll in ashes; make you mourning as for an only son, most bitter lamentation, for the spoiler shall suddenly come upon us"** (Jeremiah 6:26). **It is not stated: Upon you, but** rather **"upon us,"** for **the spoiler shall come, as it were,** both **over Me and over you.** God Himself shares this pain and His name is also disgraced.

Her words to God were: *Ribono shel olam, im aleinu lo chasata, al kedushat shimcha ha-gibor, lamah lo tachos* — "Master of the Universe, if You have no compassion on us, why will You not have compassion on Your great Name?" This is a striking moment in the Gemara — perhaps the first explicit articulation of what will become the central emotional register of *kinot*: a direct challenge to God, asking how He could allow this to happen.

For this, Yirmeyahu composed the lament: *biti ami chagri sak ve-hitpalshi be-afar* — "Daughter of my people, gird yourself with sackcloth and roll in the ashes; make mourning as for an only son, bitter lamentation." The phrase *ki pitom yavo ha-shoded aleinu* — "for the ravager will suddenly come upon us" — is notable: it says *aleinu*, "upon us," not *alayich*, "upon you." The Gemara observes: *ki kavyachol alai ve-alecha* — as if to say, it is upon Me and upon you. God Himself, as it were, is afflicted by the suffering of Israel. Yet this makes the challenge of the verse all the more acute: if God too is pained, how can God not defend His own name against the *shoded*?

## Societal Corruption

### Apprentice's Scheme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעָשְׁקוּ גֶּבֶר וּבֵיתוֹ וְאִישׁ וְנַחֲלָתוֹ״? מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו בְּאֵשֶׁת רַבּוֹ, וּשְׁוַלְיָא דְנַגָּרֵי הֲוָה. פַּעַם אַחַת הוּצְרַךְ (רַבּוֹ) לִלְוֹת, אָמַר לוֹ: שַׁגֵּר אִשְׁתְּךָ אֶצְלִי וְאַלְוֶנָּה. שִׁיגֵּר אִשְׁתּוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, שָׁהָה עִמָּהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים. קָדַם וּבָא אֶצְלוֹ, אָמַר לוֹ: אִשְׁתִּי שֶׁשִּׁיגַּרְתִּי לְךָ הֵיכָן הִיא? אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי פְּטַרְתִּיהָ לְאַלְתַּר, וְשָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהַתִּינוֹקוֹת נִתְעַלְּלוּ בָּהּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "And they oppress a man and his house, even a man and his heritage"** (Micah 2:2)? There was **an incident involving a certain man who set his eyes on his master's wife, and he was a carpenter's apprentice [*shulya*]. One time his master needed to borrow** some money, and his apprentice **said to him: Send your wife to me and I will lend her** the money. **He sent his wife to him,** and the apprentice **stayed with her for three days. He** then **went back to** his master **before** she did, and the master **said to him: Where is my wife whom I sent to you?** The apprentice **said to him: I sent her** back **immediately, but I heard that the youth abused** and raped **her on the way.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: what does the verse *ve-ashku gever u-veito ve-ish ve-nachalato* — "they oppressed a man and his household, a man and his inheritance" — mean? There was a man who lusted after his master's wife. He was a carpenter's apprentice. One day his master found himself in need of a loan. The apprentice, who had more available cash, was approached for help. Instead of simply lending the money, the apprentice said: send your wife to me and she will receive the loan. The master sent his wife. She stayed three days.

Then the apprentice came to the master and asked about his wife's whereabouts. The master said: I sent her to you. The apprentice replied: I gave her the money right away and sent her off. But what I heard, he said, was that *ha-tinokot nit'allu bah ba-derech* — the young men had sport with her on the way back. The implication is clear: the three-day absence is to be understood not as anything that occurred in the apprentice's house, but as evidence that she had been faithless on the road. This is, of course, false — a trap.

### Marriage & Servitude

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה? אָמַר לוֹ: אִם אַתָּה שׁוֹמֵעַ לַעֲצָתִי, גָּרְשָׁהּ. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי אַלְוְוךָ וְתֵן לָהּ כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. עָמַד זֶה וְגֵרְשָׁהּ, הָלַךְ הוּא וּנְשָׂאָהּ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְפוֹרְעוֹ, אָמַר לוֹ: בֹּא וַעֲשֵׂה עִמִּי בְּחוֹבְךָ. וְהָיוּ הֵם יוֹשְׁבִים וְאוֹכְלִים וְשׁוֹתִין, וְהוּא הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַשְׁקֶה עֲלֵיהֶן, וְהָיוּ דְּמָעוֹת נוֹשְׁרוֹת מֵעֵינָיו וְנוֹפְלוֹת בְּכוֹסֵיהֶן. וְעַל אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִתְחַתֵּם גְּזַר דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The master **said to** his apprentice: **What shall I do?** The apprentice **said to him: If you listen to my advice, divorce her. He said to him:** But **her marriage contract is large** and I do not have the money to pay it. The apprentice **said to him: I will lend you** the money, and **you will give her** payment of **her marriage contract.** The master **arose and divorced her,** and the apprentice **went and married her. When the time came** that the debt was due, **and he did not have** the means with which **to repay it,** the apprentice **said to** his master: **Come and work off your debt with me. And they,** the apprentice and his wife, **would sit and eat and drink, while he,** the woman's first husband, **would stand** over them **and serve them their drinks. And tears would drop from his eyes and fall into their cups,** and **at that time** the Jewish people's **sentence was sealed,** for remaining silent in the face of this injustice.

The master asked: what should I do? The apprentice said: divorce her. The master replied: her *ketubah* is too large; I cannot afford it. The apprentice said: I will lend you the money, and you can pay her *ketubah* with it. The master divorced his wife. The apprentice then went and married her. When the time came to repay the debt and the master had no means to do so, the apprentice said: come and work off your debt as my servant. And so there sat the apprentice and the former master's wife, eating and drinking, while the master stood and served them, pouring their wine.

### Master's Tears

His tears fell from his eyes into their cups. *U-be-otah sha'ah nechtam gezar din* — and at that moment, the decree was sealed. The Gemara understands this not merely as the fate of these individuals but as a symbol of the moral corruption that had consumed the entire society, and it was this moment that triggered the irreversible judgment against them.

Some say the final *makkah be-pattish* was *shetei petilot be-ner echad* — two wicks in one candle — meaning when women began entering into relationships with two men simultaneously. Again, the emphasis falls on sexual corruption and on the exploitation of the vulnerable.

It is worth noting that many of these devastating narratives — the story of the apprentice, the story of the woman in Beitar and the children, and others — are attributed to the same *amora*, Rav Yehuda. He seems to have been deeply engaged in trying to provide some theological reckoning with the catastrophe: alternating between the incomprehensible suffering that defies explanation and the model of *schar va-onesh* that seeks to locate the destruction in human failure. The same oscillation runs through *Eichah* itself: God, how could You allow this? — followed by: because we did not listen, because we did not walk in His ways. Neither answer is sufficient on its own, and the Talmud does not pretend otherwise.

## Sikrikon Land Law

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָקַח מִן הַסִּיקָרִיקוֹן וְכוּ׳: אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לוֹ: ״לֵךְ, חֲזֵק וּקְנִי״, אֲבָל בִּשְׁטָר – קָנָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אַף בִּשְׁטָר נָמֵי לֹא קָנָה, עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב לוֹ אַחְרָיוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the mishna, which states: If **one** first **purchased** land **from a Sicarius,** and afterward returned and purchased the same field from the prior landowner, so that he will be considered the legal owner of the field, his purchase is void. **Rav says: They taught** that the purchase is void **only** in a case where the prior owner **says to** the buyer when he came to acquire the field from him: **Go, take possession** of the field **and** thereby **acquire** it, as in such a case the prior owner can say that he did not actually mean to sell him the field. **But** if he sold it to him **with a bill** of sale, the buyer **acquires** the field. **And Shmuel says: Even** if he sold it to him **with a bill** of sale, the buyer **does not acquire** it **unless** the prior owner **writes him a guarantee** that if the field is repossessed by a creditor of the prior owner, the prior owner, who sold him the field, will compensate him for his loss, as by writing this guarantee he demonstrates that this is a true sale.

With the *aggadata* of the *churban habayit* concluded, the Gemara returns to the *Mishnah* and to halachic analysis of the *sikrikon* laws.

The background: during and after the Roman destruction, Romans or their agents would seize Jewish land — sometimes under threat to the original owner's life — and then sell it. The Mishnah developed a *takana* governing under what conditions a purchaser from the *sikrikon* acquires valid title, which required in its first stage that the buyer also purchase the property from the original Jewish owner. A central question is how to determine whether the original owner's subsequent sale is genuine or merely performed under duress to avoid trouble with the *sikrikon*.

Rav rules: the distinction between valid and invalid turns on the form of the second transaction. If the original owner merely said verbally *lech chazek ukeni* — go take possession of it — this is insufficient; we presume he said so only to avoid confrontation, not because he genuinely wished to sell. But if he executed a written *shtar* of sale, *kana* — the transaction is valid, because the formality of a document demonstrates genuine intent.

Shmuel disagrees: *af beshtar nami lo kana ad she-yichtov lo achrayut* — even a written *shtar* is insufficient unless it explicitly includes a clause of *achrayut* — a guarantee of indemnification, whereby the seller undertakes liability if anyone later seizes the property from the buyer. Writing a liability clause demonstrates such a high level of commitment that it can only reflect genuine intent to sell, even following a coerced transaction with the *sikrikon*.

A *braita* is cited in support of Shmuel's position.

### Wife & Husband Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לָקַח מִן הָאִשָּׁה, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִן הָאִישׁ – מִקָּחוֹ קַיָּים. מִן הָאִישׁ, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִן הָאִשָּׁה – מִקָּחוֹ בָּטֵל, עַד שֶׁתִּכְתּוֹב לוֹ אַחְרָיוּת. נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב? אָמַר לְךָ רַב: מַאי ״אַחְרָיוּת״ – נָמֵי שְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 5:2) **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If **one** first **purchased** a field belonging to a married woman **from the wife,** so that if her husband were to predecease or divorce her, the purchaser would then own it fully, **and** afterward **he returned and purchased** the same field **from the husband,** so that he will have the right to use it in the interim, **his purchase stands.** If he first acquired the field **from the husband, and** afterward **he returned and purchased** the same field **from the wife, his purchase is void, unless** the woman **writes him a guarantee.** This supports Shmuel's opinion that only when the prior owner writes the buyer a guarantee is it assumed that he sold him the field wholeheartedly. The Gemara asks: **Let us say that this** *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav,** who said that a written bill of sale suffices and a guarantee is not required. The Gemara answers: **Rav** could have **said to you: What** is the **guarantee** mentioned here? It **too** is referring to a **bill** of sale, as it suffices that she sell him the field with a bill of sale, and it is not necessary for her to write him a guarantee in addition.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar applies the same logic to a parallel case involving a husband and wife. When a woman has a lien on property through her *ketubah*, and the buyer purchases from the wife first and then from the husband, *mekacho kayam* — the purchase stands. But if he purchases from the husband first and then from the wife, *mekacho batel* — the purchase is void — because we presume the wife sold only to please her husband and not of genuine free will. This remains invalid *ad she-yichtov lo achrayut* — unless she writes a guarantee of indemnification in the *shtar*.

The Gemara asks: does this not contradict Rav, who required only a *shtar* without an explicit *achrayut* clause? Rav responds: *mai achrayut nami shtar* — by *achrayut* we mean simply a *shtar*; the document itself is what makes it binding. Rashi explains that *achrayut* here does not mean a lien on property but simply that the *shtar* makes the transaction final. Tosafot, however, suggests that the dispute between Rav and Shmuel is precisely whether *achrayut* must be written explicitly in a document or whether it is implied — with Rav holding that any *shtar* carries *achrayut* by implication.

## Three-Year Rule

### Three-Year Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לָקַח מִן הַסִּיקָרִיקוֹן, וַאֲכָלָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים בִּפְנֵי בְּעָלִים, וְחָזַר וּמְכָרָהּ לְאַחֵר – אֵין לַבְּעָלִים עַל לוֹקֵחַ שֵׁנִי כְּלוּם. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקָא טָעֵין וְאָמַר: ״מִינָּךְ זַבְנַהּ״ – אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי! אִי דְּלָא קָא טָעֵין וְאָמַר: ״מִינָּךְ זַבְנַהּ״, אֲפִילּוּ שֵׁנִי נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one purchased** land **from a Sicarius and consumed its** produce **for three years in the presence of the** prior **owner, and** then the one who purchased it from the Sicarius **returned and sold it to another** person, **the** prior **owner has no** claim **against the second buyer. What are the circumstances** of the case? **If** the second buyer **claims and says** to the prior owner: After buying the property from the Sicarius, the first buyer returned and **purchased it from you,** providing you with proper reimbursement, then **even the first** buyer should be deemed credible if he claims that he purchased the property from the prior owner. This is because he has been physically in possession of the property for three years, and this can serve as proof that he is in fact the legal owner. And **if** the second buyer **does not claim and say** to the prior owner: **He purchased** the property **from you,** then **even the second** buyer should **also not** retain possession of the property. This is in accordance with the principle that physical possession of property that is not accompanied by a claim that the property was legally acquired cannot serve as proof of ownership.

The Gemara presents a *baraita*: if someone purchased land from the *sikrikon*, ate its produce for three years in the presence of the original owners, and then sold it to a third party — *ein la-be'alim al lokeach sheni klum* — the original owners have no claim against the second purchaser.

This invokes the *halacha* of *chazakah* from Bava Batra: if one has been in possession of another's property for three years and the original owner lodges no objection, the possessor may, when challenged, claim that he purchased the property and has simply lost the *shtar*. One is not obligated to retain documents beyond three years when the owner watched and said nothing.

The Gemara presses: what exactly is the case? If the first buyer — who purchased from the *sikrikon* — is claiming to the original owner that he bought it from him and the three-year possession corroborates that claim, then even without involving the third party, the first buyer is already protected. And if the first buyer is *not* making that claim — if he says he never bought it from the original owner — then the three-year possession helps neither him nor any subsequent purchaser.

### Secondary Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא קָא טָעֵין לֵיהּ, וּכְגוֹן זֶה – טוֹעֲנִין לְיוֹרֵשׁ, וְטוֹעֲנִין לְלוֹקֵחַ. וְאִידַּךְ, אִי טָעֵין – אִין, וְאִי לָא טָעֵין – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: Actually,** it is referring to a case **where** the second buyer **does not claim** that the first buyer purchased the property from the prior owner. **And** although in general, physical possession of property that is not accompanied by a claim that the property was legally acquired cannot serve as proof of ownership, in a case **such as this** the court **makes a claim on behalf of an heir and** the court **makes a claim on behalf of a buyer.** Since heirs and buyers are generally unaware of the circumstances in which their predecessor obtained possession of the property, the court advances the claim on their behalf that it had been acquired legally. And as to **the other** party, the first buyer, **if** he **claims** that he purchased the property from the prior owner, **yes,** he retains possession of the property, as his physical possession of the property is accompanied by a proper claim. **But if he does not make** such **a claim,** he does **not** retain possession, because the court does not make the claim on his behalf.

Rav Sheshet resolves: *l'olam d'lo ka-ta'in lei* — the first buyer is simply not around or not making any claim at all. He has dropped out of the picture. The third party, having purchased the land, is now confronted by the original owner with a demand for return of the property, and does not know how to defend himself in *beit din*.

The Gemara explains that in such a case we assist the third party: we prompt him with the appropriate legal argument. We instruct him to say to the original owner: the man who sold you this land — from whom I purchased it — must have bought it from you, and he need not prove it because he was in possession for three years. The original owner saw him there for three years and made no objection; therefore we can presume the first buyer acquired it legitimately, and the third party holds it through him.

### Heirs & Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא מֵחֲמַת חוֹב וּמֵחֲמַת אַנְפָּרוּת – אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם סִיקָרִיקוֹן, וְאַנְפָּרוּת עַצְמָהּ – צְרִיכָה שֶׁתִּשְׁהֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 5:2): If property comes into one's possession **due to** payment **of a debt or due to unjust seizure [*anparot*]** of the land by a gentile, the previously mentioned laws of **Sicarii do not** apply. **And** the **unjustly seized land itself must remain** in the possession of the gentile who seized it **for** at least **twelve months** before another buyer can take possession of it.

The Gemara extends this principle: *tainan le-yores ve-tainan le-lokeach* — we provide this guided claim both for an heir and for a subsequent purchaser. Neither necessarily knows the history of the property or what they would need to say to defend their claim in court. If someone challenges an heir — saying that the father never legitimately owned the land — we step in and instruct the heir: your father possessed this for three years without objection; therefore you can claim he bought it from the original owner. The heir, like the third-party purchaser, does not need to have independent knowledge of all the legal arguments required to defend the title.

However, the Gemara qualifies: *ve-idach ei ta'an in, ei lo, lo* — the original first buyer, *Shimon* in the example, must defend himself if he is the one being challenged. We do not provide *him* with a claim he has not himself made. The prompted claim is available only to subsequent parties — heirs and purchasers — who come to the property without full knowledge of the surrounding circumstances.

## Debt & Theft

### No Sikrikon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם סִיקָרִיקוֹן! הָכִי קָאָמַר: סִיקָרִיקוֹן עַצְמָהּ – צְרִיכָה שֶׁתִּשְׁהֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, נָקְטִינַן: אֵין אַנְפָּרוּת בְּבָבֶל. וְהָא קָחָזֵינַן דְּאִיכָּא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אֵין דִּין אַנְפָּרוּת בְּבָבֶל. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בֵּי דַוּוָּר וְלָא אָזֵיל קָבֵיל, אֵימָא אַחוֹלֵי אַחֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection based on the fact that the twelve-month waiting period is relevant to the laws of Sicarii: **But didn't you say** that **the laws of Sicarii do not apply** in this case? The Gemara answers: **This is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** Property purchased from **the Sicarius himself must remain** in his possession for at least **twelve months.** **Rav Yosef says** that **we have a tradition** that **there is no unjust seizure of land in Babylonia.** The Gemara objects: **But don't we see that there is** unjust seizure of land in Babylonia? **Rather, say** that **there is no law of unjust seizure in Babylonia,** meaning that this law is not applied in Babylonia. **What is the reason** for this? **Since there is a regional seat of government [*bei davar*], and** yet the owner **does not go and complain** before it that his property had been seized, **say that he waived** his rights to the property and consequently is unable to bring a claim about it.

The Gemara presents a *baraita*: *haba machmat hov u-machmat amparut ein bo mishum sikrikon* — land seized for debt or taken by simple theft, without any threat to the original owner's life, does not fall under the *sikrikon* laws. The *sikrikon* framework — including the *takana* that after twelve months the original owner is presumed to have given up hope and the buyer need only compensate him a quarter of the land's value — applies only where the original transfer was coerced under threat to life. A simple creditor seizing land for debt, or a thief stealing it outright, does not trigger these provisions, and the original owner never loses his claim.

### Twelve Months

The *baraita* adds: *ve-amparut atzmo tzricha she-tisheh shneim asar chodesh* — and in a case of theft itself, twelve months are required. The Gemara clarifies: this means that the twelve-month waiting period before the second stage of the *takana* applies is itself a feature of the *sikrikon* law, not of the theft case. In theft, there is no *sikrikon* law at all — the original owner never loses his presumptive right to reclaim the property.

Rav Yosef then states: *naktinan, ein amparut be-Bavel* — we hold that there is no *din amparut* — no law of *amparut* — operative in Babylonia. The Gemara immediately challenges: surely theft of land occurs in Babylonia. Rav Yosef refines: what we mean is that there is no legal *din* of *amparut* in Babylonia. If someone seizes your land in Babylonia and you do not go to court to assert your claim — *keivan de-ika bei davar ve-lo azil kavel, amachulei achlei* — since there is a functioning court system and you chose not to file a claim in it, we presume you have waived your rights. The existence of a trustworthy legal framework changes the calculus entirely: a person who could have sought redress and did not is presumed to have relinquished the claim.

This is a striking observation about the relationship between legal systems and presumed intention. The Babylonian authorities commanded more trust in this respect than the Roman system — not necessarily because the Romans were unjust, but because Jews under Roman rule faced a very different relationship to imperial courts.
