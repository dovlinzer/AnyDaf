# Bava Metzia 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Borrower & Third Parties

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹאֵל אֶת הַפָּרָה וְשִׁלְּחָהּ לוֹ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ בְּיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ, אוֹ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ בְּיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹאֵל וּמֵתָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who borrowed a cow, and** the lender **sent it to** the borrower **by the hand of** his **son,** or **by the hand of his slave,** or **by the hand of his agent, or by the hand of** the borrower's **son,** or **by the hand of his slave,** or **by the hand of** the **agent of** the **borrower; and it died** on the way, the borrower **is exempt,** because the period of borrowing begins only once the cow reaches his domain.

The daf opens with the *mishna* at the bottom of 98b — a *mishna* that appeared earlier in the tractate when the *gemara* brought it in the context of a craftsman who has completed his work. Here we encounter it in its own setting. The *mishna* states: if someone borrows a cow and the owner sends it by way of his son, his slave, or his agent — or by way of the son, slave, or agent of the borrower — and the animal dies in transit, the borrower is exempt.

### Borrower Liability Rules

The background to this ruling requires some orientation. A *sho'el*, a borrower, bears the highest level of liability among the four categories of guardians. Unlike a *shomer chinam* (unpaid guardian), a *socher* (one who rents), or a *shomer sachar* (paid guardian) — all of whom have lower levels of liability — the borrower is obligated even for *onsin*, unavoidable accidents, regardless of whether there was any *peshiah*, any negligence or fault, on his part. Yet in this case the borrower is exempt, because the animal has not yet reached his hand. So long as it remains within the legal *reshut*, the domain, of the owner and has not been formally transferred, the borrower's liability has not yet commenced.

### Requested Delivery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַשּׁוֹאֵל: שַׁלְּחָהּ לִי בְּיַד בְּנִי, בְּיַד עַבְדִּי, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחִי, אוֹ בְּיַד בִּנְךָ, בְּיַד עַבְדְּךָ, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחֶךָ, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ הַמַּשְׁאִיל: הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלְּחָהּ לְךָ בְּיַד בְּנִי, בְּיַד עַבְדִּי, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחִי, אוֹ בְּיַד בִּנְךָ, בְּיַד עַבְדְּךָ, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחֶךָ, וְאָמַר לוֹ הַשּׁוֹאֵל: שַׁלַּח, וְשִׁלְּחָהּ וּמֵתָה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The borrower said to** the lender: **Send it to me by the hand of my son,** or **by the hand of my slave,** or **by the hand of my agent, or by the hand of your son,** or **by the hand of your slave,** or **by the hand of your agent. Or,** in a case **where the lender said** explicitly **to** the borrower: **I am sending it to you by the hand of my son,** or **by the hand of my slave,** or **by the hand of my agent, or by the hand of your son,** or **by the hand of your slave,** or **by the hand of your agent; and the borrower said to him: Send** it as you have said, **and he** then **sent it, and it died** on the way, then the borrower **is liable** to pay the lender the value of his cow. Since the borrower agreed to the cow's being brought to him by the hand of another, he bears liability from the moment the cow was transferred into that person's possession.

The *mishna* continues with the case where the borrower explicitly requests a particular mode of delivery, or where the lender proposes a method and the borrower consents with the word *shlach* — "send it." In either scenario, where the borrower has either named the agent or ratified the arrangement, the borrower is *chayav*. At that point, the object has entered his legal *reshut* even before it physically arrives.

### Return Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמַּחְזִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And, so too,** this is the *halakha* **at** the **time when** the borrower **returns it** to the lender. The borrower is absolved of liability only once the cow is transferred to the lender himself or to someone who the lender agreed will bring it to him.

The same principles apply in reverse when the object is being returned. If the borrower arranges the return unilaterally, he remains liable until the object reaches the owner's hand. If the return was arranged jointly or at the owner's direction, the borrower's liability concludes when the object leaves his physical possession — even if it is traveling by way of his slave.

## Slave as Intermediary

### Yad Eved Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ – חַיָּיב, יַד עֶבֶד כְּיַד רַבּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if the borrower agrees to have the lender send the cow **by the hand of** the lender's **slave,** and it died on the way, then the borrower is **liable.** The Gemara asks: But isn't the **hand of a slave** legally **like** the **hand of his master;** as long as the cow is in the possession of the lender's slave, it is not considered to have left the lender's possession. Why, then, is the borrower liable?

The *gemara* now homes in on a difficulty with the *mishna*. The *mishna* states that when the owner sends the animal *b'yad avdo* — by way of his slave — and it dies, the borrower is exempt. But this is surprising: is there not a principle of *yad eved k'yad rabo*, that the slave's hand is treated as the master's own hand? If so, how can the animal ever be said to have passed out of the owner's possession while it is being carried by his slave?

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי, דְּלָא קְנֵי לֵיהּ גּוּפֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents two resolutions: **Shmuel said:** The mishna is referring **to a Hebrew slave, whose** master **does not acquire** his **person.** Therefore, property in the slave's possession is not considered to be in his master's possession.

*Shmuel* offers a straightforward resolution: the *mishna* is dealing with an *eved Ivri*, a Hebrew slave, not an *eved Kna'ani*, a Canaanite slave. The principle of *yad eved k'yad rabo* applies in its fullest sense only to the Canaanite slave, whose very body is *kanuy*, entirely owned, by the master. A Hebrew slave involves a much lower level of ownership, and so we do not treat his actions as equivalent to the master's. Hence the *mishna* can consistently say that when the owner sends the animal via his Hebrew slave, the borrower is not yet liable — the animal has genuinely left the owner's domain — and yet when the borrower's side sends it via their agent, the borrower becomes *chayav*.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי, נַעֲשֶׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לֵיהּ: הַכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל וְהִיא תָּבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: You** may **even say** that the mishna is referring **to a Canaanite slave,** as this case may be **considered like** one in which the borrower **said to** the lender: **Hit** the cow **with a stick and** then **it will come** on its own to me. Just as in that case the borrower is liable once the cow leaves the lender's domain, so too in this case he is liable once the cow leaves the lender's domain, irrespective of the fact it was brought by the lender's slave.

*Rav* disagrees and offers a different answer: even if we grant that the *mishna* is speaking of a Canaanite slave, there is no difficulty. The case of sending an animal by way of a Canaanite slave is treated as equivalent to saying *hakishehu b'makla v'yavo* — "hit the animal with a stick and it will come to you." This is the formal act of *kinyan*, of acquisition, that applies to animals in particular, since one cannot physically drag a living animal the way one performs *meshicha* on an inanimate object. Striking the animal so that it moves toward the recipient constitutes a valid act of *meshicha*. Alternatively, since the Canaanite slave's body is entirely the possession of the master — *gufo kanuy* — the slave's carrying of the animal is treated less like one person transporting something for another and more like an extension of the animal itself moving, such that the borrower's act of acquiring the animal can be complete in this fashion. This is how *Rav* explains that even in the case of a Canaanite slave, the borrower can be *chayav*.

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַשּׁוֹאֵל הַפָּרָה וְשִׁלְּחָהּ לוֹ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ – חַיָּיב. בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one borrows a cow; and,** with the agreement of the borrower, the lender **sends it to** him **by the hand of his son** or **by the hand of his agent;** and it dies on the way, then the borrower is **liable.** If the lender sent it **by the hand of his slave,** then the borrower is **exempt.** The final clause appears to contradict the mishna's ruling.

A *braita* is then cited that appears to contradict this framework: *hashoel para v'shilchah lo b'yad bno, b'yad shlucho — chayav; b'yad avdo — patur*. This *braita* states explicitly that when the animal is sent by way of the slave — without distinguishing type of slave — the borrower is *patur*, not liable. This generates an apparent contradiction with the *mishna* which, on *Rav*'s reading, holds the borrower liable even when a Canaanite slave delivers the animal.

## Resolving Sources

### Shmuel Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל: מַתְנִיתִין בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי, בָּרַיְיתָא בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי. אֶלָּא לְרַב קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Granted, according to** the resolution of **Shmuel,** one can explain that **the mishna** is referring **to a Hebrew slave,** whereas **the *baraita*** is referring **to a Canaanite slave. But according to** the resolution of **Rav,** who maintained that the mishna is referring to a Canaanite slave, the contradiction is **difficult.**

For *Shmuel*, reconciliation is easy. The *mishna* speaks of a Hebrew slave (*eved Ivri*), and the *braita* speaks of a Canaanite slave (*eved Kna'ani*). Each source is internally consistent, and the two can coexist without difficulty.

### Rav's Difficulty

For *Rav*, however, the contradiction is genuine. Having argued that even a Canaanite slave can serve as the vehicle for a valid *kinyan*, *Rav* is left with one source ruling *chayav* and another ruling *patur* — both apparently referring to the same type of slave.

### Rav's Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: לָא תֵּימָא נַעֲשָׂה כְּאָמַר לוֹ, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: בְּאָמַר לוֹ הַכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל וְהִיא תָּבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a modified version of Rav's resolution: **Rav** could **say to you: Do not say** in explanation of the mishna that the case may be **considered like** one in which the borrower **said to** the lender: Hit the cow with a stick and then it will come on its own. **Rather, say** that it **is referring to** a case where the borrower actually **said to** the lender: **Hit** the cow **with a stick and** then **it will come** to me on its own. Accordingly, one can explain that the *baraita* is referring to a case where he did not say this, and therefore, as long as the cow is still in the possession of the slave, the borrower is not liable.

*Rav*'s resolution is to reinterpret the *mishna*. When the *gemara* originally said *na'aseh k'omeir* — "we treat it as if he said" *hakishehu b'makla v'yavo* — that formulation was imprecise. *Rav* now corrects himself: the *mishna* is not a case of analogy or legal fiction. Rather, *b'amar lei* — the *mishna* must actually be referring to a case where the borrower literally said those words: "hit the animal and send it to me." In that case, and only in that case, is the borrower *chayav* once the animal has left the owner's *reshut*. If the borrower merely said "send it" without specifying *hakishehu b'makla*, then even on *Rav*'s view the borrower would not be *chayav* until it arrives in his hand.

### Hekisha Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַשְׁאִילֵנִי פָּרָתְךָ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: בְּיַד מִי? וְאָמַר לוֹ: הַכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל וְתָבֹא. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָאת מֵרְשׁוּת מַשְׁאִיל וּמֵתָה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **As** it **was stated:** One said to another: **Lend me your cow. And** the lender **said to** the borrower: **By whose hand** shall I send it? **And** the borrower **said to him: Hit** the cow **with a stick and** then **it will come** on its own to me. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** that **Rav says:** In this case, the *halakha* is that **once** the cow **left the domain of the lender and** then **it died,** the borrower is **liable.**

The *gemara* attempts to support *Rav*'s position from a *braita* that describes exactly this scenario: the borrower asks for the cow, the lender asks how it should be sent, and the borrower says *hekishah b'maklo v'hi tavo* — "hit it with your stick and it will come." *Rav Nachman* citing *Rabbah bar Avuha* citing *Rav* teaches that as soon as the animal exits the owner's *reshut*, it is the borrower's *chiyuv* if it dies. The *gemara* then asks whether this *braita* lends support to *Rav*'s position.

### Rav Ashi Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה חֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹאֵל לִפְנִים מֵחֲצֵרוֹ שֶׁל מַשְׁאִיל, דְּכִי מְשַׁלְּחָהּ לַהּ – וַדַּאי לְהָתָם אָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the *baraita* is not a conclusive proof of Rav's opinion. It is possible to say: **With what are we dealing here;** with a case **where** the **courtyard of** the **borrower** is situated **further in** from the public domain **than** the **courtyard of** the **lender,** such **that when** the lender **sends** the cow to him, **it will certainly go to there.** Consequently, the borrower is willing to accept liability from the moment the lender sends it.

*Rav Ashi* objects: that *braita* cannot serve as general support, because it is addressing a very particular case — one in which the borrower's *chatzer* is situated *lifnim michatzer shel mash'il*, within the owner's larger *chatzer*. Only in that topographic arrangement does the *braita*'s ruling make sense.

### Chatzer B'Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאִיכָּא גַּזְיָיתָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָא סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ דִּלְמָא קָיְימָא הָתָם וְלָא אָתְיָא אָזְלָא לְהֶדְיָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּסָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if that is the case addressed in the *baraita*, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? It is obvious. The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** for the case of a courtyard in **which there are corners. Lest you say:** The borrower **does not rely** on the possibility that the animal will come to him, as **perhaps** the animal will go and **stand there** in one of the corners **and not come straight** to the borrower's courtyard, the *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that even in such a case **he relies** on the assumption that the cow will nevertheless come to him, and so he accepts liability.

The specific arrangement *Rav Ashi* envisions is a large private courtyard belonging to the owner, within which the borrower holds a smaller domain. In this configuration, when the borrower says "hit the animal and send it to me," the animal need not pass through a *reshut harabim* at all — it travels entirely within enclosed private space. *Rashi* explains that this is crucial: once the animal is struck and set in motion toward the borrower's domain, there is no intermediate public space where it might stray, and so the borrower's assumption that it will arrive is well-founded, sufficient to establish his *chiyuv* from the moment it leaves the owner's possession.

One might then ask what the *braita* adds beyond the obvious. The *gemara* answers: the novelty lies in a case where there are intermediate spaces within the large *chatzer* — gaps or sub-sections within the private domain through which the animal might wander rather than travel directly. The *braita* teaches that even so, the borrower's *da'at*, his reliance that the animal will come, is established and his liability attaches.

The *Ra'avad*, cited in the *Shitta Mekubetzet*, adds a related point: the arrangement must specifically be that the borrower's domain lies within the owner's domain, not the reverse. If the owner's *chatzer* were inside the borrower's *chatzer*, then the owner's ordinary movement of the animal — including moving it out toward the *reshut harabim* — would routinely pass through the borrower's domain, and so the act of sending the animal in that direction would not carry the same unambiguous meaning of a formal transfer to the borrower.

## Meshicha & Guardianship

### Huna: Use Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַשּׁוֹאֵל קַרְדּוֹם מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, בִּקַּע בּוֹ – קְנָאוֹ, לֹא בִּקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says:** In the case of **one who borrows an ax from another,** once **he has chopped** wood **with it, he has acquired it,** but as long as **he has not chopped** wood **with it, he has not acquired it.**

The discussion now transitions to a related but distinct question. *Huna* states: *hashoel kardom mechaveiro — baka bo, kena'o; lo baka bo, lo kena'o*. If I borrow an axe from my friend, I have not fully "acquired" it for the purposes of the loan until I have actually used it. Merely taking it, performing the formal act of *meshicha*, does not suffice.

### Why Use Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לׇאוֹנָסִין – מַאי שְׁנָא פָּרָה דְּמִשְּׁעַת שְׁאֵילָה? אֶלָּא לַחֲזָרָה, בִּקַּע בּוֹ – לָא מָצֵי הָדַר בֵּיהּ מַשְׁאִיל, לֹא בִּקַּע בּוֹ – מָצֵי מַשְׁאִיל הָדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: With regard **to what** matter does one acquire the ax? **If we say** he has acquired it **for** the purpose of being liable for **unavoidable mishaps,** i.e., his liability as a borrower begins once he uses the ax, then one could ask: **What is different** when one borrows **a cow, that** one is liable as a borrower **from the moment of borrowing,** even before one makes use of it? **Rather,** Rav Huna refers **to** one's right of **retraction,** as follows: Once the borrower **has chopped** wood **with it,** the **lender cannot renege on** his commitment to lend the item, but as long as the borrower **has not chopped** wood **with it,** the **lender can renege on** his commitment to lend the item and prevent the borrower from borrowing the item.

*Rashi* explains the underlying principle: *ein meshicha konah b'shomrim* — the standard act of *meshicha* that creates ownership for a purchaser does not create the *chiyuv* of a *shomer*, a guardian. The borrower is a species of *shomer*, and so the same formal act of taking possession that would close a sale does not automatically generate the guardian's obligation. What then does? Actual use. The ruling does not concern liability for *onsin*, which would be the borrower's concern once the loan is effective — rather, it concerns the point at which the loan becomes irrevocable. If the borrower has not yet used the axe, the lender can retract: *lo baka bo, matzi mash'il hadar beih* — the owner may say, I changed my mind, please return it. But once the borrower has used it, the loan is fixed and the lender cannot rescind.

### Me'ila Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַמַּשְׁאִיל קַרְדּוֹם שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – מָעַל לְפִי טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה שֶׁבּוֹ, וַחֲבֵירוֹ מוּתָּר לְבַקֵּעַ בּוֹ לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with this opinion, Rav Huna **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami, as Rabbi Ami says: One who lends** another **an ax** that is **of** property **consecrated** to the Temple treasury **has** there by **misused** consecrated property. He is liable to pay the Temple treasury **according to the financial advantage** he received from lending the ax. **And** despite this, that **other** person, i.e., the borrower, is **permitted to chop** wood **with it *ab initio*.**

This position is then contrasted with that of *Rabbi Ami*. *Rabbi Ami* states: if the lender has a *kardom shel hekdesh* — an axe that is temple property — and lends it out, the lender is *chayav* in *me'ila*, the misappropriation of temple property, *lefi tovat hana'ah shebo* — according to the small benefit he derived from having the axe available to lend. And the borrower, *Rabbi Ami* continues, is permitted to use the axe *l'chatchila*, as a matter of right, because once it has been lent and the lender has already incurred *me'ila*, the axe is no longer *hekdesh* in the borrower's hands.

### Rabbi Ami Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לֹא קְנָאוֹ – אַמַּאי מָעַל? וְאַמַּאי חֲבֵירוֹ מוּתָּר לְבַקֵּעַ בּוֹ לְכַתְּחִילָּה? נַיהְדְּרֵיהּ וְלֹא לִיקְנְיֵיהּ וְלָא לִימְעוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how it is apparent that Rabbi Ami disagrees with Rav Huna: **And if** one holds that the borrower **does not acquire** the ax from the moment of borrowing, **why has** the lender **misused** consecrated property? To be liable for misuse of consecrated property, one must acquire the item, thereby removing it from the Temple treasury. **And** furthermore, **why is** that **other** person **permitted to chop** wood **with it *ab initio*?** If the borrower does not acquire the ax from the moment of borrowing, **let** the borrower **return** the ax **and not acquire it, and** consequently the lender **will not have misused** consecrated property, as it will have turned out that nothing significant occurred. Rather, it is evident that Rabbi Ami holds that the act of lending is fully completed as soon as the borrower takes the ax, even before he has used it.

*Rabbi Ami*'s position thus stands in explicit disagreement with *Rav Huna*: *meshicha* is *koneh* for *shomrim*, it creates the borrower's legal acquisition even before use, and this is the case even *mid'oraita* — on a Torah level. The taking of the axe is sufficient to effect the legal transfer, such that the lender is *chayav* in *me'ila* and the borrower is free to use the object.

### Rabbi Elazar View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁתִּקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בַּלָּקוֹחוֹת, כָּךְ תִּקְנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בַּשּׁוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, with this opinion, Rav Huna **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says:** In the **same way in which** the Sages **instituted for buyers** that they can acquire an item by **pulling** it, and from that point the transaction is completed, **so** too **they instituted for bailees** that their rights and responsibilities are initiated by their **pulling** the item they agreed to safeguard, and from that point the transfer of the item to the bailee is completed.

*Rabbi Elazar* takes a position that navigates between the two. He agrees that *meshicha* works to create the *shomer*'s obligation — *kederech shetiknu meshicha belokochot, kach tiknu meshicha b'shomrim*, just as the Sages enacted that *meshicha* functions as a formal acquisition for buyers, so too did they enact that it functions to create the guardian's legal status. However, for *Rabbi Elazar* this is explicitly *mi'derabbanan*, by rabbinic enactment, not a *d'oraita* rule.

### Baraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁתִּקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בַּלָּקוֹחוֹת – כָּךְ תִּקְנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בַּשּׁוֹמְרִים, וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁקַּרְקַע נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה – כָּךְ שְׂכִירוּת נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This** opinion of Rabbi Elazar **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as** the Sages **instituted for buyers** that they can acquire an item by **pulling** it, **so** too **they instituted for bailees** that their rights and responsibilities are initiated by them **pulling** the item they agreed to safeguard. **And just as** **land is acquired** either **through** the buyer giving **money** to the seller, or **by** the seller giving the buyer **a bill** of sale, **or by** the buyer performing an act of **taking possession, so** too, **a rental is acquired** either **through** the renter giving **money** to the owner, or **by** the owner giving the renter a rental **document, or by** the renter performing an act of **taking possession.**

A *braita* is then cited in support of this position, stating explicitly: just as *meshicha* was enacted for purchasers, so too was it enacted for guardians. The same *braita* goes on to address *schirus*: just as land is acquired through money, a *shtar* (contract), or *chazaka*, so too a lease (*schirus*) of land is effected through money, a *shtar*, or *chazaka*.

### Land Lease Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכִירוּת מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיהּ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שְׂכִירוּת קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the assumption that the *baraita* is referring to rental of movable property, the Gemara asks: With regard to the case of **renting** in the *baraita*, **what is its purpose,** i.e., why is it mentioned in connection with acts of acquisition that are effective with regard to land? **Rav Ḥisda said:** The *baraita* is referring to the **rental of land.**

One might wonder what land leases have to do with the preceding discussion of guardians and *meshicha*. *Rav Chisda* clarifies: *schirus karka* — we are speaking of leasing land, not movable objects. The acquisition methods for real property — payment, written contract, and *chazaka* (physical possession conducted openly in a manner consistent with ownership) — are the operative modes for a land lease as well. Giving someone a set of keys to the property, notably, does not in itself constitute a formal act of *kinyan* in this framework; one must actually pay, document, or be present on the land in the manner of an owner.

## Theft: Hedyot vs. Hekdesh

### Date Bulk Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּגָזֵיל חֲבִיצָא דְתַמְרֵי מֵחַבְרֵיהּ וְאִית בַּהּ חַמְשִׁין תַּמְרֵי, אַגַּב הֲדָדֵי מִזְדַּבְּנָן בְּחַמְשִׁין נְכֵי חֲדָא, חֲדָא חֲדָא מִזְדַּבְּנָן בְּחַמְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the mention in the previous discussion of one who misuses consecrated property, the Gemara cites a related matter. **Shmuel says:** In the case of **one who robs another of a cake [*ḥavitza*] of** pressed **dates, and in** the cake **there are fifty dates,** which, when sold **together, sell for fifty-less-one** *perutot* and when they are sold **one by one, they sell for fifty** *perutot*, the sum that the robber is liable to pay as compensation to the robbery victim depends on who the robbery victim is.

*Shmuel* now introduces a case concerning theft and the valuation of damages. If a *gazlan* steals a large barrel of dates — fifty dates packed together — and those fifty dates, sold wholesale as a lot, fetch forty-nine units, while sold individually they would fetch fifty, the question is what the thief must pay back. *Shmuel* rules: *l'hedyot meshalem chamishin naki chada* — when stealing from a normal person, the thief pays back forty-nine, the wholesale price.

### Hekdesh Full Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהֶדְיוֹט – מְשַׁלֵּם חַמְשִׁין נְכֵי חֲדָא, לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ – מְשַׁלֵּם חַמְשִׁין וְחוּמְשַׁיְיהוּ, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּמַזִּיק – דְּלָא מְשַׁלֵּם חוּמְשָׁא. דְּאָמַר מָר: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ״ – פְּרָט לְמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** If one robbed, and is paying compensation **to, a common person [*hedyot*], he pays fifty-less-one** *perutot*. If one robbed another of a cake that was consecrated to the Temple treasury and he is paying compensation **to** the **Temple** treasury, **he pays fifty** *perutot* **and** an additional **one-fifth** of the value as a fine for having misused consecrated property **for each** one of the cakes. **This is not so with regard to one liable for** causing **damage** to consecrated property, **who does not pay the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **as the Master says:** The verse states: **"And a man, if he eats sacrificial** food in error, he should add its fifth on it" (Leviticus 22:14). By specifying that one who eats consecrated property is liable to pay the one-fifth payment, the verse **excludes one who is liable for** causing **damage** from that requirement.

The rule for temple property is different. *Lehekdesh meshalem chamishin v'chomsho* — for *hekdesh*, the thief pays back the full fifty units plus the *chomesh*, the additional fifth that *me'ila* requires as a penalty for misappropriating temple property.

A further distinction follows: *mah she'ein ken b'mazik d'lo meshalem chomsha* — this *chomesh* does not apply to one who merely damages temple property. The *mazik*, the one who causes damage without misappropriation, does not pay the additional fifth. The textual basis is the verse *ish ki yochal kodesh — prat l'mazik*: the Torah's language of "eating" sacred food (applied by analogy from *trumah* to *hekdesh* via a *gezeira shava*) implies intentional use, which excludes the case of damage. Causing damage to temple property thus incurs regular tort liability but not the *chomesh* of *me'ila*.

### Bulk Discount Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: לְהֶדְיוֹט אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם חַמְשִׁין נְכֵי חֲדָא? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אֲנָא חֲדָא חֲדָא הֲוָה מְזַבְּנִינָא לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye objects to this: Why,** when he pays compensation **to a common person, does he pay fifty-less-one? Let** the victim **say to** the robber: **I would have sold them one by one** and received fifty *perutot* for them; you should therefore compensate me for that entire amount.

The question is then raised by *Bibi bar Abaye*: why must the thief pay the wholesale price to the ordinary victim? Couldn't the owner of the dates say, "I sell retail, not wholesale — I would have sold them one by one, and you owe me each date's individual value"?

### Field Damage Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״שָׁמִין בֵּית סְאָה בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׂדֶה״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: We learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 55b): If an animal causes damage to another's field, the court **appraises** a large piece of land with **an area required for** sowing **one *se'a* of seed [*beit se'a*] in that field,** including the garden bed in which the damage took place. The court appraises how much it was worth before the animal damaged it and how much is it worth now, and the owner must pay the difference. The court appraises not only the garden bed that was eaten or trampled, but rather the depreciation in value of the bed as part of the surrounding area. This results in a smaller payment, as the damage appears less significant in the context of a larger area. In this case as well, the value of the entire cake is evaluated, not what it would be worth were one to divide it into smaller units.

*Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* responds: *bishamin beit se'ah b'oto sadeh t'nan* — we assess damage based on the value of a *beit se'ah* within that field, meaning we evaluate loss against the whole field rather than the individual items destroyed. Just as when an animal enters a field and eats a portion, we do not calculate the precise value of each stalk consumed but rather assess the proportional diminution of the entire field's value — and the field assessed as a whole is always valued at the wholesale rate — so too here. The rule reflects a general principle of damage assessment that looks to aggregate value rather than retail per-unit pricing.

## Me'ila Requirements

### Stone Not Me'ila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל דִּין הֶדְיוֹט לָאו כְּדִין גָּבוֹהַּ דָּמֵי? וְהָתְנַן: נָטַל אֶבֶן אוֹ קוֹרָה מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ – לֹא מָעַל, נְתָנָהּ לַחֲבֵירוֹ – הוּא מָעַל, וַחֲבֵירוֹ לֹא מָעַל. בְּנָאָהּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – לֹא מָעַל עַד שֶׁיָּדוּר תַּחְתֶּיהָ בְּשָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that Shmuel holds** that the ***halakha* of** compensation paid to **a common person is not like** the ***halakha* of** compensation paid to **the Most High,** i.e., to the Temple treasury? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 19b): One who physically **took a stone or** a cross **beam from** among items **consecrated** to the Temple treasury is **not** considered to have **misused** consecrated property. If **he gave it to another,** he is considered to have **misused** consecrated property, **but** that **other** person is **not** considered to have **misused** consecrated property. If, after taking a stone or cross beam that was consecrated property one **built it into** the structure of **his house,** he is **not** considered to have **misused** consecrated property **until he resides underneath it,** thereby deriving benefit **of** the **value of one *peruta*.**

A *braita* is introduced that details the stages at which *me'ila* occurs for temple property: *natal even o korah mehekdesh — lo maal*. If someone takes a stone or a wooden beam from *hekdesh*, that act alone does not yet constitute *me'ila*.

### Giving to Friend

The *braita* continues: *Nesanah l'chavero — hu maal, v'chavero lo maal*. If that person gives the stone or beam to a friend, the original taker has now committed *me'ila*, but the friend has not yet.

### Building Me'ila

The *braita* further continues: *Bana b'toch beiso — lo maal ad sheyador tachtea b'shaveh perutah*. If the friend builds the beam into his house, he still has not committed *me'ila* until he has actually lived in the house and derived benefit worth at least a *perutah*, the minimum measurable unit of value. The underlying principle is that *me'ila* requires a tangible, quantifiable *hana'ah* — a benefit that can be assigned a monetary value. Purely symbolic or potential benefit does not suffice.

### Me'ila-Borrowing Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיתֵיב רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הֲדַר בֵּיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל מֵהַהִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu was sitting before Rabbi Yoḥanan, and he was sitting and saying in the name of Shmuel,** in reference to that mishna: **That is to say: One who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge must pay him rent.** Just as one is considered to derive benefit from a cross beam by residing underneath it and must pay for that benefit, so too, one is considered to derive benefit from the courtyard by residing in it, and he must pay for that benefit. It is apparent from this statement of Shmuel that the *halakhot* of compensating a common person are similar to and can be derived from those of the Temple treasury. This contradicts Shmuel's previous statement in the Gemara. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him** in resolution of this difficulty: **Shmuel retracted that** statement of his.

*Rav Abbahu* reports that he was sitting before *Rabbi Yochanan* and taught in the name of *Shmuel*: *zot omeret* — this *braita* teaches us something about a *hedyot* case as well. Specifically, *Shmuel* infers from the rule about the beam from *hekdesh* — that liability requires actual benefit of a *shaveh perutah* — that one who dwells in another's *chatzer* without the owner's knowledge (*shelo mida'ato*) must pay the owner for that benefit. *Shmuel* thus draws a parallel between *hekdesh shelo mida'at* and *sho'el shelo mida'at*, using the temple property case to illuminate the ordinary borrowing case.

### Mada'at Contradiction

This inference, however, creates a difficulty. *Shmuel* elsewhere distinguishes between *hekdesh* and *hedyot* — he treats them differently with respect to the bulk date discount. And more pointedly, there appears to be a contradiction within *Shmuel*'s own teachings. On one hand, he draws an analogy from *hekdesh shelo mida'at* to *sho'el shelo mida'at*. On the other hand, this analogy implies that the two are governed by the same standard, which conflicts with the intuition — and the explicit logic — that *hekdesh* should be stricter than *hedyot*. The *Almighty*, as the owner of temple property, always knows where that property is — *hekdesh shelo mida'at* from the borrower's perspective is never truly unknown to the "owner." This means *hekdesh shelo mida'at* should more closely resemble *she'ilah mida'at* — borrowing with the owner's full knowledge — not *she'ilah shelo mida'at*.

### Shmuel Retracts

The *gemara* notes: *hadar beh Shmuel* — *Shmuel* retracted one of these teachings. The question then is: which one did he retract? Was it the teaching that drew the analogy between *hekdesh shelo mida'at* and *sho'el shelo mida'at*? Or was it the earlier teaching about the bulk date discount, which distinguished *hekdesh* from *hedyot*? The *gemara* entertains the possibility that *Shmuel* retracted the date-discount distinction and actually held that *hekdesh* and *hedyot* are governed identically.

### Rava Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּמֵהַהִיא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, דִּלְמָא מֵהָא הֲדַר? לָא, מֵהַהִיא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ כִּדְרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת – כְּהֶדְיוֹט מִדַּעַת דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But from where** is it apparent **that he retracted that** statement concerning residing in another's courtyard; **perhaps he retracted this** ruling, concerning one who stole a cake of pressed dates? The Gemara answers: **No,** it must be **that** statement, concerning residing in another's courtyard, that **he retracted, in accordance with** the statement **of Rava. As Rava says:** Using **consecrated** property **without** the Temple treasurer's **knowledge is like** using property belonging to **a common person with** his **knowledge.** Since, ultimately, the true owner of consecrated property is God, benefit from it is always considered to be have been derived with the owner's knowledge. Consequently, one cannot derive an inference from the *halakha* of deriving benefit from consecrated property to a case of deriving benefit from a common person's property without his knowledge. Accordingly, it is more reasonable that Shmuel retracted his ruling about residing in another's courtyard.

The *gemara* resolves this by citing *Rava*: *hekdesh shelo mida'at ke-hedyot mida'at dami* — *hekdesh shelo mida'at* is legally equivalent to borrowing from an ordinary person *with* that person's knowledge. This means the analogy *Shmuel* drew — from *hekdesh shelo mida'at* to *sho'el shelo mida'at* — is incorrect. *Rava* explicitly rejects that direction of learning. Since *Rava* articulates this principle clearly, the *gemara* concludes that *Shmuel* must have ultimately agreed with it, meaning the teaching he retracted was the second one: the inference from *hekdesh shelo mida'at* to *sho'el shelo mida'at*. The teaching about the date-discount distinction between *hedyot* and *hekdesh* stands.

*Rashi* explains the logic underlying *Rava*'s position: since the Divine Owner of *hekdesh* always knows where the temple property is, there is no true *shelo mida'at* from the owner's side. This makes *hekdesh shelo mida'at* — misappropriation that the human actor did not realize was *me'ila* — legally comparable to ordinary *she'ilah mida'at*, where the owner is fully aware and has in some sense consented to the use.

## Damages & Market Value

### Wine Barrel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי שָׁקוֹלָאֵי דִּתְבַרוּ חָבִיתָא דְּחַמְרָא לְחַנְווֹאָה, בְּיוֹמָא דְשׁוּקָא מִיזְדַּבְּנָא בְּחָמֵשׁ, בִּשְׁאָר יוֹמֵי מִיזְדַּבְּנָא בְּאַרְבַּע. אַהְדַּרוּ לֵיהּ בְּיוֹמָא דְשׁוּקָא – מַהְדְּרוּ לֵיהּ חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא, בִּשְׁאָר יוֹמֵי – מַהְדְּרוּ לֵיהּ חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another ruling concerning differing rates of compensation: **Rava says:** With regard to **these porters who broke a shopkeeper's barrel of wine,** which **on market day sells for five** dinars **and on other days sells for four** dinars, if they **restore** its value **to him on market day,** then it is sufficient if **they compensate him** with **a barrel of wine,** which he could then sell for five dinars. If they wish to repay him **on other days,** i.e., on a non-market day, **they** must **compensate him** with **five** dinars. They cannot discharge their debt by giving him a barrel of wine, as on those days it is worth only four dinars.

*Rava* presents a practical case: carriers (*shakulay*) broke a barrel of wine (*chavita de-chamra*) belonging to a wine merchant. On the day of the market (*yoma de-shuka*) the wine sells for five units; on all other days it sells for four. The question is what the carriers owe.

### Market Day Price

On the day of the market itself, the carriers may return payment by providing a barrel of equivalent wine — which they could sell for five. On any other day, they must pay five dinars — the market-day value — because that is the price the merchant would have realized had the barrel not been broken at the critical moment.

### Alternate Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ חַמְרָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי, אֲבָל הֲוָה לֵיהּ חַמְרָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי – הָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְזַבּוֹנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara qualifies Rava's ruling: **And we said** that the porters must pay five dinars on a non-market day **only** in a case in **which** the shopkeeper **did not have** other barrels of **wine to sell** on that market day and consequently lost out on a potential sale worth five dinars. **But** if **he had** another barrel of **wine to sell,** then **he should have sold it.** The fact he did not sell the wine he had demonstrates that the porters did not cause him to lose out on a sale worth five dinars.

However, this rule is qualified: *lo amran ela de-lo hava lei chamra le-zivuni* — this higher liability applies only when the merchant had no other wine available to sell. If he had other wine that he could have sold on market day, then his loss from the broken barrel is not the full five units, because he was not actually prevented from selling on that day.

### Labor Deduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנַכֵּי לֵיהּ אֲגַר טִירְחֵיהּ וּדְמֵי בַּרְזַנְיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further qualifies Rava's ruling: **And** when the porters pay the five dinars, **they deduct from it the** usual **value of** the shopkeeper's **effort** in selling a barrel **and the** usual **cost of tapping** a barrel, as the shopkeeper was spared these costs.

In that case — where the merchant had other wine — the carriers owe less. *U-manchit lei agar tircha u-dmei barzanyata* — one subtracts the amount of labor (*agar tircha*) and the cost of tapping the barrel (*dmei barzanyata*). The *barzanyata* refers to the professional tapping of the barrel, which in the ancient wine trade was a skilled task with its own cost. A second interpretation takes *barzanyata* to refer to a crier who would announce the availability of wine for sale — a form of advertising — though the first reading, rooted in *barza* meaning the spigot or faucet of a wine barrel, appears more linguistically natural. In either interpretation, these ancillary costs are deducted from the carrier's liability, reflecting the principle that damages are assessed net of the expenses the owner would have incurred in any event to bring the wine to market.
