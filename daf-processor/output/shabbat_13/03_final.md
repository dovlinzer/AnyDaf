# Shabbat 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Candlelight Reading

### Intro & Gezeirot Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ הַחַזָּן כּוּ׳
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **in truth they said** that **the attendant** sees where in the book the children under his supervision are reading, but he himself should not read.

We begin daf 13 by picking up two lines from the bottom of 12b, at the phrase *v'emet amru*, where the Gemara is wrapping up a series of *gezeirot* — protective rabbinic decrees — concerning Shabbat. The opening theme of this section has been activities prohibited on Shabbat because they might lead to a transgression. The discussion began with carrying on *erev Shabbat* and has moved to various activities one might perform by candlelight, where the concern is that a person will come to tilt the candle to improve the light — a prohibited act on Shabbat. As we will see, the sugya then shifts radically to a different concern: not Shabbat-related at all, but the general principle that doing one thing can lead to something else entirely.

### Chazzan Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא ״רוֹאֶה״. מַאי לָאו, לִקְרוֹת?! לָא, לְסַדֵּר רָאשֵׁי פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתָיו. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲבָל מְסַדֵּר הוּא רָאשֵׁי פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתָיו, וְכוּלַּהּ פָּרָשָׁה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asked: **Didn't you say in the first clause** of the mishna that the attendant **sees? Doesn't** that mean that he sees in order **to read?** How can that part of the mishna conclude by saying that he may not read? The Gemara answers: **No,** it does not mean that the attendant is permitted to actually read; rather, he is only permitted **to** look and **arrange the beginning of his sections** of the Torah that **he** must read the next day. **And so too, Rabba bar Shmuel said: However, he may arrange the beginning of his sections** that he must read the next day. The Gemara asks: **And** may he **not** read **the entire section?**

The Mishnah at the end of 12b states: *v'emet amru ha-chazzan ro'eh heichan tinokot korin aval lo yikra* — even though one may not read by candlelight, they said that the *chazzan* — the community functionary who also often served as teacher of children — may look to see where the children are reading, but may not himself read. This is apparently connected to the ancient practice of reviewing the weekly *parasha* — the *ma'avir sedrah* — prior to or on Shabbat itself, which seems to be a quite venerable custom. The Gemara asks: *mai lav likros* — doesn't the phrase "he may see" actually mean he can read? That would seem to contradict the principle that one may not read by candlelight lest one come to *l'hatot*, to tilt the candle.

### Chazzan Reading Question

The Mishnah's formulation — *ro'eh* without *yikra* — raises the question of what meaningful distinction exists between seeing and reading. The answer given is *lo l'sader rashei parshiyosav* — he may only organize the beginnings of the *parshiyot*. The Rambam explains this to mean that the *chazzan* may find the starting point and read the first two or three words to get the children started, but he cannot conduct a sustained reading on his own. This is confirmed by a further statement attributed to Rabbah bar Shmuel: *aval mesader hu rashei parshiyosav* — he may orient the children at the beginning of the portions, but no more. The Gemara then asks: and he cannot read the entire *parasha*?

### Children Reading Tosefta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר, הַתִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן הָיוּ מְסַדְּרִין פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת וְקוֹרִין לְאוֹר הַנֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in a *Tosefta*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The schoolchildren would organize the sections and read** the book **by candlelight.**

At the top of 13a, the Gemara raises a *Tosefta* in challenge: *Rabban Gamliel omer ha-tinokot shel beit rabban hayu mesadrim parshiyoseihen v'korin l'or ha-neir* — Rabban Gamliel says that the young children studying in their master's house — the famous *tinokot shel beit rabban* — would organize their *parshiyot* and actually read by candlelight. The Gemara notes the force of this: *hayu korin l'or ha-neir* — they were reading, not merely identifying, by candlelight. Presumably one would not actively have small children violate a rabbinic decree.

### Eimas Rabban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא — רָאשֵׁי פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתָיו, וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא — שָׁאנֵי תִּינוֹקוֹת, הוֹאִיל וְאֵימַת רַבָּן עֲלֵיהֶן לָא אָתֵי לְאַצְלוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** that the *Tosefta* is only referring to **the beginning of the sections. And if you wish, say** instead that **children are different** in this regard. **Since the fear of their teacher is upon them, they will not come to adjust** the wick. Even on a weekday, fear of their teacher will prevent them from tending to the lamp during their study.

The Gemara offers two possible resolutions. The first is that *rashei parshiyosav* — "reading" here refers only to reading the beginnings of the portions, consistent with the limitation already established. The second, and more interesting, resolution is *shani tinokot* — children are different. Why? *Ho'il v'eimas rabban aleihem lo asi l'atzloyei* — because the fear of their teacher is upon them, they will not come to tilt the candle. Even on a weekday, no child would dare move the candle without the teacher's permission. The *eimas rabban* functions as an effective deterrent against the prohibited action that the decree was designed to prevent.

### Group Protection Principle

The *eimas rabban* explanation is one instance of a broader principle at work here: when multiple people are engaged in an activity together as a group, we are less concerned that they will violate the relevant prohibition, because one person will remind the other. This is distinct from a case where multiple individuals happen to be using the same candle independently for their own separate purposes — in that case, each is acting alone and the mutual-reminder dynamic does not apply. But where they are acting in concert, the group dynamic itself provides a measure of protection.

## Zav & Zavah Eating

### Zav Zavah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ לֹא יֹאכַל הַזָּב. תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בּוֹא וּרְאֵה עַד הֵיכָן פָּרְצָה טׇהֳרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁלֹּא שָׁנִינוּ ״לֹא יֹאכַל הַטָּהוֹר עִם הַטְּמֵאָה״, אֶלָּא לֹא יֹאכַל הַזָּב עִם הַזָּבָה מִפְּנֵי הֶרְגֵּל עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Similar** to this decree of Shabbat, the Sages issued a decree that **the *zav* may not eat** with his wife, the *zava,* even though they are both ritually impure, because by eating together they will come to excessive intimacy and become accustomed to sin. **It was taught** in a *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Come and see to what extent ritual purity was widespread in Israel, as we did not learn: The ritually pure may not eat with the ritually impure** woman; **but rather, the *zav* may not eat with the *zava*,** although they are both ritually impure, **lest he become accustomed to sin.**

The Gemara now shifts to a statement entirely unrelated to Shabbat: *ha-zav lo yochal im ha-zava mipnei hergel aveirah* — a *zav* should not eat with a *zava*, because it will lead to *hergel aveirah*, accustoming oneself to transgression. What is this doing in Masechet Shabbat? One explanation is that a whole collection of teachings of the form "do not do A, lest it lead to B" were transmitted together — the prohibition against sitting for a haircut before *Mincha* lest one miss *davening*, various Shabbat-related concerns, and this concern about *zav* and *zava* eating together — and they were all gathered here because the core of the collection dealt with Shabbat. Another explanation is that this statement was part of the famous list of eighteen *gezeirot* enacted in the upper chamber of Chananya ben Guryon, where Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai agreed upon a series of decrees — and our Mishnah is about to describe that list.

### Hergel Aveira Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ: לֹא יֹאכַל זָב פָּרוּשׁ עִם זָב עַם הָאָרֶץ, שֶׁמָּא יַרְגִּילֶנּוּ אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **On a similar note,** the Sages said: **A *zav* who** generally **distances** himself from ritual impurity, eats ritually pure food, and is careful about separating tithes, **may not eat with a *zav* who is an *am ha'aretz*,** who does not distance himself from ritual impurity and is not careful about separating tithes, due to the concern **lest** the *am ha'aretz* **accustom him** to frequently spend time **with him,** by means of a shared meal.

What does *hergel aveirah* mean in this context? Following Rashi, the picture is as follows. The *tahor* people are eating together, and a *zav* and a *zava*, both being *tamei*, cannot eat with them. They must go off and eat by themselves — and we will see in the Gemara that the assumption is that everyone, not just *kohanim* dealing with *terumah*, was careful about the laws of *tahara*. So the *zav* and the *zava* cannot eat with the *tahor* group, and the concern is that if they eat alone together in private, it will lead to sexual relations — a concern of *erva* and *karet*.

One could ask: why would this require a special *gezeirah*? Would it not already be covered by the general prohibition of *yichud*? Rashi, and similarly the Rambam, apparently assumes we are speaking of husband and wife. A stranger pairing would fall under existing *yichud* concerns, but here the novelty is the case of husband and wife: even though husband and wife are ordinarily permitted to eat together at home even when she is a *niddah*, here the concern is that the private circumstances of being isolated from their normal eating group will create conditions leading to sexual relations. This will open into the Gemara's broader discussion of the *harchakot* between husband and wife during *niddah*.

There is, however, a significantly simpler reading of this passage, advanced by the *Mishnat Eretz Yisrael* commentary of the Safrai family. According to them, there is no need to introduce sexual concerns at all. The *hergel aveirah* is entirely about *tumah*. The reasoning is: of course two strangers who are both *tamei* can eat together — being *tamei* does not obligate you to eat alone. But husband and wife who are both *tamei* eating together is problematic because the next time only one of them will be *tamei*, and being accustomed to eating together, they will continue doing so — causing the *tahor* one's food to become *tamei*. The concern is specifically the husband-and-wife relationship because that intimacy creates habitual patterns that persist even when the *tumah* circumstances change.

### Purity Practice Braita

The *braita* is introduced in which Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: *bo u'rei ad heichan partza tahara b'Yisrael* — come and see how extensively the practice of *tahara* had spread in Israel. He observes that the Mishnah does not need to state that a *tahor* person should not eat with a *tamei* person — that was taken entirely for granted. Even though there is nowadays no technical prohibition against eating one's own food while *tamei*, unless one is a *kohen* dealing with *terumah*, the practice of eating *be-tahara* had become so pervasive that the *tahor* eating with the *tamei* did not even need to be stated; everyone already understood this. The Gemara's observation here is significant from a social-historical perspective: the *chaburot*, the defined eating groups, and the boundaries of who is a *chaver* — a member of such a group — were deeply boundary-defining markers of identity. The only thing that needed to be stated explicitly was the *zav* and *zava* case.

### Zav Parush Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַרְגִּילוֹ אֶצְלוֹ מַאי הָוֵי? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִילֶנּוּ דְּבָרִים טְמֵאִין. אַטּוּ זָב פָּרוּשׁ לָאו דְּבָרִים טְמֵאִין אָכֵיל? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִילֶנּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵינָן מְתוּקָּנִין. וְרָבָא אָמַר: רוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִין הֵן, אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא יְהֵא רָגִיל אֶצְלוֹ וְיַאֲכִילֶנּוּ דְּבָרִים טְמֵאִין בִּימֵי טׇהֳרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **And if he accustoms him to be with him, what of it,** what is the problem? **Rather, say: Lest he feed him impure items.** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the *zav* who** generally **distances** himself from ritual impurity **does not eat impure things?** In his impure state, everything he touches automatically becomes impure, so why would he be concerned with regard to impure items? **Abaye said:** This prohibition is due to **a decree** issued by the Sages **lest** the *am ha'aretz* **feed him** food **items that are not tithed. Rava said:** He needn't worry about items that are not tithed. Even if his friend was an *am ha'aretz*, there is a general principle in effect that **most *amei ha'aretz* tithe** their fruits. **Rather,** the Sages were concerned **lest he become accustomed** to spending time **with** the *am ha'aretz* even after the period of his impurity **and he feed him impure items even during the days of his purity.**

A parallel case is then introduced: *lo yochal zav parush im zav am ha-aretz* — a *zav* who is normally careful about *tahara* should not eat with a *zav* who is an *am ha-aretz*, one who is not generally careful about *tumah ve-tahara* or about separating *terumot u-ma'asrot*. The concern, again phrased with the language of *hergel*, is *shema yargilennu etzlo* — perhaps he will become accustomed to being in his company.

The Gemara immediately asks: *ve-chi margilo etzlo mai havei* — so what if he becomes accustomed to being with him? What is the concern? The Gemara suggests: *shema ya'achilennu dvarim tme'im* — perhaps the *am ha-aretz* will feed him *tamei* things. But the Gemara objects: *atu zav parush lo dvarim tme'im achal* — the careful *zav* already eats *tamei* things himself, since he is a *zav*! So what difference does eating the *am ha-aretz*'s food make?

### Rava's Hergel Aveira

Abaye and Rava offer different resolutions. Abaye says: *gezeirah shema ya'achilennu dvarim she-einam metukanim* — we are concerned that the *am ha-aretz* will feed him food from which *terumot u-ma'asrot* have not been properly separated. The intimacy created by being thrown together — both excluded from the *tahor* eating group, eating as a private unit — may generate a particular closeness, a sharing of food, and the *am ha-aretz*'s bread may well be *tevel*. The reason the *zav* example is chosen may be precisely because it is a condition that could last many days, creating an extended period of this problematic intimacy rather than just a single occasion.

Rava demurs. *Rov amei ha-aretz measrin hen* — the majority of *amei ha-aretz* do in fact separate their tithes, and our concern about *demai* — produce of uncertain tithe status — is only rabbinic in nature. We would not construct a rabbinic safeguard against another rabbinic concern of this kind. Rava therefore offers a different explanation: *shema yehei ragil etzlo ve-ya'achilennu dvarim teme'im bimei taharato* — we are concerned not about what will happen during the period of *tumah*, but about what will happen afterward. Once the *zav parush* becomes friendly and comfortable with this *am ha-aretz* during the week or more they spend eating together, he may continue to spend time with him even after he becomes *tahor* — and then he will eat food that is genuinely *tamei*.

This debate between Abaye and Rava is instructive for understanding the *zav* and *zava* case as well. Abaye's concern is about what goes wrong immediately, in the present. Rava's concern is about a future consequence of present intimacy. These two frames map onto the two readings of the *zav* and *zava* prohibition: if the concern is what happens right now — they are alone together, the opportunity presents itself — that corresponds to Rashi's reading that the concern is sexual relations, since the *tumah* concern cannot be the issue in the present moment when both are equally *tamei*. If the concern is what might happen later, then the *tumah*-based reading of the Safrai commentary becomes more plausible — they eat together while both are *tamei*, develop the habit, and then continue when only one is *tamei*.

## Husband-Wife Bed Niddah

### Pajamas Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: נִדָּה מַהוּ שֶׁתִּישַׁן עִם בַּעֲלָהּ הִיא בְּבִגְדָהּ וְהוּא בְּבִגְדוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** An additional **dilemma was raised** before the Sages with regard to the requirement to distance oneself from prohibition and impurity: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a menstruating woman? May she sleep with her husband** in one bed while **she** is **in her clothes and he** is **in his clothes?**

The Gemara now introduces a new question, which appears to emerge from nowhere but is in fact intimately connected to the preceding discussion: *niddah mahu she-tishon im ba'alah hi bevigdah ve-hu bevigdo* — may a husband and wife sleep in the same bed when she is a *niddah*, provided they are both wearing their garments? By "garments" — *bigdah* — Rashi clarifies that the Gemara means pajamas, since the normal mode of sleeping for husband and wife, as part of the obligation of *onah*, involved *kiruv basar* — physical closeness, sleeping together unclothed. There is indeed a Gemara in Ketubot that states a wife can compel a *get* if her husband insists on sleeping together only while clothed, since *she'erah kesutah ve-onatah lo yigra* encompasses not just the act of sex but the physical intimacy of sleeping together. The question here is therefore whether wearing pajamas constitutes a sufficient *heker* — a meaningful marker — to permit sleeping in the same bed, or whether they require separate beds entirely.

### Cheese & Meat Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: הָעוֹף עוֹלֶה עִם הַגְּבִינָה עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא עוֹלֶה וְלֹא נֶאֱכָל. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּלֵיכָּא דֵּיעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from what we learned in a mishna: **The fowl is** permitted to be **placed** together with **the cheese on the table,** although **it may not be eaten** with cheese. This is **the statement of Beit Shammai. Beit Hillel say:** The fowl **is neither** permitted to be **placed** together with the cheese on the table, **nor may it be eaten** with it. According to the opinion of Beit Hillel, which is the *halakha*, not only must one distance himself from the sin itself, but one must also make certain that items that are prohibited together are not placed together. The Gemara rejects this: **There it is different as there are not** several **consciousnesses.** When the fowl and the cheese are on one person's table, he is liable to err and eat them both, as there is only one consciousness there, his. That is not the case when there are two people in one bed. In that case, there are two consciousnesses and there is no concern that they will both forget the prohibition.

Rav Yosef introduces a striking comparison: *ha-of oleh im ha-gevinah al ha-shulchan ve-eino ne'echal* — Beit Shammai holds that poultry and cheese may be placed on the same table together, even though they cannot be eaten together. Beit Shammai is not concerned that having them on the table simultaneously will lead one to eat them together. Beit Hillel, however, says *lo oleh velo ne'echal* — not only may they not be eaten together, they may not even be on the same table. Beit Hillel is concerned that the proximity of cheese and chicken will lead one to eat them together. By analogy, one might argue that husband and wife in the same bed will similarly lead to transgression — even if there is a *heker* of pajamas.

### Da'at Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּהֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא דֵּיעוֹת שָׁאנֵי. דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי אַכְסְנָיִים אוֹכְלִין עַל שֻׁלְחָן אֶחָד, זֶה אוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר וְזֶה אוֹכֵל גְּבִינָה, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין. וְלָאו אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ אָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר אַמֵּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, אֲבָל מַכִּירִין זֶה אֶת זֶה — אֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **So too, it is reasonable** to say **that where there are** two or more **consciousnesses** it **is different, as it was taught in the latter clause** of that mishna, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Two guests** in one house **may eat on one table this one eating meat and this one eating cheese, and they need not be concerned.** That is not a proof. **Was it not said with regard to this** *halakha* that **Rabbi Ḥanin bar Ami said** that **Shmuel said: They only taught** that the two of them may eat on one table **when they are not familiar with each other; however,** if **they are familiar with each other it is prohibited** for them to eat on one table, as there is room for concern that due to their familiarity they will share their food and come to sin.

The Gemara rejects the analogy, however, on a crucial ground: *d'leika de'ot* — in the case of cheese and chicken, there is no *da'at*, no autonomous opinion or will in either the cheese or the chicken. They are inanimate objects sitting in front of you, and since neither can exercise any restraint, the only relevant question is whether you will remember. But where two human beings with *da'at* are involved, there is hope that if one begins to initiate something inappropriate, the other will remember the prohibition and pull back. Two people are present with independent will, and that mutual check is itself a significant safeguard. This, the Gemara indicates, is what makes the husband-and-wife situation different from the inanimate cheese-and-chicken comparison.

The Gemara notes that this logic is confirmed by what the Mishnah states at the end: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer shenei achsanain ochlin al shulchan echad zeh basar ve-zeh gvinah ve-ein choshashin* — two strangers at an inn may eat at the same table, one eating meat and one eating cheese, without concern. The reason is exactly as stated: since there are two people, one of them should remember if the other reaches toward the forbidden item.

### Strangers Qualifier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי נָמֵי, מַכִּירִין זֶה אֶת זֶה נִינְהוּ. הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם דֵּיעוֹת אִיכָּא, שִׁינּוּי לֵיכָּא. הָכָא אִיכָּא דֵּיעוֹת וְאִיכָּא שִׁינּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** if so, **these too,** the husband and his wife, **are familiar with each other.** There is room for concern that they will not keep appropriate distance, and therefore they may not sleep together in one bed even if he is wearing his clothes and she is wearing her clothes. The Gemara rejects this: **How can you compare** these two cases? **There,** in the case of meat and milk, although **there are** two **consciousnesses, there is no** noticeable **change** from the norm, as the meat and the cheese are on the table without any obvious indication to remind them not to mix the food items. While, **here,** in the case of the menstruating woman, **there are** two **consciousnesses and there is** also **a** noticeable **change** from the norm, as it is unusual for people to sleep in their clothes. The fact that they are both dressed constitutes a change.

However, a qualification is attached to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling. Rav Chanin bar Ami in the name of Shmuel states: *lo shanu ela she-ein makirin zeh et zeh* — this leniency applies only when the two diners are strangers who do not recognize one another. *Aval makirin zeh et zeh asurin* — but if they know each other, it is forbidden. The reasoning is straightforward: a stranger would not have the familiarity to simply reach over and say "Could I have a piece of your cheese?" But friends, being comfortable with one another, might. And once that social barrier is lowered, we become concerned the prohibition will be crossed.

This qualification seems to be directly applicable to our case. *Ve-ha hacha nami makirin zeh et zeh ninhu* — a husband and wife certainly know each other well. Why should we not apply the same concern and prohibit them from sleeping in the same bed even with pajamas?

### Two Protective Factors

The Gemara responds decisively. *Hachi hashta? Ha-tam de'ot ika shinui leika* — in the cheese-and-meat case at the inn, yes, there are two people with *da'at*, but there is no *shinui*, no marking, no sign of anything unusual about the situation. Nothing in the arrangement signals that caution is required. *Ha-ka ika de'ot ve-ika shinui* — but in our case of husband and wife in bed, both factors are present: first, there are two people with *da'at*, so one may remind the other, and second, there is a *shinui* — they are wearing pajamas, which is itself a departure from their normal mode of sleeping together, a *heker* that signals "something is different here, be careful."

### Both Factors Present

Thus, according to the Gemara's analysis as presented here, the combination of two protective factors — *de'ot* (the autonomous will of two human beings) and *shinui* (the wearing of pajamas as a sign) — should in principle be sufficient to permit husband and wife to sleep in the same bed during *niddah*. The pajamas function as a constant reminder, and together with the mutual-check dynamic of two people with *da'at*, the conditions would appear to satisfy the criteria by which we ordinarily judge whether A will lead to B. The Gemara has not yet concluded the matter, however, as we will see when the *zav* and *zava* case is reintroduced.

## Zav Zavah Revisited

### Zav Zavah No Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא יֹאכַל הַזָּב עִם הַזָּבָה מִשּׁוּם הֶרְגֵּל עֲבֵירָה. הָכָא נָמֵי, דֵּיעוֹת אִיכָּא שִׁינּוּי לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from what we learned in our mishna: **The *zav* may not eat with the *zava*** due to concern that excessive intimacy will lead them to **become accustomed** to sin. Even eating together is prohibited. The Gemara answers: **Here, too,** although **there are** two **consciousnesses, there is no** noticeable **change.**

The Gemara now brings back the original ruling about *zav* and *zava* eating together, using it as a challenge to the conclusion just reached. If the combination of *de'ot* and *shinui* is sufficient to permit husband and wife in bed during *niddah*, then why are *zav* and *zava* prohibited from eating together? After all, there are also two people with *da'at* in that situation. The implied question is: does the *zav* and *zava* case constitute a difficulty for the reasoning that allowed the husband and wife in bed?

The overall framework being established is this: wherever both factors are present — two people with independent will, and some form of marking or distinction — we are not concerned that A will lead to B. Where only one factor is present, or neither, we are concerned.

### No Shinui in Zav

The Gemara resolves the challenge: *ha-ka nami de-ika shinui leika* — in the case of *zav* and *zava* eating together, there is indeed no *shinui*, no sign or marker present in the situation to serve as a *heker*. They are eating together in what appears to be a completely ordinary arrangement, with nothing to signal that they must exercise caution. Two people with *da'at* alone is insufficient — both *de'ot* and *shinui* are required. Since the *zav* and *zava* case has *de'ot* but lacks *shinui*, the prohibition stands.

This analysis also illuminates what was said earlier about the case in the inn. Extending the logic: if a *heker* were present in the *zav* and *zava* situation — some visible marker indicating the nature of their prohibition — it is conceivable that the concern would be mitigated. The absence of such a marker is what makes the case problematic.

### Biblical Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אֶל הֶהָרִים לֹא אָכָל וְעֵינָיו לֹא נָשָׂא אֶל גִּלּוּלֵי בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶת אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ לֹא טִמֵּא וְאֶל אִשָּׁה נִדָּה לֹא יִקְרָב״. מַקִּישׁ אִשָּׁה נִדָּה לְאֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ: מָה אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ הוּא בְּבִגְדוֹ וְהִיא בְּבִגְדָהּ — אָסוּר, אַף אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה הוּא בְּבִגְדוֹ וְהִיא בְּבִגְדָהּ — אָסוּר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a different resolution from that which was taught in a *baraita*: It is stated: **"And he has not eaten upon the mountains, neither has he lifted up his eyes to the idols of the house of Israel, neither has he defiled his neighbor's wife, neither has he come near to a woman in her impurity"** (Ezekiel 18:6). This verse **juxtaposes a menstruating woman to his neighbor's wife. Just as** lying together with **his neighbor's wife,** even when **he** is **in his clothes and she** is **in her clothes, is prohibited, so too,** lying with **his wife** when she is **menstruating,** even when **he** is **in his clothes and she** is **in her clothes, is prohibited.**

The Gemara then introduces a *pasuk* from Ezekiel to anchor the prohibition of husband and wife in bed during *niddah*. The verse describes a *tzaddik* who *el ha-harim lo achal* — did not eat toward the mountains (understood by the Gemara as not relying on *zechut avot* but maintaining his own merits), *ve-einav lo nasa el gillulei beit Yisrael* — did not worship idols, *ve-et eshet re'ehu lo timme* — did not defile his fellow's wife through adultery, *ve-el isha niddah lo yikrav* — and did not draw near to a woman in her *niddah*. The Gemara draws a *hekesh*: *mah eshet re'ehu hu bevigdo ve-hi bevigdah assur* — just as being in bed with another man's wife wearing pajamas is prohibited, *af ishto niddah hu bevigdo ve-hi bevigdah assur* — so too, a man may not be in bed with his own wife when she is a *niddah*, even if both are wearing pajamas. This *hekesh* effectively overrides the reasoning that had seemed to permit it.

## Niddah Prohibition Approaches

### Rashi's Yichud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי פְּדָת: דְּאָמַר רַבִּי פְּדָת לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא קוּרְבָה שֶׁל גִּלּוּי עֲרָיוֹת בִּלְבַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ אֶל כׇּל שְׁאֵר בְּשָׂרוֹ לֹא תִקְרְבוּ לְגַלּוֹת עֶרְוָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this conclusion **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rabbi Pedat, as Rabbi Pedat said: The Torah only prohibited intimacy that** involves **engaging in prohibited sexual relations,** as it **is stated: "None of you shall approach to any that is near of kin to him, to uncover their nakedness"** (Leviticus 18:6). The prohibition of intimacy in the Torah applies exclusively to relations, and all other kinds of intimacy that do not include actual relations are not included in the prohibition. When there is separation, they did not issue a decree.

Three major *rishonim* approach the logical basis of this prohibition differently. Rashi understands the connection to *eshet re'ehu* as grounding the prohibition in *issur yichud* — the prohibition of seclusion with a woman who is forbidden to one. By analogy, a man may not be in the same intimate situation with his wife during *niddah*.

### Tosafot Yetzer Hara

Tosafot raises an objection to Rashi: the concept of *issur yichud* obviously does not apply to a husband and wife in their own bedroom — of course they may be secluded together. So Rashi cannot be saying that the prohibition of husband and wife in bed during *niddah* is literally an *issur yichud*. Tosafot instead understands the *hekesh* to be pointing to a different reason: the *yetzer hara* of sexual desire, particularly when the opportunity is so immediate and present, will override a person's normal capacity for restraint. Just as we would clearly be concerned about a man being in bed with another man's wife even with pajamas — because the sexual drive would overpower ordinary inhibition — so too with a man's own wife during *niddah*, the *yetzer hara* is powerful enough that the *de'ot* and *shinui* together are insufficient to guarantee restraint.

### Ramban Intrinsic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא כִּי הָוֵי אָתֵי מִבֵּי רַב הֲוָה מְנַשֵּׁק לְהוּ לְאַחְווֹתֵיהּ אַבֵּי חָדַיְיהוּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אַבֵּי יְדַיְיהוּ. וּפְלִיגָא דִידֵיהּ אַדִּידֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: אֲפִילּוּ שׁוּם קוּרְבָה אָסוּר, מִשּׁוּם ״לָךְ לָךְ אָמְרִי נְזִירָא סְחוֹר סְחוֹר, לְכַרְמָא לָא תִּקְרַב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Ulla, when he would come from the house of his teacher, would kiss his sisters on their chests. And some say: On their hands.** Ulla was not concerned about violating the prohibition of displaying affection toward a relative forbidden to him, as his intention was not to have relations with them. The Gemara adds that **his** action **was in contradiction to** a saying of **his, as Ulla said: Even any intimacy is prohibited** with a woman with whom he is forbidden to engage in sexual relations **due to** the reason formulated as an adage: **Go around, go around, and** do not approach **the vineyard, they say to the nazirite.** They advise the nazirite, who is forbidden to consume any product of a vine, that he should not even approach the vineyard. The same is true with regard to the prohibition of forbidden relations. According to Ulla, one must distance himself from them to whatever degree possible.

The Ramban offers what is arguably the most satisfying explanation, and one that directly addresses the question raised earlier about other forms of physical intimacy falling short of actual sexual relations. The Ramban argues: does anyone truly think that the only reason a man cannot be in bed with another man's wife is the concern that it might lead to sex? Of course not. It is intrinsically prohibited — the physical intimacy itself, even intentional touching short of sex, even the erotic quality of lying together, is on its own terms a violation of the prohibition pertaining to a married woman. Sexual touch, and indeed the general physical and erotic proximity involved, is itself part of the ambit of the prohibition, not merely a step on a slope toward the prohibited act.

The Ramban uses the analogy of *chatzi shiur* — just as one may not eat even a half-*kazayit* of a forbidden food even though that amount does not constitute the full violation, so too sexual intimacy short of intercourse falls within the prohibitory zone of *gilluy arayot*, even if it does not constitute the core act. Applying this to the *niddah* case, the Ramban says the Gemara is not merely concerned that sleeping together in pajamas will *lead* to sex. Even setting aside where it might lead, the physical intimacy of lying together in bed is itself problematic when the woman is a *niddah* — not because of *yetzer hara* overcoming restraint, but because that closeness is intrinsically improper at a time when they are prohibited to one another. This reading also corresponds to the question raised about what is prohibited beyond sex itself — the entire category of *kiruv basar* during *niddah* falls, in the Ramban's view, under a genuine prohibition, not merely a prudential *gezeirah* against eventual transgression.

This debate among the *rishonim* maps onto a broader dispute about the nature of the *harchakot* — the distancing practices between husband and wife during *niddah*. One school of thought holds that all the distancing requirements are *gezeirot*: rabbinic safeguards designed to prevent the couple from arriving at actual intercourse, which is the only act biblically prohibited. On this view, all the *harchakot* are derivative of the concern that A will lead to B will lead to C. The other view, associated with the Ramban, holds that physical intimacy is itself an independent problem during *niddah*, not merely because of where it leads.

### Rav Padda

Rav Padda now comes into tension with the Ramban's reading. The Gemara indicates that *lo asra Torah ela kurva shel gilluy arayot bilvad* — the Torah only prohibited sexual intercourse itself in the *arayot* prohibitions, not other forms of physical intimacy. His proof text is *ish ish el kol she'er besaro lo tikrevu legalot ervah* — the prohibition of *kiruv* is specifically *legalot ervah*, to uncover nakedness, meaning the sexual act itself. The *rishonim* debate whether Rav Padda means to limit only the biblical prohibition or the rabbinic one as well.

Rashi and Tosafot propose various explanations of how Rav Padda's statement limits the rabbinic scope as well. The Ramban, however, offers a reading that he considers the most precise. He argues that *lo asra Torah* sometimes means not prohibited even rabbinically. But his key insight is that Rav Padda's statement applies specifically to the case of *she'er* — one's relatives, including one's wife — as opposed to *eshet re'ehu*, another man's wife. The *pasuk* says *el kol she'er besaro*, which the Ramban reads as referring to the category of relatives. For relatives — one's sister, daughter, mother, and wife — physical intimacy that is not sexual is not prohibited; the prohibition extends only to the sexual act itself. For another man's wife, by contrast, even non-sexual physical intimacy falls within a broader prohibition. This explains why Ulla's behavior with his sister, discussed next, is not contradicted by Rav Padda's statement.

### Ulla's Kiss

The Gemara relates that when *Ulla* would return from the house of study after an extended period away, *havi minashek lela-achvosei* — he would kiss his sisters — *abei chadaiyu*, on their chest, or according to another version, *abei yadaiyu*, on the back of their hands. The point of the report is that Ulla practiced a form of physical affection with his female relatives that would be impermissible with other women — consistent with the Ramban's reading that *she'er*, one's relatives, are treated differently.

### Ulla Contradiction

Tosafot resolves the apparent self-contradiction by distinguishing between a general principle and individual application. As a general rule, we do not want people engaging in certain forms of physical contact because we are concerned it will lead to sexual thoughts and activity — hence Ulla's general statement. However, if a person knows with certainty that for him a particular touch carries no sexual resonance whatsoever — as Ulla understood about kissing his own sister — then there is no prohibition applicable to him. The general guideline exists as a safeguard for people who cannot be certain of their own responses, not as a categorical prohibition binding in all cases regardless of individual circumstance. This reading also has implications for the question of non-sexual touch more broadly — for example, the question of handshaking — where some authorities hold that if the touch genuinely carries no erotic significance for the parties involved, there may be no prohibition at all.

## Widow & Eliyahu Story

### Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִיד אֶחָד שֶׁשָּׁנָה הַרְבֵּה, וְקָרָא הַרְבֵּה, וְשִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַרְבֵּה, וּמֵת בַּחֲצִי יָמָיו. וְהָיְתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ נוֹטֶלֶת תְּפִילָּיו וּמְחַזַּרְתָּם בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת, וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה ״כִּי הוּא חַיֶּיךָ וְאוֹרֶךְ יָמֶיךָ״, בַּעֲלִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה הַרְבֵּה וְקָרָא הַרְבֵּה
>
> **Translation:** **The** Sage in the **school of Eliyahu taught** a *baraita* that deals with this *halakha*: There was **an incident involving one student who studied much** Mishna **and read much** Bible, **and served Torah scholars** extensively, studying Torah from them, **and,** nevertheless, **died at half his days,** half his life expectancy. **His wife** in her bitterness **would take his phylacteries and go around with them to synagogues and study halls, and she said to** the Sages: **It is written in the Torah: "For it is your life and the length of your days"** (Deuteronomy 30:20). If so, **my husband who studied much** Mishna, **and read much** Bible,

The Gemara now brings a *baraita* from the school of Eliyahu — the *Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu* — that appears in *Avot D'Rabbi Natan*. The story: there was once a student who had studied a great deal of Mishnah, had read extensively in *Mikra*, and had spent much time in the presence of Torah sages. Nevertheless, *u-met ba-chatzi yamav* — he died in the middle of his days, at a young age. His wife took his *tefillin* and began making the rounds of the synagogues and study halls. She posed the same question wherever she went: the Torah says *ki hu chayecha ve-orech yamecha* — the Torah is your life and the length of your days. My husband learned much, read much, served the sages — why did he die so young? Nobody could answer her. One time, the narrator — Eliyahu the Prophet, this being *Tanna D'Bei Eliyahu* — visited her, and she recounted the whole story.

### Bleeding Days Strictness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַרְבֵּה, מִפְּנֵי מָה מֵת בַּחֲצִי יָמָיו? וְלֹא הָיָה אָדָם מַחְזִירָהּ דָּבָר. פַּעַם אַחַת נִתְאָרַחְתִּי אֶצְלָהּ וְהָיְתָה מְסִיחָה כׇּל אוֹתוֹ מְאוֹרָע. וְאָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: בִּתִּי, בִּימֵי נִדּוּתֵךְ מַה הוּא אֶצְלֵךְ? אָמְרָה לִי: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם, אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶצְבַּע קְטַנָּה לֹא נָגַע [בִּי].
>
> **Translation:** **and served Torah scholars extensively, why did he die at half his days?** Where is the length of days promised him in the verse? **No one would respond to her** astonishment **at all.** Eliyahu said: **One time I was a guest in her** house, **and she was relating that entire event** with regard to the death of her husband. **And I said to her: My daughter, during the period of your menstruation, how** did **he** act **toward you? She said to me: Heaven forbid, he did not touch me even with** his **little finger.**

Eliyahu asked her: *bimei niddatach mah hu etzlech* — during the days of your *niddah*, how did your husband behave with you? She answered: *chas ve-shalom afilu be-etzba ketanah lo naga bi* — God forbid, he did not even touch me with his pinky finger. They were extremely strict during the period of active bleeding — stricter, as we will see, than the days that followed.

### White Days Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימֵי לִבּוּנִיךָ מַהוּ אֶצְלֵךְ? אָכַל עִמִּי וְשָׁתָה עִמִּי וְיָשַׁן עִמִּי בְּקֵירוּב בָּשָׂר, וְלֹא עָלְתָה דַּעְתּוֹ עַל דָּבָר אַחֵר. וְאָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: בָּרוּךְ הַמָּקוֹם שֶׁהֲרָגוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא נָשָׂא פָּנִים לַתּוֹרָה. שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״וְאֶל אִשָּׁה בְּנִדַּת טוּמְאָתָהּ לֹא תִקְרַב״.
>
> **Translation:** **In the days of your white** garments, after the menstrual flow ended, and you were just counting clean days, **how did he act toward you** then? She said to me: **He ate with me, and drank with me, and slept with me with bodily contact and,** however, **it did not enter his mind about something else,** i.e., conjugal relations. **And I said to her: Blessed is the Omnipresent who killed him** for this sin, **as** your husband **did not show respect to the Torah. The Torah said: "And to a woman in the separation of her impurity you should not approach"** (Leviticus 18:19), even mere affectionate contact is prohibited.

Eliyahu then asked: *bimei libunech mah hu etzlech* — and during your white days, your *yemei libunah*, how did he behave? She said: *achal immi ve-shassah immi ve-yashan immi be-kiruv basar* — he ate with me, drank with me, and slept with me in full physical closeness, without clothing. *Ve-lo altah da'ato al davar acher* — and it never led to anything further; they were always careful not to have sex. But they were physically intimate together, sleeping *be-kiruv basar*, without the *shinui* of pajamas.

### Eliyahu's Verdict

Eliyahu responded: *baruch ha-Makom she-harago* — blessed is God who killed him. *Shelo nasa panim la-Torah* — he was not able to misrepresent the Torah. *She-harei amrah Torah ve-el isha be-niddat tumatah lo tikrav* — for the Torah says you should not draw near to a woman in her impurity of *niddah*, and physical intimacy, not only intercourse, is included in this prohibition. Eliyahu's verdict is that the husband's death was on account of this behavior during the *yemei libunah*.

### Wide Bed Apron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: מִטָּה חֲדָא הֲוַאי. בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי: אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, סִינָר מַפְסִיק בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** That student did not actually sleep with her with bodily contact; rather, **it was** in **one bed** that they slept without contact. **In the West,** in Eretz Yisrael, **they say** that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef said:** When they would sleep together in one bed, she wore **a belt [*sinar*]** from the waist down that **would separate between him and her.** Nevertheless, since the matter is prohibited, that student was punished.

Rav Dimi, when he came from Eretz Yisrael, offered an alternative reading of the story: *mita chada havai* — it was a wide bed. The understanding was that they were in the same bed but at opposite ends, not actually lying next to each other in *kiruv basar*. They thought the wide bed constituted sufficient separation. Even so, Eliyahu ruled that this was wrong — they should not have been in the same bed at all. This carries direct practical implications: the general ruling is that husband and wife may not share a bed even at opposite ends during *niddah*, even with a pillow or physical barrier between them. The *mita chada* case is the classic basis for the ruling that a wide bed with a divider does not solve the problem.

Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef offered yet another reading: *sinar mafsik beino le-veinah* — there was an apron, a garment from the waist down, separating between them. They thought this approximated *hu bevigdo ve-hi bevigdah* — sufficient clothing to constitute a *shinui* from the waist down even if they were together from the waist up. Yet again, Eliyahu ruled that this was insufficient.

### Taboo vs Halacha

This story has a great deal to teach beyond its immediate ruling. Reading it alongside early writings of *chachmei Ashkenaz* and the *Geonim*, it becomes clear that alongside the halakhic framework of *niddah*, there existed popular practices — not specifically halakhic, but widespread — centered around taboos associated with menstrual bleeding itself. These taboos were not about the woman's halakhic status as a *niddah* but about the menstruation as a kind of ritual impurity in a more visceral, folk-religious sense. We find evidence that women after the bleeding stopped would bathe, wear white, and that the period following the cessation of bleeding was treated with noticeably less strictness than the bleeding period itself — not because of any halakhic distinction, but because the taboo associated with the blood itself had passed.

This appears to be precisely what is going on in the story. The *talmid* — despite all his learning — was not operating from a halakhic framework. He was following the taboo-based common practices of his time: extreme stringency during active bleeding, because the blood itself was the object of the taboo; and then a relaxation to full *kiruv basar* once the bleeding stopped, because in his understanding the danger had passed. He thought he was being pious. He was not. He was replacing *halacha* with custom-based taboo, and the consequences of the two are inverted.

### Chumrot to Kulos

This brings us to what is arguably the central thematic point of the entire passage: the story illustrates the phenomenon of *chumrot* that lead to *kulot* — stringencies that generate leniencies. By being extraordinarily strict during the bleeding days based on taboo rather than *halacha*, the *talmid* created the psychological and practical conditions for extraordinary leniency during the *yemei libunah*. The two are linked. The taboo-based stringency of the active bleeding period and the taboo-based leniency of the white days are two sides of the same non-halakhic coin.

Eliyahu's statement *shelo nasa panim la-Torah shelo ke-halacha* — that the husband misrepresented the Torah, did not follow it as *halacha* — is precisely this indictment. One cannot treat *hilchot niddah* as a system of taboo and superstition. It is *halacha*: an *issur* and a set of safeguards. The entire framing of the earlier Gemara — the comparison to cheese and meat, the analysis of *de'ot* and *shinui*, the careful reasoning about when A leads to B — is the Gemara insisting that *niddah* is to be treated as *halacha*, not as taboo. No kooties, no mystical danger of the bleeding itself. Simply an *issur* of sexual relations during a defined period, with attendant rabbinic safeguards structured rationally around preventing that transgression. And indeed, reading through Masechet Niddah, as we did not long ago, one is struck by how completely absent the taboo framework is from the *Chazal*'s own presentation. They insist on the halakhic character of the material throughout.

Eliyahu's harsh verdict — *baruch ha-Makom she-harago* — must be understood in context. The wife was not merely expressing private grief to her rabbi. She was going from *beit midrash* to *beit midrash*, publicly challenging the Torah's promise of longevity for those who study it, and implicitly threatening the community's confidence in the halakhic framework itself. Eliyahu's response, while undeniably harsh, was addressed to that public challenge. And it is worth noting that *Eliyahu HaNavi* was ultimately, in the tradition, held to have been too harsh — a quality for which he paid his own consequences. That does not, however, diminish the substance of his ruling on *hilchot niddah*, which stands as a clear articulation of the Gemara's insistence that this domain belongs entirely to *halacha* and not to the world of taboo.
