# Nedarim 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trumah & Shlichus

### Mishna Setup

The Gemara begins with the *mishna* of *hatorim et trumato* — the case in which one who is forbidden to derive *hana'ah* from a second party may nevertheless have that party separate *trumah* on his behalf. The reasoning is that performing a *mitzvah* service for someone is not classified as *hana'ah* in the conventional sense. But the Gemara perceives a further dimension of the *hana'ah* problem that requires examination.

The *mishna* specifies that the separation is performed *leda'ato* — with the awareness of the grain's owner. This qualification immediately raises the issue discussed the previous day: even a service that is not itself *hana'ah* can become *hana'ah* if the one performing it does so in the capacity of the other person's appointed *shaliach*. The *kohanim* who bring a *korban* on a person's behalf, for instance, are understood to be acting as that person's representative, and that representative relationship is itself classified as *hana'ah*. The same question now arises regarding the separation of *trumah*.

### Service vs. Agency

The central tension of the *sugya* can be stated precisely: performing a *mitzvah* service for someone is not *hana'ah*, but acting as that person's formally appointed *shaliach* is. The Gemara must therefore locate a scenario in which the separation of *trumah* is valid and effective, yet does not require a direct appointment of *shlichut*. If such an appointment is necessary, the very act of serving as one's agent — whatever the nature of the service — constitutes *hana'ah* and would be prohibited.

The question thus sharpens: if Shimon separates *trumah* on Reuven's grain using Reuven's own grain, Shimon cannot act on his own initiative. The *Torah* confers the right to separate *trumah* exclusively on the *ba'al hakri*, the owner of the pile. Just as one cannot marry a woman on another's behalf without formal *shlichut*, one cannot separate another person's *trumah* without being appointed an agent. If Shimon therefore needs Reuven's authorization, Shimon has become Reuven's *shaliach* — and that is precisely what creates the *hana'ah* problem.

### Own Grain Scenario

The Gemara introduces a specific question: *hatorim mishelo al shel chaveiro* — what if Shimon separates *trumah* using his own grain to cover both his grain and Reuven's grain simultaneously? Here Shimon is not touching Reuven's pile at all; he is using his own grain as the *trumah* to exempt Reuven's stack. This is an even greater act of generosity — he saves Reuven from using any of his own produce for the *mitzvah*.

The Gemara asks: *tzarich da'ato o lo* — does Shimon require Reuven's awareness and consent to do this? The argument for not requiring consent is *keivan d'zechus hu lo* — since it is objectively a benefit to Reuven, the principle of *zachin* may operate automatically and Shimon may proceed without permission. The counterargument is *mitzvah deilei* — *venicha lei lim'evad b'gufei*: it is Reuven's own *mitzvah*, and a person may prefer to spend his own money to fulfill his own *mitzvot* rather than have someone else do it for him, even at a cost saving. The question is therefore not whether *zachin* applies in principle, but whether the facts are sufficiently unambiguous to invoke it — whether the benefit clearly outweighs the loss of the *mitzvah* opportunity.

### Zachin Principle

The principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — that one may acquire something on behalf of another without that person's knowledge when it is objectively beneficial — is well-established. The question among the *Rishonim*, however, is what the principle is grounded in. One possibility is that *zachin* operates as a form of *shlichut*: we presume, given the obvious benefit, that the beneficiary would have wanted this done and therefore retroactively appoints the one acting on his behalf as his *shaliach*. The other possibility is that *zachin* is not *shlichut* at all but rather a distinct mechanism — sometimes called *zachin mishum yad* — whereby the one acting on another's behalf is treated as a physical extension of the beneficiary, analogous to placing an object in his yard or in his hand, and this mechanism functions specifically in the domain of *kinyanim*.

The significance of this distinction is acute here. If *zachin* is merely a *yad* mechanism for transferring property, it cannot explain the *trumah* scenario, because here the question is not about acquiring property but about performing a religious act and serving as a representative. For *zachin* to permit Shimon to separate *trumah* on Reuven's behalf without formal appointment, *zachin* must work as a form of presumed *shlichut*. This *sugya* therefore becomes a central source for the position — cited by *Tosafot* — that *zchiya* operates as a form of *shlichut* generally.

The practical stakes of this question are considerable. Some authorities have sought to apply *zachin* in certain *agunah* situations — for instance, where a *beit din* might deliver a *get* on a husband's behalf, treating themselves as his agents on the grounds that it is objectively beneficial for him to free his wife. If *zachin* is limited to the *yad* model and cannot create *shlichut*, such a mechanism is unavailable. But if the *sugya* here establishes that *zachin* can function as presumed *shlichut* — that wherever something is clearly to a person's benefit, another may act as his agent — the possibility gains traction.

Similarly, the conversion of a minor infant is classically justified through *zachin*: since conversion is unambiguously beneficial, the *beit din* may be presumed to act as the infant's agents, and the conversion takes effect.

### Ran on Zachin

The *Ran* addresses this in a concise formulation. On the opening question of *hatorim mishelo al shel chaveiro tzarich da'ato o lo*, he writes that *zachin l'adam shelo befanav u'mistama havei lei kishluchi* — by operation of *zachin*, the actor becomes the other's *shaliach* automatically. *Tosafot* is even more explicit, stating that *zchiya mita'am shlichut* — *zachin* works on the basis of *shlichut*, and this is the *sugya* from which that principle is derived throughout the Talmud. The fact that the Gemara here is willing to apply *zachin* not merely in the context of acquiring property but in the context of performing a religious act and serving as another's representative demonstrates that *zachin* can function as genuine assumed *shlichut*.

### Hana'ah & Agency

The conceptual distinction the Gemara is navigating must be stated precisely. Performing a *mitzvah* service for someone — teaching him *Torah*, bringing a *korban* on his behalf as an independent act of generosity — is not *hana'ah*. The act of doing a *mitzvah* does not create a *hana'ah* relationship even when the beneficiary gains from it, because *mitzvot lav le-hanos nitnu* — *mitzvot* are not given for personal enjoyment in the conventional sense.

The problem arises specifically when the person doing the service is doing so in the capacity of an appointed representative. If I say to Shimon "be my *shaliach* to throw this rock into the river," throwing a rock into a river is not in itself *hana'ah* — but being appointed my agent to carry out my will is itself a form of *hana'ah*, regardless of what the appointed task is. The gratification of one's will through a designated representative is classified as *hana'ah*. This is the Gemara's operative assumption: even when the underlying act is neutral, *shlichut* itself is *hana'ah*.

This is what distinguishes the *trumah* case from other improvements. One person mowing another's lawn, for instance, would be prohibited as *hana'ah* simply because the pruning provides a physical benefit. But separating *trumah* on someone's grain, if done as a private act of service without formal appointment, is not prohibited. The difficulty is that separating *trumah* on someone else's grain is one of those acts that inherently requires authorization — unlike mowing a lawn, it is not something one may simply do on another's property.

### Two Scenarios

The Gemara considers two possible ways the *mishna* may be read, corresponding to two distinct scenarios.

The first scenario: Shimon takes *trumah* from Reuven's own grain (*mishelo shel ba'al hakri*). In this case, the *mishna*'s phrase *leda'ato* must refer to Reuven's awareness. But if Reuven must be aware of and consent to what Shimon is doing with his grain, then Shimon has been appointed a *shaliach*, and that is precisely the *hana'ah* problem. The Gemara initially considers this reading and rejects it.

The second scenario: Shimon uses his own grain (*mishelo*) to cover Reuven's pile. Here, one might think Reuven's awareness is unnecessary, since it is objectively beneficial. But the Gemara presses: if Reuven must nonetheless be aware, has Shimon not again been made a *shaliach*?

The Gemara's resolution is to distinguish between formal *shlichut* and something more diffuse — the mere absence of objection. Drawing on a statement of Rava that will be quoted shortly, the Gemara frames the operative model as: *kol harotzeh litrom yavo veyitrom* — whoever wishes to separate *trumah* is welcome to do so. Reuven has not appointed any specific *shaliach*; he has simply expressed that he does not object to whoever wishes to act on his behalf. This differs categorically from a formal appointment. *Shlichut* requires direct empowerment of a designated agent; general acquiescence that anyone may act is something else. Reuven is *beda'ato* — aware — but not *bishlichuso* — not through formal agency. And since Shimon was not formally appointed, the *hana'ah* of carrying out Reuven's *shlichut* does not apply.

This reading may also imply that *trumah* separation, unlike actions such as *kiddushin*, does not require formal *shlichut* as a matter of *halakha*. If the owner of the pile simply does not object, the separation may be valid even in the absence of a formally constituted agency relationship — and it is this legal peculiarity of *trumah* that makes the *mishna*'s case possible.

## Trumah Rights

### Distribution Rights

Having resolved the threshold question of how Shimon may separate *trumah* on Reuven's behalf without creating a formal *shlichut*, the Gemara turns to a second and distinct question, posed by Rabbi Yirmiyah to Rabbi Zeira: when Shimon uses his own grain (*mishelo*) to separate *trumah* that exempts Reuven's pile, *tovas hana'ah shel mi* — who has the right to decide which *kohen* receives that *trumah*?

*Tovas hana'ah* is the right to designate to which *kohen* the *trumah* will be given. The *trumah* must go to some *kohen*, but the choice of which *kohen* belongs to someone, and that choice has value — one may wish to benefit one's son-in-law, a favorite student, or a beloved community *kohen*. The question is whether this right belongs to Reuven, whose grain was exempted, or to Shimon, whose grain actually became the *trumah*.

The argument for Shimon: *ilav peira de'ha'ich mi mitkna kirya de'hai* — if not for Shimon's grain, Reuven's pile would never have been rendered permissible. The *trumah* came from Shimon's property; it is his grain that became sanctified. The argument for Reuven: *ilav kirya de'hai lo haryan peira de'hai trumah* — without Reuven's pile of grain, Shimon's act would have accomplished nothing. Both elements were necessary.

### Hana'ah Objection

The *hana'ah* concern intrudes here in a new way. If it is determined that Reuven retains the *tovas hana'ah* — that Reuven gets to decide which *kohen* receives the *trumah* — then Shimon has given Reuven something of genuine value. Shimon's grain became the *trumah*, yet Reuven controls its disposition. Reuven has acquired a right over property that was never his, property whose *tovas hana'ah* has some monetary value. This would constitute a tangible benefit flowing from Shimon to Reuven, which would be prohibited if Reuven is *mudar hana'ah* from Shimon.

### Verse Analysis

The Gemara resolves this through a *pasuk*. The verse states: *es kol tvuas zar'acha venasata* — "all the yield of your grain, and you shall give." The *trumah* is defined in relation to the grain from which it was separated and of which it is a portion. The owner of the pile from which the *trumah* was taken is the one who "gives" it — who holds the right of designation. Since the *trumah* was taken to exempt Reuven's grain, it is identified with Reuven's pile. Reuven is therefore the one who gives the *trumah* to the *kohen*, and Reuven retains the *tovas hana'ah*.

### Baraita Challenge

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *torim et trumato ve'et masrotav leda'ato* — someone may take *trumot* and *ma'asrot* with his awareness. If this *baraita* is read as Shimon taking from Reuven's grain on Reuven's grain, and if *tovas hana'ah* belongs to Reuven as established above, then Shimon has given Reuven a benefit — not just performing the *mitzvah* act but actually conferring on Reuven the right of disposition over grain Reuven did not previously control. This would constitute *hana'ah*, rendering the entire arrangement problematic.

### Own Grain Reading

The Gemara entertains the possibility that the *baraita* refers to Shimon using his own grain — the scenario in which his grain becomes the *trumah*. On this reading, the *baraita*'s *leda'ato* would refer to Shimon's own awareness, and no formal appointment of Shimon as *shaliach* would be required.

### Consent Model

The Gemara ultimately declines this reading and returns to the earlier resolution. The *baraita* may indeed concern Shimon working with Reuven's own grain, and *leda'ato* refers to Reuven's awareness — but this awareness does not constitute formal *shlichut*. It is the model of *kol harotzeh litrom yavo veyitrom*: Reuven expresses general acquiescence, and whoever acts under that general permission is not a formally constituted agent. Reuven is *beda'ato* but not *bishlichuso*. This preserves the validity of the separation while avoiding the *hana'ah* of formal agency.

### Rav Abba

The section concludes with a statement of Rav Abba citing Rav Yochanan: *hamakdish mosif chomesh umitkaper oseh trumah uvo mishelo al she'eino shelo tovas hana'ah shelo* — the last clause being directly on point. One who separates *trumah* using his own grain to exempt grain that belongs to another retains the *tovas hana'ah* — it is he who designates which *kohen* receives the *trumah*. The reasoning is symmetric with the *pasuk*-based analysis: since it was his grain that became the *trumah*, he retains the rights that attach to that grain.

## Kedusha & Ownership

### Animal Sanctification

The statement of Rav Abba contains two additional clauses that illuminate the same conceptual distinction from different angles. Consider the case of Shimon who, wishing to help Reuven fulfill his obligation to bring a *korban chatat*, takes one of his own sheep, declares it *hekdesh*, and designates it as a *chatat* for Reuven. The sanctification itself Shimon can perform unilaterally — he can give Reuven a sanctified animal as a gift. Yesterday's discussion established that actually bringing the *korban* on Reuven's behalf without Reuven's awareness is problematic, but the sanctification is a separate act that Shimon may perform with his own property. Reuven then has a sanctified animal with which to fulfill his obligation.

### Chomesh Question

Now suppose the animal develops a *mum* — a disqualifying blemish — and must be redeemed. The *halakha* requires that when the owner redeems sanctified property, he must add a *chomesh* — a fifth (which is actually one quarter of the new value, becoming one fifth of the total). The question is: if either Reuven or Shimon could redeem the animal, which of them must add the *chomesh*?

Rav Abba's statement answers: *hamakdish mosif chomesh* — the one who originally sanctified the animal adds the fifth. That is Shimon. Even though the animal is Reuven's *korban* and Reuven is the one seeking *kapparah*, the monetary ownership relationship traces back to Shimon. The animal was Shimon's property; he is the one who transferred it into *hekdesh*; and when it comes to the transaction of redemption — which is essentially a property matter — Shimon retains the ownership relationship that triggers the obligation to add a fifth. If Reuven redeems it, he pays the standard redemption value without the *chomesh*.

### Temura Rights

The clause *hamitkaper oseh tmurah* addresses the reverse question. *Tmurah* is the prohibited but legally operative act of attempting to substitute one animal for a sanctified one — both animals then become *kadosh*, and the actor has violated a *lo ta'aseh*. The *halakha* restricts who may perform *tmurah*: only the *ba'al hakorban* — the owner of the *korban*, the one for whom it is being brought — can effect a *tmurah*. If someone else attempts it, the act is legally inert.

So who is the *ba'al hakorban* here? Rav Abba rules: *hamitkaper oseh tmurah* — Reuven, the one seeking *kapparah*, is the *ba'al hakorban*. If Reuven attempts to perform *tmurah*, it takes effect (and he has violated a prohibition). If Shimon attempts it, nothing happens. This is because *tmurah* is not a property transaction; it is an act grounded in the religious identity of the *korban* — whose atonement it is, for whom it is being brought. From that perspective, the animal belongs to Reuven.

### Ownership Split

The conceptual distinction that emerges is precise and elegant. From the perspective of monetary-property ownership — the kind of ownership that governs transactions, redemptions, and the addition of a *chomesh* — the animal traces back to Shimon, who brought his own property into the sacred domain. From the perspective of religious ownership — whose *korban* it is, who has the relationship to the *kedusha* that enables acts like *tmurah* — the animal belongs to Reuven, whose *kapparah* is at stake.

The *trumah* case follows the same structure. When Shimon uses his own grain as *trumah* to exempt Reuven's pile, the grain that becomes *trumah* was Shimon's. The right to designate which *kohen* receives it — *tovas hana'ah* — traces to ownership of the grain that became *trumah*, and therefore belongs to Shimon. This parallels the *chomesh* ruling: the one whose property became the sacred object retains the property-adjacent rights.

## Teaching Torah

### Mishna Distinctions

The *mishna* states that one who is forbidden to benefit another may nonetheless teach that person *midrash*, *halachot*, and *aggadot* — all forms of *Torah she'be'al peh* — but may not teach him *mikra*, the written Biblical text. He may, however, teach *mikra* to that person's children.

The distinction requires explanation. *Midrash* here means *midrash halacha* — the derivation of *halacha* from *psukim*. *Halachot* refers to straightforward rulings in the style of the *mishna*. *Aggadot* covers non-legal homiletical material. All of these are forms of *Torah she'be'al peh* and may be taught freely. *Mikra* — the Biblical text itself — is the one category that may not be taught directly to the person who cannot receive *hana'ah*.

The permission to teach the person's children *mikra* is explained on the model of paying off a creditor: the benefit flows to the children directly, and only indirectly reaches the father, who is relieved of the cost of hiring a teacher. Indirect benefit of this kind — analogous to paying one's debt — does not constitute the prohibited *hana'ah*.

### Why No Mikra

The obvious question is: if teaching *Torah she'be'al peh* is permitted because performing a *mitzvah* service is not *hana'ah*, why is teaching *mikra* any different? Teaching *psukim* is also a *mitzvah* act. What distinguishes it from teaching *midrash* or *halachot*?

### Paid vs. Unpaid

Shmuel answers: *bemakom shenotlin sachar al hamikra ve'ein notlin sachar al hamidrash* — in a place where teachers are paid for teaching *mikra* but not for teaching *midrash*. The prohibition is not about the content per se but about the economic structure. When teaching *mikra* is a paid service and teaching *Torah she'be'al peh* is not, then the one who teaches *mikra* for free is effectively giving the recipient money — he is forgoing payment he would otherwise be entitled to collect. That waived payment is a form of *hana'ah*. Teaching *Torah she'be'al peh*, by contrast, is not a service that commands a fee, and therefore the act of teaching it creates no *hana'ah* even when the recipient benefits.

The Gemara clarifies that this teaches us something precise: even in a place where it is permissible to charge for teaching *mikra*, one may only take a fee for *mikra* itself — not for *midrash*. The principle is that while *mikra* may be compensated in certain circumstances, *Torah she'be'al peh* may not.

### Torah & Payment

This raises the deeper question: why is it acceptable to pay for *mikra* at all, and why is *Torah she'be'al peh* categorically different? The Gemara will address this through the specific nature of what is being compensated, but the underlying norm requires articulation.

### Moshe's Model

The prohibition on payment for teaching *Torah* is grounded textually. The verse states: *va'oti tziva Hashem ba'eis hi lilamed eschem chukim u'mishpatim* — God commanded me at that time to teach you statutes and ordinances. And another verse: *re'eh limadti eschem chukim u'mishpatim ka'asher tzivani Hashem elokai* — "see, I have taught you statutes and ordinances as Hashem my God commanded me." The emphasis on *ka'asher tzivani* — "as He commanded me" — is read to mean that Moshe taught within certain parameters set by the divine model. *Mah ani bechinam af atem bechinam* — just as I taught you without charge, so too you must teach others without charge.

The precise force of the *derashah* is interesting. The simplest reading of *ka'asher tzivani* may be that God taught Moshe *bechinam*, and Moshe is bound to replicate that model in his own teaching. Alternatively, the verse may mean that the manner in which Moshe taught Israel should be the model for how Israel teaches subsequent generations. Either way, the *halacha* is established: Torah may not be taught for pay.

This generates the well-known practical difficulty: how are Torah teachers and rabbis paid? The standard *halachic* answer is *s'char batala* — compensation for opportunity cost, for the other productive activities the teacher foregoes by devoting himself to teaching. This framework, however, produces counterintuitive results when pressed. One would expect that the teacher with the best alternative economic opportunities should command the highest salary, while the teacher whose only skill is teaching — however gifted — should command the lowest. The highest salary would go to the computer programmer who also happens to teach, not to the finest educator who has devoted himself entirely to Torah study. The tension between *s'char batala* as a theoretical framework and professional compensation as a practical reality is a recognized difficulty, though its full exploration lies beyond the immediate scope of the Gemara here.

## Minors & Babysitting

### Recipient Identity

The Gemara now presses Shmuel's distinction. If the only reason one may not teach *mikra* to the person from whom one cannot take *hana'ah* is that teaching *mikra* is a paid service in a given locale, then the prohibition should apply only when the teacher would normally be paid. But the *mishna* treats the prohibition as categorical. How does Shmuel's answer account for this?

### Minor Benefit

The Gemara introduces a different model: *s'char shimur* — compensation for babysitting. When children are sent to study *mikra*, the parents are not only paying for instruction in Torah; they are also paying the teacher to watch their children. The payment for *mikra* is therefore really payment for childcare, and this explains why teachers of *mikra* to young children can collect a fee while teachers of *Torah she'be'al peh* cannot. *Torah she'be'al peh* is studied by adults who do not need to be watched, and therefore no fee is attached. *Mikra* is associated with young children who do require supervision, and the fee reflects that supervision.

On this model, when Charlie is forbidden to provide *hana'ah* to a given person and that person sends his children to be taught *mikra*, Charlie is giving the parent a benefit: the parent does not have to pay a babysitting fee. That constitutes *hana'ah*.

### Adult Exception

The Gemara, working with the *s'char shimur* model, must now account for the *mishna*'s text. The *mishna* says he may not teach *mikra* to the person himself but may teach it to the person's children. Reading the *mishna* with *s'char shimur* in mind, the Gemara proposes: *lo yilamedenu mikra bekatan* — he may not teach *mikra* to a minor. The prohibition applies specifically when the recipient is a child, because that is when a supervision fee would normally be charged. *Im haya gadol* — if the person is an adult, *melamed lo ulbanav mikra* — one may teach him directly, and may teach his children as well, the latter because the benefit to the adult is only indirect.

This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *mishna*. The text of the *mishna* does not obviously refer to minors, and the *sugya* acknowledges the strain of this interpretation.

### Indirect Benefit

The case of teaching the adult's children is governed by the principle of indirect benefit. Shimon teaches Reuven's children; Reuven benefits because he does not have to pay for a teacher or babysitter. But this benefit reaches Reuven only through a third party — his children — and indirect benefit of this kind, analogous to having one's debt paid, does not constitute prohibited *hana'ah*. The prohibition on *hana'ah* applies to direct benefit; what arrives through an intermediary is categorically different.

## Shabbat Lessons

### Baraita Rule

A *baraita* is cited: *tinokot lo korin batchila beshabbat ela shonin barishon* — children may not begin studying a new lesson for the first time on *Shabbat*, but they may review material they have already learned. The natural assumption is that this distinction tracks some *halachic* concern about *Shabbat*, and the Gemara initially frames it in terms of whether the teaching constitutes a paid service — which would raise the question of earning wages on *Shabbat*.

### Shabbat Earnings

On the reading that payment for *mikra* is for *s'char pisuk ta'amim* — compensation for teaching cantillation — the *baraita* makes sense. The first lesson involves learning the *trop* for the first time, which is the paid component. Teaching the *trop* on *Shabbat* would mean earning wages on *Shabbat*, which is problematic. Review, by contrast, focuses on meaning and comprehension rather than the initial instruction in cantillation, and carries no fee. Therefore the distinction between first lesson and review maps onto the distinction between a paid and an unpaid service.

The *s'char shimur* model, however, creates a difficulty. If the teacher is always being paid for babysitting regardless of whether it is the first lesson or a review, the distinction between the two should be irrelevant from a *Shabbat* earnings perspective.

### Havlah Principle

The Gemara deflates the entire wages-on-*Shabbat* framework by noting that *havla* — absorption of *Shabbat* compensation within a larger fee — is permitted. The concern about earning wages on *Shabbat* applies only where the *Shabbat* payment stands alone and is unconnected to any broader work arrangement. When payment for *Shabbat* services is absorbed into a weekly, monthly, annual, or multi-year wage, the *Shabbat* component is legally dissolved into the larger arrangement and there is no prohibition.

Teaching arrangements are almost always structured as ongoing employment agreements. The teacher who is hired for the school year, the week, or the month is not separately earning wages for *Shabbat*; his *Shabbat* services are bundled into his overall compensation. On this basis, the concern about *Shabbat* wages is inapplicable to the teaching context, and the *baraita*'s prohibition on first lessons cannot be derived from a wages-on-*Shabbat* analysis.

### Weekly Wages

A *baraita* is cited to confirm that *havla* is operative in precisely these care-based employment relationships. One who is hired to watch a child, a *parah adumah*, or seeds planted for *ktziras ha'omer* for a single day on *Shabbat* is not paid for the *Shabbat* service, and consequently is treated as a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid guardian — with the lower standard of liability that entails. But if the employment is structured as a weekly (*sechir shabbat* — meaning here a week), monthly (*sechir chodesh*), annual (*sechir shana*), or seven-year (*sechir shavua*) arrangement, then the *Shabbat* service is absorbed into the overall fee. The worker does receive pay for *Shabbat*, and accordingly is treated as a *shomer sachar* — a paid guardian — with the higher liability that follows. This confirms that *havla* applies and that *Shabbat* earnings integrated into a broader arrangement are permitted.

The implication is that the *baraita* prohibiting first lessons on *Shabbat* cannot be about wages. Under normal employment conditions, wages for *Shabbat* teaching are absorbed via *havla* and present no problem.

### Simcha Focus

The Gemara therefore offers an entirely different reason for the prohibition on first lessons on *Shabbat*: *mishum deyifnu avotehen deyinukei bemitzvasa deshabbosa* — so that the fathers of these children should turn their attention to the *mitzvah* of *Shabbat*. The purpose is that parents should be free to spend *Shabbat* with their children, to enjoy *oneg Shabbat* together, to eat the *Shabbat* meal together without being pulled away by the demands of a child who has to learn new material. Beginning a new lesson is taxing; it demands sustained focus and extended time, and it disrupts the *Shabbat* atmosphere of rest and family enjoyment.

The *Ran* formulates this as: the fathers of the children turn to the *mitzvot* of *Shabbat* and enjoy time with their children — *umishtasha'im im hatinokot* — and if the children were learning new lessons, the fathers would not want to take them away from their study and would therefore be separated from them. *Tosafot* frames it more prosaically: beginning the lesson would cause the children to arrive very late to the *Shabbat* meal.

### Shabbat Eating

The Gemara appends an additional explanation: *mishum d'beshabata achlin veshasin* — on *Shabbat* people eat and drink beyond their normal patterns, *veyakar aleihon alma* — and the world feels heavy upon them. When one eats outside one's normal routine, one is not in the optimal mental state for focused first-time learning. Beginning new material under these conditions is poor pedagogy; the child will not absorb the lesson well. This is supported by a statement of Shmuel: *shinuy veset techillat choli me'ayim* — a change in one's normal routine is the beginning of intestinal trouble. Hard material encountered for the first time while in an altered physical state will not be properly retained.

### Babysitting Critique

The *s'char shimur* model therefore fails to account for the *baraita*'s distinction between first lessons and review on *Shabbat*. If the teacher is always collecting a babysitting fee whether he is giving a first lesson or a review, there is no basis within that framework to differentiate the two — the fee is the same, the *havla* applies equally, and no *Shabbat* wages concern arises in either case. The prohibition on first lessons must rest on the reasons identified — either the demand on parental attention or the pedagogical effects of unusual eating — not on any analysis of fees.

## Ta'amim & Pisuk

### Charging Model

Returning to the analytical framework: the debate between *s'char shimur* and *s'char pisuk ta'amim* determines the scope of what may be charged for when teaching *mikra* to children. If *s'char pisuk ta'amim* is correct — that payment is for the *trop* instruction — then charging is permitted specifically for that component and not for the teaching of the textual content itself. This is what the Gemara means when it says that even in a place where it is acceptable to pay for teaching *mikra*, one may charge only for *mikra* and not for *midrash*; and within *mikra*, the chargeable component is the cantillation instruction.

### De'Oraita Debate

This leads to a striking question. The one who holds *s'char shimur* does not adopt *s'char pisuk ta'amim*, and the Gemara asks why. The answer given is: *kasavar pissuk teamim de'oraita* — he holds that *pisuk ta'amim* is *de'oraita*. If the cantillation and division of the text is part of the *Torah* itself — intrinsic to its identity, not extrinsic — then it cannot be charged for any more than teaching the words of the *Torah* can be charged for. One who holds *pisuk ta'amim de'oraita* cannot justify a fee for teaching *trop* on the grounds that it is a peripheral or ancillary skill.

### Nechemia's Verse

The Gemara cites a statement of Rav Ika bar Avin in the name of Rav Chananel in the name of Rav, based on a verse in Nechemiah: *vayikre'u besefer Torat Elokim meforash veshum sechel vayavinu bamikra* — "and they read in the book of the Torah of God distinctly, and they gave the sense, and they caused the people to understand the reading." This verse is parsed as containing multiple distinct components of Torah transmission, each of which is identified.

### Components Listed

*Vayikre'u besefer Torat Elokim* — they read in the book of the Torah of God — *zeh mikra*: this refers to the Biblical text itself. *Meforash* — distinctly, explicitly — *zeh targum*: this is the Aramaic translation. And on this the Gemara notes parenthetically: does this mean the *Targum* itself is *de'oraita*, given at Sinai? The question is whether *de'oraita* is being used to mean "commanded by the Torah" or simply "central to the Torah's identity." *Veshum sechel* — and they gave the sense — *elu hapessukim*: these are the divisions of the *psukim*, the knowledge of where one verse ends and the next begins. This is not yet *pisuk ta'amim* in the full sense but the more basic knowledge of *pessukim* demarcation. *Vayavinu bamikra* — and they caused them to understand in the reading — *zeh pissuk ta'amim*: this is the cantillation system itself, the *trop*, which indicates the relationship between words, analogous to commas and semicolons in punctuation.

*Ve'amrei lah* — and some say — *elu hamesorot*: the final component is alternatively identified as the *masorot* — the tradition governing *chaserot viyeterot*, which letters are written fully and which are written defectively, and other questions of orthography and textual transmission.

### De'Oraita Meaning

There is thus a dispute: is *pisuk ta'amim* included among the components identified in Nechemiah's verse, or is it the *masorot*? Either way, the verse is read as establishing that at least some of these components — *targum*, the demarcation of *psukim*, cantillation or *masorot* — are part of the *Torah*'s core identity.

The deeper question is what *de'oraita* means in this context. It may mean that these elements were literally given at *Har Sinai*, in which case the *Targum* — and the *trop* — would have been transmitted to Moshe together with the text. Alternatively, and perhaps more straightforwardly, *de'oraita* may mean that these elements are part of the essential identity of *Torah*, not peripheral tools but constitutive components of what *Torah* is. The question is analogous to asking whether Hebrew biblical grammar belongs in *limudei kodesh* or *limudei chol* — whether one recites *birchat ha'Torah* before studying biblical grammar, whether it is considered *Torah* proper or a scholarly discipline about *Torah*. The *trop* shows which words are connected to which, shapes meaning, and affects *psak halacha* in some contexts; yet the Gemara seems to suggest that one authority views it as sufficiently distinct from *Torah* content itself that payment for teaching it may be justified. The other authority, holding *pissuk teamim de'oraita*, regards it as too central to *Torah* to permit compensation, and must locate the justification for teachers' fees elsewhere — in *s'char shimur*. This debate and its further ramifications will continue into the following *amud*.
