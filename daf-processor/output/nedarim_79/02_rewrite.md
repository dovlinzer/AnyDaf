# Nedarim 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumcheh & Beit Din

### Hatarat Nedarim Overview

We begin today's *daf* at the bottom of 78b, in the middle of a discussion concerning *hatarat nedarim*. The Gemara has established that *hatarat nedarim* — the annulment of vows — may be performed either by a *yachid mumcheh*, a single expert authority, or by a court of three laypeople.

### Defining Mumcheh

The precise definition of *mumcheh* is itself a matter of dispute. One view holds that it requires *semicha* from Moshe Rabbeinu — a chain of ordination reaching back to Sinai — a standard that effectively renders the category inapplicable in post-Talmudic times. A second view requires an extraordinarily high level of knowledge and brilliance, a threshold so demanding that even some *Amoraim* may not have attained it. The third approach, which appears to be the inclination of the Ran, is that *mumcheh* simply means *rashei hamatot* — someone who stands among the distinguished Torah authorities of his generation. On this view, every generation has its *mumchehim*; it is not an exclusive club of five, but neither is it open to all.

### Shulchan Aruch Mumcheh

The *Shulchan Aruch*'s position appears to set an even more accessible standard. It initially frames *mumcheh* in terms of *gimur vesavir* — full mastery and comprehension of the relevant *halachot* of *nedarim* — suggesting that genuine expertise in this area suffices. Interestingly, however, the *Shulchan Aruch* adds that in the present day, no one qualifies as a *mumcheh*. The apparent tension between these two statements — a relatively modest theoretical threshold paired with the practical conclusion that no one meets it — is not easily resolved.

### Beit Din Members

When *hatarat nedarim* is performed by a court of three, the question arises as to the required qualifications of each member. All authorities agree that the members cannot be entirely ignorant of what is transpiring; each must be sufficiently engaged to meaningfully participate in the process. The intuitive position, therefore, is that at least one member must possess *gimur vesavir* — genuine mastery — while the other two need only *savir*, the capacity to understand what is explained to them. On this model, one person truly knows the material, explains it to the others, and they comprehend it well enough to function as legitimate members of the *beit din*.

### Savir Standard

Here too, however, the *Shulchan Aruch* introduces a surprising ruling: it appears to hold that all three members need only meet the *savir* standard. This raises an obvious difficulty — if all three are merely *savir*, who is doing the explaining? Presumably some fourth party, not a formal member of the *beit din*, would need to provide the necessary background. The *Shulchan Aruch* does not resolve this, but the ruling reflects a notably permissive approach to the qualifications required for *hatarat nedarim*.

## R. Chanina Silence

### Silence Indicates Intent

Picking up approximately five lines down on 78b, the Gemara records a striking interpretation attributed to Rabbi Chanina. The Torah states that if a husband remains silent for a day upon hearing his wife's vow, the vow is established — *yekimenu*, it becomes *hakamah*, and he loses the power to annul. Rabbi Chanina proposes a radical reinterpretation of this rule. The reason silence constitutes *hakamah*, he argues, is not the silence itself but rather what the silence indicates. If a husband is quiet for an entire day without annulling the vow, we infer that he was genuinely accepting of it — he was effectively ratifying the *neder* through his inaction. The *pasuk*, on this reading, is not establishing a categorical rule about silence but rather a presumption about what silence reveals.

### Spite Silence

This interpretive move opens the door to significant qualification. If the husband is silent not because he accepts the vow but for some other reason — say, to cause his wife anxiety, to manipulate her emotionally, to let her remain uncertain about whether the *neder* will stand — then his silence does not indicate acceptance at all. He has his own agenda, entirely unrelated to *hakamah*. In such a case, argues Rabbi Chanina, the silence should not be treated as *hakamah*, and the husband's ability to perform *hafarah* should remain intact.

### Heifer After 10 Days

Rabbi Chanina follows this reasoning to its conclusion: even after ten days have passed, if the silence was clearly motivated by spite or manipulation rather than genuine acceptance, the husband retains the right to perform *hafarah*. The operative concept is *yom shamo* — the day is defined by what the silence indicates. Where silence plainly does not indicate acceptance, the clock does not run on the husband's ability to annul.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges this position from a Baraita that enumerates the circumstances under which a father regains control over his daughter's vows after the husband's involvement. The Baraita states: *eimatai amru met habaal nishkena reshut la'av* — when does the father regain authority? When the husband neither heard the vow, or heard it and remained silent (*shama veshatak*), or heard it and annulled it and then died on that very day. By contrast, if the husband was *mekayem* — actively affirming the vow — or was silent and then died after 24 hours, the father cannot annul. Rava notes that this Baraita appears to make no distinction based on the reason for the silence. Any silence sustained past the end of the *halachic* day appears to constitute *hakamah* regardless of intent.

### 24 Hour Silence

Rava's logic is pointed: the Baraita simply states that silence beyond the day is *hakamah*, without qualifying whether the silence was motivated by acceptance or by spite. If Rabbi Chanina were correct that spite-motivated silence never constitutes *hakamah*, one would expect the Baraita to distinguish between the two cases. Its failure to do so seems to imply that all silence, whatever its motivation, yields *hakamah* by the end of the day.

### Shatak Purpose

The Gemara responds with a distinction. When the Baraita speaks of *shatak* — of silence — it is specifically referring to silence undertaken with the intent of *kiyum*, of ratifying the vow. That is the ordinary case: a husband who is making up his mind, uncertain whether to annul, and lets the day pass without acting. That silence constitutes *hakamah* by the end of the day. The Baraita was never addressing the exceptional case of silence motivated by spite. Rava's challenge therefore does not compel Rabbi Chanina's defeat.

The Gemara then presses further. If *shatak al menat lekayem* — silence specifically intended as *kiyum* — is the case under discussion, that is effectively identical to an explicit verbal *kiyum*. Why distinguish it from the Baraita's earlier phrase *shama vekiyem*, where the husband actively ratified the vow? The Gemara's answer, as explained by the Ran, is that there are two meaningfully distinct scenarios.

### Ran's Two Scenarios

The Ran explains that *hakamah* does not require verbalization. A husband who, at a given moment, genuinely and actively desires to be *mekayem* the vow right now — even without saying a word — has performed a valid *kiyum* immediately. His silence in that instant, if it is apparent that this is his intent, is sufficient. By contrast, if the husband is in a state of deliberation — thinking about doing *kiyum* at some later point, not yet resolved — then we cannot credit him with an immediate *kiyum*, because his intent is not yet evident. In that case, it is only the passage of the full *halachic* day without *hafarah* that resolves the ambiguity and renders the silence meaningful as *hakamah*.

Thus *shama vekiyem*, where the husband actively ratifies, and *shatak al menat lekayem stam* — neutral deliberative silence — are not identical: the first works immediately, the second requires the full day. The classic ruling that silence until the end of the day constitutes *hakamah* addresses only the intermediate *stam* case, where the husband is neither actively ratifying nor actively spiting, but simply undecided.

## Hakeimah vs Heifer

### Chomer in Each

The Gemara now introduces a Baraita cited by Rav Chisda asserting that there are respects in which *hakamah* is stronger than *hafarah*, and respects in which *hafarah* is stronger than *hakamah*.

### Hakeimah Non-Verbal

The first area where *hakamah* is stronger: silence itself can produce *kiyum*. As established above, the passage of the full day without *hafarah* constitutes *hakamah* even without the husband ever uttering a word.

### Heifer Verbal Only

By contrast, silence never produces *hafarah*. *Hafarah* requires verbalization — some form of explicit speech — and no amount of silence, however prolonged, can serve as an annulment of the vow.

### Kiyum In Heart

These two points are, in fact, deeply connected. The Baraita states: *kiyem belibo kiyem; heifer belibo eino mufar* — if one ratified the vow in his heart, it is ratified; if one annulled the vow in his heart, it is not annulled. *Kiyum* does not require verbalization; it can be achieved through internal intention alone. *Hafarah* must be spoken aloud. The reason silence over the course of the day yields *hakamah* is precisely because we infer from that silence that the husband's internal disposition is one of acceptance — and since *kiyum* operates through internal disposition, the inference suffices. *Hafarah*, which operates exclusively through speech, cannot be accomplished in this indirect manner.

One can see immediately how these two points reinforce each other: the effectiveness of silent *kiyum* explains why the passage of time without speech yields *hakamah*, and the requirement of speech for *hafarah* explains why no analogous inference can work in the opposite direction.

### Mutual Exclusion

The Baraita then notes one area of symmetry: *kiyem ein yakhol lehafer; heifer ein yakhol lekayem* — once one has performed *kiyum*, *hafarah* is no longer available; once one has performed *hafarah*, *kiyum* is no longer available. This mutual exclusivity applies in both directions equally.

The Baraita promised an area where *hafarah* is stronger than *hakamah*, yet so far only areas of *hakamah*'s strength have been identified.

### Undoing Hakeimah

The Gemara supplies the answer in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *nish'alin al ha-hekem ve-ein nish'alin al ha-hefer* — one may seek release from *hakamah* through a *chakham*, but not from *hafarah*. Since *hakamah* operates by reinforcing the *neder* — by treating it as fully affirmed — it partakes of the character of a *neder* itself, and just as a *neder* may be undone through *hasharat nedarim* before a *chakham*, so too a *hakamah* may be unwound in that way. *Hafarah*, once performed, cannot be undone. In this respect, *hafarah* is definitively stronger.

## Three Tyufot

### Tyufa 1: Spite

The Gemara now marshals three successive Tannaitic challenges against Rabbi Chanina's position. The first is brought by Rav Kahana. The Baraita addresses a case of *shoteik al menat lemekat* — a husband who is silent specifically to cause his wife distress — and rules that by the end of the *halachic* day, this silence nonetheless constitutes *hakamah*. This appears to be a direct and explicit rejection of Rabbi Chanina, who held that spite-motivated silence should not yield *hakamah* regardless of how much time has passed.

### Three Silences

The Gemara responds by questioning whether the Baraita is indeed addressing spite-motivated silence, suggesting it might be referring to the more ordinary case of silence aimed at eventual *kiyum*. The Gemara then resolves the matter by noting that the word *hecherish* — "he was silent" — appears three times in the relevant *pasuk*. Each mention extends the ruling to an additional type of silence: one occurrence addresses silence where the husband intends immediate *kiyum*; a second addresses deliberative silence; and the third — the surplus mention — extends the law to even spite-motivated silence. The Torah's threefold use of the word of silence is thus read as a deliberate textual signal that all forms of silence, irrespective of motivation, yield *hakamah* by the end of the day. This constitutes a clear refutation of Rabbi Chanina.

### Tyufa 2: Shabbat

The second challenge is raised by Rava. The Gemara elsewhere permits a husband to perform *hafarah* on *Shabbat* — an act that would otherwise seem to raise concerns — specifically because if the vow was taken as *Shabbat* was ending, he must have the opportunity to annul before the day closes. The reasoning is: if he cannot perform *hafarah* on *Shabbat*, then the day ends and the vow becomes established. The very fact that we permit *hafarah* on *Shabbat* to prevent this outcome is Rava's basis for a powerful argument.

### Shabbat Inference

Consider the logic carefully. If a husband cannot perform *hafarah* on *Shabbat* — whether due to a rabbinic prohibition — then his silence on that day is not a free choice. He is silent because *halacha* prevents him from acting, not because he wishes to ratify the vow. According to Rabbi Chanina, such silence — silence whose reason is clearly not acceptance — should not become *hakamah*. If that were so, he would not lose the right to perform *hafarah* at the end of *Shabbat*, and we would have no reason to permit *hafarah* on *Shabbat* in the first place. The very existence of the dispensation to perform *hafarah* on *Shabbat* demonstrates that the reason for one's silence is legally irrelevant — any silence sustained through the end of the day becomes *hakamah*, period. This is another *tyufta* against Rabbi Chanina.

### Tyufa 3: Ignorance

The third challenge comes from Rav Ashi. The Gemara addresses a case where a husband says: I knew that my wife took a vow, but I did not know that I had the power to perform *hafarah*. He was never taught the relevant laws. Three days have now passed. The ruling: *yaper* — he may still perform *hafarah*. The reasoning is that *beyom shamo* — "on the day that he heard" — is defined not from the moment he first heard the words of the *neder*, but from the moment he became aware that he was in a position to act on it. Hearing without the contextual knowledge that one has the ability to respond is not halachically meaningful hearing.

### Hearing Debate

This ruling sets up a related debate about a parallel case. What if the husband says: I knew I had the power to perform *hafarah*, but I did not realize that what my wife said constituted a *neder*? Rabbi Meir rules that this still counts as hearing; the husband heard the words, knew he had the relevant legal power, and his failure to recognize the statement as a *neder* does not reset the clock. The *chakhamim* disagree and hold that this is analogous to the previous case — without realizing it is a *neder*, one has not truly "heard" it in the halachically operative sense, and the day begins only when full awareness is achieved.

What is notable for our purposes is what both Rabbi Meir and the *chakhamim* appear to agree upon: once the clock does begin — once hearing is established — the reason for the husband's subsequent silence does not matter. If a man is silent because he is clueless about *halacha*, that too becomes *hakamah* by the end of the day. The debate is only about when the clock starts, not about whether the motivation behind silence modulates the *hakamah* rule. This is the third and final *tyufta* against Rabbi Chanina's intent-based limitation.

## Ran & R. Akiva Eiger

### Ran On Silence

Returning to the Ran's discussion, the Gemara's principle that *kiyum* may be accomplished non-verbally — indeed, through the very mechanism of silence — requires explanation. The Ran addresses this directly: we learn from the law of silence on the day of hearing that *hakamah* does not require verbalization. The fact that sustained silence through the end of the day yields *hakamah* demonstrates that *kiyum* operates through internal disposition, not through speech.

### Intent Evidence

Why, then, does the silence need to last until the end of the day? The Ran explains: until sunset, it is simply not yet clear what the silence means. Perhaps the husband is planning to perform *hafarah* shortly. The ambiguity cannot be resolved before the day ends. Only once the full day has passed without any act of *hafarah* does the silence become unambiguous evidence that the husband's disposition is one of acceptance. As the Ran puts it: since we are going exclusively by what the husband intends, and we do not have direct access to his inner state, we require the accumulated weight of an entire day's silence to establish that his intent is indeed *kiyum*.

### Active Desire

From this the Ran draws a further inference: if the husband at some earlier moment during the day genuinely and actively desires — not merely contemplates — *kiyum*, that intent is effective immediately, without needing to wait until sunset. The Ran describes this as analogous to crossing a finish line: if you have actually crossed it, you do not need to wait for the race to be over. In such a case it is as though the full day has already been discharged, because the husband's will is unambiguously directed toward *kiyum* at this very moment.

### Akiva Eiger Problem

Rabbi Akiva Eiger raises a sharp challenge against this position. The Ran has argued, on the one hand, that since *kiyum* operates through internal intent, and since we cannot access a person's internal thoughts directly, we require the silence of a full day as evidence of that intent. But on the other hand, the Ran also holds that if the husband actively desires *kiyum* right now, his intent is immediately effective — without waiting for the day to end. The difficulty is this: if, as the Ran acknowledges, we normally cannot access what a person is thinking, and if we therefore require observable evidence (the passage of the full day) to establish *kiyum* — then how can an immediate unspoken desire for *kiyum* ever work? In the case of immediate intent, there is no equivalent evidence. The husband has said nothing and done nothing; we simply have no basis on which to credit his inner desire with halachic effect.

### Evidence Requirement

The *Gilyon HaShas* addresses this challenge. The underlying principle, he explains, is that internal thoughts — *devarim shebelev* — do not generally carry halachic significance. However, where those thoughts are *muchach uvalui lechol* — evident and manifest to observers — they do acquire halachic weight. This is the logic of the silent *hakamah* that accrues over the course of the day: the sustained silence renders the husband's inner acceptance evident to those around him. By the end of the day, *galui lanu kavanato* — his intent is disclosed to us — and that is why it is legally effective.

### Observable Evidence

Applying this framework to Rabbi Akiva Eiger's challenge, the *Gilyon HaShas* resolves that even in the case of immediate *kiyum*, there would need to be some form of observable evidence — something visible to others — for the immediate intent to take legal effect. Merely thinking, with nothing to disclose the thought to any observer, would fall under the general rule that unmanifest internal states carry no halachic weight. Silence accumulated over the day provides precisely such evidence; a bare internal desire, unaccompanied by any observable act or signal, does not. The question of immediate *kiyum* is thus left with some difficulty, though the *Gilyon HaShas*'s framework at least clarifies what form of evidence would theoretically be required.

## Heifer Categories

### Nedarim To Meifer

With the beginning of the final chapter of the tractate, the Gemara turns from the question of *who* may perform *hafarah* and *when* to the question of *which* vows are subject to *hafarah* at all. The opening of the *perek* establishes: *ve-elu nedarim shehu mefer* — these are the vows that a husband annuls. Before proceeding, it is worth noting the Ran's observation that the same categories applicable to the husband — *inuy nefesh* and the relational category of *bein ish leishah* — apply equally to the father with respect to his daughter, at least according to the Ran's position. The Rambam disagrees and holds that the father's authority over his minor daughter's vows is unlimited; the more restricted categorization applies only to the husband, who is dealing with an emancipated adult.

### Inuy Nefesh

The Torah frames the husband's authority over his wife's vows around the phrase *leanot nefesh* — to afflict the soul, or more precisely, to afflict the body. The first category of vows subject to *hafarah* is accordingly those involving *inuy nefesh*, vows whose effect constitutes a form of bodily affliction.

### Inuy Examples

The *Mishnah* offers two initial examples of *inuy nefesh*: a vow to refrain from bathing and a vow to refrain from wearing makeup. These are presented as paradigmatic cases of self-denial that rise to the level of *inuy nefesh*.

### Bein Ish Leishah

The second category is derived from the Torah's phrase *bein ish leishah* — between a man and his wife — and encompasses vows that, while perhaps not constituting bodily affliction, directly affect the marital relationship and the dynamics between husband and wife. This category opens a separate channel of authority for the husband beyond the *inuy nefesh* framework.

### R. Yosi Position

Rabbi Yosi disputes the *Mishnah*'s classification. He holds that refraining from bathing and refraining from makeup do not rise to the level of *inuy nefesh*. This is a striking position, particularly given the Roman-era context in which the Talmud was composed, where public bathing was a regular and socially normative practice. Yet Rabbi Yosi maintains that *inuy nefesh* requires a higher threshold — something closer to a complete and significant deprivation — and that these examples fall short. Instead, Rabbi Yosi proposes his own paradigm cases of *inuy nefesh*. What Rabbi Yosi does with bathing and makeup, then, is presumably to place them in the *bein ish leishah* category rather than *inuy nefesh* — an important distinction, as the Gemara will go on to explain.

### Non-Inuy Examples

Rabbi Yosi's conception of *inuy nefesh* is notably demanding. He illustrates with two cases where *hafarah* is *not* available: if a wife vows to abstain from the fruit of a particular country, that is not *inuy nefesh*, because fruit from other countries remains available — *yavi mimedina acheret*, go fetch it from another region. Similarly, if she vows to abstain from produce purchased at a particular storekeeper's shop, that too is not *inuy nefesh*, since she can simply shop elsewhere. These cases lack the quality of genuine, inescapable deprivation that *inuy nefesh* requires.

### Sole Supplier

There is, however, an exception to the storekeeper case. If the husband's only practical supplier of food was that particular storekeeper — for example, because the storekeeper was the only one who would extend him credit, while no other vendor would deal with him on those terms — then forbidding the produce of that storekeeper's shop does constitute *inuy nefesh*. Whatever the theoretical availability of alternatives, the practical reality is that this family's food supply depends entirely on that one source, and to eliminate it is genuine deprivation. The Ran further applies this principle to the country case: when the *Mishnah* says that forbidding fruit from a particular country is not *inuy nefesh* because one can obtain it from elsewhere, this presupposes that the alternative country is genuinely comparable — both are secondary sources. If the forbidden country is in fact the primary source of the household's food, the analysis would follow the storekeeper model and the vow would indeed constitute *inuy nefesh*.

## Permanence Analysis

### Only Inuy Mufar

The Gemara initially reads the *Mishnah* as holding that only *inuy nefesh* vows are subject to *hafarah* by the husband. Vows lacking *inuy nefesh* cannot be annulled.

### Baraita Citation

A Baraita immediately challenges this reading by citing the Torah's phrase *bein ish leishah bein av levito* — explicitly establishing that vows *bein ish leishah*, in the relational category, are also subject to *hafarah*. The Baraita confirms that both categories — *inuy nefesh* and *bein ish leishah* — lie within the husband's authority.

### Why Distinguish

This raises a pointed question. If the husband can annul vows in both categories, why does Rabbi Yosi insist that bathing and makeup are *not inuy nefesh*? If they fall into the *bein ish leishah* category, the husband can annul them anyway. What halachic work is the categorical distinction doing?

### Suggested Theory

The Gemara proposes a theory: although the husband can perform *hafarah* on both categories, the categories differ in their duration of effect. *Inuy nefesh* vows, when annulled, are annulled permanently — *meifer le-olam*. But *bein ish leishah* vows, when annulled, are effective only during the marriage; upon divorce, the *neder* reasserts itself. On this theory, the mechanism of *hafarah* differs between the two categories: in the case of *inuy nefesh*, the husband possesses genuine annulment authority; in the case of *bein ish leishah*, his power is more like a right to suspend the vow's operation for as long as the marital relationship continues. This would explain why Rabbi Yosi needs to distinguish the categories — whether a vow falls into *inuy nefesh* or merely *bein ish leishah* has real consequences for the permanence of the *hafarah*.

### R. Yochanan Nuri

This theory is supported, at least prima facie, by the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri in the *Mishnah*. If a wife takes a vow forbidding the product of her labor to her husband — *maaseh yadeha* — the husband is not technically required to annul it, since that labor belongs to him anyway. But Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri advises that he should annul it regardless. The reason: if they later divorce, he would no longer own her labor, and the vow — never having been formally annulled — would spring back into effect and forbid her labor to him. This would prevent them from remarrying, since no marriage can function if the wife's labor is forbidden to her husband. The advice to annul proactively demonstrates that *hafarah* of a *bein ish leishah* vow can have lasting effect — it will protect against the vow's reinstatement even after divorce. This seems to support the theory that *hafarah* of such vows does indeed carry some lasting force.

### Theory Rejected

The Gemara ultimately rejects the theory that *bein ish leishah hafarah* lapses upon divorce. Even after the couple divorces, a *hafarah* that was performed during the marriage remains effective. The case of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri confirms this: the whole point of his advice is that the husband should perform *hafarah now* so that it will remain in effect even if they divorce — which would make no sense if *bein ish leishah hafarah* automatically lapsed upon divorce.

### Refined Distinction

The Gemara therefore refines the distinction. Both *inuy nefesh* and *bein ish leishah* vows, when annulled, remain annulled even after divorce. The difference lies in what happens if the woman subsequently marries another man. For *inuy nefesh* vows, the original husband's *hafarah* is effective *bein le'atzmo bein le'acheirim* — both for himself and for others, meaning the vow remains annulled even with respect to a new husband. For *bein ish leishah* vows, the *hafarah* is effective *le'atzmo* — for himself — but not *le'acheirim* — not for a new husband. Once she marries another man, the vow reasserts itself in her new relationship.

The underlying logic is that *bein ish leishah hafarah* is not a free-standing annulment of the *neder* but rather a suspension rooted in the particular marital relationship. After divorce, the couple might still remarry, and in that potential relationship the *hafarah* retains its relevance — it remains *bein ish leishah* even in the divorce state, since the bond has not been fully severed beyond all prospect of reunion. But once she marries another man, the original marital context that grounded the *hafarah* is definitively gone, the old husband's sphere of authority over this *neder* is extinguished, and the *neder* reasserts itself in her new marriage. With *inuy nefesh*, by contrast, the husband's authority is absolute: he possesses genuine annulment power, and what he annuls stays annulled regardless of subsequent relationships.
