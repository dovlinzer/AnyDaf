# Sanhedrin 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Proverbs 31:29 Aggada

### Yosef, Boaz, Palti

The daf opens in the midst of an extended *aggadic* passage that grew out of a *mishnah* concerning a king's marriages. The discussion turned to David, Saul, and Michal — and specifically to Palti ben Laish, to whom Michal had been given in marriage until she was returned to her rightful husband David. Rabbi Yochanan now draws on that narrative to expound the verse in Proverbs 31: *rabos banos asu chayil v'at alis al kulanu* — "Many daughters have done valor, but you have surpassed them all."

*Rabos banos asu chayil*, Rabbi Yochanan teaches, refers to Yosef and Boaz, who withstood sexual temptation. As the *gemara* discussed on the preceding daf, however, their restraint was qualified in certain respects: Boaz resisted temptation for only a single night, and the woman in question was unmarried; Yosef faced a married woman and a more sustained situation, yet she was not actually lying beside him. *V'at alis al kulanu* — "but you have surpassed them all" — this is Palti ben Laish, who lived as Michal's husband for years and, according to the *gemara*, never engaged in marital relations with her throughout that entire time.

It is striking that a verse explicitly praising women is here applied entirely to men and their resistance of temptation. The question of Michal's own restraint — and her own moral achievement — is conspicuously absent from the *gemara*'s discussion.

### Charm, Vanity, Fear

Rabbi Yochanan offers a second interpretation using the verse *sheker hachen v'hevel hayofi, yiras Hashem hi tishallal* — "False is charm and vain is beauty; a woman who fears God, she shall be praised." *Sheker hachen* — false charm — is Yosef; the quality was ultimately superficial in the sense that the woman he refused was married and not physically beside him. *V'hevel hayofi* — vain beauty — is Boaz; what he did appears admirable but lacks the ultimate depth of achievement. *Yiras Hashem hi tishallal* — the one who truly fears God — is Palti ben Laish. Once again, a verse framed around a woman's virtue is read as a portrait of male restraint, and once again the asymmetry in the *gemara*'s attention is remarkable.

### Generations Comparison

The *gemara* then offers an alternative interpretation (*davar acher*) of the same verse, now shifting from personal moral restraint to the domain of *Torah* study. *Sheker hachen* refers to the generation of Moshe, and *v'hevel hayofi* to the generation of Yehoshua — both represent great eras that nonetheless, for all their outward glory, do not represent the ultimate achievement in *Torah* learning. *Yiras Hashem hi tishallal* refers to the generation of Chizkiya, during which, the *gemara* reports elsewhere, a search from Dan to Be'er Sheva turned up no *amei ha'aretz* whatsoever — even children possessed remarkable Torah knowledge. We tend to romanticize the generations of Moshe and Yehoshua, but the *gemara* insists that the genuine flowering of *talmud Torah* came under Chizkiya.

### Yehuda bar Ilai Era

A second *davar acher* goes further still. Now the generations of Moshe and Yehoshua together are classified as *sheker hachen*, and the generation of Chizkiya itself is demoted to *hevel hayofi*. The true *yiras Hashem hi tishallal* is the generation of Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai. About him it was said: six students would cover themselves with a single *tallis* — they were so impoverished they could not afford separate blankets — and yet they would engage in Torah study.

### Mesirut Nefesh Analysis

This raises an important conceptual distinction. The praise of Chizkiya's generation centered on the breadth and depth of Torah knowledge — its penetration into every corner of the population. The praise of Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai's generation, by contrast, centers on *mesiras nefesh*, the self-sacrifice for *Torah* learning demonstrated through dire poverty. These are two different criteria of greatness, and the *gemara* seems to apply them differently to different generations. Chizkiya's era remains the paradigm of Torah knowledge as a mass achievement; Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai's era exemplifies what it means to sacrifice everything for learning.

The lecturer notes that in our own time, the *mesiras nefesh* of that kind of grinding poverty is largely absent. Yet in terms of the sheer number of Jews engaged in *Torah* study and the number of *yeshivos* in existence, the present era may rival or exceed every historical period — with the possible exception of the generation of Chizkiya.

## Royal Mourning Laws

### King Confinement

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the laws of mourning as they apply to a king. Unlike the *kohen gadol*, for whom the relevant law derives from a *pasuk* — *min hamikdash lo yeitzei* — the restrictions on a king are rooted in the practical need to protect and affirm his royal status as a public figure. Accordingly, if an immediate relative of the king dies, he does not leave the entrance of his palace. Even during mourning, his public persona must remain intact; private grief does not transform the king into a private individual.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: if the king wishes to accompany the funeral bier, he may do so, since we find that David followed the bier of Avner. Avner had been the general under Saul who had come over to David's side, before being treacherously killed by Yoav ben Tzeruya. The verse states: *vehamelech David holeich achar hamita* — King David walked after the bier.

### David & Avner Case

The *chachamim* respond: that was a special exception — *lo hayah hadavar ela lefayeis et ha'am*. David needed to appease the people, who suspected him of complicity in Avner's death. Avner had been on the verge of bringing all of Israel over to support David's realm during the interregnum under Ishboshet, Saul's son. His murder therefore threatened David's entire ascension to the kingship, and public suspicion was a genuine political danger. The action was therefore exceptional and cannot serve as a normative proof. This dynamic parallels the earlier *mishnah*, where Rabbi Yehuda similarly cited a verse only to receive the reply that it was a unique circumstance.

### Consolation Meal

The *mishnah* also addresses the *seudat havra'ah* — the consolation meal that mourners are fed by those who come to comfort them. When the king is fed this meal, all of the people recline on the ground while he reclines on the *dargash*. Just as in the case of the *kohen gadol* a *safsal* (a low bench) was used, so too the king occupies a position that is closer to the ground than normal seating but not directly on the floor as the other mourners are. Even in his grief, the distinction of his rank is preserved.

### Procession Order

A *baraita* addresses the arrangement of men and women in funeral processions. Where the local custom was for women to follow behind the coffin and men to walk in front, that custom is followed; where the custom was the reverse, that is followed. Rabbi Yehuda, however, maintains that women should always walk in front of the coffin — and once again he derives this from David's following of Avner's bier, reasoning that since David walked behind, the women must have been in front.

Once again the *chachamim* respond: *lo hayah hadavar ela lefayeis et ha'am* — this was an exception for the specific purpose of appeasing the people, and no general rule may be derived from it.

### David's Strategy

The *gemara* explains more precisely how David succeeded in this appeasement. He did not remain with one group of mourners in the procession but moved back and forth — leaving the men to go among the women, then returning from the women to go among the men — so that everyone could see that he was personally and conspicuously mourning for Avner. Because he was constantly shifting between groups, no conclusion can be drawn about which group — men or women — was positioned in front or behind. The verse confirms his success: *vayedu kol ha'am vechol Yisrael ki lo hayta mehamelech lehamis et Avner* — all the people and all Israel knew that it had not come from the king to put Avner to death.

The *gemara* also notes that the concern regarding the placement of women in front of men — specifically, whether men might gaze at women during the procession — does not appear to have been treated as decisive here. The accepted practice, wherever it exists, is followed in either direction.

### Havra'ah Wordplay

Rava expounds a further dimension of the episode. The verse states: *vayavo kol ha'am lehavrot et David* — all the people came to give David the *havra'ah*, the consolation meal. The word *lehavrot* as written in the text is similar to the word *lehachrot*, to cut down or destroy. Rava reads this as a *ketiv-keri* nuance: *techilah lehachroto* — initially the people came with the intent to destroy him, suspecting him of having ordered Avner's death — *ul'vasof lehavroto* — but in the end, after witnessing his mourning, they came to give him the proper consolation of *havra'ah*. The word carries both meanings simultaneously, capturing the transformation that David's public behavior achieved.

## Avner's Punishment

### Avner's Protest

The *gemara* asks: *mipnei mah ne'enash Avner?* — why was Avner punished? The presumption is that even a treacherous death implies some divine accounting. The answer given: *mipnei shehayah lo limchot beShaul velo michah* — because he had the ability and standing to protest Saul's massacre of Nov, the city of Kohanim, and he did not speak up. The *gemara* draws on the broader principle, stated in multiple places, that a bystander who has the power to protest and remains silent bears responsibility.

Rav Yitzchak disputes this: Avner did protest, but *lo ne'enah* — he was not heeded. His protest was ineffective, and therefore if he was punished, the reason must lie elsewhere.

### David's Eulogy

Both opinions derive their position from the same verse — David's eulogy over Avner: *hayakemot naval yamut Avner, yadecha lo asurot veraglecha lo linchoshtayim hugashu* — "Should Avner die the death of a degenerate? Your hands were not bound, your feet were not put in chains."

According to the view that Avner did *not* protest, David's words are not an expression of astonishment at injustice, but rather an indictment: your hands were not bound, your feet were not chained — no one was stopping you — *mai ta'ama lo machita?* — why then did you not protest? And therefore *kinfol lifnei bnei avla nafaltah* — you fell as one falls at the hands of the treacherous — meaning this is why it happened to you.

### Injustice Reading

According to the view that Avner *did* protest, the same words are read differently. David is *itmuhei mitamei* — genuinely astounded at the injustice. Your hands were free, your feet were unbound — you acted as you should have acted, you lived a righteous life — *mai ta'ama kinfol lifnei bnei avla nafaltah?* — how could it be that you suffered such a disgraceful death? The eulogy, on this reading, is an authentic lament over incomprehensible injustice.

### Delayed Kingship

If Avner did protest Saul, the *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama ye'anesh* — why was he then punished at all? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers: *sheshaha malchus beis David shtei shanim u'mechtza* — because Avner delayed the establishment of the house of David by two and a half years, during which he supported Mephibosheth rather than throwing his considerable influence behind David's kingship. Rashi and Tosafot discuss the precise chronology, noting that seven and a half years elapsed between Saul's death and David's accession over all Israel, and debating how the two and a half years of Mephibosheth's rule fit into that period. But whichever the exact reconstruction, the core point is clear: Avner supported the wrong claimant when he should have supported David.

## Dargash Definition

### Luck Bed

Having established that the king reclines on the *dargash* while the other mourners sit on the floor, the *gemara* now asks: *mai dargash?* — what exactly is a *dargash*? Ulla answers: *arsa degada* — a bed of luck. As Rashi explains, *gad* here relates to the concept found in Tanakh of setting a table for *gad*, an idol or symbol of fortune. The *dargash*, on this reading, was a decorative bed placed in the home for good fortune — similar in function to a symbolic ceremonial object — on which no one would normally sit. Its low, decorative character would make it appropriate for a mourner.

### Functionality Challenge

The *rabbanan* immediately challenge Ulla: *mi ika midei d'ad ha'idna lo oshvinei v'hashta moshvinei leh?* — is it sensible that something which has never been sat upon, existing purely as decoration, should now suddenly be appropriate to sit on? What is the significance of placing a mourner on an object that is not functionally a seat?

### Mourning Context

Rava deflects the challenge: *dilma midei d'havah achila ushtiya* — perhaps it is no different from the general inversion that the *seudas havra'ah* represents. Normally, the mourner feeds himself; now others feed him. The entire framework of mourning involves doing things differently, creating a *topsy-turvy* quality that reflects the disrupted world of *aveilus*. That something is normally not used as a seat is therefore not itself a sufficient reason to say it cannot serve as a mourner's seat.

### Bed Overturning

Rava then redirects the challenge more precisely. The real difficulty with Ulla's definition is posed by a *baraita*: *dargash eino tzarich lichfoso ela zokfo* — for a *dargash*, one does not need to overturn it as one overturns beds during mourning; instead, one stands it on its head, tilting it ninety degrees. If the *dargash* were simply a decorative non-functional bed, why would this distinction apply?

The general rule from the *baraita* is that the overturning of beds extends to all beds in the house: *hachofeh mitato lo mitato bilvad hu kofeh ela kol mittes sheyesh b'soch beiso kofeh*. Why would this decorative bed be exempt from standard overturning?

The *gemara* answers: this poses no difficulty. Just as a bed designated for storing *keilim* — utensils — rather than for sleeping is not subject to the overturning requirement (*im hayta myuchedes l'keilim eino tzarich lichfoso*), so too a decorative bed that is not made for sleeping would be similarly exempt. The *dargash* is therefore tilted rather than fully overturned: still an expression of *kefiyat hamita*, but in a modified form.

### Strap Release

Nevertheless, the *gemara* identifies a deeper difficulty. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *dargash matir karbitin v'hu nofel me'elav* — for a *dargash*, one simply releases the straps and it falls of its own accord. This implies that the *dargash* is a bed or chair whose surface is held up by straps, and that releasing those straps constitutes the required mourning practice in lieu of full overturning. But if the *dargash* were simply a decorative non-functional bed, *karbiti mi ika* — would such a bed even have straps in the first place? The structural feature that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel describes is inconsistent with a purely decorative object.

### Leather Bed

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different definition entirely. When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of one of the rabbis — Rav Tachlifa, who was frequently present in the marketplace of the tanners (*shuka d'gilda'ei*) and therefore had direct knowledge of such objects — that a *dargash* is an *arsa d'tzala*, a leather bed. Specifically, it is a bed frame onto which a leather hide is strapped as the sleeping surface. This explains the straps mentioned by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rashi explains why such a bed would be tilted rather than fully overturned: its frame has no posts extending above the height of the leather surface, so if it were flipped upside down, the leather would be pressed against the ground and ruined. One therefore tilts it onto its side rather than inverting it completely.

### Strap Placement

Rabbi Yirmiyah, citing Rabbi Yochanan, adds a further structural distinction: *dargash sirugo mitocho* — the straps of a *dargash* pass through holes located within the interior of the frame or legs, with the straps threaded through and knotted from the inside. A regular *mitah*, by contrast, was strung with ropes that went over the top of the frame — *siruga me'al gabah* — crisscrossing on the exterior surface to form the lattice on which a mattress or hay would be placed.

### Wood Finishing

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *mishnah* concerning the point at which wooden utensils become susceptible to *tumah*. It teaches that a bed or *arisa* becomes complete — *gmar melacha* — when it is smoothed with fish skin (the ancient equivalent of sandpaper). But if a regular *mitah* has its ropes wrapped around the exterior of the frame, the frame itself would be entirely hidden and there would be no reason to polish it to a smooth finish. The visible wood would never be exposed.

### Both Have Holes

The *gemara* resolves the challenge simply: both the *dargash* and the regular *mitah* have their straps or ropes threaded through interior holes. The earlier distinction — that one goes through internal holes and the other over the exterior surface — cannot be the point of differentiation between them.

### Rings vs Holes

The actual structural distinction, the *gemara* concludes, lies elsewhere. In a regular *mitah*, the ropes go *in and out* through simple holes bored in the frame — one might imagine a pattern not unlike the eyelets in a shoe, with a rope passing back and forth through holes to create the crisscross surface. In a *dargash*, by contrast, the straps are attached to *afkasa* — external rings fixed to the frame — through which the leather straps are tied. The *dargash* uses rings; the regular bed uses holes. This accounts for why Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel can describe the release of straps as the operative mourning action: the rings holding the leather surface can be untied, causing it to fall.

### Halacha Ruling

Rav Yaakov, in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, rules: the *halacha* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — for a *dargash*, one simply releases the straps rather than overturning the bed.

### High Posts

Rav Yitzchak bar Ami adds a related ruling: if a regular bed has tall posts (*naklitei*) projecting upward from the headboard or footboard — the type used to support a canopy or draped sheet — then fully overturning the bed would still leave it elevated far off the ground, defeating the purpose of *kefiyat hamita*. In such a case, one does as with the *dargash*: one tilts it ninety degrees onto its side, standing it on its head, and that is sufficient.

## Royal Prerogatives

### War & Roads

The *gemara* moves to the next *mishnah*, which outlines further royal prerogatives. The king may take the nation out for an *optional war* — *milchemes hareshut*, a war of expansion beyond the conquest of the Land — but only with the authorization of the *beit din* of 71. A war of self-defense or the original conquest of Eretz Yisrael does not require this; it is specifically the discretionary expansion of territory that demands the consent of the *sanhedrin* as representative of the people.

The king may also break through fences and property in order to make a path, and no one may prevent him — *v'ein mamchin b'yado*. Rashi reads this not as the king passing through on a royal journey, but specifically as securing access to his own fields. The *derech hamelech* — the king's road — has no fixed minimum width; it may be as wide as necessity requires.

Additionally, when the people take spoils in war, the king receives the first choice of the plunder — *notel chelek b'rosh*. Rashi adds that the king also receives the finest portion and takes half of the total spoils.

### Seventy-One

The *gemara* notes that the requirement of 71 for optional war was already taught in the very first *mishnah* of this *masechet*. It answers: since this chapter is devoted to a comprehensive treatment of the laws of *kohen gadol* and *melech*, the laws of the king are being gathered and restated in full, including those already mentioned elsewhere. The repetition is purposeful — it belongs to the complete picture of royal law being assembled here.

## Royal Powers Debate

### Powers Permitted

The *gemara* now enters one of its most famous debates regarding the nature of monarchy. Rav Yehuda, in the name of Shmuel, states: *kol ha'amur b'parshas melech melech mutar bo* — everything enumerated in the *parshas hamelech* in 1 Samuel 8 — Shmuel's warning to the people about what a king will do to them — those are actual, legitimate royal powers. When Shmuel told the people that the king would conscript their sons, seize their daughters, appropriate their fields, and exact tithes of their produce, this was not a warning of abuse but a description of rights genuinely belonging to the king.

### Fear, Not Rights

Rav disagrees fundamentally: *lo ne'emra parsha zu ela l'ayem aleihem* — this entire passage was stated only to instill fear, not to grant the king actual authority to do these things. As Rashi explains Rav's position: the purpose was that *eimas melachim* — the awe of kingship — should rest upon the people, but the king does not in fact possess those powers as legal entitlements. If one were to call his bluff, there would be no *halachic* basis for the king to enforce them.

A careful reading of the passage in Shmuel itself makes it difficult to sustain Rav's interpretation in its straightforward sense: after Shmuel delivers the entire warning, the text records explicitly that the people refused to listen — *vayema'anu ha'am lishmo'a b'kol Shmuel* — and insisted they wanted a king anyway. The pedagogical force of the passage seems to have been aimed at changing their minds, not at establishing the *eimas* of an already-existing monarchy. Nevertheless, Rashi follows Rav's formulation: the passage was stated so that when they would eventually have a king, the proper fear of royal authority would be in place.

### R. Yose & R. Yehuda

The *gemara* identifies this as a *tannaitic* dispute. Rabbi Yose holds that everything stated in *parshas hamelech* the king is actually permitted to do — corresponding to the position of Shmuel. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the *parsha* was stated only in order to instill fear — *som tasim alecha melech shetehei eimaso alecha*, "you shall set upon yourself a king that his fear shall be upon you" — and this is where Rashi locates the textual basis for that reading. On Rabbi Yehuda's view, the purpose of *parshas hamelech* is to ensure that the institution of kingship carries real psychological weight and authority, not to enumerate actual legal powers.

It should be noted that it is this same Shmuel who established the principle of *dina d'malchuta dina* — the law of the [ruling] kingdom is the law — and some have sought to connect that principle to Shmuel's position here recognizing the breadth of royal authority.

### Three Commandments

The *gemara* cites an additional ruling associated with Rabbi Yehuda. He teaches: *sheloshah mitzvos nitztavu Yisrael bichnisatan la'aretz* — three commandments were given to Israel upon their entry into the Land, and these are collective national obligations tied to the establishment of sovereignty and the ordering of national life. The first is *leha'amid lahem melech* — to establish a king. The second is *ulimchot zaro shel Amalek* — to eradicate the seed of Amalek. The third is *velivnot lahem beit habechirah* — to build the Temple. These three form a progression: establish the governing authority, eliminate the archetypal enemy, and erect the center of divine worship.

### Response to Complaint

Rabbi Nehorai takes a sharply different position. He maintains that *parshas hamelech* was stated only *keneged taro'umtan* — in response to their complaint — as indicated by the verse *ve'amarta asimah alai melech*, "and you will say, let me set a king upon myself." For Rabbi Nehorai, appointing a king is not a *mitzvah* at all; it is a concession to popular demand. Rashi makes explicit that on this view even *som tasim alecha melech* in the Torah is not a commandment but rather a description of what will happen when the people demand it. This tension — is *malchus* an ideal or merely a permitted accommodation? — runs through Shoftim, Samuel, and the entire *sugya*.

### Elders vs. People

A *baraita* cites Rabbi Eliezer, who offers a nuanced middle position. The request for a king was not monolithic: *zkeinim shebador kahogen sha'alu* — the elders of the generation asked properly. Their request — *tnah lanu melech lishofteinu*, "give us a king to judge us" — was rooted in the desire for justice, strong central authority, and proper governance. It was the *amei ha'aretz*, the common people, who *kilkelu* — who corrupted the request — as expressed in the verse: *vehayinu gam anachnu kechol hagoyim ushfatanu malkeinu veyatza lefaneinu v'nilcham milchamoseinu* — "and we shall be like all the nations, and our king will judge us and go out before us and fight our wars."

Rashi identifies two elements in the people's flawed motivation. The phrase *kechol hagoyim* reflects a desire for assimilation, for Israel to be a nation like any other rather than a people defined by its unique relationship with God. And *veyatza lefaneinu* — "he will go out before us" — reflects an improper emphasis on military conquest and expansion. The elders sought justice and governance; the people wanted military glory and national normalcy. Both are in the same verse, but they represent fundamentally different visions of what a king is for.

## Order of Commandments

### Sequence Logic

Rabbi Yose (here teaching the same tradition as Rabbi Yehuda regarding the three commandments) notes that while the three obligations are clear — establish a king, destroy Amalek, build the Temple — their *sequence* requires derivation. He states: *eini yode'a eizeh mehem techila* — I do not know which comes first. The question is resolved through careful attention to specific biblical texts.

### King First

The verse states: *ki yad al kes Ya milchama l'Hashem b'Amalek* — "for a hand is upon the throne of God; a war of God against Amalek." The word *kisei* — throne — signifies a king, as demonstrated by the verse describing Solomon: *vayeshev Shlomo al kisei Hashem lemelech* — Solomon sat upon the throne of God as king. Therefore, before the war against Amalek can be waged, there must be a throne — and a throne means there must first be a king. The sequence of the first two commandments is thus established: first appoint a king, then destroy Amalek.

### Historical Order

The remaining question is whether building the Temple should precede the destruction of Amalek. This is resolved from the verse: *v'heniach lachem mikol oyveichem* — "and He will give you rest from all your enemies" — only after which comes the obligation to build the *beit habechirah*. Peace from enemies must precede the Temple's construction.

The historical record confirms this sequence exactly. Saul was appointed king first. God then commanded Saul to destroy Amalek. Only after David had subdued Israel's enemies — as recorded in the verse *vayehi ki yashav hamelech David b'veiso v'Hashem hiniach lo misaviv*, "and it came to pass that King David sat in his house and God had given him rest from all his enemies round about" — did the initiative to build the Temple arise, ultimately fulfilled by Solomon. The *gemara* concludes by citing David's words to the prophet Nathan, in which he expresses his discomfort at dwelling in a house of cedars while the Ark rests in a temporary shelter — the opening of the sequence that would culminate in Solomon's construction of the *beit habechirah*.
