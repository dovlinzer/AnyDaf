# Bava Kamma 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Chisda & Rami bar Chama

### Gozel u-Ma'akhil Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו, וְהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. וְאִם הָיָה דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it to **his children, or** who **left** a stolen item **to them** and then died, the children **are exempt from paying** the victim of the robbery after their father's death. But **if** the stolen item **was something that serves** as a legal **guarantee** of a loan, the heirs **are obligated to pay.**

The daf opens at the bottom of 111b with the phrase *amar mar*, picking up from the discussion of the *mishna* concerning *ha-gozel u-ma'akhil et banav* — one who steals an object and feeds it to his children, or leaves the stolen article for his children to inherit. The *mishna* rules that the children are *patur* — exempt from payment — unless the matter involves *achrayut nechasim*, in which case they are *chayav*. The precise meaning of *achrayut nechasim* in this context is left somewhat open, and the entire ensuing discussion turns on two foundational positions that must be kept clearly in view throughout.

### Rav Chisda: After Ye'ush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גָּזַל וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים, וּבָא אַחֵר וַאֲכָלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ – רָצָה מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה, רָצָה מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל כַּמָּה דְּלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּמָרֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥisda says:** If one **robbed** another, **and the owners** of the stolen item **have not** yet **despaired** of retrieving it, **and another** person **came,** took it **from** the robber **and ate it,** if the owner **wishes he may collect from this one,** i.e., the first robber, and if **he wishes he may collect from that one,** i.e., the second robber. **What is the reason** that he may collect from whomever he chooses? It is because **as long as the owners did not despair** of retrieving it, **it remains the possession of its owner,** so that when the second robber stole it from the first, he was in fact stealing from the original owner. Nevertheless, since the first robber was already obligated to return the item, his obligation remains in force and the owner may demand payment from him if he wishes.

The first position is that of Rav Chisda. According to Rav Chisda, if a second party comes and consumes a stolen object before *ye'ush* — before the original owner has despaired of recovering it — that second party is obligated to pay. The principle is stated as follows: *gezel ve-lo nityaeishu ha-be'alim u-va acher ve-akhal* — if an object was stolen and the owner had not yet despaired, and another person came and ate it — then *ratza mi-ze gove ve-ratza mi-ze gove*: the owner may collect from either party. This means that for Rav Chisda, the case in the *mishna* where children who are fed stolen food are *patur* must necessarily be speaking of a situation *after ye'ush*, not before. Before *ye'ush*, the owner retains a claim against whoever consumed the object; only after *ye'ush* does the children's exemption make sense on his account.

### Rami bar Chama Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: רְשׁוּת יוֹרֵשׁ כִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated that **if one left** a stolen item **to** his children as an inheritance, the children **are exempt from paying** the owner. **Rami bar Ḥama said: That is to say** that **the domain of an heir is comparable to the domain of a purchaser.** Just as an item that is purchased leaves the domain of the seller, an item that is inherited leaves the domain of the deceased and is considered the property of the heir. Since the owner has despaired of retrieving the item and the item has changed domains, it is entirely the property of the new owner, and he is exempt from payment.

The second position is that of Rami bar Chama, who invokes the principle *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach dami* — that a heir's acquisition of property is treated like a purchaser's acquisition, constituting a *shinui reshut*, a change of domain. This principle is relevant to the second clause of the *mishna*, which speaks of *hiniach lifneihem* — the father leaving the stolen article in front of the children — with the *mishna* ruling that the children are *patur*. The straightforward reading of this clause is that the *gezeila* is still *be-ein*, still physically present and extant, and yet the children need not return it. The question is: on what grounds? Rami bar Chama answers that when combined with *ye'ush* — the owner's despair — the *shinui reshut* inherent in the children's inheritance (*reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*) suffices to exempt them.

### Reconciling Positions

Putting the two positions together: the exemption in the first part of the *mishna*, where the children ate the stolen food, works according to Rav Chisda only *after ye'ush*. The exemption in the second part, where the object is left before them and is still extant, is explained by Rami bar Chama as a case of *ye'ush* combined with *shinui reshut*. If one does not accept either of these explanations — neither that we are speaking after *ye'ush* nor that *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* — then one would have to say that the reason the children are *patur* in the second case is simply that the *gezeila* was in fact consumed, and the only variable between the two cases of the *mishna* is whether the children consumed it during the father's lifetime or after his death.

## Braita of Rabbi Oshaya

### Kayemet vs. Consumed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו, וְהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב חִסְדָּא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this opinion based on what **we learned** in the mishna: In the case of **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it **to his children, or** one who **left** a stolen item **to them** as an inheritance, the children **are exempt from paying** the victim of the robbery after their father's death. This appears to be **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda,** who holds that one who steals from a thief is obligated to pay the owner. The Gemara answers: **Rav Ḥisda** could have **said to you: When that** mishna **is taught,** it is addressing a case where it is **after** the owners had already **despaired** of retrieving the item, whereas Rav Ḥisda was referring to a case where the owners had not yet despaired.

The *gemara* now turns to the *braita* of Rabbi Oshaya and tests both positions against it. The *braita* states: when the *gezeila* is no longer present — *ein ha-gezeila kayemet* — the children are *patur*. The converse is implied: when the stolen object is still *kayemet*, they are obligated to return it.

### Testing Rav Chisda

This immediately raises the question: does the *braita* constitute a refutation of Rav Chisda? If the exemption when the object has been consumed applies even before *ye'ush*, that would cut against Rav Chisda's position that before *ye'ush* the second party who consumes the object is liable.

### Rav Chisda's Defense

Rav Chisda deflects the challenge: *ki tanya hi le-achar ye'ush* — this *braita* is speaking only of a case after *ye'ush*. Just as the *mishna's* exemption for the children who ate the stolen food is, in Rav Chisda's reading, set after *ye'ush*, so too the *braita* speaks in that same post-*ye'ush* frame.

### Testing Rami bar Chama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם. נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא? אָמַר לְךָ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **The Master said** in the *baraita* that if the **stolen item is extant,** the heirs **are obligated to pay. Let us say that** this *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rami bar Ḥama,** since according to his understanding, the heirs should be exempt from payment because the stolen item is considered to have changed ownership when they inherited it. The Gemara answers: **Rami bar Ḥama** could have **said to you** that **when that** *baraita* **is taught,**

The *gemara* then presses the other side. The *braita* states that when the *gezeila* is still present — *gezeila kayemet* — the children are *chayavim le-shalem*, obligated to pay. But this seems to contradict Rami bar Chama, who maintained that *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* — the children's inheritance constitutes a *shinui reshut* — and the *mishna* seemed to indicate that even when the object is *be-ein*, the children are *patur*. If the *braita* says that when it is *kayemet* they must pay, does this not undermine the principle of *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*?

### Rami bar Chama's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** it is referring to a case where it is **before** the owners have **despaired** of retrieving their property. Consequently, the heirs have not acquired the stolen property, and it must be returned.

Rami bar Chama responds in kind: *ki tanya hi lifnei ye'ush* — this *braita* is speaking of a case before *ye'ush*. Before *ye'ush*, of course *reshut yoresh* does not function as *reshut loke'ach*, and the children would have to return the object. It is only after *ye'ush* that *ye'ush* combined with *shinui reshut* exempts them. So both positions survive the challenge from the *braita* by assigning it to the opposite side of the *ye'ush* divide.

## Rava on Interest Money

### Interest Money Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה מַתְנֵי לְהָא דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אַהָא: הִנִּיחַ לָהֶן אֲבִיהֶן מָעוֹת שֶׁל רִבִּית, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיּוֹדְעִין שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל רִבִּית – אֵין חַיָּיבִין לְהַחְזִיר. אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: רְשׁוּת יוֹרֵשׁ כִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava teaches that** statement **of Rami bar Ḥama** cited above **with regard to this** *baraita*: If **their father left them money** that he obtained by taking **interest,** which is prohibited, **even if they are aware that** the money **is** from **interest, they are not obligated to return** the money to the debtor who paid it. **Rami bar Ḥama said: That is to say** that **the domain of an heir is comparable to the domain of a purchaser,** and because the money has changed domains, the heirs have acquired it.

The *gemara* records that Rav Adda bar Ahava taught the principle of Rami bar Chama — *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* — not in connection with the stolen object cases of the *mishna*, but rather in connection with a *braita* about interest money: *heniach lahem avihem ma'ot shel ribit* — the father left his children money that he had collected through charging interest. Even though the children are aware that this is *ribit* money, *ein chayavim lehachzir* — they are not obligated to return it. From this, Rami bar Chama inferred *zot omeret reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach dami* — the *braita's* exemption can only be explained if the heir's domain is treated like a purchaser's, constituting a genuine change of possession.

The logic being: if the money were still considered to be in the continuity of the father's estate, one would expect an obligation to restore it. The fact that the children are exempt must mean that once it passes to the heirs, it is in a genuinely new domain — and that is the principle of *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ רְשׁוּת יוֹרֵשׁ לָאו כִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ דָּמֵי, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אַל תִּקַּח מֵאִתּוֹ נֶשֶׁךְ וְתַרְבִּית״ – אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵחֵי בַּהֲדָךְ; לְדִידֵיהּ קָא מַזְהַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, לִבְרֵיהּ לָא מַזְהַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually, I will say to you** that **the domain of an heir is not comparable to the domain of a purchaser. And** the reason they do not have to return the money is because **it is different here, as the verse states: "Take no interest of him or increase,** but fear your God, that your brother may live with you" (Leviticus 25:36), which teaches that you must **return** the interest **to him so that he may live with you.** Since the interest is returned for this reason and not because it is considered stolen goods, it is apparent that **the Merciful One is cautioning him,** the lender, to return it, but **the Merciful One is not cautioning his son** to return it.

Rava challenges this inference. *Le-olam eima lecha reshut yoresh lav ke-reshut loke'ach dami* — one can actually maintain that the heir's domain is not like a purchaser's, that there is no fundamental *shinui reshut* when property descends to children. The money remains, in a sense, a continuation of the father's estate. And yet the children are still exempt from returning the *ribit* money — not because of *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*, but for an entirely different reason.

### Vchi Achicha Imach

Rava points to the *pasuk* that underlies the entire obligation to return *ribit*. The verse says *al tikach me'ito neshech ve-tarbit* — do not take from him interest — and concludes *ve-chai achicha imach*, that your brother should live together with you. From this phrase, the *gemara* derives *ahadreih leih ki heichi de-nichi bahadach* — return it to him so that the two of you may live well together. The obligation to return *ribit* is thus not grounded in the straightforward principle that the money belongs to someone else the way a stolen object does. The person who was charged interest paid it willingly; it is not simply theft. Rather, the Torah issues a special directive aimed at restoring a proper relationship between the lender and borrower.

### Personal Obligation Only

The distinction carries decisive consequences. *Le-didei kamzahar lei rachmana* — it is the individual who charged interest whom the Torah commands to make this restoration. *Livrei lo kamzahar lei rachmana* — the Torah's command does not extend to his son. The obligation to return *ribit* is thus a *personal* obligation incumbent on the one who actually charged it, not a property-based claim against the estate. It follows that the children's exemption has nothing to do with whether *reshut yoresh* is or is not like *reshut loke'ach*. Even if the money is still considered to be in the same domain, the obligation to return it simply does not transfer to the heirs, because the Torah addressed only the one who charged *ribit* and not his children.

## Application to Mishna/Braita

### Braita Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַבָּרַיְיתָא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַמַּתְנִיתִין. מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַמַּתְנִיתִין – אֲבָל אַבָּרַיְיתָא, רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא כְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **The one who teaches** Rami bar Ḥama's statement **with regard to the *baraita*,** i.e., Rav Adda bar Ahava, **all the more so** would apply it **to the mishna,** since there is no other explanation for why the heirs are exempt from payment. Conversely, according to **the one who teaches** Rami bar Ḥama's statement **with regard to the mishna,** it is limited to that case. **But with regard to the *baraita*,** he holds that **Rami bar Ḥama teaches it as** explained by **Rava,** that the heirs are not exempt from payment because the domain of an heir is comparable to the domain of a purchaser, but rather because heirs are never required to return interest.

Having established these two understandings of Rami bar Chama's teaching — whether it was said in connection with the *braita* about *ribit* or with the *mishna* about the stolen object — the *gemara* now analyzes the implications of each.

*Man de-masni lah a-baraita kol sheken a-matnitin*: the one who applies the principle of *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* to the *braita* about *ribit* would certainly apply it to the *mishna* as well. The reasoning, as Rashi explains, is that the *mishna's* plain meaning — that even when the *gezeila* is *be-ein* the children are *patur* — can only be explained by *ye'ush* combined with *shinui reshut*, which is precisely *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*. The *mishna* therefore provides the stronger case for the principle. If you invoke *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* in the *braita* — where there is an alternative explanation available — you would certainly apply it to the *mishna*, which demands it.

### Mishna Application

The converse is not symmetric. *Aval a-baraita Rami bar Chama ke-Rava masni lah* — but when it comes to the *braita* about *ribit*, one could well follow Rava's reasoning and say that the exemption for the children has nothing to do with *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*. The *ribit* money is fundamentally the lender's own property — it was freely paid to him, even if that transaction was forbidden. The reason the children need not return it is simply that the Torah's directive to restore the relationship is a personal obligation on the one who charged *ribit*, and it does not pass to his heirs. This has no bearing on the question of *shinui reshut* at all. So if Rami bar Chama's principle was stated only with respect to the *mishna*, the *braita* about *ribit* can be explained on entirely different grounds.

## Mishna: Two Cases (II)

### Case 1: Fed Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. הִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם – גְּדוֹלִים, חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם; קְטַנִּים, פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. וְאִם אָמְרוּ גְּדוֹלִים: אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין חֶשְׁבּוֹנוֹת שֶׁחִשֵּׁב אָבִינוּ עִמָּךְ – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 10:21): With regard to **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it to **his children,** the children **are exempt from paying** the owner. In a case where **he left** the stolen items **to them** as an inheritance, if the heirs are **adults they are obligated to pay,** and if they are **minors they are exempt from paying. If the adult** heirs **said: We do not know** what **calculations our father made with you** and whether he paid you for the stolen goods, **they are exempt.**

The *gemara* cites a *braita* that tracks the structure of our *mishna*. *Ha-gozel u-ma'akhil et banav peturin li-shalem* — one who steals and feeds it to the children, the children are exempt from payment. This mirrors the first clause of the *mishna* straightforwardly.

### Case 2: Left Before Them

*Hiniach lifneihem* — if he left it in front of them — and here the plain sense, as throughout this discussion, is that the *gezeila* is *be-ein*, the object still exists — *gedolim chayavim li-shalem, ketanim peturim li-shalem*: adult children are obligated to pay, minor children are exempt. The reasons for each of these rulings deserve examination. Adult children must return the object because, whether we are speaking before *ye'ush* or after *ye'ush* under a view that denies *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach*, there is always a framework under which a *be-ein* stolen object must be returned. In neither scenario can the adult heirs retain it.

## Gezeilah Be'ein Rule (II)

### Adult Ignorance Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִי: ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין״ – פְּטוּרִים?! אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר, גְּדוֹלִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ: יוֹדְעִים אָנוּ חֶשְׁבּוֹנוֹת שֶׁחִשֵּׁב אָבִינוּ עִמָּךְ, וְלָא פָּשׁ לָךְ גַּבֵּיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Because they say: We do not know,** they are **exempt?** Since it is clear that they are in possession of stolen property, how can they be exempt due to the uncertain claim that perhaps their father repaid the owner after the theft? **Rava said** that the heirs are certain about their claim and that the *baraita* should be formulated differently, and **this is** what the *baraita* **is saying:** In the case of **adult** heirs **who said** to the claimant: **We know the calculations our father made with you and there is nothing of yours left with him,** as he paid his debt to you, they **are exempt.**

The case of the minor children is different — they cannot be sued because requiring them to answer testimony in court is a violation of the principle of *mekabelin eidut she-lo bifnei ba'al din*: one cannot proceed with a case when the opposing party is not capable of defending themselves. A minor does not have full legal standing to face accusers in court, so the proceeding must wait until they reach majority. The stolen object will be retrieved, but not until the children can respond.

The *braita* then introduces a wrinkle: *ve-im amru gedolim einanu yod'in cheshbonot she-chishav avinu imach* — if the adult children declare that they do not know the arrangement their father made with the claimant, perhaps the father paid for the object or was given permission to keep it — in that case, *peturim*, they are exempt. The *gemara* presses: does simply saying "we don't know" actually exempt them? You can see plainly that the object is in their home; the claimant knows what his property looks like. How does a plea of ignorance relieve them of the obligation to return what appears to be a stolen item?

## Rava's Reinterpretation and Burden of Proof

Rava reinterprets the clause entirely. *Hachi ka-amar*: what the *braita* is actually saying is this: *gedolim she-amru yod'in anu cheshbonot she-chishav avinu imach ve-lo pashat lach gabai ve-lo midei peturim* — if the adult children affirmatively state that they *do* know the arrangement, and specifically that their father paid the claimant and nothing further is owed, in that case they are exempt. The reason is *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-rayah* — possession is presumptive and the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract property from another's possession. Even if the claimant can demonstrate the object was once his, it is now in the children's possession, and if the children assert their father paid for it, they are believed. The original reading — where "we don't know" creates an exemption — is rejected; it is only an affirmative claim of knowledge of the father's arrangement that suffices.

## Alternative Braita

### Consumption Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל בָּנָיו – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. הִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם וַאֲכָלוּם – בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים בֵּין קְטַנִּים, חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it to **his children,** the children **are exempt from paying.** If **he left** stolen items **to them** as an inheritance **and they consumed them, whether they are adults or minors, they are obligated** to pay the owner.

The *gemara* cites a further *braita*: *tanya idach*, there is another teaching. *Ha-gozel u-ma'akhil banav peturin li-shalem* — one who steals and feeds the children, they are exempt — consistent with what we have seen. *Hiniach lifneihem ve-achalum, bein gedolim bein ketanim chayavim* — if the father left the *gezeila* before the children and they consumed it, whether adults or minors, they are obligated.

### Minor Liability Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטַנִּים מִי מִיחַיְּיבִי?! לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא דְּאַזֵּיק אַזּוֹקֵי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶם וַעֲדַיִין לֹא אֲכָלוּם – בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים בֵּין קְטַנִּים, חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Are **minors obligated** to pay? **Let** it **be only** like a case **where one caused damage,** and a minor who causes damage is exempt. **Rav Pappa said** that the *baraita* should be formulated differently, and **this is** what the *baraita* **is saying:** If **he left** the stolen items **to them** as an inheritance **and they did not yet consume them, whether they are adults or minors, they are obligated** to return the stolen items, since the items are still extant.

This is a striking formulation. *Ketanim mi-mechayvei?* — how can minors be obligated? In the previous discussion the object was still *be-ein* and the question was whether one can accept testimony against a minor. But here the object has been consumed, so this is not about retrieval. The children would now be obligated to pay from their own resources. But *velo yehei ela de-azik azuki* — even if a minor damages property outright, they incur no monetary liability. There is no mechanism for obligating a minor to pay damages. How then can this *braita* impose liability on minors who consumed a stolen object?

Rav Papa resolves the difficulty by correcting the text: *hachi ka-amar, hiniach lifneihem ve-adayin lo achlum* — the word *achalum*, "they ate it," is a misreading. The *braita* is actually saying they have not yet eaten it, the *gezeila* is still around. *Bein gedolim bein ketanim chayavim* then means that both adults and minors are obligated to return it. This *braita* thus differs from the previous one, which exempted minors even when the object was *be-ein*; this *braita* obligates even minors. The disagreement between the two *braitot* reflects the underlying debate — parallel to the dispute between Sumchos and the *rabbanan* — about whether court proceedings may be conducted against a minor who holds property that belongs to another.

## Borrowed Cow Case

### Cow Inheritance Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִנִּיחַ לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם פָּרָה שְׁאוּלָה – מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בָּהּ כׇּל יְמֵי שְׁאֵלָתָהּ. מֵתָה – אֵין חַיָּיבִין בְּאוֹנְסֶיהָ. כִּסְבוּרִים שֶׁל אֲבִיהֶם הִיא, וּטְבָחוּהָ וַאֲכָלוּהָ – מְשַׁלְּמִין דְּמֵי בָשָׂר בְּזוֹל. הִנִּיחַ לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** In the case of children whose **father** died and **left them a cow** that he had **borrowed, they may use it for the entire duration of its loan.** Nevertheless, if it **died, they are not liable** to pay if its death was caused by **an unavoidable accident,** despite the fact that a borrower is liable for damage caused by an unavoidable accident. This is because liability for accidental damage pertains only to the actual borrower, i.e., the deceased father. If the children **thought that it** actually **belonged to their father and they slaughtered it and ate it, they** are liable to **pay** the owner **the value of inexpensive meat.** Rava adds: If **their father left them guaranteed property,** i.e., land, **they are liable to pay.**

The *gemara* now introduces a new case. *Hiniach lahem avihem para she'ula* — their father left them a borrowed cow. The father had borrowed the cow for a fixed period, and upon his death the children inherited his rights in the arrangement — meaning they may use the cow for the duration of the original loan period. One way of conceptualizing *she'ila* is as a *matana le-guf le-feirot li-zman* — a gift of the use of an object for a limited time, analogous to how one can gift property itself or its usufruct for a specified term. The children step into the borrower's shoes and may continue to use the cow during that time.

### Death by Accident

*Meita, ein chayavim be-onsa* — if the cow dies of natural causes, not through any fault of the children, they are not obligated to pay. This requires explanation. A *sho'el* — a borrower — is normally obligated even in cases of *onsin*, accidents beyond one's control; the borrower assumes maximum liability. Why then are the children exempt? The *braita* is operating on the premise that the obligation to pay for *onsin* is a personal obligation assumed by the individual who undertook the *she'ila* — namely, the father. The property rights in the cow — the right to use it — can descend to the children as an inheritance. But the personal obligation of the *sho'el* does not transfer to the heirs. The father took on this obligation; it is his alone.

### Slaughter and Consumption

*Kesvurim shel avihem hi* — if the children did not realize the cow was borrowed and assumed it belonged to their father, and they slaughtered and ate it, *meshalmin demei basar be-zol*: they pay only the discounted value of the meat, approximately two-thirds of the cow's market value, as Rashi specifies.

The reasoning here is layered. We have already established that the children bear no obligation as *sho'el* — they did not personally assume that liability. One might then argue they should pay as *mazik* — as ones who damaged another's property — since they slaughtered someone else's cow. But *adam mu'ad le-olam* applies only when one's relationship to the property is understood from the outset as an encounter with another's object; it is only in that context that even accidental damage generates liability. Here, the children had every reason to believe the cow was their own father's property, located in their home when he died. Their relationship to it was fundamentally one of assumed ownership, not of engagement with another's property. They are therefore not *mazik* in the full sense. The only claim against them is the benefit they received — the value of the meat they consumed — and that benefit is assessed at two-thirds of market value.

### Achrayut Nechasim Relevance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא, וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Some teach this** final statement of Rava **with regard to the first clause,** which states that the heirs are exempt from payment for accidental damage. According to this interpretation, if the father left them land, they are obligated to pay for the cow if it dies due to unavoidable accident. **And some teach it with regard to the latter clause,** which states that heirs who slaughter and consume a cow must pay the owner the value of inexpensive meat. According to this interpretation, if the father left them land, they must pay for the full value of the cow.

There is a final clause: *heniach lahem avihem achrayut nechasim chayavim le-shalem* — if the father left them real property with liens on it, they are obligated to pay. The *gemara* debates which of the two preceding cases this clause refers to, noting that some teach it against the first case — the cow dying of natural causes — and some teach it against the second case — the slaughter and consumption.

## Rav Papa on Obligation Timing

### Obligation at Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָיְתָה פָּרָה גְּנוּבָה לוֹ, וּטְבָחָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת – חַיָּיב; שֶׁכְּבָר חַיָּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה, קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּבֹא לִידֵי אִיסּוּר שַׁבָּת. הָיְתָה פָּרָה שְׁאוּלָה לוֹ, וּטְבָחָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת – פָּטוּר; שֶׁאִיסּוּר שַׁבָּת וְאִיסּוּר גְּנֵיבָה בָּאִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Pappa says:** If **he had a stolen cow** in his possession, **and he slaughtered it on Shabbat,** he is **liable** to pay **because he was already rendered liable** for the **theft before he came to** transgress the **Shabbat prohibition** of slaughtering an animal on Shabbat. But if **he had a borrowed cow** in his possession **and he slaughtered it on Shabbat,** he is **exempt** from payment, **as** the transgression of **the Shabbat prohibition** of slaughtering an animal on Shabbat **and the prohibition against theft** occur **as one,** as the act of slaughter is tantamount to the theft of the animal. This indicates that according to Rav Pappa, a borrower's liability to pay for accidental damage is initiated only when the damage is inflicted. Accordingly, in the case discussed by Rava, since the damage was not inflicted during the father's lifetime, the deceased's property was never liened to the cow's owner, and consequently the heirs are not obligated to pay for any accidental damage.

To understand these positions, the *gemara* introduces a teaching of Rav Papa that bears directly on the question. *De-amar Rav Papa*: Rav Papa's view is that the obligation of a *sho'el* — a borrower — arises at the moment the damage occurs, not from the moment the borrowing began.

### Borrowed Before Shabbat

The *gemara* illustrates with the following case: one who stole a cow before *Shabbat* and slaughtered it on *Shabbat* — *chayav*, obligated to pay four or five, the penalty for *geneiva* with *tevicha*. Why? *She-kevar nischayev be-geneiva kodem she-ba li-yedei issur Shabbat* — because the principal obligation and the *kefel* were incurred at the moment of theft on Thursday, before any *chillul Shabbat* took place. The additional payment of four or five is a *kenas* — a rabbinic fine — and a *kenas* incurred prior to a capital offense is not absorbed by the principle of *kim lei be-drabba minei*.

However, if the person had *borrowed* the cow on Thursday and slaughtered it on *Shabbat*, he is *patur* — exempt — because *issur Shabbat ve-issur geneiva ba'im ke-echad*: the prohibition of *Shabbat* and whatever theft-equivalent obligation arises from destroying the borrowed object come simultaneously. There is no prior moment at which a monetary obligation was created and then survives the *chillul Shabbat*. The conclusion is that Rav Papa's position is that the *sho'el's* obligation arises at the moment of the damaging event, not retroactively from the moment the loan was struck.

This also means the borrower owes nothing as *sho'el* for what happened on *Shabbat* — and one might ask whether he owes as *mazik*. The answer is no, because that monetary obligation also arises at the same moment as the *chillul Shabbat*, and is absorbed by *kim lei be-drabba minei*.

### Obligation at Borrowing

The contrasting view holds that when the father borrowed the cow, the owner of the cow immediately acquired a lien on the father's property — because the obligation to return the cow (or its value) attaches from the moment of borrowing. On this view, the moment the father took the cow on loan, the cow's owner became a creditor of the father, with all the normal implications for *achrayut nechasim*. The cow subsequently dying — even during the children's custodianship — simply triggers the collection of an obligation that came into existence when the father was alive and against which his estate was already encumbered.

### Achrayut Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַסֵּיפָא, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא. מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא – אֲבָל אַרֵישָׁא לָא, וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who teaches it with regard to the first clause** holds it to be true **all the more so with regard to the latter clause,** where the heirs actually consumed the meat themselves. **And,** accordingly, this understanding **differs** with the forthcoming opinion **of Rav Pappa.** By contrast, **the one who teaches it with regard to the latter clause** holds it to be true in that clause exclusively, **but with regard to the first clause,** it is **not** true. **And this is** consistent with the forthcoming opinion of **Rav Pappa.**

This debate determines how to read the *achrayut nechasim* clause in our *braita*. If one teaches it against the *first* case — where the cow died of natural causes — one necessarily disagrees with Rav Papa. On Rav Papa's view, the obligation of the *sho'el* did not exist until the cow died, at which point it was already in the hands of the children and the father's estate bore no prior lien. There is therefore nothing to collect from real estate. But if one holds that the obligation began at the moment of borrowing — while the father was alive — then the estate was already encumbered, and the cow's owner may collect from the real property even though the children themselves were not personally obligated as *sho'el*.

If, on the other hand, one teaches the *achrayut nechasim* clause against only the *second* case — the slaughter and consumption — then Rav Papa's position is preserved. In the case where the children slaughtered the cow thinking it was their father's, the question of *achrayut* is distinct: it is not about *sho'el* liability at all, but about whether the children, by consuming the cow, are seen as a continuation of the father's domain. Rashi's formulation suggests that the presence of real estate indicates a degree of continuity between the children and the father — analogous in spirit to *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* — so that if there is real property, they pay full value rather than the discounted benefit assessment.

## Pasuk on Returning Geze'ila

### Veheishiv Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵילָה אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״? יַחֲזִיר כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּזַל.
>
> **Translation:** After having cited two *baraitot* that express different opinions with regard to the obligation of heirs to pay for property stolen by their deceased father, the Gemara cites a third *baraita* that presents both opinions. **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "Then it shall be, if he has sinned, and is guilty, **that he shall restore the item that he robbed"** (Leviticus 5:23); **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "that he robbed"?** It means that the robber must **return the same** item **that he robbed.**

The *gemara* now turns to an explicit *pasuk*. *Ve-heishiv et ha-gezeila asher gazal* — you shall return the stolen object that you stole. The *braita* asks: *mah talmud lomar asher gazal* — what does the added phrase "that you stole" come to teach? *Yachzir ke-ein she-gazal* — one must return it in the form in which it was stolen, i.e., only when the object still exists as it was.

### Children Exempt When Consumed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַגּוֹזֵל וּמַאֲכִיל אֶת בָּנָיו – פְּטוּרִין מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. הִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶן – בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים בֵּין קְטַנִּים, חַיָּיבִין. מִשּׁוּם סוֹמְכוֹס אָמְרוּ: גְּדוֹלִים חַיָּיבִין, קְטַנִּים פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **From here,** based on this exposition, the Sages **stated:** In a case of **one who robs** another of food **and feeds** it to **his children,** the children are **exempt from paying** the owner. If **he left** stolen goods **to them** as an inheritance, **whether they are adults or minors, they are obligated** to return the stolen goods. **They said in the name of Sumakhos:** If the heirs are **adults** they **are obligated,** but if they are **minors** they **are exempt.**

From this the *gemara* derives: *mikan amru, ha-gozel u-ma'akhil et banav peturim li-shalem* — from here they said that one who steals and feeds the stolen object to his children, those children are exempt from payment. Tosafot notes that this *pasuk*-based derivation actually works better *not* according to Rav Chisda. Rav Chisda grounded the children's exemption in the *ye'ush* framework — the obligation of the consuming party depends on whether *ye'ush* has occurred. But this *pasuk* says something broader: the obligation to return a stolen object applies only when it still exists. If it has been consumed — even before *ye'ush* — the children are not obligated, not as *gazlan* and not as *mazik*. The Torah here provides a special teaching: for the children of the thief, the obligation to make restitution applies only when the object is still present.

### Obligation Only If Exists

The logic is carefully bounded. For the *gazlan* himself, destruction of the object does not extinguish his obligation — he must pay the equivalent value. But the *pasuk* teaches that for *his children*, the *she'ila* of restitution is tethered to the physical existence of the object: *yachzir ke-ein she-gazal*. If it no longer exists, the children bear no liability — not as secondary *gazlanim*, not as *mazik*.

## Gezeilah Be'ein Rule (II)

### Adults Must Return

Returning to the case where the *gezeila* is *be-ein* — still extant — the *braita* cited earlier rules: *hiniach lifneihem bein gedolim bein ketanim chayavim* — if the father left the stolen object in front of them and it is still present, whether adults or minors they are obligated. The adult children clearly must return it, as there is no framework — whether before or after *ye'ush*, whether or not one accepts *reshut yoresh ke-reshut loke'ach* — under which they may retain another's property when it is plainly before them.

### Minors Cannot Be Sued

The minor children present a different problem. Even if the object is unambiguously identifiable as belonging to the claimant, one cannot bring a court case against a minor to compel its return. This is the principle of *mekabelin eidut she-lo bifnei ba'al din*: court proceedings must be conducted in the presence of the opposing party, and a minor does not have the standing to defend himself. The owner must wait until the children reach majority.

### Sumchos vs. Mainstream

*Mishum Sumchos amru*: in the name of Sumchos — a student of Rabbi Meir — there is a teaching that even when the *gezeila* is *be-ein*, only adult children are obligated, while minors are exempt. The mainstream *rabbinic* position, however, holds that minors are obligated even when the object is *be-ein* — i.e., proceedings may be conducted against them. The difference between these positions reflects a deeper disagreement about how to define the status quo and whose possession presumptively prevails when the other party is a minor, a question to which the *gemara* now turns with a series of concrete cases.

## Court Cases vs. Minors

### Rabbi Yirmiya Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר חֲמוּהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה טְרַק גַּלָּא בְּאַפֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין, אָמַר: שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא תּוֹבֵעַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא מַיְיתִינָא סָהֲדִי דְּאַחְזַקִי בֵּיהּ בְּחַיֵּי דַּאֲבוּהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מְקַבְּלִין עֵדִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts a related incident: **The son of Rabbi Yirmeya's father-in-law,** i.e., his wife's brother, who was a minor, **shut the doors** of his father's house **before Rabbi Yirmeya** in order to prevent Rabbi Yirmeya from establishing legal possession of the house or a chamber that he claimed belonged to him. Rabbi Yirmeya **came before Rabbi Avin** to file a legal claim against his brother-in-law. Rabbi Avin **said** to Rabbi Yirmeya: Your brother-in-law is **claiming** ownership of that which is **his,** since he retains the presumptive ownership of his father's house upon his father's death. Rabbi Yirmeya **said to him: But I can bring witnesses who can testify that I took possession of it during his father's lifetime.** Rabbi Avin **said to him: But does** the court **accept witnesses** who testify **in the absence of a litigant?** Since the defendant is a minor his presence is not legally recognized, and the court does not accept testimony against him.

The *gemara* presents an actual case. The brother-in-law of Rabbi Yirmiya — his wife's brother, who had inherited his father-in-law's estate and was apparently a minor — barred Rabbi Yirmiya from entering a property. Rabbi Yirmiya claimed that he had purchased the property from the father-in-law during the father's lifetime, and sought to enter and take possession. The minor heir refused entry, claiming the property as part of the inherited estate.

### Rabbi Avin's Refusal

Rabbi Yirmiya brought the matter before Rabbi Avin, seeking to litigate his claim. Rabbi Avin refused to allow the case to proceed. The minor was holding property that was presumptively part of his inherited estate — the status quo was that this property belonged to the heir. Rabbi Yirmiya then pressed: he was prepared to bring witnesses who could establish that he had taken possession of the property during the father's lifetime — *she-achzakti bei be-chayei de-avuha*. Rabbi Avin replied: *ve-chi mekablin edim she-lo bifnei ba'al din?* — do we accept testimony not in the presence of the opposing party? The minor cannot defend himself in court, so the case cannot proceed.

### Stolen Object Retrieval

The *gemara* challenges Rabbi Avin's position: *ve-ha katania bein gedolim bein ketanim chayavim* — we have the *braita* that says one can retrieve a stolen object from minors, whether adults or children. This implies that proceedings against minors are in fact permitted in at least some circumstances. Rabbi Avin responded that yes, the *machlokes* of Sumchos stands: Sumchos holds that even for a stolen object one cannot proceed against minors, and it is Rabbi Yirmiya who has the minority opinion of Sumchos on his side — here operating against him. Rabbi Yirmiya objected: *ichpal kuli alma ve-ka'i ke-Sumchos?* — are we really going to fold up the majority ruling and act like Sumchos just to take away his property?

At this point the matter came before Rabbi Abbahu, who cited a teaching of Rabbi Yosef bar Chama in the name of Rabbi Oshaya.

### Seizure vs. Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, אֲתָא וּמְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ. אָמַר: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְכוּ הָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא? דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: תִּינוֹק שֶׁתָּקַף בַּעֲבָדָיו, וְיָרַד לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֶה שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״שֶׁלִּי הוּא״, אֵין אוֹמְרִים: נַמְתִּין עַד שֶׁיַּגְדִּיל; אֶלָּא מוֹצִיאִין מִיָּדוֹ מִיָּד, וְלִכְשֶׁיַּגְדִּיל יָבִיא עֵדִים וְנִרְאֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **In the meantime, the matter circulated and** eventually **came before Rabbi Abbahu,** who **said: Did you not hear that which Rav Yosef bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Oshaya** says? **As Rav Yosef bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says:** With regard to **a child who took his slaves and descended into the field of another and said: It is mine,** the court **does not say: Let us wait until he matures** before we assess his claims. **Rather,** the court **removes it from his possession immediately, and when he matures he may bring witnesses** to attest to his claim **and we will see** how to judge the case. Similarly, Rabbi Yirmiya should be awarded the disputed property until his minor brother-in-law may litigate in court.

Rabbi Oshaya's teaching: if a minor, together with his servants, invaded a neighbor's field and claimed it as his own — *amar sheli* — the *halacha* is *ein mamtinin lo ad she-yagdil* — we do not wait for him to grow up. Rather, *motzi'im mi-yado mi-yad*, we immediately remove the field from his grasp and restore it to its proper owner. When he reaches majority he may bring witnesses and make his case.

### Status Quo Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם הוּא דְּמַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ – דְּלָא קָיְימָא לֵיהּ אַחֲזָקָה דַּאֲבוּהּ; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ חֲזָקָה דַּאֲבוּהּ, לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rabbi Abbahu's assertion: **Is** the case that Rav Yosef bar Ḥama discusses **comparable** to the case of Rabbi Yirmeya? **It is there,** in Rav Yosef bar Ḥama's case, **that we seize** the property **from him, because there is no presumption** of ownership **from his father; but where he does have a presumption** of ownership **from his father, no,** we do not seize his property. Since Rabbi Yirmeya's brother-in-law has a presumption of ownership due to the fact that the property belonged to his father, he retains control of the property.

At first glance this seems to support Rabbi Yirmiya's position: we do adjudicate against minors in property matters. But the *gemara* draws a sharp distinction. *Ha-sham hu de-mafkinan minei de-lo kayma lei chazaka de-avuh* — in that case we remove the property from the minor because he has no presumptive claim, no *chazaka* inherited from his father. The minor simply grabbed a neighbor's field. There is no question about the status quo: it was the neighbor's field before, it remains the neighbor's field, and the minor's seizure does not alter that.

*Aval heicha de-is lei chazaka de-avuh* — but in the case of Rabbi Yirmiya's brother-in-law, the minor is the presumed heir of his father's estate, and the status quo is that the property is in his possession as the natural heir. In that configuration, *lo* — we do not adjudicate against him. He holds the property as a continuation of his father's legitimate ownership, and any attempt to disturb that arrangement must wait until the minor reaches adulthood.

The common denominator is the principle of preserving the status quo. When a minor *seized* property, the status quo is the neighbor's prior possession, and we restore it. When a minor *inherited* property, the status quo is the minor's presumed ownership, and we protect it. In both cases we wait for the minor to grow up before entertaining a claim that would alter the recognized status quo. The debate between Sumchos and the *rabbanan* ultimately concerns how to characterize the status quo when it comes to chattel — *metaltelin* — where possession itself conveys presumptive ownership. Sumchos holds that even for a known stolen object the minor's physical possession establishes the status quo in his favor; the *rabbanan* maintain that the known prior ownership of the claimant constitutes the relevant status quo.

## Testimony Without Defending Party

### General Rule with Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַבִּי שַׁבְּתַאי: מְקַבְּלִין עֵדִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין. תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְכִי מְקַבְּלִין עֵדִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין?!
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the issue of testimony in the absence of a litigant, the Gemara discusses this matter in greater detail. **Rav Ashi said** that **Rabbi Shabbtai says:** The court **accepts witnesses** even **in the absence of a litigant. Rabbi Yoḥanan wondered at this** statement of Rav Ashi, and said: **Does** the court in fact **accept witnesses in the absence of a litigant?**

The *gemara* now pivots to a broader discussion of the principle *mekablin edim she-lo bifnei ba'al din* — accepting testimony not in the presence of the opposing party. Rav Assi cites Rav Shabtai: there are circumstances in which one does accept such testimony. Rabbi Yochanan was astonished: *ve-chi mekablin edim she-lo bifnei ba'al din?* — can one really proceed without the opposing party present?

### Urgency Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה הוּא חוֹלֶה אוֹ עֵדָיו חוֹלִים, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ עֵדָיו מְבַקְּשִׁין לֵילֵךְ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וְשָׁלְחוּ לוֹ וְלֹא בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina received** the following guideline **from** Rabbi Yoḥanan: The court accepts testimony in the absence of the defendant only in a case **where** the plaintiff **was ill, or his witnesses were ill, or his witnesses wished to travel overseas and** the judges **sent** for the defendant **and he did not come.** Since there is a concern that the plaintiff will not be able to present his claims at a later date, the testimony is accepted even in the absence of the defendant.

Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina clarified that Rabbi Yochanan was not dismissing the possibility entirely. Rather, Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina understood the teaching to apply only where there is genuine urgency: for example, the claimant himself is gravely ill and may not survive to bring his case; or the witnesses are ill; or the witnesses are about to travel overseas and will be unavailable. In such pressing circumstances, the *beit din* may accept testimony even without the opposing party.

### Refusal After Summons

But even urgency alone does not suffice. There is an additional requirement: *ve-shigru lo ve-lo ba* — the court must have sent word to the opposing party, and he must have refused to appear. Only when there is genuine urgency *and* the defendant has been given the opportunity to attend and has declined does the court proceed in his absence. Tosafot suggests the alternative reading that perhaps the condition is simply that he did not come — not necessarily that he actively refused — but the plain reading implies that he was summoned and declined.

### Case Already Opened

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְקַבְּלִין עֵדִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין. אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא, לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן דִּפְתַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּדִינֵיהּ, וּשְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ וְלָא אֲתָא. אֲבָל לָא פְּתַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּדִינָא, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל אָזֵילְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The court **accepts witnesses in the absence of a litigant. Mar Ukva said:** This statement **was explained to me** personally **by Shmuel himself,** and he said that it applies in a case **where** the court **opened his case and sent for him, but he did not appear. But** if it **did not** yet **open the case for him, he may say to** the plaintiff: **I am going to the High Court** to resolve this case and will not appear in the local court.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel offers a related ruling: *mekablin edim she-lo bifnei ba'al din* — one accepts testimony without the opposing party. And the further clarification: this applies when the court case was already opened — *ki patekhu lei be-dina* — and the defendant was present at the opening but subsequently refused to continue appearing. In that scenario, his non-appearance gives the court the right to continue the proceedings without him. He had his chance to engage, the proceedings were properly initiated in his presence, and his subsequent non-cooperation is at his own risk.

### Right to Escalate Venue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי פָתְחוּ לֵיהּ נָמֵי, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל אָזֵילְנָא! אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּגוֹן דְּנָקֵט דִּיסְקָא מִבֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then even **when** the court already **opened** the case **for him, he** should **also** be **able** to **say to them: I am going to the High Court** to resolve this case. The Gemara answers that **Ravina said:** The defendant may not defer an opened case to the High Court if, **for example,** the local court **obtained a license from the High Court** authorizing it to adjudicate the case.

*Aval lo patekhu lei be-dina* — but if the case was not yet formally opened, the defendant may say *ana le-vei dina gadola azlina* — I wish to have this case heard before the major *beit din*, not this local court. Parties have the right to demand adjudication before the *beit din ha-gadol* in Jerusalem, and as long as that right has not been foreclosed, a defendant who refuses to appear at a local *beit din* is not in default — he may simply be insisting on his right to a different forum. His non-appearance therefore cannot be used to justify proceeding without him.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: is this right to escalate the venue available only to the defendant, or also to the claimant? If the claimant can demand the major *beit din*, that creates a significant imbalance — a well-resourced claimant could effectively coerce settlements simply by threatening to drag the other party all the way to Jerusalem to defend themselves, since not everyone has the means to do so. This mirrors a perennial concern about how the party with greater resources to sustain protracted litigation gains a structural advantage.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty: if the defendant always has the right to demand the *beit din ha-gadol*, then even once the case has been opened, he should be able to insist on switching venues — *ki patekhu lei nami matzi amar lei le-vei dina gadola azlina*. The *gemara* answers by citing Ravina: we are speaking of a case where the court holds authorization from the *beit din ha-gadol* itself — *de-nakit diska mi-vei dina gadola*. When the major court has already delegated authority to this local court to hear the case, the defendant's right to escalate is foreclosed. Before the case was opened, the local court had no such delegation and the defendant could insist on the higher forum; once the case is opened by a court with the *beit din ha-gadol's* authority, that option is no longer available, and his non-appearance may properly be treated as a default.

## Document Validation

### Rav Allows Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מְקַיְּימִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין מְקַיְּימִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אֲבָהוּ: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהוּעַד בִּבְעָלָיו וְלֹא יִשְׁמְרֶנּוּ״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: יָבֹא בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר, וְיַעֲמוֹד עַל שׁוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § Having discussed testimony in the absence of a litigant, the Gemara addresses a similar matter. **Rav says:** The court **ratifies a document in the absence of a litigant. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The court **does not ratify a document in the absence of a litigant. Rav Sheshet said to Rabbi Yosei bar Abbahu: I will explain Rabbi Yoḥanan's reasoning to you. The verse states** with regard to an ox that gored: **"And warning has been given to its owner and he has not secured it"** (Exodus 21:29). **The Torah** means to **say:** Let **the owner of the ox come and stand by his ox** when the testimony about the ox is provided. Similarly, a document may be ratified only in the presence of the individual it implicates.

A final question is raised concerning *kiyum shtar* — the validation of a legal document. *Amar Rav: mekaymin et ha-shtar she-lo bifnei ba'al din* — Rav rules that one may authenticate the signatures on a document even without the opposing party present. The document already bears signatures; the proceeding is merely one of verifying that those signatures are genuine, which is a preliminary step before the document can be used in court. This is not, on Rav's view, equivalent to conducting a trial against the opposing party.

### Rabbi Yochanan Objects

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *ein mekaymin et ha-shtar she-lo bifnei ba'al din* — one may not validate a document in the opposing party's absence. He grounds this in a *pasuk*: *ve-hu'ad bi-ve'alav ve-lo yishmerenu* — the owner of a goring ox is warned about the ox's dangerous propensity and does not guard it. From this the *Torah* implies *yavo ba'al ha-shor ve-ya'amod al shoro* — the owner of the ox has the right to be present when testimony is given about his ox. The principle is that one has a right to face one's accusers, even when those accusers are directed against one's property rather than against oneself. Authentication of a document is, on Rabbi Yochanan's view, sufficiently adversarial to trigger this right.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: מְקַיְּימִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין, וַאֲפִילּוּ עוֹמֵד וְצוֹוֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ***halakha*** is that the court **ratifies a document in the absence of a litigant, and** this is the case **even** if the other litigant **stands and screams** in protest that the document is a forgery. **And if he said: Give me time until I bring witnesses and disqualify the document, we give him** time before forcing him to pay. **If he has come** with witnesses within the time allotted to him, **he has come,** and the court reviews the case accordingly. **If he does not come** within the time allotted to him, **we give him** an additional three days on which to bring witnesses, when the court is in session: **Monday, Thursday, and Monday.**

Rava rules definitively: *hilkheta, mekaymin et ha-shtar she-lo bifnei ba'al din* — the *halakha* follows Rav. One may validate a document without the opposing party. The underlying rationale is that *kiyum shtarot* is in essence rabbinic in character: *de-oraita*, any properly signed document is presumed genuine, and the authentication process is a rabbinic safeguard layered on top of that presumption. Because the document is already self-authenticating at the Torah level, the validation proceeding is not truly a court case being conducted against the opposing party. It does not cross the threshold that would require his presence, and Rabbi Yochanan's concern — rooted in the right to face accusers — does not apply to this more technical, preliminary procedure.
