# Gittin 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Titus & Onkelos

### Churban Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי דִּפְסַיק אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ – כֹּל יוֹמָא מְכַנְּשִׁי לֵיהּ לְקִיטְמֵיהּ וְדָיְינִי לֵיהּ, וְקָלוּ לֵיהּ וּמְבַדְּרוּ [לֵיהּ] אַשַּׁב יַמֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **That which he decreed against himself,** as he undergoes the following: **Every day his ashes are gathered, and they judge him, and they burn him, and they scatter him over the seven seas.**

Today's *daf* is 57, and we continue in the *aggadeta* — some of the most essential *aggadeta* about the *Churban ha-Bayit*. We pick up at the bottom of 56b, having just dealt with the downfall of Titus and the gnat that found its way into his nostril. One must acknowledge something almost darkly comic in this material: Vespasian's foot swells up, and Titus is undone by an insect in his nose. Even though Vespasian is something of a relatively sympathetic figure in these narratives, the theme running through all of this is very much the bringing low of the mighty — a note of mockery directed at the great powers of Rome.

### Titus & Gnat

By way of background: Titus, having destroyed the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, asked that after his death God would not be able to judge him, and requested that his ashes be scattered over the seven seas so that he could not be gathered for judgment.

### Onkelos Raises Titus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּנְקְלוֹס בַּר קְלוֹנִיקוּס בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּטִיטוּס הֲוָה. בָּעֵי לְאִיגַּיּוֹרֵי, אֲזַל אַסְּקֵיהּ לְטִיטוּס בִּנְגִידָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן חֲשִׁיב בְּהָהוּא עָלְמָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל. מַהוּ לְאִידַּבּוֹקֵי בְּהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִילַּיְיהוּ נְפִישִׁין, וְלָא מָצֵית לְקַיּוֹמִינְהוּ. זִיל אִיגָּרִי בְּהוּ בְּהָהוּא עָלְמָא וְהָוֵית רֵישָׁא, דִּכְתִיב: ״הָיוּ צָרֶיהָ לְרֹאשׁ וְגוֹ׳״ – כׇּל הַמֵּיצַר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל נַעֲשָׂה רֹאשׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּמַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Onkelos bar Kalonikos, the son of Titus's sister, wanted to convert** to Judaism. **He went** and **raised Titus** from the grave **through necromancy,** and **said to him: Who is** most **important in that world** where you are now? Titus **said to him: The Jewish people.** Onkelos asked him: **Should I** then **attach** myself **to them** here in this world? Titus **said to him: Their commandments are numerous, and you will not be able to fulfill them.** It is best that you do as follows: **Go** out and **battle against them in that world, and you will become the chief, as it is written: "Her adversaries [*tzareha*] have become the chief"** (Lamentations 1:5), which means: **Anyone who distresses [*meitzer*] Israel will become the chief.** Onkelos **said to him: What is the punishment of that man,** a euphemism for Titus himself, in the next world? Titus **said to him:**

Picking up four lines from the bottom of 56b, the *Gemara* introduces *Onkelos bar Kalonikos bar achatei de-Titus* — Onkelos, the son of the sister of Titus. He *ba'ei le-giurei*, he wished to convert, and indeed the tradition identifies him as *Onkelos ha-Ger*, the famous convert who produced the authoritative Aramaic translation of the Torah.

*Azal askei le-Titus be-negida* — he went and raised Titus from the dead through a form of necromancy. He asked him: *man chashiv be-hahu alma* — who is important in the world to come? Titus replied: *Yisrael* — the Jewish people. Onkelos then asked: *ma lei devukei behu* — should I therefore cleave to them in this world? Titus replied: *milaihu nefishan velo matis le-kiyumaihu* — their commandments are many and you will not be able to fulfill them all. Rather: *zil igrei behu be-hahu alma* — go and attack them in this world, *ve-havis reisha* — and you will be a leader. As the verse says, *hayu tzareha le-rosh* — its enemies became the head. The implication, taken at face value, is almost troubling: afflict Israel and you will become a leader. The commentators struggle to reread this, suggesting it means that only one who is already a great leader will manage to oppress Israel — but that is admittedly not the plain *peshat* of the *Gemara* as it stands.

### Titus's Punishment

Onkelos then asked Titus: *dinei de-hahu gavra be-mai* — what is the judgment of that man, meaning yourself, in the world to come? Titus answered: *be-mai de-pasak anafshei* — whatever I decreed upon myself. *Kol yoma machneshi lei le-katmei* — every day my ashes are gathered from the seven seas, *ve-dayni lei* — I am re-judged, *ve-kalu lei* — I am burned again, *ve-mabdru a-shva yamei* — and my ashes are scattered once more over the seven seas. This cycle repeats daily. His counsel to Onkelos, then, is: you cannot win in the world to come, but you might as well be successful in this world by oppressing Israel.

## Onkelos & Balaam

### Balaam Summoned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אַסְּקֵיהּ לְבִלְעָם בִּנְגִידָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן חֲשִׁיב בְּהָהוּא עָלְמָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל. מַהוּ לְאִידַּבּוֹקֵי בְּהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֹא תִדְרוֹשׁ שְׁלוֹמָם וְטוֹבָתָם כׇּל הַיָּמִים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּמַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע רוֹתַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Onkelos then **went and raised Balaam** from the grave **through necromancy. He said to him: Who is** most **important in that world** where you are now? Balaam **said to him: The Jewish people.** Onkelos asked him: **Should I** then **attach** myself **to them** here in this world? Balaam **said to him: You shall not seek their peace or their welfare all the days** (see Deuteronomy 23:7). Onkelos **said to him: What is the punishment of that man,** a euphemism for Balaam himself, in the next world? Balaam **said to him:** He is cooked **in boiling semen,** as he caused Israel to engage in licentious behavior with the daughters of Moab.

*Azal askei le-Bilam be-negida* — Onkelos then went and raised Balaam from the dead through necromancy. He asked the same question: *man chashiv be-hahu alma* — who is important in the world to come? Balaam too answered: *Yisrael*. And again the follow-up: *ma lei devukei behu* — should I cleave to them?

### Balaam's Hatred

Balaam's answer was the opposite of what one might hope: *lo tidrosh shelomam ve-tovatam kol ha-yamim* — do not seek after their well-being or their good, ever. The counsel is straightforward hatred toward Israel in this world.

### Balaam's Torment

Onkelos asked Balaam as well: *dinei de-hahu gavra be-mai* — what is your punishment? *Be-shichvat zera rotachat* — in boiling semen. Two explanations are offered for this: one, that Balaam caused *Bnei Yisrael* to sin with *Benot Moav*, and the other, that it reflects his own personal licentiousness.

## Yeshu Raised

### Yeshu Summoned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אַסְּקֵיהּ בִּנְגִידָא לְיֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן חֲשִׁיב בְּהָהוּא עָלְמָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל. מַהוּ לְאִדַּבּוֹקֵי בְּהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טוֹבָתָם דְּרוֹשׁ, רָעָתָם לֹא תִּדְרוֹשׁ, כׇּל הַנּוֹגֵעַ בָּהֶן כְּאִילּוּ נוֹגֵעַ בְּבָבַת עֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Onkelos then **went** and **raised Jesus the Nazarene** from the grave **through necromancy.** Onkelos **said to him: Who is** most **important in that world** where you are now? Jesus **said to him: The Jewish people.** Onkelos asked him: **Should I** then **attach** myself **to them** in this world? Jesus **said to him: Their welfare you shall seek, their misfortune you shall not seek,** for **anyone who touches them is** regarded **as if he were touching the apple of his eye** (see Zechariah 2:12).

*Azal askei be-negida le-poshei Yisrael* — the text as we have it reads "sinners of Israel," but the original *girsa* is *Yeshu ha-Notzri* — Jesus. Onkelos raised him through necromancy and asked the same question: *man chashiv be-hahu alma* — who is important in the world to come? The answer was again: *Yisrael*.

### Yeshu's Advice

Here, strikingly, the answer diverges from what both Titus and Balaam had said. Onkelos asked: *ma leidvuki behu* — should I cleave to them? And the reply was: *tovatam drosh ra'atam lo tidrosh* — seek their well-being and do not seek their harm. This does not necessarily mean one should convert, but the counsel is to be good to them in this world. *Kol hanogea bahen kenogea be-vavat eino* — anyone who harms them is as one who pokes at the pupil of his own eye.

### Yeshu's Torment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּמַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּצוֹאָה רוֹתַחַת. דְּאָמַר מָר: כׇּל הַמַּלְעִיג עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים נִידּוֹן בְּצוֹאָה רוֹתַחַת. תָּא חֲזִי מָה בֵּין פּוֹשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לִנְבִיאֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Onkelos **said to him: What is the punishment of that man,** a euphemism for Jesus himself, in the next world? Jesus **said to him:** He is punished **with boiling excrement. As the Master said: Anyone who mocks the words of the Sages will be sentenced to boiling excrement.** And this was his sin, as he mocked the words of the Sages. The Gemara comments: **Come** and **see the difference between the sinners of Israel and the prophets of the nations of the world.** As Balaam, who was a prophet, wished Israel harm, whereas Jesus the Nazarene, who was a Jewish sinner, sought their well-being.

Onkelos asked about his punishment as well: *dinei de-hahu gavra be-mai* — what are you judged with? *Be-tzoa rotachat* — in boiling excrement. *De-amar mar kol ha-mal'ig al divrei chachamim nidon be-tzoa rotachat* — for anyone who mocks the words of the sages is judged in boiling excrement. It is notable that the sin attributed here is specifically mocking the sages — framing the figure as a Jewish heretic rather than as an external oppressor like Titus or Balaam.

### Heretic Contrast

The *Gemara* then draws the lesson explicitly: *ta chazi ma bein poshei Yisrael le-niviei umot ha'olam* — come and see the difference between sinners from Israel and the prophets of the nations who are idolaters. Even in the world to come, under harsh divine judgment, the idolaters — Titus and Balaam — still advise enmity toward Israel, while the Jewish heretic, even in his torment, says to seek Israel's well-being.

## Power of Shame

### Shame's Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גָּדוֹל(ה) כֹּחָהּ שֶׁל בּוּשָׁה, שֶׁהֲרֵי סִיַּיע הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת בַּר קַמְצָא, וְהֶחְרִיב אֶת בֵּיתוֹ, וְשָׂרַף אֶת הֵיכָלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** To conclude the story of Kamtza and bar Kamtza and the destruction of Jerusalem, the Gemara cites a *baraita*. It **is taught: Rabbi Elazar says: Come and see how great is the power of shame, for the Holy One, Blessed be He, assisted bar Kamtza,** who had been humiliated, **and** due to this humiliation and shame **He destroyed His Temple and burned His Sanctuary.**

*Tanya*, it was taught in a *baraita*: *amar Rabbi Elazar, bo u're'eh kama gadol kocha shel busha* — come and see how great is the power of shame. This serves as a concluding reflection on the story of *Kamtza* and *Bar Kamtza*, which was covered previously, and which the *Gemara* now wraps up thematically.

### Kamtza Story

*She-harei siya HaKadosh Baruch Hu et Bar Kamtza* — God assisted *Bar Kamtza*, meaning He guided events through this story, *ve-hachiv et beito ve-saraf et heichalo* — and ultimately it led to the destruction of the *Beis ha-Mikdash* and the burning of the *Heichal*. This is worth pausing over. Earlier in the *Gemara*, the destruction was attributed to *Rabbi Zecharya ben Avkulas* and his excessive humility: *hechriv et bateinu ve-saraf et heichaleinu* — he destroyed our homes and burned our sanctuary. Here, God Himself is identified as the agent who destroyed His own house. And yet the *Gemara* also frames it as coming through *Bar Kamtza*'s shame. Who, then, bears the responsibility?

### Causes of Destruction

These layered attributions — the excessive humility of leadership, the shame of *Bar Kamtza*, and ultimately God's own guidance of events — all coexist in the *Gemara* without contradiction, and each illuminates a different dimension of the *churban*. There is also a case to be made that the entire story of *Kamtza* and *Bar Kamtza* is not merely a tale about the sin of shaming, however real and serious that sin is, nor only about *sinat chinam* in a narrow interpersonal sense. It reflects something more politically concrete: the factionalism and internal warfare of the period. The Zealots were spending their energy fighting one another. There was no compelling reason that the Jews had to rebel against Rome when they did, nor that they could not have negotiated a way to preserve the *Beis ha-Mikdash*. The destruction was in large measure brought about by internal strife and a failure of leadership — precisely the *Gemara*'s broader point about the rabbis of that generation who saw what was happening and did not act.

## Tur Malka Chickens

### Wedding Tree Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתַּרְנְגוֹלָא וְאַתַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא חֲרִיב טוּר מַלְכָּא: דַּהֲווֹ נְהִיגִי כִּי הֲווֹ מַפְּקִי חַתְנָא וְכַלְּתָא, מַפְּקִי קַמַּיְיהוּ תַּרְנְגוֹלָא וְתַרְנְגוֹלְתָּא, כְּלוֹמַר: פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ כְּתַרְנְגוֹלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § It was previously mentioned (55b) that the place known as **the King's Mountain [*Tur Malka*] was destroyed on account of a rooster and a hen.** The details of what happened are as follows: **It was customary** in that place **that when they would lead a bride and groom** to their wedding, **they would take out a rooster and a hen before them,** as if **to say** in the manner of a good omen: **Be fruitful and multiply like chickens.**

The *Gemara* now shifts to the story of *Tur Malka*, another city that was destroyed. *Aturnegoila ve-tarnegoilta chariv Tur Malka* — on account of a hen and a rooster, the city of *Tur Malka* was destroyed. The practice there was: *ki havu mapki chatan ve-challa*, when a wedding procession would go out, *mapki kameihu tarnegoila ve-tarnegoilta* — they would lead a rooster and a hen in front of the couple. *Kolomar pru u'revu ka-tarnegolim* — this symbolized the wish that they be as fruitful as hens and roosters.

### Caesar's Carriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָא חָלֵיף גּוּנְדָּא דְרוֹמָאֵי, שַׁקְלִינְהוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ. נְפַלוּ עֲלַיְיהוּ מְחוֹנְהוּ. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְקֵיסָר: מְרַדוּ בָּךְ יְהוּדָאֵי! אֲתָא עֲלַיְיהוּ. הֲוָה בְּהוּ הָהוּא בַּר דָּרוֹמָא, דַּהֲוָה קָפֵיץ מִילָא וְקָטֵיל בְּהוּ. שַׁקְלֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְתָאגֵיהּ וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ אַאַרְעָא. אֲמַר: רִיבּוֹנֵיהּ דְּעָלְמָא כּוּלֵּיהּ, אִי נִיחָא לָךְ, לָא תִּמְסְרֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא – לְדִידֵיהּ וּלְמַלְכוּתֵיהּ, בִּידֵיהּ דְּחַד גַּבְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** **One day a troop [*gunda*] of Roman** soldiers **passed by** there while a wedding was taking place **and took** the rooster and hen **from them.** The residents of the city **fell upon them and beat them.** The soldiers **came and said to the emperor: The Jews have rebelled against you.** The emperor then **came against them** in war. Among the residents of the King's Mountain **there was a certain man** named **bar Deroma who could jump** the distance of **a *mil*, and he killed** many of the Romans, who were powerless to stand up against him. **The emperor** then **took his crown and set it on the ground** as a sign of mourning. **He said: Master of the Universe, if it is pleasing to You, do not give over that man,** a euphemism for himself, **and his kingdom into the hands of** only **one man.**

*Yoma chad hava ka chalif gunda de-Romai* — one day a troop of Romans was passing through. There happened to be a wedding procession at the time, and the hungry soldiers *shaklineihu mineihu* — grabbed the chickens.

### Jews Fight Back

*Naflu alaihu* — the people attacked the soldiers, demanding their chickens back. *Machunu* — they struck them. *Azlu amru lei le-Keisar* — the Romans went and reported to Caesar: *mardu becha Yehuda'ei* — the Jews are rebelling against you. *Ata alaihu* — and Caesar came with an army against them.

### Bar Droma Hero

*Havah behu hahu Bar Droma* — there was in this community a man called *Bar Droma*, *de-havah kafitz mila* — who could leap a *mila*, and *ve-katlu behu* — he would cut down the Romans as he vaulted through them. The imagery is almost of a superhero, and the parallel to the stories that may have inspired the Asterix comic series — a tiny village resisting Rome through what amounts to superhuman ability — is not difficult to see. Just as Asterix is a story of a small enclave that held out against Rome, Bar Kochba's revolt is clearly the historical event that gave rise to that imagination.

### Caesar Prays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכְשְׁלֵיהּ פּוּמֵּיהּ לְבַר דָּרוֹמָא, וַאֲמַר: ״הֲלֹא אַתָּה אֱלֹהִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ וְלֹא תֵצֵא אֱלֹהִים בְּצִבְאוֹתֵינוּ״. דָּוִד נָמֵי אָמַר הָכִי! דָּוִד אַתְמוֹהֵי קָא מַתְמַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the end it was the words issuing from **his** own **mouth** that **caused bar Deroma to stumble, as he uttered** this verse in complaint against God: **"Have You not rejected us, O God, so that You go not forth, O God, with our hosts?"** (Psalms 60:12). The Gemara asks: But did not **David also say this?** The Gemara answers: **David uttered** these words **as a question,** wondering whether they were true, whereas bar Deroma pronounced them as a statement of fact.

*Shaklei Keisar le-tageih ve-asfeh a-ara* — Caesar took his crown and placed it on the ground. He prayed: *Ribonei de-alma*, Master of the world, *im nicha lach lo timsereih le-hahu gavra le-didei u-malchuteih be-yadei de-chad gavra* — if it please You, do not hand me and my entire kingdom over to one man. His prayer was heard: *achshaleih pumeih le-Bar Droma* — Bar Droma's mouth caused him to stumble. Bar Droma said: *halo atah Elohim zenachtanu ve-lo tetzei Elohim be-tzivotenu* — Have You not, O God, abandoned us, and You do not go out with our armies? This is a complaint against God rather than an expression of wonder.

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: this is a verse from *Tehillim* — David also said it! The *Gemara* responds that *David itmuhei ka-matmei* — David was expressing astonishment, as if to say, "Could it be that You have abandoned us?" It was a cry of disbelief. Bar Droma, by contrast, was leveling a charge: God, You abandoned us. And this distinction proved fatal.

### Bathroom Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָל לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, אֲתָא דְּרָקוֹנָא שַׁמְטֵיהּ לְכַרְכְּשֵׁיהּ וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אֲמַר: הוֹאִיל וְאִיתְרְחִישׁ לִי נִיסָּא, הָא זִימְנָא אֶישְׁבְּקִינְהוּ. שַׁבְקִינְהוּ וַאֲזַל. אִיזְדְּקוּר וַאֲכַלוּ וּשְׁתוֹ, וְאַדְלִיקוּ שְׁרָגֵי, עַד דְּאִיתְחֲזִי בִּלְיוֹנָא דְגוּשְׁפַּנְקָא בְּרָחוֹק מִילָא. אֲמַר: מִיחְדָּא קָא חָדוּ בִּי יְהוּדָאֵי! הֲדַר אֲתָא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts what happened to bar Deroma: **He entered an outhouse, a snake came** and **eviscerated him, and he died.** The emperor **said: Since a miracle was performed for me,** as I had no part in bar Deroma's death, **I will let** the rest of the people **be this time** and take no further action against them. **He let them be and went** on his way. **They leapt** about, **ate, drank, and lit** so many **candles** in celebration **that the image [*bilyona*]** imprinted **on a seal [*gushpanka*] was visible from a distance of a *mil*.** The emperor then **said: The Jews are rejoicing over me.** So **he went back** and **came against them.**

*Ala be-veit ha-kisei* — Bar Droma went to the bathroom. *Ata darkona* — a serpent appeared — and *shamteih le-karkashei* — his innards dropped out, *ve-nach nafshei* — and he died. The Superman of the Jewish resistance died in the bathroom. As with Titus's gnat and Vespasian's swollen foot, the manner of death is almost grimly comic.

### Caesar Returns

*Ho-il ve-itrachash li nisa* — Caesar acknowledged that a miracle had occurred for him. He declared: *ha zimna ishvakinhu* — this time I will leave them. *Ve-azal izdakar* — and when Caesar departed, the Jews rose up in celebration, *ve-achalu ve-shatu ve-adliku shragei* — eating, drinking, and lighting candles. So great was the illumination that *ad de-itchazi dilyona de-gushpanka be-richuk mila* — the impression of a signet ring could be made out from a distance of a *mila*. Caesar, observing this celebration as he withdrew, interpreted it as mockery: *michdi chadu be-Yehuda'ei* — the Jews are laughing at me. *Hadar ata alei* — and he turned back and attacked.

### Tur Malka Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: תְּלָת מְאָה אַלְפֵי שְׁלִיפֵי סַיְיפָא עֲיַילוּ לְטוּר מַלְכָּא, וּקְטַלוּ בַּהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי וּתְלָתָא לֵילָווֹתָא; וּבְהָךְ גִּיסָא הִלּוּלֵי וְחִנְגֵי, וְלָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי הָנֵי בְּהָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says: Three hundred thousand men with drawn swords entered the King's Mountain and massacred** its inhabitants **for three days and three nights. And** at the same time **on** the other **side** of the mountain, **weddings and** other **festivities** continued to be celebrated, **and they did not know about each other,** owing to the enormous size of the place.

*Amar Rav Assi: tlat me'a alfi shlifei saifa ailu le-Tur Malka* — 300,000 men with drawn swords entered *Tur Malka*, *ve-katlu bah tlata yoma ve-tlata leilavata* — and they killed there for three days and three nights.

### City Divided

*U've-hach gisa hilulei ve-chingei* — on one side of the city the slaughter was underway, while on the other side, celebrations and festivities continued. *Ve-lo hava yada hanei be-hanei* — the one half of the city did not know what was happening in the other. This detail is haunting in its own right. It may strain the imagination as to how such a thing is possible logistically, but its truth as a historical phenomenon — that in times of catastrophe communities in close proximity can remain entirely unaware of one another's fate — is well documented. One thinks of Jewish communities in Eastern Europe during the *Shoah*, where Jews in one country had no knowledge of the mass murder already underway in neighboring lands.

The deeper point of these stories about *Tur Malka* is often overlooked, because on *Tisha B'Av* people tend to stop the reading of this material before reaching it, since it no longer concerns the *Churban ha-Bayit* directly. But the *Gemara* is making precisely this point: the destruction was not only of the *Beis ha-Mikdash*. It was the destruction of a civilization, of cities, of an entire population and way of life. The rooster and hen, the wedding procession — these are prosaic details of traditional life, precisely the kind that are swept away in catastrophe. The Jews of *Tur Malka* could not adapt even slightly to the Roman presence, could not absorb even the minor disruption of the requisitioned chickens. And ultimately everything was destroyed.

## Yanai's Villages

### Jacob's Beauty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בִּלַּע ה׳ וְלֹא חָמַל אֵת כׇּל נְאוֹת יַעֲקֹב״ – כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵלּוּ שִׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא עֲיָירוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ לְיַנַּאי הַמֶּלֶךְ בְּהַר הַמֶּלֶךְ. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: שִׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא עֲיָירוֹת הָיוּ לוֹ לְיַנַּאי הַמֶּלֶךְ בְּהַר הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת הָיוּ בָּהּ כְּיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, חוּץ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ שֶׁהָיוּ בָּהֶן כִּפְלַיִם כְּיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the verse: **"The Lord has swallowed up without pity all the habitations of Jacob"** (Lamentations 2:2), it is related that **when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This** is referring to the **six hundred thousand cities** that **King Yannai had in the King's Mountain.** As **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav Asi says: King Yannai had six hundred thousand cities in the King's Mountain, and each of them** had a population as great **as** the number of **those who left Egypt, except for three** of those cities, the population of which **was double** the number of **those who left Egypt.**

*U've-hach gisa hilulei ve-chingei* — having described the slaughter of *Tur Malka*, the *Gemara* now quotes the verse: *bala Hashem ve-lo chamal et kol ne'ot Yaakov* — God swallowed up and had no mercy on all the dwellings of Jacob. This verse from *Eicha* frames what follows.

*Ki ata Rabin amar Rabbi Yochanan: ailu shitin ribo ayarot she-hayu le-Yanai ha-melech be-Har ha-Melech* — these are the 600,000 villages that the Hasmonean king Yanai had on *Har ha-Melech*. *Ve-chol achat ve-achat hayu bah ke-yotzei Mitzrayim* — and each one of them had as many inhabitants as those who went out of Egypt, which is to say 600,000 males of fighting age, and by extension a total population of perhaps two million per village. The arithmetic, at 600,000 villages each with something like two million people, produces numbers that are frankly staggering.

### Three Villages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ שֶׁהָיוּ בָּהֶן כִּפְלַיִם כְּיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם. אֵלּוּ הֵן: כְּפַר בִּישׁ, כְּפַר שַׁיחֲלַיִים, כְּפַר דִּכְרַיָּא. כְּפַר בִּישׁ – דְּלָא יָהֲבִי בֵּיתָא לְאוּשְׁפִּיזָא. כְּפַר שַׁיחֲלַיִים – שֶׁהָיְתָה פַּרְנָסָתָן מִן שַׁחֲלַיִים. כְּפַר דִּכְרַיָּא – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁהָיוּ נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן יוֹלְדוֹת זְכָרִים תְּחִלָּה, וְיוֹלְדוֹת נְקֵבָה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה, וּפוֹסְקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **These** are **those** three cities: **Kefar Bish, Kefar Shiḥalayim, and Kefar Dikhrayya.** The Gemara explains the meaning of these place-names. **Kefar Bish,** Evil Town, was called by that name because its inhabitants **would not open** their **houses to guests. Kefar Shiḥalayim** was referred to by that name because **their livelihood was** derived **from** the cultivation of **cress [*shaḥalayim*].** As for **Kefar Dikhrayya,** Town of Males, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Their women would first give birth to boys, and afterward give birth to girls, and** then **they would stop** having children.

*Chutz mi-shalosh she-hayu bahen kiflayim ke-yotzei Mitzrayim* — with the exception of three villages that had double that number. These three were *Kfar Bish*, *Kfar Shichlayim*, and *Kfar Dichraya*. Their names are expressive. *Kfar Bish* — "the bad village" — *dela yahavi beis ushpiza*, because they did not provide lodging to wayfarers, in the manner of Sodom. *Kfar Shichlayim* — named for *shichlayim*, a type of watercress, because the residents made their living from that vegetable. *Kfar Dichraya* — "the village of males" — because, as Rabbi Yochanan explains, *she-hayu nesheihem yoldos zacharim techilla ve-yoldos nekeiva ba-achrona ve-poskos* — the women would first bear sons, then daughters, and then stop. The precise critique embedded in this last name is somewhat unclear.

### Ulla Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הָהוּא אַתְרָא, וַאֲפִילּוּ שִׁיתִּין רִיבְּווֹתָא קְנֵי לָא מַחְזֵיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שַׁקּוֹרֵי מְשַׁקְּרִיתוּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אֶרֶץ צְבִי״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ, מָה צְבִי זֶה – אֵין עוֹרוֹ מַחֲזִיק אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ, אַף אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – בִּזְמַן שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין עָלֶיהָ רָוְוחָא, וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאֵין יוֹשְׁבִין עָלֶיהָ גָּמְדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: I myself saw that place, and it could not hold even six hundred thousand reeds,** all the more so that number of people. **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Ḥanina: You lie** with your exorbitant exaggerations. Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: With regard to** Eretz Yisrael **it is written: Land of the deer** (see Jeremiah 3:19). **Just as the skin of a deer cannot hold its flesh,** for after the animal is skinned, its hide shrinks, **so too,** with regard to **Eretz Yisrael, when it is settled, it expands, but when it is not settled, it contracts.** This explains how a place that is so small today could have been so highly populated prior to the Temple's destruction.

*Amar Ulla: ledidi chazi li hahu atra* — Ulla said, I myself saw that place. *Ve-afilu shitin ribo kanei lo machzik* — it cannot even contain 600,000 reeds, let alone 600,000 villages. Ulla is directly calling out the exaggeration. In the *Gemara*'s version, a *min* — a heretic, though the parallel to *Tzadoki* in some texts suggests the original *girsa* was indeed *min* — challenges Rabbi Chanina: *shiku meshakritsu* — you people are lying.

### Land Expands

Rabbi Chanina responds: *Eretz Tzvi kesiv* — the land of Israel is described in Scripture as the land of the deer. *Mah tzvi zeh ein oro machzik et besaro* — just as a deer's hide, once removed, cannot be stretched back over the carcass that it once contained, because it shrinks outside the body, *af Eretz Yisrael, bi-zman she-yoshveha aleha ravcha* — so too the land of Israel, when its people dwell upon it, it expands. *U-bi-zman she-ein yoshveha aleha gamda* — and when its people are gone from it, it contracts. The physical smallness of the land today in no way contradicts the tradition that at its height it contained all those villages, because the land itself is of a different nature when inhabited by its people.

## Kfar Sekaniah

### Three Rabbis Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מִנְיוֹמֵי בַּר חִלְקִיָּה וְרַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִיָּה וְרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא הָווּ יָתְבִי גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי, אָמְרִי: אִי אִיכָּא דִּשְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא מִכְּפַר סְכַנְיָא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם, לֵימָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **Rav Minyumi bar Ḥilkiya, Rav Ḥilkiya bar Toviya, and Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya were** once **sitting together. They said: If there is someone who has heard anything about Kefar Sekhanya of Egypt,** which was in that region, **let him relate** it.

*Rav Minyumi bar Chilkiya ve-Rav Chilkiya bar Tuvya ve-Rav Huna bar Chiya havu yasvei gabei hadadi* — three rabbis were sitting together and one of them said: *ika de-shamei lei milsa mi-Kfar Sekanya shel Mitzrayim leima* — if anyone has heard something about this place, *Kfar Sekanya* of Egypt, let him speak. A note on the geography: Soncino suggests that the original text may have read *Kfar Sekanya shel Notzrim* rather than *Mitzrayim* — that is, Nazareth — and that this was a place that later became associated with early Jewish-Christian sectarianism. Steinsaltz notes a city in the Galilee called *Sekanya*. Whatever the precise location, the tradition being invoked is of a place that had once been a model of piety and righteousness.

### Betrothed Couple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַח חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ וַאֲמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָרוּס וַאֲרוּסָתוֹ שֶׁנִּשְׁבּוּ לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם, וְהִשִּׂיאוּם זֶה לָזֶה. אָמְרָה לוֹ: ״בְּבַקָּשָׁה מִמְּךָ, אַל תִּגַּע בִּי, שֶׁאֵין לִי כְּתוּבָּה מִמְּךָ״, וְלֹא נָגַע בָּהּ עַד יוֹם מוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **One of them began** the discussion **and said:** There was **an incident involving a betrothed man and woman** from there **who were taken captive by gentiles and** the latter **married them off to each other.** The woman **said to** the man: **Please do not touch me, as I do not have a marriage contract from you,** and it is prohibited for us to live together without one. **And until** the day of **his death** the man **did not touch** the woman.

*Patach chad mineihu ve-amar: maaseh be-arus ve-arusa* — one of the three opened and said: there was a story involving a betrothed man and woman — meaning they had completed *kiddushin* but had not yet had *chuppah* and were not yet living together as husband and wife. *She-nishbu le-vein ha-ovdei kochavim* — they were taken captive by idolaters. *Ve-hisi'um zeh la-zeh* — and the captors, apparently not realizing the two were already married, put them together for the purpose of having them produce children as slaves.

One might think: they are already married, so what is the problem? The issue is that they had only *kiddushin* without *nisuin*, and one may not live as husband and wife without a *kesuva*. *Ve-amra lo: bevakasha mimcha al tiga bi, she-ein li kesuva mimcha* — she said to him: please do not touch me, for I have no *kesuva* from you. *Ve-lo naga bah ad yom moso* — and he did not touch her until the day he died.

### Grain Price Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַח אִידַּךְ וַאֲמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה וְעָמְדוּ אַרְבָּעִים מוֹדִיּוֹת בְּדִינָר; נֶחְסַר הַשַּׁעַר מוֹדְיָא אַחַת, וּבָדְקוּ, וּמָצְאוּ אָב וּבְנוֹ שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל נַעֲרָה מְאוֹרָסָה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים; וֶהֱבִיאוּם לְבֵית דִּין וּסְקָלוּם, וְחָזַר הַשַּׁעַר לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Another** Sage **began** his remarks **and said: It once happened that** the market price of **forty *se'a*** of grain **stood at one dinar.** And then **the rate went down one *se'a* [*modeya*],** so that only thirty-nine *se'a* were sold for a dinar. **And they checked** to see what sin had caused this, **and they found a father and son who had engaged in sexual intercourse with a betrothed young woman on Yom Kippur. They brought** the offenders **to court and stoned them, and the rate returned to its** former **level.**

*Patach idach ve-amar: maaseh ve-amdu arbaim modiyot be-dinar* — the second rabbi opened and told: there was a time in this town when forty measures of grain could be bought for a dinar — grain was plentiful and cheap, a sign of prosperity. Then *nechsar ha-sha'ar* — the rate dropped, so that now only thirty-nine measures could be bought for a dinar. They investigated the cause. *U-vadeku u-matzu av u-veno she-vau al naara meurasah be-yom ha-kippurim* — and they found a father and son who had committed adultery with a betrothed young woman on Yom Kippur. The gravity of this sin is compounded at every level: the forbidden relationship, the sacred day, the violation of a betrothed woman, and the fact that it was father and son together. *Ve-heviyu le-veit din ve-sakalu* — they brought them to *beit din* and stoned them. *Ve-chazar ha-sha'ar li-mkomo* — and the grain price returned to its prior level. So thorough was their communal responsibility that even a single transgression had a measurable effect on the prosperity of the entire town — and so thorough was their justice that when the sin was addressed, the effect was reversed.

### False Accusation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַח אִידַּךְ וַאֲמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ, וְהָיְתָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה. מָה עָשָׂה? הָלַךְ וְזִימֵּן אֶת שׁוֹשְׁבִינָיו וְהֶאֱכִילָן וְהִשְׁקָן, שִׁיכְּרָן וְהִשְׁכִּיבָן עַל מִיטָּה אַחַת, וְהֵבִיא לוֹבֶן בֵּיצָה וְהִטִּיל בֵּינֵיהֶן, וְהֶעֱמִיד לָהֶן עֵדִים, וּבָא לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Yet **another** Sage **began** his remarks **and said:** There was **an incident** there **involving a man who set his eyes upon his wife to divorce her, but her marriage contract was large** and he wished to avoid having to pay it. **What did he do? He went and invited his friends, gave them food and drink, made them drunk, and lay** his friends and his wife **in one bed. He** then **brought the white of an egg,** which has the appearance of semen, **and placed it** on the sheet **between them. He** then **stood witnesses over them** so that they could offer testimony, **and went to court** claiming that his wife had committed adultery.

*Patach idach ve-amar: maaseh be-adam echad she-natan einav be-ishto le-garesha* — the third rabbi told: a man wished to divorce his wife. *Ve-hayetah ketubatah merubah* — but her *kesuva* was very large, and he sought a way to avoid paying it. He arranged to manufacture evidence against her. *Halach ve-ziman et shoshvinav* — he invited his friends. *Ve-he'echilan ve-hishkan* — he fed them and plied them with drink until they were drunk. *Ve-hishkivan al mita achat* — he had them all lie together on one bed. *Ve-hevi loven beitza ve-hitipah beineihen* — he brought egg white and placed it among them, making it appear as semen. *Ve-he'emid lahen eidim* — and he stationed witnesses to observe. He then came before *beit din* claiming that these men had committed adultery with his wife. The staging of the scene recalls something almost theatrical in its calculated wickedness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שָׁם זָקֵן אֶחָד מִתַּלְמִידֵי שַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן, וּבָבָא בֶּן בּוּטָא שְׁמוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶן, כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִשַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן: לוֹבֶן בֵּיצָה סוֹלֵד מִן הָאוּר, וְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע דּוֹחָה מִן הָאוּר. בָּדְקוּ וּמָצְאוּ כִּדְבָרָיו, וֶהֱבִיאוּהוּ לְבֵית דִּין וְהִלְקוּהוּ, וְהִגְבּוּהוּ כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain Elder of the disciples of Shammai the Elder was there, and Bava ben Buta** was **his name. He said to them: This is** the tradition that **I received from Shammai the Elder: Egg white** on a bedsheet **contracts** and hardens when heated **by fire, whereas semen is absorbed** into the sheet **by the fire. They checked** the matter **and found in accordance with his statement** that the substance on the sheet was not semen but egg white. **They** then **brought** the husband **to court, administered lashes to him, and made him pay** his wife's **marriage contract** in full.

*Havah behu hahu Bar Droma* — there was present one elder from the disciples of Shammai the Elder, Bava ben Buta. *Amar lahem: kach mekubalni mi-Shammai ha-Zaken, loven beitza soled min ha-or, ve-shichvat zera docha min ha-or* — he said: this is the tradition I received from Shammai the Elder: egg white, when exposed to heat, shrivels and contracts, while semen is absorbed into cloth when brought near fire. *U-vadeku u-matzu ki-dvarav* — they tested it and found exactly as he had said: it was egg white, not semen. *Ve-heviuhu le-veit din ve-hilkuhu* — the husband was brought before *beit din* and flogged. *Ve-higbu ketubatah mimenu* — and they compelled him to pay his wife's *kesuva*.

The progression of the three stories is instructive. The first speaks to the personal righteousness of individuals. The second and third speak to the communal structures of justice: the *beit din* acting swiftly and correctly, the town taking full responsibility for the sins committed within its bounds. This stands in sharp contrast to the rabbinic failures described earlier in the tractate, where the leadership of Jerusalem saw what was unfolding and did not act.

### Abaye Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: וּמֵאַחַר דַּהֲווֹ צַדִּיקִים כּוּלֵּי הַאי, מַאי טַעְמָא אִיעֲנוּשׁ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אִיאַבּוּל עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׂמְחוּ אֶת יְרוּשָׁלִַם וְגִילוּ בָהּ כׇּל אוֹהֲבֶיהָ, שִׂישׂוּ אִתָּהּ מָשׂוֹשׂ כׇּל הַמִּתְאַבְּלִים עָלֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: But since** those in the city **were so righteous, what is the reason that they were punished** and destroyed? Rav Yosef **said to him:** It is **because they did not mourn for Jerusalem, as it is written: "Rejoice with Jerusalem, and be glad with her, all you that love her, rejoice with joy with her, all you that did mourn for her"** (Isaiah 66:10). The verse teaches that one who mourns for Jerusalem will rejoice in its rebuilding, and one who fails to mourn for Jerusalem is destroyed.

*Amar leih Abaye le-Rav Yosef: ve-achar de-havu tzaddikim kulei hai* — Abaye asked Rav Yosef: if they were so righteous, why were they punished?

### No Mourning

*Amar leih: mishum de-lo iabalu al Yerushalayim* — because they did not mourn over the destruction of Jerusalem. As the verse says: *simchu et Yerushalayim ve-gilu vah kol ohaveha, sisu itah masos kol ha-mitablim aleha* — rejoice with Jerusalem and exult in her, all who love her; be glad with her in gladness, all who mourned for her. Only those who share in the mourning will share in the rejoicing.

This is a historically grounded observation. The *Churban* was felt differently depending on proximity and circumstance. How much did a community in the Galilee, far from Jerusalem, truly feel the destruction of the *Beis ha-Mikdash* as having altered their daily lives? One can imagine a town — and Nazareth is in the Galilee, far from Jerusalem — that was genuinely righteous in its internal conduct but that did not cultivate the larger national and communal sense of loss. Highly responsible within their own *arba amos*, their own village, but without the broader sense that they were part of something larger whose destruction demanded mourning. This is the critique the *Gemara* levels at them: personal and communal righteousness alone is not sufficient. One must also feel the loss of *Yerushalayim* as one's own.

## Beitar Cedar Trees

### Tree Planting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשָּׁקָא דְרִיסְפַּק חֲרִיב בֵּיתֵּר: דַּהֲווֹ נְהִיגִי כִּי הֲוָה מִתְיְלִיד יָנוֹקָא – שָׁתְלִי אַרְזָא, יָנוֹקְתָּא – שָׁתְלִי תּוּרְנִיתָא; וְכִי הֲווֹ מִינַּסְבִי, קָיְיצִי לְהוּ וְעָבְדִי גְּנָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated earlier that the city of **Beitar was destroyed on account of a shaft from a carriage.** The Gemara explains that **it was customary** in Beitar that **when a boy was born they would plant a cedar** tree and when **a girl** was born they **would plant a cypress [*tornita*]. And when they would** later **marry** each other **they would cut** down these trees **and construct** a wedding **canopy** for them with their branches.

The *Gemara* now moves to the destruction of *Beitar*. *De-hava nahigi ki avei misyalid yanuka* — the practice there was: when a baby boy was born, *shatlei arza* — they would plant a cedar tree; *yanukta* — when a baby girl was born, *shatlei turnisa* — they would plant a pine or cypress tree. It is a beautiful *minhag*, rooting the lives of the children literally in the land.

### Trees for Chuppah

*Ve-ki aveu minsevei* — when the boy and girl grew up and married, *kaitzei lo* — they would cut down their respective trees *ve-avdu ganana* — and construct a *chuppah* from them. The marriage canopy was thus built from trees planted at the moment of birth, intertwining the couple's origins with the culmination of their lives together.

### Caesar's Axle Breaks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָא חָלְפָא בְּרַתֵּיה דְּקֵיסָר, אִתְּבַר שָׁקָא דְרִיסְפַּק. קַצּוּ אַרְזָא וְעַיִּילוּ לַהּ. אֲתוֹ נְפוּל עֲלַיְיהוּ מְחוֹנְהוּ. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְקֵיסָר: מְרַדוּ בָּךְ יְהוּדָאֵי! אֲתָא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One day the emperor's daughter passed by** there and **the shaft of the carriage** in which she was riding **broke.** Her attendants **chopped down a cedar** from among those trees **and brought it to her.** Owing to the importance that they attached to their custom, the residents of Beitar **came** and **fell upon them and beat them.** The attendants **came** and **said to the emperor: The Jews have rebelled against you.** The emperor then **came against them** in war.

*Yoma chad avei kachalfa bartei de-Keisar* — one day Caesar's daughter was passing through *Beitar* when *itbar ashaka de-rispak* — the axle of her carriage broke. *Katzu arza* — the soldiers cut down a nearby cedar tree to repair the carriage.

### Jews Defend Tree

*Asu naflu alaihu* — the Jews of *Beitar* attacked the Romans for cutting down the tree. *Machun* — they beat them. *Asu amri leh le-Keisar: mardu bach Yehuda'ei* — they went and told Caesar that the Jews were rebelling. *Asu alaihu* — and Caesar came with his army.

Once again, the inability to absorb even the slightest disruption to their traditional practices — however beautiful those practices were — proved catastrophic. The very qualities that made the community admirable in peacetime, their rootedness in custom and land, could not accommodate the realities of Roman occupation.

### Beitar Destroyed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גָּדַע בׇּחֳרִי אַף כֹּל קֶרֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵלּוּ שְׁמוֹנִים [אֶלֶף] קַרְנֵי מִלְחָמָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לִכְרַךְ בֵּיתֵּר בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁלְּכָדוּהָ, וְהָרְגוּ בָּהּ אֲנָשִׁים וְנָשִׁים וָטַף, עַד שֶׁהָלַךְ דָּמָן וְנָפַל לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל. שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר קְרוֹבָה הָיְתָה? רְחוֹקָה הָיְתָה מִיל.
>
> **Translation:** It was in connection with the war that ensued that the Sages expounded the following verse: **"He has cut off in His fierce anger all the horn of Israel"** (Lamentations 2:3). **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: These are the eighty thousand** officers bearing **battle trumpets** in their hands, **who entered the city of Beitar when** the enemy **took it and killed men, women, and children until their blood flowed into the Great Sea. Lest you say** that the city **was close** to the sea, know that **it was a *mil* away.**

*Gada be-chori af kol keren Yisrael* — God cut down in fierce anger all the horn of Israel. *Amar Rav Zeira amar Rav Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan: eilu shemonim elef karnei milchamah she-nichnasu le-krach Beitar* — these are eighty thousand horns of war that entered the city of *Beitar* when they conquered it, *ve-hargu bah anashim ve-nashim ve-taf* — and they slaughtered men, women, and children.

### Blood Flows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁהָלַךְ דָּמָן וְנָפַל לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי נְחָלִים יֵשׁ בְּבִקְעַת יָדַיִם, אֶחָד מוֹשֵׁךְ אֵילָךְ, וְאֶחָד מוֹשֵׁךְ אֵילָךְ, וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים שְׁנֵי חֲלָקִים מַיִם וְאֶחָד דָּם. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים בָּצְרוּ גּוֹיִם אֶת כַּרְמֵיהֶן מִדָּמָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, בְּלֹא זֶבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** *until the blood* flowed all the way to the Mediterranean. Lest one think the sea was nearby, the *Gemara* notes: **It is** similarly **taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: There are two rivers in the Yadayim Valley** in that region, **one flowing one way and one flowing the other way. And the Sages estimated** that in the aftermath of this war these rivers were filled with **two parts water to one part blood.** Likewise, **it was taught in a *baraita*: For seven years the gentiles harvested their vineyards** that had been soaked **with the blood of Israel without** requiring any additional **fertilizer.**

*Ad she-halach daman ve-naful le-yama ha-gadol* — until the blood flowed all the way to the Mediterranean. Lest one think the sea was nearby, the *Gemara* notes: *rechokah hayetah mil* — it was a *mil* away, and the blood flowed that distance. *Tanya, Rabbi Eliezer ha-Gadol omer: shnei nechalim yesh be-Bikat Yadayim* — there are two wadis in *Bikat Yadayim*, near *Beitar*, one flowing one direction and one the other, *ve-shi'aru chachamim shnei chalakim mayim ve-echad dam* — and the sages estimated that at that time these wadis ran two-thirds water and one-third blood. *Ve-masnista tana: sheva shanim batzru ovdei kochavim et karmeihem mi-daman shel Yisrael belo zevel* — and the *braita* teaches that for seven years afterward the non-Jews harvested their vineyards fertilized by the blood of Israel without any additional manure. The image is searing, and one cannot help but think of what is known about the soil of Auschwitz.

## Zechariah's Blood

### Elder's Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, סָח לִי זָקֵן אֶחָד מֵאַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם: בְּבִקְעָה זוֹ הָרַג נְבוּזַרְאֲדָן רַב טַבָּחִים מָאתַיִם וְאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה רִבּוֹא, וּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם הָרַג תִּשְׁעִים וְאַרְבַּע רִבּוֹא עַל אֶבֶן אַחַת, עַד שֶׁהָלַךְ דָּמָן וְנָגַע בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל זְכַרְיָה, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדָמִים בְּדָמִים נָגְעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the Babylonian exile following the destruction of the First Temple, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: An old man from** among **the inhabitants of Jerusalem related to me: In this valley** that lies before you, **Nebuzaradan, captain of the guard** of the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar, **killed 2,110,000** people. **And in Jerusalem** itself **he killed 940,000** people **on one stone, until the blood** of his victims **flowed and touched the blood of Zechariah to fulfill what is stated: "And blood touches blood"** (Hosea 4:2).

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: sach li zaken echad mei-anshei Yerushalayim* — a certain elder from the men of Jerusalem told him: *be-vika zu harag Nevuzaradan Rav Tabachim* — in this valley Nevuzaradan, the head of the butchers, Nebuchadnezzar's chief general, slaughtered 211 myriads — 2.1 million — and in Jerusalem itself, 940,000. *Al even achas, ad she-halach daman ve-naga be-damo shel Zechariah* — all of them on one stone, until their blood reached and joined the blood of Zechariah. This Zechariah was a *kohein* who had been murdered by order of King Yoash because he refused to stop rebuking the people in the name of God — not the *Zechariah ha-Navi* of the *Tanach* who returned from Babylonian exile and figures in the second *Beis ha-Mikdash* period, but an earlier figure. *Ve-damim be-damim nagu* — and the bloods met one another, fulfilling the verse.

### Blood Bubbles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לִדְמֵיהּ דִּזְכַרְיָה, דַּהֲוָה קָא מִרְתַח וְסָלֵיק, אֲמַר: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דַּם זְבָחִים דְּאִשְׁתְּפוּךְ. אַיְיתִי דְּמֵי וְלָא אִידְּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the details of what happened: Nebuzaradan **found the blood of Zechariah,** the son of Jehoiada the priest, and saw **that it was bubbling up** from the ground, and **he said: What is this?** Those in the Temple **said to him:** It is **sacrificial blood that had been poured** there. **He brought** animal **blood,** compared it to the blood bubbling up from the ground, **and** saw that **it was not similar** to it.

*Ashkechei le-damei de-Zechariah, de-hava kamartach ve-salik* — Nevuzaradan, upon entering the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, found the blood of Zechariah bubbling on the floor. *Amar: mai hai* — he asked, what is this? *Amru lei: dam zvachim de-ishtapuch* — the Jews told him it was sacrificial blood that had been spilled. *Ayise damma ve-lo dami* — he brought animal blood for comparison, and it did not match. He knew they were lying.

### Zechariah Confession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי אָמְרִיתוּ לִי – מוּטָב, וְאִי לָאו – מְסָרֵיקְנָא לְבִשְׂרַיְיכוּ בְּמַסְרְקֵי דְפַרְזְלֵי. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי נֵימָא לָךְ? נְבִיָּיא הֲוָה בַּן דַּהֲוָה קָא מוֹכַח לַן בְּמִילֵּי דִשְׁמַיָּא, קַמְינַן עִילָּוֵיהּ וּקְטַלְינַן לֵיהּ, וְהָא כַּמָּה שְׁנִין דְּלָא קָא נָיַיח דְּמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **said to** them: **If you tell me** whose blood this is, it will be **well** for you. **But if not, I will comb your flesh with iron combs. They said to him: What shall we say to you? He was a prophet among us, who used to rebuke us about heavenly matters,** and **we rose up against him, and killed him** (II Chronicles 24:20–22), **and for many years** now **his blood has not settled.**

*Amar lehu: ei amritu li mutav, ve-ei lav masrikna le-visraichu be-masreki de-farzla* — tell me the truth, or I will flay your flesh with iron combs. *Amru lei: navi haya banu de-hava kamocheach lan be-milei de-shmaya, kaminnu alavei ve-katlinan lei* — they confessed: there was a prophet among us who rebuked us with words of Heaven, and we rose up against him and killed him. *Ve-ha kamma shanin de-lo ka nayach damei* — and for many years his blood has not stopped bubbling.

### Sanhedrin Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מְפַיֵּיסְנָא לֵיהּ. אַיְיתִי סַנְהֶדְרִי גְּדוֹלָה וְסַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה קְטַל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. בַּחוּרִים וּבְתוּלוֹת קְטַל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. אַיְיתִי תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן קְטַל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זְכַרְיָה, זְכַרְיָה, טוֹבִים שֶׁבָּהֶן אִיבַּדְתִּים, נִיחָא לָךְ דְּאֹבְדִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ? כְּדַאֲמַר לֵיהּ הָכִי, נָח.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **said to them: I will appease** Zechariah. **He brought** the members of **the Great Sanhedrin and** of **a lesser Sanhedrin** and **killed them alongside** the bubbling blood, **but** it still **did not settle.** He then brought **young men and virgins and killed them alongside it, but** it still **did not settle. He** then **brought schoolchildren and killed them alongside it, but** it still **did not settle.** Finally Nebuzaradan **said to him: Zechariah, Zechariah, I have killed the best of them.** Would it **please you if I destroyed them all? When he said this,** the blood at last **settled.**

*Amar lehu: ana mefaysina lei* — Nevuzaradan said he would appease the blood. *Ayise Sanhedrin Gedolah ve-Sanhedrin Ketana ve-katlinhu alavei* — he slaughtered the Great Sanhedrin and the Small Sanhedrin over it, *ve-lo nach* — and the blood did not rest. The pattern of leadership bearing responsibility is reinforced: the very institutions of Jewish governance that had failed are the first to be called to account. *Bachurim u-vesulos ve-katlinhu alavei* — he slaughtered young men and young women over it, and still it did not rest. *Ayise tinokot shel beis rabban ve-katlinhu alavei* — he brought the schoolchildren and slaughtered them over the blood, and still it did not stop boiling.

### Blood Settles

*Amar lei: Zechariah, Zechariah, tovim she-bahen ibadtum* — Nevuzaradan said: Zechariah, Zechariah, I have destroyed the best among them. *Nicha lach de-avdinhu le-chulhu* — do you want me to wipe out all of them? *K'd-amar lei hachi, nach* — when he said this, the blood finally ceased to boil.

### Nevuzaradan Converts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הַרְהַר תְּשׁוּבָה בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: וּמָה אִם עַל נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת, כָּךְ; הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּקְטַל כׇּל הָנֵי נִשְׁמָתָא – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה! עֲרַק, אֲזַל שַׁדַּר שְׁטַר פִּרְטְתָא בְּבֵיתֵיהּ, וְאִגַּיַּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **At that moment** Nebuzaradan **contemplated** the idea of **repentance** and **said** to himself: **If, for** the death of **one soul,** that of Zechariah, God punishes the Jewish people in **this** manner, then **that man,** that is to say, I, **who has killed all of those souls, all the more so** will be I be subject to great punishment from God. **He fled, sent to his house a document detailing** what was to be done with his property, **and converted** to Judaism.

*U-ve-hahu sha'ata hirher teshuvah be-da'atei* — at that very moment, Nevuzaradan had a thought of *teshuvah* in his mind. He said: *u-mah al nefesh achas kach, hahu gavra de-katil kulei hanei nishmasa, al achas kamma ve-chamma* — if for one soul such vengeance is exacted, then I who have killed all these souls — how much more greatly will I be punished?

*Arak* — he fled. *Shadar shetar partisa le-veiseh* — he sent a document home to distribute his property and settle his affairs. *Ve-isgayar* — and he converted.

## Enemies Convert

### Naaman & Nev.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: נַעֲמָן גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב הָיָה; נְבוּזַרְאֲדָן גֵּר צֶדֶק הָיָה;
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** a *baraita* relating to this matter: **Naaman,** commander of the army of the king of Aram (see II Kings, chapter 5), was not a convert, as he did not accept all of the mitzvot, but rather he **was a *ger toshav*,** a gentile who resides in Eretz Israel and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot. **Nebuzaradan,** by contrast, **was a convert,** as explained previously.

*Tanu rabbanan: Naaman ger toshav haya* — the *Braita* teaches: Naaman, healed of his *tzara'at* by Elisha, became a *ger toshav* — he abandoned *avoda zara* but did not become a full Jew, as is evident from the plain meaning of the *psukim*. *Nevuzaradan ger tzedek havay* — but Nevuzaradan became a full convert. So the very general who destroyed the *Beis ha-Mikdash* in its first iteration ultimately entered the covenant of Israel.

### Haman's Line

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל הָמָן לִמְּדוּ תּוֹרָה בִּבְנֵי בְרַק; מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל סִיסְרָא לִמְּדוּ תִּינוֹקוֹת בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם; מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל סַנְחֵרִיב לִמְּדוּ תּוֹרָה בָּרַבִּים – מַאן אִינּוּן? שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that some **of Haman's descendants studied Torah in Bnei Brak,** and some **of Sisera's descendants taught children** Torah **in Jerusalem,** and some **of Sennacherib's descendants taught Torah in public. Who are they?** They are **Shemaya and Avtalyon,** the teachers of Hillel the Elder.

*Mi-bnei banav shel Haman lamdu Torah bi-Vnei Brak* — the grandchildren of Haman studied Torah in *Bnei Brak*. The descendants of the man who sought to annihilate the Jewish people are found learning Torah in one of the great centers of Jewish life.

### Sisra's Line

*Mi-bnei banav shel Sisra limdu tinokot bi-Yerushalayim* — the descendants of Sisra taught schoolchildren in Jerusalem.

### Sancheriv's Line

*Mi-bnei banav shel Sancheriv limdu Torah ba-rabbim* — and the descendants of Sennacherib taught Torah in public. *U-man inun? Shmaya ve-Avtalyon* — and who are these? Shemaya and Avtalyon, the great rabbinic leaders who were the teachers of Hillel and Shammai themselves. The enemies of Israel — Haman a descendant of *Amalek* — ultimately find their descendants not only entering the Jewish people but becoming its greatest teachers. There is a *teshuvah* of Rav Oshry, *She'eilot U-Tshuvot Mi-Ma'amakim*, written in the aftermath of the *Shoah*, in which the question of whether a German soldier consumed by guilt might be permitted to convert is addressed. The precedent of Nevuzaradan and the descendants of Haman are part of the broader conversation about whether even one who participated in such evil can find a path to repentance and entry into the Jewish people. The *Rambam* takes up related questions regarding whether *Amalek*, if they do *teshuvah*, can become converts.

## Yaakov & Esav

### Zechariah Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״נָתַתִּי אֶת דָּמָהּ עַל צְחִיחַ סָלַע לְבִלְתִּי הִכָּסוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** As for the incident involving the blood of Zechariah, **this is** alluded to by **that which is written: "I have set her blood upon the bare rock that it should not be covered"** (Ezekiel 24:8).

*Haynu de-ksiv: nasati et damayich al tzhiach sela le-vilti hikasos* — this is the verse: I have placed your blood on the bare rock so that it cannot be covered over. The blood of Zechariah could not be absorbed, could not be hidden, lying exposed on the floor of the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, demanding justice.

### Hadrian & Vespa.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַקּוֹל קוֹל יַעֲקֹב וְהַיָּדַיִם יְדֵי עֵשָׂו״ – ״הַקּוֹל״, זֶה אַדְרִיָּינוּס קֵיסָר, שֶׁהָרַג בַּאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם שִׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא עַל שִׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא, כִּפְלַיִם כְּיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם. ״קוֹל יַעֲקֹב״ – זֶה אַסְפַּסְיָינוּס קֵיסָר, שֶׁהָרַג בִּכְרַךְ בֵּיתֵּר אַרְבַּע מְאוֹת רִבּוֹא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים רִבּוֹא. ״וְהַיָּדַיִם יְדֵי עֵשָׂו״ – זוֹ מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה, שֶׁהֶחְרִיבָה אֶת בָּתֵּינוּ, וְשָׂרְפָה אֶת הֵיכָלֵנוּ, וְהִגְלְתָנוּ מֵאַרְצֵנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos its discussion of the destruction of the Temple and the calamities that befell Israel, the Gemara cites the verse: **"The voice is the voice of Jacob, but the hands are the hands of Esau"** (Genesis 27:22), which the Sages expounded as follows: **"The voice"; this** is the cry stirred up by **the emperor Hadrian, who** caused the Jewish people to cry out when he **killed six hundred thousand on six hundred thousand in Alexandria of Egypt, twice** the number of men **who left Egypt. "The voice of Jacob"; this is** the cry aroused by **the emperor Vespasian, who killed four million** people **in the city of Beitar. And some say:** He killed **forty million** people. **"And the hands are the hands of Esau"; this is the wicked kingdom** of Rome **that destroyed our Temple, burned our Sanctuary, and exiled us from our land.**

*Ha-kol kol Yaakov ve-ha-yadayim yedei Esav* — the voice is the voice of Jacob and the hands are the hands of Esau. The *Gemara* now interprets this verse in terms of the great destructions. *Ha-kol ze Adryanus Keisar she-harag be-Alexandrya shel Mitzrayim shishim ribo al shishim ribo* — the voice is Hadrian, who killed in Alexandria of Egypt 600,000 times 600,000, *kiflayim ke-yotzei Mitzrayim* — double those who went out of Egypt. Rashi explains: *ha-kol tzevacha u-vechi ola mi-Yaakov* — the voice, meaning the outcry and wailing, rises from Jacob, because of the hands of Esau. *Kol Yaakov ze Aspasyanus Keisar* — in another reading, the voice of Jacob refers to Vespasian, *she-harag bi-krach Beitar arba me'os ribo* — who killed in *Beitar* four million, *ve-amri lah arba'as alafim ribo* — or according to another version, forty million. *Ve-ha-yadayim yedei Esav zu malchus ha-resha'a she-hachriva et beisenu ve-sarfa et heichaleinu ve-higlisenu me-artzenu* — the hands of Esau, this is the evil kingdom of Rome, which destroyed our home, burned our sanctuary, and exiled us from our land.

### Prayer & War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הַקּוֹל קוֹל יַעֲקֹב״ – אֵין לְךָ תְּפִלָּה שֶׁמּוֹעֶלֶת, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב. ״וְהַיָּדַיִם יְדֵי עֵשָׂו״ – אֵין לְךָ מִלְחָמָה שֶׁנּוֹצַחַת, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל עֵשָׂו.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively, "the voice is the voice of Jacob"** means that **no prayer is effective** in the world **unless** some member of **the seed of Jacob has** a part **in it.** The second clause in the verse, **"and the hands are the hands of Esau,"** means that **no war grants victory unless** some member of **the seed of Esau has** a part **in it.**

*Davar acher: kol kol Yaakov, ein lecha tefilla she-mo'elet she-ein bo mi-zaro shel Yaakov* — another interpretation: you have no prayer that is effective unless it contains something of the descendants of Jacob. *Ve-ha-yadayim yedei Esav, ein lecha milchama she-notzachat she-ein bo mi-zaro shel Esav* — and you have no war that is won unless there is something from the descendants of Esau involved in it.

*Heinu de-amar Reb Elazar: be-shot lashon techavei, be-chirchurei lashon techavei* — and this is what Rabbi Elazar said: in the slander of the tongue it will be hidden, in the contentiousness of the tongue it will be hidden. Rashi connects this back to the story of *Kamtza u-Bar Kamtza* — the in-fighting, the *lashon hara*, that ultimately caused the destruction.

### Two Destructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל, שָׁם יָשַׁבְנוּ גַּם בָּכִינוּ בְּזׇכְרֵנוּ אֶת צִיּוֹן״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֶרְאָהוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדָוִד חוּרְבַּן בַּיִת רִאשׁוֹן וְחוּרְבַּן בַּיִת שֵׁנִי. חוּרְבַּן בַּיִת רִאשׁוֹן – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל שָׁם יָשַׁבְנוּ גַּם בָּכִינוּ״. בַּיִת שֵׁנִי – דִּכְתִיב: ״זְכוֹר ה׳ לִבְנֵי אֱדוֹם אֵת יוֹם יְרוּשָׁלִָים, הָאוֹמְרִים עָרוּ עָרוּ עַד הַיְסוֹד בָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "By the rivers of Babylonia, there we sat down and wept, when we remembered Zion"** (Psalms 137:1)? This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, showed David the destruction of the First Temple and the destruction of the Second Temple.** He saw the destruction of **the First Temple, as it is stated: "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept."** He saw the destruction of the **Second Temple, as it is written** later in that same psalm: **"Remember, O Lord, against the children of Edom the day of Jerusalem, when they said: Raze it, raze it, to its very foundation"** (Psalms 137:7), as the Second Temple was destroyed by the Romans, "the children of Edom."

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: mai de-chtiv, al naharot Bavel sham yashavnu gam bachinu be-zochreinu et Tziyon* — what does this verse mean? *Melamed she-her'ah HaKadosh Baruch Hu le-David churban bayit rishon ve-churban bayit sheni* — God showed David both the destruction of the First Temple and the destruction of the Second. *Churban bayit rishon: she-ne'emar al naharot Bavel* — the First Temple's destruction: *by the waters of Babylon*. *Bayit sheni: de-chtiv zechor Hashem li-vnei Edom et yom Yerushalayim ha-omrim aru aru ad ha-yesod bah* — the Second Temple: remember, God, against the children of Edom the day of Jerusalem when they said, destroy it, destroy it to its very foundation. The *bnei Edom* here is read not as the literal Edomites but as the figurative Edom — Rome — that destroyed the Second *Beis ha-Mikdash*.

## Four Hundred

### Children Captive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אַמֵּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאַרְבַּע מְאוֹת יְלָדִים וִילָדוֹת שֶׁנִּשְׁבּוּ לְקָלוֹן, הִרְגִּישׁוּ בְּעַצְמָן לְמָה הֵן מִתְבַּקְּשִׁים, אָמְרוּ: אִם אָנוּ טוֹבְעִין בַּיָּם – אָנוּ בָּאִים לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ami** who says this, **and some say** that **it was taught in a *baraita*:** There was **an incident involving four hundred boys and girls who were taken as captives for** the purpose of **prostitution.** These children **sensed on their own what they were expected** to do, and **they said: If we** commit suicide and **drown in the sea,** will **we come to** eternal **life in the World-to-Come?**

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, ve-iteima Reb Ami* — it was taught: *maaseh be-arba me'ot yeladim u-me'ot yeladot she-nishbu le-kalon* — four hundred boys and girls were taken captive for degradation — meaning they were to be used for sexual purposes by their Roman captors. *Hirgishu be-atzmam lema hen mitbakshim* — they sensed why they were being taken.

### Eldest Interprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ לָהֶן הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶן: ״אָמַר ה׳ מִבָּשָׁן אָשִׁיב, אָשִׁיב מִמְּצוּלוֹת יָם״ – ״מִבָּשָׁן אָשִׁיב״, מִבֵּין שִׁינֵּי אַרְיֵה; ״אָשִׁיב מִמְּצוּלוֹת יָם״, אֵלּוּ שֶׁטּוֹבְעִין בַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** They asked: **"if we were to drown ourselves in the sea, would we come to the world to come?"** The oldest child **among them expounded** the verse: **"The Lord said, I will bring back from Bashan, I will bring them back from the depths of the sea"** (Psalms 68:23). **"I will bring back from Bashan,"** i.e., **from between the teeth [*bein shen*] of the lion,** and **"I will bring them back from the depths of the sea"** is referring to **those who drown in the sea** for the sake of Heaven.

They asked: *im anu tov'im ba-yam anu ba'im le-chayei ha-olam ha-ba* — if we were to drown ourselves in the sea, would we attain the world to come? *Darash lahen ha-gadol she-bahen* — the eldest interpreted for them the following verse: *Amar Hashem mi-Bashan ashiv, ashiv mi-metzulot yam* — the Lord said, from the Bashan I will bring back, I will bring back from the depths of the sea. *Mi-Bashan ashiv: mi-bein shinei arayot ashiv* — from the Bashan, meaning from the teeth of the lions. *Mi-metzulot yam: elu she-tvu'im ba-yam* — from the depths of the sea, these are those who drown themselves in the sea. God declares that He will bring back those who give their lives to the deep rather than surrender themselves to the lions — Rome.

### Children Jump

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ יְלָדוֹת כָּךְ, קָפְצוּ כּוּלָּן וְנָפְלוּ לְתוֹךְ הַיָּם. נָשְׂאוּ יְלָדִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמָן, וְאָמְרוּ: מָה הַלָּלוּ, שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לְכָךְ – כָּךְ; אָנוּ, שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכֵּנוּ לְכָךְ – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה! אַף הֵם קָפְצוּ לְתוֹךְ הַיָּם. וַעֲלֵיהֶם הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי עָלֶיךָ הֹרַגְנוּ כׇל הַיּוֹם, נֶחְשַׁבְנוּ כְּצֹאן טִבְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **When the girls heard this, they all leapt and fell into the sea. The boys** then **drew an *a fortiori*** inference **with regard to themselves and said: If these** girls, **for whom** sexual intercourse with men **is their natural way,** act in **such** a manner, then **we, for whom** sexual intercourse with men **is not our natural way,** should **all the more so** conduct ourselves likewise. **They too leapt into the sea. Concerning them** and others like them **the verse states: "As For Your sake we are killed all the day long; we are reckoned as sheep for the slaughter"** (Psalms 44:23).

*Kivan she-shamu yeladot kach, kafzu kulan ve-naflu le-toch ha-yam* — when the girls heard this interpretation, they all jumped and fell into the sea. *Nas'u yeladim kal va-chomer be-atzmam* — the boys then made a *kal va-chomer* upon themselves: *ve-amru: u-ma halalu she-darkan le-kach, kach anu kach* — if the girls, for whom this form of abuse is in some sense more expected, were willing to do this, *anu she-ein darkanu le-kach, al achat kama ve-chamma* — we, who would suffer what is even less natural, how much more so. *Af hen kafzu le-toch ha-yam* — they too jumped into the sea.

### Sheep Verse

*Ve-aleihem ha-katuv omer: ki alecha horagnu kol ha-yom, nechshavnu ke-tzon tivcha* — and upon them the verse says: for Your sake we are killed all the day; we are counted as sheep to the slaughter.

### Halakhic Debate

This *midrash* is of considerable *halachic* significance. *Tosafot* and the *chachmei Ashkenaz* drew upon it in the context of the Crusader persecutions, when Jews faced the prospect of forced baptism without any direct threat of death. The question is: is one permitted — or perhaps even obligated — to take one's own life in order to avoid being forced to commit *aveira*? The doctrine of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* applies when the choice offered by the oppressor is: convert or die. But what if the oppressor will not kill you, only forcibly sin against you? May one actively choose death in that circumstance?

Haym Soloveitchik argued at length that there is no sound *halachic* basis for this, and that *Tosafot*'s reliance on *midrashim* like this one reflects a post-facto rationalization of what Jews had already done in extremis, rather than a genuine *halachic* derivation. His argument begins from the premise that *midrashim* are not a legitimate source for *psak halacha*.

One may question this premise at its root. Even conceding that a *midrash* is not a standard *halachic* text in the way a *sugya* of *Gemara* is, it nonetheless expresses the values and attitudes of *Chazal*. One cannot read this *midrash* without sensing clearly that *Chazal* regarded what these children did as not merely understandable but admirable — perhaps even praiseworthy. And if one knows what *Chazal*'s attitude toward a phenomenon is, that cannot be entirely excluded from the way one reads *halachic* texts bearing on the same phenomenon, even if the *midrash* itself does not constitute direct *halachic* evidence.

*Tosafot* on this *Gemara* reads: *kafzu kulan ve-naflu le-toch ha-yam*. The countertext they must address is the story of Rabbi Chanina ben Teradyon, who when being burned said *mutav she-yitla mi she-netana ve-al yachavel be-atzmo* — better that God should take my soul and I should not injure myself. *Tosafot* explains the distinction: *hachi yire'im hayu mi-yisurim* — these children feared the torture. *De-amrinan: ilmale nigdu le-Chananya Mishael ve-Azarya palchu le-tzalma* — for we are told that had Chananya, Mishael, and Azarya been tortured, they would have worshipped the idol. The fear is not of death but of capitulation under torture. Furthermore, *Tosafot* notes: *ve-od al korcham hayu me'anim otam ve-lo hayu horgim otam klal* — in addition, they could have been raped without being killed. If the oppressor will neither kill them nor offer them the option of honoring *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, and if the only escape from the sin is death, then *Tosafot* holds that what these children did was at least an acceptable course of action.

This remains an important *Gemara* in the larger conversation about martyrdom, the limits of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, and the circumstances — if any — under which *halacha* regards self-destruction as other than forbidden.
