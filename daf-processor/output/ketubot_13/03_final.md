# Ketubot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Virginity Dispute

### Claims Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וְלֹא מָצָא לָהּ בְּתוּלִים. הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: מִשֶּׁאֵרַסְתַּנִי נֶאֱנַסְתִּי, וְנִסְתַּחֲפָה שָׂדֵהוּ. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁלֹּא אֵרַסְתִּיךָ, וְהָיָה מִקָּחִי מִקָּח טָעוּת — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּין, אֶלָּא הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת בְּעוּלָה עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְאָרֵס, וְהִטְעַתּוּ, עַד שֶׁתָּבִיא רְאָיָה לִדְבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a case of one **who marries a woman and did not find her hymen** intact, and **she says: After you betrothed me I was raped, and his,** i.e., her husband's, **field was inundated,** meaning that it is his misfortune that she is not a virgin, as she was raped after betrothal. **And he says: No; rather,** you were raped **before I betrothed you, and my transaction was a mistaken transaction. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives; rather, this** woman assumes **the presumptive status of one who engaged in intercourse when she was not yet betrothed and she misled him, until she brings proof** supporting **her statement.**

The Mishnah that opens our discussion on 12b is the culmination of a theme that has run through the tractate from its very first pages. From the outset, Ketubot has been concerned with the day of the wedding and the morning appearance in court, with the varying amounts of the *kesubah* depending on the woman's status. Now we finally arrive at the scenario that makes all of that relevant: the morning after the wedding, when a dispute erupts.

The case is introduced as follows: *hanoise es ha'isha velo matza lah besulim* — he married a woman and found no evidence of virginity. Notably, in this case the wife does not dispute the underlying fact; she does not claim she was a virgin at the time of the wedding. Instead, the debate is over the interpretation of events. She says, *mishe'erastani ne'enasti* — "After our *kiddushin*, I was raped." In the striking idiom of the Mishnah: *venistachfa sadehu* — "his field was flooded." The imagery is vivid if jarring; the point is that something happened to him, an event that befell his property after he had already acquired it, and therefore she is still entitled to the full *kesubah* of 200.

The husband's counter-claim is equally direct: *lo ki ela ad shelo erastich* — "No, you were not a virgin even before the *kiddushin*." And with that he invokes the concept of *mekach taus* — the entire transaction was entered into under a misapprehension. The precise implications of *mekach taus* for the validity of the *kiddushin* itself remain complex, but at the very least the husband is arguing that he owes nothing close to 200, and perhaps owes nothing at all.

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives**

The Mishnah records a three-way Tannaitic debate. *Rabban Gamliel ve'Rabbi Eliezer omrim ne'emenes* — Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say she is believed. Rabbi Yehoshua demurs sharply: *lo mipiha anu chayim* — "We do not live from her mouth, from her words alone." In his view, *harei zu bechezkas be'ula ad shelo tisarast* — she carries the presumption of having been a *be'ulah* before the *kiddushin*, and she has deceived him. Until she brings actual proof of her claim, that presumption stands against her.

### Monetary Framework

One natural way to frame this dispute is through the lens of *dinei mamonos*. She holds a *shtar* — her *kesubah* — which she claims is worth 200. He disputes that valuation and contends it is worth 100 or perhaps zero. When there is a monetary dispute of this kind and no clear evidence, the governing principle should be *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the party seeking to extract money from another's possession bears the burden of proof. The Gemara's initial approach to our Mishnah is precisely this — to apply that rule and determine who, on that basis, should prevail.

## Bari vs. Shema

### Definite vs. Doubtful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ — רַב יְהוּדָה וְרַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי: חַיָּיב, וְרַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי: פָּטוּר. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי חַיָּיב — בָּרִי וְשֶׁמָּא בָּרִי עָדִיף. רַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי פָּטוּר — אוֹקִי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated:** With regard to one who approaches another and says: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says: I don't know, Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna say:** The respondent is **obligated** to pay, because he did not deny the claim, **and Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** He is **exempt** from payment. The Gemara elaborates. **Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda say** that the respondent is **obligated** to pay based on the principle: When there is a **certain** claim, e.g., that of the claimant, **and an uncertain** claim, e.g., that of the respondent, the **certain** claim **prevails. Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** The respondent is **exempt** based on the principle: **Establish the money in the possession of its owner,** and the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. Since the claimant does not support his claim with proof, the money remains in the possession of the respondent.

The Gemara now introduces a parallel case to sharpen the analysis. *Maneh li beyadcha vehalla omer eini yodea* — one person says to another, "You owe me a hundred," and the other responds, "I don't know." This is to be distinguished from the case where the second party concedes the debt but claims he may have repaid it; there, everyone agrees he must pay, since he acknowledges the obligation without being able to affirm its discharge. Here, however, the claimant has a *bari* — a definite, certain claim — while the respondent holds a *shema* — a position of genuine uncertainty.

### Definite Prevails

Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna rule: *chayiv* — the respondent must pay. Their principle is *bari ve'shema bari adif*: when a definite claim confronts an uncertain one, the definite claim prevails.

### Possession Rule

Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan rule: *patur* — the respondent is exempt. Their principle is *oki mamona bechezkas mara* — money remains in the possession of the one currently holding it, and to extract it requires concrete proof. The *hamotzi mechavero* rule stands.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה — דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הִיא. דִּתְנַן: הָיְתָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ: מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר זֶה? מֵאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי, וְכֹהֵן הוּא — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: This** ruling **of Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda is** essentially the statement **of Shmuel, as we learned** in a mishna (13a): In the case of an unmarried woman **who was pregnant, and** the Sages **said to her: What is the nature of this fetus,** i.e., who is the father. And she says: It is **from a man** called **so-and-so and he is a priest** and is certainly of valid lineage. **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible,** and the fetus is deemed to be of valid lineage. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel.**

At this point, Abaye reports to Rav Yosef a tradition in the name of Shmuel: the ruling of Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna — that the *bari* claim prevails — is in fact the position of Shmuel. The proof is from the very Mishnah we will be examining on 13a.

The Mishnah there presents a case of a single woman who is pregnant. She is asked *ma tivo shel ubar zeh* — what is the nature of this fetus, meaning: is there any concern that this child is a *mamzer* or otherwise *pasul*? She responds: *me'ish ploni ve'kohen hu* — the father is so-and-so, and he is a *kohen*, meaning a man of unblemished status. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say she is believed, both regarding her own personal status and presumably the fetus's. Rabbi Yehoshua says she is not believed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה. מַאי אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה? אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר אוֹקֵי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ — אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בָּרִי עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said to Rav Yehuda: Big-toothed one [*shinnana*], you said to us in the name of Shmuel** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel even in the first** dispute, the dispute cited in the mishna, which is the first in a series of disputes with regard to conflicting claims. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **even the first?** What is unique about that dispute relative to the others? The Gemara answers: The novel element in the first dispute is that the claim of the bride is accepted **despite the fact that** although **there is** room **to say: Establish the money in the possession of its owner,** and since the money is in the possession of the husband and the woman is the claimant, **Rabban Gamliel said** that the **certain** claim of the bride **prevails** over the uncertain claim of the groom, who can only speculate about when she was raped.

And Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *halacha ke'Rabban Gamliel* — we rule like Rabban Gamliel. Then Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda pressed Rav Yehuda further, calling him *Shinena* — "sharp one" — and reminded him that Shmuel had said *halacha ke'Rabban Gamliel af berishona*, meaning: Shmuel rules like Rabban Gamliel not only in the case of personal status, but even in the *first* Mishnah — the monetary dispute about the *kesubah* value. And what makes that first case a larger *chiddush*? Because there, one could have said *okim mamona bechezkas marei* — keep the money where it is. Despite that, Shmuel rules for her.

This gives us the conclusion: *amar Rabban Gamliel bari adif* — even against the *chezkas mamona* of the husband, her *bari* claim carries the day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב יְהוּדָה וְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְרַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? אָמַר לְךָ רַב נַחְמָן: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי, אֲפִילּוּ כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּאִיכָּא מִגּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that it is **Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna who say** their ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel,** who says that a certain claim prevails over an uncertain one even to collect money from the possession of the respondent. **And** it is **Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan who say** their ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** that one does not collect money based merely on a claim. The Gemara rejects that suggestion. **Rav Naḥman** could have **said to you:** That **which I said, is even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel. Rabban Gamliel says his ruling only there,** with regard to claims of a groom and a bride, **where there is a *miggo*,** a halakhic argument that the ability to make a more advantageous claim grants credibility to the claim one actually makes, that bolsters the bride's claim. She could have claimed that she wasn't raped at all, but rather that her hymen was ruptured by wood. That is a more advantageous claim because she is not disgraced in the eyes of the groom. Therefore, her claim that she was raped is accorded credibility. **However here,** where one claims that another owes him money, **what *miggo* is there** bolstering his claim and according it credibility?

The Gemara then asks directly: perhaps the dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Huna on one side and Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan on the other is simply a reprise of the Tannaitic dispute in our Mishnah — Rabban Gamliel versus Rabbi Yehoshua? To this question, Rav Nachman himself responds: the positions need not collapse into one another, and he can agree with Rabban Gamliel in our Mishnah even while maintaining his general rule. This is the burden of the next section.

## Reconciling Positions

### Migo & Chazakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם אֶלָּא דְּאָמְרִינַן אוֹקְמַהּ אַחֲזָקָה, אֲבָל הָכָא, מַאי חֲזָקָה אִית לֵיהּ לְהַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** Rav Naḥman could have said to you: **Rabban Gamliel says his ruling only there, where we say: Establish her** legal status according to her **presumptive status** as a virgin, and the husband's claim seeks to undermine that presumptive status. **However here, what presumptive status does this** claimant **have** supporting the claim that another owes him money? Therefore, even Rabban Gamliel would concede that his certain claim does not prevail.

Rav Nachman explains: *ad kan lo ka'amar Rabban Gamliel hatam ela d'ika migo* — Rabban Gamliel's ruling in the Mishnah is not simply about *bari adif* in the abstract. It is grounded in additional considerations that are particular to this case. Specifically, the woman has a *migo*. A *migo* is the principle that if a party could have made a stronger or more favorable claim, the fact that she chose a lesser or more vulnerable one gives that claim additional credibility.

### Could Lie Better

What is her *migo* here? As we shall see, the next Mishnah involves the claim of *mukas etz* — that a woman's non-virginal state results not from intercourse but from a physical injury. In the first Mishnah, the wife is not claiming *mukas etz*; she is conceding she was raped. Had she wanted to lie in a more self-serving way, she could have claimed *mukas etz*, which would have affected her status less adversely. The fact that she volunteered the more damaging claim — that she was raped — lends her words greater credibility. She had a better lie available and chose not to tell it.

Alternatively — or additionally — she holds a *chazakah*. Every person is born with the presumption of whatever status they began with. A woman is born a virgin, and therefore we maintain that presumption until the last possible moment. If we are uncertain when her virginity was lost, we push that event as late as we can — meaning after the *kiddushin*, which supports her claim.

Neither of these factors operates in the simple *maneh li beyadcha* case. The person demanding money has no *migo* — there is no "better lie" he declined to tell — and he has no *chazakah* of a previous debt. Hence Rav Nachman can say: by the clean *bari ve'shema* case, I maintain *okim mamona bechezkas mara*; but in our Mishnah, there are the additional supports of *migo* and *chazakah*, which justify the ruling of Rabban Gamliel independently of any general *bari adif* principle.

It is worth noting that Tosafos develops an even more textured analysis here by distinguishing between a *bari tov* and a *bari garu'a*, and between a *shema tov* and a *shema garu'a*. Not all *bari* claims are of equal strength, and not all *shema* positions are of equal weakness. In our Mishnah, the wife's *bari* is in one sense weak — she is the only one who knows the truth and faces no real check on her ability to lie — while the husband's *shema* is also correspondingly weak, since he really should have some independent knowledge of the situation. Tosafos notes that this actually makes our Mishnah a *bigger chiddush* than the simple *maneh li beyadcha* case, rather than a smaller one. But that analysis belongs to a deeper level of the discussion.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא כִּדְקָא מְשַׁנֵּינַן, דְּרַב נַחְמָן הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּאִם כֵּן, קַשְׁיָא הִלְכְתָא אַהִלְכְתָא. דְּקַיְימָא לַן הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב נַחְמָן בְּדִינֵי, וּבְהָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Indeed,** it is **also reasonable** to explain **as we are teaching, that it is Rav Naḥman who said** his ruling **in accordance** with the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel. Because if** that were not **so,** it is **difficult,** as there would be a contradiction between one ***halakha*** and another ***halakha*. Since,** on the one hand, **we maintain** a principle in halakhic ruling that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman in civil law; and,** on the other hand, **in that** case involving the claims of the bride and the groom **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel.**

The Gemara confirms that this answer is sound: *hach nami mistabra kidimishanidin* — it makes good sense the way we have answered. The proof is structural: we rule like Rav Nachman in monetary cases, which means our baseline *psak* is *lav bari adif*. Yet we also rule like Rabban Gamliel in our Mishnah, as Shmuel established. The only way to hold both positions without contradiction is to say that our Mishnah is a special case — that it is the *migo* or the *chazakah* (or both) that tips the scales for the woman here, not a general *bari adif* rule. *Shema mina* — this is a sound proof of that resolution.

The Gemara concludes by noting the practical ruling: *hilchesa* — the law — follows this structure, holding like Rav Nachman in general monetary disputes while affirming Rabban Gamliel's ruling in the specific context of our Mishnah.

## Mukat Etz Case

### Competing Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: מוּכַּת עֵץ אֲנִי. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא דְּרוּסַת אִישׁ אַתְּ. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים, אֶלָּא הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת דְּרוּסַת אִישׁ, עַד שֶׁתָּבִיא רְאָיָה לִדְבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where **she says: I** am one whose hymen was **ruptured by wood,** i.e., she admits that her hymen is not intact but claims that it was not ruptured through intercourse, **and** the groom **says: No; rather, you are** one who was **trampled by a man,** and your hymen was ruptured through intercourse, **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible** and her claim is accepted because she certainly knows what actually happened. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives; rather, she** retains **the presumptive status of** one who **was trampled by a man, until she brings proof** supporting **her statement** that her hymen was ruptured by wood.

The Mishnah now introduces its second case, which runs in parallel to the first but with a significant shift in the nature of the competing claims. Again, both parties agree that the wife was not a virgin at the time of the wedding. The wife, however, says: *mukas etz ani* — I was injured by a piece of wood; my non-virginal state results from a physical injury, not from sexual intercourse. The husband counters: *lo ki ela drusas ish at* — no, you had intercourse with a man before the *kiddushin*. The same debate among the Tannaim follows: Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer believe her; Rabbi Yehoshua does not, maintaining her presumptive status as *drusas ish* until she proves otherwise.

### Rabbi Meir Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעֲנָתַיְיהוּ בְּמַאי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בְּמָאתַיִם וּמָנֶה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: בְּמָנֶה וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What are their** respective financial **claims? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The bride claims that since her hymen was ruptured by wood, her legal status is that of a virgin and she is entitled to a marriage contract of **two hundred** dinars; **and** the groom claims that she engaged in intercourse and is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars. Rabbi Elazar said:** The bride claims that although her hymen is not intact she did not completely deceive him, as she never engaged in intercourse, and therefore she is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars; and** the groom claims that she engaged in intercourse, rendering the betrothal a mistaken transaction, and therefore she is entitled to **nothing at all.**

The Gemara now asks: what exactly are the monetary stakes in this second Mishnah, and how do the opinions of Rabbi Meir and the Rabbanan determine the structure of the dispute? Rabbi Yochanan reads the Mishnah as a debate conducted within the framework of Rabbi Meir. She is claiming 200, and he is countering that she deserves only 100. Why? Rabbi Meir holds that *bein hikkir bah bein lo hikkir bah masayim* — whether or not the husband was aware that the woman was a *mukas etz*, her *kesubah* is the full 200. Hence, she claims: I am a *mukas etz*, and under Rabbi Meir's ruling I deserve 200. He responds: you are in fact a *be'ulah*, not a *mukas etz*, and a *be'ulah* deserves only 100. The dispute is between 200 and 100, situated entirely within Rabbi Meir's position.

### Rabbanan Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בְּמָאתַיִם וּמָנֶה, סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: בֵּין הִכִּיר בָּהּ, וּבֵין לֹא הִכִּיר בָּהּ — מָאתַיִם. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בְּמָנֶה וְלֹא כְּלוּם — סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: בֵּין הִכִּיר בָּהּ, בֵּין לֹא הִכִּיר בָּהּ — מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The dispute between the bride and groom is whether the marriage contract **is two hundred** dinars **or one hundred dinars,** because the *tanna* of the mishna **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: Whether** the husband **was aware that her** hymen was ruptured by wood **or whether he was not aware of her** condition, she receives payment of her marriage contract of **two hundred** dinars and it is not a mistaken transaction. **And Rabbi Elazar says: The dispute** between the bride and groom is whether the marriage contract **is one hundred dinars or** whether she is entitled to **nothing at all,** because the *tanna* of the mishna **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who said: Whether** the husband **was aware that her** hymen was ruptured by wood or **whether he was not aware of her** condition, she receives payment of her marriage contract of **one hundred dinars.**

Rabbi Elazar reads the Mishnah differently — as a debate conducted within the framework of the Rabbanan, with the stakes being 100 or zero. The Rabbanan hold that a *mukas etz* receives 100 regardless of whether the husband knew. She claims she is a *mukas etz* and therefore entitled to 100. He counters that she is a *be'ulah* — and since he entered the marriage under the presumption that she was a *besulah* and discovered otherwise, she receives nothing. This is the *kinsa bechezkas besulah venimtzes be'ulah* scenario, which yields zero under his reading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לֹא קָאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — דְּקָא מוֹקֵי לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? קָסָבַר: כְּנָסָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת בְּתוּלָה וְנִמְצְאת בְּעוּלָה — יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה מָנֶה. הָכָא, הוּא קָאָמַר מָנֶה, וְהִיא קָאָמְרָה מָנֶה, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין טַעֲנָה דִידֵיהּ לְטַעֲנָה דִידַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, Rabbi Elazar did not state** his explanation of the mishna **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Yoḥanan because he** preferred to **establish** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** according to which the *halakha* is ruled, and not the opinion of Rabbi Meir. **However, what is the reason that Rabbi Yoḥanan did not state** his explanation of the mishna **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Elazar?** The reason that Rabbi Yoḥanan explained the mishna in that manner is that **he holds:** If the groom **married** a woman **with the presumptive status of a virgin, and she is found to be a non-virgin, she** receives payment of her **marriage contract of one hundred** dinars. Therefore, **here,** according to the explanation of Rabbi Elazar, who explains the mishna according to the opinion of the Rabbis, **he is saying** that she engaged in intercourse and is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars; and she is saying** that her hymen was ruptured by wood and she is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars. What** difference **is there between his claim and her claim?** Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan explains the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who holds that the dispute between the bride and the groom is whether she is entitled to a marriage contract of two hundred dinars or a marriage contract of one hundred dinars.

The Gemara explains why Rabbi Yochanan cannot read the Mishnah within the framework of the Rabbanan: under the Rabbanan's position, and with Rabbi Yochanan's own view that *kinsa bechezkas besulah venimtzes be'ulah yesh lah kesubah maneh* — even if she was discovered to be a *be'ulah*, she still receives 100 — the husband's claim and the wife's claim would yield identical amounts. If she says *mukas etz* she gets 100, and if he says *be'ulah* she also gets 100. There would be no dispute to adjudicate, no *nafka minah*. Hence Rabbi Yochanan must situate the Mishnah within Rabbi Meir.

### Two Cases Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי: חֲדָא לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, וַחֲדָא לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** Again we question: **Granted, according to Rabbi Elazar,** who explains that the dispute between the bride and the groom is with regard to whether she is entitled to a marriage contract of one hundred dinars because her hymen was ruptured by wood, or whether she is entitled to nothing because she engaged in intercourse, **that is** why the *tanna* **teaches two** similar disputes, in this mishna and in the previous one. **One** dispute, in this mishna, comes **to exclude** the opinion **of Rami bar Ḥama,** who said: If he was not aware that her hymen was ruptured by wood, everyone agrees that she receives no marriage contract at all, as the marriage was a mistaken transaction. From this mishna it is clear that according to her claim that her hymen was ruptured by wood, she is entitled to one hundred dinars. **And one** dispute, in the previous mishna, is brought in order **to exclude** that **which Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin** said that **Rav Sheshet said:** If the groom married a woman with the presumptive status of a virgin and she is found to be a non-virgin, she receives payment of her marriage contract of one hundred dinars. In the previous mishna he claims: Before I betrothed you, you were raped and my transaction was a mistaken transaction, indicating that she is entitled to nothing. **However,** according **to Rabbi Yoḥanan, why do I** need the *tanna* to teach **two** similar disputes?

The Gemara then asks: given that Rabbi Elazar reads both Mishnayot within the framework of the Rabbanan, we can readily explain why two separate Mishnayot are needed, since each one delivers a distinct *chiddush*. But what is the purpose of having two cases according to Rabbi Yochanan?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וַחֲדָא לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. קַמַּיְיתָא — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִיגּוֹ — לָא מְהֵימְנָא. בָּתְרָיְיתָא — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִיגּוֹ, מְהֵימְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The two similar disputes were necessary. **One** is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabban Gamliel** and the extent of the credibility that he accords to her claim; **and one** is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua** and the extent to which he does not accord credibility to her claim. The Gemara elaborates: **The first** mishna, where she admits she was raped but claims that it was after betrothal, **conveys to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua: That even though there is** room **to say** that her claim is bolstered by a ***miggo*, she is not deemed credible. The second** mishna, where she claims that her hymen was ruptured by wood and he claims that she engaged in intercourse, **conveys to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of Rabban Gamliel: That even though there is no** room **to say** that her claim is bolstered by a ***miggo*,** in his opinion **she is deemed credible.**

According to Rabbi Elazar, the first Mishnah — where the wife claims she was raped after *kiddushin* — establishes that *kinsa bechezkas besulah venimtzes be'ulah* yields zero. This rejects the position embraced by Rabbi Chiya bar Avin, who held that even when a woman enters as a presumed *besulah* and is found to be a *be'ulah*, she retains 100. The second Mishnah — the *mukas etz* case — establishes that *kinsa bechezkas besulah venimtzes mukas etz* still entitles her to 100. This rejects the position of Rami bar Chama, who held that any misrepresentation, even a *mukas etz* situation, results in zero. Each Mishnah is thus targeting a different mistaken view.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, however, who situates the second Mishnah within Rabbi Meir, the *chiddush* about *mukas etz* not yielding zero is obviously present there. And since Rabbi Yochanan himself holds that *nimtzes be'ulah* still yields 100, the first Mishnah under the Rabbanan's framework would also not yield zero. So what is the second Mishnah adding?

The Gemara's answer, attributed to Rav Sheshet, is that the two Mishnayot are needed to demonstrate the full extent of the opposing Tannaitic positions. The first Mishnah reveals the strength of Rabbi Yehoshua's position: *af al gav de'ika le'meimar migo lo mehemena* — even though the wife has a *migo* in that case (she could have claimed *mukas etz* rather than rape, a less self-incriminating claim), Rabbi Yehoshua still does not believe her. The second Mishnah reveals the strength of Rabban Gamliel's position: *af al gav de'leka le'meimar migo ne'emenes* — even though in the *mukas etz* case the wife has no *migo* (she is already making the best possible claim; *mukas etz* does not diminish her status), Rabban Gamliel believes her and she receives the full 200. The two cases are thus needed to trace the full arc of the disagreement between the Tannaim.

## Seen with Man (II)

### Status Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רָאוּהָ מְדַבֶּרֶת עִם אֶחָד, וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ: מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ זֶה? אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי, וְכֹהֵן הוּא. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים, אֶלָּא הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת בְּעוּלָה לְנָתִין וּלְמַמְזֵר, עַד שֶׁתָּבִיא רְאָיָה לִדְבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If people **saw** a woman **speaking to one** man, but they did not recognize him, **and they said to her: What is the nature [*tivo*] of this man?** And she said to them: **He is a man** called **so-and-so and he is a priest; Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible, and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives. Rather, she** assumes **the presumptive status of** one who **engaged in intercourse with a Gibeonite or with a *mamzer*,** men of flawed lineage who disqualify her from marrying a priest, **until she brings proof** supporting **her statement.**

The Mishnah now shifts from monetary disputes to questions of personal status. *Ra'uah medaberes im echad* — a woman was seen speaking with a man. The Mishnah will ask what we are to make of this, and in particular whether we believe her account of who that man was. The question is not merely abstract; it bears directly on the woman's fitness to marry a *kohen* and on the status of any child she might have. If she had relations with a *mamzer* or a non-Jew, she would become *pasul* to a *kohen*, and the child's lineage would be compromised.

It is worth noting in passing that elsewhere in Ketubot, a wife who *medaberes im kol adam bashuk* — who speaks to strangers in the marketplace — is grounds for divorce without payment of the *kesubah*. This was a highly segregated society, and a woman's speaking to a strange man was not taken as an innocuous act.

She is asked: *ma tivo shel ish zeh* — what is the story of this man? She responds: *ish ploni ve'kohen hu* — it was so-and-so, and he is a *kohen*. The mention of *kohen* is not strictly necessary; the point is that the man is of valid Jewish lineage and that nothing about the encounter could have compromised her status.

### Tannaim Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible, and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives**

The same Tannaitic debate recurs with the same formulations. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say she is believed. Rabbi Yehoshua says *lo mipiha anu chayim* — we do not live from her words. He holds that she carries the presumption of having had relations with a *nasin* or a *mamzer*, rendering her *pasul* to a *kohen*, until she can prove otherwise.

## Seclusion vs. Sex

### Medaberet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי מְדַבֶּרֶת? זְעֵירִי אָמַר: נִסְתְּרָה, רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: נִבְעֲלָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לִזְעֵירִי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי מְדַבֶּרֶת. אֶלָּא לְרַב אַסִּי, מַאי מְדַבֶּרֶת? לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״אָכְלָה וּמָחֲתָה פִיהָ וְאָמְרָה לֹא פָעַלְתִּי אָוֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **speaking** mentioned in the mishna? **Ze'eiri said:** It means that **she secluded herself** with a man and it is unknown whether she engaged in intercourse. **Rav Asi said:** It means that **she engaged in intercourse. Granted, according to Ze'eiri, that is** why the mishna **teaches** the case employing the term **speaking,** as it is certain only that they were in seclusion. Perhaps all they did was speak. **However, according to Rav Asi, what is** the reason the mishna employed the term **speaking** if the reference is to intercourse? The Gemara answers: The mishna employed **a euphemism, as it is written** with regard to licentious women: **"She eats and wipes her mouth and says: I have done no wickedness"** (Proverbs 30:20). The verse euphemistically ascribes the act of intercourse to the mouth instead of to the appropriate body part.

Before we can analyze the Tannaitic debate, we must establish what the Mishnah's first case actually involves. The Mishnah says she was *medaberes im echad* — "speaking with someone." But does this mean she merely conversed with a man, possibly in seclusion, or does it mean something more?

### Zeiri: Seclusion

Zeiri says: *medaberes* means *nistera* — she went into seclusion with a man. The term is a description of private contact, not of sexual intercourse per se. Under this reading, the Mishnah is saying that even in a case where we only know she was alone with a man — without any direct knowledge of sexual activity — Rabbi Yehoshua holds that her status is in suspicion.

### Rav Assi: Sex

Rav Assi says: *medaberes* means *niv'ala* — she actually had sexual relations. The word *medaberes* is used because it is *lishana me'alya* — a euphemism, a refined term. The Mishnah employs indirect language to describe what is in fact a known sexual encounter.

### Mouth Euphemism

The Gemara brings a proof for Rav Assi's reading from the verse in Proverbs that describes the way of an adulterous woman: *achla umachta pi'a* — she eats and wipes her mouth. The "mouth" language is itself a euphemism for sexual activity. The Gemara connects this to the *medaberes* formulation in our Mishnah, suggesting that speech and mouth language function as established biblical idioms for sexual conduct.

This reading has significant practical consequences. If Zeiri is correct and the Mishnah's first case is merely seclusion, then Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling is extraordinarily stringent: even without knowing that any intercourse occurred, a woman's status becomes suspect based solely on private contact. This would seem to clash with the ruling of Rav, who holds that *malkin al ha-yichud ve-ein osrin al ha-yichud* — we administer rabbinic lashes for seclusion of a married woman with a stranger, but we do not thereby forbid her to her husband. If even a married woman does not lose her status from mere seclusion, why would a single woman's lineage-related status be compromised? The Gemara addresses this tension, suggesting that when it comes to *yuchasin* — questions of lineage and fitness to marry a *kohen* — a special stringency applies: *ma'ala asu be'yuchasin*.

### Two Cases Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִזְעֵירִי — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי: ״מְדַבֶּרֶת״, וּ״מְעוּבֶּרֶת״. אֶלָּא לְרַב אַסִּי — תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא, לְהַכְשִׁיר בָּהּ. וַחֲדָא, לְהַכְשִׁיר בְּבִתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Ze'eiri,** who said that speaking means that she secluded herself with him, **that is** the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches two** cases in the mishna: A woman **speaking** to a man **and** a woman who is **pregnant. However, according to Rav Asi,** if speaking means that she engaged in intercourse, **why do I** need **two** cases addressing the same issue? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* teaches **one** case, the case of speaking to a man, **to deem her fit** to marry a priest, because although she engaged in intercourse, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Gamliel rule that she is deemed credible and is not considered to have engaged in intercourse with a man of flawed lineage. **And** the *tanna* teaches **one** case, the case of the single woman who is pregnant, in order **to deem her daughter** born from that pregnancy **fit** to marry a priest, as Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Gamliel rule the mother is deemed credible.

The Gemara asks: according to Zeiri, why does the Mishnah need two separate cases — the case where she is *medaberes* and the case where she is pregnant? Each case adds a distinct dimension. The first — seclusion — tests how far Rabbi Yehoshua's stringency extends (even without knowing she had sex). The second — pregnancy, where we know beyond doubt that she had sex — tests how far Rabban Gamliel's leniency extends (even when the fact of intercourse is established).

According to Rav Assi, however, since the first case already involves known sexual relations, the two cases could seem redundant. The Gemara answers: *chada lehachsher ba ve'chada lehachsher be'vita* — one case establishes her own status as valid, and the other establishes that the fetus's status is also valid. Why might the fetus's status be treated differently? Because the woman herself has a *chazakah* — she started life with a presumptively valid status, and we are reluctant to undermine it. But the fetus has no prior status; its lineage is entirely unknown. One might therefore have said that even if we trust her account of her own status, we remain doubtful about the fetus. The second case teaches that Rabban Gamliel believes her even with respect to the child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּכְשִׁיר בָּהּ — מַכְשִׁיר בְּבִתָּהּ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּכְשִׁיר בָּהּ — פּוֹסֵל בְּבִתָּהּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? רַב אַסִּי סָבַר כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְדִבְרֵי הַמַּכְשִׁיר בָּהּ — מַכְשִׁיר בְּבִתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says:** With regard **to the statement of the one who deems her fit** to marry a priest, **he deems her daughter fit** to do so as well. **However, according to the one who says:** With regard **to the statement of the one who deems her fit** to marry a priest, **he** nevertheless **deems her daughter unfit, what is there to say?** Why did the mishna cite two cases addressing the same issue? The Gemara answers: **Rav Asi holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said:** With regard **to the statement of the one who deems her fit** to marry a priest, **he deems her daughter fit** to do so as well.

This analysis holds, the Gemara notes, according to the position that *le'divrei ha-machsher ba machsher be'vita* — according to those who validate her, they validate the child as well. But there is a competing view that *le'divrei ha-machsher ba posel be'vita* — even those who accept her own status would still consider the child of doubtful status. Under that view, both cases in the Mishnah are about her status only, and if the first case already establishes that point, the second adds nothing. The Gemara resolves this by concluding that Rav Assi held the former position — that validating the mother means validating the child — so that the two cases are indeed each necessary.

## Location Analysis

### Ruins Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה עִם אֶחָד לְסֵתֶר, אוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה — וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ: מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ זֶה? כֹּהֵן הוּא, וּבֶן אֲחִי אַבָּא הוּא. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים, אֶלָּא הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת בְּעוּלָה לְנָתִין וּלְמַמְזֵר, עַד שֶׁתָּבִיא רְאָיָה לִדְבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection: If** people **saw that** a woman **entered with one** man **into seclusion, or into a ruin,** which is typically located outside the city, and if a man and woman meet there it is presumably in order to engage in sexual relations, **and** people **said to her: What is the nature of this man** with whom you secluded? She said to them: **He is a priest, and he is the son of my father's brother,** a respectable person of impeccable lineage. **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives; rather, she** assumes **the presumptive status of** one who **engaged in intercourse with a Gibeonite or with a *mamzer*,** men of flawed lineage who disqualify her from marrying a priest, **until she brings proof** supporting **her statement.**

A *baraita* is now introduced to further elaborate the Mishnah's cases. The *baraita* speaks of a woman who went into a *churba* — a ruin — with a man. Rashi notes that a *churba* carries a stronger presumption of sexual activity than mere seclusion; ruins are places where people go specifically for trysts, away from public view, making the assumption of intercourse more reasonable than in a simple case of seclusion. The *baraita* then presents two sub-cases: *churba de'mata* — a ruin within the city — and *churba de'dabra* — a ruin in the field.

### Kosher Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִזְעֵירִי — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי: לְסֵתֶר אוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה. אֶלָּא לְרַב אַסִּי דְּאָמַר נִבְעֲלָה, תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא קָתָנֵי: לְסֵתֶר דְּחוּרְבָּה. וְהָא לְסֵתֶר אוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה קָתָנֵי! חֲדָא לְחוּרְבָּה דְמָתָא, וַחֲדָא לְחוּרְבָּה דְּדַבְרָא. וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן חוּרְבָּה דְמָתָא — בְּהָא קָמַכְשַׁר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, מִשּׁוּם דְּרוֹב כְּשֵׁרִים אֶצְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Ze'eiri,** who said that speaking means that she secluded herself, **that is** the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches two** cases where there is merely concern: The case of: **Into seclusion,** where there is lesser concern that she engaged in intercourse, **and** the case of: **Into a ruin,** where there is greater concern. **However, according to Rav Asi, who said** that speaking means that **she had intercourse,** and only in that case is she not deemed credible according to Rabbi Yehoshua, **why do I** need **two** cases? The Gemara answers: According to Rav Asi, the *tanna* **is teaching one** case: If people saw a woman enter with one man **into the seclusion of a ruin,** where the likelihood is that that they entered to engage in relations. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the mishna **teaching** two different cases: **Into seclusion or into a ruin?** The Gemara suggests a different explanation. According to Rav Asi, two cases are necessary, **one** with regard **to a ruin in the city and one** with regard **to a ruin in the field,** distant from the city. **And** both cases **are necessary, because if** the *tanna* **taught us** only the case of **a ruin in the city,** one might have concluded that **in that** case **Rabban Gamliel deems her fit** to marry a priest **due to** the fact **that the majority** of the people **in its proximity are honorable** and of impeccable lineage.

*De'i ashmeinan churba de'mata* — had the *baraita* mentioned only the ruin in the city, we might have understood Rabban Gamliel's ruling narrowly. In that case, *rov ksheirim etzlah* — the majority of the people in the city who might have been her partner are halachically valid Jews; non-Jews and *mamzerim* are rare. Rabban Gamliel's willingness to believe her in that context would be unsurprising.

### Invalid Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְחוּרְבָּה דְּדַבְרָא, דְּרוֹב פְּסוּלִין אֶצְלָהּ — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָהִיא — בְּהָהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אֲבָל בְּהָא — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard **to a ruin in the field, where the majority** of the people **in its proximity are unfit** and of flawed lineage, as people from all over the world pass the ruin in the field and the majority of the people in the world are of flawed lineage, **say** that Rabban Gamliel **concedes to Rabbi Yehoshua** and deems her unfit to marry a priest. **And if** the *tanna* **taught us** only **that** case of a ruin in the field, one might have concluded that **in that** case **Rabbi Yehoshua said** she is not deemed credible, because the majority of the people there are of flawed lineage. **However here,** in the case of a ruin in the city, where the majority of the people are of impeccable lineage, **say** that Rabbi Yehoshua **concedes to Rabban Gamliel** that she is deemed credible. Therefore, both cases were **necessary.**

Conversely, *churba de'dabra* — a ruin in the field — presents a different demographic reality. In the open countryside, *rov psulim etzlah* — the majority of men she might have encountered are non-Jews, who would render her *pasul* to a *kohen* if she had relations with them. One might have thought that even Rabban Gamliel would concede to Rabbi Yehoshua in that case, since the statistical likelihood cuts against her.

### Yichus Severity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: זוֹ עֵדוּת שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה כְּשֵׁרָה לָהּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים בִּשְׁבוּיָה שֶׁנִּשְׁבֵּית וְיֵשׁ לָהּ עֵדִים שֶׁנִּשְׁבֵּית, וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי, שֶׁאֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲבָל. וּמָה הֶפְרֵשׁ יֵשׁ בֵּין זוֹ לָזוֹ? לָזוֹ — יֵשׁ עֵדִים, ולָזוֹ — אֵין לָהּ עֵדִים. אָמַר לָהֶם: אַף לָזוֹ יֵשׁ עֵדִים, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּרֵיסָהּ בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רוֹב גּוֹיִם פְּרוּצִים בַּעֲרָיוֹת הֵם. אָמַר לָהֶן: אֵין אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס לַעֲרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the *Tosefta*: **This,** i.e., that she engaged in intercourse with a man of impeccable lineage, **is testimony that a woman is fit** to testify. **And Rabbi Yehoshua says: She is not deemed credible. Rabbi Yehoshua said to** the Sages: **Do you not agree in the case of a woman who was taken captive, and she has witnesses** testifying **that she was taken captive, and she says: I am pure,** i.e., I was not violated by my captors, **that she is not deemed credible?** The assumption in that case is that most captive women are violated by their captors. The Sages **said to him: But** there is a difference between the cases. **And what difference is there between this** case of a captive, where the woman is not accorded credibility, **and that** case of a woman who secluded herself with a man? **For this** captive, **there are witnesses** that she was taken captive, and due to the prevalent immorality in that situation, she loses the presumptive status of virtue and is considered to have certainly engaged in intercourse. **But for this** woman who secluded herself with a man, **she does not have witnesses** that she engaged in intercourse. Since she could have claimed that she did not engage in intercourse and instead admitted that she engaged in intercourse and claimed that it was with a man of impeccable lineage, she should be accorded credibility. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: Even for that** woman, the one who secluded herself, **there are witnesses, because her belly is between her teeth,** i.e., her pregnancy is conspicuous and therefore she does not have the option of claiming that she did not engage in intercourse. The Sages **said to him:** There remains a difference between the cases, as **most gentiles are steeped in sexual immorality.** Therefore, presumably, they engaged in intercourse with the captive woman. However, in the case of the woman in seclusion there is no presumption that she engaged in intercourse specifically with a man with flawed lineage. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: There is no steward for** restraining **sexual immorality,** and therefore, everyone is suspect in that regard. Therefore, this woman, since she engaged in intercourse, lost her presumptive status of virtue, and there is no basis to trust her that it was with a person of impeccable lineage.

Conversely, had the *baraita* mentioned only the field case, we might have understood Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling as limited to that scenario, where the majority are *psulim*. We might then have thought that in the city — where most are *kasher* — Rabbi Yehoshua would concede to Rabban Gamliel. By including both cases, the *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehoshua maintains his stringency *afilu be'rov ksheirim* — even where the majority are valid. His position is grounded not in statistics but in the categorical principle of *ma'ala asu be'yuchasin*: when it comes to questions of lineage and fitness for priestly marriage, we apply an elevated standard of scrutiny regardless of the demographic majority.

The Gemara confirms this analysis by citing a statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *mi'divrei ha-machsher machsher afilu be'rov psulim, u'mi'divrei ha-posel posel afilu be'rov ksheirim* — those who validate her do so even where most are *psulim*, and those who invalidate her do so even where most are *ksheirim*. The positions are absolute in both directions, which explains why the comparison to the captive woman could be introduced without those who defend her immediately objecting that the cases are statistically incomparable.

## Baraita Stringency

### Captive Comparison

Rabbi Yehoshua now challenges Rabban Gamliel with a comparison: *i atem modim bishvuya she'nishbet ve'yesh lah edim she'nishbet ve'hi omeret tehora ani she'eina ne'emenes* — do you not concede that if a woman has been taken captive by non-Jews, and there are witnesses to that captivity, she is not believed when she claims she was not violated? That case is one where you accept that a woman's assurances about her own sexual history cannot be credited. How, then, can you believe this woman when she identifies the man she was with?

Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer concede the captive case: *aval* — yes, that is true.

### Non-Jews Difference

They then press back: *mah hefresh bein zo l'zo?* — what difference is there between the captive and this woman? The answer is: *l'zo yesh edim u'l'zo ein edim* — in the case of the captive, there are witnesses to the captivity itself, establishing that she was in the hands of non-Jews who we presume would violate her. Here, in the case of seclusion or private contact, there are no such witnesses establishing that she was with a man who would be *pasul*. The cases are therefore not comparable.

### No Guardian

Rabbi Yehoshua counters: *af l'zo yesh edim she'harei k'reisa bein sheineiha* — but in the pregnant case, there is visible evidence; her pregnancy is itself the proof that she has had relations. So why is the pregnant woman more trustworthy than the captive?

They respond: *rov ovdei kochavim prutzim ba'arayot hem* — non-Jews are presumed to be sexually aggressive; when a woman is in their captivity, we assume violation occurred with non-Jews specifically, which is what renders her *pasul*. But when a Jewish woman goes into private with an unidentified man, we do not presume that the man was one who would disqualify her.

Rabbi Yehoshua's rejoinder is the principle that has become one of the most cited phrases in this area of halacha: *ein apotropus la'arayot* — there is no guardian over sexual urges. The impulse toward forbidden relations does not discriminate by nationality. If we are going to assume that non-Jewish captors will violate a woman, we must equally assume that when a man and woman are in private, relations occur. The argument from the captive therefore applies to the seclusion case as well.

### Status & Shtuki

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה בְּגוּפָהּ, אֲבָל עֵדוּת אִשָּׁה בְּבִתָּהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הַוָּלָד שְׁתוּקִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **are these** divergent **statements** of Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua **said?** It is **in the case of the testimony of a woman with regard to herself,** to render her fit to marry a priest. **However,** in the case of **testimony of a woman with regard to** the fact that the father of **her daughter** is a man of unflawed lineage, **everyone agrees** that her testimony is not deemed credible, and the legal status of **the child** is that of **a *shetuki*,** the identity of whose father is unknown and to whom all the stringencies that apply to a *mamzer* apply, due the uncertainty of his lineage.

The *baraita* concludes on a notably stringent note. When does the *ne'emanus* principle — that she is believed about her own status — apply? Only when *bata hee gufa* — she herself comes forward and testifies. But when the question is about the child — *aval he'idah isha be'vita* — the *baraita* rules *divrei ha'kol ha'velad shtuki*: everyone agrees that the child is a *shtuki*, a person of doubtful paternity whose claim of "who is my father?" is met with silence. This is a striking and severe conclusion. Even according to Rabban Gamliel, who validates the woman's own status, the child's status cannot be established on the basis of her testimony alone.

The Gemara at this point draws together the threads of the debate, noting that the exchange between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua moves through several shifting positions — the seclusion case, the pregnancy case, the captive comparison — each party advancing and retreating depending on which sub-case is under discussion. The *baraita* also confirms the reading of Zeiri that *medaberes* means seclusion rather than known intercourse, since the whole discussion about the captive comparison only makes sense if the first case involves doubt about whether intercourse occurred at all.

The discussion of Rabbi Yochanan's statement — *le'divrei ha-machsher bah machsher be'vita, u'le'divrei ha-posel bah posel be'vita* — and the question of whether those who validate her necessarily validate the daughter, will be taken up at the start of the next session.
