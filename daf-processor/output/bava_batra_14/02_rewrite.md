# Bava Batra 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Half-Slave & Pru U'rvu

### Gudo o Agud

Before turning to the new material on *daf* 14, it is worth briefly revisiting several *Tosafot* on 13a that bear directly on yesterday's discussion of *gudo o agud* — the question of whether a court may compel forced division of jointly held property. In that context, the *Gemara* introduced the classic debate of *Beit Hillel u'Beit Shammai* concerning a slave who is half free and half still enslaved, and whether his master may be compelled to free him entirely on account of the *mitzvah* of *pru u'rvu*.

### Slave & Pru U'rvu

The first *Tosafot*, beginning with the words *reish shifcha yachol*, raises a pointed difficulty. *Tosafot* notes — drawing on a *be'aya* in Yevamos — that all opinions agree a slave cannot fulfill *pru u'rvu*, since the children of a slave are legally not considered his children at all; they follow the mother's status. If so, the question sharpens: what precisely is the basis for compelling the master to free him in the name of *pru u'rvu*? Even if one were to argue that the free half of the person retains some capacity to fulfill *pru u'rvu*, *Tosafot* rejects this: were the person a full slave, *pru u'rvu* would not apply to him at all — *kevan she'avad meyuchas achareiha*, since the child's lineage follows the mother. There is thus no obvious mechanism by which *pru u'rvu* drives the obligation to free him.

### Lashevet Yetzarah

*Tosafot* resolves this by invoking the verse *lo tohu bera'ah, lashevet yetzarah* — God did not create the world for chaos, but for habitation. The *Ri* (*Rabbeinu Yitzchak*) rules that we do not compel the master to free the slave merely for the sake of *pru u'rvu* in its technical sense. Yet *mishum d'lashevet yetzarah shayach afilu b'tzad avdus* — the principle of *lashevet yetzarah* applies even on the side of his enslavement, because he is still bringing children into the world. This raises a genuine conceptual question: is *lashevet yetzarah* simply an explication of the weight and scope of *pru u'rvu*, or does it constitute an independent obligation beyond the formal parameters of *pru u'rvu*? *Tosafot* appears to embrace the latter: technically, this person is not fulfilling *pru u'rvu* in the legal sense, yet he is still bringing offspring into the world, which satisfies the thrust of *lashevet yetzarah*. A subsequent *Tosafot* — citing the *Rivam* — similarly holds that *lashevet yetzarah* applies even in his condition of partial servitude, and concludes that it is precisely the weighty, world-purpose-oriented character of *lashevet yetzarah* that gave *Chazal* the impetus to institute the *takkana* compelling the master to free him — not a bare claim of *pru u'rvu* alone.

### Forcing Master to Free

A further *Tosafot* raises a different question: *amay kofin* — why do we need any *takkana* at all? The *mitzvat assei* of *pru u'rvu* should simply override the *lo ta'asseh* of *lo yihiyeh kadesh*. The reason a half-slave cannot marry a freed woman is that doing so would violate *lo yihiyeh kadesh* — a prohibition that normally concerns male prostitution but is applied here to a slave marrying a free woman. The standard rule is that a positive commandment overrides a prohibition. So why not simply invoke *assei docheh lo ta'asseh* and allow him to marry and fulfill *pru u'rvu* without any need for the *takkana* of freeing him?

### Tosafot Answers

*Tosafot* offers two answers. The first, in the name of *Rabbeinu Yitzchak*, is a matter of timing: *mishat ha'araah ka'avir lav* — from the very beginning of the sexual act, one transgresses the *lav*. The *assei* of *pru u'rvu*, however, is not fulfilled until the act is complete — *assei d'Pru U'rvu lo mekayem ad gmar biyah* — since only then does the possibility of conception arise. Since the transgression of the *lav* precedes the fulfillment of the *assei*, the mechanism of *assei docheh lo ta'asseh* cannot operate cleanly here. One might alternatively frame the act of intercourse as a mere *hechsher mitzvah* rather than the *maaseh mitzvah* of *pru u'rvu* itself — which opens broader questions about how to define the operative act of fulfilling *pru u'rvu*, particularly when one holds it is the man's *mitzvah*.

The second answer is that the woman has no *mitzvah* of *pru u'rvu*. The classic rabbinic position is that women are exempt from *pru u'rvu*. Even if the man could be permitted to transgress the *lo ta'asseh* through the mechanism of *assei docheh lo ta'asseh*, the woman has no *assei* of her own — and who permits her to transgress? *Assei docheh lo ta'asseh* would authorize him to act; it says nothing about her.

*Tosafot* then continues into the broader question of whether it is permissible for one person to transgress a prohibition in order to enable another person to fulfill a *mitzvah* — and specifically, whether the master may be permitted to transgress the prohibition against freeing slaves in order to enable the slave to fulfill a *mitzvah*. These are rich questions that *Tosafot* explores at length, and they underscore how much conceptual depth is embedded in what appeared to be a subsidiary discussion from yesterday's *daf*.

## Sefer Torah Structure

### Scroll Requirements

We now turn to the *daf* proper, picking up at the top of 14a. The *Gemara* here is concerned not with the ritual *Sefer Torah* used for *Kriat HaTorah* in the synagogue, but with scrolls used functionally — as books for learning — which could contain the entire *Torah*, *Nevi'im*, and *Kesuvim* in a single scroll. Despite their non-ritual character, the *Gemara* establishes certain physical requirements for such scrolls.

### Blank Parchment

One basic requirement is that a blank section of *klaf* be left at the beginning and end of the scroll, to facilitate wrapping around the pole.

### Rolling Direction

*Tosafot*'s understanding of the *Gemara* — which is more functionally intuitive — holds that one rolls from the end toward the beginning. Under this reading, the blank *klaf* left at the end of the scroll (i.e., the end of *Devarim*) provides enough parchment to wrap around the pole, so that the written text does not sit directly against it. The blank section at the beginning similarly allows the opening of the scroll to be fully wrapped after rolling.

*Rashi*, by contrast, reads the rolling direction as beginning to end, which creates the counterintuitive result that one opens the scroll from the back. *Rashi* accordingly reverses the *girsa* of the *Gemara* — holding that the beginning needs enough blank *klaf* to wrap the circumference (*heikef*) and the end needs enough to wrap the pole (*amud*). *Tosafot* maintains the opposite assignment.

### Wrapping Contradiction

The *Gemara* itself registers the tension. The phrase *techilas ha'sefer v'sofo k'dei l'gol* — the beginning and end of the scroll are sufficient to wrap — creates an ambiguity: sufficient to wrap what? If both ends need only enough to wrap a pole (*amud*), the requirement regarding the circumference (*heikef*) is left unaddressed. If both need enough to wrap the full circumference, that contradicts the other requirement. The two ends seem to serve different purposes, and reading the *Gemara* as assigning one to each function creates the apparent contradiction the *Gemara* flags.

### Two Poles

*Rav Ashi* resolves the difficulty: the *Beraita* about the beginning and end is discussing a *Sefer Torah* specifically — and a *Sefer Torah* has two poles, one at each end. The *Gemara* quotes a supporting *Beraita*: *kol hasfarim niglalim mitichilatan l'sofam* — all [other] scrolls are rolled from beginning to end — *v'sefer Torah niglal l'emtzi'ito* — but a *Sefer Torah* is rolled toward its middle. Because a *Sefer Torah* has a pole at each end, one can open it at any point in the middle, which serves the practical need of a large scroll.

With two poles, one end of the scroll wraps around the pole itself (*amud*), and the other end needs enough blank *klaf* to wrap around the full circumference (*heikef*) of the rolled scroll. The contradiction dissolves: the two ends are not parallel requirements; each serves a distinct function.

### Jerusalem Scribes

*Rav Elazar bar Rav Tzadok* confirms that this was actual practice: *kach hayu kotvim sefarim sheb'Yerushalayim osim sifreihem* — this is how the scribes of Jerusalem would make their scrolls.

### Ritual vs. Book

The simple reading of this *Gemara* is that we are not speaking of these scrolls as ritual objects in the technical sense — they are not the *Sefer Torah* used for *Kriat HaTorah*, with all its attendant *halachic* requirements. This raises the question: what is the *Gemara*'s authority here? If these are just books, why not let practical considerations govern? The answer seems to be that even when a *sefer* is not a ritual object in the narrow sense, there remains an obligation of *kavod* — respect — for the text. Rolling an entire *Tanach* on a single pole would require constant unwinding to find one's place, which is simply undignified. Similarly, the prohibition mentioned elsewhere against combining all of *Navi* in one scroll, according to some opinions, reflects a concern for maintaining proper distinction between the different *sefarim*. These are not ritual requirements in the sense that one is or is not *yotze* a *mitzvah*, but they reflect the *Gemara*'s insistence on maintaining appropriate honor for sacred texts.

This distinction between categories is itself worth pausing on. There are effectively three tiers: the *mitzvas ksivas Sefer Torah*, the *Sefer Torah* used for public *Kriat HaTorah*, and an ordinary *sefer* used for learning. We tend to assume that the detailed *halachic* requirements described in the *Gemara* — and codified by the *Rambam* — apply to the middle category. The *Rambam*, however, holds that all those requirements pertain specifically to the *mitzvas ksivas Sefer Torah*, and that for public *Kriat HaTorah* one may technically use a *Chumash*. The question of what language a scroll may be written in — *kol hasefarim nichtavim bechol lashon* — and what script must be used, thus depends entirely on which of these three categories is under discussion.

## Torah Proportions

### Length-Width Equality

The *Gemara* now presents a *Beraita* governing the proportions of a *Sefer Torah*: *ein osin Sefer Torah lo orko yoseir al hekefo velo hekefo yoseir al orko* — one does not make a *Sefer Torah* whose length exceeds its circumference, nor whose circumference exceeds its length. The requirement is bidirectional: length and circumference must be equal.

To visualize this: the *orko* is the height of the scroll as it stands — the height of the columns of writing. The *heikef* is the circumference of the fully rolled scroll. The *Gemara* will indicate that the standard measure for each is six *tefachim*. With six *tefachim* as the height and six *tefachim* as the circumference, and using the Talmud's assumption that *pi* equals three, the diameter of the rolled scroll comes to two *tefachim* — approximately six inches — yielding a scroll roughly eighteen inches tall and six inches wide when rolled.

This is a demanding physical specification. One begins with a sheet of *klaf* that is six *tefachim* tall, and the thickness of the *klaf*, the tightness of the script, and the total amount of text must combine so that when fully written and rolled, the circumference is also exactly six *tefachim*. The *Maharsha* notes, and the *Gemara* will make explicit, that the number six derives from the dimensions of the *Luchot* — the Tablets of Stone were six *tefachim* tall, and that measure became the template for the *Sefer Torah*.

### Six Tefachim

When asked *bekama* — what is the proper height — *Rabbi* (Yehudah HaNasi) answers: *b'gevil, shisha* — on *gevil*, the answer is six. The *girsa* and the reasoning will be clarified when the *Gemara* derives six *tefachim* from the dimensions of the *Luchot*.

### Gevil & Klaf

*Gevil* refers to the full-grain leather — the entire thickness of the animal hide used as-is, with writing on the side where the hair grew. The alternative is *klaf*: the hide is split down its thickness, and one writes on the inner face of the outer half — the surface created by the cut. Writing is always on the point of the cut; the difference is which half of the split hide one uses. *Gevil* uses the whole hide; *klaf* uses the outer half; *duchsustus* uses the inner half. For a *Sefer Torah*, one writes on the hair side of *gevil* and on the cut surface of *klaf*.

When asked what the measure would be for *klaf*, *Rabbi* answers: *beklaf bekama eini yodea* — for *klaf*, I do not know. The difficulty with this answer is not immediately obvious: if the requirement is simply that height equal circumference, one need only adjust the size of the script. If *klaf* is thinner than *gevil*, the rolled scroll will have a smaller circumference for the same number of columns, requiring larger script or a different column width to compensate. Why would *Rabbi* not know the answer?

### Scribal Stories

The challenge of achieving the exact proportions in practice is illustrated by two famous stories. *Rav Huna* wrote seventy *Sifrei Torah* and only once — *ela chad* — did a single scroll happen to come out with the height equal to the circumference. *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* wrote one *Sefer Torah* on the skin of a calf — *amshka d'eglei* — and it worked out on the first attempt. The calf skin happened to be precisely the right thickness. *Yavu bei rabanan einaihu* — his colleagues gave him the evil eye — *venach nafshei* — and he died. The conclusion seems to be that either you will never achieve it, or you will achieve it and suffer for it.

### Rabbi Ami's 400

The *Gemara* then reports that *Rabbi Ami* wrote four hundred *Sifrei Torah*. *Rav Hamnuna* is skeptical: *dilma Torah tziva lanu Moshe katav* — perhaps what is meant is not four hundred complete *Sifrei Torah*, but four hundred instances of writing the verse *Torah tziva lanu Moshe*. *Tosafot* supports this reading by citing *Rabbi Yitzchak*, who derives from the *Gemara* in chapter 3 of Sukkah (*perek* 3) that *Torah tziva lanu Moshe* is the distillation of what a father teaches his young child as soon as the child can speak — *mai Torah? Torah tziva lanu Moshe*. The verse thus serves as the condensed embodiment of the concept of *Torah*. A similar reinterpretation is offered when *Rava* tells *Rav Zeira* that *Rabbi Yannai* planted four hundred vineyards: *dilma shtayim keneged shtayim ve'achas yotzei zanav* — perhaps it means the minimum halakhic configuration of a vineyard, two vines parallel to two with one extending between them, which is sufficient to constitute a *kerem* for the relevant *halachic* purposes.

## Ark Dimensions

### Ark Size

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita* challenging the six-*tefach* standard for *Sefer Torah* proportions: *amatayim vachatzi orko ve'amah vachatzi rochbo ve'amah vachatzi komaso* — the *Aron* that *Moshe* made was, per the *pesukim* in *Shemot*, two and a half *amot* long, one and a half *amot* wide, and one and a half *amot* tall. Taking the standard *amah* as six *tefachim*, these dimensions convert to fifteen *tefachim* in length, nine *tefachim* in width, and nine *tefachim* in height.

### Tablets Placement

The dimensions of the *Luchot* are then given: *vehaluchos arkan shisha verochban shisha ve'ovyan shelosha* — the Tablets were six *tefachim* long, six *tefachim* wide, and three *tefachim* thick. These were rectangular solids, not the arch-shaped form familiar from iconography. They were placed lengthwise within the *Aron* — *munachos keneged orko shel aron* — meaning both *Luchot* were laid end-to-end along the length of the *Aron*.

### Interior Space

*Abaye* proceeds to work out the arithmetic. The two *Luchot* placed lengthwise consume twelve *tefachim* of the *Aron*'s interior. With a total exterior length of fifteen *tefachim*, and subtracting a half-*tefach* for each wall (*chatzi l'kosel zeh v'chatzi l'kosel zeh*), the usable interior length is fourteen *tefachim*. Fourteen minus twelve leaves two *tefachim* of open space at one end. Similarly for the width: the *Luchot* are six *tefachim* wide; the interior width is nine minus one (a half for each wall) equals eight *tefachim*... but the *Gemara* actually works through this in the width direction as well, arriving at two *tefachim* of open space on the width side after accounting for the wall thickness.

### Torah in Ark

That remaining space — two *tefachim* — is where the *Sefer Torah* rested inside the *Aron*, according to *Rabbi Meir*. The *Gemara* knows this from *Devarim*, where *Moshe* instructs the *Levi'im*: *lakioach et sefer hatorah hazeh vesamtem oto b'tzad aron berit Hashem* — take this *Sefer Torah* and place it *b'tzad aron*, at the side of the *Aron*. *Rabbi Meir* reads *b'tzad* as meaning inside the *Aron*, at the wall — not in the middle between the *Luchot*, but pressed against the inner wall.

That the *Sefer Torah* was indeed inside (rather than outside) the *Aron* is derived, remarkably, from the verse *ein ba'aron rak shnei luchos ha'avanim asher hiniach sham Moshe* — there is nothing in the *Aron* except the two stone *Luchot* that *Moshe* placed there. The *Gemara* reads *ein... rak* as a double limitation — *miyut achar miyut* — and applies the rule that a double limitation is actually an expansion: *ein miyut achar miyut ela l'rabot*. Therefore, paradoxically, the verse that says there is nothing there except the *Luchot* is itself the proof that there was something else — namely, the *Sefer Torah*.

### Width Math

The width calculation mirrors the length calculation. The *Luchot* occupy six *tefachim* of width. After subtracting wall thickness, the interior width allows for two *tefachim* of open space. This is exactly the second dimension of the *Sefer Torah*: six *tefachim* in height, two *tefachim* in width. The *Aron*'s interior space thus provides the derivation for the precise proportions of a *Sefer Torah* — six by two — with the six *tefachim* figure then feeding back into the requirement that height equal circumference.

*Tosafot* addresses an obvious question: if accessing the *Aron* required entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim*, which was forbidden except to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, when would anyone ever take out the *Sefer Torah* — and why do we need to specify that it fit without being *dachuk* (overly tight)? *Tosafot* answers that entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim* was permitted when necessary to maintain the *Sefer Torah* — to prevent it from becoming moldy or deteriorating — and cites the *Gemara* in Eruvin to this effect. There are also Talmudic sources (*masechtot ktanot*) which speak of three *Sifrei Torah* found in the *heichal*, used to adjudicate textual variants by majority — an indication that *Sifrei Torah* in the *Beis Hamikdash* served as authoritative reference texts against which other scrolls were compared.

## Meir vs. Yehuda

### 5-Tefach Amah

*Rabbi Yehuda* disputes *Rabbi Meir*'s calculation by challenging a foundational premise: when measuring the sacred vessels of the *Beis Hamikdash*, the operative *amah* is five *tefachim*, not six. This is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s general position regarding all the *keilim* of the *Beis Hamikdash*. Accordingly, all the *Aron* dimensions must be recalculated. Two and a half times five is twelve and a half *tefachim* in length; one and a half times five is seven and a half *tefachim* in width.

### Recalculation

The *Luchot* dimensions are not disputed — they remain six by six by three — since that measurement derives from *masorah* rather than from the *amah* used for vessels. But now the arithmetic yields a dramatically tighter fit. The interior length is twelve and a half minus one *tefach* for the two walls, leaving eleven and a half *tefachim*. The *Luchot* together take up twelve *tefachim*. There is simply no room — not even room for a *Sefer Torah*, and barely room for the *Luchot* themselves. The walls of the *Aron* are reduced to a single finger-breadth (*etzba*) on each side, an almost impossibly tight fit.

On the width side: interior width is seven and a half minus a half-*tefach* for the two walls = seven *tefachim*, minus the six occupied by the *Luchot* = one *tefach* of open space. (The *girsa* of the *Gemara* here is somewhat difficult, as *Tosafot* and the commentators note, since the arithmetic of the finger-breadths for the walls does not divide evenly; various readings have been proposed.)

### Decorative Poles

What fills the one *tefach* of open space in the width, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, is not a *Sefer Torah* but *amudim* — symbolic poles. The *Gemara* derives their presence from the verse in *Shir HaShirim*: *apiryon asa lo hamelech Shlomo me'atzei haLevanon* — *Shlomo* made an *apiryon* (a palanquin or canopied bed) from the cedars of Lebanon — *amudav asa kesef* — its pillars were made of silver. The *Gemara* understands this as a reference to the poles within the *Aron*, made of silver. *Tosafot* in the name of *Rabbeinu Tam* explains: *nireh le'Rabeinu Tam desiman le'ha'amadat Yisrael haya* — these poles were a symbol of the standing and sustenance of the Jewish people. They were not made of gold specifically *shelo lehazkir ma'aseh egel* — so as not to evoke the sin of the Golden Calf.

The arrangement, then, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, is that the *Luchot* would be slightly shifted toward the center, leaving a half-*tefach* on each side, with a silver pillar in each of those spaces. The visual result is striking: the *Luchot* flanked by silver poles, evoking precisely the image of a *Sefer Torah* on its *atzei chayim*. Even without a physical *Sefer Torah* inside the *Aron*, the symbolic form of a *Sefer Torah* is present.

This is a remarkable observation. The *Luchot* — the most inaccessible sacred object, housed in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, accessible only to the *Kohen Gadol* once a year — are arranged so as to appear as a *Sefer Torah* flanked by its poles. The *Gemara* in *Yevamot* (or wherever the relevant passage appears) teaches that *yekara hi mikohen gadol shenichnas lifnai velifnim* — the *Torah* is more precious than the *Kohen Gadol* who enters the *lifnai velifnim*. What is implicit here is the translation of the supremely hierarchical and inaccessible (*Kodesh Kodashim*, *Kohen Gadol*, *Yom Kippur*) into the universally accessible: the *Sefer Torah*, open to all. The *amudim* inside the *Aron* make that equation visible.

### Philistine Box

Before the *argaz* — the box sent by the *Pelishtim* along with the returned *Aron* containing gold vessels as an *asham* offering — arrived, *Rabbi Yehuda* has a difficulty: where was the *Sefer Torah* kept? According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, there was no room inside the *Aron* for a *Sefer Torah*. The *Gemara* answers: *dappa hava nafik minei* — there was a small shelf extending from the back wall of the *Aron*, and the *Sefer Torah* rested on it.

Once the *argaz* arrived — placed *mitzido*, outside and beside the *Aron*, as the verse states: *ve'et klei hazahav asher hasheivotem lo asham tasimu va'argaz mitzido* — *Rabbi Yehuda* has a place for the *Sefer Torah* as well: *ve'alav Sefer Torah munach* — the *Sefer Torah* rested on top of this box. The verse *lakioach et sefer hatorah hazeh vesamtem oto mitzad aron berit Hashem* is then read as meaning literally outside and beside — not inside the *Aron* at all.

The symbolism compounds. Within the *Beis Hamikdash*'s most sacred chamber, there stood the *Aron* — containing the *Luchot* flanked by silver *amudim* evoking a *Sefer Torah* — and beside it a wooden box with an actual *Sefer Torah* resting on top. An *aron* containing a *Sefer Torah*, standing next to the *Aron HaKodesh*: this is essentially the arrangement every *beit knesset* replicates. The *aron kodesh* in shul, housing a *Sefer Torah*, directly echoes the structure of the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

## One Pole Math

### Pi = 3

The *Gemara* now returns to work through the mathematical implications of the *Sefer Torah* dimensions for *Rabbi Meir*'s position. If the circumference is six *tefachim*, then by the Talmudic approximation *pi* = 3, the diameter of the rolled scroll is two *tefachim*. This is geometrically consistent: circumference = pi × diameter = 3 × 2 = 6.

### Two Poles Problem

But a difficulty arises. The *Gemara* has established that a *Sefer Torah* is rolled toward its middle — *niglal l'emtzi'ito* — implying two poles. If there are two poles, each pole takes up some physical space. A scroll with a diameter of exactly two *tefachim* and a two-*tefach* wide space in the *Aron* leaves no room for a second pole. Moreover, if there are two poles, the center of the scroll is exposed when the scroll is open — *kevan de'emtzoito nigleh nafik lei mitrei tefachei* — meaning the total width would exceed two *tefachim* once the additional pole is accounted for. One might ask: why could the second pole not simply be fit into the gap that exists geometrically between the circular cross-section of the rolled scroll and the rectangular corners of the *Aron*? The answer seems to be that once any pole is attached, the combined width exceeds the available space.

### One Pole Solution

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* resolves the problem directly: *Sefer Azara litchilato hu niglal* — the *Sefer Torah* of the *Azara* had only one pole. It was rolled entirely in one direction, toward the beginning, without a second pole at the other end. (This also answers why the phrase is *litchilato* — toward the beginning — rather than *l'emtzi'ito*.) With only one pole, the diameter is exactly two *tefachim*, fitting perfectly within the two-*tefach* space.

### Slack & Wiggle

*Rav Ashi* adds a further refinement. Even with one pole, placing a two-*tefach* diameter scroll into a two-*tefach* space allows no room to insert or remove it. *Decharich bei purta vecharchayei le'eil* — one leaves the scroll wound slightly less than fully tight, and wraps the tiny excess of parchment on top, creating a small amount of wiggle room. The *Sefer Torah* can thus be eased in and out without being *dachuk* — forced.

## Torah Placement

### Back Shelf

*Rabbi Yehuda* is now asked: before the *argaz* of the *Pelishtim* arrived, where did the *Sefer Torah* sit? According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, there was no room inside the *Aron* for the scroll. His answer, as already noted, is that *dappa hava nafik minei* — a small plank or shelf protruded from the back of the *Aron* — and *yasiv alav Sefer Torah*, the *Sefer Torah* rested upon it.

### Meir: Inside Ark

For *Rabbi Meir*, the verse *vesamtem oto mitzad aron berit Hashem* is interpreted as placing the *Sefer Torah* inside the *Aron* but at the inner wall — *hu miba'ei lei demanach lei mitzad velo manach lei beini luchot*. It would not be placed between the *Luchot* in the center of the *Aron*, but pressed against the inner side wall. *Ule'olam begavei de'arona yasiv* — it always sits within the interior of the *Aron*, just toward the edge rather than the middle.

### Poles Outside

Accordingly, for *Rabbi Meir*, the *amudim* — the decorative silver poles derived from *Shir HaShirim* — were on the outside of the *Aron*, not inside it. *Mibara'ei* — from the outside. The debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* thus becomes a matter of inversion: each holds that one of the two elements (the *Sefer Torah* and the *amudim*) was inside the *Aron* and the other was outside; they disagree about which was which.

## Ark Contents

### Broken Tablets

*Rabbi Meir* requires a source for the placement of the *shivrei luchot* — the broken Tablets — inside the *Aron*, since for him the verse *ein ba'aron rak* is already fully expended on proving the presence of the *Sefer Torah*. The *Gemara* derives this from *Rav Huna*'s reading of a different verse: *asher nikra sham shem Hashem Tzeva'ot yoshev hakruvim* — it is called there the name of *Hashem Tzeva'ot*. *Rav Huna* notes the doubled word: either *sham sham* (there, there) or *shem shem* (name, name). This doubling teaches that both the *luchot* and the *shivrei luchot* were present — *sheluchot veshivrei luchot munachim ba'aron*.

### Yishmael School

For *Rabbi Yehuda*, who cannot use *ein ba'aron rak* for the *Sefer Torah* (since the *Sefer Torah* was outside the *Aron* according to him), the *shem shem* doubling is instead available to prove the *shivrei luchot*. But then *Rabbi Yehuda* needs his own source. He finds it in the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*: *tani debei Rabbi Yishmael* — *asher shibarta vesamtam ba'aron* — even those *luchot* that you broke, place them in the *Aron*. This is read as a direct divine command to include the broken fragments alongside the whole Tablets.

### Breaking Approved

The other *amora* — for whom *debei Rabbi Yishmael* is needed to answer a different question — must derive the presence of the *shivrei luchot* from *Reish Lakish*'s teaching: *amar lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe, yashar kochacha she-shibarta* — the Holy One said to *Moshe*: well done for having broken them. The breaking of the *Luchot* was not merely forgiven; it was retroactively approved. *God* affirmed that shattering the Tablets was the correct act in that moment.

### Broken Tablets Power

It is worth dwelling on the theological resonance with which this *daf* concludes. The *Gemara* has spent its final pages establishing that the broken *Luchot* remained in the *Aron* alongside the whole ones — not discarded, not hidden, but preserved at the center of *kedushah*. And *Reish Lakish*'s *yashar kochacha* suggests that the breaking was not a tragic necessity but something affirmed. There is a power in the broken *Luchot* that is not despite their brokenness but somehow because of it.

Read alongside the broader discussion of this *daf*, the arc becomes visible. The *Luchot* — direct from *Hakadosh Baruch Hu*, housed in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, accessible only to the *Kohen Gadol* once a year — represent the most hierarchical, protected, inaccessible form of *Torah*. The *Sefer Torah* — present in every *beit knesset*, on every bookshelf, carried by every Jew — represents its most universally accessible form. What the *Gemara* has done across this *daf* is make explicit that these two are not in opposition but in continuity. The broken *Luchot* in the *Aron* sit beside the *Sefer Torah*. The *amudim* flanking the *Luchot* evoke the poles of a *Sefer Torah*. The *argaz* beside the *Aron* holds a *Sefer Torah* on its lid — the original *aron kodesh* of every subsequent *beit knesset*. The move from the inaccessible *Luchot* to the accessible *Sefer Torah* is not a fall from something greater; *yashar kochacha she-shibarta* — it was the right thing.
