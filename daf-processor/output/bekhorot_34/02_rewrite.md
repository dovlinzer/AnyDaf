# Bekhorot 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bloodletting & Mum

### Intro: Blood Clot

The daf opens — or rather, continues — with the *mishna* from 33b, covering a case in which a *bechor* in the possession of a *kohen* has developed a blood clot (*dam*) and requires bloodletting in order to survive. The concern is that the act of letting blood may produce a *mum* — a permanent blemish — which would be halachically significant given the prohibition understood by *Chazal* from the verse *lo yihiyeh bo mum*: one may not make a *mum* in *kodshim*. The prohibition presumably applies only to a permanent *mum*, and so the debate turns on how to navigate the animal's medical need against the risk of creating such a blemish.

### R. Yehuda: Prohibit

Rabbi Yehuda takes the most stringent position: one may not perform bloodletting at all. His concern is that even if one intends to let blood only in a location that will not produce a *mum*, the anxiety and urgency of the moment will cause the person to inadvertently perform the procedure in a location where a permanent *mum* does result. To prevent this, Rabbi Yehuda rules that the animal must simply be left to die.

### Sages: Conditional

The *chachamim* take the opposite policy view. If one prohibits bloodletting entirely, they reason, the owner will abandon caution altogether and deliberately inflict a *mum* in order to be rid of the problem — and then slaughter the animal on that basis. Therefore, the *chachamim* permit bloodletting, but only in a location where no *mum* will result. If a *mum* does accidentally result, the animal may still not be slaughtered on that basis; the policy concern about what such permission would lead to remains in force.

### R. Shimon: Lenient

Rabbi Shimon is more permissive still. He allows bloodletting even in a location where a *mum* will or may result — the precise scope of his position will be clarified shortly. The *mishna* thus presents three views ranging from complete prohibition to significant latitude.

### Biblical Source

Underlying the entire debate is a biblical prohibition. *Chazal* derive from the verse *lo yihiyeh bo mum* a prohibition against making a *mum* in *kodshim*. It is worth noting that this verse is understood not as a descriptive statement but as a normative one: "there shall be no blemish in it" is read as "you shall not make a blemish in it." Naturally, only a permanent *mum* triggers the concern, which is why the location of the bloodletting matters so much.

## Baraita: Three Views

### R. Meir Position

A *baraita* now presents a parallel set of opinions, while adding important nuance. The *baraita* opens with the view of Rabbi Meir: *makizin lo et hadam bimkom she-eino oseh bo mum, ve-ein makizin lo et hadam bimkom she-oseh bo mum* — one may let blood only in a place where it will not make a *mum*, but not in a place where it will. This maps exactly onto the position of the *chachamim* in the *mishna*, as expected: the anonymous *chachamim* opposing Rabbi Yehuda in a *mishna* typically reflects the view of Rabbi Meir.

### Sages: No Slaughter

The *baraita*'s *chachamim* go one step further: one may let blood even in a location where a *mum* will certainly result (*af makom she-oseh bo mum*), but one may not slaughter the animal on the basis of that *mum* (*u-vilvad she-lo yishchat al oto mum*). This introduces a position that lies between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon — permitting the creation of the *mum* for medical purposes while retaining the policy restriction against *shechita* on that basis.

### R. Shimon: Allow

Rabbi Shimon in the *baraita* goes further still: *af nishchat al oto mum* — one may even slaughter the animal on the basis of the *mum* that was made in the course of the bloodletting procedure. This makes explicit what was only implied in the *mishna*: Rabbi Shimon not only permits making the *mum* but also permits *shechita* on its basis.

### R. Yehuda: Forbid All

Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *baraita* matches his *mishna* position precisely: *afilu met ein makizin lo et hadam* — even if the animal will die, one may not let blood at all, out of concern that it will lead to an impermissible *mum*.

The *baraita* thus clarifies the spectrum of opinion and, crucially, introduces an explicit debate about whether *shechita* on the basis of a medically-necessary *mum* is permissible.

## Terumah Comparison

### Comparison Intro

The *gemara* now records a teaching transmitted by Rabbi Eliezer to his son — and according to some, by Rabbi Chiyya to his son — comparing the debate of the *baraita* to a parallel dispute in an entirely different context: *ke-machloket kan kach machloket be-chavit shel terumah* — just as they debate here, so too they debate regarding a barrel of *terumah*.

### Terumah Setup

The case is as follows: a barrel of *terumah* wine has developed a doubtful *tumah* — it is uncertain whether it became *tamei*. This creates what amounts to the worst of all possible situations. If the *terumah* were definitively *tamei*, it could not be eaten or drunk, but one could derive benefit from it through its destruction — for example, by sprinkling the wine as an air freshener, a legitimate form of benefit that comes through consumption. If it were *tahor*, it could simply be eaten. But in a state of doubt, one can neither drink it (since it may be *tamei*) nor sprinkle it (since it may be *tahor*, and sprinkling would be an inappropriate use of *terumah* that is fit to eat). The barrel is thus completely unusable in its current state of doubt.

### R. Eliezer Terumah

Rabbi Eliezer says: *im haita bemakom haturpah yanichena bemakom hamutzna* — if it was in an exposed place, put it in a private place; if it was uncovered, cover it. Even though these actions will not change the barrel's status or resolve the doubt — the *terumah* will remain doubtfully *tamei* and unusable — Rabbi Eliezer maintains that one is obligated to protect it from further possible *tumah*. Since it might in fact be *tahor*, the duty to guard it remains in force.

### R. Yehoshua Terumah

Rabbi Yehoshua takes the pragmatic view: *im haita munachat bemakom mutzna yanichena bemakom haturpah* — if it was in a private place, move it to a public place; if it was covered, uncover it. The goal is to expose the *terumah* to conditions that will cause it to become definitively *tamei* — not directly through one's own action, but indirectly. Once it is definitively *tamei*, one can at least salvage something from the situation by using it in a permissible form of benefit through destruction.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel takes neither approach: *lo yachadesh bah davar* — do nothing at all. One should neither protect the barrel nor expose it. Both courses of action may raise their own concerns, and since neither resolves the underlying problem, the correct policy is simply to leave the barrel alone.

### Correspondence

The *gemara* attempts to draw a correspondence between the two disputes. Rabbi Meir of the *baraita*, who permits bloodletting only where no *mum* results — addressing the animal's need while avoiding anything halachically problematic — is said to parallel Rabbi Eliezer, who protects the *terumah* without taking any questionable action. The common thread is: help the object at risk, but do nothing that crosses a halachic line. The *chachamim* of the *baraita*, who permit doing something halachically questionable (creating a *mum*) for the sake of salvaging the situation, parallel Rabbi Yehoshua, who permits the indirect exposure of *terumah* to *tumah* in order to make it usable. Rabbi Yehuda, who says do nothing and let the animal die, parallels Rabban Gamliel's hands-off approach.

### Objection 1: Direct/Indi…

The *gemara* immediately subjects this comparison to a battery of objections. The first: *dilma ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Meir hatam — dekavid beyadayim* — perhaps Rabbi Meir's restriction in the *bechor* case is specifically because one is performing a direct, hands-on act that may produce a *mum*. By contrast, in Rabbi Yehoshua's scenario with the wine, the *tumah* would come about only indirectly (*de-garma*) — the person merely exposes the barrel, and external agents cause the *tumah*. Perhaps, then, Rabbi Meir would actually agree with Rabbi Yehoshua by the *terumah* case, where only indirect action is involved.

### Objection 2: Facts/Urgen…

The second objection runs in the opposite direction: perhaps Rabbi Eliezer's protectiveness by the wine is specifically because the facts remain unresolved — *shema yavo Eliyahu ve-yitaharenu*, perhaps Elijah will come and clarify matters, and the doubt will be resolved. There is something unknown, so one must be cautious. But in the *bechor* case, the facts are not in doubt — if nothing is done, the animal will die within hours. Given that urgency, perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would permit even making a *mum* in order to save the animal, in line with the *rabbanan*.

### Objections 3-6

The *gemara* continues pressing the analysis from every direction, reversing the logic for each position in turn. The *rabbanan* of the *baraita* who permit making a *mum* may do so specifically because of the urgency — *de-ei shavik lei mayit*, if left alone it will die — and they might agree with Rabbi Eliezer in the *terumah* case, where the facts are uncertain and Elijah might yet resolve the doubt. By symmetry, Rabbi Yehuda's hands-off ruling in the *bechor* case may reflect only his concern about direct action (*bekavid beyadayim*), while in the *terumah* case — where exposure is only indirect — he might side with Rabbi Yehoshua. And Rabban Gamliel's inaction in the *terumah* case may be driven by the fact-uncertainty rationale (*shema yavo Eliyahu*), not by a general policy of non-intervention; in the *bechor* case, where the urgency is real and immediate, he might permit action in line with the *rabbanan*.

### Cases Not Parallel

The conclusion is clear: the two disputes are not genuinely comparable despite their surface similarities. The *bechor* case involves direct action under conditions of urgency with known facts; the *terumah* case involves indirect action under conditions of factual uncertainty with no urgency. These differences cut in opposite directions for each position, making it impossible to draw firm correspondences. The *gemara* adds a further and decisive point: *hacha bikrai peligi ve-hacha bikrai peligi* — in each case, the dispute is grounded in its own specific scriptural sources, which are distinct from each other, as will now be shown.

## Scripture: Mum B'Ba'al M…

### Assumption: Blemished

Turning to the scriptural underpinnings of the *bechor* debate, the *gemara* — in a teaching of Rav Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — proceeds from a foundational assumption: a *bechor* about to die from a blood clot is treated as if it already has a *mum*, as if it were a *ba'al mum*. The entire scriptural analysis that follows rests on this premise.

### Tosafot: Analysis

This assumption is not self-explanatory, and *Tosafot* addresses it directly. The animal does not display any visible *mum* — and a *mum* is by definition something visible. *Tosafot* suggests two possible approaches. One is that since the animal is effectively doomed — it cannot survive without bloodletting, and bloodletting will produce a *mum* — it is treated, in some sense, as already dead, and a dead animal has no *mum* in the ordinary sense. The other, and perhaps more workable, framing is not to say the animal is literally a *ba'al mum* but rather that it cannot be seen as a *tam* — a fully unblemished animal eligible for the altar. A *tam* animal is one that can actually be brought as a *korban*, but this *bechor* cannot realistically reach the *Beit Hamikdash* before dying; it will not survive. Therefore, while it may not technically carry a visible *mum*, it falls outside the category of *tamim*, and the relevant halachic analysis proceeds on that basis — essentially as an analogy to the case of making a *mum* in an animal that already has a *mum*, rather than a literal categorization.

### Debate Restated

With this framing in place, the *gemara* restates the core debate: Rabbi Meir prohibits making any *mum* in such an animal, even given its precarious state; the *chachamim* permit it, reasoning that since the animal is already effectively outside the category of *tamim*, creating a further *mum* causes no additional wrong.

### Textual Parallels

The scriptural basis for this dispute is now laid out. The *gemara* cites two precedents where everyone agrees (*hakol modim*) about the law. The first: *ha-kol modim be-mechameitz achar mechameitz she-hu chayav* — all agree that one who causes dough of a *mincha* offering to become *chameitz* is liable even if the dough had already become *chameitz* through someone else's action. The basis is that the Torah uses two distinct formulations: *lo teiafeh chameitz* (do not bake it as *chameitz*) and *lo teiaseh chameitz* (do not make it *chameitz*), signaling that each stage of the process constitutes an independent transgression. Even after the dough is already *chameitz*, a second person who bakes it is also liable, because the prohibition is about the act at each stage of the process, not merely about producing the final state. The second precedent: *ha-kol modim be-mesareis achar mesareis she-hu chayav* — all agree that one who castrates an animal (*mesareis*) after it has already been partially castrated by another is also liable. The Torah lists multiple forms of genital damage — *m'ruach, chasuach, nasuk, karus* — to indicate that each distinct act of mutilation constitutes a separate transgression, even if the animal was already sterile from an earlier act. In both cases, the point is that even when the critical state has already been achieved, performing a subsequent act in that same category carries liability.

### R. Meir: Act Focus

These two cases of universal agreement set up the contested question. The dispute, the *gemara* says, concerns specifically *ha-matil mum be-va'al mum* — making a *mum* in an animal that already has a *mum*. Here the views diverge. Rabbi Meir holds: *kol mum lo yiheyeh bo* — the verse "no blemish shall be in it" is understood as "you shall not make any blemish in it," with the word *kol* ("any") indicating that the prohibition applies regardless of whether a *mum* already exists. The act of making a *mum* is prohibited as an act, independent of what state the animal was already in.

### Sages: Result Focus

The *rabbanan* read the verse differently. The full verse begins *temimi yiheyeh le-ratzon* — "it shall be unblemished to be acceptable" — and continues *kol mum lo yiheyeh bo*. The *rabbanan* argue that the prohibition is conditional: it applies only when the animal is *tamim*, only when making a *mum* would move it from the state of being unblemished to the state of being blemished. If the animal is already not *tamim* — already ineligible for the altar — then making a *mum* in it does not violate this prohibition, because the relevant transition (*temimi* to *ba'al mum*) is not occurring.

### Act vs. Result

This is the fundamental conceptual divide. Rabbi Meir understands the prohibition as targeting the *act* of making a *mum*: the act is intrinsically prohibited in any animal with sanctity, regardless of its current condition. The *rabbanan* understand the prohibition as targeting the *result*: the prohibited outcome is the transformation of a *tamim* animal into a *ba'al mum*, and if that transformation cannot occur because the animal is already blemished, there is no prohibition.

### Grama Implication

This conceptual divide has a direct implication for *grama* — indirect action. If the prohibition is about producing the result, then causing the result indirectly is equally forbidden; both direct and indirect action equally bring about the prohibited state. This is why the *rabbanan*, according to this analysis, also prohibit *grama* when the animal is *tamim*: even if you wrap a piece of dough around the animal's ear so that a dog bites it off, you have still brought about the prohibited result, and the verse *kol mum* extends to cover indirect causation. Rabbi Meir, on the other hand, who frames the prohibition as being about the act itself, may not extend the prohibition to *grama* at all. If the problem is performing the act, then indirect causation — where one does not personally perform the act — would not be prohibited. These positions are, in this sense, two sides of the same coin: the *rabbanan* who permit making a *mum* in an already-blemished animal are also the ones who extend the prohibition to indirect action when the animal is *tamim*; Rabbi Meir, who forbids even blemish-upon-blemish, may not require the prohibition to reach indirect action.

## Scripture: Terumah

### Guarding Source

The *gemara* turns to the parallel scriptural basis underlying the *terumah* dispute, as reported by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, and corroborated by Reish Lakish and Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha. The relevant verse is *va-ani hinei natati lecha et mishmeret terumotai* — "and I hereby give you the guarding of My *terumot*." The debate concerns whether this obligation of *shemirah* — guarding — extends to *terumah* whose status is in doubt.

### R. Eliezer: Dual

Rabbi Eliezer reads the verse as referring to two distinct categories of *terumah*, both of which must be guarded. The word *terumotai* is read in the plural — "My *terumot*" — indicating that the duty of guarding applies both to *terumah tehorah*, definitively pure *terumah*, and to *terumah teluyah*, *terumah* of doubtful status. Even when the *terumah* is of uncertain purity and cannot currently be used, the obligation to protect it from further *tumah* remains. Therefore, one must put it in a covered, protected place.

### R. Yehoshua: Singular

Rabbi Yehoshua reads the same verse through its written form. As noted, the word is actually spelled *terumati* — without a *vav* — which could be read as the singular: "My *terumah*." On this reading, the obligation of guarding applies only to *terumah* that is fully functional and beneficial to the *kohen* — that is, *terumah tehorah*. Doubtful *terumah*, from which no benefit can currently be derived, carries no guarding obligation. Since one need not guard it, one is free to expose it so that it becomes definitively *tamei*, after which some limited form of benefit can at least be extracted.

### Different Bases

This confirms the *gemara*'s earlier point: the two disputes — that of the *bechor* and that of the *terumah* — are not only different in their circumstances and policy logic, they are grounded in entirely separate scriptural sources with no textual connection to each other.

### Benefit Logic

Rabbi Yehoshua's position is then refined further. The phrase *natati lecha* — "I have given it *to you*" — is understood as the operative limiting condition. The obligation to guard *terumah* exists only insofar as the *terumah* is genuinely *lecha*, genuinely available to and beneficial for the *kohen*. Doubtful *terumah* that cannot be eaten or otherwise used is not, in any meaningful sense, *lecha* at this moment. Since you cannot benefit from it, you bear no obligation to guard it — and since no guarding obligation exists, the pragmatic course is to allow it to become definitively *tamei* so that some benefit can be recovered.

### Possibility Argument

Rabbi Eliezer counters: the doubtful *terumah* is still potentially *lecha* — it may yet prove to be *tahor*. One's current inability to benefit from it stems from ignorance of its true status, not from its actual condition. The object is possibly fit; therefore the guarding obligation remains. As the *gemara* phrases it: *shema yavo Eliyahu ve-yitaharenu* — perhaps Elijah will come and clarify the matter. The *terumah* might be *tahor*; that possibility is enough to sustain the duty of protection.

## Ruling: R. Shimon

### Rav Yehuda Ruling

With the theoretical analysis complete, the *gemara* turns to the practical *halacha*. Rav Yehuda cites a ruling in the name of Shmuel: *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the law follows Rabbi Shimon.

### Which R. Shimon?

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak immediately challenges this ruling with a pointed question: *hai Rabbi Shimon* — which Rabbi Shimon? There is a Rabbi Shimon in the *mishna* and a Rabbi Shimon in the *baraita*, and their positions are meaningfully different.

### If Mishna

If the ruling follows the Rabbi Shimon of the *mishna*, then the teaching is redundant. It is already well established elsewhere that Shmuel rules that *davar she-eino mitkavein* — an unintentional act — is *mutar*. Rav Chanan bar Ashi already transmits this ruling from Shmuel explicitly, and Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin teaches it without attribution to any particular case. A ruling by Shmuel that the *halacha* follows the Rabbi Shimon of the *mishna* would add nothing new.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The *gemara* makes the broader *machlokes* explicit: Rav rules like Rabbi Yehuda — that is, an action likely to produce a prohibited consequence even unintentionally is forbidden — while Shmuel rules like Rabbi Shimon, permitting such unintentional acts. This is confirmed by Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi reporting in Rav's name that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda, and by Rav Chanan bar Ashi in Shmuel's name that it follows Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin transmits the same *machlokes* without a chain of names.

### Two Explanations

The *gemara* thus raises a conceptual question: the ruling attributed to Shmuel here is stated in the context of the *bechor* case, but what precisely is Shmuel saying? There are two distinct rationales that could underlie permission to make a *mum* in the *bechor* with a blood clot: either the animal's condition is treated as equivalent to already having a *mum*, making this a case of *mum* upon *mum* — which is what the earlier scriptural analysis established — or the act is permitted because one's intention is solely to let blood, not to create a *mum*, making this a case of *eino mitkavein*.

### Rashi Approach

Rashi takes a striking position. He holds that even where a *mum* will *certainly* result from the bloodletting, it can still be classified as *eino mitkavein*, because the person's purpose is surgical — to save the animal — and not to create a blemish. What appears to be a *pesik reisha* (an inevitable consequence) is reframed by Rashi as *eino mitkavein* because the agent's intent is categorically different from the prohibited act. This is reminiscent of the category of *melacha she-eina tzricha le-gufah* familiar from Shabbat: you know exactly what you are doing, but your purpose diverges entirely from the purpose that defines the prohibition. Rashi extends this concept beyond Shabbat, treating it as a form of *eino mitkavein* applicable here as well.

### Tosafot Distinction

*Tosafot* objects to this reading and draws a sharper distinction between the two texts. In the *mishna*, Rabbi Shimon says *af al pi she-hu oseh bo mum* — "even though it makes a *mum*" — while the *baraita* uses the phrase *bimkom she-oseh bo mum* — "in a place where it makes a *mum*." *Tosafot* argues that *bimkom she-oseh bo mum* means the *mum* is certain to result from the procedure. In that scenario, the only coherent explanation for why Rabbi Shimon permits it is the *mum*-upon-*mum* rationale: the animal is already treated as blemished, so creating an additional *mum* is not prohibited. The *mishna*, however, says only "even though it makes a *mum*" — which *Tosafot* reads as a case where a *mum* might result accidentally, not inevitably. That case is straightforwardly explained as *eino mitkavein* in the ordinary sense: one does not intend the blemish and it is not certain to occur.

### Must Be Baraita

This distinction is decisive. If the *mishna*'s Rabbi Shimon is simply relying on the principle of *eino mitkavein* — a principle Shmuel already endorses explicitly — then Shmuel's ruling would tell us nothing new by citing that Rabbi Shimon. The ruling must therefore be referring to Rabbi Shimon of the *baraita*, whose position goes further: he permits *shechita* on the basis of the *mum* that was created, without any policy-based restriction. Accordingly, Rav Yehuda's ruling in Shmuel's name is that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon of the *baraita* — one may perform bloodletting even where a *mum* will certainly result, and one may subsequently slaughter the animal on the basis of that *mum*, without concern that such permission will be exploited. This is confirmed explicitly by Rav Sheshes the son of Rabbi Idi, who transmits the ruling in those precise terms: *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon de-baraisa*.

## Nicking Ear

### Nicking Prohibited

A new *mishna* introduces a related but distinct case. If someone intentionally nicks the ear of a *bechor* — a classic form of *mum*, since cuts in the ear characteristically do not heal — he may never slaughter the animal on the basis of that *mum*. Unlike the bloodletting case, where making the *mum* was at least done out of medical necessity, here the person deliberately produced a *mum* in order to gain permission to slaughter the *bechor*. All agree that *shechita* on the basis of this deliberately inflicted *mum* is never permissible.

### R. Eliezer: Forever

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: the *bechor* is permanently disqualified. Even if a new, independent *mum* later appears on the animal — a blemish that had nothing to do with the owner's act — one may still never slaughter the animal. The prohibition extends to all future *shechita*, regardless of what blemish is invoked.

### Sages: Another Blemish OK

The *chachamim* disagree. Their position is that while one may never slaughter on the basis of the deliberately inflicted *mum*, if a new, independently occurring blemish subsequently appears, *shechita* on that basis is entirely permissible. The prohibition is limited to the product of the forbidden act; it does not color the animal's status in perpetuity.

### Deterrent Logic

The policy logic behind Rabbi Eliezer's position is clear. If the only consequence of deliberately creating a *mum* is that you cannot slaughter on the basis of that specific *mum*, there is no meaningful deterrent. The person might reason: if no one notices, I benefit; if they do notice, I lose nothing beyond what I already had. Making the disqualification permanent — so that the animal can never be slaughtered at all — creates a genuine disincentive.

### Question Severity

This prompts a challenge: does Rabbi Eliezer consistently impose such severe and permanent penalties? The question is sharpened by a contrasting case.

### Tzaraat Parallel

The *gemara* cites a case of *tzaraat* — a skin condition creating ritual impurity — in which someone intentionally excises the affected spot (*baheret*) in order to achieve *tahara*. Since the spot is no longer present, it cannot be evaluated by a *kohen*. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *le-ch'she-yivaled lo nega acher yitaher mimenu* — the person will only achieve *tahara* when a new *nega* appears and he is subsequently declared *tahor* from that one, at which point we assume the same process would have resolved the original spot. Notably, Rabbi Eliezer does *not* say the person is permanently impure; he allows one specific path to *tahara*, even if it may take a very long time. The *chachamim* in this context are actually stricter than Rabbi Eliezer: they say *tahara* is possible only if the condition spreads to cover the entire body (*ad she-tifrach be-kulo*) or, in *Tosafot*'s reading, if the remaining portion of the spot shrinks below the requisite minimum size — in either case, one would be *tahor* even were the excised portion still present.

### Rabbah Resolves

Rabbah bar Rav Yosef resolves the apparent inconsistency in Rabbi Eliezer's positions: *ki kanis Rabbi Eliezer be-mamono, be-gufo lo kanis* — Rabbi Eliezer imposes the permanent penalty when the transgression involves *property*; when it involves a person's *body*, he does not.

### Property vs. Body

Two considerations explain the distinction. First, a penalty of permanent bodily impurity would be an extraordinarily severe imposition on a person — too severe to serve merely as a policy deterrent. Second, the deterrence calculus differs: in the case of the *bechor*, if there is no permanent consequence to deliberately making a *mum*, the owner faces no real downside from the scheme. In the case of *tzaraat*, however, the prospect of remaining *tamei* indefinitely until a second *nega* appears — which may never happen — is already a severe enough deterrent without extending it to permanent disqualification.

## Formalism vs. Policy

### Rava Question

Rava presses the analysis further. Even granting the distinction between property and person, one still needs to account for the fact that the *chachamim* also shift positions between the two cases. In the *mishna* about the *bechor*'s ear, the *chachamim* say that a new independently-arising *mum* makes *shechita* permissible — a relatively lenient position. Yet in the *tzaraat* case, the *chachamim* are the strict ones, allowing *tahara* only under quite limited circumstances. How is the *rabbanan*'s position internally consistent?

### Different Approaches

The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* are, at a foundational level, operating from entirely different jurisprudential frameworks. Rabbi Eliezer reasons from policy: what penalty, calibrated to the severity of the transgression and the practical realities of the case, will best deter future violations? The *chachamim* reason from legal formalism: when someone performs a prohibited act, the proper response is to negate that act — to treat it juridically as though it never occurred.

### Formalism: Negate

Under the *chachamim*'s formalist approach, the consequence of any prohibited act is that the act is voided. The law proceeds as though the person had not done what they did.

### Nick Negated

In the case of the deliberately nicked ear: the nick is treated as if it never happened. The *mum* is negated, juridically speaking. That means the only lasting consequence is that this particular *mum* is unavailable as a basis for *shechita* — since we treat it as nonexistent — but any other, independently arising *mum* is entirely independent of what the owner did and may be relied upon freely.

### Spot Still There

In the case of *tzaraat*: the excision is treated as if it never happened. The spot is deemed still present. This means the person's path to *tahara* must be one that would work *even if the original spot were still there*. That is why the *chachamim* require either that the condition spread to cover the whole body (which would produce *tahara* regardless) or that the remaining fragment shrink below the minimum size (which would equally mean the original spot, had it been present, was also subthreshold). In both scenarios, *tahara* is achieved through a process that is valid independently of whether the excised portion ever existed.

### Policy: Deterrent

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, is not engaged in negating acts. He is asking: what is the appropriate deterrent, proportionate to the nature and stakes of the transgression? For property violations where the practical temptation is real and the downside of getting caught is otherwise negligible, a severe and permanent penalty is warranted. For violations affecting a person's body, where the suffering involved in an open-ended wait for another *nega* already creates significant deterrence, a lesser penalty suffices. This policy-sensitivity, rather than a formal rule about negating actions, drives all of Rabbi Eliezer's rulings in these cases.
