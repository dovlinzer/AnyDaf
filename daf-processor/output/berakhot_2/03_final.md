### Mishna: Shema Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵאֵימָתַי קוֹרִין אֶת שְׁמַע בָּעֲרָבִין? מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִים לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. עַד סוֹף הָאַשְׁמוּרָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד חֲצוֹת. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From when does one recite *Shema* in the evening? From the time when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*.** Until when does the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* extend? **Until the end of the first watch.** The term used in the Torah (Deuteronomy 6:7) to indicate the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* is *beshokhbekha*, when you lie down, which refers to the time in which individuals go to sleep. Therefore, the time for the recitation of *Shema* is the first portion of the night, when individuals typically prepare for sleep. **That is the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. The Rabbis say: until midnight. Rabban Gamliel says** one may recite *Shema* **until dawn.**

*Berachot* *daf says very simply* 2 עמוד א.

*me'eimatai* קורין את *shema* בערבין? — When do you begin saying Shema in the evening?

משעה שהכהנים *nichnasim* לאכול בתרומתן — From the time that the Kohanim enter in to eat their terumah. The Kohanim, if they had become tamei during the day, would go to the mikvah near the end of the day. They would be able to eat terumah at nightfall, once the residual tumah was removed. So when they come in to eat their terumah, that is when you can begin saying the evening Shema.

*ad* סוף האשמורה הראשונה — Until the end of the first watch. The night was divided into watches — not a wristwatch, but the watches of a city's guards — and until the end of the first watch, roughly three to four hours into the night, you could say the Shema. דברי רבי אליעזר — that is the position of Rabbi Eliezer.

וחכמים אומרים *ad* *chatzos* — The Chachamim say until midnight. Assuming a day with sunrise and sunset both at six, Rabbi Eliezer's position gives you until approximately 9 or 10 PM; the Chachamim say until midnight.

רבן גמליאל אומר *ad* שיעלה עמוד השחר — Rabban Gamliel says until the morning star rises, normally assumed to be about 72 minutes before sunrise, meaning you can essentially say Shema throughout the entire night until the day begins to break.

### Shema End-Time Debate

This debate is not about when *shema begins* — that will be discussed in the Gemara — but about how late you may still recite it. Rashi (not on this daf but on the next) states simply that the dispute turns on how one reads the verse in this week's parasha, Va'etchanan: ובשכבך ובקומך — when you go to sleep and when you rise up. The question is: does this mean the time when people are going to bed, or does it mean during the entire period of sleep?

That people begin going to bed at nightfall seems agreed upon by all, especially in an era without electric light, when people worked from daybreak and retired at dark. The dispute concerns how late into the night the obligation extends. If it means only the time when people are heading to bed, then it lasts perhaps a few hours into the night. If it encompasses the entire period of sleep, then it extends all the way through until daybreak. The middle position — that of the Chachamim, who say chatzot — requires separate analysis.

### Rabban Gamliel's Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה וּבָאוּ בָנָיו מִבֵּית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא קָרִינוּ אֶת שְׁמַע, אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם לֹא עָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר חַיָּיבִין אַתֶּם לִקְרוֹת. וְלֹא זוֹ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא כָּל מַה שֶּׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים ״עַד חֲצוֹת״, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates that Rabban Gamliel practiced in accordance with his ruling. There was an **incident** where Rabban Gamliel's **sons returned** very late **from a wedding hall. They said to him,** as they had been preoccupied with celebrating with the groom and bride: **We did not recite *Shema.* He said to them: If the dawn has not** yet **arrived, you are obligated to recite** *Shema*. Since Rabban Gamliel's opinion disagreed with that of the Rabbis, he explained to his sons that the Rabbis actually agree with him, **and** that it is **not only** with regard to the *halakha* of the recitation of *Shema*, **but rather, wherever the Sages say until midnight, the mitzva** may be performed **until dawn.**

Rabban Gamliel here rules in accordance with his own position.

### Midnight vs. Dawn Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים וְאֵבָרִים, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר וְכָל הַנֶּאֱכָלִים לְיוֹם אֶחָד, מִצְוָתָן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר. אִם כֵּן, לָמָה אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים ״עַד חֲצוֹת״ — כְּדֵי לְהַרְחִיק אָדָם מִן הָעֲבֵירָה:
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Gamliel cites several cases in support of his claim, such as **the burning of fats and limbs** on the altar. Due to the quantity of offerings each day, the priests were often unable to complete the burning of all of the fats and limbs, so they continued to be burned into the night, as it is written: "This is the law of the burnt offering: The burnt offering shall remain upon the pyre on the altar all night until morning, while the fire on the altar burns it" (Leviticus 6:2). **And,** with regard to **all** sacrifices, such as the sin-offerings and the guilt-offerings **that are eaten for one day** and night; although the Sages state that they may be eaten only until midnight, by Torah law they may be eaten **until dawn.** This is in accordance with the verse: "On the day on which it is offered must you eat. Do not leave it until the morning" (Leviticus 7:15). **If so, why did the Sages say** that they may be eaten only **until midnight?** This is **in order to distance a person from transgression,** as if one believes that he has until dawn to perform the mitzva, he might be negligent and postpone it until the opportunity to perform the mitzva has passed.

The Mishna then continues: ולא זו בלבד אמרו — it is not only regarding this that they said so. It may be unclear whether this is Rabban Gamliel still speaking or the Mishna itself inserting a comment. אלא כל מה שאמרו *chachamim say it's* *ad* *chatzos* מצותן *ad* שיעלה עמוד השחר — Anything the sages said must be done by midnight, the real halakhic obligation extends through the entire night. Midnight is not a halakhically significant time in the ordinary sense — the Torah does not treat it as a meaningful boundary the way it treats, say, midday in certain contexts.

Two examples are given. First: הקטר חלבים ואיברים מצותן *ad* שיעלה עמוד השחר — burning the fats and limbs of sacrifices. The sacrifice had to be offered by day, and the blood sprinkled by day, but the fats and limbs could be burned on the altar throughout the entire night. If they were left off the altar the entire night, however, they could not be burned the following day.

Second: וכל הנאכלים ליום אחד מצותן *ad* שיעלה עמוד השחר — all korbanot eaten for one day. This refers primarily to kodshei kodashim such as the chatat and the asham, eaten by the Kohanim for one day, and the todah, a korban of thanksgiving, which the Yisrael eats only for a day and the following night. The mitzvah of eating all of these extends until daybreak.

אם כן למה אמרו *chachamim say it's* *ad* *chatzos*? — If so, why did the sages say until midnight? כדי להרחיק אדם מן העבירה — In order to distance a person from transgression. If you know you have the entire night, you keep pushing it off, and before you know it you have fallen asleep or become too impaired to say it. So they told you to say it by midnight — but the underlying obligation extends throughout the night.

### Fence: Ad Chatzot

This is, in essence, the rabbinic enactment of midnight as a fence against transgression — leharchik adam min ha'aveira. The Chachamim, in setting midnight as the deadline, were not identifying a genuine halakhic time limit, but were creating a protective boundary to ensure people actually fulfilled the obligation.

### Rashi: Fats & Chatzot

Rashi points out that the burning of the fats was never subject to the midnight limit at all. That case was only included here because it was organically part of the broader statement about eating sacrifices. The midnight-as-fence idea never applied to it.

There is also a small question whether this final clause — that whatever the sages said until midnight really means until dawn — is part of Rabban Gamliel's own statement or a comment of the Mishna. If it belongs to Rabban Gamliel, then what he is saying is: in principle I agree you should aim for midnight, but if you missed it, you still have the whole night. Under this reading, Rabban Gamliel is partially accommodating the Chachamim's position. Alternatively — and this is normally how it is understood — it is the Mishna inserting a note: even the rabbis who say midnight agree in principle with Rabban Gamliel that the actual obligation extends all night. The distinction matters for how we read the tension between Rabban Gamliel and the Chachamim.

## Why Berakhot Begins Shas

### Order of Tractates

Why do we begin Shas with Berakhot? We begin with Seder *zeraim because* Zeraim is the first order of the Mishna, and Berakhot is the first tractate of Zeraim. Now, tractates within a seder are ordered by size — the tractate with the most chapters comes first — and Berakhot has the most chapters in Zeraim. That explains the mechanics. But it is worth asking what the connection is between Berakhot and the agricultural framework of Zeraim in the first place, since while part of Berakhot concerns blessings over food, much of it deals with Shema, Tefillah, and blessings over a wide range of things.

### Zeraim = Daily Life

One way to think about this is to read Seder Zeraim not narrowly as agriculture, but more broadly as the order of daily life. In an agricultural society, daily life is very much organized around planting, harvesting, and the ritual obligations attached to the land. But another central component of daily life — equally defining and equally constant — is prayer, blessings, and the recitation of Shema. Under this broader reading, Berakhot fits naturally as the opening tractate of Zeraim, because both are about the structure of everyday religious existence.

### Six Sedarim Structure

If you take this view seriously, you can trace a coherent progression across all six sedarim of Shas. You begin with Zeraim — daily life. You move to Moed — the special days, Shabbat, Yom Tov, and the calendar. Then Nashim — from the individual to the family. The word nashim means women, and also wives; this order is concerned, from the perspective of the male subject of most halakhic discourse, with family law and the relationships that define domestic life. Then Nezikin — from the family outward into society: civil law, damages, courts, interpersonal obligations, the individual functioning within a broader community. And only at the very end do you arrive at Kodshim and Taharot — the laws of the Mikdash and purity.

This structure can be read in two very different directions. On one reading, everything builds toward Kodshim and Taharot, and the Temple stands as the culminating ideal toward which all of Jewish life is oriented. On another reading — arguably more resonant for life after the destruction — what actually defines the texture of a sanctified religious life is determined long before you reach Kodshim and Taharot. It is determined by Shema and Shemoneh Esrei and Berakhot, by the laws of the land and of special days, by how we behave in our families and in our communities. The bulk of what our religious life is made of is already fully present in the earlier orders.

### Implicit in Mishna

Several things are implicit in the very first Mishna that deserve to be made explicit. The first is that the Mishna assumes you are already in the conversation. It opens with "from when do we read Shema at night?" without first establishing that there is an obligation to read Shema at all. The obligation, the verse, the relevant context — all of this is taken for granted. The Mishna similarly assumes familiarity with the Kohanim's terumah — that there exists a time when they must wait before eating it, and that they undergo immersion near the end of the day. You are simply expected to know all of this.

### Rabbinic Fence-Making

The second implicit point is that the very first Mishna already introduces rabbinic legislation alongside biblical obligation. On one level, the rabbis are interpreting Torah: they are telling us when *beshochbecha uvekumecha* begins and ends. But at the same time, they are legislating — building fences, protecting people from unintentional transgression. The midnight enactment is not an interpretation of any verse; it is a protective measure. The principle underlying it is ועשו משמרת למשמרתי — make a fence for my teaching. This dual role of the rabbis, as both interpreters and legislators, is already fully on display in the first Mishna of Shas.

### Q: Shemoneh Esrei

One might ask: if this tractate deals with prayer, why doesn't the Mishna begin with Shemoneh Esrei? In fact, Masechet Berakhot is considerably more logically organized than most other masechtot. The tractate moves through its topics in clear progression: one chapter for Shema, then Shemoneh Esrei, then Birkat Hamazon, and so on. Beginning by asking "when does the obligation begin?" is the natural starting point for the topic at hand. Shemoneh Esrei will be addressed in due course.

## Gemara Opening Questions

### Tanna Heicha Kai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנָּא הֵיכָא קָאֵי דְּקָתָנֵי ״מֵאֵימָתַי״?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Mishna opens with the laws concerning the appropriate time to recite *Shema* with the question: From when does one recite *Shema* in the evening? With regard to this question, the Gemara asks: **On the basis of what** prior knowledge **does** the ***tanna*** of our mishna ask: **From when?** It would seem from his question that the obligation to recite *Shema* in the evening was already established, and that the *tanna* seeks only to clarify details that relate to it. But our mishna is the very first mishna in the Talmud.

The Gemara notices that the Mishna opens with a timing question without first establishing what obligation is being timed. It is as if the Mishna enters a conversation already in progress — first tell me there is an obligation, then tell me when it begins. This question gestures toward the same point noted above: the Mishna assumes a great deal of shared context.

### Evening Before Morning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ: מַאי שְׁנָא דְּתָנֵי בְּעַרְבִית בְּרֵישָׁא? לִתְנֵי דְּשַׁחֲרִית בְּרֵישָׁא! תַּנָּא אַקְּרָא קָאֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״. וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: זְמַן קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע דִּשְׁכִיבָה אֵימַת? — מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: יָלֵיף מִבְּרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר יוֹם אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And furthermore, what distinguishes the evening** *Shema*, that it was taught **first? Let** the *tanna* **teach** regarding the recitation of **the morning** *Shema* **first.** Since most mitzvot apply during the day, the *tanna* should discuss the morning *Shema* before discussing the evening *Shema*, just as the daily morning offering is discussed before the evening offering (*Tosefot HaRosh*). The Gemara offers a single response to both questions: **The *tanna* bases himself on the verse as it is written:** "You will talk of them when you sit in your home, and when you walk along the way, **when you lie down, and when you arise"** (Deuteronomy 6:7). By teaching the laws of the evening *Shema* first, the *tanna* has established that the teachings of the Oral Torah correspond to that which is taught in the Written Torah. **And** based on the Written Torah, **the *tanna* teaches** the oral law: **When is the time for the recitation of *Shema* of lying down** as commanded in the Torah? **From when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*.** Just as the Written Torah begins with the evening *Shema*, so too must the Oral Torah. However, there is another possible explanation for why the mishna opens with the evening *Shema* rather than with the morning *Shema*. **If you wish,** you could **say** instead that the *tanna* **derives** the precedence of the evening *Shema* **from the** order of the **creation of the world. As it is written** in the story of creation: **"And there was evening, and there was morning, one day"** (Genesis 1:5). According to this verse, day begins with the evening and not the morning. For both of these reasons it was appropriate to open the discussion of the laws of the recitation of *Shema* with the evening *Shema*.

A related question: why does the Mishna discuss the evening Shema before the morning Shema? Since halakhically the day begins at night, one might think this is obvious. But the Gemara still raises it, and gives two answers.

The first: *tanna opened* אקרא קאי — the Tanna was anchored in the verse, דכתיב בשכבך ובקומך. The verse mentions lying down before rising up, and so the Mishna follows the verse's order: the Shema of shechivah, of lying down, is treated first. The implicit question is then: *zman* קריאת *shema* דשכיבה *eimat* — when does the Shema of lying down begin? And the answer: משעה שהכהנים *nichnasim* לאכול בתרומתן.

The second answer: ואיבעית אימא יליף מבריאתו של עולם — one may derive it instead from the account of creation, דכתיב ויהי ערב ויהי בוקר יום אחד. The Jewish day begins at night, so the Mishna naturally starts with the night.

### ABBA Literary Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״בַּשַּׁחַר מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וְאַחַת לְאַחֲרֶיהָ, בָּעֶרֶב מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וּשְׁתַּיִם לְאַחֲרֶיהָ״, לִתְנֵי דְּעַרְבִית בְּרֵישָׁא! תַּנָּא פָּתַח בְּעַרְבִית, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי בְּשַׁחֲרִית, עַד דְּקָאֵי בְּשַׁחֲרִית, פָּרֵישׁ מִילֵּה דְשַׁחֲרִית, וַהֲדַר פָּרֵישׁ מִילֵּה דְעַרְבִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does the **latter clause** of the mishna, which appears later in the chapter, **teach: In the morning one recites two** blessings **before** *Shema* **and one** blessing **afterward, and in the evening one recites two** blessings **before** *Shema* **and two afterward?** Based upon the above reasoning, the mishna **should have taught** the blessing recited before and after the **evening** *Shema* **first.** The Gemara answers: Indeed, the ***tanna* began** by discussing the laws regarding the recitation of **the evening** *Shema*, **and then taught** the laws regarding the recitation of **the morning** *Shema*. **Once** he **was already dealing with the morning** *Shema*, **he explained the matters of the morning** *Shema*, **and then explained the matters of the evening** *Shema*.

The Gemara then raises a further question: if the Mishna starts with the evening Shema, why does the later discussion of the berachot of Shema begin with the morning? בשחר מברך שתים לפניה ואחת לאחריה — in the morning you say two berachot before and one after; ובערב מברך שתים לפניה ושתים לאחריה — in the evening, two before and two after. This seems inconsistent with opening the tractate at night.

The Gemara gives a straightforward literary answer: *tanna opened* פתח בערבית והדר *tanna opened* בשחרית — the Tanna opened with the evening Shema and then moved to the morning Shema. *ad* דקאי בשחרית פריש מילי דשחרית — having arrived at the morning Shema, he continues to discuss the morning's berachot. והדר פריש מילי דערבית — and then he returns to discuss the evening berachot. This is an ABBA chiastic structure: night, morning, morning's berachot, night's berachot.

### Two Models of Shema


What is noteworthy is that by casually introducing both verses — *beshochbecha uvekumecha* and vayehi erev vayehi voker — the Gemara is actually gesturing toward two fundamentally different models for understanding the time of Shema. Under one model, the times of Shema are simply the halakhic periods of night and day. We define nightfall and daybreak in the standard halakhic sense, and Shema is said accordingly. Under the other model, the obligation is tied to the human experience of going to sleep and waking up — not to the abstract legal categories of night and day. Under this second model, for example, the position that permits Shema throughout the entire night might not be because one holds that Shema applies to the full halakhic night, but rather because the entire night is the time during which one is asleep. These two verses, placed here at the start of the Gemara's discussion, plant the seeds for two genuinely distinct conceptual frameworks that will continue to inform the analysis throughout the sugya.

## Kohanim & Tzeit Hakochav…

### Milta Agav Orcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִים לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. מִכְּדִי כֹּהֲנִים אֵימַת קָא אָכְלִי תְּרוּמָה? — מִשְּׁעַת צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים, לִתְנֵי: ״מִשְּׁעַת צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים״! מִלְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָמַשְׁמַע לַן — כֹּהֲנִים אֵימַת קָא אָכְלִי בִּתְרוּמָה — מִשְּׁעַת צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים. וְהָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכַפָּרָה לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify the rest of the mishna. **The Master said** in the mishna that the beginning of the period when one recites *Shema* in the evening is **when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*.** However, this does not specify a definitive time. **When do the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*? From the time of the emergence of the stars.** If that is the case, then **let** the *tanna* **teach** that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* is **from the time of the emergence of the stars.** The Gemara responds: Indeed it would have been simpler to say that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* begins with the emergence of the stars, but the particular expression used by the *tanna* **teaches us** another **matter in passing: When do priests partake of their *teruma*? From the time of the emergence of the stars. And** the *tanna* **teaches us** a new *halakha* parenthetically: failure to bring an **atonement** offering **does not prevent** a priest from eating *teruma*.

The Gemara returns to analyze the Mishna's formulation. Since we know that Kohanim eat terumah from *tzeit hakochavim*, why doesn't the Mishna simply say "from when the stars come out" instead of describing the Kohanim entering to eat their terumah? The Gemara answers: מילתא אגב אורחא קא משמע לן — the Mishna is teaching something in passing. By mentioning the Kohanim eating terumah, it incidentally informs us *kohanim eat* *eimat* קא אכלי בתרומה — when Kohanim eat terumah: משעת *tzeis* הכוכבים — from when the stars come out.

The immediate difficulty with this answer, however, is that it seems circular. To learn from the Mishna's formulation that Kohanim eat terumah at *tzeit hakochavim*, you already have to know that the Kohanim eat terumah at *tzeit hakochavim* — otherwise the passage tells you nothing new in passing. Other mishnayot use language like הערב שמשו without specifying the stars. So how exactly does citing the Kohanim convey this information?

### Tosefta: Tzeit Siman


The resolution comes when we turn the page: there is a Tosefta — a parallel work to the Mishna — which states explicitly: משעה שהכהנים *nichnasim* לאכול בתרומתן... משעת *tzeis* הכוכבים. The Tosefta appends the phrase *tzeit hakochavim* directly to the description of the Kohanim eating. So if one reads the Tosefta alongside the Mishna — as the rabbis presumably did — the Mishna's formulation does indeed inform you of two things at once: that *shema begins* at *tzeit hakochavim*, and that this is when the Kohanim are permitted to eat their terumah.

### Kohanim: Slice of Life


There is also a deeper point embedded in this formulation. Even if the Mishna could simply have said "from when the stars come out" — which is purely a halakhic marker — it chose instead to describe the Kohanim returning home to eat their terumah. This is not merely a technical signal for nightfall. It evokes the going-to-sleep model rather than the halakhic night-and-day model. The image is of people coming home at the end of the day, going through the mikvah, and now sitting down to eat. The time of Shema is framed within that human rhythm, not as an abstract legal category.

### Berakhot: Slice of Life


This quality — grounding the law within the textures of daily life — is strikingly pronounced throughout Masechet Berakhot in a way that sets it apart from most other tractates. In this masechet you find out about Kohanim coming home to eat their terumah. Turning the page, you will find out about when poor people eat their evening meal, about what happens on Erev Shabbat when normal people come in from the day, about someone who is up in a tree when the time to say Shema arrives, about someone who is traveling. The Mishna already showed us Rabban Gamliel's sons returning from a party late at night. This is a masechet saturated with the details of how people actually live.

And this connects directly to the verses of Shema themselves: בשבתך בביתך ובלכתך בדרך ובשכבך ובקומך — when you sit in your house, when you walk on the road, when you lie down, when you rise. The Shema is an obligation that inhabits all dimensions of daily life, and the way this masechet frames its discussion reflects that quality.

### Terumah Post-Mikdash


One might ask: were Kohanim actually eating terumah when the Mishna was written? After the destruction of the *beit haMikdash*, the status of terumah — whether it retained its d'Oraita status or became d'Rabbanan — is itself subject to debate. This is a legitimate question. But there is also a larger point at stake. One of the animating concerns of Masechet Berakhot, situated as it is in a post-Mikdash world, is precisely the question of how to transfer the kedushah of a Mikdash-centered religious life into the fabric of everyday existence. The introduction of Kohanim and terumah — and further on, the burning of sacrificial fats — is also an implicit gesture toward that other world of kedushah, and part of what the masechet is doing is drawing those worlds into conversation with one another.

### Nichnasim: Where From?


The word nichnasim — "enter" — invites the question of where exactly the Kohanim are entering from. The Gemara in Shabbat suggests that Kohanim went to the mikvah right before nightfall. It appears that the mikvaot were located somewhat on the outskirts, and the Kohanim were immersing there and then returning — entering back into their homes. This is another detail that places you concretely inside the life being described: Kohanim walking back from the mikvah at the edge of town as the day comes to an end, about to sit down to their terumah meal as the stars come out. You are not reading an abstraction; you are inside that moment.

### Avodah Shebalev Link


The introduction of the Kohanim and the later mention of sacrificial fats might also be understood as an implicit framing of Shema and Tefillah as *avodah shebalev* — the service of the heart. By opening with the world of Kohanim, terumah, and the altar, the Mishna places prayer within the same conceptual space as priestly avodah, suggesting that in a post-Mikdash world, the heart's devotion takes on the role that Temple service once played. This connects to the broader point above about transferring kedushah into everyday life.

## Kaparah Lo Me'akva

### Kaparah Lo Me'akva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וּבָא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְטָהֵר״ — בִּיַּת שִׁמְשׁוֹ מְעַכַּבְתּוֹ מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאֵין כַּפָּרָתוֹ מְעַכַּבְתּוֹ מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** In cases where an impure priest is required to immerse himself in a ritual bath and bring an atonement offering, even if he already immersed himself, he is not completely ritually pure until he brings the atonement offering. Nevertheless, he is still permitted to partake of *teruma*. Taught in passing in our mishna, this is articulated fully in a *baraita*, based on a close reading of the biblical passages. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the laws of ritual impurity, it is said: "One who touches it remains impure until evening. He should not eat of the consecrated items and he must wash his flesh with water. And the sun sets and it is purified. Afterwards, he may eat from the *teruma*, for it is his bread" (Leviticus 22:6–7). From the passage: **"And the sun sets and it is purified,"** that the absence of **the setting of his sun prevents him from partaking of *teruma*, but** failure to bring **the atonement** offering **does not prevent him from partaking of *teruma*,** may be inferred.

The Gemara now identifies a second halakhic teaching that the Mishna communicates in passing — beyond the timing of Shema and beyond the time when Kohanim eat terumah. That teaching is: *kaparah* *lo* מעכבא — a pending sacrifice does not prevent a person from eating terumah once nightfall has arrived.

### Uva Hashemesh Vetaher


This is derived from the verse: ובא השמש *v'taher mean* — "the sun sets and he becomes pure." The immediate context of this verse concerns a person who became tamei through a one-day tumah, went to the mikvah that day, and now waits for the residual tumah to be removed. ביאת שמשו מעכבתו מלאכול בתרומה — the setting of the sun is what holds him back from eating terumah. Once sunset and then nightfall arrive, he may eat. ואין כפרתו מעכבתו — but if he still has a pending sacrifice to bring — relevant for those with seven-day tumot such as a zav or zavah, who after their seven-day waiting period and immersion must still bring a sacrifice the next day — that pending sacrifice does not hold him back from eating terumah. He may eat terumah from nightfall even before bringing the sacrifice.

Now, the underlying question about this verse is sharper than the kaparah point. The verse says ובא השמש *v'taher mean*. Two distinct questions arise. First, does ובא השמש mean the setting of the sun — ביאת שמשו — or does it mean the sun rising, ba in the sense of coming rather than going? Second, what does *v'taher mean* mean — does it mean טהר יומא, that the day has been fully cleansed, implying you must wait until the stars come out; or does it mean טהר גברא, that the person himself becomes pure — possibly by bringing his sacrifice?

The second reading, associated by Rashi with the reading טהר גברא, might suggest that what the verse is describing is not the passive passage of nightfall but the active purification of the individual through the bringing of his sacrifice. Under this reading, ובא השמש might refer to the sunrise — the sun coming up on the following day — at which point the man brings his sacrifice and becomes fully pure. That would mean a kaparah does indeed prevent eating terumah until the sacrifice is brought.

### Taher Yoma Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״וּבָא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ בִּיַּת הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, וְהַאי ״וְטָהֵר״ — טְהַר יוֹמָא. אָמַר רַבָּהּ בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְיִטְהָר״, מַאי ״וְטָהֵר״ — טְהַר יוֹמָא, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״אִיעֲרַב שִׁמְשָׁא וְאִדַּכִּי יוֹמָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the proof offered in the *baraita*: **From where** do we know **that the** phrase: **"And the sun sets"** refers to **the** complete **setting of the sun,** and therefore, **"and it is purified"** refers to the fact that **the day is pure,** i.e., and the sun sets and it is purified is one phrase meaning that the sun will set, the air will clear, and the stars will emerge (Rav Hai Gaon)? **Rabba bar Rav Sheila said: If so,** that: And it is purified, means that the priest goes and purifies himself, then **let the verse say** unambiguously: **And he will become purified.** Since the Torah does not employ that language, the conclusion is: **What is** the meaning of the expression: **And it is purified?** It means **the day is pure,** no residue of day remains, **as people say** colloquially: **The sun has set and the day is purified.**

Rabbah bar Rav Shila resolves this by attending to the grammar of the verse. If the verse meant to say that the man actively brings his sacrifice and thereby becomes pure, it would have used the active reflexive: *ve'yitehar* — and he shall purify himself. But the verse says *v'taher mean*, a form that reads as something happening on its own. This means טהר יומא — the day itself becomes cleansed. The day is fully over; all light has been removed. And this gives us not merely sunset but the full onset of night — which in halakhic terms means *tzeit hakochavim*, the emergence of the stars.

### I'arev Shimsha


Rabbah bar Rav Shila supports this with a folk expression: איערב שמשא ואידכי יומא — the sun has set and the day has been cleansed. The day is completely over. The folk idiom captures precisely the same idea: it is not enough that the sun has dipped below the horizon; the day must be fully purged of light, and that is when night truly begins.

### West Resolves from Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַעֲרָבָא, הָא דְּרַבָּהּ בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ, וּבְעוֹ לַהּ מִיבַּעְיָא: הַאי ״וּבָא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ בִּיַּת שִׁמְשׁוֹ הוּא, וּמַאי ״וְטָהֵר״ — טְהַר יוֹמָא, אוּ דִילְמָא בִּיַּת אוֹרוֹ הוּא, וּמַאי ״וְטָהֵר״ — טְהַר גַּבְרָא. וַהֲדַר פָּשְׁטוּ לַהּ מִבָּרַיְיתָא. מִדְּקָתָנֵי בְּבָרַיְיתָא, סִימָן לַדָּבָר — צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ — בִּיַּת שִׁמְשׁוֹ הוּא, וּמַאי ״וְטָהֵר״ — טְהַר יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they did not hear this** explanation given **by Rabba bar Rav Sheila. They raised the dilemma: Does the expression: And the sun sets, refer to the** actual **setting of the sun, and does: And it is purified, mean the day clears away? Or perhaps it refers to the setting of its light** at sunset, in which case **what is** the meaning of: **And it is purified?** It refers to **the purification of the person.** In other words, in Eretz Yisrael, they attempted to clarify the *halakha* based on the biblical passage, but were unable to do so. **Ultimately they resolved** this dilemma **from a *baraita*. It was taught in a baraita** that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* corresponds to the time when priests are permitted to eat of their *teruma*, **a sign for which is the emergence of the stars.** Therefore, **derive from here** that "and the sun sets" refers to **the complete sunset, and the expression "and it is purified"** means **the day clears away,** as the Sages in Babylonia concluded.

The Gemara adds that in the West — in Eretz Yisrael — this teaching of Rabbah bar Rav Shila was not known. They raised the same question independently: does ובא השמש mean ביאת שמשו — sunset as the threshold — making *v'taher mean* mean טהר יומא and pointing to *tzeit hakochavim*? Or does it mean ביאת אורו — the going away of the sun's light, i.e., ordinary sunset — making *v'taher mean* mean טהר גברא, referring to the sacrifice?

They did not resolve it through the hermeneutical argument about *ve'yitehar* versus *v'taher mean*. Instead, they resolved it from an external rabbinic source: והדר פשטוה מברייתא — they derived it from a Braita. The Braita states: סימן לדבר *tzeis* הכוכבים — the sign for the matter is the emergence of the stars. From this transmitted rabbinic ruling, they concluded: *shema* מינה ביאת שמשו הוא — that what the verse intends is sunset in the sense of the day fully setting, and that *v'taher mean* means טהר יומא — the day is completely cleansed at *tzeit hakochavim*.

### Two Modes of Tradition


This passage is worth pausing over because it places side by side two fundamentally different modes of rabbinic tradition. The question is: how do we know what a verse means? One approach is hermeneutic engagement — attending closely to the word choice of the Torah, noticing that *ve'yitehar* would have been the appropriate form if human action were required, and therefore deriving that *v'taher mean* must mean something automatic, something the day does on its own. This is the mode of Rabbah bar Rav Shila. The other approach is essentially traditionary — the rabbis have passed down that *tzeit hakochavim* is the relevant threshold, and the Braita simply states it as a received ruling: סימן לדבר *tzeis* הכוכבים.

Both are legitimate forms of halakhic knowledge, and they arrive at the same conclusion. But one is grounded in creative textual interpretation, the other in the authority of transmitted tradition. There is a further point worth noting. The Braita, in the style typical of the Mishna, is apodictic — it simply states the law. The Gemara, which is all about conversation and active engagement, is unsatisfied with that. When the Gemara asks "how do we know the verse means this?" it is pressing beyond the tradition to the underlying reasoning, preferring to locate the meaning within the text itself. In a sense, as one might observe, what the Gemara has done is take a received ruling and demand that it be recontextualized as a hermeneutic derivation — transforming one modality of tradition into the other.

## Three Shema Start Ti (II)

### Poor Man: Bread & Salt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִים לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. וּרְמִינְהוּ: מֵאֵימָתַי קוֹרִין אֶת שְׁמַע בָּעֲרָבִין — מִשֶּׁהֶעָנִי נִכְנָס לֶאֱכוֹל פִּתּוֹ בְּמֶלַח, עַד שָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹמֵד לִיפָּטֵר מִתּוֹךְ סְעוּדָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In our mishna, **the Master said:** The beginning of the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* is: **From the time when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*.** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this opinion from a *baraita* that states that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* is: **From when a poor person enters to eat his bread with salt until he rises from his meal.**

The Gemara now challenges the Mishna's description of when evening *shema begins*. The Mishna says it begins when the Kohanim enter to eat their terumah. But a Braita teaches: *me'eimatai* קוראים *shema* בערבית? — When do you read Shema at night? — משעני נכנס לאכול פיתו במלח — from when the poor person comes in to eat his bread with salt.

This is a very different model — and also very much a slice of life. Notice the detail: not just when someone begins eating, but what they are eating. A poor man eating bread with salt. You are suddenly placed inside that house. And the window for saying Shema is strikingly short: *ad* שעה שעומד לפטור מסעודתו — until the time when his meal is normally over. The obligation is tied to the period right before going to sleep, with no long extension into the night. Tosafot, noting that the man is eating during this window, suggests that practically speaking he says Shema just before sitting down to eat — having come into the house but not yet started the meal.

One might also observe that the poor man eats later than one might initially assume — he is out working longer. This will become significant shortly.

### Erev Shabbat Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מֵאֵימָתַי מַתְחִילִין לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע בְּעַרְבִית — מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם נִכְנָסִין לֶאֱכוֹל פִּתָּן בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים זַכָּאִין לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. סִימָן לַדָּבָר: צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲנַחְנוּ עֹשִׂים בַּמְּלָאכָה וְחֶצְיָם מַחֲזִיקִים בָּרְמָחִים מֵעֲלוֹת הַשַּׁחַר עַד צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיוּ לָנוּ הַלַּיְלָה מִשְׁמָר וְהַיּוֹם מְלָאכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the *Tosefta*: **From when does one begin to recite** *Shema* **in the evening? From the time when people enter to eat their bread on Shabbat eve.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** As they do in our mishna, **the Rabbis say: From the time when the priests are eligible to partake of their *teruma*, a sign for which is the emergence of the stars. And although there is no** explicit **proof** that the emergence of the stars is when one may begin to recite the evening *Shema*, **there is an allusion** in the book of Nehemiah to the fact that the emergence of the stars is generally considered the beginning of the night. As **it is stated** with regard to the building of the walls of Jerusalem: **"And we perform the work, and half of them grasp their spears from dawn until the emergence of the stars"** (Nehemiah 4:15). **And it says: "That in the night they may be a guard to us, and may labor in the day"** (Nehemiah 4:16). From here we ascertain that the day ends with the emergence of the stars.

A Tosefta gives yet another formulation. *me'eimatai* מתחילים לקרות קריאת *shema* בערבית? — When do you begin Shema at night? משעה שבני אדם *nichnasim* לאכול פיתן בערבי שבתות — from when people come in to eat their bread on erev Shabbat. This is not the poor man on a weekday, but ordinary, middle-class people on erev Shabbat, who finish their work somewhat earlier and come in before dark. דברי רבי מאיר — that is Rabbi Meir's position. וחכמים אומרים — the Chachamim say: משעה שהכהנים זכאים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim are permitted to eat their terumah. Note the shift in register: the Mishna described the Kohanim actually eating; the Chachamim here speak in legal terms — when they are entitled to eat. סימן לדבר *tzeis* הכוכבים — the indicator for this is the emergence of the stars.

## Kaparah Lo Me'akva (II)

### Nechemiah: Zecher Ladavar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲנַחְנוּ עֹשִׂים בַּמְּלָאכָה וְחֶצְיָם מַחֲזִיקִים בָּרְמָחִים מֵעֲלוֹת הַשַּׁחַר עַד צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיוּ לָנוּ הַלַּיְלָה מִשְׁמָר וְהַיּוֹם מְלָאכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* continues: although there is no explicit **proof** that the emergence of the stars is when one may begin to recite the evening *Shema*, **there is an allusion** in the book of Nehemiah to the fact that the emergence of the stars is generally considered the beginning of the night. The verse cited is from Nechemiah, describing the workers rebuilding the Beit HaMikdash: **"And we were doing the work, and half of them were holding spears, from the morning star until the stars came out."** And another verse: **"And the night would be our guard and the day work."**

The Tosefta continues: ואף על פי שאין ראיה לדבר *zecher* לדבר — although there is no proof from Torah for this, there is an allusion. The distinction matters: halakhic law can only be definitively derived from Torah; when a source from Nevi'im or Ketuvim is cited, it is zecher ladavar — a hint or echo, not a binding proof.

The verse cited is from Nechemiah, describing the workers rebuilding the *beit haMikdash*: ואנחנו עושים במלאכה וחציים מחזיקים ברמחים מעלות השחר *ad* *tzeis* הכוכבים — we were doing the work, and half of them were holding spears, from the morning star until the stars came out. And another verse: והיו לנו הלילה משמר והיום מלאכה — at night we would stand guard, and by day we would work.

Taken together, these verses define the boundaries of the workday: from *alot hashachar* until *tzeit hakochavim*. If that is what constitutes day, then *tzeit hakochavim* is when night begins — which is when terumah becomes permissible and when Shema is first recited.

### Why Second Verse Needed


One might ask why a second verse is needed. The first verse already tells you that work ran from *alot hashachar* until *tzeit hakochavim*. Why add the verse that says "at night we would guard, by day we would work"?

The answer is that without the second verse, one might argue that the workers in Nechemiah were simply dedicated people who pushed beyond the natural day — starting before dawn and finishing after nightfall, going extra early and extra late. The second verse forecloses this interpretation. By stating that the day is for work and the night is for guarding, it establishes that the first verse is tracking the natural boundaries of day and night, not the unusual dedication of a particular workforce. The work ran from *alot hashachar* until *tzeit hakochavim* because those are the natural markers of day — not because the workers were exceptionally zealous.

## Three Shema Start Ti (II)

### Ani vs. Kohen: Same Time?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא, וַדַּאי פְּלִיגָא אַמַּתְנִיתִין. רֵישָׁא, מִי לֵימָא פְּלִיגִי אַמַּתְנִיתִין? לָא, עָנִי וְכֹהֵן חַד שִׁיעוּרָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara begins its analysis by clarifying whether there is an actual contradiction here, or whether different expressions are being employed to describe the same time. **The latter clause** of the *baraita*, which established that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* ends when a poor person rises from his meal, **certainly disagrees with our mishna.** Since the poor person clearly does not continue eating until the end of the first watch, this *baraita* certainly contradicts our mishna. With regard to **the first clause** of the *baraita*, however, which establishes the beginning of the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema*, **shall we say that it disagrees with our mishna?** The Gemara immediately rejects this idea: **No,** the time when **the poor person** eats **and** the time when **the priest** is purified and permitted to partake of his *teruma* **are one** and the same **time.**

The Gemara now examines whether the poor man's time and the Kohen's time are the same. A moment ago it was suggested — and left open — that they could be. The Gemara now presses harder. The end of the Braita with the poor man's time clearly disagrees with the end of the Mishna — the poor man has only a short window, while the Mishna gives you until the end of the first watch, or midnight, or all night. But what about the beginning — does the poor man start at the same time as the Kohanim? The Gemara initially says *lo* — they could be the same. The *ani would* and the *kohen* are one time: *ani would* וכהן חד שיעורא הוא.

### Full Braita: All Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מֵאֵימָתַי מַתְחִילִין לִקְרוֹת שְׁמַע בָּעֲרָבִין — מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁקָּדַשׁ הַיּוֹם בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים מְטוֹהָרִים לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים טוֹבְלִין לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתָן. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא כֹּהֲנִים מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם הֵם טוֹבְלִים? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אוֹמֵר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁעָנִי נִכְנָס לֶאֱכוֹל פִּתּוֹ בְּמֶלַח. רַבִּי אַחַאי וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁרוֹב בְּנֵי אָדָם נִכְנָסִין לְהָסֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The preceding was the text of the *baraita*. Returning to our question, the Gemara presents a fuller Baraita listing all the opinions on when evening Shema begins: **From when does one begin to recite** the **evening** *Shema*? **From the time when the day becomes sanctified on the eve of Shabbat, this is the statement of Rabbi Eliezer,** who established an earlier time for *Shema*. **Rabbi Yehoshua,** like our mishna, **says: From the time when the priests are eligible to partake of their *teruma*.** **Rabbi Meir says:** The time for the recitation of *Shema* begins before the priests were purified, **from when the priests immerse themselves in order to partake of their *teruma*.** **Rabbi Yehuda said to** Rabbi Meir: How is it possible that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* corresponds to the time of the priests' immersion? **Do the priests not immerse themselves during the day,** so that with nightfall and the onset of a new day they will be purified? If so, how can that time be called night? **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* begins **when the poor person enters to eat his bread with salt.** But **Rabbi Aḥai, and some say Rabbi Aḥa, says: From the time when most people enter to recline** at their meal during the week.

The Gemara then presents a fuller Braita listing all the opinions on when evening *shema begins*:

Rabbi Eliezer says: משישקע החמה — from when the sun sets, i.e., from kidush hayom on erev Shabbat, which is effectively sunset.

Rabbi Yehoshua says: משעה שהכהנים מטוהרים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim are fully purified to eat their terumah.

Rabbi Meir says: משעה שהכהנים טובלים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim immerse to eat their terumah. This is before sunset — they immerse in the mikvah right before nightfall.

Rabbi Chanina says: משעה שעני נכנס לאכול פיתו במלח — from when the poor man comes in to eat his bread with salt.

Rabbi Achai (and some say Rabbi Acha) says: משעה שרוב בני אדם *nichnasim* להסב — from when the majority of people come in to recline. This is the Roman-style reclining at a proper meal — not sitting down, but reclining on a dining couch, as at the Pesach seder. This would naturally happen somewhat later in the evening, after preparation, and would represent the middle class's dinner hour.

### Three Social Tiers


Here one may observe a striking social panorama: three tiers of society serve as the reference points for when *shema begins*. There are the Kohanim — the priestly class, defined by their ritual life. There are the aniyim — the poor, eating bread with salt at the end of their long working day. And there are rov bnei adam — ordinary middle-class people, reclining for a proper evening meal. The question of which group sets the standard — whose daily rhythm defines the time of Shema — is itself a question worth reflecting upon. The Gemara describes all three without adjudicating which is the "natural" model.

### Chanina vs. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ עָנִי וְכֹהֵן חַד שִׁיעוּרָא הוּא, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שִׁעוּרָא דְּעָנִי לְחוּד וְשִׁיעוּרָא דְּכֹהֵן לְחוּד, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ מְאוּחָר?
>
> **Translation:** The preceding was the text of the *baraita*. Returning to our question, **if you say** that **the time of the poor person and the priest are one** and the same **time,** then the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina is** identical to that of **Rabbi Yehoshua.** However, the fact that they are cited together indicates that they are not, in fact, the same. **Rather, must** one **not conclude from this the time for** the **poor person is separate and the time for** the **priest is separate?** Since no objection is raised, the Gemara concedes: Indeed, **conclude from this.** Having established that the time of the poor person and that of the priest are different, the Gemara seeks to determine: **which one is later?**

The Gemara now forces the conclusion that the poor man's time and the Kohen's time must in fact be different. Rabbi Chanina says *shema begins* when the ani eats; Rabbi Yehoshua says it begins when the Kohanim are purified to eat terumah. These appear to be two distinct opinions in a debate. If they were describing the same moment, there would be nothing to argue about — they would simply be two descriptions of one time. Therefore: *ani would* שיעורא לחוד וכהן שיעורא לחוד — the time of the ani is distinct from the time of the kohen. *shema* מינה.

### Yehuda Challenges Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא כֹּהֲנִים מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם הֵם טוֹבְלִים!
>
> **Translation:** We learned that **the Master said** in a *baraita* that the time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* according to Rabbi Meir begins with the time of the immersion of the priests. Regarding this, **Rabbi Yehuda said** to Rabbi Meir: **Do the priests not immerse themselves during the day?**

Having established the full spectrum of opinions, the Gemara returns to Rabbi Meir's position: *shema begins* when the Kohanim go to the mikvah. Rabbi Yehuda challenges this: והלא *kohanim eat* מבעוד יום טובלים — the Kohanim immerse while it is still day. How can the night Shema begin at a moment that is halakhically daytime? If your framework is night and day, you cannot start the nighttime obligation before night has begun.

### Meir: Bein Hashmashot Yo…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ דַּאֲנָא אַבֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת דִּידָךְ קָא אָמֵינָא? אֲנָא אַבֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָא אָמֵינָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת כְּהֶרֶף עַיִן, זֶה — נִכְנָס, וְזֶה — יוֹצֵא, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לַעֲמוֹד עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Meir said** to Rabbi Yehuda **as follows**: **Do you maintain that I am speaking of your** definition of **twilight? I am speaking of Rabbi Yosei's** definition of **twilight, as Rabbi Yosei said: Twilight is like the blink of an eye;** night **begins and** day **ends and** the time between them is so brief, **it is impossible to quantify.** According to this opinion, the priests immerse themselves just before the emergence of the stars, when it is already considered night.

Rabbi Meir's response: מי סברת דאנא אבין השמשות דידך קאמינא? — Do you think I am referring to your *bein hashmashot*? Rabbi Yehuda's *bein hashmashot*, as developed in tractate Shabbat, runs from sunset until the stars come out — roughly thirteen to fifteen minutes. Prior to that period is unambiguously day. Rabbi Meir says: I am not placing Shema there.

אנא אבין השמשות דרבי יוסי קאמינא — I am speaking of Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashot*. Rabbi Yosi holds that *bein hashmashot* is not a span of time but a split second — an instant that occurs immediately before the stars come out. דאמר רבי יוסי בין השמשות כהרף עין — like the blink of an eye. זה נכנס וזה יוצא ואי אפשר לעמוד עליו — one goes in and one comes out, and it is impossible to fix that precise moment. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yosi, almost the entire period between sunset and tzeit is halakhically still day. Rabbi Meir positions the immersion of the Kohanim in this window — after sunset but still, per Rabbi Yosi, halakhically day.

What this tells us is significant: for Rabbi Meir, it does not bother us that the night *shema begins* during what is halakhically still day. What matters is that the sun has set — the day has functionally ended for human experience — and people are beginning to wrap up and come home. The going-to-sleep framework, rather than the abstract night-and-day framework, is determinative.

### Ani Later Than Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּעָנִי מְאוּחָר דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּעָנִי מוּקְדָּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא הַיְינוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּעָנִי מְאוּחָר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Having established that the time of the poor person and that of the priest are different, the Gemara seeks to determine: **which one is later?** The Gemara suggests that **it is reasonable** to conclude that **the** time of the **poor person is later. As if you say** that **the poor person is earlier,** it would be impossible to establish a time earlier than that established by Rabbi Yehoshua, unless we hold that night begins with sunset, in which case the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina is** identical to that of **Rabbi Eliezer. Rather, must** one **not conclude from this that the poor person is later?** The Gemara notes: Indeed, **conclude from this.**

Having established that the ani's time and the Kohen's time are different, the Gemara asks: which is later? הי מינייהו מאוחר? מסתברא דעני מאוחר — it stands to reason that the ani eats even later than *tzeit hakochavim*. The poor man works longer; he comes in after the stars have already come out. This makes intuitive sense.

The logical argument is as follows: if we were to say the ani is earlier than tzeit, then since the next earlier time is sunset (Rabbi Eliezer's position), and we have no reason to assume a distinct slice of time between sunset and tzeit, we would effectively collapse the ani's time into Rabbi Eliezer's. Since Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Eliezer appear to present distinct positions, they cannot refer to the same moment. Therefore the ani must be placed on the other side of tzeit — later, not earlier. אלא לאו *shema* מינה דעני מאוחר. *shema* מינה.

### Trei Tannai Aliba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר! תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אַדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר! תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רֵישָׁא לָאו דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara closes by noting apparent contradictions within the positions of individual Tannaim across the various sources cited. **One opinion of** **Rabbi Meir** seems to **contradict** another opinion of **Rabbi Meir**. The Gemara responds: **Two *tanna'im*,** students of Rabbi Meir, expressed different opinions **in accordance with Rabbi Meir's** opinion. So too, the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer** cited in the mishna **contradicts** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer** cited in the *baraita*. In the mishna, Rabbi Eliezer holds that the time for the recitation of *Shema* begins with the emergence of the stars: From the time when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*, while in the *baraita*, he states that the time for the recitation of *Shema* begins when the day becomes sanctified on the eve of Shabbat. The Gemara responds: There are two possible resolutions to the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Eliezer's opinion. Either **two** *tanna'im* expressed different opinions **in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion, **or if you wish, say** instead that **the first clause** of the mishna, according to which we begin to recite *Shema* when the priests enter to partake of their *teruma*, **is not** actually **Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion. Only the second half of the statement: Until the end of the first watch, was stated by Rabbi Eliezer.

The Gemara closes by noting apparent contradictions within the positions of individual Tannaim across the various sources cited. קשיא דרבי מאיר דרבי מאיר — Rabbi Meir's position in one Braita does not match his position in another. The resolution: תרי תנאי אליבא דרבי מאיר — there are two traditions about what Rabbi Meir actually held, reflected in different formulations. Similarly: קשיא דרבי אליעזר דרבי אליעזר — Rabbi Eliezer in the Braita places a different time than Rabbi Eliezer in the Mishna. Again: תרי תנאי אליבא דרבי אליעזר — two traditions about his position.

An alternative resolution is offered for the Rabbi Eliezer problem: ואי בעית אימא רישא לאו דרבי אליעזר היא — one may argue that the beginning of the Mishna — when the Kohanim eat their terumah — was not part of Rabbi Eliezer's statement at all. Rabbi Eliezer's contribution to the Mishna was only the end-time: *ad* סוף האשמורה הראשונה. The start-time, as formulated in the Mishna, need not be attributed to him.
