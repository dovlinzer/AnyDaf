### Mishna: Shema Times

ברכות דף 2 עמוד א.

מאימתי קורין את שמע בערבין? — When do you begin saying Shema in the evening?

משעה שהכהנים נכנסים לאכול בתרומתן — From the time that the Kohanim enter in to eat their terumah. The Kohanim, if they had become tamei during the day, would go to the mikvah near the end of the day. They would be able to eat terumah at nightfall, once the residual tumah was removed. So when they come in to eat their terumah, that is when you can begin saying the evening Shema.

עד סוף האשמורה הראשונה — Until the end of the first watch. The night was divided into watches — not a wristwatch, but the watches of a city's guards — and until the end of the first watch, roughly three to four hours into the night, you could say the Shema. דברי רבי אליעזר — that is the position of Rabbi Eliezer.

וחכמים אומרים עד חצות — The Chachamim say until midnight. Assuming a day with sunrise and sunset both at six, Rabbi Eliezer's position gives you until approximately 9 or 10 PM; the Chachamim say until midnight.

רבן גמליאל אומר עד שיעלה עמוד השחר — Rabban Gamliel says until the morning star rises, normally assumed to be about 72 minutes before sunrise, meaning you can essentially say Shema throughout the entire night until the day begins to break.

### Shema End-Time Debate

This debate is not about when Shema begins — that will be discussed in the Gemara — but about how late you may still recite it. Rashi (not on this daf but on the next) states simply that the dispute turns on how one reads the verse in this week's parasha, Va'etchanan: ובשכבך ובקומך — when you go to sleep and when you rise up. The question is: does this mean the time when people are going to bed, or does it mean during the entire period of sleep?

That people begin going to bed at nightfall seems agreed upon by all, especially in an era without electric light, when people worked from daybreak and retired at dark. The dispute concerns how late into the night the obligation extends. If it means only the time when people are heading to bed, then it lasts perhaps a few hours into the night. If it encompasses the entire period of sleep, then it extends all the way through until daybreak. The middle position — that of the Chachamim, who say chatzot — requires separate analysis.

### Rabban Gamliel's Sons

מעשה ובאו בניו מבית המשתה — There was a story that the sons of Rabban Gamliel came back from a party — a mishteh, which may indicate a wedding celebration, but in any case a place where they had been out all night, likely drinking, and not in the most focused state of mind for reciting Shema. They came home and said: לא קרינו את שמע — we have not yet said Shema. אמר להם: אם לא עלה עמוד השחר חייבים אתם לקרות — He told them: if the morning star has not yet risen, you are still obligated to recite it. Rabban Gamliel here rules in accordance with his own position.

### Midnight vs. Dawn Rule

The Mishna then continues: ולא זו בלבד אמרו — it is not only regarding this that they said so. It may be unclear whether this is Rabban Gamliel still speaking or the Mishna itself inserting a comment. אלא כל מה שאמרו חכמים עד חצות מצותן עד שיעלה עמוד השחר — Anything the sages said must be done by midnight, the real halakhic obligation extends through the entire night. Midnight is not a halakhically significant time in the ordinary sense — the Torah does not treat it as a meaningful boundary the way it treats, say, midday in certain contexts.

Two examples are given. First: הקטר חלבים ואיברים מצותן עד שיעלה עמוד השחר — burning the fats and limbs of sacrifices. The sacrifice had to be offered by day, and the blood sprinkled by day, but the fats and limbs could be burned on the altar throughout the entire night. If they were left off the altar the entire night, however, they could not be burned the following day.

Second: וכל הנאכלים ליום אחד מצותן עד שיעלה עמוד השחר — all korbanot eaten for one day. This refers primarily to kodshei kodashim such as the chatat and the asham, eaten by the Kohanim for one day, and the todah, a korban of thanksgiving, which the Yisrael eats only for a day and the following night. The mitzvah of eating all of these extends until daybreak.

אם כן למה אמרו חכמים עד חצות? — If so, why did the sages say until midnight? כדי להרחיק אדם מן העבירה — In order to distance a person from transgression. If you know you have the entire night, you keep pushing it off, and before you know it you have fallen asleep or become too impaired to say it. So they told you to say it by midnight — but the underlying obligation extends throughout the night.

### Fence: Ad Chatzot

This is, in essence, the rabbinic enactment of midnight as a fence against transgression — leharchik adam min ha'aveira. The Chachamim, in setting midnight as the deadline, were not identifying a genuine halakhic time limit, but were creating a protective boundary to ensure people actually fulfilled the obligation.

### Rashi: Fats & Chatzot

Rashi points out that the burning of the fats was never subject to the midnight limit at all. That case was only included here because it was organically part of the broader statement about eating sacrifices. The midnight-as-fence idea never applied to it.

There is also a small question whether this final clause — that whatever the sages said until midnight really means until dawn — is part of Rabban Gamliel's own statement or a comment of the Mishna. If it belongs to Rabban Gamliel, then what he is saying is: in principle I agree you should aim for midnight, but if you missed it, you still have the whole night. Under this reading, Rabban Gamliel is partially accommodating the Chachamim's position. Alternatively — and this is normally how it is understood — it is the Mishna inserting a note: even the rabbis who say midnight agree in principle with Rabban Gamliel that the actual obligation extends all night. The distinction matters for how we read the tension between Rabban Gamliel and the Chachamim.

## Why Berakhot Begins Shas

### Order of Tractates

Why do we begin Shas with Berakhot? We begin with Seder Zeraim because Zeraim is the first order of the Mishna, and Berakhot is the first tractate of Zeraim. Now, tractates within a seder are ordered by size — the tractate with the most chapters comes first — and Berakhot has the most chapters in Zeraim. That explains the mechanics. But it is worth asking what the connection is between Berakhot and the agricultural framework of Zeraim in the first place, since while part of Berakhot concerns blessings over food, much of it deals with Shema, Tefillah, and blessings over a wide range of things.

### Zeraim = Daily Life

One way to think about this is to read Seder Zeraim not narrowly as agriculture, but more broadly as the order of daily life. In an agricultural society, daily life is very much organized around planting, harvesting, and the ritual obligations attached to the land. But another central component of daily life — equally defining and equally constant — is prayer, blessings, and the recitation of Shema. Under this broader reading, Berakhot fits naturally as the opening tractate of Zeraim, because both are about the structure of everyday religious existence.

### Six Sedarim Structure

If you take this view seriously, you can trace a coherent progression across all six sedarim of Shas. You begin with Zeraim — daily life. You move to Moed — the special days, Shabbat, Yom Tov, and the calendar. Then Nashim — from the individual to the family. The word nashim means women, and also wives; this order is concerned, from the perspective of the male subject of most halakhic discourse, with family law and the relationships that define domestic life. Then Nezikin — from the family outward into society: civil law, damages, courts, interpersonal obligations, the individual functioning within a broader community. And only at the very end do you arrive at Kodshim and Taharot — the laws of the Mikdash and purity.

This structure can be read in two very different directions. On one reading, everything builds toward Kodshim and Taharot, and the Temple stands as the culminating ideal toward which all of Jewish life is oriented. On another reading — arguably more resonant for life after the destruction — what actually defines the texture of a sanctified religious life is determined long before you reach Kodshim and Taharot. It is determined by Shema and Shemoneh Esrei and Berakhot, by the laws of the land and of special days, by how we behave in our families and in our communities. The bulk of what our religious life is made of is already fully present in the earlier orders.

### Implicit in Mishna

Several things are implicit in the very first Mishna that deserve to be made explicit. The first is that the Mishna assumes you are already in the conversation. It opens with "from when do we read Shema at night?" without first establishing that there is an obligation to read Shema at all. The obligation, the verse, the relevant context — all of this is taken for granted. The Mishna similarly assumes familiarity with the Kohanim's terumah — that there exists a time when they must wait before eating it, and that they undergo immersion near the end of the day. You are simply expected to know all of this.

### Rabbinic Fence-Making

The second implicit point is that the very first Mishna already introduces rabbinic legislation alongside biblical obligation. On one level, the rabbis are interpreting Torah: they are telling us when beshochbecha uvekumecha begins and ends. But at the same time, they are legislating — building fences, protecting people from unintentional transgression. The midnight enactment is not an interpretation of any verse; it is a protective measure. The principle underlying it is ועשו משמרת למשמרתי — make a fence for my teaching. This dual role of the rabbis, as both interpreters and legislators, is already fully on display in the first Mishna of Shas.

### Q: Shemoneh Esrei

One might ask: if this tractate deals with prayer, why doesn't the Mishna begin with Shemoneh Esrei? In fact, Masechet Berakhot is considerably more logically organized than most other masechtot. The tractate moves through its topics in clear progression: one chapter for Shema, then Shemoneh Esrei, then Birkat Hamazon, and so on. Beginning by asking "when does the obligation begin?" is the natural starting point for the topic at hand. Shemoneh Esrei will be addressed in due course.

## Gemara Opening Questions

### Tanna Heicha Kai

תנא היכא קאי דקתני מאימתי? — Where was the Tanna standing that he asked "from when"? The Gemara notices that the Mishna opens with a timing question without first establishing what obligation is being timed. It is as if the Mishna enters a conversation already in progress — first tell me there is an obligation, then tell me when it begins. This question gestures toward the same point noted above: the Mishna assumes a great deal of shared context.

### Evening Before Morning

A related question: why does the Mishna discuss the evening Shema before the morning Shema? Since halakhically the day begins at night, one might think this is obvious. But the Gemara still raises it, and gives two answers.

The first: תנא אקרא קאי — the Tanna was anchored in the verse, דכתיב בשכבך ובקומך. The verse mentions lying down before rising up, and so the Mishna follows the verse's order: the Shema of shechivah, of lying down, is treated first. The implicit question is then: זמן קריאת שמע דשכיבה אימת — when does the Shema of lying down begin? And the answer: משעה שהכהנים נכנסים לאכול בתרומתן.

The second answer: ואיבעית אימא יליף מבריאתו של עולם — one may derive it instead from the account of creation, דכתיב ויהי ערב ויהי בוקר יום אחד. The Jewish day begins at night, so the Mishna naturally starts with the night.

### ABBA Literary Structure

The Gemara then raises a further question: if the Mishna starts with the evening Shema, why does the later discussion of the berachot of Shema begin with the morning? בשחר מברך שתים לפניה ואחת לאחריה — in the morning you say two berachot before and one after; ובערב מברך שתים לפניה ושתים לאחריה — in the evening, two before and two after. This seems inconsistent with opening the tractate at night.

The Gemara gives a straightforward literary answer: תנא פתח בערבית והדר תנא בשחרית — the Tanna opened with the evening Shema and then moved to the morning Shema. עד דקאי בשחרית פריש מילי דשחרית — having arrived at the morning Shema, he continues to discuss the morning's berachot. והדר פריש מילי דערבית — and then he returns to discuss the evening berachot. This is an ABBA chiastic structure: night, morning, morning's berachot, night's berachot.

### Two Models of Shema

What is noteworthy is that by casually introducing both verses — beshochbecha uvekumecha and vayehi erev vayehi voker — the Gemara is actually gesturing toward two fundamentally different models for understanding the time of Shema. Under one model, the times of Shema are simply the halakhic periods of night and day. We define nightfall and daybreak in the standard halakhic sense, and Shema is said accordingly. Under the other model, the obligation is tied to the human experience of going to sleep and waking up — not to the abstract legal categories of night and day. Under this second model, for example, the position that permits Shema throughout the entire night might not be because one holds that Shema applies to the full halakhic night, but rather because the entire night is the time during which one is asleep. These two verses, placed here at the start of the Gemara's discussion, plant the seeds for two genuinely distinct conceptual frameworks that will continue to inform the analysis throughout the sugya.

## Kohanim & Tzeit Hakochav…

### Milta Agav Orcha

The Gemara returns to analyze the Mishna's formulation. Since we know that Kohanim eat terumah from tzeit hakochavim, why doesn't the Mishna simply say "from when the stars come out" instead of describing the Kohanim entering to eat their terumah? The Gemara answers: מילתא אגב אורחא קא משמע לן — the Mishna is teaching something in passing. By mentioning the Kohanim eating terumah, it incidentally informs us כהנים אימת קא אכלי בתרומה — when Kohanim eat terumah: משעת צאת הכוכבים — from when the stars come out.

The immediate difficulty with this answer, however, is that it seems circular. To learn from the Mishna's formulation that Kohanim eat terumah at tzeit hakochavim, you already have to know that the Kohanim eat terumah at tzeit hakochavim — otherwise the passage tells you nothing new in passing. Other mishnayot use language like הערב שמשו without specifying the stars. So how exactly does citing the Kohanim convey this information?

### Tosefta: Tzeit Siman

The resolution comes when we turn the page: there is a Tosefta — a parallel work to the Mishna — which states explicitly: משעה שהכהנים נכנסים לאכול בתרומתן... משעת צאת הכוכבים. The Tosefta appends the phrase tzeit hakochavim directly to the description of the Kohanim eating. So if one reads the Tosefta alongside the Mishna — as the rabbis presumably did — the Mishna's formulation does indeed inform you of two things at once: that Shema begins at tzeit hakochavim, and that this is when the Kohanim are permitted to eat their terumah.

### Kohanim: Slice of Life

There is also a deeper point embedded in this formulation. Even if the Mishna could simply have said "from when the stars come out" — which is purely a halakhic marker — it chose instead to describe the Kohanim returning home to eat their terumah. This is not merely a technical signal for nightfall. It evokes the going-to-sleep model rather than the halakhic night-and-day model. The image is of people coming home at the end of the day, going through the mikvah, and now sitting down to eat. The time of Shema is framed within that human rhythm, not as an abstract legal category.

### Berakhot: Slice of Life

This quality — grounding the law within the textures of daily life — is strikingly pronounced throughout Masechet Berakhot in a way that sets it apart from most other tractates. In this masechet you find out about Kohanim coming home to eat their terumah. Turning the page, you will find out about when poor people eat their evening meal, about what happens on Erev Shabbat when normal people come in from the day, about someone who is up in a tree when the time to say Shema arrives, about someone who is traveling. The Mishna already showed us Rabban Gamliel's sons returning from a party late at night. This is a masechet saturated with the details of how people actually live.

And this connects directly to the verses of Shema themselves: בשבתך בביתך ובלכתך בדרך ובשכבך ובקומך — when you sit in your house, when you walk on the road, when you lie down, when you rise. The Shema is an obligation that inhabits all dimensions of daily life, and the way this masechet frames its discussion reflects that quality.

### Terumah Post-Mikdash

One might ask: were Kohanim actually eating terumah when the Mishna was written? After the destruction of the Beit HaMikdash, the status of terumah — whether it retained its d'Oraita status or became d'Rabbanan — is itself subject to debate. This is a legitimate question. But there is also a larger point at stake. One of the animating concerns of Masechet Berakhot, situated as it is in a post-Mikdash world, is precisely the question of how to transfer the kedushah of a Mikdash-centered religious life into the fabric of everyday existence. The introduction of Kohanim and terumah — and further on, the burning of sacrificial fats — is also an implicit gesture toward that other world of kedushah, and part of what the masechet is doing is drawing those worlds into conversation with one another.

### Nichnasim: Where From?

The word nichnasim — "enter" — invites the question of where exactly the Kohanim are entering from. The Gemara in Shabbat suggests that Kohanim went to the mikvah right before nightfall. It appears that the mikvaot were located somewhat on the outskirts, and the Kohanim were immersing there and then returning — entering back into their homes. This is another detail that places you concretely inside the life being described: Kohanim walking back from the mikvah at the edge of town as the day comes to an end, about to sit down to their terumah meal as the stars come out. You are not reading an abstraction; you are inside that moment.

### Avodah Shebalev Link

The introduction of the Kohanim and the later mention of sacrificial fats might also be understood as an implicit framing of Shema and Tefillah as avodah shebalev — the service of the heart. By opening with the world of Kohanim, terumah, and the altar, the Mishna places prayer within the same conceptual space as priestly avodah, suggesting that in a post-Mikdash world, the heart's devotion takes on the role that Temple service once played. This connects to the broader point above about transferring kedushah into everyday life.

## Kaparah Lo Me'akva

### Kaparah Lo Me'akva

The Gemara now identifies a second halakhic teaching that the Mishna communicates in passing — beyond the timing of Shema and beyond the time when Kohanim eat terumah. That teaching is: כפרה לא מעכבא — a pending sacrifice does not prevent a person from eating terumah once nightfall has arrived.

### Uva Hashemesh Vetaher

This is derived from the verse: ובא השמש וטהר — "the sun sets and he becomes pure." The immediate context of this verse concerns a person who became tamei through a one-day tumah, went to the mikvah that day, and now waits for the residual tumah to be removed. ביאת שמשו מעכבתו מלאכול בתרומה — the setting of the sun is what holds him back from eating terumah. Once sunset and then nightfall arrive, he may eat. ואין כפרתו מעכבתו — but if he still has a pending sacrifice to bring — relevant for those with seven-day tumot such as a zav or zavah, who after their seven-day waiting period and immersion must still bring a sacrifice the next day — that pending sacrifice does not hold him back from eating terumah. He may eat terumah from nightfall even before bringing the sacrifice.

Now, the underlying question about this verse is sharper than the kaparah point. The verse says ובא השמש וטהר. Two distinct questions arise. First, does ובא השמש mean the setting of the sun — ביאת שמשו — or does it mean the sun rising, ba in the sense of coming rather than going? Second, what does וטהר mean — does it mean טהר יומא, that the day has been fully cleansed, implying you must wait until the stars come out; or does it mean טהר גברא, that the person himself becomes pure — possibly by bringing his sacrifice?

The second reading, associated by Rashi with the reading טהר גברא, might suggest that what the verse is describing is not the passive passage of nightfall but the active purification of the individual through the bringing of his sacrifice. Under this reading, ובא השמש might refer to the sunrise — the sun coming up on the following day — at which point the man brings his sacrifice and becomes fully pure. That would mean a kaparah does indeed prevent eating terumah until the sacrifice is brought.

### Taher Yoma Drasha

Rabbah bar Rav Shila resolves this by attending to the grammar of the verse. If the verse meant to say that the man actively brings his sacrifice and thereby becomes pure, it would have used the active reflexive: ויתהר — and he shall purify himself. But the verse says וטהר, a form that reads as something happening on its own. This means טהר יומא — the day itself becomes cleansed. The day is fully over; all light has been removed. And this gives us not merely sunset but the full onset of night — which in halakhic terms means tzeit hakochavim, the emergence of the stars.

### I'arev Shimsha

Rabbah bar Rav Shila supports this with a folk expression: איערב שמשא ואידכי יומא — the sun has set and the day has been cleansed. The day is completely over. The folk idiom captures precisely the same idea: it is not enough that the sun has dipped below the horizon; the day must be fully purged of light, and that is when night truly begins.

### West Resolves from Braita

The Gemara adds that in the West — in Eretz Yisrael — this teaching of Rabbah bar Rav Shila was not known. They raised the same question independently: does ובא השמש mean ביאת שמשו — sunset as the threshold — making וטהר mean טהר יומא and pointing to tzeit hakochavim? Or does it mean ביאת אורו — the going away of the sun's light, i.e., ordinary sunset — making וטהר mean טהר גברא, referring to the sacrifice?

They did not resolve it through the hermeneutical argument about ויתהר versus וטהר. Instead, they resolved it from an external rabbinic source: והדר פשטוה מברייתא — they derived it from a Braita. The Braita states: סימן לדבר צאת הכוכבים — the sign for the matter is the emergence of the stars. From this transmitted rabbinic ruling, they concluded: שמע מינה ביאת שמשו הוא — that what the verse intends is sunset in the sense of the day fully setting, and that וטהר means טהר יומא — the day is completely cleansed at tzeit hakochavim.

### Two Modes of Tradition

This passage is worth pausing over because it places side by side two fundamentally different modes of rabbinic tradition. The question is: how do we know what a verse means? One approach is hermeneutic engagement — attending closely to the word choice of the Torah, noticing that ויתהר would have been the appropriate form if human action were required, and therefore deriving that וטהר must mean something automatic, something the day does on its own. This is the mode of Rabbah bar Rav Shila. The other approach is essentially traditionary — the rabbis have passed down that tzeit hakochavim is the relevant threshold, and the Braita simply states it as a received ruling: סימן לדבר צאת הכוכבים.

Both are legitimate forms of halakhic knowledge, and they arrive at the same conclusion. But one is grounded in creative textual interpretation, the other in the authority of transmitted tradition. There is a further point worth noting. The Braita, in the style typical of the Mishna, is apodictic — it simply states the law. The Gemara, which is all about conversation and active engagement, is unsatisfied with that. When the Gemara asks "how do we know the verse means this?" it is pressing beyond the tradition to the underlying reasoning, preferring to locate the meaning within the text itself. In a sense, as one might observe, what the Gemara has done is take a received ruling and demand that it be recontextualized as a hermeneutic derivation — transforming one modality of tradition into the other.

## Three Shema Start Ti (II)

### Poor Man: Bread & Salt

The Gemara now challenges the Mishna's description of when evening Shema begins. The Mishna says it begins when the Kohanim enter to eat their terumah. But a Braita teaches: מאימתי קוראים שמע בערבית? — When do you read Shema at night? — משעני נכנס לאכול פיתו במלח — from when the poor person comes in to eat his bread with salt.

This is a very different model — and also very much a slice of life. Notice the detail: not just when someone begins eating, but what they are eating. A poor man eating bread with salt. You are suddenly placed inside that house. And the window for saying Shema is strikingly short: עד שעה שעומד לפטור מסעודתו — until the time when his meal is normally over. The obligation is tied to the period right before going to sleep, with no long extension into the night. Tosafot, noting that the man is eating during this window, suggests that practically speaking he says Shema just before sitting down to eat — having come into the house but not yet started the meal.

One might also observe that the poor man eats later than one might initially assume — he is out working longer. This will become significant shortly.

### Erev Shabbat Entry

A Tosefta gives yet another formulation. מאימתי מתחילים לקרות קריאת שמע בערבית? — When do you begin Shema at night? משעה שבני אדם נכנסים לאכול פיתן בערבי שבתות — from when people come in to eat their bread on erev Shabbat. This is not the poor man on a weekday, but ordinary, middle-class people on erev Shabbat, who finish their work somewhat earlier and come in before dark. דברי רבי מאיר — that is Rabbi Meir's position. וחכמים אומרים — the Chachamim say: משעה שהכהנים זכאים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim are permitted to eat their terumah. Note the shift in register: the Mishna described the Kohanim actually eating; the Chachamim here speak in legal terms — when they are entitled to eat. סימן לדבר צאת הכוכבים — the indicator for this is the emergence of the stars.

## Kaparah Lo Me'akva (II)

### Nechemiah: Zecher Ladavar

The Tosefta continues: ואף על פי שאין ראיה לדבר זכר לדבר — although there is no proof from Torah for this, there is an allusion. The distinction matters: halakhic law can only be definitively derived from Torah; when a source from Nevi'im or Ketuvim is cited, it is zecher ladavar — a hint or echo, not a binding proof.

The verse cited is from Nechemiah, describing the workers rebuilding the Beit HaMikdash: ואנחנו עושים במלאכה וחציים מחזיקים ברמחים מעלות השחר עד צאת הכוכבים — we were doing the work, and half of them were holding spears, from the morning star until the stars came out. And another verse: והיו לנו הלילה משמר והיום מלאכה — at night we would stand guard, and by day we would work.

Taken together, these verses define the boundaries of the workday: from alot hashachar until tzeit hakochavim. If that is what constitutes day, then tzeit hakochavim is when night begins — which is when terumah becomes permissible and when Shema is first recited.

### Why Second Verse Needed

One might ask why a second verse is needed. The first verse already tells you that work ran from alot hashachar until tzeit hakochavim. Why add the verse that says "at night we would guard, by day we would work"?

The answer is that without the second verse, one might argue that the workers in Nechemiah were simply dedicated people who pushed beyond the natural day — starting before dawn and finishing after nightfall, going extra early and extra late. The second verse forecloses this interpretation. By stating that the day is for work and the night is for guarding, it establishes that the first verse is tracking the natural boundaries of day and night, not the unusual dedication of a particular workforce. The work ran from alot hashachar until tzeit hakochavim because those are the natural markers of day — not because the workers were exceptionally zealous.

## Three Shema Start Ti (II)

### Ani vs. Kohen: Same Time?

The Gemara now examines whether the poor man's time and the Kohen's time are the same. A moment ago it was suggested — and left open — that they could be. The Gemara now presses harder. The end of the Braita with the poor man's time clearly disagrees with the end of the Mishna — the poor man has only a short window, while the Mishna gives you until the end of the first watch, or midnight, or all night. But what about the beginning — does the poor man start at the same time as the Kohanim? The Gemara initially says לא — they could be the same. The עני and the כהן are one time: עני וכהן חד שיעורא הוא.

### Full Braita: All Opinions

The Gemara then presents a fuller Braita listing all the opinions on when evening Shema begins:

Rabbi Eliezer says: משישקע החמה — from when the sun sets, i.e., from kidush hayom on erev Shabbat, which is effectively sunset.

Rabbi Yehoshua says: משעה שהכהנים מטוהרים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim are fully purified to eat their terumah.

Rabbi Meir says: משעה שהכהנים טובלים לאכול בתרומתן — from when the Kohanim immerse to eat their terumah. This is before sunset — they immerse in the mikvah right before nightfall.

Rabbi Chanina says: משעה שעני נכנס לאכול פיתו במלח — from when the poor man comes in to eat his bread with salt.

Rabbi Achai (and some say Rabbi Acha) says: משעה שרוב בני אדם נכנסים להסב — from when the majority of people come in to recline. This is the Roman-style reclining at a proper meal — not sitting down, but reclining on a dining couch, as at the Pesach seder. This would naturally happen somewhat later in the evening, after preparation, and would represent the middle class's dinner hour.

### Three Social Tiers

Here one may observe a striking social panorama: three tiers of society serve as the reference points for when Shema begins. There are the Kohanim — the priestly class, defined by their ritual life. There are the aniyim — the poor, eating bread with salt at the end of their long working day. And there are rov bnei adam — ordinary middle-class people, reclining for a proper evening meal. The question of which group sets the standard — whose daily rhythm defines the time of Shema — is itself a question worth reflecting upon. The Gemara describes all three without adjudicating which is the "natural" model.

### Chanina vs. Yehoshua

The Gemara now forces the conclusion that the poor man's time and the Kohen's time must in fact be different. Rabbi Chanina says Shema begins when the ani eats; Rabbi Yehoshua says it begins when the Kohanim are purified to eat terumah. These appear to be two distinct opinions in a debate. If they were describing the same moment, there would be nothing to argue about — they would simply be two descriptions of one time. Therefore: עני שיעורא לחוד וכהן שיעורא לחוד — the time of the ani is distinct from the time of the kohen. שמע מינה.

### Yehuda Challenges Meir

Having established the full spectrum of opinions, the Gemara returns to Rabbi Meir's position: Shema begins when the Kohanim go to the mikvah. Rabbi Yehuda challenges this: והלא כהנים מבעוד יום טובלים — the Kohanim immerse while it is still day. How can the night Shema begin at a moment that is halakhically daytime? If your framework is night and day, you cannot start the nighttime obligation before night has begun.

### Meir: Bein Hashmashot Yo…

Rabbi Meir's response: מי סברת דאנא אבין השמשות דידך קאמינא? — Do you think I am referring to your bein hashmashot? Rabbi Yehuda's bein hashmashot, as developed in tractate Shabbat, runs from sunset until the stars come out — roughly thirteen to fifteen minutes. Prior to that period is unambiguously day. Rabbi Meir says: I am not placing Shema there.

אנא אבין השמשות דרבי יוסי קאמינא — I am speaking of Rabbi Yosi's bein hashmashot. Rabbi Yosi holds that bein hashmashot is not a span of time but a split second — an instant that occurs immediately before the stars come out. דאמר רבי יוסי בין השמשות כהרף עין — like the blink of an eye. זה נכנס וזה יוצא ואי אפשר לעמוד עליו — one goes in and one comes out, and it is impossible to fix that precise moment. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yosi, almost the entire period between sunset and tzeit is halakhically still day. Rabbi Meir positions the immersion of the Kohanim in this window — after sunset but still, per Rabbi Yosi, halakhically day.

What this tells us is significant: for Rabbi Meir, it does not bother us that the night Shema begins during what is halakhically still day. What matters is that the sun has set — the day has functionally ended for human experience — and people are beginning to wrap up and come home. The going-to-sleep framework, rather than the abstract night-and-day framework, is determinative.

### Ani Later Than Kohen

Having established that the ani's time and the Kohen's time are different, the Gemara asks: which is later? הי מינייהו מאוחר? מסתברא דעני מאוחר — it stands to reason that the ani eats even later than tzeit hakochavim. The poor man works longer; he comes in after the stars have already come out. This makes intuitive sense.

The logical argument is as follows: if we were to say the ani is earlier than tzeit, then since the next earlier time is sunset (Rabbi Eliezer's position), and we have no reason to assume a distinct slice of time between sunset and tzeit, we would effectively collapse the ani's time into Rabbi Eliezer's. Since Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Eliezer appear to present distinct positions, they cannot refer to the same moment. Therefore the ani must be placed on the other side of tzeit — later, not earlier. אלא לאו שמע מינה דעני מאוחר. שמע מינה.

### Trei Tannai Aliba

The Gemara closes by noting apparent contradictions within the positions of individual Tannaim across the various sources cited. קשיא דרבי מאיר דרבי מאיר — Rabbi Meir's position in one Braita does not match his position in another. The resolution: תרי תנאי אליבא דרבי מאיר — there are two traditions about what Rabbi Meir actually held, reflected in different formulations. Similarly: קשיא דרבי אליעזר דרבי אליעזר — Rabbi Eliezer in the Braita places a different time than Rabbi Eliezer in the Mishna. Again: תרי תנאי אליבא דרבי אליעזר — two traditions about his position.

An alternative resolution is offered for the Rabbi Eliezer problem: ואי בעית אימא רישא לאו דרבי אליעזר היא — one may argue that the beginning of the Mishna — when the Kohanim eat their terumah — was not part of Rabbi Eliezer's statement at all. Rabbi Eliezer's contribution to the Mishna was only the end-time: עד סוף האשמורה הראשונה. The start-time, as formulated in the Mishna, need not be attributed to him.
