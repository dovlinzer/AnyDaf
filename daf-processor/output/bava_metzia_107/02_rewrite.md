# Bava Metzia 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Field Rental & Crops

### Mishna: Crop Changes

The *daf* opens at the bottom of 107b with the *mishna* governing a tenant farmer who has rented a field under an agreed crop arrangement. The *mishna* establishes: if someone received a field to plant barley, he may not substitute wheat, since wheat depletes the soil more heavily than barley. However, if the field was leased for wheat, the tenant may plant barley instead, since barley is less damaging than what was originally specified. On the parallel axis of grain categories: if the field was leased for *tevuah* (cereal grain), he may not plant *kitnit* (legumes), but if it was leased for *kitnit*, he may substitute *tevuah*.

### RSbG: Contract Compliance

In both cases, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents and forbids any substitution whatsoever. His position is that whatever was stipulated in the contract must be honored — planting something different, even something less harmful to the soil, constitutes a violation of the agreement.

### Chisda's Challenge

The *gemara* opens with Rav Chisda's question: what is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's rationale? On the face of it, if the substitute crop is *less* damaging to the field than what was contracted, the landowner ought to have no complaint — one could even argue the tenant is doing him a favor. The principle *zachi le'adam shelo befanav, aval ein chav leshelo befanav* would suggest that acting in someone's benefit without their presence is permitted, whereas causing harm is not. A less damaging crop should therefore fall on the permissible side of that line.

### No Deception

The answer given is rooted in a verse from the prophets: *She'erit Yisrael lo ya'asu avlah velo yedabru kazav velo yimatze befihem lashon tarmit* — the remnant of Israel shall not commit wrongdoing, shall not speak falsehood, and no deceitful tongue shall be found in their mouth. The principle is that Jews are forbidden to misrepresent what they undertook to do. If a contract specifies one crop, planting a different one — even a superior one for the soil — constitutes a form of deception relative to the agreed terms.

### Purim Collections

This prompts a *meitivi*: a challenge from a *baraita* concerning collections for *Purim*. The *baraita* states that funds collected for *Purim* may only be distributed for *Purim* needs, and a poor person may not even take from those funds so much as a strap for his sandal — unless the town council explicitly permitted it. This represents the opinion of Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Meir. Yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes a lenient position there, suggesting that one need not be overly strict when no real damage is caused. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is lenient in the *Purim* case, why is he strict here about crop substitution? The two positions appear inconsistent. Rashi explains Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's lenient position in the *Purim* case: when there is truly no loss, people do not insist on strict compliance. Why, then, is he strict with field rental?

### Field Rotation Damage

Abaye resolves the tension by invoking the teaching of his master, Rabbah bar Nachmani: the reason Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids even the "less damaging" substitution is that he is concerned about cumulative damage over multiple seasons. The very statement *hai man de'nicha leih detifshir arei* — one who wants his field to deteriorate — should plant wheat one year and barley the next. The alternation between the two, done carelessly, can in fact harm the field over time. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's concern is therefore not about the single-year effect of the substitution but about the multi-year rotation pattern.

### Plowing & Soil Care

The *gemara* elaborates: planting one year in one direction and the following year perpendicular to that can damage the field. The qualification is added: this concern applies only when the field has *not* been plowed a second time between planting seasons — *dela keriv utnei*. If a full additional plowing cycle is performed after harvest and before the next planting, the damage is mitigated. This connects to a broader agricultural question about whether plowing precedes or follows sowing — a practice that varied between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel, and which is treated differently in contexts such as *hilchot shevi'it* and Shabbat. In any event, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's prohibition is explained: when a landlord specifies a particular crop, it is because he is tracking the rotation of his field across years, and the tenant cannot unilaterally alter that pattern even in a direction that seems beneficial in isolation.

## Grain vs. Legumes

### Bavel vs. EY Ruling

The *gemara* records that Rav Yehuda taught Ravin the ruling *tevuah lo yizra'ena kitnit* — but in the opposite formulation from the *mishna*. When the student protests that this contradicts the *mishna*, Rav Yehuda responds: *lo kashya — ha lan ve'ha le'hu* — there is no contradiction, because what I am teaching applies to us in Bavel, and what the *mishna* says applies to them in Eretz Yisrael. Regional variation in *halacha* based on local agricultural conditions is well-attested, and this is simply another instance of it.

### Rain vs. Irrigation

The Nimukei Yosef explains the underlying distinction: the difference between the two rulings turns on whether a region's fields are watered by rainfall or by river irrigation. Eretz Yisrael is predominantly rain-fed (*baal*), while Bavel depends on irrigation from its major river systems. The relative impact of *tevuah* versus *kitnit* on the soil differs depending on the water source, and this is what generates the divergent rulings. The Nimukei Yosef further applies this principle to his own context, addressing whether Spain — where he wrote — falls into the rain-fed or irrigation category.

## Tachal in Flax

### Hardened Cress

The *gemara* transitions to a series of teachings framed as Rav Yehuda addressing Ravin bar Rav Nachman with the phrase *Ravin achi* — my brother Ravin. The first concerns *tachal* (garden cress, *Lepidium sativum*), a small-leafed herb also used medicinally, which sometimes grows among *pishtan* (flax). Rav Yehuda teaches that such garden cress growing among flax fields carries no concern of theft — because while it is growing, it actually damages the flax. However, once the cress has hardened and produced its seeds, even cress growing among flax does become subject to theft prohibitions, because by that point it has already done all the damage it will do to the flax. As the *gemara* states: *mai de'afsidei afsidei* — what it damaged, it has already damaged. The cress is now fully grown, the harm to the flax is done, and the cress itself has value that the owner may not be deprived of.

### Cress Damages Flax

Rav Yehuda's baseline ruling rests on Rashi's explanation that the cress, while still growing among the flax, is actively harming it — and therefore the cress lacks independent legal protection during that period.

## Border Mound Trees

### Shared Orchard Saying

Rav Yehuda says to Ravin bar Rav Nachman: *hanei diley dilakh u'dilakh diley* — what is mine is yours and what is yours is mine. This is not a generous offer but a description of a practical reality: when two neighboring fields are divided by a *metzar* (a raised boundary mound), and trees have been planted along that mound, it sometimes happens that a tree whose trunk appears on one side actually has its roots drawing nourishment from the other side, and vice versa. The result is that what looks like your tree is nourishing itself from my land, and what looks like my tree draws from yours.

## Tachal in Flax…

### Border Planting

The exception to the general permissibility of taking garden cress from among flax applies when the cress is found growing specifically on the borders between fields — *agvulei*. In that case it is prohibited to take the cress, since a person who deliberately plants cress on a field boundary has clearly cultivated it intentionally, and it therefore belongs to that owner.

## Border Mound Trees…

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The *gemara* records a formal dispute: Rav says that a tree standing on the *metzar* is assigned according to which way it leans — *hanoteh le'kan le'kan ve'hanoteh le'kan le'kan* — if the tree leans toward one side, it belongs to that side. Shmuel says *cholkin* — the fruit is split between the two field owners.

### Baraita Support

A *meitivi* is raised: a *baraita* states explicitly that a tree standing on the *metzar* is subject to *yachloku* — the owners divide the fruit. This appears to be a *tiyuvta de'Rav*, a direct refutation of Rav's position. The *gemara* then reports: *tirgeman Shmuel aliba de'Rav* — Shmuel himself provided an interpretation of the *baraita* that would align it with Rav's view.

### Metzer Definition

Before proceeding to the resolution, the *gemara* requires clarity on what a *metzar* actually is. It is not merely an imaginary line; it is a raised mound of earth running between two adjacent fields, serving as a physical border marker. In the agricultural context of this *sugya*, an orchard has been planted along this mound — meaning the trees literally grow on the elevated strip of soil between the two properties.

This is where a significant *machloket* between Rashi and Tosafot emerges regarding what it means for a tree to stand *al ha'metzar*:

### Rashi: Roots & Fruit Spl…

Rashi understands that the *metzar* has real physical width, and the trees planted on it may have their roots extending to one side while the trunk and fruit overhang the other. The *gemara*'s case, according to Rashi, involves trees where the roots are on one side of the imaginary borderline within the mound, while the canopy and fruit are on the other. The principle *diley dilakh u'dilakh diley* describes precisely this situation: a tree whose fruit appears to be entirely on one owner's side is in fact nourished by roots drawing from the adjacent field. Rashi's illustration requires two such trees, leaning in opposite directions, to generate the mutual scenario where each owner has trees whose fruit belongs to the other.

The difficulty for Rashi is then raised: given the principle derived elsewhere in Bava Batra 27a that *shadei nufo batar ikaro* — the canopy of a tree follows its roots, meaning a tree belongs to the property where it is planted — why would a tree whose roots are on one side not simply belong entirely to that side? Rashi's answer lies in the precise configuration: the tree is *mamash al ha'metzar*, directly on the mound, with roots on one side of the border within that mound.

### Tosafot: Shared Space

Tosafot object to Rashi's approach on grounds that their version of the text does not read *al ha'metzar* but rather *al tzad ha'metzar* — beside the *metzar*, not on it. For Tosafot, the principle that a tree's ownership follows its roots renders Rashi's entire construction unnecessary and internally inconsistent. Instead, Tosafot propose that the *metzar* is a boundary line with no spatial width — it is an infinitesimally thin dividing line, not a strip of land. The roots of a tree planted on this line therefore exist in shared space, belonging to neither owner exclusively. Since the roots are in genuinely shared territory, ownership of the fruit cannot be determined by root location. The fruit then falls on one side or the other, and that is what Rav is tracking — which direction the fruit hangs. When Rav says *hanoteh le'kan le'kan*, he is acknowledging that the fruit determines apparent ownership, but the deeper legal reality is shared roots, which is why Shmuel's ruling of *yachloku* ultimately prevails.

### Reconciling Views

The *gemara*'s phrase *tirgeman Shmuel aliba de'Rav* — Shmuel interpreted the case according to Rav — represents an attempt to show that Shmuel's rule of *yachloku* need not flatly contradict Rav's principle. There are cases in which even Rav would agree to split the fruit, and Shmuel is identifying the conditions under which that convergence occurs.

### Rav's Exception

The specific condition under which even Rav would agree to *yachloku* is described as *b'memale kol ha'metzar kulo* — when the roots fill the entire *metzar*. According to Rashi, this means the roots extend equally to both sides of the boundary within the mound. According to Tosafot, it means the roots occupy the full shared space of the boundary line, with the tree's weight and fruit hanging off to one side. In this configuration, even Rav acknowledges that the fruit cannot simply be assigned to the side it physically hangs over, because the root structure that nourishes the tree is equally shared.

The *gemara* then asks: *ei hachi, mai lemimra* — if that is the case, what is the ruling actually teaching us? Surely if the roots are equally on both sides, of course we split the fruit? The answer is: *lo tzricha d'tali t'unei l'chad gisa* — it is necessary to teach us this rule precisely when the fruit load hangs heavily to one side. One might have thought that in such a case, whoever the fruit falls on should receive it, since the physical reality favors them. The ruling teaches that even then, *yachloku*.

### Cutting Method

But *yachloku* raises a practical question: how does one actually divide the fruit equally when the mass of it hangs entirely to one side? The *gemara* addresses this. If one simply cuts the tree along the line of the *metzar* — running the cut in the direction of the boundary — the owner whose side the fruit is on would receive all or most of it, and the distribution would be unequal. Instead, the cut should be made *perpendicularly* — across the line of the *metzar*, slicing through the mass of the tree horizontally, such that each owner receives an equal portion of the fruit even though the whole canopy is displaced to one side. This is the meaning of *mah chazit d'falagat hachi, pelag hachi* — why do you think the division goes *this* way? Divide it *that* way instead. The underlying *svara* is that since the roots are equally shared, the fruit must also be divided equally, and the only way to achieve that when the canopy is asymmetric is to cut perpendicularly to the *metzar* rather than along it.

## Proximity Issues

### City Proximity

The *gemara* continues with the series of teachings addressed by Rav Gidal to Rav Nachman in the *Ravin achi* format: do not purchase land adjacent to a city. The concern involves issues of safety and interference — proximity to populated areas creates complications for agricultural property.

### Harvest Field Access

This connects to a ruling of Rav, transmitted through Rav Huna: it is forbidden for a person to stand in his neighbor's field at the time when the grain is standing and ready for harvest. The concern is one of intrusion at a moment of particular vulnerability for the crop.

### Deuteronomy Blessings

The *gemara* then records an encounter in which Rav Abba found students of Rav and asked them: how did Rav interpret the verses in Deuteronomy — *baruch ata ba'ir u'varuch ata ba'sadeh, baruch ata b'vo'echa u'varuch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed are you in the city, blessed in the field, blessed in your comings and goings?

The students reported Rav's interpretations as follows. *Baruch ata ba'ir* — blessed in the city — means that your house should be near the *beit ha'keneset*. This initially appears to contradict Rav's earlier warning against purchasing land near a city, a tension the *gemara* will resolve. *Baruch ata ba'sadeh* — blessed in the field — means that your property should be near your home. *Baruch ata b'vo'echa* — blessed in your coming — means that you should not find your wife in a state of doubtful *niddah* when you return home from travel. *Baruch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed in your going — means that your descendants should be like you.

### Alternative Blessing

Rabbi Yochanan offers a competing set of interpretations. *Baruch ata ba'ir* — blessed in the city — means that your bathroom should be near your table. Rabbi Yochanan explicitly rejects the *beit ha'keneset* reading, and the *gemara* explains that this is consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's position elsewhere: *sachar pesi'ot yesh* — there is reward for each step one walks to the *beit ha'keneset*, and therefore proximity to the synagogue is actually undesirable from a spiritual-reward perspective. *Baruch ata ba'sadeh* — blessed in the field — means that your assets should be divided in thirds: a third in grain, a third in olives, and a third in grapes — the Mediterranean agricultural triad. *Baruch ata b'vo'echa u'varuch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed in your comings and goings — means that your departure from the world should be as pure as your arrival into it, just as you entered without sin, so may you leave without sin.

### Walled City Distinction

The *gemara* returns to the tension in Rav's position — how can Rav say one should live near the *beit ha'keneset* while also warning against buying land adjacent to a city? The resolution: *ha d'mehader lei shura v'ritka, ha d'lo mehader lei shura v'ritka* — the two cases are distinct. When the city has a surrounding wall and moat, the field adjacent to it carries none of the dangers that prompted Rav's warning, and proximity to the *beit ha'keneset* is indeed a blessing. When the city lacks a wall, the adjacent land is exposed to the various problems Rav cautioned against, and one should not purchase it.

## Disease Interpretations

### Rav: Evil Eye

The *gemara* now addresses a series of interpretations of the verse from Deuteronomy 7:15: *ve'hesir Hashem mimcha kol choli* — and God will remove from you all illness. What is the *choli* — the illness — that God promises to remove? Rav says: *zo ayin* — this is the evil eye, *ayin ha'ra*. The *gemara* notes that this is *Rav l'taameih* — consistent with Rav's general orientation on such matters. The evidence: Rav would go to the cemetery and perform certain acts — Rashi explains this involved a form of inquiry addressed to those buried there — and Rav determined that 99 out of 100 deaths were caused by the evil eye, with only one attributable to natural causes.

### Shmuel: Wind

Shmuel says the *choli* of the verse refers to wind — *ruach*. This too is *Shmuel l'taameih*, consistent with Shmuel's broader view that *hakol be'ruach* — wind is the underlying cause of most illness. One might object: what about people killed by the Roman government — surely wind does not account for those deaths? The *gemara* responds: even in those cases, were it not for the wind aggravating the situation, a medicinal ointment — *sama* — would have allowed the body to heal and survive. Rashi understands *avdei lehu sama v'chayei* as referring to a healing compound that could have restored the body under better conditions.

### Chanina: Cold

Rabbi Chanina says the *choli* is *tzina* — cold. This is likewise consistent with Rabbi Chanina's position elsewhere: *hakol b'yedei Shamayim chutz mi'tzinim pachim* — everything is in the hands of Heaven except for cold drafts. Rabbi Chanina cites the verse *tzinim pachim b'derech ikesh, shomer nafsho yirchak meihem* — thorns and snares lie in the path of the crooked, one who guards his soul will keep away from them.

### Yosi: Excretions

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina says the *choli* refers to bodily excretions — *tzoah*. He connects this to a ruling that the discharge of the nose and the discharge of the ear are mostly harmful in large quantities, though small amounts may be beneficial: *ruban kashah u'miutan yafeh*.

### Elazar: Bile

Rabbi Elazar says the *choli* is *marah* — bile, the substance produced by the gallbladder. In the ancient medical framework of the four humors, bile was held responsible for a wide range of physical ailments.

### Bile Diseases

A *baraita* is cited in support of Rabbi Elazar's view: *machalah zo marah* — the word *machalah* (illness) in the verse refers specifically to the gallbladder and its bile. Why is *marah* called *machalah* in general? Because *hi machalah kol gufo shel adam* — it afflicts the entire body of a person. A further explanation: *machalah* is used because there are 83 illnesses that depend upon *marah*, stemming from dysfunction of the bile or gallbladder. And all 83 of these illnesses are counteracted by *pat shacharit b'melach v'kiton shel mayim* — morning bread with salt and a jug of water.

## Morning Bread Benefits

### First Benefits

The *baraita* continues with a formal enumeration: thirteen beneficial things are said about *pat shacharit* — eating bread in the morning. It protects from the sun (*chamah*), from cold (*tzina*), from harmful winds (*zikin*), and from demons (*mazikin*). It brings wisdom (*machkimat*), success in legal proceedings (*zocheh ba'din*), enables Torah study (*lilmod Torah*), and enables teaching Torah (*lelamed*).

### More Benefits

The remaining benefits: one's words are heard (*devarav nishma'in*), one's learning is retained (*talmudo mitkayem b'yado*), one's body does not produce fever or excess heat (*ein besaro ma'aleh hevel*), one is drawn only to his wife and not to other women (*nizkak l'ishto v'eino mitaveh l'ishah acheret*), and the bread kills intestinal worms (*horeg kinah she'bivnei me'ayim*).

### Jealousy & Love

An additional opinion adds: *af motziah et ha'kinah u'machnisah et ha'ahavah* — morning bread also removes jealousy and brings love.

### Running Speed

Rabbah cites to Rava bar Mari a popular saying: sixty runners cannot catch up to a man who has eaten his morning bread. The idiom expresses the vigor and alertness that *pat shacharit* confers.

### Seasonal Eating

The rabbinic instruction drawn from this is: *hashkem ve'echol bakayitz mipnei ha'chamah u'vahoref mipnei ha'tzina* — rise early and eat in the summer because of the heat, and in the winter because of the cold. Morning bread protects against both extremes.

### Isaiah Source

When Rava bar Mari asks for a scriptural basis, Rabbah cites Isaiah 49:10: *lo yir'avu velo yitzma'u velo yakeim sharav va'shamesh* — they shall not hunger nor thirst, nor shall heat or sun strike them. The *gemara* explains the verse as a chain: the protection from heat and sun is a consequence of not being hungry or thirsty, and not being hungry or thirsty results from eating morning bread — the elements of the verse are mutually dependent.

### Deuteronomy Source

Rava offers a superior scriptural basis from Deuteronomy: *va'avadtem et Hashem Elokeichem u'verach et lachmecha v'et meimecha* — and you shall serve the Lord your God, and He will bless your bread and your water. The *gemara* interprets: *va'avadtem et Hashem Elokeichem* refers to *keriyat shema* and *tefillah*. *U'verach et lachmecha v'et meimecha* refers to bread dipped in salt and a jug of water. The verse then continues: *va'hasiroti machalah mi'kirbecha* — and I will remove illness from among you. The sequence is complete: prayer, then morning bread with salt and water, and God will remove all illness.

### Bread & Salt

The prescription is thus: *pat be'melach v'kiton shel mayim* — bread dipped in salt accompanied by a jug of water, eaten in the morning after *tefillah*. This practice is attested in later *siddur* traditions as well; the Tunisian liturgical tradition, for instance, includes after *shacharit* a passage on *pat shacharit* explicitly citing this *gemara*.

## Land Surveying Rules

### Survey Precision

The *gemara* continues with practical land law. Rav Yehuda instructs Rav Adda the surveyor: *lo tizalzel be'mishchata* — do not be careless in your measurements. Every small piece of land matters, because *kol purta u'purta chazi l'churkama de'rishka* — even a tiny parcel could grow saffron, which was among the most expensive commodities of the ancient world.

### Irrigation Zones

However, Rav Yehuda qualifies: the four *amot* around an irrigation channel — *anigra* — may be measured with somewhat less stringency. Rashi explains that this zone within the field actually belongs collectively to the owners of the surrounding parcels, and since they are co-owners of that strip they can effectively waive precise measurement; the matter of a few inches one way or another does not create a wrongful taking.

### River Banks

Regarding rivers, the rule is different: *de'nahara lo timshach klal* — do not measure the river banks at all. Rav Yehuda states the underlying principle: the four *amot* around the irrigation channel belong to the private co-owners of that field system, but the banks of a public river belong to the entire public — *de'nahara de'chulei alma* — and therefore their precise dimensions cannot be treated as anyone's private property to be measured away.

### Barge Path Clearance

Rabbi Ami made a public announcement: vegetation on both sides of a river must be cleared to the width needed for barge-pullers (*nagdei*) to walk while pulling their ropes — *melo kitfei nagdei bi'trei evrei nahara kutzzu*. Barge-pullers needed to walk along the riverbank while towing vessels with long ropes stretched out behind them, and the cleared path had to be wide enough for that purpose.

### Excessive Clearance

Rav Natan bar Sharya took this ruling and cleared sixteen *amot* on each side. The residents of Mashrunya came and struck him for it. He had reasoned that river banks constitute a *reshut ha'rabbim* — public domain — and the standard width of a *reshut ha'rabbim* is sixteen *amot*.

### Public Domain Distinction

The *gemara* rejects his reasoning: *velo hi* — that is incorrect. In a standard public thoroughfare, sixteen *amot* are required because people need that width to move through freely. On a riverbank, the requirement is functional rather than fixed: *mishum amtuchei ashleihen hu* — the purpose is for barge-pullers to stretch out their towing ropes, and *melo kitfei nagdei sagi* — the width of the barge-pullers' shoulders is sufficient. The principle is that even within the category of public space, different functional needs generate different physical requirements.

### Neighbor Coordination

Rabba bar Rav Huna owned a forest on the side of a river. When told he must clear his portion, he responded: *kutzzu ila'i ve'tata'i va'hadar nikutz ana* — let those above and below me on the river clear their portions first, and then I will clear mine.

### Self-First Principle

This was challenged on the basis of Resh Lakish's principle: *hitkosheshu va'koshu* — the verse says gather yourselves and gather, interpreted as *kshot atzmecha ve'achar kach kshot acherim* — adorn yourself first and then attend to others. One is obligated to fulfill one's own duty without waiting for others to fulfill theirs. If clearing the riverbank is required, Rabba bar Rav Huna cannot condition his compliance on his neighbors' prior compliance.

### Practical Exception

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *aba de'vei Parzak Rofila hava* — the land adjacent to Rabba bar Rav Huna's forest belonged to a man named Parzak, who was a military general. Parzak had made clear that he would clear his side only if Rabba bar Rav Huna cleared his first — but in reality, Parzak was not going to do it regardless. In this specific circumstance, Rabba bar Rav Huna's clearing alone would have been pointless: *de'ei mamtu lehu ashleihu masgei lehu* — if there is enough space on the other side for the barge-pullers to stretch their ropes, the path will function. *Ve'ei lo, lo masgei lehu* — and if there is not, the barge-pullers will remain on whichever side of the river has a workable path and will not cross to Rabba bar Rav Huna's side simply because he cleared a portion. Clearing his section in isolation would accomplish nothing for the public benefit the rule is meant to serve — and therefore it was reasonable to withhold compliance under those particular conditions.

This brings the *daf* to 108a.
