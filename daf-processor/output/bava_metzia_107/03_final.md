# Bava Metzia 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Field Rental & Crops

### Mishna: Crop Changes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְזׇרְעָהּ שְׂעוֹרִים – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה חִטִּים. חִטִּים – יִזְרָעֶנָּה שְׂעוֹרִים, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹסֵר. תְּבוּאָה – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה קִטְנִית. קִטְנִית – יִזְרָעֶנָּה תְּבוּאָה, וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who receives a field from another** in order **to plant it** with **barley,** he may **not plant it** with **wheat,** as wheat weakens the field more than barley does. But if he receives it in order to plant **wheat,** he may **plant it** with **barley** if he wishes, but **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids** it. Similarly, if he receives it to plant **it with grain** he may **not plant it** with **legumes,** as they weaken the field more than grains do, but if he receives it in order to plant **legumes** he may **plant it** with **grain, but Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids** it.

The *daf* opens at the bottom of 107b with the *mishna* governing a tenant farmer who has rented a field under an agreed crop arrangement. The *mishna* establishes: if someone received a field to plant barley, he may not substitute wheat, since wheat depletes the soil more heavily than barley. However, if the field was leased for wheat, the tenant may plant barley instead, since barley is less damaging than what was originally specified. On the parallel axis of grain categories: if the field was leased for *tevuah* (cereal grain), he may not plant *kitnit* (legumes), but if it was leased for *kitnit*, he may substitute *tevuah*.

### RSbG: Contract Compliance

In both cases, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents and forbids any substitution whatsoever. His position is that whatever was stipulated in the contract must be honored — planting something different, even something less harmful to the soil, constitutes a violation of the agreement.

### Chisda's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁאֵרִית יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יַעֲשׂוּ עַוְלָה וְלֹא יְדַבְּרוּ כָזָב וְלֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּפִיהֶם לְשׁוֹן תַּרְמִית״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reason of** the ruling of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel?** The landowner has apparently suffered no loss from the cultivator's actions. His reasoning is **as it is written: "The remnant of Israel shall not do iniquity, nor speak lies, neither shall a deceitful tongue be found in their mouth"** (Zephaniah 3:13). In other words, one may not retract from an obligation accepted upon oneself, even if no one suffers as a result.

The *gemara* opens with Rav Chisda's question: what is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's rationale? On the face of it, if the substitute crop is *less* damaging to the field than what was contracted, the landowner ought to have no complaint — one could even argue the tenant is doing him a favor. The principle *zachi le'adam shelo befanav, aval ein chav leshelo befanav* would suggest that acting in someone's benefit without their presence is permitted, whereas causing harm is not. A less damaging crop should therefore fall on the permissible side of that line.

### No Deception

The answer given is rooted in a verse from the prophets: *She'erit Yisrael lo ya'asu avlah velo yedabru kazav velo yimatze befihem lashon tarmit* — the remnant of Israel shall not commit wrongdoing, shall not speak falsehood, and no deceitful tongue shall be found in their mouth. The principle is that Jews are forbidden to misrepresent what they undertook to do. If a contract specifies one crop, planting a different one — even a superior one for the soil — constitutes a form of deception relative to the agreed terms.

### Purim Collections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מִגְבַּת פּוּרִים לְפוּרִים, וְאֵין מְדַקְדְּקִין בַּדָּבָר. וְאֵין הֶעָנִי רַשַּׁאי לִיקַּח מֵהֶן רְצוּעָה לְסַנְדָּלוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִתְנָה בְּמַעֲמַד אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מֵיקֵל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: The **Purim collection is** only **for** the **Purim** feast, **but one does not scrutinize the matter** by limiting the allocation for the poor to the exact costs of the meal and no more. **And** it is **not permitted for a poor person to purchase** even **a strap for his sandal from it unless he stipulated in the presence of the people of the city** that he may do as he wishes with the money he receives. This is **the statement of Rabbi Ya'akov, who said** it **in the name of Rabbi Meir. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** is **lenient,** as he maintains that donors are not particular in this regard. This shows that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is not bothered by a change of mind if no harm results.

This prompts a *meitivi*: a challenge from a *baraita* concerning collections for *Purim*. The *baraita* states that funds collected for *Purim* may only be distributed for *Purim* needs, and a poor person may not even take from those funds so much as a strap for his sandal — unless the town council explicitly permitted it. This represents the opinion of Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Meir. Yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes a lenient position there, suggesting that one need not be overly strict when no real damage is caused. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is lenient in the *Purim* case, why is he strict here about crop substitution? The two positions appear inconsistent. Rashi explains Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's lenient position in the *Purim* case: when there is truly no loss, people do not insist on strict compliance. Why, then, is he strict with field rental?

### Field Rotation Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן כִּדְמָר. דְּאָמַר מָר: הַאי מַאן דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּתִתְבַּיַּר אַרְעֵיהּ – לִיזְרְעַהּ שַׁתָּא חִטֵּי וְשַׁתָּא שְׂעָרֵי, שַׁתָּא שְׁתִי וְשַׁתָּא עֵרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's reason** is **as explained by the Master,** Abaye's teacher Rabba, **as the Master says: One** for **whom it is preferable to** grow crops and yet have **his land** remain as fertile as if it **lay fallow should plant wheat** one **year and barley** the next **year.** In addition, he should plant one **year lengthwise and** the following **year crosswise.** In this manner, he will prevent the field from being weakened.

Abaye resolves the tension by invoking the teaching of his master, Rabbah bar Nachmani: the reason Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forbids even the "less damaging" substitution is that he is concerned about cumulative damage over multiple seasons. The very statement *hai man de'nicha leih detifshir arei* — one who wants his field to deteriorate — should plant wheat one year and barley the next. The alternation between the two, done carelessly, can in fact harm the field over time. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's concern is therefore not about the single-year effect of the substitution but about the multi-year rotation pattern.

### Plowing & Soil Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא כָּרֵיב וְתָנֵי, אֲבָל כָּרֵיב וְתָנֵי – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And we said** that his use of the land with-out following these dictates would weaken it **only where** he does **not plow and repeat** his plowing, i.e., plow the field twice before reseeding it, **but** if he **plows and repeats** his plowing, **we have no** problem **with it.** Consequently, the reasoning of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is that a change of crops at the wrong time can weaken the field.

The *gemara* elaborates: planting one year in one direction and the following year perpendicular to that can damage the field. The qualification is added: this concern applies only when the field has *not* been plowed a second time between planting seasons — *dela keriv utnei*. If a full additional plowing cycle is performed after harvest and before the next planting, the damage is mitigated. This connects to a broader agricultural question about whether plowing precedes or follows sowing — a practice that varied between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel, and which is treated differently in contexts such as *hilchot shevi'it* and Shabbat. In any event, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's prohibition is explained: when a landlord specifies a particular crop, it is because he is tracking the rotation of his field across years, and the tenant cannot unilaterally alter that pattern even in a direction that seems beneficial in isolation.

## Grain vs. Legumes

### Bavel vs. EY Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּבוּאָה לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה קִטְנִית [וְכוּ׳]. מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרָבִין: תְּבוּאָה – יִזְרָעֶנָּה קִטְנִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: תְּבוּאָה – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה קִטְנִית! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא לַן וְהָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that one who receives a field to plant it with **grain may not plant it** with **legumes. Rav Yehuda taught Ravin** that the *halakha* is that one who receives a field to plant it with **grain** may **plant it** with **legumes.** Ravin **said to him: But didn't we learn** in the mishna that one who receives a field to plant it with **grain may not plant it** with **legumes?** Rav Yehuda **said to him:** This is **not difficult,** as **this** *halakha* **is for us,** the residents of Babylonia, **and that** *halakha* **is for them,** the residents of Eretz Yisrael. Since what type of seed to plant depends on the quality of the land, in Eretz Yisrael, where the land is weak, there is concern that the land will be weakened. This concern does not apply to Babylonian fields.

The *gemara* records that Rav Yehuda taught Ravin the ruling *tevuah lo yizra'ena kitnit* — but in the opposite formulation from the *mishna*. When the student protests that this contradicts the *mishna*, Rav Yehuda responds: *lo kashya — ha lan ve'ha le'hu* — there is no contradiction, because what I am teaching applies to us in Bavel, and what the *mishna* says applies to them in Eretz Yisrael. Regional variation in *halacha* based on local agricultural conditions is well-attested, and this is simply another instance of it.

### Rain vs. Irrigation

The Nimukei Yosef explains the underlying distinction: the difference between the two rulings turns on whether a region's fields are watered by rainfall or by river irrigation. Eretz Yisrael is predominantly rain-fed (*baal*), while Bavel depends on irrigation from its major river systems. The relative impact of *tevuah* versus *kitnit* on the soil differs depending on the water source, and this is what generates the divergent rulings. The Nimukei Yosef further applies this principle to his own context, addressing whether Spain — where he wrote — falls into the rain-fed or irrigation category.

## Tachal in Flax

### Hardened Cress

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרָבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן: רָבִין אֲחִי, הָנֵי תַּחְלֵי דְּבֵי כִתָּנָא אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גְּבוּלִין – יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said to Ravin bar Rav Naḥman: Ravin my brother, those cress** plants that grow **among flax are not subject to** the prohibition of **robbery,** as the one taking them is effectively weeding the field, and the owner prefers that the cress not grow so as to not affect the growing flax. But if the plants **stand on the boundary** of where the flax is growing, so that their remaining there would not have a negative impact on the growing flax, **they are subject to** the prohibition of **robbery,** as it is assumed that the owner grew the plants there intentionally.

The *gemara* transitions to a series of teachings framed as Rav Yehuda addressing Ravin bar Rav Nachman with the phrase *Ravin achi* — my brother Ravin. The first concerns *tachal* (garden cress, *Lepidium sativum*), a small-leafed herb also used medicinally, which sometimes grows among *pishtan* (flax). Rav Yehuda teaches that such garden cress growing among flax fields carries no concern of theft — because while it is growing, it actually damages the flax. However, once the cress has hardened and produced its seeds, even cress growing among flax does become subject to theft prohibitions, because by that point it has already done all the damage it will do to the flax. As the *gemara* states: *mai de'afsidei afsidei* — what it damaged, it has already damaged. The cress is now fully grown, the harm to the flax is done, and the cress itself has value that the owner may not be deprived of.

### Cress Damages Flax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הוּקְשׁוּ לְזֶרַע – אֲפִילּוּ דְּבֵי כִתָּנָא נָמֵי יֵשׁ בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. מַאי טַעְמָא? מַאי דְּאַפְסֵיד – אַפְסֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the cress plants were sufficiently grown to the point that they were **hardened with seed** growing inside, i.e., they were fully grown, then **even** if they were **among the flax, they are subject to** the prohibition of **robbery. What is the reason?** Concerning **that** flax **to which** they have already **caused a loss,** they have already **caused the loss.** Since the flax has already been damaged, by removing the cress now he does nothing to improve the crop and merely takes something from the owner.

Rav Yehuda's baseline ruling rests on Rashi's explanation that the cress, while still growing among the flax, is actively harming it — and therefore the cress lacks independent legal protection during that period.

## Border Mound Trees

### Shared Orchard Saying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרָבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן: רָבִין אַחִי, הָנֵי דִּילִי – דִּילָךְ, וְדִילָךְ – דִּילִי. נְהוּג בְּנֵי מִצְרָא: אִילָן הַנּוֹטֶה לְכָאן – לְכָאן, וְהַנּוֹטֶה לְכָאן – לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** Ravin bar Rav Naḥman and Rav Yehuda owned adjacent fields, and their trees leaned over the boundaries. **Rav Yehuda said to Ravin bar Rav Naḥman: Ravin my brother, these** that are **mine are yours, and** those that are **yours are mine.** This is because it **is customary** with regard to **those** whose fields **border** on the field of their neighbors that with regard to **a tree that tilts to here,** i.e., to the property of one of them, its fruit belongs **to** the owner of the field **here,** to where it is tilting, **and** with regard to **one that tilts to there,** i.e., to the other side, its fruit belongs **to** the owner of the field **there,** as neighbors are not particular which portion of the land actually grew the fruit.

Rav Yehuda says to Ravin bar Rav Nachman: *hanei diley dilakh u'dilakh diley* — what is mine is yours and what is yours is mine. This is not a generous offer but a description of a practical reality: when two neighboring fields are divided by a *metzar* (a raised boundary mound), and trees have been planted along that mound, it sometimes happens that a tree whose trunk appears on one side actually has its roots drawing nourishment from the other side, and vice versa. The result is that what looks like your tree is nourishing itself from my land, and what looks like my tree draws from yours.

## Tachal in Flax (II)

### Border Planting

The exception to the general permissibility of taking garden cress from among flax applies when the cress is found growing specifically on the borders between fields — *agvulei*. In that case it is prohibited to take the cress, since a person who deliberately plants cress on a field boundary has clearly cultivated it intentionally, and it therefore belongs to that owner.

## Border Mound Trees (II)

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: אִילָן הָעוֹמֵד עַל הַמֶּיצֶר, אָמַר רַב: הַנּוֹטֶה לְכָאן – לְכָאן. וְהַנּוֹטֶה לְכָאן – לְכָאן, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As** a dispute **was stated** about this issue between *amora'im*: With regard to **a tree that stands on the border** between fields, **Rav says: That** which **tilts to here,** i.e., to this field, its fruit belongs **to** the owner of the field **here, and that** which **tilts to there** belongs **to** the owner of the field **there. And Shmuel says:** The owners of the two fields **divide** all the fruit.

The *gemara* records a formal dispute: Rav says that a tree standing on the *metzar* is assigned according to which way it leans — *hanoteh le'kan le'kan ve'hanoteh le'kan le'kan* — if the tree leans toward one side, it belongs to that side. Shmuel says *cholkin* — the fruit is split between the two field owners.

### Baraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אִילָן הָעוֹמֵד עַל הַמֶּיצֶר – יַחְלוֹקוּ. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב! תַּרְגְּמָא שְׁמוּאֵל אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַב, בִּמְמַלֵּא כָּל הַמֶּיצֶר כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a tree that stands on the border** between fields, **they divide** its fruit. This is apparently **a conclusive refutation of Rav.** The Gemara responds: **Shmuel interpreted** the *baraita* so that it should be **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav** as referring to a case **where** the tree **fills the entire boundary,** and consequently it belongs to both of them.

A *meitivi* is raised: a *baraita* states explicitly that a tree standing on the *metzar* is subject to *yachloku* — the owners divide the fruit. This appears to be a *tiyuvta de'Rav*, a direct refutation of Rav's position. The *gemara* then reports: *tirgeman Shmuel aliba de'Rav* — Shmuel himself provided an interpretation of the *baraita* that would align it with Rav's view.

### Metzer Definition

Before proceeding to the resolution, the *gemara* requires clarity on what a *metzar* actually is. It is not merely an imaginary line; it is a raised mound of earth running between two adjacent fields, serving as a physical border marker. In the agricultural context of this *sugya*, an orchard has been planted along this mound — meaning the trees literally grow on the elevated strip of soil between the two properties.

This is where a significant *machloket* between Rashi and Tosafot emerges regarding what it means for a tree to stand *al ha'metzar*:

### Rashi: Roots & Fruit Spl…

Rashi understands that the *metzar* has real physical width, and the trees planted on it may have their roots extending to one side while the trunk and fruit overhang the other. The *gemara*'s case, according to Rashi, involves trees where the roots are on one side of the imaginary borderline within the mound, while the canopy and fruit are on the other. The principle *diley dilakh u'dilakh diley* describes precisely this situation: a tree whose fruit appears to be entirely on one owner's side is in fact nourished by roots drawing from the adjacent field. Rashi's illustration requires two such trees, leaning in opposite directions, to generate the mutual scenario where each owner has trees whose fruit belongs to the other.

The difficulty for Rashi is then raised: given the principle derived elsewhere in Bava Batra 27a that *shadei nufo batar ikaro* — the canopy of a tree follows its roots, meaning a tree belongs to the property where it is planted — why would a tree whose roots are on one side not simply belong entirely to that side? Rashi's answer lies in the precise configuration: the tree is *mamash al ha'metzar*, directly on the mound, with roots on one side of the border within that mound.

### Tosafot: Shared Space

Tosafot object to Rashi's approach on grounds that their version of the text does not read *al ha'metzar* but rather *al tzad ha'metzar* — beside the *metzar*, not on it. For Tosafot, the principle that a tree's ownership follows its roots renders Rashi's entire construction unnecessary and internally inconsistent. Instead, Tosafot propose that the *metzar* is a boundary line with no spatial width — it is an infinitesimally thin dividing line, not a strip of land. The roots of a tree planted on this line therefore exist in shared space, belonging to neither owner exclusively. Since the roots are in genuinely shared territory, ownership of the fruit cannot be determined by root location. The fruit then falls on one side or the other, and that is what Rav is tracking — which direction the fruit hangs. When Rav says *hanoteh le'kan le'kan*, he is acknowledging that the fruit determines apparent ownership, but the deeper legal reality is shared roots, which is why Shmuel's ruling of *yachloku* ultimately prevails.

### Reconciling Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לָא צְרִיכָא דִּתְלֵי טוּנֵיהּ לְחַד גִּיסָא. וְאַכַּתִּי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ פְּלוֹג הָכִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי חָזֵית דְּפָלְגַתְּ הָכִי – פְּלוֹג הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** this is obvious; **what** is the purpose **of** the *baraita* **stating** its ruling? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a situation **where its boughs hang** more **to one side.** The Gemara asks: **But still, what** is the purpose **of stating** it? The novelty of this *halakha* remains unclear. The Gemara explains: It is necessary, **lest you say that** the owner of the field above which the boughs hang can **say to** the owner of the other field: Let us **divide** the fruit **in this** manner, where each of us receives the fruit that grows over his land. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us that** the owner of the other field may **say to him: What did you see** to make you prefer **to divide** the fruit **in this** manner, based on bisecting the tree in one direction; **divide** the fruit instead **in that** manner, i.e., based on bisecting the tree in the other direction, so that we each receive an equal share.

The *gemara*'s phrase *tirgeman Shmuel aliba de'Rav* — Shmuel interpreted the case according to Rav — represents an attempt to show that Shmuel's rule of *yachloku* need not flatly contradict Rav's principle. There are cases in which even Rav would agree to split the fruit, and Shmuel is identifying the conditions under which that convergence occurs.

### Rav's Exception

The specific condition under which even Rav would agree to *yachloku* is described as *b'memale kol ha'metzar kulo* — when the roots fill the entire *metzar*. According to Rashi, this means the roots extend equally to both sides of the boundary within the mound. According to Tosafot, it means the roots occupy the full shared space of the boundary line, with the tree's weight and fruit hanging off to one side. In this configuration, even Rav acknowledges that the fruit cannot simply be assigned to the side it physically hangs over, because the root structure that nourishes the tree is equally shared.

The *gemara* then asks: *ei hachi, mai lemimra* — if that is the case, what is the ruling actually teaching us? Surely if the roots are equally on both sides, of course we split the fruit? The answer is: *lo tzricha d'tali t'unui l'chad gisa* — it is necessary to teach us this rule precisely when the fruit load hangs heavily to one side. One might have thought that in such a case, whoever the fruit falls on should receive it, since the physical reality favors them. The ruling teaches that even then, *yachloku*.

### Cutting Method

But *yachloku* raises a practical question: how does one actually divide the fruit equally when the mass of it hangs entirely to one side? The *gemara* addresses this. If one simply cuts the tree along the line of the *metzar* — running the cut in the direction of the boundary — the owner whose side the fruit is on would receive all or most of it, and the distribution would be unequal. Instead, the cut should be made *perpendicularly* — across the line of the *metzar*, slicing through the mass of the tree horizontally, such that each owner receives an equal portion of the fruit even though the whole canopy is displaced to one side. This is the meaning of *mah chazit d'falagat hachi, pelag hachi* — why do you think the division goes *this* way? Divide it *that* way instead. The underlying *svara* is that since the roots are equally shared, the fruit must also be divided equally, and the only way to achieve that when the canopy is asymmetric is to cut perpendicularly to the *metzar* rather than along it.

## Proximity Issues

### City Proximity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרָבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן: רָבִין אֲחִי, לָא תִּזְבֹּין אַרְעָא דִּסְמִיכָא לְמָתָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד עַל שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹמֶדֶת בְּקָמוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said to Ravin bar Rav Naḥman: Ravin my brother, do not buy land that is near a town, as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** It is **prohibited for a person to stand by another's field when its ripe grain is standing,** i.e., when its produce is ready for harvest, as he might harm the produce with the evil eye. Similarly, land near a town may be harmed by the people of the town watching it.

The *gemara* continues with the series of teachings addressed by Rav Gidal to Rav Nachman in the *Ravin achi* format: do not purchase land adjacent to a city. The concern involves issues of safety and interference — proximity to populated areas creates complications for agricultural property.

### Harvest Field Access

This connects to a ruling of Rav, transmitted through Rav Huna: it is forbidden for a person to stand in his neighbor's field at the time when the grain is standing and ready for harvest. The concern is one of intrusion at a moment of particular vulnerability for the crop.

### Deuteronomy Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְתַלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי אָמַר רַב בְּהָנֵי קְרָאֵי ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בָּעִיר וּבָרוּךְ אַתָּה בַּשָּׂדֶה״, ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּבֹאֶךָ וּבָרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּצֵאתֶךָ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But when Rabbi Abba encountered Rav's students** he **said to them: What does Rav say with regard to** the meaning of **these verses** of blessing: **"Blessed shall you be in the city, and blessed shall you be in the field"** (Deuteronomy 28:3), and: **"Blessed shall you be when you enter, and blessed shall you be when you exit"** (Deuteronomy 28:6)?

The *gemara* then records an encounter in which Rav Abba found students of Rav and asked them: how did Rav interpret the verses in Deuteronomy — *baruch ata ba'ir u'varuch ata ba'sadeh, baruch ata b'vo'echa u'varuch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed are you in the city, blessed in the field, blessed in your comings and goings?

### Rav's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בָּעִיר״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא בֵּיתְךָ סָמוּךְ לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת. ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בַּשָּׂדֶה״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ נְכָסֶיךָ קְרוֹבִים לָעִיר. ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּבֹאֶךָ״ – שֶׁלֹּא תִּמְצָא אִשְׁתְּךָ סְפֵק נִדָּה בִּשְׁעַת בִּיאָתְךָ מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ. ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּצֵאתֶךָ״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ צֶאֱצָאֵי מֵעֶיךָ כְּמוֹתְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And they said to him: This** is what **Rav said: "Blessed shall you be in the city"** means **that your house should be adjacent to a synagogue,** and the phrase: **"Blessed shall you be in the field"** means **that your property should be near the city. "Blessed shall you be when you enter"** means **that you will not find your wife** in a state where it is **uncertain** whether she has the halakhic status of a menstruating **woman when you come in from a journey,** which would render her forbidden to you. **"Blessed shall you be when you go out"** means **that those who emerge from you,** i.e., your descendants, **should be like you.**

The students reported Rav's interpretations as follows. *Baruch ata ba'ir* — blessed in the city — means that your house should be near the *beit ha'keneset*. This initially appears to contradict Rav's earlier warning against purchasing land near a city, a tension the *gemara* will resolve. *Baruch ata ba'sadeh* — blessed in the field — means that your property should be near your home. *Baruch ata b'vo'echa* — blessed in your coming — means that you should not find your wife in a state of doubtful *niddah* when you return home from travel. *Baruch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed in your going — means that your descendants should be like you.

### Alternative Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא אָמַר הָכִי, אֶלָּא: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בָּעִיר״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא סָמוּךְ לְשׁוּלְחָנְךָ, אֲבָל בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת לָא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: שְׂכַר פְּסִיעוֹת יֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Abba **said to them: Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say this, but** he interpreted the verse as follows: **"Blessed shall you be in the city,"** means **that there should be a bathroom near your table, but** he did **not** refer to **a synagogue.** The Gemara adds: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning** in this regard, **as he says: There is a reward** for the **steps** one takes to reach the location of a mitzva, and one who lives adjacent to a synagogue will not have the opportunity to earn this reward.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a competing set of interpretations. *Baruch ata ba'ir* — blessed in the city — means that your bathroom should be near your table. Rabbi Yochanan explicitly rejects the *beit ha'keneset* reading, and the *gemara* explains that this is consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's position elsewhere: *sachar pesi'ot yesh* — there is reward for each step one walks to the *beit ha'keneset*, and therefore proximity to the synagogue is actually undesirable from a spiritual-reward perspective. *Baruch ata ba'sadeh* — blessed in the field — means that your assets should be divided in thirds: a third in grain, a third in olives, and a third in grapes — the Mediterranean agricultural triad. *Baruch ata b'vo'echa u'varuch ata b'tzeitecha* — blessed in your comings and goings — means that your departure from the world should be as pure as your arrival into it, just as you entered without sin, so may you leave without sin.

### More on Rabbi Yochanan's Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בַּשָּׂדֶה״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ נְכָסֶיךָ מְשׁוּלָּשִׁין: שְׁלִישׁ בִּתְבוּאָה, שְׁלִישׁ בְּזֵיתִים, וּשְׁלִישׁ בִּגְפָנִים. ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּבֹאֶךָ וּבָרוּךְ אַתָּה בְּצֵאתֶךָ״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא יְצִיאָתְךָ מִן הָעוֹלָם כְּבִיאָתְךָ לְעוֹלָם. מָה בִּיאָתְךָ לְעוֹלָם בְּלֹא חֵטְא, אַף יְצִיאָתְךָ מִן הָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Rabbi Yoḥanan's exposition of the verses: **"Blessed shall you be in the field"** means **that your property should be** divided into **thirds: One-third** should be invested **in grain, one-third in olives, and one-third in grapevines. "Blessed shall you be when you enter and blessed shall you be when you exit"** means **that your exit from the world should be like your entry into the world: Just as your entry into the world** was **without sin, so too your exit from the world** should be **without sin.**

### Walled City Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּמְהַדַּר לֵיהּ שׁוּרָא וְרִתְקָא, הָא דְּלָא מְהַדַּר לֵיהּ שׁוּרָא וְרִתְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, it is evident that Rav approves of one whose property is located near a city. How does this accord with his statement that there is concern for the evil eye when one's field is viewed by people? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement is referring to **a wall and** an additional **partition [*ritka*] that surround** the plot and prevent it from being harmed by the evil eye. **That** statement is referring to a case **where a wall and** an additional **partition do not surround it.**

The *gemara* returns to the tension in Rav's position — how can Rav say one should live near the *beit ha'keneset* while also warning against buying land adjacent to a city? The resolution: *ha d'mehader lei shura v'ritka, ha d'lo mehader lei shura v'ritka* — the two cases are distinct. When the city has a surrounding wall and moat, the field adjacent to it carries none of the dangers that prompted Rav's warning, and proximity to the *beit ha'keneset* is indeed a blessing. When the city lacks a wall, the adjacent land is exposed to the various problems Rav cautioned against, and one should not purchase it.

## Disease Interpretations

### Rav: Evil Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהֵסִיר ה׳ מִמְּךָ כׇּל חֹלִי״, אָמַר רַב: זוֹ [הָ]עַיִן. רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ. דְּרַב סָלֵיק לְבֵי קִבְרֵי, עֲבַד מַאי דַּעֲבַד, אֲמַר: תִּשְׁעִין וְתִשְׁעָה בְּעַיִן רָעָה, וְאֶחָד בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to expounding the themes of blessings and the evil eye. The Torah states: **"And the Lord will take away from you all sickness"** (Deuteronomy 7:15). In interpreting this verse, **Rav says: This** verse is speaking about the evil **eye.** The Gemara comments: **Rav** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rav went to a graveyard,** and **did what** he **did,** i.e., he used an incantation to find out how those buried there died, and he **said: Ninety-nine** of these died **by the evil eye, and** only **one** died **by** entirely **natural means.**

The *gemara* now addresses a series of interpretations of the verse from Deuteronomy 7:15: *ve'hesir Hashem mimcha kol choli* — and God will remove from you all illness. What is the *choli* — the illness — that God promises to remove? Rav says: *zo ayin* — this is the evil eye, *ayin ha'ra*. The *gemara* notes that this is *Rav l'taameih* — consistent with Rav's general orientation on such matters. The evidence: Rav would go to the cemetery and perform certain acts — Rashi explains this involved a form of inquiry addressed to those buried there — and Rav determined that 99 out of 100 deaths were caused by the evil eye, with only one attributable to natural causes.

### Shmuel: Wind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: זֶה הָרוּחַ. שְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַכֹּל בָּרוּחַ. וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל: הָא אִיכָּא הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת! הָנָךְ נָמֵי, אִי לָאו זִיקָא, עָבְדִי לְהוּ סַמָּא וְחָיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: This** term: "All sickness," refers to **the wind.** The Gemara comments: **Shmuel** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Shmuel says: Every** injury suffered by people is due **to the wind** that enters wounds and bodily cavities. The Gemara asks: **But according to Shmuel, aren't there** those **executed by the monarchy** and others killed by traumatic injury and not the wind? The Gemara responds: With regard to **these too, were it not** for **the wind, they would prepare a medicine for** those injured people **and** they would be healed and **live,** but the wind prevents this from happening.

Shmuel says the *choli* of the verse refers to wind — *ruach*. This too is *Shmuel l'taameih*, consistent with Shmuel's broader view that *hakol be'ruach* — wind is the underlying cause of most illness. One might object: what about people killed by the Roman government — surely wind does not account for those deaths? The *gemara* responds: even in those cases, were it not for the wind aggravating the situation, a medicinal ointment — *sama* — would have allowed the body to heal and survive. Rashi understands *avdei lehu sama v'chayei* as referring to a healing compound that could have restored the body under better conditions.

### Chanina: Cold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: זוֹ צִינָּה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַכֹּל בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם, חוּץ מִצִּנִּים פַּחִים. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִנִּים פַּחִים בְּדֶרֶךְ עִקֵּשׁ שׁוֹמֵר נַפְשׁוֹ יִרְחַק מֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says: This** phrase: "All sickness," refers to **the cold, as Rabbi Ḥanina says: All** occurrences that befall man are **at the hands of Heaven, except for** excess **cold** and **heat, as it is stated: "Cold and heat are on the path of the perverse; he who guards his soul shall keep far from them"** (Proverbs 22:5). This indicates that cold and heat are forms of harm caused by man, from which one can protect himself.

Rabbi Chanina says the *choli* is *tzina* — cold. This is likewise consistent with Rabbi Chanina's position elsewhere: *hakol b'yedei Shamayim chutz mi'tzinim pachim* — everything is in the hands of Heaven except for cold drafts. Rabbi Chanina cites the verse *tzinim pachim b'derech ikesh, shomer nafsho yirchak meihem* — thorns and snares lie in the path of the crooked, one who guards his soul will keep away from them.

### Yosi: Excretions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר: זוֹ צוֹאָה. דְּאָמַר מָר: צוֹאַת הַחוֹטֶם, וְצוֹאַת הָאוֹזֶן – רוּבָּן קָשֶׁה, וּמִיעוּטָן יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: This** phrase: "All sickness," refers to **excrement, as the Master says:** With regard to **excrement of the nose,** i.e., mucous**, and excrement of the ear,** i.e., earwax, if a large amount is emitted, having **much of it** is **harmful, but** having **a bit of it** is **beneficial.**

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina says the *choli* refers to bodily excretions — *tzoah*. He connects this to a ruling that the discharge of the nose and the discharge of the ear are mostly harmful in large quantities, though small amounts may be beneficial: *ruban kashah u'miutan yafeh*.

### Elazar: Bile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: זוֹ מָרָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״מַחֲלָה״ – זוֹ מָרָה, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״מַחֲלָה״ – שֶׁהִיא מַחֲלָה כׇּל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״מַחֲלָה״, שֶׁשְּׁמוֹנִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה חֳלָאִים תְּלוּיִן בַּמָּרָה. וְכוּלָּן, פַּת שַׁחֲרִית בְּמֶלַח וְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל מַיִם מְבַטַּלְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: This** term: "All sickness," refers to **the gall bladder.** The Gemara adds: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the term: **"Sickness," this** refers to **the gall bladder. And why** is the gall bladder **called sickness?** It is **because it makes a person's entire body ill. Alternatively,** it is called **sickness because eighty-three diseases,** the numerical value of *maḥala*, sickness, **are dependent on the gall bladder.** The Gemara comments: **And** with regard to **all of them,** consuming **bread** in **the morning with salt and** drinking **a large jug of water negates their** ill effects, as a simple morning meal is beneficial to the body.

Rabbi Elazar says the *choli* is *marah* — bile, the substance produced by the gallbladder. In the ancient medical framework of the four humors, bile was held responsible for a wide range of physical ailments.

### Bile Diseases

A *baraita* is cited in support of Rabbi Elazar's view: *machalah zo marah* — the word *machalah* (illness) in the verse refers specifically to the gallbladder and its bile. Why is *marah* called *machalah* in general? Because *hi machalah kol gufo shel adam* — it afflicts the entire body of a person. A further explanation: *machalah* is used because there are 83 illnesses that depend upon *marah*, stemming from dysfunction of the bile or gallbladder. And all 83 of these illnesses are counteracted by *pat shacharit b'melach v'kiton shel mayim* — morning bread with salt and a jug of water.

## Morning Bread Benefits

### First Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר דְּבָרִים נֶאֱמְרוּ בְּפַת שַׁחֲרִית: מַצֶּלֶת מִן הַחַמָּה, וּמִן הַצִּנָּה, וּמִן הַזִּיקִין, וּמִן הַמַּזִּיקִין, וּמַחְכִּימַת פֶּתִי, וְזוֹכֶה בַּדִּין, לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה וּלְלַמֵּד, וּדְבָרָיו נִשְׁמָעִין, וְתַלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a related *baraita*: **The Sages taught** that **thirteen matters** of praise **were stated with regard to** a meal of **bread** eaten in **the morning:** It **protects** the diner **from the heat, and from the cold, and from the winds, and from the harmful spirits; and it makes the simple wise, and** one who consumes it will be **victorious in judgment,** he will merit **to learn Torah and to teach** it, **and his statements are heard, and his study will remain in his possession.**

The *baraita* continues with a formal enumeration: thirteen beneficial things are said about *pat shacharit* — eating bread in the morning. It protects from the sun (*chamah*), from cold (*tzina*), from harmful winds (*zikin*), and from demons (*mazikin*). It brings wisdom (*machkimat*), success in legal proceedings (*zocheh ba'din*), enables Torah study (*lilmod Torah*), and enables teaching Torah (*lelamed*).

### More Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ מַעֲלֶה הֶבֶל, וְנִזְקָק לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ מִתְאַוֶּה לְאִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת, וְהוֹרֶגֶת כִּינָּה שֶׁבִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִם. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף מוֹצִיאָ[ה] אֶת הַקִּנְאָה וּמַכְנֶיסֶ[ת] אֶת הָאַהֲבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, **his flesh does not generate** excess **sweat, and he engages in intercourse with his wife** at the proper time, **and he does not lust for another woman, and** this meal is so advantageous that it even **kills** any **louse in** his **intestines. And some say** it **even removes jealousy and brings in love.** Since he is completely healthy, he is not inclined to be angered by others.

The remaining benefits: one's words are heard (*devarav nishma'in*), one's learning is retained (*talmudo mitkayem b'yado*), one's body does not produce fever or excess heat (*ein besaro ma'aleh hevel*), one is drawn only to his wife and not to other women (*nizkak l'ishto v'eino mitaveh l'ishah acheret*), and the bread kills intestinal worms (*horeg kinah she'bivnei me'ayim*).

### Jealousy & Love

An additional opinion adds: *af motziah et ha'kinah u'machnisat et ha'ahavah* — morning bread also removes jealousy and brings love.

### Running Speed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה לְרָבָא בַּר מָרִי: מְנָא הָא מִילְּתָא דְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: שִׁיתִּין רָהֹיטֵי רְהוּט וְלָא מְטוֹ לְגַבְרָא דְּמִצַּפְרָא כְּרַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** In relation to the above *baraita*, **Rabba said to Rava bar Mari: From where is this matter that people say** derived: **Sixty runners ran but could not catch the man who ate in the morning,** and the Sages likewise **said: Arise early and eat, in the summer due to the sun and in the winter due to the cold,** so that one's body should have the strength to withstand the climate.

Rabbah cites to Rava bar Mari a popular saying: sixty runners cannot catch up to a man who has eaten his morning bread. The idiom expresses the vigor and alertness that *pat shacharit* confers.

### Seasonal Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשְׁכֵּם וֶאֱכוֹל, בְּקַיִץ מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה וּבַחוֹרֶף מִפְּנֵי הַצִּינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The rabbinic instruction drawn from this is: **Rise early and eat, in the summer due to the sun and in the winter due to the cold,** when one's body needs the reinforcement to sustain itself against extreme temperatures.

The rabbinic instruction drawn from this is: *hashkem ve'echol bakayitz mipnei ha'chamah u'vahoref mipnei ha'tzina* — rise early and eat in the summer because of the heat, and in the winter because of the cold. Morning bread protects against both extremes.

### Isaiah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִרְעָבוּ וְלֹא יִצְמָאוּ וְלֹא יַכֵּם שָׁרָב וָשָׁמֶשׁ״, לֹא יַכֵּם שָׁרָב וָשָׁמֶשׁ – כֵּיוָן דְּלָא יִרְעָבוּ וְלֹא יִצְמָאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava bar Mari **said to him:** It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "They shall not hunger nor thirst, neither shall the heat nor sun smite them"** (Isaiah 49:10). Why will **the heat and the sun not smite them? Since they shall not hunger nor thirst,** as they rose early to eat.

When Rava bar Mari asks for a scriptural basis, Rabbah cites Isaiah 49:10: *lo yir'avu velo yitzma'u velo yakeim sharav va'shamesh* — they shall not hunger nor thirst, nor shall heat or sun strike them. The *gemara* explains the verse as a chain: the protection from heat and sun is a consequence of not being hungry or thirsty, and not being hungry or thirsty results from eating morning bread — the elements of the verse are mutually dependent.

### Deuteronomy Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לִי מֵהָתָם, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא לָךְ מֵהָכָא: ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם אֵת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם וּבֵרַךְ אֶת לַחְמְךָ וְאֶת מֵימֶיךָ״. ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם אֵת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ – זוֹ קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּתְפִלָּה. ״וּבֵרַךְ אֶת לַחְמְךָ וְאֶת מֵימֶיךָ״ – זוֹ פַּת בַּמֶּלַח וְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל מַיִם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ: ״וַהֲסִרֹתִי מַחֲלָה מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You said to me** that it is derived **from there, but I say to you** that it is derived **from here,** a different verse: **"And you shall serve the Lord your God, and He will bless your bread and your water"** (Exodus 23:25), which he interprets as follows: **"And you shall serve the Lord your God," this** refers to the **recitation of *Shema* and** the *Amida* **prayer,** both of which constitute daily service of God. **"And He will bless your bread and your water," this** refers to **bread with salt and a large jug of water** consumed after morning prayers. **From that** point **onward,** the rest of the verse: **"And I will take sickness away from your midst,"** will be fulfilled.

Rava offers a superior scriptural basis from Deuteronomy: *va'avadtem et Hashem Elokeichem u'verach et lachmecha v'et meimecha* — and you shall serve the Lord your God, and He will bless your bread and your water. The *gemara* interprets: *va'avadtem et Hashem Elokeichem* refers to *keriyat shema* and *tefillah*. *U'verach et lachmecha v'et meimecha* refers to bread dipped in salt and a jug of water. The verse then continues: *va'hasiroti machalah mi'kirbecha* — and I will remove illness from among you. The sequence is complete: prayer, then morning bread with salt and water, and God will remove all illness.

### Bread & Salt

The prescription is thus: *pat be'melach v'kiton shel mayim* — bread dipped in salt accompanied by a jug of water, eaten in the morning after *tefillah*. This practice is attested in later *siddur* traditions as well; the Tunisian liturgical tradition, for instance, includes after *shacharit* a passage on *pat shacharit* explicitly citing this *gemara*.

## Land Surveying Rules

### Survey Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב אַדָּא מָשׁוֹחָאָה: לָא תְּזַלְזֵל בְּמִשְׁחֲתָא, דְּכֹל פּוּרְתָּא וּפוּרְתָּא חֲזִי לְכוּרְכְּמָא רִישְׁקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב אַדָּא מָשׁוֹחָאָה: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דְּאַנִּיגְרָא – זַלְזֵל בְּהוּ. דְּאַנַּהֲרָא לָא תִּמְשְׁחִנְהוּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said to Rav Adda the surveyor**: **Do not treat measuring lightly** even for small areas of land, **as each little bit** is **suitable for** growing the **cultivated [*rishka*] saffron,** a very expensive product. **Rav Yehuda** further **said to Rav Adda the surveyor:** With regard to the **four cubits** measured adjacent to an irrigation **channel,** you **may treat them lightly,** and it is not necessary to provide an exact measurement of them when calculating the areas of fields. And as for the four cubits adjacent **to a river,** do **not measure them at all,** but simply estimate the size and include them in the larger measurement.

The *gemara* continues with practical land law. Rav Yehuda instructs Rav Adda the surveyor: *lo tizalzel be'mishchata* — do not be careless in your measurements. Every small piece of land matters, because *kol purta u'purta chazi l'churkama de'rishka* — even a tiny parcel could grow saffron, which was among the most expensive commodities of the ancient world.

### Irrigation Zones

However, Rav Yehuda qualifies: the four *amot* around an irrigation channel — *anigra* — may be measured with somewhat less stringency. Rashi explains that this zone within the field actually belongs collectively to the owners of the surrounding parcels, and since they are co-owners of that strip they can effectively waive precise measurement; the matter of a few inches one way or another does not create a wrongful taking.

### River Banks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דְּאַנִּיגְרָא – לִבְנֵי אנִיגְרָא. דְּאַנַּהֲרָא – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: In this regard, **Rav Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rav Yehuda says:** The **four cubits** adjacent **to a channel** belong **to the residents of** the houses alongside **the channel,** while the four cubits adjacent **to a river** belong **to everyone.**

Regarding rivers, the rule is different: *de'nahara lo timshach klal* — do not measure the river banks at all. Rav Yehuda states the underlying principle: the four *amot* around the irrigation channel belong to the private co-owners of that field system, but the banks of a public river belong to the entire public — *de'nahara de'chulei alma* — and therefore their precise dimensions cannot be treated as anyone's private property to be measured away.

### Barge Path Clearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרִיז רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְלֵא כַּתְפֵי נַגָּדֵי בִּתְרֵי עֶבְרֵי נַהֲרָא – קוּצוּ. רַב נָתָן בַּר הוֹשַׁעְיָא קַץ שִׁיתְּסַר אַמְּתָא. אֲתוֹ עֲלֵיהּ בְּנֵי מַשְׁרוֹנְיָא, דַּפְנוּהּ. הוּא סָבַר כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְלָא הִיא: הָתָם בָּעֵינַן כּוּלֵּי הַאי, הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אַמְתּוֹחֵי (אַשְׁלֵיהֶן) [אַשְׁלַיְהוּ] הוּא, כִּמְלֵא כַּתְפֵי נַגָּדֵי סַגִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami** would **announce: Cut down** the trees along the width of the **full shoulders** of **the pullers** of the boat **on both sides** of **the river** so that the trees should not interfere with the pulling of the boats. The Gemara relates: **Rav Natan bar Hoshaya** instructed people to **cut down sixteen cubits** on each side of the river, and **the residents of Mashronya came upon him and beat him** for issuing this directive. The Gemara explains: **He holds** that any pathway must be made as wide as **like a public domain,** which is sixteen cubits wide. **But** that **is not so,** as **there,** in the case of a public domain, **we** do **require that much** space; **here,** however, the space **is** necessary needs to be only enough **to** enable **the stretching of the ropes** to pull the boats. Therefore, the width of the **full shoulders of the pullers is** the **sufficient** measure of space needed in order not to interfere with the pulling of the boats.

Rabbi Ami made a public announcement: vegetation on both sides of a river must be cleared to the width needed for barge-pullers (*nagdei*) to walk while pulling their ropes — *melo kitfei nagdei bi'trei evrei nahara kutzzu*. Barge-pullers needed to walk along the riverbank while towing vessels with long ropes stretched out behind them, and the cleared path had to be wide enough for that purpose.

### Excessive Clearance

Rav Natan bar Sharya took this ruling and cleared sixteen *amot* on each side. The residents of Mashrunya came and struck him for it. He had reasoned that river banks constitute a *reshut ha'rabbim* — public domain — and the standard width of a *reshut ha'rabbim* is sixteen *amot*.

### Public Domain Distinction

The *gemara* rejects his reasoning: *velo hi* — that is incorrect. In a standard public thoroughfare, sixteen *amot* are required because people need that width to move through freely. On a riverbank, the requirement is functional rather than fixed: *mishum amtuchei ashleihen hu* — the purpose is for barge-pullers to stretch out their towing ropes, and *melo kitfei nagdei sagi* — the width of the barge-pullers' shoulders is sufficient. The principle is that even within the category of public space, different functional needs generate different physical requirements.

### Neighbor Coordination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא אִבָּא אַגּוּדָּא דְנַהֲרָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נִיקּוֹץ מָר! אֲמַר לְהוּ: קוּצוּ עִילָּאֵי וְתַתָּאֵי, וַהֲדַר נִיקּוֹץ אֲנָא. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״הִתְקוֹשְׁשׁוּ וָקוֹשּׁוּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קְשׁוֹט עַצְמְךָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ קְשׁוֹט אֲחֵרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Rabba bar Rav Huna had a certain forest on the bank of a river. They said to him: Let the Master cut down** the trees on the riverbank in accordance with the above statement. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to them:** Let **those above and below** me along the river **cut down** their trees first, **and then I will cut down** my trees. I will achieve nothing by cutting down my trees on my own. The Gemara asks: **How can he do so,** i.e., wait for others to act? **But isn't it written: "Gather yourselves together, and gather [*hitkosheshu vakoshu*]"** (Zephaniah 2:1), **and Reish Lakish says** concerning this: **Adorn [*keshot*] yourself and afterward adorn others.** Therefore, one must first perform the required action himself before offering advice to others.

Rabba bar Rav Huna owned a forest on the side of a river. When told he must clear his portion, he responded: *kutzzu ila'i ve'tata'i va'hadar nikutz ana* — let those above and below me on the river clear their portions first, and then I will clear mine.

### Self-First Principle

This was challenged on the basis of Resh Lakish's principle: *hitkosheshu va'koshu* — the verse says gather yourselves and gather, interpreted as *kshot atzmecha ve'achar kach kshot acherim* — adorn yourself first and then attend to others. One is obligated to fulfill one's own duty without waiting for others to fulfill theirs. If clearing the riverbank is required, Rabba bar Rav Huna cannot condition his compliance on his neighbors' prior compliance.

### Practical Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אִבָּא דְּבֵי פַרְזַק רוּפִילָא הֲוָה, וְאָמַר: אִי קָיְיצוּ – קָיֵיצְנָא, וְאִי לָא קָיְיצוּ – אַמַּאי אִיקּוֹץ. דְּאִי מִמַּתְחִי לְהוּ – אַשְׁלַיְיהוּ מִסְתַּגִּי לְהוּ, וְאִי לָא – לָא מִיסְתַּגֵּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **There** it **was the forest of the house of Parzak, the general, and** it was obvious that they would pay no attention to a Jewish scholar. Rabba bar Rav Huna therefore **said: If** the workers of the Persian officer **cut down, I will cut down** as well, **and if** they do **not cut down, why should I cut down** for no purpose? **Since if** the pullers can **stretch their ropes they can go** along this side of the river, **and if not, they will be unable to walk,** but will have to cross over to the other side of the river. Therefore, no advantage exists to cutting down the trees that block part of the river.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *aba de'vei Parzak Rofila hava* — the land adjacent to Rabba bar Rav Huna's forest belonged to a man named Parzak, who was a military general. Parzak had made clear that he would clear his side only if Rabba bar Rav Huna cleared his first — but in reality, Parzak was not going to do it regardless. In this specific circumstance, Rabba bar Rav Huna's clearing alone would have been pointless: *de'ei mamtu lehu ashleihu masgei lehu* — if there is enough space on the other side for the barge-pullers to stretch their ropes, the path will function. *Ve'ei lo, lo masgei lehu* — and if there is not, the barge-pullers will remain on whichever side of the river has a workable path and will not cross to Rabba bar Rav Huna's side simply because he cleared a portion. Clearing his section in isolation would accomplish nothing for the public benefit the rule is meant to serve — and therefore it was reasonable to withhold compliance under those particular conditions.

This brings the *daf* to 108a.
