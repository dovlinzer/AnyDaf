# Menachot 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Smicha vs. Tenufa

### Smicha Weightier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בַּסְּמִיכָה מִבַּתְּנוּפָה, וּבַתְּנוּפָה מִבַּסְּמִיכָה – שֶׁאֶחָד מֵנִיף לְכׇל הַחֲבֵרִים, וְאֵין אֶחָד סוֹמֵךְ לְכׇל הַחֲבֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** There is an aspect of greater **stringency with regard to placing hands than** there is **with regard to waving, and** there is an aspect of greater stringency **with regard to waving than** there is **with regard to placing hands.** The stringency with regard to placing hands is **that** if several people are partners in bringing an offering, **one** of them **waves** the offering **on behalf of all the** other **partners, but one cannot** fulfill the requirement of placing hands if he alone **places hands on behalf of all the** other **partners;** rather, each member must place hands himself.

The *daf* opens with a *mishnah* that systematically compares *smicha* — the pressing of hands upon a sacrifice — with *tenufa*, the ritual waving performed with certain offerings. The *mishnah* identifies ways in which each practice is more demanding than the other, and the *gemara* then unpacks the textual and logical foundations for these distinctions.

### Individual Duty


The first and central way in which *smicha* is weightier is stated in the *mishnah* itself: *shehayachid manif l'chol chaverav v'ein echad somech l'chol chaverav* — one person waves on behalf of all his partners, but no single person performs *smicha* on behalf of all his partners. When a *chabura*, a group of partners, brings a *korban* jointly, only one member of the group performs *tenufa*, whereas every individual member of the group must perform *smicha* personally. As the *gemara* will explain, the physical constraints of *tenufa* make it impossible for all partners to wave simultaneously or sequentially. *smicha*, by contrast, can be done sequentially, and each person is required to do so. This reflects the underlying character of *smicha* as an expression of personal ownership and connection to the sacrifice — an act that demands each individual's direct participation. From the *mishnah*'s framing, this universal individual requirement is presented as a stringency: *smicha* imposes a greater demand precisely because it cannot be delegated.

### Tenufa Weightier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בַּתְּנוּפָה – שֶׁהַתְּנוּפָה נוֹהֶגֶת בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹת הַיָּחִיד וּבְקׇרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּבּוּר, בְּחַיִּים וּבִשְׁחוּטִין, וּבְדָבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים וּבְדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּסְּמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The stringency with regard to waving** is **that waving is practiced in** the cases of both **offerings of an individual,** e.g., peace offerings, where the breast and thigh and sacrificial portions are waved, **and in** the cases of **communal offerings,** e.g., the two lambs sacrificed on *Shavuot*, which are waved together with the two loaves; and it is practiced both **in** the cases of offerings when they are **alive,** e.g., the guilt offering of a leper and the lambs of *Shavuot*, **and in** the cases of offerings after they are **slaughtered,** e.g., the breast and thigh. By contrast, placing hands is practiced with a live animal. A further stringency is that waving is practiced both **in** the case of **an item in which there is a living spirit,** i.e., an animal offering, **and in** the case of **an item in which there is not a living spirit,** e.g., the *omer* offering, the *sota* meal offering, and the loaves accompanying a thanks offering and the ram of the nazirite, whereas placing hands is only ever performed upon living beings.

The *mishnah* then turns to identify the ways in which *tenufa* is weightier than *smicha*.

### Tenufa Scope


*Tenufa* has a far broader scope of application. The *mishnah* states: *shetnufa noheget b'korbanos hayachid u'v'korbanos hatzibbur* — *tenufa* applies both to individual offerings and to communal offerings. Among individual offerings, one performs *tenufa* with the breast and thigh of a *shelamim*. Among communal offerings, *tenufa* is performed in the context of the *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* that accompany the *shtei halechem* on *shavuot*. Furthermore, *tenufa* is performed both with living animals — as with the lambs brought on *shavuot*, which are waved alive — and with slaughtered animals — as with the breast and thigh of every *shelamim* after *shechita*.

### Animate/Inanimate


Beyond the distinction between living and slaughtered animals, *tenufa* extends even to objects that have no life at all: *u'v'davar she'ein bo ruach chayim* — with things that possess no living spirit. This includes the breads that accompany the lambs of *shavuot*, the loaves of the *todah* offering, and, as Rashi notes, even the *nesachim*, the libation offerings that accompany the *korban*. *smicha*, by contrast, is performed only upon a living animal; it does not apply to non-living objects, nor to animals after *shechita*.

## Textual Sources

### Baraita Smicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קׇרְבָּנוֹ – לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קׇרְבָּן לִסְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to placing hands: "And he shall place his hand on the head of his offering" (Leviticus 3:2). The term **"his offering"** serves **to include all** of the **owners of an offering** in the requirement **of placing hands,** i.e., each one must perform it.

The *gemara* now proceeds to examine the textual basis for these principles, beginning with a *baraita* that addresses the rule requiring every individual owner to perform *smicha*. The *baraita* cites the verse *korbano* — "his offering" — with its emphasis on personal possession, to derive that every co-owner of a *korban* must personally perform *smicha*. The language of individual ownership, *korbano*, demands an individual act.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה תְּנוּפָה שֶׁנִּתְרַבְּתָה בִּשְׁחוּטִין נִתְמַעֲטָה בְּחוֹבְרִין, סְמִיכָה שֶׁלֹּא נִתְרַבְּתָה בִּשְׁחוּטִין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּתְמַעֵט בְּחוֹבְרִין?
>
> **Translation:** It is necessary for the verse to teach this, **as** one **might** have thought: **Could it not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference** that only one partner needs to place his hands on the offering? The inference is as follows: **If** the requirement of **waving, which was amplified to apply** also **to slaughtered** animals, nevertheless **was limited with regard to** an offering jointly owned by a number of **partners,** as only one of them waves on behalf of all of them, then with regard to the requirement of **placing hands, which was not amplified to apply** also **to slaughtered** animals, **is it not logical that it was** also **limited with regard to** an offering jointly owned by **partners,** and it is sufficient for one partner to place his hands on behalf of the others?

But the *baraita* does not simply assert this conclusion — it first confronts a seemingly compelling argument in the opposite direction. One might have thought, the *baraita* reasons, that a *kal v'chomer* should lead us to the reverse conclusion. *tenufa*, which is weightier and broader in scope — applying even to *shchutim*, slaughtered animals — and yet is nonetheless restricted when it comes to a *chabura*, requiring only one person to wave on behalf of all. If *tenufa*, with its greater scope, demands only one representative from the group, then *smicha*, which is narrower and less broadly applied, should surely require no more — and perhaps even less — of each participant. By this logic, one person's *smicha* should suffice for the entire group.

This argument has genuine force. There is something counterintuitive about saying that the narrower *mitzvah* imposes a greater individual burden. Yet the *kal v'chomer* itself is what the *baraita* is setting aside, and the *mishnah*'s framing — treating universal individual participation as a *stringency* of *smicha* — reflects exactly this tension.

### Korbano Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״, לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קׇרְבָּן לִסְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this inference, **the verse states: "His offering," to include each of the owners of an offering in** the requirement of **placing hands.**

The *baraita* concludes that the *kal v'chomer* is overridden by the explicit verse: *talmud lomar korbano* — the Torah's insistence on "his offering" teaches that every individual who is a co-owner of the *korban* must personally perform *smicha*. *l'rabot kol ba'alei korban l'smicha* — to include all owners of the offering in the obligation of *smicha*.

### Reverse Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִתְרַבֶּה תְּנוּפָה בְּחוֹבְרִין מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה סְמִיכָה שֶׁלֹּא נִתְרַבְּתָה בִּשְׁחוּטִין, נִתְרַבְּתָה בְּחוֹבְרִין, תְּנוּפָה שֶׁנִּתְרַבְּתָה בִּשְׁחוּטִין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּתְרַבְּתָה בְּחוֹבְרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one could suggest the opposite inference and conclude that the requirement of **waving should be amplified with regard to partners, through** the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **If** the requirement of **placing hands, which was not amplified to apply** also **to slaughtered** animals, **was amplified with regard to** an offering jointly owned by **partners,** requiring each partner to perform it himself, then with regard to the requirement of **waving, which was amplified to apply** also **to slaughtered** animals, **is it not logical that it was** also **amplified with regard to** an offering jointly owned by **partners,** requiring each partner to perform it himself?

Having established that every partner must perform *smicha*, the *baraita* then asks: can the same reasoning be extended to *tenufa*? If *smicha* — the narrower practice — requires every individual, should not *tenufa* — the broader and weightier practice — equally or even more so require every individual? The *kal v'chomer* now runs in the opposite direction: *u'ma smicha she-lo nitrabta b'shechutin*, *smicha*, which does not extend to slaughtered animals and is thus more limited in scope, nonetheless requires every individual. *tenufa*, which does extend to *shchutim* and is broader, should certainly require each individual as well.

### Chatzitza Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, הֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לַינְפּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי – קָא הָוְיָא חֲצִיצָה, לָינֵיף וְלֶיהְדַּר וְלָינֵיף – ״תְּנוּפָה״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא תְּנוּפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: This inference cannot be correct, **because** it is obvious that only one of the partners needs to perform the waving. **It is not possible** to have all of them perform it, **as how would it be done?** If one says: **Let all of** the partners **wave together,** with each one placing his hands under those of another, that is difficult: There **would be** an invalidating **interposition** between the offering and hands of the partners who are not directly holding onto the offering. And if one says: **Let** one partner **wave, and then** the next one **will wave,** and so on, that would also be invalid, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah that one must perform **a waving,** using a singular noun, which indicates that one waving, **but not** multiple **wavings,** should be performed.

The *gemara*'s answer is striking: there is no textual rebuttal. The reason all partners do not perform *tenufa* is simply *mishum d'lo efshar* — because it is physically impossible. If all were to wave simultaneously, *havi chatzitza* — the hands of one person would interpose between another person's hand and the object being waved. *tenufa* requires direct contact with the object, and with multiple hands, that contact cannot be maintained for every participant. The possibility of sequential waving, on the model of *smicha*, is rejected for a different reason.

### Singular Form


The exclusion of sequential *tenufa* rests on a linguistic observation: *tenufa Rachmana v'lo tenufot* — the Torah uses the singular abstract noun *tenufa*, "a waving," not the plural. This indicates that only a single *tenufa* may be performed. The verb used in connection with *smicha*, by contrast, carries no such singular restriction — it is expressed as an action rather than a counted noun — and so multiple sequential acts of *smicha* are perfectly permissible.

## Smicha Shechutim

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּסְמִיכָה בִּשְׁחוּטִין לֵיתַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that placing hands is not performed upon a slaughtered offering. The Gemara questions this: **And is there no** instance of **placing hands** performed **on slaughtered** animals?

The *gemara* now challenges the principle that *smicha* does not apply to animals after *shechita*. The challenge comes in the form of a question: *u'smicha b'shechutin leita?* — is it truly the case that *smicha* does not apply to slaughtered animals?

### Kohen Gadol Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל רוֹצֶה לְהַקְטִיר, הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ, וְהַסְּגָן בִּימִינוֹ. הִגִּיעַ לְמַחֲצִית הַכֶּבֶשׁ – אָחַז סְגָן בִּימִינוֹ וְהֶעֱלָהוּ, וְהוֹשִׁיט לוֹ הָרִאשׁוֹן הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל, סוֹמֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶם וְזוֹרְקָן.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Tamid* 33b): **When the High Priest** would **want to sacrifice** the daily offering, as it is his right to be the one to sacrifice it whenever he wishes to, **he would ascend the ramp** to the top of the altar, **and the deputy [*segan*]** High Priest would also ascend **to the right** of the High Priest. If it occurred that the High Priest **reached halfway** up **the ramp** and grew tired, the **deputy** would **hold** him **by his right hand** to assist him **and** would **bring him up** to the top of the altar. **And the first** of the group of priests who had been selected to bring the limbs of the daily offering to the altar would **hold out the head and the** right hind **leg** of the offering to the High Priest, **who would place** his **hands upon them, and** then **he would throw** the limbs onto the fire of the altar.

The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* as a potential refutation. The *mishnah* describes the procedure followed when a *kohen gadol* wished to perform the *avoda* of burning the limbs of an *olah* upon the *mizbe'ach*. The *kohen gadol* always retained the prerogative to participate in the *avoda* if he so chose, and the *mishnah* describes the ceremonial procedure that was arranged to honor him in this role.

### Relay Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹשִׁיט הַשֵּׁנִי לָרִאשׁוֹן שְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, נוֹתְנוֹ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, סוֹמֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶם וְזוֹרְקָן. נִשְׁמַט הַשֵּׁנִי וְהָלַךְ לוֹ, וְכָךְ הָיוּ מוֹשִׁיטִין לוֹ שְׁאָר כׇּל הָאֵבָרִים, סוֹמֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶם וְזוֹרְקָן.
>
> **Translation:** Then **the second** priest **would hold out the two forelegs to the first** priest, and the first priest would **give them to the High Priest,** who would **place** his **hands upon them and** then **throw them** onto the fire. At this stage **the second priest** would **slip away and leave,** as he was no longer needed. The first priest remained where he was, as he was still needed to present the other limbs of the offering to the High Priest. **And in this manner** the other priests who had been selected **would hold out the rest of all the limbs** to the first priest, who would present them to the High Priest, who would then **place** his **hands upon them and throw them** onto the fire.

When the *kohen gadol* ascended the *kevesh*, the ramp leading to the *mizbe'ach*, the *segan* — the deputy *kohen gadol* — would stand at his right hand. Upon reaching the midpoint of the ramp, the *segan* would grasp the *kohen gadol*'s right hand and help him to the top, suggesting that the *kohen gadol* required assistance and that the *segan* lent him support in the manner of an honor guard.

Once at the top, an elaborate relay would unfold. The first *kohen* in the sequence would extend to the *kohen gadol* the *rosh veharegel* — the head and foot of the *olah*. The *kohen gadol* would then perform *smicha* upon them and throw them onto the fire.

The second *kohen* in the sequence would then hand his portions — the two forelegs — to the first *kohen*, who in turn would pass them to the *kohen gadol*. The *kohen gadol* would again perform *smicha* and throw them onto the *mizbe'ach*. The second *kohen* would then withdraw, and the process would repeat: a third *kohen* would approach, pass his portions through the same chain, and the *kohen gadol* would perform *smicha* and throw them on. If the *kohen gadol* wished, he could perform the *smicha* himself while allowing others to do the throwing; the procedure was flexible in that regard. The result was a grand ceremonial production, with multiple *kohanim* participating in an elaborate chain, all to give honor to the *kohen gadol* in his role at the altar.

### Smicha & Throwing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה – הוּא סוֹמֵךְ, וַאֲחֵרִים זוֹרְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes: **And when** the High Priest **wants, he** may merely **place** his **hands** upon the limbs, **and** then the **other** priests **throw** the limbs onto the fire of the altar.

The apparent difficulty is clear: the *kohen gadol* was performing *smicha* on portions of a slaughtered animal — *shechutim* — which the principle under discussion holds to be inapplicable.

### Honor Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** This mishna apparently demonstrates an instance of placing hands performed upon a slaughtered animal. In resolution of this difficulty, **Abaye said:** In the mishna **there,** the placing of hands is not in fulfillment of the requirement to do so to an offering; rather, it is done merely **due to the eminence of the High Priest,** so that his sacrifice of the limbs of an offering is more distinguished than when performed by ordinary priests.

Abaye resolves the difficulty directly: *amar Abaye hatam mishum kevodo dekohen gadol* — what is described there is done purely for the honor of the *kohen gadol*. This *smicha* carries no *halachic* significance; it is not a fulfillment of any obligation, but rather a ceremonial gesture whose entire purpose is the dignity and splendor of the *kohen gadol*'s participation. The principle that *smicha* does not apply to slaughtered animals therefore remains intact.

## Ben Sira Account

### Emergence Glory

Before the *gemara* moves to the next *perek*, a remarkable digression is warranted. The procedure described in the *mishnah* — the *kohen gadol* ascending the ramp, receiving portions from his *kohen* brethren in a grand relay, and offering them on the *mizbe'ach* — is not only preserved in Rabbinic literature. It finds an ancient parallel in the Wisdom of Ben Sira, a work composed in the second century before the Common Era, whose fiftieth chapter contains a celebrated description of the *kohen gadol* Shimon ben Yochanan in all his glory.

The passage opens: *mah nehedar behashkifo me'ohel uvetzeito mibeit haparochet* — how glorious was he when he visited the Tent and when he emerged from the *kodesh hakodashim*. Ben Sira then lavishes a series of luminous comparisons upon the *kohen gadol*: like a star of light among clouds, like a full moon at the season of the festival, like the sun blazing upon the sanctuary of a king, like a rainbow seen in clouds, like a flowering branch in days of appointed times, like a lily upon flowing waters, like a flower of Lebanon in days of summer, like fire of frankincense upon the meal offering. This sequence will sound immediately familiar: the *pizmon* sung on *yom kippur*, *mah nehedar mareh Kohen Gadol betzeito mibeit Kodesh Hakodashim*, appears to draw its inspiration ultimately from this very passage of Ben Sira, making that liturgical poem far more ancient than one might otherwise suppose.

### Receiving Portions


Ben Sira continues with a description that maps directly onto what the *mishnah* records. The text states: *be'aloto al mizbeach hod veyehedar ezrat mikdash bekabelo netachim miyad achiv* — when he ascended upon the glorious *mizbe'ach* and honored the courtyard of the sanctuary, receiving the portions from the hands of his brothers, the other *kohanim*. This is the identical relay procedure described in the *mishnah*: the *kohen gadol* at the top of the ramp, receiving the *netachim*, the limbs of the *olah*, passed to him by his priestly colleagues.

### Priestly Splendor


Ben Sira then describes the surrounding scene: *saviv lo ateret banim keshtilei arazim balevanon veyakifuhu kearvei nachal kol benei Aharon bichvodam* — surrounding him was a crown of sons, like saplings of cedars of Lebanon; all the sons of Aharon in their glory encircled him. *ve'ishei hashem beyadam* — the fire offerings of the Lord were in their hands, *neged kol kehal Yisrael ad kalot shert hamizbeach lesader maarachot elyon* — before all the congregation of Israel, until he finished his service at the altar, arranging the firewood piles on high. The entire tableau — the *kohen gadol* at the center, ringed by the *kohanim* in full ceremonial array, the fire offerings passing through their hands toward the altar — is preserved with striking fidelity in this ancient text.

### Fire vs. Blood


This passage invites a broader theological observation. Throughout *masechet zevachim*, the halachic focus of animal sacrifice falls overwhelmingly on the blood: *ki hadam al hamizbe'ach hu yechaper*, for the blood upon the altar effects atonement. The burning of the limbs upon the altar, the *olah* ascending in flame, has seemed throughout that discussion to be secondary — almost incidental — to the central atonement function of the blood. In *menachot*, the burning of the *ketoret* and the *kometz* assumes greater prominence, but even there the paradigm differs. Yet here, in both the *mishnah*'s description of the *kohen gadol*'s *avoda* and in Ben Sira's account, it is the act of placing the *netachim* upon the fire that receives all the attention and ceremonial elaboration. The *torah* itself devotes substantial language to the *ishei rei'ach nicho'ach*, the fire offerings of pleasing aroma, and to the act of raising and burning the parts of the sacrifice upon the altar. What emerges from the *mishnah* and from Ben Sira is that the offering of the limbs to the fire carried its own distinct weight of sanctity and splendor in the lived experience of the *beit hamikdash* — one that stands alongside, and does not simply reduce to, the atoning function of the blood.

## Shtei Halechem

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם נִילּוֹשׁוֹת אַחַת אַחַת, וְנֶאֱפוֹת אַחַת אַחַת. לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים נִילּוֹשׁוֹת אַחַת אַחַת, וְנֶאֱפוֹת שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם. וּבִדְפוּס הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָן; כְּשֶׁהוּא רוֹדָן, נוֹתְנָן לִדְפוּס כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִתְקַלְקְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The two loaves** that are brought on the festival of *Shavuot* from the new wheat are each made from a tenth of an ephah of fine flour. They **are kneaded one** by **one and they are baked one** by **one,** i.e., each loaf is placed separately in the oven. **The** loaves of the **shewbread are kneaded one** by **one and baked two** by **two,** i.e., two loaves are placed in the oven at the same time. **And** the baker **would prepare** the shewbread **in a mold [*defus*]** when he made the dough. **When he removes** the shewbread from the oven **he** again **places** the loaves **in a mold so that their** shape **will not be ruined.**

With the conclusion of *perek shtei middot* — *hadran alach shtei midot* — the *gemara* opens the next *perek* and returns, at least briefly, to the subject of *menachot* proper. The *mishnah* states: *shtei halechem niloshot achat achat vene'afot achat achat* — the two loaves of *shavuot* are kneaded individually and baked individually.

### Lechem Hapanim


By contrast, the *lechem hapanim* — the twelve showbreads — follows a different rule: *niloshot achat achat vene'afot shtayim shtayim* — each loaf is kneaded individually, but they are baked in pairs, two at a time.

### Mold Stages


Because the *lechem hapanim* was required to assume a precise and elaborate shape, molds were essential to its production at every stage. The *mishnah* records that three separate molds were used. First, while the dough was still raw, it was placed in a mold to give it the proper form. Second, when transferred to the oven, a separate mold was used during baking so that the shape would be maintained throughout the firing process — presumably because a different material was required for oven use. Third, upon removal from the oven, the loaf was placed into yet another mold to prevent it from crumbling or losing its form as it cooled: *kedei shelo yitkalkel*, so that it should not be ruined. The experience of bakers who work with shaped breads or molded cakes will be familiar with the importance of each of these stages in preserving the integrity of the final form.

## Textual Basis

### Isaron Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרֹנִים יִהְיֶה הַחַלָּה הָאֶחָת״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּילּוֹשׁוֹת אַחַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the two loaves are kneaded one by one and baked one by one. The loaves of the shewbread are also kneaded one by one but are baked two at a time. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And you shall take fine flour, and bake twelve cakes from it; two-tenths of an ephah shall be in one cake. And you shall set them in two arrangements, six in an arrangement, upon the pure Table before the Lord" (Leviticus 24:5–6). The phrase **"Two-tenths of an ephah shall be in one cake" teaches that** the loaves of the shewbread **are kneaded one** by **one.**

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei?* — what is the textual basis for these rules? A *baraita* is cited that derives each principle from specific verses. The starting point is the verse pertaining to the *lechem hapanim*: *shnei esronim yihye hachala ha'achat* — two *esronim* shall be for each single loaf. Each of the twelve loaves of *lechem hapanim* thus contained two *esronim* of flour — a notably large quantity, given that a standard *mincha* consists of one *isaron*, and that the loaves of the *todah* offering, though individually smaller, comprise one *isaron* distributed across ten loaves.

### Individual Kneading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁאַף שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם כָּךְ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived **that this** is **also** the *halakha* with regard to **the two loaves,** i.e., that they are kneaded one at a time? **The verse states: "Shall be,"** to include the two loaves.

From the phrase *hachala ha'achat* — "the one loaf" — the *baraita* derives that each loaf is treated as an individual unit: *melamed shenilosha achat achat*, this teaches that they are kneaded one by one. The singular emphasis in the verse — "the one loaf" — establishes individual processing at the kneading stage. The extension of this rule to the *shtei halechem* is derived from the superfluous word *yihye* in the same verse.

### Paired Baking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאֲפִיָּיתָן שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְשַׂמְתָּ אוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that the baking** of the loaves of the shewbread is performed **two** by **two? The verse states: "And you shall set them [*vesamta otam*],"** the plural form indicating that two loaves should be baked together.

The rule that the *lechem hapanim* are baked in pairs is derived from a different verse: *vesamta otam* — you shall place them. The word *otam*, "them," is interpreted as referring to a placement in the oven — as Rashi explains, *sima rishona she'ata ose mehen dehainu batanur*, "the first placing you do with them, which is in the oven." The word *otam* carries a minimum plural value of two, establishing that two loaves are placed in the oven together.

### Otam Exclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **the two loaves** brought on *Shavuot* should **also** be baked in **this** manner. **The verse states: "Them [otam],"** which is a term of exclusion, indicating that only the loaves of the shewbread are baked two at a time, but not the two loaves brought on *Shavuot*.

One might then ask whether the same paired-baking rule applies to the *shtei halechem* as well. The *gemara* considers this possibility and rejects it on the basis of the very same word *otam*: *talmud lomar otam* — the word "them" carries an exclusionary force, indicating that it is specifically these loaves — the *lechem hapanim* — that are baked in pairs, and not the *shtei halechem*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״אוֹתָם״, הָא אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ? אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״וְשַׂמְתָּם״! מַאי ״וְשַׂמְתָּ אוֹתָם״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't you** already **derive** from **this** term: **"Them,"** that the shewbread must be baked two loaves at a time? The Gemara answers: **If so,** i.e., if the term "them" teaches only that the shewbread is baked two loaves at a time, **let the verse say: And you shall set them [*vesamtam*],** using the shortened form. **What** is the verse teaching by using the longer form **"*vesamta otam*"? You** may **learn from** the verse **two** matters, both that the loaves of the shewbread should be baked two at a time and that this requirement does not apply to the two loaves brought on *Shavuot*.

The *gemara* anticipates the obvious objection: how can the word *otam* serve double duty — first to establish paired baking, and then to exclude the *shtei halechem* from it? The answer lies in the grammatical form of the verse. Had the Torah simply written *vesamtam* — "and you shall place them" — the plural would already have been conveyed by the verb conjugation alone, and the minimum of two would follow automatically. The fact that the Torah writes the separate word *otam* — making "them" a distinct term — indicates that this word is doing additional work: *shma mina tarti*, we derive two things from it. The placement is two at a time, establishing the minimum; and it is *otam* specifically — only the *lechem hapanim* — that follows this rule, excluding the *shtei halechem*.

### Three Molds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ אוֹתָם״ – בִּדְפוּס. שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּפוּסִין הֵם: נוֹתְנָהּ לִדְפוּס וַעֲדַיִין הִיא בָּצֵק, וּכְמִין דְּפוּס הָיָה לָהּ בַּתַּנּוּר, וּכְשֶׁהוּא רוֹדָהּ נוֹתְנָהּ בַּדְּפוּס כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְקַלְקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the shewbread was placed in a mold, and with regard to this **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall set them"** (Leviticus 24:6), which means to set them **in a mold. There are three molds** that are used in the Temple in the preparation of the loaves. First, the baker **places** the shewbread **in a mold** while **it is still dough. And** second, **there was a type of mold for** the shewbread **in the oven,** in which the loaves were baked. **And when he removes [*rodah*]** the shewbread from the oven, **he places it in** a third **mold so that its** shape **will not be ruined.**

The *baraita* continues with the three-mold requirement. The *gemara* grounds this in the verse *vesamta otam*, which it reads as referring to a placing in a mold: if you want to bake these loaves in a precise and prescribed shape, a mold is required, and the *baraita* identifies three distinct stages at which a mold was necessary. The first mold shaped the dough before baking. The second mold, in the oven, maintained the shape during baking. The third received the loaf upon its removal from the oven.

### Expansion Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַהְדְּרַהּ לִדְפוּס קַמָּא? כֵּיוָן דְּאָפֵי לַהּ, נָפְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why is a third mold necessary? **Let him return** the shewbread **to the first mold,** in which the dough was kneaded. The Gemara answers: **Once** the dough **is baked, it rises,** and no longer fits into the first mold.

A natural question arises: if you already had a mold before baking and a different mold inside the oven, why not simply return the baked loaf to the original pre-baking mold rather than using a third? The *gemara*'s answer is straightforward: *keivan de'aphi lafha* — once the bread was baked, it expanded. The baked loaf was larger than the raw dough had been, and it would no longer fit in the original mold. A third mold, sized to accommodate the expanded loaf, was therefore required.

## Shape Debate

### Open Box View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, כֵּיצַד עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated: How is the shewbread prepared,** i.e., in what shape? **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** It was rectangular, with a wide base and two parallel walls with an open space between them, **like a box that is open** on two sides.

The *gemara* then turns to a dispute regarding the precise shape of the *lechem hapanim*. Rabbi Chanina holds that the *lechem hapanim* was shaped *kemin teiva perutza* — like an open box. On this view, the loaf consisted of a flat base with walls rising up on two sides, forming a U- or box-like cross-section, open at the top.

### V-Shaped View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that the shewbread was **like a rocking boat,** i.e., a triangular-shaped boat with a narrow base from which two walls rise at angles. Since the boat does not have a wide base it rocks from side to side.

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, holds that the loaf was shaped *kemin sefina rokedes* — like a racing boat or a shaking boat. Rashi describes this as a vessel whose hull narrows to a V at the bottom, enabling it to move rapidly through water. According to Rashi, the cross-section of the loaf came to a pointed or V-shaped bottom. Tosafot qualifies this somewhat, suggesting that the walls above the V-bottom may have continued upward at right angles, but for the purposes of the *gemara*'s analysis, the essential feature is the pointed or angled lower profile.

### Frankincense Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה – הַיְינוּ דַּהֲווֹ יָתְבִי בָּזִיכִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת – הֵיכִי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי בָּזִיכִין? מָקוֹם עָבֵיד לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a box that is open** on two sides, **this is** the reason **that** the **bowls of frankincense could rest** upon it. The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat, how would** the **bowls rest** upon it? The Gemara answers: The baker **prepared** a flat **place for** the bowls to rest, on the side of the shewbread.

The *gemara* now tests each view against the practical requirements of the *lechem hapanim*. The Torah commands that two dishes of frankincense — *zichin* — be placed *al hamaarachot*, upon the stacks of bread, which the *gemara* understands to mean upon the topmost loaf of each of the two columns. According to the open-box view, this presents no difficulty: *hainu dehavu yasvi b'zichin* — the flat base of the open box provides a natural surface on which the frankincense dishes can rest securely. This is a clear advantage of Rabbi Chanina's position.

The V-shaped view, however, faces an apparent difficulty: *heichi havu yasvi b'zichin?* — how would the frankincense dishes rest on a V-shaped bottom? There is no flat surface to receive them. The *gemara* answers that one would make a place for them — *makom avid lah* — either by flattening a small area within the V, or by creating a niche in one of the walls into which the dish could be inserted, or by allowing the dish to be suspended and supported by the two rising sides of the V. Some adjustment to the bread's form would accommodate the placement, and the requirement could be met.

### V-Shape Rod Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה – הַיְינוּ דַּהֲווֹ יָתְבִי קָנִים, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת – קָנִים הֵיכִי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי? מוּרְשָׁא עָבֵיד לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a box that is open** on two sides, **this is** the reason **that** the **rods could rest** upon the it. The shewbread was placed on the Table in two arrangements. In each arrangement the lowest loaf was placed on the Table and the remaining loaves were set one above the other, with rods separating the loaves. There were fourteen rods for each arrangement, each loaf being placed upon three rods, except for the uppermost loaf, which was placed on only two rods. The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat, how were** the **rods resting** upon the shewbread? Since the loaves had a narrow base, they would rest on only one rod. The Gemara answers: The baker **would make a protrusion in** the base of the loaves, which would slightly widen their pointed base, enabling them to rest with stability upon the rods.

On the open-box view, the rods separating successive loaves in the stack would rest on the thick walls of the loaf below. The perpendicular side-walls of the open box have a certain thickness at their upper edge, and the rods can lie across those edges, spanning from one side-wall to the other. This provides a natural and stable resting place for the rods: *hainu dehavi yasvi kani* — this is how we understand where the rods would sit.

Under the V-shaped view, however, the walls narrow as they converge toward the point at the bottom, and the upper edges of those walls — wherever they terminate — may not offer sufficient flat surface to support the rods. The *gemara* addresses this: *mosha avid lehu* — one would fashion a protrusion from the dough itself. A small amount of dough would be built out at the appropriate height, creating a ledge or extension — *davka* — specifically to provide a resting surface for the rods that would lie above.

### Box Stability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת – הַיְינוּ דְּבָעֵינַן סְנִיפִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה – סְנִיפִין לְמָה לִי? אַגַּב יוּקְרָא דְּלֶחֶם תָּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat, this is** the reason **that we require panels.** Since the loaves do not have a wide base they cannot stand on their own without the support of the rods and panels. The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a box that is open** on two sides, **why do I** need **panels?** The loaves could stand without the assistance of rods and panels. The Gemara answers: If there were no panels supporting the loaves from the sides and the loaves were placed on top of each other, **due to the weight of** the upper **loaves** the lower loaves **would break [*telaḥ*].**

The *gemara* then raises a stability question that bears directly on the open-box view. If the bread is a flat-bottomed box whose width extends fully across the table, how do the panels keep the stack of twelve loaves — six to a column — in place? The panels are meant to support the bread from the sides, preventing lateral movement, but if the open box fully occupies the width of the table at its base, it is not immediately clear how there is any room for panels along the sides, or how the panels engage the bread to steady it. This is a pointed structural difficulty.

The *gemara* then asks why the open-box view would need panels at all. If the bread has a flat stable base, why not simply stack the loaves directly one upon another, without the elaborate apparatus of panels and rods? The answer is: *agav yukra delachma chals* — because of the sheer weight of the bread, the loaves below would be crushed under the accumulated weight of those above. The panels and rods serve not primarily to prevent the stack from toppling, but to distribute the load — to interpose a structural framework that transfers weight away from the bread itself so that the lower loaves are not damaged. Under the boat-shaped view, by contrast, the entire apparatus of panels and rods is essential simply to keep the V-shaped loaves from toppling at all, quite apart from the weight distribution problem.

### Panel Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת – סְנִיפִין עַל גַּבֵּי שֻׁלְחָן מוּנָּחִין. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה – סְנִיפִין הֵיכָא מַנַּח לְהוּ? אַאַרְעָא מַנַּח לְהוּ? אִין, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת – סְנִיפִין עַל גַּבֵּי שֻׁלְחָן מוּנָּחִין, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר כְּמִין תֵּיבָה פְּרוּצָה – סְנִיפִין עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע מוּנָּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat,** it is evident that the **panels are placed on** the **Table,** in order to prevent the slanted loaves from falling to the ground. The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who said** the shewbread was **like a box that is open** on two sides, **where would** the priest **place** the **panels?** Since the wide base of the loaves reached the edge of the Table, there was no room for the panels there. **Would** the priest **place them on the ground?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** the panels were placed on the ground, **as Rabbi Abba bar Memel said: According to the statement of the one who says** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat,** the **panels are placed on the Table,** whereas **according to the statement of the one who says** the shewbread was **like a box that is open** on two sides, the **panels are placed on** the **ground.**

The structural difference between the two views extends to where the panels are physically positioned. Rabbi Abba bar Memel articulates this explicitly. According to the view that the bread is shaped like a racing boat, *snifin algabei shulchan munachim* — the panels rest on the surface of the table itself. Because the V-shaped bread narrows to a point at the bottom, there is physical space between the base of the bread and the edges of the table, and the panels can be erected beginning from the table's surface, rising alongside the bread as it widens upward. According to the view that the bread is like an open box, however, *snifin algabei karka munachim* — the panels rest on the ground. Since the flat-bottomed box occupies the full width of the table from its base upward, there is no room on the table's surface for the panels; they must be built from the floor and rise up along the outer edges of the table to frame the stack from without.

### Mutual Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הַלֶּחֶם מַעֲמִיד אֶת הַסְּנִיפִין, וְהַסְּנִיפִין מַעֲמִידִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם? כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rabbi Yehuda said: The loaves support the panels and the panels support the loaves,** i.e., they lean against one another? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan. According to the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina, the panels stood on the ground and did not require the support of the loaves.

Finally, the *gemara* asks how the principle stated by Rabbi Yehuda fits into this debate: *halechem ma'amid et hasnifin vehasnifin ma'amidin et halechem* — the bread supports the panels and the panels support the bread. This reciprocal or mutual tension — where bread and panels press against one another, each holding the other in place — accords with the view that the bread is shaped like a racing boat. On that view, the structural arrangement is one of tension and counter-pressure: the V-shaped loaves, suspended on angled rods and surrounded by panels, exert lateral force against the panels while the panels prevent the loaves from splaying outward. Rashi explains that the bread's pressure on the bent rods pushes against the panels, while the panels in turn hold the bread from collapsing — a system of mutual structural tension. On the open-box view, by contrast, the relationship between bread and panels is less obviously reciprocal in this way: the weight of the bread bears down on the rods, and the panels prevent lateral displacement, but the sense of the bread actively pressing against and sustaining the panels is less clear. As the lecturer acknowledges, the precise mechanics of Rabbi Yehuda's principle as applied to the boat-shaped view remain somewhat difficult to fully visualize, but the *gemara* aligns his formulation with that opinion.
