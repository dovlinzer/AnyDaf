# Kiddushin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yi'ud: Erusin or Nisu'in?

### Rava's Core Question

The daf opens in the middle of 18b, in the context of a broader discussion of the *ama ivriya* — the Hebrew maidservant sold by her father while still a minor. A central feature of that institution is *yi'ud*: the possibility that the master, or his son, may designate her as a wife, effected either by the words *im livno yiyadena* or *asher lo ye'ada*, using the original purchase money as *kesef kiddushin*. The remainder of today's material is devoted entirely to working through the legal ramifications of *yi'ud*.

Rava bar Avuh opens with a pointed question: *yi'ud nisuin osa o erusin osa* — does *yi'ud* create full marriage (*nisuin*) or merely betrothal (*erusin*)? The question is structurally parallel to an earlier discussion about *kiddushei bi'ah*, where the same issue arose: since that form of kiddushin involves immediate physical intimacy, does it bypass the *erusin* stage and go straight to *nisuin*? Here the concern is analogous. The girl is already living in the master's home as a slave. When he performs *yi'ud*, designating her as his wife, does that act produce full *nisuin* — given that she is already present in his household — or is it only *erusin*, leaving *nisuin* as a separate subsequent stage?

The practical difference — the *nafka mina* — is considerable: does he inherit her if she dies? If he is a *kohen*, may he become *tamei* for her? May he annul her vows? All of these are consequences of *nisuin*, not of *erusin* alone. Does the obligation of *she'era kesuta ve'onata* — her sustenance, clothing, and conjugal rights — take effect immediately upon *yi'ud*, or only after a further step? All of these questions hinge on the answer.

### Shifcha Achar Ishut

The *Gemara* offers a first attempt at resolution: *ta shema*, come and hear. The Torah prohibits what we discussed on the previous daf — either *shifcha achar shifcha* or *shifcha achar ishut*, depending on how one reads the verse. One reading of *be'vigdo vah* — interpreting the word as *beged*, garment — is that once the master has "spread his garment over her," he may no longer sell her. This prohibits selling her as a slave after *yi'ud* has already created a marital bond.

Now, if the master performs *yi'ud* and subsequently dies, the *Gemara* notes that he cannot then sell her again — but that he *could* perform *yi'ud* again, meaning he could be *mekadesh* her. This raises the critical difficulty: if *yi'ud* created *nisuin*, then once *nisuin* has occurred, the father has no further authority over her whatsoever — *kivan de'niset shuv ein le'aviha reshut bah*. Why would the Torah need a special verse to prevent the father from reselling her? If she is fully married, she is already entirely out of his domain. The very need for a verse to forbid this act seems to imply that she remains under some residual paternal control — which is only possible if *yi'ud* created only *erusin*, not *nisuin*.

This appears to be a compelling proof. However, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak deflects it. He argues that *be'vigdo vah* in this context is not discussing *yi'ud* at all — it is discussing a completely separate scenario. The *braita* is teaching that if a father first gave his daughter in ordinary *kiddushin* to a man, and that man subsequently died, the father may no longer sell her as a slave. It is *not* addressing the case where she was initially sold as a *shifcha* and then received *yi'ud*. This is admittedly a strained reading of the *braita*, but it neutralizes the proof. The discussion of her being returned to her father's authority is not evidence about the nature of *yi'ud* — it concerns a case of ordinary *kiddushin* where the father loses the right to sell her thereafter.

### Braita Reinterpreted

The *Gemara* now presents a *braita* that initially appears unrelated to the *erusin*-versus-*nisuin* question but is in fact being mined for a proof. The *braita* records a dispute: one opinion holds that the father may not sell his daughter to close relatives (where *yi'ud* would be impossible, since *kiddushin* with a relative does not take effect), while Rabbi Eliezer permits this. All agree, however, that the father *may* sell her to a man who is prohibited from marrying her by a *lav* but for whom *kiddushin* would nonetheless be valid if performed — such as a *kohen gadol* marrying an *almana*, or a *kohen* marrying a *gerushah*. Even though such a marriage is forbidden, *kiddushin* in those cases is *tofes*, legally effective, and therefore *yi'ud* remains a theoretical possibility.

The *Gemara* asks: this is still trying to resolve the original question of whether *yi'ud* creates *erusin* or *nisuin*, yes — and keeps working through the implications.

### Almanah/Gerushah Proof

The *Gemara* now asks about the *almana* case more closely. How does this girl come to be an *almana* in the first place? If she married herself while a minor, the *kiddushin* would not have been valid — a minor cannot contract her own marriage. It must therefore be that her father previously gave her in *kiddushin* to some man, that man died, and now she is an *almana*. But in that case, how can the father now sell her as a *shifcha* at all? That would be *shifcha achar ishut* — selling her into servitude after she has already been in a marital bond — which is prohibited according to the *tanna* we have been following!

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by proposing that the scenario works according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *ma'ot harishonot lav le'kiddushin nitnu* — that the original purchase money was not designated as *kesef kiddushin*. Under this view, when the father initially sold his daughter, that act was purely a sale into servitude. The *kiddushin* occurred only later, through the act of *yi'ud* performed by the master — an independent transaction between the master and the girl in which the father played no role. Accordingly, the father never sold her *le'ishut*. The *ishut* happened separately, on its own. When the master dies, the father is therefore not in the position of someone who sold his daughter into marriage. He sold her into servitude, period. The subsequent marital bond was created independently. And that is why it is now permissible to discuss him selling her again — to a *kohen* or *kohen gadol* — because it would be *shifcha achar shifcha*, not *shifcha achar ishut*. The father was never the one who created the *ishut*.

### Ma'os Rishonos Debate

The deeper issue now emerges. Was the original purchase money — the *ma'ot rishonot* — the *kesef kiddushin*, or was it the remainder of the service still owed? The question matters enormously for whether the father was ever a party to the *kiddushin*.

The suggestion had been that the entire discussion of selling an *almana* or *gerushah* to a *kohen* works only according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda — who holds that the original money was *not kesef kiddushin*, and therefore the father was not the one who married her off. But the *Gemara* notes that there is a view — that of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — that even Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda may agree that it is the original money that serves as *kesef kiddushin*. If so, the father effectively did marry her off, and the problem of *shifcha achar ishut* returns. How then can the *braita* discuss him selling her as an *almana* or *gerushah* to a *kohen*?

The *Gemara*'s answer is that the scenario follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds the opposite of what we have been assuming: according to Rabbi Eliezer, *shifcha achar ishut* is actually permitted — it is *shifcha achar shifcha* that is prohibited. This resolves the difficulty neatly: if *shifcha achar ishut* poses no barrier, the father can sell her regardless of whether the original money was *kesef kiddushin*.

### Yi'ud = Erusin

Having worked through these preliminary difficulties, the *Gemara* now moves toward a definitive resolution. The *Rambam* rules that *yi'ud* creates only *erusin* — which is indeed where the dominant weight of the *Gemara*'s proofs has pointed throughout this discussion. The critical argument is this: the very fact that we are discussing scenarios where, after *yi'ud*, the father retains sufficient control to sell his daughter again or to contract a new marriage for her — all of this presupposes that after *yi'ud* she remains only an *arusa*, not a full *nisuah*. If *yi'ud* had created *nisuin*, she would have been entirely removed from her father's domain and none of these subsequent discussions would have any traction.

The *Gemara* does attempt one further rejoinder: perhaps one can distinguish between *nisuin* effected by the father and *nisuin* effected by the girl herself — *nisuin didah* versus *nisuin de'aviha*. But the *Gemara* rejects this distinction immediately. The reason a father loses authority over his daughter after *nisuin* is not because he performed a particular act that now prevents a subsequent act. It is the simple reality that she has become part of another man's household. Whether the *nisuin* was arranged by her father or arose independently through *yi'ud*, the result — her integration into another household — would be the same. Therefore the distinction cannot be sustained, and the proof stands: *yi'ud* creates only *erusin*.

## Conditions for Yi'ud

### Minor Son Question

Reish Lakish now raises a new question: *mahu she-ye'ayeid adam le'veno katan* — can a father perform *yi'ud* designating the *ama ivriya* for a son who is himself a minor? The Torah says *im livno yiyadena* — he may designate her for his son. Does "his son" mean any son, however young? Or does the son have to be an adult?

This question has a structural uniqueness. In ordinary *kiddushin*, a minor boy can never be the subject of a *kiddushin* arrangement. A girl can be married off while a minor, because her father acts on her behalf. But a minor boy cannot enter *kiddushin*: he cannot perform the act himself (lacking legal capacity), and there is no *shlichut le'katan* — an agent cannot act on behalf of a minor. Therefore, in ordinary law, *kiddushei katan* — a minor boy being betrothed — simply does not exist as a Torah institution. The question here is whether the unique Torah institution of *yi'ud*, which explicitly places the father in the active role, changes this calculus. The father himself is performing the *yi'ud* — perhaps the Torah permits it even for a minor son, since the father is the actor.

Two positions are suggested. One reads *beno* expansively — "his son, any son," even a *katan*. The other argues *beno dumya dideyh*: the son must parallel the father — just as the father is an adult, so too must the son be an adult. The logic is that while the father performs the act, what is fundamentally occurring is the establishment of a marital bond for the son, and a minor cannot be a party to such a bond.

### Minor Marriage Proof

Rabbi Zeira proposes a proof from a verse in the context of adultery: *ish asher yinaf et eshet re'ehu* — a man who commits adultery with his neighbor's wife. The verse specifies *ish* — a man — to exclude a minor: a minor who commits an act of adultery is not subject to the capital penalty. Similarly, the phrase *asher yinaf et eshet re'ehu* — adultery with a man's wife — excludes *eshet katan*, the wife of a minor: one who commits adultery with the wife of a minor is not liable.

But this immediately raises a question: where does one ever encounter the concept of "the wife of a minor"? For the Torah to need to exclude it, the institution must exist in some form. And if *yi'ud* can create a marital bond even for a minor son, that would be precisely such a case. The *Gemara* infers: if the Torah is excluding *eshet katan* from the laws of adultery, it implies that such a status exists — and the source of that status would be *yi'ud* performed by the father for his minor son. This would prove that *yi'ud* can indeed be performed for a *katan*.

### Yibum Alternative

Rav Ashi counters with an alternative source for *eshet katan*: *yibum*. If a man dies and his surviving brother is a boy between nine and thirteen years old, the physical act of *bi'ah* creates *yibum* — levirate marriage — even without intent, since *yibum* does not require conscious decision-making in the way that ordinary *kiddushin* does. If the mere physical act of intercourse, performed even by a minor over the age of nine, creates a valid marital bond through *yibum*, then the Torah's exclusion of *eshet katan* from the laws of adultery addresses precisely this scenario. It need not be addressing *yi'ud* at all. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the verse does not prove anything about *yi'ud* for a minor son — the *yibum* scenario sufficiently explains the Torah's need for that exclusion.

### Rashi/Tosafot Debate

A fundamental dispute between Rashi and Tosafot emerges here, concerning what the verse is actually teaching — the *kamashmalan* — in the *yibum*-of-a-minor case.

Rashi holds that if a boy of nine performs the act of *yibum* with his *yevamah*, the *yibum* is fully valid: she is his wife in every legal sense — he inherits her, he may become *tamei* for her, and so forth. *De'mid'oraita chazei lei* — she is biblically bound to him through the *zika*, and his act of *bi'ah* completes the acquisition. The *kamashmalan* of the verse is therefore not that the marriage is invalid, but rather that despite the validity of the marriage, there is no liability for adultery in such a case. The reasoning appears to be that since the husband is not yet a full adult, the marriage — while legally real — has not yet manifested as a complete marital reality, and therefore the capital punishment for adultery does not apply.

Tosafot disagrees sharply. Tosafot reads the verse as teaching that the marriage itself is not a Torah-level marriage at all. What might have appeared to be a valid *yibum* — because there is a *zika*, there is the physical act, there is the *yevamah* — is in fact only rabbinic in force. *Kamashmalan d'eino koneh ela mi'derabbanan* — the acquisition is only rabbinic. The verse teaches that on the Torah level, such a *yibum* does not create a marriage. Under this view, the practical consequences diverge dramatically from Rashi's: according to Tosafot, if another man subsequently has relations with this woman, she does not need a *get* — since there was no Torah-level marriage — whereas according to Rashi she is fully married and would require a *get* or *chalitzah*.

### Rabbi Aivo's Rule

Returning to the substance of *yi'ud* itself, the *Gemara* cites a statement of Rabbi Aivo in the name of Rabbi Yannai: *ein yi'ud ela be'gadol, ein yi'ud ela mi'da'as* — *yi'ud* can only be performed for an adult son, and it can only be performed *mi'da'as*. The *Gemara* asks: why state both conditions? Is this not redundant?

The *Gemara* answers that Rabbi Aivo is giving a reason rather than two separate rules: the reason *yi'ud* requires a *gadol* is precisely *because* it requires *da'as* — conscious awareness or consent. Since *da'as* is required, and a *katan* cannot supply the requisite *da'as*, it follows that the son must be an adult. The two clauses function together as principle and rationale.

This raises a further question about the nature of *da'as* in this context. Does it mean merely that the son must be informed, or does it mean that the son is the true principal in the *kiddushin* — that the father acts only as his authorized representative? The Rambam rules that the son must grant his father explicit permission to perform the *yi'ud* on his behalf. Under that reading, the father is genuinely acting as the son's agent, and the son's *da'as* is the operative one. This would explain why the son must be an adult: only an adult can authorize an agent, and only an adult can be the party entering into marriage.

### Girl's Da'as Required

Having established the requirement of the son's *da'as*, the *Gemara* now turns to a separate question: does the girl herself need to be part of the process in some meaningful way? Must *yi'ud* be performed with her knowledge?

### Abaye's Source

Abaye son of Rav Avuha derives this from the verse itself: *asher lo ye'adah* — "that he did not designate her" — which he reads as a positive requirement: *melamed shetzarich le'ya'adah*, the verse teaches that he must designate her, meaning he must make the *yi'ud* known to her. Rashi explains the term as *lashon dei'ah* — an expression of knowledge or awareness — indicating that she must be informed.

### When Kiddushin Occurs

The *Gemara* then explains the *yi'ud* requirement by connecting it to Abaye's teaching: *be'kiddushei yi'ud ve'aliba de'Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda de'amar ma'os rishonot lav le'kiddushin nitnu*. Why does this requirement of her *da'as* specifically arise according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda? Because if we follow the position that the original purchase money is the *kesef kiddushin* — that the *kiddushin* was effected at the time of the original sale by the father — then the girl need not be involved. The father is the active party, and in ordinary *kiddushin* effected by the father, the daughter need not be informed. But according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, the original money was *not kesef kiddushin*, and the *kiddushin* occurs later through the remaining service owed to the master — a transaction that takes place between him and her directly, without the father. Since the father is out of the picture and the *kiddushin* is happening between the two of them, she must be made part of the process, which requires at minimum that she be informed.

### Rav Nachman's View

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, however, argues that even if one holds that the original money *is* the *kesef kiddushin* — even according to the majority view — there remains a special scriptural requirement that the girl be informed. *Shani hacha de'amar Rachmana ye'adah* — here the Torah itself says "he designated her," imposing a unique condition on *yi'ud* that does not apply in ordinary *kiddushin*. Even though technically the *kiddushin* flows from the money given to the father, the Torah nonetheless demands that when *yi'ud* is actually performed, the girl must be made aware. It is a *gezeirat hakatuv* — a scriptural decree unique to this institution.

The Rambam rules simply that a man cannot perform *yi'ud* on the *ama ivriya* — either for himself or for his son — except *mi'da'atah*: with her awareness. Whether *da'atah* means she must actively acquiesce, or merely that she must be informed, remains somewhat open. The Rambam does not state that she can refuse and invalidate the *yi'ud*, which suggests that *da'atah* means notification rather than consent. The analogy to *mi'un* — a rabbinic mechanism by which a minor girl can reject a marriage — is instructive: since *mi'un* is a rabbinic institution, one cannot import it into this context to argue that she must agree on a Torah level.

## Ma'os Rishonos Question

### Rabbi Yosi's Position

Having woven the *ma'ot rishonot* debate through multiple earlier discussions, the *Gemara* now sets out Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda's position explicitly. It is cited from a *braita*: the Torah juxtaposes *ye'adah* (designation) and *hepdah* (redemption) — *asher lo ye'adah*. From this juxtaposition Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda derives that *yi'ud* can only be performed when there is still enough time remaining in the girl's service for her to redeem herself.

### Prutah Requirement

*Tzarich she'yish'heh bayom kedei fidyah* — there must be enough remaining on the day of *yi'ud* to constitute a *prutah*'s worth of service. Just as the girl can redeem herself by buying back her remaining service with a *prutah*, the master can perform *yi'ud* using that same *prutah* of remaining service as the *kesef kiddushin*. But if there is no service left — if the six years are at their final instant and there is not a *prutah*'s worth of labor remaining — *yi'ud* cannot be performed.

*Mikan amar Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda:* if there is enough time left in the day for her to perform a *prutah*'s worth of service, she is *mekudeshet*; if not, she is not. And the *Gemara* draws the explicit conclusion: *alma kasvar ma'ot rishonot lav le'kiddushin nitnu* — this demonstrates that in Rabbi Yosi's view, the original purchase money was not designated as *kesef kiddushin*. The *kiddushin* is being effected only through the remaining service, and that is why a *prutah*'s worth of remaining service is indispensable.

### Rav Nachman Counters

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak again presents the counter-position: one need not conclude that Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda rejects the *ma'ot rishonot* as *kesef kiddushin*. One can maintain that even he holds the original money becomes *kesef kiddushin*, and nonetheless require a *prutah* of remaining service. *Shani hacha de'amar Rachmana ve'hepdah* — it is a *gezeirat hakatuv* arising from the juxtaposition of *ye'adah* and *hepdah*. The Torah is imposing an independent condition: *yi'ud* may only be performed when redemption remains a live option — i.e., when at least a *prutah* of service remains. But the *kesef kiddushin* itself is still the original money; the *prutah* of remaining service is not itself the *kiddushin* but rather a threshold condition mandated by the verse.

## Yi'ud Extends Normal Law

### Minor Daughter Kiddushin

The *Gemara* now uses the framework established by *yi'ud* — particularly the mechanism through which *kiddushin* can occur without the father being the active party — to derive a broader ruling about ordinary *kiddushin*. The question under consideration is whether an underage daughter can herself accept *kesef kiddushin*, given that her father has signaled his approval. This touches directly on the *yi'ud* case: according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, the *kiddushin* occurs between the master and the girl directly, without the father. She is a *ketanah*, yet the *kiddushin* is valid. How is that possible?

### Father's Authorization

Rav Amram cites Rav Nachman: *amar adam le'vito ketana, tzei ve'kabeli kiddushayich* — a father can instruct his underage daughter to go and accept *kesef kiddushin* on his behalf. The daughter goes, receives the money from the prospective husband, and the *kiddushin* is valid. This ruling is derived from the logic of *kiddushei yi'ud*: since we must explain how *yi'ud* creates valid *kiddushin* even when the father is not the active party and the girl is a *ketanah*, the answer must be that the father's intent combined with her physical act of receiving is sufficient. And once we establish that principle, we can apply it to ordinary *kiddushin* as well.

### Tosafot Problem

Tosafot raises a significant difficulty: how can a minor girl effect a *kinyan* — an acquisition — of the ring or money? The problem is not merely one of *shlichut* (agency); even setting aside the inability of a minor to be an agent, the fundamental issue is that a *katan* or *ketanah* has no capacity for *kinyan*. Acquisition requires legal capacity, which a minor lacks. How then can the girl's receiving of the *kesef* constitute a valid *kinyan*, even if the father has authorized it?

### Two Da'asos Model

Tosafot resolves this through the concept of *da'as acheres makneh oso* — a transfer effected by the *da'as* of another party. Two distinct mental acts combine through the medium of the girl's hand. The prospective husband provides the *da'as* to transfer the object to her — his intent to convey the ring makes the transfer effective even without her independent capacity. Her hand serves as the physical vehicle through which the ring passes. At the same time, the father provides the *da'as* that she should be married — his intent constitutes the operative will behind the *kiddushin* itself. She does the act of receiving, the husband provides the *da'as* for the transfer of the object, and the father provides the *da'as* for the *kiddushin*. These two *da'asos* work through her hand, and together they constitute a valid *kiddushin*.

This model is precisely what explains *kiddushei yi'ud* according to Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda: when the father sold his daughter into servitude with the implicit expectation of *yi'ud*, he provided — at the time of the original sale — the *da'as* that if the master wished to marry her, he consented to it. When the master later performs *yi'ud*, the girl receives back the service still owed to her (which serves as the *kesef kiddushin*), and the combination of the father's earlier *da'as* and her act of receiving creates the valid *kiddushin*.

### Loan as Kesef

Rava cites Rav Nachman with a further application of this framework: *ha'mekadesh be'milveh ve'yesh lo mashkon mekudeshes* — if a man had lent money to a woman and held a *mashkon* (collateral) from her, and he subsequently was *mochel* (waived) the debt in the context of *kiddushin*, she is *mekudeshes*. The *hana'ah* — the benefit she receives from the waiver of her debt — serves as the *kesef kiddushin*.

### Mashkal Analogy

The *Gemara* explains the application of this case through the *mashkon*. Tosafot reads it as follows: one might have thought that when the creditor still holds the *mashkon*, there is insufficient *gemirut da'as* — finalization of intent — to constitute a proper *kiddushin*. After all, even though he declared the debt waived, he has not yet returned her collateral. Perhaps the *mochel* is not complete, and therefore the *hana'ah* is insufficient to serve as *kesef kiddushin*. The ruling teaches that even in such a case, *mekudeshes* — the *kiddushin* is valid.

The source for this ruling is again the framework of Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda. *Lav amar Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda ma'ot rishonot lav le'kiddushin nitnu* — the original money is not the *kesef kiddushin*; it is the remaining service that serves as *kesef kiddushin*. That remaining service is effectively a debt owed to the master: she owes him labor, and *hi gufa mashkon hi* — she herself is the collateral. Her presence in his household, her ongoing service, constitutes the security for the "debt" of labor. When he performs *yi'ud* and is *mochel* the remaining service, he is using that waiver — with her as the *mashkon* — as the *kesef kiddushin*. *Ve'ki meshayar bah shaveh prutah miyad havei kiddushin* — and from this case we can learn that the parallel scenario of a loan with a *mashkon* also works. The *Gemara* acknowledges that the analogy is not perfectly tight — in the *mashkon* case the creditor holds the woman's object as collateral, while in the *yi'ud* case the woman herself is the collateral — but the principle is being established.

## Yi'ud Procedure

### Yi'ud Declaration

The *Gemara* now sets out the mechanics of how *yi'ud* is performed: *omer lah bifnei shnayim harei at mekudeshet li, harei at me'oreset li* — he declares before two witnesses that she is betrothed to him. This is the standard *erusin* declaration, made in the presence of two valid witnesses, as required for any *kiddushin*. The *Gemara* adds that this can be performed even at the very end of the six years — *afillu b'sof shesh*, even *samuch le'shkiat hachama*, within moments of sunset on the last day. From that point on, *noheg bah minhag ishut ve'eino noheg bah minhag shifcha* — he treats her as a wife and no longer as a slave.

### Rabbi Yosi Condition

Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda reiterates his condition: *im yesh she'hut bayom kedei la'asot imo shaveh prutah, mekudeshes; ve'im lav, einah mekudeshes* — she is *mekudeshes* only if, at the time of *yi'ud*, there remains enough time in the day for her to perform a *prutah*'s worth of service; if not, the *yi'ud* does not take effect.

### Mashkal Delayed Effect

The *Gemara* then introduces a *mashal* — an analogy: *le'omer le'ishah hitkadshi li me'achshav ve'le'achar shloshim yom* — "be married to me from now, and it shall take effect in thirty days." If another man attempts to betroth her within those thirty days, she is nonetheless *mekudeshes la'rishon* — betrothed to the first man. Since the *kiddushin* was stated to begin *me'achshav*, from now, it has already commenced, and a subsequent betrothal cannot override it.

Rav Acha b'rei d'Rava explains that this *mashal* is addressed specifically to the Rabbis — the position of *chachamim* who hold that the original money is the *kesef kiddushin*. For the Rabbis, *kiddushei yi'ud* works precisely like *me'achshav ve'le'achar zman*: the money changes hands at the time of the initial sale — *me'achshav* — and the *kiddushin* is fully realized only years later at the time of *yi'ud* — *le'achar zman*. The two events are bound together: the first provides the *kesef*, and the second completes the *kiddushin*.

### Why Mashkal Matters

The *Gemara* asks *peshita* — isn't this obvious? If it began with the original money, of course a subsequent act cannot override it. The *Gemara* answers: *mah d'teima hala lo amar lah me'achshav* — one might have thought that since the master never explicitly said *me'achshav* at the time of the original sale, and since he had not yet even decided at that moment that he wished to perform *yi'ud*, perhaps the whole *kiddushin* takes effect only at the moment of *yi'ud* and is entirely prospective. Under that reading, anything occurring before the *yi'ud* declaration would not be subject to the earlier money at all. The ruling therefore teaches: *kamashmalan* — when he ultimately performs *yi'ud*, the *kiddushin* is treated retroactively as having begun when the money changed hands, analogous to *me'achshav*, and the entire sequence is bound together.

### No Interference Rule

The practical consequence follows directly: if during the period between the initial sale and the *yi'ud* declaration, a third party attempts to betroth this woman, the subsequent *kiddushin* does not take effect. Just as in the *me'achshav ve'le'achar shloshim yom* case the earlier *kiddushin* prevails, so too in *yi'ud* — once the master ultimately declares *yi'ud*, the *kiddushin* is treated as having begun when the money was first paid, and any intervening attempt by another man to create *kiddushin* with her is void.
