# Bava Kamma 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Attribution

### Theft & Madai

The *gemara* on 103b had been discussing to what extent the *mishnah* could be attributed to either Rabbi Akiva or Rabbi Tarfon. The *mishnah* states that if one steals from another even a minimal value — *shaveh prutah* — and swore falsely about it, the *gazlan* must pay it back even if he must travel as far as Madai: *yolichenu acharav l'Madai*. Madai, presumably somewhere in Persia, represents an extraordinarily distant journey, and the *mishnah*'s point is that the obligation to return stolen property knows no geographical limit.

### Five People Case

The *gemara* had previously attempted to connect this *mishnah* to the well-known dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon concerning a person who stole from one of five people but did not know which of them was the victim. In that case, Rabbi Tarfon holds that the thief may simply leave the payment with the group and discharge his obligation, while Rabbi Akiva holds that he must actively attempt to repay each of the five. The *gemara* had suggested that our *mishnah* might align with Rabbi Akiva's more stringent position — but that line of reasoning was rejected.

### Tarfon & Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי מַתְנִיתִין, דְּכֵיוָן דְּיָדַע לְמַאן גַּזְלֵיהּ, וְאוֹדִי לֵיהּ; כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי מָמוֹנָא לְמָרֵיהּ – הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: "יִהְיוּ לִי בְּיָדְךָ". הִלְכָּךְ, נִשְׁבַּע – אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: "יִהְיוּ לִי בְּיָדְךָ", כֵּיוָן דְּבָעֵי כַּפָּרָה, לָא סַגִּי עַד דְּמָטֵי לִידֵיהּ; הָא לָא אִישְׁתְּבַע – הָוֵי גַּבֵּיהּ פִּקָּדוֹן, עַד דְּאָתֵי וְשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The case of **the mishna is different,** and the *halakha* stated here could be in accordance with the opinions of both Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva. **For as** the robber **knows whom he robbed and admitted to him** that he robbed him, **since it is possible to return the money to its owner,** the robbery victim **is as the one who says to** the robber: The money you owe me **will be** kept **in your possession** as a deposit **for me. Therefore,** in a case where the robber **took** a false **oath, even though** the owner is considered to have **said to him:** The money you owe me **will be** kept **in your possession** as a deposit **for me, since** the robber **requires atonement** for his false oath, **it is not possible** for him to achieve atonement **until** the money **reaches the possession of** the robbery victim. **But** in a case where the robber **did not take** a false **oath,** the money **is** considered as a **deposit** given **to** the robber **until** the owner **comes and takes it.**

Resuming the discussion, the *gemara* now proposes: *ella l'olam Rabbi Tarfon hi* — the *mishnah* should actually be read in accordance with Rabbi Tarfon's view. The proposal is that Rabbi Tarfon would concede a higher level of liability specifically in a case where the thief swore falsely about what he stole. Ordinarily, Rabbi Tarfon would permit the thief to leave a reasonable approximation of the payment and consider the obligation discharged; but having compounded his wrongdoing with a false oath, even Rabbi Tarfon would agree that a heightened standard of personal restitution applies.

### Korban Asham

The *gemara* grounds this reasoning in a *pasuk*: *l'asher hu lo yitnenu b'yom ashmato* — whatever belongs to its rightful owner must be given to him on the day of his guilt. The verse ties the *korban asham*, the guilt offering brought for theft, to the obligation of personal delivery. Whenever a *korban asham* is implicated — that is, when the theft rises to the level of requiring such an offering — the thief bears a correspondingly elevated responsibility not merely to deposit the payment somewhere accessible, but to place it directly in the hands of the person to whom it is owed.

### Akiva's Position

The *gemara* then clarifies the difference between the two Tannaim. Rabbi Akiva — *d'af al gav d'lo ishtava kanis* — holds that this elevated level of obligation applies even without a false oath. Rabbi Tarfon, by contrast, concedes to Rabbi Akiva's position only when a false oath has been sworn; his leniency is reserved for the case where no oath was taken. The *gemara* therefore asks: since Rabbi Tarfon in any event must concede a higher liability when an oath is involved, and Rabbi Akiva extends this even without an oath, why does the *mishnah* need to specify the oath at all? What is the *mishnah* teaching us that was not already clear?

### Admission Case

The *gemara* presses further: *mai irya nishba* — why does it matter whether he swore? Rabbi Akiva's position already mandates the higher standard regardless. A *braita* is then cited: *modeh Rabbi Tarfon b'omer l'shnayim gazalti echad mikem* — Rabbi Tarfon agrees with Rabbi Akiva in a case where the thief said to two people, "I stole a *maneh* from one of you, but I do not know which of you," and therefore must pay both. Crucially, there was no oath in this case. Why does Rabbi Tarfon agree? *Shekvar hoda mipi atzmo* — because he already admitted it of his own accord. Admission, the *braita* teaches, is also a trigger for the higher level of liability, entirely independent of a false oath. Once a person voluntarily confesses, he has so entrenched himself in his liability that a lenient discharge of his obligation is no longer available.

## Rava's Shomer

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי מַתְנִיתִין, דְּכֵיוָן דְּיָדַע לְמַאן גַּזְלֵיהּ, וְאוֹדִי לֵיהּ; כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי מָמוֹנָא לְמָרֵיהּ – הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: "יִהְיוּ לִי בְּיָדְךָ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The case of **the mishna is different,** and the *halakha* stated here could be in accordance with the opinions of both Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva. **For as** the robber **knows whom he robbed and admitted to him** that he robbed him, **since it is possible to return the money to its owner,** the robbery victim **is as the one who says to** the robber: The money you owe me **will be** kept **in your possession** as a deposit **for me.**

*Ella amar Rava* — the *gemara* now introduces a fundamentally different approach. *Tosafot* notes that there is a textual question about whether the word *ella* appears in the *gemara*; Rashi's reading includes it, making this an outright rejection of the Rabbi Tarfon framework. Rava argues: *shani matnitin* — the *mishnah* is categorically different from the case used for comparison. Stop attempting to read the *mishnah* through the lens of the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon. Those cases involve a thief who does not know whom he stole from; our *mishnah* involves a thief who knows exactly who his victim is, and whose only difficulty is the victim's geographic distance. These are fundamentally different situations and cannot be equated.

### Knows Victim

According to Rava's reading, the *mishnah* presents a scenario in which the *gazlan* knows precisely who he stole from. The question is not about resolving ambiguity between potential victims; it is about the *gazlan*'s obligation when the victim is identified but geographically inaccessible. This distinction, Rava insists, changes the entire legal analysis.

### Gazlan to Shomer

Once the thief knows who he stole from and admits it, Rava argues, something remarkable occurs: *keivan deyada leman gazlei ve'odei lei, keivan de'efshar le'ahadoora mamona lemarei, hava lei keman de'amar lei yehei li beyadecha* — because he knows who was wronged and it is possible to return the money to its rightful owner, the *nigzal* is treated as though he had said to the thief, "Hold it in your possession for me." The thief's legal status undergoes a transformation. He is no longer a *gazlan* in the full sense — a person actively resisting restitution — but has been reclassified, as it were, as a *shomer pikadon*, a watchman of a deposit. He holds the money in trust until the owner comes to claim it. This is admittedly a striking legal fiction: the thief becomes a *shomer* without any actual communication from the victim, simply by virtue of his admission and the identifiability of the victim. The Gemara acknowledges the automatic nature of this status change — *keman de'amar lei*, "as though he said" — and it is genuinely unusual that a *shomer* relationship arises without an explicit request from the depositor.

### Shomer Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, נִשְׁבַּע – אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: "יִהְיוּ לִי בְּיָדְךָ", כֵּיוָן דְּבָעֵי כַּפָּרָה, לָא סַגִּי עַד דְּמָטֵי לִידֵיהּ; הָא לָא אִישְׁתְּבַע – הָוֵי גַּבֵּיהּ פִּקָּדוֹן, עַד דְּאָתֵי וְשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** in a case where the robber **took** a false **oath, even though** the owner is considered to have **said to him:** The money you owe me **will be** kept **in your possession** as a deposit **for me, since** the robber **requires atonement** for his false oath, **it is not possible** for him to achieve atonement **until** the money **reaches the possession of** the robbery victim. **But** in a case where the robber **did not take** a false **oath,** the money **is** considered as a **deposit** given **to** the robber **until** the owner **comes and takes it.**

Given this transformation in status, the significance of the false oath becomes clear. *Hilkach nishba* — if the thief, now functioning as a *shomer*, takes a false oath, he has violated the terms of his *shmirah*. *Keivan deba'ei kapara lo sagi ad demati liyadei* — since he requires atonement for this violation, that atonement cannot be achieved until the money actually reaches the hands of its rightful owner. This is why the *mishnah* obligates him to travel even to Madai: the *kapara* is incomplete until personal delivery is effected.

### No Oath Shomer

If, however, the thief took no oath at all, his status as a *shomer* is undisturbed. *Hava gabei pikadon ad de'atei veshakil lei* — the money simply sits with him as a *pikadon* until the owner comes and retrieves it. He has no obligation to pursue the victim; it is the victim who must come and claim what is his. This cleanly accounts for the *mishnah*'s structure: the oath is the dividing line between the obligation to travel to Madai and the mere passive status of a *shomer* waiting for the owner to appear. And because Rava's reading makes the *mishnah* comprehensible without reference to the Akiva–Tarfon dispute, the *mishnah* according to this approach is *lidvrei hakol* — agreed upon by all opinions.

## Shaliach & Witnesses

### Shaliach Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִתֵּן לֹא לִבְנוֹ וְלֹא לִשְׁלוּחוֹ. אִיתְּמַר: שָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ בְּעֵדִים – רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ, רַבָּה אָמַר: לָא הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the robber wishes to return the stolen item, he **may not give** the payment **to** the robbery victim's **son** to return it to the robbery victim, **nor to his agent.** The Gemara comments: It **was stated** with regard to **an agent who was appointed in** the presence of **witnesses** to collect a debt from another: **Rav Ḥisda said:** Such an agent **is** a legally recognized **agent,** so that the debtor is considered to have repaid his debt as soon as he transfers the money to the agent, and the debtor will not be held responsible if an accident occurs causing the loss of the money before the agent gives it to the creditor. **Rabba said:** Such an agent **is not** a legally recognized **agent,** and the debtor remains responsible until the money reaches the creditor's possession.

The *mishnah* had stated *lo yitein lo livno velo lishlucho* — the thief may not give the payment to his own son or his own *shaliach*, and by extension not to the son or *shaliach* of the *nigzal* either. This raises the question of what happens when the *nigzal* appoints a *shaliach* with witnesses — *shaliach she'asao be'eidim*. Normally, appointing a *shaliach* requires no formalities whatsoever; one simply asks another person to act on one's behalf. But does the presence of witnesses at the appointment confer a special legal status on the *shaliach* — enough to make the transfer of funds to him count as payment?

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ – לְהָכִי טְרַחִי וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּעֵדִים, דְּלֵיקוּ בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **Rav Ḥisda said** that he **is an agent** because it was **for this** reason that the creditor **took the trouble and appointed** the agent **in** the presence of **witnesses, in order to place** the matter **under** the agent's **domain.**

Rav Chisda holds: *havi shaliach* — a *shaliach* appointed with witnesses does count as a proper *shaliach*. His reasoning is straightforward: *le'hachi tarchi ve'ukmei be'eidim deliku bivrushay* — the whole point of going to the trouble of bringing witnesses was so that the *shaliach* should stand in the place of the appointing party. If the thief pays this formally-appointed *shaliach*, the payment is complete, and any subsequent loss is the *nigzal*'s responsibility.

### Rabba's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר לָא הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ – הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִינִישׁ מְהֵימְנָא הוּא; אִי סָמְכַתְּ – סְמוֹךְ, אִי בָּעֵית לְשַׁדּוֹרֵיה בִּידֵיהּ – שַׁדַּר בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that he **is not an agent** because **this is** what the creditor **is** assumed to be **saying** to the debtor: This individual **is a trustworthy person. If you** are willing to **rely** on him, then **rely** on him to convey the payment to me. Similarly: **If you wish to send** the debt **in his possession,** then **send** it **in his possession.**

Rabba disagrees: *lo havi shaliach* — even a *shaliach* made with witnesses does not count as a proper *shaliach* for purposes of this *mishnah*. In Rabba's reading, the presence of witnesses at the appointment does not transform the *shaliach* into a legal representative capable of receiving payment on the *nigzal*'s behalf. The witnesses serve only to signal that the appointer regards this person as trustworthy: *inish mehaymna hu* — he is a reliable fellow. But trustworthiness is not the same as legal agency. The *mishnah*'s prohibition on using a *shaliach* remains fully in force, and the obligation is discharged only when the money reaches the *nigzal* himself.

### Bava Metzia Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַשּׁוֹאֵל אֶת הַפָּרָה, וְשִׁילְּחָהּ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ, בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ; אוֹ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ, בְּיַד עַבְדּוֹ, בְּיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹאֵל; וּמֵתָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a mishna that poses a difficulty to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda. **We learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 98b): With regard to **one who** enters into an agreement to **borrow a cow** from another, **and** the owner **sent it** to the borrower **in the possession of his** own **son,** or **in the possession of his** own **slave,** or **in the possession of his** own **agent, or** if he sent it **in the possession of the borrower's son,** or **in the possession of** the borrower's **slave,** or **in the possession of** the borrower's **agent, and** the cow **died,** the borrower is **exempt** from paying for the cow, as it never entered his possession.

The *gemara* brings a proof from a *mishnah* in Bava Metzia. The *mishnah* there teaches: *ha'shoel et hapara ve'shilcha beyad bno beyad avdo beyad shlucho* — if one borrows a cow and sends it back via his son, his slave, or his *shaliach*, or via the son, slave, or *shaliach* of the borrower, and the cow dies in transit, the borrower is exempt. Normally, a *shoel* bears an extremely high level of liability; yet here, even though the cow was sent with someone acting as his *shaliach*, the borrower is not liable.

The *gemara* then asks: what does "his *shaliach*" mean in this context? If the *shaliach* was not appointed with witnesses, how could we even know he was the *shaliach*? — *mena yadinan*. It must therefore refer to a *shaliach* appointed with witnesses, and yet the *mishnah* rules the borrower exempt. This is a strong proof for Rabba: even with witnesses, the *shaliach* does not effect the legal transfer. *Kashya le'Rav Chisda* — this presents a genuine difficulty for Rav Chisda's position.

### Chisda's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שְׁלוּחוֹ הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלֹא (עָשָׂה) [עֲשָׂאוֹ] בְּעֵדִים, מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אֶלָּא (דְּעָשָׂה) [דַּעֲשָׂאוֹ] בְּעֵדִים – וְקָתָנֵי דְּפָטוּר? קַשְׁיָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא! כִּדְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בִּשְׂכִירוֹ וּלְקִיטוֹ, הָכָא נָמֵי בִּשְׂכִירוֹ וּלְקִיטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the case of the mishna: With regard to **this agent of** the borrower, **what are the circumstances** surrounding his appointment? **If** the mishna is discussing a case **where he did not appoint** him as his agent **in** the presence of **witnesses, how do we know** that he was appointed as an agent? **Rather,** it must be that it is a case **where he appointed** him as his agent **in** the presence of **witnesses, and** yet the mishna **teaches that** the borrower is **exempt.** This poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda,** who holds that one acquires property through his agent. According to his reasoning, the borrower should be held responsible for the cow once it reaches the possession of the agent. **It is as that** which **Rav Ḥisda said** concerning a different mishna: This is stated **with regard to his hired** hand **or his** regular **harvester; here also** the mishna in *Bava Metzia* is **with regard to his hired** hand **or his** regular **harvester.** The mishna discusses a case where there were no witnesses to the appointment of the agent, and therefore it does not pose a difficulty to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda. Yet, since it is well known that this person is constantly in the owner's service, he is assumed to be acting as his agent.

Rav Chisda responds by reinterpreting the *mishnah* in Bava Metzia: *bischiro uvelikito* — the word *shaliach* in that *mishnah* does not refer to a formally-appointed legal representative at all. It refers either to a *sachir*, a hired laborer who simply went and did this task as part of his employment, or to a *lakito*, a sharecropper or tenant who performed the errand informally. Rashi notes that his teachers offered one interpretation of *lakito* while he himself offers another, which gives us an interesting glimpse into Rashi's methodology and the interplay between his own analysis and the tradition he received from his teachers in Germany — a question of broader significance regarding how much of Rashi's commentary represents his own innovative contribution versus the received tradition of the early Ashkenazic authorities.

The same reading is then applied to our *mishnah*: when our *mishnah* prohibits giving the payment to a *shaliach*, it too means only an informal, quasi-representative — *bischira u'likita* — not a properly and formally appointed *shaliach*. A true *shaliach* appointed with witnesses, according to Rav Chisda, would work. So the *gemara* then asks: why does our *mishnah* specifically mention the *shaliach beit din* as the valid exception, without mentioning a formally-appointed *shaliach* with witnesses? If both count equally, the *mishnah* should have stated the case of the private *shaliach* with witnesses explicitly.

### Who Appoints

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ בְּעֵדִים – מַאי, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּהָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ? אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֲבָל נוֹתֵן הוּא לִשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין; לִפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידֵיהּ – שָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ בְּעֵדִים, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּהָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ! אָמְרִי: לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ; שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין, לָא שְׁנָא עֲשָׂאוֹ נִגְזָל וְלָא שְׁנָא עֲשָׂאוֹ גַּזְלָן, הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ – פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ. שְׁלִיחַ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ בְּעֵדִים, דְּכִי עֲשָׂאוֹ נִגְזָל הוּא דְּהָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ, עֲשָׂאוֹ גַּזְלָן לָא הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ – לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **an agent who** the robbery victim **appointed in** the presence of **witnesses, what** is the *halakha*? Would one say that **indeed,** one appointed before witnesses **is** a legally recognized **agent?** If that is the case, **instead of teaching** in **the latter clause: But he may give** the payment **to an agent of the court,** which indicates that this is the only agent to whom he may give it, **let** the mishna **make a distinction within** the case of an agent appointed by the robbery victim **itself and teach: An agent who** the robbery victim **appointed in** the presence of **witnesses is indeed** a legally recognized **agent.** If an agent appointed in the presence of witnesses is a legally recognized agent, why does the mishna shift to discuss an agent of the court? The Sages **say** in response: Although the *tanna* of the mishna could have stated the case of an agent appointed by the robbery victim in the presence of witnesses, **he** could **not** have stated this *halakha* **definitively,** as with regard to **an agent of the court,** there **is no difference** whether the **robbery victim appointed him** as an agent, i.e., the court appointed him at the behest of the robbery victim, **and** there **is no difference** whether the **robber appointed him** as an agent, i.e., the court appointed him at the behest of the robber. In both cases **he is** a legally recognized **agent.** Therefore, **he** could state the *halakha* concerning an agent of the court **definitively.** By contrast, with regard to **an agent who was appointed in** the presence of **witnesses, that** only **when** the **robbery victim appointed him** as an agent **he is an agent,** but when the **robber appointed him** as an agent, **he is not an agent,** as the robber cannot appoint an agent to receive payment on behalf of the robbery victim, **he** could **not** have stated this *halakha* **definitively.**

The *gemara* answers on Rav Chisda's behalf: the *shaliach beit din* is mentioned in the *mishnah* because of its unique generality. *Lo shena asao nigzal lo shena asao gazlan* — whether the *shaliach beit din* was requested by the *nigzal* or by the *gazlan*, it works in either case, because the *beit din*'s appointment renders the *shaliach* an impartial agent. A privately-appointed *shaliach* with witnesses, by contrast, is only effective when it was the *nigzal* who made the appointment — *hu dehavai shaliach* — because in that case the *nigzal* has voluntarily chosen to extend his trust and bear the attendant risk. This is precisely the point raised by one of the students: why would the *nigzal* ever agree to such an arrangement? The answer is that if he does so willingly, he has effectively transferred the risk to himself. But if the *gazlan* unilaterally appointed the *shaliach* with witnesses, *lo havi shaliach* — the *shaliach* does not acquire effective legal status, because the *nigzal* never agreed to modify the terms of the obligation.

The *gemara* also notes that the *mishnah*'s formulation was designed to exclude the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who holds that even for a *shaliach beit din* the distinction between whether the *nigzal* or the *gazlan* made the appointment matters. Our *mishnah*, by endorsing the *shaliach beit din* without qualification, rejects Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's view. On balance, Rav Chisda's reading of the *mishnah* requires considerable interpretive effort and produces a somewhat convoluted account of the *mishnah*'s language; Rabba's straightforward reading fits the *mishnayot* more naturally.

## Yochanan & Elazar

### Agree: Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ בְּעֵדִים, הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the opinion of two other *amora'im* with regard to the status of an agent appointed in the presence of witnesses. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar both say: An agent who was appointed in** the presence of **witnesses is** a legally recognized **agent.**

The *gemara* now introduces a parallel Eretz Yisrael tradition: *Rabbi Yochanan v'Rabbi Elazar d'amrei tarvaihu shliach she'asao be'eidim havi shliach* — both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar agree that a *shliach* appointed with witnesses counts as a valid *shliach* in this context. Their position aligns with that of Rav Chisda rather than Rabba. It is worth noting the geographic pattern: two Babylonian Amoraim — Rav Chisda and Rabba — dispute the question, while two Eretz Yisrael Amoraim both concur on the more permissive view.

### Casual vs Official

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ; בְּמַמְצִיא לוֹ שָׁלִיחַ, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: "אִית לִי זוּזֵי גַּבֵּי פְלָנְיָא, וְלָא קָא מְשַׁדַּר לְהוּ. אִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ, דִּלְמָא אִינִישׁ הוּא דְּלָא מַשְׁכַּח לְשַׁדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The mishna does not mean to disqualify an agent appointed by the robbery victim in the presence of witnesses, but rather is referring to a case **where** the robbery victim **provides** the robber **with an agent** without appointing him in the presence of witnesses, in **that he says to** someone: **I have money** owed to me that is currently **with so-and-so, and he is not sending it. Present yourself to him,** as **perhaps** he wishes to return it but **cannot find a person with whom to send it.**

How then do Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar explain our *mishnah*, which seemingly prohibits using a *shaliach*? Their answer: *be'mamtsi lo shaliach* — the *mishnah* refers to a situation of casual, impromptu appointment. The *nigzal* did not formally designate an agent; rather, someone happened to be passing through and was told informally, *ait lei zuzei gabi ploni* — "I have money owed to me by so-and-so; perhaps if you stop by, you can check whether he'll send it with you." That is not an official appointment; it is a casual suggestion. For such an informal arrangement, no legal transfer of responsibility occurs, and the *gazlan* remains liable until the money reaches the *nigzal* directly. A true formal appointment with witnesses, however, is another matter entirely. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar thus read the *mishnah*'s prohibition as restricted to the casual case, leaving intact their ruling that a properly witnessed appointment constitutes valid *shlichut*. Like Rav Chisda, they must therefore also read the *mishnah*'s reference to *shliach* as equivalent to Rav Chisda's *bischiro u'v'likito* — an informal representative rather than a legal *shaliach*.

## Money via Shaliach

### Shmuel: No Dyuqani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מְשַׁלְּחִין מָעוֹת בִּדְיוֹקְנֵי, וַאֲפִילּוּ עֵדִים חֲתוּמִים עָלֶיהָ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִם עֵדִים חֲתוּמִים עָלֶיהָ – מְשַׁלְּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses another matter relating to the appointment of an agent. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** One who owes a debt **may not send money** with another to the creditor **on the basis of a figure [*deyokani*]** or sign used in place of a signature that is associated with the creditor. The possession of this sign on the part of the other is not sufficient proof that he has been appointed as an agent. **And** this is the *halakha* **even if witnesses** verifying that this is the creditor's sign **are signed on it. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: If** there are **witnesses signed on it, one may send** the money with the other.

*Amar Rabbi Yehuda amar Shmuel: ein meshalchin ma'ot b'dyuqani* — Shmuel rules that money cannot be sent by means of a *dyuqani*. Rashi explains the term: the owner of a *pikadon* provides a *shliach* with a letter bearing his signature — *chotamo b'igeret* — and instructs him to present it to the designated party and collect the funds. The idea is that the signed authorization serves as proof that the bearer was indeed sent by the rightful owner. Nonetheless, Shmuel rules this is insufficient. *V'afilu eidim chatumim aleha* — even if witnesses have signed the *dyuqani*, it still does not work. The threshold of proof required to transfer liability — to establish that this *shliach* was genuinely dispatched by the owner — is not met by a signed document alone, even a witnessed one.

### Yochanan: Valid

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *im eidim chotmim aleha meshalchin* — if witnesses have signed the authorization, the money may indeed be sent. In Rabbi Yochanan's view, a witnessed document functions like a *shtar*, a formal legal instrument authorizing the *shaliach*, and is sufficient to constitute proper *shlichut*. This is consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's broader position that a *shaliach* appointed with witnesses acquires genuine legal standing.

### Rabbi Abba Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לִשְׁמוּאֵל מַאי תַּקַּנְתָּא? כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא הָוֵי מַסֵּיק זוּזֵי בִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב סָפְרָא: בַּהֲדֵי דְּאָתֵית, אַיְיתִינְהוּ נִיהֲלִי. כִּי אֲזַל לְהָתָם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ: מִי כָּתַב לָךְ: "הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי"? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. אִי הָכִי, זִיל בְּרֵישָׁא, וְיִכְתּוֹב לָךְ "הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי".
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say: According to Shmuel, what is the rectification,** i.e., what recourse is available to one who wishes to collect a debt without traveling to the debtor's location? The Gemara answers: One may act **like that** course of action **of Rabbi Abba,** who **was owed money by Rav Yosef bar Ḥama.** Rabbi Abba **said to Rav Safra: When you come** back from Rabbi Yosef bar Ḥama's locale, **bring** my money **to me. When** Rav Safra **went there, Rava,** Rav Yosef bar Ḥama's **son, said to him: Did** Rabbi Abba **write you** a document stating: **I have received** payment, so that my father will have proof that he has absolved himself of the debt? Rav Safra **said to him: No.** Rava responded: **If so, first go** to him **and let him write you** a document stating: **I have received** payment, and then my father will pay you.

The *gemara* then asks: according to Shmuel, what practical mechanism can a debtor use to repay a distant creditor? The answer is provided through a narrative. Rabbi Abba had money owed to him by Rav Yosef bar Chama. He asked Rav Safra, who was traveling to that locale, to collect it on his behalf. When Rav Safra arrived, Rava bar Rav Yosef — the son of Rav Yosef bar Chama — challenged him: *mi katav lach hitkabalti* — did Rabbi Abba write you a document of *hitkabalti*, an acknowledgment stating that receipt of the money by the *shaliach* constitutes receipt by the creditor himself? Rav Safra said no. Rava bar Rav Yosef therefore refused to pay, instructing him: *ei hachi, zil bereisha veyichtov lach hitkabalti* — go back first and obtain this *hitkabalti*, and only then will I release the funds. The logic is clear: without such an authorization, handing the money to the *shaliach* does not discharge the debt; if the money is then lost or stolen in transit, the debtor remains liable.

### Death Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי כָּתַב לָךְ נָמֵי "הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי", לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא; דִּלְמָא אַדְּאָתֵית שָׁכֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא, וְנָפְלוּ זוּזֵי קַמֵּי יַתְמֵי, וְ"הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי" דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאֶלָּא מַאי תַּקַּנְתָּא? זִיל נַקְנִינְהוּ לָךְ אַגַּב אַרְעָא, וְתָא אַתְּ כְּתוֹב לַן "הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי".
>
> **Translation:** **Ultimately,** Rava **said to him: Even if he writes you** a document stating: **I have received** payment, **it is nothing,** since **perhaps by** the time **you arrive** back here, **Rabbi Abba will have died and the money will fall before** his **orphans** as an inheritance, **and** the document stating: **I have received** payment, that was written **by Rabbi Abba, will be nothing,** as the debt is no longer owed to him, but rather, to his heirs. Rav Safra **said to him: Rather, what is the rectification?** Rava replied: **Go,** and Rabbi Abba will **transfer to you** the rights to the money owed to him **by means of** acquisition of **land** from him, **and** then **you** should **come** and **write for us** a document stating: **I have received** payment. Then, we will pay you.

When Rav Safra returned with the *hitkabalti*, Rava bar Rav Yosef confronted him with a second, more penetrating objection: *ei katav lach nami hitkabalti, lav klum hu* — even now that you have the *hitkabalti*, it is still worthless. Why? *D'dilma ad'aitit shakiv Rabbi Abba* — perhaps by the time you return, Rabbi Abba will have died. Since Rabbi Abba was apparently quite elderly at the time, this was a realistic concern. And the *hitkabalti* was issued only in Rabbi Abba's name; it cannot operate as an acknowledgment of receipt for his heirs. If Rabbi Abba dies before the money reaches him, the *yorshim* will have a fresh claim against the debtor, and the *hitkabalti* — which speaks only to Rabbi Abba's receipt — will be legally ineffective against them.

One might ask: ordinarily we do not assume that a person will die in the interim — we send *korbanot*, appoint *shelichim* for *kiddushin* and *gittin*, and transact all manner of legal business across distances without worrying about the other party's death. The *gemara* here seems to treat the risk of Rabbi Abba's death as a genuine concern, perhaps because of his advanced age — which suggests that the general rule operates only where there is no specific indication of particular vulnerability.

### Kinyan Agav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא הֲוָה מַסֵּיק תְּרֵיסַר אַלְפֵי זוּזֵי בֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אַקְנִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא אַגַּב אַסִּיפָּא דְבֵיתֵיהּ. כִּי אֲתָא, נְפַק לְאַפֵּיהּ עַד תְּוָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: And this is **like that** course of action **of Rav Pappa,** who **was owed twelve thousand dinars** by a resident of **Bei Ḥozai,** and **he transferred** his claim to the money **to Rav Shmuel bar Abba by means of** the acquisition of **the threshold of his house. When** Rav Shmuel bar Abba **came** from Bei Ḥozai with the money in hand, Rav Pappa **went out as far as Tavakh toward him** in his excitement to receive the money.

*Amar lei: v'ella mai takanta? Zil nakninhu lach agav arra* — go and effect a *kinyan agav*. The mechanism of *kinyan agav* — acquisition carried on the back of land — allows movable property and money to be transferred together with a land transaction. The debtor could sell Rabbi Abba a tiny parcel of land — even as small as a single *amah* — and include the debt payment as property acquired *agav*, on the back of that land transaction. The transfer of title to both the land and the money would then occur immediately and simultaneously, without the need for a *shaliach* to physically transport the funds. Once the *kinyan* is effected, the money belongs to Rabbi Abba — or, crucially, to his estate — regardless of what subsequently happens to anyone involved. This device cuts out the entire problem of the *shaliach*: there is no intermediary who might die or lose the money; the transfer is legally instantaneous.

### Rav Papa Example

The practical utility of this solution is illustrated by a story involving Rav Papa. Rav Papa had 13,000 *zuz* owed to him in Bei Chozai — a distant location. He arranged the transfer by way of *kinyan agav*, acquiring the funds through Rav Shmuel bar Abba *agav* — on the back of — even as small a property as *asifa d'beiteih*, the threshold of his house. When the *shaliach* finally arrived, Rav Papa went out to greet him as far as the town of Tavach — a sign not merely of personal warmth, but of profound relief and satisfaction that the halakhic puzzle had been solved. The point was not the 13,000 *zuz* per se, but the elegant legal solution that had been devised to navigate all the complications of transferring money through an intermediary.

## Chomesh Status

### Mishnah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְכוּ׳. אַלְמָא חוֹמֶשׁ מָמוֹנָא הוּא, וְאִם מָיֵית – מְשַׁלְּמִי לֵיהּ יוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the robber **gave** the robbery victim **the principal** value of the stolen item, but did not give him the additional one-fifth payment required of a robber, he is not required to pursue the robbery victim in order to return the additional one-fifth payment. The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** the additional **one-fifth** payment **is** considered **monetary restitution** rather than a fine. **And** accordingly, **if** the robber **died,** the **heirs** of the robber must **pay it** to the robbery victim.

The *mishnah* had stated that even after the *gazlan* has paid back the principal — *natan lo et ha-keren* — he remains obligated to pay the *chomesh*, the twenty-five percent additional payment, and must travel even to Madai to do so. The *gemara* draws an inference from this formulation.

### Chomesh Mammon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי: נָתַן אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְנִשְׁבַּע עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ – הֲרֵי מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ עַל חוֹמֶשׁ. אַלְמָא חוּמְשָׁא מָמוֹנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And we also learned** in the mishna that if the robber **gave** the robbery victim **the principal and takes** a false **oath** to him **concerning the** additional **one-fifth** payment, asserting that he had already paid it, the robber **adds** an additional **one-fifth** payment **apart from** the additional **one-fifth** payment about which he had taken a false oath. **Apparently,** the additional **one-fifth** payment **is monetary restitution,** as one is obligated to pay an additional one-fifth for taking a false oath only with regard to monetary restitution, not with regard to fines.

*Alma chomesh mamona hu* — it is evident from the *mishnah* that *chomesh* is treated as *mamon*, a straightforward monetary obligation, and not as a *kenas*, a punitive fine. This matters significantly for several reasons. First, a *kenas* cannot be collected from the thief's heirs; it is a personal penalty that dies with the person. But our *mishnah* implies that even if the thief dies, the heirs must still pay the *chomesh*, suggesting it is a transferable monetary debt rather than a personal fine. Second, the *gemara* cites a further *mishnah*: *natan et ha-keren v'nishba al ha-chomesh, harei mosif chomesh al ha-chomesh* — if the thief paid the principal and then swore falsely about the *chomesh*, he now owes an additional *chomesh* on the *chomesh* — that is, twenty-five percent of twenty-five percent, amounting to one-sixteenth. *Alma chumsha mamona hu* — only if the *chomesh* is itself a monetary obligation does it make sense for it to compound in this way. A *kenas* does not behave like a monetary debt capable of generating further liability through a false oath. The *chomesh* thus occupies an interesting intermediate space: the Torah has imposed what might technically be characterized as a penalty, yet the halachic system treats it with the full apparatus of ordinary monetary liability — including transmissibility to heirs and the capacity to generate compounded obligations. This parallels the analogous questions raised earlier in Bava Kamma about *chatzi nezek*, where similar tensions between the character of an obligation as *mamon* or *kenas* required careful analysis.

### Heirs Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ, וָמֵת – יוֹרְשִׁין מְשַׁלְּמִים קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וּפְטוּרִין מִן הָאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who robs another and takes** a false **oath to him** that he did not rob him, **and** then **dies,** his **heirs pay the principal and** additional **one-fifth** payments to the robbery victim, **but are exempt from** the obligation to bring **the guilt-offering.**

A *braita* confirms and sharpens the point: *hagozel et chaveiro v'nishba lo v'meit, yorshin meshalmin keren v'chomesh u'peturim min ha-asham* — one who steals from another, swears falsely about it, and then dies: his heirs must pay both the principal and the *chomesh*, but they are exempt from bringing the *korban asham*. The exemption from the *korban asham* is obvious — the *korban* is the personal atonement of the wrongdoer and cannot be transferred to his descendants. But the obligation to pay both *keren* and *chomesh* is transmitted, confirming that these are monetary debts in the full sense. *Yorshin b'nei shillumei chomsha d'avuon havu* — the heirs are indeed obligated to pay their father's *chomesh*.

## Son's Liability

### Son Pays Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹרְשִׁין בְּנֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי חוּמְשָׁא דַּאֲבוּהוֹן הָווּ – אַלְמָא חוֹמֶשׁ מָמוֹנָא הוּא, וּבָעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי יוֹרְשִׁין. וּרְמִינְהוּ, עֲדַיִין אֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵימָתַי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם חוֹמֶשׁ עַל גֶּזֶל אָבִיו – בִּזְמַן שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁבַּע לֹא הוּא וְלֹא אָבִיו;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** are **heirs obligated** to **pay the** additional **one-fifth** payment **of their father,** indicating that **apparently** the additional **one-fifth** payment **is monetary restitution, and** therefore the **heirs must pay** it? **But** one could **raise a contradiction** from a *baraita*. After having stated that one is required to add the additional one-fifth payment only for a robbery he commits himself, and not for a robbery committed by his father, the *baraita* states: **I** would **still say** that **when does** an heir **not pay** the additional **one-fifth** payment **for his father's robbery?** It is only **when neither he nor his father took** a false **oath.**

The *gemara* now encounters a tension with a contradictory *braita*: *adayin ani omer, eimasai eino meshalem chomesh al gezel aviv* — when does a son *not* have to pay *chomesh* for his father's theft? *Bizman shelo nishba lo hu v'lo aviv* — only when neither the father nor the son had sworn about it. The implication is that if at least one of them swore, the son would be obligated to pay the *chomesh*. The *braita* then asks: *hu v'lo aviv, aviv v'lo hu, hu v'aviv, minayin* — how do we know that any of these combinations — the son swore but not the father, the father swore but not the son, or both swore — obligates the son in *chomesh*? The answer is derived from the verse: *talmud lomar asher gazal v'asher asak* — the Torah's language encompasses the full range of the obligation.

### Admission Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא וְלֹא אָבִיו, אָבִיו וְלֹא הוּא, הוּא וְאָבִיו – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל וַאֲשֶׁר עָשָׁק" – וְהוּא לֹא גָּזַל וְלֹא עָשַׁק. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן שֶׁהוֹדָה, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא הוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** But if **he** took a false oath, **but not his father;** or if **his father** took a false oath, **but not he;** or if **he and his father** both took false oaths; **from where** is the heir's exemption derived? **The verse states:** "He shall restore the item **that he robbed," and** "the item **that he has acquired through exploitation"** (Leviticus 5:23), **and** in this case the heir **did not rob and did not exploit.** Since the verse states the obligation to pay the additional one-fifth payment when discussing one who robbed or exploited another, and the heir has done neither, he is exempt from payment. This *baraita* states that heirs are not obligated to pay the additional one-fifth payment. **Rav Naḥman said** that it is **not difficult. Here,** in the mishna and the first *baraita*, they discuss a case **where** the robber **admitted** his obligation and therefore was required to pay the additional one-fifth payment, and this obligation is transferred to his heir; **there,** in the latter *baraita*, it discusses a case **where he did not admit** his obligation and was never required to pay the additional one-fifth payment.

Rav Nachman resolves the apparent contradiction between the *braita* that obligates the heirs in *chomesh* and the *braita* that seems to condition the obligation on oath-taking: *lo kashya, kan she'hodeh, kan she'lo hodeh* — there is no contradiction. The difference turns on whether an admission was made. When there has been an admission, the analysis differs from a case where there has been none.

### Oath Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לֹא הוֹדָה, קֶרֶן נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁלֵּם! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁלֵּם; וְהָא מִדְּקָא מְהַדַּר אַחוֹמֶשׁ – לְמֵימְרָא דְּקֶרֶן מְשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer: **If** the latter *baraita* discusses a case where **he did not admit** his obligation, then the *halakha* would be that the heir **would not pay even the principal,** as the obligation is not known by anyone. **And if you would say** that **indeed** it is so **that he does not pay** the principal, **but** isn't it understood **from** the fact that the *tanna* **searches for** a source to teach the heir's exemption from only the additional **one-fifth** payment, **that** it means **to say that he does pay the principal.**

Specifically: *ei lo hodeh, keren nami lo meshalem* — if there was no admission, the son does not even pay the principal until witnesses come to establish the claim. The *chomesh* discussion must therefore be set against a backdrop in which the claim has been substantiated in some way, and the question is whether an oath was also taken that would generate the additional *chomesh* liability.

### Principal Always

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא, וַעֲדַיִין אֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵימָתַי הוּא מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן עַל גֶּזֶל אָבִיו – בִּזְמַן שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע הוּא וְאָבִיו. אָבִיו וְלֹא הוּא, הוּא וְלֹא אָבִיו, לֹא הוּא וְלֹא אָבִיו – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: גְּזֵילָה וָעוֹשֶׁק, אֲבֵידָה וּפִקָּדוֹן – יֵשׁ תַּלְמוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also proves that the heir must pay the principal from the continuation of the same *baraita*. **And further,** it **is taught** in that *baraita*: **And I** would **still say: When does** the heir **pay the principal for his father's robbery?** It is only **when he and his father** both **took** false **oaths.** But if **his father** took a false oath, **and not he;** or if **he** took a false oath, **but not his father;** or if **neither he nor his father** took false oaths; **from where** is the heir's obligation to pay the principal derived? **The verse states** the following terms: **"Robbery," and "exploitation," "lost item," and "deposit"** (Leviticus 5:23); and **there is a derivation [*yesh talmud*]** here to derive that the heir is obligated to pay the principal in all of these cases.

A parallel *braita* addresses the *keren* separately: *eimasai hu meshalem keren al gezel aviv* — when must the son pay even just the principal of his father's theft? *Bizman she'nishba hu v'aviv, o aviv v'lo hu, hu v'lo aviv* — when either the father or the son or both had sworn about it. But what if neither swore — *lo hu v'lo aviv, minayin* — how do we know the son must still pay the *keren*? The answer: *talmud lomar gezeila v'oshek aveida u'pikadon* — the Torah groups together stolen objects, cheated property, lost objects, and deposits, indicating that the underlying stolen property generates an obligation to return it regardless of whether any oath was taken. The *keren* attaches to the fact of the theft itself; the *chomesh* attaches to the oath.

### Yesh Talmud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: "יֵשׁ תַּלְמוּד" קָאָמַר מָר, אוֹ "יִשְׁתַּלְמוּ" קָאָמַר מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: "יֵשׁ תַּלְמוּד" קָאָמֵינָא, וּמֵרִיבּוּיָא דִּקְרָאֵי אֲמַרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And Rav Huna sat and stated this *halakha*,** and **Rabba, his son, said to him:** Was **the Master saying** that **there is a derivation [*yesh talmud*], or** was **the Master saying** that **they will be paid [*yishtalmu*]?** Rav Huna **said to him: I am saying: There is a derivation, and** it is **from the additional** expressions in the **verses** that **I say** it can be derived. It is apparent that an heir is obligated to pay the principal, and this refutes Rav Naḥman's attempted resolution.

The *braita* concludes with the cryptic phrase *yesh talmud* — which prompted the following exchange. Rav Huna was teaching this *braita* in session. His son Rabba challenged him: *yesh talmud ka'amar mar?* — Did you say, literally, "there is a Talmud"? *O yeshtalmu ka'amar mar?* — Or did you say "they shall pay," meaning *yeshtalmu*, which would make obvious syntactic sense as a conclusion to the list? Rav Huna replied: I said *yesh talmud*, precisely as I said. *U'miribbuyya de-krai amrei* — this phrase signals that the obligation is derived through a *midrash halacha*, an inclusion drawn from the grouping of terms in the relevant *pesukim*. The phrase *yesh talmud* is therefore a technical marker indicating that a *derashah* of the text is what extends the obligation to cases of *aveida* and *pikadon* alongside *gezeilah* and *oshek*.

### Rav Huna Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי "לֹא הוֹדָה"? לֹא הוֹדָה אָבִיו, וְהוֹדָה בְּנוֹ. וְנִחַיַּיב בְּנוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ אַשְּׁבוּעָה דִידֵיהּ! אָמְרִי: בְּשֶׁאֵין גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what** did Rav Naḥman mean when he suggested that the second *baraita* is referring to a case where **he did not admit** his obligation and exempts his heir from paying the additional one-fifth but obligates him to pay the principal? The *baraita* is referring to a case where the **father did not admit, but his son did admit,** and therefore he is obligated to pay the principal. The Gemara asks: **And** let **his son be liable** to pay the additional **one-fifth** payment **for his own** false **oath.** The Sages **say: In** the case of the *baraita*, it is **where** the **stolen item is no** longer **extant.**

The *gemara* then works through the remaining logical cases. If the father made no admission — *lo hoda aviv* — but the son admitted — *ve'hoda beno* — the son's own admission generates his liability for *chomesh* based on his own *shvu'a*. But what if the stolen property no longer exists? *Be'she'ein gezela kayyama* — if the physical stolen object is gone, the son cannot pay back even the *keren*, since he did not personally steal anything and has no personal liability for his father's theft absent a surviving object or some form of enforceable claim.

The *gemara* then considers whether an obligation that runs through land — *achrayut* — could fill this gap. But land is generally not subject to the laws of theft — *karka einah nigzelet* — and so *chomesh* would not apply to a claim grounded in real property. This is the explanation offered by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *lefi she'ein meshalmin chomesh al kaparat shibbud karka'ot* — one does not pay *chomesh* for a denial pertaining to the encumbrance of land. Alternatively, the *gemara* considers whether the case involved a loan made without a *shtar* — *milva al peh* — which ordinarily cannot be collected from heirs or from subsequent purchasers of property.

### Saddlebag Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּשֶׁאֵין גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת, אֲפִילּוּ קֶרֶן נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁלֵּם! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא אַחְרָיוּת. וְכִי אִיכָּא אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים מַאי הָוֵי? מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה הִיא, וּמִלְוָה עַל פֶּה אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין וְלֹא מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת! אָמְרִי: בְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין. אִי בְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין, אֲפִילּוּ חוֹמֶשׁ נָמֵי מְשַׁלֵּם! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין חוֹמֶשׁ עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת. רָבָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה דִּיסַקַּיָּיא שֶׁל אָבִיו מוּפְקֶדֶת בְּיַד אֲחֵרִים; קֶרֶן מְשַׁלֵּם – דְּהָא אִיתֵיהּ, חוֹמֶשׁ לָא מְשַׁלֵּם – דְּכִי אִישְׁתְּבַע בְּקוּשְׁטָא אִישְׁתְּבַע, דְּהָא לָא הֲוָה יָדַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **If in** the case of the *baraita* it is **where** the **stolen item is not extant,** the *halakha* should be that the heir **does not pay even** the **principal,** as the *halakha* is that while heirs must return an item stolen by their father, they are not obligated to compensate the owner if the item is no longer extant. The Gemara answers: **No,** the ruling **is necessary** in a case **where there is guaranteed** property, i.e., land, which the heirs inherited from the robber. Heirs are obligated to compensate the owner of a stolen item with inherited land, even if the stolen item itself is no longer extant. The Gemara asks: **And** even **when there is guaranteed property,** i.e., land, **what of it?** After all, such a debt **is** legally considered **a loan by oral** agreement, **and** the creditor of **a loan by oral** agreement **cannot collect from the heirs** of the debtor, **nor** can he collect **from those who purchased** land from the debtor. The Sages **say** in response: The reference here is to a case **in which** the father had already **stood trial** and was obligated to pay. In such a case, the debt is considered like a loan with a promissory note, which a creditor may collect from the debtor's heirs. The Gemara rejects this: **If** it is a case **in which** the father had already **stood trial** and was obligated to pay, then the heir, who took a false oath and later confessed, would have to **pay even** the additional **one-fifth** payment **as well,** as this would be akin to any monetary obligation; but the *baraita* rules that he is obligated to pay only the principal. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** The heir is exempt **because one does not pay** the additional **one-fifth** payment **for the denial of** a debt that is secured by **a lien on land. Rava said** a different explanation: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the stolen item is still extant, yet the heir is exempt because **his father's sack [*disakaya*]** containing the stolen item **was deposited in the possession of others.** Accordingly, the heir **pays the principal, since** the stolen item **is** extant, but **he does not pay** the additional **one-fifth** payment **because when he took an oath** that it was not in his possession, **he took an oath truthfully, as he did not know** that his father had stolen the item.

Rava offers a final, elegant resolution: *hacha be'mai askinan? Ke'gon she'hayta diskiya shel aviv mufkedet beyad acherim* — the case under discussion involves a situation in which the father's *diskiya* — a closed double saddlebag or pouch — was in someone else's possession. The exact contents of the bag were unknown to the son.

*Keren meshalem de'ha itei* — the son pays the *keren* because the physical object still exists and can be identified and returned. *Chomesh lo meshalem* — but he does not pay the *chomesh*, because *dechi ishtaba bekushta, ishtaba de'ha lo hava yada* — when he swore, he swore truthfully, in the sense that he genuinely did not know the contents of the closed bag. The son's oath about the theft was simultaneously true enough — in the sense that his ignorance was genuine — that it does not generate *chomesh* liability, yet false enough in its practical effect — given that a theft claim does attach to the bag — that it does not exempt him from paying the *keren*. This is Rava's proposed scenario: a case in which the son is liable for the principal but not the additional twenty-five percent, because his oath, while ultimately misleading, was not a knowing false oath of the kind that triggers *chomesh*.
