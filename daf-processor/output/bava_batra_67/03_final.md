# Bava Batra 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oil Press Sale

### Press Sale Basics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר בֵּית הַבַּד – מָכַר אֶת הַיָּם וְאֶת הַמֶּמֶל וְאֶת הַבְּתוּלוֹת, אֲבָל לֹא מָכַר אֶת הָעֲבִירִים וְאֶת הַגַּלְגַּל וְאֶת הַקּוֹרָה. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הוּא וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ״ – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹכֵר בֵּית הַבַּד – מָכַר אֶת הַקּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **who sells an olive press** without specifying what is included in the sale **has sold** with it **the *yam* and the *memel* and the *betulot*,** the immovable elements of the olive press. **But he has not sold** with it **the *avirim* and the *galgal* and the *kora*,** the movable utensils of the olive press. **When** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **it and everything that is in it, all these** components **are sold** along with the olive press, even the movable utensils. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** One **who sells an olive press has sold the *kora*** as well, as it is the most fundamental element of the olive press.

The daf opens on 67b with a continuation of the Mishnah's systematic treatment of sales: when one sells a particular piece of property or commercial installation, which components are included by default, and which require an explicit, broader formulation? The governing principle established in prior sugyot is that when selling real property — a home, a field, or a commercial structure — only those items that are physically attached to the ground are transferred as part of the sale. Movable implements, however intimately connected to the operation, do not transfer unless the seller employs an expansive formula such as *hu ve-khol mah she-betocho* ("it and everything in it"). Even that broader phrase captures items that are practically and conceptually bound up with the property's function — not random contents that happen to be on the premises.

With this framework in mind, the Mishnah turns to the *beit ha-bad*, the oil press.

### Press Components

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״יָם״ – טְלָפְחָא. ״מֶמֶל״ – אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: מַפְרַכְתָּא. ״בְּתוּלוֹת״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּלוֹנְסוֹת שֶׁל אֶרֶז, שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בָּהֶן אֶת הַקּוֹרָה. ״עֲבִירִים״ – כִּבְשֵׁי. ״גַּלְגַּל״ – חוּמַרְתָּא. ״קוֹרָה״ – קוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the terms used in the mishna. The ***yam*** is the **lentil,** the round stationary container into which the olives are placed before being crushed. As for the ***memel*, Rabbi Abba bar Memel said:** This is the **crusher,** the utensil used to pound and crush the olives. As for the ***betulot*, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** These are the **cedar posts [*klonsot*] that support the beam** of the olive press. The ***avirim*** are the **pressers,** wooden boards that are placed on top of the crushed olives, and upon which the beam is lowered in order to press the olives. The ***galgal*** is the ***ḥumrata*,** a round stone that is placed on the beam to weigh it down. The ***kora*** is the heavy wooden **beam** used to press down upon the olives and thereby extract the oil.

Understanding what the Mishnah includes or excludes requires some reconstruction of the physical layout of an ancient oil press. According to the Rashbam, olive pressing proceeded in three distinct stages. First, the olives underwent an initial crushing — a mortar-and-pestle type operation. Second, they were ground further in a *reichayim*, a millstone arrangement. Third, and most critically, the crushed olives were subjected to sustained pressure from a massive *kora*, a heavy beam, which was lowered onto them and left to press out the remaining oil over time. It is the *kora* that is the defining piece of equipment of the *beit ha-bad*.

### Yam, Mamal, Betulot

The Mishnah states that when one sells a *beit ha-bad*, one sells the *yam*, the *mamal*, and the *betulot*. The Gemara identifies the *yam* — literally "sea" — as the *tlafcha*, a lentil-shaped trough or cistern into which all the pressed olive oil collects. The name apparently refers to its shape. The *mamal*: Rav Abba bar Mamal identifies it with the *mafrachta*, the crusher. The Rashbam explains that this refers to the mortar-like structure fixed to the ground that enables the initial crushing of the olives — the structure itself is attached, even if the pestle-like implement used within it is not. Because it is fixed to the ground, it is sold with the press. The *betulot* — literally "virgins," a somewhat opaque designation — Rabbi Yochanan explains as cedar poles, *konesot shel erez*, that are embedded in the ground and serve to support and stabilize the structure through which the *kora* is raised and lowered. These poles, being fixed to the ground, are included in the sale.

### Avirim, Galgal, Kora

By contrast, the Mishnah lists three items that are *not* sold as part of the default sale: the *avirim*, the *galgal*, and the *kora*. The Rashbam explains the *avirim* as planks — *nisarim* — that are placed on top of the olives beneath the *kora* in order to more effectively distribute the pressure. Because these planks are not fixed to the ground and can be removed and used elsewhere, they are not sold automatically. The *galgal* — literally a wheel — the Gemara explains as a *chumresa*, the mechanism used to raise and lower the *kora*. Whether understood as a pulley system or, more precisely, as a screw-type device (as modern scholarship on ancient oil press technology would suggest), this apparatus is not affixed to the ground and therefore does not transfer. Finally, and most strikingly, the *kora* itself — the enormous beam that is the very heart of the press's operation — is also not sold by default, for it too rests without being fixed to the ground.

### Kol Ma Shebetocho

The Mishnah continues: if the seller said *hu ve-khol mah she-betocho*, "it and everything in it," then *harei kullan mekhurין* — all of these items are sold. Rabbi Eliezer dissents on one point: even without the expansive formula, the seller of a *beit ha-bad* has sold the *kora*. His reasoning is explicit — *she-lo nikra beit ha-bad ela al shem ha-kora*: the very name and identity of an oil press is defined by its beam. The *kora* is so central, so constitutive of what a *beit ha-bad* is, that one cannot plausibly be understood to have sold the press while retaining the beam. Rabbi Eliezer's position is limited to the *kora* alone; he does not extend this logic to the other movable equipment.

### Mamal & Mafrachta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹכֵר בֵּית הַבַּד – מָכַר אֶת [הַ]נְּסָרִים, וְאֶת הַיְּקָבִים, וְאֶת הַמַּפְרֵכוֹת, וְאֶת הָרֵיחַיִם הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת – אֲבָל לֹא הָעֶלְיוֹנָה. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הוּא וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ״ – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – לֹא מָכַר לֹא אֶת הָעֲבִירִים, וְלֹא אֶת הַשַּׂקִּין, וְלֹא אֶת הַמַּרְצוּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, 3:2): One **who sells an olive press** without specifying what is included in the sale **has sold** with it **the boards** that are fixed in place to hold the olives; **and the vats** for collecting the oil, **and the crushers,** used to pound and crush the olives before pressing them, **and the lower millstone,** i.e., the stationary millstone base; **but not the upper** millstone. **But when** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **it and everything that is in it, all these** components **are sold. Both** in **this** case, where he executes the sale without specification, **and** in **that** case, where he adds the phrase that he is selling everything that is in the olive press, **he has not sold the** wooden **boards** that are placed on top of the crushed olives when they are being pressed, **nor** has he sold **the sacks, nor** has he sold **the leather bags** for carrying the olives, as these are all movable goods that are not specifically part of the olive press.

The Gemara's identification of the *mamal* with the *mafrachta* invites a closer look at the three-stage process the Rashbam describes. Stage one is the initial crushing, akin to a mortar and pestle. Stage two is the grinding in a *reichayim*, a millstone. Stage three is the sustained pressing under the *kora*. The *mafrachta* corresponds to the first stage — specifically, the fixed structure (the mortar) into which the crushing implements are applied. The Rashbam considers and rejects the possibility that *mafrachta* refers simply to the striking implement itself, since that would not be attached to the ground and thus its sale with the press would require separate explanation. The fixed mortar structure, however, is clearly *mekhubar le-karka* and thus transfers automatically.

### Betulot Poles

Rabbi Yochanan's identification of the *betulot* as cedar poles supporting the *kora* mechanism is consistent with the visual logic of the press. These poles, planted in the ground, hold the superstructure through which the beam is manipulated. Being fixed in the earth, they are unambiguously part of what transfers in a standard sale — no expansive language is needed. The poetic designation "virgins" for these structural poles remains unexplained by the Gemara, but the functional identification is clear.

### Avirim & Galgal

The *avirim* — which we may render as "pressers" — are planks laid over the olives to distribute the *kora*'s weight. The *galgal*, per the Rashbam, is the raising-and-lowering mechanism: *vin be-la'az*, he writes, *she-megalgalin bah et ha-kora le-ha'alot* — a device by which the beam is wound up or lowered. Whether this is precisely a screw or another mechanical arrangement, the point is that it is removable and therefore not sold. The *kora* itself, as noted, is also movable — massive and weighty, but not sunk into the ground — and it too is excluded from the automatic sale, except according to Rabbi Eliezer.

### Baraita: Equipment

A *baraita* then elaborates on what *is* sold when selling a *beit ha-bad*, presenting a richer list than the Mishnah alone. The *baraita* states: one sells the *nisarim* (planks), the *yekavim* (vats — the same root as *yekev*, used in the Torah for wine and oil collection: *mi-gornekha u-mi-yikvekha*), the *mafrakhot* (the mortar structures), and the *reichayim ha-takhtonim* (the lower millstones). The qualification regarding millstones is significant: *aval lo et ha-elyonah* — the upper millstone is not sold, because the upper stone is movable while the lower is fixed. Here again, *kol mah she-betocho* would include everything; without it, only the fixed lower stone transfers. When the seller does say everything, the *baraita* confirms *harei kullan mekhurін*, all is sold — but even then, the *avirim* are still excluded, because those planks, while certainly used in the press, are the kind of implement one might take elsewhere; they are not seen as sufficiently bound up with the fixed apparatus of the press itself. Similarly, sacks and leather bags (*sakkim* and *martzufim*) remain with the seller — these are purely incidental to the fixed operation.

### Planks Debate

A tension emerges between the Mishnah and the *baraita* regarding planks. In the Mishnah, the *avirim* (planks used to press the olives down) are among the items *not* sold even when *kol mah she-betocho* is said. Yet the *baraita* seems to include *nisarim* among the items that *are* sold. The Rashbam resolves this by distinguishing between two different types of planks. The *avirim* of the Mishnah are the loose pressers placed atop the olives — portable, detachable, and usable elsewhere. The *nisarim* of the *baraita*, by contrast, are planks fixed around the base of the pressing area to prevent the olives from scattering when the *kora* bears down on them — *ke-ein nisarim she-notnim saviv ha-zeitim be-veit ha-bad kedei she-lo yitpazru anah va-anah be-khevid ha-kora aleihem*. The Rashbam adds that these latter planks are affixed to the ground, *me-khubbarim sham*, and thus they transfer automatically. The two categories occupy different positions along the spectrum of attachment, which explains why the Mishnah and *baraita* appear to diverge.

## Bathhouse Sale

### Bathhouse Basics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הַמֶּרְחָץ – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַנְּסָרִין, וְאֶת הַסְּפָלִין, וְאֶת הַבֵּלָנִיּוֹת. בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הִיא וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ״ – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – לֹא מָכַר לֹא אֶת הַמְּגוּרוֹת שֶׁל מַיִם, וְלֹא אֶת אוֹצָרוֹת שֶׁל עֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **who sells a bathhouse** without specifying what is included in the sale **has not sold** with it **the boards** that are placed on the floor, **nor** has he sold **the basins or the curtains [*habilaniyot*]. When** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **it and everything that is in it, all these** components **are sold** along with the bathhouse. **Both** in **this** case, where he executes the sale without specification, **and** in **that** case, where he adds the phrase that he is selling everything that is in the bathhouse, **he has not sold the tanks of water, nor** has he sold **the storerooms for wood,** as an explicit sales agreement is required for these matters.

The Roman bathhouse had a characteristic three-room structure: an outer vestibule where patrons arrived clothed; a middle room serving as the changing area; and an inner room — the steam bath or sauna — in which bathers sat or stood on wooden planks elevated above smoldering coals, with steam rising as water was poured over the heated stone or coals beneath.

### Bathhouse Non-Sales

When one sells a *merchatz*, the Mishnah specifies what is *not* transferred. The *nesarim* — the wooden planks on which bathers stand — are not sold. Counterintuitively, these planks are apparently not nailed down. This makes functional sense: the planks need to be lifted periodically to allow access to the coal pit beneath for stoking and maintenance, and in a facility built primarily of stone, nailing would not be practical in any case. Because they are not fixed, they do not transfer. Similarly excluded are the *sfalim* — either basins or pitchers used for pouring water — and the *balaniyot* (or, in some textual traditions, *vilayot*), which the Mishnah identifies as the towels.

### Bathhouse Inclusivity

If the seller used the formula *hi ve-khol mah she-betochah*, "it and everything in it," then *harei kullan mekhurін* — the planks, the basins, the towels, all are sold.

### Supplies Excluded

However — and this qualification holds regardless of which formula is used — *ubein kakh u-vein kakh*, neither formulation transfers the supplies that feed the bathhouse. The *magorot shel mayim* (the Rashbam reads this as *bereikhot mayim*, pools of water), the water sources or reservoirs that supply the bathhouse, are not sold. Nor are the *otzarot shel etzim*, the wood sheds that store fuel for the coals. The distinction is between equipment and supplies: the planks, the basins, and even the towels are the bathhouse's *kelim*, its functional apparatus, and those transfer when broadly worded language is used. But the consumable inputs — water and wood — and the structures that house them are a separate commercial matter entirely.

### Storage Rooms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הַמֶּרְחָץ – מָכַר אֶת בֵּית הַנְּסָרִין, וְאֶת בֵּית הַיְּקָמִין, וְאֶת בֵּית הַסְּפָלִים, וְאֶת בֵּית הַוִּילָאוֹת; אֲבָל לֹא אֶת נְסָרִין עַצְמָן, וְלֹא יְקָמִין עַצְמָן, וְלֹא סְפָלִים עַצְמָן, וְלֹא וִילָאוֹת עַצְמָן. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הִיא וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ״ – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ, לֹא מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַבְּרֵיכוֹת הַמְסַפְּקוֹת לוֹ מַיִם – בֵּין בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וּבִימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְלֹא בֵּית כִּינּוּס הָעֵצִים. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״בֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ וְכׇל תַּשְׁמִישָׁיו אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, 3:3): One **who sells a bathhouse** without specifying what is included in the sale **has sold** with it **the storeroom** for **the boards, and the storeroom** for **the** implements called ***yekamin*, and the storeroom for the basins, and the storeroom for the curtains [*vilaot*], but** he has **not** sold **the boards themselves, nor the *yekamin* themselves, nor the basins themselves, nor the curtains themselves. When** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **it and everything that is in it, all these** components **are sold** along with the bathhouse. **Both** in **this** case, where he executes the sale without specification, **and** in **that** case, where he adds the phrase that he is selling everything that is in the bathhouse, **he has not sold him the pools that supply him with water, whether in the summer season or in the rainy season, nor** has he sold him **the storeroom for the wood. But if** the seller **says to** the buyer: **I am selling you the bathhouse and all of its accompaniments, all these** components **are sold.**

A *baraita* elaborates on what the Mishnah left underdeveloped, specifying what *is* sold in the default case. The *baraita* lists: *beit ha-nesarim* (the room where planks are stored at the end of the day), *beit ha-yakmin* (the Rashbam, after considering and rejecting another reading, identifies *yakmin* as head-towels, i.e., the room where head-towels are stored), *beit ha-sfalim* (the room for the basins or benches — and the reading *safsalim*, "benches," which are built into the stone, may be preferable), and *beit ha-vilayot* (the room for the body-towels). The *chiddush* here is substantial: one might have supposed that selling the bathhouse conveyed only the bathhouse itself — its three rooms — while the adjacent storage sheds or closets remained the seller's. The *baraita* teaches otherwise. These storage rooms, built to house the bathhouse's rotating equipment, are so integrally connected to the bathhouse's operation that they are included in the sale even without expansive language. They are *mekhubar le-karka* and serve the *merchatz* directly.

### Rooms vs. Contents

The *baraita* then states with equal clarity what is *not* sold: *aval lo et ha-nesarim atzmam, ve-lo et ha-yakmin atzmam, ve-lo sfalim atzmam, ve-lo et ha-vilayot atzmam* — the rooms transfer, but not the items stored within them. You acquire the storage closet, not the towels and planks inside it. When the seller says *hu ve-khol mah she-betochah*, then everything — rooms and contents alike — is sold. Even then, however, the water sources and wood sheds remain excluded: *im machar et ha-breichot ha-maspekot lo mayim* — the pools that supply the water, *bein bi-ymot ha-chamah bein bi-ymot ha-geshamim*, whether for summer or winter use, are not transferred; nor is *beit kinus ha-etzim*, the woodshed.

### Tashmishim Language

The *baraita* introduces a third, still-broader formula: *im amar lo beit merchatz ve-khol tashmishav ani mokher lakh* — "the bathhouse and all its *tashmishim*," all its appurtenances. With this formulation, *kullan mekhurін* — everything, including all the equipment and storage rooms, is fully transferred. The word *tashmishim* designates the things that serve and attend to the facility, and it is an even more expansive term than *kol mah she-betochah*. This distinction will immediately become significant.

## Sesame Seeds Case

### Store & Sesame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״בֵּית הַבַּד וְכׇל תַּשְׁמִישָׁיו אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״. הֲוַיָא הָנְהוּ חַנְוָאתָא אַבָּרַאי, דַּהֲווֹ שָׁטְחוּ בְּהוּ שׁוּמְשְׁמֵי. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person **who said to another: I am selling you** this **olive press and all of its accompaniments. There were certain stores outside** of the olive press, **where,** in addition to the ordinary services that these stores provided, **sesame seeds would** also **be spread out** to dry before they would be pressed for their oil. The seller and the buyer disagreed about whether these stores were included in the sale, and the buyer **came before Rav Yosef,** presenting him with his claim to ownership of the stores.

The Gemara now presents a case that tests the limits of *kol tashmishav*. A seller said to a buyer: *beit ha-bad ve-khol tashmishav ani mokher lakh* — "the oil press and all its appurtenances." The question is whether two features of this particular *beit ha-bad* complex are included. First, there were shops (*chanvatah*) situated just outside the press complex — perhaps a concession operation serving the workers, selling food and provisions. Second, on the roof of those shops, sesame seeds were being dried, apparently in preparation for pressing them into sesame oil in the same equipment. Does the expanded formula *kol tashmishav* reach these?

The Rashbam is careful to analyze both features independently. The rooftop drying of sesame seeds might seem like a genuine *tashmish* of the *beit ha-bad* — those seeds are destined to be pressed in the same facility. But the store itself sells bread, wine, and provisions to workers: *eila ikkar tashmishan li-mkhor pat ve-yayin hu* — its primary function is an independent commercial enterprise, not the servicing of the press. Even if the press's workers patronize it, a store that generates its own revenue from its own customers is not a *tashmish* of the *beit ha-bad* in any meaningful sense.

### Tashmishim Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵינָא, אִם אָמַר: ״בֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ וְכׇל תַּשְׁמִישָׁיו אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֵין כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָזֵינַן – אִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״בֵּית הַבַּד וְכׇל תַּשְׁמִישָׁיו, וְאִלֵּין מִצְרָנַהָא״ – קָנֵי, וְאִי לָא – לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: We learned** in the previously cited *baraita* that **if** the seller **says** to the buyer: **I am selling you the bathhouse and all of its accompaniments, all these** components **are sold.** Rav Yosef held that in this case too, the disputed stores were sold. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** in a *baraita*: **They are not all sold? Rather,** the issue should be resolved as **Rav Ashi said: We consider** the seller's statement, and **if he said to** the buyer: I am selling you **the olive press and all of its accompaniments, and these are its boundaries,** and he included the area of the stores within those boundaries, the buyer **has acquired** those stores, **but if** the seller does **not** say this, **he has not acquired** them, as they are not actually part of the olive press.

The parties came before Rav Yosef. He ruled that based on the *baraita* about the bathhouse — *im amar beit ha-merchatz ve-khol tashmishav ani mokher lakh, harei kullan mekhurIn* — the formula *kol tashmishav* is an expansive one, and even ambiguous appendages like this store and its rooftop should be included. The argument, while not fully explicit, seems to be that *tashmishav* is meant to sweep broadly, capturing everything that attends to the press's operation in any connected way.

### Abaye Limits

Abaye challenged Rav Yosef from a *baraita* taught by Rabbi Hiyya: *ein kullan mekhurIn* — not everything is sold even when *tashmishav* is said. This demonstrates that the category of *tashmish* has inherent limits; some things remain outside it even under the broadest formula.

### Boundaries Rule

Rav Ashi offered the resolution: *chazina* — we assess the facts on the ground. If the seller said *beit ha-bad ve-khol tashmishav* and then drew boundaries — *eilein mitzranah* — that explicitly encompassed the shops and the rooftop area, then the buyer acquires them: *kani*. If not, *lo kani*, he does not. One might ask: is it not trivially true that drawing explicit boundaries makes the included items part of the sale? The answer is that earlier discussions in this tractate established that boundary-drawing language is sometimes used merely to identify which property is being sold, not to expand the sale beyond its natural scope — *metzarim hirchiv lo*. Here, however, when the seller has also employed the expansive language *kol tashmishav*, the combination of expansive formula plus explicit boundary-drawing is definitive: those ambiguous adjacent enterprises are sold.

## Town Sale (II)

### Town Structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הָעִיר – מָכַר בָּתִּים; בּוֹרוֹת, שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת; מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת וְשׁוֹבָכוֹת; בֵּית הַבַּדִּין וּבֵית הַשְּׁלָחִין; אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַמִּטַּלְטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **who sells a city** without specifying what is included in the sale **has sold** with it **the houses, the pits,** the **ditches and caves, the bathhouses and the dovecotes,** and **the olive presses and *beit hashelaḥin*,** as will be explained in the Gemara, **but** he has **not** sold **the movable property** in the city.

The Mishnah now presents what is, from a certain vantage point, a striking case: the sale of an entire *ir*, a town. One is reminded that *ir* in rabbinic usage denotes a town rather than a large city (*krach*); a *kfar* is a village below that. But even at the scale of a town, it is conceptually striking that a single individual could own it entirely — though parallels to company towns are not hard to find.

When one sells an *ir*, the Mishnah teaches that one has sold: the houses (*batim*), the wells and cisterns (*borot, shichin u-me'arot*), the bathhouses (*merchatza'ot*), the pigeon coops (*shovakhot*), the oil presses (*batei badim*), and the *beit ha-shlachin* — the irrigated fields. In short, all the land and all the structures — residential, commercial, industrial — transfer. The variety of these categories is notable: the Mishnah does not distinguish between homes and enterprises, between wells and bathhouses.

### Movables & Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַמִּטַּלְטְלִין. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הִיא וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ״ – אֲפִילּוּ הָיוּ בָּהּ בְּהֵמָה וַעֲבָדִים, הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הָעִיר – מָכַר אֶת הַסַּנְטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **But when** the seller **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **it and everything that is in it, even if there were cattle and** Canaanite **slaves in** the city, **all these** entities **are sold. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** One **who sells a city has sold** with it **the *santar*,** the meaning of which will be explained in the Gemara.

Nevertheless, *aval lo et ha-metaltalin* — movable property is not included. Even an all-encompassing sale of an entire town does not, by default, sweep up its contents. If the seller said *hi ve-khol mah she-betochah*, however, then all the contents transfer as well — subject, of course, to the seller actually owning those items. Whatever belongs to the townspeople cannot be sold by the town's owner.

The Mishnah extends the *kol mah she-betochah* formula further: *afilu hayu bah behemah va-avadim, harei kullan mekhurIn* — even animals and slaves are included. This is the *chiddush*. Why would one have thought these might be different? Because animals and slaves can move on their own volition — they are not stationary fixtures of the town the way a building or even a barrel would be. Something that moves independently seems intuitively less "contained in" the town than something that stays put. The Mishnah therefore teaches that even these self-moving entities are sold when the comprehensive formula is used.

The Rashbam notes that once this formula is in play, even food products are included — the formula is fully comprehensive.

### Animals & Slaves

Rabban Gamliel adds: *ha-mokher et ha-ir machar et ha-santar* — one who sells a town has also sold the *santar*. The meaning of this term is itself the subject of Gemara debate.

### Santar Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״סַנְטֵר״? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא. שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אַבְטוּלְמוֹס אוֹמֵר: בָּאגֵי. מַאן דְּאָמַר בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בָּאגֵי; מַאן דְּאָמַר בָּאגֵי – אֲבָל בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא לָא מִיזְדַּבַּן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term ***santar*? Here** in Babylonia **they interpreted** it to mean the land **registrar [*bar maḥavanita*]** in charge of keeping track of property boundaries. **Shimon ben Avtolemos** disagrees and **says** that it is referring to **the fields** that surround the city. The Gemara comments: **The one who says** that *santar* means the land **registrar** understands that according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, when one sells a city, **all the more so** are **the fields** that surround the city included in the sale. **But the one who says** that it means **the fields** that surround the city holds that the land **registrar** is **not sold** with the city.

The Gemara records two interpretive traditions. The first, cited as the local Palestinian interpretation: *bar machvanita* — some type of person, whom the Rashbam identifies as a slave whose function is to know and report the boundary markers and property records of the town: *eved ha-munneh le-haggid u-le-achvuyei techumi ha-sadot*. This slave serves as a living registry of the town's property arrangements — a registrar, embedded in the commercial and civic life of the town, owned by the town's owner, and indispensable to its functioning: *ve-i efshar le-ir be-lo oto*. Shimon ben Aptolmos offers the second interpretation: *bagi* — an irrigated field attached to the town. On this reading, the *santar* is not a person but land.

## Town Sale (II)

### Slave Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ, עַבְדָּא כְּמִטַּלְטְלָא דָּמֵי; דְּאִי כִּמְקַרְקַע דָּמֵי, נִיזְדַּבַּן אַגַּב מָתָא! וְאֶלָּא מַאי, עַבְדָּא כְּמִטַּלְטְלָא דָּמֵי?! מַאי אֲפִילּוּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Avya, said to Rav Ashi: Learn from** the mishna that the legal status of a Canaanite **slave is like** that of **movable property, as if** it is **like** that of **land,** the slave **should be sold along with the city.** Rav Ashi responded: **Rather, what** do you claim, that the legal status of a Canaanite **slave is like** that of **movable property?** If that is the case, **what** is the meaning of the mishna's statement that **even** if there were cattle and Canaanite slaves in the city, they are all sold? This is obvious, as the slaves should be treated no differently than the rest of the city's movable property.

Rav Acha bar Rav Avya raised a sharp question to Rav Ashi from the Mishnah's treatment of slaves. The Mishnah teaches that slaves are not automatically sold with the town even though all *karka* (landed property) is sold automatically. May we infer from this that *avda ke-mitaltlei dami* — that slaves are legally classified as chattel rather than land? For if slaves were considered *karka*, they should transfer automatically *agav mata*, as part of the land, just as houses and fields do.

### Movement Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – שָׁאנֵי בֵּין מִטַּלְטְלָא דְנָיֵיד מִמִּטַּלְטְלָא דְּלָא נָיֵיד; אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא עַבְדָּא כִּמְקַרְקַע דָּמֵי – שָׁאנֵי בֵּין מְקַרְקַע דְּנָיֵיד, לִמְקַרְקַע דְּלָא נָיֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say?** You must explain that there **is a difference between movable property that moves about** by itself, such as slaves, **and movable property that does not move about** by itself, i.e., inanimate objects. In exactly the same manner, one can claim that **even** if **you say** that the legal status of a Canaanite **slave is like** that of **land,** there **is a difference between land that moves about** by itself, i.e., slaves, **and land that does not move about** by itself.

The Gemara pushes back. Even granting that the question is well posed, the inference does not follow. The response: *ella mai ait lakh le-meimar* — even if you want to say *avda ke-mitaltlei dami*, why does the Mishnah say *afilu* ("even") slaves are sold when *kol mah she-betochah* is used? The word *afilu* signals that there was reason to think slaves would not be included. But if slaves were simply ordinary chattel, their inclusion would need no special emphasis — ordinary chattel transfers with *kol mah she-betochah* without any drama.

The answer: *shani be-mitaltlei de-nayyadi mi-mitaltlei de-lo nayyadi* — there is a meaningful difference between movable property that moves on its own and movable property that does not. Chattel that sits still is, in some sense, "contained" within the town. A slave who walks around on his own volition is conceptually detached from the town's fixed domain. That conceptual detachment could equally apply whether he is classified as *karka* or *metaltalin* — the self-movement is what creates the intuition that he falls outside *kol mah she-betochah*, not his legal categorization. *Afilu teima de-ke-karka dami* — even if you say slaves are legally land, you might have thought they were excluded from the town sale because their movement makes them conceptually distinct. *Shani bein karka de-nayyadi le-karka de-lo nayyadi* — there is a difference between land that moves and land that does not. The Mishnah's *afilu* therefore proves nothing about the fundamental legal classification of slaves.

## Santar Debate (II)

### Santar Scribe

The Gemara now examines the two interpretations of *santar* in detail. The Rashbam understands *bar machvanita* — picking up on the Aramaic root meaning "to tell" or "to speak" — as a slave who serves as the town's living records keeper, articulating (*le-achvuyei*) the boundaries of the various fields and properties. This slave's role is uniquely tied to the town's identity and governance; he is owned by the *sar ha-ir*, the town's master, and his knowledge is, in a sense, what makes orderly civic life possible.

### Santar Guard

Rabbeinu Chananel offers a different reading: *santar hu shomer ha-ir ve-notra de-kharmei* — the *santar* is the town's guard, the sentry who watches over the fields and vineyards. This reading connects *santar* to the Latin *santarius* ("guardsman of fields") or a related Greek form, and *santar* may indeed be a loanword from that root — Rashi too identifies a Greek or Latin etymology. This interpretation has the advantage of linguistic transparency: "sentry" is a recognizable function, and the word *santar* maps cleanly onto it. One might also note that the Aramaic *bar machvanita* (literally "son of the jaw/mouth") could itself be a folk-etymological reading that the Rashbam then builds upon, while the actual word's origin is the Greek/Latin sentry.

### Santar Field

Shimon ben Aptolmos reads *santar* as *bagi* — an irrigated field. On this reading, what Rabban Gamliel is adding to the default town sale is not a person but a tract of land — specifically the irrigated fields lying beyond the town's borders. This interpretation creates a clear hierarchy of difficulty: the irrigated fields adjacent to the town are land, and land normally transfers; the *chiddush* would be that these fields, even lying outside the town proper, are considered appurtenant to it. A human being, by contrast, is not normally sold with real estate at all.

### Field vs. Person

The Gemara observes that the two readings generate opposite hierarchies of *chiddush*. If *santar* means a person (*bar machvanita*), then the *tanna kamma* already includes the irrigated fields (*beit ha-shlachin*) in the town sale, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's addition is that even the sentry — a person — is also sold. The greater novelty is the person. If *santar* means the irrigated field, then the *tanna kamma* refers to orchards (*ginunita*, reading *shlachin* from *shilchayikh pardes rimonim*), and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds that even the more distant irrigated fields beyond the orchard are sold. These two readings are shown to be internally consistent with the broader Mishnaic structure, and the Gemara presses each one to see whether the debate's contours make sense.

---

## Remnants & Suburbs

### Yehuda's Terms

Rabbi Yehuda enters the discussion with a different set of positions: *santar eino machur, aval ankolmus machur* — you do not sell the *santar*, but you do sell the *ankolmus*. The Rashbam derives *ankolmus* from *kulmus* (quill), identifying him as the town's scribe. This scribe has a defined role in the town's administrative and commercial life — recording transactions, maintaining documents — and Rabbi Yehuda holds that this figure transfers with the town while the *santar* does not.

### Both Persons

The Gemara initially presses: if the *ankolmus* is a person, surely the *santar* is also a person, and we are simply debating which slave is sold with the town. The Gemara does not accept this inference as determinative — *midi irya?* — and notes that there is no formal requirement that the two terms occupy the same category. It remains possible, on some readings, that *santar* is a field while *ankolmus* is a person, and Rabbi Yehuda is making a claim that you sell a person but not the field. The Gemara, however, finds this difficult to sustain.

### Non-Included Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: סַבְרוּהָ, מַאי ״שְׁלָחִין״ – בָּאגֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשֹׁלֵחַ מַיִם עַל פְּנֵי חוּצוֹת״; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא – אֲמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: בָּאגֵי מִיזְדַּבְּנִי, בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא לָא מִיזְדַּבַּן; וַאֲתָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּר מַחְווֹנִיתָא נָמֵי מִיזְדַּבַּן. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּאגֵי – תַּנָּא קַמָּא נָמֵי הָכִי קָאָמַר! מִי סָבְרַתְּ מַאי ״שְׁלָחִין״ – בָּאגֵי?! לָא; מַאי ״שְׁלָחִין״ – גִּינוּנְיָיתָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁלָחַיִךְ פַּרְדֵּס רִמּוֹנִים״; אֲבָל בָּאגֵי – לָא מִיזְדַּבְּנִי. וַאֲתָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר: אֲפִילּוּ בָּאגֵי נָמֵי מִזְדַּבְּנִי. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: סַבְרוּהָ, מַאי ״שְׁלָחִין״ – גִּינוּנְיָאתָא; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּאגֵי – אָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: גְּנוּנָיְיָתָא מִיזְדַּבְּנִי, בָּאגֵי לָא מִיזְדַּבְּנִי; וַאֲתָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר: אֲפִילּוּ בָּאגֵי מִיזְדַּבְּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that the discussion took place as follows: The Sages initially **assumed** that **what** is meant by ***shelaḥin*?** This is referring to irrigated **fields,** fields that require additional irrigation to supplement the rain that they receive. **As it is written:** "Who gives rain upon the earth **and sends [*shole'aḥ*] water upon the fields"** (Job 5:10). **Granted, according to the one who says** that *santar* means the land **registrar, the first *tanna*** of the mishna **said** that **the fields** that surround the city **are sold** with the city, but the land **registrar is not sold, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel comes to say** that **even** the land **registrar is sold. But according to the one who says** that *santar* means **fields, this** is what **the first *tanna* is saying as well.** In what way, then, does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagree with the first *tanna*? The Gemara rejects this proof: **Do you maintain** that **what** is meant by ***shelaḥin*** is irrigated **fields?** This is **not** the case. Rather, **what** is meant by ***shelaḥin*?** This is referring to **gardens** found within the city, **as it is stated: "Your shoots [*shelaḥayikh*] are an orchard of pomegranates"** (Song of Songs 4:13). **But the fields** that surround the city **are not sold. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel comes to say** that **even** the **fields are sold as well.** This is one version of the discussion. **Some say** that the discussion took place as follows: The Sages initially **assumed** that **what** is meant by ***shelaḥin*?** This is referring to **gardens** found within the city. **Granted, according to the one who said** that *santar* means **the fields** that surround the city, **the first *tanna*** of the mishna **said** that **the gardens** found within the city **are sold** along with the city, but **the fields** that surround the city **are not sold, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel comes to say** that **even the fields** that surround the city **are sold.**

The *baraita* appended to Rabbi Yehuda's statement lists what is definitively not included in a town sale. A careful examination of the Gemara's discussion of *santar* yields the following: The *shi'areiha* — "its remnants" — requires definition. The Gemara asks: *mai shi'areiha?* What are these remnants? They are called *bizlei*. What is *bizlei*? Rabbi Abba explains: *piskei bagi* — strips of irrigated fields. These are not the contiguous irrigated fields immediately adjacent to the town, but more distant, outlying strips that are separated from the main fields by some natural or artificial break. Even in a town sale, those far strips are not conveyed.

### Distant Fields

This creates an apparent tension. If Rabbi Yehuda says the *santar* is not sold, and the *santar* (on the "field" reading) means irrigated fields, then Rabbi Yehuda holds that the irrigated fields are not sold. But the *baraita* indicates that the distant *piskei bagi* are not sold — implying that the standard *bagi* are sold. The Gemara resolves this by reversing Rabbi Yehuda's formulation: *afoch* — *santar machur, ankolmus lo machur*. That is, Rabbi Yehuda actually holds that the *santar* (the irrigated fields) is sold, while the *ankolmus* (the scribe) is not. The *baraita* then makes sense: the standard fields transfer; only the outlying strips do not. The Gemara then asks whether Rabbi Yehuda has now been assimilated to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position — and answers that he agrees with Rabban Shimon on the *santar* (the fields) but disagrees regarding the daughter towns. Rabban Shimon holds that selling a town includes its *banot*, its satellite settlements: *mocher ir machar benoteiha*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the daughter towns are not sold. On that point they diverge. *Rav Yehuda savir lei ke-vatei be-chada u-flig bei be-chada* — he agrees on one point and disagrees on another.

### Partial Agreement

At the end of the discussion, the Gemara has not formally resolved which reading of *santar* is definitive. Both the "sentry" interpretation and the "irrigated field" interpretation remain alive in the sources. That said, the sentry reading has considerably more to recommend it. The standard Mishnaic term for irrigated fields is already *beit ha-shlachin*, which appears at the beginning of this very Mishnah — it would be redundant for *santar* to mean the same thing. Moreover, the comparison to *ankolmus* — clearly a person — makes most natural sense if *santar* is also a person. The etymology connects cleanly to the Greek or Latin word for a guardian or field-warden. The debate in the Mishnah is therefore most naturally read as concerning whether the town's sentry-slave is included in the sale, with Rabban Shimon holding that he is and the *tanna kamma* holding that he is not — while the orchards and irrigated fields transfer in any case as land appurtenant to the town.
