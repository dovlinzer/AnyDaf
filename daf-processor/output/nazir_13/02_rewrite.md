# Nazir 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Conditional Nezirut

### Opening Review

Today's daf is 13. We begin by revisiting the *mishnah* at the bottom of 12b, which was briefly introduced at the close of the previous session but requires a full reading before proceeding to the *gemara*.

### Son/Daughter Status

The *mishnah* opens: *hareini nazir l'khsheyihyeh li ven* — "I will be a *nazir* if I have a son." If a son is indeed born, *harey zeh nazir* — he becomes a *nazir*. But what if the child born is a daughter, a *tumtum*, or an *androgynous*? A *tumtum* is a person whose genitalia is concealed, so one cannot determine whether the child is male or female. An *androgynous* child possesses both sets of genitalia, raising its own *halachic* classification questions. In all such cases, the *mishnah* rules *eino nazir* — he does not become a *nazir*.

The implied *chiddush* here is that when a man vows *nezirut* contingent on having a *ben*, a *tumtum* does not suffice to trigger the vow even as a *safek*, because *ben* is understood to mean a definite son. If, however, he had said *l'khsheyihyeh li vlad* — "when I have a child" — then even if the child born is a daughter, a *tumtum*, or an *androgynous*, he becomes a *nazir*, since he used the more inclusive term.

### Miscarriage Case

The *mishnah* continues: *hipilah ishto eino nazir* — if his wife miscarried, he is not a *nazir*. The underlying assumption of the *mishnah* appears to be that if the miscarried child had been considered viable — *kalu lo chadashav*, having reached full term — it would in principle fall within the category of *vlad* and potentially trigger the vow. Nonetheless, the *tanna kamma* rules that the person is not a *nazir*, and the *gemara* will explain why.

There is an important distinction worth noting here between a *nefel* — a miscarriage of a non-viable fetus — and what might be termed *nolad met*, a stillbirth of a child that had been viable until the moment of death. The *mishnah* uses the term *hipilah* precisely because the status of the child is uncertain, and that uncertainty is what drives the *halachic* discussion that follows.

### Tanna Kamma vs R. Shimon

The position of the *tanna kamma* is identified by the *gemara* as that of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *lo nachis inish nafshai l'safeka* — a person who makes a conditional vow does not intend to be bound when the condition is in doubt. The reasoning is that being a *safek nazir* is worse than being a *vadai nazir*: you must observe all the restrictions of *nezirut* but have no means of ending it, since you cannot bring the *korbanot* of a *nazir* without certainty that you are one. Rabbi Yehuda therefore concludes that the person intends his vow to take effect only if the condition is definitely fulfilled. Since there is doubt whether the miscarried child was viable, the vow does not take effect at all.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that the person is indeed a *safek nazir* and must grapple with that status.

### Safek to Vadai

Rabbi Shimon provides a practical resolution. The *mishnah* states: *yomar* — the person should declare — *im hayah ben kayama, hareini nazir chovah* — "if that child was viable, I am a *nazir* by virtue of my original vow." *V'im lav, hareini nazir nedavah* — "and if it was not viable, I now accept *nezirut* as a freewill gift." By conditioning the current *nezirut* on both possibilities, the person transforms himself from a *safek nazir* into a *vadai nazir* in either case. He can then complete his *nezirut*, bring his *korbanot*, and be done.

### Second Child Born

The *mishnah* then addresses the scenario where *chazrah v'yaldah* — his wife subsequently gives birth to a living child. According to the *tanna kamma*, the first miscarriage did not satisfy the condition, so this birth does. The person is unambiguously a *nazir*.

According to Rabbi Shimon, however, the situation is once again uncertain. It is possible that the first child was viable and the first *nezirut* was obligatory, making the current birth trigger only a new freewill consideration. Or the first child was not viable, in which case the first *nezirut* was freewill, and now the obligatory *nezirut* has been triggered. Rabbi Shimon applies the same resolution: *yomar, im harishon ben kayama, harishon chovah v'zo nedavah* — "if the first was viable, my first *nezirut* was obligatory and this new one I accept as freewill." *V'im lav, harishon nedavah v'zo chovah* — "if the first was not viable, my first was freewill and this one is my obligatory *nezirut*." For each ambiguous birth, the person resolves *safek nezirut* into *vadai nezirut* through conditional declaration.

## Gemara Analysis

### Initial Question

The *gemara* opens with *hai mai l'memra* — what is the point? The opening clause of the *mishnah* — that one who vows *nezirut* contingent on having a son, and has a son, becomes a *nazir* — seems self-evident. The *gemara* answers that the *chiddush* lies in the subsequent clause.

### Ben vs Child

The *gemara* first addresses why it was necessary to state the case of *ben*. Perhaps, the *gemara* suggests, one might have thought that *ben* is not strictly "son" but rather derives from the root *l'hibanot*, meaning "to be built" — and thus *ben* functions as a generic term for child. The *mishnah* therefore teaches *kamashma lan d'lo* — we do not interpret it that way. *Ben* means son.

The answer is admittedly not fully satisfying. There are many other words in biblical Hebrew that function as generic terms for offspring. The more interesting *chiddush*, one might argue, concerns the *tumtum*: a *tumtum* is a *safek* — either a son or daughter — so why does the presence of a *tumtum* not trigger even a *safek* obligation? Moreover, what happens if some time later the membrane covering the *tumtum*'s genitalia is removed and the child is discovered to be male — does one retroactively become a *nazir*? The *gemara* does not pursue this line of inquiry, but it is a striking question.

### Tumtum Problem

The *gemara* similarly questions the *chiddush* of the *vlad* case. If he said "when I have a child," it seems obvious that even a daughter, *tumtum*, or *androgynous* would qualify. The *gemara* responds that one might have thought *vlad d'michshav beinei inshi ba'inan* — that "child" means a child considered significant in the eyes of people, which is to say, a son. Society's historical preference for male children is the subtext of the *gemara*'s observation. The *mishnah* therefore teaches that we take his words literally: he said *vlad*, and any child qualifies.

The *gemara* then turns to the *chiddush* of the miscarriage case, which is identified as the real crux of the *mishnah*. The question of who holds this position leads the *gemara* to identify the *tanna kamma* as Rabbi Yehuda, based on an earlier ruling involving a conditional vow about a threshing floor. The principle is the same: *lo nachis inish nafshai l'safeka* — one who makes a conditional vow intends certainty, not doubt.

### Retroactive Viability

With this framework established, the *gemara* considers whether and how the birth of a subsequent child might retroactively resolve the doubtful status of a previously miscarried child.

### Animal Sanctification

*Ba'i minei Rav Abba mi'Rav Huna* — Rav Abba posed a question to Rav Huna. The case: a man says *hareini nazir l'khsheyihyeh li ven* — "I will be a *nazir* if I have a son" — and his wife miscarried. Operating under Rabbi Yehuda's principle, he is definitively not a *nazir*. Nonetheless, he separated out an animal as a *korban*. His wife then gives birth to a living child. The question is: was he a *nazir* from the time of the first child, and is the animal already sanctified?

The *nafka mina* is practical: if one were to use the animal for shearing or labor — *legizato ul'avoda bo* — would one be in violation of *me'ilah*? If the animal was sanctified when he set it aside, it is *asur*; if not, it is permitted.

The *gemara* immediately clarifies: according to Rabbi Shimon, there is no question. From the moment of the miscarriage, the man was already a *safek nazir* under Rabbi Shimon's ruling. The interesting question arises specifically according to Rabbi Yehuda, who would normally have said the person is not a *nazir* at all. Does the subsequent birth of a living child retroactively establish that the miscarried child was in fact *ben kayama* — viable — and that the man was therefore a *nazir* from the earlier point?

The *gemara* is probing a nuanced middle position: perhaps the evidence supplied by the second living child is sufficient to move Rabbi Yehuda from his default position of non-obligation. It might not render the man a *vadai nazir* from that earlier moment — which could make him *chayav me'ila* — but perhaps enough to impose some form of obligation. The *gemara* asks: *kadosh o lo kadosh* — is the animal sanctified or not? The *gemara* concludes *teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

### Twin Viability

The question of whether the birth of the second child can retroactively establish the viability of the first is complicated by a further consideration. *Tosafot* observes that the *gemara*'s implicit assumption — that the second child's viability would prove the first's — may not be warranted. The key variable is whether these are identical or fraternal twins.

If the twins are identical, they derive from the same fertilized egg split in two, and they are biologically the same age. In that case, the viability of the second child would indeed establish that the first was also viable. If, however, they are fraternal twins, two separate eggs may have been fertilized at different times — perhaps one egg fertilized on the first day after intercourse and another on the third. This could mean that the twins differ by as much as two days in gestational age, which in turn could place one at the threshold of viability and the other just below it.

*Tosafot* makes this explicit, citing the language *deshema miktzat hatipah nikletah miyad umiktzat hatipah hashniyah lo nikletah ad achar shney yamim* — that perhaps part of the semen was absorbed immediately and part only two days later. If that is the case, then the twin born alive may actually have been conceived slightly earlier, and the one who was miscarried, though appearing to be the same age, was in fact two days younger and had not yet crossed the threshold of *ben kayama*. This explains why the *gemara* leaves the question unresolved as *teiku*, rather than simply concluding that the second birth settles the matter.

## Two Nezirut Declarations

### And Me Question

*Ba'i minei Ben Rechumi me'Abaye* — Ben Rechumi posed a question to Abaye. The case: a man declares *hareini nazir l'khsheyihyeh li ven* — "I will be a *nazir* when I have a son" — and a companion overhears him and says *ve'alai* — "and upon me." What does this mean? Is the companion saying he will be a *nazir* when he himself has a son, or when the original speaker has a son?

### Alai vs Ani

The *gemara* proceeds to a parallel case. What if rather than saying *ve'alai*, the companion says *va'ani* — "and I"? At first glance, both seem to convey the same meaning. Yet the *gemara* probes whether *va'ani* might carry a different intent. One possibility is *anfshah ka'amar* — he is speaking about himself, meaning "and when I have a son, I too will be a *nazir*." The other possibility is *ve'rachimna lach kvatich* — "and I care for you as I care for it," expressing not a personal vow inspired by the other's example, but rather an expression of solidarity with the speaker. In this reading, *va'ani* means: "Because of my bond with you, if you have a son, I too will be a *nazir*."

The distinction between *alai* and *ani* is subtle. *Alai* — "upon me" — places the obligation directly on oneself, stepping away, so to speak, from the original speaker. *Ani* — "and I" — may instead be oriented toward the relationship: "I am with you in this," suggesting that the *nezirut* is conditioned on the original speaker's son rather than one's own.

An alternative version of the text, noted in the margin (*samech alef*, denoting *sfarim acherim*), has the original vow formulated as *l'khsheyihyeh lecha ven* — "when you have a son" — making the question even more pointed. If Michael says "I will be a *nazir* when you have a son" and Dov says "and me," does "and me" follow Michael's formulation (conditioned on Michael's son) or does the principle of *adam karov eitzel atzmo* — one is closest to oneself — pull the declaration toward Dov's own son?

### Ksifa Interpretation

The *gemara* then introduces the concept of *ksifa lei milta* — embarrassment in another's presence — as a factor shaping intent. *Tosafot*'s reading is as follows: when the companion says *va'ani* in the presence of the original speaker, there is a social pressure not to appear indifferent to the other's situation. Accordingly, *va'ani* in the presence of the speaker would naturally be understood as "and I am here for you — if you have a son, I too will be a *nazir*," because to say "and I, when I have a son" in that moment would be to ignore the person standing before you. The embarrassment of appearing self-focused in front of someone who has just expressed so much significance to having a son pulls the declaration toward the other person's condition.

### Third Party Case

The *gemara* then raises the case of a third party: *amar hareini nazir l'khsheyihyeh l'ploni ven* — Michael says "I will be a *nazir* if Charlie has a son." Charlie is not present. If Dov then says *va'ani*, there is no direct social pressure to express loyalty to Charlie, since Charlie is not in the room.

### Loyalty Motivation

The question is whether the dynamic of loyalty still operates indirectly. Even though Charlie is absent, Dov has witnessed Michael's deep commitment to Charlie's welfare — so much so that Michael is prepared to accept *nezirut* on Charlie's behalf. Dov may therefore feel a pull not of loyalty to Charlie, but of loyalty to Michael. The reasoning would be: Michael, I see how much you care for Charlie. Because I care for you, I will also be a *nazir* if Charlie has a son.

This analysis reveals two distinct psychological models at work in these declarations. In the first model, someone observes a *nazir* or hears a vow and says "me too" — inspired by the idea and adopting it for themselves. In the second model, the "me too" is not about inspiration but about solidarity — not "you gave me a good idea" but "I am with you in this." The two models yield different *halachic* conclusions about whether the companion's vow is conditioned on his own child or on the original speaker's. The *gemara* leaves this as *tiku* — unresolved.

## Immediate/Delayed Start

### Two Nezirut Setup

The next *mishnah* presents the following case: *hareini nazir v'nazir k'sheyihyeh li ven* — "I will be a *nazir* [starting now] and a *nazir* when I have a son." The *mishnah* is working through the interaction between a *nezirut* that begins immediately and one conditioned on a future event.

### Order Problem

The subtlety lies in the order of the declarations. When the man says "and a *nazir*" without specifying a future time, even though it was his second statement, it is understood to take effect immediately. What he has in effect said is: my second *nezirut* begins now, and my first *nezirut* — the one conditioned on having a son — will begin when the condition is met.

This creates an unusual inversion: the person is currently practicing his *second* *nezirut* while his *first* *nezirut* has not yet taken effect. When his son is born and the first *nezirut* comes due, it preempts the second: *maniach et shelo u'moneh shel beno* — he sets aside his own ongoing *nezirut* and counts the thirty days of the son-*nezirut*. Then, *achar kach mashlim et shelo* — he completes the remaining days of his own *nezirut*.

### No Shaving

The *mishnah*'s use of the word *mashlim* — completing or making up — carries an important implication. *Tosafot* makes explicit what the *mishnah* leaves implicit: it is impossible that the man would have shaved at the conclusion of the son-*nezirut* before completing his own, because the remaining days of his own *nezirut* would then be fewer than thirty. The requirement of *giddel pera* — the growth of hair for the requisite period — means that one cannot validly conclude a *nezirut* without at least thirty days of uninterrupted hair growth since the last shaving. Accordingly, there is no shaving between the two periods; the man does a straight continuous run and brings his *korbanot* only at the end.

This yields a significant practical consequence: because of the unusual order in which these *neziruyot* took effect, the man avoids bringing two separate sets of *korbanot*. Normally, two accepted *neziruyot* require the first to conclude — with shaving and *korbanot* — before the second begins. Here, because the second was already underway before the first came due, the first is entitled to preempt the second without requiring the second to formally conclude first. And since the second was never formally concluded, it does not need to restart from scratch — only the remaining days need to be completed.

### Hundred Days

To appreciate the full scope of the issue, it is helpful to look ahead to the *mishnah* on 15a, which presents a variant: *hareini nazir l'khsheyihyeh li ven v'nazir mei'hayom* — "I will be a *nazir* when I have a son and a *nazir* starting now for a hundred days." Here the immediately-effective *nezirut* is one of a hundred days rather than the standard thirty.

The *mishnah* establishes a threshold: *nolad lo ven ad shivim lo hifsid klum* — if the son is born within the first seventy days, he loses nothing. Why seventy? Because if the son is born by day seventy, there will be at least thirty days remaining in the hundred-day *nezirut* after the thirty-day son-*nezirut* concludes. That means the son-*nezirut* is entirely encompassed within the time frame of the hundred-day *nezirut*, and the man gets to "double-dip": both *neziruyot* are satisfied by the same period of observance, and no days beyond the hundred need be added.

If, however, the son is born after day seventy, the son-*nezirut* will push beyond the hundred-day framework. The *mishnah* then employs the term *soter* — the later birth undermines the double-dipping. *Tosafot* clarifies that *soter* here does not mean that all prior days are invalidated entirely, but rather that *ein tiglachat pachos mishloshim yom* — a valid conclusion of *nezirut* cannot occur with fewer than thirty days of hair growth. The man is therefore required to add a full thirty days at the end rather than simply the remaining balance.

### Three Scenarios

There are thus three distinct structural scenarios for overlapping *neziruyot*. In the first, the man declares a conditional *nezirut* first and the immediate *nezirut* second, meaning his immediate *nezirut* is number one and he simply performs it, brings his *korbanot*, and then waits for the condition to be met before performing the second. This is the straightforward case with no unusual interactions.

In the second scenario — the *mishnah* currently before us — the man starts what is structurally his second *nezirut* before his first has taken effect. When the first comes due, it preempts the second; when it concludes, the man completes the second. No intermediate shaving occurs, and only one set of *korbanot* is brought at the end.

In the third scenario, the immediately-effective *nezirut* is long enough (a hundred days) that the son-*nezirut* falls entirely within its span. If the birth occurs early enough, the man double-dips entirely and needs no additional days at all. If the birth occurs too late, the double-dipping is lost and additional days must be added.

## Post-Dated Nezirut

### Rava Case Setup

*Ba'i Rava* — Rava posed a question. The case: *amar hareini nazir l'achar esrim yom u'me'akhshav me'ah yom* — "I will be a *nazir* after twenty days, and starting now, a *nazir* for a hundred days." Unlike the previous *mishnah*, where the timing of the conditional *nezirut* was determined by an uncertain future event (the birth of a son), here the man has explicitly post-dated his first *nezirut* to begin after twenty days. The question is whether the hundred-day *nezirut*, declared to start immediately, actually takes effect.

### Hair Growth Logic

The *gemara* frames the question as follows: *keivan dehani me'ah be'esrim lo shalmi, lo chayilin* — since the hundred days will not be completed by the time the twenty-day *nezirut* begins, perhaps the hundred-day *nezirut* should not take effect at all. The logic is: why begin something you cannot finish before being interrupted?

On the other hand, *keivan d'it lei giddel sei'ar l'vasof, shapir dami* — since there will still be an opportunity for *giddel sei'ar* — the required hair growth — after the conclusion of the entire arrangement, perhaps the hundred-day *nezirut* should take effect and the man will simply complete the remaining days afterward.

The mention of *giddel sei'ar* implies that a *korban* is being brought at the juncture between the two *neziruyot* — the twenty-day *nezirut* concludes with shaving, and then the question is whether the hundred days must start fresh or whether the days already counted are somehow preserved. Unlike the prior case, where the unusual ordering arose spontaneously from an unpredictable birth, here the man has deliberately structured the arrangement from the outset. *Tosafot* notes that this deliberate pre-arrangement may change the analysis: when a person consciously lays out the sequence in advance, the expectation may be that each *nezirut* must conclude properly before the next begins.

### Small vs Large

The *gemara* then asks why Rava needed to pose the question specifically with a hundred-day *nezirut*. Why not ask the question with a standard *nezirut mu'etat* — a short, thirty-day *nezirut*?

The *gemara* answers that the two questions are nested: *chada migu chada kamiba'ya lei* — one question is built upon the other. If we are uncertain about a small *nezirut*, we would certainly wonder about a large one; if we resolve the large one, the small one may or may not follow. The cases are therefore not interchangeable.

### Sufficient Remainder

In the case of a short *nezirut*, if the man began the hundred days immediately and then had to stop for the twenty-day *nezirut*, he would have only ten days remaining at the end. *Keivan d'asara yomin hu d'yasiran, lo salik lehu hanin asara* — since those ten days are not sufficient to stand on their own as a *nezirut*, perhaps the hundred-day *nezirut* should not have begun at all.

But in the case of the hundred-day *nezirut*, the remaining days after the twenty-day interruption would be substantial — perhaps eighty days. *Keivan d'isya tmanin yomin l'vasof, salik lei* — since eighty days remain at the end, that is certainly sufficient to warrant the original hundred-day *nezirut* taking effect. Whether this logic prevails, or whether the deliberate post-dating of the twenty-day *nezirut* imposes a different framework entirely, is left as an open question — and the discussion continues on the following daf.
