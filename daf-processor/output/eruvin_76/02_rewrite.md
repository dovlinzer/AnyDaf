# Eruvin 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beit Sha'ar Concept

### Ten Houses Setup

The daf opens — picking up from the bottom of 75b, at the passage beginning *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — with a case involving ten connected houses arranged in a row, *ze lifnim mizeh*, each opening into the next, with only the innermost house having a direct opening to the courtyard. Anyone wishing to reach the outermost house must pass through all the others in succession.

Rashi makes clear at the outset that only a house which directly opens into the *chatzer* is implicated in the *eruv* obligation. A house that has no direct opening to the courtyard at all is simply irrelevant to the *eruv*. One might therefore have assumed that only house number one — the single house with a direct opening to the *chatzer* — need participate. What the *gemara* goes on to teach is more precise and more striking.

### Shmuel's Ruling

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *pnimi nosein es eruvo ve-dayo* — it is the innermost house, number ten, that gives the *eruv*, and that alone is sufficient. Houses one through nine are entirely exempt. The reason is that since the only route to house ten runs through houses one through nine, those outer houses are reclassified as a *beis sha'ar* — a gatehouse — for house ten. A *beis sha'ar* is not a house in the halachic sense; it is a mere passageway appended to a dwelling. Because houses one through nine have lost their status as houses, they are no longer part of the *chatzer* dynamic at all. Only house ten retains the identity of a true house and therefore bears the *eruv* obligation.

This is a notable conceptual move. One might imagine that the presence of residents in houses one through nine would create the kind of *regel ha-oseret* concern that arose in earlier discussions of multiple households sharing a courtyard. But the *gemara* forestalls that entirely: the concept of *beis sha'ar* does not merely exempt those houses from contributing — it strips them of halachic residential identity altogether. Even if people are living there, once the structure of access has rendered them functionally gatehouses, they cease to be "houses" for purposes of *eruv*. As one might observe, this is a striking halachic reality: a substantial, inhabited dwelling can lose its identity as a *bayis* simply because of the pattern of physical access.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

*Rabbi Yochanan omer*: *afilu chitzon* — even the outer house must participate. Read simply, this would mean Rabbi Yochanan holds that all ten houses remain *batim* in the full sense, each obligated in the *eruv* because all of them eventually open into the *chatzer*. The *gemara*, however, refuses this reading entirely: *chitzon beis sha'ar?* — it cannot be that number one, the outermost house, is merely a gatehouse. That makes no sense. The *gemara* therefore reinterprets Rabbi Yochanan: what he means by "*afilu chitzon*" is not house one but house nine — the house one step outward from the innermost house ten. In other words, Rabbi Yochanan holds that *even* number nine, which is directly adjacent to and serves as the sole approach to house ten, must contribute to the *eruv*.

### Gatehouse Debate

This reinterpretation reveals the actual dispute. The *gemara* frames it as follows: *b'mai kamifligi? Mar savar beis sha'ar de-yachid shmei beis sha'ar, u-mar savar lo shmei beis sha'ar.* One opinion holds that a *beis sha'ar* can exist even for a single house — there is no conceptual barrier to calling something a gatehouse even when it leads only to one dwelling behind it. The other opinion holds that a true *beis sha'ar* requires a more complex structure, something that serves as the gateway to multiple dwellings or a larger compound. For a lone single house, you cannot call the approach to it a gatehouse.

The underlying agreement between the positions is itself significant: the *gemara* takes it as given that both opinions accept the basic *beis sha'ar* framework. The only question is whether that framework can apply when only a single house lies beyond the "gatehouse." The dispute is purely about the outer boundary of the concept, not about whether access patterns can redefine residential identity in the first place.

## Two Courtyards Case

### Three Houses Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a related case, transmitted in the name of *Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha amar Rav*: *shtei chatzerot ve-gimel batim beineihem* — two courtyards with three houses between them. The scenario can be visualized as follows: courtyard A and courtyard B are independent of each other, each opening separately to the public domain. Between them stand three houses in a row. House one opens directly into courtyard A; house three opens directly into courtyard B; house two in the middle has no independent external opening — it is accessible only by passing through either house one (from A's side) or house three (from B's side).

### Eruv Placement

The *gemara* describes the operative halachic configuration: *zeh be-derech zeh ve-nosei es eruvo be-zeh ve-zeh be-derech zeh ve-nosei es eruvo be-zeh* — courtyard A passes through house one and deposits its *eruv* in house two; courtyard B passes through house three and deposits its *eruv* in house two. *Zeh naasah beis sha'ar le-zeh ve-zeh naasah beis sha'ar le-zeh* — house one becomes a *beis sha'ar* for house two from A's direction, and house three becomes a *beis sha'ar* for house two from B's direction. Since the *eruv* of each courtyard is deposited in house two, house two itself is exempt from contributing, as the established rule is that the house in which the *eruv* is deposited need not itself provide bread. Houses one and three are reclassified as *batei sha'ar*. The result is that none of the three houses must independently contribute to either *eruv*.

### Structural vs. Intentional

Rashi's commentary illuminates a deeper dimension of this case. He indicates that it is possible — indeed part of the *chiddush* — that house two actually has its own independent access. It could be entered directly from outside without passing through house one or house three. If that is so, then house one would not *structurally* have to serve as a gatehouse in the way that houses one through nine did in the previous case. There, the architecture itself forced one house to serve as the only route to another. Here, the access through house one is a matter of deliberate choice in how the *eruv* is deposited.

The *gemara* is thus teaching two distinct principles. The first, established earlier, is structural: if the only physical route to a house runs through another, the intervening house automatically becomes a *beis sha'ar*. The second, which emerges here, is intentional: even where independent access exists, if when depositing the *eruv* one deliberately routes through an outer house to reach an inner one, that act of traversal is itself sufficient to confer *beis sha'ar* status on the outer house — at least for *eruv* purposes. The very act of choosing to walk through a house in order to reach the destination redefines the house you walked through.

## Rava's Test Case

### Independent Houses

Rava would pose the following riddle to his students as a test case — *badik lehu Rava le-rabbanan*. The scenario: *bei rechovata de-chatzeirata u-trei batim beineihu* — two courtyards with only two houses between them. Again each house has its own independent access; one need not pass through either to reach the other from outside. *Zeh be-derech zeh ve-notein eruvo be-zeh ve-zeh be-derech zeh ve-notein eruvo be-zeh* — courtyard A passes through house one and deposits its *eruv* in house two; courtyard B passes through house two and deposits its *eruv* in house one.

The scenario can be stated precisely: from A's perspective, walking through house one to deposit the *eruv* in house two makes house one a *beis sha'ar*; from B's perspective, walking through house two to deposit the *eruv* in house one makes house two a *beis sha'ar*. The result, if both operations are valid, would be that house one is simultaneously a *beis sha'ar* from A's perspective and a *bayis* from B's perspective, while house two is simultaneously a *bayis* from A's perspective and a *beis sha'ar* from B's perspective.

### Dual Status Problem

The riddle Rava poses is: *kanu eruvo lo?* — did the *eruvin* work? Does A's *eruv* in house two take effect and does B's *eruv* in house one take effect simultaneously?

The problem is apparent. If A's act of traversing house one converts it into a *beis sha'ar*, then B cannot deposit an *eruv* there, because *ha-notein eruvo be-veit sha'ar ve-achsadrah eino eruv* — one who deposits an *eruv* in a *beis sha'ar* or an open colonnade has not made a valid *eruv*. But if we protect B's ability to deposit in house one by refusing to classify it as a *beis sha'ar*, then house one retains its full status as a *bayis* and must contribute to A's *eruv* in the normal way.

As one might note: from a common-sense perspective, these are clearly two independent houses, each freely accessible. The whole *beis sha'ar* classification rests on the deliberate choice of how one routes through on the way to deposit the *eruv*. Yet the *gemara* is taking that choice seriously enough to allow it to redefine the house entirely.

### Consistency Required

*Amrei lei* — the rabbis answered Rava: *shneihem lo kanu eruv* — neither *eruv* works. The principle invoked is *ma nafshach*: you must be consistent. *I beis sha'ar mashvit lei* — if you are going to call house one a *beis sha'ar* for A because A walked through it on the way to house two, then B cannot deposit an *eruv* there. *I bayis mashvit lei* — if you are going to say it remains a *bayis* so that B can deposit an *eruv* there, then it retains full residential status and must contribute to A's *eruv* in the normal way. A single concrete entity cannot hold contradictory definitions simultaneously.

### Twilight Analogy

This leads the *gemara* to a suggestive comparison. *U-mai shna mi-de-Rava?* — how is this different from a ruling of Rava himself? *De-amar Rava*: if two people, call them Reuven and Shimon, commissioned a single agent to make an *eruv techumin* for each of them, and the agent placed Reuven's *eruv* before nightfall and Shimon's *eruv* during *bein ha-shmashot* — the ambiguous twilight period — then Reuven's *eruv* was consumed during *bein ha-shmashot* and Shimon's *eruv* was consumed after nightfall. *U-shneihem kanu eruv* — both *eruvin* are valid.

The scenario requires explanation. For Reuven's *eruv* to have been validly consumed during *bein ha-shmashot*, the *eruv* must have been in place when *bein ha-shmashot* began — which presupposes that *bein ha-shmashot* had not yet arrived when he placed it, i.e., it was still day. But for Shimon's *eruv* to work, it must have been placed while it was still day (*mibod yom*). Since Shimon placed it during *bein ha-shmashot*, that period must be counted as day for his *eruv* to be valid. So for Reuven we treat *bein ha-shmashot* as night, and for Shimon we treat the same period as day — and we let both *eruvin* stand. This is possible because *safek eruv le-kula*: where there is doubt in a rabbinic matter, we rule leniently.

If we are prepared to call the same time period "night" for one person and "day" for another — a seemingly contradictory pair of classifications — why can we not similarly call house one a *beis sha'ar* relative to A and a *bayis* relative to B?

### Contradiction Problem

The *gemara* distinguishes: *hachi hashta? Hasam safek yemama safek leila — lo minchara milta.* In the *bein ha-shmashot* case, the underlying reality is genuine uncertainty. The time period is actually of doubtful status; it may be night or may be day. When Reuven and Shimon each come separately, we apply the lenient ruling to each in turn. There is no single visible phenomenon standing before us declared to be contradictory things at once; the two rulings are simply separate applications of leniency to a genuinely ambiguous situation.

*Aval hacha* — here, the house in question is a concrete, actual entity standing in front of everyone. *Ei legabei de-hai bayis, legabei de-hai bayis; ei legabei de-hai beis sha'ar, legabei de-hai nami beis sha'ar* — if it is a *bayis* for one side, it must be a *bayis* for the other; if it is a *beis sha'ar* for one side, it must be a *beis sha'ar* for the other too. A concrete object cannot simultaneously hold contradictory formal identities. This is not a matter of ruling leniently under conditions of doubt — it is a matter of assigning opposite legal definitions to the same real thing at the same moment, which the *gemara* refuses to permit.

Rashi, at the conclusion of his commentary here, puts the matter pointedly: *ve-hachi havei milei de-rabbanan ke-chucha ve-tulula, ve-lo niten pesach li-tzdukin li-rdos* — to allow such contradictions would make the words of the Rabbis into a laughingstock and would hand the Sadducees — that is, the anti-rabbinic critics — an opening for mockery. The distinction between the two cases, then, rests on both a technical and a dignitary rationale: not only is it conceptually problematic to assign contradictory identities to a concrete object, but the appearance of the law itself demands internal coherence.

## Opening Dimensions

### Four by Four Rule

The *gemara* now turns to the beginning of a new *perek*, addressing the requirements for an opening between two courtyards to allow them to combine into a single *eruv* or to maintain their separate status. The *mishnah* establishes: *chalon she-bein shtei chatzerot* — a window between two courtyards must be *arba al arba* — four by four *tefachim* in size — and *be-toch asarah* — situated within ten *tefachim* of the ground. If these conditions are met, the two courtyards may choose to make two separate *eruvin* or, if they wish, to join as one: *me-arvin shnayim, ve-im ratzu me-arvin echad*.

The *gemara* notes that the four-by-four *shiur* might suggest that the *mishnah* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's opinion that anything within four *tefachim* is considered connected — *lavud* — implying that anything less than four is treated as absent. The *gemara* rejects this inference: *afilu teima ke-rabbanan*. Even the Rabbis who disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel on *lavud* would agree that a minimum of four by four is required for a meaningful opening. The two *shiurim* operate in different domains. The dispute about *lavud* concerns whether narrow gaps in a physical structure are treated as closed; the requirement of four by four for an opening concerns whether an aperture is large enough to constitute functional access. *De-ika arba al arba chashiv, ve-ilo lo chashiv* — four by four is significant; less is not.

### Ten-Handbreadth Limit

The *mishnah* further stipulates that the opening must be within ten *tefachim* of the ground. If it is higher — *le-mala me-asarah* — the two courtyards are completely separate: *me-arvin shnayim ve-ein me-arvin echad*.

### Height Restriction

Rashi explains the reasoning: a wall of ten *tefachim* constitutes a full halachic partition. Anything above the ten-*tefach* line of an otherwise solid wall is already, as it were, beyond the boundary — the wall has already done its full separating work within those ten *tefachim*. An opening that falls entirely above the ten-*tefach* line therefore does not penetrate the functional barrier at all; it opens only into the space above it, which is not the operative domain. Access at such a height is simply irrelevant to combining two ground-level courtyards.

There is a known exception to this principle: a ladder placed against the wall can effectively convert the wall into an opening, since the ladder itself constitutes a form of accessible passage within the wall's structural zone. But absent such a device, an opening above ten *tefachim* has no combining effect.

### Partial Openings

The *gemara* derives an additional *chiddush* from the *mishnah*'s precise formulation. What if the window is mostly above the ten-*tefach* line but a small portion of it dips below? The *gemara* reads: *teima de-kula le-mala me-asarah — aval miktzatah be-toch asarah me-arvin shnayim im ratzu me-arvin echad* — even if virtually the entire opening is above ten, as long as any portion, however small, falls within the ten-*tefach* zone, it is sufficient. The opening is valid.

A *baraita* is cited in confirmation: *kula le-mala me-asarah u-miktzatah be-toch asarah* — entirely above ten but the smallest amount within ten — valid; and conversely, *kula be-toch asarah u-miktzatah le-mala me-asarah* — almost entirely within ten with the slightest bit above — also valid. The *gemara* notes that once the first case is stated, the second is self-evident; the teaching presents both in the manner of *zu ve-ein tzarich lomar zu* — this and, needless to say, that. The operative *chiddush* is that even a millimeter of the opening within the ten-*tefach* zone suffices.

## Circular Openings

### Rabbi Yochanan's Rule

Rabbi Yochanan addresses the case of a circular window: *chalon agul tzarich she-yehei be-heikefo esrim ve-arba'ah tefachim, u-shnayim u-mashehu me-hen be-toch asarah* — a circular opening must have a circumference of twenty-four *tefachim*, and two *tefachim* plus a hairsbreadth of it must fall within the ten-*tefach* zone. The rationale: *she-im yerabe-nu — nimtza mashehu be-toch asarah* — if you inscribe a square within it, some portion of that square will fall within ten. The claim has two parts: that a circumference of twenty-four yields an inscribed square of four by four, and that having two *tefachim* plus a bit of the circle below the ten-*tefach* line guarantees that part of the inscribed square also falls below it.

### Square in Circle

The geometry proceeds as follows. The *gemara* uses the approximation that any circle with a circumference of three has a diameter of one — that is, *pi* equals three. A circumference of twenty-four therefore yields a diameter of eight and a radius of four. An inscribed square within a circle of radius four would, according to Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning, be exactly four by four. As for the two-*tefachim* requirement: if the radius is four, the bottom of the inscribed square lies two *tefachim* above the bottom of the circle (since the half-diagonal of the square equals the radius, and going from the center down to the bottom of the square is two, while from the bottom of the square to the bottom of the circle is another two). Therefore, for any portion of the inscribed square to lie below the ten-*tefach* line, the bottom of the circle must be more than two *tefachim* below that line — hence the requirement of two plus a hairsbreadth.

### Correct Math

The *gemara* immediately challenges the underlying geometry. Working with the same approximation of *pi* as three, the *gemara* notes: *bitmuni sagi?* — wouldn't eight suffice as a circumference? If the requirement is simply a four-*tefach* diameter (analogous to a four-by-four square), then a circumference of twelve should suffice. The *gemara* rejects this: *hani mili be-igula* — that would only be true if the circle itself were the required opening. But since we need to inscribe a four-by-four square *within* the circle, a circle of diameter four is insufficient; the square's corners would extend outside it.

The *gemara* then attempts the calculation in the other direction. *Kama meruba yetar al igula — rivia*: a square circumscribing a circle has a circumference one-quarter greater. If a circle has a circumference of three, the square around it has a circumference of four. Therefore a circle of circumference twelve (diameter four) is surrounded by a square of circumference sixteen (four by four). But the *gemara* again distinguishes: *hani mili igula d'nafik mi-go ribua* — that is the relationship when the circle is inscribed inside the square. What is needed here is the reverse: a square inscribed inside the circle, *ribua d'nafik mi-go igula*. Because the corners of the square protrude — *mishum mursha de-karnasa* — a larger circle is required to contain them.

*Kol amta be-ribua amta u-trei chumshai be-alakhsona* — the *gemara* applies the standard approximation that the diagonal of a unit square is 1.4. A four-by-four square therefore has a diagonal of 5.6, which is also the diameter of the circumscribed circle. A circle of diameter 5.6 has a circumference of 5.6 times 3, which equals 16.8. The correct answer is therefore *be-shivsar naki chumsha sagiya* — seventeen minus a fifth, i.e., 16.8, is sufficient. A circumference of twenty-four is simply too large.

### Caesarean Sages

The *gemara* attributes the figure of twenty-four to the Caesarean scholars — *ke-dayyane de-Keisari* — who are said to have held: *igula mi-go ribua — riva; ribua mi-go igula — palga* — a circle within a square loses a quarter, while a square within a circle loses a half. According to Rashi, their error was as follows: starting with a four-by-four square (circumference sixteen), they added a half of sixteen to arrive at twenty-four as the circumference of a circle that would contain it. This ratio — adding a half — is mathematically incorrect. As Tosafot points out, one can see immediately that it is impossible: a circumference of twenty-four yields a diameter of eight, and a diameter of eight is obviously far larger than any square with a side of four would require.

### Tosafot Defense

Tosafot does not accept that the Caesarean scholars made so elementary a mathematical error. He argues instead that Rabbi Yochanan misunderstood what they were saying. The scholars of Caesarea were speaking about *area*, not *circumference*, and Rabbi Yochanan mistakenly applied their statement to circumference.

Tosafot's reconstruction: draw a circle; draw the square that circumscribes it; draw the square that is inscribed within it. The ratio of the circumscribed square's area to the circle's area is four to three — that is, the circle is three-quarters the area of the surrounding square. This is the same three-to-four ratio that holds for circumferences as well, since it reflects the relationship of *pi* to four. Now, the inscribed square's area is exactly half the circumscribed square's area — this can be seen by observation, since the diagonal of the inscribed square divides the outer square into exactly equal halves. So the three figures stand in the area ratios: circumscribed square four, circle three, inscribed square two.

What the Caesarean scholars said was: relative to the outer square, the inner square is a *palga* — a half. That is, if the outer square has area sixteen, the inscribed square has area eight — but an area of eight means a side-length of approximately 2.83, not four. They were not saying that a four-by-four inscribed square requires a circle of circumference twenty-four. Rabbi Yochanan, according to Tosafot, confused the area relationship of square-to-square with a circumference calculation, thereby arriving at a figure that is demonstrably wrong. The scholars of Caesarea are thus exonerated from mathematical error; Rabbi Yochanan applied their statement in a domain it was never intended to address.

## Houses vs. Courtyards

### Higher Openings OK

Having established the rules for openings between courtyards, the *gemara* now turns to openings between houses. *Amar Rav Nachman: lo shanu ella chalon she-bein shtei chatzerot* — the rule that the opening must be within ten *tefachim* applies only to a window between two courtyards. *Aval chalon she-bein shtei batim — afilu le-mala me-asarah nami, im ratzu le-areiv me-arvin echad* — but a window between two houses, even if it is higher than ten *tefachim*, can still combine the two dwellings as a single *eruv* unit.

### House as Filled

The reason: *beisa ke-man de-mali dami* — a house is considered as if it were filled. The interior of a house is occupied, used, and inhabited throughout its full height in a way that a courtyard is not. A low fence adequately separates two courtyards because a courtyard's relevant domain is at ground level; but a partial wall dividing two adjacent rooms or two houses does not create the same sense of separation, because the residents use the space all the way up. An opening placed high in a wall between two houses therefore still functions as a meaningful point of connection between the two spaces, and the ten-*tefach* limit that governs courtyard walls does not apply.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges Rav Nachman from a *baraita*: *echad li chalon she-bein shtei chatzerot, ve-echad li chalon she-bein shtei batim, ve-echad li chalon she-bein shtei aliyot, ve-echad li chalon she-bein shtei gagin, ve-echad li chalon she-bein shtei chadarim — kullan arba al arba be-toch asarah* — whether the window is between courtyards, between houses, between upper stories, between roofs, or between rooms, in all cases it must be four by four and within ten *tefachim*. This *baraita* appears to apply the ten-*tefach* requirement uniformly to all types of spaces, with no distinction for houses.

### Size vs Height

Rav Nachman resolves the difficulty: *targemah a-arba al arba* — the *baraita* is to be understood as speaking about the four-by-four requirement, which indeed applies to all cases equally. The phrase *be-toch asarah* in the *baraita* is addressed only to the courtyard cases; it is not a uniform rule across all categories. The resolution is admittedly a stretch of the *baraita*'s language, but the conceptual distinction remains sound: the minimum size of four by four *tefachim* is universal, but the height restriction of ten *tefachim* applies only to openings between courtyards and not to openings between houses or fully enclosed spaces. Where the space is enclosed and fully utilized — *beisa ke-man de-mali dami* — an opening at any height serves as a genuine connection.

## Ceiling Openings

### Ladder Requirement

The final case of the daf addresses a *lul* — a trapdoor or ceiling opening — between a lower room and an upper story. *Ba'ei minei Rav Abba me-Rav Nachman: lul ha-patuach min ha-bayit la-aliya* — an opening in the ceiling leading from the house up to the attic — *tzarich sulam kavua le-hatiro o ein tzarich sulam kavua le-hatiro?* — does combining these two spaces require that there be a fixed, permanent ladder in place, or is it sufficient without one? Rav Abba frames his question as being about whether a permanent (*kavua*) ladder is necessary, or whether a temporary, movable (*arai*) ladder would suffice.

### Wall vs. Roof

Rav Nachman's initial response draws a distinction: *hani mili min ha-tzad* — the principle that a house is considered filled, and therefore openings at any height suffice, applies to openings in a wall, on the side of the space. *Aval be-emtza — lo* — but an opening in the middle of the ceiling is different. A ceiling opening does not carry the same intuitive sense of spatial connection between the two rooms; perhaps for such an opening a ladder would be required.

### No Ladder Needed

However, the *gemara* subsequently cites a definitive tradition: *amar Rav Yosef bar Manyumi amar Rav Nachman — echad sulam kavua ve-echad sulam arai eino tzarich* — whether fixed or temporary, no ladder is required at all. Rav Abba, it turns out, had drawn an incorrect inference from Rav Nachman's initial statement; he had assumed that the ruling meant no *fixed* ladder was needed, but that at least a movable ladder would be required. The authoritative version of the ruling is more expansive: the existence of the opening itself, four by four in size, is sufficient to combine the two spaces without any ladder whatsoever — regardless of whether the opening is in a wall or in a ceiling.

The principle of *beisa ke-man de-mali dami* thus extends fully to ceiling openings as well. Just as a house's filled character renders height restrictions inapplicable to wall openings, so too it renders the absence of a ladder irrelevant for ceiling openings. As long as the opening meets the minimum size of four by four *tefachim*, it combines the rooms — and no means of access beyond the opening's bare existence is required.
