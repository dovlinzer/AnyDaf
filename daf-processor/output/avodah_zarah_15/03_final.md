# Avodah Zarah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Behemah Gasah & Shabbat

### Small vs. Large Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִמְכּוֹר בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה לַגּוֹיִם — מוֹכְרִין, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִמְכּוֹר — אֵין מוֹכְרִין. וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶם בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה, עֲגָלִים וּסְיָיחִים, שְׁלֵמִין ושְׁבוּרִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בַּשְּׁבוּרָה, וּבֶן בְּתִירָא מַתִּיר בַּסּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In **a place where** the residents **were accustomed to sell small domesticated animals to gentiles, one may sell** them. In **a place where they were not accustomed to sell** them, **one may not sell** them. **But in every place, one may not sell them large livestock, calves, or foals,** whether these animals are **whole or damaged.** The Sages prohibited these sales lest a Jew's animal perform labor for the gentile on Shabbat in violation of an explicit Torah prohibition, as explained in the Gemara. **Rabbi Yehuda permits** the sale **of a damaged** animal because it is incapable of performing labor, **and ben Beteira permits** the sale **of a horse** for riding, because riding a horse on Shabbat is not prohibited by Torah law.

The Mishnah at the bottom of 14b establishes two distinct categories governing the sale of animals to non-Jews. With regard to *behemah dakah* — lighter animals such as sheep and goats — the Mishnah frames the matter as one of local custom: there are places where it is the practice to sell and places where it is not. The *behemah gasah*, however — large animals such as cows and horses — is treated categorically: *ein mocherin behemah gasah*, we do not sell these animals to non-Jews, full stop, and this applies everywhere without qualification.

### Why Care After Sale?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּלִרְבִיעָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן, מֶעְבַּיד בַּיהּ מְלָאכָה חָיְישִׁינַן. וְנֶיעְבֵּיד, כֵּיוָן דְּזַבְנַהּ קַנְיַיהּ! גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם שְׁאֵלָה וּמִשּׁוּם שְׂכִירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, we are not concerned about** the gentile engaging in **bestiality** with the animal, but **we are concerned** about him **putting** the animal **to work** on Shabbat. The Gemara expresses puzzlement: **And let** the gentile **put** it **to work.** Why should one be concerned about this possibility? **Since he bought it, he acquires it** and may put it to work on Shabbat, as it no longer belongs to the Jew. The Gemara answers: Selling it is prohibited by rabbinic **decree due to** the concern of **lending and due to** the concern of **leasing** the animal to the gentile, as in those cases the animal would be performing work on Shabbat when it is owned by a Jew.

The Gemara at the top of 15a, six lines in, immediately asks: *maitaama* — what is the reason? The concern surrounding *behemah dakah* centered on *reva'iyah*, bestiality. That concern, the Gemara clarifies, does not apply to *behemah gasah*: *d'le-reva'iyah lo chaishinan*. After all, the feared misconduct with smaller animals — associated with nomadic shepherding culture — is simply not a realistic concern with large animals. Instead, the Gemara pivots: *ma'avid bah melacha chaishinan*, what we are concerned about is labor, specifically the use of these animals for *melacha* on *Shabbat*.

This immediately prompts an obvious difficulty. Once we sell the animal, it is no longer ours — *v'neivid keivan de'zabna kinya*, why should we care what the new owner does with it on *Shabbat*?

### Gezeirah: Lending & Testing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁאֲלַהּ קַנְיַיהּ, וְאַגְרַהּ קַנְיַיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָמֵי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יֵיבָא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם נִסְיוֹנֵי, דְּזִמְנִין דְּזַבְּנַהּ לַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: "תָּא נַסְּיַיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ", וְשָׁמְעָה לֵיהּ לְקָלֵיהּ וְאָזְלָא מֵחֲמָתֵיהּ, וְנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּתֵיזִיל, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מְחַמֵּר אַחֵר בְּהֶמְתּוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהַמְחַמֵּר אַחֵר בְּהֶמְתּוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: But during that time period, the act of **borrowing** the animal causes the gentile to temporarily **acquire it, and** likewise, by **leasing** the animal, he temporarily **acquires it.** Why, then, is it a problem if the gentile puts the animal to work on Shabbat? **Rather, Rami, son of Rav Yeiva, said:** Selling is prohibited by rabbinic **decree due to** the concern with regard to **testing. As at times, one sells** an animal **to** a gentile when it is **close to sunset of Shabbat eve, and** one **says to him: Go** and **test** the animal, **and it hears the voice of** its Jewish owner **and walks because of his** command. **And it is beneficial to** the Jewish seller **that** the animal **should walk,** as he wants to demonstrate to the gentile that it is fit for labor. **And** in this manner, **he is** considered **one who drives his** laden **animal on Shabbat. And one who drives his** laden **animal on Shabbat** is **liable** to bring **a sin-offering.**

The Gemara answers: *gezeirah mishum she'ilah u'mishum sechirut* — it is a rabbinic decree extending to cover lending and leasing. The concern is that if we permit outright sale, we will slide toward permitting the lending or renting of animals to non-Jews, where the Shabbat work prohibition remains directly implicated because the animal technically still belongs to a Jew.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *she'ilah kanya ve'agrah kanya* — does borrowing or renting actually constitute a form of acquisition? The question of where the line falls between "this is now yours" and "this still belongs to me" is real and nuanced, and it maps readily onto familiar situations. A renter of an apartment cannot dig holes in the living room or light bonfires regardless of their sense of possession. Ownership boundaries in rental situations are not self-evident.

### Testing Near Shabbat

The Gemara offers a different and more specific rationale. *Ella amar Rav breh Rami breh de-Rav Yeivi: gezeirah mishum nisyoni* — the decree is on account of *nisyoni*, testing. When purchasing a large animal, a prospective buyer naturally wants a demonstration of the animal's capabilities: how well it carries a load, how responsive it is. The Gemara specifies the troublesome scenario precisely: *de-zamnin de-zavna la nihaleih samuch lishkiat hachamah de-ma'alei Shabta* — sometimes the sale happens close to sunset on *erev Shabbat*. The seller, eager to demonstrate the animal's quality, calls out to it, and the animal — hearing its owner's familiar voice — responds and moves under load. *Ve-amar lo ta nisyei nihali de-shama lei kalia ve-azla meichamanteih.*

The Gemara characterizes this as a violation of *mechamer*, the prohibition against driving an animal to perform labor on *Shabbat*: *hamichamer achar behema be-Shabbat chayav chatat*. The seller has not physically done anything — he has simply spoken — and yet through his voice he has activated the animal to perform *melacha*, and this constitutes a real transgression. The *Tosafot* and other *rishonim* note that this is admittedly a narrow and specific scenario — it requires the sale to be happening on *erev Shabbat*, the use of voice, the animal's recognition of its owner, and all the circumstances aligning — but it grounds the prohibition in an identifiable concern.

### Renting vs. Owning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: וּשְׂכִירוּת מִי קָנְיָא? וְהָתְנַן: אַף בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַשְׂכִּיר — לֹא לְבֵית דִּירָה אָמְרוּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּכְנִיס לְתוֹכוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׂכִירוּת קָנְיָא, הַאי כִּי קָא מְעַיֵּיל — לְבֵיתֵיהּ קָא מְעַיֵּיל! שָׁאנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דַּחֲמִירָא, דִּכְתִיב: "וְלֹא תָבִיא תוֹעֵבָה אֶל בֵּיתֶךָ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, objects to** the Gemara's assumption that leasing confers ownership. **And** by **leasing** an item, **does one** actually **acquire** it? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna (21a): **Even in a place** with regard to **which** the Sages **said** that it is permitted for a Jew **to rent** a house to a gentile, **they did not say** that one may rent it **for** use as **a residence, because** the gentiles will **bring** objects of **idol worship into it?** The objection is as follows: **And if it enters your mind** to say that through **leasing** one **acquires** an item or property, then **when this** gentile **brings** the idols **into** the house **he brings them into his** own **house.** Why, then, is it prohibited for a Jew to rent a residence to a gentile? The Gemara answers: **Idol worship is different, as it is** a particularly **severe** prohibition, and therefore even an item that does not entirely belong to a Jew is treated with great stringency. **As it is written: "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house"** (Deuteronomy 7:26), and this house still retains the name of its Jewish owner.

Rav Shisha the son of Rav Idi presses the question of *sechirut* further. *Usechirut mi kanya?* — does leasing actually constitute acquisition? He cites a Mishnah that will appear on 21a: even in places where it is permitted to rent items to a non-Jew, one may not rent an apartment, *lo leveit dirah amru mipnei she-machnis letochoh avodat kochavim* — because of the concern that he will bring *avodat kochavim*, idolatrous practice, into the space.

If *sechirut* were a full acquisition, then once rented the space would be entirely the renter's and no concern would attach to the owner. The fact that we restrict what can be rented implies the owner retains some relationship to the property. *Shani avodah kochavim d'chamira* — the domain of idolatry is treated with special stringency, grounded in the verse from *Devarim*: *v'lo tavi toevah el beitecha*, do not bring an abomination into your home. Even when one has rented a space, the prohibition of allowing *toevah* into what remains legally connected to the Jewish owner applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: וּשְׂכִירוּת מִי קָנְיָא? וְהָא תְּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִכֹּהֵן — יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה, וְכֹהֵן שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ עָלָיו — לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׂכִירוּת קָנְיָא, אַמַּאי לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה? פָּרָה דִּידֵיהּ הִיא! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׂכִירוּת לָא קָנְיָא. וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ שְׂכִירוּת לָא קָנְיָא, גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם שְׂכִירוּת, וּגְזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם שְׁאֵלָה, וּגְזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם נִסְיוֹנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Mesharshiyya,** also **objects to** the Gemara's assumption that leasing confers ownership. **And** by **leasing** an item, **does one** actually **acquire** it? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:9): **An Israelite who rented a cow from a priest may feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as the animal belongs to a priest; **and** conversely, **a priest who rented a cow from an Israelite, although the responsibility to feed it is incumbent upon him, he may not feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as it does not belong to him. **And if it enters your mind** to say that through **leasing** one **acquires** the item, **why can't** the priest **feed it** vetches of *teruma*? After all, **it is** currently **his own cow. Rather, learn from** here that one **does not acquire** an item through **leasing.** The Gemara comments: **And now that you** have **said** that one **does not acquire** an item through **leasing,** and therefore an animal that was leased to a gentile still belongs to the Jew, the original proposal can be accepted: The reason that one cannot sell large livestock to gentiles is a rabbinic **decree due to** the concern of **leasing, and a decree due to** the concern of **lending** the animal to the gentile, **and** also **a decree due to** the concern of **testing.**

Rav Yitzchak the son of Rav Mesharsheya raises yet another challenge on the *sechirut* question, this one from the laws of *terumah*. An *Yisrael* who rents a cow from a *kohen* may feed it *karshinei terumah* — a leguminous plant used as fodder that has the status of *terumah*. But a *kohen* who rents a cow from an *Yisrael*, even though he bears the obligation to feed it, may not feed it *karshinei terumah*. *V'i salka da'atach sechirut kanya, amai lo yachilena? Parah dideih hi* — if the leasing were equivalent to acquisition, the *kohen* renting the cow should be able to feed it whatever is his, since it would be his cow. The fact that he cannot do so demonstrates: *ela shma mina sechirut lo kanya*, leasing is not equivalent to purchase.

And now that we have established that *sechirut* is not full acquisition, the Gemara draws the full picture: *v'hashta d'amart sechirut lo kanya, gzera mishum sechirut, u-gzera mishum she'elah, u-gzera mishum nisyoni* — the prohibition on selling large animals to non-Jews is undergirded by all three concerns: the leasing concern, the lending concern, and the *nisyoni* concern of demonstrating the animal near *Shabbat*.

## Housing & Idolatry

### No Apartment Rentals

The Gemara's discussion of *sechirut* and ownership naturally raises the question of renting housing. The relevant Mishnah, which will be studied on 21a, teaches that even in locales where renting to non-Jews is permitted in general, one may not rent them a residential dwelling — *lo leveit dirah amru mipnei she-machnis letochoh avodat kochavim* — out of concern that the tenant will introduce idolatrous practice into the space.

### Ownership Boundaries

This ruling raises a fundamental conceptual question: if *sechirut* is not acquisition, then when a non-Jew rents an apartment, whose home is it really? And if it remains in some meaningful sense the Jewish owner's home, what responsibility does the owner bear for what transpires inside? The concern is not merely practical but spiritual: the character of the space and the practices conducted within it implicate the owner even after the keys have changed hands.

This maps onto intuitive analogies. A renter may possess the interior of a space without possessing it in every relevant sense — a landlord can and does restrict certain uses, whether making structural changes or activities that affect the building or neighbors. The Gemara is asking the same question from a spiritual dimension: in what sense does the Jewish owner's relationship to the property continue after he has rented it to a non-Jew?

### Toevah Prohibition

The Gemara resolves this with a principle specific to *avodat kochavim*: *shani avodah kochavim d'chamira*, idolatry carries a special stringency beyond other prohibitions. The basis is the verse in *Devarim*: *v'lo tavi toevah el beitecha* — do not bring an abomination into your home. The word *toevah* here is not merely technical but connotes something morally and spiritually repugnant. Crucially, the prohibition is not limited to practicing idolatry oneself; it extends to allowing it to enter into one's home. Even after rental, where the tenant has taken possession for purposes of ordinary use, the owner's Jewish home retains enough of its character that enabling *avodat kochavim* within it violates this prohibition.

This creates a unique category: *sechirut* may not constitute acquisition for most legal purposes, but the heightened prohibition around idolatry means that the owner cannot divest responsibility for what occurs in his property simply by signing a lease.

## Middleman Device

### Rav Adda's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא שְׁרָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי חֲמָרָא אַיְּדָא דְּסַפְסִירָא, אִי מִשּׁוּם נִסְיוֹנֵי — הָא לָא יָדְעָה לְקָלֵיהּ דְּאָזְלָא מֵחֲמָתֵיהּ, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם שְׁאֵלָה וּשְׂכִירוּת — כֵּיוָן דְּלָא דִּידֵיהּ הִיא לָא מוֹשֵׁיל וְלָא מוֹגַר, וְעוֹד — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא נִיגַלֵּי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rav Adda permitted** the owners of a donkey **to sell** their **donkey** to gentiles **by means of** a Jewish **middleman [*desafseira*].** He reasoned as follows: **If** the concern is **due to testing,** in this case the animal **does not recognize the voice of** the middleman so **that it would walk because of him. And if** the concern is **due to lending and leasing, since** the donkey **is not his,** that middleman **would neither lend nor lease** it. **Additionally,** the middleman would not lease or lend the animal **because** he wants to sell it and **does not** want any **blemish to be revealed in it.**

A practical solution to these restrictions is presented in the case of *chamra l'yada d'sapsira*: Rav Adda permitted someone to sell their donkey to a non-Jew by routing the transaction through a *sapsir*, a middleman or broker, rather than conducting the sale directly.

### Middleman Advantages

Rav Adda articulates three reasons why the middleman device resolves the concerns raised above. First, *i mishum nisyoni, halo yada l'kala d'azal mechmatei* — if the concern is the *nisyoni* problem, the test-drive near *Shabbat*, that concern evaporates because the animal does not know the middleman's voice. The seller's voice is what activates the animal; the middleman's voice carries no such power. Second, *i mishum she'elah u'sechirut, keivan d'lav didei, lo moshil v'lo mogar* — if the concern is that the animal might be lent or leased back in a problematic way, this too is resolved: because the animal does not belong to the middleman, he has no authority to lend or lease it to anyone. Third, *mishum d'la naga l'vei muma* — the middleman will not handle the animal in any way that might cause a *mum*, a blemish, because he has no interest in damaging goods he is merely brokering.

One might reasonably ask whether the non-Jewish buyer could simply approach the middleman and request that he relay a request to the original owner for a test-drive or a rental arrangement. Rav Adda's ruling is a case study, and not every permissive ruling resolves every possible objection. The *nisyoni* concern in particular is tied to a very specific scenario — the seller using his own voice on *erev Shabbat* — and the middleman structure severs that specific link.

## Rav Huna's Case

### Sale & Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא זַבֵּין הָהִיא פָּרָה לְגוֹי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד מָר הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימוֹר לִשְׁחִיטָה זַבְנַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna sold a certain cow to a gentile. Rav Ḥisda said to him: What is the reason** that **the Master acted in that manner?** Rav Huna **said to him:** I can **say** that **he purchased it** in order **to slaughter** it, not to use it for labor.

The Gemara records that Rav Huna sold a cow to a non-Jew. Rav Chisda challenged him: *mai ta'ama avad mar hachi?* — master, what is your reasoning? The challenge is straightforward. The Mishnah establishes *ein mocherin behemah gasah*, and a cow is paradigmatically a *behemah gasah*. How did Rav Huna permit himself to do this?

Before Rav Huna's defense is presented, it is worth noting the shift in the underlying concern. With *behemah dakah* the primary worry was *reva'iyah*. With *behemah gasah*, the Gemara has been explaining, the concern is *melacha* on *Shabbat*. Bestiality with a large animal — a horse or cow — is simply not a realistic concern in the same way.

### Shechita Assumption

Rav Huna's defense is: *eimor l'shechitah zabna* — we may assume the animal is being purchased for slaughter, not for labor. The non-Jew wants to eat, and a cow is a plausible candidate for *shechita*. If the buyer's purpose is to slaughter the animal for food, then the Shabbat work concern never arises, because the animal will not be put to labor at all.

### Shemitah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּאָמְרִינַן כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִמְכּוֹר אָדָם פָּרָה הַחוֹרֶשֶׁת בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְשׁוֹחְטָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna added: **And from where do you say that** in **a case like this we say** that the animal will be slaughtered, and one is not concerned about placing a stumbling block before the blind, despite the fact that the animal could be used to violate a prohibition? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 5:8) that **Beit Shammai say: A person may not sell a cow that plows in the Sabbatical** Year, as it is prohibited to plow during the Sabbatical Year and the buyer presumably wants it for this purpose. **And Beit Hillel permit** selling the cow, **since** the buyer **can slaughter it** rather than use it for plowing. This shows that according to Beit Hillel, whose opinion is accepted as *halakha*, one may assume that an animal will be used for a permitted purpose, rather than for a prohibited action.

Rav Huna then supports his position with a precedent from another domain. He cites the dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding the *shemitah* year: *Beit Shammai lo yimkor adam para choreshet bashvi'it* — Beit Shammai rules that one may not sell a plowing cow during the Sabbatical year, since the buyer will presumably use it to plow, which is forbidden during *shemitah*. *U-Beit Hillel matirin mipnei sheyachol leshochta* — but Beit Hillel permits such a sale because we can assume the buyer intends to slaughter it. The *halacha* follows *Beit Hillel*. We do not project forbidden intent onto a buyer; we assign the innocent motive — slaughter — and permit the transaction.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם אֵין אָדָם מְצוֶּּוה עַל שְׁבִיתַת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, הָכָא אָדָם מְצוֶּּוה עַל שְׁבִיתַת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: Are** these matters **comparable? There,** with regard to the Sabbatical Year, **a person is not commanded to** let **his animal rest during the Sabbatical** Year, as there is no prohibition against his animal performing labor. Therefore, there is no reason to decree that the sale is prohibited lest he lend, lease, or test the animal. As for the concern that he is misleading the buyer and encouraging him to sin, he may rely on the fact that the buyer probably intends to slaughter the animal. But **here,** with regard to selling an animal to a gentile, **a person is commanded to** let **his animal rest on Shabbat,** and therefore the Sages decreed the sale prohibited in case he comes to lend, lease, or test the animal.

Rava challenges the comparison: *mi dami?* — are these really analogous? The core difference is that *shemitah* and *Shabbat* impose different obligations regarding animals. *Hatam ein adam mitzuveh al shvitat behemto bashvi'it* — in the Sabbatical year, a person has no commandment to ensure that his animal rests; the commandment of *shemitah* applies to the land, not to the animal. *Vehacha adam mitzuveh al shvitat behemto beShabbat* — on *Shabbat*, however, there is an explicit positive commandment to ensure one's animal rests. This commandment is one of the *aseret hadibrot* themselves. The two cases are therefore not parallel: the Beit Hillel leniency in *shemitah* operates in an area where no animal-rest obligation exists, while selling an animal that will work on *Shabbat* implicates a direct commandment.

### Abaye Extends Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאָדָם מְצוֶּּוה אָסוּר? וַהֲרֵי שָׂדֶה, דְּאָדָם מְצוֶּּוה עַל שְׁבִיתַת שָׂדֵהוּ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, וּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִמְכּוֹר אָדָם שְׂדֵה נִיר בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהוֹבִירָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **And** does this mean that **wherever a person is commanded** to allow his possessions to rest it is **prohibited** to sell an item to one who might use it to perform labor, even if he might also use it for an innocent purpose? **But** there is the case of **a field, as a person is commanded to** let **his field rest during the Sabbatical** Year, **and** yet **we learned** in a *baraita* that **Beit Shammai say: A person may not sell a plowed field during the Sabbatical** Year, as it is presumed that the buyer will sow it, **and Beit Hillel permit** this sale, **since** the buyer **can let it lie fallow** during the Sabbatical Year. In this case, although one is commanded to let his field rest during the Sabbatical Year, he may still sell it under the assumption that the buyer will use the field in a permitted manner.

Abaye presses back on Rava's distinction. If the rule is that wherever one is commanded regarding one's possessions one cannot sell an item that might be used in violation of that commandment, then we should find the same prohibition even in *shemitah* cases involving the land itself. *Veharei sadeh de'adam mitzuveh al shvitat sadehu bashvi'it* — a person is commanded to let his field rest during the Sabbatical year. Yet the Mishnah records: *Beit Shammai lo yimkor adam sadeh nir bashvi'it u-Beit Hillel matirin mipnei sheyachol lehovirah* — Beit Shammai forbids selling a plowed and prepared field during *shemitah*, but Beit Hillel permits because one can assume the buyer will leave it unworked this year and use it the following year. Here we have a case where the seller *is* commanded regarding the rest of his possession — the field — and yet Beit Hillel still permits the sale on the strength of an innocent assumption about use. This would seem to undercut Rava's distinction.

### Innocent Motive Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיתְלֵא תָּלֵינַן, וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּמְצוֶּּוה, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא לְמִיתְלֵי — לָא תָּלֵינַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵינוֹ מְצוֶּּוה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Anywhere that it is** possible **to assign** an innocent motive, **one assigns** such a motive, **and** this applies **even though one is commanded** to allow the item to rest. **And anywhere that it is not** possible **to assign** an innocent motive, **one does not assign** an innocent motive, **even though one is not commanded** to allow the item to rest.

Rav Ashi rejects the attempts to derive a clean rule from the commanded/non-commanded distinction, and proposes instead a more nuanced principle: *kol heicha d'ika lemitli talinan v'af al gav d'mitzuveh, v'chol heicha d'leika lemitli lo talinan af al gav d'eino mitzuveh*.

The determining factor is not whether one is commanded regarding the rest of the item in question, but whether it is *possible to assign an innocent motive*. Wherever we can credibly "hang" the transaction on a permitted purpose — as we can say a cow might be purchased for *shechita*, or a field might be held fallow this year and worked next — we do so, and the sale is permitted, even where a rest commandment exists. Conversely, where no such innocent motive can plausibly be assigned — as in the case of specific implements like the plow, yoke, or winnowing fork during *shemitah*, which have no plausible use other than the forbidden one — we cannot assign such a motive, and the sale is forbidden, even where no commandment specifically applies to those tools.

This is a striking principle. The operative question is not the formal structure of the commandment but the practical availability of a credible innocent reading of the transaction.

## Rabbah vs. Abaye

### Suspect Buyer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה זַבֵּין הָהוּא חֲמָרָא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הֶחָשׁוּד לִמְכּוֹר לְגוֹי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי טַעְמָא עָבֵד מָר הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל זַבֵּינִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אָזֵיל וּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ לְגוֹי! לְגוֹי קָא מְזַבֵּין, לְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא קָא מְזַבֵּין?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabba sold a certain donkey to a Jew who was suspected of selling** large livestock **to a gentile. Abaye said to** Rabba: **What is the reason** that **the Master acted in this manner?** Rabba **said to him: I sold** the donkey **to a Jew.** Abaye **said to him: But he will go and sell it to a gentile.** Rabba responded: Is the only possibility that **he will sell to a gentile,** and **he will not sell it to a Jew?** Since there is no reason to assume that he will sell specifically to a gentile rather than to a Jew, there is no problem in selling to him.

The Gemara now presents a case study that tests these principles in a different direction. *Rabbah zavin hahu chamra l'Yisrael hachashid limkor l'oved kochavim* — Rabbah sold a donkey to a Jew who was suspected of selling animals onward to idolaters.

### Abaye Objects

Abaye immediately challenged Rabbah: *mai ta'ama avad mar hachi?* — master, why did you do this? Rabbah defended himself: *ana l'Yisrael zavini* — I sold it to a Jew. The transaction on its face is entirely within the community; there is no prohibition on selling a donkey to a fellow Jew. Abaye pressed: *v'ha azil u'mzavin lei l'oved kochavim* — but he is going to go and sell it to a non-Jew. Rabbah pushed back: is that certain? *L'oved kochavim ka mzavin l'Yisrael lo ka mzavin?* — you're concerned he'll sell it to a non-Jew, but couldn't he just as readily sell it to a Jew? We don't know what he'll do. Must I refuse to transact with anyone who might conceivably pass something along to the wrong party?

The dynamic of this exchange is important to follow. Rabbah is taking a lenient position and Abaye is challenging him sufficiently that — as the Gemara will reveal — Rabbah ultimately changes his mind. Abaye then cites Rav Nachman's ruling to clinch the argument.

### Lifnei Iver

Running beneath this entire dispute is the principle of *lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — do not place a stumbling block before the blind. If I sell something to a person who will then use it to violate a prohibition, I have enabled that violation, and the Torah holds me responsible. The question the Gemara is pressing is: how many links in the chain does *lifnei iver* extend?

The clearest case is a direct transaction: I sell prohibited merchandise to someone who will use it for a forbidden purpose. But what of a transaction one step removed — I sell to a Jew who will sell to a non-Jew, thereby enabling the non-Jew's prohibited use? And does it matter whether the intermediate party is a Jew or a *kuti*? These gradations are not merely theoretical; they determine the practical contours of permissible commerce.

## Kutim vs. Goyim

### Small Animals to Kutim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִמְכּוֹר בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה לַכּוּתִים — מוֹכְרִין, שֶׁלֹּא לִמְכּוֹר — אֵין מוֹכְרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁידִי אַרְבִיעָה, וּמִי חֲשִׁידִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין מַעֲמִידִין בְּהֵמָה בְּפוּנְדְּקָאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, זְכָרִים אֵצֶל זְכָרִים, וּנְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר נְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל זְכָרִים וּזְכָרִים אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת. וְאֵין מוֹסְרִין בְּהֵמָה לָרוֹעֶה שֶׁלָּהֶן, וְאֵין מְיַיחֲדִין עִמָּהֶם, וְאֵין מוֹסְרִין לָהֶם תִּינוֹק לְלַמְּדוֹ סֵפֶר וּלְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת, אֲבָל מַעֲמִידִין בְּהֵמָה בְּפוּנְדְּקָאוֹת שֶׁל כּוּתִים, זְכָרִים אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת וּנְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל זְכָרִים, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר זְכָרִים אֵצֶל זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת. וּמוֹסְרִין בְּהֵמָה לָרוֹעֶה שֶׁלָּהֶן, וּמְיַיחֲדִין עִמָּהֶם, וּמוֹסְרִין לָהֶם תִּינוֹק לְלַמְּדוֹ סֵפֶר וּלְלַמְּדוֹ אוּמָּנוּת; אַלְמָא לָא חֲשִׁידִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rabbah's opinion from a *baraita*: In **a place where** the people **were accustomed to sell small livestock to Samaritans, one may sell** the animals to them; in a place **where** the people were **not** accustomed **to sell** them **one may not sell** the animals to them. **What is the reason** that the sale of small livestock to Samaritans is prohibited? **If we say** that it is **because** Samaritans **are suspected of** engaging in **bestiality, are they suspected** of this practice? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not keep an animal in the inns of gentiles. Male** animals may not be placed **with men,** as they are suspected of engaging in bestiality, **and female** animals may not be left **with women,** despite the fact that there is no concern that they may engage in bestiality. **And needless to say,** it is prohibited to leave **female** animals **with men, and male** animals **with women.** The *baraita* continues: **And one may not entrust an animal to** a gentile **shepherd, and one may not seclude oneself with** gentiles, due to the danger that this entails. **And one may not entrust a child to them to teach him** how to read **books or to teach him a craft. But one may keep an animal in the inns of Samaritans,** as they are not suspected of violating a Torah prohibition and engaging in bestiality. **Male** animals may be placed **with women and female** animals may be left **with men, and needless to say,** it is permitted to leave **male** animals **with men and female** animals **with women.** The *baraita* concludes: **And one may entrust an animal to** a Samaritan **shepherd, and one may seclude oneself with** Samaritans, **and one may entrust a child to them to teach him** how to read **books and to teach him a craft.** The Gemara infers from the *baraita*: **Evidently,** Samaritans are **not suspected** of engaging in bestiality, yet livestock may not be sold to them, as they are suspected of selling it to gentiles.

Abaye advances his case against Rabbah by citing a *baraita*: *makom shenahagu limkor behemah dakah lekutim mocherim, shelo limkor ein mocherim* — in a place where it is the custom to sell small livestock to *kutim*, Samaritans, one may sell them; in a place where it is not the custom, one may not. The question is what drives this restriction on *behemah dakah* in the places where it applies, and the *baraita* will illuminate the differential treatment of *kutim* versus outright idolaters.

### Trust in Kutim

The Gemara explores the basis for distinguishing between *kutim* and *ovdei kochavim* more broadly. Is the restriction on *behemah dakah* in some places a concern about *reva'iyah*? The *baraita* presents extensive evidence that *kutim* are substantially trusted: *ma'amidin behemah b'fundekaot shel kutim, zakharim etzel nekevot u-nekevot etzel zakharim* — we may stable our animals at Samaritan inns even with males next to females and vice versa, the very arrangements we would not permit with idolaters. Furthermore, *mosrim behemah le-ro'eh shelahem* — we entrust our animals to their shepherds. And we have *yichud* with them, and we entrust our children to them to be taught *sefer* and a craft.

The basis for this trust is explicit: *kutim* accept the *Torah she-bikhtav*. They observe the written Torah, even though they do not accept the *Torah she-be'al peh*. Because of this, they are presumed to abide by the prohibitions the written Torah itself would restrain them from, including *reva'iyah*. *Alma lo chashidi* — they are simply not suspected of such misconduct.

### Fear of Resale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶם לֹא זַיִין וְלֹא כְּלֵי זַיִין, וְאֵין מַשְׁחִיזִין לָהֶן אֶת הַזַּיִין, וְאֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן לֹא סַדָּן וְלֹא קוֹלָרִין וְלֹא כְּבָלִים וְלֹא שַׁלְשְׁלָאוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, אֶחָד גּוֹי וְאֶחָד כּוּתִי. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי נֵימָא דַּחֲשִׁידִי אַשְּׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, וּמִי חֲשִׁידִי? הָאָמְרַתְּ: וּמְיַיחֲדִין עִמָּהֶן! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתֵי לְזַבּוֹנַהּ לְגוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not sell weapons to** gentiles **or** the auxiliary **equipment of weapons, and one may not sharpen weapons for them. And one may not sell them stocks** used for fastening the feet of prisoners, **or** iron **neck chains [*kolarin*], or foot chains, or iron chains.** This prohibition applies equally to **both a gentile and a Samaritan.** Abaye analyzes this *baraita*: **What is the reason** for the prohibition against selling these items to Samaritans? **If we say that they are suspected of bloodshed,** that is difficult: **But are they suspected** of this? **Didn't you say** that **one may seclude oneself with them,** which indicates that they are not suspected of bloodshed? **Rather,** it is prohibited to sell these items to Samaritans **because they will come to sell them to a gentile.** According to this reasoning, it should likewise be prohibited to sell a donkey to a Jew who is suspected of selling animals to gentiles.

And yet, *behemah gasah* presents a distinct problem even with *kutim*. Given the Mishnah's blanket statement — *ein mochrin lahem behemah gasah*, with no qualification for custom, unlike the *behemah dakah* rule — there is an additional concern beyond *reva'iyah*: if we sell a large animal to a *kuti*, he might in turn sell it to a non-Jew. Even if the *kuti* himself is trusted not to misuse the animal, the chain does not end with him. And this is precisely the parallel to the case of Rabbah selling to the suspect Jew: in both cases, the concern is not the immediate buyer's conduct but what happens at the next link.

The implication Abaye is drawing is unmistakable: just as we do not sell *behemah gasah* to a *kuti* because he might sell it to an idolater, we similarly cannot sell it to a Jew who is suspected of doing the same.

### Weapons Forbidden

The *baraita* continues with a further restriction that applies to both *ovdei kochavim* and *kutim* alike, with no distinction between the two: *ein mochrin lahem lo zayin v'lo klei zayin v'ein mashchizin lahem et ha-zayin* — we may not sell them weapons or weapon accessories, and we may not sharpen weapons for them. Additionally, *ein mochrin lahem lo sadan v'lo kularin v'lo kevalim v'lo shalshelaot shel barzel* — we may not sell them a *sadan* (leg shackles for slaves), *kularin* (neck collars), *kevalim* (chains), or iron chains. And this applies equally to idolaters and *kutim*: *echad ovdei kokhavim ve-echad kutim*.

### Weapons Resale Fear

This creates an apparent tension. If we trust *kutim* so substantially — entrusting them with our animals, our children, permitting *yichud* — why are we treating them exactly like idolaters when it comes to weapons and restraints? *I nima dechashidi ashfichut damim, umi chashidi?* — if the reason is that we suspect them of bloodshed, are they really so suspected? We just said we have *yichud* with them. That level of trust is incompatible with suspecting them of violence.

The resolution: *ela mishum de'ati lezavona le'ovdei kochavim* — the concern is not that the *kutim* themselves will use these items to shed blood, but that they will sell them onward to idolaters who will. The restriction is not a character judgment about *kutim* but a practical concern about what happens downstream. This is identical in structure to the *behemah gasah* concern: we don't doubt the *kuti*'s own conduct; we worry about where the item ends up.

And this conclusion feeds directly back into Abaye's challenge to Rabbah: Rav Nachman has ruled explicitly — *kederech she'amru asur limkor le'ovved kochavim, kach asur limkor le'Yisrael hachashud limkor le'ovved kochavim* — just as it is forbidden to sell certain items directly to an idolater, it is equally forbidden to sell to a Jew who is suspected of reselling to an idolater. The chain of liability runs through the intermediary.

## Rabbah vs. Abaye (Continued)

### Rav Nachman Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: כּוּתִי לָא עָבֵיד תְּשׁוּבָה, יִשְׂרָאֵל עָבֵיד תְּשׁוּבָה, וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָמְרוּ אָסוּר לִמְכּוֹר לְגוֹי, כָּךְ אָסוּר לִמְכּוֹר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הֶחָשׁוּד לִמְכּוֹר לְגוֹי! רְהַיט בָּתְרֵיהּ תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: פַּרְסָא בְּחָלָא, וְלָא אַדְרְכֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that there is a difference between a Jew and a Samaritan, as **a Samaritan will** likely **not repent** and will sell to a gentile, whereas **a Jew will** likely **repent** and not sell these items, this reasoning is incorrect. **But doesn't Rav Naḥman say** explicitly that **Rabba bar Avuh says: Just as** the Sages **said** that **it is prohibited to sell to a gentile, so too it is prohibited to sell to a Jew who is suspected of selling to a gentile?** When Rabba heard this and realized that Abaye was correct, **he ran three parasangs after** the buyer who purchased his donkey to revoke the sale, as the Jew was suspected of selling to gentiles; **and some say** that he ran **one parasang through sand. But he did not** succeed in **overtaking him.**

Abaye finalizes his argument by invoking Rav Nachman's ruling explicitly: *kederech she'amru asur limkor le'ovved kochavim, kach asur limkor le'Yisrael hachashud limkor le'ovved kochavim*. This is not merely Abaye's personal opinion; it is the accepted position as stated by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha. Just as we forbid selling to an idolater directly, we equally forbid selling to a Jew who is known to be suspected of funneling such sales to idolaters. The *lifnei iver* concern does not evaporate simply because the prohibited transaction is one step removed from the original seller.

Rabbah had attempted to deflect this: *v'chi teima, kuti lo avid teshuvah, Yisrael avid teshuvah* — perhaps one could argue that a *kuti* will not do *teshuvah* and change his ways, whereas the suspect Jew might repent and decide not to sell the donkey to an idolater after all. But Rav Nachman's ruling forecloses this: the established rule treats the suspected-Jew case identically to the idolater case, and the possibility of *teshuvah* does not lift the prohibition at the moment of sale.

### Rabbah's Retraction

Upon hearing this reasoning, Rabbah understood he had made a mistake. *Rahit batrei tlata parsei* — he ran three *parsa'ot* after the Jew to whom he had sold the donkey in order to retract the sale. Some versions of the tradition say he ran only one *parsa* — but through sand, an extraordinarily difficult effort. A *parsa* is approximately four kilometers, making three *parsa'ot* somewhere between twelve kilometers and, by some reckonings, between 7.2 and 8.7 miles. *V'lo adrachei* — but he was unable to catch him.

Nevertheless, the legal outcome is clear: Rabbah, upon reflection, agreed that the sale had been improper. The ruling stands that one may not sell to a Jew suspected of selling onward to those who would misuse the item.

## Necessity & Coexistence

### Bandits & Weapons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי בַּר אַבָּא: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָסוּר לִמְכּוֹר לְגוֹי, אָסוּר לִמְכּוֹר לְלִסְטִים יִשְׂרָאֵל. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דַּחֲשִׁיד דְּקָטֵיל — פְּשִׁיטָא, הַיְינוּ גּוֹי. וְאִי דְּלָא קָטֵיל, אַמַּאי לָא? לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא קָטֵיל, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּמַשְׁמוֹטָא, דְּזִימְנִין דְּעָבֵיד לְאַצּוֹלֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the *baraita* that discusses the prohibition against selling weapons, the Gemara relates that **Rav Dimi bar Abba says: Just as it is prohibited to sell to a gentile, it is prohibited to sell to** an armed **bandit** who is **a Jew.** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this prohibition? **If** the thief **is suspected of killing,** isn't it **obvious** that it is prohibited? After all, he **is** the same as **a gentile.** Providing a Jew who might kill with weapons is no different from giving a weapon to a gentile, as in both cases one violates the prohibition: Do not place a stumbling block before the blind. **And if** he is a bandit **who does not kill, why not** sell to him? The Gemara answers: **Actually,** Rav Dimi bar Abba is referring to a bandit **who does not kill, and here we are dealing with** a bandit **who steals, as sometimes he makes use** of his weapon **to save himself** when he is caught. Consequently, it is prohibited to sell him weapons in case he kills with them in self-defense.

Rav Bibi bar Abba extends the principle further: just as we may not sell to a non-Jew, we may not sell to a *listim* — a Jewish bandit or outlaw. The Gemara immediately probes the contours of this rule. *Heichi dami* — under what circumstances does it apply? If the *listim* in question is suspected of murder, then the case is trivial — *peshita, hainu oved kochavim*, he is simply equivalent to an idolater for purposes of this prohibition, and of course we may not arm him. But what if this is a bandit who specializes in theft and not in killing? Why not sell to him?

The Gemara answers: *be'mashmuta de'zimnin de'avid le'atzolei nafsha* — we are dealing with a bandit who carries a weapon not because he plans to use it offensively but because he wants it for self-protection in case a situation escalates. The reason we nonetheless cannot sell to him is that the weapon is what enables his continued life of crime. The bandit's confidence in his ability to use the weapon if necessary is precisely what makes the weapon instrumentally dangerous, even if he does not plan to use it. The same *lifnei iver* logic applies: we may not supply what enables the forbidden enterprise.

### Shield Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן תְּרִיסִין, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן תְּרִיסִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּמַגְּנוּ עֲלַיְיהוּ, אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ חִיטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי נָמֵי לָא! אָמַר רַב: אִי אֶפְשָׁר, הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: One may not sell shields [*terisin*] to** gentiles, despite the fact that they are used for protection, not to attack others. **And some say: One may sell shields to them.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** behind the opinion that prohibits selling shields to gentiles? **If we say** it is **because they protect them** in wartime, **if so,** then **even wheat and barley should not** be sold to them. **Rav said:** **If** it were **possible** to avoid selling produce to gentiles without incurring their animosity, **indeed** it would be prohibited to sell them. Since limiting sales to gentiles to such an extent would cause great harm, it is only prohibited to sell them shields.

The Gemara now introduces a dispute regarding a specific category of item: *trisim*, shields. *Ein mochrin lahem trisim, v'yesh omrim mochrin lahem trisim* — one opinion holds we may not sell shields to non-Jews; another permits it. Shields are protective by nature — they are defensive items, not weapons of attack. This makes the case more ambiguous than swords or spears.

The Gemara probes the reasoning of the prohibitive opinion. *Eileima mishum de'meginu aleihu* — if the reason is that shields protect them in battle, then by the same logic we should refuse to sell them *chiti u'se'ari*, wheat and barley, which sustain their armies equally. That conclusion is clearly untenable.

### Rav's Pragmatism

Rav addresses this directly: *i efshar, hachi nami* — if it were possible to refuse everything without consequence, one might indeed forbid even food staples. The underlying logic of restricting all commerce that might benefit an enemy is coherent in principle. But the practical reality is that total commercial isolation from the surrounding population would inflict massive harm — economic, social, and political — on the Jewish community. Rav's position reflects a frank pragmatism: comprehensive restriction of trade would cause greater harm than selective trade. The animosity generated by total isolation would be more dangerous than the marginal benefit gained from any particular restriction.

The principle, therefore, is not "restrict everything that could theoretically benefit an enemy," but rather a targeted prohibition on items whose primary use is violence or idolatry. Items that serve broad civilian purposes — food, protective gear used for escape rather than attack — fall outside the prohibition.

### Shields Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: תְּרִיסִין הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא, דְּכִי שְׁלִים זֵינַיְיהוּ קָטְלִי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מוֹכְרִים לָהֶם תְּרִיסִין, דְּכִי שְׁלִים זֵינַיְיהוּ מִעְרָק עׇרְקִי. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הֲלָכָה כְּיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say:** With regard to **shields, this is the reason that** one is **not** allowed to sell them to gentiles: **As when their** use of their **weapon is finished** in battle, **they kill with these** shields. **And** accordingly, the reason that **some say** in the *baraita* that **one may sell shields to them** is because they maintain that this is not a concern, **as when their weapon is finished they flee,** rather than use their shield as a weapon. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance** with the opinion cited as: **Some say.**

The Gemara presents one rationale for permitting shields: *v'chi shalim zayin u'marka raki* — at the conclusion of battle, soldiers throw down their weapons and use their shields to flee. The shield is thus primarily associated with retreat and self-preservation rather than with offensive violence.

The final ruling: *amar Rav Nachman bar Abba bar Avuha halacha k'yesh omrim* — the *halacha* follows the permissive opinion. Shields may be sold to non-Jews. The assumption is that they will be used for protection and escape, not for aggression, and this suffices to assign the innocent motive that Rav Ashi's principle requires. Where we can credibly hang the transaction on a permitted purpose, we do so — and the *halacha* reflects that we should seek coexistence where it can be responsibly achieved, rather than defaulting to isolation.
