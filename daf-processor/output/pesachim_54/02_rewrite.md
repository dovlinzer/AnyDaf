# Pesachim 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yom Kippur Candles

### Mishnah: Candles on Yom…

We are at the *mishnah* on *amud bet* of daf 54, still within the fifth *perek* — a *perek* that has become genuinely rich and interesting. Its opening engaged the core meta-question that so many of us want to understand more deeply: what makes *minhagim* binding, and under what circumstances can they or cannot they be changed? No definitive black-and-white answers emerged, but what followed was a wide-ranging collection of particular practices: *shemita* issues, *Pesach* questions such as working on *erev Pesach* and eating roasted meat at the *leil seder*, *Avoda Zara* questions about selling to non-Jews, matters touching *Nezikin*, *Tahara*, and *Zeroim*. Now we arrive at a *Yom Kippur* issue.

The *mishnah* opens: *makom she-nahagu le-hadlik et ha-ner be-leil Yom ha-Kippurim, madlikin* — in a place where the custom is to light candles on the night of *Yom Kippur*, one lights. *Makom she-nahagu shelo le-hadlik, ein madlikin* — in a place where the custom is not to light, one does not light.

### Why Light Candles?

Why do we light candles on *erev Shabbat*? Because there is light present when one is eating — the connection to *shalom bayit* is specifically realized through the meal and the preference that one light in the place where one will eat. On *Yom Kippur*, however, one is not eating. So should candles be lit at all?

That is precisely the question. If one has a *minhag* to light, one does; if one has a *minhag* not to light, one does not. One might ask whether the default assumption is that one lights and some communities have a *minhag* not to, or the reverse. As the *Gemara* will make clear — in a manner analogous to the earlier discussion about roasted meat on *Pesach* — there appear to be active *minhagim* in both directions, each grounded in its own reasoning, not simply one practice that exists and one that is the absence of practice.

### Public Lighting

The *mishnah* continues: *u-madlikin be-vatei kenesiot u-vatei midrashot* — one lights in the synagogues and study halls, *u-mevo'ot ha-afelim* — in dark alleyways, *ve-al gabei ha-cholim* — and beside the sick. In these contexts, lighting is essentially equivalent to turning on the lights for safety or basic function, or in the case of the *beit knesset*, perhaps carrying a dimension of *kavod beit ha-knesset* as well. Occasionally the sick person needs light, and occasionally needs darkness — the *mishnah* elsewhere notes that one may extinguish a candle before a *choleh*. The practical lighting in public spaces and for the infirm is not in dispute; the question concerns domestic lighting.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

A *baraita* is then cited: *Beit Shammai le-hadlik* — those who say one should light — and those who say one should not light — *sheneihem le-davar echad nitkavenu* — both had one thing in mind. What was that one thing? According to *Rashi*, and as alluded to in the *Gemara*, the concern underlying both positions is the prohibition of marital relations on *Yom Kippur*. One school holds that one should *davka* have the lights burning so that there is no *yetzer ha-ra* that might lead to transgression — since one typically does not engage in relations in a lit room. The other school holds that one should *davka* extinguish the lights, because seeing one's spouse could arouse desire and lead to violation.

Rav Yehoshua reports that Rava expounded the *pasuk* *netzer mata'ai ma'aseh yadai le-hitpa'er*, applying to this case the verse *ve-amech kulam tzaddikim* — all of your people are righteous. Whether one lights or one does not, both sets of communities are pursuing the same goal by two different theories of human psychology and *yetzer ha-ra*.

## Fire Blessing Debate

### Blessing Only Motzaei Sh…

From the discussion of candles on *Yom Kippur*, the *Gemara* transitions to an associated matter: the blessing over fire. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *ein mevarachin al ha-or ela be-motzaei Shabbat*, one recites the blessing *borei me'orei ha-esh* only on *motzaei Shabbat*, *ho'il u-techilat beriyato hu* — because that is when fire was originally created.

One might assume the reason we light the *havdalah* candle on *motzaei Shabbat* is simply to mark the transition from a period when one could not use fire — essentially as a celebration of restored access to flame. The *Gemara*, however, is now pointing toward a different, or at least primary, rationale.

### Fire and Creativity

Note first that *borei me'orei ha-esh* contains within it an ambiguity: is it about *or* — light — or about *esh* — fire itself? In either case, the blessing is understood here not as a reaction to the *Shabbat* prohibition on fire, but as a commemoration of the original creation of fire.

To appreciate the weight of this claim, consider the ancient significance of fire-making. The image of Prometheus stealing fire from the gods captures something universal about the cultural depth of this act. For *Chazal*, fire is the necessary foundation of civilization. The moment God completes Creation and *Shabbat* arrives, the world is, in a sense, handed over to human beings: *u-milu et ha-aretz ve-khivsuha*, conquer the earth and subdue it — build your society, take creative control. And the first profound creative act, the foundational one, is the making of fire. So for *Chazal*, that moment of human creativity occurred precisely on *motzaei Shabbat*, as the first moment after *Shabbat* ended. The entire week begins by recalling that first human creative act.

This is why, according to this statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, one does *not* recite *borei me'orei ha-esh* on *motzaei Yom Kippur*. The blessing is not about what was prohibited on *Yom Kippur* any more than we go out and plow a field on *motzaei Shabbat* simply because plowing was forbidden. The blessing is about the symbolism of the creation of fire, and that symbolism is specific to *motzaei Shabbat*.

A question naturally arises about whether an electric light could fulfill the requirement. Electricity is, in a meaningful sense, the most astonishing instance of humanity harnessing fire — clean, efficient, universally accessible. As long as electricity is categorized as *esh*, it would be sufficient. There is a historical note worth mentioning: when the question of electricity first arose, some rabbis who wished to demonstrate that electric lights constituted *esh* and therefore could not be used on *Shabbat* would *davka* recite *havdalah* over electric lights. Other considerations might make this *lechatchila* suboptimal — such as the traditional preference for a mingling of flames — but *bidi'avad* a single flame suffices, and the classification as *esh* is what matters.

### Ulla's Inquiry

Now a narrative enters the *Gemara*. A certain elder — in one version identified as Rabbah bar Bar Chana — heard Rav Yehuda's teaching and responded *yasher koach*, affirming it. And *ve-chen amar Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan, it is reported, said the same thing.

The transmission of this teaching is then complicated by a scene: Ulla was riding on a donkey, with Rabbi Abba on his right and Rabbah bar Bar Chana on his left — an arrangement that already hints at the relative standing of the participants. Rabbi Abba addressed Ulla: is it truly the case that you said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that one does not recite the blessing on *or* except on *motzaei Shabbat* because that is when fire was originally created — implying that one does *not* recite it on *motzaei Yom Kippur*?

Ulla turned and gave Rabbah bar Bar Chana a *bisha* — a dirty look. He assumed it was Rabbah bar Bar Chana who had attributed this position to Rabbi Yochanan (via Ulla), since that is precisely what one version of the story above recorded. Rabbah bar Bar Chana quickly defended himself: *ana lav ha amri* — I never said that. I never claimed Rabbi Yochanan said one does not recite the blessing on *motzaei Yom Kippur*. What I said was something different entirely.

### Yom Kippur Shabbat Case

What Rabbah bar Bar Chana actually reported was the following. A *tanna* taught before Rabbi Yochanan: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says that when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, even in a place that has the custom not to light candles on *erev Yom Kippur*, one nevertheless lights — *mipnei kvod ha-Shabbat*. Even without the eating component, the dimension of *kvod Shabbat* — distinct from *oneg Shabbat* though often intertwined with it — demands that candles be lit. And Rabbi Yochanan responded to that teaching by saying: *va-chakhamim osrin* — the rabbis disagree. Even when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, communities that maintain the custom not to light on *erev Yom Kippur* do not light.

So the statement attributed to Rabbi Yochanan relating to *Yom Kippur* and lighting was not about *motzaei Yom Kippur* at all. It was about the case of *Yom Kippur* that falls on *Shabbat*, and whether communities that don't light on *erev Yom Kippur* would override their *minhag* for the sake of *kvod Shabbat*. Rabbi Yochanan rules they do not. That is the teaching Rabbah bar Bar Chana had reported, and it was entirely consistent with Rabbi Yochanan separately holding that one *does* recite *borei me'orei ha-esh* on *motzaei Yom Kippur*.

Ulla, upon hearing the clarification, responded with the expression *adatahei* — meaning, in effect: that is indeed the right thing to say; that is a proper report of Rabbi Yochanan's position. Rabbi Yosef then applied a verse to this exchange: *mayim amukim etzah be-lev ish, ve-ish tevunah yidlenah* — deep waters is the counsel within a person's heart, and a person of understanding will draw them out. *Ze Ulla* — Ulla represents the deep waters, as it was his unstated assumption, communicated only through a sharp glance, that something had been misattributed to him. *Ve-ish tevunah yidlenah — ze Rabbah bar Bar Chana*, who was perceptive enough to understand why Ulla was angry, to figure out exactly what was being alleged, and to defend himself by clarifying what he had actually said.

Meanwhile, the broader question remains: did Rabbi Yochanan say one recites *borei me'orei ha-esh* on *motzaei Yom Kippur* or not? The tradition reported by Rabbi Binyamin bar Yephet in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: *mevarachin al ha-ner bein be-motzaei Shabbat bein be-motzaei Yom Kippurim* — one blesses over fire both on *motzaei Shabbat* and on *motzaei Yom Kippur*. And *ve-chen ama devar* — that is indeed the widespread practice of the people.

## Fire Contradiction

### Conflicting Sources

The *Gemara* now raises a direct contradiction. Against the tradition that one recites *borei me'orei ha-esh* on *motzaei Yom Kippur*, there stands an opposing teaching: *ein mevarachin al ha-ner ella be-motzaei Shabbat ve-hi techillat beriyato hu* — one does not bless over fire except on *motzaei Shabbat*, for that is when it was originally created. This is word for word identical to the formulation of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. Moreover, a statement in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that we rule like Rav Yehuda — that all the blessings should be recited together *al ha-kos* — appears in the context of this very *baraita*, suggesting Rabbi Yochanan may well be endorsing the position that the blessing belongs only to *motzaei Shabbat*.

So we appear to have conflicting reports. One tradition in the name of Rabbi Yochanan says the blessing is recited on *motzaei Yom Kippur* as well; another body of teaching implies it is not.

### New vs. Old Fire

*Lo kashya* — the *Gemara* resolves the contradiction with a crucial distinction: *kan be-or she-shavat, kan be-or ha-yotze min ha-etzim u-min ha-avanim* — here we speak of fire that has "rested" (i.e., was burning through *Shabbat*), and there we speak of fire newly produced from wood and stones.

The practical application of this distinction is well known, even if its underlying logic often eludes people. On *motzaei Shabbat* one recites the blessing specifically over newly created fire — fire freshly struck from flint or kindled from scratch — because the entire purpose of the blessing is to commemorate the original creation of fire by human hands. On *motzaei Yom Kippur*, by contrast, the tradition is to recite the blessing only over a flame that was burning *through* Yom Kippur — a *yahrtzeit* candle or similar.

This is genuinely counterintuitive and confuses nearly everyone who encounters it. If the idea on *motzaei Yom Kippur* were to signify that we now can make fire after a day when we could not, one would expect the symbol to be a *newly* created fire. The answer is that this is precisely the point: we do *not* want to create that association on *motzaei Yom Kippur*. The symbolism of newly created fire belongs exclusively to *motzaei Shabbat* — to that first human creative act. To avoid any confusion between the two occasions, the rule on *motzaei Yom Kippur* insists on old fire. The term *nur she-shavat* — fire that "rested" — is somewhat misleading, since the candle was burning the entire day; the operative point is not anything positive about an old flame but rather the deliberate exclusion of a newly struck flame, so as not to blur the symbolism of *motzaei Shabbat*.

This same principle resolves a second apparent contradiction between two *braitot*: one teaching that one *does* recite the blessing over fire newly produced from wood and stone, and another teaching that one does *not*. *Lo kashya* — *kan be-motzaei Shabbat, kan be-motzaei Yom Kippur* — on *motzaei Shabbat* one specifically uses new fire; on *motzaei Yom Kippur* one specifically uses old fire.

### Blessing Order

A brief additional note on the ordering of *havdalah* blessings: the *Gemara* mentions that Rabbi — Rabbeinu HaKadosh — *mefarzeran*, he scattered them, reciting the blessings separately rather than as a unit. For instance, upon seeing fire on the way home from *shul*, he would immediately recite *borei me'orei ha-esh* even before reaching the cup. Rabbi Chiyya, by contrast, *mechanseran* — bundled them all together to be recited *al ha-kos*. Rabbi Yosef bar Abdimi clarifies: even though Rabbi scattered them in practice, he would nonetheless *return* and arrange them all *al ha-kos* as well — *chozer ve-sodran al ha-kos*. The practical ruling is that everything is recited *al ha-kos*, though it is important to know that conceptually the components are not inseparably bound to one another, and if circumstances require, *borei me'orei ha-esh* and *besamim* may be recited independently of *havdalah*.

## Fire Creation

### Ten Things Created

*Ve-ha-or be-motzaei Shabbat nivra* — the *Gemara* has just stated that fire was created on *motzaei Shabbat*. But is that so? A *baraita* challenges this: *asarah devarim nivru be-erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot* — ten things were created on *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*. This list is also found in *Pirkei Avot*, with slight variation. The items are: the well of Miriam, the *man*, the rainbow, *ktav u-michtav* — script and writing (Rashi understands *ktav* as the alphabet and *michtav* as the implements of writing, though the distinction is not entirely clear), the *luchot*, the burial place of Moses, the cave in which Moses and Elijah each stood when the divine Presence was revealed to them, the opening of the donkey's mouth in the Bilaam episode, and the opening of the earth to swallow the wicked in the episode of Korach.

If fire is among the things created on *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*, that contradicts the claim that it was created on *motzaei Shabbat*.

### Liminal Space

This list is a fascinating and somewhat heterogeneous collection. Most items are miraculous events or objects, yet a few — such as the written alphabet — seem to reflect profound human innovation. What principle unites them?

The key is the concept of *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot* itself. The six days of *Bereishit* established the natural order of things. *Shabbat* then arrived as a kind of seal upon Creation — a closing of the natural order, after which everything is fixed for eternity. But this raises a challenge: where in this schema is there room for human creativity? Where is there room for things that partially violate the natural order — whether genuine miracles like the opening of the earth, or quasi-miraculous things like the invention of writing, or the human mastery of fire?

If all such things were assigned to *motzaei Shabbat*, they would seem entirely external to the natural order, something grafted onto it from outside. If they were simply part of the six days, they would lose their distinctive character. *Chazal*'s solution is the liminal space of *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*: that gray zone between the closing of the natural order and the arrival of *Shabbat* which seals it. In this twilight, the seeds of human creativity and of certain departures from the natural order were planted. They belong neither fully to the natural order of the six days nor fully to the sealed world of *Shabbat* and after. This framework allowed the world to function according to consistent laws of nature and yet also to contain creativity and events that appear to transcend those laws.

This also explains why the list does not include *Keri'at Yam Suf* or the great wilderness miracles. The *Rambam* raises this question in his *Peirush ha-Mishnayot*, and a compelling answer is that those events represent a *total* violation of the natural order — miracles that come from entirely outside the way God set the world in motion. The items on this list, by contrast, are embedded within the natural order even as they stretch it. Some, like the *man*, are closer to miraculous; others, like writing, are genuinely human achievements that nonetheless carry a dimension of the extraordinary. It is not the same degree of rupture as the splitting of the sea.

### Creativity vs. Miracle

Now another tradition is cited alongside the established list. *Rabbi Nechemia omer mishum aviv* — Rabbi Nechemia says in the name of his father: even *ha-or ve-ha-pered* — fire and the mule — were created at that liminal moment. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer mishum aviv* — Rabbi Yehoshua says in the name of his father: even *ha-ayil ve-ha-shamir* — the ram that Abraham found at the *Akeidah* and the *shamir* worm used to cut stone for the *Beit ha-Mikdash* without metal tools.

Rabbi Yehuda adds: even the *tzevet* — the tongs. He was accustomed to say: *tzavta be-tzavta mit'abda* — tongs are made using tongs. You need to hold metal in a fire with tongs in order to shape it. So who made the first tongs? *Birya bidei shamayim hi* — it must be a heavenly creation, said Rabbi Yehuda. The *hakhamim* responded: perhaps the first tongs were cast in a mold and set correctly — *ya'asena be-dfus ve-yichavenena kiven* — poured as molten metal, requiring no prior tongs. In that case, *birya bidei adam hi* — it is a human creation entirely. (The formula *ya'asena be-dfus* is in fact the same language used earlier in *Pesachim* by Beisos ben Zunin in his proposal to make matzah in a mold — likely borrowed from precisely this passage.)

The philosophical issue of the tongs is the classic problem of lifting oneself by one's bootstraps: how does human civilization develop the tools necessary to transcend its current level of development? At some point there must have been a first, miraculous leap — a first tool made without the preceding tool — and that is what this discussion tries to capture.

### Gehennom Fire

The *Gemara* now reconciles the contradiction about when fire was created. *Ha lo kashya: ha be-nur didan, ha be-nur de-Gehennom* — there is no difficulty. The fire that concerns us — the fire of human civilization — was made on *motzaei Shabbat*. The fire of *Gehennom* was made on *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*. So the *baraita*'s listing of fire among the *erev Shabbat* creations refers to the fire of *Gehennom*, not to the fire of human civilization.

But the *Gemara* immediately asks: was the fire of *Gehennom* really created on *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*? A different *baraita* teaches that *shiva devarim nivru kodem she-nivra ha-olam* — seven things were created *before* the world was created — and among them is *Gehennom*. This is a completely different category from *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*. These seven things are not liminal creations squeezed between the natural order and *Shabbat*; they are the foundational metaphysical prerequisites for a world of religious meaning to exist at all.

What are the seven? *Torah* — without God's message and direction there is no meaningful world. *Teshuva* — the possibility of repentance, the capacity for human beings to go astray and return, to change who they are and repair their relationship with the divine. This is utterly foundational to any conception of a meaningful human life. *Gan Eden ve-Gehennom* — the reality that actions have ultimate consequences, that how we live results in reward or punishment; in another reading, the concept of a possible utopia and a possible dystopia, the poles within which human history operates. *Kisei ha-kavod* — God's heavenly throne — and *Beit ha-Mikdash*, its earthly counterpart, representing together the principle that God is not a remote first cause but a king with a throne, whose presence can be experienced in this world: *ha-shamayim kisi ve-ha-aretz hadom raglai*. And *shemo shel Mashiach* — the name, or concept, of the Messiah.

The phrase *shemo shel Mashiach* is deliberately chosen over simply *Mashiach*. The Messiah himself was not created before the world; what was established was the concept. The idea of a Messiah removes Jewish civilization from a consciousness of myth. Greek thought understood history as cyclical, endlessly repeating. Jewish thought contains a cyclical element — the reliving of *yetziat Mitzrayim* each *Pesach*, the application of old frameworks to new historical configurations, the sense that Rome is *Edom* is Christianity — but it is also fundamentally linear and directional. History is moving toward a redeemed reality. The best description of the Jewish concept of history may in fact be a corkscrew: cycling, but also vectored toward ultimate redemption. *Shemo shel Mashiach* anchors this, rooting communal life not only in the reliving of ancient archetypes but in orientation toward a future that is genuinely different from the present.

The *Gemara* then provides *pesukim* for each of these seven pre-Creation concepts. *Torah*: *Hashem kanani reishit darko* — "God acquired me at the very beginning of His way" (Proverbs 8:22). *Teshuva*: *beterem harim yuladu* — before the mountains were born — followed by *tashev enosh ad daka va-tomar shuvu benei adam* — "return, O people" (Psalms 90:2–3). *Gan Eden*: *va-yita Hashem Elokim gan be-Eden mi-kedem* — "God planted a garden in Eden from before" (Genesis 2:8). *Gehennom*: *ki aruch me-etmol tofeth* — "for *tofeth* was arranged from yesterday" (Isaiah 30:33). *Kisei ha-kavod* u-*Beit ha-Mikdash*: *kisei kavod marom me-rishon mekom mikdasheinu* — "a throne of glory, exalted from the beginning, the place of our sanctuary" (Jeremiah 17:12). *Shemo shel Mashiach*: *yehi shemo le-olam lifnei shemesh yinon shmo* — "may his name endure forever; before the sun, *yinon* is his name" (Psalms 72:17).

So the *Gemara* resolves: *chalalo hu she-nivra kodem she-nivra ha-olam* — the *space* of *Gehennom* was established before the world was created. *Ve-or didei be-erev Shabbat ivrei* — and the *fire* of *Gehennom* was created on *erev Shabbat bein ha-shmashot*.

But now a further complication: is it really true that the fire of *Gehennom* was created on *erev Shabbat*? Another *baraita* is brought: Rabbi Yosei says that the fire which the Holy One created on the *second day* of Creation — *yom sheini* — is a fire that will never be extinguished, as the verse says: *ki tolatam lo tamut ve-esham lo tichbeh* — "their worm shall not die and their fire shall not be quenched" (Isaiah 66:24). And Rav Bana'ah son of Rav Ulla adds: this explains why the phrase *ki tov* does not appear in the account of the second day of Creation — because that is the day when the fire of *Gehennom* was created.

Rabbi Eliezer adds a coda: even though *yom sheini* was not graced with *ki tov*, it was retroactively included on the sixth day when the Torah says *va-yar Elokim et kol asher asah ve-hineh tov me'od* — "and God saw all that He had made and behold it was very good." Rashi notes that the existence of the fire of *Gehennom* is ultimately good, as it gives human beings reason to remain on the right path and ensures that justice is realized in the world.

So the complete resolution is: *chalalo* — the hollow, the conceptual space of *Gehennom* — was before the world was created. Its fire was created on *yom sheini*. And what then of the *baraita* that listed fire among the *erev Shabbat* creations? That must refer to a different dimension of the fire of *Gehennom*, or the *Gemara* adjusts its reading accordingly. Our fire — human fire — requires its own accounting, which the *Gemara* now provides.

### Our Fire Created

*Ve-or didan* — and as for human fire — *be-machshava alah le-hibarot be-erev Shabbat, ve-lo nivra ad motzaei Shabbat* — it arose in God's mind to be created on *erev Shabbat*, but was not actually created until *motzaei Shabbat*. This resolution is not merely technical. It is profound.

The concept of *machshava techila* — divine thought preceding actualization — appears elsewhere in the tradition, most famously in *Lecha Dodi*: *sof ma'aseh be-machshava techila*, the last thing made was first in thought. Here the distinction between *machshava* and *beri'ah* is doing something beautiful and specific: the divine component, the spark of inspiration that would be implanted in the human being, was already present in the *erev Shabbat* moment — embedded within the natural order, in that liminal space. But the actual creative act, the human execution, took place only when Adam was given the world to work with, beginning on *motzaei Shabbat*.

A *baraita* makes this explicit: *Rabbi Yosei omer — shnei devarim alu be-machshava le-hibarot be-erev Shabbat ve-lo nivre'u ad motzaei Shabbat*. Two things were in God's plan on *erev Shabbat* but were only created on *motzaei Shabbat*. And on *motzaei Shabbat* what happened? *Natan ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu de'ah be-Adam ha-Rishon me'ein dugma shel ma'alah* — God gave Adam a spark of wisdom, an echo of something truly heavenly. And with that divine spark, Adam brought two stones, ground them against each other, and fire came forth — *va-yatza mehem or*. And the second thing: he brought two animals — a horse and a donkey — crossbred them — *ve-hirkhiv zu be-zu* — and from them came the mule — *va-yatza mehem pered*.

What this captures is the unity of the divine and human dimensions of creativity. The divine component was present in the *erev Shabbat* liminal space. But the human creative act — fire, the mule — required human beings to actually be given the world and to work in it, beginning from *motzaei Shabbat*. This is the meaning of the verse *va-yashev Hashem Elohim et ha-adam ba-gan le-ovdah u-le-shomrah* — and it is why the image of fire as the first human creative act has always carried civilizational weight. It is, in every sense, a spark from the divine.

## Adam's Creations

### Fire and Mule

The *baraita* just cited establishes that both fire and the mule were among the things God planned on *erev Shabbat* and that Adam actually created on *motzaei Shabbat* through the divine spark of wisdom placed in him. As noted, crossbreeding a horse and a donkey to produce a mule is prohibited as *kilayim*, and yet the mule was an essential beast of burden without which the foundations of ancient civilization would have been impossible. The creative act is censured and yet also recognized as having been divinely inspired — a nuanced and characteristically *talmudic* way of holding two truths in tension.

### Ana's Genealogy

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a different tradition: *pered bimei Ana hu she-haya* — the mule was first produced in the days of Ana. The verse states: *hu Ana asher matza et ha-yeimim ba-midbar* — "he is Ana who found the *yeimim* in the wilderness" (Genesis 36:24). The term *yeimim* here is read not as its more literal meaning — perhaps watercourses or springs — but as mules: Ana discovered, or more precisely invented, them. *Ve-dorshei chamurot hayu omrim* — those who interpret difficult passages would say — *Ana pasul haya*, Ana himself was born of an illicit union. *Lefichach hevi pasul la-olam* — therefore he brought a *pasul* creature into the world. There is an element of moral censure here: the one who engaged in transgressive breeding was himself a product of illicit breeding.

Where do we know Ana was *pasul*? The verse in *Bereishit lamed-vav* lists *va-elu venei Se'ir ha-Chori: Tzi'veon ve-Ana* — listing Tzi'veon and Ana as brothers. But another verse says *ve-elu venei Tzi'veon: Aya ve-Ana* — listing Ana as the *son* of Tzi'veon. Is Ana Tzi'veon's brother or his son?

The answer must be: both. *Alma ba Tzi'veon al imo ve-holid mimenah Ana* — Tzi'veon had relations with his own mother and fathered Ana. Thus Ana was simultaneously Tzi'veon's son and, through the shared mother, his brother. This genealogical scandal explains both the *pasul* designation and the *midrashic* appropriateness of Ana being the inventor of hybrid breeding.

### Ana Question

The *Gemara* raises an objection: *dilma trei Ana havu* — perhaps there were simply two different people named Ana, one Tzi'veon's brother and one Tzi'veon's son? This is not inherently impossible.

Rava responds: *amina milta de-Shavur Malka lo amrah* — I will say something that even the Persian king did not say. (*Shavur Malka* is the Persian king, and calling Shmuel by that title was a way of honoring his authority in monetary law and jurisprudence. *U-mano Shmuel* — and who is meant? Shmuel.) The solution: *amar kra hu Ana asher matza et ha-yeimim* — the verse says *hu Ana* — *he* is Ana. The repetition *hu Ana de-me'ikara* — he is the same Ana mentioned earlier — establishes that the Ana who found the mules is identical with the Ana who was Tzi'veon's son and also his brother. There were not two people named Ana; there was one, and the biblical text signals this through the emphatic *hu*.

Rav Papa uses the same formulation — *amina milta de-Shavur Malka lo amrah* — attributing the clever reading to Rava (or in some versions, to Rabbah).

## Hidden Things

### Seven Hidden Things

*Tanu rabbanan — shiva devarim mechusin mi-bnei adam* — the Rabbis taught: seven things are hidden from human beings, and these are they.

*Yom ha-mita* — the day of one's death. The *Maharsha* and *Rashi* both observe that this concealment serves a purpose: if a person knew when they would die, they would not live their days in a meaningful way. Knowing the precise endpoint of life might either lead to reckless living earlier (since "there is still time") or to paralysis and despair. The uncertainty keeps each day potentially significant.

*Ve-yom ha-nechama* — the day of consolation. Rashi understands this as: when one is in the midst of suffering and anguish, one does not know when relief will come. The *Maharsha* offers a somewhat different reading that is not fully recalled here.

*Ve-omek ha-din* — the depth of judgment. On one reading this means the profound difficulty of rendering proper legal judgment. Rashi also suggests it means one is never fully aware of the degree to which one will be held accountable for one's actions — which again serves a moral function: if the full weight of divine accounting were known, it might either terrify into paralysis or, conversely, if known precisely, enable a kind of cynical calculation.

*Ve-ein adam yodea ma be-libo shel chavero* — one does not know what is in the heart of one's friend. A person may appear friendly and well-disposed, while their inner thoughts are something altogether different.

*Ve-ein adam yodea ba-meh mishtaker* — one does not know how one will ultimately earn a living.

*U-malkhut beit David matai tachazoer* — one does not know when the Davidic kingdom will be restored, i.e., when a more perfect world will come into being. *U-malkhut chayevet matai tichleh* — and when the wicked empire will finally cease.

What binds these together into a single list is not entirely transparent, but the first cluster relates to judgment, mortality, and accountability in personal life, while the final pair relates to the trajectory of history and redemption. All are things one might desperately wish to know, and all remain hidden.

### Three Necessary Evils

A *tanna* teaches: *gimel devarim alu be-machshava le-hibarot* — three things came up in God's plan to create — *im lo alu din hu she-ya'alu* — and even if they had not come up in the plan, the logic of things would have demanded them. These are things that seem at first glance painful or undesirable, yet serve an essential function.

*Al ha-met she-yasriach* — that a dead body would decompose. *Al ha-met she-yishtakach min ha-lev* — that the memory of the dead would fade from the heart. *Ve-al ha-tevua she-tarkiv* — and that grain would rot.

### Life's Necessities

Each of these "necessary evils" enables human life to continue. If a dead body did not decompose, Rashi observes, people would not bury their dead; they would remain in the constant presence of the corpse, unable to move forward. The second follows directly: even apart from physical decomposition, the natural fading of grief from the forefront of consciousness — without ever fully forgetting the person — is what allows the bereaved to re-engage with life. One does not decompose emotionally alongside the deceased.

The third — that grain rots — serves an economic function. Rashi explains that if grain could be hoarded indefinitely without loss, those who controlled the supply could manipulate markets without limit, holding grain off the market forever. The perishability of grain creates pressure to sell, which keeps prices accessible and prevents indefinite monopolistic hoarding. Currency is mentioned in some versions as a related item: the *Gemara* notes that the prevalence of money serves those who do not make their living from agriculture, and currency is a far from trivial invention — its availability profoundly affects who can survive in a complex economy.

What is striking about the framing *din hu she-ya'alu* — the logic of things demands them — is the acknowledgment that these are things about which one has a *ta'anah*, a genuine complaint. Decomposition is horrifying. Forgetting loved ones feels like a betrayal. Grain rotting is a loss. The formulation validates the protest even while insisting that these realities are not accidents or cruelties but necessities embedded in the structure of a livable world. One need not pretend they are pleasant. One only need recognize that without them, life could not go on.
