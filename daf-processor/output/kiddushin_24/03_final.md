# Kiddushin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gito v'Yado Principle

### Mishna Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, עֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גִּיטּוֹ מִיָּד רַבּוֹ? כֵּיוָן דְּגָמַר ״לָהּ״ ״לָהּ״ מֵאִשָּׁה, כְּאִשָּׁה, אוֹ דִילְמָא אִשָּׁה דְּאִיהִי מָצֵי מְקַבֶּלֶת גִּיטָּהּ – שָׁלִיחַ נָמֵי מָצֵי מְשַׁוְּיָא. עֶבֶד דְּאִיהוּ לָא מְקַבֵּל גִּיטֵּיהּ – שָׁלִיחַ נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי מְשַׁוֵּי. בָּתַר דְּבַעְיָא הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: ״לָהּ״ ״לָהּ״ מֵאִשָּׁה, כְּאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** in the case of **a Canaanite slave, what is** the *halakha* with regard to the possibility **that** a slave **can appoint an agent to accept his bill** of manumission **from the hand of his master?** Does one say that **since** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar **derives** the verbal analogy of **"*lah*"** and **"*lah*" from a woman,** which teaches that a slave can be freed with a document, therefore, a slave is also **like a woman** in that he too can appoint an agent? **Or perhaps,** in the case of **a woman, as she can accept her bill of divorce** herself, **she can also appoint an agent,** whereas **a slave** is different, **as he cannot accept his bill** of manumission himself, and consequently **he cannot appoint an agent either. After raising the dilemma,** Rabba **subsequently resolved it:** Since Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar derives the verbal analogy of **"*lah*"** and **"*lah*" from a woman,** a slave is also treated **like a woman** with regard to his ability to appoint an agent.

The present sugya picks up in the middle of the discussion on 23a concerning *eved kenaani* — how a Canaanite slave is acquired and, more precisely, how a slave acquires his own freedom. The *mishnah* records a *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* on this question. Rabbi Meir holds that a slave may acquire his freedom *bekesef al yedei acherim* — through money paid by others on his behalf — or *uvishtar al yedei atzmo* — by personally receiving a *shtar shichrur*, a document of freedom, from his master. The *chachamim* appear to disagree, holding *bekesef al yedei atzmo* — that the slave himself may hand the money over — but *uvishtar al yedei acherim* — that the *shtar shichrur* must be received through another person.

The *shakla ve-tarya* that preceded this point on 23a suggested that there may in fact be a third opinion embedded in the *mishnah* — that of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. On this reading, the *chachamim* actually permit the slave to acquire his freedom through either money or a *shtar* on his own, while Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is the one who specifically requires that the *shtar shichrur* be received *al yedei acherim*, through someone other than the slave himself.

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָנֵי כָּהֲנֵי שְׁלוּחֵי דְרַחֲמָנָא נִינְהוּ. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׁלוּחֵי דִידַן נִינְהוּ, מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דַּאֲנַן לָא מָצֵינַן עָבְדִינַן וְאִינְהוּ מָצֵי עָבְדִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But** consider **that which Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says** with regard to the service in the Temple: **These priests are the agents of the Merciful One,** i.e., they perform the Temple service as the emissaries of God. **As if it enters your mind** that **they are our agents, is there anything that we cannot do but** agents **can do** on our behalf? Since it is prohibited for non-priests to serve in the Temple, priests cannot be considered the agents of the Jewish people.

It is against this backdrop that the *gemara* opens with the last two words of 23a: *bayei Rava le-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — Rava poses a question directed at the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. The question is: *eved kenaani ma hu sheyaseh shaliach lekabel gito miyad rabo* — can a Canaanite slave appoint a *shaliach* to receive his *get* of freedom from his master?

The framing of the question turns on a *gezeirah shavah*. The law of slave manumission is derived from the law of divorce through a verbal analogy on the word *la* — appearing in both contexts — linking the freeing of a slave to the *get* given to a woman. Since a woman may appoint a *shaliach* to receive her *get* on her behalf, one might think a slave should likewise be able to do so. But Rava presses further: *o dilma ishah de'ihi matzi mekabel giteh shaliach nami matzi mashvei* — perhaps a woman can appoint a *shaliach* precisely because she herself has the capacity to receive her own *get*. *Eved de'ihu lo mekabel giteh* — but a slave, according to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who does not have the capacity to receive his own *get shichrur* directly — *shaliach nami lo matzi mashvei* — would likewise be unable to appoint a *shaliach* to receive it for him.

The underlying assumption seems to be that this conclusion would be absurd, that surely a slave should be able to appoint an agent. After posing the question, however, Rava resolves it himself: *la-la me-ishah* — the *gezeirah shavah* connecting the slave's freedom to a woman's divorce does in fact extend to this, and therefore the slave is compared to a woman in the relevant respects.

It is worth pausing to appreciate how a principle worked out in Masechet Gittin resurfaces here. The rule of *gito v'yado ba'im k'echad* — that the slave's *get* and his acquiring hand come simultaneously — is central to understanding why Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that the slave cannot receive his own *shtar shichrur* directly. This is precisely why Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's opinion is of particular interest, and why tracking opinions across different *masekhtot* is essential to a full understanding of any given *sugya*.

### Shaliach Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָא עַבְדָּא, דְּאִיהוּ לָא מָצֵי מְקַבֵּל גִּיטֵּיהּ וּשְׁלִיחַ מָצֵי מְשַׁוֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** This statement leads to the following question: **But** is it **not** true that an agent can be appointed to perform a task that cannot be done by the one who sent him? **But** according to Rava's conclusion there is the case of **a slave, who is unable to accept his bill** of manumission **himself, and** yet **he can appoint an agent** to receive it for him.

This brings into view a more general principle — that a *shaliach* cannot be empowered to perform an act that the person appointing him could not perform himself. This rule is derived from Masechet Nazir (daf 12) and applies broadly to the laws of *shlichut*.

### Kohanim Shlichut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא שָׁיְכִי בְּתוֹרַת קׇרְבָּנוֹת כְּלָל, עֶבֶד שָׁיֵיךְ בְּגִיטִּין. דְּתַנְיָא: נִרְאִין הַדְּבָרִים שֶׁהָעֶבֶד מְקַבֵּל גִּיטּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ מִיַּד רַבּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא מִיַּד רַבּוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **And** that **is not so.** The difference between the cases is that **an Israelite is not involved in the *halakhot* of offerings at all,** as a non-priest is never permitted to sacrifice offerings. By contrast, **a slave is** somewhat **involved in bills** of manumission, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **It seems that a slave can accept the bill** of manumission **of another** slave **from the hand of the master of** that **other** slave. **But** he **cannot** accept a bill of manumission **from the hand of his own master,** who wishes to free another of his slaves. In this case there is no acquisition, as the document has not left the master's property, as anything given to a slave is considered the property of his master. With regard to the issue at hand, since a slave can accept a bill of manumission, at least on behalf of another slave, the *halakhot* of these documents are relevant to him, and therefore he can appoint an agent to receive his bill of manumission.

The *gemara* connects this to a famous question raised by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, which appears in Masechet Nedarim: *hani kohanei shluchei de-rachmana ninhu* — are the *kohanim* agents of God, or are they agents of *Bnei Yisrael* when they perform the *avodah*? The question is framed precisely in terms of this principle of *shlichut*: *mi ika midi de'anan lo matzinan avdinan ve'ihu matzi avid* — is there any act a *shaliach* can perform that the person appointing him could not perform himself? Since a *Yisrael* cannot perform the *avodah*, and the *kohanim* do perform it, the *kohanim* cannot be agents of *Bnei Yisrael*. They must therefore be *shluchei de-rachmana* — agents of God. The practical *nafka mina* raised in Masechet Nedarim concerns whether a *kohen* may perform the *avodah* for someone who is *mudar hana'ah* from him — that is, if the *kohen* is acting as his agent, the prohibition would apply, whereas if the *kohen* is God's agent, it would not.

### Slave vs. Woman

The *gemara* draws a distinction between the *sugya* here and the *sugya* in Nedarim. A *Yisrael* has no connection to *korbanot* whatsoever — *Yisrael lo shaychei b'torat korbanot klal* — and therefore there is no basis for any principle of *shlichut* that could apply to him with respect to the *avodah*. By contrast, *eved shayach begittin* — a slave is fully connected to the notion of his bill of freedom. The slave is not categorically excluded from the legal framework in the same way a *Yisrael* is excluded from performing the *avodah*.

### Friend's Get Shichrur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הַכֶּסֶף מִשֶּׁל אֲחֵרִים. נֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: אֵין קִנְיָן לְעֶבֶד בְּלֹא רַבּוֹ, וְאֵין קִנְיָן לְאִשָּׁה בְּלֹא בַּעְלָהּ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ קִנְיָן לְעֶבֶד בְּלֹא רַבּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ קִנְיָן לְאִשָּׁה בְּלֹא בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Meir the slave can be freed by means of money given by others, while the Rabbis hold that he can be freed by means of money given by himself, **provided that the money** he gives **belongs to others.** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that **they disagree with regard to this** principle, and **that Rabbi Meir maintains: There is no acquisition for a slave without his master,** i.e., a slave has no personal property and therefore his master immediately owns whatever the slave acquires, which means a slave cannot receive his redemption money himself. **And** similarly **there is no acquisition for a woman without her husband. And the Rabbis maintain: There is acquisition for a slave without his master, and there is acquisition for a woman without her husband.**

A *baraita* is cited to illustrate this distinction: *nirin hadevarim she'ha'eved mekabel gito shel chaveiro miyad rabo shel chaveiro* — it appears from the logic of the matter that a slave may receive his fellow slave's *get shichrur* from that fellow slave's master, acting as an agent for his friend. *Aval lo miyad rabo shelo* — but he cannot receive his own *shtar shichrur* from his own master. The reason is not that a slave is categorically incapable of receiving a *get shichrur*, but rather the specific problem of *kol ma shekana eved kana rabo* — whatever a slave acquires, his master acquires. Therefore, if the slave were to receive his own *get shichrur* into his own hand, it would be as if the master were giving it back to himself, and the slave would never actually receive it. This is the very logic of *gito v'yado ba'in k'echad* — the slave's *get* and his acquiring hand come as one — which Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar does not accept, and which is therefore the crux of his distinctive position.

## Conditional Gifts

### Kol Mah Shekana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין קִנְיָן לְעֶבֶד בְּלֹא רַבּוֹ, וְאֵין קִנְיָן לְאִשָּׁה בְּלֹא בַּעְלָהּ. וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאַקְנִי לֵיהּ אַחֵר מָנֶה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לְרַבָּךְ רְשׁוּת בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that **Rav Sheshet said: Everyone agrees** that **there is no acquisition for a slave without his master, and there is no acquisition for a woman without her husband, and** so, **with what are we dealing here?** This is a unique case in **which another** individual **transferred one hundred dinars to** a slave **and said to him:** I am giving this to you **on the condition that your master has no rights to it.**

The *gemara* now shifts from the *sugya* of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and returns to the original *machloket* in the *mishnah* between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*. The *chachamim* had said that while the slave may pay his own freedom through money, *uvlvad sheyehei hakesef mishel acheirim* — the money must nonetheless belong to others. This requires explanation: if the slave can hand the money over himself, why must the money belong to someone else?

The *gemara* raises the possibility that the *machloket* turns on the underlying question of *kol ma shekana eved kana rabo* — whatever a slave acquires, his master acquires. Does this mean a slave is entirely incapable of acquiring anything for himself, or might there be some qualified form of independent acquisition?

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כִּי אָמַר לֵיהּ ״קְנִי״ – קָנֵי עֶבֶד וְקָנֵי רַבֵּיהּ. וְכִי אָמַר לֵיהּ ״עַל מְנָת״ – לֹא כְּלוּם קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״עַל מְנָת״ – אַהֲנִי לֵיהּ תְּנָאֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the two opinions according to this explanation. **Rabbi Meir maintains** that **when** this third party **said to** the Canaanite slave: **Acquire** the money, the **slave acquires** it and **his master** immediately **acquires** it. **And when he said to him: On the condition** that your master has no rights to it, **he has said nothing to him,** i.e., his condition is ineffective. **And the Rabbis maintain** that **since he said to him: On the condition, his condition is effective** and the master does not acquire the money. Therefore, the slave owns this money, with which he can redeem himself.

The *gemara* initially proposes that Rabbi Meir holds *ein kinyan l'eved belo rabo* — a slave cannot acquire anything without it immediately transferring to the master. Similarly, *ein kinyan l'isha belo ba'ala* — whatever a woman acquires transfers to her husband. On this reading, Rabbi Meir maintains there is simply no independent avenue of acquisition available to the slave.

### Chachamim Position

The *chachamim*, on this initial reading, would hold *yesh kinyan l'eved belo rabo* — a slave can have independent acquisition, and similarly *yesh kinyan l'isha belo ba'ala* — a woman can acquire independently of her husband. This is why the *mishnah* needed to specify that the money must belong to others: since the slave can in principle have his own property, one might have thought he could use his own funds, so the *mishnah* tells us that even according to the *chachamim* the money cannot be the slave's own.

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: כֹּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּקָנֵי עֶבֶד וְקָנֵי רַבֵּיהּ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן דְּאַקְנִי לֵיהּ אַחֵר מָנֶה וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתֵּצֵא בּוֹ לְחֵירוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said:** In **a case like this, everyone agrees that** the **slave acquires** the money, **and his master** automatically **acquires** that which was acquired by the slave. **And with what are we dealing here,** in the dispute between the Sages and Rabbi Meir? This is referring to a case **where another** person **transferred one hundred dinars to him and said to him: On the condition that you are emancipated with it.**

Rabba bar Rav Sheshet in the name of Rav Sheshet, however, rejects this framing entirely. He maintains that in truth everyone — both Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* — agrees that *ein kinyan l'eved belo rabo*, a slave cannot acquire anything without it transferring to the master, and *ein kinyan l'isha belo ba'ala* as well. The *machloket* must therefore concern something else.

The case under discussion, Rav Sheshet argues, must involve a third party who goes to the slave and says: *al menat she'ein lerabcha reshut bo* — here is money on the condition that your master has no rights to it. The donor is attempting to circumvent *kol ma shekana eved kana rabo* by building in a condition that the master should not acquire it.

Rabbi Elazar then refines the case still further. It cannot simply be that someone gives the slave money hoping the slave will use it to buy his freedom through a subsequent transaction, because everyone would agree that the slave's momentary acquisition of the money would immediately vest in the master. Rather, the operative case according to Rabbi Elazar is one where someone hands money to the slave while simultaneously declaring *al menat sheteitzei bo l'cheirut* — on the condition that you go free through this. The third party is not giving the slave money to hold; he is attempting to purchase the slave's freedom by transferring funds directly to the slave, with the freedom as the condition.

### Ownership Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כִּי אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״קְנִי״ – קָנֵי עֶבֶד וְקָנֵי רַבֵּיהּ. וְכִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״עַל מְנָת״ – לֹא כְּלוּם קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי הָא לָא קָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ, דְּהָא לָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֶלָּא ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתֵּצֵא בּוֹ לְחֵירוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute according to this interpretation. **Rabbi Meir maintains** that **when he said to him: Acquire** the money, the **slave acquires** it, **and his master** immediately **acquires** the one hundred dinars from him. **And when he said to him: On the condition, he has said nothing to him. And the Rabbis maintain: He** does **not transfer** the money **to** the slave himself, **as he said to him only: On the condition that you are emancipated with it.** In other words, he gave it to the slave only for him to hand over the money to his master for his emancipation. His condition is valid and the slave is emancipated.

Given this framing, the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* becomes clear — and, striking, almost counterintuitive. Both agree that whatever the slave acquires goes to the master. The question is whether that very mechanism can serve as the vehicle for the slave's freedom.

Rabbi Meir holds: *ki amar lei kani kani eved v'kani rabo* — when the donor says "acquire it," the slave acquires and therefore the master acquires, *v'chi amar lei al menat lo klum ka'amar lei* — and the condition *al menat* is legally void. One cannot impose a condition that subverts the fundamental nature of the slave relationship. The condition therefore has no legal force, and the money simply passes to the master.

The *rabbanan* hold that *keivan d'amar lei al menat ahanei lei t'nai* — once the donor formulates the condition, the condition does have operative legal force, and through this mechanism the slave may go free. The *rabbanan* understand that the slave acquires the money as an extension of the master, and that this is precisely what makes the freedom possible — he acquires it for the master, and simultaneously goes free. As one might observe, it is almost the reverse of what one expects: the slave acquires his freedom *precisely because* he has no independent legal standing. His acquisition being the master's acquisition is what allows the third party's directed gift to function as a purchase of freedom.

It should be noted that the analogy to a married woman, while related, is not identical. A married woman has various layers of ownership — reserved assets, *shibudim*, *peirot* — that distinguish her situation from that of an *eved kenaani* in important respects. An *eved kenaani* has no independent legal standing in any domain analogous to a free person; the principle of *kol ma shekana eved kana rabo* is far more comprehensive than anything that applies to a married woman. Similarly, none of this applies to an *eved Ivri* — a Hebrew slave — who retains full personhood and legal standing. In Masechet Bava Metzia, in the discussion of *eilu metzi'os*, the *eved Ivri* is listed among those who retain ownership over found objects, which illustrates the fundamental halakhic distinction between the two categories of slavery.

## Ma'aser Sheni

### Woman & Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין אִשָּׁה פּוֹדָה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּלֹא חוֹמֶשׁ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִשָּׁה פּוֹדָה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּלֹא חוֹמֶשׁ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּזוּזֵי דְבַעַל, וּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּבַעַל – שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ דְּבַעַל קָא עָבְדָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **A woman may not redeem** her husband's **second tithe without** adding **one-fifth** of its value to the redemption money, as is required from all those who redeem their own second tithe. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Meir: A woman may redeem second tithe without** adding **one-fifth.** The Gemara inquires: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that she redeems second tithe **with** her **husband's money,** which he gave her for this purpose, **and the tithe** belongs **to** her **husband,** in this case **she acts** with **the agency of** her **husband.** Just as he is required to add one-fifth when he redeems his own second tithe, it is clear that she too must add one-fifth.

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that appears to contradict the positions of both Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* as they have been understood: *ein ishah podeh Ma'aser Sheni belo chomesh* — a woman may not redeem *Ma'aser Sheni* without paying the *chomesh*. By way of background: normally, *Ma'aser Sheni* produce is to be brought to Yerushalayim and eaten there. If the distance is too great or the quantity too large, the Torah permits redeeming the produce onto money — but when one redeems one's own *Ma'aser Sheni*, a *chomesh* — a surcharge of one-fifth of the final amount, which works out to 25% of the original value — must be added. The *chomesh* functions as a kind of penalty on using redemption rather than physically transporting the produce. The statement that a woman *must* pay the *chomesh* implies that she is acting as an agent of her husband when redeeming *his* produce, and therefore the exemption that might apply to her does not.

### Rabbi Meir Woman

Against this, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar transmits an opinion in the name of Rabbi Meir: *ishah podeh Ma'aser Sheni belo chomesh* — a woman may redeem *Ma'aser Sheni* without the *chomesh*, treating her as sufficiently independent of her husband in this context that she is not considered his agent.

### Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בְּזוּזֵי דִידַהּ וּמַעֲשֵׂר דִּידֵיהּ – ״אִישׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא אִשָּׁה!
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** one might say that this is referring **to** a case where she uses **her money and** it is **his tithe.** This is also difficult, as in this case the Rabbis would agree that she is exempt from paying the additional fifth, as **the Merciful One states:** "And if **a man** shall redeem any of his tithe" (Leviticus 27:31). The emphasis of the term "man" teaches: This applies to a man, **but** this does **not** apply to **a woman** who redeems her husband's tithe.

The *gemara* immediately interrogates what the actual case is. If we are dealing with the husband's money and the husband's *Ma'aser Sheni* produce, then she is plainly performing her husband's *shlichut*, and the first position — that she must pay the *chomesh* — clearly applies. If we are dealing with her own money and her own *Ma'aser Sheni*, then there is a *drasha* in the Torah — *ish amar Rachmana velo ishah*, the Torah says "a man" to exclude a woman — which might exempt her from the *chomesh* entirely. Rashi understands this exclusion to mean *ish mi-maasero amar Rachmana velo ishah mi-maaser ba'alah* — a man pays the *chomesh* when redeeming his own *Ma'aser Sheni*, but a woman is considered like an outsider with respect to her husband's *Ma'aser Sheni*, and therefore is not subject to the *chomesh*. Tosafot, by contrast, connects the exemption to the broader question of *talmud Torah* and the verse *ve-lamadeta* that appears in the context of *Ma'aser Sheni*, understanding the obligation of *chomesh* to be bound up with the *mitzvah* dimension of eating *Ma'aser Sheni* — a *mitzvah* from which women may be exempt. The question of how intrinsic the *chomesh* is to the *pidyon* of *Ma'aser Sheni* generally — whether it is simply part of the *mitzvah* or a separable surcharge — underlies this dispute between Rashi and Tosafot.

In any event, neither of those simple cases generates the interesting *machloket*. The *gemara* therefore looks for a case that would produce the apparent contradiction to the positions on *eved kenaani*. It arrives at the following: someone else comes to the woman and gives her money, saying *al menat sheteitzei bo la-cheirut* — that is, on the condition that she use it specifically to redeem the *Ma'aser Sheni*. This is the precise parallel to the case of the slave. And yet here, Rabbi Meir appears to hold that the condition *does* work — that the woman can use this designated money to redeem the *Ma'aser Sheni* without *chomesh* — while the *chachamim* appear to hold that it does not work and she must pay *chomesh*.

This is directly contradicted by what we saw in the context of the slave, where Rabbi Meir said conditions do not work and the *chachamim* said they do. Hence: *rami de-Rabbi Meir a-de-Rabbi Meir, rami de-Rabbanan a-de-Rabbanan* — there is an internal contradiction in Rabbi Meir's position and an internal contradiction in the *Rabbanan*'s position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא, דְּאַקְנִי לַהּ אַחֵר מָנֶה, וַאֲמַר לַהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּפְדִּי בּוֹ אֶת הַמַּעֲשֵׂר״, וְאִיפְּכָא שְׁמַעְנָא לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** correct to say that we are dealing with **a case like this** following one: **Where another** person **transferred one hundred dinars to her, and said to her:** I am giving this to you **on the condition that you redeem** your husband's **tithe with it? And** if so, **we heard them** say the **opposite** with regard to the issue of whether such a condition is effective. According to Rabbi Meir's reasoning presented in the case of a slave, anything a woman acquires should be acquired by her husband, which would mean that it is as though her husband himself redeemed it. How, then, could Rabbi Meir rule that a woman redeems second tithe without adding one-fifth? Additionally, the Rabbis said that the money received by the slave is not immediately acquired by his master, and yet here they consider the money received by the wife as belonging to her husband.

One proposed resolution is *ipcha shamina lehu* — we simply heard the two sources with their attributions reversed, and by flipping one of them the contradiction disappears. Abaye endorses this: *ipoch* — reverse them. But Rava objects: *l'olam lo sipoach* — one should not simply reverse *mishnayot* and *baraisos* to make them fit. There must be an organic distinction that resolves the apparent tension.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵיפוֹךְ. רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וְהָכָא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר דַּאֲתָא מִבֵּי נָשָׁא עָסְקִינַן, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂר מָמוֹן הֶקְדֵּשׁ הוּא, וְלָא קָנֵי לֵיהּ בַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Reverse** the opinions in the case of redemption of tithes. **Rava said: Actually do not reverse** them, **and here, with regard to tithes, we are dealing with tithe she received from her father's house** as an inheritance. **And Rabbi Meir** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says: Tithe is property** that belongs to the **Temple** treasury, **and** therefore **the husband does not acquire it,** unlike the regular property a woman brings to her marriage. Consequently, a woman can redeem it with her husband's money without adding one-fifth, as the husband is not the owner of the tithe.

Rava proposes that the two cases are not actually parallel. In the *Ma'aser Sheni* case, he specifies: *dhacha b'ma'aser d'asya mibei nasha askinan* — we are dealing with *Ma'aser Sheni* that the woman has inherited from her father's estate, where she has no brothers and is the sole heir. In this case, the *Ma'aser Sheni* produce belongs entirely to her, not to her husband. It is not, therefore, a case of someone giving her designated funds to redeem her husband's *Ma'aser Sheni*; rather, she is redeeming her own *Ma'aser Sheni* with her own inherited property.

### Ma'aser Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאָמְרִי: מָמוֹן הֶדְיוֹט הוּא, וְקָנֵי לֵיהּ בַּעַל, הִילְכָּךְ שְׁלִיחוּתָא דְבַעַל קָעָבְדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** conform **to their** standard line of **reasoning, as they say: Tithe is common,** i.e., non-sacred, **property.** It is not owned by the Temple treasury. **And** consequently, the **husband acquires it** like other property of his wife, to whose profits he is entitled. **Therefore, she performs** with **the agency of** her **husband** and must add the fifth.

But Rava goes further, pointing to what he regards as the more fundamental *machloket*: *Rabbi Meir l'taimei d'amar Ma'aser mamon hekdesh hu* — Rabbi Meir holds that *Ma'aser Sheni* belongs to *hekdesh*, that is, it is property of God. Consequently, *velo kanei lei ba'al* — the husband does not acquire the woman's *Ma'aser Sheni* when she inherits it, because it was never truly hers as ordinary property to begin with. Therefore, when she redeems it, she is acting on her own account, and Rabbi Meir's exemption from *chomesh* makes sense.

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *mamon hedyot hu* — *Ma'aser Sheni* is ordinary personal property, *v'kanei lei ba'al* — and therefore the husband acquires it when she inherits it. When she redeems it, she is acting as her husband's agent — *shlichusa d'ba'al ka'avda* — and the *chomesh* therefore applies to her.

This is a remarkable *machloket* with broad implications. *Ma'aser Sheni* occupies a unique place among the various categories of *hekdesh* and *kodshim*. Unlike *terumah*, where the produce belongs to the owner and must be transferred to the *kohen*, *Ma'aser Sheni* — on Rabbi Meir's view — belongs to God, and the owner must go to Yerushalayim to eat it there, in a sanctified space, because it is where it belongs. Yet *Ma'aser Sheni* is distinct from *korbanos* and other *kodshim* in that it may be eaten by *Yisraelim* and is subject to redemption and transfer in ways that most *hekdesh* is not — it is a more fluid form of *kedushah*, yet retains at its core an obligation that originates from God. It is similar in some ways to *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah*, while remaining distinct.

## Injury Mechanisms

### Tooth & Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּכוּלָּם, עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶם לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: With regard to any of** the twenty-four extremities, if a master strikes his slave and injures him there, **the slave is emancipated by means of these injuries, and** he **requires a bill of manumission** from his master. This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Meir says: He does not require** a bill of manumission. **Rabbi Eliezer says: He requires** one. **Rabbi Tarfon says: He does not require** one. **Rabbi Akiva says: He requires** one.

A *baraita* is cited: *yotzei beshein ve'ayin u-roshei evarim she-einan chozrin* — a slave goes free when his master knocks out his tooth or his eye, and also through injury to the tips of limbs that do not grow back. There are twenty-four such tips, which also carry significance in the laws of *nega'im*, where the tips of limbs cannot be examined for *tzara'at* from a single vantage point. The explicit biblical source covers only *shen* and *ayin* — tooth and eye. The question is therefore raised: from where do we derive that injury to the tips of other limbs also triggers the slave's freedom?

The *gemara* answers: *dumya de-shein ve'ayin* — by analogy to tooth and eye. *Ma shein ve'ayin mum she-bagalui ve-einan chozrin* — just as tooth and eye are visible blemishes that do not grow back, *af kol mum she-bagalui ve-einan chozrin* — so too any visible blemish that does not grow back.

### Klal U'Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי רִיבּוּיָא הוּא, אֲפִילּוּ הִכָּהוּ עַל יָדוֹ וְצָמְתָה וְסוֹפָהּ לַחְזוֹר נָמֵי, אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: הִכָּהוּ עַל יָדוֹ וְצָמְתָה וְסוֹפָהּ לַחְזוֹר – אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהּ לְחֵירוּת? אִם כֵּן, שֵׁן וָעַיִן מַאי אַהֲנִי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if it is an amplification,** which includes additional injuries, the slave should **also** be emancipated **even if he struck him on his hand and it withered,** i.e., became temporarily paralyzed, **but would eventually** be expected to **regenerate. Why,** then, **is it taught** in a *baraita*: If **he struck him on his hand and** the hand **withered but** it **will eventually regenerate, the slave is not emancipated by means of** this injury? The Gemara answers: **If so,** that even limbs that will eventually regain their function are included in this *halakha*, **what** purpose do the examples of **tooth and eye serve?** These two items that are explicitly stated in the verse would not exclude anything. Rather, only injuries similar to the details of an tooth and an eye are included, but not temporary injuries.

Before accepting this, the *gemara* considers a series of hermeneutical alternatives. One might argue that since both *shen* and *ayin* are explicitly mentioned in the Torah, they function as *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad* — two verses that arrive together with the same teaching — and the rule is *ein melamdin*, one cannot derive a generalization from two explicit cases that cover the same ground. The *gemara* responds that both cases are independently necessary. Had the Torah written only *shein*, one might have thought even a milk tooth — *shein she-yanak bah*, a tooth that grew in while the child was still nursing — would suffice. The inclusion of *ayin* tells us that the paradigm is something that is *nivra imo*, created with the person from birth. Conversely, had the Torah written only *ayin*, one might have read the paradigm as limited to limbs present from birth, excluding teeth that grow in later. *Tzricha* — both are necessary, and therefore the two cases do not function as redundant examples that prevent generalization.

Another hermeneutical path is proposed and rejected: perhaps the verse should be read as *klal u-prat* — general and specific — such that *ki yakkeh* is the general and *shein ve'ayin* is the specific, which would *limit* the application to tooth and eye only. The *gemara* notes, however, that the verse concludes with *le-chofshi yishalchenu* — "he shall send him free" — which adds a second general term. The structure therefore becomes *klal u-prat u-klal*, and by the rules of this hermeneutic, the concluding general term expands the application beyond the specific examples in the middle. The expansion is still constrained, however: *ei ata dan ela ke-ein ha-prat* — the generalization must conform to the nature of the specific examples.

### Specific Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכְרִיעִים לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן, שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה זִכְּתָה לוֹ, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּשְׁאָר אֵבָרִים, הוֹאִיל וּקְנָס חֲכָמִים הוּא. קְנָס הוּא?! קְרָאֵי קָא דָרְשִׁינַן! אֶלָּא: הוֹאִיל וּמִדְרַשׁ חֲכָמִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Those who** discuss matters and **decide before the Sages say** the following compromise: **Rabbi Tarfon's statement appears** correct **with regard to a tooth and an eye, since** in these cases **the Torah** explicitly **benefitted him.** Because his right to emancipation is stated explicitly in the verse, the slave does not require a bill of manumission. **And Rabbi Akiva's statement** is reasonable **with regard to other limbs, since it is a rabbinic penalty.** The Gemara is puzzled by this claim: **Is it** a rabbinic **penalty? We interpret** the **verses,** and therefore this *halakha* applies to the other limbs by Torah law. **Rather,** these Sages meant that is necessary to give a bill of manumission **since it is a rabbinic interpretation** and is not written explicitly in the Torah.

What defines the category shaped by the *prat*? *Mum she-bagalui ve-einan chozrin* — a visible blemish that does not grow back — is one dimension. But the *gemara* adds a further qualification: *batel m'melachto* — the injury must also render the affected limb unable to perform its normal function. A tooth that has been knocked out cannot chew; an eye that has been blinded cannot see. Any injury to the body that is visible, permanent, and disabling in this way falls within the category.

Two concrete examples are provided by a *baraita*: if the master pulled the slave's beard and thereby dislocated the slave's jaw — *talash b'zikano v'dildel bo etzem* — the slave goes free, because a dislocated jaw is visible, does not heal on its own, and prevents normal eating. Similarly, if the master struck the slave's arm and it withered — *hikahu al yado v'tzamtah* — but eventually the arm healed, the slave does *not* go free, because the injury was not permanent. The *baraita* makes this explicit: *ein eved yotzei bo l'cheirut*. This concrete ruling serves to limit the scope of *klal u-prat u-klal*, anchoring the requirement of *eino chozer* — that the injury must be one that does not heal — as an essential qualification.

### Tannaim Dispute

The *gemara* turns to a deeper dispute: does the injury itself effect the slave's freedom automatically, or does it merely create an obligation on the master to issue a *get shichrur*? A *baraita* records the full range of *tanna'itic* opinion. Rabbi Shimon holds that for all such injuries — including tooth and eye — the slave goes free but *requires* a *get shichrur*: the injury creates an obligation, it does not itself accomplish the manumission. Rabbi Meir holds the opposite: *eino tzarich* — no *get shichrur* is required; the injury itself liberates the slave. Rabbi Eliezer agrees with Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Tarfon agrees with Rabbi Meir.

The *gemara* also records the view of the *machri'im lifnei chachamim* — those who adjudicated before the *chachamim* — who adopt a split ruling.

### Chachamim Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן, שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה זִכְּתָה לוֹ, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּשְׁאָר אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon's statement appears** correct **with regard to a tooth and an eye, since** in these cases **the Torah** explicitly **benefitted him.** Because his right to emancipation is stated explicitly in the verse, the slave does not require a bill of manumission. **And Rabbi Akiva's statement** is reasonable **with regard to other limbs.**

*Nir'in divrei Rabbi Tarfon b'shein ve'ayin* — the reasoning of Rabbi Tarfon appears correct with respect to tooth and eye, *shehatorah zachta lo* — because the Torah itself explicitly granted the slave his freedom through these specific injuries, and therefore the injury itself is the operative mechanism. *Ve-divrei Rabbi Akiva b'sha'ar eivarim* — but with respect to other limbs, whose inclusion is derived only through the *midrash* of the *chachamim* rather than being explicit in the Torah, Rabbi Akiva's position appears correct: a *get shichrur* is still required.

The *gemara* notes that to call the derivation for other limbs *midrash chachamim* — as opposed to explicit Torah — is not to call it merely rabbinic. The *drasha* from *klal u-prat u-klal* does carry *de'oraita* force. Nevertheless, what is learned through *drashot* has a somewhat lesser degree of textual explicitness than what is stated plainly in the *pesukim*, and the *Rambam* is particularly attentive to this distinction — though calling it purely rabbinic would be an overstatement. This reflects a broader *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish about whether the majority of Torah is *Torah she-bichsav* or *Torah she-ba'al peh*, and whether *drashot* are uncovering something latent in the written text or transmitting an oral tradition that was subsequently anchored to verses.

As for the substance of the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon: Rabbi Shimon derives from a *gezeirah shavah* of *shiluach-shiluach* — the freeing of a slave compared to the divorce of a woman — that just as a woman requires a *shtar*, so too a slave requires a *shtar*. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, reads the phrase *le-chofshi yishalchenu* as indicating that at the moment of the injury the slave is *already* free — *hava lei chofshi me'ikara* — and no subsequent document is needed to accomplish what the injury itself has already accomplished. On this view, the injury does not merely obligate the master to free the slave; rather, by injuring the slave in this way, the master has forfeited his position as slave master entirely, and the slave goes free as a direct consequence.

## Direct vs. Indirect

### Direct Contact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִכָּהוּ עַל עֵינוֹ וְסִמְּאָהּ, עַל אׇזְנוֹ וְחֵרְשָׁהּ – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. נֶגֶד עֵינוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה, כְּנֶגֶד אׇזְנוֹ וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ – אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. אָמַר רַב שֶׁמֶן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְמֵימְרָא דְּקָלָא לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If a slave owner **struck** his slave **on his eye and blinded him,** or **on his ear and deafened him, the slave is emancipated by means of these** injuries. If he struck **near his eye and** as a result **he does not see,** or **near his ear and he does not hear, the slave is not emancipated by means of these** injuries. **Rav Shemen said to Rav Ashi: Is this to say that a sound** that causes damage **is nothing?**

A new *baraita* is introduced that addresses the question of what type of injury triggers a slave's freedom. *Hikeihu al eino v'sima'ah* — if the master struck the slave's eye and blinded it, *al ozno v'chershah* — or struck his ear and deafened it — *eved yotzei bahen l'cheirus* — the slave goes free. The injury here is a direct physical contact to the affected organ, even though the resulting damage is to the function of the organ rather than to its physical structure. Striking the eye and causing blindness, or striking the ear and causing deafness, constitutes the kind of visible, permanent, functional loss that brings the slave his freedom.

### Non-Contact Injury

By contrast, *keneged eino v'eino ro'eh* — if the master did something in front of the slave's eye such that the slave lost his sight without any physical contact to the eye — *keneged ozno v'eino shomei'a* — or caused the slave to go deaf through some non-contact means — *ein eved yotzei bahen l'cheirus* — the slave does not go free. The *baraita* thus distinguishes between a direct physical blow to the organ and a non-contact mechanism that produces the same functional result.

### Sound Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: תַּרְנְגוֹל שֶׁהוֹשִׁיט רֹאשׁוֹ לַאֲוִיר כְּלִי זְכוּכִית, וְתָקַע בּוֹ וּשְׁבָרוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב: סוּס שֶׁצָּנַף וַחֲמוֹר שֶׁנָּעַר, וְשָׁבְרוּ כֵּלִים בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת – מְשַׁלְּמִים חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rami bar Yeḥezkel teach** a *baraita*: With regard to **a rooster that stuck its head into the airspace of a glass vessel and crowed into it and** the noise **broke it,** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage** like a regular case of damage. **And Rav Yosef said** that the Sages of **Rav's study hall say:** With regard to **a horse that neighed or a donkey that brayed and they** thereby **broke vessels in the house,** their owners must **pay** for **half** of **the damage.** Although these two examples are different, as one results in full payment and the other in half payment, it is clear that sound can be a source of liability for damages.

This distinction prompts an immediate challenge from Rav Shemen to Rav Ashi: does this mean that *kol* — sound — has no physical force in halacha? Is non-contact acoustic injury simply a legal nullity? Surely not, since a *baraita* recorded by Rami bar Yechezkel in the context of Bava Kamma addresses precisely this: a chicken that inserts its head into a glass vessel and crows inside it, shattering the glass without any contact — according to Sumchos, the owner pays full *nezek*. And Rav Yosef transmits from *bei Rav* that if a horse whinnies or a donkey brays and the sound breaks vessels inside the house, the owner pays *chatzi nezek*. Whether one follows Sumchos (full payment) or the *rabbanan* (half payment), the point stands that sound-induced property damage does create liability for *nezikin*. Why, then, should non-contact acoustic injury to a slave not suffice to free him?

### Person vs. Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי אָדָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּבַר דַּעַת הוּא – אִיהוּ מְיבַעֵית נַפְשֵׁיהּ. כִּדְתַנְיָא: הַמַּבְעִית אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. כֵּיצַד? תָּקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵרְשׁוֹ – פָּטוּר, אֲחָזָהוּ וְתָקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵרְשׁוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: A person is different, as, since he is** mentally **competent it is he who frightened himself.** It was not the physical sound that caused the damage but the slave's subjective fear, which is not a direct result of the action. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that injury can result from being frightened: **One who frightens another** and causes him injury is **exempt according to human laws but liable according to the laws of Heaven. How so?** If **one shouted into** another's **ear and deafened him,** he is **exempt.** But if **he held him and shouted into his ear and deafened him,** he is **liable** according to human laws as well, as he took physical hold of him. Once he physically took hold of him, he is liable for all the resultant damage.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty with a crucial distinction: *shani adam dekivan debar daat hu ihu miv'it nafsheih* — a person is different, because being a *bar daat*, a person with awareness, the victim is in some sense involved in the act of being injured. The precise nature of this involvement is elusive. It is not that the slave is actively complicit in his own hearing loss, but somehow the victim's status as a *bar daat* — as a person with legal consciousness and awareness — distinguishes the case from that of a *kli*, a glass vessel, which has no such standing. The *gemara* does not spell out the mechanism in full, but the distinction operates to mean that non-contact injury to a person has a different halakhic character than non-contact damage to an object.

### Blowing Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּבְעִית אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. כֵּיצַד? תָּקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵרְשׁוֹ – פָּטוּר, אֲחָזָהוּ וְתָקַע בְּאׇזְנוֹ וְחֵרְשׁוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who frightens another** and causes him injury is **exempt from human courts but liable in the heavenly court. How so?** If **one blows into someone's ear and deafens him,** without holding the person, he is **exempt.** But if **he held the person and then blew into his ear and deafened him,** he is **liable.** The holding of the victim converts what would otherwise be a non-contact acoustic injury into one with a sufficient element of physical contact to create liability.

A final *baraita* is cited to crystallize the distinction: *hamav'it chavero patur midinei adam ve-chayav b'dinei shamayim* — one who startles another person and causes injury is exempt from human courts but liable in the heavenly court. Then the specific case: *taka be'ozno ve'chershah patur* — if one blows into someone's ear and deafens him, without holding the person, he is exempt. *Achazo ve-taka be'ozno ve'chershah chayav* — but if he held the person and then blew into his ear and deafened him, he is liable. The holding of the victim converts what would otherwise be a non-contact acoustic injury into one with a sufficient element of physical contact to create liability. This *baraita* neatly encapsulates the principle that non-contact injury to a *bar daat* occupies a different legal category — and, by extension, that only direct physical injury to the relevant organ suffices to free a slave through the mechanism the Torah established for *shen* and *ayin*.
