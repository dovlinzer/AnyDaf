# Eruvin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Replenishing & Informing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִתְמַעֵט הָאוֹכֶל — מוֹסִיף וּמְזַכֶּה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ. נִתּוֹסְפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן — מוֹסִיף וּמְזַכֶּה, וְצָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If the food** in the barrel for the merging of the alleyway **diminished** and was less than the requisite measure, **one** may **add** a little of his own **and confer possession** to the others, **and he need not inform** them of his addition. However, if new residents **were added to** the residents of the alleyway, **he may add** food on behalf of those residents **and confer possession** to them, **and he must inform** the new residents of their inclusion in the merging of alleyways.

### Replenishing with Same/Different Food

The daf begins at 81b, precisely at the passage of *nitosfu alehem mosif u'mezake* — the case of replenishing a depleted *eruv*. The context is one in which food had been collected for an *eruv* or *shituf* and then became depleted — eaten, lost, or otherwise consumed. If one is replenishing with the same type of food as was originally used, the Gemara indicates that one need not seek the participants' permission anew. If, however, one wishes to replenish with a different type of food — say, the original was wine and one now wishes to add oil — then depending on circumstances, particularly if the original supply had been entirely consumed and one is effectively starting from scratch, consultation may be required.

An underlying ambiguity runs through this entire discussion: whose food is being used for the replenishment? If one is contributing from one's own stores and simply conferring the benefit upon the courtyard's residents, why should their permission be necessary at all? One is merely doing them a benefit. If, however, one is drawing upon the participants' own food to which one happens to have access, then any significant new action might well require their authorization, since they had initially consented to participate but not necessarily to this new arrangement. The Gemara does not address this distinction directly, and the ambiguity persists.

### New Residents & Notification

The Mishnah adds that if new residents have moved into the courtyard between one Shabbat and the next, one must add food to the *eruv* and *tzarich lehodiya* — one must inform the newcomers. Again, it is not entirely clear whether the obligation to inform arises because one is taking from their food, or whether even when using one's own food there is still a requirement to notify them. The Gemara leaves this ambiguous as well.

### R. Yehuda: No Notification Required for Pure Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין, אֲבָל בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת — מְעָרְבִין בֵּין לְדַעַת וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what case is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to a joining of** Shabbat **boundaries, but with regard to a joining of courtyards, one** may **establish an *eiruv*** for a person either **with his knowledge or without his knowledge.**

In response to this Mishnah, Rav Shizbi said in the name of Rav Chisda: *zot omeret chalukin alav chaverav al Rabbi Yehuda* — this teaches that Rabbi Yehuda's colleagues disagree with him. The Rabbi Yehuda in question is the one who appears in the upcoming Mishnah: *bameh devarim amurim be-eruvei techumin* — when is it that a person's awareness and buy-in are required? It is specifically by *eruv techumin*, where making an *eruv* in one direction benefits the person in that direction but curtails his range in the opposite direction. There, one clearly needs the person's participation from the outset. *Aval be-eruvei chatzerot* — but when it comes to a courtyard *eruv*, which only benefits everyone involved, *me'arvin ben l'daato ben shelo l'daato* — one may make the *eruv* whether or not the person is aware, since it is an unqualified benefit.

The Gemara thus assumes that our Mishnah, which requires informing new residents even when doing something beneficial for them, conflicts with Rabbi Yehuda's position. The Gemara does not entertain the possibility that the cases differ based on whose food is being used — it simply assumes that the question of notification applies equally in both scenarios. The Gemara notes: *peshita de-chalukin* — obviously they disagree! Our Mishnah requires notification; Rabbi Yehuda does not.

### Two-Alleyway Courtyard Issue

But then the Gemara reconsiders. One might have thought that our Mishnah requiring notification applies only to the case of *chatzer she-bein shtei mavo'ot* — a courtyard situated between two alleyways, as discussed the previous day, where the courtyard has a choice of which alleyway to affiliate with. In such a case, joining alleyway A is not obviously to one's benefit over alleyway B, and the courtyard's participation may not even obstruct other courtyards. There, naturally, one would need the residents' buy-in. But *aval chatzer she-bemavoi echad* — if the courtyard has only one alleyway it can join, where the benefit is clear and non-participation would obstruct others, *eima lo* — perhaps even our Mishnah would agree that no notification is needed, or one could even compel participation. *Kama shma lan* — so the Gemara tells us that our Mishnah speaks even of the single-alleyway case, and still requires notification.

The conclusion of this passage is therefore that there is a genuine dispute between our Mishnah and Rabbi Yehuda: our Mishnah holds that even a courtyard with only one alleyway — where the benefit of participation is unambiguous and where non-participation creates a problem for others — nonetheless must have its residents informed. Rabbi Yehuda holds that no notification is required under these circumstances. And the Gemara, for its part, does not resolve the question of whose food is being used, making it genuinely difficult to understand why our Mishnah would require notification if one is using exclusively one's own food to benefit others.

## Shiur: Seudot vs. Grogeret

### Large Number Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הוּא שִׁיעוּרָן? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְרוּבִּין — מְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת לְכוּלָּם. בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מוּעָטִין — כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the measure** of food required for a merging of the alleyways? **When** the residents of the alley **are numerous, food for two meals** is sufficient **for all of them; when they are few,** less than a certain number, **a dried fig-bulk for each and every one of them** is enough.

The Mishnah concludes with the question of *kama shiuro* — what is the minimum quantity of food required for a valid *eruv*? The answer is: *shtei seudot* worth of food for the entire group when the group is large; but when the group is small, each person must contribute a quantity the size of a *grogeret* — a dried fig. One might ask: what constitutes a "large" number?

### Eighteen-Person Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הוּא מְרוּבִּין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה בְּנֵי אָדָם. שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה, וְתוּ לָא: אֵימָא מִשְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **How many are numerous,** and how many are considered few? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Eighteen people** are considered numerous. The Gemara registers surprise: **Eighteen and no more?** The Gemara answers: **Say: From eighteen upward.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *shmonah asar bnei adam* — eighteen people. The Gemara immediately asks: *shmonah asar v'tu lo* — only exactly eighteen, and no more? The clarification follows: the intent is *mishmonah asar v'eilach* — eighteen and above. Any group of eighteen or more is considered a "large number," and *shtei seudot* worth suffices for everyone.

### Grogeret to Seudot Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה דְּנָקֵט? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִנֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּא: כׇּל שֶׁאִילּוּ מְחַלְּקוֹ לִמְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת בֵּינֵיהֶן וְאֵין מַגַּעַת גְּרוֹגֶרֶת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד — הֵן הֵן מְרוּבִּין, וְסַגִּי בִּמְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת. וְאִי לָא — הֵן הֵן מוּעָטִין נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is** the significance of the number **eighteen** that **he cited?** Why this figure in particular? The Gemara answers that **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, said: It was explained to me** personally **by my father,** Rav Yehuda: **Any** case where, **if** one were to **divide** the food of **two meals between them and it does not amount to** the measure of **a dried fig for each and every one of them, these are the very ones** the *tanna* called **numerous, and** in this case **food for two meals suffices** for all of them. **And if not, these are the very ones** the *tanna* termed **few,** which means food in the measure of a dried fig is required for each of them.

The Gemara then asks: why specifically eighteen? Rav Yitzchak the son of Rav Yehuda explained it in the name of his father: *kol she'ilu mechalko le'mazon shtei seudot beineihem ein magia k'grogeret le'chol echad v'echad* — if you were to divide two meals' worth of food among all the participants and each person would receive less than a *grogeret*, that is a large group and two meals' worth applies. The logic is that the required quantity is the lesser of the two standards: either a *grogeret* per person or *shtei seudot* for everyone. For seventeen people, each contributing a *grogeret* amounts to a total less than *shtei seudot*; therefore one still goes by a *grogeret* per person. But once there are eighteen people, eighteen *grogeros* exceeds the value of *shtei seudot*, so the *shtei seudot* figure becomes the operative standard. And in passing, the Gemara notes: *d'shtei seudot havyan shmonah asar grogerot* — two meals' worth of food equals eighteen dried fig portions in volume.

This is precisely why, practically speaking, a rabbi making an *eruv* for an entire city — whether ten thousand residents or more — can satisfy the requirement with a single box of *matzos*, since *shtei seudot* worth suffices for any number of participants.

## Types of Food for Eruv

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין, חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וּמִן הַמֶּלַח, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כִּכָּר הוּא עֵירוּב, אֲפִילּוּ מַאֲפֶה סְאָה, וְהוּא פְּרוּסָה — אֵין מְעָרְבִין בָּהּ. כִּכָּר כְּאִיסָּר וְהוּא שָׁלֵם — מְעָרְבִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One** may **join** courtyards **and merge** alleyways **with all** types of food, **except for water and salt,** as they are not considered foods. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua says** that a different limitation applies: A whole **loaf** may be used for **an *eiruv*.** With regard to **a baked** product even the size **of a *se'a*, if it** consists of **pieces, one** may **not join** courtyards **with it.** However, with regard to **a loaf,** even one the size of **an *issar*, if it is whole, one** may **join** courtyards **with it.**

The next Mishnah addresses what type of food may be used for an *eruv*. *Bechol me'arvin u'mishtatfin* — one may use any food for an *eruv* or *shituf*, *chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — with the exception of water and salt, which are not considered real foods in the relevant sense. This is the position of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua, however, says: *kikar hu eruv* — only a loaf of bread constitutes a valid *eruv*. Moreover, even the form matters: *afilu maafeh se'ah vehu prusa ein me'arvin bah* — even if one has a gigantic loaf, large enough to be the baking of an entire *se'ah* of flour, if it is not whole, one cannot use it. Conversely, *kikar ke'issar vehu shalem me'arvin bah* — if one has a tiny roll no larger than a small coin but it is whole and intact, one may use it.

Rashi explains that this means each individual participant must contribute a whole loaf, not that the combined *eruv* as a whole must consist of whole loaves. Rashi adds at the end of his commentary: one must still satisfy both conditions — each person's contribution must be in the form of a whole loaf, and the total quantity must reach at least a *grogeret* per person in volume. If there are a thousand participants, one would theoretically need a thousand tiny whole rolls. How this interacts with the rule of *shtei seudot* for large groups is left somewhat open by Rashi, though a reasonable reading is that once the group exceeds eighteen, two large loaves — or one whole loaf of sufficient size — suffice for everyone, while for smaller groups each individual must provide a whole loaf.

### Whole Loaf Definition

The operative principle emerging from Rabbi Yehoshua's position is not the distinction between bread and other foods per se, but rather the requirement of wholeness: *shalem* versus *prusa*. A loaf the size of a coin qualifies if it is whole; a loaf as large as a wedding bread does not qualify if it is broken.

### Whole vs. Broken Bread

This gives rise to the question: does the Tanna Kamma — the first, anonymous position in the Mishnah that permits all foods — also require that the bread be whole? Or is the debate between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehoshua purely about whether bread is required at all? The Gemara proceeds to examine this question in detail.

## Two-Place Enumeration

### Why State Twice?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין, חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וְהַמֶּלַח!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's ruling concerning the foods which may or may not be used for an *eiruv* and to merge alleyways, the Gemara poses a question: This ruling is apparently superfluous, as we have already **learned** it **once** before in another mishna: **One** may **establish an *eiruv* and a merging of alleyways with all** kinds of food, **except for water and salt.**

The Gemara at the top of 81a notes: *tanina chada zimna* — we have already taught this rule! The formulation *bechol me'arvin u'mishtatfin, chutz min hamayim u'min hamelach* — that one may use any food except water and salt — appeared earlier in the *perek* called *bechol me'arvin*, which discussed *eruvei techumin*. Why is it being stated again here by *eruvei chatzerot*?

### Rabba's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּאָמַר: כִּכָּר — אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן ״בַּכֹּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** This addition comes **to exclude** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua** in the mishna, who **said** that **a loaf, yes,** it may be used for an *eiruv*; but **anything else, no,** other foods may not be used. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that an *eiruv* may be established **with all** kinds of food, not only bread.

Rabba answers: *la'apukei mi-deRabbi Yehoshua d'amar kikar in midei achrinei lo* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, who says that only bread qualifies. By stating the rule twice, the Mishnah underscores that we do not rule like Rabbi Yehoshua — in both contexts. Rashi explains that one might have thought Rabbi Yehoshua voiced his bread requirement only in the context of *eruvei chatzerot*, and that by *eruv techumin* he would concede that any food is acceptable. By repeating the *bechol me'arvin* formulation in both places, the Mishnah implies that Rabbi Yehoshua in fact held his position in both cases, and that the Tanna Kamma is therefore responding to him in both contexts. Hence the need for the double statement.

### Abaye's Challenge and Girsa Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת וּבַכֹּל מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין שִׁיתּוּפֵי מְבוֹאוֹת, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לְעָרֵב בְּפַת אֶלָּא בֶּחָצֵר בִּלְבַד. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר פַּת — אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְקָתָנֵי ״בַּכֹּל״!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection** from a *baraita*: **One** may **establish a joining of courtyards with all** kinds of food, **and** likewise **one may establish a merging of alleyways with all** kinds of food. **They said** that **one** must **establish an *eiruv* with bread only with regard to** an *eiruv* of **a courtyard. Who did you hear that said** that **bread, yes,** it may be used for an *eiruv*, but **anything else, no,** it may not be used? It was **Rabbi Yehoshua, and** yet the *baraita* **teaches: With all** kinds of food. This proves that the phrase: One may establish an *eiruv* with all kinds of food, does not necessarily exclude Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion.

Abaye then challenges this with a *braita*: *bechol me'arvin eruvei chatzerot uvechol mishtatfin shitufei mevo'ot, velo amru le'arev bepat ela bechatzer bilvad* — one may use any food for *eruvei chatzerot* and *shitufei mevo'ot*, and the requirement of bread applies only in the chatzer setting. This raises an immediate problem: first the *braita* says one may use anything, then it says bread is required — which is it?

Here the *girsa* becomes critical. Tosafot's version of the *braita* omits the phrase *eruvei chatzerot* from the opening clause. Without that phrase, the *braita* reads: one may use anything for an *eruv* — meaning *eruv techumin* — and anything for *shituf mevo'ot*, but when it comes to *eruvei chatzerot*, there one requires bread. Under this reading, the *braita* actually demonstrates that Rabbi Yehoshua agrees that for *eruv techumin* any food is acceptable; his bread requirement applies only to *eruvei chatzerot*.

### Reinterpretation: Wholeness, Not Food Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּאָמַר שְׁלֵימָה — אִין, פְּרוּסָה — לָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן ״בַּכֹּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said:** It comes **to exclude** a different aspect of the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, as** Rabbi Yehoshua **said: A whole** loaf, **yes,** it is fit to be used as an *eiruv*, but **a broken** loaf, **no,** it is not suitable for this purpose. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that one may prepare an *eiruv* **with all** kinds of bread, even a broken loaf.

With this reading, the implication is significant: the first *bechol me'arvin* stated in the earlier chapter about *eruvei techumin* was in fact *divrei hakol* — a position everyone, including Rabbi Yehoshua, agreed to. The debate between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehoshua only emerges in the context of *eruvei chatzerot*. Given this, the earlier *bechol me'arvin* statement was uncontested, and the question is: what does restating *bechol me'arvin* here accomplish? The Gemara's answer is that in this context the phrase does not mean all types of food — since by *eruvei chatzerot* even the Tanna Kamma may agree that bread is required — but rather all forms of bread: *la'apukei mi-deRabbi Yehoshua d'amar shleima in prusa lo kama shma lan bechol* — to exclude Rabbi Yehoshua's position that only a whole loaf qualifies and a broken piece does not. The word *bechol* here thus teaches that any form of the bread is acceptable, whole or broken, and not that any type of food is acceptable.

The conclusion is striking: by *eruv techumin* everyone agrees any food works; by *eruvei chatzerot* the Gemara seems to be converging on a reading where even the Tanna Kamma agrees it must be bread, and the debate between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehoshua is only about whether it must be a *whole* loaf. The Gemara then proceeds to take this question of wholeness as its central concern.

## Whole Loaf & Eivah

### Broken Loaves & Competition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְרוּסָה מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר רַבִּי: מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Yehoshua's position itself: **And** with regard to a **broken** loaf of bread, **what is the reason** that it may **not** be used for an *eiruv*? **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** The reason is **due to** potential **enmity** between neighbors.

*U-frusa mai ta'ama lo* — why does Rabbi Yehoshua disqualify a broken loaf? Rabbi Yosi ben Shaul said in the name of Rabbi: *mishum eivah* — because of enmity or social rivalry. The concern is that if broken loaves were permitted, participants would begin comparing contributions: one person gives a whole loaf while another gives a partial loaf, and this gives rise to ill-feeling and competition. Just as there is a takkanah that on Tu B'Av all the young women should dress uniformly so as to eliminate the embarrassment of the less wealthy, so too by the *eruv* one requires a uniform standard — everyone gives a whole loaf — in order to prevent this sort of invidious comparison.

### Escalation Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: עֵירְבוּ כּוּלָּן בִּפְרוּסוֹת מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁמָּא יַחְזוֹר דָּבָר לְקִלְקוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** If **they all established** the ***eiruv* with broken** loaves of bread, **what is** the *halakha*? In this case there is no cause for enmity. Rav Ashi **said to him:** There is nonetheless a concern **lest the problem recur,** as one of them might give an entire loaf and proceed to complain about his neighbor who donated only a partial loaf.

### Deterioration of Practice

Rav Acha the son of Rava asked Rav Ashi: what if everyone agreed to give broken loaves? If the concern is only comparison between participants, and everyone brings the same broken piece, what objection remains? Rav Ashi responded: *shema yachzor davar le-kilkulo* — we fear that once one permits broken loaves even as a uniform practice, someone will then choose to demonstrate generosity by bringing a whole loaf, prompting others to respond in kind with even larger loaves, and the entire standardization unravels. Therefore, even a collective arrangement of broken loaves is disqualified, because it is inherently unstable and leads to the deterioration of the practice.

There is also a beautiful dimension here, beyond the legalistic concern about *eivah*. The Gemara earlier emphasized that the very name *eruv* — meaning mixture or joining — captures the essential idea of creating community. Bread itself symbolizes this: flour, water, and other ingredients come together into something unified. The whole loaf embodies the aspiration of the *eruv* — smaller contributions joining into a coherent whole — even if this is not the Gemara's explicit reasoning here.

## Defining Wholeness

### Challah & Dimua Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן שָׁאוּל: נִיטְּלָה הֵימֶנָּה כְּדֵי חַלָּתָהּ וּכְדֵי דִימּוּעָהּ — מְעָרְבִין לוֹ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** A portion of dough, known as *ḥalla*, must be set aside and given to the priests. If *ḥalla* was not set aside from the dough, it must be separated from the baked bread. Moreover, if one part *teruma* fell into a hundred parts non-sacred produce, a proportionate amount must be removed from the mixture and given to a priest, after which the remainder may be eaten. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Shaul said: If one removed from** the loaf **as much as** must be set aside for **its *ḥalla*, or as much as** must be separated **from a mixture** of *teruma* and non-sacred produce, **he** may **establish an *eiruv* with** that loaf.

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of ben Shaul: *nitla heimena k'dei challah u-k'dei dimua me'arvin bah* — if from a loaf there was removed an amount equivalent to *challah* or an amount equivalent to *dimua*, one may still use it for the *eruv*. This addresses the question of how much material must be missing before a loaf is no longer considered whole. *Challah* refers to the portion of dough that must be separated and given to a *kohen* when baking above the minimum quantity. *Dimua* refers to a mixture in which forbidden material — such as *terumah* or *challah* — fell into a loaf, and one must then remove one one-hundredth of the mixture; that one one-hundredth is the *dimua* amount.

### Contradiction and Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּדֵי דִימּוּעָהּ — מְעָרְבִין לוֹ בָּהּ, כְּדֵי חַלָּתָהּ — אֵין מְעָרְבִין לוֹ בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Wasn't it was taught** otherwise in a *baraita*: If a loaf was missing **as much as** must be removed **from a mixture** of *teruma* and non-sacred produce, **one may establish an *eiruv* with it,** but if it was missing **as much as** must be removed for **its *ḥalla*, one may not establish an *eiruv* with it?**

The question then arises from a *braita*: *k'dei dimua me'arvin bah k'dei challah ein me'arvin bah* — if the missing amount equals the *dimua* portion one may use the loaf, but if the missing amount equals the *challah* portion, one may not! This contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that both amounts are acceptable.

### Homeowner vs. Baker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּחַלַּת נַחְתּוֹם, הָא — בְּחַלַּת בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as the two sources are not dealing with the same amounts. In **this** case, where the *tanna* permitted a loaf that was missing the amount that must be removed for *ḥalla*, he is referring **to a baker's *ḥalla*,** which is a smaller amount and is therefore not considered a significant reduction of the loaf. However, in **that** case, where the *tanna* did not permit a loaf that was lacking the amount that must be removed for *ḥalla*, it is referring **to an** ordinary **homeowner's *ḥalla*.** This *ḥalla* portion is larger in size, and consequently the loaf may not be used for an *eiruv* if it is missing such a large amount.

The resolution is: *lo kashya ha be-challas nachtom ha be-challas ba'al habayit* — no difficulty; one case refers to a baker's *challah* and the other to a homeowner's *challah*, because the percentage differs between them. As the Mishnah teaches: a homeowner baking for private consumption — even if he bakes a hundred loaves for his son's wedding — separates one twenty-fourth as *challah*. A baker (*nachtom*) who bakes to sell in the marketplace, as well as a woman who bakes at home for sale, separates only one forty-eighth. The rationale for the baker's reduced percentage is either that it reflects a rough estimate of large-quantity production, or more compellingly, that the rabbis showed a measure of consideration for the baker's profit margin and did not demand as large a portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: שִׁיעוּר חַלָּה אֶחָד מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה. הָעוֹשֶׂה עִיסָּה לְעַצְמוֹ וְעִיסָּה לְמִשְׁתֶּה בְנוֹ, אֶחָד מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה. נַחְתּוֹם שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה לִמְכּוֹר בַּשּׁוּק, וְכֵן הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁעָשְׁתָה לִמְכּוֹר בַּשּׁוּק — אֶחָד מֵאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this is **as we learned** in a mishna: **The measure of *ḥalla*** fixed by the Sages is **one twenty-fourth** of the dough. Consequently, **one who prepares dough for himself or dough for his son's** wedding **feast,** the measure for *ḥalla* is **one twenty-fourth.** However, **a baker who prepares** dough **for sale in the market, and likewise a woman who prepares** dough **for sale in the market,** is required to separate only **one forty-eighth** of the dough, as the Sages were lenient with those who sell their wares so that they should not suffer loss.

Accordingly, when a homeowner's *challah* is removed — one twenty-fourth of the loaf — that is a substantial portion, large enough to render the loaf no longer whole and therefore disqualified for use as an *eruv*. When a baker's *challah* is removed — one forty-eighth, a mere two percent — the loaf remains substantially intact and is still considered *shalem*.

### Sewn Bread & Visibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: תְּפָרָהּ בְּקֵיסָם מְעָרְבִין לוֹ בָּהּ. וְהָא תַּנְיָא אֵין מְעָרְבִין לוֹ בָּהּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — דִּידִיעַ תִּיפְרָהּ, הָא — דְּלָא יְדִיעַ תִּיפְרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **one who connected** the two portions of a broken loaf **with a** wood **chip, one** may **establish an *eiruv* with it,** as it looks whole. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a case of this kind that **one** may **not establish an *eiruv* with it?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as in **this** case, where it may not be used for an *eiruv*, we are dealing with a situation **where the seam is conspicuous;** however, in **that** case, where it may be used for an *eiruv*, the reference is to a situation **where the seam is not conspicuous.**

Rav Chisda rules: *tafra bekisam mearvin bo* — if a loaf broke in two and was then "sewn" back together with a splinter or toothpick, holding the halves in place, one may use it for the *eruv*. A *braita*, however, contradicts this: one may not use such a loaf. The resolution: *lo kashya ha d'yedia tafra ha d'lo yedia tafra* — if the repair is visibly apparent it is disqualified, but if the repair is concealed and the loaf appears whole, it is valid. Throughout this discussion, the Gemara operates under the assumption that we rule like Rabbi Yehoshua on the requirement of wholeness, and the entire analysis is aimed at defining what "whole" means for practical purposes.

Tosafot raises a further refinement: why does the passage speak of *k'dei challah* — "the amount of *challah*" — rather than simply saying *challah nitalah* — "the *challah* was taken"? This phrasing suggests that perhaps the ruling applies not only when the *challah* was formally separated, but even when a piece broke off accidentally, so long as the missing piece is equivalent in size to the *challah* portion. Tosafot ultimately does not favor this reading — preferring to limit the ruling to actual *challah* removal — but acknowledges that a straightforward reading of the Gemara could support the broader application, namely that even accidental breakage of a certain size disqualifies the loaf.

## Grain Varieties for Eruv

### Rice vs. Millet Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת אוֹרֶז וּבְפַת דּוֹחַן. אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל, בְּפַת אוֹרֶז — מְעָרְבִין, וּבְפַת דּוֹחַן — אֵין מְעָרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Shmuel said: One** may **establish an *eiruv*** even **with rice bread or with millet bread. Mar Ukva said:** This ruling **was explained to me by Mar Shmuel** himself. **With rice bread one** may **establish an *eiruv*, but with millet bread one** may **not establish an *eiruv*,** as it is difficult to bake edible bread out of millet.

Rav Zeira said in the name of Shmuel: *me'arvin be-pas orez* — one may use rice bread for an *eruv*. However, Mar Ukva adds a clarification received directly from Shmuel: *pas orez me'arvin pas dochen ein me'arvin* — rice bread is valid, but millet bread is not. The distinction is not about the halakhic category of *hamotzi*, since rice is well-known not to carry the obligation of *hamotzi* in the normative sense. The *chiddush* here is precisely that even food not classified as *lechem* for the purposes of *birkat ha'mazon* may qualify for *eruv* if it is sufficiently staple-like and satiating. The question is therefore one of relative edibility and adequacy as a food.

### Edibility Standard

The implicit principle is that what qualifies for *eruv* must be something a person would genuinely eat as sustenance. Rice, despite its halakhic status, is sufficiently edible and satiating to meet this standard. Millet, apparently, is not — it is less palatable and less nourishing in the relevant sense, and therefore does not qualify.

### Lentil Bread & Yechezkel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת עֲדָשִׁים. אִינִי?! וְהָא הָהִיא דַּהֲוַאי בִּשְׁנֵי דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְשַׁדְיַיהּ לְכַלְבֵּיהּ וְלָא אַכְלַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said** that **Rav said: One** may **establish an *eiruv* with lentil bread.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Is that so?** Is such bread edible? **But there was that** lentil bread **in the days of Mar Shmuel, which he threw to his dog, and** even **it would not eat it.** Clearly, lentil bread is not fit for human consumption.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abin said in the name of Rav: *me'arvin be-pas adashin* — one may even use bread made from lentils for an *eruv*. The Gemara immediately challenges this. Was there not a person in the time of Shmuel who made such a lentil-bean bread and threw it to his dog to test its edibility, and even the dog would not eat it? If lentil bread is so unpalatable that a dog refuses it, how can it qualify for the *eruv*?

### Mixed Grain Bread & Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דִּשְׁאָר מִינִים הָוְיָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתָּה קַח לְךָ חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִים וּפוֹל וַעֲדָשִׁים וְדוֹחַן וְכוּסְּמִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** bread which the dog refused to eat **was** a mixture of **various types** of grain. It was baked in order to discover the taste of a bread of mixed ingredients and was similar to that which the prophet Ezekiel was commanded to eat, as it is written: **"Take you for yourself wheat, and barley, and beans, and lentils, and millet, and spelt,** and put them in one vessel, and make them for yourself into bread" (Ezekiel 4:9). This bread is unfit for human consumption, as even a dog at times will not eat it. However, bread prepared from lentils alone is edible.

The Gemara responds: *hahi de-shar minim hava* — that was not pure lentil bread; it was a multi-grain mixture. And the *braita* identifies its source: the verse in Yechezkel, *ve'atah kach lekha chitin u'se'orim* and *ufol va'adasim vedochen ve'chusmin* — take wheat, barley, beans, lentils, millet, and spelt — a compendium of grains described by God in His instruction to the prophet. This multi-grain bread, made from an indiscriminate mixture of grains, is indeed inedible. But bread made purely from lentils is a different matter and is reasonably edible.

### Ezekiel Bread Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָהִיא צְלוּיָה בְּצוֹאַת הָאָדָם הֲוַאי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִיא בְּגֶלְלֵי צֵאַת הָאָדָם תְּעֻגֶנָה לְעֵינֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: That** bread of Ezekiel's **was roasted in human excrement, as it is written:** "And you shall eat it as barley cakes, **and you shall bake it with human excrement, in their sight"** (Ezekiel 4:12).

The full context of the Yechezkel passage is one of siege, famine, and prophetic enactment. God instructs Yechezkel to eat a distasteful, multi-grain bread as a symbol of privation: *ve-hi be-gilalei tzo'at adam te'agennah* — and you shall bake it with human excrement as fuel. The Gemara identifies this as the element that made the bread in Shmuel's time inedible: Rav Pappa states that *hahi tzluya be-tzo'at adam hava* — that bread was baked by burning human excrement. Tosafot explains that the person in Shmuel's time was conducting an experiment to understand what God had commanded Yechezkel, reproducing the bread exactly as described, and discovered it was entirely inedible. This, rather than the grain mixture alone, was the decisive factor in its repulsiveness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְעוּגַת שְׂעֹרִים תֹּאכְלֶנָּה״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לְשִׁיעוּרִים. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: עֲרִיבָתָהּ כַּעֲרִיבַת שְׂעוֹרִים, וְלֹא כַּעֲרִיבַת חִטִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned this verse, the Gemara asks a related question: **What is** the meaning of: **"And you shall eat it as barley [*seorim*] cakes"? Rav Ḥisda said:** The verse means that he should eat it **in** small **measures [*leshiurim*],** not as a satisfying meal. **Rav Pappa said: Its preparation** must be **like the preparation of barley** bread, coarse bread with regard to which one invests little effort, **and not like the preparation of wheat** bread.

The Gemara further adds: *mai ugat se'orim tochalennah* — why does the verse also speak of barley cakes? Rav Chisda explains: *lishiurim* — to be eaten little by little, in small measured portions, further conveying the atmosphere of siege and scarcity, in which one eats whatever minimal sustenance is available in restricted quantities. Rav Pappa adds a further detail: *arivuta ke'arivat se'orim velo ke'arivat chitin* — even the kneading process for this bread followed the inferior method used for barley rather than the better method used for wheat, resulting in a substandard product.

The passage has gained a certain notoriety in contemporary culture, as the verse Yechezkel 4:9 was adopted as the name of a commercial bread product. As one online commentator pointed out, the biblical "recipe" was meant to describe famine food under siege conditions — millet was historically poor man's food used to feed livestock — and had the manufacturers continued to verse 12, they would have encountered the instruction to bake with human dung.

## Practical Halacha & Custom

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The Shulchan Aruch rules in siman 366, se'if 6: *ein me'arvin be-ferusah afilu gedolah harbeh aval bishelimah afilu ketanah me'od me'arvin bah* — one may not make an *eruv* with a broken piece of bread, regardless of how large it is; but with a whole loaf, even an extremely small one, one may make an *eruv*, provided the total quantity meets the *shiur*. The Shulchan Aruch proceeds to address the *challah* and *dimua* cases as well.

### Rema's Flour Interpretation

The Rema adds an important qualification: *ve-yesh she-perishu ha de-ein me'arvin rak be-pas shalem hainu she-kol ha-eruv be-yachad yihiye pas shalem* — there are those who interpret the requirement of a whole loaf to mean not that each individual must contribute a whole loaf, but rather that the *eruv* as a combined unit should constitute a whole loaf. Under this reading, *shalem* describes the end product of the collective contribution, not each individual's portion.

### Community Unified Loaf

Accordingly, the Rema reports: *ve-chen nahagu lekabetz mikol bayit u-vayit me'at kemach* — the accepted practice was for each household to contribute a small amount of flour. From all of these contributions combined, one would then bake *challah achas sheleimah* — a single whole loaf — and use that loaf as the *eruv*. *Ve-chen ha-minhag pashut be-khol medinos elu* — this was the widespread custom. The loaf had to be large enough to contain *shtei seudot* worth of food, and the practice is deeply symbolic: just as the *eruv* creates a unified community out of disparate households, so too the loaf is formed by bringing together smaller contributions from each home into a single, unified whole — an embodiment of the *eruv*'s essential meaning.

### Matzo Box Practice

In modern practice, the procedure has been simplified considerably: a box of *matzos* is used. *Matzos* are whole loaves, they are durable and do not spoil, and a full box easily constitutes *shtei seudot* worth for any size community, even one of tens of thousands. The community representative takes possession of the box on behalf of the entire *kahal*, and the *eruv* is thereby constituted for everyone. The Mishnah Berurah elaborates on the practice: the *shamash* gives the *eruv* to the rabbi on *erev Pesach* for him to acquire it on behalf of the entire congregation. The *eruv* is typically done on *erev Pesach* precisely because *matzos* are the obvious food to use at that time of year. The Mishnah Berurah also addresses the case where one forgot to transfer possession before Yom Tov began, and under what conditions the *eruv* nonetheless remains valid — a discussion that connects directly to the ongoing Gemara analysis of ownership transfer and *zechiyah*.

## Money & Ownership Transfer

### R. Eliezer: Money Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹתֵן אָדָם מָעָה לַחֶנְוָנִי וְלַנַּחְתּוֹם כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּזַכֶּה לוֹ עֵירוּב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person** may **give a *ma'a*** coin **to a grocer or a baker,** if they live in the same alleyway or courtyard, **so that** the grocer or baker **will confer upon him possession** of wine or bread **for** a merging of the alleyway or **an *eiruv*,** if other residents come to them to purchase these products for that purpose. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

The next Mishnah introduces the mechanism of transferring ownership through monetary payment: *nosein adam ma'ah le-chanvani ve-la-nachtom kedei she-yizkeh lo eruv divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — a person may give a *ma'ah* of money to a shopkeeper or a baker so that the baker will thereby acquire a share in the *eruv* on the person's behalf. Rabbi Eliezer holds that this works: paying the money creates the necessary ownership interest.

### Chachamim: No Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא זָכוּ לוֹ מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: His money did not confer possession on him,** as the transfer of money alone is not a valid mode of acquisition and cannot confer possession. One must perform a valid mode of acquisition, e.g., pulling an article into one's possession, to transfer ownership.

*Ve-chachamim omrim lo zachu lo ma'osav* — the Sages disagree: the money did not effect a valid acquisition. Their reasoning is that for movable goods, a *kinyan* requires *hagbahah* — lifting the object — or *meshichah* — drawing it to oneself. Money alone does not complete the transaction. The person who paid has not done either of these, and therefore never actually acquired a portion of the bread.

Moreover, there is a compounding problem. One might think that even without a personal *kinyan*, the person would be included in the communal *zechiyah* when someone takes possession of the bread on behalf of the entire courtyard. But the Sages hold that by paying the baker, the person has signaled his intent to purchase — and by doing so has effectively excluded himself from those who receive the bread as a free benefit from the communal *zechiyah*. He indicated he wants to buy bread, not receive it. The result is that the purchase did not go through (no *meshichah*), and he has excluded himself from the communal acquisition — leaving him in limbo.

### Private Individual Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים בִּשְׁאָר כׇּל הָאָדָם, שֶׁזָּכוּ לוֹ מְעוֹתָיו. שֶׁאֵין מְעָרְבִין לָאָדָם אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Rabbis **concede with regard to all** other **people,** apart from grocers and bakers, **that** if one gave them money for the food of an *eiruv*, **his money confers possession upon him, as one** may **establish an *eiruv* for a person only with his knowledge** and at his bidding.

*U-modim bi-sh'ar kol adam she-zachu lo ma'osav* — the Sages, however, agree that paying an ordinary private individual does work. The distinction is fundamental: when one gives money to a baker, one is attempting to purchase bread from a vendor — a commercial transaction requiring *meshichah*. But when one gives money to a friend and says "make an *eruv* for me," one is not purchasing an object at all. One is appointing that person as a *shaliach* — an agent — and the *shaliach* then goes and takes physical possession of the bread on the person's behalf. When the agent lifts or draws the bread, the *kinyan* is complete, and one is fully included in the *eruv*.

### Knowledge Requirement

The requirement of *da'at* — awareness and intentional participation — is what makes the difference in these borderline cases.

## R. Yehuda on Awareness

### Techumin vs. Chatzerot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין, אֲבָל בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת — מְעָרְבִין לְדַעְתּוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתּוֹ, לְפִי שֶׁזָּכִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, וְאֵין חָבִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what case is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to a joining of** Shabbat **boundaries, but with regard to a joining of courtyards, one** may **establish an *eiruv*** for a person either **with his knowledge or without his knowledge.** The reason is **because one may act for a person's benefit in his absence, but one may not act to a person's disadvantage in his absence.** As a participant in a joining of courtyards benefits from his inclusion in the *eiruv*, his consent is not required. However, with regard to a joining of Shabbat boundaries, although it enables one to go farther in one direction, he loses the option of traveling in the opposite direction. When an action is to a person's disadvantage, or if it entails both benefits and disadvantages, one may act on that person's behalf only if he has been explicitly appointed his agent.

Rabbi Yehuda now articulates the position cited at the beginning of the daf. *Bameh devarim amurim be-eruvei techumin* — the requirement of full awareness and participation applies specifically to *eruv techumin*, because placing an *eruv techumin* in one direction extends the person's range in that direction while reducing it in the other. This is an inherent harm, and one cannot impose it without the person's knowledge and consent.

*Aval be-eruvei chatzerot me'arvin le-da'ato ve-shelo le-da'ato* — but by *eruvei chatzerot*, since participation is purely beneficial, the *eruv* may be made whether or not the person is aware. Applying this to the case of the baker: even if the person signaled his intent to purchase rather than to receive, says who cares what he signaled? He is being given a benefit regardless, and his prior conduct should not disqualify him. The *eruv* goes through because it is entirely to his benefit.

### Pure Benefit Principle

*Lefi she-zochin le-adam shelo be-fanav* — one may confer a benefit upon a person even outside of their presence and without their awareness. This is the animating principle of Rabbi Yehuda's leniency. When the action is an unqualified benefit, no consent is required, and the benefit takes effect even for someone who is absent and unaware.

### No Harm Without Knowledge

The converse principle is equally clear: *ve-ein chavin le-adam ela be-fanav* — one cannot impose a disadvantage upon someone without their presence and participation. This is precisely why *eruv techumin* requires full awareness: because making an *eruv techumin* in one direction necessarily takes away the person's ability to travel in the other direction on that Shabbat. It is therefore not a pure benefit, and Rabbi Yehuda concedes that the person's active participation is indispensable.

## Third-Party Kinyan

### Four Times of Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? הָא לֹא מָשַׁךְ! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבָהוּ: עֲשָׂאוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּאַרְבָּעָה פְּרָקִים בַּשָּׁנָה, דִּתְנַן: בְּאַרְבָּעָה פְּרָקִים אֵלּוּ מַשְׁחִיטִין אֶת הַטַּבָּח בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ. אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה אֶלֶף דִּינָר וְאֵין לַלּוֹקֵחַ אֶלָּא דִּינָר אֶחָד — כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִשְׁחוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **What is the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion that one who gave money to a grocer or a baker has acquired possession of the food for the *eiruv*? This ruling is difficult, as **he did not** perform a transaction by **pulling** the food into his possession, and one can acquire an object only by performing a valid act of acquisition. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Abbahu said: Rabbi Eliezer established** this acquisition so that it should be **like the four times during the year** that the payment of money effects acquisition, **as we learned** in a mishna: **On these four times** every year, on the eves of Passover, *Shavuot*, Rosh HaShana, and the Eighth Day of Assembly, one who paid for meat may **force the butcher to slaughter** an animal against his will. **Even** if his **ox** was **worth a thousand dinar, and the customer has** paid for **only one dinar's** worth of meat, the customer may **force** the butcher **to slaughter** it, so that the buyer can receive his meat.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer* — what is Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning for holding that money effects the *kinyan* for the baker scenario? After all, there was no *meshichah*. Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *asa'un Rabbi Eliezer k'arba'ah perakim ba-shanah* — Rabbi Eliezer applied the principle of the four times of the year, where money alone suffices to complete a transaction.

### Butcher Slaughter Principle

The four times of the year are described in a Mishnah in Chullin: *b'arba'ah perakim elu mashchitin es ha-tabbah al karcho* — at these four times one may compel the butcher to slaughter an animal even against his will. The case: *afilu shor shaveh elef dinar ve-ein lokeach ela dinar* — even if the ox is worth a thousand dinars and the buyer purchased only a dinar's worth of meat, since the buyer has acquired that portion and the only way to provide it is by slaughtering the ox, the butcher may be compelled to do so. Furthermore, *im meis meis la-lokeach* — if the animal dies in the interim, the loss falls on the buyer, because from the moment the money was paid the buyer already became the owner.

### Four Times Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אִם מֵת, מֵת לַלּוֹקֵחַ. מֵת לַלּוֹקֵחַ? הָא לֹא מָשַׁךְ! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּשֶׁמָּשַׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, if** the ox **died, it died at the buyer's** expense. That is to say, he must bear the loss and is not entitled to get his dinar back. The Gemara asks: Why is this so? The customer **did not pull** the ox into his possession. As he did not perform an act of acquisition, he has not acquired any part of the ox, and his dinar should therefore be restored to him. **Rav Huna said:** We are dealing here with a case **where he did pull** the ox into his possession.

What are these four times? Rashi lists them: *erev Yom Tov ha-rishon shel Pesach* — the eve of the first Yom Tov of Pesach, for the *Seder* and the *korban Pesach*; *erev Yom Tov shel Atzeres* and *erev Yom Tov shel Shavuos*; *erev Rosh Hashanah*; and *erev Yom Tov ha-achron shel chag* — the eve of Shemini Atzeres. Tosafot notes that the first day of Sukkos is not included because people are occupied with building the *sukkah* and acquiring *lulav* and *esrog*, and do not have as elaborate a meal. In the *Galil*, *erev Yom Kippur* is also included, since the obligation to eat on *erev Yom Kippur* was treated there as a genuine *seudas Yom Tov*.

The common thread is that these are times when everyone is eating a festive meal and will therefore want meat. The entire mechanism is designed to ensure that buyers who have committed their money are protected — the transaction is considered final immediately upon payment so that the butcher cannot back out.

### Zikha Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה אֵינוֹ כֵּן. לְפִיכָךְ אִם מֵת, מֵת לַמּוֹכֵר. אַמַּאי? הָא מָשַׁךְ! אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם בְּשֶׁלֹּא מָשַׁךְ, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן בְּשֶׁזִּיכָּה לוֹ עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, say the latter clause** of that mishna as follows: With regard to **the rest of the days of the year, it is not so. Therefore, if** the animal **died, it died at the seller's** expense. If, as Rav Huna claims, the mishna is referring to a case where the purchaser had already pulled the animal into his possession, **why** must the seller suffer the loss? Since the customer **pulled** it into his possession and has acquired it, the ox died in his possession. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said: Actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the customer **did not pull** the animal into his possession. **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a case **where** the butcher **conferred possession upon** his customer **by means of another** person, i.e., the butcher conferred possession upon the customer by instructing another person to acquire a dinar's worth of the ox's meat on his behalf, without having obtained his consent.

The Gemara challenges the mechanism. *V'halo mashach* — he didn't do *meshichah*! If the buyer took physical possession — *be-shemaschach*, Rav Huna says — then why is any of this remarkable? And if he did take possession, then the rule should apply year-round, not only at these four times. The *seifa* of the Mishnah states: *bi-she'ar yemot ha-shanah eino kein* — the rest of the year this does not apply, and *im meis meis la-mocher* — if the animal dies, the seller bears the loss because the transaction was not finalized. So it is clear that the four-times rule is about something different from actual *meshichah*.

Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak resolves this: *le'olam bi-shelo mashach* — indeed, the buyer did not do *meshichah*. *Bakha be-mai askinan bi-she-zikhah lo al yedei akher* — the mechanism is *zikha al yedei akher*: a third party takes possession on the buyer's behalf. The butcher or his employee physically acquires one dinar's worth of meat within the animal on the buyer's behalf.

### Benefit Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאַרְבָּעָה פְּרָקִים אֵלּוּ דִּזְכוּת הוּא לוֹ — זָכִין לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, דְּחוֹב הוּא לוֹ — אֵין חָבִין לוֹ אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, **at these four times, when it is for his benefit,** as everyone wishes to buy meat on these days, **one may act for his benefit in his absence,** and the acquisition is valid. With regard to **the rest of the days of the year, when it is to his disadvantage,** as it obligates him in payment and he might have no interest in this purchase, **one may act to his disadvantage only in his presence.**

But if a third party can do this, why does it work only at the four times of the year and not year-round? The answer hinges on the principle established earlier: a third party can acquire something on your behalf only when doing so is an unequivocal benefit to you — *zkhut hu lo*. At these four times of the year, when everyone is committed to eating a festive meal, it is clearly to the buyer's advantage to have the transaction finalized immediately, locking in the butcher and preventing him from reneging. The risk that the animal might die is outweighed by the certainty of having one's purchase secured. *Zakhin lo shelo be-fanav* — a third party can therefore acquire on the buyer's behalf without his presence.

The rest of the year, however, the calculus is different. The buyer might prefer flexibility — perhaps he will decide not to have meat after all, or will find a better price elsewhere. Having the transaction finalized without *meshichah* could disadvantage him. *De-chovah hu lo* — it may actually be a burden rather than a benefit, and therefore *ein chavin lo ela be-fanav* — it can only be done with his participation.

## Biblical Kinyan & Ra (II)

### Biblical Kinyan Invoked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אִילָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּאַרְבָּעָה פְּרָקִים אֵלּוּ הֶעֱמִידוּ חֲכָמִים דִּבְרֵיהֶן עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ila said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: At these four times, the Sages based their statement on Torah law,** i.e., they ruled in accordance with Torah law. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said: By Torah law,** the payment of **money** is an effective act of acquisition, which **acquires** movable property.

Rav Ila said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *be-arba'ah perakim elu he'emidu chakhamim divreihem al divrei Torah* — at these four times of the year, the rabbis relied on the biblical rule rather than their own ordinance. *De-var Torah ma'ot konot* — biblically, money effects the transaction. The rabbis ordinarily override this with the requirement of *meshichah*, but at these four times, given the strong interest in protecting buyers' ability to obtain festive meat, the rabbis step back and allow the biblical law to operate: money alone finalizes the sale.

### Sellers' Liability Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה — גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר לוֹ: נִשְׂרְפוּ חִיטֶּיךָ בָּעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **And why,** then, **did** the Sages **say** that the mode of acquisition of **pulling,** not monetary payment, **acquires** movable goods? It is **a decree** issued by the Sages, **lest** the seller **say to** a buyer who has already paid for his merchandise: **Your wheat was burned in the upper story** of my house, and you have lost everything.

## R. Eliezer's Analogy

### Eruv Analogy

Rabbi Eliezer applies the same reasoning to the *eruv*: just as the rabbis permitted the biblical money-*kinyan* to operate at the four times of the year in order to facilitate festive meat purchases, so too Rabbi Eliezer says one may rely on the biblical money-*kinyan* when making an *eruv*. The interest in ensuring that people can carry in their courtyard on Shabbat is analogous to the interest in ensuring people have meat for Yom Tov.

### Parallel Interest

The parallel is precise: in both cases, there is a pressing practical interest in having the transaction finalized as quickly and easily as possible, and in both cases Rabbi Eliezer holds that this interest justifies invoking the biblical rule that money effects a *kinyan* without the need for *meshichah* or *hagbahah*.

---

## Biblical Kinyan & Ra (II)

The reason is that on these four occasions everyone buys meat, and therefore the butcher who promised to supply the customer with meat must give it to him, even if this causes the butcher a considerable loss.

Tosafot raises an obvious objection: the same problem arises in reverse. If the buyer has paid money but the transaction is not yet final, the seller might lose the money before returning it, and the money belongs to the buyer in the same way the goods would. Why is money safer in the seller's hands than goods? Tosafot answers: the laws of *shomrim* — the custodial obligations — impose a higher standard of care on someone holding money than on someone holding ordinary goods. The practical result is that leaving money with a seller entails a higher level of legal protection for the buyer than leaving goods with a seller. By replacing money-*kinyan* with *meshichah*, the rabbis effectively ensured that the person holding the item always has a personal stake in its preservation, and the buyer's interests are protected at a higher standard.

## Baker vs. Private Person

### Money + Baker Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מָעָה, אֲבָל כְּלִי — קוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** also **said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where he gave him **a *ma'a*, but** if he gave him **a utensil, he acquires** the food for the *eiruv* by the mode of acquisition known as exchange.

Shmuel rules: *lo shanu ela ma'ah* — the rule that money does not work applies specifically when the person gave money. *Aval kli koneh* — but if instead of money one gives an object to the baker, that does work.

### Object = Chalifin

The reason is that giving an object to effect a transaction constitutes *kinyan chalifin* — the acquisition through exchange — rather than *kinyan kesef*. Unlike monetary payment, *kinyan chalifin* is not subject to the same rabbinic displacement, and therefore an object handed to the baker functions as a valid *kinyan* even in the commercial context.

### Zechei vs. Arev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לוֹ ״זְכֵה לִי״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״עָרֵב לִי״, שָׁלִיחַ שַׁוְּיֵהּ, וְקָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** further **said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where he said to** the grocer or baker: **Confer possession upon me; but if he said** to him: **Establish an *eiruv* on my behalf,** he clearly intended to **appoint him** his **agent** to establish an *eiruv* on his behalf, **and** therefore the *eiruv* **is acquired** by means of his agency.

Shmuel adds a further distinction: *lo shanu ela de-amar lo zechei li* — the rule that money fails with a baker applies only when the person said to the baker "acquire this on my behalf" — that is, when the person is trying to effect a proprietary transaction through the baker. But *aval amar lo arev li* — if instead the person said "make an *eruv* for me," this phrasing transforms the baker into a *shaliach*, not a vendor. By saying "make an *eruv* for me" the person is appointing an agent, not attempting a commercial purchase.

### Private Person Shaliach

*Shaliach shavya ve-kanei* — he has made the baker his agent, and the agent's subsequent physical acquisition of the bread constitutes a valid *kinyan* on his behalf. When one gives money to a private individual with the instruction "make an *eruv* for me," the individual acts as a *shaliach* throughout, and the money is merely compensation for the service rather than a purchase price — the *kinyan* is effected through the agent's physical possession of the bread.

## Halacha like R. Yehuda

### Universal Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּעֵירוּבִין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what** case **is this statement,** that one may establish an *eiruv* only with a person's knowledge, **said?** This *halakha* applies to a joining of Shabbat boundaries, but not a joining of courtyards. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** in this regard, **and not only that, but any place where Rabbi Yehuda taught** a *halakha* **with regard to *eiruvin*,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that by *eruvei chatzerot* one need not have the participants' awareness since it is purely beneficial. *Ve-lo od* — and not only that: *kol makom she-shanah Rabbi Yehuda be-Eruvin halacha ke-moso* — wherever Rabbi Yehuda rules on any matter within *Eruvin*, the halacha follows him.

### Eruv vs. Mechitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה לְרַב יְהוּדָה: אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל אֲפִילּוּ בְּמָבוֹי שֶׁנִּיטְּלוּ קוֹרוֹתָיו אוֹ לְחָיָיו? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּעֵירוּבִין אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא בִּמְחִיצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥana from Baghdad said to Rav Yehuda:** Did **Shmuel state** this ruling **even with regard to an alleyway whose** cross **beam or side post was removed** during Shabbat? Rabbi Yehuda maintains that it is permitted to carry in this alleyway on that same Shabbat. **He said to him: I spoke to you with regard to** the acquisition of ***eiruvin*, and not with regard to partitions.**

Rav Chana bar Katina challenges this broad statement: does Shmuel's ruling apply even to the case discussed later, where a *mavoi*'s beams or posts were removed on Shabbat — where Rabbi Yehuda rules leniently? Rav Yehuda responds: *be-Eruvin amarti lakh ve-lo be-mechitza* — I said we follow Rabbi Yehuda regarding the laws of *Eruvin* themselves, not regarding the laws of *mechitza* — the construction and maintenance of partitions. The question of whether a *mavoi*'s walls remain valid after a structural change belongs to a different halakhic category from the rules of joining courtyards and establishing communal food-sharing. This distinction has been noted before in the masechta.

### Disagreement or Explanation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: ״הֲלָכָה״, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״אֵימָתַי״ וּ״בַמֶּה״ בְּמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְפָרֵשׁ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** As it is stated that the ***halakha*** is in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda, this proves **by inference that there is a dispute** concerning this issue. **But didn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say: Any place where Rabbi Yehuda says when, or in what** case is this, **in the Mishna,** he intends **only to explain the** earlier **statement of the Rabbis,** not to disagree with them.

Rav Acha the son of Rava then raises a further question to Rav Ashi: if the halacha follows Rabbi Yehuda, it implies *mi-klal de-pligi* — that there are those who disagree with him. But who actually disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda's position that *eruvei chatzerot* may be made without the residents' awareness? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: *kol makom she-amar Rabbi Yehuda eimasi u-vameh be-mishnasennu eino ela le-faresh divrei Chachamim* — whenever Rabbi Yehuda uses the phrases "when does this apply?" or "in what circumstances?" in a Mishnah, he is not disagreeing with the Sages but merely elaborating and qualifying their ruling in an expository fashion. Under this reading, Rabbi Yehuda's "when does this apply? By *eruv techumin*" is not a dissenting opinion but an explanation of the Sages' own position, and therefore no one actually disputes him.

### New Residents Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי? וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: נִתּוֹסְפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן — מוֹסִיף וּמְזַכֶּה, וְצָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ!
>
> **Translation:** Before addressing this question, the Gemara expresses surprise over the claim itself: **And** do Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages **not dispute** this issue? **Didn't we learn** in an earlier mishna: If new residents **were added to** the original residents of the alleyway, **he may add** to the *eiruv* for those residents **and confer possession** on them, **and he must inform** the new residents of their inclusion in the merging of alleyways. Apparently, this *tanna* maintains that one must inform them even with regard to a joining of courtyards. This ruling contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, which proves that there is at least one Sage who does not accept his opinion.

The Gemara asks: *ve-lo pligi*? Can one really say they do not disagree? But we just learned in the Mishnah at the beginning of today's daf: *nitosfu aleihem mosif u-mezakeh ve-tzarich le-hodia* — when new residents move in, one adds food and must inform them. If no one disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda, why does our Mishnah require notification for what is an unambiguous benefit?

### Resolution: Two-Alleyway Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם בְּחָצֵר שֶׁבֵּין שְׁנֵי מְבוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** the mishna is referring **to a courtyard** situated **between two alleyways,** in which case the residents of the courtyard may join a merging with whichever alleyway they prefer.

The Gemara's response: *hasam be-chatzer she-bein shenei mevo'ot* — that case involves a courtyard situated between two alleyways, where the courtyard has a choice of which *mavoi* to affiliate with. Since joining alleyway A may not be an unequivocal benefit if the courtyard might have preferred alleyway B, it is not a pure *zkhut*, and therefore even Rabbi Yehuda would agree that notification is required there. Where the courtyard has only one alleyway and participation is an unambiguous benefit, nobody requires notification.

But this resolution is challenged immediately: we just began today's daf with Rav Shizbi saying in the name of Rav Chisda that *chalukin alav chaverav al Rabbi Yehuda* — the Sages do in fact disagree with Rabbi Yehuda! That itself implies that our Mishnah is a counter-position to Rabbi Yehuda and not merely a description of the two-alleyway case, which Rabbi Yehuda himself would concede.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית?! מָר סָבַר פְּלִיגִי, וּמָר סָבַר לָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Are you raising a contradiction between** the opinion of **one person and** that of **another?** In other words, a difficulty cannot be raised from the statement of one *amora* against those of another. **One Sage,** Shmuel, **maintains** that Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages **disagree, and one** other **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **maintains** that they **do not disagree.**

The Gemara closes the discussion with characteristic equanimity: *gavra a'gavra karmis* — one is simply presenting one *amora* in tension with another. *Mar savar pligi u-mar savar lo pligi* — some authorities hold that Rabbi Yehuda's "when does this apply" represents a genuine disagreement with the Sages, and others hold it is merely explanatory and that everyone agrees. According to both positions, however, the practical halacha is the same: we follow Rabbi Yehuda. Either there is no dispute and his position is universal, or there is a dispute and we rule like him. In either case, when it comes to *eruv chatzerot*, where the participation is a pure benefit to all involved, one may make the *eruv* on behalf of others even without their explicit awareness — and especially so when using one's own food rather than theirs. That is the settled halacha with which we conclude.
