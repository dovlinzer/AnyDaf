# Rosh Hashanah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bal Te'acher & Heir

### Heir & Bal Te'acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: יוֹרֵשׁ, מַהוּ בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״? ״כִּי תִדּוֹר נֶדֶר״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא לָא נְדַר, אוֹ דִלְמָא: ״וּבָאתָ שָּׁמָּה וַהֲבֵאתֶם שָׁמָּה״, וְהָא מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira asks:** In the case of **an heir, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to** the prohibition of: **You shall not delay?** That is to say, does an heir transgress the prohibition against delaying a vow taken by his father? The Gemara explains the two sides of the question: Is it that **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"When you shall vow a vow** to the Lord your God, you shall not delay paying it" (Deuteronomy 23:22), **and this** one, the heir, **did not make a vow? Or perhaps** the essence of the mitzva is as it says: **"And there you shall come; and there you shall bring** your burnt-offerings and your sacrifices" (Deuteronomy 12:5–6), **and this** one, the heir, **is obligated** to come and bring his father's offerings.

The *daf* opens by continuing a discussion of *bal t'acher* — the prohibition against delaying the fulfillment of sacrificial vows or obligations. The Gemara has already established that *bal t'acher* is not limited strictly to vows: one who is obligated to bring a *chatas* or an *asham* is equally bound by this prohibition even without having taken a formal vow. With that background in place, Rabbi Zeira poses a pointed question: *yoresh mahu b'bal t'acher* — does an heir who inherits a consecrated animal transgress *bal t'acher* if he delays bringing it?

The case requires careful analysis. The heir did not take the original vow, and the obligation does not arise from any personal act on his part. It stems entirely from the fact that he has inherited a sanctified animal. This distinguishes him both from the classic case of a person who makes a vow and from the case of one obligated in a *chatas* or *asham*, where the obligation originates directly with the individual. On the other hand, there is a general verse — *u'vashta shama v'heveisa shama* — enjoining one to bring one's *korbanot* to the *Beis Hamikdash*, and perhaps that general obligation is sufficient to link the heir to *bal t'acher* as well.

### Textual Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא — ״מֵעִמָּךְ״, פְּרָט לְיוֹרֵשׁ. וְהַאי ״מֵעִמָּךְ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, זֶה לֶקֶט שִׁכְחָה וּפֵאָה! קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״עִמָּךְ״, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ ״מֵעִמָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an answer to this, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** a *baraita* that says: The verse states: "For the Lord your God will surely require it **from you"** (Deuteronomy 23:22), which is interpreted to mean: **To the exclusion of an heir.** This teaches that an heir does not transgress the prohibition against delaying a vow taken by his father. The Gemara asks: **But this** phrase **"from you" is necessary** to teach a different *halakha*, namely, that one transgresses the prohibition against delaying even for **gleanings, forgotten sheaves, and the** produce of the **corner** of his field. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ḥiyya derived two *halakhot* from this word. **He read into** the verse: **"You [*imakh*],"** which he expounded as coming to include gleanings, forgotten sheaves and the produce of the corner of the field in the prohibition, **and he read into** the verse: **"From you [*me'imakh*],"** with the extra letter *mem* coming to exclude an heir.

The Gemara resolves the question by citing a *braisa* taught by Rabbi Chiya. The Torah uses the phrase *me'imach* — "from you" — and the Gemara derives from this: *prat l'yoresh*, you are held liable, not your heir. The *yoresh* is therefore exempt from *bal t'acher*.

The Gemara immediately asks: is not this same word *me'imach* already needed to derive the inclusion of *leket*, *shichcha*, and *peah* within the category of *bal t'acher* — a derivation noted earlier as among the more surprising extensions of this prohibition? The Gemara resolves this elegantly: *kari be me'imach v'kari be imach* — the word *me'imach* yields two teachings simultaneously. The base word *imach* — "with you" — establishes the obligation regarding the poor person's gifts, while the prepositional *mem* of *me'imach* adds the limiting teaching: "you," not your heir.

The upshot is significant: although *bal t'acher* does not require the person to have made the vow himself — as proven by its application to *chatas* and *asham* — it does require that the obligation originate with the person. An heir who inherits a sanctified animal may still be obligated by the positive commandment of *u'vashta shama v'heveisa shama* to bring it, but he does not transgress *bal t'acher*.

## Women & Bal Te'acher

### Women's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִשָּׁה, מַה הִיא בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הָא לָא מִיחַיְּיבָא בִּרְאִיָּה, אוֹ דִלְמָא הָא אִיתַהּ בְּשִׂמְחָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira asks** another question on this topic: In the case of **a woman** who made a vow, **what is** the *halakha* **with regard to** the prohibition: **You shall not delay?** The Gemara explains: **Do we say** that since **she is not obligated to appear** in the Temple on the pilgrimage Festivals, as this obligation is a positive, time-bound mitzva, from which women are exempt, she also does not transgress if she is late in bringing her offering? **Or perhaps,** since **she is** obligated **in** the mitzva of **rejoicing** on a Festival, she should also be obligated in some of the other mitzvot connected to the day?

Rabbi Zeira's second question follows directly: *isha mah hi b'bal t'acher* — is a woman subject to *bal t'acher*? At first glance this seems puzzling, since a woman who takes a vow is certainly obligated to fulfill it. The difficulty, however, lies in the relationship between *bal t'acher* and the *regalim*. The entire Gemara has been developing the connection between *bal t'acher* and the pilgrimage festivals, treating the failure to bring a *korban* during a *regel* as the operative moment of delay. Since women are not obligated in *aliyah l'regel* — they may remain home while their husbands make the pilgrimage — a woman never squanders a concrete opportunity to bring her *korban* in the manner that triggers *bal t'acher*. She was never in Yerushalayim, never passed through the *Beis Hamikdash*, and therefore, one might argue, never incurred the specific *ikhur* — the delay — that *bal t'acher* targets.

The counter-possibility raised in the Gemara is that women are included in the *mitzvah* of *simcha* — the rejoicing of the festival — based on the verse *vesamachta ata u'veisecha*, "you and your household shall rejoice." If so, a woman has an obligation to be in Yerushalayim for the *regalim*, and the logic of squandered opportunity would apply to her just as it does to men.

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָא אִיתַהּ בְּשִׂמְחָה. וּמִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִשָּׁה — בַּעֲלָהּ מְשַׂמְּחָהּ! לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Derive this from** the fact that **she is** also obligated **in** the mitzva of **rejoicing** on a Festival. The Gemara asks: **And did Abaye** actually **say this,** that a woman is obligated to rejoice on a Festival? **But didn't Abaye say:** As for **a woman, her husband must make her joyful** on a Festival? This means that the obligation falls not on the woman, but upon her husband. The Gemara answers: Abaye **stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Zeira.** Rabbi Zeira holds that women are bound by a positive mitzva to rejoice on a Festival. Since, according to Rabbi Zeira's opinion, they are obligated in the mitzva of rejoicing on a Festival, they are also subject to the prohibition: You shall not delay.

Abaye responds to Rabbi Zeira by affirming the force of the *simcha* argument: since a woman is obligated in *simcha*, she has reason to be in Yerushalayim, and therefore *bal t'acher* applies to her. The Gemara, however, immediately complicates Abaye's position by citing another statement in his name: *isha ba'alah mesamchah* — "a woman is made joyful by her husband." This statement seems to undercut the claim that a woman's independent presence in Yerushalayim is required, and the Gemara asks whether Abaye is being consistent.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

The tension between these two statements of Abaye gives rise to an important dispute between Rashi and Tosafot regarding the precise meaning of *isha ba'alah mesamchah*.

Rashi understands this phrase to mean that a woman fulfills the obligation of *simcha* through her husband's provision — specifically, through receiving nice clothing and similar gifts — and she has no independent requirement to partake of the *shalmei simcha* at all. On this reading, a woman need not be in Yerushalayim at all; the husband's departure for the *regel* while providing for his wife at home is sufficient. Accordingly, a woman would never have the concrete opportunity to bring her *korban* to the *Beis Hamikdash* that is the basis of *bal t'acher*.

Tosafot, in the name of Rabbeinu Tam, reads the statement differently. The obligation of *simcha* does fall upon the husband rather than the wife, but this means that the husband must bring the *shalmei simcha* — it is his obligation to do so. The wife, however, would still be present in Yerushalayim to eat from those *korbanot*, even if the act of bringing them is not hers. The distinction is between the obligation to bring and the opportunity to partake. On Tosafot's reading, the question of *bal t'acher* for women becomes more nuanced: even if she eats in Yerushalayim, it is not her own obligation to appear before the *Beis Hamikdash* in the manner that creates a squandered opportunity.

The Gemara then notes — in response to an implicit question — that according to Rabbi Zeira himself, who raised the question, the relevant framing must be his own view that women are included in *simcha* through the *korbanot*; otherwise, on the view that women have no connection to these sacrifices at all, there would be no basis for the question in the first place. The question of whether *bal t'acher* applies to women thus ultimately hinges on deeper disagreements about what defines a "squandered opportunity": whether it requires an obligation to be present in the *Beis Hamikdash*, an obligation to be in Yerushalayim, or whether *bal t'acher* operates independently of the *regalim* altogether as a function of calendar time.

## Bechor & Year

### When Year Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּכוֹר, מֵאֵימָתַי מוֹנִין לוֹ שָׁנָה? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלַד, רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנִּרְאָה לְהַרְצָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In the case of **a firstborn** animal, **from when does one** begin to **count a year** with regard to the prohibition against delaying? **Abaye said:** One counts **from the time it is born. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** One counts **from the time it is fit for appeasement,** i.e., from its eighth day, when it is fit to be brought as an offering, as explicitly stated in the Torah (see Leviticus 22:27).

The Gemara turns from *bal t'acher* to a related question about time: *bechor me'eimatai monim lo shana* — from when does one begin counting the year for a *bechor*? The Torah requires that a firstborn animal be eaten *shana b'shana*, year by year. The question is what event starts the clock.

Abaye holds that the year begins *me-sha'ah she-nolad* — from the moment of birth. Rav Acha bar Yaakov holds it begins *me-sha'ah she-ra'uy l'hartza'ah* — from the moment the animal becomes fit to be brought as a *korban*, which is eight days after birth.

### Tam vs. Ba'al Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא בְּתָם, הָא בְּבַעַל מוּם. בַּעַל מוּם מִי מָצֵי אָכֵיל לֵיהּ? דְּקִים לֵיהּ בֵּיהּ שֶׁכָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The two Sages **do not disagree. This** one, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, who said that one counts from the time that the animal is fit for appeasement, is speaking **about an unblemished** firstborn, which must be brought to the altar for sacrifice. **That** one, Abaye, who said that one counts from the time that the animal is born, is speaking **about a blemished** animal. Since it was already fit to be slaughtered from the day of its birth, if one does not give it to the priest within a year of that day he transgresses the prohibition against delaying. The Gemara asks: **Can an animal with a blemish** really **be eaten** immediately on the day that it is born? Isn't there a requirement to wait eight days before slaughtering it, in order to ascertain that the animal is viable and was not born prematurely? The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **where one is certain** of when the animal was conceived and **that its months** of gestation **have been completed,** so that it may be presumed to be viable.

The Gemara resolves the apparent dispute by distinguishing between two cases: *ha b'tam, ha b'ba'al mum* — the two opinions are not actually in conflict but address different scenarios. For an unblemished animal (*tam*), which is fit to be brought as a *korban*, the year does not begin until it is eligible to be offered, namely eight days after birth. For a blemished animal (*ba'al mum*), which will never be brought as a *korban* but will instead be slaughtered and eaten by the *kohen* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the year begins immediately upon birth, since there is no reason to wait.

A question arises: can a *ba'al mum* really be eaten on the very day it is born? Rashi notes that a newborn animal might be a *nefel* — a non-viable animal — and therefore unfit for consumption. The Gemara answers that there are cases where it is evident at birth that the animal completed a full gestation (*de-kim lei b'de-ika de-kalu lo chodashav*), and in such cases one may indeed begin eating it immediately and the year commences from that day.

## Four New Years Braitta

### Nissan & Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לָחֳדָשִׁים, וּלְעִיבּוּרִין, וְלִתְרוּמַת שְׁקָלִים. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף לִשְׂכִירוּת בָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that expands upon what was taught in the mishna: **On the first of Nisan is the New Year for** counting **the months** of the year, **and for leap years, and for collection of** the **shekels** that had been collected in Adar and used to purchase animals for communal offerings and other needs of the Temple; **and some say** that it is **also** the New Year **for the renting of houses.**

Having concluded the immediate *bal t'acher* discussion and the question of the *bechor*'s year, the Gemara introduces a *braisa* — *tanu rabbanan* — that enumerates additional *roshei shanah* not included in the *Mishnah*. The *braisa* states: *b'echad b'Nissan, Rosh Hashanah l'chodashim* — the first of Nisan is the new year for months. Nisan is month one, Iyar is month two, and so on throughout the year.

The *braisa* also lists: *l'iburim* — for leap years; *l'trumat shekalim* — for the collection of new *shekel* funds for the communal sacrifices; and, according to some, even for house rental contracts. Each of these will be discussed in turn.

### Aviv Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָחֳדָשִׁים מְנָלַן — דִּכְתִיב: ״הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם רֹאשׁ חֳדָשִׁים רִאשׁוֹן הוּא לָכֶם לְחׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה. דַּבְּרוּ אֶל כׇּל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה וְיִקְחוּ לָהֶם אִישׁ שֶׂה לְבֵית אָבוֹת שֶׂה לַבָּיִת. וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה וְשָׁחֲטוּ אוֹתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. וּכְתִיב: ״שָׁמוֹר אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָאָבִיב״, אֵיזֶהוּ חֹדֶשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אָבִיב, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר — זֶה נִיסָן, וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that the first of Nisan is the New Year **for months? As it is written: "This month shall be to you the beginning of months; it shall be the first month of the year to you. Speak to all the congregation of Israel, saying: On the tenth day of this month they shall take to them every man a lamb, according to the house of their fathers, a lamb for a household…And you shall keep it until the fourteenth day of the same month:** And the whole assembly of the congregation **shall slaughter it** toward evening" (Exodus 12:2–6). **And** elsewhere **it is written: "Observe the month of ripening** and keep Passover to the Lord your God; for in the month of ripening the Lord your God brought you forth out of Egypt by night" (Deuteronomy 16:1). **Which is the month in which there is a ripening** of grain? **You must say** that **this is Nisan, and it is called the first** month of the year.

The Gemara first asks: *l'chodashim mina lan* — how do we know Nisan is the first month? The answer comes from the verse *hachodesh hazeh lachem rosh chodashim* — "this month shall be for you the first of months," from the passage in which the Torah designates the month of the Pesach offering as month one.

But this is somewhat circular: how do we know which month the Pesach offering was brought? The verse tells us it was brought in month one — but which month is that? The answer is provided by a second verse: *shamor et chodesh ha'aviv v'asita pesach* — observe the month of the *aviv*, the spring ripening of the grain, and bring the Pesach. We know from agricultural reality that this ripening occurs in Nisan.

The Gemara sharpens the derivation. *Eizehu chodesh she-yesh bo aviv? Hevei omer zeh Nisan* — which month has the ripening of the grain? That is Nisan. And this month is called *rishon*, first.

The Gemara anticipates objections: perhaps Iyar should be considered the month of *aviv*, since some grain ripening extends into it? The answer: *be'inan aviv v'leika* — the full sense of *aviv* is not present in Iyar. Perhaps Adar, since some early ripening occurs there? The answer: *be'inan rov aviv v'leika* — Adar lacks the *majority* of the ripening. The Gemara then asks: but does the verse actually say "the majority" of the ripening? This challenge stands.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא אִיָּיר? בָּעֵינָא אָבִיב, וְלֵיכָּא. וְאֵימָא אֲדָר? בָּעֵינָא רוֹב אָבִיב, וְלֵיכָּא. מִידֵּי ״רוֹב אָבִיב״ כְּתִיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one could **say** that it is the month of **Iyyar.** The Gemara answers: A month of **ripening is required, and** in Iyyar **there is no** ripening, as the grain has already ripened. The Gemara asks further: **But** one could **say** that it is the month of **Adar,** the month in which some of the grain begins to ripen. The Gemara answers: The month in which **most of the ripening** takes place **is required, and this is not** the case in Adar, as most of the grain ripens during the next month, the month of Nisan. The Gemara asks: But **is most of the ripening written** in the verse? The verse merely speaks of the month of ripening, and since there is some ripening in Adar, it is possible that this is the first month.

### Tishrei & Harvest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, מֵהָכָא: ״אַךְ בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי בְּאׇסְפְּכֶם אֶת תְּבוּאַת הָאָרֶץ״, אֵיזֶהוּ חֹדֶשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אֲסִיפָה, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר — זֶה תִּשְׁרִי, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״שְׁבִיעִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ḥisda said:** It is derived that Nisan is the first of the months **from here.** The verse states: **"Also on the fifteenth day of the seventh month, when you have gathered in the fruit of the land"** (Leviticus 23:39). **Which is the month in which there is a gathering** of the grain of the field before the rains begin? **You must say** that **this is Tishrei, and it is called the seventh** month. Therefore, Nisan is the first month of the year.

Rav Chisda therefore offers a different prooftext: *ach b'chamishah asar yom la-chodesh ha-shevi'i b'aspe'chem et tvuat ha'aretz* — "but on the fifteenth day of the seventh month, when you gather in the produce of the land," referring to Sukkot. *Eizehu chodesh she-yesh bo asifah? Hevei omer zeh Tishrei v'kari lei shevi'i* — which month is characterized by the ingathering? Tishrei. And it is called the seventh month. Since Tishrei is the seventh, Nisan is the first.

But again the Gemara objects: perhaps the seventh month refers to the seventh counting from Iyar, making the ingathering month Marcheshvan? The answer: *be'inan asif v'leika* — Marcheshvan lacks the full ingathering. And Elul? Perhaps the ingathering begins there, counting from Adar? Again: *be'inan rov asif v'leika* — the majority of the ingathering is not in Elul. And again the Gemara presses: does the verse actually say "majority"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא מְרַחְשְׁוָן, וּמַאי ״שְׁבִיעִי״ — שְׁבִיעִי לְאִיָּיר? בָּעֵינָא אָסִיף, וְלֵיכָּא. וְאֵימָא אֱלוּל, וּמַאי ״שְׁבִיעִי״ — שְׁבִיעִי לַאֲדָר? בָּעֵינָא רוֹב אָסִיף, וְלֵיכָּא. מִידֵּי ״רוֹב אָסִיף״ כְּתִיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one could **say** that verse is referring to the month of **Marḥeshvan, and what is** meant by **seventh?** It is the **seventh** month **from** the month of **Iyyar.** The Gemara rejects this possibility: A month of **gathering is required, and** in Marḥeshvan **there is no** gathering, as the crops have already been gathered in, and the fields have already begun to be plowed to prepare them for the next year's planting. The Gemara asks further: **But** one could **say** that it is the month of **Elul,** the month in which the gathering of the grain from the fields begins, **and what is** meant by **seventh?** It is the **seventh** month **from** the month of **Adar.** The Gemara answers: The month in which **most of the gathering** takes place **is required, and this is not** the case in Elul, as most of the gathering is done in Tishrei. The Gemara asks: But **is most of the gathering written** in the verse?

### Kabbalah & Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא, דָּבָר זֶה מִתּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ, מִדִּבְרֵי קַבָּלָה לָמַדְנוּ: ״בְּיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה לְעַשְׁתֵּי עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ הוּא חֹדֶשׁ שְׁבָט״. רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וַתִּלָּקַח אֶסְתֵּר אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ אֶל בֵּית מַלְכוּתוֹ בַּחֹדֶשׁ הָעֲשִׂירִי הוּא חֹדֶשׁ טֵבֵת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ravina said: This matter we did not learn from the Torah of Moses, our teacher;** rather, **we learned it from the texts of the tradition,** i.e., the Prophets and Writings: **"On the twenty-fourth day of the eleventh month, which is the month of Shevat"** (Zechariah 1:7). If Shevat is the eleventh month, Nisan must be the first month. **Rabba bar Ulla said:** It is derived **from here,** as it is stated: **"So Esther was taken to the king Ahasuerus into his royal house in the tenth month, which is the month of Tevet"** (Esther 2:16).

Ravina then concedes the point: *devar zeh mi-torat Moshe Rabbeinu lo lamadnu* — we cannot prove the identity of Nisan with certainty from the Torah alone. The agricultural anchors are suggestive but not definitive; there is always the possibility of a one-month shift. Rather, *mi-divrei kabbalah lamadnu* — we learn it from the books of *kabbalah*, meaning the prophetic writings: the *Nevi'im* and *Ketuvim*.

The critical evidence comes from post-exilic texts, in which numbered months are explicitly identified with named Babylonian months. Because this identification occurred after the return from Babylonian exile, these texts uniquely correlate the traditional numbering with specific named months, thus providing a fixed point of reference.

The Gemara adduces several such verses: from Zechariah, *beyom esrim v'arba'ah la-ashtei asar chodesh hu chodesh Shevat* — the twenty-fourth day of the eleventh month, which is Shevat. Since Shevat is eleven, Nisan is one. From Esther: *ba-chodesh ha-asiri hu chodesh Tevet* — the tenth month is Tevet, confirming Nisan as one. From Nehemiah: *b'arba'ah asar la-chodesh ha-tshi'i b'Chislev* — the ninth month is Kislev. From Esther again: *ba-chodesh ha-shelishi hu chodesh Sivan* — the third month is Sivan. And: *shneim asar hu chodesh Adar* — Adar is the twelfth, so Nisan is the first. Finally, the most explicit verse: *ba-chodesh ha-rishon hu chodesh Nisan* — "in the first month, which is Nisan."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״בְּאַרְבָּעָה לַחֹדֶשׁ הַתְּשִׁיעִי בְּכִסְלֵו״. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וַיִּקָּרְאוּ סוֹפְרֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ בָּעֵת הַהִיא בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁלִישִׁי הוּא חֹדֶשׁ סִיוָן״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״הִפִּיל פּוּר הוּא הַגּוֹרָל לִפְנֵי הָמָן מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם וּמֵחֹדֶשׁ לְחֹדֶשׁ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר הוּא חֹדֶשׁ אֲדָר״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, מֵהָכָא: ״בַּחֹדֶשׁ הָרִאשׁוֹן הוּא חֹדֶשׁ נִיסָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** It is derived **from here: "On the fourth day of the ninth month, in Kislev"** (Zechariah 7:1). **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** It is derived **from here,** as it is stated: **"And the scribes of the king were called at that time in the third month, that is the month of Sivan"** (Esther 8:9). **Rav Ashi said:** It is derived **from here: "He cast *pur*, that is, the lot, before Haman from day to day, and from month to month, to the twelfth month, that is, the month of Adar"** (Esther 3:7). **And if you wish, say** that it is derived **from here: "In the first month, that is, the month of Nisan"** (Esther 3:7).

The Gemara asks why all the other *amora'im* did not simply quote that last verse. The answer is that *dilma rishon rishon l'milta* — "first" there might mean the first month in which this particular story began, not necessarily that Nisan is first in the monthly order. Hence the other verses, each linking a numbered month to a named month, are necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלְּהוּ, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמְרִי מֵהַאי? דִּלְמָא מַאי ״רִאשׁוֹן״ — רִאשׁוֹן לְמִילְּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And all of** the others, **what is the reason** that **they did not say** that it is derived **from here,** the last verse mentioned, which is explicitly referring to Nisan as the first month? The Gemara answers: It is because one could **perhaps** have said: **What is** meant here by **first?** It means **the first** in relation **to its matter,** i.e., the months of the decree, and so it cannot be proven from here that Nisan is the first of the months of the year.

### Month Nomenclature

Tosafot raises a meta-level question: even granting all of these verses, how do we know which month is "Nisan"? The derivation from the prophets assumes we already know the order and identity of the months. The answer is that we do have an independent tradition (*kabbalah*) establishing the sequence of the months — the prooftexts serve to correlate the numbered months of the Torah with the named months already known by tradition.

The Yerushalmi states the historical situation explicitly: *shemot ha-chodashim alu b'yadam mi-Bavel* — the names of the months came up with the Jewish people from Babylonia. Before the exile, the Torah uses numbered months, and the earlier books of *Tanakh* employ descriptive agricultural names — *yerach ha-eitanim* for Tishrei (the month of the strong ones, when the forefathers were born), *yerach Bul* (the month of falling leaves and the feeding of stored produce to animals), and *yerach Ziv* (the month of the blossoming of trees). Tosafot's point is that there was a three-stage transition: first, numbered months only; then, descriptive agricultural designations beginning to serve as names; finally, the adoption of the Babylonian month names upon the return from exile — the names now familiar as Nisan, Iyar, Sivan, and so on. Some of these Babylonian names have associations with Babylonian deities, a fact that raises its own questions.

## Nissan & Leap Years

### Intercalation Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעִיבּוּרִין לְעִיבּוּרִין מִנִּיסָן מָנִינַן? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין מְעַבְּרִין הַשָּׁנָה לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, וְאִם עִיבְּרוּהָ — אֵינָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת. אֲבָל מִפְּנֵי הַדְּחָק — מְעַבְּרִין אוֹתָהּ אַחַר רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה מִיָּד. וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵּן, אֵין מְעַבְּרִין אֶלָּא אֲדָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** on the first of Nisan is the New Year **for leap years.** The Gemara asks: Do **we** really **count leap years from Nisan? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The court **may not declare a leap year before Rosh HaShana, and if** they did **declare** a leap year before Rosh HaShana, the declaration is not valid and **it is not** considered **a leap year. But due to pressing circumstances,** e.g., religious persecution, **it may be declared immediately after Rosh HaShana.** At that time, the Sages may declare that the coming year will be a leap year in accordance with their calculations. **Even so,** the additional month **added** to the **leap year can be only** a second **Adar.** In what sense, then, is Nisan the New Year for leap years?

The *braisa* also lists Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah l'iburim* — the new year for leap years. The Gemara challenges this: does one really determine leap years starting from Nisan? That would mean deciding a full twelve months in advance. But a *braisa* teaches: *ein me'avrin ha-shanah lifnei Rosh Hashanah* — one does not intercalate the year before Rosh Hashanah, meaning before Tishrei. And if one did so prematurely, it does not take effect. Under exceptional pressure — for instance, if there is fear of a governmental decree preventing intercalation later, or if the *nasi* may not be available — one may intercalate immediately after Rosh Hashanah. But in any case, *ein me'avrin ela Adar* — the only month that may be added is a second Adar, since the purpose of intercalation is to ensure that Pesach falls in the spring, and the adjustment is therefore made immediately before Pesach.

### Confusion Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר עַד הַפּוּרִים? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: שׁוֹאֲלִין בְּהִלְכוֹת הַפֶּסַח קוֹדֶם לַפֶּסַח שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בְּחָמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute: **What is the rationale of the one who said** that a leap year can be declared only **until Purim?** It is **since the Master said: One asks** about **the *halakhot* of Passover thirty days before Passover.** Immediately following Purim, on the fifteenth of Adar, people already began to immerse themselves in the *halakhot* of Passover, which occurs on the fifteenth of Nisan. Were the court to declare a leap year after Purim, the festival of Passover would then be delayed for another month. In that case, there is concern that people **will come to demean** the prohibition against **leavened bread** and not observe Passover on its new date in the proper manner.

One reason cited in the Gemara for not intercalating too late in the year concerns the thirty-day pre-Pesach learning period: *sho'alin b'hilchot ha-Pesach kodem l'Pesach sheloshim yom* — we begin publicly teaching the laws of Pesach thirty days before the festival. One opinion held that intercalation must occur before Purim, because once the public teaching of the laws of Pesach begins, people internalize the message that Pesach is thirty days away. If the rabbis then declare an intercalated year, adding another thirty days, the community will have been sent a contradictory message. Rashi explains the danger: people will have already begun preparing for Pesach in earnest, and when told it is now a leap year, they may not believe the announcement and may inadvertently observe Pesach in Adar Sheni, failing to recognize the true Pesach when Nisan arrives. The concern is analogous to the boy who cried wolf — repeated false signals desensitize people to the real one.

The opinion permitting intercalation through the end of Adar responds that the community understands intercalation to be a matter of calculation (*chushbana*), and they will accept that the rabbis finalized their determination only now. People know that such decisions can come late, and will adjust accordingly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ — מִידָּע יְדִיעַ דְּשַׁתָּא מְעַבַּרְתָּא בְּחוּשְׁבָּנָא תַּלְיָא מִלְּתָא, וְסָבְרִי: חוּשְׁבָּנָא הוּא דְּלָא סְלֵיק לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן עַד הָאִידָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sages, Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Papeyyas, who are not concerned about this, what do they say? They say that people **know that a leap year is dependent on the calculation, and they** will **assume that the Sages did not complete the calculation until now,** after Purim. Since this is a matter of common knowledge, there is no concern that declaring a leap year at that late time will lead to a disregard of the *halakhot* of Passover.

### Endpoint Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי עִיבּוּרִין — הַפְסָקַת עִיבּוּרִין. דִּתְנַן: הֵן הֵעִידוּ שֶׁמְּעַבְּרִין הַשָּׁנָה כׇּל אֲדָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: What is** meant here by **leap years?** It means **the end of the leap year.** Once the month of Nisan has arrived, the previous year can no longer be declared a leap year, **as we learned** in a mishna: **They,** Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Papeyyas, **testified that** the court **may declare a leap year all** through the month of **Adar.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak clarifies what the *braisa* means when it calls Nisan the *Rosh Hashanah* for *iburim*: *hafsakat iburim* — it is the *termination* of the opportunity for intercalation, not its initiation. Since intercalation can only occur in Adar, the last moment to declare a leap year is the last day of Adar. Once the first of Nisan arrives, the previous year can no longer be made a leap year. This is confirmed by the *Mishnah* in tractate *Eduyot*, which teaches that one may intercalate the year throughout all of Adar — up to its very last day.

Thus, the *braisa*'s inclusion of Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah l'iburim* reflects not the beginning of a leap year's declaration, but the closing of the prior year's window. Our *Mishnah*, by contrast, is interested in how years begin — not how prior years end — and therefore does not include *iburim* in its list.

## Nissan & Shekalim

### Fresh Funds Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִתְרוּמַת שְׁקָלִים. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״זֹאת עוֹלַת חֹדֶשׁ בְּחׇדְשׁוֹ לְחׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה״ — אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: חַדֵּשׁ וְהָבֵא קׇרְבָּן מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה. וְגָמְרִי ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ מִנִּיסָן, דִּכְתִיב: ״רִאשׁוֹן הוּא לָכֶם לְחׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the first of Nisan is the New Year **for collection of** the **shekels** that had been collected in Adar and were used to purchase animals for communal offerings and other needs of the Temple. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rabbi Yoshiya said** that **the verse states: "This is the burnt-offering of each month in its month throughout the months [*leḥodshei*] of the year"** (Numbers 28:14). The seemingly superfluous term: "Throughout the months [*leḥodshei*] of the year," should be understood as follows: **The Torah is saying** here: **Renew [*ḥaddesh*]** the year **and bring an offering from the new collection** of the shekels. **And it is derived** by way of a verbal analogy between one instance of the word **"year"** and another instance of the word **"year"** that the year begins for this purpose **from Nisan, as it is written** with regard to Nisan: **"It shall be the first month of the year to you"** (Exodus 12:2).

The *braisa* further designates Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah* for the *trumat shekalim* — the collection of new funds for the communal sacrifices. Rabbi Yoshiah derives this from the verse *zot olat chodesh b'chodsho l'chodshei ha-shanah* — "this is the burnt offering of each month in its month, for the months of the year." The phrase *l'chodshei ha-shanah* implies renewal: the communal sacrifices must come from fresh, newly collected funds each year. The starting point for this renewal is Nisan, linked by the same phrase *l'chodshei ha-shanah* to the verse that identifies Nisan as the first month.

This connection is reinforced by a *gezeirah shavah* — the verse governing new *korbanot* uses *chodshei ha-shanah* (months and year together), and the verse establishing Nisan uses the same language. This shared phrasing links them: the renewal of communal sacrificial funds begins in Nisan. The Gemara notes that one cannot apply a verse where "year" appears with "months" to a different verse where "year" appears alone (as in *me-reishit ha-shanah*, associated with Tishrei), precisely because the presence of "months" is the operative link.

### Old vs. New Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר הַבָּאִין בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן — מִצְוָה לְהָבִיא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ. וְאִם הֵבִיא מִן הַיָּשָׁן — יָצָא, אֶלָּא שֶׁחִיסֵּר מִצְוָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר הַבָּאִין בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן — מִצְוָה לְהָבִיא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ, וְאִם הֵבִיא מִן הַיָּשָׁן — יָצָא, אֶלָּא שֶׁחִיסֵּר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **communal offerings that are brought on the first of Nisan,** there is **a mitzva to bring** them **from the new** contribution of shekels collected for that year. However, **if one brought** them **from the old** contribution, i.e., from last year's shekels, **he has fulfilled** the obligation with regard to the offerings, **but he lacks the mitzva** of bringing them from the new shekels. The Gemara comments: **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **communal offerings that are brought on the first of Nisan,** there is **a mitzva to bring** them **from the new** contribution of shekels. However, **if one brought** them **from the old** contribution, **he has fulfilled** the obligation with regard to the sacrifices, **but he lacks the mitzva** of bringing them from the new shekels.

The practical law is stated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *korbanot tzibbur ha-ba'im b'echad b'Nissan, mitzvah l'havi min ha-chadash* — for the communal sacrifices beginning on the first of Nisan, there is a *mitzvah* to bring them from the newly collected funds. *V'im hevi min ha-yashan, yatza* — if one brought them from old funds, the obligation is still discharged; the sacrifice is valid. *Ella she-chisar mitzvah* — but the specific *mitzvah* of renewal has been missed. A parallel *braisa* confirms this ruling.

### Private Donations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָחִיד שֶׁהִתְנַדֵּב מִשֶּׁלּוֹ — כְּשֵׁרִין, בִּלְבַד שֶׁיִּמְסְרֵם לַצִּבּוּר. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא לֹא יִמְסְרֵם לַצִּבּוּר יָפֶה יָפֶה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **If** a private **individual volunteered** to bring the communal offerings **from his own** property, **they are fit** for the altar, **provided that he transfers them over to the community.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that one may donate of his own property to the community, provided that he transfers it to the community in the proper manner. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **we should be concerned** that **perhaps** **he will not transfer them over to the public without reservation,** and in his heart he will hold on to them as his own, therefore the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not a matter of concern.

A further law follows: *yachid she-nitnadav mishelo* — if a private individual donates an animal from his own property to be used as a communal sacrifice, it is valid (*ksherim*), provided he truly transfers it to the community — *u'vilvad she-yimserem l'tzibbur*. The Gemara asks: is this not obvious? If he genuinely transfers it, why would there be any concern? The answer is that one might have worried that even an ostensibly sincere transfer retains some element of personal attachment — perhaps the donor will continue to think of the animal as his own. The Gemara therefore rules that we take him at his word: if the transfer appears complete and he declares it complete, we accept it. The *korban* is now fully the community's.

As for why our *Mishnah* does not include Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah* for *shekalim*: since the law permits bringing from old funds *b'diavad*, it is not absolute. The ruling lacks the definitive quality that would warrant inclusion in the *Mishnah*'s list of the four primary new years.

## Nissan & House Rentals

### Default Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף לִשְׂכִירוּת בָּתִּים. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ לְשָׁנָה — מוֹנֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם. וְאִם אָמַר ״לְשָׁנָה זוֹ״, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא עָמַד אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד בַּאֲדָר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ יוֹם אֶחָד בְּנִיסָן — עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that the first of Nisan is **also** the New Year **for** the **renting of houses. The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **If one rents out a house to another** person **for a year, he counts twelve months from day to day. But if he said** that he was renting it **for this year,** then **even if** the agreement **was made only on the first of Adar, once the first of Nisan arrived** one month later, **it is counted as a year,** and the rental contract comes to end.

The *braisa* concludes with the position of *yesh omrim*: Nisan also serves as *Rosh Hashanah* for rental contracts. The *tanna'im* of the *braisa* teach: *ha-maskir bayit l'chaveiro l'shanah* — one who rents a house to another for "a year" counts twelve calendar months from the rental date, returning to the same day of the calendar year regardless of whether that spans twelve or thirteen months. But *v'im amar l'shanah zu* — if one says "for this year," the contract runs until the end of the current year, defined as the last day of Adar. Once the first of Nisan arrives, the year has turned. Thus, even if the rental began on the first of Adar, the arrival of Nisan — just thirty days later — constitutes the end of that year.

The Gemara adds that even authorities who hold that "one day in a year counts as a full year" for certain purposes (such as counting the years of a king's reign) do not apply that principle here. The reason: *d'lo tarach inish l'migar beisa l'vatzir mi-tlasin yomin* — no one bothers to draw up a rental contract for less than thirty days. Therefore, the minimum period implied by "this year" in a rental context is a full month, and a rental of less than a month would be read as extending through to the end of the *following* year.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יוֹם אֶחָד בַּשָּׁנָה חָשׁוּב שָׁנָה — שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּלָא טָרַח אִינִישׁ לְמֵיגַר בֵּיתָא לִבְצִיר מִתְּלָתִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Even according to the one who said** that **one day in a year is considered a year, it is different here,** with regard to rental *halakhot*, **as a person does not take the trouble to rent a house for less than thirty days.** Therefore, if one rented a house after the first of Adar, the remaining days of Adar are not considered a full year.

### Rainy Season Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא תִּשְׁרִי? סְתָם כִּי אָגַר אִינִישׁ בֵּיתָא — לְכוּלְּהוּ יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים אָגַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that the first of **Tishrei** is the New Year for the renting of houses, and so if one rents a house for a year in the summer the year would come to an end in Tishrei? The Gemara answers: **A person who rents a house without specification** intends to **rent** it **for all of the rainy season,** until Nisan, when the rainy season comes to a close.

The Gemara asks why the "end of the year" in rental contracts should be tied to Nisan rather than Tishrei. The answer is practical and intuitive: *stam ki agar inish beisa, l'chulu yemot geshamim agar* — when someone rents a house, they typically want it for the rainy season. The rainy season in Eretz Yisrael begins after Tishrei. No one wants to be evicted or to begin apartment-hunting at the onset of the rainy season. Therefore, defining the "end of the year" as Tishrei would create exactly this hardship, and it is inconceivable that rental convention would operate this way.

### Minority View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּבָרַיְיתָא, וְתַנָּא דִּידַן? בְּנִיסָן נָמֵי מִישְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי קִיטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why didn't **the first *tanna* of the *baraita* and the *tanna*** of **our** mishna include the first of Nisan as the New Year for the renting of houses? They hold that **even in Nisan it is common** that the sky becomes **covered with clouds** and rain falls. Therefore, one who rents a house does not have in mind to rent it only until the first of Nisan, as presumably he does not want to find himself in a situation where he is homeless when it is still raining.

The first opinion in the *braisa*, as well as the author of our *Mishnah*, does not include Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah* for rental contracts. Their reasoning: *b'Nisan nami shchichi mitri* — rain occurs in Nisan as well. April showers are a reality, and no renter is more comfortable being evicted in Nisan than in Tishrei. If both months can bring rain, there is no principled reason to fix the "end of the year" at either one; local custom and context must determine what parties meant by "this year."

### Tosafot on Payment

One might think that the amount paid would resolve ambiguity — if the rent paid is clearly six months' worth at the going rate, and the contract runs from Rosh Chodesh Adar to Rosh Chodesh Nisan, surely the parties intended one month, not one year. Tosafot cautions against this inference: even if the sum seems inconsistent with the stated duration, we do not necessarily conclude the contract meant something other than what it says. The excess payment could be construed either as a *matanah* — a gift, essentially a signing bonus — or as a *pikadon*, a deposit the tenant expects to recover. Which of these applies has significant halakhic consequences, but neither reading allows us to simply read the sum as decisive evidence of the parties' intent regarding the duration. Tosafot's caution reflects a broader principle in *dinei mamonot*: payment alone cannot override the literal terms of a contract when those terms are susceptible to multiple readings.

## Elul vs. Tishrei

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאֶחָד בֶּאֱלוּל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. מַנִּי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בְּאֶחָד בֶּאֱלוּל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **On the first of Elul is the New Year for animal tithes.** The Gemara comments: **Who is** the author of the opinion cited in this mishna? **It is Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a mishna that **Rabbi Meir says: On the first of Elul is the New Year for animal tithes.**

Having exhausted the discussion of Nisan, the Gemara returns to the *Mishnah*'s next entry: *b'echad b'Elul, Rosh Hashanah l'ma'aser behema* — the first of Elul is the new year for the animal tithe. The Gemara asks: *manni* — who is the anonymous author of this ruling? The answer is Rabbi Meir, as explicitly stated in a *Mishnah* elsewhere: *Rabbi Meir omer b'echad b'Elul, Rosh Hashanah l'ma'aser behema*.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָרְגָלִים. מַנִּי — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: בְּאֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי. רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּמְצִיעֲתָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Festivals,** i.e., that the first of Nisan is the New Year for Festivals, **who is** the author of the opinion cited in the mishna? **It is Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that one transgresses the prohibition against delaying only if the three Festivals have passed in their proper order, with Passover first. **Say the last clause** of the mishna, which states that **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say:** The New Year for animal tithes is **on the first of Tishrei.** Can it be that **the first clause and the last clause** follow the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, while the middle clause** relating to animal tithes follows the opinion of **Rabbi Meir?**

The *Mishnah* then immediately states the minority view: *Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabbi Shimon omrim b'echad b'Tishrei* — the first of Tishrei is the new year for *ma'aser behema*. This creates a structural puzzle. The opening of the *Mishnah* established Nisan as *Rosh Hashanah l'melachim u'l'regalim*. The Gemara has already determined, based on an earlier *braisa*, that the rule of *regalim* for purposes of *bal t'acher* — namely that the *regalim* must be observed in order beginning with Pesach — is the position of Rabbi Shimon. Then the *Mishnah* adopts the view of Rabbi Meir for *ma'aser behema* (Elul rather than Tishrei), and immediately cites Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Eliezer as dissenting on that question.

### Rabbi's Selectivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי הִיא, וְנָסֵיב לַהּ אַלִּיבָּא דְּתַנָּאֵי. בִּרְגָלִים סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּבְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** The entire mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and he takes** the mishna **according to** the opinions of different ***tanna'im*. With regard to the Festivals, he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, while with regard to animal tithes he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

Rav Yosef explains that Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — *Rebbi* — is the *Mishnah*'s redactor, and he exercises deliberate selectivity: *v'nasiv lei aliba d'tana'i* — he chooses among the positions of the *Tanna'im* as he sees fit. *B'regalim savar lah k'Rabbi Shimon* — on the question of the *regalim* and *bal t'acher*, he follows Rabbi Shimon. *U'v'ma'aser behema savar lah k'Rabbi Meir* — on the question of *ma'aser behema*, he follows Rabbi Meir. The *Mishnah* is thus a composite, drawn from two different authorities, each authoritative in his own domain according to Rebbi's judgment.

## Beyond Four Years

### Additional Candidates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַרְבָּעָה? חֲמִשָּׁה הָווּ! אָמַר רָבָא: אַרְבָּעָה לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל; לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַרְבָּעָה — דַּל רְגָלִים, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַרְבָּעָה — דַּל מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. מֵיתִיבִי: שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּנִיסָן — רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לָעוֹמֶר. שִׁשָּׁה בְּסִיוָן — רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לִשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** how are there **four** New Years? If the *tanna* of the mishna holds that the first of Elul is the New Year for animal tithes, **there are five** New Years: The first of Nisan, the fifteenth of Nisan, the first of Elul, the first of Tishrei, and the fifteenth of Shevat. **Rava said: There are** only **four** New Years **according to each opinion:** There are **four according to Rabbi Meir,** who **removes** the New Year for **Festivals,** as according to him there is no fixed time from which to begin counting the Festivals. **According to Rabbi Shimon's** opinion as well there are **four** New Years, for he **removes** the New Year for **animal tithes,** as according to him it is on the first of Tishrei, which is already listed. The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **The sixteenth of Nisan is the New Year for the *omer*** offering, as from this date onward it is permitted to eat from the new crop of grain. **The sixth of Sivan is the New Year for the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering of two loaves from the new wheat brought on *Shavuot*, as from this day onward it is permitted to sacrifice meal-offerings in the Temple from the new grain.

The resolution of the *Mishnah* through Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon creates a new problem. If Rebbi adopts Rabbi Shimon's position on the *regalim*, then the first of Nisan (for kings) and the fifteenth of Nisan (for *regalim*) are two separate entries — yet both fall in Nisan. Adding Elul (for *ma'aser behema*), Tishrei (for years, *shemitah*, and *yovel*), and the fifteenth of Shevat (for trees), we already have five entries, not four. But now a *braisa* raises the number further: *shisha asar b'Nisan, Rosh Hashanah l'omer* — the sixteenth of Nisan is the new year for the *Omer*, marking the beginning of permitted consumption of the new grain; and *shisha b'Sivan, Rosh Hashanah l'shtei halechem* — the sixth of Sivan (Shavuot) marks the new year for the *shtei halechem*, after which new grain may be brought as a *Mincha* offering.

These additions would bring the total to six or seven, depending on how one counts. Rava would go from four to six; Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, who counts by months rather than individual dates, should still reach five, since Sivan would constitute a new month.

### Rav Papa's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּי קָא חָשֵׁיב — מִידֵּי דְּחָיֵיל מֵאוּרְתָּא. מִידֵּי דְּלָא חָיֵיל מֵאוּרְתָּא — לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: When** the *tanna* of the mishna **counts** New Years, he counts only **those that begin in the evening; those that do not begin in the evening he does not count.** Since the New Years associated with the *omer* and the two loaves do not begin in the evening but only from the time that they are sacrificed, he does not include them.

Rav Papa offers the first resolution: *ki chashiv, midei d'chaya m'ortsa — midei d'lo chaya m'ortsa lo kachashiv* — the *Mishnah* counts only those new years that begin the night before, i.e., at nightfall at the onset of the new day. The *Omer* and the *shtei halechem* are brought in the middle of the day; they do not commence at nightfall and therefore are excluded from the count.

The Gemara objects: the *regalim* are also effective only during the day — one can only bring *korbanot* in the daytime — yet the *regalim* are counted. Rav Papa responds that the *regalim* are different: the underlying obligation to bring the *korbanot* arose long before the *regel* itself. The *regel* is not the beginning of the obligation but the moment at which delay becomes a violation. Since the obligation *mechayav v'kai* — was already in place — the *regel* functions as a terminus rather than an initiation.

The Gemara further presses: *Yovel* also takes effect only in the daytime — *ta'aviru shofar b'chol artzechem*, the blowing of the shofar that activates *Yovel*, is understood to occur during the day of Yom Kippur. Yet *Yovel* is included in the *Mishnah*. The Gemara responds that the *Mishnah* here follows the position of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds that *Yovel* already takes effect from Rosh Hashanah — the night before Yom Kippur — not from Yom Kippur itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי רְגָלִים, דְּלָא חָיְילִי מֵאוּרְתָּא, וְקָחָשֵׁיב! כֵּיוָן שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מֵעִיקָּרָא, מִיחַיַּיב וְקָאֵי. וַהֲרֵי יוֹבְלוֹת, דְּלָא חָיְילִי מֵאוּרְתָּא, וְקָחָשֵׁיב! רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה חָיֵיל יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** the New Year for **Festivals, which does not begin in the evening,** as the prohibition against delaying is not transgressed in the evening, when the Festival begins, but only in the morning, after the daily offering has been brought and one is able to bring the vowed animal to the altar, **and** nevertheless the *tanna* **counts** it. The Gemara answers: **Since he had to bring** his vow by the Festival, **he stands liable from the beginning** of the Festival for transgressing the prohibition against delaying. The Gemara asks further: **But there is** the New Year for **the Jubilee, which does not begin in the evening** but from the time of the *shofar* blast on Yom Kippur during the day, **and** nevertheless the *tanna* **counts** it. The Gemara answers: The mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, who said** that **the Jubilee Year begins on Rosh HaShana,** and the blowing of the *shofar* merely completes the release of the slaves. Therefore, the New Year for the Jubilee is included.

### Rav Sheshet's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר: כִּי קָא חָשֵׁיב — מִידֵּי דְּלָא תְּלֵי בְּמַעֲשֶׂה, מִידֵּי דִּתְלֵי בְּמַעֲשֶׂה — לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said** a different explanation for why the New Year for the *omer* offering and the New Year for the two loaves are not mentioned in the mishna: **When** the *tanna* of the mishna **counts** New Years, he counts only **those that do not depend upon an action; those that depend upon an action,** e.g., the offering of the *omer* or the two loaves, **he does not count.**

Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi offers a simpler principle: *ki kachashiv midei d'lo tali b'ma'aseh* — the *Mishnah* counts only those new years that are not dependent on an action. Dates that carry inherent significance by virtue of the calendar alone are included; dates whose significance derives from a specific act performed on them are not. The sixteenth of Nisan is not really a date — it is the day on which the *Omer* is brought, and it is that act of bringing which matters. Similarly, the sixth of Sivan is the date of the *shtei halechem* offering, and the significance is action-dependent.

The Gemara raises the same objection: the *regalim* are also action-dependent — they are about bringing *korbanot*. Rav Sheshet responds: *bal t'acher mimela chaya* — the *regalim* function differently. They make one liable not for having done something on that day, but for *not* having done something. It is the failure to act that the date activates, not the action itself. The date has independent significance as the point at which liability crystallizes.

And again the Gemara presses: *Yovel* also requires blowing the shofar, an action — yet it is listed. Again the answer: the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, for whom *Yovel* takes effect from Rosh Hashanah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי רְגָלִים, מִידֵּי דִּתְלֵי בְּמַעֲשֶׂה, וְקָא חָשֵׁיב! ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״ מִמֵּילָא חָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** the New Year for **the Festivals, which depends upon an action,** i.e., the sacrifice of the daily offering, since no offering may be brought before the daily offering, **and** nevertheless the *tanna* **counts** it. The Gemara answers: This is not so, as the transgression of the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** does not depend upon anything else; rather, it **begins on its own** as soon as the Festival begins.

### Rav Ashi's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אַרְבַּע רָאשֵׁי הַשָּׁנִים הֵם שֶׁהֵן בְּאַרְבַּע רָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an alternative answer as to why the *tanna* did not include all the other New Years. **Rav Ashi said:** The *tanna* said that **there are four New Years that fall on four New Moons.** However, there are also other New Years that do not fall on New Moons.

Rav Ashi provides a final, clean resolution: *arba'ah Roshei Hashanim hen she-hen b'arba'ah Roshei Chadashim* — there are four new years, and they fall on four first-of-the-month dates. The *Mishnah* counts only those *roshei shanah* that fall on the first of a month. The sixteenth of Nisan and the sixth of Sivan are immediately excluded on this basis. The *regalim* in the context of *bal t'acher* refer primarily to the fifteenth of Nisan — already counted as part of the Nisan block — and do not add a separate new year on a different first of a month.

The Gemara raises one remaining difficulty: the fifteenth of Shevat (*Tu B'Shevat*), as held by Beit Hillel, is not the first of the month. Rav Ashi's principle would require that the new year for trees fall on the *first* of Shevat, which is Beit Shammai's position. The Gemara therefore concludes: *sheloshah l'divrei hakol* — three of the four new years are agreed upon by everyone and fall on the first of their respective months. The fourth — the new year for trees — is a matter of dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Rav Ashi's principle of counting only first-of-the-month *roshei shanah* applies as an absolute rule to three, and by Beit Shammai's reckoning also to the fourth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאֶחָד בִּשְׁבָט — כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁלֹשָׁה לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל, בְּאֶחָד בִּשְׁבָט — מַחְלוֹקֶת בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: Does Rav Ashi count the first of Shevat as one of the New Years; but **the first of Shevat is** a New Year only according to **Beit Shammai,** and by the time of Rav Ashi, the *halakha* was known to be in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel? The Gemara answers that **this is what** Rav Ashi **is saying:** There are **three** New Years that **all agree** occur on the first of the month, **and the first of Shevat is** subject to **a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.**
