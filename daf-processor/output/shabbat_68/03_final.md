# Shabbat 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Klal Gadol - Principle

### New Perek, Shabbat Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כְּלָל גָּדוֹל אָמְרוּ בְּשַׁבָּת: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת. הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת. הַיּוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַב מְלָאכָה וּמְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Sages **stated a significant principle with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat: One who forgets the essence of Shabbat,** i.e., one who is entirely ignorant of the mitzva of Shabbat according to Torah law, **and performed numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot*, is liable** to bring **only one sin-offering** for all those labors when he becomes aware that those actions were prohibited. **One who knows the essence of Shabbat** but forgets which day is Shabbat, i.e., one who lost track of the days of the week, **and performs numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each Shabbat** when he becomes aware that he performed those actions on Shabbat. **One who is aware that** the day **is Shabbat** but temporarily forgot that certain labors were prohibited **and performed numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each primary category of labor.**

With daf 68 we arrive at a new *perek* — and, more significantly, a new dimension of *Masechet Shabbat* altogether. The preceding six chapters concerned themselves with preparations on *erev Shabbat*; only now do we enter into the laws of Shabbat proper. The *perek* opens with a *mishna* that begins, *klal gadol amru b'Shabbat* — "a great principle they stated regarding Shabbat" — and this phrase itself will occupy a substantial portion of the *gemara's* attention before it reaches the substantive halachic material.

### Case 1: Forgot Shabbat Entirely

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **One who forgets the essence of Shabbat, and performed numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot*, is liable** to bring **only one sin-offering.**

The *mishna* presents three distinct cases, each defined by the nature of the person's error and each carrying different consequences for the number of *chatat* offerings required.

The first case: *kol hashocheiach ikar Shabbat v'asa melachos harbeh b'Shabbatos harbeh* — someone who forgets the very existence of Shabbat, the fact that there is such a concept in the *Torah*, and over the course of many Shabbatot performs many different *melachos*. When such a person is eventually reminded — perhaps after ten years of unknowing violation — they bring only *achat*, one *chatat* offering. The reasoning is that there was only one fundamental error: forgetting that Shabbat exists at all. Every subsequent violation flows from that single mistake, and therefore a single offering suffices.

### Case 2: Forgot Which Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **One who knows the essence of Shabbat** but forgets which day is Shabbat **and performs numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each Shabbat.**

The second case: *hayodea ikar Shabbat v'asa melachos harbeh b'Shabbatos harbeh* — someone who knows that Shabbat exists but, as the *gemara* will explain, forgets which day of the week is Shabbat. Perhaps this person's days became confused each week. In such a case, *chayav al kol Shabbat v'Shabbat* — one is liable for every Shabbat separately, because each week constitutes a distinct act of forgetting. The error is not one global mistake but a recurring one, a fresh lapse each week, and therefore the liability is assessed week by week.

One might ask: what if the confusion began with a single initial error — a one-time disorientation from which all subsequent mistakes derived? The *gemara* addresses this and makes clear that, objectively, each week's violation is defined as a separate act, even if causally linked to a first mistake.

### Case 3: Forgot Melacha Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיּוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַב מְלָאכָה וּמְלָאכָה. הָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה אַחַת, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **One who is aware that** the day **is Shabbat** but temporarily forgot that certain labors were prohibited **and performed numerous prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each primary category of labor. One who performs numerous prohibited labors** subsumed under a **single** category **of labor is liable** to bring **only one sin-offering.**

The third case: *hayodea shehu Shabbat v'asa melachos harbeh b'Shabbatos harbeh* — someone who knows it is Shabbat but did not know, or forgot, that a particular *melacha* or *melachos* were forbidden. Here the liability is *chayav al kol av melacha u'melacha* — one *chatat* for each distinct category of *melacha*. If such a person did not know that watering plants was forbidden, and violated this over ten Shabbatot, they bring one *chatat*. If they additionally did not know that kindling fire was forbidden, they bring a second *chatat*. The principle here is that the liability tracks the category of the error, not the number of Shabbatot on which it was repeated.

### One Category = One Offering

A final refinement of the *mishna*: *v'oseh melachos harbeh m'ein melacha achas* — if one performs many *melachos* that all belong to the same essential category, they bring only one *chatat*, since the fundamental mistake was one. Watering, planting, pruning, and fertilizing are all *toldos* of the *av melacha* of planting — they all fall under the same heading. Even if performed in many variations on a single Shabbat, a single *chatat* suffices.

### Thirty-Nine Categories

There are several *chidushim* embedded in this *mishna*, and it is worth taking a moment to appreciate them. The *mishna* organizes the *melachos* into categories — *avos* — and the following *mishna* will enumerate thirty-nine of them. The *gemara* will ask: what is the halachic significance of counting thirty-nine? The answer: it informs us that one could theoretically be liable for up to thirty-nine separate *chataos* for a single Shabbat, one for each *av melacha*, if one forgot the prohibition on every individual category.

This is itself a *chiddush*. One might have thought that just as eating from different parts of a *neveilah* constitutes a single prohibition regardless of how many bites one takes, violating Shabbat in multiple distinct ways — planting and cooking and lighting a fire — should be treated as multiple instantiations of the same transgression of Shabbat. The *mishna* teaches otherwise: the different categories of *melacha* are considered independent sins, each requiring its own *chatat*. Within a category, however, all variations are subsumed under a single offering. This dual structure — multiplicity across categories, unity within them — is the first great *chiddush* of the *perek*.

The second *chiddush* is structural: where one's error lies determines the unit of liability. Error about Shabbat's existence generates one *chatat* total. Error about which day is Shabbat generates one per Shabbat. Error about a particular *melacha* generates one per category of *melacha* forgotten. The axis of the mistake is the axis of the obligation.

## Klal Gadol - Analysis

### Linguistic Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא תְּנָא "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל"? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי: "עוֹד כְּלָל אַחֵר אָמְרוּ", תְּנָא "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל". וְגַבֵּי שְׁבִיעִית נָמֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי: "עוֹד כְּלָל אַחֵר אָמְרוּ", תְּנָא "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל". וְהָא גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר, דְּקָתָנֵי: "כְּלָל אַחֵר אָמְרוּ", וְלָא תָּנֵי "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל"!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara attempts to clarify the language of the mishna and asks: **Why did** the mishna **teach** the phrase: **A significant principle? If you say** it is **because** of the following reason, it is problematic. Here, because the *tanna* **wants to teach** in a mishna later in the chapter with regard to a matter that includes two *halakhot* employing the term: **Furthermore,** they stated **another principle;** therefore, in this mishna, which relates to a greater number of *halakhot*, he **taught** employing the term: **A significant principle.**  **And with regard to the Sabbatical Year as well, because** in a later mishna (*Shevi'it* 7:2) the *tanna* **wants to teach: Furthermore, another principle,** at the beginning of the chapter he **taught** employing the phrase: **A significant principle.** There too, the choice of language is understood.  **However, with regard to** the *halakhot* of **tithes,** where the mishna (*Ma'asrot* 1:1) states two principles one after the other, the *tanna* **taught** later in the same mishna: And furthermore, they stated **another principle, and** even so, at the beginning of the mishna the *tanna* **did not teach: A significant principle,** opting instead to say simply: They stated a principle.

With the substance of the *mishna* now established, the *gemara* turns to what might at first appear a marginal linguistic question: *mai ta'ama tana klal gadol* — why does the *mishna* use the phrase *klal gadol*, a "great" principle, rather than simply *klal*? One thinks of the famous formulation in *masechet Avot* — *zeh klal gadol ba-Torah, v'ahavta l'rei'acha kamocha* — where the greatness refers to the sweeping moral scope of the principle. But what warrants the designation here?

The *gemara* considers a first answer: perhaps *gadol* is used because the *mishna* intends to signal that a second, lesser *klal* is coming later. Indeed, the *gemara* in *Shabbat* later introduces another principle — about the minimum quantities required for the prohibition of carrying — and that *klal* is of narrower scope. So the first one is called *klal gadol* to distinguish it.

This framing draws the *gemara* to observe that *masechet Shevi'it* similarly opens its seventh *perek* with *klal gadol amru ba-Shevi'it* — and this parallel is striking: the seventh *perek* of *Shabbat* (the seventh day) and the seventh *perek* of *Shevi'it* (the seventh year) both open with *klal gadol*. This cannot be coincidental.

But the *gemara* notes a difficulty: *masechet Ma'aser* introduces two *klalim* but does not call the first one *gadol*. If *gadol* merely signals that a second *klal* follows, why not by *ma'aser* as well?

### Avot and Toldot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין: שַׁבָּת וּשְׁבִיעִית דְּאִית בְּהוּ אָבוֹת וְתוֹלָדוֹת — תְּנָא "גָּדוֹל", מַעֲשֵׂר דְּלֵית בֵּהּ אָבוֹת וְתוֹלָדוֹת, לָא תְּנָא "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל". וּלְבַר קַפָּרָא, דְּתָנֵי "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל" בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, מַאי אָבוֹת וּמַאי תּוֹלָדוֹת אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin said** that the term: A significant principle, is not dependent on the existence of another principle; rather, it is dependent on the significance of the principle. Therefore, with regard to the *halakhot* of **Shabbat and the Sabbatical Year, which include primary categories and subcategories,** the *tanna* **taught** in the mishna: **A significant** principle. With regard to the *halakhot* of **tithes, which do not include primary categories and subcategories** and all its *halakhot* are on equal footing, **he did not teach** employing the term: **A significant principle.** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to** the variant reading of the mishna taught by **bar Kappara, who taught** the phrase: **A significant principle, with regard to tithes, what primary categories and subcategories are there** with regard to tithes?

The *gemara* offers a deeper explanation in the name of Rabbi Yossi bar Avin: *Shabbat ve-Shevi'it de-is behu avot ve-is behu toldot* — both Shabbat and *Shevi'it* share the conceptual structure of *avos* (primary categories) and *toldos* (derived subcategories). By Shabbat this is explicit. By *Shevi'it*, the *Torah* only explicitly prohibits four agricultural activities — do not plant, do not prune, do not harvest grain, do not harvest grapes — but these are understood as *avos* from which additional prohibited activities are derived. Since both Shabbat and *Shevi'it* possess this major-category structure, both open with *klal gadol*, a *klal* that belongs to a domain of great scope.

*Ma'aser*, by contrast, has no such *avos* and *toldos* structure, and therefore does not use *klal gadol*.

### Scope Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, גָּדוֹל עוֹנְשָׁהּ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל שְׁבִיעִית, דְּאִילּוּ שַׁבָּת אִיתַהּ בֵּין בְּתָלוּשׁ בֵּין בִּמְחוּבָּר, וְאִילּוּ שְׁבִיעִית בְּתָלוּשׁ לֵיתַהּ בִּמְחוּבָּר אִיתַהּ. וְגָדוֹל עוֹנְשָׁהּ שֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית יוֹתֵר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר, דְּאִילּוּ שְׁבִיעִית אִיתַהּ בֵּין בְּמַאֲכַל אָדָם בֵּין בְּמַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה, וְאִילּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר בְּמַאֲכַל אָדָם אִיתֵהּ בְּמַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה לֵיתֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't this the reason** the Mishna employs the term: A significant principle; because it is significant relative to other principles? The scope of the materials whose use warrants **punishment for** desecrating **Shabbat is greater than** the scope of the materials whose use warrants punishment for desecrating **the Sabbatical Year. As** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat are** in effect **both with regard to** plants that are **detached** from the ground and **with regard to** those that are **attached, while** the *halakhot* of **the Sabbatical Year with regard to detached** plants, they **are not** in effect, but **with regard to attached** plants they **are** in effect. **And** the scope of the materials whose use warrants **punishment** for desecration **of the Sabbatical Year are greater than** the scope of the materials whose use warrants punishment for violating the *halakhot* **of tithes. As,** by Torah law, the *halakhot* of **the Sabbatical Year are** in effect **both with regard to human food** and **with regard to animal food, while** the *halakhot* of **tithes are** in effect **with regard to human food,** but **with regard to animal food** they **are not** in effect.

Yet the *gemara* notes that this explanation, while internally coherent, cannot be the sole basis for the term *gadol* — because as we shall see, Bar Kappara's version of the *mishna* uses *klal gadol* by *ma'aser* as well, and *ma'aser* has no *avos* and *toldos*. Therefore *gadol* must mean something else.

The *gemara* proposes a second explanation: *gadol* refers to relative scope. Shabbat's *issur melacha* is *chamur* — more severe and broader in scope than that of *Shevi'it*. How so? *De-ilu Shabbat ita bein be-talush bein be-mechubar* — by Shabbat, the prohibition applies whether the object of the *melacha* is still attached to the ground or has already been detached. *Ve-ilu Shevi'it be-talush leita be-mechubar ita* — by *Shevi'it*, the *melacha* prohibition applies only to things currently growing from the ground. Thus the scope of Shabbat's *melacha* prohibition is broader, and its punishment is more severe — *chayav mita*, whereas *Shevi'it* carries only a *lav*. Shabbat is therefore *gadol* relative to *Shevi'it*.

### Bar Kappara Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבַר קַפָּרָא דְּתָנֵי "כְּלָל גָּדוֹל" בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר: גָּדוֹל עוֹנְשׁוֹ שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל פֵּיאָה, דְּאִילּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר אִיתֵהּ בִּתְאֵנָה וְיָרָק וְאִילּוּ פֵּיאָה לֵיתַהּ בִּתְאֵנָה וְיָרָק. דִּתְנַן, כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בַּפֵּיאָה: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אוֹכֶל וְנִשְׁמָר וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ וּלְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאַחַת וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם — חַיָּיב בְּפֵיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according **to** the opinion of **bar Kappara, who taught** the phrase: **A significant principle, with regard to tithes** as well: **The** scope of the materials for which one warrants **punishment** for violating the *halakhot* of **tithes is greater than** the scope of the materials for which one warrants punishment for violating the *halakhot* of ***pe'a*. As,** by rabbinic law, the obligation of **tithes is** in effect **with regard to** both **figs and vegetables, while** the obligation of ***pe'a* is not** in effect **with regard to figs and vegetables. As we learned** in a mishna in tractate *Pe'a*: **They stated a principle with regard to *pe'a*: Anything that is food, and is protected, and grows from the ground, and is gathered as one, and one brings it in** to storage **to preserve is obligated in *pe'a*.** 

This comparative logic then cascades down the chain: *Shevi'it* is in turn *gadol* relative to *ma'aser*, because *Shevi'it* applies even to foods fit for animal consumption (*be-maachal beheimah*), and the *pasuk* by *Shevi'it* explicitly includes *"u-livhemtecha ve-la-chayah asher be-artzecha."* Moreover, the *kedushah* of *Shevi'it* applies even to items used as dyes. *Ma'aser*, by contrast, applies only to human food — and biblically, as Rashi notes, only to *dagan, tirosh, ve-yitzhar* (grain, wine, and oil), with the rabbinic extension reaching other fruits and vegetables but still restricted to human food. Thus *Shevi'it* is *gadol* relative to *ma'aser*, and *ma'aser* in Bar Kappara's version is *gadol* relative to *pe'ah*.

### Pe'ah vs Ma'aser Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹכֶל — לְמַעוֹטֵי סְפִיחֵי סְטִיס וְקוֹצָה. וְנִשְׁמָר — לְמַעוֹטֵי הֶפְקֵר. וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — לְמַעוֹטֵי כְּמֵיהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת. וּלְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאַחַת — לְמַעוֹטֵי תְּאֵנָה. וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם — לְמַעוֹטֵי יָרָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that which is excluded by each criterion in the mishna. **Food, to exclude the aftergrowths of woad [*satis*] and madder.** As these plants are used for dyeing and not for food, the obligation of *pe'a* does not apply to them. **And protected, to exclude ownerless** crops, which by definition are not protected. **And grows from the ground, to exclude truffles and mushrooms,** which, unlike other plants, do not draw sustenance from the ground. **And is gathered as one, to exclude** the **fig** tree whose fruit is gathered throughout an extended period, as the figs do not all ripen together. **And one brings it in** to storage **to preserve; to exclude vegetables,** which cannot be stored for lengthy periods.

This requires us to examine the scope comparison between *ma'aser* and *pe'ah*. The *mishna* in *Pe'ah* teaches — *klal amru be-pe'ah* (note: no *gadol*) — that *pe'ah* applies to anything that is food, guarded (not *hefker*), grows from the ground, is harvested at one time, and can be stored for preservation. These five criteria determine what requires *pe'ah*.

*Ma'aser*, by Bar Kappara's formulation, shares the first three criteria — food, guarded, growing from the ground — but does not include the latter two: *lekitaso ke-echad* (harvested all at one time) and *machniso le-kiyum* (can be stored). This absence of the latter two criteria means *ma'aser* applies to a broader range of produce. Specifically, figs are excluded from *pe'ah* because they are harvested at different times throughout the year, and vegetables are excluded because they cannot be stored. Both figs and vegetables are nonetheless subject to *ma'aser*. Hence *ma'aser* has broader scope and is called *gadol* relative to *pe'ah*.

### Tosafot Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר תְּנַן, כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אוֹכֶל וְנִשְׁמָר וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — חַיָּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. וְאִילּוּ לְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאַחַת וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם לָא תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **While, with regard to tithes, we learned** in a mishna: **They stated a principle with regard to tithes: Anything that is food, and is protected, and grows from the ground is obligated in tithes; we did not learn** with regard to tithes, the following criteria: **Gathered as one, and which one brings in** to storage **to preserve.** Apparently, figs and vegetables are obligated in tithes, making the scope of the materials obligated in tithes greater than the scope of those obligated in *pe'a*.

*Tosafot* raises an interesting question: since both *ma'aser* and *pe'ah* were rabinnically extended to cover all types of produce — fruits and vegetables not covered by biblical law — why did the rabbis apply a narrower scope to *pe'ah* than to *ma'aser*? Why introduce the additional criteria of *lekitaso ke-echad* and *machniso le-kiyum* for *pe'ah* but not for *ma'aser*?

*Tosafot* answers by pointing to the qualitative difference in how the two obligations function. By *ma'aser*, the process is straightforward: you complete the harvest entirely, then set aside a tenth and distribute it. By *pe'ah*, however, the poor are physically present in the field as you harvest — one sees this vividly in the book of Ruth — hovering and waiting for their portion. If the produce is not harvested all at one time, the poor must return repeatedly throughout the season; if the produce cannot be preserved, they may invest significant effort and time only to receive something that quickly spoils. The rabbis therefore determined that it would not serve the poor's interests to obligate *pe'ah* on produce that is harvested piecemeal throughout the year or that rots quickly. The result is a narrower scope for *pe'ah*, driven by practical concern for the very beneficiaries the institution was designed to serve — an approach that is, as one might note, simultaneously paternalistic and realistic.

What the *gemara* accomplishes by raising the linguistic question of *gadol* is to draw a rich set of comparisons across Shabbat, *Shevi'it*, *ma'aser*, and *pe'ah* — mapping a descending hierarchy of scope and severity, while also highlighting the deep structural parallel between Shabbat and *Shevi'it* in their shared system of *avos* and *toldos*.

## Tinok Shenishba Status

### Never Knew = Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מַתְנִיתִין בְּתִינוֹק שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּה לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם, וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם. אֲבָל הִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses an individual who forgets the very essence of Shabbat. The Gemara seeks to understand how a Jew could forget the very existence of Shabbat. It was **Rav and Shmuel who both said: Our mishna** is referring **to** both **a child who was taken captive among the gentiles** and never educated **and a convert who converted among the gentiles** and never learned the *halakhot* of Shabbat. **However,** one who once **knew** of the essence of Shabbat **and ultimately forgot is liable for each and every Shabbat,** as we learned in the mishna with regard to one who knows the essence of Shabbat.

The *gemara* now turns to the heart of the first clause of the *mishna*: who exactly is the person described as having "forgotten the existence of Shabbat"? Rav and Shmuel, in a joint ruling, identify the *mishna's* case as referring not to someone who once knew of Shabbat and then forgot, but to someone who never knew at all. *Matnitin be-tinok shenishba le-vein ha-nochrim ve-ger shenitgayer le-vein ha-nochrim* — the *mishna* addresses either a Jew who was taken captive as an infant and raised among non-Jews, or a convert who converted in a non-Jewish environment and never had access to knowledge of Shabbat.

### Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: "הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת״, לָאו מִכְּלָל דְּהַוְיָא לֵיהּ יְדִיעָה מֵעִיקָּרָא?! לָא, מַאי "כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת״ — שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׁכוּחָה מִמֶּנּוּ עִיקָּרָהּ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify this approach. **We learned** in our mishna: **One who forgets the essence of Shabbat. Doesn't** this phrase indicate **by inference that he was aware** of Shabbat **originally?** In order to forget one must have previously been aware. This poses a difficulty to the opinion of Rav and Shmuel. The Gemara refutes this: **No, what is** the meaning of: **One who forgets the essence of Shabbat? That the essence of Shabbat** was always **forgotten from him,** i.e., he never knew it.

The *tinok shenishba* — the infant taken captive — never knew of their Jewish identity, let alone of Shabbat. The *ger shenitgayer bein ha-nochrim* — the convert who converted among non-Jews — presents a somewhat more complex case. A convert knows he or she is becoming Jewish, yet may have done so in circumstances where the full content of Jewish law was not transmitted. One might argue that a convert bears some residual responsibility to seek out that knowledge; nevertheless, the *gemara* treats this case alongside the *tinok shenishba* as the paradigm of "never knowing."

Rav and Shmuel maintain that even such a person, who bears no fault whatsoever, is obligated to bring one *chatat* upon learning of their Jewish identity and of all the violations they unknowingly committed.

### Tosafot Question

A *baraita* now elaborates on this principle. The Tosafot raises a sharp question on the case of the *ger shenitgayer*. By law, a conversion requires a *beit din* of three — *bifnei gimel*. If there was a *beit din*, what do we mean that the person never knew about Shabbat? Is not informing a convert about the *mitzvos* a fundamental part of the conversion process?

Tosafot answers: *v'lo hodiu mitzvos Shabbat* — the *beit din* simply did not get around to mentioning Shabbat specifically among the *mitzvos* they conveyed. The *beit din* was present and functional; the oversight was theirs, not the convert's.

### Kabbalat Mitzvot Principle

This leads *Tosafot* to articulate a significant principle about *geirut*: *bedi'avad*, the failure to inform a convert of the *mitzvos* does not invalidate the conversion. This is distinct from the requirement of *kabbalat mitzvot* — acceptance of the commandments — which must be present. But *kabbalat mitzvot* can function like signing a blank check, as it were: one accepts whatever the *mitzvos* are, even without knowing their precise content. *Na'aseh v'nishma* is the model. The *gemara* here, and a small number of related passages, thus forms a primary basis for the ruling that a conversion performed *bedi'avad* without a full enumeration of the *mitzvos* is nonetheless valid.

## Gemara Analysis

### Linguistic Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח מַאי — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת? אַדְּתָנֵי הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת, לִיתְנֵי "הִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח", וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **However,** based on that understanding, in the case of **one who knew** the essence of Shabbat **and ultimately forgot, what** is the *halakha*? Is he **liable for each and every Shabbat?** If so, **instead of** the mishna **teaching** the next *halakha*: **One who knows the essence of Shabbat and performs many labors on multiple *Shabbatot*** is **liable to** bring a sin-offering **for each and every Shabbat, let it teach: One who knew** the essence of Shabbat **and ultimately forgot and, all the more so, one** who knows the essence of Shabbat would be liable for each Shabbat.

The *gemara* now subjects the *mishna's* language to scrutiny. If the first case of the *mishna* — *hashocheiach ikar Shabbat* — describes someone who "forgot" the existence of Shabbat, doesn't the very word *shachach* (forgot) presuppose that the person once knew? Forgetting implies prior knowledge. This would seem to contradict Rav and Shmuel, who say the *mishna* addresses someone who never knew at all.

### Case Escalation

The *gemara* initially attempts to resolve this by proposing an escalating reinterpretation of the *mishna*. Perhaps every case in the *mishna* is actually describing the next, more severe level of fault: "forgot the existence of Shabbat" really means "never knew," "forgot which day was Shabbat" really means "knew the existence but forgot it entirely," and so on upward.

### Knew Then Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת" — מִי שֶׁהָיָה יוֹדֵעַ עִיקָּרָהּ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת וּשְׁכֵחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: According to the opinion of Rav and Shmuel, **what** is the meaning of the phrase: **One who knows the essence of Shabbat? One who** once **knew the essence of Shabbat and** has now **forgotten it.**

Following this logic, if one knew the existence of Shabbat and then completely forgot about it — a case more severe than never knowing — then one would be liable not merely once but once per Shabbat, exactly as the *mishna's* second case specifies. The *gemara* thus attempts to show that Rav and Shmuel's position can be reconciled with the *mishna* by reading each clause of the *mishna* as referring to the next level up.

### Reinterpretation

The difficulty with this strategy becomes apparent when the *gemara* tries to apply it consistently. If we are reinterpreting each clause upward, then the third case — forgetting a particular *melacha* — should become: you forgot which day was Shabbat. But there is no further clause beyond the third to describe what happens when you know everything but forget the *melacha*. The chain of re-escalation breaks down. One cannot coherently re-read "forgot a *melacha*" as "forgot which day was Shabbat," since these are categorically different kinds of forgetting — one about Shabbat itself, one about its content. There is no natural way to complete the re-interpretation, and the *gemara* acknowledges the impasse.

### Rejection of Escalation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא שְׁכֵחָהּ, מַאי — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל מְלָאכָה וּמְלָאכָה? אַדְּתָנֵי הַיּוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא שַׁבָּת וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל מְלָאכָה וּמְלָאכָה, לִיתְנֵי "הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת", וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הָא! אֶלָּא, מַתְנִיתִין כְּשֶׁהִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח, וּדְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי כְּהִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח דָּמֵי. וְהָכִי אִיתְּמַר: רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֲפִילּוּ תִּינוֹק שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּה בֵּין הַגּוֹיִם וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם — כְּהִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח דָּמֵי, וְחַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But** if **he did not forget** the essence of Shabbat, and he knows that today is Shabbat, **what** would the *halakha* be? Certainly he would be **liable for each and every prohibited labor.** If so, **instead of teaching** the *halakha*: **One who knows that it is Shabbat and performs many labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable for each and every labor, let** the mishna **teach** the *halakha*: **One who knows the essence of Shabbat** is liable for each and every labor that he performs **and all the more so** that **one** who is aware that today is Shabbat would be liable for each labor. **Rather,** when **our mishna** refers to forgetting, it is referring to a case **where he knew and ultimately forgot. And** the case described by **Rav and Shmuel also** has the same legal status **as one who knew and ultimately forgot. And it was stated as follows:** It was **Rav and Shmuel who both said: Even a child who was taken captive among the gentiles and a convert who converted among the gentiles** have the same legal status **as one who knew and ultimately forgot, and** they are **liable** to bring a sin-offering for their unwitting transgression, even though they never learned about Shabbat.

The *gemara* therefore rejects this entire reinterpretation strategy. *Matnitin ke-shita de-shachach kach* — the *mishna* is taken at face value in its simple reading. One who knew the existence of Shabbat and then forgot about it entirely is treated as a single, unitary error: one *chatat*, full stop. Rav and Shmuel do not claim that "knew and then forgot" becomes like "forgot each week." The *mishna* reads simply on that point.

### Final Position

What Rav and Shmuel are actually saying, now clarified, is a *chiddush* about the *tinok shenishba* case: *afilu tinok she-nishba bein ha-nochrim ve-ger she-nitgayer bein ha-nochrim, ke-hikro be-sof shachach dami ve-chayav* — even someone who never knew Shabbat at all is treated halachically as though they knew and forgot, and is obligated to bring one *chatat*. The *chiddush* is not about escalating liability but about extending liability to the completely blameless. Even total ignorance — not negligence, not carelessness, but complete absence of knowledge — nevertheless generates a single *chatat* offering.

## Exempt Position

### Yochanan & Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: דַּוְקָא הִכִּיר וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁכַח — אֲבָל תִּינוֹק שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּה בֵּין הַגּוֹיִם וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish who both said:** He is liable to bring a sin-offering **specifically** if **he knew** of the essence of Shabbat **and ultimately forgot. However, a child who was taken captive among the gentiles and a convert who converted among the gentiles** are **exempt** from bringing a sin-offering. They have the legal status of one who performed the prohibited labor due to circumstances beyond his control.

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish take the opposing position. Only where there is some degree of fault — where the person knew of Shabbat and subsequently forgot — can liability attach. But a *tinok shenishba* or a *ger shenitgayer* who truly never knew: *patur*, completely exempt, no *chatat* required.

### Korban Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּלָל גָּדוֹל אָמְרוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. כֵּיצַד? — תִּינוֹק שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּה לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם, וְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר בֵּין הַגּוֹיִם, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת. וְחַיָּיב עַל הַדָּם אַחַת, וְעַל הַחֵלֶב אַחַת, וְעַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אַחַת, וּמוֹנְבַּז פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** They have the legal status of one who performed the prohibited labor due to circumstances beyond his control. The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **They stated a significant principle with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat: One who forgets the essence of Shabbat,** i.e., one who does not know that there is a mitzva of Shabbat in the Torah, **and performs many prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable** to bring **only one** sin-offering. **How so?** With regard to **a child who was taken captive among the gentiles and a convert who converted among the gentiles** and does not know the essence of Shabbat; **and** if **he performed many prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot,* he is only liable** to bring **one sin-offering** for all his unwitting transgressions. **And he is liable** to bring **one** sin-offering **for** all **the blood** he unwittingly ate before he learned of the prohibition; **and one** sin-offering **for** all **the forbidden fat** that he ate; **and** one **for** all the **idolatry** that he worshipped. **And Munbaz,** one of the Sages, **deems** him **exempt** from bringing any sacrifice.

This makes immediate intuitive sense. If someone grew up without any knowledge of their Jewish identity — never knew they were Jewish, never had any reason to observe Shabbat, bore no negligence whatsoever — on what basis would one obligate them to bring a sin offering? Clearly they are *onussim*, people acted upon by circumstance, in the fullest sense of the word.

Yet Rav and Shmuel's position is not as counterintuitive as it might appear, once one understands a broader conception of what a *chatat* accomplishes. The *gemara* in *Horayot* illuminates this with a telling parallel: if a *beit din* issues an erroneous ruling and the majority of *Klal Yisrael* follows it and sins in good faith, it is the *beit din* that brings a *korban*. But if fewer than a majority followed the ruling, each individual brings a *chatat* — even though they acted in perfect good faith on the authority of the highest legal body. The individual bears no moral fault; yet the *chatat* is theirs to bring. This suggests that the *chatat* is not solely a mechanism of guilt-processing. It also serves to repair a metaphysical rupture: something in the world was broken by the act, and the offering is the vehicle for restoring it. The person who did the act — however blameless — is the one whose offering effects the repair.

Similarly by *Yom Kippur*, the fundamental *avodah* of *Yom Ha-Kippurim* is described as *ve-chipper al ha-kodesh* — to purify the *Mikdash* itself — because sin, regardless of intent, is understood to impurify the sacred precincts. The *korban* addresses that rupture independent of the sinner's culpability.

This is Rav and Shmuel's conceptual foundation: even one who is entirely blameless performed the act, and the act generates an obligation.

### Horayot Parallel

The *Horayot* parallel clarifies why Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish disagree. One might argue that in the *Horayot* case the individual was part of *Klal Yisrael* and acted as a member of the community responding to its legal authorities — a connection that generates at least some form of communal ownership of the act. A *tinok shenishba*, by contrast, had no connection to that community and no claim on its legal framework whatsoever. For Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, it is the total disconnection of the *tinok shenishba* that places him outside the scope of any *chatat* obligation.

### Rambam Ruling

Remarkably, the Rambam rules like Rav and Shmuel: a *tinok shenishba* who later learns of their Jewish identity brings one *chatat*. The Rambam describes this obligation as *ke-shogeg* — in the category of an inadvertent sinner — by which he appears to mean not that there is some element of negligence, but that the same legal category applies. What matters is not the presence of fault but the fact that one performed the act; the *chatat* obligation tracks the act, not the actor's moral state.

## Baraita Details

### Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָךְ הָיָה מוֹנְבַּז דָּן לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הוֹאִיל וּמֵזִיד קָרוּי "חוֹטֵא" וְשׁוֹגֵג קָרוּי "חוֹטֵא", מָה מֵזִיד שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה — אַף שׁוֹגֵג שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Munbaz deliberated before Rabbi Akiva as follows: Since** one who commits a transgression **intentionally is called** a **sinner** in the Torah **and** one who commits a transgression **unwittingly is called** a **sinner, just as** one who commits the transgression **intentionally** is liable for punishment only in a case **where he had** prior **knowledge** that it was prohibited, **so too,** one who commits the transgression **unwittingly** is liable to bring a sin-offering only in a case **where he had** prior **knowledge.**

A *baraita* now elaborates the position of the *mishna* in greater detail, providing concrete illustrations of the *tinok shenishba* case. The *baraita* opens identically to the *mishna* — *klal gadol amru ba-Shabbat* — and then specifies: the case of "forgetting the existence of Shabbat" is illustrated by *tinok shenishba le-vein ha-nochrim ve-ger shenitgayer bein ha-nochrim*, someone who spent their life among non-Jews and never knew of Shabbat. Such a person, upon learning of their situation, brings one *chatat* for all the Shabbat violations of their entire life.

### Multiple Categories

The *baraita* then extends this to other categories of prohibition. All the times such a person consumed blood — *achat*. All the violations of *chelev* — one *chatat*. All the acts of *avoda zara* — one *chatat*. The *baraita* is enumerating the thirty-six categories of sin for which a *chatat* is generally brought, and making clear that for each category, regardless of how many times the violation was repeated across a lifetime, the *tinok shenishba* brings one offering per category, not one per incident. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* about categories applies here as well.

### Munbaz Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֲרֵינִי מוֹסִיף עַל דְּבָרֶיךָ: אִי מָה מֵזִיד שֶׁהָיְתָה הַיְּדִיעָה בִּשְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה — אַף שׁוֹגֵג שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה בִּשְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: I will elaborate upon your statement** and follow your reasoning to its logical conclusion and thereby test the validity of your reasoning. **If so, just as** one who commits the transgression **intentionally** is liable for punishment only in a case **where** he had **the awareness** that he was sinning **at the time** that he performed **the action, so too,** with regard to one who commits the transgression **unwittingly,** say that he is only liable to bring a sin-offering in a case **where he had awareness** that he was sinning **at the time** that he performed **the action.** If that is the case, it is no longer an unwitting transgression.

The *baraita* notes that Munbaz articulated this position before Rabbi Akiva, suggesting that the debate between them is the very debate that underlies the disagreement between Rav and Shmuel on one side and Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on the other.

## Akiva vs Munbaz

### Chotei Linking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁהוֹסַפְתָּ. אָמַר לוֹ: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, אֵין זֶה קָרוּי שׁוֹגֵג אֶלָּא מֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Munbaz **said to him: Yes,** there is nothing unusual about that. In my opinion it is correct **and all the more so** now that **you have elaborated** upon my statement. Awareness at the time that one is performing the action is one of the criteria of my definition of an unwitting transgression, as will be explained below. Rabbi Akiva **said to him: According to your statement,** since while performing the action one is aware that it is prohibited, his action **is not called unwitting; rather,** it is a full-fledged **intentional** transgression.

Munbaz's argument is built on a verbal *heikesh*, a juxtaposition of terms. He observes that in one place the *Torah* uses the word *chotei* (sinner) to describe a *meizid* (intentional transgressor), and in another context uses the same word for a *shogeg* (inadvertent transgressor). He infers: *mah meizid hayta lo yedi'ah, af shogeg hayta lo yedi'ah* — just as by the *meizid* there is knowledge, so too by the *shogeg* there must be knowledge. A *shogeg* who never had any awareness that the act was prohibited cannot truly be called a *shogeg* and therefore incurs no *chatat* obligation.

### Akiva Response

Rabbi Akiva responds with a *kal va-chomer* that turns Munbaz's argument against itself. *Hareni mosif al devarecha* — let me extend your reasoning further. If you want to compare *shogeg* to *meizid* in terms of knowledge, consider that *meizid* possesses not just prior awareness but knowledge *be-sha'at ma'aseh* — at the very moment of transgression. Following your logic, a *shogeg* would likewise need to know *at the moment of the act* that he is violating. But that would make him a *meizid*. And by Munbaz's logic, even someone who knows while committing the act that it is forbidden could be called a *shogeg* and bring a *chatat*. Munbaz accepts this conclusion — *hen, ve-chol sheken she-osafta* — yes, and that is actually a valid point: there are circumstances where one acts knowing the act is forbidden and yet remains in the category of *shogeg*. This response, however, only deepens the problem; for Rabbi Akiva's interlocutor, a person who knows at the moment of transgression is not a *shogeg* at all but a *meizid*, and Munbaz's response — that one can know and still be *shogeg* — strikes the *tanna kamma* as untenable.

### Baraita Affirmed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא "כֵּיצַד תִּינוֹק". בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל נִיחָא. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, קַשְׁיָא! אָמְרִי לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לָא מִי אִיכָּא מוֹנְבַּז דְּפָטַר? אֲנַן דְּאָמְרִינַן כְּמוֹנְבַּז.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to our issue: **In any case,** as an example of one who forgot the essence of Shabbat, **it was taught: How so? A child** who was taken captive. **Granted,** according **to** the opinion of **Rav and Shmuel** it works out **well,** as they consider the legal status of a child taken captive equal to that of one who unwittingly forgot the essence of Shabbat. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** who consider the legal status of a child taken captive equal to that of one who committed the action due to circumstances beyond his control and is therefore exempt, **it is difficult** because he is liable to bring a sin-offering according to the opinion of the Rabbis in the *baraita*. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** could have **said to you: Isn't there** the opinion of **Munbaz** who **deemed him exempt** in that case? **We stated** our opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Munbaz.**

The *gemara* then affirms that this *baraita* supports the position of Rav and Shmuel over Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. The *baraita* takes the *tinok shenishba* as the prime example of "forgetting the existence of Shabbat" and assigns that person a *chatat* — the position of Rabbi Akiva and the *tanna kamma*. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, however, align themselves with Munbaz — a *da'at yachid*, a minority opinion, and a relatively obscure one at that, set against the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Akiva. The *gemara* acknowledges the asymmetry: to go against Rabbi Akiva and the *tanna kamma* in favor of Munbaz is a significant choice, but Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish were persuaded that the logic of exempting the entirely unknowing was compelling.

## Knowledge Requirement

### Kares Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְמוֹנְבַּז? דִּכְתִיב: "תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ "וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה״ — הִקִּישׁ שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד. מָה מֵזִיד שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה, אַף שׁוֹגֵג שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ יְדִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale for** the opinion of **Munbaz?** Is it based entirely upon the fact that the Torah refers to sinners, both intentional and unwitting, as sinners? The Gemara explains that the source for the opinion of Munbaz is **as it is written:** "The native of the children of Israel, and the stranger who lives among them, **there shall be one law for you, for one who acts unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:29), **and adjacent to it** is the verse: **"And the person who acts with a high hand,** whether a native or a stranger, he blasphemes God, and that soul shall be cut off from the midst of his people" (Numbers 15:30). The Torah **juxtaposes unwitting** transgression **to intentional** transgression. **Just as** one who commits the transgression **intentionally** is only liable in a case **where he had** prior **knowledge, so too,** one who commits the transgression **unwittingly** is only liable in a case **where he had** prior **knowledge.**

The *gemara* now clarifies the actual *derasha* through which the rabbis understand when a *chatat* is required, and why it differs from what Munbaz derived. The starting point is a general principle: a *chatat* is brought only for sins whose intentional commission is punishable by *kares*. This principle is learned from a juxtaposition in the *Torah* in the context of *avoda zara*.

### Juxtaposition

The *Torah* writes *Torah achas yihiyeh lachem la-oseh bi-shgaga* — one law for those who act in error — and then immediately juxtaposes this to *ve-ha-nefesh asher ta'aseh be-yad ramah... et Hashem hu megadef*, the case of one who acts presumptuously and thereby blaspheming God, a case that carries *kares*. This juxtaposition is between the *shogeg* who brings a *chatat* and the *meizid* who receives *kares*.

### Rabbinic Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי "תּוֹרָה אַחַת״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְמַקְרֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי לִבְרֵיהּ: "תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה״, וּכְתִיב: "וְכִי תִשְׁגּוּ וְלֹא תַעֲשׂוּ אֵת כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת הָאֵלֶּה״, וּכְתִיב: "וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה״. הוּקְשׁוּ כּוּלָּם לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: מָה לְהַלָּן דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִים עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what do the Rabbis do with** the juxtaposition derived from **that** verse: **One law?** The Gemara answers: **They require it for that which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught his son.** It is written: **"There shall be one law for you, for one who acts unwittingly." And it is written: "And if you err, and do not perform all these commandments** that God spoke to Moses" (Numbers 15:22). **And it is written: "And the person who acts with a high hand,** he blasphemes God and that soul shall be cut off from the midst of his people" (Numbers 15:30), from which we learn that **all** the mitzvot **are** derived from this **juxtaposition to idolatry. Just as there,** with regard to idolatry, the reference is to **a matter which, for its intentional** violation, **one is liable to** be punished with ***karet*,** as it is stated: "And that soul shall be cut off," **and for its unwitting** violation **one is liable to** bring **a sin-offering; so too, any matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** be punished with ***karet*, for its unwitting** violation **one is liable to** bring **a sin-offering.**

Munbaz reads this juxtaposition as establishing a knowledge requirement: just as *meizid* implies prior awareness, so must *shogeg*. The *rabanan*, however, read it differently and more precisely. *Hukshu kulam le-avoda zara* — the entire framework of *chatat* is linked to the paradigm of *avoda zara*: *mah le-hallan davar she-chayavim al zedono kares u-veshigagaso chatat* — what characterizes the paradigm is that intentional commission brings *kares*, and inadvertent commission brings a *chatat*. From this the *rabanan* derive the universal rule: a *chatat* is owed only for a category of sin that, when done intentionally, carries *kares*. Shabbat violation is precisely such a sin.

What the juxtaposition teaches, on the rabbinic reading, is not that the *shogeg* must have had prior knowledge — it is that the relevant criterion for *chatat* liability is the severity of the sin, not the cognitive state of the sinner. Any sin carrying *kares* for the intentional violator generates a *chatat* for the inadvertent one, regardless of the degree of the violator's prior awareness.

## Modern Application

### Karaites

The preceding discussion concerned itself with the purely halachic question of whether a *tinok shenishba* brings a *chatat* offering — a question most directly relevant in the era of the *Beit Ha-Mikdash*. Yet the conceptual category of *tinok shenishba* has had a long and consequential afterlife in halachic discourse, particularly in the Rambam's thinking and its reception by later *poskim*.

The Rambam's engagement with *tinok shenishba* began in the context of the Karaites. In his earlier writings, the Rambam was sharply hostile to Karaites — suggesting they had no share in the *Olam Ha-Ba*, denying them the status of members of *Klal Yisrael* in meaningful senses. Later in his life, following more positive encounters with certain Karaite communities, the Rambam revised his tone significantly. He argued that one cannot hold Karaites responsible for their rejection of rabbinic tradition, because they were raised within that tradition and educated to believe that the Karaite interpretation was correct and the rabbinic one mistaken. They are therefore in the halachic position of a *tinok shenishba*: their errors are those of people who genuinely did not know otherwise, not of willful rebels.

### Shulchan Aruch

The *Beit Yosef* extended the Rambam's application of *tinok shenishba* further. In a range of practical halachic contexts where a *mechalel Shabbat* would ordinarily be subject to various exclusions and restrictions, the *Beit Yosef* and subsequently the *Shulchan Aruch* held that these sanctions do not apply to Karaites, precisely because their Shabbat violations flow from sincere — if mistaken — religious conviction formed through upbringing and education. They believe they are right and we are wrong; they are not rebels but *tinokei shenishbu*.

### Rav Kook

In the modern period, as large portions of the Jewish people moved away from traditional observance — often through the influence of secular ideologies and a broader societal context that had abandoned religious frameworks — Rav Kook and other later authorities extended the *tinok shenishba* category significantly. Rav Kook argued that the non-observant Jews of the modern era, even those raised in nominally traditional homes, are in a meaningful sense *tinokei shenishbu*, because the overwhelming influence of surrounding society effectively displaced the religious formation that would otherwise have taken hold.

### Inclusivity Tension

This extension of *tinok shenishba* is not without difficulty. It is, as the *gemara* itself illustrates, a category generated in the context of someone who genuinely had no access whatsoever to Jewish identity or knowledge. Extending it to individuals who have access to traditional Judaism but choose — based on upbringing, education, or the ambient culture — not to embrace it introduces a degree of tension. On one hand, the extension is inclusive and compassionate, refraining from categorizing vast numbers of Jews as deliberate rebels against Torah. On the other hand, it carries a patronizing dimension: it effectively says that even a person who has encountered traditional Judaism and consciously rejected it, or who maintains a different form of Jewish identity, is not truly making a free and informed choice — they are simply the product of forces they cannot see past.

The *gemara's* discussion, it should be noted, focuses on the narrow question of *chatat* liability. Interestingly, the Rambam rules like Rav and Shmuel that even a *tinok shenishba* brings a *chatat*, and yet the Rambam is the very authority invoked to extend leniency in other areas. The concept of *tinok shenishba* thus operates on two levels: as a halachic category for *korban* liability, and as a broader conceptual framework — *ke-onuss*, as the Rambam frames it — indicating that the person cannot be held to full moral accountability for conduct that flowed from circumstances entirely beyond their control. It is this second sense, the Rambam's use of *tinok shenishba* as an expression of *ones*, that has proven most consequential for contemporary halachic discourse.
