# Pesachim 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tisha B'Av Melacha

### Tisha B'Av Customs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלָאכָה בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב — עוֹשִׂין, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלָאכָה — אֵין עוֹשִׂין, וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים בְּטֵלִים. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם עַצְמוֹ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In **a place where** people **were accustomed to perform labor on the Ninth of Av, one performs** labor. In **a place where** people **were accustomed not to perform labor,** one does **not perform** labor. **And in all places Torah scholars are idle** and do not perform labor on the Ninth of Av, due to the mourning over the Temple's destruction. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** With regard to the Ninth of Av, **a person should always conduct himself** as **a Torah scholar** and refrain from performing labor.

The daf begins by picking up from the *mishna* on 54b, which addresses regional *minhagim* surrounding *melacha* on *Tisha B'Av*. The *mishna* states: *makom shenahagu laasot melacha b'Tisha B'Av osin* — in a place where the custom is to permit *melacha* on *Tisha B'Av*, one may do it; *makom shenahagu shelo laasot melacha ein osin* — where that is not the custom, one does not do it. This is striking, because in certain respects *Tisha B'Av* carries the character of *aveilut*, and indeed the mourning intensifies as the day itself approaches. We might have expected a uniform prohibition. Yet we find different practices, and *melacha* is not strictly forbidden across the board.

### Talmidei Chachamim

The *mishna* continues: *uv'chol makom talmidei chachamim batelim* — in all places, *talmidei chachamim* do not perform *melacha*. They devote the day to *kinnot*, learning, and matters connected to the mourning. This reflects a reality both ancient and contemporary: there are those who make a point of treating *Tisha B'Av* as a day fully devoted to mourning, even when it falls on a weekday that would otherwise demand professional obligations.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* adds: *l'olam yaaseh adam atzmo talmid chacham* — a person should always regard himself as a *talmid chacham* in the sense that he adopts greater stringencies upon himself. The word *talmid chacham* here means a disciple of the sages — not necessarily a towering *gadol*, but one from the class of *Torah* students whose entire orientation and time are devoted to religious pursuits. Such people tend naturally toward *chumra* and, practically speaking, often have the opportunity to observe it as well.

### Shmuel & Babylon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין תַּעֲנִית צִיבּוּר בְּבָבֶל אֶלָּא תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב בִּלְבַד. לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ אָסוּר? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ מוּתָּר. וְכִי תֵּימָא קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל תַּעֲנִית צִיבּוּר בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ מוּתָּר, וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: ״אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם״. לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי, לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת? לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel said: The only communal fast in Babylonia** during which all the stringencies of a communal fast are observed **is the Ninth of Av.** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say,** based on the parallel he drew between them, **that Shmuel holds** that **the Ninth of Av** is as stringent as communal fast days, in that **during twilight** on the Ninth of Av all activities prohibited on the Ninth of Av are **prohibited? But didn't Shmuel say:** During **twilight of the Ninth of Av** all activities prohibited on the Ninth of Av are **permitted,** and the Sages did not decree any prohibitions during this time? **And if you say that Shmuel holds:** With regard to **every communal fast, during twilight** those activities considered to be afflictions are **permitted, didn't we learn** in a mishna with regard to a public fast day: **One may eat and drink while it is still day?** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What** does the expression: While it is still day, come **to exclude? What, isn't it to exclude twilight** of a communal fast day, when these activities are prohibited? The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is **to exclude** the time **after dark,** when these afflictions are certainly in effect.

The *gemara* opens with a teaching of *Shmuel*: *ein ta'anit tzibur b'Bavel ela Tisha B'Av bilvad* — there is no *ta'anit tzibur* in *Bavel* except for *Tisha B'Av* alone. *Ta'anit tzibur* in its technical sense — as developed in *Masechet Ta'anit* — refers to the full-blown public fast declared in response to a drought, which, after a series of escalating cycles, becomes a complete day-fast accompanied by public prayer, additional *brachot* in the *Shmoneh Esrei*, *slichot*, and the prohibition of *melacha*. *Rashi* explains that *Bavel* is a water-rich region not dependent on rain in the way that *Eretz Yisrael* is. Therefore, either the need for such a fast will never arise there, or categorically, *Bavel* does not warrant that status even if rain were somehow needed. In either reading, the historical fasts — such as *Tzom Gedaliah* and *Shiva Asar B'Tammuz* — will never achieve the weight of a full *ta'anit tzibur* in *Bavel*. The one exception is *Tisha B'Av*, which is not rain-based and which alone is observed there as a complete fast with all the stringencies of *ta'anit tzibur*.

### Bein Hashmashos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: אֵין בֵּין תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב לְיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה סְפֵיקוֹ אָסוּר, וְזֶה סְפֵיקוֹ מוּתָּר. מַאי סְפֵיקוֹ מוּתָּר — לָאו בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת? לָא, כִּדְאָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: לִקְבִיעָא דְיַרְחָא, הָכָא נָמֵי — לִקְבִיעָא דְיַרְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this *baraita* **supports** Shmuel's opinion: **The only** difference **between the Ninth of Av and Yom Kippur is that** with regard to **this,** Yom Kippur, **its uncertainty is prohibited,** as eating and drinking on Yom Kippur is prohibited by Torah law, **whereas** with regard to that, the Ninth of Av, **its uncertainty** is **permitted,** as the afflictions of the Ninth of Av are rabbinic decrees. **What** is the meaning of the expression: With regard to that, the Ninth of Av, **its uncertainty** is **permitted?** Is it **not** referring to **twilight,** with regard to which there is uncertainty whether it is day or night? Apparently, it is permitted to eat during twilight on the Ninth of Av. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is **as Rav Sheisha, son** of **Rav Idi, said** in a different context: It is referring to uncertainty with regard to the **determination of the** first day of the new **month,** which would require observance of the Festival for two days. **Here, too,** the *baraita* is referring to uncertainty with regard to **determination of** the first day of the new **month.** Since the Ninth of Av is a fast of rabbinic origin, there is no requirement to observe two days.

The *gemara* initially assumes that *Shmuel*'s equation of *Tisha B'Av* with *ta'anit tzibur* implies that the *bein hashmashot* period — the twilight transition — is forbidden on *Tisha B'Av*, just as we treat *bein hashmashot* stringently for *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. However, *Shmuel* himself also stated that one may eat during *bein hashmashot* of *Tisha B'Av*. The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by suggesting that perhaps *Shmuel* holds that *bein hashmashot* is permitted on every full-day *ta'anit tzibur*, since they are all *drabbanan*. But this is challenged from a *mishna* in *Ta'anit* that states *ochlin v'shotin mib'od yom* — eating and drinking must cease from the day before — implying that *bein hashmashot* is excluded from the permitted eating period.

The *gemara* deflects this challenge: *lo, l'me'utei mishechashicha* — the *mishna* is not emphasizing the *bein hashmashot* boundary but rather contrasting the full-blown fast from the earlier rain-based fasts, where eating the prior night was permitted. The key distinction is whether the fast begins at night or only in the morning — not whether *bein hashmashot* specifically is forbidden. Thus the *gemara* reconciles *Shmuel*'s statements: both for *ta'anit tzibur* and for *Tisha B'Av*, eating during *bein hashmashot* may be permitted according to *Shmuel*.

## Three Fast Types

### Full-Day Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: עוּבָּרוֹת וּמְנִיקוֹת — מִתְעַנּוֹת וּמַשְׁלִימוֹת בּוֹ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמִּתְעַנּוֹת וּמַשְׁלִימוֹת בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וּבֵין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ אָסוּר. וְכֵן אָמְרוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב אֵינוֹ כְּתַעֲנִית צִיבּוּר. מַאי לָאו, לְבֵין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת? לֹא, לִמְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: Pregnant women and nursing women fast and complete** the fast **on** the Ninth of Av **in the manner that they fast and complete** the fast **on Yom Kippur, and** during **twilight on** the Ninth of Av **it is prohibited** to eat or drink. **And they likewise said** so **in the name** of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say that?** Didn't **Rabbi Yoḥanan say: The Ninth of Av is not like a communal fast** decreed to pray for rain? **What, isn't it** referring to the matter of **twilight?** Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that it is permitted to eat and drink during twilight on the Ninth of Av. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to performing **labor,** which is prohibited on the Ninth of Av, in contrast to other fasts.

The *gemara* now attempts to support *Shmuel*'s position that *bein hashmashot* of *Tisha B'Av* is permitted. It cites a *braita*: *ein bein Tisha B'Av l'Yom HaKippurim ella shezeh sefeiko assur v'zeh sefeiko mutar* — the only difference between *Tisha B'Av* and *Yom Kippur* is that the doubtful period of one is forbidden and the doubtful period of the other is permitted. The immediate inference is that this *safek* refers to *bein hashmashot* — directly supporting *Shmuel*'s position that *bein hashmashot* is permitted on *Tisha B'Av*.

### Safek Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָסוּר לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיּוֹשִׁיט אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּמַיִם בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָסוּר לְהוֹשִׁיט אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. מְסַיַּיע לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָסוּר לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיּוֹשִׁיט אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּמַיִם בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָסוּר לְהוֹשִׁיט אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. מֵיתִיבִי: אֵין בֵּין תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב לְתַעֲנִית צִיבּוּר אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה וְזֶה מוּתָּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ. הָא לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם — זֶה וָזֶה שָׁוִין. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי תַּעֲנִית צִיבּוּר תַּנְיָא: כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ ״אָסוּר בִּרְחִיצָה״, לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ, אֲבָל פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו — לֹא! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּנָּא קוּלֵּי קוּלֵּי קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This statement **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said:** It is **prohibited for a person to extend his finger into water on the Ninth of Av, just as it is prohibited** for him **to extend his finger into water on Yom Kippur.** The Gemara **raises an objection: The only** difference **between the Ninth of Av and a communal fast is that** on **this** day, a communal fast, **performance of labor is prohibited, and on that** day, the Ninth of Av, **performance of labor is permitted in a place where** people **are accustomed** to perform labor. By inference, with regard to **all** other **matters this and that are equal. However, with regard to a communal fast, it was taught** in a *baraita*: **When** the Sages **said** that **bathing is prohibited, they said** it **only** with regard to washing **one's entire body, but** with regard to washing **one's face, one's hands, and one's feet** in increments, the Sages did **not** say that it was prohibited. Apparently, with respect to washing, the Rabbis were stricter with regard to the Ninth of Av than they were with regard to a communal fast. **Rav Pappa said: The *tanna* is teaching** a series of **leniencies.**

The *gemara*, however, does not accept this inference straightforwardly. It invokes an alternative interpretation in the name of *Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi*: *lo, l'kavia d'yarcha* — the *safek* referred to is not *bein hashmashot* but a genuine calendrical doubt about which day is *Rosh Chodesh*. In the era of the *Beis HaMikdash*, if one genuinely did not know which day was *Rosh Chodesh*, a real doubt would arise as to the date of *Yom Kippur*, and one would be required to fast both days. No such obligation applies to *Tisha B'Av* — one would never be required to keep two days. *Tosafot* makes clear that the general practice of *sfeika de'yoma* — keeping two days due to doubt — does not apply to *Yom Kippur*, and indeed there is an obvious reason: who would fast for two full consecutive days? The *gemara* acknowledges the reference to the extraordinary circumstances of the *Mir Yeshiva* in Shanghai during the Second World War, where a handful of students, uncertain about where to draw the international date line, were indeed stringent and fasted two full days of *Yom Kippur*. But this is a remarkable exception, not a general practice.

The *gemara* thus deflects the proof from the *braita*: the *safek* referred to is the calendrical doubt, not *bein hashmashot*. Admittedly, this is a somewhat strained reading, since the calendrical doubt is an unusual hypothetical — particularly so in the context of *Tisha B'Av*, where the presence of the *Beis HaMikdash* would itself dissolve the fast.

The *gemara* now shifts focus. If the *braita* implies that *Tisha B'Av* and *Yom Kippur* are otherwise equivalent in all their practices, this supports *Rabbi Elazar*, who ruled: *assur lo l'adam she'yoshit etzba'o b'mayim b'Tisha B'Av* — it is forbidden to extend even one's finger into water on *Tisha B'Av*, just as on *Yom Kippur* — *k'derech she'assur lo l'hoshit etzba'o b'Yom HaKippurim*. This is more stringent than the bathing rules of *ta'anit tzibur*, where face, hands, and feet — *panav yadav v'raglav* — are permitted.

### Pregnant Women

*Rava* expounds: *uberos umenikot mit'anot umashlimot bo* — pregnant and nursing women must fast and complete the full fast on *Tisha B'Av*, just as they do on *Yom Kippur*. This differs from the minor historical fast days, where even a generally healthy pregnant woman is exempt. On *Tisha B'Av*, the threshold is significantly higher — though whether one must reach a level of actual *sakana* or whether some intermediate threshold applies is a separate question not fully resolved here. What is clear is the basic rule: the fast is complete, parallel to *Yom Kippur*.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים וְכוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: לָא חָיְשִׁינַן לְיוּהֲרָא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: חָיְשִׁינַן לְיוּהֲרָא? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ, דִּתְנַן: חָתָן, אִם יִרְצֶה לִקְרוֹת קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן — קוֹרֵא. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אֲמַר: לֹא כׇּל הָרוֹצֶה לִיטּוֹל אֶת הַשֵּׁם יִטּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna: **And in all places Torah scholars are idle** and do not perform labor on the Ninth of Av, and according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel one should always conduct himself like a Torah scholar in this regard and refrain from performing labor. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that **we are not concerned about presumptuousness** when a person conducts himself like a Torah scholar? **And** conversely, do **the Rabbis hold** that **we are concerned about presumptuousness? Didn't we hear them** say **the opposite? As we learned** in a mishna: With regard to the recitation of *Shema* on one's wedding night, the Rabbis said that **if a groom wishes to recite *Shema* on the first night** despite his exemption, he may do so. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Not everyone who wishes to assume the reputation** of a God-fearing person **may assume** it, and consequently not everyone who wishes to recite *Shema* on his wedding night may do so. Their opinions in that mishna appear contrary to their opinions in the current mishna.

*Rava* concludes his exposition by stating that *bein hashmashot* of *Tisha B'Av* is forbidden — *u'vein hashmashot shelo assur*. This places *Rava* in direct disagreement with *Shmuel*, holding that *Tisha B'Av* resembles *Yom Kippur* in that the twilight period is prohibited.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוּחְלֶפֶת הַשִּׁיטָה. רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ: דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עָבְדִי מְלָאכָה וְאִיהוּ לָא עָבֵיד — מִיחְזֵי כְּיוּהֲרָא, אֲבָל הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קָרֵי וְאִיהוּ נָמֵי קָרֵי — לָא מִיחְזֵי כְּיוּהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The** attribution of the **opinions is reversed** in one of the sources. **Rav Sheisha, son** of **Rav Idi, said: Do not reverse** either text, as it is possible to resolve the difficulty in another manner. The contradiction between the statement of **the Rabbis** here **and** the statement of **the Rabbis** there is **not difficult. Here,** on the Ninth of Av, **since everyone is performing labor and he is not performing labor,** his idleness is conspicuous and **appears like presumptuousness. However, there,** in the case of reciting *Shema* on one's wedding night, **it does not appear like presumptuousness, as everyone is reciting** *Shema* **and he is also reciting** it with them.

The *gemara* reports that the same position — that *bein hashmashot* of *Tisha B'Av* is forbidden — was also stated in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*. But this prompts a challenge: *Rabbi Yochanan* himself stated that *Tisha B'Av eino k'ta'anit tzibur* — *Tisha B'Av* does not have the weight of a *ta'anit tzibur*. If the initial assumption of the *gemara* was that equating *Tisha B'Av* with *ta'anit tzibur* signaled that *bein hashmashot* is forbidden, then *Rabbi Yochanan*'s statement distancing *Tisha B'Av* from *ta'anit tzibur* should imply the opposite — that *bein hashmashot* is permitted.

The *gemara* therefore asks: *mai lav l'bein hashmashot* — does *Rabbi Yochanan* not mean that the distinction between them is precisely the *bein hashmashot* issue? The *gemara* rejects this: *lo, l'melacha* — no, the distinction *Rabbi Yochanan* draws concerns *melacha*. On *ta'anit tzibur*, *melacha* is forbidden; on *Tisha B'Av*, it is in principle permitted, as the *mishna* established. But this, responds the *gemara*, adds nothing new — the *mishna* already told us that *melacha* on *Tisha B'Av* is a matter of regional *minhag* and not strict prohibition. *Aval meitsar lo assar* — no one actually prohibited *melacha* on *Tisha B'Av*. So the statement of *Rabbi Yochanan* must mean something else.

The *gemara* offers two liturgical explanations. First: *l'tefillat ne'ila* — on a full-blown *ta'anit tzibur*, an additional prayer of *ne'ila* was recited, as we know from *Masechet Ta'anit*. *Tisha B'Av* does not carry this obligation. One might object that *Rabbi Yochanan* himself said *ul'vai sheyitpalel adam veholech kol hayom kulo* — would that a person would pray the entire day — implying no restriction on additional *tefillah*. But the *gemara* resolves this: *hatam chova, hacha reshut* — there, in *ta'anit tzibur*, *ne'ila* is an obligatory part of the liturgy, a formal fourth *tefillah*; here, on *Tisha B'Av*, additional prayer is merely voluntary.

A second explanation is offered: *l'esrim v'arba* — on a full *ta'anit tzibur*, six additional *brachot* of petition and supplication are inserted into the *Shmoneh Esrei*, bringing it to twenty-four. This too is not practiced on *Tisha B'Av*. The underlying distinction makes sense: the *ne'ila* and the twenty-four *brachot* belong to the structure of rain-based petitionary fasting, where the entire community stands together before God in urgent collective *bakasha*. *Tisha B'Av* is historical and mourning-based in character — not the same kind of pressing petition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אַדְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָתָם הוּא דְּבָעֵינַן כַּוּוֹנָה, וַאֲנַן סָהֲדֵי דְּלָא מָצֵי כַּוּוֹנֵי דַּעְתֵּיהּ — מִיחְזֵי כְּיוּהֲרָא: אֲבָל הָכָא לָא מִיחְזֵי כְּיוּהֲרָא, אָמְרִי: מְלָאכָה הִיא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, פּוֹק חֲזִי כַּמָּה בַּטְלָנֵי אִיכָּא בְּשׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the contradiction **between** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** here **and** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** there is **not difficult. There,** in the case of reciting *Shema* on one's wedding night, **it is that we require concentration, and it is clear to all** that **he is unable to concentrate** because of his preoccupation with the mitzva that he must perform. Therefore, if he recites *Shema* **it appears like presumptuousness.** It is as though he is announcing: I am able to concentrate although others in my situation are not. **However, here,** by not performing labor on the Ninth of Av **it does not appear like presumptuousness,** as people **say: It is** because **he has no labor** to perform. **Go out and see how many idle people there are in the marketplace,** even on days when it is permitted to perform labor.

*Rav Pappa* then offers yet another reading: *ino k'rishonot ella k'achronot* — *Tisha B'Av* is not like the earlier, smaller rain-based fasts, but it is like the final, full-blown stage of the rain cycle. This effectively reinterprets *Rabbi Yochanan* to align him with *Rava*: *Tisha B'Av* is as severe as the last stage of *ta'anit tzibur*, and *bein hashmashot* is forbidden.

The *braita* is then cited again as a challenge: *ein bein Tisha B'Av l'Yom HaKippurim ella shezeh sefeiko assur v'zeh sefeiko mutar* — and the question recurs whether *safek* means *bein hashmashot* or the calendrical doubt of *Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi*. The *gemara* returns to the same answer: *lo, l'kavia d'yarcha* — it refers to the genuine calendar doubt, not *bein hashmashot*. In sum: there is a genuine debate among the *rishonim* as to how we rule on eating during *bein hashmashot* of *Tisha B'Av* upon its entry, and this debate is rooted in the unresolved tensions of this *sugya*.

The *gemara* then raises a broader inference from the *braita*: if *Tisha B'Av* and *Yom Kippur* are alike in all other respects, and *Rabbi Elazar*'s ruling is supported, then a further comparison becomes relevant. A second *braita* states: *ein bein Tisha B'Av l'ta'anit tzibur ella shezeh assur b'asiyat melacha v'zeh mutar b'asiyat melacha* — the only difference between *Tisha B'Av* and *ta'anit tzibur* is the question of *melacha*: on *ta'anit tzibur*, *melacha* is forbidden; on *Tisha B'Av*, it is technically permitted. This establishes a way in which *ta'anit tzibur* is stricter than *Tisha B'Av*.

### Bathing Rules

The picture that emerges is not a simple continuum. *Ta'anit tzibur* prohibits *melacha* and is likely stricter regarding *bein hashmashot*, but is more lenient in bathing. *Tisha B'Av* prohibits *melacha* only as a matter of regional *minhag*, is debated regarding *bein hashmashot*, but is as strict as *Yom Kippur* in bathing. Each fast type has its own character and its own logic. The prohibition of *melacha* on *ta'anit tzibur* serves the urgent communal dimension — everyone gathered in the streets, petitioning together for rain. *Tisha B'Av*'s *aveilut* dimension is weighty and real, yet lacks the same pressing urgency, and hence *melacha* is treated differently. The bathing restriction, by contrast, has an *aveilut* component that intensifies *Tisha B'Av*'s stringency beyond what *ta'anit tzibur* demands.

### Work Restrictions

The *gemara* returns to the *mishna*'s statement that *talmidei chachamim* refrain from *melacha* everywhere, and that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says everyone should adopt this practice. The *gemara* infers that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds *lo chashinan l'yuhara* — we are not concerned that such behavior will appear ostentatious — while the Rabbis who restrict this to *talmidei chachamim* hold *chashinan l'yuhara* — we are concerned about the appearance of sanctimony.

The *gemara* then notes a tension with another context. In the *mishna* concerning a *chassan* on his wedding night: the *tanna kamma* says that if he wishes to recite *krias shema* he may do so, despite being exempt due to distraction; *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says *lo kol harotzeh litol es hashem yitol* — not everyone who wishes to take upon himself the reputation of piety should be entitled to. There, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* is the one concerned about religious ostentation — the reverse of his position in our *mishna*.

## Yuhara Concept

### Self-Stringency

The *gemara* thus finds the positions apparently reversed. *Rabbi Yochanan* responds: *muchelefes hashita* — reverse the attributions in our *mishna*. If *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* in the *chassan* case is worried about *yuhara*, then logically the Rabbis are the ones in our *mishna* who say everyone should refrain from *melacha*, and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* is the one who limits it to *talmidei chachamim*.

### Context Matters

*Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi* disagrees: *lo tehafech* — do not reverse the positions. There is no contradiction, and both can be resolved as originally stated. *D'rabbanan ad'rabbanan lo kashya*: the Rabbis here and the Rabbis there adopt different positions without inconsistency. The key is context. In the case of *Tisha B'Av*, if everyone in the city is performing *melacha* and you alone refrain, *mechazi k'yuhara* — it looks like you are displaying piety ostentatiously. But in the case of *krias shema*, everyone else is also reciting it. If you recite it as well, you are doing exactly what everyone else does, and there is nothing conspicuous about it — *lo mechazi k'yuhara*.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* similarly presents no contradiction. In the *krias shema* case, the *chassan* needs *kavanah* and it is well known that he cannot achieve it on his wedding night. Everyone watching knows he is a *chassan* tonight. If he recites *krias shema*, people will say he is acting as though he has *kavanah* when demonstrably he does not — that is a visible act of *yuhara*. By contrast, in the *melacha* case, if someone simply sits and does not work, no one can know the reason. He might simply have no work today.

### Idle Workers

This final point is made explicit: *amar melacha hu d'leis lei* — people will assume he simply has no work. *Puk chazi kamma batlanei ika b'shuka* — go outside and look at how many people in the marketplace are idle, not because of piety but simply because they have nothing to do at that moment. He does not stand out as someone making a show of religious stringency.

The *sugya* raises a broader question about the concept of *yuhara* in *halacha*: on one hand, religious striving and *chumra* can encourage genuine growth and aspiration; on the other hand, the concern is twofold — the communal problem of standing out in a way that alienates others or invites resentment, and the personal problem of using visible *chumrot* as a substitute for working on genuine deficiencies. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension abstractly but addresses it case by case, distinguishing when context makes behavior conspicuous and when it does not.

## Erev Pesach Work

### Judea/Galilee

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּיהוּדָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂים מְלָאכָה בְּעַרְבֵי פְסָחִים עַד חֲצוֹת, וּבַגָּלִיל לֹא הָיוּ עוֹשִׂים כׇּל עִיקָּר. הַלַּיְלָה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹסְרִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין עַד הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Apropos the discussion of performing labor on Passover eve, differences in other customs were cited. **And the Rabbis say: In Judea,** people **would perform labor on Passover eves until midday, and in the Galilee** people **would not perform** labor on Passover eve **at all.** With regard to performing labor on **the night** before Passover eve, the night between the thirteenth and fourteenth of Nisan, **Beit Shammai prohibit** performing labor, **and Beit Hillel permit** doing so **until sunrise.**

The *gemara* now transitions back to the main topic of the *mishna* concerning *minhag* and *melacha* on *Erev Pesach* in the morning. *Chatzot* is the boundary after which no *melacha* may be done, and this applies universally. The question is the morning period. *V'Chachamim omrim* — the Sages say: *bi-Yehudah hayu osim melachah be-arvei Pesachim ad chatzot u-va-Galil lo hayu osim kol ikar* — in *Yehudah*, the practice was to perform *melacha* on *Erev Pesach* up until *chatzot*; in the *Galil*, they did not do it at all.

### Shammai/Hillel

As for the night before: *Beit Shammai* forbids it, while *Beit Hillel* permits it until sunrise. The language here is striking — *osrim* and *matirin*, the vocabulary of prohibition and permission, rather than the *minhag*-language that has governed the discussion until this point.

### Minhag or Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מֵעִיקָּרָא תְּנָא מִנְהָגָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף תְּנָא אִיסּוּרָא! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בִּיהוּדָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂים מְלָאכָה בְּעַרְבֵי פְסָחִים עַד חֲצוֹת, וּבַגָּלִיל אֵינָם עוֹשִׂים כׇּל עִיקָּר. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מָה רְאָיָיה יְהוּדָה וְגָלִיל לְכָאן? אֶלָּא, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלָאכָה — עוֹשִׂים, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲשׂוֹת — אֵין עוֹשִׂים. מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִנְהָגָא — מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִיסּוּרָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks with regard to the mishna: **Initially,** at the beginning of the chapter, the *tanna* **taught** that in certain places there is merely a **custom** not to perform labor, **and** yet **ultimately,** in this latest mishna, **he taught that** according to the opinion of Beit Shammai, **it is prohibited** to perform labor. Apparently, performance of labor is not dependent on custom but is actually prohibited. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **not difficult,** since **that** first mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** and **this** current mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: In Judea they would perform labor on Passover eves until midday, and in the Galilee they would not perform** labor **at all. Rabbi Meir said to him: What proof** do you cite **from Judea and the Galilee to** the discussion **here? Rather,** in **a place where** people **were accustomed to perform labor, one performs** labor, **and** in **a place where** people **were accustomed not to perform** labor, **one does not perform** labor. The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Meir is speaking** about **custom, by inference, Rabbi Yehuda is speaking** about a **prohibition** against performing labor in the Galilee.

The *gemara* notes this shift: *me-ikara tani minhaga, u-ve-sof tani issura* — initially the text speaks of custom, and then suddenly it speaks in the language of halachic prohibition. Is this a *minhag* or an actual *din*? *Rabbi Yochanan* answers: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Meir, ha Rabbi Yehuda* — no difficulty. It is a dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* about the very nature of the practice.

### Meir vs. Yehuda

A *braita* is cited in which *Rabbi Yehuda* presents the *Yehudah/Galil* distinction, and *Rabbi Meir* responds: *mah re'ayah Yehudah ve-Galil le-kan* — why should I care what they do in *Yehudah* or *Galil*? The rule is simply that you follow the *minhag* of your locale, wherever you are. The *gemara* infers from this exchange that while *Rabbi Meir* treats the entire matter as purely *minhag*, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s invocation of regional practice implies something more: an actual *psak* that differs between regions. *Minhag* does not require you to cite regional precedent to validate it — you simply follow your locale. But if *Rabbi Yehuda* is presenting *Yehudah* and *Galil* as evidence of how one should rule, he must understand this as a question of *halacha*, where different authoritative *psakim* apply in different regions. Hence it is *Rabbi Yehuda* who is the author of the *mishna*'s *Yehudah/Galil* formulation, and he who leads into the *Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel* debate, which is framed in properly halachic language.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר מוּתָּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַמְנַכֵּשׁ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר וְנֶעְקְרָה בְּיָדוֹ — שׁוֹתְלָהּ בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּיט, וְאֵין שׁוֹתְלָהּ בִּמְקוֹם הַגְּרִיד. בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר — אִין, בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — לָא. מִכְּדִי שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הַרְכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת לִשְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — שׁוּב אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר מוּתָּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה, לְמָה לִי שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר? וְהָאִיכָּא אַרְבֵּיסַר וַחֲמֵיסַר וּמִקְצָת שִׁיתְּסַר! אָמַר רָבָא: בַּגָּלִיל שָׁנוּ. וְהָאִיכָּא לֵילְיָא! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כְּבֵית הִלֵּל — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם לְנַכֵּשׁ בַּלַּיְלָה. רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בִּיהוּדָה, וּבְהַשְׁרָשָׁה חַד ״מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ״ — אָמְרִינַן, תְּרֵי ״מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ״ — לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda hold that performance of labor on the fourteenth is permitted** everywhere? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **one who is weeding** a field on **the thirteenth** of Nisan **and** a stalk of grain **was uprooted in his hand, he plants it in a muddy place** so that it will take root before the *omer* offering is brought on the sixteenth of Nisan? It will then be permitted to eat the grain after the *omer* offering is brought. However, **one** should **not plant it in a dry place,** as it will not take root there immediately. If it begins to sprout only after the *omer* offering is brought, that grain will remain prohibited until after the following year's *omer* offering is brought. **On the thirteenth** of Nisan, **yes,** this is the *halakha*; however, **on the fourteenth,** one **may not** replant the stalk of grain. **Now, we learned** that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Any graft that does not take hold within three days will no longer take hold. If it could enter your mind** that **performing labor on the fourteenth is permitted, why** do **I** need this *halakha* to be taught specifically with regard to the **thirteenth?** It would have been a greater novelty had he taught the *halakha* with regard to a case that occurs on the fourteenth. **Aren't there** three days remaining for grain planted on the fourteenth to take root before the *omer* offering, i.e., **the fourteenth** of Nisan, **the fifteenth** of Nisan, **and part of the sixteenth** of Nisan? **Rava said: They taught** this *halakha* of replanting a stalk of wheat with regard to **the Galilee;** as mentioned in the *baraita*, Rabbi Yehuda says that in the Galilee they do not perform labor at all. **And isn't there the night** between the thirteenth and the fourteenth of Nisan, during which according to the opinion of Beit Hillel, labor is permitted even in the Galilee, which is the *halakha*? Rabbi Yehuda could have taught the *halakha* with regard to weeding on the night before the fourteenth. **Rav Sheshet said:** Rabbi Yehuda said this **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** who prohibit performing labor that night. **Rav Ashi said:** There is no reason to suggest implausibly that Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, contrary to the accepted *halakha*. **Actually,** Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance **with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel.** However, he did not teach the case about the night before the fourteenth of Nisan **because it is not the** typical **manner of people to weed at night.** It is virtually impossible to identify weeds in the dark. **Ravina said: Actually,** it can be explained that Rabbi Yehuda is referring **to Judea.** With regard to a plant **taking root, we state** only **once** the principle: The legal status of **part of the day is like** that of **the entire** day, but we **do not state twice** the principle: The legal status of **part of the day is like** that of **the entire** day. When discussing a plant that was replanted on the fourteenth, in the tally of three days, the legal status of part of both the fourteenth and the sixteenth cannot be like that of entire days. A plant takes root after a fixed amount of time, and this is not affected by formalistic halakhic principles like: The legal status of part of the day is like that of the entire day.

The *gemara* then challenges whether *Rabbi Yehuda* could ever hold that *melacha* is permitted at all on the morning of the fourteenth. A *braita* teaches that if one is weeding a wheat field on the thirteenth of *Nisan* and accidentally pulls up a stalk, one may replant it in muddy soil — where it will take root — but not in hard ground. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that any planting that does not take root within three days will not take root at all. The *gemara* asks: since the stalk needs only the fourteenth, fifteenth, and part of the sixteenth to take root, why does the *braita* specify the thirteenth? If *melacha* were permitted on the fourteenth, one could simply replant on the fourteenth. This implies *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *melacha* is forbidden on the fourteenth.

*Rava* answers: *bi-Galil shanu* — the *braita* speaks of the *Galil*, where no *melacha* is done, so naturally it specifies the thirteenth. *Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi* adds: and as for the night before, *Beit Shammai* prohibits it as well, so the *braita* reflects the most stringent position. *Rav Ashi* responds: even assuming *Beit Hillel*, it is simply not the practice of people to weed their fields at night — a practical observation rather than a halachic one. *Ravina* offers a further resolution: even in *Yehudah*, where some do *melacha* in the morning, the principle of *miktzas hayom kekulo* — a partial day counting as the whole — only applies once; one cannot apply it to two partial days simultaneously, and therefore the thirteenth is specified regardless.

The broader tension underlying the *Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel* dispute concerns how formally to treat the day. *Beit Shammai* treats the entire day including the night as a unit requiring preparation-mode from the outset. *Beit Hillel* takes a more practical view: the morning hours before *chatzot* are the pressing time for *hachanos*, and that is where the focus should be. The question of how to balance the formal dimension of sanctifying the entire day against the practical urgency of the morning is genuine and unresolved by neat formula.

The *gemara* also touches on the broader phenomenon by which *minhag* becomes *halachisized* — a process visible in *Erev Pesach* work restrictions, in *kitniyot*, and in the customs of *sefira*. Some practices remain clearly in the domain of *minhag* without acquiring formal halachic status; others are subjected to the full apparatus of *psak halacha*. What determines which category a practice falls into is itself an interesting question.

## Completing Work

### Two Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁהִתְחִיל בָּהּ קוֹדֶם לְאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, גּוֹמְרָהּ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר. אֲבָל לֹא יַתְחִיל בָּהּ בַּתְּחִלָּה בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְגוֹמְרָהּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלֹשׁ אוּמָּנִיּוֹת עוֹשִׂים מְלָאכָה בְּעַרְבֵי פְסָחִים עַד חֲצוֹת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַחַיָּיטִין, וְהַסַּפָּרִים, וְהַכּוֹבְסִין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף רַצְעָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Meir says:** With regard to **any labor that one began before the fourteenth** of Nisan, **he may complete it on the fourteenth** before midday. **However, one** may **not begin** to perform that labor **from the outset on the fourteenth, even if he is able to complete it** before midday. **And the Rabbis say:** The practitioners of only **three crafts** are permitted to **perform labor until midday on Passover eve, and they are: Tailors, barbers, and launderers,** whose work is needed for the Festival. **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says: Even shoemakers** are permitted to work on the fourteenth.

The *mishna* now states: *Rabbi Meir omer kol melacha she'hitchil bah kodem le-arba'ah asar gomra b'arba'ah asar* — any *melacha* that one began before the fourteenth may be completed on the fourteenth. *Aval lo yatchil bah betchilah b'arba'ah asar* — but one may not begin something new on the fourteenth, even if there would be time to complete it. This allowance to finish previously started work holds even in locales where *melacha* is otherwise not done on the morning of *Erev Pesach*. Then the *mishna* states that the *Chachamim* identify three crafts — *chayatin* (tailors), *saparim* (barbers), and *kovsin* (launderers) — who may work on *Erev Pesach* regardless of *minhag*.

### Tzorech Hamoed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד תְּנַן, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — אֲפִילּוּ מִיגְמַר נָמֵי לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא: שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד תְּנַן, אֲבָל לְצוֹרֶךְ — אַתְחוֹלֵי מַתְחֲלִינַן, אוֹ דִילְמָא: בֵּין לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ, מִיגְמַר — אִין, אַתְחוֹלֵי — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Was it with regard to labor **for the purpose of the Festival** that **we learned** in the mishna that Rabbi Meir permits completing labor on the fourteenth, **but** labor **that is not for the purpose of the Festival may not even be completed? Or perhaps** it was with regard to labor **that is not for the purpose of the Festival** that **we learned** that Rabbi Meir permits completing labor on the fourteenth, **but** with regard to labor **that is for the purpose** of the Festival, **we** may even **initiate** it. **Or perhaps,** with regard to **both** labor **that is for the purpose of the Festival and** labor **that is not for the purpose** of the Festival, **completing, yes,** it is permitted, but **initiating, no,** it is prohibited.

The *gemara* raises a question: does *Rabbi Meir*'s allowance to finish previously started *melacha* apply only when the work is *l'tzorech hamoed* — in service of the festival — or even when it is not? And conversely, does *l'tzorech hamoed* alone, without having started earlier, suffice to permit beginning anew? Three logical possibilities emerge: first, that both criteria — *l'tzorech hamoed* and having started earlier — are required; second, that either one is sufficient; third, that *l'tzorech hamoed* is irrelevant and the only operative factor is whether one began earlier, in which case finishing is always permitted.

### Small Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֲבָל לֹא יַתְחִיל בַּתְּחִילָּה בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר אֲפִילּוּ צִלְצוֹל קָטָן אֲפִילּוּ שְׂבָכָה קְטַנָּה. מַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ״? לָאו אֲפִילּוּ הָנֵי, דִּלְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד, מִיגְמַר — אִין, אַתְחוֹלֵי — לָא?! מִכְּלָל דְּשֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ — מִיגְמַר נָמֵי לָא גָּמְרִינַן! לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּשֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ מִיגְמַר נָמֵי גָּמְרִינַן, וּמַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ״? אֲפִילּוּ הָנֵי נָמֵי, דְּזוּטְרֵי נִינְהוּ, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הַתְחָלָתָן זוֹ הִיא גְּמַר מְלַאכְתָּן — נַתְחֵיל בְּהוּ נָמֵי לְכַתְּחִילָּה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from that which we learned: **However, one may not begin** work **from the outset on the fourteenth, even** if it is only manufacturing **a small belt, or even a small hairnet. What** is the meaning of the term **even** in this context? **Isn't it** that **even** with regard to **those** items **that are for the purpose of the Festival, completing, yes,** it is permitted, but **initiating, no,** it is prohibited? And **by inference,** with regard to labor **that is not for the purpose** of the Festival, **we may not even complete** labor that was begun previously. This supports the first possibility cited above. The Gemara rejects this answer: **No; actually** this means that even labor **that is not for the purpose** of the Festival **we** may **also complete,** in accordance with the third possibility above. **And what** is the meaning of the term **even?** It is that this *halakha* applies **even** to **these** items, a belt and a hairnet, **which are small; as it could enter your mind to say:** Since they are small and **their initiation is their completion, let us even initiate** their manufacture on the fourteenth ***ab initio*.** Therefore, **it teaches us** that even with regard to this type of labor, initiating is prohibited. This *baraita* does not provide an unequivocal resolution to the dilemma.

A *braita* is cited: *aval lo yatchil betchilah b'arba'ah asar afilu tzilzul katan, afilu shvacha ktana* — one may not begin anew on the fourteenth, even something as small as a little belt or a tiny woven cap that takes only minutes to complete. The *gemara* probes what the word *afilu* — "even" — is emphasizing. Is it saying that even these small *l'tzorech hamoed* items may not be started? The *gemara* considers but rejects this reading. The *chiddush* of the *braita* is different: one might have thought that for such tiny items, starting them is effectively the same as finishing, since the whole *melacha* is completed so quickly. The *braita* teaches that even so, beginning counts as beginning — *l'chatchila matchil bahen nami l'chatchila* — and does not receive the finishing exemption. The *braita* thus does not resolve the *l'tzorech hamoed* question.

### Both Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁהִיא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — גּוֹמְרָהּ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִתְחִיל בָּהּ קוֹדֶם אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, אֲבָל לֹא הִתְחִיל בָּהּ קוֹדֶם אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — לֹא יַתְחִיל בָּהּ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, אֲפִילּוּ צִילְצוֹל קָטָן, אֲפִילּוּ שְׂבָכָה קְטַנָּה. לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — לָא. הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ נָמֵי גָּמְרִינַן, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד, מִיגְמַר — אִין, אַתְחוֹלֵי — לָא. תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁהִיא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — גּוֹמְרָהּ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — אָסוּר, וְעוֹשִׂים מְלָאכָה בְּעַרְבֵי פְסָחִים עַד חֲצוֹת בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ. מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ — אִין, לֹא נָהֲגוּ — לָא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד — לָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** yet another resolution to the dilemma. **Rabbi Meir says:** With regard to **any labor that is for the purpose of the Festival,** **one completes it on the fourteenth. When** is that the case? It is **when he initiated** the labor **prior to the fourteenth. However,** if **he did not initiate** the labor **prior to the fourteenth, he should not initiate it on the fourteenth, even** if it is only manufacturing **a small belt or even a small hairnet.** The Gemara analyzes this: When it is **for the purpose of the Festival, yes,** one may complete this labor. However, when it is **not for the purpose of the Festival, no,** one may not complete it, in accordance with the third possibility above. The Gemara rejects this analysis: Actually, **the same is true; we** may **also complete** the task **even when it is not for the purpose of** the Festival. **And this** comes to **teach us that even** when it is **for the purpose of the Festival, completing** a labor, **yes,** one may do so, but **initiating** a labor, **no,** one may not, in accordance with the third aforementioned possibility. **Come** and **hear** yet another resolution to the dilemma. **Rabbi Meir says:** With regard to **any labor that is for the purpose of the Festival, one** may **complete it on the fourteenth, and** if it **is not for the purpose of the Festival, it is prohibited** to complete it. **And one** may **perform labor on Passover eves until midday in a place where** people **were accustomed** to do so. The Gemara infers: In a **place where** people **were accustomed** to perform labor, **yes,** one may do so. However, in a place **where** people **were not accustomed** to perform labor, **no,** one may not do so. **And learn from it** that **for the purpose of the Festival, yes,** one may perform labor. However, if it is **not for the purpose of the Festival, no,** one may not do so, in accordance with the first aforementioned possibility. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is so.

A clearer *braita* is then cited: *Rabbi Meir omer kol melacha shehi l'tzorech hamoed gomra b'arba'ah asar; eimatai, bizman shehit'chil bah kodem arba'ah asar; aval lo hit'chil bah kodem arba'ah asar, lo yatchil bah b'arba'ah asar* — any *melacha* that is *l'tzorech hamoed* may be finished on the fourteenth, but only if it was begun before the fourteenth; if it was not begun before the fourteenth, it may not be started anew even though it is *l'tzorech hamoed*. This would seem to establish clearly that both criteria are required.

The *gemara* attempts to escape this conclusion by suggesting that the *braita*'s mention of *l'tzorech hamoed* is incidental — that even *shelo l'tzorech hamoed* one could finish, and *l'tzorech hamoed* is mentioned only to teach that even so, one cannot begin afresh. But this reading is strained. A final, unambiguous *braita* settles the matter: *kol melacha shehi l'tzorech hamoed gomra b'arba'ah asar, v'sheino l'tzorech hamoed assur* — any *melacha* that is *l'tzorech hamoed* may be finished; anything not *l'tzorech hamoed* is forbidden. Both criteria are required: the work must have been started before the fourteenth and must serve the purpose of the *moed*. This applies specifically in locales where *melacha* is not generally permitted in the morning — in places where the *minhag* allows it, no special license is needed.

## Permitted Professions

### Three Crafts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַחַיָּיטִין, שֶׁכֵּן הֶדְיוֹט תּוֹפֵר כְּדַרְכּוֹ בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. הַסַּפָּרִין וְהַכּוֹבְסִין, שֶׁכֵּן הַבָּא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִים מְסַפְּרִים וּמְכַבְּסִים בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הָרַצְעָנִים, שֶׁכֵּן עוֹלֵי רְגָלִים מְתַקְּנִים מִנְעָלֵיהֶם בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in explanation of their opinion: **The tailors** may perform labor on Passover eve, **as a layperson** is permitted to **sew in his** usual **manner during the intermediate days** of the Festival. Since this type of labor is permitted during the intermediate days, when the prohibition against labor is more stringent than on Passover eve, one may be lenient on Passover eve as well. **Barbers and launderers,** may perform labor on Passover eve, **as one who arrives from a country overseas or one who leaves prison** who did not have time to cut their hair or launder their clothing before the Festival **may cut their hair and wash** their clothing **on the intermediate days** of the Festival. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Shoemakers** may **also** perform their labor, **because Festival pilgrims** may **repair their shoes during the intermediate days of the Festival.**

The *mishna* states that the *Chachamim* permit three crafts to work on *Erev Pesach* regardless of local *minhag*: tailors, barbers, and launderers. Separately, *Rabbi Yossi bar Yehudah* adds shoemakers.

### Other Contexts

A *braita* provides the rationale, not by directly invoking *l'tzorech hamoed* for *Erev Pesach*, but by pointing to leniencies extended to these crafts in other halachic contexts. Tailors: *sheken hedyot tofer k'darko b'cholo shel moed* — a layperson is permitted to sew in the ordinary fashion on *Chol Hamoed*, so there is precedent for treating sewing as permissible even in restricted periods. Barbers and launderers: *sheken habaim mimedinat hayam v'hayotzim mibeit ha'asurim mesaprim umachavsin b'cholo shel moed* — those who return from overseas travel or emerge from prison are permitted to get a haircut and launder their clothes on *Chol Hamoed*, clearly based on the principle of *kvod haregel*. Since these leniencies exist in contexts that are halachically restricted — namely *Chol Hamoed*, where the *Masechet Moed Katan* treats these very activities as *assur* except for specific exceptions — the *gemara* takes those exceptions and generalizes them as the basis for permitting these professions on *Erev Pesach* even in places where *melacha* is otherwise not done.

### Shoemakers

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Betira* adds shoemakers: *sheken olei regalim metaknim man'aleihem b'chol hamoed* — pilgrims ascending to *Yerushalayim* are permitted to repair their sandals on *Chol Hamoed*, since they have been walking throughout their journey and have had no opportunity to do so.

### Inference Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: לְמֵידִין תְּחִילַּת מְלָאכָה מִסּוֹף מְלָאכָה, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין לְמֵידִין תְּחִילַּת מְלָאכָה מִסּוֹף מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosei **disagree?** The Gemara answers: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds: We learn** the *halakha* with regard to **the initiation of labor from the** *halakha* with regard to the **conclusion of labor;** if it is permitted to repair shoes, it is also permitted to initiate their production. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds: We cannot learn** the *halakha* with regard to **the initiation of labor from** the *halakha* with regard to **the conclusion of labor;** therefore, although it is permitted to repair shoes, it is prohibited to produce new ones.

The dispute between the Sages and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Betira* over whether to include shoemakers turns on an inferential principle: *mar savar lemedin techilat melacha misof melacha* — one position holds that we may learn the permissibility of making shoes (the beginning of the craft) from the permission to repair shoes (the end of the craft). *U'mar savar ein lemedin techilat melacha misof melacha* — the other position holds that one may not generalize in this way: the fact that we allow repair under certain circumstances does not mean we allow the full craft of shoemaking, and therefore the profession as a whole does not receive the general exemption. The entire framing in terms of analogical reasoning from other contexts is notable — rather than a direct *l'tzorech hamoed* analysis, the *gemara* grounds the leniency in the logic of existing exceptions elsewhere in the halachic system.
