# Avodah Zarah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bloodletting & Herbs

### Vinegar & Fish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלָּא לְסִיבּוּרֵי, וּמוֹנִינֵי לְתַעֲנִיתָא, וְחִילּוּפָא סַכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Vinegar** is good **for** one to consume **after bloodletting, and** eating **small fish** is good **for** one who has completed **a fast, but the reverse** is **a danger.**

The daf opens in the middle of a medical discussion that belongs to a broader tradition of rabbinic medicine — some of it fascinating, some of it unsettling when measured against what we know today. The Gemara states: *chala lesiborei umoninei leta'anita* — vinegar is beneficial for one who has undergone bloodletting, and small fish are beneficial after a fast. Then comes the crucial warning: *vecheilufa sakanta* — to reverse these and give fish after bloodletting or vinegar after a fast is dangerous.

### Bloodletting Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּחְלֵי וְסִיבּוּרָא סַכַּנְתָּא, אִישָּׁתָא וְסִיבּוּרָא סַכַּנְתָּא, כְּאֵיב עֵינָא וְסִיבּוּרֵי סַכַּנְתָּא. שֵׁנִי לַדָּג דָּם, שֵׁנִי לַדָּם דָּג, שְׁלִישִׁי לוֹ סַכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Eating **cress and** then undergoing **bloodletting** poses **a danger.** With regard to one who suffers from **a fever and** undergoes **bloodletting,** this poses **a danger** to his life. Similarly, one who suffers from **pain of the eye and** undergoes **bloodletting endangers** to his life. On the **second** day **after** eating **fish** one may let **blood,** and on the **second** day **after** letting **blood** one may eat **fish.** With regard to eating fish on the **third** day **after** letting blood, or letting blood on the third day after eating fish, both of these actions pose **a danger.**

The Gemara then enumerates further dangers associated with bloodletting. *Tachlei vesibura sakanta* — cress combined with bloodletting is dangerous. Similarly, *ishta vesibura sakanta* — fever with bloodletting is dangerous. And *ke'ev eina vesibura sakanta* — eye pain with bloodletting is dangerous. The cumulative picture is striking: bloodletting combined with almost anything poses a risk.

For those who wish to eat fish in proximity to bloodletting, the Gemara provides a narrow window. One may let blood on the second day after eating fish, and one may eat fish on the second day after letting blood — but a third-day combination poses danger.

### Mnemonic & Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּקִּיז דָּם לֹא יֹאכַל חגב״ש — לֹא חָלָב, וְלֹא גְּבִינָה, וְלֹא בְּצָלִים, וְלֹא שַׁחֲלַיִם. אִם אָכַל, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נַיְיתֵי רְבִיעֲתָא דְּחַלָּא וּרְבִיעֲתָא דְּחַמְרָא, וּנְעַרְבְּבִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי וְנִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who lets blood may not eat** the following foods, corresponding to the acronym ***ḥet*, *gimmel*, *beit*, *shin*.** That is, he may consume **neither milk [*ḥalav*], nor cheese [*gevina*], nor onions [*betzalim*], nor cress [*sheḥalim*]. If he ate** one of these, **Abaye said:** He **should bring a quarter***-log* **of vinegar and a quarter***-log* **of wine and mix them together and drink** the mixture.

The *Tanna* then teaches the mnemonic *chagvash*, an acronym in the style of military shorthand combining the initial letters of *chalav*, *gevina*, *betzalim*, and *shchalim* — milk, cheese, onions, and cress. One who is undergoing bloodletting should not eat any of these. The Gemara does not simply leave the prohibition there, however. In a characteristically pragmatic move, it immediately addresses the case of one who did eat these foods — clearly anticipating that the prohibition will be violated. Abaye teaches: *naiti revi'ata dechala revi'ata dechamra venearbevinhu bahadi hadadei venishti* — bring a quarter-log of vinegar, a quarter-log of wine, mix them together, and drink.

### Bathroom Etiquette

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מִפְּנֵה — לָא מִפְּנֵה אֶלָּא לְמִזְרָחָהּ שֶׁל עִיר, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָשֵׁה רֵיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And when he defecates, he should defecate only toward the east of the city, because the odor** of the excrement after that treatment **is offensive.** Since the wind does not usually blow from the east, it is less likely to spread the stench.

The Gemara then turns to a piece of guidance that is at once mundane and geographically curious: *vechi mefane, lo mefane ela lemizrachah shel ir mishum dekashi reicha* — when one goes to relieve oneself after bloodletting, one should go only to the eastern side of the city, because the odor is especially intense and spreads into the city. The prevailing winds in the region of the Talmud's composition — the Land of Israel and Babylonia — blow primarily from the west, which would indeed carry an odor westward through the city if one were positioned to its east. This is a detail that makes sense only in its specific geographic and meteorological context, and it is worth noting that Rav Sherira Gaon cautioned against following this and similar medical passages without the guidance of contemporary physicians.

### Unkli Bone Cure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַעֲלִין אוּנְקְלֵי בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי אוּנְקְלֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: אִיסְתּוּמְכָא דְּלִיבָּא. מַאי אָסוּתָא? מַיְיתֵי כַּמּוֹנָא כֵּרְוַיָּיא וְנִינְיָא וְאַגְדָּנָא וְצִיתְרִי וְאַבְדָּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: One may lift** the ***unkali* on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the ***unkali*? Rabbi Abba said:** It is the **edge of the ribs [*istumkha*] near the heart** which sometimes bend inward, in which case they must be lifted and straightened into their proper position. The Gemara asks: **What** is **the cure** for one whose *unkali* has been bent? He should **take cumin, caraway, mint [*ninya*], wormwood, satureja, and hyssop.**

Rav Yehoshua ben Levi teaches that it is permitted to restore the *unkli* even on *Shabbat*. The Gemara asks: *mai unkli*? Rav Abba explains: *istumcha d'libba* — it is a bone or structure near the heart, situated at the edge of the ribs. When it bends or is displaced inward, it requires being lifted and returned to its proper position. The cure for this condition involves a combination of herbs: *kammon* (cumin), *ninya* (caraway or mint), *agadana* (wormwood or a similar herb), *tzatrei* (something resembling onion or garlic), and *avadata* (hyssop, akin to the *ezov* described in the Torah).

### Wine/Water Cures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלִיבָּא בְּחַמְרָא, וְסִימָנָךְ ״וְיַיִן יְשַׂמַּח לְבַב אֱנוֹשׁ״. לְרוּחָא בְּמַיָּא, וְסִימָנָךְ ״וְרוּחַ אֱלֹהִים מְרַחֶפֶת עַל פְּנֵי הַמָּיִם״. לְכוּדָא בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וְסִימָנָךְ ״וְכַדָּהּ עַל שִׁכְמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** This remedy is beneficial for several ailments, and the Gemara presents each of these in turn: **For** curing the **heart,** the above combination should be taken **with wine, and your mnemonic** for this is the verse: **"And wine that makes glad the heart of man"** (Psalms 104:15). **For** curing an ailment that arises due to **the wind [*ruḥa*],** one drinks the mixture **in water, and your mnemonic** for this is the verse: **"And the spirit [*ruaḥ*] of God hovered over the face of the waters"** (Genesis 1:2). **For childbirth [*lekhuda*],** a woman in labor drinks the mixture **with beer [*shikhra*], and your mnemonic** for this is the verse: **"With her pitcher [*vekhadah*] upon her shoulder [*shikhmah*]"** (Genesis 24:15).

This herbal mixture, the Gemara explains, serves different ailments depending on the medium with which it is taken. *L'libba b'chamra* — for heart ailments, it should be taken with wine. The scriptural hint: *v'yayin yesamach levav enosh* — "wine gladdens the heart of man" (Psalms 104:15). For conditions involving the lungs or breath — *shachefet*, a respiratory or consumptive illness — the mixture should be taken with water, and the hint is *v'ruach Elohim merachefet al pnei hamayim* from Bereishit: the spirit of God hovered over the waters. For a woman on the birthing stones, *lachuda lachuda b'shichra* — the mixture taken with a fermented beer-like drink is indicated, and the hint is *v'kada al shichmah*, from the story of Rivka: *kuda* (the one seated on the birthing stones) and *shichra* resonate phonetically with *shichmah* (her shoulder).

Each of these three scriptural associations works on its own internal logic. The first links wine explicitly with the heart. The second associates breath or spirit (*ruach*) with water (*mayim*). The third is a more intricate phonetic connection: *kad* or *kuda* to the birthing position, and *shichra* to *shichmah*.

### Rav Acha & Rav Ashi's Approaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שָׁחֵיק לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ מְלֵא חֲמֵשׁ אֶצְבְּעָתֵיהּ, וְשָׁתֵי לֵיהּ. רַב אָשֵׁי שָׁחֵיק כֹּל חַד וְחַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ, וְשָׁקֵיל מְלֵא אֶצְבְּעֵיהּ רַבָּתִי וּמְלֵא אֶצְבְּעֵיהּ זוּטַרְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, ground all of these together and took a handful of** the mixture **and drank it. Rav Ashi ground each and every one** of the herbs **separately and he took all** that he could hold between **his large finger and his small finger** and drank it.

The Gemara then records that Rav Acha the son of Rava would grind all of these herbs together at once, take a full handful — *melo chamesh etzbaos* — and drink. Rav Ashi, by contrast, would grind each herb separately and take only a small pinch, measured between the thumb and little finger.

### Honey Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲנָא עֲבַדִי לְכֹל הָנֵי וְלָא אִיתַּסַּאי, עַד דַּאֲמַר לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא: אַיְיתִי כּוּזָא חַדְתָּא וּמַלְּיֵיהּ מַיָּא, וּרְמִי בֵּיהּ תַּרְוָודָא דְּדוּבְשָׁא דִּתְלֵי לֵהּ בֵּי כוֹכְבֵי, וְלִמְחַר אִישְׁתִּי. עֲבַדִי הָכִי וְאִיתַּסַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: I tried all these** remedies **and I was not healed until a certain Arab said to me: Bring a new jug and fill it with water and place in it a ladle [*tarvada*] of honey that is suspended among the stars,** i.e., add the ladle at night, **and drink it on the next day.** Rav Pappa concludes: **I did this and I was healed.**

Rav Papa states plainly: *ana avdi l'chulhu hanech v'lo itasai* — I tried all of the above and I did not get better. What finally cured him came from an unexpected source. A non-Jew he encountered advised him to bring a new, unused small cup, fill it with water, add a large spoonful of honey, leave it outside under the open sky, and drink it the next day. *Avdi hachi v'itasai* — I did this and I recovered.

The Gemara does not editorialize on why this remedy worked where the rabbinic herbal protocols failed. The passage is notable for its candor: a respected *amora* acknowledges that elaborate rabbinic formulas proved ineffective for him personally, while a simple folk remedy from a non-Jewish traveler succeeded. What happens to water left outside overnight with honey is left unexplained — though one might observe that warmth and fermentation both figure plausibly in the outcome. The passage invites humility about the limits of any system of medical knowledge.

## Healing Foods

### Six Cures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מְרַפְּאִין אֶת הַחוֹלֶה מֵחׇלְיוֹ וּרְפוּאָתָן רְפוּאָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: כְּרוּב, וּתְרָדִין, וּמֵי סִיסִין (יְבֵישָׁה וְקֵיבַת) [יְבֵישִׁין וְקֵיבָה], וְהֶרֶת, וְיוֹתֶרֶת הַכָּבֵד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף דָּגִים קְטַנִּים, וְלָא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁדָּגִים קְטַנִּים מַפְרִין וּמַרְבִּין כׇּל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Six items heal a sick person from his illness and their cure is** a permanent **cure, and these are: Cabbage, beets, water** in which **dried chamomile** was soaked, and **the stomach** contents of an animal, and **the womb** of an animal, **and the lobe of the liver. And some say: Small fish** are **also** included in this list. **And moreover,** eating **small fish causes a person's entire body to flourish and to grow.**

The Gemara presents two symmetrical traditions — one listing foods that heal the sick, the other listing foods that worsen illness. The *Tanna* teaches: *shisha devarim merapim et hacholeh mecholyov urefuatan refuah* — six things cure the sick person and the cure they provide is genuine healing. These are: *kruv* (cabbage), *tardin* (beets, or by some readings spinach), *mei sissin yeveshah* (water in which chamomile has been dried and soaked), *keivat* (the stomach of an animal), *harat* (the womb of a small animal), and *yotrat hakaved* (a portion of the liver). Some add small fish to this list. The Gemara adds that small fish not only heal but make the entire body *paris umarbeh* — multiply and flourish — drawing on the symbolic association of fish with fertility and abundance.

### Ten Harms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים מַחְזִירִין אֶת הַחוֹלֶה לְחׇלְיוֹ, וְחׇלְיוֹ קָשֶׁה, אֵלּוּ הֵן: הָאוֹכֵל בְּשַׂר שׁוֹר, שׁוֹמֵן, בְּשַׂר צָלִי, בְּשַׂר צִיפֳּרִים, וּבֵיצָה צְלוּיָה, וְשַׁחֲלַיִם, וְתִגְלַחַת, וּמֶרְחָץ, וּגְבִינָה, וְכָבֵד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף אֱגוֹזִים. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף קִשּׁוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ten items return a sick person to his illness, and his illness** becomes even more **severe** than it originally was, and **they are: One who eats ox meat, fat, roasted meat, bird meat, and a roasted egg, and cress; and** the act of **shaving, and bathing, and** the consumption of **cheese, and liver. And some say: Nuts** are **also** included in this list. **And some say: Cucumbers** are **also** included in this list.

The corresponding list moves in the opposite direction. *Asara devarim machzirim et hacholeh lecholyov vecholyov kasheh* — ten things return the sick person to his illness and make that illness more severe. These are: the meat of an ox, fat, roasted meat, the meat of birds, a roasted egg, cress, a haircut, bathing in a bathhouse, cheese, and liver. Some add nuts, and some add cucumbers.

This list is striking for several reasons. The prohibition on bathing and haircuts alongside rich foods suggests that physical exertion and digestive burden were understood as equally taxing to a weakened constitution. The Rambam, it should be noted, rules in accordance with this passage — which is remarkable given that Rav Sherira Gaon, who preceded him, had cautioned against following rabbinic medical guidance literally. Whether the Rambam understood the list as reflecting permanent physiological truth or as context-dependent counsel for the ill is a question worth examining. What the passage makes clear is that these instructions apply to a person who is *already sick*, not to healthy individuals. The logic is that a weakened body cannot bear the digestive and physical demands that these foods and activities impose.

### Cucumbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן קִשּׁוּאִין? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן קָשִׁין לְכׇל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כַּחֲרָבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Why are they called cucumbers [*kishu'in*]? Because they are as harmful [*kashin*] to a person's entire body as swords.**

The *Beit Midrash* of Rabbi Yishmael offers an etymological observation: *lama nikra sheman kishu'in* — why are they called *kishu'in* (cucumbers)? *Mipnei shehen kashin lechol gufo shel adam kacharavot* — because they are as hard on the entire body as swords. The word *kashin* (hard or harmful) is embedded in the name *kishu'in* itself. This kind of wordplay serves a mnemonic and pedagogical function: the name of the food encodes the warning about it.

## Haircuts & Blorit

### Mirror Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מִסְתַּפְּרִין מֵהֶן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל הַמִּסְתַּפֵּר מִגּוֹי רוֹאֶה בַּמַּרְאָה, וְגוֹי הַמִּסְתַּפֵּר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לִבְלוֹרִיתוֹ שׁוֹמֵט אֶת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not have his hair cut by** gentiles **anywhere. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A Jew who has his hair cut by a gentile** should **observe** the gentile's actions **in a mirror** while he cuts his hair. **And** in the case of **a gentile who has his hair cut by a Jew, when** the Jew **reaches** the gentile's **forelock he removes his hand** and does not cut it, because it is associated with idol worship.

The Gemara now returns to the Mishnah's prohibition: *ein mistaprin mehen bechol makom* — one may not take a haircut from non-Jews anywhere. The *baraita* teaches that a Jew who does take a haircut from an *oved kochavim* should watch the barber in a mirror throughout, and when a non-Jew has his hair cut by a Jew, the Jewish barber should remove his hand when he reaches the *blorit*.

What is the *blorit*? In later Hebrew the term denotes the forelock — the curl or sweep of hair at the front of the head. Rashi understands it as a long braid worn at the back. The association is with idolatrous practice, and the prohibition on cutting it is accordingly tied to the concern against participation in or facilitation of idol worship.

The mirror detail raises an immediate analytical question noted by *Tosafot*: if you see in the mirror that the barber is raising a blade to your neck, what exactly can you do about it at that point? The answer the Gemara gives is not about practical intervention but about social signaling. *Mitchazi ke'adam chashuv* — the one who watches the mirror appears as a person of importance, a person of consequence, and a barber who perceives his client as such will be correspondingly careful. The mirror functions not as a surveillance device but as a deterrent through projected status.

*Tosafot* also raises the question of *Shabbat* and mirrors — specifically whether looking in a mirror on *Shabbat* is problematic because one might be tempted to pluck a white hair one notices, which would constitute a violation. This detail reveals that vanity was hardly an invention of modernity.

### Domain Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יִשְׂרָאֵל הַמִּסְתַּפֵּר מִגּוֹי רוֹאֶה בַּמַּרְאָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, לְמָה לִי מַרְאָה? וְאִי בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, כִּי רוֹאֶה מַאי הָוֵי? לְעוֹלָם בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְכֵיוָן דְּאִיכָּא מַרְאָה — מִתְחֲזֵי כְּאָדָם חָשׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: A Jew who has his hair cut by a gentile** should **observe** the gentile's actions **in a mirror.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** it is referring to a haircut performed **in a public domain, why do I** need **a mirror?** After all, the gentile will not harm a Jew in public. **And if** it occurs **in a private domain,** even if the Jews **observes** the gentile's actions, **what of it?** How does the fact that the Jew is watching prevent the gentile barber from harming him? The Gemara explains: **Actually,** this is referring to a haircut **in a private domain, but since there is a mirror** in place, the Jew **appears as an important person** whom the gentile will hesitate to attack.

The Mishnah reflects a dispute. Rabbi Meir holds *ein mistaprin mehem bechol makom* — nowhere is one permitted to take a haircut from a non-Jewish barber. The *Chachamim* permit it in a *reshut harabim* (public domain) but not *beino leveino* — not in a private, secluded setting. The Gemara then asks about the mirror teaching: if the concern is danger, then in a *reshut harabim* there is no need for a mirror because witnesses are present. And in a *reshut hayachid*, does the mirror actually help? The Gemara resolves that the case is always *reshut hayachid*, and the function of the mirror is exactly the status-projection described above: the barber, seeing a man who watches himself with care, will treat that person with greater caution.

### Rav Chana Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא הֲוָה מִסְתַּפֵּר מִגּוֹי בִּשְׁבִילֵי דִנְהַרְדְּעָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חָנָא, חָנָא, יָאֵי קוֹעָיךְ לְזוּגָא! אָמַר: תֵּיתֵי לִי דַּעֲבַרִי אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a relevant incident: **Rav Ḥana bar Bizna was having his hair cut by a gentile in** one of **the side streets of Neharde'a.** The barber **said to him: Ḥana, Ḥana; Your throat is appealing to the razor.** Rav Ḥana bar Bizna **said:** I have this **coming to me, as I violated** the ruling **of Rabbi Meir,** who stated that one may not have his hair cut by a gentile in any location.

The Gemara records a personal narrative of Rav Chana bar Bizna, who went to have his hair cut by a non-Jewish barber in the *shvilim* of Neharde'a — the alleyways or side paths of that city. The barber looked at him and said: *Chana, Chana, ya'e kavayich lezuga* — Chana, Chana, how beautiful your neck is next to my scissors. Rav Chana survived this encounter, but the fright was real. His reaction is telling: *titi li de'avarei ada deRabbi Meir* — this is what I deserve for having transgressed the ruling of Rabbi Meir.

What is notable is the direction of his self-reproach. He does not blame the barber for being threatening, nor does he conclude that non-Jewish barbers are inherently dangerous. He connects his fear directly to his failure to observe the ruling of Rabbi Meir. The Gemara then clarifies his reasoning: he had believed that the alleyways of Neharde'a, since people were commonly present there, were equivalent in status to a *reshut harabim*, and thus permitted even according to the *Chachamim*. The episode establishes that the *Chachamim's* leniency is restricted to genuine public space, and that a side street, even a busy one, does not automatically acquire that status — though Rav Chana had reasoned otherwise. *Tosafot*'s extended comment on this passage (*Hamistaper*) ultimately acknowledges that the common practice of obtaining haircuts from non-Jewish barbers can be justified if another person accompanies the client, removing the condition of *beino leveino*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא עֲבַר? אֵימַר דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד מִי אֲמוּר? וְהוּא סָבַר: שְׁבִילֵי דִנְהַרְדְּעָא, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים, כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And didn't** Rav Ḥana bar Bizna **violate** the ruling **of the Rabbis** as well? **Say** that **when the Rabbis stated** that it is permitted to have one's hair cut by a gentile, they were referring to a haircut performed **in a public domain;** but with regard to a haircut performed **in a private domain, did they say** that it is permitted? Since the side streets of Neharde'a cannot be considered a public domain, evidently Rav Ḥana bar Bizna violated the ruling of the Rabbis. The Gemara explains: **And** Rav Ḥana bar Bizna **maintains:** With regard to **the side streets of Neharde'a, since many people are present** there, **they are similar to a public domain,** and it would therefore be permitted to have one's hair cut there according to the opinion of the Rabbis.

### Blorit Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגוֹי הַמִּסְתַּפֵּר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לִבְלוֹרִיתוֹ שׁוֹמֵט אֶת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in the case of **a gentile who has his hair cut by a Jew, when** the Jew **reaches** the gentile's **forelock, he removes his hand** and does not cut it, because it is associated with idol worship.

The *baraita* teaches that when a non-Jew is receiving a haircut from a Jewish barber, the Jew must remove his hand upon reaching the *blorit* — that distinctive lock of hair associated with idolatrous practice. The concern is not about the Jew's own appearance but about facilitating the non-Jew's idolatrous practices by maintaining the very hairstyle used in that worship.

### Distance Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב מַלְכִּיָּה אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שָׁלֹס אֶצְבָּעוֹת לְכׇל רוּחַ וָרוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** space should the Jew leave around the forelock? **Rav Malkiyya says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: Three fingerbreadths in each and every direction.**

How close may the Jewish barber cut before he must stop? Rav Malkia citing Rav Adda bar Ahava: *shalosh etzba'ot l'chol ruach v'ruach* — three finger-breadths in every direction from the *blorit*. The barber must leave a margin of three fingers all around.

### Name Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חֲנִינָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: שַׁפּוּד, שְׁפָחוֹת, וְגוּמוֹת — רַב מַלְכִּיּוֹ; בְּלוֹרִית, אֵפֶר מִקְלֶה, וּגְבִינָה — רַב מַלְכִּיָּה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַתְנִיתִין וּמַתְנִיתָא — רַב מַלְכִּיָּה, שְׁמַעְתָּתָא — רַב מַלְכִּיּוֹ, וְסִימָנָךְ: מַתְנִיתָא מַלְכְּתָא. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שְׁפָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In addition to Rav Malkiyya, whose ruling was just cited, there was another *amora* known as Rav Malkiyyu. In order to avoid confusing the two, the Gemara records their respective rulings. **Rav Ḥanina, son of Rav Ika, says:** The statements concerning **a skewer, maidservants, and follicles** were issued by **Rav Malkiyyu;** the rulings concerning **a forelock, burnt ashes, and cheese** were stated by **Rav Malkiyya.** **Rav Pappa said** a different opinion: Statements from the **Mishna and *baraita*** were issued by **Rav Malkiyya,** whereas rulings of ***halakha*** that are not related to a mishna or *baraita* were taught by **Rav Malkiyyu. And the mnemonic** to remember this is: **The Mishna is a queen [*malketa*],** i.e., the statements that are referring to a mishna were made by Rav Malkiyya, whose name is similar to the Aramaic term for queen. The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** the opinions of Rav Ḥanina and Rav Pappa? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to the *halakha* concerning **maidservants.** According to Rav Ḥanina, this *halakha* was stated by Rav Malkiyyu, whereas Rav Pappa holds that it was taught by Rav Malkiyya, as it is referring to a dispute in a mishna.

This ruling is attributed to Rav Malkia, and the Gemara pauses to address a potential confusion: there are two *amoraim* with nearly identical names, Rav Malkia and Rav Malkio, and their rulings are sometimes attributed interchangeably. Rav Chanina the son of Rav Ika provides a mnemonic: the rulings concerning the *shapud* (a skewer and the question of leaving it on a fire), *shfachot* (that a woman with maidservants should still keep herself occupied with work, lest idleness lead to licentiousness), and *gumot* (dimples or indentations in the skin used to assess age) belong to Rav Malkio. The rulings concerning *blorit* (the hair discussed here), *efer miklah* (burnt ashes), and *gevinah* (cheese, to be discussed shortly) belong to Rav Malkia.

Rav Papa offers his own mnemonic: *matniti umatnita* are associated with Rav Malkia, while *shematata* is associated with Rav Malkio. The logic behind this distinction is phonological: *matnita* and *malkata* share a feminine grammatical form, and *malkia* sounds similarly feminine, and so the *mishnaic* rulings — those that appear in the more formal, authoritative register — are attributed to Rav Malkia. The practical difference between the two that the Gemara identifies is the ruling of *shfachot*, the question of women with maidservants.

## Forbidden Items

### Wine & Clay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה: הַיַּיִן, וְהַחוֹמֶץ שֶׁל גּוֹיִם שֶׁהָיָה מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ יַיִן, וְחֶרֶס הַדְרְיָינִי, וְעוֹרוֹת לְבוּבִין. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַקֶּרַע שֶׁלּוֹ עָגוֹל — אָסוּר, מָשׁוּךְ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna discusses the halakhic status of various items that belong to gentiles. **These are items** that **belong to gentiles** and **are prohibited** to Jews, **and their prohibition is** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited: Wine, and vinegar belonging to gentiles that was originally wine, and Hadrianic earthenware, and hides** with a tear opposite the **heart. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** A hide is **prohibited** only **when** the **tear** around its heart is **circular,** but if it is **elongated** it is **permitted,** as gentiles will sacrifice a heart only when it has been removed by a circular laceration.

The Mishnah now opens a new category of discussion, shifting from the dangers of association with non-Jews to the distinct legal category of *isur hana'ah* — items from which not merely consumption but any form of benefit is prohibited. The Mishnah introduces its list: *ha-yayin*, wine of non-Jews; *ha-chometz shel ovdei kochavim she-haya mi-tchilato yayin*, vinegar of non-Jews that was originally wine; and *cheres Adriani*, Hadrianic clay.

The Hadrianic clay deserves explanation. This refers to clay vessels that were never fired — left unbaked — and that were soaked in wine. Because the clay was soft and porous, it absorbed the wine thoroughly, becoming a kind of concentrated wine-in-clay. One could carry a shard of this material, place it in water, and reconstitute a wine-like drink as the clay dissolved. The shard might appear to be an ordinary potsherd but was in fact saturated with wine — and wine from non-Jewish sources carries the prohibition of *yayin nesech*. The material is therefore prohibited even though it does not look like wine.

### Leather Hides

The Mishnah also prohibits *orot levuvin* — hides or leather pieces that bear a tear directly opposite the heart. The significance of this marking is that it is characteristic of a specific idolatrous practice: the animal's skin was cut and the heart extracted through the opening as part of a ritual. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel provides a means of distinguishing ritual use from ordinary slaughter: if the tear is *agol* — round — that indicates the hide was used in idolatrous sacrifice, and it is forbidden. If the tear is *mashuch* — elongated, like a simple incision — it may have been an ordinary cut, and the hide is permitted.

### Sacrificial Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּשָׂר הַנִּכְנָס לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — מוּתָּר, וְהַיּוֹצֵא — אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּזִבְחֵי מֵתִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Meat that enters the** house of **idol worship** is **permitted, and** meat **that exits** this house is **prohibited, because it is** considered **as offerings to the dead,** i.e., to idols; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

Rabbi Akiva rules that meat entering a house of idolatrous worship is permitted, but meat exiting such a place is forbidden because it is treated as *zivchei meitim* — offerings to the dead, meaning to idols. This ruling is notable. One might expect that proximity to an idolatrous context would render meat suspect at the point of entry, but Rabbi Akiva reasons otherwise: the meat has not yet been used in any ritual act when it enters, and its status has not changed. Only upon exit — having presumably been part of the sacrificial offering — does it acquire forbidden status.

### Festival Commerce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוֹלְכִים לַתַּרְפּוּת — אָסוּר לָשֵׂאת וְלָתֵת עִמָּהֶן, וְהַבָּאִים — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Those going to a festival of idolatry [*tarput*],** it is **prohibited to engage in business with them. And** with regard to **those who are coming** from it, it is **permitted** to engage in business with them.

The Mishnah rules that it is forbidden to conduct commerce with those who are on their way to *tarpot* — a festival of idolatrous worship — but those returning from such a festival may engage in commerce. The word *tarpot* itself is understood in two ways: it may be a reference to *teraphim*, the household idols mentioned in the story of Rachel, suggesting a festival centered on idol veneration; or it may derive from *beit haturpah*, a euphemism for a place of licentiousness, suggesting a different kind of forbidden gathering. The logic of the commercial prohibition is that doing business with those traveling to the festival provides them with means or goodwill that will redound to the honor of their idols — either through gratitude expressed to the idol for their business success, or through the use of those funds in the festival itself.

### Vessels & Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹדוֹת הַגּוֹיִם וְקַנְקַנֵּיהֶן, וְיַיִן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל כָּנוּס בָּהֶן — אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין אִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה. הַחַרְצַנִּים וְהַזַּגִּין שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לַחִין — אֲסוּרִין, יְבֵישִׁין — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Wineskins and jugs** belonging **to gentiles,** which have **a Jew's wine contained in them,** are **prohibited** to Jews, **and their prohibition is** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Their prohibition is not** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.** Residual grape **seeds and grape skins belonging to gentiles,** which are left behind after the grapes are crushed for wine, are **prohibited, and their prohibition is** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Moist** grape residues are **prohibited,** but **dry** residues are **permitted.**

The Mishnah further addresses *nodot* — leather flasks — and *kankanehem* — clay jugs — of non-Jews that contain Jewish wine. Rabbi Meir rules that such wine is forbidden, and that the prohibition is *isur hana'ah*. The *Chachamim* disagree and hold that the prohibition is not *isur hana'ah* — the wine may not be consumed, but benefit may still be derived from it.

The Mishnah also addresses *chartzanim vezagin* — the seeds and grape skins left over from the non-Jewish winemaking process. These are byproducts of *yayin nesech* production. Rabbi Meir again rules that they are forbidden with *isur hana'ah*. The *Chachamim* distinguish: if these byproducts are still moist — and Rashi indicates that a twelve-month window defines this state — they are forbidden. Once dry, they are permitted.

### Fish & Cheese

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוּרְיָיס, וּגְבִינַת בֵּית אוּנְיָיקִי שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — אֲסוּרִין, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין אִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Fish stew [*murayes*] and cheese of Beit Unyaki belonging to gentiles are prohibited, and their prohibition is** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Their prohibition is not** that of **an item from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.**

The Mishnah lists *hamorais* — a type of small fish — and *gevinat beit unaiki* — cheese from Beit Unaiki — as items of non-Jews that are forbidden with *isur hana'ah* according to Rabbi Meir, while the *Chachamim* again limit the prohibition to consumption alone and do not extend it to *isur hana'ah*.

Beit Unaiki was a place in northwestern Asia Minor — roughly the region of modern Turkey — and was specifically associated with a practice of using wine from idolatrous sources in the cheesemaking process. It was therefore identified as a particularly unreliable source for cheese. This geographical specificity is significant: the prohibition is not abstract but rooted in documented practice at a known location.

## Non-Jewish Cheese

### Cheese Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שָׁאַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אֶת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ כְּשֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ, אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָסְרוּ גְּבִינוֹת גּוֹיִם? אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקֵיבָה שֶׁל נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: Rabbi Yishmael asked Rabbi Yehoshua** a series of questions **while they were traveling along the road.** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: For what** reason **did** the Sages **prohibit** the **cheeses of gentiles?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: Because** gentiles **curdle** cheese **with the stomach** contents **of** an unslaughtered **animal carcass,** and as the carcass of an unslaughtered animal is not kosher, cheese that is curdled with it is likewise prohibited.

Rabbi Yehuda reports a conversation between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yehoshua that took place as they were walking along the road. Rabbi Yishmael posed the question directly: *mipnei mah asru gevinot ovdei kochavim* — on what grounds have the Sages forbidden the cheese of non-Jews? The question is sharper than it might appear. Since milk can only be curdled into cheese from a kosher animal — there is, for instance, no such thing as camel cheese; only milk from *behemot tehorot* can be made into cheese — the underlying ingredient would seem to be unobjectionable. What then is the concern?

### Calf Stomach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא קֵיבַת עוֹלָה חֲמוּרָה מִקֵּיבַת נְבֵילָה, אָמְרוּ: כֹּהֵן שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ יָפָה שׂוֹרְפָה חַיָּה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ: אֵין נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין!
>
> **Translation:** In response, Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: But aren't the stomach** contents **of a burnt-offering** subject to a more **stringent** prohibition **than the stomach** contents **of** an unslaughtered **animal carcass?** And yet **they said: A priest who is open-minded [*shedato yafa*]** with regard to what he eats **may swallow [*shorefah*]** the stomach contents of a burnt-offering while they are **raw, and** the other Sages **did not agree with him. But** the Sages **said: One may not** derive **benefit** from the stomach contents of a burnt-offering *ab initio*, **and** if one did derive benefit from them, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property. According to both opinions, deriving benefit from the stomach contents of a burnt-offering is not prohibited by Torah law. Since the *halakha* with regard to a burnt-offering is more stringent than that of an animal carcass, why would deriving benefit from the carcass be prohibited, while deriving benefit from the burnt-offering is permitted?

Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *mipnei shema'amidin otah bekeivah shel nevelah* — because they curdle the milk using the stomach of a *neveila*, an animal that died without proper *shechita*. This is not a hypothetical problem. The traditional process of cheesemaking requires a curdling agent, which historically was obtained from the stomach lining of a slaughtered animal — the rennet. If the animal whose stomach lining is used was itself a *neveila*, the cheese produced using that lining is tainted.

Rabbi Yishmael then raises a comparative question: *vehalo keivat olah chamurah mikeivat nevelah* — is not the stomach of an *olah* even more severe than the stomach of a *neveila*? He is comparing two categories of prohibited stomach-use. An *olah* is an offering entirely consecrated to God; using its stomach contents for cheesemaking might seem to involve the additional prohibition of *me'ila*, misappropriation of sacred property. The Gemara clarifies: a *kohen* of liberal disposition (*sheda'ato yafa*) might eat the stomach contents of an *olah* while still raw, and the Sages did not concur with this practice, but they also determined *ein nehenim velo moalin* — that while one should not benefit from these stomach contents, doing so does not constitute *me'ila* in the technical sense. The *olah's* stomach contents are not subject to the biblical prohibition of *me'ila*, even though benefit from them is rabbinically discouraged.

The logical consequence is this: with respect to the *neveila* stomach, the prohibition is *issur achila* — eating is forbidden, but benefit may be derived. With respect to the *olah* stomach, even *hana'ah* is restricted, yet it is not full *me'ila*. Neither category clearly explains why the resulting cheese should be forbidden with *isur hana'ah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקֵיבַת עֶגְלֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabbi Yishmael: The cheese of gentiles is prohibited **because they curdle it in the stomach** contents **of calves** used for **idol worship.** Since it is prohibited to derive benefit from such calves, cheese curdled in their stomach contents is also prohibited.

The answer, when it comes, reframes the problem entirely: *mipnei shema'amidin otah bekeivat eglei avodat kochavim* — because the cheese is actually curdled using the stomach of calves that were used for idolatrous purposes. The real concern is not *neveila* at all but the use of a stomach that is intrinsically connected to idol worship. Since we are prohibited from deriving benefit from objects used in idolatrous practice, the cheese curdled within such a stomach absorbs that prohibition.

### Cheese Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה לֹא אֲסָרוּהָ בַּהֲנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: If** that is **so, why didn't** the Sages **prohibit** deriving any **benefit** from the cheese, instead of merely prohibiting its consumption?

This leads Rabbi Yishmael to press further: *im ken lama lo asaruha behana'ah* — if the stomach used was that of an idolatrous calf, why then did the Sages not extend the prohibition to *isur hana'ah*? The logic of *isur hana'ah* from objects of idol worship would seem to demand it. Rabbi Yehoshua does not answer the question.

### Song of Songs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשִּׂיאוֹ לְדָבָר אַחֵר, אָמַר לוֹ: יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הֵיאַךְ אַתָּה קוֹרֵא ״כִּי טוֹבִים דֹּדֶיךָ מִיָּיִן״ אוֹ ״כִּי טוֹבִים דֹּדַיִךָ״? אָמַר לוֹ: ״כִּי טוֹבִים דֹּדַיִךָ״. אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין הַדָּבָר כֵּן, שֶׁהֲרֵי חֲבֵירוֹ מְלַמֵּד עָלָיו: ״לְרֵיחַ שְׁמָנֶיךָ טוֹבִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Instead of answering Rabbi Yishmael, Rabbi Yehoshua **diverted his** attention **to another matter** and **said to him: Yishmael, how do you read** the following verse in the Song of Songs (1:2)? Do you read it as: **For Your love [*dodekha*] is better than wine,** or as: **For your love [*dodayikh*] is better** than wine? The first version, which is in the masculine form, would be a reference to God, whereas the second version, in the feminine, would be a reference to the Jewish people. Rabbi Yishmael **said to him** that it should be read in the feminine: **For your love [*dodayikh*] is better** than wine. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: The matter is not so, as another** verse **teaches with regard to it: "Your ointments [*shemanekha*] have a goodly fragrance**... therefore do the maidens love you" (Song of Songs 1:3). This phrase, which appears in the next verse, must be describing a male, and therefore it can be deduced that the preceding verse is also in the masculine form.

Instead, *he'si'o ledavar acher* — he diverted him to another matter. He asked Rabbi Yishmael how he reads a particular verse from Shir HaShirim: *ki tovim dodecha miyayin* — is the word *dodecha* to be read in the masculine form or in the feminine form, *doda'ich*? Rabbi Yishmael said he reads it in the feminine. Rabbi Yehoshua responded: it cannot be so, because the very next verse uses the masculine: *lerach shemanecha tovim* — for the fragrance of your oils, which is clearly masculine. Since the two verses are parallel and contiguous, the grammatical gender must be consistent, and *dodecha* should therefore be understood as masculine.

The abruptness of this pivot — from a sharp halachic question about *isur hana'ah* to a grammatical discussion of Shir HaShirim — is itself part of the narrative. Rabbi Yehoshua is not answering the question because he does not have a satisfying answer. The Gemara will eventually take up the question of why the prohibition on non-Jewish cheese was not extended to *isur hana'ah*.

## Wine of Libation

### Wine Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יַיִן מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר חֵלֶב זְבָחֵימוֹ יֹאכְלוּ יִשְׁתּוּ יֵין נְסִיכָם״, מָה זֶבַח אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף יַיִן נָמֵי אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where do we** derive that **wine** belonging to gentiles is prohibited? **Rabba bar Avuh says** that **the verse states: "Who did eat the fat of their offerings, and drank the wine of their drink-offering"** (Deuteronomy 32:38). This verse juxtaposes the fat of gentile sacrifices to their wine: **Just as** deriving **benefit from** their **offering is prohibited, so too,** deriving **benefit from** their **wine** is **prohibited.**

The Gemara asks: *yayin minalan* — from where in the Torah do we derive the prohibition on *yayin nesech*? The question is more nuanced than it first appears. One might argue that anything used in idolatrous worship is automatically forbidden — so why should wine require a special derivation? The Gemara's question implies that it is not obvious whether the prohibition on *yayin nesech* is rabbinic in origin (*gezeira* of the Sages) or derives from a scriptural source, and it seeks to identify whether there is a *pasuk* that specifically grounds this prohibition.

### Deuteronomy Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶבַח גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּצָּמְדוּ לְבַעַל פְּעוֹר וַיֹּאכְלוּ זִבְחֵי מֵתִים״, מָה מֵת אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף זֶבַח נָמֵי אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. וּמֵת גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָלַן? אָתְיָא ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״ מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַתָּמׇת שָׁם מִרְיָם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְעָרְפוּ שָׁם אֶת הָעֶגְלָה בַּנָּחַל״, מָה לְהַלָּן אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: ״כַּפָּרָה״ כְּתִיב בָּהּ, כְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive the prohibition with regard to **an offering itself?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "They joined themselves also unto Baal of Peor, and ate the offerings to the dead"** (Psalms 106:28). This verse teaches that **just as** deriving **benefit from a corpse is prohibited, so too,** deriving **benefit from an offering** of idolatry **is prohibited.** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive the prohibition of **a corpse itself?** The Gemara answers: It **is derived from** a verbal analogy between the words **"there"** and **"there"** employed **with regard to** the **heifer whose neck is broken** to absolve a city from bearing responsibility for the death of a visitor. **It is written here: "And Miriam died there"** (Numbers 20:1), **and it is written there:** "And the elders of that city shall bring down the heifer unto a rough valley, which may neither be plowed nor sown, **and shall break the heifer's neck there in the valley"** (Deuteronomy 21:4). **Just as there,** deriving **benefit from** the heifer **is prohibited, so too here,** deriving **benefit from** a corpse is **prohibited.** **And there, from where do we** learn that deriving benefit from the heifer is prohibited? The Sages **said in the school of Rabbi Yannai:** A term of **atonement is written with regard to** the heifer whose neck is broken (Deuteronomy 21:8), just **as** it is written with regard to **sacrificial** animals. This teaches that deriving benefit from the heifer is prohibited, just as deriving benefit from sacrificial animals is prohibited.

Rava bar Avuha provides the answer by citing a verse in Deuteronomy 32: *asher chelev zevacheimo yocheilu yishtu yein nesichem* — those who eat the fat of their sacrifices shall drink the wine of their libations. The verse places the *zevach* (the sacrifice) and the *yayin nesech* (the libation wine) in direct juxtaposition. From this, the Gemara derives that the status of the wine of libation can be inferred by analogy to the sacrificial offering itself. The Gemara will then proceed in subsequent passages to work through a chain of textual juxtapositions: if the *zevach* is compared to the wine in this verse, and if the *zevach* is compared elsewhere to something that carries *isur hana'ah*, and if that status can be traced through the laws of *eglah arufah* and the case of Miriam and the domain of *kodshim*, then we can ultimately establish a scriptural basis for the prohibition against deriving benefit from *yayin nesech*. That full chain of analysis carries the Gemara into the material ahead.
