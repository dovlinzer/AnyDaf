# Taanit 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Great to Anonymous

### Theme Shift

The daf opens in the middle of 21b, and it is worth pausing to appreciate the thematic shift that has been building. The preceding material dealt with figures of towering individual stature — Choni HaMa'agel looms large in the background, even before the Gemara has formally introduced his story, and alongside him we have encountered figures such as Rav Adda bar Ahava, in whose merit walls refrained from falling, and Nachum Ish Gamzu, whose presence likewise protected buildings from collapse. These are figures one might describe as *echad bedoro* — singular individuals of their generation, possessing extraordinary personal merit.

The Gemara now pivots deliberately away from this model. What follows is a sustained meditation on anonymous people performing small deeds of kindness, and the remarkable power such deeds carry. The shift is profound: we move from the great and the famous to the unrecognized and the modest, from dramatic miraculous protection to the quiet infusion of *yashrus* into the fabric of daily life.

### Midah KeNeged Midah

Two brief examples anchor this new theme. An entire neighborhood was spared from pestilence because of a person who had lent shovels to others to dig graves — since pestilence causes death, there is an evident *midah keneged midah* at work. Another neighborhood was saved from fire because a woman had shared her heated oven. Here too the *midah keneged midah* is transparent. These are not grand gestures; they are the ordinary generosities of ordinary people, and yet they carry the weight of divine protection for entire communities.

## Rav Yehuda

### Locusts

With this thematic backdrop established, the Gemara turns to a series of stories about sages who were acutely sensitive to the potential spread of calamity. They said to Rav Yehuda: *atu kimtzei* — locusts are arriving. He immediately declared a fast. The people around him said *lo ka mafsidan* — don't worry, it won't really affect us. Presumably they felt the locusts were still far away. Rav Yehuda responded: *zavda aisu bahadehayehu?* — did they perhaps bring their lunch with them? His point was sharp: do not trivialize the threat. The locusts will consume the crops; the only way they could spare you is if they arrived with their own provisions. This connects to the theme of the Mishnah regarding distant places joining the fast, though the Gemara does not make this explicit here.

### Plague Species

The Gemara then relates a second incident involving Rav Yehuda. He was told *ika motana bechazirei* — there is a pestilence among the pigs — and he declared a fast. The concern is that such a disease could cross the species barrier and affect human beings. The Gemara raises the question: perhaps Rav Yehuda holds that a plague spreading among one species will spread to all species — *makat meshulachat miMin echad meshulachat mikol haminim*? The Gemara deflects this generalization: *shani chazirei dedamyan ma'ayeihu livnei inshi* — pigs are a special case because their intestines are similar to human intestines, making cross-species transmission particularly plausible in this instance. The observation is empirical rather than theoretical, and the Gemara resists extending it into a universal principle. Indeed, some of the most devastating diseases in history have been precisely those that crossed species lines.

## Distant Plagues

### Shmuel

The Gemara now presents a parallel series involving Shmuel, Rav Yehuda's teacher. They told Shmuel: *ika mosana beChuzai* — there is a plague in Chuzai, a distant place. He declared a fast. They objected: is it not far away? He replied: *leka ma'abra hacha depasik lei?* — is there no crossing-point, no ford or pass, that would stop it from reaching us? There is nothing to prevent it from spreading here. The concern about disease spreading over distance thus connects back to the Mishnah's discussion of surrounding areas that join the fast, though the Gemara will revisit the precise scope of this principle.

It is worth noting that among the categories listed in the Mishnah, *dever* — plague — was notably not included among those for which one is *matri'in bechol makom*, proclaiming everywhere. The Mishnah indicated: *vechen ir she-yesh bah dever umapoles, oso ha-ir misanis umasri'a, vechol sevivoseha misanios velo masrios* — the immediate surroundings fast, but not distant places. Yet here Shmuel is fasting for *dever* in a place not considered its immediate surroundings.

### Rav Nachman

The series continues with Rav Nachman. He was told: *ika mosana be-Eretz Yisrael* — there is a plague in Eretz Yisrael. He, sitting in Bavel, declared a fast. This is a considerably larger leap: from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel, and moreover for *dever*, which the Mishnah treats as only a local concern. His reasoning was expressed in a striking phrase: *im gvira loka shefacha lo kol sheken?* — if the mistress of the house is being smitten, will the maidservant not certainly be affected as well? His framing is not naturalistic but metaphysical or theological: if Eretz Yisrael is being punished, Bavel will not be spared either.

### Caravan Spread

The Gemara now analyzes these two cases together. If Rav Nachman's reasoning rests on the *gvira-shefacha* principle — Eretz Yisrael to Bavel — what follows is that this logic would *not* apply to two places both in *Chutz La'aretz*, *shifcha ve-shifcha*, maidservant to maidservant, so to speak. Yet Shmuel fasted for a plague in Chuzai, also in Bavel, also *Chutz La'aretz*. How so?

The Gemara resolves: *shani hasam keivan de-ika shayrusa de-levai ve-asya bahadya bahadya* — that case is different, since there are caravans that travel between those places, accompanying the disease and bringing it along. People traveling between those cities carry the disease with them. This is the practical reason Shmuel said there is no crossing that would stop it: the regular traffic between these places is itself the vector of transmission. The Mishnah's limited scope for *dever* thus gives way to a more dynamic assessment — what matters is not raw geographic distance but the degree of human travel and contact between places.

In sum: Rav Nachman's fast for Eretz Yisrael is explained theologically, while Shmuel's fast for Chuzai is explained epidemiologically. Both represent an expansion beyond the Mishnah's literal parameters, each justified on its own terms.

## Abba Umna

### Heavenly Greetings

The Gemara now resumes the central theme of anonymous individuals possessing remarkable merit. *Abba Umna* — Abba the bloodletter — would receive a daily greeting from the heavenly *yeshiva*. Abaye received such a greeting every *erev Shabbos*, and Rava only once a year, on *erev Yom Kippur*. Here, set against the names of two of the greatest Amoraim, stands a bloodletter whom no one had previously heard of — and he outranks them both in this daily heavenly recognition. The contrast is striking and deliberate, continuing the Gemara's sustained meditation on the hidden greatness of unassuming individuals.

### Deeds Explained

Abaye felt despondent upon learning this — *chalusha da'atei de-Abaye mishum de-Abba Umna* — and the heavenly voice replied: *lo matzis leme'evad ke-ovdei* — you cannot replicate his deeds. What, then, were the deeds of Abba Umna?

When he performed *milta* — bloodletting — he would seat men separately and women separately, since the procedure required exposing parts of the body and mixing the sexes would not be *tzeni'us*. Beyond this, he had fashioned a special garment with a pre-made slit positioned precisely at the shoulder, through which the bloodletting horn was already inserted. When a woman came to him, he would dress her in this robe so that the procedure could be performed without his seeing her uncovered body. The care taken here — both the separation of the sexes as a general matter and the specially designed garment for female patients — reflects an extraordinary commitment to *tzni'us* in the context of his professional practice.

He also maintained a private box where patients could leave payment discreetly as they departed. Those who had money would deposit it; those who could not afford to pay would simply leave without embarrassment — *deleis leh lo michsif*. There was no collection moment, no direct exchange of money, no opportunity for a patient to be shamed by an inability to pay.

### Torah Students

When a *tzurva me-rabbanan* — a Torah student — came for treatment, Rav Abba Umna would not charge any fee — *agra minei lo shakil* — even if the student could have afforded to pay. This was professional courtesy of the most generous sort. Moreover, after the student rose from the procedure, Abba Umna would press money into his hands and say: *zil barei nafshach* — go and nourish yourself, recognizing that after bloodletting a person might feel weakened and hungry. He gave them money for a proper meal. Not only did he forgo his fee — he actively subsidized their recovery.

One participant in the shiur raised an important observation: when the heavenly voice tells Abaye *you cannot do like his deeds*, this should not be read as a comment on Abaye's level of righteousness. Rather, Abaye sat in the *beit medrash*; he did not have the daily opportunity to do these kinds of things. The person in the world, in the marketplace, in a profession that brings him into contact with suffering people every single day, has a kind of access to *gemilus chasadim* that the scholar sequestered in learning simply does not. These are deeds born of circumstance and opportunity, and that, it seems, is precisely the Gemara's point.

### Bedding Test

One day, Abaye sent two of his students to test Abba Umna — *shadar Abaye zuga de-rabbanan le-mivdakeih*. Abba Umna welcomed them warmly, fed them, gave them drink, and prepared fine bedding for them to sleep on. In the morning, the students rolled up the expensive bedding, took it with them, and went out to the marketplace — they had effectively stolen it.

When they encountered Abba Umna in the market, they said to him: *le-shimei mar heichi shavu* — tell us, what do you think these are worth? They asked him to appraise the very items they had taken from him. He gave them a figure. They pressed: perhaps it is worth more? He replied: *be-hachi shaklinu* — that is what I paid for them. He knew the items, understood what had happened, and said nothing about the theft.

### Pidyon Shvuyim

They then told him: *didach ninhu ve-shaklinu minach* — they are yours, and we took them from you. They asked: *be-mai chashatin* — what did you suspect us of? He answered: *amina pidyon shvuyim iklu lehu le-rabbanan* — I assumed that the Torah scholars must have had a pressing need for funds, perhaps to redeem captives, and they were embarrassed to ask me directly. He did not entertain the thought that they had simply stolen.

When they urged him to take back the items, he declined: *me-hahu sha'ata asachtinhu mi-da'tai le-tzedakah* — from the moment I assumed you would use them for *pidyon shvuyim*, I had already mentally dedicated them to charity in my heart. He would not reclaim them.

This story evokes a well-known account told of the Chofetz Chaim: that when guests he had taken in overnight made off with his silver *menorahs*, he ran after them calling *ani mochel, ani mochel* — I forgive, I forgive — so that they would not be guilty of the prohibition of theft. A lawyer recounting this story to a judge was asked: do you really believe that? He replied: I don't know, but I can tell you they don't tell stories like that about you and me.

The larger significance of Abba Umna's story is profound. It is not that his individual acts are unheard of in kind; it is that they represent the total and quiet infusion of *yashrus* and sensitivity into every dimension of his professional life — in his treatment of patients, in his protection of their dignity, in his generosity toward Torah students, in his response to what looked like outright theft.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### City Protection

The Gemara turns now to Rava's despondence at receiving his heavenly greeting only once a year, compared to Abaye's weekly greeting. The heavenly response was: *mistayecha de-mageni zachutach akula karchach* — it is sufficient for you that your merit protects the entire city. This is a remarkable consolation: even if Rava stands below Abaye on this particular hierarchy of *Olam Haba* greetings, his merit functions as a shield for an entire city. One might point out that Abaye's merit presumably protected his city as well, which means the distinction between the two remains somewhat unexplained — but the Gemara does not resolve this fully. What it does underscore is the recurring theme: the hierarchy of *zechut* is not transparent to human perception, and even those we consider the greatest must look upward and recognize those who surpass them.

## Rav Beroka

### Olam Haba Query

The Gemara introduces Rav Beroka Choza'ah, who would frequent the marketplace of Bei Lapat. Eliyahu the prophet would regularly appear to him there — a mark of his own considerable righteousness. Rav Beroka once asked Eliyahu: *ika behai shuka bar alma de-ati* — is there anyone in this marketplace who is deserving of *Olam Haba*? The question presumably referred to others, not himself. Eliyahu answered: no.

### Unlikely Candidate

As they stood there, a man appeared who was wearing black shoes — not the Jewish practice — and whose garment bore no *tzitzit*, or more precisely, no string of *techelet*. Eliyahu pointed to him: *hai bar alma de-ati hu* — this man is a *ben olam haba*. Rav Beroka ran after him and asked: *mai ovdach* — what are your deeds? The man replied: *zil idna ve-ta lemachar* — go now and come back tomorrow.

## Prison Guard

### Prisoner Separation

The next day, Rav Beroka returned and asked again. The man identified himself: *zandukna ana* — I am a prison guard. *Ve-asarna gavra lachod ve-nashi lachod* — I lock up male prisoners separately from female prisoners to prevent immoral behavior and the possibility of assault. He went further: *ve-ramina puraya bein hanei le-hanei ki heicha de-lo leitu lidei issura* — he would place his own bed between the two sections so that no transgression could occur. The commitment to *tzni'us* as a central organizing value — seen already in Abba Umna's practice — recurs here with striking force.

### Woman Protection

He continued: *ki chazina bat Yisrael de-yahvei nuchrim ala einaihu, masrana nafshai u-matzilna lah* — when I see a Jewish woman upon whom the non-Jewish prisoners have cast their eye with evil intent, I am willing to risk myself to save her. Once, there was a betrothed young woman in the prison upon whom the non-Jews had set their sights. He took the dregs of wine — *durdaya de-chamra* — and smeared them on the hem of her cloak, so that they would think she was in a state of *niddah* and recoil from her. Their revulsion at the thought of approaching a menstruating woman protected her, and he accomplished this without placing himself in direct physical confrontation.

### Decree Warning

As for his non-Jewish appearance — the black shoes, the absence of *tzitzit* — the man explained: *ayeilna ve-nafkina beini nuchrim ki heicha de-lo leidu de-yuhada'ei ana* — I move in and out among non-Jews so that they will not know I am Jewish. When an anti-Jewish decree is issued — *ki hava gezeirata* — *modena lehu le-rabbanan de-libau rachamei u-vatli le-gezeirataihu* — I inform the Rabbis so that they can pray and the decree will be annulled.

When Rav Beroka asked why he had not answered the first day, the man explained: at that moment he had just learned of a new decree and needed to go immediately to warn the Rabbis. He could not afford to be delayed answering questions.

The Gemara then notes — and Rav Beroka's earlier question prompted this comparison — that Eliyahu had initially said there was *no one* in that marketplace deserving of *Olam Haba*. This requires a moment's reflection. The principle of *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek le-olam haba* — that all of Israel has a share in the world to come — is actually not standard rabbinic theology. Standard rabbinic theology holds that *tzaddikim* earn *olam haba* and *resha'im* face *gehinnom*, with the *beinonim* in uncertain status. The reassuring declaration that begins tractate Sanhedrin — *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek* — was added later as an introductory statement so that the tractate would not begin with a harsh enumeration of those who have *no* share, but it represents an outlier in the broader sweep of rabbinic theological thought.

The deeper significance of this figure is perhaps the most powerful expression of the Gemara's recurring theme. Here is not merely an anonymous person, but a person who *looks like a sinner* — no *kippa*, no *tzitzit*, dressed like a non-Jew. The person you would most readily dismiss, or worse, condemn, is in fact among those with the greatest merit. And notably, unlike the earlier stories where righteous individuals protected people through their metaphysical *zechut* — as Rav Adda bar Ahava's merit held up walls — this man saves people through direct, worldly, practical action: separating prisoners, outwitting would-be assailants, warning Rabbis of decrees. His *hashpa'a* on the world is entirely this-worldly.

## Jesters

### Joy & Peace

As Rav Beroka and Eliyahu stood speaking, two men approached. Eliyahu said: *hani nami bnei alma de-ati* — these two also have a share in *Olam Haba*. Rav Beroka asked them what they did. They replied: *inash beduchei anan* — we are jesters. *Mevadchinan atzvei* — we make sad people laugh. And when they see two people who are quarreling with one another — *de-it lehu tigra* — *tarchinnan ve-avdinan lehu shelama* — they work to lighten the atmosphere and bring them to make peace with one another.

This passage was widely cited in early Chassidic literature as testimony to the power of joy and laughter. But what strikes here, in the context of all that has preceded, is the consistent pattern: people whom society would consider the most marginal and lowly — a bloodletter, a prison guard, a pair of clowns — are the ones the Gemara holds up as exemplars of *Olam Haba*. And in each case their deeds are deeply practical, utterly this-worldly: protecting dignity, preventing assault, bringing laughter to a grieving person, reconciling enemies.

One might add, as a participant observed: these are all people who are *in the world*, working, and it is precisely their working lives that give them the opportunity for these acts. The scholar in the *beit medrash* cannot do what Abba Umna did, not because he is less righteous, but because he does not have that particular field of action. There is something here that validates the entire rabbinic enterprise in a deeper sense — the claim that *kedushah* is not the province of those who withdraw from the world, but of those who inhabit it with full integrity.

## Mishnah Matri'in

### Four Categories

The Gemara returns to the Mishnah's halachic material. *Al elu matri'in bechol makom* — for the following one calls out in every place. The term *matri'in* minimally refers to special prayers; it may also incorporate fasting, and the Mishnah leaves some ambiguity. The Rabbis taught: *al ha-shidafon ve-al ha-yerakon* — blight and jaundice, diseases of grain; *ve-al ha-arbeh ve-al ha-chasil* — locusts in their various forms; *ve-al chaya ra'ah* — dangerous wild animals appearing in inhabited areas.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva held: for *shidafon* and *yerakon*, even the slightest amount — *bechol shehu* — is sufficient to trigger the universal proclamation. There is no minimum threshold; the slightest sign of grain disease requires everyone to pray everywhere. Similarly, regarding locusts: *afilu lo nira be-Eretz Yisrael ela kanaf echad, matri'in aleihem* — even if only a single wing is sighted in Eretz Yisrael, the response must be universal, because locusts travel in swarms and a single sighting is ominous for all. Whether Rabbi Akiva is disagreeing with the *Tanna Kamma* or merely explicating it is not entirely resolved.

## Wild Animals

### Location Time

The Gemara elaborates on the case of *chaya ra'ah*. When is a dangerous wild animal considered *meshulachat* — a spreading, rampage-type problem that triggers the public response — and when is it an isolated occurrence? *Nir'asa ba-ir* — seen in a city — that is *meshulachat*. *Ba-sadeh* — in the field — is *eina meshulachat*. *Ba-yom* — in the daytime — is *meshulachat*; *ba-layla* — at night — is *eina meshulachat*.

### Behavior Indicators

Further criteria: if the animal sees two people and runs after them both, *meshulachat* — an animal that pursues multiple people has become dangerously untamed. If it hides from two people, *eina meshulachat*. If it attacked two people and ate one of them, *meshulachat*. Somewhat counterintuitively, if it ate both of them, *eina meshulachat* — perhaps it was merely hungry, and a hungry animal acting out of hunger is different from an animal on an unprovoked rampage.

### Contextual Analysis

The Gemara identifies an internal contradiction in these criteria. It was said: seen in the city — *meshulachat*, regardless of day or night. But separately it was said: daytime is *meshulachat*, nighttime is not. Which standard governs?

The Gemara resolves: the two conditions are conjunctive, not disjunctive. An animal is *meshulachat* only if it appears *in the city* and *in the daytime*. If it comes to the city only at night, the concern is lesser. And if it is in the field, even by day, it is not yet *meshulachat* — unless it additionally takes action.

There is then a further contradiction: it was said that chasing two people is *meshulachat*, while hiding from them is *eina meshulachat* — implying that merely standing without running away is an intermediate case that is *meshulachat*. But separately it was suggested that even standing in a field is not a problem. The Gemara resolves: *kan be-sadeh ha-smucha le-agam kan be-sadeh she-eina smucha le-agam* — two types of field: a field adjacent to a forest or swamp, and a field that is not.

The result is a graduated analysis. In the city, daytime: *meshulachat* by appearance alone. In a field near a forest: only if the animal actively chases people, since near its natural habitat a bolder presence is less alarming. In a field not near a forest: even if it merely stands without fleeing from people, *meshulachat*, since that itself is anomalous behavior. And in the forest itself, the animal's home terrain, even a killing is not automatically *meshulachat* — unless it killed people and did not eat them, demonstrating aggression unconnected to hunger.

### Infant Case

The Gemara also addressed the case of *alta la-gag ve-natla tinok me-arisa* — an animal that went up to a rooftop and took an infant from its cradle — which is *meshulachat*. Rav Pappa explains this involves the hide of hunters — a concealed location where, it seems, the infant was being kept, making the act of theft by a wild animal even more remarkable. The point is that this remains *meshulachat* even in such unusual circumstances.

## Sword

### Peaceful Sword

The Gemara next addresses *cherev* — troop movements — as a category requiring proclamation *bechol makom*. The Rabbis taught: *cherev she-amru eino tzarich lomar cherev she-eino shel shalom* — it goes without saying that hostile troops constitute a crisis. *Ela afilu cherev shel shalom* — even troops on a peaceful passage, not directed against the local population but merely crossing through on their way to fight elsewhere, require the public fast. Once armies are on the march, no one knows where things will end.

### Paroh Necho

The paradigmatic case of a *cherev shel shalom* is that of Paroh Necho, passing through Eretz Yisrael in the time of King Yoshiyahu. Paroh Necho was marching eastward to wage war against other nations; his quarrel was not with Yoshiyahu or with Yehuda. Yet Yoshiyahu went out to block his passage, and the result was Yoshiyahu's death.

The Gemara cites the verse: Paroh Necho sent messengers to Yoshiyahu saying: *ma li valach melech Yehuda* — what business do I have with you, king of Yehuda? *Lo alecha ata ha-yom* — I am not coming against you today. *Ki el beit milchamti* — I am going to the place of my war. *Ve-elohim amar le-vahalani* — and God has commanded me to hurry. *Chadal lecha me-elohim asher imi ve-al yashchitecha* — desist from opposing God who is with me, lest He destroy you.

The end of the story is Yoshiyahu's mortal wounding by archers, his being carried back to die. It is a story that resonates in many of the *kinot* recited on Tisha B'Av.

### Yoshiyahu Punishment

The Gemara asks: *mah elohim asher imi* — what is this "God who is with me" that Paroh Necho invokes? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *zu avoda zara* — he meant his idol, his god in the lowercase sense. This gave Yoshiyahu a reason to discount the warning: *ho'il ve-ka batach be-avoda zara, nachilina lei* — since he relies on idolatry, I can prevail against him.

The verse then says *vayoru ha-morim la-melech Yoshiyahu* — the archers shot at King Yoshiyahu — and he cried out *ha'avirouni ki hechaleti me'od*. The Gemara asks what *hechaleti* means. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *mela'med she-asu kol gufo ki-kivra* — they made his entire body like a sieve, pierced through with innumerable arrows.

The Gemara then asks: *mipnei ma ne'enash Yoshiyahu* — why was Yoshiyahu punished? He was among the most righteous kings. Rav Shmuel bar Nachmeni said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *mipnei she-haya lo le-malech be-Yirmiyahu ve-lo nimlach* — because he should have consulted the prophet Yirmiyahu and did not.

### Yoshiyahu Reasoning

What was Yoshiyahu's own reasoning? He derived it from the verse in the *berachot*: *ve-cherev lo ta'avor be-artzechem* — no sword shall pass through your land. He reasoned: *ilima cherev she-eino shel shalom* — if this means hostile troops will not attack us, that is already implied by *ve-nasati shalom ba-aretz*; peace in the land means no one attacks us. The verse adding *no sword shall pass through* must therefore mean something beyond that — *afilu shel shalom*, even a peaceful sword, even troops merely transiting, shall not pass through when the nation is worthy. He concluded that the generation was worthy, that he was a righteous king, and therefore he was obligated to uphold this blessing.

### Generation Unworthiness

The Gemara counters: *ve-hu eino yodeia she-ein doro domeh yafeh* — he did not know that his generation was not worthy. He may have been righteous, but the sins of his generation undermined the very blessing he sought to invoke on their behalf.

As he lay dying, Yirmiyahu saw his lips moving. Concerned — *shema chas ve-shalom milsa de-lo hagna amar* — perhaps in his pain he was saying something improper, perhaps complaining against God — Yirmiyahu leaned close and heard that Yoshiyahu was in fact *matzdik ha-din*, affirming the justice of God's judgment. He was whispering: *tzadik hu Hashem ki fihu marisi* — God is righteous, for I have defied His word.

The Gemara ties this to Megillat Eichah, authored by Yirmiyahu: this verse from Eichah is understood as Yirmiyahu recording the final words of Yoshiyahu. And Yirmiyahu then opened his lament: *ruach apeinu mashiach Hashem* — the breath of our nostrils, God's anointed — he who had been the great hope of the generation, who might have been the *mashiach* who could have prevented the destruction, was caught, stumbled, because the generation had gone so far astray that even a righteous king could not fully escape the consequences. This is indeed the subject of the *kina* beginning *vayekonen Yirmiyahu al Yoshiyahu*.

As for the precise relevance to *cherev shel shalom*: Yoshiyahu's own *derashah* demonstrates the principle — the *beracha* guarantees that even a peaceful sword shall not pass through, which means even a peaceful sword is by nature a cause for concern and action. The fact that he was punished for acting on that reasoning does not invalidate the underlying principle; it was his generation's unworthiness, not the principle itself, that brought about his downfall.

## Diseased Grain

### Grain Amount

The Gemara relates that elders descended from Yerushalayim to their cities and declared a fast because of an oven-full — *malei pi kivshan* — of *yerakon* observed in Ashkelon. A question arises: when the text says *kemalei pi tannur*, does it mean the quantity of grain that literally fills an oven, or the quantity of grain that, when baked into bread, would fill an oven? Since bread expands in baking, the latter represents considerably less grain. The Gemara suggests the answer is bread filling the mouth of the oven.

### Teiku

But a further ambiguity remains: *kekisuyah de-tannura* — is it just enough bread to cover the opening of the oven? Or *kidara de-rifta de-hadra lei le-fuma de-tannura* — bread stacked from the floor of the oven all the way up to and filling the opening? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. The story itself implies there is some minimum amount below which one would not declare a fast — which stands in some tension with Rabbi Akiva's view that even the slightest amount requires universal proclamation.

## Wolves Story

### Tumah Analysis

The Gemara then cites Ulla in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: *ma'aseh ba'u ze'evim shnei tinokot ve-kiyium derech beit ha-re'i* — a story in which wolves devoured two children and excreted them. The case came before the *chachamim*, who ruled: *teharu et ha-basar ve-tim'u et ha-atzamot* — the flesh, having passed through the animal's digestive tract and been transformed into excrement, is no longer halachically considered meat and is therefore *tahor*; the bones, remaining structurally bones, retain their status and are *tamei*.

### Discourse Shift

One cannot help but notice the sharp shift in register. Within the very material dealing with the halachos of *ta'anit*, we had been engaged with questions that carried a sense of religious urgency and lived experience — what does it mean when a plague spreads, what does it mean when troops march, how do we respond as a community? The discussion of the diseased grain and now the *tumah* and *taharah* status of what wolves excreted is clinical, detached, almost coldly analytical. It is the mode of standard halachic discourse, and that is not a criticism — it is how *halacha* operates. But the contrast with the more experientially engaged halachic discussions earlier in the tractate is notable and worth marking.

## Shabbat Proclamation

### Four Situations

The Mishnah states: *al elu matri'in be-Shabbat* — for the following one cries out even on Shabbat. The Rabbis taught: *ir she-heikifuha acherim o nahar, ve-achas sefina ha-mitarefet ba-yam, ve-achas yachid she-nirdaf mipnei acherim u-mipnei listim u-mipnei ruach ra'ah* — a city under siege or threatened by a flooding river, a ship caught in a storm at sea, or an individual being pursued by hostile forces, by bandits, or by an evil spirit.

### Individual Fast

To this the Rabbis add: *al kulan yachid rashai lehageif et atzmo be-ta'anis* — in all these situations, an individual may afflict himself with a fast. This is noteworthy: the general rule is that *ta'anis tzibbur* is decreed by the community. The *chiddush* here is that in these urgent situations — particularly given that this may be referring specifically to Shabbat, when a public fast would not be declared — an individual may nevertheless fast on his own.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *ein ha-yachid rashai lehageif et atzmo be-ta'anis*. An individual may not afflict himself with a fast in these circumstances. His reason: *shema yitzarech la-briyot ve-ein ha-briyot merachamim alav* — lest he subsequently need others to care for him and others will not have compassion on him, either because they feel he brought his weakness upon himself or simply out of indifference. One may not put oneself in a position of need through voluntary deprivation.

### Soul Alive

Rav Yehuda said in Rav's name: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosi? The verse *vayehi ha-adam le-nefesh chaya* — the person became a living soul — is interpreted as a divine command: *neshama she-natati becha, hachaye* — the soul I have given you, keep it alive.

But Rabbi Yosi's position may be operating on an additional level, consistent with his position elsewhere in the Mishnah. Rabbi Yosi had said earlier in the tractate that one may cry out on Shabbat to summon people to *action*, but not to add special *tefillos*. His reasoning there was that the correct response to an emergency is to act, not to pray. Here, similarly, Rabbi Yosi may be saying: when the ship is sinking, when the river is flooding, when the city is under siege — this is not the moment to fast and weaken yourself. This is the moment to act, to lift sandbags, to fight, to save lives. Fasting on Shabbat in these circumstances does not help the cause; it undermines it. Keep yourself strong and act. This reading would make Rabbi Yosi's position here entirely consistent with his position in the Mishnah and would ground the principle of *hachaye* — keep the soul alive — not merely as a prohibition on self-harm but as an affirmative command: when there is work to be done, keep yourself capable of doing it.
