# Menachot 59b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemen v'Levonah

### Intro: Menachot Types

The *mishnah* on 59b continues the discussion of the different types of *menachos* and their constituent ingredients. The *mishnah* on 59a had established that some *menachos* receive both oil and *levonah* (frankincense), some receive one or the other, and some receive neither. The two offerings that receive neither are the *minchas chotei* — the sin-offering *minchah* brought in the context of a *korban oleh v'yored*, whose form varies according to the donor's economic means — and the *minchas kena'os*, the *minchah* of a *sotah*. In both of these cases the *Torah* states explicitly: *lo yasim aleha shemen v'lo yiten aleha levonah* — one may not place oil on it nor give frankincense upon it. This constitutes a negative prohibition, and it is the subject of the present *mishnah*.

### Oil/Frankincense Transgr…

The *mishnah* establishes that one transgresses the prohibition for placing oil alone and for placing *levonah* alone; even a single violation is sufficient. Furthermore, if one places oil on the *minchah*, the *mishnah* states *nasan aleha shemen pasalah* — one has not only transgressed but has invalidated the *minchah*, rendering it unfit for use. The *levonah*, by contrast, is different: *yelaktenah*, one simply picks it off, and the *minchah* remains *kasher*. This is because *levonah* — as has been noted previously — is not like salt or other loose substances but resembles crystals or pebble-like granules, and it can therefore be physically removed from the surface of the flour. One transgresses even though the violation is correctable.

The *mishnah* then adds: *nasan shemen al sheyareha eino over b'lo sa'aseh* — if one places oil upon the *shirayim*, the remnants of the *minchah* after the *kemitzah* has been taken, one does not transgress the negative prohibition. This is in contrast to the prohibition of *lo sei'aseh chametz mechlekam*, which applies even to remnants. The implication is that the prohibition of oil applies specifically before the *minchah* has been fully processed, not to the remnants afterward.

### Oil vs. Frankincense

A *baraita* is introduced that makes the distinction explicit. The verse states *lo yasim aleha shemen* — do not place oil on it — and *if* one places it, one has invalidated. One might think the same is true of *levonah*: place it and it is invalidated. The *baraita* explains the asymmetry: *posolni bashemen she'ee efshar lekato* — oil invalidates because it is impossible to gather it back up once absorbed — *u'machshirni be'levonah she'efshar lilketa* — but *levonah* leaves the *minchah kasher* because it can be picked off and removed. The physical reality of absorption versus removability is the operative distinction.

### Vessel Upon Vessel

The *mishnah* also clarifies that *nasan kli al gabei kli lo pasalah* — if one placed a vessel containing oil on top of the *minchah*, the *minchah* is not invalidated. The prohibition of *lo yasim aleha shemen* is understood to mean direct application; placing oil in a vessel that rests on the *minchah* does not constitute the prohibited act. By the same logic, such an act would presumably not constitute a transgression either.

One might raise the question — drawn from the laws of *lav hanitak la'aseh* — of why one transgresses when the *levonah* can be removed. The answer is that the concept of *lav hanitak la'aseh* requires a specific *mitzvat aseh* linked to the prohibition; the mere fact that a violation is physically reversible does not prevent transgression. One can, for example, turn on a light on *Shabbos* and extinguish it afterward, and the transgression has occurred regardless.

### Baraita: Oil/Frankincense

The *baraita* thus presents the rule cleanly: oil, once applied, is absorbed and cannot be recovered, so it invalidates; *levonah*, being crystalline and unabsorbed, can be picked off and the *minchah* restored. The word *ki chatas* — "for it is a *chatas*" — in the relevant verse signals that even after a foreign substance has been placed, the *minchah* retains its status as a *chatas*, provided it can be corrected.

A question arises regarding who may actually transgress this prohibition. It need not be the *kohen* alone. Since some stages of the *minchah*'s preparation — in particular *kiddush kli*, the sanctification in a vessel — can occur before the *kohen* takes over at the stage of *kemitzah*, the owner of the *minchah* could theoretically apply oil or *levonah* and thereby transgress.

### Ground Frankincense

*Rava bar bar Chanah* posed a question to *Rabbi Yochanan*: *natan lah levonah shechukah mahu* — what is the rule if one took *levonah* and ground it to a powder, reducing the crystals to something resembling fine salt? Such ground *levonah* would embed itself within the flour of the *minchah*, becoming so thoroughly mixed that it could not be gathered up and removed. Does this constitute an invalidation?

The question has two possible framings. The *baraita* said that *levonah* does not invalidate because *efshar lilketa* — it can be picked off. If that is the operative criterion, then ground *levonah*, which cannot be picked off, would invalidate the *minchah*. But perhaps the real criterion is simply *d'lo mivla* — that it is not absorbed into the substance of the flour itself. Ground *levonah* mixed with flour is, after all, analogous to *yavesh beyavesh* — discrete particles sitting adjacent to one another — even if the mixture is inseparable in practice. If non-absorption is the standard, then even ground *levonah* would not invalidate, since each granule merely rests beside each kernel without chemically infusing into it.

There is an additional practical complication: if ground *levonah* is mixed throughout the flour, how could one perform *kemitzah*? A *kemitzah* taken from such a mixture would be a *kemitzah chaserah* — deficient — since it would not consist entirely of the correct ingredient. The *gemara* does not directly address this concern at this stage, proceeding instead with the theoretical question of invalidation.

### Absorption vs. Removal

*Rav Huna* asks the same question of *Rabbi Yochanan* and frames it as follows: is the *kashrut* of *levonah* on a *minchah* predicated on the fact that *efshar lilketa* — it can physically be gathered up — or is it sufficient that *lo mivla* — it is not absorbed? The *mishnah* itself says *yelaktenah*, pick it off. The *baraita* likewise says it is *kasher* because it can be picked off. The *gemara* attempts to argue that these sources perhaps state *chada v'od* — one condition and an additional stronger one — meaning: first, it is not absorbed (*d'lo mivla*), and second, as a further advantage, it can actually be removed. On this reading, non-absorption alone might be sufficient.

However, when pressed, *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* cites a *baraita* that settles the matter. The *baraita* rules that if *levonah* was placed on a *minchas chotei* or *minchas kena'os*, one removes it and the *minchah* is *kesheirah*. Crucially, the *baraita* then specifies: if before the *levonah* was removed a *kohen* entertained a *machshavah* — a disqualifying intention — of *chutz lizmano* (the wrong time, yielding *pigul*) or *chutz limkomo* (the wrong place), the *minchah* is *pasul* but carries no *karet*. After the *levonah* is removed, a subsequent thought of *chutz lizmano* does create *pigul* with *karet*.

The proof is decisive: if the *minchah* were *kasher* even while the *levonah* remained (provided only that the *levonah* is not absorbed), one would expect a *pigul* thought at that stage to take full effect. But the *baraita* shows that while the *levonah* is present, the thought does not achieve the status of *pigul* — it produces *psul* without *karet* — because the *minchah* is in a *pasul* state. Only once the *levonah* is actually removed does the *minchah* return to full *kashrut*, and only then can a subsequent thought create *pigul* with *karet*. The conclusion is therefore that physical removal is required, not merely non-absorption. If the *levonah* cannot be removed — as in the case of ground *levonah* — the *minchah* remains *pasul*.

## Reversibility & Dachuy

### Baraita: Removal & Pigul

The *baraita* cited by *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* presents the following scenario in full: if *levonah* was placed on a *minchas chotei* or *minchas kena'os*, one picks off the *levonah* and it becomes *kesheirah*. If, however, before the *levonah* was removed a *kohen* had a thought of *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo*, the *minchah* is *pasul* and there is no *karet*. The reason a *chutz lizmano* thought does not generate *pigul* at this stage is that the *minchah* is currently *pasul* due to the presence of the *levonah*, and one cannot render a *pasul* offering into *pigul*. After the *levonah* is removed, the *minchah* is restored to *kashrut*, and a subsequent *chutz lizmano* thought would create full *pigul* with *karet*.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* interpret the *baraita* somewhat differently. According to *Tosafot*, the *pasul* mentioned in the *baraita* — the result of the *machshavah* before the *levonah* is removed — simply describes the existing state: the *minchah* is *pasul* because the *levonah* is on it, not because the thought itself created any new *psul*. On *Tosafot*'s reading, once the *levonah* is removed the *minchah* is fully restored. According to *Rashi*, the wrong thought itself, even though insufficient to constitute *pigul*, carries enough force to create an independent disqualification; and therefore even after removing the *levonah*, the *minchah* remains *pasul* by dint of the thought.

### Reversible Invalidity

This *baraita* raises a profound conceptual difficulty. The *minchah* was *kasher*, then became *pasul* (upon the addition of *levonah*), and then returns to being *kasher* (upon removal of the *levonah*). This is a striking *gemara*: we have an instance of reversible *psul*. The principle of *dichuy* — that once a *korban* has been rejected or pushed aside, it cannot be restored to its original status — would seem to apply here. How can something be *pasul* as a *korban* and then become *kasher* again?

This question is especially sharp because *dichuy* is not a concern limited to animals or living things; it applies to inanimate objects and certainly to offerings. The principle is: *mi she'nirah v'nidche* — something that was once valid, became invalid, and was pushed aside cannot simply re-enter the circle of eligibility.

The *gemara* as we have it reads *v'tehave pach* — why is the presence of *levonah* not treated like a vessel of oil overturned on the *minchah*, which would fully invalidate it? And further, why does any *machshavah* at all have effect if the *minchah* is already *pasul* and therefore *dachuy*?

### Tosafot Textual Criticism

*Tosafot* offers a remarkable analysis of the textual tradition here. *Tosafot* maintains that the original *girsa* of the *gemara* was simply *v'tehave dichuy* — let it be *dichuy*; how does the *minchah* escape the fate of being permanently pushed aside? At some point, a commentator wrote in the margin a *peirush* — an explanation — asking, *u'mai pasla b'machshava*, why does any thought have effect? The word *peirush*, written in an abbreviated form (*peh-yud* with a slash), was subsequently misread by a later copyist as the word *pach*, and the entire phrase was incorporated into the body of the text, generating the version we now have. *Tosafot* thus reconstructs how the current *girsa* came to be, and argues that the core question is simply the *dichuy* problem — the subsequent question about *machshavah* is a secondary gloss that became embedded in the text.

### Abaye's Answer

*Abaye* answers the *dichuy* question as follows: *chatas karya rachmana* — the *Torah* itself calls it a *chatas*, and by insisting on this designation even when the *levonah* has been placed upon it, the *Torah* is signaling that the *minchah* retains its fundamental status. It does not become fully *nidche* — fully cast aside — because the *Torah* is asserting its continued identity as a valid *chatas* that merely requires correction.

### Rava & Rav Ada

*Rava* offers an alternative answer: this entire passage follows the opinion of *Chanina Hamitzri*, who fundamentally does not subscribe to the principle of *dichuyin*. A *baraita* records that *Chanina Hamitzri* rules that on *Yom Kippur*, even if the *sa'ir hamishtale'ach* (the scapegoat) died and the blood of the other goat is already in the vessel, one brings a replacement goat *shelo b'hagralah* — without a new lottery — and partners it with the existing blood. In that scenario, the blood was technically *nidche* while no paired goat existed, yet it is reused once the problem is fixed. *Chanina Hamitzri* is thus the authority who does not recognize *dichuy* as a categorical bar, and the present *baraita* would be following his view.

### Rav Ashi: B'Yado

*Rav Ashi* advances yet another answer, one that operates without relying on either a special *pasuk* or *Chanina Hamitzri*'s dissenting position. He states the principle: *kol she'b'yado lo havei dichuya* — anything that lies within one's power to correct is never considered *dichuy* in the first place. The very concept of *dichuy* implies that the object has been cast away beyond recovery. Something that can be fixed immediately and easily — like scooping *levonah* off the surface of flour — was never truly rejected. It occupied a temporarily impaired state, but the impairment was always understood to be correctable, and so the *korban* never left its central standing.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Dachuy

*Rav Ada* adds that the reasoning of *Rav Ashi* is in fact the most compelling, and supports it from the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*, who is normally the authority who maintains the stringency of *dichuyin*. A *mishnah* records that *Rabbi Yehuda* rules: if the blood of the *Yom Kippur* service was spilled, the *sa'ir hamishtale'ach* must be left to die, and if the *sa'ir hamishtale'ach* died, the blood must be spilled — because in each case, the remaining element was *nidche* during the interval it had no valid partner. So *Rabbi Yehuda* takes *dichuy* seriously.

Yet a *baraita* also records that *Rabbi Yehuda* says that at the end of *Pesach*, one would fill a vessel with the mixed blood that had pooled on the floor of the *Beis Hamikdash* — blood from the various *korban Pesach* offerings — and throw it in a single casting against the base of the *mizbe'ach*, in case any individual's blood had accidentally spilled and never been properly applied. Here, blood that was at some point not in a *klei shareis* and therefore technically unusable is nevertheless employed once collected. The reason *Rabbi Yehuda* permits this is precisely *b'yado* — it was always within the ability of the *kohanim* to collect and reapply the blood. When the means of correction are readily at hand, even *Rabbi Yehuda* does not classify the situation as *dichuy*.

This demonstrates that *b'yado* is the operative distinction even for the strictest authority on *dichuy*. Not every situation where something is temporarily invalid constitutes true *dichuy*; only where the impairment lies beyond one's ability to rectify does the status of *dichuy* attach. The case of *levonah* placed on a *minchah*, where one can simply pick the *levonah* off, is therefore never *dichuy* — it is merely a correctable impairment that leaves the *minchah*'s fundamental status intact.

## Shiurim: Oil & Levonah

### Oil Shiur

Having established the nature of the prohibitions and the conceptual framework of *dichuy*, the *gemara* turns to the question of *shiurim* — the minimum quantities required for a transgression. *Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef* cites *Rabbi Yochanan* on the measure required for oil.

### Oil: Kol Shehu

*Rabbi Yochanan* rules: *natan mashehu shemen al gabei kezayit minchah pasal* — even the smallest amount of oil placed upon a *kezayit*'s worth of *minchah* invalidates. The reasoning proceeds from the verse *lo yasim* — the word *simah*, "placing," carries no inherent minimum; it can refer to depositing even the tiniest quantity. There is no implied *shiur* in the act of placing. However, there must be a meaningful quantity of *minchah* upon which the oil is placed, and we derive this from the word *aleha* — upon it — which implies that the *minchah* itself must be of some significance. The minimum measure of significance for an edible substance is a *kezayit*, since that is the smallest *shiur* recognized for eating. The requirement is therefore any amount of oil on at least a *kezayit* of *minchah*.

### Frankincense: Kezayit

The rule is reversed for *levonah*. *Rabbi Yochanan* rules: *natan kezayit levonah al gabei mashehu minchah pasal* — one must place a *kezayit*'s worth of *levonah* to transgress, but even on the smallest amount of *minchah*. The reasoning turns on the word *lo yiten* — "do not give." *Netinah*, giving, implies conveying something of substance; to give something suggests there was a thing of significance being transferred. Accordingly, the *levonah* must amount to a *kezayit*. As for the *minchah* itself, the word *aleha* now operates differently: because the *Torah* has already used *aleha* once (by oil), and invokes it again (by *levonah*), we apply the principle of *ein ribui achar ribui ela lema'et* — a doubled expression of inclusion actually serves to restrict — and therefore the *minchah* need only be the smallest amount.

### Double Language Rule

The principle of *ribui achar ribui* is worth noting. When the *Torah* uses an amplifying expression — such as *aleha* — once, it would normally include everything of significance. When it doubles that expression for a second prohibition, the redundancy is interpreted as deliberately limiting the scope: in the case of *levonah*, one transgresses even if the *minchah* is only a *mashehu*.

### Sima vs. Nitan

*Rav Yitzchak* then considers a further question. Now that we have established that *netinah* requires a *kezayit* of *levonah*, perhaps *simah* — the term used for oil — should similarly be read in light of *netinah* and require a *kezayit* of oil as well. He poses this as an open question: *mi banyan simah k'netinah* — do we translate and interpret *simah* through the lens of *netinah*, requiring a *kezayit* of oil too? Or does *simah* retain its ordinary meaning of any amount, even a *kol shehu*? The *gemara* leaves this as *teku* — unresolved.

### Tosafot on Nitan

*Tosafot* raises a related question: where does the principle that *netinah* implies a *kezayit* come from in the first place? *Rashi* traces it to the *parshah* of the past week, which states that one who inadvertently eats *terumah* belonging to a *kohen* must *v'natan l'kohen et hakodesh* — give the *kohen* the sanctified equivalent. The giving there is of something edible, and *chazal* understand that one must give something of substance — a *kezayit* — because that is the minimum measure of eating. The compensation, which automatically becomes *terumah* in the *kohen*'s hands, must therefore be a *kezayit*.

*Tosafot*, however, notes a difficulty: by *terumah*, even the tiniest amount of grain can exempt an entire heap (*keri*) from the status of *tevel*. If *netinah* requires a *kezayit*, how can a single grain accomplish *terumah*? *Tosafot* acknowledges this question without fully resolving it. One approach — noted in the shiur though not offered by *Tosafot* himself — distinguishes between two dimensions of the *mitzvah* of *terumah*: the act of separating even a single grain suffices to exempt the heap from *tevel*, while the distinct *mitzvah* of actually giving something to the *kohen* requires a substance of meaning and therefore a *kezayit*. *Tosafot* also notes that in other contexts, *netinah* measured against monetary value might require a *perutah* rather than a *kezayit*, depending on the lens through which the act of giving is being evaluated — whether one is thinking in terms of edibility or monetary worth.

## Oil on Remnants

### Remnants Rule

The *mishnah* establishes that oil placed upon the *shirayim* — the remnants of the *minchah* after *kemitzah* — does not constitute a transgression, in contrast to the prohibition that applies to the *minchah* itself before *kemitzah*.

### Frankincense After Oil

A *baraita* then raises the question: if one has already placed oil on a *minchah* — thereby rendering it *pasul* — and then proceeds to place *levonah* upon that same now-*pasul minchah*, does one transgress the prohibition of *levonah* as well?

### Tosafot Explanation

*Tosafot* explains that this is the crux of the *baraita*. The *hava amina* is that since the *minchah* is already *pasul* from the oil, placing *levonah* upon it may be analogous to placing oil on the remnants of an already-processed *minchah*, where one does not transgress. The counterargument — drawn from the case of *mechameitz achar chametz*, where one transgresses each time one continues to cause leavening — suggests that each successive prohibited act on the same *minchah* constitutes its own transgression. The *baraita* resolves this: the word *aleha* — upon it — indicates that both oil and *levonah* can be transgressed on the same *minchah*, and one does indeed transgress the *levonah* prohibition even after the *minchah* has been rendered *pasul* by oil. The word *aleha* refers to the body of the *minchah* itself, not to a *minchah* in some special status.

*Tosafot* notes, however, that the answer is ambiguous and can be read in the reverse direction as well: *aleha* might mean each prohibition must be committed on its own separate *minchah*, such that putting *levonah* after *shemen* on the same *pasul minchah* would not constitute a second transgression. The plain reading of the *baraita*, however, supports the first interpretation — that one does transgress both prohibitions even on a single *minchah*.

### Vessel Upon Vessel Revis…

The *baraita* also addresses the *kli al gabei kli* case again: if one places oil inside a vessel and sets that vessel on top of the *minchah*, *lo pasalah* — the *minchah* is not invalidated. The prohibition of *lo yasim aleha shemen* requires direct application to the body of the *minchah* itself. The *Torah*'s language, on this reading, speaks to the *minchah* directly — *b'gufa shel minchah* — and not to an indirect placement via an intervening vessel. Accordingly, such an act neither invalidates the *minchah* nor constitutes a transgression.

## Hagashah Categories

### Mishna Categories

The *gemara* now moves to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the procedures required for different types of *menachos* — specifically *hagashah* (touching to the altar) and *tenuphah* (waving). The *Torah* instructs that the *minchah* be brought to the *mizbe'ach*: *v'higishah el hamizbe'ach* — the *kohen* touches it to a corner of the *mizbe'ach*. The *mishnah* organizes the *menachos* into four categories: those requiring *hagashah* without *tenuphah*; those requiring both; those requiring *tenuphah* without *hagashah*; and those requiring neither.

### Hagashah Only: Ten

The *mishnah* begins with the largest category — those requiring *hagashah* without *tenuphah* — and lists ten. These are: *minchas hasoles* (plain flour with oil), *hamachavas* (the pan-fried variety), *hamarcheshes* (the deep-pan variety), *hachalos* and *harekikin* (the two forms of *ma'afeh tanur*, i.e., oven-baked — the bread-like *chalos matzos belulos bashemen* and the wafer-like *rekikei matzos meshuchim bashemen*). These five correspond to the basic variations of *menachos* enumerated in *Vayikra*. The remaining five on this list are: *minchas kohanim*, *minchas kohein mashiach*, *minchas nochrim* (a non-Jew's *minchah*), *minchas nashim* (a woman's *minchah*), and *minchas chotei*.

The list reaches ten rather than including the *minchas ha'omer*, because the *omer* also requires *tenuphah* and therefore falls into a different category. The *minchas chotei* does appear here, because the governing principle for *hagashah* is that any *minchah* of which some portion is burned on the *mizbe'ach* — i.e., where a *kemitzah* is taken and offered — requires *hagashah*.

### Kohanim Minchah

The inclusion of *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohein mashiach* on this list is noteworthy. Both of these are *menachos* of which nothing is eaten — the entire offering is burned on the *mizbe'ach*. Nevertheless, the *tanna kamma* holds that they require *hagashah*. The basis is the general rule that any *minchah* that is brought as a standalone offering and of which some portion reaches the *mizbe'ach* is subject to *hagashah*. *Minchas nesachim* — the meal-offering accompanying animal sacrifices — is excluded because it does not come independently.

### Rabbi Shimon: Kemitzah

*Rabbi Shimon* disputes the inclusion of *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohein mashiach*. He states: *ein bahem hagashah* — these do not receive *hagashah* — *lefi she'ein bahem kemitzah* — because they have no *kemitzah*. For *Rabbi Shimon*, the operative criterion for *hagashah* is not merely that some portion is burned, but specifically that a *kemitzah* is performed — i.e., the *kohen* takes a handful of the *minchah* that is then offered on the altar, while the remainder (*shirayim*) is eaten. In *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohein mashiach*, the *kohen* does not take a *kemitzah*; the entire offering is burned. *Rabbi Shimon* therefore holds: *v'chol she'ein bahem kemitzah ein bahem hagashah* — wherever there is no *kemitzah*, there is no *hagashah*.

### Rav Papa: Ten Count

*Rav Papa* observes that whenever the *mishnah* presents such a list in this tractate, it always numbers exactly ten. He notes this is not accidental; the count is designed to exclude the opinion of *Rabbi Shimon* regarding *chalos* and *rekikin*. *Rabbi Shimon* holds that for a *minchas ma'afeh tanur* one may bring five *chalos* and five *rekikin* together, creating a third variation beyond the two standard alternatives of ten of one kind or ten of the other. If *Rabbi Shimon*'s position were accepted, there would be eleven items on the list rather than ten. The count of exactly ten therefore implicitly rejects *Rabbi Shimon*'s allowance of a mixed offering of half *chalos* and half *rekikin*.

## Hagashah Sources

### Baraita Verses

The *gemara* seeks the scriptural basis for which *menachos* require *hagashah*. A *baraita* addresses this. The key verse is: *v'heiveisa et haminchah asher yei'aseh mei'eileh laHashem v'hikrivah el hakohein v'higishah el hamizbe'ach* — "and you shall bring the *minchah* that is made from these to God; bring it to the *kohen* and he shall touch it to the *mizbe'ach*." This verse follows the enumeration of the five standard variations of voluntary *menachos* in *Vayikra*, and its plain meaning extends *hagashah* to the entire *minchah*.

The *baraita* asks: had the verse said only *asher yei'aseh mei'eileh laHashem* without the word *minchah*, one might have thought that only the *kometz* itself — the portion going directly to God — requires *hagashah*. The verse's use of the word *haminchah* teaches that the entire *minchah*, including its *shirayim*, is the referent of *hagashah*.

### Cheshibat Tzadim

Even with this verse, one might know only that the standard five voluntary *menachos* require *hagashah*. How does one extend *hagashah* to the *minchas chotei*, which lacks oil and *levonah*?

The *baraita* proposes a logical analogy: just as the voluntary *minchah* requires *hagashah*, so too should the obligatory *minchas chotei* by analogy. The *gemara* challenges this: the voluntary *minchah* has oil and *levonah*, which makes it distinctive; perhaps only offerings with those ingredients require *hagashah*, and the *minchas chotei* — which lacks them — does not.

The *baraita* responds by invoking the *minchas sotah*, which also lacks oil and *levonah* yet explicitly receives *hagashah* from a verse of its own. From *minchas sotah* one can prove that absence of oil and *levonah* is no barrier to *hagashah*. But the *minchas sotah* requires *tenuphah* — perhaps that special feature accounts for its *hagashah* — and one cannot prove from it that *minchas chotei* receives *hagashah*.

The *baraita* then applies *cheshibat tzadim* — the method of eliminating distinguishing features. The voluntary *minchah* has oil and *levonah* but no *tenuphah*; the *minchas sotah* has *tenuphah* but no oil and *levonah*. Each has a distinctive feature absent from the other. Nevertheless, both share a common denominator — *kemitzah* — and both require *hagashah*. One is therefore entitled to include any *minchah* with *kemitzah*, such as the *minchas chotei*, in the requirement of *hagashah*.

Even this logical derivation can be challenged: the two source cases (*minchas nedavah* and *minchas sotah*) are both eligible to be brought by anyone, rich or poor, while the *minchas chotei* is restricted to the poor alone. Perhaps this difference in eligibility removes it from the analogy. It is for this reason that the verse *et haminchah* — with its extra *et* — is needed to include the *minchas chotei* explicitly. The logical argument brings us to the threshold of inclusion, but because a distinction can technically be drawn, a scriptural hook is required to confirm it.

### Rabbi Shimon Inclusions

*Rabbi Shimon* does not disagree with the ultimate *halachah* but uses the verse's language differently. He reads *v'heiveisa* — "and you shall bring" — as including the *minchas ha'omer* in the requirement of *hagashah*, linking it to the verse in the *omer* passage: *v'haveitem et omer reishit ktzirchem el hakohein* — "you shall bring the *omer* of the first of your harvest to the *kohen*." Similarly, *Rabbi Shimon* reads *v'hikrivah* — "and he shall bring it near" — as including the *minchas sotah*, connecting it to the verse *v'hikriv otah el hamizbe'ach* in the *sotah* passage.

The *minchas sotah*'s inclusion is then tested logically as well: if the *minchas chotei* — which lacks *tenuphah* — requires *hagashah*, then all the more so the *minchas sotah*, which additionally requires *tenuphah*, should require *hagashah*. But this logic is challenged: the *minchas chotei* is made of *solet* (fine wheat flour), while the *minchas sotah* is made of *kemach* (coarser barley flour), a seemingly inferior ingredient. Perhaps the *minchas sotah*'s lower grade removes it from the standard. Therefore the verse *v'hikrivah* must explicitly include it.

### Kemitzah = Hagashah

The *baraita* ultimately articulates the common denominator: *she'shavu le'kemitzah ve'shavu le'hagashah* — the cases that share *kemitzah* are precisely the cases that share *hagashah*. Any *minchah* upon which *kemitzah* is performed requires *hagashah*. This formulation follows *Rabbi Shimon*'s approach, since it is *kemitzah* — the taking of a partial portion for the altar while the remainder is eaten — rather than mere burning that determines whether *hagashah* applies. The *baraita*'s logic therefore implicitly supports *Rabbi Shimon*'s view that *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohein mashiach*, which have no *kemitzah*, do not require *hagashah*, in contrast to the *tanna kamma* who requires it for any *minchah* of which some portion is burned.
