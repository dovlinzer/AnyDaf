# Meilah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview & Setup

### Daf 20 Setup

Daf 20 of *Meilah* marks the culmination of the tractate, with only two dapim remaining. Despite the sense that we have barely gotten started, this daf brings us to the conceptual heart of the material: what precisely constitutes an act of *me'ilah*, and how does the law of *shlichut* operate within that framework.

### Previous Concepts

In the preceding material, we have examined various forms of *me'ilah* — acts through which one derives benefit (*hana'ah*) from consecrated property (*hekdesh*), particularly where such benefit involves a *pegam*, a diminishment of the object. We have also established the principle of *ein me'ilah achar me'ilah*: once an act of *me'ilah* has been committed, the sanctity (*kedushah*) of the object is discharged, and no subsequent act can constitute a second *me'ilah* — with the exception of items possessing *kedushat haguf*, intrinsic sanctity, such as korbanot. With those foundations in place, the Mishnah on 19b turns to a form of *me'ilah* we have not yet fully examined: that of appropriation.

## Appropriation & Taking

### Beam Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶבֶן אוֹ קוֹרָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא מָעַל. נְתָנָהּ לַחֲבֵרוֹ – הוּא מָעַל, וַחֲבֵרוֹ לֹא מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** If **one took** for his use **a consecrated stone or a beam, that** person **is not liable for** its **misuse.** If **he gave** the stone or the beam **to another, he is liable for** its **misuse and the other** person **is not liable for** its **misuse.**

The Mishnah on the bottom of 19b introduces the *me'ilah* of appropriation — the act of stealing or taking possession of consecrated property. The Mishnah states: *natal even o karah shel hekdesh, harei zeh lo ma'al* — if one takes a stone or a beam belonging to *hekdesh*, one has not yet committed *me'ilah* — *ad she'nisnah le'chaveiro, hu ma'al ve'chaveiro lo ma'al* — until one gives it to another person. It is the giver who has committed *me'ilah*, not the recipient.

This immediately raises the question: why is the very act of taking not itself *me'ilah*? After all, if one sneaks into *hekdesh*'s warehouse and removes an object, that would seem to be a paradigmatic act of theft. The Gemara will address this as a special case, but even before that discussion, one can appreciate the conceptual difficulty. If one sanctifies a beam — that is, dedicates it to *hekdesh* — and then takes it back, the act of "taking" is not entirely self-evident. Merely having intent to retain the object may not suffice. The Mishnah resolves this by anchoring the act of appropriation in the moment of giving: once the item is handed to another person, one has clearly removed it from *hekdesh*'s domain, and *me'ilah* is established.

The reason the recipient (*chaveiro*) does not commit *me'ilah* is precisely because *ein me'ilah achar me'ilah*: the original actor has already discharged the *kedushah* through the act of giving, and therefore the object is no longer in a state that can generate a second *me'ilah*.

A further conceptual question presents itself: is this form of *me'ilah* — appropriation — categorically distinct from the *hana'ah*-based *me'ilah* we have been studying, or are the two ultimately unified? The Mishnah itself seems to invite a unifying reading: when one gives the beam to a friend, one could characterize that act as deriving *hana'ah* through the disposition of the object — using it to benefit another person, just as one who takes *hekdesh* oil and applies it to a friend's skin commits *me'ilah* through the *hana'ah* of the recipient. Yet the simpler reading is that this is a genuinely distinct category, rooted in the act of taking rather than the act of benefiting. The two modes of *me'ilah* are not entirely separate — we have seen that half a *perutah* of appropriation and half a *perutah* of *hana'ah* combine — but they remain conceptually distinguishable.

### Building in House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנָאָהּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיָּדוּר תַּחְתֶּיהָ בְּשָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he built** the stone or the beam **into his house, he is not liable for** its **misuse until he resides beneath it** and derives benefit equal to **the value of one *peruta*** from it.

The Mishnah continues: *bana'ah betoch beito, harei zeh lo ma'al ad she'yehaneh tachteha be'shveh perutah* — if one built the beam into one's own house, one has not committed *me'ilah* until one derives a *perutah*'s worth of benefit from dwelling beneath it. This seems counterintuitive. Surely building a consecrated beam into one's house is itself an act of possession? The Gemara will ask precisely this question, along with a second objection: even before one dwells beneath the beam, the mere improvement of the house would seem to constitute *hana'ah*. Both of these challenges will be taken up shortly.

### Bathhouse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל פְּרוּטָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא מָעַל. נְתָנָהּ לַחֲבֵרוֹ – הוּא מָעַל, וַחֲבֵרוֹ לֹא מָעַל. נְתָנָהּ לְבַלָּן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רָחַץ מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הַמֶּרְחָץ פָּתוּחַ לְפָנֶיךָ, הִכָּנֵס וּרְחוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one took** for his use **a consecrated *peruta*, that** person **is not liable for** its **misuse.** If **he gave** the *peruta* **to another, he is liable for** its **misuse and the other** person **is not liable for** its **misuse.** If **he gave** the *peruta* **to a bathhouse attendant [*levallan*], although he did not bathe, he is liable for misuse** of the *peruta*. The reason is **that** at the moment he receives the *peruta*, the attendant in effect **says to** the owner of the *peruta*: **The bathhouse is open before you, enter and bathe.** The benefit derived from that availability is worth one *peruta*.

The Mishnah then presents the bathhouse case: *nasno labalan, af al pi shelo rachatz, ma'al* — if one gave *hekdesh* money to the bathhouse attendant (*balan*), even if one did not yet bathe, one has committed *me'ilah*. Why might one have thought otherwise? Because one might reason that until the transaction is complete — that is, until the service is actually rendered — the money has not truly been transferred. In a commercial exchange, handing over money before receiving goods does not finalize the transaction. So perhaps one has not yet fully divested *hekdesh*'s property.

The Mishnah rejects this reasoning. The bathhouse attendant says: *harei ha'merchatz patuach lefanecha, hacheins u'rechotz* — the bathhouse is open before you, enter and bathe. By accepting payment and extending this invitation, the attendant has completed the transaction. The right to enter and bathe is now in the hands of the one who paid, regardless of whether bathing has actually occurred. The transaction is legally binding from the moment of payment, and therefore *me'ilah* is established at that point.

### Combined Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲכִילָתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָיָיתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ, אֲכִילָתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָיָיתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **One's consumption** of half of a *peruta* of consecrated food **and another's consumption** of half of a *peruta* of consecrated food that the first person fed him; and likewise **one's benefit** of half of a *peruta* derived from a consecrated item **and another's benefit** of half of a *peruta* derived from a consecrated item that the first person provided him; and similarly **one's consumption and another's benefit** derived or **one's benefit** derived **and another's consumption,** all these **join together** to constitute the requisite measure of one *peruta* for liability for misuse, **and** that is the *halakha* **even if much time** has passed between these various acts of consumption and deriving of benefit.

The final passage of the Mishnah in this section — which the Gemara will not extensively analyze — establishes a general principle of combination in *me'ilah*: *achilato ve'achilat chaveiro, hana'ato ve'hana'at chaveiro, achilato ve'hana'at chaveiro, hana'ato ve'achilat chaveiro, mitztarpin zeh im zeh, va'afilu le'zman merubeh* — one's own eating and another's eating, one's own benefit and another's benefit, one's eating and another's benefit, one's benefit and another's eating — all of these combine with one another, even over an extended period of time.

Unlike the *me'ilah* of eating (*achilah*), which is bound by the constraints of a *kezayit* within the time of *toch kedei achilat pras*, the *me'ilah* of *hana'ah* is measured in monetary value — a *perutah* — and can accumulate over time and across multiple recipients. Significantly, this principle applies even when the *hana'ah* accrues to a friend: if one takes *hekdesh* oil and applies it to another person's skin, it is one's own *me'ilah*, even though it is the friend who receives the physical benefit. This brings us back to the conceptual question: is the appropriation-based *me'ilah* of the earlier cases simply a species of *hana'ah*, where handing over the beam to a friend constitutes deriving *hana'ah* through the recipient? That reading would collapse the distinction between the two modes. The alternative — and perhaps more accurate — reading maintains that appropriation is its own form of *me'ilah*, even if the two share certain structural features.

## Treasurer Exception

### Shmuel's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא הוּא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא חֲבֵירוֹ?! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּגִזְבָּר, הַמְּסוּרוֹת לוֹ, עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one takes a stone or a beam belonging to the Temple treasury, he is not liable for misuse, but if he gives it to another he is liable for misuse. The Gemara asks: **What is different** with regard to **him** retaining possession of the item, **and what is different** with regard to him giving it to **another,** that he is rendered liable when he gives it to another? **Shmuel said: We are dealing with a treasurer [*begizbar*]** of the Temple, **to whom** the consecrated property **is given.** Since all the consecrated property is meant to be in his possession and charge, he is not guilty of misuse, provided that he does not transfer it to another.

The Gemara opens by asking the obvious question: *mai shna hu mai shna chaveiro* — what is the difference between the taker and the recipient? Why must one wait until giving the beam to another person in order to have committed *me'ilah*? Shmuel answers: *be'gizbar ha'mesuros lo askinan* — we are speaking of the temple treasurer (*gizbar*), a functionary who has legitimate authority to handle all of *hekdesh*'s materials.

For the temple treasurer, the very act of picking up an item — even with some personal intent — does not constitute theft, because he is authorized to handle these objects in the ordinary course of his duties. One could imagine that stealing would begin the moment he removes an item from the temple precinct and brings it home; even a warehouse manager who pockets an item from the stockroom has stolen. Yet the Gemara frames it more broadly: since the treasurer maintains ongoing authority over these materials, his possession of them does not constitute a meaningful act of taking. The act of taking only crystallizes when he transfers the item to another party, at which point it is unambiguously removed from *hekdesh*'s sphere.

This also illuminates the case of one who sanctifies an object already in one's own home and then "takes" it back — a scenario in some ways even more conceptually challenging than the treasurer case, since the item was never in the physical possession of *hekdesh* to begin with. The Gemara's framework consistently locates the act of stealing in the transfer to another, rather than in the initial assertion of personal possession.

### Building Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנָאָהּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב כּוּ׳. לְמָה לִי עַד שֶׁיָּדוּר תַּחְתֶּיהָ? כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁנְּיַהּ – מָעַל! אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִנִּיחָהּ עַל פִּי אֲרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to state that the individual is not liable **until he resides beneath it?** That is not precise, **as once he** has **changed it,** by chiseling it for the purpose of fitting it into the structure, he has already **committed misuse. Rav says:** In this instance, the individual in question did not change the item in order to build with it. Rather, this is referring to a case **where he placed it upon a window.** Since mere placement does not constitute a change, there is no misuse until he lives beneath it.

This leads to the hard middle case: why is building the beam into one's house not itself an act of misappropriation? The Gemara asks, *lama li ad d'dar tachtei* — why must one wait until actually dwelling beneath it? The Gemara offers what appears to be a textual variant: *keivan de'shani, ma'al* — once one has altered the item, one has committed *me'ilah*. Some versions read *de'vana'ah* — once one has built it in. The underlying logic, on either reading, is that a genuine physical act of taking possession — hammering the beam in, plastering it — should constitute *me'ilah* without requiring the additional step of deriving benefit from dwelling beneath it.

## Mechubar Lekarka

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּבָנֵי לַהּ, מִיהַת מָעַל. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְבַיִת – אֲסָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** nevertheless, **once he built it** and subsequently derived benefit from it, **in any event he** has **committed misuse.** Although the *halakha* is that misuse does not apply to items that are attached to the ground, this does not apply to an item built into a structure. **Let us say** that this statement **supports** the opinion **of Rav, as Rav said:** With regard to **one who prostrates** himself **to a house** in an act of idolatry, **he renders** the house **prohibited.** The house assumes the status of a worshipped item, from which one may not derive benefit. Notwithstanding the *halakha* that an item attached to the ground does not become prohibited as an object of idolatry, the house does not assume the status of an item attached to the ground, since it is built of materials that were earlier detached from the ground.

Rav responds: *kegon she'henichah al pi arubah* — the Mishnah is speaking of a case where one merely placed the beam atop a skylight opening, without actually building it into the structure. One has not altered or embedded the beam; it simply sits there. In that case, placing the beam does not yet constitute an act of taking. One is using the beam — deriving some incidental benefit — but one has not yet taken possession of it in the full sense. The act of appropriation requires something more final, and therefore *me'ilah* is not established until one derives measurable *hana'ah*.

This reading is admittedly difficult with respect to the simple meaning (*peshat*) of the word *bana* in the Mishnah, which implies actual building. The Gemara is pulling back from the Mishnah's apparent implication, arguing that even the act of building does not constitute *me'ilah* in the full appropriative sense unless it involves genuine physical integration — and that the Mishnah's case of "building" is actually the intermediate scenario of mere placement.

### Roof Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: הֲנָאָה הַנִּרְאֵת לָעֵינַיִם אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** The mishna does not necessarily support the opinion of Rav. It can be claimed that the item is subject to misuse even if one maintains that a detached item which becomes attached gains the status of an attached item, in accordance with the opinion that if someone worships a house, he does not render it prohibited. Nevertheless, in the case of the stone or beam the individual is liable for misuse, due to another principle: With regard to misuse, the **Torah prohibited** all **benefit that appears** readily **to the eyes,** e.g., one who uses consecrated property as building materials. It makes no difference that the item is now attached to the ground.

The upshot of Rav's position is a conceptual distinction between using and taking. If the treasurer places a *hekdesh* beam on his roof without affixing it, he is using something that does not belong to him — and that use generates *hana'ah* — but he has not yet taken it. Taking requires either giving it to another, or actually integrating it into his property through genuine construction. This is the framework within which the Mishnah's middle case is resolved.

### Visible Benefit

The Gemara now pivots to what it presents as a side question, though it touches on a fundamental issue in the law of *me'ilah*: the distinction between *metaltelin* (chattel) and *mechubar lekarka* (that which is attached to the ground). There is no *me'ilah* with respect to *mechubar lekarka* — items fixed to the earth. This creates an obvious tension: once a beam is built into a house, it becomes attached to the structure, which is in turn attached to the ground. So why would *me'ilah* apply at all once the beam has been integrated?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַדָּר בְּבַיִת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה מִמֶּנָּה – מָעַל. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הָתָם בְּשֶׁהִקְדִּישׁוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף בְּנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof for Rav's opinion that an item which was previously detached and then becomes attached to the ground retains the status of a detached item. **Let us say** that a *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rav: With regard to **one who resides in a house consecrated** to the Temple treasury, **once he derives benefit from** the house he has **committed misuse.** Evidently, the built house is considered detached, which is why it is subject to misuse. **Reish Lakish said:** This *baraita* does not support the opinion of Rav either. **There,** the *baraita* is speaking of a case **where he consecrated** the detached building materials **and ultimately built** the house. Since the items were detached when he consecrated them, the prohibition of misuse applies even if they were subsequently attached to the ground.

The Gemara raises the case of one who worships a house (*hamishtachaveh le'vayit asaro*) and notes that Rav holds such a house is considered *metaltelin*, not *karka*. The reasoning draws on a distinction that *mechubar lekarka* may render something immune to certain halachic consequences — just as worshipping a mountain does not make the mountain forbidden, since the Torah says *eloheihem al he'harim ve'lo he'harim eloheihem* — their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains themselves do not become their gods. Things that are genuine *karka* cannot be rendered forbidden by idolatrous worship. But a house, according to Rav, occupies a different status: it is either not truly *mechubar lekarka*, or it is *mechubar lekarka* but not *karka* in the fullest sense, and therefore it retains the status of *metaltelin* and can be affected by such prohibitions.

The relevance to *me'ilah* is direct: if *me'ilah* applies to a consecrated beam built into a house, that implicitly assumes the beam retains *metaltelin* status even after construction. This would support Rav's position.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika offers a different answer: *hana'ah hanir'et la'einayim asra Torah* — the Torah prohibits *hana'ah* that is visible and evident to the eye. This is a difficult and somewhat puzzling response. The laws of *mechubar lekarka* are generally formalistic and categorical; it is hard to see how the visibility or obviousness of the benefit should override a categorical determination of attachment. The *mefaresh* essentially accepts the formulation at face value. *Tosafot* integrates it with additional considerations. But on its own, the claim that visible *hana'ah* cuts through the *mechubar lekarka* exemption seems to lack a clear principled basis. After all, dwelling in a cave belonging to *hekdesh* is equally visible a form of benefit, yet a cave is clearly *mechubar lekarka* and produces no *me'ilah*. The Rambam, notably, ignores this answer entirely.

### Sanctified Chattel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּנָאוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישׁוֹ, מַאי – לֹא מָעַל? מַאי אִירְיָא רָהֵיט וְתָנֵי הַדָּר בְּבַיִת שֶׁל מְעָרָה – לֹא מָעַל. לֵימָא הַדָּר בְּבַיִת שֶׁל אֲבָנִים שֶׁבְּנָאוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישׁוֹ – לֹא מָעַל!
>
> **Translation:** In light of Reish Lakish's interpretation, the Gemara objects: **But** if **he built** the house **and ultimately sanctified it** when it was attached to the ground, **what** is the *halakha*? Evidently, **he has not committed misuse.** If so, **why** does the *tanna* of the *baraita* **run specifically to teach** the subsequent case: With regard to **one who resides in** a consecrated **house of a cave,** i.e., a domicile that has always been attached and was not built from detached components, **he** has **not committed misuse,** as an attached item is not subject to misuse. Why does the *baraita* mention this very different case? **Let** the *baraita* **say** simply: With regard to **one who resides in a stone house, which he built and ultimately sanctified, he** has **not committed misuse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ., הָא לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: One can **say** that although the *tanna* could have taught the case of the built house instead of a cave, he preferred to mention a cave, as **this** case of the cave is **conclusive for him.** Under all circumstances, one who lives in a consecrated cave is exempt from misuse, because the cave was always attached to the ground. By contrast, **that** case of the built house is **not conclusive for him,** as a consecrated house is not always exempt from misuse. If the house is built from previously consecrated materials, one who lives within it is liable for misuse.

The Gemara then cites a *beraita*: *hadar be'vayit shel hekdesh, keivan she'neheneh mimena, ma'al* — one who dwells in a house belonging to *hekdesh*, once one has derived a *perutah*'s worth of benefit, has committed *me'ilah*. This appears to support the position that a house retains *metaltelin* status and is therefore subject to *me'ilah*.

Reish Lakish, however, offers a key qualification: *hatam be'she'hikdisho u'le'vasof bena'o* — that case speaks specifically of a situation where the materials were sanctified first and then the house was built from them. When the stones and beams were consecrated, they were consecrated while still *metaltelin*. That original status — sanctified as chattel, fit for *me'ilah* as chattel — persists even after they are built into a structure.

The Gemara then asks: if that is Reish Lakish's reason, why does the *beraita* use the case of dwelling in a house to demonstrate that there is *no* *me'ilah* when things are *mechubar lekarka*, rather than using the case of a stone house that was built and only subsequently sanctified? The Gemara answers: the *beraita* prefers a categorical case. A cave is categorically and unambiguously *mechubar lekarka* — there is never *me'ilah* for dwelling in a cave. A stone house presents a more complex picture: sometimes there is *me'ilah*, sometimes there is not, depending on the sequence of sanctification and construction. The *beraita* prefers the clean case.

The resolution, then, is twofold. Either one follows Rav's position that anything beginning as *metaltelin* retains that status even when built into the ground, in which case there is no difficulty at all. Or, setting aside Rav's general position, the more specific answer is that when an item is sanctified while still in *metaltelin* form, it retains its candidacy for *me'ilah* even after it is integrated into a permanent structure. The *nir'eh la'einayim* answer, by contrast, appears strained and is not accepted by the Rambam.

## Shlichut & Agency

### Shlichut Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשָּׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. לֹא עָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an agent who performed his agency** properly, if he was tasked to make use of a particular item, and the one who appointed him forgot that it was a consecrated item, **the homeowner,** who appointed him, **is liable for misuse** of the consecrated item, as the agent acted on his behalf. Contrary to other cases of agency, where the guiding principle is that there is no agency in the performance of a transgression, and the agent is liable, in this case there is agency, and the homeowner is liable for the action of the agent. But if he **did not perform his agency** properly, **the agent is liable for misuse** of the consecrated item, as once the agent deviates from his agency, he ceases to be an agent, and his actions are attributable to him.

With the basic acts of *me'ilah* established, the Mishnah introduces the final *perek* of the tractate: the law of *shlichut* in *me'ilah*. Normally, *ein shaliach le'dvar aveirah* — an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression; if one instructs another to commit a sin, it is the agent's sin, not the sender's. *Me'ilah*, however, is an exception: the act of an agent can be attributed to the sender. This exception has a structural parallel to the *hana'ah*-based *me'ilah* we have already seen — just as one who takes *hekdesh* oil and applies it to another person's skin commits *me'ilah* through the recipient's benefit, so too when one sends an agent and the agent acts, the sender remains the causal factor. The agent's act can suffice to generate the sender's liability.

It should be noted that the standard case of *me'ilah* throughout this discussion is *be'shogeg* — an act of inadvertence, where the person genuinely believed the object was permitted. That is the case in which one brings a *korban* and adds a fifth. The cases of *shlichut* we are about to examine likewise envision inadvertent misappropriation.

### Liver vs Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״תֵּן בָּשָׂר לָאוֹרְחִים״, וְנָתַן לָהֶם כָּבֵד; ״כָּבֵד״, וְנָתַן לָהֶם בָּשָׂר – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל. אָמַר לוֹ: ״תֵּן לָהֶם חֲתִיכָה חֲתִיכָה״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: טְלוּ שְׁתַּיִם! וְהֵם נָטְלוּ שָׁלֹשׁ – כּוּלָּם מָעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Give meat to the guests, and he gave them liver;** or if he said: Give them **liver, and he gave them meat, the agent is liable for misuse** of the consecrated item, as he deviated from his agency. If the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Give them** meat, **a piece** for this guest and **a piece** for that guest, **and** the agent **says:** Each of you **take two** pieces, **and** each of the guests **took three** pieces, **all of them are liable for misuse.** The homeowner is liable for their consumption of the first piece of meat, as with regard to that piece his instructions were fulfilled. The agent is liable for the second piece, which he added to the instructions of the homeowner. Finally, the guests are liable for the third piece, which they took at their own initiative beyond the instructions of the agent.

The Mishnah states: *ha'shaliach she'asah shlichuto, ba'al ha'bayit ma'al* — if the agent fulfilled his agency, it is the sender who has committed *me'ilah*. *Lo asah shlichuto, ha'shaliach ma'al* — if he violated his agency, it is the agent who has committed *me'ilah*.

The Mishnah provides an illustration: *amar lo ten basar le'orchin* — the homeowner instructs his servant to give meat to the guests, and that meat happens to be *korban* meat — perhaps *basar kodshei kodashim lifnei zerikat damim*, or *olah* meat — which the homeowner did not realize. The agent, however, gives them liver (*kaved*) instead, or conversely, the owner said liver and the agent gave meat. In either case, *ha'shaliach ma'al* — the agent has committed *me'ilah*. Liver is not meat; when one says "give them meat," one intends the standard cut of flesh, not internal organs. The agent has therefore acted on his own initiative, beyond the scope of his instructions, and the *me'ilah* is his.

### Portions Case

The Mishnah then presents a more complex case: the homeowner instructs his servant to give each guest one piece of meat (*ten lahem chatichah chatichah*). The servant tells the guests to each take two (*telu shtayim shtayim*), and the guests themselves take three (*ve'hem natlu shalosh shalosh*). The Mishnah rules that *kulam ma'alu* — all three parties have committed *me'ilah*: the homeowner for the first piece, which was distributed according to his instructions; the agent for the second piece, which he offered on his own authority; and the guests for the third piece, which they took on their own initiative.

As with the beam case, the *me'ilah* here appears to be established at the moment of taking, before anyone has necessarily consumed anything. This reinforces the understanding that appropriation — the act of taking possession and removing from *hekdesh*'s domain — is itself a valid form of *me'ilah*.

## Shlichut vs Category

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תַּנָּא דְּכׇל מִילְּתָא דְּמִימְּלַךְ עֲלַהּ שָׁלִיחַ תַּרְתֵּי מִילֵּי הָוְיָין? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דִּתְנַן: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק – מוּתָּר בְּדִילּוּעִין, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that an agent is considered to have diverged from the instructions of the homeowner if he gives each of the guests liver instead of meat or vice versa. This indicates that meat and liver are considered two different types of items, as giving one in place of the other would normally be done only after consultation with the homeowner. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this *halakha*, **that any** case involving **an item** about **which** the **agent** would normally **consult** whether to give it or to give another item in its stead **is** considered to involve **two** separate **items** with regard to the prohibition of misuse? **Rav Ḥisda said that** this **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. As we learned** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 54a): In the case of **one who takes a vow that vegetables** are prohibited to him, without specifying which types of vegetable, he is **permitted to** eat **gourds,** as people do not typically include gourds in the category of vegetables; **but Rabbi Akiva deems it prohibited** for him to eat gourds. Rabbi Akiva maintains that as an agent would consult the homeowner before buying gourds instead of vegetables, they are in the same category. He reasons that if they were not both in the same category, the agent would not even bother consulting the homeowner if he would prefer gourds instead. Therefore, Rabbi Akiva maintains that any item about which the agent would ask is included in the same category as the item he specified, and they are not two separate items.

The Gemara opens by identifying the underlying principle of the liver-versus-meat case and asking whose opinion it reflects. The question is: *man tanna de'chol milsa de'mimlach alei shaliach tartei milei havyan* — who is the *tanna* who holds that anything about which an agent would need to consult with his sender (*mimlach aleh shaliach*) is considered a categorically different item?

The assumption is that liver and meat share this character: if sent to procure meat and only liver is available, the agent would return and ask — do you want liver instead? This is not the same as asking whether to bring a completely unrelated item; it is a gray-zone substitute, close enough to require inquiry, but not so close as to be automatically included. The Gemara posits that our Mishnah — which treats the agent's substitution of liver for meat as a violation of his *shlichut* — reflects the view of the *chachamim* who dispute Rabbi Akiva, and not Rabbi Akiva himself.

Rav Chisda articulates this: *amr Rav Chisda de'la ke'Rabbi Akiva* — our Mishnah is not in accordance with Rabbi Akiva. The relevant dispute concerns one who vows to abstain from vegetables (*hanoder min ha'yerek*): the *chachamim* rule that gourds (*diluim*) are not included in such a vow, while Rabbi Akiva rules that they are forbidden. The *chachamim*'s reasoning, as spelled out in that Mishnah, is precisely the *mimlach* principle: if you send your agent to the market to buy vegetables and there are no vegetables, he will return and ask, "there were no vegetables — shall I bring gourds?" That question indicates that gourds are not simply "vegetables," even if they share a family resemblance. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: the fact that the agent asks about gourds rather than about, say, meat, shows that gourds are close enough to vegetables to fall within the category. Similarly, liver and meat occupy this gray zone.

### Abaye's Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, מִי לָא בָּעֵי לְאִימְּלוֹכֵי? כִּי אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר נַחְמָנִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** as **doesn't Rabbi Akiva concede that** the agent **needs to consult** the one who appointed him? Since the agent failed to consult him, and he acted on his own when he gave liver instead of meat, he is not considered to have performed his agency. Consequently, he is liable for misuse. In other words, the ruling of the mishna is not due to the fact that meat and liver are considered two different types of items, but because the agent failed to perform his agency. **When the Rabbis said** this *halakha* **before Rava, he said to them: Naḥmani,** i.e., Abaye, **is saying well.**

Abaye offers a striking refinement: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva* — one could say our Mishnah reflects even Rabbi Akiva's view. The key is to distinguish between two different questions that the *mimlach* principle answers. In the context of *nedarim*, the question is categorical: does the gray-zone item fall within the defined category of the vow? Rabbi Akiva says yes — gourds are vegetables, liver is meat. The *chachamim* say no. That debate is about classification.

In the context of *shlichut*, the question is different: it is not about category, but about the proper conduct of an agent. Even Rabbi Akiva would agree that when an item falls into the gray zone, the agent is *expected to ask* before substituting. The agent's failure to ask — his decision to go ahead and give liver when instructed to give meat — is what constitutes a violation of his agency. The *mimlach* principle in *shlichut* is not about whether liver counts as meat; it is about whether the agent acted within the reasonable scope of his commission. He should have checked in. Since he did not, he acted on his own, and the *me'ilah* is his.

### Rava Approves

When the Rabbis (here meaning Rava's students) reported Abaye's reasoning to Rava, Rava responded: *shapir ka'amar Nachmani* — Nachmani spoke well. (*Nachmani* being Abaye's name, or at least one of the names by which he was known — whether it was his proper name or a later appellation is a matter of discussion.) Rava's endorsement of Abaye's distinction is notable; Rava does not frequently align himself with Abaye's positions.

The upshot is a clean analytical distinction: the question of whether an agent violates his *shlichut* is independent of the question of how to classify items for purposes of a vow. Even if Rabbi Akiva categorizes liver as falling within "meat," he would still agree that an agent who substitutes liver for meat without asking has stepped outside his commission. The *shlichut* bounds are violated not by category error, but by failure to consult.

## Meat Classification

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְתַנָּא קַמָּא, מַאי שְׁנָא בִּבְשַׂר עוֹפוֹת? מִשּׁוּם דִּרְגִיל אִינִישׁ דְּאָמַר: לָא אַשְׁכַּחִי בִּשְׂרָא דְּחֵיוְתָא, וַאֲתַאי בִּשְׂרָא דְצִיפְּרָא. אִי הָכִי, הָכִי נָמֵי עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ לְמֵימְרָא: לָא אַשְׁכַּחִי בִּשְׂרָא דְּחֵיוְתָא, וַאֲתַאי דָּגִים!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the above *baraita*, the Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **the first *tanna*, what is different about meat of birds** that he considers it in the same category as regular meat? It must be **because a person normally says,** when he cannot find meat and returns to the one who appointed him: **I did not find meat of animals but I brought meat of birds** instead. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **also** with regard to fish **a person is apt to say: I did not find meat of an animal, but I brought fish** instead. Why, then, is fish considered a separate category?

The Gemara now turns to identify who among the *tannaim* disputes Rabbi Akiva in the classification debate, associating that position with a specific named authority. The *beraita* cited is as follows: *ha'noder min ha'basar assur be'chol minei basar* — one who vows to abstain from meat is forbidden all types of meat. This includes: the head (*rosh*), feet (*raglayim*), trachea (*kaneh*), liver (*kaved*), heart (*lev*), all internal organs, and the meat of fowl (*basar of*). The one thing excluded is fish flesh and grasshoppers (*basar dagim ve'chagavim*). This first position — which implicitly is that of Rabbi Akiva — takes an expansive view: even items that require inquiry fall within the category of "meat," with only the most obviously distinct items (fish and grasshoppers) excluded.

### R. Shimon b. Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַתִּיר בָּרֹאשׁ וּבָרַגְלַיִם, בַּקָּנֶה וּבַכָּבֵד, וּבָעוֹפוֹת וּבַדָּגִים וּבַחֲגָבִים. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: קְרָבַיִם לָאו בָּשָׂר הֵן, וְאוֹכְלֵיהֶן לָאו בַּר אִינִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits** him to eat meat **of the head, of the feet, of the windpipe, of the liver, of the heart, of birds, and** needless to say also **of fish and of grasshoppers.** **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would likewise say: Innards are not** considered **meat, and one who eats them is not a person,** i.e., innards are unfit for human consumption. It can be inferred from here that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees with Rabbi Akiva and maintains that although an agent who fails to find meat would consult the one who appointed him and then replace the meat with liver, the liver is not considered meat with regard to vows.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes a far more restrictive view: *mattir be'rosh u've'raglayim u've'kaneh u've'kaved u've'of u've'dagim ve'chagavim* — the head, feet, trachea, liver, fowl, fish, and grasshoppers are all excluded from the vow. Why? Because all of these are items about which the agent would need to ask. They fall in the gray zone, and the gray zone is, for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, outside the category. He reinforces this position with a characteristically sharp remark: *kerevayim lav basar hen ve'ochleihem lav bar inash* — internal organs are not real meat, and those who eat them are not really human. They belong to dog food, not to the human table. While this strengthens his specific claim about internal organs, it sits somewhat uneasily with his broader theoretical principle, since the most extreme examples are not what drives his general rule.

### Fish Challenge

The Gemara raises a challenge to the first opinion (Rabbi Akiva): *mai shna de'vasar of mishum de'ragil inish de'amar lo ashkach bisra de'chayusa va'asai bisra de'tzipra* — why is fowl included within "meat"? The reason offered is that an agent would plausibly say, "I could not find red meat (*bisra de'chayusa*), but I brought chicken." That substitution makes sense; chicken is a recognizable substitute for meat. But if that is the logic, the Gemara continues, someone might equally say, "I could not find red meat, but I brought fish." By the same reasoning, fish should also be included in the category — yet even Rabbi Akiva excludes fish.

### Rav Papa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּיוֹם הַקָּזָה עָסְקִינַן, דְּלָא אָכֵיל אִינִישׁ דָּגִים. אִי הָכִי, צִיפְּרָא נָמֵי לָא נֵיכוֹל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּמְסוֹכַר וְאָכֵל צִיפְּרָא – פָּרַח לִיבֵּיהּ כְּצִיפְּרָא! וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: אֵין מַקִּיזִין דָּם לֹא עַל הַדָּגִים, וְלֹא עַל הָעוֹפוֹת, וְלֹא עַל בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּיוֹמָא דְּכָיְיבִין לֵיהּ עֵינֵיהּ עָסְקִינַן, דְּלָא אָכֵיל דָּגִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: We are dealing with the day** of one's **bloodletting, as a person** in that condition **does not eat fish.** Since it was accepted at the time that eating fish after bloodletting is harmful, the agent would never consider buying fish instead of meat, and would not even consult with the one who appointed him as to whether or not to purchase fish. The Gemara asks: **If so, he** would **not eat birds either, as Shmuel said:** With regard to **one who lets blood and eats the meat of a bird, his heart** rate accelerates and **flies like a bird.** Clearly, bird meat is also deleterious for one's health after bloodletting. **And furthermore it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One does not let blood before** eating **fish, nor before** eating **birds, nor before** eating **salted meat.** **Rather, Rav Pappa said: We are dealing with** a case which occurred **on a day that his eyes hurt him, as** people **do not eat fish** on that day, since fish are harmful to the eyes. Therefore, the agent would neither purchase fish nor consult with the homeowner whether to do so.

Rav Papa resolves this by relocating the entire question into specific contextual settings. The cases under discussion are not generic meals, but medically defined situations. When one has undergone bloodletting (*hakkazat dam*), the dietary expectations are sharply constrained. On a day of bloodletting, one would eat only serious red meat — not fish, because fish is simply not what one eats on such a day. And therefore, in the context of a vow made on such a day, the word "meat" clearly encompasses chicken (since one might reasonably substitute chicken for red meat even on a bloodletting day) but not fish.

The Gemara challenges even this: if one would not eat fish on a bloodletting day, one would also not eat fowl on such a day. Shmuel himself states that *damsuchar*, one who has had blood drawn from the shoulders, and then eats fowl, *parach libei ke'tzipra* — his heart will fly away like a bird — meaning it is medically inadvisable. A *beraita* likewise rules that one should not let blood in preparation for eating fish or fowl. Therefore, on a bloodletting day, both fish and fowl would be excluded from one's intended "meat."

Rav Papa then shifts to a second setting: *biyoma de'cha'ivei lei einei* — on a day that one's eyes are hurting. On such a day, people would not eat fish (fish was considered harmful for eye ailments), and therefore "meat" on that day would encompass fowl but not fish. This contextual resolution, while specific, provides a principled basis for treating fowl and fish differently within the vow's scope.

The entire digression, it should be noted, is subsidiary to the main point established by Abaye and endorsed by Rava: that regardless of these classification disputes, the *shlichut* question is separate. Even Rabbi Akiva, even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — all would agree that when an agent substitutes one item for another in a gray-zone situation without inquiring, he has violated his *shlichut*, and the *me'ilah* falls on him rather than on the sender.

## Partial Violation

### Agency Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לוֹ חֲתִיכָה כּוּ׳. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מוֹסִיף עַל שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Give him** meat, **a piece** for each guest, and the agent says: Each of you take two pieces, and each of the guests took three pieces, all of them are liable for misuse. The Gemara suggests: One can **learn from** the mishna that if an agent **adds to his agency** he **is** still considered **an agent,** and therefore the one who appointed him is also liable, as the agent did not uproot his instructions entirely.

The Gemara returns to the case of the escalating portions — one piece ordered, two offered by the agent, three taken by the guests — and presses the conceptual question more sharply. If the agent gives more than was ordered, should that not completely negate the *shlichut* altogether? Perhaps one ought to say: two is categorically different from one. The agent who hands out two pieces is not doing the homeowner's bidding at all; he is doing his own thing entirely. In that case, not only is the second piece the agent's *me'ilah*, but the first piece should also be his — the homeowner would never be *mo'el* at all.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: דְּאָמַר שָׁלִיחַ: טוֹל אַחַת מִדַּעְתּוֹ וְאַחַת מִדַּעְתִּי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: עָקַר שָׁלִיחַ שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, וְלָא לִמְעוֹל – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** that this inference is not necessarily correct, because the mishna can be explained as referring specifically to a case **where** the **agent said** to the guests: **Take one** piece of meat **in accordance with the intent** of the homeowner **and one** piece **in accordance with my intent.** Rav Sheshet adds that it is necessary for the mishna to teach this *halakha*, **lest you say** that the **agent has uprooted the agency of the homeowner** by adding to his instructions, **and** therefore the homeowner is **not** liable for the prohibition of **misuse** even for the first piece of meat. Consequently, the mishna **teaches us** that the homeowner is also liable.

Rav Sheshet answers by limiting the scope of the case: *de'amar shliach tol achas mi'da'ato ve'achas mi'da'ati* — the case is where the agent explicitly tells the guests: one piece is from the homeowner, one piece is from me. When the agent makes this delineation explicit — one steak he hands out on behalf of the *ba'al ha'bayit*, one steak he adds from his own initiative — there is no question of the two being conflated. The first piece is the homeowner's transaction, carried out faithfully. The second piece is the agent's own action. The *me'ilah* for the first falls on the homeowner, and the *me'ilah* for the second falls on the agent.

This parallels a broader halachic question, discussed in *Kesuvot*, about agency in purchases: if one sends an agent to purchase one *amah* of land and the agent purchases two, is the sale binding for the first *amah*? The clean answer is yes — where the agent clearly separates the two transactions — but the question itself indicates that under ambiguous circumstances, one might argue that the agent's overreach undermines even the portion he was authorized to perform.

### Agency Preserved

The *mashmah lan* — the novelty of the Mishnah's ruling — is that even when the agent goes beyond his instructions, the *shlichut* is not entirely negated. Provided the agent delineates clearly — "this one is on the homeowner, this one is on me" — the original commission remains intact. The homeowner's *me'ilah* for the first piece is not undermined by the agent's independent action regarding the second. And *kol she'ken* — all the more so — if the agent had not explicitly said anything but simply handed out an extra piece, the presumption would be that the first piece was still carried out on behalf of the homeowner. The Gemara casts this as the primary *chiddush* of the Mishnah: overreach does not collapse the underlying *shlichut*, as long as the authorized portion remains separable. This discussion sets the stage for the concluding material of the tractate, which will continue to develop the relationship between *shlichut* and *me'ilah* in this framework of taking and appropriation.
