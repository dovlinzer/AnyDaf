# Bava Batra 159 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inheritance Pre-Sale

### Kasha Shebidinei Mamonos

The daf opens at a peculiar juncture in the *Gemara*. We are told that a ruling was transmitted from *Eretz Yisrael* to *Bavel* accompanied by the declaration that it constituted a *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot* — one of the hardest, or at the very least a very hard, ruling in monetary law. The *Gemara* then proceeded to analyze the reasoning behind that ruling and concluded that it was, in fact, entirely straightforward. This conclusion sets the *Gemara* on a search: if that ruling is not genuinely difficult to understand, then what was the ruling that was actually sent, and why was it called *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*?

### Chanoch's Reclamation

The ruling that was initially under discussion involved a son — call him Reuven, Yaakov's son — who pre-sold his anticipated inheritance to Mordechai. Reuven then died, and Chanoch, Reuven's son, came and reclaimed the property. The apparent difficulty is that Chanoch is receiving his inheritance *through* his father Reuven — or so it seems — and if that is the case, why should he be able to void the sale? The sale passed through Reuven, and Reuven had already disposed of the property. (Tosafot has a different way of learning the sugya altogether, though we bracket that issue for the moment.)

### Grandson Inheritance

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent difficulty by suggesting that a grandson may inherit directly from his grandfather, bypassing his father. If Chanoch inherits straight from Yaakov without the inheritance passing through Reuven at all, then Reuven's pre-sale becomes irrelevant to Chanoch's claim, and there is nothing surprising about Chanoch's reclamation. But that explanation dissolves the *kasha*: if the ruling makes perfect sense, it can hardly be called *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. The *Gemara* is therefore compelled to seek a different candidate for the ruling that was sent.

## Witness Signature Timing

### Gazlan Witness

The *Gemara* now proposes a different ruling as the true *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. The case is one of a witness who signed a *shtar* — say, a document recording that Charlie borrowed a thousand dollars from David — before becoming disqualified as a witness. At the time of signing, Reuven was a perfectly upstanding *kosher ed*. Subsequently, however, Reuven entered a life of crime and became a *gazlan*, which renders him halachically disqualified from giving testimony. The *shtar* now comes before *beis din*, but the signatures have not yet been validated (*mekuyam*). For David to use the *shtar* against Charlie, he must validate the signatures.

The most natural person to validate Reuven's signature would be Reuven himself, testifying that the signature on the document is his. But Reuven is no longer a *kosher ed* — he cannot testify at all. The *Gemara* states: *hu eno meid al ktav yado* — he cannot testify to his own signature. Yet *aval acherim meidim* — other people can come and attest that the signature is Reuven's. (Some versions of the text read *yecholim lekayem ktav yado* — others are capable of validating his handwriting.)

### Validation Paradox

At first glance this seems entirely reasonable, but the *Gemara* presses: *hashta ihu lo meheman acherim mehemani*? He himself cannot testify, yet others are believed? What makes this conceptually troubling is that when other witnesses validate Reuven's signature, they are essentially corroborating his testimony — but his testimony is inadmissible. One might respond that there is an obvious distinction: his testimony is inadmissible *today*, but the other witnesses are testifying to the fact that he gave his testimony *a year ago*, when he was still a *kosher ed*. The act of signing the *shtar* functions as the presentation of testimony, and that act occurred before he became a *gazlan*. Why should that be problematic?

### Predating Suspicion

The Rashbam explains the difficulty as follows. When other witnesses validate a signature, they are not testifying that the signatory gave testimony at a specific past date — they are only attesting to the identity of the handwriting. And therein lies the problem: given that we now know Reuven is a *gazlan*, a person who will transgress for money, we have reason to suspect that he may have forged the entire document. He could have written and signed it yesterday and simply predated it to a year ago, when he was still *kosher*. Merely confirming that the signature is Reuven's does not tell us *when* he signed it. If he is capable of dishonesty, why should the date on the document be trusted? Other witnesses validating the signature does not dispel the possibility of fraudulent predating.

### Prior Beit Din Viewing

The *Gemara* responds: *u-mai kushya* — why is this genuinely difficult? Perhaps the ruling is limited to a case where the *shtar* was already seen by *beis din* before Reuven became a *gazlan*. This is Tosafot's explanation: the *shtar* was brought before *beis din* a year ago while Reuven was still a valid witness. The signatures were not validated at the time, but the document was seen. Now that Reuven has become a *gazlan*, he cannot validate his own signature. But once other witnesses come and validate it, we know the signature was already present on the *shtar* when *beis din* saw it last year — at which point Reuven was still *kosher*. The forgery concern is thus neutralized by the prior *beis din* viewing. The ruling is sound, but it is confined to that circumstance. If the *shtar* appears before *beis din* for the first time today, the concern of predating would remain unresolved, and the validation by other witnesses would not suffice.

The *Gemara* thus concludes: if the difficulty was as described, then the ruling makes sense only when *beis din* had already seen the *shtar* — and that is not the kind of ruling one would call *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. The *Gemara* is again left without a satisfactory candidate, and presses on.

## Relative Witness

### Son-in-Law Status

The *Gemara* now proposes yet another ruling as the authentic *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. The case this time involves someone who possessed knowledge of a transaction and signed a *shtar* as a witness before he had any familial relationship to the parties. Subsequently — *ve-naaseh chotno* — he became the son-in-law of the relevant party. He is now a *karov*, a relative, and is therefore disqualified from giving testimony. Once again: *hu eno meid al ktav yado* — he cannot validate his own signature. Yet *aval acherim meidim* — others may attest to his signature.

### Prior Viewing Solution

The same conceptual problem recurs. If we are concerned that someone may have fabricated a document and forged a date, then confirming the identity of the signature does not help — he might have signed it yesterday and predated it to a time before he became a relative. So the *Gemara* offers the same response: perhaps here too the *shtar* was previously seen by *beis din* before he became a relative, so the prior viewing eliminates the predating concern.

### No Prior Viewing

But the *Gemara* now introduces a crucial distinction. *Ve-ha-amar Rav Yosef bar Minyumi amar Rav Nachman af al pi she-lo huchzak ktav yado be-veit din* — there is a received tradition that in the case of the son-in-law, the ruling applies even if the *shtar* first appears before *beis din* today, without any prior viewing. This is a striking departure from the *gazlan* case. Here we are told explicitly that even without the safeguard of a prior *beis din* viewing, the validation by other witnesses is sufficient. The question is: why?

---

## Gzeirat Hamelek

### Gazlan Dishonesty

To understand the distinction, the *Gemara* articulates a fundamental principle about the different categories of *psulei eidus* — those who are disqualified from testimony. A *gazlan* is disqualified because of a concern about dishonesty. Someone who will commit transgressions for money may well lie for money, may well forge documents for money. The *psul* of the *gazlan* is therefore rooted in a concern that he might not be telling the truth. Consequently, once we suspect him of potential dishonesty, confirming the identity of his signature without knowing *when* he signed it does not dispel the concern.

### Relative Formalism

The disqualification of a relative, by contrast, operates on entirely different grounds. The *Gemara* states: *dilma gzeiras ha-melekh hu* — perhaps the reason a relative is disqualified is not because we suspect him of lying, but rather because it is a formal, categorical exclusion — a *gzeiras ha-melekh*, a divine decree. On this view, we do not suspect the relative of being dishonest; his testimony is simply inadmissible as a formal matter. This has nothing to do with character or credibility.

This distinction is crucial. The *psul* of the *gazlan* and the *nogea ba-davar* (one who has a direct financial stake) is grounded in a concern about lying: if you will act corruptly for money, perhaps you will lie for money. But a relative — we might ordinarily assume — would lie for the sake of family loyalty. That would place the relative's disqualification in the same category as the *gazlan*, and the same predating concern would apply. The *Gemara* rejects this assumption.

### Moshe Aharon Proof

The *Gemara* offers a striking proof. *D'ei lo seima hachi, Moshe ve-Aharon le-chosanam* — if you insist that the disqualification of relatives is based on a concern about lying, consider: could Moshe and Aharon not testify for their sons-in-law? Would anyone suspect *them* of lying? Of course not. *Ela gzeiras Melech* — rather, it is simply a formal exclusion, a decree that relatives are categorically excluded from testimony — not because we suspect them of lying, but because that is the rule.

It is worth noting that some *rishonim*, when citing this principle, slightly reframe the *Gemara*'s example: they ask why Moshe and Aharon could not come together and testify as two witnesses on behalf of any party, given that they are brothers. There, the disqualification is not because they are relatives of the interested party, but because they are related to each other. That is an equally compelling illustration: of course Moshe and Aharon would never lie. *B'chayecha!* And yet they cannot testify together. The formal exclusion applies without regard to the actual likelihood of deception. It is a *gzeiras ha-kasuv*.

### Date Trustworthiness

This distinction resolves the puzzle of the son-in-law case perfectly. Since the disqualification of a relative is not rooted in any concern about dishonesty — it is purely formal — we have no reason to suspect that the witness forged the document and predated it. He is, on the contrary, a trustworthy individual whom we simply cannot allow to testify for technical, formal reasons. Once other witnesses confirm that the signature is genuinely his, we proceed on the assumption that a trustworthy person would have signed the document honestly and dated it accurately. The date is presumed truthful, and therefore we can infer that he signed it before he became a relative — at which point his testimony was fully valid.

In the *gazlan* case, by contrast, we cannot make this inference. Even after confirming the identity of the signature, we remain suspicious that he might have signed and predated the document fraudulently. The prior *beis din* viewing is the only mechanism by which that suspicion can be removed. In the son-in-law case, no such mechanism is needed, because the suspicion never arises in the first place. And *that* is why the ruling — that other witnesses may validate the signature of a man who has since become a relative, even without a prior *beis din* viewing — is genuinely *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. The result seems counterintuitive: he himself cannot testify, yet others can validate what is essentially his testimony. But once we understand that his disqualification is purely formal and carries no taint of dishonesty, the ruling becomes coherent — and its coherence is itself the source of the conceptual difficulty.

## Original Case Return

### Chanoch Presale

Having worked through the cases of the *gazlan* and the relative witness, the *Gemara* returns to its original discussion. *Ela amar Rava le-olam ke-da-amina me-ikara* — Rava says: in truth, the ruling that was sent was indeed the original ruling about Chanoch, who came to reclaim the property that his father Reuven had pre-sold to Mordechai.

### Through Father Paradox

And now the *Gemara* identifies precisely *why* this ruling is *kasha she-be-dinei mamonot*. Chanoch inherits in place of his father — he is receiving the inheritance *through* his father Reuven. That is what entitles him to the property at all. And yet, even though the inheritance passes through his father, the pre-sale that Reuven made to Mordechai is not valid against Chanoch. How can this be? The very thing that entitles him to the property — inheriting through his father — is what should make the sale binding on him. That paradox is the *kasha*.

## Grandson & Deaths

### Tachat Avotecha

The *Gemara* now turns to the verse *tachat avotecha yihiyu vanecha* — "in place of your fathers shall be your sons" — which was earlier cited in the discussion as a possible proof that a grandson inherits directly from his grandfather. Could this verse serve as a legal principle establishing that grandchildren bypass their fathers in the line of inheritance? The *Gemara* answers: *hahu be-vracha ketiv* — that verse is written in the context of a blessing, not as a halachic formulation. It speaks to the idea that grandchildren carry forward their grandparents' legacy and reputation, but it establishes no legal rule about the mechanics of inheritance. Inheritance passes through the son, and a grandson who receives an estate is conceptually receiving it through his deceased father, not directly from his grandfather.

### House Collapse Case

But this conclusion raises a difficulty. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *nafal ha-bayis alav ve-al aviv* — a house collapsed on a father and son. They both died, but the order of deaths is unknown. The son had debts: there was a woman holding a *kesuvas isha* and a *baal chov* to whom he owed money. The question of which death occurred first determines who receives the estate. The *ba'al chov* argues that the father died first and then the son, meaning the son briefly held the property — long enough for the creditor's lien to attach — before dying. Chanoch (the grandson) argues the opposite: that the son died first, then the grandfather, so the inheritance bypassed the son entirely and came to him directly from his grandfather. If this argument is valid, it seems to presuppose that a grandson *can* inherit directly from a grandfather, bypassing a deceased father — which would undermine the *Gemara*'s conclusion that the verse is only a blessing, not a legal principle.

### Baraita Dispute

The *Gemara* carefully reads the *baraita*. The phrase *yorshei ha-av* and *yorshei ha-ben* — the heirs of the father and the heirs of the son — at first seems to refer to a straightforward dispute between the son's descendants and the father's estate. But the *Gemara* points out that if the dispute were simply between the son's heirs and the father's heirs in the most natural reading, the order of death would not matter: everyone would receive their share regardless of who died first. There would be no genuine dispute, and no reason to divide.

### Grandfather Grandson

The *Gemara* therefore re-reads the *baraita*. The case is not between a father and son simpliciter, but between a grandfather and a grandson — where the father (*Reuven*) is not in the picture, having died previously, and the question is which of the other two — the grandfather (*Yaakov*) and the grandson (*Chanoch*, here Yaakov's grandson through Reuven) — died first. Alternatively, the *Gemara* considers a reading involving the father and an uncle (the father's brother), who is outside the house and unaffected by the collapse.

Working through the scenarios: if *Reuven* dies first, then *Yaakov* dies, the entire estate goes to *Yaakov*'s remaining son, *Shimon* — directly, with no passage through *Reuven*. If *Yaakov* dies first, half his estate passes to *Reuven* and half to *Shimon*; then when *Reuven* dies, his half passes on — but having passed through *Reuven*, it is subject to his creditors' claims. In that scenario, *Shimon* ultimately receives the property, but a portion of it traveled through *Reuven*, making it available to *Reuven*'s *baal chov*.

The *Gemara* ultimately concludes from this *baraita* that there is no proof that a grandson inherits directly from his grandfather bypassing the son. In fact, a grandson inherits through his father as a matter of principle — and the proof is that Chanoch also receives *Reuven*'s *chelek bechorah* (firstborn's portion), which demonstrates that he is standing *in his father's stead*, not bypassing him. The question of why, in that case, Reuven's pre-sale does not bind Chanoch remains the *kasha* — the *Gemara* never fully resolves the mechanics of how such a pre-sale could operate in the first place, and it seems that the very concept of pre-selling one's inheritance is inherently fragile, perhaps amounting to no more than a personal undertaking that cannot bind one's heirs.

## Son Mother Grave

### Rav Sheshet Query

Because the *sugya* has now established that a grandson inherits through his dead father — which means, in effect, that the son stands as the vehicle through which inheritance passes even after his death — the *Gemara* transitions to a related question about *inheriting through the grave*. The question is posed to Rav Sheshet: *ben mahu she-yirash et imo bakever le-hanchil le-achiv min ha-av* — can a son who has already died inherit from his mother in order to pass that inheritance on to his paternal half-brothers?

The scenario is as follows. Yosef, the son of Rachel, dies. Then Rachel dies. Had Yosef been alive when Rachel died, he would have inherited from her. The question is: does Yosef's death before Rachel's death sever any connection between Rachel's estate and Yosef's paternal half-brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and so forth — who are sons of Yaakov by Leah and are completely unrelated to Rachel? Or does Yosef, as it were, inherit from Rachel *bakever* — while already in the grave — and pass that inheritance to his half-brothers, who stand in his stead?

This is a critical question. We have established that a grandson can represent a dead father. Can half-brothers represent their dead brother to inherit from a woman to whom they have no relationship whatsoever?

### Mother Son House

The *Gemara* brings as evidence the classic case of *nafla ha-bayis alav ve-al imo* — the house that fell on a son and his mother. Both died; the order of death is unknown. The two possible sets of heirs dispute who receives the estate. The schools — *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* — agree that the estate is divided (*yachloku*) between the heirs of the mother and the heirs of the son, regardless of the order of death. This implies that the question of which death came first matters: the mother's relatives claim the son died first (leaving the estate with the mother, and thence to her relatives), while the son's heirs claim the mother died first (so Yosef inherited from her and then passed it to them).

### If Grave Permitted

But now the *Gemara* presses: *ve-im issa* — if it were true that a son can inherit from his mother *bakever*, even while he is already dead, then why does it matter who died first? Even if the son died first, let him inherit from his mother in the grave and pass the inheritance on to his paternal half-brothers. The dispute about the order of death would then be irrelevant: the half-brothers should receive the property in either scenario. But the *Gemara* treats the dispute as meaningful, which implies that when the son dies before the mother, the inheritance does *not* pass through him to his half-brothers.

### No Mother Grave

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ein ha-ben yoresh et imo bakever le-hanchil la-achin min ha-av* — a son does not inherit from his mother while in the grave in order to benefit his paternal half-brothers. Once the son is dead, his half-brothers cannot step into his shoes to claim from a woman to whom they are entirely unrelated. The inheritance does not travel through the grave in this direction.

---

## No Grave Inheritance

### Sibling Replacement

Abaye offers the conceptual reasoning. The principle is: *ne'emra sibah ba-ven ve-ne'emra sibah ba-ba'al* — the Torah articulates the mechanism by which inheritance can be diverted when it passes through a woman, and that mechanism involves either a son or a husband. The half-brothers are neither. They cannot step into the place of their dead brother to inherit from a woman who has no connection to them. A son can represent his dead father in the line of inheritance — the grandson-for-grandfather case — because the son *is* the legal continuation of the father in the chain of descent. But half-brothers do not stand in that relationship to their deceased brother.

### Husband Analogy

Abaye draws an explicit analogy: just as a husband does not inherit from his father-in-law once his wife has died — because the link that connected him to that estate has been severed — so too a son does not function as a conduit through the grave when the result would be to benefit parties who have no independent claim to the estate. *Ma sibah ha-amura ba-ba'al ein ha-ba'al yoresh ishto bakever, af sibah ha-amura ba-ven ein ha-ben yoresh imo bakever le-hanchil la-achin min ha-av.* The inheritance does not travel to people for whom the relationship has been cut — either because the wife died (severing the husband's connection) or because the son died (severing the half-brothers' connection). The estate in such a case returns to the mother's own kin, not to those whose only link to it was through a deceased intermediary who himself has no living descendants to represent him.

## Misc. Cases

### Bar Sisan Sale

The *Gemara* closes with a series of cases involving questions of possession and the burden of proof. In the first case, a man says to his fellow: *nichsei de-Bar Sisan mezavinna lakh* — I am selling you all the property of Bar Sisan that I possess. It turns out that among the properties under discussion there is one piece of land that is *called* "of the house of Bar Sisan" but was not actually acquired from Bar Sisan himself — it simply goes by that name. The question is whether that field is included in the sale.

### Rav Nachman Rule

The case came before Rav Nachman, and he ruled: *ve-ukmah be-yada de-loke'ach* — the property goes to the buyer. Since it is called *Bar Sisan*, it falls under the terms of what was sold.

### Rava Challenge

Rava challenged Rav Nachman: *dina hachi*? Is that really the ruling? The principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah* — he who seeks to remove property from another's possession must bring proof — should apply here. The buyer is the one seeking to take this field away from the seller's possession; it is the buyer who should have to prove his claim.

### House Case

But there is an apparent contradiction, because in a different case, Rav Nachman seems to take the opposite position. In that case, a man found someone occupying his house. The occupier claimed: I bought it from you and I have been *okhel* — using the property — for three years, establishing a *chazakah*. The original owner countered: I was living in the inner chamber of the house the whole time, so the *chazakah* is ineffective, since I was continuously present. The case came before Rav Nachman, who said: *zil berur achiltach* — go and prove that your use of the property was uninterrupted and without the owner's knowledge. Here Rav Nachman placed the burden on the person claiming the *chazakah*, which seems to favor the original owner.

Similarly, Rava himself seems to take contradictory positions: in the *Bar Sisan* case he argued that the burden falls on the buyer (who is removing from the seller's possession), yet in the house case he argued that once the occupier has established a prima facie *chazakah*, *he* becomes the *muchzak* (the one in possession), and the original owner then becomes the *motzi* who must bring proof.

### Muchzak Key

The *Gemara* resolves both apparent contradictions with a single elegant principle: do not ask who is the buyer and who is the seller — ask who is the *muchzak*, who currently holds the property. *Hacha mocher ka'i be-nichsei, hatam loke'ach ka'i be-nichsei* — in the *Bar Sisan* case, the seller is in possession of the disputed field; in the house case, the buyer (the one claiming *chazakah*) is in possession of the house. The *muchzak* is always favored; the burden falls on whoever is trying to dislodge him.

### Burden Context

This explains Rava's position perfectly: in both cases he invokes *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah*, but the *motzi* is a different party in each case. In the *Bar Sisan* case, the buyer is the *motzi* — he is trying to take the field from the seller's possession. In the house case, the original owner is the *motzi* — once the occupier has demonstrated three years of *chazakah*, he becomes the *muchzak*, and it is the original owner who must now prove his claim.

As for Rav Nachman's apparently inconsistent rulings: in the *Bar Sisan* case, the seller is in possession and the field is called *Bar Sisan*, so the burden falls on him to prove that the term in the contract does not cover this field. In the house case, even though Rav Nachman places a demand on the *chazakah* claimant, this is not inconsistent — Rav Nachman treats the *chazakah* as functionally equivalent to a *shtar mecher*, a deed of sale. Just as a *shtar* that is challenged must be validated, so too a *chazakah* that is challenged — by the owner's claim that he was continuously present in the inner chamber — must be defended. The challenge need not be conclusive; it need only be serious enough to require a response. In both rulings, the *Gemara* concludes, Rav Nachman is operating from a consistent underlying logic once context and possession are properly understood.

With this, we conclude the ninth *perek*. *Hadran alach mi she-hotziu.*
