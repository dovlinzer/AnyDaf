# Yevamot 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen & Ketana

### Ailonit & Zonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט לֹא יִשָּׂא אַיְלוֹנִית, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים לֹא יִשָּׂא אַיְלוֹנִית, שֶׁהִיא זוֹנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בַּתּוֹרָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין זוֹנָה אֶלָּא גִּיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת וְשֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A common priest may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman [*aylonit*],** who is incapable of bearing children, **unless he** already **has a wife and children. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if **he has a wife and children, he may not marry a sexually underdeveloped woman, as she is the *zona*** about whom it is **stated in the Torah** that a priest may not marry her. Intercourse with her is considered a licentious act because she is incapable of bearing children. **And the Rabbis say: The only** women in the category of ***zona*,** who are therefore forbidden to a priest, are **a female convert, a freed** maidservant, **and** any woman **who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse** with a man she is prohibited from marrying.

We pick up at the top of 61b, having already introduced the position of Rabbi Yehuda that an *ailonit* — a woman who is not merely infertile but whose very physical development places her categorically outside the realm of childbearing — is forbidden to a *kohen* because she falls under the category of *zonah*. This flows from a particular perspective in which sexual relations entirely outside the theoretical framework of procreation constitute an act of *zenut*. That view is certainly not the only rabbinic attitude toward marital intimacy, but it is consequential for the discussions that follow.

The *gemara* then quoted the position of Rabbi Eliezer that a *kohen* cannot marry a *ketana* — a minor girl — and it now attempts to determine why. Significantly, the *gemara* does not suggest that the category of *zonah* is relevant here, nor that marrying a minor would somehow constitute *bi'at zenut* in any meaningful sense. Even within the broader theoretical context of procreation, the *gemara* does not even raise that possibility. So what, then, is the prohibition about?

### Rabba's Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כֹּהֵן לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה: פּוֹק עַיֵּין בָּהּ, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא בָּעֵי לַהּ רַב הוּנָא מִינָּךְ. נְפַק עַיֵּין בָּהּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A priest may not marry a minor. Rav Ḥisda said to Rabba: Go** and **investigate** this *halakha*, **as in the evening Rav Huna** will **ask you** the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. **He went** and **investigated it,** and arrived at the following conclusion: **Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and he** also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

The first suggestion comes from Rabba, who proposes a combination of the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda holds that an *ailonit* is forbidden to a *kohen*; Rabbi Meir operates on the principle of *chaishinan lemiut* — we are concerned about minority likelihoods. Combining these two views, Rabba suggests: do not marry a *ketana* because she might grow up to become an *ailonit*. That is Rabba's proposal; now the *gemara* examines whether it holds up.

### Challenge to Rabba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא. וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: אַיְלוֹנִית זוֹנָה הָוְיָא. וּכְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִי סָבַר לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: קָטָן וּקְטַנָּה לֹא חוֹלְצִין וְלֹא מְיַבְּמִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: יָפֶה אָמַרְתָּ שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְצִין. ״אִישׁ״ כְּתִיב בַּפָּרָשָׁה, וּמַקְּשִׁינַן אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ. אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אֵין מְיַבְּמִין?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who** says that **one must be concerned for the minority.** Rabbi Meir does not allow one to assume that an unknown case is similar to the majority of cases. Consequently, one must take into account the possibility that a minor will turn out to be sexually underdeveloped, although this will not be true of most individuals. **And** he also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that **a sexually underdeveloped woman is a *zona*** and therefore forbidden to a priest. The Gemara challenges Rabba's explanation: **And does** Rabbi Eliezer **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A boy minor and a girl minor may not perform *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: You spoke well** when you said **that they may not perform *ḥalitza*,** as the term **"man" is written in the passage** of *ḥalitza* (Deuteronomy 25:7–10), which limits the *halakha* to an adult male, **and we compare a woman to a man** and therefore limit *ḥalitza* to an adult woman. **However, what is the reason** that **they may not perform levirate marriage?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [אָמַר לָהֶם:] קָטָן — שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא סָרִיס, קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּמָּצֵא אַיְלוֹנִית, וְנִמְצְאוּ פּוֹגְעִין בְּעֶרְוָה. וְתַנְיָא: קְטַנָּה מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת וְאֵינָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **said to them: A boy minor** may not perform levirate marriage **lest he be found** to be **a eunuch,** i.e., one who is incapable of fathering children for his late brother. Similarly, **a girl minor** may not perform levirate marriage **lest she be found** to be **sexually underdeveloped** when she grows up. In either case, the mitzva of levirate marriage does not apply, **and they turn out** to have **encountered a forbidden relative. And it was taught** in a different *baraita*: **A girl minor enters into levirate marriage but does not perform *ḥalitza*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.** This proves that Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with Rabbi Meir and is not concerned that a girl may turn out to be sexually underdeveloped.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִי סָבַר לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״זוֹנָה״ — זוֹנָה כִּשְׁמָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״זוֹנָה״ זוֹ מוּפְקֶרֶת. רַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לְהַשְׁקוֹתָהּ, וּבָא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ — עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to challenge Rabba's explanation of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. **And does** Rabbi Eliezer **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Wasn't it is taught in** a *baraita*: The ***zona*** forbidden to a priest is as **the name *zona*** implies, i.e., a married woman who committed adultery; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Akiva says: A *zona* is** a woman, even an unmarried woman, **who** is **available** to all, i.e., she has intercourse with whoever is interested. **Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash says: Even** if **her husband went to make her drink** the bitter waters after she disregarded his warning not to seclude herself with a certain man, and **he had intercourse with her on the way, he has** thereby **caused her** to become **a *zona*** because she was forbidden to him at the time, despite the fact that she is his wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: זוֹנָה — זוֹ אַיְלוֹנִית. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין זוֹנָה אֶלָּא גִּיּוֹרֶת וּמְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת וְשֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: פָּנוּי הַבָּא עַל הַפְּנוּיָה שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת — עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: A *zona* is a sexually underdeveloped woman. And the Rabbis say:** The term ***zona*** applies **only to a female convert, a freed** maidservant, **and one who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse. Rabbi Elazar says:** Even in the case of **an unmarried man who had intercourse with an unmarried woman not for the purpose of marriage, he has** thereby **caused her** to become **a *zona*.** This *baraita* proves that Rabbi Eliezer does not agree with Rabbi Yehuda.

The *gemara* challenges this reconstruction: does Rabbi Eliezer really hold like Rabbi Meir? The challenge draws from a *baraita* in which Rabbi Meir himself rules that a minor boy and a minor girl — a *katan* and a *ketana* — cannot perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*. When the Sages noted to Rabbi Meir that his position against *chalitzah* is well-founded — since the *parasha* uses the word *ish*, a man, and we derive that the woman must be an adult as well — they pressed him on why *yibbum* should also be prohibited. After all, *yibbum* does not require intent; it functions as a halakhic act of intercourse once performed, and since a girl above the age of three and a boy above the age of nine are considered to have performed such an act in a legally cognizable sense, why should the *mitzvah* not be fulfilled?

Rabbi Meir's answer is that a minor boy might later turn out to be a *saris* — a man with a fundamental physical condition that places him outside the normative category of procreation — and a minor girl might turn out to be an *ailonit*. If that were the case, there would be no *mitzvah* of *yibbum* to begin with, and what would have occurred retroactively would be a forbidden sexual act, since without the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* the woman remains forbidden as a brother's wife. This is precisely why Rabbi Meir's concern is formulated as *shema yimtza saris* and *shema timtza ailonit* rather than merely *shema yimtza akar* — there is something categorically significant about the *ailonit* and the *saris* beyond simple infertility, and that categorical status is what renders them outside the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* entirely.

The *gemara* now demonstrates that Rabbi Eliezer does not hold like Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Eliezer explicitly rules that a *ketana* may perform *yibbum*, even though she cannot perform *chalitzah* — since *chalitzah* requires adulthood as derived from the word *ish*. Rabbi Eliezer is manifestly unconcerned with the minority possibility that she might become an *ailonit*. This eliminates the first leg of Rabba's combination.

The *gemara* then proceeds to show that Rabbi Eliezer also does not hold like Rabbi Yehuda, by quoting a *baraita* that presents a full range of *tanna'ic* positions on the definition of *zonah*.

### Zonah Definitions

The *baraita* records a fascinating and consequential dispute among the *Tannaim* about the biblical category of *zonah* that renders a woman forbidden to a *kohen*, conducted with almost no subsequent *gemara* analysis despite its weighty halakhic implications. The positions are as follows:

The first opinion says simply: *zona kishmah* — a *zonah* as her name implies. This is the position of Rabbi Eliezer. Rashi explains that *zona kishmah* in Rabbi Eliezer's usage refers to a woman who committed adultery — she strayed from beneath her husband to others — and that for Rabbi Eliezer, the category of *zonah* is limited to a married woman who engaged in such an act.

Rabbi Akiva says: *zona* refers to a *mufkeret* — a promiscuous woman who has indiscriminate relations, even if not for payment.

Rabbi Matya ben Charash says: even a woman whose husband went to administer the *sotah* waters to her and a man had relations with her along the way — even that forbidden act within the very context of marriage renders her a *zonah*. This is a striking position: an isolated act of forbidden intercourse occurring within an otherwise completely legitimate marriage suffices to confer the status of *zonah*.

Rabbi Yehuda says: *zonah* refers to an *ailonit* — which is precisely the view we encountered in the *mishnah*.

The *Chachamim* say: *zonah* applies only to a female convert or a freed slave woman who had relations in the manner of *zenut*. This position is more limiting than that of Rabbi Matya ben Charash, since the *beilat zenut* in question would need to be an act forbidden under penalty of *kares* or an *issur kehunah*, and the concern extends either to having had relations with a non-Jew or to a more foundational view of how to regard the non-Jewish world. The implications for *kohanim* today — particularly regarding the question of a woman who has had relations with a non-Jew — are real and not infrequent.

One further opinion deserves attention: Rabbi Elazar says that any act of extramarital sex — even between two unmarried, unattached individuals, even in a committed relationship — renders the woman a *zonah*. He goes even further than Rabbi Akiva, who required promiscuity. Notably, a brief *Tosafot* on *panuy ha-ba al penuyah* adds that the prohibition applies *a fortiori* if the man is himself married, since then he is being *mezanneh al ishto* — unfaithful to his wife. What is fascinating here is that *Tosafot* is invoking a concept of marital fidelity as incumbent on both partners, even within a halakhic framework that historically permitted polygamy. This likely reflects the normative reality of Ashkenazic life after *Rabbeinu Gershom*, where monogamy had become the communal standard, and *Tosafot* appears to incorporate that sensibility into its reading.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלָיו״ — לְרַבּוֹת הַבּוֹגֶרֶת. וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״בְּתוּלָה״ — בְּתוּלָה שְׁלֵימָה מַשְׁמַע! אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** Even in the case of **an unmarried man who had intercourse with an unmarried woman not for the purpose of marriage, he has** thereby **caused her** to become **a *zona*.** The *baraita* previously cited concluded with: **Rav Amram said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

For the purposes of the *gemara*'s question, the critical point is that Rabbi Eliezer — the one who prohibits a *kohen* from marrying a *ketana* — holds *zona kishmah*, which in Rashi's reading means a woman who committed adultery. This is emphatically not the category of *ailonit*. The combination Rabba attempted — Rabbi Yehuda's view that an *ailonit* is a *zonah* together with Rabbi Meir's principle of *chaishinan lemiut* — cannot be attributed to Rabbi Eliezer, since Rabbi Eliezer holds neither position. Both legs of the combination collapse.

## Alternative Reasons

### Kohen Gadol Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָכָא בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עָסְקִינַן. לְאֵימַת קָנֵי לַה, לְכִי גָדְלָה [לְכִי גָדְלָה] — בְּעוּלָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that a priest may not marry a minor must be explained differently: **Here we are dealing with a High Priest,** and the problem is as follows: **When can he acquire her** as his wife? Only **when she is grown up.** However, if they had started living together as husband and wife when she was a minor, then when she is grown up and the marriage can legally take effect, **she is** already **a non-virgin,** and a High Priest is commanded to marry a virgin.

The *gemara* must now find an alternative explanation for why Rabbi Eliezer prohibits a *kohen* from marrying a *ketana*. Rava proposes: perhaps the case concerns a *kohen gadol*. A *kohen gadol*, it is widely held, may not marry a *bogeret* — a fully mature woman past *na'arus*. Now consider the situation: if a *ketana* betroths herself to a *kohen gadol*, when will the marriage take legal effect? Only when she reaches adulthood. By that time, she will already be a *be'ulah* — a non-virgin — because intercourse will have occurred between betrothal and the moment the *kiddushin* is legally binding. On this reasoning, the prohibition would not be unique to Rabbi Eliezer at all.

### Seduction Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְכַלֵּי לֵב, אִי דְּקַדְּשַׁהּ אֲבוּהּ — מֵהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הוּא דְּקָנֵי לַהּ. וְאִי דְּקַדִּשָׁה נַפְשַׁהּ — הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This explanation is **without reason. If her father betrothed her** to her husband, her husband **acquired her from that time,** as betrothal that a father carries out on his daughter's behalf when she is a minor is effective by Torah law. **And if** the minor **betrothed herself,** is **this Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion **and not** that of **the Rabbis?** The Rabbis would certainly agree that a High Priest may not marry a minor under these circumstances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט — וְחָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תִּתְפַּתֶּה עָלָיו. אִי הָכִי יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי! פִּתּוּיי קְטַנָּה אוֹנֶס הוּא, וְאוֹנֶס בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִישְׁרָא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: Actually,** Rabbi Eliezer's ruling includes **a common priest,** and the reason he cannot marry a minor is that **we are concerned lest she be seduced** by another man, due to her tender age and naïveté, while married **to him.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the same concern should apply to **an Israelite also.** The Gemara answers: **The seduction of a minor is** considered **rape, and a rape** victim remains **permitted** to her husband **in** a case where she is married to **an Israelite,** but not if she is married to a priest.

Rava himself immediately dismisses this suggestion — calling it *mi-blei lev*, a thought offered without sufficient consideration. He notes: if the father betrothed her, the marriage takes effect immediately and there is no problem. If she betrothed herself, the difficulty that would arise — she would be a *be'ulah* at the moment the *kiddushin* becomes binding — would be a problem for every *posek*, not only Rabbi Eliezer.

Rava therefore returns to the case of a *kohen hedyot* and offers a different rationale: we are concerned that she might be seduced by another man, rendering her forbidden to her *kohen* husband. One might object that this concern should apply equally to a non-*kohen* — why should any man marry a *ketana* if she is vulnerable to seduction? The answer is that *pittuy ketana ones hu* — seduction of a minor is legally equivalent to rape. Therefore, if a man has relations with a *ketana*, it is considered *ones*, non-volitional on her part, and she remains permitted to a non-*kohen* husband. But if she is married to a *kohen*, it makes no difference whether the act was volitional or not — she is forbidden to him regardless. This asymmetry is what makes the *ketana* specifically problematic for a *kohen*.

### Naara Age Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְהַאי תַּנָּא הוּא דְּתַנְיָא: ״בְּתוּלָה״, יָכוֹל קְטַנָּה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִשָּׁה״. אִי אִשָּׁה, יָכוֹל בּוֹגֶרֶת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּתוּלָה״. הָא כֵּיצַד? יָצְתָה מִכְּלַל קַטְנוּת, וְלִכְלַל בַּגְרוּת לֹא בָּאתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Rabbi Eliezer's ruling applies specifically **to a High Priest, and it is** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states: **"A virgin** of his own people shall he take for a wife [*isha*]" (Leviticus 21:14), one **might** have thought a High Priest may marry **a minor; the verse** therefore **states** that he must marry **a woman [*isha*],** i.e., an adult. **If** he must marry a **woman,** one **might** have thought it means **a grown woman. The verse** therefore **states** that he must marry **a virgin,** which excludes a grown woman, who is considered only a partial virgin because her hymen is not fully intact. **How so?** He must marry a woman who **has left the class of minority but** who **has not** yet **reached the class of grown womanhood,** i.e., he must marry a maiden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא דְּתַנְיָא: ״בְּתוּלָה״, אֵין בְּתוּלָה אֶלָּא נַעֲרָה. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהַנַּעֲרָה טוֹבַת מַרְאֶה מְאֹד בְּתוּלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: It is** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The High Priest must marry **a virgin, and** the term **virgin** refers **only** to **a maiden. And** a verse **similarly states: "And the maiden was very fair to look upon, a virgin,** and no man had known her" (Genesis 24:16).

Rav Pappa returns to the case of a *kohen gadol* and offers a different resolution, citing a specific *tanna*. The *baraita* reads: one might think a *kohen gadol* may marry a *ketana* — the *talmud lomar* teaches *isha*, a woman. And one might think he may marry a *bogeret* — the *talmud lomar* teaches *betula*. Since *betula* in this context means one whose *betulim* are fully intact — which is not the case with a *bogeret* — and *isha* excludes a *ketana*, the *kohen gadol* is restricted to the narrow window between the two: a girl who is *yatz'ah mi-klal ketana u-le-klal bagrut lo ba'at* — who has left the category of *ketana* but has not yet entered full maturity. That is precisely a *na'ara*, a girl between twelve and twelve-and-a-half years of age.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds another version of the same conclusion, citing a *tanna* who derives it differently: *ein betula ela na'ara* — *betula* means only a *na'ara*. The proof text is the verse describing Rivkah: *ve-ha-na'ara tovat mareh me'od betula ve-ish lo yada'ah* — the two terms are presented together, indicating that they describe the same status. The conclusion is identical: a *kohen gadol* may only marry a *na'ara*, the intermediate stage. All of these are distinct answers to a single question, and the *sugya* presents them as alternative approaches rather than a single resolved position.

## Halachic Ruling

### Rav Amram Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

At the close of this *sugya*, the *gemara* records: *amar Rav Amram ein halacha k'Rabbi Elazar* — we do not rule like Rabbi Elazar's position that any act of extramarital sex between two unmarried individuals renders the woman a *zonah* forbidden to a *kohen*. This ruling carries significant practical weight. Nowadays, when questions arise about whether a woman's prior sexual history disqualifies her from marrying a *kohen*, it is the position of the *Chachamim* — limiting *zonah* to a convert or freed slave woman who had relations in the manner of *zenut* — that governs, not Rabbi Elazar's sweeping definition. The ruling *ein halacha k'Rabbi Elazar* ensures that we do not extend the prohibition to every case of premarital relations.

As for whether an *ailonit* is forbidden to a *kohen*, since we rule like the *Chachamim* who restrict *zonah* to specific categories, the *ailonit* would not be included. The broader and more fraught questions — such as the *kohen* whose prospective wife has had relations with a non-Jew — belong to the category the *Chachamim* address, and those situations arise with some regularity in contemporary *psak*.

## Pru U'Revu Basics

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יִבָּטֵל אָדָם מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בְּרָאָם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A man may not neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply unless he** already **has children. Beit Shammai say:** One fulfills this mitzva with **two males, and Beit Hillel say: A male and a female, as it is stated: "Male and female He created them"** (Genesis 5:2).

The preceding discussion of an *ailonit* forbidden to a *kohen*, and the broader question of whether non-procreative marital sex constitutes *zenut*, forms a natural bridge to the *mishnah* that now opens on the primary *gemara* discussion of the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu*. It is worth noting that the central *sugyot* on *pru u'revu* are found here in Yevamot rather than in Kiddushin or Kesuvos — which is fitting, since Yevamot addresses questions of personal status and the legal dimensions of sexual relations, and *pru u'revu* belongs naturally in that conversation.

The *mishnah* frames the obligation not as "when have you fulfilled *pru u'revu*?" but rather as "do not cease from *pru u'revu* until you have children." This framing is deliberate. Unlike taking a *lulav* and *esrog*, *pru u'revu* is not a single, discrete act of fulfillment. People may try for many years without success. Moreover, in cases such as a man whose wife of twenty years has died — the very scenario at the heart of *yibbum* — the framing of "do not stop" is apt: the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* remains incumbent upon him and should motivate remarriage.

### Shammai/Hillel

The *mishnah* presents the dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* on the minimum required to fulfill *pru u'revu*. *Beit Shammai* says: two males. *Beit Hillel* says: a male and a female, as the verse states *zachar u-nekevah bera'am* — male and female He created them.

### Hillel's Purpose

The verse *Beit Hillel* cites — *zachar u-nekevah bera'am* — is not itself the verse of *pru u'revu*. Its invocation requires explanation. *Beit Hillel*'s reasoning appears to be twofold. First, there is the practical concern of continuing to populate the world: a son and a daughter leave behind both male and female contributors to the next generation. Second, and perhaps more profoundly, the verse gestures toward *imitatio Dei* — replicating the original divine act of creation by bringing forth male and female into the world. The *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* is thus not merely instrumental but participates in the ongoing work of creation inaugurated when God fashioned *adam* and *chavah*.

A practical dimension of *Beit Hillel*'s position also deserves attention. Because the requirement is a boy *and* a girl, couples who have two children of the same sex must continue. This means that the actual average number of children produced under *Beit Hillel*'s standard will exceed two: roughly half of couples will stop at two — those who happen to have a boy and a girl — while the other half will continue until they achieve the required combination. The expected birth rate under *Beit Hillel* is therefore something in the range of two-and-a-half children per couple, not exactly two, which has its own demographic interest.

### Obligations and Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה בָּטֵיל, מֵאִשָּׁה לָא בָּטֵיל. מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְאָדָם כַּמָּה בָּנִים, אָסוּר לַעֲמוֹד בְּלֹא אִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא טוֹב הֱיוֹת הָאָדָם לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the mishna's wording that if **he** already **has children he may neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply,** but **he may not neglect** the mitzva to have **a wife.** This **supports** what **Rav Naḥman said** in the name of **Shmuel, who said: Even** if **a man has several children, it is prohibited to remain without a wife, as it is stated: "It is not good that the man should be alone"** (Genesis 2:18).

The *gemara* also raises the question of what it means for a man who already has children to be *batel* — released — from *pru u'revu*, but who is still not released from the obligation to be married. This is derived from the statement of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *af al pi she-yesh lo le-adam kama banim, assur la'amod belo isha* — even if a man has several children, he may not remain without a wife. The proof text is *lo tov heyot ha-adam levado* — it is not good for a person to be alone. Marriage is thus an intrinsic good, not merely an instrument for procreation. This is an important clarification: elsewhere the value of marriage is framed instrumentally, as a safeguard against transgression, but this *gemara* grounds it in the inherent worth of human companionship.

### Selling Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — בָּטֵיל מִפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, וּבָטֵיל נָמֵי מֵאִשָּׁה. נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל! לָא: אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה בַּת בָּנִים, יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה דְּלָאו בַּת בָּנִים. נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לִמְכּוֹר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בִּשְׁבִיל בָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** a different version of the inference from the mishna: If **he** already **has children, he may neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply and** he **may also neglect** the mitzva to have **a wife. Shall we say** this **is a conclusive refutation of** what **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel** said? The Gemara responds: **No,** it means that **if he does not have children he must marry a woman** capable **of** bearing **children,** whereas if **he has children he may marry a woman who is not** capable **of** bearing **children. A practical difference** between a man who has children and one who does not is whether he is permitted **to sell a Torah scroll in order to** marry a woman capable of having **children.** This is permitted only for one who does not yet have children.

The *gemara* then considers how the *mishnah* might be read in the opposite direction — that once *pru u'revu* is fulfilled, one is also released from any obligation to marry. The *gemara* resolves the tension: the *mishnah* means only that after fulfilling *pru u'revu*, one need not specifically marry a fertile woman, nor need one go so far as to sell a *Sefer Torah* to fund a marriage. But the intrinsic obligation to be married remains. The statement *batel mi-pru u'revu* does not mean *batel mi-isha*.

### Shammai Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? יָלְפִינַן מִמֹּשֶׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּנֵי מֹשֶׁה גֵּרְשׁוֹם וֶאֱלִיעֶזֶר״. וּבֵית הִלֵּל: יָלְפִינַן מִבְּרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם. וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, לֵילְפֵי מִבְּרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Beit Shammai say** that one fulfills the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply when he has **two males.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Beit Shammai?** The Gemara answers: **We learn** this **from Moses as it is written: "The sons of Moses, Gershom and Eliezer"** (I Chronicles 23:15). Since Moses did not have any other children, two sons must be sufficient to fulfill the mitzva. **And** the reason of **Beit Hillel** is that **we learn from the creation of the world,** as mankind was created male and female. The Gemara asks: **And Beit Shammai, let them learn from the creation of the world** as well.

Returning to the *mishnah*, the *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama de-Beit Shammai?* What is the reasoning of *Beit Shammai* for requiring two males? The answer is: *yalfina mi-Moshe* — we learn from Moshe Rabbeinu, who had two sons: Gershom and Eliezer. This is presented not as a logical argument about the purpose of *pru u'revu* but as a proof of its minimum threshold.

The *gemara* immediately asks why *Beit Shammai* does not instead learn from the creation of the world — *beri'ato shel olam* — as *Beit Hillel* does, which would point toward a son and a daughter. And conversely, why does *Beit Hillel* not learn from Moshe? The *gemara* answers both questions together.

### Necessity vs. Normative Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵין דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל נָמֵי, לֵילְפוּ מִמֹּשֶׁה! אָמְרִי לָךְ: מֹשֶׁה מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** We do not derive a rule from a case of necessity. Mankind was initially created with a male and female because otherwise reproduction would not have been possible. However, this fact cannot serve as a source that the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply is fulfilled only once one has a son and a daughter. The Gemara asks: **And Beit Hillel, let them also learn from Moses.** Beit Hillel could **say to you: Moses acted** based **on his own perception** when he separated from his wife.

*Beit Shammai* responds to the argument from creation: *ein danin mi-efshar mi-she'i efshar* — one cannot derive a rule from a case of necessity. The world could not have been populated unless Adam and Chava had both male and female children; that was a structural requirement of the beginning of creation, not a model for individual obligation. The fact that God commanded *pru u'revu* for the first pair necessarily implied both sexes, but that tells us nothing about what is required today when the world is already populated.

*Beit Hillel* responds to the argument from Moshe: *Moshe mi-da'atei hu de'avid* — Moshe acted on his own understanding, and his behavior is not a normative model. His case belongs to the three things Moshe decided on his own initiative and in which his judgment was confirmed by God — *ve-hiskima da'ato le-da'at ha-Makom*.

## Moshe's Exception

### Wife Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים עָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה מִדַּעְתּוֹ, וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם: פֵּירַשׁ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, וְשִׁיבֵּר הַלּוּחוֹת, וְהוֹסִיף יוֹם אֶחָד. פֵּירַשׁ מִן הָאִשָּׁה, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? אֲמַר: וּמָה יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁלֹּא דִּבְּרָה עִמָּהֶם שְׁכִינָה אֶלָּא לְפִי שָׁעָה, וְקָבַע לָהֶם זְמַן, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״אַל תִּגְּשׁוּ אֶל אִשָּׁה״ — אֲנִי, שֶׁמְּיוּחָד לְדִבּוּר בְּכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה, וְלֹא קָבַע לִי זְמַן — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם שׁוּבוּ לָכֶם לְאׇהֳלֵיכֶם. וְאַתָּה פֹּה עֲמוֹד עִמָּדִי״.
>
> **Translation:* **as it is taught in** a *baraita*: **Moses did three things** based **on his own perception, and his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent: He separated from** his **wife, he broke the tablets, and he added one day** to the days of separation before the revelation at Sinai. When Moses **separated from** his **wife** after the revelation at Sinai, **what did he interpret** that led him to do so? **He said: If** in the case of **Israel, with whom the Divine Presence spoke only temporarily and for whom** God **set** a specific **time for** revelation, **the Torah stated: "Do not approach a woman"** (Exodus 19:15), **I,** Moses, **who am set aside for** divine **speech all the time and for whom** God **did not set** a specific **time, all the more so** I must separate from my wife. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as it is stated** after the revelation at Sinai: **"Go say to them: Return to your tents; and you, stand here with Me"** (Deuteronomy 5:26–27).

The first of the three decisions attributed to Moshe's independent judgment is *perash min ha-isha* — he separated from his wife after the revelation at Sinai. The *gemara* reconstructs Moshe's reasoning: if the entire Jewish people, who received the divine communication only for a brief, defined moment — and were told not to approach a woman in preparation for it — how much more so does Moshe, who communes with God at any time without advance notice, need to remain in a constant state of readiness? Moshe could never know when God would speak to him, and therefore he could not maintain a marital relationship. The confirmation that God approved this decision comes from the verse: *lech emor lahem shuvu lachem le-oholeichem* — go tell them to return to their tents — followed immediately by *ve-ata po amod imadi* — but you, stand here with Me. The instruction for Israel to return to their wives is paired with an instruction for Moshe to remain permanently in divine attendance. This is read not as a temporary arrangement but as an ongoing existential state.

### Broken Tablets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* שִׁיבֵּר אֶת הַלּוּחוֹת, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? אָמַר: וּמָה פֶּסַח, שֶׁהוּא אֶחָד מִשֵּׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִצְווֹת, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״. הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּדִים — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר שִׁבַּרְתָּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: יִישַׁר כֹּחֲךָ שֶׁשִּׁבַּרְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:* Moses **broke the tablets** following the sin of the Golden Calf. **What did he interpret** that led him to do so? Moses **said: If** in the case of the **Paschal lamb, which is** only **one of 613 mitzvot, the Torah states: "No alien shall eat of it"** (Exodus 12:43), excluding not only gentiles but apostate Jews as well, then here, in the case of the Golden Calf, where the tablets represent **the entire Torah and** where **the Jewish people** are **apostates,** as they are worshipping the calf, **all the more so** must they be excluded from receiving them. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as it is written:** "The first tablets **that you broke [*asher shibbarta*]"** (Exodus 34:1), **and Reish Lakish said:** The word *asher* is an allusion to the fact that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: May your strength be true [*yishar koḥakha*] that you broke** the tablets.

The second decision is *shiber ha-luchot* — Moshe broke the tablets. The *gemara* reconstructs his reasoning: if *Pesach*, which is only one of 613 *mitzvot*, excludes one who has become *mechuyyav* — estranged from God through his deeds — from participation, then how much more so should a people who have become *mumarim* in some sense be excluded from receiving the entire Torah in its totality? On this reasoning, Moshe concluded that presenting the tablets to Israel in its current state would be wrong, and he broke them.

The divine confirmation is the verse: *asher shibarta* — which you broke — and Reish Lakish reads this with a characteristic wordplay: *yishar kochacha asher shibarta* — well done, *shkoyach*, that you broke them. The phrase *asher*, understood here almost in the sense of *ashrei*, becomes an expression of divine endorsement.

One further point arises: the underlying *kal va-homer* from *Pesach* is itself noted as imperfect — *Tosafot* observes that *ein zeh kal va-chomer gamur*, since *Pesach* involves a *korban kodashim* and the *korban* dimension may make it more exclusive, whereas the entire Torah has a dimension of outreach and *teshuvah*. Nevertheless, this was Moshe's reasoning, and it was confirmed.

### Extra Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הוֹסִיף יוֹם אֶחָד מִדַּעְתּוֹ, מַאי דְּרַשׁ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתָּם הַיּוֹם וּמָחָר״. הַיּוֹם כְּמָחָר: מָה מָחָר לֵילוֹ עִמּוֹ, אַף הַיּוֹם לֵילוֹ עִמּוֹ. וְלַיְלָה דְּהָאִידָּנָא נְפַק לֵיהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי לְבַר מֵהָאִידָּנָא. וְהִסְכִּימָה דַּעְתּוֹ לְדַעַת הַמָּקוֹם — דְּלָא שָׁרְיָא שְׁכִינָה עַד שַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:* When Moses **added one day** to the days of separation before the revelation at Sinai based **on his** own **perception, what did he interpret** that led him to do so? He reasoned **that** since **it is written: "And sanctify them today and tomorrow"** (Exodus 19:10), the juxtaposition of the words "today" and "tomorrow" teaches that **today** is **like tomorrow: Just as tomorrow** the men and women will separate for that day **and** the **night** preceding **it, so too, today** requires separation for the day **and** the **night** preceding **it.** Since God spoke to him in the morning, **and the night of that day** already **passed,** Moses said: **Conclude from** this that separation must be in effect for **two days aside from now,** i.e., not including the day of the command. Therefore, he extended the mitzva of separation by one day. **And his perception agreed with the perception of the Omnipresent, as** the **Divine Presence did not rest** upon Mount Sinai **until Shabbat** morning, as Moses had determined.

The third decision is *hosif yom echad mi-da'ato* — Moshe added one extra day of separation before the revelation at Sinai. The Torah instructed: *ve-kidashtam ha-yom u-machar* — prepare them today and tomorrow. Moshe calculated that *machar leilo imo* — tomorrow includes the night before it. If the command was issued at some point during the day, then "today" had already partially elapsed; to count two full days starting from the night, Moshe had to push the beginning of the period back. The math therefore required beginning the count from the previous evening, adding what amounted to a third day. The divine confirmation is derived from the same structural reading: God's intent corresponded to Moshe's conclusion.

The *gemara*'s point in presenting all three cases is to establish that Moshe's behavior — including his having had only two sons and apparently not having more children — cannot serve as a normative halakhic model. *Moshe mi-da'atei hu de'avid ve-hiskima da'ato le-da'at ha-Makom*: he acted uniquely, under unique circumstances, and his judgment was uniquely confirmed. *Beit Hillel* is therefore correct not to derive the minimum of *pru u'revu* from Moshe's family.

## Baraita Variants

### Two Males/Females

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּשְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נָתָן אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתּוֹסֶף לָלֶדֶת אֶת אָחִיו אֶת הָבֶל״: הֶבֶל וַאֲחוֹתוֹ, קַיִן וַאֲחוֹתוֹ, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי שָׁת לִי אֱלֹהִים זֶרַע אַחֵר תַּחַת הֶבֶל כִּי הֲרָגוֹ קָיִן״. וְרַבָּנַן? אוֹדוֹיֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹדְיָא.
>
> **Translation:* § **It is taught in** a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says** that **Beit Shammai say:** The mitzva to be fruitful and multiply is fulfilled with **two males and two females. And Beit Hillel say: A male and a female. Rav Huna said: What is the reason of Rabbi Natan, in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** It is **as it is written: "And again she bore his brother [*et aḥiv*] Abel [*et Hevel*]"** (Genesis 4:2). The use of the superfluous word "et" indicates that she gave birth to **Abel and his sister,** in addition to **Cain and his sister. And it states: "For God has appointed me another seed instead of Abel; for Cain slew him"** (Genesis 4:25). This indicates that one must have at least four children. **And the Rabbis,** how do they understand this verse? In their opinion, Eve **was thanking God** for granting her another child, but one is not obligated to have four children.

After establishing the basic positions of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, the *gemara* presents alternative versions of their dispute recorded by Rabbi Natan. The first *baraita* has Rabbi Natan reporting that *Beit Shammai* requires two males and two females. This version essentially combines both the *imitatio dei* logic — one of each sex — and the *pru/revu* structure, perhaps reading *pru* as generating one pair and *revu* as doubling it to two pairs.

Ravina asks: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Natan according to *Beit Shammai*? The proof is from the verse describing Chava's births: *va-tosef laledet et achiv et Hevel* — and she continued to give birth to his brother Hevel. The word *et* is understood as indicating a sister who was born alongside each male child — Kayin was born with a twin sister, and Hevel with a twin sister. Subsequently, when Kayin killed Hevel, Chava had Shet — with the verse explaining: *ki shat li Elohim zera acher tachat Hevel ki harago Kayin* — God has given me another seed in place of Hevel, whom Kayin slew. This implies that by Hevel's death, the fulfillment of *pru u'revu* was incomplete, requiring replacement. The *Hagahot ha-Bach* reads this as indicating that *pru u'revu* requires two males and two females, since Hevel's death left a deficit that Shet came to fill. The Rabbis, however, reject this derivation: Chava's words were simply an expression of gratitude — *hoda'aya de-ka moda* — not a statement about the requirements of *pru u'revu*.

### Male & Female

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ זָכָר אוֹ נְקֵבָה. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נָתָן אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹהוּ בְרָאָהּ לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״, וְהָא עֲבַד לַהּ שֶׁבֶת.
>
> **Translation:* **It is taught in another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says** that **Beit Shammai say:** The mitzva to be fruitful and multiply is fulfilled with **a male and a female. And Beit Hillel say: Either a male or a female. Rava said: What is the reason of Rabbi Natan in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel?** It is **as it is stated: "He did not create it a waste; He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18), **and one has made** the earth **inhabited** to a greater degree by adding even one child to the world.

The second *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Natan presents an even more striking version: here, *Beit Shammai* requires a male and a female — what we had previously understood to be the *Beit Hillel* position — while *Beit Hillel* requires only one child of either sex. Rava explains *Beit Hillel*'s position in this version from the verse in Yeshayahu: *lo tohu bera'ah la-shevet yetzarah* — God did not create the world to be desolate but to settle it. Even contributing one additional person to the settled world is a fulfillment of *la-shevet*. This is a notably minimalist reading of the *mitzvah*, and it places the entire weight of *pru u'revu* on the concept of *yishuv ha-olam* — settling the world — rather than on any particular numerical threshold.

It is striking that across these *baraitot*, the rationale of *la-shevet yetzarah* plays a decisive role not only in determining the minimum number of children required, but also, as we will see, in shaping the question of what happens when those children predecease the parent.

## Non-Jews & Converts

### Convert's Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים בְּגַיּוּתוֹ וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — דְּהָא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּקָטָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:* § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over the following issue: If a man **had children when he** was **a gentile and he** subsequently **converted, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He has** already **fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply, and Reish Lakish said:** He has **not fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply. Rabbi Yoḥanan said he has fulfilled** the mitzva **to be fruitful and multiply, as he** already **had** children. **And Reish Lakish said he has not fulfilled** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply,** as the legal status of **a convert who** just **converted is like** that of **a child** just **born,** and it is considered as though he did not have children.

The *gemara* next addresses a case that sits at the intersection of the legal question of *pru u'revu* and the broader question of a convert's legal continuity: what of a person who had children as a non-Jew and then converted? He has biological children who were legally recognized as his children under the laws governing non-Jews. Does this count toward the fulfillment of *pru u'revu*?

Two considerations are in tension. On one hand, as a non-Jew he did not bear the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* in the same sense as a Jew. On the other hand, as is well established, *ger she-nitgayer ke-katan she-nolad dami* — a convert is like a newborn child — which means his prior legal relationships may no longer be recognized after conversion.

### Conversion Debate

Rabbi Yochanan says: he has fulfilled *pru u'revu* — *kiyam pru u'revu* — because he has children. Reish Lakish says: he has not — *lo kiyam pru u'revu* — because as a convert he is like a newborn, and those children are no longer legally considered his.

The *gemara* notes that this dispute tracks a parallel one: the same two *amora'im* dispute whether the first child born to this man after his conversion has the status of *bechor* for purposes of inheritance. Rabbi Yochanan says the first post-conversion child is not a *bechor le-nachala*, because the man's biological firstborn preceded the conversion and is considered *reishit ono* — the first of his strength — in a biological sense. Reish Lakish says the post-conversion child is a *bechor*, because the convert starts fresh. The *gemara* notes that both disputes are necessary, as the reasoning in each case might not entail the conclusion in the other.

### Inheritance/Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים בְּגַיּוּתוֹ וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין לוֹ בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה, דְּהָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ ״רֵאשִׁית אוֹנוֹ״. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: יֵשׁ לוֹ בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה, גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר כְּקָטָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara comments: **And they follow their** regular line of reasoning, **as it was stated:** If **one had children** when **he** was **a gentile and he** subsequently **converted, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He does not have a firstborn with regard to inheritance,** i.e., the first son born to him after his conversion does not inherit a double portion, **as** this man already **had "the first of his strength"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), the Torah's description of the firstborn in this context, before he converted. **And Reish Lakish said: He does have a firstborn with regard to inheritance,** as the legal status of **a convert who** just **converted is like** that of **a child** just **born.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָהִיא קַמַּיְיתָא, בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי בְּנֵי פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה נִינְהוּ, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן נַחֲלָה, דְּלָאו בְּנֵי נַחֲלָה נִינְהוּ — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. וְאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא — בְּהָא קָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אֲבָל בְּהַהִיא — אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara adds: **And** it is **necessary** to state their opinions in both cases. **As, had it** only been **taught** to **us with regard to that first** case of the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply, one might have said that **it is** only **in that** case that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** his opinion, **because from the outset,** gentiles **are also subject to** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply. However, with regard to inheritance,** since **they are not subject** to the *halakhot* of **inheritance,** one might **say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan **concedes to Reish Lakish. And** conversely, **if** their dispute **was stated** only **with regard to this** issue of inheritance, I would have said that **it is** only **in this** case that **Reish Lakish said** his opinion, as the *halakhot* of inheritance do not apply to gentiles. **But with regard to that** case, the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply, one might **say** that **he concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Consequently, it is **necessary** for both disputes to be recorded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״בָּעֵת הַהִיא שָׁלַח בְּרֹאדַךְ בַּלְאֲדָן בֶּן בַּלְאֲדָן מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל וְגוֹ׳״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּגַיּוּתָן — אִית לְהוּ חַיִיס, נִתְגַּיְּירוּ — לֵית לְהוּ חַיִיס.
>
> **Translation:* **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises an objection to Reish Lakish** based upon the verse: **"At that time Berodach-baladan, son of Baladan, king of Babylon, sent** a letter" (II Kings 20:12), which indicates that gentiles are considered to be the children of their parents. Therefore, when they convert, they should already have fulfilled the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply. Reish Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **When they are gentiles they do have** family **lineage,** but when **they convert they do not have lineage,** as they now belong to the family of the Jewish people and their previous lineage is disregarded.

The *gemara* spells out why both cases must be stated explicitly. With respect to *pru u'revu*, one might have argued that Rabbi Yochanan acknowledges the biological children because even as non-Jews, those individuals were *bnei pru u'revu* — they were born within a normative framework where *pru u'revu* applies to all of humanity. The verse supports this: God commanded *pru u'revu* first to Adam and Chava, and then again to Noach, making it a universal obligation. This is not a major *chiddush* — it is the plain sense of the Torah's universal address. Nevertheless, the question of whether non-Jews are formally *metzuveh* in *pru u'revu* is debated among *rishonim*, and its practical implications are real: is it a *mitzvah* to arrange a *shidduch* between two non-Jewish individuals as part of *milu et ha-aretz*?

In the inheritance case, by contrast, one might have thought that Rabbi Yochanan would concede to Reish Lakish, since inheritance operates according to strictly halakhic categories of legal parentage rather than the broad biological framework that underlies *pru u'revu*. And correspondingly, one might have thought that in the *pru u'revu* case, Reish Lakish might concede to Rabbi Yochanan. Since each case has a distinct internal logic, both disputes must be stated.

### Slaves vs. Non-Jews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִין בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חַיִיס, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁבוּ לָכֶם פֹּה עִם הַחֲמוֹר״: עַם הַדּוֹמֶה לַחֲמוֹר. מֵיתִיבִי: ״וּלְצִיבָא חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בָּנִים וְעֶשְׂרִים עֲבָדִים״! אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: כְּ״פַר בֶּן בָּקָר״. אִי הָכִי, הָכָא נָמֵי! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּיַחֲסִינְהוּ בִּשְׁמַיְיהוּ וּבִשְׁמָא דַאֲבוּהוֹן, וְהָכָא לָא מְפָרֵשׁ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: יַחֲסִינְהוּ בְּדוּכְתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי בַּאֲבוּהוֹן וּבְאַבָּא דַאֲבוּהוֹן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלָחֵם הַמֶּלֶךְ אָסָא אֶל בֶּן הֲדַד בֶּן טַבְרִימּוֹן בֶּן חֶזְיוֹן מֶלֶךְ אֲרָם הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּדַמֶּשֶׂק לֵאמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:* **Rav said: Everyone agrees with regard to** a Canaanite **slave, that he does not have lineage, as it is written** that Abraham said to his slaves: **"Remain here with the donkey"** (Genesis 22:5). This verse is interpreted to mean that they are **a nation comparable to a donkey,** which has no lineage. The Gemara **raises an objection** based upon a verse pertaining to Jonathan's Canaanite slave: **"And Ziba had fifteen sons and twenty servants"** (II Samuel 9:10), which indicates that a slave's sons are in fact considered his sons. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** This is **like** the expression: **A bullock, son of a bull.** The word son in this context merely denotes progeny, not lineage. **The Gemara asks: If so, here too,** with regard to gentiles, there is no proof from the verse about Berodach-baladan that they have family lineage. The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as** the Bible **identified him by his name and by his father's name,** thereby emphasizing the family connection. **But here,** it does **not specify** the names of Ziba's children. **And if you wish, say** instead that the Bible **identified** gentiles **elsewhere by their father and their father's father, as it is written: "And King Asa sent them to Ben-hadad, son of Tabrimmon, son of Hezion, king of Aram, who dwelled in Damascus, saying"** (I Kings 15:18).

Rav Ami states a point on which all agree: with respect to a slave, even the initial legal recognition of parentage was absent. A slave's children are not legally his children in any halakhically recognized sense, and therefore even Rabbi Yochanan would concede that a freed slave who had children during his enslavement cannot fulfill *pru u'revu* through those children. The verse *shvu lachem po im ha-chamor* — remain here with the donkey — is read as *im ha-domeh la-chamor*, those who are similar to donkeys, understood as a reference to a slave among those accompanying Avraham to the *akeida*, illustrating that a slave lacks the legal standing of a full person in this regard.

The *gemara* then asks: if non-Jewish parentage is merely biological, why do we assume it is legal? The answer is that the biblical text explicitly traces paternal descent — *ben so-and-so ben so-and-so* — in the case of non-Jews, while no such genealogical recognition appears in contexts involving slaves. Moreover, in some cases non-Jewish parentage is traced across multiple generations — grandfather and great-grandfather — indicating not merely biological reproduction but a recognized family structure. This distinguishes the non-Jewish case from the slave case and supports the initial recognition of legal parentage for non-Jews, even if that recognition dissolves upon conversion according to Reish Lakish.

## Children's Longevity

### Death of Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיתְּמַר: הָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים וּמֵתוּ, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:* § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over the following issue: If a man **had children and they died, Rav Huna said:** He has **fulfilled** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply** through these children. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He has **not fulfilled** the mitzva.

The final question the *gemara* raises on this daf is whether *pru u'revu* is fulfilled if a person's children predecease him. This question connects to the earlier material about Chava's having Shet in place of Hevel — which the *gemara* initially read as suggesting that the death of a child can undo the fulfillment of *pru u'revu* — and it also connects to the purpose-driven analysis of *la-shevet yetzarah*. The case is stated clearly: *hayu lo banim u-mesu* — he had children and they died.

### Huna's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קִיֵּים — מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב אַסִּי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ כׇּל נְשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּגּוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי רוּחַ מִלְּפָנַי יַעֲטוֹף וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara clarifies the reasons for their opinions: **Rav Huna said** he has **fulfilled** the mitzva **due to** a statement **of Rav Asi, as Rav Asi said** that the reason for this mitzva is that the Messiah, **son of David, will not come until all the souls of the body have been finished,** i.e., until all souls that are destined to inhabit physical bodies will do so, **as it is stated: "For the spirit that enwraps itself is from Me,** and the souls that I have made" (Isaiah 57:16).

Rav Huna rules: *m'kayem pru u'revu* — he has fulfilled *pru u'revu*. The children were born; the biological event of birth suffices.

Rav Huna grounds his position in the view of Rabbi Assi, who states: *ein ben David ba ad she-yichlu kol ha-neshamot she-ba-guf* — the son of David, the *Mashiach*, will not come until all the souls stored in the heavenly repository known as *guf* have been brought into the world. On this understanding, the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* functions as the mechanism for drawing down souls and thereby hastening the coming of *Mashiach*. Each birth depletes the store; each soul that enters the world brings redemption closer. If this is the purpose of *pru u'revu*, then even if a child dies, the soul has been brought into the world and the goal has been advanced. The birth itself is the *kiyum*, regardless of how long the child lives.

### Living Settlement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קִיֵּים פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — ״לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:* **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** he has **not fulfilled** the mitzva, as **we require "He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18), **and** this **is not** fulfilled when the children have passed away and no longer inhabit the earth.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *lo kiyam pru u'revu* — he has not fulfilled the *mitzvah*. His reasoning draws again on *la-shevet yetzarah*: the purpose of procreation is to settle the world, and settling the world requires living people who actually contribute to it. A child who has died is no longer settling the world. The fulfillment of *pru u'revu* thus requires not only that the children be born, but that they survive and inhabit the world.

This raises a further question about the practical scope of Rabbi Yochanan's position. If a man has children and they live for decades — and then, at the age of, say, seventy, predecease their ninety-year-old father — does he retroactively lose his *kiyum*? The *gemara* does not fully resolve this, but the underlying principle of *la-shevet* suggests that there is a threshold of lived contribution to the world, not merely the fact of birth.

### Raising Children

Rabbi Yochanan's position, that *la-shevet* requires living children who actually inhabit and contribute to the world, opens a broader conceptual window. If *pru u'revu* is not simply about the biological event of birth but about bringing people into the world who then settle and sustain it, then the *mitzvah* encompasses not just the having of children but their raising. This understanding carries significant halakhic implications for couples who face infertility. If part of the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* is raising children rather than merely birthing them, adoption may constitute at least a partial *kiyum* of *pru u'revu*. Some *poskim* have indeed understood it this way — that bringing children to adulthood and contributing to their formation as people who will settle the world is itself a dimension of *pru u'revu*, not merely a worthy act adjacent to it.
