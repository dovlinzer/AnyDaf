# Bava Batra 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodesh Kodashim Measurement

### Pesuk Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וְהַבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה הַמֶּלֶךְ שְׁלֹמֹה לַה׳ – שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה אׇרְכּוֹ, וְעֶשְׂרִים רׇחְבּוֹ, וּשְׁלֹשִׁים אַמָּה קוֹמָתוֹ״; וּכְתִיב: ״וְלִפְנֵי הַדְּבִיר – עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה אֹרֶךְ, וְעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה רֹחַב, וְעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה קוֹמָתוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** **It is written: "And the house that King Solomon built for the Lord, was sixty cubits in length, and twenty cubits in width, and its height was thirty cubits"** (I Kings 6:2). **But it is** also **written: "And before the partition was twenty cubits in length, and twenty cubits in width, and its height was twenty cubits"** (I Kings 6:20).

The daf opens with a well-known contradiction in *pesukim* concerning the height of the *dvir* — the *kodesh kodashim* — in the time of Shlomo. One *pasuk* gives its height as twenty *amot*, while another, in the context of describing the dimensions of the entire *beis hamikdash*, implies a height of thirty *amot*. The *gemara* sets out to resolve this tension.

### 20 Amot Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ: כִּי קָא חָשֵׁיב – מִשְּׂפַת כְּרוּבִים וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to** the villagers that the reason for the difference is that **when** the latter verse **calculated** the height, it did so **from the** upper **edge of the cherubs and upward.**

The resolution offered is that the measurement of twenty *amot* refers specifically to the space *above* the *keruvim*, not to the full height of the chamber from floor to ceiling. In the *kodesh kodashim*, which measured twenty by twenty *amot* in the time of Shlomo, Shlomo had placed two large *keruvim* standing upright on the ground, each ten *amot* tall, with their wings extended — one wing touching one wall, the other extending toward the center. The twenty *amot* of height cited in the relevant *pasuk*, then, was measured not from the floor but from the tops of the *keruvim* upward, capturing only the blank space above them.

But this immediately raises a question: why measure only the empty space above? What is the *gemara* teaching by drawing our attention to that particular span? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the verse **teaching us** by considering only the area above the cherubs?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה; מָה לְמַעְלָה אֵין מְשַׁמֵּשׁ כְּלוּם, אַף לְמַטָּה אֵין מְשַׁמֵּשׁ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The verse **teaches us this:** The area **below** the cherubs **is like** the area **above** them; **just as** the area **above** the cherubs' wings, which were spread out in the air, **was not used for anything,** i.e., it was empty space, **so too** the area **below** them **was not used for anything** and was empty.

The answer given is *hah kamashma lan lemata kelemala* — that the space below is like the space above. Just as the space above the *keruvim* was entirely empty and functioned for no physical object, so too the space below — occupied by the *aron* and the *keruvim* themselves — was, in a certain sense, as if it were empty. The measurement communicates a theological point, not merely an architectural one. This supports the statement of Rabbi Levi, or according to some, Rabbi Yochanan.

### Masoret: Non-Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי לֵוִי – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דָּבָר זֶה מָסוֹרֶת בְּיָדֵינוּ מֵאֲבוֹתֵינוּ – מְקוֹם אָרוֹן וּכְרוּבִים אֵינוֹ מִן הַמִּדָּה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אָרוֹן שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה, יֵשׁ לוֹ רֶיוַח עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת לְכׇל רוּחַ וְרוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Levi, as Rabbi Levi said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who said: **This matter is a tradition handed** down **to us by our ancestors: The space** occupied by the **Ark** of the Covenant **and** the **cherubs is not** included **in the measurement** of the Holy of Holies in which it rested, as miraculously it did not occupy any space at all. The Gemara comments: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: When they brought **the Ark that Moses crafted** into the Holy of Holies in the Temple of King Solomon, even though the total width of the Holy of Holies was only twenty cubits, nevertheless the Ark **had ten cubits** of empty **space** between it and the wall **in each and every direction.**

Rabbi Levi — or Rabbi Yochanan — states: *davar zeh masoret beyadeinu me'avoseinu* — this is a tradition in our hands going back to our forefathers: *mekom aron ve'keruvim eino min hamiddah* — the place of the *aron* and the *keruvim* is not part of the measurement. The precise formulation varies in different textual traditions, some including the word *keruvim* and some not, but the principle is the same: if you measured from the western wall of the *kodesh kodashim* to the *aron*, you would find ten *amot*; if you measured from the eastern wall to the *aron*, you would also find ten *amot*; and yet the *aron* itself, occupying physical space in the middle, somehow contributed nothing to the overall measurement. The entire chamber remained, by the count, twenty *amot* from wall to wall.

This is, as the *gemara* presents it, frankly extraordinary — something from another dimension, as it were. The theological significance is striking. The *aron* and the *keruvim*, the very objects that most concretely represent God's presence in the world, are precisely the objects that take up no physical space. One is reminded of Shlomo's own words at the dedication of the *beis hamikdash*: *af ki hashamayim ushmei hashamayim lo yechalkelucha af ki habayis hazeh* — even the heavens cannot contain You, God; how much less this house. The paradox of a finite structure housing an infinite presence is built into the very architecture. The most sacred objects in the most sacred space are, in a measurable sense, not there.

This may also speak to the reality of the second *beis hamikdash*, in which there was no *aron* at all — raising the question of whether God's presence could truly be said to dwell there. If even in the first *beis hamikdash* the *aron* was, in a physical sense, non-present, then the absence of the *aron* in the second temple does not necessarily mean the absence of the Divine. The physicality of the *aron* was never the full point.

### Moshe's Aron Paradox

The *gemara* adds a *tannaitic* source confirming the principle through the parallel case of Moshe's *aron*. The same miraculous non-measurement that applied to Shlomo's *keruvim* applied to Moshe's *aron*.

## Keruvim Configuration

### Keruvim by Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבְנַאי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּרוּבִים – בְּנֵס הֵן עוֹמְדִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְחָמֵשׁ אַמּוֹת כְּנַף הַכְּרוּב הָאֶחָת, וְחָמֵשׁ אַמּוֹת כְּנַף הַכְּרוּב הַשֵּׁנִית, עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת מִקְצוֹת כְּנָפָיו וְעַד קְצוֹת כְּנָפָיו״. גּוּפַיְיהוּ הֵיכָא הֲווֹ קָיְימִי? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּנֵס הֵן עוֹמְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbenai says** that **Shmuel says:** The **cherubs stood miraculously** and did not occupy any physical space, **as it is stated: "And five cubits was one wing of the cherub, and five cubits was the second wing of the cherub; ten cubits from the tip of its wings until the tip of its wings"** (I Kings 6:24). Accordingly, the wings of two cherubs, standing side by side, would occupy the entire twenty cubits width of the Sanctuary. But if so, **where,** in what space, **were their bodies standing?** Since their wings alone, which protruded from the sides of cherubs' bodies, occupied twenty cubits, there was no room left in which their bodies could stand. **Rather,** one must **conclude from** the verse that the cherubs **stood miraculously** and did not occupy any physical space.

Following naturally from the preceding discussion of Moshe's *aron*, the *gemara* turns to Shlomo's *keruvim* and the statement of Rav Chana in the name of Shmuel: *keruvim benes hen omdin* — the *keruvim* stood by means of a miracle. The basis for this claim is a *pasuk* that specifies each wing of a *keruv* was five *amot*, and that from wing-tip to wing-tip the span was ten *amot*. With two *keruvim*, that accounts for twenty *amot* of wingspan — the full width of the *kodesh kodashim*. But then *gufaihu heichi havu ka'imi* — where were their bodies? The body of each *keruv* must have occupied some additional space, pushing the total beyond twenty *amot*. The only resolution, according to Shmuel, is that like the *aron*, the bodies of the *keruvim* took up no space — they stood by miracle.

### Abaye: Chicken Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִלְמָא בּוֹלְטִין כְּתַרְנְגוֹלִין הֲווֹ קָיְימִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this** proof: **But perhaps they stood** with their bodies **emerging** beneath their wings, **like chickens,** with their wings protruding above them from the same point in the center of their backs. If so, their bodies would stand beneath their wings and would not occupy any additional space.

Abaye challenged this conclusion, arguing that there is no need to invoke a miracle. *Vedilma boltin kinarda degalia havu ka'imi* — perhaps they were arranged like a chicken, with the body positioned *below* the wings rather than between them. If the *keruvim* held their wings up and outward from the back, with their bodies hanging lower, then the five *amot* of each wing could be measured horizontally from tip to tip without the body interrupting that span at all. The bodies would occupy space at a lower plane, not competing with the wingspan measurement.

### Rava: Non-Overlapping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: וְדִלְמָא זֶה שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד זֶה הֲווֹ קָיְימִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** also **objects to this** proof: **But perhaps they stood** so that **this** one **was not next to that** one and the wings of the two cherubs overlapped, thereby allowing for the additional space occupied by their bodies.

Rava offered a different challenge: *vedilma zeh shelo kineged zeh havu* — perhaps the *keruvim* were not directly facing one another, and their wings did not meet in the middle at all. Rather, each *keruv*, body and all, might have extended eleven *amot* or more, with the wings of one passing slightly beyond the midpoint while those of the other did the same — but running *side by side*, on parallel planes, rather than touching. The bodies would be offset, not occupying the same slice of space.

### Rav Achai: Diagonal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: וְדִלְמָא בַּאֲלַכְסוֹנָא הֲווֹ קָיְימִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov** also **objects to this** proof: **But perhaps they were standing in a diagonal [*ba'alakhsona*]** alignment from one corner of the Holy of Holies to the diagonally opposite corner. In this way there would be enough space for their bodies and their wings.

Rav Achai bar Yaakov proposed yet another reading: *vedilma be'alakhsona havu ka'imi* — perhaps the *keruvim* stood diagonally across the chamber rather than perpendicular to the walls. The *Rashbam* illustrates this with a diagram. Standing at an angle, the effective span of each *keruv* along any given axis would be reduced, allowing both bodies and wings to fit within the twenty *amot* without requiring a miracle.

### Rav Huna: Tapering Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְדִלְמָא בֵּיתָא מֵעִילַּאי רָוַוח!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua** also **objects to this** proof: **But perhaps** the width of twenty cubits stated in the verse refers only to the width at ground level, whereas **the room widened at the top** and was therefore able to accommodate both their wings and the width of the bodies.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua raised a consideration that echoes an earlier discussion in the *gemara*: *vedilma beshmaya a'eil ravcha* — perhaps the airspace expanded at greater heights. The floor of the *kodesh kodashim* measured twenty *amot* across, but perhaps the walls tapered inward as they descended, such that at the height where the wings were spread, the available space was somewhat greater than twenty *amot*, leaving room for the bodies in addition to the full wingspan.

### Rav Papa: Bent Wings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וְדִלְמָא מִיכָּף הֲווֹ כָּיְיפִי יְדַיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** also **objects to this** proof: **But perhaps they were folding their wings** somewhat; since their wings were not fully extended they did not actually fill the full twenty cubits of the Sanctuary.

Rav Papa's challenge: *vedilma michaf havu chafi yadaihu* — perhaps the wings were not spread flat and straight. If the wings curved or bent inward toward the middle, the linear distance from tip to body would be less than five *amot* even though the measurement along the curve of the wing itself reached five *amot*. The stated wingspan would thus be consistent with both wings meeting in the center even with the bodies accounted for.

### Rav Ashi: Crossed Wings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא שַׁלְחוֹפֵי הֲווֹ מְשַׁלְחֲפִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi** also **objects to this** proof: **But perhaps** their wings **crossed over** one another, so that they did not occupy so much space.

Finally, Rav Ashi asked: *vedilma shalchufei havu meshalchefi* — perhaps the wings of the two *keruvim* were crossed or overlapped, positioned on slightly different planes so that they interlaced with one another. Rather than meeting side by side at the midpoint, one pair of wings would be slightly in front of and slightly behind the other, allowing the full spans to fit without the bodies creating any arithmetic problem.

What is striking about this sequence is that the original statement of Shmuel invoked a miracle — the same type of miraculous non-measurement that was readily accepted for the *aron*. Yet when it came to the *keruvim*, a long series of *Amoraim* pressed to find natural explanations before accepting that conclusion. This likely reflects the intuition that the miracle of the *aron* pertains to Moshe's *aron*, and perhaps one should not extend it casually to Shlomo's *keruvim* without exhausting the natural possibilities first.

## Keruvim Orientation

### Facing Each Other or Toward the House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הֵן עוֹמְדִין? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; חַד אָמַר: פְּנֵיהֶם אִישׁ אֶל אָחִיו, וְחַד אָמַר: פְּנֵיהֶם לַבַּיִת. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר פְּנֵיהֶם אִישׁ אֶל אָחִיו, הָא כְּתִיב: ״וּפְנֵיהֶם לַבַּיִת״! לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם, כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § Continuing its focus on the cherubs, the Gemara asks: **How were** the cherubs **standing? Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagree about this. **One says: Their faces** were turned **one toward the other. And one says: Their faces** were turned **toward the House,** i.e., the Sanctuary. The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who says** that **their faces** were turned **one toward the other, isn't it written: "And their faces were toward the House"** (II Chronicles 3:13)? How does he explain the meaning of this verse? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as their faces miraculously changed directions in reflection of the Jewish people's relationship to God. **Here,** when it states that the cherubs faced each other, it was **when the Jewish people do the will of God. There,** the verse that describes that the cherubs faced the Sanctuary and not toward each other, was **when the Jewish people do not do the will of God.**

Having addressed how the *keruvim* fit within the *kodesh kodashim*, the *gemara* turns to the question of how they were oriented. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar dispute the matter. One holds *pneihem ish el achiv* — they faced one another, as described in the *pasuk* in Shemot concerning the *keruvim* atop the *aron*. The other holds *pneihem labayit* — they faced outward toward the main hall of the *beis hamikdash*, toward the *parochet*.

The difficulty is that there are *pesukim* supporting both positions. One could answer that the *keruvim* on the *aron* faced one direction and the larger *keruvim* Shlomo placed standing on the floor of the *kodesh kodashim* faced another. But the *gemara* seeks an understanding that applies consistently to both.

### Temporal Orientation Change

The resolution is a temporal one: *lo kashya, kan bizman she-Yisrael osin retzono shel Makom, kan bizman she-ein Yisrael osin retzono shel Makom* — there is no contradiction, because the orientation changed depending on the spiritual state of Israel. When Israel was fulfilling God's will, the *keruvim* faced one another, conveying intimacy — the intimacy between God and *Bnei Yisrael*, represented by the two figures drawing close. When Israel was not doing God's will, they turned their faces away from one another, like a couple in a dispute who are still living together but giving each other the silent treatment — the relationship persisted but the closeness was suspended.

This also addresses a deeper concern. One might have imagined that one *keruv* represented God and the other represented Israel, and that they therefore faced Israel's direction. But this interpretation is theologically problematic, as it moves perilously close to the notion of a divine image — something the Torah strenuously prohibits. The better understanding is that the two *keruvim* together represent the *relationship* between God and Israel, not the parties separately.

### Angled Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״וּפְנֵיהֶם לַבַּיִת״, הָא כְּתִיב: ״וּפְנֵיהֶם אִישׁ אֶל אָחִיו״! דִּמְצַדְּדִי אַצְדּוֹדֵי – דְּתַנְיָא, אוּנְקְלוֹס הַגֵּר אָמַר: כְּרוּבִים – ״מַעֲשֵׂה צַעֲצֻעִים״ הֵן, וּמְצוֹדְדִים פְּנֵיהֶם כְּתַלְמִיד הַנִּפְטָר מֵרַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** they stood as described in the verse: **"And their faces were toward the House," isn't it written: "With their faces one toward the other"** (Exodus 25:20). How does he explain the meaning of this verse? The Gemara answers: **They were angled sideways** so that they turned both to each other and toward the Sanctuary, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Onkelos the Convert said** that the **cherubs were** of the **form of children,** as the verse states: "And in the Holy of Holies he made two cherubim of the form of children; and they overlaid them with gold" (II Chronicles 3:10), **and their faces were angled sideways** toward the Ark of the Covenant, **like a student taking leave of his teacher.**

According to the opinion that the primary orientation was *pneihem labayit* — facing the *beis hamikdash* — the *gemara* must account for the *pasuk* that says they faced one another. The answer is *demitzaddad tzadudei* — their faces were slightly angled. They were primarily directed outward toward the *beis hamikdash*, but turned slightly toward one another as well.

A *beraita* is cited in which Onkelos Ha-Ger says the *keruvim* were *ma'aseh tza'atzu'im* — made in the manner of children or child-like figures. (The word *keruv* itself may derive from the Aramaic *keravya*, meaning a child or infant, which is why the English word "cherub" evokes a baby-faced image.) The *beraita* continues: *umetzudadim pneihem ketalmid haniftar merabbo* — their faces were angled like a student taking leave of his master. The student walks backward toward the door, still facing his teacher out of respect, yet simultaneously turning slightly toward the exit, ready to run off. So too the *keruvim*: predominantly facing outward toward the *bayit*, but with their faces slightly inclined toward one another.

## Well Access Rights

### Normal Hours Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בּוֹר לִפְנִים מִבֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – נִכְנָס בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁדֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם נִכְנָסִין, וְיוֹצֵא בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁדֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם יוֹצְאִין. וְאֵינוֹ מַכְנִיס בְּהֶמְתּוֹ וּמַשְׁקָהּ מִבּוֹרוֹ, אֶלָּא מְמַלֵּא וּמַשְׁקָהּ מִבַּחוּץ. וְזֶה עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ פּוֹתַחַת, וְזֶה עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ פּוֹתַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who has** ownership of **a cistern** located **beyond the house of another,** i.e., the cistern can be accessed only by entering the property of the other, and also has access rights to that cistern, **may enter** the house to access his cistern only **at a time when it is usual for people to enter, and may leave** only **at a time when it is usual for people to leave. And** in addition, **he may not bring his animal** into the house **and water** it **from his cistern; rather, he** must **fill** a pail with water from the cistern **and water** his animal **outside. And this** one, the owner of the cistern, **constructs for himself a lock** on the entrance to the cistern to prevent the homeowner from drawing water from it, **and that** one, the homeowner, **constructs for himself a lock.**

The *gemara* now returns to what had been an earlier thematic concern — the rules governing access rights when property is situated within or behind someone else's property. The *mishnah* addresses the case of *mi sheyesh lo bor lifnim mibeiso shel chavero* — one who owns a well that is located inside another person's house. The *Rashbam* understands *lifnim* here to mean genuinely within the house, not merely behind it, though the practical point is that to reach the well one must pass through the other person's home.

In such a case, the well-owner's access rights are bounded: *nichnas besha'ah shederech benei adam nichnasim, veyotzei besha'ah shederech benei adam yotzim* — he may enter and exit only during the normal hours when people ordinarily come and go. This is not simply a matter of when people are awake; it is a matter of when it would be socially acceptable to receive a guest. Entering very early in the morning or late at night, when the household would not normally receive visitors, is not within his rights.

### Animal Restriction

Furthermore, *ve'eino machnis behemto umashkeh miboro* — he may not bring his animals into the house to drink directly from the well. His rights of access are real but constrained by the nature of the space. *Ella memaleh umashkeh mibachutz* — he must draw the water himself and bring it outside to his animals. He is entering another person's home, and that basic fact shapes the extent of what his access rights entail.

### Dual Locks

The *mishnah* adds a notable procedural detail: *vezeh oseh lo posachas vezeh oseh lo posachas* — there are two locks on the well, one belonging to the homeowner and one belonging to the well-owner. The well-owner's lock is readily understood: he needs to protect his water from being used by the homeowner without authorization. The homeowner's lock, however, requires explanation. One might initially read the *mishnah* as meaning that the homeowner's lock is on the front door, preventing the well-owner from entering at unauthorized times. But the *gemara* clarifies — citing Rabbi Yochanan — that *sheneihem labor*, both locks are on the well itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פּוֹתַחַת לְהֵיכָא? אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנֵיהֶם לַבּוֹר. בִּשְׁלָמָא בַּעַל הַבּוֹר, בָּעֵי לְאִשְׁתַּמּוֹרֵי מַיָּא דְּבוֹרֵיהּ; אֶלָּא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – לְמָה לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִשּׁוּם חֲשַׁד אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the owner of the cistern and the homeowner each construct a lock. The Gemara asks: **A lock to where? Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Both of them** construct a lock on the opening **to the cistern** to prevent the other from accessing it unilaterally. The Gemara asks: **Granted, the owner of the cistern** constructs a lock, as **he wants to protect the water of his well. But why** does **the homeowner** construct a lock? **Rabbi Elazar said: Due to a suspicion** that the owner of the cistern might enter the house at a time when the owner of the house is not present, and thereby be secluded together in the house with the homeowner's **wife.**

### Wife's Privacy Lock

The *gemara* then asks why the homeowner needs his own lock on the well, and Rabbi Elazar answers: *mishum chashad ishto* — because of suspicion regarding his wife. The concern is not merely one of general privacy or preventing overuse of an access right; it is specifically that allowing someone a standing license to enter the house whenever he wishes creates an uncomfortable situation when the homeowner is not present and his wife is home alone. The homeowner's lock ensures that the well-owner cannot enter without the homeowner's active participation — a structural safeguard against any impropriety or even the appearance of it.

## Garden Access Rights

### Garden Hours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גִּינָּה לִפְנִים מִגִּינָּתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ – נִכְנָס בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁדֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם נִכְנָסִים, וְיוֹצֵא בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁדֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם יוֹצְאִין; וְאֵינוֹ מַכְנִיס לְתוֹכָהּ תַּגָּרִין; וְלֹא יִכָּנֵס מִתּוֹכָהּ לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֶה אַחֶרֶת; וְהַחִיצוֹן זוֹרֵעַ אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who has** ownership of **a garden** located **beyond the garden of another,** and also has access rights to it, **may enter** his garden only **at a time when it is usual for people to enter, and may leave** only **at a time when it is usual for people to leave.** Furthermore, he **may not bring merchants into** his garden, **and he may not** enter the garden solely in order to use it as a passageway, to **enter from it into another field. And the** owner of the **outer** garden **may sow the path** leading to the inner garden.

A parallel *mishnah* addresses the case of *mi sheyesh lo ginah lifnim miginato shel chavero* — one who has a garden situated behind another person's garden, with access rights through that outer garden. The same time restriction applies: *nichnas besha'ah shebnei adam nichnasin, veyotzei besha'ah shebnei adam yotzin* — entry and exit are restricted to normal hours when people ordinarily come and go.

### No Commercial Use

The *mishnah* adds: *ve'eino machnis letochah tagrin* — he may not bring merchants through the outer garden to conduct business in his own. His access right is personal. If, for example, he wishes to sell the produce of his inner garden, he cannot bring a buyer through the outer garden for that purpose. The right of passage was granted for his own use, not as a commercial thoroughfare.

### No Shortcuts

Nor may he use the path as a shortcut to other destinations: *velo yikanes mitochah letoch sadeh acheret* — he may not pass through the outer garden and continue on to a third property. The access right exists solely for the purpose of reaching his own garden. One could theoretically disguise such a shortcut by pretending to visit the garden before moving on, but the *mishnah* is addressing honest conduct: the right is bounded by its purpose.

### Planting Rights

The *mishnah* then rules: *vehachitzon zorei'a et haderech* — the outer garden owner may plant along the path. Because the path running through the outer garden has not been transferred to the inner garden owner — he merely has access rights, not ownership — the outer garden owner retains full ownership of that strip of land and may plant it. The inner garden owner can walk through the planted area, perhaps trampling what grows there, but he cannot prevent the planting.

### Side Path Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתְנוּ לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מִן הַצַּד מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶן – נִכְנָס בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה, וְיוֹצֵא בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁרוֹצֶה; וּמַכְנִיס לְתוֹכָהּ תַּגָּרִין. וְלֹא יִכָּנֵס מִתּוֹכָהּ לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֶה אַחֶרֶת. זֶה וָזֶה אֵינָן רַשָּׁאִים לְזוֹרְעָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If the court **gave him** an access **path from the side** of the outer garden, **with the agreement of both of them,** he **may enter at** any **time he wants, and leave at** any **time he wants, and may bring merchants into** the inner garden. **But** he **may** still **not** enter the garden solely in order to **enter from it into another field.** In such a case, **neither this** one, the owner of the inner garden, **nor that** one, the owner of the outer garden, **is permitted to plant** that side path.

A different arrangement is addressed next: *natnu lo derech min hatzad mida'at sheneihem* — where by mutual agreement, a side path was designated rather than a straight cut through the middle of the garden. In this case, since both parties agreed and the access was not simply imposed, the inner garden owner acquires a stronger sense of right in that path. Consequently, *nichnas besha'ah shehu rotzeh, veyotzei besha'ah shehu rotzeh* — he may enter and exit at whatever time he wishes, including in the middle of the night, since he is using the side path and not cutting through the outer garden.

He may also bring merchants through this path: *umachnis letochah tagrin*. His greater degree of right, born from the bilateral nature of the agreement, extends his entitlements in this respect. However, one restriction remains unchanged: *velo yikanes mitochah letoch sadeh acheret* — he still may not use the path as a shortcut to other properties. The path was agreed upon for his garden's sake, and its purpose remains bounded accordingly.

As for planting: *vezeh vezeh einam resha'im lezor'ah* — in the case of a side path established by agreement, neither party may plant it. The inner garden owner cannot plant it because it is still not his; the outer garden owner cannot plant it because the inner garden owner has a sufficiently firm right of access that planting would interfere with it. The side path sits in a middle category — more stable than a mere imposed access right, but still not full ownership.

## Irrigation Canal Rights

### Canal Allotments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״אַמָּה בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת לְתוֹכָהּ, וְאַמָּה מִכָּאן וְאַמָּה מִכָּאן לַאֲגַפֶּיהָ. ״אַמָּה בֵּית הַקִּילוֹן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אַמָּה אַחַת לְתוֹכָהּ, וַחֲצִי אַמָּה מִכָּאן וַחֲצִי אַמָּה מִכָּאן לַאֲגַפֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** If a field owner says to another: **I am selling you** from my land a water **channel** fit **for** bringing water to **an irrigated field,** he is required to **give him** land **two cubits** wide **for the inside** of the channel, **and** one **cubit on this** side **and** one **cubit on that** side **for its banks.** If he said to him: **I am selling you a shadoof [*kilon*] channel,** he is required to **give him** land **one cubit** wide **for the inside** of the channel **and half a cubit on this** side **and half a cubit on that** side **for its banks.**

The *gemara* now introduces a series of related cases taught by Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel, continuing the theme of access rights. The first case: *amah bat hashlachin* — an irrigation canal, so called because it is approximately one *amah* deep and is used for irrigating a *beis hashlachim*, a field requiring intensive watering. When one sells another the right to such a canal, the seller gives: *noten lo beis amos letocha, ve'ama mikan ve'ama mikan le'agaseha* — a grant of two *amot* and an *amah* on each side for the *agafim*, the banks.

There is a question of how to parse this formulation. One reading takes it as listing two separate grants: the two *amot* are for potential expansion of the canal, and the additional *amah* on each side is for access — standing space to allow the buyer to dig or maintain it. A second reading, preferred by the *Rashbam*, takes the phrase as a single statement: two *amot* total, meaning one *amah* on each side, designated for access, parallel to the garden case. Under this reading, the emphasis is on access rather than expansion. Either way, these grants do not necessarily constitute a transfer of ownership; they represent rights.

### Non-Irrigation Use

The case changes when the sale is *amah beis hakillon* — a channel used not for field irrigation but for other purposes, such as watering animals or washing clothes. (Some texts read *beis hasillon*, from a Greek word related to a type of pump or conduit.) For this lesser use, the allotment is reduced: *noten lo amah achas letocha, vechatzi amah mikan vechatzi amah mikan le'agaseha* — one *amah* for the channel itself, and half an *amah* on each side for the banks. The precise correlation between the intended use of the water and the required standing space alongside it may not be immediately obvious, but presumably the scale of the enterprise — the equipment used, the volume drawn — corresponds to the lateral space needed for maintenance and access.

### Edge Planting Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹתָן אֲגַפַּיִים – מִי זוֹרְעָם? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה זוֹרְעָם, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה נוֹטְעָם. מַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹרְעָם – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן נוֹטְעָם. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר נוֹטְעָם – אֲבָל זוֹרְעָם לָא, חַלְחוֹלֵי מְחַלְחֲלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **those banks, who** has permission to **sow them? Rav Yehuda** says that **Shmuel says: The field owner may sow them** with vegetables or crops. **Rav Naḥman** says that **Shmuel says: The field owner may plant them** with trees. The Gemara elaborates: **The one who says** that the field owner **may sow them** holds that **all the more so he may plant them** with trees. **And the one who says** that **he may plant them** with trees holds that he may only plant trees, **but sowing them** with other plants is **not** permitted. This is because the roots **perforate** the ground, which weakens it and can cause damage to the water channel.

The *gemara* then asks about the banks themselves: *otzon agaseha nizra'im* — may these *agafim* be planted? The question recalls the garden case, where the outer owner could plant the path because it remained his property. Here, the situation is somewhat different because the *agafim* are directly contiguous with the canal and were specifically allocated in connection with the sale.

Two opinions are reported, both in the name of Shmuel, attributed to Rav Yehudah and Rav Nachman respectively. One says *baal hasadeh zoraam* — the field owner may plant seeds, i.e., vegetables, there. The other says *baal hasadeh notaam* — the field owner may plant trees. The relationship between these two opinions is not simply a matter of one permitting more than the other. In fact, the *gemara* treats planting vegetables as the more striking ruling, because while planting trees (*nota*) obviously implies planting seeds (*zore'a*) is permitted as well, the reverse is not true.

The reason is *chalchulei mechalchali* — vegetables planted at the edge of a canal undermine and loosen the banks. The root systems and moisture-drawing behavior of vegetables destabilizes the soil at the canal's edge, potentially causing the banks to collapse into the canal. This would harm both the canal's integrity and the buyer's standing space alongside it. Trees, by contrast, are held to send their roots downward rather than horizontally through the bank in the same destabilizing fashion. Therefore, the opinion that permits only trees — not vegetables — is the more protective one for the canal buyer, while the opinion permitting vegetables is the more expansive grant to the field owner.

### Bank Collapse Repair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַמַּת הַמַּיִם שֶׁכָּלוּ אֲגַפֶּיהָ – מְתַקְּנָהּ מֵאוֹתָה שָׂדֶה; בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ אֲגַפֶּיהָ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **a water channel whose banks collapsed,** the owner of the channel may **repair it** with earth **from that field** through which the channel runs, even though the field does not belong to him. It is permitted because **it is known that** when **its banks collapsed** the earth that the banks were made from spread **only into that** surrounding **field.**

A further *halakha* of Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel: *amat hamayim shekalu agapeha* — if the banks of a water channel have actually collapsed, *metaknah me'osah sadeh* — he repairs them using dirt from that same field. The rationale offered is *beyadua shelo kalu agapeha ela be'osah sadeh* — it is known that the banks did not collapse except into that field; the dirt that formed the banks found its way into the field owner's land when they fell.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא, וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: מַיָּיךְ אַשְׁפְּלוּהָ לְאַרְעָיךְ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן קִבֵּל עָלָיו בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: But let** the field owner **say to** the owner of the channel: **Your water** in your channel **carried away your earth** from your banks, so you have no right to take earth from my field. **Rather, Rav Pappa said** he may repair the banks with earth from the field **because** when **the field owner** sold the rights to the channel **he accepted that condition upon himself.**

Rav Pappa challenged this: *veleima lei maya'ach ashpluha le'ara'ai* — let the field owner say, "Your water caused them to collapse into your canal; the dirt is at the bottom of the canal, not in my field." Rav Pappa therefore reformulates the basis for the ruling: *she'al menat kein kibeil alav baal hasadeh* — the field owner accepted this arrangement implicitly when the original transaction was made. Just as certain access rights are implied by the nature of the transaction even when not explicitly stated, so too is the right to draw upon the surrounding field for dirt to rebuild collapsed banks understood to be part of what the field owner took on when he sold or granted the canal rights.

## Public Road Transfer

### Road Relocation Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיְתָה דֶּרֶךְ הָרַבִּים עוֹבֶרֶת לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ; נְטָלָהּ וְנָתַן לָהֶם מִן הַצַּד – מַה שֶּׁנָּתַן נָתַן, וְשֶׁלּוֹ לֹא הִגִּיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who had a public thoroughfare passing through his field,** and he **appropriated it and** instead **gave** the public an alternative thoroughfare **on the side** of his property, the *halakha* is that the thoroughfare **that he gave** them, **he gave** them, and they may use it. **But** the original thoroughfare that he took **for himself has not reached him,** i.e., he cannot appropriate it for his personal use.

The *gemara* now turns to a *mishnah* that introduces a new but thematically related scenario: *mi she'hayetah derech harabbim overet betochah* — one who has a public road running through his field. Imagine a large agricultural field bisected by a public thoroughfare, making efficient farming difficult. The field owner decides to relocate the road: *natal venasan lahem min hatzad* — he takes back the middle portion and assigns the public a path along the edge of his field instead.

The *mishnah*'s ruling is stark: *mah shenasan nasan, veshello lo higi'a* — what he gave them, he gave; and what was his did not come back to him. The field owner loses on all counts. He does not recover the original road through the middle, and the public retains the new road he offered along the edge. This outcome is clearly not the one the field owner intended, and it demands explanation. Either the transaction was legitimate — in which case, why can't he reclaim the old road he was supposedly taking back? — or it was illegitimate — in which case, why does the public keep the new road?

An important preliminary question is who, in fact, has the authority to enter into such an arrangement on behalf of the *rabbim*. The *Rashbam* notes that the *gemara* initially considers this to be permissible on the basis of *kofin al midas Sedom* — the principle that where one party gains and the other suffers no loss, one can compel cooperation. Since the field owner would argue that the public is not harmed by receiving one equivalent road in place of another, perhaps the transaction can be conducted unilaterally, without formal representation of the other side. However, this analogy to *midas Sedom* is imperfect, as a classic case of *zeh neheneh vezeh lo chaser* involves no exchange at all — here something is given and something is taken.

### Path Width Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דֶּרֶךְ הַיָּחִיד – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. דֶּרֶךְ הָרַבִּים – שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה. דֶּרֶךְ הַמֶּלֶךְ – אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר. דֶּרֶךְ הַקֶּבֶר – אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר. הַמַּעֲמָד – דַּיָּינֵי צִפּוֹרִי אָמְרוּ: בֵּית אַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The standard width of **a private path** is **four cubits.** If a field owner sells the right to pass through his field to an individual, without specifying the width of the path, he must provide him with a path four cubits wide. The standard width of **a public thoroughfare** is **sixteen cubits.** The width of **a king's thoroughfare has no** maximum **measure,** as the king may appropriate whatever width thoroughfare he wishes. The width of the **path for** the burial procession to **a grave has no** maximum **measure.** With regard to **the** practice of **standing** and comforting the mourners following a funeral, **the judges of Tzippori said** that the standard requisite size is the **area required for sowing four *kav* of seed.**

Before turning to the analysis of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* records several standard measurements. *Derech hayachid arba amot* — a private path must be four *amot* wide, enough to accommodate a wagon and allow individual passage. *Derech harabbim shesh esrei amah* — a public road requires sixteen *amot*. *Derech hamelech ein lah shiur* — the King's Highway has no defined limit. If the king and his forces are passing through, they may proceed by whatever route is most direct and convenient for them, and no private claim of property stands as an obstacle.

### Burial Road Rights

*Derech hakever ein lah shiur* — similarly, a burial road has no fixed limit. The *Rashbam* offers two interpretations of this rule. The first is that when a funeral procession must pass through a town or a field on the way to a grave, they have the right to proceed along whatever route is necessary, analogous to the King's Highway — though unlike the king's forces, they could not knock down a structure; they could only traverse open ground such as fields or grass. The second interpretation is more specific: this is the case of someone who has sold another the right of a *derech hakever* — a burial road — to access a family cave or family burial plot. In that case, once the right is sold, there is no limit on how many people may use the path simultaneously, or on its effective width when the entire family processes through.

### Mourning Space

The *mishnah* adds a ruling from *dayanei Tzipori* — the judges of Tzipori — regarding a related matter: *beit arba kavin*. Since a *se'ah* is six *kavin* and a *beit se'ah* is fifty by fifty *amot*, four *kavin* represents two-thirds of that area. The judges of Tzipori specified this as the required standing space for those who come to greet the mourners after a burial — the area needed to accommodate the crowd gathering to offer condolences and pay their respects.

## Road Transfer Analysis

### Illegitimate Transaction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי שֶׁלּוֹ לֹא הִגִּיעוֹ? לִינְקוֹט פַּזְרָא וְלִיתֵּיב! שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ לָא עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם פְּסֵידָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why** does the mishna rule that the public thoroughfare that the landowner took **for himself has not reached him?** If by making the exchange the original thoroughfare now belongs to him, **let him take a stick [*pazra*] and sit** on the thoroughfare and physically prevent anyone from passing through. Apparently, the Sages did not permit him to do so. The Gemara suggests: Does it follow that one can **conclude from** the mishna's ruling that **a person may not execute judgment for himself even in circumstances** where refraining from acting will cause him **a loss?** This would contradict the accepted *halakha* that one may do so.

The *gemara* now addresses the core analysis of the *mishnah*'s ruling. Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav asks: if the original transaction gave the field owner rights to reclaim the old road, why can't he simply take a pitchfork, station himself at the entrance to the old path, and prevent people from using it? After all, *lo avid inish dina lenafshei* — one may not take the law into one's own hands — but that principle typically yields when a person is protecting his own property from active loss. A property owner defending what is his from ongoing trespass should be entitled to enforce his right. The fact that this is apparently not possible suggests something more fundamental: the transaction itself was not valid.

### Gezeirah: Twisted Roads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִתֵּן לָהֶן דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן. רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן לָהֶם דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava:** Although generally one may do so, in this case the Sages issued **a decree** prohibiting it, **lest he give them a circuitous route** that will lengthen the distance the public will have to travel. **Rav Mesharshiyya said in the name of Rava** that the ruling of the mishna applies only **where he** actually **gives them a circuitous route** instead of the original straight thoroughfare. But one may exchange a public thoroughfare for an equally straight thoroughfare, appropriating the original for his personal use.

Rav Zevid in the name of Rava explains that the transaction is indeed illegitimate, and the reason is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic enactment: *gezeirah shema yithen lahem derech akalton* — lest one give the public a twisted, roundabout road. Because there is no one to represent the *rabbim* in negotiating this exchange, the field owner's claim that the new road is equivalent to the old one cannot be verified or challenged. A unilateral arrangement could easily be abused: the field owner offers a path that is technically a road but is in practice inconvenient, circuitous, or poorly situated. To prevent this abuse, the rabbis invalidated all such transactions categorically.

Rav Mesharshiya also in the name of Rava offers a superficially similar formulation but with a meaningfully different position: he is actually describing a case where the owner *did* give a twisted road. Under his reading, a genuinely equivalent road could be validly exchanged — the *mishnah* is simply discussing a case where the road offered was not in fact equivalent, and therefore the transaction fails. This position is more permissive than Rav Zevid's across the board invalidation.

### Rav Ashi: Any Change Harmful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כׇּל מִן הַצַּד – דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן הִיא; קְרוֹבָה לָזֶה, וּרְחוֹקָה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Any** alternative path **on the side** of the original path **is** considered **a circuitous route,** as it is **close for this** person **and it is far for that** person. While some will benefit from the change, it will be detrimental to others. Therefore, one may never exchange a public path for an alternative path.

Rav Ashi states a more sweeping principle: *kol min hatzad derech akalton hi* — any side road is, by definition, a twisted road for someone. *Kerova lizeh verachoka lizeh* — what is close for one person is far for another. Any change in the routing of a public road inevitably benefits certain travelers and inconveniences others. The people who used to live along the original road now have a longer walk; the people who happen to live near the new road benefit. Since no change can ever be neutral for all members of the public, and since the public has no formal representative in the negotiation, any such unilateral transaction must be considered illegitimate. The field owner simply lacks the authority to impose a road change on a community of diverse interests.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Path Selection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: שִׁקְלוּ דִּידְכוּ וְהַבוּ לִי דִּידִי! הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: רַבִּים שֶׁבֵּרְרוּ דֶּרֶךְ לְעַצְמָם, מַה שֶּׁבֵּרְרוּ בֵּרְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a field owner provides an alternative thoroughfare through his field for the public to use, the public may use both thoroughfares. The Gemara suggests: **But let him say to them: Take your** original thoroughfare back **and give me my** thoroughfare that I provided you. The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** If the **public selected a thoroughfare** through a privately owned field **for themselves** even without gaining the permission of the field owner, **that which they selected, they selected,** and they have the right to use it.

Given that the transaction was not valid, the *gemara* asks: the field owner should be able to recover his old road — *shaklu didchu vehavu li didi*, take back yours and give me mine. Why does the *mishnah* rule that he does not get it back?

The *gemara* answers with a *tannaitic* source: *hamani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this follows the position of Rabbi Eliezer. As taught in a *beraita*, Rabbi Yehudah says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *rabbim shebereru lahem derech le'atzmam, mah shebereru bereru* — if the public selects a path for themselves, whatever they have selected is selected and is theirs. The *gemara* immediately asks: *rabbim gazlanin ninhu* — are the *rabbim* simply thieves, empowered to appropriate whatever path they walk through? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – רַבִּים גַּזְלָנִים נִינְהוּ?! אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָבְדָה לָהֶן דֶּרֶךְ בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, are** the members of the **public** entitled to be **robbers?** Why should they be permitted to appropriate land from a private owner? **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** Rabbi Eliezer refers only to a case **where** the public **lost a thoroughfare in that field,** e.g., the field was plowed over and the original course of the thoroughfare is not known. In such a case, the public has the right to determine the course anew.

Rav Giddel in the name of Rav clarifies: this applies specifically to a case where the *rabbim* had a path through a field that was lost — perhaps washed away by rain or otherwise obliterated — and they now need to reconstitute it. Since they have an underlying right to *some* path through that field, whichever route they now choose becomes their path. The word *bereru* — they selected — makes sense in this context: they already had a right; they are now exercising that right by selecting among the available options.

### Unresolved Question

The *gemara* then applies this to our case: since the field owner offered the side road, even though the original transaction was invalid, the act of offering is treated as analogous to the *rabbim* reconstituting a lost path, or at least as triggering their right to select a path. But this analogy is strained. In Rabbi Eliezer's case, the *rabbim* had an existing underlying right and simply needed to select among available routes. In our case, the original road still exists — it has not been lost — and the field owner is trying to reclaim it. Why should the offer of a substitute road, made in the context of an illegitimate transaction, confer upon the *rabbim* rights to both roads?

The *gemara* leaves this question open, and the daf concludes with this unresolved tension: the transaction was invalid, the field owner logically should recover his original road, and yet the ruling of the *mishnah* — that he does not — remains in need of a fully satisfying justification.
