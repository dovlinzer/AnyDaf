# Niddah 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Birth Pains & Zavah

### Intro: Birth Pains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַמְקַשָּׁה — נִדָּה. קִשְּׁתָה שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים בְּתוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם, וְשָׁפְתָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, וְיָלְדָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לַיְלָה וָיוֹם כְּלִילֵי שַׁבָּת וְיוֹמוֹ, שֶׁשָּׁפְתָה מִן הַצַּעַר וְלֹא מִן הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a pregnant woman **who experiences** labor **pains,** and they are accompanied by an emission of blood**,** her status is that of **a menstruating woman.** If **she experienced** these **pains** accompanied by emissions of blood for **three** consecutive **days within the eleven days** between periods of menstruation**,** during which time emissions of blood render a woman a *zava*, **and she rested** from labor **for** a twenty-four-hour **period, i.e.,** the pangs subsided**, and** she then **gave birth,** it indicates that the emissions were not due to her imminent labor**,** and **this** woman **is** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** She is considered a *zava* only if the pangs subsided for a twenty-four-hour period of **a night and** the following **day, like Shabbat evening and its** accompanying **day.** Additionally, she is considered a *zava* in a case **where she rested from the pain** of labor **but not** necessarily **from the** flow of **blood.**

We pick up at the bottom of 36b, dealing with the *halacha* that bleeding resulting from birth pains does not render a woman a *zavah*. It may render her a *niddah*, but not a *zavah*. The *gemara* had already established a *drasha* from the *pasuk* describing the *zavah*: the Torah uses the phrase *zov damah* — her blood — to exclude blood that results from a fetus. A woman who bleeds as a consequence of birth pains cannot thereby become a *zavah*.

### Niddah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ כׇּל הַמְקַשָּׁה נִדָּה הִיא? אָמַר רַב: נִדָּה לְיוֹמָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תִּשְׁפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that a pregnant woman who experiences an emission of menstrual blood due to labor pains is considered a menstruating woman. This statement apparently applies to all blood due to labor. The Gemara therefore asks: **Is this to say that any** woman **who experiences** an emission due to labor **pains is a menstruating woman?** But if a woman experiences an emission due to labor pains in the eleven days of *ziva*, she is not rendered a *zava*, nor can this render her a menstruating woman. **Rav said:** It is correct that a woman who experiences an emission of blood due to labor during the eleven days of *ziva* is not rendered a *zava*, or even a lesser *zava*, who must observe one clean day for the day she experienced an emission. But she is **a menstruating woman for** one **day,** i.e., she is prohibited to her husband on the day of the emission, and in the evening she may immerse and become permitted to him. **And Shmuel said:** It is a rabbinic decree that she must observe a clean day, as **we are concerned lest she rest** from labor and thereby be rendered a lesser *zava*, as the cessation of pangs would indicate that her emission was not due to her imminent labor.

The *gemara* then raises an obvious challenge: if the same word *zovah* — "her flow" — appears in the context of *niddah* as well, should we not apply the same exclusion there? That is, perhaps a woman cannot become a *niddah* either if the blood results from the fetus. One might reason that *zovah machmas atzmah velo machmas vlad* — her flow must come from herself, not from the fetus — and apply this equally to *niddah*. The *gemara* counters: when the Torah elsewhere says simply *ishah ki tihiyeh zavah*, "if a woman flows," that broader phrase implies that even an external stimulus (*ones*) creates *tumah*. Thus the specific phrase *zovah* — her flow — excluding fetal blood applies only where we have first established that *ones*-induced bleeding does not count, namely by *zavah*, not by *niddah*. The question thus remains: how do we know the exclusion is limited to *zavah* and does not extend to *niddah*?

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֲמַר קְרָא ״תֵּשֵׁב״, יֵשׁ לְךָ יְשִׁיבָה אַחֶרֶת שֶׁהִיא כָּזוֹ, וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? קוֹשִׁי בִּימֵי זִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** The **verse states** with regard to the days of purity after childbirth: **"She shall observe** the blood of purity" (Leviticus 12:5). This indicates that **you have another** instance of an **observance that is like this one,** i.e., where the woman is not rendered impure by an emission of blood. **And which** is **this** other case? **This** is the case of blood that is emitted due to labor **pains during** the eleven **days of *ziva*.**

Reish Lakish offers a proof from a *pasuk* regarding the *yoledet*: *veshishim yom vesheshet yamim teshev al demei taharah* — she shall sit upon blood of purity. The verse teaches that a woman who gives birth has a period during which her blood is *tahor*. Reish Lakish argues: this tells us there is another analogous period of *taharah* — namely, bleeding during the *zivah* days that accompanies birth pains. However, as the *gemara* itself notes, this does not fully resolve the question, since one could equally read the *pasuk* as implying that bleeding during *niddah* days associated with birth pains is also *tahor*. The verse establishes the principle that birth-related blood can be *tahor*, but does not itself specify *zivah* over *niddah*.

### Abuh of Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: אָמַר קְרָא ״וּטְמֵאָה שְׁבֻעַיִם כְּנִדָּתָהּ״ וְלֹא כְּזִיבָתָהּ, מִכְּלָל דְּזִיבָתָהּ טָהוֹר, וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ — קוֹשִׁי בִּימֵי זִיבָה. וְהַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״וְטָמְאָה שְׁבוּעַיִם כְּנִדָּתָהּ״, ״דָּמָהּ״ לְמָה לִי? אִי לָאו ״דָּמָהּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא כְּנִדָּתָהּ וְלֹא כְּזִיבָתָהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשׁוֹפִי. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Shmuel's father said** the **verse states: "And she shall be impure two weeks, as in her menstruation"** (Leviticus 12:5), **and not: As in her *ziva*. By inference,** one may conclude **that** there is an instance in which an emission of blood that occurs during **her** days of ***ziva*** is **pure. And which** case is **this? This** is the case of blood that is emitted due to labor **pains during** the eleven **days of *ziva*. And now that it is written: "And she shall be impure two weeks as in her menstruation," why do I** need the term **"her blood"** (Leviticus 15:25) from which it is likewise derived that blood emitted due to labor pains during the eleven days of *ziva* is pure? The Gemara answers: **If not** for the term **"her blood," I would say** the verse is teaching that a woman who gives birth is impure **as in her menstruation, and not as in her *ziva*, and** therefore, **even** if she experienced bleeding during her days of *ziva* **in** a state of **resting** from labor pains, she remains pure. Consequently, the term "her blood" **teaches us** that this *halakha* applies only if she experiences bleeding due to labor pains.

The *gemara* therefore abandons the previous approaches and turns to the answer of Abuh of Shmuel. The Torah states, regarding a woman who gives birth to a girl: *vetama shevuayim kenidatah* — she shall be impure two weeks *as a niddah*, and not *as a zavah*. The question is: why does the Torah draw the comparison to *niddah* rather than *zavah*? Even granting that there are differences in the details of their respective *tumos*, the very act of comparison implies something meaningful — that in certain respects the *yoledet* is like a *niddah* and unlike a *zavah*. This yields the inference *michlal dezivatah tahor*: there is a context in which *zivah* blood is *tahor*, namely birth pains during the *zivah* period. Hence birth pains during the *zivah* period do not create *zavah* status.

### Her Flow Principle

The *gemara* then asks: given that we derive the ruling from *kenidatah*, what do we do with the verse of *damah* — her blood — the phrase that had earlier seemed to be the source? The answer is that without *damah*, one might have thought that *zavah* is excluded entirely during pregnancy — that any bleeding whatsoever during pregnancy would never create *zavah* status. This would not be unreasonable: the model of *zavah*, as the Rambam makes clear, is analogous to the *zov* of a man, an unnatural, pathological emission. Pregnancy involves considerable bleeding and spotting, and one might argue that no bleeding during pregnancy can ever qualify as the unnatural flow of a *zavah*. The verse *damah* therefore comes to limit the exclusion: only blood that is directly attributable to the fetus — meaning blood that occurs simultaneously with the birth pains — is excluded from *zavah* status. Bleeding during pregnancy that is not concurrent with birth pains can still render a woman a *zavah*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁילָא בַּר אֲבִינָא עֲבַד עוֹבָדָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב. כִּי קָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב אַסִּי: זִיל צַנְּעֵיהּ, וְאִי לָא צָיֵית — גָּרְיֵיהּ. הוּא סָבַר ״גַּדְּיֵיהּ״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rav, a woman who emits blood during the eleven days of *ziva* due to labor pains is considered like a menstruating woman for that day, i.e., she is prohibited to her husband until she immerses in the evening. The Gemara relates that **Sheila bar Avina ruled** in an actual **case in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav.** The Gemara further relates that **when Rav was dying, he said to Rav Asi: Go** and **hide** this *halakha*, i.e., inform Sheila bar Avina that my ruling is incorrect. **And if he does not listen** to you, **drag him [*garyei*]** to your side with convincing claims. Rav Asi misheard and **thought** that Rav **said to him *gadyei*,** excommunicate him.

Having established that birth-pain bleeding is only excluded from *zavah* status when the pains continue uninterrupted up to the birth, the *gemara* turns to the practical question: what is the *halacha* when a woman bleeds together with birth pains and we do not yet know whether the pains will continue or whether there will be a break? Since the exemption depends on continuity of pains through to the birth, a break would retroactively make the bleeding count toward *zavah* status. May she assume she remains *tehorah*, or must she be stringent?

### Rav's Position

Rav and Shmuel disagree on this point. Rav holds that she may essentially assume she is *tehorah* — she should avoid relations that day, but that night she is already permitted, without need to wait to see whether the pains will eventually break. Shmuel holds that she must assume she is *temei'ah* and treat the bleeding as potentially significant.

### Shila's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב, אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֲדַר בָּךְ״, דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ רַב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם אִיתָא דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ, לְדִידִי הֲוָה אָמַר לִי. לָא צָיֵית גַּדְּיֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא מִסְתְּפֵי מָר מִדְּלֵיקְתָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה, דְּהוּא אִיסִי בֶּן גּוּר אַרְיֵה, דְּהוּא אִיסִי בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּהוּא אִיסִי בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל — אֲסִיתָא דִּנְחָשָׁא דְּלָא שָׁלֵיט בֵּיהּ רִקְבָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וַאֲנָא שֵׁילָא בַּר אֲבִינָא — בּוּכְנָא דְּפַרְזְלָא דִּמְתַבַּר אֲסִיתָא דִּנְחָשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **After Rav died,** Rav Asi went to Sheila bar Avina and **said to him: Retract your** ruling, **as Rav** himself **retracted his** opinion. Sheila bar Avina **said to** Rav Asi: **If it is so, that he retracted his** opinion, **he would have told me,** as I was his student; he would not have sent a messenger to inform me of such a retraction. Consequently, Sheila bar Avina **did not listen** to Rav Asi. Rav Asi **excommunicated him,** as he thought that this was Rav's instruction. Sheila bar Avina **said to** Rav Asi: **But is the Master not afraid of the fire,** i.e., of a punishment for offending me? Rav Asi **said to him,** linking in a fanciful manner his own name to the various names of an important *tanna*: **I am Isi ben Yehuda, who is Isi ben Gur Arye, who is Isi ben Gamliel, who is Isi ben Mahalalel,** and this name alludes to **a copper mortar [*asita*], over which rot has no power,** i.e., no harm will befall me. Sheila bar Avina **said to** Rav Asi: **And I am Sheila bar Avina,** and my father's name alludes to **an iron pestle [*bukhna*], which smashes a copper mortar,** i.e., harm may befall you through the merit of my ancestors.

Shila bar Avina ruled in practice like Rav. A woman had one day of bleeding accompanying birth pains, and he permitted her to her husband that night, relying on Rav's lenient position.

### Deathbed Reversal

When Rav was dying, he said to Rav Asi — apparently present or nearby — *tzanyei*: conceal what I said, or, in Rashi's reading, tell Shila to reverse himself. Rav was retracting his ruling. Rashi understands *tzanyei* to mean Rav was telling Rav Asi to instruct Shila to reverse his practice. Notably, Rashi understands Rav's reversal as moving toward an even more lenient position — that she would be permitted even on the same day — not toward stringency. This reading is surprising, and cuts against what one might expect from a deathbed reversal accompanied by such urgency.

### Rav Asi's Cherem

Rav Asi, however, misheard Rav's instruction. Rav said *gayreihu* — try to draw him, to persuade him to reverse himself. Rav Asi thought he had heard *gadyeihu* — cut him off, meaning place him in *cherem*. Tosafot notes that both textual variants spell the word *gadye*, differing only in whether the *dalet* carries a *dagesh*, and that this subtle phonetic distinction accounts for the discrepancy in meaning. After Rav's death, Rav Asi approached Shila and said: Rav reversed himself — you must reverse your ruling as well.

## Rav's Reversal

### Shila Refuses

Shila refused. He argued: if Rav had indeed reversed himself, he would have told me directly. Rav had issued his ruling to me personally, and if he had changed his position, common sense dictates that he would have communicated this to me first. Since Rav never told me directly, I do not accept that he reversed himself. *Lo tzayit* — he refused to comply. Rav Asi, believing he had heard Rav instruct him to place Shila in *cherem* if he did not comply, proceeded to do so.

### Copper & Iron

The exchange that followed was anything but a halakhic discussion. Shila said to Rav Asi: *velo mistafi mar miguchalta* — are you not afraid of being burnt by my ember? The *gemara* in another context warns that one must be cautious about the honor of sages, lest one be consumed by their fire. Rav Asi replied: I am Issi ben Yehuda, Issi ben Gur Aryeh, Issi ben Gamliel, Issi ben Mahalalel — tracing a long lineage — and I am *asita de-nechasha*, a copper mortar, upon which no rot takes hold. I am not afraid of your fire. Shila replied: *va-ana Shila bar Avina, buchna de-parzela* — and I am Shila son of Avina, an iron pestle, *de-mitbar asita de-nechasha*, that can break a copper mortar. The exchange had entirely left the realm of *halacha* and become a contest of personal honor — the mirror image of the very thing Rav had tried to avoid by urging Rav Asi merely to try to persuade Shila. Rav had said: reason with him. Instead, Rav Asi moved immediately to *cherem*, which produced this inflammatory response.

### Rav Asi Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלַשׁ רַב אַסִּי. עַיְּילוּהּ בְּחַמִּימֵי אַפְּקוּהּ מִקָּרִירֵי, עַיְּילוּהּ בְּקָרִירֵי אַפְּקוּהּ מֵחַמִּימֵי, נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Asi fell ill** and contracted one sickness after another, so that when **he entered the heat** of a fever, **he exited** a sickness where he felt **cold,** and when **he entered into** a sickness where he felt **cold, he exited the heat** of a fever. Eventually, **Rav Asi died.**

Rav Asi fell ill. The Talmud describes the attempts to treat him: they placed him in hot water when he had chills, then in cold water when he had fever, cycling between extremes in a vigorous attempt to heal him — and *nach nafshei de-Rav Asi*, he died.

### Myrtle Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל שֵׁילָא, אֲמַר לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: צְבִית לִי זְוַודְתָּא דְּלָא לֵיזִיל וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ לְרַב מִילֵּי עִילָּוַאי. צְבִיתָה לֵיהּ זְוַודְתָּא. נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּשֵׁילָא, חֲזוֹ דְּפָרְחָא אָסָא מֵהַאי פּוּרְיָא לְהַאי פּוּרְיָא, אָמְרִי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ עֲבַדוּ רַבָּנַן פְּיָיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** Immediately upon Rav Asi's death, **Sheila** bar Avina **went** and **said to his wife: Prepare for me provisions [*zevadata*],** i.e., shrouds for my burial, as I will soon die. This is in order **that** Rav Asi **will not go and tell Rav matters** of criticism **about me,** that I did not listen to Rav Asi and that I caused his death because I took offense when he excommunicated me. His wife **prepared for him** the **provisions,** and **Sheila passed away.** The biers of Rav Asi and Sheila bar Avina were brought together for burial. Those accompanying the dead **saw that** the **myrtle** that was customarily placed on a bier was **flying from this bier to that bier. They said: Conclude from it** that **the Sages,** i.e., Rav Asi and Sheila bar Avina, **have made peace** with one another.

When Shila heard of Rav Asi's death, he said to his wife: prepare my burial shrouds. His stated reason was that he feared Rav Asi would ascend to heaven and complain about him to Rav — tell Rav that Shila had refused to listen and had not done the right thing. One senses in this, beneath the somewhat legalistic framing, a genuine recognition that Shila himself bore some responsibility for what had transpired. His wife prepared the shrouds, *nach nafshei de-Shila*, and Shila died.

### Heaven's Peace

Those present then saw *deparcha asa* — a myrtle branch flying *me'hai puraya le'hai puraya*, from one bier to the other. People said: *shema mina avdu rabbanan paisa* — we see from this that the rabbis have made peace. They had ascended, learned Torah together, and been reconciled in heaven. The irony, as one might note, is that we do not actually know the content of Rav's reversal — which direction he reversed — and that remains unknown to us even now, though presumably it is known in heaven. The myrtle vision offers a hopeful symbol: that in the end, both sages recognized they had each erred, and reconciliation was possible.

## Interrupting Clean D (II)

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: קוֹשִׁי מַהוּ שֶׁתִּסְתּוֹר בְּזִיבָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a woman who experiences an emission of blood due to labor pains is not rendered a *zava* if she sees the blood in days when she can become a *zava*, but rather a menstruating woman if she sees the blood on days during which she can be a menstruating woman. In this regard **Rava raises a dilemma:** In general, if a *zava* experiences an emission of blood while counting seven clean days, her count is negated and she must begin a new count of seven clean days. But **what is** the *halakha* with regard to a *zava* who experienced an emission of blood due to labor **pains?** Does this emission **negate** her count **with regard to *ziva*?**

The *gemara* continues on 37a. Rava poses a new question: *koshi mahu she-tistor be-zivah* — given that birth-pain bleeding does not create *zavah* status, does it nonetheless interrupt a woman's seven clean days? Suppose a woman had bled during pregnancy without birth pains and became a *zavah*, and is now counting her seven clean days; then she bleeds again, this time with birth pains. That birth-pain blood cannot make her a *zavah* — but does it interrupt the count?

The conceptual tension Rava identifies is this: on one hand, *davar ha-metamei soter* — perhaps anything that causes *tumah* can interrupt, and since birth-pain blood can cause *tumah* during the *niddah* period, it has at least potential *tumah*-causing capacity. On the other hand, perhaps the principle is *davar ha-gorem soter* — only blood that can specifically cause *zavah* status can interrupt the seven clean days, since the reason an interruption matters is that the blood reasserts her *zavah* trajectory; blood that can never make her a *zavah* should have no power over the clean days.

### Abaye's Male Zav Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אוֹנֶס בְּזִיבָה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹרֵם, וְסוֹתֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: The case of an emission **of *ziva*** due to **circumstances beyond** one's **control will prove** the *halakha* with regard to this dilemma, **as** such an emission **does not cause** one to be rendered a *zav*, **and** yet **it negates** one's count of seven clean days.

Abaye responds: *ones be-zivah yochiach* — let me bring a proof from a male *zav*. We learned previously that a man becomes a *zav* only through flows that are not due to external causes (*ones*); a flow caused by external stimulation does not count toward making him a *zav*. Yet once a man has already completed two flows and is counting his seven clean days, a flow due to *ones* — which cannot make him a *zav* — does interrupt the clean days. This, says Abaye, answers Rava's question: we see that a flow which cannot create *zav* status can nonetheless interrupt the seven clean days.

### Ones & Third Level

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאיֵי, הַאי נָמֵי גּוֹרֵם הוּא, דִּתְנַן: רָאָה רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה — בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, שְׁנִיָּה — בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, שְׁלִישִׁית — אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. וּלְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: אַף בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, הָכִי נָמֵי כֵּיוָן דְּלָא גָרֵים לָא סָתַר?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** This is **not so [*la'ei*],** as **this** emission of *ziva* due to circumstances beyond one's control **is also** a substance that **causes** one to be rendered a *zav*, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Zavim* 2:2): With regard to a man who **saw a first sighting** of *ziva*, **one examines him** to determine whether the discharge was caused by circumstances beyond his control. After the **second** sighting of *ziva* as well, **one examines him.** But after the **third** sighting **one does not examine him,** as even if the third sighting occurred due to circumstances beyond his control he is nevertheless rendered a *zav* on its account. Abaye asked Rava: **And according to Rabbi Eliezer, who said: Even** after **the third** discharge **one examines him,** will you **indeed** say that **since** an emission of *ziva* due to circumstances beyond one's control **does not cause** one to be rendered a *zav*, **it does not negate** his count?

The *gemara* pushes back: one cannot prove from the case of *ones* that the flow has no *zav*-creating capacity, because in fact a flow due to *ones* does carry some weight in that regard. A Mishnah teaches: the first flow is examined to rule out external causes; the second is examined similarly; but the third — the flow that completes *zav* status and obligates a *korban* — is not examined at all. A third flow counts even if it was due to *ones*. Hence a flow of *ones* does partially make a man a *zav*, advancing him from two-flow status to full three-flow *zav*, and therefore one cannot use it as an example of a flow that cannot make him a *zav* at all.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָכִי נָמֵי. תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, אַף בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, בָּרְבִיעִית אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. מַאי לַָאו לִסְתִירָה? לָא, לְטַמּוֹיֵהּ לְהַהִיא טִיפָּה בְּמַשָּׂא. תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, בָּרְבִיעִית אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. לְקׇרְבָּן אָמַרְתִּי, וְלֹא לִסְתִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **According to Rabbi Eliezer,** this is **indeed** the case. The Gemara attempts to reject Rava's explanation of the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** after **the third** discharge **one examines him** to establish whether he must bring an offering, but after **the fourth** discharge **one does not examine him. What, is it not** correct to say that as he was already rendered a *zav* after three discharges, an examination after the fourth discharge is **for** the matter of **negating** any clean days counted thus far? If so, Rabbi Eliezer maintains that an emission of *ziva* due to circumstances beyond one's control negates one's count. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** it is possible that the purpose of the examination after the fourth discharge is to determine whether **to render impure that drop** of *ziva* such that it imparts impurity **through carrying.** According to Rabbi Eliezer, the discharge of a *zav* imparts impurity through carrying, even if the discharge occurred due to circumstances beyond his control. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another *baraita*: **With regard to the third** discharge, **Rabbi Eliezer says** that **one examines him,** but after **the fourth** discharge **one does not examine him.** The reason is as **I said,** that the purpose of these examinations is **to** determine liability to bring **an offering, and** they do **not** pertain **to** the matter of **negating** any clean days counted thus far. Since the fourth sighting does not affect liability to bring an offering there is no need for an examination. Evidently, Rabbi Eliezer maintains that a discharge that occurs due to circumstances beyond one's control does negate his count.

At this point the *gemara* introduces the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that even the third flow must be examined — *af bishlishi bodkin oto*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, a flow of *ones* never contributes toward *zav* status at any level. The *gemara* then asks: would Rabbi Eliezer say that such a flow also cannot interrupt the seven clean days? The response is direct: *le-Rabbi Eliezer hachi nami* — yes, according to Rabbi Eliezer, a flow of *ones* would not interrupt the clean days.

The *gemara* then cites a fuller version of that *braita* in which Rabbi Eliezer himself clarifies the matter: when he says that the fourth flow need not be examined, he means this only with respect to the *korban* — the fourth flow is irrelevant to whether a *korban* is brought. But *le-stira* — regarding interruption of the seven clean days — even Rabbi Eliezer agrees that a flow of *ones* can interrupt. Here, then, is an explicit statement that according to Rabbi Eliezer, a flow that cannot make a man a *zav* can nonetheless interrupt his clean days.

### Father of Rabbi Avin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹרֵם סוֹתֵר, לְרַבָּנַן מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין: מָה גָּרַם לוֹ זוֹבוֹ — שִׁבְעָה, לְפִיכָךְ סוֹתֵר שִׁבְעָה; מָה גָּרַם לוֹ קִרְיוֹ — יוֹם אֶחָד, לְפִיכָךְ סוֹתֵר יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concedes: **Rather, according to Rabbi Eliezer,** one can **resolve** the dilemma and conclude **that** even **a substance that does not cause** one to be rendered a *zav* **negates** one's count. But **what** is the *halakha* **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear that** which **the father of Rabbi Avin teaches** with regard to the question of why a discharge of *ziva* causes a *zav* to negate his entire count of clean days, whereas a seminal emission negates only the day of the emission itself: **What** did **his *ziva* cause for him?** An impurity of **seven** days. **Therefore,** a discharge of *ziva* causes him to **negate** his count of **seven** clean days. By contrast, **what** did **his seminal emission cause for him?** An impurity of **one day** alone. **Therefore,** a seminal emission causes him to **negate** only **one day** of his count.

The *gemara* then cites a *braita* taught by the father of Rabbi Avin: *ma garam lo zovo shiva* — what does the *zov* flow cause the man? A seven-day *tumah*. Therefore *soter shiva* — it interrupts all seven clean days. Conversely, a seminal emission (*keri*) causes only a one-day *tumah*, and therefore interrupts only one day, not all seven. The critical observation is the language *ma garam lo* — what does it cause — which links the power of interruption explicitly to the causal capacity of the flow. The *gemara* notes: had the *tanna* simply meant to describe the length of *tumah*, he would have said "his flow causes seven days of *tumah*, therefore it interrupts seven days." The choice of *garam* — causes — signals a conceptual principle: *davar ha-gorem soter*, only that which causes the *tumah* can interrupt it.

### Davar Ha-Gorem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שִׁבְעָה? אִילֵּימָא דִּמְטַמֵּא שִׁבְעָה — הַאי ״מָה זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא שִׁבְעָה״ מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא לָאו דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם סוֹתֵר, דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹרֵם אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן, אֵין קוֹשִׁי סוֹתֵר בְּזִיבָה. וְאִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר סוֹתֵר — הָהוּא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What** is the meaning of the claim that *ziva* causes an impurity of **seven** days? **If we say** it means merely **that** *ziva* **renders him impure** for **seven** days, then the father of Rabbi Avin **should have** stated: **Just as his *ziva*** causes him to be impure for **seven** days, so too, it negates his count of seven clean days. **Rather, is it not** that the mention of causation indicates that this is what he is saying: **A substance that causes** one to be rendered a *zav* **negates** one's count of seven clean days, whereas **a substance that does not cause** one to be rendered a *zav*, e.g., an emission of blood due to labor pains, **does not negate** one's count. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is the opinion of the Rabbis. With regard to the *halakha*, **Abaye said: We have a tradition** that blood emitted by a woman due to labor **pains does not negate** the seven clean days **of *ziva*,** in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And if you a find *tanna* who said** that it does **negate** them, the statement of **that** *tanna* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

Accordingly, the *gemara* concludes: *davar ha-gorem soter, davar she-eino gorem eino soter* — only a flow that can cause a person to become a *zav* or *zavah* can interrupt the seven clean days; a flow that cannot cause that status cannot interrupt. The ruling regarding the female case now follows directly: birth-pain bleeding, which can never make a woman a *zavah*, cannot interrupt her seven clean days, even though in another context it may cause *tumah* as *niddah* blood. According to Rabbi Eliezer, however — who holds that even a flow of *ones* that cannot create *zav* status can interrupt the clean days — birth-pain bleeding would interrupt. The *gemara* thus resolves that the two positions on interruption by birth-pain blood map onto the broader dispute between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Eliezer.

## Interrupting Clean D (II)

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מָרִינוּס אוֹמֵר: אֵין לֵידָה סוֹתֶרֶת בְּזִיבָה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֵינָהּ סוֹתֶרֶת וְאֵינָהּ עוֹלָה. רָבָא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ סוֹתֶרֶת וְעוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Marinus says:** In the case of a *zava* who gave birth in the middle of counting seven clean days, the **birth does not negate** her count of seven clean days **of *ziva*.** With regard to this statement, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether her days of impurity **may be counted** toward the seven clean days of *ziva*? **Abaye says:** The birth **does not negate** her count, **but it does not count** toward the seven clean days. **Rava says:** The birth **does not negate** her count **and** it also **counts** toward the seven days.

Abaye states the conclusion as received *halacha*: *naktinan, ein koshi soter be-zivah* — we hold that birth-pain bleeding does not interrupt the seven clean days, since it cannot make a woman a *zavah*. If one encounters a *tanna* who says it does interrupt, that position follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that even a flow without *gorem*-capacity can interrupt.

It is worth noting in this context an important related *halacha* discussed earlier in the *masechta*: the case of a *poletet shichvat zera* — a woman from whom semen is discharged — and whether this interrupts her counting of the clean days. Rashi's position is that *davar ha-gorem soter* applies only to a full interruption requiring the woman to start her seven days over entirely; a situation where only one day is lost does not need to satisfy that criterion. Some *rishonim* find this distinction difficult, and it bears directly on the well-known *Ashkenazi* practice that women do not begin counting their seven clean days until day six, in part due to concerns about *poletet shichvat zera*.

## Childbirth & Interruption

### Rav Merinos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאַחַר תִּטְהָר״ — ״אַחַר״, ״אַחַר״ לְכוּלָּן, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא טוּמְאָה מַפְסֶקֶת בֵּינֵיהֶם. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא עוֹלָה — הַיְינוּ דְּלָא מַפְסְקָה טוּמְאָה, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵינָהּ עוֹלָה — אַפְסִיק לֵיהּ לֵידָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** that the birth is counted toward the seven days? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "But if she is purified from her *ziva* then she shall count for herself seven days, **and after that she shall be pure"** (Leviticus 15:28). The word **"after"** indicates that she shall be pure only **after** counting **all of them,** i.e., **that there should not be an impurity separating between** any of the seven clean days. Rava explains: **Granted, if you say** the birth **counts** toward the seven clean days, **this is** in accordance with the requirement **that there should not be an impurity separating** between any of the seven clean days, as they remain consecutive. **But if you say** the birth **does not count** toward the seven clean days, then the **birth separates between** the seven clean days.

The *gemara* moves to a related topic. A *braita* cites Rav Merinos: *ein leida soteret be-zivah* — the act of giving birth itself does not interrupt a woman's seven clean days. The case is a woman who had bled without birth pains, became a *zavah*, began counting her seven clean days, and then gave birth. Childbirth involves substantial bleeding, but Rav Merinos rules that this is not *dam niddah* in the ordinary sense — it is birth blood, and as such does not interrupt the *zavah* counting. After the birth bleeding ceases, she may resume counting from where she left off.

### Non-Bleeding Days

A further question is then raised: *mahu she-ta'aleh* — may the days of the *leida* period during which she is not bleeding count toward her seven clean days? The *yoledet* has a period of *tumah* of one or two weeks regardless of bleeding. If during that period the bleeding stops, can she count those non-bleeding days toward completing her *shiva nekiyim*?

This question has practical relevance: a woman who miscarries carries *tumah* of *leida* once the fetus is at least forty days old, and that *leida tumah* extends for a week (for a male) or two weeks (for a female). Since every woman today is treated as requiring seven clean days — effectively as a *zavah gedolah* — the question of whether those non-bleeding *leida* days may be counted is significant.

### Abaye's View

Abaye holds: *eina soteret ve-eina olah* — the *leida* days neither interrupt the seven clean days nor count toward them. She is *temei'ah* during that period, and even if she is not bleeding, those days do not advance her count.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ — ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ וְלֹא מִנִּגְעָהּ, ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ וְלֹא מִלֵּידָתָהּ. וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר לָךְ: תְּנִי חֲדָא ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ וְלֹא מִנִּגְעָהּ, וְלָא תִּתְנֵי ״וְלֹא מִלֵּידָתָהּ״. וְרָבָא, הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ וְלֹא מִלֵּידָתָהּ, אַיְּידֵי דְּאִצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לֵידָה, תְּנָא נִגְעָהּ אַטּוּ לֵידָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״מִזּוֹבָהּ״ וְלֹא מִנִּגְעָהּ, הַאי מִ״וְּכִי יִטְהַר הַזָּב מִזּוֹבוֹ״ נָפְקָא — ״מִזּוֹבוֹ״ וְלֹא מִנִּגְעוֹ! וְאַבָּיֵי — חַד בְּזָב וְחַד בְּזָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** further **said: From where do I say** that the birth is counted toward the seven clean days? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "But if she is purified **from her *ziva*** then she shall count for herself seven days" (Leviticus 15:28). This indicates that she counts seven clean days **from her *ziva* and not from her leprosy,** i.e., she begins counting seven days from the cessation of her *ziva*, even if she is a leper. Likewise, she counts seven clean days **from her *ziva* and not from her** giving **birth,** as she counts seven clean days even if they continue through her days of impurity. The Gemara notes: **And Abaye** could have **said to you:** From this verse **teach one** derivation, i.e., **from her *ziva* and not from her leprosy, but do not teach:** From her *ziva* **and not from her** giving **birth. And Rava** would respond: **What is this** suggestion? **Granted, if you say** that the *tanna* of the *baraita* taught: **From her *ziva* and not from her** giving **birth,** one can understand why the *tanna* also teaches: From her *ziva* and not from her leprosy: **Since it was necessary for** the *tanna* to teach this *halakha* with regard to **birth,** he **taught** it with regard to **her leprosy, due to** the fact that he taught it with regard to **birth. But if you say** that the *tanna* taught only: **From her *ziva* and not from her leprosy,** then the verse is unnecessary, as **this** *halakha* **is** already **derived from** another verse: **"And when the *zav* is purified of his *ziva*"** (Leviticus 15:13), i.e., **from his *ziva* and not from his leprosy. And Abaye** could respond: **One** verse discusses the case **of a *zav* and** the other **one** discusses the case **of a *zava*, and** both verses **are necessary.**

Rava holds: *eina soteret ve-olah* — the *leida* days do not interrupt, and those days during which she does not bleed do count toward the seven clean days, even though the *tumah* of *yoledet* continues.

### Rava's Proof

Rava marshals two proofs. First, the *gemara* cites a *braita*: *v'achar tithar* — "and afterwards she shall be purified" — is interpreted to mean that the seven clean days must form one uninterrupted unit, with no *tumah* breaking them. *Ei amart bishlama olah* — if you say that the non-bleeding *leida* days can count, there is no problem: those days are not a gap in the sequence, since they themselves advance the count. But *ei amart eina olah* — if you say they cannot count, then if she had accumulated three clean days before childbirth and then entered a two-week *leida* period during which no days count, and then resumes counting, how can that be called a single unit of seven? The *leida* period creates an irreparable break in the sequence. The *braita*'s language — that *tumah* should not interrupt *between* the days — implies that *leida* days are not a severing interruption, which supports Rava's view that they can count.

Second, Rava cites a *braita*: *mizovah* — from her flow she may purify herself. Even a woman who is a *metzora'at* can complete her *zavah* counting and immerse without first being purified of her *tzara'at*. And then: *velo milleidatah* — and even if she is a *yoledet*, this does not prevent her from counting and completing her seven clean days. Rashi explains that this does not literally mean she immerses while still a *yoledet*, since the *tumah* of *yoledet* and *zavah* share the same domain and must both be resolved before she is permitted to her husband; rather, it means that her *yoledet* status does not prevent the *leida* days from counting toward completion of the seven clean days. This is an explicit ruling in accordance with Rava.

### Abaye's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבָּנַן לָא יָדְעִין הִיא דְּאִשְׁתְּמַע לַן מִלְּתוּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּימֵי נִדָּתָהּ״ — כָּךְ יְמֵי לֵידָתָהּ, מָה יְמֵי נִדָּתָהּ אֵין רְאוּיִן לְזִיבָה וְאֵין סְפִירַת שִׁבְעָה עוֹלָה מֵהֶן, אַף יְמֵי לֵידָתָהּ שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיִן לְזִיבָה — אֵין סְפִירַת שִׁבְעָה עוֹלָה מֵהֶן. וְרָבָא, הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מִסְתָּר נָמֵי סָתְרָה. וְכִי דָנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר? אָמַר רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: עַל כׇּרְחָךְ הִקִּישָׁן הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** And the Rabbis do not know this teaching, as it emerges for us from the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer. **Abaye says: From where do I say** that although the birth does not negate the count of a *zava*, it does not count toward the seven clean days? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* which addresses the verse: "If a woman be delivered, and bear a male, then she shall be impure seven days; as in the days of her menstrual sickness she shall be impure" (Leviticus 12:2). The superfluous phrase: **"Her** menstrual **sickness she shall be impure,"** serves **to include** the ruling **of Ḥananya,** son of Rabbi Yehoshua's brother, that for a woman to be considered one who gives birth as a *zava*, she must have rested from labor for the entire third day. The Gemara analyzes this apparent derivation from Scripture: The verse compares the *halakha* of the impurity of birth to the impurity of menstruation, indicating that **the days of her menstruation are like the days of her** giving **birth: Just as the days of her menstruation are unfit for *ziva*,** as a woman may be rendered a *zava* only through emissions during the eleven days following the seven days of menstruation, **and** the **counting of seven** clean days of *ziva* **is not reckoned from them** because as long as she remains a *zava* she cannot be deemed a menstruating woman; **so too,** with regard to **the days of her** giving **birth, which are unfit for *ziva*** because a woman cannot be rendered a *zava* on account of blood emitted due to childbirth, the **counting of seven** clean days of *ziva* **is not reckoned from them. And Rava,** who maintains that the days of impurity may be counted toward the seven clean days, could say to you: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said** that not only is the birth not counted toward the seven clean days, it **also negates** any days counted thus far. The Gemara analyzes the aforementioned *baraita*, which compares the *halakha* of the impurity of birth to the impurity of menstruation, with regard to *ziva*: **But does one derive** the **possible from** the **impossible?** In other words, how can the *halakha* with regard to impurity after giving birth be derived from that of the days of menstruation? While it is possible for a woman to give birth as a *zava*, it is impossible for a *zava* to simultaneously attain the status of a menstruating woman. **Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami says:** This *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said** that **one derives** the **possible from** the **impossible. And Rav Sheshet says** a different explanation: Although generally one does not derive the possible from the impossible, **perforce the verse juxtaposed** the days of impurity after birth and those of menstruation, and a juxtaposition in the verse is expounded even if one case is possible while the other is not.

Abaye responds to the first proof by reinterpreting the *braita*: *she-lo tehei tumat zivah mafseket beineihem* — the *braita* is saying that *zivah tumah* should not interrupt the seven clean days. Since the woman is not in a state of *zivah tumah* during the *leida* period — she is in *leida tumah*, which is a distinct category — the *braita* simply does not address whether those days count. It only tells us that a *zivah*-type interruption would destroy the continuity; it says nothing about *leida* days.

To the second proof, Abaye simply proposes emending the *braita*: remove the phrase *velo milleidatah*. The *braita* should only contain *mizovah velo minega'ah* — that *metzora'at* status does not prevent completion of *zavah* counting — and not the phrase about *leida*. Rava responds that this reading is untenable: if the *braita* only tells us about *metzora'at*, there was no need to state it, since the same ruling for men — *mizovo velo minega'o* — is already stated elsewhere, and we could derive the female case by extension. The only reason the *braita* adds the female case independently is to teach the novel *halacha* of *leida* — that *yoledet* status does not bar her from counting toward her clean days. The *metzora'at* clause is included incidentally alongside it. Abaye responds that in fact one cannot automatically extend from male *zav* to female *zavah* or vice versa, since each has distinctive stringencies, and therefore the *braita* must spell out both *metzora'at* cases independently. The phrase about *leida* is thus unnecessary and should be removed.

Abaye then advances his own proof: a *braita* teaches *kimei nidasah kach yimei leidatah* — just as her *niddah* days are, so are her *leida* days. The comparison: during the *niddah* period, a woman cannot become a *zavah*, and the *niddah* days do not count toward the seven clean days of a *zavah*. Similarly, the *leida* days should not count. Rava responds that this *braita* reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer — specifically Rabbi Eliezer's ruling *danin efshar mi-she-ein efshar*, that one may draw a legal inference from a possible situation compared to an impossible one. In ordinary cases one cannot learn from what never occurs, but since the Torah explicitly equates *yoledet* and *niddah* — *kimei nidas devosah titma* — the equation is compelled by the verse itself, even across the *efshar/she-ein efshar* divide. Rav Sheshet (or, in another version, Rav Achdavoi bar Ami in the name of Rav Sheshet) states this point: *al korchach hikishan hakatuv* — the text itself forces the comparison, which justifies the inference. Accordingly, Abaye's proof from the *braita* holds only according to Rabbi Eliezer, but does not compel the conclusion for the *Rabbanan*.

Ultimately, Rashi himself, in a responsum cited by Tosafot, rules that even Abaye concedes in practice that the non-bleeding *leida* days may count toward the seven clean days. Tosafot notes that Rashi writes: *d'Abaye modeh d'yimei leida she-eina ro'ah bahem olim lisfirat zivtah* — that in practice even Abaye agrees these days count. The entire discussion, Rashi indicates, is conducted according to Rav Merinos's position, but the final *halacha* follows Rava.

## Break in Pains

### Pains & Bleeding Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁפְתָה מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה, מַהוּ? רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: טְמֵאָה, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In this regard, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **she rested from** both **this and that,** from labor pains and emissions of blood, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rav Ḥisda says** that as she rested from labor pains it is evident that the emissions of blood were not due to her imminent labor, and she is **ritually impure. Rabbi Ḥanina says** that as the emissions of blood also ceased it is apparent that they were due to her imminent labor, and she is **ritually pure.**

The *gemara* now returns to the main topic of birth pains and raises a new question: *shavta mizeh umizeh mahu* — what is the *halacha* if there is a full cessation of both the pains and the bleeding? We already know that a break in pains alone, while bleeding continues, creates an interruption and undermines the *koshi* exemption. But if both stop entirely — no pains and no bleeding — does that also create a break, or does it actually favor the woman?

The question is genuinely two-sided. On one hand, a complete cessation seems an even clearer break — if even pains alone ceasing creates an interruption, surely the cessation of both must do so. On the other hand, one might argue the opposite: bleeding without pains suggests that the bleeding is at least partly independent of the pains, calling into question whether even the earlier bleeding with pains was truly *koshi*-related. But if both stop together, there is no evidence of bleeding independent of pains, and the earlier bleeding may be attributed entirely to the *koshi*, leaving it *tahor*.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda rules: *temei'ah*. A full cessation creates a break just as a cessation of pains alone does. The bleeding is no longer connected to the *leida* process.

### Rav Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ שֶׁיָּצָא, וַחֲיָילוֹתָיו לְפָנָיו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁחֲיָילוֹתָיו שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ הֵן. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּבָעֵי נַפֵּישׁ חַיָּילוֹת טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says,** in explanation of his opinion: Hear **a parable;** to what is this case comparable? It is comparable **to a king who left** his palace, **and his soldiers** left **before him.** Although the king travels behind them, **it is known that they are the soldiers of the king.** Likewise, although both the labor pains and the blood subsided, it is clear that the blood she emitted was due to the approaching birth, and therefore she is not a *zava*. **And Rav Ḥisda says:** By the same parable, i.e., assuming that the soldiers arrive before the king, **all the more so that** there **must** be **many more soldiers** accompanying the king upon his arrival. The lack of soldiers before the arrival of the king indicates that they are not in fact soldiers of the king. Likewise, the cessation of labor pains before the birth indicates that the previous emissions of blood were not due to her imminent labor. She is therefore rendered a *zava*.

Rav Chanina rules: *tehorah*. When both pains and bleeding stop together, the earlier pains and bleeding remain attributed to the *leida*, and the woman is *tehorah*. He explains with a *mashal*: *mashal le-melech she-yatza ve-chayalotav lefanav* — it is like a king whose troops march in advance before him. Everyone knows that these are the king's troops. The birth pains that preceded the delivery, even with a gap before the actual birth, are the advance troops — they are recognizably connected to the event that follows. They need not march uninterrupted all the way to the last moment.

### Chisda Objects

Rav Chisda objects: *kol sheken deva'ei nafish chayalot tefei* — on the contrary, as the king approaches, the number of troops should increase. If the pains were truly connected to the birth, they would have intensified and continued. A gap in pains, Rav Chisda argues, demonstrates that the pains have discharged their function and are not building continuously toward the birth. The debate thus crystallizes around two conceptual models: according to Rav Chisda, true *koshi* must persist uninterruptedly up to the delivery; according to Rav Chanina, pains that precede delivery and are accompanied by bleeding are inherently connected to the birth, and the only thing that can sever that connection is bleeding without pains — which shows the bleeding is not fully dependent on the pains.

### Mishna Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לַיְלָה וָיוֹם כְּלֵילֵי שַׁבָּת וְיוֹמוֹ, שֶׁשָּׁפְתָה מִן הַצַּעַר וְלֹא מִן הַדָּם. טַעְמָא דְּמִן הַצַּעַר וְלֹא מִן הַדָּם, הָא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה — טְהוֹרָה. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection against the opinion of Rav Ḥisda: **We learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** She is considered a *zava* only if the pangs subsided for a twenty-four-hour period of **a night and** the following **day, like Shabbat evening and its** accompanying **day.** Additionally, she is considered a *zava* in a case **where she rested from the pain** of labor **but not from the** flow of **blood.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** she is rendered a *zava* is **that** she rested **from the pain** of labor **and not from the** flow of **blood. But** if she rested **from** both **this and that,** from labor pains and emissions of blood, she is **ritually pure.** The mishna is apparently **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda.**

Rav Chisda then attempts to prove his position from a Mishnah: *kashta shlosha yamim betoch achad asar yom veshavta me'et le'et veyalda harei zu yoledet be-zov* — if a woman had birth pains for three days within the eleven-day *zivah* period, then had a break of a full twenty-four hours, and then gave birth, she is a *yoledet be-zov*. Rav Chisda asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario? If it is as literally stated — three days of pain with bleeding, then a full day of nothing, then birth — why was it necessary to give three days of pain? The same conclusion could be reached with two days of pain and bleeding and one day of bleeding without pain: the painless bleeding on day three would itself constitute the break, yielding the same *yoledet be-zov* result. The only reason the Mishnah gives three full days of *koshi* and a separate fourth day of complete cessation must be to teach that a full cessation of both pain and bleeding also constitutes a break — thereby supporting Rav Chisda.

### Chanina Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא מִבַּעְיָא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה דִּטְמֵאָה, דְּפָסְקִי לְהוּ חַיָּילוֹת לִגְמָרֵי, אֲבָל מִן הַצַּעַר וְלֹא מִן הַדָּם, אֵימַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמִדָּם לָא פָּסְקָה, מִקּוֹשִׁי נָמֵי לָא פָּסְקָה, וְהָא תּוּנְבָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דִּנְקַט לַהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rav Ḥisda** could **say to you: It is not necessary** to teach **that** if she rested **from** both **this and that** she is **impure, as,** in terms of the above parable, the king's **soldiers have ceased entirely,** i.e., both the labor pains and the blood have completely subsided. **But** with regard to a case where she rested **from the pain but not from the blood,** one might **say** that **just as she did not cease** emitting **blood, so too, she did not cease** experiencing labor **pains, and** the fact **that** she does not sense any pain **is** because **she was seized by a general disorientation,** i.e., she was so weakened by the labor that she was unable to discern pain. Consequently, she should remain pure. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that if she does not feel labor pains, this is indicative that the previous emissions of blood were not due to her imminent labor, and she is a *zava*.

Rav Chanina responds: *lo, le-olam ke-dik-tani* — no, take the Mishnah literally, the scenario is as stated: three days of pain and bleeding. The reason the Mishnah needed to give three days rather than two is to exclude a different ruling — that of a different Rav Chanina mentioned earlier — who had held that if a woman bleeds on the day immediately before giving birth, that bleeding is always considered connected to the birth even if there was a twenty-four-hour break before it. To refute that position, the Mishnah specifically gives the scenario of three full days of *koshi*, then a break, then birth: since the bleeding on day three was followed by a full twenty-four-hour gap with nothing, even though day three's bleeding was the day before the birth, the break is sufficient to create a *yoledet be-zov* status. The Mishnah is thus teaching against that specific position, and its formulation of three days of *koshi* followed by a clean day is not intended to address the question of a complete double cessation at all. The debate between Rav Chisda and Rav Chanina therefore remains unresolved.
