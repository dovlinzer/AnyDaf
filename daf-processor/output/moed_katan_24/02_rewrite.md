# Moed Katan 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avelut on Shabbat

### Private vs. Public

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion about *yesh avelut b'Shabbat* — whether mourning practices are observed on Shabbat. One dimension of that question concerns whether Shabbat counts toward the *shiva* because certain practices are actively maintained on it, or simply because the seven-day period is measured from the outset regardless. The Gemara had previously been weighing evidence from a *braita* that was interpreted to show that at least one opinion prohibits *tashmish hamitah* — sexual relations — during *aninut*, and, by extension, during Shabbat as well. That reading was offered as support for the position of *yesh avelut b'Shabbat*.

Yet even on that view, the suggestion is clearly not full observance of *avelut*. Rather, as the daf makes explicit, the range of practices that continue on Shabbat is limited to what the Gemara calls *devarim she-b'tzina* — private observances. In principle, one might have argued for comprehensive observance of *avelut* on Shabbat. After all, Shabbat is not fundamentally about *simcha* — joy — and it is precisely the *simcha* dimension of a festival that is cited elsewhere as the basis for interrupting *avelut*. Indeed, it is worth noting that *nichum avelim* — comforting mourners — is permitted and even practiced on Shabbat; the Rosh describes a communal custom of publicly visiting the *avel* after synagogue. If Shabbat does not demand joy in a way that precludes mourning, perhaps full observance could stand.

And yet Shabbat is also not the occasion for *tza'ar* — distress — and the Yerushalmi frames its character as one of *bracha* and spiritual pleasure. Accordingly, there is a distinction between the *avel's* public display of anguish, which belongs to the realm Shabbat forecloses, and acts of comfort extended by others, which belong to *gemilut chasadim* and carry no such prohibition. The *avel* changing his seat is admittedly a more public kind of act, but the basic framework holds: the degree of *avelut* that may be observed on Shabbat is limited to private observance — *devarim she-b'tzina*.

### Shmuel: Ein Avelut

The Gemara records that Rabbi Yochanan posed a question to Shmuel: *yesh avelut b'Shabbat o ein avelut b'Shabbat*? Shmuel's answer was unambiguous — *ein avelut b'Shabbat*, mourning is not practiced on Shabbat. Following this, the Gemara reports that the rabbis sitting before Rav Pappa were citing in Shmuel's name the ruling that an *avel* who engages in sexual relations during his period of *avelut* is deserving of death — *chayav mita*. The juxtaposition with Shmuel's statement that there is no *avelut* on Shabbat is jarring, and the question naturally arises: is this statement about *tashmish hamitah* being made in the context of Shabbat?

### Tashmish Hamitah

Rav Pappa corrects the rabbis on two counts. First, what Shmuel actually said was *assur* — forbidden — not *chayav mita*. Second, it was not Shmuel who said it at all; it was Rabbi Yochanan. This matters considerably. The prohibition of *tashmish hamitah* for an *avel* is not a novelty — it has always been among the recognized practices of *avelut*. Why would Rabbi Yochanan need to say it? The natural reading is that we are still operating within the context of the Shabbat debate: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan, who holds *yesh avelut b'Shabbat*, is the one asserting that the prohibition of *tashmish hamitah* extends even to Shabbat, while Shmuel, who holds *ein avelut b'Shabbat*, would not say so.

### Shmuel's Position

The Gemara then clarifies what Shmuel did in fact say — and it has nothing to do with either Shabbat or sexual relations. Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi cites Shmuel as teaching: an *avel* who fails to let his hair grow wild (*para*) or who fails to rend his garments (*peirem*) is *chayav mita* — and a verse from the Torah is adduced in support. The verse in question is the command given to Elazar and Itamar after the deaths of Nadav and Avihu: *rosheichem al tifrau u'vigdeichem lo tifromu v'lo samusu* — do not let your hair go wild, do not rend your garments, lest you die. The logic, as Shmuel reads it, is *mutatis mutandis*: the sons of Aharon were told not to perform the normal practices of *avelut* or they would die. By inversion, ordinary *aveilim* who fail to perform those same practices will likewise face such a consequence.

The derivation is admittedly far from *pshat*. The verse addresses a specific situation tied to the *avodah* of the *mishkan*, not a general law of *avelut*. Nevertheless, Shmuel is using it rhetorically to underscore the seriousness of observing *hilchot avelut*. The Gemara concludes that whatever the rabbis heard in Shmuel's name about *tashmish hamitah* was simply a misattribution: they must have heard Shmuel's exegetical point about *priat harosh* and *keri'at habegadim*, not a statement about sex or about Shabbat. Shmuel's actual position — that *ein avelut b'Shabbat* — is thus left intact, and there is no tension with his earlier statement.

There may be a loose thematic association that gave rise to the confusion: unkempt hair and torn garments appear in contexts involving *sotah* as well, lending a kind of resonance across different areas of *halacha*. But the Gemara's point is clear: the rhetoric about sex belongs to a separate concern entirely — most likely a recognition that private practices of *avelut*, precisely because they carry no social visibility, were vulnerable to being quietly disregarded. The *avel* sits *shiva* in an environment of communal presence and social expectation. The private practices — those that no one else will see — are the ones most likely to be compromised. It is not surprising that the tradition felt the need to address those with particular emphasis.

## Public vs. Private

### Pachas Chova

The Gemara now turns to a more systematic treatment of the public/private distinction. Shmuel states: *pachas chova, neter reshus*. The acronym *pachas* stands for *priat harosh* — the *avel's* obligation to uncover or expose his head — *hachzarat kera le'achorav* — turning the rent garment so the tear faces behind — and *zekifat hamita* — setting the beds upright. These are obligations on Shabbat. That is, on Shabbat, the *avel* is *obligated* to perform the opposite of the normal *avelut* practice: to uncover his face, to turn the rent portion of the garment to the back, to right the overturned beds. All of these are highly visible signs of *avelut*, and their reversal is required on Shabbat precisely because of their public character.

*Neter*, by contrast, stands for *ne'ilat hasandal* — wearing shoes — *tashmish hamitah*, and *rechitzat yadayim v'raglayim b'chamin* — washing with hot water at night. These are *reshut* — discretionary, or in the language the Rosh will interpret, obligatory private practices. Regarding these, the language is puzzling. *Reshut* sounds like permission, like the *avel* may choose whether to observe them or not. The Rosh, however, argues that there is no middle ground: if Shabbat does not forbid a practice, then the underlying obligation of *avelut* reasserts itself in full, and the *avel* is obligated to maintain those private practices on Shabbat just as on a weekday. This reading has halachic force — it is indeed how we rule. And yet the language of the Gemara itself, which says *reshut* rather than *chova*, does create some tension, and the plain sense of the word really does seem to suggest discretion.

### Three Private Acts

The three private acts named by Shmuel deserve attention. *Ne'ilat hasandal* — the prohibition against wearing shoes — is perhaps counterintuitive as a *devar she-b'tzina*. Walking outside without shoes would seem obvious to any passerby. Shmuel's rationale is that *lav kulei alma avidi d'saymi masanyeihu* — not everyone wears shoes in all situations, so the bare foot of an *avel* does not necessarily announce his status. *Tashmish hamitah* is the paradigmatic private act — entirely between husband and wife, and known to no one else. *Rechitza b'chamin* — bathing with hot water — is similarly invisible: one cannot tell by looking at someone whether they have bathed or not.

The Rosh's ruling is that these private practices are not merely permitted on Shabbat but obligatory: since Shabbat does not stand in their way, the full force of the *avelut* obligation governs them. The *avel* does not wash with hot water, does not engage in *tashmish hamitah*, does not wear shoes — even on Shabbat.

### Rav's Disagreement

Rav disputes one element of Shmuel's framework: *af priat harosh reshus* — even *priat harosh*, the question of head covering, should be treated as discretionary, meaning the *avel* may (and therefore must, per the Rosh) maintain the covering of his head on Shabbat. According to Rav, veiling the head is not so demonstrably a sign of *avelut* that it must be suspended. This places *priat harosh* in the same category as the other private practices, making it obligatory on Shabbat rather than something that must be reversed.

### Shmuel's Logic

The Gemara explains the reasoning behind Shmuel's more restrictive view. The key passage is this: Shmuel holds *kol kera she-eino b'sha'at chimum eino kera* — any rending of garments that does not occur at the time of emotional intensity is not a valid *keri'at habegadim* — and *kol atifa she-eina k'atifat Yishme'elim eina atifa* — any head wrapping that does not resemble the full Arab-style wrapping is not a genuine *atifa* for purposes of *avelut*. Rav Nachman demonstrates that this wrapping covers the face to the edge of the beard — encompassing the nose, mouth, and cheeks. Because Shmuel defines the *atifat harosh* of *avelut* as this complete and unmistakable veiling of the face, such an act is entirely unambiguous in its message. Nobody walks around with their face covered to the cheekbone unless they are an Arab or an *avel*. Shmuel therefore rules that this practice cannot be maintained on Shabbat, since it is an inherently public declaration of *avelut*.

Rav, by contrast, may understand the practice of *atifat harosh* as a more modest head covering — something that ordinary people might wear for various reasons — and therefore it lacks the demonstrative character that would prohibit it on Shabbat.

The *atifat Yishme'elim* concept, incidentally, carries implications beyond *avelut*. It intersects with discussions among the Geonim about the proper wearing of a *tallit*, since some hold that the biblical *mitzvat tzitzit* requires a garment worn in the full wrapping style — draped over the head and body in the manner of this complete *atifa*. The question of what constitutes a genuine *atifa* thus recurs in other halachic contexts.

As for practical application: the *Shulchan Aruch* rules that an *avel* is obligated in *atifat harosh*, which it describes as covering the head with a cloak or cloth and drawing part of it over the mouth and top of the nose. This is to be maintained at all times, but when visitors arrive for *nichum avelim*, the *avel* uncovers his head in their honor. The Rema, however, notes that in Ashkenazic communities the custom of *atifa* is not practiced at all, and that this is the established *minhag*. Tosafot had noted that performing such a wrapping in their communities would appear bizarre or even ridiculous in the eyes of the surrounding population — and so, like the practice of overturning the beds, it gradually fell away because it lost its communicative force or became practically absurd.

One might ask how the logic of *mochiacha alav* — one practice revealing or concealing the *avel's* status — operates across the full system. The Gemara states that if the *avel* is wearing shoes, then his shoes make his status evident and counteract any inference from his covered head: *minalav mochiach alav*. Tosafot is puzzled by this: if wearing shoes reveals his status, then by the same logic, any one normal behavior — upright beds, uncovered face — should reveal his status and allow all other *avelut* practices to proceed. Tosafot tentatively suggests that shoes carry a particularly strong indicative force — *hocha'a tfei* — but acknowledges the difficulty. In any case, this reasoning has no practical echo in the *halacha* as it developed.

The question of shoes is itself nuanced. In principle the prohibition is *ne'ilat hasandal*, and the standard *psak* is limited to leather shoes. The source for not wearing shoes includes a *pasuk* that speaks of going barefoot, which some authorities read as demanding literal barefoot-going rather than merely the avoidance of leather. This may explain why some are more stringent about shoes during *avelut* than on *Tisha B'Av* or *Yom Kippur*, where sneakers are widely accepted.

### Shmuel's Logic

Returning briefly to the structure of the *pachas/neter* framework: the Gemara presents a statement from Rabbi Yochanan that qualifies the rule about not wearing shoes. *Lo shanu ela she-ein minalim b'raglav* — the exemption from demonstrating *avelut* through bare feet applies only when the *avel* is in fact barefoot. *Aval yesh minalim b'raglav, minalav mochiach alav* — if he is wearing shoes, the shoes themselves make clear that he is not observing *avelut*, which then perhaps permits him to engage in other visible expressions of *avelut* since the shoes are already announcing his non-*avel* status. As noted, Tosafot finds this logic difficult to contain within a coherent system.

### Kol Kera Sha'at Chimum

Before returning to the full discussion of *devarim she-b'tzina*, the Gemara introduces the principle that underpins Shmuel's position on *atifat harosh*: *kol kera she-eino b'sha'at chimum eino kera* — any rending of garments that does not occur at the moment of heightened emotional intensity is not a valid rending. This is stated here in a parenthetical way, but the Gemara will return to it extensively in the next section. The core idea is that the *kriah* must happen in the context of acute emotional response — ideally at the moment of death itself, or in its immediate aftermath. This cannot be policed by reference to an individual's inner state; instead it is framed objectively as a question of timing and context.

## Rending Garments

### Shmuel & Rabbi Yochanan

The Gemara returns to the principle of *sha'at chimum* through two striking stories. The rabbis told Shmuel that Rav had died. Shmuel rent twelve garments and declared: *azal gavra de-hava mistafina minei* — the man of whom I was in awe has gone. Similarly, the rabbis told Rabbi Yochanan that Rabbi Chanina had died. Rabbi Yochanan rent thirteen garments of silk and made the same declaration. In both cases, the rending took place upon hearing the news — not necessarily in the immediate aftermath of the death itself. The Gemara apparently assumes that if the *kriah* is not performed at the actual time of death or burial, it falls outside the objective window of *sha'at chimum*, even if subjectively the person is hearing the news for the first time.

### Rabbis' Exception

The Gemara resolves the tension: *shani rabbanan de-keivan de-chol sha'ata midkri shma'teta* — rabbis are different, because at every moment they are engaged with Torah, and in engaging with their Torah they are also, at every moment, aware of the absence of the colleague they have lost — the one who would challenge them, dispute with them, sharpen their thinking. Therefore, for a Torah scholar mourning a fellow scholar, *u'ke-sha'at chimum dami* — it is as if the emotional intensity of the moment of death is perpetually present.

### Tosafot: Subjective

Tosafot extends this logic significantly. He writes: *ve-li nireh de-ve-chol sha'ah she-yuchal le-hitchamem al meto afilu zman merubeh achar mitato karei shapir bi-sh'at chimum* — in my view, any time that a person can genuinely become emotionally overwrought over the deceased — even considerably after the death — that qualifies as *sha'at chimum*. The case of the rabbis, whose ongoing Torah learning keeps them in perpetual *chimum*, is one illustration of this principle, but the principle itself is broader and essentially subjective: wherever genuine emotional intensity is present and can be genuinely aroused, *kriah* can be performed.

One implication of this analysis: if *kriah* is not performed within the proper *sha'at chimum*, there is no ongoing *chiyuv* to perform it. The question then arises whether rending a garment at an improper time would constitute *bal tashchit* — wanton destruction of property. Most would say yes, since if there is no *chiyuv*, the rending accomplishes nothing and simply ruins a garment.

### Switching Garments

The Gemara cites a *braita*: during the entire *shiva*, the *avel* wears the rent portion of the garment in front, where it is visible. If he wishes to change garments during the week, he must change and rend the new garment as well. On Shabbat, however, he turns the garment around so the rent is behind him — concealed. If on Shabbat he wishes to change garments, he may do so and need not rend the new one.

The difficulty is apparent: how can re-rending during the *shiva* be obligatory if it is not *sha'at chimum*? The Gemara answers: *ki tanya hahi bichvod aviv ve'imo* — that rule applies only in the case of one's parents. For parents, the obligation to wear the rent garment throughout the *shiva* — and to re-rend whenever changing garments — is an expression of *kvod aviv ve'imo* that operates independently of the *sha'at chimum* requirement. It is not an act of *kriah* per se, but an ongoing display of honor and mourning that must be maintained. For other relatives, there is no such obligation, and changing garments during the week does not require a new *kriah*. This is indeed the operative *halacha*.

### Sewing Torn Garments

A further question: may these additional rendings — those done not at *sha'at chimum* but for *kvod aviv ve'imo* — be sewn up after the *shiva*? The father of Rabbi Oshaya and Bar Kappara debate the point. One holds *ein mis'achin* — they cannot be sewn; even though the rending was not at *sha'at chimum*, since it was halachically required it carries the full status of *kriah* and cannot be repaired. The other holds *mis'achin* — they may be sewn, since their status is categorically distinct from the original *sha'at chimum* rending. The Gemara attempts to establish which of the two was the father of Rabbi Oshaya based on what his son Rabbi Oshaya ruled, but the conclusion is uncertain: Rabbi Oshaya may have learned his ruling from his *rebbe* Bar Kappara rather than from his father.

## Private Mourning

### Rava's Teaching

Returning to the broader question of *devarim she-b'tzina* on Shabbat, the Gemara cites Rava: *avel metayel be'unkali betoch beiso* — an *avel* may walk about in his house in a certain kind of garment, and by context, in a rent garment on Shabbat. This introduces the spatial dimension: is *devarim she-b'tzina* defined by the nature of the act, or by the setting in which it occurs? Is something private because it is intrinsically invisible to others, or because it happens to take place where no one is looking?

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye found his *rebbe* Rav Yosef walking around his house on Shabbat with a *sudar* — a cloth — draped over his head in the manner of *atifat harosh*. Abaye challenged him: does the master not hold that there is no *avelut* on Shabbat? Rav Yosef responded that Rabbi Yochanan had said *devarim she-b'tzina noheg* — private practices are observed. The force of the word *noheg* — are practiced, are followed — supports the Rosh's reading that these are obligations, not merely permissions. And yet the question remains: what made this veiling *devarim she-b'tzina*? Was it because Rav Yosef was in his house with no one observing him? Or because the particular kind of head covering he was using was not the full *atifat Yishme'elim* and therefore was not, by its nature, a demonstrative act of *avelut*?

### Tosafot's Doubt

Tosafot raises this as an explicit unresolved question: *mesapka lei* — he is in doubt. The passage about Rava walking in his house in rent garments seems to suggest that being *betoch beiso* — within one's house — is itself the determining factor for *devarim she-b'tzina*. If so, any *avelut* practice done in the privacy of one's home, when no one is watching, would be permitted or even obligatory. Tosafot leaves the question open: does *devarim she-b'tzina* refer to the intrinsic character of the act — its capacity to announce *avelut* to observers — or to the actual absence of observers?

### Modern Practice

The practical conclusion, as the *Shulchan Aruch* formulates it, is that *devarim she-b'tzina* in contemporary practice is limited to *tashmish hamitah* and *rechitza* — bathing. Shoes are classified as *parhesia* in our day because of changed social conventions. The question of not learning Torah is raised as a possible instance of *devarim she-b'tzina*, since a person's failure to learn on a given occasion is not self-evidently an expression of mourning. There is also the fascinating ruling — cited from the Rosh, whose source is *Rabbeinu Tam* — that if an *avel* is regularly called to *aliyot* at a particular position every Shabbat, and he fails to receive that *aliya* while in *avelut*, this constitutes *devarim she-b'farhesia* — a public declaration of his status — and must be avoided. Rabbeinu Tam himself, who habitually received *shlishi* each Shabbat, was once in *avelut* when the *chazzan* did not call him; he arose and took the *aliya* on his own initiative, reasoning that his conspicuous absence from his customary spot would itself have been the *parhesia*. The practice of *maavir sedra* — reviewing the weekly *parasha* twice with *targum* — may similarly be continued, since it resembles the recitation of *Keri'at Shema* and similar obligations.

In the end, the questions around *reshut* — whether the Gemara's language denotes genuine discretion or simply the absence of a Shabbat prohibition — and around the spatial vs. intrinsic definition of *tzina* — are largely left without a clean resolution in the text. The *halacha* as we have it, following the Rosh, collapses the middle ground: what Shabbat does not forbid, *avelut* obligates. But the plain language of the Gemara, and Tosafot's explicit *safek*, leave traces of a more nuanced and perhaps more open-ended original discussion.

## Mourning Children

### Rabbi Eliezer

The *daf* turns to a *mishna* that asks which festivals interrupt *avelut* and count as *regalim* for this purpose. Rabbi Eliezer holds that since the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, *Shavuot* has the status only of Shabbat — it interrupts *avelut* for the day but does not carry the weight of a full *regel*. The reasoning is that *Shavuot* derives much of its distinctive character from the *avodah* in the *Mikdash*, and in the absence of the *Mikdash* it has comparatively little ritual content beyond the prohibition of *melacha*. One might also note the absence of any *tashlumin* structure spanning seven days.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel disagrees and holds that *Shavuot* — and indeed *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur* — all carry the status of full *regalim* that are *mafsikin* the *avelut*. This is striking in the case of *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur*, which are not primarily associated with *simcha*. Nevertheless, Rabban Gamliel extends the interrupting force of *regalim* to encompass them. Rav Gidal bar Menashia cites Shmuel as ruling: *halacha k'Rabban Gamliel* — we rule in accordance with Rabban Gamliel.

### Under 30 Days

The *mishna* continues with age-based gradations in burial and mourning practices for young children. A child who dies within thirty days is not carried out in a coffin but is brought against the chest — *be'cheik*. The burial takes place with one woman and two men, but not one man and two women due to concerns of *yichud*. Abba Shaul permits even one man and two women when the circumstances require it. Furthermore, one does not form the row of consolers after the burial — the formal post-burial comforting ritual — and neither the *birkat avelim* nor the *tanchumei avelim* are recited. The underlying principle is that a child who has not survived thirty days has not yet acquired *chezkat chayim* — a halachic presumption of viability — and in the conditions of the ancient world especially, such a child was treated halachically as akin to a *nefel*, a miscarriage.

### Tosafot: Viability

Tosafot introduces an important distinction. Rashi had explained the thirty-day rule as applying specifically to a child who had not been carried to full term — whose months were incomplete — and for whom viability was therefore not established. Tosafot disputes this and separates two distinct questions. The thirty-day threshold and its attendant burial practices concern the *burial context* specifically, not the *shiva* context. Therefore, a child born at full term who dies within the first month, while the reduced burial formalities may still apply, would nonetheless obligate *shiva* observance — since a full-term birth establishes the child's viability, and there is *avelut* even if the burial is conducted more quietly. The different frameworks — one governing the scope of community mourning and burial rites, the other governing the parents' personal *avelut* obligations — need not align perfectly. Tosafot also notes a distinction in language: the degree of mourning demanded of the community is framed in terms of *tza'aro* — anguish over the child — while more elaborate burial honors are framed as *lichvodo* — honors due to the deceased — and these two considerations operate on different scales.

### 30 Days to 3 Years

Once a child has survived thirty days, the burial becomes more formal. He is carried out in a small coffin — *dlusk'ma*. Rabbi Yehuda specifies that this coffin must be one that cannot be carried on a single shoulder but must be carried in the arms — *b'agapayim*, according to Rabbeinu Gershom, by two people. The row of consolers is formed, and the *birkat avelim* and *tanchumei avelim* are recited. Once a child has reached one year — or, according to Rabbi Akiva, the age of two years, or the size of a two-year-old — he is carried on a full funeral bier with a full coffin. Tosafot notes that in another version of the text, the *tanna kamma* required three years before the full funeral bier, with Rabbi Akiva extending the threshold downward to two years or the equivalent size.

### Community Recognition

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar adds that once a child has reached the stage where he is carried on a full bier, *rabim mit'atzvim alav* — the community gathers in collective mourning. Rabbeinu Gershom explains this as *margishim u'mitza'arim* — communal awareness and shared grief. Rabbi Eliezer ben Azaria formulates the principle most clearly: *nikar l'rabim* — once a child is known in the community, *rabim mis'askin imo* — the community becomes actively involved in his burial and mourning. The converse follows: *eino nikar l'rabim, ein rabim mis'askin imo* — a child who has not yet made any mark in the community, who has not yet been seen in the streets or recognized by neighbors, does not draw communal participation. This is not a comment on the parents' grief, which may be profound regardless of the child's age. It is a recognition that communal mourning responds to communal presence and communal bonds.

### Eulogies by Class

The *mishna* then addresses the question of *hesped* — eulogy — for young children: *aniyim b'nei shalosh, ashirim b'nei chamesh* — children of the poor are eulogized beginning at age three, children of the wealthy beginning at age five. Rabbi Yehuda citing Rabbi Yishmael says: children of the poor at five, children of the wealthy at six. Rabbeinu Gershom explains the distinction between rich and poor by noting that a poor person's suffering over his children is greater, since he has fewer other sources of joy or consolation in life. One might alternatively have thought the distinction relates to visibility — wealthy families can afford to shelter their children more at home, while poor children are sooner out in public and thus sooner recognized by the community — but the formulation of *lefi she-ein lo simcha acheres* emphasizes the depth of parental suffering.

### Ben Zekunim

The *mishna* concludes with a further ruling: children of elderly parents — *bnei zekunim* — are treated like children of the poor, meriting earlier *hesped*. The reasoning parallels Rabbeinu Gershom's approach: parents who finally had a child in their old age — the classic *ben zekunim* — suffer the loss with a particular intensity that warrants treating it on par with the greater suffering attributed to poor families.

On the attribution of the halachic ruling: Rav Gidal bar Menashia cites Rav as ruling *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda she'amar mishum Rabbi Yishmael* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda citing Rabbi Yishmael on the question of eulogies for children of rich and poor families. The Gemara then clarifies an earlier potential confusion: when Rav Gidal bar Menashia had cited Shmuel as ruling *halacha k'Rabban Gamliel*, that ruling did not pertain to the status of *regalim* but to these laws of mourning practices and communal participation in the burial of young children. The two rulings — one in the name of Shmuel regarding Rabban Gamliel, and one in the name of Rav regarding Rabbi Yehuda citing Rabbi Yishmael — belong to different halachic questions and must not be conflated. The daf concludes here; the definitive *psak halacha* regarding the *regalim* that interrupt *avelut* will be completed in the following discussion.
