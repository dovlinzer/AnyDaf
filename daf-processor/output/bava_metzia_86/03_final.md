# Bava Metzia 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Resh Lakish & R. Chiya

### Caves & Lament

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה מְצַיֵּין מְעָרָתָא דְּרַבָּנַן, כִּי מְטָא לִמְעָרְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אִיעֲלַמָא מִינֵּיהּ. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! לֹא פִּלְפַּלְתִּי תּוֹרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ? יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: תּוֹרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ פִּלְפַּלְתָּ, תּוֹרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ לֹא רִיבַּצְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish was demarcating** burial **caves of the Sages. When he arrived at the cave of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** the precise location of his grave **eluded him.** Reish Lakish **became distressed,** as he was apparently unworthy of finding the grave. **He said: Master of the Universe! Did I not analyze the Torah like** Rabbi Ḥiyya? **A Divine Voice emerged and said to him: You did analyze the Torah like him,** but **you did not disseminate Torah like him.**

The *daf* opens with a striking account of Resh Lakish engaged in the practice of marking the caves of the *rabbanan* — presumably so that people would know to avoid them for reasons of ritual impurity, or perhaps reflecting an early custom of visiting such sites in prayer. When he arrived at the cave of Rabbi Chiya, the location was hidden from him: he was right there, yet he could not find it. *Chalash da'atei* — he became despondent. He called out: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, was I not as analytically sharp in *Torah* — was my *pilpul* not as great as his?

### Heavenly Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: תּוֹרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ פִּלְפַּלְתָּ, תּוֹרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ לֹא רִיבַּצְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A Divine Voice emerged and said to him: You did analyze the Torah like him,** but **you did not disseminate Torah like him.**

A *bat kol* responded: *Torah kamoto pilpalta* — yes, in your analytical prowess and depth of understanding, you were his equal. But *Torah kamoto lo ravatzta* — you did not spread *Torah* as he did. This response draws us back to a theme that will recur throughout the *daf*: the distinction between intellectual greatness and *ma'asim tovim* — the actual work of disseminating *Torah* throughout *Klal Yisrael*.

### R. Chanina & R. Chiya De…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲווֹ מִינְּצוּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: בַּהֲדֵי דִּידִי קָא מִינְּצֵית? חַס וְחָלִילָה, אִי מִשְׁתַּכְחָא תּוֹרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל – מַהְדַּרְנָא לַהּ מִפִּילְפּוּלַי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בַּהֲדֵי דִּידִי קָא מִינְּצֵית? דַּעֲבַדִי לְתוֹרָה דְּלֹא תִּשְׁתַּכַּח מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Ḥanina and Rabbi Ḥiyya would debate** matters of Torah, **Rabbi Ḥanina** would **say to Rabbi Ḥiyya:** Do you think **you can debate with me? Heaven forbid! If the Torah were forgotten from the Jewish people, I** could **restore it with my** powers of **analysis** and intellectual acumen. **Rabbi Ḥiyya said to Rabbi Ḥanina:** Do you think **you can debate with me?** You cannot compare yourself to me, **as I am acting** to ensure that **the Torah** will **not be forgotten by the Jewish people.**

This tension is given its sharpest expression in the dispute between Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Chiya. When they would argue, Rabbi Chanina would say to Rabbi Chiya: *bahadi didi menatzat*? You think you can contend with me? *Chas v'shalom* if *Torah* were ever forgotten from the Jewish people, *mehadreina lah mipilpuli* — I would be able to restore it through my great analytical ability, deriving everything from first principles. Rabbi Chiya responded: and you think *you* can contend with *me*? For *d'avdi l'Torah d'lo tishtakach m'Yisrael* — I have already acted to ensure it will never be forgotten in the first place. And what exactly did he do?

## R. Chiya's System

### Flax to Scrolls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עָבֵידְנָא – אָזֵילְנָא וְשָׁדֵינָא כִּיתָּנָא וְגָדֵילְנָא נִישְׁבֵּי, וְצָיֵידְנָא טְבֵי וּמַאֲכֵילְנָא בִּשְׂרַיְיהוּ לְיַתְמֵי, וְאָרֵיכְנָא מְגִילָּתָא וְכָתֵבְנָא חֲמִשָּׁה חוּמְשֵׁי, וְסָלֵיקְנָא לְמָתָא וּמַקְרֵינָא חַמְשָׁה יָנוֹקֵי בְּחַמְשָׁה חוּמְשֵׁי, וּמַתְנֵינָא שִׁיתָּא יָנוֹקֵי שִׁיתָּא סִדְרֵי, וְאָמַרְנָא לְהוּ: עַד דְּהָדַרְנָא וְאָתֵינָא אַקְרוֹ אַהֲדָדֵי וְאַתְנוֹ אַהֲדָדֵי וַעֲבַדִי לַהּ לְתוֹרָה דְּלֹא תִּשְׁתַּכַּח מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya elaborated: **What do I do** to this end? **I go and sow flax** seeds **and twine nets** with the flax, **and** then **I hunt deer and feed their meat to orphans.** Next **I prepare parchment** from their hides **and I write the five books** of the Torah on them. **I go to a city and teach five children the five books,** one book per child, **and I teach six** other **children the six orders** of the Mishna, **and I say to them: Until I return and come** here, **read each other** the Torah **and teach each other** the Mishna. This is how **I act** to ensure that **the Torah will not be forgotten by the Jewish people.**

Rabbi Chiya describes a remarkable self-sustaining system. *Azlina v'shadina kitna v'gadlina nishbei* — I went and planted flaxseeds, grew them, and fashioned from them nets. *V'tzaidna tavi* — I used those nets to catch deer. I slaughtered the deer and fed their meat to orphans, *l'shem shamayim* at every stage of the process. From the hides of the deer, *v'arichna megilta* — I prepared scrolls. *V'katavna chamisha chumshei* — and upon those scrolls I wrote each of the five books of the *Torah*.

### Teaching Model

Then *v'salikna l'mata* — I went to a city, and *umakrina chamisha yenukei b'chamisha chumshei* — I paired five children, each one to learn one of the five books of the *Chumash*, one *Bereishit*, one *Shemot*, and so on. *Umathnina shisha yenukei shisha sidrei* — and I took six children and taught each one of them one of the six orders of *Mishnah* — oral, of course, since the *Mishnah* was *b'al peh*.

### Learner & Teacher

Then he said to them: *ad d'dadra v'ata* — while I am away, *akru ahadadi v'atnu ahadadi* — teach each other. The child who knows *Bereishit* should teach all the others *Bereishit*; the one who knows *Zeraim* should teach everyone *Zeraim*. And in this way, *d'avdi lah l'Torah d'lo tishtakach m'Yisrael* — I ensured that *Torah* would not be forgotten from Israel.

From a modern perspective, this represents something like a truly sustainable system — not merely a generous donation or a one-time act of scholarship, but an entire self-perpetuating structure, built from the ground up: from planting flax to producing scrolls to training teachers. And notice the pedagogical principle at work. This was not an opportunity to display analytical brilliance, the *guf pilpul* that the *Gemara* references so often. This was the hard, patient work of teaching basic text — memorization of *Chumash* and *Mishnah* — to children. As the Maharsha explains, Rabbi Chiya made every child simultaneously a *lomeid* and a *melameid*, a learner and a teacher. Each child had ownership over a portion of the tradition, was empowered to transmit it, and was not merely a passive recipient. The contrast with Rabbi Chanina's remark is pointed: Rabbi Chanina says, in effect, *if* Torah is forgotten, I could restore it — which almost implies a wish to demonstrate his own greatness. Rabbi Chiya's response is: it is not about you; it is about what you actually do for *Klal Yisrael*.

### Affirmation & Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים מַעֲשֵׂי חִיָּיא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲפִילּוּ מִמָּר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲפִילּוּ מֵאַבָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַס וְחָלִילָה! לֹא תְּהֵא כְּזֹאת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **This is what Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: How great are the deeds of** Rabbi **Ḥiyya! Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Are his deeds **even greater than the Master's,** i.e., yours? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Yes.** Rabbi Yishmael persisted: Are they **even** greater **than** those of my **father,** Rabbi Yosei? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Heaven forbid! Such** a statement **shall not be** heard **among the Jewish people,** that someone is greater than your father, Rabbi Yosei.

This is precisely the context for understanding the next statement. *V'hainu d'amar Rebbi: gedoli ma'aseh Chiya* — and this is what Rebbe meant when he said, great are the deeds of Chiya. The emphasis falls on *deeds* — not merely on intellectual stature. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi then asked: was he even greater than you, Rebbe? Rebbe said: yes. Was he even greater than *Abba* — than your father? Here Rebbe said: *chas v'shalom lo tehei kazos b'Yisrael* — God forbid, one should never say such a thing. The reference, as Rashi clarifies, is to Rabbi Yishmael's own father, Rabbi Yosi. It is worth noting the interesting resonance here: Rebbe's own father was the one who had demonstrated great *anavah*, calling himself *chometz ben yayin* in relation to Rebbe — yet even that comparison would not be made with Rabbi Chiya.

## Heavenly Visions

### Dream Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֶמֶשׁ נִרְאָה לִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: אֵצֶל מִי אַתָּה תָּקוּעַ? אָמַר לִי: אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֵצֶל מִי? אֵצֶל רַבִּי יַנַּאי. וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אֵצֶל מִי? אֵצֶל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא. וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אֵצֶל מִי? אֵצֶל רַבִּי חִיָּיא. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֵצֶל רַבִּי חִיָּיא לֹא? אָמַר לִי: בַּאֲתַר דְּזִקּוּקִין דְּנוּרָא וּבָעוֹרִין דְּאֶשָּׁא – מַאן מְעַיֵּיל בַּר נַפָּחָא לְתַמָּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Last night, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, appeared to me** in a dream. **I said to him: Near whom are you placed** in the upper realms? **He said to me: Near Rabbi Yoḥanan.** I asked: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan is near whom?** He replied: **Near Rabbi Yannai. And Rabbi Yannai is near whom? Near Rabbi Ḥanina. And Rabbi Ḥanina is near whom? Near Rabbi Ḥiyya.** Rabbi Zeira added: **I said to** Rabbi Yosei: **But isn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** worthy of being placed **near Rabbi Ḥiyya? He said to me: In a place of fiery sparks and burning fires, who can bring** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **son of Nappaḥa, there?**

The *Gemara* shifts to a series of remarkable visions. Rabbi Zeira reports: *emesh chezi li Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina* — last night Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina appeared to me, presumably in a dream. I asked him: *eitzel mi ata takua* — by whom are you situated up there in *Olam Haba*, who is your companion? He replied: by Rabbi Yochanan. And Rabbi Yochanan is by whom? By Rabbi Yannai. And Rabbi Yannai by whom? By Rabbi Chanina — the same Rabbi Chanina who had just debated Rabbi Chiya. And Rabbi Chanina by whom? By Rabbi Chiya. So the hierarchy in *Gan Eden* places Rabbi Chiya at the highest level.

### Bar Nafcha Issue

Rabbi Zeira then asks: but why is Rabbi Yochanan not situated directly beside Rabbi Chiya? Was he not great enough? The answer he receives is: *ba'atar d'zikikin d'nura u've'urin d'esha* — in a place where fire blazes and flames shoot out, *man ma'ayei bar nafcha letaman* — how can you bring the son of the smelter there? The Maharsha explains that *bar nafcha* refers to one who works the bellows, fanning the flames — precisely the wrong person to bring into a place already ablaze. Rabbi Yochanan himself bore the name *bar nafcha* — whether from his father's name or his profession — and the *Gemara* seems to be saying that the extraordinary heat generated between Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Chanina in their learning required a certain distance. It does not diminish Rabbi Yochanan's stature, but simply indicates that the two needed their space.

### Chariot Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חֲבִיבָא: אִשְׁתַּעִי לִי רַב חֲבִיבָא בַּר סוּרְמָקֵי, חֲזֵי לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן דַּהֲוָה שְׁכִיחַ אֵלִיָּהוּ גַּבֵּיהּ, דִּלְצַפְרָא הֲווֹ שַׁפִּירָן עֵינֵיהּ, וּלְאוּרְתָּא דָּמְיָין כִּדְמִיקַּלְיָן בְּנוּרָא. אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? וַאֲמַר לִי: דַּאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ לְאֵלִיָּהוּ: אַחְוִי לִי רַבָּנַן כִּי סָלְקִי לִמְתִיבְתָּא דְּרָקִיעַ. אָמַר לִי: בְּכוּלְּהוּ מָצֵית אִסְתַּכּוֹלֵי בְּהוּ, לְבַר מִגּוּהַרְקָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּלָא תִּסְתַּכֵּל בֵּיהּ. מַאי סִימָנַיְיהוּ? בְּכוּלְּהוּ אָזְלִי מַלְאֲכֵי כִּי סָלְקִי וְנָחֲתִי, לְבַר מִגּוּהַרְקָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּמִנַּפְשֵׁיהּ סָלֵיק וּנְחֵית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥaviva said: Rav Ḥaviva bar Surmakei told me: I** once **saw one of the Sages whom Elijah** the prophet **would visit,** and **his eyes** looked **beautiful** and healthy **in the morning, but appeared to be charred by fire in the evening. I said to him: What is this** phenomenon? **And he said to me: I said to Elijah: Show me the Sages upon their ascension to the heavenly academy.** Elijah **said to me: You may gaze at all of them except for** those in **the chariot [*miguharka*] of Rabbi Ḥiyya, upon whom you may not gaze.** I asked Elijah: **What are the signs** of Rabbi Ḥiyya's chariot, so I will know when not to look? He said: **Angels accompany all** of the other Sages' chariots **as they ascend and descend, except for the chariot of Rabbi Ḥiyya, which ascends and descends of its own accord,** due to his greatness.

Rabbi Chaviva then relates what Rabbi Chaviva bar Surmti told him. There was one of the *rabbanan* who regularly received visits from Eliyahu HaNavi, and one morning his eyes appeared healthy and clear; by evening, *damyan k'nimiklin benura* — they looked as if they had been scorched by fire. When asked what happened, he explained: I had asked Eliyahu to show me the *rabbanan* as they ascend to the heavenly academy, the *mesivta d'rakiya*. Eliyahu told him: *b'chulu matzei at l'istakulei b'hu* — you may look at all of them, *lebar mi'geharkei d'Rabbi Chiya* — except for the chariot of Rabbi Chiya: *d'lo tistaklei bei* — you must not look at it as it ascends.

One might ask: how will I know which chariot is his, in order to avoid looking? Eliyahu explained: for all the other sages, *azlei malachei ki salkei ve'nachetei* — angels escort their chariots up and down, providing an accompaniment. But the chariot of Rabbi Chiya, *d'minafshei salkei ve'nachetei* — rises and descends entirely on its own, through the power of Rabbi Chiya himself, without any angelic escort.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מְצַאי לְאוֹקְמָא אַנַּפְשַׁאי, אִסְתַּכַּלִי בָּהּ: אֲתוֹ תְּרֵי בּוּטִיטֵי דְנוּרָא וּמַחְיוּהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא וְסַמּוֹנְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ. לִמְחַר אֲזַלִי אִשְׁתַּטַּחִי אַמְּעָרְתֵּיהּ, אָמֵינָא: מַתְנְיָיתָא דְּמָר מַתְנֵינָא, וְאִתַּסַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **I was unable to restrain myself,** and **I gazed upon** Rabbi Ḥiyya's chariot. **Two fiery flames came and struck that man,** i.e., me, **and blinded his eyes. The next day, I went and prostrated on** Rabbi Ḥiyya's burial **cave** in supplication. **I said: I study the *baraitot* of the Master,** Rabbi Ḥiyya; please pray on my behalf. **And** my vision **was healed,** but my eyes remained scorched.

*Lo matzei le'okmei anafshei* — he could not restrain himself. *Istaklei bei* — he looked. *Asu trei butiti d'nura* — two tongues of fire shot forth, *v'samyanu l'einai* — and they blinded his eyes.

### Cave Healing

The following day, he went and prostrated himself at Rabbi Chiya's cave. He pledged: *matnisa d'mar matnina* — it is your *baraitos* alone that I teach; I study and disseminate only your *Tosefta*, and no other *baraitos*. He appealed to Rabbi Chiya's merit, and *v'itsei* — he was healed.

This episode is worth pausing over. Rabbi Chiya was not the greatest *mipalpel ba'Torah* of his generation — that was Rabbi Chanina. Yet Rabbi Chiya held the highest position in *Gan Eden*, and his chariot ascended with its own power, generating fire of such intensity that merely glancing at it blinded a *talmid chacham*. This is the same dynamic we noted earlier: the one who was not the most brilliant intellectual — Rabbi Chiya, and similarly Rebbe himself — was nonetheless the one to whom we owe the entire *Torah she'be'al peh* as we have it. Rabbi Chiya organized and produced the *Tosefta*; Rebbe devoted himself to organizing the *Mishnah*. It is not always the most dazzling *pilpul* that leaves the deepest mark.

## Eliyahu & Patriarchs

### Eliyahu Delayed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלִיָּהוּ הֲוָה שְׁכִיחַ בִּמְתִיבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי, יוֹמָא חַד רֵישׁ יַרְחָא הֲוָה, נְגַהּ לֵיהּ וְלָא אֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא נְגַהּ לֵיהּ לְמָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּאוֹקֵימְנָא לְאַבְרָהָם וּמָשֵׁינָא יְדֵיהּ וּמְצַלֵּי וּמַגְנֵינָא לֵיהּ, וְכֵן לְיִצְחָק וְכֵן לְיַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Elijah was** often **found in the academy of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **One day it was a New Moon,** the first of the month, and Elijah **was delayed and did not come** to the academy. Later, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Elijah: **What is the reason** that **the Master was delayed?** Elijah **said to him: I** had **to wake up Abraham, wash his hands, and** wait for him to **pray, and** then **lay him down** again. **And similarly,** I followed the same procedure **for Isaac, and similarly for Jacob** in turn.

Eliyahu would periodically visit the *yeshiva* of Rebbe here on earth. One day — it was *Rosh Chodesh* — he arrived late. Rebbe asked: why were you delayed? Eliyahu explained: *ad d'mokeimna le-Avraham* — I had to wake up Avraham, *umashina yadei* — and wash his hands, *umatzli* — and then he prays, *umagneina lei* — and then I lay him back to sleep. And so too for Yitzchak, and so too for Yaakov. Whether this refers to their *neshamos* in *Shamayim* or to something more literal — perhaps a rousing of the Patriarchs as they lie in *Me'arat HaMachpelah* — the *Gemara* leaves somewhat open.

### Mashiach Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלוֹקְמִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי! סָבַרי: תָּקְפִי בְּרַחֲמֵי וּמַיְיתִי לֵיהּ לְמָשִׁיחַ בְּלָא זִמְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi asked: And let** the Master **wake them** all **together.** Elijah responded: **I maintain** that if I were to wake all three to pray at the same time, **they** would **generate powerful prayers and bring the Messiah prematurely.**

Rebbe then asked: and are there not comparable figures in this world today? Could you not wake all three of the Patriarchs at the same time so that they might pray together? Eliyahu replied: *savri d'nefishi berachamei* — the concern is that their combined prayers would be too powerful, *u'maisei lei le'mashiach belo zamnei* — and they would bring *Mashiach* before its proper time. Presumably the Patriarchs pray on *Rosh Chodesh* for the redemption — *Rosh Chodesh* being the great symbol of the waxing and waning of renewal — and waking them simultaneously would unleash a power that heaven was not yet ready to absorb.

### Fast for Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְיֵשׁ דּוּגְמָתָן בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיכָּא רַבִּי חִיָּיא וּבָנָיו. גְּזַר רַבִּי תַּעֲנִיתָא, אַחְתִינְהוּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא וּבָנָיו. אֲמַר ״מַשִּׁיב הָרוּחַ״ – וּנְשַׁבָה זִיקָא. אֲמַר ״מוֹרִיד הַגֶּשֶׁם״ – וַאֲתָא מִיטְרָא, כִּי מְטָא לְמֵימַר ״מְחַיֵּה הַמֵּתִים״ – רְגַשׁ עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbe Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Elijah: **And is there anyone** alive **in this world** who is **comparable to them** and can produce such efficacious prayers? Elijah **said to him: There are Rabbi Ḥiyya and his sons. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **decreed a fast,** and the Sages **brought Rabbi Ḥiyya and his sons down** to the pulpit to pray on behalf of the congregation. Rabbi Ḥiyya **recited** the phrase in the *Amida* prayer: **Who makes the wind blow, and the wind blew.** Rabbi Ḥiyya **recited** the next phrase: **Who makes the rain fall, and rain fell. When he was about to say** the phrase: **Who revives the dead, the world trembled.**

Rebbe then asked: is there anyone in *this* world with comparable combined power of prayer? Eliyahu replied: *ika Rabbi Chiya u'vanav* — Rabbi Chiya and his sons. Their prayer together would be as powerful as the combined prayer of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov.

Armed with this knowledge, Rebbe immediately declared a *tanis*, organizing a communal fast with the intention of hastening the redemption. *Achtinu le'Rabbi Chiya u'vanav* — he appointed Rabbi Chiya and his sons as *shlichei tzibur*. The *Tosafos* at 86a notes that this was all three of them together — an unusual arrangement — and draws a parallel to the *Midrash* that in a *tanis tzibur* three people would descend before the *teivah*, as it is written regarding Moshe with Aharon and Chur.

### Eliyahu's Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בִּרְקִיעָא: מַאן גַּלִּי רָזַיָּא בְּעָלְמָא? אָמְרִי: אֵלִיָּהוּ. אַתְיוּהּ לְאֵלִיָּהוּ מַחְיוּהּ שִׁתִּין פּוּלְסֵי דְנוּרָא. אֲתָא אִידְּמִי לְהוּ כְּדוּבָּא דְנוּרָא, עָל בֵּינַיְיהוּ וְטַרְדִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said in heaven: Who is the revealer of secrets in the world? They said** in response: It is **Elijah. Elijah was brought** to heaven, whereupon **he was beaten with sixty fiery lashes.** Elijah **came** back down to earth **disguised as a bear of fire. He came among** the congregation **and distracted them** from their prayers, preventing Rabbi Ḥiyya from reciting the phrase: Who revives the dead.

*Amar mashiv ha'ruach* — Rabbi Chiya declared the blessing for wind, and the wind blew. *Amar morid ha'gashem* — he declared the blessing for rain, and the rain came. But Rebbe's goal was not merely the rain. *Ki mata le'meimar mechayeh meisim* — when Rabbi Chiya was about to say *mechayeh meisim*, the world shook — *ragash alma*. Up in heaven they asked: who revealed this secret to the world? They pointed to Eliyahu. *Aitu Eliyahu u'machiu shishin pulsei d'nura* — Eliyahu was brought forward and given sixty lashes of fire. Then — all of this apparently occurring in the instant before Rabbi Chiya reached the words *mechayeh meisim* — *asa idmi lei k'duva d'nura* — Eliyahu appeared among them like a bear of fire, *v'tardinhu* — and he scattered the congregation, breaking up the prayer before that blessing could be uttered. This was, it later emerges, why he had been late to Rebbe's *yeshiva*: the fire and the chaos of that episode were still the immediate backdrop.

## Samuel & Smicha

### Doctor Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל יַרְחִינָאָה אָסְיֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי הֲוָה. חֲלַשׁ רַבִּי בְּעֵינֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶימְלֵי לָךְ סַמָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָכֵילְנָא. אֶשְׁטַר לָךְ מִשְׁטָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָכֵילְנָא. הֲוָה מוֹתֵיב לֵיהּ בְּגוּבְתָּא דְסַמָּנֵי תּוּתֵי בֵּי סַדְיֵיהּ, וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel Yarḥina'a was the physician of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. One time, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **felt a pain in his eye.** Shmuel **said to him: I will place a medication in** your eye. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: I cannot** have the medication placed directly in my eye, as I am afraid it will cause me too much pain. Shmuel **said to him: I will apply a salve** above your eye, not directly in it. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him:** Even that **I cannot** bear. Shmuel **placed** the medication **in a tube of herbs beneath his pillow, and** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **was healed.**

The *Gemara* now turns to Shmuel Yarchina'ah — the Shmuel we know simply as Shmuel. Why *Yarchina'ah*? Rashi explains it derives from *yereach*, the moon, because Shmuel was a great astronomer who understood the calculation of *Rosh Chodesh* — an ironic echo of the *Rosh Chodesh* theme we just left. He was also Rebbe's personal physician, a man of remarkable worldly knowledge: astronomer, doctor, a kind of early Rambam. When Rebbe suffered a problem with his eye, Shmuel asked: may I apply a salve inside? Rebbe could not tolerate it. An ointment on top? That too. So *hava motiv lei b'guphsah d'samay tutei bei sadya* — Shmuel placed some type of medicated substance beneath the pillow or bed so that its healing fragrance would rise, and *v'itasi* — Rebbe was healed.

### Book Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה קָא מִצְטַעַר, רַבִּי לְמִסְמְכֵיהּ וְלָא הֲוָה מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא לִצְטַעַר מָר, לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי סִיפְרָא דְּאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: שְׁמוּאֵל יַרְחִינָאָה חַכִּים יִתְקְרֵי, וְרַבִּי לָא יִתְקְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **made efforts to ordain** Shmuel Yarḥina'a as a rabbi **but was unsuccessful,** as Shmuel always demurred. Shmuel Yarḥina'a **said to him: The Master should not be upset** about my refusal, as I know that I am not destined to be ordained as a rabbi. **I myself saw the book of Adam the first** man, which contains the genealogy of the human race, **and it is written in it** that **Shmuel Yarḥina'ah** shall **be called a wise [*ḥakim*]** physician, **but he shall not be called rabbi.**

Now Rebbe wished to grant Shmuel *smicha* — ordination. As Rashi explains, every time he attempted to gather the sages together, Shmuel was unavailable; and when Shmuel was present, the sages could not be assembled. *Velo hava mistayia milta* — it simply never worked out. Shmuel told Rebbe: do not be distressed on my account. *B'didi chazi li siphra d'Adam HaRishon* — I was shown the book of Adam HaRishon — which Rashi understands as the heavenly book in which all future generations are recorded before history unfolds. *Uchtiv bei*: it was written therein that *Shmuel Yarchina'ah chachami itkari, veRebbi lo yitkari* — Shmuel will be called the *chacham*, the wise one, but will never be called *Rabbi*. He would not receive *smicha*. Yet far from diminishing him, this did not prevent Shmuel from becoming one of the most quoted *Amoraim* in the *Gemara*.

### Wise in Monies

The Maharsha draws a striking connection. We *pasken* like Shmuel in *dinei mamonot* — monetary law — but not in *issur ve'heter* — ritual law. The word *chacham* tends to appear in the context of *dinei mamonot*, where a *chacham* is required for certain rulings. And *smicha*, with its formulation of *yoreh yoreh*, is the ordination to rule in *issur ve'heter*. The very title he receives in the heavenly book — *chacham* and not *Rabbi* — precisely parallels the halachic rule that we follow him specifically in monetary matters and not in ritual ones.

## Mishnah & Gemara Edit

### End of Tannaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי וְרַבִּי נָתָן – סוֹף מִשְׁנָה. רַב אָשֵׁי וְרָבִינָא – סוֹף הוֹרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabbi Natan** are **the end of the Mishna,** i.e., the last of the *tanna'im*, the redactors of the Mishna. **Rav Ashi and Ravina** are **the end of instruction,** i.e., the end of the period of the *amora'im*, the redacting of the Talmud, which occurred after the period of the *tanna'im*.

The *Gemara* now records another passage from that same heavenly book, applying to the broader sweep of rabbinic history: *Rebbi v'Rabbi Natan sof Mishnah* — Rebbe and Rabbi Natan are the end of the *Mishnah* period. *Rav Ashi v'Ravina sof hora'ah* — Rav Ashi and Ravina are the end of the period of *hora'ah*, of authoritative teaching.

### Rebbe's Organizing

The plain meaning might simply be that Rebbe and Rabbi Natan were the last of the *Tannaim*, and Rav Ashi and Ravina the last of the *Amoraim*. But Rashi reads these terms in a much more specific editorial sense. He explains: *ad yemeihem lo amru ish et devarav b'veit hamidrash hayu talmidim gorsim shemu'ah shemu'ah levadah* — until Rebbe's time, each sage would transmit his own individual tradition in the *beit midrash*, and *velo hayu masechtos sedurot* — there was no organized structure. Individual oral traditions existed in abundance, but they were scattered, with no consistent arrangement. *Vehen kalelu ha'amur b'doros shelifneihem v'sidru hamasechtos* — Rebbe and Rabbi Natan gathered all the material from prior generations and organized it into the tractates we know. Rashi notes that there is a small amount of Tannaic material that postdates Rebbe — for instance, the end of tractate *Sotah* states that after Rebbe died, *anavah* departed from the world, which must have been written after his death — but essentially the entire structure of the *Mishnah* is attributed to Rebbe, with Rabbi Natan playing an important supporting role whose name appears less often.

### Rav Ashi & Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסִימָנָךְ: ״עַד אָבוֹא אֶל מִקְדְּשֵׁי אֵל אָבִינָה לְאַחֲרִיתָם״. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, אִישְׁתַּעִי לִי רַב חָמָא בַּר בְּרַתֵּיה דְּחַסָּא: רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי אַגַּב שְׁמָדָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And your mnemonic** to remember that Rav Ashi and Ravina redacted the Talmud is the verse: **"Until I entered into the sanctuary [*mikdashei*] of God, and considered [*avina*] their end"** (Psalms 73:17). The sanctuary, *mikdashei*, alludes to Rav Ashi, while the term *avina* alludes to Ravina, which is a contraction of Rav Avina. The phrase: Their end, is interpreted as a reference to the redacting of the Talmud.

The role of Rav Ashi and Ravina is even more interesting, because it bears directly on how the *Gemara* itself was composed. Rashi writes: *ad yimeihem lo haysah Gemara al haseder* — until their time there was no organized *Gemara* associated with each *Mishnah* in sequence. When someone asked the reason for a particular *Mishnah*, or a halachic question arose in practice, a discussion would unfold — *kol echad v'echad omer ta'amo* — but it was entirely ad hoc, unconnected to any consistent structure. *V'Rav Ashi v'Ravina sidru shmu'os Amoraim shelifneihem v'kavu al seder masechtos* — Rav Ashi and Ravina organized all the Amoraic material that preceded them, assigned each discussion to its relevant *Mishnah*, and then did something further: *v'hikshu kushyos she'yeish l'hashiv u'feirukim she'reuyim l'taretz* — they added a final layer of questions and answers, the *kushyos* and *teirutzim*, and *kavu hakol ba'Gemara* — they fixed it all in the *Gemara* as we have it.

### Stam Authorship

Rashi goes further, enumerating the key terms — *eisevei*, *meisevei*, *raminhu*, *iba'ya l'hu* — and identifying these as the work of Rav Ashi and Ravina themselves. In other words, according to Rashi, Rav Ashi and Ravina are the authors of the *stam*, the anonymous voice of the *Gemara*.

This is a significant position that has not gone without challenge. Academic scholars, and indeed the *Igeres* of Rav Sherira Gaon, do not fully share this reading. Rav Sherira Gaon was famously asked how the *Mishnah* and *Gemara* were written, and in his response he describes not a sudden act of compilation but a layering process. He argues that the structure was always organized according to the order of *masechtos* and *mishnayos*, with each generation adding its contribution in sequence. What Rav Ashi and Ravina represented, in his telling, was the final halachic contribution — the last generation to introduce new *hora'ah* into the *Gemara*. The *Rabanan Savorai* who came after them continued to add material, but without introducing new halachic decisions, only further discussion and clarification. A well-known example is the opening *sugya* of tractate *Kiddushin*, which poses a series of grammatical and terminological questions — why *mekudeshes* here and *nikneis* there, *lashon zachar* versus *lashon nekevah* — without introducing any new halachic substance, drawing only on pre-existing material. Similarly at the beginning of *Bava Kamma*. These, according to Rav Sherira Gaon, represent the post-Rav Ashi layer. Whether the *stam* was entirely their creation, or whether it accumulated in stages across multiple generations, thus remains a live scholarly debate.

## Rabbah's Hiding

### Tax Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, אִישְׁתַּעִי לִי רַב חָמָא בַּר בְּרַתֵּיה דְּחַסָּא: רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי אַגַּב שְׁמָדָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אֲכַלוּ בֵּיהּ קוּרְצָא בֵּי מַלְכָּא, אֲמַרוּ: אִיכָּא חַד גַּבְרָא בִּיהוּדָאֵי דְּקָא מְבַטֵּל תְּרֵיסַר אַלְפֵי גַּבְרֵי מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל יַרְחָא בְּקַיְיטָא וְיַרְחָא בְּסִתְוָא מִכְּרָגָא דְּמַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: Rav Ḥama, son of the daughter of Ḥasa, told me** that **Rabba bar Naḥmani died due to** the fear of a decree of religious **persecution.** The Gemara explains: His enemies **accused him [*akhalu beih kurtza*]** of disloyalty **in the king's palace,** as they **said: There is one man from** among **the Jews who exempts twelve thousand Jewish men from the king's head tax** two months a year, **one month in the summer and one month in the winter.** Since many people would study in Rabba's study hall during the months of Adar and Elul, he was being blamed for preventing those people from working during those months.

The *Gemara* now moves to another great sage of the Amoraic period: Rabbah bar Nachmani. The word *Rabbah* in the *Gemara* without further qualification refers to Rabbah bar Nachmani. *Agav shmada d'nach nafshia* — because of a government decree, his death ultimately came about. The story begins with informers going to the royal court. *Ika chad gavra bi'huda'ei* — there is one man among the Jews, they said, who causes twelve thousand Jewish men to abandon their work and their tax obligations for one month in the summer and one month in the fall. These were the months of *Yarchei Kalla*, the periods before *Pesach* and *Sukkot* when masses of Jews would gather at the *yeshiva* to study intensively — and during those months, the royal tax could not be collected. *Mikarga demalka* — they were absenting themselves from the king's poll tax.

### Officer Healed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדַּרוּ פְּרֵיסְתְּקָא דְמַלְכָּא בָּתְרֵיהּ וְלָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. עֲרַק וַאֲזַל מִפּוּמְבְּדִיתָא לְאַקְרָא, מֵאַקְרָא לְאַגְמָא, וּמֵאַגְמָא לְשִׁחִין, וּמִשִּׁחִין לִצְרִיפָא, וּמִצְּרִיפָא לְעֵינָא דְמַיִם, וּמֵעֵינָא דְמַיִם לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא. בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. אִיקְּלַע פְּרֵיסְתְּקָא דְמַלְכָּא לְהָהוּא אוּשְׁפִּיזָא דְּרַבָּה. קָרִיבוּ תַּכָּא קַמֵּיהּ וְאַשְׁקוּהוּ תְּרֵי כָּסֵי, וְדַלְיוּהּ לְתַכָּא מִקַּמֵּיהּ הֲדַר פַּרְצוּפֵיהּ לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent a messenger [*peristaka*] of the king after him, but he** was **not** able to **find him.** Rabba bar Naḥmani **fled and went from Pumbedita to Akra, from Akra to Agma, from Agma to Shiḥin, from Shiḥin to Tzerifa, from Tzerifa to Eina Demayim, and from Eina Demayim** back **to Pumbedita.** Ultimately, **he was found in Pumbedita,** as **the king's messenger arrived** by chance **at that same inn where Rabba** bar Naḥmani was hiding. The inn attendants **placed a tray before** the messenger **and gave him two cups to drink. They** then **removed the tray from before him and his face was** miraculously **turned backward.**

A royal officer was dispatched to seize Rabbah. He could not find him. Rabbah fled from Pumbedita to one village, then another — *mi'pumbedita l'akra mi'akra l'agma mi'agma l'shchin mi'shchin l'tzrifa l'eina damim* — from location to location, evading capture. The officer happened to arrive at the same *ushpiza*, the same inn, where Rabbah was hiding. The innkeeper unwittingly served the officer two cups of wine across a meal — a problematic pairing, exposing him to the influence of *shedim*. The officer's face was afflicted and turned around. The innkeeper, alarmed, consulted Rabbah: this is a royal officer, what can we do? Rabbah instructed them: bring the plate back before him, serve him another cup — thus making it three cups at one continuous meal rather than the forbidden pairing of two — and remove the plate. *V'itsei* — he will be healed. They did so, and the officer recovered.

### Multiple Escapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי נַעֲבֵיד לֵיהּ? גַּבְרָא דְמַלְכָּא הוּא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: קָרִיבוּ תַּכָּא לְקַמֵּיהּ, וְאַשְׁקוּהּ חַד כָּסָא, וְדַלְיוּהּ לְתַכָּא מִקַּמֵּיהּ וְלִתַּסֵּי. עֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ הָכִי וְאִתַּסִּי. אֲמַר: מִידָּע יָדַעְנָא דְּגַבְרָא דְּקָא בָעֵינָא הָכָא הוּא. בְּחֵישׁ אַבָּתְרֵיהּ וְאַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. אֲמַר: אָזֵלְינָא מֵהָא, אִי מִקְטָל קָטְלוּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא – לָא מְגַלֵּינָא, וְאִי נַגֹּידֵי מְנַגְּדִין לֵיהּ – מְגַלֵּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The attendants **said to** Rabba bar Naḥmani: **What should we do with him? He is the king's man,** and we cannot leave him like this. Rabba bar Naḥmani **said to them: Place a tray before him and give him one cup to drink, and** then **remove the tray from before him and he will be healed. They did this, and he was healed.** The messenger **said: I am certain that the man I seek is here,** as this unnatural event must have befallen me on his account. **He searched for** Rabba bar Naḥmani **and found** out where he was. The messenger **said** that they should tell Rabba bar Naḥmani: **I will leave this** inn and will not disclose your location. Even **if they will kill that man,** i.e., me, **I will not disclose** your location. **But if they will beat him,** me, **I will disclose** your whereabouts, as I cannot bear being tortured.

The officer, now realizing that someone of great wisdom must be concealed in this inn, understood that the man he was seeking was present. *Meida yadana d'gavra d'beina hacha* — I know the man I am looking for is here. He found Rabbah, but out of gratitude for his healing, he told Rabbah: I will leave and report that I could not find you. If they execute me for this, *lo maglina* — I will not reveal your location. But if they torture me, *maglina* — then I cannot withstand it, and I will tell them. The candor is deeply human: torture can be worse than death. They brought Rabbah before the officer, placed him in a room, and locked the door. Rabbah prayed, *paka ashita* — the door burst open — and he fled again, returning to *Agma*, one of his earlier hiding places. He sat there on a felled palm tree and continued to learn.

### Purity Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא רַחֲמֵי, פְּרַק אֲשִׁיתָא עֲרַק וַאֲזַל לְאַגְמָא. הֲוָה יָתֵיב אַגִּירְדָּא דְּדִקּוּלָא וְקָא גָרֵיס. קָא מִיפַּלְגִי בִּמְתִיבְתָּא דִרְקִיעָא: אִם בַּהֶרֶת קוֹדֶמֶת לְשֵׂעָר לָבָן – טָמֵא, וְאִם שֵׂעָר לָבָן קוֹדֵם לַבַּהֶרֶת – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Naḥmani **prayed for mercy,** and **the wall crumbled. He fled and went to** hide in **a swamp. He was sitting on the stump of a palm** tree **and studying** Torah alone. At that moment, the Sages **in the heavenly academy were disagreeing** with regard to a *halakha* of leprosy. In general, a leprous spot includes two signs of impurity, a bright white spot and a white hair. The basic *halakha* is that **if** the **snow-white leprous sore [*baheret*] preceded the white hair** then the afflicted person is **ritually impure, but if the white hair preceded the *baheret*,** he is **pure.**

Meanwhile, a debate was unfolding in the heavenly court. The *Mishnah* in tractate *Nega'im* teaches that *tzara'at* depends on the sequence of symptoms: *im baheret kodemet l'se'ar lavan tamei* — if the white skin-spot preceded the white hair, the person is *tamei*; *v'im se'ar lavan kodem labaheret, tahor* — but if the white hair came first and the spot followed, the person is *tahor*. The question before the heavenly court was: what if it is a *safek* — a genuine doubt as to which appeared first?

### Heavenly Call

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אוֹמֵר: טָהוֹר, וְכוּלְּהוּ מְתִיבְתָּא דִרְקִיעָא אָמְרִי: טָמֵא. וְאָמְרִי: מַאן נוֹכַח? נוֹכַח רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי: אֲנִי יָחִיד בִּנְגָעִים, אֲנִי יָחִיד בְּאֹהָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The heavenly debate concerned a case of **uncertainty** as to which came first, the spot or the hair. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says:** The individual is **pure, but every** other member of **the heavenly academy says:** He is **impure. And they said: Who can arbitrate** in this dispute? They agreed that **Rabba bar Naḥmani** should **arbitrate, as Rabba bar Naḥmani** once **said: I am preeminent in** the *halakhot* of **leprosy** and **I am preeminent in** the *halakhot* of ritual impurity imparted by **tents.**

The Holy One, Blessed be He, ruled *tahor*. The entire heavenly *mesivta d'rakiya* ruled *tamei*. One might ask: is there ever doubt before heaven? But perhaps the point is that God, who perceives the full truth, weighs the doubt in favor of *tahor*, while the human condition — and the human halachic process — must deal with uncertainty in the direction of stringency, and thus *tamei*. The heavenly court sought a deciding voice. *Nochi Rabbah bar Nachmani* — let us summon Rabbah bar Nachmani, for he himself declared: *ani yachid b'nega'im, ani yachid b'ohalos* — I am the supreme authority in the laws of *nega'im* and in *ohalos*. A heavenly messenger was dispatched — just as the king's officer had been sent to find Rabbah, now the divine King sent a messenger as well.

### Death & Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדַּרוּ שְׁלִיחָא בָּתְרֵיהּ. לָא הֲוָה מָצֵי מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת לְמִקְרַב לֵיהּ, מִדְּלָא הֲוָה קָא פָסֵיק פּוּמֵּיהּ מִגִּרְסֵיהּ. אַדְּהָכִי נְשַׁב זִיקָא וַאֲוַושׁ בֵּינֵי קְנֵי, סְבַר גּוּנְדָּא דְפָרָשֵׁי הוּא. אֲמַר: תִּינַח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא, וְלָא יִמְּסַר בִּידָא דְמַלְכוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent a messenger** from heaven **after him** to take his soul up to the heavenly academy, but **the Angel of Death was unable to approach** Rabba bar Naḥmani, **as his mouth did not cease from his** Torah **study. In the meantime, a wind blew and howled between the branches.** Rabba bar Naḥmani **thought** that the noise was due to **an infantry battalion [*gunda*]** about to capture him. **He said: Let that man,** i.e., me, **die and not be given over to the hands of the government.** The Angel of Death was therefore able to take his soul.

*Lo hava matzei malach hamaves l'mikrav lei* — the *malach hamaves* could not approach Rabbah, because *mi d'lo hava pasik pumei migirsa* — his mouth never ceased learning. Then a wind rose and rustled through the reeds, and Rabbah thought it was the king's troops coming to seize him. *Teinach nafshi d'hu gavra v'lo yimsar b'yada d'malchusa* — let me die, rather than be handed over to the government. And in that moment of interrupted learning, the *malach hamaves* was able to take him. As his soul departed, the heavenly court demanded his ruling. He declared: *tahor, tahor* — siding, notably, with God's position and against the full *mesivta d'rakiya*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה קָא נָיְחָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֲמַר: טָהוֹר, טָהוֹר. יָצָאת בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: אַשְׁרֶיךָ רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי שֶׁגּוּפְךָ טָהוֹר, וְיָצָאתָה נִשְׁמָתְךָ בְּטָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **As he was dying, he said** in response to the dispute in heaven: It is **pure;** it is **pure. A Divine Voice emerged** from heaven **and said: Happy are you, Rabba bar Naḥmani, as your body is pure and your soul left** you **with** the word: **Pure.**

There is a striking parallel here to the famous story of the *Tanur shel Achnai*, where Rabbi Eliezer and the full academy were locked in debate — he saying *tahor* and the rest saying *tamei* — and where Rabbi Eliezer's soul also departed with the word *tahor* on his lips. The parallel is unmistakable: in both cases, a lone sage sides with a minority position, and the departure of his soul is marked by that same word.

### Burial Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַל פִּתְקָא מֵרְקִיעָא בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא: רַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי נִתְבַּקֵּשׁ בִּיְשִׁיבָה שֶׁל מַעְלָה. נְפַקוּ אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא וְכוּלְּהוּ רַבָּנַן לְאִיעֲסוֹקֵי בֵּיהּ. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי דּוּכְתֵּיהּ. אֲזַלוּ לְאַגְמָא חֲזוֹ צִפְּרֵי דִּמְטַלְּלִי וְקָיְימִי, אָמְרִי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הָתָם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **A note [*pitka*] fell from heaven** and landed **in** the academy of **Pumbedita.** The note read: **Rabba bar Naḥmani was summoned to the heavenly academy,** i.e., he has died. **Abaye and Rava and all of the** other **Rabbis went out to tend to his** burial; however, **they did not know the location of his** body. **They went to the swamp** and **saw birds forming a shade and hovering** over a certain spot. The Rabbis **said:** We can **conclude from this** that **he is there.**

*Nafal piska mi'rakiya b'Pumbedita* — a note descended from heaven to Pumbedita announcing that Rabbah bar Nachmani had been called up to the heavenly *beit midrash*. Abaye and Rava and all the *rabbanan* went out to tend to his body, but *lo havu yadei duchtei* — they did not know where he was. He had been in hiding. They went to *Agma* and saw birds hovering and providing shade over one spot. *Shema mina hasam hu* — he must be there. They eulogized him for three days and three nights. A heavenly proclamation came: anyone who stops will be placed in *nidui*. They continued for seven days, until another heavenly announcement: *l'chulu b'seichem l'shalom* — go home in peace. You have done enough.

### Wind Toss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַפְדוּהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי וּתְלָתָא לֵילָוָתָא. נְפַל פִּתְקָא: כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ יְהֵא בְּנִידּוּי. סַפְדוּהּ שִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי. נְפַל פִּתְקָא: לְכוּ לְבֵיתְכֶם לְשָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **lamented him for three days and three nights. A note fell** from heaven, upon which was written: **Anyone who removes himself** from the lamentations **shall be ostracized.** Accordingly, **they lamented him** for **seven days.** Another **note fell** from heaven, stating: **Go to your homes in peace.**

That same day that Rabbah died, a great heavenly wind arose, *v'darei l'hahu taya* — and lifted a certain Arab nomad riding on his camel from one bank of the *Nahar Pappa* and threw him to the other side. There was, it seems, a heavenly agitation related to the circumstances of Rabbah's burial.

### Arab's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יוֹמָא דְּנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דַּלְיֵיהּ זַעְפָּא וּדְרִי לְהָהוּא טַיָּיעָא כִּי רְכִיב גַּמְלָא מֵהַאי גִּיסָא דִּנְהַר פָּפָּא וְשַׁדְיֵיהּ בְּהָךְ גִּיסָא. אֲמַר: מַאי הַאי? אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא דִּידָךְ הוּא, וְרַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי דִּידָךְ, אַתְּ דְּרַבָּה וְרַבָּה דִּידָךְ – אַמַּאי קָא מַחְרְבַתְּ לֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא? נָח זַעְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** On **that day when** Rabba bar Naḥmani **died, a hurricane lifted a certain Arab [*taya'a*]** merchant **while he was riding** his **camel.** The hurricane carried him **from one side of the Pappa River and threw him onto the other side. He said: What is this?** Those present **said to him: Rabba bar Naḥmani has died. He said before** God: **Master of the Universe! The entire world is Yours and Rabba bar Naḥmani is** also **Yours. You are to Rabba and Rabba is to You,** i.e., you are beloved to each other. If so, **why are You destroying the world** on his account? **The storm subsided.**

The Arab, shaken and bewildered, asked what this was about. People told him: *nach nafsheih d'Rabbah bar Nachmani* — Rabbah bar Nachmani has died and the world is in tumult. The Arab then turned in prayer — and remarkably, this nomad prays in Aramaic: *Ribbono Shel Olam, kulei alma didach hu, v'Rabbah bar Nachmani didach hu* — the whole world is Yours, and Rabbah bar Nachmani is Yours. *Ant d'Rabbah v'Rabbah didach* — You are Rabbah's and Rabbah is Yours; the two of you are bound together. *Amai ka m'charvat lei l'alma?* — Why then are you destroying the world? And *nach za'fyah* — the heavenly anger subsided.

## Workers' Rights (II)

### Custom Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה וְכוּ׳. הַכֹּל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּאַתְרָא דִּנְהִיגִי מִכְרַךְ רִיפְתָּא וּמִשְׁתֵּה אַנְפָּקָא. דְּאִי אָמַר לְהוּ קַדִּימוּ וְאַיְיתֵי לְכוּ, אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא כֹּל כְּמִינָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the mishna (83a), which teaches that an employer must provide his laborers with sustenance, **all in accordance with the regional custom.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the inclusive term: **All?** The Gemara answers: This serves **to include a place where it is customary** for the laborers to **eat bread and drink a quarter-***log* **[*anpaka*] of wine. As, if** in such a case the employer were to **say to them: Arise early** in the morning **and I will bring you** this sustenance, so as not to waste work time, **they may say to him: It is not in your power** to compel us to do so.

The *Gemara* returns to a halachic framework: *hakol k'minhag hamedina* — all worker arrangements follow the custom of the land. Whatever the local practice is with regard to meals, breaks, and provisions for workers, that practice governs the employment relationship unless otherwise specified. The *Gemara* asks: what does *hakol* — "all" — come to include? It comes to include situations where the practice is to provide bread and drink in a certain measure. If an employer says to his workers, come early and I will give you your meal before the work begins, the workers can respond: *lav kol kminach* — you do not have the right to impose that. The standard practice is to receive the meal after the working hours have begun. The *Tosafos* notes a second interpretation: if an employer says he will bring the food to the workers at their worksite rather than allowing them to take their customary break and return to the house, the workers can insist on following the established *minhag*.

## Matya Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן מַתְיָא שֶׁאָמַר לִבְנוֹ צֵא וּשְׂכוֹר וְכוּ׳. מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר! חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְאִם פָּסַק לָהֶם מְזוֹנוֹת רִיבָּה לָהֶן. וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן מַתְיָא שֶׁאָמַר לִבְנוֹ צֵא שְׂכוֹר לָנוּ פּוֹעֲלִים, הָלַךְ וּפָסַק לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת. וּכְשֶׁבָּא אֵצֶל אָבִיו, אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, אֲפִילּוּ אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה לָהֶן כִּסְעוּדַת שְׁלֹמֹה בִּשְׁעָתוֹ, לֹא יָצָאתָ יְדֵי חוֹבָתְךָ עִמָּהֶן, שֶׁהֵן בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there was **an incident involving Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya, who said to his son: Go out and hire** laborers for us. His son hired the laborers and stipulated that he would provide sustenance for them. The Gemara asks: After the mishna has stated that all practices are in accordance with the regional custom, how can it cite this **incident,** which seems **to contradict** the previous ruling, as Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya and his son did not follow the regional custom? The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and this** is what it **is teaching:** All practices are in accordance with the regional custom, **but if** the employer pledged to provide sustenance for them, **he has increased** his obligation to **them,** since if he had meant to give them no more than the accepted amount, he would not have made any stipulation at all. The mishna then continues: **And** there is **also** a supporting **incident involving Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya, who said to his son: Go out** and **hire laborers for us.** His son **went,** hired them, **and pledged** to provide **sustenance for them** as a term of their employment, without specifying the details. **And when he came** back **to his father** and reported what he had done, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya **said to him: My son, even if you were to prepare a feast for them like that of** King **Solomon in his time, you would not have fulfilled your obligation to them, as they are the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.**

The *Gemara* then presents a famous story: *maaseh b'Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya she'amar l'vno* — there was an incident with Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya who instructed his son to go hire workers, with an implied promise of food. When the son returned, his father told him: even if you make for them a feast like the feast of Solomon in his prime, *lo yatzata yedei chovascha* — you would not have fulfilled your obligation to them, because *heim bnei Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* — they are the children of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, whose due is beyond any ordinary reckoning. Go back and stipulate with them that you are not committing to anything unlimited.

The *Gemara* notes an apparent tension: this story seems to contradict *hakol k'minhag hamedina*, since the *minhag* is presumably bounded and finite. The resolution is *chasuri m'chasra*: the *Mishnah* is incomplete as stated, and the full teaching is that this case applies specifically when an employer has already agreed to the custom of providing food *and then explicitly stipulates* an additional commitment — *v'ei pasak lehu mezoni, ariv lehu* — that explicit stipulation goes beyond the *minhag* and creates a more expansive obligation.

## Solomon & Abraham

### Solomon's Feast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּסְעוּדְתָּא דְּאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ עֲדִיפָא מִדִּשְׁלֹמֹה? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי לֶחֶם שְׁלֹמֹה לְיוֹם אֶחָד שְׁלֹשִׁים כֹּר סֹלֶת וְשִׁשִּׁים כֹּר קָמַח. עֲשָׂרָה בָּקָר בְּרִאִים וַעֲשָׂרָה בָּקָר רְעִי וּמֵאָה צֹאן לְבַד מֵאַיָּל וּצְבִי וְיַחְמוּר וּבַרְבֻּרִים אֲבוּסִים״! וְאָמַר גּוּרְיוֹן בֶּן אֲסִטְיוֹן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הַלָּלוּ לַעֲמִילָן שֶׁל טַבָּחִים. וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: הַלָּלוּ לְצִיקֵי קְדֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that the feast of Abraham, our forefather, was superior to that of** King **Solomon? But isn't it written: "And Solomon's provision for one day was thirty measures of fine flour, and sixty measures of meal; ten fat oxen, and twenty oxen out of the pastures, and a hundred sheep, beside harts, and gazelles, and roebucks, and fatted fowl"** (I Kings 5:2–3). **And Guryon ben Asteyon says in the name of Rav: These** measures of flour mentioned in the verse **were** used merely **for the bakers' well-worked dough [*la'amilan*]** that was placed in the pot to absorb the steam. **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: These** measures of flour were used **for** meat **pudding,** a mixture of wine, flour, and leftover meat, **in a pot.**

With Solomon invoked as the standard of extraordinary feasting, the *Gemara* now examines what that feast actually entailed. The verse records Solomon's daily provisions: *vayehi lechem Shlomo leyom echad sheloshim kor solet* — thirty *kor* of fine flour, sixty *kor* of bran flour, ten prime cattle, twenty pasture-fed cattle, one hundred sheep, in addition to various wild animals — deer, gazelle, and *yachmur* — and *barburim abusim*, stuffed fowl (whether goose, duck, or fattened hens, the precise modern identification is uncertain).

Yet this prodigious quantity is then subjected to a remarkable reinterpretation. Guryon ben Astiyon said in the name of Rav: all that bread was not the bread of the meal itself — it was *la'amilan shel tabachim*, the bread used to clean and absorb the overflow from the cooking pots. Rav Yitzchak adds: all that meat was not the main course — it was *l'tzikei kedeira*, some form of minced meat appetizer. The actual feast itself was something far greater.

### Thousand Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֶלֶף נָשִׁים הָיוּ לִשְׁלֹמֹה, כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת עָשְׂתָה לוֹ בְּבֵיתָה כָּךְ. מַאי טַעְמָא – זוֹ סְבוּרָה שֶׁמָּא אֶצְלִי סוֹעֵד הַיּוֹם, וְזוֹ סְבוּרָה שֶׁמָּא אֶצְלִי סוֹעֵד הַיּוֹם. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי אַבְרָהָם כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶל הַבָּקָר רָץ אַבְרָהָם וַיִּקַּח בֶּן בָּקָר רַךְ וָטוֹב״. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: ״בֶּן בָּקָר״ – אֶחָד, ״רַךְ״ – שְׁנַיִם, ״וְטוֹב״ – שְׁלֹשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak** further **says:** King **Solomon had one thousand wives, each one of whom would prepare for him at her home** a feast of **such** proportions. **What is the reason** that they did this? **This** wife **reasoned: Perhaps he will feast with me today, and that** wife **reasoned: Perhaps he will feast with me today. But with regard to Abraham, it is written: "And Abraham ran to the herd, and fetched a calf tender and good"** (Genesis 18:7), **and Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** in explanation of the verse: **"A calf"** indicates **one;** the word **"tender"** means an additional one, i.e., **two; "and good"** indicates yet another one. This makes a total of **three** calves, a considerably smaller feast than that of Solomon.

Rav Yitzchak further states: *elef nashim hayu li'Shlomo* — Solomon had a thousand wives, *kol achas v'achas asta lo b'veisa kach* — and each one prepared this very feast in her own house. *Zu sevura shema etzli so'ed hayom* — each one thought: perhaps he will dine by me today. So the actual total was a thousand times what was described in the verse.

By contrast, the feast of Abraham when he received the three angels appears far more modest. The verse says simply: *v'el habakar ratz Avraham vayikach ben bakar rach va'tov* — Abraham ran to the cattle and took a calf, tender and good. It sounds like a single animal.

### Three Calves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם תְּלָתָא תּוֹרֵי לִתְלָתָא גַּבְרֵי, הָכָא לְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל וִיהוּדָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְהוּדָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל רַבִּים כַּחוֹל אֲשֶׁר עַל הַיָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to Abraham, he prepared **three oxen for three people,** whereas **here,** in the case of Solomon, his wives would prepare a feast **for the entire** realms of **Israel and Judah, as it is stated: "Judah and Israel were many, as the sand which is by the sea** in multitude, eating and drinking and making merry" (I Kings 4:20). Abraham's feast was proportionately greater than that of Solomon.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *ben bakar* — a calf — that is one. *Rach* — soft — that is a second. *Va'tov* — and good — that is a third. Three calves, one for each of the three guests. A possible objection is raised: perhaps *rach va'tov* are simply adjectives describing a single calf — one tender, good animal? The *Gemara* responds: if so, the verse would have simply written *rach tov*. The presence of the *vav* — *va'tov* — signals that it is an independent addition, coming *l'drasha*, to teach a separate animal. And since *va'tov* thus refers to a third calf, *rach nami l'drasha* — the word *rach* must similarly indicate its own separate calf. Three calves for three people. *Hasam tlata torei li'tlata gavrei* — there in the case of Abraham, three calves for three men.

When the verse then says *vayitnehu el ha'na'ar vayimaher la'asot oso* — he gave it to the lad to prepare — the singular *oso* might suggest one animal. But the *Gemara* resolves this: *kol chad v'chad yeiv l'na'ar chad* — each animal was given to a separate youth to prepare simultaneously.

As for the sequence in which it was served — *vayikach chem'a v'chalav u'ven habakar asher asa vayiten lifneihem* — he took butter, milk, and the prepared calf and placed them before the guests — the *Gemara* explains: *dekama dekama dematya ayti l'kama'ihu* — as each dish was ready, he brought it before them in sequence. Like courses at a fine meal: the dairy dishes first, then they were removed, then the meat. Some note that this sequencing is itself suggestive regarding the separation of milk and meat in practice, though this is reading somewhat into the text. And why were three separate calves necessary? *K'dei le'achilan shalosh leshonos b'chardal* — in order to serve three tongues in mustard to each guest, which was considered a great delicacy.

### Endless Obligation

Now the comparison becomes clear. Solomon's feast, enormous as it was, was ultimately a finite quantity — spread across an entire kingdom. Abraham's feast, though appearing modest on its face, was given to three individuals and was truly lavish per person. And since the children of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are the descendants of the one who provided such a feast, any explicit commitment to feed them without limit means that you can never fully discharge the obligation. The feast of Solomon himself would not suffice. And that is precisely why Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya instructed his son to go back and clarify the terms of his promise before it became binding.
