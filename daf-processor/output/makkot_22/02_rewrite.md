# Makkot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Single Act, Multiple Acts

### Continuity & Warnings

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion concerning the relationship between multiple transgressions and a single continuous act. The governing principle, familiar from earlier in the tractate, is that a single unbroken act produces only one set of lashes, regardless of how many times the forbidden conduct is repeated, so long as only one warning was issued at the outset. If, however, a separate warning was administered before each successive iteration of the act, each such iteration constitutes a distinct transgression warranting its own set of lashes. The *mishnah* applies this principle to the case of *shaatnez*: if a person removes and re-dons a garment containing a forbidden wool-linen mixture, each donning following a separate warning generates a new liability for lashes.

### Rav Assi's Position

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rav Bibi in the name of Rav Assi: *lo poshet velo lovesh mammash ela afilu machnis umotzi beit hatzavar ukli shelo* — one need not actually strip the garment completely and redress. Even if a person merely withdraws one arm from a sleeve and then reinserts it, that partial removal followed by partial reinsertion constitutes a new act of "putting on," sufficient to generate a new transgression if accompanied by a fresh warning.

### Wearing vs. Putting On

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika demurred, gesturing to indicate that only a full withdrawal and reinsertion — *yulei ve'afukei* — would qualify. According to the *Rivan*'s explanation, he requires complete removal and redressing. Rav Ashi goes furthest. He holds that one need not even perform any physical movement at all, provided only that sufficient time has elapsed — *kedei lifshot velilvosh*, enough time to remove and replace the garment. In other words, the mere continued wearing of *shaatnez*, given the theoretical opportunity to have removed it, constitutes a new punishable act.

### Act Definition

Rav Ashi's position is genuinely puzzling. If the *issur* is simply *lo tilbash shaatnez* — the ongoing state of wearing — then one would expect that the choice not to remove the garment when one had the opportunity would suffice: I had the chance to remove it and did not, therefore I have chosen to continue wearing it. That would be a coherent theory of passive liability. But Rav Ashi introduces the additional requirement that enough time passed to both remove *and* replace the garment, not merely to remove it. This suggests he is conceptualizing the violation not as an ongoing state of *levisha* but as a virtual, theoretical act of *levishas* — a renewed putting-on — that he imagines occurring during that window of time. This is a striking middle position: the violation is modeled as an act of donning, yet it is entirely notional, not physical.

One might extend this framework to analogous cases, such as a *nazir* who continues drinking. There, the relevant act is drinking, not the picking up of a cup, and so the theory of virtual repetition would presumably apply there as well. But why specifically the time to both remove and replace, rather than merely the time to remove? The answer seems to lie precisely in Rav Ashi's conceptual framing: since he is treating the ongoing wearing as a constructive, theoretical act of re-donning, the minimum window he requires is the time it would take to accomplish that complete act.

## Plowing & Kilayim

### Mishna's 8 Sins

The *gemara* shifts to the second part of the *mishnah*, which enumerates how a person can transgress eight separate *lavin* in the course of plowing a single furrow: *yesh choresh telem echad vechayav alav mishum shemoneh lavin*. One furrow, eight prohibitions. The *mishnah* specifies, among other things, the prohibition of *kilayim b'kerem* — planting mixed species in a vineyard.

### Covering = Planting

Rav Yannai reports a ruling issued in a gathering of scholars: *bechabura nimnu vegamru, hachofer bekilayim lokeh* — they took a vote and concluded that one who covers *kilayim* seeds with dirt receives lashes, because that covering constitutes a form of planting. The prohibition, *lo tizra karmicha kilayim*, speaks of *zeriya* — planting — and covering seeds with earth is deemed a form of *zeriya*, since it is the act of bringing the seed into productive contact with the soil.

### Yochanan's Reply

Rabbi Yochanan responded: *lav mishnateinu hi zo?* — is this not already implicit in our own *mishnah*? He asks: how is it that the *mishnah* lists the prohibition of *kilayim* as one of the lashes incurred by plowing a furrow? Plowing a furrow is not planting a seed — one might presume it generates no *kilayim* violation at all. The only coherent answer is *lav demichasseh behadi de'azil* — that as the plow advances, it turns over dirt that covers the pre-existing seeds, and that covering constitutes a form of planting. The *chiddush* of Rav Yannai, Rabbi Yochanan is saying, is already embedded in the *mishnah* itself.

### Pearl Analogy

Rav Yannai responded with a memorable rejoinder: *ei lav de'dalina lach chaspa mi mishkachat marganita tutei* — had I not lifted the pottery shard for you, you never would have found the pearl hidden beneath it. This is a sharp and accurate observation about the nature of insight. Once the idea is stated — that covering with dirt is a form of planting — the *pshat* of the *mishnah* appears obvious, even inevitable. But before the idea was articulated, it was not obvious at all. Rabbi Yochanan can only say "of course, it is already in the *mishnah*" because Rav Yannai first revealed it. The best test of a genuine *pshat* is precisely that once stated it becomes self-evidently correct — yet the fact that it required articulation proves it was a discovery.

### Reish Lakish Agrees

Reish Lakish added: *ei lav de'kalsach gavra raba* — had this great man not praised you, meaning, had Rabbi Yochanan not confirmed by his response that the idea is already embedded as *pshat* in the *mishnah*, Reish Lakish would have said the *mishnah* follows Rav Yannai's *chiddush* as a minority position. But since Rabbi Yochanan agreed and treated it as plain *pshat*, it is clearly the straightforward reading of the *mishnah*. The implication is that Reish Lakish is himself persuaded by Rabbi Yochanan's confirmation.

### Rabbi Akiva View

This agreement having been established, the *gemara* notes that had Rabbi Yochanan not endorsed that *pshat*, one could have read the *mishnah* entirely differently. The *mishnah* could have reflected the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *af hamekayem* — even one who merely allows *kilayim* to remain in his field, without doing anything active — transgresses. This is a remarkable *chiddush*: passive toleration of *kilayim* is itself a punishable act, just as, in the earlier discussion, merely continuing to wear *shaatnez* may itself constitute an active violation. Indeed it is worth noting that both cases involve *kilayim* — one in the domain of *beged kilayim shaatnez*, one in *kilayim b'kerem* — and both raise the identical structural question: why is a passive state treated as an actionable *maaseh* sufficient to generate lashes?

The *gemara* here does not resolve why Rabbi Akiva's *kilayim* constitutes a *lav she'yesh bo maaseh* rather than a *lav she'ein bo maaseh*, which ordinarily does not generate lashes. The same question, left unanswered, applies to Rav Assi's position on *shaatnez*. The two problems are parallel.

### Verse Exegesis

The source for Rabbi Akiva's position is a *braisa* that derives it from a close reading of the verse: *sadchecha lo tizra kilayim*. On the surface, the verse prohibits the act of *zeriya* — planting. Rabbi Akiva, however, reads the verse as *kilayim sadchecha lo* — the *kilayim* shall not be in your field — separating the verb *tizra* from the object *kilayim sadchecha*, and reading the latter phrase independently to forbid the mere presence of *kilayim* in one's domain.

### Active vs. Passive

The *gemara* offers a possible way of reconciling the active-versus-passive tension within Rabbi Akiva's ruling. When the *mishnah* says one gets lashes for plowing a furrow while *kilayim* is present, perhaps the act of plowing is not itself a form of planting the *kilayim* — the plow does not plant them — but rather it constitutes an active demonstration that one intends to maintain the *kilayim* in the field. A person who sits inside the house while *kilayim* grows in his field is entirely passive. A person who goes out to the field and plows, cultivates, weeds, and fertilizes that field, without destroying the *kilayim*, is actively showing his desire to preserve them. This active demonstration transforms what would otherwise be passive toleration into the kind of affirmative conduct that warrants lashes. On this reading, the two questions resolve each other: the act is not planting *kilayim* per se, but rather the cultivation-as-endorsement that Rabbi Akiva's reading of *kilayim sadchecha lo* targets.

It is worth noting, almost parenthetically, that Rav Yannai's *chiddush* about covering as planting appears to be anticipated by the *braisa* itself, which states *hamechapeh bekilayim lokeh* — one who covers seeds of *kilayim* with dirt receives lashes. This seems already to encode the idea. The *gemara* leaves this without comment.

## Yom Tov Violations

### Ula's Challenge

Having established the *mishnah*'s scenario — plowing a furrow on *Yom Tov* with *kilayim* present — the *gemara* proceeds to ask why the *mishnah* stops at eight transgressions. Ula challenges Rav Nachman: *dilki nami mishum zorea b'Yom Tov* — the person should also receive lashes for the prohibition of planting on *Yom Tov*. Since the act of plowing is itself a form of planting the *kilayim*, and this is occurring on *Yom Tov*, the same furrow should generate two separate *Yom Tov* violations: one for plowing (*choresh*) and one for planting (*zorea*). Why does the *mishnah* not count both?

The initial response — *tana v'shiyer*, the *tanna* listed an incomplete list — is immediately rejected. The entire rhetorical point of the *mishnah* is to tell you the maximum: it explicitly says *shemoneh lavin*, eight. If nine could be derived from the identical scenario, the *mishnah* should have said nine.

### Shabbat vs Yom Tov

Rava resolves the difficulty with a fundamental distinction: *yesh chiluk melachos b'Shabbos v'ein chiluk melachos b'Yom Tov*. On *Shabbos*, each distinct *melacha* carries its own identity as a separate *aveira*. This is demonstrated by the rule that one who forgot it was *Shabbos* and performed all the *melachos* brings thirty-nine separate *korbanos* — each *melacha* is conceptually distinct. On *Yom Tov*, however, there is no such differentiation among *melachos*. Breaking *Yom Tov* by plowing and breaking *Yom Tov* by planting are not two separate *aveiros*; they are simply two different modes of committing the single *aveira* of violating *Yom Tov*. It is as though one received a single warning not to break *Yom Tov* and then broke it twice — whether by making two furrows or by making one furrow and planting — the result is one set of lashes, because the identity of the transgression is unified.

### Gid HaNasheh

This thesis faces an immediate challenge from Abaye, who cites a *mishnah* that appears to treat different *melachos* on *Yom Tov* as distinct *aveiros*. The case: one who cooks a *gid hanasheh* — the sciatic sinew, which is treated in this *mishnah* as flesh — in milk on *Yom Tov* and then eats it. The *mishnah* counts five separate sets of lashes: (1) eating a *gid hanasheh*, (2) eating *basar b'chalav*, (3) cooking *basar b'chalav*, (4) cooking without a permitted purpose (*she-lo letzorech*) — since one cooked something one cannot eat, the cooking serves no legitimate *Yom Tov* function — and (5) *havarah*, kindling the fire.

Crucially, both (4) and (5) are *Yom Tov* violations, and they are counted as two separate *aveiros*. This directly contradicts Rava's position that different *melachos* on *Yom Tov* are not distinct.

### Havarah Issue

The *gemara* initially attempts to address this by reinterpreting the fifth transgression: perhaps it is not *havarah* at all.

### Hekdesh Wood

The *gemara* proposes two revisions to the case. First, perhaps the fire was kindled with *atzei asheirah* — wood belonging to *avodah zarah* — making the fifth violation a transgression of deriving benefit from idolatrous property: *v'lo yidbak b'yadcha me'umah min hacherem*. This is immediately rejected, because if *asheirah* wood were involved, there should be yet a sixth transgression — *lo tavi to'evah el beitecha*, the prohibition against bringing such wood into one's possession.

The *gemara* therefore settles on a second revision: the wood was *atzei hekdesh* — consecrated property — and the prohibition is derived from the verse *v'ashreichem tisrefun ba'eish, lo taasun kein la-Hashem Elokeichem*, which is read to forbid the destruction of *hekdesh*. This is clearly not the *pshat* of the *mishnah*, as the *mishnah* makes no mention of *asheirah* wood or *hekdesh* wood. But it is the answer the *gemara* works with.

### Yom Tov Unified

The conclusion, then, is as follows: the *mishnah* about the *gid hanasheh* does not, in fact, count two separate *Yom Tov melachos* as two *aveiros*. The fifth transgression is not *havarah* as a *Yom Tov* violation but rather the forbidden use of *hekdesh* wood, which is an entirely independent prohibition. With that revision accepted, Rava's principle stands: two different *melachos* on *Yom Tov* are treated as a single *aveira* of breaking *Yom Tov*, not as two distinct *aveiros*. Plowing and planting in the *mishnah*'s scenario are simply two ways of breaking *Yom Tov*, and since a single warning was issued, only one set of lashes results. This is an important and counterintuitive principle, and it stands in sharp contrast to the *Shabbos* framework, where each *melacha* retains its distinct identity.

## Added Transgressions

### Nachal Eitan

Having resolved the internal tension within the *mishnah*'s scenario, the *gemara* opens a more playful register, entertaining ways that additional transgressions could theoretically be appended to the furrow-plowing case by modifying the scenario's circumstances. Rav Hoshaya asks: why not also include the prohibition of planting or plowing in the *nachal eitan* — the valley designated for the *eglah arufah* ritual? The verse *asher lo ye'aveid bo v'lo yizara* prohibits working or planting in that valley. Since the *mishnah*'s scenario already involves planting and plowing, one could simply specify that the field is located in the *nachal eitan* and gain another transgression.

### Erasing Name

Rav Chanina asks: why not include one who erases God's name in the course of plowing? The prohibition of erasing a divine name is derived from *v'ibadtem et shemam... lo ta'asun kein la-Hashem Elokeichem*. One could imagine the plow passing over a piece of parchment on which God's name is written, causing its erasure. By this point, the *gemara* is beginning to construct scenarios that are only incidentally related to the act of plowing itself.

### Leprous Spot

Rabbi Abbahu presses further: why not include one who cuts off a leprous spot (*baheret*), which is forbidden under *hishamer b'nega hatzaraas*? One could imagine — and the *gemara* is clearly now approaching the absurd — a person positioning a diseased limb in the path of the plow in order to excise the spot. The *gemara* is simply enumerating any conceivable violation that could, under some contrived circumstance, accompany the act of plowing.

### Choshen & Ephod

Abaye continues: one who displaces the *choshen* — the High Priest's breastplate — from the *ephod* transgresses a prohibition derived from *lo yizach hachoshen*. One could imagine the High Priest standing in the field, being jostled by the plow. Abaye adds further: one who removes the staves of the *aron* from their rings likewise violates *lo yasuru*. The scenario now involves, somehow, the Ark of the Covenant being in the path of the furrow.

### Asheira Wood

Rav Ashi returns to more realistic territory: one who plows with a plow constructed from *asheirah* wood derives benefit from *avodah zarah* property, in violation of *v'lo yidbak b'yadcha me'umah min hacherem*. This is at least not incidental to the act of plowing itself — the wood is integral to the implement.

### Fruit Trees

Ravina asks: why not include one who cuts down good fruit trees? If the furrow passes through an orchard and the plow fells productive fruit trees without purpose, that would constitute *bal tashchis*, derived from *ki mimenu tochal v'oso lo tichrot*. This too is a reasonably natural adjunct to the act of plowing.

### Oath Not to Plow

The most substantive addition is raised by Rav Zeira to Rabbi Mani: why not include a case where the person swore an oath not to plow? An oath of that kind would add a violation of *shevua* to the other transgressions. However, if one specifies that the oath was not to plow on *Yom Tov* — which fits the *mishnah*'s existing scenario — the *shevua* would not take effect at all, for the principle of *mushba ve'omed me'Har Sinai*: one cannot take an oath to repeat a Torah obligation or prohibition, since one is already "sworn from Sinai" to observe it. The oath is therefore redundant and does not generate additional liability.

## Issur Kolel

### Oath Problem

The problem with adding the *shevua* violation is thus the doctrine of *mushba ve'omed me'Har Sinai*. An oath not to plow on *Yom Tov* cannot take effect because the Torah has already imposed that prohibition.

### Generic Oath

The resolution: specify that the oath was not simply *not to plow on Yom Tov* but rather *shevua shelo achrosh bein b'chol bein b'Yom Tov* — a generic oath never to plow at all, on weekdays as well. Since the oath clearly does take effect on ordinary weekdays — where there is no prior prohibition — it has a legitimate scope of application and is not entirely redundant.

### Kolel Principle

This triggers the principle of *issur kolel*: when a prohibition encompasses a broader range of situations, it takes effect even in the specific area where it is technically redundant. Because the oath not to plow is valid on weekdays, it is a genuine *issur* with real force across a wide domain. By virtue of that broader applicability, it takes effect *even* on *Yom Tov*, adding a *shevua* violation on top of the pre-existing *Yom Tov* prohibition. This is the one addition in this entire sequence that the *gemara* treats with full analytical seriousness. All the previous cases — the *choshen*, the *aron*, the leprous spot, the erased name — were almost entirely incidental to the act of plowing, constructed violations of extreme artificiality. This case, by contrast, genuinely concerns an oath about plowing, and the question of why it was not included in the *mishnah* is a real halachic question deserving a real answer.

## Oaths Not Listed

### Reversible Excluded

The *gemara* offers an answer to why the *mishnah* nonetheless omits the *shevua* case: *midei d'islei b'she'ila lo katani* — the *mishnah* does not list prohibitions that are subject to reversal through *she'ila*, i.e., petitioning a sage for release. An oath, even one governed by *issur kolel*, can in principle be annulled through *hataras nedarim*, and the *mishnah*'s catalog of transgressions is limited to absolute, irrevocable prohibitions.

### Bechor Challenge

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: one of the cases in the *mishnah* involved an animal that was *hekdesh* — consecrated property — and sanctification is created through a verbal act, similar to an oath, and is in principle reversible through *she'ila*. The *gemara* responds: *b'bechor* — the *mishnah* refers not to an animal consecrated by a human verbal act, but to a *bechor*, a firstborn animal, whose sanctity is Torah-imposed without any speech act by its owner and therefore admits of no annulment.

### Nezir Challenge

A stronger challenge: the *mishnah* also includes a *nazir* in a cemetery. *Nezirut* is created through a vow — a verbal declaration — and is equally subject to *hataras nedarim*. Why is *nezir* included if reversible prohibitions are excluded?

### Nezir Shimshon

The *gemara* responds: *b'nazir Shimshon* — the *mishnah* refers specifically to a *nazir Shimshon*, which cannot be revoked. The *Rivan* explains: the defining feature of *nezirus Shimshon* is that it was not self-imposed through a voluntary declaration but was decreed by divine mandate — the angel announced to Shimshon's parents that *nazir yiheyeh ha-yeled*, the child shall be a *nazir*. Because the *nezirut* originated not from the person's own speech act but from a divine command, it cannot be undone through *she'ila* — there is no human petitioner to release it, and the originating authority is not a human vow.

This leaves the *mishnah* in an awkward posture: does the *halachic* category of *nazir Shimshon* require an actual angelic visitation? The *Rivan* entertains but rejects the alternative explanations. One might have said, for instance, that the category applies when a parent accepted the *nezirut* on a child's behalf — in which case the child, when grown, cannot individually revoke it, even if the parent theoretically could. Or one might argue that among the distinctive features of *nezirus Shimshon* as a formal category is simply that it is irrevocable regardless of who accepted it. The *Rivan* inclines toward the angel-visitation explanation, acknowledging that this limits the category to extraordinary circumstances.

### Hataras Nedarim

A further difficulty: one of the unique features of *nazir Shimshon* is that, unlike ordinary *nezirus*, it does not prohibit contact with the dead — as the story of Shimshon himself makes clear, since he was perpetually engaged with mortality. If *nazir Shimshon* does not restrict contact with the dead, how can the *mishnah* use it as the paradigm case for lashes in a cemetery? The *gemara* notes as well that *nazirus Shimshon* is explicitly mentioned in the formula of *hataras nedarim* recited on *Erev Rosh Hashanah*, where different textual versions either include *nazirus Shimshon* among the vows being released or specifically except it. This very ambiguity in the *nusach* reflects the underlying debate about whether *nezirus Shimshon* is subject to *she'ila* at all.

### No Issur Kolel

The *gemara* ultimately abandons the entire approach. *Ela hai tanna issur kolel leis lei* — the *tanna* of our *mishnah* simply does not hold of the principle of *issur kolel*. On this view, even a generic oath not to plow ever does not take effect on *Yom Tov*, because on *Yom Tov* the person was already prohibited by the Torah and remains, as it were, *mushba ve'omed me'Har Sinai*. The *tanna* rejects the mechanism by which a broader *shevua* could piggyback on its weekday validity to generate liability for *Yom Tov* as well. Consequently, no version of the *shevua* scenario produces a genuine additional *aveira* for the *mishnah* to count, and its absence from the list requires no further explanation. The *mishnah* may freely include reversible prohibitions — the argument from *she'ila* is abandoned — but the *shevua* case simply fails as a matter of this *tanna*'s *halachic* framework.

## Lashes Count

### 39 Not 40

The *mishnah* now turns to the administration of lashes itself. The Torah prescribes forty lashes — *arba'im yakenu lo yosif* — yet the *mishnah* records that the number administered is thirty-nine: *arba'im chaser achat*.

### Verse Reread

The derivation rests on a reading of the phrase *bemispar arba'im*: the number that is within, or adjacent to, forty — meaning the highest number that falls short of forty. The *gemara* formulates this as *minyan shehu samuch le-arba'im* — a count that approximates forty — yielding thirty-nine. Rabbi Yehuda dissents from this reinterpretation.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda holds that the full forty lashes are administered, and he derives from the verse *mah makkot ha-eleh bein yadecha* that the fortieth lash is given between the shoulder blades. The *gemara* notes, however, that this verse's plain meaning concerns false prophets who, in the messianic era, will attempt to disavow their prophetic past and attribute their bruises to an ordinary altercation: *asher hukkeiti beit me'ahavai*. The rabbis read it instead as governing corporal punishment administered in school settings, not court-ordered lashes — a reading invoked once before in the tractate.

### Assessment

Having already departed from the Torah's explicit number of forty, the *mishnah* goes further: not every offender receives even the thirty-nine. The court first assesses (*omeid*) how many lashes the person can withstand, and whatever number is determined must be divisible by three, since the lashes are divided across three locations on the body. The logic seems to be: once the rabbis have established that forty does not mean forty, the number itself is no longer sacrosanct, and the court's authority to calibrate the punishment appropriately becomes operative.

### Lock-In Rule

A critical rule governs the assessment: once the process of lashing has begun, the court is locked into its original number. If the assessment was thirty-nine and the person proves unable to withstand the full amount after lashing has commenced, he goes free entirely — the court cannot simply reduce the number mid-process, because its authority is limited to the assessment made prior to commencement. Symmetrically, if the assessment was eighteen and the person proves more resilient than expected once lashing begins, the court cannot increase the number; he receives only the assessed eighteen and goes free of any additional liability. The principle is: *laka in, lo laka lo* — only once lashing has begun are these constraints in force; prior to commencement, the assessment may in principle be revised.

### Rabbinic Authority

Rava famously marks the passage with an observation about rabbinic authority: *kama tipsha'i she'ar inshi de-kaimi mikmei Sefer Torah v'lo kaimi mikmei gavra raba*. How foolish are ordinary people, who rise before a *Sefer Torah* when it enters a room but do not rise before a great Torah sage. The proof: the Torah writes forty, and the rabbis said thirty-nine, and their word prevails. The *Sefer Torah* says forty; the *gavra raba* says thirty-nine; and at the end of the day, it is the sage's interpretation that determines what "forty" means in practice. Just as, in constitutional terms, the text of a constitution is ultimately given meaning by the court interpreting it, so too the Torah's numbers are given operative meaning by the rabbinic sages. Their interpretive authority supersedes the raw text.

This observation also illuminates why the departure from forty was felt necessary in the first place. Once the number forty is opened up to reinterpretation — once it has been established that forty can mean thirty-nine — the entire principle of a fixed, irrevocable count is undermined, and the authority to calibrate the punishment further, based on the individual's capacity, flows naturally from that same source of rabbinic interpretive power.

### Reassessment

The *mishnah*'s implication — that reassessment is permissible before lashing begins but not after — is challenged by a *braisa* that appears to prohibit reassessment even before commencement. If the court assessed forty and then reconsidered before a single lash was struck, concluding the person could not withstand forty, the *braisa* says he goes free entirely. Similarly, if assessed at eighteen and then found capable of more before lashing began, the court is still bound to eighteen. This seems to prohibit pre-commencement reassessment as well, contradicting the *mishnah*.

### Same Day

Rav Sheshet resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between assessments made for the same day and assessments made in advance for a future day. If the assessment and the lashing are scheduled for the same day, a reassessment conducted before lashing begins is treated as a correction of an obvious error — analogous to a retraction made within the immediate frame of the original decision — and is permissible. If, however, the assessment was made on one day for a lashing to take place on a different day, no reassessment is permitted even before the process begins, because the original assessment was made as a considered, forward-looking determination, not an in-the-moment judgment subject to instant revision. The *gemara* acknowledges that this distinction is not fully transparent — one might argue that a lashing on a different day is precisely the situation where new information about health or capacity should most readily allow reassessment — but Rav Sheshet's formulation is the *gemara*'s resolution of record.

## Two Sins One Act

### Bundle or Separate

The final *mishnah* addresses a person who performs one act that transgresses two separate *lavin* simultaneously — such as, in the *mishnah*'s scenario, plowing with *kilayim* on *Yom Tov* or on *Shemita*, where a single furrow generates two distinct punishments. The question is how the administration of two separate sets of lashes is structured.

### Single Counts

If the court makes a single bundled assessment (*omed echad*) for both transgressions together, the offender receives whatever number of lashes that unified assessment yields — perhaps thirty-nine, perhaps thirty-six, perhaps forty-two — and that total counts toward both sets of liability simultaneously. The lashes are not divided into a first set for transgression one and a second set for transgression two; rather, one continuous administration of lashes discharges both obligations within whatever the court's assessment permits.

### Dual Sessions

The alternative is that the court makes two separate assessments, treating each transgression independently. In that case, the offender receives the first set of lashes today, heals, and returns for the second set on a subsequent occasion. The practical consequence of choosing between the bundled and separate models is significant: the separate model will almost always result in the offender receiving more total lashes.

### Braisa

A *braisa* challenges the bundled model, asserting *ein omdin omed echad bishnei lavin* — one may not make a single assessment for two transgressions. This appears to mandate the separate model categorically.

### Threshold of 42

Rav Sheshet resolves the contradiction by introducing a threshold. The key question is whether the offender can withstand forty-two or more lashes. This number is not arbitrary: thirty-nine lashes satisfy the first transgression in full; the minimum number of lashes that can be attributed to the second transgression while still constituting a coherent unit (divisible by three) is three. Therefore, thirty-nine plus three equals forty-two. Only if the court's assessment reaches at least forty-two can the lashes meaningfully encompass both transgressions. At forty-two or above, a single assessment that bundles both transgressions is possible: the first thirty-nine satisfy transgression one, and the remainder — three, six, nine, or whatever the assessment yields beyond thirty-nine — begins to address transgression two.

### Below 42

If the person can only withstand forty-one or fewer lashes, the bundled model is unavailable. In that range, even the full administered count cannot reach thirty-nine for transgression one and still leave any remainder for transgression two, since there is no number below forty-two that both fully satisfies transgression one and contributes something to transgression two. Consequently, below the threshold of forty-two, the court must proceed with two separate sessions: it administers lashes for transgression one today, waits for the offender to recover, and holds a second session for transgression two. Whether at or above the threshold the court *must* use the bundled model, or merely *may*, appears to be at the court's discretion — it can opt for either approach. But below forty-two, the separate model is mandatory. The *gemara* concludes here, and the discussion continues in the following session.
