# Bava Kamma 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishna Overview

### Archaic Mishna Language

The daf begins at the top of 14b, where we continue with a series of *mishnayot* that are notably cryptic, written in what one can only describe as an archaic layer of Tannaitic language. One wonders about the scholarly consensus: the assumption is that these *mishnayot* are among the earliest in the *mishna*. It is, in a way, a testament to the remarkable editorial achievement of Rebbe in organizing and standardizing *Shas* that most *mishnayot* do not read like this. The opacity here is striking precisely because it stands apart from the more discursive style that characterizes most of the *mishna*.

### Shum Kesef Mishnah

The *mishna* reads: *shum kesef, shaveh kesef, bifnei beis din, ve-al pi eidim, bnei chorin bnei bris, ve-nashim bikhlal ha-nezek, ve-hanizak ve-hamazik betashlumin.* Assessment of money, monetary equivalent, before a court, through witnesses, free persons, members of the covenant, women are included in damages, and both the damaged party and the damager participate in the payment. It reads almost as a legal poem, and the *gemara* will now work through each phrase methodically to determine what it actually means.

---

## Para and Talit

### Rav Yehuda's Read

The *gemara* opens with the first phrase: *mai shum kesef?* What does "assessment of money" mean? Rav Yehuda explains: *shum zeh lo yehei ela bekesef* — when you assess the loss, you do so in terms of the monetary value, the dollar amount. The *gemara* then notes that we have already learned this teaching, since a *braita* elaborates on it more explicitly.

### Cow/Garment Case

The *braita* presents the following scenario: *para she-hizika talit ve-talit she-hizika para* — a cow and a garment got into a kind of altercation and damaged one another. Tosafot works out precisely how this could occur: if the *talit* caused the *para* to slip, that would function like a *bor birshut harabim*; if the *para* ate the *talit*, that would need to occur in *reshut hanizak* and would be a form of *shen*. Perhaps the cow gored the garment, or consumed it in an unusual manner. Regardless of the precise mechanism, the *braita* rules: *ein omrim tze para be-talit ve-talit be-para* — we do not simply declare a swap, saying that the cow covers the garment's damage and the garment covers the cow's damage and the whole matter is settled. Rather, *shamnin otan bidamim* — you assess each loss in monetary terms, determine the difference, and whoever holds the balance pays that difference.

### Tosafot Chidushim

This ruling may seem self-evident, yet Tosafot identifies two distinct *chidushim* embedded here. The first concerns the case where the *para* damaged the *talit*. If the ox gored and was a *tam* — an animal that has not yet established a pattern of goring — the well-known rule is that the owner pays half the damage, capped at the value of the goring animal itself. One might have thought that rather than merely owing money, the injured party automatically acquires a proprietary interest in the goring ox itself. The second *chidush* addresses the reverse scenario: the *talit* functioned as a *bor* and caused the cow to slip. One might have thought that even though the owner of the *talit* owes money for the damage to the cow, the injured party of the cow should at least be able to seize the *talit* itself that caused the harm — especially in a case where the owner of the *talit* has since died and left no landed property from which a lien could be exercised. The *braita* teaches that this too is incorrect. What is owed in both cases is a monetary obligation, not a proprietary claim on the damaging object.

### Rabbi Akiva's Ownership

The first *chidush* speaks directly to a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva holds the position of *yuchlaf hashor*: as soon as your ox gores my animal, I automatically become a part-owner in your ox. It is not merely that you owe me money; I now own a percentage of your animal as of the moment of the goring. This *mishna*, according to Tosafot, rules against Rabbi Akiva. When the *braita* says we do not simply declare a swap — *ein omrim tze para be-talit* — it is teaching that you do not acquire ownership of the goring ox. The owner of the ox owes you money, limited to the ox's value, but your claim does not translate into direct ownership of the animal.

The second *chidush* is perhaps less intuitive. By ordinary principles, one can only collect debts from landed property on which there is a lien. If the owner of the *talit* that caused the slip has died and left no land, the injured party might argue: surely I should at least have rights to the *talit* itself, the very object that caused my loss, even if I cannot collect from the orphans' other assets. The *braita* says no. The obligation here is purely monetary. How one pays is a separate question — one can pay in chattel, not necessarily in cash — but the nature of the obligation is defined as an amount of money owed, and not as any proprietary claim on the damaging object itself.

## Shaveh Kesef

### Chattel Payment

The *gemara* now turns to the next phrase: *shaveh kesef*, monetary equivalent. The natural reading would seem to be that one need not pay in actual cash; any chattel equivalent in value will suffice — even *subin*, bran, as the *gemara* uses as its example. This is how the term is most classically understood.

### Braita's Land Read

What the *gemara* actually does with this phrase is, however, surprising. A *braita* interprets *shaveh kesef* in a completely unexpected way: *shaveh kesef melamed she-ein beis din nizkakin ela le-nekhasim she-yesh lahem achrayus* — *shaveh kesef* teaches that *beis din* will only deal with, that is to say seize or levy, property that bears liens, which means land. According to this reading, *shaveh kesef* refers not to chattel as a payment medium but specifically to real property.

This is genuinely puzzling on two levels. First, why should *shaveh kesef* mean land, when it most naturally means chattel equivalent in monetary value? Second, why would *beis din* be limited to seizing land when the *mazik* is alive and directly obligated? The *braita* adds a further clause: if the *nizak* went ahead and physically seized chattel from the *mazik* on his own, *beis din* will allow him to keep it. Why? If *beis din* cannot officially levy chattel, why would a unilateral seizure by the *nizak* be validated?

The *gemara* resolves the second question: the rule applies specifically *be-yasmei*, where the *mazik* has died and we are dealing with orphans. The heirs are not directly obligated in their father's debt; collection from the estate is limited to landed property on which liens had been established. And liens, the *gemara* clarifies, are created automatically — the moment one causes damage, the injured party holds a lien on the *mazik*'s land. *Beis din* does not create the lien; it assists in exercising it.

The case of unauthorized seizure during the *mazik*'s lifetime is resolved by reference to the principle of *shetafas mechayim*: if the *nizak* seized chattel while the *mazik* was still alive, at a moment when the debt was directly owed by a living obligor, *beis din* will validate that seizure retroactively as payment. One might note, as was raised in the course of the *sugya*, that this bears resemblance to the principle of *avid inish dina le-nafshei* — taking the law into one's own hands — a topic that receives fuller treatment later in the *gemara* at 27b.

### No Fixed Price

The *gemara* then circles back to the primary puzzle: since when does *shaveh kesef* mean land? Rabba bar Ulla offers an explanation: *davar ha-shaveh ke-kesef* means something that can be worth an unlimited sum, with no fixed market price — and that is land, because land is formally excluded from the *parasha* of *ona'ah*, the prohibition of overcharging. Since there is no fixed price for land, it can be said to be equivalent to any amount of money, and hence is called *shaveh kesef*.

The *gemara* objects: slaves and documents of debt also lack the prohibition of *ona'ah*. Rabba bar Ulla then reformulates: *davar ha-nikneh be-kesef* — something acquired with money. Land is acquired through a monetary transaction, whereas chattel is acquired through *meshikha* and *hagbahah*, physical acts of acquisition. The *gemara* presses again: slaves are also acquired with money. Rashi and Tosafot note that the inclusion of *shtaros* — loan documents — in this category is itself not straightforward, since transferring debt obligations does not clearly follow the same rules. Regardless, the objection stands at least for *avadim*.

### Like Money Not Money

Rav Ashi then proposes an altogether different reading: *shaveh kesef ve-lo kesef* — something that is *like* money but is not money itself. And who is *like* money? *Ve-hani kulu kesef ninhu* — all chattel is considered money. This follows from a point established earlier in the *gemara* regarding *meitav*: when one pays with chattel, even low-quality chattel, it can serve as payment, because movable property is presumed to be readily liquidable and therefore functionally equivalent to cash. What is genuinely *like* money but not quite money is land. Hence *shaveh kesef* designates land, because land is the one category that approximates but does not equal cash in terms of its acquisition and transfer mechanisms.

As the *gemara* itself acknowledges, this is not the straightforward reading of *shaveh kesef*. The most natural interpretation — and indeed the one that governs its use throughout *halakha* — is simply that payment can be made in any chattel equivalent in value to money. But the *braita* reads it as land, and the *gemara* works through various attempts to explain how that reading is derived.

## Bifnei Beis Din

### Court Requirement

The *gemara* next addresses *bifnei beis din* — assessment and payment must occur in the presence of a court. One might have thought that this refers to the situation where one sold off all one's property before going to court, leaving the plaintiff with nothing to collect. But the *gemara* immediately objects: if any of the sold property was land, the injured party should still be able to collect based on the existing lien. *Beis din* does not create liens; liens arise automatically at the moment of damage or borrowing. The court's role is to assist in enforcing them, not to generate them. Selling one's land before appearing in court should therefore make no difference to the plaintiff's ability to levy on those properties.

### Semicha Authority

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets *bifnei beis din*: *prat le-beis din hedyotos* — it excludes a court of commoners, a *beis din* that lacks the ordained authority traced back to Moshe Rabbeinu. This *semikhah* — distinct from the modern rabbinical ordination that bears the same name — empowers judges to adjudicate capital cases and *knasot*, monetary fines. A court empowered only to handle standard monetary litigation (*dinei mammonot*) cannot adjudicate the types of claims present here. The reason is that some of the obligations arising from the laws of damages — as the *gemara* is about to elaborate — are classified as *knas*, punitive fines rather than compensatory payments. Imposing a punishment requires a court that holds a level of divine authority, conceptually linked to the verse *ad ha-Elohim yavo devar shneihem*. A court of *hedyotos* lacks that standing.

### Valid Witnesses

The *mishna* continues: *al pi eidim* — through witnesses. This seems obvious; of course adjudication requires witnesses. The *chidush*, the *gemara* explains, lies in the phrase that follows: *prat le-modeh be-knas ve-achar kach ba'u eidim she-hu patur* — to exclude the case where one admitted to a fine and witnesses subsequently arrived. Since we are dealing with cases of *knas*, the rule of self-admission — *modeh be-knas patur* — comes into play.

---

## Modeh Beknas

### Self-Admission Exclusion

The underlying principle here is that self-admission cannot create an obligation to pay a fine. This distinguishes a *knas* fundamentally from *mamon*, ordinary civil liability. In matters of compensation, self-admission suffices and even obligates. In matters of fine, it does not.

### Fines as Punishment

The reason for this distinction is structural. A fine is conceptually a punishment imposed through monetary means, analogous to lashes — a punishment imposed through the body. Just as lashes require objective testimony and cannot be self-imposed, so too a monetary fine requires objective testimony. The principle of *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* — a person cannot render himself wicked — functions here not merely as a procedural protection but as a substantive rule: the court's authority to punish depends on externally established guilt. Self-incrimination does not supply that foundation. Moreover, the court imposing the fine must possess the requisite *semikhah*, since the fine represents not compensation but a divinely authorized sanction.

### Witnesses After Admission

There is a further debate referenced in the *gemara*: if one admits to a fine and witnesses subsequently arrive, is one obligated? One opinion holds that subsequent witnesses do render one liable — the initial admission is simply irrelevant but does not permanently foreclose the obligation. A second opinion holds that having admitted first, the person is permanently exempt, even if witnesses later corroborate the event. The *gemara* notes that if one follows the second view, the *chidush* of *al pi eidim* is that witnesses alone create the fine obligation. If one follows the first view — that subsequent witnesses do create liability — then *al pi eidim* is needed for a different purpose, which leads into the next clause: *bnei chorin bnei bris*.

---

## Bifnei Beis Din (II)

### Bnei Chorin Bnei Brit

Valid witnesses must be *bnei chorin* — free persons — and *bnei bris* — members of the covenant. *Bnei chorin le-mautei avadim*: free persons to exclude non-Jewish slaves, who are not valid witnesses, even though they bear a partial obligation in *mitzvot*. *Bnei bris le-mautei ovdei kochavim*: members of the covenant to exclude non-Jews. The *gemara* establishes that both exclusions are necessary. One might have thought that excluding slaves — who occupy a diminished societal standing because *halakha* does not recognize their family relationships — is straightforward, whereas non-Jews, who do possess recognized family relationships and are of equal standing in society, might be includable. Conversely, one might have excluded non-Jews on account of their greater distance from the obligations of *mitzvot*, while thinking slaves, who are partly obligated in *mitzvot*, might qualify. Each exclusion is therefore necessary to convey that neither category is valid.

One notable observation arises here: the list of valid witnesses mentions *bnei chorin* and *bnei bris* but not women — even though the *mishna* explicitly mentions women in its subsequent clause. Women are in practice excluded from serving as witnesses, yet the *braita* does not include that exclusion in this list. A possible explanation is that it is simply taken for granted, though the asymmetry is noteworthy.

This raises the question, engaged in medieval practice, of how women's testimony might be handled in *dinei mammonot*. In medieval Ashkenaz, where women were frequently active participants in commerce, the *rishonim* developed a framework of accepting women's testimony not as formal *edut* but as *birur davar* — a form of circumstantial evidentiary weight that operates outside the technical framework of witness law. This approach sidestepped the formal disqualification while addressing the practical needs of a community where women were often central economic actors.

The related question of women serving as *dayanim* — judges — is taken up by Tosafot in the context of the verse *asher tasim lifneihem*, which the *gemara* elsewhere reads as referring to the judges before whom cases are brought, rather than the litigants. If *lifneihem* there includes women as parties to litigation, could it also be read to include women as judges? Tosafot notes that the general principle — *kol ha-kasher ladun kasher le-ha'id* — would seem to foreclose this, since women are disqualified as witnesses and therefore disqualified as judges. But Tosafot reframes the principle: it applies to men who are qualified to judge being qualified to testify. A woman might, on this reading, be a qualified judge even though she cannot serve as a witness. The case of Devorah is raised and then somewhat set aside: *shema hayu mekabelim otah aleihem mipnei Shekhinah* — perhaps they accepted her authority on account of her prophetic status, which constitutes a form of consensual arbitration rather than a demonstration of women's formal judicial standing. Tosafot leaves the question of whether a woman can serve as a *kasher dayan* as genuinely open.

## Women in Damages

### Women Included

The *mishna* states: *ve-nashim bikhlal ha-nezek* — women are included in the laws of damages. The *gemara* asks: *mena hani mili?* From where is this derived?

### Masculine Language Defau…

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, and similarly the school of Rabbi Yishmael, derive it from the verse *ish o isha ki ya'asu mikol chatot ha-adam* — if a man or woman commits any of the sins of a person. From this, *hishvah ha-katuv isha le-ish le-khol onashim she-ba-Torah* — the Torah equates women to men with regard to all punishments in the Torah. The reason such a verse is needed, as Tosafot explains, is that the entire Torah is written *be-lashon zachar*, in the masculine gender. As in classical legal drafting, the masculine formulation is presumed to function as a gender-neutral default unless explicitly stated otherwise. But this raises the question of whether women are automatically included or whether explicit inclusion is required in each domain.

### Textual Proofs

Multiple verses are brought to cover different domains. The school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from *ish o isha* that women are included in all punishments. The school of Rabbi Elazar derives from *ve-eileh ha-mishpatim asher tasim lifneihem* — the word *lifneihem* is not specifically masculine, and from it we learn *hishvah ha-katuv ish le-isha le-khol dinim she-ba-Torah*: civil law applies equally to men and women. The school of Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yose Ha-Gelili derive from *ve-nigach ish o isha* — an ox that gores a man or a woman — that with respect to being the injured party, the victim of a goring, men and women are treated identically: *hishvah ha-katuv ish le-isha le-khol mitot she-ba-Torah*.

All three are necessary.

### Women's Hava Amina

### Not Bar Mitzvah

### Women Judges Question

Had only the first verse been stated — that women bring *korbanot* for sins — one might have said the Torah showed *rachmanus* on women so that they could achieve *kaparah*, but that this does not extend to civil law, where women, presumed not to be primary actors in commerce (*ish de-var masa u-matan*), might not be subject to the full framework of litigation. Had only the civil-law inclusion been stated, one might have inferred it was necessary only to ensure that women could function in economic life (*ki heichi de-tehavi lah chiyusa*), but that when it comes to *kaparah* — atonement — a woman, *de-lav bat mitzvah* in the sense of being less comprehensively obligated in *mitzvot*, perhaps does not fully access the atonement mechanism in the same way as a man who is more completely obligated. Had only these two been stated without the verse about the gored victim, one might — and here the *hava amina* becomes genuinely disturbing — have thought that if an ox kills a woman, the owner need not pay *kofer*, because *kofer* is linked to the gravity of the life taken, and a life with fewer *mitzvot* obligations might be seen as less fully within that calculus. The *gemara* closes this *hava amina* by pointing out that *kofer* is paid because *ika ibbud neshama* — there is a loss of life — and therefore of course it applies to a woman equally. All three verses are necessary, and the *maskana* is that women are included in all domains of Torah law — as agents, as litigants, and as victims — unless explicitly excluded.

The *gemara* here is, as the *hava amina* stage reveals, working with certain presumptions about women's status that are difficult for the modern reader. The conclusion is unequivocal in equalizing women across the board in tort law, but the very structure of the *tzricha* — needing proofs in each domain — reflects underlying assumptions that the rabbis themselves were working to address through the exegetical process.

## Both Parties Pay

### Joint Participation

The *mishna* concludes: *ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik be-tashlumin* — both the injured party and the damager participate in the payment of damages. The *gemara* will now examine what this phrase means in light of the central dispute about the nature of *palga nizka*, half damages by *keren*.

---

## Half Damages Debate

### Rav Papa Compensation

*Palga nizka* — half damages in the case of a *tam* ox that gores — is the subject of a famous *itra'mar* between Rav Papa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua. Rav Papa holds: *mamona* — it is genuine compensation. His reasoning: *kasvar stam shvarim lav be-chezkat shimur kaimi* — ordinary oxen are not presumed to be inherently self-restrained; even an ox that has never gored still requires supervision. If an ox has not been watched and it goes out and gores, there is an element of negligence on the owner's part. Strictly speaking, therefore, the owner *should* pay full damages. *U-ve-din hu de-ba'ei le-shlumai kulei* — by strict logic he should pay everything. But the Torah has *rachmanus* on him — *de-akhti lav ya'ad*, the ox has not yet established a pattern of goring, has not gone through the *muad* process, and so the owner stands right at the edge of negligence. Because the negligence is borderline, the Torah goes easy on him and requires only half. But it remains genuine *mamon*: the owner was genuinely negligent, and what he pays is real compensation for real damages attributable to his failure.

### Rav Huna Fine

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua holds: *knasa* — it is a fine. *Kasvar stam shvarim be-chezkat shimur kaimi* — ordinary oxen *are* presumed to be naturally restrained and not in need of close supervision. Therefore the owner was *not* negligent. *U-be-dino de-lo le-shalem klal* — by strict logic he should pay nothing at all. But the Torah fined him anyway — *ki heikhi de-lintrei le-torei* — to incentivize a higher level of care. By knowing that even a *tam* goring will cost half the damage, every ox owner is pushed to maintain vigilance beyond what strict negligence liability would demand. The half payment is not compensation for actual fault; it is a punitive incentive to prevent harm.

### Borderline Agreement

Both positions, when placed side by side, agree on one thing: the *tam* goring occupies a genuine borderline. That is precisely why the Torah set the payment at half, not full and not zero. Rav Papa reads the halved payment as the Torah moderating a genuine obligation downward; Rav Huna reads it as the Torah imposing a liability where none strictly existed. One says it is slightly above the threshold of negligence with the Torah being lenient; the other says it is slightly below that threshold with the Torah being strict. The disagreement is about which side of the line the *tam* goring falls on.

## Mamona or Knasa

### Incomplete List

The *gemara* now attempts to resolve the *machloket* between Rav Papa and Rav Huna from Tannaitic sources. The first attempted proof: the *mishna* that enumerates the differences between *tam* and *muad* does not include *modeh be-knas* — self-admission — among those differences. If *palga nizka* were a *knas*, one would expect the list to mention that a *tam* cannot be paid on self-admission while a *muad* can. The omission suggests it is *mamona*. The *gemara* deflects: *amar tana ve-shayer* — the *tanna* taught an incomplete list.

---

## Both Parties Pay (II)

### Compensation Model

Returning to the *mishna*'s phrase *ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik be-tashlumin*: if *palga nizka* is *mamona*, the meaning is elegant and precise. The *mazik* genuinely owes full compensation, but pays only half; the *nizak* effectively absorbs the other half of his own loss. Both parties thus participate in the payment: the *mazik* pays half, and the *nizak* bears half. They each lose an equal portion of the damages.

### Fine Model

If *palga nizka* is *knasa*, the framing is different. The *mazik* technically owes nothing, yet receives a windfall of half the damage's value. The *nizak*, meanwhile, is out the full loss of his animal. In what sense can one say the *nizak* "participates in payment"? What *tashlumin* is the *nizak* paying when the entire amount is a fine that the *mazik* did not truly owe?

### Carcass Depreciation

The *gemara* answers: *lo nitzrecha ela le-pichut neveila* — the phrase teaches that the *nizak* bears the depreciation of the carcass. When the animal is gored and killed, the carcass immediately begins to lose value. The *nizak* suffers that ongoing loss and cannot pass it to the *mazik*. That is how the *nizak* "participates in the payment" even under the *knasa* model: not by paying for half of what is owed, but by absorbing the diminishing value of what he was left with.

---

## Mamona or Knasa (II)

### Counter Incomplete

The *gemara* notes that the teaching about carcass depreciation appeared already in an earlier *mishna* — *tashlumei nezek melamed she-ha-bealim metaplin be-neveila* — and asks whether the earlier *mishna* and the current one overlap or address different cases. The answer: *chada be-tam ve-chada be-muad* — one addresses goring by a *tam* and the other goring by a *muad*. Both cases need to be stated: in the *tam* case one might have thought the *nizak* is partly spared since the *mazik* pays only half; in the *muad* case one might have thought the *mazik*, paying full, should also absorb the carcass depreciation. Each case requires explicit teaching.

Returning to the first proof attempt: the *mishna* listing differences between *tam* and *muad* omitted *modeh be-knas*. The *gemara* says this does not prove the list is complete, since another difference was also omitted: *kofer*. A *muad* pays full *kofer* and a *tam* pays none. The *gemara* responds: *ha mani Rabbi Yosei Ha-Gelili hi de-amar tam meshalem chatzi kofer* — this *mishna* may follow Rabbi Yosei Ha-Gelili, who holds that a *tam* does pay half *kofer*. If so, the distinction between *tam* and *muad* on *kofer* does not exist according to that opinion, and there is nothing left off the list with respect to *kofer*. So the first proof remains inconclusive; the list may simply be incomplete.

### Self-Admission Proof

The *gemara* then brings a *braita*: *heimit shori et ploni o shoro shel ploni, harei zeh meshalem al pi atzmo* — if I admit that my ox gored and killed a person or gored another's ox, I pay based on my own admission. The *gemara* asks: is this not speaking even of a *tam*? If so, it shows that half damages by a *tam* is *mamona*, since the owner pays on his own admission. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps the *braita* speaks only of a *muad*. But then, the *gemara* responds, what would the ruling be for a *tam*? If *palga nizka* is *knasa*, then by a *tam* one should not pay on self-admission. Why does the *braita* not tell us that explicitly — contrasting *muad* with *tam* — rather than contrasting the case of goring a person or animal with the case of goring a slave? *Liflog ve-litni bididah* — let the *braita* divide the *tam/muad* distinction internally. The *gemara* answers: the *braita* is speaking entirely of *muad* cases and has no occasion to address *tam* goring at all.

### The Key Rule

The *gemara* advances what appears to be a decisive *braita*: *ze ha-kelal, kol ha-meshalem yoter al mah she-hizik eino meshalem al pi atzmo* — here is the general rule: anyone who pays *more* than the actual damage does not pay on self-admission. Payment in excess of actual damage is the hallmark of *knas*. The implicit counterpart: if you pay *less* than the damage, it would seem to be *mamon*, compensation. And half damages is less than full — so it should be *mamona*.

### Resolving Exception

The *gemara* immediately presses: does paying *less* than the damage always mean it is *mamon*? The *braita*'s formulation says if you pay *more* you cannot pay on self-admission — implying that if you pay less, you can. But should one say that if you pay less it is *always* compensation? That would mean one should have stated the rule differently: *kol she-eino meshalem kamah she-hizik* — anyone who does not pay the *exact* amount of the damage does not pay on self-admission. That broader formulation would capture both more and less than exact damage as potential *knas*. The fact that the *braita* said only "more" seems to prove that paying less is *mamon*. This appears to be a strong proof — *tiyuvta*.

### Tzerorot Example

The *gemara* responds: *lo psika lei* — the rule cannot be stated in that broader form, because there are cases where one pays *less* than the full damage and it is nonetheless *mamona*, genuine compensation. The example: *chatzi nezek tzerorot* — the half damages paid for pebbles kicked by an animal in the public domain. In that case, the animal's owner was fully negligent; the indirect causation through the pebbles simply means the law reduces the payment to half as a matter of *halakha*, not as a punitive measure. It is *mamona*, but it is less than full damage. Because this exception exists, the *braita* could not formulate the rule as "any amount other than exact damages is *knas*" — that would have incorrectly classified *chatzi nezek tzerorot* as a fine. Hence the rule was stated only for the "more than" case, leaving "less than" ambiguous. The attempted proof from this *braita* therefore fails to establish definitively that *chatzi nezek keren* is *mamona*.

## Final Ruling Knasa

### Ruled as Fine

After systematically deflecting every Tannaitic proof that would have established *palga nizka* as *mamona*, the *gemara* delivers its ruling: *ve-hilkheta palga nizka knasa* — the *halakha* is that half damages of a *tam* goring is a *knas*, a fine. This is remarkable. The *gemara* just concluded *tiyuvta* — a definitive refutation — of the *mamona* position, and then immediately rules as *knasa*. The *gemara* acknowledges the tension: *tiyuvta ve-hilkheta?* It responds: *ein* — yes. The reason the *tiyuvta* was considered so powerful was because the *braita* said "more than the damage" — implying less than the damage is *mamona*. But in fact that implication does not hold universally, since *chatzi nezek tzerorot* is *mamona* while paying less than full. The *braita*'s formulation was not meant to be exhaustive in the other direction. With that resolved, the *gemara* is free to rule that *chatzi nezek keren* is *knasa*, in accordance with Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua.

### Implications Pending

The practical consequences of classifying *chatzi nezek* of a *tam* as *knasa* are substantial and will be taken up in the *gemara* that follows. Among the most significant: from what source does one pay the *knas*? The *gemara*'s resolution of that question — touching on the rule of payment from the body of the animal, the principle of *modeh be-knas patur*, and related issues — will be the subject of the continuation.
