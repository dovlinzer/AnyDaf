# Sanhedrin 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashiach Introduction

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ אֵימַת אָתֵי בַּר נַפְלֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן בַּר נַפְלֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָשִׁיחַ. מָשִׁיחַ בַּר נַפְלֵי קָרֵית לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דִּכְתִיב ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא אָקִים אֶת סֻכַּת דָּוִיד הַנֹּפֶלֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Rabbi Yitzḥak: Have you heard when the son of giants [*bar niflei*] will come?** Rabbi Yitzḥak **said to him: Who** is **the son of giants?** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** He is the **Messiah.** Rabbi Yitzḥak asked him: Do **you call the Messiah son of giants?** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Yes, as it is written: "On that day I will establish the Tabernacle of David that is fallen [*hanofelet*]"** (Amos 9:11).

The *daf* opens two lines from the bottom of 96b, picking up from the preceding discussion of *Sancheriv* and *Nevuchadnezzar* and the fall of those empires. The connection to what follows is not entirely clear on the surface. One possible reading is that the prior sugya focused on those who came to war against Israel and inaugurated the *galus*, and the Gemara now naturally raises the next question: when will *Mashiach* come? This transition carries the discussion into a sustained and remarkable section of Sanhedrin 97 dedicated to the nature of redemption, the signs of *Mashiach*'s coming, and the dangers of trying to calculate the appointed time.

### Bar Nafli

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוֹר שֶׁבֶּן דָּוִד בָּא בּוֹ, תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מִתְמַעֲטִים, וְהַשְּׁאָר עֵינֵיהֶם כָּלוֹת בְּיָגוֹן וַאֲנָחָה, וְצָרוֹת רַבּוֹת וּגְזֵרוֹת קָשׁוֹת מִתְחַדְּשׁוֹת, עַד שֶׁהָרִאשׁוֹנָה פְּקוּדָה, שְׁנִיָּה מְמַהֶרֶת לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** During **the generation in which** the Messiah, **son of David, comes, Torah scholars decrease; and** as for **the rest** of the people, **their eyes fail with sorrow and grief, and troubles increase. And the harsh decrees will be introduced; before the first passes the second quickly comes.**

*Amar leh Rav Nachman le-Rav Yitzchak*: Rav Nachman said to Rav Yitzchak, *mi shemi'a lakh eimat ati Bar Nafli* — have you heard when the son of Nafli will come? Rav Yitzchak responded: who is this Bar Nafli? Rav Nachman replied: *Mashiach*. Rav Yitzchak pressed further: you are calling *Mashiach* "Bar Nafli" — the son of those who have fallen? Rav Nachman confirmed this, citing the verse *ba-yom ha-hu akim et sukkat David ha-nofelet* — on that day I will raise up the fallen booth, the fallen dominion, of King David.

What is striking here is that the emphasis falls not on the glory and triumph that *Mashiach* will bring, but on the suffering and collapse from which he will emerge. The very name given to *Mashiach* — Bar Nafli, son of the fallen — frames the redemption as something that rises out of hardship and tragedy.

### R. Yochanan: Generation…

The passage we have just read establishes this portrait immediately. *Hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan*: Rabbi Yochanan taught that in the generation when the son of David comes, *talmidei chakhamim* will be diminished — their numbers will shrink. The rest of the people will have their eyes dimmed with anguish and mourning. Tremendous afflictions and harsh decrees will come in rapid succession — before the effects of the first tragedy have passed, the next is already on its way. This portrait of the generation preceding *Mashiach* is entirely consistent with the epithet Bar Nafli: it will be a time of great difficulty and suffering, with the redemption emerging from the depths of the fall.

## Shmita Cycle

### 7-Year Progression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁבוּעַ שֶׁבֶּן דָּוִד בָּא בּוֹ, שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה מִתְקַיֵּים מִקְרָא זֶה ״וְהִמְטַרְתִּי עַל עִיר אֶחָת וְעַל עִיר אַחַת לֹא אַמְטִיר״. שְׁנִיָּה, חִיצֵּי רָעָב מִשְׁתַּלְּחִים. שְׁלִישִׁית, רָעָב גָּדוֹל, וּמֵתִים אֲנָשִׁים וְנָשִׁים וָטַף, חֲסִידִים וְאַנְשֵׁי מַעֲשֶׂה, וְתוֹרָה מִשְׁתַּכַּחַת מִלּוֹמְדֶיהָ. בִּרְבִיעִית: שׂוֹבַע וְאֵינוֹ שׂוֹבַע. בַּחֲמִישִׁית: שׂוֹבַע גָּדוֹל, וְאוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין וּשְׂמֵחִין, וְתוֹרָה חוֹזֶרֶת לְלוֹמְדֶיהָ. בְּשִׁשִּׁית: קוֹלוֹת. בִּשְׁבִיעִית: מִלְחָמוֹת. בְּמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית, בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **the seven-year** period, i.e., the Sabbatical cycle, **during which** the Messiah, **son of David, comes:** During **the first year, this verse will be fulfilled: "And I will cause it to rain upon one city and cause it not to rain upon another city"** (Amos 4:7). During the **second** year of that period, **arrows of famine will be shot,** indicating that there will be famine only in certain places. During the **third** year **there will be a great famine, and men, women, children, the pious, and men of action will die, and the Torah is forgotten by those who study it. During the fourth** year there will be **plenty but not** great **plenty. During the fifth** year there will be **great plenty and they will eat, and drink, and rejoice, and the Torah will return to those who study it. During the sixth** year, heavenly **voices** will be heard. **During the Sabbatical** Year, **wars,** e.g., the war of Gog and Magog, will be waged involving the Jewish people. **During** the year after **the conclusion of the Sabbatical** Year, **the son of David will come.**

*Tanu rabbanan*: our rabbis taught regarding the *shavua* — the seven-year *shmita* cycle — in which *Mashiach* will come. The progression unfolds year by year. In the first year, rain will fall on one city but not another, producing sporadic famine alongside relative plenty. In the second year, pangs of hunger will spread to everyone. By the third year, a great famine will set in — men, women, and children will die, along with the righteous and people of good deeds; the *Torah* will be forgotten by those who had learned it, overwhelmed by hunger. In the fourth year, *sova ve-eino sova* — there will be satiation but not full satiation, the pendulum beginning to swing upward. By the fifth year, great satiation returns: people will eat, drink, and rejoice, and the *Torah* will return to those who had learned it. This is the shape of a U-curve. In the sixth year, *kolot* — sounds — will come; Rashi understands these as sounds heralding the arrival of *Mashiach*. In the seventh year, great wars will take place, presumably including the war of *Gog u-Magog*. And finally, *bemotzei shvi'is*, at the conclusion of the seventh year, *ben David ba* — *Mashiach* will come.

The Gemara's description is striking enough that one who is so inclined might read it as a lens through which to view more recent history — the devastation of the third year echoing the Holocaust, in which men, women, and children perished and entire *Torah* communities were destroyed, followed by an upward arc of rebuilding. That reading remains, of course, speculative, but the structure of the passage invites it.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא כַּמָּה שָׁבוּעֵי דַּהֲוָה כֵּן, וְלָא אֲתָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשִׁשִּׁית קוֹלוֹת, בִּשְׁבִיעִית מִלְחָמוֹת – מִי הֲוָה? וְעוֹד, כְּסִדְרָן מִי הֲוָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Haven't there been several Sabbatical** cycles during **which** events **transpired** in **that** manner **and** nevertheless, the Messiah **did not come? Abaye said: Have** the phenomena: **During the sixth** year, heavenly **voices,** and **during the Sabbatical** Year, **wars, transpired? And furthermore, have** all these phenomena **transpired in** the **order** in which they were listed in the *baraita*?

Rav Yosef raised an objection: *ha kama shevi'isa dahava ken velo asa* — we have had several *shmita* cycles that seemed to follow this pattern, and yet *Mashiach* did not come.

### Abaye Response

Abaye responded by sharpening the challenge: *bishishis kolos bishvi'is milchamos mi hava* — did we actually have the sixth year with those sounds heralding *Mashiach*, and the seventh year with those specific wars? That did not happen. And moreover, *kesidran mi hava* — nor did the events occur in the precise sequence described. Some years of famine and some years of plenty, yes, but not in the exact U-shaped order laid out in the *braita*. Furthermore, even granting that such events occurred, it does not follow that when this pattern appears *Mashiach* will necessarily come — only that these are the conditions preceding his arrival.

## Generation Collapse

### Social Breakdown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, דּוֹר שֶׁבֶּן דָּוִד בָּא בּוֹ – בֵּית הַוַּועַד יִהְיֶה לִזְנוּת, וְהַגָּלִיל יֶחֱרַב, וְהַגַּבְלָן יֵאשַׁם, וְאַנְשֵׁי גְבוּל יְסוֹבְבוּ מֵעִיר לָעִיר וְלֹא יְחוֹנָנוּ, וְחׇכְמַת הַסּוֹפְרִים תִּסְרַח, וְיִרְאֵי חֵטְא יִמָּאֵסוּ, וּפְנֵי הַדּוֹר כִּפְנֵי כֶלֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: During the generation that the son of David comes, the hall of the assembly** of the Sages **will be** designated **for prostitution, and the Galilee will be destroyed, and the Gavlan,** i.e., Bashan, **will be desolate, and the residents of the border** who flee the neighboring gentiles **will circulate from city to city and will receive no sympathy. The wisdom of scholars will diminish, and sin-fearing** people **will be despised. And the face of the generation will be like the face of a dog** in its impudence and shamelessness.

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Yehuda says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *batei va'ad* — houses of assembly, places where sages would gather, even the *beis midrash* — will become houses of fornication. The Galilee will be destroyed. The *Gavlan* — understood by some as the Golan or the northern region near *Har Chermon* — will be laid desolate. The people of the border cities will wander from city to city and will not be given rest or welcome, *velo yechanenu*. For those who would read this in a contemporary lens, the image of border communities evacuated and wandering from city to city, unsettled, could hardly be more vivid.

### Wisdom Spoiled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָאֱמֶת נֶעְדֶּרֶת״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּהִי הָאֱמֶת נֶעְדֶּרֶת וְסָר מֵרָע מִשְׁתּוֹלֵל״. מַאי ״וַתְּהִי הָאֱמֶת נֶעְדֶּרֶת״? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַב: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית עֲדָרִים עֲדָרִים וְהוֹלֶכֶת לָהּ. מַאי ״וְסָר מֵרָע מִשְׁתּוֹלֵל״? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא: כׇּל מִי שֶׁסָּר מֵרָע מִשְׁתּוֹלֵל עַל הַבְּרִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the truth will be lacking, as it is stated: "And the truth is lacking [*ne'ederet*], and he who departs from evil is negated"** (Isaiah 59:15). **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **And the truth is lacking [*ne'ederet*]?** The Sages of **the study hall of Rav said:** This **teaches that** truth **will become like** so many **flocks [*adarim*] and walk** away. **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"And he that departs from evil is negated"?** The Sages of **the study hall of Rabbi Sheila said: Anyone who deviates from evil is deemed insane by the people.**

*Ve-chochmat ha-sofrim tisrach* — the wisdom of the sages will be spoiled. *Ve-yirei cheit yim'asu* — those who fear sin will be despised. Values will be turned upside down. *U-fnei ha-dor kipnei kelev* — the face of the generation will be like the face of a dog. The Maharsha explains that dogs appear friendly and loyal while concealing betrayal beneath the surface. *Ve-ha-emet ne'ederet* — and truth will be absent and lost.

### Truth Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא, לֵיכָּא קוּשְׁטָא בְּעָלְמָא. אֲמַר לִי הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן, וְרַב טָבוּת שְׁמֵיהּ – וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי שְׁמֵיהּ – דְּאִי הֲווֹ יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ כֹּל חֲלָלֵי דְּעָלְמָא לָא הֲוָה מְשַׁנֵּי בְּדִיבּוּרֵיהּ. זִימְנָא חֲדָא אִיקְּלַעִי לְהָהוּא אַתְרָא, וְקוּשְׁטָא שְׁמֵיהּ, וְלָא הֲווֹ מְשַׁנֵּי בְּדִיבּוּרַיְיהוּ, וְלָא הֲוָה מָיֵית אִינִישׁ מֵהָתָם בְּלָא זִימְנֵיהּ. נְסֵיבִי אִיתְּתָא מִינְּהוֹן, וַהֲווֹ לִי תְּרֵין בְּנִין מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the lack of truth, **Rava says: Initially I would say** that **there is no truth** anywhere **in the world.** There was **a certain one of the Sages, and Rav Tavut is his name, and some say Rav Tavyomei is his name,** who was so honest **that if they were** to **give him the entire world, he would not deviate** from the truth **in his statement.** He said to me: **One time I happened** to come **to a certain place, and Truth is its name, and** its residents **would not deviate** from the truth **in their statements, and no person from there would die prematurely. I married a woman from** among **them, and I had two sons from her.**

The Gemara asks: what does *vatehi ha-emet ne'ederet* — truth will be lost — actually mean? *Amri d'vei Rav*: this teaches that truth will gather itself into flocks and wander away. The Maharsha explains that with respect to *Torah* and *talmidei chakhamim*, this does not mean truth will vanish utterly, but rather that those who carry truth will have to abandon the corrupt centers of civilization and seek out places of their own in which to dwell.

The verse continues: *ve-sar me-ra mishtolel* — one who turns from evil will be mocked. *Amri d'vei Rabbi Shila*: anyone who turns away from evil will be seen as a fool in the eyes of the general population. Good will be viewed as stupidity.

*Amar Rava*: from the outset, Rava says, *me-reish hava amina leit kushta b'alma* — I used to say there is no real truth in this world. This world is one of *sheker*; no one can stand fully by the principle of truth. Then one of the rabbinic students — *Rav Tavut* by name, some say *Rav Tavyomi* — told Rava the following. This student was renowned for his absolute commitment to truth: even if given all the wealth in the world, *lo hava meshanei be-dibburei* — he would not misrepresent himself or say an untruth. And he told Rava that he had once come to a certain place called *Kushta* — Truth — where no one ever fudged anything, and no one died before his ripe old age. He settled there, married a woman from the city, and had two sons.

### Rava's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה יָתְבָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ וְקָא חָיְיפָא רֵישָׁא. אֲתַאי שִׁיבָבְתַהּ, טְרַפָא אַדַּשָּׁא. סְבַרִי: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא. אֲמַרִי לַהּ: לֵיתַהּ הָכָא. שְׁכִיבוּ לֵיהּ תְּרֵין בְּנֵיהּ. אֲתוֹ אִינָשֵׁי דְּאַתְרָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בְּמָטוּתָא מִינָּךְ, פּוֹק מֵאַתְרִין וְלָא תְּגָרֵי בְּהוּ מוֹתָנָא בְּהָנָךְ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **One day his wife was sitting and washing the** hair on her **head. Her neighbor came** and **knocked on the door. He thought:** It is **not proper conduct** to tell the neighbor that his wife is bathing. **He said to her: She is not here.** Since he deviated from the truth **his two sons died. The people** residing **in** that **place came before him** and **said to him: What** is the meaning of **this? He said to them: This was** the nature of the **incident,** and told them what happened. **They said to him: Please leave our place and do not provoke** premature **death upon these people.**

One day his wife was washing her hair when a neighbor knocked on the door. He thought: *lav orach ar'a* — it is not appropriate to say my wife is in the bath. So he told the neighbor: she is not here. His two sons then died. The people of the city came to him and asked what had happened. He explained that he had slightly misrepresented the situation to protect his wife's privacy. They said to him: please, *puk me-asra* — leave our city, lest you incite death among our people.

This is a genuinely arresting story, and it leaves one somewhat unsettled. On the one hand, it illustrates in dramatic terms the absolute standard of truth maintained in that city, and the lethal consequence of even the smallest departure from it. On the other hand, the Gemara elsewhere permits fudging in certain contexts — such as *kevod ha-beriyot*, human dignity and privacy — which is precisely what he was trying to protect. One is left wondering what the ultimate message is: is this a story demanding the harshest possible commitment to literal truth even at the cost of another's privacy? The Gemara does not resolve the tension explicitly.

## Alt. Accounts

### R. Nehorai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי נְהוֹרַאי אוֹמֵר, דּוֹר שֶׁבֶּן דָּוִד בָּא בּוֹ – נְעָרִים יַלְבִּינוּ פְּנֵי זְקֵנִים, וּזְקֵנִים יַעַמְדוּ לִפְנֵי נְעָרִים, וּבַת קָמָה בְּאִמָּהּ, וְכַלָּה בַּחֲמוֹתָהּ, וּפְנֵי הַדּוֹר כִּפְנֵי כֶלֶב, וְאֵין הַבֵּן מִתְבַּיֵּישׁ מֵאָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion of the messianic period. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Nehorai says: During the generation** in **which the son of David comes, youths will humiliate elders and elders will stand** in deference **before youths, and a daughter will rebel against her mother, and a bride against her mother-in-law, and the face of the generation will be like the face of a dog, and a son will not be ashamed before his father.**

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Nehorai says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *nearim yalbinu pnei zekenim* — youth will cause the faces of elders to go white with embarrassment. The elders will be expected to stand before the young rather than the reverse. A daughter will rise against her mother, a daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law. The face of the generation will be like the face of a dog. And a son will not be ashamed before his father. The passage distributes the specific failures interestingly: sons toward fathers, daughters toward mothers, daughters-in-law toward mothers-in-law.

### R. Nechemiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר, דּוֹר שֶׁבֶּן דָּוִד בָּא בּוֹ, הָעַזּוּת תִּרְבֶּה, וְהַיּוֹקָר יְעַוֵּת, וְהַגֶּפֶן יִתֵּן פִּרְיוֹ וְהַיַּיִן בְּיוֹקֶר, וְנֶהֱפָכָה כׇּל הַמַּלְכוּת לְמִינוּת, וְאֵין תּוֹכֵחָה. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁתִּתְהַפֵּךְ כׇּל הַמַּלְכוּת לְמִינוּת. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי קְרָא? ״כֻּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן טָהוֹר הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Neḥemya says: During the generation that the son of David comes, arrogance will proliferate and the cost** of living **will corrupt** people so they will engage in deceit. **The vine will produce its fruit, and** nevertheless, **the wine** will be **costly. And the entire** gentile **monarchy will be converted to** the **heresy** of Christianity, **and there will be no** inclination among the people to accept **rebuke.** This *baraita* **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The son of David will not come until the entire kingdom will be converted to heresy. Rava says: What is the verse** from which this statement is derived? It is the verse: **"It is all turned white; he is ritually pure"** (Leviticus 13:13).

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Nechemiah says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *ha-azut tirbeh* — brazenness will increase. *Ve-ha-yoker ya'avot* — that which deserves honor will be perverted. Rashi reads this as the proper showing of honor being turned on its head. The vine will give its fruit and there will be plentiful wine — that is the one seemingly positive note — yet *ve-ha-yayin be-yoker*, the wine will be very expensive. Rashi understands this to mean that demand will be so high, driven by widespread drunkenness, that price will be extreme despite abundance.

*Ve-nehepcha kol ha-malkhut le-minus* — and the entire kingdom will turn to *minut*, heresy. Since the Romans of Rabbi Nechemiah's time — the second century — practiced *avodah zarah*, and *minut* in the Gemara often refers to a form of Jewish heresy, specifically to what would become Christianity, this is most naturally read as a prediction that the Roman Empire would convert to Christianity. Constantine's conversion of Rome to Christianity occurred at the beginning of the fourth century, roughly two centuries after Rabbi Nechemiah. He may have sensed the direction in which things were moving. And though that conversion has long since occurred, *Mashiach* has not yet come, suggesting this is one necessary sign among many, not a sufficient condition alone.

### R. Yitzchak Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁתִּתְהַפֵּךְ כׇּל הַמַּלְכוּת לְמִינוּת. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי קְרָא? ״כֻּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן טָהוֹר הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** This *baraita* **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The son of David will not come until the entire kingdom will be converted to heresy. Rava says: What is the verse** from which this statement is derived? It is the verse: **"It is all turned white; he is ritually pure"** (Leviticus 13:13).

*Mesayei lei le-Rabbi Yitzchak* — Rabbi Nechemiah's teaching supports Rabbi Yitzchak's position, for Rabbi Yitzchak said: *ein Ben David ba ad she-tit'hapekh kol ha-malkhut le-minut* — *Mashiach* will not come until the entire kingdom turns to heresy. For Rabbi Yitzchak this is the defining prerequisite. That turning occurred approximately 1,700 years ago, yet we still await *Mashiach*.

*Amar Rava mai kra* — Rava asks, what is the scriptural basis? *Kulo hafakh lavan tahor hu* — if the entire diseased area turns white, it is pure. The image of *tzara'at* spreading until it covers everything completely, which counterintuitively signals purity, is invoked as a parallel: when the darkness spreads completely, that becomes the sign of the turn toward redemption.

## Arrival Conditions

### Divine Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יָדִין ה׳ עַמּוֹ [וְגוֹ׳] כִּי יִרְאֶה כִּי אָזְלַת יָד וְאֶפֶס עָצוּר וְעָזוּב״. אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּרְבּוּ הַמָּסוֹרוֹת. דָּבָר אַחֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּתְמַעֲטוּ הַתַּלְמִידִים. דָּבָר אַחֵר: עַד שֶׁתִּכְלֶה פְּרוּטָה מִן הַכִּיס. דָּבָר אַחֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּתְיָיאֲשׁוּ מִן הַגְּאוּלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶפֶס עָצוּר וְעָזוּב״ – כִּבְיָכוֹל אֵין סוֹמֵךְ וְעוֹזֵר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"For the Lord shall judge His people** and atone for His servants, **when He sees that their power is gone and there is none shut up or left"** (Deuteronomy 32:36). From the phrase "their power is gone" it is derived that **the son of David will not come until informers will proliferate. Alternatively,** the Messiah will not come **until the** number of **students** of Torah **diminishes. Alternatively,** the Messiah will not come **until** the ***peruta* will cease from the purse. Alternatively,** the Messiah will not come **until they despair from the redemption, as it is stated: "And there is none shut up or left," as though there were no supporter or helper for the Jewish people.**

*Tanu rabbanan* — our rabbis taught. The verse reads: *ki yadin Hashem amo* — God will judge His people — *ki yireh ki azlat yad ve-efes atzur ve-azuv* — when He sees that their power is gone and there is none guarded or supported. From this verse the Gemara derives multiple conditions for *Mashiach*'s arrival.

### Hand Extended

Four distinct readings are offered. First: *ein Ben David ba ad she-yirbu ha-mesorot* — *Mashiach* will not come until informers against Jews to the government greatly multiply, reading *azlat yad* as a hand extended against them. Second: *ad she-yim'atu ha-talmidim* — until the *talmidei chakhamim* are diminished, for as Rashi notes, *Torah* is what would strengthen Israel and its absence leaves them unsupported. Third: *ad she-tichleh perutah min ha-kis* — until no money remains in the pocket, reading *azlat yad* as the hand reaching into one's pocket and finding nothing. Fourth — and perhaps closest to the plain sense of the verse: *ad she-yit'yashu min ha-ge'ulah* — until they despair of redemption entirely. It is at the moment of the greatest loss of hope, when it feels as though there is nothing left to hold and support Israel, *kibi-yakhol ein somekh ve-ozer le-Yisrael* — as though there is none supporting or helping Israel — that *Mashiach* will come.

## Computing End Danger

### R. Zeira Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, כִּי הֲוָה מַשְׁכַּח רַבָּנַן דְּמִעַסְּקִי בֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּמָטוּתָא, בָּעֵינָא מִנַּיְיכוּ לָא תְּרַחֲקוּהּ, דִּתְנֵינָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה בָּאִין בְּהֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת, אֵלּוּ הֵן: מָשִׁיחַ, מְצִיאָה, וְעַקְרָב.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as** in **that** practice **of Rabbi Zeira,** who, **when he would find Sages who were engaging in** discussions about the coming of the Messiah, **said to them: Please, I ask of you, do not delay** his coming by calculating the end of days. **As we learn** in a *baraita*: There are **three** matters that **come** only **by means of diversion of attention** from those matters, **and these are they:** The **Messiah, a lost item, and a scorpion.**

Rabbi Zeira's practice was the following: when he found rabbis deeply engaged in trying to compute when *Mashiach* would come, he would say to them — *bi-metuta*, please, *lo terachakuha* — do not push it further away. The very act of preoccupying oneself with calculating the moment of arrival, Rabbi Zeira teaches, delays rather than hastens the coming.

This is not merely the principle of a watched pot never boiling. There is a deeper concern. The tradition of *Mashiach* gives the Jewish people hope when oppressed and in exile; it orients the believer toward an ultimate redemption. But *Torah* and *mitzvot* have always been profoundly this-worldly. Preoccupying oneself with computing a future arrival date distracts from the work that needs to be done in the present — which is, in fact, the very work that would bring *Mashiach* closer.

### 3 Unbidden Things

*De-tanya* — as was taught in a *braita*: three things come *be-hesech ha-da'at* — when one is not paying attention to them. These are *Mashiach*, a found object (*metziah*), and a scorpion (*akrav*). If one goes about deliberately looking for a *metziah*, one will not find it; it is only when going about one's business that one stumbles upon a lost object. Likewise a scorpion strikes when one is not alert. The Maharsha attempts to link all three by connecting them to the question of what the messianic era will look like — whether it will be reward or punishment, a *metziah* or an *akrav*. But the broader point seems to be that such computations are not only futile; they actively distract from the present-world labor that gives the belief in *Mashiach* its meaning.

## Cosmic Timeline

### 6000 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: שֵׁית אַלְפֵי שְׁנֵי הָוֵי עָלְמָא, וְחַד חָרוּב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִשְׂגַּב ה׳ לְבַדּוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: תְּרֵי חָרוּב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְחַיֵּינוּ מִיֹּמָיִם בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי יְקִמֵנוּ וְנִחְיֶה לְפָנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ketina says: Six thousand years is** the duration of **the world,** and **it is in ruins** for **one** thousand years. The duration of the period during which the world is in ruins is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And the Lord alone shall be exalted on that day"** (Isaiah 2:11), and the day of God lasts one thousand years. **Abaye says: It is in ruins** for **two** thousand years, **as it is stated: "After two days He will revive us; in the third day He will revive us, and we shall live in His presence"** (Hosea 6:2).

*Amar Rav Katina*: the world has a lifespan of 6,000 years — *sheis alfei shanei havei alma* — and one thousand years of destruction thereafter, after which a new world will be created. The source is the verse *ve-nisgav Hashem levado ba-yom ha-hu* — God will stand alone on that day — combined with the principle that one day in God's eyes is a thousand years. That millennium will be a period in which God alone stands; all of creation will be destroyed, and a new world will come into being at the end of that thousand years.

Abaye offers a variant: *terei charuv* — two thousand years of destruction — citing the verse *yechayeinu mi-yomayim, ba-yom ha-shlishi yekimenu ve-nichyeh le-fanav* — He will revive us after two days; on the third day He will raise us up and we will live before Him. On this reading, *techiyat ha-meitim* — the resurrection — occurs at the transition from the second to the third thousand-year period, which would coincide with the beginning of the era of *yemot ha-Mashiach*.

### Shmita Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב קַטִּינָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַשְּׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמֶּטֶת שָׁנָה אַחַת לְשֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, כָּךְ הָעוֹלָם מְשַׁמֵּט אֶלֶף שָׁנִים לְשִׁבְעַת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִשְׂגַּב ה׳ לְבַדּוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״מִזְמוֹר שִׁיר לְיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת״ – יוֹם שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ שַׁבָּת. וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי אֶלֶף שָׁנִים בְּעֵינֶיךָ כְּיוֹם אֶתְמוֹל כִּי יַעֲבֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ketina: Just as the Sabbatical** Year **abrogates** debts **once in seven years, so too, the world abrogates** its typical existence for **one thousand years in** every **seven thousand years, as it is stated: "And the Lord alone shall be exalted on that day," and it states: "A psalm, a song for the Shabbat day"** (Psalms 92:1), meaning **a day,** i.e., one thousand years, **that is entirely Shabbat. And it says** in explanation of the equation between one day and one thousand years: **"For a thousand years in Your eyes are but like yesterday when it is past,** and like a watch in the night" (Psalms 90:4).

*Tani kevatei de-Rav Katina* — a *braita* supports Rav Katina's position. Just as the *shmita* year is one year in seven during which the land lies fallow, so too the world will lie fallow for one thousand years out of every seven thousand. The scriptural support comes from the same verse — *ve-nisgav Hashem levado ba-yom ha-hu* — as well as the psalm: *Mizmor Shir le-Yom ha-Shabbat, yom she-kulo Shabbat* — a day of complete rest. The tension here is worth noting: we pray for *Olam ha-Ba* in terms of *Shabbat* as *me'ein Olam ha-Ba*, a foretaste of perfection — yet here the "day of complete rest" sounds far more cataclysmic and destructive than any idyllic, Edenic image. The two conceptions sit in some tension. Finally: *ki elef shanim be-einekha ki-yom etmol ki ya'avor* — a thousand years in Your eyes are like a yesterday that has passed.

### 3 Epochs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ: שֵׁשֶׁת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה הָוֵי עָלְמָא, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים תּוֹהוּ, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים תּוֹרָה, שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Eliyahu taught: Six thousand years is** the duration of **the world. Two thousand** of the six thousand years are characterized by **chaos; two thousand** years are characterized by **Torah,** from the era of the Patriarchs until the end of the mishnaic period; and **two thousand years** are **the period of** the coming of **the Messiah.**

*Tana d'vei Eliyahu* divides the 6,000 years into three epochs of two thousand years each. The first two thousand years are *tohu* — void and emptiness — covering the period from creation until *Avraham Avinu*. By conventional chronology *Avraham* was born in the year 1948 from creation. Rashi connects the beginning of the *Torah* epoch to the moment when *Avraham* and *Sarah* began bringing people under *kanfei ha-Shekhinah* — Rashi dates this to when *Avraham* was 52 years old, corresponding approximately to the year 2000 from creation. Until that moment the world is a desolate world. The *Ramban* on *Bereishit* attempts a parallel structure dividing the world into epochs of one thousand years, each corresponding to a day of creation, but that is a separate framework.

The second two thousand years are the epoch of *Torah* — beginning with *Avraham* and *Sarah* bringing people closer to God and culminating with *Ma'amad Har Sinai* and the full flourishing of *Torah* and *mitzvot* within the Jewish people. Calculating forward, two thousand years from approximately year 2000 brings us to year 4000 from creation — corresponding roughly to the year 250 of the common era, the end of the period of the *Tanna'im* and the beginning of the Talmudic period. This is the epoch of *Torah* in the fullest sense.

The third two thousand years are *yemot ha-Mashiach* — the era in which *Mashiach* can come. This is the period that was, from the Gemara's perspective, already in progress. The Gemara adds: *uvavonoteinu she-rabu, yatze'u mehem mah she-yatze'u* — but due to our great iniquities, years have passed in this final epoch without *Mashiach* arriving. Those are lost opportunities. Yet *Mashiach* can still come at any point within this remaining two-thousand-year window.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲוֹנוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁרַבּוּ, יָצְאוּ מֵהֶם מָה שֶׁיֵּצְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** That is the course that history was to take, **but due to our many sins,** the Messiah did not come after four thousand years passed, and furthermore, the years **that elapsed since** then, which were to have been the messianic era, **have elapsed.**

The Gemara then records that *Eliyahu* told Rav Yehuda the brother of Rav Sila the *chasid*: the world will last no fewer than 85 *yovlot* — jubilee cycles. At 50 years per *yovel*, 80 *yovlot* equals 4,000 years; adding the remaining five brings us to 4,250, meaning *Mashiach* cannot come before approximately the year 500 of the common era. When Rav Yehuda asked whether *Mashiach* would come at the beginning or the end of that final *yovel*, and whether the entire period must be completed first, *Eliyahu* answered each time: *eini yodea* — I do not know. Rav Ashi summarizes: before that point, do not anticipate *Mashiach*; from that point onward, look for him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֵלִיָּהוּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה אֲחוּהּ דְּרַב סַלָּא חֲסִידָא: אֵין הָעוֹלָם פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁמוֹנִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יוֹבְלוֹת, וּבַיּוֹבֵל הָאַחֲרוֹן בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ אוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. כָּלֶה אוֹ אֵינוֹ כָּלֶה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָכִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד הָכָא לָא תִּיסְתַּכֵּי לֵיהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אִיסְתַּכֵּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Elijah** the prophet **said to Rav Yehuda, brother of Rav Sala Ḥasida: The world will** exist **no fewer than eighty-five Jubilee** cycles, or 4,250 years. **And during the final Jubilee, the son of David** will **come.** Rav Yehuda **said to** Elijah: Will the Messiah come **during the beginning of** the Jubilee **or during its end?** Elijah **said to** Rav Yehuda: **I do not know.** Rav Yehuda asked: Will this last Jubilee cycle **end** before the Messiah comes **or** will it **not** yet **end** before his coming? Elijah **said to him: I do not know. Rav Ashi says: This** is what **Elijah said to him: Until that time do not anticipate his** coming; **from this** point **forward anticipate his** coming.

## Specific Computations

### Roman Scroll

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מָצָאתִי אָדָם אֶחָד וּבְיָדוֹ מְגִילָּה אַחַת כְּתוּבָה אַשּׁוּרִית וּלְשׁוֹן קֹדֶשׁ. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: זוֹ מִנַּיִין לָךְ? אָמַר לִי: לַחֲיָילוֹת שֶׁל רוֹמִי נִשְׂכַּרְתִּי וּבֵין גִּינְזֵי רוֹמִי מְצָאתִיהָ. וְכָתוּב בָּהּ: לְאַחַר אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים וּמָאתַיִם וְתִשְׁעִים וְאֶחָד שָׁנָה לִבְרִיאָתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הָעוֹלָם יִתּוֹם. מֵהֶן מִלְחֲמוֹת תַּנִּינִים, מֵהֶן מִלְחָמוֹת גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג, וּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ. וְאֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַדֵּשׁ אֶת עוֹלָמוֹ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שִׁבְעַת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: לְאַחַר חֲמֵשֶׁת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Taḥlifa sent** a message **to Rav Yosef: I found one man, and in his hand** there was **one scroll written** in ***Ashurit*** script **and** in **the sacred tongue,** Hebrew. **I said to him: From where** did **this** scroll come **into your** possession? **He said to me: I was hired to** serve in **the Roman army and I found** the scroll **among the Roman archives.** It was clear that the scroll was written by Jews, not Romans. **And it is written in** the scroll: **After 4,291 years** have elapsed **from the creation of the world, the world will end; during** those years there will be **the wars of the sea monsters** between the leviathan and the animals, and **among** those years there will be **the wars of Gog and Magog and the remaining** years of the **messianic period.** Then the world will be destroyed. **And the Holy One, Blessed be He, will renew His world only after** the passage of **seven thousand years. Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, says** that **it was stated: After** the passage of **five thousand years.**

Rav Chana bar Tachlifa sent the following to Rav Yosef: I found a certain man — possibly a non-Jew — carrying a scroll written in *Ashuris*, the square Hebrew script, and in the Hebrew language. He told me he had hired himself out as a mercenary to the Roman army and found this scroll in the storehouses of Rome — evidently among items plundered when Rome conquered Israel. Written in it was: *le-achar arba'at alafim u-masayim ve-tish'im ve-achat shana le-olam* — after 4,291 years from creation, the world will come to its end. The *hagahah* records a variant reading but the figure is essentially consistent with the 4,250 computed earlier.

### End Wars

The scroll continues: some of that ending will come through *milchamos taninim* — wars of sea creatures, possibly the *Livyasan*, which some read as a metaphor for wars between great national superpowers. Some will come through *milchamos Gog u-Magog*. And *she'ar yemot ha-Mashiach* — the remainder of the time — will be the period of *Mashiach*.

### 7000 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַדֵּשׁ אֶת עוֹלָמוֹ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שִׁבְעַת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Holy One, Blessed be He, will renew His world only after** the passage of **seven thousand years.**

*Ve-ein ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu mechadesh et olamo ela le-achar shivat alafim shana* — God will not renew His world until after seven thousand years. This clarifies the sequence: *Mashiach* comes sometime after the 4,290-year mark, the wars of *Gog u-Magog* follow, but the full renewal and re-creation of the world does not occur until the end of the seventh millennium.

### R. Acha Variant

Rav Acha the son of Rava offers a variant: *le-achar chameishot alafim shana* — after five thousand years. This makes the sequence slightly more coherent: *Mashiach* and the wars of *Gog u-Magog* arrive sometime in the fifth millennium, and the world renews at the end of that millennium, all within the seven-thousand-year framework.

## Computation Impossible

### R. Nasan Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר, מִקְרָא זֶה נוֹקֵב וְיוֹרֵד עַד תְּהוֹם: ״כִּי עוֹד חֲזוֹן לַמּוֹעֵד וְיָפֵחַ לַקֵּץ וְלֹא יְכַזֵּב אִם יִתְמַהְמָהּ חַכֵּה לוֹ כִּי בֹא יָבֹא לֹא יְאַחֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: This verse penetrates and descends until the depths;** just as the depths are unfathomable, so too, the period depicted in the following verse is unquantifiable. **"For the vision is yet for the appointed time; and it declares of the end, and does not lie; though it tarry, wait for it, because it will surely come; it will not delay"** (Habakkuk 2:3).

*Tanya* — Rabbi Nasan says: *mikra zeh nokeiv ve-yored ad ha-tehom* — this verse pierces and descends to the very depths. The verse in question is from *Chavakuk*: *ki od chazon la-mo'ed ve-yafei'ach la-ketz ve-lo yekhazeiv; im yitmahmeah chakkeh lo, ki vo yavo lo ye'acher* — for the vision is yet for its appointed time, it declares toward the end and will not lie; if it tarries, wait for it, for it will surely come and will not delay. The plain sense of *ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* is that it declares the end; but as we will see, the Gemara also reads *yafei'ach* as "blow away" — meaning blow away and destroy all the calculations that have been made. Rabbi Nasan's point is that this verse is fathomless — you cannot reach the bottom of it, and no computation will succeed.

### Three Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא כְּרַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁהָיוּ דּוֹרְשִׁין: ״עַד עִדָּן עִדָּנִין וּפְלַג עִדָּן״. וְלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי, שֶׁהָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ: ״הֶאֱכַלְתָּם לֶחֶם דִּמְעָה וַתַּשְׁקֵמוֹ בִּדְמָעוֹת שְׁלִישׁ״. וְלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, שֶׁהָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ: ״עוֹד אַחַת מְעַט הִיא וַאֲנִי מַרְעִישׁ אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאֶת הָאָרֶץ״. אֶלָּא מַלְכוּת רִאשׁוֹנָה – שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, מַלְכוּת שְׁנִיָּה – חֲמִשִּׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם, וּמַלְכוּת בֶּן כּוֹזֵיבָא – שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Messiah will come **not in accordance with** the opinion of **our Rabbis, who would interpret** the verse: **"For a period and periods and a half period"** (Daniel 7:25), to mean that the duration of the ultimate exile will be three and a half times the duration of the period of the exile in Egypt. **And** the Messiah will come **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Simlai, who would interpret** the verse: **"You have fed them with the bread of tears and have given them tears to drink in great measure [*shalish*]"** (Psalms 80:6), to mean that the duration of the ultimate exile will be three times the duration of the period of the exile in Egypt. **And** the Messiah will come **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who would interpret** the verse: **"Yet once, it is a little while, and I will shake the heavens and the earth"** (Haggai 2:6), to mean that the redemption would transpire soon after the destruction of the Temple. **Rather, the first,** great, Hasmonean **monarchy** ruled **seventy years. The second kingdom,** of Herod and his descendants, ruled **fifty-two** years, **and** the duration of **the monarchy of bar Koziva,** or bar Kokheva, was **two and a half years.**

The Gemara proceeds to dismiss three specific rabbinic computations. The first is the calculation based on the verse in Daniel: *ad idan ve-idanin u-felag idan* — until a time, times, and half a time. Rashi explains that *idan* means 400 years, the duration of the Egyptian exile; two *idanin* plus a half gives 1,400 years. Counting 1,400 years from the time of Daniel — just before the *Beit ha-Mikdash* of 420 years — brings one to approximately the year 1000 of the common era. That did not come to pass.

The second computation belongs to Rabbi Simlai, who expounded: *he'ekhaltem lechem dim'ah va-tashkemo bi-dema'ot shalish* — I fed them bread of tears and gave them tears to drink in full measure. Rashi reads *shalish* as "a third," understanding the Egyptian and Babylonian exiles — 400 and 70 years respectively, totaling 470 — as one third of the ultimate exile. Multiplying by three yields 1,410 years, essentially identical to the first calculation. Counting 1,410 years of exile from the *Churban ha-Bayit* brings one to approximately the year 1500. That too did not come to pass.

The third is Rabbi Akiva's, who expounded: *od achat me'at va-ani mar'ish et ha-shamayim ve-et ha-aretz* — just a little more and I will cause heaven and earth to tremble. The Gemara understands that Rabbi Akiva read this as indicating *Mashiach* would come very soon after the *Churban* — which is why he believed Bar Kokhba was *Mashiach*.

The Maharsha reads the verse from Chavakuk as elegantly dismissing all three. *Ki od chazon la-mo'ed* — there is yet another vision of what *mo'ed* means (addressing the *idan ve-idanin* computation). *Ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* — and blow away the interpretation of the third (Rabbi Simlai's). *Ve-lo yekhazeiv* — and it is not Bar Koziva — for Barkochba was referred to by the sages as *ben Koziva*, meaning the liar. Thus the verse simultaneously undermines all three attempted computations.

### 3 Kingdoms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַלְכוּת רִאשׁוֹנָה – שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, מַלְכוּת שְׁנִיָּה – חֲמִשִּׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם, וּמַלְכוּת בֶּן כּוֹזֵיבָא – שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the first,** great, Hasmonean **monarchy** ruled **seventy years. The second kingdom,** of Herod and his descendants, ruled **fifty-two** years, **and** the duration of **the monarchy of bar Koziva,** or bar Kokheva, was **two and a half years.**

The Gemara adds: *malkhut rishon shivim shanah, malkhut shniya chamishim u-shtayim, u-malkhut Ben Koziva shtei shanim u-mechtza* — there was a kingship of 70 years (the successful phase of the *Beit Chashmonai*), a kingship of 52 years (Herod), and the rule of Bar Kokhba for two and a half years. The precise connection to what immediately precedes is not fully spelled out, but the Maharsha reads it as a response to Rabbi Akiva's expectation: Rabbi Akiva thought that "just a little more" meant *Mashiach* was imminent in his day and that Barkochba was the *Mashiach*. But the Gemara is pointing out that even after all of those brief sparks of Jewish self-sovereignty passed — Hasmonean, Herodian, and Bar Kokhba — *Mashiach* still did not come. All of those were not the true *Mashiach*; they passed, and *Mashiach* will come only afterward.

## Curse Computers

### Tiphach Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְיָפֵחַ לַקֵּץ וְלֹא יְכַזֵּב״? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: תִּיפַּח עַצְמָן שֶׁל מְחַשְּׁבֵי קִיצִּין, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ (אֶת) הַקֵּץ וְלֹא בָּא – שׁוּב אֵינוֹ בָּא. אֶלָּא חַכֵּה לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם יִתְמַהְמָהּ חַכֵּה לוֹ״. שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר: אָנוּ מְחַכִּין וְהוּא אֵינוֹ מְחַכֶּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלָכֵן יְחַכֶּה ה׳ לַחֲנַנְכֶם וְלָכֵן יָרוּם לְרַחֶמְכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"And it declares [*veyafe'aḥ*] of the end, and does not lie"? Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: May those who calculate the end of days be cursed [*tippaḥ*], as they would say once the end** of days that they calculated **arrived and** the Messiah **did not come,** that **he will no longer come** at all. **Rather,** the proper behavior is to continue to **wait for his** coming, **as it is stated: "Though it tarry, wait for it." Lest you say we are** expectantly **awaiting** the end of days **and** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **is not awaiting** the end of days and does not want to redeem His people, **the verse states: "And therefore will the Lord wait, to be gracious to you; and therefore will He be exalted, to have mercy upon you;** for the Lord is a God of judgment; happy are all they who wait for Him" (Isaiah 30:18).

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: *tipach atzmotam shel mechashvei kitzin* — may the bones of those who compute the ends of days be blasted. The word *ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* in the verse is now read as an imprecation: blow away, destroy, those who blow toward the end — those who try to calculate when it will arrive.

### Computation Failure

The reason for this severity is explained: those who compute set a date, *Mashiach* does not come by that date, and then they say *Mashiach* will never come — *shuv eino ba*. The false expectation generated by calculation leads directly to despair of redemption altogether, which is far worse than never having made the calculation.

### Just Wait

The alternative, drawn from the rest of the verse, is: *chakkeh lo* — simply wait for him. *Im yitmahmeah, chakkeh lo* — if he tarries, wait for him. And lest one wonder whether God Himself is waiting and hoping for this time — *lachen yechakveh Hashem la-chananchem ve-lachen yarum le-rachamchem* — therefore God waits to show you grace; therefore He rises to show you compassion. God too is, so to speak, waiting and longing for the redemption.

### Justice Prevents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁאָנוּ מְחַכִּים, וְהוּא מְחַכֶּה, מִי מְעַכֵּב? מִדַּת הַדִּין מְעַכֶּבֶת. וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁמִּדַּת הַדִּין מְעַכֶּבֶת, אָנוּ לָמָּה מְחַכִּים? לְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵי כׇּל חוֹכֵי לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** And seemingly, **since we are awaiting** the end of days **and** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **is** also **awaiting** the end of days, **who is preventing** the coming of the Messiah? It is **the** divine **attribute of judgment** that **prevents** his coming, as it is written: "For the Lord is a God of judgment," and we are not worthy. **And since the attribute of judgment prevents** the coming of the Messiah and we are not worthy of redemption, **why do we await** his coming daily? We do so in order **to receive a reward** for awaiting his coming, **as it is stated: "Happy are all they who wait for Him."**

This raises a penetrating question: if we are waiting and God is waiting, *mi me'akev* — who is preventing *Mashiach* from coming? *Midat ha-din me-akevet* — the attribute of justice is what delays it. Everyone wants the redemption; but we must earn it, and the divine attribute of justice stands in the way until we do.

This leads naturally to a further question: if *midat ha-din* is what prevents it, and waiting for *Mashiach* does not overcome *midat ha-din*, then why is it important to wait for him at all? Why should we cultivate a sense of longing for something that will not arrive merely because we long for it? The Gemara implicitly asks whether such waiting distracts from the work — the *teshuvah* and *ma'asim tovim* — that would actually be effective.

### Reward Waiting

*Lekabel sakhar* — to receive reward, *shene'emar ashrei kol chokhei lo* — as it says, praiseworthy are all who wait for Him. The act of waiting itself, expressing a genuine belief in ultimate redemption, carries its own value and its own reward. The proper balance is clear: have faith, believe in redemption, work toward a better world because one believes it is achievable — but do not allow the anticipation of that future moment to consume the work that needs to be done right now. The two must be held in tension.

## 36 Righteous

### Abaye 36

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא פָּחֵית עָלְמָא מִתְּלָתִין וְשִׁיתָּא צַדִּיקֵי דִּמְקַבְּלִי אַפֵּי שְׁכִינָה בְּכֹל דָּרָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״אַשְׁרֵי כׇּל חוֹכֵי לוֹ״ – ״לוֹ״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא תְּלָתִין וְשִׁיתָּא הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos that verse, **Abaye said: The world** has **no fewer than thirty-six righteous** people **in each generation who greet the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "Happy are all they who wait for Him [*lo*]"** (Isaiah 30:18). **The numerical value of *lo*,** spelled *lamed vav*, **is thirty-six.**

*Amar Abaye*: the famous statement — *lo pachot almaya mi-tlasin ve-shita tzadikim*, the world has no fewer than thirty-six *tzadikim* — *d'mekablei appei Shekhinah be-chol dara* — who receive the countenance of the Divine Presence in every generation.

### Gematria 36

The proof: *ashroi kol chokhei lo* — praiseworthy are all who wait for *him*. The word *lo* — for him — has the numerical value of 36: *lamed* equals 30, *vav* equals 6.

### Rava Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: דָּרָא דְּקַמֵּי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי אַלְפֵי פַּרְסָה הָוֵאי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״סָבִיב שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר אָלֶף״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּמִסְתַּכְּלִי בְּאִיסְפַּקְלַרְיָא הַמְּאִירָה, הָא דְּמִסְתַּכְּלִי בְּאִיסְפַּקְלַרְיָא שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְאִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rava say: The row** of the righteous **before the Holy One, Blessed be He,** extends **eighteen thousand parasangs, as it is stated** with regard to the city of God at the end of days: **"It shall be eighteen thousand reeds round about,** and the name of the city from that day shall be: The Lord is there" (Ezekiel 48:35)? The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult; this** statement of Abaye refers to the thirty-six righteous people **who view** the Divine Presence **through a luminous crystal [*be'ispaklarya*],** and **that** statement of Rava refers to the multitudes **who view** the Divine Presence **through a crystal that is not luminous.**

But does this not contradict Rava's statement? Rava taught that the row of people standing before the *Kadosh Barukh Hu* stretches *tamnei srei alfei parsa* — eighteen thousand *parasangs*, citing the verse *saviv shemonah asar alef* — around him eighteen thousand. How can only thirty-six receive the Divine Presence if eighteen thousand stand before God?

### Clear/Cloudy Lens

*Lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The thirty-six are those who *mistaklei be-ispaklaria ha-meirah* — who see God through a clear, luminous lens. The eighteen thousand are those who see through a clouded lens — they are present before God, but their perception is indirect and dimmed. The thirty-six occupy the innermost circle, the most direct apprehension of the Divine.

One textual nuance: Abaye uses the word *dara*, which in this context can mean either "in every generation" or "row" (as Rava uses it in his statement). Reading Abaye as meaning "in every generation" fits better with the broader context of the *lamed vav tzadikim* as a phenomenon of every era.

### S.b.Y. Few

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי נְפִישִׁי כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וְהָאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: רָאִיתִי בְּנֵי עֲלִיָּיה וְהֵן מוּעָטִין. אִם אֶלֶף הֵם – אֲנִי וּבְנִי מֵהֶם, אִם מֵאָה הֵם – אֲנִי וּבְנִי מֵהֶם, אִם שְׁנַיִם הֵם – אֲנִי וּבְנִי הֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are** those who view the Divine Presence through a crystal that is luminous **so numerous? But doesn't Ḥizkiyya say** that **Rabbi Yirmeya says in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai: I have seen members** of the group **of** the spiritually **prominent,** who are truly righteous, **and they are few. If they** number **one thousand, I and my son are among them. If they** number **one hundred, I and my son are among them;** and **if they** number **two, I and my son are they.** Apparently, it is conceivable that there are no more than two who view the Divine Presence through a luminous crystal.

But is even thirty-six too large a number? *Ve-ha'amar Chizkiyah amar Rabbi Yirmeyah mishum Shimon bar Yochai*: Chizkiyah said in the name of Rabbi Yirmeyah in the name of Shimon bar Yochai — *ra'iti bnei aliyah ve-hen mu'atin* — I have seen those of true spiritual stature, and they are very few. Presumably this is from his period of seclusion in the cave. If there are a thousand, he said, I and my son are among them. If there are a hundred, I and my son are among them. If there are two — it is me and my son. How could there be thirty-six who receive the Divine Presence at the highest level?

### Clear/Cloudy Lens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּעָיְילִי בְּבַר, הָא דְּעָיְילִי בְּלָא בַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** statement of Abaye is referring to those **who** may **enter** to view the Divine Presence only **by** requesting and being granted **permission [*bar*]** from the angels. **That** statement of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai is referring to the select few **who** may **enter** to view the Divine Presence even **without** requesting **permission,** for whom the gates of Heaven are open at all times.

*Lo kashya* — not difficult. *Ha d'ayli b'var, ha d'ayli be-lo var*: there are those who enter the divine presence only with permission — these are the thirty-six — and there are those so elevated that they enter even without permission. It is the latter category, those who need no permission, that may be as few as two. Whatever precisely this means, the distinction is between levels of intimacy with the divine: the thirty-six enjoy the highest access; the even smaller number — perhaps only two — enjoy an intimacy that transcends even the framework of permission.

## Repentance Debate

### R. Eliezer: If

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: כָּלוּ כׇּל הַקִּיצִּין, וְאֵין הַדָּבָר תָּלוּי אֶלָּא בִּתְשׁוּבָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: דַּיּוֹ לָאָבֵל שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד בְּאֶבְלוֹ. כְּתַנָּאֵי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין תְּשׁוּבָה – נִגְאָלִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין נִגְאָלִין. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אִם אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּשׁוּבָה אֵין נִגְאָלִין? אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַעֲמִיד לָהֶן מֶלֶךְ שֶׁגְּזֵרוֹתָיו קָשׁוֹת כְּהָמָן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין תְּשׁוּבָה וּמַחְזִירָן לְמוּטָב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: All the ends** of days that were calculated **passed, and the matter depends only upon repentance and good deeds.** When the Jewish people repent, they will be redeemed. **And Shmuel says: It is sufficient for the mourner to endure in his mourning** to bring about the coming of the Messiah. Even without repentance, they will be worthy of redemption due to the suffering they endured during the exile. The Gemara notes: This dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*: Rabbi Eliezer says: If the Jewish people repent they are redeemed, and if not they are not redeemed. Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: If they do not repent, will they not be redeemed** at all? **Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will establish a king for them whose decrees are as harsh as** those issued by **Haman, and the Jewish people** will have no choice but to **repent, and** this will **restore them to** the **right** path.

*Amar Rav*: *kalu kol ha-kitzin* — all of the computed ends of days have expired and run out. The matter now depends entirely on *teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim* — on our repentance and good deeds. *Ve-Shmuel amar*: Shmuel says, *daiyo la-avel she-ya'amod be-evlo* — it is enough for the mourner to stand in his mourning. We have suffered long enough; God will bring *Mashiach* regardless. This appears to be the position that *Mashiach* will come even without *teshuvah*, and it leads directly into the debate of the *Tanna'im*.

*K'tana'ei* — this dispute echoes a debate among *Tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer says: *im Yisrael osim teshuvah nigalin ve-im lav ein nigalin* — if Israel does *teshuvah* they will be redeemed; if not, they will not be redeemed. The position is clear and absolute.

### R. Yehoshua: Evil King

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: you mean to say that if Israel does not do *teshuvah* they will never be redeemed — that there will be no *Mashiach*? *Ela* — rather, *Ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu ma'amid lahem melekh she-gezeirotav kashot ke-Haman* — God will raise up against them a king whose decrees are as harsh as Haman's. *Ve-Yisrael osim teshuvah u-machzirim le-mutav* — and as a result, the external pressure will cause Israel to repent and improve their ways. Rabbi Yehoshua does not say *Mashiach* will come without *teshuvah*; the point is that God is committed to bringing the redemption regardless, and if Israel will not generate *teshuvah* from within, God will compel it from without.

### Scripture Quotes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין תְּשׁוּבָה – נִגְאָלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוּבוּ בָּנִים שׁוֹבָבִים אֶרְפָּא מְשׁוּבֹתֵיכֶם״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״חִנָּם נִמְכַּרְתֶּם וְלֹא בְכֶסֶף תִּגָּאֵלוּ״? ״חִנָּם נִמְכַּרְתֶּם״ – בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, ״וְלֹא בְּכֶסֶף תִּגָּאֵלוּ״ – לֹא בִּתְשׁוּבָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: If the Jewish people repent they are redeemed, as it is stated: "Return, wayward children, I will heal your iniquities"** (Jeremiah 3:22). **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: But isn't it already stated:** "So says the Lord: **You were sold for naught, and without money you shall be redeemed"** (Isaiah 52:3)? Rabbi Yehoshua explains: **"You were sold for naught"** means you were sold **for idol worship,** which is a sin with no basis. **"And without money you shall be redeemed"** means you will be redeemed **not through repentance and good deeds,** but through the will of God.

*Tanya idach* — a second *braita* records the debate. Rabbi Eliezer cites: *shuvu banim shovavim erpa meshuvoteikhem* — return, wayward children, I will heal your waywardness — proving that *teshuvah* is the prerequisite. Rabbi Yehoshua counters: *chinam nimkartem ve-lo be-khesef tiga'elu* — you were sold for nothing and you will not be redeemed with money. *Chinam nimkartem* — you gave yourselves over to *avodah zarah* for nothing. *Ve-lo be-khesef tiga'elu* — and your redemption will not come through *teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim*. On this reading Rabbi Yehoshua appears to be saying that the redemption will not be contingent on our *teshuvah* and good deeds at all.

To harmonize this with his earlier position, the best reading is that Rabbi Yehoshua means: the redemption need not come through self-initiated *teshuvah*; God will compel Israel's return if necessary, and it is that divinely-induced *teshuvah* — not Israel's own *teshuvah* — that brings the redemption.

### Ownership Forced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״שׁוּבוּ אֵלַי וְאָשׁוּבָה אֲלֵיכֶם״? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״כִּי אָנֹכִי בָּעַלְתִּי בָכֶם וְלָקַחְתִּי אֶתְכֶם אֶחָד מֵעִיר וּשְׁנַיִם מִמִּשְׁפָּחָה וְהֵבֵאתִי אֶתְכֶם צִיּוֹן״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said to Rabbi Yehoshua: But isn't it already stated: "Return to Me and I will return to you"** (Malachi 3:7)? **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: But isn't it already stated: "For I have taken you to Myself; and I will take you one from a city and two from a family, and I will bring you to Zion"** (Jeremiah 3:14), unconditionally?

Rabbi Eliezer then cites: *shuvu elai ve-ashuva aleikhem* — return to Me and I will return to you — which requires Israel to move first. Rabbi Yehoshua responds with: *ki anoki ba'alti vakhem ve-lakachti etkhem echad me-ir u-shnayim mi-mishpachah va-heveti etkhem Tzion* — because I have taken ownership over you, and I will take you, one from a city and two from a family, and bring you to Zion. The image is of God seizing Israel and forcing the ingathering even against their will — an act of divine compulsion, not of Israel's own initiative.

### Despised Redeemed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״בְּשׁוּבָה וָנַחַת תִּוָּשֵׁעוּן״? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ גֹּאֵל יִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשׁוֹ לִבְזֹה נֶפֶשׁ לִמְתָעֵב גּוֹי לְעֶבֶד מֹשְׁלִים
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: But isn't it already stated: "In ease [*beshuva*] and rest shall you be saved"** (Isaiah 30:15), indicating that redemption is dependent upon repentance [*teshuva*]? **Rabbi Yehoshua said to Rabbi Eliezer: But isn't it already stated: "So says the Lord, Redeemer of Israel, his Holy One, to him who is despised of man, to him who is abhorred of the nation, to a servant of rulers:**

Rabbi Eliezer then cites: *be-shuvah va-nachat tivvashe'un* — through returning and rest you will be saved — reading *shuvah* as *teshuvah*. Rabbi Yehoshua counters with: *ko amar Hashem go'el Yisrael kedosho, li-vzoh nefesh le-mita'ev goy le-eved moshlim, melakhim yir'u ve-kamu sarim ve-yishtachavu* — so says God the Redeemer of Israel, to the one despised, abhorred by nations, a slave to rulers, kings will rise and officers will prostrate themselves. The message: even in a state of utter degradation and unworthiness, God will redeem Israel.

Rabbi Eliezer's position is consistent throughout: *teshuvah* is the necessary and indispensable prerequisite for redemption. This also aligns with Rav's ruling. Shmuel's position — and Rabbi Yehoshua's — is somewhat less clear in its full extent: does it mean God will force *teshuvah* upon Israel and then redeem them, or that redemption will ultimately come irrespective of the moral condition of Israel because God's ownership and commitment to His people is unconditional? The *sugya* will continue to develop this question.
