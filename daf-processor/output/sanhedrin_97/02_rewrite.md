# Sanhedrin 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashiach Introduction

### Opening Context

The *daf* opens two lines from the bottom of 96b, picking up from the preceding discussion of *Sancheriv* and *Nevuchadnezzar* and the fall of those empires. The connection to what follows is not entirely clear on the surface. One possible reading is that the prior sugya focused on those who came to war against Israel and inaugurated the *galus*, and the Gemara now naturally raises the next question: when will *Mashiach* come? This transition carries the discussion into a sustained and remarkable section of Sanhedrin 97 dedicated to the nature of redemption, the signs of *Mashiach*'s coming, and the dangers of trying to calculate the appointed time.

### Bar Nafli

*Amar leh Rav Nachman le-Rav Yitzchak*: Rav Nachman said to Rav Yitzchak, *mi shemi'a lakh eimat ati Bar Nafli* — have you heard when the son of Nafli will come? Rav Yitzchak responded: who is this Bar Nafli? Rav Nachman replied: *Mashiach*. Rav Yitzchak pressed further: you are calling *Mashiach* "Bar Nafli" — the son of those who have fallen? Rav Nachman confirmed this, citing the verse *ba-yom ha-hu akim et sukkat David ha-nofelet* — on that day I will raise up the fallen booth, the fallen dominion, of King David.

What is striking here is that the emphasis falls not on the glory and triumph that *Mashiach* will bring, but on the suffering and collapse from which he will emerge. The very name given to *Mashiach* — Bar Nafli, son of the fallen — frames the redemption as something that rises out of hardship and tragedy.

### R. Yochanan: Generation…

*Hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan*: Rabbi Yochanan taught that in the generation when the son of David comes, *talmidei chakhamim* will be diminished — their numbers will shrink. The rest of the people will have their eyes dimmed with anguish and mourning. Tremendous afflictions and harsh decrees will come in rapid succession — before the effects of the first tragedy have passed, the next is already on its way. This portrait of the generation preceding *Mashiach* is entirely consistent with the epithet Bar Nafli: it will be a time of great difficulty and suffering, with the redemption emerging from the depths of the fall.

## Shmita Cycle

### 7-Year Progression

*Tanu rabbanan*: our rabbis taught regarding the *shavua* — the seven-year *shmita* cycle — in which *Mashiach* will come. The progression unfolds year by year. In the first year, rain will fall on one city but not another, producing sporadic famine alongside relative plenty. In the second year, pangs of hunger will spread to everyone. By the third year, a great famine will set in — men, women, and children will die, along with the righteous and people of good deeds; the *Torah* will be forgotten by those who had learned it, overwhelmed by hunger. In the fourth year, *sova ve-eino sova* — there will be satiation but not full satiation, the pendulum beginning to swing upward. By the fifth year, great satiation returns: people will eat, drink, and rejoice, and the *Torah* will return to those who had learned it. This is the shape of a U-curve. In the sixth year, *kolot* — sounds — will come; Rashi understands these as sounds heralding the arrival of *Mashiach*. In the seventh year, great wars will take place, presumably including the war of *Gog u-Magog*. And finally, *bemotzei shvi'is*, at the conclusion of the seventh year, *ben David ba* — *Mashiach* will come.

The Gemara's description is striking enough that one who is so inclined might read it as a lens through which to view more recent history — the devastation of the third year echoing the Holocaust, in which men, women, and children perished and entire *Torah* communities were destroyed, followed by an upward arc of rebuilding. That reading remains, of course, speculative, but the structure of the passage invites it.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef raised an objection: *ha kama shevi'isa dahava ken velo asa* — we have had several *shmita* cycles that seemed to follow this pattern, and yet *Mashiach* did not come.

### Abaye Response

Abaye responded by sharpening the challenge: *bishishis kolos bishvi'is milchamos mi hava* — did we actually have the sixth year with those sounds heralding *Mashiach*, and the seventh year with those specific wars? That did not happen. And moreover, *kesidran mi hava* — nor did the events occur in the precise sequence described. Some years of famine and some years of plenty, yes, but not in the exact U-shaped order laid out in the *braita*. Furthermore, even granting that such events occurred, it does not follow that when this pattern appears *Mashiach* will necessarily come — only that these are the conditions preceding his arrival.

## Generation Collapse

### Social Breakdown

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Yehuda says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *batei va'ad* — houses of assembly, places where sages would gather, even the *beis midrash* — will become houses of fornication. The Galilee will be destroyed. The *Gavlan* — understood by some as the Golan or the northern region near *Har Chermon* — will be laid desolate. The people of the border cities will wander from city to city and will not be given rest or welcome, *velo yechanenu*. For those who would read this in a contemporary lens, the image of border communities evacuated and wandering from city to city, unsettled, could hardly be more vivid.

### Wisdom Spoiled

*Ve-chochmat ha-sofrim tisrach* — the wisdom of the sages will be spoiled. *Ve-yirei cheit yim'asu* — those who fear sin will be despised. Values will be turned upside down. *U-fnei ha-dor kipnei kelev* — the face of the generation will be like the face of a dog. The Maharsha explains that dogs appear friendly and loyal while concealing betrayal beneath the surface. *Ve-ha-emet ne'ederet* — and truth will be absent and lost.

### Truth Lost

The Gemara asks: what does *vatehi ha-emet ne'ederet* — truth will be lost — actually mean? *Amri d'vei Rav*: this teaches that truth will gather itself into flocks and wander away. The Maharsha explains that with respect to *Torah* and *talmidei chakhamim*, this does not mean truth will vanish utterly, but rather that those who carry truth will have to abandon the corrupt centers of civilization and seek out places of their own in which to dwell.

The verse continues: *ve-sar me-ra mishtolel* — one who turns from evil will be mocked. *Amri d'vei Rabbi Shila*: anyone who turns away from evil will be seen as a fool in the eyes of the general population. Good will be viewed as stupidity.

*Amar Rava*: from the outset, Rava says, *me-reish hava amina leit kushta b'alma* — I used to say there is no real truth in this world. This world is one of *sheker*; no one can stand fully by the principle of truth. Then one of the rabbinic students — *Rav Tavut* by name, some say *Rav Tavyomi* — told Rava the following. This student was renowned for his absolute commitment to truth: even if given all the wealth in the world, *lo hava meshanei be-dibburei* — he would not misrepresent himself or say an untruth. And he told Rava that he had once come to a certain place called *Kushta* — Truth — where no one ever fudged anything, and no one died before his ripe old age. He settled there, married a woman from the city, and had two sons.

### Rava's Story

One day his wife was washing her hair when a neighbor knocked on the door. He thought: *lav orach ar'a* — it is not appropriate to say my wife is in the bath. So he told the neighbor: she is not here. His two sons then died. The people of the city came to him and asked what had happened. He explained that he had slightly misrepresented the situation to protect his wife's privacy. They said to him: please, *puk me-asra* — leave our city, lest you incite death among our people.

This is a genuinely arresting story, and it leaves one somewhat unsettled. On the one hand, it illustrates in dramatic terms the absolute standard of truth maintained in that city, and the lethal consequence of even the smallest departure from it. On the other hand, the Gemara elsewhere permits fudging in certain contexts — such as *kevod ha-beriyot*, human dignity and privacy — which is precisely what he was trying to protect. One is left wondering what the ultimate message is: is this a story demanding the harshest possible commitment to literal truth even at the cost of another's privacy? The Gemara does not resolve the tension explicitly.

## Alt. Accounts

### R. Nehorai

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Nehorai says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *nearim yalbinu pnei zekenim* — youth will cause the faces of elders to go white with embarrassment. The elders will be expected to stand before the young rather than the reverse. A daughter will rise against her mother, a daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law. The face of the generation will be like the face of a dog. And a son will not be ashamed before his father. The passage distributes the specific failures interestingly: sons toward fathers, daughters toward mothers, daughters-in-law toward mothers-in-law.

### R. Nechemiah

*Tanya* — it was taught. Rabbi Nechemiah says: in the generation when *Mashiach* comes, *ha-azut tirbeh* — brazenness will increase. *Ve-ha-yoker ya'avot* — that which deserves honor will be perverted. Rashi reads this as the proper showing of honor being turned on its head. The vine will give its fruit and there will be plentiful wine — that is the one seemingly positive note — yet *ve-ha-yayin be-yoker*, the wine will be very expensive. Rashi understands this to mean that demand will be so high, driven by widespread drunkenness, that price will be extreme despite abundance.

*Ve-nehepcha kol ha-malkhut le-minus* — and the entire kingdom will turn to *minut*, heresy. Since the Romans of Rabbi Nechemiah's time — the second century — practiced *avodah zarah*, and *minut* in the Gemara often refers to a form of Jewish heresy, specifically to what would become Christianity, this is most naturally read as a prediction that the Roman Empire would convert to Christianity. Constantine's conversion of Rome to Christianity occurred at the beginning of the fourth century, roughly two centuries after Rabbi Nechemiah. He may have sensed the direction in which things were moving. And though that conversion has long since occurred, *Mashiach* has not yet come, suggesting this is one necessary sign among many, not a sufficient condition alone.

### R. Yitzchak Sign

*Mesayei lei le-Rabbi Yitzchak* — Rabbi Nechemiah's teaching supports Rabbi Yitzchak's position, for Rabbi Yitzchak said: *ein Ben David ba ad she-tit'hapekh kol ha-malkhut le-minut* — *Mashiach* will not come until the entire kingdom turns to heresy. For Rabbi Yitzchak this is the defining prerequisite. That turning occurred approximately 1,700 years ago, yet we still await *Mashiach*.

*Amar Rava mai kra* — Rava asks, what is the scriptural basis? *Kulo hafakh lavan tahor hu* — if the entire diseased area turns white, it is pure. The image of *tzara'at* spreading until it covers everything completely, which counterintuitively signals purity, is invoked as a parallel: when the darkness spreads completely, that becomes the sign of the turn toward redemption.

## Arrival Conditions

### Divine Judgment

*Tanu rabbanan* — our rabbis taught. The verse reads: *ki yadin Hashem amo* — God will judge His people — *ki yireh ki azlat yad ve-efes atzur ve-azuv* — when He sees that their power is gone and there is none guarded or supported. From this verse the Gemara derives multiple conditions for *Mashiach*'s arrival.

### Hand Extended

Four distinct readings are offered. First: *ein Ben David ba ad she-yirbu ha-mesorot* — *Mashiach* will not come until informers against Jews to the government greatly multiply, reading *azlat yad* as a hand extended against them. Second: *ad she-yim'atu ha-talmidim* — until the *talmidei chakhamim* are diminished, for as Rashi notes, *Torah* is what would strengthen Israel and its absence leaves them unsupported. Third: *ad she-tichleh perutah min ha-kis* — until no money remains in the pocket, reading *azlat yad* as the hand reaching into one's pocket and finding nothing. Fourth — and perhaps closest to the plain sense of the verse: *ad she-yit'yashu min ha-ge'ulah* — until they despair of redemption entirely. It is at the moment of the greatest loss of hope, when it feels as though there is nothing left to hold and support Israel, *kibi-yakhol ein somekh ve-ozer le-Yisrael* — as though there is none supporting or helping Israel — that *Mashiach* will come.

## Computing End Danger

### R. Zeira Warning

Rabbi Zeira's practice was the following: when he found rabbis deeply engaged in trying to compute when *Mashiach* would come, he would say to them — *bi-metuta*, please, *lo terachakuha* — do not push it further away. The very act of preoccupying oneself with calculating the moment of arrival, Rabbi Zeira teaches, delays rather than hastens the coming.

This is not merely the principle of a watched pot never boiling. There is a deeper concern. The tradition of *Mashiach* gives the Jewish people hope when oppressed and in exile; it orients the believer toward an ultimate redemption. But *Torah* and *mitzvot* have always been profoundly this-worldly. Preoccupying oneself with computing a future arrival date distracts from the work that needs to be done in the present — which is, in fact, the very work that would bring *Mashiach* closer.

### 3 Unbidden Things

*De-tanya* — as was taught in a *braita*: three things come *be-hesech ha-da'at* — when one is not paying attention to them. These are *Mashiach*, a found object (*metziah*), and a scorpion (*akrav*). If one goes about deliberately looking for a *metziah*, one will not find it; it is only when going about one's business that one stumbles upon a lost object. Likewise a scorpion strikes when one is not alert. The Maharsha attempts to link all three by connecting them to the question of what the messianic era will look like — whether it will be reward or punishment, a *metziah* or an *akrav*. But the broader point seems to be that such computations are not only futile; they actively distract from the present-world labor that gives the belief in *Mashiach* its meaning.

## Cosmic Timeline

### 6000 Years

*Amar Rav Katina*: the world has a lifespan of 6,000 years — *sheis alfei shanei havei alma* — and one thousand years of destruction thereafter, after which a new world will be created. The source is the verse *ve-nisgav Hashem levado ba-yom ha-hu* — God will stand alone on that day — combined with the principle that one day in God's eyes is a thousand years. That millennium will be a period in which God alone stands; all of creation will be destroyed, and a new world will come into being at the end of that thousand years.

Abaye offers a variant: *terei charuv* — two thousand years of destruction — citing the verse *yechayeinu mi-yomayim, ba-yom ha-shlishi yekimenu ve-nichyeh le-fanav* — He will revive us after two days; on the third day He will raise us up and we will live before Him. On this reading, *techiyat ha-meitim* — the resurrection — occurs at the transition from the second to the third thousand-year period, which would coincide with the beginning of the era of *yemot ha-Mashiach*.

### Shmita Analogy

*Tani kevatei de-Rav Katina* — a *braita* supports Rav Katina's position. Just as the *shmita* year is one year in seven during which the land lies fallow, so too the world will lie fallow for one thousand years out of every seven thousand. The scriptural support comes from the same verse — *ve-nisgav Hashem levado ba-yom ha-hu* — as well as the psalm: *Mizmor Shir le-Yom ha-Shabbat, yom she-kulo Shabbat* — a day of complete rest. The tension here is worth noting: we pray for *Olam ha-Ba* in terms of *Shabbat* as *me'ein Olam ha-Ba*, a foretaste of perfection — yet here the "day of complete rest" sounds far more cataclysmic and destructive than any idyllic, Edenic image. The two conceptions sit in some tension. Finally: *ki elef shanim be-einekha ki-yom etmol ki ya'avor* — a thousand years in Your eyes are like a yesterday that has passed.

### 3 Epochs

*Tana d'vei Eliyahu* divides the 6,000 years into three epochs of two thousand years each. The first two thousand years are *tohu* — void and emptiness — covering the period from creation until *Avraham Avinu*. By conventional chronology *Avraham* was born in the year 1948 from creation. Rashi connects the beginning of the *Torah* epoch to the moment when *Avraham* and *Sarah* began bringing people under *kanfei ha-Shekhinah* — Rashi dates this to when *Avraham* was 52 years old, corresponding approximately to the year 2000 from creation. Until that moment the world is a desolate world. The *Ramban* on *Bereishit* attempts a parallel structure dividing the world into epochs of one thousand years, each corresponding to a day of creation, but that is a separate framework.

The second two thousand years are the epoch of *Torah* — beginning with *Avraham* and *Sarah* bringing people closer to God and culminating with *Ma'amad Har Sinai* and the full flourishing of *Torah* and *mitzvot* within the Jewish people. Calculating forward, two thousand years from approximately year 2000 brings us to year 4000 from creation — corresponding roughly to the year 250 of the common era, the end of the period of the *Tanna'im* and the beginning of the Talmudic period. This is the epoch of *Torah* in the fullest sense.

The third two thousand years are *yemot ha-Mashiach* — the era in which *Mashiach* can come. This is the period that was, from the Gemara's perspective, already in progress. The Gemara adds: *uvavonoteinu she-rabu, yatze'u mehem mah she-yatze'u* — but due to our great iniquities, years have passed in this final epoch without *Mashiach* arriving. Those are lost opportunities. Yet *Mashiach* can still come at any point within this remaining two-thousand-year window.

The Gemara then records that *Eliyahu* told Rav Yehuda the brother of Rav Sila the *chasid*: the world will last no fewer than 85 *yovlot* — jubilee cycles. At 50 years per *yovel*, 80 *yovlot* equals 4,000 years; adding the remaining five brings us to 4,250, meaning *Mashiach* cannot come before approximately the year 500 of the common era. When Rav Yehuda asked whether *Mashiach* would come at the beginning or the end of that final *yovel*, and whether the entire period must be completed first, *Eliyahu* answered each time: *eini yodea* — I do not know. Rav Ashi summarizes: before that point, do not anticipate *Mashiach*; from that point onward, look for him.

## Specific Computations

### Roman Scroll

Rav Chana bar Tachlifa sent the following to Rav Yosef: I found a certain man — possibly a non-Jew — carrying a scroll written in *Ashuris*, the square Hebrew script, and in the Hebrew language. He told me he had hired himself out as a mercenary to the Roman army and found this scroll in the storehouses of Rome — evidently among items plundered when Rome conquered Israel. Written in it was: *le-achar arba'at alafim u-masayim ve-tish'im ve-achat shana le-olam* — after 4,291 years from creation, the world will come to its end. The *hagahah* records a variant reading but the figure is essentially consistent with the 4,250 computed earlier.

### End Wars

The scroll continues: some of that ending will come through *milchamos taninim* — wars of sea creatures, possibly the *Livyasan*, which some read as a metaphor for wars between great national superpowers. Some will come through *milchamos Gog u-Magog*. And *she'ar yemot ha-Mashiach* — the remainder of the time — will be the period of *Mashiach*.

### 7000 Years

*Ve-ein ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu mechadesh et olamo ela le-achar shivat alafim shana* — God will not renew His world until after seven thousand years. This clarifies the sequence: *Mashiach* comes sometime after the 4,290-year mark, the wars of *Gog u-Magog* follow, but the full renewal and re-creation of the world does not occur until the end of the seventh millennium.

### R. Acha Variant

Rav Acha the son of Rava offers a variant: *le-achar chameishot alafim shana* — after five thousand years. This makes the sequence slightly more coherent: *Mashiach* and the wars of *Gog u-Magog* arrive sometime in the fifth millennium, and the world renews at the end of that millennium, all within the seven-thousand-year framework.

## Computation Impossible

### R. Nasan Verse

*Tanya* — Rabbi Nasan says: *mikra zeh nokeiv ve-yored ad ha-tehom* — this verse pierces and descends to the very depths. The verse in question is from *Chavakuk*: *ki od chazon la-mo'ed ve-yafei'ach la-ketz ve-lo yekhazeiv; im yitmahmeah chakkeh lo, ki vo yavo lo ye'acher* — for the vision is yet for its appointed time, it declares toward the end and will not lie; if it tarries, wait for it, for it will surely come and will not delay. The plain sense of *ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* is that it declares the end; but as we will see, the Gemara also reads *yafei'ach* as "blow away" — meaning blow away and destroy all the calculations that have been made. Rabbi Nasan's point is that this verse is fathomless — you cannot reach the bottom of it, and no computation will succeed.

### Three Rejected

The Gemara proceeds to dismiss three specific rabbinic computations. The first is the calculation based on the verse in Daniel: *ad idan ve-idanin u-felag idan* — until a time, times, and half a time. Rashi explains that *idan* means 400 years, the duration of the Egyptian exile; two *idanin* plus a half gives 1,400 years. Counting 1,400 years from the time of Daniel — just before the *Beit ha-Mikdash* of 420 years — brings one to approximately the year 1000 of the common era. That did not come to pass.

The second computation belongs to Rabbi Simlai, who expounded: *he'ekhaltem lechem dim'ah va-tashkemo bi-dema'ot shalish* — I fed them bread of tears and gave them tears to drink in full measure. Rashi reads *shalish* as "a third," understanding the Egyptian and Babylonian exiles — 400 and 70 years respectively, totaling 470 — as one third of the ultimate exile. Multiplying by three yields 1,410 years, essentially identical to the first calculation. Counting 1,410 years of exile from the *Churban ha-Bayit* brings one to approximately the year 1500. That too did not come to pass.

The third is Rabbi Akiva's, who expounded: *od achat me'at va-ani mar'ish et ha-shamayim ve-et ha-aretz* — just a little more and I will cause heaven and earth to tremble. The Gemara understands that Rabbi Akiva read this as indicating *Mashiach* would come very soon after the *Churban* — which is why he believed Bar Kokhba was *Mashiach*.

The Maharsha reads the verse from Chavakuk as elegantly dismissing all three. *Ki od chazon la-mo'ed* — there is yet another vision of what *mo'ed* means (addressing the *idan ve-idanin* computation). *Ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* — and blow away the interpretation of the third (Rabbi Simlai's). *Ve-lo yekhazeiv* — and it is not Bar Koziva — for Barkochba was referred to by the sages as *ben Koziva*, meaning the liar. Thus the verse simultaneously undermines all three attempted computations.

### 3 Kingdoms

The Gemara adds: *malkhut rishon shivim shanah, malkhut shniya chamishim u-shtayim, u-malkhut Ben Koziva shtei shanim u-mechtza* — there was a kingship of 70 years (the successful phase of the *Beit Chashmonai*), a kingship of 52 years (Herod), and the rule of Bar Kokhba for two and a half years. The precise connection to what immediately precedes is not fully spelled out, but the Maharsha reads it as a response to Rabbi Akiva's expectation: Rabbi Akiva thought that "just a little more" meant *Mashiach* was imminent in his day and that Barkochba was the *Mashiach*. But the Gemara is pointing out that even after all of those brief sparks of Jewish self-sovereignty passed — Hasmonean, Herodian, and Bar Kokhba — *Mashiach* still did not come. All of those were not the true *Mashiach*; they passed, and *Mashiach* will come only afterward.

## Curse Computers

### Tiphach Curse

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: *tipach atzmotam shel mechashvei kitzin* — may the bones of those who compute the ends of days be blasted. The word *ve-yafei'ach la-ketz* in the verse is now read as an imprecation: blow away, destroy, those who blow toward the end — those who try to calculate when it will arrive.

### Computation Failure

The reason for this severity is explained: those who compute set a date, *Mashiach* does not come by that date, and then they say *Mashiach* will never come — *shuv eino ba*. The false expectation generated by calculation leads directly to despair of redemption altogether, which is far worse than never having made the calculation.

### Just Wait

The alternative, drawn from the rest of the verse, is: *chakkeh lo* — simply wait for him. *Im yitmahmeah, chakkeh lo* — if he tarries, wait for him. And lest one wonder whether God Himself is waiting and hoping for this time — *lachen yechakveh Hashem la-chananchem ve-lachen yarum le-rachamchem* — therefore God waits to show you grace; therefore He rises to show you compassion. God too is, so to speak, waiting and longing for the redemption.

### Justice Prevents

This raises a penetrating question: if we are waiting and God is waiting, *mi me'akev* — who is preventing *Mashiach* from coming? *Midat ha-din me-akevet* — the attribute of justice is what delays it. Everyone wants the redemption; but we must earn it, and the divine attribute of justice stands in the way until we do.

This leads naturally to a further question: if *midat ha-din* is what prevents it, and waiting for *Mashiach* does not overcome *midat ha-din*, then why is it important to wait for him at all? Why should we cultivate a sense of longing for something that will not arrive merely because we long for it? The Gemara implicitly asks whether such waiting distracts from the work — the *teshuvah* and *ma'asim tovim* — that would actually be effective.

### Reward Waiting

*Lekabel sakhar* — to receive reward, *shene'emar ashrei kol chokhei lo* — as it says, praiseworthy are all who wait for Him. The act of waiting itself, expressing a genuine belief in ultimate redemption, carries its own value and its own reward. The proper balance is clear: have faith, believe in redemption, work toward a better world because one believes it is achievable — but do not allow the anticipation of that future moment to consume the work that needs to be done right now. The two must be held in tension.

## 36 Righteous

### Abaye 36

*Amar Abaye*: the famous statement — *lo pachot almaya mi-tlasin ve-shita tzadikim*, the world has no fewer than thirty-six *tzadikim* — *d'mekablei appei Shekhinah be-chol dara* — who receive the countenance of the Divine Presence in every generation.

### Gematria 36

The proof: *ashroi kol chokhei lo* — praiseworthy are all who wait for *him*. The word *lo* — for him — has the numerical value of 36: *lamed* equals 30, *vav* equals 6.

### Rava Objection

But does this not contradict Rava's statement? Rava taught that the row of people standing before the *Kadosh Barukh Hu* stretches *tamnei srei alfei parsa* — eighteen thousand *parasangs*, citing the verse *saviv shemonah asar alef* — around him eighteen thousand. How can only thirty-six receive the Divine Presence if eighteen thousand stand before God?

### Clear/Cloudy Lens

*Lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The thirty-six are those who *mistaklei be-ispaklaria ha-meirah* — who see God through a clear, luminous lens. The eighteen thousand are those who see through a clouded lens — they are present before God, but their perception is indirect and dimmed. The thirty-six occupy the innermost circle, the most direct apprehension of the Divine.

One textual nuance: Abaye uses the word *dara*, which in this context can mean either "in every generation" or "row" (as Rava uses it in his statement). Reading Abaye as meaning "in every generation" fits better with the broader context of the *lamed vav tzadikim* as a phenomenon of every era.

### S.b.Y. Few

But is even thirty-six too large a number? *Ve-ha'amar Chizkiyah amar Rabbi Yirmeyah mishum Shimon bar Yochai*: Chizkiyah said in the name of Rabbi Yirmeyah in the name of Shimon bar Yochai — *ra'iti bnei aliyah ve-hen mu'atin* — I have seen those of true spiritual stature, and they are very few. Presumably this is from his period of seclusion in the cave. If there are a thousand, he said, I and my son are among them. If there are a hundred, I and my son are among them. If there are two — it is me and my son. How could there be thirty-six who receive the Divine Presence at the highest level?

*Lo kashya* — not difficult. *Ha d'ayli b'var, ha d'ayli be-lo var*: there are those who enter the divine presence only with permission — these are the thirty-six — and there are those so elevated that they enter even without permission. It is the latter category, those who need no permission, that may be as few as two. Whatever precisely this means, the distinction is between levels of intimacy with the divine: the thirty-six enjoy the highest access; the even smaller number — perhaps only two — enjoy an intimacy that transcends even the framework of permission.

## Repentance Debate

### R. Eliezer: If

*Amar Rav*: *kalu kol ha-kitzin* — all of the computed ends of days have expired and run out. The matter now depends entirely on *teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim* — on our repentance and good deeds. *Ve-Shmuel amar*: Shmuel says, *daiyo la-avel she-ya'amod be-evlo* — it is enough for the mourner to stand in his mourning. We have suffered long enough; God will bring *Mashiach* regardless. This appears to be the position that *Mashiach* will come even without *teshuvah*, and it leads directly into the debate of the *Tanna'im*.

*K'tana'ei* — this dispute echoes a debate among *Tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer says: *im Yisrael osim teshuvah nigalin ve-im lav ein nigalin* — if Israel does *teshuvah* they will be redeemed; if not, they will not be redeemed. The position is clear and absolute.

### R. Yehoshua: Evil King

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: you mean to say that if Israel does not do *teshuvah* they will never be redeemed — that there will be no *Mashiach*? *Ela* — rather, *Ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu ma'amid lahem melekh she-gezeirotav kashot ke-Haman* — God will raise up against them a king whose decrees are as harsh as Haman's. *Ve-Yisrael osim teshuvah u-machziram le-mutav* — and as a result, the external pressure will cause Israel to repent and improve their ways. Rabbi Yehoshua does not say *Mashiach* will come without *teshuvah*; the point is that God is committed to bringing the redemption regardless, and if Israel will not generate *teshuvah* from within, God will compel it from without.

### Scripture Quotes

*Tanya idach* — a second *braita* records the debate. Rabbi Eliezer cites: *shuvu banim shovavim erpa meshuvoteikhem* — return, wayward children, I will heal your waywardness — proving that *teshuvah* is the prerequisite. Rabbi Yehoshua counters: *chinam nimkartem ve-lo be-khesef tiga'elu* — you were sold for nothing and you will not be redeemed with money. *Chinam nimkartem* — you gave yourselves over to *avodah zarah* for nothing. *Ve-lo be-khesef tiga'elu* — and your redemption will not come through *teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim*. On this reading Rabbi Yehoshua appears to be saying that the redemption will not be contingent on our *teshuvah* and good deeds at all.

To harmonize this with his earlier position, the best reading is that Rabbi Yehoshua means: the redemption need not come through self-initiated *teshuvah*; God will compel Israel's return if necessary, and it is that divinely-induced *teshuvah* — not Israel's own *teshuvah* — that brings the redemption.

### Ownership Forced

Rabbi Eliezer then cites: *shuvu elai ve-ashuva aleikhem* — return to Me and I will return to you — which requires Israel to move first. Rabbi Yehoshua responds with: *ki anochi ba'alti vakhem ve-lakachti etkhem echad me-ir u-shnayim mi-mishpachah va-heveti etkhem Tzion* — because I have taken ownership over you, and I will take you, one from a city and two from a family, and bring you to Zion. The image is of God seizing Israel and forcing the ingathering even against their will — an act of divine compulsion, not of Israel's own initiative.

### Despised Redeemed

Rabbi Eliezer cites: *be-shuvah va-nachat tivvashe'un* — through returning and rest you will be saved — reading *shuvah* as *teshuvah*. Rabbi Yehoshua counters with: *ko amar Hashem go'el Yisrael kedosho, li-vzoh nefesh le-mita'ev goy le-eved moshlim, melakhim yir'u ve-kamu sarim ve-yishtachavu* — so says God the Redeemer of Israel, to the one despised, abhorred by nations, a slave to rulers — kings will rise and officers will prostrate themselves. The message: even in a state of utter degradation and unworthiness, God will redeem Israel.

Rabbi Eliezer's position is consistent throughout: *teshuvah* is the necessary and indispensable prerequisite for redemption. This also aligns with Rav's ruling. Shmuel's position — and Rabbi Yehoshua's — is somewhat less clear in its full extent: does it mean God will force *teshuvah* upon Israel and then redeem them, or that redemption will ultimately come irrespective of the moral condition of Israel because God's ownership and commitment to His people is unconditional? The *sugya* will continue to develop this question.
