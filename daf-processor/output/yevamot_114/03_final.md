# Yevamot 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shotah Divorce

### Rabbi Yitzchak View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּטֵּית וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה שׁוֹטָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַפִּקַּחַת בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ. וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת? שֶׁלֹּא יִנְהֲגוּ בָּהּ מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that one whose wife **became imbecilic** may not divorce her. **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: By Torah law, an imbecilic woman may be divorced, just as it is** in the analogous case of **a** halakhically **competent woman** who was divorced **against her will.** Since there is no need for a woman to agree to receive a bill of divorce, an imbecile's lack of sound mind does not prevent her from being divorced. **And** if so, **what is** the **reason** that the Sages **said** that an imbecilic woman **may not be divorced?** The reason is so **that** people **should not treat her in the manner of ownerless** property.

The daf picks up near the top of 113b with a discussion of *gerushin* as they apply to a *shotah* — a woman who is not of sound mind. The foundational principle at stake is whether *da'at* on the part of the wife is required for a *get* to take effect. Since divorce is a unilateral act initiated by the husband, the wife need not actively participate. Rabbi Yitzchak states this explicitly: *d'var Torah*, biblically speaking, *shotah mitgareshet* — a woman who is not of sound mind can be divorced. The proof is straightforward: just as a woman of full capacity can be divorced against her will, so too her own mental participation is not a prerequisite. The act is the husband's alone.

### Rabbinic Protection

If that is true biblically, why did the Rabbis rule otherwise? The Gemara explains: *shelo yinhagu bah minhag hefker* — so that people should not treat her licentiously. A woman who is mentally incapacitated and no longer under the protection of the marital framework is dangerously vulnerable. The Rabbis therefore prohibited the husband from divorcing her, effectively conscripting him into the role of her protector. Once he has married her, the responsibility is his, and he cannot simply dissolve the marriage and leave her exposed.

### Preservation Capacity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר גִּיטָּהּ וְיוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר עַצְמָהּ — מִי נָהֲגִי בָּהּ מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר? אֶלָּא, דְּאֵין יוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר לֹא גִּיטָּהּ וְלֹא עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What are the** precise **circumstances** of this case? **If we say** that this woman **knows how to guard her bill of divorce,** i.e., she understands the concept of a bill of divorce, **and** she also **knows how to take care of herself, would** people **treat her in the manner of ownerless** property? She is capable of protecting herself. **Rather,** the mishna is evidently referring **to a woman who does not know how to guard her bill of divorce, nor** does she know how to take care of **herself.**

The Gemara then asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If she possesses enough mental capacity to preserve her *get* and to protect herself from exploitation, then there is no concern of *minhag hefker* in the first place. And if she lacks even the capacity to preserve what is placed in her hand, then the biblical mechanism of divorce itself would not function — for the school of Rabbi Yannai taught that *v'natan b'yadah*, "and he shall place it in her hand," implies a woman who has a *yad*, a domain of genuine control. *Yad* is not merely the physical hand; it signifies protected possession. If she cannot protect anything placed in her hand, there is no *yad*, and the divorce fails even biblically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבַר תּוֹרָה שׁוֹטָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת? וְהָא אָמַר דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: ״וְנָתַן בְּיָדָהּ״, מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ יָד לְגָרֵשׁ עַצְמָהּ, יָצְתָה זוֹ, שֶׁאֵין לָהּ יָד לְגָרֵשׁ עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is it correct that **by Torah law an imbecilic woman may be divorced? But** didn't a Sage of **the school of Rabbi Yannai say,** with regard to the verse: "He shall write her a bill of divorce **and give it into her hand"** (Deuteronomy 24:3), that this refers only **to** a woman **who has a hand,** i.e., she has enough intellectual capacity **to** accept a bill of **divorce** for **herself.** This serves **to exclude this** imbecilic woman, **who does not have a hand to** accept her **divorce** for **herself.**

Furthermore, the school of Rabbi Yishmael derived an additional requirement from the phrase *v'shillachah mibeito* — "and he shall send her from his house." This teaches that the *get* is only effective for *mi shemeshullachat v'einah chozeret*, a woman who understands what it means to have been sent away and does not return. A woman who would be sent and simply wander back, not grasping that she is now divorced, is not biblically divorced — even though her role in the process is entirely passive. This is a striking point: even where the act is entirely imposed upon her, if she cannot comprehend the status being conferred, the divorce is ineffective.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשִׁלְּחָהּ מִבֵּיתוֹ״, מִי שֶׁמְּשַׁלְּחָהּ וְאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, יָצְתָה זוֹ, שֶׁמְּשַׁלְּחָהּ וְחוֹזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** this slightly differently. The verse continues: **"And send her out of his house"** (Deuteronomy 24:3); this indicates that one may divorce only the type of woman **whom he will send away and she will not return.** It serves **to exclude this** imbecilic woman, as **he will send her away and she will return,** as she does not understand the concept of divorce.

The resolution, then, is: *lo tzricha* — the case in question is a woman who can preserve her *get* and understands the meaning of her divorced status, but cannot protect herself from abuse. At that level, there is no biblical deficiency in the *get*, but there remains a rabbinic prohibition — precisely the *shelo yinhagu bah minhag hefker* concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּיוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר גִּיטָּהּ וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר עַצְמָהּ. דְּבַר תּוֹרָה שׁוֹטָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, דְּהָא יוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר גִּיטָּהּ. וַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן לָא לַיפְּקַהּ, שֶׁלֹּא יִנְהֲגוּ בָּהּ מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** Rabbi Yitzḥak's *halakha* is **necessary** in the case **of** a woman **who knows how to guard her bill of divorce but does not know how to take care of herself. By Torah law** this **imbecilic woman may be divorced, as she knows how to safeguard her bill of divorce, and** yet **the Sages said** that her husband should **not divorce** her, so **that** people **should not treat her in the manner of ownerless** property, because she does not know how to take care of herself.

### Tosefta Requirements

The *Tosefta* frames this concisely: if she knows to guard her *get* (implicitly including, it seems, an understanding of her divorced status), and the Rabbis nonetheless said *lo tifkah* — she may not be divorced — it is solely because of *shelo yinhagu bah minhag hefker*. The prohibition is thus entirely rabbinic in character.

### Rav Idi Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי דִידַהּ ״נִשְׁתַּטֵּית לֹא יוֹצִיא״, וְגַבֵּי דִידֵיהּ ״לֹא יוֹצִיא עוֹלָמִית״. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״עוֹלָמִית״ וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״עוֹלָמִית״? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הָא — דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הָא — דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The language of the mishna **is also precise** in this case, **as it teaches with regard to this** woman that if **she became an imbecile he may not divorce** her; **and** yet, **with regard to** the husband, the mishna states that if he became an imbecile **he may never divorce** her. **What is different here, that** the mishna **teaches** using the word: **Never, and what is different there, that** it **does not teach: Never? Rather,** one can **learn from here that this** *halakha*, that an imbecilic man may not give a divorce, applies **by Torah** law, and **this** one, that a man may not divorce an imbecilic woman, applies by **rabbinic** law, and therefore the mishna does not add the phrase: Never.

Rav Idi observes that this conclusion can also be inferred from the language of the Mishnah itself. When the *Mishnah* addresses the case of the wife becoming a *shotah*, it states *lo yotzi* — he shall not divorce her. But when the husband is the *shotah* or *cheresh*, the *Mishnah* uses the formulation *lo yotzi olamit* — he may never divorce her. Why the addition of *olamit* in his case and not in hers?

The answer: in his case, the divorce would be ineffective even biblically — he is entirely outside the *parsha*. The *Mishnah* therefore says *olamit*, never, because there is no circumstance under which his act would be valid. In her case, however, the prohibition is rabbinic; biblically it would work. The *Mishnah* therefore does not say *olamit*, signaling that the bar is not absolute incapacity but a rabbinic enactment — and that *bedi'avad*, after the fact, the *get* would take effect.

## Cheresh Comparison

### Inconsistency Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, אִישׁ פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, וְאִשָּׁה קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: אִשָּׁה פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, וְאִישׁ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said:** What is the reason that the husband of a woman who became a deaf-mute may divorce her, whereas a man who becomes a deaf-mute may not divorce his wife? **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars with regard to the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri:** Is it **obvious to him** that a deaf-mute **man** may not divorce his wife, **and he raised** his **dilemma** with regard to **a woman,** i.e., why she may be divorced if she is a deaf-mute? **Or perhaps,** it is **obvious to him** why one may divorce a deaf-mute **woman, and he raised** his **dilemma** with regard to a deaf-mute **man,** i.e., why he may not divorce his wife.

The Gemara raises a question attributed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri. The inquiry concerns the asymmetry between the *cheresh* — a deaf man — and the *chereshet* — a deaf woman — with respect to divorce. The question is which direction his challenge runs: is it obvious to him that a deaf man cannot divorce his wife and he wants to know why a deaf woman *can* be divorced? Or is it the opposite — that it is obvious that a deaf woman can be divorced and he is puzzled as to why a deaf man cannot?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, מִדְּקָאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה הָאִישׁ הַמְגָרֵשׁ לָאִשָּׁה הַמִּתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה יוֹצֵאת לִרְצוֹנָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִרְצוֹנָהּ, וְהָאִישׁ אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא אֶלָּא לִרְצוֹנוֹ — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אִישׁ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ. אַדְּרַבָּה, מִדְּקָאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: אַף זוֹ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אִשָּׁה קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof: **Come** and **hear from what** the Rabbis **said** in response **to** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri: **The man who divorces** his wife **is not similar to the woman who is divorced, as the woman is divorced** whether **she is willing or unwilling, and the man divorces** his wife only **willingly. Learn from here** that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **raised** his **dilemma** with regard to a deaf-mute **man,** not a woman, as the reply of the Rabbis is referring to the man, not the woman. The Gemara rejects this proof: **On the contrary, from** the fact **that the Rabbis said to him: This** woman, **too, has a similar** status, one can **learn from here** that he **raised** his **dilemma** with regard to a deaf-mute **woman.**

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the content of the response given to him. They answered: *eino domeh ish m'garesh l'isha mitgereshet* — the man who divorces is not comparable to the woman being divorced, for a woman is divorced willingly or unwillingly, while a man can only divorce willingly. Since the response concludes with the point about the man requiring volition, it seems the question was about the man. *Shema minah*, the Gemara concludes: his question was why a deaf man cannot divorce if a deaf woman can be divorced.

The Gemara then challenges this reading: since the response also includes the testimony of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada regarding a *chereshet* married by her father, the resolution addresses the woman's case — suggesting his question was actually about her. *Shema minah*, the Gemara then concludes the opposite: his question was why a deaf woman can be divorced.

### Actor Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְדִבְרֵיהֶם קָאָמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִישׁ לָא מָצֵי מְגָרֵשׁ — אִשָּׁה נָמֵי לָא מִיגָּרְשָׁא, אֶלָּא לְדִידְכוּ, מַאי שְׁנָא אִשָּׁה וּמַאי שְׁנָא אִישׁ! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה הָאִישׁ הַמְגָרֵשׁ לָאִשָּׁה הַמִּתְגָּרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri spoke to** the Rabbis **in accordance with their statement,** i.e., he formulated his statement so as best to argue with their opinion, as follows: **According to my** opinion, **just as** a deaf-mute **man cannot divorce** his wife, **so too** a deaf-mute **woman cannot be divorced. However, according to your** opinion, **what is the difference** between the case of a deaf-mute **woman and** the case of a deaf-mute **man? They said to him: The man who divorces** his wife **is not similar to the woman who is divorced.**

The Gemara resolves the impasse: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri was arguing *l'divrehem* — according to the position of the Sages. On his own view, just as a man who is a *cheresh* cannot divorce, a woman who is a *chereshet* cannot be divorced. But according to them, he challenges the inconsistency: what is the difference between a man and a woman? The Sages then explain both sides of the asymmetry. The man must be an *actor* in the process — he cannot be a *cheresh* and perform a valid act of divorce. The woman is not an actor; she is the recipient of the act. Therefore a *chereshet* can be divorced even if she lacks full *da'at*. There is no inconsistency once one understands the structural difference between acting and being acted upon.

One might ask how this differs from *kiddushin*, where analogously the man is the actor and the woman the recipient. Indeed the Gemara in *Kiddushin* draws certain parallels between *kiddushin* and *gittin*. But the decisive difference lies precisely in the element of *b'al korchah* — the woman can be divorced against her will, a dimension that has no parallel in *kiddushin*. And it is worth noting that biblically, the woman's willing consent to marriage is never depicted in the *pesukim*; the *Rishonim* read the verses as addressing a case of a minor whose father acts on her behalf, and the cases of an adult woman arranging her own marriage emerge from rabbinic inference, not explicit biblical text.

### Gudgada Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רָבָא: מֵעֵדוּתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא, אָמַר לְעֵדִים: ״רְאוּ גֵּט זֶה שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן״, וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״כִּנְסִי שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **Rabbi Yoḥanan** ben Gudgada **testified** that in the case of a deaf-mute minor whose father married her off, which is a marriage that is valid by Torah law, she may nevertheless be divorced once she matures. **Rava said: From the testimony of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgada** one may learn that if a husband **said to witnesses: See this bill of divorce that I am giving** my wife, **and** yet **he said to her: Take this promissory note, she is divorced,** despite the fact when he gave his wife the bill of divorce she did not know what it was.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada testified about a *chereshet* who was married by her father — a marriage that was binding *d'oreita* — and who could subsequently be divorced. This establishes that a *chereshet* stands in a position analogous to a *ketanah* married by her father: the *da'at* of the woman herself is not required for the marriage to take effect biblically, nor for the *get* to dissolve it.

### Deception Ruling

Rava draws a further inference from this testimony. Since Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada established that the wife's *da'at* is not required in the *gerushin* process, Rava rules that the following case is also valid: if a man showed the witnesses a document, said "see this *get* I am about to give my wife," and then handed it to her saying *kinsi shtar chov zeh* — here, take this promissory note — she is divorced. She was deceived into thinking she received a debt document, yet *harei zo megureshet*, it is a valid divorce. The reasoning is direct: just as Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada ruled that her *da'at* is not required, so too in this case it is not required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא לָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעְתַּהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעְתַּהּ. פְּשִׁיטָא?
>
> **Translation:** This *halakha* is derived from Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgada's statement in the following manner: **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgada say** that **we do not require her consent,** as there is no need for the woman to understand that she is receiving a bill of divorce? **Here, too, we do not require her consent,** and even if she believes that she is receiving a bill of debt, she is divorced. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** it **obvious** that there is no need for the woman's consent? What is the novel element in Rava's statement?

The Gemara asks: is this not obvious? It responds: one might have thought that since he described it to her as a promissory note, this constitutes an act of *bittul* — nullification of the *get*. The husband does have the capacity to annul a *get* before delivery. Perhaps calling it something else should be read as such an act?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִדַּאֲמַר לַהּ ״כִּנְסִי שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה״ — בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. אִי אִיתָא דְּבַטְּלֵיהּ — לְעֵדִים הֲוָה קָאָמַר לְהוּ, וּמִדְּלָא אֲמַר לְעֵדִים — לָא בַּטְּלֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר הָכִי — מֵחֲמַת כִּיסּוּפָא הוּא דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rava's statement is necessary, **lest you say: From** the fact **that** the husband **said to her: Take this promissory note, he has** thereby **nullified the bill of divorce.** Rava therefore **teaches us** that this is not the case, for **if it is** so, that he nullified the bill of divorce, **he would have said** so **to the witnesses. And from** the fact **that he did not say** this **to the witnesses, he** evidently **did not nullify** the bill of divorce **at all. And** the reason **that** the husband **said this,** i.e., that she should take this bill of debt, **it was due to shame that he said this to her,** as he meant to divorce her all along, but he did not want her to know at the time what he was doing.

The Gemara rejects this: if he had genuinely wished to nullify it, *l'eidim hava amar lehu* — he would have told the witnesses. He made a point of showing the witnesses that it was a *get*. Since he did not inform the witnesses of any nullification, *lo batlei* — it was not nullified. And the reason he called it a promissory note when giving it to her? *Machamat kisufa* — he was embarrassed to tell her to her face that he was divorcing her.

This carries an implicit *chidush* regarding the requirement of *mishullachat v'einah chozeret* discussed earlier. One might have thought that even in cases involving a *chereshet* or *ketanah*, the woman must be informed of her divorced status at the moment of delivery. The Gemara's ruling here seems to indicate that as long as she is the type of person capable of understanding what being divorced means, it is not necessary for that understanding to be operative at the precise moment the *get* is placed in her hand. The concept of *mishullachat v'einah chozeret* speaks to the woman's general cognitive capacity, not to a requirement of actual awareness in the moment.

## Katan Ochel Neveilos

### Intervention Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבַר טְלֵי וְטַלְיָא וְלִיטַיְּילוּ הָתָם, דְּאִי מַשְׁכְּחִי לְהוּ, מַיְיתֵי לְהוּ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: קָטָן אוֹכֵל נְבֵלוֹת — אֵין בֵּית דִּין מְצֻוִּוין לְהַפְרִישׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **and lead boys and girls and let them walk there** where the keys were lost, **and if they find the keys they will bring them** to you of their own accord, without you saying anything to them. The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Pedat **maintains** that with regard to **a minor** who **eats** meat from **unslaughtered animals** or violates other prohibitions, **the court is not commanded to prevent him** from doing so.

The Gemara transitions to a celebrated *sugya* that at first seems entirely unrelated to the foregoing discussion of *cheresh* and *shotah*, but which will be seen at the end to tie directly into the *Mishnah* of the current chapter. The question is: *katan ochel neveilos* — if a minor is eating *neveilos* or *treifos*, forbidden foods — *bei dina metzuvan l'hafrisho o lo*, is *beis din* — the court, representing the community — obligated to intervene and stop him?

This is not the question of parental *chinuch*. The obligation of *chinuch* in the Gemara emerges almost exclusively in the context of a father training his son in positive *mitzvos* — taking *lulav*, wearing *tefillin*, donning *tzitzis*. Whether *chinuch* applies equally to preventing transgressions is itself debated, with some *Rishonim* arguing surprisingly that *chinuch* is confined to positive commandments. But bracketing that question entirely, the present inquiry is about the community's responsibility — and by extension the general obligation of any third party — to prevent a minor from sinning. Does such a responsibility exist?

Several threads motivate the question. If a minor commits an act that would be a sin for an adult, is an *aveira* being done that, even if no individual is liable for it, represents a metaphysical reality requiring communal response? Or is it simply a *neutral* act from the standpoint of *din*? And if the former, does communal tolerance of it — failing to intervene — compromise the character of the community? These are the questions undergirding the *sugya*.

### Keys Retrieval Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: לֹא יֹאמַר אָדָם לְתִינוֹק ״הָבֵא לִי מַפְתֵּחַ״, ״הָבֵא לִי חוֹתָם״, אֶלָּא מַנִּיחוֹ תּוֹלֵשׁ, מַנִּיחוֹ זוֹרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Let us say that** the following source **supports his** opinion: **A person may not tell a child** on Shabbat: **Bring me a key, or: Bring me** my **seal** from the public domain. **However, he may allow** the child to **detach** plants and **allow him** to **throw** in the public domain. This shows that one need not be strict with a child who transgresses a prohibition, but one may not tell a child to transgress a prohibition.

The *sugya* opens with a case involving Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna, who lost the keys to the *beis medrash* in the *reshus harabim* before *Shabbos*. He came before Rabbi Padas, who instructed him: go take some children and have them stroll (*litaylu*) in the *reshus harabim*. If they happen upon the keys, they will bring them, and by that innocent discovery the keys reach you — without any *melacha* being performed by an adult, and without any explicit instruction to the children to carry.

The inference drawn is that Rabbi Padas holds: *katan ochel neveilos, ein beis din metzuvin l'hafrisho* — the community is not obligated to stop a minor from sinning — and moreover, it is apparently permissible to arrange circumstances in which the minor will incidentally perform an act that would be prohibited for an adult.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״תּוֹלֵשׁ״ — בְּעָצִיץ שֶׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב, ״זוֹרֵק״ — בְּכַרְמְלִית דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Abaye said** that this is no proof, as it is possible that **detaching** plants is referring **to an unperforated flowerpot,** as the prohibition against detaching plants from a vessel of this kind applies by rabbinic law. Similar, when it states: **Throw,** this can be referring to throwing **in a *karmelit*** rather than the public domain. A *karmelit* is an intermediate domain between public and private domains, which has the status of a public domain **by rabbinic** law. However, perhaps one is obligated to stop the child if he is transgressing a Torah prohibition.

The Gemara cites a *baraisa* in support: a person may not tell a minor to bring his key or seal on *Shabbos* — that would be explicit instruction. But if the minor is pulling out grass or throwing objects into the public domain, one may leave him (*manicho*) — there is no obligation to stop him. This is consistent: you cannot direct a minor to do an *aveira*, but you need not intervene when he does one on his own.

The Gemara challenges this, noting through Abaye that perhaps these cases involve only rabbinic violations — pulling plants from an unperforated pot, or throwing into a *karmelis* rather than a true *reshus harabim*. Perhaps the permissiveness of non-intervention applies only to rabbinic prohibitions, and a biblical violation would be different.

### Fire Extinguishing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: גּוֹי שֶׁבָּא לְכַבּוֹת — אֵין אוֹמְרִים לוֹ ״כַּבֵּה״ וְ״אַל תְּכַבֶּה״, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין שְׁבִיתָתוֹ עֲלֵיהֶם. קָטָן הַבָּא לְכַבּוֹת — אוֹמְרִים לוֹ ״אַל תְּכַבֶּה״, שֶׁשְּׁבִיתָתוֹ עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** the following statement (*Shabbat* 121a): If **a gentile comes to extinguish** a Jew's fire on Shabbat, **one may not say to him: Extinguish, or: Do not extinguish, because** responsibility for **his rest is not** incumbent **upon** the Jew. However, if a Jewish **child comes to extinguish** a fire on Shabbat, **they do say to him: Do not extinguish,** despite the fact that he is not yet obligated in mitzva observance, **because** responsibility for **his rest is** incumbent **upon** the Jew. This shows that one must prevent a minor from violating a Torah prohibition.

The Gemara brings a *baraisa* about *Shabbos*: if a non-Jew comes to extinguish a fire on *Shabbos*, one may neither tell him to extinguish it (*amira le-goy*) nor tell him to refrain — the non-Jew's *Shabbos* observance is not the Jew's responsibility. But if a minor comes to extinguish the fire, *omrim lo al tikhbe* — one must tell him not to do it — *she-shevisato aleihem*, because his *Shabbos* observance is the responsibility of those around him. This seems to establish a clear obligation to intervene when a minor would violate *Shabbos* — a biblical prohibition.

### Father's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת אָבִיו, דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי גּוֹי דְּעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִי שְׁרֵי? גּוֹי אַדַּעְתָּא דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is referring **to** a minor **who is acting with his father's consent.** Even if the father did not tell him explicitly what to do, the child is aware of his father's wishes, and acts on his behalf. The Gemara asks: The **same** reasoning can be applied **with regard to a gentile, that he acts with the Jew's consent,** and yet in this case **is it permitted** for him to perform labor on behalf of a Jew? The Gemara answers: **A gentile acts in accordance with his** own wishes. As an adult he is responsible for his own decisions and is not considered to be following the instructions of others.

Rabbi Yochanan explains the *baraisa*: the case of the minor extinguishing the fire is *be-oso da'as aviv* — the minor is doing it with his father's intent in mind, for the father's sake. He is not acting independently; he understands that his father wants him to put out the fire and is acting in that spirit. This makes the minor's act an extension of the father's will — not quite an explicit *amira*, but functionally close to one.

This raises an immediate question: if a non-Jew similarly extinguishes a fire because he knows his Jewish neighbor would want it, should that not also be prohibited? The Gemara responds: *ovdei kochavim ada'ata de-nafshei avid* — a non-Jew always acts for his own interests. Even when doing something to please a Jew, he calculates his own benefit — perhaps the Jew will pay him, or treat him more favorably. His act is therefore fundamentally self-motivated. The minor, by contrast, acts essentially as an extension of his parent, without the self-interested calculation of an independent adult.

This interpretation is striking because it means the Gemara treats the obligation to intervene by the fire not as flowing from a unique biblical *mitzvah* to ensure one's children observe *Shabbos* — even though such a *mitzvah* would seem to exist from the *Aseres ha-Dibros*, which explicitly includes *binkha u-viskha* among those whose *Shabbos* rest the householder is responsible for. Rather, the Gemara frames the issue in terms of the minor acting as an agent of the father, analogous to *amira le-goy*. The positive biblical obligation to ensure one's children keep *Shabbos* is effectively not invoked. Later *Acharonim* note this and suggest that perhaps the *Shabbos* case is indeed a special one due to that biblical source, but the Gemara itself does not develop this point.

Furthermore, the cases throughout this *sugya* — both the fire and the next case involving the grandfather's house — seem to involve parental figures. The Gemara treats them under the same rubric as the general community's obligation, implying that even parents, beyond whatever *chinuch* obligations they hold, are governed by the same framework as the broader *beis din* question. The implication is that if *beis din* need not intervene in cases of biblical violations, perhaps parents need not either — a conclusion that sits in tension with the *chinuch* obligation, though some *Rishonim* limit *chinuch* to positive commandments.

Rabbi Yochanan's conclusion leaves the general question of whether one must intervene in biblical violations unresolved. He demonstrates only that by *Shabbos*, the minor doing it *be-oso da'as aviv* creates a problem akin to *amira*; it does not demonstrate an independent communal obligation to prevent biblical violations.

### Grandfather Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּן חָבֵר שֶׁרָגִיל לֵילֵךְ אֵצֶל אֲבִי אִמּוֹ עַם הָאָרֶץ — אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִילֶנּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵינָם מְתוּקָּנִים. מָצָא בְּיָדוֹ פֵּירוֹת — אֵין זָקוּק לוֹ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּדְמַאי הֵקֵילּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear: The son of a *ḥaver*,** one who is devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, especially the *halakhot* of ritual purity, *teruma*, and tithes, **is accustomed to going to his mother's father,** who is **an ignoramus** and therefore is not known to be as careful to separate *teruma* and tithes. In this case, the son need **not** be **concerned lest** his grandfather **feed him** food **items that are not tithed.** If the father **found fruit in** the child's **hand,** and he does not know where the fruit is from, **he is not bound** to separate tithes from the fruit. This indicates that one need not prevent a minor from eating forbidden food. **Rabbi Yochanan said:** The Sages **were lenient with regard to doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*].** Since the prohibition against eating this produce applies only due to doubt, and most ignoramuses do separate tithes, the Sages were lenient in uncertain cases of this kind.

The Gemara brings another case. A child of a *chaver* — one who maintains strict standards of *terumot u'maasrot* — regularly visits his maternal grandfather who is an *am ha'aretz* and does not separate *tithes* properly. The Gemara rules: *ein chosheshin shema ya'achilenu devarim she'einam metukanim* — one need not be concerned that the grandfather will feed him improperly tithed produce. And if the child comes home with fruit in his hand, *ein zakuk lo* — there is no obligation to take it away from him.

Rabbi Yochanan limits the leniency: *b'demai hiklu* — they were lenient specifically with *demai*, produce that is *questionably* tithed, which is itself a rabbinic concern. This seems to imply that for a definite biblical violation — certain *tevel* — one would be obligated to intervene.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דִּדְמַאי, הָא וַדַּאי בְּעָא לְעַשּׂוֹרֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת אָבִיו! אֶלָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, קָאֵי הָכָא מְדַחֵי, קָאֵי הָכָא מְדַחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **Rather,** the **reason** that the Sages were lenient is **that** it is ***demai*,** from which it may be inferred that **if it was definitely** untithed, the father would be **required to tithe** the fruit. **But didn't Rabbi Yochanan** himself **say that** one need stop a minor only **when he is acting with his father's consent? Rather, Rabbi Yochanan is uncertain** with regard to this *halakha*, and therefore **in this case he refutes** it, and **in that case he refutes it.** In other words, he reached no definite conclusion about this matter, and therefore he treats each case on its own merits.

But the Gemara points out the tension: did Rabbi Yochanan not say earlier that the problem by the fire was specifically because the minor was acting *be-oso da'as aviv* — for the father's sake? That reasoning would suggest that in general there is no obligation to intervene, regardless of the severity of the violation. So which is it?

The Gemara concludes: *Rabbi Yochanan tzericha mispaka lei* — Rabbi Yochanan is uncertain. In each case he deflects: the fire case can be explained by the *da'as aviv* rationale; the *demai* case can be explained by its rabbinic character. Neither case forces a definitive ruling on whether there is a general obligation to intervene when a minor commits a biblical transgression.

### Impure Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּן חָבֵר כֹּהֵן שֶׁרָגִיל לֵילֵךְ אֵצֶל אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ — אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִילֶנּוּ תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה, מָצָא בְּיָדוֹ פֵּירוֹת — אֵין זָקוּק לוֹ! בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear:** With regard to **the son of a *ḥaver* priest who is accustomed to going to his mother's father, a priest** who is also **an ignoramus, one** need **not** be **concerned lest** his grandfather **feed him ritually impure *teruma*.** If the father **found fruit in** the child's **hand, he is not bound** to take it from him. This shows that even when the concern involves ritually impure *teruma*, which is a Torah prohibition, one is not required to ensure that a minor does not sin. The Gemara rejects this: This is no proof, as this is referring **to *teruma*** that is separated **by rabbinic** law, not a doubt concerning a Torah prohibition.

The Gemara raises a variant: the child of a *chaver* who is a *kohen* visits his maternal grandfather who is also a *kohen* but an *am ha'aretz*. The concern now is *terumah tmeiah* — impure *terumah* — which is a biblical prohibition. The Gemara rules: *ein chosheshin shema ya'achilenu terumah tmeiah*. Even here, there is no obligation to intervene proactively. The Gemara, however, qualifies this as applying to *terumah d'rabbanan* — rabbinic-level *terumah*, such as fruit-based *terumah* or *terumah* in the post-Temple era. And so again we cannot derive from this case a ruling about biblical-level obligations.

### Nursing Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יוֹנֵק תִּינוֹק וְהוֹלֵךְ מִגּוֹיָה וּמִבְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין בְּיוֹנֵק שֶׁקֶץ. וְלֹא יַאֲכִילֶנּוּ נְבֵלוֹת וּטְרֵפוֹת שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים. וּמִכּוּלָּן יוֹנֵק מֵהֶם וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת. וּבְגָדוֹל אָסוּר. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: נוֹהֲגִין הָיִינוּ שֶׁיּוֹנְקִים מִבְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear: A child may regularly suckle from a gentile woman; and** a child may suckle from **a non-kosher animal. And** in both cases **one** need **not** be **concerned that he** might be considered one who **suckles from a detestable creature. But one may not feed** a child unslaughtered **animal carcasses, or animals with wounds that will cause the animals to die within twelve months [*tereifot*],** or **repugnant creatures, or creeping animals.** A child **may suckle from all these,** including the non-kosher creatures, **even on Shabbat, but** in the case **of an adult,** it is **prohibited** for him to suckle on Shabbat even from a kosher animal. **Abba Shaul says: We were accustomed to suckle from a kosher animal on a Festival,** rather than milk it by hand, in the usual way.

The Gemara cites the ruling that an infant may nurse from a non-Jewish woman and even from a non-kosher animal, and one need not be concerned: *ein chosheshin b'yoneik sheketz* — there is no worry about this nursing being a forbidden act. However, it is explicitly prohibited to actively feed the infant *neveilos u'treifos* or *shekatzim v'remashim* — the prohibition is on active feeding, not on passive non-intervention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין בְּיוֹנֵק שֶׁקֶץ! הָתָם מִשּׁוּם סַכָּנָה. אִי הָכִי — גָּדוֹל נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** this *tanna* **teaches** that **one** need **not** be **concerned that he** might be considered one who **suckles from a detestable creature,** which indicates that a child may be left to eat forbidden food. The Gemara rejects this: **There,** permission is granted **due to a danger,** because a child must eat. The Gemara asks: **If so,** it should **also** be permitted for **an adult,** as saving a human life supersedes these prohibitions.

The Gemara asks: can this case be used to prove that *beis din* need not intervene? The answer is that the leniency here is entirely due to *sakana* — danger. *Stam tinok mesukan eitzel chalav* — an infant's need for milk is presumed to be a health consideration, and any restriction that might prevent the infant from obtaining sufficient milk is overridden on those grounds. The case therefore proves nothing about the general question of non-intervention in *aveiros* by minors.

### Nursing Melacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּדוֹל בָּעֵי אוּמְדָּנָא. קָטָן נָמֵי לִיבְעֵי אוּמְדָּנָא! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: סְתָם תִּינוֹק מְסוּכָּן אֵצֶל חָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **An adult requires consultation,** i.e., doctors or other experts must examine him and establish that he is dangerously ill. The Gemara retorts: **A minor should also require consultation** as to whether he is in danger. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** There is no need for a special consultation, as in an **unspecified** case **a child is in danger with regard to milk.** It can be assumed that a child needs milk, and if he does not get it, he will be in danger.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: נוֹהֲגִין הָיִינוּ שֶׁיּוֹנְקִים מִבְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא סַכָּנָה — אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת נָמֵי, וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא סַכָּנָה — אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב אָסוּר! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאִיכָּא צַעֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the last opinion in the above *baraita*. **Abba Shaul says: We were accustomed to suckle from a kosher animal on a Festival.** The Gemara inquires: **What are the circumstances? If there is** imminent **danger** to a person, **even on Shabbat** it should **also** be permitted. **And if there is no danger, even on a Festival** it should be **prohibited.** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where there is suffering,** i.e., they suffer from thirst but no danger is involved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָסָבַר מְפָרֵק כִּלְאַחַר יָד הוּא. שַׁבָּת דְּאִיסּוּר סְקִילָה — גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן, יוֹם טוֹב דְּאִיסּוּר לָאו — לָא גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** Abba Shaul **holds** that sucking directly from an animal **is** considered the prohibited labor of **extracting** performed **in an unusual manner.** It is prohibited to remove food from a source that is not fit for consumption. However, in this case he does not do so in the usual way of milking, but by sucking, and therefore it is prohibited by rabbinic law. Consequently, on **Shabbat, when** it is **a prohibition** punishable by **stoning, the Sages issued a decree** in this case, even if the milking is done in an unusual manner. Conversely, with regard to **a Festival, when** labor is a **negative prohibition** that is not punishable by stoning, **the Sages did not issue a decree** in a situation that involves suffering. In any case, this source offers no proof with respect to how to treat a child who transgresses.

The Gemara cites Abba Shaul's testimony: *nohagim hayinu she-yonkim m'beheimah tehorah b'Yom Tov* — they would directly suckle from a kosher animal on *Yom Tov*. The Gemara asks: if there is *sakana*, it should be permitted even on *Shabbos*; if there is no *sakana*, it should be forbidden even on *Yom Tov*. What then is the case?

The answer: *de-ika tzaar* — there is distress but not danger. And the ruling is based on the principle that direct suckling from an animal is considered *mesfarek ke-lachar yad* — an unusual manner of extracting the milk, making it only a rabbinic violation (*shinui*). Given that it is only rabbinic: on *Shabbos*, whose violation carries the penalty of *sekilah*, the Rabbis maintained their decree even in cases of *tzaar*. On *Yom Tov*, whose violation is only a *lav* without capital punishment, the Rabbis did not extend their decree to a case of mere *tzaar*, and it is therefore permitted.

*Tosafot* raises a difficulty from a parallel *sugya* in the chapter *Af al pi*, which indicates that direct suckling from an animal is treated as *mesfarek ke-lachar yad* and permitted on *Shabbos* in cases of *tzaar* as well. This creates an apparent contradiction: can *tzaar* suffice to permit a rabbinic violation on *Shabbos*? *Tosafot* distinguishes between degrees of illness and distress — *choleh* versus *mitzta'er* — and acknowledges that different *sugyos* seem to apply different thresholds.

*Tosafot* also raises a prior question: is expressing milk from an animal even a biblical violation? The argument that *dash* — threshing — applies only to *gidulei karka*, things that grow from the ground, would make milk entirely a rabbinic category, and the need for the *ke-lachar yad* dispensation would be under question. *Tosafot* acknowledges there are opinions that even without the *ke-lachar yad* reasoning, milk from animals would never be a biblical violation of *dash* since it does not come from the ground.

These discussions carry significant practical implications for the *halacha* of nursing mothers on *Shabbos*. If expressing milk by hand is either not a biblical violation because it is *ke-lachar yad*, or not a biblical violation because milk does not derive from *gidulei karka*, the threshold for permitting it is considerably lower. The *Tzitz Eliezer* is notably lenient on matters relating to nursing mothers and *Shabbos*, reasoning that the need to maintain the milk supply — even in anticipation of future nursing — falls within the category of *sakana* for an infant, since *stam tinok mesukan eitzel chalav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּם כִּי שֶׁקֶץ הֵם״, לֹא תַּאֲכִילוּם — לְהַזְהִיר הַגְּדוֹלִים עַל הַקְּטַנִּים. מַאי לָאו, דְּאָמַר לְהוּ לֹא תֵּאכְלוּ! לָא, דְּלָא לִיסְפּוֹ לֵיהּ בְּיָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear:** The verse states, with regard to creeping animals and other non-kosher animals: **"You shall not eat them [*tokhlum*] for they are a detestable thing"** (Leviticus 11:42). The Sages interpret this verse as though it said ***ta'akhilum*, do not feed them** to others. The verse comes **to warn adults concerning minors,** i.e., not only is it prohibited for adults to eat these items themselves, they may also not feed them to minors. **What, is it not** the case **that** this means adults must **say to** children: **Do not eat,** and prevent them from transgressing? The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No,** it means **that** an adult **may not feed** the minor non-kosher food directly **by** direct **action,** but this does not prove that one must stop a child from eating non-kosher food of his own accord.

The Gemara then returns to the core *sugya* with additional attempts to resolve the question through *derashos* from *pesukim*. The verse *lo sochlum* — do not eat them — regarding forbidden creatures generates the *derasha*: *lehazhir gedolim al ha-ketanim*, to warn adults about minors. Does this mean adults must actively stop minors from eating forbidden foods? Or does it only mean adults may not feed them directly — *de-lo lispei lei b'yadei*? The Gemara repeats this exchange for the verse *ki nefesh mikem lo tokhal dam* regarding blood, and again for *emor v'amarta* regarding *tumah* for *kohanim*. In each case the *derasha* sounds like an obligation of active intervention, and in each case the Gemara deflects: perhaps it only forbids active feeding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כׇּל נֶפֶשׁ מִכֶּם לֹא תֹאכַל דָּם״, לְהַזְהִיר הַגְּדוֹלִים עַל הַקְּטַנִּים. מַאי לָאו, דְּאָמְרִי לְהוּ לֹא תֵּאכְלוּ! לָא, דְּלָא לִיסְפּוֹ לְהוּ בְּיָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear,** as the verse states: **"No soul of you shall eat blood"** (Leviticus 17:12). This serves **to warn adults concerning minors. What, is it not** the case **that** this means adults must **say to** children: **Do not eat blood?** The Gemara responds: **No,** here, too, it means **that** an adult may not feed minors **by** direct **action.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אֱמוֹר ... וְאָמַרְתָּ״, לְהַזְהִיר גְּדוֹלִים עַל הַקְּטַנִּים. מַאי לָאו, דְּאָמַר לְהוּ לָא תִּיטַּמּוֹ! לָא, דְּלָא לִיטַמּוֹ לְהוּ בְּיָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear,** as the verse states: **"Speak** to the priests, the sons of Aaron, **and say** to them: None shall become impure for the dead among his people" (Leviticus 21:1). This reiteration of "speak" and "and say" comes **to warn adults concerning minors. What, is it not** the case **that** this means adults must **say to** children: **Do not become impure?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is possible to interpret **that** an adult should **not render** children **impure by** direct **action.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שְׁקָצִים — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיסּוּרָן בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, אֲבָל דָּם — דְּעַד דְּאִיכָּא רְבִיעִית, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן דָּם — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא כָּרֵת, אֲבָל שְׁרָצִים, אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: And all these three cases **are necessary,** despite the fact that they apparently teach the same *halakha*, i.e., that adults may not feed minors forbidden food. **As,** had the *tanna* **taught us** only the case of **repugnant creatures,** we would have said that the *halakha* is stringent in this case **because they are prohibited by any amount,** i.e., a creeping animal or insect is prohibited on pain of lashes even if it is very small, provided that it is a whole creature (*Tosafot*). **However,** with regard to eating **blood, which** is not a Torah prohibition **unless there is** at least the volume of **a quarter***-log*, one might **say** that there is **no** prohibition against directly feeding minors, as this prohibition is more lenient. **And** had the *tanna* **taught us** only the case of **blood,** we would have said that they were stringent with regard to blood **because there is** the punishment of ***karet*** for eating blood. **However, for creeping animals,** whose consumption does not entail *karet*, one might **say** that this *halakha* does **not** apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיסּוּרָן שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן טוּמְאָה — כֹּהֲנִים שָׁנִים, מִשּׁוּם דְּרִיבָּה בָּהֶן מִצְווֹת יְתֵרוֹת, אֲבָל הָנֵי, אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** had the *tanna* **taught us** only **these two** cases, we would have said it is **because their prohibitions apply equally to all** Jews. **However,** in the case of **ritual impurity,** which is a mitzva for priests alone, one might **say** that there is **no** prohibition against directly rendering minors ritually impure. **And** had the *tanna* **taught us** only the case of **ritual impurity,** we would have said that the case of **priests is different** and more stringent, **because** the Torah **includes** extra mitzvot **for them. However,** with regard to **these** prohibitions of creeping animals and blood, one might **say** that there is **no** prohibition against directly feeding minors. Therefore, it was **necessary** to state all these cases.

The Gemara then explains why all three cases are necessary. Had only the case of *shekatzim* been stated, one might think *dam* (blood) is excluded since it requires a *revi'it* to violate. Had only *dam* been stated, one might exclude *shekatzim* since *dam* has the weight of *karet*. Had only those two been stated, one might exclude *tumat kohanim* since it applies only to *kohanim*, who have heightened responsibility. And had only *tumat kohanim* been stated, one might think the other cases, which apply universally, need not carry the same responsibility. Each case is necessary, and yet none definitively resolves whether the obligation is one of intervention or merely a prohibition on active feeding.

## Deaf Siblings Case

### Initial Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנֵי אַחִין, אֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ וְאֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ, נְשׂוּאִין שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת פִּקְּחוֹת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת? תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another relevant source with regard to a minor who eats the meat of unslaughtered animals. **Come** and **hear:** If **two brothers, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent,** were **married to two** halakhically **competent sisters, and the deaf-mute married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do?** His brother's wife is **released** without levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, **due to** the prohibition with regard to **a wife's sister.**

Having covered the entire *sugya* of *katan ochel neveilos*, the Gemara now demonstrates how it connects to the *Mishnah* of this chapter, which deals with a *cheresh* and his obligations in *yibbum* and *chalitzah*. The case involves two brothers, one hearing (*pikeach*) and one deaf (*cheresh*), married respectively to two sisters, one hearing (*pikachet*) and one deaf (*chereshet*).

### Deaf Dies First

If the deaf brother — married to the hearing sister — dies first, the hearing brother is now potentially obligated in *yibbum* toward his brother's widow. But that widow is his own wife's sister — *achot isha* — and is therefore an *ervah* toward him. She requires neither *yibbum* nor *chalitzah*. *Tetzei mishum achot isha* — she simply goes free.

### Hearing Dies First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת? מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו אֲסוּרָה לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the deaf-mute** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do? He divorces his wife with a bill of divorce,** which is as valid as their original marriage. **And his brother's wife is forbidden** to him **forever.** He must divorce his wife, as the levirate bond applies by Torah law, whereas his marriage is valid by rabbinic law. However, in practice he is unable to consummate levirate marriage with the *yevama* or perform *ḥalitza* with her.

The more complex case: if the hearing brother — married to the hearing sister — dies first, what is the deaf brother's obligation? He has a biblical obligation of *yibbum* toward his brother's hearing widow, but he is rabbinically married to her sister. He cannot perform *yibbum* because it is *achot isha*, and he cannot perform *chalitzah* because he is a *cheresh*. He must therefore give his wife a *get* — divorcing his rabbinic wife — and his brother's widow is *asurah alav le-olam*, forever forbidden to him.

### Both Deaf Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט? תִּיתִּיב גַּבֵּיהּ, קָטָן אוֹכֵל נְבֵלוֹת הוּא! מִשּׁוּם אִיסּוּרָא דִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **Why** should **he divorce his wife with a bill of divorce? Let her** continue to **live with him,** as a deaf-mute **is** in the same category **as a minor who eats** the meat of **unslaughtered animals,** for he is not legally competent. Consequently, even if he were to engage in sexual relations with her, this would not be considered a transgression. The Gemara answers: If the matter concerned the deaf-mute alone, this would be the case. However, here he must divorce her **due to the prohibition** that applies to **her,** as his wife is legally competent, and just as she is forbidden to him, he is also forbidden to her.

The Gemara uses these cases to drive the *sugya* toward its real question. In the first variant it asks: why must the hearing brother not remain with both women when the deaf brother dies? The answer: because *she* — the widow — is a hearing woman, fully obligated in *halacha*, and allowing the configuration to continue would violate *her* prohibition. *Mishum issura didah.*

In the second variant: why must the hearing brother married to the deaf woman divorce her when the hearing brother dies? Because *he* — the surviving brother — is a hearing man, and it is his prohibition at stake. *Mishum issura deilei.*

These two cases establish the principle: if at least one party in the forbidden arrangement is fully obligated halakhically, *beis din* will not permit the status quo. The question then crystallizes: what if *both* parties are deaf — *cheresh* and *chereshet* — married to *chereshes*? In that case, neither party bears a biblical obligation. Is *beis din* obligated to intervene?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנֵי אַחִין, אֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ וְאֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ נְשׂוּאִין לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, אַחַת פִּקַּחַת וְאַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת. מֵת חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת? תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ. מֵת פִּקֵּחַ בַּעַל פִּקַּחַת, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה חֵרֵשׁ בַּעַל חֵרֶשֶׁת? מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear:** If **two** halakhically **competent brothers** were **married to two sisters, one** of whom is **a deaf-mute and** the other **one** halakhically **competent, and the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **do?** The deaf-mute is **released due to** the prohibition with regard to **a wife's sister.** If **the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the** halakhically **competent** sister **died, what should the** halakhically **competent** brother **married to the deaf-mute** sister **do? He divorces his wife with a bill of divorce,** as the halakhically competent sister came before him for levirate marriage, and the legal status of her levirate bond is higher than that of his marriage to his wife, a deaf-mute. **And he** releases **his brother's wife,** who is not a deaf-mute, **by means of *ḥalitza*,** as they are both competent and can therefore perform *ḥalitza*.

The *Mishnah* rules: *motzi ishto b'get* — he must divorce his wife. And so the Gemara asks: *v'hacha de-lav issura didah ika, v'lav issura deilei ika* — here, neither her prohibition nor his prohibition is at stake. Both are deaf. Why must they separate? The Gemara demands a *get*, yet on the logic of *katan ochel neveilos*, one would think there is no obligation to intervene.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא דְּלָאו אִיסּוּרָא דִידַהּ אִיכָּא, וְלָאו אִיסּוּרָא דִּידֵיהּ אִיכָּא, וְקָתָנֵי: מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט! אָמַר רַב שְׁמַעְיָה: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם הַתָּרַת יְבָמָה לַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: **But here** is a case **where there is no prohibition** that applies to **her and there is no prohibition** that applies to **him,** as they are both deaf-mutes who are not prohibited to each other, **and** yet it **is taught** that **he divorces his wife with a bill of divorce. Rav Shemaya said:** This is a rabbinic **decree due to** the danger of **permitting a *yevama* to** a member of the **public.** Since people might think that the deaf-mute sister is his full-fledged wife, they are likely to conclude that it is permitted for this *yevama* to marry someone other than the *yavam*, like any other sister of the wife of the *yavam*. However, in actual fact she is not the sister of the wife of her *yavam*, as the deaf-mutes are not married by Torah law, and she may not marry any other man because she is a *yevama* whose *yavam* cannot marry her or perform *ḥalitza*. For this reason, the Sages decreed that this deaf-mute husband must divorce his wife. In sum, this source too does not provide conclusive proof with regard to the question of a minor who eats meat from unslaughtered animals.

### Gezeirah Answer

Rav Shemaya answers: *gezeira mishum achot yevama lashuk* — it is a rabbinic decree, not because we demand they stop violating a prohibition that doesn't apply to them, but for an entirely different reason. If the deaf brother and his deaf wife simply continue living together without any *get*, observers will conclude that no *get* or *chalitzah* was necessary — they will infer that the *yevama*, the brother's widow, is free to remarry as if she were a simple case of *achot isha* with no *zikah*, no bond of *yibbum* at all. But that would be a serious error: the *zikah* is real, even if only rabbinic, and the *yevama* is not free to simply go out and remarry. Allowing the deaf couple to remain together without a *get* would cause people to treat the *yevama* as *mutar lashuk* — free for the marketplace — when she is not.

Therefore, the reason *beis din* demands a *get* in this case has nothing to do with the obligation to stop the *cheresh* couple from committing an *aveira*. The Gemara is explicit: the *sugya* of *katan ochel neveilos* remains genuinely unresolved — we do not know whether *beis din* must intervene when a minor (or a *cheresh*) commits a biblical violation. The *get* requirement here is a *gezeira* with an entirely independent rationale: to prevent the *yevama* from being wrongly released to the general public.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ חֵרֵשׁ
>
> **Translation:**

With this, the chapter concludes: *Hadran alach ha-cheresh*.

## Husband Death Report

### Three Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלְכָה הִיא וּבַעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם. שָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וְשָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם. וּבָאָה וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — תִּנָּשֵׂא. ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — תִּתְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who went, she and her husband, overseas,** if there was **peace between him and her,** i.e., the couple were not fighting at the time, **and** there was also **peace in the world,** i.e., there was no war at that time, **and** the woman **came** back by herself **and said: My husband died, she may marry** on the basis of her own testimony. Likewise, if she said: **My husband died,** and they did not have children, but her husband had a brother, **she may enter into levirate marriage.**

The final section of the daf opens a new chapter — the *perek* known as *Ha-Isha Shalom* — which concerns a woman who reports that her husband has died overseas and seeks to remarry or perform *yibbum*. The *Mishnah* identifies the conditions under which she is believed: first, that there was *shalom beino leveina* — peace within the marriage; second, that there was *shalom ba-olam* — peace in the world, i.e., no war; and third, of course, that she has come forward and stated that her husband died. When all three conditions are met, *tinasei* — she may remarry — and *tisyabeim* — she may perform *yibbum*.

### Lost Credibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וּמִלְחָמָה בָּעוֹלָם. קְטָטָה בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וְשָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם. וּבָאתָה וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן בָּאתָה בּוֹכָה, וּבְגָדֶיהָ קְרוּעִין. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ תִּנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** If there was **peace between him and her** when they left **but** there was **war in the world,** or if there was **a quarrel between him and her and peace in the world, and she came and said: My husband died, she is not deemed credible,** as she may be mistaken or lying. **Rabbi Yehuda says: She is never deemed credible** when she testifies that her husband died, **unless she came crying and her clothing was torn,** in which case it is apparent that she is speaking the truth. **They said to him:** This is an incorrect distinction. Rather, **both this** woman who cries **and this** woman who does not cry **may marry** on the basis of their own testimony.

If any of the conditions is absent, she is not believed. If their marriage was good but there is war in the world, she is not believed. If there is peace in the world but they were fighting — *ktata beino leveina* — she is not believed even then, and cannot remarry based solely on her testimony. The concern in the case of marital strife is that she might be deliberately fabricating, motivated perhaps by the knowledge that if she remarries and her husband later reappears, he will be compelled to divorce her — a result she may be seeking. The concern in the case of war is different: not fabrication, but honest misapprehension, as the Gemara will explain.

### Yehudah View

Rabbi Yehudah takes a stricter position: *l'olam eina ne-emenes* — she is never believed based on her testimony alone, even when all the favorable conditions are present, *ela im kein ba-a bocha u'vgadeha kru'im* — unless she arrives crying with her garments already rent. Only the presence of corroborating behavioral evidence warrants credibility. The Sages reject this: *achas zu v'achas zu tinasei* — in either case she may remarry, whether or not she displays such signs.

### War Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנָא ״שָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ״, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״קְטָטָה בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ״. תְּנָא ״שָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם״, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״מִלְחָמָה בְּעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The *tanna* of the mishna **taught** the clause: **Peace between him and her, because** later **he wanted to teach** the clause: **A quarrel between him and her.** Likewise, **he taught: Peace in the world, because he wanted to teach** afterward: **War in the world.** In other words, the fact that there was peace between them or peace in the world is of importance only because the opposite is the case in other situations dealt with in the mishna. Therefore, the *tanna* emphasized the lack of disruptions in the first case cited by the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמִלְחָמָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי: סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּא בְּכֹל הָנֵי דְּאִיקְּטוּל, הוּא פָּלֵיט? אִם תִּימְצָא לוֹמַר, כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ נָטְרָא עַד דְּחָזְיָא, זִימְנִין דְּמָחוּ לֵיהּ בְּגִירָא אוֹ בְּרוּמְחָא וְסָבְרָא וַדַּאי מֵת, וְאִיכָּא דְּעָבֵד סַמְתָּרִי וְחָיֵה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** that one does not rely on her testimony **when** there is **war?** It is **because she says** what **she imagines** to be the case: Can it **enter one's mind** that **among all these** people **who were killed,** her husband alone **is saved? If you say: Since there was peace between him and her, she guards herself** and waits **until she** actually **sees** that he died; even so, **at times** it might happen **that** his enemies **strike him with an arrow or spear and she thinks** that **he is certainly dead** due to the wound, **and** yet this is no proof that he is dead, as **there are** instances **when** someone **prepares medicine [*samterei*]** for the wounded person **and he survives,** despite his apparently fatal wound.

The Gemara clarifies the reason for the disqualification in the case of war. Rava explains: *mishum de-amra bididamei* — because she speaks based on *didamei*, on impressionistic inference rather than certain knowledge. She reasons: *bechol hanei de-iktal hu palat* — all those others died; surely my husband was not the one who escaped? If an entire battalion was wiped out and her husband was among them, she naturally assumes he is dead.

Yet it is possible that she did not actually witness his death. *Zimnin de-machvu lei bigira o vi-rumcha* — sometimes he is struck by an arrow or spear, and she assumes the wound was fatal. *V'ika de-avdi samtrei v'chayei* — but some injuries are dressed and the person survives. Even a faithful wife who would not intentionally deceive the court may, under the conditions of war and siege, honestly report a death that did not occur. The Gemara continues on the following *daf*.
