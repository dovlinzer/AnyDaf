# Zevachim 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Owner vs. Kohen Thought

### Rebbe Yose Introduction

The daf opens on 47a, picking up approximately four lines from the top, wrapping up the fourth *perek* before beginning the fifth. The final line of the *mishnah* had presented the position of *Rebbe Yose*, who argued that even when a *kohen* fails to maintain the proper intention during the *avodah*, the *korban* remains valid by virtue of *tnai beis din* — a standing condition established by the court. That much was clear. But appended to his ruling was a further statement that seemed to stand apart from *Rebbe Yose*'s main point: that *machshava* follows only the *oved*. The *Gemara* now turns to examine who this statement is directed against, and why.

### Rabbi Eliezer Opinion

The *Gemara* introduces the matter with the framing *masnisin delo ki hai tana* — our *mishnah* (and indeed, as will become apparent, the entire *masechta*) does not accord with a particular *tana*. A *braisa* is then cited: *amar Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yose, shamati she'habalim mefaglin* — Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose testified that he had heard that the owners of a *korban* can render it *pigul*. The implication is striking. Normally the entire framework of *Masechet Zevachim* concerns what the *kohanim* do — the technical *avodos* performed in the *Beis HaMikdash*. But Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose introduces the possibility that the *ba'alim*, the owners, have their own role: since they are connected to the *korban* in a meaningful way, their thought carries independent weight.

### Machshava Follows Oved

Against this view, the final line of the *mishnah* asserts that *machshava* follows only the *oved*. This principle can be understood as the operative logic behind *Rebbe Yose*'s broader position: if the owner's *da'as* were to matter, the entire *korban* would be placed in the hands of non-experts whose intentions are unknown and uncontrolled. The condition of *tnai beis din* might itself be read as a legal mechanism for marginalizing the owner's intent — a way of saying that whatever the owner may be thinking, the court's standing presumption governs the *korban*'s status. Either way, the *Gemara*'s reading is clear: *machshava* is the province of the one performing the *avodah*, not of the one who brought the animal.

### Shechita vs. Dam

An alternative interpretation, grounded in the *Tosefta*, offers a different way of understanding the underlying debate. The *mishnah* opened with the phrase *lesheim shisha devarim hazevach nizbach* — the sacrifice is slaughtered. One might argue that the relevant mental state attaches to the act of *shechita* itself, and since *shechita* can be performed by a non-*kohen* — even by the owner — the owner's intention during *shechita* might be considered formative. Against this, the position that *machshava* follows the *oved* would insist that the *avodah* properly begins with the *kibbul ha-dam* and the subsequent blood rites, and it is there — performed exclusively by the *kohanim* — that intent is legally operative. While the *Gemara* itself reads the debate as being about the four *avodos* versus the owner, this alternative reading situates the disagreement in the contrast between *shechita* and *dam*, and between the act of the *zoveiach* and the act of the *oved*.

### Rava's Source Hikriv

The *Gemara* then asks: what is the textual basis for Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose's position? *Amar Rava: de'amar kra, yakriv hamakriv es korbano* — a verse in Bamidbar 15 reads *vehikriv hamakriv korbano la'Hashem*. The apparent redundancy — *hikriv hamakriv* — is taken to signal that the *makriv*, the one who brings the offering, refers not to the *kohen* performing the *avodah* but to the owner presenting the *korban*. In the ordinary idiom of *Zevachim*, the person designated as performing the *avodah* would naturally be the *kohen*; but here the *Torah* draws attention to the one whose *korban* it is. This provides the textual anchor for Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose's claim.

One might ask whether the owner's *machshava* in this framework would be effective at any moment, or only during the four *avodos*. This is a genuine question touching on a classic *chakira*: is it the thought alone that makes *pigul*, anchored to an act as a requirement of connectivity, or is it the *avodah* itself that is rendered invalid when framed by a particular intention? If the former, then in principle the owner's thought at any point during the *avodos* might suffice. The *Gemara* does not resolve this fully, but the implication is that the owner's *machshava* is still bounded by the period of the four *avodos* — it is not a free-floating invalidation.

### Rashi on Makriv

Rashi explains the derivation as follows: *ama be'alim bichlal makriv* — the *ba'alim* are included within the category of *makriv*. The doubling of the verbal root in *hikriv hamakriv* signals that the one "bringing" the sacrifice encompasses not only the *kohen* performing the sacred rite but also the owner in whose name the *korban* is offered. While it remains somewhat unclear why this *pasuk* more than others establishes this inclusion, the principle Rashi draws is that for purposes of *machshava* and its capacity to create *pigul*, the owner is recognized — at least by Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose — as a legitimate subject of *halachic* intentionality.

## Abaye's Three Tannaim

### Three Tannaim Intro

*Amar Abaye*: Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — all three hold the principle that *ze machashev veze oved havya machshava*, meaning that even when one person thinks and a different person acts, that thought can nonetheless be *halachically* significant. Abaye is identifying a structural similarity across three otherwise disparate *halachic* domains, each involving the unusual case of a *machshava* that operates independently of the one performing the act.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

The first case — *Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose ha de'amran* — is what has just been discussed: the *ba'alim* are connected to their *korban* in a way that, according to Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose, gives their thought operative weight even though they are not performing the *avodah*.

### Shechita Avodas Kochavim

The second case involves a ruling of Rabbi Eliezer himself, found in a *mishnah*: *hashochet la'avodas kochavim shechitata kesheira, Rabbi Eliezer posel* — the standard ruling is that if one slaughters an animal intended for *avodas kochavim*, the *shechita* is valid; but Rabbi Eliezer holds it invalid. The scenario envisioned is one in which a *kohen* or Jew performs the *shechita* while the non-Jewish owner harbors the intention to offer the animal to his deity. The non-Jew is not performing the act — he is merely thinking. Yet Rabbi Eliezer holds that the owner's intention to dedicate the animal to *avodas kochavim* is sufficient to render the *shechita* problematic, making the meat the equivalent of *zivchei meisim*, an offering to idols, and thus forbidden. The parallel to the first case is clear: an owner, not performing the act, whose *machshava* nonetheless defines the character of what is being done.

### Shabbos Crumbs Case

The third case is a ruling of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar regarding *Shabbos*. A general principle governs *Shabbos* liability: one is not *chayav* for carrying out an object unless it meets the threshold of *lehatznia* — it must be the kind of thing that a normal person would set aside and preserve. This has two components. First, the object must be objectively suitable for preservation — something that has real use and value. An object forbidden in *hana'ah*, such as *chametz* on *Pesach*, fails this test objectively. Second, even an object that is in principle suitable must be of sufficient quantity that normal people would bother to preserve it; a crumb so tiny that people routinely let it be trampled fails the subjective threshold.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's case involves an object that fails one of these thresholds — say, a crumb too small for ordinary people to bother saving. But a particular individual, Reuven, decided that this crumb was significant to him and set it aside (*hitzni'o*). He thereby established its subjective importance. Subsequently, Shimon — a different person — comes along and carries that crumb out into *reshus harabim* on *Shabbos*. *Nischayav zeh bemachshava shel zeh* — Shimon is *chayav* by virtue of the *machshava* of Reuven. The significance that made the crumb legally cognizable was established by someone who did not perform the act of carrying; the one who actually carried it out performed the act without independently having established the object's importance.

In all three cases, the same conceptual structure is at work: one person's *machshava* defines or frames an act performed by another person. As Abaye notes, however, while these cases share this structure, they are not identical in *halacha*, and one does not automatically imply the other.

### Gemara Hierarchy

The *Gemara* now proceeds to analyze the hierarchical relationships among the three positions. It begins with the observation that *travayhu is lehu de'Rabbi Eliezer be'Rabbi Yosi* — the *tannaim* responsible for the latter two cases (*Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*) would certainly agree with Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose's ruling about *pigul*. The argument runs as follows: *hashta bechutz amrinan* — if we say outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, where the *Torah* does not particularly emphasize *machshava* as a transformative legal force, another person's thought can define an act, then *bifnim mibaya* — inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, where the *Torah* explicitly emphasizes the power of *machshava* (as expressed in the special *parsha* of *pigul*, *lo yeichashev lo pigul yihyeh*), there is all the more reason to say that even one who is not performing the act can invalidate it through thought. So both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would follow Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose into the domain of *pigul*.

The reverse, however, does not hold: *Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yosi leis leih de'travayhu* — Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose might not accept the positions of the other two. His reasoning could be restricted to the *Beis HaMikdash* context, where the *Torah* heightens the power of *machshava*. Outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, where no such emphasis exists, he might not extend the principle — *dilma bifnim hu de'amrinan bechutz lo amrinan*.

### Pigul & Beis Mikdash

Within the latter two cases, a further hierarchy exists. *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is leih de'Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who applies the principle to *Shabbos*, would certainly apply it to *avodas kochavim* as well. If it holds in the *Shabbos* domain — where, as will be seen, there is actually a strong reason to say it should *not* apply — then surely it holds by *avodas kochavim*, which has stronger grounds for the principle. But *Rabbi Eliezer leis leih de'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — Rabbi Eliezer, who accepts the principle by *avodas kochavim*, might not accept it by *Shabbos*. The reason is that *avodas kochavim* bears a resemblance to the *Beis HaMikdash* context: *shechita* for idolatrous purposes has an analogy to *shechita* in the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the category of *shchutei chutz* reflects this structural parallel. Thus if one accepts that inside the *Beis HaMikdash* another person's *machshava* can invalidate — which is Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Yose's position — it is coherent to extend that to *avodas kochavim*, which mirrors that structure. *Aval Shabbos* — but *Shabbos* is different.

### Melechet Machsheves

The *Gemara* identifies a specific principle that makes *Shabbos* not merely a weaker case than *avodas kochavim*, but in certain respects a domain where another person's *machshava* is *less* likely to carry weight than elsewhere: *meleches machsheves asra Torah*. The *Torah*'s prohibition of *Shabbos* labor is defined by the concept of *meleches machsheves* — a significant, intentional act. This principle generates a range of exemptions throughout *hilchos Shabbos*: if one intends to throw an object four *amos* and it travels eight *amos*, one is *patur*; if one intends to pick one fig and picks another, one is *patur*. The act must conform precisely to the doer's intent to generate *chiyuv*. This means that by *Shabbos* the doer's own intent is especially central — it is the alignment of the actor's intention with his act that constitutes the prohibited *melacha*. Consequently, there is a positive reason to say that *Shabbos chiyuv* requires the *machshava* to belong to the one performing the act, not to a different person. Even if elsewhere we might extend another person's *machshava* to define an act, *Shabbos* presents a specific counterargument: the very principle of *meleches machsheves* pulls in the direction of requiring the doer's own intent. Rabbi Eliezer, who holds by *avodas kochavim*, therefore need not hold by *Shabbos*, where this countervailing principle is operative.

The broader analytical picture Abaye draws, then, is one of a graduated hierarchy: the *Beis HaMikdash* context, with its explicit *Torah* emphasis on *machshava*, is most hospitable to extending another person's thought. *Avodas kochavim* shares enough structural resemblance to the *Beis HaMikdash* to plausibly carry the principle across. *Shabbos*, despite being a domain where one *tana* does apply the principle, has an internal reason — *meleches machsheves* — that actually argues against applying it. The *Gemara*'s hierarchy reflects these distinctions, producing a structure in which each position implies those above it but not necessarily those below.

With this, the fourth *perek* concludes. It is worth pausing to note that this last *mishnah* — with its brief acknowledgment that the owners' intent might matter, and the lone *tana* who affirms it — represents something of an anomaly within the *masechta*. *Masechet Zevachim* is overwhelmingly focused on what the *kohanim* do: the precise technical requirements of each *avodah*. The *Torah*'s language elsewhere — *l'reiach nichoach la'Hashem*, the sense that a person is *bringing* something to God — recedes almost entirely into the background. That broader dimension resurfaces only here, in this concluding *mishnah*, with its formulas of *leshem zoveach*, *leshem shem*, and the minority voice that gives the owner's intent a legal foothold. Having noted it, the *masechta* moves on. *Hadran alach Beis Shammai.*

## Eizehu Mekoman Intro

### Perek Five Overview

The fifth *perek*, *Eizehu Mekoman*, is familiar to many from the *davening*: it is recited early in the morning service, during *Birchos Hashachar* before *Pesukei D'Zimra*. As a literary composition, it reads quite differently from what has preceded it in *Masechet Zevachim*. Rather than engaging in the kind of fine-grained analysis of *machshava* and *psel* that has occupied the fourth *perek*, it offers a clean, organized presentation of the *halachos* governing where each category of sacrifice is slaughtered and how its blood is handled. It is the kind of chapter one might have imagined as an introduction to the *masechta* — a distilled, well-ordered survey of the *avodah* organized by sacrificial category.

### North Slaughter Rule

*Eizehu mekoman shel zevachim* — where is the place of slaughter for the sacrifices? The *mishnah* begins with *kodshei kodashim*, the most sacred category of offerings. This encompasses essentially every sacrifice whose meat goes either to the *kohanim* or entirely onto the *mizbeach* — the *olah*, *chatas*, *asham*, *shalmei tzibbur*, and *menachos*. Excluded are offerings such as *shelamim* and *korban Pesach*, where the owners themselves eat the meat, which fall into the category of *kodashim kalim*. The general rule is stated: *kodshei kodashim shechitatan ba-tzafon* — they are slaughtered in the north of the *mizbeach*. The *mishnah* will go on to detail the additional specifications that distinguish the individual *kodshei kodashim* from one another, but this is the overarching rule that unites them.

### Yom Kippur Par Se'ir

The *mishnah* begins its itemized treatment with the most inward of all sacrifices: *par v'sair shel Yom HaKippurim* — the bull and the goat of Yom Kippur. As *chata'os* they are *kodshei kodashim*, and accordingly *shechitatan ba-tzafon*, they are slaughtered in the north. *V'kibbel damam bi-kli sharet ba-tzafon* — the blood is caught in the north as well, in a *kli sharet*. The *kohen* may not slaughter on the northern side while catching the blood across the line; the *kibbul* must also occur in the north.

*V'daman te'un hazaya bein ha-badim ve-al ha-parochet ve-al mizbe'ach ha-zahav* — the blood requires sprinkling in three places: between the staves of the *Aron*, on the *parochet*, and on the golden *mizbeach*. This is the distinctive Yom Kippur blood rite: the *kohen gadol* enters the *Kodesh Kodashim* and sprinkles once upward and seven times downward before the *Aron*, then exits and does the same on the *parochet*, and then applies blood to the corners and top of the *mizbeach ha-zahav*.

*Matana achat mehen me'akevet* — any single one of these applications, if omitted, invalidates the *avodah*. This was established earlier in the *masechta* in the context of the *chata'os penimiyos*: the inner *chata'os* whose blood enters the *Kodesh* require that each application be performed, and a single omission prevents the *korban* from being valid.

*Shirei ha-dam haya shofech al yesod ma'aravi shel mizbe'ach ha-chitzon* — the remainder of the blood was poured on the western base of the outer *mizbeach* as the *kohen* exited. *Ve-im lo natan lo ikev* — but if he did not pour the *shirayim*, the *korban* is not invalidated. Whether *shirayim* are *me'akev* was a *machlokes* among earlier authorities; this *mishnah* rules they are not.

### Parim Nisrafim

The *mishnah* continues with *parim ha-nisrafim u-se'irim ha-nisrafim* — the bulls and goats that are burned. The bull is the *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur*, offered when the Sanhedrin issues an erroneous ruling that leads the community astray. The goat corresponds to the case of *he'elem davar* involving *avodas kochavim*, as discussed in *Masechet Horayos*. These too are *chata'os* whose blood is applied inside, but unlike the Yom Kippur offerings, their blood is not taken into the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

*Shechitatan ba-tzafon ve-kibbul daman bi-kli sharet ba-tzafon* — same rule as above: slaughtered in the north, blood caught in the north in a *kli sharet*. *V'daman te'un hazaya al ha-parochet ve-al mizbe'ach ha-zahav* — but the blood rite here does not include sprinkling before the *Aron*. The *kohen* does not enter the *Kodesh Kodashim*; he sprinkles only on the *parochet* and on the *mizbeach ha-zahav*. *Matana achat mehen me'akevet* — again, any single omitted application invalidates.

*Shirei ha-dam haya shofech al yesod ma'aravi shel mizbe'ach ha-chitzon* — the remainder is poured on the western base of the outer *mizbeach* upon leaving the *Ulam*. *Ve-im lo natan lo ikev* — and if it was not done, it does not invalidate. *Elu ve-elu nisrafin be-veis ha-deshen* — both the Yom Kippur offerings and these are burned in the *beis ha-deshen*, the ash-removal site outside the Temple precincts, where the entrails are burned on the *mizbeach* in the ordinary way but the meat itself is burned outside.

### Kibbul Dam Question

The *Gemara* raises a pointed question: *ve-nisni nami ve-kibbel daman bi-kli sharet ba-tzafon* — given that the rule about catching blood in a *kli sharet* in the north applies not only to *parim ha-nisrafim* but to all *kodshei kodashim* subsequently discussed in the *mishnayos*, why was this rule not included in the umbrella statement at the outset of the *perek*? The general heading stated only that *kodshei kodashim* are slaughtered in the north; why not also state there that their blood is caught in a *kli sharet* in the north?

### Asham Metzora Exception

The *Gemara* answers: *keivan de-ika asham metzora de-kibbel damo be-yad* — because there exists the *asham metzora*, the guilt offering of the *metzora* (the person afflicted with *tzara'as*), in which the blood is caught by the *kohen*'s hand rather than in a vessel. In this offering, the *kohen* dips his finger into the blood held in his palm and applies it to the ear, thumb, and toe of the *metzora* as part of the purification ritual. Since this application involves catching the blood by hand, it cannot be said universally that all *kodshei kodashim* require blood caught in a *kli sharet*. The *mishnah* therefore omitted this from the general statement.

However, the *Gemara* notes, it is also true that the *asham metzora* does require a *kli sharet* — specifically for the blood that is used for the *avodah* at the *mizbeach*. So the rule is not entirely inapplicable; it applies to one dimension of the blood service. This is why when the individual offerings are discussed specifically, the *kli sharet* requirement is stated: it is true for those offerings in the relevant respect.

### Two Kohanim Braita

A *braisa* is then cited to clarify the mechanics of the *asham metzora*. The verse states *velakach* — the *kohen* shall take from the blood. One might have thought this means he takes it with a vessel (*yachol bekli*). But the verse continues, *venatan* — he shall put it on the ear of the *metzora*. Just as the putting is done with the *kohen*'s own hand and finger (*ma netina be-atzmo shel kohen*), so too the taking — the *kibbul* — is done with his own hand (*af lekicha be-atzmo shel kohen*).

But one might then have supposed that even the blood going to the *mizbeach* is caught by hand. To this the *braisa* responds: *ki kachatat ha-asham hu* — this *asham* is compared to a *chatas*; just as a *chatas* requires a vessel for the *kibbul* (*ma chatas taun kli*), so too this *asham* requires a vessel for the *avodah* at the *mizbeach* (*af asham taun kli*).

*Nimtzeta ata omer*: the practical conclusion is that the *asham metzora* requires two *kohanim* — *shnei kohanim mekablin et damo, echad be-yad ve-echad be-kli*. One *kohen* catches blood in a *kli sharet* and proceeds to the *mizbeach* to perform the blood rite there in the ordinary fashion. A second *kohen* catches blood in his hand and brings it to the *metzora* for the purification rite. The *avodah* at the *mizbeach* looks like any other sacrifice; the purification rite is performed intimately, hand to ear, as the *Torah* prescribes — paralleling the oil, which the *Torah* likewise specifies is poured directly onto the *kohen*'s hand.

This is precisely why the *mishnah* could not state *kibbul dam bi-kli sharet* as a universal rule for *kodshei kodashim*: the *asham metzora* bifurcates the *kibbul*, requiring a vessel for one purpose and the hand for another. The *mishnah* chose not to issue a blanket rule that would be immediately qualified, while preserving the *kli sharet* requirement when dealing with each offering individually.
