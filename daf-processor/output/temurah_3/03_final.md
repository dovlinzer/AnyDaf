# Temurah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Temurah Basics

### Masechet Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַקׇּדָשִׁים — הַכֹּל מְמִירִין, חוּץ מִן הַצִּבּוּר וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הַיָּחִיד אֶחָד מִשּׁוּם עַצְמוֹ אִם הִקְדִּישׁ קׇרְבַּן, לֹא שֶׁהוּא רַשַּׁאי לְהָמִיר, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם הֵמִיר מוּמָר, וְסוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA. **With regard to all consecrated animals, everyone can effect substitution** by uttering the formula of substitution, **except for the public and partners.** If the public or partners sanctified an animal as an offering, an individual cannot effect substitution for it. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even an individual who sanctified a personal offering** can **not effect substitution; rather,** it means that **it is not permitted for** a person **to effect substitution, but if one substituted** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated animal, **the substitution takes effect, and** the one who substituted the non-sacred animal **incurs the forty** lashes.

Masechet Temurah opens with the straightforward biblical prohibition: *lo yachlifenu velo yamir oto* — one may not transfer or substitute a consecrated animal. Yet, as becomes immediately apparent, the *masechet* devotes the overwhelming bulk of its attention not to the prohibition itself but to its consequences. The Torah states: *ve'im hamir yamir behemah bivehemah vehayah hu utemurato yihyeh kodesh* — if one attempts the exchange, the *kedusha* is not removed from the first animal, and the second animal also becomes *kadosh*. In many cases, that second animal may even be brought as a *korban*. The central drama of the *masechet*, then, is what happens after someone has violated the prohibition — what are the legal effects of that transgression?

### Why Do It?

This raises an immediate question: why would anyone attempt *temurah* in the first place? Nobody in their right mind would do so. The transgressor violates a prohibition, yet accomplishes nothing: the first animal retains its *kedusha*, and if one wished to sanctify the second animal, one could do so directly. The entire scenario seems to presuppose either genuine foolishness or, more plausibly, someone who knows the act is prohibited but does not appreciate its legal futility.

### Sin & Lashes

Does this lack of awareness about the consequences affect one's liability for *malkus*? It does not. To incur lashes, one must willfully transgress a prohibition — but one need not fully understand the legal consequences of that transgression. A person who knows that substitution is forbidden, yet does it anyway while remaining unaware that it will leave the first animal *kadosh* and render the second *kadosh* as well, has still willfully violated the *lav* and is liable. The transgression consists simply in uttering the words of substitution; no physical act is required. That alone constitutes the violation.

### Torah's Reason

This brings into focus a deeper question about the nature of the prohibition. If someone were to say, "I simply want the second animal to become *kadosh* as well, without intending to remove the *kedusha* from the first," that would not constitute *temurah* at all. *Temurah* is defined specifically as the *attempt to substitute* — to remove *kedusha* from the first animal and transfer it to the second. The Torah's prohibition targets this attempt. Even though the attempt fails on its own terms, the Torah forbids the very act of trying to replace one *korban* with another.

### Sin vs Effect

This leads to a fundamental question that animates much of the discussion: is the consequence of *temurah* — that both animals become *kadosh* — a *punishment* imposed because of the sin, or is it simply the natural legal *consequence* of having applied *kedusha* to the second animal? The *gemara* frames this question sharply: what if the person who performed *temurah* committed no sin?

## Temurah & Sin

### Minor's Temurah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: קָטָן מַהוּ שֶׁיָּמִיר? הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּקָטָן שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּכֵיוָן אַקְדּוֹשֵׁי לָא אַקְדֵּישׁ, אֲמוֹרֵי מֵמַיר? אֶלָּא כִּי קָמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ — בְּקָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים. מִי אָמְרִינַן, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: ״אִישׁ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כִּי יַפְלִיא נֶדֶר״ — לְרַבּוֹת מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ, דְּקׇדְשׁוֹ קָדוֹשׁ. מִדְּאַקְדּוֹשֵׁי מַקְדֵּישׁ, אֲמוֹרֵי נָמֵי מֵמַיר, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו בַּר עוּנְשִׁין הוּא, בִּתְמוּרָה לָא מִיתְּפִיס?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** With regard to **a minor, what is** the *halakha*? Is he able **to effect substitution** or not? The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** where this question arises? **If we say** that it is referring **to a minor who has yet to reach the age of** responsibility for his **vows,** i.e., twelve years and one day, **you should not raise the dilemma, as since he cannot consecrate** an animal by means of a vow, **can he effect substitution? Rather, when he raises this dilemma,** it is **with regard to a minor who has reached the age of** responsibility for his **vows.** **Do we say** that a minor can effect substitution, **since the Master said** with regard to consecration: The verse states: "When **a man** shall clearly utter a vow" (Leviticus 27:2). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** the unusual formulation: **Shall clearly utter [*yafli*] a vow,** instead of the more conventional term: Shall take a vow [*yiddor*]? This serves **to include a discriminating** minor **[*mufla*] on the brink of adulthood,** teaching **that his consecration takes effect.** Perhaps, from the fact **that he can consecrate** an animal, **he can also effect substitution. Or perhaps, since no** minor **is subject to punishments, he cannot apply** sanctity to an animal **by** an act of **substitution,** which would incur a punishment.

The *gemara* explores this through two cases. First: a minor. A child who is within approximately one year of *bar* or *bat mitzvah* and who understands what he or she is doing has the capacity to create *kedusha*. If such a minor utters the formula of *temurah* — declaring that this non-*korban* animal should stand in place of the *korban* — has the minor sinned? Not in a full halachic sense. Does the second animal nevertheless become *kadosh*? This question goes to the heart of whether *temurah*'s effect is about sin or about the act. The *gemara* leaves this as an unresolved question.

### Non-Jew Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר קָטָן עָבֵיד תְּמוּרָה, דְּהָא אָתֵי לִכְלַל עֳונָשִׁין, גּוֹי מַהוּ שֶׁיָּמִיר? מִי אָמְרַתְּ מִדְּאַקְדּוֹשֵׁי מַקְדֵּישׁ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגּוֹיִם, שֶׁנּוֹדְרִים נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲמוֹרֵי נָמֵי מֵמַיר. אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אָתֵי לִכְלַל עֳונָשִׁין, כִּי עָבֵיד תְּמוּרָה לָא קָדְשָׁה?
>
> **Translation:** And even **if you say** that **a minor can perform substitution, as he will come to** an age when he is **subject to punishments, what is** the *halakha* as to whether **a gentile** can **effect substitution? Do you say** that he can do so from the fact **that his consecration takes effect, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Any man [*ish ish*]…**that brings his offering." **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** repetitively **"*ish ish*"?** This serves **to include gentiles,** teaching **that they** can **take vows** to bring **vow offerings and gift offerings as a Jew** can. Since the consecration of a gentile takes effect, perhaps **he can effect substitution as well. Or perhaps, since he will not come to** a time when he is **subject to punishments,** therefore **when he performs** an act of **substitution,** the animal is **not consecrated.**

The second and more pointed case is that of a non-Jew. A non-Jew can sanctify an animal and bring it as a *korban* — the *halacha* accepts this. But for a non-Jew, there is no concept of sin with respect to *temurah* at all, in a qualitatively different sense than a minor. Can a non-Jew perform *temurah*? The *gemara* notes that this question is, in a sense, moot: one of the established *halachot* governing the status of *mukdashei akum* — animals sanctified by non-Jews — is that *temurah* cannot be performed on them. The question therefore cannot be tested in the simple form in which it was posed.

### Rav Abahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: קׇדְשֵׁי גוֹיִם — לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא, אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין עֲלֵיהֶם נְסָכִים, אֲבָל קׇרְבָּנוֹ טָעוּן נְסָכִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Zevaḥim* 5:6): With regard to animals **consecrated by gentiles,** one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them, he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property after the fact. **And** if one consumes **them one is not liable for** committing a transgression **with regard to** the prohibitions of ***piggul*** if they were sacrificed with the intent to consume them beyond their designated time, of ***notar*** if he consumed them beyond their designated time, **and** of consuming **ritually impure** offerings if he was impure. Gentiles **cannot render** a non-sacred animal **a substitute** for one they consecrate. **And one does not bring libations for** the offerings of a gentile as independent offerings, **but his offering requires libations.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.**

But the *gemara* then refines the question. *Ki kamibaya li behekdesh akum miskaper beyisrael* — the real question is when a non-Jew sanctifies an animal for a Jew to use as his *korban*. In such a case, the non-Jew provides the animal, but it is the Jew's *korban*. So who is the relevant party for *temurah*: the one who sanctified (*makdish*), or the one for whom the *korban* is being brought (*miskaper*)?

To resolve this, the *gemara* cites the principle of Rav Abahu in the name of Rav Yochanan: *hamakdish mosif chomesh* — the one who sanctifies an object is the one who must add the *chomesh* (the additional fifth) when redeeming it. Anyone else who redeems *hekdesh* pays only the market value. This establishes that in the *chomesh* context, we follow the *makdish*, not someone else. However: *umiskaper oseh temurah* — by contrast, when it comes to *temurah*, it is the *miskaper*, the one for whom the *korban* is being brought, who is considered the owner and who can perform *temurah*. If I sanctify my animal for another Jew to use as his *korban*, it is now his *korban*, and he — not I — would be in a position to perform *temurah* (and thereby transgress).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, וּמִתְכַּפֵּר עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, וְהַתּוֹרֵם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ עַל שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ — טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Solve this** dilemma **by** invoking a statement **of Rabbi Abbahu, as Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If **one consecrates** an animal as an offering to be brought by another, and the animal develops a blemish disqualifying it for sacrifice, if the one who consecrated it desires to redeem it, he **adds one-fifth** to its value, just as he would were it his own offering. By contrast, if the one achieving atonement with the offering desires to redeem it, he does not need to add one-fifth. **But the one achieving atonement** with the offering **can render** another animal **a substitute** for it as if he had consecrated it. **And** if **one separates *teruma*,** the portion of produce designated for a priest, **from his own** produce **for** the purpose of exempting the produce **of another, the benefit of discretion** as to which priest or Levite will receive it **is his.**

A parallel case is cited: *hatorem mishelo al chavero tovat hana'ah shelo* — if one takes *terumot u'ma'asrot* from one's own grain in order to exempt a friend's grain, the *terumah* and *ma'aser* so separated belong to the one who did the separating, not the friend. So the one who separated gets to designate which *kohen* receives it, not the friend whose grain was also covered. This illustrates how these roles of *makdish* and *miskaper* are parsed differently in different contexts.

### Hybrid Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם הוּא דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר מִתְכַּפֵּר, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ תְּחִילָּתוֹ וְסוֹפוֹ בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲבָל הָכָא, הָכִי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: מִי בָּעֵינַן מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף דְּתֵיקוּ בִּרְשׁוּת מַאן דְּעָבֵיד תְּמוּרָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama could **say to** this: **There,** in Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, the offering **comes on behalf of** the **Jew** who consecrated it. **Due to that** reason, **we follow** the **one atoning, and** during both the **initial** consecration of the offering **and its ultimate** sacrifice the animal **is in the possession of a Jew,** who is able to effect substitution. **But here,** where a gentile consecrated the animal, **this is how he raises the dilemma: Do we require that from the initial** consecration of the offering **until the ultimate** act of sacrifice **it** must **be in the possession of one who** is able to **effect substitution** or not? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand [*teiku*]** unresolved.

Returning to the *sha'aleh*: if a non-Jew sanctified the animal, but it is now being used as a Jew's *korban*, do we say it is a fully valid *korban Yisrael* such that the Jew can now perform *temurah* on it? Or does the fact of the non-Jewish sanctification leave the animal at a lesser level of *kedusha*, one that cannot be the subject of *temurah* even when the *miskaper* is a Jew?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from Rav Abahu's ruling, but the attempt fails. The *gemara* responds: *hasam hu dekasi mikoach yisrael* — in Rav Abahu's case, a Jew sanctified the animal for another Jew, so it began with a Jew and ends with a Jew. *Vehavlei techilato vesofo beyad yisrael.* Therefore it was obvious that the *korban* retained its full Jewish *kedusha* and only the question of ownership needed to be resolved. *Aval hacha hacha kamibaya li* — but in our case the entire question is whether we require that the animal remain in the domain of someone capable of performing *temurah* from beginning to end. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

It is worth pausing on what has happened here. The original question — does *temurah* require sin, and thus can a minor or non-Jew perform it? — has been substantially reframed. The question about the non-Jew, specifically in the hybrid case, has shifted away from the person of the non-Jew and toward the *kedusha* of the animal. The *gemara* is no longer asking about whether sinfulness is required for *temurah*'s effect, but rather whether the level of *kedusha* imparted by a non-Jew's sanctification is the type of *kedusha* upon which *temurah* can operate. That question remains open.

Rashi, it is worth noting, comments that although the *gemara* left the minor's case as an open question, he understood the conclusion to be that a minor cannot perform *temurah*. If that is correct, it implies that some connection to sin is indeed required for *temurah*'s effect. But if a minor can perform *temurah*, the relationship between sin and the act of *temurah* remains genuinely unresolved.

## Non-Jew Sanctity

### Mukdashei Akum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מוּקְדְּשֵׁי גּוֹיִם לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. לֹא נֶהֱנִין — מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין — מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited on the previous *amud*: With regard to animals **consecrated by gentiles,** one **may not derive benefit** *ab initio*, **but** if one benefited from them after the fact, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property. The Gemara explains: The *halakha* that one **may not benefit** from them *ab initio* is **by rabbinic law. And** that *halakha* that if one benefited from them after the fact **he is not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property is **by Torah law.**

Having been drawn into the subject by the discussion of the non-Jew's *korban*, the *gemara* now turns to a sustained analysis of *mukdashei ovdei kochavim* — animals sanctified as *korbanot* by non-Jews. The *braita* states: *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit from such animals, yet one does not transgress the biblical prohibition of *me'ilah*. This captures the intermediate status of these *korbanot*: they possess genuine *kedusha* yet at a diminished level, such that while benefit is rabbinically prohibited, the biblical *me'ilah* violation is not triggered.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִּמְעֹל מַעַל וְחָטְאָה בִּשְׁגָגָה״, וְיָלְפִינַן ״חֵטְא״ ״חֵטְא״ מִתְּרוּמָה, וּבִתְרוּמָה כְּתִיב: ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְלֹא גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that one is not liable for misuse? **As it is written** with regard to misuse of consecrated property: **"If anyone commit a trespass, and sin through error,** in the holy items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). **And we derive** a verbal analogy **from** the use of the word **"sin"** in this verse and the word **"sin"** used in the context of ***teruma*:** "They shall therefore keep My charge, lest they bear sin for it" (Leviticus 22:9). **And with regard to *teruma* it is written** explicitly: "Even of all the hallowed items of **the children of Israel"** (Numbers 18:8), from which one can infer: **But not** of **gentiles.** The *halakhot* of misuse as well, therefore, do not apply to animals consecrated by gentiles.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — how do we derive that there is no *me'ilah* for a non-Jew's *korban*? The verse states: *nefesh ki tim'ol ma'al vechata'ah bishgagah* — if a *nefesh* transgresses *me'ilah* in error. From the word *nefesh*, a *gezeirah shavah* is made to *terumah*, where the verse uses the same language of sinning, and in the context of *terumah* it is written *bnei Yisrael*. The *gemara* concludes: the limitation to *bnei Yisrael* applies equally here, meaning only things sanctified by Jews carry the full *me'ilah* liability. A non-Jew can sanctify a *korban* — this is indeed a significant *chiddush* — but the *kedusha* of that *korban* does not carry the same set of legal consequences.

### Temurah Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר״, וּכְתִיב בְּרֵישׁ עִנְיָן: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר אִישׁ כִּי יַפְלִיא נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** a gentile **cannot render** a non-sacred animal **a substitute** for one he has consecrated. The Gemara cites the source for this *halakha*: **As it is written** with regard to substitutions: **"He shall not exchange it, nor substitute** it" (Leviticus 27:10), **and it is written at the beginning of the matter: "Speak to the children of Israel, and say to them: When a man shall clearly utter a vow** of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2). Since the passage specifically addresses the children of Israel, the *halakhot* of substitution apply only to them, not to gentiles.

In this same context, the *braita* states: *ve'ein osin temurah* — one cannot perform *temurah* on animals sanctified by a non-Jew. The derivation: the prohibition *lo yachlifenu velo yamir oto* appears at the end of a *parsha* that opens *Daber el bnei Yisrael*. Since the *parsha* of *temurah* is contextualized within a framework addressed to *bnei Yisrael*, the same limitation applies — only *bnei Yisrael*, not non-Jews.

## Non-Jew Sanctity (II)

### Two Drashot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאִיתַּקַּשָׁא תְּמוּרַת בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, וּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעֲשֵׂר דָּגָן. וְגַבֵּי מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן כְּתִיב: ״(כֹּל) [כִּי אֶת] מַעְשַׂר בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ לַה׳... בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — וְלֹא גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **another version** of the source for this *halakha*: The *baraita* states: **And** a gentile **cannot render** a non-sacred animal **a substitute** for one he has consecrated. **What is the reason?** The reason is **that** the **substitution of** consecrated **animals is juxtaposed with the animal tithe** (see Leviticus 27:32), **and the animal tithe** is juxtaposed **with the grain tithe. And with regard to the grain tithe it is written: "For the tithe of the children of Israel, which they set apart as a gift to the Lord,** I have given to the Levites for an inheritance" (Numbers 18:24), indicating that the *halakhot* of the grain tithe apply only to the **children of Israel, but not** to **gentiles.** From the juxtapositions, one derives that the same holds for substitution.

A second derivation for the exclusion of non-Jews from *temurah* is offered: *de'itkesh temurah behemah le'ma'aser behemah* — *temurah* is *hekesh*'d to *ma'aser behemah*. The Torah discusses *temurah* in the general case and then specifically in the context of *ma'aser behemah*, as both passages use the same language of *lo yachlifenu velo yamirenu*. *Ma'aser behemah* is in turn linked to *ma'aser dagan*, and by *ma'aser dagan* it is written *ki et ma'asar bnei Yisrael asher yarimu l'Hashem* — specifically *bnei Yisrael*.

The two derivations address, in effect, different aspects of the exclusion. The first *drasha* — from the opening of the *parsha* — excludes the *person* of the non-Jew: a non-Jew's act of *temurah* has no legal force. The second *drasha* — through the chain of *hekesh* — focuses on the *kedusha* of the animal itself: an animal whose sanctification derives from a non-Jew does not possess the category of *kedusha* upon which *temurah* can operate. This distinction becomes practically relevant in the hybrid case discussed earlier: when a non-Jew sanctifies an animal for a Jew, the person of the non-Jew is no longer the operative party, but the *kedusha* imparted by the non-Jew may still be of the lesser type. That is the precise question that was left as *teiku*.

### Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מֵבִיא (עֲלֵיהֶן) נְסָכִים, אֲבָל קׇרְבָּנוֹ טָעוּן נְסָכִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶזְרָח״ — אֶזְרָח מֵבִיא נְסָכִים וְאֵין הַגּוֹי מֵבִיא נְסָכִים. יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא עוֹלָתוֹ טְעוּנָה נְסָכִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כָּכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And one does not bring libations** for a gentile as an independent offering, **but his offering requires libations.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara explains: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to libations: "All that are **homeborn** shall do these after this manner, in presenting an offering made by fire, of a sweet savor to the Lord" (Numbers 15:13). One who is **homeborn,** i.e., a Jew, **brings libations, but a gentile does not bring libations.** One **might** have thought that a gentile's **burnt offering should not require libations;** therefore, **the verse states: "So** shall it be done for each bull, or for each ram, or for each of the lambs, or of the goats" (Numbers 15:10–11). This teaches that every burnt offering, even that of a gentile, requires libations.

The *braita* further states: *ve'ein mevi'in nesachim aleihem* — one does not bring *nesachim* with the *korbanot* of non-Jews. Yet: *vekorbano ta'un nesachim divrei Rabbi Shimon* — his *korban* does require *nesachim*. The apparent tension is resolved as follows: the non-Jew himself cannot be the provider of the *nesachim*; they come instead from the *tzibbur*. Rashi, citing another *gemara*, interprets this differently and removes the bracketed word *aleihem*, understanding that a non-Jew can in fact bring *nesachim* for his own *korban*, but unlike a Jew's *nesachim* which may come days after the *korban*, a non-Jew's *nesachim* must accompany the *korban* directly. The derivation: *debar el bnei Yisrael* — the verse introducing *nesachim* begins with *ezrach*, a citizen, meaning a Jew: *mevi nesachim ve'ein oved kochavim mevi nesachim*. Yet the *korban* itself requires *nesachim*, derived from *kacha ta'asu* — so shall you do.

### Rabbi Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: רוֹאֶה אֲנִי בְּכוּלָּן לְהַחְמִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״לַה׳״ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yosei said: I see** the logic of the opinion that **in all of** these cases it is correct **to be stringent** about the offerings of gentiles. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason? With regard to** the offering of a gentile, **it is written:** "Whosoever he be of the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel, that brings his offering, whether it be any of their vows, or any of their gift offerings, which are brought **to the Lord** for a burnt offering" (Leviticus 22:18). The verse equates the offerings of gentiles, "the strangers in Israel," with those of Jews, and indicates that the consecrated status of the former is identical to that of the latter.

Rabbi Yosi takes a stringent position: *ro'eh ani bechulah lehachmir* — he would apply full *kedusha* status to all *korbanot* of non-Jews, including *me'ilah*, *pigul*, *notar*, *tumah*, and *temurah*. His reasoning: the verse from which we derive that a non-Jew can sanctify a *korban* — *ish ish mipnei Yisrael asher yakriv korbano lashem* — explicitly concludes with *lashem*, dedicated to God. The dedication is to God with full force, and therefore carries full *kedusha* consequences.

### Types of Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּכִי גָּמְרִינַן מְעִילָה ״חֵטְא״ ״חֵטְא״ מִתְּרוּמָה, דֻּומְיָא דִּתְרוּמָה (דקדוש) [דִּקְדוֹשָׁה] קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל בקדשי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת דִּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to** animals **consecrated for the altar, but with regard to** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** one who benefits **from them** is liable for **misusing** consecrated property. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason?** The reason is **that when we learn** that items consecrated by gentiles are not subject to liability for **misuse** by the verbal analogy between the word **sin** and another instance of the word **sin from** the context of ***teruma*,** this applies only to consecrated property that is **similar to *teruma*, which is consecrated** with **inherent sanctity. But with regard to** an item that has the **sanctity of the Temple maintenance, whose sanctity** inheres in its **value,** as it will ultimately be sold and the proceeds used for Temple maintenance, the analogy does **not** apply.

Even the first opinion, which grants lesser status to a non-Jew's *korban*, limits that lesser status to *kodshei mizbe'ach* — animals brought on the altar as acts of worship. *Aval bekodshei bedek habayit* — if a non-Jew simply donates money or property to the *Beit HaMikdash*, that carries full *kedusha* and full *me'ilah* liability. How do we know? Because the derivation that limited *me'ilah* was based on a *gezeirah shavah* to *terumah*, and *terumah* is *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic *kedusha*, the kind that defines the identity of the object. *Kodshei bedek habayit*, by contrast, is *kedushat damim* — a monetary *kedusha*, a property transfer. The limitation derived from *terumah* applies only when the sanctification is of the *terumah*-type — intrinsic and identity-defining — not when it is a straightforward property donation to the Temple treasury.

## Lav Shein Bo Maase

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: כׇּל ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה״ בַּתּוֹרָה, עָשָׂה (בָּהּ) מַעֲשֶׂה — חַיָּיב, לֹא עָשָׂה (בָּהּ) מַעֲשֶׂה — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who effects substitution receives lashes. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **any prohibition in the Torah,** if one **performed an action** to transgress **it,** he is **liable** to receive lashes. But if he **did not perform an action** to transgress **it,** he is **exempt** from receiving lashes.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *kol lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah, asah bo ma'aseh chayav, lo asah bo ma'aseh patur* — all negative prohibitions in the Torah: if one performed an act in violating them, one is liable to lashes; if no act was performed, one is exempt. This is the general principle of *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh*, and it is well established. The *gemara* now introduces it because the *masechet* is about to address one of the unusual features of our *mishnah*: the ruling that one who performs *temurah* incurs lashes — despite the fact that *temurah* consists entirely of a verbal act.

### Temurah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְלָלָא הוּא דְּלָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה פָּטוּר? וַהֲרֵי מֵימֵר, דְּלָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא, וְלָקֵי, דִּתְנַן: ״לֹא שֶׁהוּא רַשַּׁאי לְהָמִיר, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם הֵמִיר מוּמָר, וְסוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** it an established **principle that** one who transgresses **a prohibition that does not involve an action** is **exempt? But there is** the prohibition of **substitution, which is a prohibition that does not involve an action, and** one is **flogged** for transgressing it, **as we learned** in the mishna: That is **not** to say **that it is permitted for** a person **to effect substitution; rather,** it means **that if one substituted** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated animal, **the substitution takes effect, and** the one who substituted the non-sacred animal **incurs the forty** lashes.

Is the principle of Rav truly absolute? The *gemara* challenges immediately: *ve-harei meimar* — but consider *temurah*! It is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — there is no physical act — yet our *mishnah* says *lo she-hu rashai lehamir, ela she'im hemir mumar, vesoveg et ha-arba'im* — you are not permitted to perform *temurah*, but if you did, it takes effect, and you receive forty lashes. That is a case of a purely verbal transgression resulting in lashes.

### Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה — לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav** could have **said to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna written? **It is** in accordance with that opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said:** With regard to **a prohibition that does not involve an action, one is flogged for** transgressing **it.** The Rabbis, however, disagree.

Rav's response: *hamani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — our *mishnah* follows the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav* — that even without a physical act, lashes apply. The inference to Rabbi Yehuda's position comes from a *sugya* elsewhere concerning *notar*: two *tannaim* each exempt *notar* from lashes but for different reasons. Rabbi Yehuda's reason is *hatra'at safek* — the warning is doubtful, because when one is warned not to leave meat over, the transgression does not occur until hours later. Rabbi Yehuda does not cite the absence of an act as the reason for exemption, which implies that for him, absence of an act is not, by itself, a sufficient ground for exemption. Hence Rabbi Yehuda would agree that a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* can still carry lashes.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לְמַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? וְהָא אוֹקֵימְתַּאּ לְרֵישָׁא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּקָתָנֵי ״הַכֹּל מְמִירִין״, ״הַכֹּל״ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי יוֹרֵשׁ, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! הַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָותֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא — דְּלָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא — דְּאִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ, יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ מֵמִיר, וְתַנָּא דִידַן סָבַר: יוֹרֵשׁ סוֹמֵךְ, יוֹרֵשׁ מֵמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **But can you establish the mishna** as being **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? But we established the first clause** of the mishna **as not** being **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it teaches: Everyone substitutes** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated animal, and the Gemara added (2a): **What** does the expansive term **everyone** serve **to include?** It serves **to include an inheritor, and** accordingly, this mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that an inheritor cannot effect substitution. The Gemara answers: The ***tanna*** of **this** mishna **holds in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda **with regard to one** matter, **that one is flogged for** transgressing **a prohibition that does not involve an action, and disagrees with him with regard to one** other matter, **as Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **an inheritor does not place** his **hands** on the head of the offering when he sacrifices it, and **an inheritor** is **not** able to **substitute** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated one that he inherited; **and the *tanna* of our** mishna **holds** that **an inheritor places** his **hands** on the head of the offering when he sacrifices it, and **an inheritor** is able to **substitute** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated one that he inherited.

But this raises a difficulty: was not the beginning of our *mishnah* — the passage about heirs and who can perform *temurah* — established as *not* following Rabbi Yehuda? Rabbi Yehuda holds that an heir cannot perform *smicha* and cannot perform *temurah*, while our *mishnah* includes the heir as someone who can. How then can one part of the *mishnah* follow Rabbi Yehuda while another contradicts him?

The *gemara* resolves this: *hai tanna savar lei k'vosei b'chada u-falig lei b'chata* — the author of our *mishnah* agrees with Rabbi Yehuda on one point (*lav she'ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav* — lashes apply even without an act) and disagrees with him on another (the heir's status). This is entirely standard: a *tanna* may selectively adopt another *tanna*'s ruling on one issue without accepting the full range of that *tanna*'s positions.

## Exceptions

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: כׇּל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה — לוֹקֶה, לֹא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה — פָּטוּר, חוּץ מִנִּשְׁבָּע, וּמֵימֵר, וּמְקַלֵּל חֲבֵירוֹ בַּשֵּׁם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה — חַיָּיב. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמְרוּ: אַף הַמַּקְדִּים תְּרוּמָה לְבִיכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Idi bar Avin says** that **Rav Amram says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili:** With regard to **every prohibition in the Torah,** if one **performed an action** to transgress **it,** he is **flogged.** But if he **did not perform an action** to transgress **it,** he is **exempt** from lashes, **except for one who takes an oath, and one who effects substitution, and one who curses another using the name** of God. For these three transgressions, **even though he did not perform an action,** he is **liable** to receive lashes. **They said in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: Also one who** separates ***teruma* prior to** designating the **first fruits** is flogged, even though *teruma* can be separated by intention alone, without an action.

Rav Idi bar Avin transmitted in the name of Rav Yitzchak, who transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili: *kol lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah, asa bo ma'aseh lokei, lo asa bo ma'aseh patur* — the general principle is confirmed. But *chutz* — three exceptions: *min ha-nishba* — one who takes a false oath, *u-memir* — one who performs *temurah*, and *u-mekalel chaveiro ba-shem* — one who curses another with God's name. In all three cases, lashes apply despite the absence of a physical act.

Tosafot notes that there are arguably additional exceptions — *eidim zomemim* (false witnesses) and *motzi shem ra* (defamatory accusation) — but these are qualitatively different. There, while the transgression consisted of words, those words were aimed at real-world consequences: a person could have been executed on the basis of the testimony or the accusation. The speech was, in effect, a form of action with direct this-worldly impact. The three cases enumerated by Rabbi Yosi HaGlili are the more striking exceptions precisely because the words had no such direct real-world consequence: one performed *temurah* verbally, one took a vain oath, one uttered a curse. These are genuinely the hard cases for the principle.

### Shvuat Shav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבָּע מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אָמַר קְרָא ״כִּי לֹא יְנַקֶּה ה׳ אֵת אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׂא אֶת שְׁמוֹ לַשָּׁוְא״, בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה אֵין מְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ, אֲבָל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וּמְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the sources for these exceptions. **From where do we** derive that **one who takes an oath** using the name of God is flogged? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Meir** that **the verse states: "For the Lord will not hold him guiltless that takes His name in vain"** (Exodus 20:7). One can infer: **The higher,** i.e., heavenly, **court does not hold him guiltless,** as repentance alone will not atone for the sin, **but the lower,** i.e., earthly, **court flogs him,** thereby allowing him to atone, **and absolves him** of guilt.

The first exception: a false oath of *shvuat shav*. This is an oath that is utterly in vain — classically, swearing to something universally known to be true (or false). No purpose is served; the words themselves constitute the offense.

### Shvua Derivation

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir: *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa shemo lashav* — God will not cleanse one who takes His name in vain. From this the *gemara* derives a crucial structure.

### Courts & Kaparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְאֵימָא לָא תִּיהְוֵי לֵיהּ נְקִיּוּת כְּלָל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״לֹא יִנָּקֶה״ וְלִישְׁתּוֹק, ״ה׳״ לְמָה לִי? בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה הוּא דְּאֵין מְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ, אֲבָל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה מַלְקִין וּמְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But** why not **say** that the verse means **he cannot be absolved** of guilt **at all?** Abaye **said to him: If so, let the verse write** only: **"Will not hold him guiltless," and be silent. Why do I** need the verse to specify: "For **the Lord** will not hold him guiltless"? This serves to teach that **it is the higher court that will not hold him guiltless, but the lower court flogs** him **and absolves him** of guilt.

*Beit Din shel ma'alah ein menakin oto* — the heavenly court will not cleanse such a person. *Aval beit din shel matah malkin u-menakin oto* — but the earthly court can lash this person and thereby cleanse him. This reflects a deep understanding in *Chazal* that lashes are not merely punitive but serve as *kaparah* — they atone and restore the transgressor to a clean slate. The weight of *shvuat shav* is so great — which is why Jewish practice has historically avoided even truthful oaths — that without earthly intervention, the heavenly court will not forgive. But when *beit din* acts, the lashes function as a mechanism of atonement that restores the person's standing.

Rav Pappa asked: perhaps we should simply read *lo yenakeh* as meaning the person is never cleansed at all? The *gemara* responds: if so, the verse should have written simply *lo yenakeh* and stopped there. The explicit mention of *Hashem* indicates that *God*, on His own, will not cleanse — but the human court, acting through its authority, can do so.

### Shvuat Sheker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר מְנָלַן? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: ״לַשָּׁוְא״ ״לַשָּׁוְא״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים, אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִשְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא — תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לִשְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר, דְּלוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that one who takes **an oath in vain** using the name of God is flogged. **From where do we** derive that the same holds for **a false oath?** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yoḥanan himself says** the derivation: The verse states with regard to an oath taken in vain: "You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain; for the Lord will not hold him guiltless that takes His name in vain" (Exodus 20:7). It says: **"In vain…in vain," twice** in this verse. **If** the second instance of the term **is not** necessary for the **matter of an oath** taken **in vain,** which is derived from the first instance, **apply it** to the **matter of a false oath,** to teach **that** one who takes such an oath **is flogged.**

The *gemara* turns to *shvuat sheker* — a false oath about something that is not obviously vain. How do we know that *shvuat sheker* also incurs lashes? Rabbi Yochanan, now in his own name, notes that the verse repeats *lashav* twice: once in *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* and once in *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa shemo lashav*. *Im eino inyan lishvuat shav* — since one repetition suffices for the case of a vain oath, the second comes to teach us: *tnehu inyan lishvuat sheker* — apply it to a false oath as well, *delokei* — that lashes apply there too.

### Shvuat Sheker (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא דַּאֲמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ וַאֲכַל — הָתָם (הוּא) מַעֲשֵׂה עֲבַד, וְאֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁאוֹכַל״ וְלָא אֲכַל — הָהוּא מִי לָקֵי? וְהָאִיתְּמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאוֹכַל כִּכָּר זֶה הַיּוֹם״ וְעָבַר הַיּוֹם וְלֹא אָכַל — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמְרִי: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu objects to this** explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **What are the circumstances** of the **false oath** under discussion? **If we say that** the one taking the oath **said:** I take an oath **that I will not eat** a certain item, **and he** then **ate** that item; **there he performed an action** to violate his oath by eating the item, and the prohibition would then involve an action. **And** if it was **rather** a case **where he said:** I take an oath **that I will eat** a certain item, **and he did not eat** that item; **is that** individual **flogged? But wasn't it stated:** If one says: I take **an oath that I will eat this loaf today, and the day passed and he had not eaten** it, **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** both **say: He is not flogged.**

But what type of *shvuat sheker* does this refer to? Rav Pappa presses the question. If the false oath concerns the future — "I swear I will not eat" — and then one eats, the act of eating is itself the violation: *hatam hama'aseh avid* — there, an act was performed. That is not a case of *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* at all; the act of eating is the transgression of the *shvuah*. What about the other direction: one swears "I will eat this loaf today," and then does not eat it? A *gemara* in *Shvuot* records that both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree no lashes apply here, but for different reasons: Rabbi Yochanan because it is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — the violation was through inaction — and Reish Lakish because it is *hatra'at safek* — the warning is doubtful, since when warned, one has hours remaining before the transgression occurs, so the warning is not *samuch l'ma'aseh*, not juxtaposed to the act itself. For a valid *hatra'ah*, the warning must immediately frame the person's awareness that they are sinning now; a warning given long before the moment of transgression does not achieve that psychological immediacy.

### Future Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: תְּהֵא בְּ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Abbahu says: Let** the case **be where** one says: I take an oath that **I ate** a certain item, and in fact he did not eat it; **or** he said: I take an oath that **I did not eat** a certain item, and in fact he did eat it. In these cases, he transgresses the prohibition by speech alone, without an action.

So where do lashes apply for *shvuat sheker*? Rav Abahu answers: *b'achalti v'lo achalti* — when one swears about the past. "I ate" or "I did not eat." There, the focus is not on any future performance but on the speech act itself — the words of the oath are where the wrong occurs, just as in *shvuat shav*. And just as *shvuat shav* incurs lashes because the transgression inheres in the act of speaking, so too a *shvuat sheker* that is past-oriented, where the lie occurs at the moment of utterance, incurs lashes.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא דְּקָא מְרַבֵּה ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ מֵ״אוֹכַל״ וְלֹא אָכַל? אָמַר רָבָא: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה שֶׁקֶר דּוֹמֶה לְשָׁוְא, מָה שָׁוְא לְשֶׁעָבַר, אַף שֶׁקֶר לְשֶׁעָבַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about an oath concerning the past, **that** the verse **includes** one who takes an oath of: **I ate, or: I did not eat,** as liable to be flogged, more **than** one who takes an oath of: **I will eat, and** then **did not eat?** In both cases, there is no action performed. **Rava says: The Torah explicitly includes** a case of **a false** oath that is **similar to** a case of an oath taken **in vain: Just as** an oath taken **in vain** refers **to the past, so too,** the **false** oath for which one is flogged must pertain **to the past.**

A *braita* then threatens to complicate matters. It describes *shvuat bituy* — a future-oriented oath of "I will not eat this loaf," which one then violates by eating — as the paradigmatic case for which one incurs lashes and, if violated inadvertently, brings a sliding-scale *korban*. By contrast, *shvuat shav* — where the violation is in the utterance itself — incurs lashes but the unintentional case is exempt from a *korban*. The *gemara* asks: does each of these formulations, by saying "this is the case," implicitly exclude the past-oriented *shvuat sheker* from lashes? If so, this would contradict our principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״, וַאֲכָלָהּ — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. וְזוֹהִי שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי, שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד. זוֹהִי לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְלֹא ״אָכַלְתִּי״, דְּלָא לָקֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised an objection to Rabbi Abbahu** from a mishna (*Shevuot* 27b): If one says: I take **an oath that I will not eat this loaf;** I take **an oath that I will not eat it;** I take **an oath that I will not eat it, and he** then **ate it, he is liable** for **only one** violation. **And this is** the **oath of an utterance for which one is liable** to receive **lashes for its intentional** violation, **and** to bring **a sliding-scale offering for its unwitting** violation. Rabbi Yirmeya asks: When the mishna uses the limiting term: **This is** the oath, **what** does it serve **to exclude?** Is it **not to exclude** a case where one said: I take an oath that **I ate** a certain item, when in fact he did not eat it, **or:** I take an oath that **I did not eat** a certain item, when he did eat it, to teach **that** he is **not flogged** in those cases? This would contradict the explanation of Rabbi Abbahu.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty. The phrases *zo hi* — "this is the one" — are not intended to contrast with the past-oriented case at all. Rather, *mishum deka'ei lehaba memait lehaba* — since the *braita* is discussing future-oriented oaths throughout, its contrasts are internal to the future-oriented category. The contrast is between a future oath violated by an act (one swore not to eat and then ate — lashes apply) and a future oath violated by inaction (one swore to eat and then did not — no lashes). The past-oriented case is simply not in view; there is no implication whatsoever that it is exempt. With that resolved, the principle stands: a *shvuat sheker* about the past incurs lashes, because the speech act of swearing falsely is itself the violation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, זוֹ הִיא דְּעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, אֲבָל ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ לָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן. וּמַנִּי? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, אֲבָל מִילְקָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** in both cases one is flogged for violating the prohibition intentionally. Rather, the phrase means: **This is** the oath **for which** one **brings an offering for its unwitting** violation, **but** in a case where one unwittingly said: **I ate** a certain item, when he in fact did not eat it, **or: I did not** eat a certain item, when he did eat it, **he does not bring an offering. And whose** opinion does this mishna represent? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who said:** One is **liable** to bring an offering **only for** an oath that refers to **the future, but** one is **flogged** even for an oath that pertains to the past.

### Speech as Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵימֵר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְתַנָּא: לָא תִּתְנִי ״וּמֵימֵר״, מִשּׁוּם דִּבְדִיבּוּרוֹ עָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** § In listing those who incur the punishment of lashes even though they did not perform an action in their transgression, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili was cited above as including: **And one who effects substitution.** The Gemara adds: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to the *tanna*** who would recite the *baraitot* in his academy: **Do not teach: And one who effects substitution,** in this regard, **because** one who effects substitution **performs an action** of consecration **by his speech.**

This brings into relief the underlying principle that unifies all three exceptions: in each case, speech functions as a sufficient act. For *shvuat shav* and past-oriented *shvuat sheker*, the words of the oath are themselves the transgression — the speech act is where the wrong occurs. For *temurah*, similarly, the verbal declaration of substitution is the transgression. These are not cases of pure inaction; they are cases where the action is the speech itself. That is what distinguishes them from a future-oriented oath violated by inaction, where the expected act never occurred and thus there is no actionable performance to anchor the lashes. Where the speech is the act — where the wrong inheres in the words at the moment of their utterance — lashes apply even without any accompanying physical act.
