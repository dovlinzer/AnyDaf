# Gittin 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechubar & Ketzitzah

### Previous Materials

The previous discussion established the range of materials on which a *get* may be written, culminating in the case of a *get* written on the horn of a cow or the arm of a slave. In the latter case, one cannot cut off the arm — one must give the entire slave. Yet the analysis raised a subtler point: even for the horn, which could in principle be cut off, the act of cutting that intervenes between the writing and the giving of the *get* seems to constitute a problem — *mechusar ketzitzah*, literally "lacking a cutting," meaning that the required act of separation had not yet taken place at the time of writing.

*Tosafot* engaged with the question of what exactly counts as an invalidating act of cutting. Does trimming a margin from a larger sheet of paper constitute *ketzitzah*? *Tosafot* considers this trivial and not problematic. But when the *get* occupies a small piece of a very large sheet and must be cut away, *Tosafot* entertains a genuine debate about whether that separation invalidates. The more fundamental conclusion of *Tosafot* is that the only act of cutting that truly invalidates is one that is transformative in nature — separating something that was growing (plucking a leaf from a tree on which the *get* was written) or cutting a horn from an animal. These acts are qualitatively different from simple trimming. With this framing in mind, we turn to the *mishna* on 22b.

### Mishna: Mechubar

*Ein kotvin bimchubar lekarka* — one may not write a *get* on something that is attached to the ground. If one nevertheless did write it on something *mechubar*, and then uprooted it, signed it, and gave it to her — *kasher*, it is valid.

This requires explanation. The *gemara* will grapple with the precise logic, but the surface reading of the *mishna* already implies a certain structure: *lechatchila* one does not write on *mechubar*, presumably because one may come to sign it while still attached; *bedi'avad*, if one did write it while attached, as long as one signed it only after it was separated, the *get* is valid.

The question of why *mechubar* is invalid at all is never explicitly resolved by the *gemara*, though it is taken for granted. One might have thought that since we established yesterday that a *get* written on the hand of a slave can be given by transferring possession of the slave, the same logic could apply here — write a *get* on the bark of a tree, give the woman the deed to the property, and she simultaneously acquires the *get* through her acquisition of the land. Yet that analogy seems to fail. The *Rambam* understands the problem in terms of *mechusar ketzitzah* — the *get* requires separation — but that itself begs the question of why one cannot simply transfer the property. The *gemara* seems to assume, without spelling it out fully, that a *get* must ultimately be something movable — *metaltelin* in character — and cannot remain permanently *mechubar*.

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

The *mishna* continues: *Rabbi Yehuda posel ad sheyehei ketivato vechatimato bitalush* — Rabbi Yehuda invalidates unless both the writing and the signing occurred after separation.

The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda maps onto the foundational dispute about what gives a *get* its legal force. Rabbi Meir holds *eidei chatima kartei* — it is the signatures that constitute the *get*, and the *pasuk* of *vekatav* is interpreted as pointing to the moment of signing. If so, all the requirements of the *get* attach to the signing. As long as the signing occurred when the object was no longer *mechubar*, the *get* is valid, even if the writing took place while it was still attached. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds *eidei mesira kartei* — the witnesses present at the moment of handover are what give the *get* its legal force, not the signatures. For him, the *Torah*'s emphasis on *ketivah* falls on the act of writing itself. The writing must therefore occur when the object is *talush*, already separated. Signing after separation is insufficient if the writing took place while still attached.

### R. Y. ben Beteira

The *mishna* adds a third opinion: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira omer ein kotvin lo al niyar machuk velo al diftera* — one may not write a *get* on erased paper or on *diftera*, because such surfaces *yakhol lehizdayef*, they are susceptible to forgery.

The concern is precise. If a witness signs on a surface that already shows erasure, and the top portion of the document is subsequently altered — the original content erased and something new written in its place — the forgery is undetectable. The signature appears on the same kind of surface as the text, so everything looks consistent. Contrast this with a clean document whose top portion has been erased: the erasure below a clean signature would be immediately suspicious. Here, when the signatures themselves appear on an erasable surface, there is no way to know whether what is above them is original.

*Vechachamim machshirin* — and the *Chachamim* permit it. This is striking. How can one permit writing a *get* on a forgeable surface? The *gemara* will explain that the *Chachamim* here are those who hold *eidei mesira kartei*. If the witnesses who are physically present at the handing over of the *get* are what validates it, then their direct observation at the moment of delivery provides sufficient security. Even if the document were subsequently forged, those witnesses saw what was actually there at the time. The *gemara* will unpack this further.

### Tofes vs. Toref

The *gemara* opens its analysis with a statement of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel*: *vehu sheshiyar makom haToref* — the *mishna*'s ruling that the *get* is valid if signed after separation applies only when the writer left blank the place of the *toref*, the essential content.

This introduces the critical distinction between *tofes* and *toref*. The *tofes* is the boilerplate — the standard formulaic language that appears in every *get*. The *toref* is the individualized essential content: the names of the parties, the date, the place, and the essential declaration of divorce (*harei at muteret lechol adam*). *Rashi* defines the *toref* as *giluyav shel shetar* — the name of the man, the name of the woman, and the time. A brief look at the text of the *get* as preserved in the *Rif* (45b of the *dapei harif*) illustrates the full scope of what this entails: the date, the place, the full names and alternate names of both parties, the voluntary declaration, the threefold formula of release, the permission to remarry any man, and the closing declaration *veharei at muteret lechol adam kedas Moshe veYisrael*. It is these elements — not the connecting formulaic language — that constitute the *toref*.

The *gemara* thus reinterprets the *mishna*: when the *mishna* says the *get* is valid if signed after separation, it does not simply mean that one wrote the entire *get* while it was attached and then merely signed it after separation. Rather, *vehu sheshiyar makom haToref* — one left the essential parts blank while it was attached, and filled them in only after separation. The same statement is then attributed by the *gemara* to *Rav Elazar* in the name of *Rabbi Oshaya* and to *Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*. The *gemara* then identifies this position as that of *Rabbi Elazar*, who holds *eidei mesira kartei*, meaning that since it is the writing — not the signing — that is the determinative act, the entire *toref* must be written after separation.

### R. Yehuda vs. R. Lakish

*Resh Lakish* disputes this reading. He insists: *chatamo shaninu* — the *mishna* says "signed it," and one cannot reinterpret the word *chatamo* to mean "filled in the essential parts." The *mishna* means what it says: it was signed after separation. According to *Resh Lakish*, the *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir, who holds *eidei chatima kartei*. The entire *get* — including the *toref* — could have been written while still attached, because all the requirements cluster around the moment of signing, not writing. The signing is what transforms the document into a *get*, and as long as that signing occurs after separation, the *get* is valid *bedi'avad*.

Thus we have two complete readings of the *mishna*. According to the reading attributed to the position of *Rabbi Elazar* (*eidei mesira kartei*): one may write the *tofes* while still attached, but must write the *toref* — including *harei at muteret lechol adam* — only after separation; what the *mishna* calls "signing" is a reference to filling in the essential content. According to *Resh Lakish* and the position of Rabbi Meir (*eidei chatima kartei*): one may write even the *toref* while still attached, and the *mishna* means exactly that signing — the witnesses' signatures — occurred after separation.

### Why Mechubar Invalid

The *gemara* never fully explicates the conceptual basis of the *mechubar* prohibition, but one reading that emerges — symbolically if not legally — is that a *get* must be like *metaltelin*, something mobile and transferable, rather than something permanently rooted in the earth. The *Rambam* frames this in terms of *mechusar ketzitzah*: the *get* lacks the necessary separation. Whatever the precise rationale, the *gemara* takes it as axiomatic that something *mechubar lekarka* cannot serve as a *get*, and the dispute between the *tanna'im* concerns only the question of when — at writing or at signing — the relevant act of separation must have already occurred.

## Atzitz Nakuv

### Pottery vs. Leaf

The *gemara* now applies these principles to a novel case: *katavo al cheres shel atzitz nakuv* — one wrote the *get* on the pottery of a flowerpot that has a hole in the bottom.

The significance of the hole (*nakuv*) requires explanation. A flowerpot with a hole in the bottom is considered *mechubar lekarka* — attached to the ground — even if there is air space between the bottom of the pot and the earth below. The hole creates a halachic connection: we view the dirt in the pot as a continuation of the ground beneath, and therefore the plant and its container are treated as if anchored to the earth, even though they are physically movable. A flowerpot without a hole (*atzitz she'eino nakuv*), by contrast, is treated as *metaltelin*.

If one wrote the *get* on the pottery of such an *atzitz nakuv*, the *gemara* rules it is *kasher* — because *d'shakil lei veyahiv lei nihala*, one can simply pick up the pot and give it to the woman. The act of giving the pot itself, with the *get* written on it, constitutes the delivery.

But if one wrote the *get* on a leaf of the plant growing in the *atzitz nakuv*, a dispute arises.

### Abaye vs. Rava

*Abaye amar kasher* — Abaye rules it valid, for the same reason: one picks up the pot, leaf and all, and hands it to the woman. *Rava amar pasul* — Rava rules it invalid, *gezeirah shema yiktom*, out of concern that one will pluck the leaf off. The fear is that the husband, wishing to give the *get*, will detach the leaf from the plant — thereby severing something *mechubar* from its living source — which constitutes the problematic *ketzitzah* that invalidates the *get*.

The disparity between the leaf and the pottery is noteworthy. We are not similarly afraid that the husband will smash the pottery. This asymmetry is presumably because plucking a leaf is far simpler and more natural an act than breaking a clay pot.

### Tosafot Questions

*Tosafot* uses this case as a platform to examine a series of fundamental questions about the nature of *ketzitzah* and *mechubar* status.

The first question concerns what type of cutting invalidates a *get*. One reading of the *gemara*'s structure suggests that only an act that transitions something from *mechubar* to *lo mechubar* — severing something living from its source — constitutes the invalidating *ketzitzah*. Plucking a leaf does exactly that: it was *mechubar* and is now *talush*. Breaking off a piece of pottery, by contrast, is analogous to trimming a margin off a larger sheet of paper — it changes the shape of the *metaltelin* but does not sever a *mechubar* connection. Under this reading, breaking the pot would not invalidate the *get*.

The second and more intricate question is this: when the husband delivers the pot to the woman, does it still have to be *lo mechubar* at the moment of delivery? After all, the *get* is written on a *mechubar* object. Can a *mechubar* object be given as a *get*? *Tosafot* assumes that it must be made *lo mechubar* at the time of delivery. But here the analysis becomes subtle. If one simply lifts the pot and places it on a table — thereby eliminating the halachic connection to the ground, since there is no longer open air space between the hole and the dirt — the pot becomes *lo mechubar* without any physical alteration to the *get* itself. According to *Tosafot*, this is permissible and valid. What is impermissible is plucking the leaf: that act also makes the *get* *lo mechubar*, but it does so by physically severing a living connection, which constitutes an invalidating *ketzitzah*.

The result is a carefully calibrated distinction. Placing the pot on a table: the *get* becomes *lo mechubar*, no physical change to the *get* itself, valid. Plucking the leaf: the *get* becomes *lo mechubar*, but through an act of *ketzitzah* that intervenes between writing and delivery, invalid. The *gemara* itself does not spell out these distinctions explicitly; they are *Tosafot*'s analysis of the implications.

### Kinyan Agav

The *gemara* now pivots from the *gittin* discussion to explore the *kinyan* law of the *atzitz nakuv*. The case: the pot belongs to one person and the seeds — and by implication the soil — belong to another.

If the owner of the seeds wishes to acquire the pot (*machar baal atzitz lebaal zeraim*), he simply performs *meshichah* — drawing the pot toward himself — since the pot is *metaltelin* and that is how *metaltelin* is acquired. *Keivan shemashach kanah* — once he performs *meshichah* on the pot, he acquires it.

The reverse case is more complex. If the owner of the pot wishes to acquire the seeds, he cannot use *meshichah*, because the seeds in a *nakuv* pot are classified as *karka* — real property — even though they are movable. Real property is acquired through *chazakah* (an act of improvement or possession applied to the land), through *kesef* (payment, as *Rashi* notes), or through a deed (*shtar*). The *gemara* rules: *lo kanah ad sheyachzik bazeraim* — he does not acquire until he performs *chazakah* on the seeds themselves, such as by watering them.

This raises the question of how acquiring the *karka* (seeds) also transfers the *metaltelin* (pot). The answer is the principle of *kinyan agav* — piggybacking. The *mishna* in Kiddushin teaches that *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut niknim im nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut* — chattel can be acquired together with real property through the methods of real property acquisition (payment, deed, or *chazakah*). By performing *chazakah* on the seeds (which are *karka*), one simultaneously acquires the pot (*metaltelin*) *agav* — on the back of — the *karka* acquisition.

### Chazaka on Seeds

The full rule as articulated by the *gemara*: if one person owns both the pot and the seeds and sells them together, the buyer acquires both once he performs *chazakah* on the seeds. The *chazakah* on the *karka* (seeds) drags along the *metaltelin* (pot) through *kinyan agav*.

*Tosafot* raises a further nuance: in the standard *kinyan agav* discussed in the first chapter of Kiddushin, when one sells chattel *agav* a piece of land, one must explicitly link them — saying, in effect, "acquire this chattel *agav* this land." But in the present case, where the pot and the seeds are physically integrated — the seeds are literally growing in the pot — *Tosafot* considers whether such an explicit declaration is necessary. The reasoning is that the pot is intrinsically secondary to and dependent on the seeds; the two are seen as essentially a single unit. If so, the *chazakah* on the seeds may draw the pot along automatically, without any explicit *agav* declaration. *Tosafot* leaves this as a *tzarich iyun*.

The practical paradigm underlying *kinyan agav* more broadly may in fact be this very case. The classic image is purchasing a furnished estate: acquiring the field brings the cattle, equipment, and produce with it as a natural extension of the acquisition. The flowerpot case, where the pot literally contains and is integrated with the seeds, may be the foundational model on which the broader rule — even for unconnected chattel — is built.

### Chazaka on Pot Fails

The *gemara* then states the negative rule: *hechzik baatzitz af atzitz lo kanah* — if a buyer who wishes to acquire both pot and seeds performs *chazakah* on the pot rather than on the seeds, she acquires neither the pot nor the seeds.

The logic: *chazakah* is a method of acquiring *karka*, not *metaltelin*. Performing *chazakah* on the pot — say, by painting it — is performing *chazakah* on *metaltelin*, which is a legal nullity. It neither acquires the pot (which as *metaltelin* requires *meshichah*, not *chazakah*) nor the seeds (which require *chazakah* performed on the *karka* itself, not on the container). The *chazakah* was directed at the wrong object and accomplishes nothing. *Ad sheyachzik bazeraim* — one must perform the *chazakah* on the seeds themselves.

## Border Tree Case

### Nikvu vs. Nofu

The *gemara* now turns to a case that occupies it at length: *nikvah ba'aretz venofo bechutz la'aretz* — a flowerpot whose hole (and thus its halachic *mechubar* connection to the ground) is situated in *Eretz Yisrael*, but whose foliage extends over the border into *chutz la'aretz*.

The practical stakes involve whether the fruit is obligated in *terumot uma'asrot*. *Abaye amar batar nikvo azlinan* — Abaye holds that we follow the location of the hole: since the hole is in *Eretz Yisrael*, the pot is halachically rooted there, and the fruit is *chayav* in *terumot uma'asrot*. *Rava amar batar nofo azlinan* — Rava holds that we follow where the foliage actually grows: since the foliage is in *chutz la'aretz*, the fruit is exempt.

The *gemara* clarifies when this dispute applies. *Bed'ashrush kulei alma lo pligi* — if the roots have actually taken hold in the ground of *Eretz Yisrael*, everyone agrees: it is an *Eretz Yisrael* plant, defined by where it is rooted. The dispute pertains only to *bed'lo ashrush* — when the plant has not taken root, meaning it remains genuinely movable. In that case, Rava can argue that since the entire pot could theoretically be moved to the other side of the border, it is the actual location of the foliage and fruit that determines status.

### Baraita: Half/Half

The *gemara* challenges the proposition that when the roots have genuinely taken hold, everyone agrees the plant follows the soil. It cites a *baraita*: *ilan miktzato ba'aretz umiktzato bechutz la'aretz* — a tree partly in *Eretz Yisrael* and partly outside. *Rabbi* says: *tevel vechullin meuravim zeh bazeh* — every fruit is a mixture of *tevel* (obligated) and *chullin* (exempt), because the tree is being nurtured by both types of soil simultaneously. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says: *hagadel bechiyuv chayav, hagadel bepattur pattur* — what grows in *Eretz Yisrael* is *chayav*, and what grows in *chutz la'aretz* is exempt.

The implication seems to be that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* divides the fruit based on where the actual foliage is located — and this is a case of a tree that presumably has taken root. If so, even in a rooted tree, one authority holds that we follow the foliage rather than the soil, which would suggest that the debate between Abaye and Rava is not limited to the *atzitz nakuv* case.

### Foliage Defense

The *gemara* asks: is this *baraita* indeed a case where the foliage alone determines status, irrespective of root location? *Amar leih Abaye* — Abaye responds: perhaps this challenge fails. The *baraita* about the two gardens — *Rabbi Meir omer shel elyon* (the upper gardener's, defined by whose soil it grows from), *Rabbi Yehuda omer shel tachton* (the lower gardener's, defined by whose airspace it occupies) — was about a question of ownership, not about *Eretz Yisrael* status. The ability to destroy the other party's claim (by removing the soil or filling in the airspace) determines possession. That is a different legal question entirely from whether fruit is *chayav* in *ma'asrot*. So the earlier *baraita* does not prove that when a tree is rooted in *Eretz Yisrael* we follow the foliage.

The *gemara* then presses further: *mai lav miktzat nofah ba'aretz umiktzat nofah bechutz la'aretz* — is the *baraita* not a case where half the foliage is on each side, with the roots irrelevant? If so, even in a rooted tree, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* divides by foliage, demonstrating that one can follow the foliage even when roots are present.

### Rock Divides Roots

The *gemara* responds: *lo — miktzat shorashin ba'aretz umiktzat shorashin bechutz la'aretz* — in the *baraita*, it is not just the foliage that is divided; the roots themselves are divided, running partly into *Eretz Yisrael* and partly into *chutz la'aretz*.

But this creates a problem: if the roots are mixed, how can *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* say that one portion of the fruit is purely *chayav* and the other purely *pattur*? The entire tree is drawing nutrients from both soils — every fruit should be a mixture, as *Rabbi* holds. The *gemara* therefore postulates a special case: *demafsik tsunma* — a rock runs along the border underground, physically separating the roots on one side from the roots on the other. The roots in *Eretz Yisrael* go only into *Eretz Yisrael* soil; the roots in *chutz la'aretz* go only into *chutz la'aretz* soil. Underground, they do not intermingle.

### Mixed Nutrients

But even with the rock dividing the underground roots, once the nutrients enter the trunk of the tree, do they not mix? The *gemara* identifies this as the precise point of the dispute between *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. *Mar savar avira mivalbel* — *Rabbi* holds that above ground, in the trunk and branches, all the nutrients merge; every fruit therefore receives a mixture of *Eretz Yisrael* and *chutz la'aretz* nourishment, and every fruit is simultaneously *tevel* and *chullin*. *Umar savar hai lechudei kai vehai lechudei kai* — *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that even above ground, the nutrients from each side continue to feed only their respective side of the tree; the separation maintained underground by the rock persists functionally into the branches and fruit. Consequently, the fruit on the *Eretz Yisrael* side is nourished purely from *Eretz Yisrael* soil, and the fruit on the *chutz la'aretz* side is nourished purely from *chutz la'aretz* soil.

The *gemara*'s resolution thus preserves the principle: when roots are genuinely in the ground, the determination of status tracks where the nutrients originate — i.e., the soil. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s ability to split the fruit depends not on ignoring the roots but on positing that the rock keeps the root systems fully separate, so that each side of the tree is effectively its own independent nutritional unit. The ability to split the fruit by foliage location is therefore not a case of overriding root-defined status; it is a case where the root-defined status itself is already cleanly divided. The *gemara* thereby protects the conclusion that in the *atzitz nakuv* case — where the plant has genuinely taken root in *Eretz Yisrael* — everyone would agree to follow the soil, while when it has not taken root, Rava and Abaye dispute whether we follow the hole or the foliage.

## Forgery & Witnesses

### Three Leathers

The *gemara* now turns to the ruling of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* against writing on forgeable surfaces, and opens with a classification that provides the halachic context for *diftera*. *Amar Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi mishmeih de-Ulla: gimmel orot hen* — there are three categories of leather.

The first is *matza*: untreated leather that has not been salted, treated with flour, or processed with gallnut (*afitz*). Because it is of minimal utility, its halachic significance (*hilchata*) pertains to *hotza'at Shabbat* — the minimum *shiur* of carrying that incurs liability on *Shabbat*. As taught by *Rabbi Shmuel bar Yehuda*, the relevant *shiur* is the amount sufficient to wrap a small weight — the tiny weights used in commerce, wrapped in leather to prevent wear. Abaye specifies this as approximately the smallest weight used in Pumbedita.

The second is *chippa*: leather that has been salted but not treated with flour or gallnut. Its halachic significance also pertains to *hotza'at Shabbat*, with a *shiur* corresponding to the amount needed to make a *kamia* — an amulet wrapper — as per the *mishna*.

The third is *diftera*: leather that has been salted and treated with flour but not with gallnut. It is this partial processing that creates the problematic surface: it can be written on, but subsequent erasure would not be evident, since the gallnut treatment that would "fix" the writing permanently has not been applied. For contrast, fully processed *klaf* — which includes treatment with gallnut — has a *shiur* corresponding to the smallest *parasha* of *tefillin*, reflecting its use for sacred writing.

### R. Y. ben B. Rejects

*Vechachamim machshirin* — the *Chachamim* permit writing a *get* on *diftera*, but *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* forbids it. The concern he raises is precise: the problem is not simply that the text could be altered. The problem is that because the witnesses' signatures appear on the same erasable surface as the text, any alteration to the text would look entirely consistent with the rest of the document. If the signatures appeared on clean parchment and the text above them had been erased and rewritten, the discrepancy would be immediately apparent. But when signatures and text alike appear on *diftera* — a surface where erasure leaves no visible trace — there is no way to detect whether the original text has been replaced.

The specific scenario the *gemara* envisions: a husband writes a *get* on *diftera* with a *tenai* — a condition — in it; the wife then erases the *tenai* and replaces it with neutral text, leaving the signatures intact. Alternatively, one could imagine a more extreme forgery: erasing the entire original document above the signatures and replacing it with a *get*, when the original was an entirely different document such as a loan instrument. The forgery is undetectable precisely because the signatures, the text, and the surface all present identically.

### Chachamim Allow

Why do the *Chachamim* permit it nonetheless? *Amar Rabbi Elazar* — the *gemara* identifies the *Chachamim* as holding *eidei mesira kartei*, the position of Rabbi Elazar. If what validates a *get* is not the signatures themselves but the testimony of the witnesses physically present at the moment of delivery, then the forgeable nature of the writing surface is not fatal. Those witnesses saw the *get* at the time it was handed over; they read it; they know what it said. Even if someone subsequently forged the document, the witnesses' testimony about what they directly observed remains the operative legal validation.

### R. Elazar: Time Limit

The *gemara* then adds a significant qualification from Rabbi Elazar himself: *lo hikhshir Rabbi Elazar ela le-altar* — Rabbi Elazar permitted such a document only when the witnesses came immediately before *beit din* and testified. *Aval mikan ve-ad asara yamim lo* — but if ten days were to pass, he would not permit it.

The reason: *chashinan dilma havei bei tenai vezayafteh* — we are concerned that there was a *tenai* in the *get* and it was forged in the interim. This may seem paradoxical: if Rabbi Elazar holds *eidei mesira kartei*, why does the passage of time matter? He is relying on witnesses, not on the document itself. The answer, as *Tosafot* works out at length, lies in the imperfection of human memory. When witnesses testify immediately, their recollection of the document's precise contents is sharp. Combined even with a partially reliable document, their memory fills in any gaps. But with a forgeable document, the witnesses' memory alone must carry the entire evidentiary weight, because the document cannot corroborate their account. After ten days, memory of specific details — particularly any *tenai* — may fade, and we can no longer trust it with sufficient confidence.

The contrast with an unforgeable document is instructive. Were the document reliable, witnesses could point to it and say: "That is the *get* we saw; these are the signatures we witnessed." Memory and document mutually reinforce each other. With a forgeable document, the witnesses' memory stands alone. Therefore, Rabbi Elazar insists on immediate testimony.

### Kinyan vs. Rayah

*Tosafot* draws an important conceptual distinction that illuminates the entire sugya. There are two fundamentally different types of legal documents: a *shtar kinyan* and a *shtar rayah*.

A *shtar kinyan* is an instrument — it functions as the operative legal act. A *get* is the paradigmatic example: the document itself effects the divorce. Similarly, a deed of sale transfers ownership, and a writ of manumission frees a slave. The question for such documents is whether they function as valid instruments, not whether they can prove a historical fact.

A *shtar rayah*, by contrast, is an evidentiary document — its purpose is to prove what happened. A promissory note proves that a loan occurred; a title deed proves who owns a field. Such documents serve their function only insofar as they reliably testify to past events.

*Tosafot* argues that when Rabbi Elazar permits writing on *diftera*, this applies to *shtar kinyan* documents. A *get* written on *diftera*, in the presence of witnesses at delivery, functions as a valid instrument of divorce — it effects the *kinyan* of *gerushim*. The question of whether it can subsequently prove the divorce is separate. For *shtar rayah* documents, however, even Rabbi Elazar would require a non-forgeable surface, because a document whose testimony cannot be trusted cannot serve the purpose of an evidentiary instrument at all.

This also clarifies the position of Rabbi Meir. For Rabbi Meir, *eidei chatima kartei*: the signatures constitute the *get*. A *get* must testify for itself — it is *mukhach mitocho*. A document that is *mezuyyaf mitocho* — susceptible to internal forgery — cannot serve as a *get* for Rabbi Meir even in principle, not because it fails as *rayah* but because it fails as a *kinyan* instrument. For Rabbi Meir, a document that cannot self-authenticate is not a *get* at all, regardless of what witnesses say.

### R. Yochanan

*Rabbi Yochanan amar: afilu mikan ve-ad asara yamim* — Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with Rabbi Elazar's ten-day limitation and permits reliance on such a document together with witnesses even after ten days have passed. His reasoning: *de-ee issa de-havei bei tenai midkar dakhiri* — if there had been a *tenai* in the *get*, the witnesses would remember it. Their memory on this point remains sufficiently reliable even after ten days.

The *gemara* then states: *amar Rabbi Elazar lo hikhshir Rabbi Elazar ela begitten* — Rabbi Elazar's permit was limited to *gitten*. Other documents (*shear shtarot*) may not be written on *diftera*. The *gemara* cites the verse *unesatem bikhli cheres lema'an yaamdu yamim rabbim* — "place them in clay vessels so that they last many days" — as a basis for requiring that other documents be written on durable surfaces.

*Tosafot* clarifies what "other documents" means here. It does not mean all non-*get* documents indiscriminately. Rather, it refers to *shtar rayah* documents — those whose function is evidentiary rather than instrumental. For other *shtar kinyan* documents, such as a writ of manumission or a deed of sale, Rabbi Elazar might well agree that they too can be written on *diftera*, since they too function as instruments rather than as proof. The prohibition would apply specifically to *shtar rayah* documents, because the entire purpose of such a document is to testify reliably to past events — a function a forgeable document cannot serve.

Rabbi Yochanan's position extends further: *afilu beshtarot* — even for *shtar rayah* documents, a forgeable surface can be used, provided witnesses testify alongside it. The verse about lasting "many days" is, on his view, merely *eitzah tovah* — good practical advice about how to write a reliable document — but not a formal legal requirement. If witnesses are available to testify, their testimony combined with even a forgeable document may suffice.

To summarize the positions at the close of this sugya: for Rabbi Meir (*eidei chatima kartei*), a forgeable *get* is simply not a *get* — it cannot serve as a *shtar kinyan* for divorce, because the document must testify for itself, and a forgeable document cannot do so. For Rabbi Elazar (*eidei mesira kartei*), a forgeable *get* functions perfectly well as a *shtar kinyan*; the concern is only about reliability of witness memory for purposes of proof, which is why he limits the permit to immediate testimony. For Rabbi Yochanan, even this temporal limitation is rejected — witnesses reliably remember whether there was a *tenai*, even after ten days — and indeed the logic may extend even to *shtar rayah* documents when witnesses are present. The distinction between *shtar kinyan* and *shtar rayah* thus provides the organizing framework within which all these positions can be precisely located.
