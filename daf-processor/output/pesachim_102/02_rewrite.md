# Pesachim 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinui Makom Debate

### Shinui Makom Intro

The daf before us, 102, continues a discussion that has been building over the preceding pages: does *shinui makom* — changing one's location mid-eating — require a new *bracha rishona*? The question arose specifically in the context of wine: one recited *borei pri hagafen*, drank, even in a *makom seudah*, and then wished to continue drinking in a different location. Does that relocation obligate a new *bracha*? This is primarily a *brachot* question, though it arises here in the context of Pesachim.

### Rav, Shmuel, R. Yochanan

Rav and Shmuel both held that a new *borei pri hagafen* is required. Rabbi Yochanan disagreed, maintaining that *shinui makom* never triggers an obligation to repeat the *bracha*. A *braita* was then cited as a proof against Rabbi Yochanan, stating explicitly that *shinui makom* obligates a new *bracha* — appearing to refute his position outright.

### Rav Huna's Position

The *Gemara* then introduced a debate between Rav Huna and Rav Sheshet. Rav Huna drew a meaningful distinction: if, in the original location, one was engaged in a meal that required a *bracha achrona* in that same place — such as *bentching* or *al hamichya* — then even upon moving to a different location and continuing to eat or drink, no new *bracha* is required. The logic is that since one may always return to the original location to *bentch*, the *shinui makom* does not sever the continuity of the eating experience. It remains an extension of the primary meal established in the first place. In all other scenarios, Rav Huna agrees that a *shinui makom* does require a new *bracha*.

### Rav Sheshet's View

Rav Sheshet took a more sweeping position: regardless of whether a fixed meal was established in the original location, changing one's place always obligates a new *bracha*. There is no mitigating factor.

### Return to Same Place

A further *braita* was introduced involving a scenario somewhat different from the basic case: people who changed locations and then returned. One might have assumed that returning to the original place would resolve the issue for all opinions, yet the *Gemara* treats it as substantively analogous. The case is this: people were sitting, drinking wine — and presumably, as the *Gemara* understands it, eating a proper meal — and then left to go to *shul* or to attend to *chatan v'kallah*. Upon their return, must they recite a new *bracha*?

The *braita* states they need not recite a new *bracha*, provided they left people behind. This implies that the group had indeed uprooted themselves (*akru ragleihem*) from a serious, fixed meal — one requiring *hamotzi* — and yet, the only reason they are exempt from a new *bracha* upon return is that others remained. This appears to cut against Rav Huna: it suggests that even in a case of a fixed *seudah*, one would ordinarily need a new *bracha* after *shinui makom*, with the exemption applying only because people were left behind. That is precisely where the prior discussion left off.

## Hessech Hadaat Discussion

### Subjective Intent Factor

At this point it is worth pausing on a conceptual question raised by Tosafot at the bottom of 102b: the category of *hessech hadaat* — distraction from one's eating intent. This is the subjective element that the *Gemara* itself does not explicitly invoke in this discussion. Is the subjective dimension relevant here at all, or is this entire sugya organized around the objective fact of location?

The *Rishonim* do address this. One formulation found among them is that *shinui makom* constitutes *hessech hadaat* by definition — meaning, we presume that when a person changes location, they have mentally disengaged from their eating, and thus when they resume it constitutes a fresh experience. The more accurate reading, however, is that *shinui makom* functions as an *objective* counterpart to *hessech hadaat*, not as proof of actual subjective disengagement. Just as *hessech hadaat* creates a sense of subjective discontinuity, *shinui makom* creates an objective sense of discontinuity. Halacha, after all, prefers formal, objective criteria over invisible subjective states.

### Objective vs. Subjective

This distinction helps clarify why the *Gemara* does not invoke *hessech hadaat* directly. Consider: if one remains seated in place and falls asleep during a meal, or becomes entirely absorbed in watching something and forgets about the food entirely, that would be a genuine *hessech hadaat* — a subjective rupture in the eating experience. *Shinui makom*, by contrast, operates as an objective marker, independent of one's mental state. This is also why formal, objective criteria such as *koveia seudah* can come in to mitigate the issue: when one has established a fixed meal in a place, things are more readily seen as continuous regardless of where one wanders.

One might still ask: to what degree can specific positive intent overcome the issue of *shinui makom*? Tosafot does address this in the context of *kiddush bimkom seudah*, discussing an intermediate case — moving between the roof and the floor of the same building. There, Tosafot says, having it in mind that one intends to continue would suffice to constitute one *makom*. Moving from inside to outside, however, is a full *shinui makom* that intention cannot resolve; moving within the same room requires no conscious intention at all. The middle case — different stories of the same building — is where subjective intent becomes the operative factor. This intersection of subjective intent and objective reality applies to the broader *shinui makom* question as well.

### Same Building Case

Tosafot's discussion of the same-building case thus occupies a genuine middle ground between the fully objective cases on either end of the spectrum, illustrating that the dichotomy between objective and subjective is not absolute but admits of degrees.

## Friends Uprooting Braita

### Rabbi Yehuda Attribution

Returning now to the *braita* that apparently challenged Rav Chisda: the *Gemara* at the top of 102a records that Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak identifies the author of that *braita* — the one about uprooting oneself and leaving people behind — as Rabbi Yehuda. Crucially, there is a majority opinion, the *Chachamim*, who argue against Rabbi Yehuda and hold that no new *bracha* is required. Once this is recognized, the *braita* is no longer a simple refutation of Rav Chisda's position.

### Braita Text Analysis

The *braita* reads: *chaverim shehayu mesubin* — a group that was reclining and eating a meal, specifically *devarim teunim bracha achareihem bimkomam* — something that requires a *bracha achrona* in that same place, such as *bentching*. *V'akru ragleihem leilech l'beis haknesses o l'beis hamidrash* — they arose to go to *shul* or the study hall. *Keshen yotzin ein teunim bracha l'mafrea* — when they leave, they need not recite a concluding *bracha* on what they have already eaten. *U'cheshen chozrin ein teunim bracha l'chatchila* — and when they return, they need not recite a new opening *bracha*. They simply return, continue eating, and *bench* at the end.

The *Rishonim* note that part of the *chiddush* of *ein taun bracha l'mafrea* — not needing to *bench* before leaving — is that we are not concerned one might forget to return. One might have thought that since there is a risk of forgetting to come back, we would require *bentching* before departure as a precaution. The *braita* nevertheless permits leaving without *bentching*, since the plan is to return.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Condition

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies all of this: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this all apply? Only *bizman shehinichu sham miktzas chaverim* — when they left some members of the group behind. If they did not leave anyone behind, then *keshen yotzin teunim bracha l'mafrea* — they must *bench* before leaving — and *keshen chozrin teunim bracha l'chatchila* — they must recite a new *hamotzi* upon returning.

### Chachamim Dispute

The *Chachamim*, however, disagree entirely. Even without leaving people behind, no new *bracha* is required upon return. This is consistent with Rav Chisda's position, and consistent with the principle that when one has established a *seudah* in a place, *shinui makom* does not interrupt it. It is only Rabbi Yehuda who insists on a new *bracha* when no one was left behind.

---

## Braita Supports Rav Huna

### Braita Implications

The *Gemara* now draws out the implications of this *braita*. According to the *Chachamim*, who rule that no new *bracha* is required upon return, the reason is evidently that the meal was *devarim teunim bracha achareihem bimkomam* — a fixed meal requiring *bentching* in place. The further implication is: *aval devarim she'ein teunim bracha l'achareihem bimkoman* — had it not been such a case, had they been merely drinking wine or eating something that does not require a concluding *bracha* in that location, then even the *Chachamim* would concede that *keshen yotzin teunim bracha l'mafrea* and *keshen chozrin teunim bracha l'chatchila* — they would need to *bench* upon leaving and make a new *bracha* upon returning. The general implication of the *braita*, in other words, is that *shinui makom* ordinarily requires a new *bracha*, with the exception carved out precisely because it was a fixed *seudah*. This supports Rav Huna and does not contradict him.

### Contradiction Proof

The *Gemara* then asks: does this *braita* also constitute a contradiction to Rabbi Yochanan? After all, Rabbi Yochanan holds that *shinui makom* never requires a new *bracha* under any circumstances. This *braita* seems to assume that without the *seudah* factor, one would indeed need a new *bracha* — which would undermine his position. The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan was already contradicted earlier, where the *Gemara* concluded *tiyuvta d'Rabbi Yochanan tiyuvta*. So the question is whether this *braita* adds a further layer of refutation.

### R. Yochanan's Defense

Rabbi Yochanan could respond as follows: the *Chachamim* would actually say that even without a *seudah*, no new *bracha* is ever required due to *shinui makom*. Their position is as sweeping as his own. The reason the *braita* specifies the *seudah* case is not to imply that in its absence the ruling changes, but rather to set the stage for demonstrating how extreme Rabbi Yehuda's stringency is.

### Akru Ragleihem Reason

Why does the *braita* use the phrase *akru ragleihem* — uprooting themselves — which specifically evokes the language of a fixed meal? The answer is: *l'hodi'acha kocho d'Reb Yehuda* — to inform you of the severity of Rabbi Yehuda's position. Even in a case of a fully established meal, with all the formal markers of *kvias seudah*, Rabbi Yehuda still requires a new *bracha* unless people were left behind. The *chiddush* is how far Rabbi Yehuda goes, not that the *Chachamim* limit their leniency to the *seudah* scenario.

## Practical Scenarios

### Three Positions

To organize the range of opinions: Rabbi Yochanan holds that *shinui makom* never requires a new *bracha*, regardless of what one was eating. Rav Huna holds that a new *bracha* is required after *shinui makom* unless one had established a fixed *seudah* — one requiring a *bracha achrona* in place, such as *bentching* — in the original location; in that case, the continuity of the meal overrides the *shinui makom*. Rav Sheshet holds that *shinui makom* always requires a new *bracha*, without exception. Among the *Rishonim*, the debate about how we *pasken* is significant: the *Rif* rules like Rav Sheshet, while others follow Rav Huna. We certainly do not *pasken* like Rabbi Yochanan.

### Friends Wine Case

The scenario now before the *Gemara* is: *chaverim shehayu mesubin lishtos yayin v'akru ragleihem v'chazaru* — a group of friends who were reclining to drink wine, then uprooted themselves and returned. The *braita* states *ein tzrichim levarech* — they do not need to recite a new *bracha*. This is stated without qualification, without the Rabbinical caveat about who was left behind. At least according to Rav Chisda, the emphasis falls on the *akru ragleihem* — they had established a fixed *seudah* — and therefore upon their return, no new *bracha* is necessary.

This case also illustrates the role of *hessech hadaat* in the broader discussion. With coffee or wine taken casually outside a *seudah*, the halacha is more *machmir* — a new *bracha* is required after changing location — precisely because there is no formal meal structure creating continuity. In such cases, the psychological reality of *hessech hadaat* is likely operative: one simply walks away, thinks about other things, and when one returns, a genuine mental discontinuity has occurred. The *seudah*, by contrast, creates a persistent framework that keeps one tethered to the original eating context even across an interruption, which is why the *halacha* is actually more lenient there — a result that can seem counterintuitive at first, since one might expect the more significant obligation to be the more demanding one, but the logic runs in the opposite direction.

### Rav Chisda Ruling

The *Gemara* records: *tanya kevasei d'Rav Chisda* — it was taught in accordance with Rav Chisda. This confirms that the *braita* supports the position that when one has *koveia seudah* in a place and then leaves and returns, no new *bracha* is required.

### Machmir Positions

The stricter positions — Rav Sheshet and by extension Rabbi Yehuda — reflect the view that regardless of what was established, the physical act of uprooting oneself from one location to another creates an interruption that demands a fresh *bracha*. Intent and context can mitigate this, but only within the boundaries that the formal analysis permits.

## Interruptions & Mitzvot

### Don't Interrupt Rule

There is an additional dimension to the phrase *akru ragleihem leilech l'beis haknesses* — they uprooted themselves to go to *shul* — that deserves attention. The *Baal HaMaor* and the *Ra'avad* both understand this to be teaching a broader point: one should not ordinarily interrupt a meal. This is not merely a matter of etiquette. Given the *brachot* implications — the risk of forgetting to *bench*, the complications of *hessech hadaat*, the questions around *shinui makom* — one ought to finish a meal and *bench* before leaving for a discretionary matter (*dvar reshut*). The *braita* permits interrupting the meal specifically because what draws these people away is a *dvar mitzvah*: attending *shul* or accompanying a *chatan* and *kallah*. Only for purposes of *mitzvah* is one permitted *lechatchila* to leave mid-meal in this way.

### Davening Interruption

Tosafot raises a related and practically relevant question by noting the case of someone who forgot to daven *Mincha* and remembers in the middle of eating. Suppose one gets up and davens *Mincha* at home — is that an interruption that constitutes *shinui makom* or a *hefsek* requiring a new *bracha*? Tosafot rules *she'ein tzarich bracha afilu lechatchila* — no new *bracha* is needed, even *lechatchila*. The *braita* speaks of *akru ragleihem leilech l'beis haknesses* — it was specifically the *shinui makom* involved in going to *shul* that raised the *bracha* question. The act of davening itself, performed in the same location where one was eating, does not constitute a *hefsek*. The problem in the *braita* arose only because of the physical relocation. Tosafot addresses an apparent counter-proof from another *Gemara* and dismisses it. The ruling is: if one must interrupt a meal to daven, the davening itself creates no interruption — only the *shinui makom* would.

### Context & Intention

This leads naturally to a broader question about what actually defines *shinui makom*. Context and intention appear to play a real role in how we draw the boundaries. One might argue that *shinui makom* only constitutes a meaningful interruption when it constitutes a genuinely *new activity* — when the relocation represents a departure from the eating context. When the *shinui makom* is a natural extension of or accompaniment to the meal — going to the bathroom, stepping onto the terrace at a wedding smorg, moving between stations at a buffet — the continuity of the eating framework arguably remains intact, and the change of location is absorbed into the same experience. When one walks out entirely and engages in something wholly separate, the break is more real. The intersection of subjective intent and objective location is thus genuinely complex, and the degree to which positive intent can preserve continuity across an objective *shinui makom* is a question the *Rishonim* leave with some nuance.

## Chaveirim Definition

### Chaveirim vs. Benei Chab…

The term *chaverim* that appears throughout these *braitot* raises a question of its own: is it being used technically or generally? In the Tannaitic literature, a *chaver* or *ben chaburah* carries precise technical meaning — these were members of an elite group who were scrupulous about eating their *chulin* in a state of *taharah*, who would not eat together with outsiders, and for whom the rules of purity were rigorously observed in the context of food. Given that all of these *braitot* involve eating scenarios, one might wonder whether the use of *chaverim* and *benei chaburah* is deliberate and technical, or whether the *braita* is simply using natural, familiar language to describe any group of people eating together. The more likely reading is the latter — that these terms are not being deployed in their strict technical sense here but are describing any assembled group engaged in a shared meal. There is no evident reason in the context of these discussions why they would need to be technically restricted to Torah-observant purity groups.

### Chaburah & Intent

That said, it is worth noting that the model of the *chaburah* — with its emphasis on intentionality around eating, on the deliberate maintenance of *taharah*, and on the formal boundaries of who is and who is not part of the eating circle — may not be entirely irrelevant. One attractive suggestion is that the intentionality which once governed the *chaburah* framework around *tumah v'taharah* is, at a later stage, transposed onto the framework of *brachot*: the cohesion of a group, their shared intent in eating together, and their collective responsibility for the structure of the meal, now expressed through the mutual obligation around reciting *brachot*. It is even possible that *shinui makom* as a halachic category is itself rooted, at some level, in the concerns of *tumah v'taharah* — where moving from place to place always carried purity implications for those eating in a state of *taharah*. Whether or not that connection is historically provable, it is conceptually suggestive.

## Friends at Shabbat Onset

### Shabbat Interruption

The next *braita* introduces a new scenario: *benei chaburah shehayu mesubin* — a group that was reclining and eating — *v'kiddesh aleihem hayom* — and then the day became sanctified, meaning it was Friday and *Shabbat* arrived while they were mid-meal. Now they face an obligation to both *bench* and recite *kiddush*, and the question is how to manage this.

### Rabbi Yehuda Order

Rabbi Yehuda rules that the meal must be interrupted. One brings a cup of wine and recites *kiddushat hayom*, and a second person recites *birkat hamazon* on a separate cup. Notably, according to the *girsa* before us, Rabbi Yehuda requires *kiddush* first and *birkat hamazon* second — an order that might initially seem surprising, since one would naturally expect to first conclude the weekday meal with *bentching* and only then inaugurate *Shabbat* with *kiddush*.

### Text Variations

There are in fact variant *girsaot* of this *braita* in the *Rishonim*: some readings have Rabbi Yehuda specifying that *birkat hamazon* comes first and *kiddush* second. Our *girsa*, however, gives *kiddush* priority.

### Tosafot's Logic

Tosafot explains our *girsa* by way of a technical consideration. If one takes a *kos* of wine and recites *birkat hamazon* upon it — with the assumption throughout this *sugya* being that *birkat hamazon* is recited *al hakos* — and it has now become Friday night, a problem arises: how can one drink from the *kos* of *birkat hamazon* after completing *bentching*? It is already *Shabbat*, and one may not drink wine on *Shabbat* night without first reciting *kiddush*. Therefore, *kiddush* must come first, so that one may then proceed to *bentching* and drink from its cup. The act of *kiddush* enables the drinking of the *birkat hamazon* wine. As a further device to signal the interruption of the weekday meal before *kiddush*, one removes the table — *mesallek hashulchan* — which formally marks the break. But in terms of the sequence of *brachot*, *kiddush* must precede *birkat hamazon* for this technical reason.

## Rabbi Yosi's Leniency

### Continue Eating

Rabbi Yosi takes a more lenient approach: *ochel v'holech ad she-techeshach* — one continues eating until it becomes fully dark. There is no obligation to interrupt the meal when *Shabbat* arrives.

### Yosi's Order

Once the meal concludes naturally, Rabbi Yosi prescribes the following order: *kos rishon mevarech alav birkat hamazon* — on the first cup, one recites *birkat hamazon* — and *hasheni kiddushat hayom* — on the second cup, *kiddushat hayom*. This is the intuitive order: first conclude the meal, then inaugurate *Shabbat*.

### Yosi Ambiguity

A subtle difficulty in Rabbi Yosi's position is worth noting. During the meal, Rabbi Yosi permits continued eating after *Shabbat* has begun because it is a continuation of a pre-existing weekday meal. But once the meal has concluded and one recites *birkat hamazon al hakos*, the question arises: by this point, should Rabbi Yosi share Rabbi Yehuda's concern? After all, now that the meal is over and one must drink the wine of *birkat hamazon*, it is no longer mid-meal — one is post-meal on *Shabbat* night. Perhaps one ought to first recite *kiddush* before drinking from the *kos* of *birkat hamazon*, just as Rabbi Yehuda would require. Rabbi Yosi apparently does not share this concern — perhaps because the permission already granted to continue eating extends, by continuity, to the wine accompanying *birkat hamazon*, which is still understood as part of the same eating event. But the position is not without ambiguity.

## Poreis Mappah Custom

### Our Custom Practice

All of this discussion of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi is, as the *Gemara* notes, not directly operative for us, because we *pasken* like the practice of *poreis mappah umekaddesh* — spreading the tablecloth and reciting *kiddush* without interrupting the meal in the way Rabbi Yehuda describes. The modern practice accommodates *Shabbat*'s arrival during a meal with a different mechanism.

### No New Bracha Needed

One practical consequence of *poreis mappah umekaddesh*: if one has been drinking wine throughout the meal and then covers the bread and recites *kiddush*, no new *borei pri hagafen* is needed. In fact, one recites *kiddush* beginning directly with *asher kideshanu b'mitzvotav v'ratzah vanu*, skipping *borei pri hagafen* entirely — the strikingly bold-seeming act of making *kiddush* without its usual opening blessing. The reason is that *borei pri hagafen* is not technically a *bracha* of *kiddush* itself; it is incorporated into *kiddush* because it is needed to drink the wine of *kiddush*, and in order to make *kiddush al hayayin*. Since one has already been drinking wine continuously during the meal, no new *borei pri hagafen* is required — that obligation was already discharged. When *kiddush* requires *borei pri hagafen* on behalf of others at the table who need it, it functions there as part of the *kiddush* structure, but when it is not needed at all, one simply proceeds without it.

### New Hamotzi Debate

There is, however, a genuine dispute among the *Rishonim* about whether a new *hamotzi* is required after *poreis mappah umekaddesh*. One view holds that the act of *kiddush* — even though performed at the same table with the food present — inaugurates a new *Shabbat* meal and severs continuity with what preceded it, much like a *shinui makom* would. As a new meal, it would require a new *hamotzi*. Others disagree: yes, it has become *Shabbat*, but there has been no interruption, and the bread is still on the table. No new *hamotzi* is needed.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The *Shulchan Aruch* records both opinions. The *Mishnah Berurah* rules in accordance with the principle of *safek brachot l'hakel* — where there is doubt about whether a *bracha* is required, one does not recite it. The result is a striking experiential scenario: one recites *kiddush* without *borei pri hagafen*, then uncovers the *shtei lechem* and eats without *hamotzi*. This is the actual *halacha* in practice, and on a Purim that falls on a Friday — with a *Purim seudah* running into *Shabbat* — one can have the experience of living through this ruling directly.

## Two Brachot One Cup

### Two Kedushot Rule

The *Gemara* now returns to the question of Rabbi Yosi's scenario — why does Rabbi Yosi require two cups, one for *birkat hamazon* and one for *kiddush*? Why not recite both on the same cup? The question is posed by the *Gemara* itself: *v'nimrinhu l'travaihu achada kassa* — why not say both on a single cup of wine? This question applies specifically to Rabbi Yosi, since according to Rabbi Yehuda the very point is to use the two *brachot* to mark a separation, so combining them on one cup would be conceptually self-defeating. But according to Rabbi Yosi, who sees the *kiddush* as integral to and following from the meal, one might argue that a single cup should suffice.

Rav Huna citing Rav Sheshet rules: *ein omrim shtei kedushot al kos echad* — one does not recite two sanctifications upon a single cup.

### Bundle Principle

The reason, as explained by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, is *lefi she'ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot* — one does not bundle *mitzvot* together. The *Rashbam* understands this as a concern about the appearance of treating the *mitzvot* as a burden: by recycling the cup and getting double use from it, one conveys the impression that these ritual acts are an inconvenience to be dispatched efficiently rather than independent obligations deserving of their own weight and attention.

### Kos Significance

There is a deeper point here about what it means to perform something *al hakos* at all. One may not always be aware that the practice of reciting *sheva brachot al hakos*, *birkat erusin al hakos*, and *birkat bris milah al hakos* are all later developments; the *Gemara* does not assume them. But the very institution of performing a ritual act *al hakos* is what gives it weight and transforms it into a formal religious event. If one were to say *sheva brachot* without a cup of wine, without the *kos*, the act would not carry the same ritual force or experiential gravity. The cup lends identity and significance to the *bracha* recited over it. It follows that when one attempts to recite two distinct sanctifications over the same cup, each loses its identity: is this a *kos* of *birkat hamazon*, or a *kos* of *kiddush*? The categories blur, and neither retains its proper weight. The principle of *ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot* is therefore especially apt in the context of *kiddush al hakos*, where the cup is constitutive of the ritual's identity.

## Limited Wine Case

### One Cup Solution

The *Gemara* raises an apparent challenge to the rule just stated. A *braita* teaches: *hanichnas l'veiso b'motza'ei Shabbat* — one who enters his home on *motza'ei Shabbat* — *mevarech al hayayin v'al hamaor v'al habesamim* — recites *havdalah* with wine, fire, and spices. (The order here differs from our current practice, which will be discussed later.) *V'achar kach omer havdalah* — and then says *havdalah*. This is the normal *havdalah al hakos*.

But then: *v'im ein lo ela kos echad* — if one has only a single cup of wine — *manicho l'achar hamazon* — one sets it aside until after eating, *umeshalshelan kulan l'acharav* — and strings all the *brachot* together after the meal in a daisy-chain on that one cup. The *Gemara* captures the image with the word *meshalshel* — like a *shalshelet*, a chain — meaning all the *brachot* are linked together onto the single cup. So here, it seems, we have *birkat hamazon* and *havdalah* both on the same cup, which would appear to violate the principle just established.

### Daisy Chain Brachot

The practical sequence in the *ein lo ela kos echad* scenario is to recite *birkat hamazon* on the cup and then follow it immediately with *havdalah*, using the same cup for both. This appears to be precisely the bundling that Rav Sheshet prohibited.

### Exception Ruling

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *eino domeh* — the cases are not alike. When one has two cups available, one is obligated to use separate cups for separate *brachot*, so that each retains its distinct identity. But when only one cup exists, one is granted latitude. The rule of *ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot* applies when one has a choice; it does not demand that one forgo a *mitzvah* entirely because the ideal vessel is unavailable.

### Poor Person Exception

Tosafot asks a further question about the case: if one has come home after *shul* on *motza'ei Shabbat* and has only one cup of wine, why not simply make *havdalah* immediately and then eat? The answer, according to Tosafot, involves a poor person who intends to make his primary meal that evening and wants to have *birkat hamazon al hakos*, as the *Gemara* throughout assumes is the standard practice. If he uses his only cup for *havdalah* now, he will have no cup for *birkat hamazon* later. Therefore, we permit him to eat his meal first, even though ordinarily one may not eat before *havdalah*, in order to enable *birkat hamazon al hakos* followed immediately by *havdalah* on the same cup. This is a striking leniency: the concern for *birkat hamazon al hakos* is treated as significant enough to override the normal prohibition against eating before *havdalah* when it is the only available solution for a person with limited means.

This ruling also highlights a broader assumption of the *Gemara* that is easy to overlook: throughout all of these discussions, *birkat hamazon al hakos* is taken completely for granted, even for an individual eating alone. The *Gemara* never entertains the possibility of *bentching* without a cup of wine; the *kos* is simply assumed as the normal accompaniment to *birkat hamazon* in every scenario.

## Yom Tov After Shabbat

### Two Cups Standard

The *Gemara* raises the scenario of *Yom Tov she'chal lihyot achar Shabbat* — a *Yom Tov* that falls on the night immediately following *Shabbat*. In such a case, one must recite both *havdalah* from *Shabbat* and *kiddush* for *Yom Tov* at the same time. The *Gemara* notes that in this scenario, one obviously has two cups of wine available. Tosafot explains why this is obvious: by the end of *Shabbat*, one might conceivably have consumed all available wine over the course of the day, but the beginning of *Yom Tov* is an occasion of *simcha* and festivity — *ach sameach* — and presumably one's wine supplies are still adequate. It would be unusual to begin *Yom Tov* without wine.

### Rav Yaknah Order

Rav rules: *Yaknah* — the order is *yayin*, *kiddush*, *ner*, *havdalah*. One begins with wine, recites *kiddush* for *Yom Tov*, then the blessing over fire, and concludes with *havdalah*. The question immediately arises: why does *kiddush* precede *havdalah*? Chronologically, one is ending *Shabbat* and entering *Yom Tov*, so logically *havdalah* should come first. The *Gemara* treats both *kiddush* and *havdalah* as performed on the same *kos*, which raises the further question of how this is consistent with the rule against using one cup for two sanctifications — a point addressed in the following section.

### Kiddush First Reason

The *Rashbam* offers two explanations for why *kiddush* precedes *havdalah*. First, *kidushat hayom adifa me-havdalah* — the sanctification of the incoming day takes priority, as one prefers to emphasize the holiness one is entering rather than the departure from the previous holiness. Second — and this is a principle that recurs in other contexts — when *havdalah* is pushed to the end of the order, it signals a kind of reluctance to end *Shabbat*. Just as on *Motza'ei Shabbat Chanukah* one lights the *Chanukah* candles first before *havdalah* in order to express that reluctance, so too here, placing *havdalah* after *kiddush* demonstrates that one does not rush to conclude *Shabbat*. The very act of doing *kiddush* first — even though one is in a sense already inaugurating the new day — carries this expressive significance.

## Havdalah Kiddush Unity

### Same Concept

The *Gemara* resolves the underlying tension between the rule of *ein omrim shtei kedushot al kos echad* and the practice of combining *kiddush* and *havdalah* on one cup. The resolution: *havdalah v'kiddush chada milta hi* — *havdalah* and *kiddush* are the same thing conceptually. On the surface this seems paradoxical: *kiddush* declares the day holy, while *havdalah* distinguishes the holy from the mundane. They appear to operate in opposite directions. But at a deeper level, both perform the same function: they define the nature of the day and distinguish it from what surrounds it. Both *kiddush* and *havdalah* are acts of *dibur b'kidush* — of articulating and demarcating the boundary of a sanctified time. Since they share this fundamental conceptual identity, using one cup for both does not muddy the waters. The cup retains a coherent identity as the vessel upon which the nature of the day is being declared — in both of its dimensions at once.

### Same Cup Allowed

The practical consequence is that not only is combining *kiddush* and *havdalah* on one cup permitted, but the entire *Yaknah* or *Yaknehaz* sequence is performed on a single cup. The cup's identity is not compromised because both acts are facets of the same overarching concept.

### Benching vs. Kiddush

By contrast, *birkat hamazon v'kiddush terei milei ninhu* — *birkat hamazon* and *kiddush* are two conceptually distinct and unrelated things. One belongs to the domain of sanctifying time; the other emerges from the fact that one has eaten a meal. They are from different halachic universes. Therefore, using one cup for both is inappropriate — it strips each act of its distinct identity and creates conceptual confusion about what the cup represents. This is the principled distinction that underlies the entire section: it is not merely about having enough wine, but about preserving the conceptual integrity of what is performed *al hakos*.

## Wedding Two Cups

### Bundling Issue

Tosafot takes up the application of *ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot* to the wedding meal. At a *chuppah* and *seudas nissu'in*, one recites both *birkat hamazon* and *sheva brachot*, each traditionally *al hakos*. The question is whether this constitutes an impermissible bundling of two distinct ritual acts on a single cup. Tosafot notes that some have the practice of using separate cups for *birkat hamazon* and *sheva brachot* at the wedding meal precisely for this reason — to avoid the appearance of bundling two different *mitzvot*.

### Custom Practice

The standard practice that emerges from this concern is to use two cups: one for *birkat hamazon* and a second for *sheva brachot*. In this way, each act retains its distinct identity on its own cup, and the concern of *chavilot chavilot* is avoided.

### Wine Mixing Magic

In practice, this gives rise to the well-known custom of pouring from both cups into a third cup, and then from the third back into the original two, so that each of the two primary cups contains wine from both. The purpose is to ensure that the *mechaber* — the one leading *bentching* — and the *chatan* and *kallah* each receive wine that combines both cups. The procedure, which can appear elaborate or mysterious to observers, is ultimately a simple act of distribution aimed at ensuring that all parties drink from wine associated with both the *birkat hamazon* and the *sheva brachot*.

### Meshullam Leniency

Tosafot records a dissenting view: Rabbeinu Meshullam held that one may say it all on a single cup. His reasoning is that the case of *birkat hamazon* and *kiddush* — which the *Gemara* explicitly prohibits on one cup — involves *terei milei* — two conceptually unrelated things. But *birkat hamazon* and *sheva brachot* are not unrelated. The *sheva brachot* are recited *because* there is a meal: *birchat hamazon gorem l'birchat nissu'in* — the *bentching* occasions the *sheva brachot*. The blessing of the meal is the cause of and context for the *sheva brachot*, making them integral to one another rather than conceptually foreign. Following this logic, the two are *chada milta* — one thing — and using a single cup is not merely permitted but perhaps even appropriate. This is a compelling position, even if it is not the accepted practice.

## Related vs. Unrelated

### Same Event Rule

The principle emerging from the *Gemara* and Tosafot is that the permissibility of combining two *brachot* on one cup — or the obligation to use separate cups — depends on the conceptual relationship between the two acts and the context in which they occur. When two ritual acts are part of the same event, share a common conceptual framework, and flow from the same cause or occasion, they may share a cup. The cup retains a coherent identity because both acts belong together.

### Separate Events Rule

When the two acts, even if conceptually linked in some abstract sense, occur at separate events or are separated by a meaningful break, separate cups are required. The cup used at one event cannot carry over its ritual identity to a different event. Each event demands its own vessel.

### Eirusin Nisuin Case

Tosafot applies this principle to the case of *birchat erusin* and *birchat nissu'in*, which are both blessings connected to marriage and are in that sense thematically related. Nevertheless, the accepted practice is to recite them on two separate cups. Tosafot gives two reasons. First, historically, *erusin* and *nissu'in* often took place at entirely separate times — *erusin* could precede *nissu'in* by a year, with the *erusin* blessings recited in the *beis ha'av* and the *nissu'in* blessings at the *beis hechasan*. Their origins as distinct events, separated in time and location, shape how we treat them even when they occur together under the same *chuppah*. Second, and more immediately, the *ketubbah* is read between the two sets of blessings. The reading of the *ketubbah* is intentionally designed to create a separation — a *hefsek* — between *erusin* and *nissu'in*, transforming what would otherwise be a single ceremony into two formally distinct acts. Given this deliberate interruption, two separate cups are required.

## Rav vs. Shmuel Order

### Rav Yaknah

Returning to the *gufa* — the body of the sugya on *Yom Tov* following *Shabbat*: Rav rules *Yaknah* — *yayin*, *kiddush*, *ner*, *havdalah*. The cup begins with *kiddush* for *Yom Tov* and concludes with *havdalah* from *Shabbat*. As discussed, the reason *kiddush* precedes *havdalah* is to give primacy to the sanctification of the incoming day and to signal reluctance in departing *Shabbat*.

### Shmuel Yanhak

Shmuel disagrees and rules *Yanhak* — *yayin*, *ner*, *havdalah*, *kiddush*. In Shmuel's order, one first completes the full *havdalah* — including the blessing over fire — and only then recites *kiddush* for the new *Yom Tov*. This sequence is logically intuitive: one formally ends *Shabbat* first and then inaugurates the new day. The practical and conceptual reasoning is that one ought to first resolve the outgoing sanctity before declaring the incoming one. The accepted ruling — *Yaknehaz*, which is Rav's order with *shehecheyanu* added at the end — will be elaborated further in the discussion that follows.

## No Besamim Rule

### Rashbam Position

A final question that the *Gemara* addresses in this context: why is there no *besamim* — no spices — when *Yom Tov* follows *Shabbat*? The *Rashbam* explains that on *Yom Tov*, one also has a *neshama yesaira* — an additional soul — and therefore the *besamim* are unnecessary. The entire function of *besamim* at *havdalah* is to revive a person from the spiritual loss suffered when the *neshama yesaira* departs at the end of *Shabbat*. If *Yom Tov* itself provides a *neshama yesaira*, no such revival is needed.

### Tosafot Correction

Tosafot takes issue with the *Rashbam*'s reasoning. If the *neshama yesaira* were a feature of *Yom Tov* in the same way it is of *Shabbat*, then at *motza'ei Yom Tov* — at the conclusion of every *Yom Tov* — we would be obligated to use *besamim* at *havdalah*. We do not do so. Therefore, the *neshama yesaira* cannot be the operative mechanism here.

Tosafot's own explanation is that while *Yom Tov* does not carry a *neshama yesaira*, it does carry *simcha* — genuine joy. The *simcha* of *Yom Tov*, of entering a festival, provides sufficient spiritual elevation and vitality that the person does not experience the same sense of diminishment and loss that ordinarily accompanies the departure of *Shabbat*. The *besamim* exist to restore and revive; when the simcha of *Yom Tov* itself provides that restoration, *besamim* become superfluous.

### Ner Necessity

As for the blessing over *ner* — fire — that is included even when *Yom Tov* follows *Shabbat*: fire is permitted on *Yom Tov* in a way that it is not on *Shabbat*, and the *havdalah* blessing over *ner* specifically marks this distinction. The transition from *Shabbat* to *Yom Tov* is a moment at which the prohibition on creating or transferring fire is lifted, making the blessing over *ner* genuinely meaningful as a marker of what changes between the two days. *Besamim*, by contrast, marks only the departure of *Shabbat* holiness — a departure that is, in this case, immediately softened by the arrival of *Yom Tov* joy.
