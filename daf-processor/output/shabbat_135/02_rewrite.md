# Shabbat 135 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exceptions to Bris M (II)

### Safek, Androgynos, Bein

The Gemara picks up at the very bottom of 134b and addresses the question of when *bris milah* does not override *Shabbat*. The *Mishnah* identified two cases — *safek* and *androgynos* — and the *Gemara* now elaborates through a *baraisa*.

The *baraisa* begins with a *derashah* from the verse *uvayom hashmini yimol besar arlaso* — "on the eighth day you shall circumcise the flesh of his *arlah*." The possessive *arlaso*, "his *arlah*," is read as a limiting term: *arlaso vadai docheh es haShabbos* — a definite *arlah* overrides *Shabbat*, but a doubtful one does not. The *baraisa* then derives three cases from this principle. First: *arlaso vadai docheh es haShabbos velo androgynos docheh es haShabbos* — the *androgynos*, the person born with both sets of genitalia, does not override *Shabbat*, since his male status is not beyond doubt. *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees: since the *androgynos* possesses male genitalia, the *arlah* is definite, and the *bris milah* does override *Shabbat* — and, he adds, there is a penalty of *kares* for failing to perform it. We will return to the *androgynos* case at greater length below.

### Bein Hashmashos - Birth…

The second exclusion: *velo nolad bein hashmashos docheh es haShabbos* — a child born during *bein hashmashos*, the twilight period, does not override *Shabbat*. If born between Friday and *Shabbat*, the child may be a Friday baby, in which case *Shabbat* falls on the ninth day and the *bris milah* would not override it. If born between *Shabbat* and Sunday, the child may be a Sunday baby, making *Shabbat* the seventh day — equally insufficient. Either way, the doubt prevents *Shabbat* from being overridden.

The *baraisa* then introduces a fourth case: *velo nolad keshu mahul docheh es haShabbos* — a child born already circumcised, without a foreskin, does not override *Shabbat*. And here the *baraisa* records a dispute: *Beis Shammai* holds that one is still required to extract a drop of blood — *tzarich lehatif mimenu dam bris* — while *Beis Hillel* holds that no such act is required. This case of the child born without a foreskin raises a deeper conceptual question that becomes the primary focus of the *amud*: the institution of *hatafat dam bris*, and what it reveals about the nature of the *mitzvah* of *bris milah* itself.

## Hatafat Dam Brit

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

Before turning to the conceptual discussion, it is worth noting what precisely is at stake in the dispute. Two distinct questions are entangled: whether *hatafat dam bris* is required at all for such a child, and if so, whether it overrides *Shabbat*. These two questions are not necessarily answered together, and the *Gemara* will tease them apart. The underlying issue, as *Rashi* emphasizes, concerns the role of blood in the act of *bris milah* — whether the drawing of blood is itself a component of the *mitzvah* even when there is no foreskin to remove, or whether the entire purpose of *bris milah* is the excision of the foreskin and nothing more. Both of these threads are intertwined: if one believes that even without a foreskin there is a ritual act to be performed, it becomes crucial to identify what that act consists of, and the extraction of blood provides one answer. These are not merely theoretical concerns — they have significant *lemaaseh* consequences, as we will see.

### R. Shimon - Agreement

*Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*: *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* do not actually disagree about a child born without a foreskin — both agree that *hatafat dam bris* is required. *Lo nechelku Beis Shammai uVeis Hillel al nolad keshu mahul shetzarich lehatif mimenu dam bris*. The reason, he explains, is *mipnei she'arlaso kevushah hi* — because his *arlah* is in fact buried beneath the skin. There is foreskin tissue present, and by extracting a drop of blood some incision is made into that buried foreskin. What, then, is the actual dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*?

### Convert's Status

*Al mah nechelku? Al ger shenisgayer keshu mahul* — the debate is about a convert who comes before us already circumcised. In this case, unlike a child born without a foreskin, there is no possibility of a buried foreskin: the foreskin was surgically removed, either for medical reasons or following his own people's customs — the *Gemara* elsewhere references Arabs, for whom circumcision was common practice even in pre-Islamic times. Since there is no foreskin, buried or otherwise, *Beis Shammai* maintains that one must nonetheless extract the blood of the covenant — *tzarich lehatif mimenu dam bris* — while *Beis Hillel* maintains that no act is required at all: *eino tzarich lehatif mimenu dam bris*.

### Conceptual Foundations

At this point the *Gemara* has presented two different pictures of what the dispute is. According to the *tanna kamma* of the *baraisa*, the dispute of *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* concerns a child born without a foreskin — whether *hatafat dam bris* is required even on a weekday. According to *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, both houses agree you perform *hatafat dam bris* for such a child, and the dispute concerns the case of a convert. These two framings reflect a deeper conceptual divide about what *hatafat dam bris* accomplishes and why.

The position that requires nothing — neither *hatafat dam bris* nor anything else — for the child born without a foreskin is in many ways the most logically straightforward. The purpose of *bris milah* is to remove the foreskin; if there is no foreskin, there is no *mitzvah* to perform. One does nothing on the weekday and certainly nothing on *Shabbat*. Even if one grants the concept of *orlah kevushah*, a buried foreskin, one might respond that if the foreskin is not covering the *ever* it is not functioning as a foreskin, and there is therefore no obligation to address it.

The position that requires *hatafat dam bris* on a weekday holds that even when the foreskin is not covering the organ, there remains a ritual act to perform — and that act must be understood as something done in relation to a foreskin. Hence the necessity of positing a buried foreskin. But this position, taken to its logical conclusion, raises a further question: if there truly is a buried foreskin and one is genuinely performing an act of *bris milah* upon it, should that not override *Shabbat*? Which leads to the final position: that *hatafat dam bris* does override *Shabbat*, because the buried foreskin is certainly present and the act of drawing blood upon it constitutes a genuine performance of the *mitzvah*.

## Exceptions to Bris M (II)

### Safek - Viability Doubt

Having enumerated the cases excluded from overriding *Shabbat*, the *Gemara* asks: what precisely is the content of the *safek* case? We have already covered *androgynos*, *bein hashmashos*, and the child born without a foreskin. What doubt remains? The *Gemara* answers: *le'atuye hach de'tanu rabbanan* — to include that which the Rabbis taught in the following *baraisa*: *ben shivah mechalelin alav es haShabbos, uven shemonah ein mechalelin alav es haShabbos* — for a child born in the seventh month of gestation one may violate *Shabbat*, but for one born in the eighth month, one may not. And: *safek ben shivah safek ben shemonah — ein mechalelin alav es haShabbos* — if there is doubt whether the child was born in the seventh or eighth month, one does not violate *Shabbat*.

This is a striking and paradoxical position. The *Gemara* holds that a seventh-month infant is viable, but an eighth-month infant — born between the seventh and ninth months — is not considered viable at all. The *baraisa* goes further: *ben shemonah harei hu ke'even* — an eighth-month infant is treated like a stone. Not only does one not violate *Shabbat* to save its life, but one may not even move it, since it is *muktzah*. The one concession is that *imo shokha umenikato mipnei hasakanah* — the mother may lean down and nurse him, not because of concern for the infant's survival, but because of the danger to the mother herself if she does not nurse.

It is worth noting what *Tosafos* says about this extreme position. Even in the case of a definitively identified eighth-month infant — with no possibility of it being a seventh- or ninth-month child — the *Ri* rules *mutar letaltelo ulmolo beShabbat*, that one may move the infant and perform *bris milah* on *Shabbat*, provided there is no specific negative indicator — *im ein reisha besa'aro vetzipornav* — based on the condition of the infant's hair and fingernails that would more directly attest to non-viability. *Tosafos* is thus already moving away from the *Gemara*'s categorical ruling, limiting the eighth-month *halakhah* to cases where there is affirmative evidence of non-viability. And of course, today the reality of incubators and modern neonatology changes these practical realities further still. The *Gemara*'s distinction, granting greater presumptive viability to a seventh-month infant than an eighth-month one, is one we do not apply in practice without strong supporting indicators.

*Tosafos* also addresses the *Gemara*'s reason permitting the mother to nurse — the *sakana* — and notes that even without a *sakana*, the mother should be permitted to nurse, raising interesting questions about whether expressing milk involves a biblical or rabbinic prohibition on *Shabbat*. In this case, however, actual nursing is taking place, so those concerns do not directly arise.

The upshot for the *safek* case: if there is doubt whether a child is a viable seventh-month infant or a non-viable eighth-month infant, one does not perform *bris milah* on *Shabbat*, since if the child falls into the eighth-month category there is no license to override *Shabbat* for the *bris milah* at all, and doubt is insufficient to override *Shabbat*.

## Rav Adda Bar Abba

### Birth Without Foreskin

The *Gemara* now records the *amora'ic* rulings on the question of *hatafat dam bris*. *Rav* rules like the *tanna kamma*: the debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* concerns whether one performs *hatafat dam bris* for a child born without a foreskin — *Beis Hillel* rules that one does not, and the *halakhah* follows *Beis Hillel*. Hence *Rav*'s position: one does not perform *hatafat dam bris* for such a child, neither on a weekday nor certainly on *Shabbat*. *Shmuel*, by contrast, rules like *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*: both *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* agree that *hatafat dam bris* is performed for such a child, and the debate between the houses concerns only the case of the convert. Hence according to *Shmuel*, *hatafat dam bris* is performed for the child born without a foreskin.

### Refused by Mohelim

It is against this backdrop that the *Gemara* recounts the story of *Rav Adda bar Abba*, who had a son born on *Shabbat* without a foreskin. He brought the infant to thirteen *mohalim* in succession — *ahadrei a'treisar mahulai* — and each one refused to perform any procedure. Their reasoning was clear: a child born without a foreskin does not have a *bris milah* that overrides *Shabbat*, and so there is nothing to be done.

### Tragic Consequence

Since no *mohel* would act, *Rav Adda bar Abba* took matters into his own hands. Untrained in the procedure, he himself attempted to perform the act — *ad d'shavye k'rus shofkha* — and tragically mutilated his son, rendering him a *krus shofkha*. Recognizing the gravity of what had occurred, he said: *teisi li d'avri a'deRav* — I deserve this outcome, for I acted against the ruling of *Rav*. Since *Rav* rules that one does not perform *hatafat dam bris* for a child born without a foreskin, *Rav Adda bar Abba* should never have attempted the procedure at all.

### Rav Nachman Corrects

*Rav Nachman* corrected him sharply: *a'deRav lo avar? a'deShmuel lo avar?* — you think you only violated *Rav*'s position? You violated *Shmuel*'s position too. *Eimar d'amar Shmuel* — when did *Shmuel* say to rule like *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*? That was *b'chol*, on a weekday. *Shmuel* may well hold that *hatafat dam bris* is performed on a weekday for a child born without a foreskin, but he never said it would override *Shabbat*.

The *Gemara* explains *Rav Adda bar Abba*'s reasoning: *hu savar vadai orlah kevushah hi* — he understood *Shmuel*'s position to mean that there is *definitely* a buried foreskin in such a child, and therefore performing *hatafat dam bris* is a full act of *bris milah*, which would override *Shabbat* just as ordinary *bris milah* does. *Rav Nachman*'s correction is that the position requiring *hatafat dam bris* is grounded only in *suspicion* of a buried foreskin — and a doubtful buried foreskin, while sufficient to warrant the act on a weekday, does not override *Shabbat*.

This brings us to the dispute of *Rabbah* and *Rav Yosef*, which had been developing in the background of this discussion.

## Buried Foreskin Debate

### Rabbah - Suspicion

*Rabbah* holds: *chaishinan shema orlah kevushah hi* — those who require *hatafat dam bris* for a child born without a foreskin do so only out of suspicion that perhaps there is a buried foreskin. It is a *safek*, not a certainty.

### Rav Yosef - Certainty

*Rav Yosef* holds: *vadai orlah kevushah hi* — there is definitely a buried foreskin. Those who require *hatafat dam bris* do so because they are certain the foreskin is present. On this view, one would presumably be required to perform *hatafat dam bris* even on *Shabbat*, since it constitutes a definite act of *bris milah*.

### R. Elizer Hakappar

*Rav Yosef* brings a proof from a *baraisa* attributed to *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar*. That *baraisa* states: *lo nechelku Beis Shammai uVeis Hillel al nolad keshu mahul shetzarich lehatif mimenu dam bris* — both houses agree one must perform *hatafat dam bris* for a child born without a foreskin. What do they debate? *Al l'challel* — whether it overrides *Shabbat*. *Beis Shammai* holds it does override *Shabbat*; *Beis Hillel* holds it does not.

*Rav Yosef*'s inference: since *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* presents the debate as being about whether *hatafat dam bris* overrides *Shabbat*, and *Shmuel* rules like *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* who holds both houses agree you perform *hatafat dam bris*, one might expect the *tanna kamma* — who presents the debate as whether you perform *hatafat dam bris* at all — to hold that when you do perform it, you would certainly override *Shabbat*, since that would be a less contentious position than the *machloket* recorded by *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar*. This supports *Rav Yosef*'s view that the position requiring *hatafat dam bris* does so because the buried foreskin is a certainty, sufficient to override *Shabbat*.

### Tanna Kamma Analysis

The *Gemara* pushes back: *v'dilma* — perhaps the opposite is true. Perhaps the *tanna kamma* holds that according to everyone one does *not* override *Shabbat*, and it is *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* who records that *Beis Shammai* would override it. If so, *Rav Yosef* cannot derive from the *tanna kamma*'s position that *Shabbat* would be overridden.

The *Gemara* responds: *im ken* — if that is true, if the *tanna kamma* already holds that everyone agrees one does not override *Shabbat*, then what purpose does *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* serve? *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar taama d'Beis Shammai ata l'ashmian?* He would be coming only to tell us that *Beis Shammai* holds one does override *Shabbat* — but we do not rule like *Beis Shammai*! According to *Beis Hillel* nobody overrides *Shabbat*, and according to the *tanna kamma* nobody overrides *Shabbat* — so *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* adds nothing of practical significance.

### Weekday vs. Shabbat

The *Gemara* resolves this with a reframing: *v'dilma hachi ka'amar* — perhaps *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* is coming to say something qualitatively different. The *tanna kamma* presents the debate as being about whether one performs *hatafat dam bris* even on a *weekday*. *Rabbi Eliezer Hakappar* comes and says: *lo nechelku Beis Shammai uVeis Hillel b'davar zeh* — they never debated the weekday at all; on a weekday everyone agrees one performs *hatafat dam bris*. Their debate is exclusively about whether it overrides *Shabbat*.

So the *Gemara* now has two fully distinct versions of the dispute. In the first version, *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* debate whether *hatafat dam bris* is required at all for a child born without a foreskin. In the second version, both houses agree it is required, and they debate only whether it overrides *Shabbat*. *Shmuel* follows *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, and the question of whether *Shmuel* extends his ruling to *Shabbat* remains unresolved — resolved only by the clarification from *Rav Nachman* that *Shmuel* spoke only of weekdays.

## Philosophy of Dam Brit

### Against Hatafat

The *Gemara*'s analysis yields a clear spectrum of positions regarding *hatafat dam bris* for a child born without a foreskin, and each reflects a distinct philosophical understanding of what *bris milah* requires.

The most restrictive position — that no *hatafat dam bris* is required, not on a weekday and certainly not on *Shabbat* — follows most naturally from viewing *bris milah* as entirely result-oriented. The *mitzvah* is to remove the foreskin; if no foreskin is present there is nothing to do. One might grant the theoretical concept of *orlah kevushah*, a buried foreskin, but if that tissue is not covering the *ever* it is not functioning as a foreskin, and there is no obligation to address it. This position does not require one to deny that blood plays a role in *bris milah* — it simply holds that without a foreskin there is no occasion for that role to manifest.

### For Hatafat

The position requiring *hatafat dam bris* on a weekday holds that even when the foreskin is not covering the organ, there remains a ritual act to be performed. This necessarily requires positing *orlah kevushah* — some buried foreskin tissue that the extraction of blood addresses. On this view, the *mitzvah* of *bris milah* is not purely about achieving the end result of an uncovered *ever*, but involves a ritual act in relation to a foreskin even when that foreskin is not prominently present.

### Shabbat Override

For that act to override *Shabbat*, however, requires an additional step: not only must there be a buried foreskin, but its existence must be certain — *vadai orlah kevushah* — and the act of extracting blood upon it must be sufficiently constitutive of the *mitzvah* of *bris milah* that it bears the same *Shabbat*-overriding force as ordinary circumcision. This is the more tenuous position, endorsed perhaps only by *Beis Shammai* in one version of the dispute.

### Modern Applications

These questions have very significant practical implications in contemporary *halakhah*. When a circumcision is performed in a hospital before the eighth day, or when a *bris milah* is performed under conditions that may not have been *halachically* valid, does *hatafat dam bris* have any meaning or function? Some hold there is no value to it at all — the act of *bris milah* requires an actual foreskin, and absent even the possibility of *orlah kevushah*, *hatafat dam bris* accomplishes nothing. Others hold that *hatafat dam bris* does carry independent significance — that it represents a ritual act of *bris milah* in its own right, even when the foreskin has been surgically removed and the concept of *orlah kevushah* does not apply. These are positions that go to the heart of how the *mitzvah* is conceptually structured.

### Convert Exception

One case is unambiguous: a male convert always requires *hatafat dam bris*, regardless of one's general position on the matter. The *Gemara* itself indicates that the case of a convert who has already been circumcised is different — there, without *orlah kevushah* as a possibility, it would seem no act is required. Yet *Tosafos* cites the *Halachos Gedolos*, who draws on *sugyos* in *Yevamos*, to conclude that a convert does definitively require *hatafat dam bris*. And this is indeed the ruling we follow. The reason appears to be that by a convert, the act of *bris milah* is itself an integral component of the conversion process — *geirus* requires the act, not merely the result. Hence unlike the case of an infant, where one might argue the entire purpose is to achieve the absence of a foreskin, by a convert there is an independent requirement for the act of *bris milah* as part of *geirus*, and *hatafat dam bris* satisfies that requirement regardless of whether a foreskin remains.

## Maternal Impurity Rule

### Rabbi Assi - Tumat Leida

The *Gemara* now introduces an important *halakhah* from *Rabbi Assi*: *kol she'imo teme'ah leida nimol lishmona* — whenever the mother undergoes the impurity of childbirth, the child is circumcised on the eighth day. *V'chol she'ein imo teme'ah leida ein nimol lishmona* — if the mother did not undergo *tumat leida*, the child does not have an eighth-day *bris milah*. This *halakhah* is derived from the conjunction of verses in *Parshat Tazria*: *isha ki tazria v'yalda zachar v'tam'ah shivat yamim* — "when a woman bears seed and gives birth to a male she shall be impure for seven days" — followed immediately by *uvayom hashmini yimol besar arlaso* — "and on the eighth day the flesh of his *arlah* shall be circumcised." The juxtaposition teaches that the eighth-day requirement is conditioned on the mother having undergone *tumat leida*.

### C-Section Exception

The most practically significant application of this principle is the *yotzei dofen* — the child born by caesarean section. The verse specifies *ki tazria v'yalda*, linking the birth to the place from which seed enters; accordingly, the *Gemara* learns that *tumat leida* applies only when the birth occurs through the natural birth canal. A birth by C-section does not generate *tumat leida* in the mother. The consequence, according to *Rabbi Assi*'s principle, is that a child born by C-section is not subject to the eighth-day requirement, and therefore cannot override *Shabbat*.

This is in fact the *halakhah* as we rule it. Given the high incidence of caesarean births today, this is a critically important *halakhah* for families, rabbis, and *mohalim* to know. A child born by C-section does not have its *bris milah* performed on *Shabbat*.

### Abaye Objection

*Abaye* raises a powerful objection: *dorot harishonim yokhichu* — the early generations prove otherwise. Consider *Avraham* and *Yitzchak*: *Sarah* did not undergo *tumat leida* — that category had not yet been legislated at *Har Sinai* — and yet *Yitzchak* had his *bris milah* on the eighth day, as the Torah records explicitly. This demonstrates that the eighth-day *bris milah* has nothing to do with *tumat leida*, and the connection *Rabbi Assi* draws cannot be correct.

### Torah Changed Law

The *Gemara* responds: *nitna Torah v'nitchadsha halakhah* — when the Torah was given, the *halakhah* changed. *Avraham*'s *bris milah* was given as a *mitzvah* with eighth-day timing regardless of any other factor. But with the giving of *Parshat Tazria* at Sinai, a new *halakhah* came into being, conditioning the eighth-day requirement on the mother's *tumat leida*. *Avraham* and *Yitzchak* are therefore not a proof — the regime governing *bris milah* before *matan Torah* was different from that which applies now.

The *Gemara* then notes a further complication, citing a dispute between *Rav Huna* and *Rav Chiyya bar Rav* regarding a *yotzei dofen* and a child born with two foreskins — whether one overrides *Shabbat* for such children. The *Gemara* clarifies that the debate is solely about overriding *Shabbat* — *aval lishmona vadai mahalinan lei* — but no one disputes that the eighth day is at least the appropriate time for the *bris milah*. *Abaye* responds that in his view the two questions are linked — *ha baha talya* — one is dependent on the other: the reason the *bris milah* does not override *Shabbat* is precisely because there is no obligation to perform it on the eighth day. Once the child is no longer in the *parsha* of the eighth day, the override of *Shabbat* falls away as a natural consequence.

## Slaves & Servants

### Baraita Four Cases

The *Gemara* now grounds this debate in a *baraisa* that applies the question of eighth-day *bris milah* to the two categories of slaves mentioned in the Torah in connection with *Avraham*'s household. The Torah twice commands that *bris milah* be performed on *yelid bayit* — children born in the household, i.e., born to female slaves already owned — and on *miknat kesef* — children purchased with money. In one instance the eighth day is specified; in the other it is not. The *baraisa* draws out from this that both categories admit of two sub-cases: a *yelid bayit* circumcised on day one, a *yelid bayit* circumcised on day eight, a *miknat kesef* circumcised on day one, and a *miknat kesef* circumcised on day eight. This chiastic structure frames the entire discussion.

### Yeled Bayit

*Yesh yelid bayit she-nimol lishmonah* — what is the case of a *yelid bayit* circumcised on day eight? *Lakach shifchah v'nitabra etzlo v'yaldah* — you purchased a female slave who was not pregnant, she conceived in your household and gave birth there. This is the paradigmatic case: the child was conceived and born to a mother who was in your household throughout, analogous in structure to a standard birth, and so the eighth day applies.

### Miknei Kesef

For *miknat kesef*, the case of day eight is: *lakach shifchah me'uberes* — you purchased a pregnant female slave. By purchasing the pregnant slave you simultaneously acquire the fetus, so it is a *miknat kesef* — a direct purchase of the infant. *V'hitvila v'achar kach yaldah* — and she went to the *mikveh* becoming quasi-Jewish, and then gave birth. The eighth day applies.

The case of *miknat kesef* on day one is simpler: *lakach shifchah u'vlada imah* — you purchased a slave together with her already-born infant. The infant was born before it entered your possession, so it never had a birth within your household or your ownership. That is a *miknat kesef* circumcised on day one.

### Rav Chama's View

*Rav Chama* provides the key to generating all four cases. He holds that the factor determining whether the eighth day applies is whether the slave woman had immersed in the *mikveh* before giving birth — whether she had attained quasi-Jewish status and thereby incurred *tumat leida*. *Yaldah v'achar kach hitvilah* — if she gave birth and only afterwards went to the *mikveh*, she was not quasi-Jewish at the time of birth, had no *tumat leida*, and the child is circumcised on day one — even though it is a *yelid bayit*, conceived and born in your household. *Hitvilah v'achar kach yaldah* — if she immersed and then gave birth, she had *tumat leida* and the child is circumcised on day eight.

According to *Rav Chama*, this is how you generate the case of *yelid bayit* on day one: the child was conceived and born in your household, but the mother had not yet immersed, and therefore there was no *tumat leida*. Similarly for *miknat kesef* on day eight: the mother immersed before giving birth, generating *tumat leida*. The logic follows *Rabbi Assi*'s principle directly: *tumat leida* is the determining factor.

### Tanna Kamma Disagrees

The *tanna kamma* of the *baraisa*, however, does not share *Rav Chama*'s view. *V'Tanna Kamma lo shani lei bein hitvilah v'achar kach yaldah bein yaldah v'achar kach hitvilah* — the *tanna kamma* draws no distinction based on whether the slave immersed before or after birth. *D'af al gav d'eina mitama'ah leida* — even if the mother has no *tumat leida*, the child may still be circumcised on the eighth day. The *tanna kamma* holds that the eighth-day requirement for these slave children is based on the nature of their incorporation into the Jewish household, not on the mother's quasi-Jewish status or *tumat leida*.

### Detailed Analysis

The *Gemara* now examines how each position generates all four permutations of the *baraisa*. According to *Rav Chama*, the cases flow naturally from the *mikveh* status of the mother. *Yelid bayit* on day eight: mother immersed before birth, has *tumat leida*, eighth day. *Yelid bayit* on day one: mother gave birth before immersing, no *tumat leida*, day one. For *miknat kesef* on day eight: *lakach shifchah uveres v'hitvilah v'achar kach yaldah* — purchased pregnant, she immersed, then gave birth. For *miknat kesef* on day one: the infant was purchased after birth — obvious case.

*Rav Chama* adds a further nuance for *miknat kesef* on day one: even when the mother has *tumat leida*, it is possible to have a *miknat kesef* that is day one — *k'gon shelakach zeh shifchah v'zeh ubar* — where one person purchased the mother and another person purchased the fetus. The mother may be quasi-Jewish with *tumat leida*, but the fetus was separately purchased and its owner did not acquire the mother. Since the owner of the fetus does not own the mother, the birth did not take place in his household in any meaningful sense, and therefore it is day one.

For the *tanna kamma*, generating *yelid bayit* on day one is more challenging. Since the *tanna kamma* holds that *tumat leida* is irrelevant, a child conceived and born in your household should always be day eight. The *Gemara* proposes *Rabbi Yirmiah*'s answer: *belokeach shifchah le'ubrah* — a case where you purchased the female slave specifically for the rights to her offspring, not for herself. You own the fetus but not the mother directly. This is analogous to the earlier case where two people purchased separately — you own the fetus, but the mother is not truly a member of your household, and therefore the birth does not carry the character of *yelid bayit* that generates the eighth-day requirement.

The *Gemara* notes that this resolution depends on the principle that *kinyan peiros lav kekinyan haguf* — owning something for its produce is not equivalent to owning the thing itself. If one holds *kinyan peiros kekinyan haguf dami* — that ownership of the produce constitutes ownership of the object — then even in *Rabbi Yirmiah*'s case you effectively own the mother, and the birth is fully *yelid bayit*. On that view, it becomes very difficult for the *tanna kamma* to generate a case of *yelid bayit* on day one.

### Special Condition

*Rav Mesharshaya* provides a solution for this impasse: *belokeach shifchah al menat shelo lehatbilah* — a case where you purchased this slave woman on the explicit condition that she would never be brought to the *mikveh*, that she would remain permanently and entirely non-Jewish with no quasi-Jewish status whatsoever. In such a case, even if you own her fully, she is never fully incorporated into the Jewish household in the relevant sense, and therefore a child born to her — even conceived in your house, even with you as the full owner — is circumcised on day one rather than day eight.

## C-Section Halacha

### Unresolved Debate

The *Gemara*'s analysis makes clear that the question of whether *tumat leida* determines the eighth-day obligation — and hence whether a C-section child's *bris milah* overrides *Shabbat* — is a genuine *machloket tannaim*. *Rabbi Assi* holds that *tumat leida* is the determining factor; the *tanna kamma* of the *baraisa* holds that it is not. Practically, this leaves the *posek* without a clear ruling.

### Rosh's Solution

The *Rosh* addresses this directly. Given the unresolved state of the debate, he rules as follows: the child born by C-section should have his *bris milah* on the eighth day — because it is possible that the eighth-day requirement does apply, and one cannot perform the *bris milah* earlier without risking having violated the *mitzvah*. On the other hand, the *bris milah* does not override *Shabbat* — because it is equally possible that the child follows the ruling of *Rabbi Assi*, is not subject to the eighth-day requirement, and therefore has no license to override *Shabbat*. The *Halachos Gedolos* similarly expresses uncertainty about how to rule. The practical outcome of the *safek* is thus: eighth day, but not on *Shabbat*.

### Modern Practice

This ruling is directly relevant today, given the frequency of caesarean births. It is critical that families inform the rabbi or *mohel* when a birth was by C-section, and any competent *mohel* should know to ask this question. The consequence is clear and halachically significant: no matter when the eighth day falls, if the child was delivered by C-section the *bris milah* is not performed on *Shabbat*.

## Viability & Non-Viable

### Thirty Day Rule

A *baraisa* is cited: *tanya Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer — kol sheshaha sheloshim yom be'adam eino nefel* — any infant that survives thirty days is no longer considered a *nefel*, a potentially non-viable birth. Before thirty days there remains uncertainty about the infant's viability. The verse *ufduyav miben chodesh tifdeh* — "its redemption from one month you shall redeem" — is cited as support: the Torah designates one month as the threshold of established viability.

### Eight Days Animals

By contrast, for animals the threshold is eight days: *shemonat yamim bebehemah eino nefel* — after eight days an animal is considered viable. The source is *miyom hashemini vahaleich yeira'eh lekorban* — it may be brought as a *korban* from the eighth day onward, implying that survival to eight days establishes viability.

### Viability Contradiction

This ruling generates a sharp question. If an infant younger than thirty days is not yet established as viable — *halo she'sha feika havei* — then on what basis do we perform *bris milah* on *Shabbat* for an eight-day-old infant? The infant has not yet reached the threshold of established viability. Perhaps this child will not survive and is in fact a *nefel* — in which case there would be no license to override *Shabbat*, and performing *bris milah* would be a prohibited act.

### Rav Adda Resolution

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* resolves the question with a *mimah nafshach* argument: *mimalin oto mimah nafshach* — one performs the *bris milah* in either case, because either way it is permissible.

### Two Outcomes

*Im chai hu* — if the child is viable, then *shapir kamaheil* — performing *bris milah* on *Shabbat* is entirely warranted, since a viable infant's eighth-day *bris milah* does indeed override *Shabbat*. *V'im lav — mechateich bivasar hu* — and if the child is not viable, then the act of cutting is not a *Shabbat* violation at all, since drawing blood from something not considered alive does not constitute the *melacha* of making a wound; it is comparable to cutting into a piece of meat. This is a strikingly harsh formulation — and it parallels the *Gemara*'s earlier statement about the eighth-month infant being treated *ke'even*, like a stone. One might have expected that even a potentially non-viable infant should retain some status as a living being, making any act upon it at least arguably prohibited. Yet the *Gemara* presses the logic to its conclusion: a *nefel* has no *chiyuv* of *bris milah*, and acts performed upon it do not carry the prohibited character of acts performed upon the living. The *Gemara* will continue to explore the implications of this position on the following *daf*.
