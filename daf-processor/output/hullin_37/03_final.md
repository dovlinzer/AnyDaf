# Hullin 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Signs of Life

### Transition to Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַמְסוּכֶּנֶת, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתְּפַרְכֵּס בַּיָּד וּבָרֶגֶל. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ אִם זִינְּקָה. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בַּלַּיְלָה, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא כְּתָלִים מְלֵאִים דָּם – כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁזִּינְּקָה, וּכְמִדַּת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁתְּפַרְכֵּס אוֹ בַיָּד אוֹ בָרֶגֶל, אוֹ עַד שֶׁתְּכַשְׁכֵּשׁ בִּזְנָבָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal **that is in danger** of imminent death, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** The slaughter is valid **only** in a case **where** after the slaughter **it convulses with its foreleg and with its hind leg. Rabbi Eliezer says: It is sufficient if** blood **spurted** from the neck. **Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of **one who slaughters at night and the next day he awoke and found walls full of blood,** the slaughter is **valid,** as it is clear **that** the blood **spurted, and** this is **in accordance with the rule of Rabbi Eliezer. And the Rabbis say:** It is valid **only** in a case **where it convulses with its foreleg or with its hind leg, or** in a case **where it wags its tail.**

The daf begins at 37a, at the top of the page, with the *mishnah*. Having set aside the discussions of *taharot* and *zera'im*, we now return to the laws of *shechita*. This particular *mishnah* is remarkable precisely because of how the *gemara* uses it as a springboard into fundamental conceptual and halachic distinctions between *treifah*, *neveilah*, and the *mesukenet* — an animal that is moribund, on the verge of death.

### R. Shimon ben Gamliel


The *mishnah* opens: *hashocheit hamisukenet* — one who performs *shechita* on an animal that is in danger of imminent death, not due to injury but due to old age or illness. The concern is not exotic; it is straightforward. Because the animal was about to die anyway, one must verify that it did not in fact expire a moment before *shechita* was completed — that the act of *shechita* actually terminated the animal's life rather than finding it already dead. If the animal simply dropped dead during the process, the resulting carcass is a *neveilah*, not a properly slaughtered animal.

To address this concern, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that one must observe the animal thrashing — *ad shetiparkes beyad uveragel* — with both a foreleg and a hind leg. This vigorous movement demonstrates that sufficient life force remained in the animal even as *shechita* concluded. A normal, healthy animal requires no such demonstration, but this animal, presumptively on death's doorstep, must show that *shechita* — and not natural death — was what ended its life.

### R. Eliezer: Blood


Rabbi Eliezer takes a different approach: *dayah im zinkah* — it is sufficient if the blood spurted forth. The forceful ejection of blood is itself evidence of enough residual vitality at the moment of *shechita*.

### R. Shimon: Night Case


Rabbi Shimon then presents a practical application of Rabbi Eliezer's position: if one performed *shechita* at night on such an animal and could not observe any movement, and arose in the morning to find the walls splattered with blood — *kosalim mele'im dam* — the animal is *kosherah*, because the blood-spatter proves retroactively that the blood spurted. The *gemara* will clarify that *kosalim* here refers not to the walls of the barn or house but to the *koslei beit hashechitah* — the immediate walls of the point of slaughter, that is, the flesh at the site of the cut. This is admittedly a very low threshold, and whether it reflects the straightforward reading of the *mishnah* is debatable. But that is Rabbi Shimon's interpretation, working from Rabbi Eliezer's criterion.

One might ask: why would anyone *shecht* a dying animal at all? The answer is economic. A two-thousand-pound animal on the verge of death represents a substantial loss. If it dies unslaughtered, the carcass is a *neveilah* and cannot be sold to Jews as kosher meat. If it is slaughtered in time, the meat may be sold normally. The incentive to *shecht* is therefore very real — particularly for a family with only a few animals, one of which is failing.

### Rabbis: Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה וְאֶחָד בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה, בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְלֹא הֶחְזִירָה – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֶלָּא הוֹצָאַת נֶפֶשׁ בִּלְבָד. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁהָיְתָה בְּחֶזְקַת מְסוּכֶּנֶת, אֲבָל אִם הָיְתָה בְּחֶזְקַת בְּרִיאָה, אֲפִילּוּ אֵין בָּהּ אֶחָד מִכׇּל הַסִּימָנִים הַלָּלוּ – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is the *halakha* with regard to **both a small animal,** e.g., a sheep, **and a large animal,** e.g., a cow, that is in danger of imminent death. The slaughter of **a small animal that** when being slaughtered **extended its foreleg** that was bent **and did not restore** it to the bent position **is not valid, as** extending the foreleg **is only** part of the natural course of **removal** of the animal's **soul** from its body and not a convulsion indicating life. **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is in a case **where the presumptive status** of the animal **was** that it was **in danger** of imminent death. **But if its presumptive status was** that it was **healthy,** then **even if there were none of these indicators,** the slaughter is **valid.**

The Sages (the majority position, *chachamim*) disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and adopt a more lenient threshold. They require only *o beyad o beragel* — either a foreleg or a hind leg, not both. They further add the option of the animal moving its tail: *o ad shetichashkesh biznavah*. This standard applies equally to small livestock (*behemah dakkah*) such as sheep or goats, and to large livestock (*behemah gasah*) such as cattle.

### Leg Extension Excluded


However, the *mishnah* qualifies the Sages' position with an important exclusion. If a small animal merely extended its foreleg but did not pull it back — *pashtah yadah v'lo hechzirah* — this does not constitute valid movement. Such extension, the *mishnah* explains, is *she'einah ela hotza'at nefesh bilvad* — it is nothing more than the body's natural reflex at the moment of dying. The criterion for a *mesukenet* is not any motion whatsoever, but motion that evidences genuine remaining life force — something that distinguishes purposive movement from a mere death spasm.

### Signs Req'd: Moribund


The *mishnah* concludes with an important limitation: *bameh devarim amurim* — when do all these requirements apply? Only *shehayitah bechezkat mesukenet* — when the animal was presumptively moribund. But if the animal was in presumptive good health (*bechezkat beriah*), then even if none of these signs of movement appeared, the *shechita* is valid. The entire apparatus of required movement is a function of the specific concern that the animal might have pre-deceased the *shechita*. Where that concern does not arise, the requirements do not apply.

## Three Categories

### Why Permit Mesukenet?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְסוּכֶּנֶת מִמַּאי דְּשַׁרְיָא? וּמִמַּאי תִּיסַּק אַדַּעְתִּין דַּאֲסִירָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת הַחַיָּה אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכְלוּ״, חַיָּה אֱכוֹל, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ חַיָּה לֹא תֹּאכַל. וְהָא מְסוּכֶּנֶת אֵינָהּ חַיָּה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where** is it known **that** the flesh of an animal **in danger** of imminent death **is permitted** by means of slaughter? The Gemara responds with a question: **And from where would it enter your mind that it is prohibited?** The Gemara explains that one may have thought it is prohibited, **as it is written: "These are the living beings [*haḥayya*] that you may eat** among all the animals that are on the earth" (Leviticus 11:2). One might have thought that the verse is saying: **Eat** an animal that is fit to **live [*ḥayya*], but do not eat** an animal **that is not** fit to **live. And this** animal **in danger** of imminent death **is not** fit to **live.**

Rather than immediately analyzing which of the various movement criteria is the most adequate sign of life, the *gemara* pivots to address the larger conceptual question underlying the *mishnah*: why is *shechita* of a *mesukenet* permitted at all? The *mishnah* proceeds on the assumption that it is *kosher* — but this requires justification.

The *gemara* presents the potential source of prohibition: the verse *zot hachayah asher tochlu* — "this is the animal that you shall eat." The implication drawn is *chayah echol, she'einah chayah lo tochal* — you may eat a living animal, but not one that cannot live. And a *mesukenet*, being on the verge of death, is not in a position to live. This is in fact the same verse that underlies one of the primary debates about *treifah* — whether a *treifah* is a formal halachic category defined by *halachah l'moshe miSinai*, or whether its basis is the empirical prediction that such an animal will not survive the year. The camp holding *treifah einah chayah* — that the defining problem of *treifah* is that the animal will not survive — grounds that position precisely in this verse. The *gemara* now asks: should not the same logic apply to render a *mesukenet* forbidden?

The *gemara* frames three interrelated categories whose conceptual boundaries must be defined: *neveilah* — an animal that dies without *shechita*; *treifah* — an animal slaughtered but bearing a fatal internal injury; and *mesukenet* — an animal about to die without any specific injury. The *mesukenet* borders on *neveilah* because *neveilah* is what it will soon become. It also borders on *treifah* because both involve an animal that will not survive long. The *gemara* must therefore demonstrate that *mesukenet* is genuinely distinct from both and that its *shechita* is halachically efficacious.

### Neveilah Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נְבֵלָה לֹא תֹּאכֵל, מִכְּלָל דִּמְסוּכֶּנֶת שַׁרְיָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מְסוּכֶּנֶת אֲסִירָא – הַשְׁתָּא מֵחַיִּים אֲסִירָא, לְאַחַר מִיתָה מִיבְּעֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The fact that its meat is permitted is derived **from the fact that the Merciful One states that you shall not eat an unslaughtered animal carcass,** as it is written: "You shall not eat any unslaughtered carcass" (Deuteronomy 14:21); one learns **by inference** that eating the meat of an animal **in danger** of imminent death **is permitted. As, if it enters your mind** that eating the meat of an animal **in danger** of imminent death **is prohibited, now** if an animal is **prohibited while alive,** is it **necessary** to state that it is prohibited **after death?**

The *gemara* advances its first proof: *neveilah lo tochal* — you may not eat a *neveilah*. From this prohibition, we may infer — *miklal demesukenet sharya* — that anything short of the animal having actually died remains permissible. Were a *mesukenet* itself already forbidden, the verse prohibiting *neveilah* would be superfluous: *de'i salka da'atach mesukenet asira, hashta mechayim asira — le'achar mitah mibaya?* If even when alive it is forbidden, why would the Torah need to reiterate its prohibition once it has died? The very necessity of explicitly forbidding *neveilah* demonstrates that the prohibition begins only at the point of actual death.

The *gemara* here operates on the assumption that the animal follows a natural trajectory from illness or old age into death. An animal becomes *mesukenet*, then eventually dies and becomes a *neveilah*. If *mesukenet* were forbidden, *neveilah* adds nothing new to the already-prohibited continuum.

### Mesukenet-Neveilah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הַיְינוּ נְבֵלָה הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר הִיא לָכֶם לְאׇכְלָה הַנֹּגֵעַ בְּנִבְלָתָהּ״, לְאַחַר מִיתָה הוּא דְּקַרְיַיהּ רַחֲמָנָא נְבֵלָה, מֵחַיִּים לָא אִקְּרַי נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof. **And** that is not a legitimate inference, as **perhaps** the halakhic status of **an unslaughtered carcass is** the same as **that of** an animal **in danger** of imminent death, and the prohibition: "You shall not eat any unslaughtered carcass," includes both. The Gemara answers: **It would not enter your mind** to say so, **as it is written: "And if any animal of which you may eat dies, one who touches its carcass** shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:39). This indicates that **it is after death that the Merciful One calls** the animal **a carcass; while alive,** the animal **is not called a carcass.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning. Perhaps *neveilah* and *mesukenet* are the same category — that is, the Torah's prohibition of *neveilah* applies from the moment the animal is moribund. Like a dead man walking, the animal is already considered a *neveilah* from the point it becomes *mesukenet*.

The *gemara* dismisses this: *lo salka da'atach* — that cannot be correct. The verse states *vechi yamut min habehemah asher hi lachem le'ochlah, hanogea benivlatah* — when the animal you may eat dies, one who touches its *neveilah* becomes impure. *le'achar mitah hu dikra rachmana neveilah* — the Torah designates the animal *neveilah* only after it has died. *mechayim lo ikri neveilah* — while it is still alive, it cannot be called a *neveilah*.

The *gemara* acknowledges that there are other Talmudic discussions — such as an animal cut in half — where the *neveilah* designation is more complicated, and such cases touch on the broader question of what constitutes halachic death. But for the present purposes, the *gemara* establishes clearly: *neveilah* requires actual death, not merely impending death.

### Treifah Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: הַיְינוּ נְבֵלָה הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת, מֵחַיִּים – בַּעֲשֵׂה, לְאַחַר מִיתָה – בְּלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions that understanding. **And perhaps, actually I will say to you:** The halakhic status of **an unslaughtered carcass is** the same as **that of** an animal **in danger** of imminent death, but if one slaughters the animal in danger of imminent death **while alive,** he would be **in** violation of **a positive** mitzva: "These are the living beings that you may eat" (Leviticus 11:2), whereas **after** its **death,** he would be **in** violation of **a prohibition:** "You shall not eat any unslaughtered carcass" (Deuteronomy 14:21).

The *gemara* then proposes a new challenge. Perhaps the proof should come not from *neveilah* but from *treifah*: since *treifah* is forbidden — *mide'amar Rachmana treifah lo sochal* — we can infer that a *mesukenet* must be permissible, for if even an animal about to die without injury were forbidden, the prohibition of *treifah* would be redundant. A *treifah* is essentially a *mesukenet* with an injury — a *mesukenet* plus. If being moribund alone triggers the prohibition, what does the specific prohibition of *treifah* add?

The *gemara* anticipates the rejoinder: perhaps there is no redundancy, because *mesukenet* and *treifah* carry different levels of prohibition. *mesukenet*, on this reading, is forbidden by a positive commandment — the *aseh* of *zot hachayah asher tochlu*, "eat only what can live" — while *treifah* triggers an additional negative prohibition (*lav*). Similarly, if *mesukenet* is forbidden by both a *lav* and an *aseh* simultaneously, then *neveilah* imposes yet another layer of prohibition, so that an animal which began as *mesukenet*, became *treifah*, and then died would violate two *lavin* and an *aseh*.

Despite the ingenuity of this construction, the *gemara* continues pressing: if *treifah* and *mesukenet* both add prohibitions atop a baseline of being moribund, why does the Torah need to separately forbid *neveilah*? By the time the animal dies, it already carries multiple prohibitions — what does the *neveilah* prohibition add that isn't already present? The *gemara*'s answer is that by that logic, all three categories collapse into one animal passing through successive stages of a single continuum, each adding more prohibition. But then there is no reason to posit any independent conceptual category at all. The *gemara* has not yet fully resolved this, and the proof must come from elsewhere.

## Fats Verse Proof

### Verse: Two Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹּאכַל – מִכְּלָל דִּמְסוּכֶּנֶת שַׁרְיָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מְסוּכֶּנֶת אֲסִירָא, הַשְׁתָּא מְסוּכֶּנֶת דְּלָא מְחַסְּרָא אֲסִירָא, טְרֵפָה מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the fact that its meat is permitted is derived **from the fact that the Merciful One states you shall not eat that an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** as it is written: "And you shall not eat any animal that was mauled in the field [*tereifa*]" (Exodus 22:30). From here, one learns **by inference** that eating the meat of an animal **in danger** of imminent death **is permitted. As, if it enters your mind** that eating the meat of an animal **in danger** of imminent death **is prohibited, now** that an animal **in danger** of imminent death **that is not lacking** any limb **is prohibited,** is it **necessary** to state that **a *tereifa*,** an animal that was mauled and lacking body parts, is prohibited?

The *gemara* introduces the decisive proof from the following verse (*vayikra* 7:24): *v'cheilev neveilah v'cheilev treifah ye'aseh l'chol melachah v'achol lo tocheluhu* — "the fat of a *neveilah* and the fat of a *treifah* may be used for any labor, but you shall not eat it." On the surface, this verse serves two purposes found elsewhere in the *gemara*. First, it establishes that although the fat of a *neveilah* or *treifah* is forbidden for consumption, it may be used for benefit — the classic halachic principle of *asur b'achilah u'mutar b'hana'ah*. Second, by permitting the fat "for any labor," the verse implies that even labor involving sanctified objects is permitted, which indicates that the fat of a *neveilah* does not impart *tumah*.

Here, however, the *gemara* employs the verse for a third, distinct teaching.

### Issur Chal Al Issur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הַיְינוּ טְרֵפָה הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה! אִם כֵּן, נְבֵלָה דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? וּמָה מֵחַיִּים קָאֵי עֲלַהּ בְּלָאו וַעֲשֵׂה, לְאַחַר מִיתָה מִיבְּעִיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof. **And** that is not a legitimate inference, as **perhaps** the halakhic status of **a *tereifa*** that is lacking body parts **is** the same as that of an animal **in danger** of imminent death that is not lacking body parts, and both are included in the category of *tereifa*. This would render one who slaughters either **to** be **in violation** of both **a positive** mitzva: "These are the living beings that you may eat," **and a prohibition:** "And you shall not eat any animal that was mauled in the field [*tereifa*]." The Gemara questions that understanding: **If so, why do I** need the prohibition **that the Merciful One writes** with regard to **an unslaughtered carcass? If while** an animal is **alive one stands in** violation of **a prohibition and a positive** mitzva, **is** it **necessary** to state that it is prohibited **after death?**

*L'mai hilchata amrah Torah* — for what halachic purpose did the Torah state this verse? The *gemara* teaches: *yavo issur neveilah v'yachol al issur cheilev* — the prohibition of *neveilah* can take effect on top of the pre-existing prohibition of *cheilev*. The fat (*cheilev*) of an animal is forbidden from the moment the animal is born; the fat is already an *issur cheilev* in the living animal. When the animal subsequently dies — becoming a *neveilah* — or sustains a fatal injury — becoming a *treifah* — a second prohibition attaches to that same substance.

This directly engages the halachic principle of *issur chal al issur* — whether a new prohibition can take hold where one already exists. The verse is teaching that it can: *yavo issur treifah v'yachol al issur cheilev* — the prohibition of *treifah* takes effect on top of the pre-existing prohibition of *cheilev*.

### Application: Mesukenet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״חֵלֶב נְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה״, וּמִדִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״נְבֵלָה״, מִכְּלָל דִּטְרֵפָה לָאו הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather, from the fact that the Merciful One writes** that the prohibition against eating **an unslaughtered carcass** takes effect upon the prohibition against eating forbidden fat, one learns **by inference that** the ***tereifa*** in the verse **is not the same as** an animal **in danger** of imminent death. Rather, the *tereifa* in the verse is an animal that was mauled and is lacking body parts, and it is only that animal that is prohibited after slaughter. Eating an animal in danger of imminent death after slaughter is permitted.

The *gemara* now applies this to *mesukenet*. *v'ei salka da'atach treifah hainu mesukenet*, if you maintain that *treifah* and *mesukenet* are fundamentally the same category, that any animal doomed to die, whether by injury or otherwise, is captured under the same prohibition, then the verse need only have said: the fat of a *neveilah* may be used for all labor — and from there I would have constructed a *kal vachomer*: if the prohibition of *treifah* applies to *cheilev* even while the animal is alive, then certainly, as the situation worsens and the animal actually dies, the prohibition of *neveilah* should similarly take effect. There would have been no need to state both *cheilev neveilah* and *cheilev treifah* explicitly. The fact that both are stated — that the verse must separately address the fat of a *neveilah* and the fat of a *treifah* — demonstrates that they are distinct categories that cannot be inferred from one another. And the only way they remain genuinely distinct is if *mesukenet*, the shared background condition of being moribund, is removed from the equation as an independent prohibition.

## Three Categories (II)

### Rashi: Continuum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הַיְינוּ נְבֵלָה, הַיְינוּ טְרֵפָה, הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין וַעֲשֵׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And** that is not a legitimate question, as **perhaps** the halakhic status of **an unslaughtered carcass is** the same as **a *tereifa*** that is lacking body parts, and **is** the same as **that of** an animal **in danger** of imminent death that is not lacking body parts. Therefore, when the Torah writes the word "carcass," it is the same as though it had written *tereifa* and the same as though it had written an animal in danger of imminent death. The Torah prohibits each, **and the result will be that he will violate two prohibitions and a positive** mitzva.

The *gemara*'s phrase that generated this entire analysis — *hainu treifah hainu mesukenet hainu neveilah*, "it is [the same as] *treifah*, it is [the same as] *mesukenet*, it is [the same as] *neveilah*" — might seem puzzling. In what sense are these three things the same? Rashi's answer here is both brilliant and deeply conceptual.

Rashi explains that the moment one introduces *mesukenet* as a distinct halachic category — one defined not by injury, not by death, but purely by the fact that the animal is in the process of dying — the entire framework shifts. *mesukenet* reframes *treifah* and *neveilah* not as independent categories but as points on a single continuum. On this reading, *treifah* is simply a *mesukenet* with an injury; *neveilah* is a *mesukenet* that has completed its trajectory and died. Everything is about being in the process of dying or having died.

Once that framing is accepted, the *kal vachomer* becomes compelling: if the prohibition of *treifah* applies to *cheilev* at an earlier stage — while the animal is still alive but moribund by virtue of its injury — then certainly the prohibition of *neveilah* should apply at the later stage, when the process has culminated in actual death. The one follows inexorably from the other, and you would never need to state the *neveilah* prohibition explicitly.

But as Rashi points out, this is precisely why the verse *must* state both: because *treifah* and *neveilah* are in reality *not* on the same continuum. *treifah* is about injury. *neveilah* is about having died without *shechita*. They are conceptually distinct tracks. The only thing that would have unified them is *mesukenet* — the idea that being-about-to-die is itself the operative concern. Remove *mesukenet* from halachic consideration, and *treifah* and *neveilah* revert to being independent categories, each requiring its own prohibition, and the *kal vachomer* collapses. The *gemara*'s conclusion, therefore, is precisely this: the fact that the Torah must address both separately is the proof that they are conceptually distinct — and that distinctness is only coherent if *mesukenet* per se is not a halachic prohibition.

## Sudden Death Case

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ הַיְינוּ טְרֵפָה הַיְינוּ מְסוּכֶּנֶת, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: ״נְבֵלָה״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְהָךְ נְבֵלָה דְּלָא אָתְיָא מִכֹּחַ מְסוּכֶּנֶת. וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ גִּיסְטְרָא. הָתָם נָמֵי, אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא הֲוַי מְסוּכֶּנֶת פּוּרְתָּא מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִיפְסֹק לְרוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi objects to this: And perhaps, actually I will say to you** that the halakhic status of **a *tereifa* is** the same as that of an animal **in danger** of imminent death, **and** with regard to **that which you said: Why do I** need the prohibition with regard to **an unslaughtered carcass that the Merciful One writes,** one can explain that it is necessary **for that unslaughtered carcass that does not come as a result of danger** of imminent death. **And what are the circumstances** of that unslaughtered carcass? It is in a case **where one rendered** the animal like **a shard,** by cutting it into two widthwise. The Gemara rejects that distinction: **There too,** in the case where one rendered the animal like a shard, it is **impossible that** the animal **was not** at least **somewhat in danger** of imminent death **before he cut the majority** of the animal.

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenges this conclusion. He suggests: *v'dilma le'olam eima lach hainu treifah hainu mesukenet* — perhaps *treifah* and *mesukenet* should indeed be treated as essentially the same category. If so, the separate prohibition of *neveilah* would be needed not because *neveilah* and *treifah* are conceptually distinct, but rather to address a case where *neveilah* arises through a path entirely outside the normal *mesukenet* trajectory.

### Cleaved Animal


Mar bar Rav Ashi illustrates: *she'asah gistera* — an animal that was cleaved in half. Such an animal died suddenly, not as the culmination of a slow decline through *mesukenet*. It is a *neveilah* that never passed through the stages of illness or infirmity. For this category — *neveilah* arising not from the *mesukenet* trajectory — the Torah needed to state the prohibition explicitly, since it cannot be inferred from *treifah*.

### Gemara Response


The *gemara* responds: *hasam nami ee efshar delo havya mesukenet purta mikamei delifskay leruba* — even in the case of sudden death, it is impossible for the animal not to have been momentarily *mesukenet* just before the lethal blow was completed. Any animal, even one apparently in full health, passes through at least a fleeting moment of being moribund in the instant before death overtakes it. Thus the "sudden death" scenario cannot serve as a genuinely independent path to *neveilah* that escapes the *mesukenet* framework.

### Drasha: Extra Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, אִם כֵּן לֵימָא ״חֵלֶב נְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה״, ״חֵלֶב״ ״חֵלֶב״ לְמָה לִי? זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאֵין חֶלְבָּהּ חָלוּק מִבְּשָׂרָהּ, וְיֵשׁ לְךָ אַחֶרֶת שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ חָלוּק מִבְּשָׂרָהּ, וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? מְסוּכֶּנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that there is a different source for the fact that the meat of an animal in danger of imminent death is permitted. **If** it is **so** that an animal in danger of imminent death is included in the category of *tereifa*, **let** the verse **say: The fat of an unslaughtered carcass and a *tereifa*. Why do I** need it to be written: **"Fat** of a carcass and the **fat** of a *tereifa*"? The term "fat" is repeated to teach that **it is this** case **where** the status of **its fat is not distinct from** the status of **its meat,** and both are prohibited; **but you have another** case **where** the status of **its fat,** which is forbidden, **is distinct from** the status of **its meat,** which is permitted. **And which** case is that? **That is** the case of an animal **in danger** of imminent death.

The *gemara* therefore offers an alternative resolution, more technical in character. The verse could have read simply *cheilev neveilah u'treifah* — the fat of *neveilah* and *treifah*. Why does it say *v'cheilev neveilah v'cheilev treifah*, with *cheilev* appearing twice? This extra word teaches: *zu hi she'ein chelba chaluk mibesarah* — for *neveilah* and *treifah*, the fat and the meat are under the same prohibition; there is no differentiation between them. But *v'yesh lecha acheret shechelba chaluk mibesarah* — there is another case where the fat and the meat are treated differently, where the fat is forbidden but the meat is permitted. *v'eizo zoh? zu mesukenet* — and that case is the *mesukenet*. The fat of a *mesukenet* is forbidden as *cheilev*, but the meat of the *mesukenet* is entirely permissible. The extra word *cheilev* in the verse is thus a technical indication that only for *neveilah* and *treifah* does the prohibition extend equally to fat and meat — implicitly carving out *mesukenet* as a case where the meat remains permitted.

This resolution, while valid, is admittedly less satisfying than the conceptual analysis offered by Rashi. The deeper insight remains Rashi's: that the entire framework hinges on whether *mesukenet* belongs to the continuum or stands apart from it, and that the *gemara*'s proof establishes it must stand apart.

## Yechezkel Passage

### Yechezkel's Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא, ״וָאֹמַר אֲהָהּ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים הִנֵּה נַפְשִׁי לֹא מְטֻמָּאָה וּנְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא אָכַלְתִּי מִנְּעוּרַי וְעַד עַתָּה וְלֹא בָא בְּפִי בְּשַׂר פִּגּוּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived **from here: "Then I said: Ah, Lord God, my soul has not become impure; and from my youth until now I have not eaten an unslaughtered carcass or a *tereifa*, and no *piggul* flesh came into my mouth"** (Ezekiel 4:14).

The *gemara* offers a further proof for the permissibility of *mesukenet* from the book of *yechezkel*. Yechezkel declares to God: *hinei nafshi lo metuma'ah, u'neveilah u'treifah lo achalti mine'urai v'ad atah, v'lo va befi basar piggul* — behold, my soul has not become impure, I have not eaten *neveilah* or *treifah* from my youth until now, and no meat of *piggul* has entered my mouth. Yechezkel was a *kohen*, and *piggul* — sacrificial meat rendered invalid by improper thought during the offering — belongs to the world of *kodshim*.

### Mesukenet Subject

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנֵּה נַפְשִׁי לֹא מְטֻמָּאָה״ – שֶׁלֹּא הִרְהַרְתִּי בַּיּוֹם לָבֹא לִידֵי טוּמְאָה בַּלַּיְלָה, ״וּנְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא אָכַלְתִּי מִנְּעוּרַי״ – שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי בְּשַׂר כּוֹס כּוֹס מֵעוֹלָם, ״וְלֹא בָא בְּפִי בְּשַׂר פִּגּוּל״ – שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי מִבְּהֵמָה שֶׁהוֹרָה בָּהּ חָכָם. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נָתָן אָמְרוּ: שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי מִבְּהֵמָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ מַתְּנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **"My soul has not become impure"** means **that I did not consider** any sinful thoughts **during the day** that would cause me **to come to impurity** due to a seminal emission **at night. "And from my youth until now I have not eaten an unslaughtered carcass or a *tereifa*"** means **that I never ate** the **flesh of** an animal that was in danger of imminent death, leading one to say: **Slaughter** it, **slaughter** it quickly, before it dies. **"And no *piggul* flesh came into my mouth"** means **that I never ate from an animal** with regard to which there was uncertainty whether it is forbidden and **a Sage issued a ruling** to permit it. **They said in the name of Rabbi Natan** that the last portion of the verse means: **That I never ate from an animal whose gifts** to which members of the priesthood are entitled, i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, **were not** already **separated.**

The *gemara* presses: what is so praiseworthy about not eating *neveilah* and *treifah*? These are universally forbidden. If the verse means nothing more than that he observed basic halachic prohibitions, Yechezkel's boast is unremarkable. The *gemara* must therefore interpret his declaration as referring to something beyond the literal meaning.

The *gemara* reinterprets: *shelo achalti basar kus kus me'olam* — he never ate meat that was slaughtered in the frantic fashion of an animal about to die — meat of a *mesukenet*. That is what he calls *neveilah* and *treifah* in his declaration: not technically prohibited meat, but the kind of borderline meat that was only barely rescued from *neveilah* by a hurried *shechita*. This is indeed something beyond the ordinary halachic obligation, which is precisely why it merits mention as a personal virtue.

### No Rabbi Ruling


The *gemara* further interprets *basar piggul* in Yechezkel's declaration. Two readings are offered. The first: *shelo achalti mibeheimah shehorei bah chacham* — he never ate meat from an animal over which a rabbi had been asked to give a ruling. Even when the rabbi's ruling was that the meat was permissible, the very fact that a question had arisen was enough for Yechezkel to decline to eat it. A second reading, attributed to *mishum Rabbi Natan*: *shelo achalti mibeheimah shelo hurmu matnoteha* — he never ate from an animal from which the priestly gifts (*zero'a, lechayayim, v'keivah* — the forearm, cheeks, and maw) had not been separated out. Unlike *terumah*, whose non-separation renders grain halachically prohibited as *tevel*, failure to give the priestly gifts from an animal does not render the meat itself forbidden. The meat remains *kosher*. Yet Yechezkel refused to eat it as a matter of personal scruple.

What is noteworthy here is the implicit shift in Yechezkel's formulation. The language of *piggul* — improper thought corrupting a sacred act — belongs to the world of *kohanim* and the Temple. In the post-*Churban* world in which Yechezkel speaks, the ruling of the rabbi replaces the thought of the *kohen* as the analogous intervention over a questionable matter. The reinterpretation thus reflects the transition from a Temple-based Judaism to a rabbinic-based one.

### Logical Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא שַׁרְיָא – הַיְינוּ רְבוּתֵיהּ דִּיחֶזְקֵאל, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אֲסִירָא – מַאי רְבוּתֵיהּ דִּיחֶזְקֵאל?
>
> **Translation:** The proof is: **Granted, if you say** that **it is permitted** to slaughter and eat an animal in imminent danger of death, then **that is the greatness of Ezekiel,** as, although eating it is permitted, he refrained from doing so. **But if you say** that **it is forbidden** to slaughter and eat that animal, **what** in that action attests to **the greatness of Ezekiel?** Apparently, it is permitted to slaughter and eat an animal in danger of imminent death.

The *gemara* now completes the proof: *ee amarta bishlama sharaya, hainu revusei deYechezkel* — if *mesukenet* is permissible, then Yechezkel's declaration that he never ate it makes sense as a statement of exceptional personal stringency, a *chumrah* that goes beyond the letter of the law. *ela ee amarta asira, mai revusei deYechezkel* — but if *mesukenet* were forbidden, in what way is Yechezkel's abstention praiseworthy? He would merely have been obeying the law. The interpretive force of the *gemara*'s reading of *neveilah* and *treifah* in Yechezkel's verse as referring to *mesukenet* — rather than to literally prohibited meat — only makes sense if *mesukenet* is actually permitted. The *gemara* acknowledges that this proof is somewhat circular, since it is the Rabbis who chose to read the verse as referring to *mesukenet*, and their interpretive choice presupposes the conclusion. Nonetheless, it functions as further support.

### Rashi: Disgust


Rashi's comment on this passage adds a further nuance. In his explanation of *revusei deYechezkel*, Rashi writes: *v'af al gav desharaya, kivan d'me'isa lo achaltay* — even though *mesukenet* is permitted, since it is disgusting, he did not eat it. On this reading, Yechezkel's abstention from *mesukenet* was not an expression of elevated halachic piety, nor a *chumrah* held up as a religious ideal, but simply an aesthetic revulsion. He found it repellent to eat meat from an animal that had been flailing on death's door. This reading has significant implications: it suggests that Yechezkel's various declarations — including the one about not having asked a rabbi — reflect his personal idiosyncrasies and *chumrot*, not standards to which all Jews should aspire. In particular, the *chumrah* of never eating meat over which a rabbinic question was raised would be extraordinarily burdensome if universalized, and Rashi's framing comfortably places it in the realm of one individual's scruples rather than communal obligation.

## Mesukenet Definition

### Cannot Stand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מְסוּכֶּנֶת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כֹּל שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ וְאֵינָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת. רַב חֲנִינָא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת בְּקָעִיּוֹת. רָמִי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת קוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of an animal **in danger** of imminent death? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** It is **any** animal with regard to **which one stands it** on its feet **but it does not stand** unaided. **Rav Ḥanina bar Shelamya in the name of Rav said:** That indicator is so clear that **even** if that animal maintains sufficient strength in its jaw and **eats pieces of wood,** if it is unable to stand, its status is that of an animal in danger of imminent death. **Rami bar Yeḥezkel said:** That is the case **even** if that animal **eats beams.**

Having established that *mesukenet* is permissible but requires signs of life at *shechita*, the *gemara* now turns to define *mesukenet* itself, since the *mishnah* provides no precise parameters. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav states: *kol she'ma'amidin otah v'einah omedet* — any animal that, when one attempts to stand it up, collapses and cannot remain standing. The inability to support itself is the defining criterion.

### Rava: Even Chewing


Rav Chanina bar Shmaya adds in the name of Rava: *afilu ochelet bikyot* — even if the animal is still capable of chewing on wood chips, its status as *mesukenet* is not thereby undermined. The strength of its jaw and mouth is irrelevant; if it cannot stand, it is *mesukenet*. The capacity to chew offers no counter-evidence to the inability to stand.

### Rami: Eating Beams


Rami bar Yechezkel sharpens this point even further: *afilu ochel korot* — even if the animal eats beams of wood. The image is deliberately extreme: an animal with seemingly robust oral capacity and strength in its teeth and jaw is nonetheless considered *mesukenet* if it cannot remain on its feet. The incapacity to stand is the operative criterion, and no degree of chewing ability overrides it.

### Sura and Pumbedita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי. בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי: הֵיכִי דָּמְיָא מְסוּכֶּנֶת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ וְאֵינָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת, וַאֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת בְּקָעִיּוֹת. רָמִי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת קוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **In Sura, they** would **teach** the exchange in **that** manner. **In Pumbedita, they** would **teach** the exchange in **this** manner: **What are the circumstances** of an animal **in danger** of imminent death? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** It is **any** animal with regard to **which one stands it** on its feet **but it does not stand** unaided, **even** if that animal maintains sufficient strength in its jaw and **eats pieces of wood. Rami bar Yeḥezkel said:** That indicator is so clear that **even** if that animal maintains sufficient strength in its jaw **and eats beams,** if it is unable to stand its status is that of an animal in danger of imminent death.

The *gemara* notes that this teaching circulated in somewhat different formulations across the two great Babylonian academies. In Sura, the tradition incorporated the addendum about eating wood chips directly into Rav Yehuda's original statement in the name of Rav: *kol she'ma'amidin otah v'einah omedet, v'afilu ochelet peka'ot*. Rami bar Yechezkel then extended this by saying "even if it eats beams." In Pumbedita, the formulation was parallel but structured slightly differently. In both versions, however, the substance is the same: inability to stand is the defining mark of *mesukenet*, and vigorous chewing does not negate it.

## Signs of Life: Shmuel

### Shmuel's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְתַלְמִידִי דְּרַב, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי אָמַר רַב בִּמְסוּכֶּנֶת? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב:
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel found students of Rav** after Rav's death. **He said to them: What did Rav say with regard to** an animal **in danger** of imminent death? **They said to him: This** is what **Rav said:**

*Ashkechinhu Shmuel letalmidei deRav* — Shmuel encountered the students of Rav, presumably in the marketplace. He asked them: *mai amar Rav bemesukenet?* — what did Rav say that was of interest regarding *mesukenet*? The students reported that Rav had addressed not only the definition of *mesukenet* but also what constitutes valid *pirchus* — the movement required at the moment of *shechita* to demonstrate residual life.

### Movement List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹעָה וְהֵטִילָה רֶיעִי וְכִשְׁכְּשָׁה בְּאׇזְנָהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה פִּירְכּוּס. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ לְאַבָּא לְאֹזוֹזֵי אוּנֵּי, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר כֹּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ [עוֹשָׂה] דְּבָרִים שֶׁהַמֵּתָה עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **lows, or excreted excrement, or wiggled its ear** during the slaughter, **that is a convulsion,** and the slaughter renders eating the flesh of the animal permitted. Shmuel **said to them: Is it necessary** according **to Abba,** i.e., Rav, for the animal **to move its ears** during the slaughter, which requires a considerable life force? **As I say: Any** movements of the animal **that are not matters that the death** of the animal **engenders** are convulsions sufficient to render the slaughter valid.

The students reported the following list: *go'ah, v'hitilah re'i, v'kiskesah b'oznah* — lowing (a low moaning or bellowing), defecating, and moving the ear. Rashi explains that these are alternatives, not cumulative requirements: any one of these three during the act of *shechita* constitutes sufficient *pirchus* for a *mesukenet*.

### Ear Movement Critique


Shmuel's response was pointed: *itztarichah lei l'Abba le'azu'ei unei?* — does Rav (*abba*) need the animal to move its ear? Shmuel found it peculiar to include the movement of an ear on the list, not because it is invalid as a sign, but because the movement of an ear requires considerable vitality in a dying animal — considerably more than the other movements listed. For an animal in the process of being slaughtered, moving an ear is a demanding act. Including it on the list alongside lowing and defecating suggests a higher threshold than necessary and is potentially misleading about what is actually required.

### Shmuel's Principle


Shmuel therefore articulates a broader principle: *kol she'eino devarim shehamisah osah* — any movement that is not a natural reflex of the dying body suffices as evidence of life. This is the generative criterion, not any specific movement. The list of lowing, defecating, and ear-movement is simply illustrative. Any action that cannot be attributed to the spontaneous bodily reactions accompanying death demonstrates that the animal was still alive with genuine vitality at the conclusion of *shechita*. This, of course, connects directly back to the *mishnah*'s exclusion of *pashtah yadah v'lo hechzirah* — the mere extension of a limb without its retraction — as a natural death reflex that proves nothing.

### Rav Called Abba


Shmuel's use of the name *abba* for Rav invites brief comment. Rashi explains that *abba* was an honorific, like calling someone a "father figure." Tosafot, however, maintain that *abba* was Rav's actual given name, and *rav* was the honorific title — the Babylonian equivalent of *rebbi*, designating the preeminent halachic authority of the generation. Tosafot draws the comparison to *rebbi* for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and notes that the same phenomenon repeats in later generations, where a single great figure becomes known simply as "the Rav."
