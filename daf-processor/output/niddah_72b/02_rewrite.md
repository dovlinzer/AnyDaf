# Niddah 72b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomeret Yom Status

### Mishna Setup

The daf opens with a citation from our *mishna*: *hav lo yom she-acharav ve-shimsha* — she observed the following day and then had relations. The case involves a woman who saw blood during the eleven days between *niddah* periods. Such a woman is a *shomeret yom keneged yom* — she observes the day following her bleeding as a clean day before immersing in the *mikvah*. If, for instance, she bled on day five of the eleven days, she waits through part of day six, immerses, and then has relations. At the moment of immersion and cohabitation, she is technically permitted. The complication arises if she bleeds later that same day: her subsequent bleeding would retroactively undermine her *mikvah* and render her a *zavah ketanah* — a minor *zavah* — at the time of immersion. The *mishna* characterizes this behavior as *tarbut ra'ah*, bad or dissolute conduct, and rules that any contact she has made, and any act of relations, remain *taluy*, in abeyance, pending whether she bleeds before the day's end.

### Universal Agreement?

The *gemara* asks: *mai lav divrei hakol hi?* — doesn't this appear to represent a universal ruling? The question is whether Beit Shammai might hold that she is rabbinically *temeiah* for the remainder of the day regardless of whether she subsequently bleeds, making the ruling of *tarbut ra'ah* distinctively the position of Beit Hillel alone. The *gemara* answers: *lav Beit Hillel hi* — indeed, it is only Beit Hillel's position.

### Beit Shammai Critique

A *baraita* records the rejoinder of Beit Shammai to Beit Hillel: *amar len Beit Shammai le-Beit Hillel, ve-chi le-zo atem korin tarbut ra'ah?* — Is this all you call it, merely bad behavior? Noteworthy here is the shift in the *baraita*'s framing: although the *mishna* opens by describing the woman, the *baraita* pivots to the man, pointing out that this husband *lo nitkaven zeh ela le-vilat niddah* — his intent was nothing other than to have relations with a *niddah*.

### Intent Discussion

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *niddah salka da'atakh?* — can it really be said he intended to have relations with a *niddah*? This is the eleven-day period, not the *niddah* period, so if anything this would be a *zavah*, not a *niddah*. The *gemara* corrects: *ela eima le-vilat zavah* — say rather he intended to have relations with a *zavah*. Yet even that formulation is imprecise, since at this point she is not yet a full three-day *zavah gedolah*. The *gemara* therefore refines further: *ela eima le-vilat shomeret yom keneged yom* — he intended to have relations with a woman in the status of *shomeret yom*, a *zavah ketanah* watching one day after the next. The point of Beit Shammai is not that the man has literally decided to sin, but rather that a responsible person would not engage in an act so likely to turn out retroactively prohibited.

### Transgression Status

For Beit Shammai, therefore, the characterization of *tarbut ra'ah* is far too mild. The conduct constitutes something far closer to actual transgression. This may be an indication — though the *gemara* does not make it conclusive — that Beit Shammai would rule that the woman is considered rabbinically *temeiah* for the remainder of the day even if she does not bleed at all. With this, the discussion of Beit Shammai's rabbinic status for the remainder of the observance day concludes.

## Day Ten Status

### Day Ten Question

The *gemara* turns to a new *amar*, a reported dispute: *itmar, asiri* — what is the status of day ten? Day ten sits in an ambiguous position within the eleven-day *zavah* period. On one hand, it falls within the eleven-day window and a woman who observes day eleven as a clean, non-bleeding day following bleeding on day ten would be fulfilling a meaningful act of *zavah* process. On the other hand, bleeding on day ten cannot possibly build into a *zavah gedolah* — a major *zavah* requiring seven clean days — because days ten and eleven would make her a *zavah ketanah*, and day twelve would place her back in the *niddah* period. There is thus no scenario in which bleeding on day ten could combine with two subsequent days in the *zavah* period to yield three consecutive days of *zavah* bleeding.

### Rabbi Yochanan View

*Rabbi Yochanan amar asiri ke-tishi* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that day ten is analogous to day nine. Just as day nine requires the biblical observance of a following clean day — *mah tishi ba'i shimmur* — so too day ten requires the biblical observance of day eleven, since day eleven remains within the *zavah* period.

### Resh Lakish View

*Resh Lakish amar asiri ke-achad asar* — Resh Lakish holds that day ten is analogous to day eleven. Just as day eleven requires no biblical observance of the following day — because the next day falls in the *niddah* period and cannot extend a *zavah* sequence — so too day ten does not require biblical observance of day eleven, since bleeding on day ten can no longer build toward a *zavah gedolah*.

### Position Analysis

While we generally rule in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan, Resh Lakish's position has a compelling internal logic. The entire institution of observing a following clean day appears designed to track the process by which a woman could become a *zavah gedolah*. If bleeding on day ten cannot possibly contribute to that outcome — since days ten, eleven, and then the *niddah* period make any three-day *zavah gedolah* impossible — there is a principled basis for saying that day ten does not carry that biblical requirement. Nonetheless, that is Resh Lakish's view, and the debate is left without resolution.

The *gemara* notes that some report this dispute in connection with our *mishna*, while others report it as arising in the context of a related statement, to which we now turn.

## Halakha Lemoshe

### Tradition Teaching

*Amar lo Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah le-Rabbi Akiva*: Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah said to Rabbi Akiva — and this is drawn from tractates *Menachot* and *Zevachim* — *afilu atta marbeh ba-shemen ba-shemen kol ha-yom, eini shome'a lach* — even if you spend the entire day multiplying *ba-shemen ba-shemen*, I will not listen to you.

The background to this exchange is a dispute about the quantity of oil brought with the loaves accompanying a *todah* offering. The *Torah* uses the word *ba-shemen* multiple times, and Rabbi Akiva applied the hermeneutic principle of *ein ribbuy achar ribbuy ela le-ma'et* — repeated emphasis actually serves to reduce — to conclude that the required measure is a *chatzi log*, a half-*log*, rather than the standard full *log*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah does not dispute the *halakha*; he disputes the method. He refuses to accept that elaborate textual derivations are the source of these laws at all.

This exchange crystallizes a fundamental tension within *Torah she-be'al peh*. There are, broadly speaking, two schools of thought. The first holds that the tradition is primarily about transmission — *mesorah*, *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — and that scholars are conduits faithfully conveying what has been received. Rabbi Eliezer, for instance, famously declared that he never taught anything he had not first heard from his own teachers. The second school, exemplified par excellence by Rabbi Akiva, holds that the *Torah* is given for human creative engagement — for *derash*, for deriving meaning from every letter and tittle, and generating *halakha* through a rigorous process of interpretation.

### Examples Listed

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah states his position plainly: *chatzi log shemen le-toda, revi'it yayin le-nazir, ve-achad asar yom shebein nida le-nida* — the half-*log* of oil for the *todah*, the *revi'it* of wine that a *nazir* is forbidden to drink, and the eleven days between one *niddah* period and the next are all *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. These are received traditions, not derivations. Do not attempt to derive them from textual doubling or any other hermeneutic device.

To spell out what is meant by eleven days between *niddah* periods: a woman bleeds and is a *niddah* for seven days, days one through seven. From day eight through day eighteen — eleven days — any bleeding does not produce *niddah* status but rather *zavah* status. Beginning on day nineteen, if she bleeds again, she is once more a *niddah*. These eleven days, the *nazir*'s *revi'it*, and the *todah*'s *chatzi log* are all, according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, purely transmitted traditions from Sinai.

It is in the context of the phrase *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* applied to the eleven days that Tosafot note the debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish is understood to have been introduced: *mai halakha?* — what is the *halakha* of these eleven days? Though the content of the eleven-day period is clear — during this period bleeding produces *zavah* rather than *niddah* status — the two *Amoraim* use this statement as a hook for their discussion of the special *halachot* that apply to days ten and eleven of that cycle.

Rabbi Yochanan reads: *halacha achad asar* — day eleven has one special *halakha*, namely that bleeding on it does not require observing the following day. Bleeding on days one through ten, however, means day eleven can still function as the observed clean day for the prior bleeding — a standard feature of the *zavah* process, not a special one unique to day eleven.

Resh Lakish reads: *halachot achad asar* — day eleven has two special *halachot*. First, bleeding on day eleven does not require observing day twelve, since day twelve falls in the *niddah* period. Second, if a woman bled on day ten, she does not need to observe day eleven as her clean day — because, as argued above, that bleeding cannot build into a *zavah gedolah*, making the observance of day eleven in that capacity halakhically unnecessary. In this reading, day eleven is special in both directions: it does not trigger observation of the following day, and it need not serve as the observed day for prior bleeding on day ten.

## Eleven Days Derivation

### Tradition vs. Derivation

Having been introduced to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's claim that these laws are *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, the *gemara* registers surprise: *hani halachot ninhu? hani kraya ninhu!* — Are these merely received traditions? These are derivable from *pesukim*! The *gemara* then presents a *baraita* that works through precisely such derivations.

### Three Days Passage

The *baraita* opens with what would have been the most natural reading of the relevant *pesukim* in *Chumash*. The *Torah* in the relevant passage states: *isha ki yazuv zov damah yamim rabbim belo et niddatah o ki tazuv al niddatah* — if a woman's blood flows for many days, not at the time of her *niddah* period, or if it flows beyond her *niddah*, then she is a *zavah*. The immediately preceding section states: *isha ki tihyeh zavah dam yihyeh zovah bivsarah shiv'at yamim tihyeh veniddatah* — if a woman has a flow and blood flows from her flesh, seven days shall she be in her *niddah* status.

The *baraita* raises the possibility: *yachol haro'ah gimel yamim betichilat niddah retzufim tehei zavah* — perhaps if a woman bleeds for three consecutive days even during her normal *niddah* period she becomes a *zavah*, given that the *Torah* uses the phrase *yamim rabbim*, many days. And what would the first *parasha* describe — a *niddah* who bled only one day?

The *gemara* rejects this reading by directing attention to the phrase *belo et niddatah*: not at the time of her *niddah*. The operative distinction between *niddah* and *zavah* is not simply the quantity of days of bleeding, but the time during which the bleeding occurs. The *niddah* period is understood to be the seven days of *tumah* that follow the onset of menstrual bleeding. A woman who bleeds thereafter — from day eight onward — is in a position to become a *zavah*. The phrase *belo et niddatah* thus establishes that *zavah* bleeding must occur outside those seven days.

One might ask: but does *al niddatah* — the alternative formulation in the same *pasuk* — not suggest bleeding during the *niddah* period? The *gemara* understands *belo et niddatah* to refer to bleeding immediately following the *niddah* period (from day eight), while *al niddatah* means bleeding that is slightly more removed — beginning from day nine. In this way two distinct scenarios of *zavah* bleeding are described by the *pasuk*: one commencing immediately after the *niddah* period on day eight, and one commencing a day later on day nine.

From *yamim rabbim* — many days — the *baraita* derives that the *zavah* sequence requires three days: *yamim* being plural yields at least two, and *rabbim* adds a further increment to reach three. This gives us the baseline case: three consecutive days of bleeding after the *niddah* period, constituting a *zavah gedolah*.

### Eleven Days Upper Bound

The *baraita* has now established two legitimate starting points for a *zavah gedolah* sequence: days one, two, and three after the *niddah* period, derived from *belo et niddatah*; and days two, three, and four, derived from *al niddatah*. But the question remains: how do we know the entire eleven-day window? The derivation proceeds as follows.

If a woman bled on days one, two, and three, her seven clean days would run from day four through day ten. Any day that could serve as one of those seven clean days — counting toward her eventual *taharah* — could also, by the same logic, be a day on which bleeding makes her a *zavah*. Day four illustrates this: it can serve as the first of the seven clean days for a woman who bled on days one, two, and three; therefore, bleeding on day four can itself constitute *zavah* bleeding. The same reasoning extends through day ten — all of days four through ten can count as clean days for the days-one-through-three sequence, and therefore all of days four through ten can also be days on which bleeding produces *zavah* status.

Similarly, the scenario grounded in *al niddatah* — bleeding on days two, three, and four — generates clean days from day five through day eleven. Therefore days five through eleven can also be days of *zavah* bleeding. Combining the two scenarios, days one through eleven of the post-*niddah* period are all potential *zavah* days.

### Day Twelve Exclusion

One might ask why the count stops at eleven. Why not extend to day twelve? The *baraita* answers: day twelve cannot serve as a clean day for either of the two *pasuk*-grounded scenarios. For the days-one-through-three scenario, the clean days are four through ten. For the days-two-through-four scenario, the clean days are five through eleven. Day twelve is a clean day for neither. Since the entire derivation rests on the principle that a day fit to be a clean day is also fit to be a *zavah* day, day twelve, having no such eligibility, falls outside the *zavah* period. The *gemara* phrases this: *marbe ani achad asar she-ra'uy le-sfirat o ki tazov u-motzi ani shneim asar she-ein ra'uy le-sfirat o ki tazov* — I include eleven because it can count among the clean days for the *o ki tazov* scenario, and I exclude twelve because it cannot.

It is worth pausing to note what the derivation has accomplished. An eleven-day *zavah* window, which Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah declared to be *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, has been worked out with considerable ingenuity from the *pesukim* — as Rabbi Akiva's school would have it. The *gemara*'s observation is pointed: *alma kra'ei* — evidently these are derived from *pesukim*.

### Expanding to Fewer Days

The *Torah*, as noted, discusses only a *zavah* who bleeds *yamim rabbim* — three days — and then counts seven clean days. It says nothing explicit about a woman who bleeds only two days or only one day. The *baraita* now asks: *ve-ein li ela sheloshah yamim, shenei yamim minayin?* — I know only the three-day case; from where do I derive a two-day *zavah*?

The answer comes from the phrase *yemei* — written without the additional *mem* that gives *yamim* its fuller plural — used in the *pasuk* *yamim rabbim o kol yemei zov tumata*. The word *yemei* by itself indicates a lesser plural, understood as two. Therefore even two days of *zavah* bleeding produces *zavah* status.

### One Clean Day

*Yom echad minayin* — from where is a single-day *zavah* derived? From the phrase *kol yemei* — all the days — which the *baraita* reads as meaning any of the days, including even a single day. Thus the concept of a *zavah ketanah* — one who bleeds for only one or two days in the eleven-day period — is established, however obliquely, from the text.

## Tumah Transfer

### Equal Tumah Status

Having established the lesser categories of *zavah* — one-day and two-day — from *pesukim*, the *baraita* extends their *tumah* status to all the consequences of *zavah* *tumah*. The *Torah*'s general statement *tme'ah hi* — she is *tmeiah* — is applied equally to a three-day *zavah gedolah*, a two-day *zavah*, and a one-day *zavah*. From this phrase the *baraita* learns that the man who has relations with any of these women becomes equally *tamei* as she is — not merely at the level of *rishon* produced by ordinary contact, but at the level of an *av ha-tumah*. This parallels the explicit *Torah* law concerning a *niddah*: just as the man who has relations with a *niddah* becomes a first-order *tamei*, so too the man who has relations with a *zavah* of any degree.

It should be noted that the *gemara* does not spell out here — though it is critical for practical *halakha* — that the prohibition of sexual relations itself applies equally to a *zavah* as to a *niddah*. The *Torah*'s explicit *karet* prohibition is stated only in the context of *niddah*. The extension to *zavah* is assumed on the basis that the *tumah* is equated, and where the *tumah* is the same, the prohibition follows.

### Asymmetry Noted

A significant asymmetry emerges in the same analysis. While a woman who is a *zavah* — even a *zavah ketanah* — renders the man she has relations with *tamei*, the converse is not true: *ve-ein ha-zav metame mah she-hu bo'el* — a man who is a *zav* does not render the woman he has relations with *tamei*. This is not a general statement that sexual contact transfers *tumah* symmetrically; the *Torah*'s laws of *tumah* transfer are highly specific. A *metzora*'s *tumah*, for instance, does not transfer to a woman through sexual contact. The transmission in the case of *niddah* and *zavah* is strictly unidirectional.

### Textual Basis

The textual basis for this asymmetry is the word *hi* — she — in the *pasuk* *tme'ah hi*: *hi* *metame'ah bo'alah, ve-ein ha-zav metame mah she-hu bo'el* — she makes the man she has relations with *tamei*, but a *zav* does not make *tamei* what he has relations with. The use of *mah* — what — rather than *mi* — who — in the formulation *mah she-hu bo'el* is striking and appears to reflect an objectifying perspective: she is treated as a *tamei* object that the man handles, and so the *tumah* transfers to him, whereas when a man is the *tamei* party the woman is not framed in equivalent terms.

## Akiva vs. Tradition (III)

### Pesukim Conclusion

The foregoing derivation prompts the *gemara*'s resolution of the earlier tension: *alma kra'ei* — evidently these are *pesukim*, not mere *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. The conclusion is that the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah about the eleven days and related *halachot* maps precisely onto the debate about the *chatzi log* of oil for the *todah*. Rabbi Akiva would say: these laws are derived from *pesukim*, as the *baraita* has demonstrated at length. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah would say: they are tradition, *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, and no *pasuk*-based derivation, however elaborate, changes that fundamental claim. The two positions reflect two genuinely different schools of thought about the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*.

---

## Tumah Transfer (II)

### Kal Vachomer

The *gemara* refuses to let the asymmetry rest without testing it further. Perhaps a *kal va-chomer* could overcome the exclusion of the *zav*'s partner from *tumah*. The argument runs: *umah hi she-eina metame'ah bi-re'iyot ka-yamim* — a woman, as a *zavah*, does not become *temeiah* as easily as a man becomes a *zav*, because her three-day *zavah* status requires the bleeding to occur on three separate consecutive days. A man's *zav* status, by contrast, can be triggered by two flows on the same day, without requiring them to be spread across consecutive days — making it easier, in a sense, for a man to achieve *zav* status. Yet despite being the less stringently conditioned status, *metame'ah bo'alah* — the *zavah* renders her sexual partner *tamei*. If so, should it not follow *kal va-chomer* that the *zav*, whose condition arises more easily, should likewise render his partner *tameiah*?

### Gender Analysis

The *gemara* answers: *talmud lomar hi* — the *Torah* says *hi*, she. *Hi metame'ah bo'alah ve-ein ha-zav metame mah she-hu bo'el* — she renders her partner *tamei*; the *zav* does not render his partner *tamei*. The *kal va-chomer* is thus explicitly blocked by the word *hi*.

The use of *mah* — what — rather than *mi* — who — in the phrase *mah she-hu bo'el* warrants attention in its own right. It reflects an objectifying posture in the language of the *pasuk*: the woman with whom he has relations is described as a *mah*, a thing, rather than a *mi*, a person. This may not be incidental. The logic of *tumah* transfer here mirrors the transfer that occurs when a *niddah* or *zavah* sits on a surface: the surface acquires *tumah* equal to hers because she — the *tamei* person — is the active source, and the object she contacts absorbs that *tumah*. The same dynamic appears to underlie the case of relations: she is the *tamei* party, and the man she is in contact with absorbs that *tumah* at an equal level. The *zav*, in this framing, is not analogized to the *niddah* sitting on a surface; the woman he is with is not cast as the passive object in the same way, and hence the transfer does not operate.

## Vessels Tamei

### Explicit Case

Having derived the concept of a *zavah ketanah* from *pesukim*, the *gemara* turns to the explicit case of how a *zavah* affects *mishkav u-moshav* — the beds and seats on which she lies or sits. The *Torah* states explicitly: *kol ha-mishkav asher tishkav alav kol yemei zovah ke-mishkav niddatah yihyeh lah ve-chol ha-keli asher teshev alav tamei yihyeh ke-tum'at niddatah* — everything she lies on during all the days of her flow shall be like her *niddah* lying; everything she sits on shall be *tamei* like her *niddah* *tumah*. For the three-day *zavah gedolah*, this is explicit in the *Torah*.

The *gemara* then raises the same question it raised about the *tumah* of her person: *ve-ein li ela sheloshah yamim* — I know only the three-day case. *Shenei yamim minayin?* — from where does a two-day *zavah* produce *mishkav u-moshav tumah*? Again the answer comes from *yemei* in the phrase *kol yemei zovah*, which, as a lesser plural, implies two days. And a one-day *zavah*? From *kol yemei* — all the days — understood as any day of flow, however brief.

## Akiva vs. Tradition (III)

### Two Schools

This extended series of derivations — the eleven-day period, the *zavah ketanah*, the *tumah* transferred through relations, the *mishkav u-moshav* applying to even a one-day *zavah* — all accomplished through careful reading of *pesukim*, stands in the sharpest possible contrast to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's insistence that these same laws are simply *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. The two positions represent, at a deep level, two fundamentally different understandings of the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*. Rabbi Akiva and his school hold that the *Torah* is given for human creative engagement, that every letter and tittle is available for derivation, and that the process of *derash* is the primary mechanism by which *halakha* is generated. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah and his school hold that the tradition itself is the primary vehicle — that the link to the text may be made as an *asmachta*, an after-the-fact support, but that the real authority derives from the received chain of transmission.

---

## Vessels Tamei (II)

### Two Days Derivation

Picking up the thread within the *mishkav u-moshav* discussion, the *baraita* formalizes the derivation for a two-day *zavah*: the word *yemei* — used in the phrase *kol yemei zovah* in the context of *mishkav* — indicates a lesser plural, understood as referring to two days. A two-day *zavah* therefore also renders her lying and seating *tamei* as an *av ha-tumah*.

### One Day Derivation

For a one-day *zavah*, the phrase *kol yemei* is read as encompassing any configuration of days of flow, including even a single day. This derives that a *zavah ketanah* who bled only one day also produces *mishkav u-moshav tumah*. With this, the *baraita*'s entire program of derivations is complete: from the eleven-day window, through the categories of *zavah ketanah*, through the rules of *tumah* transfer via relations and via *mishkav u-moshav*, all has been worked out — in Rabbi Akiva's manner — from the language of the *pesukim*.

## Akiva vs. Tradition (III)…

### Rambam Defense

The tension between these two schools — tradition versus derivation — was not merely an ancient academic dispute. It became existentially important in the context of the Rambam's polemic against the Karaites. The Karaites, looking at the rabbinic tradition, saw the great debates of the Talmud — the disputes between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, between *Amoraim* and *Tanna'im* — as evidence of the fraudulence of the rabbinic claim to possess an authoritative transmitted tradition. If you truly have a received tradition, they argued, why is there so much disagreement? The debates themselves prove that no genuine tradition exists, and hence the entire enterprise of Rabbinic Judaism lacks legitimacy.

The Rambam's response was carefully constructed. He acknowledged that some elements of *Torah she-be'al peh* are genuine *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — transmitted without derivation, received as pure tradition. But he insisted that these are a tiny fraction of the whole, almost infinitesimally small. The vast majority of *Torah she-be'al peh* — on the Rambam's accounting, essentially all of it except this tiny core — is derived through the application of hermeneutic principles to the text of the *Torah*. And when *halakha* is derived by human intellect applying methodological principles, it is entirely natural and indeed expected that different scholars, applying the same principles with integrity, will reach different conclusions. The debates are not evidence of a broken chain of transmission; they are the natural product of an open-ended process of human derivation.

### Rambam Theory

The Rambam's position is therefore this: *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* was never debated. Wherever there is genuine transmitted tradition, there is agreement. But because that category is so small, the debates that fill the Talmud are not debates about what was transmitted; they are debates about what the derivation process yields. One can even observe this within the *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* framework itself: there is debate about whether something is *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, but not, according to the Rambam's ideal model, about the content of what was transmitted. The eleven-day period, the *chatzi log* for the *todah*, the *revi'it* for the *nazir* — if these are truly transmitted traditions, no one disputes the *halakha* itself.

An interesting complication arises here: even that claim may require qualification. The debates recorded in the Talmud sometimes touch on things labeled *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, raising the question of whether the Rambam's description can be sustained in every case. Nonetheless, the Rambam's general framework has remained the dominant position for understanding why *Torah she-be'al peh* contains so much internal debate.

### Modern Critiques

A very different challenge arose in the 19th century with the emergence of Reform Judaism. Where the Karaites had argued that the rabbinic tradition must be wholly transmitted because derivation cannot be authoritative, the Reform critique ran in the opposite direction: the derivations are obviously not serious. Could anyone sincerely claim that the eleven-day *zavah* period is genuinely derivable from the *pasuk*'s use of *kol yemei* and *belo et niddatah*? The argumentation, they said, is forced and fanciful, and therefore the entire enterprise of rabbinic derivation is illegitimate.

Interestingly, one possible response to this challenge — essentially inverting the Rambam's response to the Karaites — was to embrace the tradition side of the equation: these laws are *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, or are at minimum rooted in ancient tradition, and the *pasuk* connections are *asmachtot*, textual supports added after the fact rather than genuine derivations. The *pesukim* are not doing the work; the tradition is doing the work, and the *pesukim* simply provide a convenient anchor.

### Legitimacy Criteria

This raises a deeper question: if derivation is the primary mechanism for generating *halakha*, what prevents any sufficiently skilled scholar from deriving any conclusion he wishes? The *gemara* itself acknowledges the concern, famously noting that a member of the *Sanhedrin* had to be able to argue — through textual analysis — that a *sheretz*, an inherently *tamei* creature, is in fact *tahor*. This means that the mechanics of derivation are flexible enough to support virtually any conclusion. What then distinguishes legitimate *halakhic* derivation from mere sophistry?

The Rambam's answer, at least in part, is that the legitimacy lies in the integrity of the process and the rigor with which the hermeneutic principles are applied. The *mishna* in a different context notes that when the students of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not attend sufficiently to their masters' teachings, the disputes in Israel multiplied. The Rambam interprets this not as a failure of memory or transmission but as a failure to master the process of derivation itself. When scholars are not trained with sufficient rigor in the methodology, derivations proliferate without discipline. The debates that resulted are thus not evidence of chaos, but rather of imperfect mastery of a demanding process — a process that, when practiced with full integrity, ought to converge, at least in principle, on defensible results.

## Ongoing Authority

### Post-Talmud Process

One might ask whether the process of halakhic derivation described by Rabbi Akiva — and endorsed in principle by the Rambam — continues in the post-Talmudic period. The answer is clearly yes in the sense that *psak halakha* continues, responsa literature grows, and the ongoing interpretation of sources never ceases. The notion of *lo ba-shamayim hi* — the Torah is not in heaven — lends further support to Rabbi Akiva's school: the interpretive process is given to human beings to carry out, and that process does not conclude with the closing of the Talmud.

### Text Shift

There is, however, a meaningful shift. While the *Tanna'im* and *Amoraim* derived *halakha* directly from the *Torah*, and even some *Amoraim* — such as Shmuel — produced original *midrashim* on *pesukim* to derive central *halachic* principles (including one of the foundational laws of *hilchos niddah*, the requirement of *hargasha*), the post-Talmudic period involves a displacement of the primary interpretive text. The text that later authorities interpret is no longer principally the *Torah* itself but rather the *gemara* and the *Rishonim*. The process of *derash* continues, but its object has shifted from the written *Torah* to the accumulated layers of rabbinic literature.

### Innovation Balance

The enduring question is where the line lies between interpretations that fall within an acceptable range and those that constitute a rupture in continuity. The response process — *psak halakha* in every generation — necessarily involves interpreting existing sources in new contexts, and any intellectually serious reading of the *Poskim* reveals genuine creativity. At the same time, there is a felt sense of boundary: interpretations that would strike the tradition as severing continuity with what came before are experienced as illegitimate, regardless of how cleverly they may be constructed. Defining that boundary precisely is itself one of the ongoing questions of *halakhic* theory, and it is not one to which any simple, universally accepted answer has been given.

## Day/Night Question

### Day/Night Distinction

Before closing, the *gemara* entertains one final question. Rav Shmaya raises the following to Abba: *eima bi-yamama tehei zavah u-ve-laya tehei niddah* — perhaps the distinction between *niddah* and *zavah* is not only temporal in terms of which seven-day or eleven-day period she is in, but also in terms of time of day. The *Torah* uses the word *yamim* — days — suggesting daylight hours. Perhaps if a woman bleeds at night she would be a *niddah*, while bleeding during the day would make her a *zavah*.

### Night Seven Answer

The *gemara* answers from a *pasuk*: *al niddatah* — juxtaposed to her *niddah*. The *niddah* period ends at nightfall of the seventh day. Therefore the time immediately following — *be-laya*, the night of day eight — is already the onset of the potential *zavah* period, and a woman who bleeds at that point is called a *zavah*. The resolution is that it is the sequence of the days within the calendar cycle that determines *niddah* versus *zavah* status, not whether the bleeding occurs during daylight or at night.

## House of Elijah

### Halakha Teaching

The *gemara* closes with a teaching from the school of Elijah, familiar from the daily liturgy: *kol ha-shoneh halachot be-chol yom muvtach lo she-hu ben olam ha-ba she-ne'emar halichot olam lo, al tikrei halichot ela halachot* — whoever studies *halachot* each day is guaranteed a share in the world to come, for it is written *halichot olam lo*, and one should read not *halichot* — pathways — but *halachot*. The association is not random. The word *halachot* in the *gemara* of *Berachot* that we are about to begin is illustrated by the example of *achad asar yom bein niddah le-niddah* — the eleven days between *niddah* periods. And indeed the students of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel made this same law the locus of their dispute in that tractate. A straightforward *halacha pesukah* — a settled *halakha* — is what one is to study each day as a guarantee of *olam ha-ba*.

### Final Dialectic

Yet the closing teaching carries within it the very dialectic that has run through the entire daf. The word *shoneh* — he who studies — shares its root with *mishna*. And *halachot* are not *gemara*; *halachot* are the bottom line, the *psak*, the settled ruling. The *gemara* is thus ending with a reminder that the great discussions, debates, and derivations — Rabbi Akiva's creativity, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's insistence on tradition, the twelve-cycle journey through *Shas* — must ultimately resolve into *halakha*. One cannot live in the debate alone. The goal of learning is not only the dialectic process but the *halacha pesukah*, the clear ruling that guides practice. And yet, as this very daf has shown, even the *halacha pesukah* has its own history — arriving through either received tradition or human derivation, or perhaps through a combination of both that no single theory fully captures.

### Completion
