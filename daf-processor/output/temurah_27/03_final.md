# Temurah 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tachat Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״תַּחַת״ מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאִיתְּפוֹסֵי וּלִישָּׁנָא דְּאַחוֹלֵי. לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַתְפּוֹסֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאִם תַּחְתֶּיהָ תַּעֲמוֹד הַבַּהֶרֶת״. דְּאַחוֹלֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״תַּחַת הַנְּחוֹשֶׁת אָבִיא זָהָב״, וְהִלְכָּךְ, גַּבֵּי קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ דְּעָבְדִין תְּמוּרָה — לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַתְפּוֹסֵי הוּא, גַּבֵּי קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת דְּלָא עָבְדִין תְּמוּרָה — לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַחוֹלֵי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said,** in response: **You find** that the term: **In place of [*taḥat*], is** sometimes **a term that** indicates the **associating** of one sanctity with another, **and** it **is** sometimes **a term that** indicates **desacralization.** It is **a term that** indicates the **associating** of one sanctity with another, **as it is written: "But if the white leprous spot stays in its place [*taḥteha*]"** (Leviticus 13:23). In this verse, the word "*taḥteha*" indicates that the white leprous spot remains in its place. This usage of the word is fitting for substitution, since when sanctity is transferred by substitution from a consecrated animal to a non-sacred animal, the sanctity of the consecrated animal remains in place, despite the fact that the non-sacred animal is also consecrated. Yet *taḥat* is also a term **that** indicates **desacralization, as it is written: "In place of [*taḥat*] brass I will bring gold,** and in place of iron I will bring silver, and in place of wood brass, and in place of stones iron" (Isaiah 60:17). In this verse, *taḥat* means replacement, which is what occurs in desacralization, where one item is replaced by another. **And therefore,** the term should be understood in accordance with the context: **With regard to** animals **consecrated** for sacrifice upon the **altar, which render** a non-sacred animal for which they are exchanged **a substitute,** *taḥat* **is a term that** indicates the **associating** of one sanctity with another. **With regard to** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance, which do not render** a non-sacred animal for which they are exchanged **a substitute,** *taḥat* **is a term that** indicates **desacralization.**

### Temurah vs Mechallel

The sugyot of the preceding dappim have established the general framework governing the language of *temurah* and *pidyon*, and the Gemara on 27a now turns to the precise linguistic conditions under which the word *tachat* — "instead of" — triggers one consequence or the other. The fundamental distinction, stated earlier and now refined, is the following: language that effects an exchange, such as *temurah*, *chalifa*, or *tachat* in its replacing sense, constitutes an act of *temurah*. Language that transfers *kedushah* away from the sanctified animal — *mechallel*, to desanctify — is the proper act of *pidyon*. If the original animal is a *ba'al mum*, desanctification is precisely what one is supposed to do; if it is not a *ba'al mum*, using desanctification language accomplishes nothing.

The conceptual difference runs deeper than mere vocabulary. *Pidyon* begins with the sanctified animal: one starts with the *korban* and removes its *kedushah*. *Temurah* begins from the other direction — one leaves the sanctified animal in place and introduces an unsanctified animal, a *chullin* animal, attempting to have it take the place of the sacred one. This is what the Gemara calls *atfusa*, grabbing: the *kedushah* of the first animal is still in place and it is, as it were, seized onto the second animal. That is why the act of *temurah* is forbidden — one is impinging upon and attempting to dethrone the status of the sanctified animal — and it is also why the *chullin* animal becomes *kadosh*: because the first animal retained its sanctity throughout, and that sanctity spreads.

The Gemara had already established that the word *tachat* is inherently ambiguous. In the context of *kodshei bedek habayit* — temple treasury sanctity — where the institution of *temurah* does not apply, *tachat* is read as signifying transfer: the *kedushah* moves from one object to another. In the context of *kodshei korbanot*, where *temurah* is a relevant category, *tachat* carries the sense of the first remaining in place while the second attempts to replace it — and that is the language of *temurah*. The remaining question, which the Gemara now takes up in earnest, is how to interpret an ambiguous *tachat* when both readings are theoretically available.

### Rava on Meaningful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַחוֹלֵי הוּא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה הֶקְדֵּשׁ בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even** with regard to animals **consecrated** for sacrifice upon the **altar,** which render a non-sacred animal for which they are exchanged a substitute, **you** can **find** that *taḥat* **is a term that** indicates **desacralization. For example,** in a case **where a consecrated** animal **was blemished** and can be desacralized because it is unfit to be sacrificed, and one placed that consecrated animal next to a non-sacred animal and said: This animal is in place of [*taḥat*] that animal. Although the blemished sacred animal can render a non-sacred animal for which it is exchanged consecrated as a substitute, in this context the term *taḥat* means desacralization.

Rava states: *afilu bekodshei mizbe'ach*, even when dealing with sacrificial animals, one can find in the word *tachat* the sense of *lechullin* — redemption, desanctification — rather than replacement. When would that be the case? *Kegon dehayah hekdesh ba'al mum* — where the *korban* in question has a *mum* and is therefore subject to redemption. In any context where redemption is the meaningful and appropriate act, the ambiguous word *tachat* will be interpreted in that redemptive sense, because that is what the word *can* mean and because that is what one *should* be doing in such a situation.

The logic is precise: we interpret language in the way that is meaningful over the way that is meaningless, even if the meaningful interpretation is unfavorable. If the animal is *tamim* — unblemished — then the language of redemption is meaningless; a *tamim korban* cannot be redeemed. So even though interpreting *tachat* as *temurah* imposes a sin upon the speaker, it is the only meaningful interpretation available, and therefore it stands. The *kedushah* transfers to the other animal, and the speaker has transgressed. We do not interpret words in a way that renders them empty simply to shield someone from the consequences of an *aveirah*. Only when a meaningful positive interpretation is available — as when the animal has a *mum* and redemption makes sense — do we opt for that interpretation instead.

### Rav Ashi Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ בְּבַעַל מוּם נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַחוֹלֵי, וּמַשְׁכַּחַת לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַתְפּוֹסֵי. יָדוֹ אַקּוֹדֶשׁ — חוֹל הָוֵי, יָדוֹ אַחוֹל — קוֹדֶשׁ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ashi said: Even in** the case of **a blemished** animal, mentioned by Rava, sometimes **you find** that *taḥat* **is a term that** indicates **desacralization and** sometimes **you find** that *taḥat* **is a term that** indicates the **associating** of one sanctity with another. If the owner's **hand** is resting **on the consecrated** animal and he says: This animal is in place of [*taḥat*] that animal, it **is** a term of **desacralization,** as the placement of his hand indicates that his intention is to desacralize the consecrated animal, with its sanctity transferred to that non-sacred animal. If the owner's **hand** is resting **on the non-sacred** animal when he says: This animal is in place of that animal, then *taḥat* **is** a term of substitution, as the placement of his hand indicates that his intention is for the non-sacred animal to be **consecrated** with the same sanctity as the consecrated animal.

Rav Ashi refines Rava's principle. Even when the animal is a *ba'al mum*, one cannot say categorically that *tachat* will always be interpreted as redemption. It is possible, says Rav Ashi, to find in the language of *tachat* even with a *ba'al mum* the sense of *atfusa* — the *temurah* reading. There are therefore two possible meaningful interpretations when a *ba'al mum* is present: redemption (*achulei*) on one hand, and replacement (*atfusa*) on the other. The question becomes how to choose between them, and the answer, says Rav Ashi, lies in context.

### Hand Position

The clearest illustration of the conceptual difference between *mechallel* and *temurah* emerges here. If one's hand is on the sanctified animal — the *kodesh* — and one pronounces an ambiguous *tachat*, we interpret it as *lechullin*, as desanctification. Why? Because the act being performed is physically starting from the *kodesh*; one is removing its sanctity, which is the meaning of *pidyon*. *Yado al hakodesh*, *chol havei* — one's hand is on the sacred one and the result is that it becomes *chullin*.

If, however, one's hand is on the non-sanctified animal — the *chullin* — then the act is fundamentally different. One is defining the non-sanctified as taking the place of the sacred, which is the structure of *temurah*. *Yado al hachol*, *kodesh havei* — even if the *korban* had a *mum*, even if redemption would have been perfectly appropriate, the *kodesh* retains its sanctity and the non-sanctified animal acquires *kedushah* as a consequence of the *temurah*.

Taken together, the Gemara establishes a three-tiered hierarchy for interpreting ambiguous language. First: unambiguous language is followed without qualification. Second: where the language is ambiguous, it is interpreted in the meaningful way over the meaningless way, even to the speaker's detriment. Third: where two meaningful interpretations are available — as when the *korban* has a *mum* — context determines the reading, specifically where one's hand rests. This framework governs all the cases that follow.

## Two Meanings

### Abaye Mixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי אַבָּיֵי: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל קֹדֶשׁ בַּעֲלוֹת מוּם, וּשְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין תְּמִימוֹת, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ תַּחַת אֵלּוּ״, מַהוּ? מִי אָמַר לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי וְלָקֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּרָא — לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ הֶיתֵּרָא וְעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raises a dilemma:** If **there were** standing **before him two sacrificial animals** that **were blemished and two non-sacred animals** that **were unblemished,and,** without placing his hands on any animal, **he says: These** animals **are hereby in place of [*taḥat*] those** animals, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the dilemma: **Did he** intend to **say** *taḥat* **as association,** i.e., to consecrate the two non-sacred animals as substitutes, **and** therefore **he is flogged** two sets of lashes for the two violations of the prohibition against consecrating an animal as a substitute? **Or perhaps,** does one say that since **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, **a person does not cast aside the permitted** manner **and perform the prohibition?** If so, here too his intention was to desacralize the two blemished animals, and therefore he is not flogged.

Abaye now poses a question that presses directly on the edge case implied by Rav Ashi's ruling. The scenario involves four animals: two *kodesh* animals that are *ba'alei mum*, and two *chullin* animals that are *temimot* — unblemished. The speaker pronounces a single phrase — *harei elu tachat elu*, "these are *tachat* those" — covering all four animals at once without specifying hand position. What is the *halacha*?

The question arises precisely because there are two possible meaningful interpretations. When the *korban* has a *mum*, both *temurah* and redemption are meaningful. If one's hand is on neither set of animals, or on both simultaneously, the contextual indicator that Rav Ashi relied upon is absent. Abaye therefore asks: *mi amrinan l'atfusinhu b'laki* — do we say the language means *temurah*, grabbing, and the speaker receives *malkot*? Or — *kol heicha d'ika heiteira lo shavik inish heiteira v'avid issura* — wherever a permissible interpretation is available, do we assume a person would not abandon the permissible and perform the forbidden?

### Favorable Bias

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּרָא לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ וְעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל קוֹדֶשׁ, וְאַחַת מֵהֶן בַּעֲלַת מוּם, וּשְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין, וְאַחַת מֵהֶן בַּעֲלַת מוּם, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ תַּחַת אֵלּוּ״, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן, מִדִּתְמִימוֹת תַּחַת תְּמִימוֹת לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי, תְּמִימוֹת נָמֵי תַּחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי? אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּירָא לָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, וְהַהִיא בָּתְרָיְיתָא לְאַחוֹלֵי הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, **a person does not cast aside** the permitted manner **and perform the prohibition,** in a case where **there were** standing **before him two sacrificial animals, one of which was blemished** and one of which was unblemished, **and two non-sacred animals, one of which was blemished** and one of which was unblemished, **and he said: These** animals **are hereby in place of [*taḥat*] those** animals, **what is** the *halakha*? Although he certainly intended to transfer the sanctity of the unblemished sacrificial animal to a non-sacred animal by substitution, it is unclear which of the two non-sacred animals he intended to consecrate as a substitute. Once again, the Gemara explains the dilemma: **Do we say** that **since** he intended for **the unblemished** non-sacred animals to be consecrated as substitutes **in place of the unblemished** consecrated animals, i.e., **to associate** their sanctity, **so too** he intended to consecrate **the unblemished** non-sacred animal as a substitute **in place of the blemished** consecrated animal, i.e., **to associate** its sanctity, and he therefore is flogged three sets of lashes? **Or perhaps, here too** one applies the principle that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, a person **does not perform the prohibition.** If so, his intention with regard to **this latter** blemished consecrated animal was **to desacralize** it, and therefore he is not flogged for transferring its sanctity to a substitute.

The Gra notes that this formulation — that a person does not abandon a *heter* to perform an *issur* — might seem to raise a question of subjective motivation. But the more precise framing here is interpretive rather than motivational: where two meaningful readings exist and no contextual indicator resolves the ambiguity, which interpretation should we as a matter of principle assign to the speaker's words? The Gemara concludes, consistent with what was already implicit in Rava's initial statement, that we bias toward the favorable interpretation. We will not read language as sinful when an equally meaningful innocent reading is available.

This establishes that the hierarchy has a further principle operating within its third tier. When both interpretations are meaningful and context does not determine the matter — no hand position, no clear indicator — we do not choose randomly or worst-case; we choose favorably. The result in Abaye's case: the phrase *harei elu tachat elu* is read as *pidyon* for the *ba'alei mum* and not as *temurah*, so the speaker incurs no *malkot* on account of those animals.

With this principle in hand, Abaye immediately poses a harder question: *hayu lefanav shtei behemot shel kodesh*, v'*achat meihen ba'alat mum* — suppose one had two *kodesh* animals, one of them a *ba'al mum* and one *tamim*, along with two *chullin* animals, one *ba'al mum* and one *tamim*, and pronounced the same single phrase for the entire group. The issue now is that for the *tamim kodesh* animal, the language of redemption is meaningless and *tachat* can only mean *temurah*. Can the fact that one portion of the phrase necessarily carries the *temurah* meaning bleed over to determine the interpretation for the other portion — the *ba'al mum* — as well?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמַר: תְּמִימָה תַּחַת תְּמִימָה — לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי, בַּעֲלַת מוּם תַּחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם — לְאַחוֹלֵי. אוֹ דִלְמָא, תְּמִימָה דְּחוּלִּין תַּחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, בַּעֲלַת מוּם דְּחוּלִּין תַּחַת תְּמִימָה דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ לָקֵי?
>
> **Translation:** Once again, the Gemara explains the dilemma: **Did he** intend to **say** that **the unblemished** non-sacred animal should be consecrated as a substitute **in place of the unblemished** consecrated animal, and with regard to this pairing he intended **to associate** the sanctity by substitution, while **the blemished** non-sacred animal should be consecrated **in place of the blemished** consecrated animal, and for this pairing he intended **to desacralize** the consecrated animal. If so, he is flogged only for the first pairing and not for the second pairing. **Or perhaps,** he intended that **the unblemished non-sacred** animal should be consecrated as a substitute **in place of the blemished consecrated** animal, and **the blemished non-sacred** animal should be consecrated as a substitute **in place of the unblemished consecrated** animal, **and** since he intended to perform substitution in both cases he is **flogged for both** pairings.

The Gemara presents two possibilities. The first (*mi amrinan*) is to line up like with like: *tmimah tachat tmimah l'atfusi*, the unblemished *chullin* for the unblemished *korban* — clearly *temurah*, since redemption is impossible — and *ba'alat mum tachat ba'alat mum lechullin*, the blemished for the blemished — interpreted as redemption, so no *malkot* for that pairing. The second possibility (*o dilma*) is to cross the pairings: *tmimah d'chullin tachat ba'alat mum d'hekdesh*, and *ba'alat mum d'chullin tachat tmimah d'hekdesh* — with the result that both are interpreted as *temurah* and lashes apply for both.

The second reading is puzzling. If the *ba'al mum d'hekdesh* is being exchanged for the *tmimah d'chullin*, that is precisely the classic act of redemption — transferring from a blemished *korban* onto an unblemished animal is exactly what *pidyon* requires. Why should we read it as *temurah*?

### Rashi Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּירָא — לָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, וּלְאַחוֹלֵי הוּא, וְלָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, one **does not perform the prohibition,** this would resolve the previous dilemma, as his intention must have been **to desacralize** the blemished consecrated animal, **and** therefore he is **not flogged** two sets of lashes, one can raise the following dilemma: In a case where **there were three sacrificial animals before him, one of which was blemished** while the other two were unblemished, **and three non-sacred animals,** all of which **were unblemished, and he said: These** animals **are hereby in place of [*taḥat*] those** animals, what is the *halakha*?

Rashi's answer is striking. Rashi holds that the *Torah*'s formulation of *temurah* — *ra b'tov o tov b'ra* — implies that an exchange of *ra* for *ra*, a blemished *korban* for a blemished *chullin* animal, is categorically not an act of *temurah*. Since *ra b'ra* cannot constitute *temurah*, we are forced to read blemished-for-blemished as redemption, and that is why it does not generate *malkot*. The overall inclination is to read everything as *temurah* because the unblemished pairing compels that reading, but since *ra b'ra* is halachically incapable of being *temurah*, the blemished pairing is pushed toward the *pidyon* interpretation as a matter of necessity.

Tosafot strongly objects. Tosafot argues that nowhere in the Gemara has it ever been established that *ra b'ra* is incapable of *temurah*, and indeed earlier passages in the tractate appear to indicate the opposite. Tosafot therefore cannot accept Rashi's reasoning and looks for an alternative account. The Gra takes an entirely different path and reverses the *girsa* of the Gemara altogether. According to the Gra, the case of *ba'al mum* for *ba'al mum* is the one that follows the *tamim*: if the *tamim* pairing means *temurah*, then the blemished-for-blemished pairing likewise means *temurah*, because there is no compelling reason to redeem a *ba'al mum* onto another *ba'al mum* — it would be a purposeless act. It is precisely the crossing case — *ba'al mum* for *tamim* — that constitutes the classic redemption and is therefore read as *pidyon*. The Gra's reversed *girsa* thus avoids Rashi's novel claim entirely, and aligns the outcome with the logic of the entire preceding discussion.

## Pattern Chazakah

### Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּירָא לָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, וְהַהִיא בָּתְרָיְיתָא לְאַחוֹלֵי הָוֵי. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּירָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאַכַּתִּי גַּבְרָא לָא אִיתַּחְזַק בְּאִיסּוּרֵי, לָא שָׁבֵיק הֶתֵּירָא וְעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, one **does not perform the prohibition.** If so, his intention with regard to **this latter** blemished consecrated animal was **to desacralize** it, and therefore he is not flogged for transferring its sanctity to a substitute. With this principle in hand, the Gemara now asks: But what if we add more animals where the blemished interpretation is available? According to one who says that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, **a person** therefore **does not** perform the prohibited action — does this principle apply even when the person **has not** yet been **established as** one who regularly transgresses **a prohibition**?

Abaye continues his line of inquiry. Now suppose there are three *kodesh* animals — two *temimot* and one *ba'al mum* — and three *chullin* animals opposite them, all *temimot*, and one says the single phrase *harei elu tachat elu*. For two-thirds of the *kodesh* group, the word *tachat* can only mean *temurah*. The question is whether the majority-*temurah* character of the phrase overwhelms the one-third for whom a favorable interpretation was available.

*Mi amrinan midtemimot tachat temimot l'atfusei* — since for the unblemished animals the only reading is *temurah*, and since this is now the dominant meaning established by the majority of the group, does that determine the word's meaning even for the *ba'al mum*, so that *temimot nami tachat ba'al mum l'atfusei* as well? Or do we say — *kol heicha d'ika heiteira lo avid issura* — that even here, wherever a favorable interpretation is available for any animal within the phrase, we apply it to that animal regardless of the majority?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן, מִדִּתְמִימוֹת תַּחַת תְּמִימוֹת לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי, תְּמִימוֹת נָמֵי תַּחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי? אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶיתֵּירָא לָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, וְהַהִיא בָּתְרָיְיתָא לְאַחוֹלֵי הָוֵי. וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאַכַּתִּי גַּבְרָא לָא אִיתַּחְזַק בְּאִיסּוּרֵי, לָא שָׁבֵיק הֶתֵּירָא וְעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו אַרְבַּע בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל קֹדֶשׁ וְאַחַת מֵהֶן בַּעֲלַת מוּם, וְאַרְבַּע בְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ תַּחַת אֵלּוּ״, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן, מִדִּתְמִימוֹת תַּחַת תְּמִימוֹת לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי, תְּמִימוֹת נָמֵי תַּחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם לְאַתְפּוֹסֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: **Do we say** that **since** he intended for **the unblemished** non-sacred animals to be consecrated as substitutes **in place of the unblemished** consecrated animals, i.e., **to associate** their sanctity, **so too** he intended to consecrate **the unblemished** non-sacred animal as a substitute **in place of the blemished** consecrated animal, i.e., **to associate** its sanctity, and he therefore is flogged three sets of lashes? **Or perhaps, here too** one applies the principle that **anywhere that there exists** both **a permitted** and a prohibited manner to perform an action, a person **does not perform the prohibition.** If so, his intention with regard to **this latter** blemished consecrated animal was **to desacralize** it, and therefore he is not flogged for transferring its sanctity to a substitute. **And if you say** that **here too, since** this **man** has **not** yet been **established as** one who regularly transgresses **a prohibition,** until he has done so three times, therefore it is assumed that he **does not cast aside the permitted** manner **and perform the prohibition, Rav Ashi** therefore **raises** the following **dilemma:** If **there were four sacrificial animals** standing **before him, one of which was blemished** and the other three were unblemished, **and four non-sacred animals,** all of which were unblemished, **and he said: These** animals **are hereby in place of [*taḥat*] those** animals, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the dilemma: **Do we say** that **since** he intended for **the unblemished** non-sacred animals to be consecrated as substitutes **in place of the unblemished** consecrated animals, i.e., **to associate** their sanctity, **so too** he intended to consecrate **the unblemished** non-sacred animal as a substitute **in place of the blemished** consecrated animal, i.e., **to associate** its sanctity, and he therefore is flogged four sets of lashes?

The Gemara further suggests a possible reason why the favorable interpretation might still apply even in the face of a majority: *keivan d'ika gavra lo itchazek b'issura* — because the person has not yet established a *chazakah* of *issur*. A *chazakah* requires three instances. With only two *temimot* animals for which *temurah* is the forced reading, the pattern of sinful behavior has not been established, and therefore we are not yet prepared to presume sinful intent — or, framed interpretively, to assign the *temurah* reading — to the remaining animal.

### Four Animals

Building on the *chazakah* principle, Rav Ashi poses the next case. *Hayu lefanav arba behemot shel kodesh*, with three of them *temimot* and one *ba'al mum*, and four *chullin* animals opposite, and one pronounces *harei elu tachat elu*. Now for three of the *kodesh* animals, *tachat* can only mean *temurah*. Three instances — and the question becomes: has the person now established a *chazakah* of *issur* through this single phrase?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא וַדַּאי, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתַּחְזַק גַּבְרָא בְּאִיסּוּרֵי (בְּכוּלָּן), לָקֵי בְּאַרְבַּע מַלְקִיּוֹת. אוֹ דִלְמָא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיתַּחְזַק בְּאִיסּוּרָא, לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ הֶיתֵּירָא וְעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, וְהָוֵי בָּתְרָיְיתָא לְאַחוֹלֵי הָוֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Again, the Gemara clarifies the dilemma: **Here, since** it is **certain** that he intended for three unblemished non-sacred animals to be consecrated as substitutes in place of the three unblemished consecrated animals, this **man** has been **established as** one who regularly transgresses **a prohibition.** Therefore, it is assumed that he also intended for one of the unblemished non-sacred animals to be consecrated as a substitute in place of the blemished consecrated animal, which means he is liable to be **flogged four** sets of **lashes** for the four substitutions. **Or perhaps, even though** in this case he has been **established as** one who transgresses **the prohibition,** nevertheless even such **a person does not cast aside the permitted** manner **and perform the prohibition. And** if so, his intention with regard to **this latter** blemished animal was **to desacralize** it. The Gemara concludes that all of these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

*Mahu?* Do we say — *hacha vadai keivan d'itchazek gavra b'issura laki b'arba malkiyot* — that since the pattern has now been established three times over and a *chazakah* is in place, the fourth animal — the *ba'al mum* — is also swept into the *temurah* interpretation, and the speaker receives four sets of *malkot*? This raises a subsidiary question about *hasra'at safek*: since all of this occurs in a single act, one might wonder at what point the warning could have been calibrated. In a sense, the warning itself might define what is being done, and whether one must specify how many sets of lashes the act will incur is not fully clear. Regardless, the Gemara proceeds on the assumption that *malkot* can be administered.

Or do we say — *af al gav d'itchazek b'issura, lo shavke inish de'avid issura*, *v'havi batrayta achulei* — that even an established pattern of *issur* is insufficient to override the favorable interpretation for the *ba'al mum*, and the last animal is still read as *pidyon*?

### Teiku Ending

The Gemara does not resolve this question. *Teiku* — the matter stands unresolved. These questions are among the most conceptually elegant in the tractate, precisely because they expose the layered structure of how ambiguous language is adjudicated in the domain of *kodashim*. The entire architecture emerges: unambiguous language controls; ambiguous language is given the meaningful over the meaningless reading; between two meaningful readings, the favorable trumps the unfavorable; and where even that principle is complicated by majority or by *chazakah*, the question becomes genuinely unresolvable.

## Fair Value

### Effect Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה הֶקְדֵּשׁ בַּעַל מוּם יֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, וְצָרִיךְ לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּמִים מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אַף צָרִיךְ לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּמִים דְּבַר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If the consecrated** animal **was blemished,** and he said: This consecrated animal is desacralized, with its sanctity transferred to that non-sacred animal, the consecrated animal **is desacralized.** The owner is required to conduct an appraisal to ascertain the relative value of the two animals, and if the consecrated animal was worth more than the non-sacred animal, he must pay the difference to the Temple treasury. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The consecrated animal **is desacralized by Torah law** even if the consecrated animal was worth one hundred dinars and the non-sacred animal was worth only one *peruta*. **But by rabbinic law,** the owner **is required to conduct** an appraisal to ascertain the relative **value** of the two animals and to pay the difference to the Temple treasury, so that the Temple treasury not suffer a loss. **And Reish Lakish says: Even the requirement to conduct** an appraisal, to ascertain the relative **value** of the two animals and to pay the difference to the Temple treasury, applies **by Torah law.**

The Gemara turns to a new topic introduced by the final clause of the *Mishnah*: *vetzarich la'asot damim* — when performing *pidyon*, one must assess the value. The question is what happens when the exchange was unequal. The last line of the *Mishnah* stated simply that when a *ba'al mum* is redeemed, value must be determined. The Gemara now asks: if one did not achieve an equal exchange, what is the status of the *pidyon*?

Rabbi Yochanan says: *yatza lechullin devar Torah* — the desanctification takes effect *de'oraita* even if the exchange was unequal. The requirement to make up the difference is *midivreihem*, rabbinic. Reish Lakish disagrees: *af tzarich la'asot damim devar Torah* — even biblically, a fair exchange is required, and if it was not achieved, the *pidyon* does not take effect at all.

### Ona'ah Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִי נֵימָא אַאוֹנָאָה, בְּהָא נֵימָא רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אַף צָרִיךְ לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּמִים דְּבַר תּוֹרָה? וְהָא תְנַן: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אוֹנָאָה: הָעֲבָדִים, וְהַשְּׁטָרוֹת, וְהַקַּרְקָעוֹת, וְהַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **What are we dealing with** here, i.e., what is the case that is subject to the disagreement between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish? **If we say** that they disagree in a case where the difference in value between the two animals was exactly one-sixth, which is the difference **in** value that constitutes **exploitation** by Torah law, and the *halakha* is that such a transaction is valid but one must return the difference in value, would **Reish Lakish say** that **even** in such a case **the requirement to conduct** an appraisal to ascertain the relative **value** of the two animals applies **by Torah law?** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 56a): **These are matters that,** even if the disparity between the value and the payment is one-sixth, **are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation: Slaves, documents, lands, and consecrated property.**Since consecrated property is not subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation, why would Reish Lakish say that one must repay the difference in value between the two animals to the Temple treasury by Torah law?

The Gemara asks: what precisely is the nature of the shortfall being discussed? If it was the amount of *ona'ah* — the threshold that governs overcharging in ordinary commercial transactions — the analysis becomes more complex, because *hekdesh* occupies a unique position in the *halachot* of *ona'ah*.

By way of background: the law of *ona'ah* prohibits charging above the market rate. If the overcharge is less than one-sixth, the sale stands and the overcharged party is presumed to have waived the difference. If it is exactly one-sixth, the overcharge must be returned but the sale holds. If it exceeds one-sixth, the entire transaction is void — *mekach ta'ut*, a mistaken sale. Now the *Mishnah* in Bava Metzia teaches that *ona'ah* does not apply to slaves, documents, land, and *hekdesh*. This means that by *hekdesh*, one cannot invoke *ona'ah* to demand return of an overage.

Given this, the Gemara asks: if the shortfall was exactly one-sixth, would Reish Lakish say that *de'oraita* it must be made up? The *Mishnah* has already taught that *ona'ah* does not apply to *hekdesh* — how then can Reish Lakish insist on a biblical obligation to return the overage? *Ela bittul mekach* — the case under discussion must therefore be one of more than one-sixth, the threshold for *bittul mekach*, nullification of the entire transaction. Perhaps at that level of disproportion, even *hekdesh* — excluded only from the *ona'ah* sixth-return rule — is subject to nullification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַבִּטּוּל מִקָּח בְּהָא נֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן צָרִיךְ לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּמִים מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אַקַּרְקָעוֹת דְּחוּלִּין, וְרַבִּי יוֹנָה אָמַר אַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת, תַּרְוַויְיהוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי: אוֹנָאָה אֵין לָהֶן, בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח יֵשׁ לָהֶם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish disagree with regard to a case **of nullification of a transaction,** i.e., where the difference in value between the two animals was greater than one-sixth. Would **Rabbi Yoḥanan say** in such a case that one is **required to conduct** an appraisal to ascertain the relative **value** of the two animals **by rabbinic law?** **But didn't Rabbi Yirmeya say** this ruling **with regard to non-sacred lands, and Rabbi Yona said** it **with regard to consecrated property, and both of them said** it **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: They are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation,** in the case of a disparity of one-sixth. But **they are** subject to **nullification of a transaction,** in the case of a greater disparity. If so, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yona, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that when the disparity between the value and the payment is greater than one-sixth, the transaction is nullified by Torah law. But in that case, why would Rabbi Yoḥanan rule here that only by rabbinic law is he required to calculate the difference and pay its value?

The Gemara then asks whether, given all this, we should understand Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish as debating the *bittul mekach* scenario. If so, it would seem that Rabbi Yochanan should also hold that the transaction is void — for there is a tradition in his name (*Rav Yirmiyah* citing Rabbi Yochanan on land, *Rav Yona* citing him on *hekdesh*) that when the disproportion exceeds a sixth, the sale is indeed nullified. This creates an apparent contradiction: how can Rabbi Yochanan say the *pidyon* takes effect if he himself holds *bittul mekach* applies to *hekdesh*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אַבִּטּוּל מִקָּח, וְאֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** they disagree in a case **of nullification of a transaction,** i.e., more than one-sixth, **and** one should **reverse** attribution of the opinions; the opinion previously attributed to Rabbi Yoḥanan is actually that of Reish Lakish, and the opinion attributed to Reish Lakish is the ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The Gemara proposes reversing the attributions of the debate — assigning the "*de'oraita* it doesn't take effect" position to Rabbi Yochanan and the "*divreihem*" position to Reish Lakish, in line with the tradition that Rabbi Yochanan holds *bittul mekach* by *hekdesh*. But this reversal only works according to the version (*Rav Yona*) that Rabbi Yochanan said *bittul mekach* applies specifically to *hekdesh*. According to the other version (*Rav Yirmiyah*), Rabbi Yochanan only applied *bittul mekach* to *karka* but not to *hekdesh* — and according to that version, even Rabbi Yochanan would hold that *hekdesh* is never subject to *bittul mekach*, making the reversal untenable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ אֵיפוֹךְ? הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אַקַּרְקָעוֹת. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אַקַּרְקָעוֹת, אֲבָל הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת אֵין לָהֶן בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח, הֵיכִי אֵיפוֹךְ לְהָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how can you say** that one should **reverse** the attribution of the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish? **This works out well according to the one who says** that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds **that consecrated property** is not subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation if the disparity between the value and the payment is one-sixth, but it is subject to nullification of the transaction if the disparity between the value and the payment is greater than one-sixth. According to this opinion, that of Rabbi Yona, it is apparent **all the more so that lands** are subject to nullification if the disparity is greater than one-sixth. **But according to the one who said** that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds **that** only **lands** are subject to nullification if the disparity is greater than one-sixth, **but** with regard to **consecrated property there is no nullification of a transaction,** i.e., Rabbi Yirmeya, **how** can **he reverse** attribution of **that** opinion, and claim that Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that he is required to pay the difference in value by Torah law? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** According to my opinion, **do not reverse** attribution of the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish. In other words, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that he is required to pay the difference in value by rabbinic law. This is consistent with Rabbi Yirmeya's ruling that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that there is no nullification of a transaction in the case of consecrated property. Only Rabbi Yona would reverse attribution of the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish in order for Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion to be consistent with his statement, as cited by Rabbi Yona, that there is nullification of a transaction in the case of consecrated property.

### Shmuel Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֶקְדֵּשׁ שָׁוֶה מָנֶה שֶׁחִילְּלוֹ עַל שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה מְחוּלָּל. דְּרַבִּי יוֹנָה לֵית לֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אִית לֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. לָא, בֵּין דְּמָר וּבֵין דְּמָר — אִית לֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּרַבִּי יוֹנָה סָבַר: כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל — דִּיעֲבַד, אֲבָל לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא אָמַר, וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It has been established that Rabbi Yona maintains that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan consecrated property is subject to nullification of a transaction, whereas Rabbi Yirmeya disputes that claim. The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that **they disagree with regard to** a statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: Consecrated property worth one hundred dinars [*maneh*] that one desacralized,** with its sanctity transferred **to** a coin **worth one *peruta*, is desacralized.** Since consecrated property is not subject to nullification of a transaction, it is desacralized by coins worth any sum. The suggestion is **that Rabbi Yona is not** of the opinion that the *halakha* follows the ruling **of Shmuel, and Rabbi Yirmeya is of** the opinion that the *halakha* follows the ruling **of Shmuel.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No; both this Sage and that Sage are of** the opinion that the *halakha* follows the ruling **of Shmuel.** The difference between them is **that Rabbi Yona maintains** that **when Shmuel stated** his *halakha*, he was referring to consecrated property that one desacralized **after the fact, but he did not say** that one may do so ***ab initio*; and Rabbi Yirmeya maintains** that Shmuel rules that it is permitted to desacralize consecrated property with coins worth any sum, **even *ab initio*.**

A further complication: the Gemara asks whether the entire debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is a dispute over Shmuel's ruling. Shmuel states: *hekdesh shaveh maneh shechillalo shaveh perutah, mechulal* — if *hekdesh* worth a *maneh* was redeemed onto something worth a single *perutah*, it works *bedi'avad*. One is not permitted to do so — one is cheating *hekdesh* — but post facto it takes effect. This being so, how can either Rabbi Yochanan or Reish Lakish hold that the *pidyon* fails to take effect?

The Gemara answers: *bein lemar bein lemar is lei d'Shmuel* — both positions accept Shmuel's ruling. The debate is not whether Shmuel's ruling is valid, but rather how to interpret it.

### Intent Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ מַתְנִיתִין ״אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים... הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת״ — כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין לָהֶן אוֹנָאָה — אֵינָן בְּתוֹרַת אוֹנָאָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכְּדֵי אוֹנָאָה חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead, with regard to the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Actually, do not reverse** the attribution of their respective opinions. In fact, they disagree with regard to a disparity of one-sixth, and Reish Lakish holds that one is required to pay the difference value by Torah law. **And** as for **that which** poses **a difficulty for you** with regard to the opinion of Reish Lakish, that it is taught in **the mishna: These are matters** that are not subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation: Slaves, documents, lands, and **consecrated property,** which indicates that one is required to pay the difference in value by rabbinic law, that mishna should be understood **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Ḥisda.** **As Rav Ḥisda said:** What is the meaning of the phrase: **Are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation?** This means that **they are not** subject to the standard **principles of exploitation** at all; rather, more stringent *halakhot* apply in these cases. **As, even** if the disparity is **less than the measure of exploitation,** i.e., less than one-sixth, **one may renege** on the transaction, and in the case of consecrated property he is required to pay the value of the disparity.

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension by re-reading Shmuel's *bedi'avad* language in a non-standard way, a re-reading embraced by both Rashi and Tosafot, though acknowledged to be somewhat forced. Normally, *lekatchilah* and *bedi'avad* refer to whether an act was performed properly in the first place or only retrospectively. Here, however, the Gemara reads these terms as referring not to the timing or intentionality of the act in the usual sense, but rather to the speaker's awareness of what he was doing.

When Shmuel says *hekdesh* worth a *maneh* redeemed onto a *perutah* takes effect, that is the case of *lekatchilah* in this reframed sense: the person *knowingly and deliberately* performed an unequal exchange. He was fully aware that the values were wildly mismatched and chose to proceed. In that case, since *hekdesh* is his to transfer in a sense — he created this *kedushah* and has the latitude to redeem it — the act takes effect even though it was impermissible. The *bedi'avad* case, by contrast, is where the person was *trying* to do a fair exchange and it turned out to be unequal without his knowledge. In that scenario, the exchange is so far from what he intended that it fails — not technically as a case of *bittul mekach* in the usual commercial sense, but by analogy to *bittul mekach*: since the outcome was so divergent from his intention, it does not take effect.

Under this framing, the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is not about *bittul mekach* in the traditional sense at all. It is rather about the case where one tried to be fair and failed. Rabbi Yochanan holds that even in that case, the *pidyon* takes effect — make up the difference, but it worked — and the requirement to pay the difference is only rabbinic. Reish Lakish holds that in such a case it does not take effect and must be performed again. Both positions are consistent with Shmuel, who spoke only of the case of deliberate inequality.

This is a genuinely striking re-reading of the entire *sugya*. The reason it is compelling, however, is that the standard model of *bittul mekach* maps imperfectly onto *pidyon*. In the case of a *korban*, there is no "other side" in the way that a seller is the other side in a commercial transaction. It is not as though *hekdesh* owns the animal and is selling it — the owner is simply transferring *kedushah*. The language of voided transaction is thus somewhat artificial when applied here. The Gemara's re-framing as a question of intention versus outcome is therefore both more coherent conceptually and more honest about the unique nature of *pidyon*.

The *Rambam* rules that the obligation to make up the difference is always rabbinic — the *pidyon* takes effect in all cases, and the shortfall is addressed only *mid'rabbanan*. This follows the line of Rabbi Yochanan.

### Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: לָא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא בְּשָׁמוּי בִּתְרֵי, אֲבָל בְּשָׁמוּי בִּתְלָתָא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָתֵי בְּמֵאָה — לָא הָדַר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to a case in which there was a disparity between the assessed value of property and its actual value, where one must pay the difference to the Temple treasury, **Ulla said:** The Sages **said** that one must pay the value of the disparity to the Temple treasury **only** if the value of the consecrated property **was** initially **assessed by** only **two** people and afterward three other people determined that the consecrated property was worth more. **But** in a case where the value of the consecrated property **was** initially **assessed by three** people, as required by *halakha*, and that amount was paid to the Temple treasury, **even if** afterward **one hundred** people **came** and assessed the value of the consecrated property at a higher value, **one** need **not return** the disparity of value to the Temple treasury.

Ulla adds a further qualification to the entire framework. He states: *lo amru ela b'shumai b'trei* — the foregoing rules regarding paying the difference apply only where the initial assessment was performed by two people. Shmuel's case, and the debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, all presuppose a scenario in which two assessors established a value, the exchange was made, and a later assessment revealed the discrepancy.

If, however, the assessment was performed by three — by a *beis din* — then *af al gav d'asi b'me'ah*, even if a hundred subsequent experts arrive and say the assessment was off, the original *beis din* determination stands: *lo hadar*, it does not go back.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: הֵיכָא אָמְרִינַן מְאָה כִּתְרֵי וּתְרֵי כִּמְאָה? לְעִנְיַן עֵדוּת, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן אוּמְדָּנָא — בָּתַר דֵּעוֹת אָזְלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rav Safra say: Where did one say** the ruling that **one hundred** witnesses **are like two** witnesses, **and two** witnesses **are like one hundred** witnesses? That principle applies specifically **to the matter of testimony. But** with regard **to** the **matter of assessments, we follow** the majority of **opinions.** If so, in Ulla's case, one should follow the assessment of the one hundred and pay the disparity to the Temple treasury.

The Gemara challenges this: is a hundred really no different from two? Rav Safra states: *heicha d'amar me'ah k'trei u'trei k'me'ah* — the equivalence of a hundred and two holds for testimony (*eidus*), where there is a formal rule that two witnesses carry the same legal weight as a hundred. But for assessment of values, *basar de'os azlinan* — we follow the weight of expert opinion, and more opinions carry more weight. There is no formal threshold analogous to the two-witness rule that would make three assessors equivalent to a thousand. Accordingly, the later and more authoritative assessment should override the earlier one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ תְּלָתָא וּתְלָתָא, לָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר בָּתְרָא? דְּיַד הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** even in a case where **three** people initially assessed the value of the consecrated property, **and** afterward **three** other people assessed the consecrated property at a higher value, **don't we follow the latter** assessment? Isn't there is a principle **that the** Temple treasury of **consecrated** property always **has the advantage?**

The Gemara presses further: even if it were three assessors on each side, should we not follow the later one at least *yad hekdesh al elyonah* — because *hekdesh* is always entitled to the more favorable outcome? Why should the original assessment of three hold firm?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר עוּלָּא: צָרִיךְ לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּמִים מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם, וְכֹל דְּרַבָּנַן אַקִּילוּ בֵּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Ulla holds** that the *halakha* that the owner **is required to conduct** an appraisal, to ascertain the relative **value** of the two animals and to pay the difference to the Temple treasury, applies **by rabbinic law,** not by Torah law, **and the Sages were lenient** with regard to **all rabbinic laws.** Therefore, even if one hundred people assessed the value of the consecrated property at a higher value than the earlier assessment, one is not required to pay the disparity in value to the Temple treasury.

The Gemara resolves this: Ulla holds like Rabbi Yochanan that the obligation to make up the difference is only rabbinic, and since it is only rabbinic, the Rabbis treated the matter leniently. If a formal *beis din* of three made the assessment, the Rabbis did not require reassessment even if one subsequently became available, in order to make the practical administration of *pidyon* workable. Since the entire obligation is *d'rabbanan*, the Rabbis calibrated their enactment with appropriate leniency.

Tosafot raises a related question: is there not a *halacha* requiring ten people to assess *hekdesh*? The answer is that the requirement of ten applies when the *gazbar* — the temple treasurer — is performing the redemption as a formal act on behalf of *hekdesh*, selling sanctified property that has come into the temple's possession. That is a formal transactional act requiring oversight, and the Gemara derives the number ten from a *pasuk*. But when the *ba'al habayit* — the owner — is performing his own *pidyon* on a *korban* or on property still in his possession, he acts with considerably more latitude. The assessment question in our *sugya* pertains to that latter scenario.

## Generic Language

### Generic Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי זוֹ תַּחַת עוֹלָה״, ״תַּחַת חַטָּאת״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״תַּחַת חַטָּאת זוֹ״, וְ״תַחַת עוֹלָה זוֹ״, ״תַּחַת חַטָּאת וְתַחַת עוֹלָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בַּבַּיִת״ — הָיָה לוֹ, דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said: **This** non-sacred animal **is hereby in place of a burnt offering,** or: It is **in place of a sin offering, he has said nothing,** as he did not say that it was in place of a specific offering. If he said: It is **in place of this sin offering, or:** It is **in place of this burnt offering,** or if he said: It is **in place of a sin offering** that I have in the house, **or:** It is **in place of a burnt offering that I have in the house,** and **he had** that offering in his house, **his statement stands,** i.e., is effective.

The final section of the *perek* addresses a different dimension of *temurah* language: the requirement that the exchange formula must refer to a specific animal, not a generic category. The *Mishnah* states: *harei zo tachas olah tachas chatas, lo amar klum* — if one said "this animal is instead of an *olah*, instead of a *chatas*," without referring to any specific designated animal, one has said nothing. *Temurah* requires an act of attempted replacement directed at a particular sanctified animal. The *kedushah* of an existing *korban* — its *kedushah* clinging to it — is what can be seized upon and transferred to the second animal. A generic reference to a category of sacrifice has no such existing *kedushah* to grab onto, and the statement is therefore meaningless.

If, however, one said *tachas chatas zo* — instead of this particular *chatas* — or *tachas olah zo* — instead of this *olah*, or *tachas chatas v'tachas olah sheyesh li babayit* — instead of the *chatas* and *olah* that I have at home — and one actually possesses those animals, the statement is legally effective. Notably, the designated animals need not be physically present; they need only exist and be specifically identified. *D'varav kayamim* — the words stand, the act of *temurah* is accomplished (with all its negative consequences), and the other animal becomes *kadosh*.

### Non-Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עַל הַבְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה וְעַל בַּעֲלַת מוּם ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ עוֹלָה״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לְעוֹלָה״ — יִמָּכְרוּ, וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶם עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If he **said with regard to a non-kosher animal and with regard to a blemished animal: These** animals **are hereby** designated as **a burnt offering, he has said nothing.** If he said: **These** animals **are hereby** designated **for a burnt offering,** the animals **should be sold, and he brings a burnt offering** purchased **with the money** received from **their** sale.

A different case: one says of a non-kosher animal — a camel, for instance — or of an animal with a *mum*, "behold, this is an *olah*." The *Mishnah* states: *lo amar klum* — one has said nothing. This requires attention, because normally one would assume that sanctifying a *ba'al mum* produces *kedushat damim* — monetary sanctity — even if it cannot be an actual *korban*. The point here is that the phrasing matters: to say that a *ba'al mum* "is an *olah*" is meaningless, because a *ba'al mum* cannot be an *olah*. The declaration fails entirely. If, however, one said *harei elu l'olah* — "these should go toward an *olah*" — then they are sold and the proceeds are used to purchase an *olah*. The phrasing *l'olah*, expressing purpose rather than identity, imports *kedushat damim* and is effective.

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — הָא אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if one said: This non-sacred animal is hereby in place of a burnt offering, he has said nothing. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as, if it** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, didn't** Rabbi Meir say: **A person does not utter a statement for naught.** In other words, if one issues a statement that cannot be fulfilled as stated, it is interpreted in a manner that renders it relevant. Consequently, when he said: This non-sacred animal is in place of a burnt offering, he must have been referring to a burnt offering that he had in his house.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav states: *masnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — our *Mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Meir. For Rabbi Meir holds *ein adam motzi devarav l'vatalah* — a person does not utter words in vain, and we will interpret one's words in a meaningful way wherever possible. Tosafot notes that this principle could apply to the first clause of the *Mishnah* — taking the generic formula *harei zo tachas olah* and interpreting it as if it meant *tachas olah zo*, thereby rendering it an act of *temurah* directed at some specific animal. But Tosafot finds it more convincing that Rabbi Meir's principle applies primarily to the second clause: even when one says *harei zo olah* of a *ba'al mum* or a camel, Rabbi Meir would interpret it as *harei zo l'olah* — for an *olah* — rather than saying that the words accomplish nothing. The leap from "this is an *olah*" to "this should go toward an *olah*" is smaller than the leap from a generic formula to a specific *temurah*, and Rabbi Meir's interpretive charity operates more naturally there.

### Gender Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תִּימָּכֵר שֶׁלֹּא בְּמוּם.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as we learned** in a mishna (19b) that **Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of one who designates a female animal for a guilt offering, since a female is unfit to be sacrificed as that offering, its halakhic status is like that of a blemished animal in the sense that it does not become inherently sacred; rather, only its value is sacred. Therefore, **it may be sold without** it having developed **a blemish,** and a guilt offering is purchased with the money received from its sale.

The Gemara appends a final observation. The *Mishnah* addressed non-kosher animals and *ba'alei mum* in connection with the rule that they do not need a *mum* to be redeemed: *d'lo chazyan lo ba'in muma* — since they are unfit for offering, the requirement of a *mum* as a precondition for redemption does not apply. A camel sanctified for *hekdesh* can be redeemed without any *mum*; it is analogous to sanctifying a car, which equally needs no *mum*.

But what of an animal of the wrong gender designated for a sacrifice that requires a different gender — a female dedicated as an *asham* or as an *olah* where a male is required? Such an animal is not as radically unfit as a camel; it is a *kosher behemah* of the wrong specification. The Gemara implies that the *Mishnah*'s silence on this case indicates that it does require a *mum* before redemption — it is not so fundamentally disqualified as to be treated like a non-kosher animal. Rav Yehuda citing Rav concludes: *masnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon*, for Rabbi Shimon states *timacher shelo b'mum* — such animals may be sold without a *mum*. Our *Mishnah* apparently holds otherwise: wrong gender does not rise to the level of radical disqualification that would dispense with the *mum* requirement, and therefore the standard rule applies.

*Hadran lach tish'at mar'in.*
