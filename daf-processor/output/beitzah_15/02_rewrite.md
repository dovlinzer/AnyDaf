# Beitzah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shatnez & Nolad

### Overview & Intro

Today's daf is 15. We will conclude the first *perek* and begin the second *perek*, starting at the very bottom of 14b. The *mishna* states that one may send all various types of items on *Yom Tov* according to *Beis Hillel*, provided the item has at least some theoretical use on *Yom Tov*. One of the items listed is *kilayim shatnez* — a mixture of linen and wool. The *gemara* therefore seeks to determine what legitimate use can be made of *shatnez* on *Yom Tov*.

### Shatnez as Curtain

One cannot claim it may be used for sitting, because although that is technically permitted on a biblical level, the Rabbis prohibited sitting on *shatnez* even when it is beneath ten cushions, out of concern that a thread might come to rest directly against one's skin in a manner that would constitute wearing. The *gemara* therefore concludes — with the word *ella* at the very last line of 14b — *ella b'vilo*: we are discussing using the *shatnez* as a curtain, a *vilon*. Since it functions as a hanging curtain rather than a garment worn against the skin, there is no prohibition.

At this point the *gemara* cites a teaching of Ulla: *mipnei mah amru vilon tamei* — why did the Rabbis rule that a *vilon* is susceptible to *tumah*? Why is a curtain not considered a structural part of the house, exempt from the laws of *tumah* applicable to vessels? Ulla answers: *mipnei she'hashamash mischamem kenegdo* — because an attendant will warm himself by the curtain, wrapping himself in it, thereby qualifying it as a vessel with a functional use. If this is so, however, the same concern ought to apply to *shatnez*: one might come to wrap oneself in the curtain for warmth, which would constitute wearing. The *gemara* therefore recognizes a difficulty.

### Hard Shatnez

The *gemara* resolves this on 15a by saying *ella b'kashan* — we must be speaking of a very stiff, hard type of cloth, one with no loose threads, which produces no warmth and therefore raises no concern, even rabbinically, that one will come to wear it. The rabbinic restriction on sitting on *shatnez* would similarly not apply: the cloth is hard and stiff, sitting on it is in any case biblically permitted, and since a loose thread cannot migrate onto one's body and produce warmth, the concern is absent.

This principle — that warmth is a defining characteristic of wearing — is articulated by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *hai namta gamda d'Narash sharei* — that hard garment from the town of Narash is permitted. According to Rashi, this means it is permitted not to wear, but to sit on: it is *shatnez*, but because it is hard and one is sitting on it rather than wearing it, it is permitted.

Rav Pappa then adds: *ardelin ein bahem mishum kilayim* — the *ardelin*, which Rashi explains on the basis of Geonic literature as a type of material placed at the heel of the foot, perhaps an insole or a heel-pad, does not constitute *kilayim*. Though it is part of one's footwear, it sits beneath the foot rather than on the body, and being hard, it raises no concern of warmth.

Rava adds a further case: *hanei tzrari d'pshiti* — those small coin-pouches made of *shatnez* fabric that one keeps pressed against the chest contain no *kilayim* violation, because although the pouch rests against the body, it provides no warmth and therefore does not meet the definition of wearing.

One might ask whether this warmth-based leniency extends to something genuinely worn, such as a belt. Rashi is clear, and *Tosafot* discusses this at length: the relevance of warmth as a limiting criterion applies only within the rabbinic domain, in cases where the item is not functioning as ordinary clothing. Where something is genuinely being worn — worn in the full biblical sense — the question of warmth does not operate as a leniency. If something is real clothing, it is prohibited regardless.

The *gemara* adds a nuance with respect to the coin-pouches: if one keeps coins in the pouch, it remains stiff and provides no warmth — *ein bahem mishum kilayim*. But if one keeps seeds (*bazreini*) in it, the pouch becomes malleable and effectively supple against the body — *yesh bahem mishum kilayim*, this is a problem. Rav Ashi, however, rules that in either case there is no violation, reasoning that since this is not the normal manner of warming oneself, even if it happens to provide warmth, it does not constitute *derech chimum* and therefore does not meet the legal definition of wearing. The centrality of this concept of purposive warmth as the defining criterion of *levisha* is thus the thread running through this entire sugya.

### Nails & Unsewn

The *mishna* then states: *aval lo sandal hamesumar* — but one may not send a nailed sandal as a gift on *Yom Tov*. This is followed by additional cases: one may also not send a shoe that is not yet sewn (*min'al she'eino tafur*), and Rabbi Yehuda adds that one may not send an unsewn white leather shoe.

### Sandal Story

The *gemara* asks: *sandal hamesumar mai ta'ama lo* — why not send a nailed sandal? The answer is *mishum ma'aseh shehayah* — because of the famous incident. This refers to an episode discussed in Masechet Shabbat: Jews hiding from the Romans in a cave heard a noise, feared it was the enemy, and in their panic rushed out of the cave. In the resulting crush, people were trampled to death, and the nailed sandals — whose nails apparently protruded from the sole like metal cleats — contributed to the carnage. The Rabbis thereafter decreed that one may never wear a *sandal hamesumar*.

One might ask: why does this prohibition apply specifically on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*? The *gemara* implies that the incident occurred in that context, but logically there is no reason to limit the prohibition to those days. One practical answer is that on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, when there is already a long list of restrictions, people are more likely to heed an additional prohibition. As Rav Moshe was once asked about recorded music during *Sefirah*: he responded that he believes recorded music is problematic throughout the year, but since nobody listens, at least one can enforce the restriction during *Sefirah* when people are already accustomed to restrictions.

### Abaye on Sandals

Abaye clarifies the precise contours of the *sandal hamesumar* prohibition: *sandal hamesumar assur lina'alo umutar letaltelo* — one may not wear it, but it is not *muktzeh* and may be moved. The proof that it is not *muktzeh* comes from the language of the *mishna* itself: *ein meshalchin* — one does not send it as a gift. If the sandal were truly *muktzeh*, the *mishna* would not need to specify that one cannot send it; the *muktzeh* prohibition would already cover that. The fact that sending it is singled out as the prohibition implies that moving it is otherwise permitted — that it has some use, just not wearing.

If so, one might ask: since it does have some use — one might scratch one's back with it or find another incidental purpose — why can it not be sent as a gift on *Yom Tov*, just as *shatnez* can be sent because it has some use? The answer, which will become clearer presently, is that the concern here is not merely one of usability but of appearance: if one sends a nailed sandal as a gift, the recipient will assume the sender intended it to be worn, and will come to wear it. It is this specific concern that led the Rabbis to prohibit sending it.

### Unsewn Shoes

The *mishna* also addresses *min'al she'eino tafur* — a shoe whose leather has not yet been sewn to the sole. The *gemara* notes: *pshita* — this is obvious, there is no use for it on *Yom Tov*. The *gemara* counters: *lo tzericha*, the ruling is necessary even in a case where the shoe is held together by small pegs (*pasiche*), so that it is nominally attached but not fully sewn. One might have thought that since it can temporarily be worn in that state, it is permissible to send — the *mishna* teaches it is not.

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af lo min'al lavan* — not even a white shoe may be sent. *Tanya*: a *baraisa* records that Rabbi Yehuda permits a shoe once the leather has been blackened (*bashachor matir*) but forbids it while it remains white (*ossur belavan*), because it still requires the blackening compound to be worked into it. Rabbi Yosi goes further: even a blackened shoe is forbidden because it still requires polishing — *mipnei shetzarich letzachtzacho* — it is not yet complete.

### Shoe Regions

The *gemara* resolves the apparent dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi by observing that each ruled in accordance with local practice in his region: *mar ki atreih u-mar ki atreih*. In one sage's locale, the leather was placed flesh-side down against the foot — that is, the inner surface of the hide, which was softer, faced the wearer. In such a case the outer surface, which had more cracks and crevices, was on the outside and required more preparation before it was presentable. In the other sage's locale, the flesh side faced outward, and therefore it was that surface which was rough and required shining. Each sage's ruling thus reflects the particular construction methods and finishing standards of his region, and there is no actual disagreement in principle.

### Principle: Usage

The *mishna* concludes with a general rule: *zeh ha-klal kol she-na'asim bo b'Yom Tov meshalechin oto* — the principle is that anything that can be used on *Yom Tov* may be sent as a gift.

## Tefillin & Yom Tov

### Rav Sheshet

The *gemara* states: *Rav Sheshet shara lehu l'rabanan l'shidurei tfillin b'yoma tava* — Rav Sheshet permitted the rabbinic students to send *tefillin* as gifts on *Yom Tov*. This is immediately striking, because the obvious question is: what use can one make of *tefillin* on *Yom Tov*? *Tefillin* are not worn on *Yom Tov* — *Yom Tov* is itself referred to as an *os*, a sign, as is *Shabbat*, and where the sign of *Yom Tov* or *Shabbat* is present, the sign of *tefillin* is not required.

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets the *mishna*'s principle. The language of the *mishna* is: *zeh ha-klal kol she-na'asim bo b'Yom Tov meshalechin oto*. The word *b'Yom Tov* may modify either the use (*she-na'asim*) — anything usable on *Yom Tov* itself may be sent — or the sending (*meshalechin*) — anything usable at all may be sent on *Yom Tov*. Rav Yosef reads it the latter way: *kol she-na'asim bo b'chol meshalechin oto b'Yom Tov* — anything that one can use on a weekday may be sent on *Yom Tov* as a gift, even if it cannot be used on *Yom Tov* itself. This is an admittedly broad reading, but it resolves the *tefillin* question.

### Rashi/Tosafot

This solution, however, raises the problem of the *sandal hamesumar*: by the same logic, one could use a nailed sandal on a weekday, so why not send it on *Yom Tov*? Rashi and *Tosafot* address this directly, though interestingly the *gemara* itself does not. Their answer is that the concern with the *sandal hamesumar* is not merely about usability but about the risk that the recipient will assume the item is permitted for wearing and will come to wear it on *Yom Tov*. With *tefillin*, this concern does not apply: everyone knows that *tefillin* are not worn on *Yom Tov*, so no one will be misled.

Rashi and *Tosafot* also make a fascinating comparative observation. Even if someone were to err and put on *tefillin* on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* — when it is *lav zman tefillin*, not the time for *tefillin* — the violation involved is arguably less severe than transgressing the rabbinic prohibition of wearing a *sandal hamesumar*. Wearing *tefillin* when it is not their time is a matter of debate but does not clearly constitute a violation of a specific prohibition. By contrast, wearing a *sandal hamesumar* violates an explicit rabbinic enactment rooted in a tragic historical incident.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges Rav Sheshet's ruling directly: *v'anan tnan kol she-na'asim bo b'Yom Tov meshalechin oto* — the *mishna* says anything usable on *Yom Tov* may be sent. Since *tefillin* cannot be used on *Yom Tov*, how can one send them? The *gemara* responds with the reinterpretation described above — that *b'Yom Tov* modifies the sending rather than the use.

### Road Scenario

Abaye, perhaps dissatisfied with that exchange, pivots: *tfillin ho'il v'atu l'idan neima behu milta* — since we have arrived at the topic of *tefillin*, let us say something further about them. The *gemara* presents the case of one who was traveling on the road wearing *tefillin* and the sun set — either nightfall on a weekday, when one should not wear *tefillin*, or, as Rashi explains, Friday afternoon when *Shabbat* arrives and *tefillin* must not be worn on *Shabbat*. The ruling is: *mani'ach yado aleihem ad shemagi'a l'veito* — one places one's hand over the *tefillin* and walks home without removing them. On a weekday, removing them risks dropping them; on *Shabbat*, removing them would constitute carrying. Covering them prevents the misleading appearance of wearing *tefillin* at night or on *Shabbat*, and one waits until reaching home.

### Study Hall

The *gemara* then presents a parallel case: one was sitting in the *beis medrash* with *tefillin* on one's head — and *ukiddesh alav hayom* — *Shabbat* commenced. Note the distinction in formulation: *shak'ah alav chamah* (the sun set) in the road case, versus *ukiddesh alav hayom* (the day became sanctified) here, indicating that the second scenario explicitly involves *Shabbat*. Now one faces a compound problem: wearing *tefillin* at night, the prohibition on *Shabbat* itself, and the issue of carrying them home — for the *beis medrash* is out in the fields, unguarded and unlocked, and leaving *tefillin* there unprotected is unacceptable. The ruling is again: *mani'ach yado aleihem ad shemagi'a l'veito* — cover them and walk home.

One might wonder: does wearing something on one's body on *Shabbat* not constitute carrying? The answer is that carrying in the manner of wearing (*derech levisha*) is treated differently from ordinary carrying. However, for *tefillin* — which are not normally worn on *Shabbat* or at night — this is not genuinely *derech levisha*, and at minimum it places the act in the rabbinic domain. The Rabbis permitted this rabbinic form of carrying in order to bring the *tefillin* to safety, even though under ordinary circumstances one would not be permitted to do so.

### Three Cases

The *gemara* now introduces a challenge from a *baraisa* that presents the same scenarios but with different rulings. In the road case: *mani'ach yado aleihem ad shemagi'a l'vais hasamuch lechomah* — one covers the *tefillin* and may bring them only to the first house near the city wall, not all the way home. In the *beis medrash* case: *mani'ach yado aleihem ad shemagi'a l'vais hasamuch l'veis hamedrash* — one may bring them only to the nearest private house that will safeguard them.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — ha demintara, ha delo mintara* — there is no difficulty. If the *tefillin* will be adequately protected in the first available house, one must leave them there. If they will not be protected even in the first house, one is permitted to carry them all the way home.

The *gemara* then presses further: if the concern is that they are entirely unprotected, why limit the leniency to cases where one is already wearing the *tefillin*? Even if they were lying on the ground, one should be permitted to put them on and carry them home by wearing them. And indeed, from the *mishna* in Masechet Shabbat: *hamotzei tefillin — machenesam zug zug* — one who finds *tefillin* may bring them in one pair at a time, putting them on and wearing them as the means of carrying them into the city. So why does the *baraisa* only permit this when one was already wearing the *tefillin*?

### Jewish Robbers

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing three distinct cases. The first: the *tefillin* are exposed to all manner of danger — dogs, theft, any threat. In this case, even if the *tefillin* were lying on the ground, one may pick them up, put them on, and wear them all the way home. The second: *demintara mechamas kalbei v'lo demintara mechamas ganvei* — the *tefillin* are protected from dogs (they are inside the *beis medrash*) but not from thieves, since the building is unlocked. Here is where the *gemara* makes its striking observation: *mahu deseima rov listim Yisrael ninhu v'lo mezalzelu behu* — one might have thought that since most thieves in the area are Jewish, and a Jewish thief would not treat *tefillin* disrespectfully, perhaps there is no real danger to the sanctity of the *tefillin* even if they are stolen. Therefore, one might think, there is no grounds to permit putting on the *tefillin* on *Shabbat* to bring them home. The *gemara* rules — *ka mashma lan* — that this reasoning is rejected, and the leniency does apply: since thieves might still steal them, even if they would not mistreat them, the leniency of wearing them home is granted. However, this applies only if one is already wearing them; if the *tefillin* are merely lying there, one may not pick them up and put them on in this intermediate scenario. The third case: the *tefillin* are safe from all significant danger — in that situation, the latitude given is minimal, and one may bring them only to the first available house, not all the way home.

Thus there are three graduated scenarios: fully unprotected, permitting one to put on the *tefillin* and carry them home; partially unprotected from thieves who would respect them, permitting continuation of wearing but not initiating it; and sufficiently safe, in which case only minimal transport to the nearest house is allowed.

With that, *hadran alach pirka* — we conclude the first *perek*.

## Eruv Tavshilin

### Prohibition/Permission

The second *perek* opens with the case of *Yom Tov she'chal l'hiyos erev Shabbos* — a one-day *Yom Tov* that falls on Friday (as in *Eretz Yisrael*). The *mishna* states: *lo yevashel betchila miyom tov l'Shabbos* — one may not, at the outset, cook on *Yom Tov* simply in order to prepare for *Shabbat*. However: *aval mevashel hu l'Yom Tov v'im hosir hosir l'Shabbos* — one may cook for *Yom Tov*, and whatever is left over from the *Yom Tov* meal may be used for *Shabbat*.

One might initially think this creates a wink-and-nod solution: cook abundantly for *Yom Tov* and enjoy the leftovers on *Shabbat*. The *gemara*, however, will make clear that this cannot be done *derech haarama* — as a contrived strategy. Only if one genuinely cooked for *Yom Tov* may one benefit from the leftovers; one cannot pretend to require a large quantity in order to manufacture surplus for *Shabbat*.

### Eruv Remedy

The proper mechanism for cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* is the *eruv tavshilin*. The *mishna* states: *v'oseh tavshil me'erev Yom Tov v'somech alav l'Shabbos* — one prepares a cooked dish on *erev Yom Tov* and relies upon it for *Shabbat*, thereby permitting one to continue cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat's* sake. The rationale is that by cooking something on *erev Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*, one has already begun the *Shabbat* preparations before *Yom Tov* began. The cooking on *Yom Tov* is thus a continuation of what was initiated on Thursday, and not a fresh act of cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* from scratch — *betchila*.

### Shammai/Hillel

How much must one cook for the *eruv tavshilin*? *Beis Shammai* says *sheni tavshilin* — two separate cooked dishes. *Beis Hillel* says *tavshil echad* — one cooked dish suffices. Despite this dispute, they agree that if one has a fish and the egg used to prepare it, *she'hein sheni tavshilin* — this counts as two dishes for *Beis Shammai*, who is thus somewhat flexible in how the second item may be constituted.

### Loss of Eruv

If the *eruv tavshilin* was eaten or lost — *achlo o she'avad* — then even though it was properly prepared on *erev Yom Tov*, one may not continue cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*, because the *eruv* no longer exists. However: *v'im shayar mimeno kol shehu somech alav l'Shabbos* — if even a minimal amount of the *eruv* remains, one may continue cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*.

### Zachor Verse

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for this institution? Shmuel cites the verse: *Zachor et yom haShabbos l'kadsho* — Remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it. The *gemara* derives from this: *zochreihu me'acher sheba l'hashkicho* — remember it because something is coming that will cause you to forget it. The whole institution of *eruv tavshilin* is rooted in the fear that *Shabbat* will be forgotten.

### Rashi View

What does it mean that one will forget *Shabbat*? Rashi explains: one becomes so absorbed in the festivity of *Yom Tov* — in the permitted cooking, in the *simchas Yom Tov* — that one simply neglects to prepare adequately for *Shabbat*. The *eruv tavshilin* prepared on *erev Yom Tov* serves as a reminder: when *Yom Tov* arrives and one sees what was set aside, one remembers that *Shabbat* is still coming and that preparations must be made. It is, on this reading, essentially a mnemonic device, a string around one's finger.

### Ran View

The Ran offers a different explanation. The forgetting is not psychological but legal: because of the prohibition on cooking from *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*, one will come to have nothing prepared for *Shabbat* — not because one forgot it exists, but because one was legally constrained from preparing for it. The *eruv tavshilin* therefore serves not merely as a reminder but as the mechanism that unlocks the legal permission to cook for *Shabbat*. On this reading, cooking from *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* is fundamentally prohibited, and the *eruv tavshilin* is the Rabbinic remedy — the tool that converts the otherwise forbidden act into a permitted continuation of prior preparations.

The Ran presses his own position further: if the purpose of requiring an *eruv tavshilin* is to ensure one prepares for *Shabbat*, one might expect that forgetting to make the *eruv* would be remedied leniently, since after all the entire institution is meant to help. But in fact the opposite is true: if one forgot to make an *eruv tavshilin*, one cannot cook for *Shabbat* at all. This, the Ran explains, is intentional — the penalty for negligence on this *Yom Tov* is that one is unable to prepare for *Shabbat*, so that one will be more careful on the next *Yom Tov*.

### Rava Logic

How does the *eruv tavshilin* actually function as a legal mechanism? The *gemara* records the explanation of Rava: *kedei sheyivru maneh yafeh l'Shabbos u'maneh yafeh l'Yom Tov* — so that one will designate a fine dish for *Shabbat* and a fine dish for *Yom Tov*. The act of setting aside a dish on *erev Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* trains one to think, when *Yom Tov* arrives, that *Shabbat* still requires its own fine preparation. This is the functional analog of Rashi's explanation: the *eruv* is a reminder mechanism, and the consequence of not making it is the penalty of being unable to cook for *Shabbat* on *Yom Tov*, which provides the incentive to remember in the future.

One might ask: but what is the actual legal logic? The reminder reminds one to cook — but what permits the cooking? The Ran articulates the difficulty precisely: on Rava's reading, the entire point is to ensure that one has food for *Shabbat*. If one forgot to make an *eruv tavshilin*, the punishment is to have no food for *Shabbat* — which makes the situation worse, not better. The Ran's resolution, already noted above, is that this outcome is intentional: the short-term penalty produces long-term compliance. But this does mean, on Rava's reading, that fundamentally the cooking from *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* is permitted, and the *eruv* requirement is a rabbinic imposition designed to ensure appropriate mindfulness — not a gateway through which an otherwise forbidden act becomes permitted.

### Rav Ashi Logic

Rav Ashi offers a substantially different and more internally coherent explanation: *kedei she'yomru ein ofin miYom Tov l'Shabbos kal v'chomer miYom Tov l'chol* — the purpose is so that people will say: one may not bake on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*, and certainly not from *Yom Tov* for a weekday. Without this requirement, people would come to assume that *Yom Tov* is of sufficiently lower sanctity that one may freely cook on it for the next day, including even for a weekday. To underscore the sacred character of *Yom Tov* and prevent this erosion, cooking from *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* is only permitted when structured as a continuation of what was begun before *Yom Tov*.

On Rav Ashi's reading, then, there is a fundamental legal problem with cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* — or even more so for a weekday — and the *eruv tavshilin* is the mechanism by which what would otherwise be an independent, prohibited act is reframed as a legitimate continuation. This reading is characterized as *l'k'vod Yom Tov* — the whole institution is designed to honor the sanctity of *Yom Tov* — whereas Rava's reading is *l'k'vod Shabbos* — it is designed to ensure proper honor for *Shabbat*.

The question of *hachanah d'Rabbah* is relevant here: Rabbah elsewhere derived from the verse *et asher tofu efu* that there is a *d'oraisa* prohibition of preparing on *Yom Tov* for the next day. *Tosafot*, in a discussion on 2b, asks how a rabbinic institution like *eruv tavshilin* can uproot a biblical prohibition of *hachanah*. *Tosafot* addresses this, suggesting that the biblical *hachanah* concern applies to things that are complete in themselves and only lack preparation — and that there are other resolutions as well, including Rava's principle of *ho'il u'miklei orchin*. The question is acknowledged as a penetrating one, and it is satisfying to see that *Tosafot* explicitly raises it.

### Why Erev?

The *gemara* now asks: the *mishna* states that the *eruv tavshilin* must be made on *erev Yom Tov*. On Rav Ashi's reading this is self-evident — the entire point is that one must begin the cooking before *Yom Tov* commences, so that the *Yom Tov* cooking is a continuation rather than an independent act. But on Rava's reading — where the *eruv* is simply a reminder — why must it specifically be made on *erev Yom Tov*? One could make such a reminder even on *Yom Tov* itself. The *gemara* responds: *in hachi nami* — in principle that would suffice, since making the *eruv* at any point reminds one to prepare for *Shabbat*. However, *gezeirah shema yifsha* — we decree otherwise out of concern for negligence. If one is permitted to wait until *Yom Tov* begins, one will be caught up in *Yom Tov* and forget to make it at all. Therefore the Rabbis required it to be made before *Yom Tov*, so that it becomes part of one's pre-*Yom Tov* checklist.

### Bake/Cook Verse

The *gemara* then cites a *baraisa* that grounds the *eruv tavshilin* in a different verse: *et asher tofu efu v'et asher tevashelu bashelu* — bake what you are going to bake and cook what you are going to cook. Rabbi Eliezer derives from this: *ein ofin ela al ha'afui v'ein mevashelin ela al hamevushal* — one may only continue baking on something that has already been baked, and only continue cooking on something already cooked. This is the direct textual support, serving as an *asmachta*, for the principle that one may cook on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* only by extending what one began previously — and this verse's dual mention of baking and cooking separately will later give rise to the requirement for an *eruv* containing both a baked item (*matzah*) and a cooked one (typically an egg).

## Rabbi Eliezer Story

### Six Groups

The *gemara* has just mentioned Rabbi Eliezer in connection with the *eruv tavshilin*, and now introduces a narrative about him. *Maaseh b'Rabbi Eliezer shehaya yoshev v'doresh kol hayom kulo* — there is a story that Rabbi Eliezer was teaching *halachos* of *Yom Tov* on *Yom Tov* itself, and the *shiur* went on for the entire day. As groups of students successively rose and departed, Rabbi Eliezer commented on each departure. When the first group left — *yatzta kas rishonah* — he said: *halalu baalei pitasos* — these are people with great barrels of wine; they are so abundantly supplied that they cannot wait. As the second group departed he called them *baalei chaviyos* — people with standard barrels. The third group departing earned the appellation *baalei kadim* — people with pitchers. The fourth, *baalei luginim* — people with smaller vessels. The fifth, *baalei kosos* — people who have cups of wine waiting.

### Criticism Escalates

When the sixth group began to leave — *hischilu kas shishis latzes* — Rabbi Eliezer said: *halalu baalei me'eira* — these are people of curse. Rashi explains this harsh judgment: by this point, virtually no one remained in the *beis medrash* from the general congregation, and Rabbi Eliezer feared he would be left with only his students. The *Aruch* offers an alternative reading: they are cursed because they have sacrificed the best of the *shiur* by staying so long without gaining its full benefit, and yet they are returning so late that they will not enjoy a proper meal either — they have lost in both directions.

### Reassurance

At this point, Rabbi Eliezer turned his gaze upon his students: *nosnu einov b'talmidim* — he fixed his eyes on them. Their faces began to change color — *hayesa p'neihem mishtannim* — for now only the students remained, and they feared his displeasure was directed at them. Rabbi Eliezer immediately reassured them: *banai, lo lachem ani omer* — my sons, what I said was not about you. *Ella l'hallalu sheyatzu* — it was about those who left. *Shemanichim chayei olam v'oskim b'chayei sha'ah* — they abandoned eternal life, the Torah, and busied themselves with the life of the moment, choosing food over the opportunity to learn on the *regel*.

### Permission to Eat

When the *shiur* finally concluded, Rabbi Eliezer said to his remaining students: *lechu ochlu mashmannim ush'su mamtakim v'shilchu manos l'ein nachon lo* — go, eat fine foods and drink sweet drinks, and send portions to one who has nothing prepared. *Ki kadosh hayom l'Adoneinu* — today is consecrated to our Lord. This verse is from the time of Ezra. *V'al te'atzeivu ki chedvas Hashem hi mauzchem* — do not be saddened, for the joy of God is your strength. Rabbi Eliezer reassured the students: do not be distressed that you have not yet eaten, for you have been sustained by the joy of Torah. Now the time has come to go home and enjoy the *Yom Tov* meal.

### All-or-Nothing

The *gemara* asks: *v'ha simchas Yom Tov mitzvah hi* — but *simchas Yom Tov* is a *mitzvah*! Why did Rabbi Eliezer give people a hard time for going home to eat? The *gemara* answers: *Rabbi Eliezer l'taimei* — Rabbi Eliezer acts consistently with his own position. *D'amar simchas Yom Tov reshus* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the physical dimension of *simcha* on *Yom Tov* — eating and drinking — is optional, not obligatory. This is stated explicitly in a *baraisa*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer, ein lo l'adam b'Yom Tov ella, o ochel v'shoseh, o yoshev v'shoneh* — a person on *Yom Tov* must choose: either spend the entire day eating and drinking, or sit and learn. It is an all-or-nothing proposition. There are two forms of *simcha*, physical and spiritual, and Rabbi Eliezer demands full commitment to one or the other. His critique of the departing groups was therefore not that they left to fulfill a *mitzvah*, but that they attended the *shiur* and then abandoned it midway. If one intended to devote the day to food, one should have stayed home; if one came to learn, one should have remained until the end.

## Rabbi Yehoshua

### Verse Reconciliation

Rabbi Yehoshua takes the more moderate and familiar position. The *gemara* records: Rabbi Yochanan observed that both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua derive their positions from a single verse — or rather, from a tension between two formulations.

### Two Interpretations

One verse states: *atzeres l'Hashem Elokecha* — it is a holy convocation for God. Another states: *atzeres tihyeh lachem* — it shall be for you. Is the day dedicated entirely to God, or entirely to you? Rabbi Eliezer resolves the tension by ruling: *o kulo l'Hashem o kulo lachem* — either all of it for God, spent entirely in Torah study, or all of it for you, spent entirely in eating and drinking. Rabbi Yehoshua resolves it with his characteristic principle of *chelkeihu*: divide it. *Chetzyo l'Hashem v'chetzyo lachem* — half of the day is devoted to God through Torah and prayer, and the other half to one's own physical enjoyment of *Yom Tov*. This is the normative halachic position.

It is worth noting a certain tension in Rabbi Eliezer's overall corpus: in a different context involving *Sukkos*, Rabbi Eliezer praised those who remain at home on the *regel* and celebrated *v'samachta atah u'veisecha* — you and your household — emphasizing the physical, domestic dimension of *simcha*. Here, by contrast, he emphasizes total dedication to Torah. The two positions are not easily harmonized, and the difficulty is worth noting.

## Divine Support

### No Eruv Gift

The *gemara* now returns to the verse cited in the story of Rabbi Eliezer: *v'shilchu manos l'ein nachon lo* — send portions to one who has nothing prepared. What does *l'ein nachon lo* mean? Rav Chisda interprets: *l'mi shelo hinniach eruv tavshilin* — it refers to someone who did not make an *eruv tavshilin* and therefore has nothing to cook for *Shabbat*. One who has properly prepared should send food to this person on *Yom Tov*.

---

## Divine Support…

### Compassion View

A second interpretation is recorded: *ika d'amri* — some say — *l'ein nachon lo* refers to someone who did not have an opportunity to make an *eruv tavshilin* because he was too busy in the time immediately before *Shabbat*. On this reading, one should have compassion (*rachmanus*) on such a person and send him food. However, someone who had ample opportunity to make an *eruv tavshilin* and simply neglected to do so — *poshea hu* — is considered negligent, and perhaps one need not send him anything at all. This is a striking position; the full implications regarding relying on a communal *eruv* established by the *rav* of the city will be addressed in subsequent discussion.

### God Pays Debts

The *gemara* concludes the daf with a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon: *amar lahem hakadosh baruch hu l'Yisrael, banai, lovu alai u'kadshu kedushas hayom* — God says to the Jewish people: my children, borrow against My account and sanctify the day; spend money on food and on the honor of *Yom Tov* even if you must go into debt. *Eminu bi* — trust in Me. *Va'ani poreia* — I will repay your debts. The message with which the daf concludes is one of trust in divine support: one who spends generously on *simchas Yom Tov* need not fear the financial burden, for God promises to stand behind those expenses.

## Rabbi Yehoshua…

### Eruv Connection

The discussion of *simchas Yom Tov* and the *eruv tavshilin* are thus bound together in a common concern: ensuring that both dimensions of *Yom Tov* — the spiritual and the physical — are honored properly, that *Shabbat* is not forgotten in the midst of celebration, and that no one is left without food on the holy day.
