# Bava Batra 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzedakah & Chesed

### Opening & Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶן רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לְתַלְמִידָיו: בָּנַי, מַהוּ שֶׁאָמַר הַכָּתוּב: ״צְדָקָה תְרוֹמֵם גּוֹי, וְחֶסֶד לְאֻמִּים חַטָּאת״?
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to his students: My sons, what is** the meaning of **that which the verse states: "Righteousness exalts a nation, but the kindness of the peoples is sin"** (Proverbs 14:34)?

The *sugya* of *tzedakah* continues on 10b, picking up with a *tanya* that opens a remarkable teaching of Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai to his students. He poses the question: *banai, mahu she'amar hakatuv* — what is meant by the verse *tzedakah teromem goy ve-chesed le'umim chatat*? Righteousness, or *tzedakah*, will lift up a nation, and *chesed* — acts of kindness — performed by nations is a sin. The verse is genuinely difficult to parse. One approach actually interprets *chesed* not as kindness but as iniquity, in the manner of the verse *ki tikach achoto chesed hi*, where *chesed* carries a negative valence. In any case, the verse is hard, and Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai invites his students to offer their readings.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְאָמַר: ״צְדָקָה תְּרוֹמֵם גּוֹי״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד בָּאָרֶץ״; ״וָחָסֶד לְאֻמִּים חַטָּאת״ – כׇּל צְדָקָה וָחֶסֶד שֶׁאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם עוֹשִׂין, חֵטְא הוּא לָהֶן; שֶׁאֵינָם עוֹשִׂין אֶלָּא לְהִתְגַּדֵּל בּוֹ, כְּמוֹ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דִּי לֶהֱוֹן מְהַקְרְבִין נִיחוֹחִין לֶאֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא, וּמְצַלַּיִן לְחַיֵּי מַלְכָּא וּבְנוֹהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer answered and said: "Righteousness exalts a nation"; these are** the people of **Israel, as it is written: "And who is like your people Israel, one nation on the earth?"** (I Chronicles 17:21). **"But the kindness of the peoples is sin,"** meaning that **all the** acts of **charity and kindness that the nations of the world perform are** counted as **a sin for them, since they perform them only to elevate themselves** in prestige, **as it is stated: "That they may sacrifice offerings of pleasing aroma to the God of heaven, and pray for the life of the king and of his sons"** (Ezra 6:10).

Rabbi Eliezer answers first. *Tzedakah teromem goy* — the *goy* referred to here is Israel, as the verse states *umi ke'amcha Yisrael goy echad ba'aretz*. *Ve-chesed le'umim chatat*: any *tzedakah* and *chesed* that the nations of the world perform — *kol tzedakah ve-chesed she-umot ovdei kochavim osim* — is a sin for them. Why? *She'einam osim ela lehitgadel bo*: they do not act *leshaim shamayim*. They give only to aggrandize themselves, to appear as great philanthropists. The supporting verse is drawn from the time of Ezra, when a foreign king contributed to the rebuilding of the *Beit Hamikdash* but appended the condition that offerings be brought and prayers offered for the life of the king and his sons — *umetzalyin l'chayei malka u'venohi*. This reveals that even ostensibly generous acts by non-Jewish kingdoms ultimately serve their own interests.

It is worth pausing here. Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai and his students lived at the moment of the *churban Beit Hamikdash*, with Rome as both their provider of infrastructure and their existential oppressor. The earlier *Gemara* in *Avodah Zarah* captures this tension precisely — some sages acknowledged the genuine benefits Rome bestowed upon its inhabitants through roads, water systems, and civic order, while others insisted those acts were done purely for Rome's own benefit. Here, Rabbi Eliezer's position clearly reflects the latter view.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְעָבֵיד הָכִי, לָאו צְדָקָה גְּמוּרָה הִיא?! וְהָתַנְיָא, הָאוֹמֵר: ״סֶלַע זֶה לִצְדָקָה, בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּחְיוּ בָּנַי״, וּ״בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁאֶזְכֶּה לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה צַדִּיק גָּמוּר! לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּאן בְּגוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if one acts this way, is it not full-fledged charity? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one who says:** I am contributing **this *sela* to charity so that my sons will live, or** if he says: I am performing the mitzva **so that I will merit** a share in **the World-to-Come, this** person **is a full-fledged righteous person,** as far as that mitzva is concerned, even though he has his own welfare in mind? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** the statement that he is considered absolutely righteous is **with regard to a Jew;** while **there,** the statement that such benefaction is not credited as charity is **with regard to a gentile.**

But then the *Gemara* interjects with an Aramaic question. If someone gives *tzedakah* with a mixed motive — *ha'omer sela zu litzdakah bishvil sheyichye beni uvishvil she'ezkeh lechayei ha'olam haba*, donating a *sela* for charity in the hope that his sick son recovers or that he will merit *olam haba* — the *tanya* declares *harei zeh tzaddik gamur*, he is considered a complete *tzaddik*. How, then, can we condemn the non-Jew's charity as sinful when Jews themselves give with ulterior motives and are still praised?

The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — kan b'Yisrael, kan b'ovdei kochavim*. There is no contradiction, because the cases are categorically different. Rashi explains: when a Jew gives *shelo lishma*, his underlying disposition remains *leshamayim* — *ben yihye ben lo yihye, eino meharher achar midat hadin*. Even if the hoped-for result does not materialize, the Jew does not regret having given the *tzedakah*. The non-Jew, by contrast, gives *ela limnat sheyichye*, exclusively on condition that his desired outcome is achieved, and *im lav mitcharet* — if it is not, he regrets the act entirely. One teacher suggested that the original text may have read *tzedakah gemurah* rather than *tzaddik gamur*, which would flow more naturally from the *Gemara's* earlier language, but the parallel manuscripts consistently read *tzaddik gamur*. In any case, Rashi explains the declaration as applying to this particular act, not necessarily to the person's overall standing.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנָה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְאָמַר: ״צְדָקָה תְּרוֹמֵם גּוֹי״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד״; ״וָחָסֶד לְאֻמִּים חַטָּאת״ – כׇּל צְדָקָה וָחֶסֶד שֶׁאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם עוֹשִׂין, חֵטְא הוּא לָהֶן; שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּמָּשֵׁךְ מַלְכוּתָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָהֵן מַלְכָּא, מִלְכִּי יִשְׁפַּר עֲלָךְ, וַחֲטָיָךָ בְּצִדְקָה פְרוּק, וַעֲוָיָתָךְ בְּמִחַן עֲנָיִן, הֵן תֶּהֱוֵי אַרְכָא לִשְׁלֵוְתָךְ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua answered** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's challenge to interpret the verse **and said: "Righteousness exalts a nation"; these are** the people of **Israel, as it is written: "And who is like your people Israel, one nation** on the earth." **"But the kindness of the peoples is sin"** means that **all the** acts of **charity and kindness that the nations of the world perform is** counted as **a sin for them, since they perform them only to perpetuate their dominion, as it is stated** by Daniel to Nebuchadnezzar: **"Therefore, O king, let my counsel be acceptable to you, and break off your sins by charity, and your iniquities by showing mercy to the poor; that there may be an extension of your serenity"** (Daniel 4:24).

Rabbi Yehoshua then offers his reading. The structure is identical — *goy* means Israel — but his explanation of why non-Jewish *chesed* constitutes sin differs subtly: *she'ein osim ela k'dei shetimshach malchutan*, they do it only to extend their kingdoms. His supporting verse is from Daniel's counsel to Nebuchadnezzar: *v'chata'ach b'tzedakah perok ve'avayach b'michan anayin, hen lehavay archa l'shalvatach* — atone for your sin with *tzedakah* to the poor, and your kingdom will be prolonged. The motivation is explicitly the perpetuation of rule, not genuine concern for others.

One might note that the supporting verses in both Rabbi Eliezer's and Rabbi Yehoshua's positions appear to converge on a similar theme — extending the life and glory of the kingdom — even though their stated explanations differ, one emphasizing personal aggrandizement and the other political perpetuation. There is also a meaningful distinction between the two positions regarding scope: Rabbi Eliezer uses the phrase *lehitgadel bo*, which can apply to any individual, while Rabbi Yehoshua speaks of *malchutan*, suggesting his reading applies specifically to rulers and governments rather than to private individuals. This raises the broader question of whether the entire discussion concerns governmental *tzedakah* or includes individual non-Jews who give charity.

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְאָמַר: ״צְדָקָה תְּרוֹמֵם גּוֹי״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״דִּכְתִיב: וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל [וְגוֹ׳]״; ״וְחֶסֶד לְאֻמִּים חַטָּאת״ – כׇּל צְדָקָה וָחֶסֶד שֶׁגּוֹיִים עוֹשִׂין, חֵטְא הוּא לָהֶן; שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֶלָּא לְהִתְיַהֵר בּוֹ, וְכׇל הַמִּתְיַהֵר נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֵד יָהִיר לֵץ שְׁמוֹ, עוֹשֶׂה בְּעֶבְרַת זָדוֹן״ – וְאֵין ״עֶבְרָה״ אֶלָּא גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹם עֶבְרָה הַיּוֹם הַהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel answered and said: "Righteousness exalts a nation"; these are** the people of **Israel, as it is written: "And who is like your people Israel,** one nation on the earth." **"But the kindness of the peoples is sin"** means that **all the** acts of **charity and kindness that the nations of the world perform is** counted as **a sin for them, since they perform them only in order to act haughtily through them, and whoever acts haughtily falls into Gehenna, as it is stated: "The proud and haughty one, scorner is his name, acts in arrogant wrath"** (Proverbs 21:24). **And wrath means nothing other than Gehenna, as it is stated: "That day is a day of wrath"** (Zephaniah 1:15).

Rabban Gamliel's reading follows the same structural form — *goy* is Israel — and his explanation is that non-Jewish *tzedakah* and *chesed* are performed *ela l'hityaher bo*, to make oneself appear righteous, which is distinct from both personal glory and political calculation. The *Gemara* appends a serious warning: *ve-chol hamityayer nofel be-Gehinnom*, whoever presents himself as more righteous than he is will fall into *Gehinnom*, for such a person is truly a *letz*, a mocker — *zed yahir letz shemo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: עֲדַיִן אָנוּ צְרִיכִין לַמּוֹדָעִי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: ״צְדָקָה תְּרוֹמֵם גּוֹי״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד״; ״וָחָסֶד לְאֻמִּים חַטָּאת״ – כׇּל צְדָקָה וָחֶסֶד שֶׁאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם עוֹשִׂין, חֵטְא הוּא לָהֶן; שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֶלָּא לְחָרֵף אוֹתָנוּ בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹא וַיַּעַשׂ ה׳ כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֵּר, כִּי חֲטָאתֶם לַה׳ וְלֹא שְׁמַעְתֶּם בְּקוֹלוֹ, וְהָיָה לָכֶם הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel said: We still need** to hear what **the Moda'i** has to say, as **Rabbi Eliezer HaModa'i says: "Righteousness exalts a nation"; these are** the people of **Israel, as it is written: "And who is like your people Israel, one nation on the earth." "But the kindness of the peoples is sin"** means that **all the** acts of **charity and kindness that the nations of the world perform is** counted as **a sin for them, since they perform them only to taunt us with them, as it is stated** that the Babylonian officer Nebuzaradan said: **"The Lord has brought it, and done according as He has said; because you have sinned against the Lord and have not obeyed His voice, therefore this matter is come upon you"** (Jeremiah 40:3).

Rabban Gamliel then says: *adayin anu tzerichim le-Modai* — we still need Rabbi Eleazar HaModai.

### Rav Nechunya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנָה רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה וְאָמַר: ״צְדָקָה תְרוֹמֵם גּוֹי וָחֶסֶד״ – לְיִשְׂרָאֵל; וּ״לְאֻמִּים – חַטָּאת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana answered and said: "Righteousness exalts a nation and kindness"** is referring to **Israel; and** in addition, **"of the peoples is sin."**

Rabbi Eleazar HaModai provides yet another variant: any *tzedakah* and *chesed* the nations do is a sin for them, and his proof-text suggests the motivation is to make Israel look bad — to use acts of apparent righteousness as a form of implicit accusation against the Jewish people, in the spirit of *Nevuzaradan's* taunt at the time of the *churban*.

Then Rav Nechunya ben HaKanah offers the most sweeping reading. He parses the verse differently by placing the division at a different point: *tzedakah teromem goy ve-chesed le-Yisrael — ule'umim chatat*. Both *tzedakah* and *chesed* accrue to Israel, and the nations have only sin. He manages to bring the word *chesed* from the verse into the column of Israel's merits — not merely acknowledging that Israel gives *tzedakah*, but that Israel also performs *chesed*, an added distinction that the other readings did not emphasize.

### RYZ's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לְתַלְמִידָיו: נִרְאִים דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה מִדְּבָרַי וּמִדִּבְרֵיכֶם, לְפִי שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן צְדָקָה וָחֶסֶד לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלָאוּמּוֹת חַטָּאת. מִכְּלָל דְּהוּא נָמֵי אָמַר; מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶם רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַחַטָּאת מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל – כָּךְ צְדָקָה מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to his students: The statement of Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana appears** to be more precise **than** both **my statement and your statements, because he assigns** both **righteousness and kindness to Israel, and sin to the peoples** of the world. The Gemara asks: **By inference,** it appears **that he,** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, **also offered** an interpretation of this verse. **What is it? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to them** that the verse should be understood as follows: **Just as a sin-offering atones for Israel, so charity atones for the nations of the world.**

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai then declares that Rav Nechunya's words appear better than his own and those of his students — *nirin divrei Rav Nechunya ben HaKanah mi-divrai u-mi-divreichem* — precisely because Rav Nechunya manages to bring *chesed* into the domain of Israel as well. But then the *Gemara* observes: *michlal de-hu nami amar* — his praise of Rav Nechunya implies that he himself had his own reading. What was it? A *baraita* preserves it: *keshem she-ha-chatat mechaperet al Yisrael, kach tzedakah mechaperet al umot ha'olam* — just as the *korban chatat* atones for Israel, so *tzedakah* atones for the nations of the world. This is the one genuinely positive reading in the entire collection — *tzedakah* is itself a form of atonement for non-Jews, analogous to the sacrificial system available to Israel.

This creates a striking paradox. Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai's own teaching is the most generous toward non-Jewish *tzedakah*, yet he declares Rav Nechunya's position — perhaps the most restrictive — to be the best. The question of why he would embrace the most negative formulation when his own view is the most positive remains genuinely difficult. One possibility is that his attitude evolved over time, particularly given his famous negotiations with Rome in the wake of the *churban*, which might have shaped a more accommodating posture at an earlier stage and a harsher one later.

## Donations & Shalom

### Ifra Hormiz Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיפְרָא הוֹרְמִיז אִימֵּיהּ דְּשַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא, שַׁדַּרָה אַרְבַּע מְאָה דִּינָרֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, וְלָא קַבְּלִינְהוּ. שַׁדַּרְ[תִּ]ינְהוּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְקַבְּלִינְהוּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם מַלְכוּת. שְׁמַע רַבִּי אַמֵּי, אִיקְּפַד, אֲמַר: לֵית לֵיהּ: ״בִּיבֹשׁ קְצִירָהּ תִּשָּׁבַרְנָה, נָשִׁים בָּאוֹת מְאִירוֹת אוֹתָהּ״?! וְרָבָא – מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם מַלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Ifera Hurmiz, the mother of King Shapur,** king of Persia, **sent four hundred dinars to Rabbi Ami, but he did not accept them. She** then **sent them to Rava, and he accepted them for the sake of peace with the kingdom. Rabbi Ami heard** what Rava had done **and was angry. He said: Does** Rava **not accept** the lesson of the verse: **"When the boughs are withered, they shall be broken off; the women shall come and set them on fire"** (Isaiah 27:11), meaning that when righteousness has ceased from a particular nation, it is time for its citizens to be punished, and therefore we should not help them perform any meritorious deeds, which would delay their punishment? The Gemara asks: **And** why did **Rava** accept the money? The Gemara answers: He did so **for the sake of peace with the kingdom.**

The *Gemara* immediately moves from the theoretical to the practical. Ifra Hormiz, the mother of King Shavor, had appeared earlier in the tractate when she sent money for use in a great *mitzvah* and the discussion turned to *pidyon shvuyim*. Now she sends four hundred *dinarei* directly to Rabbi Ami. The context is pointed: we have just concluded a lengthy discussion about how non-Jewish *tzedakah* is performed for self-interested purposes and constitutes a form of sin. What happens, then, when a non-Jewish benefactress sends money directly to a Jewish communal leader? Do you take it?

Rabbi Ami refused to accept it. Rava, by contrast, accepted it — *mishum shalom malchut*, for the sake of maintaining good relations with the government.

### Ami's Objection

When Rabbi Ami heard that Rava had accepted the money, he was upset. He cited the verse *yavesh k'tzirah tishavarna nashim ba'ot me'irot ota* — when they wither, they shall be broken, and women will come to set them alight — understood through Rabbeinu Gershom as a reference to the non-Jewish nations' eventual downfall when they lose their *zechut*. The concern is precisely that accepting their *tzedakah* grants them *zechut*, spiritual credit, which strengthens them and delays their reckoning.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אַמֵּי נָמֵי, מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם מַלְכוּת! דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִפְלְגִינְהוּ לַעֲנִיֵּי גוֹיִים. וְרָבָא נָמֵי – לַעֲנִיֵּי גוֹיִים יַהֲבִינְהוּ; וְרַבִּי אַמֵּי דְּאִיקְּפַד, הוּא דְּלָא סַיְּימוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** did **Rabbi Ami** not **also** see the importance of accepting the money **for the sake of peace with the kingdom?** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ami maintains that Rava **should have distributed** the money **to the gentile poor** rather than to the Jewish poor, as it is a disgrace to the Jews to require the kindness of the nations of the world in order to support their poor. The Gemara comments: In fact, **Rava also gave** the money **to the gentile poor** and not to the Jewish poor. **And Rabbi Ami got angry** because **those who reported the story to him did not conclude it before him;** consequently, Rav Ami was not informed that Rava had indeed given the money to the gentile poor.

The *Gemara* then harmonizes the positions. Rava, too, accepted the money with the intent to distribute it to non-Jewish poor — *l'aniyei ovdei kochavim yavinhu*. And why did Rabbi Ami get upset? Because when they informed him that Rava had accepted the money, they did not complete the account by telling him that Rava had directed it to the non-Jewish poor. *Hu d'lo siyemu kamei.*

The logic is striking. By routing the funds to non-Jewish poor rather than to Jewish poor, Rava denies Ifra Hormiz the particular *zechut* of having supported the Jewish community, while still maintaining the *shalom malchut* justification for not having refused outright. The implicit cost of this solution is that Jewish poor do not benefit — a tension the *Gemara* acknowledges but seems to resolve entirely through the *shalom malchut* framework rather than through the humanitarian considerations one might have expected to carry greater weight.

### Rashi Analysis

Rashi raises a pointed question: is this not a problem of *gneivat da'at*? Ifra Hormiz presumably understood her donation to be destined for the Jewish poor. By redirecting it to non-Jewish poor, are we not misrepresenting ourselves to her?

Rashi answers by appealing to established practice. Since it is well-known — as the *Gemara* in *Gittin* states — that Jews customarily support the non-Jewish poor alongside the Jewish poor *mipnei darchei shalom*, there is no misrepresentation. Ifra Hormiz gave the money into the discretion of the Jewish communal leadership, and that discretion is understood to include care for non-Jewish poor as part of standard practice. There is therefore no *gneivat da'at*. Rashi implies, however, that if she had explicitly designated the funds for Jewish poor, the situation would be different: either the money must be refused or the *shalom malchut* concern alone would need to override the designation.

## Benjamin & Munbaz

### Benjamin Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל בִּנְיָמִין הַצַּדִּיק, שֶׁהָיָה מְמוּנֶּה עַל קוּפָּה שֶׁל צְדָקָה; פַּעַם אַחַת בָּאתָה אִשָּׁה לְפָנָיו בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, פַּרְנְסֵנִי! אָמַר לָהּ: הָעֲבוֹדָה, שֶׁאֵין בְּקוּפָּה שֶׁל צְדָקָה כְּלוּם! אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, אִם אֵין אַתָּה מְפַרְנְסֵנִי, הֲרֵי אִשָּׁה וְשִׁבְעָה בָּנֶיהָ מֵתִים! עָמַד וּפִרְנְסָהּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. לְיָמִים חָלָה וְנָטָה לָמוּת, אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אַתָּה אָמַרְתָּ: כׇּל הַמְקַיֵּים נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, כְּאִילּוּ קִיֵּים עוֹלָם מָלֵא; וּבִנְיָמִין הַצַּדִּיק, שֶׁהֶחֱיָה אִשָּׁה וְשִׁבְעָה בָּנֶיהָ, יָמוּת בְּשָׁנִים מוּעָטוֹת הַלָּלוּ? מִיָּד קָרְעוּ לוֹ גְּזַר דִּינוֹ. תָּנָא: הוֹסִיפוּ לוֹ עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם שָׁנָה עַל שְׁנוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita*: The following **was said about Binyamin the righteous, who was appointed** supervisor **over the charity fund. Once, a woman came before him during years of drought** and **said to him: My master, sustain me. He said to her:** I swear **by the Temple service that there is nothing** left **in the charity fund. She said to him: My master, if you do not sustain me, a woman and her seven sons will die. He arose and sustained her with his own** funds. **After some time, he fell deathly ill. The ministering angels said to the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, You said** that **anyone who preserves a single life in Israel is** regarded **as if he has preserved an entire world. Should** then **Binyamin the righteous, who saved a woman and her seven sons, die after these few years,** still in his youth? **They immediately tore up his sentence.** A Sage **taught: They added twenty-two years to his life.**

A *baraita* is introduced: Benjamin HaTzaddik, who served as the administrator of the *kuppah shel tzedakah*, the communal charity fund, once had a woman come to him during a famine. She said: *Rebbi, parneseini* — sustain me. He replied with an oath that the fund was empty, all funds having been distributed during the crisis. She answered: if you do not support me, a woman and her seven children face death. He rose and supported her from his own personal resources.

Later, Benjamin HaTzaddik fell gravely ill. The ministering angels then presented the following argument before the Holy One, Blessed be He: You have said that whoever sustains one soul of Israel is as though he sustained an entire world — and Benjamin HaTzaddik sustained a woman and her seven children. Could such a person die so young? Immediately his decree was torn up, and twenty-two years were added to his life.

### Life-Saving Merit

The *baraita* underscores the principle articulated in the angels' petition: *kol ha-mekayeim nefesh achat mi-Yisrael ke-ilu kiyem olam malei*. The act of sustaining even one soul carries cosmological weight. Benjamin HaTzaddik did not merely give charity — he gave from his own pocket when the communal fund had nothing left, and he did so for a woman with seven dependents. The heavenly response was proportionate to the extraordinary nature of the deed.

### Munbaz Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְמוֹנְבַּז הַמֶּלֶךְ, שֶׁבִּזְבֵּז אוֹצְרוֹתָיו וְאוֹצְרוֹת אֲבוֹתָיו בִּשְׁנֵי בַּצּוֹרֶת, וְחָבְרוּ עָלָיו אֶחָיו וּבֵית אָבִיו, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲבוֹתֶיךָ גָּנְזוּ וְהוֹסִיפוּ עַל שֶׁל אֲבוֹתָם, וְאַתָּה מְבַזְבְּזָם! אָמַר לָהֶם: אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ לְמַטָּה, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי לְמַעְלָה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֶת מֵאֶרֶץ תִּצְמָח, וְצֶדֶק מִשָּׁמַיִם נִשְׁקָף״. אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַיָּד שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַיָּד שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צֶדֶק וּמִשְׁפָּט מְכוֹן כִּסְאֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident involving King Munbaz, who liberally gave away his treasures and the treasures of his ancestors in the years of drought,** distributing the money to the poor. **His brothers and his father's household joined together against him** to protest against his actions, **and they said to him: Your ancestors stored up** money in their treasuries **and added to** the treasures **of their ancestors, and you are liberally distributing** it all to the poor. King Munbaz **said to them:** Not so, **my ancestors stored up below, whereas I am storing above, as it is stated: "Truth will spring out of the earth and righteousness will look down from heaven"** (Psalms 85:12), meaning that the righteous deeds that one has performed are stored up in heaven. **My ancestors stored up** treasures **in a place where the** human **hand can reach,** and so their treasures could have been robbed, **whereas I am storing up** treasures **in a place where the** human **hand cannot reach,** and so they are secure, **as it is stated: "Righteousness and justice are the foundation of Your throne"** (Psalms 89:15).

The *Gemara* continues with the story of Munbaz the King. During years of famine, he distributed his own treasuries and those of his ancestors to the poor — *she-bizbez otzrotav ve-otzrot avotav bishnei batzoret*. His brothers and his father's house confronted him: your ancestors added to the family fortune generation after generation, and you are squandering everything they accumulated. Whether Munbaz was a gentile king who converted or, as Rashi suggests, a son of Queen Helena and thus connected to the *Chashmonaim*, his family's complaint carries a different valence depending on his background — if he was a convert, then his biological family is chastising him precisely because he has embraced values that his conversion has instilled in him, which gives the story a particularly poignant dimension.

Munbaz responds with a series of parallel formulations, each structured as: my ancestors stored up *X*, while I have stored up *Y*.

### Metaphors & Rewards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה פֵּירוֹת, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי דָּבָר שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה פֵּירוֹת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִמְרוּ צַדִּיק כִּי טוֹב, כִּי פְרִי מַעַלְלֵיהֶם יֹאכֵלוּ״. אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ [אוֹצְרוֹת] מָמוֹן, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי אוֹצְרוֹת נְפָשׁוֹת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פְּרִי צַדִּיק עֵץ חַיִּים, וְלוֹקֵחַ נְפָשׁוֹת חָכָם״. אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ לַאֲחֵרִים, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי לְעַצְמִי – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״. אֲבוֹתַי גָּנְזוּ לָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וַאֲנִי גָּנַזְתִּי לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָלַךְ לְפָנֶיךָ צִדְקֶךָ, כְּבוֹד ה׳ יַאַסְפֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **My ancestors stored up something that does not generate profit,** as money sitting in a treasury does not increase, **whereas I am storing up something that generates profit, as it is stated: "Say of the righteous, that it shall be well with them, for they shall eat the fruit of their doings"** (Isaiah 3:10). **My ancestors stored up treasures of money, whereas I am storing up treasures of souls, as it is stated: "The fruit of the righteous is a tree of life, and he that wins souls is wise"** (Proverbs 11:30). **My ancestors stored up for others,** for their sons and heirs, when they themselves would pass from this world, **whereas I am storing up for myself, as it is stated: "And it shall be as righteousness to you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13). **My ancestors stored up for this world, whereas I am storing up for the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And your righteousness shall go before you, the glory of the Lord shall be your rearguard"** (Isaiah 58:8).

His first response: *avotai ganezu le-mata, va-ani ganazti le-ma'ala* — my ancestors gathered for below, I have gathered for above, as the verse states *emet me-eretz titzmach ve-tzedek mi-shamayim nishkaf*, truth grows from the ground and righteousness looks down from heaven. His second: *avotai ganezu be-makom she-ha-yad sholelet bo, va-ani ganazti be-makom she-ein ha-yad sholelet bo* — they stored in a place vulnerable to seizure or theft; I stored in a place beyond the reach of any hand, as the verse states *tzedek u-mishpat mechon kisecha*, righteousness is the foundation of God's throne, safely beyond earthly reach.

His third formulation: *avotai ganezu davar she-eino oseh peirot, va-ani ganazti davar she-oseh peirot* — my ancestors stored capital without yield; I invested in something that produces fruit, as the verse states *imru tzaddik ki tov ki pri ma'alaleihem yocheilu*. This draws on the well-known principle that *adam ochel peirotehem ba-olam ha-zeh ve-ha-keren kayemet lo le-olam ha-ba* — one enjoys the fruits of such acts in this world while the principal remains intact for *olam haba*.

### Paradox Explained

The fourth formulation captures the deepest paradox: *avotai ganezu otzrot mamon, va-ani ganazti otzrot nefashot* — they stored up monetary treasure; I stored up souls, as the verse states *pri tzaddik etz chayim ve-lokeiach nefashot chacham*.

The fifth and final formulation is the most rhetorically elegant. One would have expected Munbaz's family to argue that he is giving away what is theirs and keeping nothing for himself, while he is the one who is truly wasting the inheritance. He turns it entirely around: *avotai ganezu la-acherim va-ani ganazti li-atzmiy* — my ancestors stored up for others, while I have stored up for myself. They accumulated wealth that they inevitably left behind; he found the only mechanism by which treasure can truly accompany a person, by distributing it in the form of *tzedakah*. As the verse states, *ule-cha tihiyeh tzedakah* — your *tzedakah* will remain yours. And finally: *avotai ganezu le-olam ha-zeh, va-ani ganazti le-olam ha-ba*, as the verse states *ve-halach lefanecha tzidkecha kevod Hashem ya'asfecha*. This concludes the entire major *sugya* of *tzedakah*.

## Land Division

### Residency & Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם קָנָה בָּהּ בֵּית דִּירָה – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם קָנָה בָּהּ קַרְקַע כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its analysis of the mishna, which taught that one must reside in a place for twelve months in order to be considered a resident for the purposes of issues such as paying taxes. But if he **bought** himself **a residence in** the city, **he is** immediately considered **like** one of **the people of the city.** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: If he bought any amount of land in** the city, and not necessarily a residence, **he is** immediately considered **like** one of **the people of the city.**

The *Gemara* returns to the last line of the *Mishnah*, which had been the launchpad for the entire *tzedakah* digression. The context is the question of when a person acquires the standing of a town resident for purposes of communal obligations, including contributions to *tzedakah* funds. The *Mishnah* had established the general rule of twelve months, but noted that if one purchases a *beit dirah* — a dwelling — in the town, that residence is conferred immediately.

### Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם קָנָה שָׁם קַרְקַע הָרְאוּיָה לְבֵית דִּירָה – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר! תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** otherwise in a different *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: If** one **bought land that is suitable for a residence, he is** immediately considered **like** one of **the people of the city.** This contradicts the first *baraita*. The Gemara answers: This is a dispute between **two *tanna'im* and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah* does not follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds in the *baraita* that even purchasing any piece of land (*karka kol shehu*) in the town suffices. A second *baraita* presents a slightly different formulation of his position — it must be land fit for building a residence (*karka hara'uyah le-beit dirah*), not just any land. Even a plot designated for agricultural use at a distance from the settlement might not qualify if it could not plausibly serve as the site of a home. The *Gemara* resolves the two versions of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's statement as a dispute between two *tannaim* who each report his position differently.

### Chatzer Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין חוֹלְקִין אֶת הֶחָצֵר – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה, וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה. וְלֹא אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין לָזֶה, וְתִשְׁעָה קַבִּין לָזֶה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ תִּשְׁעַת חֲצָיֵי קַבִּין לָזֶה, וְתִשְׁעַת חֲצָיֵי קַבִּין לָזֶה. וְלֹא אֶת הַגִּינָּה – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ חֲצִי קַב לָזֶה, וַחֲצִי קַב לָזֶה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: בֵּית רוֹבַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court **does not divide a courtyard** at the request of one of the joint owners **unless there will be** in it four by **four cubits for this one and** four by **four cubits for that one,** i.e., this minimum area for each of the joint owners. **And** the court does **not** divide **a** jointly owned **field unless there is** space **in it** to plant **nine *kav*** of seed **for this one and nine *kav*** of seed **for that one. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The court does not divide a field **unless there is** space **in it** to plant **nine half-*kav*** of seed **for this one and nine half-*kav*** of seed **for that one. And** the court does **not** divide a jointly owned **garden unless there is** space **in it** to plant **a half-*kav*** of seed **for this one and a half-*kav*** of seed **for that one. Rabbi Akiva says** that half that amount is sufficient, i.e., the **area required for sowing a quarter-*kav* of seed [*beit rova*].**

A new *Mishnah* begins, returning to the core topic of forced partition of shared property. *Ein cholkin et ha-chatzer ad sheyehei arba amot lazeh ve-arba amot lazeh* — a shared courtyard cannot be divided against one party's will unless each person would receive a minimum of four *amot*. Four *amot* is approximately six feet — a small but functionally meaningful space in front of one's door. The *Mishnah* then addresses fields: *ein cholkin et ha-sadeh ad sheyehei bah tisha kavin lazeh ve-tisha kavin lazeh* — a field for growing grain cannot be divided unless each portion amounts to nine *kavin*, a measure of area reflecting the yield of seed required to make the parcel agriculturally viable.

### Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֶת הַטְּרַקְלִין, וְלֹא אֶת הַמּוֹרָן, וְלֹא אֶת הַשּׁוֹבָךְ, וְלֹא אֶת הַטַּלִּית, וְלֹא אֶת הַמֶּרְחָץ, וְלֹא אֶת בֵּית הַבַּד, וְלֹא אֶת בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן כְּדַי לָזֶה וּכְדַי לָזֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁיֵּחָלֵק, וּשְׁמוֹ עָלָיו – חוֹלְקִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the court does **not** divide **a hall [*hateraklin*], a drawing room, a dovecote, a cloak, a bathhouse, an olive press, and an irrigated field unless there is enough for this one** to use the property in the usual manner **and enough for that one** to use the property in the usual manner. **This is the principle: Anything** for **which** when it **is divided,** each of the parts is large enough to **retain the name** of the original item, the court **divides** it. **But if** the parts will **not** retain the original name, the court **does not divide** it.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, holding that the minimum for a field is nine half-*kavin* per person rather than nine full *kavin*. The *Mishnah* then turns to gardens: *ve-lo et ha-gina ad sheyehei bah chatzi kav lazeh ve-chatzi kav lazeh* — a vegetable garden requires only half a *kav* per person, since intensive vegetable cultivation yields significant value from a much smaller area. Rabbi Akiva reduces the minimum further to a *beit rova*, a quarter-*kav*. A *kav* is a measure of volume, but when applied to land it corresponds to the area that would be sown with that volume of seed, so each measure translates into a meaningful acreage threshold.

### Indivisibles

The *Mishnah* then lists categories of property that cannot be divided even if the mathematical minimum might be satisfied: a *traklin* and a *moran*, which Rashi identifies as two types of large or stately homes; a *shovach* or dovecote; a *tallis* in the sense of a large cloak; a *merchatz* or bathhouse; a *beit ha-bad* or olive press; and a *beit ha-shlachin*, an irrigated field. For these categories, the standard is not a fixed quantitative threshold but rather *kidei lazeh u-kidei lazeh* — enough that each portion remains a meaningful unit of whatever the original thing was.

### Divisibility Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים; אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים, אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – יַחְלוֹקוּ. וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים – לֹא יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** does this rule apply? It applies **when** the joint owners **do not both wish** to divide the item; when only one of the owners wishes to divide the property, he cannot force the other to do so. **But when both of them wish** to divide the item, **they may divide** it, **even** if each of the owners will receive **less than** the amounts specified above. **But** in the case of **sacred writings,** i.e., a scroll of any of the twenty-four books of the Bible, that were inherited by two people, **they may not divide** them, **even if both of them wish** to do so, because it would be a show of disrespect to cut the scroll in half.

The *Mishnah* then articulates the governing principle: *zeh ha-klal, kol she-yeichalek u-shemo alav cholkin* — the rule is that forced partition is permissible only when each resulting portion retains the name of the original. If two brothers share a mansion and dividing it would leave each with a room rather than a smaller mansion, the division cannot be forced. One can always voluntarily divide with mutual consent, *aval bizman she-sheneihem rotzim afilu pachos mikan yachaloku*, but a forced partition requires that the divided portions remain recognizable as instances of the same category of thing.

The sole exception is *kitvei ha-kodesh* — sacred scrolls. Even with the consent of both parties, Torah scrolls or other scriptural scrolls may not be divided. The reasoning is not expressed in terms of the divisibility principle but rather the inherent sanctity of the object. One might be tempted to say: I want the first half of *Yeshayahu* for learning, and you take the second half. But the *bizayon* to sacred texts overrides any such arrangement, even when both parties agree.

## Four Amot

### Rav Assi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ – חוּץ מִשֶּׁל פְּתָחִים. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵין חוֹלְקִים אֶת הֶחָצֵר – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת לָזֶה, וּשְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת לָזֶה. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה, וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – כִּדְרַבִּי אַסִּי; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The four cubits** of the courtyard **which they said** each of the joint owners must receive is **in addition to** the space in front of **the entrances** to each of the houses that is assigned to the owner of the house for loading and unloading. **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: The court **does not divide a courtyard unless** its area is sufficient so that **there will be in it eight cubits for this one and eight cubits for that one.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that it suffices that there be **four cubits for this one and four cubits for that one? Rather, conclude from it** that the *baraita* was taught **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Asi.** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that it is so.

The *Gemara* now addresses a textual difficulty. Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, states: *arba amot she-amru, chutz shel pesachim* — the four *amot* referenced in the *Mishnah* are measured exclusive of the space directly in front of the doorways. This means that before any calculation of how to divide the remaining courtyard, each resident first receives a dedicated four *amot* strip opposite his door, and only then is the remaining space assessed to see whether each party can receive an additional four *amot* to justify forced partition.

A *baraita* appears to support this reading by stating that no division may occur unless there are eight *amot* for this one and eight *amot* for that one — a figure twice the *Mishnah's* four. The *Gemara* resolves: this is not a contradiction. The *baraita* counts the four *amot* in front of the doorway plus the four *amot* of courtyard space, yielding eight total per person, while the *Mishnah* references only the four *amot* of courtyard space after the door space has already been allocated. *Shema mina ke-derav Assi, shema mina.*

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָצֵר מִתְחַלֶּקֶת לְפִי פְּתָחֶיהָ. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: נוֹתְנִין אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לְכׇל פֶּתַח וּפְתַח, וְהַשְּׁאָר חוֹלְקִין בְּשָׁוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Further with regard to the division of a courtyard, **Rav Huna says: A courtyard is divided according to its entrances.** Each of the owners receives a share of the courtyard in proportion to the number of entrances that his house has opening onto the courtyard. **And Rav Ḥisda says: Four cubits are allotted** to each of the owners **for each and every entrance, and the rest** of the courtyard **is** then **divided equally** between them.

Rav Huna offers a different approach to the question of courtyard division: *chatzer mitchaleket lefi ptacheiha* — a courtyard is divided according to its doorways. If one resident has a house with two doors opening onto the courtyard and another has only one, the first receives twice the share. This has the striking implication that one could substantially improve one's claim to courtyard space simply by adding additional doorways to one's house.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: פְּתָחִים שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. הָיָה לָזֶה פֶּתַח אֶחָד, וְלָזֶה שְׁנֵי פְתָחִים; זֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פֶּתַח אֶחָד – נוֹטֵל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְזֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי פְתָחִים – נוֹטֵל שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת; וְהַשְּׁאָר חוֹלְקִין בְּשָׁוֶה. הָיָה לָזֶה פֶּתַח רָחָב שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת – נוֹטֵל שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח, וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בֶּחָצֵר. אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בֶּחָצֵר – מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: נוֹטֵל שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת בְּאוֹרֶךְ הֶחָצֵר, וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בְּרוֹחַב הֶחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda:** Each of **the entrances** opening **to a courtyard** is allotted **four cubits.** If **this one has one entrance and that one has two entrances, the one who has one entrance takes four cubits, and the one who has two entrances takes eight cubits, and they divide the rest** of the courtyard **equally** between them. If **this one had an entrance eight cubits wide,** he **takes eight cubits adjacent to the entrance and four cubits in the courtyard.** The Gemara expresses surprise: **What are** these **four cubits in the courtyard doing here?** Doesn't it all depend on the size of the courtyard? **Abaye said: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** For the entrance **he takes eight cubits along the length of the courtyard and four cubits along the width of the courtyard.** In other words, he takes a strip four cubits wide along the entire length of his entrance.

Rav Chisda offers what appears to be a middle position and the most intuitively fair resolution: *notin arba amot le-chol petach u-fetach, ve-ha-sha'ar cholkin be-shava* — each doorway receives its designated four *amot*, and whatever courtyard space remains after all doorways have been allocated is divided equally between the residents. A *baraita* supports Rav Chisda's position explicitly. If one resident has a single door and another has two, the first receives four *amot* opposite his door and the second receives eight *amot* opposite his two doors, and the remainder is divided equally.

The *baraita* introduces a further case: if a doorway is eight *amot* wide, the resident receives eight *amot* in length opposite the door and four *amot* in the courtyard — and Abaye clarifies that this means eight *amot* in the length of the courtyard by four *amot* in the width. In other words, the width of one's allocation in the courtyard corresponds to the width of the doorway itself, with four *amot* as the minimum depth into the courtyard. A doorway that is eight *amot* wide would be exceptionally large — well over twelve feet — and the passage assumes this is an unusual case establishing the principle's outer limit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַאי פֵּירָא דְסוּפְלֵי – יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לְכׇל רוּחַ וָרוּחַ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא מְיַיחַד לֵיהּ פִּתְחָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says: A pit for** holding **animal food [*peira desuflei*] has four cubits on each and every side** so that there will be sufficient space for the animals to stand. The Gemara adds: **And we said** this **only when** the pit **has no special entrance** to reach it, but rather it is accessed from all sides.

Ameimar introduces another application: if there is a *pira de-sufli* — Rashi identifies this as a pit for storing date seeds — in the courtyard, it receives four *amot* of access space on each of its four sides. However, this rule applies only if there is no dedicated door opening directly into it (*delo meyached lei pitzcha*). If there is a door facing it, then only four *amot* directly in front of that door are allocated, because the door provides immediate and sufficient access.

### Special Structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מְיַיחַד לֵיהּ פִּיתְחָא – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לִפְנֵי פִתְחוֹ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אַכְסַדְרָה – אֵין לָהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם פֵּירוּק מַשָּׂאוֹ; הָכָא – אֶפְשָׁר דְּעָיֵיל לְגַוַּאי וּמְפָרַק. מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֶחָד שַׁעֲרֵי בָתִּים, וְאֶחָד שַׁעֲרֵי אַכְסַדְרָאוֹת – יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא, בְּאַכְסַדְרָה דְּבֵי רַב. אַכְסַדְרָה דְּבֵי רַב פְּשִׁיטָא – אִידְּרוֹנָא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא! אֶלָּא בְּאַכְסַדְרָה רוֹמָיְיֹתַא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **he designates an entrance** leading to this pit and does not approach it from all sides, **he has four cubits only in front of his entrance. Rav Huna says: A portico [*akhsadra*]** that opens onto a courtyard **does not have** the adjoining **four cubits.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that four cubits were granted for each entrance? The four cubits were granted so that the owner would have room **for unloading** his animal of **its burden. Here he can go inside** the portico **and unload. Rav Sheshet raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Both gates of houses and gates of porticos have** the adjoining **four cubits** for unloading. The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **is taught, it is** specifically **with regard to the portico of a study hall,** which is closed and is not used for unloading burdens. The Gemara expresses surprise at this answer: It is **obvious that** this is the *halakha* governing **the portico of a study hall,** as **it is a proper room. Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to **a Roman portico,** which is more open than the portico of a study hall, but also not used for unloading.

Rava, citing Rav Huna, states: *achsadra ein la arba amot* — an *achsadra*, a portico or colonnaded structure, does not receive four *amot* in front of it. Tosafot holds that an *achsadra* has three walls; Rashi reads it as having no enclosed walls at all, more like a pavilion. The *Gemara* then brings Rav Sheshet's challenge from a *baraita* stating that the gateways of both houses and *achsadraot* do receive four *amot*. The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the *achsadra de-vei Rav* — an enclosed or semi-enclosed study space — from the *achsadra Rumita*, a Roman-style portico with low partial walls, which is the case Rava was discussing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית שַׁעַר, אַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת – יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. הָיוּ חֲמִשָּׁה בָּתִּים פְּתוּחִין לְמִרְפֶּסֶת – אֵין לָהֶן אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּלְבַד. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: לוּל שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלִין – יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אוֹ אֵין לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם פֵּירוּק מַשָּׂאוֹ; הָכָא – מְטַפֵּס וְעוֹלֶה, מְטַפֵּס וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A gatehouse, a portico, and a balcony** with a staircase leading down to the courtyard **have** the adjoining **four cubits** for unloading. Even if **five houses open onto the balcony, they only have four cubits** in front of the entrance to the staircase that leads to the balcony. **Rabbi Yoḥanan asked Rabbi Yannai:** Does **a chicken coop** that also has a staircase **have four cubits or does it not have four cubits?** Rabbi Yannai **said to him: What is the reason** that four cubits are granted? It is so that the owner will have room **for unloading** the **burden** of **his** animal. **Here** this space is not needed, as the chickens **climb up** to get into the coop **and climb down** to get out. No additional place is required for the owner to stand alongside them.

A *baraita* is then cited: *beit shaar, achsadra, u-mirpeset yesh lahen arba amot* — a gatehouse, a portico, and an upper landing all receive four *amot*. The *mirpeset* here is the shared external walkway of a multi-unit upper story, like the exterior corridor of a multi-family building, with stairs descending into the courtyard. If five separate homes open onto a shared *mirpeset*, they collectively receive only four *amot* at the point where the stairway descends — not four *amot* per home.

### Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: בַּיִת חֶצְיוֹ מְקוֹרֶה וְחֶצְיוֹ אֵינוֹ מְקוֹרֶה – יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אוֹ אֵין לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת; לָא מִבַּעְיָא קֵירוּיוֹ מִלְּגָיו – דְּאֶפְשָׁר דְּעָיֵיל לְגַוַּאי וּמְפָרֵק; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ קֵירוּיוֹ כְּלַפֵּי חוּץ – אֶפְשָׁר דְּעָיֵיל לְגַוַּאי וּמְפָרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asked Rav Naḥman:** If **a house** is **half roofed and half unroofed, does it have** the adjoining **four cubits or does it not have** the **four cubits?** Rav Naḥman **said to him: It does not have** the **four cubits.** The Gemara comments: It is **not necessary** to state this *halakha* when the house's **roofing is** over **the inside** portion of the house, **since it is possible** for the owner **to go inside and unload** his animal. **Rather, even** when the house's **roofing is** over **the outside** portion of the house, it is not given four cubits, as even in such a case **it is possible** for the owner **to go inside and unload** his animal there.

The *Gemara* then makes explicit what has been implicit throughout: *ta'ama mai mishum perik mas'o* — the entire reason for the four *amot* is to allow a person to unload his burden. When you arrive at your door carrying goods on a donkey or with heavy loads, you need a clear space to set down the load, steady the animal, retrieve your key, and enter. That is the functional justification for the minimum clearance.

This explains why an *achsadra* with open access does not require four *amot* — *ha'cha efshar de-ayeil ligavai u-mfarek*, you can simply walk in and unload inside. The same logic governs the question of a house half-covered and half-uncovered: Rav Nachman rules that neither configuration requires four *amot* in front. If the unroofed portion faces outward, you can walk through without difficulty since there is no height clearance issue. If the roofed portion faces outward, you can still pass through and unload in the inner uncovered section. Similarly, the chicken coop gets no four *amot* because chickens hop in and out on their own — they do not need a loading area.

## Multi-Unit Dwellings

### Hostels Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא מֵרַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי, שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לְהַחֲזִיר פִּתְחוֹ לְמָבוֹי אַחֵר – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו, אוֹ אֵין מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna asked Rabbi Ami:** If **one of the residents of an alleyway** onto which several courtyards open **wishes to alter his entrance to a different alleyway,** i.e., to make an entrance to his courtyard that will open onto a different alleyway, can **the residents of the** other **alleyway prevent him** from opening this entrance, **or can they not prevent him** from doing so? Rabbi Ami **said to him: The residents of** the other **alleyway can prevent him** from making the change.

Rav Huna asks Rabbi Ami: when quartering troops in shared accommodation — *achsanya* — and the courtyard space or its resources need to be allocated, does distribution follow the doorways or the number of people? Rabbi Ami answers: *lefi benei adam*, by the number of people. This contrasts with the rule for *zevel* — fertilizer or dung — that accumulates in a shared courtyard from the various households.

### Roofed Spaces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכְסַנְיָא – לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְחַלֶּקֶת, אוֹ לְפִי פְתָחִים מִתְחַלֶּקֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְחַלֶּקֶת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זֶבֶל שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – מִתְחַלֶּקֶת לְפִי פְתָחִים. אַכְסַנְיָא – לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna further asked: When a king issues **a billeting** order to the residents of a courtyard, obligating them to house his soldiers in their homes, is the burden **divided according to** the number of **people** in each household, **or** is it **divided according to** the number of **entrances** that each house has opening into the courtyard? Rabbi Ami **said to him: It is divided according to the** number of **people** in each household. The Gemara comments: **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **The dung in a courtyard** which the residents wish to use **is divided** among them **according to** the number of **entrances** that open into the courtyard, whereas **billeting** is divided **according to** the number of **people** in each household.

The *baraita* confirms: *zevel she-be-chatzer mitchaleket lefi ptachim* — dung in a courtyard is divided according to doorways, while *achsanya lefi benei adam* — quartered troops are allocated according to the number of individuals. As Tosafot explains, the question underlying the *achsanya* case is precisely whether courtyard resources are apportioned by the number of doorways (on the theory that each door represents one household unit) or by the number of people present (on the theory that consumption and impact scale with population). The *baraita* distinguishes the two cases: *zevel* naturally accumulates from households as units, so doorways are the relevant measure, while the burden of quartering soldiers falls on persons, not on architectural features.

## Mavoy Access

### New Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִסְתּוֹם כְּנֶגֶד פִּתְחוֹ – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו, שֶׁמַּרְבֶּה עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says:** If **one of the residents of an alleyway wishes to close off** an area **adjacent to his entrance,** i.e., to build a structure in the alleyway adjacent to the entrance to his courtyard, **the residents of the alleyway can prevent him** from doing so. This is because **it will increase the way for them,** since they will have to circumvent the structure when going to and from their homes.

Rav Huna asks Rabbi Ami: if one of the residents of a *mavoy* wishes to redirect his doorway to open onto a different *mavoy* — *lehachzir pitcho lemavoy acher* — can the current residents of the *mavoy* prevent him? Rabbi Ami answers: *benei mavoy me'akveina lei*, yes, they can block him.

The next question in the *Gemara* concerns a sealed door — *hayah satum u-bikeish lefotcho*, a door that had previously been open onto the *mavoy* and was then sealed, and now the resident wishes to reopen it. Rabbi holds that in this case *ein benei mavoy me'akveina alav* — the residents of the *mavoy* cannot prevent him from reopening a door he once had. Since his right of access was established when the door originally existed, sealing it did not permanently forfeit that right. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, however, disagrees.

## Multi-Unit Dwellings…

### Dung Heaps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי – כּוּלָּן מִשְׁתַּמְּשׁוֹת עִם הַחִיצוֹנָה, וְהַחִיצוֹנָה מִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת לְעַצְמָהּ. וְהַשְּׁאָר מִשְׁתַּמְּשׁוֹת עִם הַשְּׁנִיָּה; וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה מִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת לְעַצְמָהּ וּמִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת עִם הַחִיצוֹנָה. נִמְצֵאת פְּנִימִית מִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת לְעַצְמָהּ, וּמִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת עִם כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **five courtyards open onto an alleyway, all** the residents of the alleyway may **use** that part of the alleyway that faces the entrance to **the outermost** courtyard, **and** the residents of **the outermost** courtyard may **use** only that part of the alleyway adjacent to **its own** entrance. **And** similarly, the residents of **the other** courtyards may **use** that part of the alleyway that faces **the second** courtyard, i.e., the one next to the outermost courtyard, **and** the residents of **the second** courtyard may **use** that part of the alleyway adjacent to **its own** entrance **and** they may **use** that part of the alleyway facing **the outermost** courtyard. The *baraita* continues: The **outcome** is that the residents of **the innermost** courtyard may **use** that part of the alleyway adjacent to **its own** entrance, **and** they may **use** that part of the alleyway facing **each and every** other courtyard as well.

The *baraita* is revisited in light of the distinction between the *zevel* and *achsanya* cases. *Zevel she-be-chatzer mitchaleket lefi ptachim* — dung in a shared courtyard is apportioned by doorways, because it arises from household waste, the accumulation of animal dung and refuse that each household contributes through its door. *Achsanya lefi benei adam* — quartered troops are apportioned by persons, because what matters there is how many individuals are consuming resources and generating demands on the space. The juxtaposition in the *baraita* clarifies the underlying rationale: the criterion for division in each case follows the nature of what is being divided and what most meaningfully represents each party's claim.

## Mavoy Access…

### Graduated Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לְהַחֲזִיר פִּתְחוֹ לְמָבוֹי אַחֵר – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו. הָיָה סָתוּם וּבִקֵּשׁ לְפוֹתְחוֹ – אֵין בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי – כּוּלָּן מִשְׁתַּמְּשׁוֹת בַּמָּבוֹי זוֹ עִם זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one of the residents of an alleyway wishes to alter his entrance to a different alleyway, the residents of the** other **alleyway can prevent him** from doing so. If **there had been** an entrance there beforehand which is now **sealed and he wishes to open it, the residents of the** other **alleyway cannot prevent him** from doing so; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If there are **five courtyards which open onto an alleyway,** the residents of all the courtyards **may all use the alleyway with each other.**

The *Gemara* turns to the arrangement of multiple courtyards opening into a single *mavoy*. A *baraita* establishes graduated usage rights: if there are five courtyards opening into a *mavoy*, all of them may use the space in front of the outermost courtyard, since everyone must pass through it to reach the *reshut ha-rabim*. The outermost courtyard, however, may only use its own space — it has no need to traverse any other part of the *mavoy*. Working inward, each courtyard can use the space of all courtyards between it and the exit, but the space directly opposite each courtyard belongs, in the first instance, to that courtyard's residents.

### Innermost Rights

The innermost courtyard has the fullest rights of all — *nim-tzeit ha-pnimit mishtemshet le-atzmah u-mishtemshet im kol achat ve-achat* — because it must traverse the entire length of the *mavoy* to exit, and therefore has a valid claim to the space of every courtyard it must pass through. This has an implication for the sealing question: since the innermost resident has rights to the entire alleyway length, there is a question whether the outermost resident can simply appropriate the far end of the *mavoy* for his own exclusive use.

### Sealed Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצֵרוֹת מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמַיְיהוּ? חַסּוֹרֵי מִחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְכֵן חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי – כּוּלָּן מִשְׁתַּמְּשׁוֹת עִם הַחִיצוֹנָה, וְהַחִיצוֹנָה מִשְׁתַּמֶּשֶׁת לְעַצְמָהּ וְכוּ׳; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי – כּוּלָּם מִשְׁתַּמְּשׁוֹת בַּמָּבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise about this last ruling: **Courtyards, who mentioned anything about them?** Why are they mentioned, considering that the *baraita* is not discussing the *halakhot* of courtyards? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: Additionally,** if there are **five courtyards which open onto an alleyway,** the residents of all the courtyards **may all use** the area facing **the outermost** courtyard, **and the** residents of the **outermost one** may **use** only the area facing their **own** entrance; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If there are **five courtyards which open onto an alleyway,** the residents of all the courtyards **may all use the alleyway.**

The *Gemara* frames the question of the sealed door as a *tannaic* dispute. Rabbi holds that a door that was once open and subsequently sealed can be reopened, and the other *mavoy* residents cannot prevent it. Since the right existed originally, sealing the door was a voluntary act that did not forfeit that underlying entitlement. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees.

### Zoning Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הָיָה סָתוּם וּבִקֵּשׁ לְפוֹתְחוֹ – אֵין בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו, אֲבָל פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara directs the discussion to the details mentioned in the *baraita*. **The Master said:** If **there had been** an entrance there beforehand that is now **sealed and he wishes to open it, the residents of the** other **alleyway cannot prevent him** from doing so. **Rava says: This was taught only when he did not break its doorposts,** i.e., when the doorposts remained intact even after the entrance was sealed. **But if he** sealed the entrance and **broke its doorposts,** thereby demonstrating that the entrance had been completely negated, **the residents of the** other **alleyway can prevent him** from opening a new entrance.

The final point in the *sugya* is a *Tosefta* cited by Tosafot: if a person has a store opening onto the *reshut ha-rabim* and wishes to open a rear entrance into a shared courtyard — *l'chatzer ha-shutafin* — the partners in that courtyard can prevent him, *mipnei she-marbe aleihem et ha-derech*. Tosafot understands *et ha-derech* as a reference to increased foot traffic: opening a commercial establishment into the shared courtyard will draw customers and generate substantially more pedestrian activity than the residents bargained for when they entered the shared arrangement. This is a genuine zoning concern — the right to peaceful enjoyment of a shared space includes the right not to have it converted into a commercial thoroughfare without consent.
