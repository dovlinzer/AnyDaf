# Beitzah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Carrying & Yom Tov

### Shift to Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹצִיאִין לֹא אֶת הַקָּטָן, וְלֹא אֶת הַלּוּלָב, וְלֹא אֶת סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: One may carry out** on a Festival **neither a minor** child, **nor a *lulav*, nor a Torah Scroll into the public domain,** as none of these are required for the preparation of food; **and Beit Hillel permit** it.

With Beitzah 12 we move away from the *muktzeh* concerns of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel that have dominated the preceding discussion. The previous Mishnah dealt not strictly with *muktzeh* but with related rabbinic questions — the latitude given on Yom Tov for matters such as restoring shutters — still within the *derabbanan* realm. Here, however, we turn to a genuinely important Mishnah concerning the biblical dimensions of Yom Tov and the parameters of what is permitted.

### Three Cases

The Mishnah states: Beis Shammai say one may not carry out a minor, nor a *lulav*, nor a *sefer torah* into the public domain on Yom Tov; Beis Hillel permit it. The question at stake is how much latitude we have to carry on Yom Tov. Most people assume that carrying on Yom Tov is essentially unrestricted — that there is simply no prohibition. This Mishnah reveals that according to Beis Shammai that assumption is incorrect.

### Beis Shammai Rule

Even carrying a child, a *lulav*, or a *sefer torah* — all of which involve *mitzvah* purposes — is forbidden according to Beis Shammai. Rabbeinu Chananel explains the case of the minor as referring to a *bris milah*, making the *mitzvah* context explicit. Yet despite these weighty purposes, Beis Shammai prohibits the carrying. The reason is that none of these acts serves the one explicitly sanctioned purpose: *ochel nefesh*, food preparation. Beis Shammai holds that carrying is no different from any other *melacha* — permitted only when directed toward food, forbidden otherwise. As the Gemara will make clear, this is not merely a rabbinic restriction: performing such carrying would constitute a biblical violation of Yom Tov, subject to lashes.

### Beis Hillel Permits

Beis Hillel permits carrying in these cases. But the Mishnah leaves open a broader question: Beis Hillel permits carrying for *mitzvah* purposes — what about carrying for other purposes entirely? In practice, we carry freely on Yom Tov without pausing to ask whether our carrying serves a *mitzvah* or food-related purpose. Whether that reflects the full scope of Beis Hillel's position is precisely what the Gemara will explore. Two questions thus frame the coming discussion: how broad is Beis Hillel's allowance, and to which *melachos* does it extend?

## Shechitah Olah

### Baraita on Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹלַת נְדָבָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב — לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The *tanna*** who reviews *mishnayot* **teaches** a *baraita* **before Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi: One who slaughters a gift offering on a Festival is flogged** for transgressing the prohibition: "No manner of work shall be done on them" (Exodus 12:16). Since this slaughtering was not performed for the purpose of eating, the action constitutes a prohibited labor on a Festival.

A *tana* — one of the memorizers of the oral tradition — taught a *baraita* before Rabbi Yitzchak bar Abdimi: one who *shechts* a free-will *olah* on Yom Tov receives lashes. The choice of a *nedavah olah* is deliberate and instructive. An *olah* is entirely consumed on the altar; the one who offers it eats nothing from it. The case therefore is not one of *mekalkel* — purposeless destruction — but of a constructive, meaningful act that simply lacks the specific purpose of eating. The question it poses is: what is the *halacha* if one performs *shechitah* on Yom Tov for a purpose other than eating? The *baraita* answers: lashes. It makes no difference that the act is *shechitah*; if it is not performed for the purpose of eating, it constitutes a violation of Yom Tov.

### Beis Shammai Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּאֲמַר לָךְ מַנִּי — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוֹצָאָה לְצוֹרֶךְ — הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ. דְּאִי בֵּית הִלֵּל, הָא אָמְרִי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוֹצָאָה לְצוֹרֶךְ — הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ. הָכָא נָמֵי, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה שְׁחִיטָה לְצוֹרֶךְ — הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi **said to** that *tanna*: **Who** could have **said** this *baraita* **to you? It is** evidently in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say** that **we do not say: Since carrying out was permitted** on a Festival **for the purpose** of food preparation, it **was also permitted not for that purpose. For if you say** the *baraita* is accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel, they say: Since carrying out was permitted for the requirements** of food preparation, **it was also permitted not for** these **requirements. Here, too,** with regard to the prohibited labor of slaughtering, **since slaughter was permitted for the requirements** of food preparation, **it was also permitted not for** these **requirements.** It was permitted for any purpose that benefits people, whether directly or indirectly.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Abdimi responded to the *tana*: who is the source of this teaching? This is a Beis Shammai ruling. The reason is that our Mishnah explicitly demonstrates that Beis Shammai does not apply the principle of *mitoch* — the idea that once a *melacha* is permitted for one purpose, it is permitted more broadly. The Mishnah shows that even though carrying may be permitted for food purposes, it remains forbidden for non-food purposes. The implication carries over to *shechitah*: permitted for eating, forbidden for other purposes, and not merely rabbinically — one receives lashes for the violation.

### Mitoch Principle

What then is Beis Hillel's position? The Gemara infers it from the Mishnah: *mitoch she-huttra hotza'ah le-tzorech huttra nami she-lo le-tzorech* — since carrying was permitted for a purpose (food), it is permitted even without that purpose. And since Beis Hillel says *mitoch* by carrying, the same logic would apply to *shechitah*: once permitted for the purpose of eating, it is permitted even when the purpose is a *korban* that will not be eaten.

This suggests that the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel debate is not confined to carrying. Formulated in the context of carrying, it now appears to be a broader dispute running through the *melachos* of Yom Tov generally — with Beis Shammai holding the allowance is always limited to *ochel nefesh*, and Beis Hillel holding that once a *melacha* was permitted for food it carries a broader license. Two critical questions remain about Beis Hillel's position: first, how much of a *tzorech* is required, if any at all — is a *mitzvah* purpose required, or something less? And second, to which *melachos* does the principle apply? Both questions will occupy much of the Gemara's analysis.

## Rabbah Questions

### Scope of Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבָּה: מִמַּאי דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי? דִּלְמָא בְּעֵרוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְשַׁבָּת וְאֵין עֵרוּב וְהוֹצָאָה לְיוֹם טוֹב קָא מִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba strongly objects to this** reasoning: **From where** do you infer **that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree over this** issue? **Perhaps they disagree** about the following: The *halakhot* of ***eiruv* and** the prohibition against **carrying out** apply **to Shabbat,** but **there are no** *halakhot* of ***eiruv* nor** a prohibition against **carrying out** on a Festival.

Rabbah challenges the inference just drawn. The Gemara has treated the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel dispute as a general debate about whether *mitoch* applies across the *melachos* of Yom Tov. Rabbah asks: who says that is what they are debating? Perhaps their dispute is narrow — concerned specifically with carrying, not with a general principle applicable to all *melachos*.

### Eruv & Hotza'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: עֵרוּב הוֹצָאָה לְשַׁבָּת, וְעֵרוּב הוֹצָאָה לְיוֹם טוֹב. וּמַר סָבַר: עֵרוּב הוֹצָאָה לְשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין עֵרוּב הוֹצָאָה לְיוֹם טוֹב, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא תוֹצִיאוּ מַשָּׂא מִבָּתֵּיכֶם בְּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת״. בְּשַׁבָּת — אִין, בְּיוֹם טוֹב — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute according to this explanation: One **Sage,** Beit Shammai, **holds** that the *halakhot* of ***eiruv*** and the prohibition against **carrying out** apply **to Shabbat, and** similarly the *halakhot* of ***eiruv*** and **carrying out** apply **to a Festival.** The only difference is that, on a Festival, carrying, like other types of prohibited labor, is permitted for the sake of food preparation. **And** one **Sage,** Beit Hillel, **holds** that the *halakhot* of ***eiruv*** and the prohibition against **carrying out** apply **to Shabbat,** but **there are no** *halakhot* of ***eiruv*** nor a prohibition against **carrying out on a Festival, as it is written: "Neither carry forth a burden out of your houses on the Shabbat day"** (Jeremiah 17:22), from which Beit Hillel inferred: **On Shabbat, yes,** carrying from one domain to another is indeed prohibited; **on a Festival, no,** it is not prohibited. According to this explanation, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about whether a prohibited labor that is permitted for the purpose of food preparation on a Festival is also permitted when it does not serve that purpose.

The phrase Rabbah uses is *eruv ve-hotza'ah*, which Rashi explains as referring to whether carrying as a category is subject to prohibition at all. There is unquestionably a prohibition of carrying on Shabbos — from which the Rabbanan derived the entire framework of *eruv chatzeiros* and its related laws. But perhaps, Rabbah suggests, there is no prohibition of carrying on Yom Tov, and consequently none of the *derabbanan* structures of *eruv* apply either.

### Carrying Status

On this reading, the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel debate would be solely about whether carrying is forbidden on Yom Tov at all. Beis Shammai holds that there is indeed such a prohibition on Yom Tov, just as on Shabbos, and therefore the only permissible carrying is for *ochel nefesh*. Beis Hillel holds there is no prohibition of carrying on Yom Tov — and therefore carrying requires no justification whatsoever. If that is so, the debate is entirely about carrying and cannot be generalized to other *melachos*, which would remain governed by the requirement of *ochel nefesh*.

### Jeremiah Source

The biblical basis for Beis Hillel's view, on this reading, is the verse in Yirmiyahu: *ve-lo totzi'u masa mibateichem be-yom ha-Shabbat* — do not carry a load from your house on the Shabbos day. From *be-yom ha-Shabbat* the Gemara derives: on Shabbos yes, on Yom Tov no. Carrying is a Shabbos prohibition, not a Yom Tov one. If Beis Hillel holds this, the implication is a blanket allowance for carrying — no justification required — but also a clear limit: this reasoning is specific to carrying and cannot extend to *shechitah*, fire, or cooking, which remain governed by the requirement of *ochel nefesh* as standard *melachos* of Yom Tov.

## Carrying Unique

### Rashi: Not Core

Rashi's explanation sheds light on why carrying stands apart. In the narrow commentary, Rashi notes: even granting that there is a prohibition of *hotza'ah*, we learn it not from the core verse *lo ta'aseh kol melacha* but from the verse *vaya'aviru kol bamachaneh* — the announcement in the wilderness calling for an end to donations for the *Beis Hamikdash*, from which we derive the prohibition of carrying. And the *pasuk* in Yirmiyahu limits that prohibition to Shabbos. Rashi thus suggests that carrying is not fundamentally included in the prohibition of *lo ta'aseh kol melacha*. To know that carrying is forbidden at all we needed a separate verse. *Shechitah*, by contrast, is an *av melacha*, squarely within *lo ta'aseh kol melacha* as applied on Yom Tov — and therefore even Beis Hillel would not extend a blanket allowance to *shechitah* on the basis of this reasoning alone.

### Melacha Gru'ah

This leads to an idea developed by Tosafot as well, and discussed by the *Rishonim* generally: carrying is a *melacha gru'ah* — a deficient or degraded *melacha*. The conceptual reason is that carrying is the one *melacha* that is non-creative. Every other *melacha* produces something new — it transforms raw material into a finished state. Carrying merely moves an object from point A to point B. Nothing is made; nothing is changed. This is why, when the Gemara in Shabbos discusses the parameters of carrying, it requires extensive *pesukim* to define its particulars — what distance, what domain, what direction — because the act itself has no intrinsic productive character. For all other *melachos*, what matters is the end result: it makes no difference whether food was cooked by frying or roasting; what defines the prohibition is the transformed object. Carrying is categorically different.

### Commerce Role

There is also a distinct sociological dimension to carrying. The verses in Yirmiyahu describing violations of Shabbos in Yerushalayim depict people streaming into the city carrying wheat and goods to the marketplace. The image is not of craftsmen creating objects but of merchants transporting wares for sale. Transportation and conveyance are central to commerce — the shipping and transport industries are the lifeblood of trade — but commerce is not the same as *melacha*. *Melacha* is fundamentally about creating; commerce is about moving and exchanging. Carrying thus belongs as much to the commercial world as to the creative one, and its inclusion within the thirty-nine *melachos* reflects a deliberate extension on Shabbos, not an organic fit.

### Shopping Purpose

This point becomes even sharper in the context of Yom Tov. We have already learned that on Yom Tov one may engage in quasi-commercial activity — going to a shopkeeper and "borrowing" goods to be paid for afterward — in order to procure what is needed for cooking. If someone were told that Yom Tov permits acts related to food, the first things that come to mind are fire and cooking, not carrying. Yet when one reflects on the full process of food production, the first step is going shopping — acquiring the ingredients, conveying them home. Carrying is the act of procurement; it is essential to food preparation at its earliest stage. This is precisely why, conceptually, carrying has a natural connection to the Yom Tov allowance, and why Beis Hillel's broad permission for it — whether grounded in the *mitoch* principle or in a categorical exclusion — makes sense.

## Rambam Framework

### Two Verses

To understand the *mitoch* principle at a deeper structural level, one must attend to a significant point that Ramban raises about the nature of the *melachos* of Yom Tov. There are, in fact, two distinct formulations in the Torah. In one place, the Torah speaks of *ach asher ye'achel le-chol nefesh hu levado ye'aseh lachem* — only what is eaten for any person may you prepare. Elsewhere, wherever the Torah prohibits work on Yom Tov, it uses a qualifier: *kol meleches avodah lo ta'asu* — all laborious work you shall not do — rather than the unqualified *lo ta'aseh kol melacha* of Shabbos.

### Case-by-Case

The *ochel nefesh* formulation implies a case-by-case analysis. Every act must be evaluated: is this being done for the sake of *ochel nefesh*? The baseline is prohibition; the *hetter* is the justification of food preparation. Under this framework, every time one decides whether to perform a *melacha* on Yom Tov, the question is whether the specific act is directed toward eating.

### Categorical Exclusion

The *meleches avodah* formulation implies something structurally different. Rather than requiring justification act by act, it categorically excludes certain types of *melacha* from the prohibition. What counts as *meleches avodah*? Laborious work — the kind done in the field, in industry, in commerce — as opposed to the kind done in the home to produce a meal, which is qualitatively different in character. Under this framework, certain *melachos* are simply not in the prohibited category. Ramban notes that these two formulations are in fact parallel: one place says *kol melacha* and supplies the *hetter* of *asher ye'achel le-chol nefesh*; elsewhere it simply says *meleches avodah*, using the very *ochel nefesh* criterion to define which *melachos* are prohibited in the first place. They point to the same result, but they conceptualize the allowance differently — one as a narrow exception, the other as a categorical exclusion.

## Mitoch Scope

### Building Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לִיפַּלְגוּ בַּאֲבָנִים! אֶלָּא מִדְּלָא מִפַּלְגִי בַּאֲבָנִים, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בְּהוֹצָאָה שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to this** explanation of Rabba: **However, if** that is **so,** that the dispute is whether the prohibition against carrying out applies on a Festival, Beit Hillel should permit one to move *muktze* objects, as the prohibition of handling *muktze* is an extension of the prohibition against carrying out. Consequently, **let them differ with regard to** whether or not it is permitted to carry out **stones** on a Festival. **Rather, from** the fact that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **do not disagree with regard to stones** but concerning objects that serve some sort of purpose, **conclude from this:** Everyone agrees that carrying out is prohibited on a Festival, and the Torah permitted it only when it is necessary for sustenance. **Instead, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to carrying out** that is **not for the purpose** of food preparation.

This question of categorical breadth has immediate practical implications. Tosafot in Shabbos notes that the Gemara there holds that making cheese is a violation of *boneh* — constructing — because milk coagulates and solidifies into a formed object. One might therefore reason: since *boneh* can be performed for food purposes (making cheese), *mitoch she-huttra le-tzorech huttra nami she-lo le-tzorech* — once permitted for a purpose, it is permitted even without that purpose — and therefore one could build a building on Yom Tov for any reason at all. Tosafot raises this inference and acknowledges that biblically it might in fact follow, while suggesting it remains rabbinically forbidden. This is a striking position — and it highlights why the scope of *mitoch* is not self-evident. Does the principle apply to every *melacha* for which a food-related use can be found? Or is it limited to specific *melachos*?

The Gemara, when it applies *mitoch*, does so only in connection with carrying, *shechitah*, fire, and cooking — precisely the *melachos* that are directly and essentially involved in food production. The Gemara never suggests extending *mitoch* to *boneh* on account of cheese-making. This strongly implies that *mitoch* is anchored to the *melachos* that are functionally constitutive of food preparation, not extended mechanically to any *melacha* that happens to admit a food-related application.

## Tosafot View

### Requires Tzorech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: בְּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוֹצָאָה לְצוֹרֶךְ הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ פְּלִיגִי. דְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמְבַשֵּׁל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה בְּחָלָב בְּיוֹם טוֹב וַאֲכָלוֹ — לוֹקֶה חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan also holds** that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **disagree with regard to** the principle: **Since carrying out is permitted** on a Festival **for the purpose** of food preparation, it **was also permitted not for** this **purpose,** as **the *tanna* teaches** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan: One who cooks the sciatic nerve in milk on a Festival and eats it is flogged** for violating **five** distinct prohibitions.

Up to this point the question has been whether the *mitoch* debate is a general principle or limited to carrying. Now the question shifts to the interior of Beis Hillel's position: even granting *mitoch*, how broad is the allowance?

Rav Yosef presses the Gemara with a challenge: if Beis Hillel held that carrying was categorically not forbidden on Yom Tov — that there was simply no prohibition of *hotza'ah* — then they should have permitted carrying stones. Instead they enumerated specific cases of permissible carrying: a minor, a *lulav*, a *sefer torah*. The fact that Beis Hillel's Mishnah lists these cases, rather than simply declaring carrying universally permitted, implies that even according to Beis Hillel there is a prohibition of carrying on Yom Tov — and it becomes permitted through *mitoch*. But *mitoch* is not infinite. Carrying stones for no purpose at all falls outside its scope. Tosafot reads this as the natural and correct reading of the Gemara: you still need some *tzorech*, some justification, even according to Beis Hillel.

Rashi, it should be noted, reads this differently: *mitoch*, once invoked, is infinite. Once the allowance is established, everything is permitted and no justification is needed. But the Gemara's own question — why not debate stones? — argues more naturally for Tosafot's reading.

### Chananel Strict

Rabbeinu Chananel adopts the most stringent reading of this requirement: the *tzorech* must be a *mitzvah* purpose. Only an act serving a *mitzvah* constitutes a sufficient *tzorech yom tov* to bring it within the *mitoch* allowance. On this view, the Mishnah's examples are not illustrative of a broader class; they define the class.

### Simcha Standard

Tosafot explicitly rejects this strict reading. He notes: *lav davka katan le-molo* — it is not specifically a case of *bris milah* that qualifies the *katan*. Even if one simply wants to take a child on a walk on Yom Tov, carrying the child falls within *mitoch*. The standard is not *mitzvah* but *tzorech yom tov* — any purpose fitting the spirit of Yom Tov enjoyment and *simcha*.

### Ball Game

Tosafot invokes an evocative proof: people on Yom Tov play ball — in Tosafot's idiom, *pelota* — in the public domain. They carry for the sake of fun, not for any *mitzvah* purpose. This is not merely a theoretical allowance; it reflects observed practice. Any act that genuinely serves the Yom Tov spirit of *simcha* and joy satisfies the *tzorech* threshold. The limit is at the other extreme: carrying a bookcase to a friend's house — *schlepping* something that has nothing to do with Yom Tov — would not qualify. But that threshold is extremely easy to clear in practice, which is why we carry freely without thinking about justification. The theoretical requirement exists; practically, almost everything qualifies.

## Yochanan & Cooking

### Mitoch Extended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל גִּיד, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם אוֹכֵל גִּיד, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם אוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם הַבְעָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** How so? (1) **He is flogged due to** the prohibition of **cooking** the sciatic **nerve,** which is prohibited because the sciatic nerve is unfit for consumption; (2) **and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **eating** the sciatic **nerve,** which is explicitly prohibited by the Torah; (3) **and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **cooking meat in milk;** (4) **and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **eating meat** cooked **in milk;** (5) **and** lastly, **he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire on a Festival.

The Gemara now introduces Rabbi Yochanan's view: he holds that the *mitoch* principle operates not merely for carrying but as a general principle applying to other *melachos* as well — specifically, fire and cooking. This is the issue raised earlier in passing: just as we tend to carry on Yom Tov without asking whether justification is needed, we also light fire and cook without a moment's hesitation.

### Gid Baraita

A *tana* taught before Rabbi Yochanan the following *baraita*: one who cooks *gid ha-nasheh* with milk on Yom Tov and eats it receives five sets of lashes. The enumeration is instructive. First: lashes for cooking the *gid* on Yom Tov without legitimate purpose — since one may not eat *gid ha-nasheh*, the cooking serves no permissible food purpose. Second: lashes for eating *gid ha-nasheh*, which is prohibited regardless of milk. Third: lashes for cooking meat with milk, the cooking prohibition of *basar be-chalav* — and Rashi here pointedly notes that one must prove this prohibition even exists, citing the principle that *lo tevashel gdi be-chalav imo* is stated three times: once for eating, once for *hana'ah*, and once for cooking. It is striking that Rashi must argue for the prohibition of cooking meat and milk at all, so thoroughly have we internalized the focus on eating. Fourth: lashes for eating *basar be-chalav*. Fifth: lashes for *havarah* — for the fire kindled on Yom Tov without legitimate purpose.

The *baraita* thus contemplates receiving lashes both for cooking without a proper *tzorech* and for making a fire without a proper *tzorech*. This implies that cooking and fire, like *shechitah*, require justification on Yom Tov, and that absent justification, one has violated a biblical prohibition.

### Rejection & Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פּוֹק תְּנִי לְבָרָא, הַבְעָרָה וּבִשּׁוּל אֵינוֹ מִשְׁנָה. וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר מִשְׁנָה — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוֹצָאָה לְצוֹרֶךְ הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ. הָכָא נָמֵי, לָא אָמְרִינַן: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הַבְעָרָה לְצוֹרֶךְ, הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ. דְּאִי בֵּית הִלֵּל — כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרִי מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הוֹצָאָה לְצוֹרֶךְ הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ, הָכָא נָמֵי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּתְּרָה הַבְעָרָה לְצוֹרֶךְ — הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** that *tanna*: **Leave and teach it outside,** i.e., this *baraita* is not fit for discussion in the study hall. The opinion that there is a prohibition against **kindling and cooking** on a Festival **is not a mishna** worthy of serious consideration. **And if you say** that **it is a mishna** rather than an error, this statement is still not in accordance with the *halakha*, as **it is** following the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say: We do not say: Since carrying out was permitted** on a Festival **for the purposes** of food preparation, it **was also permitted** when performed **not for** these **purposes. Here, too,** we do **not** say: **Since kindling was permitted** on a Festival **for the purposes** of food preparation, **it was also permitted** when performed **not for** these **purposes.** **If** one were to suggest that this *baraita* follows the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** this cannot be the case, **as they say: Since carrying out was permitted for the purposes** of food preparation, **it was also permitted** when performed **not for** these **purposes. Here, too, since kindling was permitted for** the **purposes** of food preparation, **it was also permitted** when performed **not for** these **purposes.**

Rabbi Yochanan rejected the *baraita* outright: *puk tani le-vara* — take this teaching outside. It has no place. Specifically, the components concerning *havarah* and *bishul* — fire and cooking — do not belong. If the *baraita* is accepted at all, it represents the position of Beis Shammai, who holds that *mitoch* does not apply, and therefore fire and cooking remain biblically restricted to food purposes. But according to Beis Hillel — and Rabbi Yochanan holds that the *halacha* follows Beis Hillel — *mitoch she-huttra le-tzorech huttra nami she-lo le-tzorech* applies equally to fire and cooking. Just as carrying became broadly permitted once the food-related allowance was established, so too fire and cooking carry a broader license. Therefore, one would not receive lashes for making a fire or cooking on Yom Tov even without a specific food purpose.

## Mitoch Scope…

### Food Melachos

The Gemara's application of *mitoch* is thus confirmed across four *melachos*: carrying, *shechitah*, fire, and cooking. These are the *melachos* that are directly constitutive of food preparation — from procurement through processing. The Gemara does not extend *mitoch* beyond this cluster, reinforcing the understanding that the principle is anchored to what is functionally essential to Yom Tov food production.

### Open Questions

Two questions remain open. The first concerns the threshold of justification: must every individual act of cooking or carrying be purposeful, or is *mitoch* an infinite license once the category is established? Rashi holds the latter — *mitoch* is total, and no justification is needed. Tosafot holds that justification is always required, though the threshold is low and almost universally reachable. The second question concerns which *melachos* fall within *mitoch*: the Gemara confirms the four food-related *melachos*, but the principle of *meleches avodah* — categorical exclusion of non-laborious work — might operate differently.

### Candle Case

A practical instance of these open questions emerges from a case discussed in the Yerushalmi: *ner shel batalah* — lighting a candle on Yom Tov for no purpose. Is this permitted? The question arises in a contemporary context as well: since we have electric lights, the practical necessity of a *ner* on Yom Tov is often absent. When it comes to *havdalah* between Shabbos and Yom Tov, the question arises whether one may light two flames for the *ner* if the second flame serves only a *minhag* and not a strict *halachic* requirement. The *Poskim* resolve this by treating the *minhag* itself as sufficient *tzorech*. But the underlying question — must some degree of justification be present, or is making a fire on Yom Tov categorically unrestricted — remains a live issue. The psychological reality is that we treat fire and carrying on Yom Tov as though no restriction exists; the theoretical framework revealed by the Gemara is more nuanced.

Notably, just as carrying is identified as *melacha gru'ah* — on the edges of the thirty-nine *melachos* — fire has a parallel status in one Talmudic opinion: *havarah le-lav yatzas*, that making a fire is not one of the thirty-nine *melachos* for which one is liable to death, but only a negative prohibition. It is perhaps not coincidental that the two *melachos* for which we most naturally assume a near-categorical Yom Tov allowance are precisely those which occupy the margins of the *lamed tes melachos*.

## Challah & Gifts

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹלִיכִין חַלָּה וּמַתָּנוֹת לַכֹּהֵן בְּיוֹם טוֹב, בֵּין שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵאֶמֶשׁ, בֵּין שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵהַיּוֹם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The separation of *ḥalla* is permitted on a Festival, as one is permitted to prepare dough and bake it on a Festival, and bread may not be eaten without first separating *ḥalla*. **Beit Shammai say: One may not bring** separated ***ḥalla* or** any of the other priestly **gifts,** i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw of a slaughtered animal, **to a priest on a Festival,** though it is permitted to separate them from an animal slaughtered on a Festival. This is prohibited regardless of **whether they were separated last evening,** i.e., before the Festival, **or whether they were separated today. And Beit Hillel permit** it.

With this the Gemara shifts from biblical analysis to a *derabbanan* realm, and the contrast in register is notable. The next Mishnah states: Beis Shammai say one may not deliver *challah* or the priestly gifts (*matanos*) to the *kohen* on Yom Tov; Beis Hillel permit it.

### Separation Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה: חַלָּה וּמַתָּנוֹת — מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, וּתְרוּמָה — מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מוֹלִיכִין אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה — כָּךְ אֵין מוֹלִיכִין אֶת הַמַּתָּנוֹת. אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל: לֹא, אִם אֲמַרְתֶּם בִּתְרוּמָה — שֶׁאֵינוֹ זַכַּאי בַּהֲרָמָתָהּ, תֹּאמְרוּ בְּמַתָּנוֹת — שֶׁזַּכַּאי בַּהֲרָמָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai said to** Beit Hillel: This *halakha* can be derived by **an analogy: *Ḥalla* and** the other **gifts** are both considered **a gift to the priest, and** likewise ***teruma*** separated from produce is also **a gift to the priest. Just as** you agree **that one may not bring *teruma*** to a priest on a Festival, **so too, one may not bring the** other **gifts. Beit Hillel said to them: No,** granted, **if you said** so **with regard to *teruma*,** the reason is **that separation is not allowed** on a Festival, but how **will you say** the same **with regard to the** other **gifts,** concerning **which separation is allowed** on the Festival? It is therefore permitted to bring these gifts to a priest as well.

The context is as follows. We have established that one may bake bread on Yom Tov and may *shecht* an animal. Once bread is baked, one must separate *challah* before eating. Once an animal is *shechted*, one must set aside the *matanos* — the *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah*, the foreleg, cheeks, and maw — for the *kohen*. While the food does not become *treif* if the *matanos* are not separated, practically one sets them aside immediately lest they become mixed in and one inadvertently deprive the *kohen* of his portion. So the separation of *challah* and *matanos* is permitted and expected on Yom Tov as a necessary adjunct to permitted activities.

The question is whether, having separated them, one may deliver them to the *kohen* on Yom Tov. The concern is that permitting delivery of *challah* and *matanos* may lead to delivering *terumah* to the *kohen* — and if one delivers *terumah*, one may come to separate *terumah*, which is forbidden on Yom Tov. This is admittedly a *gezeira le-gezeira* — a rabbinic precaution built on another rabbinic precaution — but that is the basis of Beis Shammai's restriction.

Beis Hillel responds: *challah* and *matanos* are permitted to be separated on Yom Tov; *terumah* is not. Therefore these cases are categorically different and cannot be assimilated to one another. Beis Shammai's comparison — both are gifts to the *kohen*, so if you cannot give *terumah* you cannot give these either — fails because the underlying act of separation has a different status. Where separation is permitted, delivery should follow. Where it is not, the prohibition on delivery stands.

## Three Versions

### Yehuda Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵהַיּוֹם וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ מֵהַיּוֹם, וְשֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵאֶמֶשׁ וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ מֵאֶמֶשׁ. מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֶלָּא אֲחֵרִים. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַמַּתָּנוֹת שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב שֶׁמּוֹלִיכִין עִם הַמַּתָּנוֹת שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵהַיּוֹם וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ מֵהַיּוֹם. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְהוֹלִיכָן בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹלִיכִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹלִיכִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It enters your mind** to explain **that** when the mishna states: **They were separated today,** it means: From animals **slaughtered today. And** the phrase: **They were separated last evening,** is referring to animals **slaughtered last evening.** The Gemara asks: If so, **whose opinion is** expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not Rabbi Yosei nor Rabbi Yehuda, but** the opinion of ***Aḥerim*, as it is taught** in the *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not dispute with regard to gifts separated on the eve of a Festival, that one may bring** them to a priest on a Festival day itself along **with gifts separated that day and** from **those** animals **slaughtered that day. They disputed only** the *halakha* **of bringing** gifts separated the day before **by themselves, as Beit Shammai say: One may not bring** these gifts by themselves, **and Beit Hillel say: One may bring** them.

The Gemara asks which of three *tanna'im* our Mishnah follows, and quotes a *baraita* presenting three distinct versions of the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel debate. The first version, that of Rabbi Yehuda, holds that Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel do not dispute the case where the animal was *shechted* today or the bread was baked today — in such cases, since there was no opportunity to do anything before Yom Tov, everyone agrees the *matanos* may be delivered. Moreover, Beis Shammai even permits bundling yesterday's gifts together with today's when making the delivery. The dispute in this version is narrow: only gifts that were separated as stand-alone items from the previous day, delivered by themselves, are contested.

### Yosi Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַמַּתָּנוֹת שֶׁמּוֹלִיכִין, לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא עַל הַתְּרוּמָה, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹלִיכִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹלִיכִין. וְכָךְ הָיוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל דָּנִין: חַלָּה וּמַתָּנוֹת — מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, וּתְרוּמָה — מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמּוֹלִיכִין אֶת הַמַּתָּנוֹת, כָּךְ מוֹלִיכִין אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה. אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: לֹא, אִם אֲמַרְתֶּם בְּמַתָּנוֹת שֶׁזַּכַּאי בַּהֲרָמָתָן — תֹּאמְרוּ בִּתְרוּמָה שֶׁאֵין זַכַּאי בַּהֲרָמָתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** That is not the correct record of the dispute, as **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not dispute about** the fact **that one may bring the gifts** from an animal to a priest on a Festival. **They disputed only with regard to *teruma*, as Beit Shammai say: One may not bring** it, **and Beit Hillel say: One may** even **bring** *teruma*. **And Beit Hillel would reason** as follows: ***Ḥalla* and gifts** are **a gift to the priest, and *teruma*** is **a gift to the priest. Just as one may bring the** other **gifts** on a Festival, **so too, one may bring *teruma*. Beit Shammai said to them: No; if you said** so **with regard to the** other **gifts,** concerning **which their separation is allowed** on the Festival, **will you say** the same **with regard to *teruma*, whose separation is not allowed?**

Rabbi Yosi's version is broader in its area of agreement. He holds that Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel do not dispute *matanos* at all — both agree they may be delivered on Yom Tov in all cases. The sole dispute is whether *terumah* may be delivered. Beis Shammai says it may not; Beis Hillel says it may. In this version it is Beis Hillel making the comparison: just as everyone agrees *matanos* may be delivered, so too *terumah* — both are gifts to the *kohen*, and the prior allowance to separate *matanos* supports the allowance to deliver. Beis Shammai counters that the cases differ: *matanos* you are entitled to separate on Yom Tov; *terumah* you are not.

### Acherim Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַתְּרוּמָה שֶׁאֵין מוֹלִיכִין, לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא עַל הַמַּתָּנוֹת, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים אֵין מוֹלִיכִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים מוֹלִיכִין. לֵימָא אֲחֵרִים הִיא וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** ***Aḥerim* say** that the dispute was as follows: **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not dispute with regard to *teruma*, that one may not bring** it to a priest on a Festival. **They disputed only** the case **of the** other **gifts, as Beit Shammai say: One may not bring** the other gifts, **and Beit Hillel say: One may bring** them. The Gemara proposes: **Let us say** that the mishna **is** only in accordance with the opinion of ***Aḥerim*, and not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara does not even suggest that the mishna might be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, as it is clear that it cannot be reconciled with his explanation.

The third version, attributed to *Acherim*, holds the strictest position for Beis Shammai: Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree only that *terumah* may not be delivered. On *matanos*, they dispute: Beis Shammai forbids delivery in all cases; Beis Hillel permits it. This version, unlike the first two, gives Beis Shammai no concession on gifts at all. Since our Mishnah presents Beis Shammai as forbidding delivery whether the items were separated yesterday or today, it sounds — at first — like the *Acherim* position.

## Rava Resolution

### Separation Today

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִי קָתָנֵי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ מֵהַיּוֹם וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ מֵהַיּוֹם? שֶׁהוּרְמוּ קָתָנֵי, וּלְעוֹלָם: שְׁחִיטָתָן מֵאֶמֶשׁ. לֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא וְלֹא אֲחֵרִים! אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אֲחֵרִים, וּבְהָנָךְ דְּנִשְׁחֲטוּ מֵאֶמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is it taught** in the mishna: **They were separated that day and were slaughtered that day?** No; **it teaches: They were separated, and actually** the mishna should be explained as follows: **They were slaughtered last evening** and separated today, which is exactly what Rabbi Yehuda said. The Gemara asks: If so, **let us say** that the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and not** in accordance with the opinion of ***Aḥerim*?** The Gemara rejects this: **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of ***Aḥerim*,** it can still be claimed that, in their opinion, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disputed the case **of these** animals that **were slaughtered last evening,** not those slaughtered on the Festival.

Rava clarifies: the Mishnah's language is *shehurmu* — they were separated. The operative variable is when the separation occurred, not when the *shechitah* or baking took place. The Mishnah is discussing a case where separation happened on Yom Tov, but the underlying *shechitah* or baking occurred the previous day. In this framing, Beis Shammai's blanket refusal is consistent with the possibility that even *Acherim* would concede when the *shechitah* itself took place today.

### Concession

Rava argues that even *Acherim* — the strictest version — would agree that Beis Shammai permits delivery when the animal was *shechted* on Yom Tov itself. The dispute in *Acherim*'s formulation concerns only items connected to a *shechitah* done yesterday. Accordingly, our Mishnah's case, which stipulates separation today but leaves *shechitah* from yesterday, is fully compatible with *Acherim*.

### Bundling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ טְפֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same opinion as that of **Rabbi Yehuda.** What, then, is the difference between the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and *Aḥerim*? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is with regard to **secondary** gifts. If one has gifts separated before a Festival, is he permitted to join them as secondary gifts along with others separated on the Festival and transport them together to a priest? Rabbi Yehuda maintains that secondary gifts may be brought to a priest, according to the opinion of Beit Hillel, whereas *Aḥerim* prohibit it.

The remaining difference between the positions is then identified: *tfeila* — the bundling together of yesterday's items with today's. Rabbi Yehuda's version holds that Beis Shammai, conceding about today's items, permits bundling yesterday's gifts along with them into a single delivery. *Acherim*'s version does not include this concession. Where Rabbi Yehuda adds the bundling allowance, *Acherim* grants nothing beyond the case of today's *shechitah*.

## Ruling

### Halacha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַב טוֹבִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחֶמְיָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ גַּרְבָּא דְחַמְרָא דִּתְרוּמָה, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְאַמְטוֹיֵי לְכֹהֵן הָאִידָּנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute cited in the *Tosefta*, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Tovi, son of Rabbi Neḥemya, had a bottle of** *teruma* **wine. He came before Rav Yosef** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **bringing** this wine **to a priest now,** on a Festival? Rav Yosef **said to him: That is what Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who maintains that Beit Hillel permit one to bring *teruma* to a priest on a Festival, and the *halakha* is in accordance with their opinion.

The *halacha* is stated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: the ruling follows Rabbi Yosi. This is the broadest position in terms of Beis Shammai's concessions — indeed, on Rabbi Yosi's reading, Beis Shammai concedes *matanos* entirely and disputes only *terumah*. And on that debate, the ruling follows Beis Hillel, who permits delivering even *terumah* to the *kohen* on Yom Tov.

### Case Study

The ruling is illustrated by a practical case. Tuvi, son of Rav Nechemiah, had a flask of wine that was *terumah* — it had been separated prior to Yom Tov and was sitting in his possession. He came before Rabbi Yosi with a question: may he deliver this wine to a *kohen* on Yom Tov? The wine had been set aside before Yom Tov, not freshly separated that day.

### Yosi Permits

Rabbi Yosi responded by transmitting the ruling: thus said Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi. Go ahead and deliver it to the *kohen* today. According to this ruling, both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree that *matanos* may be delivered; the debate is only about *terumah*; and we rule like Beis Hillel that *terumah* too may be delivered on Yom Tov. The only restriction that remains undisputed is the separation of *terumah* on Yom Tov — that one may not do. But *terumah* that was already separated and is sitting in one's possession may be brought to the *kohen* on Yom Tov itself.
