# Bava Batra 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daughters & Blessing

### Reviah vs. Merivah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיְהִי כִּי הֵחֵל הָאָדָם לָרֹב עַל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה, וּבָנוֹת יֻלְּדוּ לָהֶם – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רְבִיָּיה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מְרִיבָה בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **"And it came to pass, when men began to multiply [*larov*] on the face of the earth, and daughters were born to them"** (Genesis 6:1). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** *Larov* means that **propagation [*reviyya*] came to the world** through these daughters. **Reish Lakish says: Strife [*meriva*] came to the world.**

The Gemara opens with the verse from Bereishit describing the proliferation of humanity upon the earth and the birth of daughters. Two great Amoraim read this verse in starkly contrasting ways. Rabbi Yochanan interprets *ki larov al pnei ha'adamah* as teaching that daughters bring *reviah* — increase — into the world. Daughters grow to become women who give birth, and so their birth is a blessing for the world's fecundity. Reish Lakish, however, reads the phrase as signaling *merivah* — strife — that comes into the world. These are not contradictory positions demanding resolution; they represent a genuine dispute over the valence of daughters' birth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ רְבִיָּיה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם, מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נִכְפְּלוּ בְּנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אִיּוֹב?
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: According to you who say** that due to the daughters **propagation came to the world, for what** reason **were** the number of **Job's daughters not doubled,** when at the end of the story God doubled everything that Job had lost (see Job 1:3, 42:12)?

The dispute comes into focus through a question Reish Lakish presses upon Rabbi Yochanan: if daughters are such a blessing, why, when Iyov was rewarded at the end of the book of Iyov, were his sons doubled in number but not his daughters? If *reviah* is the point, why not twice as many daughters?

### Iyov's Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: נְהִי דְּלֹא נִכְפְּלוּ בְּשֵׁמוֹת, אֲבָל נִכְפְּלוּ בְּיוֹפִי – דִּכְתִיב: "וַיְהִי לוֹ שִׁבְעָנָה בָנִים וְשָׁלוֹשׁ בָּנוֹת, וַיִּקְרָא שֵׁם הָאַחַת יְמִימָה, וְשֵׁם הַשֵּׁנִית קְצִיעָה, וְשֵׁם הַשְּׁלִישִׁית קֶרֶן הַפּוּךְ".
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Granted,** the numbers of Job's daughters **were not doubled in name,** meaning they did not become twice as many, **but they were doubled in beauty, as it is written: "He had also seven sons and three daughters. And he called the name of the first Jemimah, and the name of the second was Keziah, and the name of the third one was Keren-happuch"** (Job 42:13–14). All three names relate to the daughters' beauty.

Rabbi Yochanan's response is that though his daughters were not doubled *bishmot* — in their names, that is, in their numbers — they were doubled *beyofi*, in beauty. The Gemara proceeds to demonstrate this from the very names given to Iyov's three daughters at the end of the book.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָמִימָה – שֶׁהָיְתָה דּוֹמָה לַיּוֹם. קְצִיעָה – שֶׁהָיָה רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף כִּקְצִיעָה. קֶרֶן הַפּוּךְ – אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא: שֶׁדּוֹמָה לְקַרְנָא דְקֶרֶשׁ. מְחַיְּיכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא, קַרְנָא דְקֶרֶשׁ לָקוּתָא הִיא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כְּכוּרְכְּמָא דְרִישְׁקָא בְּמִינֵיהּ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי תִקְרְעִי בַּפּוּךְ".
>
> **Translation:** **Jemimah [*Yemima*];** in her beauty **she was similar to the day [*yom*]. Keziah; her scent wafted like** the **cassia [*ketzia*]** tree. **Keren-happuch; in the school of Rav Sheila they say: She was similar to the horn [*keren*] of a *keresh*,** an animal whose horns are particularly beautiful. **They laughed at this in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, since it is considered **a blemish** when a person resembles **the horn of a *keresh*. Rather, Rav Ḥisda said:** She was **like garden saffron [*kekurkema derishka*],** which is the best **of its kind.** *Keren* refers to a garden, and *pukh* means ornament, **as it is stated: "Though you enlarge** your eyes **with paint [*pukh*],** you beautify yourself in vain" (Jeremiah 4:30).

Yemima, the Gemara explains, was as beautiful as the day — *shehaytah domah leyom*. Ketziah was so fragrant as to recall the *ketziah* herb. As for Keren Hapuch, the school of Rabbi Shila interpreted her name as resembling the horn of a *cheresh*, some manner of antelope. The Sages in Eretz Yisrael, however, ridiculed this interpretation — *karna decheresh lekuta hi*, a horn of that animal is a curse, not a blessing. Rather, Rav Chisda explains: she was like *kurchama derishka*, saffron growing in the garden, which Rashi understands as the most praiseworthy of its kind. The verse *ki tikre'i ba-puch einayich* confirms that *puch* refers to something associated with beauty, much as eye shadow enhances appearance.

This is what draws the entire discussion of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish into the context of the end of the Iyov narrative — a question about whether the restored daughters represent an amplified blessing.

### Tannaitic View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי אִיתְיְלִידָא לֵיהּ בְּרַתָּא, הֲוָה קָא חָלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ: רְבִיָּה בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר קַפָּרָא: תַּנְחוּמִין שֶׁל הֶבֶל נִיחֶמְךָ אֲבוּךְ – [דְּתַנְיָא:] אִי אֶפְשָׁר לָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא זְכָרִים וּבְלֹא נְקֵבוֹת; אֶלָּא אַשְׁרֵי לְמִי שֶׁבָּנָיו זְכָרִים, אוֹי לוֹ לְמִי שֶׁבָּנָיו נְקֵבוֹת. אִי אֶפְשָׁר לָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא בַּסָּם וּבְלֹא בּוּרְסִי; אַשְׁרֵי מִי שֶׁאוּמָּנוּתוֹ בּוּסְמִי, אוֹי לְמִי שֶׁאוּמָּנוּתוֹ בּוּרְסִי.
>
> **Translation:** It is reported that **a daughter was born to Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and **he was upset** that he did not have a son. **His father said to him: Propagation has come to the world** through the birth of a daughter. **Bar Kappara said to** Rabbi Shimon: **Your father has consoled you with meaningless consolation, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The world cannot endure without males and females,** as both are needed for the perpetuation of humanity. **But fortunate is he whose children are males and woe to him whose children are females.** Similarly, **the world cannot endure without either a spice dealer** whose wares are sweet-smelling, **or a tanner [*bursi*],** who is engaged in a foul-smelling occupation. **Fortunate is he whose occupation is a spice seller,** and **woe to him whose occupation is a tanner.**

The Gemara reports that when Rabbi Shimon, the son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, had a daughter, he was disappointed. His father consoled him with the principle: *reviah ba'ah le'olam* — increase comes to the world. Yet Bar Kappara dismissed this consolation sharply — *nichumim shel hevel nichmach avuch*, your father comforted you with a consolation that is *hevel*, emptiness. The implication, of course, is that the consolation was hollow.

The Gemara supports this with a *baraita*: *ee efshar la'olam belo zacharim uvelo nekevot* — the world cannot exist without both males and females; both are needed. And yet the *baraita* does not treat them symmetrically: *ashrei lemi shebanav zacharim, oy lo lemi shebanav nekevot* — praised is one whose children are males, woe to one whose children are females. The Gemara offers a parallel: the world cannot exist without perfumers or without tanners, yet one is praised for being a perfumer and bewailed for being a tanner — somebody must do unpleasant but necessary work. This is a striking and difficult passage, and it deserves to be named as such. The tension between the necessity of daughters and the lament attending their birth reflects social realities that are historically attested and that challenge contemporary sensibilities. Rather than explaining away what the Gemara says, it is more honest to acknowledge it directly and allow the reader to grapple with it on their own terms.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי
>
> **Translation:** —parallel to a dispute between *tanna'im*—

This dispute about how to regard the birth of daughters reflects a *machlokes Tannaim*, the Gemara notes — *ketannai*.

### Abraham's Bakol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַה׳ בֵּרַךְ אֶת אַבְרָהָם בַּכֹּל" – מַאי "בַּכֹּל"? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ בַּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ בַּת. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: בַּת הָיְתָה לוֹ לְאַבְרָהָם, וּ"בַכֹּל" שְׁמָהּ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: אִיצְטַגְנִינוּת הָיְתָה בְּלִבּוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, שֶׁכׇּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב מַשְׁכִּימִין לְפִתְחוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: אֶבֶן טוֹבָה הָיְתָה תְּלוּיָה בְּצַוָּארוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, שֶׁכׇּל חוֹלֶה הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ מִיָּד מִתְרַפֵּא; וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּפְטַר אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ מִן הָעוֹלָם – תְּלָאָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּגַלְגַּל חַמָּה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אִידַּלִּי יוֹמָא אִידַּלִּי קְצִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states: **"And the Lord blessed Abraham with everything [*bakkol*]"** (Genesis 24:1), and the Sages disagree about **what *bakkol*** means. **Rabbi Meir says:** The blessing is **that he did not have a daughter. Rabbi Yehuda says:** On the contrary, the blessing was **that he had a daughter. Others say: Abraham had a daughter and her name was Bakkol. Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says: Abraham our forefather was so knowledgeable in astrology [*itztagninut*] that all the kings of the East and the West would come early to his door** due to his wisdom. This is the blessing of *bakkol*, that he possessed knowledge that everybody needed. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: A precious stone hung around the neck of Abraham our forefather; any sick person who looked at it would immediately be healed. When Abraham our forefather died, the Holy One, Blessed be He, hung** this stone **from the sphere of the sun,** which from that point on brought healing to the sick. **Abaye said: This** explains the adage **that people say: As the day progresses, sickness is lifted.**

The Gemara moves from there to the verse *v'Hashem berach et Avraham bakol* — and God blessed Avraham with *bakol*, with everything. What does *bakol* mean? Four Tannaitic opinions are presented.

Rabbi Meir says *shelo haytah lo bat* — he was blessed in that he had no daughters, reflecting the negative valuation of daughters found in the *baraita* above. Rabbi Yehuda says the opposite: *shehaytah lo bat* — the blessing *bakol* means he had a daughter as well, and that itself was the blessing of completeness. *Acherim* — Others — go further and say not only did Avraham have a daughter, her name was actually *Bakol*, so that when God blessed Avraham *bakol*, He was literally blessing him with his daughter whose name was Everything.

Rabbi Eliezer ha-Modai offers an entirely different reading, unrelated to sons or daughters. He says *bakol* refers to the gift of *itztagninus* — astrological knowledge — that was in Avraham's heart, such that all the kings of east and west would come to his door seeking his interpretations.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai says that Avraham possessed a precious stone hung around his neck — *even tova haytah teluya betzavaro shel Avraham Avinu* — and whoever was ill and looked upon it was immediately healed. When Avraham died, God placed this stone in the sun, which is why people say *idli yoma idli katzira* — as the day grows stronger, the sick gain relief. The healing power of sunlight reflects, in this reading, that Avraham's stone was absorbed into the sun's rays and continues to bring healing to the world.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁלֹּא מָרַד עֵשָׂו בְּיָמָיו. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁעָשָׂה יִשְׁמָעֵאל תְּשׁוּבָה בְּיָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** what is the blessing of *bakkol*? **That Esau did not rebel in** Abraham's **lifetime,** that is to say, as long as Abraham lived Esau did not sin. **Alternatively,** the blessing of *bakkol* is **that Ishmael repented in** Abraham's **lifetime.**

The Gemara then turns to two additional interpretations of *bakol*, both understood as special gifts Avraham received before he died. One is *davar acher shelo marad Esav b'yamav* — another reading of "everything" is that Avraham was blessed in that Esav, who would become so wicked, waited until after Avraham's death to rebel. He did not have to witness his grandson's *rishus* — that itself was a blessing.

A second *davar acher*: *she'asah Yishmael teshuvah b'yamav* — Yishmael did *teshuvah* during Avraham's lifetime, and Avraham was thus blessed to witness that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא מָרַד עֵשָׂו בְּיָמָיו – מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיָּבֹא עֵשָׂו מִן הַשָּׂדֶה, וְהוּא עָיֵף", וְתָנָא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם נִפְטַר אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, וְעָשָׂה יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ תַּבְשִׁיל שֶׁל עֲדָשִׁים לְנַחֵם אֶת יִצְחָק אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that **Esau did not rebel in** Abraham's **lifetime? As it is written:** "And Jacob was cooking a stew **and Esau came in from the field and he was faint"** (Genesis 25:29), **and** a *baraita* **taught: On that day Abraham our forefather passed away, and Jacob our forefather prepared a lentil stew to comfort Isaac, his father,** as it was customary to serve mourners lentil stew.

The proof that Esav refrained from his sins during Avraham's lifetime comes from the verse *vayavo Esav min hasadeh v'hu ayef* — that he came from the field exhausted, which the Tanna connects to the day of Avraham's death. Yaakov was making a stew of lentils to comfort Yitzchak — *tavshil shel adashim l'nachem et Yitzchak aviv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [וּמַאי שְׁנָא שֶׁל עֲדָשִׁים?] אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: מָה עֲדָשָׁה זוֹ אֵין לָהּ פֶּה, אַף אָבֵל אֵין לוֹ פֶּה. דָּבָר אַחֵר: מָה עֲדָשָׁה זוֹ מְגוּלְגֶּלֶת, אַף אֲבֵילוּת מְגַלְגֶּלֶת וּמְחַזֶּרֶת עַל בָּאֵי הָעוֹלָם. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ לְנַחוֹמֵי בְּבֵיעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is different about lentils** that they in particular are the fare customarily offered to mourners? **They say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **in the name of Rabba bar Mari: Just as this lentil has no mouth,** i.e., it does not have a crack like other legumes, **so too a mourner has no mouth,** that is, his anguish prevents him from speaking. **Alternatively, just as this lentil is** completely **round, so too mourning comes around to the inhabitants of the world.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the two explanations? The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to whether it is appropriate **to console** a mourner **with eggs,** which have no opening but are not completely round.

The Gemara asks: why specifically lentils? The Sages in Eretz Yisrael explain in the name of Rabba bar Mari: just as a lentil has no mouth — its surface is smooth with no crevice — so too a mourner should sit in silence rather than challenge or protest. An alternative explanation: lentils are spherical, and *avelut* is similarly spherical — it rolls through the world and returns to all who live. The practical difference between these two readings is whether eggs may be used for mourners' consolation: eggs have no mouth but are not perfectly spherical, so they would fit the first reason but not the second.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חָמֵשׁ עֲבֵירוֹת עָבַר אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם – בָּא עַל נַעֲרָה מְאוֹרָסָה, וְהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ, וְכָפַר בָּעִיקָּר, וְכָפַר בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, וְשָׁט אֶת הַבְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That wicked** Esau **committed five transgressions on that day** that Abraham died: **He engaged in sexual intercourse with a betrothed maiden, he killed a person, he denied the principle** of God's existence, **he denied resurrection of the dead, and he despised the birthright.**

The Gemara then notes that Rabbi Yochanan teaches Esav committed five sins on that very day: *ba al na'arah m'orasah* — he had relations with a betrothed young woman; *harag et hanefesh* — he committed murder; *kafar b'ikar* — he denied the fundamental principle of God; *kafar b'tchiyat hametim* — he denied resurrection; and *shat et habechorah* — he despised the birthright, which is the only one made explicit in the text. Each of the first four is derived by *gezeirah shavah* or verbal parallel with other verses. A *Tosafot* notes a conceptual difficulty: according to the Gemara in the chapter of the four death penalties, the legal status of *eirusin* does not apply to the descendants of Noach, since for them betrothal is effected through *chuppah* without *kiddushin*. If so, how can Esav be culpable for relations with a *na'arah m'orasah*? The answer of the *Ri* is that even if it was not legally prohibited, it was morally reprehensible — *micho'ar hadavar* — and just as despising the birthright, though he was not commanded about it, was still counted against him, so too here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁעָשָׂה יִשְׁמָעֵאל תְּשׁוּבָה בְּיָמָיו – מְנָלַן? כִּי הָא דְּרָבִינָא וְרַב חָמָא בַּר בּוּזִי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם רָבָא... וְהָא יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ "גְּוִיעָה" וַ"אֲסִיפָה"! אַדְּהָכִי אִיתְּעַר בְּהוּ רָבָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: דַּרְדְּקֵי, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יִשְׁמָעֵאל עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה בְּחַיֵּי אָבִיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּקְבְּרוּ אֹתוֹ יִצְחָק וְיִשְׁמָעֵאל בָּנָיו".
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we** derive **that Ishmael repented in** Abraham's **lifetime? From** the incident involving **Ravina and Rav Ḥama bar Buzi,** who **were sitting before Rava, and Rava was dozing** while they were talking... Ravina objected: **But isn't there Ishmael, about whom *gevia* and *asifa* are written,** as it is stated: "And these are the years of the life of Yishmael…and he expired and died [*vayyigva vayyamot*]; and was gathered to his people" (Genesis 25:17)? **Meanwhile Rava,** who had heard the discussion in his dozed state, fully **awoke** and **said to them: Children [*dardekei*], this is what Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Ishmael repented in the lifetime of his father, as it is stated: "And Isaac and Ishmael, his sons, buried him"** (Genesis 25:9).

As for Yishmael's *teshuvah*: the Gemara narrates that Ravina and Rav Chama bar Buzi were sitting before Rava, who dozed off. The students were discussing whether the expression *vayigva* — he expired — always signals a righteous death. One objected from the *dor hamabul*: it says *vayigva kol basar* by those who perished in the flood, and they were not righteous. The response was that the tradition applies specifically when the verse says both *vayigva* and *vayei'asef el amav* — he expired and was gathered to his people. Then the other raised a further difficulty: by Yishmael it says *vayigva vayei'asef* — was he righteous? At that moment, Rava woke up and told them: *dardakei* — children — Rabbi Yochanan taught that Yishmael did *teshuvah* in his father's lifetime.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ! אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, "וַיִּקְבְּרוּ אוֹתוֹ עֵשָׂו וְיַעֲקֹב בָּנָיו", מַאי טַעְמָא לָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן? אֶלָּא מִדְּאַקְדְּמֵיהּ – אַדְבּוֹרֵי אַדְבְּרֵיהּ, וּמִדְּאַדְבְּרֵיהּ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּשׁוּבָה עֲבַד בְּיָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps** the verse **listed them in the order of their wisdom;** that is to say, perhaps in fact Ishmael preceded Isaac but the Torah did not list them in that order. The Gemara answers: **But if that is so,** consider that the verse states: **"And Esau and Jacob, his sons, buried him"** (Genesis 35:29). **What is the reason** that the verse there **did not list them in the order of their wisdom? Rather, since** Ishmael **allowed** Isaac **to precede him,** it is clear that he **made** Isaac **his leader, and since he made him his leader, learn from it that he repented in** Abraham's **lifetime.**

The proof is from the verse *vayikberu oso Yitzchak veYishmael banav* — Yitzchak and Yishmael buried Avraham. Not only did Yishmael come to the burial, but he allowed Yitzchak to go first, thereby accepting Yitzchak's primacy as Avraham's principal heir. One might object that the verse is simply listing them in order of wisdom or importance, not reflecting their actions. But this cannot be the principle, because when Yitzchak was buried, the verse says *vayikberu oso Eisav veYaakov banav* — Esav is listed first. Esav, who did not do *teshuvah*, pressed ahead to be first; Yishmael, who did do *teshuvah*, stepped back and placed Yitzchak before him — *adburei adbreiyei*. From that deferral we derive that *teshuvah avad beyamav* — he did *teshuvah* in his lifetime.

## Death & Righteous

### Three Patriarchs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה הִטְעִימָן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה מֵעֵין הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, אֵלּוּ הֵן: אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב. אַבְרָהָם – דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: "בַּכֹּל". יִצְחָק – דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: "מִכֹּל". יַעֲקֹב – דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: "כֹּל".
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There were **three** people **to whom the Holy One, Blessed be He, gave** already **in this world a taste of the World-to-Come. They are: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Abraham, as it is written with regard to him:** "And the Lord blessed Abraham **with everything"** (Genesis 24:1). **Isaac, as it is written with regard to him:** "And I have eaten **from everything"** (Genesis 27:33). **Jacob, as it is written with regard to him:** "Because I have **everything"** (Genesis 33:11). This teaches that already in their lifetimes they merited everything, i.e., perfection.

Following the discussion of *bakol*, the Gemara transitions to a series of numerical teachings about categories of righteous individuals and their deaths. The *baraita* teaches: *shlosha hit'iman ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ba'olam hazeh me'ein ha'olam haba* — three people God gave a foretaste of the World to Come in this world. They are Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov. The proof for each is the expression of completeness in the verses: Avraham was blessed *bakol* — with everything; Yitzchak said *va'ochal mikkol* — I ate from everything, before he gave the blessing; and Yaakov said *yesh li kol* — I have everything, when speaking to Esav. To say one has *everything* points to the truest everything, which is the *olam haba*. This is the source for the phrase *bakol mikkol kol* in *bentching*, expressing that Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov were each blessed with this sense of totality.

### No Yetzer Hara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה לֹא שָׁלַט בָּהֶן יֵצֶר הָרָע, אֵלּוּ הֵן: אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב; דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: "בַּכֹּל", "מִכֹּל", "כֹּל". וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף דָּוִד, דִּכְתִיב: "וְלִבִּי חָלַל בְּקִרְבִּי". וְאִידַּךְ – צַעֲרֵיהּ הוּא דְּקָא מַדְכַּר.
>
> **Translation:** There were **three** people **over whom the evil inclination had no sway. They are: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as it is written** with regard **to them,** respectively: **"With everything," "from everything," "everything."** The completeness of their blessings means that they did not have to contend with their evil inclinations. **And some say** that **even David** was not subject to his evil inclination, **as it is written: "And my heart has died within me"** (Psalms 109:22), meaning that the evil inclination in his heart was nullified as if his heart had died. **And** how does **the other** authority, who does not include David in his list, explain this verse? **He is mentioning his travails.** David means to say that his heart died within him owing to all the suffering that he endured, but he says nothing about his evil inclination.

The Gemara continues: *shelosha lo shalat bahen yetzer hara* — over three people the *yetzer hara* had no mastery. Again, the three Patriarchs, proven from *bakol mikkol kol*. One must be careful not to misread this. *Tosafot* clarifies: it cannot mean the *yetzer hara* had no influence whatsoever — for then, how would they have earned reward for their righteousness? Virtue requires the possibility of failure. Rather, *Tosafot* explains based on the principle *raglei chasidav yishmor*: once a person has spent the greater portion of his years without sinning, he is unlikely to sin thereafter. The Patriarchs, having pushed themselves so strenuously to distance themselves from sin from the beginning of their lives, received divine assistance such that from a certain point onward the *yetzer hara* no longer prevailed over them — *siyayam ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu mikan va'eilach shelo shalat bahen yetzer hara*.

There is an additional opinion: *ve'yesh omrim af David* — some add David, on the basis of the verse *v'libi chalal bekirbi* — my heart is empty within me — understood as the absence of the *yetzer hara* from his heart. The other view says David was merely describing the suffering of his afflictions, not a freedom from the *yetzer hara*.

### Six No Malach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה לֹא שָׁלַט בָּהֶן מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, מֹשֶׁה, אַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם. אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב – דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: "בַּכֹּל", "מִכֹּל", "כֹּל"; מֹשֶׁה, אַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם – דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: "עַל פִּי ה׳".
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There were **six** people **over whom the Angel of Death had no sway** in their demise, **and they are: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, Moses, Aaron, and Miriam. Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as it is written** with regard **to them,** respectively: **"With everything," "from everything," "everything";** since they were blessed with everything they were certainly spared the anguish of the Angel of Death. **Moses, Aaron, and Miriam, as it is written** with regard **to them** that they died **"by the mouth of the Lord"** (Numbers 33:38; Deuteronomy 34:5), which indicates that they died with a kiss, and not at the hand of the Angel of Death.

The next *baraita*: *shlosha lo shalat bahen malach hamavet* — over six, the angel of death had no mastery. This does not mean they did not die, but rather that they did not suffer in their deaths; they died *beneshikah*, with the divine kiss, peacefully, without the torments associated with the *malach hamavet*. The six are Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam. For the Patriarchs, the proof is again *bakol mikkol kol* — having all good means no suffering in death. For Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam, the proof is the phrase *al pi Hashem* — they died by the word of God, literally by the divine mouth, alluding to the divine kiss.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִרְיָם לָא כְּתִיב בָּהּ: "עַל פִּי ה׳"! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִרְיָם נָמֵי בִּנְשִׁיקָה מֵתָה, דְּאָתְיָא "שָׁם"–"שָׁם" מִמֹּשֶׁה. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נֶאֱמַר בָּהּ "עַל פִּי ה׳" – שֶׁגְּנַאי הַדָּבָר לוֹמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to Miriam it is not written: "By the mouth of the Lord." Rabbi Elazar says: Miriam also died with a kiss, as this is learned** through a verbal analogy between the word **"there"** mentioned in regard to Miriam: "And Miriam died there" (Numbers 20:1), and the word **"there"** mentioned in regard **to Moses:** "And Moses died there" (Deuteronomy 34:5). **And for what** reason **is "by the mouth of the Lord" not stated with** regard to **her? It is unseemly to mention** death by a kiss with regard to a woman.

Rabbi Elazar adds that Miriam also died *beneshikah*, derived by *gezeirah shavah*: by Moshe it says *vayamot sham*, and by Miriam it says *vatamot sham* — the same language signals the same manner of death. If so, why does the verse not say *al pi Hashem* by Miriam? Because, the Gemara says, *shegnai hadavar lomar* — it would be unseemly to speak of the divine kiss in that way across the male–female divide, as it were. The language is therefore deliberately omitted, though the reality is derived through the verbal parallel.

### Seven Preserved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁבְעָה לֹא שָׁלַט בָּהֶן רִמָּה וְתוֹלֵעָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, מֹשֶׁה, אַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם, וּבִנְיָמִין בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב – דִּכְתִיב [בְּהוּ]: "בַּכֹּל", "מִכֹּל", "כֹּל". מֹשֶׁה, אַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם – דִּכְתִיב [בְּהוּ]: "עַל פִּי ה׳". בִּנְיָמִין בֶּן יַעֲקֹב – דִּכְתִיב: "וּלְבִנְיָמִין אָמַר: יְדִיד ה׳, יִשְׁכֹּן לָבֶטַח עָלָיו". וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף דָּוִד, דִּכְתִיב: "אַף בְּשָׂרִי יִשְׁכֹּן לָבֶטַח". וְאִידַּךְ – הָהוּא רַחֲמֵי הוּא דְּקָא בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There were **seven** people **over whom the worm and the maggot had no sway, and they are: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, Moses, Aaron and Miriam, and Benjamin, son of Jacob. Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as it is written with regard to them,** respectively: **"With everything," "from everything," "everything." Moses, Aaron, and Miriam, as it is written with regard to them: "By the mouth of the Lord"; Benjamin, son of Jacob, as it is written: "And to Benjamin he said: The beloved of the Lord, he shall dwell in safety by Him"** (Deuteronomy 33:12). Even in death, he rests securely, unbothered by worms. **And some say** that **even David** is included, **as it is written: "My flesh also dwells secure"** (Psalms 16:9). The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** authority, who does not include David, explain this? The Gemara answers: **He is asking for mercy,** that his flesh should dwell secure and not be subject to worms and maggots, but his request was denied.

The *baraita* continues: *shivah lo shaltah bahen rimah vetolei'ah* — seven people were not consumed by worms or maggots after death. The seven are Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, Moshe, Aharon, Miriam, and Binyamin ben Yaakov. For the Patriarchs the proof is again *bakol mikkol kol*; for Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam it is *al pi Hashem*. The connection to bodily preservation is explained by Rashi: the *malach hamavet*, in the process of taking the soul, deposits a drop of bile or poison into the body that causes it to decompose. Since these individuals did not die through the *malach hamavet*, their bodies were not subject to that decay.

Binyamin is added from the verse *yedid Hashem yishkon lavetach alav* — the beloved of God will dwell in security — which implies security even in death, meaning the body rests undisturbed.

*Ve'yesh omrim af David* — some add David, from the verse *af besari yishkon lavetach* — my flesh will dwell in security. The other opinion responds that David was praying for this — *rachamei hu dekabei* — it was not a statement of fact but a petition.

### Four Sinless Deaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה מֵתוּ בְּעֶטְיוֹ שֶׁל נָחָשׁ. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: בִּנְיָמִין בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, וְעַמְרָם אֲבִי מֹשֶׁה, וְיִשַׁי אֲבִי דָּוִד, וְכִלְאָב בֶּן דָּוִד. וְכוּלְּהוּ גְּמָרָא, לְבַר מִיִּשַׁי אֲבִי דָוִד דִּמְפָרַשׁ בֵּיהּ [קְרָא], דִּכְתִיב: "וְאֶת עֲמָשָׂא שָׂם אַבְשָׁלֹם תַּחַת יוֹאָב עַל הַצָּבָא, וַעֲמָשָׂא בֶן אִישׁ וּשְׁמוֹ יִתְרָא הַיִּשְׂרְאֵלִי, אֲשֶׁר בָּא אֶל אֲבִיגַיִל בַּת נָחָשׁ אֲחוֹת צְרוּיָה אֵם יוֹאָב" – וְכִי בַּת נָחָשׁ הִיא? וַהֲלֹא בַּת יִשַׁי הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: "וְאַחְיֹתֵיהֶם צְרוּיָה וַאֲבִיגַיִל"! אֶלָּא בַּת מִי שֶׁמֵּת בְּעֶטְיוֹ שֶׁל נָחָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There were **four** people **who died** only **because of the counsel of the** primordial **snake,** in the wake of which all of humanity became mortal, and not on account of any personal sin. **And they are: Benjamin, son of Jacob; Amram, father of Moses; Yishai, father of David; and Chileab, son of David. And all of these are known through tradition except for Yishai, father of David, with regard to** whom **it is written explicitly: "And Absalom placed Amasa over the army instead of Joab, and Amasa was the son of a man whose name was Ithra the Israelite, who engaged in intercourse with Abigail, daughter of Nahash, the sister of Zeruiah, mother of Joab"** (II Samuel 17:25). **But was** Abigail **the daughter of Nahash? Was she not the daughter of Yishai, as it is written: "And their sisters were Zeruiah and Abigail"** (I Chronicles 2:16)? **Rather,** she was called "daughter of Nahash" to indicate that **she was the daughter of one who died** only **because of the counsel of the snake.**

The final *baraita* in this sequence: *arba'ah metu be'etyo shel nachash* — four people died on account of the serpent, meaning they had no personal sin warranting death; they died solely because human mortality entered the world through the nachash's deed in the Garden of Eden. The four are Binyamin ben Yaakov, Amram avi Moshe, Yishai avi David, and Khil'av ben David. These are figures who are almost entirely absent from the active narrative of Tanakh. The implication seems clear: to die without any sin of one's own, one must have essentially withdrawn from active engagement with the world. Those who act, lead, and contend in the world will inevitably, despite their best efforts, accumulate some degree of wrongdoing. A completely blameless death is the province of those who never became protagonists.

For all four, these are matters of tradition — *kulhu gemara* — except for Yishai, whose sinlessness is derived from a verse. The *pasuk* describes Amasa as the son of a man named Yitra *haYisraeli*, *asher ba el Avigayil bat Nachash achot Tzruya em Yoav* — who came to Avigayil the daughter of Nachash, the sister of Tzruya the mother of Yoav. But was Avigayil truly the daughter of Nachash? The verse elsewhere makes clear she was a daughter of Yishai, sister of David. Rather, *bat mi shemet be'etzo shel nachash* — she was the daughter of one who died only on account of the serpent and not due to any sin of his own. The name *Nachash* here is therefore not a proper name but a descriptor applied to Yishai: one who died solely because of the *nachash*'s deed.

This sequence of teachings forms a fitting, if implicit, coda to the entire *aggadeta* of Iyov. The Iyov narrative circles around the question of divine justice and undeserved suffering. The Gemara ends this long excursus with the acknowledgment that some people do in fact die without having sinned in any way that warranted death — they die simply because human beings are mortal, a consequence of events in the *gan eden*. Whether or not that resolves the theological tension, it at least locates the phenomenon honestly within the tradition's framework.

## Cistern Distance

### Underground Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יַחְפּוֹר אָדָם בּוֹר סָמוּךְ לְבוֹרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירו,ֹ וְלֹא שִׁיח, וְלֹא מְעָרָה, וְלֹא אַמַּת הַמַּיִם, וְלֹא נִבְרֶכֶת כּוֹבְסִין – אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִכּוֹתֶל חֲבֵירוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, וְסָד בְּסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may not dig a pit close to the pit of another,** in order to avoid damaging the latter's pit. **And** similarly, one **may not** dig **a ditch, nor a cave,** i.e., a covered pit, **nor a water channel, nor a launderer's pond,** which is a pit used for washing clothes, **unless he distanced** all of these **three handbreadths from the wall of another and he plasters lime** on the place where there is water.

With the conclusion of the *aggadeta*, the Gemara turns to the second *perek* of Bava Batra, which addresses a different set of neighborly obligations — not the forced construction of dividing walls, but the activities one undertakes in one's own property that may cause indirect or direct damage to a neighbor.

The *Mishnah* states: *lo yachpor adam bor samuch l'boro shel chaveiro* — a person may not dig a *bor* adjacent to his neighbor's *bor*. The *bor* — a cistern, that is, an excavated pit that collects rainwater without drawing on an underground spring — is the paradigm case. Nor may one dig a *shiach* (a long narrow ditch), a *me'arah* (a cave-like covered excavation), or an *amat hamayim* (an irrigation canal) too close to the neighbor's cistern, nor a *nifrechet kovsin* (a laundry ditch). All such underground construction projects risk weakening the walls of a neighboring cistern if built too close.

The exception: *ella im kein hirchik mi-kotel shel chaveiro shelosha tefachim* — unless one distances the work three *tefachim* from the neighbor's *kotel*, and also plasters — *v'sad b'sid*. The specification of three *tefachim* from the *kotel* rather than from the *bor* itself is significant, as the Gemara will explain below. Since these underground projects work with water, the liquid seeps through the earth and can damage the neighboring cistern; distancing and plastering together form the required preventive measures.

### Heat-Giving Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַרְחִיקִים אֶת הַגֶּפֶת, וְאֶת הַזֶּבֶל, וְאֶת הַמֶּלַח, וְאֶת הַסִּיד, וְאֶת הַסְּלָעִים, מִכּוֹתְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים; אוֹ סָד בְּסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And one must distance the solid residue** of produce that has been pressed free of its oil, e.g., the refuse of olives from which oil has been squeezed, **and** animal **manure, and salt, and lime, and rocks three handbreadths from the wall of another,** as all these items produce heat and can damage the wall. **Or, alternatively, he** may **plaster** the wall **with lime** to prevent damage.

The *Mishnah* continues with a second category of potential damage: *u-marchikin et ha-geves v'et ha-zevel v'et ha-melach v'et ha-sid v'et ha-sla'im mi-kotel shel chaveiro shelosha tefachim* — one must distance *geves* (remnants from the olive press), *zevel* (excrement), *melach* (salt), *sid* (plaster), and *sla'im* (certain rocks that give off heat) three *tefachim* from the neighbor's wall. All of these materials generate heat, and proximity to the wall may cause structural damage.

A textual question arises regarding the phrase that follows. The first category requires both distancing *and* plastering — *ve-sad be-sid*. But here the language may read *o sad be-sid* — or plaster — making it an either/or requirement: one either distances the materials three *tefachim* or plasters the wall of the cavity containing them. *Tosafot* addresses the variant *girsa* directly, holding that the second category requires only one or the other, whereas the underground works require both.

### Agricultural Activities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַזְּרָעִים, וְאֶת הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה, וְאֶת מֵי רַגְלַיִם מִן הַכּוֹתֶל – שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** One must** likewise **distance seeds,** i.e., one may not plant seeds, **and** one may not operate **the plow, and** one must eliminate **urine, three handbreadths from the wall** of another.

The *Mishnah* adds a third category: *marchikin et ha-zra'im v'et ha-macharesha v'et mei raglayim min ha-kotel shelosha tefachim* — one must keep seeds, plowing, and urination three *tefachim* from the neighbor's wall. Seeds and plowing can loosen the soil near a wall and undermine its stability; urine can similarly cause structural harm. These must all be kept at the requisite distance.

### Millstones & Ovens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַרְחִיקִים אֶת הָרֵיחַיִם שְׁלֹשָׁה מִן הַשֶּׁכֶב, שֶׁהֵן אַרְבָּעָה מִן הָרֶכֶב; וְאֶת הַתַּנּוּר – שְׁלֹשָׁה מִן הַכִּלְיָא, שֶׁהֵן אַרְבָּעָה מִן הַשָּׂפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And one must distance a mill** from a neighbor's wall by **three** handbreadths **from the lower stone** of the mill, **which is four** handbreadths **from the** smaller **upper stone** of the mill. **And** there must be a distance of **three** handbreadths **from the** protruding **base [*hakalya*]** of **an oven** until the wall, **which is four** handbreadths **from the** narrow **upper rim [*hassafa*]** of the oven.

The *Mishnah* addresses two further cases with slightly more complex distance measurements. For *reichayim* — millstones — the required distance is three *tefachim* from the *shechev*, the lower stationary stone, which translates into four *tefachim* from the *rechev*, the upper moving stone, since the *rechev* sits atop the *shechev* and extends further upward.

Similarly for ovens: three *tefachim* from the *kilya* — which Rashi explains as the lower edge of the plaster cradle in which the pottery-style oven sits — translating into four *tefachim* from the *safa*, the upper rim of that plaster. *Tosafot* understands the oven differently, as a potbelly stove: the *kilya* is the potbelly portion and the *safa* is the base, with three *tefachim* required from the potbelly and four from the base.

## Wall Width Standard

### Three Handbreadth Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פָּתַח בְּבוֹר – וּמְסַיֵּים בְּכוֹתֶל; לִיתְנֵי: אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִבּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The *tanna* of the mishna **opens by** speaking of **a pit:** A person may not dig a pit close to the pit of another, **and** yet **he ends with** a reference to **a wall:** Unless he distances it from the wall of another. **Let** the *tanna* **teach: Unless he distanced** his pit or ditch **three handbreadths from the pit of another,** just as he begins his statement by referring to a pit. Why does the mishna suddenly mention a wall here?

The Gemara now raises a pointed observation: *patach be-vor mesayem be-kotel* — the *Mishnah* opened with the case of a *bor* but concluded by specifying the distance from the *kotel*, the wall. If the concern is proximity to the neighbor's cistern, should not the *Mishnah* have said to distance oneself from the cistern itself, not from its wall?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יְהוּדָה: "מִכּוֹתֶל בּוֹרוֹ" שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said, and some say it was Rav Yehuda** who said: **We learned** that the mishna means: **From the wall of his pit.**

Abaye — and some say it was Rav Yehuda — resolves this: *mi-kotel boro shananu* — when we say three *tefachim*, we mean from the wall of his cistern. This formulation carries an important implicit teaching: a *bor* by its nature has a surrounding wall, and a standard such wall is three *tefachim* wide. This is not merely a technical point about how to measure distance; it is an independent statement about the normative width of a cistern wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי: אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִבּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּכוֹתֶל בּוֹר שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים; נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לְמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר – כִּדְתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: "בּוֹר וְכוֹתְלֶיהָ אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ" – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא הַכּוֹתֶל שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But** even so, **let it teach: Unless he distanced** his excavations **three handbreadths from the pit of another,** and one would understand that the term pit is referring to the wall of the other's pit. The Gemara responds: By using the phrase: From the wall of another, **this teaches us** incidentally **that the wall of a pit** must be at least **three handbreadths** thick, as the wall of the other's pit occupied the full three handbreadths between the cavity of his pit and the property of his neighbor. The practical **difference** of this observation is **with regard to buying and selling, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says to another: I am selling you a pit and its walls, the wall** of the pit **must be** at least **three handbreadths** thick.

One might ask why the *Mishnah* would not simply say to keep three *tefachim* from the *bor* itself. The answer is that by specifying the *kotel*, the *Mishnah* tells us what the standard wall width is — namely three *tefachim*. The practical consequence for measurement is substantial: if I must distance my excavation three *tefachim* from my neighbor's wall, and his wall is itself three *tefachim* wide, then my excavation begins six *tefachim* from the hollow of his cistern.

There is a direct *nafka mina le-makach umimkar* — a practical implication for commercial transactions. A *baraita* teaches: *ha'omer le-chaveiro bor ve-kotlo ani mocher lach* — one who says to his fellow, "I am selling you a cistern and its wall" — *tzarich sheyehei ha-kotel shelosha tefachim* — that wall must be three *tefachim* wide. The standard width of three *tefachim* is thus both a construction norm and a term that carries legal content in a sale. If a seller promises a *bor* and its *kotel*, the buyer is entitled to a wall meeting that standard measure.

## Boundary Digging

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַבָּא לִסְמוֹךְ בְּצַד הַמֶּצֶר – אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: סוֹמֵךְ, וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ. בְּשָׂדֶה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to **one who comes to** dig any of these excavations or **place** any of the items listed in the mishna **close to the boundary** of his field, where his neighbor currently has no pit, **Abaye says: He may** dig or **place** them **close** to the boundary; **and Rava says: He may not** dig or **place** them **close** to the boundary. The Gemara explains: **All agree that he may not** dig or **place** them **close** to **a field** of his neighbor if **that** field **is designated for** digging **pits,** as he will thereby restrict his neighbor from using his field in the manner it is expected to be used. **When they disagree** it is **with regard to a field that is not designated for** digging **pits.**

The Gemara now turns to a fresh question: what if a person wishes to be the first to dig a cistern in a field, and there is no existing cistern on the neighbor's side — may he dig right up to the boundary line itself? This is the *she'elah* of *haba lismoch betzad hametzar* — one who comes to press up against the border.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: סוֹמֵךְ – דְּהָא אֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת. רָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאַתְּ אִימְּלַכְתְּ וַחֲפַרְתְּ, אֲנָא נָמֵי מִמְּלַכְנָא וְחָפַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Abaye says: He may** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary, **as** the neighbor's field **is not designated for pits,** so he is not causing any damage by doing so. **Rava says: He may not** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary, **as** the neighbor can **say to him: Just as you changed your mind and dug** a pit, **I too** might **change my mind and dig** a pit, and I will no longer be able to dig near my border if you dig your pit close to the boundary.

Abaye's position — *somech* — is that in a field that is not normally suited for cisterns (*sadeh she'einah asuyah le-vorot*), one may dig right up to the boundary line. Since it is not normal practice to build cisterns in such a field, there is no realistic expectation that the neighbor will do the same. Accordingly, there is no imposition upon the neighbor: one is not forcing the other party to absorb the entire required distance within their own property. One simply exercises the right to use one's own land as one chooses, without being obligated to anticipate an unusual use the neighbor is unlikely to make.

Rava disagrees: *eino somech* — one may not dig right at the boundary line even in a field not ordinarily used for cisterns. His reasoning is that the neighbor can say: *ki heichi de'at imlacht ve-chafarta*, just as you changed your mind and decided to dig an unusual cistern, *ana nami imlachna ve-chafarna* — I too may change my mind and decide to dig one. The fact that it is not customary does not eliminate the neighbor's right to make a different choice in the future. If I dig at the boundary line, I may be forcing the neighbor — should they later choose to build a cistern — to absorb the entire required distancing within their own property. That is an unfair imposition, and it is therefore prohibited regardless of whether the field is the sort normally used for cisterns.

In a field that *is* normally suited for cisterns (*sadeh ha'asuyah le-vorot*), there is no dispute: everyone agrees the person may not dig right up to the boundary line, since one can readily anticipate that the neighbor will want to do the same, and building at the line forces the full distancing burden onto the neighbor's property.

### Rava's Position

The Gemara contextualizes the Abaye-Rava debate within the broader dispute between Rabbi Yose and the *Rabbanan* — a foundational disagreement about who bears primary responsibility for preventing damage in neighboring properties. The *Rabbanan* hold *al ha-mazik le-harchik et atzmo* — the one causing damage must distance himself. Rabbi Yose holds *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* — the one liable to be damaged must protect himself; the active party is free to do as he wishes within his own domain.

Abaye's leniency aligns with this framework but adds nuance. Even the *Rabbanan*, who require the *mazik* to distance himself, would agree that if there is no existing cistern on the neighbor's side, there is no act of damage being committed at the time of digging. One is not threatening anything present. If no *bor* exists on the other side, one is not the *mazik* in any immediate sense. The *Rabbanan*'s rule applies when there is an existing cistern to protect — *be'idna de-kanta ita le-vor*, at the time of planting the roots have not yet spread, is the comparable principle in the tree-and-well case — but not when there is nothing there yet to be damaged.

### Rabbi Yose Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל סוֹמֵךְ, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּשָׂדֶה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: סוֹמֵךְ – אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן, דְאָמְרִי: מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הָאִילָן מִן הַבּוֹר עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ אַמָּה – הָתָם הוּא דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא נָטַע, אִיתָא לְבוֹר; אֲבָל הָכָא, בְּעִידָּנָא דְּקָא חָפַר – לֵיתָא לְבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this discussion: **All agree** that **one may** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary of **a field that is not designated for** digging **pits. When they disagree** it is **with regard to a field that is designated for** digging **pits. Abaye says: One may** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary; and this is the *halakha* **even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** in the mishna (25b): **One must distance a tree twenty-five cubits from a cistern,** lest the roots of the tree grow and damage a neighbor's cistern in his field. The difference is that **there it** is prohibited, **as when he plants** the tree **there is** already **a cistern. But here,** at the time **when he digs** his cistern, **there is** as yet **no cistern** in his neighbor's field.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ – וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר: זֶה חוֹפֵר בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְזֶה נוֹטֵעַ בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי הָתָם, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא נָטַע – לֵיתַנְהוּ לְשׇׁרָשָׁיו דְּמַזְּקִי לַהּ לְבוֹר; אֲבָל הָכָא – אָמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל מָרָא וּמָרָא דְּקָא מָחֲיֵית, קָא מְרַפֵּית לַהּ לְאַרְעַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** One **may not** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary; and this is the *halakha* **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says** in that same mishna: **This one may dig in his** field **and that one may plant in his** field, i.e., one need not distance his tree for fear of damaging his neighbor's field through expanding roots. The reason that Rava maintains that his ruling is correct even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei is that **this matter** applies only **there, as when he plants** the tree, **its roots, which** might **damage the cistern, are not** yet present. Consequently, when he plants he does not cause any damage. **But here,** the neighbor can **say to him: Each and every strike of yours with the hoe loosens my earth,** and therefore you are already causing damage as you dig your cistern.

A striking element of Rava's position is that he invokes even Rabbi Yose as support. Rabbi Yose permits planting a tree right up against a neighbor's existing cistern, on the grounds that at the time of planting, the roots have not yet extended into the other's property — *lish'at shav d'maskei lei l'vor*, right now they do nothing — and Rabbi Yose holds that indirect, eventual consequences do not obligate the planter to distance himself. Yet even Rabbi Yose, Rava argues, would prohibit digging a cistern at the boundary line.

Why the distinction? Because in the case of digging, there is an argument that the damage is direct and immediate. *Kol mara u-mara de-kamachis, kamarfis lei l'ara* — every shovel blow weakens the land. Even without an existing cistern on the other side, in a field where cisterns are a normal use of the land, every act of digging diminishes the structural capacity of the adjacent soil. The neighbor can say: right now, as you dig, you are degrading my land's utility for the very type of construction it would naturally support. This is not an eventual future harm like spreading roots — it is a present and ongoing weakening of the ground. Therefore, even Rabbi Yose would concede that in this case the digger cannot press right up to the boundary line.

## Mishna Resolution

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לֹא יַחְפּוֹר אָדָם בּוֹר סָמוּךְ לְבוֹרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. טַעְמָא דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹר, הָא לֵיכָּא בּוֹר – סוֹמֵךְ; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל סוֹמֵךְ, מַתְנִיתִין בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof: **We learned** in the mishna that **a person may not dig a pit close to the pit of another,** unless he does so at a distance of three handbreadths from his neighbor's wall. The Gemara analyzes this statement: Apparently, the **reason** he may not dig close to the boundary of his neighbor's field **is that there is a pit** there, from which it may be inferred that if **there is no pit he** may dig his pit **close** to his neighbor's wall. **Granted, according to that** second **version** of the dispute, **in which you said: All agree** that **one may** dig these excavations or **place** these items **close** to the boundary of **a field that is not designated for** digging **pits,** one can explain that **the mishna** is referring **to a field that is not designated for** digging **pits.**

The Gemara now confronts a challenge to Rava from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* states: *lo yachpor adam bor samuch l'boro shel chaveiro* — one may not dig a cistern adjacent to one's neighbor's cistern. The implication of *taama de'ika bor* — the reason is that a cistern is already present — is *ha leika bor, somech*: if there were no cistern on the other side, one could go right up to the boundary line. But this is precisely what Rava denies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְבוֹרוֹת פְּלִיגִי – בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to that** first **version** of the dispute, **in which you said** that **they disagree with regard to a field that is not designated for** digging **pits,** there is a difficulty. **Granted,** this **works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava,** who says that it is prohibited to dig a pit near the boundary in this case. **But according to** the opinion of **Abaye,** the ruling of this mishna is **difficult,** as if the mishna is referring to the wall of the neighbor's pit, this indicates that the first pit was dug close to the boundary.

If Rava holds that even without an existing cistern on the other side one cannot dig at the boundary, why does the *Mishnah* depict the first party with a wall, implying he pulled back? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יְהוּדָה: מִכּוֹתֶל בּוֹרוֹ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rava** could have **said to you: Wasn't it stated with regard to that** mishna that **Abaye says, and some say it was Rav Yehuda** who says: **We learned** that the mishna means: **From the wall of his pit?** This indicates that the neighbor must distance the edge of his pit from the boundary by the thickness of his wall, which is three handbreadths. Therefore, even according to the ruling of the mishna, one may not dig his pit directly adjacent to the boundary.

Rava's response draws on the earlier resolution of Abaye and Rav Yehuda: *mi-kotel boro shananu* — the *Mishnah* is talking about distancing from the *wall* of the cistern. And the point is not merely a measurement clarification. The *Mishnah* describes the first party as already having a wall around his cistern. Why does the first party have a wall? Because even the first party — the one who dug before anyone else was there — was not permitted to dig at the boundary line and therefore set his cistern back, leaving room for the wall. The *Mishnah*'s scenario of an existing cistern with a wall is Rava's evidence that the prohibition applies universally: even the first digger could not go to the boundary line. The wall's existence in the *Mishnah* is testimony to that fact.

Building on this, Rava reads the *Mishnah*'s scenario as inherently teaching that the three-*tefach* distancing requirement is not contingent on there being an existing cistern across the border. Whether or not the neighbor has built anything, the requirement to maintain distance applies. The case described — with an existing cistern and wall on the neighbor's side — is simply the vehicle through which the *Mishnah* communicates that even the first actor had to stay back. One should not read the *Mishnah* as implying that without a cistern on the other side, the boundary line would be open for digging. That implication, Rava says, is dissolved once one understands that the wall mentioned in the *Mishnah* represents the constraint already operative on the first party.

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי: מַתְנִיתִין שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחְפּוֹר בְּבַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Abaye** could have **said to you: The mishna** is referring to the specific case **where** both neighbors **came to** dig their **pits at the same time.** Consequently, they must both distance their pits from one another. If there is no pit as yet next to the boundary, and the neighbor is not digging at that point, one may dig his pit alongside the boundary.

For Abaye, however, this reading creates its own difficulty. If Abaye holds that one *may* dig right up to the boundary line when no cistern exists on the other side, why does the *Mishnah* depict the first party with a wall, implying he pulled back? Abaye's answer is that the *Mishnah* is addressing a scenario in which both parties come to dig simultaneously — *matnitin she-bau lachpor be-vat achat*. When both are digging at the same time, neither can claim priority; neither can press to the boundary line at the expense of the other. Both must therefore share the required distance equidistantly, each pulling back from the boundary line in equal measure. In that scenario, walls naturally appear on both sides, and the required distance between the two cisterns is shared rather than borne entirely by one party.

According to one understanding of Abaye, had a single party come first without any existing cistern on the other side, no wall would have been necessary and the cistern could have been dug at the boundary line. But when both come simultaneously, the equidistant sharing of the required distance is itself the operative principle — and it is this scenario that the *Mishnah* describes.
