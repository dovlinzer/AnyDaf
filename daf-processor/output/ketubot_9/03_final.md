# Ketubot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Brachot Aveilim & Shiva

### Intro Aveilim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בְּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — בְּשׁוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is **with regard to** combining with others to form a quorum of three to recite **Grace after Meals,** as a mourner is obligated to recite Grace after Meals. However, **when Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that mourners are not included in the tally, it is **with regard to** the quorum of ten men required to form **a line** to comfort the mourners following the burial.

The Gemara on 8b introduces the institution of *birchat aveilim*, the special blessing recited for mourners, which likewise requires a *minyan* — with the important distinction that the *aveilim* themselves do not count toward that *minyan*. The Gemara also discusses the *rechava*, the practice of the mourners walking back from the burial while the community forms two rows to offer *nechama*, and the *seudat havra'ah*, the meal of comfort served to the mourner immediately upon returning. Whether the *minyan* requirement for that *seudat rechava* differs from the *minyan* required for the *birchat hamazon* at subsequent meals is not entirely clear from the Gemara, but what does emerge plainly is that *birchat aveilim* forms a recognized component of *birchat hamazon* in the house of mourning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְבָרְכִים בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה, וַחֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. וּבִרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. בְּרָכָה בְּשׁוּרָה מִי אִיכָּא? אֶלָּא כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּרְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara raises an objection: **However, that which Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One recites the blessing of the grooms in** a quorum of **ten, and the grooms are included in the tally, and one recites the blessing of the mourners in** a quorum of **ten, and the mourners are not included in the tally. Is there a blessing** recited **in the line** formed to comfort the mourners? Clearly, this statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan cannot be explained as referring to the line. **Rather, when Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that mourners are not included in the tally, it was **with regard to** the blessing recited **in the square** adjacent to the cemetery, where the meal of comfort takes place and various blessings are recited to comfort the mourners. Mourners are not included in the requisite quorum of ten.

The Gemara then asks: the statement of Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that *birchat chatanim* is recited with ten throughout the entire seven days, with the *chatanim* counting toward the *minyan*, parallels a teaching that *birchat aveilim* is recited with ten throughout the entire seven days, but with the *aveilim* excluded from the *minyan* — since the roles of *menachamim* and *aveilim* are fundamentally distinct. The Gemara asks: where do we find *birchat rechava* repeated throughout the week? And it answers: *meshkachat lah bipanim chadashot* — just as *panim chadashot*, new faces, arriving during the week generate renewed *simcha* and therefore occasion the recitation of *sheva berachot* anew, so too when new *menachamim* arrive there is a fresh opportunity for *nechama*, and *birchat aveilim* would be recited again.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְבָרְכִין בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, וַחֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. וּבִרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. בִּרְכַּת רְחָבָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה מִי אִיכָּא? מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּפָנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However,** with regard to **that which Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One recites the blessing of the grooms in** a quorum of **ten all seven** days of celebration, **and the grooms are included in the tally; and one recites the blessing of the mourners all seven** days of mourning **in** a quorum of **ten, and the mourners are not included in the tally, is there a blessing** recited **in the square all seven** days? The meal of comfort and the associated blessings take place directly after the burial, not throughout the seven days of mourning. The Gemara answers: **You find** blessings recited throughout the seven-day mourning period **in** a case where **new faces** who did not attend the burial are present. In that case, eulogies and words of comfort are repeated, and the blessing of the mourners is recited again.

It is worth noting that the Rambam, in his discussion of *hilchot berachot*, records the *nusach* of *sheva berachot* in full but offers only a simplified version of *birchat aveilim*, which likely reflects the fact that by his time this practice had largely fallen into disuse. The Rambam's formulation adds to the fourth *beracha*, *hatov vehametiv*, language of *dayan emet* — affirming divine truth and judgment — and then provides an opportunity to add one's own words of *nechama* for the *avel* before the *harachamanim*, which function as a kind of appendage to the four main *berachot*. Whether this captures the full original *nusach* as the Gemara presents it is, as we shall see, an open question.

### Rabbi Chiya Bar Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא מַקְרֵי בְּנֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מַתְנִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה. שְׁכֵיב לֵיהּ יָנוֹקָא. יוֹמָא קַמָּא לָא אֲזַל לְגַבֵּיהּ. לִמְחַר דַּבְרֵיהּ לִיהוּדָה בַּר נַחְמָנִי מְתוּרְגְּמָנֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם אֵימָא מִלְּתָא כׇּל קֳבֵיל יָנוֹקָא. פְּתַח וַאֲמַר: "וַיַּרְא ה׳ וַיִּנְאָץ מִכַּעַס בָּנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו", דּוֹר שֶׁאָבוֹת מְנָאֲצִים לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — כּוֹעֵס עַל בְּנֵיהֶם וְעַל בְּנוֹתֵיהֶם, וּמֵתִים כְּשֶׁהֵם קְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** § This is **similar** to **that** incident **involving Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba, who was the Bible teacher of the sons of Reish Lakish, and some say** that he was **the Mishna teacher of the son of Reish Lakish. His child died. On the first day,** Reish Lakish **did not go to** comfort **him. The next day, he took Yehuda bar Naḥmani, his disseminator,** with him to comfort Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba. Reish Lakish **said to** his disseminator: **Stand and say a matter** of comfort **with regard to** the death of **the child. He began and said: "And the Lord saw it and He abhorred them, due to the provocation of His sons and His daughters"** (Deuteronomy 32:19). **A generation in which the fathers abhor the Holy One, Blessed be He, He is angered at their sons and their daughters, and they die when they are small.**

The Gemara relates the following story. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba served as the *mikrei b'nei* — the *cheder* teacher — of Resh Lakish's sons. A child of his died. On the first day Resh Lakish did not go to pay a *shiva* call, and on the second day he went, bringing with him Yehuda bar Nachmani, his *meturgeman*. A *meturgeman* was a kind of public announcer who would receive the Sage's words and then proclaim them to the assembled students or community; he was sometimes given a degree of latitude to elaborate. Resh Lakish instructed Yehuda bar Nachmani: *kum eima miltha kaveil yanuka* — rise and say some words in commemoration of the child who had passed away.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בָּחוּר הֲוָה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: "עַל כֵּן עַל בַּחוּרָיו לֹא יִשְׂמַח ה׳ וְאֶת יְתוֹמָיו וְאֶת אַלְמְנוֹתָיו לֹא יְרַחֵם כִּי כֻלּוֹ חָנֵף וּמֵרַע וְכׇל פֶּה דֹּבֵר נְבָלָה בְּכׇל זֹאת לֹא שָׁב אַפּוֹ וְעוֹד יָדוֹ נְטוּיָה". מַאי "וְעוֹד יָדוֹ נְטוּיָה"? אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב: הַכֹּל יוֹדְעִין כַּלָּה לָמָּה נִכְנְסָה לַחוּפָּה. אֶלָּא, כׇּל הַמְנַבֵּל פִּיו, וּמוֹצִיא דְּבַר נְבָלָה מִפִּיו, אֲפִלּוּ נֶחְתַּם לוֹ גְּזַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה לְטוֹבָה — נֶהְפָּךְ עָלָיו לְרָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those who **say** that the child who died **was** not a small child, but **a youth, and this is what** the disseminator **said to him: "Therefore the Lord shall have no joy in their young men, nor shall He have compassion on their orphans and widows, for everyone is a flatterer and an evildoer, and every mouth speaks obscenity. For all this His anger is not turned away, and His hand is still outstretched"** (Isaiah 9:16). **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"And His hand is still outstretched"? Rav Ḥanan bar Rav said: Everyone knows why a bride enters the wedding canopy.** It is the step before consummation of the marriage. **However,** one should not speak about it unnecessarily, as **anyone who profanes his mouth and issues a matter of profanity from his mouth, even if a positive decree of seventy years was sealed for him,** nevertheless, **it is transformed for him into an evil** decree.

Yehuda bar Nachmani stood and opened with the verse *vayar Hashem vayina'atz mikas banav uvnotav* — "God saw and was provoked by the anger of His sons and daughters." He interpreted this to mean: in a generation whose fathers provoke God, God's anger is directed at their children, and the children die young. He went on to cite a second possibility — that the deceased was a young adult, not an infant — and quoted another verse: *al kein al bachurav lo yismach Hashem v'et yetomav v'et almanotav lo yerachem*, "therefore God will not rejoice over his young men, nor will He show compassion to their orphans and widows, for all of them are faithless and evildoers, and every mouth speaks *nevala*." The Gemara takes up this last phrase in a related digression. Rav Chana bar Abba teaches that *hakol yod'im kalla lama nichnesa l'chuppa* — everyone knows why the bride enters the *chuppa* — and yet whoever commits *nival peh*, who speaks of such matters explicitly, even if seventy years of good fortune were decreed for him, it will be reversed for evil. It is a striking formulation, particularly because the statement is framed entirely around the *kalla* rather than the groom — an unusual focus given the Gemara's general tendency to address male sexual consciousness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְנַחוֹמֵי — צַעוֹרֵי קָמְצַעַר לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this incident: **He came** at the behest of Reish Lakish **to comfort** Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba **and** instead **he upset him** by attributing the death of his son to his transgressions!

The Gemara itself voices the obvious critique: *ata lenichumei tzo'arei ka metza'er lei* — he came to offer consolation and instead caused anguish. The Gemara then offers an exculpatory reading: what Yehuda bar Nachmani meant to say was that Rabbi Chiya bar Abba was so important a figure that he bore the suffering of an entire generation — it was the generation's sin, but a person of such high *madrega* is the one who bears its consequences. Even granting that interpretation, however, the statement remained a painful one to deliver, and it stands as a vivid illustration of how people say hurtful things at a *shiva* call even with the best of intentions.

### Seven Brachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם אֵימָא מִלְּתָא כְּנֶגֶד שְׁבָחוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. פָּתַח וְאָמַר: "הָאֵל הַגָּדוֹל בְּרוֹב גׇּדְלוֹ, אַדִּיר וְחָזָק בְּרוֹב נוֹרָאוֹת, מְחַיֵּה מֵתִים בְּמַאֲמָרוֹ, עוֹשֶׂה גְדוֹלוֹת עַד אֵין חֵקֶר וְנִפְלָאוֹת עַד אֵין מִסְפָּר. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ מְחַיֵּה הַמֵּתִים".
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to his** disseminator: **Stand and say a statement with regard to the praiseworthiness of the Holy One, Blessed be He. He began and said: God, Who is great in the abundance of His greatness, mighty and strong in the abundance of His awesome** deeds, **Who revives the dead in** fulfillment of **His statement, Who does great deeds beyond comprehension, wondrous deeds without number. Blessed are You, Lord, Who revives the dead.**

Resh Lakish then turned to Yehuda bar Nachmani and gave successive instructions, each producing a distinct *beracha*. The first instruction: *kum eima milta keneged shivcho shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — rise and say words in praise of God. Yehuda bar Nachmani proclaimed:

> *el gadol berov godlo adir vechazak berov nora'ot mechaye metim bema'amaro ose gedolot ad ein cheker venifla'ot ad ein mispar — baruch ata Hashem mechaye hametim.*

This is a full *baruch ata Hashem* blessing, framed as praise of God, but unmistakably resonant in the setting of a *beit avel*: God who revives the dead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם אֵימָא מִלְּתָא כְּנֶגֶד אֲבֵלִים, פָּתַח וְאָמַר: "אַחֵינוּ הַמְיוּגָּעִים, הַמְדוּכָּאִין בָּאֵבֶל הַזֶּה, תְּנוּ לְבַבְכֶם לַחְקוֹר אֶת זֹאת. זֹאת הִיא עוֹמֶדֶת לָעַד, נָתִיב הוּא מִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְרֵאשִׁית. רַבִּים שָׁתוּ, רַבִּים יִשְׁתּוּ. כְּמִשְׁתֵּה רִאשׁוֹנִים כָּךְ מִשְׁתֵּה אַחֲרוֹנִים. אַחֵינוּ, בַּעַל נֶחָמוֹת יְנַחֵם אֶתְכֶם. בָּרוּךְ מְנַחֵם אֲבֵלִים".
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to him: Stand and say a statement with regard to the mourners. He began and said: Our brothers, who are exhausted, who are overwhelmed by this mourning, set your heart to examine this: This is what stands forever. It is a path from the six days of Creation,** i.e., death exists since Creation, and it is well known that this is the fate of man. **Many have drunk** from the poisonous cup of death, **and many will drink. As** was the consequence of the **drink of the first** who have drunk, **so too** will be the consequence of the **drink of the last** who will drink. **Our brothers, may the Master of solace comfort you. Blessed** are You, Lord, **Who comforts the mourners.**

Resh Lakish then said: *kum eima milta keneged avelim* — now say something directed toward the mourners. This is different in character from the preceding blessing, which praised God. The *birchat aveilim* proper follows:

> *acheinu hameyuga'im hameduka'im ba'avel haze — tnu levavkhem lachkor et zot: zot omedet la'ad, nativ hu misheshet yimei Bereshit. Rabim shatu verabim yishtu — kemishte rishonim kach mishte acharonim. Acheinu ba'al nechamot yenachem etkhem — baruch menachem avelim.*

The message is one of consolation through universal human experience: this is the way of the world since the six days of creation, many have drunk from these waters before you, and many will drink after — the Master of consolations should comfort you. The blessing concludes: *baruch menachem avelim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: "רַבִּים שָׁתוּ" — לֵימָא. "רַבִּים יִשְׁתּוּ" — לָא לֵימָא. "מִשְׁתֵּה רִאשׁוֹנִים" — לֵימָא. "מִשְׁתֵּה אַחֲרוֹנִים" — לָא לֵימָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, וְכֵן תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לְעוֹלָם אַל יִפְתַּח אָדָם פִּיו לַשָּׂטָן. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, מַאי קְרָא: "כִּסְדוֹם הָיִינוּ לַעֲמוֹרָה דָּמִינוּ". מַאי אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ — "שִׁמְעוּ דְבַר ה׳ קְצִינֵי סְדוֹם וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** with regard to the statement concerning the mourners: **Let him say: Many have drunk; let him not say: Many will drink. Let him say: The drink of the first; let him not say: The drink of the last.** This is **as Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said, and likewise it was taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei: A person should never open his mouth to Satan** and speculate about potential disasters. **Rav Yosef said: What is the verse** from which it is derived? **"We should have almost been as Sodom, we should have been like unto Gomorrah"** (Isaiah 1:9), after which, **what did** the prophet **reply to them? "Hear the word of the Lord, rulers of Sodom;** give ear unto the law of our God, people of Gomorrah" (Isaiah 1:10). Isaiah drew the analogy and immediately it was realized.

Abaye comments that one should not say *rabim yishtu* — "many will drink" — referring to future deaths, so as not to invoke future suffering. And one should not say *kemishte acharonim* — "like the drinking of those who come after" — because, as Resh Lakish himself taught, and so too it is taught in the name of Rabbi Yosi: *le'olam al yiftach adam piv l'Satan* — one should never open one's mouth to the Satan by invoking the possibility of future misfortune. Rav Yosef adds a proof-text: when Israel said *k'Sdom hayinu l'Amora daminu* — "we have become like Sodom, we resemble Amora" — the very next verse addresses them as rulers of Sodom, fulfilling the feared comparison.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם אֵימָא מִלְּתָא כְּנֶגֶד מְנַחֲמֵי אֲבֵלִים. פָּתַח וְאָמַר: "אַחֵינוּ גּוֹמְלֵי חֲסָדִים בְּנֵי גּוֹמְלֵי חֲסָדִים, הַמַּחְזִיקִים בִּבְרִיתוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ (שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ׳כִּי יְדַעְתִּיו לְמַעַן אֲשֶׁר יְצַוֶּה אֶת בָּנָיו וְגוֹ׳׳). אַחֵינוּ, בַּעַל הַגְּמוּל יְשַׁלֵּם לָכֶם גְּמוּלְכֶם. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, מְשַׁלֵּם הַגְּמוּל".
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** the disseminator: **Stand and say a statement with regard to those who comfort the mourners. He began and said: Our brothers, bestowers of loving-kindness, sons of bestowers of loving-kindness, who embrace the covenant of Abraham our Patriarch, as it is stated: "For I know him, that he will command his children…**to do righteousness and justice" (Genesis 18:19). **Our brothers, may the Master of reward pay you your just deserts. Blessed are You,** Lord, **Who pays the just deserts.**

Resh Lakish then directed Yehuda bar Nachmani: *kum eima milta keneged menachmei avelim* — now say a blessing for the *menachamim*, for those who have come to comfort the mourners. This blessing celebrates the comforters as *gomlei chasadim bnei gomlei chasadim*, practitioners of *chesed* in the tradition of Abraham our father, and concludes: *baruch ata Hashem meshalem hagemul* — blessed is God who repays acts of kindness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם אֵימָא מִלְּתָא כְּנֶגֶד כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל. פָּתַח וְאָמַר: "רִבּוֹן הָעוֹלָמִים, פְּדֵה וְהַצֵּל, מַלֵּט, הוֹשַׁע עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִן הַדֶּבֶר וּמִן הַחֶרֶב וּמִן הַבִּיזָּה וּמִן הַשִּׁדָּפוֹן וּמִן הַיֵּרָקוֹן וּמִכׇּל מִינֵי פּוּרְעָנִיּוֹת הַמִּתְרַגְּשׁוֹת וּבָאוֹת לָעוֹלָם, טֶרֶם נִקְרָא וְאַתָּה תַּעֲנֶה. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, עוֹצֵר הַמַּגֵּפָה".
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** the disseminator: **Stand and say a statement with regard to the entire Jewish people. He began and said: Master of the worlds, redeem and save, rescue and deliver Your people, Israel, from the pestilence and from the sword, and from spoil, and from the blight, and from the mildew, and from all types of afflictions that suddenly erupt and come to the world. Before we call and You will respond. Blessed are You,** Lord, **Who halts the plague.** Apparently, several blessings are recited on the days following the burial.

Finally: *kum eima milta keneged kol Yisrael* — say a blessing on behalf of all of Israel. The text is a plea for divine protection:

> *Ribbono shel olam — pedeh ve'hatzel, malet ve'hosha amcha Yisrael min hadever u'min hacherev u'min habizah, u'min hashidafon u'min hayarkon, u'mikol minei puranyot hamitragshot u'va'ot la'olam. Terem nikra ve'atah ta'aneh — baruch ata otzer hamagefah.*

What emerges from this extended passage is that *birchat aveilim* had a defined structure — one blessing for the deceased, one for God (*mechaye hametim*), one for the mourners (*menachem avelim*), one for the *menachamim*, and one for the community at large — while the precise wording within each blessing appears to have been more flexible. The *meturgeman* was given latitude to compose the content, provided the themes and conclusions (*siumim*) were honored. This stands in some contrast to the fixed text of *birchat chatanim* presented on the preceding *daf*. Whether these blessings were formally tied to *birchat hamazon* or could stand independently is not entirely resolved — the Gemara did not foreground a *minyan* requirement in this passage, though that may simply be because it had already been established.

### Ten Cups Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר עוּלָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹסוֹת תִּקְּנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּבֵית הָאֵבֶל: שְׁלֹשָׁה קוֹדֶם אֲכִילָה, כְּדֵי לִפְתּוֹחַ אֶת בְּנֵי מֵעָיו. שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּתוֹךְ אֲכִילָה, כְּדֵי לִשְׁרוֹת אֲכִילָה שֶׁבְּמֵעָיו. וְאַרְבָּעָה לְאַחַר אֲכִילָה, אֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד "הַזָּן", וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד בִּרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד "בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלָיִם", וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד "הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב".
>
> **Translation:** § In connection with comforting mourners, **Ulla said, and some say that it was taught in a *baraita*: The Sages instituted ten cups** of wine to be drunk **in the house of the mourner: Three** cups **prior to** the **meal, in order to open his intestines,** i.e., whet his appetite; **three during** the **meal, to soak the food in his intestines** in order to facilitate digestion; **and four** cups **after** the **meal,** each corresponding to a blessing in the Grace after Meals. **One corresponds to** the first blessing: **Who feeds** all; **one corresponds to the** second blessing, the **blessing of the land; one corresponding to the third blessing: Who builds Jerusalem; and one corresponding to** the fourth blessing: **Who is good and does good.**

Ulla taught — and some say this was a *Baraita* — that the Sages instituted ten cups of wine to be drunk in the house of mourning: three before the meal, to open the appetite; three during the meal, to settle the food; and four after the meal, corresponding to the four blessings of *birchat hamazon* — *hazan*, *birkat ha'aretz*, *boneh Yerushalayim*, and *hatov vehametiv*. The logic of drinking wine in a house of mourning presumably reflects its role in dulling grief, echoing the imagery of *mishte* — the drinking party — that runs through the passage, and consistent with the halachic distinction between an *onen*, who does not drink wine before burial, and an *avel*, for whom wine was permitted as a way of bearing grief.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶם אַרְבָּעָה: אֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד חַזָּנֵי הָעִיר, וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד פַּרְנָסֵי הָעִיר, וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאֶחָד כְּנֶגֶד רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. הִתְחִילוּ (הָיוּ) שׁוֹתִין וּמִשְׁתַּכְּרִין, הֶחְזִירוּ הַדָּבָר לְיוֹשְׁנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Later, the Sages **added to those four** additional blessings: **One, noting** the actions of **the attendants of the city [*ḥazzanei ha'ir*],** who tend to burials and other communal needs; **one, noting** the actions of **the leaders of the city,** who would provide funding for the burial of the poor; **one, noting the Temple,** commemorating its destruction; **and one, noting** the actions of **Rabban Gamliel.** The people **began** observing this ordinance instituted by the Sages, **and they would drink and become intoxicated.** Therefore, the Sages **restored the matter to its previous** status and established that they drink no more than ten cups.

The Sages later added four more cups: one in honor of the *chazanei ha'ir*, the communal functionaries; one for the *parnasei ha'ir*, the community leaders who supported the mourners; one *keneged beit hamikdash*; and one in memory of Rabban Gamliel. The first two reflect the communal support structure around the mourning family. The one *keneged beit hamikdash*, Rashi explains, is not merely a messianic gesture but an acknowledgment of communal *avelut* over the *churban* — we are all *aveilim* on the destruction of the Temple, and God should also console us and rebuild it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? דְּתַנְיָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיְתָה הוֹצָאַת הַמֵּת קָשָׁה לִקְרוֹבָיו יוֹתֵר מִמִּיתָתוֹ, עַד שֶׁהָיוּ מַנִּיחִים אוֹתוֹ וּבוֹרְחִין. עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְנָהַג קַלּוּת בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְהוֹצִיאוּהוּ בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן. וְנָהֲגוּ כׇּל הָעָם אַחֲרָיו לְהוֹצִיא בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן. אֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְהָאִידָּנָא נְהוּג עָלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּצַרְדָּא בַּר זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the connection between **Rabban Gamliel** and a house of mourning? It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Initially, the funeral** expenditures **for the deceased were more taxing for his relatives than his death,** as the burials were opulent, **until** it reached a point where people **would abandon the deceased and flee.** This continued **until Rabbi Gamliel came and conducted himself in a self-deprecatory** manner, instructing the people that they were **to take him** for burial **in** plain **linen garments. And all the people conducted themselves following his** example, and instructed their families **to take** them for burial **in** plain **linen garments. Rav Pappa said: And today, everyone is accustomed** to bury the dead in plain garments, **even in rough cloth [*tzerada*] worth one *zuz*.**

The cup *keneged* Rabban Gamliel is explained by a *Baraita*: in earlier times, the cost of burial was so crushing to a family that relatives would abandon their dead rather than bear the expense. *Barishona hayu hotza'at hamet kashah likrovav yoter mimitato* — the funeral was harder for the family than the death itself. Rabban Gamliel — the *Nasi*, a man of great wealth — commanded that when he died he be buried in simple linen shrouds, and all the people followed his lead. In memory of this act of compassion, a cup was drunk in his honor. Rav Papa adds that in his own day the practice had become even simpler, with the most minimal burial garments costing almost nothing. It is worth emphasizing the deliberate message of Rabban Gamliel's choice: precisely because he was the most prominent figure of his generation, his insistence on a simple funeral carried maximum force as a statement of principle.

However, once the count reached fourteen cups, people were drinking to excess and becoming drunk. The Sages therefore reversed course — *hechziru davar l'yoshno* — and reverted to the original ten.

It is striking that both the *birchat aveilim* and the *asara cosot* have largely disappeared from contemporary practice. The *daf* as a whole has a clear thematic unity: just as 8a presented the *birchat chatanim* for the wedding setting, 8b presents the *birchat aveilim* for mourning — a beautiful structural pairing of the liturgies that accompany life's two great threshold moments.

## Non-Virginity Claim

### Pesach Patuach Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הָאוֹמֵר "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי" — נֶאֱמָן לְאוֹסְרָהּ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: A groom who says: I encountered an unobstructed orifice,** claiming that when he consummated the marriage he discovered that his bride was not a virgin, **is credible to render her forbidden to himself.** Although it is not always possible to corroborate his claim with testimony that his wife committed adultery after betrothal, he is credible to render her forbidden to him as though she had in fact committed adultery.

The Gemara now turns to the central legal concern of the *daf*: the husband who, the morning after the wedding, claims his wife was not a virgin. There are two distinct ways he might make this claim. The first is the absence of blood — a relatively concrete form of evidence, especially given that the practices described later in the *perek* involved guards at the door and a special sheet to be inspected. The second is what the Gemara calls *tainas pesach patuach* — the claim that during the act of intercourse itself he experienced no obstruction, no hymen, and thereby concluded experientially that she was not a virgin. This second claim carries an additional layer of uncertainty: not only is it without external evidence, but one might argue that even in his own mind he does not actually know what he is talking about — his subjective experience may simply be an error.

Rabbi Elazar states: *ha'omer pesach patuach matzati ne'eman l'osrah alav* — one who says "I found the door already open" is believed to the extent that she becomes forbidden to him. The principle at work is *shavya anafshei chaticha d'issura* — a person who declares something forbidden binds himself by that declaration, as when one declares a piece of meat to be *chazir*: even without objective confirmation, he is bound by his own characterization of reality. The effect here is that, given his claim that she is not a virgin and therefore may have committed adultery, a married woman who commits adultery being forbidden to her husband, he cannot continue living with her. The ruling contains two *chiddushim*: first, that *petach patuach* is a recognized claim and not dismissed as the speculation of someone who simply does not know what he is talking about; and second, that *shavya anafshei chaticha d'issura* can operate even where the forbidden status results not from a direct assertion ("this is forbidden") but from a factual claim whose halachic consequence is a *safek* of forbidden status.

### Safek Sefeika Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? סְפֵק סְפֵיקָא הוּא: סָפֵק תַּחְתָּיו, סָפֵק אֵין תַּחְתָּיו. וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר תַּחְתָּיו: סָפֵק בְּאוֹנֶס, סָפֵק בְּרָצוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** is she forbidden to him? It **is** a case of **compound uncertainty.** It is **uncertain** whether she engaged in intercourse while **under his** jurisdiction, after betrothal, in which case she would be forbidden to him, and it is **uncertain** whether she engaged in intercourse while **not under his** jurisdiction, in which case she would not be forbidden to him. **And if you say** that she engaged in intercourse while **under his** jurisdiction, it is **uncertain** whether she engaged in intercourse **by coercion,** in which case she would not be forbidden to him, and it is **uncertain** whether she engaged in intercourse **willingly,** in which case she would be forbidden to him. In cases of compound uncertainty, the ruling is lenient. Why, then, is his claim deemed credible?

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: *ve'eima sfeik sfeika hu* — even granting his claim at face value, this is a case of double doubt, and a *sfek sefeika* should be permissive. The analysis runs as follows. The first *safek*: perhaps she had sexual relations *tachtav* — after the *kiddushin*, which would constitute adultery — or perhaps it was before the *kiddushin*, in which case she was unchaste but did not commit adultery against him and would not be forbidden. The second *safek*: even if we assume it occurred after *kiddushin*, perhaps it was *b'ones* — rape — in which case she did nothing wrong and is not forbidden to her husband — or perhaps it was willful. For both conditions to render her forbidden, the encounter must have been after *kiddushin* and consensual. A *sfek sefeika* in the direction of leniency should therefore permit her to remain with her husband.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּאֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּאֵשֶׁת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּכְגוֹן דְּקַבֵּיל בַּהּ אֲבוּהּ קִידּוּשִׁין פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **No, it is necessary** to teach this ruling only **in** the case of **the wife of a priest,** who is rendered forbidden to her husband even if she engaged in intercourse by coercion. In that case, there is a single uncertainty. **And if you wish, say** instead that this ruling is relevant **even to the wife of an Israelite, and** it is in a case **where her father accepted** her **betrothal** when she was **less than three years and one day old.** Intercourse with a girl less than three years old does not permanently rupture the hymen, and therefore there is no uncertainty whether she engaged in intercourse before or after betrothal. Clearly, it took place after betrothal, and there is only one uncertainty: Did she engage in intercourse by coercion or willingly?

The Gemara answers: *lo tzricha* — the ruling is needed specifically in cases that reduce the double doubt to a single doubt. One such case is *eshet kohen*: the wife of a *kohen* is forbidden even if the violation was through rape, so the second *safek* disappears. The other case is where the father betrothed her before she was three years old: since the Gemara assumes that penetration before age three leaves no lasting physical effect (*biah* under three causes the hymen to regenerate), the absence of a hymen can only reflect an encounter after she turned three — at which point she was already betrothed — thus eliminating the first *safek* regarding timing.

Tosafot raises an additional question: what about a case where the act occurred willingly but she was under twelve? Such an act would be treated as *ones* under the principle of *pituy ketanah ones* — the seduction of a minor is legally equivalent to rape — and would not render her forbidden. Is this not yet another branch of *sfek sefeika*? Tosafot resolves this by observing that this scenario collapses into the same category as *ones*, since statutory incapacity and physical coercion are both subsumed under *ones*. For a *sfek sefeika* to function, the two doubts must be genuinely distinct questions; where they merge into the same legal category, they do not multiply into a *sfek sefeika*. The upshot is: in the ordinary case, the double doubt insulates the wife from being rendered forbidden. Only where the *sfek sefeika* is dismantled — through *eshet kohen* or *pachot mibat shalosh* — does the husband's claim take effect.

### Sotah Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה נֶאֱסֶרֶת עַל בַּעְלָהּ אֶלָּא עַל עִסְקֵי קִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה, וּכְמַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And did Rabbi Elazar say that? But didn't Rabbi Elazar say: A woman is forbidden to her husband** due to adultery, **only over matters of jealous warning and seclusion, and as** it was in **the incident that transpired** involving David and Bathsheba? A wife is forbidden to her husband only in a case where he warns her not to seclude herself with a certain man and witnesses testify that she subsequently entered into seclusion with him.

The Gemara now raises a sharp internal contradiction. Rabbi Elazar himself elsewhere stated: *ein isha ne'eset al ba'ala ela al iskei kinu v'stira* — a woman is not rendered forbidden to her husband except through the process of *kinui v'stira*, the formal warning-and-seclusion procedure that initiates the *sotah* process. How can Rabbi Elazar simultaneously hold that a husband's subjective claim of *pesach patuach* is sufficient to forbid the wife? The one statement seems to demand the full *sotah* mechanism; the other allows a bare assertion to suffice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה, בְּקִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה הֲוָה? וְעוֹד, מִי אַסְרוּהָ?
>
> **Translation:** **And** how can **you understand** it in that manner? **Was the incident that transpired with jealous warning and seclusion? Furthermore, did** the Sages **render** Bathsheba **forbidden** to her husband? Had she been forbidden to her husband, she would have also been forbidden to David, based on the following principle: Just as an adulteress is forbidden to her husband, she is also forbidden to her paramour.

The Gemara first considers whether the *ma'aseh shehaya* — the story invoked to support the *sotah* principle — refers to David and Batsheva, and immediately questions this: was there any *kinui v'stira* in that story? And furthermore, why was Batsheva not rendered forbidden to David if adultery had clearly occurred? These questions stand as difficulties that the Gemara must address.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה נֶאֱסֶרֶת עַל בַּעְלָהּ אֶלָּא עַל עִסְקֵי קִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה, מִמַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה, דְּלָא הֲוָה קִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה וְלָא אִיתַּסְרָא. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא: קִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה — אִין, "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ" — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **That** is **not difficult,** as **this is** what Rabbi Elazar **is saying:** The fact that **a woman is forbidden to her husband** due to adultery **only over matters of jealous warning and seclusion** is derived **from the incident that transpired** involving David and Bathsheba, **as there was no jealous warning and seclusion,** and therefore **she was not forbidden** to her husband. **In any case,** it is **difficult,** as the statements of Rabbi Elazar are contradictory. It may be inferred: By means of **jealous warning and seclusion, yes,** a man renders his wife forbidden to him; by means of the claim that he encountered **an unobstructed orifice, no,** he does not render her forbidden.

The Gemara begins by untangling the statement itself. The correct reading is: *ein isha ne'eset al ba'ala ela al iskei kinu v'stira mi'ma'aseh shehaya* — we learn that the only mechanism is *kinui v'stira* from the case of David and Batsheva precisely because there was no *kinui v'stira* there, and therefore she was not rendered forbidden. The argument is: from the fact that she was not forbidden despite the adultery having demonstrably occurred, we infer that without the *sotah* process, no prohibition attaches.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ: קִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה — אִין, עֵדִים — לָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that inference: **And** according **to your reasoning,** that the statement of Rabbi Elazar restricts to jealous warning and seclusion the manner in which a husband can render his wife forbidden, infer: By means of **jealous warning and seclusion, yes,** a man renders his wife forbidden to him; by means of the testimony of two **witnesses** that she engaged in adulterous relations, **no,** he does not render her forbidden. That cannot be so, as clearly two witnesses establish her as one who committed adultery and render her forbidden to her husband.

The Gemara presses: *uto v'tisbara* — does this reasoning make sense? She clearly committed adultery; why should the absence of a formal procedure matter when the act is known? And more pointedly, a straightforward objection: *kinui v'stira in edim lo* — according to you, does the prohibition apply only through *kinui v'stira* and never through witnesses? Of course if there are two witnesses it applies. So the statement cannot be excluding all other mechanisms. Rather, it addresses cases below the threshold of two witnesses: absent *edim*, the only other operative mechanism is the *sotah* process. The question then remains: does a husband's lone assertion constitute a mechanism below or at the level of two witnesses?

### Self-Testimony Weights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה נֶאֱסֶרֶת עַל בַּעְלָהּ בְּעֵד אֶחָד, אֶלָּא בִּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים. וְקִינּוּי וּסְתִירָה — אֲפִילּוּ בְּעֵד אֶחָד נָמֵי. וּפֶתַח פָּתוּחַ — כִּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather, this is** what Rabbi Elazar **is saying: A woman is not rendered forbidden to her husband through** the testimony of **one witness. Rather,** she is rendered forbidden **only by means of** the testimony of **two witnesses** who testify that she engaged in adulterous relations. **And** if there was **jealous warning and seclusion,** she is rendered forbidden **even by means of** the testimony of **one witness as well,** if he comes after the husband warned his wife and testifies that she engaged in adulterous relations. **And** the legal status of the claim: I encountered **an unobstructed orifice,** is **like** that of **two witnesses,** and it renders her forbidden even without jealous warning and seclusion.

The Gemara's resolution is as follows: *petach patuach k'shnei edim dami* — a claim of *pesach patuach* carries the evidentiary weight of two witnesses. Why? Because *ne'eman adam al atzmo k'mea edim* — a person is believed against himself like a hundred witnesses. When a person makes a self-incriminating claim, it carries maximal credibility precisely because no one would voluntarily accept a disadvantageous ruling without good reason.

The Gemara is aware that the question is sharper than the answer. The original statement was that a woman is forbidden only through *kinui v'stira* — meaning that anything less than two witnesses is insufficient, and only the *sotah* process fills the gap. Now we are told that the husband's own claim counts as two witnesses. But this seems to undercut the entire force of the restriction: if I say my wife committed adultery and that statement alone counts as two witnesses, then the restriction adds little. The Gemara accepts this tension because both rulings are attributed to Rabbi Elazar and must be reconciled; the resolution is that the husband's self-testimony operates as a special mechanism, analogous to — but not the same as — objective testimony from outsiders.

It should be noted that *ne'eman adam al atzmo* operates in the monetary sphere in a straightforward way — if one says "I owe a thousand *zuz*" one is bound. But in matters of prohibition and, critically, in criminal matters, no person can testify against himself to generate an objective criminal sanction. What the Gemara describes here is not criminal self-incrimination but a form of personal restriction: based on your own stated understanding of reality, you are bound to conduct yourself accordingly, even though it will have indirect consequences for the wife.

### David Batsheva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה, מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא אֲסָרוּהָ? הָתָם אוֹנֶס הֲוָה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן:
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** with regard to **the incident that transpired** involving David and Bathsheba: **For what** reason did the Sages **not deem her forbidden,** when clearly David committed adultery with a married woman? The Gemara answers: **There it was rape,** and she did not engage in intercourse willingly. **And if you wish, say** instead that the Sages did not deem her forbidden, **as that which Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said:**

The Gemara returns to the question of David and Batsheva directly. Tosafot frames the difficulty sharply: if the principle of *shavya anafshei chaticha d'issura* means that when a husband claims his wife committed adultery he is bound by that claim and cannot continue living with her, then why did David continue living with Batsheva? He knew he had committed adultery with her. Why was he not compelled to separate?

The Gemara offers two answers. The first: *hasam ones havei* — in that case it was *ones*. David did not use physical violence, but what genuine choice did Batsheva have when the king summoned her? The Gemara is working with a broader conception of *ones* that encompasses situations of severe social coercion. The halachic consequence is significant and, as the Gemara notes, somewhat ironic: the prohibition on a woman who commits adultery applies only when the act was willful. If she was raped, she committed no wrong and is not forbidden to her husband — nor to the man who violated her. Since Batsheva was compelled, she did not commit willful adultery, and therefore no prohibition attached to David's living with her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַיּוֹצֵא לְמִלְחֶמֶת בֵּית דָּוִד, גֵּט כְּרִיתוּת כּוֹתֵב לְאִשְׁתּוֹ. דִּכְתִיב: "וְאֶת אַחֶיךָ תִּפְקֹד לְשָׁלוֹם וְאֶת עֲרֻבָּתָם תִּקָּח", מַאי "וְאֶת עֲרֻבָּתָם תִּקָּח"? תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: דְּבָרִים הַמְעוֹרָבִין בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Anyone who goes to a war** waged by the royal **house of David writes a** conditional **bill of divorce to his wife.** This was done to prevent a situation in which the wife of the soldier would be unable to remarry because her husband did not return from battle and there were no witnesses with regard to his fate. The conditional bill of divorce accorded the wife the status of a divorcée and freed her to remarry, **as it is written: "And to your brothers bring greetings and take their pledge [*arubatam*]"** (I Samuel 17:18). **What is** the meaning of: **And take *arubatam*? Rav Yosef taught:** It is referring to **matters that are shared [*hame'oravin*] between** the husband **and** his wife, i.e., marriage. Since apparently it was customary for men at war to send their wives a conditional divorce, and since Uriah later died, Batsheba assumed divorced status retroactively from the time that he set out to war. Therefore, she was not forbidden to David.

The second answer the Gemara offers is attributed to Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hayotzei l'milchemes beis David get krisus kosev l'ishto* — anyone who went out to fight in the wars of the house of David first wrote a *get* for his wife. The practice of writing a conditional *get* before going to war is explained in two ways: Rashi says it was to spare the wife from the *yibbum* obligation if the husband died childless; Tosafot objects that *kol hayotzei* implies universal practice, not just men with brothers, and argues instead that the concern was *agunah* — a woman whose husband's fate is unknown. The *get* was written on the condition that it would take effect if the husband did not return within a specified period after the war's end.

Uriah had therefore given Batsheva such a conditional *get* before going to war. When David ensured that Uriah did not return — as the narrative of Tanach makes plain — the *tenai* was fulfilled retroactively. The *get* took effect retroactively, Batsheva was divorced from Uriah, and technically the act between David and Batsheva did not constitute adultery, since she was no longer Uriah's wife in halachic terms from the moment the condition was fulfilled. The proof-text: *v'es arubasam tikach* — the word *aruvah* (bond/pledge) is interpreted by Rav Yosef as a reference to the instruments of the husband-wife bond, i.e., the *getin*, that David was to collect from the soldiers before they marched.

This resolution is cited alongside the *ones* resolution as an alternative (*v'iba'is eima*), and both stand in the Gemara.

## Wed Wedding Court

### Wednesday Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת לַיּוֹם הָרְבִיעִי. לְיוֹם רְבִיעִי — אִין, לְיוֹם חֲמִישִׁי — לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם אִיקָּרוֹרֵי דַעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the credibility of the claim: I encountered an unobstructed orifice, **Abaye said: We, too, learn** in the mishna proof for the opinion of Rabbi Elazar: **A virgin is married on Wednesday.** Abaye infers: **On Wednesday, yes,** a virgin is married; **on Thursday, no,** she is not married. **What is the reason** for this ruling? It is **due to** the fact **that** if the marriage were to be held on Thursday, several days would elapse before the court would next convene, and in the interim **his resolve will cool** and his anger subside. The concern is that consequently he will fail to claim before the court that his bride was not a virgin.

The Gemara returns to the *takana* of the fourth-*amud* mishnah: *b'sula niseis l'yom harevi'i* — a virgin marries on the fourth day of the week, i.e., Wednesday. The Gemara has been assuming throughout that this is not mere custom but an enacted *takana* in which there is a communal interest. The specific mechanism identified is *icrurei da'ata* — the concern that if one waits too long, the husband will cool off and not pursue the matter.

### Takana Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי? אִי לְמִיתַּב לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה, נִיתֵּיב לַהּ! אֶלָּא לְאוֹסְרָהּ עָלָיו, וּדְקָא טָעֵין טַעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And for what** matter is that a source of concern? **If** the concern is with regard **to giving her** payment for her **marriage contract,** i.e., if he fails to go to court, her legal status at the time of marriage will remain that of a virgin, and when the time comes she will receive payment for her marriage contract to which she is not entitled; then **let him give** it **to her** if he wishes. Why is that a concern? **Rather, it is** with regard **to rendering her forbidden to him, and** that would result in a case **where he makes a claim.**

One might ask: why should the community care whether the husband cools off? The Gemara poses this directly: *ul'mai* — for what purpose do we want him to bring his claim to court the next morning? If the purpose is merely to allow him to deprive her of her *ketuva* — to protect his own financial interests — then this is purely his concern. If he cools off and decides not to pursue it, that is his prerogative. There is no communal interest in helping a husband reduce his wife's *ketuva*.

The answer must therefore be: *ela l'osra alav* — the concern is that she may have committed adultery and therefore be forbidden to him. In that case, the community has a genuine interest in ensuring the matter is adjudicated promptly, because we do not want a husband and wife living together when they may be halachically forbidden to each other. This is the only rationale that justifies a *takana* to ensure the husband can appear in court the following morning.

### Blood vs Door

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, דְּקָטָעֵין טַעֲנַת "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ"! לָא, דְּקָטָעֵין טַעֲנַת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** referring to a case **where he makes the claim:** I encountered **an unobstructed orifice,** after engaging in intercourse with his bride, and his claim is accorded credibility to render her forbidden to him in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar? The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** it can be explained that it is a case **where he makes the claim** that there was no **blood,** which would have resulted from rupture of the hymen had she been a virgin. That is a claim based on objective, verifiable evidence and not merely dependent on his subjective sensation.

With that purpose established, a closer look reveals which type of claim the *perek* is primarily addressing. The Gemara asks: *v'dekhatani ta'anah mai* — what claim is the husband making when he comes to court the next morning? Is it the claim of *pesach patuach* — based on his subjective experience during intercourse — or the claim of *tainas damim* — the absence of blood on the sheet?

The Gemara concludes: *lo dekhatani tainas damim* — the *perek* is dealing with the claim of absence of blood. This is the more concrete and objectively assessable claim: there were witnesses positioned at the door, there was a special sheet, and its inspection either confirmed or did not confirm blood. The *perek*'s central scenario is therefore this evidentiary framework. The *pesach patuach* claim — that of subjective experiential inference — is Rabbi Elazar's ruling and stands as a significant *chiddush*, but it is not necessarily the baseline case that the whole structure of Wednesday weddings and next-morning court appearances was designed to address.

### Judah Galilee

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָאוֹמֵר "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי", נֶאֱמָן לְהַפְסִידָהּ כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: הָאוֹכֵל אֵצֶל חָמִיו בִּיהוּדָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּעֵדִים — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִטְעוֹן טַעֲנַת בְּתוּלִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּתְיַיחֵד עִמָּהּ. בִּיהוּדָה הוּא דְּלָא מָצֵי טָעֵין, הָא בַּגָּלִיל מָצֵי טָעֵין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said** that a groom **who says: I encountered an unobstructed orifice, is deemed credible** with regard **to causing her to lose her marriage contract. Rav Yosef said: What is he teaching us? We** already **learned** explicitly in a mishna (12a): A man **who eats at** the house of **his father-in-law in Judea** after betrothal, **without witnesses** to attest to the fact that he was not alone with her, **cannot make a claim about** his bride's **virginity** after marriage, **because** in accordance with the custom in Judea, the assumption is **that he secluded himself with her** and it was he who engaged in intercourse with her. The Gemara infers: It is **in Judea that he cannot claim** that she is not a virgin, **but in the Galilee, he can claim** that this is the case.

The Gemara introduces a regional distinction. A *baraita* teaches that one who ate in the home of his father-in-law in Judah without witnesses — alluding to the known practice in Judah where a betrothed couple might be permitted to be alone together during the *erusin* period — cannot thereafter claim that his wife was not a virgin, because the obvious retort is that any non-virginity may be attributable to premarital relations with him. By contrast, in Galilee, where such seclusion was not permitted, the husband's claim retains validity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי, אִי לְאוֹסְרָהּ עָלָיו, בִּיהוּדָה אַמַּאי לָא? אֶלָּא לָאו, לְהַפְסִידָהּ כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, וּדְקָא טָעֵין טַעֲנָה. מַאי לָאו, דְּקָא טָעֵין טַעֲנַת "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ"! לָא, דְּקָא טָעֵין טַעֲנַת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And for what** matter is this claim directed? **If it is to render her forbidden to him,** then **in Judea why** is the claim **not** credible? If he is certain that he did not engage in intercourse with her, and finds that she is not a virgin, apparently she committed adultery and that claim should render her forbidden. **Rather, is it not** that he is seeking **to cause her to lose her marriage contract** in a case **where he makes a claim?** And **what, is it not** referring to a case **where he makes the claim:** I encountered **an unobstructed orifice,** and apparently he is accorded credibility? The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** it can be explained that it is a case **where he makes the claim** that there was no **blood.**

The Gemara then sharpens the question: in Judah, the husband cannot make the claim — for what purpose? If it is merely to make her forbidden to him based on his own say-so (*l'osrah alav*), then even in Judah this should apply, because the principle of *shavya anafshei chaticha d'issura* operates on his own consciousness regardless of external suspicion. The fact that in Judah his claim is undermined only makes sense if what he is trying to do is to hurt her — specifically, to deprive her of her *ketuva*. And that is the claim the regional distinction addresses: in Judah, where prior seclusion was possible, his claim that she was not a virgin is not accepted for the purpose of causing her monetary loss. In Galilee, it is.

### Rashi Credibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָאוֹמֵר "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי", נֶאֱמָן לְהַפְסִידָהּ כְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said** that a groom **who says: I encountered an unobstructed orifice, is deemed credible** with regard **to causing her to lose her marriage contract.**

This brings the Gemara to a foundational question that will animate the entire remainder of the *perek*: on what basis is the husband believed at all, given that he has no witnesses and no objective supporting evidence, to cause his wife to lose her *ketuva*?

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states: *ha'omer pesach patuach matzati ne'eman l'hafsidah kesubasah* — a man who says "I found the door already open" is believed to cause her to forfeit her *ketuva*. Rashi provides the foundational rationale, and it is significant: *de'amrinan ee lav dekushta hu, lo hayah mafsid se'udaso* — we reason that if his claim were false, he would not have wasted all the money he spent on the wedding feast. He threw a *se'udat nisuin*, invited guests, incurred great expense — and all that to make one night of marriage and then claim she was not a virgin? Moreover, if he hated her from the start, he would simply have divorced her without going to all this trouble.

Rashi's reasoning is essentially one of implausibility: the claim made the morning after the wedding carries a built-in credibility that a claim made years later would never enjoy. A husband who has just spent a fortune on a wedding celebration and who comes to court the next morning with this complaint is presumed to be telling the truth, because no one would construct such an elaborate scenario merely to avoid paying a full *ketuva*. The moment he makes this claim, moreover, he faces real consequences — not just a reduced *ketuva* obligation, but potential forced divorce. A man who genuinely wanted out of the marriage without this claim could have found simpler means. Thus the timing and context lend the claim a degree of credibility that the law recognizes.

This ruling, and Rashi's explanation of it, will be central to the rest of the *perek*, which unfolds the full picture of what happens in court the morning after a Wednesday wedding: what claim the husband makes, how it is evaluated, and what its halachic consequences are both for him and for his wife.
