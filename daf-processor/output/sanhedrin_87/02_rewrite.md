# Sanhedrin 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zaken Mamre Overview

### Opening Context

The daf before us, 87a, opens a sustained treatment of *zaken mamre* — the rebellious elder — which is among the most conceptually rich topics in all of Shas. It is here that the *zaken mamre* receives his primary discussion, and it is here that he is classified among those who receive *chenek*.

### Mishna & Pasuk

The *mishna* frames the rebellion of the *zaken mamre* as directed *al pi beis din* — against the authority of a properly constituted court. As support, the *mishna* cites the verse *ki yipale mimcha davar lamishpat*, "if a matter is hidden from you regarding judgment." This is somewhat puzzling. Later in the same passage, the Torah states explicitly *ha'ish asher ya'aseh b'zadon l'vilti shemo'a el hashofet* — the man who acts presumptuously, refusing to listen to the judge — making the context of rebellion against *beis din* entirely clear. It is worth noting that by *Ben Sorer u-Moreh*, the Torah specifies *al pi aviv v'imo* to indicate parental authority, because that specification is needed. Here the context seems self-evident. Yet the *mishna* still finds it necessary to anchor the law in a *pasuk*, which invites further reflection.

### Three Courts

The *mishna* then introduces a structural point of cardinal importance: *shlosha batei dinim hayu sham* — there were three *batei dinim* on *Har HaBayit* itself. This is distinct from the broader hierarchy of local and regional courts, a topic the *Yerushalmi* addresses at the outset of the *masechet*. Here we focus specifically on the courts within the Temple Mount. One *beis din* sat *al petach Har HaBayit*, at the entrance to *Har HaBayit*; one sat *al petach ha-Azara*, at the entrance to the *Azara*; and the third sat in *Lishkat HaGazit*, the Chamber of Hewn Stone — the seat of the *Sanhedrin* — which was built half within the *Azara* and half outside it.

### Rashi's Geography

*Rashi* clarifies the geography with precision. The first *beis din*, at *petach Har HaBayit*, is identified as *sha'ar hamizrachi she-p'nim min ha-chel lifnei Ezrat Nashim* — the eastern gate, situated inside the *chel* and before *Ezrat Nashim*. The second court was positioned further in, somewhere before *Ezrat Yisrael*. The third — *Lishkat HaGazit* — was, as *Rashi* states, *b'nuya b'toch Azara chetzyo b'kodesh v'chetzyo b'chol*: built within the *Azara* but spanning into the non-sacred area. The reason for this arrangement becomes clear from *Masechet Yoma*: it is forbidden to sit within the *Azara* itself, so the court was constructed straddling the boundary, permitting the judges to be seated in the permitted portion while still maintaining their connection to the sacred space of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Appeal Process

The *mishna* describes the dispute process with a particular stylization worth examining. When there was a legal disagreement in a local court, the dissenting voice — the individual who refuses to accept the ruling — presents the case upward. He says: *kach darashti ve-kach darshu chaverai, kach limadeti ve-kach limdu chaverai* — here is how I interpret it, and here is how my colleagues interpret it; here is what I have learned, and here is what they have learned. Notably, it is this dissenting figure who presents the case in full; the opposing majority does not appear to speak for itself.

When the *beis din* receives the appeal, the *mishna* says: *im sham'u, omrim lahem* — if they have heard, if they possess a tradition, they tell them. *Ve-im lav* — if they do not — the parties proceed to the next level. The language distinguishes pointedly between *kach darashti* and *kach limadeti*: the former reflects independent interpretive reasoning from *pesukim*, while the latter reflects received tradition.

### Tradition vs. Intellect

This distinction opens a profound structural question. Suppose the receiving court lacks an explicit tradition on the matter but is nonetheless unanimous — all its members agree, by reason and analysis, that the dissenter is wrong. May the dissenter be turned away on those grounds alone? Reading the *mishna* carefully, it appears not. The only thing that definitively ends the dissenter's ability to demand a higher ruling is an explicit tradition against his position. If the court's opposition rests purely on their own intellectual analysis — however unanimous — the dissenter may reply: "That is just your interpretation. Perhaps I am right and you are wrong." And he continues upward.

There is something both beautiful and demanding about this. Until the matter reaches the final arbiter, any individual may maintain, however outnumbered, that the majority is mistaken. This is not obstinacy that the law condemns — it is intellectual integrity that the law respects, so long as it does not cross into active defiance of the binding ruling.

### Final Authority

Once the case reaches *Beis Din HaGadol* in *Lishkat HaGazit*, the dynamic shifts entirely. *She-mimenu yetze Torah le-chol Yisrael* — from there Torah goes forth to all of Israel. The authority there is not merely majoritarian; it is constitutive. At that point, even if their ruling rests on reasoning rather than tradition, even if it is decided by vote, their decision is final and binding. *Mi-kach hora'ah yotzei.* This prompts a natural question: what if the *Beis Din HaGadol* itself votes and it is close — say, thirty-six to thirty-five? Presumably, normal principles of majority rule would apply there. The individual has exhausted his path of legitimate challenge. What is striking is the parallel: the famous case of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus, who famously refused to be bound by majority vote and adhered to his tradition even against the *Beis Din* — which illustrates how powerful and rare it is when someone actually holds that position to its end. Once you reach *Beis Din HaGadol*, there must legally be a conclusion. Even if the dissenter is ultimately right, and even if everyone else is wrong, that court has the final say.

## Intellectual Freedom

### Teaching After Ruling

After the *Beis Din HaGadol* rules against him, the *zaken mamre* returns home. *Shana ve-limmed ba-derech shehaya lomed* — if he continues teaching in the manner he always had, he is *patur*, exempt. He may return and say openly: I know what *beis din* ruled. I believe they are wrong. Here is my reasoning, and here is why I believe the true *halacha* is otherwise — but of course, legally, you must follow their ruling. That is entirely permitted.

### Ruling vs. Opinion

The liability arises only *ve-im hora la'asos* — if he issues an active ruling directing people to follow his position rather than theirs. *She-ne'emar v'ha'ish asher ya'aseh b'zadon*: the *pasuk* uses the language of *action*, teaching that the offense is not voicing disagreement but translating that disagreement into a *hora'ah*, a binding directive to act. *Eino chayav ad sheyoreh la'asos* — he is not liable until he rules in a way that produces action.

The implications for intellectual freedom are remarkable. A scholar may dispute the highest court of Israel from now until the end of his days, articulating why he believes they are in error, provided he does not arrogate to himself the role of directing others to act contrary to their ruling. The law is not about objective correctness — it is about who holds binding decisional authority. And it protects the scholar's right to maintain and express his position even after that authority has spoken. One might add: by continuing to teach and attract students, such a scholar may, over time, influence the very composition of future courts. What looked like a lone voice might eventually reshape the *halacha* through legitimate means.

### Talmid Exemption

*Talmid she-hora la'asos patur* — a student who has not received *semicha* and thus does not yet hold the status to sit on a *beis din*, if he issues a ruling telling people to defy *Beis Din HaGadol*, is nonetheless exempt. One might think the opposite: his *chutzpah* in defying the *Sanhedrin* without even possessing the standing to do so ought to make his offense worse. But the law reasons differently — precisely because he lacks that standing, he is not perceived as a genuine threat to the court's authority. Nobody receives his pronouncement with the same weight as they would that of a fully credentialed elder. *Nimtzeu chumro kulo*: what is formally his shortcoming — that he has not yet attained the status of a *beis din* member — turns out to be his leniency, exempting him from the law's reach entirely.

## Pasuk Analysis

### Yipla & Mufla

The *gemara* now opens a systematic analysis of the verse *ki yipale mimcha davar*, unpacking each term to identify the categories of person and case to which *zaken mamre* applies. The word *yipale* is related to *mufla* — a term denoting expertise and recognized authority. The verse thus signals at the outset that we are speaking of someone who is *mufla she-be-veit din*, a figure of genuine stature fit to sit on a *beis din* — not a mere student. This is the first criterion established by the *pasuk*.

### Mimcha & Yoetz

The word *mimcha* — "from you" — is rendered *zeh yoetz*, an advisor. At first blush this sounds like a personal qualification, but as the *gemara* will develop, it refers to authority over *kiddush hachodesh* and *ibur shana*. The *gemara* supports this by citing the verse *mimcha yatza choshev al Hashem ra'ah yoetz beliya'al* — "from you emerges one who devises evil against God, an advisor of wickedness." The precise connection remains somewhat opaque at this stage, but the *gemara* will return to it when demonstrating how a dispute about calendar intercalation can constitute a debate qualifying a *zaken mamre*.

### Davar Definition

*Davar* — a matter — is interpreted as referring to *halacha*, specifically *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*. This is significant: the offense of *zaken mamre* is not limited to disputes over *pesukei* d'Oraita. Even when the underlying disagreement concerns a received oral tradition — a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — the *zaken mamre* framework applies. The *pasuk* uses the word *davar* precisely to encompass this category.

### Mishpat & Din

*Lamishpat* — "for judgment" — is glossed by *Rashi* (following the later *gemara*) as referring to *gezeirah shavah*. It is admittedly difficult to derive that *mishpat*, meaning judgment, connotes a hermeneutical principle like *gezeirah shavah*. Nevertheless, the implication is meaningful: the categories being debated range from *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* (the orally transmitted tradition, *davar*) to *gezeirah shavah* (the interpretive-hermeneutical derivation, *lamishpat*). Together they span the full spectrum of how *halacha* is established beyond the explicit text.

### Blood Types

*Bein dam l'dam* — between blood and blood — refers to the three types of *dam* associated with female *tumah*: *dam niddah*, *dam leidah*, and *dam zivah*. Each of these will be shown to have its own *tannaitic* debate, and each debate carries implications for a *chiyuv kares*.

### Types of Judgments

*Bein din l'din* — between judgment and judgment — encompasses three modes of adjudication: *dinei nefashot*, capital cases; *dinei mamonot*, monetary cases; and *dinei makkot*, cases involving lashes. Each category, as the *gemara* will show, has its own internal disputes with *tannaim*, and each can be connected — however indirectly — to a scenario involving a *chiyuv kares*.

### Leprosy Types

*Bein nega l'nega* — between affliction and affliction — covers three types of *tzara'at*: *nig'ei adam*, afflictions of a person; *nig'ei batim*, afflictions of houses; and *nig'ei vegadim*, afflictions of garments. Each of these has *tannaitic* debates regarding the criteria for *tumah* and *tahara*, and each will be illustrated in turn.

### Verbal Acts

*Divrei* — matters involving speech — refers to *charamim*, *arachin*, and *hekdeshot*: consecrations, valuations, and dedications. These are cases where the legal consequence flows entirely from a verbal act — what the earlier word *yipale* suggests through its root meaning of *hafla'ah*, a pronounced verbal commitment. The *zaken mamre* can equally disagree about the scope and application of such verbal acts.

### Disputes & Rituals

*Rivot* — disputes — is interpreted as referring to *hashka'at sotah*, the ritual of having the *sotah* woman drink the bitter waters in the context of a husband's accusation, and to *arifat eglah arufa*, the breaking of the heifer's neck in the case of an unsolved murder. Both of these rituals share a defining characteristic: they address situations of factual indeterminacy — the *sotah* procedure arises from a *dispute* between husband and wife, while the *eglah arufa* responds to a killing whose perpetrator remains unknown. *Rashi* notes that the word *rivot* fits them both because they each arise from unresolved conflict or uncertainty, not merely from interpersonal friction.

### Gifts to Poor

*Be-sha'arecha* — "in your gates" — is read as referring to *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah*, the agricultural gifts designated for the poor. The verse's reference to what is adjudicated "in your gates" encompasses obligations that are distributed locally and whose scope and parameters could equally be the subject of dispute.

### Going Up

*Ve-kamta ve-alita* — "you shall arise and go up" — teaches that the *Beis HaMikdash* stands at a greater elevation than the rest of *Eretz Yisrael*, whether literally or spiritually. The *gemara* then extends this further: *Eretz Yisrael gevu'ha mi-kol ha-aratzot* — Israel is higher than all other lands. The proof-text is the verse in *Yirmiyahu* predicting that in the future, people will no longer speak of God as the One who *brought up* the Jewish people from Egypt, but as the One who *brought up* and gathered them from all the lands — *he'ela va-asher hevi et zera beis Yisrael me-eretz tzafona u-mi-kol ha-aratzot* — using the verb of ascent for the journey from all lands to Israel. *Tosafot* acknowledges that there are mountains in Israel higher than the Temple Mount, so the statement is at minimum partly spiritual in force.

### Court Location

*El ha-makom asher yivchar Hashem* — the place that God will choose — teaches that the court must literally be situated in that designated place on *Har HaBayit*. *Melamed she-ha-makom gorem*: the location is causally constitutive of the law. If the *Beis Din HaGadol* is not actually sitting in the *Beis HaMikdash* on *Har HaBayit*, the *zaken mamre* does not incur liability — regardless of everything else. This is the final clause of the *braita*.

## Three Tannaitic Views

### Rabbi Meir Position

A *braita* now presents three distinct *tannaitic* positions on the conditions that trigger *zaken mamre* liability. *Zaken mamre eino chayav ela al davar she-zedono karet u-shegagato chatat, divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that the *zaken mamre* is liable only when the matter he is ruling on is one for which willful violation incurs *karet* and inadvertent violation incurs a *korban chatat*. The underlying logic is that the *zaken mamre* belongs to a special class of authorities whose rulings carry unique legal weight — alongside the *Beis Din HaGadol* whose erroneous collective ruling occasions the *par he'elem davar shel tzibur*, and the *kohen gadol* who brings a special *korban* when he has ruled and acted on his own ruling. These special *hora'ah*-related *korbanot* all arise from rulings concerning weighty sins — those for which one ordinarily brings a *chatat* if performed *be-shogeg* and incurs *karet* if performed *be-mezid*. The *zaken mamre*, though he himself is acting *be-mezid* and the problem is not primarily that he *machshil* others, is read through this same legal lens: the category of sin he is ruling about must meet this threshold of gravity.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with this criterion. *Al davar she-ikaro mi-divrei Torah u-ferusho mi-divrei soferim* — he requires not that the sin be of a certain weight, but that it be of a certain jurisprudential character. Specifically, Rabbi Yehuda requires that the matter being ruled on be grounded in a verse of the Torah but given its content through Rabbinic interpretation. The reasoning is structural: the defining role of *Beis Din HaGadol* is to be the authoritative interpreter of the Torah's text. *Al pi ha-Torah asher yorucha* — "according to the Torah that they teach you" — defines their authority as interpretive. When the *zaken mamre* rules in the same domain, he puts himself forward as a competing interpreter of the Torah. That is the genuine threat. If, by contrast, he simply rules against an explicit *pasuk* or against a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* without invoking the interpretive process at all, he is doing something reprehensible, and he may be *machshil* people, but he is not positioning himself as a rival interpretive authority. He is not competing for the same role they hold. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yehuda, only when the ruling is in the domain of Torah-rooted, rabbinically-developed interpretation does the *zaken mamre* become liable. This position is intellectually compelling, though it sits somewhat in tension with the *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* category included in the *braita*, which is not based on a *pasuk* at all.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon goes furthest. *Afilu dikduk echad mi-dikdukei sofrim* — even a single point of precision from among the details of Rabbinic teaching suffices. It is not entirely clear how far *dikdukei sofrim* extends for Rabbi Shimon. The term can refer to genuinely Rabbinic enactments — in which case the *zaken mamre* could incur liability even by defying a ruling about something like *muktzah* or *netilat yadayim* — or it can refer to fine points in the interpretation of earlier authoritative rulings, which would still be an act of competing interpretation even if the underlying text is Rabbinic rather than Biblical. Under the latter reading, Rabbi Shimon differs from Rabbi Yehuda only in extending the scope of interpretive authority beyond *divrei Torah* to *divrei soferim*, but the essential nature of the conflict — competing to be the authoritative interpreter of binding law — remains the same. Under the broader reading, Rabbi Shimon encompasses even pure legislative disagreements, not merely interpretive ones. The precise scope remains unresolved.

### Rabbi Meir's Reasoning

*Mai ta'amei de-Rabbi Meir?* The *gemara* asks for Rabbi Meir's source. He derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* — *gmar davar davar*. The word *davar* appears here in *ki yipale mimcha davar* and appears there in the context of the *par he'elem davar*, *ve-ne'elam davar me-einei ha-kahal* — when the *Beis Din HaGadol* rules erroneously and the community acts on it. In that context, the *hora'ah* that occasions the communal *korban* is specifically a *hora'ah* regarding something for which one incurs *karet* if done *be-mezid* and brings a *chatat* if done *be-shogeg*. Since both passages speak of a *hora'ah* situation using the same word *davar*, Rabbi Meir learns by *gezeirah shavah* that just as there the ruling must concern a *karet*/*chatat* sin, so too here the *zaken mamre*'s ruling must concern the same category. *Ma hailan davar she-chayav al zedono karet ve-al shigagato chatat — af kan davar she-chayav al zedono karet ve-al shigagato chatat.*

### Rabbi Yehuda Reading

Rabbi Yehuda's derivation comes from the phrase *al pi ha-Torah asher yorucha*. He reads *Torah* as referring specifically to the scriptural text, and *yorucha* as the act of teaching based on that text. The *pasuk* is thus saying: follow what they derive and teach based on the Torah's verses. This defines the *beis din*'s authority as hermeneutical — their role is to interpret scripture. Only when the *zaken mamre* places himself in that same interpretive role, ruling on a matter *she-ikaro mi-divrei Torah*, does he genuinely compete with their authority and incur liability. The straightforward reading of the *pasuk* — that *Torah* refers to the ruling itself and *yorucha* to the act of instructing — is set aside in favor of this more precise construction.

## Kiddush Hachodesh Debate

### Request & Setup

Having set out the three positions, the *gemara* turns to demonstrating them in practice. *Amar lei Rav Huna bar Chinana le-Rava: targema li le-ha masnisin aliba de-Rabbi Meir* — Rav Huna bar Chinana asks Rava to explain how the *braita*'s enumerated categories all work according to Rabbi Meir's position, which requires each disputed matter to carry the weight of a *karet*/*chatat* sin. Rava redirects the task to Rav Papa: *puk targema lei* — go and explain it to him. The *gemara* will now go through each category in the *braita* and show, according to Rabbi Meir, how the relevant dispute could implicate a *chiyuv kares*. Crucially, what this exercise really does is demonstrate that in every single category — *kiddush hachodesh*, *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, *gezeirah shavah*, blood types, judicial procedure, *tzara'at* — there exist genuine *tannaitic* disputes. The point is not that the *zaken mamre* is saying something offensive. He may be entirely correct. The problem is solely that he continues ruling after *beis din* has ruled. Every area he might dispute is an area where the *tannaim* themselves dispute, and the *gemara* is showing that the debate between *zaken mamre* and *beis din* could be just such a classic *tannaitic* disagreement.

### Yoetz Qualification

The first term — *zeh yoetz* — is now expanded: *she-yodea le-aber shanim ve-likboa chadashim*, he must be someone capable of ruling on *ibur shana* and *kiddush hachodesh*. This can be understood as a personal qualification for the *zaken mamre*, but it also identifies the type of matter they could be disputing: a ruling about *kiddush hachodesh* itself.

### Month Addition Debate

The *gemara* illustrates with a *mishnah*: two witnesses came and testified that one may add an intercalary month all through the month of *Adar* — *kol Adar*. The dispute is with those who had said the deadline was only *ad ha-Purim*, until *Purim*. This could be precisely the type of debate between a *zaken mamre* and *beis din* — can the year be intercalated after *Purim* has passed?

### Pesach Connection

How does this connect to a *chiyuv kares*? The answer is direct: a dispute over whether the year is intercalated is a dispute over when *Pesach* falls. *De-i le-hai gisa ka sharei chametz be-Pesach ve-i le-hai gisa ka sharei chametz be-Pesach* — whichever position you adopt, if the other position is correct, you will be eating *chametz* on *Pesach* according to that position, and eating *chametz* on *Pesach* is *chayav karet*. The calendar dispute thus directly implicates a *karet* sin, satisfying Rabbi Meir's criterion.

## Eleven Days Debate

### Niddah & Zava

The next category is *davar* — understood as *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — and the *gemara* illustrates it with the *halacha* of the eleven days. To understand this debate, some background is essential. A woman's menstrual period, according to Torah law, renders her a *niddah* for seven days from the onset of bleeding. After the seventh day she immerses in a *mikvah*. Following this seven-day *niddah* period, there is a window of eleven days — days eight through eighteen from the onset — during which she can potentially become a *zavah*. A *zavah gedolah*, a major *zavah*, is a woman who bleeds on three consecutive days within this eleven-day window. Such a woman requires seven clean days before she may immerse. A *zavah ketanah* — one who bleeds on only one or two days in the window — requires one clean day. If she bleeds on day nineteen, she returns to the status of *niddah*. This structure of seven *niddah* days followed by eleven *zavah* days is itself a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — it has no explicit source in a *pasuk*.

### Day Ten Dispute

The specific dispute concerns day ten of the eleven-day window. If a woman bleeds on day ten, may she immerse without first observing a clean day, or must she wait for day eleven to be clean before immersing? *De'itmar: asiri ke-teshi'i o asiri ke-achat asar?* Rabbi Yochanan holds *asiri ke-teshi'i* — day ten is like day nine. Just as a woman who bleeds on day nine must observe a clean day before immersing, so too one who bleeds on day ten must ensure that day eleven is clean. Resh Lakish holds *asiri ke-achat asar* — day ten is like day eleven. Since a woman bleeding on day eleven requires no clean day, similarly a woman who bleeds on day ten requires none.

### Mikva Implications

The reasoning of each position follows the structure of the eleven-day period. Rabbi Yochanan notes that day ten remains within the eleven-day window where *zavah* status can accumulate; therefore, the same requirement of a clean day applies as on any other day within that window. Resh Lakish reasons that on day ten, it is already impossible for the woman to become a *zavah gedolah* — even if she bleeds on days ten, eleven, and twelve, day twelve would mark the beginning of a new *niddah* period, not a third *zavah* day — and therefore the protective requirement of a clean day is inapplicable, just as on day eleven itself. The *nafka mina* for a *chiyuv karet* is transparent: if the woman does not require a clean day but her husband believes she does, they will abstain unnecessarily; if she does require a clean day but follows the lenient ruling and immerses, she remains *tamei* and subsequent relations with her husband are *chayav karet*.

## Gezeirah Shavah Debate

### Daughter Derivation

The third category — *lamishpat*, interpreted by *Rashi* as *gezeirah shavah* — is illustrated by the prohibition against a man having relations with his daughter who is not his wife's daughter. There is no explicit verse in the Torah prohibiting this directly. Rava transmits in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi that it is derived through a double *gezeirah shavah*: *atya henah henah, atya zimah zimah* — two parallel verbal identifications from separate verses establish that a man is forbidden to have relations with such a daughter.

### Kareis Connection

This prohibition falls into the gravest category of forbidden relations. The connection to *chayav karet* — or indeed to something even graver — is direct. *Tosafot* observes that this is actually the only example in this entire passage that is not itself a matter of debate among *tannaim*. Nevertheless, it is entirely conceivable that a *zaken mamre* might reject the *gezeirah shavah* on which the prohibition rests — this is, after all, the nature of disputes about *gezeirot shavot* — and rule that such relations are permitted. In that case, people who follow his ruling would violate a prohibition that is undeniably a *chiyuv karet* or worse, satisfying Rabbi Meir's criterion completely.

## Blood Impurities

### Green Blood Debate

The category *bein dam l'dam* — disputes among the three types of blood: *dam niddah*, *dam leidah*, and *dam zivah* — is now illustrated in sequence. For *dam niddah*, the *gemara* cites *biplugta de-Akavya ben Mehalalel ve-Rabanan*. *Dam ha-yarok* — greenish or yellowish blood — is the subject: Akavya ben Mehalalel rules that such blood renders a woman *tamei* as *dam niddah*, while the Sages rule it *tahor*. If a *zaken mamre* ruled as Akavya and people treated such blood as *tahor*, and then a woman had relations with her husband while still in the *niddah* status, the result would be a *chiyuv karet*. Conversely, if he ruled as the Sages and people treated it as *tahor* when in fact it is *tamei* — same result.

### Leidah Wellsprings

For *dam leidah*, the *gemara* cites *biplugta de-Rav ve-Levi*. This is the well-known debate about the source of a woman's blood during her *yemei tohar* — the period of purity following childbirth. After giving birth to a boy, a woman is *tamei* for seven days, like a *niddah*. Then, from day eight through day forty, even if she bleeds, she is *tahor* — her blood is *dam tohar*, which does not convey *tumah*. Beginning from day forty-one, her blood is once again *tamei niddah*-blood. The question is: why is the blood between days eight and forty *tahor*?

Rav holds *ma'ayan echad hu — ha-Torah tim'ato ve-ha-Torah tihara*: there is one wellspring, one source of blood in the woman. The Torah declared it *tamei* during the first seven days and declared it *tahor* during the subsequent period. The blood's physical source is unchanged; its legal status has simply been redefined by the Torah for that span. Levi holds *shtei ma'ayanot hen*: there are two distinct wellsprings. During the *yemei tohar*, the blood comes from a different source — not the uterine source that generates *niddah*-blood — and at the end of day forty, *nistam ha-tahor ve-niftach ha-tamei*: the *tahor* source closes and the *tamei* source reopens.

### Continuous Bleeding

The practical difference between these positions, the *nafka mina*, arises when a woman bleeds continuously across the transition point — for instance, from the end of day seven into day eight, or from day forty into day forty-one without a break. If there are two wellsprings, then continuous bleeding across the boundary is impossible to attribute to a single source. The physical continuity of the bleeding suggests it is coming from the same source throughout — which would undermine the premise of two separate sources. In that case, Levi's position might lead to the conclusion that the blood is *tamei* even during the *yemei tohar*, since the evidence points to a single source. But if the Torah has simply changed the legal status of blood from the same source, as Rav holds, then a continuous bleed from day seven to day eight is automatically *tahor* from the moment of transition, just as a continuous bleed from day forty to forty-one becomes *tamei* at the transition point, because the legal category changes regardless of physical continuity.

### Zivah Childbirth

For *dam zivah*, the *gemara* cites *biplugta de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbi Yehoshua*. The context is a woman who bleeds during her eleven-day *zavah* period while experiencing the pains of labor. Under Torah law, blood that flows as a result of childbirth — *zov machamat vlad* — does not render a woman a *zavah*, even if it appears during the eleven-day window. The question is: what constitutes a sufficient break between the labor pains and the delivery to sever the legal connection between them, such that the woman becomes a *zavah* after all?

*Hishtah shlosha yamim betoch achat asar yom* — if a woman experienced childbirth pains for three consecutive days during her eleven-day window, that would normally make her a *zavah gedolah*. But if those pains are connected to an imminent birth, the bleeding is attributed to childbirth rather than *zivah*. The dispute concerns the minimum break required: *im shaftah me-eis le-eis, ve-yaldah* — if she had a full twenty-four-hour relief from labor pains and then gave birth, Rabbi Eliezer rules *yoledes be-zov* — she is both a *yoledes* and a *zavah*. The twenty-four-hour break severs the connection between her earlier pains and the ultimate delivery, so the three days of bleeding are classified as *zivah*-blood rather than childbirth-blood. Rabbi Yehoshua is more lenient: *lailah va-yom ke-leil Shabbos ve-yomo* — the break that disqualifies her must consist of a full night followed by a full day, patterned after the calendrical day of *Shabbat*. Moreover, *ve-shavsa min ha-tzaar v'lo min ha-dam* — what must cease for this period is not the bleeding but the *pain*. Only if she has had this precise form of relief — a full night and full day without labor pains — does she become a *zavah*. Under Rabbi Yehoshua's view, a twenty-four-hour break that does not fall night-first-then-day is insufficient, making her *zavah* status harder to establish. Again, whether a woman is a *zavah* or not has direct implications for relations with her husband, and therefore for a *chiyuv karet*.

## Dinei Mamonos

### Two-Judge Ruling

The category *bein din l'din* is now taken up, beginning with *dinei mamonot*. The *gemara* cites *biplugta de-Shmuel ve-Rabbi Abahu*. Shmuel rules that *shnayim she-danu, dineihen din* — if two judges adjudicated a monetary case, their ruling is legally binding, albeit the court that did so is described as *beis din chatzuf*, a brazen court. Rabbi Abahu rules *le-divrei ha-kol ein dineihen din* — the ruling is universally invalid and has no binding legal force.

### Marriage Scenario

The connection to *chayav karet* may not be immediately obvious, since what is being debated is the validity of adjudication, not the substance of the monetary law. *Rashi* explains the mechanism: suppose two parties are disputing ownership of an object — a cloak, a coin, whatever — and two judges rule in favor of one of them. That person then uses the object to effect *kiddushin* with a woman: *harei at mekudeshet li*. If the two-judge ruling is valid, the object belonged to him, the *kiddushin* takes effect, and the woman is his wife. If she then cohabits with another man, that is adultery — *chayav karet*. If the ruling is invalid, he never legitimately owned the object, the *kiddushin* is void, and she has not committed adultery. Any monetary dispute, therefore, can be linked — through the mechanism of marriage effected by means of the disputed object or sum — to a potential *chiyuv karet*, satisfying Rabbi Meir's standard.

## Dinei Nefashos

### Rabbi View

Continuing through *bein din l'din*, the *gemara* addresses *dinei nefashot*. Rabbi holds *nefesh tachas nafesh mammon*: in the case of a person who intended to kill one individual but inadvertently killed another — the classic case where Reuven aimed at Shimon and killed Levi — the verse *ve-nasata nefesh tachas nafesh* is read as requiring monetary compensation rather than execution. Rabbi's derivation relies on a *gezeirah shavah*: just as *nesina* in the context of the miscarriage case means payment, so *nesina* here means payment as well.

### Chachamim View

The Sages dispute this: in their reading, either the person is executed, or — even if execution is not applicable — the principle of *kim lei bi-d'rabba minei* applies, barring any monetary obligation where a capital punishment looms. The person who killed inadvertently while aiming elsewhere either faces execution or faces nothing at all; he does not pay damages.

### Payment Marriage Link

The connection to *karet* follows the same structural logic as *dinei mamonot*: if monetary damages are owed according to Rabbi's ruling, and the killer uses that payment to effect *kiddushin* with a woman, then whether she is his wife or not — and consequently whether subsequent relations with another man constitute adultery — depends on whether the payment was legitimately his. The *gemara* does not belabor the point, having established the logical pattern.

## Dinei Makkos

### Three Judges

The third sub-category of *bein din l'din* is *dinei makkot*. The *mishna* itself records the dispute: *makkot bi-shlosha* — lashes are administered by a court of three judges, as the standard ruling requires.

### Twenty-Three Judges

*Mishum Rabbi Yishmael amru: be-esrim u-shlosha* — in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, it was taught that lashes require a court of twenty-three. The debate concerns the minimum court composition for a valid *makkot* proceeding.

### Compensation Link

The link to *chayav karet* follows the by-now-established pattern. *Rashi* explains: if a person was lashed by a court of three judges, and Rabbi Yishmael's position is correct that twenty-three were required, then the lashes were administered illegitimately. The recipient is entitled to compensation for the unlawful punishment. If he receives that compensation and uses it to effect *kiddushin*, the status of his marriage depends on whether the payment was validly owed — which depends on whether the original *beis din* proceeding was valid. From there the chain leads to potential *karet* in the same manner as all preceding cases.

## Tzaraas of People

### Spot vs. Hair

The category *bein nega l'nega* begins with *nig'ei adam*, afflictions of a person. The *gemara* cites the well-known debate from the *mishna*: *im baheres kodem le-se'ar lavan, tamei* — if the white spot appeared before the white hair, he is *tamei*. *Im se'ar lavan kodem la-baheres, tahor* — if the white hair preceded the spot, he is *tahor*.

### Safek Status

The case of *safek* — when it is unknown which came first — yields a ruling of *tamei* according to the first opinion.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yehoshua's position is recorded with the single word *keiha* — "it is dim." Rava explains: *keiha tahor* — Rabbi Yehoshua means that in this situation of doubt, the ruling is *tahor*. This is his compact way of expressing disagreement with the stringent position. *Rashi* connects the *karet* implication to the following scenario: if the person is ruled *tahor* erroneously and he enters the *Beis HaMikdash*, there is a potential *karet*-level violation — entry while *tamei* can implicate severe punishment — or other scenarios in which a disputed *tzara'at* ruling creates the conditions for a severe sin.

## Tzaraas of Houses

### Corner Requirement

For *nig'ei batim*, the *gemara* cites Rabbi Eliezer the son of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that a house never becomes *tamei* from *tzara'at* unless the spot appears in a very precise configuration: *kishnei gerisim al shtei avanim bi-shtei ktalim be-kern zavis* — the equivalent of two beans' size spread across two stones on two walls, at a corner. The spot must literally wrap around an interior corner of the house, spanning both adjacent walls.

### Size Specs

The dimensions Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon specifies are: *archo kishnei gerisim, rovcho ke-gris* — the length of the affliction is two beans, the width is one bean. This means the spot is rectangular in proportion, oriented along the corner.

### Kiros Derivation

His textual source: the verse uses both *kir* (singular wall) and *kiros* (plural walls). *Eizo kir she-hi ke-kiros? Hevei omer: zeh kern zavis* — what kind of wall is like walls (plural)? The answer is the corner: a corner is simultaneously one wall that is also two walls meeting. This derivation limits the scope of house *tzara'at* significantly compared to the Rabbinic majority position, creating a genuine dispute about when a house becomes *tamei*.

## Tzaraas of Clothes

### Full Spread

The final category is *nig'ei vegadim*, and the authority cited is Rabbi Nosson ben Avtulmos. The question he addresses is: if a spot of *tzara'at* began on a garment and then spread to cover the entire garment, what is the status? Rabbi Nosson ben Avtulmos rules *tahorah* — it is pure. Just as a person whose *tzara'at* spreads to cover his entire body from head to toe is declared *tahor* (the principle of *parcha be-chulo tahor* for a person), so too a garment whose *tzara'at* spreads to cover it entirely is *tahor*.

### Head to Toe

The derivation proceeds by *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kerachat ve-gabachat be-adam, ve-ne'emar kerachat ve-gabachat be-vaged* — the same language of a bald spot and a smooth patch appears both in the context of a person's head and in the context of a garment. *Mah lehalan parcha be-chulo tahor, af kan parcha be-chulo tahor* — just as when the affliction covers the person entirely he is *tahor*, so too when the affliction covers the garment entirely it is *tahor*. The Sages presumably dispute this ruling, and the debate between them constitutes the *tannaitic* controversy that a *zaken mamre* and *beis din* could reproduce.

The *gemara* will continue tomorrow, completing the catalogue and reinforcing the overarching point: in each and every domain the *braita* lists, there are genuine disputes among *tannaim*, any one of which could in principle be the very disagreement that defines the confrontation between a *zaken mamre* and *beis din*. The *zaken mamre* may well be right. But once *beis din* has ruled, his continued ruling in opposition — and not the substance of what he says — is what the Torah declares intolerable.
