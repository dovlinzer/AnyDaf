# Zevachim 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nichnas Lifnim

### Intro: Blood Inside

Daf 83 opens by completing the discussion of the rule that when the blood of a *chatas* — and according to Rabbi Akiva, of other *korbanos* as well — is brought inside the *heichal* or the *kodesh hakodashim*, it renders the offering invalid. The *gemara* had closed the previous discussion with a related question: what if it was the *chatas* of *Yom Kippur*, whose blood was brought into the *kodesh hakodashim*, brought back out, and then brought in again? With those threads tied off, the discussion now turns to a specific question that arises from the relevant verse: *v'chol chatas asher yuva midamah l'chapper bakodesh el hakodesh penimah* — and any *chatas* whose blood is brought inside for the purpose of atonement. The operative phrase is *l'chapper bakodesh*: does this mean the *intent* with which the blood was brought, or does it mean the blood was actually *used* in a rite of atonement?

### L'Chapper: Intent vs. Use

A *braisa* records the debate between *tannaim* on precisely this question. The issue is how to interpret the phrase *l'chapper bakodesh* in the verse that invalidates an outer *chatas* whose blood was brought inside. Is it sufficient that the blood was brought in with the *intent* to effect atonement, or must the blood have actually been *used* in the atonement process?

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

Rabbi Eliezer argues for the first reading — intent alone is sufficient — by means of a *gezerah shavah*. The same phrase *l'chapper bakodesh* appears in the verse governing *Yom Kippur*: *v'chol adam lo yihyeh b'Ohel Moed b'vo'o l'chapper bakodesh*, that no person may be present in the *Ohel Moed* when the *Kohen Gadol* enters *l'chapper bakodesh*. There, Rabbi Eliezer observes, *l'chapper* clearly refers to prospective intent — people are required to clear out *before* any atonement is performed. The atonement has not yet happened; the *Kohen Gadol* is merely entering for that purpose. By linking the two uses of *l'chapper bakodesh*, Rabbi Eliezer concludes: just as in that case the phrase is forward-looking, so too by the outer *chatas*, it is the intent with which the blood is brought — not the actual performance of atonement — that is determinative.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and reaches a different conclusion through a different link. He notes that *l'chapper bakodesh* also appears in a second *Yom Kippur* verse: *v'es par hachatas v'es se'ir hachatas asher huva es damam l'chapper bakodesh* — the ox of the *chatas* and the goat of the *chatas* whose blood was brought in to atone, *l'chapper bakodesh*. Unlike the verse cited by Rabbi Eliezer, this passage looks backward. It describes what was already done with the blood. Moreover, it is in this context that the subsequent burning of these animals is commanded — and that burning would only have been carried out if the blood had in fact been properly used in the atonement rite. Rabbi Shimon therefore concludes: *mah lehalan keshekipper af kan keshekipper* — just as in that verse the atonement was actually performed, so too in our case, the *l'chapper bakodesh* requires that the blood was actually used, not merely that it was brought with that intent.

### Gemara Resolution

The *gemara* then asks: *bemai kamiflagi* — what is the underlying principle that leads each opinion to its respective *Yom Kippur* verse? The answer is that each *tanna* follows a different rule of textual comparison.

Rabbi Eliezer holds *danim chutz michutz* — one compares an outer *chatas* to something that similarly belongs outside. The outer *chatas* blood steps over a boundary when it enters inside, just as the *Yom Kippur* verse about people leaving the *Ohel Moed* is also about a boundary: remaining outside while something proceeds within. Accordingly, both uses of *l'chapper bakodesh* share the feature of operating from the outside, and the verse there is clearly prospective — it is about intent, not completed action.

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds *danim behemah mibehemah* — one compares the description of the blood to another description of blood, not to a description of a person's movement. In the verse about the outer *chatas*, it is the blood that is described as being brought *l'chapper bakodesh*; similarly, in the second *Yom Kippur* verse, it is the blood of the bull and the goat that is described as having been brought *l'chapper bakodesh*. The two instances share the same grammatical and conceptual structure — blood, not a person, is the subject — and in the latter verse the blood was unambiguously used in actual atonement. He further notes *v'ein danim behemah me'adam*: we do not derive the rule for blood from a verse about the *Kohen Gadol's* purpose in entering.

### Rabbi Yehudah: Intent

A third position in the *mishnah* belongs to Rabbi Yehudah, who reads *l'chapper* differently still: the blood must have been brought *b'meizid* — intentionally — not *b'shogeg*. The most plausible reading of his position is that it addresses a case where the one who brought the blood did not realize it was outer *chatas* blood, mistaking it for the blood of an inner *chatas*. This is a distinct dimension of the dispute, focusing on the actor's state of mind rather than on whether the ritual was completed. The *gemara* now wants to know how Rabbi Yehudah himself holds in the earlier debate: does he require that the blood was actually used in atonement, or is intent sufficient?

### Rabbi Yehudah Analysis

The *gemara* approaches this question through the words of Rabbi Yirmiyah, who directs attention to a *braisa*. By examining Rabbi Yehudah's use of a textual derivation in that *braisa*, the *gemara* will be able to determine which side of the intent-versus-use debate he occupies.

### Tumat Begadim

The *braisa* in question concerns the *halacha* of *tumat begadim*, the impurity contracted by garments. The verse describing the *Yom Kippur* offerings — *v'es par hachatas v'es se'ir hachatas asher huva es damam l'chapper bakodesh* — concludes by stating that the one who burns these animals must launder his garments. The *braisa* asks: from the phrase *asher huva es damam l'chapper bakodesh*, which characterizes the *Yom Kippur* offerings as those whose blood was brought in to atone, why does the verse use the words *par hachatas* and *se'ir hachatas* rather than simply *hapar* and *hase'ir*? One might have thought the *halacha* of *tumat begadim* applies only to the specific bull and goat of *Yom Kippur*. But we in fact know this *halacha* to apply to all inner *chatas* offerings — including the *par ha'elem davar* and the *se'ir avodah zarah*. The question is how we know.

Rabbi Yehudah in the *braisa* says: *talmud lomar chatas chatas* — from the repetition of the word *chatas* in *par hachatas* and *se'ir hachatas*. This extra word extends the *halacha* of *tumat begadim* to all inner *chatas* offerings.

Rabbi Meir disagrees: *eino tzarich* — no such extra derivation is needed. The verse itself already supplies the key: *asher huva es damam l'chapper bakodesh* — the defining characteristic is that their blood was brought inside to atone. Any inner *chatas* that falls into that category is subject to the same *halacha*. The words *par hachatas* and *se'ir hachatas* are therefore available for a different use entirely.

The *gemara* then presses: why doesn't Rabbi Yehudah himself use the phrase *l'chapper bakodesh* to establish the applicability to all inner *chatas* offerings, since that seems to be the more direct textual hook? The answer is *lav mishum d'mibai leh l'gzerah shavah* — it must be that Rabbi Yehudah is reserving *l'chapper bakodesh* for a different *halacha*, namely the *gezerah shavah* linking the outer *chatas* to the *Yom Kippur* usage. And since he uses the *l'chapper bakodesh* of the backward-looking verse — the one where the blood was actually used in atonement — this tells us that Rabbi Yehudah holds, in the main debate, that actual use of the blood is required, not merely intent. He therefore needs the extra *chatas chatas* to derive the *tumat begadim halacha* from a different source.

Rabbi Meir, by contrast, does not use *l'chapper bakodesh* for the *tumat begadim* derivation — he uses the explicit description of the verse — and must therefore be applying *l'chapper bakodesh* to the outer *chatas* question. Since the verse he would link to is the one describing blood actually used in atonement, Rabbi Meir similarly holds that actual use, not intent alone, is what renders the outer *chatas* invalid.

### Rabbi Yehudah Analysis 2

The *gemara* then explains why Rabbi Yehudah, having reserved *l'chapper bakodesh* for the *gezerah shavah*, cannot in turn derive the *tumat begadim* extension from the explicit description of the verse the way Rabbi Meir does. The reason is that Rabbi Yehudah needs the explicit description — *asher huva es damam l'chapper bakodesh* — specifically for the *gezerah shavah* that establishes the rule for the outer *chatas*. Since he has committed those words to that purpose, he must fall back on the extra word *chatas chatas* for the *tumat begadim* extension. Rabbi Meir, who does not need *l'chapper bakodesh* for the *gezerah shavah* (having derived it through a different route), is free to use it for *tumat begadim*, leaving *chatas chatas* available for nothing in particular.

The discussion closes with a brief exchange about the broader theological significance of the *kodesh* being cleansed — *l'chapper* as purifying the *mikdash* — but that is set aside for another occasion.

## Hamizbe'ach Mekadesh

### Intro: Altar Sanctifies

With the close of the previous *perek*, which encompassed extensive material on mixtures (*ta'arovot*) and related issues, the *gemara* now transitions to a new chapter. The central subject is the principle of *hamizbe'ach mekadesh et haraui lo* — the altar sanctifies things that are fit for it. This principle, encountered throughout the *masechet*, is now addressed directly: if something that is *pasul* is placed on the altar, under what conditions does it remain there rather than being removed? The phrase *haraui lo* — what is fit for it — is the crux of the debate.

### Rabbi Yehoshua: Fire

Rabbi Yehoshua states: *kol haraui l'ishim* — it applies to anything fit for the fire. If a *pasul* item belongs on the fire of the altar, once it is placed there it does not come down: *im ala lo yered*. The verse he derives this from is *hi ha'olah al mokdah* — it is the *olah* on the pyre. Just as an *olah* belongs on the fire, so too anything fit for the fire (*rauy l'ishim*) that ascends the altar does not descend, even if it is found to be *pasul*. What this excludes, on his view, are liquids like blood and wine — things placed on the altar but not designated for the fire. Those, if *pasul*, must be removed.

### Rabban Gamliel: Altar

Rabban Gamliel offers a broader criterion: *kol haraui lamizbeach im ala lo yered* — anything fit for the altar, once placed there, does not come down. His proof text is the same verse — *hi ha'olah al mokdah al hamizbeach* — but he reads the emphasis differently: not *al mokdah* (on the fire), but *al hamizbeach* (on the altar). Just as an *olah* belongs on the altar, so anything fit for the altar, including blood and wine, is sanctified by being placed there and does not come down even if *pasul*.

The *gemara* thus states: *ein bein divrei Rabban Gamliel l'divrei Rabbi Yehoshua ela hadam v'hanesachim* — the practical difference between them comes down to blood and wine offerings. According to Rabban Gamliel, if blood or wine that is *pasul* is placed on the altar, it stays. According to Rabbi Yehoshua, it must come down, since it does not belong on the fire.

### Debate: Blood & Wine

One might ask: according to Rabban Gamliel, who includes everything fit for the altar, what is ever *excluded* by the phrase *haraui lo*? That question will be developed in the *gemara* below. For now, the debate between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua is sharpened to its essential point: does *haraui lo* mean fit for the fire, or fit for the altar in any capacity?

### Textual Analysis

Before proceeding to the *gemara*, both Rashi and Tosafot make an important textual observation about the structure of this *mishnah*. The *mishnah* opens with an anonymous phrase — *hamizbe'ach mekadesh et haraui lo* — and then presents the debate between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua about what that phrase means. Tosafot notes that this pattern recurs elsewhere: for instance, in Tractate Pesachim, an early anonymous teaching that *shtei shurot b'martef* (two rows of the wine cellar) require checking is inherited by Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, who then debate what *shtei shurot* means. Similarly, the opening *mishnah* of Tractate Gittin states the rule that one who brings a *get* from overseas must declare it was written and signed in his presence, and later *tannaim* debate which locations count as "overseas." In all these cases, an early formulation was transmitted with undefined terms, and later *tannaim* debated the meaning of those terms. The opening phrase of the *mishnah* here — *hamizbe'ach mekadesh et haraui lo* — appears to be exactly such an early inherited teaching, and Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua are not simply expressing personal views but are interpreting a transmitted *mesorah* whose terms had become contested.

This is a significant structural feature to recognize throughout the *mishnayot*: what appears to be a simple three-part debate — anonymous opening statement, then two named opinions — is often better understood as an early formulation whose undefined terms are being clarified by later *tannaim*.

### Rabbi Shimon: Shikhlul

Rabbi Shimon presents a third criterion, structurally different from both of the previous ones. He holds that the key is not whether the item goes on the fire or merely on the altar, but whether the offering comes *biglal atzmah* — on its own account — or *biglal zevach* — by virtue of another offering. His derivation: *mah olah haba'ah biglal atzmah af kol haba'im biglal atzmah* — just as an *olah* comes entirely on its own account, so too anything that comes on its own account stays on the altar. *Yatzu nesachim habaim biglal zevach* — excluded are wine and flour offerings that come accompanying an animal sacrifice. If the *zevach* is valid but the *nesachim* are *pasul*, they come down; if the *nesachim* are valid but the *zevach* is *pasul*, the *nesachim* come down as well because they have nothing to accompany. Even if both are *pasul*, the *zevach* stays on the altar and the *nesachim* come down. For Rabbi Shimon, dependency (*shikhlul*) is the operative criterion, not the distinction between fire and altar.

## Harauy Lo Definition

### Komatim Pasul

The *gemara* now asks: *she'ein haraui lo lema'utei mai* — what does the phrase "not fit for it" actually exclude, especially according to the more expansive view of Rabban Gamliel, who includes even blood and wine? The answer will not be about the severity of the *psul* — that is a question for later *mishnayot* — but about how intrinsically connected an item is to the altar in the first place.

### Rav Pappa: Kometz

Rav Pappa answers: *lema'utei kmatzim shelo kidshu bikeli* — what is excluded is the *kometz* of a *mincha* that was not sanctified through placement in a *kli shares* (a service vessel). The normal procedure when taking a handful from a *mincha* is to place the *kometz* in a vessel, thereby sanctifying and designating it for the altar. If one bypassed this step and placed the *kometz* directly on the altar without first putting it in a vessel, it would have to be removed — even on Rabban Gamliel's expansive view.

The reasoning reflects a fundamental distinction between the *kometz* and other altar items. With animal offerings, one knows from the outset which parts are destined for the altar — the blood, the *emurim*, and in the case of an *olah*, the meat. These parts are effectively pre-labeled. With a *mincha*, however, the specific handful that will go to the altar is not designated until the *kometz* is taken and then placed in the vessel. The act of placing it in the vessel is what completes its designation as the altar's portion. Without that step, the item has not yet been fully connected to the altar, and therefore it is not *raui lo* — it is not fit for it in the relevant sense.

### Ravina Challenge

Ravina challenges this distinction by pointing to a case discussed by Ulla: *emurei kodshim kalim she-he'alam lifnei zrikas damam* — the *emurim* (designated limbs) of a *kodshim kalim* offering such as a *shlamim*, which were placed on the altar before the blood was sprinkled. Those too are not yet fully ready for the altar at the moment of their placement — the blood has not yet been thrown — and yet Ulla states that they remain on the altar and do not come down. If the *emurim* can remain despite not yet being fully designated in a *halachic* sense, why should the unsanctified *kometz* be different?

### Emurim Kodshim Kalim

The answer requires understanding a specific feature of *kodshim kalim*. A *shlamim* is characterized by the fact that the animal essentially remains in the owner's domain in a meaningful sense — that is why there is no *me'ila* (liability for misuse of consecrated property) with respect to a *shlamim* before the blood is thrown. The *emurim*, though physically identifiable as the altar's portion, are not yet fully under the altar's control — they have not yet become fully *God's portion*, as it were — until *zerikas dam* is performed. Before that moment, even the *emurim* are not subject to *me'ila*. In that sense, they resemble the unsanctified *kometz*: both lack full designation for the altar at the moment of placement.

### Missing Act vs. Designat…

Nevertheless, the *gemara* draws a key distinction between the two cases. By the *emurim* of *kodshim kalim*, nothing is *missing in the emurim themselves*. They are physically and substantively in the state they need to be. What is incomplete is an *external act* — the throwing of the blood — which is performed on a different object entirely. Because the *emurim* themselves lack nothing, they are considered sufficiently connected to the altar to remain there.

By the *kometz* that was not placed in a vessel, the situation is the reverse. The deficiency is *internal to the object itself*: the act that must be performed — placing the *kometz* in a *kli shares* — is an act that must be done to the *kometz* itself. The designation of the *kometz* as the altar's portion is simply not complete. This is therefore categorically different from the *emurim*, and the *kometz* must be removed.

## Four Positions

### Rabbi Yehoshua Fire

Returning now to the positions in the *mishnah*, the *gemara* examines the derivations underlying each view. Rabbi Yehoshua derives his principle from the verse *hi ha'olah al mokdah* — he emphasizes the word *mokdah*, the fire: just as an *olah* is fit for the fire, so only things fit for the fire (*rauy l'ishim*) remain on the altar once placed there. This excludes blood and wine, which are placed on or at the altar but are not consumed by fire.

It is worth noting — as Michael observed — that the *gemara* is explicit that the *mizbeach* itself is what sanctifies, not the fire. Accordingly, even Rabbi Yehoshua does not mean that an item must literally be on the fire to remain. The criterion is the *type* of object: if it is the kind of thing meant for the fire, it stays, wherever exactly on the altar it has been placed. What he excludes is an object that, by its very nature, is never burned.

### Two Mizbeach

Rabban Gamliel, by contrast, emphasizes the word *mizbeach* in the same verse: *hi ha'olah al mokdah al hamizbeach*. Rabbi Yehoshua acknowledges that *mizbeach* appears in the verse — so what does Rabban Gamliel do with the word *mokdah*, and what does Rabbi Yehoshua do with the word *mizbeach*? The *gemara* works through these questions carefully.

### Mizbeach Sanctifies

For Rabbi Yehoshua, *al hamizbeach* teaches not a criterion of eligibility but the mechanism of sanctification: it is the *mizbeach* that sanctifies upon contact, not the fire. An item placed on the *mizbeach* — even not yet on the fire — acquires the status of being sanctified by the altar. *Mokdah* is then the criterion: only items that are *the kind that go on the fire* are covered by this rule. For Rabban Gamliel, *mokdah* is not the eligibility criterion but rather conveys the separate *halacha* that logs that fly off the fire must be replaced — derived from *al mokdah* as a directive to maintain the fire — while *mizbeach* provides the broader criterion of fitness for the altar in any capacity.

### Two Pasul Types

The *gemara* introduces a further dimension: there are two *mezbeach* usages in the verse — *al mokdah al hamizbeach* — and the *gemara* suggests that one of them addresses an additional question: what kind of *psul* is covered by the rule? Specifically, *chad dehava lei shas hachosher v'chad dela hava lei shas hachosher* — one teaches about an item that had a *shas hachosher* (a moment when it was fit), and one teaches about an item that never had such a moment.

The distinction is significant. If blood was collected and was momentarily valid, then became *tamei*, it had a *shas hachosher* — a period of fitness. But if an animal was slaughtered with an intention of *pigul*, the blood and *emurim* were *pasul* from the very moment they became those objects — they never had a moment of fitness. One might have thought this latter type of *psul* is so fundamental that the altar could not sanctify it. One usage of *mizbeach* therefore teaches that even items that never had a *shas hachosher* are sanctified by the altar and remain there.

The opposing *tanna* holds that once the Torah has stated the novel principle that even *pasul* items remain on the altar, there is no reason to distinguish between the two types of *psul*: *lo shana dehava lei shas hachosher lo shana dela hava lei shas hachosher*. The inclusion is complete, and a separate derivation is unnecessary.

### Rabbi Yosi Kevasim

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili introduces yet another approach. The verse states *kol hanogeah bamizbe'ach yikdash* — whatever touches the altar is sanctified. One might have thought this applies universally, even to things with no connection to the altar whatsoever. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili therefore derives a limiting criterion from the adjacent verse, which mentions *kevasim bnei shanah* in the context of the *olat tamid*: *mah kevasim re'uyim af kol ra'uy* — just as lambs are fit (to be brought), so too only things that are fit (in the basic sense of being eligible as sacrifices) are sanctified by the altar. On his view, this excludes both birds — which are not included in *kevasim* — and *mincha* offerings.

### Rabbi Akiva Olah

Rabbi Akiva similarly derives from the verse *olah*: *mah olah re'uyah af kol ra'uy* — just as an *olah* is fit, so too only fit things are sanctified by the altar. Rabbi Akiva's term of reference (*olah*) includes birds, since bird *olos* exist; accordingly, he would include an invalid bird *olah*. But it excludes *mincha*, since *olah* refers to animal offerings. The word *kevasim* in the verse, for Rabbi Akiva, serves a different purpose: it rules out an animal that was a *ba'al mum* even when alive — one that was invalid even before entering the realm of *korbanot* entirely.

### Four Criteria

There are thus four distinct positions: Rabban Gamliel — anything fit for the altar, including blood and wine; Rabbi Yehoshua — only things fit for the fire; Rabbi Yosi HaGlili — things like *kevasim*, meaning animal offerings (excluding birds and *mincha*); and Rabbi Akiva — things like *olah*, meaning animal offerings including birds, but excluding *mincha*. Among these, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva are more restrictive than Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua in that they exclude *mincha* entirely, while differing from each other on the question of bird offerings.

## Test Cases

### Kometz in Vessel

Rav Pappa identifies the practical difference between the *tannaim* in the *mishnah* and the *tannaim* in the *braisa*: *kmatzim shekidshu bikeli ika beynaihu* — the case of a *kometz* that was properly sanctified in a vessel. Once the *kometz* has been placed in a *kli shares*, it is fully designated for the altar. For the *tanna* of the *mishnah* (Rabban Gamliel or Rabbi Yehoshua), once the *kometz* is in a *kli*, it is *raui* and remains on the altar. For the *tannaim* of the *braisa* (Rabbi Yosi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva), who require the item to be like an animal offering, a *kometz* — even properly sanctified — comes down, because it is a *mincha*.

### Independent Mincha

Reish Lakish then works through a series of cases to map out the implications of each position. First: a *mincha* that comes *bifnei atzmah* — on its own, independently. This would include a *minchas nesachim* brought on its own without an accompanying animal offering. For Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua, such a *mincha*, if *pasul*, does not come down. For Rabbi Yosi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva, it comes down, since it is not an animal offering.

### Mincha with Zevach

Second: a *mincha* that comes *im hazevach* — accompanying an animal sacrifice. Now Rabbi Shimon's criterion of *shikhlul* (dependency) comes into play. For Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua, it still does not come down — they do not distinguish between independent and accompanying offerings. But for everyone else — Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Shimon — it comes down, each for his own reason.

### Nesachim Alone

Third: *nesachim haba'im bifnei atzman* — wine offerings brought independently, not accompanying an animal. For Rabban Gamliel, they do not come down — anything fit for the altar remains. For Rabbi Shimon, they also do not come down — wine brought independently is coming *biglal atzmah*, on its own account. But for the other three — Rabbi Yehoshua (who requires fitness for the fire), Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, and Rabbi Akiva (both of whom require an animal-like offering) — the wine comes down.

### Nesachim w/ Zevach

Fourth: *nesachim haba'im im hazevach* — wine accompanying an animal sacrifice. Here, only Rabban Gamliel says the wine does not come down. Rabbi Yehoshua says it comes down because it is not fit for the fire. Rabbi Shimon says it comes down because it is not coming on its own account. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva say it comes down because it is not an animal offering. Rabban Gamliel alone includes it: he does not care whether the item goes on the fire, does not care whether it accompanies something else, and does not require it to be an animal offering. Any item fit for the altar in any capacity remains there.

### Positions Chart

The positions thus map cleanly: Rabban Gamliel is uniquely inclusive. Rabbi Yehoshua occupies an intermediate position — inclusive of *mincha* but exclusive of liquids. Rabbi Shimon cuts differently — inclusive of independent wine but exclusive of accompanying wine. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva share the most restrictive criterion (animal offerings only), differing only on whether bird *olos* are included.

### New Halacha

The *gemara* then presses: *pshita* — all of this could have been worked out without the case of the independently brought *minchas nesachim*. What *chiddush* does that case actually contribute? The answer is that the *chiddush* is the *halacha* itself — that one may bring a *minchas nesachim* independently.

### Rava Minchas

This is the ruling of Rava: *misnadeiv adam minchas nesachim b'chol yom* — a person may voluntarily bring a *minchas nesachim* on any day, independently, without bringing it as part of an animal offering. This is not self-evident, since a *minchas nesachim* is by its nature an accompanying offering; the *chiddush* is that it may nonetheless be brought independently as a voluntary contribution. The *gemara* asks: if that is the point, simply cite Rava's ruling directly rather than embed it in this more complex analysis. The *gemara* responds that the real *chiddush* lies elsewhere.

### Accompanying Nesachim

The genuine novelty is in the case of *nesachim haba'im im hazevach* — specifically what happens when the accompanying wine is brought not on the same day as the animal but on a subsequent day. The *halacha* is that if *nesachim* were not brought together with the animal, they may be brought the next day or even later. One might have thought that in such a case, since the wine arrives separately, it is effectively coming *bifnei atzman* — on its own — and Rabbi Shimon would concede that it does not come down. The *gemara* teaches: *kol mashma lan* — no. Even wine brought on a later day as a makeup offering for an animal sacrifice is still considered to be coming *biglal hazevach*, on account of the animal, not on its own account. Rabbi Shimon therefore maintains that it must come down even in this case. We will continue from here tomorrow.
