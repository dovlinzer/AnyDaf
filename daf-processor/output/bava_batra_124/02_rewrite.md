# Bava Batra 124 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bechor & Property Value

### Bechor Double Portion

The daf picks up at the bottom of 123b, in the middle of a discussion about whether a *bechor* receives a double portion in property that has increased in value after the father's death. The general rule is that a *bechor* does not collect *k'rauy k'muchzak* — he does not receive double in assets that were merely destined to come to the father, in the way that he receives double in assets already in the father's possession. Nevertheless, a *baraita* had stated that when property increases in value naturally, the *bechor* does receive the double portion. The Gemara will reveal that this is a disputed principle, attributed specifically to Rabbi and contested by the *rabbanan*.

### Stake vs. Estate Frame

One conceptual approach to the *bechor*'s double portion in post-mortem appreciation is to reframe the question entirely: perhaps the *bechor* is not receiving double in new value at all, but is simply receiving the natural yield on his already-established double stake in the principal. If a bank account earns interest after the father's death, each heir's share of that interest flows proportionally from his ownership of the principal — the *bechor*, holding two-thirds, simply earns interest on two-thirds. On this reading, no special *halacha* about *rauy* or *muchzak* is needed; the *bechor* earns the increase because he already owns more of the underlying asset.

### Tosafot Division Theory

Tosafot, however, draws a critical distinction: this logic applies only after the estate has been divided. Once each heir has taken possession of his specific portion, any subsequent appreciation belongs to him as owner of that portion. But prior to division, one cannot say that the *bechor* already holds two-thirds of the principal — the estate remains undivided, and any increase accrues to the estate as a whole, not to the *bechor*'s as-yet-unclaimed share. The question then becomes whether the *bechor* is entitled to double in the estate's post-mortem increase, and that is precisely where the dispute between Rabbi and the *rabbanan* lies.

To illustrate: if the estate is worth one million dollars and has not yet been divided, and it subsequently appreciates by ten thousand dollars, the *bechor* cannot claim that his portion of the estate grew by a proportional amount. He has not yet received his portion. When he comes to collect his double share, the *halacha* limits him to double of what the father possessed at the time of death. The ten thousand dollars that accrued afterward would, absent Rabbi's principle, be divided equally among all the heirs. What Rabbi says is that natural appreciation — appreciation requiring no investment by the heirs — is treated as though it had already existed in the father's possession, and therefore the *bechor* takes double of it as well.

## Rented Cow & Growth

### Baraita Sources

The Gemara presents a *baraita* addressing the case of a cow the father left behind, either rented out to others (*muchkeres*) or put out for profit-sharing (*mushkeres*), or alternatively left to graze freely in the wild (*ro'ah ba'eifer*). In all these cases, if the cow gave birth after the father's death, the *bechor* takes double of the offspring.

### Rented vs. Grazing

The Gemara immediately questions the *baraita*: if we already know from the case of a cow grazing freely that the *bechor* gets double — since the birth is entirely spontaneous and requires no effort — why do we need to be told the case of a rented or profit-sharing cow? Intuitively, one might have thought that a rented cow is more difficult: it is not even on the estate's premises, and to call the calf "already in the father's possession" seems like a greater stretch.

### Auto Growth Principle

The Gemara answers: the two cases illuminate each other. The emphasis in *ro'ah ba'eifer* — grazing literally in the dust, in the wild — highlights that the essential criterion is *shivcha di-meimeila*, an increase that comes about by itself, without requiring any expenditure from the heirs. The case of the rented or profit-sharing cow is then shown to satisfy the same criterion: from the perspective of the estate, the birth happens without any investment on the heirs' part. The fact that the person who rented the cow may have to feed it or that the profit-sharing partner has to exert effort is irrelevant — the heirs themselves invest nothing. Since from the heirs' vantage point the increase is entirely automatic, it is treated as *shivcha di-meimeila*, and the *bechor* gets double.

### Rashbam on Orphans

The Rashbam articulates this reasoning precisely. His concern is that one might have thought the rented cow does not qualify as automatic increase — after all, other people are exerting effort in connection with the animal. He answers: since the *yetomim* themselves do not plow, toil, or bear any costs in connection with it, and crucially since they do not pay for its feed (*d'ein ma'alin li-mezonot*), the increase is reckoned as *shivcha di-meimeila* from their perspective. It is this absence of investment on the part of the heirs that determines whether an increase qualifies, and wherever that criterion is met, the *bechor* takes double.

## Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

### Attribution to Rabbi

The Gemara now asks: whose position does this *baraita* represent? The answer is *Rabbi hi* — it is Rabbi's position. A further *baraita* makes the dispute explicit.

### Natural = Possessed

The *baraita* reads: the *bechor* does not receive double in appreciation that occurred in the estate after the father's death — this is the view of the *rabbanan*. Rabbi says: I maintain that the *bechor* does receive double in appreciation that occurred after the father's death, provided the appreciation came about naturally and not through the effort and investment of the heirs (*shivchu yetomim*). For Rabbi, natural increase is deemed as though it was already under the father's dominion, and the *bechor* therefore receives double of it. The *rabbanan* hold that only what was fully and actually in the father's possession at the time of death transfers with the double portion.

## Debts & Liens

### Debt Owed In

Among the cases listed is the following: if the heirs inherited a *shtar chov* — a debt instrument, money owed to the estate — the *bechor* takes double. This is presented as a case alongside the natural appreciation cases, and it invites examination of which authority it follows and what the underlying conceptual framework is.

### Accounts Receivable

The conceptual question is whether a debt owed to the estate is considered *muchzak* — already in the father's possession — or *rauy*, merely destined to come. On one reading, money that the father lent out is entirely out of his control at the time of death: the borrower holds it, and repayment is contingent on future events. On this reading, it seems squarely *rauy*. On another reading, however, since the father extended credit and holds a legal claim — he shipped goods, he performed services, he made a loan memorialized in a document — the receivable is very much a part of his estate. The principle *shtar chov ha-omed ligbot k'gavuy dami*, that a debt standing ready for collection is treated as though already collected, intersects directly with this question, even though the Gemara does not invoke it explicitly at this stage.

Rabbi's position, as will emerge, is that the debt — including interest owed by a non-Jew — is treated as part of the estate because it accrues automatically. The *rabbanan* dispute this, though with some internal nuance that will be addressed below.

### Debt Owed Out

The *baraita* also addresses the inverse: if the estate owes a debt, the *bechor* pays double his proportional share of that creditor's claim. The creditor holds a lien against the real estate, and since the *bechor* receives two-thirds of the real estate, two-thirds of the creditor's claim falls on him and one-third on the other brother. Whether the creditor chooses to collect the full amount from one heir or distributes his collection across both, the proportional liability is structurally tied to the proportional inheritance, and the *bechor*'s larger share carries a correspondingly larger burden.

### Waiving Double

Here the Gemara introduces an important *chiddush*: if the *bechor* says *eini noten ve-eini notel* — I will neither pay the extra portion nor receive it — he is permitted to do so. This option is unique to the double portion. A person cannot waive his basic share of an inheritance: *yerusha* descends automatically, and no *kinyan* or declaration, whether before or after the parent's death, can prevent an heir from receiving his statutory portion. Once received, he may give it away, but he cannot block himself from receiving it. The double portion of the *bechor* is different, because the verse says *latet lo pi shnayim* — "to give him double" — which the Gemara reads as conferring a *matana*, a gift. Just as one cannot be compelled to accept a gift against one's will, the *bechor* can decline his extra portion.

What would motivate a *bechor* to waive a benefit? The most obvious case is when the estate's debts equal or exceed its value. If the creditor is going to take everything anyway, the *bechor* gains nothing from accepting the double portion and immediately surrendering it to satisfy the lien. One might ask: since the creditor will pursue all the heirs regardless, what practical difference does the waiver make? Tosafot addresses this: if, for example, the other brothers are minors against whom the creditor cannot presently collect, the *bechor*'s waiver rolls his additional portion back into the general estate and effectively places it behind the shield of his minor siblings' legal protection. The creditor can still pursue the adult *bechor* for his basic share, but the extra amount, now absorbed into the general estate, cannot immediately be seized. Eventually the creditor may collect it when the minors come of age, but there is always the chance that circumstances change in the interim.

### Rabbeinu Tam View

Rabbeinu Tam offers an alternative reading of this case. He suggests that the *bechor*'s waiver is not directed at a debt against the estate but at a personal debt the *bechor* himself owes to a third party. Suppose the *bechor* personally owes a large sum. He knows that upon receiving his double portion, his creditor will immediately seize it. By declaring *eini notel*, he declines the extra inheritance so that it flows instead to his brothers — beyond the reach of his creditor. He still owes the money personally, but since he never received the extra portion, his creditor may never be able to collect. It is, as Rabbeinu Tam's reading implies, an early form of asset protection.

## Rabbi vs. Rabbanan (II)

### Rabbanan Limit

The Gemara now articulates the *rabbanan*'s principle directly. Their position is that only what was fully in the father's actual possession transfers with the double portion. The post-mortem increase, however natural, has not been in the father's domain.

### Verse Interpretations

The *machloket* turns on competing readings of the Torah's language. The *rabbanan* read *latet lo pi shnayim* — the double portion is described as something "given" — and derive from this that it functions like a *matana*. Just as one cannot give a gift of something one does not yet possess, the father cannot transmit a double portion in something that had not come into his hands before his death. Whatever increases after death is new, and the *bechor* has no prior claim to double of it.

Rabbi reads the verse differently. The Torah equates the *chelek bechorah* — the firstborn's extra portion — with the *chelek pashut*, the normal portion. Just as all heirs receive increases in the estate even if those increases only materialized after the father's death (since no one argues that the other sons lose the benefit of natural appreciation), so too the *bechor* continues to receive double of natural appreciation. The extra portion tracks the normal portion in this regard.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: do not the *rabbanan* also see the verse comparing the extra portion to the normal one? They do, but they interpret that comparison more narrowly — it teaches only that the *bechor*'s two portions must be physically contiguous: he receives one larger, unified plot rather than one portion in the north of a field and another in the south, with his brother's portion in between.

Conversely, does not Rabbi also contend with *latet lo*, the language of gift? He does, but he reads that phrase as establishing only the rule that the *bechor* may waive his extra portion, since gifts cannot be forced on an unwilling recipient. The comparison to a *matana* does not, for Rabbi, carry the implication that the property must have been fully possessed by the father. Everyone agrees that the *bechor* can waive the extra portion, and everyone agrees that the two portions must be adjacent. The sole dispute is whether natural increase qualifies as already possessed.

## Rav Pappa Refinement

### Agreed Natural Cases

Rav Pappa seeks to narrow the scope of the dispute by identifying cases where all opinions agree. If a palm tree grew thicker, or if land became enriched through natural flooding or soil improvement, all agree that the *bechor* takes double. The object is recognizably the same thing — only larger, only richer. This is considered so closely continuous with what the father possessed that even the *rabbanan* treat it as part of the original estate.

### Object Transformation

The dispute, Rav Pappa explains, arises in cases of genuine transformation: *shachas* — young shoots just emerging from the ground — that have become stalks of grain, or small saplings that have grown into full date palms. Physically, the growth is entirely natural and required no human intervention. But the entity has changed its essential character: it is now called something different and functions as something different.

### Transform Dispute

On this point Rabbi and the *rabbanan* divide. Rabbi maintains that even transformation is natural growth — *shivcha di-meimeila* — and since it happened without any investment by the heirs, it is treated as though the father had already possessed the resultant object. The *rabbanan* hold that because the object has genuinely changed its identity, what now exists is in effect a new object, not merely an improved version of the old one. The father never possessed this new object, and therefore the *bechor* cannot take double of it.

## Psak Halacha Authori (II)

### Both Legitimate

Rabba bar bar Chana quotes Rabbi Chiyya: *asa k'divrei Rabbi asa, k'divrei chachamim asa* — if a judge ruled like Rabbi, he has acted legitimately; if he ruled like the sages, he has also acted legitimately. There is no established *psak* that definitively resolves the dispute, and a *dayan* is free to adjudicate either way. This freedom is meaningful precisely because there is a threshold beyond which a position is considered settled *halacha*, and a judge who departs from it would be *to'eh bi-dvar mishna*, rendering a reversible error. Here, both positions remain within the range of legitimate adjudication.

### Halacha Ke-Rabbi Rules

This prompts the Gemara to note a broader unresolved question: is it uncertain whether the general principle is *halacha ke-Rabbi mechaveiro velo mechaveirav* — we rule like Rabbi when he disagrees with a single colleague, but not when he disagrees with many — or whether the rule is that we follow Rabbi even against multiple colleagues. Since this general principle is itself unresolved, it is unclear whether the majority position of the *rabbanan* in this dispute carries decisive weight against Rabbi, and therefore both rulings remain viable.

### Rav Nachman Rejects

Rav Nachman citing Rav goes further: *assur la'asot k'divrei Rabbi* — one is not permitted to rule like Rabbi. The Rashbam explains that this is stronger than merely saying the *halacha* follows the *chachamim*; it implies that Rabbi's position is actually rejected. Accordingly, if a judge did rule that way, the judgment would potentially need to be reversed. This reflects the view that *halacha ke-Rabbi mechaveiro velo mechaveirav* — Rabbi's ruling authority holds only against a single disputant, not against the majority.

### Rav Nachman Permits

Rav Nachman in his own name, however, states: *mutar la'asot k'divrei Rabbi* — one is permitted to rule like Rabbi. The Gemara interprets this as equivalent to saying one is obligated to follow Rabbi's position, understanding *mutar* in its strong sense: the *halacha* follows Rabbi even against multiple colleagues, and ruling like the *chachamim* would be the error. This is a striking reading of the word *mutar*, and the Gemara acknowledges the interpretive tension it creates.

## Books of Beit Rav

### Midrashei Halacha ID

At this juncture the Gemara notes that Rav Nachman taught from *sh'ar sifrei d'vei Rav* — the other books of the house of Rav. The occasion to explain this phrase arises naturally, since the upcoming *baraita* will be a *drash* on a *pasuk* in Devarim and thus derives from the *Sifrei*.

The *midrashei halacha* are the halakhic midrashim on the four books of the Torah following Bereishit. The *Mechilta* — meaning "collection" — covers Shemos. The *Sifra*, meaning "the book," covers Vayikra and is the earliest and most extensively developed of the *midrashei halacha*; indeed, there is a scholarly argument that it was the first *Torah she-be'al peh* ever committed to writing. The *Sifrei* covers Bamidbar and Devarim, and editions of the *Sifrei* itself sometimes carry the label *Sifrei d'vei Rav* on their title pages. The *Mechilta* is also included in *sh'ar sifrei d'vei Rav* — the remaining books — while the *Sifra* is *the* book, the primary reference, and the others are secondary.

---

## Psak Halacha Authori (II)

### Rava: Prohibited

Rava stakes out a position between the extremes: *assur la'asot k'divrei Rabbi* — it is prohibited to rule like Rabbi — *aval im asa, asuy* — but if one did so rule, the judgment stands. The ruling is not the preferred *psak*, but it is not so far outside the range of legitimate positions that we would overturn it. The Gemara explains that this reflects the view that we incline toward the *chachamim* as a default, but we do not entirely reject Rabbi's position. One might say that this represents a middle path: not the open-ended latitude of Rabba bar bar Chana, not the full endorsement of Rav Nachman in his own name, but a nuanced ruling that treats the *chachamim* as the preferred authority while leaving Rabbi's ruling within the bounds of non-reversible adjudication.

---

## Books of Beit Rav (II)

### Rashbam Sources

The Rashbam provides a precise bibliographical note. *Sifrei d'vei Rav* refers to the *Torat Kohanim* — the *Sifra*, the *baraita* of Vayikra. *Sh'ar sifrei d'vei Rav* refers to the *midrash* on Bamidbar from *Va-yehi bi-nso'a ha-machaneh* through the end of that book, the *midrash* on Devarim (*Ve-eileh ha-devarim* through the end), and the *Mechilta* covering Shemos from *Ha-chodesh ha-zeh lachem* through the end. All three of these are called *sh'ar sifrei d'vei Rav*.

The Rashbam then adds the famous principle: the anonymous voice in the *Mishna* (*stam Matnitin*) is Rabbi Meir; the anonymous voice in the *Sifra* is Rabbi Yehuda; the anonymous voice in the *Sifrei* is Rabbi Shimon — that is, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai; the anonymous voice in *Seder Olam* is Rabbi Yose; and the anonymous voice in the *Tosefta* is Rabbi Nechemia. All of them follow the line of Rabbi Akiva, as all these sages were students of Rabbi Akiva.

### Debei Rav Meaning

As for the meaning of *d'vei Rav* in this context, early authorities disagree. The Rambam, in the *Mishneh Torah*, understands it as referring to the school of Rav the *amora*, who was responsible for the final editing of these volumes — meaning that even though the content derives from *tannaim*, the compilation as we have it was redacted under Rav's supervision. Rashi in Chullin interprets it differently: *d'vei Rav* means "the study hall," and the expression denotes the *midrash* that was universally familiar in the great academies.

## Debt Owed Analysis

### Debt Braita

The Gemara cites a *baraita* taught by Rami bar Chama from the *Sifrei* on the verse *be-chol asher yimatzei lo* — "with everything that is found belonging to him." The verse is read to exclude an increase in value that occurred after the father's death: the *bechor* takes double only in what was found in the father's possession at the time of death. This exclusion applies *a fortiori* to increases caused by the heirs' own efforts and investment, in which the *bechor* certainly does not receive double. The Gemara identifies this *baraita* as reflecting the position of the *rabbanan*, not Rabbi.

### No Double Position

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states: *ein bechor notel pi shnayim bi-milveh* — the *bechor* does not take double of a loan. The Gemara immediately asks: according to whom? If this is according to the *rabbanan*, the statement is so obvious as to need no teaching — the *rabbanan* already deny the *bechor* double even in natural appreciation of property that was physically within the estate's domain. How much more obviously would they deny double in a loan, where the money was completely outside the estate's control? The statement must therefore be directed at Rabbi's position — it comes to tell us that even according to Rabbi, a loan does not qualify as *shivcha di-meimeila*.

### Attribution Resolved

But the Gemara immediately challenges this: we have a *baraita* stating that if the heirs inherit a *shtar chov*, the *bechor* takes double — both of the principal (*milveh*) and of the interest (*ribis*). This *baraita* clearly contemplates that a debt owed to the estate, including interest accrued on a loan to a non-Jew, is fully subject to the double portion. That *baraita* must be Rabbi's view. So now the statement of Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name — that the *bechor* does not take double of a loan — cannot be according to Rabbi after all, since Rabbi appears to say the opposite. The Gemara resolves: the *baraita* allowing double on the loan is indeed Rabbi's view; the statement of Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name is *l'olam rabbanan* — in fact it does represent the *rabbanan*, and it is not as obvious as the Gemara initially supposed.

### Rabbi on Loans

The explanation is that Rabbi treats money owed to the estate — both principal and interest from a non-Jewish borrower — exactly as he treats rental income or the natural birth of a calf: it accrues automatically, without any effort by the heirs, and therefore it is treated as *shivcha di-meimeila*, as though it were already in the father's domain. The double portion applies. Interest on a loan to a non-Jew, which accumulates without requiring any action from the estate, is on the same footing as money from a rental property and is included in the *bechor*'s double.

### Middle Position

A report from Eretz Yisrael — *shalchu mi-tam* — introduces a middle position: *bechor notel pi shnayim bi-milveh aval lo bi-ribis* — the *bechor* takes double of the loan principal but not of the interest. The Gemara again asks: according to whom? If this is the *rabbanan*, we just established that the *rabbanan* deny double even on the principal. If it is Rabbi, we just said Rabbi grants double on both principal and interest.

### Rabbanan Split

The Gemara resolves: this is in fact the *rabbanan*'s position — but it reflects one version of what the *rabbanan* hold. There is an internal dispute within the *rabbanan* about the status of the principal of a loan memorialized in a document. One view — the position of *shalchu mi-tam* — holds that since the creditor possesses the *shtar* and holds liens on the debtor's property, the debt is sufficiently concrete and already-claimed that it counts as part of the estate: *milveh keivan d'nakit shetara k'man d'gavya dami*, once you hold the document it is as though you have already collected. On this view the *rabbanan* grant double of the principal. The interest, however, is genuinely new money that accrued after the father's death with no analogous anchor in a document, and the *rabbanan* deny double of it. The other version of the *rabbanan* — the position stated by Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name — holds that even the document is not enough, and neither the principal nor the interest is considered part of the estate for purposes of the *bechor*'s double.

The structure is thus: Rabbi holds that both principal and interest are part of the estate. The *rabbanan* divide — one view grants the principal alone, treating the *shtar* and its attendant liens as sufficient to make the debt already-possessed; another view denies double even of the principal, holding that money outside the father's physical control is simply not part of his estate.
