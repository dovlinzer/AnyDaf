# Sanhedrin 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten Tribes Debate

### Akiva: No Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים אֵינָן עֲתִידִין לַחֲזוֹר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּשְׁלִכֵם אֶל אֶרֶץ אַחֶרֶת כַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה״. מָה הַיּוֹם הוֹלֵךְ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר – אַף הֵם הוֹלְכִים וְאֵינָן חוֹזְרִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The ten tribes are not destined to return** to Eretz Yisrael, even during the messianic era, **as it is stated: "And He cast them into another land, as it is this day"** (Deuteronomy 29:27). **Just as the day passes never to return, so too,** the ten tribes **go** into exile **and do not return;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The daf opens on 111b at the *mishna* in the middle of the page, continuing the extended discussion of who does and does not receive a portion in the *olam haba*. The *mishna* now turns to the ten tribes who were sent into exile.

The *drasha* proceeds from the verse *vayashlichem el eretz acheret kayom hazeh* — "and He cast them into a different land as this very day." As Rashi notes, the reference is specifically to the ten tribes because they were all cast out at once to a single location. The *drasha* fastens on the phrase *kayom hazeh*: just as a day goes and evaporates and does not come back, so too they will go and not return — *divrei Rabbi Akiva*.

The language here is striking. The word *chozrim* suggests that the question is not primarily about whether the ten tribes have a portion in the *olam haba*, but rather: will there ever again be twelve tribes of Israel? Will those ten tribes, in the era of *yemos hamashiach* or *olam haba* — however that is understood — be identified, located, and gathered back, so that Israel is once again constituted as twelve tribes? Rabbi Akiva appears to say: no. This is a remarkable position as a vision of the national future, all the more so given that a number of *halachot* — such as *yovel* and *shemita* — cannot apply without all twelve tribes present. Rashi, it should be noted, oscillates between reading this as concerning the descendants returning to the land of Israel or the original generation itself.

### Eliezer: Day Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״כַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה״ – מָה יוֹם מַאֲפִיל וּמֵאִיר, אַף עֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים שֶׁאֲפֵילָה לָהֶן כָּךְ עֲתִידָה לְהָאִיר לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: "As it is this day,"** meaning **just as the day darkens and** then the sky **brightens** the next day, with regard to **the ten tribes as well,** although **it is dark for them** now, **so it is destined to brighten for them.**

### Brayta: Olam HaBa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתְּשֵׁם ה׳ מֵעַל אַדְמָתָם בְּאַף וּבְחֵמָה וּבְקֶצֶף גָּדוֹל״. ״וַיִּתְּשֵׁם ה׳ מֵעַל אַדְמָתָם״ – בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיַּשְׁלִכֵם אֶל אֶרֶץ אַחֶרֶת״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 13:12): **The ten tribes have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the Lord rooted them out of their land in anger, and in wrath, and in great indignation;** and He cast them into another land, as it is this day" (Deuteronomy 29:27). **"And the Lord rooted them out of their land"** indicates **in this world; "and cast them into another land"** indicates **for the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

A *brayta* now enters, and crucially, it does not use the language of *lachzor* — of returning — but instead frames the question in terms of *olam haba* directly:

The *brayta* reads the same verse in two parts: *vayitashem Hashem me'al admatam* — He uprooted them from their land *ba'olam hazeh*, in this world — and *vayashlichem el eretz acheret* — He cast them to a different place *le'olam haba*, in the future world. Here the focus shifts to the people themselves, the generation that was exiled, and whether they will be resurrected. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva in the *brayta* as well.

### Shimon: Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם כַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה – אֵינָן חוֹזְרִין, וְאִם לָאו – חוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda of the village of Akko says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: If their actions** continue to be **"as it is this day"** and they continue to sin, **they do not return** to Eretz Yisrael. **And if not,** and they repent, **they return** to Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, citing Rabbi Shimon, introduces a conditional framework and returns to the *chozer* language:

The use of *chozer* here is telling, since it implies that the discussion is about their descendants, who are the ones capable of changing their behavior. The people who died are dead; their actions cannot change.

### Rebbi: Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בָּאִים הֵם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִתָּקַע בְּשׁוֹפָר גָּדוֹל וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The members of the ten tribes **come to the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And it shall come to pass on that day, that a great shofar will be sounded,** and they shall come who were lost in the land of Assyria and who were dispersed in the land of Egypt, and they shall worship the Lord at the holy mountain in Jerusalem" (Isaiah 27:13).**

The *brayta* then returns to the *olam haba* language with the position of Rabbi Judah the Prince:

His proof-text is the verse *vehaya bayom hahu yitaka bashofar gadol uva'u ha'ovdim be'eretz ashur* — those exiled to the land of Assyria, which was indeed Sennacherib's exile of the ten tribes, will come back. One might note that even this verse appears to speak of the descendants returning rather than the original generation being resurrected, leaving the precise contours of the debate — whether it concerns *teshiyat hameitim* or the ingathering of the exiles — genuinely ambiguous throughout.

### Rabba's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שַׁבְקַהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לַחֲסִידוּתֵיהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָלֹךְ וְקָרָאתָ בְּאׇזְנֵי יְרוּשָׁלִַים לֵאמֹר זָכַרְתִּי לָךְ חֶסֶד נְעוּרַיִךְ אַהֲבַת כְּלוּלוֹתָיִךְ לֶכְתֵּךָ אַחֲרַי בַּמִּדְבָּר בְּאֶרֶץ לֹא זְרוּעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Akiva abandoned his piety** and uncharacteristically interpreted the verses harshly, **as it is stated:** with regard to the generation of the wilderness: **"Go and cry in the ears of Jerusalem saying, so says the Lord:** I remember for you the affection of your youth, the love of your espousals, how you went after Me in the wilderness, in a land that was not sown"** (Jeremiah 2:2).

Rabba bar bar Chana, citing Rabbi Yochanan, now delivers a sharp evaluation:

Just as was said before regarding the *dor hamidbar*, Rabbi Akiva here too — in denying the ten tribes any return or portion in the *olam haba* — forgot his compassionate side, notwithstanding that *psukim* were available to support their eventual return. The proof-text invoked is the verse in Yirmiyahu: *shuva meshiva Yisrael* — return, O faithless Israel — and the preceding charge to call out *lo ator le'olam*, I will not hold enmity forever. God declared that He would not begrudge it forever and would bring them back — precisely the *chesed* that Rabbi Akiva, in his interpretation, chose to overlook.

## Wicked & Children

### Children Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חֲסִידוּתֵיהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: קְטַנֵּי בְּנֵי רִשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הִנֵּה הַיּוֹם בָּא בֹּעֵר כַּתַּנּוּר וְהָיוּ כׇל זֵדִים וְכׇל עֹשֵׂה רִשְׁעָה קַשׁ וְלִהַט אֹתָם הַיּוֹם הַבָּא אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אֲשֶׁר לֹא יַעֲזֹב לָהֶם שֹׁרֶשׁ וְעָנָף״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the** characteristic **piety of** Rabbi Akiva mentioned above? The Gemara answers: It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The minor children of the wicked of the Jewish people,** who died with neither their own virtue nor the virtue of their parents, **do not come into the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "For behold the day is coming; it burns like a furnace, and all the arrogant and all who do wickedly shall be straw; and the day that is coming shall burn them up, says the Lord of hosts, so that it will leave them neither root nor branch"** (Malachi 3:19).

The *gemara* now asks: where do we see that Rabbi Akiva ever *did* show such compassion in matters relating to the *olam haba*? The answer comes from a *brayta*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֹׁרֶשׁ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וְעָנָף״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא; דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** "Root" and "branch" are referring to descendants; they will have neither **a root in this world** nor **a branch in the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabban Gamliel.**

The proof-text is the verse in Malachi: *ki hineh hayom ba boer katanur* — a day burning as a furnace — *vehayu kol zeidim vechol osei rish'ah kash* — all of the wicked will be like stubble — and *asher lo ya'azov lahem shoresh ve'anaf* — no root and no branch will be left to them. *Shoresh ba'olam hazeh* — they will have no root in this world — *ve'anaf le'olam haba* — and no branch in the world to come.

The theological difficulty is obvious and the *gemara* acknowledges it directly: because someone is a *rasha*, their child will have no *olam haba*? The Torah itself states *lo yumtu avot al banim uvanim lo yumtu al avot* — fathers shall not be put to death for sons, nor sons for fathers. The general resolution is that this refers to children who died while still young — perhaps understood, in some sense, as an extension of their parents' punishment — a concept that has roots in *Tanach* and the *gemara* even though it sits in tension with the principle of individual accountability. Rashi formulates it precisely: these are the minor children of the *resha'im* who were themselves young and had not sinned — the debate is about them.

One should pause here to note something significant. Until this point the discussion has concerned very specific categories of people who lose their portion in the *olam haba* — those who say *ein Torah min hashamayim*, those who deny resurrection, and so forth. Now the *gemara* reverts to a default assumption that all *resha'im* in general lack a portion in the *olam haba*, which is in fact the more common theological posture throughout the *gemara*. Not only do the *resha'im* themselves lack a portion, but, according to the opening position in this *brayta* attributed to Rabban Gamliel, even their minor children do not.

### Akiva Compassion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: בָּאִים הֵם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שֹׁמֵר פְּתָאִים ה׳״, שֶׁכֵּן קוֹרִין בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם לְיָנוֹקָא פַּתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: They come into the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "The Lord preserves the simple [*peta'im*]"** (Psalms 116:6), **as in the cities overseas** the residents **call a child *patya*.**

It is precisely here that Rabbi Akiva shows the compassion that Rabba bar bar Chana praised:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״גֹּדּוּ אִילָנָא וְחַבְּלוּהִי בְּרַם עִקַּר שׇׁרְשׁוֹהִי בְּאַרְעָא שְׁבֻקוּ״. וְאֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״לֹא יַעֲזֹב לָהֶם שֹׁרֶשׁ וְעָנָף״? שֶׁלֹּא יַנִּיחַ לָהֶם לֹא מִצְוָה וְלֹא שִׁיּוּרֵי מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the verse **states** with regard to Nebuchadnezzar: **"Hew down the tree and destroy it; yet leave the stump of its roots in the earth"** (Daniel 4:20). If that is the case with regard to Nebuchadnezzar, all the more so it is the case with regard to the wicked people of Israel. **But how do I realize** the meaning of the phrase: **"So that it will leave them neither root nor branch"?** It means that God **will not leave for** the wicked **a mitzva nor the remnants of a mitzva** for which they will be rewarded.

His proof-text is the verse *shomer peta'im Hashem* — God watches the *peta'im*, which Rabbi Akiva reads here as referring to the children, noting that in the islands they call a child *pasya*. Additionally, the verse *godu ilana vechabluhi, beram ikar shoreshohi be'ar'a shevoku* — chop down the tree and destroy it, but leave the root in the ground — teaches that even if the wicked person is cut down, the children will be left to grow, or at least to be resurrected in *olam haba*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״שֹׁרֶשׁ״ – זוֹ נְשָׁמָה, ״וְעָנָף״ – זֶה הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively, "root"; this** is referring to the **soul. "Nor branch"; this** is referring to **the body.**

Rabbi Akiva then reinterprets the verse about *shoresh ve'anaf*: *shelo yanich lahem lo mitzvah velo shiyurei mitzvah* — that God will not leave the *resha'im* even the remnant of a *mitzvah* unrewarded; God will pay them fully for whatever good they did, so that they may be punished fully in *olam haba* with nothing held back. An alternative reading: *shoresh zu neshamah ve'anaf zu haguf* — the root is the soul and the branch is the body — neither will remain, meaning they will not be resurrected. But on the question of the children's fate, Rabbi Akiva rules that they do receive *olam haba*.

### Jew vs. Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קְטַנֵּי בְּנֵי רִשְׁעֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, אֵין בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וּתְּאַבֵּד כׇּל זֵכֶר לָמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **But everyone agrees** that **the minor children of the wicked of the nations of the world will not come into the World-to-Come,** as they have no virtue at all. **And Rabban Gamliel derives** this matter concerning those children **from** the verse: **"And you have caused all their memory to perish"** (Isaiah 26:14).

The *gemara* then draws a distinction. The debate above concerned *katnei rishay Yisrael* — the minor children of wicked Jews. But regarding *katnei bnei rishay ovdei kochavim* — the minor children of wicked non-Jews — *divrei hakol*, everyone agrees: *ein ba'im le'olam haba*, they do not receive a portion in the *olam haba*. Not only do the wicked non-Jewish parents not receive a portion, but their children do not either, derived from the verse *ve'tavad kol zakhar lamo* — all remnant of them will be destroyed.

What this passage implicitly confirms — significant as a background assumption — is that *chasidei umot ha'olam* do have a portion in the *olam haba*, a position debated in the *Tosefta* and elsewhere but assumed here as the baseline that makes the negative ruling about the children of the *resha'im* meaningful by contrast.

## Child's Merit

### Birth vs. Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: קָטָן, מֵאֵימָתַי בָּא לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא? רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי – חַד אָמַר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלַד, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁסִּיפֵּר. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנּוֹלַד – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָבֹאוּ וְיַגִּידוּ צִדְקָתוֹ לְעַם נוֹלָד כִּי עָשָׂה״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁסִּיפֵּר – דִּכְתִיב: ״זֶרַע יַעַבְדֶנּוּ יְסֻפַּר לַה׳ לַדּוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to **a minor** who dies, **from when,** i.e., from what stage, **does he come into the World-to-Come?** There is a dispute between **Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **One** of them **says: From the moment that he is born, and** the other **one says: From the moment that** he begins to **talk.** The Gemara elaborates: **The one who says: From the moment that he is born,** derives it from **that** which **is stated: "They shall come and shall declare His righteousness to a people that shall be born, that He has done this"** (Psalms 22:32). **And the one who says: From the moment that** he begins to **talk,** derives it from **that** which **is written: "Their seed shall serve Him; it shall be told of the Lord to the coming generation"** (Psalms 22:31), as from the moment he talks, he belongs to God.

The *gemara* now moves to a distinct but related question: at what stage does a child acquire the *olam haba*? Assuming, as Rabbi Akiva holds, that the children of the *resha'im* do receive a portion, and applying this universally to all children — at what point does the soul develop to the stage where, if the child dies, there will be a resurrection?

*Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi* dispute this. One says: from the very moment of birth — *mishaa shenolad* — deriving it from the verse *yavou v'yagidu tzidkato l'am nolad* — they will declare God's righteousness to the people that has been born. The other says: from the time he begins to speak — *mishaa shesiper* — derived from *zera ya'avdenu yesupar la'Hashem lador* — the seed that worships Him, that will speak for the generations, meaning the ability to speak is the threshold.

### Ravina: Conception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: רָבִינָא אָמַר, מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנִּזְרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״זֶרַע יַעַבְדֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that additional *amora'im* expressed opinions about the same matter. **Ravina says: From the moment that** the child **is conceived,** even before his birth, he can enter the World-to-Come, **as it is written: "Their seed shall serve Him"** (Psalms 22:31); even as seed the child merits to enter the World-to-Come.

Ravina goes further and says: from the moment of conception — *mishaa shenizra* — derived from *zera ya'avdenu*, the seed will worship You. The *gemara* notes this is interesting but cautions against drawing *halachic* implications about when Jewish law regards something as a life. These are distinct questions: when does a soul enter; when will it receive *olam haba*; when is something considered a life for purposes of *halacha* — and they are not to be conflated.

### Nachman: Brit Milah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר, מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנִּימּוֹל, דִּכְתִיב: ״עָנִי אֲנִי וְגֹוֵעַ מִנֹּעַר נָשָׂאתִי אֵמֶיךָ אָפוּנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: From the moment that he is circumcised, as it is written: "I am poor and close to death from youth; I suffered your terrors [*emekha*], I am numb"** (Psalms 88:16). From the moment that one bears the mark that the Jewish people have due to fear of God [*eima*], he turns to God and he belongs to Him.

Nachman bar Yitzchak says: from the day of *brit milah* — *mishaa shenimol*. The proof-text is the verse *ani ani ve'goveia mi-noar nasati ematcha afunah* — I have carried Your fear from youth and it has come upon me — which Rashi understands as indicating that the circumcision performed out of fear of God is what makes the child's eventual death a "real" death, in the sense that the soul will receive *olam haba*. Admittedly, as the *gemara* acknowledges, the connection between that verse and *brit milah* is difficult to see clearly. This may also underlie the practice of performing *brit milah* on a boy who dies before his eighth day before burial, connected to the *midrash* that Avraham Avinu stands at the gates of *Gan Eden* and admits only male children who have undergone *brit milah*.

### Meir: Amen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״אָמֵן״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פִּתְחוּ שְׁעָרִים וְיָבֹא גוֹי צַדִּיק שֹׁמֵר אֱמֻנִים״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שֹׁמֵר אֱמֻנִים״, אֶלָּא ״שֶׁאוֹמֵר אָמֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir: From the moment that** the child **will say amen, as it is stated: "Open the gates, so that the righteous nation that keeps faithfulness may enter"** (Isaiah 26:2). **Do not read** the verse as **"that keeps faithfulness [*shomer emunim*]"; rather,** read it as: **That says amen [*she'omer amen*].** From the moment he says amen, the gates to the World-to-Come open.

A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Meir says: from the time he is able to say *Amen* — *mishaa yomar Amen*. The verse is *pitchu she'arim veyavo goy tzaddik shomer emunim* — open the gates, let the righteous nation, the keeper of faithfulness, enter. Read *al tikrei shomer emunim ela shomer amenim* — not "keeper of faithfulness" but "those who say *amen*" — indicating that the ability to make this affirmation is the religious threshold at which the soul earns a portion in the *olam haba*.

## Amen & Gehinnom

### Amen Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אָמֵן״? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״אֵל מֶלֶךְ נֶאֱמָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the term **amen? Rabbi Ḥanina says:** It is an acronym of the words: **God, faithful King [*El Melekh ne'eman*].**

The *gemara* asks: *mai Amen*? What is the significance of *Amen* as an affirmation? Rabbi Chanina explains: it is an acronym for *Kel Melech Ne'eman* — God is the trustworthy King — a kind of swearing by God, a calling of God as witness to affirm something.

### Lakish Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָכֵן הִרְחִיבָה שְּׁאוֹל נַפְשָׁהּ וּפָעֲרָה פִיהָ לִבְלִי חֹק״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לְמִי שֶׁמְּשַׁיֵּיר אֲפִילּוּ חוֹק אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: **"Therefore, the netherworld has enlarged itself and opened its mouth without measure [*livli ḥok*]"** (Isaiah 5:14), **Reish Lakish says:** It is referring to **one who leaves even one statute [*ḥok*]** unfulfilled; the netherworld expands for him.

The *gemara* then cites a verse: *lachen hirchiva she'ol nafshah ufara piha livli chok* — therefore *she'ol* has opened wide its soul and yawned open its mouth *livli chok*, without limit. Reish Lakish interprets: *l'mi shemeshayer afilu chok echad* — to the one who abandons even one *chok*, even one *mitzvah*. Thus the depths open for anyone who neglects even a single commandment.

### Yochanan Mercy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִיחָא לְמָרַיְיהוּ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לְהוּ הָכִי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לֹא לָמַד אֶלָּא חוֹק אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is not satisfactory to** God, **their Master, that you said this about them,** as according to Reish Lakish's opinion most of the Jewish people would be doomed to Gehenna. **Rather, even if one learned only one statute,** he has a share in the World-to-Come, and "*livli ḥok*" means one who has learned no statutes at all.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *lo nicha lemareihu de'amartu lehu hachi* — it is not pleasing to their Master, to God, that you have said this about God's creatures — that failing to perform even a single *mitzvah* consigns one to *Gehinnom*. Rather, Rabbi Yochanan says: *afilu lo lamad ella chok echad* — even one who learned only one law will be saved from *Gehinnom*. Only one who has performed *no* commandment at all will go to *Gehinnom*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר) ״וְהָיָה בְכׇל הָאָרֶץ נְאֻם ה׳ פִּי שְׁנַיִם בָּהּ יִכָּרְתוּ יִגְוָעוּ וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית יִוָּתֶר בָּהּ״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל שֵׁם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִיחָא לְמָרַיְיהוּ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לְהוּ הָכִי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל נֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **that** which **is stated: "And it shall come to pass that in all the land, says the Lord, two parts shall be excised and die, but the third shall remain in it"** (Zechariah 13:8), **Reish Lakish says:** "The third" means that only **one-third** of the descendants **of Shem,** son of Noah, will remain, and everyone else will die. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Reish Lakish: **It is not satisfactory to** God, **their Master, that you said this about them,** that the overwhelming majority of the world will be destroyed. **Rather, even** as many as **one-third** of the descendants **of Noah,** one-third of the population of the world, will remain.

The *gemara* continues with a verse about the future: *vehaya bechol ha'aretz neum Hashem pi shenayim bah yikareitu veyigva'u vehashlishit yivater bah* — two parts will be cut off and expire, and a third will remain. This is read as pertaining to the era of *Gog uMagog* or the future times. Reish Lakish interprets: *shlishi shel Shem* — this means a third of Shem, son of Noach, not a third of all humanity; and Rashi presses this even further to a third of a third of a third. Rabbi Yochanan again demurs: *lo nicha lemareihu de'amartu lehu hachi* — their Master does not appreciate your having decreed such a heavy judgment on His creatures. Rather: *afilu shlishi shel Noach* — just one third of Noach — meaning only two thirds of the world will be destroyed, not eight ninths. This is, it should be noted, the same recurring dynamic: Reish Lakish reads strictly, Rabbi Yochanan responds that such a reading is too severe a verdict for God's creatures.

### Kahana Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי אָנֹכִי בָּעַלְתִּי בָכֶם וְלָקַחְתִּי אֶתְכֶם אֶחָד מֵעִיר וּשְׁנַיִם מִמִּשְׁפָּחָה״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְמָרַיְיהוּ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לְהוּ הָכִי, אֶלָּא אֶחָד מֵעִיר מְזַכֶּה כָּל הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ, וּשְׁנַיִם מִמִּשְׁפָּחָה מְזַכִּין כָּל הַמִּשְׁפָּחָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"For I have taken you to Myself: And I will take out one of a city, and two of a family"** (Jeremiah 3:14), **Reish Lakish says:** The meaning of this **statement** is **as it is written,** that only individuals will be spared and the rest will be destroyed. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: It is not satisfactory to** God, **their Master, that you said this about them. Rather,** the merit of **one from the city causes the entire city** to **benefit, and** the merit of **two from a family causes the entire family** to **benefit** and be redeemed.

The verse *ki anochi ba'alti bachem velakachti etchem echad me'ir ushnayim mimishpacha* — I have taken you, one from a city and two from a family — indicates that only a tiny remnant will be saved in future times. Reish Lakish reads this literally: *devarim kichtavan*, only this tiny number. Rabbi Yochanan objects in the familiar formulation: it is not pleasing to their Master that you have said this. On the contrary: *echad me'ir mezakeh kol ha'ir* — one righteous person in an entire city confers merit on the whole city — and *shnayim mimishpacha mezakeh kol hamishpacha* — two from a family confer merit on the entire family.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְמָרַיְיהוּ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לְהוּ הָכִי, אֶלָּא אֶחָד מֵעִיר מְזַכֶּה כָּל הָעִיר, וּשְׁנַיִם מִמִּשְׁפָּחָה מְזַכִּין כָּל הַמִּשְׁפָּחָה.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, the Gemara relates that **Rav Kahana sat before Rav, and sat and said:** The meaning of **this statement** is **as it is written. Rav said to him: It is not satisfactory to** God, **their Master, that you said this about them. Rather,** the merit of **one from the city causes the entire city** to **benefit, and** the merit of **two from a family causes the entire family** to **benefit** and be redeemed.

The same exchange is then replayed between Rav and Rav Kahana. Rav Kahana sat before Rav and advanced the strict reading, *devarim kichtavan*. Rav responded with the identical formulation — *lo nicha lemareihu de'amartu lehu hachi* — it is not good for their Master that you said this about God's creatures, and echoed Rabbi Yochanan's more expansive interpretation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא חָיֵיף רֵישֵׁיהּ וְסָלֵיק וְיָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְלֹא תִמָּצֵא בְּאֶרֶץ הַחַיִּים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵילָט קָא לָיְיטַתְּ לִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קְרָא קָאָמֵינָא, לֹא תִּמָּצֵא תּוֹרָה בְּמִי שֶׁמְּחַיֶּה עַצְמוֹ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that Rav **saw that** Rav Kahana **was washing** the hair on **his head and** then **arose and sat before Rav.** Rav **said to** Rav Kahana: **"Nor shall it be found in the land of the living [*haḥayyim*]"** (Job 28:13). Rav Kahana thought that Rav addressed that verse to him and **he said to** Rav: **Are you cursing me?** Rav **said to him:** It is **a verse** that **I am saying** to remind you that **Torah will not be found in one who sustains [*meḥayye*] himself** in an indulgent manner **in its** study; rather, Torah is acquired through suffering and difficulty.

What follows is a brief and illuminating vignette. Rav saw Rav Kahana spending considerable time grooming and arranging his appearance before coming to sit in the *yeshiva*. Rav said to him: *velo timatzei be'eretz hachayim* — he will not be found in the land of the living. Rav Kahana was alarmed: *milet ka layet lei* — are you cursing me? Rav replied: *kra kamina* — I am simply reading a verse; I am not pronouncing a curse. The real point was this: *lo timatzei Torah bemi shemechayeh atzmo aleiha* — Torah will not be found in one who makes himself too comfortable in this world. *Mamit atzmo aleiha* — one must bring a quality of self-abnegation and privation to one's *Torah* study; that kind of comfortable, well-groomed approach to learning is not how one acquires genuine *kinyan* in *Torah*.

## Remnant & Redemption

### Simai Proportion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַב סִימַאי אוֹמֵר, נֶאֱמַר: ״וְלָקַחְתִּי אֶתְכֶם לִי לְעָם״, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״וְהֵבֵאתִי אֶתְכֶם״. מַקִּישׁ יְצִיאָתָן מִמִּצְרַיִם לְבִיאָתָן לָאָרֶץ. מָה בִּיאָתָן לָאָרֶץ – שְׁנַיִם מִשִּׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא, אַף יְצִיאָתָן מִמִּצְרַיִם – שְׁנַיִם מִשִּׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the few that are destined to be redeemed: **Rav Simai says** that **it is stated: "And I will take you to Me as a people"** (Exodus 6:7), **and** juxtaposed to that verse **it is stated: "And I will bring you** into the land" (Exodus 6:8). The Torah **compares their exodus from Egypt to their entry into the land; just as** during **their entry into the land** only **two of six hundred thousand** entered the land, as they all died in the wilderness except for Caleb and Joshua, **so too,** during **their exodus from Egypt,** in terms of the ratio, **only two of six hundred thousand** left Egypt and the rest died there.

A *tanna* teaches in the name of Rabbi Simai: the verse states *velakachti etchem li le'am* — I will take you for Me as a people — and then *veheveisi etchem* — I will bring you into the land. The *gemara* establishes a *hekesh*, a comparison, between the going out from Egypt and the entering into the land. When they entered the land, how many of the 600,000 who came out of Egypt went in? Two — Yehoshua and Kalev. So: *ma bi'asom la'aretz shenayim mishishim ribbo* — the entry into the land involved two out of every 600,000.

Applying the *hekesh* in reverse: *af yetziasam miMitzrayim shenayim mishishim ribbo* — when 600,000 came out of Egypt, those 600,000 represented only two out of every 600,000 who were left behind in Egypt. In other words, the proportion of those who actually emerged from Egypt relative to those who perished there was two to 600,000 — a staggering figure, and one that echoes the broader discussion here about what proportion of humanity survives into the future era.

### Rava Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְכֵן לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָנְתָה שָּׁמָּה כִּימֵי נְעוּרֶיהָ וּכְיוֹם עֲלוֹתָהּ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: And likewise,** that will be the situation **in the messianic era, as it is stated: "And she shall respond there, as in the days of her youth, and as in the day when she came up out of the land of Egypt"** (Hosea 2:17). The ultimate redemption and the exodus from Egypt are juxtaposed, indicating that in the messianic era too, only few will survive.

Rava draws the implication directly: *vechen limos haMashiach* — and the same proportions will obtain in the messianic era. Only two out of every 600,000 will remain. The proof-text is *ve'ansah shamah kimei ne'ureha vekiyom alosah me'eretz Mitzrayim* — she will respond there as in the days of her youth and as on the day she came up from Egypt — indicating that the ratio of survivors in the final redemption will mirror that of the Egyptian redemption. *Le'asid lavo*, the same calculus applies.

## Moses Egypt

### Elazar Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, פַּעַם אַחַת נִכְנַסְתִּי לַאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם. מָצָאתִי זָקֵן אֶחָד וְאָמַר לִי: בֹּא וְאַרְאֶךָּ מָה עָשׂוּ אֲבוֹתַי לַאֲבוֹתֶיךָ. מֵהֶם טִבְּעוּ בַּיָּם, מֵהֶם הָרְגוּ בַּחֶרֶב, מֵהֶם מָעֲכוּ בְּבִנְיָן. וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה נֶעֱנַשׁ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמֵאָז בָּאתִי אֶל פַּרְעֹה לְדַבֵּר בִּשְׁמֶךָ הֵרַע לָעָם הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, says: One time I entered Alexandria of Egypt. I found one old man and he said to me: Come and I will show you what my ancestors,** the Egyptians, **did to your ancestors,** the Jewish people. Some **of them they drowned in the sea,** some **of them they killed with the sword,** and **some of them they crushed in the buildings. And** it is **over this matter,** Moses' protest of the afflictions suffered by the Jewish people, that **Moses, our teacher, was punished, as it is stated: "For since I came to Pharaoh to speak in Your name, he has done evil to this people,** neither have You delivered Your people at all" (Exodus 5:23).

A *baraita* cites Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose, who recounts: once I went into Alexandria in Egypt and found a certain old man who said to me — *bo ve'arekha mah asu avosai la'avosayicha* — come, let me show you what my forefathers did to your forefathers. Some they drowned in the sea — alluding to *kol haben hayillod hayora tashlichuhu*, the decree to drown the Hebrew male infants. Some they killed by the sword. Some they crushed into the building structures during the enslavement.

And for this, says the *baraita*, Moshe Rabbeinu was punished — *ve'al davar zeh ne'enash Moshe Rabbeinu* — as the verse says: *ume'az basi el Pharaoh ledabber bishmecha hera la'am hazeh* — from the day I came to Pharaoh in Your name, he has done evil to this people. Moshe complained to God about all the suffering, and that complaint brought a punishment upon him.

### Patriarchs Faith

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: חֲבָל עַל דְּאָבְדִין וְלָא מִשְׁתַּכְחִין. הֲרֵי כַּמָּה פְּעָמִים נִגְלֵיתִי עַל אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב בְּאֵל שַׁדַּי, וְלֹא הִרְהֲרוּ עַל מִדּוֹתַי, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לִי ״מַה שְּׁמֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to** Moses: **Woe over those who are gone and are no** longer **found; as several times I revealed Myself to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as God Almighty [*El Shaddai*] and they did not question My attributes, and did not say to Me: What is Your name?**

God's response to Moshe is rendered as a kind of mourning for a lost generation: *chaval al de'avdin velo mishtachchin* — woe for those who were lost and can no longer be found. God tells Moshe: you are not like the previous generations. *Harei kamma pe'amim nigleisi el Avraham Yitzchak veYaakov be'El Shaddai* — many times I revealed Myself to Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov in the mode of *El Shaddai* — and *velo hirhiru middosai* — and they never questioned My ways, never challenged Me.

## Moses Egypt…

### Abraham's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לְאַבְרָהָם ״קוּם הִתְהַלֵּךְ בָּאָרֶץ לְאׇרְכָּהּ וּלְרׇחְבָּהּ כִּי לְךָ אֶתְּנֶנָּה״. בִּקֵּשׁ מָקוֹם לִקְבּוֹר אֶת שָׂרָה וְלֹא מָצָא, עַד שֶׁקָּנָה בְּאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שֶׁקֶל כֶּסֶף, וְלֹא הִרְהֵר עַל מִדּוֹתַי.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to Abraham: "Arise, walk through the land in the length of it and in the breadth of it; for unto you I will give it"** (Genesis 13:17). Ultimately, **he sought a place to bury Sarah and did not find** one **until he purchased** it **for four hundred silver shekels, and he did not question My attributes** and did not protest that I failed to fulfill My promise to give him the land.

God elaborates: I told Avraham *kum hishalech ba'aretz le'orkah ulerochbah ki lecha etnenah* — arise and walk through the land, for I will give it to you. Yet when the time came to bury Sarah, he found no place and had to purchase the cave at the high price of 400 *shekel* — *velo hirhir achar middosai* — and he never said: God, where is the promise? He accepted My judgment faithfully.

### Isaac Jacob

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לְיִצְחָק: ״גּוּר בָּאָרֶץ הַזֹּאת וְאֶהְיֶה עִמְּךָ וַאֲבָרְכֶךָּ״. בִּקְּשׁוּ עֲבָדָיו מַיִם לִשְׁתּוֹת וְלֹא מָצְאוּ, עַד שֶׁעָשׂוּ מְרִיבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּרִיבוּ רֹעֵי גְרָר עִם רֹעֵי יִצְחָק לֵאמֹר לָנוּ הַמָּיִם״. וְלֹא הִרְהֵר אַחַר מִדּוֹתַי.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to Isaac: "Sojourn in this land, and I will be with you and will bless you"** (Genesis 26:3). **His servants sought water to drink and they did not find** it **until they started a quarrel, as it is stated: "And the herdsmen of Gerar quarreled with Isaac's herdsmen saying: The water is ours"** (Genesis 26:20), **and he did not question My attributes.**

I told Yitzchak *gur ba'aretz hazos ve'ehyeh imcha va'avarekheka* — dwell in this land and I will be with you and bless you. Yet when his servants sought water they found none until they were forced into conflict — *vayyarivu ro'ei Gerar im ro'ei Yitzchak* — and Yitzchak never said: God, what about Your promise to be with me? He did not question My ways.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לְיַעֲקֹב: ״הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר אַתָּה שֹׁכֵב עָלֶיהָ לְךָ אֶתְּנֶנָּה״. בִּיקֵּשׁ מָקוֹם לִנְטוֹעַ אׇהֳלוֹ, וְלֹא מָצָא, עַד שֶׁקָּנָה בְּמֵאָה קְשִׂיטָה, וְלֹא הִרְהֵר אַחַר מִדּוֹתַי, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לִי: ״מַה שְּׁמֶךָ״, וְאַתָּה אָמְרַתְּ לִי: ״מַה שְּׁמֶךָ״ בַּתְּחִלָּה, וְעַכְשָׁיו אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לִי: ״וְהַצֵּל לֹא הִצַּלְתָּ אֶת עַמֶּךָ״. ״עַתָּה תִרְאֶה אֵת אֲשֶׁר אֶעֱשֶׂה לְפַרְעֹה״. בְּמִלְחֶמֶת פַּרְעֹה אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, וְאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בְּמִלְחֶמֶת שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאֶחָד מְלָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to Jacob: "The land upon which you lie, to you I will give it"** (Genesis 28:13). **He sought a place to pitch his tent and he did not find** one **until he purchased** it **for one hundred coins, and he did not question My attributes, and did not say to Me: What is Your name? And you,** Moses, **ask Me: What is Your name, initially,** after witnessing My greatness more than they ever did. **And now you say to Me: "Neither have You delivered Your people"** (Exodus 5:23). The verse then states: **"Now shall you see what I will do to Pharaoh"** (Exodus 6:1). One can infer: **The war with Pharaoh** and his downfall **you** shall **see, but you will not see the war with the thirty-one kings** in Eretz Yisrael, as you will not be privileged to conquer Eretz Yisrael for the Jewish people.

And I told Yaakov *ha'aretz asher ata shochev aleha lecha etnenah* — the land on which you lie I will give to you. Yet when he sought simply to pitch his tent, he had to purchase the site near Shechem for a hundred *kesitah* — *velo hirher achar midotai* — and he did not say: God, where is the promise You made? They trusted that the promise would be fulfilled. They did not ask to understand My name. *Velo amru li mah shemecha* — they never asked what My name was.

## God's Name

### Name Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְמַהֵר מֹשֶׁה וַיִּקֹּד אַרְצָה וַיִּשְׁתָּחוּ״. מָה רָאָה מֹשֶׁה?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: "And the Lord passed before him and proclaimed: The Lord, the Lord, compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in loving-kindness and truth, extending loving-kindness to thousands of generations…**and Moses made haste and bowed his head toward the earth and prostrated himself"** (Exodus 34:6–8), the Gemara asks: **What did Moses see** in these attributes that caused him to hastily prostrate himself?

God continues: *ve'ata amarta li mah shemecha betechila* — but you, Moshe, first demanded My name — *ve'achshav ata omer li vehatzel lo hitzalta et amecha* — and now you are telling Me that I have not saved Your people. This constitutes a powerful reading of the opening of *Parshat Va'eira*: Moshe asked for God's name, the Patriarchs never did; Moshe challenged God's conduct, the Patriarchs never did. God responds: *ve'ata tireh asher e'eseh lePharaoh* — now you will see what I do to Pharaoh.

### Thirty-One Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִלְחֶמֶת פַּרְעֹה אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, וְאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בְּמִלְחֶמֶת שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאֶחָד מְלָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** One can infer: **The war with Pharaoh** and his downfall **you** shall **see, but you will not see the war with the thirty-one kings** in Eretz Yisrael, as you will not be privileged to conquer Eretz Yisrael for the Jewish people.

*Bemilchemet Pharaoh ata ro'eh ve'i ata ro'eh bemilchemet shloshim ve'echad melachim* — you will see the battle against Pharaoh, but you will not see the battle of the thirty-one kings — that is, the conquest of Canaan — because of your lack of faith. Moshe will not enter the land of Israel. The *midrash* thus explains Moshe's punishment in terms of a contrast between his challenging God in a moment of crisis and the Patriarchs' unwavering trust across decades of unfulfilled promises.

What this has to do with the Alexandrian's account — and the statement that Moshe was punished — now becomes clear: the Egyptians committed atrocities, Moshe could not tolerate the suffering of his people and challenged God, and that challenge, however understandable it may seem, ultimately brought his punishment. The *gemara* then pivots to the revelation at *Har Sinai*, where God reveals Himself to Moshe in the *yud-gimel midot*, another episode of divine self-disclosure to Moshe.

## Thirteen Midot

### Erech Apayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלָא אָמַר: ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״ רָאָה, וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: ״אֱמֶת״ רָאָה. תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״ רָאָה, דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁעָלָה מֹשֶׁה לְמָרוֹם מְצָאוֹ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב וְכוֹתֵב ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם״ לַצַּדִּיקִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamla says: He saw** the attribute of **slow to anger; and the Rabbis say: He saw** the attribute of **truth. It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said: He saw** the attribute of **slow to anger, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **When Moses ascended on high, he discovered the Holy One, Blessed be He, sitting and writing: Slow to anger.** Moses **said before Him: Master of the Universe,** is Your attribute of **slow to anger** only to be used **for the righteous?**

The *gemara* cites a dispute: *mah ra'ah Moshe* — what did Moshe see that caused him to prostrate himself at the moment of the revelation of the *yud-gimel midot*? Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel says: *erech apayim ra'ah* — he saw the attribute of long-suffering, and was so moved by it that he bowed. The *rabbanan* say: *emet ra'ah* — he saw the attribute of truth, of *din*, and bowed to pray that God would not apply strict judgment against *Bnei Yisrael*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: ״אַף לָרְשָׁעִים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רְשָׁעִים יֹאבֵדוּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא חָזֵית (מַאי) דְּמִבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** God **said to him:** It is an attribute **even for the wicked.** Moses **said to Him: Let the wicked be doomed.** God **said to him: Now, you** will **see that you will need** this, as ultimately you will reconsider that statement.

The *gemara* then favors the view of Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, and brings a *tanna* teaching: when Moshe ascended on high, he found the Holy One sitting and writing *erech apayim*. Moshe said: Master of the universe — *erech apayim latzadikim* — let this attribute of long-suffering be only for the righteous. God responded: *af laresha'im* — even the wicked receive *erech apayim*. Moshe objected: *resha'im yovedu* — let the wicked be destroyed, why extend long-suffering to them? God replied: *hashta chazit mai demiba'ei lach* — now mark this, you will need it yourself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁחָטְאוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא כָּךְ אָמַרְתָּ לִי ״אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם לַצַּדִּיקִים״?
>
> **Translation:** **When the Jewish people sinned** in the sin of the spies and Moses asked God to forgive them, the Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to** Moses: **Didn't you say to Me** that the attribute of **slow to anger** is **for the righteous** alone? They are not worthy of atonement.

And indeed, when Israel sinned — in the *cheit ha'egel* — and Moshe came to pray for forgiveness, God reminded him: you yourself said that *erech apayim* should apply only to the *tzadikim*. Moshe replied: *Ribono shel olam, velo kach amarta li af laresha'im* — but then You told me it applies even to the wicked — and it is with that attribute that I now plead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, וְלֹא כָּךְ אָמַרְתָּ לִי: ״אַף לָרְשָׁעִים״? וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעַתָּה יִגְדַּל נָא כֹּחַ ה׳ כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתָּ לֵאמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** Moses **said before Him: Master of the Universe, and isn't this** what **You said to me:** It is an attribute **even for the wicked? And that is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And now, I pray You, let the power of my Lord be great, as You have spoken, saying"** (Numbers 14:17). Moses was repeating God's promise with regard to His employment of the attribute of slow to anger.

This is the meaning of the verse *ve'ata yigdal na koach Hashem ka'asher dibarta lemor* — let God's strength now be great, as You Yourself declared it — invoked by Moshe precisely to call God to the *erech apayim* that God had affirmed extends even to *resha'im*.

### Crown Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חַגָּא הֲוָה סָלֵיק וְאָזֵיל בְּדַרְגָּא דְּבֵי רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא. שַׁמְעֵיהּ לְהָהוּא יָנוֹקָא דְּאָמַר: ״עֵדֹתֶיךָ נֶאֶמְנוּ מְאֹד לְבֵיתְךָ נַאֲוָה קֹדֶשׁ ה׳ לְאֹרֶךְ יָמִים״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״תְּפִלָּה לְמֹשֶׁה וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם רָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ḥagga was walking up the stairs in the school of Rabba bar Sheila. He heard a certain child who said: "Your testimonies are very sure; sanctity becomes Your house, Lord, for the length of days"** (Psalms 93:5). The phrase "length of days" is a reference to the attribute of slow to anger. **And juxtaposed to it** is the phrase: **"A prayer of Moses"** (Psalms 90:1), indicating that this chapter was also stated by Moses our teacher. Upon hearing those verses, Rabbi Ḥagga **said: Conclude from it** that it was the attribute of **slow to anger** that Moses **saw.**

Rabbi Chaga, passing by the house of Rabbah bar Shila, heard a child reciting the verse *edotecha ne'emnu me'od leveitecha na'ava kodesh Hashem le'orech yamim* — Your testimonies are greatly faithful, for Your holy house, God, for all future times. He noted that this verse appears adjacent to *tefila leMoshe*, a prayer of Moshe, and read it as meaningful in context. The *le'orech yamim* of the verse perhaps evokes *erech apayim*, affirming that Moshe's prostration was indeed out of gratitude for the *mida* of *erech apayim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִהְיוֹת עֲטָרָה בְּרֹאשׁ כׇּל צַדִּיק וְצַדִּיק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיֶה ה׳ צְבָאוֹת לַעֲטֶרֶת צְבִי וְלִצְפִירַת תִּפְאָרָה לִשְׁאָר עַמּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, is destined to be a crown on the head of each and every righteous** person, **as it is stated: "In that day shall the Lord of hosts be for a crown of glory, and for a diadem of beauty, unto the remnant of His people"** (Isaiah 28:5).

Then: *atid HaKadosh Baruch Hu lihyot atarah be'rosh kol tzaddik ve'tzaddik* — in the future times, God will be a crown on the head of every righteous person — as the verse states *yihye Hashem tzevaot le'ateret tzevi u'le'tzfirat tiferah lish'ar amo* — a crown of glory and a tiara of beauty for the remnant of His people.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לַעֲטֶרֶת צְבִי וְלִצְפִירַת תִּפְאָרָה״? לָעוֹשִׂים רְצוֹנוֹ וּמְצַפִּים לִישׁוּעָתוֹ. יָכוֹל לַכֹּל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לִשְׁאָר עַמּוֹ״ – לְמִי שֶׁמֵּשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּשִׁירַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **"For a crown of glory, and for a diadem of beauty"?** It means: A crown **for those who perform His will and** a diadem **for those who await His salvation.** One **might** have thought that this extends **to everyone** in those categories. Therefore, **the verse states: "Unto the remnant of His people,"** meaning that God will be a crown and a diadem **to one who** humbly **establishes himself like a remnant,** and does not conduct himself with self-importance.

And again: the remnant.

### Virtues Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּלְרוּחַ מִשְׁפָּט לַיּוֹשֵׁב עַל הַמִּשְׁפָּט וְלִגְבוּרָה מְשִׁיבֵי מִלְחָמָה שָׁעְרָה״. ״וּלְרוּחַ מִשְׁפָּט״ – זֶה הָרוֹדֶה אֶת יִצְרוֹ, ״וְלַיּוֹשֵׁב עַל הַמִּשְׁפָּט״ – זֶה הַדָּן דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ, ״וְלִגְבוּרָה״ – זֶה הַמִּתְגַּבֵּר בְּיִצְרוֹ, ״מְשִׁיבֵי מִלְחָמָה״ – זֶה שֶׁנּוֹשֵׂא וְנוֹתֵן בְּמִלְחַמְתָּהּ שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, ״שָׁעְרָה״ – אֵלּוּ שֶׁמַּשְׁכִּימִין וּמַעֲרִיבִין בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets a verse in Isaiah. **"And for a spirit of justice to him that sits in judgment and for strength to them who retaliate in battle to the gate"** (Isaiah 28:6). **"And for a spirit of justice"; this** is referring to **one who subdues his** evil **inclination** and prevents himself from sinning. **"To him that sits in judgment"; this** is referring to **one who judges a true judgment truthfully. "And for strength"; this** is referring to **one who overcomes his** evil **inclination. "Them who retaliate in battle"; this** is referring to one **who gives and takes** and engages **in the battle,** i.e., in the discourse **of Torah. "To the gate"** is referring to **those** Torah scholars **who arrive early and remain late within** the gates of **synagogues and study halls.**

Who are those that merit this? *Le'osei retzono u'metzapim li'yeshuato* — those who do God's will and await His salvation. *Tzvi* is read as *tzivyon*, desire — those who desire to fulfill God's will — and *tzfirah* is read as those who *metzapim*, look forward, to God's ultimate redemption and await the *Mashiach*. Might this include everyone? The verse limits it: *lish'ar amo* — to the remnant of His people, meaning *le'mi she'mesim atzmo ke'shirayim* — to one who makes himself humble, like leftovers.

The subsequent verses are then read as further descriptions of those who merit *olam haba*. *U'leruach mishpat le'yoshev al hamishpat* — the spirit of justice to the one who sits in judgment — is interpreted as *zeh hadan din emet le'amito*, one who renders genuinely true, deeply considered justice. *U'ligvurah meshivei milchamah sha'arah* — and valor to those who return war to the gate — is read as *zeh she'noseh ve'noten be'milchamtah shel Torah*, one who engages in the back-and-forth battle of Torah learning. The distinction between *rodeh et yitzro* and *mitgaber al yitzro* is noted: *rodeh* suggests ongoing struggle; *mitgaber* implies ultimate victory — and Rashi suggests *mitgaber* may even mean sublimating the *yetzer* and channeling it toward good, not merely suppressing it. *Sha'arah* — at the gate — refers to *elu she'mashkimim u'ma'arivim be'vatei knesiyot u'vatei midrashot*, those who rise early and stay late in the *beit midrash* and *beit knesset*.

### Wine Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה מִדַּת הַדִּין לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מָה נִשְׁתַּנּוּ אֵלּוּ מֵאֵלּוּ? אָמַר לָהּ: ״וְגַם אֵלֶּה בַּיַּיִן שָׁגוּ וּבַשֵּׁכָר תָּעוּ [וְגוֹ׳] פָּקוּ פְּלִילִיָּה״. וְאֵין פּוּקָה אֶלָּא גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִהְיֶה זֹאת לְךָ לְפוּקָה״. וְאֵין פְּלִילָה אֶלָּא דַּיָּינִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַן בִּפְלִלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **The attribute of justice said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, how do these** people, who possess these qualities, **differ from those** who engage in Torah study but lack these qualities? God **said to** the attribute of justice that those others do not receive the crown and diadem because: **"These also reel through wine, and stagger through strong drink…they stumble [*paku*] in judgment [*peliliyya*]"** (Isaiah 28:7). **And *puka*** in this context is referring to **nothing other than Gehenna, as it is stated: "That this shall not be a cause of stumbling [*puka*] for you"** (I Samuel 25:31). **And** the term of ***pelila*** in the verse in Isaiah is referring to **nothing other than judges, as it is stated: "And he shall pay as the judges [*biflilim*] determine"** (Exodus 21:22). Those who lack the qualities listed in the previous verses are not worthy of the aforementioned reward.

The *midat hadin* then poses a challenge before the Holy One: Master of the universe — *mah nishtanu elu me'elu* — what distinguishes these from the others? What made the righteous different from those who did not walk this path? God responds with the verse: *ve'gam eleh be'yayin shagu u'va'shechar ta'u u'paku peliliyah* — even these others erred with wine and strayed with strong drink. In other words, the others simply gave themselves over to laziness and indulgence, while the righteous applied themselves. *Ve'ein pukah ela Gehinnom* — and *pukah* means *Gehinnom* — and *ve'ein pelilah ela dayanim* — *pelilah* means judges — they will be judged in *Gehinnom*, as derived from *ve'natan biflilim*. The concluding message is clear: the path to *olam haba* is devotion to *mitzvot* and serious, sustained study of *Torah*.

## Ir Hanidachat

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַנְשֵׁי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָצְאוּ אֲנָשִׁים בְּנֵי בְּלִיַּעַל מִקִּרְבֶּךָ וַיַּדִּיחוּ אֶת יֹשְׁבֵי עִירָם לֵאמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The residents of an idolatrous city have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Certain men, wicked persons, are gone out from your midst, and have subverted the inhabitants of their city, saying:** Let us go and let us worship other gods that you have not known" (Deuteronomy 13:14).**

The *gemara* now arrives at a new *mishna*, which explains why this chapter — *chelek* — is not the final *perek* of *Sanhedrin*. The final subject of *chelek* concerns *anshei ir hanidachat*, the people of an apostate city, and their status vis-à-vis *olam haba*. This then leads into the *halachot* of the *ir hanidachat*, and the *masechta* concludes with *eileh hen hanichnakim*, those subject to strangulation. That is the overall structure.

### Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָן נֶהֱרָגִים עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מַדִּיחֶיהָ מֵאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר וּמֵאוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט, וְעַד שֶׁיּוּדַח רוּבָּהּ, וְעַד שֶׁיַּדִּיחוּהָ אֲנָשִׁים. הִדִּיחוּהָ נָשִׁים וּקְטַנִּים, אוֹ שֶׁהוּדַּח מִיעוּטָהּ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ מַדִּיחֶיהָ חוּצָה לָהּ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כִּיחִידִים, וּצְרִיכִין שְׁנֵי עֵדִים וְהַתְרָאָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** idol worshippers **are not executed** as residents of an idolatrous city **unless its subverters are from that city and from that tribe, and unless most of** the inhabitants of the city **are subverted, and unless men subvert** the inhabitants of the city. If it occurs that **women or children subvert** the inhabitants of the city, **or that a minority of** the inhabitants of the city **were subverted, or that its subverters were from outside** the city and were neither residents of that city nor members of that tribe, **these** idol worshippers **are** judged **as individuals. And** to judge the inhabitants of a city **one requires two witnesses and forewarning for each and every one** who engaged in idol worship.

Several criteria must all be satisfied before a city acquires the status of *ir hanidachat* — and before its inhabitants are liable to *hereg* rather than being treated simply as many individual sinners, each of whom would receive *sekilah* for *avodah zarah* but whose property would not be destroyed:

The *madichim* — the seducers who led the city astray — must be from the city itself, citizens of the city, *madicheha me'otah ha'ir*, and from the same tribe as the city. A majority of the city must have worshipped *avodah zarah*. And the seducers must have been adult men — not women or minors. If the enticers were women or children, or if only a minority of the city worshipped *avodah zarah*, or if the enticers came from outside the city, then *harei elu kiyachidim* — all the individuals are treated as individuals. And each individual requires two witnesses and forewarning: *shnei eidim vehatra'ah l'chol echad ve'echad*.

### Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּיְחִידִים מִבַּמְרוּבִּים, שֶׁהַיְּחִידִים בִּסְקִילָה; לְפִיכָךְ מָמוֹנָם פָּלֵט, וְהַמְרוּבִּין בְּסַיִיף; לְפִיכָךְ מָמוֹנָם אָבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **This is a stringency with regard to individuals** who worship idols that is more stringent **than** the *halakha* **with regard to multitudes** who worship idols: **As the individuals** who worship idols are executed **by stoning; therefore,** since there is a stringency with regard to their mode of execution, **their property is spared** and is inherited by their heirs. **And the multitudes** are executed **by the sword;** therefore, since there is a leniency with regard to their mode of execution, **their property is eliminated.**

There is a dimension in which the status of individuals is actually more stringent than that of the *ir hanidachat*: individuals who worshipped *avodah zarah* receive *sekilah*, a harsher form of capital punishment. The counterbalance is that their property is not destroyed. The majority that constitutes the *ir hanidachat* receives *hereg* by the sword — a comparatively lighter death penalty — but their property is destroyed. The individual's greater culpability is perhaps because he acted alone rather than being swept along by the mass of the community.

### Travelers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֵּה תַכֶּה אֶת יֹשְׁבֵי הָעִיר הַזֹּאת לְפִי חֶרֶב״. הַחַמֶּרֶת וְהַגַּמֶּלֶת הָעוֹבֶרֶת מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מַצִּילִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** From the verse: **"You shall smite the inhabitants of that city with the edge of the sword"** (Deuteronomy 13:16), it is derived that **the caravan of donkeys and the caravan of camels that move from place to place, these** donkey or camel drivers **save** the city. If they were residing in the city for a period, they could join the minority of permanent residents who were not subverted to idol worship in order to create a majority and prevent the destruction of the city and its contents.

What of donkey drivers or camel drivers passing through the city? Do transient travelers count toward the population for purposes of determining majority? The *mishna* rules: *harei elu matzilim otah* — they are counted as part of the city, and therefore if they did not worship *avodah zarah* they could tilt the balance toward a minority, preventing the city's destruction. As Rashi notes, the reverse would equally be true: if they did worship *avodah zarah* and tipped the scales toward a majority, the city would be destroyed. The *gemara* will address what length of stay is required for transient travelers to be considered members of the city.

## City Destruction

### Burning Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הַחֲרֵם אֹתָהּ וְאֶת כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בָּהּ וְאֶת בְּהֶמְתָּהּ לְפִי חָרֶב״, מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: נִכְסֵי צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ אוֹבְדִין, שֶׁבְּחוּצָה לָהּ – פְּלֵיטִין. וְשֶׁל רְשָׁעִים, בֵּין שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ בֵּין שֶׁבְּחוּצָה לָהּ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ אוֹבְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated: "Destroy it utterly, and all that is in it and its animals, with the edge of the sword"** (Deuteronomy 13:16). **From here,** the Sages **stated: The property of the righteous,** who did not engage in idol worship, **that is inside** the city **is destroyed** with the rest of the city and its contents; but the property of the righteous **that is outside** the city **is spared. And** the property **of the wicked, whether** it is **inside** the city **or whether it is outside** the city, **these** items **are destroyed.**

The verse *hacherem osah ve'es kol asher bah ve'es behemtah lefi charev* — destroy it and all that is in it and all its animals by the sword — is the source for burning and destroying the property of the city. From here the *mishna* derives: *nichsei tzadikim she'betochah avudim* — even the property of the *tzadikim*, those within the city who did not worship *avodah zarah*, is destroyed — since the verse says *kol asher bah*, all that is in it. But property belonging to those righteous people that was located outside the city is spared. And the property of the wicked — those who did worship *avodah zarah* — is destroyed whether it is located inside or outside the city, though the precise derivation of this last point remains to be worked out in the *gemara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר, ״וְאֶת כׇּל שְׁלָלָהּ תִּקְבֹּץ אֶל תּוֹךְ רְחֹבָהּ וְגוֹ׳״, אִם אֵין לָהּ רְחוֹב, עוֹשִׂין לָהּ רְחוֹב. הָיְתָה רְחֹבָהּ חוּצָה לָהּ, כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתָהּ לְתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated: "And you shall gather all of its spoils into the midst of its square"** (Deuteronomy 13:17). **If** the city **has no square, one creates a square for** the city in order to fulfill the mitzva as it is written. **If there was a square outside of** the city, **they bring it inside** the city by expanding the city wall to include the square.

The physical mechanics of the destruction are then specified through the verse *ve'es kol shelalah tikbotz el toch rechovah* — gather all its booty into its public square. If the city has no *rechov*, a public square or street, one is made for it. If its main street is on the outside of the city, it is redirected inward.

### Desolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְשָׂרַפְתָּ בָאֵשׁ אֶת הָעִיר וְאֶת כׇּל שְׁלָלָהּ כָּלִיל לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, שְׁלָלָהּ, וְלֹא שְׁלַל שָׁמַיִם. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת שֶׁבָּהּ יִפָּדוּ, וּתְרוּמוֹת יֵרָקְבוּ, מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ יִגָּנְזוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated** in the continuation of the verse: **"And you shall burn it with fire, both the city and all its spoils, entirely for the Lord your God."** The mishna infers: **"Its spoils," but not the spoils of Heaven. From here** the Sages **stated: The consecrated** property **in it,** which was no longer the property of its inhabitants, **must be redeemed, and *terumot*** are neither eaten nor burned; rather **they must** be left to **decay.** And **second tithe and sacred scrolls** that were in the city **must be interred.**

The verse *ve'saraftah ba'esh es ha'ir ve'es kol shelalah kalil la'Hashem Elohecha* — you shall burn the city and all its booty completely to God — requires burning the city in fire. The word *kalil* evokes the *olah* offering. Rabbi Shimon states: if you carry out the judgment of the *ir hanidachat*, God accounts it as if you have brought a fully consumed burnt offering. *Ve'haysah tel olam* — it will be an eternal mound. Rabbi Yose HaGelili rules that the site may not even be planted as a garden or orchard. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *lo tivaneh od* — it may not be rebuilt as a city, *kemos she'haysah einah nivnes* — as it was it cannot be rebuilt — but it may be made into gardens and orchards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיְתָה תֵּל עוֹלָם״ – לֹא תֵּעָשֶׂה גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא תִּבָּנֶה עוֹד״ – לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִבְנֵית, אֲבָל נַעֲשֵׂית הִיא גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"And it shall be a heap forever"** (Deuteronomy 13:17), meaning: The idolatrous city **shall not be converted** even into **gardens and orchards;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** From the end of that verse: **"It shall not be built again,"** it is derived: To restore it **to the way it was** before destruction, **it** may **not** be **built; but it may be converted into gardens and orchards.**

### Holy Objects

The verse *ve'saraftah ba'esh* specifies *shelalah ve'lo shel shamayim* — its own booty but not property belonging to Heaven. From here: *hekdeshos she'bah yipadu* — sanctified objects within it are redeemed rather than burned. *U'trumos yirkavu* — *terumah* within it is allowed to rot. *Ma'aser sheni ve'kisvei ha'kodesh yignazu* — *ma'aser sheni* and holy scripture are buried. These objects occupy a strange middle category: they are not to be directly destroyed, but neither may they be used; they must decay or be interred unused.

### Divine Wrath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם״ – שֶׁכׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָרְשָׁעִים בָּעוֹלָם, חֲרוֹן אַף בָּעוֹלָם; אָבְדוּ רְשָׁעִים מִן הָעוֹלָם, נִסְתַּלֵּק חֲרוֹן אַף מִן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes with the verse **"And there shall cleave nothing of that which was devoted to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18). This teaches that **as long as the wicked** exist **in the world,** there is **wrath in the world;** once **the wicked are eliminated from the world, wrath leaves the world.**

The *mishna* concludes with the verse *ve'lo yidbak be'yadcha me'umah min ha'cherem* — nothing of the *cherem* shall cleave to your hand — and Rabbi Shimon's theological interpretation: *she'kol zman she'ha'resha'im ba'olam charon af ba'olam* — as long as the *resha'im* are in the world, God's anger is in the world. *Avdu resha'im min ha'olam nistalek charon af min ha'olam* — once the *resha'im* are removed from the world, God's anger is removed from the world. This accords with the end of the verse: *lema'an yashuv Hashem me'charon apo* — so that God may turn from His fierce anger.
