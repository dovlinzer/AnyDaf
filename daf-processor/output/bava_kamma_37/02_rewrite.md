# Bava Kamma 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boshet & Coin Values

### Mishna Boshet

The Gemara opens by citing a *mishna* from *Hachovel*: *hatokeia lachaveiro notein lo sela* — one who strikes his friend (near the ear, according to Rashi) must pay a *sela* as the assessment for *boshet*, for the embarrassment and shame. Any distinct damages — loss of hearing, medical expenses, and the like — would be assessed separately. This *sela* represents the standard *mishna*ic figure for *boshet*. Rabbi Yehuda then cites Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who assesses it at a *maneh*, which is 100 *zuz* — twenty-five times the amount of the *tanna kamma*, who assessed it at four *zuz*. Tosafot suggests that perhaps the *tanna kamma* was speaking of the minimum assessment for a person of low social standing, while Rabbi Yose HaGlili was addressing a person of high social standing. That distinction, however, is not the focus of the Gemara here.

### Sela Tzori v. Medina

The Gemara presents a case: a certain person struck his friend. Rashi notes immediately that *boshet* is a *knas* and is not adjudicated in Bavel, so Rashi understands the case as one involving *tofas* — seizure of the payment — though the details are not fully clear. Rav Tuvia bar Matna sent the matter before Rav Yosef and posed the following question: when the *mishna* says *sela*, does it mean *sela Tzori* or *sela Medina*? This is a genuinely confusing question because the same coin names were used across two different monetary systems. A *sela Medina* is one-eighth of a *sela Tzori* — which is itself four *zuz* — making a *sela Medina* worth only half a *zuz*. The difference between the two systems is thus a factor of eight in terms of the *sela*, or equivalently, a factor of two in terms of the *zuz*. Rav Yosef responds: we can infer the answer from the *mishna* about the goring ox.

### Ox Mishna Evidence

In the *mishna* about the goring ox, the damages are distributed in amounts denominated in *dinar zahav*, a gold *dinar* worth twenty-five *zuz*, with amounts breaking down as one hundred, fifty, twenty-five, and twenty-five. The Gemara asks: if a *tanna* were ever prepared to employ the coinage of *sela Medina*, why did the *mishna* not include the case of the ox goring a fifth time? In that scenario, the distribution would eventually yield a figure of twelve and a half *zuz*. If *sela Medina* — a half-*zuz* — were an acceptable denomination in Tannaic discourse, the *tanna* could have expressed that amount as "twelve *zuz* and a *sela*," avoiding fractional language. The presumption is that the *tanna* did not wish to speak in halves, which would mean he would never use *sela Medina* language at all, since its value is precisely a half-*zuz*. If one is in the currency system of *Tzur*, the only way to express a half-*zuz* is to say "half a *zuz*." If one were operating in *Medina* terms, one could say "a *sela*" to mean that amount — but if such language is never used, it proves that the Tannaim always refer to *sela Tzori*.

The Gemara entertains a challenge: *tanna ki rochla lisne v'leizel* — is the *tanna* like a merchant who must enumerate every possible case? The fact that the *mishna* stopped after four gorings does not mean the *tanna* committed himself to covering all possibilities.

### Tzori vs. Medina

The resolution is drawn from a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *kol kesef ha'amur ba-Torah kesef Tzori* — whenever the Torah specifies a monetary amount, it is denominated in *sela Tzori*. And rabbinic assessments — *shel divreihem* — are denominated in *kesef Medina*. The *mishna*'s assessment of *boshet*, being a rabbinic assessment of damages rather than a Torah-mandated sum, is therefore expressed in *sela Medina*, which equals half a *zuz*. The answer to Rav Tuvia bar Matna's question is accordingly that the *mishna* is using *sela Medina*, and the actual payment is only half a *zuz* — not four *zuz*.

This distinction carries significant practical implications. The Torah, for instance, requires *mohar habetulot* of fifty *kesef*, which on the *Tzori* system yields 200 *zuz*. Yet there is a well-known debate in the Gemara in Ketubot regarding whether *ketubah* is *d'oraita* or *d'rabanan*. Those who held *ketubah* to be rabbinic — a widespread position in Sfarad — interpreted the fifty *kesef* through the *Medina* lens, arriving at a *ketubah* worth only 25 *zuz*. Those who held *ketubah* to be biblical maintained it at 200 *zuz*. The result was that the value of the *ketubah* in Sfarad was one-eighth what it was in Ashkenaz. The HaRosh, who came from Ashkenaz and lived in Sfarad, faced the tension acutely: Ashkenazic *ketubot* include the phrase *d'chazi lichi mid'oraita*, while Sfaradic *ketubot* omit *mid'oraita*. The HaRosh resolved this by arguing that even if the *ketubah* is *d'rabanan*, the term *mid'oraita* is intended to signal that the amount is denominated in *kesef Tzori* — 200 *zuz*, not 25. Tosafot, by contrast, argues that the biblical origin of the fifty *kesef* figure determines the denomination regardless of the *d'oraita*/*d'rabanan* debate.

### Chanan Bisha Case

Following the ruling that *boshet* is assessed in *sela Medina*, the Gemara presents the case of *Chanan Bisha* — "Chanan the wicked" — who struck a man and was ordered by Rav Huna to pay half a *zuz*. Chanan Bisha had a *zuz* that was worn and rubbed down, and he sought to exchange it for the required payment. No merchant would accept the degraded coin. So he simply struck the man again and handed him the whole *zuz*. The punchline is pointed: when the penalty for striking someone amounts to only half a *zuz*, it may not be worth the trouble of finding change.

### Tosafot Transfer

The preceding case raises a prior legal point that Tosafot addresses. When the victim in the first case declared "give it to the poor," Rav Yosef responded: *kvar zachu bei ani'im* — the poor have already acquired it. But this is puzzling. The money was still in the pocket of the *mazik*; no physical transfer had taken place. How do the victim's words effect a transfer?

Rav Yosef's answer is *yad ani'im anan* — we, the rabbis, stand as the hand of the poor. Rashi clarifies that Rav Yosef himself was a *gabbai tzedakah*, and since the matter was adjudicated before him, when the victim said "give it to the poor" in Rav Yosef's presence, Rav Yosef could act as the representative of the poor and acquire it on their behalf. This is grounded in the principle stated by Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name that orphans do not require a *pruzbul*, because *beis din* is always presumed to act in their interests — as the Baraita of Rami bar Chama states, *Rabban Gamliel u-veiso aviahem shel yesomim hayu*, Rabban Gamliel and his court were as fathers to orphans.

Tosafot presses further. Why is *yad ani'im anan* even necessary? If a person announces he will give *tzedakah*, that itself becomes like a *neder* and should obligate him. Once he said "give it to the poor," why could he retract?

Tosafot answers by drawing an analogy to *hekdesh*: just as a person cannot consecrate something that has not yet come into his possession — *ein adam makdish davar she'lo ba l'olam* — so too one cannot transfer *tzedakah* obligations over money that is not yet in one's possession. The half-*zuz* had not yet been paid to the victim; it was still owed by the *mazik*. The victim therefore lacked the capacity to effect a transfer of ownership to the poor. What the words *could* do, says Tosafot, is create a personal obligation: had the victim said "when I receive this payment I will give it to *tzedakah*," that would function as a *neder* and bind him. But the victim did not say that. He said *tnahu la-ani'im* — "give it to the poor" — intending to transfer the money directly, not to assume a personal pledge. Since the money was not yet in his possession, the words had no transferring effect. This is why *yad ani'im anan* was needed: only because Rav Yosef, as *gabbai tzedakah*, could stand in as the representative of the poor and effectuate the acquisition on their behalf.

## Muad Framework

### Muad Mishna

The Gemara now turns to a *mishna* concerning the *muad*, the habitually vicious animal. The *mishna* states: *shor she'haya muad shehu muad l'mino ve'eino muad l'she'eino mino* — an ox that has become *muad* for goring its own kind is not necessarily *muad* for goring other kinds. Similarly, something that is *muad* for goring people is not necessarily *muad* for goring animals; something *muad* for goring small animals is not necessarily *muad* for goring large ones. For whatever category it has established a pattern, it pays full damages; for whatever category it has not established a pattern, it pays half damages. Something can gore a hundred times across a broad range of targets and yet only be *muad* for specific categories — a question the Gemara will explore at length.

### Shabbat Question

The *mishna* records that the students said to Rabbi Yehuda: suppose an animal gored on *Shabbos* and not on the weekday — what is the law? The question, at its core, is how formalist the system is. Is it sufficient that three gorings occurred on *Shabbos*, even though there is no obvious reason why an animal would confine its viciousness to one day of the week? Or do we require some causal logic?

Rabbi Yehuda ruled: *l'shabbasos meshalem nezek shalem, v'liyamos hachol meshalem chatzi nezek* — for *Shabbos* goring it pays full damages, for weekday goring it pays half. Rashi and Tosafot, however, are not entirely comfortable with pure formalism here. Rashi explains that *Shabbos* is genuinely different: the animal has a day off, is idle, and grows restless — *batel mimmellacha v'zacha da'ato alav*. Tosafot cites the Yerushalmi, which explains that on *Shabbos* people wear unfamiliar fine clothing, making them less recognizable to the animal and thus more likely to provoke it. In other words, Rashi and Tosafot understand Rabbi Yehuda's ruling not as pure pattern-formalism but as grounded in a real behavioral distinction between *Shabbos* and weekdays.

The *mishna* then addresses reversion: *eimasai hu tam?* — when would such an animal revert to *tam* status? The answer is *misheyachzor bo gimmel yemei Shabbosos* — once it has been given three opportunities, three *Shabbosos*, in the same circumstances that habituated it, and has not gored. This mirrors the *Rambam*'s understanding of *ba'al teshuva*: one confronts the same situation and does not repeat the transgression. The *shor* performs, as it were, its own *teshuva* by demonstrating restraint under identical conditions three consecutive times.

### Vav Question

A textual dispute now surfaces. *Rav Zevid amar ve'eino muad tnan; Rav Pappa amar eino muad tnan* — Rav Zevid holds that the *mishna* reads *ve'eino muad* (with a *vav*), making it descriptive; Rav Pappa holds that it reads *eino muad* (without a *vav*), making it prescriptive.

### Zevid v. Pappa

The dispute is not merely textual — it reflects a fundamental disagreement about how *muad* status generalizes.

According to Rav Pappa, reading *eino muad* without the *vav*: the *mishna* is stating a legal rule. If an animal gored three oxen, it is *muad* only for oxen, not for other animals. One identifies the evidenced pattern and limits *muad* status to precisely what has been demonstrated. There is no generalization. The animal is *muad* only for what it has concretely habituated itself to.

According to Rav Zevid, reading *ve'eino muad* with the *vav*: the *mishna* is describing a specific scenario. The standard rule is that once an animal gores three times — regardless of what it gored — it becomes *muad* for everything. The *mishna* is presenting an exceptional case: an animal that happened to be *muad* for its own kind and happened not to be *muad* for other kinds, because it actually encountered other animals on multiple occasions and refrained from goring them. The *vav* signals that the *mishna* is telling us a description of a case, not a rule limiting the scope of *muad*.

The practical stakes are significant. Under Rav Pappa, the scope of *muad* is always limited to the specific pattern evidenced. Under Rav Zevid, *muad* is presumed general unless actively rebutted by demonstrated restraint.

### Mishna Structure

The Gemara attempts to determine from the *mishna*'s structure which reading is correct.

Rav Zevid derives his position from the *seifa* of the *mishna*, which states that something is *muad l'ketanim ve'eino muad l'gedolim* — *muad* for small animals and not for large. On his reading, the *mishna* is describing a specific case in which the animal happened not to gore large ones when it had the opportunity. The implicit teaching is that in a standard case, even if an animal only gored small animals, we would assume it becomes *muad* for large ones as well — a *chiddush*, since one might have thought that a bully picks on the weak but not the strong. The *mishna* is telling us that the default is always generalization, and this particular case is an exception where the animal had demonstrated it would not attack large ones.

Rav Pappa responds: that *chiddush* is necessary even on his reading. One might have thought that once an animal has shown a predisposition within a category — say, goring small oxen — it would generalize at least within that *min*, attacking large oxen too. The *mishna* tells you it does not. Even the jump from small to large within the same species requires concrete demonstration. So the *seifa* is informative regardless of which reading one adopts.

Rav Pappa's own evidence comes from the *reisha*, which states *muad l'adam eino muad l'behema* — *muad* for goring people is not *muad* for goring animals. On his reading this is a legal rule: *muad* for one category does not extend to another. This is a striking *chiddush*, because one would reason by *kal vachomer* that an animal willing to gore a human being would certainly gore an animal, which it has less reason to fear. The *mishna* is telling us that no such generalization operates.

Rav Zevid responds: if the *mishna* were merely describing an exceptional scenario with a *vav*, one would not need to specify *muad l'adam ve'eino muad l'behema*. Since under his reading the standard case already generalizes from *behema* to *behema*, the case of generalizing from *adam* to *behema* would be obvious — it requires far less logic. The only reason the *mishna* mentions it is to address the case of *reversion*: an animal that had been *muad* for both *adam* and *behema*, then saw three animals and refrained, may have reverted its *muad* status for *behema* while remaining *muad* for *adam*. The *mishna* is telling us that such partial reversion is valid — that *hazara b'behema hazara hi*, even without demonstrated reversion from *adam*.

### Sumchus Baraita

A *baraita* is now introduced: *Sumchus omer muad l'adam muad l'behema mikal vachomer* — Sumchus says that if something is *muad* for goring humans, it is certainly *muad* for goring animals, by *kal vachomer*. The logic is self-evident: if it will gore a person, it will certainly gore an animal. The *baraita* implies that the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* held the opposite — that *muad l'adam* does not make it *muad l'behema*. This seemingly confirms that the *tanna kamma* is reading *eino muad* without the *vav*, as a legal rule, in line with Rav Pappa.

### Teshuva Focus

Rav Zevid deflects: Sumchus is not disputing the initial establishment of *muad* status, which both agree generalizes. The dispute is over *reversion* — specifically, the scenario established earlier, where an animal was *muad* for both *adam* and *behema*, then encountered animals three times without goring. The *tanna kamma* holds that *hazara b'behema hazara hi* — reversion demonstrated for animals is valid reversion, even though the animal has not reverted from goring people. Sumchus disagrees: *mikal vachomer*, since it has not reverted from goring people — the harder thing — it has not truly reverted from goring animals. *Hazara d'behema lav hazara hi*. The reversion is not sincere if the more aggressive impulse remains unreformed.

Under Rav Zevid's framework, the initial establishment of *muad* always generalizes; the debate between *tanna kamma* and Sumchus is thus narrowly about the mechanism of reversion.

### Shabbat Revisited

The Gemara adduces evidence from the *Shabbos* case in the *mishna*. The text reads: *harei zeh muad l'shabbasos ve'eino muad l'yamos hachol* — he is *muad* for *Shabbosos* and not for weekdays. *Rav Zevid amar ve'eino muad tnan* — with the *vav*, it is descriptive: the students are presenting Rav Yehuda with a scenario (the animal gored on *Shabbos* and not on the weekday) and asking the *halacha*. Rabbi Yehuda confirms it. This reading makes sense: the *shayilu* — they were asking — and *hu nami ka mehadar lehu* — he was responding to their question.

But if the *mishna* reads *eino muad* without a *vav*, as Rav Pappa would have it, then the students are not posing a question — they are already stating a ruling: *muad l'shabbasos eino muad l'yamos hachol*. And then what is Rabbi Yehuda responding to? *Agmurei hu d'ka megamrei lei* — are they teaching *him* a *halacha*? That makes no sense. The structure of the *mishna* as a dialogue between students and Rabbi Yehuda strongly supports Rav Zevid's reading: with the *vav*, it is a question; without the *vav*, there is no question to answer.

Rav Yannai adds that one can also infer this from the *reisha*. After enumerating all the cases — *muad* for this and not for that — the *mishna* concludes: *es shehu muad lo meshalem nezek shalem v'es she'eino muad lo meshalem chatzi nezek*, the thing it is *muad* for pays full, the thing it is not *muad* for pays half. If the preceding clauses were already prescriptive rulings — *muad l'adam eino muad l'behema*, etc. — then this concluding line adds nothing. By daf 37 of this tractate, no reader needs to be told that a *muad* pays full and a *tam* pays half. But if the preceding clauses describe behavior, this final line is necessary: it tells you what follows legally from the pattern that was described. Thus the *mishna*'s structure as a whole corroborates Rav Zevid.

The Gemara notes, however: *ve'im timtzi lomar nami isa l'Rav Pappa* — even so, the Gemara does not abandon Rav Pappa's framework entirely, because it is Rav Pappa's position that generates all the interesting questions about pattern identification that follow.

## Pattern Scope

### Mixed Animals

Granting Rav Pappa's framework for the purposes of inquiry, the Gemara proceeds: *nagach shor chamor vegamal na'aseh muad lakol* — if an ox gored a *shor*, a *chamor*, and a *gamal*, it becomes *muad* for all animals. The principle under Rav Pappa is to identify the smallest level of abstraction that contains all the observed gorings as a common denominator. Here the three animals are of distinct species, and the common category encompassing all of them is simply *behema*, or perhaps all large animals. One might ask whether this would extend to *adam* as well, or whether it would apply to small animals — and the answer would depend on whether the three observed victims were of comparable size and whether people had been gored as well.

The *Rif* rules on the basis of these discussions that the *halacha* follows Rav Pappa, because all the subsequent cases of pattern identification work most naturally within his framework.

### Alternating Gores

A *baraita* teaches: if an animal saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore, saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore, saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore — *na'aseh muad l'sirugin l'shorim* — it becomes *muad* for goring every other ox. The pattern of alternation is itself recognized as a *muad* pattern. If it then gores two oxen in a row, only the second goring triggers full liability — the first, as an "off" event in the alternating cycle, is still only *chatzi nezek*. A second *baraita* extends this: if the alternating pattern involves six different animals — ox, donkey, horse, camel, mule, wild ass, alternating gore and no-gore — *na'aseh muad l'sirugin l'khol*, it becomes *muad* for alternately goring any type of animal. The level of abstraction again captures the full breadth of what was observed.

### Teiku Cases

The Gemara raises a series of unresolved questions. Suppose an animal gored: ox, ox, ox, donkey, camel. Does the first three gorings — all oxen — fix its *muad* status as *muad l'shorim*, such that the subsequent donkey and camel gorings are insufficient to upgrade it to *muad lakol* (since two additional species are not yet three)? Or do we look at the last three — ox, donkey, camel — and declare it *muad lakol*?

The reverse is also asked: chamor, gamal, shor, shor, shor. Do we take the first three — chamor, gamal, shor — yielding *muad lakol*, and disregard that the last three are all oxen? Or are we drawn to the grouping of three oxen, establishing it as only *muad l'shorim*?

The Gemara applies the same uncertainty to days of the week. Suppose the animal gored on *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, then Sunday, Monday. Does the first three establish *muad* for *Shabbos* alone, leaving Sunday and Monday insufficient for a pattern of their own? Or do we take the last three — *Shabbos*, Sunday, Monday — and declare it *muad* for all days? The reverse is similarly posed: Thursday, Friday, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*. Do we take the first three, establishing *muad* for all days of the week? Or are we drawn to the grouping of three consecutive *Shabbasos*, yielding *muad* only for *Shabbos*?

All of these remain unresolved — *teiku*. The underlying question of whether the determining grouping is the first three gorings or the last three is not settled.

One should note that for any of these cases involving interim days, the analysis assumes the animal had no opportunity to gore on those days — that is, it was not presented with an ox on Sunday through Friday. If it encountered animals during those intervals and refrained, that would separately bear on its reversion from *muad* status for those days.

### Monthly Dates

The Gemara now presents a case that reveals just how thoroughly formalist the system becomes: an ox gored on the 15th of one month, the 16th of the next, and the 17th of the following month. Here there is no intuitive behavioral explanation for the pattern — unlike *Shabbos*, for which Rashi and Tosafot offered reasons involving the animal's idleness or the unfamiliar clothing of people. Nevertheless, the *muad* framework apparently applies even to such arbitrary-seeming date sequences.

The specific question raised is whether the pattern of "plus one day each successive month" — fifteenth, sixteenth, seventeenth — constitutes an established *muad* pattern, and if so, what it predicts for the following month. The problem is that while three data points exist, the "plus one" increment has only been observed twice: between the fifteenth and sixteenth, and between the sixteenth and seventeenth. The Gemara notes that this parallels a debate between Rav and Shmuel in the laws of *vesset* in *Niddah* — a woman's established cycle for the onset of her period. *Ra'ata yom tes-vav be-chodesh zeh, yom tes-zayin be-chodesh zeh, yom yod-zayin be-chodesh zeh* — she saw on the fifteenth of one month, the sixteenth of the next, the seventeenth of the next. Rav holds *kave'ah lah vesset* — she has established a cycling pattern, and next month she anticipates the eighteenth. Shmuel holds *ad she-teshalesh b'dilug* — she needs three instances of the incremental shift, meaning four months total, before the pattern is fixed. The same dispute applies to the goring ox.

### Shofar Example

Rava teaches: if an animal heard a *shofar* blast and gored, heard a *shofar* and gored, heard a *shofar* and gored — *na'aseh muad l'shofaros* — it is now *muad* to gore every time it hears a *shofar*. One might have thought this is obvious. But the Gemara raises a possible objection: perhaps the first time should not count, since the *shofar* blast was so startling and disorienting that the goring was essentially a reflexive fright response — *siyuta b'alma* — and does not yet establish a pattern of habituated vicious behavior. The Gemara rejects this: we still count all three instances, and three times is three times.

### Formalist Conclusion

By the end of the *sugya*, the system has moved decisively toward formalism. The question of *Shabbos*, which Rashi and Tosafot explained on behavioral grounds, gives way to a framework in which patterns of goring on specific calendar dates — the fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth of the month — are treated as legally constitutive *muad* patterns without any causal explanation. The analysis under Rav Pappa's framework is not asking *why* the animal gores in a given pattern; it is asking only *what* pattern the evidence supports, and identifying the smallest level of abstraction that captures the observed data.

As one might ask: what counts as a pattern? It cannot be that any three numbers constitute one. There must be some criterion of simplicity or describability — a pattern like *n*, *n*+1, *n*+2 is recognizable; a sequence like 15, 117, 34 is not. The Gemara does not spell out this criterion explicitly, but the cases it considers — same-day-of-week gorings, consecutive monthly dates, alternating gorings — all share the quality of being simply and uniformly describable. That implicit standard of what constitutes a recognizable pattern appears to be the unspoken logic underlying the entire formalist approach.
