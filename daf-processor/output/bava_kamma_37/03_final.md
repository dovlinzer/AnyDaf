# Bava Kamma 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boshet & Coin Values

### Mishna Boshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַתּוֹקֵעַ לַחֲבֵירוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ סֶלַע. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the gold dinar mentioned in the mishna, **we learned** in another mishna **there** (90a): **One who slaps [*hatokea*] another** is liable to **give him a *sela*** as a compensatory fine. **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili:** He is liable to give him **one hundred dinars.**

The Gemara opens by citing a *mishna* from *Hachovel*: *hatokeia lachaveiro notein lo sela* — one who strikes his friend (near the ear, according to Rashi) must pay a *sela* as the assessment for *boshet*, for the embarrassment and shame. Any distinct damages — loss of hearing, medical expenses, and the like — would be assessed separately. This *sela* represents the standard *mishna*ic figure for *boshet*. Rabbi Yehuda then cites Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who assesses it at a *maneh*, which is 100 *zuz* — twenty-five times the amount of the *tanna kamma*, who assessed it at four *zuz*. Tosafot suggests that perhaps the *tanna kamma* was speaking of the minimum assessment for a person of low social standing, while Rabbi Yose HaGlili was addressing a person of high social standing. That distinction, however, is not the focus of the Gemara here.

### Sela Tzori v. Medina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּתְקַע לְחַבְרֵיהּ. שַׁלְּחֵיהּ רַב טוֹבִיָּה בַּר מַתְנָה לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: סֶלַע צוֹרִי תְּנַן, אוֹ סֶלַע מְדִינָה תְּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who slapped another. Rav Toviya bar Mattana sent** an enquiry **before Rav Yosef,** asking him whether the *sela* **we learned** about in the mishna is referring to **a Tyrian *sela*,** which is worth four dinars, **or** whether the *sela* **we learned** about in the mishna is referring to **a provincial *sela*,** worth only half of a dinar, or one-eighth of a Tyrian *sela*.

The Gemara presents a case: a certain person struck his friend. Rashi notes immediately that *boshet* is a *knas* and is not adjudicated in Bavel, so Rashi understands the case as one involving *tofas* — seizure of the payment — though the details are not fully clear. Rav Tuvia bar Matna sent the matter before Rav Yosef and posed the following question: when the *mishna* says *sela*, does it mean *sela Tzori* or *sela Medina*? This is a genuinely confusing question because the same coin names were used across two different monetary systems. A *sela Medina* is one-eighth of a *sela Tzori* — which is itself four *zuz* — making a *sela Medina* worth only half a *zuz*. The difference between the two systems is thus a factor of eight in terms of the *sela*, or equivalently, a factor of two in terms of the *zuz*. Rav Yosef responds: we can infer the answer from the *mishna* about the goring ox.

### Ox Mishna Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: וּשְׁנַיִם הָרִאשׁוֹנִים דִּינַר זָהָב. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ תָּנֵי תַּנָּא סֶלַע מְדִינָה, נִפְלוֹג וְנִתְנֵי עַד תְּרֵיסַר וְסֶלַע!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: You learned this** in a mishna: **And the first two** divide the remainder, each receiving **one gold dinar,** worth twenty-five silver dinars. **And if it enters your mind** that **the *tanna* teaches** monetary sums using **a provincial *sela*** in order to calculate compensation, **let him** further **divide** the value that the first two litigants receive **and teach** a case where the belligerent ox gored an additional ox, so that their shares decrease **until** they reach **twelve dinars and one *sela*** apiece, which are twelve and a half dinars. The fact that he does not do so indicates that the mishna does not use a provincial *sela*, whose value is less than one dinar, and therefore a gold dinar cannot be divided in a manner that leaves each party with whole coins.

In the *mishna* about the goring ox, the damages are distributed in amounts denominated in *dinar zahav*, a gold *dinar* worth twenty-five *zuz*, with amounts breaking down as one hundred, fifty, twenty-five, and twenty-five. The Gemara asks: if a *tanna* were ever prepared to employ the coinage of *sela Medina*, why did the *mishna* not include the case of the ox goring a fifth time? In that scenario, the distribution would eventually yield a figure of twelve and a half *zuz*. If *sela Medina* — a half-*zuz* — were an acceptable denomination in Tannaic discourse, the *tanna* could have expressed that amount as "twelve *zuz* and a *sela*," avoiding fractional language. The presumption is that the *tanna* did not wish to speak in halves, which would mean he would never use *sela Medina* language at all, since its value is precisely a half-*zuz*. If one is in the currency system of *Tzur*, the only way to express a half-*zuz* is to say "half a *zuz*." If one were operating in *Medina* terms, one could say "a *sela*" to mean that amount — but if such language is never used, it proves that the Tannaim always refer to *sela Tzori*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תַּנָּא כִּי רוֹכְלָא לִיתְנֵי וְלֵיזִיל?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Toviya bar Mattana **said to him:** This is not proof; **should the *tanna* have continued teaching** additional cases, exhausting all possibilities, **like a peddler** selling his wares, who advertises every item of his merchandise? The cases cited in the mishna suffice to illustrate the point.

The Gemara entertains a challenge: *tanna ki rochla lisne v'leizel* — is the *tanna* like a merchant who must enumerate every possible case? The fact that the *mishna* stopped after four gorings does not mean the *tanna* committed himself to covering all possibilities.

### Tzori vs. Medina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? פַּשְׁטוּהָ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל כֶּסֶף הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה – כֶּסֶף צוֹרִי, וְשֶׁל דִּבְרֵיהֶם – כֶּסֶף מְדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? The Sages **resolved it based on** that **which Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: All** references to **coinage mentioned in the Torah** refer to **Tyrian coinage, whereas** all mentions **of** coinage in the **statements of** the Sages refer to **provincial coinage.** Therefore, a person who slaps another is fined a provincial *sela*, worth half of a dinar.

The resolution is drawn from a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *kol kesef ha'amur ba-Torah kesef Tzori* — whenever the Torah specifies a monetary amount, it is denominated in *sela Tzori*. And rabbinic assessments — *shel divreihem* — are denominated in *kesef Medina*. The *mishna*'s assessment of *boshet*, being a rabbinic assessment of damages rather than a Torah-mandated sum, is therefore expressed in *sela Medina*, which equals half a *zuz*. The answer to Rav Tuvia bar Matna's question is accordingly that the *mishna* is using *sela Medina*, and the actual payment is only half a *zuz* — not four *zuz*.

This distinction carries significant practical implications. The Torah, for instance, requires *mohar habetulot* of fifty *kesef*, which on the *Tzori* system yields 200 *zuz*. Yet there is a well-known debate in the Gemara in Ketubot regarding whether *ketubah* is *d'oraita* or *d'rabanan*. Those who held *ketubah* to be rabbinic — a widespread position in Sfarad — interpreted the fifty *kesef* through the *Medina* lens, arriving at a *ketubah* worth only 25 *zuz*. Those who held *ketubah* to be biblical maintained it at 200 *zuz*. The result was that the value of the *ketubah* in Sfarad was one-eighth what it was in Ashkenaz. The HaRosh, who came from Ashkenaz and lived in Sfarad, faced the tension acutely: Ashkenazic *ketubot* include the phrase *d'chazi lichi mid'oraita*, while Sfaradic *ketubot* omit *mid'oraita*. The HaRosh resolved this by arguing that even if the *ketubah* is *d'rabanan*, the term *mid'oraita* is intended to signal that the amount is denominated in *kesef Tzori* — 200 *zuz*, not 25. Tosafot, by contrast, argues that the biblical origin of the fifty *kesef* figure determines the denomination regardless of the *d'oraita*/*d'rabanan* debate.

### Chanan Bisha Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָנָן בִּישָׁא תְּקַע לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל הַב לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא דְזוּזָא. הֲוָה לֵיהּ זוּזָא מָכָא, בָּעֵי לְמִיתְּבֵהּ לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ פַּלְגָא דְּזוּזָא, לָא הֲוָה מִשְׁתְּקִיל לֵיהּ. תְּקַע לֵיהּ אַחֲרִינָא, וְיַהֲבֵיהּ נִהֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ḥanan the wicked slapped a certain man. He** then **came before Rav Huna** for judgment. Rav Huna **said to him: Go give him a half-dinar,** which is the fine imposed for such an act. Ḥanan the wicked **had a clipped dinar,** and **wanted to give him a half-dinar from it,** but **there was no one** who wanted **to take it** from him to give him smaller coins for it. Ḥanan the wicked then **gave him another slap,** rendering himself liable to pay an additional fine of half a dinar, **and gave him** the clipped dinar as payment.

Following the ruling that *boshet* is assessed in *sela Medina*, the Gemara presents the case of *Chanan Bisha* — "Chanan the wicked" — who struck a man and was ordered by Rav Huna to pay half a *zuz*. Chanan Bisha had a *zuz* that was worn and rubbed down, and he sought to exchange it for the required payment. No merchant would accept the degraded coin. So he simply struck the man again and handed him the whole *zuz*. The punchline is pointed: when the penalty for striking someone amounts to only half a *zuz*, it may not be worth the trouble of finding change.

### Tosafot Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּבָר זְכוֹ בֵּיהּ עֲנִיִּים. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא עֲנִיִּים הָכָא, אֲנַן יַד עֲנִיִּים אֲנַן. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִים אֵינָן צְרִיכִין פְּרוֹזְבּוּל. וְכֵן תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הַיְּתוֹמִים אֵינָן צְרִיכִין פְּרוֹזְבּוּל – רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית דִּינוֹ אֲבִיהֶן שֶׁל יְתוֹמִין הָיוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to him:** Since you already committed to give it to charity, **the poor have already acquired it** and it now belongs to them. **And although there are no poor** people **here** to acquire it, **we,** the court, **are the hand,** i.e., the legal extension, **of the poor.** We represent them, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Orphans do not require a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*]** by transferring the right of collection to the court. The reason that orphans do not require this document is because the court is legally considered to be their steward, and their debts are therefore transferred to the court automatically, even without a *prosbol*. **Similarly, Rami bar Ḥama taught: Orphans do not require a *prosbol*,** as **Rabban Gamliel and his court were** tantamount to **the fathers of orphans,** as they were vigilant to collect all of the debts owed to orphans. Subsequently, the courts in every generation have this status.

The preceding case raises a prior legal point that Tosafot addresses. When the victim in the first case declared "give it to the poor," Rav Yosef responded: *kvar zachu bei ani'im* — the poor have already acquired it. But this is puzzling. The money was still in the pocket of the *mazik*; no physical transfer had taken place. How do the victim's words effect a transfer?

Rav Yosef's answer is *yad ani'im anan* — we, the rabbis, stand as the hand of the poor. Rashi clarifies that Rav Yosef himself was a *gabbai tzedakah*, and since the matter was adjudicated before him, when the victim said "give it to the poor" in Rav Yosef's presence, Rav Yosef could act as the representative of the poor and acquire it on their behalf. This is grounded in the principle stated by Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name that orphans do not require a *pruzbul*, because *beis din* is always presumed to act in their interests — as the Baraita of Rami bar Chama states, *Rabban Gamliel u-veiso aviahem shel yesomim hayu*, Rabban Gamliel and his court were as fathers to orphans.

Tosafot presses further. Why is *yad ani'im anan* even necessary? If a person announces he will give *tzedakah*, that itself becomes like a *neder* and should obligate him. Once he said "give it to the poor," why could he retract?

Tosafot answers by drawing an analogy to *hekdesh*: just as a person cannot consecrate something that has not yet come into his possession — *ein adam makdish davar she'lo ba l'olam* — so too one cannot transfer *tzedakah* obligations over money that is not yet in one's possession. The half-*zuz* had not yet been paid to the victim; it was still owed by the *mazik*. The victim therefore lacked the capacity to effect a transfer of ownership to the poor. What the words *could* do, says Tosafot, is create a personal obligation: had the victim said "when I receive this payment I will give it to *tzedakah*," that would function as a *neder* and bind him. But the victim did not say that. He said *tnahu la-ani'im* — "give it to the poor" — intending to transfer the money directly, not to assume a personal pledge. Since the money was not yet in his possession, the words had no transferring effect. This is why *yad ani'im anan* was needed: only because Rav Yosef, as *gabbai tzedakah*, could stand in as the representative of the poor and effectuate the acquisition on their behalf.

## Muad Framework

### Muad Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא מוּעָד לְמִינוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ; מוּעָד לְאָדָם וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה; מוּעָד לִקְטַנִּים וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לִגְדוֹלִים; אֶת שֶׁהוּא מוּעָד לוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְאֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox that is forewarned with regard to its** own **species,** as it already gored other oxen three times, **but is not forewarned with regard to other species;** or an ox that is **forewarned with regard to people, but is not forewarned with regard to animals;** or one that is **forewarned with regard to small** specimens of a species, **but is not forewarned with regard to large** specimens of that species; in all these cases, if the ox gores the type of animal or person **with regard to which it is forewarned,** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage, and** if it gores an animal or person **with regard to which it is not forewarned, he pays half** the cost of **the damage.**

The Gemara now turns to a *mishna* concerning the *muad*, the habitually vicious animal. The *mishna* states: *shor she'haya muad shehu muad l'mino ve'eino muad l'she'eino mino* — an ox that has become *muad* for goring its own kind is not necessarily *muad* for goring other kinds. Similarly, something that is *muad* for goring people is not necessarily *muad* for goring animals; something *muad* for goring small animals is not necessarily *muad* for goring large ones. For whatever category it has established a pattern, it pays full damages; for whatever category it has not established a pattern, it pays half damages. Something can gore a hundred times across a broad range of targets and yet only be *muad* for specific categories — a question the Gemara will explore at length.

### Shabbat Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הֲרֵי זֶה מוּעָד לַשַּׁבָּתוֹת וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לַחוֹל? אָמַר לָהֶם: לַשַּׁבָּתוֹת – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, לִימוֹת הַחוֹל – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. אֵימָתַי הוּא תָּם? מִשֶּׁיַּחְזוֹר בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יְמֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said before Rabbi Yehuda:** What would be the *halakha* if **this** ox **is forewarned with regard to *Shabbatot* but is not forewarned with regard to weekdays? He said to them: For** damage it causes on ***Shabbatot*** its owner **pays** the **full** cost of the **damage, and for** damage it causes on **weekdays, he pays half** the cost of the **damage. When is it** rendered **innocuous** again after being forewarned with regard to Shabbat? It reverts to its innocuous status **when** its behavior **reverts** to normal, i.e., when it refrains from goring for **three days of Shabbat,** i.e., Shabbat in three successive weeks.

The *mishna* records that the students said to Rabbi Yehuda: suppose an animal gored on *Shabbos* and not on the weekday — what is the law? The question, at its core, is how formalist the system is. Is it sufficient that three gorings occurred on *Shabbos*, even though there is no obvious reason why an animal would confine its viciousness to one day of the week? Or do we require some causal logic?

Rabbi Yehuda ruled: *l'shabbasos meshalem nezek shalem, v'liyamos hachol meshalem chatzi nezek* — for *Shabbos* goring it pays full damages, for weekday goring it pays half. Rashi and Tosafot, however, are not entirely comfortable with pure formalism here. Rashi explains that *Shabbos* is genuinely different: the animal has a day off, is idle, and grows restless — *batel mimmellacha v'zacha da'ato alav*. Tosafot cites the Yerushalmi, which explains that on *Shabbos* people wear unfamiliar fine clothing, making them less recognizable to the animal and thus more likely to provoke it. In other words, Rashi and Tosafot understand Rabbi Yehuda's ruling not as pure pattern-formalism but as grounded in a real behavioral distinction between *Shabbos* and weekdays.

The *mishna* then addresses reversion: *eimasai hu tam?* — when would such an animal revert to *tam* status? The answer is *misheyachzor bo gimmel yemei Shabbosos* — once it has been given three opportunities, three *Shabbosos*, in the same circumstances that habituated it, and has not gored. This mirrors the *Rambam*'s understanding of *ba'al teshuva*: one confronts the same situation and does not repeat the transgression. The *shor* performs, as it were, its own *teshuva* by demonstrating restraint under identical conditions three consecutive times.

### Vav Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ תְּנַן. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that **Rav Zevid said:** The version of the mishna that **we learned** states: With regard to an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **but is not forewarned** with regard to other species, referring to an ox that is proven to be innocuous with regard to other species, if it gores another species, its owner pays only half the cost of the damage. **Rav Pappa said:** The version of the mishna that **we learned** states: An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species. Accordingly, the mishna is teaching that the fact that it is forewarned with regard to goring one species does not render it forewarned with regard to goring other species, until it is proven otherwise.

A textual dispute now surfaces. *Rav Zevid amar ve'eino muad tnan; Rav Pappa amar eino muad tnan* — Rav Zevid holds that the *mishna* reads *ve'eino muad* (with a *vav*), making it descriptive; Rav Pappa holds that it reads *eino muad* (without a *vav*), making it prescriptive.

### Zevid v. Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ תְּנַן – הָא סְתָמָא הָוֵי מוּעָד. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ תְּנַן – דִּסְתָמָא לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Zevid said** that the version of the mishna that **we learned** states: With regard to an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **but is not forewarned** with regard to other species, indicating that it is referring specifically to an ox that is proven to be innocuous with regard to other species. This implies that in **an ordinary** case, where there is no such proof, the ox **is** considered **forewarned** with regard to all species. **Rav Pappa,** by contrast, **said** that the version of the mishna that **we learned** states: An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species, meaning **that** in **an ordinary** case, the ox **is not** considered **forewarned** with regard to other species.

The dispute is not merely textual — it reflects a fundamental disagreement about how *muad* status generalizes.

According to Rav Pappa, reading *eino muad* without the *vav*: the *mishna* is stating a legal rule. If an animal gored three oxen, it is *muad* only for oxen, not for other animals. One identifies the evidenced pattern and limits *muad* status to precisely what has been demonstrated. There is no generalization. The animal is *muad* only for what it has concretely habituated itself to.

According to Rav Zevid, reading *ve'eino muad* with the *vav*: the *mishna* is describing a specific scenario. The standard rule is that once an animal gores three times — regardless of what it gored — it becomes *muad* for everything. The *mishna* is presenting an exceptional case: an animal that happened to be *muad* for its own kind and happened not to be *muad* for other kinds, because it actually encountered other animals on multiple occasions and refrained from goring them. The *vav* signals that the *mishna* is telling us a description of a case, not a rule limiting the scope of *muad*.

The practical stakes are significant. Under Rav Pappa, the scope of *muad* is always limited to the specific pattern evidenced. Under Rav Zevid, *muad* is presumed general unless actively rebutted by demonstrated restraint.

### Mishna Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד דָּיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא, רַב פָּפָּא דָּיֵיק מֵרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid inferred** his opinion **from the latter clause** of the mishna, whereas **Rav Pappa inferred** his opinion **from the former clause.**

The Gemara attempts to determine from the *mishna*'s structure which reading is correct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד דָּיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא – דְּקָתָנֵי: מוּעָד לִקְטַנִּים וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לִגְדוֹלִים. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – הָא סְתָמֵיהּ הָוֵי מוּעָד; הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דַּאֲפִילּוּ מִקְּטַנִּים לִגְדוֹלִים נָמֵי, מִסְּתָמָא הָוֵי מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid inferred** his opinion **from the latter clause, as it teaches:** An ox that is **forewarned with regard to small** specimens of a species, **but is not forewarned with regard to large** specimens of that species. **Granted, if you say** that the mishna **teaches** in the first clause: With regard to an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **but is not forewarned** with regard to other species, indicating that in **an ordinary** case the ox **is** considered **forewarned** with regard to all animals, **this** clause of the mishna **teaches us that even from** being forewarned with regard to **small** specimens of a species, in **an ordinary** case the ox **is** thereby considered **forewarned with regard to large** specimens of that species, which is a more far-reaching statement, as an ox is less likely to gore large animals.

Rav Zevid derives his position from the *seifa* of the *mishna*, which states that something is *muad l'ketanim ve'eino muad l'gedolim* — *muad* for small animals and not for large. On his reading, the *mishna* is describing a specific case in which the animal happened not to gore large ones when it had the opportunity. The implicit teaching is that in a standard case, even if an animal only gored small animals, we would assume it becomes *muad* for large ones as well — a *chiddush*, since one might have thought that a bully picks on the weak but not the strong. The *mishna* is telling us that the default is always generalization, and this particular case is an exception where the animal had demonstrated it would not attack large ones.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – סְתָמָא לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד, הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר מִקְּטַנִּים לִקְטַנִּים דְּעָלְמָא, סְתָמָא לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד; מִקְּטַנִּים לִגְדוֹלִים צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּלָא הָוֵי מוּעָד?
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the mishna **teaches:** An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species, meaning that in **an ordinary** case, where there is no proof to the contrary, the ox **is not** considered **forewarned** with regard to other species, there is a difficulty with the latter clause of the mishna. The Gemara explains: **Now** that **it can be said** that **from** being forewarned with regard to one species of **animal,** in **an ordinary** case, the ox **is not** thereby considered **forewarned with regard to** other species of **animals, need it be said that from** being forewarned with regard to **small** specimens of a species **it is not** thereby considered **forewarned with regard to large** specimens of that species? It must be that the mishna reads: But is not forewarned, indicating that only when the ox is proven to be innocuous with regard to other species and then it gores another species is its owner liable to pay only half the cost of the damage. Otherwise, he must pay the full cost of the damage.

Rav Pappa responds: that *chiddush* is necessary even on his reading. One might have thought that once an animal has shown a predisposition within a category — say, goring small oxen — it would generalize at least within that *min*, attacking large oxen too. The *mishna* tells you it does not. Even the jump from small to large within the same species requires concrete demonstration. So the *seifa* is informative regardless of which reading one adopts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב פָּפָּא אָמַר לָךְ: אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּיךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּפְרַץ בֵּיהּ בְּהָהוּא מִינָא – פְּרַץ בֵּיהּ, לָא שְׁנָא גְּדוֹלִים דִּידֵיהּ וְלָא שְׁנָא קְטַנִּים דִּידֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא הָוֵי מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa** could have **said to you** in response that even if the mishna reads: An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species, the latter clause is **necessary,** as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **since it breached** the norm by attacking one **of that species, it** is considered to have **breached** the norm entirely **with regard to** that species, and there **is no difference** with regard to **large** members **of** the species **and** there **is no difference** with regard to **small** members **of it,** as the ox is now likely to gore any of them. Therefore, this clause of the mishna **teaches us that it is not** considered **forewarned** with regard to the large animals of that species.

Rav Pappa's own evidence comes from the *reisha*, which states *muad l'adam eino muad l'behema* — *muad* for goring people is not *muad* for goring animals. On his reading this is a legal rule: *muad* for one category does not extend to another. This is a striking *chiddush*, because one would reason by *kal vachomer* that an animal willing to gore a human being would certainly gore an animal, which it has less reason to fear. The *mishna* is telling us that no such generalization operates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא דָּיֵיק מֵרֵישָׁא, דְּקָתָנֵי: מוּעָד לְאָדָם אֵינוֹ מוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ תְּנַן – סְתָמָא לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד; הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דַּאֲפִילּוּ מֵאָדָם לִבְהֵמָה נָמֵי, סְתָמָא לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** As stated previously, **Rav Pappa inferred** his opinion **from the former clause** of the mishna. **As** the mishna **teaches:** An ox that is **forewarned with regard to people is not forewarned with regard to animals. Granted, if you say** that **we learned** that the mishna states: An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species, which would indicate that in **an ordinary** case, where there is no proof to the contrary, **it is not** considered **forewarned** with regard to other species, then the mishna, in the next clause, **teaches us this, that even from** being forewarned with regard to **people,** in **an ordinary** case, the ox **is not** considered **forewarned with regard to animals.** The ox is considered innocuous with regard to animals, although it is more common for an ox to gore an animal than a person.

Rav Zevid responds: if the *mishna* were merely describing an exceptional scenario with a *vav*, one would not need to specify *muad l'adam ve'eino muad l'behema*. Since under his reading the standard case already generalizes from *behema* to *behema*, the case of generalizing from *adam* to *behema* would be obvious — it requires far less logic. The only reason the *mishna* mentions it is to address the case of *reversion*: an animal that had been *muad* for both *adam* and *behema*, then saw three animals and refrained, may have reverted its *muad* status for *behema* while remaining *muad* for *adam*. The *mishna* is telling us that such partial reversion is valid — that *hazara b'behema hazara hi*, even without demonstrated reversion from *adam*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – הָא סְתָמָא הָוֵי מוּעָד; הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר: מִבְּהֵמָה לִבְהֵמָה סְתָמָא הָוֵי מוּעָד, מֵאָדָם לִבְהֵמָה צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּהָוֵי מוּעָד?!
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say that** the mishna **teaches** in the first clause: With regard to an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **but is not forewarned** with regard to other species, indicating that in **an ordinary** case **it is** considered **forewarned** with regard to other species, then there is a difficulty with the following clause of the mishna. **Now** that **it can be said** that even **from** being forewarned with regard to one species of **animal,** in **an ordinary** case, **it is** thereby considered **forewarned** with regard **to** other species of **animals, need it be said that from** its being forewarned with regard to **people it is** also considered **forewarned with regard to animals?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב זְבִיד אָמַר לָךְ: רֵישָׁא אַחֲזָרָה קָאֵי – כְּגוֹן דַּהֲוָה מוּעָד לְאָדָם וּמוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה, וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ מִבְּהֵמָה – דְּקָאֵי גַּבֵּי בְהֵמָה תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי וְלָא נָגַח. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ מֵאָדָם – חֲזָרָה דִבְהֵמָה לָאו חֲזָרָה הִיא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דַּחֲזָרָה דִבְהֵמָה מִיהָא חֲזָרָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Zevid** could have **said to you** in response: **The former clause** in the mishna **relates to** the ox **reverting** to its innocuous status, i.e., a case **where** the ox **was forewarned with regard to man and forewarned with regard to animals, and reverted** to its innocuous status **with regard to animals, as it stood in** close **proximity to an animal three times and did not gore. Lest you say** that **since it did not** demonstratively **revert to** its innocuous behavior toward **people,** as it did not refrain from goring people, its **reversal with regard to animals is not** considered **a reversal,** the mishna **teaches us that** its **reversal with regard to animals is nevertheless** considered **a reversal,** and it no longer has the status of a forewarned ox with regard to goring animals.

### Sumchus Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: מוּעָד לָאָדָם מוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר – וּמָה לְאָדָם מוּעָד, לִבְהֵמָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Zevid's opinion from a *baraita*: **Sumakhos says:** An ox that is **forewarned with regard to people is** considered **forewarned with regard to animals, due to an *a fortiori*** inference: **If it is forewarned with regard to people,** is it **not** clear **all the more so** that it is forewarned **with regard to animals?** The Gemara elaborates: **By inference, the first *tanna*,** i.e., the *tanna* of the mishna, **is saying** that the ox **is not** considered **forewarned** with regard to animals, in accordance with the opinion of Rav Pappa.

A *baraita* is now introduced: *Sumchus omer muad l'adam muad l'behema mikal vachomer* — Sumchus says that if something is *muad* for goring humans, it is certainly *muad* for goring animals, by *kal vachomer*. The logic is self-evident: if it will gore a person, it will certainly gore an animal. The *baraita* implies that the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* held the opposite — that *muad l'adam* does not make it *muad l'behema*. This seemingly confirms that the *tanna kamma* is reading *eino muad* without the *vav*, as a legal rule, in line with Rav Pappa.

### Teshuva Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב זְבִיד: סוֹמְכוֹס – אַחֲזָרָה קָאֵי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְתַנָּא קַמָּא: דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ חֲזָרָה דִבְהֵמָה חֲזָרָה הִיא – חֲזָרָה דִבְהֵמָה לָאו חֲזָרָה הִיא, מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מֵאָדָם: וּמָה מֵאָדָם לָא קָא (מ)הָדַר בֵּיהּ, מִבְּהֵמָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Zevid** could have **said to you** that **Sumakhos's** statement can be interpreted as **relating to** the ox **reverting** to its innocuous status, **and this is** what he **is saying to the first *tanna*:** Contrary to **what you are saying,** that the ox's **reversal with regard to animals is considered a reversal** although it has not yet reversed its behavior toward people, I maintain that its **reversal with regard to animals is not** considered **a reversal, due to an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* of an animal forewarned with regard to **people: If** the ox **has not reverted to** its innocuous behavior toward **people,** is it **not** clear **all the more so** that it has not truly reverted **to** its innocuous behavior toward **animals?**

Rav Zevid deflects: Sumchus is not disputing the initial establishment of *muad* status, which both agree generalizes. The dispute is over *reversion* — specifically, the scenario established earlier, where an animal was *muad* for both *adam* and *behema*, then encountered animals three times without goring. The *tanna kamma* holds that *hazara b'behema hazara hi* — reversion demonstrated for animals is valid reversion, even though the animal has not reverted from goring people. Sumchus disagrees: *mikal vachomer*, since it has not reverted from goring people — the harder thing — it has not truly reverted from goring animals. *Hazara d'behema lav hazara hi*. The reversion is not sincere if the more aggressive impulse remains unreformed.

Under Rav Zevid's framework, the initial establishment of *muad* always generalizes; the debate between *tanna kamma* and Sumchus is thus narrowly about the mechanism of reversion.

### Shabbat Revisited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמְרוּ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הֲרֵי זֶה מוּעָד לְשַׁבָּתוֹת וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לִימוֹת הַחוֹל? אָמַר לָהֶן: לְשַׁבָּתוֹת – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, לִימוֹת הַחוֹל – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Come** and **hear** a proof for Rav Zevid's opinion from the mishna: The Sages **said before Rabbi Yehuda:** What would be the *halakha* if **this** ox **is forewarned with regard to *Shabbatot* but is not forewarned with regard to weekdays? He said to them: For** damage it causes on ***Shabbatot*** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage,** and **for** damage it causes on **weekdays, he pays half** the cost of **the damage.**

The Gemara adduces evidence from the *Shabbos* case in the *mishna*. The text reads: *harei zeh muad l'shabbasos ve'eino muad l'yamos hachol* — he is *muad* for *Shabbasos* and not for weekdays. *Rav Zevid amar ve'eino muad tnan* — with the *vav*, it is descriptive: the students are presenting Rav Yehuda with a scenario (the animal gored on *Shabbos* and not on the weekday) and asking the *halacha*. Rabbi Yehuda confirms it. This reading makes sense: the *shayilu* — they were asking — and *hu nami ka mehadar lehu* — he was responding to their question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – שַׁיּוֹלֵי הוּא דְּקָא מְשַׁיְּילִי [לֵיהּ], וְהוּא נָמֵי קָמַהְדַּר לְהוּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – אַגְמוֹרֵי הוּא דְּקָא מַגְמְרִי לֵיהּ?! וְתוּ, אִיהוּ מַאי קָא מַהְדַּר לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that the mishna **teaches: But was not forewarned** with regard to weekdays, **they were asking him** about the *halakha* in that case, **and likewise, he was answering them** with a ruling. **But if you say** that **it teaches:** This ox that is forewarned with regard to *Shabbatot* **is not forewarned** with regard to weekdays, the mishna would be understood as saying that an ox that is forewarned with regard to Shabbat is not considered forewarned with regard to weekdays. Is it possible that the Sages **were teaching him** this *halakha*? **And furthermore, what was he responding to them?** They had already stated themselves that the ox is not considered forewarned with regard to weekdays.

But if the *mishna* reads *eino muad* without a *vav*, as Rav Pappa would have it, then the students are not posing a question — they are already stating a ruling: *muad l'shabbasos eino muad l'yamos hachol*. And then what is Rabbi Yehuda responding to? *Agmurei hu d'ka megamrei lei* — are they teaching *him* a *halacha*? That makes no sense. The structure of the *mishna* as a dialogue between students and Rabbi Yehuda strongly supports Rav Zevid's reading: with the *vav*, it is a question; without the *vav*, there is no question to answer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַנַּאי: מֵרֵישָׁא נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: אֶת שֶׁמּוּעָד לוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְאֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לוֹ – [מְשַׁלֵּם] חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yannai said:** Rav Zevid's opinion **can be inferred from the former clause** of the mishna **as well, as it teaches:** If the ox gores an animal or person **with regard to which it is forewarned,** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage, and** if it gores an animal or person **with regard to which it is not forewarned, he pays half** the cost of **the damage.**

Rav Yannai adds that one can also infer this from the *reisha*. After enumerating all the cases — *muad* for this and not for that — the *mishna* concludes: *es shehu muad lo meshalem nezek shalem v'es she'eino muad lo meshalem chatzi nezek*, the thing it is *muad* for pays full, the thing it is not *muad* for pays half. If the preceding clauses were already prescriptive rulings — *muad l'adam eino muad l'behema*, etc. — then this concluding line adds nothing. By daf 37 of this tractate, no reader needs to be told that a *muad* pays full and a *tam* pays half. But if the preceding clauses describe behavior, this final line is necessary: it tells you what follows legally from the pattern that was described. Thus the *mishna*'s structure as a whole corroborates Rav Zevid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי – פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that the mishna **teaches** in the first clause: With regard to an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **but is not forewarned** with regard to other species, this clause **is explaining** the *halakha* in that case, i.e., if the ox is forewarned only with regard to its species, its owner is liable to pay the full cost of the damage only if it gores another ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אֵינוֹ מוּעָד״ קָתָנֵי, פַּסְקַהּ; מַאי תּוּ ״אֶת שֶׁמּוּעָד לוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְאֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק״? עַד הַשְׁתָּא – לָא אַשְׁמְעִינַן דְּהָתָם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the mishna **teaches:** An ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species **is not forewarned** with regard to other species, and the *halakha* was already **determined** at the beginning of the mishna, namely, that the ox is not considered forewarned with regard to other species, then **what** need is there for the mishna to **further** state: If the ox gores an animal or person **with regard to which it is forewarned,** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage, and** if it gores an animal or person **with regard to which it is not forewarned, he pays half** the cost of **the damage? Has** the mishna **not taught us until now that for an innocuous** ox its owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage and for a forewarned** ox **he pays the full** cost of the **damage?**

The Gemara notes, however: *ve'im timtzi lomar nami isa l'Rav Pappa* — even so, the Gemara does not abandon Rav Pappa's framework entirely, because it is Rav Pappa's position that generates all the interesting questions about pattern identification that follow.

## Pattern Scope

### Mixed Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר נָמֵי אִיתָא לִדְרַב פָּפָּא; נָגַח שׁוֹר חֲמוֹר וְגָמָל – נַעֲשָׂה מוּעָד לַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And even if you say** that **Rav Pappa's** opinion, which says that an ox that is forewarned with regard to its own species is not considered forewarned with regard to other species, **is accepted,** nevertheless, if an ox **gored an ox, a donkey, and a camel, it is** thereby **rendered forewarned** with regard **to all** of them. The ox is rendered forewarned with regard to all three species, regardless of the fact that it did not gore each individual species three times.

Granting Rav Pappa's framework for the purposes of inquiry, the Gemara proceeds: *nagach shor chamor vegamal na'aseh muad lakol* — if an ox gored a *shor*, a *chamor*, and a *gamal*, it becomes *muad* for all animals. The principle under Rav Pappa is to identify the smallest level of abstraction that contains all the observed gorings as a common denominator. Here the three animals are of distinct species, and the common category encompassing all of them is simply *behema*, or perhaps all large animals. One might ask whether this would extend to *adam* as well, or whether it would apply to small animals — and the answer would depend on whether the three observed victims were of comparable size and whether people had been gored as well.

The *Rif* rules on the basis of these discussions that the *halacha* follows Rav Pappa, because all the subsequent cases of pattern identification work most naturally within his framework.

### Alternating Gores

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רָאָה שׁוֹר – נָגַח, שׁוֹר – לֹא נָגַח, שׁוֹר – נָגַח, שׁוֹר – לֹא נָגַח, שׁוֹר – נָגַח, שׁוֹר – לֹא נָגַח; נַעֲשָׂה מוּעָד לְסֵירוּגִין לִשְׁווֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If an ox **saw an ox** and **gored** it, and subsequently saw another **ox** but **did not gore** it, again saw **an ox** and **gored** it and then saw **an ox** but **did not gore** it, and a third time saw **an ox** and **gored** it and saw **an ox** and **did not gore** it, in this case **it is rendered forewarned with regard to alternate oxen.** The ox is considered forewarned with regard to goring every other ox that it sees and is considered innocuous with regard to the oxen in between. If it then gores two oxen in a row, the owner of the ox is liable for only half the cost of the damage for the second ox.

A *baraita* teaches: if an animal saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore, saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore, saw an ox and gored, saw an ox and did not gore — *na'aseh muad l'sirugin l'shorim* — it becomes *muad* for goring every other ox. The pattern of alternation is itself recognized as a *muad* pattern. If it then gores two oxen in a row, only the second goring triggers full liability — the first, as an "off" event in the alternating cycle, is still only *chatzi nezek*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רָאָה שׁוֹר – נָגַח, חֲמוֹר – לֹא נָגַח, סוּס – נָגַח, גָּמָל – לֹא נָגַח, פֶּרֶד – נָגַח, עָרוֹד – לֹא נָגַח; נַעֲשָׂה מוּעָד לְסֵירוּגִין לַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If an ox **saw an ox** and **gored** it, and then saw **a donkey** but **did not gore** it, and saw **a horse** and **gored** it, then saw **a camel** and **did not gore** it, and saw **a mule** and **gored** it, and then saw **a wild donkey [*arod*]** and **did not gore** it, in this case it **is rendered forewarned** with regard **to alternate** animals of **all** species.

A second *baraita* extends this: if the alternating pattern involves six different animals — ox, donkey, horse, camel, mule, wild ass, alternating gore and no-gore — *na'aseh muad l'sirugin l'khol*, it becomes *muad* for alternately goring any type of animal. The level of abstraction again captures the full breadth of what was observed.

### Teiku Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: נָגַח שׁוֹר, שׁוֹר וָשׁוֹר, חֲמוֹר וְגָמָל, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If an ox **gored** **an ox,** another **ox, and** a third **ox,** and then **a donkey and a camel,** with regard to **what is** it considered forewarned?

The Gemara raises a series of unresolved questions. Suppose an animal gored: ox, ox, ox, donkey, camel. Does the first three gorings — all oxen — fix its *muad* status as *muad l'shorim*, such that the subsequent donkey and camel gorings are insufficient to upgrade it to *muad lakol* (since two additional species are not yet three)? Or do we look at the last three — ox, donkey, camel — and declare it *muad lakol*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שׁוֹר בָּתְרָא – בָּתַר שְׁווֹרִים שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאַכַּתִּי לִשְׁווֹרִים הוּא דְּאִיַּיעַד – לְמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא אִיַּיעַד; אוֹ דִילְמָא, הַאי שׁוֹר בָּתְרָא – בָּתַר חֲמוֹר וְגָמָל שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִיַּיעַד לֵיהּ לְכוּלְּהוּ מִינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the possibilities: With regard to **this last ox** that was gored together with the donkey and the camel, **do we place it** together **with** the previously gored **oxen, and** accordingly the belligerent ox **was still rendered forewarned** only **with regard to oxen,** whereas **with regard to other** species it **was not rendered forewarned? Or perhaps we place this last ox** together **with the donkey and the camel, and it was rendered forewarned with regard to all of the species** it gored.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמוֹר וְגָמָל, שׁוֹר, שׁוֹר וָשׁוֹר, מַהוּ? הַאי שׁוֹר קַמָּא – בָּתַר חֲמוֹר וְגָמָל שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִיַּיעַד לֵיהּ לְכוּלְּהוּ מִינֵי; אוֹ דִילְמָא בָּתַר שְׁווֹרִים שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאַכַּתִּי לִשְׁווֹרִים הוּא דְּאִיַּיעַד – לְמִינָא אַחֲרִינָא לָא אִיַּיעַד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds a similar dilemma: If an ox gored **a donkey and a camel,** and then **an ox, an ox, and** another **ox, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara presents the possibilities: With regard to **this first ox** that it gored, **do we place it** together **with the camel and donkey, and** the belligerent ox **was** accordingly **rendered forewarned with regard to all species? Or perhaps we place it** together **with the** two **oxen** that it gored afterward, **and** accordingly, **it was still rendered forewarned** only **with regard to oxen,** whereas **it was not rendered forewarned with regard to other species.**

The reverse is also asked: chamor, gamal, shor, shor, shor. Do we take the first three — chamor, gamal, shor — yielding *muad lakol*, and disregard that the last three are all oxen? Or are we drawn to the grouping of three oxen, establishing it as only *muad l'shorim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁבָּת, שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת, אֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת וְשֵׁנִי בְּשַׁבָּת, מַהוּ? הָא שַׁבָּת בָּתְרָיְיתָא – בָּתַר שַׁבָּת הוּא דְּשָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאַכַּתִּי לְשַׁבָּת הוּא דְּאִיַּיעַד – לִימוֹת הַחוֹל לָא אִיַּיעַד; אוֹ דִילְמָא בָּתַר אֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת וְשֵׁנִי בְּשַׁבָּת שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִיַּיעַד לֵיהּ לְכוּלֵּי יוֹמָא?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if it gored on **Shabbat,** on **Shabbat, and** on **Shabbat,** i.e., on three consecutive *Shabbatot*, and then on **Sunday and** on **Monday, what is** the *halakha*? With regard to **this last Shabbat, do we place it** together **with** the previous **Shabbat, and** the ox **was still rendered forewarned** only **with regard to Shabbat,** whereas **with regard to weekdays it was not rendered forewarned? Or perhaps we place it** together **with** the goring on **Sunday and Monday, and it was** thereby **rendered forewarned with regard to all days** of the week.

The Gemara applies the same uncertainty to days of the week. Suppose the animal gored on *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, then Sunday, Monday. Does the first three establish *muad* for *Shabbos* alone, leaving Sunday and Monday insufficient for a pattern of their own? Or do we take the last three — *Shabbos*, Sunday, Monday — and declare it *muad* for all days?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִישִׁי בְּשַׁבָּת וְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת, שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת, מַהוּ? הָא שַׁבָּת קַמַּיְיתָא – בָּתַר חֲמִישִׁי בְּשַׁבָּת וְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִיַּיעַד לְכוּלְּהוּ יוֹמֵי; אוֹ דִילְמָא הָא שַׁבָּת קַמַּיְיתָא – בָּתַר שַׁבָּתוֹת הוּא דְּשָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ, וּלְשֶׁבָּתוֹת הוּא דְּאִיַּיעַד?
>
> **Translation:** If an ox gored on **Thursday, and Friday, and Shabbat,** and then the next **Shabbat and** the next **Shabbat** after that, **what is** the *halakha*? With regard to **this first Shabbat, do we place it** together **with Thursday and Friday, and** thereby hold that the ox **was rendered forewarned with regard to all days** of the week? **Or perhaps we place this first Shabbat** together **with** the other ***Shabbatot*, and** the ox **is rendered forewarned only with regard to *Shabbatot*?**

The reverse is similarly posed: Thursday, Friday, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*, *Shabbos*. Do we take the first three, establishing *muad* for all days of the week? Or are we drawn to the grouping of three consecutive *Shabbasos*, yielding *muad* only for *Shabbos*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

All of these remain unresolved — *teiku*. The underlying question of whether the determining grouping is the first three gorings or the last three is not settled.

One should note that for any of these cases involving interim days, the analysis assumes the animal had no opportunity to gore on those days — that is, it was not presented with an ox on Sunday through Friday. If it encountered animals during those intervals and refrained, that would separately bear on its reversion from *muad* status for those days.

### Monthly Dates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָגַח שׁוֹר יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּחוֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּחוֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר בְּחוֹדֶשׁ זֶה – פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל,
>
> **Translation:** § If **an ox gored** on **the fifteenth day of this month, and** subsequently gored on **the sixteenth day of** the **month** after **that, and** then on **the seventeenth** day **of** the **month** after **that,** the *halakha* is subject to **a dispute** between **Rav and Shmuel** with regard to a parallel discussion concerning a woman whose menstrual cycle begins on a different day each month.

The Gemara now presents a case that reveals just how thoroughly formalist the system becomes: an ox gored on the 15th of one month, the 16th of the next, and the 17th of the following month. Here there is no intuitive behavioral explanation for the pattern — unlike *Shabbos*, for which Rashi and Tosafot offered reasons involving the animal's idleness or the unfamiliar clothing of people. Nevertheless, the *muad* framework apparently applies even to such arbitrary-seeming date sequences.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר: רָאֲתָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּחֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּחֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר בְּחֹדֶשׁ זֶה – רַב אָמַר: קָבְעָה לָהּ וֶסֶת, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד שֶׁתְּשַׁלֵּשׁ בְּדִילּוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was stated:** If a woman **saw** menstrual blood on **the fifteenth day of this month, and** on **the sixteenth day of** the **month** after **that, and** on **the seventeenth** day **of** the **month** after **that, Rav says: She has** thereby **established her menstrual cycle [*veset*],** i.e., a month and one day. **And Shmuel says:** Her menstrual cycle is not established **until she skips** a day **three times.** According to Shmuel, the cycle is established in this case not by the date per se, but rather by the pattern of one additional day every month. Only when this occurs for three consecutive months, i.e., when she menstruates in the fourth month, is this pattern established.

The specific question raised is whether the pattern of "plus one day each successive month" — fifteenth, sixteenth, seventeenth — constitutes an established *muad* pattern, and if so, what it predicts for the following month. The problem is that while three data points exist, the "plus one" increment has only been observed twice: between the fifteenth and sixteenth, and between the sixteenth and seventeenth. The Gemara notes that this parallels a debate between Rav and Shmuel in the laws of *vesset* in *Niddah* — a woman's established cycle for the onset of her period. *Ra'ata yom tes-vav be-chodesh zeh, yom tes-zayin be-chodesh zeh, yom yod-zayin be-chodesh zeh* — she saw on the fifteenth of one month, the sixteenth of the next, the seventeenth of the next. Rav holds *kave'ah lah vesset* — she has established a cycling pattern, and next month she anticipates the eighteenth. Shmuel holds *ad she-teshalesh b'dilug* — she needs three instances of the incremental shift, meaning four months total, before the pattern is fixed. The same dispute applies to the goring ox.

### Shofar Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁמַע קוֹל שׁוֹפָר וְנָגַח, קוֹל שׁוֹפָר וְנָגַח, קוֹל שׁוֹפָר וְנָגַח – נַעֲשָׂה מוּעָד לְשׁוֹפָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said:** If an ox **heard the sound of a shofar and gored,** and again heard **the sound of a shofar and gored,** and a third time heard **the sound of a shofar and gored, it is rendered forewarned with regard to** the sound of ***shofarot*.**

Rava teaches: if an animal heard a *shofar* blast and gored, heard a *shofar* and gored, heard a *shofar* and gored — *na'aseh muad l'shofaros* — it is now *muad* to gore every time it hears a *shofar*. One might have thought this is obvious. But the Gemara raises a possible objection: perhaps the first time should not count, since the *shofar* blast was so startling and disorienting that the goring was essentially a reflexive fright response — *siyuta b'alma* — and does not yet establish a pattern of habituated vicious behavior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָךְ שׁוֹפָר קַמָּא – סְיוּטָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּנַקְטֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't this obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this first shofar merely startled [*siyyuta*]** the ox, prompting it to gore, and that consequently it should not count for the purpose of rendering the ox forewarned, Rava **teaches us** that since the ox repeatedly gored upon hearing the sound of a shofar, this sound is considered a consistent impetus for its goring.

The Gemara rejects this: we still count all three instances, and three times is three times.

### Formalist Conclusion

By the end of the *sugya*, the system has moved decisively toward formalism. The question of *Shabbos*, which Rashi and Tosafot explained on behavioral grounds, gives way to a framework in which patterns of goring on specific calendar dates — the fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth of the month — are treated as legally constitutive *muad* patterns without any causal explanation. The analysis under Rav Pappa's framework is not asking *why* the animal gores in a given pattern; it is asking only *what* pattern the evidence supports, and identifying the smallest level of abstraction that captures the observed data.

As one might ask: what counts as a pattern? It cannot be that any three numbers constitute one. There must be some criterion of simplicity or describability — a pattern like *n*, *n*+1, *n*+2 is recognizable; a sequence like 15, 117, 34 is not. The Gemara does not spell out this criterion explicitly, but the cases it considers — same-day-of-week gorings, consecutive monthly dates, alternating gorings — all share the quality of being simply and uniformly describable. That implicit standard of what constitutes a recognizable pattern appears to be the unspoken logic underlying the entire formalist approach.
