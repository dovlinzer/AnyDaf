# Sanhedrin 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Appointment & Shmuel HaK…

### Leap Year Requirement

The *daf* opens on 10b with a *tanna rabbanan* addressing the laws of *ibbur hashana* — the intercalation of the year. The *baraita* states: *ain me'abrin et hashana ella bimezumamin* — the year may only be intercalated by those who have been specifically appointed. This principle was already encountered in the Yerushalmi, and the Bavli now takes up the same point, moving rapidly given that familiarity.

### Rabban Gamliel Story

The *baraita* continues with a *ma'aseh* involving Rabban Gamliel. He instructed his attendants: *hishkimu li shiva l'aliyah* — rise early and bring me seven people to the upper chamber. The word *hishkimu* is notable; it implies that the proceedings were to begin at first light. When Rabban Gamliel arrived, however, he found that eight people had gathered. He declared: *mi hu she'ala shelo birshut yeired* — whoever came without permission must leave. The rule is firm: only those formally appointed may participate in the intercalation.

### Shmuel HaKatan

At that moment, Shmuel HaKatan rose and declared: *ani hu she'aliti shelo birshut* — it is I who came without being called. He explained that he had not come to participate in the *ibbur hashana* itself, but rather *lilmod halacha lema'aseh hitztarchti* — he needed to observe the proceedings in order to learn practical *halacha*. Rashi notes that the phrasing here is somewhat vaguer than the parallel in the Yerushalmi, and understands it to mean that Shmuel HaKatan simply wished to observe how the process was conducted.

Rabban Gamliel responded warmly: *shev bni shev* — sit, my son, sit. *Re'uyin kol hashanim kullan lehitabber al yadecha* — all the years would be fit to be intercalated by you. Nevertheless, the sages have ruled that the *ibbur hashana* may only be performed by those who were formally appointed, and this rule cannot be set aside even for someone as worthy as Shmuel HaKatan.

The Bavli then adds a clarifying remark — signaled by the shift from Hebrew into Aramaic — that it was not in fact Shmuel HaKatan who had entered uninvited: *velo Shmuel HaKatan hu, ella inish achrini* — it was another person entirely. *Umachmat kisufa hu de'avid* — Shmuel HaKatan acted as he did purely out of concern for the other person's embarrassment. He took the blame upon himself to spare the actual intruder from humiliation. This detail illuminates and completes the story as it appeared in the Yerushalmi.

## Ethos of Modesty

### Rabbi Chiya & Garlic

The Gemara now pivots to explore more broadly the ethos of protecting others from embarrassment, even at personal cost — what one might call the ethics of not pointing the finger. A further story is introduced: *ki ha de-yatv Rabbi* — there was an incident when Rabbi (Rebbi) was sitting and delivering his *shiur*, and he detected the smell of garlic. He announced: *mi she'achal shum yetzei* — whoever ate garlic must leave. At that point, *amad Rabbi Chiya veyatza* — Rabbi Chiya rose and left. Once Rabbi Chiya left, *amdu kullan veyatzu* — everyone else rose and departed as well.

The following morning, Rabbi Shimon b'Rabbi — Rebbi's son — encountered Rabbi Chiya and said to him: *ata hu shetziaarta le'abba?* — Was it you who caused my father this anguish by coming to the *shiur* having eaten garlic? Rabbi Chiya replied: *lo tehei kazot beYisrael* — God forbid that such a thing should be.

Rashi understands the son's question as a genuine accusation: *she'achalta shum venadaf harei'ach* — that Rabbi Chiya had been the one who ate the garlic. But there is also a compelling alternative reading: the son was not accusing Rabbi Chiya of eating garlic, but rather of disrupting the entire *shiur* by rising and causing everyone else to follow. On this reading, Rabbi Chiya's response — *lo tehei kazot beYisrael* — means that it would have been worse to allow an innocent or guilty party to be shamed publicly than to endure the disruption of the class.

### Justice vs. Dignity

This alternative reading carries a pointed implicit critique of Rebbi himself. By choosing to leave, Rabbi Chiya was in effect registering a position: one should not publicly call out an individual and demand that a guilty party expose himself. The tension here is real and worth pausing over. We are in a tractate fundamentally concerned with *din* — with guilt, innocence, and judicial process — and yet here the Gemara is celebrating someone who actively shielded a wrongdoer from accountability. The juxtaposition is not accidental.

The resolution is that not everything rises to the level of a matter for the court. This is an interpersonal and communal setting, not a formal judicial proceeding. Human relationships and communal life cannot and should not operate solely on the logic of establishing guilt. There are moments when protecting a person's dignity is the more urgent value, even if it means that accountability is deferred or foregone. The question of when to speak and when to remain silent — the ethics of not snitching versus the ethics of truth-telling — is genuinely complex, and the Gemara's interest in establishing precedent for Rabbi Chiya's behavior suggests that this position requires defense precisely because it is not self-evidently correct.

### Rabbi Meir Precedent

The Gemara asks: *meheicha gamar lah mi-Rabbi Meir* — from where did Rabbi Chiya learn to act this way? He learned it from Rabbi Meir. A *baraita* is cited: a woman came to the *beis midrash* of Rabbi Meir and declared that one of the men present had married her through an act of sexual intercourse — a form of *kiddushin* that, while technically effective, is entirely improper. *Amad Rabbi Meir ve-chasav lah get krisus* — Rabbi Meir stood up and wrote her a *get*. Once he did so, *amdu ve-chasvu kulam ve-nasnu lah* — all the other members of the *beis midrash* rose and each wrote her a *get* as well, providing cover for the actual party so that no individual would have to come forward and be identified.

This case is in important respects different from the garlic story, because here the action was not merely protective of someone's dignity but also practically beneficial to the woman. Had no one come forward, she would have remained an *agunah* — unable to remarry. Rabbi Meir's stratagem simultaneously protected the guilty party from exposure and ensured that the woman received her *get*. One might also note with some amusement that Rabbi Meir was himself a scribe, and that the *baraita* says all of them wrote — perhaps the school had a scribal dimension and they were all capable of drafting a *get* independently.

### Chain of Learning

The Gemara then traces a chain of tradition: *Rabbi Meir meheicha gamar leh? Mi-Shmuel HaKatan* — and from where did Rabbi Meir learn? From Shmuel HaKatan. *Mi-Shmuel HaKatan meheicha gamar leh? Mi-Shechaniah ben Yechiel* — and Shmuel HaKatan learned from Shechaniah ben Yechiel. The verse cited is from Ezra: *va-ya'an Shechaniah ben Yechiel mi-benei Elam va-yomer le-Ezra anachnu ma'alnu be-Eloheinu* — Shechaniah spoke in the collective first person, "we have betrayed our God," even though he himself had not taken a foreign wife. By speaking in the plural, he avoided singling out the guilty parties and dividing the community into the righteous and the wicked.

*Shechaniah ben Yechiel meheicha gamar leh? Mi-Yehoshua* — and Shechaniah learned from Joshua. When God said to Joshua, *kum lach lamah zeh atah nofel al panecha chata Yisrael* — Israel has sinned (this in the context of Achan's violation of the *cherem*) — Joshua asked: Master of the Universe, who sinned? And God replied: *ve-chi diltor ani lach?* — Am I your *diltor*? The word *diltor* almost certainly derives from the Latin *delator*, meaning an informer or denouncer. God said: I am not your tattletale. *Hatel goralos* — cast lots and find out for yourself.

This is the most striking case in the chain, because here it is God Himself who declines to identify the wrongdoer, even though the situation ultimately required resolution and Achan had to be found out. The Gemara does not resolve the tension — ultimately a process of lot-casting was used, and perhaps the point is that the process itself gave Achan the opportunity to come forward voluntarily. The collective language also has a darker side: if the wrongdoer is not identified, the entire community bears the burden, and the rhetoric of *chata Yisrael* — Israel sinned — can shade into collective punishment, which is its own moral difficulty.

The chain continues: *ve-iba'is eima mi-Moshe* — or alternatively, the tradition traces back to Moses. When Moses declared *ad anah me'antem* — how long will you refuse to observe God's commandments? — referring to those who had gone out to collect *manna* on Shabbat, he addressed the entire nation collectively even though only a minority had actually transgressed. Here again the use of collective language shields the individuals while holding the community as a whole to account.

## Shmuel HaKatan Merit

### Bat Kol & Hillel

The Gemara now introduces a *tanna rabbanan* on an adjacent theme, prompted by the prominence of Shmuel HaKatan in the preceding discussion. After the last of the prophets — Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — *nistalka Ruach HaKodesh mi-Yisrael*: the spirit of holiness departed from Israel. Nevertheless, *hayu mishtamshim be-bat kol* — the sages would still make use of the *bat kol*, the heavenly echo, some attenuated form of divine communication short of full prophecy.

On one occasion, the sages were gathered in the upper story of a certain Beit Gorya in Jericho, and a *bat kol* issued from the heavens declaring: *yesh kan echad she-ra'uy she-tishreh alav Shechinah ke-Moshe Rabbeinu* — there is one person here who is worthy of having the divine presence rest upon him as it rested upon Moses. Some authorities omit the comparison to Moses, feeling it is theologically untenable given the principle that *lo kam navi od be-Yisrael ke-Moshe* — no prophet like Moses has arisen in Israel. The heavenly voice, however, was not necessarily claiming equivalence of prophetic degree; it was speaking of the worthiness of the individual for divine presence. *Ela she-ein doro zakai le-kach* — but the generation does not merit it. *Ve-natnu chachamim et eineihem be-Hillel HaZaken* — the sages cast their eyes toward Hillel the Elder as the person being indicated. When Hillel died, they eulogized him: *hai chasid hai anav* — alas, the pious one; alas, the humble one — *talmido shel Ezra*, the disciple of Ezra. It is notable that they called him a disciple of Ezra rather than of Moses: Ezra represents the world of rabbinic, post-prophetic Judaism, which is the world Hillel inhabited and shaped.

### Shmuel Worthy of Prophecy

On another occasion, the sages were gathered in an upper chamber in Yavne, and a second *bat kol* emerged with the same declaration: *yesh kan echad she-ra'uy she-tishreh Shechinah, ela she-ein doro zakai le-kach* — one person here is worthy of the divine presence, but the generation does not merit it. *Ve-natnu chachamim et eineihem bi-Shmuel HaKatan* — the sages looked toward Shmuel HaKatan. This is why the tradition about him was introduced here. His very name — *HaKatan*, the small one — may reflect his extraordinary modesty, the same quality that caused him to rise and take blame for an uninvited intruder rather than expose that person to humiliation. And yet, beneath that apparent smallness, he was in reality one of the most spiritually exalted figures of his generation.

When Shmuel HaKatan died, they eulogized him with the same words used for Hillel: *hai chasid hai anav, talmido shel Hillel* — alas, the pious one; alas, the humble one — disciple of Hillel. The chain continues: Hillel is the disciple of Ezra, and Shmuel HaKatan is the disciple of Hillel.

### Shmuel's Prophecy

*Af hu amar bi-sh'at mitato* — even Shmuel HaKatan himself, at the moment of his death, gave expression to something like prophetic vision. He declared: *Shimon ve-Yishmael le-charba* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yishmael the Kohen Gadol will fall by the sword. *Ve-chavrohi le-katla* — their colleagues, including Rabbi Akiva and those who were killed alongside him, will be put to death by other means. *Ve-sha'ar ama le-viza* — the rest of the people will be despoiled. *Ve-akan sagiyan atidin le-meiti al alma* — and great suffering will befall the world. This was a vision of the Hadrianic persecution that would come. The fact that this prophecy emerged at the moment of Shmuel HaKatan's death serves as confirmation of what the *bat kol* had declared: he was indeed one in whom the *Shechinah* was worthy of resting, and at the threshold of death, when the soul draws closest to its source, this capacity briefly manifested.

### Yehuda ben Bava

The Gemara adds that they wished to offer the same eulogy — *hai chasid hai anav* — for Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, but *nitrefa sha'ah* — the moment was snatched away from them. *She-ein maspidin al harugei malchut* — one may not deliver a formal *hesped* for those killed by the Roman government, for fear of drawing the authorities' wrath. Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava was among the *harugei malchut*, and so the eulogy that would have honored him could not be publicly delivered.

## Nasi Authority

### Nasi Consent

Returning now to the laws of *ibbur shanah*, the Gemara introduces another *tanna rabbanan*: *ein me'abrin et ha-shanah ela im ken yirtzeh Nasi* — the year may not be intercalated unless the *Nasi* consents. It is not sufficient that the *Nasi* appoint the members of the court; he must also affirmatively agree to the decision to add the month. Appointment of the participants and ratification of the outcome are two distinct requirements.

### Retroactive Validation

A *ma'aseh* illustrates how this requirement was navigated in practice. Rabban Gamliel traveled to obtain some authorization from a Roman official in Syria and was delayed in returning to Eretz Yisrael. In his absence, the appointed court proceeded with the *ibbur shanah* — but conditionally: *al menas sheyirtzeh Rabban Gamliel* — on the stipulation that Rabban Gamliel would ratify the decision upon his return. When he returned, already into Adar Sheni, he declared *rotzeh ani* — I consent — and *nimtza shana muberet* — the intercalation was thereby retroactively validated. The *chiddush* here is that ratification after the fact, provided it was stipulated from the outset as a condition, suffices to validate the court's action. Had Rabban Gamliel returned and withheld his consent, the intercalation would have been retroactively invalid — and the community would have found itself, one imagines, suddenly in the middle of Nissan.

## Grounds for Intercalation

### Valid Reasons

The Gemara presents a *tanna rabbanan* enumerating valid reasons for intercalating the year. The year may be intercalated *mipnei haderachim* — because the roads are impassable due to mud, making *aliyah l'regel* impossible — *umipnei ha-gisharim* — because bridges have been washed out — *umipnei tanurei pesachim* — because the outdoor ovens used to roast the *korban pesach* are still waterlogged and have not dried out. An additional valid reason is that diaspora communities — *galuyot Yisrael* — have set out on the pilgrimage but circumstances have delayed them and they will not arrive in time for Pesach.

### Invalid Reasons

By contrast, the *baraita* identifies reasons that are insufficient. *Aval lo mipnei hasheleg* — but not because of snow during the Nissan period; people will make the journey even through snow. *Ve-lo mipnei hatzina* — nor because of cold; that alone is not grounds for adding a month. *Ve-lo mipnei galuyot Yisrael shelo akru mimkomam* — nor because diaspora communities have not yet mobilized from their places of origin. If the people have simply failed to begin their journey when they should have, that delinquency on their part does not justify intercalation.

### Animals & Birds

A further *tanna rabbanan* addresses the status of animal and bird maturity as grounds for intercalation. One does not intercalate the year because the kids (*gedayim*) or lambs (*tela'im*) are too small — that is, one does not add a month to allow them to grow larger for the *korban pesach*, even though these are precisely the animals brought as the *paschal* offering. Similarly, one does not intercalate because the young birds (*gozalot*) have not yet matured — women in particular would bring bird offerings for post-childbirth sacrifices and would consolidate these obligations for *aliyah l'regel*, but the immaturity of the birds is not a sufficient basis for adding a month.

However, *aval osin otam se'ad lashana* — these considerations may be adduced as supporting factors. They cannot stand alone as the reason for intercalation, but once a valid reason exists, the smallness of the animals or birds may be cited as an additional rationale.

## Official Declaration

### Aramaic Edict Text

Rabbi Yanai transmits in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel the actual Aramaic text used when issuing the declaration of intercalation — a remarkable survival of what appears to be an authentic official edict:

> *Mehodinan anachna lachon d'gozlanaya rakikin, v'imraya d'arkin, v'zimna d'aviva lo mata, v'shapir miltza b'anpai, v'osifna al shata da tlatin yomin.*

We hereby inform you that the birds are still tender, the flock is still small, the time of the *aviv* — the ripening of the grain — has not yet arrived, and the matter is good in our eyes; we have therefore added thirty days to the year.

This text is a precious document. The primary operative reason is that Nissan must coincide with *chodesh ha-aviv* — the month of ripening grain — and this astronomical-agricultural condition has not yet been met. The birds and the flocks are cited as *se'ad* — supporting considerations — but the *aviv* is the essential basis. The declaration was composed in Palestinian Aramaic, the vernacular of the period. While the formal literary language of rabbinic rulings was Mishnaic Hebrew, official edicts addressed to the broader population — and particularly to the diaspora communities — would naturally have been issued in Aramaic, and it is this edict-text that the Gemara preserves.

### Month Length

One might ask: the edict specifies thirty days, but Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is recorded elsewhere as holding that *ibbur hashana* is *chodesh* — a month, which is twenty-nine days. Is there a contradiction? Rav Papa resolves this: *ratzu chodesh ratzu shloshim yom* — one may choose. Adar Sheni may be constituted as either twenty-nine or thirty days; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel does not insist that it must be only twenty-nine, but rather that both options are available.

## Father & Son Contrast

### Mighty vs. Humble

Having cited Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's declaration, the Gemara pauses to note a striking irony: *ta chazi ma ika bein takifaya kadmaya l'invatnusaya batrayei* — come and see the difference between the mighty early ones and the humble later ones. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's edict reads *v'shapir miltza b'anpai* — the matter is good in my eyes — using a first-person royal formulation. He is speaking as the sole authority whose assent makes the intercalation valid. This is the son, the later and ostensibly "humbler" generation.

Compare this to the earlier generation — the father — Rabban Gamliel, who is generally remembered for his forthright exercise of authority: it was he who compelled Rabbi Yehoshua to appear before him on what Rabbi Yehoshua maintained was Yom Kippur by his own reckoning, and who made a point throughout his tenure of asserting the supremacy of the *Nasi*. Yet, as the Gemara now shows, Rabban Gamliel's own written edict speaks in the first-person plural: *v'shapir miltza b'anpai u-ve-anpei chaverai* — the matter is good to me and to my colleagues. The seemingly more authoritarian father used the more collegial formulation; the son who came after him spoke in terms of his individual authority.

### Three Letters

The Gemara cites a *baraita* describing Rabban Gamliel seated on the steps of the Temple Mount, with his scribe Yochanan standing before him and three pieces of cloth already prepared for writing. He directed Yochanan to take each cloth in succession and compose three separate letters.

### Galilee & South Letters

The first letter was addressed to the communities of Upper and Lower Galilee: *le-achana benei Galila illa'ah, le-achana benei Galila tatta'ah* — to our brothers in the upper Galilee and to our brothers in the lower Galilee — *shelomchon yisge* — may your peace be increased. *Mehode'in anan lechon di-zman bi'ura mata* — we inform you that the time of *bi'ur* has arrived — the obligation, which falls every third year of the *shemita* cycle, to remove all *ma'aser* from one's home. In the Galilee the principal crop was olives, and so the letter specified: *leafrushei ma'asra mi-ma'atana de-zeita* — to separate *ma'aser* from the presses of olives.

The second letter was addressed to the communities of the south: *le-achana benei daroma* — to our brothers in the south — with the same blessing of peace and the same notification of *bi'ur*. In the south, the dominant crop was grain rather than olives, and so the letter read: *leafrushei ma'asra mi-umrei shivla* — to separate *ma'aser* from the bundles of wheat.

### Diaspora Letter

The third letter was addressed to the diaspora: *le-achana benei galvasa be-Bovel ve-le-achana de-vi-Madai ve-lish'ar kol galvuta de-Yisrael* — to our brothers of the exile in Babylonia and in Medai and to all the exile-communities of Israel. The greeting is notably extended: *shelomchon yisge le-alam* — may your peace be increased forever — with the word *le-alam* added, perhaps befitting communities at a greater remove who needed a more expansive expression of connection.

The body of this letter contains the intercalation notice in the same Aramaic formula: *de-gozalaya rakikin ve-imraya adkin ve-zimna de-aviva lo mata* — the birds are tender, the flock is small, the time of the *aviv* has not arrived — *ve-shafra milta be-anpai u-ve-anpei chaverai* — and the matter is good to me and to my colleagues — *ve-osifat al shatta da yomin tlatin* — and we have added thirty days to the year.

This document has the character of a genuine historical record, and indeed the *tanna* transmitting it in the Tosefta appears to have preserved the actual Aramaic text of these letters. It stands apart from nearly everything else in this section of the Gemara.

### Humility Explained

The Gemara raises what is now an apparent paradox: Rabban Gamliel, known for his assertiveness in maintaining the authority of the *Nasi*, here defers to collegial language — *me and my colleagues* — while his son Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel speaks in the singular. The Gemara offers one resolution: *dilma batar de-avruha* — perhaps this letter was written after the period when Rabban Gamliel was temporarily removed from his position as *Nasi* for overstepping. Having been deposed and reinstated, he may have emerged chastened and more inclined toward humility.

Tosafot, however, offer a more structural explanation in a single sentence: *peirush de-hayah lo chaver Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya she-hayah nasi imo* — after the incident of his removal, Rabban Gamliel did not rule alone; Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was appointed as co-*Nasi* alongside him. On this reading, Rabban Gamliel's use of collegial language was not merely a rhetorical gesture of modesty, but an accurate reflection of institutional reality: he now shared authority, and *u-ve-anpei chaverai* — and my colleagues — referred quite literally to his co-leader. The apparent humility of the father, then, was in part a consequence of the political circumstances that followed his earlier exercise of unilateral authority.

The daf thus closes with the theme of *nasi* authority held in tension with collegial governance — a theme that will continue into the next *daf*, where the Bavli will take up the third major principle encountered already in the Yerushalmi: the requirement that intercalation be performed specifically in Eretz Yisrael.
