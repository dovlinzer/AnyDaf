# Shabbat 28b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Yosef's Leather

### Tumat Ohalim Intro

The *sugya* opens by returning to a question that had occupied the preceding discussion: which materials are subject to *tumat ohalim*, the *tumah* of tents. The Gemara had established that certain fabrics — specifically those used in the construction of the *mishkan* — can, when fashioned into a tent, themselves become *tamei* through contact with a corpse beneath them, even though attached structures normally do not receive *tumah*. The question had turned to leather from non-kosher animals: does such leather also convey *tumat ohalim*? After considerable discussion, the Gemara appeared to resolve that the leather of non-kosher animals is indeed included, drawing on sources beyond the *mishkan* alone. Against this backdrop, the Gemara now asks what purpose remains for the teaching of Rav Yosef.

### Heavenly Purposes

The teaching in question is the one Rav Yosef transmitted: *lo huchshru limlechet shamayim ela or behema tehora bilvad* — only the leather of a kosher animal was rendered fit for heavenly purposes. If this principle has no bearing on the laws of *tumat ohalim*, since even non-kosher leather is included in those laws, then *le-mai hilchata* — for what practical purpose did Rav Yosef teach it?

The Gemara's first answer is *tefillin*: Rav Yosef comes to teach that one may not use the leather of a non-kosher animal in the construction of *tefillin*. But the Gemara immediately rejects this, noting that this is already known from a *pasuk*: *lema'an tihye torat Hashem beficha* — so that the Torah of God should be in your mouth. From this the Rabbis derive *min hamutar beficha*, that *tefillin* may only be made from an animal whose flesh is permitted to be placed in one's mouth — that is, a kosher animal. Rav Yosef's teaching is therefore superfluous on this point.

### Tefillin & Torah

The Gemara then suggests that Rav Yosef's teaching addresses the leather used for the boxes of the *tefillin* — not the *klaf* on which the *parshiyot* are written, but the casings themselves. And in fact this principle extends beyond *tefillin* alone: any sacred parchment that carries *kedushah* — whether *mezuzah*, *tefillin*, or *sefer torah* — falls under the *pasuk* of *torat Hashem beficha*, so the *klaf* is already covered.

But the Gemara raises another objection: even regarding the boxes of the *tefillin*, the requirement for kosher leather could be derived from Abaye's teaching that the *shin* inscribed on the *shel rosh* is a *halacha le-moshe mi-sinai*. The *shin* represents one of the letters of God's name — and Rashi explains that when the *shin* of the *shel rosh*, the *dalet* at the back of the head, and the *yud* of the *shel yad* are combined, they form the Divine name. Since the *shin* is inscribed on the leather of the *tefillin*, it too falls under *torat Hashem beficha*, and again Rav Yosef's teaching would appear unnecessary.

### Hair & Sinews

The Gemara therefore advances to yet another candidate: *le-charchan be-se'aran u-letafren be-gidan* — the requirement that the *klafim* placed inside the compartments of the *tefillin* be bound with string made from the hair of a kosher animal, and that the boxes themselves be sewn shut using the sinews of a kosher animal.

Regarding the hair: when the small parchment scrolls are rolled up and placed inside the *tefillin*, a thin string is wound around them to hold them in their scrolled shape. That string, the Gemara teaches, must come from the hair of a kosher animal. Regarding the sinews (*gidin*): in a *sefer torah*, multiple sheets of parchment are sewn together using thread made from the intestinal sinews of a kosher animal. Similarly, the boxes of the *tefillin* are sewn shut, and again the thread must derive from a kosher animal.

The Gemara then suggests that even this is already known, since these details are themselves part of a *halacha le-moshe mi-sinai*: a *baraita* establishes that *tefillin* must be square (*meruba'ot*) as a *halacha le-moshe mi-sinai*, and the same tradition includes the details that the parchments are wrapped with hair (*nichrachot be-se'aran*) and the boxes sewn with sinews (*nitparot be-gidan*). Since the *halacha le-moshe mi-sinai* itself says *be-se'aran* and *be-gidan* — using the possessive form referring to the animal — the implication that these materials must come from kosher animals is already embedded within it.

### Tefillin Straps

The Gemara therefore arrives at the ultimate purpose of Rav Yosef's teaching: *ela le-retzuot* — the straps of the *tefillin*. It is regarding the *retzuot* that one might not have known to require kosher leather, and that is what Rav Yosef teaches.

One might object: does not Rav Yitzchak's teaching that *retzuot shchorot halacha le-moshe mi-sinai* — that the straps must be black, as a *halacha le-moshe mi-sinai* — already imply the requirement of kosher leather? The Gemara rejects this: *midei gmirie shchorot, tahorot mi gmirie* — we have received a tradition that the straps must be black, but we have not received a tradition that they must be from a kosher animal. The color and the species are independent requirements, and the latter is not subsumed within the former. Therefore, it is precisely here, regarding the straps, that Rav Yosef's principle — that only the leather of a kosher animal is fit for *melechet shamayim* — adds something that could not be derived elsewhere.

## Tachash Identity

### Unique Creature

Having established the practical import of Rav Yosef's teaching, the Gemara returns to a question of broader curiosity: *mai havi alei de-tachash* — what is the conclusion regarding this *tachash*? The *tachash* had surfaced in the context of the *mishkan*, whose outer covering was made of *tachash* skins, and the question was whether those skins came from a kosher or non-kosher animal. The Gemara has already resolved that the *halacha* of *tumat ohalim* does not turn on this question, since non-kosher leather is included regardless. But what, in fact, was this creature?

Rabbi Ela citing Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish transmits the following in the name of Rabbi Meir: *tachash haya bi-ymei Moshe, birya bi-fnei atzmah hayah* — the *tachash* that existed in the time of Moses was its own unique creature. *Ve-lo hichri'u bo chachamim im min chaya im min behema hu* — the Sages could not determine whether it belonged to the category of *chaya* (wild animal) or *behema* (domesticated animal). This is reminiscent of the *koy*, regarding which the Gemara elsewhere debates the same classification with significant halachic consequences — whether its *chelev* is forbidden, whether its blood requires covering, and so forth. The designation *birya bi-fnei atzmah* may therefore mean not that it was a wholly singular creature that appeared only once, but that it occupies an ambiguous position between recognized categories.

### Physical Features

Yet the Gemara's continuation suggests something far more remarkable. *Ve-keren achat hayta lo be-mitzcho* — it had one horn in its forehead. *U-le-fi sha'a nizdamen lo le-Moshe* — and it appeared before Moses only for that particular moment in time. *Va-asah mimenu mishkan* — and from its skin the *mishkan* was fashioned. *Ve-nignaz* — and then it was hidden away, no longer accessible to future generations. The detail of *nignaz* — that it disappeared entirely after the *mishkan* was completed — reinforces the impression of a genuinely singular, perhaps mystical creature. It was not merely unclassifiable; it appears to have been a once-in-the-lifetime-of-the-world phenomenon.

### Tachash ID Theories

Two striking features have now been noted: multicolored skin (mentioned earlier in the *sugya*) and a single horn on the forehead. These features have prompted various attempts at identification. One suggestion is the narwhal, an aquatic creature possessing a distinctive elongated tusk and reportedly mottled skin; it is, however, not a kosher animal, and its protrusion is more accurately described as a tooth than a horn. The other prominent suggestion, advanced by the scholar Feliks in his systematic study of the flora and fauna of Torah literature, is the giraffe: a kosher animal with distinctive multicolored spotted markings, and with a small raised bump in the center of its head. That bump is admittedly far from what one would ordinarily envision as a *keren*, but it at least provides a plausible zoological correspondence. The *nignaz* tradition presents an obstacle to both identifications, since neither species has literally vanished, though perhaps *nignaz* refers to its absence from the Sinai region specifically.

### Kosher Status Debate

The Gemara then attempts to determine the *tachash*'s kosher status from the fact that it possessed a single horn. *Shema mina tahor hayah* — perhaps this proves it was kosher. The basis for this inference is a teaching of Rabbi Yehudah: *shor she-hikiv Adam ha-Rishon, keren achat hayta lo be-mitzcho* — the ox that Adam the first human offered as a sacrifice to God had a single horn on its forehead. This is derived from the verse: *va-titav la-Shem mi-shor par makrin mafris* — which would please God more than the ox, the bull with a horn, with split hooves. The word *mafris* — split hooves — implies a kosher species, and the word *makrin* appears to indicate a single horn.

The Gemara objects: *makrin tarti mashma* — the word *makrin* sounds like it implies two horns, not one. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds: *mikeren ksiv* — the word is actually written without the *yud*, spelling *mikeren* in the singular, indicating a single horn. So if the animal that Adam offered had a single horn and was kosher, perhaps the *tachash* — also single-horned — was likewise kosher.

The Gemara then asks whether this also resolves the *behema*/*chaya* question, since the animal was compared to an ox, which is a *behema*. But the Gemara declines this inference, noting that there is also the *keresh* — a single-horned deer, which is a *chaya*. See Rashi there. Accordingly, the existence of a single-horned *chaya* means the *tachash* could belong to either category, and the classification remains undecided. And since other single-horned animals exist without being kosher, even the kosher status remains ultimately unresolved. The Gemara thus leaves the *tachash* in the same indeterminate position Rabbi Meir described: a creature unto itself, whose precise identity the Sages could not determine.

## Twisted Cloth Wick

### Mishna Debate

The Gemara now transitions to the next *mishna*. The case is *petilat ha-beged she-kipla ve-lo hivveva* — a swatch of cloth that has been twisted into the shape of a wick but has not yet been singed. The *mishna* records a dispute: Rabbi Eliezer rules that such cloth is *tamea* and one may not light with it on Shabbat. Rabbi Akiva rules that it is *tehora* and one may light with it. Two apparently distinct rulings — one about *tumah* and one about lighting — are presented together as a single *machloket*, and the Gemara will explore whether and how they are connected.

### R. Eliezer View

The *tumah* dimension of the debate is the more immediately comprehensible half. *Bishlama le-inyan tumah*, the Gemara begins — the debate about *tumah* is readily understood. A swath of cloth three fingers by three fingers is *tamei* because it functions as a piece of clothing, suitable for use as a patch and therefore treated as a garment. But a wick is not a garment and does not receive *tumah*. The question is therefore whether the act of twisting the cloth into the shape of a wick changes its identity sufficiently to remove it from the category of *beged*.

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *kipela lo mo'il* — the twisting accomplishes nothing. Since twisting is easily undone, the cloth retains its identity as a garment. *U-ve-milsa kammaita kaiya* — it remains as it was before, susceptible to *tumah*.

### R. Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *kipela mo'il u-vatulei batil* — the act of twisting is effective, and it nullifies the cloth's status as a garment. Even without singeing, once it has been twisted into the form of a wick, it is no longer a *beged* and can no longer become *tamei*.

### Yom Tov Connection

The question is how the *tumah* debate connects to the question about using it as a wick for Shabbat candles. The Gemara introduces a solution attributed to Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Yehoshua, and likewise to Rav Adda bar Ahava: the *mishna* is dealing with a cloth that is exactly three fingers by three fingers (*gimel al gimel metzumtzamot*), and the specific scenario is that of *Yom Tov* that falls on *erev Shabbat*.

Furthermore, the Gemara establishes that in this scenario, both disputants agree with Rabbi Yehudah's position in the debate about *muktzah* on *Yom Tov*. Rabbi Yehudah holds *masikin be-keilim ve-ein masikin be-shivrei keilim*: one may use intact vessels as firewood on *Yom Tov*, but one may not use fragments of a vessel that broke on *Yom Tov*. The broken shards are considered *nolad* — newly created, having changed their form — and are therefore *muktzah*. Even though the vessel was not *muktzah* when *Yom Tov* began, once it broke, the shards are a new reality that may not be handled.

Additionally, both disputants agree with Ulla's ruling that *ha-madlik tzarich she-yadlik be-rov ha-yotzei* — one who lights must ignite most of the exposed portion of the wick, which means one must actively handle the wick for at least a few moments while lighting it.

### Broken Vessel Issue

With these premises in place, the Gemara connects the two debates. According to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that twisting does not change the cloth's identity, the twisted swatch is still a *beged* when *Yom Tov* begins — it still is, and remains, a *kli* of sorts, in the category of cloth. When one then begins to light it, the burning reduces it below three by three fingers. At that moment, it ceases to be a piece of clothing — but it did not begin *Yom Tov* as a wick; it began as a cloth. The transition from cloth to burnt remnant constitutes a *shever kli* — a broken vessel — in the sense that Rabbi Yehudah's principle applies. *Havei le-shever kli* — it now has the status of broken vessel fragments. And since one is required to keep handling it while most of it ignites, one is *u-ve-ein kamadlik be-shever kli kamadlik* — one is lighting with what amounts to a broken vessel, handling *muktzah* on *Yom Tov*.

### Wick from Start

Rabbi Akiva's position resolves this problem precisely because he holds that twisting is effective. Once one twisted the cloth before *Yom Tov*, it ceased to be a garment and became a wick. *Ve-ein torat kli alav* — it does not enter *Yom Tov* with the status of a vessel at all. When one then lights it on *Yom Tov*, one is not transforming a vessel into shards; the wick already began *Yom Tov* as something functionally equivalent to a piece of wood designated for fuel. *Ve-chi kamadlik be-etz be-alma kamadlik* — one is merely lighting a stick. As long as such a stick was designated before *Yom Tov* for use as fuel, it is not *muktzah*, and there is no problem handling it on *Yom Tov*. Thus the *tumah* debate and the Shabbat debate are joined: the very question of whether twisting changes the object's identity determines both whether it can become *tamei* and whether using it on *Yom Tov erev Shabbat* creates a *muktzah* problem.

## Three-by-Three

### Exact Measurement

Rav Yosef notes that this framework now illuminates a *baraita* that had puzzled him: the teaching of *gimel al gimel metzumtzamot* — exactly three by three fingers. He had known this teaching but had not understood its purpose, since Rabbi Eliezer would say that even a larger piece of cloth is *tamei*. Now that Rav Adda bar Ahava has explained the entire dispute in terms of Rabbi Yehudah's position about *shivrei keilim*, the precision makes sense. If the swatch were larger than three by three, it would not become smaller than three by three as soon as it began to burn, and the *shever kli* problem would not arise immediately. It is only because it is *exactly* three by three that the very first moment of burning reduces it below that threshold, instantly creating a *muktzah* situation. The specification is therefore essential to the construction of the argument.

The Gemara then adds another observation: *u-mi-de-ka-ba'ei tartei Rav Adda bar Ahava aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda, shema mina ke-Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — since Rav Adda bar Ahava constructs his explanation around Rabbi Yehudah's position on *nolad* and *shivrei keilim*, one might infer that he personally holds like Rabbi Yehudah. The Gemara challenges this: does Rav Adda bar Ahava really hold like Rabbi Yehudah? There is an independent statement of his — that if a non-Jew hollows out a stump from a log of wood on *Yom Tov*, a Jew may use it for fuel — which seems to rule *against* Rabbi Yehudah, since the object changed its form entirely on *Yom Tov* and would be *nolad* according to Rabbi Yehudah's view. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *li-divrei Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbi Akiva ka-amar, ve-lei lo sevira lei* — Rav Adda bar Ahava was explaining the debate of the *mishna* according to Rabbi Yehudah's position as held by Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva (within the framework of the *mishna*), but he himself personally does not hold like Rabbi Yehudah. He holds like Rabbi Shimon, who maintains that *nolad* does not create a *muktzah* problem on *Yom Tov*. His explanation of the *mishna* was descriptive, not autobiographical.

The Gemara then proceeds to lay out the debate of Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon in some detail. A *baraita* (which the Gemara notes is actually only inferred from Rav's conduct rather than directly stated) establishes the positions: *masikin be-keilim ve-ein masikin be-shivrei keilim divrei Rabbi Yehudah; ve-Rabbi Shimon matir* — one may use vessels but not broken vessels as fuel on *Yom Tov*, according to Rabbi Yehudah; Rabbi Shimon permits. *Masikin be-tamarin, achalun ein masikin be-gar'inieihem, divrei Rabbi Yehudah; ve-Rabbi Shimon matir* — one may use dates as fuel, but once eaten, one may not use the pits, says Rabbi Yehudah; Rabbi Shimon permits. *Masikin be-egozin, achalun ein masikin be-kelipatan, divrei Rabbi Yehudah; ve-Rabbi Shimon matir* — one may use nuts as fuel, but once eaten, one may not use the shells, says Rabbi Yehudah; Rabbi Shimon permits.

The Gemara establishes that all three cases are necessary. Had only the broken vessel been stated, one might have thought Rabbi Yehudah's concern applied exclusively there, where there is a stark categorical shift from *kli* to *shever kli*. But date pits were always pits — nothing changed their physical form. If we only knew the pits, we might have distinguished them because they were covered before and exposed after. And if only the nut shells were stated, since they were already exposed before eating, one might have thought their form did not change at all. Each case is needed to convey that Rabbi Yehudah's category of *nolad* extends even to items whose appearance did not change, as long as their functional identity shifted — from food-component to firewood — during *Yom Tov* itself.

This entire passage, the Gemara notes, was not directly taught by Rav but was inferred from his conduct: *dechi ata Rav le-Vavel, achal tamrei ve-shada kashaisa le-chiyusa* — when Rav came to Bavel he ate dates and threw the pits to his animals, handling them on *Yom Tov*. The Gemara asks whether this indicates he did not accept Rabbi Yehudah's ruling. It resolves this by suggesting the dates in question were *armyasa* — an unripe variety where some of the date flesh still adhered to the pit (as Rashi explains), or a type whose pit was itself somewhat edible when eaten together with the date (as the *Aruch* explains). In either case, the pit was not a pure *nolad*; it retained some connection to the edible portion. Whether Rav accepted Rabbi Yehudah's position regarding wholly separated pits therefore remains unresolved from this incident.

The Gemara then addresses a practical difficulty raised by Rav Shmuel bar bar Chana to Rav Yosef: according to Rabbi Yehudah, any vessel thrown into a fire on *Yom Tov* becomes a *shever kli* the moment it begins to burn — and one would then be handling *muktzah* when stirring the fire. The Gemara's solution follows the ruling of Rav Masna citing Rav: one simply adds a majority of previously prepared kosher firewood to the fire, so that one is primarily handling permitted wood, with the *muktzah* material incidental. This solution raises the question of whether one is improperly rendering *issur batel le-chatchila*, and the *Rishonim* address this: they suggest that since the item is burning anyway regardless of handling, and since the concern may more properly be classified under *tiltul min ha-tzad* — incidental handling — when the majority consists of permitted material, this leniency holds.

Returning now to the question of practical *halacha* about pre-singeing wicks: *Tosafot* (at the end of the relevant passage) notes that the widespread custom of husbands lighting the *petilot* before Shabbat and then extinguishing them so that they are singed appears to be halachically unnecessary. *Tosafot* states: *ve-nire li de-ve-chinam nahagu anashim le-hadlik ha-petilah u-le-chaboshah ke-dei she-tehei mu'evesh* — that the custom to light and extinguish the wick in order to singe it serves no mandatory purpose, since the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Eliezer — *d'shamusai hu*, he was either in a state of *cherem* or was associated with *Beit Shammai*, and the subsequent *sugyot* demonstrate it is not required. Nonetheless, the *Rishonim* affirm it as a worthy *minhag*, and practically speaking, it also makes the wick easier to light when the time comes.

### Singed Wicks Need

Rava offers an entirely different explanation of the *mishna* that disconnects the two debates entirely. *Lefi she-ein madlikin bi-ftilah she-einah mechorachat* — the reason one may not use an unsinged twisted cloth as a wick is simply that it does not draw the oil effectively and will sputter. This has nothing to do with questions of *tumah* or *shever kli*; it is a straightforward practical concern about the quality of the Shabbat light. *Ve-lo be-samrutin she-einan mechorachin* — and similarly one may not use unsinged rags. According to Rava, Rabbi Eliezer happens to hold two independent positions: first, that twisting does not change the cloth's identity so it remains *mekabel tumah*; and second, that unsinged wicks are unfit for use on Shabbat because they do not hold a flame. Rabbi Akiva disagrees on both counts: twisted cloth loses its identity and is *tehora*, and unsinged wicks are perfectly acceptable. The debates coincide but are not causally related.

Given Rava's explanation, the Gemara returns to ask: *ela ha-dei tanya Rav Yosef, gimel al gimel metzumtzamot, le-mai hilcheta?* — if the debates are not connected through the *Yom Tov* scenario, why did the *baraita* transmitted by Rav Yosef specify exactly three by three? The answer is that the specification bears on a *halacha* of *tumah* from the *mishna* in *Keilim*: the question of whether "three by three" includes the fringe threads or not. Rabbi Shimon holds that the measurement of three by three excludes the fringes, so the cloth itself must be three by three; the Sages hold that "precisely three by three" means including the fringes, so that a cloth with fringes whose central body is less than three by three is *tamei* only if the fringes bring it to exactly three by three. The *baraita*'s emphasis on "exactly" (*metzumtzamot*) thus speaks to this debate in *Keilim* about borderline measurements.

## Cloth & Use

### Keilim Mishna

Rav Hamnuna offers yet another reading of the *mishna*, one that shifts the frame entirely. He reads the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva not as referring to cloth that is exactly three fingers by three fingers, but to cloth that is *less* than three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* — a categorically different threshold. As the *sugya* had established earlier, three by three *tefachim* is the higher standard of *beged* identity: certain materials achieve the status of *beged* only at that size, and the ease with which an object loses its *beged* identity may also differ depending on whether it started from the three-finger or three-*tefach* level.

Rav Hamnuna proposes that the *mishna* is an instance of *kulei matlaniyot* — the lenient provisions that apply to rags, the sorts of menial, undignified uses to which a piece of worn-out cloth might be put. To explain this he cites the *mishna* in *Keilim*: a piece of cloth less than three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* that has been designated (*hitkino*) for one of three undignified uses — *lifkok bo et ha-merchatz* (to serve as a plug for the bathhouse drain), *le-nayer bo et ha-kedeirah* (to serve as a potholder for the pot), or *le-kanayach bo et ha-rechayim* (to serve as a rag for cleaning the mill) — is the subject of a three-way dispute about *tumah*.

### R. Eliezer Impure

Rabbi Eliezer's position in *Keilim* is that the cloth remains *tamei* regardless: *bein min ha-muchan bein she-eino min ha-muchan, tamei*. Whether the cloth was previously designated for use as clothing (*min ha-muchan*) or was already on its way to being discarded (*she-eino min ha-muchan*), the mere reassignment to these menial uses does not strip it of its status as *beged*. It is still more than three by three fingers, it has not been physically altered, and designation alone does not transform it.

### R. Yehoshua Pure

Rabbi Yehoshua takes the opposite position: *bein min ha-muchan bein she-lo min ha-muchan, tahor*. In both cases, once the cloth — which is less than three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* — has been assigned to these degrading uses, it loses its identity as a garment and becomes *tahor*. Rabbi Yehoshua reasons that the reduction in the object's stature, brought about by designation for menial work, suffices to strip it of its *beged* status, at least when the cloth is in this lower size range.

### R. Akiva Split

Rabbi Akiva occupies a middle position: *min ha-muchan, tamei; she-lo min ha-muchan, tahor*. If the cloth had previously been designated and used for clothing (*min ha-muchan*), then even reassigning it to menial purposes leaves it *tamei* — its prior history as a *beged* is not so easily erased. But if it was never properly in the clothing category to begin with (*she-lo min ha-muchan*), then the designation for menial use is sufficient to render it *tahor*.

### Ambiguous States

Ulla (with Rabba bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan) clarifies the outer limits of this debate. At both extremes, there is no dispute. If one literally threw the cloth into the garbage (*zarko be-ashpah*), everyone agrees it is *tahor* — discarding it entirely removes it from the *beged* category. If one stored it in a box together with one's sewing materials (*henicho be-kupsa*), everyone agrees it is *tamei* — the intent to continue using it as a cloth-item is unambiguous. The dispute applies only to intermediate cases: where one hung the cloth on a frame (*tala'o be-magod*) or left it on the floor behind one's door (*henicho achorei ha-delet*). These are ambiguous situations, and it is here that the three opinions diverge.

Rabbi Eliezer reasons: since you did not throw it in the garbage, *da'atei alaveih* — you still have it in mind; you may yet change your mind and use it for clothing. Therefore it remains *tamei*, even in this ambiguous state. When he calls such a cloth *she-lo min ha-muchan*, he does not mean it is fully outside the clothing domain, only that it is less designated than if it were in your sewing box. But as long as it has not been fully cast out, it retains the capacity for *tumah*.

Rabbi Yehoshua reasons: since you did not put it in your sewing box, *bitulei batleih* — you have effectively renounced it as a garment. Even a cloth that was somewhat designated for clothing, once placed in this ambiguous zone and assigned to menial work, has lost its identity. What Rabbi Yehoshua calls *muchan* (which he says is *tahor*) refers to something that, though not thrown in the garbage, is closer to discarded than preserved — and even that, he says, becomes *tahor* once assigned to *shmata* use.

Rabbi Akiva distinguishes between the two intermediate cases: if the cloth is hung on a frame (*tala'o be-magod*), he sides with Rabbi Eliezer — this is close enough to being in use that it cannot shed its *beged* identity through mere designation. But if it is left behind the door (*henicho achorei ha-delet*), he sides with Rabbi Yehoshua — that level of neglect, combined with the designation for menial work, suffices to make it *tahor*. Yet the Gemara then notes that *ve-hadar bei Rabbi Akiva le-gabei de-Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rabbi Akiva ultimately reversed himself and fully sided with Rabbi Yehoshua, holding that in all the ambiguous cases designation for menial use renders the cloth *tahor*.

### Designation Changes

Rava demonstrates that this reversal is evident from our *mishna* itself. The *mishna* uses the phrase *petilat ha-beged* — a twisted cloth. Rava asks: *mai irya de-tana petilat ha-beged? li-snei petila shel beged* — why phrase it as "a twisted-cloth," rather than "a twist of cloth"? The very formulation *petilat ha-beged* — where "cloth" modifies the wick, not the other way around — implies that this object is *still* understood as a *beged*, that its identity as cloth has not yet been dissolved. And yet, Rabbi Akiva says that the mere act of twisting and designating it as a wick is sufficient to render it *tahor*. This proves that Rabbi Akiva holds, even in situations that would seem to favor the cloth retaining its identity, that designation for the new purpose removes it from the *beged* category. His final position thus aligns with Rabbi Yehoshua's across the board: in all ambiguous intermediate cases, designation for *shmata* use overrides prior *beged* identity, at least when the cloth falls below three *tefachim* by three *tefachim*.

## Dripping Lamps

### Suspended Container

The *mishna* now turns to a new set of concerns about Shabbat lamp arrangements. *Lo yikov adam shfoferet shel beitzah vi-yimalena shemen vi-yitnena al pi ha-ner bishvil she-tehei menatefet* — one may not take an eggshell (or a vessel shaped like one), fill it with oil, and suspend it above the lamp with a small hole at the bottom so that it drips oil down into the lamp and continuously replenishes the fuel. *Va-afilu hi shel cheres* — and even if the container is made of pottery rather than an actual eggshell, this is similarly prohibited.

### Single Vessel

Rabbi Yehudah, however, permits it — under one condition: *aval im chibrah ha-yotzer mi-techilah* — if the potter originally fashioned the drip container as a single unified vessel together with the lamp body. *Mipnei she-hu kli echad* — since it constitutes one vessel, it is permitted. The Gemara will clarify the underlying concern momentarily.

### Separate Bowl

The *mishna* continues with a related prohibition: *lo yimaleh adam kearah shel shemen vi-yitnena be-tzad ha-ner vi-yiten rosh ha-petilah be-tocha bishvil she-tehei shoevet* — one may not fill a bowl with oil, place it beside the lamp, and extend the wick from the lamp into the bowl so that it draws oil from both the lamp and the bowl simultaneously. Here too Rabbi Yehudah permits it. The Gemara explains the reasoning behind the prohibition in both cases: because the supplementary oil source is not the lamp itself, one might forget that the oil in the suspended container or the separate bowl is designated for the lamp, dip bread into it, and thereby remove oil that was committed to the flame — causing, at minimum, *gram kibui*, the indirect extinguishing of the lamp before it would naturally have gone out.

### Both Cases Needed

The Gemara establishes that it was necessary for the *mishna* to state both cases separately. Had only the *shfoferet* (eggshell) been mentioned, one might have thought the Sages' concern applies there specifically because an eggshell or its ceramic equivalent is not *meisa* — not unappealing — and a person might plausibly dip bread into oil held in such a vessel. But a clay pottery vessel is *meisa* — it is unappealing — and one would never dip into it; there, one might have thought Rabbi Yehudah and the Sages agree that it is permitted. Conversely, had only the pottery vessel been stated, one would understand why Rabbi Yehudah permits it in that case but might have thought he would agree with the Sages regarding the eggshell, since there the forgetting is more likely.

Similarly, even with both eggshell cases, one might have thought Rabbi Yehudah's leniency applies only where the container is directly above the lamp (*lo mifsek* — nothing separates it), so that one is unlikely to forget the oil is for the lamp. But one might have thought he agrees with the Sages regarding the separate bowl placed beside the lamp (*mifseka* — separated in horizontal space), where forgetting is more probable. And had only the bowl been stated, one might have thought the Sages' concern applies there but not to the eggshell suspended directly overhead.

*Tzricha* — all the cases are necessary, because Rabbi Yehudah and the Sages disagree across the full range of arrangements: Rabbi Yehudah is consistently lenient, and the Sages are consistently concerned that any supplemental oil source — however positioned — creates a risk of inadvertent *kibui*. The *sugya* will continue from here.
