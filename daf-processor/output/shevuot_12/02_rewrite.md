# Shevuot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lev Beis Din & Tmidin

### Lev Beis Din Masneh

The daf begins at the very bottom of 11b, picking up where the previous session left off with a remarkable teaching of Rabbi Yochanan concerning *tmidin* — the communal daily sacrifices. The problem arises at the turn of the new year: funds collected in the previous year must give way to funds of the new year, leaving any communal sacrifices purchased with last year's money in an uncertain position. The general rule is that one may not redeem a sanctified animal unless it has developed a *mum* — a blemish that renders it unfit for sacrifice. Yet Rabbi Yochanan had asserted that in this case, the communal animals may be redeemed even without a blemish. The reason he offered was *lev beis din masneh aleihem* — an implicit stipulation by the court. Because the *beis din* is fully aware that this situation will arise, their original act of sanctification was never meant to be absolute. It was, in effect, conditional: if the animal turns out to be unnecessary as a *korban*, it was purchased merely as an asset, with only a generic *kedushas stam* rather than as a fully committed sacrificial offering.

The *Gemara*, having accepted this principle, then turned it back upon our *mishna*, where Rabbi Shimon is challenged. Rabbi Shimon had ruled that a goat designated for one *musaf* — whether for a *regel*, *Yom Kippur*, or *Rosh Chodesh* — may be used for a different *musaf* if the original is lost and subsequently recovered. The challengers pressed him: if all these *musafin* perform slightly different variations of *tumas mikdash v'kodashav*, how can one substitute for another? Rabbi Shimon's answer was that they are all variations on a single theme, and since they all address *tumas mikdash v'kodashav*, they are interchangeable. But the *Gemara* now asks: why didn't Rabbi Shimon simply invoke the principle of *lev beis din masneh aleihem*? If an implicit stipulation exists that the *beis din* can redirect the animal, then the problem dissolves without needing to argue they are all functionally equivalent.

### Challenging R. Shimon

The implication of the question is telling: since Rabbi Shimon did not appeal to this mechanism, he apparently does not accept it. The *Gemara* thus surfaces a tension between Rabbi Shimon's position and Rabbi Yochanan's general principle.

### R. Yochanan Concedes

Rabbi Yochanan concedes the point directly. His formulation, transmitted through Rabbi Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Avin, is as follows: *tmidin shelo hutzrechu l'tzibbur* — if *tmidin* were left over from the previous year — *l'didi Rabbi Shimon ein nifdim tmimim*: according to Rabbi Shimon, they cannot be redeemed without a blemish. Rabbi Shimon does not hold of the *chidush* of *lev beis din masneh aleihem*. *L'didi chachamim nifdim tmimim*: Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that his statement about redeeming without a blemish was made according to the *chachamim*, not according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Shimon vs. Yochanan

With that concession in hand, the *Gemara* proceeds to ask a sharper question: who exactly are these rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Shimon and whose position supports Rabbi Yochanan's teaching? Since Rabbi Yochanan has now acknowledged that Rabbi Shimon parts ways with him, the *Gemara* wants to identify the *tanna* whose authority undergirds Rabbi Yochanan's claim that communal animals may be redeemed without a blemish. This sets the stage for an extended examination of the Tannaitic sources.

## Tannaitic Sources

### Rabbanan de'Pligi

The *Gemara* now recapitulates the Tannaitic support previously marshaled for Rabbi Yochanan's position, but with a crucial refinement: it will demonstrate that none of the cited sources unambiguously extends the principle of *lev beis din masneh aleihem* to the specific case of the *tmidin*, where the alternative of simply waiting for a blemish is available.

### Ketores Distinction

The first candidate is the case of the *ketores* — leftover incense from the previous year, which may be redeemed and repurchased with new funds. One might think this parallels the case of the *tmidin*, since it likewise involves redeeming an item in its unblemished state. However, the *Gemara* dismisses this: *shani ketores de'lav bar re'iyah hi* — the *ketores* is categorically different, because there is simply nothing else one can do with it. Unlike an animal, the *ketores* cannot be set out to graze until it develops a blemish. When you have no alternative, the principle of *lev beis din masneh aleihem* must supply the escape route. But an animal, in theory, can wait. So the *ketores* case establishes that the mechanism exists in some form, but does not prove that it extends to a situation where another option is available.

### Parah Adumah Case

The second candidate is the *parah adumah* — the red heifer — where similarly, the ruling permitted redemption and repurchase under certain circumstances. Here too, the *Gemara* notes that most scenarios involving the *parah adumah* are ones where no alternative exists: the animal died, or was otherwise rendered unusable, and the community had no choice. However, one scenario remains troubling — if a superior animal was found, could one redeem the first and purchase the better one? In that case, one could, in principle, simply wait for the first animal to develop a blemish. Yet the *Gemara* answers: *dilma shani Parah de'damei yakrin* — perhaps the *parah adumah* is special because its cost is so significant, and there may be particular reasons to allow greater flexibility there that would not carry over to the *tmidin*. The bottom line is that the *parah adumah* case cannot serve as proof for the broader principle either.

### Challenging R. Shimon

The *Gemara* then turns to the third possible source: the rabbis who challenged Rabbi Shimon in the *mishna* itself. When they pressed him — "What would happen if a goat designated for one *musaf* was lost, and later recovered; could you use it for another?" — the implicit assumption in their challenge might be that they themselves had no problem with using one for another, because they held *lev beis din masneh aleihem*. They were asking Rabbi Shimon: fine for us, but how does it work for you?

This reading, the *Gemara* acknowledges, requires that the challengers be Rabbi Yehuda and not someone else — for it assumes that they, like Rabbi Shimon, hold that each *korban* performs a distinct function, but they resolve the practical difficulty through the mechanism of *lev beis din*. The reconstructed dialogue would run: "It is understandable according to us — *bishlama ledidi de'amina lev beis din matneh aleihem*, I understand why you can use one for another even though they each atone for something slightly different, because *beis din* implicitly stipulates. But according to you, who denies that stipulation and thus cannot change the animal's status without a *mum* — *amai yikrevu*? Why can they be used interchangeably?"

### Rabbi Meir Reading

However, the *Gemara* immediately offers an alternative reading: *dilma Rabbi Meir hi* — perhaps the challenger is Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir holds that all the goats of *Yom Kippur*, the *regalim*, and *Rosh Chodesh* perform the exact same *kapparah*, covering the full range of *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* identically. On this reading, the challenge runs differently: "It is understandable according to me — *bishlama ledidi de'amina kol hanei kapparah achat hi*, all these goats do precisely the same thing, which is why if you lose one you can use another in its place. But according to you, Rabbi Shimon, who holds that they do not perform an identical function — *amai yikrevu*? Why can you use one for the other?" This second reading of the challenge, as the *Gemara* presents it, is actually the more straightforward one: the challenger's own position is that the goats are fully identical in function, not that *beis din* stipulates.

### No Explicit Tanna

The conclusion is sobering. Since the reading of the challenge as reflecting *lev beis din masneh aleihem* is uncertain — the challenger may simply be Rabbi Meir, whose position requires no such mechanism — there is no explicit *tanna* who applies *lev beis din* to the case of an unblemished animal when the option to wait for a *mum* exists. Rabbi Yochanan is thus left without a clean Tannaitic source. The *Gemara* resolves this with the phrase *Rabbi Yochanan gemara gemiri lah* — it is simply a tradition he holds, without deriving it from any explicit *tanna*: *ledivrei Rabbi Shimon ein nifdin, ledivrei chachamim nifdin*.

Tosafot raises an interesting question in this context: what compelled Rabbi Yochanan to concede that Rabbi Shimon disagrees with him at all? Rabbi Shimon's position — that you may use a goat from one *musaf* for another — does not require *lev beis din masneh aleihem*. Rabbi Shimon simply says they are all addressing *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* and are therefore interchangeable by nature. Why should Rabbi Yochanan infer from Rabbi Shimon's silence about *lev beis din* that he rejects the principle entirely? The *Gemara* does not address this directly, but once Rabbi Yochanan made his concession, the broader consequence followed: no *tanna* explicitly endorses the redemption of an unblemished animal in a case where one could simply wait for a *mum*. And yet Rabbi Yochanan maintains this as his understanding of the *chachamim*'s position. Whether this statement is accepted as authoritative *halacha* or represents a position the *Gemara* is straining against is, as always, a question worth pursuing — the *Rambam*'s treatment of the passage would bear directly on that question.

## Leftover Qorbanos

### Mai Avdinan Lehu

The *Gemara* now pivots to a directly practical question, posing it from the vantage point of Rabbi Shimon who denies *lev beis din masneh aleihem*: *mai avdinan lehu* — what do we do with them? The scenario is the leftover *tmidin* from the previous year. Unlike the earlier discussion about a goat that got lost and was recovered in the same year, here the difficulty is structural: there is a clear demarcation that communal funds from one year cannot be used for *korbanos* of the following year. So one cannot simply offer last year's *tamid* as this year's *tamid*, even though it is the identical type of sacrifice. What then is to be done with these animals?

The *Gemara* clarifies that Tosafot regards this entire discussion as a question of *lechatchila* only — *b'dieved*, if the sacrifice was in fact offered, it would be valid. But the question remains: short of waiting for a *mum* to develop, what legitimate use can be made of these leftover communal animals?

### Kayitz Hamizbeach

Rabbi Yitzchak, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, provides the answer: *makitzin bahen es hamizbeach* — one uses them for *kayitz hamizbeach*, keeping the altar active. The term *kayitz* — related to the word for summer — evokes the image of a lull in Temple activity, a period when traffic has slowed and the altar risks falling idle. In such moments, a communal *olah* is brought not to fulfill any specific obligation but simply to maintain the rhythm of the Temple's *avodah*, to signal that business continues as usual. This is *kayitz hamizbeach*: a freewill communal offering whose purpose is to keep the altar in constant use.

### Leftover Funds Usage

To understand why this works, one must appreciate a special *halacha* governing the disposition of leftover communal funds. If, at year's end, money remains in the communal treasury that was collected for purchasing *korbanos*, it cannot simply be redirected to the new year's obligatory sacrifices. Nor is it returned. Instead, there is a specific mechanism: leftover funds from the previous year may be used to purchase *korbanos* for *kayitz hamizbeach*, even for the following year. This is not circular — it reflects a halakhic determination that *kayitz hamizbeach* offerings occupy a distinct status, more flexible than the obligatory communal sacrifices and thus eligible to be funded from residual prior-year assets.

The practical upshot is as follows. If one has leftover *money* from last year, it may go toward *kayitz hamizbeach* this year. If one has leftover *sheep* from last year — animals originally designated as *tmidin* — the same applies: since they are *olah olah* (the animal was set aside as an *olah*, and it will be brought as an *olah*), the identity has not fundamentally changed, and the flexibility afforded to *kayitz hamizbeach* accommodates this carry-over.

Rashi explains explicitly: when the altar is idle, one brings these animals as *olos lenidvas tzibur* — freewill communal burnt offerings — as established in the earlier *sugya* regarding the *se'ir* that was not brought for one occasion but could be brought for another. The *Gemara* notes that one of the specific uses of leftover funds — alongside others such as Temple repair — is this *kayitz hamizbeach* fund. A *beraisa* confirms: *mosros* — leftover communal funds — go toward *kayitz hamizbeach*, in the form of offerings comparable to what Rashi describes as a kind of delicacy brought to the altar, keeping it continually engaged.

One important limitation: the *halacha* of *ein olas ha'of b'tzibbur* — there is no communal bird *olah* — means that the *kayitz hamizbeach* sacrifice must be a full animal offering, not a bird. Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda, in the name of Rav Shimi of Neharde'a (citing a tradition passed through Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak), explains that *mosros l'nidvas tzibbur azli* — leftover funds must go toward communal freewill offerings — *ve'ein olas ha'of b'tzibbur*: a bird *olah* can only be brought by an individual, not as a communal sacrifice. The derivation from the *pasuk* emphasizing *korbano* — his personal offering — establishes that *olas ha'of* is restricted to private individuals. Rashi cites this scriptural basis. Tosafot, however, questions how absolute this rule is: if it were entirely black-and-white, Rava's apparent mockery of the ruling would be difficult to understand. Tosafot entertains the possibility that *b'dieved*, a communal bird *olah* might be valid, and that the restriction is primarily *lechatchila*.

## Chatas Status Change

### Identity Change Issue

The discussion thus far has addressed leftover *tmidin* and generic communal funds. A fundamentally harder case now arises: what of a leftover *chatas* from the previous year? Here the problem is compounded, because a *chatas* is not a *se'ir* of a *musaf* that can flow into another *musaf*; nor is it an *olah* that can be redirected as *kayitz hamizbeach*. It is a sin-offering with a fixed identity. Once the new year has begun, a *chatas* purchased with last year's funds cannot be offered as this year's *chatas musaf*. And unlike the *tmidin*, one cannot simply use it for *kayitz hamizbeach*, because *kayitz hamizbeach* calls for an *olah*, and this animal is a *chatas*.

The *Gemara* makes this explicit: *modeh Rabbi Shimon b'siyumei chatas she'ein miketzin b'gufan ela bidmeihen* — even Rabbi Shimon concedes that with leftover *chatos*, you cannot use the animal itself for *kayitz hamizbeach*; you can only use its money value. The process is: let it graze until it develops a *mum*, redeem it, and then apply the redemption money toward an *olah* for *kayitz hamizbeach*. It cannot leapfrog directly to *kayitz hamizbeach* in its current form.

But this raises a natural question: why not? If the animal's inevitable trajectory is toward an *olah* — once it develops a *mum*, is redeemed, and the money is used to purchase an *olah* — why not short-circuit the process and simply offer it directly as an *olah* now? The *Gemara* explains with the principle noted at the top of 11b, in the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon: *yiru ad sheyistavu, v'yimakru, v'yipelu dmeihem l'nedavah, she'ein chatas tzibur meisah* — a communal *chatas* that cannot be brought does not simply die as a personal *chatas* would; instead, it is left to graze until it acquires a *mum*, is redeemed, and the money is donated for a communal freewill *olah*. The funds are absorbed into the communal *nedavah* fund, the *kayitz hamizbeach* reserve. This is a settled *halacha*: by the *chatas tzibur*, you wait for the *mum*, redeem it, and the money goes to *nedavah*.

### Gezeirah Risk

The *Gemara* then articulates the reason one cannot bypass this process and simply bring the animal as an *olah* in its current form. The answer given is: *gezeirah le'achar kapparah atu lifnei kapparah*. It is a rabbinic safeguard: once the *chatas* has been superseded — once another sacrifice has been brought in its stead or the time for its use has passed — its practical identity has shifted toward that of an *olah*, and in principle one might think the animal itself could simply be offered as such. But if we were to permit that, we might come to do the same thing even when the animal could still serve as a *chatas* — when the conditions rendering it obsolete have not yet materialized. To prevent this confusion, the rabbis require that the identity change be effected through a concrete act: the development of a *mum*, followed by redemption.

This is a striking *Gemara*. One would have expected the ruling to rest on a more principled foundation: namely, that a *chatas* does not change its identity automatically, and that some concrete act is required to transform it. Instead, the *Gemara* frames the restriction as a *gezeirah* — a preventive measure against confusion. The implication seems to be that in truth, once the *chatas* can no longer serve its original function, it has already, in some sense, taken on the character of an *olah*, and one might legitimately offer it as such. The *gezeirah* is what stands in the way.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot, however, is uncomfortable with this framing. Other *gemaros* strongly imply that the identity of a *chatas* does not shift automatically — that something concrete must occur to effect the change. One important formulation from elsewhere in the *Gemara* is the concept of *nitak lire'iyah*: once the animal has been actively designated for pasture — placed in a pen to await a *mum* — that concrete act changes its identity. It is no longer a *chatas* in any operative sense; it is now on its way to being an *olah*. But this happens because of the concrete act of designation, not automatically by virtue of the passage of time or the bringing of a substitute. Tosafot thus reads the *gezeirah* explanation of our *Gemara* as somewhat anomalous, and understands the underlying rule to be that without such a concrete act, the animal retains its *chatas* identity regardless.

### Tannaitic Parallels

The *Gemara* brings three Tannaitic parallels, each introduced with the phrase *af anan nami tanina* — "we too have taught this" — all converging on the same point that the animal itself cannot be brought as an *olah* directly; only its redemption money may be so used.

The first, brought by Abaye, involves the *par* and *se'ir* of *Yom Kippur* that were lost and replaced, as well as the *se'ir* of *avoda zarah* in a similar situation. Rabbi Yehuda rules that these die — treating them as a *chatas shenitkapru ba'aleiha*, a *chatas* whose owners have achieved atonement through another means, which, like a personal *chatas*, must be left to die. Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon disagree: *yire'u ad sheyista'avu v'yimakru v'yipelu dmeihem linedavah, she'ein chatas tzibur meisah* — a communal *chatas* does not die; it grazes until it develops a *mum*, is redeemed, and the money goes to a communal *olah*. Why not simply bring the animal itself as an *olah*, given that its money is destined for that purpose anyway? *Gezeirah le'achar kapparah atu lifnei kapparah* — we worry the practice will be confused with cases where the *chatas* could still function as such.

The second parallel, adduced by Rav Ashi, involves the *hasheni* — the extra goat that remains when one of the *Yom Kippur* pair is lost or rendered unfit and a replacement pair is procured. The extra surviving goat must graze until it develops a *mum*; it is then redeemed and its money goes to a *nedavah*. Again, why not bring it as an *olah* directly, since its *Yom Kippur* function has been fulfilled by another? The same answer: *gezeirah le'achar kapparah atu lifnei kapparah*.

The third, adduced by Ravina, addresses the case of an *asham* — a guilt-offering — whose owners either died or achieved atonement through another *korban*. Unlike a personal *chatas*, which in Rabbi Eliezer's view must die, the *asham* follows a parallel track to the communal *chatas*: *yireh ad sheyista'ev v'yimakru v'yipelu damav linedavah* — graze until blemished, redeem, and the money goes to a *nedavah*. Rabbi Eliezer says it dies, like a *chatas*; Rabbi Yehoshua says its money may be used to bring a personal *olah* — *yavi bedamav olah*. The distinction between the view that the money goes to a communal *nedavah* fund versus that it funds a personal *olah* parallels the distinction between the communal and individual contexts. In all these cases, however, the animal itself may not be brought as an *olah*: *amru lo gezeirah le'achar kapparah atu lifnei kapparah*.

The *Gemara* thus establishes a consistent framework. Leftover money from last year: may be used for *kayitz hamizbeach*. Leftover *tmidin* from last year: may be used for *kayitz hamizbeach*. Leftover *chatas* or *asham* from last year: must wait for a *mum*, be redeemed, and the money then goes to *kayitz hamizbeach*. The body of the animal itself cannot serve directly as an *olah*. And the *Gemara*'s stated reason — that it is a *gezeirah* against confusion — leaves open the more principled question of whether an automatic identity change ever truly occurs, a question Tosafot pursues at length by reference to the language of *nitak lire'iyah* in parallel *gemaros*.

## Sakin Moshechtan

### Communal Bird Restriction

The *Gemara* at this stage has established that *kayitz hamizbeach* offerings must take the form of communal animal *olos*, not bird offerings. The restriction *ein olas ha'of b'tzibbur* — derived from the *pasuk*'s emphasis on individual ownership — is clear in the *lechatchila* sense, though Tosafot raises the question of whether it extends to *b'dieved*. The *Gemara*'s citation of Rav Shimi of Neharde'a establishes the Amoraic chain of transmission for this ruling and explains why Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak felt entitled to defend it against Rava's apparent skepticism.

### Sakin Moshechtan

Having established this, the *Gemara* now introduces a statement of Shmuel that supports Rabbi Yochanan's broader framework. Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: *korbanot tzibur — sakin moshechtan lama she-hen* — communal sacrifices, the knife drags them to what they are. This is a compact and somewhat cryptic principle that requires unpacking, and Rashi and Tosafot offer strikingly different explanations.

### Rashi on Sakin

According to Rashi, the principle means the following. Even though a communal animal was originally designated for a specific *korban*, if circumstances have changed — most relevantly for our *sugya*, if it is now the new year and the animal can no longer serve as last year's *tamid* — when the *shechita* is performed with a generic, unstated intent, the knife "drags" the animal to whichever legitimate identity it can assume. Rashi reads this as supporting the *halacha* that last year's sheep designated as a *tamid* can be used this year for *kayitz hamizbeach*: even though it was originally sanctified for a *tamid*, when the *kohen* takes the knife to it now for *kayitz hamizbeach*, that act of *shechita* defines what it is. The *beraisa* that immediately follows supports this reading: *modeh Rabbi Shimon b'sa'ir shelo karav b'regel yikarev b'Rosh Chodesh* — Rabbi Shimon himself concedes that if a goat was not brought on the *regel*, it may be brought on *Rosh Chodesh*; and if not on *Rosh Chodesh*, on *Yom Kippur*; and if not on *Yom Kippur*, on another *regel*. The reason given is *she'mitechilah lo ba ela l'chaper al mizbeach hachitzon* — all these *musafin* were always coming to atone via the outer altar, and all addressed *tumas mikdash*, so one may be substituted for another. Rashi reads the principle of *sakin moshechtan* as extending this flexibility: the knife, at the moment of *shechita*, seals the identity of the offering among the range of legitimate possibilities.

Significantly, the *beraisa* also implies that Rabbi Shimon's flexibility has a temporal limit. When the list of occasions runs out — after the final *regel* before the new year arrives — he stops. He does not say that the goat may be carried over into the following year. This supports the general understanding that once the new year's *korbanos* are funded by new money, last year's animals cannot simply be inserted into this year's *musaf* cycle, even according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Tosafot on Sakin

Tosafot, citing the *Aruch*, reads the principle of *sakin moshechtan* quite differently, and in a way that connects more directly to *lev beis din masneh aleihem*. Before *shechita* is performed, the animal's specific sacrificial identity has not been fully determined. It has been sanctified generically — set aside as a communal *korban* — but which *korban* precisely it is remains somewhat fluid, held open by the implicit condition of the *beis din*. Then, when the *kohen* takes the knife to it and performs *shechita* with a particular intent, the act of *shechita* draws the animal toward and solidifies that specific identity. The knife does not change the identity from something fixed to something new; rather, it resolves a genuine ambiguity that was always present.

On this reading, Tosafot says explicitly: *terem moshcham la'sakin, ein lomar eizeh tamid eizeh musaf, ela tluyim v'omdim b'tnai lev beis din* — before the knife draws them, one cannot say definitively whether this sheep is the *tamid* or the *musaf*; they are suspended in a state of conditionality, depending on the stipulation of the *beis din*. Then the knife comes and settles the matter.

This understanding illuminates the relationship between *sakin moshechtan* and *lev beis din masneh aleihem*. In the more radical formulation of *lev beis din*, a *korban* sanctified for one purpose is redirected entirely — for instance, from a *korban* to *bedek habayit*. Here, the *lev beis din* is operating within a narrower range: the animal was sanctified generically for some communal *korban* to be determined, and the *shechita* specifies which one. This is a less radical application of the same underlying idea: the *beis din*'s implicit intention is that the animal will serve whatever function the *shechita* designates within the legitimate range. Tosafot sees this as consistent with, and indeed illuminating of, Rabbi Shimon's own position — even Rabbi Shimon, who rejects the more radical *lev beis din*, may accept this more modest form of pre-*shechita* fluidity, which is why he permits using a goat from one *musaf* for another.

## Yom Kippur Atonement

### Shear Aveiros

With the Temple-law discussions concluded, the *Gemara* now turns to a new section of the *mishna* that addresses the atonement scope of the *sa'ir hamishtaleiach* — the goat sent off the cliff on *Yom Kippur*. Having labored through the complex mechanics of communal sacrifice identity and redemption over the course of the preceding pages, we arrive at what most people consider the primary concern: *al shear aveiros shebatorah* — upon all the remaining transgressions of the Torah, the scapegoat atones. The *mishna* enumerates: light transgressions and heavy ones, intentional and unintentional, those one has become aware of and those one has not, positive commandments, prohibitions, those carrying *kares*, and those carrying *mita beis din*.

### Redundancy Analysis

The *Gemara* immediately notes apparent redundancies in this list. *Hainu kalos hainu aseh* — "light" (*kalos*) seems to be the same as *aseh* and simple *lo ta'aseh*, and "heavy" (*chamuros*) seems to be the same as *krisot* and *misos beis din*. If you have already listed *aseh* and *lo ta'aseh* and *kares* and *misa beis din*, what additional information do "light" and "heavy" contribute? The *Gemara* resolves this: the pairing is meant to explain each other. "Light" means *aseh* and *lo ta'aseh*; "heavy" means *kares* and *misa beis din*. They are simply more precise elaborations, not independent categories.

### Hoda vs. Lo Hoda

A second apparent redundancy concerns *hoda* and *lo hoda* — "became aware" and "did not become aware." *Hoda hainu meizid* — if one became aware, that would seem to mean one was willful; *lo hoda hainu shogeg* — if one did not become aware, that would seem to mean one sinned unintentionally. But the *mishna* has already mentioned *shogeg* and *meizid*. What do *hoda* and *lo hoda* add?

Rav Yehuda explains: *hoda* and *lo hoda* do not refer to one's state of knowledge at the time of sinning but to one's subsequent awareness. One who sinned *bishogeg* — unintentionally — may or may not later discover that he has sinned. If he does discover it definitively, he incurs a personal obligation to bring a *chatas*, and *Yom Kippur* does not exempt him from that obligation; the *chatas* must still be brought. But *hoda* and *lo hoda* in the *mishna* refer to a different situation: one who becomes aware that he *might* have sinned, triggering the obligation of an *asham talui*. The terms are thus not synonymous with *shogeg* and *meizid* but refer specifically to the degree of awareness regarding a potential sin. The *Gemara* thus explains that *bein noda lo sfekan bein lo noda lo sfekan*: whether or not one has become aware that one might have transgressed — i.e., whether or not the *asham talui* obligation has been triggered — the scapegoat of *Yom Kippur* covers this.

### Asham Talui Exception

This distinction sharpens the exceptional status of the *asham talui* in relation to *Yom Kippur*. If, on the eve of *Yom Kippur*, one is aware that one may have eaten *chelev* — perhaps two pieces of meat were in front of him, one of which was *chelev*, and he is now uncertain which he ate — he would normally be obligated in an *asham talui*, the guilt-offering for a doubtful transgression. Once *Yom Kippur* passes, however, that *asham talui* obligation lapses. If it later emerges that he definitely sinned, he then incurs the obligation of a *chatas*; but the *asham talui* is not required after *Yom Kippur* has passed. The scapegoat thus covers both the case where one is unaware of a potential sin and the case where one is aware of the uncertainty — in either case, the *asham talui* is discharged.

What *Yom Kippur* does not cover is a definite *chatas* obligation already established by certain knowledge of having sinned. That must be addressed directly, through the appropriate *korban*.

### Kalos & Chamuros

The *mishna*'s full list, as parsed by the *Gemara*, thus divides into the following framework. *Kalos* — light transgressions — encompass positive commandments (*aseh*) and prohibitions (*lo ta'aseh*). *Chamuros* — heavy transgressions — encompass those punishable by *kares* or *misa beis din*. Across all of these categories, whether committed *bishogeg* or *b'meizid*, and whether or not the person has become aware of a possible violation (in the sense relevant to *asham talui*), the *sa'ir hamishtaleiach* of *Yom Kippur* provides atonement. The remaining discussion of exactly which forms of *tumas mikdash v'kodashav* are addressed by the inner *sa'ir* — including the case where one was not aware at the outset but was aware at the end, based on the *pasuk*'s reference to *pish'eihem* alongside *chata'osam* — will continue on the following *daf*.
