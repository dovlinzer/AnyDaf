# Bava Kamma 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tripping & Negligence

### Two Potters Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי קַדָּרִין שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין זֶה אַחַר זֶה, וְנִתְקַל הָרִאשׁוֹן וְנָפַל, וְנִתְקַל הַשֵּׁנִי בָּרִאשׁוֹן – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **two potters** carrying pots **who were walking one after the other** in the public domain, **and the first stumbled** on a bump **and fell, and the second stumbled over the first** and fell too, **the first** is **liable** to pay **for the damage incurred by the second.**

The thirty-first daf of Bava Kamma opens with a *mishnah* that introduces what might be called the Talmud's version of a multi-car collision: a chain of pedestrians, each tripping over the one before, raising intertwined questions about negligence, causation, and the categories of tortfeasor. The *mishnah* reads: two potters were walking one behind the other (*shenei kadarin she-hayu mehalchim zeh achar zeh*). The first one tripped and fell (*nitkal harishon ve-nafal*), and the second one tripped over the first (*nitkal hasheni barishon*). The *mishnah* rules: the first is liable for the damages to the second (*harishon chayav benezkei hasheni*).

This is the first time the *mishnah* uses the word *nitkal* — "tripped" — explicitly. The preceding *mishnayot* discussed pitchers breaking and water being spilled, but never named the act of tripping directly. The significance is considerable, because the question of whether *nitkal poshea hu* — whether one who trips is by definition negligent — has been the subject of an ongoing dispute framed around Rabbi Meir and the Rabbanan. Here the *mishnah* takes that discussion out of the theoretical and places it in a concrete scenario.

Notably, the earlier *mishnayot* were somewhat frustrating on this score: they consistently assumed negligence without spelling out what degree was required. Here the *mishnah* at last names the act and thereby invites the *gemara* to clarify when and why tripping generates liability.

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא תֵּימָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: נִתְקָל פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא – וְחַיָּיב; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן, דְאָמְרִי אָנוּס הוּא, וּפָטוּר – הָכָא חַיָּיב; שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד, וְלֹא עָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Do not say that the mishna is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that **one who stumbles is** considered **negligent and** therefore **liable. Rather, even** according **to the Rabbis, who say** that one who stumbles **is** generally **a victim of circumstances beyond his control and is** consequently **exempt, here,** in the case in the mishna, he is **liable, since** after falling **he had** the opportunity **to stand** up, **and he did not stand** up.

Rabbi Yochanan opens the *gemara's* discussion with a striking methodological point: do not say (*lo teima*) that our *mishnah* follows only Rabbi Meir, who holds that *nitkal poshea hu* — the one who trips is negligent. Even the *rabbanan*, who say that tripping over one's own feet constitutes an *ones* — a force-majeure event — and who would therefore ordinarily exempt such a person from liability for a broken vessel, agree that in this case the first person is *chayav*. Why? *She-hayah lo la'amod ve-lo amad* — because he had the opportunity to stand up and did not do so.

This reasoning is precisely parallel to the principle established earlier regarding broken vessels: if a pot shatters through no fault of the carrier, the carrier is nonetheless liable if he subsequently fails to remove the hazard. The tripping itself may be *ones*, but the failure to rise — once one has had the opportunity — is *peshiah*. The person who fell is not, after all, capable of being *mafkir* his own body the way he might abandon a broken vessel. He remains responsible for himself at all times, and the moment he could have stood and did not, liability attaches.

### Adam HaMazik/Bor

This raises what is, in fact, the central structural question running through the entire discussion: when the second person trips over the stationary first person — or breaks something upon colliding with him — is the first person legally an *adam hamazik* or a *bor*?

The practical stakes are significant. An *adam hamazik* — an active human tortfeasor — is *muad l'olam*, liable without regard to the victim's type of property. A *bor* — a pit, a passive hazard — is exempt (*patur bekelim*) for damage to vessels. So which category applies to a person lying inert in the road?

Three distinct analytical frameworks present themselves. The first holds that a passive, stationary person is simply a *bor* by definition: physically, the person on the ground is not acting — the victim is moving into him, not the reverse — and therefore negligence is required for liability, just as it is required to establish liability for a *bor*. The second holds that the person is always an *adam hamazik*, since his body came into contact with the victim, and the case therefore demonstrates that even *adam hamazik* requires some threshold of negligence. The third — and most nuanced — holds that the very classification of stationary/passive person as *bor* or *adam hamazik* is itself a function of agency: if the person on the ground had no opportunity to stand and made no choice to remain there, he is passive in the fullest sense and is therefore a *bor*. But once he *could* have stood and chose not to, his remaining on the ground becomes, in a meaningful sense, a decision — and that insertion of agency transforms his presence from a *bor* into an act, making him an *adam hamazik*.

On this third reading, negligence does not merely *permit* liability; it *determines category*. The same stationary body is a *bor* at one moment and an *adam hamazik* at another, depending entirely on whether the person had a meaningful opportunity to remove himself.

### Stand vs. Warn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד; הָיָה לוֹ לְהַזְהִיר, וְלֹא הִזְהִיר.
>
> וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלֹא הָיָה לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד – לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְהַזְהִיר; דִּטְרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Even** if **you say** that **he did not have** the opportunity **to stand** up, **he had** the opportunity **to warn** the person behind him, **and he did not warn** him.
>
> **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said** in response that **since he did not have** the opportunity **to stand** up, **he did not have** the opportunity **to warn** the other person either, **as he was busy** trying to stand up. Therefore, the mishna, which holds him liable, must be referring to a case where he could have stood up.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative ground for liability. Even if the fallen person did not yet have time to stand (*afilu teima lo haya lo la'amod*), there is still *chiyuv* because *haya lo lehazhir ve-lo hizhir* — he should have warned the person behind him. A simple shout — "Watch out, I'm on the ground!" — would have discharged the obligation.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, disputes this. *Keivan de-lo haya lo la'amod* — since he did not yet have the opportunity to stand — *lo haya lo lehazhir*: we cannot say he should have warned either, because *iterid* — he is preoccupied. A person who has just fallen and is in pain or scrambling to collect himself cannot simultaneously be held to a standard of attentiveness to those approaching from behind. To impose such a requirement is to demand that a person in distress maintain perfect situational awareness on behalf of others while managing a crisis of his own.

The debate is a genuinely interesting one. How much does the law accommodate human distraction and self-focus in moments of emergency? Rabbi Yochanan says: quite a lot — if you did not yet have time to stand, you are not yet required to warn. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak disagrees: warning is a lower threshold than standing, and even a fallen person retains the responsibility to call out. The two positions thus represent different calibrations of when the duty to others re-engages after an involuntary disruption.

## Beam Case

### Legitimate Stop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיָה בַּעַל קוֹרָה רִאשׁוֹן וּבַעַל חָבִית אַחֲרוֹן, נִשְׁבְּרָה חָבִית בַּקּוֹרָה – פָּטוּר, וְאִם עָמַד בַּעַל קוֹרָה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to prove that the opinion of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak is correct. **We learned** in the next mishna (31b): If **the owner of** a cross **beam was** walking in the public domain carrying his beam **first, and the owner of a barrel was** walking with his barrel **last,** i.e., behind him, and **the barrel was broken by the** cross **beam,** the one who carried the cross beam is **exempt. But if the owner of the** cross **beam stopped,** he is **liable,** since the accident was caused by his stopping.

The *gemara* brings a proof from the next *mishnah* (which will be treated fully on the following daf), concerning a person carrying a beam (*baal kora*) walking in front of a person carrying a barrel or pitcher (*baal chavit*). If the person behind speeds up and his pitcher strikes the beam of the person ahead, the beam-carrier is exempt — the collision was caused by the barrel-carrier's own forward movement. But if the beam-carrier *stopped* (*ve-im amad*), the beam-carrier is *chayav*: one does not stop in the middle of a public thoroughfare while others are walking behind.

One might instinctively ask: is the person behind not also responsible for maintaining sufficient following distance? The answer, at least partially, is that the question depends on what could reasonably be anticipated. If a person stops suddenly and without cause in a moving stream of traffic, the primary fault lies with him, notwithstanding that the person behind might also bear some responsibility for proximity.

### Load Adjustment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו שֶׁעָמַד לְכַתֵּף – דְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב, דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְהַזְהִיר?
>
> לֹא, כְּשֶׁעָמַד לָפוּשׁ.
>
> אֲבָל עָמַד לְכַתֵּף מַאי, פָּטוּר? אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ לְבַעַל חָבִית ״עֲמוֹד״ – פָּטוּר, לִפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – כְּשֶׁעָמַד לָפוּשׁ, אֲבָל עָמַד לְכַתֵּף – פָּטוּר!
>
> הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּעָמַד לָפוּשׁ, כִּי קָאָמַר לוֹ לְבַעַל חָבִית: ״עֲמוֹד״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What, is it not** referring to a situation **where** the one carrying the cross beam **stopped** in order **to adjust** the load **on his shoulder, which is the normative behavior of** one carrying a beam, and is not considered negligence? **And** nevertheless the *tanna* **teaches** that he is **liable,** as, although **he had** the opportunity **to warn** the person behind him that he was about to stop, he did not warn him. This supports the opinion of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak.
>
> The Gemara responds: **No,** it is referring to a situation **where he stopped to rest,** which could not have been anticipated by the person walking behind him. Consequently, he is liable.
>
> The Gemara asks: **But** according to this interpretation, if **he stopped to adjust** the load **on his shoulder, what** is the *halakha*? Is he **exempt?** If so, **rather than teaching** in **the latter clause** of that mishna: **But if he said to the owner of the barrel: Stop,** he is **exempt, let** the *tanna* **distinguish and teach within** the former case **itself,** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement,** that he is liable, **said?** In a case **when he stopped to rest. But** in a case where **he stopped to adjust** the load **on his shoulder,** he is **exempt.**
>
> The Gemara answers: The mishna is presented in **this** manner because it **teaches us this** novelty, **that even if he stopped to rest,** in a case **when he says to the owner of the barrel: Stop,** he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* asks: when the *mishnah* says the beam-carrier is *chayav* for stopping, does it mean he stopped to adjust the load on his shoulder (*she-amad lekatef*)? Adjusting one's load is a normal and expected thing to do — people who know this are walking behind him, and arguably they should anticipate it. If so, why is he liable? The answer suggested is *de-hava lei lehazhir*: even if stopping to adjust one's load is expected behavior, the one who stops still bears the primary obligation to warn those behind him. The analogy to our fallen person is deliberate: just as one who stops to adjust a load is expected to warn even while managing that adjustment, perhaps one who has fallen should similarly warn even while in the process of rising.

The *gemara* ultimately rejects this reading. *Lo* — the *mishnah* does not mean he stopped to adjust his load. It means he stopped simply to rest (*keshe-amad le-fush*): a complete, unjustified halt in the middle of a road without any legitimate purpose. Stopping to take a break where one has no right to do so generates liability irrespective of warning, because the very act of stopping is itself the violation. One does not take a rest break in the middle of the public road.

The implication is important: if the *mishnah* were about someone who stopped legitimately to adjust his load, the *hiddush* would be that he is exempt even without warning, since it is not reasonable to demand warning under those circumstances. That would be the more novel ruling. The fact that warning generates exemption (*im amar lo le-ba'al chavit amod — patur*) is, on its own, not especially surprising.

### Tosafot Challenge

The *gemara* then asks: if the beam-carrier stopped legitimately to adjust his load — what is the rule then? Is he *patur* without warning, because one cannot be expected to warn while adjusting a load? If so, the *gemara* argues, the *mishnah* should have made *that* distinction explicit: stopped to rest — *chayav*; stopped to adjust — *patur* even without warning. That would be the more striking *hiddush*. The *gemara* answers: *ha kamashma lan* — the teaching is telling us something else entirely. Even when a person stops for no good reason (*amad le-fush*), he can still discharge his liability through warning. One might have thought that so egregious a violation — stopping without cause in a moving roadway — generates absolute liability that warning cannot cure. The *mishnah* tells us otherwise: warning is always sufficient to exempt.

Tosafot raises a pointed question here that deserves attention. If the person walking behind the beam-carrier is expected to anticipate that people regularly stop to adjust their loads, then he should be held responsible to account for such stops regardless of why they occur. Why, then, does it matter whether the beam-carrier stopped for a good reason or not? The answer, while not fully resolving the tension, lies in relative degrees of fault. It is true that the follower bears some responsibility to anticipate normal stops. But when the leader stops for no legitimate reason, his own fault is primary and predominates — he had no right to stop at all. The two parties may share some degree of responsibility, but the allocation differs based on whether the stop was justified.

## Multi-Car Pile-Up

### Baraita Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַקַּדָּרִין וְהַזַּגָּגִין שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין זֶה אַחַר זֶה; נִתְקַל הָרִאשׁוֹן וְנָפַל, וְנִתְקַל הַשֵּׁנִי בָּרִאשׁוֹן, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי בַּשֵּׁנִי – רִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שֵׁנִי, וְשֵׁנִי חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שְׁלִישִׁי. וְאִם מֵחֲמַת רִאשׁוֹן נָפְלוּ – רִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי כּוּלָּם. וְאִם הִזְהִירוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an alternative proof from what is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **potters and glaziers who were walking one after the other,** and **the first stumbled and fell, and the second stumbled over the first,** sustaining damage, **and the third** stumbled **over the second,** also falling and sustaining damage, in this case, **the first** person is **liable** to pay **for the damage of the second, and the second** is **liable** to pay **for the damage of the third. But if they** all **fell because of the first, the first** is **liable** to pay **for the damage of them all. And if they warned each other,** i.e., each one warned the next, **they are** all **exempt.**

A *baraita* concerning potters and glass-blowers (*ha-kadarim ve-ha-zagagim*) now brings the cascade scenario into sharp focus. The potters were walking one behind the other. The first tripped and fell; the second tripped over the first; the third tripped over the second. The *baraita* rules: the first is liable for the damages of the second (*rishon chayav be-nizkei sheni*), and the second is liable for the damages of the third (*sheni chayav be-nizkei shlishi*). And if they all fell because of the first person — *ve-im machmat rishon naflu* — the first is liable for all of them (*chayav be-nizkei kulam*).

The straightforward question is why the second person is liable for the third at all. His fall was an *ones* — he tripped over the person in front of him through no fault of his own. If anything, the responsibility would seem to remain entirely with person number one. The *baraita* does not spell out the reasoning, but the implicit assumption is that person number two had some opportunity — to stand, to warn, or both — and failed to act. The *baraita*'s final clause points in this direction.

### Warning Exemption

The *baraita* concludes: *ve-im hizhiru ze et ze — pturim*. If they warned one another — if person one warned person two, and person two warned person three — all are exempt. This seems to indicate that the basis for liability in the cascade is precisely the failure to warn: had they warned, there would be no liability; since they did not warn, there is. This reading would support Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's position that warning is both the trigger of liability and its cure.

### R. Yochanan Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לָהֶן לַעֲמוֹד?
>
> לֹא, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לַעֲמוֹד.
>
> אֲבָל לֹא הָיָה לָהֶם לַעֲמוֹד מַאי, פָּטוּר? אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אִם הִזְהִירוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה – פָּטוּר; לִפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לַעֲמוֹד, אֲבָל לֹא הָיָה לָהֶן לַעֲמוֹד – פְּטוּרִין!
>
> הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּהָיָה לָהֶן לַעֲמוֹד, כִּי הִזְהִירוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **What, is it not** a case **where they did not have** the opportunity **to stand** up, and they are nevertheless liable to pay for not warning the people behind them, in accordance with the opinion of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak?
>
> The Gemara answers: **No,** it is a case **where they had** the opportunity **to stand** up and they did not do so.
>
> The Gemara asks: **But** according to this interpretation, if **they did not have** the opportunity **to stand** up, **what** would be the *halakha*? Would they be **exempt? If so, rather than teaching** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **If they warned one another, they are exempt, let** the *tanna* **distinguish and teach within** the former case **itself,** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement,** that they are liable, **said?** It is a case **where they had** the opportunity **to stand** up, **but** if **they did not have** the opportunity **to stand** up, **they are exempt.**
>
> The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is presented in **this** manner because it **teaches us this** novelty, **that even if they had** the opportunity **to stand** up, in a case **when they warned each other they are exempt.**

The *gemara* asks directly: *mai lav de-lo haya lahem la'amod* — are we not talking about a case where they did not yet have the opportunity to stand, and the liability is generated because they should have warned? If so, this *baraita* refutes Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that where one had no opportunity to stand, one is also not required to warn.

The *gemara* defends Rabbi Yochanan: *lo* — the *baraita* is in fact speaking of a case where they *did* have the opportunity to stand (*shehaya lahem la'amod*). Their liability comes from failing to stand, not merely from failing to warn.

If so, the *gemara* challenges, why does the *baraita* emphasize warning as the basis for exemption? It should have distinguished within the same case: if you had the opportunity to stand and you stood, you are obviously exempt. If you had the opportunity to stand and you did not — but you warned — are you exempt or not? That is the *hiddush*: *af al gav de-haya lahem la'amod*, even though they could have stood, if they warned they are nonetheless exempt. One might have thought that warning is insufficient when full removal of the hazard was within reach — that if you could have gotten up, nothing less would suffice. The *baraita* teaches otherwise: *ki hizhir ze et ze — pturim*. Warning is adequate even in a case where rising was possible. This resolves the apparent tension while defending Rabbi Yochanan's position that the initial trigger of liability requires the opportunity to stand.

## Body vs. Property

### First vs. Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שֵׁנִי – בֵּין בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ, בֵּין בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ. שֵׁנִי חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שְׁלִישִׁי – בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to this *halakha*, **Rava said: The first** one who stumbled is **liable** to pay **for the damage of the second, both for damage caused** to the second person **by his body and for damage caused** to him **by his property.** By contrast, **the second** is **liable** to pay **for the damage of the third** only **with regard to damage caused by his body** as result of the fall, **and not with regard to damage caused by his property.**

Rava now introduces a crucial distinction. In the three-person cascade, the first person (*rishon*) is liable for damages to the second *bein be-nizkei gufo bein be-nizkei mamono* — whether the damage was to person number two's body or to person number two's property. The second person (*sheni*), however, is liable for damages to the third only *be-nizkei gufo* — for bodily injury — but *lo be-nizkei mamono* — not for damage to the third person's property.

### Parsing Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי נִתְקָל פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא – שֵׁנִי נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב! אִי נִתְקָל לָאו פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא – אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי לִיפְּטַר!
>
> רִאשׁוֹן – וַדַּאי פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא. שְׁנִי – אַגּוּפוֹ מִחַיַּיב, דְּהָיָה לוֹ לַעֲמוֹד וְלֹא עָמַד. אַמָּמוֹנוֹ פָּטוּר, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי בֵּירָא – לָאו אֲנָא כְּרִיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rava's statement: **Whichever way you** look at it, this is difficult to understand. **If** Rava maintains that **one who stumbles is** considered **negligent, the second** person **should also be liable** to pay for all forms of damage caused by his negligence. And **if** Rava maintains that **one who stumbles is not** considered **negligent, even the first should be exempt** from liability for the damage incurred by the second.
>
> The Gemara explains: **The first is certainly** considered **negligent,** and is therefore liable to pay for damage caused by both his body and his property. **The second is deemed liable** to pay **for** damage caused by **his body, as he had** the opportunity **to stand** up **and he did not stand** up. **For** damage caused by **his property** that was lying there and that caused the third person to stumble and fall, he is **exempt, as he can say to him: I did not dig this pit,** i.e., I did not cause this obstacle. Since it was the first person who stumbled and brought about the situation where the items of the second were lying on the ground, the second is not deemed liable.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this distinction with a *mah nafshach* — a dilemma. If a person who trips is negligent (*nitkal poshea hu*), then let person two also be fully liable for person three, including property damage. And if tripping is not negligence (*nitkal lav poshea hu*), let person one be exempt from property damage as well. How can the first person have a different status from the second?

The *gemara* answers: *rishon vadai poshea hu* — the first person is certainly negligent. He tripped over his own feet. *Sheni*, however, tripped over a person who had just fallen in front of him — that is much more an *ones*. As for the second person's liability for the third: *de-hava lei le-meikam ve-lo kam* — he should have stood up and did not. That is why his body creates liability. But for his property — *amono patur* — he is exempt, because *hai bira lav ana keriteha* — he can say, "I did not dig this well." His broken vessel lying in the road was produced through *ones*, and he is exempt from liability for it.

The mechanism here involves the principle of *mafkir nezikav le-achar nefilat ones*: when a hazard is created through *ones*, the person is presumed to have abandoned ownership of it (*mafkir*), and a *bor* one has abandoned is not one's liability. Person two's vessel, broken through *ones*, is treated as if abandoned — it is not his *bor*, and he bears no responsibility for whatever damage it causes to person three.

### Rashi: Agency

This is where Rashi's explanation becomes indispensable. Rashi is explicit: *rishon* had more negligence, and that makes him an *adam hamazik*. *Sheni* had less negligence — he is treated as a *bor*. His body, having remained on the ground when it should have risen, generates liability in the *bor* category: liable for bodily injury to the next person, but not for vessel damage, since *bor* is exempt on *kelim*.

What makes this explanation genuinely fascinating is the underlying logic: it is not simply that more negligence means more liability, or less negligence means less liability. Rather, degree of negligence determines *which legal category* the person inhabits. The first person, who tripped by his own fault and then failed to rise, is considered to have exercised enough agency to be classified as an active tortfeasor — an *adam hamazik*. The second person, whose fall was itself an *ones*, is more passive; even when he fails to stand, his residual negligence is not sufficient to constitute agency in the fullest sense. He is therefore a *bor* — a passive hazard — and the rules of *bor* apply.

Rashi states this in so many words: *d'rishon havai poshea teifi m'sheni* — the first was more negligent than the second — *hilkhach rishon vadai havai mazik* — therefore the first is certainly an active tortfeasor — *ve-sheni mista'ei tefu-ei she-im asiseihu le-gufo bor* — the second, by contrast, is best characterized as a *bor* with respect to his body.

The deeper point, not spelled out by Rashi but implicit in the analysis, is that the question of *bor* versus *adam hamazik* is ultimately a question of agency. When a person is stationary and another collides with him, the physics are those of a *bor*. To convert that into an act of *adam hamazik*, we need the person to have been, in some meaningful sense, *choosing* to be there. A high degree of negligence — tripping carelessly and then failing to rise — constitutes such a choice, retroactively. A lower degree of negligence — falling because of another's negligence and then failing to rise quickly enough — does not rise to that threshold of agency.

## Vessel Problem

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין עַל נִזְקֵי גּוּפָן, וּפְטוּרִין עַל נִזְקֵי מָמוֹנָן. מַאי, לָאו אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן?
>
> לָא, לְבַר מֵרִאשׁוֹן. וְהָא ״כּוּלָּם״ קָתָנֵי! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: ״כּוּלָּן״ – הַנִּיזָּקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rava's statement from a *baraita* that comments on this case: **All of them are liable** to pay **for damage caused by their bodies and exempt** from paying restitution **for damage caused by their property. What, does this not** refer **even** to **the first,** indicating that even he is exempt from damage caused by his property?
>
> The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to all of them **except for the first.** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *baraita* **teach** the term **all of them,** indicating that the first is also included? **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** The term **all of them** refers only to **those who incurred damage,** and excludes the first one, who only caused damage to others.

A *baraita* now presses on Rava's framework. The *baraita* states: *kulam chayavin al nizkei gufan u-pturin al nizkei mamonam* — all of them are liable for the bodily harm their persons cause, and all are exempt for the property damage their belongings cause. The problem: if *rishon* is an *adam hamazik*, as Rava says, he should be liable for vessel damage too. The *baraita* appears to exempt even him.

The *gemara* initially suggests that *kulam* — "all" — refers only to *ha-nizakin*, those who were themselves damaged (i.e., persons two and three), not to person one, who is the original wrongdoer. Person one remains liable even for property damage; *kulam* simply describes the cascade victims. The *gemara* rejects this reading on linguistic grounds: if the *baraita* meant only those who were damaged, it would have said *ha-nizakin*. The word *kulam* — "all of them" — is deliberately inclusive and encompasses everyone in the sequence.

### Person as Bor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״כּוּלָּן״; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְבַר מֵרִאשׁוֹן, מַאי ״כּוּלָּן״? לִיתְנֵי ״הַנִּיזָּקִין״!
>
> אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: רִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב – בֵּין בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ דְּשֵׁנִי, בֵּין בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ דְּשֵׁנִי; וְשֵׁנִי חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי שְׁלִישִׁי – בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ בּוֹר, וְלֹא מָצִינוּ בּוֹר שֶׁחִיֵּיב בּוֹ אֶת הַכֵּלִים.
>
> הָנִיחָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: כֹּל תַּקָּלָה – בּוֹר הוּא; אֶלָּא לְרַב, דְּאָמַר: אִי אַפְקְרֵיהּ אִין, אִי לָא לָא; מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this answer: **What** is **this** interpretation? **Granted, if you say** that the term includes **even the first, this** explanation **is** consistent with **that which is taught: All of them. But if you say** that it is referring to all of them **except for the first, what is** the reason that the misleading term: **All of them** is used? **Let** the *baraita* **teach** more accurately that **those who incurred damage** are liable in turn for the damage caused by their bodies, but are exempt from paying restitution for damage caused by their property.
>
> **Rather,** this entire explanation of Rava's statement should be rejected, and it should be explained as follows: **Rava said** that **the first** is **liable both for injury** caused **to the body of the second and for damage** caused **to the property of the second, and the second** is **liable** to pay **for damage incurred by the third with regard to injury to his body but not with regard to damage to his property. What is the reason** for the exemption in the last case? It is **because** after his fall, the body of the second person **is** effectively **a pit, and we do not find that** in the category of **Pit** one is **liable** to pay restitution **for** damage caused to **vessels.**
>
> The Gemara asks: **This works out well** according **to Shmuel, who says** that **any obstacle** that was placed in the public domain **constitutes a pit,** i.e., the *halakhot* of a pit apply to it. **But** according **to Rav, who says** that **if** the one who placed it there **renounces ownership** of the hazardous object it **is** considered a pit, **but if** he does **not** renounce ownership of it then it is **not** considered a pit, **what is there to say?** The second one who fell obviously did not renounce ownership of his body, so why is he exempt from damage he caused to vessels as though he were a pit?

The *gemara* raises a further structural problem: can a person even be classified as a *bor*? This touches on the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. For Shmuel, who holds *kol takala bor hu* — any hazard in the public domain has the legal status of a *bor* — there is no difficulty. A person lying in the road is simply a hazard, and hazards are *bor*. But for Rav, who holds that a *bor* requires *hefker* — the object must be ownerless — a person can never be a *bor*, since one never abandons ownership of oneself. On Rav's view, then, the entire framework of "the second person is a *bor*" collapses.

### Kelim-Kelim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם – כִּדְאָמַר מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּדְקַשְׁיָא לָךְ ״כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין״ – תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: שֶׁהוּזְּקוּ כֵּלִים בְּכֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** Rava's statement should be explained **as** he was understood to have **said initially,** i.e., that he distinguishes between damage caused by another's body and damage caused by his property. **And as for your difficulty** from the statement in the *baraita* that **all of them are liable** to pay for the damage caused by their bodies but exempt from paying restitution for the damage caused by their property, apparently including even the first one, contrary to Rava's opinion, **Rav Adda bar Minyumi interpreted it before Ravina** as referring to a case **where vessels were damaged by vessels.** In other words, it is not the body, but the vessels of the second that were damaged by the property of the first, and since the broken vessels of the first have the status of a pit, the owner is exempt from liability for damage caused to the vessels of others.

The *gemara* resolves the *baraita*'s challenge by reverting to the earlier reading. *Le'olam ke-de-amar me'ikara* — return to the original framework. The *baraita*'s statement that all are exempt for damage caused by their property is not about their bodies being classified as *bor* at all. Rather: *targema Rav Adda bar Ahavah kamei de-Ravina she-huzzku kelim be-kelim* — the *baraita* is speaking specifically of a case where one person's vessels damaged another person's vessels. In that case, the vessels lying broken on the ground are at most a *bor*, and *bor* is exempt for vessel damage (*patur be-kelim*). This is, in a sense, a straightforward application of an established rule: of course broken vessels in the road are a *bor*, and of course *bor* is *patur be-kelim*. The *baraita* is not making a novel claim about the person's body; it is simply stating the standard rule about broken objects as passive hazards.

### All Adam HaMazik

All persons in the sequence may in fact be classified as *adam hamazik* with respect to their bodies — each one, having had the opportunity to stand (*haya lo la'amod*), is liable for the injury his body causes. The exemption on vessel damage is not because the *person* is a *bor*, but because the *vessels* themselves, once broken and lying on the ground, function as a *bor*, and *bor* does not generate liability for damage to other *kelim*. The interesting new insight, then, is the prior framework: that the degree of negligence (*rishon* versus *sheni*) may determine whether the *person's body* is classified as *adam hamazik* or *bor* — a determination with direct practical consequences for liability toward the third party's property.

## Complete Causation

### Direct Trip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִם מֵחֲמַת רִאשׁוֹן נָפְלוּ – רִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקֵי כוּלָּם. מֵחֲמַת רִאשׁוֹן הֵיכִי נָפֵיל? רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: דְּפַסְקֵהּ לְאוֹרְחֵיהּ כְּשִׁלְדָּא. רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: כְּחוּטְרָא דְסַמְיוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above: **If they** all **fell because of the first, the first** is **liable** to pay **for the damage of them all.** The Gemara asks: **How did they** all **fall because of the first? Rav Pappa said:** It is a case **where he blocked the road like a skeleton [*keshilda*],** filling the entire width of the road and causing the rest to stumble over different parts of his body. **Rav Zevid said:** He fell diagonally **like a blind man's cane,** and they all stumbled over him.

The *baraita* concluded: *ve-im machmat rishon naflu kulam — rishon chayav be-nizkei kulam*. If they all fell because of the first person, the first person is liable for all damages. One might have read this to mean: person two and person three had no opportunity to do anything — it all happened too quickly — and therefore the original negligence of person one is the sole proximate cause of all subsequent harm, making him responsible for everyone. The *gemara*, however, does not adopt this reading. Instead, it interprets the clause as describing a scenario in which persons two and three both tripped *directly* over person one — not over each other in sequence — and therefore person one is causally responsible for all of them.

### Horizontal Spread

Rav Pappa explains: *de-paska le-orkah ke-shilda* — he spread himself across the width of the road like a carcass. His body (or, according to Rashi, something he was carrying) lay horizontally across the full width of the path, so that every subsequent pedestrian tripped directly over him, not over the person who fell before them. Person two, person three — they all encounter the same original hazard. Person one is therefore the direct and unmediated cause of each subsequent fall, and his liability extends to all.

### Angled Spread

Rav Zevid offers a different image: *ke-chutra de-samyusa* — like the staff of a blind person. Here, person one lies at a diagonal angle across the road. Person two falls over this angled obstacle. Person three, observing person two on the ground, might think: "Whatever caused *him* to fall is still far ahead of me — I do not yet need to be careful." But because of the diagonal, the actual hazard — the end of person one's body or beam — is much closer to person three than it appears. Person three, misled by the geometry, does not exercise appropriate caution and trips over person one just as person two did.

Tosafot develops this reading with characteristic precision. The point of the diagonal image is to explain why person three cannot deflect liability back onto person one with the argument: "I saw person two on the ground — I should have been warned." If the figure were completely horizontal, person three would indeed have been on notice; he saw person two fall and should have understood that the path ahead was dangerous. But the diagonal creates a visual deception: seeing person two on the ground does not give person three adequate warning, because the hazard appears to be further away than it actually is. Person three's failure to exercise caution is therefore not contributory negligence, and person one remains fully liable for the injury to person three as well.
