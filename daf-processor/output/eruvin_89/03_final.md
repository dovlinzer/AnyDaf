# Eruvin 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Water & Pit Issue

### Intro & Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אָמַר רַבָּה וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא
>
> **Translation:** (From Eruvin 88b, context for the opening discussion)

Daf 89 begins with a brief return to the close of the preceding *perek*, picking up from 88b. The discussion there concerned the disposal of waste water on *Shabbat*: if one pours water into a courtyard that measures at least four by four *amot*, the water will be absorbed and there is no concern; but if the courtyard is smaller, the water will run off, creating an appearance — or an actuality — of pouring directly into the *reshut harabim*. The remedy established by the Gemara was that one must prepare a pit (*bor*) into which the water can drain. The *mishna* at the end of that *perek* then introduced a case involving two second-story balconies overlooking a shared courtyard, one of which had made such a pit and one of which had not. The ruling was that only the balcony adjacent to the pit may pour water into the courtyard; the other may not.

### Two Balconies

The case requires closer examination. On the surface, one might expect that since both balconies overlook the same courtyard and the courtyard contains a pit, either party should be able to pour into the yard — after all, there is a pit there. The *mishna*'s asymmetry, granting rights to one and not the other, calls for explanation. To visualize the scenario in plan view: House One and House Two share a *chatzer*, and it is House One that arranged the pit. The Gemara will probe why the presence of that pit does not extend its benefit to the other party as well.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי דְיוֹטָאוֹת זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ. אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ עֵירְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And likewise,** with regard to **two upper stories, one opposite the other** in the same courtyard, the residents of the one who dug a pit in the courtyard may pour water into it, while the residents of the other one who did not dig a pit in the courtyard are prohibited from doing so. **Rava said:** This *halakha* applies **even if** the residents of the two upper stories **established an *eiruv*** together.

Four lines from the bottom of 88b, Rava opens the discussion: *amar Rava v'chein shtei diyutos — afilu ervu*, even if the two households made an *eruv*, one remains unable to use the pit. Before arriving at the Gemara's ultimate resolution, it is worth understanding what Rava is introducing. The scenario, drawn flat, is simply two houses opening onto a shared *chatzer*. When two such houses share a *chatzer*, they are required to make an *eruv chatzerot* with one another. Rava is going to assert that even when they have done so, the problem of the *mishna* persists. The Gemara will later clarify that the real issue concerns a case where no *eruv* was made, but Rava begins by framing the problem more broadly.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר (לֵיהּ) אַבָּיֵי: מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם נְפִישָׁא דְמַיָּא, וְהָתַנְיָא: אַחַת לִי עוּקָה, וְאַחַת לִי גִּיסְטְרָא בְּרֵיכָה וַעֲרֵיבָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּתְמַלְּאוּ מַיִם מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — שׁוֹפְכִין לְתוֹכָן מַיִם בַּשַּׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: What is the reason** for this ruling? **If you say** it is **due to the increase** in the amount **of water,** as two upper stories pour out more water than one, **wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The same** *halakha* applies to **a pit, and the same** applies to **a cracked** earthenware **vessel** used as a receptacle for water, or a small **pond, or a basin: Even though they were** already **filled with water on Shabbat eve, one may pour water into them on Shabbat.** It is evident from here that as long as the pit is the requisite size, there is no concern about the amount of water that will flow out from it.

Abaye challenges Rava: *amar Abaye mai ta'ama* — what exactly is the problem, even if they made an *eruv*? Abaye proposes that Rava may be bothered by a concern about water quantity — *nefisha demaya*. Perhaps a small courtyard with a pit was only ever permitted when a single household uses it. Once two households pour into the same pit, there may simply be too much water for the pit to handle, and thus neither should be permitted. This would explain why the *mishna* assigns usage to only one party: we give it to the one who arranged the pit and tell the other one that they have now exceeded the threshold.

Abaye rejects this interpretation, however, by citing a *baraita*: *achad uka ve-achad gistera breicha va-areiva* — whether the receptacle is an underground pit, a pottery vessel (*gistera*), a furrow, or a kneading trough (*areiva*), all of these are valid solutions. Crucially, the *baraita* adds: *af al pi she-nitmalu mayim me-erev shabbat, shofchin letocho mayim be-shabbat* — even if they were already filled from *erev shabbat*, it remains permitted to pour into them on *Shabbat*. This demonstrates that once one has satisfied the formal criterion — possessing a pit or equivalent receptacle — the actual capacity of the vessel is irrelevant. The pit may already be full and incapable of receiving more water, yet halachically the requirement is satisfied. It follows logically that the same criterion applies regardless of how many people are using the yard; if the pit's practical capacity is irrelevant in one direction, it is irrelevant across the board. And additionally, if the concern were simply about excessive water, the *mishna* would have forbidden both parties rather than permitting one and forbidding the other.

Abaye's reading of Rava's own position, reconstructed: Rava himself read the *mishna* as saying that a single pit was only instituted for a single household. Once two households use the yard, the one-pit arrangement is insufficient. Rather than forbid both, we assign the pit's benefit to the one who made it, restoring the single-household premise and permitting that party to pour. Abaye, however, demonstrates from the *baraita* that practical capacity is simply not the operative concern, and therefore the real issue must lie elsewhere.

### Rava Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ, אֲבָל עֵירְבוּ — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated it was stated as follows. Rava said:** **They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to a case **where** the residents of the two upper stories **did not establish an *eiruv*** together, **but if they established a** joint ***eiruv*, they are** all **permitted** to pour water into the courtyard.

The Gemara then re-presents Rava's view with a correction: *ela i itmar hachi itmar — amar Rava lo shanu ela shelo eirvu*. The problem in the *mishna* arises specifically when the two households did not make an *eruv*. Without an *eruv*, neither party may carry objects from their dwelling into the *chatzer*. The party whose pit is nearby can avoid this prohibition: he pours his waste water onto his own balcony, it runs off naturally, and flows into the yard. He has not carried anything into the *chatzer* directly. The other party, whose balcony is more distant, cannot accomplish the same. If he wishes to use the pit, he would have to carry his vessel down into the yard — precisely what is forbidden without an *eruv*. We are therefore concerned (*gezeira dilma ati l'apukei mimani debatim lehatam*) that permitting him to use the pit will lead him to carry his household vessels out into the *chatzer*. But with an *eruv* — *aval eirvu mutarin* — both parties may freely use the pit, since there is no longer any concern about carrying in the *chatzer*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי לֹא עֵירְבוּ, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: גְּזֵירָה דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמָּאנֵי דְבָתִּים לְהָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where they did not establish an *eiruv*, what is the reason** that the residents who did not dig a pit may **not** pour water into the courtyard? **Rav Ashi said:** It is **a decree, lest** people **come to take out vessels** filled with water **from** their **houses into** the courtyard, to pour into the pit. In the absence of an *eiruv*, this practice is prohibited.

This concludes the discussion of the final *sugya* of *perek* "Keitzad Mishtatefin."

## Domains & No Eruv

### Objects & Movement

The penultimate *perek*, "Kol HaGagot," opens with a focused examination of what follows from the absence of an *eruv*. When a group of households sharing a *chatzer* fails to make an *eruv chatzerot* among themselves, certain movements of objects become prohibited. Specifically: one may not carry an object from one's house into the *chatzer*, nor from the *chatzer* into any house, nor consequently from one house to another, even by a route that passes through the *chatzer*. Even if the houses were somehow directly connected, their being separately owned makes them distinct *reshuyot*, and carrying between them is prohibited.

What does remain permitted is movement within a single *reshut*: one may carry within one's own house freely, and objects that began *Shabbat* in the *chatzer* may be moved throughout the *chatzer*. The *chatzer* remains a *reshut hayachid* by virtue of its *lechi* or *korah* and its enclosed walls; the only prohibition is the crossing of domain boundaries.

### Object Identity

The crucial halachic principle governing these cases is that an object's identity — the domain to which it belongs for purposes of *Shabbat* — is fixed by its location when *Shabbat* begins. An object that began *Shabbat* in the *chatzer* may circulate throughout the *chatzer*. An object that began in one's house and was then improperly carried into the *chatzer* does not acquire a new *reshut* identity; it retains its association with the house, and moving it further within the *chatzer* remains prohibited. Conversely, a *chatzer* object that began there legitimately may be moved freely within the *chatzer*, but not into any of the houses. Domain identity is a fixed attribute, not a floating one.

### Multiple Courtyards

A further question arises: if there are multiple courtyards that open into one another, and no *eruv* has been made among them — or even if an *eruv* was made within each courtyard separately but not between them — may one carry from one courtyard into the next? Are all courtyards considered, even without an *eruv*, a single domain? The reasoning that might support this: courtyards are used less intensively and less personally than houses. Houses are the primary locations of household objects and personal domain; they are distinctly owned and felt as distinct *reshuyot*. Courtyards, being more general and communal spaces, might be treated as a single undifferentiated category, such that an object starting in any courtyard could freely move to any other courtyard. This is the analytical question the *perek* will address — beginning with the specific case of rooftops.

## Rooftops Mishna

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כָּל גַּגּוֹת הָעִיר רְשׁוּת אַחַת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא גַּג גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה אוֹ נָמוּךְ עֲשָׂרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד רְשׁוּת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the roofs of the city** are considered **one domain.** It is permitted to carry from one roof to another, even if the residents of the houses did not establish an *eiruv* between them. The Sages did not prohibit carrying between roofs, as it is rare to transfer an item from one roof to another. However, it is only permitted to transfer objects between roofs **provided that** one **roof is neither ten** handbreadths **higher nor ten** handbreadths **lower** than the adjacent roof. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Each and every one** of the roofs is **a domain in and of itself.** It is permitted to carry from one to the other only if the residents of both houses established an *eiruv*.

The *mishna* opens: *kol ha-gagot ir reshut achat* — all the rooftops of a town are one domain — *u-vilvad shelo yehei gag gavo'ah asarah o namuch asarah* — provided that no one rooftop is ten *tefachim* higher or lower than its neighbor. A height differential of ten *tefachim* would apparently classify it as a distinct domain. Since all rooftops are already more than ten *tefachim* above the ground (by virtue of the walls below), this qualification — that one rooftop not be ten higher or lower than another — is puzzling, and the Gemara will address why such a height difference should matter at all. This is the position of Rabbi Meir.

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד גַּגּוֹת וְאֶחָד חֲצֵירוֹת וְאֶחָד קַרְפֵּיפוֹת — רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ לְתוֹכָן, וְלֹא לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: Roofs, courtyards, and enclosures are all one domain with regard to vessels that were inside them** when **Shabbat** began, and one may therefore carry from one of these areas to another. However, they are **not** one domain **with regard to vessels that were inside the house** when **Shabbat** began and were later taken into one of the above domains. A vessel that was inside the house when Shabbat began and subsequently carried to one of these areas may be carried from one roof, courtyard, or enclosure to another only if an *eiruv* had been established between the domains.

The *mishna* then presents two additional positions. The *Chachamim* reject Rabbi Meir's leniency: *kol echad ve-echad reshut bifnei atzmo* — each and every rooftop is its own distinct domain, and one may not carry from one to another. Rabbi Shimon is the most lenient of the three. He declares that *achat gaghot ve-achat chatzerot ve-achat karpifot* — whether rooftops, courtyards, or *karpifot* (enclosed lots surrounded by a fence) — *reshut achat hen* — they are all a single domain — *le-keilim she-shavtu letokan* — for vessels that began *Shabbat* within them — *ve-lo le-keilim she-shavtu betoch habayit* — but not for vessels that began *Shabbat* inside a house. Objects that begin in a house cannot be carried into any of these spaces even if they are accidentally brought out; they retain their house identity. But objects that began in any of these spaces — rooftop, courtyard, or *karpef* — may be moved freely among all of them.

### Rabbi Shimon

A natural question arises about Rabbi Shimon's formulation: does he mean that rooftops are one category among themselves, courtyards one among themselves, and *karpifot* one among themselves — i.e., each type is internally unified — or does he mean that all three types together form a single unified domain? The Gemara reads him in the most expansive sense: all three types are one single *reshut*. The reasoning is that all of these spaces are technically *reshut hayachid* and none of them is a home. Homes are the primary locus of personal domain; they carry a sharp and distinct identity. All other spaces — rooftops, courtyards, and *karpifot* — share the character of being *reshut hayachid* without being living spaces, and therefore constitute one broad, undifferentiated category. Under Rabbi Shimon's view, one may carry from a courtyard to a rooftop (via a ladder, since one cannot pass through a house), from that rooftop into a *karpef*, and from one *karpef* into another, as long as none of it began in a house.

The *Tanna Kamma* — Rabbi Meir — says only that all rooftops are one; whether he extends the same principle to courtyards among themselves or to *karpifot* among themselves is left open by the *mishna*. The Gemara will clarify this. As for the *Chachamim*, they dispute even the basic premise.

## Height Difference

### Height Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יָתֵיב אַבָּיֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר אָבִין, וְיָתֵיב אַבָּיֵי גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין לְמַטָּה, כָּךְ דִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye bar Avin and Rabbi Ḥanina bar Avin were sitting, and Abaye was sitting beside them, and they sat and said: Granted, the Rabbis maintain: Just as residents are divided** into separate domains **below,** and they may not carry from house to house without an *eiruv*, **so are residents divided** into separate domains **above,** on the rooftops, and it is prohibited to carry from one roof to another without an *eiruv*.

The Gemara at this point engages the two sons of Rav Avin — Abaye bar Avin and Rabbi Chanina bar Avin — who were sitting with Abaye and raising the following analytical difficulty. The *Chachamim*'s position is easy to understand: just as the inhabitants below are distinct (*keshem she-diyuran chalukin lemata kach diyuran chalukin lemala*), so too their rooftops are considered distinct domains above. But what does Rabbi Meir hold? He must hold one of two things.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין לְמַטָּה, כָּךְ דִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין לְמַעְלָה, אַמַּאי רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן? וְאִי קָסָבַר אֵין חֲלוּקִין, דְּכׇל לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה רְשׁוּת אַחַת הִיא — אֲפִילּוּ גַּג גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְנָמוּךְ עֲשָׂרָה, נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **However, Rabbi Meir, what does he maintain;** what is the rationale for his opinion? **If he maintains** that **just as residents are divided** into separate domains **below, so are residents divided** into separate domains **above, why,** in his opinion, **are they** considered **one domain? And if he maintains** that **they are not divided** into separate domains, **as any** place **above ten** handbreadths off the ground **is** considered **one domain, even** if a **roof** is **ten** handbreadths **higher or ten** handbreadths **lower** than the adjacent roof, it should **likewise** be permitted to carry from one roof to the other.

If he agrees that the rooftops follow the divisions below — that they are distinct because the inhabitants are distinct — then why does he permit carrying from one rooftop to another? And if he holds the opposite: that above, these spaces are not differentiated by the separate inhabitants below, because no one actually *lives* on the rooftop, and anything above ten *tefachim* is in any case a unified *reshut* — then why does the *mishna* in Rabbi Meir's name require that no rooftop be ten *tefachim* higher or lower than its neighbor? If the distinguishing factor between rooftops is not the separate inhabitants below but simply height, why should one height within an already-elevated space be treated differently from another? Both are well above the ground-level threshold of ten *tefachim* that would classify them as *reshut hayachid*. What is the halachic significance of a difference in elevation between one roof and another?

### Pillar Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְכוּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי, אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת וְהֵן רְשׁוּת אַחַת, כְּגוֹן עַמּוּד בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְרָחָב אַרְבָּעָה — אָסוּר לְכַתֵּף עָלָיו, גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם תֵּל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. הָכִי נָמֵי, גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם תֵּל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to them:** Have **you not heard that which Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said** that **Rabbi Meir would say: Any place that you find two domains,** i.e., places set apart from each other by disparity in height or by boundaries, **and** yet **they are** halakhically **one domain, for example, a pillar ten** handbreadths **high and four** handbreadths **wide** situated **in a private domain,** it is **prohibited to adjust** a burden on one's **shoulders upon it,** by rabbinic **decree, due to** the concern lest he come to do the same thing on **a mound in the public domain.** The legal status of a mound ten handbreadths high and four handbreadths wide located in a public domain is that of a private domain. In that case, it is prohibited by Torah law to transfer an object from the public domain to the mound. **Here too,** in the case of roofs, Rabbi Meir prohibited transferring objects between roofs with a height disparity of ten handbreadths, by rabbinic **decree, due to** the concern lest one come to transfer an object from the public domain to **a mound in a public domain.**

Abaye answers by invoking a principle in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Adimi: *amar haya Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir used to teach — that wherever one finds two *reshuyot*, specifically a *reshut hayachid* within a *reshut hayachid* — for example, a pillar standing in a *reshut hayachid* that is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide, which would itself constitute a *reshut hayachid* even were it standing in a *reshut harabim* — *asur le-katef alav*, one may not place objects on top of that pillar. The reason: *gezeira mishum tel bi-reshut harabim* — a rabbinic decree lest one come to do the same when such a pillar stands in a *reshut harabim*, which would constitute a biblical violation (transferring between a *reshut harabim* and a *reshut hayachid*).

This is a striking *chumra*. It cannot apply indoors in all cases, or one would be prohibited from placing objects on furniture or a *shtender*. The Gemara is clearly applying it in outdoor contexts. And even then, it is an extreme position — one effectively cannot, in one's own *reshut hayachid*, place objects atop a fixed elevated structure, for fear that one will confuse the case with an identical-looking structure standing in a *reshut harabim*.

### Rooftop Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר מִינָּה אֲפִילּוּ מַכְתֶּשֶׁת וַאֲפִילּוּ גִּיגִית.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye and Ḥanina bar Avin **understood** by inference **from this** ruling that in the opinion of Rabbi Meir, it would be prohibited to adjust one's burden **even** on **a mortar and even** on **a vat** that were overturned in a private domain and that are large enough to constitute private domains in their own right.

This teaching of Rabbi Meir is then applied to explain the height-difference restriction in the *mishna*. When one rooftop is ten *tefachim* higher than an adjacent one, the person standing on the lower roof is in a *reshut hayachid*, and the higher roof is itself a *reshut hayachid* elevated above the lower. Placing objects on the higher roof from the lower would be analogous to placing objects on a pillar within a *reshut hayachid* — and Rabbi Meir therefore prohibits it by the same *gezeira*. The Gemara notes that this explanation is somewhat surprising: the plain reading of the *mishna* in Rabbi Meir's name sounds as though the height difference categorically creates a distinct domain. The Gemara, however, is internally compelled to explain it as a collateral concern of Rabbi Meir — the *gezeira* regarding pillars in a *reshut harabim* — rather than as a fundamental domain distinction, because normally once something exceeds ten *tefachim* in height, further differences in elevation do not create new domain categories.

### Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי אָמַר מָר: לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא עַמּוּד וְאַמַּת הָרֵיחַיִם, הוֹאִיל וְאָדָם קוֹבֵעַ לָהֶן מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to them: The Master,** Rabba, **said as follows: Rabbi Meir spoke only** in the case of **a pillar or the** raised **base of a millstone. Since a person fixes a place for them** they are comparable to a mound in a public domain in that they are rarely moved. However, the Sages did not issue a decree in the case of portable objects.

Having proposed this principle, the Gemara considers how far it extends. One might initially suppose that Rabbi Meir's prohibition applies to any elevated structure in a *reshut hayachid* — even a barrel (*gigis*) or a mortar (*machteshet*). The Gemara clarifies: *lo amar Rebbi Meir ela amud va-amasa de-rechayim* — Rabbi Meir only said this about a pillar and the large fixed stone configuration of a millstone. The criterion is *ho'il v'adam koveia lahem makom* — because a person fixes a permanent place for these structures. The prohibition applies only to structures that are truly fixed in the landscape, part of the permanent topography. A barrel or other movable vessel does not trigger the same concern.

### Wall Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, דְּקָבוּעַ, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּשֶׁתִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר: גַּגִּין רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן, חֲצֵירוֹת רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן, קַרְפֵּיפוֹת רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. There is the case of **a wall that is between two courtyards, which** is **fixed, and** nevertheless **Rav Yehuda said: When** you analyze the matter, **you will find** that **according to Rabbi Meir** all **roofs** form a single **domain in and of themselves,** and likewise all **courtyards** form a single **domain in and of themselves,** and all **enclosures** form a single **domain in and of themselves.** It is permitted to carry from one courtyard to another, although it is not permitted to carry from a courtyard to a roof.

The Gemara then pushes further: *v'harei kosel shebein basei chatzeiros* — what about the wall between two courtyards? Such a wall is certainly *kavu'a*, fixed. According to Rabbi Meir's principle, one should be prohibited from placing objects from one's courtyard onto the top of such a wall, since the wall functions like a pillar within the *reshut hayachid*. This is a significant implication: the entire discussion in the preceding *perek* about who may use the top of a wall separating two courtyards — with all its analysis of *eruv* arrangements and competing claims — was framed as an *eruv* question, with nobody suggesting that placing objects on a wall is intrinsically prohibited. According to Rabbi Meir, one could apparently never use a wall top at all. The Gemara will now turn to resolving this tension.

## Categories

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, דִּשְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵי דֶּרֶךְ כּוֹתֶל!
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not that it is permitted to move** objects from one courtyard to another **via a** dividing **wall,** even though it is ten handbreadths high? This poses a difficulty to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who prohibits the transfer of an object from one place to a place ten handbreadths higher or lower.

The Gemara uses the wall question to clarify an important point about Rabbi Meir's position. Rabbi Yehuda states: *k'she-timtza lomar l'divrei Rebbi Meir — gagin reshus l'atzman, chatzeiros reshus l'atzman, karpifos reshus l'atzman*. Rabbi Meir holds that rooftops form their own unified domain, courtyards form their own unified domain, and *karpifot* form their own unified domain — but these three categories remain distinct from one another. One may carry within any given category but not between categories. This is the distinction between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon: Rabbi Shimon unifies all three into a single domain, while Rabbi Meir maintains their categorical separateness while unifying each category within itself. This clarification is independent of the pillar discussion and illuminates the logical structure of the three-way debate.

### Doorways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לָא, לְהַכְנִיס וּלְהוֹצִיא דֶּרֶךְ פְּתָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Yehuda said** that **Rav Sheshet said: No,** that explanation is incorrect, as Rav Yehuda meant to say that according to Rabbi Meir it is permitted **to carry in and carry out** between one courtyard and another, or from one enclosure to another, **via** the **openings** between them. However, Rabbi Meir concedes that one may not transfer objects over the wall that separates the two domains, as the wall is considered a domain in and of itself.

Now the Gemara resolves the wall question. If Rabbi Meir holds that all courtyards form a unified domain and one may carry from one courtyard to another, but also holds that one may not use a wall as a platform (due to the *gezeira* regarding pillars), how does one actually transfer objects between courtyards? The Gemara answers by citing Rav Huna bar Yehuda in the name of Rav Sheshet: *lo lehachnis ulehotzi derech p'tachim* — one carries between courtyards not over the wall but through doorways. If there is an opening in the wall between the courtyards, one simply walks the object through. The top of the wall is never implicated. The implication, which the Gemara notes, is that according to Rabbi Meir, the wall top is indeed entirely unavailable — one may never place objects on a fixed wall within a *reshut hayachid*. This is a more radical position than the preceding *perek* assumed, but it follows coherently from Rabbi Meir's *gezeira* regarding pillars.

## Chachamim & Rav

### Chachamim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד רְשׁוּת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. אִיתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say** that **each and every one** of the roofs **is a domain in and of itself. It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed about the following issue. **Rav said:** According to the Rabbis, **one may move** objects on each roof **only within four cubits.** As, according to the Rabbis, the legal status of roofs is like that of courtyards, in that it is prohibited to carry from one roof to another, and each roof is fully open to a domain into which carrying is prohibited. Therefore, it is also prohibited to carry objects farther than four cubits on each roof. **And Shmuel said: It is permitted to move** objects throughout each **entire** roof.

The Gemara now turns to the *Chachamim*'s position. The *Chachamim* rule: *kol echad ve-echad reshut bifnei atzmo* — every individual rooftop is its own separate domain. Their reasoning, articulated by Abaye bar Avin and Rabbi Chanina bar Avin, is that just as the inhabitants below are distinct (*keshem she-diyuran chalukin lemata kach diyuran chalukin lemala*), so are the rooftops above. The rooftop of Building A is an extension of the distinct identity of Building A, and it may not mix with the rooftop of Building B. Given this, one cannot carry between rooftops. But may one carry freely on one's own rooftop?

### Rav Four Cubits

Rav rules: *ein metalatelin bo ela be-arba amot* — one may carry on a given rooftop only within four *amot*. The reasoning will become clear through the principle of *parutz bimlo'o*, discussed below. Rav holds that the absence of an effective partition between Roof A and Roof B compromises Roof A's status sufficiently that even carrying within Roof A is restricted to the *Shabbat* limit of four *amot* applicable in an open public-like space.

### Shmuel Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמְחִיצוֹת הַנִּיכָּרוֹת — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בִּמְחִיצוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן נִיכָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **With regard to partitions that are conspicuous,** i.e., detached houses whose walls are distinct, **everyone agrees** that it is permitted to carry throughout each roof. **Where they disagree** is **with regard to partitions that are not conspicuous,** i.e., attached houses, which appear as though they share a common roof although they are owned by different people.

Shmuel disagrees and rules: *mutar letaltel b'kulo* — one may carry throughout one's own rooftop without restriction. Shmuel applies the principle of *gud asik mechitzta* — the walls of Building A are considered to extend upward, creating virtual *mechitzot* around Roof A. Those virtual walls, though invisible at roof level, suffice to define Roof A as a fully enclosed *reshut hayachid* in which free carrying is permitted.

### Parutz Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — לָא אָמַר: ״גּוּד אַסֵּיק מְחִיצְתָּא״. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ — דְּאָמַר: ״גּוּד אַסֵּיק מְחִיצְתָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: One may carry** on each roof **only within four cubits.** Rav **does not state** the principle: **Extend and raise the partitions** between the houses below, which states that the walls of the houses are considered to extend upward and create partitions between the roofs. **And Shmuel said:** It is **permitted to carry throughout** each **entire** roof, **as he states** the principle: **Extend and raise the partitions.**

The debate between Rav and Shmuel is anchored in the principle of *parutz bemilo'o lemakom ha'asur lo* — if a domain is completely open on one side toward a domain into which carrying is forbidden, that exposure renders the otherwise-permitted domain forbidden as well. In the rooftop scenario, Roof A and Roof B are adjacent, and one may not carry from A to B. If there is no effective partition between them — if A is completely open toward B — then A is considered *parutz bemilo'o* and one may not carry freely in A itself. Rav holds that this principle applies here, because when the buildings are attached, the wall below is invisible at roof level and cannot effectively divide A from B. Shmuel holds that the wall's extension upward via *gud asik* provides a sufficient division, and *parutz bemilo'o* therefore does not apply.

### Walls Visible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַב קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question based on that which **we learned** in the mishna: And **the Rabbis say** that **each and every one** of the roofs is **a domain in and of itself.** This indicates that each roof constitutes a discrete domain, and one may carry throughout this entire domain. **Granted according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** this works out **well, but according to** the opinion of **Rav,** it is **difficult.**

The Gemara clarifies the scope of their disagreement. When the walls between the two buildings are *nikarot* — visible — there is no dispute. Detached buildings have visible walls, and those visible walls define the boundaries of each rooftop clearly. Even if the gap between rooftops is physically small, the visual presence of the wall creates the division, and Roof A is not considered to be *parutz bemilo'o* toward B. Both Rav and Shmuel agree that in such a case, carrying on one's own rooftop is fully permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: שֶׁלֹּא יְטַלְטֵל שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּגַג זֶה, וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּגַג זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the Sages of the **school of Rav said in the name of Rav:** The ruling in the mishna is not a leniency permitting one to carry throughout the entire roof; rather, it is a stringency, ruling **that one may not move** an object **two cubits on this roof and two cubits on that roof.** The *tanna* rules that even the allowance to carry within four cubits is restricted to a single roof.

The dispute is specifically *b'mechitzot she'einam nikarot* — when the walls between the buildings are invisible at roof level, i.e., the buildings are attached. In this case, is the concealed wall sufficient to divide A from B? Rav rules: *lo amrinan gud asik mechitzta* — in this configuration, one does not apply *gud asik* to extend the invisible wall upward so as to create a meaningful partition. This does not mean Rav entirely denies *gud asik*; as Rashi notes on the final line of the Gemara, the wall certainly extends enough to make the rooftop a *reshut hayachid* in general. But it does not extend sufficiently to effect a meaningful *separation* between A and B when it is invisible below. Therefore, A is considered *parutz bemilo'o* toward B, and carrying is restricted to four *amot*. Shmuel holds that *amrinan gud asik mechitzta* — the invisible wall's upward extension is fully operative, A and B are effectively separated above, and A is therefore a complete *reshut hayachid* in which free carrying is permitted.

The simple reading of the *mishna* — *kol echad ve-echad reshut bifnei atzmo*, each rooftop is its own domain — strongly implies that one may carry freely on one's own rooftop, which aligns with Shmuel's position. The Gemara acknowledges this (*bishlama l'Shmuel nicha*) and notes the difficulty for Rav. Rav's school responds by re-reading the *mishna*: the statement that each rooftop is its own domain means only that one may carry four *amot* on one's own rooftop, and categorically may not carry from A to B — not even a step that spans both. The language "its own domain" does not imply unlimited carrying; it implies four *amot* on the home rooftop and zero from rooftop to rooftop. The *baraita* from Shmuel's school (*ein lahen ela gagan* — they have only their own rooftop) is similarly re-read by the Gemara to permit only four *amot*, not full carrying — the same explanatory move applied to the language of the *mishna* itself.

## Large & Small

### Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַתְּ אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן, וְאַהָא אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן: גַּג גָּדוֹל הַסָּמוּךְ לְקָטָן — הַגָּדוֹל מוּתָּר, וְהַקָּטָן אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said,** after an illness had caused him to forget his knowledge: **I have not heard this *halakha*** of Shmuel's with regard to roofs. His student **Abaye said to him: You** yourself **said it to us, and it was about this** that **you said it to us:** With regard to **a large roof that is adjacent to a small one,** carrying on **the large** one **is permitted,** as its partitions are distinct where it extends beyond the small one, **and** carrying on **the small one is prohibited,** as it is breached along its entire length into the other roof, onto which it is prohibited to carry.

The Gemara now introduces the case of a large rooftop (*gag gadol*) adjacent to a small rooftop (*gag katan*). This case arises when the buildings are of different sizes — Building A is larger, Building B is smaller — such that the two buildings share a portion of wall (the connected section) but Building A's wall extends visibly beyond that connection. The ruling: *hagadol mutar vehakatan asur* — the large rooftop is permitted and the small is forbidden.

### Why Difference

Why the asymmetry? The small rooftop is *parutz bemilo'o* — it is completely exposed to the adjacent space without any visible wall demarcating it. The large rooftop, by contrast, has a partial visible wall: its extended section is bounded by a visible wall, even though the portion shared with the small building has no visible divider at roof level. This partial wall suffices to prevent A from being classified as *parutz bemilo'o*, and A therefore retains its status as a permitted domain. B, lacking any such visible boundary, is fully exposed and forbidden. This case therefore appears to support Rav's position, since it demonstrates that the invisible connecting wall is not treated as a partition — if it were, B would also have a wall and would also be permitted.

### Shmuel Habitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרְתְּ לַן עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ דִּיּוּרִין עַל זֶה וְדִיּוּרִין עַל זֶה, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ הָא דְּקָטָן מְחִיצָה נִדְרֶסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And you said to us about it: Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They only taught** this *halakha* in a case **where there are residents on this** roof **and residents on that** roof, **as** the extended, virtual partition **of the small** roof is considered **a trampled partition.** The residents trample this virtual partition as they move from one roof to the other, and the entire length of the small roof is considered breached into the large one.

Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Shmuel an alternative reading of this case. Shmuel says: *lo shanu ela sheyesh diurin al zeh vediurin al zeh* — this ruling (that the large is permitted and the small forbidden) applies only when people are actually regularly residing on both rooftops. In that case, the dividing line between A and B is constantly being walked over (*mechitza nidreset*), and by virtue of that regular trampling, the line is shown to be no line at all. When people habitually cross that boundary, they demonstrate its non-existence, and therefore A is exposed to B as if the wall did not exist. In this specific circumstance, B is *parutz bemilo'o* (completely exposed, since even the connecting section is effectively abolished by regular use), and A is only partially bounded (its extended section being visible). Hence the large is permitted and the small is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵין דִּיּוּרִין עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה — שְׁנֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if **there are no residents on this** roof **and** none **on that** one, carrying on **both** roofs is **permitted.** Presumably, Shmuel's reasoning is that in this case the walls of the houses below extend upward and form partitions between the roofs, in accordance with the principle: Extend and raise the partitions.

But if people do not regularly use the rooftops and do not walk over that line — *aval ein diurin al zeh veal zeh shneihem mutarin* — then both rooftops are fully permitted, because the invisible wall, unviolated by actual use, does retain its status as a division. This reading aligns with Shmuel's general position: *gud asik* applies, and the invisible wall is effective unless actively undermined by habitual crossing.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הָכִי אֲמַרִי לְכוּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְחִיצָה עַל זֶה וּמְחִיצָה עַל זֶה, דְּגָדוֹל מִישְׁתְּרֵי בְּגִיפּוּפֵי, וְקָטָן נִפְרָץ בִּמְלוֹאוֹ. אֲבָל אֵין מְחִיצָה לֹא עַל זֶה וְלֹא עַל זֶה — שְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him:** I remember it now. **I said to you as follows: They taught** this *halakha*, that carrying is prohibited on the small roof, **only** with regard to a case **where there was an** actual **partition on** all sides of **this** roof **and an** actual **partition on** all sides of **that** roof, not only between the two roofs. In **that** case, carrying on **the large** roof **is permitted by** means of **the remnants** of the partition on either side of the opening, **and** carrying on the **small** roof **is** prohibited because it is **fully breached** into the larger one. **However, if there is no partition, neither on** all sides of **this** roof **nor on** all sides of **that** roof, carrying on **both of them is prohibited.**

Rav Yosef initially states he has no recollection of this *shemata* — this teaching — at all. Abaye informs him that he himself taught it, and that he did so in the context of this very case of the large and small rooftop. One finds this recurring motif in *Eruvin*: Rav Yosef, following his illness, frequently requires his students to restore his own teachings to him. Abaye's reading of what Rav Yosef had taught was that the operative factor was habitation — whether people were actually living up on the roof — and that the question of whether the boundary line was being walked over or not determined the ruling.

Rav Yosef, however, corrects Abaye's understanding. He says: *ana hachi amri lechu* — this is what I actually said. *Lo shanu ela sheyesh mechitza al zeh umechitza al zeh — de-gadol mishtari be-gifufei ve-katan nifrat bimlo'o*. Rav Yosef's position is more stringent than either Rav or Shmuel. He holds that the only reason A is permitted is that A has actual physical *mechitzot* surrounding it — even partial walls (*gifufei*) — whereas B is *parutz bemilo'o* by virtue of being entirely exposed. But if neither A nor B has any real *mechitza* at all — *aval ein mechitza lo le-zeh velo le-zeh, sheneihem asurin* — both are forbidden. One cannot rely on the wall below, visible or otherwise, to constitute a division above. A real *mechitza* at rooftop level is required. This is more stringent than Rav, who at least accepts the visible wall below as sufficient to permit the rooftop.

## Dwelling Mechitza

### Mechitza Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּיּוּרִין אֲמַרְתְּ לַן! אִי אֲמַרִי לְכוּ דִּיּוּרִין, הָכִי אֲמַרִי לְכוּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ מְחִיצָה רְאוּיָה לְדִירָה עַל זֶה וּמְחִיצָה רְאוּיָה לְדִירָה עַל זֶה, דְּגָדוֹל מִישְׁתְּרֵי בְּגִיפּוּפֵי וְקָטָן נִפְרָץ בִּמְלוֹאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye raised a difficulty: **But didn't you speak to us** of **residents?** Rav Yosef replied: **If I spoke to you** of **residents, this** is what **I said to you: They taught** this *halakha*, that carrying is prohibited on the small roof, **only** in a case **where there is an** actual **partition** that renders the area **fit for residence on** all sides of **this** roof, **and an** actual **partition** that renders the area fit **for residence on** all sides of **that** roof, as carrying on **the large** roof **is permitted by** means of **the remnants** of the partition on either side of the opening, **and** carrying on the **small** roof **is** prohibited because it is **fully breached** into the larger one.

Abaye, however, pushes back, insisting that the concept of *diurin* — habitation — which he remembers Rav Yosef introducing is not about whether people habitually walk over the line (Shmuel's reading) but refers to something else. Rav Yosef then refines his position further: *hachi amri lechu — lo shanu ela sheyesh mechitza re'uya le-dira al zeh u-mechitza re'uya le-dira al zeh*. What is required is not merely any halachic *mechitza* but specifically a *mechitza re'uya le-dira* — a partition suited for habitation. The definition of *mechitza re'uya le-dira* is a genuinely permanent constructed wall, the kind around which one could realistically decide to inhabit the rooftop space. A curtain, a canvas partition, or any halachically valid but physically impermanent *mechitza* does not qualify. Only a permanent wall constitutes a *mechitza re'uya le-dira*.

### Contrast Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל יֵשׁ מְחִיצָה רְאוּיָה לְדִירָה עַל הַגָּדוֹל, וְאֵין רְאוּיָה לְדִירָה עַל הַקָּטָן — אֲפִילּוּ קָטָן שְׁרֵי לִבְנֵי גָדוֹל. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא עֲבוּד מְחִיצָה, סַלּוֹקֵי סַלִּיקוּ נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if **there is a partition** that renders the area fit **for a residence on** all sides of **the large** roof, **but** there is **no** partition that renders the area fit for **a residence on the small** roof, carrying **even** on **the small** roof **is permitted for the residents of the large** roof. **What is the reason** for this? **Since** the residents of the small roof **did not erect a partition** around their roof, **they** thereby **removed themselves from here** and transferred the right to their domain to the residents of the large roof.

This leads to a subtle and consequential ruling. The contrast between permanent and temporary *mechitzot* on adjacent rooftops is not merely a matter of degree; it generates an active legal effect. If Building A has permanent walls — *mechitzot re'uyot le-dira* — around its rooftop, this signals that the occupants of A have genuinely established their presence and domain on the rooftop. Building B, which may have halachically valid but temporary and impermanent partitions, signals that its occupants have not made any serious claim to the rooftop space. The reality of A's presence above *contrasts* with B's absence, and this contrast actively diminishes B's standing as a separate domain.

### Result

The ruling that follows: *aval yesh mechitza re'uya le-dira al hagadol ve-ein re'uya le-dira al hakatan — afilu katan shari livnei gadol*. If A has permanent *mechitzot* and B has only impermanent ones, then even B's rooftop is permitted for the residents of A. Why? *Keivan d'lo avad mechitza, silukei silikei nafshei meihacha* — since B's occupants did not erect a permanent wall, they have effectively withdrawn their presence from the rooftop. The rooftop above B is no longer a genuinely contested domain; from the perspective of someone who has truly established themselves on the roof (Building A), B's rooftop has been vacated of any competing claim. A may therefore carry into B's rooftop as well, treating the combined rooftop as a single domain under A's control. B, for its part, remains restricted in the usual way and may not freely carry even on its own rooftop, since its *mechitzot* do not assert a genuine presence.

## Ladder & Habitation

### Fixed Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּהָא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עָשָׂה סוּלָּם קָבוּעַ לְגַגּוֹ — הוּתַּר בְּכׇל הַגַּגִּין כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with that which Rav Naḥman said:** If one **affixed a permanent ladder to his roof,** while the owners of the neighboring roofs did not do so, it **is permitted for him to** carry on **all the roofs.** The failure of the other owners to erect a ladder indicates that they relinquished the right to their roofs to the one who affixed the permanent ladder.

The Gemara closes with a statement of Rav Nachman that generalizes this logic into a practical tool: *asu sulam kavua l'gago* — if one installs a fixed ladder to one's rooftop, *hutar b'chol hagagin kulan* — one becomes permitted to carry across all the rooftops.

The reasoning follows directly from the principles developed above. According to the *Chachamim*, all rooftops are in principle distinct domains, each corresponding to the separate ownership of the building below. But this distinctness is, in an important sense, virtual: no one actually lives on any rooftop, and the differences are projected upward from the reality below rather than grounded in genuine habitation above. When one installs a permanent ladder to one's rooftop, one does more than gain physical access — one establishes *real*, actual habitation above. One has made a genuine claim to the rooftop space, as opposed to a merely constructive one.

### Total Use

Once genuine habitation is established — by fixed ladder, by permanent walls, or by any comparable means — that real presence trumps all the competing virtual claims of the other rooftop owners, none of whom have made a comparable investment in the space above. The person who has established real habitation may therefore treat all the rooftops as a single domain under his effective control, carrying freely across all of them. The other rooftop owners, lacking that genuine presence, do not acquire symmetric rights; they remain restricted in the ways their *mechitzot* and presence — or absence thereof — determine.

### Summary

By way of summary: the *Chachamim* hold that each rooftop is a distinct domain, since the separate inhabitants below generate separate domains above. Whether one may carry freely on one's own rooftop — even without crossing to another — depends on the debate between Rav and Shmuel. Rav holds that when buildings are attached and the wall between them is invisible at rooftop level, *parutz bemilo'o* applies and carrying is limited to four *amot*; *gud asik* is not operative in this configuration. Shmuel holds that *gud asik* does apply even for invisible walls, and one may carry freely on one's own rooftop. Rav Yosef goes further than either, holding that not even visible walls below are sufficient; only real *mechitzot* at rooftop level enable full carrying on that rooftop. And across all these positions, the one overriding leniency is the principle of establishing genuine habitation above: once a person makes a real and fixed presence on the rooftop — by permanent ladder or permanent wall — that presence overrides all virtual distinctions and grants full rights over the entire expanse of connected rooftops.
