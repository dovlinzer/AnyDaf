# Berakhot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavod HaBriyot Override

### Previous Summary

We return to the bottom of 19b, where the *sugya* has been examining the principle of *kavod habriyot* — human dignity — and its capacity to override various *halachic* obligations. The *gemara*'s basic position is that *kavod habriyot* does not override a biblical prohibition, such as wearing *kilayim*. An apparent counterexample involving *tumah* was set aside on the grounds that the *tumah* in question was only rabbinic, and a rabbinic concern *kavod habriyot* does override. The *sugya* then concluded with the case of a lost object: one need not return an *aveidah* if bending down to retrieve it would be beneath one's dignity. That, however, was characterized as a monetary matter, subject to different standards than a direct confrontation with a prohibition. Now the *gemara* poses one final question before drawing its conclusions.

### Nazir & Meis Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מִטַּמֵּא הוּא לְמֵת מִצְוָה. אַמַּאי, לֵימָא: ״אֵין חׇכְמָה וְאֵין תְּבוּנָה וְאֵין עֵצָה לְנֶגֶד ה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **but he does become impure for a *met mitzva*.** Here too, the question is asked: **Let us say** that the obligation to bury a *met mitzva*, which is predicated on the preservation of human dignity, should not override mitzvot explicitly written in the Torah, as it is stated: **"There is neither wisdom, nor understanding, nor counsel against the Lord."**

The *gemara* introduces a *braisa*: *tashma* — come and hear. The Torah states that a *nazir* may not become *tamei* even to an immediate family member. The verse singles out his sister (*velaachoso*), even though his mother and father have already been mentioned. The novel teaching concerns a case where someone — even a *nazir* — was on his way to slaughter his *korban pesach* or to perform *bris milah* for his son, and received word that a family member had died. One might have thought he should turn back and become *tamei*. The *braisa* rules: he may not. The *chidush* is not that he cannot become *tamei* — in such a case there are all the more reasons not to — but rather what the extra phrase *velaachoso* comes to permit: becoming *tamei* to a *meis mitzvah*.

A *meis mitzvah* is a corpse that has no one to bury it. Though not stated explicitly in the Torah, the rabbis read into the text the principle that this overriding obligation supersedes all else — even a *kohen gadol*, even a *nazir*, even one on his way to bring a *korban pesach* or perform *bris milah*, must stop to bury a *meis mitzvah*. The additional *braisa* similarly teaches that even a *kohen gadol*, with all his additional reasons to avoid *tumah*, is overridden by the needs of a *meis mitzvah*.

Now the *gemara* pauses to identify the animating principle behind the *meis mitzvah* obligation. What makes *meis mitzvah* different from ordinary cases where a *nazir* is forbidden to become *tamei*? If someone else is available to bury the body, the *nazir* does not become *tamei* even for his own father. The urgency must therefore derive from *kavod habriyot* — human dignity — which makes this situation qualitatively different. This is itself a striking juxtaposition: the preceding *sugya* treated the weight of *kavod harav* and earlier discussions addressed *kavod hameit*, and now we arrive at the force of *kavod habriyot* in this most extreme case.

### Limits of Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב ״וְלַאֲחוֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There it is different, as it is** explicitly **written: "And his sister,"** from which we derive that although he may not become ritually impure to bury his sister, he must do so for a *met mitzva*.

Given that *kavod habriyot* overrides the prohibition against *tumah* in the case of *meis mitzvah*, the *gemara* asks: why not say *ein chochma ve'ein tevunah ve'ein eitza leneged Hashem* — that when God's commandment is at stake, no consideration of human dignity can override it? The answer seems to be that here, in fact, it does override. So why not extend the principle further — why not use *meis mitzvah* as a paradigm showing that *kavod habriyot* overrides all prohibitions? Or alternatively, why not say that all aspects of *kavod habriyot*, not just *meis mitzvah*, override biblical *mitzvot*? That would open an enormous category.

The *gemara* answers: *shev ve'al taaseh shani* — passive non-performance is different. But this requires clarification: who is being passive in the *meis mitzvah* case? The *nazir* is actively becoming *tamei*. What does passivity have to do with it?

### Hutrah or Dehuya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְמַר מִינַּהּ! שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us derive** a general principle that human dignity takes precedence over all mitzvot in the Torah **from this** case. This possibility is rejected: This is a special case, because a case of **"sit and refrain from action"** [***shev ve'al ta'aseh*] is different.** Engaging in the burial of a *met mitzva* is not actually in contravention of a mitzva. Rather, by doing so he becomes ritually impure and is then rendered incapable of fulfilling that mitzva. We cannot derive a general principle from here that human dignity would also override a Torah prohibition in a case where that prohibition is directly contravened.

Rashi addresses this directly. He explains that the *gemara*'s answer sidesteps the question by reframing the entire situation: we are not saying that the prohibition of *tumah* is overridden for the sake of human dignity. Rather, the prohibitions against a *kohen* or a *nazir* becoming *tamei* were simply never said to apply in a case of *meis mitzvah*. The *Torah* from the outset never forbade *tumah* in this context — it is, in the classic formulation, *hutra*, permitted, not *dechuya*, pushed aside.

This is the well-known question of *hutra o dechuya*: when I violate *Shabbat* to save a dying person, have I violated *Shabbat* but done so mandatorily, or is there no violation at all in the context of saving a life? Rashi applies this framework here: the *tumah* for *meis mitzvah* was never prohibited; hence one cannot derive from it any broader principle about *kavod habriyot* overriding prohibitions.

But a difficulty remains: even granting Rashi's framing for the *tumah* itself, the *nazir* who buries the *meis mitzvah* will now be unable to bring his *korban pesach* and unable to perform *bris milah* for his son — obligations carrying *kareit* — because *tumah* stands in the way. One cannot say those prohibitions were also never applicable. To this, the *gemara* responds: the failure to perform those *mitzvot* is passive non-performance. He is not actively violating anything; he is simply not fulfilling a positive commandment as a consequence. And *kavod habriyot* does override passive non-performance even of biblical *mitzvot*.

### Synthesis of Override

The *gemara*'s conclusions can now be stated clearly. *Kavod habriyot* overrides, first, rabbinic prohibitions — and this is attested in other places in *Shas*, such as the case of someone in a field on *Shabbat* who, for reasons of dignity, must pick up stones to use for hygiene, thereby actively violating a rabbinic prohibition. Second, *kavod habriyot* overrides certain monetary obligations, such as the requirement to return a lost object, though the precise contours of this category involve only *mitzvah*-related monetary obligations, not, for instance, outright theft. Third, *kavod habriyot* overrides even biblical *mitzvot* when the failure to perform them is passive — *shev ve'al taaseh* — rather than active.

As a concrete example of the third category: suppose that fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *tekiat shofar* would require walking through a public space in a state that profoundly violates one's dignity — say, without clothing — such that remaining in place and missing the *tekia* is the only dignified option. In that case, the passive non-performance of a biblical *mitzvah* would be permissible on grounds of *kavod habriyot*.

### Subjectivity Issue

This synthesis immediately raises a serious difficulty. *Kavod habriyot* is an amorphous standard. How broadly or narrowly do we define dignity? Does dignity mean how others perceive one publicly, or one's own internal sense of self-worth? The category threatens to swallow everything: invoked constantly, it could override almost any obligation. Yet if *poskim* are too cautious to invoke it, the principle fails to serve its purpose. This tension is reflected in the historical observation — made at an Edah conference by Rabbi Ezra Labaton in a *shiur* on the use of *kavod habriyot* in Sephardic *psak* — that Sephardic *psak* tends to deploy *kavod habriyot* more readily within its *gemara*-given parameters than Ashkenazic *psak* does. Whether or not this characterization holds across the board, it illustrates that the principle's application is genuinely contested.

### Kavod HaMeit Question

A further question worth raising here concerns whose dignity is actually at stake in the *meis mitzvah* case. Is the *kavod* we are protecting the dignity of the dead person, or of the living community that must witness an unburied corpse? The *gemara*, particularly as the *Yerushalmi* understands the laws of *onen*, seems to take *kavod hameit* — the dignity of the deceased — as a meaningful concept in its own right. Yet this raises a philosophical difficulty: if *meisim einam yodim me'umah* — the dead have no awareness — in what sense can we speak of honoring *them*?

One response is that *kavod hameit* is really a displaced form of *kavod habriyot* — we honor the dead because of what it means to living society, to how we treat our *meitim* as a collective. But the *gemara* does not seem troubled by the question and treats *kavod hameit* as a self-evident concept. A counterexample makes the point vivid: if one is walking alone through a forest and encounters an unburied body that no living person knows about, there is still an obligation of *meis mitzvah*, even though no community's feelings are affected and no social dignity is at stake. This suggests that *kavod hameit* has an irreducibly independent content — though this only underscores how expansive and difficult to define these categories can be.

### Tosafot Alternative

Tosafot is dissatisfied with Rashi's approach. The argument that *tumah* was never prohibited in this context — the *hutra* framing — can be deployed in almost any case, which makes it a weak analytical tool. Tosafot therefore offers an alternative explanation: the reason *meis mitzvah* cannot serve as a paradigm for *kavod habriyot* overriding all prohibitions is not because of *hutra*, but because the prohibitions of *tumah* involved here — those governing a *nazir* and a *kohen* — are not universal. They apply only to specific categories of people. A non-universal prohibition cannot function as a general paradigm.

### Tzaar Baalei Chayim

One final and genuinely remarkable observation closes this *sugya*. The *gemara* here establishes that *kavod habriyot* is a value extracted implicitly from certain *mitzvot* — never stated as a free-standing commandment — and then given direct operative weight within *halacha*, capable of overriding rabbinic prohibitions and the passive non-performance of biblical ones. There is, as far as can be determined, only one other case in *Shas* where the *gemara* performs this same operation: *tzaar baalei chayim* — the prohibition of causing animal suffering.

The *gemara* in Bava Metzia debates whether *tzaar baalei chayim* is *d'oraissa* or *d'rabanan*. Even if it is *d'oraissa*, there is no explicit *pasuk* commanding care for animal suffering. The source is the implicit value embedded in certain *mitzvot* — most notably the obligation to unload an animal struggling under its burden. The *gemara* extracts that implicit value and gives it direct *halachic* force, to the precise effect that *tzaar baalei chayim* overrides rabbinic prohibitions — the same role *kavod habriyot* plays. These appear to be the only two instances in all of *Shas* where the *gemara* identifies a value implicit within a *mitzvah*, extracts it, and transforms it into an active, independent factor within the *halachic* system with real operative weight.

## Miracles & Kiddush Hashem

### Rav Pappa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: מַאי שְׁנָא רִאשׁוֹנִים דְּאִתְרְחִישׁ לְהוּ נִיסָּא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא אֲנַן דְּלָא מִתְרְחִישׁ לַן נִיסָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: What is different about the earlier** generations, **for whom miracles occurred and what is different about us, for whom miracles do not occur?**

Four lines into 20a, the *gemara* shifts to what initially appears to be an *aggadic* digression, though its connection to the preceding themes will become clear. Rav Pappa asks Abaye: *mai shna rishonim de'itrachish lehu nisa u'mai shna anan de'lo mitrachish lan nisa* — what is different about the early sages that miracles were performed for them, yet for us miracles are not performed?

### Learning Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם תַּנּוֹיֵי, בִּשְׁנֵי דְרַב יְהוּדָה כּוּלֵּי תַּנּוֹיֵי בִּנְזִיקִין הֲוָה, וַאֲנַן קָא מַתְנִינַן שִׁיתָּא סִדְרֵי. וְכִי הֲוָה מָטֵי רַב יְהוּדָה בְּעוּקְצִין, הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁכּוֹבֶשֶׁת יָרָק בַּקְּדֵרָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ זֵיתִים שֶׁכְּבָשָׁן בְּטַרְפֵיהֶן טְהוֹרִים, אָמַר: הֲוָיוֹת דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל קָא חָזֵינָא הָכָא. וַאֲנַן קָא מַתְנִינַן בְּעוּקְצִין תְּלֵיסַר מְתִיבָתָא, וְאִילּוּ רַב יְהוּדָה כִּי הֲוָה שָׁלֵיף חַד מְסָאנֵיהּ, אָתֵי מִטְרָא, וַאֲנַן קָא מְצַעֲרִינַן נַפְשִׁין וּמִצְוָח קָא צָוְחִינַן, וְלֵית דְּמַשְׁגַּח בַּן.
>
> **Translation:** If **it is because of** Torah **study; in the years of Rav Yehuda all of their learning was** confined to **the** order of ***Nezikin*, while we learn** all **six orders!** Moreover, **when Rav Yehuda would reach in** tractate ***Okatzin*,** which discusses the extent to which the stems of various fruits and vegetables are considered an integral part of the produce in terms of becoming ritually impure, the *halakha* that **a woman who pickles a vegetable in a pot, and some say** when he would reach the *halakha* that **olives pickled with their leaves are pure,** because after pickling, it is no longer possible to lift the fruit by its leaves, they are no longer considered part of the fruit; he would find it difficult to understand. He **would say:** Those are **the disputes between Rav and Shmuel that we see here. And we,** in contrast, **learn thirteen versions of *Okatzin*. While,** with regard to miracles, after declaring a fast to pray for a drought to end, **when Rav Yehuda would remove one of his shoes the rain would** immediately **fall, whereas we torment ourselves and cry out and no one notices us.**

Rav Pappa anticipates the most natural answer — that the earlier generations were greater *talmidei chachamim* — and immediately refutes it. In the time of Rav Yehuda, who serves as the representative of the earlier *Amoraim* being compared to, all of their learning was in *Nezikin*. That was essentially the one order they studied seriously and in depth. We, by contrast, learn all six *sedarim* of *Shas* — *shisha sidrei Shas* — and engage in broad and deep analysis across the board. We are demonstrably more learned.

The *gemara* illustrates what Rav Yehuda's limits looked like in practice: when he reached the tractate *Uktzin* — clearly the most obscure and difficult *masechet* of *Taharot* — he encountered the *mishnah* discussing a woman who pickles a vegetable in a pot, or alternatively olives pickled with their leaves, and the question of their status as *tehoros*. He said: *havayot de-Rav ve-Shmuel kachazina hacha* — I see here the kinds of profound questions that Rav and Shmuel would go to town on, but I cannot even begin to navigate them. He was confessing that this *mishnah* exceeded his grasp. Rashi and Tosafot actually disagree about what the *mishnah* itself means: Rashi understands it to concern whether leaves in the process of wilting lose their status as a *shomer* or *yad* for the purposes of transmitting *tumah*, since pickling will cause them to wither. Tosafot argues the question is rather whether the leaves themselves qualify as food, given that pickled leaves might or might not be edible. The very *pshat* of the *mishnah* is disputed — illustrating why Rav Yehuda found it so difficult.

We, says Rav Pappa, have thirteen different approaches to learning *Uktzin* — according to Rashi, thirteen distinct analytical frameworks; alternatively, thirteen different *yeshivot* engaged with it. By any measure, we are greater *talmidei chachamim* than they were. And yet: *ki havei shalif chad mesana, ata mitra* — Rav Yehuda would merely remove one shoe at the outset of a public fast for rain, and it would begin to rain. He barely had to begin the *ta'anit* and the heavens responded. We, *kamesarina nafshin u'mitzavcha katzavchina ve'leika de'mashgach ban* — we afflict ourselves deeply and cry out to God, and no one pays attention. The *gemara* is making a profound and sobering point: greater *Torah* knowledge does not by itself account for closeness to God. The case of *Choni HaMa'agel* comes to mind — he was not known for *halachic* statements, yet he had an extraordinary relationship with divine response. Who is a *chasid* and who is close to God is not always the same question as who is the greatest *talmid chacham*.

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַמָּאֵי הֲווֹ קָא מָסְרִי נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ אַקְּדוּשַּׁת הַשֵּׁם, אֲנַן לָא מָסְרִינַן נָפְשִׁין אַקְּדוּשַּׁת הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: **The previous** generations were wholly **dedicated** to **the sanctification of God's name,** while **we are not** as **dedicated to the sanctification of God's name.**

Abaye's response ties this discussion back to the theme of *kavod Hashem* that animates the preceding *sugyot*. He says: *kamai havei kamsrin nafshaihu akdushat Hashem* — the early ones would sacrifice and risk themselves for the sake of defending God's honor, for *kiddush Hashem*. We do not do that. That willingness to give up personal security and dignity for *kiddush Hashem* — not learning breadth — is what distinguished them.

### Rav Adda Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה חַזְיַיהּ לְהַהִיא כּוּתִית דַּהֲוָת לְבִישָׁא כַּרְבַּלְתָּא בְּשׁוּקָא. סְבַר דְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא, קָם קַרְעֵיהּ מִינַּהּ. אִגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא דְּכוּתִית הִיא. שַׁיְּימוּהָ בְּאַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ: מָה שְׁמִךְ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מָתוּן. אֲמַר לַהּ, מָתוּן — מָתוּן אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי שַׁוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Typical of the earlier generations' commitment, the Gemara relates: **Like this** incident involving **Rav Adda bar Ahava** who **saw a non-Jewish woman who was wearing a garment made of a forbidden mixture of wool and linen [*karbalta*] in the marketplace.** Since he **thought** that **she was Jewish, he stood and ripped it from her. It was** then **divulged that she was a non-Jew** and he was taken to court due to the shame that he caused her, and **they assessed** the payment for the shame that he caused **her** at **four hundred *zuz*.** Ultimately, Rav Adda **said to her: What is your name? She replied: Matun.** In a play on words, **he said to her: Matum,** her name, plus ***matun*,** the Aramaic word for two hundred, **is worth four hundred *zuz*.**

The example offered is Rav Adda bar Ahava. He saw a certain Samaritan woman wearing a *karbalta* in the marketplace. Assuming she was Jewish, he rose and ripped it off of her. It turned out she was not Jewish, and he was assessed 400 *zuz* — perhaps for the cost of the garment, perhaps also including a payment for the embarrassment, a matter discussed at length in Bava Kamma. He asked her name; she replied *Maton*. He responded: *Maton Maton arba me'ah zuzei shavya* — *Maton* means 200 in Aramaic, so *Maton Maton* cost him 400 *zuz*. This is offered as a paradigm of self-sacrifice for *kiddush Hashem*: he lost money, was shamed, and exposed himself to legal liability — all to protect God's honor against public violation.

### Zealotry Problem

What exactly was the violation Rav Adda was responding to? The most natural contextual reading — given that the preceding *sugya* involved *kilayim* — is that the garment was *sha'atnez*. The principle of *ein eitzah ve'ein tevunah leneged Hashem* — no consideration overrides God's command — would then motivate stripping the garment off even from someone else. Rashi describes the *karbalta* simply as *shem levush chashuv*, an important garment, without specifying *sha'atnez*. The *Mesoret HaShas*, however, cites the *Aruch*, who explains it as a red, seductive garment, thereby framing the concern as *pritzut* — immodesty leading to improper thoughts — which reframes the entire episode in terms of women's dress and male *hirhurim*.

The *Aruch*'s reading is deeply problematic. If the concern is that her garment is provoking illicit thoughts, stripping it off only worsens the situation. Moreover, it frames the episode as a man adjudicating women's dress standards and physically intervening to enforce them — a reading with serious contemporary implications.

Even the *sha'atnez* reading, which is contextually more defensible, carries difficulties. This is being held up as the model of behavior that earned the early sages their miracles. Yet the woman is *beshogeg* — she is not willfully violating. As the earlier discussion in this *sugya* raised: if someone is unknowingly wearing *sha'atnez*, does one even have an obligation to inform them, let alone physically strip their garment? The earlier framework suggested that passive non-performance by a *beshogeg* violator might be grounds for simply letting matters stand. Here Rav Adda goes considerably further — not even informing her and giving her the option to remove it herself, but acting unilaterally. Even setting aside the gender dimension, praising this kind of zealotry presents real difficulties. It resembles an act of *kinah l'Hashem* in the manner of Pinchas — appropriate perhaps in the most extreme and specific circumstances, but a dangerous model to universalize.

It is also important to note the literary and *halachic* context: this is Abaye's response to Rav Pappa's question. It is one sage's answer about why the early generations merited miracles. The *gemara* does not endorse it as a normative ruling, and the voices of *Shas* are multiple. One cannot simply extract this episode and treat it as the *gemara*'s authoritative statement about proper behavior. The connection to the theme of *kavod Hashem* — placing God's honor above one's own financial and social interests — is the central point being made, even if the specific example raises serious independent questions.

## Gender Interaction Cases

### Rav Gidel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב גִּידֵּל הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל וְיָתֵיב אַשַּׁעֲרֵי דִטְבִילָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי טְבִילוּ וְהָכִי טְבִילוּ. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לָא קָא מִסְתְּפֵי מָר מִיֵּצֶר הָרָע? אֲמַר לְהוּ: דָּמְיָין בְּאַפַּאי כִּי קָאקֵי חִיוָּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel was accustomed to go and sit** at **the gates of the** women's **immersion** sites. **He said to them: Immerse yourselves in this way, and immerse yourselves in that way. The Sages said to him: Master,** do you **not fear the evil inclination? He said to them: In my eyes,** they **are comparable to white geese.**

The *gemara* now introduces a series of cases involving the interaction between sages and women, all tied to the *mikveh*. Rav Gidel used to go and sit by the gates of the *tevilah* — the entrance to the *mikveh* — where he would instruct the women: immerse this way, immerse that way. He was evidently offering guidance about proper immersion technique as women entered.

### White Ducks Defense

The rabbis challenged him: *lo ka'mistafi mar mi'yetzer hara?* — are you not afraid of your evil inclination? Speaking to women immediately before they immerse, in such a charged context, surely inflames illicit desire. Rav Gidel replied: *damyan be'appai ki kaki chivri* — they appear to me like white ducks. They simply hold no erotic charge for him whatsoever. This response invites considerable skepticism, and the *gemara* records it without explicit endorsement.

### Rabbi Yochanan Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל וְיָתֵיב אַשַּׁעֲרֵי דִטְבִילָה. אֲמַר: כִּי סָלְקָן בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָתְיָין מִטְּבִילָה, מִסְתַּכְּלָן בִּי, וְנֶהֱוֵי לְהוּ זַרְעָא דְּשַׁפִּירֵי כְּווֹתִי. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: לָא קָא מִסְתְּפֵי מָר מֵעֵינָא בִּישָׁא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מִזַּרְעָא דְּיוֹסֵף קָא אָתֵינָא, דְּלָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא בִּישָׁא, דִּכְתִיב ״בֵּן פּוֹרָת יוֹסֵף בֵּן פּוֹרָת עֲלֵי עָיִן״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״עֲלֵי עָיִן״, אֶלָּא ״עוֹלֵי עָיִן״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yoḥanan was accustomed to go and sit** at **the gates of the** women's **immersion** sites. Rabbi Yoḥanan, who was known for his extraordinary good looks, explained this and **said: When the daughters of Israel emerge from their immersion, they will look at me, and will have children as beautiful as I. The Sages asked him: Master,** do you **not fear the evil eye? He said to them: I descend from the seed of Joseph over whom the evil eye has no dominion, as it is written: "Joseph is a bountiful vine, a bountiful vine on a spring [*alei ayin*]"** (Genesis 49:22). "*Ayin*" can mean both "spring" and "eye." **And Rabbi Abbahu said** a homiletic interpretation: **Do not read** it ***alei ayin*, rather *olei ayin*,** above the eye; they transcend the influence of the evil eye.

Rabbi Yochanan similarly used to sit by the gates of the *mikveh*. He was not speaking to the women; the *gemara* makes clear his purpose was different. His practice was that when the women of Israel came up from the *mikveh* and passed by, they would look at him — *mistaklan bi* — and thereby merit beautiful children like him.

### Conception Belief

This reflects a belief prevalent among the rabbis: that the thoughts and experiences of husband and wife at the moment of conception shape the nature of the children produced. This conviction appears also in the *gemara* in Nedarim, which discusses at length how various improper behaviors or mental preoccupations during marital relations can lead to particular outcomes in children. Rabbi Yochanan, whose exceptional physical beauty was well known, was positioning himself so that the women, having seen him, would be thinking of him — however obliquely — in the context of conception, thus leading their children to be beautiful.

This is somewhat ironic: the *gemara* in Nedarim that discusses this belief also identifies thinking of another person during marital relations as a problem. Rabbi Yochanan seems not to have been troubled by this tension, perhaps because the mental image of him would remain in the background rather than as a focused preoccupation. In any case, the *gemara* does not problematize his practice here on those grounds.

### Ayin Hara Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אֲמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְיִדְגּוּ לָרֹב בְּקֶרֶב הָאָרֶץ״, מָה דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם מַיִם מְכַסִּין עֲלֵיהֶם וְאֵין עַיִן הָרָע שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶם, אַף זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף אֵין עַיִן הָרָע שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** cited a different proof, from Jacob's blessing of Joseph's sons, Ephraim and Menashe: "The angel who redeems me from all evil shall bless the young and in them may my name be recalled, and the name of my fathers, Abraham and Isaac, **and may they multiply** [***veyidgu*] in the midst of the earth"** (Genesis 48:16). *Veyidgu* is related etymologically to the word fish [*dag*]. **Just as the fish in the sea, water covers them and the evil eye has no dominion over them,** so **too the seed of Joseph, the evil eye has no dominion over them.**

The rabbis questioned Rabbi Yochanan on different grounds: *lo ka'mistafei mar mi'ayina bisha?* — are you not afraid of the *ayin hara*? Sitting publicly as a figure of beauty, drawing the admiring gaze of many women, exposes one to the *ayin hara* — the evil eye, which at its core is about envy. The envy generated by conspicuous beauty or success is what gives the *ayin hara* its power.

Rabbi Yochanan replied: *ana mi-zara de-Yosef ka'asina* — I am from the descendants of Yosef, and the descendants of Yosef are not subject to the *ayin hara*. He cites the verse *ben porat Yosef ben porat alei ayin* — a fruitful child is Yosef, fruitful *alei ayin*. Rabbi Abbahu derives from this: do not read *alei ayin* but *olei ayin* — rising above the *ayin hara*. Rav Yossi berebi Chanina cites a different verse: *ve-yidgu la-rov be-kerev ha'aretz* — they shall multiply like fish within the land. Just as fish in the sea are covered by water and the *ayin hara* has no dominion over them, so too the seed of Yosef is beyond the reach of *ayin hara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: עַיִן שֶׁלֹּא רָצְתָה לִזּוֹן מִמַּה שֶּׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ — אֵין עַיִן הָרָע שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Joseph's **eye, which did not seek to feast on that which was not his,** Potiphar's wife, **the evil eye has no dominion over him.**

The *gemara* then offers a second, more ethical derivation: *ayin she-lo ratzah la-zun mi-mah she-eino shelo* — an eye that refused to feed on that which was not his. Yosef refused *eshet Potiphar*, refused to take what was not his — and the *ayin hara*, which operates through envy and covetousness, therefore has no hold over his descendants. One who does not covet is protected from those who covet him.

There is also a striking literary observation worth noting: the verse that establishes Yosef's protection from the *ayin hara* ends with the phrase *banot tzada alei shur* — the women step up to gaze over the wall. The verse itself evokes women gazing upon Yosef, which directly echoes the scene of women looking at Rabbi Yochanan. The *pasuk* being used to explain why Rabbi Yochanan's practice poses no danger is itself a verse about women looking at a beautiful man.

### Contextuality Principle

Beyond the *aggadic* content, these cases carry significant *halachic* weight for the *Rishonim* and *Poskim* on questions of gender interaction. Both Rav Gidel's case — where he speaks with women — and Rabbi Yochanan's case — where he sits to be observed by them — involve interactions that are not intrinsically prohibited but might be problematic depending on whether they lead to *hirhur*, improper thought. What these *gemarot* demonstrate, alongside other cases such as the *gemara* in Ketubot about a sage carrying a bride on his shoulder, is that the permissibility of such interactions is inherently contextual. If the circumstances and the individuals involved are such that no *hirhur* is generated, the activity is not prohibited. This principle becomes important for later *Rishonim* and *Poskim* evaluating a range of situations involving men and women, where the prohibition, if any, is not categorical but depends on what the interaction actually produces in the minds of those involved.

## Women & Mitzvot Obligati…

### Time-Bound Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים וּקְטַנִּים פְּטוּרִין מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין, וְחַיָּיבִין בִּתְפִלָּה וּבִמְזוּזָה וּבְבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA **Women, slaves, and minors,** who have parallel obligations in various mitzvot, **are exempt from the recitation of *Shema* and from phylacteries, but** they **are obligated in** the mitzvot of **prayer, *mezuza*, and Grace after Meals.** The Gemara explains the rationale for these exemptions and obligations.

The *gemara* transitions to a new *mishnah*: *nashim va'avadim u'ketanim peturin mikriat shema* — women, slaves, and minors are exempt from *kriat shema*. This trio — women, non-free male adults, and children — represents those who stand outside the full category of obligated adult free males. The exemption rests on the general principle of *mitzvot asei shehazman grama*: women are exempt from positive time-bound commandments. Slaves carry the same obligations as women. As for minors, while the *gemara* discusses the age of *chinuch* and the father's obligation to train his son in *kriat shema*, the children themselves bear no immediate personal obligation.

### Tefillin Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע: פְּשִׁיטָא! מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת? מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִית בַּהּ מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן. וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִתַּקַּשׁ לִמְזוּזָה — קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA With regard to the mishna's statement that women are exempt from **the recitation of *Shema*,** the Gemara asks: That is **obvious,** as *Shema* is a **time-bound, positive mitzva, and** the halakhic principle is: **Women are exempt from any time-bound, positive mitzva,** i.e., any mitzva whose performance is only in effect at a particular time. *Shema* falls into that category as its recitation is restricted to the morning and the evening. Why then did the mishna need to mention it specifically? **Lest you say: Since** *Shema* **includes** the acceptance of the yoke of **the kingdom of Heaven,** perhaps women are obligated in its recitation despite the fact that it is a time-bound, positive mitzva. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that, nevertheless, women are exempt. We also learned in the mishna that women are exempt **from phylacteries.** The Gemara asks: That too is **obvious** as well. The donning of phylacteries is only in effect at particular times; during the day but not at night, on weekdays but not on Shabbat or Festivals. The Gemara replies: **Lest you say: Since** the mitzva of phylacteries **is juxtaposed** in the Torah **to** the mitzva of ***mezuza*,** as it is written: "And you shall bind them as a sign upon your hands and they shall be frontlets between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8), followed by: "And you shall write them upon the door posts of your house and on your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9), just as women are obligated in the mitzva of *mezuza*, so too they are obligated in the mitzva of phylacteries. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that nevertheless, women are exempt.

The *mishnah* continues: women are also exempt from *tefillin*. The exemption is derived through a *drasha* connecting *tefillin* to *talmud Torah*. The verse *ve'limadetem otam et bneichem* — you shall teach your sons — is read as excluding daughters from the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah*. The verse immediately following, *ukshartam le'ot al yedechem* — and you shall bind them as a sign on your hand — is linked to the preceding *mitzvah*, so that those exempt from *talmud Torah* are similarly exempt from *tefillin*. An alternative basis is that *tefillin* may be time-bound, since they are not worn at night — though this is itself debated.

### Prayer Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיבִין בִּתְפִלָּה. דְּרַחֲמֵי נִינְהוּ. מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״עֶרֶב וָבֹקֶר וְצָהֳרַיִם״, כְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא דָּמֵי — קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna that women, slaves, and children are **obligated in prayer.** The Gemara explains that, although the mitzva of prayer is only in effect at particular times, which would lead to the conclusion that women are exempt, nevertheless, since prayer **is** supplication for **mercy** and women also require divine mercy, they are obligated. However, **lest you say: Since** regarding prayer it is **written: "Evening and morning and afternoon** I pray and cry aloud and He hears my voice" (Psalms 55:18), perhaps prayer should be **considered a time-bound, positive mitzva** and women would be exempt, the mishna **teaches us** that, fundamentally, the mitzva of prayer is not time-bound and, therefore, everyone is obligated.

Despite being exempt from *kriat shema* and *tefillin*, women are obligated in *tefilla*. One might have supposed *tefilla* to be time-bound, given that it is recited at fixed times. The *gemara* explains: *derachamei ninhu* — because prayer is *rachamim*. This is a technically significant word. *Rachamim* denotes not merely compassion as an emotional category but the beseeching of God that wells up from within — the non-formalistic core of prayer. This stands in a certain tension with the principle of *tefilla keneged korbanot*, which grounds prayer in the formal structure of the sacrificial service. The *gemara*'s claim is that at its foundation, prayer is this intensely personal experience of turning to God — universal, atemporal, not reducible to a schedule. Upon this foundation, rabbinic and formalistic structures are subsequently imposed; but the core is *rachamim*, and *rachamim* is not time-bound. Women therefore share equally in this obligation.

### Kriat Shema Exemption

The *gemara* asks: is the exemption from *kriat shema* not obvious? It is a positive time-bound commandment, and women are exempt from such commandments. The *gemara* answers that one might have thought otherwise: *kriat shema* involves the declaration of *malchut shamayim* — the Kingdom of Heaven — so central to Jewish faith that perhaps women should be obligated regardless of the time-bound exemption. The *gemara* also suggests one might have argued that since *shema* is said morning and night, it encompasses the entire day and is not truly time-bound in a meaningful sense. The *gemara* rejects both arguments: the standard exemption applies, and women are not obligated in *kriat shema*.

One might observe the interesting structural contrast here. In the discussion of *tefilla*, the *gemara* argues that what appears time-bound is actually not, because the essential human experience of prayer transcends time. In the discussion of *kriat shema*, a parallel argument — that morning and night together encompass the whole day, making it perpetual rather than time-bound — is apparently entertained and then not pursued, the *gemara* simply affirming the standard exemption on the grounds that *malchut shamayim* does not independently override the time-bound classification.

### Mezuza Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִמְזוּזָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִתַּקַּשׁ לְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה — קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna that women are obligated in the mitzva of ***mezuza*.** The Gemara asks: That too is **obvious.** Why would they be exempt from fulfilling this obligation, it is a positive mitzva that is not time-bound? The Gemara replies: **Lest you say: Since** the mitzva of *mezuza* **is juxtaposed** in the Torah to the mitzva of **Torah study** (Deuteronomy 11:19–20), just as women are exempt from Torah study, so too they are exempt from the mitzva of *mezuza*. Therefore, the mishna explicitly **teaches us** that they are obligated.

Women are obligated in *mezuza*. The *gemara* says this is obvious — so obvious it need not be stated. The potential *hava amina* is that since *tefillin* and *mezuza* are juxtaposed in the Torah, one might derive an exemption for women from *mezuza* parallel to their exemption from *tefillin*, particularly since *tefillin* is linked to *talmud Torah*. The *gemara* rules that this does not follow: *mezuza* is fundamentally about the status of the house, not about the person's obligation in *talmud Torah*, and therefore women are fully obligated.

### Prayer Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״בְּתֵת ה׳ לָכֶם בָּעֶרֶב בָּשָׂר לֶאֱכֹל וְלֶחֶם בַּבֹּקֶר לִשְׂבֹּעַ״, כְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא דָּמֵי — קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna that women are obligated to recite the **Grace after Meals.** The Gemara asks: That too is **obvious.** The Gemara replies: **Lest you say: Since it is written: "When the Lord shall give you meat to eat in the evening and bread in the morning to the full"** (Exodus 16:8), one might conclude that the Torah established fixed times for the meals and, consequently, for the mitzva of Grace after Meals and, therefore, it **is considered a time-bound, positive mitzva,** exempting women from its recitation. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that women are obligated.

Returning to the topic of *tefilla* and its underlying nature, the *gemara*'s reasoning deserves fuller examination. The *hava amina* that women might be exempt from *tefilla* runs as follows: since prayer is prescribed for morning, afternoon, and evening — *erev vavoker vetzahorayim* — it resembles a time-bound *mitzvah* and should be exempt for women. The *gemara* rejects this. Even granting that human beings naturally experience a need to pray at different times of day, the fundamental experience of prayer transcends those temporal anchors. The need to turn to God is not morning's need or evening's need; it is the need of a human being as such. Therefore *tefilla* does not fall within *mitzvot asei shehazman grama*, and women are as obligated as men.

The *halachic* consequence of this analysis is significant, and the *gemara* states it without qualification: women are obligated in *tefilla* — by which the *gemara* means *Shemoneh Esrei*, the *Amidah* — at least twice daily, *Shacharit* and *Mincha*, and perhaps *Maariv* as well, though *Maariv* has its own question of *reshut*. Virtually all *Rishonim* and *Poskim* affirm this. Yet for centuries, the practice departed dramatically from this ruling. Women were often illiterate, did not attend *shul*, and did not recite *Shemoneh Esrei* regularly. Private *tehinot* and personal prayers substituted for formal *davening*.

### Historical Practice Gap

The gap between the clear *halachic* ruling and the historical reality has generated significant discussion among *Poskim*. Some looked at the widespread phenomenon of women not reciting *Shemoneh Esrei* and felt compelled to reinterpret the obligation. Rather than holding women accountable for an obligation they were universally not fulfilling, they proposed a distinction between the biblical foundation of prayer — the personal, existential turning to God that requires no fixed formula — and the rabbinic superstructure of fixed texts twice daily at fixed times. Women are obligated in the former; the latter, being rabbinic and time-structured, might be categorized as *mitzvat asei shehazman grama* from which women are exempt. Saying *Modeh Ani* or reciting any personal prayer would then discharge the biblical obligation.

This position is frankly a post-facto accommodation of practice rather than the straightforward reading of the *gemara*. The simple reading — that women are fully obligated in *Shemoneh Esrei* — is what most *Rishonim* say. In contemporary Modern Orthodox communities, this reading has become increasingly prominent, with the recognition that the historical non-practice was a function of social conditions — illiteracy, confinement to the home, exclusion from communal religious life — rather than *halacha*. It is worth noting that in *Chareidi* communities, where one might least expect this, women do in fact recite *Shacharit* and *Mincha* privately, though not in communal settings. It appears that the *Poskim* who grappled with the phenomenon of women not *davening* were addressing conditions that predate the modern period, and that those conditions no longer describe the reality for large segments of the observant community.

### Birkat HaMazon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת בְּקִדּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם דְּבַר תּוֹרָה. אַמַּאי? מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Women are obligated to** recite the sanctification of the Shabbat day [***kiddush*]by Torah law.** The Gemara asks: **Why?** *Kiddush* is a **time-bound, positive mitzva, and women are exempt** from **all time-bound, positive mitzvot. Abaye said:** Indeed, women are obligated to recite *kiddush* by **rabbinic,** but not by Torah **law.**

The *mishnah* also obligates women in *Birkat HaMazon*. This might seem puzzling: even though *Birkat HaMazon* is not formally time-bound, there is a natural rhythm to eating — *b'erev basar le'echol v'lechem baboker lishboa*, evening for meat and morning for bread — suggesting that eating itself follows temporal patterns, which could render *Birkat HaMazon* quasi-time-bound. The *gemara* rules that this does not make it a *mitzvat asei shehazman grama*; women are obligated. The *gemara* will continue to develop the discussion of *Birkat HaMazon* in the next session, and we will address it then.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

A final textual and conceptual debate between Rashi and Tosafot bears noting. Rashi's *girsa* apparently omitted the word *peshita* — "obviously" — before the *gemara*'s ruling on *tefilla*. According to Rashi, the reason is that since *tefilla* in the form we have it is rabbinic, the entire framework of time-bound exemption for women simply does not apply to rabbinic *mitzvot*. The question of *shehazman grama* is a biblical category; for rabbinic obligations, it is irrelevant. Hence the *gemara* does not say *peshita* — of course they are obligated — because that reasoning would not apply; instead it simply states that they are obligated because of *rachamim*.

Tosafot retains the *girsa* of *peshita* and disagrees. Even for rabbinic *mitzvot*, Tosafot holds, we often pattern the obligations after biblical ones, and the principle of *shehazman grama* — with its attendant exemption for women — can apply to rabbinic *mitzvot* as well. The *gemara*'s ruling that *tefilla* is not time-bound therefore remains meaningful and necessary even for its rabbinic form. This debate between Rashi and Tosafot has implications for how one understands the scope of women's exemptions more broadly, and it turns at least in part on which textual reading one accepts.
