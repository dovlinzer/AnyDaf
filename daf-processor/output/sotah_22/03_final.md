# Sotah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women's Torah Study

### Opening & Tiflus Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹמֵר בֶּן עַזַּאי: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְלַמֵּד אֶת וְכוּ׳. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד אֶת בִּתּוֹ תּוֹרָה — מְלַמְּדָהּ תִּיפְלוּת. תִּיפְלוּת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כְּאִילּוּ לִמְּדָהּ תִּיפְלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: From here **ben Azzai states: A person is obligated to teach** his daughter Torah, so that if she drinks and does not die immediately, she will know that some merit of hers has delayed her punishment. **Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who teaches his daughter Torah is teaching her promiscuity.** The Gemara asks: Could it **enter your mind** to say that teaching one's daughter Torah is actually teaching her **promiscuity? Rather, say:** It is considered **as if he taught her promiscuity.**

Today's *daf* is 22 in *Sotah*. We pick up at the very bottom of 21b, having just worked through a series of fascinating and contested reflections on the question of teaching one's daughter *Torah*. The Gemara raised the concern of *tiflus* — the idea expressed in the famous formulation *kol hamelamed bito Torah ke'ilu limdah tiflus*, that teaching a daughter *Torah* is as if one taught her foolishness or licentiousness. This issue was itself preceded by a broader Gemara discussion about the value of *Talmud Torah* for women, arriving at the question indirectly by asking what kind of *zechus* is *toleh* for women — and exploring women's role as *eina metzuvah ve'osah*. The Gemara then moved into Rabbi Yehoshua's skepticism about women who present themselves as ascetic and uninterested in physical pleasure, and from there into the broader theme of people who do not present themselves as they truly are.

### Historical Context

Before returning to the Gemara, a brief word on the *tiflus* debate is in order. Historically, women were often not educated simply because women were not educated in general society — not necessarily from any religious conviction. But in the modern era, when women's education became both possible and expected, the question of women learning *Torah* and specifically *Torah Sheba'al Peh* became, for some communities, a serious ideological and *halachic* position grounded in the concept of *tiflus*, and for others — including the *Rav* and the Lubavitcher Rebbe — something considered absolutely essential. The real battleground has far more to do with broad sociological and religious attitudes than with the precise interpretation of this single line.

### R. Eliezer's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי "אֲנִי חׇכְמָה" מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמַּעֲמִיד עַצְמוֹ עָרוֹם עֲלֵיהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֲנִי חׇכְמָה שָׁכַנְתִּי עׇרְמָה". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְהַחׇכְמָה מֵאַיִן תִּמָּצֵא".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis** who disagree with him, **what do they do with this** verse: **"I, wisdom,** dwell with cunning [*orma*]"; how do they interpret it? The Gemara responds: **He requires that** verse **for that which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** states, interpreting the word "*orma*" as nakedness rather than cunningness, **as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: The matters of Torah do not endure except in one who stands naked for them, as it is stated: "I, wisdom, dwell with nakedness [*orma*]"** (Proverbs 8:12). This means that wisdom dwells only in one who is prepared to give away all of his possessions for the sake of Torah study. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The matters of Torah do not endure except in one who considers himself as one who does not** exist, **as it is stated: "But wisdom, it can be found in nothingness"** (Job 28:12).

As we saw, *Tosfos* quotes the *Yerushalmi* where Rabbi Eliezer takes not merely a cautionary position about *tiflus* as a loophole in *sotah* proceedings, but an outright harsh stance on the value of women learning *Torah* altogether: *mutav yisrefu divrei Torah ve'al yimsru lenashim* — it is better that the words of *Torah* be burned than transmitted to women. Whatever one makes of the attitude toward women underlying that statement, it reflects a very strong position. And yet the *Gemara* does not come down definitively on one side of the Rabbi Eliezer versus Ben Azzai debate, leaving open the question of how to rule — or even whether this is a matter of *psak* at all, rather than competing positions in *aggadic* literature.

### Rambam's Approach

The *Rambam*'s treatment is instructive here. He rules that a woman *can* learn *Torah* and that she is like *eina metzuvah ve'osah* — she performs an act that is not obligatory upon her — but he affirms *yesh lah s'char*, she receives reward for it. Then, introduced with the formula *aval amru chachamim*, he cites *kol hamelamed bito Torah ke'ilu limdah tiflus*. The phrasing *aval amru chachamim* signals explicitly that the *Rambam* is not presenting this as *halachah*, but rather as a form of religious direction or advice. He is not *paskening* it as a binding ruling.

### Context & Realities

Several distinct considerations shape how one understands Rabbi Eliezer's position. First, there is the question of whether the Gemara establishes any definitive ruling at all. Second, even within Rabbi Eliezer's own statement, how much is it *halachah* and how much shaped by the particular social realities of women in his era? Third — and this is the crucial contemporary dimension — how much do present-day realities demand a fundamentally different response, such that following Rabbi Eliezer's approach today would be not only counterproductive but genuinely destructive? The reason different communities take different positions on women's *Torah* education is not primarily because they interpret this one line differently; it is because they hold different broader views about women's roles, intellectual capacities, and relationship to society. The interpretation of the line follows from those views — not the other way around.

## Deceptive People

### Theme Introduction

Returning now to the Gemara, we are in the midst of a discussion about people who do not present themselves as they truly are. The earlier material included Rabbi Yehoshua's comment about the *isha perushah* — the woman who presents herself as ascetically pious but whose motives are suspect — and the *chassid shoteh*, someone who has absorbed the externals of *chassidus* without grasping its essence, drawn entirely toward outward form rather than inner reality. There was also the *rasha arum*, the deceptive wicked person who appears righteous while scheming in the background.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: זֶה הַמַּכְרִיעַ אֲחֵרִים בְּאוֹרְחוֹתָיו. רַבִּי זְרִיקָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זֶה הַמֵּיקֵל לְעַצְמוֹ וּמַחְמִיר לַאֲחֵרִים. עוּלָּא אָמַר: זֶה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Ḥama** says that **Rav Sheshet says:** A conniving wicked person is **one who persuades others with his ways,** convincing others to mimic his seemingly righteous behavior, in order to hide his faults. **Rabbi Zerika** says that **Rav Huna says:** A conniving wicked person is **one who is lenient** in the *halakha* **for himself and strict for others. Ulla says: This**

The Gemara continues at the bottom of 21b. *Amar Rav Sheshet*: the *rasha arum* is one who *machtiya acherim b'orchotav* — who tips other people toward his path. *Rashi* explains: *reu v'asu kamoni, l'chu b'drachai* — "Look at me, follow my example, walk in my ways." The person presents himself as a model of righteousness, saying "imitate me," while in truth this performance is designed to deflect suspicion from his real conduct. The outward *chassidus* is a cover for inner corruption: *lo nitkaven zeh l'harot atzmo k'chassid b'einei habriyot v'ein tocho k'baro*. Admittedly, this does not fit the Gemara's language perfectly. *Tosfos* offers what feels like a more compelling reading: this person gives advice to others that ultimately serves his own interests rather than theirs. Like an advisor who steers a client toward an investment because it benefits himself — the guidance appears altruistic, but is entirely self-serving. This relates to the prohibition of *lifnei iver*, though the Gemara's concern here is not with cataloguing specific *issurim* but with identifying character types: the sort of person who always presents as deeply generous and other-directed, while every move serves himself.

### Lenient to Self

*Amar Rabbi Zreika amar Rav Huna*: the *rasha arum* is *zeh hameikal le'atzmo u'machmir le'acheirim* — one who is lenient for himself and stringent toward others. This person projects an image of great *frumkeit*; whenever asked a *sha'ila*, he responds with the strictest ruling. But privately, when no one is watching, he applies the most lenient standard to himself. The rigorous public persona is inversely related to his private behavior.

### Ulla's Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁקָּרָא וְשָׁנָה וְלֹא שִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** is one **who read** the Written Torah **and learned** the Mishna **but did not serve Torah scholars** in order to learn the reasoning behind the *halakhot*. Since he believes himself knowledgeable, he issues halakhic rulings, but due to his lack of understanding he rules erroneously and is therefore considered wicked. His cunning is in his public display of knowledge, which misleads others into considering him a true Torah scholar.

*Ulla amar*: such a person is one who *kara ve'shana velo shimish talmidei chachamim* — who learned *Mikra* and *Mishna* but never genuinely attended upon Torah sages.

### Shimush Debate

What is the precise problem with *velo shimish*? *Rashi* explains: *lilmod sevarat haGemara b'ta'amei haMishna* — the person knows all the material but has never engaged in the kind of rabbinic discourse that reveals what it actually means. *She'ein Torato al boryah* — his *Torah* learning lacks depth — *ve'ein lilmod heimenno*, one cannot simply learn from *Mishna* alone, because *sheharei al yedei hata'amim yesh chilluk be'issur ve'heter, be'dinei mamonot leZakot uleChayev, uvetahara letamei uletaher* — only by understanding the reasoning behind rulings does one grasp the distinctions governing *issur ve'heter*, monetary law, and matters of *tahara*. The *Gemara* itself is always asking *mai ta'ama amar hachi* — what is the underlying reason — and tracing *ika beineihum* — the practical differences that flow from competing rationales. Without that conceptual scaffolding, a person cannot actually apply the *halachah* properly.

But why is this person an *arum*, a schemer? The Gemara explains: *vearum hu shemashmi'a kolo ve'shoneh mishnayotav*, *kasevar hu shevaki b'ta'ameihem*, *ve'nohegin bo kavod ke'talmidei chachamim* — he broadcasts his erudition, recites *mishnayos* with great confidence, and people assume he is a genuine *talmid chacham* and defer to him accordingly. We all know people of this type: very erudite, able to cite a thousand sources, impressive in their fluency — but with no real depth of understanding. The Gemara elsewhere captures this as *chamor nosei sefarim*, a donkey carrying books — all the information on its back, none of it genuinely internalized.

One might also suggest that *shimush talmidei chachamim* points to something beyond intellectual comprehension. The purpose of attending upon Torah sages is not only to acquire the conceptual tools for *halachic* reasoning, but to observe how *Torah* actually transforms character — how it shapes the small, daily ways in which one interacts with other people. A person who has only pursued the knowledge, without that investment in personal development, remains in some sense a *rasha arum*: his unrefined character traits persist beneath a surface that looks entirely like that of a *talmid chacham*.

### Xerox & Education

This discussion connects in an interesting way back to the earlier conversation about women's *Torah* education. The Gemara's concern about *tiflus* was in some quarters associated specifically with *Torah Sheba'al Peh* as distinct from *Torah Shebichtav*. In certain *chareidi* schools, the approach has been to teach girls *Mikra* but not *Rashi*, on the grounds that *Torah Sheba'al Peh* is the dangerous knowledge. There was even a period when the "progressive" approach to women studying *Gemara* was to allow it only from a photocopied sheet — as if physical distance from the *Gemara* itself mitigated something. The point is that learning *Mikra* and *Mishna* without *Gemara* and without genuine depth is not only intellectually incomplete; it is, in the Gemara's framework, precisely the profile of someone who appears knowledgeable but cannot actually navigate *halachah*. And part of what makes *Torah Sheba'al Peh* feel threatening to some may be exactly that it is empowering — it is the knowledge that actually governs how we live and act.

### Am Ha'aretz Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: קָרָא וְשָׁנָה וְלֹא שִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה עַם הָאָרֶץ. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: הֲרֵי זֶה בּוּר. רַבִּי יַנַּאי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה כּוּתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to one who **read** the Written Torah **and learned** the Mishna **but did not serve Torah scholars, Rabbi Elazar says: This** person **is an ignoramus. Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: This** person **is a boor. Rabbi Yannai says: This** person **is** comparable to **a Samaritan,** who follows the Written Torah but not the traditions of the Sages.

Having described the person who learned but did not attend upon sages, the Gemara now assigns him a category. *Rabbi Eliezer omer: harei zeh am ha'aretz* — he is like a commoner. This is notable because the *am ha'aretz* designation, while often carrying negative connotations in rabbinic literature, is actually a step back from the *rasha arum*. An *am ha'aretz* is not necessarily malicious — he is simply someone whose *halachic* practice cannot be relied upon and who is outside the rabbinic world. Perhaps he is a fine person who loves learning but lacks intellectual depth, or who has no bad intentions but simply cannot represent himself as more knowledgeable than he is. The *am ha'aretz* is better than the *rasha arum*, though one also cannot trust his *halachic* precision.

### Bur Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה מָגוֹשׁ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: רָטֵין מָגוֹשָׁא וְלָא יָדַע מַאי אָמַר, תָּנֵי תַּנָּא וְלָא יָדַע מַאי אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says: This** person **is** comparable to **a sorcerer [*magosh*],** who uses his knowledge to mislead people. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: It is reasonable to** accept the opinion of **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, as people say** proverbially: **The sorcerer chants and does not know what he is saying;** so too, **the *tanna* teaches** the Mishna **and does not know what he is saying.**

*Rav Nachmani omer: harei zeh bur* — worse still, he is a complete ignoramus. This is surprising, and *Tosfos* himself flags it: *teima, keman sevira lei?* How can someone who has learned *Mikra* and *Mishna* be worse than an *am ha'aretz*? The intuition may be that knowledge without understanding is, in a certain sense, more problematic than simple ignorance. The *am ha'aretz* at least knows what he does not know. The person who has memorized masses of material without grasping it occupies a more confused and dangerous epistemic position — though calling him a *bur*, a word associated with the most complete kind of ignorance, remains a harsh verdict.

### Kuti Analogy

*Rabbi Yannai omer: harei zeh kuti* — a Samaritan. This is a striking escalation. *Rashi* explains that since this person has not attended upon sages, *eino makpid al divrei chachamim lada'atam* — he does not concern himself with rabbinic rulings because he does not even want to know what they are. *Velo shana ela leharot atzmo ketalmid chachamim* — and whatever he has learned was only to present himself as a *talmid chacham*.

But *Rashi*'s comparison to the *Kutim* is worth examining more closely. The *Kutim* were not simply lax observers. They had a fundamentally different authority structure: a different *Sefer Torah*, a different understanding of *Torah*, and a complete non-recognition of rabbinic authority — not a failure to comply with it, but a wholesale rejection of it. So *Rashi*'s point, reflected in the prohibition on eating their bread and wine, is not merely that they were punctilious in the wrong things, but that *keivan shelo shimish chachamim, eino makpid al divrei chachamim lada'atam* — because he has not attended upon sages, he does not recognize rabbinic authority as binding on him at all. Someone who says "I want to know the texts but I don't care what the rabbinic tradition has to say about them" — who effectively becomes an authority unto himself — is more threatening than someone who is simply ignorant. The ignorant person at least knows where the authority lies. This figure rejects the entire system, like a kind of intellectual priesthood-of-the-laity: "I'll learn these texts and I won't care what anyone else has determined." *Eino makpid al divrei chachamim lada'atam* — he doesn't even want to know.

### Magus Category

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov omer: harei zeh magus* — a mage. *Rashi* interprets this as *mechashef ve'ochez et ha'einayim ve'gonev et halevavot* — someone who casts illusions, bewitching people into believing he is brilliant when there is no substance behind it. This is in the spirit of the *rasha arum*: he casts his spell, and people fall under it. One might also note, as was raised in discussion, that in the world of the Gemara a *magus* referred to a Persian priest — a figure who represents an entirely different religious tradition and authority system, not merely a charlatan. On that reading, the *magus* represents yet a further step along the same axis as the *Kuti*: not just a rejection of rabbinic authority, but an allegiance to an altogether foreign system.

### Magus & Tana

The Gemara then clarifies the comparison. *Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: mistavra keRav Acha bar Yaakov*, because people say: *rati magusa* — the magus chants — *velo yada mai amar* — and he has no idea what he is saying. He recites his incantations as pure gibberish, with no comprehension of their content. And similarly, *tani tana velo yada mai amar* — the *tana*, the memorizer of *Tannaitic* material, recites what he has memorized without understanding a word of it. As Saul Lieberman once remarked, what the rabbis wanted in their *tanna'im* — the small-*tav tanna'im*, not the *tanna'im* of the *mishnaic* period — were people with excellent memories and very little creativity. You wanted them to transmit the text accurately; you did not want them to think about it, because then they would introduce their own ideas. The comparison to the *magus*, then, is ultimately about the complete absence of comprehension beneath an imposing performance of erudition: it looks like knowledge, but it is ignorance all the way down.

## Am Ha'aretz

### Shema Criterion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ? כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית בְּבִרְכוֹתֶיהָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ צִיצִית בְּבִגְדוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים וְאֵינוֹ מְגַדְּלָן לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ קוֹרֵא וְשׁוֹנֶה וְלֹא שִׁימֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים — זֶהוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: Who is an ignoramus [*am ha'aretz*]?** It is **anyone who does not recite *Shema*** in the **morning and evening with its blessings;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** It is **anyone who does not don phylacteries. Ben Azzai says:** It is **anyone who does not have ritual fringes on his garment. Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef said:** It is **anyone who has sons and does not raise them to study Torah. *Aḥerim* say: Even if one reads** the Written Torah **and learns** the Mishna **but does not serve Torah scholars, he is an ignoramus.**

The Gemara now asks directly: *tnu rabanan, eizehu am ha'aretz?* — Who is an *am ha'aretz*? The first answer: *kol she'eino korei kri'as shema shacharis ve'arvis* — whoever does not recite *Kri'as Shema* morning and evening.

### Shema Significance

This criterion is tied to a teaching elsewhere in the Gemara — in *Menachos*, if I recall correctly — that someone who recites *Kri'as Shema* morning and evening has fulfilled the verse *lo yamush sefer hatorah hazeh mipicha vehagisa bo yomam valaylah*: if you merely frame your day with *Torah* at its beginning and end, it is as if *Torah* has not departed from your mouth the entire day. The *am ha'aretz* who does not even recite *shema shacharis ve'arvis* is, in the Gemara's view, someone entirely outside the world of *Torah*. One thinks perhaps of the figure of Tevye in Fiddler on the Roof, who has almost certainly never learned a word of *Gemara* in his life, but who understands viscerally that sitting in the *beis knesses* and learning the holy books is what he should aspire to. That self-understanding — that connection, however tenuous — is what distinguishes him from someone who has entirely vacated the world of *Torah*.

### Berakhot Significance

The Gemara adds *u'birchasa* — even the *berachos* of *Shema* matter here, and notes that these are *miderabbanan*. This is significant: the *berachos* surrounding *Kri'as Shema* double as *birchos haTorah* — someone who forgets *birchos haTorah* can rely on the *berachos* of *Kri'as Shema*, because those blessings are fundamentally about God's having chosen us and given us *Torah*. They frame the recitation of *Shema* itself as an act of *Talmud Torah*. Being outside even that minimal rabbinic framing places a person outside the entire enterprise.

### R. Meir Opinion

*Divrei Rabbi Meir* — this is Rabbi Meir's position: the *am ha'aretz* is defined by failure to recite *Shema* morning and evening.

### Tefillin Criterion

*Vachachamim omrim: kol she'eino meni'ach tefillin* — the Sages define the *am ha'aretz* as one who does not put on *tefillin*. This criterion shifts the focus from *Torah* knowledge to a fundamental daily practice: even if you are not deeply learned, at minimum the wearing of *tefillin* marks your participation in the world of *mitzvos*.

### Tefillin History

This is actually quite striking historically. We tend to hold a romanticized view of how universally observant communities were in earlier centuries. Yet we have evidence from the period of the *Rishonim* — I believe it is the *Smag*, though I am not certain — that there were communities where *tefillin* wearing was seriously challenged, and this authority would travel from town to town urging people to begin putting on *tefillin* and affixing *mezuzos* to their doors. The assumption that these practices were universal even in ostensibly observant communities may not be warranted.

### Symbolic Meaning

There is also a symbolic dimension worth noting. The *Shema* itself contains *vehayu le'os al yadecha* — and they shall be a sign upon your arm — and *uletotafos bein einecha* — and frontlets between your eyes. *Tefillin* is, in a sense, the physical embodiment of the *Shema*'s own words: even if one is not engaged in daily *Torah* study, wearing *tefillin* represents a binding of *Torah* to oneself, an external sign of internal connection. Someone who does not even perform that minimal act of symbolic identification has removed himself from the chain entirely.

### Ben Azzai

*Ben Azzai omer: kol she'ein lo tzitzis bevigdo* — whoever does not have *tzitzis* on his garment. This too is part of the *parshiyos* read in *Kri'as Shema*, and similarly functions as an outward symbol of Jewish identity and connection to *Torah*, even apart from the level of one's actual learning.

### R. Yonatan

*Rabbi Yonasan ben Yosef omer: kol sheyesh lo banim ve'eino megadlam leTalmud Torah* — whoever has children and does not raise them toward *Talmud Torah*. We are back here to the *Torah* axis. Significantly, the phrasing is not simply *eino melamdam Torah* — he does not teach them *Torah* — but *eino megadlam leTalmud Torah*, he does not raise them *toward* it. The question is whether one sees *Torah* education as an aspiration worth striving for and investing in, even if one cannot provide it directly. Is it something you see your children as needing? Does it matter to you?

### Acheirim

*Acheirim omrim: afilu korei ve'shoneh velo shimish talmidei chachamim* — even one who has learned *Mikra* and *Mishna* but has not attended upon Torah sages — *zehu am ha'aretz*. This brings us back precisely to where we began in the prior discussion. Knowledge without genuine engagement — without the character development and deep understanding that come from *shimush* — does not lift a person out of the *am ha'aretz* category.

### Severity Scale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא וְלֹא שָׁנָה — הֲרֵי זֶה בּוּר, לֹא קָרָא וְלֹא שָׁנָה — עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: "וְזָרַעְתִּי אֶת בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶת בֵּית יְהוּדָה זֶרַע אָדָם וְזֶרַע בְּהֵמָה".
>
> **Translation:** If one **read** the Written Torah **but did not learn** the Mishna, **he is a boor. With regard to** one who **did not read and did not learn** at all, **the verse states:** "Behold, the days come, says the Lord, **and I will sow the house of Israel and the house of Judah with the seed of man, and with the seed of beast"** (Jeremiah 31:26). One who has not studied at all is comparable to a beast.

The Gemara then establishes an escalating scale of severity. *Kara velo shanah* — one who knows *Mikra* but not *Mishna* — *harei zeh bur*, is a complete ignoramus in all of rabbinic discourse. And *lo kara velo shanah* — one who has learned nothing at all — *alav hakasuv omer*: the verse says *vezarati es beis Yisrael ve'es beis Yehudah zera adam vezera beheimah* — I will plant the house of Israel and the house of Judah with the seed of man and the seed of animals. In context, that verse is a *beracha* about the repopulation of the land. Here, it is deployed with the implicit suggestion that someone entirely outside the world of *Torah* is, in a certain sense, like an animal.

## Women & Talmud

### Question

Where do women fit within all these criteria for *am ha'aretz*? This is an entirely fair and pointed question. The Gemara has just laid out a comprehensive taxonomy — reciting *Shema*, wearing *tefillin*, wearing *tzitzis*, raising children for *Talmud Torah*, pursuing *shimush talmidei chachamim* — and women are exempt from, or simply absent from, most of these categories.

### Connection

This brings us back directly to the opening discussion. Juxtapose *kol hamelamed bito Torah ke'ilu limdah tiflus* with the full weight of everything the Gemara has now said about what it means to be disconnected from *Torah* — the escalating scale from *am ha'aretz* to *bur* to *kuti* to *magus* — and the tension becomes very sharp indeed. Women are told, in one tradition, that too much *Torah* learning is a problem; and yet the framework the Gemara has built places the absence of *Torah* engagement among the most troubling characteristics a person can have.

## Am Ha'aretz…

### Tosfot Complexity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יְרָא אֶת ה׳ בְּנִי וָמֶלֶךְ עִם שׁוֹנִים אַל תִּתְעָרָב״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵלּוּ שֶׁשּׁוֹנִים הֲלָכוֹת. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא — שׁוֹנִין בְּחֵטְא, וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָבַר אָדָם עֲבֵירָה וְשָׁנָה בָּהּ — הוּתְּרָה לוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"My son, fear the Lord and the king; and meddle not with those who are repeating"** (Proverbs 24:21). **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: These are** individuals **who repeatedly learn** the ***halakhot*** but do not know the reasons behind them. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious?** How else could the verse be understood? The Gemara answers: He states this **lest you say** that the verse is referring to individuals who **repeatedly** commit **sins, and** this is **in accordance with** the words of **Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says: Once a person committed a transgression and repeated it,** in his eyes **it became permitted for him.** Since the verse could be interpreted in this manner, Rabbi Yitzḥak **teaches us** that the verse is referring to those who learn without understanding.

*Tosfos* raises a critical point that cannot be passed over: *teima lari de'amar perek Hanizakin* — there is a *baraita* in *Gittin* that defines *am ha'aretz* as whoever does not eat *chullin* in a state of *tahara*, *divrei Rabbi Meir* — and the criteria there are entirely different from what we have here. *Tosfos* resolves this by stating *upirush deharbei inyanei am ha'aretz hein* — *am ha'aretz* means different things in different contexts. Sometimes the term disqualifies a person from serving as a witness; sometimes it concerns trustworthiness regarding *tithes* and *purity*; sometimes it touches on *zun*, *shemitah*, or the acquisition of certain goods. The categories can be contradictory with one another precisely because they are not describing a single unified group.

This point has broader historiographic significance. There has been considerable scholarly work — including a paper by Yair Furstenberg on the status of *am ha'aretz* in *Chazal* — arguing that we should not assume the Rabbis were always referring to a single identifiable sociological group. The term may function more like a concept — a flexible category that plays different roles in different *halachic* and social contexts. There was certainly a time when it referred to a very concrete population. But once it enters the fabric of rabbinic literature as a concept, it begins to operate in more abstract ways, and the contradictions between different *gemarot* reflect that flexibility rather than simple inconsistency. The parallel in our own world might be the whole spectrum of what it means to call someone "not *frum*" — a label that encompasses an enormous range of people and behaviors, none of which are really equivalent.

## Tannaim & Memorization

### Baraita Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַתַּנָּאִים — מְבַלֵּי עוֹלָם. מְבַלֵּי עוֹלָם סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אָמַר רָבִינָא: שֶׁמּוֹרִין הֲלָכָה מִתּוֹךְ מִשְׁנָתָן. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְכִי מְבַלֵּי עוֹלָם הֵן? וַהֲלֹא מְיַישְּׁבֵי עוֹלָם הֵן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלִיכוֹת עוֹלָם לוֹ״! אֶלָּא, שֶׁמּוֹרִין הֲלָכָה מִתּוֹךְ מִשְׁנָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **The *tanna'im*,** who recite the tannaitic sources by rote, **are** individuals **who erode the world.** The Gemara is puzzled by this statement: **Could** it **enter your mind** that they are individuals **who erode the world? Ravina says:** This statement is referring to those **who issue halakhic rulings based** on **their** knowledge of ***mishnayot*. This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehoshua said: Are they** individuals **who erode the world? Aren't they settling the world, as it is stated: "His ways [*halikhot*] are eternal"** (Habakkuk 3:6)? The Sages read the term *halikhot* as *halakhot*, inferring that one who learns *halakhot* attains eternal life. **Rather,** this is referring to those **who issue halakhic rulings based** on **their** knowledge of ***mishnayot*.**

*Atanna'im meval'ei olam* — the *tanna'im*, the memorizers of *Mishna*, destroy the world. One can appreciate the irony: the *tanna* who had to memorize this *baraisa* was memorizing a statement about how memorizers destroy the world.

### Ravina Clarification

*Mavlei olam salka daitach?* — Do they really destroy the world? *Amar Ravina*: of course not. The problem is not memorization itself — we need those people to preserve the *Mishna* with accuracy. The problem is *she'morim halacha mitoch mishnasam* — that they believe they can *pasken halachah* directly from what they have memorized.

### R. Yehoshua

*Tanya nami hachi, amar Rabbi Yehoshua: vechi mavlei olam hein? Vahala miyashvei olam hein* — are they not in fact settlers of the world? — *shenemar halichos olam lo*. The entire Talmudic discussion is built upon the foundation of memorized *mishnayos*. *Kol hashoneh halachos bechol yom* is the indispensable substrate. Without accurate memorization, there is no tradition to discuss.

### Core Issue

*Ela she'morim halacha mitoch mishnasam* — the problem is not memorizing, it is thinking one can issue rulings on its basis. The *tanna* who memorizes is *miyashvei olam*; the one who thinks his memorization qualifies him to *pasken* is *mevallei olam*. The distinction matters enormously, and the Gemara's care in making it reflects a deep awareness of how dangerous a little learning can be when misapplied.

## Mavlei Olam

### Isha Perushah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה פְּרוּשָׁה וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּתוּלָה צַלְיָינִית, וְאַלְמָנָה שׁוֹבָבִית, וְקָטָן שֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מְבַלֵּי עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **an abstinent woman** is among those who erode the world. **The Sages taught: A maiden who prays** constantly, **and a neighborly [*shovavit*] widow** who constantly visits her neighbors, **and a child whose months** of gestation **were not completed,** all **these are** people **who erode the world.**

*Isha perushah* — the woman who presents herself as separated from physical pleasures — is among the *mavlei olam*, the destroyers of the world. The skepticism, as established earlier, is that her presentation of austere piety conceals something else entirely.

### Three Types

The Gemara now adds additional categories: *besulah tzalya-nis* — a praying virgin — *ve'almanah shovevis* — a wandering widow — *ve'katan shelo kalu chodashav* — a minor whose months have not been completed — *harei elu mavlei olam*, these destroy the world. The *besulah tzalya-nis*, according to *Rashi*, is someone always engaged in prayer and devotion, projecting an image of extraordinary piety. The *almanah shovevis* is a widow constantly traveling through the community, visiting everyone. *Rashi* suggests she is a gossip — though as was noted, that does not perfectly fit the model of someone presenting themselves as other than they are; perhaps the better reading is that she visits presenting herself as motivated by care and concern when her real motivation is social engagement or information-gathering. The *katan shelo kalu chodashav* the Gemara will clarify momentarily.

### Pious Virgin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמַדְנוּ יִרְאַת חֵטְא מִבְּתוּלָה, וְקִיבּוּל שָׂכָר מֵאַלְמָנָה. יִרְאַת חֵטְא מִבְּתוּלָה — דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן שַׁמְעַהּ לְהַהִיא בְּתוּלָה דְּנָפְלָה אַאַפַּהּ וְקָאָמְרָה: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בָּרָאתָ גַּן עֵדֶן וּבָרָאתָ גֵּיהִנָּם, בָּרָאתָ צַדִּיקִים וּבָרָאתָ רְשָׁעִים. יְהִי רָצוֹן מִלְּפָנֶיךָ שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּשְׁלוּ בִּי בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: We learned** the meaning of **fear of sin from a maiden, and** the significance of **receiving** divine **reward from a widow.** The meaning of **fear of sin** can be learned **from a maiden, as Rabbi Yoḥanan heard a certain maiden who fell on her face** in prayer, **and she was saying: Master of the Universe, You created the Garden of Eden and You created Gehenna, You created** the **righteous and You created** the **wicked. May it be Your will that men shall not stumble because of me** and consequently go to Gehenna.

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yochanan: lamadnu yiras cheit mibesulah* — we can learn fear of sin from the young woman, *ve'kibul sachar mei'almanah* — and the meaning of receiving reward from the widow. What is the story of the *besulah*? *Rabbi Yochanan shama lah le'hahi besulah de'nafla apei* — Rabbi Yochanan heard this young woman fall on her face in prayer — *ve'amra: ribbono shel olam, baras Gan Eden u'varas Gehennom, baras tzaddikim baras reshaim* — You have created Paradise and *Gehennom*, righteous people and wicked people — *yehi ratzon milfanecha shelo yikashlu bi bnei adam* — may it be Your will that people not stumble through me, that men not sin on my account.

### Problematic Aspects

This is presented as a model of *yiras cheit* — and in one sense it is striking. True *yiras cheit* is not merely fear that *I* will sin, but fear that I might inadvertently cause *others* to sin. One is reminded of the prayer recited before entering the *beis hamidrash* — *shelo yikashlu*, that my colleagues not stumble through anything I say. To be so attuned to the possibility of causing harm, even unintentionally — that is a profound expression of moral seriousness.

At the same time, there is something deeply troubling here. *Yiras cheit* properly begins with concern for one's own conduct. The framing of this prayer — in which a woman's central religious preoccupation is with male desire and male sin, and in which she understands her own body as the primary locus of danger — reflects and reinscribes a way of thinking about women that is, to put it plainly, very deeply problematic. It connects to the entire fabric of *Maseches Sotah* and its preoccupations. These difficulties do not disappear simply because the text presents it as admirable.

### Righteous Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבּוּל שָׂכָר מֵאַלְמָנָה — דְּהָהִיא אַלְמָנָה דַּהֲוַאי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא בְּשִׁיבָבוּתַהּ. כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָת אָתְיָא וּמְצַלָּה בֵּי מִדְרְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לַהּ: בִּתִּי, לֹא בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת בְּשִׁיבָבוּתִיךְ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, וְלֹא שְׂכַר פְּסִיעוֹת יֵשׁ לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The significance of **receiving** divine **reward** can be learned **from a widow, as** there was **a certain widow in whose neighborhood there was a synagogue,** and despite this **every day she went and prayed in the study hall of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to her: My daughter,** is there **not a synagogue in your neighborhood? She said to him: My teacher, don't I attain a reward** for all **the steps** I take while walking to pray in the distant study hall?

The story of the *almanah* who demonstrates *kibul sachar*: there was a certain widow in whose neighborhood there was a synagogue, yet every day she would come and pray in Rabbi Yochanan's *beis hamidrash* rather than in the closer *beis haknesseth*. Rabbi Yochanan said to her: *biti, lo beis haknesseth be'shivvuseich?* — is there not a synagogue in your community? She replied: *rabbi, lo sechar psios yesh li* — do I not receive the reward for the extra walking? The lesson in *kibul sachar*: she extended herself beyond what was required, internalizing a disposition of exerting extra effort for the sake of *mitzvos* even when the effort was not strictly necessary.

### Question Raised

Yet both examples are, on closer examination, somewhat awkward as ideals. The *yiras cheit* of the young woman is entangled with the male gaze and the internalization of responsibility for male desire. The *almanah*'s extra walking, while representing admirable devotion, raises its own questions — *is* it better to pray in a *beis hamidrash* than a *beis haknesseth*? And can it be that this widow is also, perhaps, seeking connection to the world of *Torah*, which may be why she extends herself specifically to come to Rabbi Yochanan's *beis hamidrash* rather than the local synagogue? Perhaps that dimension is part of the point. But regardless, both examples are in some sense extreme, and one must ask whether extremity in this direction is genuinely praiseworthy or instead reflects an excess that has its own distortions. How can the Gemara claim these figures are *mavlei olam* when the examples are so clearly righteous?

### Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר, כְּגוֹן יוֹחָנִי בַּת רְטִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When it is stated** in the *baraita* that a maiden who prays constantly is one who erodes the world, it is referring, **for example,** to **Yoḥani bat Retivi,** who constantly prayed and pretended to be saintly but actually engaged in sorcery.

The Gemara resolves the tension: *ki ka'amar* — the original teaching does not refer to all widows and virgins, but to specific types. *Kegon Yochni bat Retivi* — like this woman Yochni bat Retivi. *Rashi* explains — drawing on what appears to be a *mesorah* not found elsewhere in rabbinic literature — that this woman was a witch who made her *parnassa* as a midwife: she would use incantations to prevent a woman from giving birth, then be called in to help, reverse the incantations, and earn a great reputation. The details of *Rashi*'s source are unclear, and he sometimes provides *aggadic* backdrop that does not appear in other texts, but the point is clear: it is *that* kind of widow who is *mavlei olam*, not every widow who moves through her community.

### Young Scholar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קָטָן שֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו״? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: זֶה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הַמְבַעֵט בְּרַבּוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **a child whose months** of gestation **were not completed? Here,** in Babylonia, **they interpreted this** as alluding to an imperfect, incomplete **Torah scholar who scorns his teachers.**

Back to *katan shelo kalu chodashav*: *hacha targimu* — here in *Bavel* they explained it as *zeh talmid chacham hamiva'eit berabbotav* — a young Torah scholar who is dismissive and disrespectful of his teachers and of those with greater age and authority, overly impressed with his own attainments.

### Gap Year Problem

*Tosfos* quotes a *Yerushalmi*: *Rabbi Chilkiya beshem Rabbi Simon: zeh shehu gadol baTorah shelo befirko, umevazeh et hagedolim mimenu* — someone who is genuinely beyond his years in *Torah* knowledge and therefore feels entitled to denigrate those who are chronologically or institutionally his seniors. This is, regrettably, a recognizable phenomenon: students who return from a year of intensive study abroad suddenly finding themselves, in their own estimation, the world's greatest *talmidei chachamim*, while their community rabbi appears to them as inadequate. The brilliance is real; the judgment is not.

### R. Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר: זֶה תַּלְמִיד שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְהוֹרָאָה וּמוֹרֶה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי רַבִּים חֲלָלִים הִפִּילָה וַעֲצוּמִים כׇּל הֲרוּגֶיהָ״. ״כִּי רַבִּים חֲלָלִים הִפִּילָה״ — זֶה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְהוֹרָאָה וּמוֹרֶה. ״וַעֲצוּמִים כׇּל הֲרוּגֶיהָ״ — זֶה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְהוֹרָאָה וְאֵינוֹ מוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba says: This is a student who has not** yet **attained** the ability **to issue** halakhic **rulings, and** yet **he issues rulings** and is therefore compared to a prematurely born child. This is **as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "For she has cast down many wounded; and a mighty host are all her slain"** (Proverbs 7:26)? **"For she has cast down [*hippila*] many wounded"; this** is referring to **a Torah scholar who has not** yet **attained** the ability **to issue rulings, and** yet **he issues rulings. "And a mighty host [*ve'atzumim*] are all her slain"; this** is referring to **a Torah scholar who has attained** the ability **to issue rulings, but does not issue rulings** and prevents the masses from learning Torah properly.

*Rabbi Abba amar: zeh talmid shelo higia lehora'ah* — not the relationship to others but the effect on the community: a student who has not reached the level of *hora'ah* and yet issues rulings. This is what makes such a person *mavlei olam* in the deepest sense. And the failure here is not necessarily one of knowledge. A person might be genuinely brilliant and yet not ready to *pasken*, because *hora'ah* demands wisdom, understanding of people, judgment about circumstances — not merely *sefarim*.

## Piskening Readiness

### Verse Application

*D'amar Rabbi Abbahu amar Rav Huna amar Rav: mai d'chtiv, ki rabim chalalim hipila* — what is the meaning of the verse "many are those she has caused to fall"? (The verse, drawn from the context of a seductive woman, is now applied to an entirely different situation.) *Zeh talmid chacham shelo higia lehora'ah u'moreh* — this refers to a *talmid chacham* who has not yet reached the level of *hora'ah* and yet rules. *Rashi* notes a play on *hipila* and *nefel* — a premature birth — connecting back to *katan shelo kalu chodashav*: like an infant born before its time, this scholar's rulings are a kind of miscarriage of judgment. He is, in effect, murdering people through his premature *psak*.

### Opposite Case

*Ve'atzumim kol harugeha* — "and great are all those she has slaughtered." *Zeh talmid chacham shehigia lehora'ah ve'eino moreh* — this refers to a *talmid chacham* who has reached the level of *hora'ah* and does not rule. If you are qualified to *pasken* and you withhold that service, you are equally culpable — perhaps more so. You leave the field open to those who are not qualified, you fail the people who need your guidance, and you close your eyes to your own responsibility. *Rashi* reads *atzumim* as a play on *otzem einav* — closing one's eyes. The *Maharsha* reads it differently: *atzum* means great, powerful — you are great, you are fit, and yet you do not act.

### Rav Moshe

This tension is captured with great personal immediacy in Rav Moshe Feinstein's introduction to the *Igros Moshe*. He writes that he was deeply hesitant to *pasken* — who was he to rule, and what if he ruled incorrectly? The Gemara itself warns: *zeh talmid shelo higia lehora'ah u'moreh, rabim chalalim hipila*. Yet Rav Moshe could not simply decline to *pasken*, because the very next line says *ve'atzumim kol harugeha* — if you are *higia lehora'ah ve'eino moreh*, you are equally causing destruction. The obligation is real and cannot be evaded by excessive humility once one has genuinely reached the level of *hora'ah*.

### 40 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה — עַד אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין. אִינִי? וְהָא רַבָּה אוֹרִי! בְּשָׁוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And until when** is it considered too premature for a scholar to issue halakhic rulings? It is **until forty years.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabba issue rulings,** even though he lived for only forty years? The Gemara answers: It is permitted for a scholar who has not studied for so long to issue rulings **when** his knowledge reaches the level of the foremost scholar in his city and **they are equals.**

*Ve'ad kamah* — until what age must one wait? *Arbaim shanah* — forty years. *Aini?* — Is this really so? *Ve'ha Rabbah ori* — Rabbah ruled on *halachic* matters, and he lived only until forty! The Gemara answers: *b'shavi* — only if one is equivalent to the *poskim* of one's generation.

### Rashi vs. Tosfot

There is a notable dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosfos* about how to read this. *Rashi* holds that if you are equivalent to the *poskim* of your generation, you may rule even before forty — you are only prohibited from ruling before forty if you are below their level. *Tosfos* reads it differently: equivalence alone is insufficient — only if you are better than everyone around you may you rule before forty; equivalence requires waiting. The distinction is between adequacy and superiority as the threshold for early *hora'ah*.

### Modern Application

*Tosfos* also clarifies that forty years does not mean forty years of age but forty years of learning — which would imply an even later start for most people. Yet one can understand the number in terms of what it was trying to achieve: *psak* requires not just accumulated knowledge but life experience, judgment, and knowledge of people — not only of *sefarim*. And contemporary *poskim* note that much of the reason for that lengthy formation was the sheer volume of material that had to be internalized from memory. Now that all the material is available in written and printed form, some of those considerations change substantially.

## Seven Types of Prushim

### Machos Perushim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַכּוֹת פְּרוּשִׁין וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, שִׁבְעָה פְּרוּשִׁין הֵן: פָּרוּשׁ שִׁיכְמִי, פָּרוּשׁ נִקְפִּי, פָּרוּשׁ קִיזַּאי, פָּרוּשׁ מְדוּכְיָא, פָּרוּשׁ ״מָה חוֹבָתִי וְאֶעֱשֶׂנָּה״, פָּרוּשׁ מֵאַהֲבָה, פָּרוּשׁ מִיִּרְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It states in the mishna: **And those who injure** themselves out of false **abstinence [*perushin*]** are people who erode the world. **The Sages taught: There are seven** pseudo-**righteous** people who erode the world: The **righteous of Shechem,** the self-**flagellating righteous,** the **bloodletting righteous,** the **pestle**-like **righteous,** the **righteous** who say: Tell me **what my obligation** is **and I will perform it,** those who are **righteous due to love,** and those who are **righteous due to fear.**

*U'makkos perushim* — the smitings of the ascetics — constitute the final category of those who destroy the world. The term *perushim* here carries a double valence: it means both Pharisees and people who separate themselves from ordinary life in the pursuit of piety. *Rashi*'s understanding is that these are people who engage in self-flagellation and the externals of extreme piety while fundamentally missing what righteousness actually requires — and worse, presenting that performance to the world, perhaps even to themselves, as genuine holiness.

### Seven Listed

*Tanu Rabbanan: shiva perushim hein* — there are seven types of false pietists. *Parush Shichmi, Parush Nikpi, Parush Kizai, Parush Meduchya, Parush Machovasi*, *Parush Mei'ahava*, and *Parush Mi'yira*.

### Shichmi Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרוּשׁ שִׁיכְמִי — זֶה הָעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה שְׁכֶם. פָּרוּשׁ נִקְפִּי — זֶה הַמְנַקֵּיף אֶת רַגְלָיו. פָּרוּשׁ קִיזַּאי — אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: זֶה הַמַּקִּיז דָּם לַכְּתָלִים. פָּרוּשׁ מְדוּכְיָא — אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: דִּמְשַׁפַּע כִּי מְדוּכְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The **righteous of Shechem [*shikhmi*]; this is** one **who performs** actions comparable to the **action** of the people of **Shechem,** who agreed to circumcise themselves for personal gain (see Genesis, chapter 34); so too, he behaves righteously only in order to be honored. The self-**flagellating righteous; this is** one **who injures his feet,** as he walks slowly, dragging his feet on the ground in an attempt to appear humble, and injures his feet in the process. The **bloodletting righteous; Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** that **this is** one **who lets blood** by banging his head **against the walls** because he walks with his eyes shut, ostensibly out of modesty. The **pestle**-like **righteous; Rabba bar Sheila says** that this is one **who** walks **bent over like** the **pestle** of a mortar.

*Parush Shichmi: zeh oseh ma'aseh Shechem* — one who performs the deeds of Shechem. *Rashi* explains: the people of Shechem underwent *bris milah* not out of genuine religious conviction but to serve ulterior interests. The act was performed but for entirely the wrong reason. The *Maharsha* adds a sharper dimension: the sequence matters. Shechem first committed a terrible act and then undertook *bris milah* — so the religious performance functions as a cover-up for prior wrongdoing. It is not even simply misguided motivation; it is a kind of religious whitewashing of genuine transgression. The principle *mitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — that improper motivation can eventually lead to proper motivation — does not apply when the religious act is being used to conceal something already done wrong.

### Nikpi Type

*Parush Nikpi: zeh hemenakeif et raglav* — who walks very daintily. *Rashi* says this person minces his steps to project an image of humility and piety. It is entirely performative — a presentation of holiness through gait, with nothing behind it.

### Kizai Type

*Parush Kizai* — *kizai* meaning bloodletting. *Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: zeh hamakiz dam lektalin* — one who bangs into walls and causes himself to bleed. *Rashi*: this person is too scrupulously *frum* to look at a woman and therefore walks into walls. The absurdity is intentional — and it calls to mind the experience of excessive scrupulosity taken to a self-harmful extreme.

### Meduchya Type

*Parush Meduchya: demishapei k'meduchya* — bent over like a pestle. The pestle has a distinctive curved shape. This person walks hunched over to display humility. Again: purely external show, with no genuine substance behind it.

### Machovasi Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרוּשׁ ״מָה חוֹבָתִי וְאֶעֱשֶׂנָּה״. הָא מְעַלְּיוּתָא הִיא! אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״מָה חוֹבָתִי תּוּ וְאֶעֱשֶׂנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **righteous** one who says: Tell me **what my obligation** is **and I will perform it,** the Gemara asks: **Isn't this virtuous** behavior, as he desires to be aware of his obligations? **Rather,** this is referring to one **who says:** Tell me **what further obligations** are incumbent **upon me and I will perform them,** indicating that he fulfills all of his mitzvot perfectly and therefore seeks additional obligations.

*Parush Machovasi*: this person says *mah chovati tu va'esenu* — tell me what my obligation is and I will fulfill it. At first the Gemara protests: *ma'alyusa hi* — that is admirable! Tell me what I need to do and I will do it. But no — the *Gemara* clarifies: the problem is the full phrasing: "I have done everything required of me; *if* there is anything more, tell me and I will do it." The person presents themselves as having already discharged all responsibilities, seeking only to identify any remaining obligations. The posture of prior completion is the problem — the claim to have already done everything, combined with the performance of eagerness to do more.

### Ahava & Yira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרוּשׁ מֵאַהֲבָה, פָּרוּשׁ מִיִּרְאָה. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא לְתַנָּא: לָא תִּיתְּנֵי פָּרוּשׁ מֵאַהֲבָה פָּרוּשׁ מִיִּרְאָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲסוֹק אָדָם בַּתּוֹרָה וּבְמִצְווֹת אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ בָּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also includes in the list of pseudo-righteous people those who are **righteous due to love** and those who are **righteous due to fear,** i.e., one who performs mitzvot due to love of their reward or due to fear of punishment. **Abaye and Rava said to the *tanna*** who transmitted this *baraita*: **Do not teach** in the *baraita*: Those who are **righteous due to love** and those who are **righteous due to fear, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person should always engage in Torah** study **and in** performance of **the mitzvot even if** he does **not** do so **for their own sake, as through** performing them **not for their own sake,** one **comes** to perform them **for their own sake.**

*Parush Mei'ahava* and *Parush Mi'yira* — motivated by love of reward or fear of punishment. These sound problematic but are actually defended. *Amar lei Abaye ve'Rava letana: ha lo tisni* — do not include these in the problematic list. Even if the *ahava* is love of reward and the *yira* is fear of punishment — improper motivations — these are not categorically bad.

*De'amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: le'olam ya'asok adam beTorah u'vemitzvos af al pi shelo lishmah, shemitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — a person should always engage in *Torah* and *mitzvos* even if not *lishmah*, because acting *shelo lishmah* will ultimately lead to *lishmah*. The problem with all the other false pietists was that they were presenting themselves as something other than what they were. Here the motivation is impure, but it is not a deception that harms others — and it carries within itself the seed of genuine transformation.

## Hidden vs. Revealed

### Hidden vs. Revealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דְּמִטַּמְּרָא מִטַּמְּרָא, וּדְמִגַּלְּיָא מִגַּלְּיָא, בֵּי דִינָא רַבָּה לִיתְפְּרַע מֵהָנֵי דְּחָפוּ גּוּנְדֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ יַנַּאי מַלְכָּא לִדְבֵיתֵיהּ: אַל תִּתְיָרְאִי מִן הַפְּרוּשִׁין, וְלֹא מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינָן פְּרוּשִׁין, אֶלָּא מִן הַצְּבוּעִין שֶׁדּוֹמִין לִפְרוּשִׁין, שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂיהֶן כְּמַעֲשֵׂה זִמְרִי וּמְבַקְּשִׁין שָׂכָר כְּפִנְחָס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** That **which is hidden is hidden, and** that **which is revealed is revealed,** but in Heaven everything is known, and **the great court** in Heaven **will exact payment from those who wear the cloak** of the righteous but are in fact unworthy. The Gemara relates: **King Yannai said to his wife** before he died: **Do not be afraid of the Pharisees [*perushin*], and neither** should you fear **from those who are not Pharisees,** i.e., the Sadducees; **rather,** beware **of the hypocrites who appear like Pharisees, as their actions are like the act of** the wicked **Zimri and they request** a **reward like** that of the righteous **Pinehas** (see Numbers, chapter 25).

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: demitamera mitamera, demigalya migalya* — what is hidden stays hidden, what is revealed stays revealed. We have just spent considerable time cataloguing people who present as something they are not. The practical reality is: we know what we know. We cannot peer behind the facade. What is hidden from us remains hidden.

### Divine Justice

*Beis dina rabba le'iparei mehani dechafu gundei* — but the ultimate High Court will ultimately bring justice to those who have wrapped themselves in a *tallis* and presented as pious when they were not. Human judgment is limited; divine judgment is not.

### King Yannai

*Amar lei Yannai Malka l'devasei* — King Yannai, who had caused the deaths of many of the Sages, says to his wife on his deathbed: *al tishari min haPerushim* — do not fear from the Pharisees. And *velo mi'mi she'einam Perushim* — nor from those who are not Pharisees. The genuinely righteous will not harm you even after what was done to them; the genuinely wicked are no threat to you either, as they have no moral standing to act.

### Tzvu'im Defined

*Ela min hatzvu'in* — fear only the *tzvu'im*, the chameleons — *she'domin laPerushim*, who appear to be ascetics and Pharisees, *shema'aseihem ma'aseh Zimri*, but whose deeds are the deeds of Zimri — who violate the most fundamental prohibitions — *umevakshim sachar k'Pinchas*, while demanding reward as if they were Pinchas.

### Final Image

This is the closing image of the *daf*, and it is a powerful one. The Gemara has traveled a long arc: from the question of women's *Torah* education and the concern about those who appear as what they are not, through the *rasha arum* and his many incarnations, through the *am ha'aretz* and *bur* and *kuti* and *magus*, through the *tanna'im* who cannot *pasken* and the scholars who can but will not, through the seven false pietists — and arrives here at the *tzvu'im*: people who act like Zimri and claim the reward of Pinchas. They are, in the Gemara's reckoning, the most dangerous figures of all — not the openly wicked, not the genuinely righteous, but those who commit transgressions while wrapping themselves in the garments of holiness. Against them, human vigilance has limits. Only divine judgment, in the end, can see through.
