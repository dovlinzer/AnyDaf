# Yoma 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Incense Preparation

### Daf 47 Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹצִיאוּ לוֹ אֶת הַכַּף וְאֶת הַמַּחְתָּה, חָפַן מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו וְנָתַן לְתוֹךְ הַכַּף. הַגָּדוֹל לְפִי גׇדְלוֹ, וְהַקָּטָן לְפִי קׇטְנוֹ. וְכָךְ הָיְתָה מִדָּתָהּ. נָטַל אֶת הַמַּחְתָּה בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶת הַכַּף בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **They brought out the spoon and the coal pan** to the High Priest so he may perform the service of the incense. **He scoops his handfuls** from the incense **and places it into the spoon. The** High Priest with **large** hands fills the spoon with incense in an amount **corresponding to the large** size of his hands, **and the** High Priest with **small** hands fills the spoon with incense in an amount **corresponding to the small** size of his hands. **And this was the measure** of the spoon, i.e., it was made to correspond to the size of his hands. **He took the coal pan in his right** hand **and the spoon in his left** hand.

Daf 47 opens a new *perek* and returns us to the *Kohen Gadol*'s service on *Yom Kippur*. The preceding material had dealt with the *Kohen Gadol* clearing the coals from the altar — specifically, moving them aside from the *mizbe'ach* and retrieving the inner coals using a gold fire pan, in a single operation rather than a transfer. Now the focus shifts to the next stage: preparing and conveying the incense into the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

### Coals & Vessels

The *mishnah* opens: *hotziu lo et hakaf ve'et hamachtah* — they brought out for him the *kaf* and the *machtah*. The *kaf* is a large ladle of some kind, and the *machtah* here — notably — does not refer to the fire pan as it did in the preceding *mishnah*, but to a container holding incense. The procedure is as follows: the *Kohen Gadol* scoops the incense from this container with both hands, filling them completely, and then transfers that incense into the ladle. He will ultimately bring into the *Kodesh Kodashim* precisely the amount that fills his two hands — no more, no less. The *mishnah* then states: *hagadol lefi godlo vehakatan lefi kotno* — a large person according to his size, a small person according to his smallness. There is no fixed universal measure for *malo chofnav*; it is defined by each individual's own hands.

### Scooping Practice

The *mishnah* concludes this passage with the phrase *vekakh haya midato* — and this was its measure. What exactly that phrase adds will become the subject of considerable *gemara* discussion later. For now, the basic picture is clear: the *Kohen Gadol* scoops with his own hands, and the amount that fills both hands is transferred into the ladle. The incense remains on his hands in the process, and the ladle then serves as the vessel in which it is conveyed.

### Right & Left Hands

The *mishnah* then states: *natal et hamachtah biyamino ve'et hakaf bismolo* — he takes the fire pan in his right hand and the ladle with the incense in his left. At this point, the *machtah* reverts to its earlier meaning of the fire pan. The use of the same word for two entirely different vessels within a single *mishnah* is, admittedly, striking. He is now prepared to enter the *Kodesh Kodashim*, carrying both items simultaneously.

This raises an important issue. It is well established that *avodah* must be performed with the right hand. One might ask, then, whether carrying the incense into the *Kodesh Kodashim* in the left hand presents a problem. The answer depends partly on whether the act of bringing these items in constitutes an *avodah* in the technical sense. The scooping and transferring are not explicitly described in the *pesukim* and could arguably fall outside the formal category of *avodah*. But the carrying itself — *vehevi mibeit laporochet*, "and he shall bring it inside" — is mentioned explicitly in the verse. And we know from analogous contexts, such as the carrying of blood to the altar, that the conveyance to the site of the *avodah* is itself considered an *avodah*. Moreover, the *Kohen Gadol* changes back into his white vestments specifically to carry these items out — suggesting the act carries formal weight. The use of the left hand for something so significant is therefore genuinely worth noting, and the *gemara* will address it.

## Why Separate Vessel

### Two Machtah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַחְתָּה? תְּנָא לֵיהּ: נָטַל אֶת הַמַּחְתָּה וְעָלָה לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְחוֹתֶה וְיוֹרֵד! הָתָם מַחְתָּה דְגֶחָלִים, וְהָכָא מַחְתָּה דִקְטוֹרֶת, דְּתַנְיָא: הוֹצִיאוּ לוֹ כַּף רֵיקָן מִלִּשְׁכַּת הַכֵּלִים, וּמַחְתָּה גְּדוּשָׁה שֶׁל קְטוֹרֶת מִלִּשְׁכַּת בֵּית אַבְטִינָס.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara expresses surprise at the statement of the mishna. In an earlier mishna, the *tanna* already **taught** that the High Priest must bring the **coal pan: He takes a coal pan and ascends to the top of the altar and rakes and descends.** Why does the *tanna* mention the taking of the coal pan again? The Gemara explains: **There** the mishna deals with the **coal pan of** burning **coals, and here** the mishna is referring to the **coal pan of incense,** which he would later scoop out. **As it was** explicitly **taught** in a *baraita*: **They brought out an empty spoon for him from the chamber of vessels, and a coal pan heaped with incense from the Chamber of the House of Avtinas.**

The *gemara* opens by noting the apparent redundancy: we just read in the previous *mishnah* that the *Kohen Gadol* took the *machtah* and ascended to the top of the *mizbe'ach* to scoop coals — so what does it mean that they now "brought out for him" the *machtah*? He already had it. The *gemara* answers: *hatam machtah degachalim, vehacha machtah dektoret* — there it was a scooper for coals, and here it is an entirely different vessel, a container for incense. These are two distinct objects that happen to share the same name.

### Kaf Source

A *baraita* elaborates: they brought out for him an empty ladle from the chamber of the vessels, and a heaped container of incense from the chamber of Beit Avtinas — the room where the *ketoret* was prepared. The *machtah* of incense here is therefore a completely different vessel from the fire pan, brought from an entirely different location. This is the proof that the *mishnah* is speaking of a distinct *machtah*, namely one that served as the vessel from which the *Kohen Gadol* scooped.

The *gemara* then asks: *kaf Yom Hakippurim lama li?* — why is a special ladle needed at all? The Torah says *melo chofnav veheivi* — fill your hands and bring it. Why not simply carry the incense in your hands?

### Single Entry Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, דְּהֵיכִי נֶעְבֵּיד? נְעַיֵּיל וַהֲדַר נְעַיֵּיל — הֲבָאָה אַחַת אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלָא שְׁתֵּי הֲבָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The High Priest uses a spoon **because it is impossible** to perform the service otherwise. The Gemara elaborates: **As what should we do? Let him bring in** the coal pan **and then bring in** the incense? **The Merciful One states one** act of **bringing** for the coals and the incense, **and not two** acts of **bringing.**

The obvious counter-suggestion is to bring the fire pan in first, set it down inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and then go back out and retrieve the incense. But the *gemara* rejects this: *hava'ah achat amar rachmana velo shtei hava'ot* — the Torah mandates a single act of bringing. The verse reads: take a full fire pan of coals from the altar, and a full two-handful of incense, *veheivi* — and bring them. The singular *veheivi* encompasses both items together. Two separate trips are not permitted.

### Teeth Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁקְלַיהּ לִקְטוֹרֶת בְּחוֹפְנָיו וְנַחֲתַיהּ [לְמַחְתָּה] עֲלַהּ וְלֵיעוּל. כִּי מָטֵי הָתָם הֵיכִי לַעֲבֵיד? נִשְׁקְלַיהּ בְּשִׁינֵּיהּ וְנַחֲתַיהּ לְמַחְתָּה? הַשְׁתָּא לִפְנֵי מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וְדָם אֵין עוֹשִׂין כֵּן, לִפְנֵי מֶלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Instead, **let him take** the **incense by his handfuls and place** the **coal pan on top of** his two handfuls **and enter,** carrying it all in one go. This suggestion is also impractical, as **when he arrives there,** in the Holy of Holies, **what should he do?** How can the High Priest put down the coal pan where it is? **Let him take** the **coal pan in his teeth and lower** it. **Now, before a king of flesh and blood one would not do so,** as it is disrespectful. **All the more so,** one would not act in this manner **before the King of kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He.**

Another possibility: hold the incense in both hands, and rest the fire pan on top. The *gemara* considers this seriously, including the question of whether the bottom of the fire pan could be insulated with leather as the handle was, and whether the handle could be repositioned or shortened to allow for stable carrying. But the *gemara* dismisses even this: *ki mati hatam heichi avid?* — once you arrive at the destination, what do you do? The fire pan is resting on your hands. To lower it to the ground, you would have to use your teeth on the handle. And to that, the *gemara* responds: *hashta lifnei melech basar vadam ein osim ken* — you would not behave this way before a human king; *lifnei melech malchei hamelachim Hakadosh Baruch Hu al achat kama vechama* — how much more so before God.

This is not merely a rhetorical flourish. The *gemara* is making a genuine halakhic point: the dignity of the *avodah* prohibits such contorted, undignified behavior. Since it is impossible to carry it in the hands without resorting to either this or some other improper maneuver, the Torah's instruction to bring *melo chofnav* must be understood as requiring a vessel to convey it. From where do we derive the model for such a vessel? *Avdinan kedashkachan binesi'im* — we look to the *nesi'im*, each of whom brought *kaf achat mele'ah ketoret* — one ladle filled with incense. That is the precedent for the *kaf*.

### Tosafot Long Handle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ — לָא אֶפְשָׁר, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר — עָבְדִינַן כִּדְאַשְׁכְּחַן בִּנְשִׂיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Therefore, it is impossible** for the High Priest to behave in another way, **and since it is impossible** to act in any other manner, **he acts as we find** with regard to the sacrifices of the **princes** during the dedication of the Tabernacle. On that occasion, the princes brought spoons filled with incense: "One golden spoon of ten shekels full of incense" (Numbers 7:14).

*Tosafot* raises a sharp objection. The *gemara* here rules out using one's teeth as undignified. But the *gemara* later discusses a situation where the *Kohen* uses the tips of his fingers — or according to some, his teeth — to draw the small ladle toward him during the transfer of incense inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. How can the *gemara* permit that while rejecting the teeth solution here?

*Tosafot* answers that the later case is fundamentally different in character. There, the ladle is not being carried in the teeth; it is resting on the hand or arm, and the teeth serve merely as an assist to pull it closer. That is a far lesser degree of impropriety than carrying something suspended in the air by one's teeth.

*Tosafot* then raises his own independent difficulty: why not make the fire pan with a very long handle — long enough to rest in the underarm? The *gemara* itself had already noted in an earlier context that the *Yom Kippur* fire pan had an unusually long handle. If the handle were long enough to reach the armpit, the *Kohen* could clamp it under his arm, carry the incense in both hands, and upon arriving at the destination, lower the fire pan to the ground by simply bending forward — no teeth required. *Tosafot* acknowledges that this remains difficult but does not fully resolve it, and notes that in any case the contortions involved would be considerable. The conclusion stands: carrying the incense in both hands while managing the fire pan is impractical in a manner consistent with the dignity of the *avodah*, and therefore a separate vessel — the *kaf* — is necessary.

## Hand Precedence

### Inversion Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶת הַמַּחְתָּה בְּיָמִין וְאֶת הַכַּף בִּשְׂמֹאל. יַצִּיבָא בְּאַרְעָא וְגִיּוֹרָא בִּשְׁמֵי שְׁמַיָּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that the High Priest **took the coal pan in his right** hand **and the spoon in his left** hand. The Gemara questions this arrangement by citing a well-known maxim: **The native is on the ground and the stranger is in the heavens;** i.e., this is the opposite of what one would expect. The main component of the mitzva is the incense, while the coal pan is required only for burning the incense. Consequently, the High priest should carry the spoon, which contains the main component of the service, in his right hand, and the accessory in his left hand.

With the necessity of the *kaf* established, the *gemara* turns to a striking asymmetry in the *mishnah*: *natal et hamachtah biyamino ve'et hakaf bismolo* — the fire pan in the right hand, the ladle with incense in the left. The *gemara* expresses this as an inversion: *ytiva be'ara veger bishmei shamaya?* — the dweller of the earth is below and the stranger above? The incense, which is far more significant in the *Yom Kippur* service, is placed in the left hand, while the coals — instrumental but secondary — are in the right.

### Quantity vs Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ מְרוּבָּה, וְזוֹ מוּעֶטֶת. וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶן שָׁוִין, וּכְמַעֲשֶׂה דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן קִמְחִית, זוֹ — חַמָּה, וְזוֹ — צוֹנֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The service is performed in this manner for reasons of comfort, as **this** coal pan is **greater** in weight, since it holds the coals, **and that** spoon of incense weighs **less. And even when the two of them are equal,** when the spoon contains three *kav* of incense **like the action of Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit,** who could hold several *kav* in his exceptionally large hands, nevertheless, **this** coal pan **is hot** and must be held carefully in the right hand, **and that** spoon of incense **is cold** and is easily carried in the left.

The *gemara* answers: *zu meruba vezu muetat* — one is a large quantity and the other is small. The fire pan contains three *kav* of coals, which is a substantial, heavy load. That is why it goes in the right hand, which provides greater control and stability. And even if one imagines a *Kohen Gadol* with exceptionally large hands such that the volume of incense equals the volume of coals — *va'afilu bizman sheshneihem shavim kema'aseh de'Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimchit* — there is still a further consideration: *zu chama vezu tzonenet*, one is hot and the other is cold. The fire pan, containing live coals, requires careful handling, and that is another reason it is carried in the dominant right hand. *Rashi* additionally notes that *meruba* implies *kveida* — heavier — which reinforces the practical reasoning.

It is worth pausing to note that, even granting these practical considerations, the *halachic* asymmetry is real: the incense, the more spiritually central element of the entire *Yom Kippur avodah*, is being carried in the left hand into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. But as noted, the Torah's own formulation of *melo chofnav* already implicitly involves the left hand in conveying the incense, so the necessity is inherent in the nature of the service.

### R. Yishmael ben Kimchit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן קִמְחִית שֶׁהָיָה חוֹפֵן אַרְבַּעַת קַבִּים בִּמְלוֹא חׇפְנָיו, וְאוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים [זֶרֶד] זָרְדוּ, וְזֶרֶד אִימָּא עָלָה לַגָּג. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּעַרְסָן, וְכִדְרַבָּה בַּר יוֹנָתָן. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יוֹנָתָן אָמַר רַבִּי יְחִיאֵל: עַרְסָן יָפֶה לַחוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara has mentioned Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit, it discusses him further. **They said about Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit** that his hands were so large **that he would scoop** up **four *kav*,** which he would hold **by his handfuls, and say: All the women selected** the best they could for their children, **but the selection of my mother rose to the roof,** i.e., my mother chose the best. Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit is referring to himself, as he matured to a great height and stature. **Some say** he was referring to his mother's selection **of flour, in accordance with** the statement of **Rabba bar Yonatan. As Rabba bar Yonatan said** that **Rabbi Yeḥiel said: Flour is beneficial** and healthy **for the sick.** Since his mother ate this flour when she was pregnant with him, her son grew heartily.

The reference to Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimchit — the *Kohen Gadol* with exceptionally large hands, whose *malo chofnav* equaled the entire volume of coals — opens a narrative digression about him and his family.

## Kimchit's Sons

### R. Yishmael Prodigy

The *gemara* reports: *amru alav al Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimchit shehaya chofen arba'at kavim bimlo chofnav* — they said of him that the fill of his two hands held four *kav*, exceeding even the three *kav* of coals. He would say: *kol hanashim zardu, vezered ima ala legag* — all women have *zardu*, but my mother's *zered* rose to the roof, meaning her achievement surpassed them all. The word *zered* is ambiguous, and the *gemara* recognizes this, offering two interpretations.

### Zerad Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, וְכִדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ. דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וַתַּזְרֵנִי [חַיִל] לַמִּלְחָמָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״הַמְאַזְּרֵנִי חַיִל״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! זֵרִיתַנִי וְזֵרַזְתַּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** this reference to selection is a euphemism for semen, **in accordance with** a statement of **Rabbi Abbahu, as Rabbi Abbahu raised a contradiction** between two verses. **It is written: "For You have girded me** [***vatazreni***] **with strength for battle"** (II Samuel 22:40), **and it is written** in a parallel verse: **"Who girds me** [***hame'azreni*] with strength"** (Psalms 18:33). What is the difference between these two expressions? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe: You selected me [*zeritani*]** with the best drop of semen that my mother absorbed, with which, **You fashioned me [*zeraztani*]** within her. This accounts for the variant forms of this expression.

The first interpretation: *ika de'amri be'arsa* — some say it refers to *arsa*, a type of coarse bread or dough. This reading is supported by a tradition cited in the name of Rabbi Chiyya: *arsa yafeh l'choleh* — *arsa* is beneficial for a sick person. Since pregnant women are often in a weakened physical state, *Rashi* explains that Kimchit's exceptional attentiveness to her diet and health during pregnancy is what produced such a large and robust son. On this reading, *zered* specifically evokes this type of nutritious food, and the phrase means: all women have done this, but my mother did it better than all of them — and that is why I turned out with such large hands.

The second interpretation: *ve'ika de'amri be'shichvat zered* — some say it refers to semen. This reading is supported by a *derasha* of Rabbi Abbahu, who notes a tension between two scriptural formulations: one verse uses *va'tezreni chayil la'milchama* (with the root *zayin-resh-dalet*), and another uses *ha'me'azreni* (with an *aleph*). Rabbi Abbahu understands the form without the *aleph* as a reference to winnowing — *zoreh* — and interprets David's statement as: You, God, have winnowed me, ensuring I was conceived from the choicest seed. *Rashi* also notes that the word *zered* is associated with something long and thin, which carries an additional allusive connection to choice sperm. On this reading, Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimchit is attributing his exceptional physical constitution not to his mother's diet but to the quality of the seed from which he was conceived — with his mother credited somehow for that as well.

It is notable throughout that the attribution is to the mother rather than the father, a point that will become more significant as the narrative continues.

### Contamination Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן קִמְחִית: פַּעַם אַחַת סִיפֵּר דְּבָרִים עִם עֲרָבִי אֶחָד בַּשּׁוּק, וְנִתְּזָה צִינּוֹרָא מִפִּיו עַל בְּגָדָיו. וְנִכְנַס יְשֵׁבָב אָחִיו וְשִׁמֵּשׁ תַּחְתָּיו. וְרָאֲתָה אִמָּן שְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים בְּיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit. **They said about Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit: Once he was talking to a certain Arab in the market** on Yom Kippur, **and a drop** of saliva **sprayed from** the Arab's **mouth onto the clothes** of Rabbi Yishmael, who was the High Priest at the time. This spittle rendered him ritually impure by rabbinic law, like the ritual impurity of a *zav*, preventing him from serving in the Temple. **And his brother Yeshevav entered and served** as High Priest on that day **in his stead. And,** consequently, **their mother saw two** of her sons serving as **High Priests on a single day.**

The *gemara* then relates: *pa'am achat siper devarim im Aravi echad ba'shuk, venitza tzinora mipiv al begadav* — once he was conversing with a certain Arab in the marketplace, and a stream of spittle from the Arab's mouth landed on his clothing. The halakhic background is that *Chazal* assigned all non-Jewish men the status of a *zav* and non-Jewish women the status of a *zavah*, making their spittle an *av ha'tum'ah*. This rendered him *tamei*, and he could not immediately immerse and become *tahor*. Consequently, *ve'nichnas Yeshevav achiv ve'shimesh tachtav* — his brother Yeshevav entered and performed the service in his stead. *Ve'ra'atah imman shenei Kohanim Gedolim beyom echad* — and his mother saw two *Kohanim Gedolim* serve on the same day.

*Rashi* understands this as referring to *Yom Kippur*. The *Maharsha* raises the difficulty that it is hard to imagine the *Kohen Gadol* was conversing with an Arab in the marketplace during *Yom Kippur* — he would have been separated from people in the *Beit Hamikdash* for a week beforehand. The *Maharsha* therefore suggests it may have been *Erev Yom Kippur*, though the significance of having his brother serve would presumably then be connected to *Yom Kippur* itself. *Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi* to the effect that this was a king or great personage, which would explain why the *Kohen Gadol* felt obligated to go out and speak with him.

### Seven Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן קִמְחִית: פַּעַם אַחַת יָצָא וְסִיפֵּר עִם הֶגְמוֹן אֶחָד בַּשּׁוּק, וְנִתְּזָה צִינּוֹרָא מִפִּיו עַל בְּגָדָיו. וְנִכְנַס יוֹסֵף (עִם) אָחִיו וְשִׁמֵּשׁ תַּחְתָּיו, וְרָאֲתָה אִמָּן שְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים בְּיוֹם אֶחָד. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁבְעָה בָּנִים הָיוּ לָהּ לְקִמְחִית, וְכוּלָּן שִׁמְּשׁוּ בִּכְהוּנָּה גְּדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And they further said about Rabbi Yishmael ben Kimḥit: Once he went out and talked to a certain officer [*hegmon*] in the market, and a drop of saliva sprayed from** the officer's **mouth onto the clothes** of Rabbi Yishmael, **and his brother Yosef entered and served** as High Priest **in his stead. And,** again, **their mother saw two** of her sons serving as **High Priests on a single day. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Kimḥit had seven sons, and they all served** in the office **of the High Priesthood,** as High Priests or as his substitute.

A second incident is then recorded: *pa'am achas yatza ve'siper im adon echad ba'shuk, ve'nitztzinah mipiv al begadav* — again he went out to speak with a great personage, again spittle landed on his clothing, again he became *tamei*. This time his brother Yosef entered and served in his stead. *Ve'ra'atah imman shenei Kohanim Gedolim beyom echad* — and again his mother saw two of her sons serve as *Kohen Gadol* on the same day. The *gemara* goes on to cite the *baraita*: *shiva banim hayu lah l'Kimchis ve'chulam shimsu bikshuna gedolah* — she had seven sons, and all of them served as *Kohen Gadol*. The *Maharsha* notes that the list of *Kohanim Gedolim* mentioned earlier in the tractate attributes these figures to the mother's name rather than the father's, and suggests that the sons may have had different fathers — making the common thread the mother, Kimchit herself.

### Kimchit's Virtue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהּ חֲכָמִים: מָה עָשִׂית שֶׁזָּכִית לְכָךְ? אָמְרָה לָהֶם: מִיָּמַי לֹא רָאוּ קוֹרוֹת בֵּיתִי קַלְעֵי שְׂעָרִי. אָמְרוּ לָהּ: הַרְבֵּה עָשׂוּ כֵּן וְלֹא הוֹעִילוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages asked her: **What** good deeds **did you perform to merit this? She said to them: In all my days, the beams of my house never saw the braids of my hair,** as she was extremely modest and was strict about covering her hair even inside her own house. **They said to her: Many** women **did so and did not succeed** to such a degree; you must have been granted a special gift from God.

The sages ask her: *mah asit she'zachit le'kach?* — what did you do to merit this? She replied: *miyamay lo ra'u koros beisi kil'ei sa'ari* — in all my days, the beams of my house never saw my hair, meaning she maintained her hair covering even inside her own home at all times. The sages respond: *harbeh asu kein ve'lo ho'ilu* — many others did the same and it did not benefit them in the same way.

This *gemara* is frequently cited in discussions of *tzniut* and hair covering, held up as the ideal of modesty carried to its fullest expression. At the same time, the sages' response introduces genuine complexity. Is their reply meant to affirm her practice as praiseworthy while noting that the reward is not automatic — that *Hashem* determines the appropriate reward for each person, as *HaMeiri* suggests? Or is it a more skeptical statement, implying that her practice alone cannot be identified as the cause of her unique merit? Both readings are available, and the tension between them is part of what makes this passage so frequently discussed.

## Kometz Mincha

### Hands Not Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּקוּמְצוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂה מִדָּה לַקּוֹמֶץ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה מִדָּה לַחֲפִינָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: "And he shall take up from it** his handful,** of the fine flour of the meal-offering, and of its oil" (Leviticus 6:8). This verse teaches **that he should not measure an amount for the handful** of a meal-offering with a utensil, but he should separate it directly by hand. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether the High Priest may **measure an amount for the handful** of incense? Must the incense also be scooped by hand?

The *gemara* now transitions to a parallel case: the *kometz* taken from a standard *mincha*. A *baraita* teaches: *shelo yaaseh midah le'kometz* — one may not pre-measure the size of his handful and then use a vessel of that size to scoop the *kometz* from the *mincha*. The verse states *veherim mimenu bekumtzo* — he shall lift from it *with his handful*, meaning the physical act of scooping must be performed with the hand itself. Pre-calibrating a vessel to match hand-size and then using that vessel would not fulfill the requirement.

This raises a parallel question: *mahu sheyaaseh midah lechafina?* — does the same apply to the double-handful of incense? Must the *Kohen Gadol* perform a physical act of scooping, or is it sufficient that he obtains the correct volume by other means? On one hand, the *kometz* verse says *bekumtzo*, using the hand explicitly. The *chafina* verse, however, says *melo chofnav* — the fill of his hands — which might indicate only a quantity rather than an action.

### Measure Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הוּא דִכְתִיב ״בְּקוּמְצוֹ״: אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״בְּחׇפְנָיו״, אֶלָּא: ״מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו קְטוֹרֶת סַמִּים דַּקָּה״, לָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא יָלֵיף ״מְלֹא״ ״מְלֹא״ מִקּוּמְצוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two sides of the dilemma: Perhaps **there,** in the case of the handful of a meal-offering, **it** is different, **as it is written "in his handful,"** which indicates that he must use his hand rather than a vessel. **However here, where it is not written: In his hands, but "and his hands full of sweet incense beaten small"** (Leviticus 16:12), perhaps this teaches that the handfuls of incense need **not** be taken directly by hand, and the High Priest may use a vessel to scoop out the required amount. **Or perhaps** this *halakha* **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between **"full"** and **"full," from "his handful,"** which is stated regarding the meal-offering. If this verbal analogy is accepted, the High Priest may likewise scoop the incense only by hand.

On the other hand, one could construct a *gezeirah shavah*: both contexts use the word *melo* — *melo kumtzo* and *melo chofnav* — and from the juxtaposition to *kumtzo* (where the physical act is required), we might infer that the same physical scooping is required for *chafina* as well. The question remains unresolved.

The *gemara* then attempts to resolve it from the *mishnah*'s phrase *vekakh haya midatah* — and this was its measure. Could this mean that the measure alone determines the requirement, validating even a pre-calibrated vessel without a physical scooping? The *gemara* entertains this reading but rejects it: the phrase can equally be read as meaning *vekakh haya chozer vecho'fenah mibifnim* — and in the same manner he would scoop it out again inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. On that reading, the *mishnah* is not commenting on whether a vessel suffices; it is describing a second act of scooping that occurs once the *Kohen Gadol* arrives inside.

The *gemara* ultimately refuses to pin down either answer from this phrase, since each proposed reading of the *mishnah* can be countered by attributing the phrase to the other meaning. The phrase *vekakh haya midatah* thus yields three possible readings: it describes the required exact quantity; it permits using a pre-calibrated vessel without physical scooping; or it describes a second scooping performed inside. The question of whether a vessel may be used in lieu of the hands remains formally unresolved.

### Scooping Technique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מְלֹא קוּמְצוֹ״. יָכוֹל מְבוֹרָץ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּקוּמְצוֹ״. אִי בְּקוּמְצוֹ, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מְלֹא קוּמְצוֹ״, כִּדְקָמְצִי אִינָשֵׁי. הָא כֵּיצַד — חוֹפֶה שְׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו עַל פִּיסַּת יָדוֹ וְקוֹמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: "His handfuls"** (Leviticus 2:2). I **might** have thought it should **be overflowing** from the handful, and therefore **the verse states: "His handful"** (Leviticus 6:8), which indicates a precise amount. **If** the *halakha* is based solely on the phrase **"his handful,"** I **might** have thought the priest may pinch a small amount **even with** just **his fingertips,** not with his entire finger. Therefore, **the verse states: "His handfuls"** (Leviticus 2:2), meaning **as people** usually **take a handful,** i.e., with their whole hand. **How** should he perform this service? **He scoops** by closing **his three fingers over the palm of his hand, and takes a handful** from the flour of the meal-offering.

A separate *baraita* describes the mechanics of the *kometz*: *melo kumtzo* — the full handful. One might think this means overflowing; therefore the verse also says *bekumtzo*, indicating a precise measure. One might then think it means just a little bit held at the tips of the fingers; therefore *melo kumtzo* indicates it must be full. The practical middle ground: *kekamtzi inshi*, the natural way people take a handful. The technique is: *chofeh shalosh etzbe'otav al pisat yado* — fold the three middle fingers down onto the palm, and scoop up into them.

### Hardest Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַחֲבַת וּבְמַרְחֶשֶׁת, מוֹחֵק בְּגוּדָלוֹ מִלְּמַעְלָה, וּבְאֶצְבָּעוֹ קְטַנָּה מִלְּמַטָּה. וְזוֹ הִיא עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. זוֹ הִיא וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא אִיכָּא מְלִיקָה, וְהָא אִיכָּא חֲפִינָה! אֶלָּא: זוֹ הִיא מֵעֲבוֹדוֹת קָשׁוֹת שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And in** a case of a meal-offering prepared in **a pan and** with regard **to** a meal-offering prepared in **a deep container,** when the flour was fried before being scooped and was therefore hard, the priest **smooths with his thumb** any flour that was overflowing **above** his handful, **and with his little finger** he smooths the flour that was pushing out **below. And this** precise taking of a handful of a meal-offering **is** the most **difficult** sacrificial **rite in the Temple.** The Gemara asks: **This one** is the hardest rite, **and no other? But there is pinching,** which is also considered extremely difficult, **and there is taking** the **handful** of incense, another complex rite. **Rather, this** taking of a handful of a meal-offering **is** one **of** the most **difficult rites in the Temple.**

For a baked *mincha* — such as one prepared in a *machvat* or *marcheshet*, a flat pan or deep pan — the task is harder, since the offering is not fine flour but a more solid substance that has been broken into halves, quarters, and eighths. There one must use *mogudlo milmala uve'etzba ketana milmata* — the thumb on top and the small finger on the bottom — to break off and smooth out whatever protrudes. The *gemara* identifies this as *zo hi avodah kasha she'bamikdash* — this is the hardest *avodah* in the *Mikdash*. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: is it truly the hardest? There is also the *melikah* of birds, which involves cutting the neck with the thumbnail in a technically demanding way. And as we will see, there is also the *chafina* — the scooping of the incense inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, where the *Kohen Gadol* must re-scoop every last bit of *ketoret* from the ladle back into his hands with nothing left behind. The *gemara* revises: these are all among *avodot kashos she'bamikdash* — the hardest *avodot* of the *Mikdash*, all sharing that distinction.

## Stuff Between Fingers

### RYB Uza Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, בָּעֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן עוּזָּאָה: בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם שֶׁל מְלֹא קוּמְצוֹ, מַהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּגַוַּאי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — דְּוַדַּאי קוֹמֶץ הוּא. דְּבָרַאי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — דְּוַדַּאי שִׁירַיִם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Uza'a raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* with regard to the flour that remains **between** the fingers **of his handfuls?** Is this flour considered to be part of his handful, or is it considered part of the remainder of the meal-offering? The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Pappa said** that with regard to the flour **that is on the inside,** toward the hand, **do not raise the dilemma, as it** is **certainly** part of **the handful.** Likewise, with regard to the flour **that is on the outside, do not raise the dilemma, as it** is **certainly** part of the **remainder** of the flour, like the rest of the meal-offering.

The *gemara* now addresses a question posed by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Uza: *bein ha'beinayim shemelo kumtzo mahu?* — what is the status of the material that lies between the fingers when one takes the *kometz*? There is the flour that lies squarely under the fingers — clearly inside the *kometz*. There is the flour visibly protruding outside on the outer surfaces of the fist — clearly the *shirayim*, the remainder of the *mincha* that is eaten. But what about the material that is exactly between the fingers — neither fully inside nor fully outside?

### Safek Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי מַאי. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ [בֶּן] עוּזָּאָה: בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם סָפֵק נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **let the dilemma be raised** with regard to the flour **between** the inside and the outside. **What is** the status of this flour? **Rav Yoḥanan said: Yehoshua ben Uza'a then resolved** his own dilemma: The flour that is **between is doubtful,** and should be treated as questionable in status.

*Rav Papa* clarifies the scope of the question: the inside material is unquestionably *kometz*, the outside material is unquestionably *shirayim* — neither presents a *she'eilah*. The genuine question is only about *de'vein beini*, the material in between. *Rav Yochanan* then reports: *hadar pashatah Yehoshua ben Uza* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Uza ultimately reached a resolution. His conclusion: *bein ha'beinayim safek ninhu* — it is a *safek*.

### Tosafot Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לְחוֹדֵיהּ תְּחִלָּה, וַהֲדַר בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם. דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם בְּרֵישָׁא — דִּילְמָא שִׁירַיִם נִינְהוּ וְהָווּ לְהוּ שִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה [לְהַקְטָרָה], וְאָמַר מָר: שִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה לְהַקְטָרָה אֵין מַקְטִירִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How does one perform** the Temple service in this doubtful case? **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** The priest **first burns the handful by itself, and then** he burns the flour that was **between. For if you say** that he should **first** burn the flour that was **between, perhaps they are remainders, and they are** therefore considered like **remainders that became reduced between taking the handful and burning,** as the priest has taken flour from the remainders of the meal-offering. **And the Master said:** With regard to **remainders that became reduced between taking the handful and the burning, one** may **not burn the handful on their** account.

At first glance this seems unsatisfying — he started by asking what it is, and concluded by saying he doesn't know. But *Tosafot* offers a penetrating analysis. He says: *lo yadana mai ka'amar hadar pashatah* — I don't know what "he resolved" means here, since originally it was also a *safek*. He answers: originally, the questioner thought there might be a definitive answer one way or the other — either the material belongs categorically to the *kometz* or categorically to the *shirayim*. The resolution was the recognition that it is not merely a matter of ignorance — *safek* here does not mean "I cannot figure out which category it belongs to." Rather, the material is by definition in an indeterminate state; it genuinely has no resolution as one category or the other.

*Tosafot*'s reading aligns with the famous analysis of *bein hashmashut*: the period of dusk after sunset. The obvious understanding of *safek* there is that we do not know the precise moment of transition from day to night. But Rav Soloveitchik develops the idea that *bein hashmashut* is not merely a moment we cannot identify — it is a halakhically distinct zone that is neither day nor night, ontologically indeterminate. Similarly, the material between the fingers is not in some category we have failed to identify; it occupies a genuine intermediate status, neither *kometz* nor *shirayim* as a matter of definition. The category *safek* here is thus an identity, not an epistemic limitation.

### Two-Stage Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי אִיקְּרִי כָּאן: כֹּל שֶׁמִּמֶּנּוּ לָאִשִּׁים — הֲרֵי הוּא בְּבַל תַּקְטִירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that there is uncertainty as to whether or not this flour is considered part of the remainder of the meal-offering, **now too,** if the priest burns the in-between flour after burning the handful, one should **apply here** the principle: **Whatever is partly** burned **in** the **fire** of the altar **is subject to** the prohibition **do not burn.** This principle states that if part of an item is burned on the altar, one who burns any other part of it, which is not designated for burning, has violated a prohibition. In this case, if the in-between flour is considered part of the remainder of the meal-offering, the priest who burns it transgresses that prohibition.

Given this indeterminate status, the *gemara* asks: *heichi avid* — what should one practically do? *Rabbi Chanina* answers: *maktir kometz lechudeih techila, vehadar bein ha'beinayim* — first burn the definitively identifiable *kometz* alone, and only afterward release and burn the material between the fingers. Why this two-stage process?

If the material between the fingers were burned first, or even simultaneously with the *kometz*, a serious problem arises: it might be *shirayim*. And if *shirayim* are lost before the *kometz* is burned, the *halacha* is that the *kometz* can no longer be offered — *shirayim shechosru bein kemitzah lehaktara, ein maktirin aleihem et hakometz*. *Tosafot* notes a debate about whether this invalidates the entire *mincha* or merely prevents eating the *shirayim*, but either way it is a problem. Therefore, the proper procedure is to burn the definitively identified *kometz* first while the full amount of *shirayim* is intact, and then release the material between the fingers afterward.

### Eliezer Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: דְּמַקְטֵיר לֵיהּ לְשׁוּם עֵצִים, כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ״ אִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה לְשׁוּם עֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, said that** the priest does not burn the flour as part of the meal-offering but **burns it for the purpose of wood.** In other words, he does not burn the flour as part of an offering but merely as fuel for the altar, which is permitted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** "But they shall not come up **for a sweet savor** on the altar" (Leviticus 2:12). This verse indicates that **you** may **not bring up** leaven and honey as a "sweet savor," an offering. **However, you** may **bring up** leaven and honey and other materials **for the purpose of wood.**

But this raises the reverse problem: once you burn the material between the fingers on the *mizbe'ach*, you may be burning *shirayim* — and there is a biblical prohibition against doing so. The verse in *Vayikra* states *lo taktiru mimenu isheh la'Hashem*, which by a *derasha* — *kadshi mimenu le'ishim* — is interpreted to prohibit burning the *shirayim* on the *mizbe'ach*: anything from which the portion that "goes to the fire" has already been separated, the remainder must not be offered up.

The *gemara* answers, citing *Rav Yehuda berei deRav Shmuel bar Papa*: one burns the material between the fingers *leshum etzim* — designating it as firewood rather than as an offering. This draws on the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who rules in an analogous case — where limbs of a *chatat* became intermingled with limbs of an *olah* — that one may burn them all on the *mizbe'ach*. His reasoning: the prohibition is *lereyach nicho'ach ee ata ma'aleh* — the verse specifies *al hamizbe'ach lo ya'alu lereyach nicho'ach*, you may not bring this material up as a pleasing offering. But burning it as firewood, without the intention of it constituting a *korban*, is permitted. Applying this to our case: one burns the material between the fingers on the *mizbe'ach* designated as fuel, not as an offering.

### Rav Mari Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רַב מָרִי: דְּקָמְצִי שַׁמִּינֵי. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר נָמֵי לְכַתְּחִילָּה דְּקָמְצִי שַׁמִּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It works out **well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, but according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who reject Rabbi Eliezer's *halakha*, **what can be said? Rav Mari said:** The solution is **that fat** priests **take** the **handfuls** of meal-offerings, as their fleshy fingers press against everything between them, and therefore no flour remains between the fingers. The Gemara states: **Now that you have arrived at this** conclusion, according to **Rabbi Eliezer as well,** it is appropriate **that fat** priests should **take** the **handfuls *ab initio*,** to avoid a situation of doubt.

The *gemara* then asks: this works according to Rabbi Eliezer. But the *rabbanan* disagree and hold that the designation does not matter — burning *shirayim* on the *mizbe'ach* is prohibited regardless of intent. What is their answer?

*Rav Mari* answers: *dekamtzai shamnei* — choose overweight *kohanim* to perform the *kemitzah*. If the *kohen* has enough flesh on his fingers such that nothing can fit between them, the problem is eliminated entirely — there is no material between the fingers and no indeterminate zone.

The *gemara* then adds: *hashta de'aseit lehachi* — now that we have arrived at this answer, even Rabbi Eliezer would agree that the ideal is to use overweight *kohanim* so that the problem does not arise in the first place. The *leshum etzim* designation is at best a *bedi'avad* solution; *lechatchila*, one should avoid the situation entirely.

One further consideration: if one burns the material between the fingers *leshum etzim*, and it turns out that material was actually *kometz*, has the *haktara* been performed validly despite the designation of "firewood"? Two approaches suggest themselves. One could hold that designation of intent is irrelevant — if the material was objectively *kometz* and was burned on the *mizbe'ach*, the *haktara* is valid regardless of what the *kohen* had in mind. Alternatively, one could apply a form of conditional stipulation — *im zeh kometz, le'shem kometz; im shirayim, leshum etzim* — if this is *kometz*, let it be for the sake of the *kometz*; if it is *shirayim*, let it be for firewood. *Rashi* appears to adopt this latter approach. The *gemara* itself, however, does not develop the point explicitly, and the question of whether such a conditional *kavanah* constitutes *breira* — which is generally problematic — remains an open one.
