# Menachot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kometz Wholeness

### Foreign Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמַץ וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ צְרוֹר, אוֹ גַרְגֵּר מֶלַח, אוֹ קוֹרֶט לְבוֹנָה – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** If a priest **removed the handful** of flour, **and a stone or a grain of salt emerged in his hand, or a pinch of frankincense** emerged in his hand, the meal offering is **unfit,** as the handful lacks a full measure on account of these items.

We begin at the last line of 11b, where the *mishnah* turns to the requirement that the *kometz* be whole and complete. Having addressed the question of the left hand, the *mishnah* now establishes that if even a tiny pebble (*tzeror*), a crystal of salt (*gargir melach*), or a fragment of frankincense (*kort shel levona*) finds its way into the *kometz*, it is *pasul* — because the handful is not considered full. The definition of fullness, it becomes clear, is remarkably precise.

### Three Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל הָנֵי לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא צְרוֹר – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָאו בַּת הַקְרָבָה הִיא, אֲבָל מֶלַח דְּבַת הַקְרָבָה הִיא – אֵימָא תִּתַּכְשַׁר. וְאִי תְּנָא מֶלַח, דְּלָא אִיקְּבַע בַּהֲדֵי מִנְחָה מֵעִיקָּרָא, אֲבָל לְבוֹנָה דְּאִיקְּבַע בַּהֲדֵי מִנְחָה מֵעִיקָּרָא – אֵימָא תִּתַּכְשַׁר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **all these** examples? Any one of them would convey the fact that the handful must contain a full measure. The Gemara explains: All of the cases are **necessary. Because if** the mishna had **taught** only the example of **a stone,** it might have been thought that only a stone diminishes the measure of the handful, **because it is not fit for sacrifice. But** with regard to **salt, which is fit for sacrifice,** as the priest places salt on the handful before burning it upon the altar, one might **say** that the handful **should be fit,** as the salt should not subtract from the handful's measure. **And if** the mishna had **taught** only the example of **salt,** it might have been thought that the salt diminishes the handful's measure **as it was not initially fixed** together **with** the entire **meal offering.** The reason is **that** the priest **salts the handful alone. But** with regard to the **frankincense, which was initially fixed** together **with** the entire **meal offering,** i.e., it is placed upon the meal offering before the priest removes a handful from it, one might **say** that the handful **should be fit** and the frankincense should not diminish from the handful's measure. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that in any of these instances the meal offering is unfit.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *kulei lama li?* Why are all three examples necessary? They appear functionally equivalent. The *Gemara* explains that each example is needed to address a distinct potential leniency. Had the *mishnah* mentioned only a pebble, one might have thought the problem was specifically that a pebble does not belong on the *mizbeach* — it is simply not an ingredient of a *korban*. But salt is different: *al kol korbanecha takriv melach* — salt is actually added to every offering, and one might have thought that since salt belongs on the *mizbeach*, its presence in the *kometz* should not constitute a deficiency. And had the *mishnah* mentioned only salt, one might have noted that salt is not originally designated together with the *mincha* — it is added afterward. Frankincense, by contrast, is placed on the *mincha* from the outset; it is one of the original ingredients. One might therefore have supposed that a grain of frankincense in the *kometz* is not a foreign substance at all but rather a legitimate component of the offering, and therefore should not invalidate it. By listing all three, the *mishnah* teaches that none of them — not even frankincense, which is an original ingredient — counts as part of a valid *kometz*. Rashi notes the question of why the *mishnah* does not simply begin with frankincense, the most compelling case, but does not fully resolve it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: הַקּוֹמֶץ הֶחָסֵר אוֹ הַיָּתֵר פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if a stone, or a grain of salt, or a pinch of frankincense emerged in the priest's hand together with the handful, the meal offering is unfit **due to** the fact **that** the Sages **said: The handful that is lacking or that is outsized** is **unfit.**

The *mishnah* then concludes with the principle: *hakometz hachaser o hayater pasul* — the *kometz* that is either lacking or, surprisingly, even one that contains more than the exact amount, is invalid.

### Chatzitza Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא מִשּׁוּם חָסֵר וְיָתֵר? וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** does the *tanna* explain that the offering is not valid **specifically because** it is **lacking or outsized? But let** the *tanna* **derive** that such a handful is not valid **due to** the fact that there is **an interposition** between the priest's hand and the handful. **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** Since there are certain instances where these items do not interpose between one's hand and the handful, e.g., when they are located **on the side** of the handful, the mishna teaches that they disqualify the handful due to the fact that they reduce its measure.

The *Gemara* raises a pointed question: given that the *mishnah* discusses a pebble or crystal in the *kometz*, why does it frame the problem in terms of lacking (*chaser*) or excess (*yater*)? Why not invoke the more familiar principle of *chatzitza* — interposition — since the foreign object interposes between the *kohen*'s skin and the flour? Rashi offers two possibilities. If the foreign object is at the outer edge of the handful, it would interpose between part of the skin and the flour. If it is in the interior, one might argue that since the flour surrounds it on all sides, it effectively divides the handful into separate units. Yet this too is not entirely satisfying — a single point of interposition amidst a mass of connected flour seems insufficient to divide the *kometz* into discrete portions. The case of *yater*, excess, is similarly difficult to explain as a *chatzitza* problem, since something overflowing through the fingers is not obviously interposing between the skin and the flour. The *Gemara*'s question, then, is not fully resolved by its own terms.

### Min HaTzad

Rabbi Yirmiya answers: *min hatzad* — the foreign object is at the edges. Rashi explains that after taking the *kometz*, the *kohen* would hold it with the palm facing upward, so as not to spill the flour. The foreign object sits at the very edges of the handful — near the tip of the pinky on one side and near the tip of the index finger on the other — and therefore does not interpose between the skin and the flour in the interior of the hand. This explains why the *mishnah* does not frame the problem as *chatzitza*: positioned at the edges, the foreign object does not technically constitute an interposition between the bulk of the *kometz* and the *kohen*'s hand. The problem is simply that the *kometz* is not full.

## Kemitza Technique

### Abaye Asks Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא: כֵּיצַד קוֹמְצִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּדְקָמְצִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava: How do** the priests properly **remove the handful** from a meal offering? Rava **said to him:** They remove it **as people** normally **remove handfuls,** by folding all of their fingers over the palm of the hand.

Moving forward, Abaye poses a practical question to Rava: *keitzad kometz* — how exactly is *kemitza* performed? Rava answers simply: *ke-dekamtzi inshi* — the way people normally take a handful. You put your hand in and scoop it up; nothing unusual. This answer, as we will see, turns out to be more nuanced than it first appears.

### Halakhic Fingers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: זוֹ זֶרֶת, זוֹ קְמִיצָה, זוֹ אַמָּה, זוֹ אֶצְבַּע, זוֹ גּוּדָל.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rava from a *baraita* discussing the mitzva function of each of the fingers: **This** small finger is for measuring **a span** (see Exodus 28:16), i.e., the distance between the thumb and the little finger. **This** fourth finger is used for **removal of a handful** from the meal offering, i.e., the measurement of a handful begins from this finger, as the priest removes a handful by folding the middle three fingers over his palm. Furthermore, **this** middle finger is used for measuring **a cubit,** the distance from the elbow to the tip of the middle finger. **This** forefinger, next to the thumb, is the **finger** used to sprinkle the blood of offerings on the altar. And finally, **this thumb** is the one on which the blood and oil is placed during the purification ritual of a leper (see Leviticus 14:17). Evidently, the little finger is not used in the removal of a handful.

The *Gemara* proceeds to identify the five halakhic fingers by name. The pinky is the *zeret*, because *zeret* denotes the span from the tip of the thumb to the tip of the pinky, which equals approximately two *tfachim*. The ring finger is the *kemitza*, because *kemitza* is performed with three middle fingers and this finger is the outermost of those three. The middle finger is the *amah*, because an *amah* is measured from the elbow to the tip of the middle finger. The index finger is the *etzba*, named for its use in the priestly blood applications — on the *chatat* and on the ear of the *metzora*. The thumb is the *godel*, because the *kohen* places oil on the thumb (*godel*) of the *metzora*. These five fingers thus each carry a halakhic designation tied to a specific ritual function.

### All Fingers Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְהַשְׁווֹת, (כְּלוֹמַר, קוֹמֵץ מְלֹא הַיָּד כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא חָסֵר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מוֹחֵק בְּאֶצְבַּע קְטַנָּה מִלְּמַטָּה).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The little finger is used **only for** the purposes of **leveling** the handful, **that is to say,** the priest first **removes a handful** with **a full hand,** i.e., all of his fingers, **so that it should not be lacking** in measure, **and then he wipes** away the protruding flour **with** his **little finger from the bottom,** and with his thumb from the top.

The *Gemara* now challenges the implication of Rava's answer. If *ke-dekamtzi inshi* means using all five fingers, that cannot be right — *kemitza* is defined precisely as a three-fingered act, not a five-fingered one. So the *Gemara* clarifies.

### Three Fingers Bent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה, אָמַר רַב: חוֹפֶה שְׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ עַל פַּס יָדוֹ, וְקוֹמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How** is the removal of the handful **performed? Rav Zutra bar Toviyya says** that **Rav says:** When the priest places his hand in the meal offering, **he bends his** middle **three fingers until** the tips of his fingers **reach over the palm of his hand, and** he then **removes the handful.**

The *Gemara* explains: *ela le-hashvot* — the point is to smooth and level the handful. The resolution, attributed to Rav Zutra bar Tuvya in the name of Rav, is that the *kohen* bends his three middle fingers (*chofeh shalosh etzba'otav*) until they reach the palm of his hand (*al pas yado*), and then takes the *kemitza*. The actual scooping is performed with these three bent fingers. The thumb and pinky are used not to scoop but to smooth away any flour that extends beyond the three fingers.

### Tanya Nami Hachi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״מְלֹא קֻמְצוֹ״ – יָכוֹל מְבוֹרָץ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּקֻמְצוֹ״. אִי בְּקֻמְצוֹ, יָכוֹל בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מְלֹא קֻמְצוֹ״, הָא כֵּיצַד? חוֹפֶה שְׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו עַל פַּס יָדוֹ וְקוֹמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes **that** this **is also taught** in a *baraita*. From the verse that states: "And he shall remove from there **his handful"** (Leviticus 2:2), one **might** have thought that the handful should be **overflowing.** Therefore, another **verse states:** "And he shall take up from it **with his handful [*bekumtzo*]"** (Leviticus 6:8). The prefix that means "with" can also mean: In, indicating that the proper measure of a handful is that which is contained within one's fingers alone. The *baraita* continues: **If** the measurement of a handful is determined by the term **"with his handful,"** one **might** have thought that the priest removes a handful **with his fingertips,** i.e., that a handful consists of that which the priest removes by folding his fingers onto themselves. Therefore, **the verse states: "His handful,"** indicating that the handful must be full and not merely that which is contained within his fingers. **How so? He scoops** by closing **his three fingers over the palm of his hand, and** in this way **takes a handful** from the flour of the meal offering.

A *braita* confirms this understanding. The Torah says *melo kumtzo* — the fullness of his handful. One might think this means an overflowing, sprouting handful (*mevoratz*), but the Torah also says *be-kumtzo* — with his handful — implying exactly the size of the hand, not more. One might then think that "with his handful" means using only the very fingertips, but *melo* — fullness — requires the complete span of the fingers. How is this achieved? *Chofeh shalosh etzba'otav al pas yado ve-komatz* — he bends his three fingers to his palm and takes the *kemitza*. Full, but not overflowing.

## Baked Minchot

### Smoothing Chunks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַחֲבַת וּבְמַרְחֶשֶׁת, מוֹחֵק בְּגוּדָלוֹ מִלְּמַעְלָה וּבְאֶצְבָּעוֹ קְטַנָּה מִלְּמַטָּה, וְזוֹ הִיא עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In** the case of **a pan** meal offering **and** that **of a deep-pan** meal offering, when the flour was fried before being scooped and was therefore hard, the priest **wipes** away **with his thumb** any flour that was overflowing **above** his handful, **and with his little finger** he wipes away the flour that was pushing out **below. And this** precise taking of the handful of a meal offering **is** the most **difficult** sacrificial **rite in the Temple,** as the priest must wipe away any protruding elements without removing any flour from the handful itself.

A further complication arises with *minchas* that are baked — *machvat u'marcheshet*. Since the Torah requires that baked offerings be broken into pieces (*patot osah p'titim*), the *kohen* is working with chunks rather than loose flour. Getting an exact *kometz* from chunks is considerably harder, since pieces will protrude unevenly. The *Gemara* therefore specifies: *mochek be-gudlo milma'alah u've-etzba ktanah milmata* — he uses his thumb above and his pinky below to smooth the *kometz* level. When working with flour and oil alone, one can take an exact handful without smoothing; with chunks, the thumb and pinky become essential tools for precision.

### Hardest Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶהוּ וְתוּ לָא? וְהָאִיכָּא מְלִיקָה, וְהָאִיכָּא חֲפִינָה! אֶלָּא זוֹ הִיא אַחַת מֵעֲבוֹדוֹת קָשׁוֹת שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This one** is the hardest sacrificial rite, **and no other? But isn't there pinching** the nape of the neck of a bird offering, which is also considered extremely difficult to perform, **and isn't there** the **scooping** of the handful of incense by the High Priest on Yom Kippur, another rite that is extremely difficult to perform? **Rather,** the *baraita* means that **this** taking of the handful of a meal offering **is one of** the most **difficult** sacrificial **rites in the Temple.**

The *Gemara* then states: *ve-zu hi avodah kashah she'be-mikdash* — this is the hardest *avodah* in the *Mikdash*. At first glance, this seems surprising. The challenge, evidently, lies in achieving an exact measurement — not too much, not too little — given that *yeter ve-chaser pasul*.

The *Gemara* itself reacts with skepticism: *zo hi? Zu ve-tu lo?* This and nothing else is difficult? What about *melikah* — the pinching of the bird's neck from behind with the *kohen*'s fingernail — and what about *chafina* — the double handful of incense taken by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, where the Torah says *melo chofnav*, the fullness of his two handfuls? The difficulty of *chafina* lies precisely in the physical dexterity required: once inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *kohen gadol* holds the incense vessel in the crook of his arm and must maneuver the contents into both cupped hands without using his hands to assist — manipulating it essentially with his elbow alone. The *Gemara* therefore amends the statement: *ela zo hi achat me-avodot kashot she'be-mikdash* — it is one of the hardest, not uniquely so.

## Rav Pappa's Questions

### Fingertips & Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, ״מְלֹא קֻמְצוֹ״ כִּדְקָמְצִי אִינָשֵׁי. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: קָמַץ בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, מַאי? מִן הַצְּדָדִין, מַאי? מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It is **obvious to me** that the term **"his handful"** means that the removal of the handful from a meal offering should be performed *ab initio* **in the manner that people** usually **remove a handful,** with their fingertips angled to the side. **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* if the priest **removed a handful with his fingertips,** i.e., if he placed his hand horizontally over the meal offering and filled his palm with flour by closing his fingers to his palm? Does this disqualify the taking of the handful or not? Similarly, if the priest took a handful **from the sides,** by passing the back of his hand back and forth over the flour in the vessel so that the flour collected in his palm by way of the side of his palm, **what** is the *halakha*? Furthermore, if he took the handful with the back of his hand placed **downward** in the vessel, and with his fingers he collected the flour **upward** into his palm, **what** is the *halakha*? Are the handfuls removed in this manner fit for sacrifice? The Gemara states: These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Having established the basic mechanics, Rav Pappa proceeds to a series of unresolved questions about whether the process of *kemitza* matters in itself or only its result. He begins: *komatz be-rosh etzba'otav mah?* — what if the *kohen* used only his fingertips, slowly accumulating the full amount without performing a normal scooping motion? Does the unconventional process invalidate the *kemitza*? He further asks about *min ha-tzdadim* — from the sides. One interpretation is that the *kohen* scoops horizontally rather than from above; Rashi suggests an alternative image of forming a tube with the hand and scooping in that manner. He also asks about *mi-mata le-ma'alah* — from below to above: rather than pressing the palm downward onto the *mincha* and scooping up, the *kohen* inserts his hand with the back facing down and scoops upward. In all these cases, the full amount may be present in the hand at the end — but the process deviated from the norm. The *Gemara* leaves all of these as *teiku* — unresolved.

### Chafina Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, ״מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו״ כִּדְחָפְנִי אִינָשֵׁי. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חָפַן בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו מַהוּ? מִן הַצְּדָדִין מַהוּ? חָפַן בְּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וְקֵרְבָן זוֹ אֵצֶל זוֹ מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It is **obvious to me** that when the Torah states: **"His handful"** (Leviticus 16:12), in the context of the scooping of handfuls of incense by the High Priest on Yom Kippur, it means **in the manner that people** usually **scoop a handful,** by placing the backs of their hands into the vessel and bringing their hands together. **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if the High Priest **scooped a handful with his fingertips? What is** the *halakha* if he took a handful **from the sides? What is** the *halakha* if **he scooped a handful with this** hand **and with that** hand separately **and** then **brought them together?** The Gemara states: These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa then turns to *chafina*, the double handful of *Yom Kippur*. The straightforward case — *ke-chofnei inshi*, the normal way one fills two hands — presents no difficulty. But Rav Pappa asks the parallel questions: what if the *kohen gadol* filled only with his fingertips? What if he scooped from the sides — either by angling his hands laterally rather than scooping from above? And what if he filled each hand separately and then brought them together (*kirvam zo etzel zo*)? Is a double handful that was assembled in two stages valid, or must the two hands fill simultaneously as a single act? Again: *teiku*.

### Process vs Result

The underlying question unifying all of Rav Pappa's inquiries is fundamental: does the *halacha* care only about the result — the correct amount of flour or incense in the hand — or does it also impose requirements on the process itself? One might frame it differently: is the specification of *kemitza* and *chafina* purely a means of ensuring the correct quantity, or is there an independent ritual requirement governing the act by which that quantity is obtained? The absence of any recorded ruling leaves this unresolved.

### Body of Cohen

One might ask: why must the *kohen* use his own body at all? Why not fashion a vessel calibrated to his own handbreadth and use that? It could be argued that the Torah specifies the act in relation to the *kohen*'s person — just as *melikah* is performed with the *kohen*'s fingernail — and that the involvement of the *kohen*'s body is itself part of the ritual specification, not merely a practical means of measurement. This does not fully answer the question, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it. What does seem clear from Rav Pappa's inquiries is that at least some degree of concern for process — not merely result — is operative: the *Gemara* treats it as genuinely uncertain whether unconventional techniques are acceptable, rather than dismissing the question as obviously irrelevant.

## Unusual Placements

### Wall Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: דַּבְּקֵיהּ לְקוֹמֶץ בְּדַפְנֵיהּ דְּמָנָא, מַאי? תּוֹךְ כְּלִי בָּעֵינַן, וְהָאִיכָּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא הַנָּחָה בְּתוֹכוֹ בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises** yet another **dilemma:** If the priest took the handful from the vessel containing the meal offering and **stuck the handful onto the side of** the second **vessel** in order to sanctify it, i.e., the handful was not placed directly into the vessel, **what** is the *halakha*? Do **we require** that the handful be **inside** the **vessel, and** that **is** the case here? **Or perhaps we require** that the handful be **placed** properly **inside** the vessel, **and** that **is not** the case in this instance. No answer is found, and the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa raises a further creative question: what if the *kometz*, which is sticky with oil, was pressed against the inner wall of the *kli sharet* rather than allowed to fall to the bottom? It was inside the vessel, but suspended against the side rather than resting on the floor. The question is framed as a tension between two principles: on one hand, the requirement may simply be that the *kometz* be *toch ha-kli* — within the vessel — and it is indeed within the vessel. On the other hand, the act of *netina ba-kli* — placing into the vessel — may require normal placement, which would mean allowing the *kometz* to settle at the base. This is again a question of process versus result, now applied to the act of *kiddush kli*. The *Gemara* leaves it as *teiku*.

### Upside-Down Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַפְכֵיהּ לְמָנָא, וְדַבְּקֵיהּ לְקוֹמֶץ בְּאַרְעִיתָא דְּמָנָא, מַאי? הַנָּחָה בְּתוֹכוֹ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָאִיכָּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כְּתִיקְנוֹ בָּעִינַן, וְלֵיכָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi raises** a similar **dilemma:** If the priest **overturned the vessel and stuck** the **handful to the underside of the vessel,** in a case where there was an indentation on the underside, **what** is the *halakha*? Do **we require** that the handful be **placed inside** the vessel, **and that** requirement **is** fulfilled here, as the handful is within the indentation? **Or perhaps we require** that it be placed **properly** in the vessel, **and that is not** the case here. The Gemara states: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Mar bar Rav Ashi then extends the inquiry: *hafkhei le-mana ve-davkei le-komitz be-ara de-mana* — what if the *kli* was turned upside down and the *kometz* was stuck to what is now the floor — which is of course the original exterior base? Rashi explains this as a vessel with a lip around its base, so that even inverted it retains a kind of inner space. A simpler reading is that the *kometz* was placed inside the *kli* in its normal orientation but the vessel was then flipped, so the *kometz* adheres to what is now the ceiling. The question again divides into two possibilities: either we require only that the *kometz* be *be-tocho* — within it — in which case this may suffice; or we require *ke-tikuno* — the normal manner of use — which is absent here. The relevance of *derech tashmisho* — the principle that *klei sharet* function as sanctifying vessels only when used in their normal manner — is noted. Placing a *kometz* against the ceiling of an inverted vessel is, almost by definition, not *derech sheirut*. But whether the question pertains to *kiddush kli* or to the act of *hanacha* by the *kohen* is itself a matter of interpretation: arguably the vessel may become sanctified, but the *mincha* may nonetheless be *pesula* because the *kohen*'s act of placing was not performed in an acceptable manner. The *Gemara* offers no resolution.

## Oil Amounts

### Too Much Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִיבָּה שַׁמְנָהּ, חִסֵּר שַׁמְנָהּ, חִיסֵּר לְבוֹנָתָהּ – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **one increased its oil, decreased its oil,** or **decreased its frankincense,** beyond the appropriate measures, the meal offering is **unfit.**

The *mishnah* continues: *riba shamna* — if there was too much oil — or if the oil was deficient — the *mincha* is invalid. This transitions from the precise requirements of the *kometz* itself to the precise proportions of the *mincha*'s ingredients. Too much oil, too little oil — either way, it is *pasul*. The parallel with the *kometz* is notable: just as the exact amount of flour must be present in the hand, so too the exact proportion of oil must be present in the *mincha*. This reflects the broader principle that since the *mincha* is a manufactured offering, every component must conform to its specified measure. The contrast with animal offerings is striking — a *shelamim* or *olah* requires *shechita*, blood application, and that is essentially the entirety of the process; but the *mincha* requires exact quantities of flour, oil, and frankincense, failure of any one of which may invalidate the entire offering.

### Two Logs Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי רִיבָּה שַׁמְנָהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** of a case where the meal offering is disqualified due to the fact that one **increased its oil? Rabbi Eliezer says:** The circumstances are a case **where he separated two *log*** of oil **for** the meal offering instead of one *log*, and mixed them into a tenth of an ephah of flour.

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami riba shamna* — what exactly is the case of too much oil? Rabbi Elazar answers: for instance, where one designated two *login* of oil for one *isaron* of *solet*. The normal proportion is one *log* to one *isaron*; he sanctified double. He did not merely add a splash of extra oil by accident — he formally consecrated two *login* to accompany this *mincha*.

### Why Sanctified?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְמַהּ כְּגוֹן דְּעָרֵיב בָּהּ שֶׁמֶן דְּחוּלִּין וָשֶׁמֶן דַּחֲבֶירְתַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And let** Rabbi Eliezer **interpret** the mishna as referring to a case **where he mixed non-sacred oil or the oil of another** meal offering **into** the meal offering.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: why did Rabbi Elazar specify that the extra oil was sanctified? Why not simply say that one added unsanctified oil (*shemen d'chulin*) or oil from another *mincha* (*shemen d'chaverta*)? The implicit assumption behind the challenge is that Rabbi Elazar chose the more difficult case — where both *login* were properly sanctified for this *mincha* — in order to teach that even then it is invalid. The *Gemara* wonders whether Rabbi Elazar believes that unsanctified or external oil would not invalidate, and only sanctified oil does.

### Unsanctified Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, שֶׁמֶן דְּחוּלִּין וָשֶׁמֶן דַּחֲבֶירְתַּהּ לָא פָּסֵל, מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא דְּפָסֵל בָּהּ שֶׁמֶן, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **non-sacred oil and the oil of another** meal offering **do not disqualify** a meal offering, **Rav Zutra bar Toviyya objects to this** claim: **If that is so,** then with regard to the **meal offering of a sinner,** of which it is stated (59b) **that oil disqualifies it, how can you find** the circumstances where it is in fact disqualified?

The *Gemara* tests this reading against the *mincha* of a sinner (*minchat choteh*), about which the Torah states *lo yasim aleha shemen ve-lo yiten aleha levona* — do not place oil or frankincense upon it. What type of oil would invalidate a *minchat choteh*? If we say that only oil sanctified *for this mincha* creates a problem — any oil the *kohen* might sanctify for a *minchat choteh* is inherently meaningless, since the Torah assigns no oil to that offering — then no oil could ever constitute a valid sanctification for it, and therefore no oil could ever technically invalidate it by Rabbi Elazar's reasoning. Unsanctified oil or oil from another *mincha* would also not invalidate, according to the proposed reading. This leads to an absurdity: nothing would ever invalidate a *minchat choteh* through excess oil.

### Both Logs Fit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא דְּחוּלִּין וְדַחֲבֶירְתַּהּ דְּפָסֵיל, אֲבָל הִפְרִישׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין, הוֹאִיל וְהַאי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ וְהַאי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ, אֵימָא לָא לִיפְּסִיל – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: In fact, non-sacred oil and the oil of another meal offering do disqualify a meal offering, **and Rabbi Eliezer is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to say **that non-sacred** oil **and** the oil **of another** meal offering **disqualify** a meal offering. **But** in a case where one **separated two *log* for** his meal offering, **since this** first *log* **is fit for** the meal offering, **and that** second *log* **is** also **fit for it,** one might **say** that even when he mixes both *log* into the meal offering, **it should not disqualify** the meal offering. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer **teaches us** that the meal offering is disqualified in this case as well.

The *Gemara* therefore reverses course in its reading of Rabbi Elazar. The formulation *lo mibaya* is invoked: Rabbi Elazar was not saying that only sanctified oil invalidates. Of course, if oil that has no place in the *mincha* — unsanctified oil or oil from another *mincha* — is mixed in, it invalidates, precisely because it is fundamentally foreign to this offering. What Rabbi Elazar is teaching is the *chiddush*: even if one sanctified two *login*, so that both are ostensibly "its oil" — *hoil ve-hai chazi leh ve-hai chazi leh*, since either *log* could have been the one used — one might have thought that because neither log is intrinsically inappropriate, using both together is not *pasul*. Rabbi Elazar teaches that it is nonetheless *pasul*, because the proportion is wrong regardless of the propriety of each component.

### Its Oil Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָא? אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ, מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״רִיבָּה שַׁמְנָהּ״? לִיתְנֵי ״רִיבָּה לָהּ שֶׁמֶן״! אֶלָּא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּהִפְרִישׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Eliezer** derive **this** conclusion? **Rava said:** The terminology of **the mishna posed a difficulty for him,** as one can ask: **Why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** that the meal offering is disqualified if **he increased its oil,** which indicates that he increased it with oil belonging to the offering itself? **Let** the mishna **teach** simply: **He increased the oil. Rather, this** is what the mishna **teaches us: That even though he** initially **separated two *log*** of oil **for** the meal offering, its own oil disqualifies the offering when there is too much.

From where does Rabbi Elazar derive this? Rava points to the language of the *mishnah* itself: *mai irya de-tani riba shamna* — why does the *mishnah* say "too much *of its* oil" (*riba shamna*)? It could have simply said "too much oil" (*riba ha-shemen*). The possessive — *of its oil* — implies that the problem arises specifically when the oil is connected to this *mincha*, i.e., when it was sanctified for it. This confirms that Rabbi Elazar's case is precisely the one involving sanctified oil, and the teaching is that even such oil, when present in excess, invalidates the *mincha*.

## Frankincense Amounts

### Lacking Levona

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִיסֵּר לְבוֹנָתָהּ – תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָסְרָה וְעָמְדָה עַל קוֹרֶט אֶחָד – פְּסוּלָה, עַל שְׁנֵי קְרָטִין – כְּשֵׁרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַל קוֹרֶט אֶחָד – כְּשֵׁרָה, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one **decreased its frankincense** beyond its appropriate measure, the meal offering is unfit. Concerning this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **decreased** its frankincense to the point that the amount **stood at** only **one pinch,** it is **disqualified,** but if the decreased amount stood **at two pinches,** it is **fit;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** If the decreased amount stood **at one pinch,** it is **fit; less than that,** it is **disqualified.**

The *mishnah* states: *chisar levona pesula* — if the frankincense was lacking, the *mincha* is invalid. This establishes that the *levona* must be complete; any deficiency renders the offering unfit.

A *braita* is cited that specifies the minimum required. If the *levona* was reduced to a single *koret* — a seed or pod of frankincense — it is *pasul*; but if two *kortin* remain, it is *kasher*. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: even one *koret* is sufficient. Less than that — a partial pod — is *pasul*, but a whole pod, however small, is valid.

### All Present?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא (רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר): קוֹמֶץ לְבוֹנָה שֶׁחָסַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – פָּסוּל. תְּנִי: קוֹרֶט לְבוֹנָה שֶׁחָסַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – פָּסוּל. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כָּאן בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה עִם הַמִּנְחָה, כָּאן בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: A handful** of flour **or frankincense that** was **decreased** by **any amount** from its full measure is **disqualified?** The Gemara answers that one should **teach** the *baraita* as follows: **A pinch of frankincense that** was **decreased** by **any amount** is **disqualified. And if you wish, say** instead that **here,** the first cited statement of Rabbi Shimon, is referring to the case **of frankincense that comes with a meal offering,** and this frankincense is disqualified only when there is less than a pinch, whereas **there,** the second statement of Rabbi Shimon, is referring to the case **of frankincense that comes by itself.** Such frankincense is disqualified if it comprises any less than its full measure.

A *braita* appears to contradict Rabbi Shimon's leniency: *kometz ve-levona she-chasar kol she-hu pasul* — if the *levona* is missing any amount at all, it is *pasul*. This seems to demand the full quantity even at the time of burning. The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction by reinterpreting the *braita*: it does not mean that you need the full original quantity of *levona* intact; rather, it means that if the single remaining *koret* is itself incomplete — if you have less than a full pod — it is *pasul*. Once you have at least one whole pod (or two, according to Rabbi Yehuda), the requirement is satisfied.

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁלֹשׁ מַחְלוֹקֹת בַּדָּבָר, רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: קוֹמֵץ בַּתְּחִילָּה וְקוֹמֵץ בַּסּוֹף, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: קוֹמֶץ בַּתְּחִילָּה וּשְׁנֵי קְרָטִין בַּסּוֹף, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: קוֹמֶץ בַּתְּחִילָּה וְקוֹרֶט אֶחָד בַּסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** There are **three disputes** of *tanna'im* **with regard to the matter. Rabbi Meir holds** that the priest must remove **a handful at the beginning, and ultimately** the entire **handful** must be burned upon the altar. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the priest must remove **a handful at the beginning, and ultimately** at least **two pinches** from it must be burned upon the altar. **And Rabbi Shimon holds** that the priest must remove **a handful at the beginning and ultimately** at least **one pinch** from it must be burned upon the altar.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, now presents the three-way dispute in organized form: *gimmel machlokos ba-davar* — three positions in the matter.

## Pesuk Interpretations

### Ve-es Kol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁלׇשְׁתָּן מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, ״וְאֵת כׇּל הַלְּבוֹנָה אֲשֶׁר עַל הַמִּנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** all **three of them interpret a single verse** differently. The verse states: "And he shall take up from there his handful, of the fine flour of the meal offering, and of the oil of it, **and [*ve'et*] all the frankincense that is upon the meal offering,** and shall make it smoke upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:8).

All three positions are derived from a single verse: *ve-es kol ha-levonah asher al ha-mincha* — "and all the frankincense that is on the *mincha*" — you shall burn it on the *mizbeach* (Vayikra 6:8). The straightforward reading of this verse appears to support Rabbi Meir.

### Kol = All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: עַד דְּאִיתָא לִלְבוֹנָה דְּאִיקְּבַע בַּהֲדֵי מִנְחָה מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir holds** that one may not make the offering smoke upon the altar **unless there** remains **the** entire measure of **frankincense that was initially fixed together with the meal offering.**

Rabbi Meir reads *kol* in its plain sense: all — the entire quantity of *levona* that was designated for this *mincha* must be burned. *Ad d'is lah la-levonah d'ikva bahadi mincha* — until you have offered up everything that was established together with the *mincha*.

### Kol = Any

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: ״כׇּל״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ חַד קוֹרֶט, ״אֶת״ – לְרַבּוֹת קוֹרֶט אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that when the verse states: **"All [*kol*],"** it is referring to any part of the frankincense, **even a single pinch,** as *kol* can mean any amount (see II Kings 2:4). And when the verse states: **"*Et*,"** this serves **to include another pinch.**

Rabbi Yehuda reads *kol* differently: *va-afilu chad karei* — even one is called *kol*. The word *kol* can mean "any" rather than "all," as evidenced by the verse *leis shifchaska kol ba-bayis* — "your maidservant has nothing in the house," where *kol* clearly means "any." Under this reading, the verse requires only that some *levona* be burned — any amount — not that the full original quantity be present.

### Et = Add One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן ״אֶת״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, at least two pinches must remain to be burned upon the altar. **And Rabbi Shimon** interprets the word "all" in the same manner as does Rabbi Yehuda, but he **does not interpret** and derive a *halakha* from the term **"*et*,"** and he therefore holds that only one pinch must remain to be burned.

But since Rabbi Yehuda reads *kol* as "any," why does he require two seeds rather than one? He derives the additional seed from the prefix *et* in the phrase *ve-es kol ha-levonah*. The *et* is an amplifying particle that adds one unit — thus: any *levona* plus one more, making a minimum of two seeds.

Rabbi Shimon does not accept homiletical derivations from *et* as adding any additional requirement. For him, *kol* means any amount, and *et* adds nothing. Therefore, even a single seed satisfies the requirement.

## Separate vs With

### With Mincha Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה עִם הַמִּנְחָה, אֲבָל בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל קוֹמֶץ בַּתְּחִילָּה וְקוֹמֶץ בַּסּוֹף. לְהָכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ ״אֲשֶׁר עַל הַמִּנְחָה״, דְּבַהֲדֵי מִנְחָה – אִין, בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef** further **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** between these *tanna'im* is **with regard to frankincense that comes with a meal offering. But with regard to frankincense that comes by itself, everyone,** even Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, **agrees** that the priest must bring **a handful at the beginning, and ultimately** the entire **handful** must be burned upon the altar. **For this** reason it **was necessary** for the verse to state: **"That is upon the meal offering,"** as this indicates **that together with a meal offering, yes,** one may burn the frankincense even if there remains only a pinch or two, but with regard to frankincense that comes **by itself,** one may **not** burn it if it is in that state.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei, again in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, clarifies the scope of the three-way dispute: *machlokes be-levonah ha-ba'ah im ha-mincha* — the debate of Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon applies only to *levona* that accompanies the *mincha* as one of its components. It is specifically in this context that the verse *ve-es kol ha-levonah asher al ha-mincha* applies, and that the question of whether *kol* means "all" or "any" arises.

When *levona* is brought as an independent offering — where a person consecrates a *kometz* of frankincense as a *korban* in its own right — all opinions agree: *kometz be-tchila ve-kometz be-sof* — a full *kometz* at the beginning and a full *kometz* must be burned at the end. In such a case, there is no special verse to permit a partial burning.

The logic underlying this distinction appears to be as follows: when *levona* accompanies a *mincha*, the primary ritual act is the *kemitza* of the flour; the *levona* is necessary for the *mincha*'s validity but is in some sense secondary. The verse *asher al ha-mincha* — "that is on the *mincha*" — opens the possibility that only some part of the *levona* need be burned for the offering to be complete. But when *levona* is the entire offering — when there is no *kemitza* from flour, no oil, nothing but the frankincense itself — the full quantity is what was consecrated, and the full quantity must be offered.

## Showbread Levona

### Two Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה עִם הַמִּנְחָה, אֲבָל בִּלְבוֹנָה הַבָּאָה בְּבָזִיכִין – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין בַּתְּחִילָּה, וּשְׁנֵי קְמָצִין בַּסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** between these *tanna'im* is **with regard to frankincense that comes with a meal offering. But with regard to frankincense that comes in bowls** together with the shewbread, **everyone,** even Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, **agrees** that there must be **two handfuls in the beginning,** one handful for each bowl, **and ultimately** there must also be **two handfuls.**

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds a third case: the *levona* brought in the two *bazichin* — the frankincense vessels or spoons — that were placed on the *shulchan* alongside the *lechem ha-panim*. Here, *divrei ha-kol shnei kmatzin be-tchila ve-shnei kmatzin be-sof* — all opinions agree that two full *kematzim* are required at the beginning and two full *kematzim* must be burned at the end. Each of the two vessels holds a *kometz*, and the total of two *kematzim* must be present at the outset and must be fully burned when the vessels are removed.

### All Agree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּבַהֲדֵי לֶחֶם אָתְיָא, כַּ״אֲשֶׁר עַל הַמִּנְחָה״ דָּמְיָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that this is the case, as with regard to this *halakha* the verse does not state the term: All, from which one might derive that it is referring to any part of the frankincense? The Gemara explains: This ruling is necessary, **lest you say** that **since** the frankincense in the bowls **comes together with bread,** i.e., the shewbread, it should be **considered as:** "Frankincense **that is upon the meal offering"** (Leviticus 6:8), and therefore Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon claim that one may sacrifice it even if less than two handfuls remain. Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The *Gemara* questions why this case needs to be stated — it seems obvious. If there is no special verse to permit a lesser amount, surely the full quantity is required, as in the case of *levona* brought as an independent offering. Why would anyone think otherwise?

The answer is that one might have argued: since the *bazichin* come together with the bread — the *lechem ha-panim* — and the bread is itself a kind of *mincha*, perhaps the *levona* in the *bazichin* falls under the category of *levona ha-ba'ah im ha-mincha*, and the lenient readings of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon would apply. If so, perhaps only one or two seeds need be burned.

The *Gemara* rejects this: the verse specifies *levona asher al ha-mincha* in the context of an offering from which a *kometz* of flour is taken. The *lechem ha-panim* involves no *kemitza*; there is no flour *kometz* taken from the showbread. Since the *levona* of the *bazichin* stands alone — there is no *kometz* component — it is treated like an independent offering of *levona*, and the full quantity must be burned.

## Excess Levona

### Mishnah Silence

The *mishnah* mentions *chisar levona pesula* — insufficient *levona* invalidates — but says nothing about excess *levona*. Does this silence imply that too much *levona* is permissible?

### Braita Says Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִיסֵּר לְבוֹנָתָהּ – הָא יָתֵיר כְּשֵׁרָה, וְהָתַנְיָא: יָתֵיר פְּסוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the priest **decreased its frankincense** beyond its appropriate measure, the meal offering is unfit. The Gemara infers from this statement that if **he increased** its frankincense, it is **fit.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if he **increased** its frankincense the meal offering is **disqualified?**

A *braita* states explicitly that excess *levona* is also *pasul*. The *mishnah*'s silence on the point is therefore not to be read as a leniency.

### Two Handfuls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said:** The *baraita* rules that the meal offering is disqualified in a case **where he separated two handfuls** of frankincense **for** the meal offering and placed both of them onto the meal offering.

Rami bar Chama identifies the case: one designated two *kematzim* worth of *levona* for a *mincha* that requires only one. The question is when this excess becomes fixed (*hukba*) as part of the *mincha* — and correspondingly, when it is too late to remedy the situation.

### Moment of Fixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הִפְרִישׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי קְמָצִין, וְאָבַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן קוֹדֶם קְמִיצָה – לֹא הוּקְבְּעוּ, אַחַר קְמִיצָה – הוּקְבְּעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Ḥama says:** In a case where **one separated two handfuls** of frankincense **for** the meal offering **and** subsequently **lost one of them,** if it was lost **before the removal of the handful** of the meal offering, the additional frankincense **was not fixed** with the meal offering, and therefore it does not disqualify the meal offering. But if this occurred **after the removal of the handful** of the meal offering, since both handfuls **were** already **fixed** with the meal offering, it is disqualified, as he increased its frankincense by a large amount.

The critical moment is the *kemitza*. It is the act of the *kohen* taking the handful that establishes what the *mincha* consists of. Before that act, the *levona* has been designated but not yet definitionally attached.

### Before Kemitza

If one of the two *kematzim* of *levona* was lost or destroyed before the *kemitza* was performed, *lo hukba* — it was not established as part of the *mincha*. The remaining single *kometz* is the correct amount, and the *mincha* is valid. The scenario envisioned is apparently one where the *levona* had been designated but not yet mixed into the *mincha* — the two units are separate, and losing one before the *kemitza* leaves the offering unimpaired.

### After Kemitza

If, however, both *kematzim* were present at the moment the *kohen* performed the *kemitza*, then *hukbe-u* — they were established together with the *mincha* at that moment — and the *mincha* is *pasul* because the *levona* was in excess at the defining instant. This explains the *mishnah*'s silence: there are scenarios where excess *levona* was designated but was lost before *kemitza*, leaving the *mincha* valid. The *mishnah* does not need to declare excess *levona* invalid in those cases.

## Showbread Excess

### Four Handfuls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הִפְרִישׁ אַרְבָּעָה קְמָצִין לִשְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין, וְאָבְדוּ שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶן קוֹדֶם סִילּוּק בָּזִיכִין – לָא הוּקְבְּעוּ, לְאַחַר סִילּוּק בָּזִיכִין – הוּקְבְּעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Ḥama says:** In a case where **one separated four handfuls** of frankincense **for** placement in the **two bowls** that accompany the shewbread, **and two of them** were subsequently **lost,** the *halakha* depends on when they were lost. If they were lost **before the removal of** the **bowls** from the Table of the shewbread, then the additional frankincense **was not** yet **fixed** with the shewbread, and the frankincense remains fit for sacrifice. But if they were lost **after the removal of** the **bowls,** then all four handfuls **were** already **fixed** with the shewbread, and therefore the frankincense is disqualified.

Rami bar Chama poses a parallel question regarding the *bazichin* of the *lechem ha-panim*. The two vessels each require one *kometz* of *levona* — two *kematzim* in total. What if four *kematzim* were designated? And then two of the four were lost?

### Before Removal

The parallel to the *mincha* case is: if two of the four *kematzim* were lost before *siluk ha-bazichin* — before the removal of the *bazichin* from the *shulchan* — then the excess was not yet established, and the two remaining *kematzim* are the correct amount, leaving the offering valid.

### After Removal

If all four were present until the moment of *siluk ha-bazichin*, then at that moment the excess was established — *hukbe-u* — and the offering is *pasul*.

### Why Repeat?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַיְינוּ הָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I also** need **this? This** statement of Rami bar Ḥama is identical to **that** previous statement, as the burning of the frankincense permits the shewbread for consumption just as the frankincense permits the meal offering for consumption. Consequently, the removal of the bowls of frankincense is comparable to the removal of the handful from a meal offering.

The *Gemara* asks: *ha tu lama li?* — why is this case necessary? Is it not the same as the *mincha* case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דִּבְרִיר קוֹמֶץ דִּידַהּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לְפוֹרְקָהּ – כְּמַאן דִּפְרִיקָה דָּמְיָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The last statement of Rami bar Ḥama is necessary, **lest you say** that **since** the **handful of** frankincense of the shewbread **is** already considered **designated** for burning, as it is placed in a separate bowl and burned in its entirety, then **once the time arrives for removing** the bowls from upon the Table of the shewbread, **it is considered as though** the bowls were already **removed,** and the shewbread should therefore be disqualified on account of the additional frankincense. Therefore, Rami bar Ḥama **teaches us** that the additional handfuls disqualify the shewbread only if they were inside the bowls at the time of their actual removal from the Table.

The *Gemara* responds that one might have thought there was a reason to distinguish: since the *bazichin* have a fixed time for their removal (*zeman le-farka*), one might have argued that the mere arrival of that time — even before the actual act of removal — already constitutes a kind of constructive removal, fixing the *levona* at that point regardless of what remained on the *shulchan*. If so, the question of whether excess was present "at the moment of removal" would effectively mean "at the moment the time for removal arrived." The case therefore teaches that no — it is the actual physical act of *siluk*, not merely the arrival of the time, that determines whether the excess has become fixed. The defining moment is the act, not the clock.
