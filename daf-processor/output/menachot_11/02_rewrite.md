# Menachot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kometz Wholeness

### Foreign Objects

We begin at the last line of 11b, where the *mishnah* turns to the requirement that the *kometz* be whole and complete. Having addressed the question of the left hand, the *mishnah* now establishes that if even a tiny pebble (*tzeror*), a crystal of salt (*gargir melach*), or a fragment of frankincense (*kort shel levona*) finds its way into the *kometz*, it is *pasul* — because the handful is not considered full. The definition of fullness, it becomes clear, is remarkably precise.

### Three Examples

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *kulei lama li?* Why are all three examples necessary? They appear functionally equivalent. The *Gemara* explains that each example is needed to address a distinct potential leniency. Had the *mishnah* mentioned only a pebble, one might have thought the problem was specifically that a pebble does not belong on the *mizbeach* — it is simply not an ingredient of a *korban*. But salt is different: *al kol korbanecha takriv melach* — salt is actually added to every offering, and one might have thought that since salt belongs on the *mizbeach*, its presence in the *kometz* should not constitute a deficiency. And had the *mishnah* mentioned only salt, one might have noted that salt is not originally designated together with the *mincha* — it is added afterward. Frankincense, by contrast, is placed on the *mincha* from the outset; it is one of the original ingredients. One might therefore have supposed that a grain of frankincense in the *kometz* is not a foreign substance at all but rather a legitimate component of the offering, and therefore should not invalidate it. By listing all three, the *mishnah* teaches that none of them — not even frankincense, which is an original ingredient — counts as part of a valid *kometz*. Rashi notes the question of why the *mishnah* does not simply begin with frankincense, the most compelling case, but does not fully resolve it.

The *mishnah* then concludes with the principle: *hakometz hachaser o hayater pasul* — the *kometz* that is either lacking or, surprisingly, even one that contains more than the exact amount, is invalid.

### Chatzitza Question

The *Gemara* raises a pointed question: given that the *mishnah* discusses a pebble or crystal in the *kometz*, why does it frame the problem in terms of lacking (*chaser*) or excess (*yater*)? Why not invoke the more familiar principle of *chatzitza* — interposition — since the foreign object interposes between the *kohen*'s skin and the flour? Rashi offers two possibilities. If the foreign object is at the outer edge of the handful, it would interpose between part of the skin and the flour. If it is in the interior, one might argue that since the flour surrounds it on all sides, it effectively divides the handful into separate units. Yet this too is not entirely satisfying — a single point of interposition amidst a mass of connected flour seems insufficient to divide the *kometz* into discrete portions. The case of *yater*, excess, is similarly difficult to explain as a *chatzitza* problem, since something overflowing through the fingers is not obviously interposing between the skin and the flour. The *Gemara*'s question, then, is not fully resolved by its own terms.

### Min HaTzad

Rabbi Yirmiya answers: *min hatzad* — the foreign object is at the edges. Rashi explains that after taking the *kometz*, the *kohen* would hold it with the palm facing upward, so as not to spill the flour. The foreign object sits at the very edges of the handful — near the tip of the pinky on one side and near the tip of the index finger on the other — and therefore does not interpose between the skin and the flour in the interior of the hand. This explains why the *mishnah* does not frame the problem as *chatzitza*: positioned at the edges, the foreign object does not technically constitute an interposition between the bulk of the *kometz* and the *kohen*'s hand. The problem is simply that the *kometz* is not full.

## Kemitza Technique

### Abaye Asks Rava

Moving forward, Abaye poses a practical question to Rava: *keitzad kometz* — how exactly is *kemitza* performed? Rava answers simply: *ke-dekamtzi inshi* — the way people normally take a handful. You put your hand in and scoop it up; nothing unusual. This answer, as we will see, turns out to be more nuanced than it first appears.

### Halakhic Fingers

The *Gemara* proceeds to identify the five halakhic fingers by name. The pinky is the *zeret*, because *zeret* denotes the span from the tip of the thumb to the tip of the pinky, which equals approximately two *tfachim*. The ring finger is the *kemitza*, because *kemitza* is performed with three middle fingers and this finger is the outermost of those three. The middle finger is the *amah*, because an *amah* is measured from the elbow to the tip of the middle finger. The index finger is the *etzba*, named for its use in the priestly blood applications — on the *chatat* and on the ear of the *metzora*. The thumb is the *godel*, because the *kohen* places oil on the thumb (*godel*) of the *metzora*. These five fingers thus each carry a halakhic designation tied to a specific ritual function.

### All Fingers Question

The *Gemara* now challenges the implication of Rava's answer. If *ke-dekamtzi inshi* means using all five fingers, that cannot be right — *kemitza* is defined precisely as a three-fingered act, not a five-fingered one. So the *Gemara* clarifies.

### Three Fingers Bent

The *Gemara* explains: *ela le-hashvot* — the point is to smooth and level the handful. The resolution, attributed to Rav Zutra bar Tuvya in the name of Rav, is that the *kohen* bends his three middle fingers (*chofeh shalosh etzba'otav*) until they reach the palm of his hand (*al pas yado*), and then takes the *kemitza*. The actual scooping is performed with these three bent fingers. The thumb and pinky are used not to scoop but to smooth away any flour that extends beyond the three fingers.

### Tanya Nami Hachi

A *braita* confirms this understanding. The Torah says *melo kumtzo* — the fullness of his handful. One might think this means an overflowing, sprouting handful (*mevoratz*), but the Torah also says *be-kumtzo* — with his handful — implying exactly the size of the hand, not more. One might then think that "with his handful" means using only the very fingertips, but *melo* — fullness — requires the complete span of the fingers. How is this achieved? *Chofeh shalosh etzba'otav al pas yado ve-komatz* — he bends his three fingers to his palm and takes the *kemitza*. Full, but not overflowing.

## Baked Minchot

### Smoothing Chunks

A further complication arises with *minchas* that are baked — *machvat u'marcheshet*. Since the Torah requires that baked offerings be broken into pieces (*patot osah p'titim*), the *kohen* is working with chunks rather than loose flour. Getting an exact *kometz* from chunks is considerably harder, since pieces will protrude unevenly. The *Gemara* therefore specifies: *mochek be-gudlo milma'alah u've-etzba ktanah milmata* — he uses his thumb above and his pinky below to smooth the *kometz* level. When working with flour and oil alone, one can take an exact handful without smoothing; with chunks, the thumb and pinky become essential tools for precision.

### Hardest Labor

The *Gemara* then states: *ve-zu hi avodah kashah she'be'mikdash* — this is the hardest *avodah* in the *Mikdash*. At first glance, this seems surprising. The challenge, evidently, lies in achieving an exact measurement — not too much, not too little — given that *yeter ve-chaser pasul*.

### Why Hardest?

The *Gemara* itself reacts with skepticism: *zo hi? Zu ve-tu lo?* This and nothing else is difficult? What about *melikah* — the pinching of the bird's neck from behind with the *kohen*'s fingernail — and what about *chafina* — the double handful of incense taken by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, where the Torah says *melo chofnav*, the fullness of his two handfuls? The difficulty of *chafina* lies precisely in the physical dexterity required: once inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *kohen gadol* holds the incense vessel in the crook of his arm and must maneuver the contents into both cupped hands without using his hands to assist — manipulating it essentially with his elbow alone. The *Gemara* therefore amends the statement: *ela zo hi achat me-avodot kashot she'be-mikdash* — it is one of the hardest, not uniquely so.

## Rav Pappa's Questions

### Fingertips & Sides

Having established the basic mechanics, Rav Pappa proceeds to a series of unresolved questions about whether the process of *kemitza* matters in itself or only its result. He begins: *komatz be-rosh etzba'otav mah?* — what if the *kohen* used only his fingertips, slowly accumulating the full amount without performing a normal scooping motion? Does the unconventional process invalidate the *kemitza*? He further asks about *min ha-tzdadim* — from the sides. One interpretation is that the *kohen* scoops horizontally rather than from above; Rashi suggests an alternative image of forming a tube with the hand and scooping in that manner. He also asks about *mi-mata le-ma'alah* — from below to above: rather than pressing the palm downward onto the *mincha* and scooping up, the *kohen* inserts his hand with the back facing down and scoops upward. In all these cases, the full amount may be present in the hand at the end — but the process deviated from the norm. The *Gemara* leaves all of these as *teiku* — unresolved.

### Chafina Variants

Rav Pappa then turns to *chafina*, the double handful of *Yom Kippur*. The straightforward case — *ke-chofnei inshi*, the normal way one fills two hands — presents no difficulty. But Rav Pappa asks the parallel questions: what if the *kohen gadol* filled only with his fingertips? What if he scooped from the sides — either by angling his hands laterally rather than scooping from above? And what if he filled each hand separately and then brought them together (*kirvam zo etzel zo*)? Is a double handful that was assembled in two stages valid, or must the two hands fill simultaneously as a single act? Again: *teiku*.

### Process vs Result

The underlying question unifying all of Rav Pappa's inquiries is fundamental: does the *halacha* care only about the result — the correct amount of flour or incense in the hand — or does it also impose requirements on the process itself? One might frame it differently: is the specification of *kemitza* and *chafina* purely a means of ensuring the correct quantity, or is there an independent ritual requirement governing the act by which that quantity is obtained? The absence of any recorded ruling leaves this unresolved.

### Body of Cohen

One might ask: why must the *kohen* use his own body at all? Why not fashion a vessel calibrated to his own handbreadth and use that? It could be argued that the Torah specifies the act in relation to the *kohen*'s person — just as *melikah* is performed with the *kohen*'s fingernail — and that the involvement of the *kohen*'s body is itself part of the ritual specification, not merely a practical means of measurement. This does not fully answer the question, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it. What does seem clear from Rav Pappa's inquiries is that at least some degree of concern for process — not merely result — is operative: the *Gemara* treats it as genuinely uncertain whether unconventional techniques are acceptable, rather than dismissing the question as obviously irrelevant.

## Unusual Placements

### Wall Placement

Rav Pappa raises a further creative question: what if the *kometz*, which is sticky with oil, was pressed against the inner wall of the *kli sharet* rather than allowed to fall to the bottom? It was inside the vessel, but suspended against the side rather than resting on the floor. The question is framed as a tension between two principles: on one hand, the requirement may simply be that the *kometz* be *toch ha-kli* — within the vessel — and it is indeed within the vessel. On the other hand, the act of *netina ba-kli* — placing into the vessel — may require normal placement, which would mean allowing the *kometz* to settle at the base. This is again a question of process versus result, now applied to the act of *kiddush kli*. The *Gemara* leaves it as *teiku*.

### Upside-Down Vessel

Mar bar Rav Ashi then extends the inquiry: *hafkhei le-mana ve-davkei le-komitz be-ara de-mana* — what if the *kli* was turned upside down and the *kometz* was stuck to what is now the floor — which is of course the original exterior base? Rashi explains this as a vessel with a lip around its base, so that even inverted it retains a kind of inner space. A simpler reading is that the *kometz* was placed inside the *kli* in its normal orientation but the vessel was then flipped, so the *kometz* adheres to what is now the ceiling. The question again divides into two possibilities: either we require only that the *kometz* be *be-tocho* — within it — in which case this may suffice; or we require *ke-tikuno* — the normal manner of use — which is absent here. The relevance of *derech tashmisho* — the principle that *klei sharet* function as sanctifying vessels only when used in their normal manner — is noted. Placing a *kometz* against the ceiling of an inverted vessel is, almost by definition, not *derech sheirut*. But whether the question pertains to *kiddush kli* or to the act of *hanacha* by the *kohen* is itself a matter of interpretation: arguably the vessel may become sanctified, but the *mincha* may nonetheless be *pesula* because the *kohen*'s act of placing was not performed in an acceptable manner. The *Gemara* offers no resolution.

## Oil Amounts

### Too Much Oil

The *mishnah* continues: *riba shamna* — if there was too much oil — or if the oil was deficient — the *mincha* is invalid. This transitions from the precise requirements of the *kometz* itself to the precise proportions of the *mincha*'s ingredients. Too much oil, too little oil — either way, it is *pasul*. The parallel with the *kometz* is notable: just as the exact amount of flour must be present in the hand, so too the exact proportion of oil must be present in the *mincha*. This reflects the broader principle that since the *mincha* is a manufactured offering, every component must conform to its specified measure. The contrast with animal offerings is striking — a *shelamim* or *olah* requires *shechita*, blood application, and that is essentially the entirety of the process; but the *mincha* requires exact quantities of flour, oil, and frankincense, failure of any one of which may invalidate the entire offering.

### Two Logs Case

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami riba shamna* — what exactly is the case of too much oil? Rabbi Elazar answers: for instance, where one designated two *login* of oil for one *isaron* of *solet*. The normal proportion is one *log* to one *isaron*; he sanctified double. He did not merely add a splash of extra oil by accident — he formally consecrated two *login* to accompany this *mincha*.

### Why Sanctified?

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: why did Rabbi Elazar specify that the extra oil was sanctified? Why not simply say that one added unsanctified oil (*shemen d'chulin*) or oil from another *mincha* (*shemen d'chaverta*)? The implicit assumption behind the challenge is that Rabbi Elazar chose the more difficult case — where both *login* were properly sanctified for this *mincha* — in order to teach that even then it is invalid. The *Gemara* wonders whether Rabbi Elazar believes that unsanctified or external oil would not invalidate, and only sanctified oil does.

### Unsanctified Oil

The *Gemara* tests this reading against the *mincha* of a sinner (*minchat choteh*), about which the Torah states *lo yasim aleha shemen ve-lo yiten aleha levona* — do not place oil or frankincense upon it. What type of oil would invalidate a *minchat choteh*? If we say that only oil sanctified *for this mincha* creates a problem — any oil the *kohen* might sanctify for a *minchat choteh* is inherently meaningless, since the Torah assigns no oil to that offering — then no oil could ever constitute a valid sanctification for it, and therefore no oil could ever technically invalidate it by Rabbi Elazar's reasoning. Unsanctified oil or oil from another *mincha* would also not invalidate, according to the proposed reading. This leads to an absurdity: nothing would ever invalidate a *minchat choteh* through excess oil.

### Both Logs Fit

The *Gemara* therefore reverses course in its reading of Rabbi Elazar. The formulation *lo mibaya* is invoked: Rabbi Elazar was not saying that only sanctified oil invalidates. Of course, if oil that has no place in the *mincha* — unsanctified oil or oil from another *mincha* — is mixed in, it invalidates, precisely because it is fundamentally foreign to this offering. What Rabbi Elazar is teaching is the *chiddush*: even if one sanctified two *login*, so that both are ostensibly "its oil" — *hoil ve-hai chazi leh ve-hai chazi leh*, since either *log* could have been the one used — one might have thought that because neither log is intrinsically inappropriate, using both together is not *pasul*. Rabbi Elazar teaches that it is nonetheless *pasul*, because the proportion is wrong regardless of the propriety of each component.

### Its Oil Language

From where does Rabbi Elazar derive this? Rava points to the language of the *mishnah* itself: *mai irya de-tani riba shamna* — why does the *mishnah* say "too much *of its* oil" (*riba shamna*)? It could have simply said "too much oil" (*riba ha-shemen*). The possessive — *of its oil* — implies that the problem arises specifically when the oil is connected to this *mincha*, i.e., when it was sanctified for it. This confirms that Rabbi Elazar's case is precisely the one involving sanctified oil, and the teaching is that even such oil, when present in excess, invalidates the *mincha*.

## Frankincense Amounts

### Lacking Levona

The *mishnah* states: *chisar levona pesula* — if the frankincense was lacking, the *mincha* is invalid. This establishes that the *levona* must be complete; any deficiency renders the offering unfit.

### One vs Two Seeds

A *braita* is cited that specifies the minimum required. If the *levona* was reduced to a single *koret* — a seed or pod of frankincense — it is *pasul*; but if two *kortin* remain, it is *kasher*. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### One Seed Valid

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: even one *koret* is sufficient. Less than that — a partial pod — is *pasul*, but a whole pod, however small, is valid.

### All Present?

A *braita* appears to contradict Rabbi Shimon's leniency: *kometz ve-levona she-chasar kol she-hu pasul* — if the *levona* is missing any amount at all, it is *pasul*. This seems to demand the full quantity even at the time of burning. The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction by reinterpreting the *braita*: it does not mean that you need the full original quantity of *levona* intact; rather, it means that if the single remaining *koret* is itself incomplete — if you have less than a full pod — it is *pasul*. Once you have at least one whole pod (or two, according to Rabbi Yehuda), the requirement is satisfied.

### Three Positions

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, now presents the three-way dispute in organized form: *gimmel machlokos ba-davar* — three positions in the matter.

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir holds: *kometz be-tchila ve-kometz be-sof* — a full *kometz* worth of *levona* is required at the beginning, and a full *kometz* worth must be burned at the end. This is the position of the *stam mishnah*, attributed to Rabbi Meir: if any *levona* is lacking, the *mincha* is *pasul*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *kometz be-tchila u-shnei kartzin be-sof* — a full *kometz* at the beginning, but only two seeds need to be burned at the end.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon holds: *kometz be-tchila ve-koret echad be-sof* — a full *kometz* at the beginning, and even a single seed suffices at the end.

## Pesuk Interpretations

### Ve-es Kol

All three positions are derived from a single verse: *ve-es kol ha-levonah asher al ha-mincha* — "and all the frankincense that is on the *mincha*" — you shall burn it on the *mizbeach* (Vayikra 6:8). The straightforward reading of this verse appears to support Rabbi Meir.

### Kol = All

Rabbi Meir reads *kol* in its plain sense: all — the entire quantity of *levona* that was designated for this *mincha* must be burned. *Ad d'is lah la-levonah d'ikva bahadi mincha* — until you have offered up everything that was established together with the *mincha*.

### Kol = Any

Rabbi Yehuda reads *kol* differently: *va-afilu chad karei* — even one is called *kol*. The word *kol* can mean "any" rather than "all," as evidenced by the verse *leis shifchaska kol ba-bayis* — "your maidservant has nothing in the house," where *kol* clearly means "any." Under this reading, the verse requires only that some *levona* be burned — any amount — not that the full original quantity be present.

### Et = Add One

But since Rabbi Yehuda reads *kol* as "any," why does he require two seeds rather than one? He derives the additional seed from the prefix *et* in the phrase *ve-es kol ha-levonah*. The *et* is an amplifying particle that adds one unit — thus: any *levona* plus one more, making a minimum of two seeds.

### Et = Nothing

Rabbi Shimon does not accept homiletical derivations from *et* as adding any additional requirement. For him, *kol* means any amount, and *et* adds nothing. Therefore, even a single seed satisfies the requirement.

## Separate vs With

### With Mincha Dispute

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei, again in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, clarifies the scope of the three-way dispute: *machlokes be-levonah ha-ba'ah im ha-mincha* — the debate of Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon applies only to *levona* that accompanies the *mincha* as one of its components. It is specifically in this context that the verse *ve-es kol ha-levonah asher al ha-mincha* applies, and that the question of whether *kol* means "all" or "any" arises.

### Separate Offering

When *levona* is brought as an independent offering — where a person consecrates a *kometz* of frankincense as a *korban* in its own right — all opinions agree: *kometz be-tchila ve-kometz be-sof* — a full *kometz* at the beginning and a full *kometz* must be burned at the end. In such a case, there is no special verse to permit a partial burning.

### Why Different?

The logic underlying this distinction appears to be as follows: when *levona* accompanies a *mincha*, the primary ritual act is the *kemitza* of the flour; the *levona* is necessary for the *mincha*'s validity but is in some sense secondary. The verse *asher al ha-mincha* — "that is on the *mincha*" — opens the possibility that only some part of the *levona* need be burned for the offering to be complete. But when *levona* is the entire offering — when there is no *kemitza* from flour, no oil, nothing but the frankincense itself — the full quantity is what was consecrated, and the full quantity must be offered.

## Showbread Levona

### Two Vessels

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosei in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds a third case: the *levona* brought in the two *bazichin* — the frankincense vessels or spoons — that were placed on the *shulchan* alongside the *lechem ha-panim*. Here, *divrei ha-kol shnei kmatzin be-tchila ve-shnei kmatzin be-sof* — all opinions agree that two full *kematzim* are required at the beginning and two full *kematzim* must be burned at the end. Each of the two vessels holds a *kometz*, and the total of two *kematzim* must be present at the outset and must be fully burned when the vessels are removed.

### All Agree

The *Gemara* questions why this case needs to be stated — it seems obvious. If there is no special verse to permit a lesser amount, surely the full quantity is required, as in the case of *levona* brought as an independent offering. Why would anyone think otherwise?

### Bread Exception

The answer is that one might have argued: since the *bazichin* come together with the bread — the *lechem ha-panim* — and the bread is itself a kind of *mincha*, perhaps the *levona* in the *bazichin* falls under the category of *levona ha-ba'ah im ha-mincha*, and the lenient readings of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon would apply. If so, perhaps only one or two seeds need be burned.

### No Kometz

The *Gemara* rejects this: the verse specifies *levona asher al ha-mincha* in the context of an offering from which a *kometz* of flour is taken. The *lechem ha-panim* involves no *kemitza*; there is no flour *kometz* taken from the showbread. Since the *levona* of the *bazichin* stands alone — there is no *kometz* component — it is treated like an independent offering of *levona*, and the full quantity must be burned.

## Excess Levona

### Mishnah Silence

The *mishnah* mentions *chisar levona pesula* — insufficient *levona* invalidates — but says nothing about excess *levona*. Does this silence imply that too much *levona* is permissible?

### Braita Says Invalid

A *braita* states explicitly that excess *levona* is also *pasul*. The *mishnah*'s silence on the point is therefore not to be read as a leniency.

### Two Handfuls

Rami bar Chama identifies the case: one designated two *kematzim* worth of *levona* for a *mincha* that requires only one. The question is when this excess becomes fixed (*hukba*) as part of the *mincha* — and correspondingly, when it is too late to remedy the situation.

### Moment of Fixing

The critical moment is the *kemitza*. It is the act of the *kohen* taking the handful that establishes what the *mincha* consists of. Before that act, the *levona* has been designated but not yet definitionally attached.

### Before Kemitza

If one of the two *kematzim* of *levona* was lost or destroyed before the *kemitza* was performed, *lo hukba* — it was not established as part of the *mincha*. The remaining single *kometz* is the correct amount, and the *mincha* is valid. The scenario envisioned is apparently one where the *levona* had been designated but not yet mixed into the *mincha* — the two units are separate, and losing one before the *kemitza* leaves the offering unimpaired.

### After Kemitza

If, however, both *kematzim* were present at the moment the *kohen* performed the *kemitza*, then *hukbe-u* — they were established together with the *mincha* at that moment — and the *mincha* is *pasul* because the *levona* was in excess at the defining instant. This explains the *mishnah*'s silence: there are scenarios where excess *levona* was designated but was lost before *kemitza*, leaving the *mincha* valid. The *mishnah* does not need to declare excess *levona* invalid in those cases.

## Showbread Excess

### Four Handfuls

Rami bar Chama poses a parallel question regarding the *bazichin* of the *lechem ha-panim*. The two vessels each require one *kometz* of *levona* — two *kematzim* in total. What if four *kematzim* were designated? And then two of the four were lost?

### Before Removal

The parallel to the *mincha* case is: if two of the four *kematzim* were lost before *siluk ha-bazichin* — before the removal of the *bazichin* from the *shulchan* — then the excess was not yet established, and the two remaining *kematzim* are the correct amount, leaving the offering valid.

### After Removal

If all four were present until the moment of *siluk ha-bazichin*, then at that moment the excess was established — *hukbe-u* — and the offering is *pasul*.

### Why Repeat?

The *Gemara* asks: *ha tu lama li?* — why is this case necessary? Is it not the same as the *mincha* case? The *Gemara* responds that one might have thought there was a reason to distinguish: since the *bazichin* have a fixed time for their removal (*zeman le-farka*), one might have argued that the mere arrival of that time — even before the actual act of removal — already constitutes a kind of constructive removal, fixing the *levona* at that point regardless of what remained on the *shulchan*. If so, the question of whether excess was present "at the moment of removal" would effectively mean "at the moment the time for removal arrived." The case therefore teaches that no — it is the actual physical act of *siluk*, not merely the arrival of the time, that determines whether the excess has become fixed. The defining moment is the act, not the clock.
