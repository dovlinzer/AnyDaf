# Sanhedrin 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbis' Hierarchy

### Four Executions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת נִמְסְרוּ לְבֵית דִּין: סְקִילָה, שְׂרֵיפָה, הֶרֶג, וָחֶנֶק. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׂרֵיפָה, סְקִילָה, חֶנֶק, וָהֶרֶג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Four types of** the **death** penalty **were given over to** the **court,** with which those who committed certain transgressions are executed. They are, in descending order of severity: **Stoning, burning, killing** by decapitation, **and strangulation. Rabbi Shimon says:** They are, in descending order of severity: **Burning, stoning, strangulation, and killing.**

Today's daf picks up at the bottom of 49b, three lines from the end, where the *Mishnah* establishes the order of the four capital punishments: *sekila*, *sereifa*, *hereg*, *chenek*. Rabbi Shimon, notably, agrees to the two pairs—*sekila* and *sereifa* belonging together as the weightier pair, *hereg* and *chenek* as the lighter—but he reverses the order within each pair. Instead of *sekila* then *sereifa*, he has *sereifa* then *sekila*; instead of *hereg* then *chenek*, he has *chenek* then *hereg*. The entirety of today's *daf* is devoted to working out how the *rabanan* arrive at their order and how Rabbi Shimon arrives at his.

### Sekila > Sereifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מִשְּׂרֵיפָה, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לִמְגַדֵּף וּלְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן פּוֹשֵׁט יָדוֹ בָּעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Stoning** is considered more **severe than burning, as** stoning **is meted out to one who blasphemes,** i.e., one who curses God, **and to one who worships idols.** For **what** reason is the **severity** of these transgressions considered greater than others? **Because** the transgressor **undermines the fundamental** tenets of Judaism.

The *Gemara* opens by establishing why *sekila* is the weightiest of the four. *Sekila chamura misereifa*—*sekila* is weightier than *sereifa*—because it is the punishment prescribed for the *megadef* (blasphemer) and the *oveid avodah zarah* (idolater). And what makes those sins so grave? *Shekein posheit yado b'ikar*—they represent a direct rebellion against God Himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לְבַת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן מְחַלֶּלֶת אֶת אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary; burning** is more **severe** than stoning, **as it is meted out to a priest's daughter who committed adultery** (see Leviticus 21:9). **And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater than others? **Because** through her sin **she profanes** not only herself but **her father** as well.

The *Gemara* immediately raises the counterargument: *adaraba*, perhaps *sereifa* is the weightier punishment, since it is the punishment given to a *bas kohen* who commits adultery, as the verse states, *ubas kohen ki tachel lehiznos es aviha hi mechalleles ba'eish tisareif*—a *bas kohen* who desecrates herself through fornication desecrates her father; she shall be burned in fire. And what makes that weighty? *Shekein mechalleles es aviha*—she brings disgrace upon her father's household, which adds a sin against persons on top of the sin against God.

### Arusa/Nesuah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן: נְשׂוּאָה יוֹצְאָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה, וְלֹא אֲרוּסָה. וּמִדְּאַפְּקַהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲרוּסָה בִּסְקִילָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The Rabbis hold** that only the **married** daughter of a priest who committed adultery **is singled out** from all those who commit adultery, **for burning, but not** one who is **betrothed,** as a betrothed woman who committed adultery, whether or not she is the daughter of a priest, is executed by stoning. **And since the Merciful One singles out a betrothed woman** who is not married from all married women who commit adultery, to be executed **by stoning, conclude from it** that **stoning** is more **severe** than burning.

Here the *Gemara* introduces the core structural argument, and it is important to understand it precisely. *Kasvari rabanan*: the *rabanan* hold that the verse concerning the *bas kohen* applies only to the *nesuah* (fully married woman) and not to the *arusah* (betrothed woman). The starting point—what everyone agrees upon independently of the *bas kohen* case—is that for an ordinary *bas Yisrael*, a *nesuah* who commits adultery receives *chenek*, while an *arusah* who commits adultery receives *sekila*. This is explicit in the *pesukim*.

Then the *Gemara* says: since the *Torah* took the *arusah bas Yisrael* out of the *sekila* category and placed her into *sereifa* — *midei d'aftar rachmana arusa b'skila, shma mina skila chamura* — you learn that *sekila* is weightier. But Rashi stops the *Gemara* here and says clearly: this is not a proof. The *Gemara* sounds as though it is deriving something from the *pesukim*, but it is not. What is actually happening is that the *rabanan* began with the assumption that *sekila* is the weightiest punishment — because it is the punishment for the most severe sins, those against God directly — and then *read* that assumption into the *pesukim*, and now they are reading it back out. The reason *sekila* is assumed to be weightier is what was stated on the previous *amud*: the idolater and the blasphemer receive *sekila*, and those have to be the gravest sins. The *pesuk* passage is simply how the *rabanan* manage the *pesukim* to be consistent with their system; it proves nothing on its own.

Stated plainly: if *sereifa* applies to the *nesuah* and *sekila* is assumed to apply still to the *arusah* — an assumption the *rabanan* impose, not derive — then since the *arusah* is considered the graver case (because she brings shame to her father's house, as the *pesukim* frame it in terms of *beis aviha*), the punishment applied to the *arusah* must be the graver punishment, which confirms *sekila* over *sereifa*. But the assumption that the *arusah* is worse, and the reading of the *pas kohen* verse as limited to the *nesuah*, are both built into their starting position, not derived from it. Rashi says the *Gemara* is simply not being precise here.

On the question of whether the actual physical experience of the punishment determines the ranking: the *Gemara* never assumes this. Even if burning alive seems more painful in practice, the *Gemara*'s ranking is based entirely on the gravity of the sin for which the punishment is prescribed, not on the subjective experience of the condemned. It is worth noting that the manner of *sereifa* as practiced by *beis din* — pouring molten lead down the throat — while certainly not pleasant, is considerably more humane than burning alive.

### Sekila > Sayif

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מִסַּיִיף, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לִמְגַדֵּף וּלְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? כְּדַאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Stoning** is considered more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword, as it is meted out to one who blasphemes and to one who worships idols. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of these transgressions considered greater? **As we said,** it is because the transgressors undermine the fundamental tenets of Judaism.

Having established the position of *sekila* relative to *sereifa*, the *Gemara* turns to proving *sekila* is weightier than *sayif* (death by the sword). Again, *sekila* is the punishment for the *megadef* and the *oveid avodah zarah*, which represents *posheit yado b'ikar*. But the *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge: *adaraba*, perhaps *sayif* is weightier — *shekein nitan l'anshei ir ha'nidachat*, it is the punishment used to destroy the entire city that committed *avodah zarah*, and moreover, *shekein mamonam avad*, their property is also destroyed, which represents an additional severity of the punishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: אֵיזֶה כֹּחַ מְרוּבֶּה, כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ אוֹ כֹּחַ הַנִּידָּח? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ. וְתַנְיָא: מַדִּיחֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת – בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In response to this objection, **you** should **say: The severity of which** transgression **is greater: The severity** of the transgression **of the one who subverts** the city, inciting them to sin, **or the severity** of the transgression **of the subverted? You must say** that **the severity of** the transgression of **the subverter** is greater. **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The subverters of an idolatrous city** are executed **by stoning.** Evidently, stoning is a more severe type of execution than decapitation.

The *Gemara* resolves this by asking: *eizeh koach merubeh, koach ha'madiach o koach ha'nidach* — which is the weightier force, the one who incites the city to *avodah zarah* or the people who were incited? Obviously the inciter bears greater responsibility. And a *beraitah* teaches that the *madiach ir ha'nidachat* — the one who subverts the idolatrous city — receives *sekila*. Since both cases involve *avodah zarah* but the inciter (who receives *sekila*) is the more culpable party, *sekila* is confirmed as the weightier punishment.

### Sekila > Chenek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מֵחֶנֶק, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לִמְגַדֵּף וּלְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? כְּדַאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Stoning** is considered more **severe than strangulation, as it is meted out to one who blasphemes and to one who worships idols. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of these transgressions considered greater? **As we said,** it is because the transgressors undermine the fundamental tenets of Judaism.

The *Gemara* then addresses *sekila*'s superiority over *chenek* (strangulation). *Sekila* is the punishment for the *megadef* and the *oveid avodah zarah*, representing *posheit yado b'ikar*. The counterargument: *chenek chamur*, since *chenek* is the punishment for one who smites a parent (*makeh aviv ve'imo*), and what makes that weighty is *hukash kevodam li'kvod ha'makom* — the honor of parents is likened to the honor of God. Tosafos raises the point that the honor of parents cannot actually exceed the honor of God, suggesting at most they could be equal, which would mean these two punishments might be equivalent — but the *Gemara* does not pursue that line.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִכְּלַל נְשׂוּאָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵחֶנֶק לִסְקִילָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since the Merciful One singles out** the case of **a betrothed Jewish woman from the category of a married Jewish woman,** changing the punishment of a betrothed Jewish woman who committed adultery **from strangulation to stoning, conclude from it** that **stoning** is more **severe.**

The resolution is the same structural move deployed earlier: since an *arusah bas Yisrael* receives *sekila* and a *nesuah bas Yisrael* receives *chenek*, and the *arusah* is assumed to represent the graver case because of the *beis aviha* context, *sekila* is confirmed as weightier than *chenek*. The *Gemara* reads this out of the *pesukim* in the same manner as before.

### Gezeira Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר "אָבִיהָ" בִּסְקִילָה, וְנֶאֱמַר "אָבִיהָ" בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. מָה "אָבִיהָ" הָאָמוּר בִּסְקִילָה – סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מִסַּיִיף, אַף "אָבִיהָ" הָאָמוּר בִּשְׂרֵיפָה – שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה מִסַּיִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The severity of burning is derived by means of a verbal analogy. The term **"her father" is stated with regard to stoning,** in the verse: "And the men of her city shall stone her with stones and she shall die, because she has done a depraved thing in Israel, to play the harlot in the house of her father" (Deuteronomy 22:21), **and** the term **"her father" is stated with regard to burning,** in the verse: "And the daughter of a priest, when she profanes herself by playing the harlot, she profanes her father; she shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 21:9). Therefore, **just as** concerning the term **"her father" that is stated with regard to stoning** it is established that **stoning** is more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword, so too,** concerning the term **"her father" that is stated with regard to burning,** it may be established that **burning** is more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword.**

Having established that *sekila* is weightier than all three others, the *Gemara* must now show that *sereifa* is weightier than *sayif* and *chenek*. The argument for *sereifa* over *sayif* runs as follows. *Sereifa* is given to a *bas kohen* who commits adultery, which is weighty because she desecrates her father's household. Against this: *sayif* is used to wipe out an *ir ha'nidachat*, and moreover their property is destroyed. The *Gemara* resolves this through a *gezeira shava*: the word *aviha* appears in the verse concerning the *arusa* who receives *sekila* (*liznos beis aviha*) and also in the verse concerning the *bas kohen* who receives *sereifa* (*es aviha hi mechalleles*). The same way we established that *sekila* is weightier than *sayif* — via the *aviha* context — we now apply that link to *sereifa*: *mah aviha ha'amur bi'skila, skila chamur mi'sayif; af aviha ha'amur bi'sereifa, sereifa chamur mi'sayif*. Whatever *sekila* established through *aviha*, *sereifa* inherits through the parallel use of the same word.

### Sereifa Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִנְשׂוּאָה בַּת כֹּהֵן מִכְּלַל נְשׂוּאָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵחֶנֶק לִשְׂרֵיפָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since the Merciful One singles out** the case of **the married daughter of a priest** who committed adultery **from the category of a married Jewish woman** who committed adultery, changing her punishment **from** execution by **strangulation to** execution by **burning, conclude from it** that **burning** is more **severe** than strangulation.

The proof that *sereifa* is weightier than *chenek* is more straightforward. Everyone agrees — both *rabanan* and Rabbi Shimon — that a *nesuah bas kohen* receives *sereifa* rather than the *chenek* that a *nesuah bas Yisrael* receives. This directly shows that *sereifa* is a more severe punishment than *chenek*: *midei d'amar rachmana l'nesuah bas kohen, miklal nesuah bas Yisrael me'chenek l'sereifa, shma mina sereifa chamura*. Since taking a *nesuah bas kohen* out of the *chenek* category and into the *sereifa* category represents an escalation, *sereifa* is demonstrably weightier than *chenek*. There is no controversy here; both positions agree on this point.

### Sayif > Chenek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַיִיף חָמוּר מֵחֶנֶק, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְאַנְשֵׁי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן מָמוֹנָם אָבֵד. אַדְּרַבָּה: חֶנֶק חָמוּר שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְמַכֵּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן הוּקַּשׁ כּוּ׳. אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, פּוֹשֵׁט יָדוֹ בָּעִיקָּר עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** Decapitation by the **sword** is considered more **severe than strangulation, as it is meted out to the people of an idolatrous city. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this case considered greater? **Because their property is destroyed** as well. The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary; strangulation** is more **severe, as it is meted out to one who wounds his father or his mother. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **Because** the honor of one's parents **is compared** to the honor of the Omnipresent. The Gemara answers: **Nevertheless,** the transgression of **one who undermines the fundamental** tenets of Judaism is **more** severe, as he defiles the honor of the Omnipresent Himself, so his punishment must certainly be the most severe.

Finally, the *Gemara* establishes that *sayif* is weightier than *chenek*. The opening move: *sayif* is the punishment for the residents of an *ir ha'nidachat*, which involves *avodah zarah* and the destruction of property — fairly weighty. Against this: *chenek* is for *makeh aviv ve'imo*, and the honor of parents is *hukash li'kvod ha'makom*. The resolution: *afilu hachi, posheit yado b'ikar adif* — even granting that comparison, a sin directly against God must outweigh a sin against parents. Since the *ir ha'nidachat* involves *avodah zarah*, which is *posheit yado b'ikar*, *sayif* is weightier than *chenek*. This is the same logic used throughout — sins against God directly occupy a categorically higher level — though one might note a certain circularity in the argument: this logic could not be invoked earlier to show that *sereifa* (given to the *bas kohen*) was secondary to *sekila*, and yet it is invoked here. The *Gemara* is working backwards from its established hierarchy and finding arguments to support each step.

## Rabbi Shimon's Order

### Bat Kohen Key

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה מִסְּקִילָה, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לְבַת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן מְחַלֶּלֶת אֶת אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says** that the order of severity is burning, stoning, strangulation, and killing. The Gemara explains the basis for his opinion. **Burning** is considered more **severe than stoning, as** burning **is meted out to a priest's daughter who committed adultery. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater than the others? **Because she profanes** both herself and **her father.**

Turning now to Rabbi Shimon's approach, the method is essentially the mirror image. Rabbi Shimon's point of departure is the *bas kohen*, and his key claim is that *sereifa* applies to the *bas kohen* whether she is a *nesuah* or an *arusah* — this is the plain reading of the *pesukim*. For the *rabanan*, limiting *sereifa* to the *nesuah* was necessary to preserve *sekila* as the weightiest punishment. For Rabbi Shimon, that limitation is artificial. The *Torah* says *sereifa* for a *bas kohen* and means it for all cases of a *bas kohen*. This is not merely an exegetical preference; it is, for Rabbi Shimon, the natural reading. And from that reading, the entire hierarchy flows differently.

### Sereifa > Sekila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לִמְגַדֵּף וּלְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן פּוֹשֵׁט יָדוֹ בָּעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary; stoning** is more **severe, as it is meted out to one who blasphemes and to one who worships idols. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of these transgressions greater? **Because** the transgressor **undermines the fundamental** tenets of Judaism.

*Sereifa chamura mi'skila* — *sereifa* is weightier than *sekila* — because *sereifa* is the punishment of a *bas kohen* who commits adultery, and what makes it weighty is *mechalleles es aviha*, she desecrates her father's household. The counterargument: *adaraba, skila chamura*, *sekila* is for the *megadef* and the *oveid avodah zarah*, and what is weighty about that is *posheit yado b'ikar*, direct rebellion against God — which logically seems like a stronger claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אַחַת אֲרוּסָה וְאַחַת נְשׂוּאָה יָצְאָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה. וּמִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲרוּסָה בַּת כֹּהֵן מִכְּלַל אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִסְּקִילָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he says** that the daughter of a priest, **whether** she is **betrothed or married, is singled out for burning. And since the Merciful One singles out** the case of **the betrothed daughter of a priest from the category of a betrothed Jewish woman,** changing her punishment **from** execution by **stoning to** execution by **burning, conclude from it** that **burning** is more **severe** than stoning, as the punishment for a priest's daughter who committed adultery must certainly be more severe than that of the daughter of a non-priest.

Rabbi Shimon's answer is anchored in the *pesukim*: since both the *arusah* and the *nesuah* who are *bas kohen* go to *sereifa* — *de'amar achas arusah ve'achas nesuah yatze'u l'sereifa* — and since an *arusah bas Yisrael* ordinarily receives *sekila*, the *Torah* is explicitly taking a *bas kohen arusah* out of the *sekila* category and upgrading her to *sereifa*. *Ve'dhaft rachmana l'arusah bas kohen miklal arusah bas Yisrael mi'skila l'sereifa, shema mina sereifa chamura.* The *Torah* moved her upward, which means *sereifa* is the higher punishment.

It is worth pausing on the logical implication that is occasionally noted in this discussion. If one uses the hierarchy of capital punishments to infer the relative severity of sins, Rabbi Shimon's position would seem to imply that adultery by a *bas kohen* is a more severe sin than *avodah zarah*. The honest answer is that using the death-penalty hierarchy to make such inferences about sin-ranking is complicated, partly because the *Gemara* itself derives some rankings from assumptions about sin-severity and others from external textual proofs, and the results do not always yield a neat system. The practical purpose of the ranking — *nidon b'chamura*, giving the person the weightier of two applicable punishments — does not require that every ranking of punishments map onto a ranking of sins that makes intuitive sense.

### Sereifa Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה מֵחֶנֶק, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לְבַת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? כְּדַאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain the order of severity according to Rabbi Shimon. **Burning** is considered more **severe than strangulation, as it is meted out to a priest's daughter who committed adultery. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **As we said,** it is because she profanes both herself and her father.

Having established that *sereifa* is the weightiest, Rabbi Shimon must show it is weightier than *chenek* and *sayif* as well. *Sereifa chamura mi'chenek*: the opening claim is that *sereifa* goes to a *bas kohen* and is therefore weighty. The challenge: *chenek* goes to *makeh aviv ve'imo*, and *hukash kevodam li'kvod ha'makom*. The resolution: if *sereifa* is weightier than *sekila*, and *sekila* is weightier than *chenek* based on the *arusah* versus *nesuah* argument — the same proof the *rabanan* themselves use — then *sereifa* is certainly weightier than *chenek*. The proof is direct: *mi'dhaft rachmana l'nesuah bas kohen miklal nesuah bas Yisrael me'chenek l'sereifa, shema mina sereifa chamura*. A *nesuah bas kohen* is taken out of *chenek* and placed into *sereifa*, demonstrating *sereifa* is the higher punishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: חֶנֶק חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְמַכֵּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן הוּקַּשׁ כְּבוֹדָם לִכְבוֹד הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary; strangulation** is more **severe, as it is meted out to one who wounds his father or his mother. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **Because the honor of** one's parents **is compared to the honor of the Omnipresent.**

For *sereifa* over *sayif*: the initial claim is *sereifa* goes to a *bas kohen*. The challenge: *sayif* wipes out an *ir ha'nidachat*, and their property is destroyed. The resolution involves the *madiach* and *nidach* dynamic — which is weightier, the one who incites the city or the people who are incited? Obviously the inciter. For Rabbi Shimon, the *madiach ir ha'nidachat* receives *chenek* (as opposed to the *rabanan* who say *sekila*), but the principle still applies: the *madiach* receives something weightier than the *ir ha'nidachat* residents who receive *sayif*. Therefore *chenek* is weightier than *sayif*. And since *sereifa* is already established as weightier than *chenek*, by transitivity — *sereifa* is weightier than *sayif* as well.

### Sekila > Sayif

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מִסַּיִיף, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לַמְגַדֵּף כּוּ׳. אַדְּרַבָּה: סַיִיף חָמוּר שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְאַנְשֵׁי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן מָמוֹנָם אָבֵד. אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי אֵיזֶה כֹּחַ מְרוּבֶּה, כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ אוֹ כֹּחַ הַנִּידָּח? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה חֶנֶק שֶׁחָמוּר מִסַּיִיף, סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מִמֶּנּוּ; סַיִיף הַקַּל – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Stoning** is considered more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword, as it is meted out to one who blasphemes** and to one who worships idols. The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary;** decapitation by the **sword** is more **severe, as it is meted out to the people of an idolatrous city. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this case considered greater? **Because their property is destroyed** as well. The Gemara answers that in response to this objection **you** should **say: But the severity of which** transgression **is greater: The severity** of the transgression **of the subverter, or the severity** of the transgression **of the subverted? You must say** that **the severity** of the transgression **of the subverter** is greater. Since those who subverted the people of an idolatrous city are executed by stoning, this punishment is evidently more severe than decapitation. **And** it is inferred ***a fortiori*** that **if** it is so that concerning **strangulation, which** is more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword,** nevertheless **stoning is** more **severe than it,** is it **not all the more so** obvious that stoning is more severe than decapitation by the **sword, which is** a more **lenient** type of execution compared to strangulation?

Rabbi Shimon must now rank the remaining punishments. *Sekila chamura mi'sayif*: the opening claim is that *sekila* goes to the *megadef* and *oveid avodah zarah*, so it must be weighty. The challenge: *sayif* goes to the *anshei ir ha'nidachat* and their property is destroyed. Resolution: which is weightier, the *madiach* or the *nidach*? The *madiach* is weightier, and Rabbi Shimon says the *madiach ir ha'nidachat* receives *chenek*. So *chenek* is weightier than *sayif*. And since *sekila* is weightier than *chenek* — established by the *arusah* versus *nesuah* argument — *sekila* is certainly weightier than *sayif* as well. *Kal va'chomer: umah chenek she'chamur mi'sayif, skila chamura heimenu; sayif ha'kal, lo kol sheken.*

### Sekila > Chenek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה מֵחֶנֶק, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתְּנָה לִמְגַדֵּף וּלְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? כְּדַאֲמַרַן. אַדְּרַבָּה: חֶנֶק חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְמַכֵּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן הוּקַּשׁ כּוּ׳. מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִכְּלַל נְשׂוּאָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵחֶנֶק לִסְקִילָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Stoning** is considered more **severe than strangulation, as it is meted out to one who blasphemes and to one who worships idols. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of these transgressions considered greater? **As we said,** it is because the transgressors undermine the fundamental tenets of Judaism. The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary; strangulation** is more **severe, as it is meted out to one who wounds his father or his mother. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **Because** the honor of one's parents **is compared** to the honor of the Omnipresent. The Gemara answers: **Since the Merciful One singles out** the case of **the betrothed daughter of a non-priest** who committed adultery **from the category of a married Jewish woman** who committed adultery, changing her punishment **from** execution by **strangulation to** execution by **stoning, conclude from it** that **stoning** is more **severe.**

*Sekila chamura mi'chenek*: the opening claim is that *sekila* goes to the *megadef* and *oveid avodah zarah*. The challenge: *chenek* goes to *makeh aviv ve'imo*, and *hukash kevodam li'kvod ha'makom*. But this is an easy resolution: *mi'dhaft rachmana l'arusah bas Yisrael miklal nesuah bas Yisrael me'chenek l'skila, shma mina skila chamura*. The *Torah* takes an *arusah bas Yisrael* out of *chenek* (the punishment of the *nesuah*) and gives her *sekila*, showing *sekila* is the weightier punishment.

### Chenek > Hereg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶנֶק חָמוּר מִסַּיִיף, שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְמַכֵּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? כְּדַאֲמַרַן. אַדְּרַבָּה: סַיִיף חָמוּר שֶׁכֵּן נִיתַּן לְאַנְשֵׁי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? שֶׁכֵּן מָמוֹנָם אָבֵד. אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי אֵיזֶה כֹּחַ מְרוּבֶּה, כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ אוֹ כֹּחַ הַנִּידָּח? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּחַ הַמַּדִּיחַ. וְתַנְיָא: מַדִּיחֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Strangulation** is more **severe than** decapitation by the **sword, as it is meted out to one who wounds his father or his mother. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **As we said,** because the honor of one's parents is compared to the honor of the Omnipresent. The Gemara raises an objection: **On the contrary;** decapitation by the **sword** is more **severe, as it is meted out to the people of an idolatrous city. And** for **what** reason is the **severity** of this transgression considered greater? **Because their property is destroyed** as well. The Gemara answers that **you** should **say** in response to this objection: **But the severity of which** transgression **is greater: The severity** of the transgression **of the subverter, or the severity** of the transgression **of the subverted? You must say** that **the severity** of the transgression **of the subverter** is greater, **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The subverters of an idolatrous city** are executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says: By strangulation.** Therefore, according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, it is clear that strangulation is a more severe type of capital punishment than decapitation.

The final step in Rabbi Shimon's system is establishing that *chenek* is weightier than *hereg* (death by the sword). This is somewhat surprising at first glance — one might have expected *hereg* to rank above *chenek* — but Rabbi Shimon's basis is the *madiach ir ha'nidachat*. The *madiach* receives something weightier than the ordinary residents of the *ir ha'nidachat*. The *nidachim* receive *sayif*; the *madiach* receives *chenek* according to Rabbi Shimon. Since the inciter must receive the weightier punishment, *chenek* is weightier than *sayif*. The *Gemara* then cites the *beraitah* explicitly: *madiach ir ha'nidachat b'skila* — according to the *rabanan*, the inciter receives *sekila* — *Rabbi Shimon omer b'chenek*, Rabbi Shimon says he receives *chenek*.

So Rabbi Shimon's complete hierarchy is *sereifa*, *sekila*, *chenek*, *hereg*. In practice, Rabbi Shimon's system is somewhat easier to derive than the *rabanan*'s. Once he places *sereifa* at the top, the *bas kohen* argument yields *sereifa* over the other three. Once *sekila* is established above *chenek* via the *arusah/nesuah* proof, most of the system is in place. The one surprising element — *chenek* above *hereg* — is carried by the *madiach/nidach* dynamic, which is the same structure the *rabanan* use to establish *sayif* above *chenek*, only with different punishments assigned to each role.

## Application & Sources

### Naara Me'orasah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְגְּלָא בְּפוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה בַּת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּינְּתָה – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. זִינְּתָה מֵאָבִיהָ – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan was wont to say** the following *baraita*: A **betrothed young woman** who is **the daughter of a priest** and **who committed adultery** is executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says:** She is executed **by burning.** A betrothed young woman who is the daughter of a non-priest and who **engaged in intercourse with her father** is executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says: By burning.**

The *Gemara* introduces a famous teaching cited as a pearl in the mouth of Rav Yochanan — *margla b'pumei d'Rav Yochanan* — meaning, Rashi explains, that it was Rav Yochanan's own formulation rather than a received tradition from a *mishnah* or *beraitah*. The case: *na'arah me'orasah bas Kohen she'zinsa b'skila* — a *na'arah me'orasah* who is also a *bas kohen* and who commits adultery receives *sekila*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *b'sereifa*. This is precisely the crux of their dispute given everything above. For the *rabanan*, *sekila* remains the weightier punishment and applies even to the *arusah bas kohen*; for Rabbi Shimon, every *bas kohen* — whether *nesuah* or *arusah* — receives *sereifa*, which is for him the weightier punishment.

### Incest Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְרַבָּנַן – נְשׂוּאָה יָצְאָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה, וְלֹא אֲרוּסָה. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – אַחַת אֲרוּסָה וְאַחַת נְשׂוּאָה יָצְאָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה. וְטַעְמָא מַאי? מִשּׁוּם דִּלְרַבָּנַן סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does** this *baraita* **teach us?** The Gemara answers: It teaches us that **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, the married** daughter of a priest **is singled out for burning, and not a betrothed** one, who is executed by stoning. **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** the daughter of a priest, **whether** she is **betrothed or married, is singled out for burning. And what is the reason** for their respective opinions with regard to the punishment of the daughter of a priest? It is **because according to the Rabbis stoning** is more **severe** than burning, whereas **according to Rabbi Shimon burning** is more **severe.**

The *Gemara* then raises a second variant: what if this *na'arah me'orasah* — an ordinary *bas Yisrael*, not a *bas kohen* — committed her act of adultery specifically with her own father? The sin of intercourse between a father and daughter carries the punishment of *sereifa*. So she is a *na'arah me'orasah*, who by universal agreement receives *sekila*, but the particular act she committed is one for which the punishment is *sereifa*. She has, in effect, incurred two applicable punishments. According to Rabbi Shimon, who holds *sereifa* is weightier, she receives *sereifa*. According to the *rabanan*, who hold *sekila* is weightier, she receives *sekila*. The logic is parallel to the *bas kohen* case: for Rabbi Shimon, *sereifa* takes over whenever it applies because it is the heavier penalty; for the *rabanan*, *sekila* always remains the heavier penalty.

### Nifka Minah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לְמִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין, נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In addition to the case of the priest's daughter who committed adultery, there is a practical **difference** between these two opinions, which is **that one who was sentenced to two** different **court**-imposed **death** penalties for two sins he committed **is punished with the** more **severe** of the two, and these *tanna'im* disagree as to which type of death penalty is more severe.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the practical *nifka minah* of all of this? The answer: *nafka mina l'mi shenischayeiv shtei misos beis din, nidon b'chamura* — whenever a person has incurred two death penalties, the rule is that they receive the weightier one. That is the operative halachic principle that makes the entire ranking matter. The debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *rabanan* about which punishment is weightier translates directly into which punishment is administered when a person's sins make two punishments applicable simultaneously. The conclusion the *Gemara* draws is explicit: *mishum d'le'rabanan skila chamura, u'le'Rabbi Shimon sereifa chamura*.

### Braitah Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, שְׁנֵי כְּלָלוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּבַת כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the source of the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that a priest's betrothed daughter who committed adultery is executed by stoning? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: Two general** *halakhot* **are stated** in the Torah **with regard to the daughter of a priest,** one rendering the betrothed daughter of a priest who committed adultery liable to be executed by stoning, and the other rendering the married daughter of a priest who committed adultery liable to be executed by strangulation.

The *Gemara* now asks: what is the source for Rabbi Shimon's position? *D'tanya, Rabbi Shimon omer: shtei kelalos ne'emru b'vas kohen* — two distinct categories are stated regarding a *bas kohen*, the *nesuah* and the *arusah*. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: do only *bas kohen* cases have these two categories? A *bas Yisrael* also has the distinction of *nesuah* receiving *chenek* and *arusah* receiving *sekila*! The *Gemara* explains: the point of departure — *ima af b'vas kohen* — is that a *bas kohen* begins from the same baseline as a *bas Yisrael*, with *chenek* for the *nesuah* and *sekila* for the *arusah*. *Ve'hotzia hakatuv nesuah miklal nesuah ve'arusah miklal arusa* — the *Torah* then removed the *bas kohen* from both of those categories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב נְשׂוּאָה מִכְּלַל נְשׂוּאָה, וַאֲרוּסָה מִכְּלַל אֲרוּסָה. מָה כְּשֶׁהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב נְשׂוּאָה מִכְּלַל נְשׂוּאָה לְהַחְמִיר, אַף כְּשֶׁהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב אֲרוּסָה מִכְּלַל אֲרוּסָה לְהַחֲמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The verse: "And the daughter of a priest, when she profanes herself by playing the harlot, she profanes her father; she shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 21:9), is stated with regard to both a betrothed woman and a married woman. **And the verse** thereby **singles out** the **married** daughter of a priest **from the category of** an ordinary **married woman,** whose punishment is execution by strangulation, **and** it singles out the **betrothed** daughter of a priest **from the category of** an ordinary **betrothed woman,** who is executed by stoning. Therefore, **just as when the verse singles out** the **married** daughter of a priest **from the category of a married woman** it is **to render** her punishment more **severe, so too, when the verse singles out** the **betrothed** daughter of a priest **from the category of a betrothed woman** it is **to render** her punishment more **severe.** This indicates that burning is a more severe type of capital punishment than stoning.

### Pesuk Reading

The mechanism of the removal is significant. The *Torah*'s statement about the *bas kohen* takes the *nesuah bas kohen* out of the *chenek* category applicable to a *nesuah bas Yisrael* and places her into *sereifa*. It simultaneously takes the *arusah bas kohen* out of the *sekila* category applicable to an *arusah bas Yisrael* and places her also into *sereifa*. *Mah keshehotzia hakatuv nesuah miklal nesuah lehachmir, af keshehotzia hakatuv arusah miklal arusah lehachmir* — just as removing the *nesuah bas kohen* from her baseline category represents an escalation (from *chenek* to *sereifa*), so too removing the *arusah bas kohen* from her baseline represents an escalation (from *sekila* to *sereifa*). This in turn establishes that *sereifa* must be weightier than *sekila*, because the *Torah* is moving the *arusah* upward by assigning *sereifa* to her.

### Zomemim Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹמְמֵי נְשׂוּאָה בַּת כֹּהֵן, בִּכְלַל זוֹמְמֵי נְשׂוּאָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל; וְזוֹמְמֵי אֲרוּסָה בַּת כֹּהֵן, בִּכְלַל זוֹמְמֵי אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The punishment for **the conspiring witnesses concerning the married daughter of a priest,** who testified falsely that she committed adultery, **is included in** the punishment for **the conspiring witnesses concerning the married daughter of a non-priest, and** the punishment for **the conspiring witnesses concerning the betrothed daughter of a priest is included in** the punishment for **the conspiring witnesses concerning the betrothed daughter of a non-priest.** The Torah is not more severe with them; the conspiring witnesses in the case of any married woman accused of committing adultery are strangled, and the conspiring witnesses in the case of any betrothed woman accused of committing adultery are stoned.

The *beraitah* continues with a ruling about *edim zomemim* (false witnesses) in cases involving a *bas kohen*. *Zomemei nesuah bas kohen bikelal zomemei nesuah bas Yisrael; ve'zomemei arusah bas kohen bikelal zomemei arusah bas Yisrael.* The verse states *es aviha hi mechalleles ba'eish tisareif* — it is *she* who is burned, implying *hi b'sereifa v'lo zomemeiha b'sereifa*. The false witnesses do not receive *sereifa*; they receive the punishment that would have applied to a *bas Yisrael* in her position.

This is a striking ruling that requires explanation. In general, *edim zomemim* receive the punishment they sought to impose on the accused. Why should witnesses who tried to have a *bas kohen* executed receive a lesser punishment? The answer lies in the specific character of the *bas kohen*'s sin. The elevated punishment of *sereifa* for a *bas kohen* is grounded in *mechalleles es aviha* — she desecrates her father's household. That desecration is something only she can commit; the adulterer she was with did not desecrate her father's household, and neither did the false witnesses. The sin of *mechalleles beis aviha* is unique to the woman and cannot be imputed to the witnesses. Therefore, we strip the *bas kohen* dimension from the punishment when it comes to the *zomemim* and apply to them the penalty that would be given to a *bas Yisrael* in the equivalent situation. This also explains why the male adulterer in such a case — even if identified — would not receive *sereifa*: the elevated punishment belongs exclusively to her by virtue of her relationship to her father's household.

### Pesuk Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וּבַת אִישׁ כֹּהֵן כִּי תֵחֵל" – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ חִלְּלָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "לִזְנוֹת" – בְּחִילּוּלִין שֶׁבִּזְנוּת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ פְּנוּיָה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "אָבִיהָ", וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן "אָבִיהָ". מָה לְהַלָּן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל, אַף כָּאן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"And the daughter of a priest [*ish kohen*], when she profanes** herself by playing the harlot, she profanes her father; she shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 21:9). One **might** have thought that the expression "when she profanes [*ki teḥel*]" is referring **even** to one who **desecrated [*ḥillela*] Shabbat;** she too should be executed by burning. To counter this, **the verse states: "By playing the harlot"; the verse is speaking of profanation through promiscuity.** One **might** have thought **even** if she is **unmarried** and she engaged in promiscuous intercourse she should be executed by burning. This is incorrect, as **here it is stated: "Her father," and there it is stated** with regard to a betrothed woman who committed adultery: "Because she has done a depraved thing in Israel, to play the harlot in the house of **her father"** (Deuteronomy 22:21). **Just as there,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband, so too here,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband.**

The *Gemara* then examines the *pesukim* more carefully. The *beraitah* reads: *u'vas ish kohen ki techel liznos* — might *techel* refer to *chillul Shabbos*, desecrating the Sabbath? No: *talmud lomar liznos*, the *Torah* specifies it is the desecration that comes through sexual sin. Might it refer to an unmarried woman, a *pnuyah*? No: *ne'emar kan aviha, v'ne'emar lehalan aviha* — the word *aviha* appears here and also in the passage about the *na'arah me'orasah* (*liznos beis aviha*), establishing a *gezeira shava*. Just as there the *Torah* specifies a woman with a *zikas habaal* — a bond to a husband — so here too the *Torah* is speaking of a woman in a marital relationship: *mah lehalan znus im zikas habaal, af kan znus im zikas habaal*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר "אָבִיהָ" אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא אֶת כׇּל הָאָדָם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר "הִיא מְחַלֶּלֶת", הֱוֵי כָּל אָדָם אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים "אָבִיהָ"? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "אָבִיהָ", וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן "אָבִיהָ". מָה לְהַלָּן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל, אַף כָּאן זְנוּת עִם זִיקַת הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps one might have thought that the verse **states "her father" only** in order **to exclude all men** except her father, i.e., she is liable to be executed by burning only if she engaged in intercourse with her father. To counter this, **when it states "she profanes,"** indicating that it is she who profanes her father and not her father who profanes himself and her, **all men are stated,** i.e., included. Therefore, **how do I realize** the meaning of the expression "she profanes **her father"?** What *halakha* does it teach? The *baraita* answers: **Here it is stated: "Her father," and there it is stated: "Her father." Just as there,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband, so too here,** the reference is to the **promiscuous intercourse** of one who has **a bond to a husband.**

One might then ask — and the *beraitah* raises this — since we are linking the two passages through *aviha* and *liznos*, why not say this case of the *bas kohen* is also specifically a *na'arah me'orasah*? The *pshat* of *beis aviha* suggests she has not yet moved out of her father's house, which points toward betrothal. Furthermore, *im mah lehalan na'arah v'hi arusah, af kan na'arah v'hi arusah* — if the *lehalan* passage specifies a *na'arah arusah*, perhaps the *bas kohen* verse is similarly limited. The *Gemara* then asks: what about a *nesuah bas kohen*, a *bogeres bas kohen*, or even an elderly *bas kohen* who commits adultery — how do we know these are included?

### Expansion Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן נַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה, אַף כָּאן נַעֲרָה וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה? נַעֲרָה וְהִיא נְשׂוּאָה, בּוֹגֶרֶת וְהִיא אֲרוּסָה, בּוֹגֶרֶת וְהִיא נְשׂוּאָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִזְקִינָה – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "וּבַת כֹּהֵן" – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **If** the *halakha* of the priest's daughter who committed adultery is compared, by means of a verbal analogy, to the *halakha* of a betrothed woman who committed adultery, then perhaps one should say that **just as there** the reference is specifically to **a young woman,** i.e., to one whose first signs of maturity appeared within the past half year, **who is betrothed, so too here,** in the case of the daughter of a priest, the reference is to **a young woman who is betrothed.** But if she is **a young woman who is married,** or **a grown woman who is betrothed,** or **a grown woman who is married, or even** if she **grew old,** and is not normally referred to as a daughter, **from where** is it derived that her punishment is execution by burning? **The verse states: "And the daughter of a priest," in any case.** It is derived from the conjunction "and," represented by the letter *vav*, that this punishment applies to any woman who is the daughter of a priest.

The answer is that *talmud lomar u'vas kohen ki techel liznos*: the verse describes her generically as a *bas kohen* without specifying *na'arah* or any particular marital status, in contrast to other passages that always open by specifying the woman's age and status. The generic term *bas kohen* encompasses her at any age and in any state of marriage. The *lehalan* passage established that there must be a marital bond (*zikas habaal*); the *bas kohen* verse then extends the ruling beyond the *na'arah me'orasah* case to all instances where a *bas kohen* is in some form of marital relationship and commits adultery. This is Rabbi Shimon's reading — both *nesuah* and *arusah* are included — whereas the *rabanan* limit the verse to the *nesuah* in order to preserve *sekila* as the weightier punishment for the *arusah* case.

### Bat Ish Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "בַּת כֹּהֵן", אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן. נִיסֵּת לְלֵוִי וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל, לְכוּתִי, לְחָלָל, לְנָתִין, וּלְמַמְזֵר מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וּבַת אִישׁ כֹּהֵן", אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָהּ כֹּהֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: From the expression **"the daughter of a priest," I have** a source **only** for the case of a priest's daughter **who married a priest. From where** is it derived that she is also liable to be executed by burning if **she married a Levite, an Israelite, a Samaritan, a *ḥalal*, a Gibeonite, or a *mamzer*? The verse states: "And the daughter of a priest,"** indicating that this *halakha* applies **even if she is not** now **a priestess.**

The *beraitah* concludes with one final question about the *bas kohen*: *ein li ela she'nisas le'Kohen* — one might think the *bas kohen*'s elevated punishment of *sereifa* applies only if she has married a *kohen*. What if she married a *Levi*, a *Yisrael*, a non-Jew, a *chalal*, a *mamzer*, or a *nasin*? Do all of these still trigger the *sereifa* penalty? The *hava amina* is admittedly unusual — particularly regarding a non-Jew, where there is no halachically binding marriage at all. But the *beraitah* raises it to dismiss it: *talmud lomar u'vas ish kohen, af al pi she'eina kohenes* — the verse specifies *bas ish kohen*, a daughter of a *kohen man*, emphasizing that it is her paternal lineage that defines her status, not the identity of her husband. However her marriage may be characterized and whoever she has married, as long as she is a *bas kohen* by virtue of her father, the full weight of the *bas kohen* ruling applies to her. Her personal *kohanic* standing is not diminished by the fact that she is married to a non-*kohen*. This distinguishes her individual identity as *bas kohen* from the question of whether she herself holds the status of *kohenes*, which sometimes carries independent halachic significance.
