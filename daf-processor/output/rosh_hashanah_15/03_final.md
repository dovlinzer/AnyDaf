# Rosh Hashanah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview & Framework

### First Mishna Scope

With today's daf, we complete the first *mishna* of Masechet Rosh Hashanah. It is worth pausing to appreciate the structural peculiarity: the entire *masechet* spans approximately 34 *daf*, and the very first *mishna* occupies roughly half of it. The remaining *mishnayot*, which will feel somewhat more immediately relevant, begin only now.

The subject matter of this first *mishna* has been the rules governing the new year for *terumot uma'asrot* and *shvi'it*, and the distinctions that apply to different categories of produce.

### Produce Categories

The *mishna* establishes the following framework. Grains and olives are determined by *shalish* — reaching one-third of their growth. Fruit trees are determined by *chanatah* — blossoming. Vegetables are determined by *lakitah* — picking. Legumes are determined by *hashrashah* — the moment of taking root.

A clarification is worth noting here regarding the vegetable rule. *Tosfot* points out that the significance of picking by vegetables is effectively equivalent to *g'mar peri* — the moment the fruit finishes developing — since picking follows almost immediately upon that completion. There is similarly one opinion for legumes that also locates the determining moment at *g'mar peri*. *Tosfot* appears to mean this seriously: if one could construct a scenario in which *g'mar peri* occurred in one year and picking in the following year, *Tosfot* would have us go by *g'mar peri* for vegetables, not by *lakitah* — even though all the available evidence speaks only in terms of "picked before Rosh Hashanah" or "picked after Rosh Hashanah." *Tosfot* is attempting to resolve other difficulties by recasting the rule in terms of *g'mar peri*.

### Etrog Hybridity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: אֶתְרוֹג שָׁוֶה לָאִילָן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּרָכִים, וְלַיָּרָק בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶחָד. שָׁוֶה לָאִילָן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּרָכִים: לְעׇרְלָה וְלִרְבָעִי וְלִשְׁבִיעִית וּלְיָרָק בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶחָד — שֶׁבִּשְׁעַת לְקִיטָתוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֶתְרוֹג שָׁוֶה לָאִילָן לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: The ***etrog*** tree **is like** an ordinary **tree in three ways and like a vegetable in one way.** How so? **It is like** an ordinary **tree in three ways: With regard to *orla*,** that the fruit of the first three years after the tree is planted is forbidden; **with regard to fourth-year produce,** that the fruit that grows in the fourth year after the tree is planted must be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there or else it must be redeemed; **and with regard to the Sabbatical Year,** that the year is determined by the time of the formation of its fruit. **And** the *etrog* is like **a vegetable in one way,** which is **that its tithe** year follows **the time of the picking** of its fruit; this is **the statement of Rabban Gamliel. Rabbi Eliezer says: The *etrog* is like** fruit of **a tree with regard to all matters,** and so its tithe year also follows the time of the formation of its fruit.

The particular focus of the *gemara* at this stage has been the *etrog*, which presents a uniquely hybrid character. Because the *etrog* continues to grow throughout the year and continuously benefits from rainfall at all times, it is treated in certain respects like a vegetable. Specifically, when it comes to *terumot uma'asrot*, the year is determined by when the *etrog* is picked — following the vegetable rule. However, for *orlah* and *shemitah*, it follows the tree rule and goes by *chanatah* — blossoming.

What makes this particularly striking is the following: even though for *ma'aser* purposes the *etrog* is treated like a vegetable and goes by when it is picked, the calendar marker that is used is *tu b'shvat* — not the first of *tishrei*. The story of *Rabbi Akiva* who picked an *etrog* after *echad b'shvat* and before *tu b'shvat* and followed the practice of *Beit Hillel* while *Beit Shammai* played it safe illustrates precisely this hybrid structure. We treat the *etrog* like a vegetable for purposes of determining *ma'aser* by picking, yet the date we consult is the *shvat* date appropriate to trees. The *etrog* is, in every sense, a legal hybrid.

## Etrog Dual Status

### Rabba's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אֶתְרוֹג אַחַר לְקִיטָתוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ כְּיָרָק, רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלּוֹ תִּשְׁרִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: Now that Rabban Gamliel has said** that **the tithe** year **of an *etrog*** follows **the time of the picking** of its fruit, **like a vegetable, its new year** for tithing **is Tishrei,** like other vegetables.

The *gemara* at the bottom of 14b begins with a statement by Rabba bar Rav Huna. His initial formulation reads: *amar Rabba bar Rav Huna hashta d'amar Rabban Gamliel etrog achar lakitah* — now that *Rabban Gamliel* says the *etrog* follows picking, *issuro k'yerek* — its prohibition is like a vegetable, and therefore *Rosh Hashanah shelo tishrei* — its Rosh Hashanah should be *tishrei*, like a genuine vegetable.

This was a debate between *Rabban Gamliel* and *Rabbi Eliezer*. *Rabbi Eliezer* held that the *etrog* follows tree rules in all respects, while *Rabban Gamliel* ruled that for *ma'asrot* it follows the vegetable rule and goes by picking. Rabba bar Rav Huna's initial statement attempted to be consistent with *Rabban Gamliel*: if we go by picking, we should also go by *tishrei* as the Rosh Hashanah. The *gemara* challenges this from a *braita*.

### Braitta Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לִיקֵּט אֶתְרוֹג עֶרֶב חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בִּשְׁבָט עַד שֶׁלֹּא תָּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, וְחָזַר וְלִיקֵּט מִשֶּׁתָּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ — אֵין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין מִזֶּה עַל זֶה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין לֹא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ עַל הַיָּשָׁן וְלֹא מִן הַיָּשָׁן עַל הֶחָדָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If **one picked** the fruit of **an *etrog*** tree **on the eve of the fifteenth of Shevat before the sun had set, and** then **he picked more** fruit **after sunset, one may not set aside *teruma* and tithes from the one for the other, as one may not set aside *teruma* and tithes from the new** crop **for the old or from the old** crop **for the new.**

The *gemara* challenges with a *braita*: *Rabbi Shimon Elazar* rules that if one picked an *etrog* on the eve of *tu b'shvat* before the sun had set, and then went back and picked again after the sun had set, one may not take *terumah* and *ma'asrot* from one batch on behalf of the other. The implication is clear: picking is the operative factor, and *tu b'shvat* is the determining date — both simultaneously. The *braita* thus establishes explicitly that the *etrog* is a hybrid: it goes by picking in the manner of a vegetable, yet uses *shvat* as the calendar marker in the manner of a tree.

### Tu B'Shevat Impact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָזֵיל בָּתַר לְקִיטָה — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְקָתָנֵי שְׁבָט! אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אֶתְרוֹג אַחַר לְקִיטָה כְּיָרָק — רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלּוֹ שְׁבָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this ruling: **Whom did you hear that** said the tithe year of an *etrog* **follows** the time of the **picking** of its fruit? It was **Rabban Gamliel. And** yet this *baraita* **is teaching** that the new year for tithing an *etrog* is the fifteenth of **Shevat,** against the opinion of Rabba bar Rav Huna, who says that according to Rabban Gamliel it is the first of Tishrei. **Rather, if this was said, it was said as follows: Rabba bar Rav Huna said: Even though Rabban Gamliel said** that the tithe year of **an *etrog* follows** the time of the **picking** of its fruit, **like a vegetable, its new year is** in **Shevat,** like a tree.

The revised formulation acknowledges the hybrid, rather than attempting to iron it out.

The practical stakes of this hybrid status become clear when the *shemitah* cycle causes a change in the *type* of *ma'aser* being taken — not merely whether produce belongs to the old or new year. Consider the third year of the *shemitah* cycle entering the fourth: in the third year, one takes *ma'aser ani*, while in the fourth year, one takes *ma'aser sheni*. For an *etrog* picked before *tu b'shvat*, even if picked four months after *rosh hashanah* of *tishrei*, the operative calendar marker is *tu b'shvat*. The type of *ma'aser* taken will accordingly reflect the prior year's status.

By contrast, for other fruit trees, the *chanatah* is what counts. A peach, for instance, that blossoms before *tu b'shvat* and is picked late in year four — when the *shemitah* cycle calls for *ma'aser sheni* — will nonetheless require *ma'aser ani*, because the blossoming occurred before *tu b'shvat* in a *ma'aser ani* year. Picking has no effect by ordinary fruit. The *etrog* at least has the advantage that its status is determined closer in time to when one is actually ready to eat it, but even so, the *tu b'shvat* date can still assign it to a different year than the one in which it is actually being consumed.

### Etrog Diagram

To make this concrete, consider the *shemitah* cycle as follows: *shemitah* precedes year one, followed by year two, three, and four. In year two and year four, *ma'aser sheni* is taken; in year three, *ma'aser ani* is taken. The *tu b'shvat* that falls partway through year three — approximately one-third into it — is the dividing line for the *etrog*. An *etrog* picked before that *tu b'shvat*, even though it has been four months since *rosh hashanah* of *tishrei* and the world around it is operating under year-three *ma'asrot* rules, is still assigned to year two and thus requires *ma'aser sheni*. Only after *tu b'shvat* does the *etrog* cross over into year three and become subject to *ma'aser ani*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָתָנֵי ״אִם הָיְתָה שְׁנִיָּה נִכְנֶסֶת לִשְׁלִישִׁית״ וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״אִם הָיְתָה שְׁלִישִׁית נִכְנֶסֶת לִרְבִיעִית״? מִילְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאֶתְרוֹג קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ יְדָא וְאַיְּידֵי דִּמְמַשְׁמְשִׁי בֵּיהּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בִּשְׁבִיעִית, לָא טָעֵין פֵּרֵי עַד תְּלָת שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different there,** in the *baraita* with regard to one who picked vegetables on the eve of Rosh HaShana, such **that it teaches** the case when **it was the second** year of the Sabbatical cycle **going into the third** year, **and what is different here,** in the *baraita* dealing with one who was picking the fruit of an *etrog* tree on the eve of the fifteenth of Shevat, such **that it teaches** the case when **it was the third** year **going into the fourth** year? Why is the same example not brought in both cases? **It teaches us a matter in passing, that it is damaging** for an ***etrog*** when the tree **is handled, and since everyone handles it in the Sabbatical Year,** as all the orchards are declared ownerless and everyone is permitted to enter and pick produce, the tree **does not bear fruit** that is fit for eating **for** another **three years.**

The *gemara* also explains why the example of the *etrog* is drawn from the third year entering the fourth — rather than the more standard example of the second year entering the third that is used for other produce. The incidental teaching embedded in this choice is that the *etrog* tree is particularly delicate — *kashi lei yada* — and is easily damaged by handling. During *shemitah*, when the orchard is *hefker* and everyone is freely picking, the tree sustains damage and will take until well into the third year after *shemitah* to begin producing fruit again. Consequently, for the *etrog*, there simply is no fruit to be picking in a scenario of the second year entering the third; the first realistic opportunity for a harvest following *shemitah* is the third year entering the fourth. Hence the *gemara* uses that case as its example. This is not necessarily to say that every *etrog* tree in *Eretz Yisrael* suffers this fate every *shemitah* cycle — it may be that many trees recover sooner — but it reflects a common enough pattern, particularly given how the *etrog*'s soft fruit is especially vulnerable to the rough handling of open public access.

The *etrog* is singled out for vegetable-like status precisely because its fruit remains on the tree continuously from year to year, always benefiting from ongoing rainfall, unlike ordinary fruit that blossoms, grows, and must be picked within a fixed season.

## Months vs. Seasons

### Calendar Date Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֶתְרוֹג, רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁלּוֹ אֵימָתַי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁבָט. שְׁבָט דָּחֳדָשִׁים, אוֹ שְׁבָט דִּתְקוּפָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דָּחֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan inquired of Rabbi Yannai:** With regard to **an *etrog*, when is its new year?** Rabbi Yannai **said to him:** It is in **Shevat.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked again: Are you referring to **the lunar month of Shevat or** to **the Shevat** of the solar **season,** which begins thirty days after the winter solstice, but on a different date each year? Rabbi Yannai **said to him:** I am referring to **the lunar month** of Shevat.

The *gemara* records that *Rav Yochanan* posed a question to *Rabbi Yannai*: *esrog rosh hashanah shelo eimasai* — when is the Rosh Hashanah of the *etrog*? Given the hybrid status of the *etrog* and the argument that it might consistently follow the vegetable rule with *tishrei* as its Rosh Hashanah, this was a genuine question. *Rabbi Yannai* answered simply: *shvat*.

*Rav Yochanan* then pressed further: *shvat dechodashim o shvat detkufah* — do we mean the calendar month of *shvat*, or *shvat* as a seasonal marker? The seasons of the year are each associated with a *tkufah*: *tkufas tishrei* is autumn, *tkufas teves* is winter, *tkufas nissan* is spring, and *tkufas tammuz* is summer. *Tkufas shvat*, as *Rashi* explains, means one month into winter — reckoned by the solar calendar. Since the lunar and solar years periodically diverge, the question arises whether we should track the calendar month called *shvat* or whether we should track the seasonal moment that the month of *shvat* was originally meant to represent.

One might ask why this question was not raised more broadly about every instance in which the *mishna* specifies a date — *rosh chodesh tishrei* for grain, *tu b'shvat* for trees, and so on. The suggestion is that the question was specifically directed at the *etrog* because the *etrog*'s status is already so tailored and anomalous that one might expect its date to be calibrated differently as well — perhaps seasonally rather than calendrically.

### Calendar Month Resolution

*Rabbi Yannai* responded: *dechodashim* — we go by the calendar month. *Tosfot* notes that this question could equally have been raised with respect to the first of *shvat* in the *mishna*, even independently of the *etrog*, since the same tension between calendar month and astronomical season applies throughout. The conclusion, however, is unambiguous: the calendar date governs.

### Leap Year Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: הָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava inquired of Rav Naḥman, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who inquired **of Rabbi Yannai: If it was a leap year, what is** the *halakha*? Does the new year for trees then move to the First Adar, which is the penultimate month in a leap year? **He said to him: Follow the majority of years.** Even in a leap year the new year for trees is in Shevat.

The *gemara* records a further query: *Rava* asked *Rav Nachman* — and some attribute the question to *Rav Yochanan* asking *Rabbi Yannai* — *haysa shanah m'uberess mahu*: if the year is a leap year, what is the law? In a leap year an extra month is intercalated, which shifts the relationship between the calendar and the seasons. One might think that *tu b'shvat*, being in some sense sensitive to seasonal conditions, should shift accordingly — that in a leap year the marker for the *etrog*'s *ma'aser* year should move. The response is *halach achar rov shanim* — we follow the majority of ordinary years and do not adjust for the leap year. The calendar date remains constant. This is, in a sense, internally consistent: if one wants to be sensitive to seasonal considerations, the very purpose of intercalating a leap year is to bring the lunar calendar back into alignment with the seasons — so the calendar date already reflects the seasonal adjustment. And if one simply wants to follow the calendar, then the date does not shift. Either way, *tu b'shvat* remains *tu b'shvat*.

## Rava's Scenarios

### Sixth→Seventh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אֶתְרוֹג בַּת שִׁשִּׁית שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִשְׁבִיעִית — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַבִּיעוּר. וּבַת שְׁבִיעִית שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִשְׁמִינִית — פְּטוּרָה בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר וְחַיֶּיבֶת בַּבִּיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: An *etrog* from the sixth** year of the Sabbatical cycle **that entered into,** and was picked in, **the Sabbatical Year is exempt from tithes,** like the produce of the Sabbatical Year, **and is** also **exempt from** the mitzva of **elimination** of Sabbatical Year produce from one's house after produce of that particular species is no longer found in the field. **A Sabbatical Year** *etrog* **that entered into,** and was picked in, **the eighth** year **is exempt from tithes but is subject to** the mitzva of **elimination** of Sabbatical Year produce from one's house.

*Rava* presents two cases involving the *etrog* and the *shemitah* cycle. The first: *esrog bat shishis shenichnesah l'shvi'is* — an *etrog* that blossomed in the sixth year and was picked in the seventh year. *Rava* rules: *peturah min hama'aser upeturah min habiur* — it is exempt from *ma'aser* and exempt from *biur*. *Biur* — the obligation to remove and make ownerless one's *shemitah* produce once that species of produce is no longer available in the field — is a marker of *kedushas shvi'is*, the sanctity of *shemitah*. Because the *etrog* blossomed in the sixth year, it carries no *kedushas shvi'is*, and therefore there is no *biur*. And it is exempt from *ma'aser* as well, for reasons that the *gemara* will clarify.

### Seventh→Eighth

The second case: *bat shevi'is shenichnesah l'shminit* — an *etrog* that blossomed in the seventh year and was picked in the eighth. *Rava* rules: *peturah bema'aser vechayeves beviur* — exempt from *ma'aser* and obligated in *biur*. This is where the scenario becomes genuinely striking. The *etrog* blossomed in *shemitah* and therefore carries *kedushas shvi'is*, which is why it is obligated in *biur*. Yet it is exempt from *ma'aser* — even though it was picked in the eighth year, a year in which *ma'aser* would ordinarily apply. The apparent tension between these two determinations is precisely what drives the subsequent discussion.

### Scenarios Diagram

To map this out clearly: in the first scenario, the tree blossoms before *tu b'shvat* of the seventh year — that is, in the sixth year — and the *etrog* is picked after *tu b'shvat*, now in the seventh year. For *shemitah* purposes, the *chanatah* in the sixth year means no *kedushas shvi'is* and no *biur*. For *ma'aser* purposes, the *lakitah* in the seventh year means no *ma'aser* — since one does not take *ma'asrot* from seventh-year produce. The result is that both obligations are absent, and they are absent for different reasons grounded in different operative moments.

In the second scenario, the tree blossoms in the seventh year — carrying *kedushas shvi'is* — but is picked in the eighth year. Here the obligations seem to pull in opposite directions: *kedushas shvi'is* (from blossoming in the seventh) argues for *biur*; picking in the eighth (a year of *ma'aser*) would seem to argue for *ma'aser*. Yet *Rava* rules that there is *biur* but no *ma'aser*. The resolution of this apparent paradox is what *Abbaye* now challenges.

## Abbaye's Challenge

### Abbaye's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא סֵיפָא — לְחוּמְרָא, אֶלָּא רֵישָׁא פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבִּיעוּר — אַמַּאי? דְּאָמְרִינַן זִיל בָּתַר חֲנָטָה, אִי הָכִי תִּיחַיַּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Granted,** in **the latter clause** the *halakha* follows the more **stringent** approach, as it follows the time of the formation of the fruit, which was in the Sabbatical Year, and therefore the *etrog* is subject to the mitzva of elimination. But in **the first clause,** which states that the *etrog* **is exempt from** the mitzva of **elimination, why** is this so? It must be **that we say** to **follow** the time of **the formation** of the fruit, which was in the sixth year. But **if so, it should be subject to tithes.**

*Abbaye* responds to *Rava*'s ruling with a penetrating challenge. He opens by saying: *bishlama seifa* — I understand the second part of your statement. You say the *etrog* blossomed in the seventh year and was picked in the eighth: it is obligated in *biur* because it has *kedushas shvi'is*, and once you have established that it is a *shemitah* fruit, the exemption from *ma'aser* follows naturally, since *shemitah* fruit does not require *ma'asrot*. That is a consistent, strict ruling — *l'chumra* — which treats the *etrog* as a *shemitah* fruit throughout.

But *ella reisha peturah min habiur amai* — what about the first part of your ruling? There you say it is blossomed in the sixth year and you say no *biur* because you go by *chanatah*. Very well. But if you go by *chanatah* to determine that it lacks *kedushas shvi'is*, then it should follow that for *ma'aser* purposes as well, it should be treated as a sixth-year fruit — which means it should be obligated in *ma'aser*. Why does *Rava* say it is also exempt from *ma'aser*?

*Abbaye*'s underlying assumption, in other words, is that *ma'aser* follows *shemitah* status. If the *shemitah* status is determined by *chanatah* and the *chanatah* was in the sixth year, then the *ma'aser* status should likewise be that of a sixth-year fruit — and a sixth-year fruit is obligated in *ma'aser*. Yet *Rava* says it is exempt. Why?

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יַד הַכֹּל מְמַשְׁמְשִׁין בָּהּ, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ תִּיחַיַּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: Everyone's hand is touching it,** as all are permitted to enter the orchards and touch all the fruit, and so the etrog is regarded as ownerless, **and** yet **you** wish to **say that it should be subject to tithes?** Even if it is not exempt as fruit of the Sabbatical Year, it is exempt from tithes because it is now ownerless.

*Rava*'s response is arresting in its practicality. He says: *yada kol memashmeshin ba v'at amrat titchayev bema'aser* — everyone's hands are grabbing at it, and you are saying it should be obligated in *ma'aser*? His argument is not a formal legal principle but an observation about reality. During *shemitah*, even if a tree technically lacks *kedushas shvi'is* — because it blossomed in the sixth year — the reality on the ground is that nobody is protecting it. The fruit of this tree grows during the *shemitah* year, when all agricultural produce is *hefker* by convention, and people are freely helping themselves. A fruit that in practice is accessible to all and is not protected — *eino min hamishomar* — cannot generate an obligation of *ma'aser*.

There is a principle in the laws of *ma'aser* that produce must be *mishumar* — guarded, protected — for the *ma'aser* obligation to apply with full force. Since the *etrog* growing during *shemitah* is in practice accessible to the public even if it is not legally *hefker*, one cannot realistically impose an obligation of *ma'aser* upon it.

This argument would potentially be bounded: if one had an enclosed *etrog* orchard and could actually prevent public access during *shemitah*, the situation might be different. The answer is that even then the fruit would not be fully *mishumar* in the relevant sense, since the social reality of *shemitah* would still pertain. But the theoretical question of whether a perfectly enclosed orchard would generate *ma'aser* liability is a genuine *nafka mina* from *Rava*'s reasoning.

### Rashi's Reading

*Rashi* offers a reinterpretation of the entire exchange that deserves careful attention, because it changes the framework considerably. According to *Rashi*, *Rava* in fact agrees with *Abbaye*'s premise: *ma'aser* follows *shemitah* status. The two rulings therefore become internally consistent on their own terms.

In the second scenario — blossomed in the seventh, picked in the eighth — the *etrog* has *kedushas shvi'is*, and since *ma'aser* follows *shemitah* status, it is exempt from *ma'aser*. *Abbaye* had no difficulty with this part.

In the first scenario — blossomed in the sixth, picked in the seventh — the *etrog* lacks *kedushas shvi'is*, and therefore, if *ma'aser* follows *shemitah* status, it should be obligated in *ma'aser* as a sixth-year fruit. *Abbaye*'s question was precisely: why then does *Rava* say it is exempt?

And *Rava*'s answer, on *Rashi*'s reading, is: you are technically correct that it lacks *kedushas shvi'is* and therefore should be obligated in *ma'aser* as a sixth-year fruit. But the practical reality is that this *etrog* is growing during *shemitah*, and people are treating it as *hefker* and picking it freely. Even though halachically it is not *hefker*, it is also not *mishumar*. Since it is not protected, the obligation of *ma'aser* cannot practically take hold. This is *Rava*'s justification for the exemption — not a rejection of *Abbaye*'s logical framework, but an acknowledgment that practical reality overrides the theoretical obligation in this specific case.

## Hamnuna vs. Braitta

### Hamnuna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר: בַּת שִׁשִּׁית שֶׁנִּכְנֶסֶת לִשְׁבִיעִית — לְעוֹלָם שִׁשִּׁית, וּבַת שְׁבִיעִית הַנִּכְנֶסֶת לִשְׁמִינִית — לְעוֹלָם שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna** argued and **said:** An *etrog* **from the sixth** year of the Sabbatical cycle **that entered into,** and was picked in, **the Sabbatical Year is always** and for all purposes considered **sixth-year** produce. **And** an *etrog* **from the Sabbatical Year that entered into,** and was picked in, **the eighth** year **is always** and for all purposes considered produce of the **Sabbatical Year.**

*Rav Hamnuna* offers a sweeping formulation. He says: *bat shishit shenichnesa l'shvi'it le-olam shishit* — a sixth-year *etrog* entering the seventh year is, in all respects, always sixth-year produce. And *bat shvi'it shenichnesa l'shminit le-olam shvi'it* — a seventh-year *etrog* entering the eighth year is, in all respects, always seventh-year produce. The *shemitah* status established at *chanatah* is permanent and comprehensive, governing both *biur* and *ma'aser* consistently and without qualification.

### Braitta Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֶתְרוֹג בַּת שִׁשִּׁית שֶׁנִּכְנֶסֶת לִשְׁבִיעִית — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַבִּיעוּר, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גָּדַל בְּחִיּוּב וְנִלְקַט בְּחִיּוּב. וּבַת שְׁבִיעִית שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִשְׁמִינִית — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַבִּיעוּר, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּבִיעוּר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גָּדַל בִּשְׁבִיעִית וְנִלְקַט בִּשְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Shimon: An *etrog* from the sixth** year **that entered into,** and was picked in, **the Sabbatical Year is exempt from tithes, and it is** also **exempt from** the mitzva of **elimination** of Sabbatical Year produce from one's house, **as there is nothing that is subject to tithes unless it** both **grew at** a time of **obligation** in tithes **and was** also **picked** at a time of **obligation.** Likewise, an *etrog* **from the Sabbatical Year that entered into,** and was picked in, **the eighth** year **is exempt from tithes, and it is** also **exempt from** the mitzva of **elimination, as nothing is subject to** the mitzva of **elimination unless it** both **grew in the Sabbatical Year and was** also **picked in the Sabbatical Year.**

The *gemara* challenges *Rav Hamnuna* from a *braita* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* citing *Rabbi Shimon*: an *etrog* that blossomed in the sixth year and was picked in the seventh is exempt from *ma'aser* and exempt from *biur*. The exemption from *biur* is intelligible — it is a sixth-year fruit without *kedushas shvi'is*. But the *braita* gives the reason for the exemption from *ma'aser* as follows: *she-ein lecha davar she-chayav bema'aser ella im ken gadal b'chiyuv v'nilkat b'chiyuv* — nothing is obligated in *ma'aser* unless it both grew in a year of *ma'aser* obligation and was picked in a year of *ma'aser* obligation. Since it was picked in the seventh year, when there is no *ma'aser*, it is exempt — not because of its *shemitah* status from *chanatah*, but because picking in *shemitah* independently exempts it.

For the second scenario — blossomed in the seventh, picked in the eighth — the *braita* says it is exempt from *ma'aser* and exempt from *biur*. The exemption from *ma'aser* is understandable, since it is *shemitah* produce. But the exemption from *biur* is justified by the parallel logic: *she-ein lecha davar she-chayav bebiur ella im ken gadal b'shvi'it v'nilkat b'shvi'it* — nothing is obligated in *biur* unless it both grew and was picked in the seventh year. Since it was picked in the eighth year, it has no *biur*.

This *braita* introduces a fundamentally different framework: the determining moment for each obligation is a combination of when the fruit grew and when it was picked, and only when both conditions point to *chiyuv* does the obligation apply. This directly contradicts *Rav Hamnuna*, who says the *shemitah* status at *chanatah* is permanent and exclusive.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא קַשְׁיָא לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, סֵיפָא קַשְׁיָא בֵּין לְרַבָּה בֵּין לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אַבְטוּלְמוֹס הֵעִיד מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה זְקֵנִים: אֶתְרוֹג — אַחַר לְקִיטָתוֹ לְמַעֲשֵׂר, וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ נִמְנוּ בְּאוּשָׁא, וְאָמְרוּ: אַחַר לְקִיטָתוֹ, בֵּין לְמַעֲשֵׂר בֵּין לִשְׁבִיעִית. שְׁבִיעִית מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The first clause** of this *baraita* poses **a difficulty** with regard **to** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna,** who says that a sixth-year *etrog* that was picked in the Sabbatical Year is subject to tithes. And **the latter clause** of the *baraita* poses **a difficulty** with regard **to** the opinions of **both Rabba and Rav Hamnuna,** as they both say that a Sabbatical-Year *etrog* that was picked in the eighth year is subject to the mitzva of elimination. **The matter is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei said: Avtolemos testified in the name of five Elders: An *etrog* follows** the time of **its picking in** the matter of **tithes. But our Sages voted in Usha and said** that an *etrog* **follows** the time of **its picking, both in** the matter of **tithes and in** the matter of **the Sabbatical Year. Who mentioned anything about the Sabbatical Year?**

The *gemara* maps out the contradictions precisely: *reisha kashya le-Rav Hamnuna, seifa kashya bein le-Rabba bein le-Rav Hamnuna* — the first part of the *braita* poses a difficulty for *Rav Hamnuna*, and the second part poses a difficulty for both *Rabba* and *Rav Hamnuna*.

The first part: in the sixth-into-seventh scenario, the *braita* says no *ma'aser* because it was picked in the seventh — consistent with *Rabba*'s outcome (though perhaps not his exact reasoning) but contradicting *Rav Hamnuna*, who would say the sixth-year status means *yes* to *ma'aser*.

The second part: in the seventh-into-eighth scenario, the *braita* says no *biur* because it was picked in the eighth. *Rabba* says yes *biur* (because it blossomed in *shemitah*), and *Rav Hamnuna* likewise says yes *biur* (since it is always seventh-year produce). Both are contradicted by the *braita*'s ruling that picking in the eighth exempts from *biur*.

The *gemara* resolves the impasse by declaring *tannai hi* — this is a dispute among *tannaim*. It then presents and expands a *braita* that reads: *esrog achar lekitaso lema'aser ve-achar chanataso l'shvi'is* — the *etrog* follows picking for *ma'aser* and blossoming for *shemitah*. That is the majority position that has governed our discussion throughout. Against this, *ve-raboseinu nimnu be-Usha ve-amru achar lekitaso bein lema'aser bein l'shvi'is* — the rabbis took a vote in *Usha* and said that the *etrog* follows picking for both *ma'aser* and *shemitah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אֶתְרוֹג — אַחַר לְקִיטָתוֹ לְמַעֲשֵׂר וְאַחַר חֲנָטָה לִשְׁבִיעִית. וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ נִמְנוּ בְּאוּשָׁא: אַחַר לְקִיטָתוֹ, בֵּין לְמַעֲשֵׂר בֵּין לִשְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **is incomplete and is teaching the following:** Avtolemos testified in the name of five Elders: **An *etrog* follows** the time of **its picking in** the matter of **tithes and** it follows the time of **the formation** of its fruit **in** the matter of **the Sabbatical Year. But our Sages voted in Usha** and decided that an *etrog* **follows** the time of **its picking, both** in the matter of **tithes and in** the matter of **the Sabbatical Year.**

### Usha Vote

The *gemara* asks: *shvi'is man dchar shmeih* — *shemitah* was never mentioned in the first opinion; what is the dispute about? The *gemara* supplies the missing context: the first opinion holds that for *shemitah* the *etrog* goes by *chanatah*, and the rabbis who voted in *Usha* held that even for *shemitah* the *etrog* follows *lakitah*.

The *braita* of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* — the one that said no *biur* because the *etrog* was picked in the eighth year — represents precisely the *Usha* minority position: that *lakitah* matters even for *shemitah* laws. The accepted ruling, as established by the *Usha* vote, rejects this position. For *shemitah* purposes, it is only *chanatah* that counts. Only for *ma'aser* does *lakitah* operate, and that is what *Rabba* and *Rav Hamnuna* are debating — with the rejected minority *braita* accounting for the position that introduced *lakitah* even into *shemitah* law.

## Yochanan & Lakish

### Always Sixth Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֶתְרוֹג בַּת שִׁשִּׁית שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִשְׁבִיעִית — לְעוֹלָם שִׁשִּׁית. כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֶתְרוֹג בַּת שִׁשִּׁית שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִשְׁבִיעִית, אֲפִילּוּ כְּזַיִת וְנַעֲשֵׂית כִּכָּר — חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם טֶבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* of Eretz Yisrael discussed this issue: **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say: An *etrog* from the sixth** year of the Sabbatical cycle **that entered into,** and was picked in, **the Sabbatical Year is always** and for all purposes considered as **sixth-year** produce. **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A sixth-year *etrog* that entered into,** and was picked in, **the Sabbatical Year,** although at the beginning of the Sabbatical Year it was only the size of **an olive-bulk and** during the Sabbatical Year **it grew to the size of a loaf** of bread, is considered sixth-year produce that is subject to tithing, and if one eats it without tithing, **he is liable for** eating **untithed produce.**

*Rav Yochanan* and *Resh Lakish* are both reported to hold: *etrog bashishit shenichnesa lishvi'it le-olam shishit* — a sixth-year *etrog* that blossoms in the sixth year retains its sixth-year status in all respects, even as it continues to grow enormously during the seventh year. The fact that the bulk of its physical growth and enlargement occurred during *shemitah* is irrelevant; the status is locked in at the moment of *chanatah*.

### Tiny Fruit Rule

When *Ravin* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported in the name of *Rav Yochanan*: if an *etrog* blossomed in the sixth year and at that time was only the size of an olive — *afilu kezayit* — and by the time it was the seventh year it had grown to the size of a loaf of bread — *venasa kikar* — it is nevertheless obligated in *ma'aser* as sixth-year produce, and is considered *tevel* until properly separated. No matter how dramatically it grew during *shemitah*, its *ma'aser* status remains anchored to the moment of *chanatah* in the sixth year. One might intuitively feel that a fruit that grew so dramatically during *shemitah* should take on the character of *shemitah* produce — but the halacha is unequivocal: *chanatah* determines status, and that determination is final.

## Tree Ripening Patterns

### Nechemia Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילָן שֶׁחָנְטוּ פֵּירוֹתָיו קוֹדֶם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בִּשְׁבָט — מִתְעַשֵּׂר לְשָׁנָה שֶׁעָבְרָה, אַחַר חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בִּשְׁבָט — מִתְעַשֵּׂר לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה. אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּאִילָן שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה שְׁתֵּי בְרִיכוֹת בַּשָּׁנָה. שְׁתֵּי בְרִיכוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כְּעֵין שְׁתֵּי בְרִיכוֹת. אֲבָל אִילָן הָעוֹשֶׂה בְּרִיכָה אַחַת, כְּגוֹן דְּקָלִים וְזֵיתִים וְחָרוּבִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָנְטוּ פֵּירוֹתֵיהֶן קוֹדֶם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בִּשְׁבָט — מִתְעַשְּׂרִין לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A tree whose fruits were formed before the fifteenth of Shevat is tithed in accordance with the previous year,** and if the fruits were formed **after the fifteenth of Shevat** it **is tithed in accordance with the coming year. Rabbi Neḥemya said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to a tree that produces two broods,** two crops, **in a** single **year. The Gemara interrupts with a question about the wording of this *baraita*: Does it enter your mind** to say **two broods?** Animals produce broods, but trees do not. **Rather, say: Like two broods,** i.e., two seasons' worth of crops. **But** in the case of **trees that produce** only **one brood** of fruit, **for example, palm** trees, **and olive** trees, **and carob** trees, which yield fruit only once a year, **although their fruit took form before the fifteenth of Shevat, they are tithed in accordance with the coming year,** since they follow the time of their fruit's picking. According to Rabbi Neḥemya, most fruit will be tithed according to the time that the fruit is picked, since only a minority of fruit trees produce two crops a year.

The *gemara* introduces a new *braita*: the rule that fruit trees go by *chanatah* and by *tu b'shvat* applies when a tree has two *breichot* — two periods of ripening — per year. The *gemara* notes that the borrowed term *breicha* (literally a brood or clutch, used of pigeons) means here something like two cycles of fruit ripening, and clarifies *k'ein shtei breichot* — like two broodings, meaning the tree produces and ripens fruit at multiple times throughout the year.

*Rabbi Nechemia* qualifies the rule: *aval ilan ha'oseh breichah achat* — but a tree that produces fruit in only one cycle per year — such as palm trees, olives, and carobs — follows a different rule. *Af al pi shechanto peiroteihem kodem tu b'shvat, mitasrin l'shanah haba'ah* — even if their fruits blossomed before *tu b'shvat*, they are tithed as belonging to the coming year, not the previous year. In other words, for single-cycle trees, we go by *g'mar peri* — when the fruit finishes ripening — rather than by *chanatah*.

This is a radical qualification of everything that has been said. The entire framework of the first chapter — trees go by *chanatah*, and *tu b'shvat* is the dividing date — was built on the assumption of trees with multiple ripening cycles. For single-cycle trees, *Rabbi Nechemia* says, we actually go by the ripening itself.

### Reasoning

The underlying logic parallels the discussion about legumes. By legumes, the *gemara* noted that while the real operative moment might ideally be *g'mar peri*, legumes are picked at various times throughout the year and we cannot use a single uniform marker; so we use *hashrashah* instead as a fixed, early, uniform date. Similarly, *Rabbi Nechemia* is reasoning about trees as follows: the reason we use *chanatah* and *tu b'shvat* as the fixed marker for most trees is that trees with multiple ripening cycles produce fruit at many different times, making *g'mar peri* an unworkable standard — it will fall at different dates for different clusters of fruit. So we look backward to the blossoming, which tends to occur in a concentrated window around *tu b'shvat*, and use that as a single, fixed point.

But if a tree only produces fruit once per year — one *breichah* — then *g'mar peri* is not scattered across the calendar. It happens at a single, identifiable time. In that case, there is no practical need to retreat to the earlier and more uniform marker of *chanatah*. We can simply use *g'mar peri* itself. The choice between *chanatah* and *g'mar peri*, in other words, is driven by practical convenience in establishing a fixed and workable standard — not by an independent principle about what categorically determines the year for trees.

The difficulty is that *Rabbi Nechemia* does not explicitly frame the shift as being between *tu b'shvat* and *rosh hashanah*, but between blossoming and finishing. Nonetheless, the structural logic appears to be: when trees blossom matters because it is a convenient single marker; when there is only one ripening, the ripening itself serves as that single marker equally well.

### Carob Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָהֲגוּ הָעָם בֶּחָרוּבִין כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The people were accustomed** to act **with regard to carobs in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya,** that their tithe year follows the time of the fruit's picking.

*Rav Yochanan* reports: *nahagu am becharuvin k'Rabbi Nechemia* — the people practiced with carobs in accordance with *Rabbi Nechemia*. Since carobs ripen only once per year, the common practice was to go by the ripening rather than the blossoming. This is notable, and as will become clear, it implies that the laws of *ma'aser* for trees are rabbinic in character — otherwise such latitude in practice would be difficult to justify.

## White Figs Challenge

### White Figs Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּנוֹת שׁוּחַ — שְׁבִיעִית שֶׁלָּהֶן שְׁנִיָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעוֹשׂוֹת לִשְׁלֹשׁ הַשָּׁנִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** from a mishna that teaches: In the case of **white fig** trees, the **Sabbatical Year for them** with regard to the *halakhot* of eating and elimination is in the **second** year of the Sabbatical cycle, **due to** the fact that their fruit **grows for three years,** and so the fruit that ripens in the second year of the Sabbatical cycle had already taken form in the previous Sabbatical Year. This indicates that the tithe follows the time of the formation of the fruit and not the time of picking.

*Resh Lakish* challenges *Rav Yochanan* from the case of *bnot shuach* — white figs. The ruling is: *shevi'it shelahen shniyah mipnei she'osot l'shalosh shanim* — their *shemitah* year is the second year of the cycle, because it takes three years for them to ripen fully. The implication is that a white fig that blossomed in the *shemitah* year will only be picked two years later, in the second year after *shemitah*, and it will carry *kedushas shvi'is* at that point because of its *shemitah* blossoming.

### Problem

This presents a direct challenge to *Rav Yochanan*'s endorsement of the *Rabbi Nechemia* position. White figs ripen only once — they are quintessentially a single-*breichah* tree. According to *Rabbi Nechemia*'s logic, single-cycle trees should go by *g'mar peri*, not by blossoming. Yet the white fig's *shemitah* status is determined by its *chanatah* in the *shemitah* year, not by when it is picked two years later. *Resh Lakish* pressed this contradiction, and *Rav Yochanan* fell silent — *ishtik* — offering no response.

### Rabbinic vs. Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא הַכֹּהֵן לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַכֹּהֵן: אַמַּאי אִישְׁתִּיק? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אָמֵינָא לָךְ אֲנָא רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לִי רַבָּנַן?! מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁבְקַתְּ רַבָּנַן וְעָבְדַתְּ כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה?! וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ ״נָהֲגוּ״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לִי אִיסּוּרָא?! דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּרָא, כִּי נָהֲגוּ שָׁבְקִינַן לְהוּ?! וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: כִּי אָמֵינָא לָךְ אֲנָא — מַעֲשֵׂר חָרוּבִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לִי שְׁבִיעִית דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **was silent** and did not respond, as though he had no answer. **Rabbi Abba the priest said to Rabbi Yosei the priest: Why was** Rabbi Yoḥanan **silent? He should have said to** Reish Lakish as follows: **I am speaking to you of** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, and you say to me** the opinion of **the Rabbis?** Rabbi Yosei the priest answered: He could not have made this argument, **because** Reish Lakish **would** then **have said to him: Do you abandon** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who constitute the majority, **and act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya,** who expresses a sole dissenting opinion? **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan should have said to him: I am speaking to you** only about how the people **practice** and that their custom follows the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya, **and you say to me** that it is a **prohibition?** Rabbi Yosei the priest answered: He could not have said this, **because** Reish Lakish **would** then **have said to him: Where there is a prohibition,** even **if they were accustomed to act** in a particular manner, **would we leave them** to continue? **Further, Rabbi Yoḥanan should have said to** Reish Lakish as follows: **I am speaking to you about the tithe of carobs, which is** only by **rabbinic** decree, as by Torah law all fruits apart from grapes and olives are exempt from tithing, **and you speak to me** about **the Sabbatical Year,** which is **by Torah law?** This being an irrefutable argument, the Gemara once again clarifies this matter.

*Rabbi Abba Ha-Kohen son of Rabbi Yose Ha-Kohen* asks why *Rav Yochanan* was silent. Surely he could have said: *amina lach ana Rabbi Nechemia* — I was citing *Rabbi Nechemia*, and you are citing the rabbinic majority against him. That is not a challenge directed at me; I was simply reporting *Rabbi Nechemia*'s view.

The *gemara* anticipates that *Resh Lakish* could have responded: *shavket Rabbanan ve-avdet k'Rabbi Nechemia* — are you entitled to set aside the majority rabbinic position in favor of *Rabbi Nechemia*? And *Rav Yochanan* could have answered: *ki amina lach ana nahagu* — I was not stating the normative *halacha*; I merely reported that the people practiced in accordance with *Rabbi Nechemia*. You are telling me something about *issura* — about the binding halacha — and that is a separate question.

But then *Resh Lakish* could have pressed further: *bemakom issura ki nahagu shavkinan lehu* — if the normative halacha is violated, how can you simply report the practice as if it were acceptable? This is precisely why *Rav Yochanan* should not have remained silent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא הַכֹּהֵן: תְּמֵיהַנִי אִם הֱשִׁיבָהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לִתְשׁוּבָה זוֹ. אִם הֱשִׁיבָהּ? הָא אוֹתְבַהּ! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אִם קִיבְּלָהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אִם לָא קִיבְּלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Abba the priest said: I wonder whether Reish Lakish** actually **raised this original objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, since it has such a clear refutation. The Gemara asks: **Whether he asked it? But he did ask it,** as is reported in the story. **Rather, say:** I wonder **if Rabbi Yoḥanan accepted** this question and was silent because he had nothing to answer, **or he did not accept it** but nevertheless remained silent because he thought the question was not worthy of an answer.

*Rabbi Abba Ha-Kohen* explains the resolution. *Rav Yochanan* could have distinguished between the two domains entirely. When I endorsed the practice of going by *g'mar peri* for carobs, I was speaking about *ma'aser* — *ma'aser charuvin derabbanan*, *ma'aser* for trees is rabbinic law. In the realm of rabbinic law, there is flexibility to go sometimes by *g'mar peri* and sometimes by *chanatah*, depending on practicality. That is why the people's practice can be accommodated.

But the challenge from white figs involves *shemitah* — *ve-at amret li shvi'is de-oraisa*. *Shemitah* law for trees is biblical. In the domain of biblical law, one cannot apply the same flexibility. Of course we go by *chanatah* for *shemitah* — the leniency *Rabbi Nechemia* introduced, and which the people followed regarding carobs, was never meant to apply to biblical-level obligations. *Rav Yochanan* was silent not because the question was unanswerable, but because — as *Rabbi Abba Ha-Kohen* suggests — the answer is sufficiently obvious that the silence may indicate *Rav Yochanan* did not consider the challenge to have genuinely engaged his position in the first place.

## Summary & Conclusion

### Trees Recap

To summarize the terrain covered: the general rule for trees is that *ma'aser* is determined by *chanatah* and the marker date is *tu b'shvat*. This rule has been qualified in two directions. First, the *etrog* — because of its continuous growth throughout the year — goes by *lakitah* for *ma'aser*, while still using *tu b'shvat* as the calendar marker and going by *chanatah* for *shemitah* purposes. Second, trees that ripen in a single cycle per year — such as carobs and, by implication, palms and olives — may go by *g'mar peri* rather than *chanatah*, at least for *ma'aser*. The practice of the people followed *Rabbi Nechemia* on this point for carobs, suggesting that in the realm of *ma'aser* for trees, there is sufficient latitude to make such an adjustment.

### Rabbinic Flexibility

The overarching theme that emerges from the final sugya is that the flexibility of the dating rules corresponds directly to the level of the law being applied. Where the obligation is rabbinic — as *ma'aser* for trees is *derabbanan* — there is room to choose whichever standard of measurement is practically workable and most accurately captures the intention of the law, whether blossoming, ripening, or picking. Where the obligation is biblical — as *shemitah* is *de-oraisa* — the rules are fixed and the flexibility disappears. The case of white figs, which require three years to ripen and yet take their *shemitah* status from the year of blossoming, illustrates precisely that for biblical-level *shemitah* obligations, the calendar of *chanatah* governs without exception, regardless of how long the fruit takes to mature. The entire discussion thus concludes, appropriately, on the theme of the distinction between *de-rabbanan* and *de-oraisa* as the organizing principle behind the apparent complexity of produce-year determinations.
