# Ketubot 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Admon & Chanan

### Opening

We begin today the final *perek* of *Ketubot*, opening at the very bottom of 104b. The *daf* itself is 105.

### Two Judges Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי דַּיָּינֵי גְּזֵירוֹת הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, אַדְמוֹן וְחָנָן בֶּן אֲבִישָׁלוֹם. חָנָן אוֹמֵר שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים, אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There were two** prominent **judges** who issued **decrees in Jerusalem, Admon and Ḥanan ben Avishalom. Ḥanan states two matters** about which the Sages disagreed; **Admon states seven.**

The *mishnah* opens: *shne dayane gzerot hayu biYerushalayim* — two judges who made decrees were in Jerusalem: *Admon* and *Chanan ben Avishalom*. As Rashi explains, *Chanan* said two things — meaning two rulings for which he was known, rulings with which the Sages did not agree — and *Admon* said seven. The subsequent discussion in the *perek* will bring in all of the distinctive rulings of each, some tying directly into the themes of *Ketubot*, others less so. The hook is here in the opening of the *mishnah*, and the package of rulings follows from it.

### Wife's Support Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהָלַךְ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְאִשְׁתּוֹ תּוֹבַעַת מְזוֹנוֹת, חָנָן אוֹמֵר:
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: With regard to the case of **one who went overseas and his wife is demanding sustenance,** claiming that her husband left her without funds and she is seeking a ruling that would provide for her from her husband's property, **Ḥanan says:**

Someone who went overseas and his wife demands her *mezonot*, her support. Obviously she must be able to put food on the table while her husband is away. The concern, however, is that he is not present to defend himself — perhaps he already prepaid her support, or there is some other outstanding question about what she has already collected.

### Chanan's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּשָּׁבַע בַּסּוֹף, וְלֹא תִּשָּׁבַע בַּתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **She takes an oath at the end** of their marriage, i.e., when she learns that her husband died. The oath is to the effect that he did not leave her any funds when he departed overseas, as she is claiming full payment of her marriage contract. **And she does not take an oath at the outset** of his trip overseas, when she demands support soon after his departure.

*Chanan* rules: *tishava basof velo tishava batchila* — she takes an oath at the end, not at the beginning. Rashi explains that "the end" means when the husband eventually dies and she comes to collect her full *ketubah*; at that point she takes the normal oath a woman takes when collecting a *ketubah*, and that oath will simultaneously cover any questions about what she collected during the period she was receiving her *mezonot*. If he does not die but eventually returns, the matter will presumably be dealt with at that point.

### Priests' Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְלְקוּ עָלָיו בְּנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים, וְאָמְרוּ: תִּשָּׁבַע בַּתְּחִלָּה וּבַסּוֹף. אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס כְּדִבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **The sons of High Priests disagreed with** Ḥanan's opinion **and said: She takes an oath** both **at the outset and at the end. Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas said:** The *halakha* is **in accordance with their statement,** i.e., that of the sons of the High Priests.

*Nechlaku alav bne kohanim gdolim* — the sons of the *Kohanim Gdolim* disputed this ruling. It is a notable fact that this group had a collective voice in *halacha*; during the period of *Bayit Sheni* there was indeed considerable turnover among the *Kohanim Gdolim* and presumably a large cohort of their sons. They maintained: *tishava batchila uvasof* — she must take an oath at the beginning when she comes to collect *mezonot*, and again at the end when collecting the *ketubah*. Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas ruled in accordance with their position.

### R. Yochanan Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: יָפֶה אָמַר חָנָן, לֹא תִּשָּׁבַע אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said** that **Ḥanan spoke well: She takes an oath only at the end.**

*Veamar Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai yafe amar Chanan* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai sides with *Chanan*: the oath should only be at the end. One might ask whether Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai is simply echoing *Chanan* without adding anything new. In general, when the *Gemara* records an opinion that appears merely to echo an earlier one, it seeks to understand the *chidush*. Here, however, it is clear that Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai is simply picking sides in an existing dispute, and the weight of his authority as a backing for *Chanan*'s position is itself significant, even without a novel ruling.

It should be noted that the *Gemara* today will not actually discuss this debate about *mezonot* and oaths at all. The discussion will focus entirely on the opening phrase — *shne dayane gzerot* — and use it as a platform for an extended treatment of the role, compensation, and integrity of judges.

### Tosafot on Name

Before turning to the *Gemara*, there is an interesting *Tosafot* on 104b worth noting. *Tosafot* states: *hachi gares Rabbeinu Tam* — Rabbeinu Tam's reading of the name is *Avishalom* rather than *Avshalom*. The reasoning: Rabbi Meir states in *Perek Chelek* that *Avshalom* — the son of David — has no portion in the *Olam Haba*. How then could anyone name their child after him? Therefore Rabbeinu Tam insists the name is the distinct form *Avishalom*, and pursues the discussion at some length. This, however, raises an obvious question that *Tosafot* elsewhere addresses: how do we have a *Tanna* named Rabbi Yishmael, if *Yishmael* in rabbinic literature is associated with wickedness? One could equally ask: who says the name was chosen after that particular person? Names recur without necessarily carrying reference to any specific historical bearer — just as modern Hebrew speakers use the name *Nimrod* without invoking the biblical figure. The observation stands, but the concern may be overstated.

## Two vs Three Judges

### Braita on Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: שְׁלֹשָׁה דַּיָּינֵי גְזֵילוֹת הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: אַדְמוֹן בֶּן גַּדַּאי, וְחָנָן הַמִּצְרִי, וְחָנָן בֶּן אֲבִישָׁלוֹם. קַשְׁיָא תְּלָת אַתְּרֵין, קַשְׁיָא גְּזֵירוֹת אַגְּזֵילוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that there were two judges who issued decrees [*gezeirot*] in Jerusalem. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following *baraita*: **There were three judges** who adjudicated cases **of theft [*gezeilot*] in Jerusalem: Admon ben Gaddai, Ḥanan the Egyptian, and Ḥanan ben Avishalom.** The fact that the *baraita* mentions **three** judges is **difficult, as** the mishna includes only **two;** and the fact that the judges are described in the mishna as those who issue **decrees** is also **difficult as** they are described in the *baraita* as judges who adjudicate cases of **theft.**

The *Gemara* raises a contradiction from a *braita*: rather than *dayanei gezeirot* — judges who made decrees — the *braita* describes *dayanei gezeilot* — judges who ruled on matters of theft — and lists not two but three: *Admon ben Gadai*, *Chanan HaMitzri*, and *Chanan ben Avishalom*. So there are two difficulties: this source has three where the *mishnah* has two, and this source says *gezeilot* where the *mishnah* says *gezeirot*. As the *Gemara* puts it: *kashya tlata atrei, kashya gezeirot agezeilot*.

### Most Important Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא תְּלָת אַתְּרֵין לָא קַשְׁיָא: דַּחֲשִׁיב לֵיהּ — קָתָנֵי, דְּלָא חֲשִׁיב לֵיהּ — לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted,** the contradiction between the statement that there were **three** judges and the statement **that** there were **two** is **not difficult,** as those **who are important to him** the *tanna* **teaches** in the mishna, **and** those **who are not important to him** the *tanna* **does not teach** in the mishna.

The first difficulty — three versus two — is resolved readily. *Dechashiv lei katani, delo chashiv lei lo katani*: the *mishnah* lists the most significant figures and omits the less prominent ones. The *mishnah* names *Admon* and *Chanan ben Avishalom* as the two most important, and leaves out *Chanan HaMitzri* as the third.

### Gezeirot & Gezeilot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גְּזֵירוֹת אַגְּזֵילוֹת קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שֶׁהָיוּ גּוֹזְרִין גְּזֵירוֹת עַל גְּזֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** the contradiction between the ruling that refers to **decrees** and the ruling that refers **to theft is difficult.** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** There is no contradiction, **as they would issue decrees concerning** matters of **theft,**

The second difficulty — *gezeirot* versus *gezeilot* — is resolved by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *shehayu gozrim gezeirot al gezeilot* — they would issue edicts in connection with matters of theft. Hence, depending on one's angle of description, they could be referred to either as *dayanei gezeirot* or as *dayanei gezeilot*.

### R. Yosei Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: קִיטְּמָה נְטִיעָה, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גּוֹזְרֵי גְזֵירוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם אוֹמְרִים: נְטִיעָה בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ — שְׁתֵּי כֶסֶף, בַּת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים — אַרְבַּע כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to an animal that **severed** a young **plant** in the field of another, **Rabbi Yosei says** that **those who issue decrees in Jerusalem said:** For **a plant one year old,** the animal's owner must pay **two silver** pieces; for a plant **two years old,** he pays **four silver** pieces.

A supporting *braita* is then cited: *kitema netiah* — if an animal enters a neighbor's property and nibbles at young plantings. Rabbi Yosei invokes *gozrei gezeirot she'biYerushalayim* — those who issue edicts in Jerusalem — and states that they would assign fixed values: a one-year-old planting is assessed at two silver coins, a two-year-old planting at four. This *braita* explicitly uses the phrase *gozrei gezeirot* in the context of what we might classify as *nezek* — tortious damage — rather than outright theft, which itself helps bridge the gap between *gezeirot* and *gezeilot*.

### Nature of Gezeirah

This passage opens an important conceptual point. We ordinarily think of a *gezeirah* in the rabbinic sense as a protective fence around the law — do not read by candlelight lest you tilt the flame, and similar safeguards. But here the term *gezeirah* is applied to something quite different: the act of judges establishing fixed parameters and guidelines for monetary liability. Why would these figures be called *dayanei gezeirot* rather than simply *dayanim*?

The answer seems to be that while an ordinary judge applies existing law to a given case, these judges had a specialized role: they were not merely adjudicating but actually setting down certain operative rules — fixing valuations for categories of damage, establishing procedures and frameworks — functioning in what we might call a legislative as well as a judicial capacity. In that sense, calling them *gozrei gezeirot* makes good sense: they were issuing binding rulings that set parameters for how the law would be applied. This also fits with the observation that later in the *perek* we will see a series of rulings attributed to *Admon*, some relating to *ketubot* and some not. The *mishnah* has collected this package around the hook of their identity as *gozrei gezeirot*.

### Nachum HaMadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי, שְׁלֹשָׁה דַּיָּינֵי גְזֵירוֹת הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: אַדְמוֹן וְחָנָן וְנַחוּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** between the *baraita* cited above and another *baraita*: **There were three** prominent **judges** who issued **decrees in Jerusalem: Admon, Ḥanan, and Naḥum.**

A second *braita* is then cited listing three *dayanei gezeirot* in Jerusalem: *Admon*, *Chanan*, and *Nachum* — with Nachum here being *Nachum HaMadai*, a figure familiar from the *Bameh Madlikin* chapter recited each *Shabbos* eve.

### Nachum Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַאן תְּנָא נַחוּם — רַבִּי נָתָן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אַף נַחוּם הַמָּדִי מִגּוֹזְרֵי גְזֵירוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיָה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that the third judge was **Naḥum? It is Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: Naḥum HaMadi was also among those who** would **issue decrees in Jerusalem, but the Sages did not agree with** his opinion.

Rav Pappa identifies the source of this tradition: it is Rabbi Natan, as taught in a *braita* — *af Nachum HaMadai mi-gozrei gezerot she-biYerushalayim haya* — even Nachum HaMadai was among those who issued edicts in Jerusalem, *velo hodu lo Chachamim* — but the Sages did not agree with him. The debate appears to be not about whether he adjudicated cases at all — evidently he did — but whether he held the specific official status of *gozrei gezeirot*. That question the Sages resolved against him.

## Jerusalem Institutions

### 394 Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי פִּנְחָס אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְתִשְׁעִים וְאַרְבָּעָה בָּתֵּי דִינִין הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, כְּנֶגְדָּן בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת, וּכְנֶגְדָּן בָּתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת, וּכְנֶגְדָּן בָּתֵּי סוֹפְרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** were there **no more** judges? **Didn't Rabbi Pineḥas say** that **Rabbi Oshaya said: There were 394 courts in Jerusalem, and a comparable number of synagogues, and a comparable number of study halls, and a comparable number of houses of teachers** of schoolchildren.

The *Gemara* presses further: are we really saying there were only two or three such figures? Rav Pinchas cites Rav Oshaya: *shelosh me'ot tishim ve-arba'ah batei dinim she-hayu biYerushalayim* — there were 394 courts in Jerusalem. And correspondingly, 394 synagogues and 394 study halls — a remarkable claim. The number 300 is often a stylized large number in rabbinic literature, but the additional 94 makes this figure feel unexpectedly precise.

### Rashi Clarifies

Rashi notes that the *batei dinim* in question are *batei dinim shel esrim u-shelosha* — courts of 23, meaning major *batei dinim*, not the minor courts of three. Three hundred and ninety-four courts of 23 in Jerusalem is a striking number. Rashi also glosses — perhaps unnecessarily — that *batei knesiyot* are for prayer and *batei midrashot* for *mishnah* and *talmud*, and also that *batei sofrim* refers to elementary schools for children. That last clarification is useful; one might have read *batei sofrim* as houses of scribal work, but Rashi indicates these are what we would call *cheder*.

### Specific to Edicts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיָּינִין טוּבָא הֲווֹ, וְכִי קָאָמְרִינַן — אַגּוֹזְרֵי גְזֵירוֹת קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There were many judges, but when we say** that there were a small number, it is specifically **concerning those who issue decrees** that **we say** so.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension: *ve-chi ka-amrinan a-gozrei gezerot ka-amrinan* — when we were speaking of two or three, we were speaking specifically about those who held the distinctive role of *gozrei gezeirot*, not about the full complement of judges in the city. The 394 courts are a different category entirely; the two or three figures were specialists in a particular legislative-judicial function.

## Judge Compensation

### 99 Talents Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: גּוֹזְרֵי גְזֵירוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן תִּשְׁעִים וָתֵשַׁע מָנֶה מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה, לֹא רָצוּ — מוֹסִיפִין לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said: Those who issue decrees in Jerusalem would take their wages, ninety-nine *maneh*,** equal to 9,900 dinars per year, **from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber.** If **they did not wish** to do so, **one adds** to their wages.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav Assi states: *gozrei gezerot she-biYerushalayim hayu notlin sacharam tishim ve-tisha maneh mi-trumat ha-lishka* — these figures who issued edicts in Jerusalem would receive their compensation of 99 *maneh* of silver from the *trumat ha-lishka*, the funds collected from the *machatzit ha-shekel* beyond what was needed for the *korbanot*. The surplus from those communal funds was directed toward ongoing Temple and communal needs, and the salary of these *gozrei gezeirot* was one such use.

### Payment Problem

This immediately raises a question: how could judges receive payment at all? The prohibition on *sachar la-dun* — taking a fee for rendering judgment — is well established, and the problem of *shochad* looms over any arrangement in which a judge receives money in connection with his judicial function. We will address this in detail shortly.

### Sustenance Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא רָצוּ? אַטּוּ בְּרַשִּׁיעֵי עָסְקִינַן? אֶלָּא: לֹא סָפְקוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רָצוּ — מוֹסִיפִין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of the phrase: If **they did not wish** to do so? Does this mean that if they desired higher wages, they were paid more? **Is that to say** that **we are dealing with wicked** people who demand wages beyond what they need? **Rather,** on the contrary, Rav Asi said that if their wages were **insufficient** for their needs, then **even if they did not wish** to receive higher wages, **one adds to their** wages so that they may devote themselves to their communal service.

*Lo ratzu* — if they did not want to accept that sum, *mosifin lahem* — the amount would be increased. The *Gemara* initially asks with some incredulity: *atu berishaye askinan* — are we dealing with wicked people who refuse their salary and need to be persuaded? The answer is: *ella lo safku* — it simply was not enough to cover their needs. The principle here is that the community must ensure these judges are provided for; if the fixed sum was insufficient, it would be increased. And even if the judges themselves were inclined to refuse the additional compensation, *mosifim aleihem* — you still increase it, because the obligation is to ensure their sustenance, not merely to offer what they are willing to accept.

## Karna's Case

### Karna Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַרְנָא הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל אִיסְתֵּירָא מִזַּכַּאי וְאִיסְתֵּירָא מֵחַיָּיב, וְדָאֵין לְהוּ דִּינָא. וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְשׁוֹחַד לֹא תִקָּח״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The Sage **Karna would take an *istera*,** a small coin, **from the innocent party, and an *istera* from the guilty party,** i.e., he would charge both parties that came to him for judgment, **and** then **he would judge their case.** The Gemara asks: **But how could he do so? Isn't it written: "And you shall take no bribe"** (Exodus 23:8), which indicates that a judge may not take money from either of the two litigants?

Karna — a first-generation *Amora* — would accept a coin from each side when litigants came before him: *hava shakil istira mizakai veistira mechayav* — he would take an *istira* from the one who was ultimately exonerated and an *istira* from the one who was ultimately liable. He would do this before rendering judgment, and then *vedayan lehu dina* — he would adjudicate the case.

### Bribe Question

The *Gemara* asks: *veheichi avid hachi* — how could he do this? *Vehachtiv shochad lo tikach* — the Torah says, do not take a bribe.

### One Side Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא שָׁקֵיל מִתַּרְוַיְיהוּ דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאַצְלוֹיֵי דִּינָא, קַרְנָא כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁקֵיל מִתַּרְוַיְיהוּ — לָא אָתֵי לְאַצְלוֹיֵי דִּינָא, וְכִי לָא אָתֵי לְאַצְלוֹיֵי דִּינָא מִי שְׁרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **this** prohibition against taking a bribe **applies only when** a judge **does not take from both** parties, as there is a concern that **perhaps he may come to pervert the judgment** in favor of the party that gave him the bribe, whereas in the case of **Karna, since he took from both** parties **he will not come to pervert the judgment,** who says that the verse is referring only to those circumstances? **Is it permitted** to take a bribe even in a case **when one will not pervert the judgment?**

The *Gemara* entertains a possible distinction: perhaps the prohibition is specifically *heicha deshakeil mechada gisa* — where one takes only from one side, because then *dilma ati leatzloyei dina* — one may come to tilt the judgment in favor of the side one took money from. But Karna, since he took equally from both sides, would not be drawn to tilt toward one over the other.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *vechi lo ati leatzloyei dina mi sharei* — just because it will not lead to tilting the judgment, does that make it permitted? A *braita* is cited: *shochad lo tikach* — do not take a bribe. What does the Torah need to tell us? If the concern were only about perverting judgment, it would have been sufficient to say *lo tateh mishpat* — do not pervert justice. By adding the prohibition on bribery, the Torah is telling us something beyond the prohibition on corrupt verdicts. *Ella afilu lezakot et hazakai ulechayev et hachayav amrah Torah shochad lo tikach* — even if your intent is to acquit the innocent and convict the guilty, even if you plan to judge correctly and fairly, the Torah says you may not accept a bribe.

The *Gemara* is identifying a scenario in which someone pays a judge and says: I only ask you to judge me honestly; if I am innocent, find me innocent; I am not asking you to rule in my favor if I am guilty. Even that arrangement is prohibited, because money paid in connection with the final judgment — even a just one — falls within the category of *shochad*. Given this, taking equal fees from both sides would seem no better: the *Gemara* finds it difficult to see why that should be categorically different.

### Challenge Equal Fees

The *Gemara's* challenge stands: *vechi lo ati leatzloyei dina mi sharei* — even granting that equal fees from both sides remove the directional bias of a traditional bribe, how does that remove the underlying prohibition?

## Sachar Batala

### Fee Voids Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַנּוֹטֵל שָׂכָר לָדוּן — דִּינָיו בְּטֵלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 58b): With regard to **one who takes a salary to judge** cases, **his judgments are void?**

The *mishnah* states plainly: *hanoteil sachar ladun dinav betelim* — one who takes a fee for rendering judgment, his judgments are void. This goes beyond a simple prohibition; it is grounds for retroactive invalidation of the ruling itself.

### Sachar Batala Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי אֲגַר דִּינָא, קַרְנָא אֲגַר בְּטֵילָא הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This applies only** when he took money as his **compensation for judging** the case, whereas **Karna** accepted the money **as compensation for unemployment,** i.e., as he could not engage in his usual work while dealing with the case, he would take compensation for this unemployment.

The *Gemara* resolves the question about Karna: *hani milei agar dina* — the voiding applies when you are paid for the act of judgment itself. *Hai tanna betorat agra hava shakeil* — but Karna was taking the money as a service fee, as compensation for his time, not as payment for the content of his ruling. This is the principle of *sachar batala* — compensation for lost opportunity, for the work one is not doing because one is sitting in judgment.

The principle has broad implications beyond the courtroom. The Torah states, in the phrase the *Gemara* invokes, *mah ani bechinam af atem bechinam* — just as I taught without charging a fee, so too you must teach the people without charge. This extends to teachers of Torah, doctors, and other roles where one might otherwise argue that payment is for a *mitzvah*. The conceptual problem is real: if rabbis and judges cannot be paid for their services, how do they support themselves?

The answer is *sachar batala*: we are not paying you for doing a *mitzvah*, we are paying you for the job you are not doing while you do the *mitzvah*. This is technically opportunity cost — compensation for the income foregone by spending time in a judicial or teaching role rather than in one's usual occupation. One might note, somewhat wryly, that taken strictly this would mean better-compensated judges are not the more skilled ones but the ones with higher market value in other fields; the person who left a lucrative law practice to become a rabbi would theoretically command a higher *sachar batala* than someone with no other marketable skill who happens to be an outstanding scholar. These complications are real, and the *Gemara* acknowledges that *sachar batala* as a practical mechanism involves a degree of legal fiction. Nevertheless, that is what permits it.

### Disgusting Fees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מְכוֹעָר הַדַּיָּין שֶׁנּוֹטֵל שָׂכָר לָדוּן, אֶלָּא שֶׁדִּינוֹ דִּין. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא אֲגַר דִּינָא, דִּינוֹ דִּין? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹטֵל שָׂכָר לָדוּן — דִּינָיו בְּטֵלִין! אֶלָּא אֲגַר בְּטֵילָא, וְקָתָנֵי: מְכוֹעָר הַדַּיָּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** it **permitted** to take money **as compensation for unemployment? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Ugly is the judge who takes a salary to judge** cases; **however, his judgments are** valid **judgments?** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this *baraita*? **If we say** that it is referring to one who accepted money as his **compensation for judging,** are **his judgments** valid **judgments? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 58b): With regard to **one who takes a salary to judge** cases, **his judgments are void? Rather,** it must certainly be referring to a situation where he takes money as **compensation for unemployment, and** yet the *baraita* **teaches: Ugly is the judge.**

A *braita* complicates the picture: *mechoar hadayan shenotel schar ladun, ella shedino din* — it is disgusting for a judge to take fees for his judgment, yet his judgment nevertheless stands. The *Gemara* asks: what case is this describing? If it is *agar dina* — a fee for rendering judgment — how can the *braita* say the ruling stands, when the *mishnah* says *dinav betelim*? It must therefore be describing *agar batala* — compensation for lost work. Yet the *braita* calls it disgusting. So even *sachar batala* is considered *mechoar*?

### Obvious Loss Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּטֵילָא דְּלָא מוֹכְחָא. קַרְנָא בְּטֵילָא דְמוֹכְחָא הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל, דַּהֲוָה תָּהֵי בְּאַמְבָּרָא דְחַמְרָא וְיָהֲבִי לֵיהּ זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** statement that the judge is ugly **applies only** when the fact that he is taking a salary for his **unemployment is not evident,** as he was not engaged in some other type of work at the time. **Karna,** however, **would take** money for his **unemployment** when **it was evident** that he was taking time off work to judge the case, **as he was examining** people's **wine stores [*ambara*]** to see which casks would last and which were going sour, **and they would pay him** one **dinar** as a salary.

The resolution: *hani milei batala dela muchach* — that is when the lost opportunity is not obvious. If it is not apparent to onlookers that the judge is genuinely forgoing other income by sitting in court, the arrangement looks like being paid for the judgment itself, even if technically it is *sachar batala*. That situation is *mechoar* — disgraceful in appearance, even if technically permitted. *Karna batala demuchach havei shakil* — but Karna's situation was one of obvious, demonstrable lost opportunity.

How was it obvious in Karna's case? *Dehavei tohei be'ambara dechamra* — he worked as a wine assessor, smelling in warehouse-style wine storage to determine which casks were approaching spoilage, and advising the owners accordingly. *Veyahavi lei zuza* — they would pay him for this service. When Karna left that work to sit in judgment, anyone could see that he was forgoing readily available income. The arrangement of collecting a fee from each litigant was therefore transparently a reimbursement for lost earnings, not a payment for the judgment.

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי דִּינָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי גַּבְרָא דְּדָלֵי לִי בַּחֲרִיקַאי וְאֵידוּן לְכוּ דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** This **resembles** an incident involving **Rav Huna. When** people **would come** for **judgment before him,** he would **say to them:** As I am unable to take time off from my work, **give me a man who can draw water for me,** to irrigate the fields in my place, **and I will judge your case.**

A parallel story: Rav Huna, when a case came before him, would say to the litigants: *havu li gavra dedali li becharitai* — provide me a man to draw water in my place. He drew water for a living, and would not sit in judgment without someone covering his ordinary daily labor. *Ve'idun lechu dina* — and then I will judge your case. Again, the lost opportunity was concrete, visible, and undeniable.

## Tosafot Compensation

### R. Tam View

> **[This section is analyzed within the context of the Sefaria text above, without a new blockquote, as it does not cite new Talmudic passages but rather discusses how medieval authorities interpret the sources we have already read.]**

Returning to the question of how the *gozrei gezeirot* of Jerusalem were paid from *trumat ha-lishka* — public Temple funds — *Tosafot* offers two distinct approaches. Rabbeinu Tam's view: *mishum de-lo assur ela mi-ba'alei dinin* — the entire problem is when money comes from the litigants themselves, because that is where the risk of *shochad* arises. When payment comes from communal funds, it is outside that ambit entirely. The concerns about *sachar ladun* and *shochad* apply specifically to the money-for-judgment relationship between judge and litigant; public funding from *trumat ha-lishka* simply does not implicate those concerns, and no further legal fiction is required.

### Community Support

A second approach — attributed in this *Tosafot* to *Rebbi*, which, it should be noted, is the *Tosafot* of the school of Rabbeinu Shimshon, as stated at the opening of the tenth *perek* at 90a — offers a different framework. It is not that payment from public funds is simply permissible because it avoids the litigant relationship. Rather: these judges sat in court at all hours and engaged in no other occupation whatsoever; they had no means of support. The community had effectively required them to have no other source of income. Having placed them in that position, the community now bore a responsibility to sustain them — not as payment for services rendered, but as an obligation of *parnasah* toward people who are impoverished as a direct consequence of communal need.

This is a striking distinction. In the *sachar batala* model, one is compensated for a real alternative that one is forgoing. For a full-time judge with no alternative occupation, there is no such alternative — but the community has created the very condition of his poverty. The payment is therefore not for the *mitzvah* of judging; it is in fulfillment of a communal duty to support someone the community has rendered unable to support himself. The two models thus represent quite different understandings of the jurisprudential problem: one sidesteps it by removing the litigant relationship; the other works around it by recharacterizing the payment as communal welfare rather than compensation for services.

## Bribes Blind (II)

### Eye Disease Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה סְמוּיוֹת עֵינֵיהֶן שֶׁל מְקַבְּלֵי שׁוֹחַד. אָדָם חָשׁ בְּעֵינָיו, נוֹתֵן מָמוֹן לָרוֹפֵא — סָפֵק מִתְרַפֵּא סָפֵק אֵינוֹ מִתְרַפֵּא. וְהֵן נוֹטְלִין שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה וּמְסַמִּין עֵינֵיהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַשּׁוֹחַד יְעַוֵּר פִּקְחִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: Come and see how blind are the eyes of those who accept bribes,** and how they ruin themselves. If **a person has pain in his eyes,** he **gives a doctor money,** and even then **it is uncertain** whether **he will be healed** or **whether he will not be healed. And** yet those judges **take** the **value** of **a *peruta*,** a small amount of money as a bribe, **and** actively **blind their eyes, as it is stated: "For a bribe blinds those who have sight"** (Exodus 23:8).

Rabbi Abahu opens a striking reflection on the nature of *shochad*. He asks: come and see how blind are the eyes of those who accept bribes. A person who has a problem with his eye gives money to a doctor, and it is uncertain whether the doctor will heal it or not — he pays without any guarantee of benefit. Yet those who accept bribes take even a trivial sum and it blinds them. The asymmetry is the point: medical treatment cannot reliably heal a diseased eye even with payment, but bribery blinds infallibly even for a trifling amount.

## Bribes Blind (II)

### Exodus Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי הַשּׁוֹחַד יְעַוֵּר עֵינֵי חֲכָמִים״, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַטִּפְּשִׁין. ״וִיסַלֵּף דִּבְרֵי צַדִּיקִים״, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לָרְשָׁעִים. מִידֵּי טִפְּשִׁים וּרְשָׁעִים בְּנֵי דִינָא נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״כִּי הַשּׁוֹחַד יְעַוֵּר עֵינֵי חֲכָמִים״, אֲפִילּוּ חָכָם גָּדוֹל וְלוֹקֵחַ שׁוֹחַד — אֵינוֹ נִפְטָר מִן הָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא סַמְיוּת הַלֵּב. ״וִיסַלֵּף דִּבְרֵי צַדִּיקִים״,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "For a bribe blinds the eyes of the wise"** (Deuteronomy 16:19); ***a fortiori*** it will certainly blind the eyes **of fools. "And perverts the words of the righteous"** (Deuteronomy 16:19); ***a fortiori*** it will certainly pervert the statements **of the wicked.** The Gemara asks: **Are fools and the wicked suitable for judgment,** i.e., to be appointed as judges? **Rather, this is what** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **said: "For a bribe blinds the eyes of the wise"; even** if he were **very wise but he took a bribe,** he **will not leave** this **world without** suffering **blindness of the heart,** i.e., he will eventually turn foolish. **"And perverts the words of the righteous";**

The *Gemara* cites the verse from Exodus 23:8: *ki hashochad ya'aver pikchim* — for bribery blinds the open-eyed. The phrase *pikchim* — the open-eyed, the perceptive — is precisely the target.

A *braita* extends this: *ki hashochad ya'aver einei chachamim* — bribery blinds the eyes of the wise; *kal v'chomer l'tipshim* — how much more so the foolish.

And further: *v'isaleif divrei tzadikim* — it perverts the words of the righteous; *kal v'chomer l'rasha'im* — how much more so the wicked.

The *Gemara* raises an obvious difficulty: *atu tipshim u'rasha'im bnei dina ninhu* — would we appoint foolish and wicked people as judges in the first place? The very framing of the *kal v'chomer* seems to assume we are speaking about sitting judges. Why would the *braita* be warning about fools and wicked men on the bench?

The resolution reframes the *kal v'chomer* entirely.

### Righteous Corrupted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ צַדִּיק גָּמוּר וְלוֹקֵחַ שׁוֹחַד — אֵינוֹ נִפְטָר מִן הָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא טֵירוּף דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **even if he is completely righteous but he took a bribe, he will not leave** this **world without becoming demented.**

The statement *ki hashochad ya'aver einei chachamim* means: even someone who is a great sage at the time he accepts a bribe, *eino niftar min ha'olam b'lo smiyut halev* — he does not leave the world without his heart becoming blinded. The first time he accepts *shochad* he is still a *chacham*; by the second time, the *shochad* has already made him into a *tipesh* — the *kal v'chomer* is thus a temporal progression, not a contrast between two different categories of judges. Similarly: *afilu tzadik gamur v'loke'ach shochad* — even a complete *tzadik* who accepts *shochad eino niftar min ha'olam b'lo tiruf hada'at* — does not leave the world without his mind being perverted, effectively becoming a *rasha*. The *kal v'chomer* again describes what the bribe does to him over time.

## King & Judge Self-Sufficiency

### King Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אֲמַר: דָּרֵשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר כֹּהֵן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״מֶלֶךְ בְּמִשְׁפָּט יַעֲמִיד אָרֶץ וְאִישׁ תְּרוּמוֹת יֶהֶרְסֶנָּה״ — אִם דּוֹמֶה דַּיָּין לְמֶלֶךְ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִכְלוּם — ״יַעֲמִיד אָרֶץ״. וְאִם דּוֹמֶה לְכֹהֵן שֶׁמְּחַזֵּר עַל הַגֳּרָנוֹת — ״יֶהֶרְסֶנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Rav Naḥman bar Kohen interpreted** a verse **homiletically** as follows. **What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The king by justice establishes the land, but he who exacts gifts [*terumot*] overthrows it"** (Proverbs 29:4)? **If a judge is like a king,** in **that he does not need anything** and is not dependent on anyone, **he establishes the land,** i.e., he can serve as a judge. **But if he is like a priest, who seeks out** his *terumot* **from** various **granaries,** as he is dependent on others, **he overthrows** the land.

Rav Dimi cites a teaching of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak on the verse: *melech b'mishpat ya'amid aretz* — a king through justice establishes the land, *v'ish trumot yeherseina* — and a man of *trumot* will destroy it. The word *trumot* is read not as donations but as related to neediness — one who is always extending his hand. The interpretation: *im domeh dayan l'melech she'eino tzarich l'klum* — if the judge is like a king who needs nothing from anyone, who is fully self-sufficient, *ya'amid aretz* — he will establish the land. *Aval im domeh l'kohen she'mechazer al hagranot* — but if he is like a *kohen* who goes from one threshing floor to the next seeking his *terumah*, who is needy, whose hand is perpetually out, *yeherseina* — that judge will destroy it.

### Needy Judge

The image of the *kohen* going from threshing floor to threshing floor is vivid: not corrupt in the obvious sense, perhaps, but dependent — always needing things from people, never approaching a case from a position of independence. That dependence, even without explicit bribery, creates the conditions for compromised judgment.

### Borrowing Judge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: הַאי דַּיָּינָא דְּשָׁאֵיל שְׁאֵילְתָא — פָּסוּל לְמֵידַן דִּינָא. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ לְאוֹשׁוֹלֵי, אֲבָל אִית לֵיהּ לְאוֹשׁוֹלֵי — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar Rav Sheila said: This judge who borrows** items **from others is disqualified from rendering judgment** because it is as though he accepts a salary. **And we said this only** in a case **where he does not have** articles **to lend** out to others but is constantly borrowing without lending objects in turn. **However, if he has** items **to lend** out to others, **we have no** problem **with it.**

Rabba bar Rav Shila adds a practical ruling: *hai dayana d'shail she'ilta* — a judge who borrows objects, *pasul l'meidan dina* — is invalid to render judgment. The *she'ilta* here is literal borrowing of objects — asking a neighbor for a ladder, a few eggs, use of a car. Rashi clarifies: *d'ragil l'shail mivnei iro b'hemot v'kelim* — it must be a habitual pattern, not a one-time occurrence. Such a judge presents himself to the community as someone with needs, as someone dependent on others — and that dependence creates precisely the dynamic of the *kohen ha'mechazer al hagranot*. People who have lent him things may have their day in court; people who anticipate being able to do him favors may calculate accordingly.

### Reciprocal Borrowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא רָבָא שָׁאֵיל שְׁאֵילְתָא מִדְּבֵי בַּר מָרִיּוֹן אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שָׁיְילִי מִינֵּיהּ? הָתָם לְאַחְשׁוֹבִינְהוּ הוּא דְּבָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rava** would **borrow** items **from the house of bar Maryon even though they would not borrow from him.** The Gemara answers: **There, he wanted to cause them to** be considered **more important** in the community.

*V'lo aman ela d'leis lei l'oshulei* — and this applies only when the judge has nothing to lend others. *Aval is lei l'oshulei* — but if he also lends to others, then *leis lan bah* — it is not a problem. When there is genuine reciprocity — borrowing a cup of sugar and lending one in return, so to speak — the relationship is that of neighbors rather than that of petitioner and patron. The power dynamic disappears, and with it the concern.

Yet the *Gemara* challenges this: *Rava shashilta midbei Bar Maryon* — Rava borrowed objects from the household of Bar Maryon, *af gav d'lo shailei minei* — even though they never borrowed from him in return, yet he remained willing to serve as their judge. The *Gemara* responds: *hasam lachshuvinhu hu d'baei* — there, Rava was not borrowing out of genuine need; he was doing it in order to honor them, to confer a sense of distinction upon them by having the great Rava come and borrow something from their household. One might still wonder whether the perception problem remains — whether those litigants, or observers, might think that Rava was now beholden to Bar Maryon — but the *Gemara* lets this stand as Rava's practice.

## Unconscious Bias

### Gift Identification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּשׁוּחְדָּא? כֵּיוָן דְּקַבֵּיל לֵיהּ שׁוּחְדָּא מִינֵּיהּ, אִיקָּרְבָא לֵיהּ דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְגַבֵּיהּ וְהָוֵי כְּגוּפֵיהּ, וְאֵין אָדָם רוֹאֶה חוֹבָה לְעַצְמוֹ. מַאי ״שׁוֹחַד״ — שֶׁהוּא חַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason for** the prohibition against taking **a bribe? Once** a judge **accepts a bribe from** one party, **his thoughts draw closer to him and he becomes like his** own **self, and a person does not find fault in himself.** The Gemara notes that the term itself alludes to this idea: **What is** the meaning of ***shoḥad*,** bribe? It can be read as: ***Shehu ḥad*,** as he is one, i.e., at one mind with the litigant.

Rava offers what is perhaps the deepest analysis of why *shochad* corrupts. *Mai tama d'shochda* — what is the operative mechanism of bribery? *Keivan d'kablah shochda minei* — once a judge accepts a gift from someone, *ikrav leh daatei legabei* — his mind draws close to that person. *Vahavei k'gufei* — and the person becomes like himself to the judge.

### Self-Blindness

*V'ein adam roeh chov l'atzmo* — a person cannot see his own faults. This is not a character failing but a structural feature of human psychology: no one can be fully objective about oneself. Once a judge has accepted something from a litigant and that person has become, subconsciously, like an extension of the judge's own self, objectivity is structurally impossible — not because the judge is wicked or even negligent, but because the mechanism of self-perception precludes it. Even the most sincere attempt at fairness cannot fully overcome the identification created by the gift. This is why the Torah's prohibition applies even when the judge intends to rule correctly: the corruption is not in the intent but in the psychology.

### Shochad Etymology

*Mai shochad* — what does the word *shochad* itself mean? *Shehu k'chad* — that he becomes as one with the person from whom he received it. The word encodes the very dynamic it prohibits.

## Love & Hate

### Recusal Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא לֵידוּן אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְמַאן דְּרָחֵים לֵיהּ, וְלָא לְמַאן דְּסָנֵי לֵיהּ. דְּרָחֵים לֵיהּ — לָא חָזֵי לֵיהּ חוֹבָה, דְּסָנֵי לֵיהּ — לָא חָזֵי לֵיהּ זְכוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: A person should not judge a case involving one whom he loves, nor involving one whom he hates.** He should not judge one **whom he loves,** as **he will not find any fault in him,** while with regard to one **whom he hates, he will not find any merit in him.**

Rav Pappa rules: *lo lidun inish dina l'man d'rachim leh* — a person should not judge a case involving someone he loves, *v'lo l'man d'sani leh* — nor someone he hates. This applies even to a mild positive or negative inclination, not only to the formally established categories of *ohev v'soneh* with which *halacha* is familiar.

### Love & Hate Effects

The reasoning follows directly from Rava's analysis: *d'rachim leh lo chazi leh chova* — one who loves the litigant will be unable to perceive any negative point against him; *d'sani leh lo chazi leh zachusa* — one who hates him will be unable to perceive any merit in his favor. The blindness is not necessarily willful; it is the natural consequence of a distorted lens.

## Community Love

### Beloved Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן דִּמְרַחֲמִין לֵיהּ בְּנֵי מָתָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּמְעַלֵּי טְפֵי, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מוֹכַח לְהוּ בְּמִילֵּי דִּשְׁמַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to **this Torah scholar who is beloved by the residents** of his town, **it is not because he is a superior** Sage than others; **rather,** it is **because he does not reprove them in Heavenly matters.** He is beloved because he is not strict with them with regard to the observance of mitzvot.

Abaye offers a sobering observation about communal dynamics: *hai tzurva meirabanan d'merachmin leh bnei masa* — a Torah scholar who is universally beloved by his community — *lav mishum d'maali tvei* — do not take this as evidence that he is exceptional. *Ella mishum d'lo mochech lhu b'milei d'shmaya* — rather, it likely reflects the fact that he is not doing his job: he is not rebuking them, not setting them straight, not challenging them in matters of heaven. They love him because he tells them what they want to hear.

### Rava's Realization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי בְּנֵי מָחוֹזָא כּוּלְּהוּ רָחֲמוּ לִי. כֵּיוָן דַּהֲוַאי דַּיָּינָא, אָמֵינָא: מִינַּיְיהוּ סָנוּ לִי וּמִינַּיְיהוּ רָחֲמוּ לִי. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזַאי דְּמַאן דְּמִיחַיַּיב (לֵיהּ) הָאִידָּנָא קָא זָכֵי לִמְחַר, אָמֵינָא: אִם מִרְחָם — כּוּלְּהוּ רָחֲמוּ לִי, אִי מִסְנוֹ — כּוּלְּהוּ סָנוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: At first I would say** that **all these residents of Meḥoza love me;** however, **once I became a judge I said** that **some of them hate me and some of them love me,** as I assumed that their feelings toward me depended on the success of their case. **When I saw that the one I declared guilty today** would be **found innocent the following day,** I realized that my rulings do not determine their attitudes, and therefore **I said: If** they **love,** then **they all love me,** and **if** they **hate,** then **they all hate me,** regardless of what happens in the courtroom.

Rava offers a personal reflection. *Meireish havah amina hani bnei Mechuza kulhu rachmu li* — at first I thought that all the people of Mechuza loved me. We know that Rava had a complicated relationship with the cosmopolitan inhabitants of Mechuza. Once he became a judge and was forced to rule against some of them, he revised his assessment: *minaihu sanu li u'minaihu rachmu li* — some of them hate me and some of them love me. He had initially assumed that universal love reflected his personal qualities; he later understood that the moment one begins ruling against people, love becomes selective.

### Accept Both

The deeper realization: *d'man d'mechayev leh hai idna mazchei leh l'machar* — the one I ruled against today I may rule in favor of tomorrow. Since the same person who is ruled against in one case will be ruled for in another, the idea that one can calibrate one's rulings to achieve universal approval is simply false. Rava concluded: *im rachmu kulhu rachmu li v'im sanu kulhu sanu li* — whatever they feel about me, they feel. A judge who is trying to be universally liked is not a good judge; you cannot satisfy everyone, and eventually you must accept that some will love you and some will hate you depending on when they appear before you and what you are forced to rule. This is, as the *Gemara* presents it, a genuinely liberating insight for anyone who must render judgment or teach with integrity.

## Non-Monetary Bribes

### Verbal Bribes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשׁוֹחַד לֹא תִקָּח״, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שׁוֹחַד מָמוֹן, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹחַד דְּבָרִים נָמֵי אָסוּר, מִדְּלָא כְּתִיב ״בֶּצַע לֹא תִקָּח״. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי שׁוֹחַד דְּבָרִים?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: "And you shall take no bribe"** (Exodus 23:8). **It is not necessary to say** that this includes **bribery** by means of **money; however, even verbal bribery,** assisting by means of speech, **is also prohibited.** The *halakha* that a bribe is not necessarily monetary is derived **from** the fact **that it is not written: And you shall take no profit.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of bribing with words?**

A *braita* establishes: *eino tzarich lomar shochad mamon* — it goes without saying that monetary bribes are prohibited; *ela afilu shochad devarim nami assur* — even *shochad devarim*, which the *Gemara* will illustrate with actions and services rather than words, is also forbidden. The word *devarim* here may be read as "things" or "matters" rather than specifically "words," and the examples that follow make clear it encompasses non-verbal favors and services.

### Shmuel Bridge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הֲוָה עָבַר בְּמַבָּרָא, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ יְדֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתָּיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This can be demonstrated **by that** episode **involving Shmuel, who was** once **crossing** a river **on** a narrow **ferry. A certain man came** along **and gave him a hand** to help him out of the ferryboat. Shmuel **said to him: What are you doing** in this place? The man **said to him: I have a case** to present before you for judgment. Shmuel **said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case,** as you did me a favor. Although no money changed hands, a bond was formed between the pair.

Shmuel was crossing a bridge when a certain man came and gave him his hand to help him across. Shmuel asked: what brings you here? The man replied: I have a court case before you — I was coming to the city for the judgment, so I helped you along the way. *Psilna lach ledina* — Shmuel declared: you have disqualified me from judging your case.

### Ameimar Feather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר הֲוָה יָתֵיב וְקָא דָאֵין דִּינָא. פְּרַח גַּדְפָּא אַרֵישֵׁיהּ, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא שַׁקְלֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתָּיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar story. **Ameimar was sitting and judging a case** when **a feather floated** and landed **on his head. A certain man came** by and **removed it** from his head. Ameimar **said to him: What are you doing** here? **He said to him: I have a case** to present before you. Ameimar **said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case,** due to the favor you performed for me.

Ameimar was seated in judgment when a feather fell on his head. A certain man came and removed it. Ameimar asked him what he was doing; the man replied that he had a case before him. *Psilna lach ledina* — you have disqualified me.

### Mar Ukva Spit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר עוּקְבָא הֲוָה שְׁדֵי רוּקָּא קַמֵּיהּ, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא כַּסְּיֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתָּיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara likewise relates: **There was spittle lying before Mar Ukva. A certain man came** by and **covered it. He said to him: What are you doing** here? **He said to him: I have a case** to present before you. Mar Ukva **said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case.**

Mar Ukva spat in front of him. A certain man came and covered the spit. Again, the man said he had a judgment before him. *Psilna lach ledina*.

What is notable in all three cases is that in each instance the person explicitly mentions the upcoming court case. This raises the question of whether the problem is specifically the framing — the fact that the favor was articulated in connection with the judgment — or whether any unsolicited favor, even without such mention, would suffice to disqualify. If someone had helped Shmuel across a bridge and only appeared in his court three hours later without any explicit connection being drawn, would recusal be required? Perhaps; but the *Gemara's* examples all feature the explicit verbal linking of the favor to the judgment. Whether *shochad devarim* requires that conscious framing by the benefactor, or whether the mere receipt of the favor creates the disqualification, is a question the *Gemara* does not resolve explicitly here.

## Sharecropper Fruit

### Early Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הֲוָה רְגִיל אֲרִיסֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ כׇּל מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא כַּנְתָּא דְפֵירֵי. יוֹמָא חַד אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ בְּחַמְשָׁה בְּשַׁבְּתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא הָאִידָּנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי, וְאָמֵינָא, אַגַּב אוֹרְחִי אַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ לְמָר. לָא קַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another incident. The **sharecropper of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, was accustomed to bringing him a basket [*kanta*]** full **of fruits every Shabbat eve. One day, he brought him** the basket **on a Thursday.** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: What is different** that you came early **now,** this week? The sharecropper **said to him: I have a case** to present before you, **and I said** to myself that **along my way I will bring to the Master** the basket of fruits, as in any case I am coming on Thursday, the day the courts are in session. Rabbi Yishmael **did not accept** the basket of fruits **from him,** and he **said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case.**

Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yossi had a sharecropper who, every Friday, would bring him a basket of produce — his share from the fields the sharecropper worked. One Thursday, the sharecropper brought the basket a day early. Rabbi Yishmael asked: why the change? The sharecropper explained that he had to come into the city early because he had a court case, and since he was passing by anyway, he thought he would bring the produce ahead of schedule.

### Recusal & Replacement

Rabbi Yishmael refused to accept the basket: *psilna lach ledina* — you have disqualified me from judging your case. He appointed two other rabbis to hear the case in his stead.

### Unconscious Arguments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתֵיב זוּזָא דְרַבָּנַן וְקָדָיְינִין לֵיהּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָזֵיל וְאָתֵי אֲמַר: אִי בָּעֵי — טָעֵין הָכִי, וְאִי בָּעֵי — טָעֵין הָכִי. אָמַר: תִּיפַּח נַפְשָׁם שֶׁל מְקַבְּלֵי שׁוֹחַד! וּמָה אֲנִי שֶׁלֹּא נָטַלְתִּי, וְאִם נָטַלְתִּי — שֶׁלִּי נָטַלְתִּי, כָּךְ, מְקַבְּלֵי שׁוֹחַד — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **seated a pair of rabbinic** scholars **and they judged** the sharecropper's case. **As** Rabbi Yishmael **was coming and going, he said** to himself: **If he wants, he** could **claim this, and if he wants, he could claim that,** i.e., he kept thinking of all the ways in which the litigant who brought him the fruits could win his case. **He said** to himself: **Blast the souls of those who accept bribes. If I, who did not accept** anything, **and if I had accepted, I** would have **accepted my own** property, as it is my sharecropper and the fruits legally belong to me, am nevertheless in **this** state of mind due to the proposed gift, **all the more so** are **those who** actually **accept bribes** inevitably biased in favor of the one who bribed them.

While the two rabbis were deliberating and Rabbi Yishmael was walking about nearby, he found himself thinking: *ei ba'ei ta'in hachi, ve'ei ba'ei ta'in hachi* — if he wants to argue this way he should, if he wants to argue that way he should. He was spontaneously generating arguments in favor of the sharecropper.

### Subconscious Bias

He exclaimed: *tipach nafsham shel mekablei shochad* — blasted be the souls of those who accept bribes! And then he reflected on his own situation: I refused to take the produce. And even had I taken it, it was mine — it was my legitimate portion of the field that was merely being delivered one day early. Yet even so, that slight early delivery, that small favor never actually accepted, was already generating unconscious advocacy on his behalf in my mind. *Al achat kama vechama hamekablei shochad* — how much more so those who actually accept bribes, even with every intention of judging fairly.

## First Shearings

### Shearings Delivery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר אֱלִישָׁע אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ הַהוּא גַּבְרָא רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵהֵיכָא אַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדּוּךְ פְּלָן. וּמֵהָתָם לְהָכָא לָא הֲוָה כֹּהֵן לְמִיתְּבָא לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי, וְאָמֵינָא, אַגַּב אוֹרְחַאי אַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ לְמָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא, לָא קַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara likewise relates with regard to **Rabbi Yishmael bar Elisha,** who was a priest, that **a certain man** once **brought him the first shearing.** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: From where are you?** The man **said to him:** I am **from such and such a place.** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: And from there to here was there no priest to** whom you could **give** the first shearing? **He said to him: I have a case** to present before you, **and I said** to myself that **along my way I will bring to the Master** the first shearing. Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case,** and he would **not accept** the first shearing **from him.**

Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Elisha — a *kohen* — was entitled to receive *reishit hagez*, the first shearings of a flock, from anyone in his vicinity. A certain man traveled a considerable distance to bring him these shearings. Rabbi Yishmael asked where he was from; when told, he asked: was there truly no *kohen* anywhere between there and here to whom you could have given this? The man admitted: I have a court case before you, and since I was traveling to the city anyway, I thought I would bring the shearings to you.

### Second Recusal

Rabbi Yishmael declared: *psilna lach ledina* — you have disqualified me. *Lo kabil minei* — he declined to accept the shearings. He again appointed two other rabbis to hear the case.

### Arguments Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתֵיב לֵיהּ זוּגָא דְרַבָּנַן וְקָדָיְינִי לֵיהּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָזֵיל וְאָתֵי, אֲמַר: אִי בָּעֵי טָעֵין הָכִי, וְאִי בָּעֵי טָעֵין הָכִי. אָמַר: תִּיפַּח נַפְשָׁם שֶׁל מְקַבְּלֵי שׁוֹחַד! וּמָה אֲנִי שֶׁלֹּא נָטַלְתִּי, וְאִם נָטַלְתִּי — שֶׁלִּי נָטַלְתִּי, כָּךְ, מְקַבְּלֵי שׁוֹחַד — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael bar Elisha **seated a pair of rabbinic** scholars **and they judged his** case. **As** Rabbi Yishmael **was coming and going, he said** to himself: **If he wants, he** could **claim this, and if he wants, he could claim that. He said** to himself: **Blast the souls of those who accept bribes. If I, who did not accept** anything, **and if I had accepted, I** would have **accepted my own** property, as I am a priest and am entitled to receive the first shearing, am nevertheless in **this** state of mind, **all the more so** are **those who accept bribes.**

And again, while the rabbis were deliberating, Rabbi Yishmael found himself pacing and thinking: *ei baisun hachi, ei baisun hachi* — if they argue this way, if they argue that way. Again, arguments in favor of the man who had been coming to bring him his due were forming in his mind unbidden.

### Entitled Benefit

His reflection again: *tipach nafsham shel mekablei shochad* — blasted be the souls of those who accept bribes. I did not accept the shearings. And even had I accepted them, I was legally entitled to them — they were literally mine by right. Yet even the entitlement, even the knowledge that this man had made a journey to bring me something I was owed, was enough to color my thinking subconsciously. *Mekablei shochad al achat kama vechama* — those who actually accept bribes, however certain they are of their own intentions, cannot escape the mechanism that Rava identified: the gift creates identification, the identification creates blindness, and the blindness is impervious to good intentions.
