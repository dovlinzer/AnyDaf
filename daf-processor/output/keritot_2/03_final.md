# Keritot 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Keritot

### Masechet Overview

*Masechet* Keritot is another short *masechet* of roughly 30 *daf*, placing it among the several brief tractates that conclude the *Seder Kodashim*. In many ways, however, it feels quite different from the other *Kodashim* material covered in recent months. The bulk of *Seder Kodashim* focuses either on the mechanics of bringing *korbanot* — the specific *mitzvot* governing the sacrificial process — or on more metaphysical questions of *kedusha*: how holiness inheres in an offering, how it transfers, and what its implications are. Keritot, by contrast, is a *masechet* centered on *mitzvot*, obligations, and transgressions in a far more conventional sense. The animating questions here are: when does one incur an obligation to bring a *korban chatat*? What counts as an unintentional violation? How many *chatatot* must one bring if multiple sins were committed in a single state of forgetfulness?

### Focus on Chatat

The pivot around which the entire *masechet* turns is the *korban chatat*. One brings a *chatat* for sins that carry *karet* when committed intentionally. But the *masechet* ranges more broadly than that. There is the *asham talui*, brought when one is uncertain whether a sin requiring a *chatat* was committed at all. There are forms of *asham* that are sin offerings but not *chatatot* in the classical sense. And there are *chatatot* that are not about sin at all — the most prominent example being the *yoledet*, a woman who has given birth, who brings a *chatat* as part of the completion of her purification. As discussed in an earlier context, *chatat* carries a meaning of cleansing and restoration, not only of atonement for wrongdoing. Part of the *masechet* thus addresses the full range of obligations that fall broadly within the *chatat* category.

A further major theme is the question of multiplicity: if one committed several sins in a single state of forgetfulness — *helem achat* — does one bring a single *chatat* or multiple *chatatot*? And if one forgot *Shabbat* entirely and performed multiple *melachot*, how many offerings does that generate? These are questions about obligations and *aveirot* in a manner that feels far more like a standard *masechet* than the more specialized *Kodashim* material preceding it.

### Text Transmission

One distinctive feature of Keritot is its language. It is among the five *masechtot* whose Aramaic has a distinctly *Yerushalmi* flavor — it does not read like standard Babylonian Aramaic. The scholarly consensus holds that this reflects an earlier redaction or a redaction in a different location within *Bavel*, in a region where the Aramaic was closer to Palestinian Aramaic. That said, the text of Keritot is considerably more stable than that of *Temurah*, where variant readings and brackets proliferated throughout. Here the actual text is relatively settled, likely because the *masechet* was studied more frequently — which makes sense, given its more accessible subject matter.

### Pseudo-Rashi Issue

In terms of *mefarshim*, the first pages of the *masechet* carry no *Tosafot* at all — and indeed *Tosafot* appears only sparsely throughout. This means the pages are quite large in terms of sheer *Gemara* text, but the bulk of *Rashi* on each page compensates by keeping the overall page dimensions manageable.

The *Rashi* here, however, is strikingly verbose. On the very first *mishna*, which lists the 36 categories of *karet*, the commentary proceeds to enumerate each sin individually, cite the *pasuk* that prohibits it, and cite the *pasuk* that establishes it as *karet* — an approach entirely unlike *Rashi*'s characteristic terseness. This raises the obvious question of authenticity. While Strack and Stemberger, the standard reference on the *Talmud*, do not mention Keritot specifically, the Jewish Encyclopedia under the heading of *Rashi* records that scholars have concluded the *Rashi* printed on Keritot is not authentic. Zunz was the first to demonstrate this. The true author is unknown — possibly one of *Rashi*'s students — but the attribution is no longer tenable. Practically speaking, the verbosity of the pseudo-*Rashi* is not a burden for the learner following along in the *shiur*, even if it requires attention during preparation.

## 36 Karets List

### Karet Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשִׁים וָשֵׁשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת בְּתוֹרָה: הַבָּא עַל הָאֵם, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אָב, וְעַל הַכַּלָּה, הַבָּא עַל הַזָּכוּר, וְעַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וְאִשָּׁה הַמְּבִיאָה הַבְּהֵמָה עָלֶיהָ, הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, וְאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אָבִיו, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אִמּוֹ, וְעַל הַנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There are **thirty-six** cases **in the Torah** with regard to which one who performs a prohibited action intentionally is liable to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*].** They are: **One who engages in sexual intercourse with** his **mother; or with the wife of** his **father,** even if she is not his mother; **or with** his **daughter-in-law.** The same punishment is imposed on a man **who engages in intercourse with** another **male [*hazekhur*], or** who copulates **with an animal; and a woman who brings an animal upon her** to engage in bestiality. The same punishment is imposed on **one who engages in intercourse with a woman and her daughter, or** with **a married woman.** The same punishment is imposed on **one who engages in intercourse with his sister, or with his father's sister, or with his mother's sister, or with his wife's sister, or with his brother's wife, or with the wife of his father's brother, or with the wife of his mother's brother, or with a menstruating woman.**

The *mishna* opens: *shloshim v'shesh kritot b'Torah* — there are 36 instances of *karet* in the Torah. The relevance to *Kodashim* is direct: the sins for which one incurs *karet* when committed intentionally are precisely the sins for which one brings a *korban chatat* when they are committed unintentionally. *Karet* is thus the organizing principle for determining which *chatatot* are required.

It is worth clarifying the structure of punishment here. *Karet* is the divine punishment for an intentional violation committed without witnesses and formal warning. But that does not mean *karet* is always the most severe consequence. Many of these same sins, if committed with witnesses and the requisite *hatara*, carry a court-imposed death penalty — *chilul Shabbat*, for example, is *mot yumat*. The death penalty, when applicable, may instead be administered by the court. In the absence of witnesses or warning, God exacts the punishment, and that is *karet*. Some sins carry only *karet* and no court penalty — *Yom Kippur* violations, for instance — while others carry the possibility of *malkot* in lieu of *karet* when witnesses are present. The relationship between *malkot* and *karet* in cases where both might theoretically apply is itself a topic the *Gemara* will address.

### What is Karet

The word *karet* — *venichrat hanefesh hahi me'ameha*, "that soul shall be cut off from its people" — is interpreted differently by various authorities. The *Gemara* most often understands *karet* as a foreshortened life; specifically, the *Gemara* states that one who dies between the ages of 50 and 60 is said to have suffered *mita shel karet*. *Rashi* in several places identifies *karet* with *ariri* — childlessness — and sometimes invokes both: a shortened life and the absence of offspring.

A necessary caution applies here: the inference runs only in one direction. Dying before 60 or living without children are genuine tragedies in their own right, and it would be deeply insensitive to draw reverse inferences from these categories onto people who have experienced such losses. The logical form *if P then Q* does not license *if Q then P*. Indeed, the *Gemara* itself records that a certain *Amora* celebrated upon reaching the relevant age precisely because it demonstrated he had definitively not died in that range — which already acknowledges how dangerous the reverse inference would be.

The *Rambam* reframes these questions in a different key altogether. For the *Rambam*, *Olam Ha-ba* refers to the world of souls after death — not the this-worldly era following the coming of *Mashiach*, as the *Gemara* typically frames it. Accordingly, the *Rambam* understands *karet* primarily as the soul's being cut off from *Olam Ha-ba* at death. This is consistent with the *Rambam*'s broader tendency to understand divine reward and punishment in terms of the soul's natural metaphysical state as shaped by one's religious and intellectual life, rather than in terms of a more transactional scheme of *sachar ve'onesh*.

### Arayot List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְגַדֵּף, וְעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְהַנּוֹתֵן מִזַּרְעוֹ לַמּוֹלֶךְ, וּבַעַל אוֹב, וּמְחַלֵּל שַׁבָּת, וְטָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל קֹדֶשׁ, וְהַבָּא אֶל הַמִּקְדָּשׁ טָמֵא, וְהָאוֹכֶל חֵלֶב, וָדָם, וְנוֹתָר, וּפִיגּוּל, וְהַשּׁוֹחֵט, וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ,
>
> **Translation:** **And** these too are liable to receive *karet*: **One who blasphemes** the name of Heaven, **and** one who **worships an idol, and one who gives of his children to *Molekh*** (see Leviticus 20:1–5), **and a necromancer, and** one who **desecrates Shabbat. And** the same is the punishment of **one who is ritually impure who ate sacrificial** food; **and one who enters the Temple** while **ritually impure; and one who eats** forbidden **fat, or** consumes **blood, or** eats meat **left over from an offering after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*], or** eats meat of **an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*]; and one who slaughters** offerings **and offers** them **up outside** the Temple.

The *mishna* begins its enumeration with *arayot* — forbidden sexual relations. The first group listed are those that also carry a court-imposed death penalty: *haba al ha'em* (intercourse with one's mother), *ve'al eshet ha'av* (one's father's wife, even if not one's biological mother), *ve'al hakallah* (one's daughter-in-law, i.e., one's son's wife), *haba al hazachar* (a man who lies with another man), *ve'al habehemah* (bestiality), *ve'ishah hamvi'ah habehemah aleha* (a woman who brings an animal upon herself), *haba al ishah u'vitah* (a man who has relations with a woman and her daughter), and *ve'eshet ish* (a married woman). All of these carry not only *karet* but a court-imposed death sentence; notably, the first three specifically carry *sekilah* — death by stoning.

### Gender in Arayot

The *mishna* — and indeed the *parshiyot* of *arayot* in *Acharei Mot* and *Kedoshim* — addresses these prohibitions from the male perspective throughout. This is not merely a descriptive feature; it also shapes which prohibitions apply to whom. The Torah does not state that an aunt is forbidden to have relations with her nephew — only that a man may not have relations with his father's sister or mother's sister. The *Gemara* in fact discusses uncle-niece marriage as not merely permitted but even particularly appropriate. The *mishna* follows the same framework, so the list as given assumes a male subject except where explicitly stated otherwise — as in the case of *ve'ishah hamvi'ah habehemah aleha*, where by definition there is no male subject to address.

The *Gemara* does address the question of how women are implicated in *arayot* despite the male framing of the Torah's language. The general principle derived is *u'meitu gam sheneihem* — "both of them shall die" — establishing that the woman participating in these acts bears the same culpability as the man.

### Arayot Continued

After the capital cases, the *mishna* lists *arayot* that carry *karet* without a court-imposed death penalty: *haba al achoto* (one's sister), *ve'al achot aviv* (one's father's sister), *ve'al achot imo* (one's mother's sister), *ve'al eshet achi aviv* (one's paternal uncle's wife). Notably, *ve'al eshet achi imo* — one's maternal uncle's wife — appears in parentheses in the text, because that prohibition is rabbinic rather than biblical, and is therefore not counted in the 36.

The *mishna* then adds *ve'al haniddah* — a man who has relations with a woman in a state of *niddah*, which carries *karet* even if she is his wife. This concludes the category of *arayot*.

It is worth pausing over the apparent asymmetry in severity. Relations with a sister carry only *karet*, not the death penalty, while relations with a woman and her daughter carry *mita*. To our intuitions, incest might seem more deeply objectionable than the latter. The *Gemara* will have occasion to address this. One additional observation: ancient Egypt's royal family routinely practiced brother-sister marriage, suggesting that our sense of that particular taboo as self-evidently extreme may reflect more recent cultural formations. And as Freud observed, strong taboos tend to arise around acts for which there exists a corresponding *yetzer hara* — taboos do not generally develop around things people have no temptation to do.

### Non-Sexual Karets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאוֹכֵל חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, וְהָאוֹכֵל וְהָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, וְהַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן, וְהַמְפַטֵּם אֶת הַקְּטֹרֶת, וְהַסָּךְ אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה. וְהַפֶּסַח וְהַמִּילָה מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** these too are liable to receive *karet*: **One who eats leavened bread on Passover, and one who eats or performs** prohibited **labor on Yom Kippur. And** the same is the punishment of **one who blends the** anointing **oil** according to the specifications of the oil prepared by Moses in the wilderness (see Exodus 30:22–33); **and one who blends the incense** according to the specifications of the incense used in the Temple service for purposes other than use in the Temple; **and one who applies the anointing oil** to his skin. **And** one is liable to receive *karet* for failure to fulfill the mitzva of bringing **the Paschal offering and the** mitzva of **circumcision,** which unlike the cases of prohibitions enumerated in the mishna, are **positive mitzvot.**

Following the *arayot*, the *mishna* turns to non-sexual transgressions carrying *karet*: *v'hamigadef* (blasphemy — the precise definition of which the *Gemara* will explore), *v'oved avodat kochavim* (idolatry), *v'hanoten mizar'o la'Molech* (passing one's child through the fire in Molech worship — a practice that raises the question of why it is listed separately from *avodah zarah*, though it clearly belongs to that broader category), *u'va'al ov* (one who performs the necromantic practice known as *ba'al ov*), and *v'mechallel Shabbat* (violation of *Shabbat*, which as noted carries *mita* if performed with witnesses and warning but *karet* otherwise).

### Temple Violations

The *mishna* continues: *v'tame she'achal kodesh* — a person who is *tamei* and eats a *korban*. This is distinct from the case where the *korban* itself is *tamei* — that is merely a *lav*. Here the *korban* retains its full *kedusha* and is eaten by a person in a state of *tumah*, representing a violation of that *kedusha* of the gravest sort, hence *karet*. Similarly, *v'haba el hamikdash tamei* — a *tamei* person who enters the *Beit HaMikdash*. These two violations carry no court-imposed death penalty; they are solely *karet*.

The *mishna* also includes *v'hashochet u'ma'aleh bachutz* — one who slaughters an animal intended as a *korban*, or who offers a *korban*, outside the *Beit HaMikdash* at a time when such external offerings are prohibited. This belongs to the same constellation of *Mikdash*-centered concerns.

### Forbidden Foods

The foods carrying *karet* are *chelev* (forbidden fat), *dam* (blood), and *notar u'pigul* — *korbanot* that were left over beyond the permitted time, or that were rendered *pigul* through improper intent at the time of the offering.

This raises the question of why these particular *ma'achalot asurot* carry *karet* when far more familiar prohibitions — pork, *neveilah*, *basar b'chalav* — carry only a *lav*. The common denominator is a *Mikdash* resonance: *chelev* and *dam* are substances that go upon the *mizbeach*. There is also an allusion in the *pesukim* linking *dam* to forms of *avodah zarah* — *lo tochlu al ha-dam lo tenachashu v'lo te'onenu* — and the explicit verse *ki ani netativ lachem al ha-mizbeach lechaper* establishes these as substances dedicated to the altar. That is presumably why *chelev* of a *chaya* carries no such prohibition — animals not fit to be offered as *korbanot* are not subject to this logic. Archaeological evidence offers a striking parallel: ancient Jewish settlements throughout the land of Israel are identifiable precisely by the consistent absence of pig bones, even in periods and contexts where other forms of non-observance are archaeologically evident. The *issur* of pig, though only a *lav*, was apparently the most durably observed prohibition across all social strata.

### Oil, Incense, Chametz

The final group of transgressions enumerated includes *v'ochel chametz b'Pesach* (eating *chametz* on *Pesach*), *v'ochel v'oseh melacha b'Yom HaKippurim* (eating or performing *melacha* on *Yom Kippur* — these are two separate transgressions), *v'hamfatem et hashemen* (compounding the *shemen hamishcha*, the anointing oil, outside its authorized context), *v'hamfatem et haketores* (compounding the incense), and *v'hasach et shemen hamishcha* (anointing oneself with the actual *shemen hamishcha*).

The laws of *shemen hamishcha* and *ketoret* were recently covered in the *parasha*. The Torah in *Ki Tisa* prescribes the formula for the *shemen hamishcha* and then states: *al besar adam lo yisach u'vematkunto lo ta'asu kamohu* — it may not be applied to a human body, and one may not produce oil in the same formulation. Both violations carry *venichrat me'amav*. Similarly, for the *ketoret*, the Torah prohibits making incense with the same composition: *v'haketores asher ta'aseh b'matkuntah lo ta'asu lachem*. One subtle difference between the two: there is no parallel prohibition of *smelling* the actual *ketoret* of the *Mikdash*, because smelling is not halachically considered an act of *hana'ah* at the biblical level, and *me'ilah* does not apply to it. The *issur* of the *ketoret* is specifically in its production for personal use — *lo ta'asu lachem* — which means one who makes *ketoret* for the express purpose of donating it to the *Beit HaMikdash* does not violate the prohibition. That leniency does not apply to *shemen hamishcha*, which may not be produced even with intent to donate.

The question arises whether burning *ketoret* outside the *Mikdash* would constitute a violation of *shochatei chutz*. That prohibition is closely bound to animals, and it is not clear that *ma'aleh ketoret bachutz* would fall within its ambit; *ma'aleh evarim bachutz* or *maktir chelev bachutz* certainly would. This is left as an open question.

Returning to the broader list: why does *chametz* on *Pesach* carry *karet* when the other prominent *ma'achalot asurot* do not? This question connects directly to the final category of the *mishna*.

### Pesach & Milah

The *mishna* concludes its enumeration with *v'hapesach v'hamila* — *Pesach* and *milah*. These are the only two *mitzvot asei* whose non-performance carries *karet*. All the preceding items were prohibitions; these two are affirmative commandments. The Torah is explicit in both cases: by *korban Pesach* it states *v'chadal la'asos hapesach venichrasah hanefesh hahi me'ameha*; by *brit mila* the verse reads *v'arel zachar asher lo yimol et besar orlato venichrasah*.

The underlying reason is that these two *mitzvot* are the most foundational markers of Jewish identity. *Milah* is the physical mark on the body — the seal of personal belonging. *Korban Pesach* is the communal act of participation in the foundational narrative of the Jewish people. It is no accident that those who were unable to bring the *korban Pesach* cried out to *Moshe*, *lamah nigara levilti hikriv et korban Hashem bemo'ado betoch benei Yisrael* — the sense of exclusion from the collective was so profound that a makeup offering, *Pesach Sheni*, was instituted. And this is why *Pesach* remains, by virtually any survey, one of the most universally observed Jewish rituals — something like 95% of those who identify as Jews participate in some form of *seder*. The powerful experiential dimension of the *seder* contributes to this, but so does the deep structure of what *Pesach* means as an expression of communal belonging.

Looking at the plain sense of the *pesukim*, *karet* is always formulated as *venichrat me'amav* or *venichrat hanefesh hahi mitoch benei Yisrael* — the soul is cut off *from the people*. The individualistic conceptions of *karet* as a shortened lifespan or exclusion from *Olam Ha-ba* are real and significant, but they overlay what the Torah itself emphasizes: exclusion from the community. It is precisely because *Pesach* and *milah* are the acts by which one declares and enacts membership in that community that their neglect carries this same punishment of communal severance. This framework also illuminates why *chametz* on *Pesach* carries *karet* among all the *ma'achalot asurot*: the prohibition of *chametz* during *chag hamatzot* is inseparable from the *Pesach* complex of participation in Jewish history. The *issur* of *chametz* is best understood as connected to the *karet* of *korban Pesach* itself.

## Intent & Chatat

### Mishna 2 Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, וְעַל לֹא הוֹדַע שֶׁלָּהֶם אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, חוּץ מִן הַמְטַמֵּא מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **For** any of **these** prohibitions, one is **liable** to receive ***karet* for its intentional** violation **and** to bring **a sin offering for its unwitting** violation. **And for their** violation in a case where it is **unknown** to him whether or not he transgressed, he is liable to bring **a provisional guilt offering,** which provides provisional atonement until he discovers whether or not he transgressed. This is the *halakha* for all of the transgressions listed above **except for one who defiles the Temple,** i.e., he enters the Temple while ritually impure, **or** partakes of **its consecrated items** while ritually impure. In these cases he does not bring a provisional guilt offering **because he is** obligated **to** bring **a sliding-scale** offering for a definite transgression. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *mishna* continues: *al elu chayavin al zedonam karet* — for all of these, if one transgresses willfully, one incurs *karet*, assuming there are no witnesses and warning that would trigger the applicable court-imposed death penalty. *Ve'al shigagatam chatat* — and for an unintentional commission of any of these sins, one brings a *chatat*.

### Shogeg Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַמְגַדֵּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה״, יָצָא מְגַדֵּף שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is the same **even** with regard to **the one who blasphemes, as it is stated** with regard to the sin offering: **"You shall have one law for him who performs the action unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:29), **excluding** one who **blasphemes, as he does not perform an action** but sins with speech.

What does it mean to transgress unintentionally? The pseudo-*Rashi* here, drawing on *Gemaras* throughout *Shas*, identifies two categories of *shogeg*. The first is a mistake of fact: one thought one was sleeping with one's wife and it turned out to be one's sister; one did not realize one was *tamei*; one did not know one was entering the *Beit HaMikdash*; one forgot it was *Shabbat*. The second is a mistake of law — but a specific kind of mistake. Pseudo-*Rashi* describes a case where one knows that a broader category is forbidden and even knows the severe consequence, but did not realize that this specific act fell within that category. For instance: one knows that *avodah zarah* is *chayav mita*, but believed that this particular act was not actually a form of *avodah zarah*. The person is fully aware of the larger prohibition but was mistaken about whether the detail at issue was encompassed by it. These cases of factual or legal mistake render one a *shogeg* and trigger the obligation of a *chatat*.

### Omer Mutar

A harder question is what happens when a person did not know the prohibition existed at all. This is the category the *Gemara* refers to as *omer mutar* — one who claims permissibility, effectively someone who was entirely ignorant of the prohibition. Is such a person a *shogeg* who brings a *chatat*, or are they in a different category?

The *Gemara* in *Makkot* addresses a parallel question with respect to murder: if someone claims killing was permitted, are they a *shogeg* who goes into *galus*, or are they too close to *meizid* to qualify? The framing there is *havei lei lemeida* — is it the person's responsibility to have known? If so, ignorance of the law is no excuse, and the person is *karov l'meizid* rather than a genuine *shogeg*. Pseudo-*Rashi* here takes the position that *omer mutar* in a case like compounding the *ketoret* would constitute *oness* — complete absence of fault. Many *Rishonim*, however, hold that the analysis is case-dependent: by murder, we say *havei lei lemeida*; by *mefatem et haketores*, even though it is an explicit *pasuk*, we might not. The spectrum runs from genuine *meizid* through *karov l'meizid*, through various grades of *shogeg*, to *karov l'oness*, and finally to full *oness*. For the obligation of a *chatat*, one must be somewhere in the middle of that range — neither a true *meizid* nor a true *oness*.

### Asham Talui

The *mishna* continues: *v'lo hoda shelahem asham talui* — if one is uncertain whether one committed such a sin, one brings an *asham talui*, an offering held in suspense pending clarification of whether the sin was actually committed. The classic case is where one slept with a woman but is uncertain whether she was one's wife or one's sister.

However, there is an exception: *chutz m'nitamei mikdash v'kodashav* — the cases of *tum'ah* related to the *Mikdash* and its *korbanot* are excluded. If one is uncertain whether one entered the *Beit HaMikdash* while *tamei* or whether one ate a *korban* while *tamei*, one does not bring a standard *chatat* but an *oleh v'yored* — a variable-cost offering. According to *Rabbi Meir*, the *asham talui* applies only where, if one knew one had sinned, one would bring a fixed *chatat kavua*. A sin whose certain commission would require an *oleh v'yored* has no corresponding *asham talui*; in a case of uncertainty about such a sin, one brings nothing.

### Blasphemer

The *mishna* further records a dispute regarding the *megadef*, the blasphemer. *Chachamim* exclude the blasphemer from the category of *asham talui* — and possibly from *chatat* entirely — citing the verse *torah achat yihyeh lachem l'oseh bishgagah*: one law for one who acts unintentionally. From here the *Chachamim* derive *yatza megadef she'eino oseh ma'aseh* — the blasphemer is excluded because blasphemy requires no physical *ma'aseh*. This raises the question of whether the exclusion from *asham talui* extends to a *chatat* as well: is a *ma'aseh* required for *chatat* liability, or only for *asham talui*? The *Gemara* will clarify the scope of this exclusion. Notably, the transgression that carries *karet* — and potentially the death penalty — requires no physical act at all; *karet* for blasphemy applies to speech alone. But for the purposes of *korban* liability, the *Chachamim* require an action.

## Gemara Numerology

### Why 36 Karets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָן כּוּלָּן בְּהֶעְלֵם אַחַת – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to state this **tally** of cases where one who performs the action is liable to receive *karet*? Why does it state a number? All thirty-six are listed in the mishna, and one could simply count them to discover how many there are. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** By listing the tally, the mishna is teaching **that if one performed all of them in** the course of **one lapse of awareness,** he is **liable** to receive punishment **for each and every one,** i.e., to bring thirty-six sin offerings.

The *Gemara* now opens with its characteristic question: *minyana lama li?* — why does the *mishna* need to state a number? If the *mishna* is going to enumerate the 36 cases of *karet* anyway, what purpose is served by announcing the count in advance?

*Rav Yochanan* answers: *kamashma lan kulam b'helem achat chayav al kol achat v'achat* — the number is stated to teach that if all 36 prohibitions (or rather, their relevant details) were forgotten simultaneously, and all were violated in that single state of forgetfulness, one would be obligated to bring a separate *chatat* for each one of the 34 prohibitions. One cannot reason that because everything was done in one state of forgetfulness only one *chatat* is owed. Each prohibition is a distinct *aveirah*, and each requires its own *chatat* when violated *b'shogeg*. (The two *mitzvot asei* — *Pesach* and *milah* — do not generate *chatat* obligations, so the operative count for purposes of *chatat* is 34, not 36.)

### 39 Melachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אַחַת, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָן כּוּלָּן בְּהֶעְלֵם אַחַת – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 73a): The **primary categories of labor** are **forty-less-one; why do I** need this **tally** of forty-less-one? The Gemara answers: By listing the tally the mishna is teaching **that if one performed all of** the prohibited labors **in one lapse of awareness** during which he was unaware of the prohibition involved, he is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every one.**

The *Gemara* immediately extends this logic to the *mishna* in *Shabbat*: *avot melachot arba'im chaser achat* — there are 39 *melachot* of *Shabbat*. *Minyana lama li?* The answer is the same: *kulam b'helem achat chayav al kol achat v'achat*. If one forgot that all 39 *melachot* were forbidden on *Shabbat* — while still knowing in some minimal sense that it was *Shabbat*, perhaps retaining awareness that *chutz l'techum* is forbidden — and one performed all 39, one would bring 39 separate *chatatot*. By contrast, if one performed the same *melacha* multiple times in one state of forgetfulness, one brings only a single *chatat*, since it is the same transgression repeated. The point is that 39 *distinct* categories of *melacha* generate 39 distinct *chatatot*, regardless of the unified state of forgetfulness.

### Mechusrei Kapparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה מְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, דְּאָמַר: חֲמִשָּׁה הָוֵי, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: גֵּר מְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה עַד שֶׁיִּזָּרֵק דָּם עָלָיו, אַהָכִי תְּנָא אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (8b): There are **four** people **who have not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete their purification process and may not eat sacrificial meat until they bring the requisite offerings. The mishna then teaches: And these are the people who have not yet brought an atonement offering: The *zav*, and the *zava*, and the woman after childbirth, and the leper. **Why do I** need this **tally** of four? 
>
> The Gemara answers: By listing the tally, the mishna serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, who says there are five** such individuals. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A convert** is also **one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering and he may not partake of sacrificial meat, although he was circumcised and immersed in a ritual bath, **until the priest sprinkles** the **blood** of his offering on the altar **on his** behalf. It is **for this** reason that the mishna **teaches four,** to emphasize that there are not five such people.

The *Gemara* moves to a later *mishna* in the *masechet*: *arba'ah mechusrei kaparah* — four categories of *mechusrei kapparah*, those who complete a purification process by bringing a *chatat* not because of sin but as the final step in becoming fully *tahor* and permitted to enter the *Mikdash* and eat *korbanot*. *Minyana lama li?* The answer here is different: *l'apokei mi'd'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*, to exclude the position of *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*, who says *chamisha havei* — there are actually five such categories.

What is *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*'s fifth? *D'tanya*: *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer ger mechusar kapparah ad she'yizarek dam alav* — a convert is lacking atonement until blood has been sprinkled on his behalf, meaning a convert must bring a *korban* to complete the conversion process. *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* understands this not as part of the conversion per se, but as the completion of a purification-type process analogous to the other four. By stating *arba'ah* — exactly four — the *mishna* makes clear that it rejects this position.

### Voluntary Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה מְבִיאִין עַל הַזָּדוֹן כִּשְׁגָגָה, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן – לֹא נִיתָּן זְדוֹנָהּ לְכַפָּרָה, אַהָכִי תְּנָא אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in that same mishna (8b): And there are **four who bring** an offering **for the intentional** transgression of a prohibition **as** they do for **the unwitting** transgression of a prohibition: One who engages in intercourse with a designated maidservant (see Leviticus 19:20–22), a nazirite who became ritually impure (see Numbers 6:9–12), one who takes a false oath that he does not know testimony on behalf of another (Leviticus 5:1), and one who takes a false oath on a deposit, i.e., that that an item belonging to another is not in his possession (Leviticus 5:21–26). **Why do I** need this **tally** of four?
>
> The Gemara answers: By listing the tally, the mishna serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: The intentional** transgressor who takes a false **oath on a deposit is not given** the opportunity **for atonement** by bringing an offering. It is **for this** reason that the mishna **teaches four,** to emphasize that taking a false oath on a deposit is included in this list.

The next *mishna* in the *masechet* states: *arba mevi'in al ha-zadon kishgagah* — there are four types of cases in which one brings a *korban* even for an intentional transgression. Normally a *chatat* is brought only for *shogeg*; these are exceptions, most of them involving the *oleh v'yored* or *asham* category rather than the standard fixed *chatat*. *Minyana lama li?* Here too the number is stated *l'apokei mi'd'Rabbi Shimon*, to exclude *Rabbi Shimon*'s position. *D'tanya: Rabbi Shimon omer shevuat ha-pikadon lo nitan zedonah l'kapparah* — by a false oath regarding a *pikadon*, a deposit entrusted to one's care, *Rabbi Shimon* holds that a willful transgression does not generate a *korban* obligation. According to him there are only three such cases. By stating four, the *mishna* clearly rejects his position.

### Five One Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה מְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד עַל עֲבֵירוֹת הַרְבֵּה, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא: נָזִיר אִם נִטְמָא טוּמְאוֹת הַרְבֵּה, וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּגוֹן דְּאִיטַּמִּי בִּשְׁבִיעִי וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וּמַנִּי? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: נְזִירוּת דְּטָהֲרָה, מִן שְׁבִיעִי הוּא דְּחָיֵיל עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (9a): There are **five** people who **bring one offering for several transgressions,** i.e., for violating the same transgression several times; and the mishna then teaches: These individuals bring one offering for several transgressions: One who engages in several acts of intercourse with an espoused maidservant; a nazirite who became ritually impure via several instances of ritual impurity; one who issues a warning to his wife with regard to entering into seclusion with several men; a leper who was afflicted with several instances of leprosy; and a woman who gave birth to several offspring. **Why do I** need this **tally** of five?
>
> The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the mishna lists the tally **because** he **wants to teach the latter clause:** This also applies to **a nazirite if he became ritually impure with several** instances **of ritual impurity.** The tally of five includes the case of a nazirite. The Gemara explains what the novelty of this case is: **And you find this,** that a nazirite can contract ritual impurity several times consecutively, in a case **where he became ritually impure on the seventh** day **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the seventh** day. **And** in accordance with **whose** opinion is it? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **the naziriteship of purity,** i.e., the naziriteship one must observe afresh after his purification, **takes effect on** the nazirite **from** the **seventh** day of his purification process.

Another *mishna* teaches: *chamishah mevi'in korban echad al aveirot harbeh* — there are five types of people who bring a single *korban* for multiple transgressions. Normally each distinct transgression requires its own *korban*; these are exceptions. *Minyana lama li?* The *Gemara* explains: *mishum d'ka-ba'ei l'mitni seifa nazir she-nitma tumot harbeh* — because the enumeration must include the case of a *nazir* who became *tamei* multiple times. The *nazir* case is not straightforwardly about *aveirot* in the standard sense, since the *korban* brought by a *nazir* after *tumah* is more about restarting the *nezirut* than about atonement for sin. Nevertheless, it is included in this category.

### Nazir Tumah Detail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי תֵּאמַר רַבִּי הִיא, כֵּיוָן דִּנְזִירוּת דְּטׇהֳרָה עַד שְׁמִינִי לָא חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **As, if you say** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, that is difficult: **Since** he says **that** the **naziriteship of purity does not take effect on** the nazirite **until** the **eighth** day, **how can you find** a case in which **these** circumstances apply, i.e., that a nazirite can be considered impure with several instances of ritual impurity in the same term of naziriteship?

The *Gemara* presents the case: a *nazir* who becomes *tamei* must undergo a seven-day purification process and then restart his *nezirut*. A dispute between *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi* (Rebbi) governs when exactly the new *nezirut* begins. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the new *nezirut* begins on day seven — upon completing the shaving (*tiglachto*) — even though the *korban* that closes out the old *tumah* is not brought until day eight. *Rabbi* holds that the new *nezirut* begins only on day eight, when the *korban* is actually brought.

This dispute has direct practical implications for the case at hand. Suppose a *nazir* becomes *tamei*, reaches day seven, and — according to *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* — thereby begins a new *nezirut*, but immediately becomes *tamei* again that same day. He now has accumulated multiple *tumot* attributable to distinct periods of *nezirut*, yet he has never reached day eight and was therefore never obligated in a *korban* for the earlier *tumah* before the new one occurred. When he ultimately brings his *korban*, it covers all these separate *tumah* events together. This is the case of a single *korban* for multiple violations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּנִטְמָא בִּשְׁבִיעִי וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בִּשְׁבִיעִי – כּוּלַּהּ חֲדָא טוּמְאָה אֲרִיכְתָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, this is not possible: **If** this case involves a nazirite **who became ritually impure on the seventh** day, **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the seventh** day, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would hold that **all these** impurities **are** in fact one instance of **long ritual impurity.** His reasoning is that the nazirite never reached the eighth day, which would renew his observance of naziriteship in purity. If so, the mishna would not refer to this as a case of multiple impurities.

According to *Rabbi*, however, the new *nezirut* does not begin until day eight. If the *nazir* becomes *tamei* on day seven and again on day seven, *kulah chada tumah arichta hi* — it all constitutes one extended *tumah* within a single *nezirut*, never generating multiple distinct statuses. And if the new *tumah* occurs on day eight, it arrives after the moment at which the obligation in the first *korban* had already been established — *keivan d'yatza b'sha'a haru'ya l'havi b'korban* — making it a separate *korban* obligation: *chayav al kol achat v'achat*. Thus according to *Rabbi*, the scenario of one *korban* for multiple *nezirut tumot* can never arise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי דְּנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי וְחָזַר וְנִטְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי, כֵּיוָן דְּיָצָא בְּשָׁעָה הָרְאוּיָה לְהָבִיא בָּהּ קׇרְבָּן, חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna is referring to a nazirite **who became ritually impure on the eighth** day **and,** after undergoing the purification process, **again became ritually impure on the eighth** day, the ruling of the mishna would not apply either. **Since he left** his state of impurity **at an appropriate time to bring** his **offering,** as he can bring his offering on the eighth day, it is not considered a continuation of the previous ritual impurity and he is **liable** to bring a separate offering **for each and every** instance of impurity. **Rather, conclude from it** that this mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.** It is to emphasize this fact that the mishna lists the tally.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella shema mina Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *mishna* follows the position of *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*. The number five is stated to make clear that the *nazir* case is genuinely included, and that the *masechet* is adopting *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*'s position rather than *Rabbi*'s.

### Oleh V'yored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה מְבִיאִין קׇרְבַּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: נָשִׂיא כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן. חֲמִשָּׁה לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: נָשִׂיא מֵבִיא שָׂעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (9a): And there are **five** people who **bring a sliding-scale offering,** which is determined by the financial status of the sinner; and the mishna then teaches (10b): These bring a sliding-scale offering: One brings such an offering for hearing the voice of an oath, and for a false oath of the utterance of the lips, and for the defiling of the Temple and its sacred foods, and the woman after childbirth, and the leper. **Why do I** need this **tally** of five?
>
> The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the mishna lists the tally **because** of the *halakha* taught in the latter clause, **as the latter clause teaches,** in a different mishna (*Horayot* 8b): The court is not liable for issuing a ruling with regard to the hearing of a voice, or for a false oath of the utterance of the lips, or for the defiling of the Temple and its sacred foods. The status of the **king is like** that of the court and he is exempt from bringing an offering in these cases. It is for this reason the mishna teaches **five, to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer, who says** that **a king brings a goat,** i.e., a fixed sin offering, instead of a sliding-scale offering for the defiling of the Temple and its sacred foods.

Another *mishna* in the collection teaches: *chamishah mevi'in korban oleh v'yored* — there are five types of *oleh v'yored*, variable-cost sin offerings. *Minyana lama li?* Because the *mishna* continues to state *nasi k'yotzei bahem* — that a *nasi* is like them — and this requires clarification. The reference is to the principle in *Horayot*: a *nasi* brings his special *korban* — a male goat — only for the same categories of sins for which a *beit din* would bring *par helam davar shel tzibbur* for misleading the public. Both the *beit din*'s special *korban* and the *nasi*'s special *korban* apply only to sins that would generate a fixed *chatat kavua* for an ordinary Israelite. For sins that would generate an *oleh v'yored* for a commoner, the *nasi* brings nothing — neither his special *korban* (since the qualifying condition is not met) nor an *oleh v'yored* (since by definition a *nasi* is wealthy and is not in the *oleh v'yored* category). One opinion holds that a *nasi* brings nothing at all for such a sin and simply has no *kapparah*; another opinion, that of *Rabbi Eliezer*, holds that a *nasi* does bring his *sa'ir* even for an *oleh v'yored* sin. By stating five *oleh v'yored* cases, the *mishna* excludes *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position and makes clear that these sins generate only an *oleh v'yored* and nothing else.

### Avot Nezikin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין, מִנְיָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין הֵן. וּלְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, מִנְיָינָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, דְּאָמַר: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין. וּלְרַבִּי חִיָּיא מִנְיָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָסוֹר וּמְפַגֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 2a): There are **four primary categories of damage:** The category of Ox, the category of Pit, the category of *Maveh*, and the category of Fire. **Why do I** need this **tally** of four?
>
> The Gemara answers: By listing the tally, the mishna serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Oshaya, who says** (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 9:1): **There are thirteen primary categories of damage. And according to Rabbi Oshaya, why do I** need this **tally** of thirteen? By listing the tally, he serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya, who says: There are twenty-four primary categories of damage. And according to Rabbi Ḥiyya, why do I** need this **tally** of twenty-four? By listing the tally, he serves **to exclude** the case of **an informer** who causes the authorities to confiscate the property of another, **and** a priest **who renders** another's offering ***piggul*,** as Rabbi Ḥiyya concedes that these are not included in the primary categories of damage.

Finally, the *Gemara* addresses the *mishna* at the opening of *Bava Kamma*: *arba avot nezikin* — four primary categories of property damage. *Minyana lama li?* *L'apokei mid'Rabbi Oshaya*, to exclude *Rabbi Oshaya*, *d'amar telat esrei avot nezikin* — he counts 13, expanding the list well beyond the standard four by including additional forms of damage. One of the rationales for stating a precise number in that *mishna* is that the category determines when one must pay with *meitav sadeihu*, the finest of one's land.

The *Gemara* then turns the question around: according to *Rabbi Oshaya*, why does he state 13? *L'apokei mid'Rabbi Chiyya*, to exclude *Rabbi Chiyya*, *d'amar esrin v'arba'ah avot nezikin* — who counts 24. And according to *Rabbi Chiyya*, why state 24? To exclude *moser u'mefagel* — the *moser* who informs on a fellow Jew to an oppressive government, causing the seizure of his property, and the *kohen* who renders a *korban pigul* through improper thought, thereby causing loss to the owner. Both of these are excluded from the *avot nezikin* because the damage is not direct, and the principle of *hezek she'eino nikar* — damage that is not physically manifest — exempts the actor from liability.

With this final exchange, the *Gemara* has completed its enumeration of the numbered *mishnayot* in and adjacent to *Masechet Keritot*, using each as an occasion to introduce the major themes and debates that will occupy the rest of the *masechet*.
