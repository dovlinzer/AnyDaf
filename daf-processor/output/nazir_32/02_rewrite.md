# Nazir 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nezirut B'Taaut Setup

### Opening Context

The *mishna* at the bottom of 31b continues the theme of *ta'ut* — error — that has occupied the preceding discussion. The previous *mishna* established that *hekdesh b'ta'ut* is *hekdesh* according to *Beit Shammai*, and the current *mishna* builds on that foundation by turning to the intertwined questions of *nezirut b'ta'ut* and *hekdesh b'ta'ut* together.

### Refused Heter Case

The *mishna* opens: *mi shenadar b'nazir venishal l'chacham ve'asaro* — someone took a vow of *nezirut* and went to a *chacham* to obtain a *heter*, but the *chacham* was unable to release him. One might ask how such a case is to be understood according to *Beit Shammai*, who does not generally accept the framework of *nezirut b'ta'ut* as grounds for release. *Tosafot* addresses this directly: for the *mishna* to function according to *Beit Shammai*, what the person asked the *chacham* must not have been a standard *heter* based on *ta'ut* or *petach* — which *Beit Shammai* would not recognize — but rather a claim that the formula of the vow was insufficiently precise. The *masechet* itself opened with the various formulations that constitute valid or invalid acceptance of *nezirut*, and there are cases where one might legitimately ask whether the precise formula was ever uttered. That type of challenge is one even *Beit Shammai* would entertain. According to *Beit Hillel*, of course, a broader claim of *ta'ut* — had I thought it through I would not have accepted — is itself sufficient grounds for a *chacham* to release the vow. In this *mishna*, whatever the basis of the request, the *chacham* ruled that he could not help, and the person remains bound.

### Beit Shammai View

The *mishna* teaches that in such a case the person counts his *nezirut* from the time he originally accepted the vow — *monei mishaa shenadar*. On its face this seems obvious: of course he counts from the onset. The *gemara* will therefore assume that the *chiddush* of the *mishna* is that this is true even if, in the interim, the *nazir* violated his *nezirut*. Perhaps the very reason he was going to the *chacham* in the first place is that he had forgotten himself and drunk wine, or violated the *nezirut* in some other way, and was now seeking retroactive release. Very appropriately, the *chacham* refused. The teaching of the *mishna*, then, is that he does not lose his accumulated days. Being made *tamei* triggers a *korban* and a fresh start, but other violations — drinking wine, shaving — do not restart the count; they incur punishment, but the duration of the *nezirut* remains anchored to the day it was originally accepted.

### Counting from Onset

The *gemara* will now explore exactly what this counting entails and whether any days need to be made up for violations — but before proceeding to that analysis, the *mishna* introduces the second case, which concerns the animal that was sanctified in anticipation of the *korban nezirut*.

## Hekdesh Ta'us Debate

### Animal Goes Free

The *mishna* continues: *nishal l'chacham vehitiro* — now suppose the *chacham* did grant a *heter*. *Hayta lo behema mufreshet* — the person had already sanctified an animal as a *korban* of his *nezirut*. *Tetzei v'tir'eh ba'eder* — it goes free and grazes with the flock. Since retroactively he was never a *nazir*, there was never a valid context for sanctifying an animal as a *korban nezirut*. As *Tosafot* explains, this is analogous to a person who sanctifies an animal as a *chatat* without ever having the sin that obligates a *chatat*: the act of sanctification is void, not merely erroneous.

### Beit Hillel Argument

*Amru Beit Hillel l'Beit Shammai* — *Beit Hillel* said to *Beit Shammai*: do you not concede this very point? There is something striking about the structure of this exchange. *Beit Hillel* is appealing to an earlier *mishna*, universally accepted, and using it to make an argument against *Beit Shammai*. This phenomenon — where *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* refer to a *mishna rishona*, an earlier ruling that predates their dispute — offers a window into the historical development of the *mishna* itself. The argument is: *ee atem modim b'ze she'hu hekdesh ta'ut sheteitzei v'tir'eh ba'eder* — do you not concede that this is a case of *hekdesh b'ta'ut* and that the animal goes free? If so, this earlier *mishna* demonstrates that *hekdesh b'ta'ut* is not *hekdesh*, and how can *Beit Shammai* maintain otherwise?

### Ma'aser Behema Counter

*Beit Shammai* responds with a counter-example from *ma'aser behema*. The process is well known: animals born that year are counted one by one as they emerge from the pen, and every tenth one is struck with a marked staff and consecrated as *ma'aser behema*. If one miscounts and calls the ninth animal *ten*, or calls the eleventh *ten*, the *halacha* is that all three — nine, ten, and eleven — are consecrated. *Beit Shammai* presses: *ee atem modim b'mi she'ta'ah v'kara l'tshi'i asiri* — do you not concede that when you mistakenly call nine *ten*, or ten *nine*, or eleven *ten*, all three acquire *kedushah*? This, says *Beit Shammai*, is proof that *hekdesh b'ta'ut* is *hekdesh*.

### Different Mechanism

*Beit Hillel* responds: *lo hashevet kidshah* — it is not the staff that consecrates, not the act of designation driven by intent. The proof is that if one were to accidentally call the eighth animal *ten*, or the twelfth, it would not be consecrated at all. The rule of nine and eleven is not a general principle about the power of erroneous sanctification. Rather, *ela katuv shekidesh asiri hu kidesh tshi'i v'et achad asar* — the Torah itself defines the tenth in a way that encompasses a margin of error: nine and eleven called *ten* through an honest mistake fall within the category of what the Torah designates as *ma'aser*. But this is a specific *halacha* in *ma'aser behema* about how the Torah defines *ten* — it is not a general principle of *hekdesh b'ta'ut*. The proof is that even within *ma'aser behema*, calling the eighth *ten* produces nothing, demonstrating that the mechanism is the Torah's definition of the tenth, not the general power of erroneous consecration.

### Basic Criteria Principle

One might then ask: why does *Beit Shammai* not respond to *Beit Hillel's* original argument — that the animal of the person who retroactively was never a *nazir* goes free? *Tosafot* explains that *Beit Shammai* did not need to respond because that case is not *hekdesh b'ta'ut* at all in the operative sense. That case involves missing the fundamental criteria that would enable the sanctification in the first place. If one is not a *nazir*, one cannot consecrate an animal as a *korban nezirut*. If one has not eaten *chelev* and thus bears no obligation of a *chatat*, one cannot consecrate an animal as a *chatat*. The absence of the basic legal context is not a case of *ta'ut* in the relevant sense — it is a case where the very precondition for the *hekdesh* never existed.

*Tosafot* phrases it precisely: *vechan hanezirus leisa hekdesh nami leisa vegarua zeh mehekdesh ta'ut* — here, since the *nezirut* itself does not exist, the *hekdesh* likewise does not exist, and this is something even weaker than *hekdesh b'ta'ut*. And therefore: *afilu Beit Shammai modu detivtal le'alter keivan dene'ekar haneder legamrei* — even *Beit Shammai* concedes that it is retroactively void, since the entire *neder* is uprooted completely. It is as if one who has no obligation declares an animal to be *lechatas* — *lo amar klum*, it is as though nothing was said, even according to *Beit Shammai*.

The *gemara* then draws this principle out symmetrically, noting that the same logic applies on *Beit Hillel's* side with respect to *temurah*. *Rabbi Yirmiyah* observes: *mi-deBeit Shammai nishma leBeit Hillel* — from *Beit Shammai* we can derive the position of *Beit Hillel* as well. *Beit Shammai* holds that *hekdesh b'ta'ut* is *hekdesh*, and yet they would concede that if the *nazir* obtains a *heter* and retroactively was never a *nazir*, the animal is not consecrated — *keivan de-iglei milta d'lav shapir nazar*, once it emerges that he was never properly a *nazir*, the fundamental basis for the *hekdesh* is gone. By the same token, *Beit Hillel*, who hold that *temurah b'ta'ut* is *temurah* — *hani milei heicha d'ika ikar hekdesh*, that applies when the original animal is indeed consecrated. But if the original *hekdesh* itself is undermined — *itakar ikar hekdesh, itakar nami temurah* — the *temurah* falls away as well. For *Beit Hillel*, the original animal must be *hekdesh* before *ta'ut* can operate; for *Beit Shammai*, one must actually be a *nazir* before an animal can be sanctified as a *korban nezirut*. In both cases, *ta'ut* is a principle that can apply within a valid framework — but it cannot substitute for the framework itself.

## Making Up Days Debate

### Violation Assumption

The *gemara* turns to the first clause of the *mishna* — *moneh mishaa shenadar*, that one counts from the day one accepted the *nezirut* — and asks: *mani masnisin?* Whose view does this *mishna* represent? And the *gemara* answers, surprisingly: *lo Rabbi Yosei velo Rabbanan* — it fits neither *Rabbi Yosei* nor the *Rabbanan* as they appear in a *braita*. The *gemara* is assuming throughout that the *mishna* is addressing a case where the *nazir* violated his *nezirut* — by drinking wine, for example — and that the *chiddush* is that despite the violation, one does not lose the days already counted.

### Days Makeup Dispute

The *braita* teaches: *mi shenadar ve'avar al neziruso, ein nizkakin lo ela im kein moneh bahen issur keyamim shenahag bahen hetter* — one who vowed and violated his *nezirut* is not received, meaning we do not accept his *korbanos*, unless he first makes up, through days of prohibited conduct, as many days as he conducted himself permissibly. So if he accepted a *nezirut* of a hundred days and drank wine for forty-five, he must complete a full hundred days of abstention before the *korbanos* can be brought. *Rabbi Yosei* disagrees: *dayo shloshim yom* — thirty days is sufficient, a complete standard unit of *nezirut*, even if many more were violated.

Either way, according to both *Rabbi Yosei* and the *Rabbanan*, some makeup is required. Yet the *mishna* appears to say simply that one counts from the onset, with no mention of additional days. The *gemara* notes that if this is the *Rabbanan*, the *mishna* is difficult in a case of *nezirut mu'etes* — a short *nezirut* — because the full amount of violated days must be made up. And if it is *Rabbi Yosei*, it is difficult in a case of *nezirut meruba* — a long *nezirut* — because even thirty additional days must be made up, and the *mishna* implies none are required.

### Both Views Possible

The *gemara* resolves this by offering a dual reading: *ee ba'is eima Rabbi Yosei, ee ba'is eima Rabbanan* — one can read the *mishna* as consistent with either position, provided one specifies the length of the *nezirut* involved.

Reading the *mishna* according to *Rabbi Yosei* is possible in one of two ways. One bold reading is that *dayo shloshim yom* does not mean *make up thirty days* but rather *thirty days is enough* — meaning *Rabbi Yosei* fundamentally rejects the requirement to make up violated days. The *braita* addresses a *nezirut mu'etes*, and *Rabbi Yosei's* statement means: he has served his thirty days, I do not require anything further. Under this reading, *Rabbi Yosei* and the *mishna* align perfectly — neither requires makeup days — and the *mishna* can be speaking of a long *nezirut*. A second reading takes *dayo shloshim yom* to mean that thirty additional days must indeed be made up when many were violated, but that for a short *nezirut* with few violations, *Rabbi Yosei* may not require makeup at all. The *mishna* would then address a *nezirut mu'etes* with few violations.

Reading the *mishna* according to the *Rabbanan* requires a more radical re-reading of the word *mishaa* itself. Rather than *moneh mishaa shenadar* — he counts from the moment he accepted — the *gemara* suggests reading it as *moneh ke-mishaa shenadar*, meaning he must recount all the days that have elapsed since he accepted the *nezirut*, because those are precisely the days he spent in violation. By inserting a *chaf* of comparison, the *mishna* is no longer saying he counts forward from the start but that he must reckon and make up every day since the start. Though no violation is explicitly mentioned in the *mishna*, the *gemara* takes for granted that this is the operative assumption — otherwise the *mishna* would be stating something entirely obvious.

## Stolen Animal Case

### Animal Stolen Setup

The following *mishna* addresses a different form of *nezirut b'ta'ut*, this one based on a mistaken factual premise at the time the vow was accepted. *Mi shenadar b'nazir ve-halach le-havi et behemto, matza she-nigneva* — someone accepted *nezirut* and went to retrieve his animal in anticipation of bringing his *korbanos*, only to discover it had been stolen. This person is coming to a *chacham* and claiming: I am a poor man; I only ever intended to become a *nazir* on the assumption that this animal was available to me. Had I known it was stolen, I never would have accepted *nezirut*. So what is the *halacha*?

The case of the stolen animal unfolds across what follows, but the essential analytical framework is introduced here. It involves the principle known as *ein potchin b'nolad* — one cannot use as the basis for a *heter* something that did not yet exist or was not yet known at the time the vow was made. The *nazir's* claim depends on the animal's status at the time of his vow, and the *mishna* will draw a precise line depending on exactly when the theft occurred relative to when the vow was taken.

### Before/After Knowledge

*Im ad she-lo nigneva behemto nadar, harei zeh nazir* — if the theft occurred only after he had already accepted the *nezirut*, then he is a *nazir*. At the time he took the vow, his assumption was correct: the animal was his. The subsequent theft could not have been anticipated and cannot retroactively define his state of mind as having been mistaken. *Ve'im mishe-nigneva behemto nadar, eino nazir* — but if the animal had already been stolen at the moment he accepted the *nezirut*, even though he was unaware of this, then he is not a *nazir*. In this case, the factual premise underlying his acceptance was false at the very moment of the vow. A *chacham* can question him: had you known at the time you accepted *nezirut* that your animal had already been stolen, would you have done so? He says no. That constitutes a classic *ta'ut* — the vow was accepted under a mistaken factual assumption, and under *Beit Hillel's* framework (since *Beit Shammai* does not really accept *nezirut b'ta'ut*), the *chacham* can release him.

The release in such a case works through the process of *hatara* as it operates in *hilchot nedarim* generally. When a vow is taken on the basis of an unexplicated assumption that turns out to be false, a *chacham* can serve as the mechanism for release. This is not explicit in the *mishna* itself — we are reading in what we know from *Masechet Nedarim* — but that is the operative framework.

## Future Destruction Error

### Ein Potchin B'Nolad

The principle at stake in the next case is *ein potchin b'nolad*: one may not open the door for a *heter* by invoking something that had not yet come into being at the time the vow was taken. Had the theft not yet occurred, it was not a fact in the world and not something the person could have anticipated. We are not fortune tellers. One cannot retroactively say: had I known what the future held, I would not have taken the vow — because there was no way to know the future. However, if the thing in question was something reasonably foreseeable — a *nolad hashachiach*, something likely to occur — then it may be used as a *petach* even if it had not yet occurred at the time of the vow. The precise line between what could and could not have been anticipated will be central to the discussions that follow.

## Stolen Animal Case…

### Nachum HaMedai Case

The *mishna* introduces the following arresting case: *keshe'alu nezirim min ha-golah* — when *nezirim* came up from the exile to bring their *korbanos* in Jerusalem — *u'matzu Beit HaMikdash charev* — and found that the *Beit HaMikdash* had been destroyed. This is a remarkable historical vignette: people who had accepted *nezirut* in the diaspora, who had not yet heard of the destruction, arriving in Jerusalem with their animals ready to bring *korbanos*, only to encounter the ruins. *Nachum HaMedai* addressed them and asked: *ilu heyissem yodim sheBeit HaMikdash charev, hayissem nozrim?* — had you known that the *Beit HaMikdash* would be destroyed when you accepted your *nezirut*, would you have become *nezirim*? They said no. And *Nachum HaMedai* released them.

---

## Future Destruction Err…

### Nachum's Reasoning

*Nachum HaMedai's* reasoning follows the logic of *nezirut b'ta'ut*: since the purpose of *nezirut* is ultimately to bring a *korban* in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and had they known the *Beit HaMikdash* would be destroyed they would not have accepted *nezirut*, their original vow was made under a false premise. This is precisely the structure of a *ta'ut* that a *chacham* can address. The difficulty, however, is that what *Nachum HaMedai* used as the basis for the *heter* was the future destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash* — something that had not yet occurred at the time they accepted *nezirut* and indeed something they could not have anticipated. This is a textbook application of *ein potchin b'nolad*, which is exactly why the *chachamim* overturned his ruling.

### Chachamim Response

*U-cheshe'va davar etzel chachamim amru* — when the matter came before the sages, they ruled: *kol she'nazar ad she-lo charav Beit HaMikdash, nazir* — anyone who accepted *nezirut* before the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash* remains a *nazir*. The fact that the *Beit HaMikdash* was subsequently destroyed cannot retroactively release a vow accepted when it was still standing. *U-meshecharav Beit HaMikdash, eino nazir* — but if someone accepted *nezirut* after the destruction, having not yet heard of it, then he is not a *nazir*. In that case, the destruction was already a fact in the world at the moment of the vow; he simply was unaware of it. That is genuine *ta'ut* — the vow was predicated on a state of affairs that had already ceased to exist. But using a future event — the anticipated destruction — as the basis for releasing someone who accepted *nezirut* while the *Beit HaMikdash* still stood: that is *ein potchin b'nolad*, and *Nachum HaMedai* erred.

## Potchin B'Nolad Debate

### Rabbi Eliezer Silenced

*Amar Rabba: shetaflu Rabbanan l'Rabbi Eliezer v'okmu b'shitasaihu* — the Rabbis overwhelmed *Rabbi Eliezer* and brought him into line with their position. *Rabbi Eliezer* in fact holds *potchin b'nolad* — as the *mishna* elsewhere records, *d'tannan: potchin b'nolad, divrei Rabbi Eliezer, va-chachamim ossrin* — *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that one may open a door for a *heter* based on something that came into being after the vow, while the *chachamim* forbid it. *Rabbi Eliezer* was active at the time of the *churban ha-Bayit* and might have been expected to defend *Nachum HaMedai's* ruling. Yet his voice does not appear in the *mishna* challenging the *chachamim*. *Rabba's* explanation is that the *Rabbanan* simply overwhelmed him — pressed him into silence, whether by persuasion or by sheer weight of consensus. This is a striking *gemara*: it does not record *Rabbi Eliezer* being convinced, only that he was silenced.

### Conditional Future

*Va'amar Rava* — and *Rava* introduces a subtle distinction: *af al pi d'amri Rabbanan ein potchin b'nolad, aval potchin b'tnai nolad* — even though the *Rabbanan* say one may not open with a *nolad*, one may open with a *conditional nolad*. The *gemara* explains: *ami lehon, ilu ata inish ve'ami l'chon de'charav Beit HaMikdash, mi haveisa nadrisah?* — you could say to the *nezirim*: not *had you known the Beit HaMikdash would be destroyed* — that would be *ein potchin b'nolad* — but rather: *had people come to you and told you that the Beit HaMikdash might be destroyed any day now, would you still have accepted your nezirut?*

The initial reading of this distinction is that it resembles *nolad hashachiach* — invoking a likely future event rather than an unknowable one. Perhaps the *nezirim*, had they stopped to take honest stock of the political realities and the military situation, might have recognized that destruction was a serious possibility. Had someone they trusted confronted them with this reality, would they have proceeded? And had they answered no — well, that is not asking them about something unknowable; it is asking whether, had they thought clearly and been properly informed, they would have acted differently.

### Rosh Advice Theory

*Tosafot* reads this differently, emphasizing the phrase *ilu ata inish ve'ami l'chon* — had *people* come and *told* you. The point is not merely about the objective probability of destruction but about what the *nezirim* would have done had they actually been pressured by advisers. The *Rosh* elaborates on this reading at length: *ilu hayu baim anashim v'hayu nosnim lachem eitzah she'lo tidru ulai yecharav Beit HaMikdash terem klos neziroschem, klum hayitem nozrim? Ve'omru ken hayinu nimlachim v'shomim l'atzasam* — had people come and counseled you not to take the vow, because the *Beit HaMikdash* might be destroyed before your *nezirut* concluded, would you have accepted? And they would say: yes, had they advised us, we would have listened to their advice. The *Rosh* states that this constitutes a valid *petach*: *pesach havei af al pi she'haeitzah tluyah b'nolad* — it is a valid opening even though the advice itself concerned a future *nolad*, because the *charat* is rooted in what people would have told them and what they would have listened to, not in the objective occurrence of the *nolad* itself. *Kivan she'machmas atzasam hayah chozer* — since their turning back would have been on account of the advice of others, this does not fall under the prohibition of *potchin b'nolad*.

The difficulty with this reading is genuine: the people never actually received such advice. Why should it matter that they would have listened to advisers who never came? Perhaps what this is really capturing is something like: had you been thinking clearly, had you had access to good judgment, would you have done this? The *petach* is not about the social pressure of friends but about what a reasonable, well-informed person in their position would have done.

### Prophetic Warning

*Amar Rav Yosef: ei havai hatam, hava amina lehon: ha-ketiv, Heichal Hashem Heichal Hashem Heichal Hashem heima* — *Rav Yosef* says: had I been there, I would have released them by citing the *pasuk* from *Yirmiyahu*, where the people foolishly declare *Heichal Hashem* — the Temple of God will protect itself — and *Yirmiyahu* is warning them of their false confidence. The implication, as *Tosafot* notes, is that the *pasuk* speaks implicitly of both a first and a second *Beit HaMikdash*: people trusted that God would protect the *mikdash*, and *Yirmiyahu* prophesied otherwise. *Rav Yosef's* argument would be that the *nezirim* should have known — from *Yirmiyahu's* warning and from the historical precedent of the first *churban* — that destruction was a real possibility. This would function as a *nolad hashachiach*: something one could and should have anticipated. Had you genuinely internalized what *Yirmiyahu* was telling you, would you have taken *nezirut*?

### Timing Objection

*Amar Abaye: nehi de-yadeitun lehu de-yicharav, mi yadeitun le-eimas?* — *Abaye* objects: granted, perhaps they should have known in some broad sense that the *Beit HaMikdash* was vulnerable to destruction; but did they know *when*? Even if they had absorbed *Yirmiyahu's* warning and the lessons of history, there was no reason to believe it would happen within the span of their *nezirut*. *Va-achti mi yadeina be-hai yoma?* — and even the additional *pasuk* from *Daniel* about seventy *shavuim* does not help, because even if one were to derive that destruction was four hundred and ninety years away, the precise timing was not knowable. To say that they should have foreseen not just that destruction would eventually come but that it would come *now*, within their *nezirut*, is to demand too much. The *gemara* accepts *Abaye's* objection and leaves *Rav Yosef's* approach unresolved.

This discussion also prompted a broader exchange about the human capacity for self-deception in precisely such circumstances. Even after the *churban* of the first *Beit HaMikdash*, people clung to the faith that God would not allow it to happen again — that the precedent was irrelevant, that this time was different. The historical parallels noted in the *Rosh's* comment are sharp: *she'hayu misyaashim min ha-puranus* — they despaired of punishment; *im yeicharev Heichal Hashem od yibaneh lanu bayit sheni, im yeicharev gam oso od yibaneh lanu shelishi* — if it falls, God will build another; if that falls too, another will come for the days of *Mashiach*. The capacity to rationalize confidence in the continuation of what one loves is not a failure unique to any generation. The very fact that *Yirmiyahu* was right once does not, as one might observe, persuade the next generation that he is still right.

## Six People Case

### Conditional Vows

The final *mishna* on the *daf* presents an intricate series of conditional vows. Six people are walking on a road when an unidentified man approaches. Each of the six makes a conditional *neder* of *nezirut* tied to the identity of the approaching person.

Person one says *hareini nazir shezeh ploni* — I will be a *nazir* if that is Reuven. Person two says *hareini nazir she-ein zeh ploni* — I will be a *nazir* if that is *not* Reuven. Person three says *hareini nazir she-echad mikem nazir* — I will be a *nazir* if one of you is a *nazir*. Since one of the first two must necessarily be correct — either it is Reuven or it is not — person three seems by all appearances destined to be a *nazir*. Person four says *hareini nazir she-ein echad mikem nazir* — I will be a *nazir* if none of you is a *nazir*. And also one of them may not be — since one of the first two must be wrong — so person four's condition is also not obviously unmet. Person five says *hareini nazir sheshneichem nezirim* — I will be a *nazir* if both of you are *nezirim*. Person six says *hareini nazir she-kulchem nezirim* — I will be a *nazir* if all of you are *nezirim*.

### Beit Shammai: All Nezirim

*Beit Shammai omrim kulam nezirim* — all six are *nezirim*. This is the position of *Beit Shammai*, who holds that *nezirut b'ta'ut* is *nezirut*. Everyone in this group wanted to become a *nazir*, and everyone thought their condition was being met, even if some were factually mistaken. The desire to accept *nezirut* was present; the error concerned the factual condition, not the intention. Under *Beit Shammai's* framework, that error does not undo the *nezirut*.

### Beit Hillel: Condition M…

*Beit Hillel omrim: eino nazir ela mi shelo nitkaymu devarav* — only those whose conditions were met become *nezirim*. The *gemara* notes that this formulation is puzzling on its face, since it seems to say only those who were *wrong* become *nezirim*. The *gemara* immediately corrects this to mean the opposite: only those who were *right*, whose conditions were fulfilled by the objective facts, are *nezirim*. Under *Beit Hillel's* understanding, if your condition was not met, you are simply not a *nazir* — there is no vow to be retroactively affirmed by the fact that you thought you were right.

### Rabbi Tarfon: None Nezir…

*Rabbi Tarfon omer: ein echad mehem nazir* — not one of them is a *nazir*. *Rabbi Tarfon* takes the opposite extreme from *Beit Shammai*: *lo nitna nezirut ela lehafla'ah* — *nezirut* was given only for *hafla'ah*, clear articulation, drawn from the verse *ki yafli lindor neder nazir*. Since at the time each person accepted *nezirut* there was genuine uncertainty about whether the condition would be met, the vow lacked the clarity required. Even persons three and four, who might seem to have had their conditions certainly met, are not *nezirim* according to *Rabbi Tarfon*: since persons one and two lacked the requisite clarity and are thus not *nezirim*, person three's condition — *one of you is a nazir* — was not met, and person four's condition — *one of you is not a nazir* — must be read narrowly, as *Tosafot* explains, to mean one is not and one is, which also fails if neither one nor two is a *nazir*. Persons five and six, who conditioned their *nezirut* on the *nezirut* of others, are therefore likewise released.

### Person Turns Away

*Hechezir panav le'achorav* — now suppose the approaching person turned away and his identity was never established. Within the framework of *Beit Hillel*, who holds that *nezirut* depends on whether the condition is met, what happens when the condition can never be evaluated? The *mishna* rules: *eino nazir* — he is not a *nazir*. If there is unresolvable doubt about whether your condition was met, you do not become a *nazir* under *Beit Hillel's* approach. This stands in contrast to *Beit Shammai*, under whose view everyone is a *nazir* regardless, and to the question *Rabbi Shimon* will now address.

### Rabbi Shimon: Safek Nazir

*Rabbi Shimon omer* — *Rabbi Shimon* says the logical position in the case of the person who turned away is that one is a *safek nazir*. The status is genuinely doubtful, and a *safek nazir* faces a particular practical problem: how does the *nezirut* ever conclude? One cannot bring a *korban* if one is in doubt about whether one is a *nazir* at all. *Rabbi Shimon's* solution is that the *safek nazir* must say: *im haya kidevarai, hareini nazir chovah* — if my condition was met, then I am a *nazir* by virtue of what I originally accepted; *ve'im lav, hareini nazir nedavah* — and if not, then I hereby accept *nezirut* as a free-will *neder* in any case. By accepting an optional *nezirut* in this conditional form, the person ensures that whatever his original status, he is now definitively a *nazir*, and can bring his *korbanos* and conclude.

What emerges from this final *mishna* is a clean spectrum of positions. *Beit Shammai* holds that *nezirut b'ta'ut* is *nezirut* — intending to be a *nazir* is enough, regardless of whether the condition was met. *Rabbi Tarfon* requires *hafla'ah*, absolute clarity at the moment of acceptance, and rejects *nezirut* whenever doubt was present at that moment. *Beit Hillel* stakes out the middle ground: *nezirut* depends on whether the condition is objectively satisfied. And *Rabbi Shimon*, working within the *Beit Hillel* framework, provides the practical resolution for the case of permanent uncertainty — the *safek nazir* accepts an additional, unconditional *nezirut nedavah* to bring the matter to a definitive close.
