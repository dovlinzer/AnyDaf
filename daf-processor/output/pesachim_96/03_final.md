# Pesachim 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach B'Tumah

### Intro: Tumah Heter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: דָּחֲקוּ טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְאָכְלוּ אֵימוּרֵי פֶּסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef asked** a similar question: **If people who are impure** due to ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse pushed** their way in **and ate portions** that are supposed to be consumed on the altar, such as the fats, the kidneys, and the fat tail, from **a Paschal lamb offered in ritual impurity,**

Daf 96 picks up at the very bottom of 95b, in what feels like a wrap-up mode for the final *mishnayos* and the final *perek* dealing with *korban pesach*. The organization is admittedly somewhat unusual: the *mishnah* immediately preceding this passage concerned the differences between *pesach rishon* and *pesach sheini*, and the *mishnah* coming up will address the differences between *pesach l'doros* and the *pesach* brought in Egypt. Situated between these two discussions is a *sugya* about *pesach haba b'tumah* — the Passover offering brought when the majority of the community is ritually impure.

The relevant background is as follows. When the community is *tamei*, there is a general *heter* permitting the *pesach* to be brought and eaten in a state of *tumah*. However, this *heter* does not apply to one whose *tumah* emanates from his body itself — a *zav* or a *zavah* — so if such a person were to eat, he would not be *chayav kares*. The position of Rabbi Eliezer extends a parallel logic to *bi'as mikdash*: just as other *tamei* people are permitted to enter the Temple precinct, even a *zav* or *zavah* who enters would not be *chayav kares*. The *Gemara* then probes whether there might still be restrictions within Rabbi Eliezer's position — for example, whether entering the *heichal* proper would remain *chayav* even for an ordinary person.

### Rav Yosef: Emurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ? מִדְּאִישְׁתְּרַי טוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר — אִישְׁתְּרַי נָמֵי טוּמְאַת אֵימוּרִין, אוֹ דִילְמָא: מַאי דְּאִישְׁתְּרַי — אִישְׁתְּרַי, מַאי דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּרַי — לָא אִישְׁתְּרַי.
>
> **Translation:** **what is** the *halakha*? Are they liable for eating sacrificial meat while they were ritually impure? Do we say that **since ritual impurity of the meat** that is eaten **was permitted, ritual impurity of the** sacrificial **portions** offered on the altar **was also permitted; or perhaps** we say that **what was permitted was permitted,** and **what was not permitted was not permitted?**

Against this backdrop, Rav Yosef poses a question at the end of 95b. Returning now to the view of the Rabbanan — who permit only the *eating* of the *pesach* in a state of *tumah*, not *bi'as mikdash* — the question is whether the *heter* for eating extends further than the *basar* of the *pesach* itself. Specifically: *dachaku t'mei meisin v'achlu emurei pesach haba b'tumah* — if *tamei meis* individuals forced their way and ate from the *emurim* (the inner organs of the *pesach* that are burned on the *mizbeiach*) in a time when *tumah* is *muttar*, what is the *halacha*?

The question carries several layers of complexity. Eating *emurim* is already prohibited under the prohibition against a *zar* — indeed, every person, even a *kohen*, is a *zar* with respect to *emurim*. Setting that aside, the narrower question is whether there is an additional *chiyuv* for being *tamei* specifically when eating *emurim*. In an ordinary time, eating *emurim* while *tamei* would entail two distinct prohibitions: the *issur zar* and the *issur tumah*. In a time when *pesach haba b'tumah* is in effect, the *heter* clearly removes the *issur tumah* from eating the *basar*. But does it extend to the *emurim* as well?

The question is framed as follows: *mi d'ishari tumas basar ishari nami tumas emurim* — since the *tumah* prohibition with respect to the *basar* has been made permissible, does the same follow for the *emurim*? Or does one say *mai d'ishari ishari, mai d'lo ishari lo ishari* — what was permitted, was permitted; what was not, remains prohibited?

### Rava: Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִכְּדֵי טוּמְאַת אֵימוּרִין מֵהֵיכָא אִיתְרַבַּי — מִטּוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר לַה׳״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָאֵימוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: After all, from where** was the *halakha* pertaining to eating the sacrificial **portions** in a state of **ritual impurity included?** It is derived **from** the *halakha* with regard to eating the **meat** in a state of **ritual impurity, as it is written:** "But the soul that eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace-offerings **that pertain to the Lord,** having his ritual impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:20); the apparently superfluous phrase "that pertain to the Lord" is there **to include the** sacrificial **portions** and establish that one is liable to receive *karet* for eating these portions in a state of ritual impurity.

Rava answers by tracing the very source of the *tumah* prohibition as it applies to *emurim*. How do we know in the first place that there is any prohibition against eating *emurim* while *tamei*? The source is a *pasuk* dealing with *basar* — the verse (Vayikra 7:20) which says *mibasar zeivach hashelamim asher l'Hashem v'tumasov alav*. The phrase *asher l'Hashem* is otherwise redundant when describing *zeivach hashelamim*, and so it is interpreted as *l'rabos es ha'emurim* — to include the *emurim*, teaching that the *tumah* prohibition on eating applies to them as well.

### Parallel Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר, אִיתֵיהּ לְטוּמְאַת אֵימוּרִין. כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר, לֵיתֵיהּ לְטוּמְאַת אֵימוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Since this is the source, there is a limitation to the *halakha* pertaining to sacrificial portions: **Anywhere that there is** liability **for** eating the **meat** in a state of **ritual impurity, there is** liability for eating the sacrificial **portions** in a state of **ritual impurity;** conversely, **anywhere that there is no** liability for eating the **meat** in a state of **ritual impurity,** such as in the case of a Paschal lamb offered in a state of ritual impurity, **there is no** liability for eating the sacrificial **portions** in a state of **ritual impurity.**

From this derivation, Rava articulates a governing principle: *kol heicha d'isa tumas basar isa tumas emurim; kol heicha d'lesa tumas basar lesa tumas emurim* — wherever the *tumah* prohibition on *basar* applies, the *tumah* prohibition on *emurim* applies; wherever it does not apply, neither does it apply to *emurim*. The conclusion: since in *pesach haba b'tumah* the *tumah* prohibition on the *basar* of the *pesach* is waived, the same waiver extends to its *emurim*. This is limited solely to the dimension of *tumah*. The *tamei* person who eats *emurim* remains *chayav* for eating them as a *zar* — that prohibition is entirely unaffected. He simply does not incur the additional *chiyuv* for *tumah*.

### Rabbi Zeira: Pesach Mitz…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֵימוּרֵי פֶּסַח מִצְרַיִם הֵיכָא אַקְטְרִינְהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּלָא שְׁוִיסְקֵי עֲבוּד?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira asked:** With regard to **the** sacrificial **portions of the Paschal lamb** that the Jewish people sacrificed in **Egypt** on the first Passover, just before they left Egypt, **where did they burn them?** The Torah does not say that they constructed an altar for this purpose. **Abaye said to him:** What place is there for such a question? **Who will tell us that they did not make them roasted** and eat them? The Jewish people had not yet been commanded with regard to the *halakhot* of sacrifices; therefore, they presumably did not distinguish between meat and forbidden fats, and they roasted and consumed the entire animal (*Arukh*).

Rabbi Zeira then asks a question that serves as a perfect bridge to the next *mishnah*: what was done with the *emurim* of *pesach Mitzrayim*? In Egypt, the lintel and doorposts served as a kind of *mizbeiach* for the *zerikah* of the blood. But where were the *emurim* burned? There was no actual *mizbeiach* in Egypt. This question naturally opens the discussion of what the *pesach* in Egypt looked like, and what distinguished it from the *pesach* of future generations.

## Pesach Mitzrayim vs L'Do…

### Abaye: Burning Emurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא תְּנָא רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁלֹשָׁה מִזְבְּחוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם: עַל הַמַּשְׁקוֹף, וְעַל שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזוֹת. וְתוּ מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rav Yosef taught** that **there were** three parts of the door upon which the blood was sprinkled that took the place of **three altars there** in Egypt. The blood was applied **upon the lintel and upon the two doorposts; and there was nothing else.** Presumably, the Jewish people did not fulfill any mitzva not explicitly mentioned in the Torah, and since no other altar is mentioned, one can conclude that they did not offer the fats and other sacrificial portions on an altar.

Abaye offers two responses to Rabbi Zeira's question. First: *uman leima lan dela sheviski avdi* — who is to say they did not simply roast the *emurim*? Perhaps they made a small fire that functioned symbolically the way the *mizbeiach*'s fire would, just as the doorposts served as the place for the blood in lieu of the altar. Second, Rav Yosef had a *baraisa* teaching that there were three "altars" at the time of *pesach Mitzrayim* — the lintel and the two doorposts — each serving as a valid surface for *zerikah*. But beyond those, there was nothing else — *vesu midei achrinei lo havei* — and perhaps the *emurim* were simply not burned at all; they may have been roasted together with the rest of the animal and eaten.

### Mishnah: Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָה בֵּין פֶּסַח מִצְרַיִם לְפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת? פֶּסַח מִצְרַיִם מִקָּחוֹ מִבֶּעָשׂוֹר, וְטָעוּן הַזָּאָה בַּאֲגוּדַּת אֵזוֹב, וְעַל הַמַּשְׁקוֹף וְעַל שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזוֹת, וְנֶאֱכָל בְּחִפָּזוֹן, בַּלַּיְלָה אֶחָד. וּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת נוֹהֵג כׇּל שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What** are the differences **between the Paschal lamb** that the Jewish people offered in **Egypt and the Paschal lamb** offered in all later **generations? The Paschal lamb** the Jewish people offered in **Egypt** had to be **taken from the tenth** of the month of Nisan **and required** the people to **sprinkle** its blood **with a bundle of hyssop,** unlike the Paschal lamb in all later years, **and** its blood was also sprinkled **upon the lintel and the two doorposts, and it was eaten with haste;** in addition, the Paschal lamb in Egypt was only **on one night,** whereas **the Paschal lamb** throughout the **generations is observed for seven** days.

With this as a launching point, the *Gemara* transitions to the *mishnah*, which addresses: *mah bein pesach Mitzrayim lepesach dorot* — what were the differences between the *pesach* brought in Egypt and the *pesach* of future generations? The *mishnah* itself mentions only a few distinctions; the *Tosefta* is considerably more expansive, and we will return to it below.

### Pesach Mitzrayim Practic…

The *mishnah* enumerates three practices unique to *pesach Mitzrayim*. First: *mikcho mibe'asor* — the lamb or kid was to be taken on the tenth of the month, as commanded in the verse *be'asor lachodesh hazeh veyikchu lahem ish seh leveit avot*. Second: *ta'un hazayah ba'agudat ezov ve'al hamashkof ve'al shtei hamezuzot* — it required sprinkling with a bundle of hyssop (*agudat ezov*) on the lintel and the two doorposts. These two requirements — the tenth-day selection and the hyssop-on-doorpost application — applied only in Egypt and not to future generations.

### Eating Requirements

Third: *ne'echal bechipazon balaylah* — it was to be eaten hurriedly and at night. This, too, the *mishnah* presents as a distinctive feature, with the implication that the *pesach* of future generations is not bound by the urgency of *chipazon* in the same manner. It was eaten *balaylah echad* — in one night — a limitation the *Gemara* will interrogate further.

### Pesach L'Doros: Seven Da…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת נוֹהֵג כׇּל שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **the Paschal lamb** throughout the **generations is observed for seven** days.

By contrast, *pesach dorot noheg kol shiv'ah* — the *pesach* of future generations extends over seven days. The *Gemara* will clarify that this cannot refer to the *korban* itself, since no one eats the *pesach* offering for seven days; rather it must refer to the prohibition of *chametz*, which applies for seven days in future generations. This introduces an interesting conflation within the *mishnah* between *pesach* as a *korban* (which has been the exclusive focus of the preceding five *perakim*) and *pesach* as a *yom tov* — the festival week with its *chametz* prohibition and *matzah* obligation. It is worth noting that shift.

## Textual Analysis

### Hazeh Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״דַּבְּרוּ אֶל כׇּל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה וְיִקְחוּ״. זֶה מִקָּחוֹ מִבֶּעָשׂוֹר, וְאֵין פֶּסַח דּוֹרוֹת מִקָּחוֹ מִבֶּעָשׂוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the meaning of this difficult phrase, as obviously, the Paschal lamb is slaughtered on the fourteenth and eaten on the evening of the fifteenth. GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where do we** conclude that the requirements of the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt do not apply to later generations? **As it is written: "Speak to all the congregation of Israel, saying: On the tenth day of this month they shall take** to them every man a lamb, according to their fathers' houses, a lamb for a household" (Exodus 12:3); and we derive from the superfluous word "this" that **this** Paschal lamb offered in Egypt had to be **taken from the tenth** of Nisan, **and the Paschal lamb of** later **generations is not taken from the tenth** of Nisan.

The *Gemara* now undertakes a systematic examination of how we know which practices were exclusive to *pesach Mitzrayim* and which were replicated for future generations. The organizing principle is the word *hazeh* — and related limiting terms such as *bo* and *mimenu* — which appear repeatedly throughout the *parshah* describing *pesach Mitzrayim*. The verse *be'asor lachodesh hazeh veyikchu* contains *hazeh*, which is read as a limitation: *hazeh mikcho be'asor ve'ein pesach dorot mikcho be'asor* — only *this* offering requires being taken on the tenth; future *pesachim* do not.

However, immediately a difficulty arises. The verse *ve'hayah lachem l'mishmeret ad arba'ah asar yom lachodesh hazeh* — which establishes a four-day inspection period (*bikur arba'ah yamim*) before *shechitah* — also contains *hazeh*. Applying the same logic would imply that this inspection requirement is also exclusive to *pesach Mitzrayim*, but that cannot be correct.

### Eating at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֶת הַבָּשָׂר בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, הָכִי נָמֵי — זֶה נֶאֱכָל בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵין אַחֵר נֶאֱכָל בַּלַּיְלָה?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Once again the Gemara questions its assertion that the phrase "this month" stated in the context of the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt indicates that its *halakhot* are not relevant to other offerings. **However, if** that is **so,** with regard to that **which is written: "And they shall eat the meat on that night"** (Exodus 12:8), **so too** it should be inferred that **this** Paschal lamb offered in Egypt **was eaten at night and no other** Paschal lamb offered in later generations must **be eaten at night?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall perform** this **service** in this month" (Exodus 13:5), in order to teach that the *halakhot* of the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt apply to later generations as well.

The *Gemara* similarly challenges: the verse *ve'achlu et habasar balaila hazeh* uses *hazeh* as well. Should we therefore say that *zeh ne'echal balaila ve'ein acher ne'echal balaila* — that *this* pesach is eaten at night and no other? In other words, is eating at night a restriction unique to *pesach Mitzrayim*?

### Va'avadta Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״הַזֶּה״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather,** if so, **why do I** need the word **"this"** stated with regard to the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt if all the *halakhot* relevant to that offering apply to the Paschal lambs of later generations? The Gemara answers that the phrase is needed **for** the derivations cited by **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya and Rabbi Akiva** (see *Berakhot* 9a) with regard to the length of time during which the Paschal lamb may be eaten.

The answer, which will be given repeatedly throughout this section: *amar kra va'avadta et ha'avodah hazot bachodesh hazeh* — the Torah states at the end of the *parshah* that "you shall perform this service in this month," a verse that speaks in future terms, pointing toward *pesach dorot*. This verse functions as a *ribuy* — a general extension of everything done in *pesach Mitzrayim* to all future *pesachim*. Consequently, whenever the *Gemara* proposes that *hazeh* or *bo* limits a given practice to *pesach Mitzrayim*, the answer is that *va'avadta et ha'avodah hazot* comes and extends it to future generations. The limiting words therefore cannot serve to exclude the practice from *pesach dorot*; they must be doing something else entirely.

### Chatzot Debate

The word *hazeh* in *balaila hazeh* is then explained as serving a different purpose: it provides the textual basis for the dispute between Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah and Rabbi Akiva in *Berakhot* regarding the time limit on eating the *korban pesach*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah derives from *va'avarti b'eretz Mitzrayim balaila hazeh* that the *pesach* may only be eaten until *chatzot*. Rabbi Akiva, however, holds that *hazeh* does not limit the eating to *chatzot*; rather, it excludes the following night — teaching that unlike *shelamim*, which may be eaten for two days and one intervening night, the *pesach* may not be eaten the night of the sixteenth. Each position thus finds a distinct function for *hazeh* without limiting the practice to *pesach Mitzrayim* alone.

### Four-Day Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה הַזֹּאת בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה״, שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל עֲבוֹדוֹת חֹדֶשׁ זֶה — כָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, the Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations** also requires examination four days prior to slaughter, **as it is written:** "And it shall be when the Lord shall bring you into the land of the Canaanite, and the Hittite, and the Amorite, and the Hivite, and the Jebusite, which He swore to your fathers to give you, a land flowing with milk and honey, **that you shall perform this service in this month"** (Exodus 13:5). From here it is derived **that all the services of this month** for all later generations **shall be like this,** including examination of the animal four days prior to sacrifice. Despite the fact that the verse connects the Paschal lamb in later years to the Paschal lamb in Egypt, the requirement to take the animal on the tenth of Nisan applied only on the first Passover.

Returning to the *bikur arba'ah yamim*: the *Gemara* shows from a *baraisa* that the four-day inspection requirement is not limited to *pesach Mitzrayim* at all — it applies both to the *tamid* and to all future *pesachim*.

### Ben Bag Bag Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לְתָמִיד שֶׁטָּעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר״, מָה לְהַלָּן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, אַף כָּאן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Bag Bag says: From where** do we know that **the daily offering requires examination four days prior to** its **slaughter? It is stated** with regard to the daily offering: "Command the children of Israel, and say to them: My food which is presented to Me for offerings made by fire, of a sweet flavor to Me, **you shall safeguard it to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), **and it states there,** with regard to the Paschal lamb: **"And it shall be for you as a safeguard until the fourteenth day of this month"** (Exodus 12:6). **Just as** in the verse **there,** the Paschal lamb **requires examination four days prior to** its **slaughter, so too here,** the daily offering **requires examination four days prior to** its **slaughter.** There is no inference from the phrase "this month" that the requirement of examining an offering four days before is limited to the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt.

Ben Bag Bag — known to most from *Pirkei Avos* but who appears here in a substantive *halachic* context — derives the obligation for the *tamid* to be inspected for four days before *shechitah* by a *gezeirah shavah*: the verse by *tamid* says *tishmeru l'hakriv li b'mo'ado*, and the verse by *pesach Mitzrayim* says *ve'hayah lachem l'mishmeret* — the word *tishmeru* and the word *mishmeret* share the same root. Just as *mishmeret* by the *pesach* means four days of inspection before *shechitah*, so too *tishmeru* by the *tamid* means four days of inspection before *shechitah*. And by *pesach dorot*, the verse *va'avadta et ha'avodah hazot bachodesh hazeh* likewise extends the four-day inspection requirement.

### Resolving Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָהוּא ״הַזֶּה״ — לְמַעוֹטֵי פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** what does **that** phrase: **"this** month," stated with regard to the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, come to exclude? The Gemara answers that it comes **to exclude** the Paschal lamb of **the second *Pesaḥ*, which is similar to it,** as it is also limited to a one day celebration. The Paschal lamb of the second *Pesaḥ* does not require examination four days prior to its slaughter.

The *Gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction as follows. The word *hazeh* that appears in the verse establishing *bikur arba'ah yamim* does carry limiting force — but it excludes only *pesach sheini* from the requirement, not *pesach dorot* generally. The reasoning is that *pesach sheini* is structurally analogous to *pesach Mitzrayim* in that it pertains solely to the *korban* and carries no *yom tov* dimension, and therefore its exclusion from *bikur* is what the *hazeh* signals. Meanwhile, the *hazeh* that appears in the verse about taking the animal on the tenth day of the month carries a different limiting force: it restricts *that* specific requirement — *mikcho be'asor* — to *pesach Mitzrayim* alone.

The result is a principled but layered structure: essentially everything done in *pesach Mitzrayim* is replicated for *pesach dorot* by virtue of *va'avadta et ha'avodah hazot*, and the limiting terms scattered throughout the *parshah* are each assigned to exclude something specific — but nearly nothing is excluded wholesale from future *pesachim*. The only practice the *Gemara* definitively restricts to *pesach Mitzrayim* at this stage is *mikcho be'asor*.

## Phrase-by-Phrase Analysis

### Arel Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכׇל עָרֵל לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּ״בוֹ״ אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״. אֶלָּא, ״בּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? דְּ״בוֹ״ אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל הוּא אוֹכֵל בְּמַצָּה וּמָרוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **The** Gemara asks: **However, if** that is **so,** with regard to that **which is written** in the context of the Paschal lamb offered in Egypt: **"And no uncircumcised person shall eat from it"** (Exodus 12:48), **so too,** should it be inferred that the expression "from it" limits the application of the verse and teaches **that "from it,"** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **he** may **not eat, but he** may **eat from the Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall perform,"** which teaches that the *halakhot* of the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt apply to later generations as well. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the expression **"from it"** stated here? The Gemara answers that it teaches that **from it,** the Paschal lamb, **he may not eat, but he eats from the *matza* and bitter herbs.** An uncircumcised person is obligated to eat *matza* and bitter herbs on Passover, just like any other Jew.

The *Gemara* continues its phrase-by-phrase survey. The verse states *v'chol arel lo yochal bo* — no uncircumcised person shall eat from it. Applying the same logic: *hacha nami bo eino ochel aval ochel b'pesach dorot* — perhaps the word *bo* limits this exclusion to *pesach Mitzrayim*, and an *arel* would be permitted to eat from future *pesachim*? The answer: *amar kra va'avadta et ha'avodah* — the extension verse replicates the practice. An *arel* is excluded from eating the *pesach* in all generations.

So what does the word *bo* accomplish here? *Bo eino ochel aval ochel hu b'matzah u'maror* — the *arel* is excluded from eating the *pesach* itself, but he may still eat *matzah* and *maror*. This is worth pausing over, because the need for a *pasuk* to teach this seems puzzling. If *matzah* and *maror* constitute independent *mitzvos*, why would anyone have thought an *arel* is excluded from them? One possibility is that the Torah might have been understood to prohibit even eating *maror* or *matzah* in the company of the *pesach* without actually eating the *pesach* itself, lest it appear as a pseudo-*pesach* observance. The *pasuk* therefore comes to clarify that eating *matzah* and *maror* is permitted even for an *arel*.

There is, however, a deeper conceptual point underlying this discussion. The Torah's primary *mitzvah* of *matzah* is not an independent *mitzvah* in the simple sense — it is tied to the manner of eating the *korban pesach*. *Chazal* derived a separate *mitzvah* of *achilas matzah* independent of the *korban*, but *maror* in our era is only *derabanan* precisely because there is no *korban pesach* — the *mitzvah* of *maror* is contingent on the *pesach*. If a person has no obligation or access to the *pesach* offering, it is not immediately obvious why he would have cause to eat *maror* at all. The *pasuk*'s teaching that the *arel* may eat *matzah* and *maror* is therefore not merely clarifying that he is *muttar* to do so in some formal sense; it is affirming that the *arel* retains a *chiyuv* in the independent *mitzvah* of *matzah*, and that eating *maror* — even without the *pesach* — is permitted to him and not prohibited as an apparent imitation of the *pesach* ritual.

### Ben Neichar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״(וְכׇל) בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּ״בוֹ״ אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא בְּפֶסַח לְדוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״. אֶלָּא, ״בּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? ״בּוֹ״ מְשׁוּמָּדוּת פּוֹסֶלֶת, וְאֵין מְשׁוּמָּדוּת פּוֹסֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to question: **However, if** that is **so,** when the verse states: **"No stranger shall eat from it"** (Exodus 12:43), **so too,** should it be inferred that it is only **from it,** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **that he** may **not eat, but he** may **eat** from **the Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall perform,"** which teaches that the *halakhot* of the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt apply to future generations as well. Consequently, anyone who was prohibited from eating the Paschal lamb in Egypt may not eat it in subsequent years either. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the qualifying phrase: **"From it"?** The Gemara answers: It is **from it,** eating the Paschal lamb, that **apostasy disqualifies,** as the term "stranger" in this context is understood to refer to a Jew whose conduct makes him estranged from God; **but apostasy does not disqualify** one from eating ***teruma*.** Therefore, a priest who has become an apostate may still consume *teruma*.

The verse continues: *kol ben nechar lo yochal bo* — no foreigner shall eat from it. The simple reading would indicate a non-Jew, but *Chazal* understood that a *pasuk* is unnecessary to exclude a non-Jew from a Jewish communal sacrifice. They therefore interpreted *ben nechar* to mean *shenitnachru ma'asav l'Aviv shebashamayim* — one who has become foreign to his Father in Heaven, i.e., an apostate who has renounced his faith.

As before, the *Gemara* asks: perhaps *bo* limits this exclusion to *pesach Mitzrayim*, and an apostate could eat from *pesach dorot*? *Va'avadta* extends it — the prohibition applies in all generations. What then is the *bo* teaching? *Bo hamarat dat poselet ve'ein hamarat dat poselet b'trumah* — for the *korban pesach*, apostasy (*hamarat dat*) disqualifies; but apostasy does not disqualify one from eating *terumah*. Even if the apostate in question is a *kohen* whose *terumah* would otherwise be permitted, renouncing his faith does not invalidate his *terumah* access. The *bo* thus localizes the disqualifying force of *hamarat dat* specifically to the *pesach*.

The *Gemara* then asks why the Torah needed both prohibitions — *arel* and *ben nechar* — when one might have seemed derivable from the other. *Ve'itztarick l'michtav arel ve'itztarick l'michtav ben nechar*. Had only the *arel* been written, one might have said the exclusion is due to something physically repugnant about the uncircumcised state — *mishum de'ma'is* — but that wouldn't apply to an apostate, who shows no external sign of his spiritual distance. Had only *ben nechar* been written, one might have said the exclusion is because he is not engaged in an act of worship — *de'ein libo lashamayim* — but an *arel* whose uncircumcision is involuntary (as in a family of hemophiliacs) may be entirely devout in his religious life and should not be excluded. Therefore both are necessary.

### Toshav and Sachir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״תּוֹשָׁב וְשָׂכִיר לֹא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּ״בוֹ״ הוּא אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to question its initial derivation: **However, if** that is **so,** when the verse pertaining to the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt states: **"A sojourner and a hired servant shall not eat from it"** (Exodus 12:45), **so too,** should it be derived that **from it,** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **he** may **not eat, but he** may **eat from the Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "And you shall perform,"** which teaches that the *halakhot* of the Paschal lamb in later generations are the same as the *halakhot* that applied in Egypt.

The verse additionally states *toshav vesachir lo yochal bo* — a sojourner and a hired hand shall not eat from it. The *Gemara* asks the same structural question — perhaps *bo* limits this to *pesach Mitzrayim* — and gives the same answer: *va'avadta* extends it. As for what *bo* adds, the *Gemara* produces a formulation about *hamarat dat* that seems to duplicate what was just said about *ben nechar*. Rashi entirely crosses out these three lines, noting that they are incoherent. The reason is that in the rabbinic reading, *toshav vesachir* refers not to foreigners but to a Hebrew bondsman: a *toshav* is a *nirtza* — one who extended his servitude through *yovel* — and a *sachir* is a Hebrew slave serving a six-year term. According to *Chazal*, such individuals *do* eat the *korban pesach*, in tension with the *pasuk's* plain sense. The treatment of these verses in other contexts involves very particular readings of the relevant *pesukim*, and Rashi accordingly deletes these lines as not fitting with the rest of the *Gemara*.

### Circumcision Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״וּמַלְתָּה אֹתוֹ אָז יֹאכַל בּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּ״בוֹ״ אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״. אֶלָּא ״בּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? ״בּוֹ״ מִילַת זְכָרָיו וַעֲבָדָיו מְעַכֶּבֶת, וְאֵין מִילַת זְכָרָיו וַעֲבָדָיו מְעַכֶּבֶת בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its line of questioning: **However, if** that is **so,** when the verse said: **"When you have circumcised him, then shall he eat from it"** (Exodus 12:44), **so too,** should it be inferred **that from it,** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **he** may **not eat** unless he first circumcises his sons and slaves, **but he** may **eat from the Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall perform,"** which teaches that this *halakha* applies to later generations as well. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the qualifying term **"from it"?** The Gemara answers that this term teaches that **from it,** the Paschal lamb, **the** lack of **circumcision of one's male** children **and slaves prevents** a father or master from eating until he ensures that all of the male members of his household have been circumcised, **but the circumcision of one's male** children **and slaves does not prevent** him **from** eating ***teruma*** if he is himself eligible to eat it.

The verse states *u'maltah oto az yochal bo* — circumcise your sons and slaves, and then you (and they) may eat from it. The *Gemara* asks: might *bo* limit this requirement of household circumcision to *pesach Mitzrayim*, such that future *pesachim* could be eaten even by one whose slaves and children are uncircumcised? *Va'avadta* extends the requirement. The *bo* then does its own narrower work: *bo b'kasher velo b'pasul* — this rule of not breaking bones applies to a valid *pesach* and not to an invalid one.

### Raw vs. Roasted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל תֹּאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ נָא״, ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ אִי אַתָּה אוֹכֵל, אֲבָל אַתָּה אוֹכֵל נָא בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״. אֶלָּא ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְרַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **However, if** that is **so,** from that **which is written: "Do not eat of it raw,** nor boiled in water, but roasted in fire" (Exodus 12:9), should it be inferred that it is only **of it,** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **that you** may **not eat** raw, **but you** may **eat raw** meat **from the Paschal lamb of** later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "You shall perform,"** which teaches that the prohibition applies to later generations as well. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the qualifying expression **"in it"?** The Gemara answers that it is necessary **for that which Rabba said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said,** that this verse serves as the basis of a verbal analogy which teaches that an uncircumcised man may not eat tithes.

Similarly, the verse *al tochlu mimenu na* — do not eat of it raw — contains the limiting term *mimenu*. The *Gemara* asks: should *mimenu* restrict the prohibition on eating raw to *pesach Mitzrayim* only? *Va'avadta* again extends it. The *mimenu* is then assigned a different function, linked to an interpretation cited in the name of Rabba citing Rav Yosef, extending certain prohibitions — particularly the *issur* on an *arel* eating — to the domain of *ma'aser*, and clarifying that while an *arel* is prohibited from eating *terumah*, the parallel prohibitions concerning *ben nechar* and unmolled household members do not carry over to *terumah* in the same fashion. *Ve'ein milat zcharav ve'avadav me'akevet biterumah* — circumcision of one's sons and slaves is not a barrier to eating *terumah*, even though it is a barrier to eating the *pesach*.

### Breaking Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא יִשְׁבְּרוּ בּוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּ״בוֹ״ אֵינוֹ שׁוֹבֵר, אֲבָל שׁוֹבֵר בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״. אֶלָּא ״בּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? ״בּוֹ״ בְּכָשֵׁר, וְלֹא בְּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **However, if** that is **so,** with regard to that **which is written: "And they shall not break a bone in it"** (Numbers 9:12), **so too,** should it be inferred that it is prohibited to break a bone only **of it,** the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt, **but one** may **break** a bone **of a Paschal lamb** sacrificed in later **generations?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: You shall perform,** which applies the *halakhot* stated with regard to the Paschal lamb sacrificed in Egypt to the Paschal lambs of later generations as well. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the qualifying expression **"in it"?** The Gemara explains that this qualification teaches that only a bone **in it, a fit Paschal lamb,** may not be broken, but there is **no** prohibition to break the bone **of an unfit** Paschal lamb.

The verse *ve'etzem lo tishberu bo* — do not break a bone of it — likewise uses *bo*. The *Gemara* asks whether this prohibition might be limited to *pesach Mitzrayim* by virtue of *bo*. *Va'avadta* extends it; the prohibition on breaking bones applies to *pesach dorot* as well. Here, too, the residual function of *bo* is to specify *bo b'kasher velo b'pasul* — that the prohibition on breaking bones applies to a valid *pesach* and not to an invalid one.

## Summary & Transition

### Mitzrayim Unique

Having gone through the central limiting terms of the *parshah* systematically, the *Gemara* arrives at its conclusions. The practices replicated from *pesach Mitzrayim* for future generations include: eating at night, the exclusion of the *arel* and *ben nechar*, the requirement to circumcise one's household, the prohibition on eating raw, and the prohibition on breaking bones. The practice categorically restricted to *pesach Mitzrayim* — identified through *hazeh* with no countervailing *va'avadta* extension — is *mikcho be'asor*, taking the animal on the tenth of the month. The word *hazeh* by *bikur arba'ah yamim* excludes only *pesach sheini*, not *pesach dorot*, from the inspection requirement.

An interesting thread runs through the entire discussion: the *Gemara* acknowledges that *va'avadta et ha'avodah hazot bachodesh hazeh* functions as a general *ribuy* bringing the totality of *pesach Mitzrayim* experience into *pesach dorot*. The limiting terms are then assigned particular, targeted functions rather than wholesale exclusions. The logic is that when *Chazal* understood a practice to be inherently local to the Egyptian context, they found a limiting word to carry that message; everything else was presumed to carry forward.

### Tosefta Details

The *Tosefta* at the end of *perek* 8 fills out a richer picture. It identifies a number of similarities that one might not have expected: *pesach Mitzrayim* was also conducted in three shifts (*gimel kitot*), just as *pesach dorot* is; and just as in future generations the *pesach* must not have any portion left over until morning, the same *lo totiru* applied in Egypt.

Among the differences the *Tosefta* enumerates: in Egypt, certain subtle *psulim* such as *etnan* and *mechir* (the hire of a prostitute and the price of a dog, which later *halacha* disqualifies from being brought as a *korban*) did not yet apply — the Torah had not yet stated those prohibitions. Furthermore, *pesach Mitzrayim* required that the animal be taken *hu ushcheino* — one's own household together specifically with one's neighbor — whereas *pesach dorot* does not require neighbors; any *chabura* will do. In Egypt, each individual slaughtered in his own home (*kol echad ve'echad shocheit betoch beito*), whereas in future generations all of Israel slaughters in one communal place. And in Egypt, the requirement *lo tetze'u ish mipetach beito* — no one was to leave his home until morning — meant that one had to sleep (*telinas laylah*) precisely where one ate; by contrast, *pesach dorot* permits eating in one place and sleeping elsewhere.

## Summary & Transition (II)

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon dissents from the ruling that neighbors need not be included in *pesach dorot*. He argues that even for future generations, one should preferably conduct the *pesach* together with one's nearest neighbor rather than traveling to celebrate with a more distant friend. His reasoning is the cultivation of neighborly bonds — *kedei shelo yaniach adam shcheino hakarov el beito veyelech la'asot pesacho etzel chaveiro* — and he anchors this in the verse *tov shachen karov me'ach rachok*, "a near neighbor is better than a distant brother."

### Emurim Service

Returning to Rabbi Zeira's original question about the *emurim* of *pesach Mitzrayim*, the *Tosefta* provides the clearest answer: *pesach Mitzrayim lo hayah ta'un damim vechelev legabei mizbe'ach* — the *pesach* in Egypt did not involve bringing the blood and fat (*chelev*) to the *mizbeiach*, in contrast to *pesach dorot*. This directly resolves the *chelev* dimension of the question. The Israelites in Egypt performed *zerikah* on the doorposts rather than on the altar, and the *emurim* were never offered in the formal *hekter* sense. *Pesach dorot*, by contrast, does require the *damim* and *chelev* to go to the *mizbeiach* in the ordinary fashion.

## Temurah of Pesach

### Yehoshua's Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁתְּמוּרַת פֶּסַח קְרֵיבָה, וּתְמוּרַת פֶּסַח אֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה, וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I heard** two rulings from my teachers: One ruling was **that the substitute of a Paschal lamb is sacrificed** as a peace-offering after Passover, and another ruling was that **the substitute of a Paschal lamb is not sacrificed** as a peace-offering after Passover; **and I cannot explain** these two rulings, as I do not remember the circumstances to which each ruling applies.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which shifts from the *pesach Mitzrayim*–*pesach dorot* comparison to the laws of *temurah* as they apply to the *korban pesach*. Rabbi Yehoshua says: *shamati she'tmurat pesach kreivah utmurat pesach einah kreivah* — "I have heard that the *temurah* of a *pesach* is brought as a *korban*, and I have heard that the *temurah* of a *pesach* is not brought — but I cannot explain the difference between the two cases." The phrase *ve'ein li lefareish* is striking: Rabbi Yehoshua received the tradition but could not reconstruct its reasoning.

It is worth noting the characteristic posture of Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Eliezer as transmitters of tradition — their strength lay in preserving and handing down received rulings with precision. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, was of the newer school: deeply analytical, creative, and willing to reconstruct the *rationale* behind traditions rather than simply store them.

### Akiva: Nidcheh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲנִי אֲפָרֵשׁ: הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו שְׁלָמִים. וְכֵן תְּמוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said: I will explain:** With regard to a lamb that is separated as a **Paschal lamb** and is then lost, leading the owner to separate another animal as its replacement, **and is** later **found before the slaughter of the** replacement **Paschal lamb,** it is left to **graze until it becomes unfit [*yista'ev*]** and disqualified for use as a sacrifice. It is then **sold** and becomes unconsecrated, **and** the owner must **bring a peace-offering with its proceeds. And so too,** the same is true with regard to **its substitute:** If one separates another lamb as a substitute for this replacement, the sanctity of the original lamb extends to the substitute as well. In the case outlined above, the substitute would graze, just like the replacement, until it developed a blemish and would then be sold. This is the circumstance in which the substitute of a Paschal lamb is not sacrificed.

Rabbi Akiva steps in: *ani afareish* — "I will explain." The key concept he introduces is *nidcheh* — an animal that has been "pushed away" or stigmatized. The *halacha* of *nidcheh* in the context of *korban pesach* operates as follows: if one sanctified an animal as a *pesach*, then lost it and sanctified a second one in its place, and the first animal subsequently reappeared — the first animal has been actively set aside. It was present, it could have been brought, and instead one designated another animal. That deliberate displacement is what constitutes *nidcheh*, and such an animal cannot itself be brought as a *shelamim*; it can only be redeemed once it develops a *mum*, with the proceeds used to purchase a *shelamim*.

Crucially, Rabbi Akiva argues that the *temurah* of a *pesach* simply mirrors the original *pesach*. The *chiddush* — and the reason Rabbi Yehoshua's tradition spoke specifically about *temurah* rather than the *pesach* itself — will emerge below.

### Before Shechitah

The first case: *hapesach shenimtza kodem shechitat hapesach*. If the lost animal was found before the new *pesach* was slaughtered, then at the moment of that *shechitah* there were two available animals. The owner actively chose the new one and thereby pushed away the old one. That old animal is *nidcheh* — stigmatized — and may not be brought directly as a *shelamim*. It must graze until it develops a *mum*, then be sold, and the proceeds used to buy a *shelamim*. And similarly, any *temurah* made from that *nidcheh* animal inherits its status: the *temurah* too cannot be brought directly and must wait for the same process.

### After Shechitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַר שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח — קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים. וְכֵן תְּמוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, if the lost lamb is found **after** the **slaughter of the** replacement **Paschal lamb, it** itself **is sacrificed as a peace-offering, and so too, its substitute** is sacrificed, which explains the ruling that the substitute of a Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering.

The second case: *achar shechitat hapesach*. If the lost animal was found only after the new *pesach* was already slaughtered, then the old animal was never actively displaced — it was simply absent when the decision was made. The owner had no opportunity to bring it; its absence was not due to his deliberate choice. This animal is not *nidcheh*, and it can be brought directly as a *shelamim*. And likewise, any *temurah* made from it can be brought directly as a *shelamim*.

## Temurah Status

### Why Temurah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלֵימָא: פֶּסַח קָרֵב וּפֶסַח אֵינוֹ קָרֵב. הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאִיכָּא תְּמוּרַת הַפֶּסַח דְּלָא קָרְבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the explanation of Rabbi Akiva: If so, **let** the mishna **say** these *halakhot* more simply, as follows: **The Paschal lamb** itself **is sacrificed and the Paschal lamb is not sacrificed.** According to Rabbi Akiva's explanation, the same rule applies to both the lamb and its substitute. What additional point is Rabbi Yehoshua making by speaking about the substitute? The Gemara answers: **This** comes to **teach us** through this formulation **that there is a** case of a **substitute Paschal lamb that is not sacrificed.** One might have thought that a substitute Paschal lamb is always treated as a peace-offering, even when it was substituted before the time of the slaughter; therefore, the mishna states that there is an instance in which it cannot be sacrificed because it is a substitute for a Paschal lamb that itself cannot be sacrificed.

The *Gemara* asks the obvious question: given that Rabbi Akiva's entire explanation focuses on the status of the original *pesach* — *nidcheh* or not — why did Rabbi Yehoshua's tradition concern itself with the *temurah* at all? Shouldn't the *mishnah* have discussed the original *pesach* directly? *Leima pesach kareiv upesach eino kareiv* — let it simply say: this *pesach* is brought and that *pesach* is not brought!

### Temurah Follows

The *Gemara* answers: *ha kamashma lan d'ika temurat pesach d'lo kareiv* — the tradition comes to teach us a *chiddush* about the *temurah* specifically. One might have thought that even if the original *pesach* is *nidcheh* and cannot be brought itself, the *temurah* — which was never personally pushed away — should always be eligible to be brought as a *shelamim*. The original animal was set aside; the *temurah* animal was never part of that drama. The *chiddush* is that this is not so: the *temurah* takes on the full status of the original *pesach*, and if the *pesach* was *nidcheh*, so too is its *temurah* — it cannot be brought directly and requires the same redemption process.

### Shechitah vs Chatzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבָּה אָמַר: קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וּלְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה שָׁנִינוּ. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וּלְאַחַר חֲצוֹת שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there was a dispute between *amora'im* about the proper text of the mishna. **Rabba said: We learned** the opinion of Rabbi Akiva in the mishna as referring to the distinction of whether the lamb is found **before** the **slaughter** of the replacement lamb **or after** the **slaughter** of the replacement lamb. **Rabbi Zeira said: We learned** that it depends upon whether it is found **before midday or after midday.** Midday is the time that determines the status of an animal as a Paschal lamb; therefore, if the lamb is found after midday, even if it is found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, its identity as a Paschal lamb is not firmly established and it may be sacrificed as a peace-offering.

There is then a *machlokes* between Rabba and Rabbi Zeira regarding what precisely defines the threshold that renders a *pesach nidcheh*. Rabba holds: *kodem shechitah v'achar shechitah shaninu* — the *mishnah's* distinction is simply about whether the lost animal was found before or after the *shechitah* of the replacement *pesach*. If found before the *shechitah*, there were two animals available at the decisive moment, and the old one was actively passed over; if found only after, the *shechitah* had already occurred and there was no opportunity to choose the old one. Rabbi Zeira disagrees: *kodem chatzot v'achar chatzot shaninu* — the operative threshold is not *shechitah* itself but *chatzot*, midday. If the lost animal was found before *chatzot*, when the time for *shechitah* had begun but the owner still had time to act, its non-selection renders it *nidcheh*; if found after *chatzot*, it was effectively out of the picture at the critical moment.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי. הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — יִרְעֶה, לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — יִקְרַב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — יִרְעֶה, לְאַחַר חֲצוֹת — יִקְרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this dispute among the *amora'im* **is like** the following dispute between ***tanna'im*** in a mishna: **The Paschal lamb that is found before the slaughter** of its substitute **shall be left to graze** until it becomes unfit; it is then sold, and the proceeds are used to purchase an animal that will be sacrificed as a peace-offering. If it is found **after the slaughter, it** itself **is to be sacrificed** as a peace-offering. This is parallel to the explanation of Rabba. **Rabbi Eliezer says** that if it is found **before midday it shall be left to graze,** but if it is found **after midday it is to be sacrificed** as a peace-offering, which is parallel to the explanation of Rabbi Zeira.

The *Gemara* notes that this dispute maps onto a *machlokes tannaim*. The *baraisa* states: *hapesach shenimtza kodem shechitah yera'eh le'achar shechitah yikareiv* — if found before *shechitah*, it must graze until a *mum*; if found after, it is brought directly as a *shelamim*. Rabbi Eliezer in the *baraisa*, however, holds: *kodem chatzot yera'eh, le'achar chatzot yikareiv* — before *chatzot* it grazes, after *chatzot* it is brought directly. The rationale is that *chatzot* marks the moment when the *pesach* was effectively expected to have been brought; an animal that was missing at that moment was naturally removed from consideration, and its later reappearance does not retroactively make one's decision to bring the new animal an act of deliberate displacement.

### Rava: After Shechitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַר שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח יָבִיא שְׁלָמִים וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה. אֲבָל נִמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — תְּמוּרָתוֹ מִכֹּחַ קְדוּשָּׁה דְּחוּיָה קָא אָתָא, וְלָא קָרְבָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that if the original lamb is found **after the slaughter of the** replacement **Paschal lamb, one** should **bring** the original lamb as a **peace-offering,** and the same applies to its substitute. **Rava said: They taught** that the substitute may be sacrificed as a peace-offering **only when** the original lamb **was found after the slaughter** of the replacement lamb **and one performed the substitution after the slaughter,** when the original lamb itself was fit to be offered as a peace-offering. **But** if the original lamb **was found before the slaughter,** even if **one performed the substitution after the slaughter** of the replacement lamb, the substitute lamb may not be sacrificed as an offering. The reason for this is that the original lamb may no longer be sacrificed as an offering and must be left to graze until it develops a blemish. Therefore, the sanctity of **its substitute comes on the strength of deferred sanctity, and** the substitute **may not be sacrificed.**

Rava adds a further refinement regarding the *temurah* specifically. The *mishnah* implies that the *temurah's* status simply mirrors the original *pesach's* status — if the *pesach* was found after *shechitah* and is not *nidcheh*, neither is its *temurah*. But Rava clarifies: this is only true if both the finding of the original animal *and the act of temurah* occurred after *shechitah*. Specifically: *lo shanu ela she'nimtza achar shechitah ve'hemir bo achar shechitah* — the case of the *mishnah* where the *temurah* is brought directly applies only when the original was found after *shechitah* and the *temurah* was also made after *shechitah*. The reasoning is that if the act of *temurah* itself was performed before the *pesach* was slaughtered, then at the moment of *shechitah* there were two candidates — the original and the *temurah* — and bringing the designated *pesach* effectively pushed away both. But if the *temurah* was made only after *shechitah*, the *temurah* animal was never present as an option during the decisive moment and was never pushed away. The discussion of this final case is left open as the *daf* concludes, to be continued in the following shiur.
