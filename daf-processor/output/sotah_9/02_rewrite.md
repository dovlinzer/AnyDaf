# Sotah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Midah K'neged Midah Intro

### Ritual to Theology

The ninth daf of Sotah opens with a return to the *mishna*, which we transition from describing the procedural mechanics of the *sotah* ritual to something of greater theological depth: the principle that everything done to the *sotah* parallels the acts she performed in her seduction and adultery. This framing is captured in the broader motto of *midah k'neged midah* — measure for measure — a concept so fundamental to the Gemara's worldview that one might note, with some amusement, that Shakespeare borrowed it from precisely here.

### Core Principle

The core claim is that God brings about events in this world that constitute fitting payback for a person's deeds. Divine justice is not abstract or deferred indefinitely; it operates through a precise correspondence between sin and consequence.

### Presumption of Guilt

It is worth pausing at a striking feature of this framing. The entire ritual leading up to the drinking of the water — the public exposure, the disheveling of hair, the rending of garments — occurs before any determination of guilt. The *mishna* and the Gemara's elaboration of *midah k'neged midah* package all of this as punishment and divine payback, yet at the time these acts are performed, it is not yet known whether the woman is guilty. The framing thus carries within it a pronounced presumption of guilt.

### Tosafot Garment

This observation is reinforced by a comment of Tosafot regarding the rending of the *sotah*'s garments. Tosafot notes that a similar garment-rending occurs before administering lashes to a condemned person — a procedure connected to the requirement that lashes be administered directly to the body. But Tosafot goes further, understanding this act as a form of punishment imposed by *beis din*. The difficulty is obvious: the person receiving lashes is known to be guilty, whereas the *sotah* has not yet been proven so. Framing the garment-rending as *beis din* punishment applied here only deepens the implicit presumption of guilt.

### Ordeal vs. Evidence

There is a further tension worth noting. The *sotah* ritual is at its core a trial by ordeal — a miraculous divine intervention meant to determine guilt or innocence. At various points the Gemara tends to downplay this miraculous dimension: it works to have her confess early, treats the water's effect as a form of evidence that could theoretically override testimony, and provides explanations for why the water might not work even when she is guilty (perhaps she or her husband had accumulated merit, or perhaps she had committed other sins). This is one approach — containing and naturalizing the ordeal.

The *midah k'neged midah* framing takes the opposite approach, heightening the miraculous dimension. Rather than minimizing divine intervention, it foregrounds it: God is actively punishing the *sotah* in precise correspondence to her sins. The more one heightens this sense of direct divine action, the more the entire ritual — and indeed the *kohen* himself — becomes merely an instrument of God's purpose.

### Human & Divine Agents

This leads to the final framing point. Half of the *midah k'neged midah* is accomplished through human agents — the *kohen* performing the ritual — while the other half is accomplished through a miracle, the water's effect on the guilty woman. When the Gemara later extends *midah k'neged midah* to divine punishments in the broader world — a man falling off a roof, being bitten by a snake — it is entirely God's doing, not the result of any human being consciously imposing punishment. By heightening the sense that God is acting directly, the *kohen* and the entire ritual are reconceived as agents and vehicles through which God operates. This reframing also carries moral weight: it becomes easier to perform this ritual — which occurs before guilt is established — when one understands the *kohen* not as an autonomous agent of punishment but as an instrument of divine justice.

## Mishna Examples

### Mishna Opening

The *mishna* opens with the general formulation: *bemida she'adam moded kach modedin lo* — in the measure that a person measures out, so it is measured out for him. It then proceeds to apply this principle specifically to the *sotah*.

### Beauty Reversed

*Hi kishta et atzma la'avera, HaMakom nivla* — she beautified herself for sin, God disfigured her. The disfigurement, as will become clear, operates through the *kohen* as God's agent.

### Revelation

*Hi gilta et atzma la'avera, HaMakom gila aleha* — she revealed herself for sin, God revealed her. Here too the *kohen*, through the rending of her garments, is the vehicle through which God's exposure of her takes place.

### Thigh & Belly

*Beyerech hitchila ba'aveira techila ve'achar kach habeten* — she began her sin with the thigh and then the belly. The term *beten* here may serve, depending on context, as a euphemism for the sexual organ, though it may also refer more literally to the physical position of the man's body weight. This will be examined further in the Gemara. The *mishna* states that therefore the punishment begins with the thigh — *tilakeh hayerech techilah ve'achar kach habeten* — as the verse states: *veyiten Hashem es yerecheich nofeles ve'es bitneich tzavah*.

### Rest of Body

*Veshar kol haguf lo palat* — the rest of the body will not escape punishment either. The *mishna* thus assures that what begins with the thigh and belly will extend to every limb. This is a compact and well-structured presentation: the beginning of the ritual, its theological underpinning, and its ultimate scope are all packaged together. Notably, however, this neat packaging remains inseparable from the assumption of guilt that pervades the entire discussion.

## Gemara Measure

### Rav Yosef

*Amar Rav Yosef: af al gav d'midah batlah, b'midah lo batlah.* Even though formal court-imposed punishments are no longer operative — now that there is no longer a *beis din* with jurisdiction over capital cases, and *dinei nefashos* no longer function — the principle of *midah k'neged midah* has not ceased. God continues to ensure that fitting punishment is meted out. The heavenly dimension of justice persists even when its earthly counterpart has lapsed.

### Four Executions

Rav Yosef elaborates, citing also a teaching of Rabbi Chiya: *miyom shecharav Beis HaMikdash af al pi shebatlah Sanhedrin, arba misos lo batlu* — from the day the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed, even though the *Sanhedrin* ceased to function, the four modes of execution did not cease. The Gemara immediately challenges this: surely they did cease? The resolution: the court-imposed executions ceased, but *din arba misos lo batlu* — the divine justice corresponding to those four modes continues. As Rashi explains, this refers to *din shamayim she'hidugmato* — the heavenly justice that mirrors each of the four modes.

### Divine Punishments

The Gemara proceeds to enumerate the divine analogs to each of the four modes of execution. One who would have been liable to *sekilah* — stoning — will either fall from a roof or be trampled by an animal. Rashi notes that stoning involved pushing the condemned from a height before placing a stone upon him, and that when an animal attacks, it similarly knocks the person to the ground. One deserving *sereifah* — burning, which in *beis din* involved pouring molten lead down the throat — will either be consumed in a fire or bitten by a snake, whose venom burns like fire. One deserving *harigah* — execution by sword — will either be handed over to the Roman authorities, who executed by sword, or attacked by bandits armed with swords. And one deserving *chanikah* — strangulation — will either drown in a river, dying by asphyxiation, or die of a choking respiratory death. One participant noted the historical observation that Roman crucifixion itself killed through asphyxiation — a striking parallel to *chanikah*.

### Scriptural Source

The Gemara then cites Rabbi's derivation of the scriptural source for *midah k'neged midah*: *b'se'ase'ah b'shalchah terivenah* — in its measure, in its sending, it will be smitten. The word *se'ase'ah* is understood as pointing to the *se'ah*, a large measure, and thus seems to apply the principle only to significant sins that merit divine intervention. This raises the question: what about smaller sins?

### Small Measures

*Ein li ela se'ah* — I might only know a large measure. What of smaller measures — a *kav*, a quarter-*kav*, a *rova*, or even smaller fractions? The Gemara expands the principle by reading *ki kol seon soen bera'ash* as "every measure" — even the smallest — is counted and paid back. And the principle that *kol pruta upruta mitztarefet lecheshbon gadol* — every tiny infraction accumulates into a large accounting — is derived from the verse in Kohelet: *achat le'achat limtzo cheshbon*, one to one until a significant reckoning is reached.

This accumulation principle carries a dual implication. On one hand, nothing escapes notice; even the smallest sin is recorded. On the other hand, the punishment may not come immediately for each small act — rather, everything is collected until the sum demands a response.

*Vechen matzinu besotah* — and we similarly found by the *sotah*, that in the measure she measured out, it was measured out against her. It is worth noting the plural formulation *madedu lah* — "they measured out for her." This is fitting: the *sotah*'s punishment is administered through multiple agents — the *kohen*, the heavenly court — working together, evoking that language of *na'aseh adam*, as if a plurality of actors is involved.

## Detailed Sotah Examp (II)

### Doorway Exposure

*Hi amdah al petach beitah leraot lo* — she stood by the doorway of her house to be visible to the man, to beckon him or to be seen when he arrived. Therefore *velifichach kohen maamidah al shaar nikanor umareh klonah lakol* — the *kohen* makes a public spectacle of her at the gate of Nikanor, displaying her shame to all. Again, this occurs before guilt is established.

### Adornment Reversed

*Hi parsah lo sudarin naim al roshah* — she spread beautiful silks over her head. Therefore the *kohen* removes her head covering and places it beneath her feet, disheveling her hair. *Hi kachalah lo paneiha* — she made up her face. Therefore, as we reach daf 9a, *paneiha morikot* — her face turns jaundiced. *Hi kachalah lo eineiha* — she applied eye makeup. Therefore *eineiha boltos* — her eyes bulge outward. *Hi kelaah lo et searah* — she braided her hair to attract him. Therefore the *kohen soter et searah* — not merely removing the hair covering but actively disheveling the hair, unbraiding whatever had been braided.

### Beckoning

*Hi heratah lo be'etzba* — she beckoned him with her finger. Therefore *tziporneiha noshrot* — her fingernails fall out. This is the first mention of this detail, and it corresponds to the *mishna*'s general assurance that *shear kol haguf lo palat* — no part of the body escapes punishment.

### Garment Binding

*Hi chagraah lo betziltzul* — she wore a fine decorated belt. Therefore the *kohen mevi chevel mitzri vekosher lah lemalah midadeiha* — the *kohen* brings an Egyptian rope and ties it above her breasts to hold her garment up. The contrast is pointed: the refined belt exchanged for a crude utilitarian rope.

### Physical Acts

*Hi pashtah lo et yerechah* — she spread open her thighs for him. Therefore *yerechah nofelet* — her thighs collapse. *Hi kiblatu al kersah* — she received him upon her belly. The word *kersah* here may refer to his physical weight resting on her, or depending on the precise act described, may refer to penetration itself; the latter reading is reinforced by the corresponding punishment — *bitnah tzavah*, her belly swells from within.

### Food & Drink

*Hi heechilat'hu maadanei olam* — she fed him the delicacies of the world. Therefore *korbanah maachal behemah* — her *korban* is made of barley, animal fodder. *Hi hishkata oto yayin meshubach bekosot meshubachin* — she gave him fine wine in beautiful goblets. Therefore *kohen mashke mayim hamarim bekeda shel cheres* — the *kohen* makes her drink bitter water from a crude earthenware shard.

## Gender Issues

### Woman Only

At this point a crucial observation arises: the entire apparatus of *midah k'neged midah* is applied exclusively to the woman. What of the male adulterer?

### Patriarchy

Part of the answer lies in the structural realities of a patriarchal society. If a wife suspects her husband of committing adultery, what practical power does she have to act on that suspicion? The *sotah* procedure exists because the husband suspects his wife, and he is the one with legal standing to initiate it.

### Adultery Definition

There is also a deeper halakhic dimension. In the framework of *halacha*, adultery as a transgression is defined relative to a married *woman*, not a married *man*. In a polygamous framework, a husband sleeping with another woman is not, technically, a betrayal of his wife, because the prohibition of adultery concerns violating another man's wife — the married man is the injured party. This explains the entire dynamic: the husband initiates the *sotah* procedure because he is the injured party; there is no parallel mechanism directed against the male adulterer, whose victim would nominally be the other woman's husband, who himself lacks practical recourse.

### Victim Blaming

This structural explanation, however, does not resolve a more pointed moral concern, which needs to be explicitly named: all of the blame is being placed on the woman. The *midah k'neged midah* catalog — she wore this, she beckoned this way, she dressed thus — carries an implicit logic that she was inviting it, that her presentation was responsible for the outcome. This is the same logic that has historically been used to shift blame onto victims of assault or coercion.

### Mitigating Factors

The Gemara imagines this encounter in terms of deliberate romantic seduction — *she* fed him delicacies, *she* gave him fine wine. But as is pointed out, the reality of such situations can be far more complicated: coercion, economic pressure, or outright force may all be operative. The Gemara's stylized account, influenced by the imagery of *Mishlei* with its powerful archetype of the seductive woman (itself a metaphor), does not capture the full range of human situations that might have brought a woman to this point. This too requires acknowledgment alongside the legal and theological analysis.

## Detailed Sotah Examp (II)

### Secrecy to Exposure

*Hi asta baseter* — she performed her deeds in secret, in hiding, so that no one else would know. *Yoshev beseter elyon sam panim* — the One who dwells on high in concealment will turn His face toward her, attending to her case. Rashi understands *sam panim* as an expression of directing attention toward her in order to punish; but *panim* also carries the connotation of bringing something into the open — making it public.

The verse offered as support is: *ve'ein noef shamra neshef, lemor lo teshureni ayin* — the eye of the adulterer watches in the dark, saying, "No eye will see me." The sinner believes secrecy is assured; God will publicize the sin.

*Davar acher*: *Hi asta baseter, HaMakom pirsemah bagalui* — she did it in private; God publicized it openly. The verse: *techaseh sinah bemashaon, tigaleh raato bekahal* — one who conceals a hateful deed in darkness, his evil will be revealed in the community. Rashi interprets the prior formulation of *sam panim* more neutrally — God focused His attention — and reserves the theme of public exposure for this second formulation. Both formulations together explain why the *sotah* ritual is conducted as a public spectacle: the exposure before the nation is itself the *midah k'neged midah* for the secrecy of the sin.

The narrative then doubles back briefly, noting that the *korban* the *sotah* brings actually precedes the water in the sequence of the ritual, though the water has been discussed first. The Gemara packages the full *midah k'neged midah* together without strict attention to procedural chronology.

## National Punishment

### Nations Timing

At this juncture the Gemara addresses an apparent redundancy. We have derived *midah k'neged midah* from multiple verses; the verse *besasa'ah beshalchah terivenah* seemed to add only the principle of the *se'ah* — a large measure. But this was already subsumed under the broader principle. What does that verse contribute independently?

The answer: *ukiderabi chanina bar papa* — it teaches the principle of Rabbi Chanina bar Papa. *De'amar Rabbi Chanina bar Papa: ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu nifra min ha'umah ad she'at shiluchah* — God does not punish a nation until the moment of its expulsion or destruction. All of the punishment owed to a nation is held back and concentrated at the ultimate moment of that nation's destruction or exile. As Rashi notes, *shiluchah* here encompasses not only exile but destruction.

This principle addresses a pressing theological problem. The Gemara does not here raise explicitly the classic difficulty of *tzaddik vera lo, rasha vetov lo* — the righteous who suffer, the wicked who prosper — but the application to nations makes the challenge acute. A nation like Rome could be oppressive and evil for centuries while remaining successful and unchallenged. The response offered here is: their punishment is not being waived, only deferred. Everything accumulates toward the final *yom hadin* when God will bring it all down upon them at once.

### Egypt Three Cups

A challenge is immediately raised from a teaching of Rava. The three cups of *Paroh* mentioned in Yosef's interpretation of the *sar hamashkim*'s dream — *kos Paroh* repeated three times — are understood as a metaphor for Egypt being made to drink the cup of punishment. Why three times? Because Egypt received its punishment in three distinct historical periods: once at the time of Moshe, through the plagues and *Kriat Yam Suf*; once at the time of *Paroh Necho*, when Nevuchadnezzar conquered Egypt in the course of his broader campaigns; and once in the future, together with all the other nations at the time of *mashiach*.

This seems to contradict Rabbi Chanina bar Papa's principle: Egypt was punished in installments, not all at once at the moment of destruction.

### Nation Identity

Before resolving this, the Gemara raises a preliminary question: perhaps the Egyptians being punished in later periods are not the same people as those of Moshe's time? Nations change composition over centuries; perhaps the *se'ah* verse speaks to the historical *Mitzrim* who have long since been replaced by a different population.

The Gemara rejects this by citing the testimony of Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Manimin, a convert of Egyptian descent who was a student of Rabbi Akiva. Manimin described his own genealogical situation: he was a first-generation *ger Mitzri*, had married a first-generation *Mitzrit*, and planned to have his son marry a second-generation *Mitzrit* — so that his grandchildren, as third-generation *Mitzrim*, would be *mutar lavo bekahal*, permitted to marry natural-born Jews, in accordance with the rule *beyuchsin holech achar hapsul* — the lineage follows the more disqualified parent — requiring that both parents of each successive generation carry the *Mitzri* status. The fact that in the era of the *tannaim* one could still identify as a first-generation Egyptian *ger* in a meaningful genealogical sense demonstrates that the Egyptian nation retains a continuous identity traceable to the *Mitzrim* of the Torah.

### Nation Identity (continued)

The Gemara then offers a resolution to the contradiction with Rabbi Chanina bar Papa's principle. *Ela ee itmar hachi itmar* — the statement was not made about a nation but about a king. *Amar Rabbi Chinana bar Pappa: ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu nifra min hamelech ad sheshlucha* — God does not punish a *king* until he is deposed or removed from power.

The Gemara further records that *Amemar* transmitted Rabbi Chinana bar Papa's teaching about the *nation* (not the king) but grounded it in a different verse: *ki ani Hashem lo shanisi ve'atem benei Yaakov lo chilitem* — I, God, have not changed, and you, children of Yaakov, have not been destroyed. *Lo shanisi le'uma v'shanisi lah* — I never smote a nation twice; when I smite a nation, it is a complete annihilation. By contrast, *ve'atem benei Yaakov lo chilitem* — Israel was smitten and survived, because *chitzai kalin ve'hein einam kalin* — God's arrows are exhausted against them and yet they persist.

This framing does not reassert the claim that a nation's punishment is all stored until one single moment, since that version was already rejected by the case of Egypt. Here the emphasis is rather on the totality and finality of punishment when it does come to other nations, in contrast to Israel's ongoing survival through repeated national sufferings.

A parenthetical note: a long Tosafot and Rashi both address the *tosefta* that underlies the Manimin passage, where Rabbi Akiva reportedly corrected Manimin's assumption — *manim paita*, you are making a mistake — arguing that *Sancheriv ubilbel es ha'umos*, Sancheriv had scrambled the nations, so the people living in Egypt in his day were not the descendants of the biblical *Mitzrim* at all. It is striking that the Gemara here accepts Manimin's testimony and the continuity of Egyptian national identity, while elsewhere the Gemara appears to accept Rabbi Akiva's contrary position. The tension between these two treatments of national continuity is noted by the *rishonim*.

A technical question was also raised: what is the difference between *ela ee itmar hachi itmar* — the formulation used here to correct an *amora*'s statement — and *chisuri michsra hachi kasani*, which emends the text of a *tannaic* source? The answer is that *chisuri michsra* applies to *tannaic* sources, whereas here, since Rabbi Chanina bar Papa is an *amora*, the Gemara uses the less drastic formulation that the statement "was transmitted thus" — not that the text is deficient, but that the report of the statement was incomplete.

### Individual Measure

Returning now to the main theme: *amar Rabbi Chamnuna: ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu nifra min ha'adam ad shetimale se'ato* — God does not punish a person until his measure is filled. The *se'ah* is a large measure. Here the principle is applied not to a nation but to an individual, and the resulting picture is more nuanced than the *midah k'neged midah* framework might suggest.

Earlier the Gemara insisted that even the smallest infraction — a thimbleful, a single *pruta* — is counted and accumulated. Now the Gemara acknowledges that punishment may not come for each small act in isolation; rather, everything accumulates until the *se'ah* — the large measure — is filled, and only then does punishment arrive. The verse: *bimlot sifko yetzer lo* — only when his desires are fully satisfied, then will his anguish come.

This is an implicit acknowledgment of the same problem that opens *Berachot* — the difficulty of *tzaddik vera lo, rasha vetov lo*. The Gemara does not raise that problem explicitly here; it simply asserts *midah k'neged midah* while offering this qualification: a *rasha* who appears to flourish may simply not yet have filled his *se'ah*. One participant observed that this might even cast a new light on the *sotah* ritual: the *kinui v'stira* procedure — the warning and seclusion — could be seen as a kind of accumulated pattern that brings the *sotah*'s measure to fullness, which would reframe the presumption of guilt not as entirely arbitrary but as based on a pattern of behavior. That reading was noted as a possible insight, though the primary intent of the passage appears to be addressing the broader question of why *resha'im* appear to prosper.

## Tangential Aggada

### Temple Treasures

At this point the Gemara introduces an apparently tangential teaching, connected only by the fact that it comes from Rabbi Chanina bar Papa. *Darash Rabbi Chanina bar Papa: mah d'ichtiv ranenu tzaddikim ba'Hashem, layisharim nava tehila.* The verse says that to the righteous, praise is a *naveh* — dwelling place. On the reading *al tikrei nava tehila ela naveh tehila*, the verse is understood as saying that the righteous have a *naveh*, a domicile, which was not taken over by their enemies. This refers to Moshe and David, whose dwelling places for God — the *Mishkan* and the *Beis HaMikdash* respectively — were never captured by enemies and used against them.

Regarding David: the verse in *Eicha*, *tave'u va'aretz she'areha* — the gates sank into the earth — is understood as describing the golden gates of the *Beis HaMikdash* that were caused to sink into the ground before the enemy could seize them.

### Hidden Vessels

Regarding Moshe: the Gemara cites the principle that when the first *Beis HaMikdash* was built, the *Ohel Moed* — the Tabernacle — along with all its components, its beams, clasps, bars, pillars, and sockets, was hidden away. It was secreted so that it would not fall into enemy hands when the *Beis HaMikdash* was ultimately destroyed.

The Gemara asks: *heicha* — where exactly were these things hidden? *Amar Rav Chisda amar Avimi: tachat mechilot shel heichal* — beneath the tunnels that run underneath the *heichal*, the sanctuary building, on the *Har HaBayis*.

## Sotah & Desire

### Desire Lost

The Gemara returns to the *sotah* with a new *baraita*. *Natna eineha bemi she'eino ra'ui lah* — she set her eyes upon one who was not appropriate for her, namely, a man who was not her husband. The consequence: *mah shebiksha lo nitan lah u'mah shebeyada natlu heimena* — what she desired she did not receive, and what she already had — her marriage, her relationship with her husband — was taken from her. This formulation offers a different moral register than the *midah k'neged midah* framework. Rather than simply calibrating punishment to crime, it carries the message of *dayenu* — be content with what you have. Desire for the inappropriate ultimately destroys what was already in one's possession.

### Lust Principle

This principle is then generalized: *kol hanotenk einav bema she'eino shelo* — anyone who gives his eyes and lusts after something that is not his and is not fitting for him to desire — *mah shemevakesh ein notnin lo, uma she'beyado notlin heimenu* — he does not receive what he desires, and what he already has is taken from him. This pattern, it must be acknowledged, does not always hold empirically. Yet it captures a real dynamic that many people recognize: that the relentless pursuit of the inappropriate or unattainable often costs a person what they already possess.

### Scriptural Examples

The Gemara proceeds to trace this pattern through a series of biblical figures. *Vechen matzinu be'nachash ha'kadmoni* — and so we find in the case of the primordial serpent, who gave his eyes to something not fitting for him. The understanding of *chazal* here is that the serpent desired *Chava* sexually — she was not only married but a human being. *Ma shebikesh lo nitan lo* — what he desired was not given to him — and what he had was taken: presumably having previously stood upright, he was cursed to crawl on his belly. God had intended him to be the king of all animals, the most cunning; now he is cursed below all animals. He sought to kill Adam and take Chava; instead eternal enmity was placed between him and the woman and between his offspring and hers.

The pattern continues with a long list: *Kayin, Korach, Bilam, Doeg, Achitofel, Geichazi, Avshalom, Adoniahu, Yoav, and Haman*. In each case, the principle holds: they desired what was not theirs, they did not receive it, and they lost what they had. Rashi on Kayin understands the object of his inappropriate desire as sexual — Hevel's twin sister, in the tradition that each of the brothers was born with twin sisters. One might wonder about Kayin's motive differently — the Midrash describes Kayin and Hevel's dispute as concerning wealth, land, and even the location of the *Beis HaMikdash* — but Rashi reads the sin as desire for the other's woman. Regarding Korach, he desired the *kehunah*; Bilam desired wealth; Avshalom desired the *malchut* — kingship — and so on.

### Ra'ui Definition

The concept of *ra'ui* — what is fitting or appropriate to desire — is worth examining. The Gemara does not prohibit desire itself; to deny the basic human drive of wanting would be unrealistic. The concept of *ra'ui* captures both the sense of proportionality — a desire that is not a crazed, disproportionate lust — and the dimension of permissibility, since in many of these cases the desired object was one that was simply forbidden to the person in question.

### Desire vs. Acting

One participant astutely observed that the list above includes people who did not merely covet but actively acted on their desire — Avshalom, Bilam, and others pursued their goals with deliberate effort. The Gemara is thus describing not passive wishful thinking but the active pursuit of inappropriate objects of desire. At the same time, it is worth noting the tension with another kind of story — the inspiring narrative of a person who has an apparently impossible dream and, precisely by fixing their gaze on it and taking step after step, ultimately realizes it. The Gemara would presumably respond that such cases involve *ra'ui lahem* — desires that are fitting, however seemingly distant. The classification of what counts as *ra'ui* versus *eino ra'ui* is where the moral weight falls, and that determination is not always straightforward.

## Order of Afflictions

### Order Question

The Gemara now takes up a technical question about the sequence of the *sotah*'s afflictions. The *mishna* states that the thigh is punished first and the belly second, because she began her sin with the thigh. But is this actually what the verses say?

### Abaye Solution

*Amar Abaye*: when the *kohen* pronounces the curse, he curses the thigh first and then the belly, following the order of the sin. But when the waters actually do their work in the body, they proceed *ki orchaihu badkei* — in accordance with the body's natural path — first the belly and then the thigh.

### Verse Challenge

The Gemara immediately challenges this. Even within the curse, does the verse not say *latzbot beten velanpil yerech* — to bloat the belly and cause the thigh to fall — i.e., the belly before the thigh?

### Kohen's Description

The resolution: *hahu demoda lah kohen debeten bereisha vehadar yerech* — that verse is the *kohen* describing to the woman what the water will do inside her body, in the order that the water will naturally proceed. He does this *shelo lehotzi la'az al mayim hamarerim* — so that no aspersion will be cast upon the bitter waters. If the *kohen* had described the effects in the wrong order and the water then affected the belly before the thigh, people might question the reliability of the waters. So when the *kohen* tells her what will happen, he states it in the physiological order — belly first, then thigh. But his actual curse is thigh first, then belly, as *midah k'neged midah* requires.

Rashi adds that the initial curse of the thigh — even before the water has taken effect — is itself part of the *midah k'neged midah*: the thigh is singled out first in the curse as a direct parallel to its role in the sin.

## Good Rewarded

### Miriam

The *mishna* now extends the *midah k'neged midah* principle from punishment to reward: *vechen lemidah tovah*. The first example is Miriam. She stood watch over the infant Moshe for a brief hour — *vatitatzav achoto merachok*, her sister stood at a distance. Therefore, when Miriam was struck with *tzaraat* and quarantined outside the camp, *vehaam lo nasa ad he'asof Miriam* — the entire nation waited seven days before traveling, until Miriam was brought back in. One hour of watching yielded seven days of national waiting.

### Yosef

*Yosef zachah likvor et aviv* — Yosef merited to bury his father, bringing him from Egypt back to Canaan. And *ein be'achav gadol mimenu* — there was none greater than him among his brothers. The verse: *vaya'al Yosef likvor et aviv, vaya'alu imo gam rechev gam parashim* — Yosef went up to bury his father, with chariots and horsemen accompanying him. Therefore, *mi lanu gadol miYosef shelo nitaseik bo ela Moshe* — who was greater than Yosef such that only Moshe would take care of his burial? Moshe — the greatest person of his generation — took charge of the bones of Yosef.

### Moshe

*Moshe zachah be'atzmot Yosef, ve'ein beYisrael gadol mimenu* — Moshe merited to carry the bones of Yosef, and there was none greater than Moshe in Israel. The verse: *vayikach Moshe et atzmot Yosef imo*. Therefore, *mi gadol miMoshe shelo nitaseik bo ela HaMakom* — who was greater than Moshe such that only God took care of his burial? The verse: *vayikbor oto bagai*. And *lo al Moshe bilvad amru ela al kol hatzadikim* — this is not said only of Moshe; all the righteous are buried by God. The verse: *vehalach lefanecha tzidkecha, kevod Hashem ya'asfecha* — the glory of God will gather you in at the time of death.

### Good Exceeds Bad

The chain — Miriam's one hour yielding seven days; Yosef caring for his father being paid back by Moshe; Moshe caring for Yosef being paid back by God — reflects not merely parallel *midah k'neged midah* but an escalating one. In the case of Miriam, one hour becomes seven days. This resonates with the broader principle articulated repeatedly in the Gemara: *midat tovah merubah mimiddat pur'anut* — the measure of goodness that God repays exceeds the measure of punishment. Good is rewarded at a higher multiple than evil is punished.

## Shimshon Eyes

### Eyes Poked

The *mishna* continues with Shimshon. *Shimshon halach achar einav* — Shimshon went after his eyes. *Lefichach nikru Plishtim et einav* — therefore the Philistines pierced out his eyes. The verse: *vayochezu Plishtim vayenakru et einav*. The case of Shimshon continues the theme of desiring what is not appropriate — in this instance, non-Jewish women. But here it is explicitly a man pursuing his desires, and it is his eyes that become both the instrument of sin and the focus of punishment.

### Free Choice

The Gemara challenges: *vehatichtiv ve'aviv ve'imo lo yadu ki meHashem hu* — but the verse states that his father and mother did not know that this was from God! Shimshon's marriage to the Timnite woman was directed by divine providence as a means through which God would act against the Philistines. If his behavior was all part of God's plan, in what sense was he punished for it?

The Gemara responds: *ki azal miha batar yashruta azal* — in going, he was following his own desires. The fact that God simultaneously operated through those same events does not relieve Shimshon of responsibility for his own motivations and choices. This is a fundamental statement about *bechirah chofshit* — free choice. A parallel challenge arises from the case of the Egyptians, whom God had decreed would enslave Israel: why were they punished? One answer is that they were pursuing their own desires and choices, not consciously fulfilling divine decree. God's plan operating through human events does not erase those humans' moral agency or the accountability that flows from it. One participant observed a counterpoint in Yosef's words to his brothers — where the same principle of divine operation through human events is invoked in a way that mitigates the brothers' guilt. The Gemara does not resolve that tension here; it is content to apply the principle of free choice to Shimshon.

### Gaza Sin

A *baraita* records: *Rebbe omer: techilas kilkulo b'Aza* — Rabbi taught: Shimshon's first sin was in Gaza. *Lefichach laka b'Aza* — therefore he was smitten in Gaza. The verse: *vayelech Shimshon Azasa vayar sham isha zona* — Shimshon went to Gaza and saw there a prostitute. And the verse recording his punishment: *vayoridu oso Azasa* — they brought him down to Gaza in chains.

### Timnah Question

The Gemara challenges: but the verse records that *vayered Shimshon Timnasa* — Shimshon descended to Timnah and took a woman there, and this incident preceded the episode with the prostitute in Gaza. So how can Rabbi say that Gaza was the beginning of his sin?

### Real Sin Gaza

The Gemara resolves: *techilas kilkulo miha b'Aza* — yes, Timnah came first, but that was a marriage. Rashi explains that the woman in Timnah, even though she was a non-Jew, was at least a woman Shimshon married. The real beginning of his moral corruption was in Gaza, where he went to a prostitute. Thus the starting point of his *kilkul* — his moral undoing — was specifically the episode of sexual licentiousness in Gaza, and that is why it is there that his punishment was ultimately administered. This introduces a moral distinction between sleeping with a non-Jewish woman in the framework of marriage versus visiting a prostitute — the latter being the more severe infraction that marks the true beginning of his downfall.

## Shimshon Delilah

### Delilah Name

The *mishna* continues: *vayehi acharei chen vaye'ehav isha b'nachal Sorek, u'shma Delilah.* A *baraita* records Rabbi's observation: *ilmalei nikra shma Delilah, re'uya haysa shetikarei Delilah* — even if her name had not been Delilah, she was worthy of being called Delilah. The name captures her essence: *dildela es kocho* — she drained his strength; *dildela es libo* — she drained his heart; *dildela es ma'asav* — she diminished his deeds, causing the *Shechina* to depart from him.

Each element is grounded in a verse. *Dildela es kocho*: *vayasar kocho me'alav* — his strength was removed from him. *Dildela es libo*: *vatere Delilah ki higid lah es kol libo* — Delilah saw that he had disclosed his entire heart to her. *Dildela es ma'asav d'istalek Shechina minei*: *v'hu lo yada ki Hashem sar me'alav* — he did not know that God had departed from him.

### True Words

The Gemara now examines how Delilah knew that Shimshon had finally told her the truth about the source of his strength. He had lied to her multiple times previously, giving false explanations, each of which she tested by summoning the Philistines while he slept — and each time he woke and overpowered them. When he finally revealed the secret of his hair, how did she know it was not another lie?

*Amar Rabbi Chanin amar Rav: nikarin divrei emes* — true words can be recognized. These three words carry great force. Perhaps the story hung together, the evidence corroborated it — Rashi notes that she could see he did not drink wine and could observe his hair — and the truth manifested in a way that distinguished it from his prior fabrications.

### Tzaddik Nature

Abaye offers a different explanation: *yad'a b'oso tzaddik d'lo mafik shem shamayim l'vatala* — she knew this *tzaddik* would never utter God's name in vain. Notice the remarkable characterization: despite everything we have heard about Shimshon's sins, Abaye calls him a *tzaddik*. When Shimshon finally said *nezir elohim ani* — I am a *nazir* of God — invoking God's name in his declaration, she recognized that this was a man who would not say such a thing falsely. *Amra hashta vadai kushta ka'amar* — now, she said, he must surely be telling the truth.

### Pressure

*Vayehi ki hitzika lo bidvareha kol hayamim vate'altzehu* — and it was when she pressed him with her words all the days that she pressured him. *Mai vate'altzehu?* — what does "pressured" mean here? *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak d'vei Rabbi Ami: besha'at gmar bi'ah nishmeta mitachtav* — at the moment of achieving climax, she withdrew. She withheld intimacy at the critical moment, repeatedly, until he was finally broken and told her the truth.

## Nazirite Laws

### Angel Instruction

The Gemara concludes with a brief discussion about the instructions given by the *malach* to the mother of Shimshon, *eshet Manoach*: *ve'ata hishamri na al tishti yayin veshachar ve'al tochli kol tamei* — now be careful, do not drink wine or strong drink, and do not eat anything impure. The plain reading might suggest *tamei* refers to non-*kosher* food. But this raises an obvious difficulty: *vechi ad hashta devarim tme'im achla?* — had she been eating non-*kosher* food until now? What is the instruction adding?

### Nazirite Impurity

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak d'vei Rabbi Ami*: the instruction refers not to non-*kosher* foods in general but to *devarim ha'asurim benazir* — the things forbidden to a *nazir*. Since Shimshon was to be a *nazir* from birth, his mother was instructed to observe, during her pregnancy, the full range of *nazirite* restrictions — not only wine itself but grapes, grape juice, grape seeds, grape skins, and all the related prohibitions that we learned in *masechet Nazir*. The term *tamei* in this context means *tamei* from the perspective of a *nazir*, i.e., forbidden to a *nazir*, not ritually impure in the ordinary sense.
