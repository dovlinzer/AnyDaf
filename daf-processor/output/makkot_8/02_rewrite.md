# Makkot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Makkot Intro

### Structure & Size

Makkot is a small *maseches*, comprising only three *perakim*. This brevity is somewhat anomalous, since *masechtos* are generally organized according to the number of their chapters, and three places it at the low end. Nevertheless, its position in the order of *shas* is not arbitrary.

### Sanhedrin Link

Makkot is clearly a continuation of Sanhedrin. The general assumption — difficult as it is to formally prove — is that it originally existed as part of Sanhedrin itself, which would have then comprised some fourteen *perakim* in total. In some manuscript traditions, it does not appear immediately following Sanhedrin, which complicates the picture somewhat. What is clear is that Makkot opens with *eidim zomemim* precisely because it is picking up from the *mishna* in Sanhedrin that discussed *zomemei bas kohen uvo'alah* — a *mishna* that, as Rashi notes on the first *daf* of Makkot, was the final *mishna* of the second-to-last *perek* of Sanhedrin. This provides further evidence that *Chelek* is not, as we might assume from our printed editions, the last chapter of Sanhedrin. The final *perek* of Sanhedrin dealt with *chenek*, the last of the *arba misos*, and concluded with the material about *zomemei bas kohen* — which then flows directly into Makkot's treatment of *eidim zomemim*. The order of *perakim* in our editions of Sanhedrin is, on this evidence, incorrect.

### Conceptual Move

Conceptually, what Makkot represents is a transition away from the *arba misos beis din* toward the other functions of *beis din* as a judicial institution. The first *perek* began with *eidim zomemim* but quickly broadened into procedural questions about *eidus* — what renders witnesses *pasul*, how separate groups of witnesses may be combined, whether witnesses must be able to see one another, and so on. It also dealt with questions such as whether a defendant who flees to a different *beis din* can have his case reopened, and the obligation to establish courts and adjudicate capital crimes whether in *Eretz Yisrael* or *Chutz La'aretz*. In this way, the end of the first *perek* served as a conclusion to the entire discussion of capital punishments, together with the procedural rules of testimony that apply specifically in capital cases.

### Galus & Lashes

The remainder of Makkot concerns itself with non-monetary, non-capital bodily punishments — specifically the two forms that *beis din* administers beyond execution. The second *perek*, which we are now studying, deals with *galus*, exile to the *arei miklat*. This institution is fascinating in its own right: the *Torah* seems to be deliberately channeling what might otherwise have become vigilante justice — the *go'el hadam* pursuing the killer — into a controlled judicial framework. The *galus* serves simultaneously as protection for the unintentional killer and as a form of *kappara* for one who did cause a death, however inadvertently. The third and final *perek* then deals with lashes, the other major non-monetary punishment administered by *beis din*.

## Exile Logic

### Lack of System

The *perek* of *Elu Hein Hagolin* presents a significant interpretive challenge: there does not seem to be a consistent underlying principle governing which cases of unintentional homicide result in exile and which do not. Already from the first *daf* of Makkot, the cases seem to resist systematization. One principle exempts a person who threw in one direction and the object went in the other — presumably because such a person is entirely blameless. Another principle exempts a person who tried to throw only partway toward the victim but the object covered the full distance. Yet this creates an obvious difficulty: if you're exempt when it goes the wrong direction and also exempt when it goes further than intended toward the person, when exactly are you liable? Rashi suggests the classic case is when you intended to throw it in that direction and the victim happened to walk into the path — but there is a *mishna* that exempts that scenario as well. What is the underlying logic?

Even setting aside questions of negligence, there are cases that seem purely formalistic — as we are about to see with the chopping of wood, where the question of whether it is the axe head that flies off or a wood chip that flies off determines *galus* liability, and it is genuinely difficult to understand why one should be categorically different from the other. And more troubling still, cases that appear virtually identical are sometimes treated differently. The honest answer is that this *perek* operates with a very high degree of specificity that does not always resolve into a neat internal logic, and that does not always seem to be internally consistent.

### Yerida Letzorech

One concept introduced in the preceding *sugya*, which deserves mention here, is the doctrine of *yerida letzorech aliyah* — downward motion for the sake of upward motion. The examples the *Gemara* gives include pressing down on the rung of a ladder or a butcher bending down before pulling upward. The phrase itself has become a significant concept in *Chassidus* literature, applied to the spiritual experience of someone who, having achieved a level of religious seriousness, suddenly finds themselves falling into a *chet* they believed they had long surpassed, and is tempted to despair. The *Gemara*'s framework offers a reframe: the descent may itself be in service of a greater ascent.

### Rung Falls

What is interesting — and the *Gemara*'s treatment is not entirely consistent on this point — is how exactly to define which direction constitutes the relevant motion. Rashi says that if you are simply pressing your foot down onto a rung and the rung remains steady, that is not a *yerida* in the relevant sense, because the rung itself is not descending. But if the rung is loose and it pushes downward, then that qualifies as *yerida*. Tosafot notes, importantly, that the mechanism of the case is not that the person fell and struck someone below, but that the rung itself fell. So the question becomes whether the rung going down counts as *yerida* when the purpose of pressing on it was to climb upward. One could plausibly categorize this case many different ways, and the *Gemara* itself seems to shift in how it frames it by the end of the *sugya*.

## Axe Head Debate

### Mishna Setup

The *mishna* at the bottom of 7b presents the following case from the *Torah*'s paradigm of unintentional homicide: the verse in Devarim describes two people who go into the forest together to chop wood, and states *v'nashal habarzel min ha'etz* — the iron was let loose from the wood — and kills someone. The question turns on what "the wood" refers to. It can be read as the wooden handle of the axe, yielding the interpretation that the iron head of the axe flew off the handle and killed someone. Alternatively, it can be read as the wood being chopped, yielding the interpretation that a splinter flew off the wood that was being struck and killed someone.

This is precisely the debate between Rabbi and the *Chachamim*. *Nishmata habarzel mikito v'harag* — the iron head came loose from its handle and killed. Rabbi says *eino goleh*, he does not go into exile. The *Chachamim* say *goleh*, he does go into exile. Then: *Min ha'etz hamitpakeia* — from the wood that was being split — a splinter flew off and killed. Rabbi says *goleh*. The *Chachamim* say *eino goleh*.

The *Gemara* does not explicitly explain why each reading should produce a different *din*. One could reason that the flying axe head is more analogous to *metassek* — a completely incidental, unanticipated consequence entirely disconnected from the intended act of chopping. You reach back, the head flies off — there is no way in which your chopping motion caused this in any ordinary sense of causation. A splinter, by contrast, is the direct and predictable result of the blow. So perhaps Rabbi's position makes sense on a negligence framework: only when you were producing the thing that caused the death — the splinter — does the death count as traceable to your action in a way that warrants *galus*. The *Chachamim*'s position is harder to parse on this framework, unless they say the reverse — that the splinter represents too much negligence, or perhaps that the axe head represents a sufficiently predictable hazard. Alternatively, as the *Gemara* will go on to suggest, there may be a formal rather than negligence-based distinction at play, involving the concepts of *kocho* and *koach kocho*.

### Rabbi's Argument

A *brayta* records Rabbi's own argument to the *Chachamim*. He asks: does the verse say *v'nashal habarzel me'etzO* — from its handle? No. It says *min ha'etz* — from the wood. Moreover, he points out a *gezera shava* within the passage itself: the word *etz* appears both in the description of the scenario — they came into the forest *lachtov etzim*, to chop wood — and in the description of the accident — *min ha'etz*. *Ma etz ha'amur lemala min ha'etz hamitpakei'a* — just as the word *etz* in the opening of the scenario refers to the wood being chopped, so too the word *etz* in the description of the accident must refer to the wood being chopped. This is a strong contextual argument.

### Rav's Answer

The *Gemara* then asks a pointed question. Rav states that both Rabbi and the *Chachamim* are actually deriving their respective positions from the very same verse — *v'nashal habarzel min ha'etz*. This seems obvious, and the *Gemara* acknowledges it. The real question is the grammatical reading. Rabbi holds *yesh eim l'masoret*, that the written form of the word — *venishal*, in the transitive — governs. The transitive form means the iron *caused* the wood to fly off, i.e., a splinter was produced. The *Chachamim* hold *yesh eim lamikra*, that the traditional oral reading — *venashal*, the intransitive — governs, meaning the iron itself flew off.

But the *Gemara* immediately objects: Rabbi is generally understood to hold *yesh eim lamikra* — indeed, there is a *brayta* in the name of Rabbi Yochanan listing Rabbi, Rabbi Yehudah ben Ro'ei, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Akiva as all holding *yesh eim lamikra*. So how can Rabbi suddenly invoke *yesh eim l'masoret* here?

The *Gemara* resolves this: *hainu dekamar lehu ve'od* — that is precisely why Rabbi added the *ve'od* argument to his contextual proof. Since the context makes clear that *etz* in this passage means the wood being chopped, not the handle, and since the verse does not say *me'etzo* but *min ha'etz*, even according to the principle of *yesh eim lamikra* we are entitled to read the verb in the transitive form. Tosafot's explanation of how these two considerations combine is particularly instructive: given the weight of contextual evidence pointing toward "the wood being chopped," we are justified in invoking the written form's transitive reading — *venishal* — rather than the intransitive *venashal* that would normally dominate under *yesh eim lamikra*. The context licenses the departure from the default reading.

### Dirt & Dates

Rav Pappa extends the debate of Rabbi and the *Chachamim* to an analogous case: *man deshada pissa l'dikla* — someone who threw a clump of dirt at a palm tree, *v'azei tamrei v'katlo* — and as a result dates flew off and killed someone. This case falls under the same dispute. Why? Because structurally, it mirrors Rabbi's category: the person used one object — the clump of dirt — to make another object — the dates — fly off and cause death, without directly handling the lethal projectile. The *Gemara* notes that this might initially seem obvious, but it is not. The point is that the *Gemara* is not analyzing degree of negligence here — for on a negligence analysis, throwing things at trees really is not so different from chopping wood in terms of the hazard one should anticipate. Rather, the *Gemara* is examining formal similarity: in both cases, one object is used to propel another. This is underscored by the *Gemara*'s framing of the question, which we turn to next.

### Kocho Debate

The *Gemara* raises the possibility that the dirt-and-dates case might actually be *more* removed from the actor than Rabbi's case of the axe, and might therefore be exempt even according to Rabbi. In the axe case, you are holding the axe when the splinter flies off — the splinter is your *kocho*, one degree removed from you. In the dirt-and-dates case, you are not holding the clump of dirt when it strikes the tree; your energy transferred to the clump, and that is your *kocho*, and then from the clump to the dates is *koach kocho* — two degrees removed. Perhaps even Rabbi would agree that *koach kocho* is too attenuated to create liability. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that this is not the case — the clump of dirt you throw is treated analogously to the axe you are swinging, and the dates are in the same position as the splinter.

The *Gemara* then asks what would constitute *koach kocho* according to Rabbi, since the dirt-and-dates case does not reach that threshold. The answer is a Rube Goldberg-style case: you throw a clump and it hits a branch, the branch hits a cluster of dates, the dates fall and kill someone — that third degree of removal would be *koach kocho* in the relevant sense, and everyone would agree you are exempt. The implication for understanding the dispute is important: one way to frame why the *Chachamim* exempt in Rabbi's axe-splinter case is that they consider the splinter to be one degree removed from you — not *kocho* in the relevant sense, but already *koach kocho*, since you were not holding the wood, only the axe, and the splinter came from the wood. This would explain the formal distinction the *Chachamim* draw. It does not, however, explain why Rabbi would exempt when the axe head flies off, so the formal framework only takes us partway.

## Public Domain

### Mishna Stones

The following *mishna* presents the case of *hazorek even l'reshus harabbim v'harag* — one who throws stones into the public domain and thereby causes a death. The *mishna* rules *harei ze goleh*, he goes into exile — even though this might seem to represent a high degree of negligence, placing it closer to the *meizid* end of the spectrum. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds a qualification: *im misheyatza ha'even miyado hotzi halah es rosho v'kiblah, harei ze patur* — if after the stone left his hand the person who was struck walked into the stone's path, the thrower is exempt.

### Own Courtyard

The *mishna* then turns to a related case: throwing stones in one's own courtyard. Here the rule is that *im yesh reshus l'nizak likannes l'sham* — if the person who was killed had permission to be there, such as a guest, a postman, anyone invited or allowed onto the property — then *goleh*, exile applies. *V'im lav, eino goleh* — but if the person had no right to be there, the thrower is exempt. This rule is derived from the *brayta* which expounds the verse *v'asher yavo es re'eihu baya'ar* — one who goes with his friend into the forest: just as the forest is a place where both the injurer and the injured have equal permission to be, so the rule of *galus* applies only when both parties had a right to be in that location.

### Abba Shaul

Abba Shaul makes a different *drasha* from the same model case. *Ma chatavas etzim reshus, af kol reshus* — just as the chopping of wood in the *Torah*'s paradigm is a discretionary act, so the law of *galus* applies only to discretionary acts. *Yatza av hamakeh es bno v'rav harodeh es talmido* — this excludes cases where what a person is doing constitutes a *mitzvah* context: a father striking his son for educational purposes, a teacher striking a student. Also excluded is *shliach beis din*, the court officer who administers lashes, whose act is explicitly mandated. In these *mitzvah* contexts, if an accident results in death, there is no *galus* liability.

There is, however, another dimension to these exemptions worth noting. In the case of the father hitting his son or the teacher hitting his student, what is happening is a willful infliction of physical force on another person. One might have expected a different analysis altogether: even setting aside the *mitzvah* context, there is something conceptually distinct about an act that involves directly, physically striking someone, as opposed to all the other scenarios — the axe, the stone — where the harm is the unintended byproduct of some other activity. In a bar fight where someone accidentally strikes a fatal blow, for instance, the degree of intentional physical involvement is very different from a wood-chopping accident. Yet the *mishna*'s framing suggests that outside the *mitzvah* exemption, even such cases could fall under *galus* liability if the degree of negligence were in the right middle range — which raises its own interesting questions about the boundaries of the institution.

### Negligence Levels

The *Gemara* opens its analysis of the public domain case by asking how one can be *chayav* for throwing stones into *reshus harabbim* — isn't that simply *meizid*, fully intentional-grade negligence? The *Gemara* answers, citing Shmuel bar Yitzchak: the case involves someone knocking down his wall — a purposeful act of deconstruction, not random stone-throwing. Still, the *Gemara* asks, doesn't even that constitute excessive negligence?

A further refinement emerges: *besoteir koslo l'ashpah* — we are dealing with someone knocking debris into an adjacent space that is technically a *reshus harabbim* but functions as a dump — an abandoned yard where people do not ordinarily go. This is not quite the same as throwing into a busy thoroughfare.

The *Gemara* then poses the question precisely: *hai ashpah heichi dami* — what exactly is the case of this dump? If *ishkichi bei rabbim* — people regularly frequent it — then *poshea hu*, you are negligent and too blameworthy for *galus*. If *lo shkichi bei rabbim* — nobody goes there — then *onus*, you are entirely free of blame and too blameless for *galus*. This is the essential search for the middle ground: not *poshea* and not *onus*.

Rav Pappa resolves it: the case that falls precisely in the middle is *l'ashpah she'asuya lifanos bah balailah v'ein asuya lifanos bah bayom* — a dump that people use to relieve themselves at night, when it is too public during the day for such activity, but which is not ordinarily used during the day. The person was clearing debris during the daytime. Since the place is genuinely not frequented in the daytime, you are not *poshea*. But since you know people use it at night, that knowledge ought to be on your radar — and since occasionally, *v'ika d'mikrei v'yasiv*, someone does wander there during the day when they have no choice, this is precisely the combination that places you in neither the *poshea* nor the *onus* category. It is exactly the right middle position.

Tosafot notes an additional structural observation: in the *Torah*'s model case, both the *mazik* and the *nizak* are in the same domain together. Here, the person causing the harm is in his private domain while the harm occurs in the public domain. This formal distinction means the dump case is an extension of the *Torah*'s paradigm, not a direct application of it — though from the standpoint of the calibrated negligence analysis, it maps cleanly onto the same framework.

### Self-Placement

Returning to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's ruling that one is exempt when the person walked into the stone's path after it was released, the *Gemara* derives this from the word *u'matza* in the verse — *u'matza es re'eihu* — the axe head found his friend. The *Gemara* argues that *matza* implies the friend was already there; it connotes discovery of something pre-existing, not something that arrived subsequently. *Prat l'mamtzi es atzmo* — this excludes a case where the person made himself found, i.e., placed himself in the path of the danger.

The *Gemara* then questions whether *matza* consistently carries this meaning, citing a verse about redeeming a sold house: *u'matza kedei geulato* — he finds the means to redeem it. In that context, the *Gemara* understands *matza* to mean *now* he comes to have the funds, not that the funds were pre-existing. Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *hacha me-inyana de-kra v'hasam me-inyana de-kra* — each instance of *u'matza* is understood from its own context. In the redemption case, the surrounding verb *v'hisiga yado* — "his hand reaches" — connotes a newly achieved capacity, and so *u'matza* there means he newly acquires the funds. In the homicide case, the whole scenario is structured around a setting that was already in place — the forest, the friends going together — and so *u'matza* means the friend was already present in that setting when the act occurred.

The practical implication remains formalistic rather than fault-based: the thrower's negligence level is identical whether the victim was standing there when he threw or walked in a moment later. Yet the law distinguishes between the two based on the physical sequence of events. When the victim was present at the moment of the act, it is described as the actor doing it to the victim. When the victim walked in afterward, it is described as the victim placing himself in the situation — *mamtzi es atzmo*. This is not a question of comparative fault, but of how the legal system categorizes the event.

## Discretion vs Mitzvah

### Mitzvah Chopping

The *Gemara* raises a challenge against the assumption underlying Abba Shaul's *drasha* that the *Torah*'s model case of wood-chopping is discretionary. One of the *Rabbanans* asks Rava: *mimai de-vi-chritat etzim de-reshut* — how do we know that the verse is speaking about discretionary chopping? *Dilma bi-chritat etzim de-sukkah* — perhaps it is talking about chopping wood to build a *sukkah*, which would be a *mitzvah*. Or *bi-chritat etzim de-ma'aracha* — perhaps it refers to chopping wood for the temple altar. If so, maybe even in a *mitzvah* context the *Torah* mandates *galus*, and there is no exemption at all.

### Means Not Mitzvot

Rava responds: *keivan de-i matza katuv eino choteh* — since if the wood were already chopped, there would be no *mitzvah* to chop new wood for a *sukkah* or for the altar. The act of chopping is merely a *machshir mitzvah*, a means of obtaining necessary material, not an intrinsic *mitzvah* act. Therefore *lav mitzvah* — it falls into the discretionary category regardless of its ultimate purpose. Building a *sukkah* is a *mitzvah*; procuring the lumber is not. The *Torah*'s model case therefore remains one of discretionary activity.

### Ravina Challenge

Ravina then challenges Rava with a pointed *kushya*: if the dividing line between discretionary and *mitzvah* is whether the act is a means or an intrinsic *mitzvah*, then consider the cases of the father hitting his son and the teacher hitting his student — those too are merely means to an end. If the child is already behaving correctly, there is no *mitzvah* to strike him; the physical force is only a means of achieving education, *chinuch*. On Rava's logic, the means-to-a-*mitzvah* should fall in the discretionary category, not the *mitzvah* category. So why does the *mishna* exempt these cases as *mitzvah* acts?

### Intrinsic Mitzvah?

The *Gemara* at this point offers a troubling answer: *af al gav d'gamir, nami mitzvah* — even if the child is learning properly and doing everything right, it is still a *mitzvah* to give him some physical discipline. *Mitzvah ka-avid, d'ksiv yaseir bincha veyinichaka* — the verse in Mishlei states, "Chastise your son and he will give you rest" — implying an intrinsic positive value to parental discipline beyond mere instrumentality. By this reading, a father striking his son is not just a means but an inherently obligatory act with its own *mitzvah* status. This is, frankly, a deeply disquieting conclusion, and the *Gemara* itself registers the discomfort — or at least one should register it on the *Gemara*'s behalf.

### Retraction

Rava retracts. *Hadar amrah Rava, lav milsa hi* — that was not correct; abandon what I just said. It is not the case that striking a child is an intrinsic *mitzvah*. The problem then resurfaces: on what basis are the cases of the father, the teacher, and the *shliach beis din* excluded from the *Torah*'s paradigm?

Rava's revised position shifts the distinction from intrinsic *mitzvah* status to *necessity*. *Va'asher yavo es re'eihu baya'ar* — the verse says *va'asher yavo*, someone who *happens to come* into the forest, *d'ba'i ayil vd'ba'i lo ayil* — meaning one who had a choice whether to go or not. This is the defining feature: the act was not necessary. And a *mitzvah* context, even one where the act is merely a means, counts as a *mitzvah* category precisely because it involves acts that were necessary — that had to be done.

### Shabbat Parallel

The *Gemara* parallels this analysis with a *sugya* elsewhere concerning *Shabbat*. The verse in Shemot says "in plowing and in harvesting you shall rest." Rabbi Yishmael interprets this as referring to *Shabbat*, and reads it to mean: *ma charish reshus, af katzir reshus* — just as plowing is always discretionary (there is never a *mitzvah* to plow), so too it is only discretionary harvesting that is prohibited on *Shabbat*. But if the harvesting is a *mitzvah*, it is permitted. The paradigmatic case: *yatza katzir ha-omer shehu mitzvah* — the harvesting of grain for the *omer* offering is a *mitzvah* and therefore permitted on *Shabbat*, even though harvesting in general is prohibited.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, reads the same verse as referring to *shvi'is* rather than *Shabbat*, and draws the principle of *tosefes shvi'is* from it — that one must add time before and after the *shmitta* year. Under Rabbi Akiva's reading, the verse is not limiting the prohibition of plowing to discretionary contexts; it is instead extending it.

### Omer Pushback

A member of the *Rabbanans* challenges Rava using this parallel: *vemi decharisha direshut* — who told you plowing is discretionary? Perhaps the verse is referring to plowing for a *mitzvah* purpose — specifically, plowing to grow wheat for the *omer*, which would make it a *mitzvah* context. And yet the verse still prohibits it on *Shabbat* — meaning there is no exemption for *mitzvah* contexts, contrary to Rabbi Yishmael's position.

Rava's response is the same as before: *keivan de-im matza charush eino choreish* — if the field were already plowed, there would be no *mitzvah* to plow it again. Therefore plowing is never necessary; it is always discretionary in the relevant sense. The same logic applies as in the *galus* context.

Ravina then challenges once more, this time specifically within the *Shabbat* framework: you are saying the distinction between discretionary and *mitzvah* is whether the act is necessary. Consider the *omer* harvest: *matza katzur eino kotzeir* — if the grain were already cut, would there be a *mitzvah* to cut it again? Presumably not. So on Rava's logic, the *omer* harvest should also be classified as discretionary, not *mitzvah*. Yet we know it is a *mitzvah* — that is the whole basis for permitting it on *Shabbat*.

### Final Necessity

The final resolution is that the line between discretionary and *mitzvah* — in both the *galus* context and the *Shabbat* context — is not intrinsic *mitzvah* value versus means-to-a-*mitzvah*, but rather necessity versus non-necessity in context. The *omer* harvest is a necessary act in a way that plowing is not: it must be performed as an act of harvesting at the right time, and if not done cannot simply be substituted by pre-existing cut grain. Plowing, even for a *mitzvah* purpose, is not like that — if the field is already plowed, nothing is lost.

What emerges from the entire discussion is that the relevant distinction drawn from *va'asher yavo* — "one who happens to come," suggesting discretion — is not a mechanical inquiry into whether the act is intrinsically obligatory or merely a means. It is a question of contextual necessity. Acts that had to be done, that could not simply have been replaced by a pre-existing state of affairs, count as *mitzvah*-adjacent for purposes of these exemptions. Acts that were not necessary in this sense — even if done in service of a *mitzvah* — remain in the discretionary category. And crucially, striking a child, which the *Gemara* initially tried to classify as an intrinsic *mitzvah*, is better understood as a necessary act of *chinuch* in context — which is what places it in the exempt category, without requiring us to say that hitting a child for no reason constitutes a *mitzvah* in itself.
