# Bava Batra 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bikkurim without Land

### Review & Ger Status

Today's daf picks up in the middle of 81b, in the midst of a discussion about whether one brings *bikkurim* when one owns the tree but not the land. The position shared by Rabbi Yanai and the *Chachamim* is that they debate the cases of owning the land — when you have two trees or three trees — but agree that when one owns the tree without the land, one brings *bikkurim* but does not make the declaration.

Two important *Tosafos* from the previous daf bear reviewing. The first concerns why a special *pasuk* is needed to exclude *bikkurim* brought from *Chutz La'aretz*. *Tosafos* draws a crucial distinction between *mitzvos hateluyos ba'aretz* — agricultural commandments tied to the land — and *bikkurim*. The former, such as *tevel* and *terumos u'ma'asros*, concern the status of the fruit themselves: the land has *kedusha* and that *kedusha* affects the produce. But *bikkurim*, though it involves produce that grows from the land, is not about the status of the produce; it is a personal obligation — a *gavra* obligation, not a *cheftza* one. Therefore a special *pasuk* was required to confine that personal obligation to the Land of Israel.

The second *Tosafos* addresses the *ger* and whether he may bring *bikkurim* and make the declaration. Two problems confront the *ger*: the first is the ability to recite *ha'adamah asher nasata li* — "the land which You gave me" — when he has no ancestral portion in the land. The second, more general problem is his ability to pronounce the *Arami oved avi* declaration. *Tosafos* cites the well-known *Yerushalmi* discussion of whether a *ger* may say *Elokeinu v'Elokei avoseinu* — whether he participates in the national narrative of the Jewish people. The *Mishnah* in *Bikkurim* appears to deny him this, but the *Yerushalmi* sides with Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the *ger* does share in that national identity. This is consistent with the approach of Pesach, where a *ger* brings a *Korban Pesach* and enters the narrative of the nation — themes that figured prominently in Rambam's famous letter to *Ovadia HaGer*.

### Core Problem

This brings us to the core difficulty: if one cannot make the declaration because one cannot say *ha'adamah asher nasata li* — why bring *bikkurim* at all? What is the justification for bringing them?

### Safek Framework

The *Gemara*'s answer, though it reads the sources with some effort, is that the case involves genuine doubt (*safek*) as to whether one owns the land. Rabbi Meir is uncertain whether owning one tree confers land ownership; the *Rabbanan* are uncertain whether two trees do. Since there is a *safek* as to whether one is obligated to bring *bikkurim*, the *halacha* is that one brings — lest one be obligated — but does not make the declaration, which might be false.

### Chullin Ba'azarah

This generates an immediate difficulty: if there is doubt whether you own land and are obligated in *bikkurim*, how may you bring them at all? Perhaps you are not obligated, in which case you are bringing unsanctified produce into the *Beis Hamikdash* as though it were a *korban* — the problem of *chullin ba'azarah*. As *Tosafos* notes, *chullin ba'azarah* is not merely the presence of an ordinary item in the Temple precincts, like having food in one's pocket; it is the act of presenting unsanctified produce as though it were a sacred offering.

The *Gemara*'s answer is that one first sanctifies the produce for *bedek habayis*. *Tosafos* notes that this resolves the presentation issue — sanctified property dedicated to *bedek habayis* would indeed be brought into the *Beis Hamikdash* and presented in a manner similar to *bikkurim*. But this creates a further problem: if it has been dedicated to *bedek habayis*, how may the *kohen* eat from it? The *Gemara* therefore says: one sanctifies it and then redeems it, and gives it to the *kohen* thereafter. A remarkable number of legal mechanisms are being deployed here — sanctification, redemption, and presentation — all to manage the underlying *safek*.

### Trumot & Ma'asrot

The *Gemara* then raises the *terumos u'ma'asros* problem. *Bikkurim* is not obligated in *terumos u'ma'asros*. But if the produce is only doubtfully *bikkurim*, then on the possibility that it is not *bikkurim* at all, it remains ordinary produce — *tevel* — and the *kohen* who receives it would be eating untithed produce.

The *Gemara*'s answer is that one separates *terumos u'ma'asros* before bringing. But this requires working through each component. *Trumah gedolah* goes to the *kohen* — that is straightforward. *Ma'aser sheni* presents no difficulty either, since *ma'aser sheni* is eaten in Yerushalayim, which is precisely where the *bikkurim* are being brought, so it can be given to the *kohen* there. *Ma'aser ani*, for the years in which it applies, can similarly be given to the *kohen* if he is poor. What of *ma'aser rishon*, which belongs to a *Levi*? The *Gemara* resolves this by citing the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: *ma'aser rishon* may also be given to a *kohen*. The *Gemara* grounds this in the ten places in the Torah where *kohanim* are called *Hakohanim HaLeviim*, demonstrating that *kohanim*, who are themselves members of the tribe of Levi, may receive *ma'aser rishon* according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — and indeed *Tosafos* notes that this may be the Torah-level reading of the entitlement. Alternatively, this may reflect the post-*Ezra* rabbinic enactment: when the *Leviim* remained in Bavel rather than returning to Eretz Yisrael with Ezra, *Ezra* imposed a *knas* upon them, redirecting the *ma'aser* from the *Leviim* to the *kohanim*. Whether one reads it as the *d'oraisa* position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah or as a rabbinic enactment, the practical result is that the entire basket may be given to the *kohen*.

### Kriha Problem

With the *chullin ba'azarah* and *terumos u'ma'asros* concerns resolved, the *Gemara* raises the next question: *v'dilma bikkurim ninhu u'va'i keriah* — perhaps they actually are *bikkurim*, in which case they require the declaration. And if you do not make the declaration, have you fulfilled the *mitzvah*? The *Gemara* initially says that *keriah* is not *me'akev* — one is *yotzei* without making the declaration. But the *Gemara* then challenges this.

### Bilah Principle

The challenge comes from a well-known principle cited by Rabbi Zeira: *kol hara'uy l'bilah ein bilah me'akeves bo, v'she'eino ra'uy l'bilah bilah me'akeves bo* — whatever is fit to be mixed, the absence of actual mixing does not invalidate it; but whatever is not fit to be mixed, the absence of mixing does invalidate it. The context is a *minchah* offering. Up to sixty *esronim* of flour may be brought in a single vessel, since it is theoretically possible to mix that quantity with oil. But sixty-one *esronim* cannot be properly mixed in a single vessel — the *Rabbanan* understood that so large a quantity cannot achieve *blilah* — and therefore must be divided among multiple vessels. If it was brought undivided, it is invalid.

The underlying principle is fundamental: sometimes the law does not require that an action actually be performed — it requires only that the item possess the *capacity* for that action. The question *Tosafos* presses is: how does one ever know whether the law demands actual performance or mere capacity? The *Rashbam* provides an answer at least within the domain of *korbanot*: where the Torah states a requirement only once, the rule is that it is not *me'akev*, meaning only the capacity is needed; where the Torah repeats the requirement, actual performance is mandated — *ela im kein shana alav hakatuv le'akev*. If stated once, the Torah is describing a quality the object must have. If stated twice, it is prescribing an act that must be performed.

Applying this to *bikkurim*: if one is *fit* to make the *keriah*, not actually making it does not prevent *yetziah*. But if one is *not fit* to make the *keriah* — lacking the capacity — then the *keriah* is *me'akev*. In our case of doubtful *bikkurim*, the *Gemara* says one lacks the capacity: if they truly are *bikkurim*, the person is fully obligated in *keriah*, yet cannot say it. Therefore *keriah* is *me'akev* and one has not been *yotzei*.

This is complicated by the *Rashbam*'s observation that there are cases in *bikkurim* — such as the *ger* — where the *halacha* establishes from the outset that one brings without the declaration. The *ger* belongs to the category of *mevi v'eino korei* — a defined category of bringing without declaration. Our case is different: if one truly owns the land, one is fully obligated in *keriah*; if not, it is not *bikkurim* at all. There is no defined intermediate category where *bikkurim* applies but *keriah* does not. Thus if these produce items fall into the *bikkurim* category, they carry a full *keriah* obligation, and the inability to fulfill it renders *keriah me'akev*.

### Two-Person Solution

The *Gemara* therefore resolves the problem by invoking the ruling of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina: one arranges matters such that two separate people are involved in the stages of harvesting and bringing. The relevant verse — *velakachta uva'ta* — requires that the same person who takes (or harvests) also brings, for the *keriah* obligation to apply. If the act is split between two people, the obligation to make the declaration never attaches to the *bikkurim* from the outset, placing them in a category that is simply not *ra'uy l'keriah* at all.

The *Rashbam* understands this as covering two scenarios: either one harvests and sends the produce with an agent, or an agent harvests and dies en route so that someone else finishes the bringing. *Tosafos* reads it as one unified scenario: the *shaliach* takes the produce from the house (which constitutes the *lekichah*), but dies before completing the journey, and another person completes the bringing — thereby ensuring that no single person performed the entire act. There is also a separate debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafos* as to whether, if the *shaliach* is alive and you catch up with him and take the produce for the final stretch, that would suffice. In any event, the practical mechanism is that the bringing is divided between two parties, so the *keriah* obligation never applies.

### False Declaration

Having established all this elaborate machinery, the *Gemara* entertains one final suggestion: given that the declaration consists of *pesukim*, why not simply recite them? After all, one reads those same *pesukim* in a Torah reading — what harm is there in saying them here?

The *Gemara* rejects this on two grounds. The first is *michzi k'shikra* — it appears as a falsehood. The *Rashbam* notes that in the *Beis Hamikdash*, one is making a formal declaration to God: *"You gave me this land"* — and if the land is not truly one's own, this is a false statement before God. The *Rashbam* cites the verse *dover shkarim lo yikon l'neged einai* — one who speaks falsehood cannot stand before God. The distinction from simply reading those verses as Torah is precise: *k'korei baTorah* — reading them as part of a Torah portion — is no problem. But making them as a formal declaration in the *Beis Hamikdash*, where they may be false, is entirely different.

The second reason, offered by Rav Mesharsheya the son of Rav Chiya, is that making the full declaration will lead observers to conclude that these are fully-fledged *bikkurim* with no *safek*, and they will therefore fail to separate *terumos u'ma'asros* from similar produce. The one who brought them with the full declaration effectively signals to others that no *safek* exists, and the next person in the same situation will eat *tevel*. It is worth noting that both of these reasons — whether *michzi k'shikra* or the concern for *terumos u'ma'asros* — appear to be rabbinic in character, yet the *Gemara* earlier treated the inability to make the declaration as rendering the produce *eino ra'uy l'keriah* and thereby making *keriah me'akev*. That tension is somewhat pointed: the rabbinic prohibition on making the declaration should not, strictly speaking, render the produce ontologically unfit for *keriah* from the Torah's perspective. But that is where the *Gemara* leaves it.

To summarize the *bikkurim* discussion: the *Gemara* explains the ruling that one brings without a declaration not by positing a genuine intermediate status — *bikkurim* without *keriah* — but by framing the entire case as a *safek* of land ownership. The mechanisms required to bring under *safek* include sanctification and redemption to address *chullin ba'azarah*, separation of *terumos u'ma'asros* under the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah to handle the *tevel* concern, and a two-person division of the harvest-and-bring process to ensure the *keriah* obligation never attaches. It is a remarkable and intricate construction.

## Land Ownership Thresholds

### One Tree Rule

Turning back to the *halacha* of buying a single or small number of trees: when one sells Michael one or two trees, he owns the trees but not the land. What he does have, however, is the right to maintain his trees in someone else's land. The irony is that precisely because he has no land of his own, he has no property line that his tree can violate. When one owns land alongside one's tree, the seller can demand that overgrowth beyond the property line be trimmed — that is *yishveh*. But when one has no land, the entire tree sits on the seller's property by right, and the buyer is entitled to have it there — roots drawing nutrients from that land, branches shading it. Accordingly, the rule is *higdil lo yishveh*: even if the tree grows very large, the land-owner cannot compel its trimming.

### Trunk vs. Roots

Now the *Gemara* addresses what happens to things that grow off of that tree. What belongs to the tree-buyer and what reverts to the land-owner? The distinction drawn is between growth from the *geza* (trunk) and growth from the *shorashim* (roots). Anything growing from the trunk belongs to the tree-owner — he owns the tree, and this is an extension of it above ground. Anything that sprouts from the roots belongs to the land-owner, since those are underground extensions of the seller's property.

How exactly do we define *geza* versus *shorashim*? Rabbi Yochanan states: *kol she-ro'eh pnei chama* — anything that sees the face of the sun, that is, anything growing above ground, is considered part of the trunk and belongs to the buyer. *V'kol she-eino ro'eh pnei chama* — whatever does not see the sun, whatever is below ground, is from the roots and belongs to the land-owner. The criterion is not the anatomical distinction between trunk and root per se, but rather whether the growth is above or below ground. This addresses the reality that trunks can dip underground and roots can emerge above it.

### Land Shift Problem

The *Gemara* then raises a concern: *dilma mati ara sirton* — what if the land level shifts? If growth has been springing from the roots and peeking above ground, and then silt or earth deposits raise the ground level, what was once visibly connected to the original tree now appears to be a cluster of separate trees. The buyer, who has been tending these shoots, might then claim: *tlata zevinta li* — you sold me three trees; *v'is li ara* — and therefore I own the land between them. After all, the rule is that purchasing three trees conveys the land. If the ground level shifts so that what are actually root-shoots look like three independent trees, the buyer could fraudulently assert a land claim that was never part of the transaction.

### Pruning Solution

Rav Nachman provides the answer: *yikotz* — prune. The tree-owner is not permitted to allow root-shoots to grow to the point where they begin to resemble independent trees. When they reach a size at which they could be mistaken for separate trees, they must be cut back. Rabbi Yochanan agrees: *yikotz*. The mechanism of mandatory pruning prevents the root-growth from ever generating a false land claim.

## Palm Tree Exception

### Palm No Trunk

Rav Nachman introduces a further ruling: *naktinan dekel ein lo geza* — we hold that a palm tree does not convey trunk-growth rights to the buyer. Whatever might sprout from the side of a palm trunk does not belong to the person who purchased the palm tree.

### Total Loss Risk

The first explanation proposed — attributed to Rav Zevid in the *Gemara*'s initial formulation — is as follows: a palm tree, unlike other fruit trees, does not regrow if it is cut down. Other trees, even if the trunk is severed, will send up new growth from the stump. A palm tree, once destroyed, is gone entirely. Because of this, the buyer of a palm tree knows from the outset that his investment is existentially at risk; a calamity to the tree means total and irreversible loss. This awareness causes the buyer to mentally give up on long-term planning — *asuchei aschei datei* — he does not think of his ownership as a long-term investment. Since he does not project long-term, he is not mentally accounting for whatever might grow from the trunk over time; he is focused only on the dates the tree produces from the top. Therefore, when he acquires the palm tree, there is no implicit understanding that he acquires trunk-growth rights.

This is an unusual idea: the very risk of total loss, rather than simply the likelihood of it, causes the buyer to abandon any long-term expectation. Yet this is how the *Rashbam* reads the *Gemara* here.

### Two Trees Challenge

Rav Pappa challenges this reasoning from our *Mishnah*: when one purchases two trees, one does not acquire rights to replant after the trees die — ownership is only for the life of the tree. So the buyer of two trees is in a position not unlike the buyer of a palm: he too knows that when the tree dies, that is it, he cannot replant. And yet the *Mishnah* says that the buyer of two trees *does* receive whatever grows from the trunk. If the prospect of a total, irreversible loss causes *asichut da'at* — an abandonment of long-term expectation — for the palm buyer, why does it not do the same for the buyer of two trees, who similarly cannot replant?

### Five-Year Insurance

Rav Pappa answers his own challenge. The two-trees case in the *Mishnah* is where one buys the trees for a defined period — five years. This seemingly less permanent form of ownership is paradoxically more protective. If the trees die within the five-year term, the buyer has the right to replant, because the contract guaranteed him trees on that land for five years, not merely the life of those particular trees. The explicit duration in the contract thus functions as an insurance policy: the buyer knows with certainty that for five years he will have a productive tree there, whether it is the original or a replacement. This confidence in the long-term viability of the investment restores his long-term planning mindset, and therefore he does receive the trunk-growth rights.

The *Rashbam*'s explanation here is striking: if you own the trees outright without a fixed term, your ownership is actually more precarious — if the tree dies tomorrow, it is a total loss. But if you purchase for five years, even though you own the trees for less time in absolute terms, you know your rights are guaranteed for that period regardless of what happens to the individual trees. Therefore the five-year buyer thinks long-term and receives trunk-growth rights; the open-ended palm-buyer does not.

### Practical Botanics

The *Gemara* then presents what it calls the final and decisive answer — one already foreshadowed at the outset of the discussion: *lephi she'ein motzi geza* — palm trees practically do not produce lateral trunk-growth. The botanical reality of palms is that they grow from the crown, not the sides. Other trees regularly produce shoots and offshoots from their trunk and root zone, and so when one buys such a tree, the expectation that one will receive whatever grows from the trunk is built into the transaction. A palm tree, by contrast, simply does not grow that way. There is never any trunk-growth to speak of, and therefore the category never arises. In the rare and unexpected event that something does sprout from a palm's trunk, it was never part of the transaction because neither party anticipated it.

This final answer is distinct from Rav Zevid's psychological argument about risk and long-term planning. The first explanation turned on the buyer's mental state given the risk of total loss. The final answer turns on practical botanical reality: trunk-growth is not a feature of the palm, and therefore the sale of a palm never implicitly included it.

## Three Trees & Land

### Three Trees Scope

When one buys three trees, one acquires land. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: the buyer acquires *tachteihen u'veineihen u'chutza lahen kemelo ora vesalo* — the land beneath them, the land between them, and outside of them to the extent of the space needed for a picker with his basket.

*Tachteihen* — beneath the trees — means the footprint of the trees themselves, so that if a tree dies the owner may replant it on that spot, and indeed use that plot for any agricultural purpose. *Beineihen* — between them — refers to the area enclosed by the three trees: three trees define a copse, a bounded area, and the entire interior space including the area under the canopy belongs to the buyer. *Chutza lahen* — outside of them — extends, as the *Rashbam* explains, beyond even the canopy line: one needs space for a worker to stand outside the tree and reach up to pick. *Ora vesalo* — the picker with his basket — is the unit of measure for this outer strip: enough room for a person holding a basket to stand at the edge of the foliage and harvest.

### Access Challenge

Rav Elazar raises a sharp challenge. The grant of outer space rests on the claim that the buyer needs it to pick from the trees. But, asks Rav Elazar: *hashta derech ein lo* — right now, the buyer does not even have a path to the trees. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* concerns whether a seller conveys access when selling trees: Rabbi Akiva rules that the seller sells *b'ayin yafa* — generously — and therefore access is implicitly included; the *Chachamim* rule that the seller sells *b'ayin ra'ah* — restrictively — and access is not included. If we follow the *Chachamim*, the buyer has no path to his trees.

Rav Elazar therefore argues: the outer picking-space is far less essential than access. If the buyer does not receive even the right to reach his trees — which is a bare necessity — why should we assume he receives the right to stand outside them and pick? *Over v'salo yesh lo?* — he gets space for a picker and basket? *Derech ein lo* — he has no path.

### Two vs. Three Trees

Rabbi Zeira draws a significant inference from Rav Elazar's challenge: if Rav Elazar finds it problematic that the three-tree buyer lacks access, he must believe that the two-tree buyer *does* have access. And this is not paradoxical — it reflects the same logic we saw with the trimming rule. When one owns land alongside trees, there is a property line, and trees that cross it must be trimmed. When one has no land and merely owns trees embedded in another's property, there is no property line to violate. The buyer can say to the seller: *ba'ara didach qaymani* — my tree is in your land anyway; by selling me this tree, you implicitly gave me the right to be here, and being here includes the right of access. The irony is that having fewer rights in one domain — no land ownership — translates into broader rights in another: since the seller committed to letting my tree reside in his land, he committed to letting me access it. But once one owns three trees and thereby owns the land, one is confined to one's own property, and the seller has not implicitly granted a path through the remaining portions of his land.

### Rabbi Akiva Rule

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then asks Rava whether Rav Elazar's reasoning implies that he does not hold like his own *rebbe* Shmuel, who ruled *halacha k'Rabbi Akiva* — that the law follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds that a seller sells *b'ayin yafa*. If the *halacha* follows Rabbi Akiva, then the buyer does have access even when he owns three trees and therefore owns land — and if he has access, there is no difficulty with granting him the outer picking-space as well. Rav Elazar's challenge only works if he assumes the buyer lacks access, which would mean rejecting Rabbi Akiva.

### Overhang Rights

The response is that Rav Elazar could in principle hold like Shmuel and like Rabbi Akiva, since his challenge is directed at explaining the *Mishnah*, and the *Mishnah* itself need not reflect Rabbi Akiva's position. The evidence is internal to the *Mishnah*: the *Mishnah* states that if the trees grow beyond the property line, the land-owner may demand trimming — *higdilu yeshafeh*. If this were a Rabbi Akiva *Mishnah*, where the seller sells *b'ayin yafa*, one might have expected that Rabbi Akiva's generosity would extend to permitting the canopy to overhang the seller's field. The *Gemara* then refines this: Rabbi Akiva's principle of *b'ayin yafa* does not mean unlimited generosity. By a *bor* — a cistern or well — granting access causes no harm to the land, and so Rabbi Akiva would be generous. But by trees, the overhang does real damage: it shades the land below and interferes with cultivation. Even Rabbi Akiva concedes — as is explicitly stated elsewhere — that a tree which extends over a neighbor's field may be cut: *ha'ilan hanoteh l'toch s'deh chaveiro shekotzetz melo hamardei* — one may trim the overhang to the width of a plow-beam. Generosity in granting access does not entail tolerating damage. Therefore our *Mishnah* could still reflect Rabbi Akiva's position: access yes, overhang no.

The practical upshot, as the *Rashbam* presents it, is that the question of the outer picking-space hinges on the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*: according to Rabbi Akiva, the buyer has access and the outer space; according to the *Chachamim*, he has neither. This is supported by a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya that explicitly grants the buyer the space beneath, between, and outside the trees — consistent with the Rabbi Akiva position.

### Outer Space Rights

A final question is raised by Abaye to Rav Yosef: *chutza lahen kemelo oreh v'salo — mi zara?* — the space outside the trees to the width of a picker and his basket — who may plant it?

Even granting that the buyer has the right to stand there and harvest, the land itself may still belong to the seller. The right of access, on the Rabbi Akiva model, is a right of ingress and use — the seller grants the buyer the right to pass through or stand on his land for the purpose of reaching and harvesting the trees. But that right of use does not necessarily convey ownership of the soil, any more than a path through someone's field becomes the walker's land. Just as access is a right to use another's land without owning it, the right to station a picker in that outer strip might be merely a right to use that strip — not to plant it.

The *Gemara*'s response is: *hachizon zoreya et haderech* — the outer party, the seller, plants the path. This establishes that the land remains the seller's to cultivate. The buyer's right is one of use, not ownership. We will take up the full analysis of this conclusion in the next shiur.
