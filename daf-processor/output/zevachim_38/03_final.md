# Zevachim 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis Shammai vs Hillel

### Two vs One Corner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחַטָּאת שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Beit Shammai maintain that in the case of **a sin offering,** which requires four placements, at least **two placements** are necessary to facilitate atonement, whereas Beit Hillel hold that even in the case of a sin offering one placement suffices. **Rav Huna said: What is the reasoning** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai?**

We pick up several lines into 38a, in the middle of a discussion concerning the debate between Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai regarding the placement of blood on the corners of the *chatas*. The Torah requires placing the blood of the *chatas* on all four corners of the *mizbe'ach*, yet both schools agree that *be'dieved* fewer than four suffice. The dispute is over precisely how few: according to Beis Shammai, two corners suffice *be'dieved*, while according to Beis Hillel, even one suffices.

### Karnos Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קַרְנוֹת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ; אַרְבָּעָה לְמִצְוָה וּשְׁתַּיִם לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: The verse states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger, and put it upon the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:25). Subsequently, the verse states: "The priest shall take of its blood with his finger, and put it on the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:30), and an additional verse states: "The priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger, and put it on the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:34). As the minimum amount justifying the use of the plural form, i.e., in the word "corners," is two, one may conclude that **there are six** references to the corners of the altar **here. Four** of them are mentioned **for a mitzva,** meaning that the priest should place the blood on all four corners of the altar *ab initio*, **and** the other **two** are mentioned **to invalidate** the offering if he did not present the blood on at least two corners.

Beis Shammai derives this position from the word *karnos* — the plural form used in the relevant *pesukim*. Reading *karnos*, *karnos*, *karnos* across the relevant passages yields a combined count of six, which, divided appropriately, produces four *lechat'chilah* and two *be'dieved*.

### Hillel's Corner Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל: ״קַרְנַת״, ״קַרְנַת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע; שָׁלֹשׁ לְמִצְוָה, אַחַת לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning for the opinion of **Beit Hillel?** The Gemara answers: The matter should be understood according to the written consonantal text, i.e., the way in which the words are actually spelled. The word **"corners"** is written plene, i.e., with a *vav*, in one of the verses, which means that it must be read in the plural form. In the other two verses, **"corners"** and **"corners"** are written deficient, i.e., without a *vav*, in a way that can be vocalized in the singular form, i.e., as *karnat*. Therefore, **there are four** references to corners **here. Three** of these references are written **to** indicate the placements of blood are performed only as **a mitzva,** i.e., they are performed *ab initio*, and the remaining **one** is written **to** indicate that its absence **invalidates** the offering, i.e., the offering is not valid if the blood was not placed against at least one corner of the altar.

The more difficult derivation is Beis Hillel's. Establishing the *lechat'chilah* requirement of four corners is not the challenge, but arriving at one *be'dieved* from the counted corners has proven elusive, and several approaches have been attempted. The *gemara* now introduces an entirely new framework — a *baraita* that grounds Beis Hillel's position not in the corner-count at all, but in a different set of words in the *Torah* entirely.

## Vekiper Drasha

### Three Vekipers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא: ״וְכִפֶּר״ ״וְכִפֶּר״ ״וְכִפֶּר״ – מִפְּנֵי הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further analyzes the opinion of Beit Hillel that even in the case of a sin offering, if the priest placed the blood on the altar with one placement, he facilitated atonement. **And a *tanna* cites** proof **for this from here,** as it was taught in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the sin offering brought by a king: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:26), and with regard to the goat brought as a sin offering by an ordinary person: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:31), and with regard to the sheep brought as a sin offering by an ordinary person: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 4:35). The repetition of this term is **due to a logical inference.**

At the end of *Parshat Vayikra*, in the passages concerning individual *korbanos* — whether of the *nasi* or of a private individual — each passage describing the placement of blood on the corners of the *mizbe'ach* concludes with the word *vekiper*, "and it will atone for him." The *tanna* of the *baraita* learns Beis Hillel's position from the fact that this word is repeated three times: *vekiper*, *vekiper*, *vekiper*. But first, the *baraita* must establish why a *pasuk* is necessary at all — why we cannot simply derive the same conclusion through logic alone.

### Logical Sufficiency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: נֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים לְמַעְלָה; מָה מַתַּן דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִים לְמַטָּה – שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת, כִּיפֵּר; אַף דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִים לְמַעְלָה – שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת, כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As** one **might** have thought to say: **Could this not** be derived through **logical inference** as follows: It **is stated** that **blood** is placed **below** the red line, **and** it **is stated** that **blood** is placed **above** the red line. **Just as** with regard to the **blood concerning** which it **is stated** that it is placed **below** the red line, **when** the priest **placed** it on the altar **with one placement he facilitated atonement, so too,** with regard to the **blood concerning** which it **is stated** that it is placed **above** the red line, **when** the priest **placed** it on the altar **with one placement he facilitated atonement.**

The *baraita* introduces the principle of *midrash halacha* by first presenting a logical argument (*din*) that might have made the *pasuk* unnecessary. *Neemru damim lemata veneemru damim lemala* — the Torah speaks of blood placed on the lower half of the *mizbe'ach* (as with all other *korbanos* that are not a *chatas*) and of blood placed on the upper half of the *mizbe'ach* (as with the *chatas*). Now, we know from a separate source — *vedam zevachecha yishapech* — that for all other *korbanos* on the lower half of the *mizbe'ach*, one application of blood suffices. If so, one might have argued by analogy: just as one *matana* suffices for blood placed below, so too one *matana* suffices for blood placed above, in the case of the *chatas*. Why, then, does the Torah need to repeat *vekiper*?

### Inner vs Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים בִּפְנִים, וְנֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים בַּחוּץ; מָה דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִים בִּפְנִים – חִיסֵּר אַחַת מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם; אַף דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִין בַּחוּץ – חִיסֵּר אַחַת מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps, **go this way,** turning away from the previous explanation and toward this explanation: It **is stated** that **blood** is presented **on** the **inner** altar, i.e., with regard to a sin offering of the community or of the High Priest; the blood of these offerings is sprinkled on the incense altar that is inside the Sanctuary. **And** it **is stated** that **blood** is presented **on** the **external** altar, i.e., with regard to the sin offering of an ordinary person, which is offered on the outer altar that is in the courtyard. **Just as** with regard to the **blood concerning** which it **is stated** that it is presented **on** the **inner** altar, if the priest **omitted one of the placements he has done nothing,** i.e., the offering is not valid, **so too,** with regard to the **blood concerning** which it **is stated** that it is presented **on** the **external** altar, if the priest **omitted one of the placements he has done nothing.**

However, the *baraita* immediately counters this argument with an alternative logical path: *o kalach lederech zu* — one could equally well argue in the opposite direction. *Neemru damim bifnim veneemru damim bachutz* — the Torah also speaks of blood placed on the four corners of the inner *mizbe'ach*, in the case of the *chatas pnimit*, and it speaks of blood placed on the four corners of the outer *mizbe'ach*, in the case of the outer *chatas*. We accept, from the *mishna*, that by the inner *chatas* all four *matanos* are *me'akev* — if even one is missing, nothing has been accomplished. One might therefore analogize the outer *chatas* to the inner *chatas*: just as all four are *me'akev* on the inner *mizbe'ach*, so too all four should be *me'akev* on the outer *mizbe'ach*.

### Need for Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה. דָּנִין חוּץ מִחוּץ, וְאֵין דָּנִין חוּץ מִבִּפְנִים; אוֹ כַּלֵּךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ – דָּנִין חַטָּאת וְאַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת, מֵחַטָּאת וְאַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת; וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ חוּץ – שֶׁאֵין חַטָּאת וְאַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the two possibilities: **Let us see to which** of the two cases the blood of an animal sin offering is more **similar.** It can be claimed: We **derive** a *halakha* stated with regard to the **external** altar **from** a *halakha* stated with regard to the **external** altar, **but** we **do not derive** a *halakha* stated with regard to the **external** altar **from** a *halakha* stated with regard to the **inner** altar. **Or, go this way: We derive** a *halakha* stated with regard to **a sin offering** whose blood is to be placed on the **four corners** of the altar **from** a *halakha* stated with regard to **a sin offering** whose blood is to be placed on the **four corners** of the altar. **But** a sin offering consisting of a bird, **which is not a sin offering** whose blood is to be placed on the **four corners** of the altar, **cannot serve as proof** to the *halakha* concerning an animal sin offering, whose blood is to be placed on the four corners of the altar.

Since the logical arguments point in opposite directions, the *gemara* considers whether one analogy is stronger than the other. On one hand, one might prefer to compare the outer *chatas* to the other *korbanos* on the outer *mizbe'ach*, since they share the same location — *danim chutz michutz ve'ein danim chutz mibifnim*. On the other hand, one might prefer to compare the outer *chatas* to the inner *chatas*, since both involve a *chatas* with four corners — *danim chatas v'arba karnos michatas v'arba karnos* — whereas the other *korbanos* on the outer *mizbe'ach* involve neither a *chatas* nor four corners. Since each analogy has a compelling basis and the arguments are genuinely balanced, logic alone cannot resolve the question. Therefore, the *Torah* explicitly teaches through the repeated *vekiper*: even if not done perfectly, atonement will be achieved. The first *vekiper* teaches that atonement is achieved even if only three *matanos* were performed; the second, even if only two; the third, even if only one. Thus three *vekipers* successively reduce the requirement from four down to one, establishing that *be'dieved* a single *matana* suffices.

### Adda's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ! אָמַר רָבָא: בַּר אַדָּא מָרִי אַסְבְּרַהּ לִי – אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִפֶּר... וְנִסְלַח״ – זוֹ הִיא כַּפָּרָה זוֹ הִיא סְלִיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** each of these verses **is necessary for itself,** to teach that atonement is achieved for each of the sins through its respective sin offering. **Rava said: Bar Adda Mari explained** this matter **to me: The verse states** with regard to each of these sin offerings: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement…and he shall be forgiven"** (Leviticus 4:26, 31, 35). **This is atonement** and **this is forgiveness;** they are one and the same. Since it would have sufficed to say: "And he shall be forgiven," the superfluous mentions of the phrase: "And the priest shall make atonement," serve to teach that the priest facilitates atonement even if he has not performed all the placements.

The *gemara* raises an immediate difficulty: perhaps the word *vekiper* is needed simply for its plain meaning — to inform us that the *korban* achieves *kapara*. Why assume it is available for this *drasha* at all? Rav Adda bar Ahava resolves this by pointing out that the *pasuk* actually uses two words in tandem: *vekiper venislach* — "and it will atone, and he will be forgiven." Since *venislach* already communicates that atonement and forgiveness are achieved, the word *vekiper* is freed from its simple function and becomes available to teach the *be'dieved* leniency. With that established, three iterations of *vekiper* progressively reduce the requirement: from four to three, from three to two, and from two to one.

## Vekiper Alternatives

### Three Above One Below

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא ״וְכִפֶּר״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ לְמַעְלָה וְאַחַת לְמַטָּה, ״וְכִפֶּר״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן אֶלָּא שְׁתַּיִם לְמַטָּה וּשְׁתַּיִם לְמַעְלָה, ״וְכִפֶּר״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן לְמַעְלָה אֶלָּא לְמַטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But even if these phrases are superfluous, do they necessarily indicate that a priest who omitted placements has nevertheless facilitated atonement? Why not **say** that these phrases serve to teach that if the priest placed all the placements he facilitated atonement even if the placements were placed in the wrong place? And expound the verses as follows: **"And** the priest **shall make atonement," even if he placed** the blood on the altar with **only three** placements **above,** on the corners of the altar, **and one below,** on the lower portion of the altar; **"And** the priest **shall make atonement," even if he placed** the blood with **only two** placements **above and two below; "And** the priest **shall make atonement," even if he did not place** the blood **above** at all, **but** only **below.**

Having established that three *vekipers* teach a reduction in the number of *matanos*, the *gemara* asks whether those three *vekipers* might be teaching something different altogether — not a reduction in the number of corners, but a shift in the *location* of the blood on the *mizbe'ach*. Perhaps the first *vekiper* teaches that atonement is achieved even if only three applications were placed above and one below; the second, even if two were above and two below; and the third, even if none were above and all four below.

### Rejection: Eliminate Cor…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר יִצְחָק: אִם כֵּן, בִּיטַּלְתָּ תּוֹרַת אַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת. וְאִי רַחֲמָנָא אָמַר, לִיבַּטְלוּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Yitzḥak says: If so,** that the priest facilitates atonement even if he placed all the placements below, **you have abolished the requirement of four corners** that is stated with regard to the sin offering (see, e.g., Leviticus 4:34), as the four corners are on the upper portion of the altar. The Gemara is puzzled by Rav Adda bar Yitzḥak's claim: **But if the Merciful One states** that this is so, **let** this requirement **be abolished.**

This reading is rejected: if the three *vekipers* are used to permit placing all the blood below the *chut hasikra* (the red line dividing the *mizbe'ach*), then the requirement to place blood on the corners — which are above — is entirely eliminated. One might respond that if the *Torah* teaches this, so be it; perhaps there is an alternative path of placing blood below, just as there are other variations in blood service. However, Rava rejects this: three *vekipers* are structurally suited to reduce a fourfold requirement by one step at a time, arriving at the number one. If the endpoint were "all four below," four *vekipers* would be required, not three.

### All Below Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁצָּרִיךְ שָׁלֹשׁ? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ קְרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** As Rav Adda bar Yitzḥak's claim was rejected, **Rava said** there is a different reason why the previous claim cannot be accepted: **What is** the **item that requires three** repetitions of "and the priest shall make atonement," i.e., to what are these verses referring? **You must say** that **these are** the **corners** of the altar. The Torah must teach that the priest facilitates atonement even if he did not place the blood on three of the four corners. But in order for the verses to teach that all four placements can be placed below, the phrase "and the priest shall make atonement" would have to be written four times.

This logic clarifies the structure of the *gemara*'s argument: three *vekipers* drive the count from four to one. The alternative reading — which would have used those same three *vekipers* to arrive at "all four below, none above" — is therefore not numerically coherent with the available text.

### Need Four Vekipers

Rava's formulation makes this precise: *eizu drush she-tzarich shalosh havei omer elu karnos* — what type of *drasha* requires exactly three steps? The *karnos*, going from four down to one. If the intended conclusion were "everything below," four steps would be needed. Three *vekipers* cannot carry that weight.

### One Above Three Below

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: ״וְכִפֶּר״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן אֶלָּא אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְשָׁלֹשׁ לְמַטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: Even so, it is possible to interpret the three repetitions of the phrase "and the priest shall make atonement" as referring to the location of the placements, without abolishing the requirement of corners, as one can **say:** "And the priest shall make atonement," even though he placed the blood on the altar with only three placements above and one below; "And the priest shall make atonement," even though he placed the blood with only two placements above and two below; **"And** the priest **shall make atonement," even though he placed** the blood with **only one** placement **above and three below.**

The *gemara* then raises a third alternative: perhaps the three *vekipers* teach that atonement is achieved even with one application above and three below. This is the middle option that the *gemara* had apparently passed over in the initial discussion.

### No Split Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא מָצִינוּ דָּמִים שֶׁחֶצְיָין לְמַעְלָה וְחֶצְיָין לְמַטָּה. וְלָא?! וְהָתְנַן: הִזָּה מִמֶּנּוּ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְשֶׁבַע לְמַטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This entire line of inquiry can be rejected, as **we do not find** a case involving **blood, half of which** is placed **above** the red line **and half of which** is placed **below** it. One either places all the blood on the lower half of the altar, as in the case of most offerings, or all of it on the upper half of the altar, as in the case of sin offerings. The Gemara asks: **And** is there really **no** case of that sort? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Yoma* 53b): The High Priest took the blood of the bull into the Holy of Holies and **sprinkled from** the blood **one** time **upward and** then **seven** times **downward?** Apparently, part of the blood of an offering can be sprinkled upward, toward the upper part of the Ark Cover, and part can be placed downward, toward the lower part of the Ark Cover.

This too is rejected, on different grounds. The objection is not numerical but structural: *lo matzinu damim she-chetzyan lema'ala ve-chetzyan lemata* — we have never found a *korban* in which the blood placement is fundamentally divided between the upper and lower halves of the *mizbe'ach* as a halachic requirement. Such a split cannot be an acceptable endpoint for *matan damim*. This, the *gemara* explains, is why this option was not considered earlier: the *gemara* was looking for a landing point that is halachically coherent, and "one above and three below" is not, because it requires a constitutive division of blood between two zones of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Yom Kippur Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַצְלִיף. מַאי כְּמַצְלִיף? מַחְוֵי רַב יְהוּדָה: כִּמְנַגְּדָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: That is not a case where half the blood is sprinkled upward and half is sprinkled downward. Instead, that sprinkling was **like a *matzlif*.** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of **like a *matzlif*? Rav Yehuda demonstrated** with his hand; it means **like one who whips.** One who whips another does not strike repeatedly in one place but directs one lash beneath the other.

The *gemara* challenges this principle with an apparent counterexample: the *mishna* regarding *Yom Kippur* states that the *Kohen Gadol* sprinkles one application above and seven below when facing the *Aron*. If taken literally, this would seem to require blood in two distinct locations — one above and seven below — directly contradicting the principle just established.

### Matzlif Meaning

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that the phrase refers not to the physical location of the blood on the *Aron* itself, but to the direction of the flicking motion — *ke-matzlif*, like the motion of one who administers lashes. Rav Yehuda demonstrated this with his finger: the motion of the wrist determines whether a given sprinkling is counted as "above" or "below," but the blood is not being split between two physical locations on the *Aron*. The *Aron* is not functioning as a *mizbe'ach* here in the relevant sense.

### Taharo Sprinkling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִזָּה עַל טׇהֳרוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: And is there really no case of that sort? But didn't we learn in a mishna (*Yoma* 58b) about the Yom Kippur service: After the High Priest sprinkled blood on the four corners of the incense altar, **he sprinkled** blood **seven times on *tohoro* of the altar.**

The *gemara* continues with a related case: on *Yom Kippur*, after completing the service inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* and at the *paroches*, the *Kohen Gadol* proceeds to the *mizbe'ach ha-zahav* and sprinkles seven times on its *taharo*. The *gemara* asks what *taharo shel mizbe'ach* means. Drawing an analogy to the phrase *tiahra tiahra de-yoma* — the brightness or purity of the day, meaning midday, the midpoint of the day — one might understand *taharo shel mizbe'ach* as referring to the middle of the *mizbe'ach* wall. If so, those sprinklings are placed at a level distinct from the corners (which are above), and we would again have blood divided between two locations.

### One Place Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְּמִזְבֵּחַ – כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״טְהַר טִיהֲרָא דְּיוֹמָא״? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: לָא, אַגִּילּוּיֵיהּ; דִּכְתִיב: ״וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר״.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** referring **to** the **middle** of the side **of the altar, as people say: Clear noon [*tihara*],** that is the middle **of the day?** Accordingly, the root *tet*, *heh*, *reish* denotes the middle; in the one case, the middle of the day, and in the other case, halfway up the altar. Now, since the blood was sprinkled on the altar seven times, inevitably some of the blood landed above the midpoint and some of it landed below the midpoint. **Rava bar Sheila said: No,** that is not the meaning of *tohoro*. Rather, *tohoro* means **on the revealed** part, i.e., the top, of the altar, **as it is written: "Like the very clear [*latohar*] sky"** (Exodus 24:10). *Tohoro* is referring to the top of the altar when it has been revealed, after the ashes of the incense are cleared and the pure gold is visible.

Raba bar Shila rejects this interpretation. *Taharo* does not mean the middle of the wall but rather the exposed, uncovered part of the *mizbe'ach* — which is its roof. Since the corners are also on the roof, all the blood placements remain in one zone: above. The *pasuk* that compares it to the clarity of the heavens (*uke'etzem ha-shamayim la-tohar*) confirms this, as it denotes what is uppermost and open. Hence there is no division of blood between two physical regions of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Shirayim Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא שִׁירַיִם! שִׁירַיִם לָא מְעַכְּבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks again: Is there not a case where some of the blood is presented above the red line and some of it is presented below? **But there is** the **remainder** of the blood, which is poured on the base of the altar even in the case of a sin offering, the main blood of which is placed on the upper portion of the altar. The Gemara answers: The pouring of the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar **is not indispensable** for atonement. Blood that is indispensable for atonement is in no instance presented half above the red line and half below it.

The *gemara* raises a further potential counterexample: for any *korban*, after the *matanos* of blood on the *mizbe'ach*, the *shirayim* — the remainder of the blood — are poured at the *yesod*, the base of the *mizbe'ach*. This is clearly a different location from the corners or the roof. Does this not constitute a required division of blood in two places? The *gemara* answers: *shirayim lo ma'akvei* — the pouring of the *shirayim* is not *me'akev*. The entire discussion concerns what is *me'akev* in the blood service, and the *shirayim* fall outside that category.

### Pnimiyim Shirayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא שִׁירַיִם הַפְּנִימִים, דְּאִיכָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מְעַכְּבִי! בְּחַד מָקוֹם קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues this line of questioning: **But there is the remainder** of the blood **of the inner** sin offerings, the main blood of which is sprinkled on the inner altar. The remainder of the blood of these offerings is poured on the base of the external altar, and **there is one who says** that this pouring of the blood is **indispensable** for atonement. The Gemara explains: When we said that there is no blood, half of which is presented above and half below, **we said** this **with regard to one place,** i.e., the same altar. The blood of inner sin offerings is sprinkled on the inner altar, while the remainder of that blood is poured on the base of the external altar. There is no case of blood, half of which is presented above and half of which is presented below on the same altar.

A sharper version of this challenge involves the *shirayim pnimiyim*: after the four *matanos* on the *mizbe'ach ha-zahav* in the service of an inner *chatas*, there is an opinion that the remainder of the blood must be poured at the *yesod* of the outer *mizbe'ach*, and that this is *me'akev*. Here we have two blood placements that are *me'akev*, and they occur not merely in different locations on one *mizbe'ach* but on entirely separate *mizbeachos*.

### One Altar Principle

The *gemara* resolves this with a limiting principle: *bechad makom kamina* — the rule that blood placement cannot require two fundamentally separate locations applies to blood service on a single *mizbe'ach*. When blood must be placed on two distinct *mizbeachos*, each *mizbe'ach* has its own independent role, and there is no paradox. But within the *avodah* of blood on any one *mizbe'ach*, the requirement cannot be constitutively split between two separate locations on that *mizbe'ach*. This principle closes off the third alternative reading of *vekiper* and confirms that the only coherent landing point is: *be'dieved*, one *matana* on the corners suffices.

## Pigul Hemshek

### Matir and Mefugel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת, וְאַחַת שֶׁבְּכׇל הַזְּבָחִים – מַתִּירוֹת וּמְפַגְּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that according to the opinion of Beit Shammai, in the case of a sin offering two placements facilitate atonement, while with regard to other offerings a single placement is sufficient. Beit Hillel disagree and say that even in the case of a sin offering one placement suffices after the fact. With regard to this issue, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: Beit Shammai say** that **two placements in** the case of **a sin offering, and one** placement **in** the case of other **offerings, they render** the offering **permitted** for eating since these actions facilitate atonement after the fact, despite the fact that blood was not placed on all four corners of the altar. **And** similarly **they render** the offering ***piggul*.** That is to say, if the priest placed two placements of the blood of a sin offering or one placement of the blood of any another offering, intending to eat or burn the offering beyond its designated time, the offering is *piggul*.

A *baraita* of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov now introduces the practical ramifications of the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel debate for the laws of *pigul*. According to Beis Shammai: two *matanos* are required for the *chatas* and one for all other *korbanos* in order to be *matir* the *korban* — that is, to render the meat permitted for consumption and to allow the *emurim* to be burned. Correspondingly, it is only during these acts that *pigul* thought is halachically operative. According to Beis Hillel: for all *korbanos* — including the *chatas* — only the first and single *matana* of blood is the *matir*, and thus only during that one act can *pigul* thought take effect.

### Oshaya's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת וְאַחַת שֶׁבְּכׇל הַזְּבָחִים – מַתֶּרֶת וּמְפַגֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel say: One** placement **in** the case of **a sin offering, and** likewise **one** placement **in** the case of **all** the other **offerings,** if done with proper intent, renders the offering **permitted** for eating **and,** if done with improper intent, **renders** the offering ***piggul*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: אִם כֵּן, לִיתְנְיַיהּ גַּבֵּי קוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya objects to this: If so, let** this *halakha* **be taught** in the fourth chapter of tractate *Eduyyot* **alongside** the other rare cases of **Beit Shammai's leniencies and Beit Hillel's stringencies.** According to Beit Hillel, a sin offering is *piggul* even if the priest intended to partake of it beyond its designated time even while performing a single placement, whereas Beit Shammai maintain that the offering is *piggul* only if he had this improper intent during two placements.

Rav Oshaya raises a striking objection: given the framing of this *baraita*, Beis Shammai appears to be the *meikel* position, since they effectively give *pigul* thought fewer opportunities to render the *korban* invalid. This is surprising, since Beis Shammai is almost always the *machmir*. Should this debate not be listed among the *kulei Beis Shammai ve'chumrei Beis Hillel* — those exceptional cases where Beis Shammai is lenient and Beis Hillel stringent?

### Machmir vs Meikel

The analysis behind Rav Oshaya's challenge is precise. With respect to being *yotzei* the *korban* — ensuring the *korban* is valid and the meat is permitted — Beis Shammai is the classic *machmir*: by the *chatas*, both *matanos* must be performed, and if the blood spills after only one, one has not been *yotzei*. Beis Hillel, conversely, is *meikel*, holding that one *matana* is sufficient. But when the lens shifts to *pigul*: assuming the principle of *ein mefaglin bechatzi matir* — that *pigul* thought can only take effect when applied to the full *matir* act, not a partial one — Beis Shammai is actually *meikel* in the domain of *pigul*. Since Beis Shammai requires two *matanos* as the *matir*, a *pigul* thought during only the first *matana* does not create *pigul*, because the *matir* was not yet complete. For Beis Hillel, by contrast, the first *matana* is the entire *matir*, and a *pigul* thought during that single act fully creates *pigul*.

### Raba's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: כִּי אִיתְּשִׁיל – לְהֶתֵּירָא אִיתְּשִׁיל; דְּהָווּ לְהוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Oshaya: **When** this question **was** initially **asked,** and the Sages stated their opinions concerning the matter, **it was asked with regard to the permission** to partake of the sin offering. In other words, the dispute arose as a result of an inquiry into the other relevant *halakha*, i.e., whether the priests may partake of a sin offering, the blood of which was placed only one time. In this case it is **Beit Shammai who are** more **stringent,** as they permit the meat of a sin offering only after two placements have been placed. For this reason, this case was not listed among the other leniencies of Beit Shammai and stringencies of Beit Hillel.

Rava resolves Rav Oshaya's challenge by explaining that the decision about how to categorize this debate was made from a specific vantage point: the question of what renders the *korban muttar* — *ki itzterich leheitera itzterich*. The listing of *kulei Beis Shammai ve'chumrei Beis Hillel* in tractate Edyos was framed from the perspective of *matir*: which school demands more before the *korban* achieves its permitted status? Viewed through that lens, Beis Shammai is plainly *machmir* and Beis Hillel *meikel*. The fact that this simultaneously makes Beis Shammai *meikel* with respect to *pigul* was not the framing criterion when the debate was categorized.

### Pigul Requirement

Tosafot raises a further question: what is the basis for assuming that Beis Shammai requires the *pigul* thought to occur at *both* of the two *matanos* in order for *pigul* to apply? Perhaps Beis Shammai holds *mefaglin bechatzi matir* — that even *pigul* thought during a partial *matir* act is sufficient — in which case either the first or the second *matana* alone could create *pigul*, and Beis Shammai would again be *machmir* even by *pigul*.

### Shammai Meikel Cases

Tosafot suggests that one can parallel the reasoning: just as Beis Hillel says one *matana* is the *matir*, and that one *matana* alone creates *pigul* — meaning they require both *matanos* by *pigul* thought for full effect, not either one — so too Beis Shammai, having established two *matanos* as the *matir*, presumably requires *pigul* thought at both before the *korban* becomes *mefugal*, consistent with *ein mefaglin bechatzi matir*. Even so, Tosafot notes, there remain specific scenarios in which Beis Shammai is *meikel* by *pigul* even if one grants that either *matana* can create *pigul*. One such scenario: if a *kohen* applied the first *matana* with *pigul* intention and the blood then spilled before the second *matana* could be performed — Beis Hillel would rule *pigul*, since for them the one *matana* is the full *matir* and the *pigul* thought was operative; Beis Shammai would rule not *pigul*, because *kol matirav* — all the *matanos* that constitute the *matir* — were not completed. A further scenario: the *kohen* had a *pigul* thought during the first *matana* and a *chutz limkomo* thought during the second. According to Beis Hillel, the *chutz limkomo* thought during the second *matana* is irrelevant — the *matir* was complete after the first — and therefore the *korban* is *pigul*. According to Beis Shammai, the second *matana* is still operative as part of the *matir*, and the *chutz limkomo* thought attached to it undermines the *pigul* created by the first, since they interact within the same composite *matir* act. In both scenarios Beis Shammai emerges as the *meikel* position in the domain of *pigul*. The underlying framing of the debate, however — and the reason it was not listed in Edyos as a case of *kulei Beis Shammai* — remains: when the question was asked, it was asked from the perspective of what makes the *korban kasher* and its meat permitted, not from the perspective of *pigul*. In that context, Beis Shammai is, as always, the *machmir*.
