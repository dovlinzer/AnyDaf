# Bava Batra 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Job's Identity & Dating

### Righteous Gentile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָסִיד הָיָה בְּאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם – וְאִיּוֹב שְׁמוֹ, וְלֹא בָּא לָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל שְׂכָרוֹ. הֵבִיא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עָלָיו יִסּוּרִין – הִתְחִיל מְחָרֵף וּמְגַדֵּף. כָּפַל לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שְׂכָרוֹ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, [כְּדֵי] לְטׇרְדוֹ מִן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **There was a pious man among the nations of the world and his name was Job, and he came into the world only to receive his reward. The Holy One, Blessed be He, brought afflictions upon him, and he began to blaspheme and curse. The Holy One, Blessed be He, doubled his reward in this world in order to expel him from the World-to-Come.**

The daf begins mid-passage on 15b, approximately fifteen lines from the top, where the Gemara has been engaged in an extended discussion of the Book of Iyov — who composed it, when it was written, and now, critically, whether Iyov himself was Jewish. The Gemara had already noted the position that Iyov was among those who returned from exile in the time of the Second *Beit HaMikdash*, a position that implies he was Jewish. This now becomes the occasion for a more direct examination of the question.

A *baraita* is cited as a challenge: *Chasid haya b'umot ha'olam v'Iyov shmo* — there was a righteous man among the nations of the world and his name was Iyov. *V'lo ba l'olam ela k'dei l'kabel scharo* — he came into the world solely in order to receive his reward. The *baraita* continues: God brought afflictions upon him, and he began cursing and blaspheming. As a result, God doubled his reward in this world in order to exclude him from the World to Come.

The thrust of the *baraita* is striking. It is not merely placing Iyov among the righteous gentiles but offering a critical reading of his character: he was perhaps a *chasid* in outward behavior, but his righteousness was conditional — performed in expectation of worldly reward. When that reward did not materialize and suffering came instead, he regretted everything and turned to blasphemy. God then settled all accounts in this world, granting him the doubled portion described at the end of the book, while forfeiting his share in the *Olam Haba*. The word *kafal* — doubled — echoes the explicit *pesuk* at the end of Iyov, where God restores twice what had been taken.

It is worth noting that this reading sits in tension with the *pshat* of the book itself, where Iyov does not ultimately blaspheme but maintains his integrity. The Gemara is here offering a critical *midrashic* lens, and as we will see throughout the rest of the daf, this lens will be applied in considerable depth.

The *baraita* clearly indicates that Iyov was not Jewish, which stands in tension with the earlier position that he was among those who returned in the time of the Second Temple. The Gemara therefore declares: *tana'ei hi* — it is a dispute among *Tannaim*.

### Job's Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִיּוֹב בִּימֵי שְׁפוֹט הַשּׁוֹפְטִים הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵן אַתֶּם כֻּלְּכֶם חֲזִיתֶם, וְלָמָּה זֶּה הֶבֶל תֶּהְבָּלוּ״ – אֵיזֶה דּוֹר שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ הֶבֶל? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה דּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל שְׁפוֹט הַשּׁוֹפְטִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught in a baraita, Rabbi Elazar says: Job lived in the days of the judging of the Judges, as it is stated: "Behold, all you yourselves have seen it; why then have you become altogether vain?" Which generation was completely vain? You must say it was the generation of the judging of the Judges.**

A *baraita* is then cited presenting several *Tanna'ic* positions on when Iyov lived. Rabbi Eliezer says Iyov lived *bimei shfot ha-shoftim* — in the time of the judges. The proof is from a verse in Iyov: *Hen atem kulchem chazitem, v'lamah zeh hevel tehavalu* — you have all seen, and why are you acting with such foolishness? The word *hevel* connects to a generation described as entirely *hevel*, and Rabbi Eliezer identifies this with the period of the judges, as we will see shortly, a period of profound moral collapse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: אִיּוֹב בִּימֵי אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא נִמְצָא נָשִׁים יָפוֹת כִּבְנוֹת אִיּוֹב בְּכׇל הָאָרֶץ״ – אֵיזֶהוּ דּוֹר שֶׁנִּתְבַּקְּשׁוּ בּוֹ נָשִׁים יָפוֹת? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה דּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ. וְאֵימָא בִּימֵי דָּוִד, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְבַקְשׁוּ נַעֲרָה יָפָה״! הָתָם בְּכֹל גְּבוּל יִשְׂרָאֵל, הָכָא בְּכׇל הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: Job lived in the days of Ahasuerus, as it is stated: "And in all the world were no women found so beautiful as the daughters of Job." In which generation were beautiful women sought? You must say it was the generation of Ahasuerus. But why not say it was in the days of David, as it is written: "And they sought a beautiful maiden"? There, in the time of David, they searched "throughout the territory of Israel"; here, in the time of Ahasuerus, they searched throughout the world.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha says Iyov lived in the time of Achashverosh, deriving this from the verse *v'lo nimtza nashim yafot kibnot Iyov b'chol ha'aretz* — no women as beautiful as the daughters of Iyov were found in all the land. The connection is to the period when beautiful women were sought throughout the entire land — that is, the search conducted for Achashverosh. The Gemara raises an objection: could we not say this refers to the time of David, when a young woman was sought for him in his old age? The Gemara deflects: there it says *b'chol gvul Yisrael* — within the borders of Israel; here it says *b'chol ha'aretz* — the entire land, pointing to a broader and more universal search, consistent with the Purim narrative. These identifications seem more like attempts to anchor a universalist story in a particular historical moment than arguments that carry strong thematic weight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אִיּוֹב בִּימֵי מַלְכוּת שְׁבָא הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּפֹּל שְׁבָא וַתִּקָּחֵם״. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִיּוֹב בִּימֵי כַּשְׂדִּים הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּשְׂדִּים שָׂמוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה רָאשִׁים״. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אִיּוֹב בִּימֵי יַעֲקֹב הָיָה, וְדִינָה בַּת יַעֲקֹב נָשָׂא – כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״כְּדַבֵּר אַחַת הַנְּבָלוֹת תְּדַבֵּרִי״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״כִּי נְבָלָה עָשָׂה בְיִשְׂרָאֵל״. וְכוּלְּהוּ תַּנָּאֵי סְבִירָא לְהוּ דְּאִיּוֹב מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל הֲוָה, לְבַר מִיֵּשׁ אוֹמְרִים;
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says: Job lived in the days of the kingdom of Sheba, as it is stated: "And Sheba fell upon them, and took them away." And the Rabbis say: Job lived in the days of the Chaldeans, as it is stated: "The Chaldeans formed three bands." And some say Job lived in the days of Jacob and married Dina, the daughter of Jacob. As it is written here: "You speak as one of the loathsome women speaks," and it is written there: "He has done a loathsome act in Israel." And all these tanna'im hold that Job was a Jew except for the opinion introduced with the phrase: And some say.**

Rabbi Natan says Iyov lived *bimei malchut Sheva* — in the time of the kingdom of Sheba — drawing directly from the internal evidence of the Iyov narrative itself, where the Shebeans attack his cattle. Whatever the kingdom of Sheba refers to historically, that is when Iyov lived.

The *Chachamim* say he lived *bimei Kasdim* — in the time of the Chaldeans. Rashi identifies this as the Chaldeans of Nebuchadnezzar, thus placing Iyov in the period of the destruction of the First *Beit HaMikdash* — a dating that carries stronger thematic resonance, perhaps connecting him to those who later returned from exile in *golah*. The proof again comes from the story itself, where the Chaldeans form three separate divisions and attack his camels.

Finally, *yesh omrim* — some say — Iyov lived in the time of Yaakov, and indeed married Dinah bat Yaakov after the events involving Shechem. The proof is from a verbal analogy: the word *nevalah* appears in Iyov — *k'davar achat ha-nevalot tedaberi* — and the same word appears in the account of Dinah — *ki nevalah asah b'Yisrael*.

### Post-Mosaic Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מֵאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם הֲוָה, בָּתַר דִּשְׁכֵיב מֹשֶׁה – מִי שָׁרְיָא שְׁכִינָה עַל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם? וְהָא אָמַר מָר: בִּקֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה עַל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, וְנָתַן לוֹ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִפְלִינוּ אֲנִי וְעַמְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it should enter your mind that he came from the nations of the world, there is a difficulty: After Moses died, did the Divine Presence rest any longer on the nations of the world? But doesn't the Master say: Moses requested that the Divine Presence not rest again on the nations of the world, and his request was granted to him, as it is stated: "That we shall be differentiated, I and Your people, from all the people that are upon the face of the earth."**

The Gemara now draws a striking conclusion: *v'chulehu Tanna'ei sevira lehu d'Iyov mi-Yisrael havah levar mi-yesh omrim* — all the *Tannaim* implicitly hold that Iyov was Jewish, except for the last opinion that he lived in the time of Yaakov. This requires explanation, because on its face, nothing about these datings implies anything about Iyov's ethnicity. Why should placing Iyov in the time of Achashverosh or the Chaldeans indicate that he was Jewish?

The Gemara explains: *d'ee salka da'atach me'chassidei umot ha'olam havah* — if you were to assume he was a righteous gentile — *basar d'shachiv Moshe mi shari Shchina al ovdei kochavim?* — after Moshe died, does the divine presence come to rest upon gentiles? Assuming that Iyov was a *navi*, as the appearance of God to him at the end of the book suggests, and assuming that all these *Tannaim* place him after the time of Moshe, he could not have been a gentile. After Moshe, *nevuah* was restricted to Israel.

The source for this restriction: *de'amar mar bikesh Moshe shelo tishreh Shchina al ovdei kochavim v'nasan lo* — Moshe requested that the divine presence not rest upon the nations, and this was granted to him, as it says *v'niflinu ani v'amecha* — you and I and your people shall be distinct from all the other nations. That distinctiveness includes the realm of prophecy.

Therefore, any *Tanna* who places Iyov after Moshe must implicitly regard him as Jewish, since a gentile could not have received prophecy in that era. The only exception is the opinion of *yesh omrim*, who places him in the time of Yaakov — well before Moshe's prayer — and thus can consistently hold that Iyov was a righteous gentile. All others must regard him as Jewish.

## Job's Licentiousness

### Licentiousness Era

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל אִיּוֹב שָׁטוּף בְּזִמָּה הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵן אַתֶּם כּוּלְּכֶם חֲזִיתֶם, וְלָמָּה זֶּה הֶבֶל תֶּהְבָּלוּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״שׁוּבִי שׁוּבִי הַשּׁוּלַמִּית שׁוּבִי שׁוּבִי וְנֶחֱזֶה בָּךְ״. אֵימָא בִּנְבוּאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״חֲזוֹן יְשַׁעְיָהוּ בֶן אָמוֹץ״! אִם כֵּן, ״לָמָּה זֶּה הֶבֶל תֶּהְבָּלוּ״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The generation of Job was awash in licentiousness, as it is stated: "Behold, all of you yourselves have seen it; why then have you become altogether vain?" and it is written: "Return, return, O Shulamite; return, return, that we may look upon you," which teaches that the phrase "you have seen it" connotes a licentious gaze. The Gemara asks: But say that the phrase "you yourselves have seen it" signifies prophecy, as it is written: "The vision of Isaiah ben Amoz." The Gemara answers: If so, why do I need the words: "Why then have you become altogether vain"? Rather, the reference must be to inappropriate licentious gazing.**

Rabbi Yochanan now offers an observation about the moral character of Iyov's generation: *doro shel Iyov shatuf b'zimah hayah* — his generation was awash with licentiousness. The proof comes from the same verse already cited: *Hen atem kulchem chazitem* — you have all seen. The word *chazitem*, to see, is linked by Rabbi Yochanan to the erotic use of the same root elsewhere — *shuvi shuvi ha-Shulamit shuvi shuvi v'nechezeh bach*, where the gazing is decidedly romantic in character. Here too, says Rashi, the *chazitem* is not a neutral visual act but a lascivious, covetous gaze.

The Gemara raises a challenge: perhaps *chazitem* refers to prophetic vision, *chazon*, as in *chazon Yeshayahu ben Amotz*? The Gemara dismisses this: the verse immediately follows *chazitem* with the rebuke *v'lamah zeh hevel tehavalu* — why are you acting with such foolishness — which would be incongruous if the vision were prophetic. The pairing of gaze with *hevel* reveals the nature of the seeing: something vain, something dissolute.

### Judges & Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בִּימֵי שְׁפוֹט הַשּׁוֹפְטִים״? דּוֹר שֶׁשּׁוֹפֵט אֶת שׁוֹפְטָיו; אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״טוֹל קֵיסָם מִבֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״ – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״טוֹל קוֹרָה מִבֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״. אֹמֵר לוֹ: ״כַּסְפְּךָ הָיָה לְסִיגִים״ – אֹמֵר לוֹ: ״סׇבְאֲךָ מָהוּל בְּמַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is the meaning of that which is written: "And it happened in the days of the judging of the Judges"? This indicates a generation that judged its judges. If a judge would say to the defendant standing before him: Remove the splinter from between your eyes, the defendant would say to him: Remove the beam from between your eyes. If the judge would say to him: "Your silver is become dross," the defendant would say to him: "Your wine is mixed with water."**

Rabbi Yochanan adds a further teaching: *mai d'chtiv vayehi bimei shefot ha-shoftim* — what does it mean that in the days when the judges judged? The phrase is homiletically rendered: *dor she-shofet et shofte'av* — a generation that judged its own judges. When a judge would tell someone to remove the splinter from his eye — *tol kisam mibein einecha* — the person could retort: remove the beam from your own eye. The judges had so completely forfeited their moral authority through their own corruption that no rebuke could carry weight.

The Gemara extends the analogy further. If a judge said *caspech haya lesigim* — your silver has become dross — the person could respond *savech mahul bamayim* — your wine is watered down. Moral instruction requires a degree of moral standing in the instructor; when that standing collapses, the entire social order collapses with it. This was the character of the generation associated with Iyov.

## Satan's Challenge

### Job vs Abraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי הַיּוֹם, וַיָּבֹאוּ בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים לְהִתְיַצֵּב עַל ה׳, וַיָּבֹא גַּם הַשָּׂטָן בְּתוֹכָם. וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הַשָּׂטָן: מֵאַיִן תָּבֹא? וַיַּעַן הַשָּׂטָן וְגוֹ׳״ – אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שַׁטְתִּי בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ, וְלֹא מָצָאתִי נֶאֱמָן כְּעַבְדְּךָ אַבְרָהָם – שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ לוֹ: ״קוּם הִתְהַלֵּךְ בָּאָרֶץ לְאׇרְכָּהּ וּלְרׇחְבָּהּ כִּי לְךָ אֶתְּנֶנָּה״, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁלֹּא מָצָא מָקוֹם לִקְבּוֹר אֶת שָׂרָה [עַד שֶׁקָּנָה בְּאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שֶׁקֶל כֶּסֶף] – לֹא הִרְהֵר אַחַר מִדּוֹתֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **"Now there was a day when the sons of God came to present themselves before the Lord, and the Satan came also among them. And the Lord said to the Satan: From where do you come? And the Satan answered the Lord, and said: From going to and fro in the earth." The Satan said to God: Master of the Universe, I have gone to and fro throughout the entire world and I have not found anyone as faithful as Your servant Abraham, to whom You said: "Arise, walk through the land in the length of it and in the breadth of it; for I will give it to you." And even so, when he did not find a place to bury Sarah before he purchased a burial site for four hundred silver shekels, he did not find fault with Your ways.**

The Gemara now turns to an extended *midrashic* reading of the narrative of Iyov, beginning with the heavenly prologue. The opening verse: *vayehi hayom vayavo'u bnei ha-elohim l'hit'yatzev al Hashem vayavo gam ha-Satan betocham* — it was the day, and the divine beings came to present themselves before God, and the Satan came among them as well.

God asks the Satan where he has come from, and the Satan replies that he has been roaming the earth. The Gemara then adds a *midrashic* expansion of the Satan's report: *shat'ti b'chol ha-olam kulo v'lo matzati ne'eman k'avdecha Avraham* — I have traversed the entire world and I have found no one as faithful as your servant Avraham. The connection to Avraham is established through the verbal echo: the Satan uses the language of *mi-hithalech ba-aretz*, which echoes the divine command to Avraham — *kum hithalech ba-aretz l'orkah u-l'rochbah ki lecha etnenah*. Avraham was promised the land, yet when the time came to bury Sarah, he could find no place of his own and was compelled to purchase a burial plot from Ephron at an exorbitant price. Despite this apparent failure of the divine promise, *lo hirher achar midotecha* — he harbored no thoughts questioning God's justice. No one, says the Satan, comes close to Avraham.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הַשָּׂטָן: הֲשַׂמְתָּ לִבְּךָ אֶל עַבְדִּי אִיּוֹב, כִּי אֵין כָּמוֹהוּ בָּאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּדוֹל הַנֶּאֱמָר בְּאִיּוֹב יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר בְּאַבְרָהָם; דְּאִילּוּ בְּאַבְרָהָם כְּתִיב: ״כִּי עַתָּה יָדַעְתִּי כִּי יְרֵא אֱלֹהִים אַתָּה״, וּבְאִיּוֹב כְּתִיב: ״אִישׁ תָּם וְיָשָׁר יְרֵא אֱלֹהִים [וְסָר מֵרָע]״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And the Lord said to the Satan: Have you considered My servant Job, that there is none like him on earth, a perfect and upright man, one who fears God and turns away from evil?" About this Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That which is stated about Job is greater than that which is stated about Abraham. As with regard to Abraham it is written: "For now I know that you fear God," with regard to Job it is written: "A perfect and an upright man, one who fears God and turns away from evil."**

God responds: *ha-samta libcha el avdi Iyov* — have you considered my servant Iyov? *Ki ein kamohu ba-aretz* — there is none like him in the land. Rabbi Yochanan observes that the praise accorded to Iyov is actually greater than that given to Avraham. By Avraham it says: *ki ata yadati ki yerei elohim ata* — now I know that you fear God. By Iyov the verse says: *ish tam v'yashar yerei elohim v'sar me-ra* — pure, upright, fearing God, and turning away from evil. Multiple appellations are applied to Iyov that are absent from the praise of Avraham.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְסָר מֵרָע״? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: אִיּוֹב וַותְּרָן בְּמָמוֹנוֹ הָיָה – מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, נוֹתֵן חֲצִי פְּרוּטָה לַחֶנְוָנִי; אִיּוֹב – וִיתְּרָהּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is meant by "and turns away from evil"? Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel says: Job was forgiving with his money. It is the way of the world that one pays the storekeeper for even half-*peruta* of merchandise purchased from him. But if somebody bought an item of such little value from Job, he would forgive him his half-*peruta*.**

What is the meaning of *v'sar me-ra* — turning away from evil? Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel explains: *Iyov vatran b'mamona hayah* — Iyov was generous with his money, not exacting in financial dealings. The usual way of the world is that if one owes a half a *peruta* to a worker, one might combine it with a purchase to make change. Iyov simply waived what was owed him, forgoing his due.

### Fear for Naught

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעַן הַשָּׂטָן אֶת ה׳ וַיֹּאמַר: הַחִנָּם יָרֵא אִיּוֹב אֱלֹהִים?! הֲלֹא אַתָּה סַכְתָּ בַעֲדוֹ וּבְעַד בֵּיתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״ – מַאי ״מַעֲשֵׂה יָדָיו בֵּרַכְתָּ״? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַנּוֹטֵל פְּרוּטָה מֵאִיּוֹב, מִתְבָּרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **"Then the Satan answered the Lord, and said: Does Job fear God for naught? Have You not made a hedge about him, and about his house, and about all that he has on every side? You have blessed the work of his hands, and his cattle is increased in the land." What is meant by: "You have blessed the work of his hands"? Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: Anyone who took a *peruta* from Job was blessed.**

The Satan responds with his famous challenge: *ha-chinam yerei Iyov elohim* — does Iyov fear God for nothing? *Halo ata sakota ba-ado u-v'ad beito* — you have placed a protective hedge around him and his household. Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak expounds the phrase *maaseh yadav berachta*: anyone who received even a small coin from Iyov was blessed — his giving was like the Rebbe's dollars, carrying a blessing in and of itself. Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Chanina adds that Iyov's flocks had burst beyond the normal bounds of nature — *partzu gidro shel olam* — his sheep were so powerful they would kill the wolves rather than the other way around.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִקְנֵהוּ פָּרַץ בָּאָרֶץ״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: מִקְנֵהוּ שֶׁל אִיּוֹב פָּרְצוּ גִּדְרוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם; מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם – זְאֵבִים הוֹרְגִים הָעִזִּים, מִקְנֵהוּ שֶׁל אִיּוֹב – עִזִּים הוֹרְגִים אֶת הַזְּאֵבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **What is meant by: "And his livestock is increased in the land"? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: Job's livestock breached the order of the world. It is the way of the world that wolves kill goats, but in the case of Job's livestock, the goats killed the wolves.**

The Satan then issues his challenge: *shelach na yadcha u-ga b'chol asher lo im lo al panecha yevarecheka* — send forth your hand and strike all that he has, and he will surely curse you to your face. *Yevarecheka* here is a familiar Talmudic euphemism, as it is in the biblical text itself. God accepts the challenge, granting the Satan dominion over everything Iyov possesses: *hineh kol asher lo b'yadecha* — all that he has is in your hand. The sole limitation: *rak elav al tishlach yadcha* — do not lay your hand on his person.

### First Afflictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי הַיּוֹם, וּבָנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו אוֹכְלִים וְשׁוֹתִים יַיִן בְּבֵית אֲחִיהֶם הַבְּכוֹר, וּמַלְאָךְ בָּא אֶל אִיּוֹב וַיֹּאמַר: הַבָּקָר הָיוּ חוֹרְשׁוֹת וְגוֹ׳״ – מַאי ״הַבָּקָר הָיוּ חוֹרְשׁוֹת, וְהָאֲתֹנוֹת רֹעוֹת עַל יְדֵיהֶם״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִטְעִימוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאִיּוֹב מֵעֵין הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **"Now there was a day when his sons and his daughters were eating and drinking wine in their eldest brother's house, and there came a messenger to Job, and said: The oxen were plowing, and the asses were feeding beside them." What is meant by: "The oxen were plowing and the asses were feeding beside them"? Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, gave Job a taste of the World-to-Come.**

The narrative then unfolds rapidly. Iyov's children are feasting in the home of the firstborn. The Gemara pauses on the verse describing the cattle plowing and the donkeys grazing nearby: *ha-bakar hayu chorshot v'ha-atonot ro'ot al yadeihem*. Rabbi Yochanan derives from this that God had given Iyov a foretaste of the *Olam Ha-ba* — things were flourishing in a supernatural way, as Rashi explains, with seed sprouting on the very day it was planted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עוֹד זֶה מְדַבֵּר – וְזֶה בָּא, וַיֹּאמַר: אֵשׁ אֱלֹהִים וְגוֹ׳. עוֹד זֶה מְדַבֵּר – וְזֶה בָּא, וַיֹּאמַר: כַּשְׂדִּים שָׂמוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה רָאשִׁים, וַיִּפְשְׁטוּ עַל הַגְּמַלִּים וַיִּקָּחוּם וְגוֹ׳. עוֹד זֶה מְדַבֵּר – וְזֶה בָּא, וַיֹּאמַר: בָּנֶיךָ וּבְנוֹתֶיךָ אוֹכְלִים וְשׁוֹתִים יַיִן בְּבֵית אֲחִיהֶם הַבְּכוֹר, וְהִנֵּה רוּחַ גְּדוֹלָה בָּאָה מֵעֵבֶר הַמִּדְבָּר, וַיִּגַּע בְּאַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹת הַבַּיִת וַיִּפֹּל עַל הַנְּעָרִים וְגוֹ׳. וַיָּקׇם אִיּוֹב וַיִּקְרַע אֶת מְעִלוֹ וַיָּגׇז אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְגוֹ׳, וַיֹּאמֶר: עָרוֹם יָצָאתִי מִבֶּטֶן אִמִּי, וְעָרוֹם אָשׁוּב שָׁמָּה; ה׳ נָתַן וַה׳ לָקָח, יְהִי שֵׁם ה׳ מְבוֹרָךְ. בְּכׇל זֹאת לֹא חָטָא אִיּוֹב וְלֹא נָתַן תִּפְלָה לֵאלֹהִים.
>
> **Translation:** **"While he was yet speaking, there came also another and said: The fire of God has fallen from heaven, and has burned up the sheep, and the servants, and consumed them… While he was yet speaking, there came also another and said: The Chaldeans formed three bands, and fell among the camels, and have carried them away, and have slain the servants with the edge of the sword… While he was yet speaking, there came also another and said: Your sons and your daughters were eating and drinking wine in their eldest brother's house; and, behold, there came a great wind from across the wilderness, and smote the four corners of the house, and it fell upon the young men, and they are dead… Then Job arose, and rent his coat, and shaved his head, and fell down on the ground and prostrated himself. And he said: I came naked out of my mother's womb, and naked I shall return there; the Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away, blessed be the name of the Lord. In all this Job sinned not, nor did he lay reproach on God."**

Messenger after messenger arrives in rapid succession, each arriving before the previous one has finished speaking. The Sabeans attack the oxen and donkeys; fire falls from heaven and destroys the flocks; the Chaldeans form three divisions and take the camels; and finally a great wind strikes the four corners of the house where Iyov's children are gathered, and they perish. Iyov rises, tears his garment, shaves his head, and declares: *Arom yatzati mi-beten imi v'arom ashuv shama, Hashem natan v'Hashem lakach yehi shem Hashem mevorach* — Naked I came from my mother's womb and naked shall I return. God has given and God has taken; may the name of God be blessed.

*B'chol zot lo chata Iyov v'lo natan tiflah l'elohim* — with all of this, Iyov did not sin, and did not ascribe any impropriety to God.

### Body Affliction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיְהִי הַיּוֹם, וַיָּבֹאוּ בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים לְהִתְיַצֵּב וְגוֹ׳. וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הַשָּׂטָן: אֵי מִזֶּה תָּבֹא? וַיַּעַן הַשָּׂטָן אֶת ה׳, וַיֹּאמַר: מִשֻּׁט בָּאָרֶץ [וְגוֹ׳]״ – אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שַׁטְתִּי בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם, וְלֹא מָצָאתִי כְּעַבְדְּךָ אַבְרָהָם – שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ לוֹ: ״קוּם הִתְהַלֵּךְ בָּאָרֶץ לְאׇרְכָּהּ וּלְרׇחְבָּהּ, כִּי לְךָ אֶתְּנֶנָּה״, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִקְבּוֹר שָׂרָה, לֹא מָצָא מָקוֹם לְקוֹבְרָהּ – וְלֹא הִרְהֵר אַחַר מִדּוֹתֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **"Again there was a day when the sons of God came to present themselves before the Lord, and the Satan came also among them to present himself before God. And the Lord said to the Satan: From where do you come? And the Satan answered the Lord, and said: From going to and fro in the earth and from walking up and down in it." The Satan said before God: Master of the Universe, I have gone to and fro across the entire world and have not found anyone as faithful as your servant Abraham, to whom you said: "Arise, walk through the land in the length of it and in the breadth of it; for I will give it to you." And when he wanted to bury Sarah, he could not find a place to bury her, and yet he did not criticize Your ways.**

The heavenly court convenes again. The same exchange takes place: God asks the Satan where he has come from, and the Satan again reports roaming the earth and again places Avraham at the pinnacle of righteousness, recalling the episode of Sarah's burial. God again directs attention to Iyov: *ve-odeinu machazik b'tumaso* — he still holds fast to his integrity. *Va-tesiteni vo levalo chinam* — and you incited me against him for nothing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הַשָּׂטָן: הֲשַׂמְתָּ לִבְּךָ אֶל עַבְדִּי אִיּוֹב, כִּי אֵין כָּמֹהוּ בָּאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳, וְעֹדֶנּוּ מַחֲזִיק בְּתֻמָּתוֹ, וַתְּסִיתֵנִי בוֹ לְבַלְּעוֹ חִנָּם״ –
>
> **Translation:** **"And the Lord said to the Satan: Have you considered My servant Job, that there is none like him on earth, a perfect and an upright man, one that fears God and turns away from evil? And still he holds fast to his integrity, although you moved Me against him, to destroy him without cause."**

Rabbi Yochanan observes here: *ilmalei mikra kasuv ee efshar l'omro* — were it not that the verse itself says this, it would be impossible to say such a thing. The imagery is of God being, as it were, persuaded or provoked — *kaviyachol k'mi she-nistatta* — as if God could be enticed. This is a striking theological problem embedded in the text itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ – כְּאָדָם שֶׁמְּסִיתִין אוֹתוֹ וְנִיסָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Were it not explicitly written in the verse, it would be impossible to say this, as it would be insulting to God's honor. The verse states: "You moved Me against him," like a person whom others persuade and allows himself to be persuaded.**

The Satan escalates his challenge: *or be-or v'chol asher la-ish yiten be-ad nafsho* — skin for skin, a person will give everything for his own life. Afflict his body and he will curse you. God grants expanded permission: *hinehu b'yadecha ach et nafsho shemor* — he is in your hand, but preserve his life.

### Satan Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעַן הַשָּׂטָן אֶת ה׳ וַיֹּאמַר: עוֹר בְּעַד עוֹר, וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר לָאִישׁ יִתֵּן בְּעַד נַפְשׁוֹ. אוּלָם שְׁלַח נָא יָדְךָ וְגַע אֶל עַצְמוֹ וְאֶל בְּשָׂרוֹ, אִם לֹא עַל פָּנֶיךָ יְבָרְכֶךָּ. וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל הַשָּׂטָן: הִנּוֹ בְיָדֶךָ, אַךְ אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ שְׁמֹר. וַיֵּצֵא הַשָּׂטָן מֵאֵת פְּנֵי ה׳, וַיַּךְ אֶת אִיּוֹב וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: קָשֶׁה צַעֲרוֹ שֶׁל שָׂטָן, יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל אִיּוֹב – מָשָׁל לְעֶבֶד שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ רַבּוֹ: שְׁבוֹר חָבִית, וּשְׁמוֹר אֶת יֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **"And the Satan answered the Lord, and said: Skin for skin, for all that a man has he will give for his life. But put forth Your hand now, and touch his bone and his flesh, and he will curse You to Your face. And the Lord said to the Satan: Behold, he is in your hand; only spare his life. So the Satan went forth from the presence of the Lord, and smote Job with vile sores from the sole of his foot to his crown." Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Satan's suffering was more difficult than that of Job. This can be explained by means of a parable involving a servant whose master said to him: Break the barrel but save its wine.**

Rabbi Yitzchak notes that the Satan's predicament was more difficult than Iyov's own: *mashal l'eved she-amar lo rabbo shevor chavit u-shmor yeina* — it is like a servant whose master said: break the barrel but don't spill the wine. The Satan had to afflict Iyov's body while leaving his soul intact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: יוֹרֵד וּמַתְעֶה, וְעוֹלֶה וּמַרְגִּיז, נוֹטֵל רְשׁוּת, וְנוֹטֵל נְשָׁמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a baraita with regard to the methods of the Satan: He descends to this world and misleads a person into sinning. He then ascends to Heaven, levels accusations against that very sinner, and inflames God's anger against him. He then receives permission to act and takes away the sinner's soul as punishment.**

A *baraita* is cited that broadens the characterization of the Satan beyond the Iyov story: *yoreid u-mat'eh v'olah u-margiz v'notel reshut v'notel neshama* — he descends and leads astray, then ascends and provokes God's anger, receives permission, and takes the person's life. This moves the discussion from the particular narrative of Iyov toward a more general description of the Satan's operating mode — seducing people into sin, then securing divine authorization to take their lives as punishment. The resemblance to the Christian conception of Satan is evident, and this is precisely where Rashi's comment becomes significant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוּא שָׂטָן, הוּא יֵצֶר הָרָע, הוּא מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת. הוּא שָׂטָן – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּצֵא הַשָּׂטָן מֵאֵת פְּנֵי ה׳״. הוּא יֵצֶר הָרָע – כְּתִיב הָתָם: ״רַק רַע כׇּל הַיּוֹם״, וּכְתִיב הָכָא: (״רַק אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ שְׁמֹר״) [״רַק אֵלָיו אַל תִּשְׁלַח יָדֶךָ״]. הוּא מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת – דִּכְתִיב: ״(רַק) [אַךְ] אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ שְׁמֹר״ – אַלְמָא בְּדִידֵיהּ קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: Satan, the evil inclination, and the Angel of Death are one, that is, they are three aspects of the same essence. He is the Satan who seduces people and then accuses them, as it is written: "So the Satan went forth from the presence of the Lord, and smote Job." He is also the evil inclination, as it is written there: "The impulse of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continuously" and it is written here: "Only upon himself do not put forth your hand." He is also the Angel of Death, as it is written: "Only spare his life"; apparently Job's life depends upon him, the Satan, and accordingly the Satan must also be the Angel of Death.**

Resh Lakish then offers a formulation that has generated much discussion: *hu Satan hu yetzer hara hu malach ha-mavet* — the Satan, the evil inclination, and the angel of death are one and the same. This identification pulls in an inward direction: the Satan is not primarily an external metaphysical force but is identical with the *yetzer hara* within the human being. The tendency to read Satan as merely the name for our internal inclination toward evil — as some *acharonim* do in explaining the purpose of blowing the *shofar k'dei l'arvav et ha-Satan* — is at least supported by this identification.

But the equation *hu malach ha-mavet* is harder to fully internalize, as the prooftexts make clear. The verse *rak elav al tishlach yadcha* — do not touch his person — and the corresponding *ach et nafsho shemor* — preserve his life — show that the Satan had the potential to take Iyov's life; therefore he must be the *malach ha-mavet*. This pushes the imagery back toward the external, the literal. The honest reading is that this Gemara holds both simultaneously, and the tension between externalizing these forces and internalizing them is part of what makes the passage so rich.

### Leshem Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: שָׂטָן וּפְנִינָה לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם נִתְכַּוְּונוּ. שָׂטָן – כֵּיוָן דְּחַזְיֵא לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא דְּנָטְיָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ בָּתַר אִיּוֹב, אָמַר: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם מִינְּשֵׁי לֵיהּ לְרַחְמָנוּתֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם. פְּנִינָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִעֲסַתָּה צָרָתָהּ גַּם כַּעַס, בַּעֲבוּר הַרְּעִימָהּ״. דַּרְשַׁהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב בְּפַפּוֹנְיָא. אֲתָא שָׂטָן, נַשְּׁקֵיהּ לְכַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi says: Both Satan, who brought accusations against Job, and Peninnah, who tormented Hannah, mother of Samuel the prophet, acted with intent that was for the sake of Heaven. As for Satan, when he saw that the Holy One, Blessed be He, inclined to favor Job and praised him, he said: Heaven forbid that He should forget the love of Abraham. With regard to Peninnah, as it is written: "And her rival wife also provoked her sore, to make her fret," i.e., Peninnah upset Hannah in order to motivate her to pray. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov taught this in Paphunya, and Satan came and kissed his feet in gratitude for speaking positively about him.**

Rabbi Levi teaches: *Satan u-Pninah l'shem shamayim nitkavnu* — both the Satan and Pninah acted with heavenly intent. The Satan saw that God was praising Iyov so lavishly and worried that Avraham's merits were being forgotten — how could it be that this other figure, possibly not even Jewish, was being praised above the greatest of the patriarchs? His challenge, on this reading, was a defense of Avraham's legacy. Similarly, Pninah provoked Channah — *v'ka'astah tzaratah gam ka'as ba'avur har'imah* — in order to prompt Channah to pray, which ultimately led to the birth of Shmuel. Rashi notes that *har'imah* can be read as *l'hartinah* — to provoke her into petitioning God.

Rava bar Yaakov reportedly delivered this teaching in the town of Pappunya. The *aggadah* concludes: the Satan came and kissed him on his feet in gratitude, since it is rare for a *darshan* to advocate on the Satan's behalf.

This teaching is susceptible to two opposing readings. One might see it as tempering the culpability of destructive actors who genuinely believed they were acting for a righteous cause. The other — and arguably more powerful — reading is a warning: even those who are absolutely convinced they are acting *l'shem shamayim* can cause enormous harm and destruction. The path to wrongdoing is often paved with self-righteous intent. The fact that the Satan comes to kiss the preacher's feet does not resolve which lesson is primary, but the very structure of the *aggadah* — linking the Satan's destructive challenge to the same category as Pninah's painful provocations — suggests that the label *l'shem shamayim* does not, by itself, justify the means.

## Job's Hidden Heresy

### Against Satan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּכׇל זֹאת לֹא חָטָא אִיּוֹב בִּשְׂפָתָיו״. אָמַר רָבָא: בִּשְׂפָתָיו לֹא חָטָא, בְּלִבּוֹ חָטָא. מַאי קָאָמַר? ״אֶרֶץ נִתְּנָה בְיַד רָשָׁע, פְּנֵי שֹׁפְטֶיהָ יְכַסֶּה, אִם לֹא אֵיפוֹ מִי הוּא״ – אָמַר רָבָא: בִּקֵּשׁ אִיּוֹב לַהֲפוֹךְ קְעָרָה עַל פִּיהָ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לֹא דִּבֵּר אִיּוֹב אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד הַשָּׂטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **"In all this Job did not sin with his lips." Rava says: A close reading of the verse indicates that he did not sin with his lips, but he sinned in his heart. What did he say that suggests that he had wicked thoughts? "The earth is given into the hand of the wicked, he covers the faces of its judges; if not he, then who is it?" Rava says: Job sought to turn the bowl upside down, that is to say, he alluded here to a heretical thought. Abaye said to him: Job was referring here only to the Satan.**

The narrative now notes that *b'chol zot lo chata Iyov bi-sfatav* — with all of this, Iyov did not sin with his lips. The Gemara emphasizes the qualifier: *bi-sfatav* — with his lips — implying that he may have sinned in his heart. Rava states directly: *bi-sfatav lo chata b'libo chata* — he did not sin with his lips, but in his heart he sinned.

The Gemara then cites a verse from Iyov: *eretz nitnah b'yad rasha pnei shofteha yechaseh im lo efo mi hu* — the land is given over to the wicked; the face of its judges is covered — if not then who is it? Rava explains: *bikeish Iyov la-hafoch k'ara al piha* — Iyov was trying to turn the bowl upside down, to overturn any sense of divine justice. He was denying *hashgacha*, denying that righteousness and wickedness have consequences.

Abaye responds: *lo dibeir Iyov ela k'neged Satan* — he spoke only against the Satan, not against God. His complaint was not that God permits injustice, but that the Satan causes evil in the world. The Gemara then notes that this is in fact a *Tanna'ic* dispute, paralleled by Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that Iyov was trying to overturn everything, and Rabbi Yehoshua, who holds that he spoke only against the Satan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״אֶרֶץ נִתְּנָה בְיַד רָשָׁע״ – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּקֵּשׁ אִיּוֹב לַהֲפוֹךְ קְעָרָה עַל פִּיהָ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לֹא דִּבֵּר אִיּוֹב אֶלָּא כְּלַפֵּי שָׂטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **This is parallel to a dispute between tanna'im, as it was taught in a baraita: "The earth is given into the hand of the wicked." Rabbi Eliezer says: Job sought to turn the bowl upside down; Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: Job was referring here only to the Satan.**

The observation is also made — in line with the reading attributed to the Maharsha — that the text said Iyov did not sin with his lips, and yet immediately proceeds to quote things Iyov actually said out loud. The resolution is that *lo chata bi-sfatav* does not mean he said nothing critical; it means that his explicit words, taken at face value, were not outright heresy. They were, at most, a grappling and a struggle. But the Gemara is now undertaking a close reading that will uncover, beneath the surface of those words, what Iyov was really thinking.

### Causation & Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל דַּעְתְּךָ כִּי לֹא אֶרְשָׁע וְאֵין מִיָּדְךָ מַצִּיל״ – אָמַר רָבָא: בִּקֵּשׁ אִיּוֹב לִפְטוֹר אֶת כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מִן הַדִּין. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בָּרָאתָ שׁוֹר – פַּרְסוֹתָיו סְדוּקוֹת, בָּרָאתָ חֲמוֹר – פַּרְסוֹתָיו קְלוּטוֹת; בָּרָאתָ גַּן עֵדֶן, בָּרָאתָ גֵּיהִנָּם; בָּרָאתָ צַדִּיקִים, בָּרָאתָ רְשָׁעִים. מִי מְעַכֵּב עַל יָדְךָ?
>
> **Translation:** **"Although You know that I am not wicked, and there is none that can deliver out of Your hand." Rava says: Job sought to exempt the whole world from judgment, claiming that all of a person's actions are directed by God, and therefore one cannot be held culpable for his misdeeds. Job said before God: Master of the Universe, You created the ox with split hooves, making it kosher, and You created the donkey with closed hooves, making it forbidden; You created the Garden of Eden, and You created Gehenna; and similarly, You created righteous people and You created wicked people; who can restrain You? Seeing that You created people as either righteous or wicked, You cannot later complain about their actions.**

Rava continues the analysis. The verse *al da'at'cha ki lo ersha v'ein mi-yadcha matzil* — on your account I have not done evil, and none can deliver from your hand — yields the following exposition: Iyov was trying to exempt the entire world from judgment. He said: *barata shor parsotav sdukot, barata chamor parsotav klutot* — you created the ox with split hooves, you created the donkey with an uncloven hoof. Everything is simply the product of divine creation. *Barata Gan Eden barata Gehennom, barata tzaddikim barata resha'im* — you created the righteous and you created the wicked. No one truly chooses; everyone is merely what God made them. The phrase *al da'at'cha* — on your account, according to your design — means: it is all predetermined, and judgment is therefore incoherent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אַהְדַּרוּ לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ [דְּאִיּוֹב] – ״אַף אַתָּה תָּפֵר יִרְאָה, וְתִגְרַע שִׂיחָה לִפְנֵי אֵל״; בָּרָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יֵצֶר הָרָע – בָּרָא לוֹ תּוֹרָה תַּבְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And how did Job's friends answer him? "You do away with fear, and impair devotion before God" with such statements. True, the Holy One, Blessed be He, created the evil inclination, but He also created the Torah as an antidote to counter its effects and prevent it from gaining control of a person.**

Iyov's friends respond, as quoted by the Gemara: *af ata tifer yira v'tigra sicha lifnei El* — you are dismantling fear of God with your words, you are taking away from the proper attitude before the Almighty. And the response to Iyov's position is: it is true that God created the *yetzer hara*, but *bara lo Torah tavlin* — he also gave the Torah as the remedy. The *yetzer hara* is part of our nature; the Torah gives us the capacity to address it. We make choices. The existence of the evil inclination does not eliminate human agency; it is the context within which human agency is exercised.

### Widow's Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״בִּרְכַּת אֹבֵד עָלַי תָּבֹא, וְלֵב אַלְמָנָה אַרְנִן״? ״בִּרְכַּת אֹבֵד עָלַי תָּבֹא״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה גּוֹזֵל שָׂדֶה מִיְּתוֹמִים, וּמַשְׁבִּיחָהּ וּמַחְזִירָהּ לָהֶן״. ״וְלֵב אַלְמָנָה אַרְנִן״ – דְּכׇל הֵיכָא דַּהֲוָה אִיכָּא אַלְמָנָה דְּלָא הֲווֹ נָסְבִי לַהּ, הֲוָה אָזֵיל שָׁדֵי שְׁמֵיהּ עִילָּוַוהּ, וַהֲווֹ אָתוּ נָסְבִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava interpreted a verse homiletically: What is the meaning of that which is written, Job saying about himself: "The blessing of him that was lost came upon me, and I caused the widow's heart to sing for joy"? "The blessing of him that was lost came upon me" teaches that Job used to steal a field from orphans, cultivate it, improve it, and then return it to them; consequently, they would bless him for the field they had lost. "I caused the widow's heart to sing for joy" teaches that anywhere that there was a widow whom no one would marry, he would go and cast his name upon her, i.e., he would start a rumor that she was related to him, and then somebody would come forward and marry her.**

A *derasha* of Rava provides an aside on Iyov's charitable deeds, illuminating the verse *birkat oved alai tavo v'leiv almana arni* — the blessing of the perishing shall come upon me, and the heart of the widow I made sing. The first clause, *birkat oved*, teaches that Iyov had a distinctive way of helping orphans: he would take possession of their field — *gozel sadeh mi-yetomim* — improve it — *u-mashpichah* — and then return it to them. From the orphan's perspective, it looked as though the field had been lost; in the end it was returned improved. The orphan was the *oved* — the one who seemed to have lost — and the experience ultimately became a *berachah*.

The second clause, *leiv almana*, describes how Iyov would assist widows who could not find a husband: he would attach his name to a widow — perhaps creating the impression of his interest in her, or vouching for her family standing — so that others would come forward to marry her, and her heart would sing.

### Equal to God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לוּ שָׁקוֹל יִשָּׁקֵל כַּעְשִׂי וְהַוָּתִי, בְּמֹאזְנַיִם יִשְׂאוּ יָחַד״ – אָמַר רַב: עַפְרָא לְפוּמֵּיהּ דְּאִיּוֹב, חַבְרוּתָא כְּלַפֵּי שְׁמַיָּא! ״לוּ יֵשׁ בֵּינֵינוּ מוֹכִיחַ, יָשֵׁת יָדוֹ עַל שְׁנֵינוּ״ – אָמַר רַב: עַפְרָא לְפוּמֵּיהּ דְּאִיּוֹב, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ עֶבֶד שֶׁמּוֹכִיחַ אֶת רַבּוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** Job further said: **"O that my vexation were thoroughly weighed, and my calamity laid in the balances"** Rav says: Dust should be put **in the mouth of Job, meaning, he should not have spoken in such a manner, as if he were weighing his deeds against those of God; may one act as if he is in a friendship with Heaven? And similarly, Job said: "There is no arbiter between us, who may lay his hand upon us both."** Rav says: Dust should be put **in the mouth of Job** for saying this; **is there a servant who rebukes his master?**

Rava focuses on the verse *lu yishkol ka'asi va-havati b'moznayim yis'u yachad* — would that my vexation were weighed, and my anguish placed in the scales together. The key word is *yachad* — together — implying that Iyov was placing himself and God on the same side of the scale, demanding that they be weighed as equals. Rava responds: *afra l'fumeih d'Iyov* — dirt in the mouth of Iyov. *Chavruta klapei Shamaya?* — you think you're on equal footing with heaven? This is a different kind of objection from those that preceded it. When Iyov questioned whether God's justice was consistent, or when he denied moral causation, the Gemara did not invoke this level of condemnation. But placing oneself on equal standing with God — that is the crossing of a line.

The distinction the Gemara is drawing is between legitimate theological struggle and an attitude of presumptuous equality. Avraham engaged God in argument over Sodom, but prefaced it with *v'anochi afar va-efer* — I am dust and ashes. The questioning was not from a position of equality but from a position of profound humility. Iyov's *yachad* — let us be weighed together — implies a parity that is theologically impermissible.

The verse continues: *lu yesh beineinu mochiach yashet yado al shneinu* — if only there were an arbiter between us to place his hand upon us both. Again: *shneinu* — the two of us, as equals. Rava repeats: *afra l'fumeih d'Iyov. Klum yesh eved she-mochiach et rabo?* — is there ever a servant who adjudicates against his master? The problem is not the questioning per se; it is the tone, the posture of equivalence.

### Resurrection Denial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כָּלָה עָנָן וַיֵּלַךְ, כֵּן יוֹרֵד שְׁאוֹל לֹא יַעֲלֶה״ – אָמַר רָבָא: מִכָּאן שֶׁכָּפַר אִיּוֹב בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** Job further said: **"As the cloud is consumed and vanishes away, so he who goes down to the grave shall come up no more."** Rava says: From here it may be inferred **that Job denied the resurrection of the dead.**

Rava identifies one further heresy in Iyov's words: *kalah anan va-yelech ken yored she'ol lo ya'aleh* — as the cloud dissipates and vanishes, so one who descends to the grave shall not rise. Rava says: *mikan she-kafar Iyov bi-tchiyat ha-meitim* — here we see that Iyov denied the resurrection of the dead. Those who descend shall not come back up.

This too is now placed alongside the other items being extracted from beneath the surface of Iyov's words — the denial of moral causation, the presumption of equality with God, and now the denial of *tchiyat ha-meitim*.

## God's Answer

### Whirlwind Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׂעָרָה יְשׁוּפֵנִי, וְהִרְבָּה פְצָעַי חִנָּם״ – אָמַר רַבָּה: אִיּוֹב בִּסְעָרָה חֵרַף, וּבִסְעָרָה הֱשִׁיבוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** He also stated: **"He crushes me with a tempest, and multiplies my wounds without cause."** Rabba says: Job blasphemed with mention of **a tempest and he was answered with mention of a tempest.**

Rava observes: *Iyov bi-se'arah charaf u-vi-se'arah heshivuhu* — Iyov blasphemed with a whirlwind, and was responded to in a whirlwind. The reference to Iyov's blasphemy is the verse *asher bi-se'arah yeshufeni* — who sweeps me away with a whirlwind. The Gemara provides the expanded *midrashic* reading of what Iyov was really saying: *Ribbono shel Olam, shema ruach se'arah avrah lefanecha v'nitchalef lecha bein Iyov le-Oyev* — Master of the Universe, perhaps a stormy wind passed before you and confused you — perhaps you mixed up *Iyov* with *oyev*, you thought I was your enemy and that is why you afflicted me. The wordplay on *Iyov* and *oyev* — Job and enemy — is the vehicle for the blasphemy: you have been mistaken, God; you don't know what you're doing. This is the *chiref* — the blaspheming — referenced by Rava.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּסְעָרָה חֵרַף – דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׂעָרָה יְשׁוּפֵנִי״; אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שֶׁמָּא רוּחַ סְעָרָה עָבְרָה לְפָנֶיךָ, וְנִתְחַלֵּף לְךָ בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב? בִּסְעָרָה הֱשִׁיבוּהוּ – דִּכְתִיב: וַיַּעַן ה׳ אֶת אִיּוֹב מִן הַסְּעָרָה, וַיֹּאמַר: [וְגוֹ׳] אֱזׇר נָא כְגֶבֶר חֲלָצֶיךָ, אֶשְׁאָלְךָ וְהוֹדִיעֵנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: **He blasphemed with mention of a tempest [*bise'ara*], as it is written: "He crushes me with a tempest."** Job **said before God: Master of the Universe, perhaps a tempest passed before You and You confused *Iyov*, Job, with *oyev*, enemy. He was answered with mention of a tempest, as it is written: "Then the Lord answered Job out of the tempest, and said:** Who is this that darkens counsel by words without knowledge? **Gird up now your loins like a man, for I will demand of you and let me know your answer."**

God responds in kind, also from within a whirlwind: *va-ya'an Hashem et Iyov min ha-se'arah vayomar, azor na k'gibor chalatzecha, esh'al'cha v'hodi'eni* — gird your loins like a mighty man, I will ask you and you will inform me.

### Hair Follicles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הַרְבֵּה נִימִין בָּרָאתִי בְּאָדָם; וְכׇל נִימָא וְנִימָא בָּרָאתִי לָהּ גּוּמָּא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ שְׁתַּיִם יוֹנְקוֹת מִגּוּמָּא אַחַת; שֶׁאִלְמָלֵי שְׁתַּיִם יוֹנְקוֹת מִגּוּמָּא אַחַת – מַחְשִׁיכוֹת מְאוֹר עֵינָיו שֶׁל אָדָם. בֵּין גּוּמָּה לְגּוּמָּה לֹא נִתְחַלֵּף לִי; בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב נִתְחַלֵּף לִי?!
>
> **Translation:** God **said to him: I have created many hairs [*nimin*] on a person, and for each hair I created its own follicle through which the hair is sustained, so that two hairs should not draw from one follicle. As were two hairs to draw from one follicle, they would impair a man's vision. Now, if I do not confuse one follicle with another, would I confuse *Iyov* with *oyev*?**

God's response takes the form of demonstrating the depth of divine knowledge and providence through a series of examples from the natural world. The first play on the word *se'arah* — storm — converts it to *sa'ar* — hair. God says: *harbe nimin barati ba-adam* — I have made many hairs on the human being. *V'chol nima v'nima barati lah gumah bifnei atzmah* — every individual hair I created its own follicle, so that no two hairs draw from the same follicle. Why? *She-imale shtayim yonkot mi-gumah achat machshichot me'or einav shel adam* — if two hairs were drawing from a single follicle they would darken a person's eyesight.

*Bein gumah le-gumah lo nitchalef li, bein Iyov le-oyev nitchalef li?* — if I can distinguish between one follicle and the next, do you think I would confuse Iyov with an enemy? The grandeur of the argument is in its very specificity: the God who governs the microscopic logistics of human hair growth certainly knows the difference between the righteous and the wicked.

### Raindrops & Thunder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִי פִלַּג לַשֶּׁטֶף תְּעָלָה [וְגוֹ׳]״ – הַרְבֵּה טִיפִּין בָּרָאתִי בֶּעָבִים, וְכׇל טִיפָּה וְטִיפָּה בָּרָאתִי לָהּ דְּפוּס בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ שְׁתֵּי טִיפִּין יוֹצְאוֹת מִדְּפוּס אֶחָד; שֶׁאִלְמָלֵי שְׁתֵּי טִיפִּין יוֹצְאוֹת מִדְּפוּס אֶחָד – מְטַשְׁטְשׁוֹת אֶת הָאָרֶץ וְאֵינָהּ מוֹצִיאָהּ פֵּירוֹת. בֵּין טִיפָּה לְטִיפָּה לֹא נִתְחַלֵּף לִי; בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב נִתְחַלֵּף לִי?!
>
> **Translation:** God further said to Job: **"Who has divided a channel [*te'ala*] for the torrent of rain, or a path for the lightning of thunder"?** I have created many drops of water **in the clouds, and for each drop I created its own channel, so that two drops should not emerge from the same channel. As were two drops to emerge from the same channel they would destroy the earth and it would not yield produce. Now, if I do not confuse one drop with another, would I confuse *Iyov* with *oyev*?**

The same demonstration is extended to the natural phenomena of rain and thunder. *Harbe tipin barati be-avim* — I made many drops in the clouds. *V'chol tipah v'tipah barati lah dfus bifnei atzmah* — every single drop has its own mold, its own form. The Gemara derives the meaning of the word *te'alah* as *dfus* — mold or form — from a verse where *te'alah* appears in the context of a constructed trench or channel. If two drops were to emerge from the same mold, they would completely destroy the land and render it incapable of producing fruit.

*Bein tipah le-tipah lo nitchalef li, bein Iyov le-oyev nitchalef li?*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְדֶרֶךְ לַחֲזִיז קֹלוֹת״ – הַרְבֵּה קוֹלוֹת בָּרָאתִי בֶּעָבִים, וְכׇל קוֹל וְקוֹל בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁבִיל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, כְּדַי שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ שְׁתֵּי קוֹלוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת מִשְּׁבִיל אֶחָד; שֶׁאִלְמָלֵי שְׁתֵּי קוֹלוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת מִשְּׁבִיל אֶחָד – מַחֲרִיבִין אֶת כׇּל הָעוֹלָם. בֵּין קוֹל לְקוֹל לֹא נִתְחַלֵּף לִי, בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב נִתְחַלֵּף לִי?!
>
> **Translation:** The second half of the aforementioned verse in Job states: **"Or a path for the lightning of thunder,"** which is interpreted as follows: God said: **I have created many thunderclaps in the clouds, and for each and every thunderclap I created its own path, so that two thunderclaps should not issue forth from the same path. As were two thunderclaps to issue from the same path, they would destroy the world. Now, if I do not confuse one thunderclap with another, would I confuse *Iyov* with *oyev*?**

Similarly with thunder: *harbe kolot barati be-avim* — I made many sounds in the clouds. *V'chol kol v'kol barati lo shvil bifnei atzmo* — every thunderclap has its own path through the sky. If two sounds were to travel the same path they would destroy the world. *Bein kol le-kol lo nitchalef li, bein Iyov le-oyev nitchalef li?*

### Ibex & Eagle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲיָדַעְתָּ עֵת לֶדֶת יַעֲלֵי סָלַע, חֹלֵל אַיָּלוֹת תִּשְׁמֹר״ – יְעֵלָה זוֹ אַכְזָרִית עַל בָּנֶיהָ, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכּוֹרַעַת לָלֶדֶת, עוֹלָה לְרֹאשׁ הָהָר כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפּוֹל מִמֶּנָּה וְיָמוּת; וַאֲנִי מַזְמִין לָהּ נֶשֶׁר שֶׁמְּקַבְּלוֹ בִּכְנָפָיו וּמַנִּיחוֹ לְפָנֶיהָ, וְאִלְמָלֵי מַקְדִּים רֶגַע אֶחָד אוֹ מִתְאַחֵר רֶגַע אֶחָד – מִיָּד מֵת. בֵּין רֶגַע לְרֶגַע לֹא נִתְחַלֵּף לִי, בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב נִתְחַלֵּף לִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **"Do you know when the wild goats of the rock give birth? Can you mark when the hinds do calve?"** This goat is cruel to her young and shows them no pity; when she squats to give birth she ascends to the top of a mountain so that the kid should fall down from her and die. And I summon her an eagle that receives it with his wings and places it before her; and if the eagle reached her one moment early or was one moment late, the kid would immediately die. Now, if I do not confuse one moment with another moment, would I confuse *Iyov* with *oyev*?**

God then turns to the animal kingdom. *Ha-yadeta et ledet ya'alei sela* — do you know the time when the mountain ibex gives birth? The *ya'alah* is described as *achzarit al baneha* — cruel to her offspring. When she crouches to give birth she ascends to the mountaintop, so that the newborn falls from the cliff and would perish. What does God do? *Mazmin lah nesher she-mekablo bi-chanafav u-minicho lefaneha* — I send an eagle that catches the falling newborn in its wings and places it gently before the mother.

*V'ilmale makdim rega echad o me'acher rega echad miyad met* — if the eagle arrives even one minute too early or one minute too late, the newborn dies. *Bein rega le-rega lo nitchalef li, bein Iyov le-oyev nitchalef li?*

### Hind's Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חֹלֵל אַיָּלוֹת תִּשְׁמֹר״ – אַיָּלָה זוֹ רַחְמָהּ צַר; בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכּוֹרַעַת לָלֶדֶת, אֲנִי מַזְמִין לָהּ דְּרָקוֹן שֶׁמַּכִּישָׁהּ בְּבֵית הָרֶחֶם, וּמִתְרַפָּה מִמּוֹלָדָהּ; וְאִלְמָלֵי מַקְדִּים רֶגַע אֶחָד אוֹ מְאַחֵר רֶגַע אֶחָד מִיָּד מֵתָה. בֵּין רֶגַע לְרֶגַע לֹא נִתְחַלֵּף לִי, בֵּין אִיּוֹב לְאוֹיֵב נִתְחַלֵּף לִי?!
>
> **Translation:** Similarly: **"Can you mark when the hinds do calve?"** The womb of this hind is narrow, which makes for a difficult delivery. When she squats to give birth, I summon her a snake [*derakon*] that bites her at the opening of the womb, which then becomes loose, and she gives birth, and if the snake reached her one moment early or was one moment late, she would immediately die. Now, if I do not confuse one moment with another moment, would I confuse *Iyov* with *oyev*?**

The analogy is then given for the hind in childbirth. The hind's womb is narrow. When she crouches to give birth, God sends a snake — *drakon* — that bites her at the womb, causing it to open and allow the birth. *V'ilmale makdim rega echad o me'acher rega echad miyad metah* — if the timing is off by even a minute, she dies in childbirth. *Bein regel le-regel lo nitchalef li, bein Iyov le-oyev nitchalef li?*

### Omniscience

The unified thrust of all these examples is that God manages the universe with a precision that goes down to the individual hair follicle, the individual raindrop, the individual thunderclap, the individual moment of an animal's birth. The one who orchestrates all of this with such perfect particularity cannot be confused about the moral standing of a human being. The challenge of Iyov — that perhaps God confused him with an enemy — is refuted not by a theological argument about divine nature but by the sheer evidence of divine care in the created order.

## Friends Arrive

### Spoke Thoughtlessly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״[אִיּוֹב] לֹא בְדַעַת יְדַבֵּר, וּדְבָרָיו לֹא בְהַשְׂכֵּל״; וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לֹא דִבַּרְתֶּם אֵלַי נְכוֹנָה כְּעַבְדִּי אִיּוֹב״! אָמַר רָבָא: מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין אָדָם נִתְפָּס בִּשְׁעַת צַעֲרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: On the one hand, the text states: **"Job has spoken without knowledge, and his words were without wisdom."** But on the other hand, **it is written** with regard to Job's friends: **"You have not spoken of Me the thing that is right, like my servant Job."** Rava said: From here it may be inferred **that a person is not held responsible for what he says when he is in distress.**

After the divine response, the text says: *Iyov lo veda'at yedaber u-vi-dvarav lo ve-haskel* — Iyov spoke without knowledge, and without understanding in his words. Rava derives from here: *mikan she-ein adam nitpas bi-sha'at tza'aro* — a person cannot be held responsible for what he says in his hour of anguish. After all the analysis that has been devoted to excavating the heresies beneath Iyov's words, the divine verdict is not condemnation but an acknowledgment that he was speaking from a place of pain, not of considered theological reflection. God does not say that Iyov sinned grievously; he says that Iyov spoke without knowledge. The tradition therefore establishes that one who speaks out in the midst of acute suffering is not held to the same standard of accountability as one who speaks deliberately.

### Crowns & Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּשְׁמְעוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת רֵעֵי אִיּוֹב אֵת כׇּל הָרָעָה הַזֹּאת הַבָּאָה עָלָיו, וַיָּבֹאוּ אִישׁ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ – אֱלִיפַז הַתֵּימָנִי וּבִלְדַּד הַשּׁוּחִי וְצוֹפַר הַנַּעֲמָתִי, וַיִּוָּעֲדוּ יַחְדָּו לָבוֹא לָנוּד לוֹ וּלְנַחֲמוֹ״ – מַאי ״וַיִּוָּעֲדוּ יַחְדָּו״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ כּוּלָּן בְּשַׁעַר אֶחָד. וְתָנָא: בֵּין כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת פַּרְסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Job's three friends heard of all this evil that was come upon him, they came every one from his own place, Eliphaz the Temanite, and Bildad the Shuhite, and Zophar the Naamathite; for they had made an appointment together to come to mourn with him and to comfort him."** What does **"they had made an appointment together"** mean? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: This** phrase **teaches that they all entered** through **one gate** at the same time. **And** a Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: There were **three hundred parasangs between each and every one** of them.

The final section of the narrative brings Iyov's three friends onto the scene. *Va-yishme'u sheloshet rei'ei Iyov et kol ha-ra'ah ha-zot ha-ba'ah eilav* — Iyov's three friends heard of all the evil that had befallen him — *Eliphaz the Temanite, Bildad the Shuhite, and Zophar the Naamathite* — and they gathered to come to mourn with him and to comfort him.

*Va-yivo'adu yachdav* — they assembled together. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav expounds: *melamed she-nichnasu kulam be-sha'ar echad* — they all entered through the same gate. And a *Tanna* teaches that they each lived 300 *parsaos* apart from one another and yet all managed to arrive at the same moment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: כְּלִילָא הֲוָה לְהוּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִילָנֵי הֲוָה לְהוּ, וְכֵיוָן דְּכָמְשִׁי הֲווֹ יָדְעִי. אָמַר רָבָא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אוֹ חַבְרָא כְּחַבְרֵי דְּאִיּוֹב, אוֹ מִיתוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How did they** all **know** at the same time what had happened to Job so that the three of them came together? **There are** those **who say** that **they** each **had a crown** which displayed certain signs when something happened to one of the others. **And there are** those **who say they** each **had trees and when** the trees **withered they knew** that sorrow had visited one of them. **Rava said** that **this** closeness between Job and his friends explains the adage **that people say: Either a friend like the friends of Job or death.**

How did they know what was happening? The Gemara offers two traditions. One says that each of them possessed a crown — *klila* — engraved with the faces of the others, and Rashi adds that each face had the person's name inscribed upon it. When one of the companions was suffering, *partzufo mishtane* — the face on the crown would change, signaling distress. The other tradition says they each had a tree that represented the friend, and when the tree withered, they knew.

Rava cites a popular saying: *o chavra k'chavrei d'Iyov o mituta* — either friends like the friends of Iyov, or death. The point is that the friendship of Iyov's companions was of such extraordinary quality that it becomes the standard by which friendship is measured. They were so attuned to one another that they knew across hundreds of *parsaos* when one of them was suffering, and they traveled immediately to be present. The saying does not romanticize what happened after they arrived — the debates between Iyov and his friends occupy most of the book, and their theology is ultimately rebuked by God — but their act of solidarity, of dropping everything and coming, is held up as the model of true *chavra*.
