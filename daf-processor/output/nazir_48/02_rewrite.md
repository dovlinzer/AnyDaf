# Nazir 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol & Nazir

### Meis Mitzvah Override

The daf opens in the middle of 48a, approximately fifteen lines down, at the margin notation *Bamidbar* 6. The sugya is concerned with establishing the source for a striking principle: the prohibitions against *tumah* incumbent upon a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir* are each exceptionally severe and categorical — neither may become *tamei* even to his closest relatives — and yet both prohibitions are overridden when a *meis mitzvah* is at stake. The question the *gemara* sets out to answer is: what is the textual and logical basis for this override?

### Kohen Gadol Source

By the *kohen gadol*, the Torah states: *al kol nafshos meis lo yavo, l'aviv u'l'imo lo yitama* — he shall not enter upon any dead soul, and shall not become *tamei* for his father or mother. The *gemara*, as framed by *Tosafos*, reasons as follows: since a *kohen gadol* is already a standard *kohen*, he is already forbidden to become *tamei* to non-relatives by virtue of the general *kohen* prohibition. The phrase *al kol nafshos meis lo yavo* need not therefore be read as restricting him from non-relatives — that is already known. Rather, it must be addressing relatives. And if relatives are already implicit in the opening clause, what purpose does the separate phrase *l'aviv u'l'imo lo yitama* serve? Since a prohibition followed by a further limiting phrase — a *miyut achar miyut* — implies an implicit permission, we derive that while for his father he is prohibited, a *meis mitzvah* overrides even this. The word *aviv* teaches no to relatives but yes to *meis mitzvah*. And the word *imo* is used to link — through a *gezeirah shavah* — to the corresponding passage in the laws of *nazir*, establishing that the prohibition applies only to *tumat meis* and not to other forms of *tumah* such as *tzara'as* or *zivah*.

### Kal Vachomer Attempt

With the *kohen gadol*'s exception established, the *gemara* turns to the *nazir*: how do we know that a *nazir*'s prohibition is similarly overridden for a *meis mitzvah*? One might have proposed a *kal v'chomer*: if a *kohen gadol*, whose sanctity is permanent and eternal, may become *tamei* for a *meis mitzvah*, then a *nazir*, whose sanctity is limited in time and assumed voluntarily, should certainly be overridden for a *meis mitzvah*. However, the *gemara* rejects this inference. The *kal v'chomer* can be reversed: a *kohen gadol* who becomes *tamei* intentionally violates a prohibition and receives lashes, but there is no *korban* associated with his violation. A *nazir* who becomes *tamei*, by contrast, must bring a *korban* and restart his *nezirut*. The *tumah* carries far heavier practical consequences for the *nazir*, and so one might argue that his *kedushah* is more precarious rather than less severe, and that a *meis mitzvah* therefore would not override it. The *kal v'chomer* fails, and a *pasuk* is needed.

## Nazir Braita Drasha

### Nefesh Meis Analysis

The *gemara* transitions to the laws of *nazir* and introduces a *braita*. The relevant verse states: *al nefesh meis lo yavo* — the *nazir* shall not enter upon a dead soul — followed by *l'aviv u'l'imo l'achiv u'l'achoso lo yitama lahem b'mosam*. The *braita* begins by asking whether the phrase *nefesh meis* might include even an animal carcass, since an animal is also called a *nefesh*, as in the verse *makeh nefesh behemah*. The *braita* resolves this by noting that a dead animal is called a *neveilah*, not a *nefesh meis* — *nefesh meis* denotes a human corpse specifically.

### Tanna Kamma vs Yishmael

The *tanna kamma* derives this restriction from the word *meis* itself: the verse speaks of *nefesh adam*, a human being. Rabbi Yishmael disagrees and says there is no need to derive it from the word *meis*. The verse says *lo yavo* — he shall not enter the same space — not merely *lo yiga*, he shall not touch. The only form of *tumah* that operates through *biah*, through being under the same roof without direct contact, is *tumat meis* of a human corpse. All other forms of *tumah* operate only through touch or carrying. Since the verse prohibits *lo yavo*, it is implicitly addressing only human corpses: *benefashot hamitamot b'viah hakatuv medaber* — the verse speaks of those corpses that convey *tumah* through entry into the same space. One who enters the same room as the corpse, even without touching, becomes *tamei* — and this is true only of human corpses. Whether this extends to non-Jewish corpses with respect to *tumat ohel* is itself a matter of *machloket* among the *tannaim*, but for Jewish corpses there is no question.

### Aviv v'Imo Signal

With this established, the *braita* proceeds: *l'aviv u'l'imo eino mitamei aval mitamei hu l'meis mitzvah* — for his father and mother he does not become *tamei*, but for a *meis mitzvah* he does. The words *aviv* and *imo* are understood as carrying a surplus meaning. Before explaining the precise function of each word, the *braita* notes that one might have thought the exception for *meis mitzvah* could be derived by logic alone: *ad shelo yomar yesh li badin* — even before the verse says so, I could have reasoned it out.

### Why Not Kal Vachomer

The logic would be a *kal v'chomer*: a *kohen gadol* whose sanctity is permanent — *kedushato kedushat olam* — may nonetheless become *tamei* for a *meis mitzvah*. A *nazir* whose sanctity is inherently time-limited — *she'ein kedushato kedushat olam* — should certainly be overridden for a *meis mitzvah*. But as already noted, the *kal v'chomer* can be deflected: a *kohen gadol* does not bring a *korban* for his *tumah*, whereas a *nazir* brings a *korban* and must restart entirely. This counter-argument — *lo im amarta b'kohen gadol she'kein eino meivi korban al tumato* — is sufficient to defeat the *kal v'chomer*.

The *braita* next considers another possible interpretation of *l'aviv u'l'imo*. Perhaps these words are not teaching that relatives are forbidden while *meis mitzvah* is permitted. Perhaps the reverse is intended: for his father and mother he is forbidden, but for other corpses — non-relatives — he is permitted? This *hava amina* has a certain logic: with relatives, the emotional pull is so strong that any boundary might dissolve. With strangers, there is less risk of overreach, so perhaps only relatives are restricted. However, the *gemara* dismisses this reading via a *kal v'chomer* from the ordinary *kohen*: even a standard *kohen hedyot*, whose level of *kedushah* is far lower, is *tamei* to relatives but not to non-relatives — relatives are the easier case for a *kohen*, not the harder one. If a lower-grade *kohen* may become *tamei* for relatives but not for non-relatives, then certainly a *nazir*, who may not become *tamei* even for relatives, cannot be *tamei* for non-relatives. So *l'aviv u'l'imo* is not telling us non-relatives are permitted.

What then is the function of these words? *L'aviv u'l'imo she'eino mitamei aval mitamei hu l'meis mitzvah* — they are extra, and their surplus signals the permission of *meis mitzvah*.

A difficulty remains, however: for the *kohen gadol*, the opening clause was already understood as referring to relatives because he is already a standard *kohen* and is obviously forbidden to non-relatives. The extra words therefore pointed to *meis mitzvah*. But for a *nazir*, there is no such prior prohibition against non-relatives. A *nazir* does not begin from a baseline of being forbidden to any corpse. Therefore, the opening clause *al nefesh meis lo yavo* might itself be needed to establish the prohibition against non-relatives, leaving no surplus at all. If the opening clause addresses non-relatives, then *aviv* is needed to teach relatives, and there is nothing left over for *meis mitzvah*.

### Gezeirah Shavah Setup

The *gemara* works through this carefully. Ultimately it concludes: *aviv* teaches that relatives are forbidden — that is its function for the *nazir*, in contrast to the *kohen gadol* where the same role was played by the opening clause. And *imo* is reserved for the *gezeirah shavah* connecting the *nazir*'s verse to the *kohen gadol*'s verse on the matter of *b'mosam* — teaching that the prohibition applies only to *tumat meis* and not to other *tumot*. That leaves *achiv* — "his brother" — as the word that teaches *meis mitzvah* is permitted. And *achoso* remains to teach something further, which we will reach shortly. The *gemara* thus maps the words as follows for the *tanna kamma* (Chachamim) approach: the opening *al nefesh meis* establishes non-relatives; *aviv* establishes relatives; *imo* functions in the *gezeirah shavah* establishing that only *tumat meis* is covered; *achiv* teaches yes to *meis mitzvah*; and *achoso* teaches something additional.

It is worth noting that the entire structure of this *sugya* is built around an interplay between the authority of the text and the authority of human reasoning. The very phrase *ad shelo yomar yesh li badin* — I could have reasoned it out even without the verse — reflects a principle found elsewhere in the *gemara*: *lama li kra, sevara hu* — why do I need a verse, this is logical. The verse is needed only where the logic is genuinely ambiguous. Where the conclusion follows clearly from reason, a verse would be superfluous.

## Meis Mitzvah Application

### Pesach & Milah Case

The *braita* now takes up the function of *achoso* — "his sister" — and what that word comes to teach. *U'l'achoso mah talmud lomar?* A case is presented: someone is on his way to slaughter his *korban Pesach* or to perform a *bris milah* for his son — both of which are *mitzvos aseh* whose violation carries *kares* — and he hears that a relative has died. Must he interrupt what he is doing and become *tamei* to attend to the burial? The instinct might be that yes, the obligation to bury relatives is powerful, perhaps powerful enough to override these other *mitzvos*.

### Tosafot Weighing

*Tosafos* in the discussion of *u'l'achoso mah talmud lomar* clarifies the scope of this rule: it applies even to a regular person — not a *kohen gadol* and not a *nazir* — who is in the process of performing *Pesach* or *milah*. *Tosafos* explains: *lo ati aseh d'tumah she'ein bah kares, d'dachi aseh d'Pesach u'milah she'yesh bahem kares* — a positive commandment to become *tamei* for a relative, which is a *mitzvah* without *kares*, does not override a weightier positive commandment associated with *kares*. One might have thought that the obligation to attend to a deceased relative, given how seriously the *gemara* treats *meis mitzvah*, would override even these powerful *mitzvos*. The *chidush* here is that it does not — at least in a situation where others are available to attend to the deceased, so that the relative does not become a *meis mitzvah*. If the person himself abandons *Pesach* or *milah* to attend to the burial, he violates the principle. This is a significant ruling given the general weight we assign to caring for the deceased.

### Achoso Drasha

But the verse then continues: *yachol lo yitamei l'meis mitzvah* — perhaps even in the case of a *meis mitzvah* he should not become *tamei*, even while performing other *mitzvos*? The answer: *talmud lomar achoso* — the word *achoso* teaches that by a *meis mitzvah*, he does become *tamei*, and moreover, *meis mitzvah* overrides even his being actively engaged (*osek*) in the *mitzvos* of *korban Pesach* and *milah*. *Achoso* therefore teaches the outer limit of *meis mitzvah*'s power: not only does it override the *kedushah* of a *nazir* and a *kohen gadol*, it overrides one's active engagement in these other critical *mitzvos*.

## Tanna Kamma & Akiva

### Tanna Kamma Method

To summarize the *tanna kamma*'s (Chachamim's) *drasha*: *aviv* establishes that relatives are forbidden; *imo* serves the *gezeirah shavah* to establish that only *tumat meis* is at issue (not other *tumot*); *achiv* teaches that *meis mitzvah* overrides; *achoso* teaches that *meis mitzvah* overrides even when one is *osek* in *Pesach* and *milah*.

### Akiva's Opening

Rabbi Akiva offers an alternative *drasha*. He says: *nefesh ilu ha'rechokim, meis ilu ha'kerovim* — within the opening clause *al nefesh meis lo yavo*, the word *nefesh* already addresses non-relatives, and the word *meis* addresses relatives. Two separate words, two separate categories. This means that by the time we reach the subsequent listing of *aviv, imo, achiv, achoso*, we have already excluded both non-relatives and relatives from any permission, and the words that follow are entirely free to teach other things. Consequently, Rabbi Akiva does not need *aviv* to prohibit relatives — he has already accomplished that from the opening clause.

### Akiva Aviv, Imo, Achiv

For Rabbi Akiva, *l'aviv u'l'imo eino mitamei aval mitamei hu l'meis mitzvah* — both *aviv* and *imo* together teach that *meis mitzvah* is permitted. This immediately raises a question: why are both words needed to convey a single ruling? The *gemara* will address this. Rabbi Akiva's use of both *aviv* and *imo* for *meis mitzvah* also creates a problem for the *gezeirah shavah* — if *imo* is occupied here, how can it simultaneously serve as the connecting word for the *gezeirah shavah* establishing that the prohibition applies only to *tumat meis*? That too will be addressed. *Achiv* and *achoso*, now freed from their earlier roles, are available for further teachings.

## Akiva Achiv & Achoso

### Akiva Achiv

For Rabbi Akiva, *achiv* teaches the following: *she'yehei kohen gadol v'hu nazir, l'achiv eino mitamei aval mitamei hu l'meis mitzvah* — this refers to a person who is both a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir* simultaneously. We already know from Rabbi Akiva's system that *meis mitzvah* overrides a *kohen gadol* alone, and that it overrides a *nazir* alone. But what of a person carrying both statuses at once, a double accumulation of prohibitions?

### Achiv Necessity

One might have thought that although a *meis mitzvah* overrides a single *lav*, it would not override two simultaneous *lavin*. Perhaps the quantitative increase in the number of prohibitions tips the scales. *Achiv* therefore comes to teach that *meis mitzvah* overrides even this double status: *mah li chad lav, mah li trei lavin* — whether there is one *lav* or two *lavin*, the *Torah*'s dispensation for *meis mitzvah* applies equally.

---

## Gezeirah Shavah Logic

### Problem Setup

This creates an apparent difficulty for Rabbi Akiva. If both *aviv* and *imo* are together needed to teach that *meis mitzvah* is permitted for a *nazir*, then neither word is free at either end of the analogy, since they are both actively employed in the verse about the *nazir*. Equally, up at the verse of the *kohen gadol*, both words would presumably also be needed to teach *meis mitzvah* there. If so, *imo* is not free at either end, and the *gezeirah shavah* between the two passages — which requires at least one word to be free — cannot be constructed.

### Solution: Already Known

The *gemara* answers concisely, and *Tosafos* elaborates: *kivan d'amar mar im hayah kohen gadol v'nazir l'achiv eino mitamei aval mitamei hu l'meis mitzvah, mah li kohen gadol lachudei, mah li nazir v'kohen gadol* — since Rabbi Akiva's own *drasha* on *achiv* already teaches that *meis mitzvah* overrides a person who is simultaneously a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir*, we have thereby already established that *meis mitzvah* overrides the *kohen gadol* as well. Once *meis mitzvah*'s override of the *kohen gadol* is already known from *achiv*, the word *imo* in the *kohen gadol*'s verse no longer needs to convey that ruling — it is freed. And once it is free at that end, the *gezeirah shavah* can be constructed even without the word being free at both ends. *Imo* is therefore available to serve the *gezeirah shavah* linking the *nazir* and the *kohen gadol* on the restriction of *tumat meis* exclusively.

---

## Akiva Achiv & Achoso (II)

### Akiva Achoso

Having established the function of *achiv* in Rabbi Akiva's system, the remaining word is *achoso*. For Rabbi Akiva, where *achoso* is no longer needed to teach the double-status case (already derived from *achiv*), *achoso* retains the role it plays in the *tanna kamma*'s system: teaching that *meis mitzvah* overrides even when one is actively engaged in other *mitzvos*, namely *korban Pesach* and *milah*. As the *braita* had stated: even if one is already on the way to perform these time-sensitive *mitzvos aseh she'yesh bahem kares*, if a *meis mitzvah* arises, he must stop and attend to it. And by way of parallel — for the *tanna kamma* (*Chachamim*), this same ruling was derived from *achoso* as well, but they needed *achiv* first for the basic *meis mitzvah* exception, having no other available word. Rabbi Akiva, having derived *meis mitzvah* from *aviv* and *imo* and double-status from *achiv*, uses *achoso* for this final teaching about overriding active engagement in other *mitzvos*.

---

## Aviv & Imo Necessity

### Aviv Alone Problem

We must now address the question of why Rabbi Akiva needs both *aviv* and *imo* to teach *meis mitzvah* — why would one of them alone not suffice? The *gemara* explains with two distinct *hava aminas*. Had the verse stated only *aviv* — the father — one might have thought: the reason the *nazir* is restricted from becoming *tamei* to his father is specifically because paternity is established by *chazakah* alone. You never know with absolute certainty who one's father is. Perhaps that very uncertainty is what generates the stringency — there is a measure of doubt about the relationship. But if so, the *tumah* restriction to the father might reflect that doubt rather than the general principle of *kedushah*. By implication, the mother — *deyadi'an deyaldasei*, whose identity as the birth mother is certain — might be permissible. So *aviv* alone could have been misread as indicating that only relationships characterized by paternal uncertainty are restricted, not maternal relationships.

### Imo Alone Problem

Conversely, had the verse stated only *imo* — the mother — one might have thought the opposite. The reason the *nazir* is restricted from becoming *tamei* to his mother might be because she is not part of the patriarchal family identity. The Torah everywhere speaks of family in terms of the father's line: *l'mishpechotam l'beit avotam*. The mother's side is not the primary identifier of lineage or family in the Torah's structure. So if the verse said only *imo*, one might have understood: yes, the mother's side is restricted precisely because it falls outside the primary family definition, but the father's side — which *is* the family — should certainly be permissible. *Imo* alone could have been misread as limiting the prohibition to the non-patriarchal line.

### Both Combined Solution

Therefore, the verse states both *aviv* and *imo*. When read together, they convey nothing that the opening clause had not already established — all relatives are prohibited. The combination adds nothing new in terms of scope, and this is precisely the point. Because the combination does not limit the prohibition to one parental line over the other, it does not subtract from what the opening clause established. The words are therefore truly surplus, and a *miyut achar miyut* — a repeated restriction after an already-complete restriction — serves to *permit* rather than to further restrict. The surplus of both words *combined* is what signals the permission for *meis mitzvah*. Had only one been present, it might have been read as a limiting clarification rather than a permissive signal. With both present, neither misinterpretation is possible, and the *drasha* emerges cleanly: they are extra, and their surplus message is that *meis mitzvah* is permitted.

This is what the lengthy *Tosafos* on this passage ultimately establishes. It navigates carefully through the apparent circularity — the words were used to teach *meis mitzvah*, yet the *gemara* seems to focus on why you need both to maintain the prohibition on relatives — and concludes that the necessity of both words is precisely what ensures the *drasha* for *meis mitzvah* is not susceptible to misinterpretation. You must say both to prevent the reader from thinking the verse is narrowing the prohibition rather than opening an exception.

## Kol & Nefesh Usage

### Kol Nafshos Role

The *gemara* returns to the opening clause of the *kohen gadol*'s verse — *al kol nafshos meis lo yavo* — and asks, given everything Rabbi Akiva has now derived from subsequent words, what does *kol nafshos* teach? The answer is that it establishes the general prohibition against *tumah* from non-relatives: *l'afukei rechokim* — to exclude the thought that any leniency might apply even to distant persons.

### Nafshot Plural

The plural form *nafshos* serves a distinct function: *l'afukei revi'is dam sheyatza mishnei meitim* — to exclude a quarter-*log* (*revi'is*) of blood drawn from two separate corpses from the principle that a *revi'is* of blood from a single corpse conveys *tumah* through *ohel*. Normally, a *revi'is* of blood from one body represents a sufficient quantity to convey *tumat ohel*, and a *kohen gadol* or *nazir* would be prohibited. One might have thought that if the same volume is drawn from two separate corpses, it does not represent a complete single *meis* and therefore ought not convey *tumat ohel*. The plural *nafshos* — *kol nafshos* — teaches that even blood from two separate corpses combining to form a *revi'is* does convey *tumat ohel*, and is therefore prohibited for a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir*.

With this, the *sugya* of 48a reaches its conclusion. The bottom line of the entire discussion, shared by both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Akiva despite their different methods of *drasha*, is consistent: both a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir* are prohibited from *tumat meis* for all relatives, but both are obligated to attend to a *meis mitzvah*, and that obligation supersedes even their heightened *kedushah* — and in fact supersedes active engagement in other weighty *mitzvos* as well.
