# Bava Kamma 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Keren Half-Damages: Knas

### Knas Status Established

The discussion opens by revisiting the central question of the previous session: when an ox gores for the first three times and the owner pays *chatzi nezek*, half-damages, is that payment a form of *mamon* — genuine compensation reflecting some degree of negligence — or is it a *knas*, a fine? The evidence had seemed to point toward *mamon*, and one *baraita* had implied as much, reasoning that any payment exceeding actual damages is a *knas*, while a payment that falls *short* of full damages must be *mamon*. The *gemara*, however, rejects this inference. It distinguishes between two types of half-damages: *chatzi nezek tzerorot*, where the animal kicks pebbles that indirectly cause damage, is *mamon*, since the owner bears full negligence and the reduction to half reflects only the indirect nature of the act; whereas *chatzi nezek* of *keren* is *knas*. The conclusion, then, is that half-damages for *keren* constitutes a *knas*, and this determination carries significant practical consequences — most immediately, the rule that a *knas* is not collected upon the tortfeasor's own admission.

### Meshuneh Animal Acts

With this conclusion in place, the *gemara* draws an immediate application before turning to the next *mishnah*. The passage begins: *de-hashta de-amart palga nizka knasa* — now that we have established that half-damages for *keren* is a *knas* — *hai kalba de-achal imrei ve-shunra de-achal tarnigola*, if a dog ate sheep or a cat ate a chicken, *meshuneh hu*, that is unusual behavior, *ve-lo magbinan be-Bavel*, and we do not collect it in Bavel. The underlying principle, not stated explicitly in the *gemara* but implicit in its reasoning, is that an animal that causes damage in an unexpected, *meshuneh* fashion is treated as a type of *keren*.

### Shen vs. Keren Question

This classification raises a sharp analytical question, which the upcoming *mishnayot* will develop: when an animal eats something not natural to it — a sheep that chews on a garment, for instance — is that an unusual form of *shen*, eating that falls outside its normal pattern, or is it a type of *keren*? The *nafkamina* between these two characterizations is not merely taxonomic. One might raise the question of whether the act could ever become a *muad* through repetition, or whether it would be a *knas* or *mamon*. But the most immediate and decisive *nafkamina* is: *patur bi-reshut ha-rabim*. If the act is classified as *shen*, the owner is exempt for damage caused in the public domain; if it is *keren*, the owner is liable even there.

### Reshut Harabim Status

This distinction — *patur bi-reshut ha-rabim* for *shen* versus *chayav bi-reshut ha-rabim* for *keren* — is thus the critical practical question when determining whether unusual eating is a form of *shen* or *keren*.

### Rishonim: Unusual=Keren

Most *rishonim* assume that unusual eating is classified as a type of *keren*. Their reasoning proceeds from the logic of the *knas* ruling itself. If half-damages were *mamon* — reflecting genuine but partial negligence — one could extend that rationale by analogy to other categories such as *shen* and *regel*, creating a parallel form of reduced liability. But *knas* is inherently more arbitrary; fines are not derived by analogy in the same way. Therefore, once we conclude that *chatzi nezek keren* is a *knas*, and we wish to impose half-damages in other analogous scenarios of unexpected damage, the only way to derive that liability is to fold those scenarios into the category of *keren* itself. This leads to a striking conceptual revision: *keren* is not defined solely by *kavana le-hazik*, a deliberate intent to cause harm, but more broadly by the fact that the animal causes damage in an unexpected, unusual manner. Any act that is *meshuneh* — unpredicted and out of character — is, by virtue of that quality alone, a *keren*.

This is a significant conceptual shift. The possibility remains open that unusual eating is simply *shen*, with the same reduction to half-damages applied by analogy rather than by reclassification. But the dominant *rishonic* position treats such acts as genuine *keren*, with full liability in *reshut ha-rabim* and all the attendant consequences.

### Knas & Bavel Restriction

Returning to the case of the dog that ate sheep or the cat that ate a chicken: since the act is *meshuneh* and classified as *keren*, and *chatzi nezek keren* is a *knas*, it follows that *lo magbinan be-Bavel* — we do not collect this payment in Bavel. Courts in Bavel lack the *semikhah* necessary to adjudicate *dinei knasos*; the authority to impose fines, like the authority to administer lashes or capital punishment, requires ordination traceable to Eretz Yisrael. The *gemara* adds a qualification: *ve-hani mili be-ravrevei* — this applies only to a large sheep or large chicken, where such eating is genuinely unusual. *Aval be-zutrei*, if the animal ate something small, *orcheih hu*, that is within its normal range and would not be *meshuneh*.

But this creates a practical problem. The victim has been injured and receives nothing, since the court cannot adjudicate a *knas* in Bavel. The *gemara* addresses this: *ve-i tafas lo mafkinan minei* — if the victim seizes property from the tortfeasor, we do not force him to return it. He deserves at least half compensation, and while the court cannot affirmatively impose the *knas*, it will not dispossess someone who has helped himself. Additionally, the victim may request that the court set a date for him to travel to Eretz Yisrael, compelling the tortfeasor to appear before a court there with proper authority. If the tortfeasor refuses to attend such proceedings, *meshamtinan leh* — we place him in *nidui*. And in any event, *meshamtinan leh ad de-masalek hezika* — we subject the tortfeasor to *nidui* until he removes the dangerous animal from his possession.

### Dangerous Animal Removal

This last provision connects to a broader obligation articulated by Rabbi Natan. The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *Rabbi Natan omer minayin she-lo yigadel adam kelev ra be-toch beito ve-al yamid sulam ra'ua be-toch beito* — how do we know that one may not keep a dangerous dog in one's home or maintain a rickety ladder in one's home? The verse states, *lo tasim damim be-veitecha*, do not bring blood upon your house — meaning one may not maintain any condition that creates danger for others. The *rishonim* debate the precise threshold: at what point is an animal dangerous enough to require removal, and does "removal" mean confining it or physically eliminating it? Tosafot distinguishes between animals that can be adequately restrained with a chain and those whose danger cannot be so neutralized. These parameters remain a subject of discussion among the *rishonim*.

### Tosafot: Tafas Limits

The principle of *tafas lo mafkinan minei* — that we do not dispossess a victim who has seized property to satisfy a *knas* we cannot adjudicate — is subjected to an important qualification by Tosafot, citing *Rabbeinu Tam*. Tosafot rules: *davka i tafas ha-mazik atzmo ke-gon kelev o shor* — the permissible seizure applies only if what the victim seizes is the damaging animal itself, such as the dog or the ox. The concern driving this limitation is that if we allowed *tafas* of any property without restriction, a bad actor could fabricate a *knas* claim, seize someone's property, and then shield himself behind the argument that Bavel courts cannot hear *dinei knasos* and therefore cannot order the return of the seized property. That, says *Rabbeinu Tam*, *yavo lidei takala* — would lead to grave abuse, as one could effectively confiscate anything and render the courts powerless to respond.

This connects to another observation implicit in Tosafot: because *keren* liability attaches to the body of the animal itself, there is a conceptual basis for the victim to seize specifically the damaging animal, insofar as the animal is the very object from which *keren* damages are normally collected.

### Tafas & Gezela Debate

Many *rishonim* take a different approach to the problem entirely. They argue that when a victim seizes another's property, that act does not begin as a *din knas* at all — it begins as a potential *gezeilah*. The court is not thereby being asked to adjudicate a fine; it is being asked to determine whether the seized item must be returned. That is a question of *dinei gezeilah*, and Bavel courts are fully competent to hear such matters. The court will examine the facts sufficiently to determine: was there in fact a damaging act? Is the value of the seized property equivalent to or greater than the damage sustained? If the seizure exceeds the damage, the excess must be returned; if it is equivalent, the seizure stands. According to this view, the prohibition on adjudicating *dinei knasos* in Bavel does not prevent the court from engaging with the facts at all — it simply means the court is resolving a *gezeilah* question rather than affirmatively imposing a fine. This debate has ramifications far beyond goring oxen, extending to cases of personal injury where certain components of the damages — such as pain and suffering — may themselves be classified as *knas*. How much of a victim's entitlement can be vindicated through *tafas* and the resulting *gezeilah* inquiry becomes a significant question in the *rishonic* literature about the scope of judicial authority in Bavel.

## Five Tam, Five Muad

### Five Tam Types

The *mishnah* enumerates five categories in which an animal is classified as *tam* — an innocent first-time offender paying only half-damages: *lo ligach*, not to gore; *ve-lo ligof*, not to push or butt; *ve-lo lishoch*, not to bite; *ve-lo lirvots*, not to crouch on something and break it; and *ve-lo livot*, not to kick. Each of these represents a form of *keren* behavior for which the animal has not yet been forewarned.

### Shen Muad (Natural)

The *mishnah* then turns to the five *muad* categories. *Ha-shein muedes le-echol et ha-ra'uy lah* — the tooth (*shen*) is *muad* to eat things that are natural for the animal to eat. Each animal is assessed according to its own species: what is normal for a goat differs from what is normal for an ox. The implication of specifying natural eating is, of course, that unnatural eating occupies a different legal space — presumably a *tam* version of *shen*, as the *mishnah* is laying the groundwork for that distinction.

### Regel Muad

*Ha-regel muedes li-shbor be-derech hiluchah* — the foot (*regel*) is *muad* to break things in its normal way of walking. Whether there exists a *tam* version of *regel* is less clear from this formulation, though the *mishnah* does not address it here.

### Shor Muad & Keren

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-shor ha-muad* — an ox that has been formally forewarned three times and is now *muad* for *keren*; and *ve-shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* — an ox that causes damage in the victim's domain. The term *muad* here carries a dual meaning: sometimes it denotes habitual behavior, and sometimes it simply means that full damages are paid. The ox goring in the victim's domain is no more habitually inclined to gore there than elsewhere; rather, *muad* in this context means the legal consequence is full payment. This is the position of Rabbi Tarfon. *Ve-adam* — a person is also always *muad* in the sense of always paying full damages for whatever harm he causes, whether awake or asleep. Here too, *muad le-olam* does not mean a person is habitually disposed to cause damage; it means that in all circumstances, the legal consequence is complete liability.

### Wild Animals Muad

*Ha-ze'ev ve-ha-ari ve-ha-dov ve-ha-namer ve-ha-bordelis ve-ha-nachash — harei elu muadim* — the wolf, lion, bear, leopard, *bordelis*, and snake are *muad* from the outset. One immediately notices that this list contains six entries, not five. The deeper question — what kind of *muad* are these animals? Is their characteristic violence a form of *shen* and *regel*, given that they act from natural instinct and appetite, or is it a form of *keren*, reflecting a kind of intentional destructive act? Instinct might suggest *keren*, since these animals seem deliberately aggressive. But stepping back, when a lion attacks a sheep for food, it is engaging in exactly what an ox does when it eats grain — it is satisfying a natural appetite. That would make it *shen*, not *keren*.

One student raised the interesting point that animals also attack out of territorial instinct — not for food at all, simply to drive away intruders. A bear, for example, when it feels threatened, will attack without any intent to eat. This kind of violence is not *shen* and is not obviously *regel*; it may genuinely constitute *keren* in the sense of purposeful harm. This point actually reinforces the reading of Rashi, who comments that these animals are *muad* for all forms of damage — *afilu bi-neshikha be-khol nezikin* — even biting, in all categories of damage. According to Rashi and, by implication, the Rambam, the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is that for these animals, all manner of violence — whether for food or otherwise — is classified as *keren muad* from the outset.

Tosafot takes a more restrictive view, attempting to confine the lion's characteristic acts to the categories of *shen* and *regel* — natural behavior for which the animal simply has no *tam* phase — and correspondingly ruling that a lion eating in *reshut ha-rabim* is *patur*, since *shen* in a public domain generates no liability. The Rambam, by contrast, does not draw this distinction, treating the lion as *muad keren* across the board. The discussion remains instructive: one must resist the anthropomorphic temptation to read violence as inherently malicious when it may simply be instinctive, while also recognizing that certain kinds of attack — territorial aggression, for instance — may genuinely belong to the *keren* category regardless of whether food is the motive.

## Animal Identity & Lore

### Domestication Doctrine

Rabbi Eliezer offers an optimistic counterpoint to the general rule that these animals are *muad* from the outset: *bi-zeman she-hem benei tarbut einam mu'adin* — when they are domesticated, they are not *muad*. A trained lion, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, can have its legal status reversed. Normally the trajectory runs from *tam* to *muad* through three acts of forewarning; for these animals, the reverse trajectory is possible — from *muad* to *tam* — through domestication. One might note that practical experience raises doubts about how fully any apex predator can be domesticated, but that concern lies outside the *halacha*'s formal framework.

The one exception is absolute: *ve-nachash muad le-olam* — a snake is permanently *muad* and can never be domesticated. Whether this reflects a purely naturalistic assessment that snakes are simply incapable of domestication, or whether it carries metaphysical resonance given the snake's role in the garden of Eden and the symbolism pervading rabbinic literature, the *gemara* leaves implicit. The text moves on without extended comment, though as will be seen, the snake's eternal *muad* status is woven into a remarkable aggadic passage later in the *sugya*.

## Muad Categories Debate

### Rabbanan vs. Tarfon

The *gemara* now turns to a structural problem in the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* appears to enumerate five *tam* and five *muad* categories, contrasting them in parallel. But the *shen* liability seems to presuppose the victim's domain (*chatzer ha-nizak*), while the *keren* as *tam* seems to be paying only half — which, according to Rabbi Tarfon, should not be possible in the victim's domain where *keren* pays full. Can the entire *mishnah* be attributed to the Rabbis, who hold that even in the victim's domain *keren* pays only half? Or to Rabbi Tarfon? The difficulty is that the later portion of the *mishnah*, which lists *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* as a *muad* category, seems to reflect Rabbi Tarfon's position. So: is the first half Rabbanan and the second half Rabbi Tarfon?

### Rashi's Reshut Solution

Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rav attempts to resolve this by arguing that the *mishnah* is entirely Rabbi Tarfon, by positing a divided domain: *reisha be-chatzer ha-meyuhedet le-peirot le-echad u-le-zeh u-le-zeh le-shorim* — the first section speaks of a courtyard where each owner has the right to bring his ox, so from the perspective of the oxen it is like *reshut ha-rabim* and *keren* pays only half; but only the victim has the right to store fruit there, so from the perspective of the fruit it is *reshut ha-nizak* and *shen* pays full. On this reading, *shen* pays full because only you have the right to store fruit there, while *keren* pays only half because we both have the right to bring our oxen there, rendering it quasi-public for that purpose. This is the reading that Rashi adopts.

### Kahana-Zebid Challenge

Rav Kahana reported that when he presented this explanation to Rav Zebid of Nehardea, Rav Zebid challenged it sharply: *mi matzit mokimat lah kulei ke-Rabbi Tarfon?* How can you possibly attribute the whole *mishnah* to Rabbi Tarfon in this manner? The *mishnah* states that *shen* is *muad* only when it eats *ra'uy lah* — things natural to it — and by implication, not when it eats something unnatural. Now, the *mishnah* teaches this *shen* liability in the victim's domain. Unnatural eating in the victim's domain would generate half-damages — classifying it as a type of *keren*. But Rabbi Tarfon holds that *keren* in the victim's domain pays full damages. So if we attribute the entire *mishnah* to Rabbi Tarfon, any unnatural eating in the victim's domain should trigger full damages, not half — yet the *mishnah* implies only half. The reading therefore cannot be coherently assigned to Rabbi Tarfon throughout.

### Later Mishnah Evidence

Ravina presses this point further using a later *mishnah*. The later *mishnah* introduces the phrase *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak keitzad* and then explicitly presents the debate: Rabbi Tarfon says full damages, while the Rabbis say half. This later *mishnah* makes plain that the phrase *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* refers specifically to *keren* in the victim's domain and is a point of disagreement between Rabbi Tarfon and the Rabbis. Therefore, if the earlier *mishnah* counts that phrase among the five *muad* categories, it is adopting Rabbi Tarfon's position that such a case pays full. But we have shown that the *mishnah* cannot be entirely Rabbi Tarfon. So we appear to be back to a divided *mishnah* — first half Rabbanan, second half Rabbi Tarfon — which is formally awkward when the *mishnah* is structured as an explicit contrast of five against five.

## Five Tam, Five Muad…

### Ravina's Rewrite

Ravina ultimately proposes a thoroughgoing rewrite of the *mishnah*: *chasurei mechasra ve-hachi katani* — the *mishnah* is missing text and should be read as follows. The five *tamim* and five *muadim* are not two separate lists of five distinct categories; rather, the five *muadim* are simply those same five *tamim* if they were formally forewarned. *Chamisha tamim hem ve-im hu'adu chamishtan muadin* — there are five *tam* types; if they were forewarned, all five become *muad*. What follows in the *mishnah* — *shen*, *regel*, *shor ha-muad*, *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak*, and *adam* — is not a separate list of five *muadim* but rather a continuation of the *mishnah* discussing other legal categories. Specifically, *shen* and *regel* are *muad* from the outset; the phrase *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* is the subject of the explicit debate between Rabbi Tarfon and the Rabbis and is not being declared *muad* by this *mishnah* — it is simply mentioning the category as a contested case; and *adam* is indeed *muad* always. Then the *mishnah* adds a further clause: *ve-yesh muadin acherim ka-yotzei ba-elu* — and there are other *muadim* analogous to these, namely the wolf, lion, bear, leopard, *bordelis*, and snake.

This is acknowledged to be a forced reading of the *mishnah*. The phrase *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* does not naturally read as introducing a debate rather than as a declaration of *muad* status, and the suggestion that the *mishnah* is "missing words" (*chasurei mechasra*) is always a difficult move. Nevertheless, this is the resolution with which the *gemara* is most satisfied.

### Baraita Support (Questio…

A *baraita* is then cited that appears to support Ravina's reading exactly, restating word for word: *chamisha tamim hem ve-im hu'adu chamisha muadin; ve-shein ve-regel muadin mi-techilatan; ve-zehu shor ha-muad; ve-shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak — machloket Rabbi Tarfon ve-Rabbanan; ve-yesh muadin acherim ka-yotzei ba-elu: ha-ze'ev ve-ha-ari ve-ha-dov ve-ha-namer ve-ha-bordelis ve-ha-nachash*. The *baraita* thus seems to confirm that *shor ha-mazik bi-reshut ha-nizak* is a debate and not a declaration, and that the dangerous animals form a separate additional category.

It is worth noting, however, what scholars have observed about such *baraitot*: occasionally a *baraita* cited as *tanya nami hachi* — "so too it was taught in a *baraita*" — appears only in the Babylonian Talmud, has no parallel in the Yerushalmi or any other tannaitic source, and is worded identically to the *amora*'s preceding statement. Since *amoraim* often formulated their legal rulings in Hebrew — the same language used by *baraitot* — it is at least possible that some such passages began as *amoraic* statements that were subsequently remembered and transmitted anonymously, eventually coming to be regarded as *baraitot*. The *gemara* itself takes the *baraita* as genuine corroboration, but the observation is worth registering.

### Five / More Muad Problem

A formal objection is then raised against the *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* says *chamisha tamim va-chamisha muadim* — five *tamim* and five *muadim* — and yet includes wolves, lions, bears, leopards, *bordelis*, and snakes, which is six animals. How can it claim to enumerate five *muadim* and then produce six? The *gemara* resolves this by returning to Ravina's reading: the word "five" in the *mishnah* refers only to the five *tam* categories and their *muad* counterparts when forewarned; the dangerous animals form a separate additional list introduced by *ve-yesh muadin acherim*, and are not counted as part of the five.

## Crouch & Vessels

### Large vs. Small Vessels

The *mishnah*'s inclusion of *lirvots* — crouching on something to break it — as a *tam* act now receives further analysis. Rabbi Elazar rules: *lo shanu ela pachim gedolim aval pachim ktanim orcheihu* — this category of *tam* applies only to large vessels; it is not in the animal's way to crouch on those. But small vessels, like the delicate perfume flasks one sees in museum collections, are small enough that an animal might naturally sit or lie down on them without any deliberate act of destruction — *orcheihu*, that is within its normal behavior.

### Walking Crush (Darko)

A *baraita* is cited that states: *ha-behema muedes la-halech ke-darkah u-le-shaber* — an animal is *muad* to break things in its normal way of walking — *u-le-ma'och et ha-adam ve-et ha-behema ve-et ha-kelim* — and to crush a person, another animal, or vessels. This seems to indicate that crushing vessels through some normal form of movement is indeed considered *darko*. The *gemara* initially treats this as relevant to the *lirvots* question, but then reconsiders.

### Side Impact Theory

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *dilma min ha-tzad* — crushing here means not crouching on an object but catching it from the side. The animal is walking along a wall, for example, and a person or object is pressed between the animal's body and the wall, or a vessel protrudes and the animal's flank strikes it as it passes. That lateral crushing is *ke-darkah* — its normal way of moving. Crouching upon a stationary object intentionally, by contrast, is *she-lo ke-darkah* and thus *tam*. Rashi raises the question that on this reading, the references to crushing *adam* and *behema* in the *baraita* would need to refer to very small individuals — a small child or a small animal — but the *gemara*'s essential distinction is between lateral impact while walking versus deliberate crouching.

## Animal Identity & Lore…

### Bordelis Identity

The *gemara* now asks: *mai bordelis?* — what exactly is this animal? *Amar Rav Yehuda nafreza* — Rav Yehuda says it is a *nafreza*. The *gemara* then asks *mai nafreza?* and Rav Yosef identifies it as an *apa*. The precise identification remains uncertain; the Soncino translation suggests something like a cheetah or hyena, and while these are biologically distinct — a cheetah is a large cat, a hyena more canine — they occupy adjacent conceptual territory in this context.

### Tzavoa Addition

Rabbi Meir adds to the list: *af tzavoa* — including the *tzavoa* as well. Rav Yosef identifies the *tzavoa* as also being an *apa*. This creates an apparent problem: if both *bordelis* and *tzavoa* are the same animal, why does Rabbi Meir need to add it separately? The *gemara* resolves that the two terms refer to different variants — one to the male, the other to the female of the species. The significance of distinguishing male and female here becomes immediately apparent.

### Transformation Sequence

A *baraita* teaches a remarkable sequence of transformations. *Tzavoa zachar*, a male hyena, *le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh atalef* — after seven years becomes a bat. *Atalef le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh arpad* — the bat after seven years becomes an *arpad*, another variety of bat. *Arpad le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh kimosh* — after seven years that becomes a type of thorn bush. *Ve-kimosh le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh choach* — after seven years that becomes a different type of thorn bush. *Ve-choach le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh shed* — after seven years that becomes a demon. The progression descends through increasingly lowly and sinister forms of existence: a despised animal, a creature of darkness, a thorny and barren plant, a demonic spirit. The word *shed* also plays on *shidro shel adam*, a person's spine, and the passage continues: *shidro shel adam le-achar sheva shanim na'aseh nachash* — the spine of a person, seven years after death, turns into a snake. This is followed by the qualification: *ve-hani mili de-lo kara be-modim* — this applies only to one who never bowed during *modim* in prayer, never lowering himself in gratitude before God, and thus in death his very backbone is transformed into the primordial serpent.

The imagery converges with the *gemara* in *Berachot* about bowing like a snake during *modim*, and with the deeper symbolism of the snake in *Gan Eden*. The descent through these forms has also been understood through the lens of *gilgul*, transmigration, as the *gemara*'s claim that these transformations literally occur across the life cycle of the animal evokes parallel ideas about the soul's passage through different forms of existence.

### Snake Eternal Muad

Rabbi Eliezer adds: *af nachash* — even the snake belongs on the list of dangerous animals. The *gemara* notes the apparent tension: in the *mishnah*, Rabbi Eliezer himself argues that domesticated animals can revert to *tam* status — yet in this *baraita*, he seems to concede that all the other animals are *muad* (which is why he needs to add the snake separately). The *gemara* resolves this by reading *af nachash* simply as *nachash* — Rabbi Eliezer is saying only that the snake is *muad* and cannot be domesticated, while the other animals on the list can be, and he is not conceding their permanent *muad* status. The snake stands alone as eternally and irreversibly *muad*.

## Lion Behavior Analysis

### Dras = Shen

*Amar Shmuel: ari bi-reshut ha-rabim — das ve-achal patur.* Shmuel rules: a lion in the public domain that *dras* and ate — that is, that trampled living prey and ate it while still alive — is exempt. Since this is *orchei le-midras*, the natural way for a lion to consume its prey, it is classified as *shen*. Just as an ordinary animal eats fruit or grain and is exempt in *reshut ha-rabim* because that is *shen*, so too a lion eating in this manner: *hachi nami ke-man de-achlah peirot ve-yerakot* — it is precisely like one who eats fruits and vegetables. The natural predatory consumption of living prey is, for a lion, its *shen*.

### Taraf = Keren

*Taraf ve-achal chayav* — but if the lion first killed the prey and then ate it, Shmuel rules it is liable. According to this ruling, killing before eating is *lav orchei* — not the natural way — and therefore constitutes an act of *keren*, generating liability in *reshut ha-rabim*. Whether that *keren* liability is *tam* or *muad* remains bound up in the earlier discussion, but it is *keren* nonetheless. Empirically, one might observe that lions typically do kill their prey before eating, raising the question of how "unnatural" such behavior truly is. The *gemara* will address this.

### Psalms Evidence

The *gemara* raises a scriptural challenge: *umi amart de-tarfah lav orchei hu? Ve-ha-ketiv "aryeh toreif bedei gurotav"* — and does it not say in Psalms that a lion slashes prey for its young? This suggests that *terifah*, killing prey, is indeed the lion's natural way. The *gemara* answers, following Rashi's reading: *bishvil gurotav* — when the lion is hunting for its cubs, it will kill the prey and transport it back. The verse continues: *u-mechaneik le-leviotav* — it strangles prey for its lionesses — again, *bishvil leviotav*, it kills to bring food home. And *va-yemalei teref chorav, u-me'onotav treifah* — it fills its cave with prey, it stores carcasses in its dwelling. *Bishvil chorav, bishvil me'onotav* — when bringing food for storage or for dependents, it kills first. But when eating on the spot for itself, it eats while the prey is still alive. That on-the-spot killing-then-eating is therefore anomalous — *keren* — while eating living prey is *shen*.

### Baraita: Full Payment

A *baraita* apparently contradicts Shmuel's ruling: *ve-chein chayah she-nichnesah le-chatzer ha-nizak ve-tarfah behemah ve-achalah basar — meshalem nezek shalem* — a wild animal that entered the victim's domain, slashed an animal, and ate it — the flesh already dead (*basar*) — pays full damages. This seems to indicate that *terifah* followed by eating is normal lion behavior, since the *baraita* treats it matter-of-factly and assesses *nezek shalem* based on the domain, not on any abnormality of method.

### Changed Mind Scenario

The *gemara* first attempts to explain the *baraita*: *ha-cha be-mai askinan she-tarfah le-hani'ach* — perhaps the lion killed the prey intending to store it, and only later ate it on the spot. The *gemara* objects: *ha achalah katani* — the *baraita* says it ate it, implying immediately after killing. The *gemara* then suggests *be-shinuyah* — the lion changed its mind: it killed with intent to store, then decided to eat on the spot. A further challenge is raised: *umi yadeinan?* — how could we ever know whether the lion intended to store and then changed its mind? And by that logic, perhaps even in Shmuel's case, where the lion killed and ate on the spot, we should say it planned to store and then changed its mind, rendering it *shen* as well.

### Domesticated Lion Theory

At this point Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes a different resolution of the *baraita*: the passage must be read as referring to two separate cases — *metzodadim katani* — it is presenting paired scenarios. In one, the lion killed for storage and did not eat; in the other, it ate the prey alive (*darsa ve-achlah*). Full payment applies to either. This preserves Shmuel's ruling intact.

Rav Yeimar then offers a different framework altogether: Shmuel's ruling — that killing before eating is *lav orchei* — applies only to a *domesticated* lion, *ari tarbut*. A wild lion's every act of predation, including killing before eating, is entirely natural — all *shen*. This would mean that Shmuel's rule is aligned with Rabbi Eliezer's position that domestication transforms the animal's legal character: the domesticated lion, no longer behaving as a wild predator, finds that certain predatory acts are now classified as *lav orchei* relative to its tamed nature. The *gemara* challenges this: if a domesticated lion's killing is *lav orchei*, then even eating living prey should be *lav orchei* for a domesticated animal — why does Shmuel distinguish between the two modes? The *gemara* then suggests that Rav Yeimar's statement was not explaining Shmuel at all, but explaining the *baraita* about the animal entering the victim's domain: *ki tanya matnitin be-ari tarbut ve-aliba de-Rabbi Eliezer* — that *baraita* speaks of a domesticated lion and follows Rabbi Eliezer's view. The *gemara* then raises the difficulty that if it is domesticated and Rabbi Eliezer applies, it should pay only half until warned three times, then full. The *gemara* replies that it had already been warned three times (*de-iyad*), making it fully *muad*. A further difficulty arises: if the lion went through the process of domestication and then renewed violence, the *baraita* categorizes this under *toldah de-shen* — a derivative of *shen* — whereas it should properly be categorized under *toldah de-keren*, given its history of becoming a *muad* through warning. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this remains difficult.

### Rambam: Muad Keren

It is notable that the Rambam completely ignores Shmuel's ruling. When the Rambam rules that a lion is *muad*, he does not distinguish between *taraf* and *daras*, does not differentiate *reshut ha-rabim* from *reshut ha-nizak*, and does not limit any acts to *shen*. He treats the lion as *muad keren* in all circumstances, returning to the straightforward *peshat* of the *mishnah* — these dangerous animals are *muad* from the outset, full stop.

### Shen vs. Keren Unresolved

The *sugya* thus ends without a clean resolution to the question of whether a lion's eating constitutes *shen* and therefore generates no liability in *reshut ha-rabim*. One student raised a pointed observation: since when does a person have the right to bring a lion into *reshut ha-rabim* in the first place? If one has no right to bring such an animal into the public domain, perhaps one can never invoke the *shen* exemption at all, because the exemption presupposes a legitimate presence in that domain. This framing would align naturally with the Rambam's position: since the lion should never be in *reshut ha-rabim*, all its acts are *keren muad*, and the *shen* framework simply never applies. The question remains instructive and the sources leave it, ultimately, unresolved.
