# Keritot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Karet vs. Malkus

### Correction: Karet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר מוּתָּר, דְּעַד כָּאן לָא קָבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן אֶלָּא אִי חֲדָא מִחַיַּיב אִי תַּרְתֵּי מִחַיַּיב, אָבֵל מִיפְּטַר לִגְמָרֵי לָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case **where one says** to himself that this is **permitted.** He is under the impression that idol worship is permitted, and his unwitting act was the result of ignorance, not forgetfulness. **As, Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman** with regard to Shabbat **only** so it could be determined **whether to deem** one who is unaware of both the essence of Shabbat and the specific prohibited labors **liable** to bring **one** sin offering, **or whether to deem** him **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings. **But** as for the possibility to **exempt him completely,** he did **not raise** such **a dilemma before him.**

Before proceeding into the new material, a correction from the previous session is in order. The position discussed there — *karet b'choteh* — was mischaracterized. That opinion, which we do not rule by, holds that for transgressions carrying *karet*, there is no concept of *beis din*-administered *malkus*; the punishment is *karet* alone. The position we do rule by is the opposite: one can receive *malkus*, and if one does, one is exempt from *karet*. These are distinct and should not be conflated.

Returning to the main thread: we were examining how many *chata'ot* one brings for various categories of transgression. For *Shabbos* and *avodah zarah*, one brings a single *chatas* if one erred about the fundamental prohibition, and separate *chata'ot* for each individual violation when one knew the general prohibition but forgot particular details. If one forgot *Shabbos* entirely and violated ten *melachos*, one brings only one *chatas*. If one knew about *Shabbos* but forgot that a particular *melacha* was forbidden, one brings a separate *chatas* for each. Analogously for *avodah zarah*: if one forgot the individual *avodos* — bowing, slaughtering, and so forth — one brings separately for each, but if one made a fundamental error about *avodah zarah* itself and performed multiple *avodos*, only one *chatas* is brought.

The *Gemara* had already asked how someone can make a fundamental error about *avodah zarah* while still performing an act of *avodah zarah*, and answered: *omer mutar* — one who considers it permitted. This renders even a categorical ignorance a *shogeg* sufficient for a *chatas*. The *Gemara* then raised the further question: if one forgot both the category and all the details, do we treat that as multiple errors or one? The ruling is that it constitutes one fundamental mistake, and only one *chatas* is brought.

### Tinok Shenishba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּתִינוֹק שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּה לְבֵין הַגּוֹיִם, דְּיָדַע דַּאֲסִירָא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְהָנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָא יָדַע דַּאֲסִירָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: You find** an act performed unwittingly with regard to idol worship **in** the case of **a child who was taken captive among the gentiles** and never taught about the Torah, **as** he **knows that idol worship** in general is **prohibited, but** with regard to **these** particular objects of **idol worship** that he is worshipping, he does **not know that they are prohibited.**

The *Gemara* at the top of 3b continues within this vein. *Rav Pappa* offers an illustration: *mishkachat lah b'tinok shenishba l'vein ha'akum* — the paradigm case is that of an infant taken captive among non-Jews, someone who grew up not knowing he was Jewish. This is where one can genuinely not know the prohibition in the most fundamental sense. It is, in essence, an extreme form of *omer mutar*.

The *Gemara* notes that there is actually a debate elsewhere, in *Shabbos*, whether this constitutes *ones* or *shogeg*. The conclusion is *shogeg* in the sense that one must bring a *korban chatas*. This is striking, because *shogeg* ordinarily implies some degree of fault, and it is difficult to assign fault to someone raised from infancy with no knowledge of his Jewish identity. What this really points to is the principle that a *chatas* is not simply a guilt offering — it atones for the objective fact that a sin was committed. To the degree that one is an *ones* in the sense that a force external to oneself compelled the act entirely, one could argue it was not one's action at all. But when one is choosing and acting — even if entirely blameless — there is a dimension of the sin that requires atonement, and one brings a *chatas*. The case of *tinok shenishba*, by this reasoning, is enough of an act of doing to obligate a *chatas*.

### Omer Mutar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּגָדוֹל – כְּגוֹן דְּקָא טָעֵי בְּהָדֵין קְרָא: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי אֱלֹהֵי כֶסֶף וֵאלֹהֵי זָהָב וְגוֹ׳״ סָבַר: כִּי אֲסִירָא הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּכֶסֶף וְזָהָב, אֲבָל דְּמִינֵי אַחֲרִינֵי שַׁרְיָא, דְּהַיְינוּ שִׁגְגַת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּזְדוֹן עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **You** may **even say** this ruling is stated **with regard to an adult,** i.e., one who was not captured by gentiles as a child, and it is speaking of a case **where he erred with regard to this verse: "You shall not make with Me gods of silver and gods of gold,** you shall not make for yourselves" (Exodus 20:20). This person **thinks** that **when bowing** to an idol is **prohibited** it applies only **to** an object of **idol worship** fashioned **of silver or gold; but** with regard to objects fashioned **of other substances,** such as wood or stone, it is **permitted. And that is** a case of an action that is **unwitting with regard to idol worship but intentional with regard to** the prohibited **rites.**

The *Gemara* then qualifies the case and distinguishes it from a straightforward *omer mutar*. The scenario is: *d'yada d'asira avodah zarah* — one knows that *avodah zarah* is forbidden (perhaps through the *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach*, which raises interesting questions if the person turns out to be Jewish), *v'hai avodah zarah lo yada d'asira* — but one did not know that this particular form of *avodah zarah* was forbidden. The *Gemara* imagines one who grew up knowing that worshipping the *Asherah* was forbidden but believing that the *Baal* was permitted, and who therefore worshipped the *Baal* through all its various *avodos*. That is a fundamental mistake even if one performed every possible *avoda*, and thus only one *chatas* is brought.

### Fundamental vs. Detail

The *Gemara* also offers a different scenario not involving someone raised among non-Jews but an ordinary adult Jew who misread the verse: *lo ta'asuni iti elohei chesef ve-elohei zahav lo ta'asu lachem* — do not make gods of gold and silver. One might conclude from this that worship of idols made of other materials is permitted. That constitutes a fundamental mistake about the prohibition — *shegagat karkavan*, as the *Gemara* terms it — even though one performed individual *avodos* knowingly, since one believed those *avodos* directed at a non-gold, non-silver idol were permitted. Again, only one *chatas* is brought.

## Category Consolidation

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב בִּיבִי אָמַר: תַּנָּא שֵׁם שַׁבָּת וְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said** a different interpretation of the mishna **in the name of Rav Beivai:** The ***tanna* teaches the** general **category of Shabbat and the** general **category of idol worship,** without mentioning the details of how many sin offerings one would be liable to bring for transgressing each prohibition.

*Rav Pappa brei de-Rav Ika* then cites a ruling: *tanna shem Shabbos ve-shem avodat kochavim katani* — the *tanna* is listing the categories of *Shabbos* and *avodah zarah*, not trying to enumerate the maximum number of *chata'ot* that can be produced within each category. One should not be troubled by the fact that the *mishna* implies a single *chatas* for *Shabbos* when in reality thirty-nine *chata'ot* are possible, or implies a single *chatas* for *avodah zarah* when four or more are possible. The *mishna* is identifying categories, not maximizing counts.

### Shabbat, Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָתָנֵי: הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְהָא יֵשׁ בִּתּוֹ מֵאֲנוּסָתוֹ, דְּלָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** can one infer this is the case? **From** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches: One who engages in intercourse with a woman and her daughter, or with a married woman. But there is** the case of **one's daughter from the woman he raped, which** the mishna **does not teach.**

This principle is reinforced by a comparison to *arayos*. The *mishna* lists a woman and her daughter and an *eshet ish*, but it does not list every possible *chiyuv karet* within *arayos*. This demonstrates that the *mishna* does not aim to maximize the number of *chata'ot*.

### Arayot Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא יֵשׁ בַּת אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבַת בִּתָּהּ וּבַת בְּנָהּ, דִּכְתִיבָא, וְלָא קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא: שֵׁם אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ קָתָנֵי? הָכִי נָמֵי: שֵׁם שַׁבָּת וְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **But there is** the case of **one's wife's daughter, and the daughter of her daughter, and the daughter of her son, which are written** explicitly, **and** yet the *tanna* **does not teach** them. **Rather,** you must say that **he teaches the** general **category of** intercourse with **a woman and her daughter,** which includes those cases. **So too, he teaches the** general **category of Shabbat and the** general **category of idol worship.**

The *Gemara* asks why a man's biological daughter from a woman he assaulted is not listed, given that it carries its own *chiyuv karet*. One reason offered is that the daughter is not made explicit in the *pesukim* in the way that granddaughter is. A further answer: *shem isha u-vita katani* — the *mishna* consolidates everything into the category of a woman and her daughter. Even though there is a halachic distinction between a wife's daughter and a biological daughter from another woman, they belong to the same conceptual category, and the *mishna* consolidates them even at the cost of omitting additional *chata'ot* that could have been listed. Here too: *hacha nami shem Shabbos ve-shem avodah zarah katani* — the point in our *mishna* is likewise one of consolidation by category, not maximization.

## Shechutei Chutz

### Shechita, Ha'ala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: ״אִי הָכִי, הָא דִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשִׁים וָשֵׁשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת בְּתוֹרָה – תְּלָתִין וּשְׁבַע נִינְהוּ, דְּאִיכָּא הַמַּעֲלֶה וְהַמַּעֲלֶה!״ מַאי קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ? נִישְׁנֵי: שֵׁם הַעֲלָאָה קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Beivai bar Abaye** raised **a difficulty: If so,** consider **that which we learned** in the mishna: There are **thirty-six** cases **in the Torah** with regard to which one who performs the action intentionally is liable to receive ***karet*. They are** actually **thirty-seven, as there is** the case of **one who offers up** the limbs of an offering slaughtered inside the Temple outside the Temple, **and one who offers up** limbs of an offering slaughtered outside the Temple outside the Temple. Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, explains the contradiction: If Rav Beivai bar Abaye maintains that the *tanna* of the mishna does not tally all the relevant prohibitions but states general categories, **what** is **difficult to him? Let him answer** that **he teaches the category of offering up** in the clause: One who slaughters offerings and who sacrifices them outside the Temple.

*Rav Acha brei de-Rav Ika* points out an apparent contradiction in the positions of *Rav Bibi bar Abaye*. On the one hand, *Rav Bibi bar Abaye* himself said that our *mishna* consolidates categories and is not trying to maximize numbers. Yet there is a *braisa* that teaches: *ha-ma'aleh evarei pnim bachutz chayav, evarei chutz bachutz chayav* — one who brings up on an altar outside the *Beis HaMikdash* the limbs of an animal slaughtered inside is liable, and one who does the same with an animal slaughtered outside is also liable. These are two forms of the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*. *Rav Bibi bar Abaye* understood them to be distinct categories, each producing a separate *chatas*. But if that is so, then since the *mishna* lists 36 *kareisin*, it should have counted both forms — why list only one, given that they are distinct?

### Location Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? שַׁבָּת וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה תְּנָא יָתְהוֹן אַבִּינְכֵיהוֹן, גַּבַּי כָּרֵיתוֹת, דְּאִירְיָא, מְשַׁנֵּי: ״שֵׁם שַׁבָּת קָתָנֵי שֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קָתָנֵי״, גַּבֵּי הַעֲלָאָה, מִי תְּנָא יָתְהוֹן בִּינְכֵיהוֹן דְּשַׁנִּי הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Are** these cases **comparable?** In the cases of **Shabbat and idol worship** the *tanna* **taught** their details **in their places [*abeinekheihon*],** on *Shabbat* 73a and *Sanhedrin* 60b, respectively. Consequently, **with regard to *karet*, where it is necessary** to mention them as part of a list of prohibitions that are punishable by *karet*, Rav Beivai bar Abaye **answers** that the *tanna* **teaches the category of Shabbat and teaches the category of idol worship,** but he did not need to specify the details in this manner. By contrast, **with regard to bringing up** an offering, **does** the *tanna* **teach them** in **their place,** so **that** Rav Beivai bar Abaye can **answer** in **this** manner?

The *Gemara* answers by drawing a distinction based on where these matters are elaborated. *Shabbos* and *avodah zarah* are discussed at length elsewhere — *Shabbos* has its own tractate and *avodah zarah* is elaborated in *Sanhedrin* — and therefore our *mishna* can consolidate without spelling out all the details, since the reader will encounter those details in their proper place. The laws of *shechutei chutz*, however, have no other dedicated place of discussion. If those two forms were genuinely distinct categories producing separate *chata'ot*, one would have expected them to be listed separately here in *Keritot*, which is precisely where that discussion belongs. The fact that the *mishna* does list both *ha-shocheit* and *ha-ma'aleh* separately confirms that this is the appropriate place to articulate any distinctions within that prohibition. The underlying principle is thus that consolidation is appropriate when elaboration will occur elsewhere; when a topic finds its primary home in *Keritot*, the *mishna* does provide separate entries.

## Two Karetin, One Lav

### Ein Onshin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: שְׁתֵּי כָּרֵיתוֹת וְלָאו אֶחָד, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שׁוֹחֵט וְהֶעֱלָה קָאָמְרַתְּ? הָנֵי שְׁנֵי לָאוִין נִינְהוּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma before Rabbi Zeira:** If there are **two** punishments of ***karet* and** only **one prohibition** written with regard to a certain act, **what is** the *halakha*? Is one liable to bring a separate sin offering for each act? Rabbi Zeira **said to him: Are you saying** this in reference to one **who slaughters** offerings outside the Temple **and** one **who offered up** the limbs of offerings outside the Temple? These do not match your description, as **these are two** separate **prohibitions.**

*Rami bar Chama* posed a question to *Rav Chisda*: what is the rule when there are two *kareisin* and one *lav*? The preceding discussion had addressed the case of two *lavin* and one *karet* — the prohibition of preparing the anointing oil and anointing oneself with it, *mefatem ve-sach shemen hamishcha*, where two distinct *lavin* share a single explicit *karet*, and the ruling is that the two *lavin* generate separate *chata'ot*. What if the situation is reversed: a single prohibition but two explicit *kareisin*?

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לְמַאן דְּגָמַר מִגְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה – נֶאֱמַר כָּאן הֲבָאָה וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן הֲבָאָה,
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira elaborates: **If** one considers the matter **according to the one who derives** the prohibition **from a verbal analogy,** it is as though both prohibitions are written explicitly. As **it is stated here** an expression of **bringing:** "Or who slaughters it outside the camp, and to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it" (Leviticus 17:3–4), **and it is stated there** an expression of **bringing:** "Who sacrifices a burnt offering or sacrifice, and to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it" (Leviticus 17:8–9).

The question is initially deflected: *shocheit* and *ma'aleh* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* would seem to be the relevant example, but the *Gemara* points out that these actually carry two separate *lavin*, not one. The explicit prohibition — *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha b'chol hamakom asher tir'eh* — mentions only *ha'ala*, the burning of limbs. How is *shechita* prohibited? One opinion derives it through a *gezeirah shavah*: both *shechita* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* and *ha'ala* outside employ the phrase *lo hevi'o* in the relevant *pesukim*. On the basis of *ein onshin ela im kein mazhirin* — the Torah will not impose a punishment without a prohibition — we must discover a prohibition for *shechita*. The *gezeirah shavah* supplies it: just as there is a prohibition for *ha'ala*, so there is a prohibition for *shechita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַלָּן לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר – אַף כָּאן לֹא עָנַשׁ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Since there is a verbal analogy linking slaughtering an offering outside the Temple to sacrificing one outside the Temple, it follows that **just as there,** with regard to sacrificing, the Torah **did not** prescribe **punishment unless** it explicitly **prohibited** the act, as it is written: "Take heed to yourself, lest you sacrifice your burnt offerings in every place that you see" (Deuteronomy 12:13), **so too here,** with regard to slaughtering, the Torah **did not** prescribe **punishment unless** it explicitly **prohibited** the act as a separate prohibition.

### Hekkesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ – אָמַר קְרָא: ״שָׁם תַּעֲלֶה... וְשָׁם תַּעֲשֶׂה״, מַקִּישׁ שְׁחִיטָה לְהַעֲלָאָה, מָה הַעֲלָאָה לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף שְׁחִיטָה לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** If one considers the matter **according to the one who derives** the prohibition **from a juxtaposition,** it is also as though both prohibitions are written explicitly. The juxtaposition is that **the verse states: "There you shall sacrifice** your burnt offerings **and there you shall do** all that I command you" (Deuteronomy 12:14). Here the verse **juxtaposes slaughtering,** in the phrase: "And there you shall do," **to offering up.** This teaches that **just as there,** with regard to offering up, the Torah **did not** prescribe **punishment unless** it explicitly **prohibited** the act, **so too here,** with regard to slaughtering, the Torah **did not** prescribe **punishment unless** it explicitly **prohibited** the act. Whether one derives the prohibition by verbal analogy or juxtaposition, it is considered as though the Torah explicitly wrote a prohibition with regard to slaughtering, and therefore this would not meet the criteria of Rabbi Yirmeya's dilemma.

A second opinion derives the prohibition from a *hekkesh* rather than a *gezeirah shavah*. The verse states *sham ta'aleh ve-sham ta'aseh* — "there shall you offer up and there shall you do," with the latter phrase encompassing *shechita* and other acts of the sacrificial service. This comparison (*hekkesh*) between *shechita* and *ha'ala* means that just as there is an explicit prohibition for *ha'ala*, so too for *shechita*. By either derivation — *gezeirah shavah* or *hekkesh* — *shechita* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* carries its own distinct *lav*, and the case of two *kareisin* with only one *lav* remains without a clear example.

## Ov and Yidoni

### One Lav, Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת וְלָאו אֶחָד קָאָמְרַתְּ? מָה הִיא – אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira continues to address Rabbi Yirmeya: **Perhaps you are stating** your dilemma with regard to **two** prohibitions punishable by the **death** penalty **and** only **one prohibition** is written with regard to them, and your dilemma is whether one brings two sin offerings if one transgressed both unwittingly, despite the fact that only one prohibition is written with regard to both acts. **What is** such a circumstance? This applies in the case of **a necromancer,** who raises up the spirit of the dead, **and a sorcerer,** who places a certain bone in his mouth for it to speak of its own accord (see Leviticus 20:6, 27).

The *Gemara* then considers a parallel case involving not *karet* but two separate *mitos*: the prohibitions of *ov* and *yidoni*, two forms of necromancy. The verse *lo yimatze vecha shoel ov ve-yidoni* contains a single prohibition covering both. Yet the verse *ov o yidoni mos yumas* — because it says "A or B shall be put to death" — is understood to impose a separate death penalty on each. So we have one *lav* and two *mitos*. If one performed both in a single state of forgetfulness, does one bring two *chata'ot*? This is the unresolved form of the question: one prohibition, two separate punishments.

## Ov and Yidoni…

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וּשְׁנֵיהֶם בְּלָאו אֶחָד נֶאֶמְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The *tanna* in tractate *Karetot* does not count the case of the sorcerer on its own **since both** the sorcerer and the necromancer **were stated in one prohibition:** "Do not turn to the necromancers and the sorcerers" (Leviticus 19:31); therefore, there is no separate obligation to bring an offering for each of them.

*Rabbi Yochanan* holds: *hen u-shneihen be-lav echad ne'emru* — both *ov* and *yidoni* were stated within a single prohibition, and therefore even though there are two separate *mitos*, only one *chatas* is brought. The principle as *Rabbi Yochanan* understands it is that it is the *lav* that defines the category for the purpose of *chatas*. Two separate *lavin* with different names generate separate *chata'ot*, as in the case of *mefatem ve-sach*; a single *lav* with two punishments does not.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says:** The case of the sorcerer is not included in the list of transgressions punishable by *karet* **since it does not involve an action,** but only speech, through the use of a bone, and one does not bring a sin offering for a prohibition that does not involve an action.

*Reish Lakish* disagrees. He holds that the fact that each — *ov* and *yidoni* — carries its own distinct death penalty is sufficient to render them separate categories, such that performing both in one state of forgetfulness would in principle require two *chata'ot*. The debate thus crystallizes: according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, the unit of analysis for *chatas* is the *lav*; according to *Reish Lakish*, a separate punishment, even under a shared *lav*, constitutes a separate category.

The *Gemara* also notes that our *mishna* lists only *ov* and not *yidoni*. *Rabbi Yochanan* explains simply: since it is one *lav* and only one *chatas* is brought, the *mishna* lists one entry, and it chose *ov* because that is the first in the *pasuk*. *Reish Lakish* offers a different explanation, which carries a distinct halachic implication.

## Ma'aseh Requirement

### Yidoni Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן?
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says:** The case of the sorcerer is not included in the list of transgressions punishable by *karet* **since it does not involve an action,** but only speech, through the use of a bone, and one does not bring a sin offering for a prohibition that does not involve an action. The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Reish Lakish, what is the reason he does not state** an explanation **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Yochanan?**

*Reish Lakish* explains why *yidoni* is absent from the *mishna*: *hoil ve-ein bo ma'aseh* — because it involves no action. The *yidoni* practice involves placing a bone in one's mouth and somehow producing sounds through it; one performs no meaningful physical act. Since there is a requirement of *ma'aseh* — an act — for the obligation of *chatas*, and *yidoni* lacks one, it is excluded from the *mishna* regardless of whether one *lav* or two are involved.

### Akiva Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּהָא חֲלוּקִין הֵן בְּמִיתוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר לְךָ: חֲלוּקָּה דְלָאו – הָוֵי חֲלוּקָּה, חֲלוּקָּה דְמִיתָה – לָא הָוְיָא חֲלוּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Reish Lakish disagrees with the explanation of Rabbi Yochanan **because** the cases of a necromancer and a sorcerer **are divided** in the verse **with regard to** the issue of the **death** penalty, i.e., one is liable to receive the death penalty for transgressing either prohibition, as it is stated: "And a man also or a woman, who are a necromancer or a sorcerer shall be put to death" (Leviticus 20:27). Consequently, one would be liable to bring a separate sin offering for the unwitting transgression of either prohibition were it not for the fact that sorcery does not involve an action. **And Rabbi Yochanan** could **say to you** that **a division of a prohibition is** considered **a division** that leads to separate sin offerings, whereas **a division of** separate **death** penalties **is not** considered **a division** in this regard.

This explanation generates a question for *Rabbi Yochanan*: if *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that no action is required, how can *Reish Lakish's* reasoning hold? *Rabbi Yochanan's* response is: *man tanna Keritot Rabbi Akiva hi de-amar lo ba'inan ma'aseh* — the *tanna* of our *mishna* in *Keritot* is *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds no action is required, as evidenced by the fact that the *mishna* includes *megadef* — blasphemy — which involves no act in the conventional sense. If even *megadef* brings a *chatas*, one cannot exclude *yidoni* on the grounds of lacking an act. According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, therefore, *yidoni* is excluded solely because it is subsumed under one *lav* with *ov*, and only one *chatas* is brought.

## Ma'aseh Requirement…

### Minimal Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן דַּאֲכַל חֵלֶב דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אָכַל חֵלֶב נְבֵילָה, אָכַל חֵלֶב מוּקְדָּשִׁין – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֵלֶב מוּקְדָּשִׁין – לוֹקֶה שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Sheshet said:** Here we are dealing with a case **where he ate** forbidden **fat of sacrificial** animals, **and** this ruling **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who renders one liable to bring two sin offerings for eating forbidden fat of sacrificial food. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **ate** forbidden **fat of an animal carcass,** or if he **ate** forbidden **fat of sacrificial** animals, he is **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes, either for transgressing the prohibitions of eating forbidden fat and eating an animal carcass, or for both eating forbidden fat and transgressing the prohibition forbidding a non-priest to partake of sacrificial animals. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat of sacrificial** animals he **is flogged** with **three** sets of lashes, because there are two separate prohibitions of forbidden fat in the case of sacrificial animals, as the Gemara will soon explain, in addition to the prohibition forbidding a non-priest to partake of sacrificial animals.

*Rav Pappa* notes that regardless of the debate about *chatas*, both *karet* and *misa* can be incurred without an act — there are cases of *chiyuv misa* for which everyone agrees, yet a *chatas* is still not brought. The requirement of *ma'aseh* is specific to *chatas*. It is, as the *Gemara* observes, somewhat counterintuitive that one can receive the most severe punishments without an act, but not bring a *chatas* without one.

*Reish Lakish* responds to the question of why he does not follow *Rabbi Yochanan*: *de-ha chalukin hen be-mitoséihen* — because the two forms carry distinct deaths, he regards them as distinct categories. His response to the *ma'aseh* question is different from *Rabbi Yochanan's*: *Rav Pappa* clarifies that *Reish Lakish* holds *Rabbi Akiva* does require some form of action, but sets the threshold very low — *ma'aseh zuta ba'ei*, a minimal act is needed. Moving the lips constitutes such an act. *Yidoni*, in which nothing of that kind occurs, falls below even *Rabbi Akiva's* minimal threshold, which is why even according to the *tanna* of our *mishna* it produces no *chatas*.

## What Is Action

### Ba'al Ov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל אוֹב, מַאי מַעֲשֶׂה אִית בֵּיהּ? הֲקָשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה. מְגַדֵּף, מַאי מַעֲשֶׂה אִית בֵּיהּ? עֲקִימַת שְׂפָתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the case of **a necromancer, what action is there in** this transgression? The Gemara answers: **The striking of his arms** against each other to raise the spirit of the dead **is** considered **an action.** The Gemara further asks: In the case of one who **blasphemes,** who merely speaks, **what action is there in** this transgression? Since Rabbi Akiva holds that an unwitting blasphemer is liable to bring a sin offering, this apparently indicates that no action at all is necessary. The Gemara answers: **The twisting of his lips** while he speaks **is** considered **an action.**

The *Gemara* then examines what constitutes a *ma'aseh* in each relevant case. By *ba'al ov*: *hakashat zero'otav havei ma'aseh* — the flapping or tensing of the arms in the conjuring act is considered an action. That is a clear enough physical movement that all opinions can agree it constitutes an act.

By *megadef*: *akimat sefatav havei ma'aseh* — moving one's lips is considered an action. This is *Rabbi Akiva's* position, or, as *Reish Lakish* frames it, the basis on which *Rabbi Akiva* holds that a minimal act suffices.

### Hishtachava'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין: הֲקָשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה זוּטָא אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן. מֵיתִיבִי: בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, כְּגוֹן שֶׁזִּיבַּח, וְקִיטֵּר, וְנִיסֵּךְ, וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה. וְקַשְׁיָא לַן, הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה לֵית בַּהּ מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **enters our mind** that **the striking of** the necromancer's **arms** against each other **is** considered **a minor action even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and they would deem him liable. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **With regard to idol worship, one is liable** to be punished **only for a matter that involves an action, for example where one slaughtered** an offering, **or burned** incense, **or poured** a libation, **or bowed. And** the following matter poses **a difficulty to us: Bowing does not involve an action.**

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a separate discussion in *Sanhedrin*: by *avodah zarah*, one is only liable if one performs an act — *kegon shezivach ve-kiter ve-nisach ve-hishtachavah*. Among these, *hishtachavayah* — bowing down — is listed as an act. But the *Gemara* asks: is bowing down really an act? One is simply moving one's body, not interacting with anything in the physical world. The same conceptual question applies to flapping one's arms and moving one's lips.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר לָא בָּעִינַן מַעֲשֶׂה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, כְּפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: An action is not required** for one to be liable to bring a sin offering for transgressing a prohibition. **And Rabbi Yochanan said: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as **the bending of his height** when he bows **is** considered **an action.**

*Reish Lakish* answers: *ha mani Rabbi Akiva hi de-amar lo ba'inan ma'aseh* — including *hishtachavayah* as an act reflects *Rabbi Akiva's* position, which accepts minimal action. *Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and says: *afilu teima Rabanan, kfifas komaso havei ma'aseh* — even the *Rabbanan* would agree that bowing one's entire body is an act, even if they would not accept merely moving the lips. The degree of full-body physical movement crosses a threshold that lip-movement does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִכְּלָל דִּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן כְּפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ לְרַבָּנַן לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה. הֲקָשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה?!
>
> **Translation:** One can conclude from here **by inference that Reish Lakish maintains** that **according to** the opinion **of the Rabbis, the bending of his height is not** considered **an action.** If so, can it be said that **the striking of his arms** against each other, which is less of a movement than bending one's height, **is** considered **an action?**

This generates a further inference. According to *Reish Lakish*, who says bowing is only *Rabbi Akiva's* standard and not the *Rabbanan's*, flapping the arms should also fall below the *Rabbanan's* threshold — and therefore the *Rabbanan* should have explicitly disagreed with the inclusion of *ba'al ov* in the *mishna*, just as they disagree with *megadef*. The *Gemara* answers: they do — *chada mi-tartei katani*, the *Rabbanan* only specified one objection, but their reasoning applies equally to both *megadef* and *ba'al ov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא כִּי קָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲקָשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה זוּטָא, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל לְרַבָּנַן לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** you must explain that **when Reish Lakish says the striking of his arms** against each other **is** considered **a minor action,** he is speaking only **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva; but according to** the opinion **of the Rabbis** it **is not** considered **an action.**

## Ba'al Ov Incense

### Ulla Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: בַּעַל אוֹב דְּקָתָנֵי בִּמְקַטֵּר לַשֵּׁד.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** The reason the Rabbis did not state: Excluding one who blasphemes and a necromancer, is that the action of the **necromancer that** the mishna **teaches** is referring **to** one who **burns** incense **to a demon** in order to raise the dead, which is a full-fledged action.

*Ulla* offers an entirely different account of the *ba'al ov* act. He says: *ba'al ov de-katni be-makter le-shed* — the *ba'al ov* in the *mishna* refers specifically to one who burns incense to invoke a spirit. This is not the flapping of the arms but a real, concrete action: the burning of incense. If so, the question of whether minimal physical movement constitutes an act becomes irrelevant for *ba'al ov* — it involves a genuine material act.

### Rava Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, הַיְינוּ עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מְקַטֵּר לַשֵּׁד עַל מְנָת לְחַבְּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this** answer from Ulla: **If so,** i.e., if the necromancer burns incense to a demon, **this is** the same as **an idol worshipper,** which is already mentioned in the mishna. **Rather, Rava said:** The mishna is referring to one who **burns** incense **to a demon in order to gather it** together with other demons, i.e., he does not burn incense to a demon by means of a rite but in a manner of sorcery, to bring the demons to one place to then make use of them.

*Rava* challenges: if one is burning incense to a spirit, is that not simply *avodah zarah*? *Rava* answers his own question: *mekater le-shed al menat le-chavro* — one is not burning incense to the spirit as an act of worship but as a means of invoking it, of making contact with or exercising control over the spirit world. The intent is not worship but invocation. That distinction takes it outside the category of *avodah zarah*.

### Chover Chaver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, הָיִינוּ חוֹבֵר חָבֶר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה חוֹבֵר חָבֶר כְּגוֹן זֶה, בִּסְקִילָה. וְאֶלָּא חוֹבֵר חֶבֶר דִּבְלָאו – הֵדֵין הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **If so, this is** the same as **a *ḥover ḥever*,** one who gathers together, which is stated in the Torah as a separate prohibition (Deuteronomy 18:11) and which is a prohibition that does not carry the punishment of *karet*. Rava **said to him:** It is true that such an individual is also a *ḥover ḥever*, but **the Torah stated: A *ḥover ḥever* such as this,** who gathers demons together, is included in the category of a necromancer and therefore his punishment is death **by stoning,** and transgressing this prohibition also results in *karet*. Abaye inquired: **But** then the case of **a *ḥover ḥever*, which** is **a prohibition** that does not entail *karet*, **what** is **it?**

*Abaye* presses: if burning incense to invoke a spirit is not *avodah zarah*, then it is *chover chaver* — invoking spirits — which is a separate prohibition, a *lav*, not a capital offense. *Abaye* asks why the *Gemara* treats it as producing *chiyuv misa*. The response is: the Torah treats this particular form of *ba'al ov* — invoking through incense — as carrying *sekila*, not merely a *lav*. The general category of *chover chaver*, which encompasses invoking small spirits, even gathering snakes and scorpions through incantation, carries only a *lav*. But the specific act of the *ba'al ov* in this form is treated more severely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְחוֹבֵר חָבֶר״ – אֶחָד חוֹבֵר גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד חוֹבֵר קָטָן, וַאֲפִילּוּ חוֹבֵר לִנְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים – חַיָּיב. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמִיצְמַד זִיבּוּרָא וְעַקְרַבָּא – אָסוּר, וְאִי קָאָתוּ בָּתְרֵיהּ – שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And a *ḥover ḥaver*"** (Deuteronomy 18:11), this is referring to both **one** who uses sorcery and thereby **gathers large** beasts into one place, **and** also to **one** who **gathers small** beasts into one place, **and even** to one who **gathers** into one place **snakes and scorpions;** all of these individuals are **liable.** With regard to this prohibition **Abaye says:** In the case of **this one, who** through sorcery **wants to gather** together **a hornet and a scorpion,** this is **prohibited. But if they are pursuing him** and he merely seeks to defend himself, it is **permitted.**

*Abaye* further elaborates: *hai man de-ba'ei le-mitzmed diburei ve-akraba* — one who seeks to gather bees or scorpions through invocation is forbidden (*chover chaver*). However, if they are coming toward one unprovoked and one uses such means to protect oneself, that may be permitted — either because it falls under *pikuach nefesh* permitting a *lav*, or because the Torah prohibited *chover chaver* in the context of active invocation for one's benefit, not passive self-defense, and the purpose defines the act.

## What Is Action…

### Megadef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר כְּפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה, עֲקִימַת שְׂפָתָיו הָוְיָא מַעֲשֶׂה גַּבֵּי מְגַדֵּף? אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי מְגַדֵּף, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בַּלֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **his lips should be** considered **an action in** the case of one who **blasphemes. Rava said:** The case of one who **blasphemes is different, since it is** primarily **in the heart.**

Returning to *Rabbi Yochanan's* position, the *Gemara* presses: if *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that bowing down is an act that everyone agrees to, why is moving one's lips not equally an act for everyone? Both are purely physical movements without interaction with the external world.

*Rava* answers: *shani megadef ho'il ve-yeshno ba-lev* — *megadef* is fundamentally different because it is in the heart. The curse is not essentially about the physical movement of the lips; it is about the expression of a thought, an attitude toward God. As *Rashi* notes in *Sanhedrin*, if one happens to utter the words of *giduf* without intending them — without understanding what one is saying — it would not constitute an act of *megadef*. The physical utterance is merely the vehicle for an internal expression. Bowing down, by contrast, is inherently about the physical act of prostration; the act itself is what it is, regardless of what passes through one's mind while doing it. The contrast between *megadef* and *hishtachavayah* is therefore not one of magnitude of physical movement, but of what is fundamentally constitutive of each act.

### Eidim Zomemim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: יָצְאוּ עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְאַמַּאי? הָא ״עַל פִּי״ כְּתִיב בָּהֶן! אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנָן בִּרְאִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** from that which is taught in a different context. It is stated in a *baraita* that all who unwittingly transgress prohibitions punishable by death are liable to bring sin offerings, **except for conspiring witnesses,** who are not obligated to bring sin offerings, **as** their transgression **does not involve an action. But why** is that so? **It is written with regard to** such witnesses: **"At the mouth** of two witnesses" (Deuteronomy 17:6). They acted through speech, and the twisting of their lips should be considered an action, as they are liable for what they actually said, not for what was in their hearts. **Rava said:** The case of **conspiring witnesses is different, since their** transgression **is** primarily **through sight,** i.e., the important part of their testimony is what they saw, which is not an action.

The *Gemara* cites a further challenge: we find elsewhere that *eidim zomemim* — false witnesses — are described as an act *she-ein bahem ma'aseh*, lacking an act. Yet they speak. If *Rabbi Yochanan* says moving one's lips is an act, why is testifying falsely not considered an act?

*Rava* answers: *shani eidim zomemim ho'il ve-yeshnam bi-re'iyah* — false testimony is fundamentally about what one saw, about an act of *re'iyah*, of seeing and bearing witness. It is the attestation of an observed fact that constitutes the substance of testimony, not the physical act of speaking. The *Gemara* adds that *Rav Hai Gaon*, according to one reading, understood this line differently, with the *girsa* reading *bi-i'ah* rather than *bi-re'iyah* — and his interpretation was that one witness can fulfill the act of false testimony by merely nodding assent while the other speaks, making no movement of the lips at all. Since testimony can be completed without even moving one's lips, it is categorized as *ein bo ma'aseh*.

The discussion across these sugyas thus coheres around a single conceptual axis: the question of what constitutes an act for the purpose of *chiyuv chatas*, how minimal that act can be, and when an apparently physical movement is rendered legally non-corporeal because the essence of the transgression lies in thought, speech, or attestation rather than in a deed in the world.

## Ochel Chelev

### Multiple Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹכֵל חֵלֶב. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כׇּל חֵלֶב שׁוֹר וְכֶשֶׂב וָעֵז לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna included in its list of those liable to receive *karet* **one who eats** forbidden **fat.** With regard to this, **the Sages taught** a *baraita* which deals with the verse: **"You shall eat no fat of ox, or sheep, or goat"** (Leviticus 7:23). This verse serves **to render one liable** to receive lashes **for each and every one,** i.e., one who eats the fat of an ox, and a sheep, and a goat is liable to receive three sets of lashes. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. And the Rabbis say he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes.

A *beraisa* is cited: *kol chelev shor ve-chesev ve-ez lo tocheilu* — do not eat any fat of ox, sheep, or goat. The question is whether this verse imposes a single prohibition on eating forbidden fat generically, or whether the separate mention of each animal creates distinct prohibitions. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds: *le-chayev al kol achas ve-achas* — one is liable separately for each animal, producing separate punishments. *Ve-chachamim omrim eino chayav ela achas* — the Sages say there is only one liability.

One might assume the debate turns on the broader principle of *lav she-bichlalos* — whether a prohibition covering multiple items generates separate *malkus* for each. But the *Gemara* clarifies that this is in fact a *malkus* question, not a *chatas* question, and the dispute is more specific.

### Yishmael Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* נֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: לוֹקִין עַל לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין לוֹקִין עַל לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that Rabbi Yishmael and the Rabbis **disagree about this** matter, **that Rabbi Yishmael maintains one is flogged for** violating **a general prohibition and the Rabbis maintain one is not flogged for** violating **a general prohibition.**

*Rabbi Yishmael's* reasoning: had the Torah intended merely to prohibit eating fat with no special significance to the list of animals, it could have written simply *kol chelev lo tocheilu*. Why specify ox, sheep, and goat? *Shema mina le-challek* — the specification must be to tell us that each constitutes a distinct prohibition, and one who eats all three in one state of forgetfulness receives separate punishments for each.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם סָבַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֵין לוֹקִין עַל לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דִּמְיַיתְּרִי לֵיהּ קְרָאֵי – נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״כׇּל חֵלֶב לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, ״שׁוֹר וְכֶשֶׂב וָעֵז״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, Rabbi Yishmael** also **maintains** that **one is not flogged for** violating **a general prohibition, but here it is different, as** elements of **the verse are superfluous.** The Gemara explains: **Let the verse write** only: **You shall eat no fat,** and all the individual types of fat would be included. **Why do I** need the additional terms: **"Ox, or sheep, or goat"? Learn from it** that the verse serves **to separate** between them and render one liable to receive a separate set of lashes for eating each type of forbidden fat.

### Sages Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? אִי לָא כְּתִיב: ״שׁוֹר וְכֶשֶׂב וָעֵז״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ חֵלֶב חַיָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע, לְהָכִי כְּתַב: ״שׁוֹר כֶּשֶׂב וָעֵז״, לְמֵימְרָא: דְּחֵלֶב שׁוֹר וְכֶשֶׂב וָעֵז הוּא דְּאָסוּר, אֲבָל דְּחַיָּה שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** how do they respond to this reading of the verse? The Gemara answers: The Rabbis would say that no element of the verse is superfluous, since **if** it had **not written: "Ox, or sheep, or goat," I would say** that **even** the **fat of an undomesticated animal is included** in the prohibition. **For this** reason the verse **writes: "Ox, or sheep, or goat," to say that it is** the **fat of an ox, or a sheep, or a goat that is forbidden, but** all the fats **of an undomesticated animal are permitted.**

The Sages respond: *lo yalfinan lav mi-karet* — one may not derive the content of a *lav* from what is stated by a *karet*. What appears in the context of *karet* applies to *karet*; one cannot carry it over to establish the scope of a separate *lav*. Therefore, the listing of all three animals in the *lav* verse was necessary to define the prohibition's scope, and it carries no additional meaning. There is accordingly only one *lav* and only one liability for *malkus*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דְּקָסָבַר: אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב ״כׇּל חֵלֶב שׁוֹר לֹא תֹאכְלוּ״, ״כֶּשֶׂב וָעֵז״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this interpretation of the dispute: The Rabbis **spoke well to** Rabbi Yishmael, i.e., their response is persuasive. The Gemara suggests an alternative explanation: **Rather, this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yishmael, as he holds: If so,** that the verse serves to exclude the fat of only an undomesticated animal from the prohibition, then **let it write: You shall eat no fat of an ox,** which would be understood as a paradigm representing every type of domesticated animal. **Why do I** need the phrase: **"Sheep or goat"? Learn from it** that the verse serves **to separate** between them and render one liable to receive a separate set of lashes for eating each type of forbidden fat.

## Lav from Karet

### Context Mention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כִּי כְּתַב קְרָא – בְּעִנְיָינָא דְקָדָשִׁים, וְדָבָר לָמֵד מֵעִנְיָינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **When the verse** concerning forbidden fat **is written** it is **in the context of sacrificial** animals, since the subsequent verse concerning the prohibition against eating forbidden fat states: "For whoever eats the fat of the animal of which men present an offering of fire to the Lord, even the soul that eats it shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:25). The verse categorizes an animal whose fat one may not eat as an "animal of which men present an offering of fire to the Lord," i.e., offerings. **And** one of the thirteen hermeneutical principles is: **A matter** is **derived from its context,** i.e., one interprets a verse according to the context in which it is written.

The verse containing the *lav* — *kol chelev shor ve-chesev ve-ez lo tocheilu* — appears in proximity to the verse containing the *karet*: *ki kol ochel chelev min ha-behemah asher takrivu mimena korban la-Hashem*. The *karet* verse specifies the prohibition in terms of *behemah asher takrivu* — an animal brought as a *korban* — and makes clear it applies to ox, sheep, and goat. Since the *lav* verse appears in the same context, could one not read the animals listed in the *lav* as already implied by the *karet* verse, making the explicit listing superfluous and therefore interpretively significant?

### Yishmael Learns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״בַּד בְּבַד״? אָמַר רָבִינָא: שֶׁלֹּא יַנִּיחַ מִשְׁקָל בְּמִשְׁקָל וְיִשְׁקוֹל. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא יָלְפִינַן דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָינוֹ? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יָלְפִינַן דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָינוֹ, וְהָכָא בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: לְמֵידִין לָאו מִלָּאו בֵּין מִלָּאו וּבֵין מִלָּאו דְּכָרֵת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Can one conclude **by inference that the Rabbis maintain we do not derive** *halakhot* by employing the hermeneutical principle of: **A matter** is **derived from its context?** This cannot be correct, as it is an accepted basic principle of exegesis. The Gemara answers: **No, everyone** agrees that **we do derive** *halakhot* by employing the hermeneutical principle of: **A matter** is **derived from its context. And here** it is **about this** issue **that they disagree: Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that **we derive** the *halakhot* of one **prohibition from** the *halakhot* of another **prohibition,** and this applies **whether** it is **from** a standard **prohibition,** whose transgression results in the punishment of lashes, **or whether from a prohibition** whose transgression results in the punishment **of *karet*.**

*Rabbi Yishmael* applies this logic: *davar ha-lamed me-inyano* — a teaching derived from its context. Since by *karet* the limitation to *shor, chesev, va-ez* is clear, one could have inferred the same for the *lav*. The explicit mention of all three in the *lav* verse is thus not needed to define the scope of the prohibition; it therefore carries additional meaning and signals distinct prohibitions for each animal.

### Sages Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָאו מִלָּאו יָלְפִינַן, לָאו מִכָּרֵת לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** maintain that generally **we derive** the *halakhot* of one **prohibition from** the *halakhot* of another **prohibition,** but **we do not derive** the *halakhot* of **a prohibition from** the punishment of ***karet*.**

The Sages respond: *lo yalfinan lav mi-karet* — one may not derive a *lav* from a *karet* — learning the content of a prohibition from the context of a penalty — or whether, as the Sages hold, such cross-derivation is inadmissible.

The debate thus ultimately hinges on whether *Rabbi Yishmael* is correct that one may derive a *lav* from a *karet* — learning the content of a prohibition from the context of a penalty — or whether, as the Sages hold, such cross-derivation is inadmissible.
