# Pesachim 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sreifah Requirement

### Debate Overview

The daf opens at the midpoint of 27b with a *mishnah* that encapsulates one of the central practical disputes of *hilchot Pesach*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer ein biyur chametz ela sreifa* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the only valid method of destroying *chametz* is burning. The *chachamim*, by contrast, rule that one may crumble and scatter the *chametz* to the wind, or cast it into the sea.

This debate arrives after several dapim of sustained analysis concerning *issurei hana'ah* — the category of objects from whose use one may derive no benefit — and the various questions attending that category: whether the prohibition is connected to *achilah*, whether it extends to *shelo kederach hana'atan*, and what precisely counts as *hana'ah* in the first place. The discussion of benefit derived from wood and from ashes led naturally to the principle that the residue of objects subject to a *mitzvas sreifah* becomes permitted once burned, unlike most other prohibited items whose ashes remain forbidden. With that background in place, the *Gemara* now turns to the question of *chametz* itself: is it subject to a *mitzvas sreifah* like *nosar* and *kodshim*, or is the Torah's directive more broadly construed?

It is worth pausing on the company *chametz* keeps here. The things one burns — primarily *kodshim* and *avodah zarah* — belong to a paradigm of complete *issur*, total taboo. *Shor haniskal*, by contrast, one buries; burning is reserved for objects surrounded by an especially dense web of prohibition. *Chametz*, as we know, is *assur behana'ah* based on a prohibition of *achilah*, which would seem to place it in a less severe category. And yet Rabbi Yehuda maintains it belongs with the things one burns — that it too partakes of this quality of radical detachment, of something one must utterly eliminate rather than merely cease to use.

### Kal Vachomer Argument

Rabbi Yehuda's first derivation, preserved in a *braita*, takes the form of a *kal vachomer*. The argument runs: *u-mah nosar she'eino be-val yeira'eh u-val yimatze, ta'un sreifa* — *nosar* (sacrificial meat left past its permitted time), which does not trigger the prohibition of *bal yeira'eh u-val yimatze* (the prohibition against *chametz* being found in one's possession), must nonetheless be burned. *Chametz*, which does trigger *bal yeira'eh u-val yimatze*, should all the more so require burning.

The internal logic deserves scrutiny. The connection being drawn is between the gravity of an object's prohibited status and the requirement to destroy it by fire. If something as "merely" prohibited as *nosar* must be burned, surely something as deeply taboo as *chametz* — which one may not even own — should require the same treatment. There is, however, a complication. By *nosar*, one has already transgressed by leaving the sacrifice over; the act of burning follows upon an established violation. With *chametz*, Rabbi Yehuda's own position (as *Rashi* notes explicitly) is that the *mitzvah* is to burn it *before* one transgresses *bal yeira'eh*, so as to prevent the transgression in the first place. The logic of the *kal vachomer* — connecting the requirement to burn to the severity of the *bal yeira'eh* violation — is therefore somewhat strained when the burning is meant to forestall rather than respond to that very violation.

### Chachamim Rebuttal

The *chachamim* challenge Rabbi Yehuda's *kal vachomer* with a pointed counter-principle: *kol din she'ata dan techilato le-hachmir ve-sofo le-hakel eino din* — any *kal vachomer* that begins as a stringency but ends as a leniency is no valid *kal vachomer* at all. What leniency emerges from requiring *sreifah*? *Lo matza etzim le-sarfo, yehei yoshev u-vatel* — if one cannot find wood with which to burn the *chametz*, one would simply be exempt from doing anything at all, since one is an *oness*. But the Torah says *tashbitu se'or mi-bateichem* — destroy the leaven from your houses — implying any means of disposal will do. By insisting on one specific method, you paradoxically relieve a person of responsibility when that method is unavailable. The *chachamim*'s ruling, they argue, is the true stringency: demanding that one exhaust every possible means of removal, with no escape through the unavailability of any single method.

This argument points toward something important beyond its immediate dialectical purpose. The *chachamim* are not simply proposing a different technique for the same underlying obligation. They are articulating a different conception of what the obligation *is*. This distinction will bear fruit in the conceptual discussion later.

It is also worth noting the broader *hashkafic* point raised in connection with this exchange: *chumros* carry real costs and often generate *kulos* in unexpected places. The assumption that being *machmir* is always without downside is precisely what the *chachamim* are disputing here — the *chumra* of requiring *sreifah* produces the *kula* of exempting someone who lacks wood entirely.

### Heikesh from Nosar

Rebuffed on the *kal vachomer*, *chazar Rabbi Yehuda ve-danu din acher* — Rabbi Yehuda returned and offered a different derivation, this time framed as an analogy rather than a logical inference. *Nosar assur be-achilah ve-chametz assur be-achilah* — both *nosar* and *chametz* are forbidden in eating. *Ma nosar be-sreifa af chametz be-sreifa* — just as *nosar* requires burning, so too *chametz*.

This derivation is structured as a *heikesh*, drawing an analogy between two cases that share the quality of being forbidden in *achilah*. The *chachamim* immediately counter: *neveila tochiach* — a *neveila* (an animal that died without proper *shechita*) is similarly forbidden in eating, yet no one suggests it must be burned. Rabbi Yehuda responds: *hefresh* — there is a distinction. *Nosar* is not merely *assur be-achilah*; it is *assur be-achilah u-va-hana'ah*. I am not proposing a rule that anything forbidden in eating must be burned; I am proposing that things totally off-limits — forbidden both to eat and to derive benefit from — require *sreifah*.

### Nosar Distinctions

The *chachamim* come back with another counterexample: *shor haniskal yochiach* — the ox that gored and killed a person is *assur be-achilah u-va-hana'ah*, and yet it is not burned; it is buried. So the combination of *issur achilah* and *issur hana'ah* alone cannot be what generates the *sreifah* requirement.

Rabbi Yehuda refines his position once more: *nosar assur be-achilah u-va-hana'ah v'yesh bo karet* — *nosar* carries the added dimension of *karet*. The combination of *issur hana'ah* and *karet* marks it as categorically taboo, something completely excised from permissible existence. And *chametz* likewise is *assur be-achilah u-va-hana'ah v'yesh bo karet*. *Ma nosar be-sreifa af chametz be-sreifa*. This seems like a compelling formulation.

The *chachamim* try once more with *chelbo shel shor haniskal* — the fat of a gored ox. The fat carries *karet* on account of being *chelev*, and the entire animal is *assur behana'ah* on account of being *shor haniskal*, yet there is no *sreifah* requirement. To this, Rabbi Yehuda does not directly respond, and the discussion moves in a different direction. The counterargument is somewhat artificial in structure — the *karet* and the *issur hana'ah* here inhere in two separate *isurim* that happen to coincide in one object rather than belonging to a single unified prohibition — which may explain why Rabbi Yehuda treats it as less than fully compelling.

It is worth pausing to note the unusual literary character of this extended exchange. It is all in Hebrew, and consists of a sustained back-and-forth between *tanna'im* debating not the *halacha* itself but the derivations that underlie it. This is different from a standard *mishnah* or *braita*, which typically states the law apodictically. It also differs from classic *midrash halacha*, which anchors itself in a *pasuk* and derives from there. Here, the disputants are working from the *halacha* and seeking to establish or undermine its derivations, which gives the passage a distinctive texture. It may well originate as a *midrash halacha* on *tashbitu se'or mi-bateichem*, subsequently transposed and embedded in its current context — the *mechilta* may well contain a parallel.

### Bal Totireu Parallel

At the top of 28a, Rabbi Yehuda attempts a third derivation. *Nosar yeshno b'val toisiru ve-chametz b'val toisiru* — both *nosar* and *chametz* share the prohibition of *bal totireu*, the injunction not to leave things over. The prohibition against leaving *nosar* past its time and the prohibition of possessing *chametz* past its permitted point reflect a shared conceptual structure: both involve a categorical imperative not to allow a certain thing to persist. This commonality, added to the previous shared features of *issur hana'ah* and *karet*, reinforces the analogy. *Ma nosar be-sreifa af chametz be-sreifa*.

### Asham Talui Challenge

The *chachamim*'s response to this third derivation is unexpected and comes from a surprising direction. *Asham talui ve-chatat ha-of haba al hasafek l'devarecha yochichu* — the *asham talui* and the *chatat ha-of* that is brought under conditions of doubt, according to your own position, serve as a counterproof.

To understand the force of this: an *asham talui* is the offering brought when one suspects but cannot confirm that one has committed an inadvertent sin warranting a *chatat* — the classic case being two pieces before one, one forbidden fat and one permitted, and one was eaten without knowing which. A *chatat ha-of haba al hasafek* arises in the context of purification, as when a woman has a miscarriage and is uncertain whether the fetus was viable enough to obligate her to bring a bird *chatat*; since without that offering she can never again eat *kodshim*, she brings it, but the *kohanim* do not eat the bird because its status is uncertain.

Now, both of these objects are *assur be-achilah u-va-hana'ah* and carry the weight of *karet* — they are *kodshim* and thus subject to *me'ila* — and they would seem to fall squarely within the framework Rabbi Yehuda has constructed. One would expect, by his reasoning, that if they become invalid (*nosar*, *tamei*, etc.) they must be burned like all other *kodshim*. Yet Rabbi Yehuda himself holds that these are buried rather than burned. The *chachamim* say: by your own logic, you have created an exception that undermines your general rule.

*Shatak Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda was silenced. He had no response.

*Rav Yosef* captures the moment with a folk saying: the ladle the carpenter carved out burned his own tongue on mustard — he was undone by his own work. *Abaye* offers another formulation: the one who made the stockade must sit in it himself. And *Rava* adds: the arrowmaker is killed by his own arrows — *midlei yadei mishtalem*, he is paid back by the work of his own hands. These are not expressions of gloating so much as a kind of wry, teasing appreciation of the irony that one's own carefully constructed position has become the instrument of one's defeat.

The practical *halachic* upshot is significant: there is a major *machlokes rishonim* about whether we rule like Rabbi Yehuda or the *chachamim*. Had it not been for Rabbi Yehuda's unusual position on the *chatat ha-of*, his argument would have been considerably stronger. The very position that created a problem for his opponents' counterargument is the one that silenced him.

## Chachamim Methods

### Multiple Options

The *mishnah*'s formulation of the *chachamim*'s position is: *mefareir ve-zoreh la-ruach* — one crumbles and scatters to the wind — *o matil la-yam* — or one throws it into the sea. The word *zoreh* here should be noted: it means to winnow or scatter, not to sow, and is spelled without an *ayin*.

### Parsing Ambiguity

An *ibaya* is raised: how exactly is the *chachamim*'s ruling to be read? The question is whether the requirement to crumble (*pirur*) applies to both methods of disposal or only to the first. One reading would have it: crumble and scatter to the wind — or crumble and cast into the sea. The alternative reading: crumble and scatter to the wind — or simply cast it into the sea as it is, without crumbling. The first reading makes *pirur* a prerequisite for both methods; the second makes *pirur* necessary only when throwing to the wind (since otherwise it will fall to the ground and be found by others), while throwing into the sea suffices without preliminary crumbling.

The practical question is immediate and very concrete: if one finds *chametz* in one's house, does one need to crumble it before flushing it down the drain? That is precisely the modern instantiation of *matil la-yam*, and whether *pirur* is required first is what the *Gemara* is working toward.

A parallel *mishnah* in the context of *avodah zarah* surfaces here. Rabbi Yosei rules that one who wishes to dispose of an idol should grind it (*shocheik*) and scatter it to the wind or cast it into the sea. The same ambiguity applies: does *shechika* (grinding) accompany both options or only the first?

### Rabbah's Distinction

*Rabbah* proposes that the two cases — *chametz* and *avodah zarah* — may operate under different rules. With *avodah zarah*, when the *mishnah* speaks of casting into the sea, it means the *Yam HaMelach* (the Dead Sea), which no one traverses and from which no one draws. Even a pristine, intact idol floating in the Dead Sea poses no real danger of retrieval or use, and therefore *shechika* is not required before casting it there. *Chametz*, by contrast, when it says cast into the sea, means any body of water — and people do encounter those. A floating loaf of bread in an ordinary river could easily wash up and be found. Therefore, *pirur* is required before casting *chametz* into the water.

### Rav Yosef Response

*Rav Yosef* inverts this reasoning entirely. *Avodah zarah delo mami'isa* — an idol does not dissolve or become disgusting when submerged in water; it remains intact and recognizable wherever it ends up. Therefore *shechika* is required regardless of where one throws it. *Chametz de-mami'is* — bread, by contrast, becomes soggy, dissolves, and renders itself effectively unrecognizable and unappetizing wherever one throws it. Therefore *pirur* is never required for *chametz* since the natural process of submersion accomplishes the same thing.

### Conflicting Braitot

The *Gemara* then produces *braitot* supporting each position. In support of *Rava*: a *braita* states explicitly that one traveling in the wilderness should crumble and scatter his *chametz* to the four winds, and one traveling by ship should crumble and cast it into the sea — the verb *mefareir* appearing in both cases, explicitly requiring crumbling for *chametz* in all situations. In support of *Rav Yosef*: a *braita* states that for *avodah zarah*, one traveling in the wilderness grinds and scatters, and one traveling by ship grinds and casts into the sea — *shechika* required in both cases, with no apparent distinction between bodies of water.

Taking these *braitot* at face value would seem to favor neither *Rabbah* nor *Rav Yosef* but rather to suggest that crumbling is always required for *chametz* and grinding always required for *avodah zarah*. But that is not the conclusion the *Gemara* draws.

### Resolution via Geography

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradictions as follows. *Shechika kashya le-Rava* — the *braita* requiring grinding of *avodah zarah* into the sea seems to challenge *Rabbah*, who said no grinding is needed when throwing into the *Yam HaMelach*. *Pirur kashya le-Rav Yosef* — the *braita* requiring crumbling of *chametz* before casting into water seems to challenge *Rav Yosef*, who said *chametz* dissolves on its own. The resolution: *shechika le-Rava lo kashya* — for *Rabbah*, the *braita* requiring grinding of *avodah zarah* refers to ordinary bodies of water, not the *Yam HaMelach*. Into the *Yam HaMelach* no grinding is required; into other seas it is. *Rabbah* never disputed the latter. *Pirur le-Rav Yosef lo kashya* — the *braita* requiring crumbling of *chametz* refers to wheat kernels (*chitei*), not to bread (*nahama*). Bread dissolves readily when submerged and requires no crumbling. Scattered wheat kernels, however, may not dissolve and therefore do require dispersal — though the practical meaning of "crumbling" wheat is simply ensuring the kernels are scattered rather than bundled together in a sack, as *Rashi* explains. There is no practical concern about *gebrochts* in this context, it should be said, since that is a much later *minhag*.

### Conceptual Framework

With this analysis in hand, a deeper conceptual point emerges — one articulated by *Rav Chayim HaLevi* on the *Rambam*. The *chachamim*'s willingness to allow disposal by methods short of burning — including throwing an intact idol into the *Yam HaMelach* without any destruction of the object itself — reveals that their understanding of the obligation is fundamentally different from Rabbi Yehuda's. For the *chachamim*, the concern is not that a specific act of destruction be performed upon the object. The concern is that the *chametz* be removed from human access and possession. Throwing something into the *Yam HaMelach* without grinding it does not destroy it in any technical sense; it merely renders it inaccessible. That suffices according to the *chachamim* because their operative category is not *hashchatah* (destruction) but *bi'ur* in the sense of removal from one's domain and from people's reach.

*Rabbi Akiva Eiger* raises a related question: the *Tur* rules that if one burned one's *chametz*, the ashes are *mutar behana'ah*. This is based on the principle of *na'aseh mitzvato* — one has fulfilled the *mitzvah* associated with the object, and so the object's prohibited character is exhausted. But why should the ashes be *mutar* even according to the *chachamim*, who permit other means of destruction? There is still a *mitzvas tashbitu* — so one has fulfilled the *mitzvah* through burning. *Rav Chayim* answers that the distinction is precisely this: if the *mitzvah* is *sreifah* — a specific act of burning performed upon the object — then the *Torah*'s concern is focused on the object and on what was done to it. When that act is performed, the object's *mitzvah* has been fulfilled, and the resulting ashes are free. But if the *Torah*'s directive is to get the *chametz* out of one's possession and away from people, then the *Torah* is not focused on the object at all. There is no concept of doing something *to* this object; there is only the concept of getting rid of it. Burning, under this conception, does not release the ashes, because the *Torah* never focused on the object to begin with. The ashes of burned *chametz*, according to the *chachamim*'s conceptual framework, would remain *assur behana'ah*.

## Chametz Shel Nochri

### Mishnah Ruling

The next *mishnah* presents a clean contrast: *chametz shel nochri she-avar alav ha-Pesach mutar behana'ah* — *chametz* owned by a non-Jew over which Pesach has passed is permitted for benefit. No *aveira* was committed; the prohibition did not apply to the non-Jew; once Pesach ends, the *chametz* is simply *chametz* again and may be used. *Ve-shel Yisrael assur behana'ah* — *chametz* that was owned by a Jew throughout Pesach, however, remains *assur behana'ah* even after Pesach ends. The *mishnah* derives this from *she-ne'emar lo yeira'eh lecha se'or* — since you as a Jew transgressed by owning it, it remains forbidden as a consequence. Whether this post-Pesach prohibition is *mi-de'oraisa* or *mi-derabbanan* is a matter of dispute, and the *Gemara* will address it.

## When Chametz Forbidden

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

The *Gemara* opens its analysis of this *mishnah* by asking: *mani matnitin* — whose position does our *mishnah* reflect? The answer, striking in its directness, is: *lo Rabbi Yehuda ve-lo Rabbi Shimon ve-lo Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* — it fits none of the three *tanna'im* we know to have debated the question of when *chametz* is *assur behana'ah*. The *Gemara* then cites a *braita* that lays out all three positions.

Rabbi Yehuda's position: *chametz bein lifnei zemano u-vein le-achar zemano over alav be-lav* — eating *chametz* before its time (meaning from the sixth hour of *Erev Pesach*, when there is already an obligation to destroy it) and after its time (after Pesach ends) both constitute a violation of a *lav*. *Toch zemano, over alav be-lav ve-charet* — during Pesach itself, one transgresses both a *lav* and incurs *karet*. So for Rabbi Yehuda, there is a biblical negative prohibition in all three periods; the difference is only that the prohibition during Pesach carries *karet* as well.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon holds the opposite: *chametz lifnei zemano u-le-achar zemano eino over alav be-lav klum* — before and after Pesach, one transgresses nothing at all. *Toch zemano, over alav be-lav ve-charet* — during Pesach, one transgresses both the *lav* and *karet*.

This requires clarification, since we have operated throughout these dapim with the assumption that eating *chametz* from midday of *Erev Pesach* is at minimum biblically prohibited. *Tosafot* clarifies that Rabbi Shimon does not mean one may blithely eat *chametz* during the sixth hour. He holds that there is a *mitzvah* of *tashbitu*, and eating *chametz* during that period would be a corollary violation of that *mitzvah* — but it is not a formal *lav* in its own right, and there is certainly no *issur hana'ah*. What we take for granted — *issur hana'ah* from midday of *Erev Pesach* — is the position of Rabbi Yehuda, not a universal assumption.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili's position is the most radical. *Temah alecha* — you should be astounded at yourself — *he chametz assur behana'ah kol shivah?* Who told you that *chametz* is even *assur behana'ah* during the seven days of Pesach? He disputes what we take as the most basic premise of *hilchot chametz*. It is a striking *Gemara*.

### Shimon Rebuttal

What does Rabbi Shimon do with the *pesuk* Rabbi Yehuda cites — *lo tochal alav chametz* — which Rabbi Yehuda reads as establishing a prohibition from the time of the *korban Pesach*, i.e., from midday of *Erev Pesach*? Rabbi Shimon responds: *ve-chi efshar lomar kein?* Can you really read the *pasuk* that way? The very same *pasuk* continues: *shivat yamim tochal alav matzot* — seven days you shall eat *matzot*. If *alav* refers to the *korban Pesach* brought from midday, then the parallel clause about eating *matzah* for seven days makes no sense as a temporal reference. The end of the *pasuk* clearly anchors the prohibition of *chametz* to the seven days of the holiday.

Therefore Rabbi Shimon inverts the *derashah*: *be-sha'ah she-yeshno be-kum echol matzah, yeshno be-bal tochal chametz* — the moment when there is an obligation to eat *matzah*, there is a prohibition of *chametz*; *u-ve-sha'ah she-eino be-kum echol matzah, eino be-bal tochal chametz* — when there is no obligation to eat *matzah*, there is no prohibition of *chametz*. The *chametz* prohibition begins when the *matzah* obligation begins: on Pesach night, not on *Erev Pesach*. And correspondingly, when Pesach ends, both end together.

### Three Pesukim Derivation

What is the source for Rabbi Yehuda's position? *Tilsa kra'ei ksivi* — three *pesukim* are written: *lo ye'achel chametz*, *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu*, and *lo tochal alav chametz*. Rabbi Yehuda reads these three formulations as addressing three distinct periods: one establishes the prohibition before Pesach (*lifnei zemano*), one during (*toch zemano*), and one after (*le-achar zemano*). *Tosafot* further notes that since one of the three uses the formulation *lo ye'achel* — even according to the position of *Chizkiya* that *lo ye'achel* establishes the *issur hana'ah* for similar prohibitions — all three carry the implication of *issur hana'ah*.

What does Rabbi Shimon do with these three *pesukim*, since for him only the during-Pesach prohibition exists? He must redirect each of the other two. One he uses to derive that the prohibition applies throughout all seven days. A second he redirects to the derivation discussed below.

### Kol Machmetzes

Rabbi Shimon uses *kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* not to establish a temporal prohibition but to teach a substantive *halacha*: *ein li ela shenischametz me-elav* — I might have thought only *chametz* that fermented on its own is prohibited. *Machmat davar acher* — if it fermented due to an external fermenting agent — *minayin?* — how do I know that is included? *Talmud lomar kol machmetzes lo tocheilu* — the word *machmetzes* (in the *hif'il* causative form) teaches that even something that was *caused* to become *chametz* by an external agent is included.

*Tosafot* clarifies that the case is not bread leavened with sourdough, which is the paradigmatic *chametz* and could never have been thought to be excluded. Rather, the question arises when the fermenting agent is something other than sourdough — a different type of fermenting substance, such as the kind produced from grape peels with their natural sugars. *Tosafot* describes how such fermenting agents were produced in the context of bread-making. In modern terms: baking powder or packets of non-sourdough yeast would fall into this category. The yeast or baking powder itself is not *chametz*. But bread that fermented because of such an agent — even if it fermented in less than eighteen minutes — is *chametz* by virtue of this *derashah* from *kol machmetzes*.

There is an implicit principle at work here worth noting: when a *derashah* rests on redundancy — the fact that a *pasuk* is "extra" and must therefore be teaching something new — once that redundancy is expended to teach one thing, it cannot simultaneously be expended to teach another. This is why, for Rabbi Shimon, the *pasuk* of *kol machmetzes* teaches the law of externally-fermented *chametz* rather than a separate temporal period of prohibition.

For Rabbi Yehuda, who uses *kol machmetzes* to derive the prohibition *le-achar zemano*, the teaching about external fermentation is derived differently: from the combination of the extra *pasuk* and the distinctive use of the word *machmetzet*, which allows him to learn two separate things from one *pasuk* — the temporal prohibition from the redundancy of the *pasuk* itself, and the law of external fermentation from the unusual choice of language.

### Pesach Mitzrayim

Rabbi Shimon's third *pasuk*, *lo ye'achel chametz*, is used to derive an entirely different category of *halacha*: the law of *Pesach Mitzrayim*. Rabbi Yossi HaGlili asks: *minayin le-Pesach Mitzrayim she-ein chimutzo noheig ela yom echad?* — how do we know that at the original Pesach in Egypt, when the holiday of *Chag HaMatzot* had not yet been established, the prohibition of *chametz* applied for only one day? The answer comes from *lo ye'achel chametz*, which appears in the context of the first *korban Pesach* and is followed by *hayom atem yotzim* — today you are going out from Egypt. The rabbinic reading is: do not eat *chametz* today, implying a one-day prohibition only.

This is a purely historical *halacha* with no practical application to subsequent generations. It is striking that a *pasuk* would be "used up" to establish a fact about a one-time event in *Pesach Mitzrayim* rather than to derive an ongoing *halachic* rule. But that is precisely what Rabbi Yossi HaGlili does with this *pasuk*, and Rabbi Yehuda — who does not employ *smuchin* (the principle of derivation from juxtaposed *pesukim*) in the same way — disputes this reading.

## Matzah Obligation

### Rabbi Yehuda on Matzah

Having established his reading of Rabbi Shimon's *derashah*, the *Gemara* now examines how Rabbi Yehuda uses the *pasuk* that Rabbi Shimon employed. Rabbi Shimon read *lo tochal alav chametz shivat yamim tochal alav matzot* as teaching that *chametz* is prohibited only when *matzah* is obligatory — i.e., during Pesach itself, not before or after. Rabbi Yehuda, who has three other *pesukim* for before, during, and after, cannot accept that reading. He therefore reads the linkage in the opposite direction.

*Hahu le-kavoa chova afilu bi-zman ha-zeh hu de-asa* — that *pasuk* comes to establish that there is a *mitzvah* of *matzah* even nowadays, even after the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*. The logic: as long as there is a prohibition of *chametz*, there is an obligation of *matzah*, and the prohibition of *chametz* applies in every generation. Therefore the obligation of *matzah* also applies in every generation.

This derivation addresses a real and substantial question. The *peshat* of most *pesukim* concerning *matzah* speaks either of seven days of *matzah* as a dietary alternative to *chametz*, or of eating *matzah* together with the *korban Pesach*. In neither formulation is there an obvious source for a specific *mitzvah* to eat *matzah* on *leil ha-seder* in the absence of a *korban*. If the seven-day *matzah* obligation is an alternative to *chametz* and not an independent *mitzvah* on each night, and if the *matzah* accompanies only the *korban Pesach*, then in a post-*Mikdash* world without a *korban* and without a daily eating-obligation, why should there be a *mitzvah* to eat *matzah* on the first night at all? Rabbi Yehuda answers: the linkage between *chametz* and *matzah* in this *pasuk* establishes that wherever there is a prohibition of *chametz*, the *mitzvah* of *matzah* follows. Since *chametz* applies in every generation, *matzah* applies in every generation, at minimum on the first night.

### Rabbi Shimon on Matzah

If Rabbi Yehuda uses the linking *pasuk* to derive *matzah*'s applicability post-*Mikdash*, and Rabbi Shimon uses it to limit *chametz* to Pesach itself, then the question is: where does Rabbi Shimon derive the obligation of *matzah* nowadays? The *Gemara* answers: *nafka lei mi-ba'erev tochelu matzos* — he learns it from the *pasuk*, *ba-rishon be-arba'ah asar la-chodesh ba-erev tochelu matzos* — on the first day, on the fourteenth of the month, in the evening you shall eat *matzot*.

This might seem obvious — does the *pasuk* not say plainly "in the evening eat *matzot*"? The difficulty is that the *pasuk* continues: *ad yom ha-echad ve-esrim la-chodesh ba-erev* — until the night of the twenty-first. Reading the *pasuk* in full, it appears to establish a seven-day obligation of eating *matzah*, from the evening of the fourteenth to the evening of the twenty-first. If one takes the *pshat* that this is an independent seven-day *mitzvah* to eat *matzah*, then all seven nights would require eating *matzah*. Since we do not hold that, we must be reading the *pasuk* differently — and Rabbi Shimon focuses on the first clause alone, *ba-erev tochelu matzos*, as establishing the first-night obligation.

### Arel and Ben Nochri

Rabbi Yehuda, for his part, also has a use for *ba-erev tochelu matzos*. He employs it not to establish the first-night *matzah* obligation in general (which he derives from the linkage *pasuk* as explained above), but to teach that the *matzah* obligation applies even to those who are not eating the *korban Pesach* — specifically the *tamei* and those who were *be-derech rechokah* (in a distant place) and were therefore exempt from bringing the *korban*. Without this *pasuk*, one might have thought: *ho'il u-ve-Pesach lo yochal matzah u-maror nami lo neichol* — since such a person does not eat the *korban Pesach*, perhaps the associated *matzah* and *maror* also do not apply. *Ka mashma lan* — this *pasuk* teaches that they do. This establishes the first stage of *matzah*'s independence from the *korban* — it applies even in the presence of a *Mikdash* to those not eating the *korban*. But Rabbi Yehuda maintains that a second *pasuk* is needed to extend this to the post-*Mikdash* world entirely.

Rabbi Shimon, however, does not need the *tamei* and *be-derech rechokah* case as a separate derivation. *Lo gara me-arel u-ven neichar* — they are no different from an uncircumcised person or a *ben neichar* (here meaning one who is a *mumar* in certain respects), whose exclusion from the *korban Pesach* is stated explicitly: *ve-chol arel lo yochal bo, ve-chol ben neichar lo yochal bo* — they shall not eat *in it*, meaning the *korban Pesach*, but they still eat *matzah u-maror*. From this *pasuk*, Rabbi Shimon already knows that *matzah* applies to those not eating the *korban*. Therefore *ba-erev tochelu matzos* is free for him to use toward the larger derivation — establishing the obligation of *matzah* in the post-*Mikdash* era entirely.

The upshot: Rabbi Yehuda requires two *pesukim* to reach the conclusion that *matzah* applies post-*Mikdash* — one for the *tamei* and *be-derech rechokah* in the presence of the *Mikdash*, and a second to extend this to our own times. Rabbi Shimon needs only one, having derived the prior step from an independent source. What is clear from both positions, and what the *Gemara* leaves us with, is that the obligation to eat *matzah* on *leil ha-seder* is not at all straightforward from the *pesukim* on their own — it requires careful and deliberate derivation, and the independence of *matzah* from *korban Pesach* is a conclusion that had to be established, not assumed.
