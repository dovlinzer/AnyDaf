# Nazir 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Six Vows Re: Reuven

### Mishna Text & Translation

The *mishna* at the bottom of 32b opens with a vivid scene: six people are traveling together when a stranger approaches from the opposite direction. One of the six declares, *hareini nazir she-zeh Ploni* — "I will be a *nazir* if that is Reuven." A second, disagreeing, says *hareini nazir she-ein zeh Ploni* — "I will be a *nazir* if that man is not Reuven." So far these two are clearly in direct opposition to one another.

### Each Condition Analyzed

Person three says *hareini nazir she-echad mikem nazir* — "I will be a *nazir* if one of you is a *nazir*." This condition seems logically guaranteed: either the stranger is or is not Reuven, so one of the first two must have their condition met. Person four says *hareini nazir she-ein echad mikem nazir* — "I will be a *nazir* if one of you is not a *nazir*." The *gemara* understands this not as "none of you is a *nazir*" but rather as asserting that one of them is not, which mirrors what person three said in reverse. Person five says *hareini nazir she-shneichem nezirim* — "I will be a *nazir* if both of you are *nezirim*," understood as referring to the first two. Person six says *hareini nazir she-kulchem nezirim* — "I will be a *nazir* if all of you are *nezirim*," which seems plainly impossible, since at most only some of the conditions can be met. Prima facie, only a subset of the group should become *nezirim*.

### Beit Shammai: All Nezirim

*Beit Shammai* says *kulam nezirim* — they are all *nezirim*. This is a striking ruling, and the principle behind it is that *hekdesh b'ta'ut* — a dedication made in error — is nonetheless valid *hekdesh*, and similarly a *nazir b'ta'ut* is nonetheless a *nazir*. The parallel is to the case discussed earlier of one who says "the first black cow that comes out of my house," where the underlying intent is simply to consecrate the first cow. The stipulation reflects an assumption about reality, not a genuine open condition. So too here: each person is not merely rolling the dice. Each is assuming they know the truth and expressing that assumption. Since they fundamentally want to be a *nazir* and are simply mistaken about the facts, *Beit Shammai* holds that the *nezirut* takes effect regardless.

### Hekdesh B'Taout Principle

One might ask why the concept of *hekdesh b'ta'ut* applies to *nezirut* at all, given that it does not apply to *nedarim*. Two considerations emerge. First, *nezirut* involves *korbanot*, so there is a dimension of *hekdesh* inherent in it, and the principle of *hekdesh b'ta'ut* operates within that framework. Second, and independently, the *nazir* is described as *kadosh yihiyeh gadel perah sear rosho* — there is a status of sanctity attached to the *nazir* himself that *nedarim* lack. This holiness-status is precisely what distinguishes *nezirut* from *nedarim* in *Beit Shammai*'s analysis.

There is also the question of whether *Beit Shammai*'s logic would extend even to a genuine *tnai* about the future — where you truly do not know what will happen — as opposed to a *ta'ut*, where you mistakenly assumed you knew the present reality. This may depend on how one reads the earlier *mishna* about the black and white cow.

### Beit Hillel: Conditional…

*Beit Hillel* rules *eino nazir ela mi she-lo nitkaymu devarav* — the only one who is a *nazir* is the one whose condition was not met. The *gemara* will address the obvious anomaly in this formulation; the intended meaning is that only those whose conditions were met become *nezirim*. In short, *Beit Hillel* applies the ordinary logic of conditional vows: you become a *nazir* when and only when your stated condition is fulfilled.

### Rabbi Tarfon: No Nezirim

*Rabbi Tarfon* takes the opposite extreme from *Beit Shammai*: *ein echad mehem nazir* — none of them is a *nazir*.

### Lo Nitna Nezirut Princip…

The *gemara* explains that *Rabbi Tarfon* holds the principle of *lo nitna nezirut ela l'hafla'ah*, derived from the verse *ish o isha ki yafli lindor neder nazir* — *nezirut* must be enunciated in a clear, unambiguous, black-and-white manner. Any element of stipulation or doubt at the moment of declaration undermines the foundational requirement. This would seem to imply that even a *tnai* about the future would be insufficient for *nezirut* according to *Rabbi Tarfon*, since the declaration must be fully resolved at the time it is made.

It is worth noting that the language of *hafla'ah* appears in the verse about *nezirut* but not in the parallel verse about *nedarim*, where the formulation is simply *ish ki yidor neder* without *hafla'ah*. This linguistic distinction is what grounds the *lo nitna nezirut ela l'hafla'ah* principle specifically in *nezirut* rather than in *nedarim* generally.

One can understand *Rabbi Tarfon*'s position as reflecting the gravity of *nezirut*: because it is such a weighty commitment — and because unlike a physical act, it exists entirely as a speech act and therefore must be fully real on its own terms — it cannot be contingent or stipulated. *Nezirut* demands absolute clarity, a quality that a conditional vow by definition lacks.

## Stranger Turns Back

### Stranger's Departure

The *mishna* then introduces a complication: *hirtiah leachorav* — the stranger turned back and went away before anyone could determine his identity.

### Beit Hillel: Condition U…

For *Beit Shammai* and *Rabbi Tarfon*, this development changes nothing — their positions are not contingent on whether the condition was ultimately verifiable. The discussion that follows concerns *Beit Hillel*'s position. According to *Beit Hillel*, since there is no way to determine whether the condition was met, the *tanna kamma* holds that no one becomes a *nazir*.

### Safek Nazir Debate

This conclusion might seem surprising: surely each person should be a *safek nazir*, a doubtful *nazir*. Indeed, that is precisely what *Rabbi Shimon* says.

### Rabbi Shimon's Dual Acce…

*Rabbi Shimon* rules that everyone becomes a *safek nazir*, and he prescribes a specific formulation to resolve the legal difficulty. Each person must declare: *im haya kidvarai harei ni nazir chova* — if my condition was indeed met, then I am obligated in *nezirut* by virtue of my original declaration; *veim lav, harei ni nazir nedava* — and if my condition was not met and I am therefore not a *nazir* by that vow, I now accept *nezirut* upon myself as a voluntary gift. This two-pronged acceptance ensures that one way or another, the person is a genuine *nazir* and is not trapped in the impossible status of *safek nazir*, which would require observing all the stringencies of *nezirut* with no mechanism to conclude it through a *korban*.

## Gemara on Conditions Met

### Beit Hillel's Anomaly

The *gemara* homes in on *Beit Hillel*'s formulation: *eino nazir ela mi she-lo nitkaymu devarav* — the one whose condition was *not* met is the *nazir*? Why would that person be a *nazir*?

### Rav Yehuda's Textual Fix

*Rav Yehuda* proposes the simplest solution: simply remove the word *lo* from the *mishna*. The correct reading should be *mi she-nitkaymu devarav* — the one whose condition *was* met is the *nazir*. This is what *Beit Hillel* intends, and the word *lo* is a scribal or transmission error.

### Abaye: Self-Reversal

*Abaye*, at the top of 33b, offers an alternative reading that preserves the *mishna*'s text. The case, he says, concerns a person who reversed himself *toch kedei dibbur* — within the unit of speech. For example, a person said "I will be a *nazir* if this man is Reuven — actually, if he is *not* Reuven." He rescinded and reversed his initial condition within the same utterance.

### Earlier vs. Later Statem…

On this reading, *lo nitkaymu devarav* — "his conditions were not fulfilled" — refers specifically to his *earlier* statement, *devarav harishonim*, even though his *later* statement, *devarav ha'acharonim*, was fulfilled. The *mishna* is saying: the person whose first condition was not met but whose second condition was, that person is a *nazir*. This is admittedly a strange way to transmit the ruling — requiring the reader to understand that *lo nitkaymu devarav* means only the first part of a self-contradicting utterance — but it is what the *mishna* requires on this interpretation.

### Identifying the Tanna

Having established that the final section of the *mishna* (where the stranger turns back) must reflect *Beit Hillel*'s framework — since for *Beit Shammai* and *Rabbi Tarfon* the stranger's departure is irrelevant — the *gemara* asks whose position within the *Beit Hillel* camp is being represented. Specifically, the *mishna* indicates that *if* the condition could have been verified as met, the person would be a *nazir*, but if it cannot be verified, he is not even a *safek nazir* (according to the *tanna kamma*). Whose opinion is that?

### Rabbi Tarfon Excluded

*Rabbi Tarfon* can be ruled out immediately. If it were *Rabbi Tarfon*, then even when the condition is verifiably met, the person would not be a *nazir*, since at the moment of declaration he did not know whether the condition was met — *keivan d'bishata d'kanazar lo yada i Ploni hu v'ilo* — and so the *nezirut* never took effect. But the *mishna* clearly implies that the status of the condition matters, meaning that if verified, the person would indeed be a *nazir*. This is incompatible with *Rabbi Tarfon*'s position. Through this analysis of the end of the *mishna*, the *gemara* is simultaneously clarifying the logic that underlies *Rabbi Tarfon*'s ruling.

## Braita: Rabbi Yehuda

### Grain Pile Case

Having ruled out *Rabbi Tarfon*, the *gemara* identifies the relevant authority through a *braita*: *hareini nazir al menas she-yehei b'kri zeh meah kor* — "I will be a *nazir* on condition that this grain pile contains one hundred *kor*." The person then goes to measure the pile and finds it has been stolen or lost, making it impossible to verify whether the condition was met.

### Rabbi Shimon: Safek Nazir

*Rabbi Shimon* says this person is *oser* — he is in a state of prohibition, meaning he is a *safek nazir*. This is the same *Rabbi Shimon* who appears in our *mishna*, and the parallel is precise.

### Rabbi Yehuda: No Nezirut

*Rabbi Yehuda* says *matir* — the person is entirely permitted, meaning he is not a *nazir* at all. When the condition cannot be verified, there is no *nezirut*. The *gemara* identifies this as the opinion matching the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna*: if the stranger turns back without being identified, no one becomes a *nazir*. Since the *mishna* does hold that the person would be a *nazir* if the condition were verified as met, this is not *Rabbi Tarfon*'s position but rather *Rabbi Yehuda*'s.

### Rabbi Shimon's Logic

*Rabbi Shimon*'s reasoning: *keivan d'ilu lo nignav dilma hava bei meah kor v'hava nazir* — since had it not been stolen, the pile might well have contained a hundred *kor*, and the person would have been a *nazir*, therefore even now, in doubt, he is a *nazir* — *misafek*. The same logic applies to the case of the stranger: since had he come before us and been identified as Reuven, the person who vowed on that condition would have been a *nazir*, therefore even when identity is uncertain, he is a *safek nazir*.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Logic

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning is articulated by *Tosafot* on the phrase *ela Rabbi Yehuda dikree hu*: *d'lo machis inish nafshei l'safeika* — a person does not intend to include cases of doubt within his condition. When one says "if this pile contains a hundred *kor*," the implicit meaning is: if it is definitively proven that the condition is met. A case of doubt is not something the person consciously included in his vow. Therefore, *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the *tnai* applies only when its fulfillment is unambiguous and proven; in cases of doubt, the person has not activated his vow and is not a *nazir*. This concludes the *gemara*'s analysis of the first *mishna*, having clarified along the way both *Rabbi Tarfon*'s principle of *lo nitna nezirut ela l'hafla'ah* and *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle that a *tnai* does not extend to *safek* cases.

## Seven Vows Re: Koy

### What Is Koy?

The next *mishna* introduces a new case, structured similarly to the first but involving a very different subject: the *koy*. The *koy* is an animal of uncertain zoological classification — some type of hybrid, perhaps between a deer and another species — whose halachic status the rabbis were unable to determine. The essential question is whether the *koy* is a *chayah* (a wild animal) or a *behemah* (a domesticated animal), or perhaps belongs to some third category altogether.

### Koy's Halachic Status

The distinction between *chayah* and *behemah* carries significant halachic consequences in two directions. First, regarding blood covering: the verse *ish ki yatzud tzeid chayah o of asher yeiachel veshafachta et damo vechiso be'afar* — one who slaughters a *chayah* or a bird must cover the blood. A *behemah* is not subject to this requirement. Second, regarding *chelev* (forbidden fat): the *chelev* of a *behemah* is forbidden because the *behemah* is a species brought on the *mizbeiach*, whereas the *chelev* of a *chayah* is permitted since the *chayah* is not a sacrificial species. Because the *koy*'s category is unknown, the rabbis imposed both stringencies: its blood must be covered and its *chelev* may not be eaten.

The *koy* also invites a broader reflection. The question of whether reality must be binary — whether every animal must be either *chayah* or *behemah* — parallels discussions about the *androgynous*, who has both male and female features. Did the rabbis consider the possibility that such an individual constitutes a third category rather than being forced into either existing one? These questions carry obvious resonance with contemporary discussions, but they arise organically from the rabbinic texts themselves.

### Mishna's Seven Vows

The *mishna* then describes a group that sees a *koy* and makes conditional *nezirut* vows. Person one says *hareini nazir she-zeh chayah* — "I will be a *nazir* if this is a *chayah*." Person two says *hareini nazir she-zeh eino chayah*. Person three says *hareini nazir she-zeh behemah*. Person four says *hareini nazir she-ein zeh behemah*.

### Both/Neither Conditions

Person five says *hareini nazir she-zeh chayah uvehemah* — "I will be a *nazir* only if this animal falls into both categories simultaneously." Person six says *hareini nazir she-ein zeh lo chayah velo behemah* — "I will be a *nazir* only if it falls into neither category."

### Collective Vows

Person seven says *hareini nazir she-echad mikem nazir* — "if one of you is a *nazir*." Person eight says *hareini nazir she-ein echad mikem nazir* — "if one of you is not a *nazir*." Person nine says *hareini nazir she-kulchem nezirim* — "if all of you are *nezirim*."

### All Nine: Nezirim

The *mishna* rules: *harei kulam nezirim* — they are all *nezirim*. This is striking. If one takes the *koy* as either a *chayah* or a *behemah*, then only some of the conditions could be met and some could not. How can all nine become *nezirim*?

*Tosafot* explains that this need not be *Beit Shammai*'s position. It can be read according to *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds that in cases of doubt they are all *safek nezirim*. What *harei kulam nezirim* means, on this reading, is that they are all in the status of *safek nazir*. There remains some ambiguity about whether persons seven through nine might be *vadai nezirim* rather than *safek nezirim* — for example, if *she-ein echad mikem nazir* means "one of you is definitely not a *nazir* (and I will be one if that's true)," then that condition may be definitively met, making person eight a *vadai nazir*. Alternatively, *she-kulchem nezirim* might mean "all of you are *safek nezirim*," in which case the condition is met and person nine is a *vadai nazir*. *Tosafot* leaves these questions open but finds it possible to read the entire *mishna* as consistent with *Rabbi Shimon* and *Beit Hillel*, with all nine being *safek nezirim*.

## Nine Nezirim or Nezirut?

### Braita: Nine People

The *gemara* notes a discrepancy between two *braitot*. The first *braita* describes the *mishna*'s case as involving *tisha nezirim* — nine *nezirim*, meaning nine separate individuals who each made one conditional vow.

### Braita: One Person

The second *braita* describes it as *tisha nezirot* — nine *nezirut* statuses, implying that one person made all nine conditional declarations and consequently bears nine distinct obligations of *nezirut*.

### Cancellation Problem

The second reading raises an immediate difficulty. If one person said both "I will be a *nazir* if this is a *chayah*" and "I will be a *nazir* if this is not a *chayah*," only one of those two conditions can logically be met. One might think the two should cancel each other out or that together they produce at most one *nezirut*, not two. Similarly with all the paired conditions. So how does one person accumulate nine separate *nezirut* statuses?

### Rav Sheshet's Answer

The *gemara* also presses on the final three conditions, which reference "you" — the other people — and cannot easily be said by a single individual about himself. *Rav Sheshet* resolves this by positing a two-stage process: nine people each made the declarations corresponding to the first portion of the *mishna*, giving rise to nine *nezirim*. Then one of them additionally declared *hareini nazir unezirot hakol alai* — "I accept upon myself all the *nezirut* statuses that you have all undertaken." This one person thereby accumulates all nine *nezirut* obligations.

### No Cancellation Principle

The deeper *chidush* of the *tisha nezirot* reading — whichever mechanism produces it — is that contradictory conditions do not cancel each other out. When a person declares *nezirut* conditionally on the *koy* being a *chayah*, that *nezirut* takes effect *misafek*. When the same person declares *nezirut* conditionally on the *koy* not being a *chayah*, that *nezirut* also takes effect *misafek*. We do not step back and say: since logically only one can be true, only one counts. Each declaration stands independently.

### Legal Indeterminacy

This conclusion connects to a deeper point. The reason the contradictory conditions do not cancel is not simply a procedural rule about how vows are processed. It reflects the underlying nature of the *koy*'s status. Whether an animal is a *chayah* or a *behemah* is not a brute empirical fact that we happen not to know; it is a *legal* determination, and in the *koy*'s case, that legal determination is genuinely indeterminate. There is no objective fact of the matter that resolves it. This is analogous to what *Rav Soloveitchik* said about *bein hashmashot*: one view holds that *bein hashmashot* is simply a period within which we cannot identify the precise moment night begins; but the alternative view holds that *bein hashmashot* is by definition a liminal period, neither day nor night, not merely an unknown but an ontological gray zone. On the second view, it makes no sense to say *mi-manafshach* (one way or the other). The period simply is what it is. So too with the *koy*: since its classification is legally indeterminate — it may be both, neither, or a third category — all the conditional possibilities remain perpetually open, none cancels the others, and all the *nezirut* statuses coexist.

## Three Prohibitions

### Three Prohibitions Listed

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna* that opens the following chapter. *Shloshah minim asurim b'nazir* — there are three categories of prohibition for a *nazir*: *hatumah*, contracting ritual impurity through contact with the dead; *hadiglachas*, shaving or cutting the hair; and *hayotze min hagefen*, consuming products of the vine. The subsequent discussion focuses primarily on the third category.

### Grape Products Combine

The *mishna* states that *hayotze min hagefen mitztarefin zeh im zeh* — all products of the vine combine with one another toward the minimum required amount. So if a *nazir* consumes a small quantity of wine, a small quantity of grapes, and a small quantity of grape skins, these amounts aggregate.

### Kezayit Standard

The minimum measure for violation is a *kezayit* — the volume of an olive — of grape matter.

### Mishna Rishona: Revi'it

The *mishna* notes explicitly that there was an earlier *mishna* — a *mishna rishona* — which stated the measure as a *revi'it* of wine (*ad she-yishte revi'it yayin*), roughly three to three-and-a-quarter ounces. The relationship between these two measures requires clarification: the *mishna rishona* may have applied specifically to wine as a liquid, measured in the standard liquid unit of a *revi'it*, while the current ruling of *kezayit* applies to solid grape matter. Or it may be that originally even solid matter was conceptually measured against the liquid standard of wine — since the prohibition of grape products is conceptually derivative of the prohibition of wine — and the current position departed from that approach. The *gemara* will elaborate on this distinction.

## Individual Grape Parts

### Rabbi Akiva: Soaked Bread

*Rabbi Akiva* extends the prohibition to *mishrat anavim*: even if a person did not consume a *kezayit* of actual grape matter, if he ate a piece of bread that was soaked in wine, and the total volume of that bread is a *kezayit*, he is liable. The wine need not constitute a *kezayit* itself; it is the soaked bread that reaches the measure. *Rabbi Akiva* derives this from the verse *v'chol mishrat anavim lo yochal* — anything soaked in wine shall not be eaten. This verse creates an independent prohibition based on the totality of the soaked item, not solely on the quantity of wine absorbed.

This principle will lead into a broader discussion applicable to *kashrus* generally — specifically the question of how forbidden and permitted substances combine when mixed, whether liability is measured by the *issur* alone or by the composite. This *sugya* is studied in the context of *Yoreh De'ah* and the laws of *kashrus*.

### Each Part Separately

The *tanna kamma* holds that each of the four vine-derived categories — wine, grapes, *chartzanim* (which, as we will see, are the seeds), and *zagim* (the outer skin) — individually triggers liability if consumed in a *kezayit* quantity. The basis is the verse *meichartzanim v'ad zag lo yochal* — "from the seeds to the skin he shall not eat" — which enumerates each component separately.

### Rabbi Eleazar: Two & One

*Rabbi Eleazar ben Azariah* introduces a stringency specific to the *chartzanim* and *zag*. He derives from the verse's plural and singular forms that liability for these components requires consuming two *chartzanim* and one *zag*. It is not enough to have a *kezayit* of seeds alone; one needs at least two seeds and one skin (or the reverse, depending on which term refers to which). Presumably the *kezayit* requirement remains in force alongside this, raising the question of how these two criteria interact when the quantities partially overlap.

### Chartzanim & Zag

Which term refers to the seeds and which to the skin? The *gemara* quotes the *tanna kamma*'s identification: *hachartzanim eilu hachitzonim* — the *chartzanim* are the outer part, meaning the skin; *hazagim eilu hapnimiyim* — the *zagim* are the inner part, meaning the seeds. That is the view of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Rabbi Yosi Corrects

*Rabbi Yosi* disagrees and offers a mnemonic to prevent confusion: *kezug she-beveheimah* — like the bell worn by an animal. A bell (*zug*) is the outer casing, while the internal clapper that strikes it is called the *inval*. Therefore, *zag* — related to *zug* — denotes the outer skin, and *chartzanim* — being plural — denotes the seeds, since each grape contains multiple seeds but only one skin. This makes intuitive sense. *Rabbi Yosi*'s identification is the accepted ruling: *chartzanim* are the seeds (as confirmed by modern Hebrew usage, where *beli chartzanim* means "seedless") and *zag* is the outer skin.

## Yotzei Min HaGefen Scope

### Gefen Itself Excluded

The *gemara* now interrogates the scope of *hayotze min hagefen*. The phrase *hayotze min hagefen* — that which issues from the vine — but not the *gefen* itself. The actual wood of the vine is excluded.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Leaves

*Rabbi Eliezer* holds a much broader position: *afilu alim velulavim bemashma* — even the leaves and the vine tendrils are included in the prohibition. The *mishna*, which does not enumerate these, is therefore inconsistent with *Rabbi Eliezer*'s view. The *lulavim* here are not the palm branches of *Sukkot* but rather the vine tendrils, the climbing appendages of the vine that wrap around supports.

### Hermeneutic Methods

The dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan* flows from a fundamental difference in hermeneutic methodology. *Rabbi Eliezer* employs the method of *ribui u'miyut*, associated with the school of *Rabbi Akiva*, while the *rabbanan* employ *klal u'prat*, associated with the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*. This is the same distinction articulated in the thirteen hermeneutic principles recited at the conclusion of the morning blessings.

### Ribui U'Miyut Explained

The *ribui u'miyut* method operates as follows. If the Torah states a broad inclusive expression and then narrows it with a specific limiting expression, the ריבוי *ribui* has already broadened the scope to everything, and the *miyut* then narrows it by excluding only the category most unlike the items specified. If there is a further *ribui*, we are already at the stage of maximum inclusion and only the most dissimilar item is excluded.

### Klal U'Prat Explained

The *klal u'prat* method works differently. A general statement (*klal*) followed by a specific statement (*prat*) limits the scope entirely to the items specified — *ein baklal ela mah shebaprat*. If there is a subsequent *klal*, giving a structure of *klal prat u'klal*, then the scope expands again to anything similar to the items in the *prat*, but only things similar to them.

The verse concerning the *nazir* reads: *miyayin v'shechar yazir* — a specification (wine and strong drink); *mikol asher ye'aseh mi-gefen hayayin* — a general inclusion (anything made from the vine); *meichartzanim v'ad zag lo yochal* — a return to specifics (from seeds to skin). This is the structure of *prat klal u'prat*, which is the reverse of the more common *klal prat u'klal*. The *gemara* assumes that the reverse structure yields the same type of conclusion as *klal prat u'klal*: anything similar to the *pratim*.

This seems counterintuitive — a structure where the specifics frame the general, double-narrowing it, might appear to be more restrictive than *klal prat u'klal*. Yet the *gemara* treats the mirror image as generating the same result. The final *prat* (*meichartzanim v'ad zag*) is understood as limiting what would otherwise be a completely open-ended inclusion, rather than doubly restricting it.

### Pri & Psolet Pri

For the *rabbanan* operating under *klal u'prat*, the scope of *hayotze min hagefen* is determined by what is similar to the *pratim* enumerated. The *gemara* identifies the criterion: *mah prat meforash pri u'psolet pri* — the *pratim* include examples of *pri* (fruit) and *psolet pri* (fruit remnant or inferior fruit). Anything that is a *pri* or a *psolet pri* from the vine is included.

### Unripe & Spoiled

*Pri* includes the grape itself. Does it require a fully ripened grape, *pri gamur*? The *gemara* rejects this, since it would include nothing not already specified in the verse. The extension of the *ribui* must include something additional, which is the *boser* — the unripe grape. Even unripe grapes are subsumed within the prohibition.

As for *psolet pri*, the *gemara* identifies this as *chometz* — vinegar, the product of spoiled wine. *Rav Kahana* adds that the extension on this side includes *invei dichrin* — blighted or otherwise spoiled grapes, perhaps wormy or otherwise damaged before harvest. So according to the *rabbanan*, the scope of *hayotze min hagefen* encompasses unripe grapes (*boser*) and various forms of spoiled or inferior grape matter, in addition to the items explicitly enumerated in the verse.

According to *Rabbi Eliezer*, by contrast, applying *ribui u'miyut*, the only exclusion is the actual wood of the vine itself (*shibshta*). Anything else that grows out of or issues from the vine — including leaves and tendrils — falls within the prohibition.
