# Yevamot 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Husband Returns Alive

### Setup: Single Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָה גֵּט מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא מֵרִאשׁוֹן תִּבְעֵי גֵּט, אֶלָּא מִשֵּׁנִי אַמַּאי? זְנוּת בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** She **requires a bill of divorce from this one and from that one.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, from the first** husband **she requires a bill of divorce,** as she is his actual wife. **But from the second, why** does she need a bill of divorce? Surely their relationship **is merely licentious.**

The sugya opens on 88b at the bottom, still discussing the case of a woman whose husband departed to a distant land, whereafter a single witness testified that he had died. Acting on this testimony — and with the permission of *beit din* — she remarried, only for her first husband to return alive. The ruling is that she must leave both husbands. The *gemara* now takes up the question of *get*: the *mishnah* states *tzericha get mizeh umizeh* — she requires a *get* from each husband.

### Rav Huna Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ גֵּירַשׁ זֶה וְנָשָׂא זֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ יוֹצְאָה בְּלָא גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** It is a rabbinic **decree lest** those who are unaware of the entire story **say** that **this** first husband **divorced** her **and** afterward **this** second man **married** her. **And** if she subsequently leaves him without a bill of divorce, they will claim that we **find a married woman leaving** her husband **without a bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* begins with a *bishlama*: it is straightforward that she needs a *get* from the first husband, since she was legally married to him and, if she ever wishes to remarry, she must be formally divorced. But why does she require a *get* from the second husband? Her union with him was *zenut b'alma* — mere adultery. She was still a married woman when she entered that relationship; no marriage was ever contracted. Why should a *get* be necessary?

Rav Huna answers: it is a *gezeira*. The Rabbis were concerned that observers would say: the first husband divorced her, she then married the second, and now she is leaving the second — all without a *get*. This perception would create the impression that a married woman can exit a marriage without a *get*, which is intolerable.

### Kiddushin Only Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: אָמְרוּ לָהּ "מֵת בַּעְלִךְ" וְנִתְקַדְּשָׁה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא בַּעְלָהּ — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ. הָתָם נָמֵי, נֵימָא: גֵּירַשׁ זֶה וְקִדֵּשׁ זֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ יוֹצְאָה שֶׁלֹּא בַּגֵּט!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** consider **the latter clause** of the mishna (92a), **that teaches** that if **they said to her: Your husband is dead, and she became betrothed** to another, **and afterward her husband came,** it is **permitted** for her **to return to** her first husband. **There too, let us say** that people might think that **this one divorced** her **and that one betrothed** her, **and** we **find a married woman leaving** her husband, i.e., her second husband, **without a bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from the continuation of the *mishnah*: if she was told her husband died and she accepted only *kiddushin* — betrothal — but did not proceed to *nisuin*, she is permitted to return to her first husband. The *gemara* asks: should we not also be concerned in that case that people will say the first husband divorced her, she became betrothed to a second man, and now she is leaving him without a *get*?

### Public Perception Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בָּעֲיָא גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Actually,** even if she was merely betrothed she **requires a bill of divorce** from the second man before she can return to her first husband.

If we apply the same logic, we would say that even in the *kiddushin*-only case she should need a *get* from the second man — *le'olam ba'ei get*, she always requires a *get*. But this leads to a further difficulty.

### Mechzir Gerushato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נִמְצָא זֶה מַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ מִשֶּׁנִּתְאָרְסָה!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** people will say that we **find this one remarrying his divorcée after she was betrothed** to another.

If she must obtain a *get* from the second man (her *mekudeshet*, her betrothed), then her first husband — having "divorced" her in the eyes of observers who believe she received a *get* — would be re-taking a woman who had been "divorced" and then betrothed to another. This implicates the prohibition of *mechzir gerushato*, the prohibition against a man remarrying his divorced wife after she has been married to another.

### R. Yose ben Kipur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן כִּיפָּר, דְּאָמַר: מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין — אֲסוּרָה, מִן הָאֵירוּסִין — מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In this regard the *tanna* holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar, who stated** a principle with regard to a divorcée who formed a relationship with another man, that if she came **from marriage** it is **prohibited** for her to return to her first husband, but if she came **from betrothal** it is **permitted.**

One might argue this is only a problem according to Rabbi Yose ben Kipur, who holds that *mechzir gerushato* applies even from *eirusin* alone — *min hanisuin asurah, min ha'eirusin muteret*, that only after full *nisuin* is she forbidden to return, but from *eirusin* she may return. Since Rabbi Yose ben Kipur's view is a *da'at yachid* not accepted as *halacha*, one could argue that public confusion about his position is itself undesirable, which would make the entire scenario problematic.

### Get Doesn't Matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּתַן לָהּ אַחֲרוֹן גֵּט — לֹא פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה, מִכְּלָל דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא גֵּט. דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא גֵּט, אַמַּאי לֹא פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **From** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna (92a) **teaches: Even though the last one gave her a bill of divorce, he has not disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood,** as she is not considered a divorcée at all, this proves **by inference that she does not require a bill of divorce** from him. The reason is **that if she requires a bill of divorce,** even if it is only due to uncertainty, **why** has he **not disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood?**

The *gemara* now adduces evidence from the *mishnah* itself: *af al pi she-natan ha-acharon get, lo pasla min hakehunah* — even if the second husband gave her a *get*, she is not disqualified from *kehunah*. This tells us something important: a *get* from the second husband does not carry the halachic force of a real *get*. For if it were a genuine *get*, it would render her a *gerushah* and disqualify her from marrying a *kohen*. The fact that it does not disqualify her proves the *get* in this case is essentially meaningless — it is given only for the purpose of appearances, not as a real act of divorce.

### Kiddushei Ta'ut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סֵיפָא, אָמְרִי: קִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** a bill of divorce given by a man to a woman who is not his wife is clearly of no account, and the reason for the ruling in **the latter clause,** with regard to betrothal, is that people will **say** there was no need for a bill of divorce because **it was a mistaken betrothal.**

The resolution is *amrei kiddushei ta'ut havu*: in the *kiddushin*-only case, people will naturally understand that the betrothal was a mistake — she accepted *kiddushin* without knowing her first husband was still alive. Therefore, when she exits the situation without a *get*, no one concludes that a married woman can leave without a *get*; everyone understands it as a nullification of a betrothal entered under false pretenses. This is what people will think.

### Kensu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא נָמֵי, אָמְרִי: נִישּׂוּאֵי טָעוּת הֲווֹ? קַנְסוּהָ רַבָּנַן. סֵיפָא נָמֵי לִיקְנְסוּהָ! רֵישָׁא, דַּעֲבַדָא אִיסּוּרָא — קַנְסוּהָ. סֵיפָא, דְּלָא עֲבַדָא אִיסּוּרָא — לָא קַנְסוּהָ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The first clause** involves a situation **where she violated a prohibition** through her intercourse, and therefore the Sages **penalized her.** Conversely, in **the latter clause, when she did not violate a prohibition,** as she simply became betrothed, **the Sages did not penalize her.**

If that is so — that in the *kiddushin* case people will say *kiddushei ta'ut havu* — why don't we say the same in the first case, where she actually married and lived with the second man? There too, was it not a marriage entered in error?

The answer is *kensu rabanan* — the Rabbis penalized her. In the first case, an actual act of prohibition took place: she cohabited with a man while still legally married. The Rabbis therefore imposed a penalty. In the second case, *delo avda issura* — she did not actually commit any prohibited act; she merely accepted some tokens of betrothal without knowing the full picture. Where no genuine transgression occurred, *lo kensu rabanan* — the Rabbis did not impose the penalty.

## Woman's Consequences

### No Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא תַּקִּינוּ לַהּ רַבָּנַן כְּתוּבָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ — הָא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that this woman **does not have,** i.e., she is not entitled to, the payment of her **marriage contract.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that **the Sages instituted a marriage contract** in general, **for** an ordinary woman? **So that she will not be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her.** The necessity to find money for her marriage contract will prevent a hasty decision to divorce her. However, in the case of **this** woman, on the contrary, the Sages actually prefer that **she will be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her,** as the marriage was forbidden and she may not remain with him. Consequently, they eliminated her marriage contract to encourage him to divorce her.

The *mishnah* states *ein lah ketubah* — she receives no *ketubah* from either husband. The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama tiknu lah rabanan ketubah?* What was the entire purpose of the rabbinic enactment of the *ketubah*? *Kedei shelo tehei kalah be'einav lehotzi'ah* — so that divorcing her would not be a trivial matter in the husband's eyes; the financial burden of the *ketubah* payment was meant to deter hasty divorce. In the present case, however, we actively want the husband to divorce her. Creating any obstacle to that divorce by requiring a *ketubah* payment would be counterproductive. Therefore, the Rabbis do not require it.

### Ketubah Nature

A question arises here: is not at least some component of the *ketubah de'oraita*? The answer is that the *ketubah* — the obligation to pay a sum upon divorce — is entirely *takanat derabbanan*. What is *de'oraita* is the clause of *she'er, kesus, v'onah*: the husband's obligations to provide food, clothing, and conjugal rights. The monetary payment upon divorce is purely a rabbinic institution.

This raises a more refined question: if the *ketubah* is a contractual document that has already been entered into, does one not need to honor the terms of a contract? Two approaches suggest themselves. One could argue that the *ketubah* is not a fully voluntary contract — it is a mandated condition of marriage — and therefore the assumption underlying the contract is that the rabbinic mandate for it remains in force. Once the reason for the *ketubah* is void — as in this case, where divorce is required — the *ketubah* itself is also void. Alternatively, and relatedly, the principle of *hefker beit din hefker* — which the *gemara* will develop at length later — gives *beit din* the authority to dissolve obligations of this kind. The final clause of the *ketubah* document supports this reading: *v'kibel alav chasan den kichomer kol shtarei ketubot v'toseftos de-nahagin bivnot Yisrael hatzni'ot ketikkun chachameinu zichronam livrachah* — the groom accepts the terms "according to the enactment of our Sages of blessed memory," which means the contract operates within the rabbinic framework that established it, and that same framework can dissolve it.

### No Support Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לָהּ פֵּירוֹת וְלֹא מְזוֹנוֹת וְלֹא בְּלָאוֹת. תְּנַאי כְּתוּבָּה — כִּכְתוּבָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further states that **she does not have** claim to **profits, or sustenance, or worn clothes.** Why not? Because the **stipulations in the marriage contract,** i.e., all the rights of a wife stemming from the stipulations that are part of a marriage contract, **are considered like** the **marriage contract** itself. Since she has no marriage contract, she does not have the stipulations in a marriage contract either.

Beyond the *ketubah* itself, the *mishnah* states *ein lah peiros, v'lo mezonot, v'lo blaot* — she is entitled to neither *peiros* nor *mezonot* nor *blaot*. To explain briefly: *nichsei milog* are properties a wife brings into the marriage that she retakes upon divorce, while the husband enjoys the profits — the *peiros* — during the marriage. In cases where the marriage is found to have been invalid (such as an *almanah l'kohen gadol*), the husband must retroactively return those profits. In our case, she does not even receive that restitution. *Mezonot* — her food support — likewise does not apply, nor do the *blaot* — the worn garments that ordinarily belong to the wife.

The *gemara* explains: *tenai ketubah ke-ketubah dami* — the conditions and appurtenances of the *ketubah* share the same status as the *ketubah* itself. Since she forfeits the *ketubah*, she forfeits everything that comes along with it.

### Competing Values

This ruling raises a genuine moral tension. The Rabbis were lenient with this woman at the outset — they permitted her to remarry on the basis of a single witness — precisely because they did not want her to become an *agunah*. As the *gemara* stated elsewhere, *mishum agunah akeilu bah rabanan*: to prevent the tragedy of a chained woman, the Rabbis eased the standard of evidence. And yet, when things go wrong through no real fault of her own, the full weight of the consequences falls on her alone.

One might argue that at minimum she should receive her *ketubah*: it is not her fault this happened, and she now finds herself with nothing. The *gemara's* response to this, implicitly, is that the Rabbis view the *ketubah* instrumentally — as a mechanism for regulating the husband's motivation, not as a social welfare provision for the wife. Since the goal here is to ensure the husband divorces her promptly and without financial hesitation, the *ketubah* serves no purpose and is withheld. Compelling as this logic is from a purely formal standpoint, it leaves the woman in a precarious position that is difficult not to find troubling. The *gemara* itself, back on an earlier *daf* (around daf 8), acknowledged the tension: *shappir avdu Rabbanan de'avdu hachi* — the Rabbis structured it correctly — though it also acknowledged the ulterior motive of preventing *iggun*.

### Return Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטְלָה מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּתָפְשָׂה לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינַּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna also teaches that if **she took** any of the above **from this** man **or from that** one she must return anything she took. The Gemara comments: This **is obvious.** Since she is not entitled to these articles, of course she must give them back. The Gemara explains: It is necessary, **lest you say** that **since she has** already **taken hold** of them **we do not remove** them **from her** possession, as this is merely a penalty and she obtained nothing that did not legally belong to her. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that the court requires her to return even these items.

*Natlah mizeh umizeh* — if she actually took anything from either husband, she must return it. One might think this is *peshita* — obvious. The *gemara* clarifies: it is necessary to state this because one might have thought that *keivan detafsa, lo mafkinan minah* — since she has already taken possession, we do not wrest it from her. In many cases of disputed property, once someone has gotten hold of something, we do not remove it. This ruling teaches that in our case we do: she must return it. This makes the ruling even more stringent from the woman's perspective — not only does she receive nothing, but whatever she may have already received must be given back.

## Mamzerim Status

### Mamzerim Both Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. תְּנַן הָתָם: אֵין תּוֹרְמִין מִן הַטָּמֵא עַל הַטָּהוֹר, וְאִם תָּרַם, בְּשׁוֹגֵג — תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, בְּמֵזִיד — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **the child** of either of the men is **a *mamzer*.** To clarify this issue, the Gemara cites a different discussion. **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Terumot* 2:2): **One may not separate** *teruma* **from ritually impure** produce in order **to** exempt **ritually pure** food, **and if he separated** *teruma* from impure food **unwittingly, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*.** If he acted **intentionally, he has done nothing,** that is, his action is of no effect.

The *mishnah* further states: *v'havlad mamzer mizu umizu* — children she bears are *mamzerim* from either husband. The *gemara* will now examine exactly what the nature of this *mamzer* status is: when is it *de'oraita* and when *de'rabbanan*? Counterintuitively, as will become apparent, the one whose *mamzeirus* is *de'rabbanan* is actually in a worse position than the one whose status is *de'oraita*.

To address this, the *gemara* pivots entirely to *Seder Zeraim*, specifically *Masechet Terumot*, and stays there for a substantial discussion.

### Terumah Analogy

The *mishnah* discussion in *Terumot* states: *ein tormin min ha-tamei al ha-tahor* — one may not separate *terumah* from *tamei* produce on behalf of *tahor* produce. If one does so *b'shogeg* — accidentally — *terumato terumah*, it counts as *terumah*. If one does so *b'meizid* — intentionally — *lo asa v'lo klum*, one has accomplished nothing.

### Bad Terumah

Before analyzing this *mishnah*, the *gemara* introduces a parallel case from *Masechet Terumot*: if one sets aside a cucumber (*kishut*) as *terumah* and it turns out to be bitter, or a melon and it turns out to be rotten — the ruling is *terumah v'yachzor v'yitrom*: it counts as *terumah* (and must be treated accordingly) but one must go back and separate *terumah* a second time from valid produce.

### Chisda vs. Natan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם כׇּל עִיקָּר, דַּאֲפִילּוּ הָהֻיא גְּרִיוָא הָדַר לְטִיבְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages debated: **What** is the meaning of the expression: **He has done nothing? Rav Ḥisda said: He has done nothing at all,** meaning **that even that *griva*** of produce he set aside as *teruma* **returns to** its former **untithed** state, as his entire act is completely disregarded.

The *gemara* now presents a dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Natan bar Rav Yehoshua regarding the precise meaning of *lo asa v'lo klum* when *tamei terumah* is separated on *tahor* produce intentionally.

Rav Chisda interprets it maximally: *lo asa v'lo klum kol ikar* — he has done absolutely nothing whatsoever. Even the *se'ah* of produce that was physically separated as *terumah* from the *tamei* pile reverts to *tevel* status; it is as if no *terumah* was ever separated from either the *tamei* pile or the *tahor* pile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נָתָן בְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם — לְתַקֵּן אֶת הַשִּׁירַיִם, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה הָוֵי. רַב חִסְדָּא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב נָתָן בְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ הָוֵי תְּרוּמָה, זִימְנִין דְּפָשַׁע וְלָא מַפְרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Natan, son of Rabbi Oshaya, said: He has done nothing** with regard to **preparing the remaining** produce from which he separated *teruma*, **but** the fruit he separated **is** itself ***teruma*.** Although the portion he set aside is sanctified as *teruma*, this does not exempt him from separating more *teruma* from ritually pure produce. The Gemara clarifies the two opinions: **Rav Ḥisda did not say** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Natan, son of Rabbi Oshaya, as, if you say it is *teruma*, on occasion** he **will be negligent and not separate** anything more, assuming that if the portion he set aside has the status of *teruma* he must certainly have done everything required.

Rav Natan bar Rav Yehoshua takes a narrower view: *lo asa v'lo klum letaken et ha-shirayim* — he has accomplished nothing with respect to permitting the *tahor* remainder for consumption. The *tahor* produce still requires valid *terumah* to be separated from it. However, the produce that was physically taken as *terumah* from the *tamei* pile does have the status of *terumah* — it is *tamei terumah* that must be burned.

The reason Rav Chisda rejects Rav Natan's position is a practical concern: *gezeira dilma pasha v'lo mafrish* — if we tell the person that the *tamei* stuff he separated really is *terumah*, he may become negligent and fail to separate valid *terumah* a second time. He will reason: "I already separated *terumah* — the fact that it must be burned is irrelevant, I fulfilled my obligation." To prevent this negligence, Rav Chisda holds that nothing the person did has any *terumah* status whatsoever, and he must start from scratch.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הַתּוֹרֵם קִישּׁוּת וְנִמְצֵאת מָרָה, אֲבַטִּיחַ וְנִמְצֵאת סָרוּחַ — תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם! שׁוֹגֵג אַמֵּזִיד קָרָמֵית? שׁוֹגֵג — לָא עֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא, מֵזִיד — קָעָבֵד אִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 3:1): With regard to **one who separates** *teruma* from **a serpent melon [*kishut*] and it was discovered** to be **bitter,** or from **a watermelon and it was discovered** to be **spoiled,** it is ***teruma*, and** yet he must **go back and separate** *teruma* from another serpent melon or watermelon. No concern is expressed in this mishna that one might neglect to set aside *teruma* a second time. The Gemara answers: **Are you raising a contradiction** between the case of **an unwitting** sinner and that **of an intentional** sinner? There is a difference between them, as one who was **unwitting did not commit a transgression** and consequently does not deserve to be penalized, whereas one who was **an intentional** sinner **did commit a transgression.**

The *gemara* then challenges Rav Chisda from the *mishnah* about the bad cucumber: there, even though the *terumah* was invalid, we say *terumah v'yachzor v'yitrom* — it has *terumah* status and he goes back to separate more. Why does the same logic of negligence not apply there? The *gemara* answers: *shogeg ameizid karamis* — you are comparing an accidental case to an intentional one. In the *shogeg* case, the person is fundamentally trustworthy and genuinely attempting to fulfill the obligation; we can rely on him to separate *terumah* again when told to do so. The *meizid* case involves someone who is already acting improperly, and we cannot trust him to do anything correctly thereafter. Therefore the concern about negligence applies only to the *meizid* case, justifying Rav Chisda's ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי שׁוֹגֵג אַשּׁוֹגֵג — הָכָא קָתָנֵי: בְּשׁוֹגֵג תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, הָתָם קָתָנֵי: תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחְזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between this ruling involving **an unwitting** sinner and another *halakha* **of an unwitting** sinner: **Here, it is taught** that if the one who separated ritually impure produce instead of ritually pure produce was **unwitting, his *teruma* is *teruma*,** which indicates that he does not have to separate *teruma* again. However, **there,** with regard to rotten fruit, **it is taught** that it is ***teruma* and** yet he must **separate** *teruma* **again.**

A further question is raised: *v'rami shogeg ashogeg* — let us compare a *shogeg* case to another *shogeg* case. In our *mishnah*, *b'shogeg terumato terumah* — accidentally separating *tamei* on *tahor* results in *terumah* status without requiring a second separation. But in the case of the bad cucumber, it says *terumah v'yachzor v'yitrom* — it is *terumah* and one must separate again. Why the difference?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם — שׁוֹגֵג קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִיטְעֲמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **There,** his was **an unwitting** act that is **close to an intentional** one, **as he should have tasted it** first to ensure that he was separating quality fruit. His failure to do so renders him virtually a willful sinner, and therefore the Sages penalized him by obligating him to set aside *teruma* again. In the case of impure *teruma*, in contrast, he may not have been able to investigate the matter when he separated the portion.

The answer: *shogeg karov lemeizid* — the *shogeg* in the case of the bad cucumber is closer to *meizid*. *Ibai lei l'mitamei* — he really should have tasted the produce before designating it as *terumah*. Since he failed to take that simple precaution, his negligence is borderline intentional. The *Tosfos* raise the obvious question: how is one supposed to taste *tevel* produce before the separation? Their answer is that one should first separate *terumah* from a small piece that one then tastes for quality; having confirmed it is fit, one gives the remainder to the *kohen* as *terumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי מֵזִיד אַמֵּזִיד — הָכָא קָתָנֵי: בְּמֵזִיד לֹא עָשָׂה כְּלוּם, הָתָם תְּנַן: הַתּוֹרֵם מִשֶּׁאֵין נָקוּב עַל נָקוּב — תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחְזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara also **raises a contradiction** between one case involving **an intentional** sinner and another case **of an intentional** sinner. **Here, it** is **taught** that in the case of **an intentional** sinner who separates *teruma*, **he has done nothing. There, we learned** in a mishna (*Demai* 5:10), that with regard to **one who separates** *teruma* **from** produce growing in a vessel **that is not perforated, for** produce that grew in **a perforated** vessel, which is considered connected to the ground, it is ***teruma*, but he must go back and separate** *teruma* a second time. This ruling is based on the principle that anything that grew in a pot without a hole does not require separation of *teruma* by Torah law. In this case, the fact that he must again set aside *teruma* does not mean that the portion he separated is not consecrated at all.

A further comparison is raised: *v'rami meizid ameizid* — comparing intentional cases to intentional cases. In our *mishnah*, intentional separation of *tamei* on *tahor* is *lo asa klum*. But another *mishnah* in *Masechet Terumot* discusses taking *terumah* from a flowerpot without drainage holes (which is not obligated in *terumah*, being treated as detached from the ground) on behalf of a flowerpot with holes (which is obligated). There the ruling is *terumah v'yachzor v'yitrom* — it counts as *terumah* and one separates again. Why is that not also *lo asa klum*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּתְרֵי מָאנֵי — צָיֵית, בְּחַד מָנָא — לָא צָיֵית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **In** a case involving **two vessels he will listen.** Since the difference between the two vessels is clear to the eye, if the owner is told he must separate *teruma* again, it can be assumed that he will comply. In contrast, **in** the case of **one vessel he will not listen,** as ritually impure and pure produce look the same to him. Consequently, if he is informed that he must set aside *teruma* a second time despite the fact that the produce he already set aside has the status of *teruma*, he will take no notice.

The *gemara* answers: *bitrei mani tza'at, uvechad mana la tza'at* — when dealing with two different types of vessels (one with holes, one without), the distinction is visually apparent and the person will understand when told to separate again. When dealing with a single vessel (as in the *tamei/tahor* case — two piles of identical-looking produce), the person will protest: "What difference does it make? This is *tevel* and that is *tevel*, they look the same to me." He will not comply with a second separation, and therefore we must tell him upfront that his action accomplished nothing at all.

### Invalid Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב נָתָן בְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר: לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם — לְתַקֵּן שִׁירַיִם, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה הָוֵי, מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: מִן הַנָּקוּב עַל שֶׁאֵין נָקוּב — תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְלֹא תֵּאָכֵל עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וּמַעֲשֵׂר מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Natan, son of Rabbi Oshaya, who said** that **he has done nothing** with regard to **preparing the remaining** produce **but it is** nevertheless ***teruma*,** in **what** way **is** this case **different from that which we learned** in a mishna (*Demai* 5:10): If one separates *teruma* **from** that which grew in **a perforated** pot **for that which is** from **a non-perforated** pot, **his *teruma* is *teruma*, but it may not be eaten until he removes** on behalf **of** that portion itself ***teruma* and tithe from another place?**

The *gemara* works through several configurations of valid and invalid *terumah* separation to clarify the positions of Rav Chisda and Rav Natan bar Rav Yehoshua and where the respective concerns about negligence and *terumah* status apply. The critical variable throughout is whether we are dealing with a *shogeg* or *meizid*, and whether the two types of produce involved are visually distinguishable.

### Rabbi Ilai Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תְּרוּמָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא, כִּדְרַבִּי אִלְעַאי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעַאי: מִנַּיִן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרָעָה עַל הַיָּפָה שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה — דִּכְתִיב: "וְלֹא תִשְׂאוּ עָלָיו חֵטְא בַּהֲרִימְכֶם אֶת חֶלְבּוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here,** with regard to ritually impure *teruma*, **it is different, as by Torah** law **it is** in fact **full-fledged *teruma*,** but the Sages penalized him by making him separate *teruma* again. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elai, as Rabbi Elai said: From where** is it derived **with regard to one who separates *teruma* from poor-**quality produce **for superior**-quality produce **that his *teruma* is *teruma*? As it is written** with regard to *teruma*: **"And you shall bear no sin by reason of it, seeing that you have set apart from it its best"** (Numbers 18:32).

Rav Natan bar Rav Yehoshua's position that *tamei terumah* intentionally separated still has genuine *terumah* status is grounded in Rabbi Ilai's teaching. Rabbi Ilai asks: *minayin li-trom min ha-ra'ah al ha-yafah she-terumato terumah* — how do we know that if one separates *teruma* from low-quality produce on behalf of high-quality produce, it nonetheless counts as *terumah*? He derives it from the *pasuk*: *v'lo tisu alav cheit ba-harimechem et chelbo mimenu* — "you shall not carry a sin upon it when you take the best of it." The *gemara* reasons: *v'ee ein kodesh, nesi'at cheit lama* — if separating from low-quality produce were not *terumah* at all, there would be no legal implication to the act, and therefore no *cheit* to speak of. The very fact that the *pasuk* speaks of a *cheit* for failing to take the *chelev* — the best — proves that when one does separate from inferior produce, the separation is legally effective as *terumah*. Otherwise, the person has done nothing at all and there is nothing to have a *cheit* about.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵין קָדוֹשׁ — נְשִׂיאוּת חֵטְא לָמָּה? מִיכָּן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרָעָה עַל הַיָּפָה — שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elai elaborates: **And if** this inferior portion is **not sanctified** as *teruma* at all, **why** is there **a bearing of sin?** If the produce does not have the status of *teruma* he has not done anything, which means that his action cannot be considered a transgression. **From here** we learn **with regard to one who separated from the bad for the good that his *teruma* is *teruma*** after the fact.

The *pasuk* itself, when read contextually, is an exhortation to remember to give *terumah* and to give it from the best. But Rabbi Ilai reads it to yield a specific *halachic* derivation: the obligation to give the best implies that giving the worst still constitutes a valid act of *terumah* — *mikan she'ha-toram min ha-ra'ah al ha-yafah, terumato terumah*.

## Beit Din Authority

### Challenge to Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה לְרַב חִסְדָּא: לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם כׇּל עִיקָּר, דַּאֲפִילּוּ הָהוּא גְּרִיוָא הָדַר לְטִיבְלֵיהּ, מַאי טַעְמָא — גְּזֵירָה דִּלְמָא פָּשַׁע וְלָא מַפְרֵישׁ, מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הָוֵי תְּרוּמָה, וּמִשּׁוּם דִּלְמָא פָּשַׁע אַפְּקוּהּ רַבָּנַן לְחוּלִּין? וְכִי בֵּית דִּין מַתְנִין לַעֲקוֹר דָּבָר מִן הַתּוֹרָה?
>
> **Translation:** After clarifying the opinions themselves, the Gemara analyzes the case in greater detail. **Rabba said to Rav Ḥisda: According to your** opinion, **that you said he has done nothing at all,** meaning **that even that *se'a*** he set aside **returns to** its former **untithed** state, **what is the reason** for this? It is a rabbinic **decree,** as **perhaps he will be negligent and will not separate** *teruma* a second time. However, **is there anything that by Torah** law **is *teruma*,** as stated by Rabbi Elai, **and** yet **due to** the concern: **Perhaps he will be negligent, the Sages removed** its status as sacred and gave **to** it the status of **non-sacred** food? **And can the court stipulate** and enact a decree **to uproot something** that applies **by Torah** law?

Rabbah now turns to Rav Chisda with a sharp challenge: according to Rav Chisda, who holds that intentionally separating *tamei terumah* on *tahor* produce accomplishes absolutely nothing — even the portion physically removed reverts to *tevel* — the reason is a *gezeira* against negligence: we worry that if we call it *terumah*, the person will not separate again.

But this is a remarkable claim. *Mi ika midi de-mide'oraita havi trumah, u-mishum dilma pasha, afkuha rabanan l'chulin?* — Can it be that something which is *terumah de'oraita* is retroactively stripped of that status and converted back into *chulin* by the Rabbis, simply because they are worried the person might not act properly? *V'chi beit din matnin la'akor davar min ha-Torah?* — Can *beit din* make a ruling that uproots something from the Torah?

### Mamzer Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאַתְּ לָא תִּסְבְּרַאּ? וְהָתְנַן: הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא מִשֵּׁנִי מַמְזֵר — אֶלָּא מֵרִאשׁוֹן אַמַּאי? אִשְׁתּוֹ הִיא, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְקָא שָׁרֵינַן לֵיהּ בְּמַמְזֶרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to** Rabba: **And you, do you not hold** that the Sages have the power to do so? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **the child is a *mamzer* from this one and from that one? Granted,** the child **from the second** man is **a *mamzer*,** as he was born to a married woman from a man who was not her husband. **However,** with regard to the child **from the first** husband, **why** is he a *mamzer*? After all, **she is his wife, and** by Torah law their son **is a full-fledged Jew. And** the Sages' declaration that he is a *mamzer* cannot be seen as a mere stringency, as **they** thereby **permit him to a *mamzeret*.** This shows that a rabbinical decree can uproot a Torah prohibition.

Before Rav Chisda responds, Rabbah himself presents the *mamzer* case as an instance of exactly this problem. *V'hatnan, havlad mamzer mizeh umizeh* — the *mishnah* declares the children *mamzerim* from both husbands. With respect to the second husband, this is straightforward: she was still legally married to the first husband when she cohabited with the second, and so the child is a genuine *mamzer de'oraita*.

But what of a child from the first husband? *Ishto hi* — she is his legal wife. *V'Yisroel ma'alya hu* — and he is a perfectly kosher Jew. How can their child be a *mamzer*? And this is not a mere stringency we can treat lightly: *v'kashrina lei b'mamzeret* — by declaring him a *mamzer*, we are binding him to a status that restricts whom he may marry. He cannot marry a *bat Yisroel*, and he cannot marry a *mamzeret* either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָסוּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָסוּר בְּמַמְזֶרֶת. וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ "מַמְזֵר" — לְאוֹסְרוֹ בְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to** Rav Ḥisda that **Shmuel said as follows:** It is **forbidden** for the child of the first husband **to** marry **a *mamzeret*. And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the child is **forbidden to a *mamzeret*. And** if so, **why does** the mishna **call him a *mamzer*?** This is **to** teach us that the Sages are stringent and render it **prohibited** for **him to** marry a regular **Jewish woman.**

Indeed, the *gemara* brings in the ruling of Shmuel — confirmed by Rabbi Yochanan when Ravin came from *Eretz Yisroel* — that this child is *assur b'mamzeret*, forbidden even to a *mamzeret*. This is a *mamzer l'chumra* in the most stringent sense: he cannot marry a *bat Yisroel* (because the Rabbis call him a *mamzer*) and cannot marry a *mamzeret* (because *de'oraita* he is a perfectly kosher Jew, and the Rabbis did not create a positive permission for him to marry a *mamzeret* — only the real prohibition). The *gemara* states explicitly: *v'amai karay lei mamzer? L'osro b'vat Yisroel* — why do we call him a *mamzer*? Specifically in order to prevent him from marrying a *bat Yisroel*. He can, however, apparently marry another *mamzer derabbanan*. This is the painful architecture of *mamzeirus derabbanan*: the child is the vehicle through which the mother is punished, and he is caught in a status that makes him, in one sense, worse off than a *mamzer de'oraita*.

### Chisda's Examples

Rav Chisda responds to Rabbah's challenge — *v'at lo tisbera?* You yourself don't think *beit din* has that power? — by marshaling examples of cases where Chazal undeniably do uproot Torah obligations. The *mamzer* case just discussed is itself such an example: a child of a perfectly valid marriage is declared a *mamzer* by rabbinic decree.

### Minor Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה בְּיַד רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא: וְאֵין בֵּית דִּין מַתְנִין לַעֲקוֹר דָּבָר מִן הַתּוֹרָה? וְהָתַנְיָא: מֵאֵימָתַי אָדָם יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ קְטַנָּה? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁתַּעֲמוֹד בְּקוֹמָתָהּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁתִּכָּנֵס לַחוּפָּה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁתִּבָּעֵל. וְיוֹרְשָׁהּ, וּמִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ, וְאוֹכֶלֶת בְּגִינוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda sent** a proof **to Rabba, in the hand** of **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna: And can the court not stipulate to uproot something prohibited by Torah** law? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From when does a man inherit** from **his minor wife,** who was married off by her mother or brother, as enacted by the Sages, a marriage that is not valid by Torah law? **Beit Shammai say: From when she stands at her** fully developed **height,** i.e., when she grows up and reaches the age of maturity. **And Beit Hillel say: From when she enters the wedding canopy. Rabbi Eliezer says: From when she has sexual relations.** As soon as she is considered his wife, all the *halakhot* of a wife apply to her. **And he inherits** from **her and becomes impure for her** if she dies, even if he is a priest, **and she eats *teruma* on his account** if he is a priest.

Rav Chisda sends Rabbah further proof via Rav Acha bar Rav Huna: the *baraita* dealing with the marriage of a minor girl (*ketanah*). When a father dies, the mother and brothers may arrange the marriage of a daughter who has not yet reached majority. Such a marriage is only *de'rabbanan*; the girl retains the right, upon reaching majority, to exercise *mi'un* — refusal — and leave without a *get*, with no blemish to her status. The *baraita* states that this husband can inherit her, can become *tamei* for her if he is a *kohen*, and she can eat *terumah* by virtue of his household. The dispute is about the precise timing.

### When Inherit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁתַּעֲמוֹד בְּקוֹמָתָהּ. אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נִכְנְסָה לַחוּפָּה? אֵימָא: מִשֶּׁתַּעֲמוֹד בְּקוֹמָתָהּ וְתִכָּנֵס לַחוּפָּה. וְהָכִי קָאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: דְּקָאָמְרִיתוּ מִשֶּׁתִּכָּנֵס לַחוּפָּה, אִי עָמְדָה בְּקוֹמָתָהּ — מַהְנְיָא לַהּ חוּפָּה, וְאִי לָא — לָא מַהְנְיָא לַהּ חוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first clarifies various details of this *baraita*. **Beit Shammai say: From when she stands at her height.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this statement: Is this the case **even though she has not** yet **entered the wedding canopy?** Betrothal alone, without the marriage canopy, is not enough to enable a man to inherit from an adult woman, let alone a minor. The Gemara answers: You must **say** that Beit Shammai meant **from when she stands at her** fully developed **height and enters the wedding canopy. And Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel as follows: That which you said,** that he inherits her **from when she enters the wedding canopy,** is not enough. Rather, **if she stands at her** fully developed **height,** the **wedding canopy is effective for** bringing about **her** marriage, **and if not,** the **wedding canopy is not effective for** bringing about **her** marriage.

*Beit Shammai* holds the husband can inherit her *mishe'ta'amod be-komatah* — from when she reaches physical maturity (in years and signs). *Beit Hillel* holds it is *mishetikanes le-chuppah* — from when she enters the *chuppah*. *Rabbi Eliezer* holds it is *mishetiba'el* — from when the marriage is consummated.

The *gemara* then clarifies *Beit Shammai's* position. One might have read them as requiring *both* maturity *and* the *chuppah*. The *gemara* explains that this is actually Beit Shammai's critique of Beit Hillel: Beit Shammai is saying to Beit Hillel — when does the *chuppah* actually take legal effect? Only if she has already reached majority. If she is too young, the *chuppah* is legally meaningless. Conversely, Beit Shammai holds that once she is of age, *chuppah* alone is sufficient — even without a second ceremony.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁתִּבָּעֵל. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אֵין מַעֲשֵׂה קְטַנָּה כְּלוּם! אֵימָא: מִשֶּׁתַּגְדִּיל וְתִבָּעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the *baraita*. **Rabbi Eliezer says: From when she has sexual relations.** The Gemara again expresses surprise: **But didn't Rabbi Eliezer say** that **the actions of a minor girl are nothing,** which indicates that marriage and intercourse with her are not considered an act of acquisition. Rather, we must **say** that he meant **from when she matures and has sexual relations.**

The *gemara* then notes a tension with Rabbi Eliezer's own position: *v'ha amar Rabbi Eliezer ein ma'aseh ketanah klum* — Rabbi Eliezer himself holds that the acts of a *ketanah* are legally null. If so, how can consummation of the marriage while she is still a minor establish his right to inherit her? The resolution is *eima mishe-tagdil ve-tibaeil* — Rabbi Eliezer must mean from when she has reached majority *and then* the marriage is consummated. This aligns him with the thrust of Beit Shammai's position.

### Hefker Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת יוֹרְשָׁהּ — וְהָא הָכָא, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אֲבוּהָ יָרֵית לַהּ, וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן יָרֵית לַהּ בַּעַל?! הֶפְקֵר בֵּית דִּין הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the issue at hand, whether the court can stipulate to uproot something prohibited by Torah law. **In any event** the *baraita* **is teaching** that **he inherits** from **her. But here** is a case **where by Torah** law **her father,** i.e., his relatives, as he is dead, **inherits** from **her,** as a minor orphan cannot marry by Torah law, **and** yet **by rabbinic** law the **husband inherits** from **her.** This shows that the Sages can uproot a Torah mitzva. The Gemara answers: This is no proof, as in monetary matters **property declared ownerless by the court is ownerless,** and therefore the court can allocate her inheritance as they see fit.

Returning to the central question: the *baraita* states *yorshah* — he inherits her. But *d'midoraita* her family inherits her property, since the marriage is only *derabbanan*. The Rabbis are therefore taking property that belongs *de'oraita* to her family and transferring it to the husband. This is a direct application of *hefker beit din hefker* — the principle that *beit din* has the authority to declare property ownerless and redistribute it. Rav Chisda is using this as proof that rabbinic authorities can indeed override Torah-level property rights.

### Ezra Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן שֶׁהֶפְקֵר בֵּית דִּין הֶפְקֵר — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כֹּל אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָבֹא לִשְׁלֹשֶׁת הַיָּמִים בַּעֲצַת הַשָּׂרִים וְהַזְּקֵנִים יׇחֳרַם כׇּל רְכוּשׁוֹ וְהוּא יִבָּדֵל מִקְּהַל הַגּוֹלָה".
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where** is it derived **that property declared ownerless by the court is ownerless? As it is stated: "And whoever does not come within three days, according to the council of the princes and the Elders, all his property should be forfeited, and himself separated from the congregation of the captivity"** (Ezra 10:8).

The *gemara* asks: *minayin she-hefker beit din hayah hefker* — what is the source for this principle? Rabbi Yitzchak cites the verse in Ezra: *kol asher lo yavo li-shloshet ha-yamim ba-atzat ha-sarim ve-ha-zekenim, yachoram kol rechusho* — whoever fails to appear within three days at the order of the princes and elders will have all his property confiscated and he will be separated from the community. The *gemara* notes that this proof is not quite a textual derivation in the classical sense — it is not using the verse to establish what God intended as law. Rather, Ezra's authoritative precedent demonstrates that *beit din* possesses this power; the assumption is that Ezra was acting within the framework of *halacha*, and therefore his action is evidence that *halacha* permits it.

### Joshua Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: "אֵלֶּה הַנְּחָלוֹת אֲשֶׁר נִחֲלוּ אֶלְעָזָר הַכֹּהֵן וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן וְרָאשֵׁי הָאָבוֹת לְמַטּוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל", וְכִי מָה עִנְיַן רָאשִׁים אֵצֶל אָבוֹת? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה אָבוֹת מַנְחִילִים בְּנֵיהֶם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצוּ — אַף רָאשִׁים מַנְחִילִים אֶת הָעָם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that the proof that the court can declare property ownerless is **from here: "These are the inheritances that Elazar the priest, and Joshua, son of Nun, and the heads of the fathers' houses of the tribes of the children of Israel distributed for inheritance"** (Joshua 19:51). **What do heads have to do with fathers?** The expression "the heads of the fathers' houses of the tribes" is unusual and vague. **Rather,** this comes **to tell you: Just as fathers bequeath to their sons anything they want** to, **so too,** the **heads,** i.e., the leaders and judges of the people, **bequeath to the people anything they want** to.

Rabbi Elazar cites a different source, from Yehoshua: *eleh ha-nechalot asher nichalu Elazar ha-Kohen v'Yehoshua bin Nun v'rashei ha-avot l'matot Bnei Yisrael* — these are the inheritances that Elazar the Kohen, Yehoshua bin Nun, and the heads of the fathers of the tribes of Israel distributed. Rabbi Elazar asks: what is the connection between *rashim* (leaders) and *avot* (fathers)? He derives: just as fathers apportion inheritance to their children however they wish, so too the *rashim* — the *beit din* — can distribute the people's property however they choose. Here, unlike the Ezra source, there is a genuine textual derivation being offered.

### Tamei Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אָבִיהָ מִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ, וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן מִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ בַּעַל! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ מֵת מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated that the husband of a minor **becomes impure for her,** even if he is a priest. The Gemara asks: **But here** is a case **where by Torah** law **her father,** not the man she married, is obligated to **become impure for her,** as the latter is not his wife by Torah law, **and** yet **by rabbinic** law her **husband defiles himself for her.** The Gemara answers: This is **because she is** considered like **a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*],** for which even a priest must become impure. Once she is married, her relatives from her father's family no longer care for her welfare, which means her husband is the only one who is entrusted with her burial.

The *baraita* also states *u-metame lah* — if the minor wife dies, her husband (if he is a *kohen*) may become *tamei* for her. But *de'oraita*, only her father may become *tamei* for her — the husband's right to do so comes from the rabbinic recognition of the marriage. The Rabbis are therefore overriding a *de'oraita* restriction on a *kohen's* becoming *tamei*.

### Met Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הָוְיָ[א] מֵת מִצְוָה? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵי זֶהוּ מֵת מִצְוָה — כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קוֹבְרִין. קוֹרֵא וַאֲחֵרִים עוֹנִין אוֹתוֹ — אֵין זֶה מֵת מִצְוָה! הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא יָרְתִי לַהּ — קָרְיָא וְלָא עָנוּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is she** in fact **a *met mitzva*? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Which** corpse **is a *met mitzva*? Any** corpse **that does not have** anyone **to bury it.** If it was in a place where if **one calls and others** would **answer him, this is not a *met mitzva*.** In contrast, this girl does have relatives who can bury her, if necessary. The Gemara answers: **Here too, since** the members of her father's family **do not inherit** from **her,** she would **call and they** would **not answer her,** as they have no desire to go to any trouble for her. Since her husband inherits from her, it is his duty to tend to her burial, and he must therefore become impure for her, as she has the status of a *met mitzva*.

The justification offered is *mishum de-havya lah met mitzvah* — she is treated as a *met mitzvah*, a corpse with no one to bury it. The *baraita* defines *met mitzvah* as *kol she'ein lo kovrim* — one for whom there are no available mourners. *Korei v'achereim onim oto, ein zeh met mitzvah* — if one calls out and others respond that they will attend to the burial, that is not a *met mitzvah*. How does this girl become a *met mitzvah*? Because the Rabbis, through *hefker beit din hefker*, have transferred her property to the husband. Her family therefore says: since we receive no inheritance from her, we decline to bury her — let the one who inherits her take care of the burial. When the husband calls out for the family to bury their daughter and they refuse, she becomes a *met mitzvah* and he, as her husband and heir, must attend to her. This is admittedly a stark picture of the rabbinic reconstruction of incentives.

### Terumah D'Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹכֶלֶת בְּגִינוֹ תְּרוּמָה. בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, the *baraita* states **she eats *teruma* on his account.** The Gemara explains: This is referring to ***teruma*** that applies **by rabbinic** law, not by Torah law.

Finally, the *baraita* states *v'ochelet bigino terumah* — she eats *terumah* by virtue of being part of his household. The *gemara* specifies: *biterumah derabbanan* — she eats only *terumah derabbanan*, since the marriage itself is *derabbanan*. A marriage that exists only by rabbinic enactment confers only rabbinic-level rights, and therefore *terumah de'oraita* is not available to her through this marriage.
