# Yevamot 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Single Witness Testimony

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וּבָאוּ וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ ״מֵת בַּעְלִיךְ״, וְנִיסֵּת, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא בַּעְלָהּ — תֵּצֵא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה, וּצְרִיכָה גֵּט מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה, וְאֵין לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה, וְלֹא פֵּירוֹת, וְלֹא מְזוֹנוֹת, וְלֹא בְּלָאוֹת — לֹא עַל זֶה וְלֹא עַל זֶה. וְאִם נָטְלָה מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה — תַּחְזִיר. וְהַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה. וְלֹא זֶה וָזֶה מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ, וְלֹא זֶה וָזֶה זַכַּאִים לֹא בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ, וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וְלֹא בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ. הָיְתָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — נִפְסְלָה מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. וּבַת לֵוִי — מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר, וּבַת כֹּהֵן — מִן הַתְּרוּמָה. וְאֵין יוֹרְשִׁין שֶׁל זֶה וְיוֹרְשִׁין שֶׁל זֶה יוֹרְשִׁין אֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. וְאִם מֵתוּ — אָחִיו שֶׁל זֶה וְאָחִיו שֶׁל זֶה חוֹלְצִין וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּתוּבָּתָהּ עַל נִכְסֵי בַּעְלָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן זַכַּאִי בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ וּבְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּיאָתָהּ אוֹ חֲלִיצָתָהּ מֵאָחִיו שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ. וְאֵין הַוָּלָד מִמֶּנּוּ מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman whose husband went overseas, and witnesses came and said to her: Your husband is dead, and she married another man on the basis of this testimony, and afterward her husband came back from out of the country, she must leave both this man and that one, as they are both forbidden to her. And she requires a bill of divorce from this one and that one, and she has neither payment of her marriage contract, nor the profits of her property used by either of them, nor sustenance, nor the worn clothes she brought to the marriage. She has rights to these claims neither against this man nor against that one.** If she took any of these items **from this man or from that one, she must return** them to him. **And the offspring is a *mamzer* from this one and from that one.** Her child from the second husband is a definite *mamzer*, as she was never divorced from her first husband, and the Sages decreed that if she returned to her first husband, a child born later from him is also a *mamzer*. **And neither this man nor that man may become impure for her upon her death, if they are priests. And neither this one nor that one is entitled to the rights that stem from the marriage bond: Neither to her found articles, nor to her earnings, nor to the nullification of her vows. If she was an Israelite woman, she is disqualified from marrying into the priesthood, and the daughter of a Levite is disqualified from partaking of the first tithe, and the daughter of a priest is disqualified from partaking of teruma.** And neither the heirs of this man nor the heirs of that one inherit her marriage contract, as she is not considered married to either of them. This clause will be explained in the Gemara. **And if they both died childless, the brothers of this one and the brothers of this one must perform ḥalitza and they do not enter into levirate marriage. Rabbi Yosei says: Her marriage contract is upon the property of her first husband. Rabbi Elazar says: The first husband is entitled to her found articles, to her earnings, and to the nullification of her vows.** Since her second marriage was an error, the first husband does not forfeit his rights. **Rabbi Shimon says: Her sexual relations or her ḥalitza with the brothers of the first husband exempts her rival wife, as it is considered a proper levirate marriage or ḥalitza, and the child from him is not a *mamzer*.**

The perek of *Ha-Ishah Rabbah* is one of the most perplexing and challenging *perakim* in all of Shas — not only analytically, but in terms of *rabbinic* justice. The central difficulty is understanding how a woman who appears to have done everything right ends up in such severe legal jeopardy: she must leave both husbands, and she loses her rights from each of them.

The *Talmud* begins by identifying the precise situation described in the *Mishnah*. A single witness came and told the woman that her husband was dead. She went to the *beit din*, received permission to remarry, and did so. Because the remarriage was predicated on the testimony of a single witness, all of these harsh consequences follow. Had two witnesses come and told her that her husband was dead, the picture would be entirely different — she could return to her first husband, the second marriage would be treated as a nullity, and so on. What the *Mishnah*'s structure implies, then, is that a single witness is in fact *believed* in such a case.

### Korban Chatas Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: נִשֵּׂאת שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת בֵּית דִּין, אֶלָּא בְּעֵדִים. מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא בִּרְשׁוּת בֵּית דִּין, וּבְעֵד אֶחָד. אַלְמָא עֵד אֶחָד נֶאֱמָן. וּתְנַן נָמֵי: הוּחְזְקוּ לִהְיוֹת מַשִּׂיאִין עֵד מִפִּי עֵד, וְאִשָּׁה מִפִּי אִשָּׁה, וְאִשָּׁה מִפִּי עֶבֶד וּמִפִּי שִׁפְחָה. אַלְמָא עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן. וּתְנַן נָמֵי: עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אָכַלְתָּ חֵלֶב״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ — פָּטוּר. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״, הָא אִישְׁתִּיק — מְהֵימַן. אַלְמָא עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אוֹ הוֹדַע אֵלָיו חַטָּאתוֹ״. וְלֹא שֶׁיּוֹדִיעוּהוּ אֲחֵרִים. יָכוֹל אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכְחִישׁוֹ יְהֵא פָּטוּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ הוֹדַע אֵלָיו״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דַּאֲתוֹ תְּרֵי וְלָא קָא מַכְחִישׁ לְהוּ, קְרָא לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לָאו — חַד, וְכִי לָא קָא מַכְחִישׁ לֵיהּ — מְהֵימַן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ עֵד אֶחָד נֶאֱמָן. וּמִמַּאי דְּמִשּׁוּם דִּמְהֵימַן? דִּלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא שָׁתֵיק, וּשְׁתִיקָה כְּהוֹדָאָה דָמְיָא? תֵּדַע, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אָמְרוּ שְׁנַיִם ״אָכַלְתָּ חֵלֶב״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ — פָּטוּר. רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, אִם הֱבִיאוּהוּ שְׁנַיִם לִידֵי מִיתָה חֲמוּרָה — לֹא יְבִיאוּהוּ לִידֵי קׇרְבָּן הַקַּל? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מָה אִם יִרְצֶה לוֹמַר ״מֵזִיד הָיִיתִי״! רֵישָׁא
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From the fact that the latter clause of the mishna teaches: If she married without the consent of the court she is permitted to return to him, this indicates that she did so not by the consent of the court, but rather by witnesses, i.e., as there are two witnesses, she does not require special permission from the court. With this in mind, it may be inferred that the first clause of the mishna, which speaks of one who acted with the consent of the court, is referring to a situation when there was one witness. Apparently, one witness is deemed credible when he testifies about a husband's death.** **And we also learned in a mishna: They established that they would allow a woman to marry if her husband was reported dead by one witness, based solely on what he learned from the mouth of another witness, i.e., hearsay testimony, and also the testimony of a woman who heard from another woman, and even the testimony of a woman who heard from a slave or from a maidservant. Apparently, one witness is deemed credible in this regard.** **And we also learned in a mishna that if one witness says to someone: You ate forbidden fat, and the accused says: I did not eat it, the accused is exempt from bringing an offering. The reason he is exempt is that the individual in question said: I did not eat it, which indicates that if he had been silent and failed to deny the accusation, the lone witness is deemed credible. Apparently, one witness is deemed credible by Torah law with regard to certain issues.** **From where do we derive this? As it is taught in a *baraita* that the verse states: "Or if his sin be known to him." This indicates that he himself must be aware of his sin, and not if it was made known to him by others.** **The Gemara clarifies this *halakha*. What are the circumstances? If we say that two witnesses came and informed him and he does not contradict them, why do I need a verse to teach this ruling? After all, the testimony of two witnesses is always accepted. Rather, is it not referring to one witness, and yet if he does not contradict the sole witness, that witness is deemed credible? One can learn from this that one witness is deemed credible with regard to prohibitions.** **And from where do you infer that the reason is due to the fact that the one witness is deemed credible? Perhaps the accused must bring an offering because he remains silent, as there is a principle that silence is considered like an admission.**

This leads the *Gemara* to a further implication. It is established that if one witness comes and says *achalta chelev* — "you ate forbidden fat" — and the accused responds *lo achalti chelev*, he is *patur* from bringing a *korban*. But if he remains silent, he would be *chayav*. This means that on the basis of a single witness, one is obligated to bring a *korban chatas* — not merely an *asham talui*, the offering brought in cases of genuine doubt, but the *chatas* itself, which implies that the sin is treated as established. This in turn implies that the single witness has been believed *m'doraisa* — a striking conclusion.

The *Gemara* asks: *minalan* — from where do we derive that a single witness can be believed at the Torah level? The source is a *baraita* interpreting the verse *o hoda eilav chataso*: his sin must become known *to him*, and not through others informing him. The *baraita* raises the possibility that even if he fails to contradict someone who accuses him, he might still be *patur* — since another person informed him, perhaps that does not satisfy the requirement of *o hoda eilav*. The verse is therefore read broadly: *mikol makom*, from any situation, he can come to know of his sin.

### Ne'emanut vs. Shtika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא קָא מְחַיְּיבִי רַבָּנַן? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּמְהֵימַן — וְהָא תְּרֵי בְּעָלְמָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מַכְחִישׁ לְהוּ אִינְהוּ — מְהֵימְנִי, וְקָא פָּטְרִי רַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִישְׁתִּיק — וּשְׁתִיקָה כְּהוֹדָאָה דָמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason that when he remains silent, the Rabbis obligate him to bring to an offering based on the testimony of one witness? If we say it is because the witness is deemed credible, but there is the case of an ordinary pair of witnesses, where even though he contradicts their claim they are deemed credible, and yet the Rabbis exempt him from bringing an offering. If so, they would certainly not obligate him to bring an offering due to the testimony of a lone witness. Rather, is it not because he remained silent, and silence is considered like an admission?**

The *Gemara* now asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is this case? If two witnesses came and the accused failed to contradict them, why would we need a verse at all? Two witnesses establish a fact; the *korban* obligation would be obvious. It must therefore be that the *baraita* is discussing a single witness — *chad* — and the inference is that if one fails to contradict a single witness, one is *chayav*. This would mean that the single witness is actually believed, *shema mina eid echad ne'eman*.

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *umimai demishum de'meiman*? Perhaps this has nothing to do with the reliability of the witness at all. Perhaps the reason for the *chiyuv* is *mishum de'kashtik* — because the accused was silent. And *shtika ke'hoda'ah damya* — silence functions as a form of admission. This is confirmed by the *seifa* of the *baraita*.

### Two Witnesses Problem

The end of the *baraita* states: if two witnesses say *achalta chelev* and the accused says *lo achalti*, he is *patur* — even against two witnesses. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees and holds him *chayav*. This is a striking passage. Our initial assumption was that two witnesses obviously should be believed — *lama li kra*, why would we need a verse to establish what is self-evident? But the *seifa* reveals that the *Rabbanan* do not convict even on the basis of two witnesses if the accused denies it. This suggests that the *Rabbanan* are not operating in the realm of *ne'emanut* — of witness reliability — at all.

### Shtika K'Hoda'ah

*Rabbi Meir* argues by way of a *kal vachomer*: if two witnesses can bring a person to the death penalty, surely they should be sufficient to obligate a *korban*, which is far less severe. The *Rabbanan* respond: the accused is not without recourse even against two witnesses, because he could always say *mezid hayiti* — I acted intentionally — in which case no *korban* would be due. This is a form of *migo*: since he has available to him a more extreme claim that would exempt him, we credit his denial as well. The deeper point is that a *korban chatas* depends not on whether a person sinned, but on whether they *know* they sinned. No external testimony can substitute for a person's own internal certainty about what occurred and what their intentions were.

### Rabbi Meir's Kal Vachomer

Returning to the opening of the *baraita*, the *Gemara* asks: *reisha mai ta'ama kemachayvei Rabbanan* — why do the *Rabbanan* hold that silence in the face of a single witness creates *chiyuv*? If it were because the single witness is *ne'eman*, then we would be back to a contradiction — here are two witnesses who are *ne'eman* in all circumstances, yet the *Rabbanan* exempt the accused even against their testimony. The answer must be that the *Rabbanan* are not working within the framework of *ne'emanut* at all. Rather, *ela lav mishum de'ishtik* — it is entirely because the accused was silent. *Shtika ke'hoda'ah damya*: silence is treated as an admission.

### Internal Knowledge Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סְבָרָא הִיא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַחֲתִיכָה סָפֵק שֶׁל חֵלֶב סָפֵק שֶׁל שׁוּמָּן, וַאֲתָא עֵד אֶחָד וְאָמַר: בָּרִי לִי, דְּשׁוּמָּן הוּא — דִּמְהֵימַן. מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא אִיתַּחְזַק אִיסּוּרָא, הָכָא אִיתַּחְזַק אִיסּוּרָא דְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְאֵין דָּבָר שֶׁבְּעֶרְוָה פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם! הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לַחֲתִיכָה דְּוַדַּאי חֵלֶב, וַאֲתָא עֵד אֶחָד וְאָמַר: בָּרִי לִי דְּשׁוּמָּן הֲוָה — דְּלָא מְהֵימַן! מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, אֲפִילּוּ אָתוּ בֵּי מְאָה לָא מְהֵימְנִי. הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּכִי אָתוּ בֵּי תְרֵי מְהֵימְנִי — חַד נָמֵי לְהֵימְנֵיהּ, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַטֶּבֶל, הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְקֻוֽנָּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is evidently based on logical reasoning: Just as it is in the case of a piece of meat about which it is uncertain if it is forbidden fat and uncertain if it is of permitted fat, and there is no way of clarifying which it is, and one witness comes and says: It is clear to me that it is permitted fat, the halakha is that he is deemed credible.** **The Gemara raises a difficulty: Is it comparable? There, the presumption of a prohibition has not been established, as there is no proof that the piece was ever forbidden, and one can therefore rely on the witness who permits it, whereas here, the presumption of the prohibition with regard to a married woman is established, and there is a principle that nothing involving those with whom relations are forbidden can be determined by fewer than two witnesses.** **In fact, this is comparable only to a case involving a piece of meat that is definitely forbidden fat, and one witness comes and says: It is clear to me that it is permitted fat, as the halakha is that he is not deemed credible.** **Is it comparable? There, when it is established as forbidden fat, even if one hundred witnesses come they are not deemed credible. Here, since if two witnesses come and say the husband is dead they would be deemed credible, let us also deem one witness credible.** **This is just as it is in the case of untithed produce, i.e., produce from which neither *teruma* nor tithe has been separated, consecrated property, and *konamot*, an alternative term for offerings used in vows creating prohibitions.**

The *Gemara* therefore proposes to abandon the verse entirely and seek a *sevara* — a logical basis. It cites the known case of *safek chelev safek shuman*: a piece of meat that might be *chelev* or might be permitted fat, where a single witness comes and declares *bari li d'shuman hu* — I am certain it is permitted — and is believed. A brief aside is warranted here: it is striking that the *Talmud* reaches for the analogy of the piece of meat to describe the legal status of a wife. Feminist scholars of the *Talmud* have noted this kind of comparison, as the *din* of the woman in this case is treated as formally identical to the *din* of a cut of meat.

### Chelev Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי טֶבֶל, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּידֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לְתַקְּנוֹ. אֶלָּא דְּאַחֵר, מַאי קָסָבַר: אִי קָא סָבַר: תּוֹרֵם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ עַל שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ דַּעַת בְּעָלִים — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לְתַקְּנוֹ, וְאִי קָסָבַר: צָרִיךְ דַּעַת בְּעָלִים, וְאָמַר: אֲנָא יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ דִּמְתַקַּן, הִיא גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? הֶקְדֵּשׁ נָמֵי, אִי קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לִפְדּוֹתוֹ. אִי קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, אִי דִּידֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לְאִיתְּשׁוֹלֵי עֲלֵיהּ. אֶלָּא דְּאַחֵר, וְאָמַר: יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ דְּאִיתְּשִׁיל מָרֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ, הִיא גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? קֻוֽנָּמוֹת נָמֵי, אִי קָסָבַר: יֵשׁ מְעִילָה בְּקֻוֽנָּמוֹת, וּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים נָחֲתָא לְהוּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לִפְדּוֹתוֹ, וְאִי קָסָבַר: אֵין מְעִילָה בְּקֻוֽנָּמוֹת, וְאִיסּוּר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּרָכֵיב לְהוּ אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ, אִי דִּידֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדוֹ לְאִיתְּשׁוֹלֵי עֲלֵיהּ, אֶלָּא דְּאַחֵר, וְאָמַר: אֲנָא יָדַעְנָא דְּאִיתְּשִׁיל מָרֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ — הִיא גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara asks: With regard to this case of untithed produce, what are the circumstances? If it is his, and he testifies that *terumot* and tithes have been separated from it, he should be deemed credible because it is within his power to prepare the produce for consumption by separating tithes whenever he wishes. Rather, you must say that he testifies with regard to untithed produce of another, but if so, what does the anonymous Sage who cited this example hold in this case?** **If he holds that one who separates tithes from his produce for that of another does not require the owner's knowledge, and he can prepare his friend's produce for consumption whenever he chooses, in this case too his testimony is deemed credible because it is within his power to prepare it. And if he holds that the owner's knowledge is required before someone else can separate the gifts, and this is referring to a situation where the witness comes and says: I know with regard to it that it is prepared, in that case, it itself, this very halakha, from where do we derive it?** **Similarly, with regard to consecrated property too, if it is merely sanctity that inheres in its value, i.e., it is not an actual offering but an item that has been dedicated to the Temple upkeep, then the reason why the testimony of one witness who says it is not consecrated is accepted is due to the fact that it is within his power to redeem it. And if this is referring to inherent sanctity, the matter still remains to be clarified: If it is his offering, then the reason is due to the fact that it is within his power to request from a Sage that the vow be dissolved, like any other vow. Rather, you must say that it is referring to the offering of another, and he said: I know with regard to it that its owner requested from a Sage that his vow be dissolved. However, here too, in this case itself, from where do we derive that he is deemed credible?** **In the case of *konamot* too, if he holds that there is misuse of consecrated objects with regard to *konamot*, i.e., he holds that articles sanctified by a *konam* have the status of consecrated property, and that the sanctity that inheres in its value applies to them, then his claim is accepted because it is within his power to redeem it. And if he maintains that there is no misuse of consecrated objects in the case of *konamot*, and it is an ordinary prohibition that rides on its shoulders, i.e., it is forbidden due to its similarity to consecrated property despite the fact that is not fully sacred, even in this case the above argument applies: If the property in question is his, it is permitted because it is within his power to request from a Sage that his vow be dissolved. Rather, you will say that the *konam* must belong to another, and he said: I know with regard to it that its owner requested from a Sage that his vow be dissolved. However, with regard to this *halakha* itself, that one witness is deemed credible in this case, from where do we derive it?**

Can this analogy actually sustain the case of the woman? The *Gemara* objects: *hatam lo itchazek issura* — in the meat case, the prohibition was never established; we simply did not know what category the piece fell into. *Hacha itchazek issura d'eshet ish* — here we know for certain that this woman was married. Furthermore, *ein davar she-be'erva pachot mishnayim* — in cases that touch on sexual prohibitions, we require no fewer than two witnesses. These two objections are related: once we say *itchazek issura d'eshet ish*, we are in the domain of *arayot*, and therefore we cannot rely on a single witness.

The objection can be sharpened further. Our case does not resemble *safek chelev safek shuman* at all. It more closely resembles a case of *vadai chelev* — a piece of fat that is definitely forbidden — where a single witness comes and says it is actually permitted. In that case, *d'lo meheiman* — the single witness is not believed.

One might ask: is it not the case that even where *itchazek issura* applies, a single witness would be ineffective? Yes — and this is precisely the point. Even the case of *safek chelev* allows a single witness only because there is no established prohibition. The moment something is *vadai chelev* or *itchazek issura*, no number of witnesses can overturn the established fact through testimony alone. The only exception would be personal knowledge — if you yourself saw a piece of meat cut from the animal's kidney, no hundred witnesses can tell you otherwise, because it is your direct knowledge, not testimony at all, that establishes the *issur*.

This leads to a further observation from the *Gemara*: *hatam afilu bei mea lo meheimni* — in the case of *vadai chelev*, even a hundred witnesses cannot permit it. But *hacha keivan d'ki atu bei trei meheimni* — in the case of the woman, if two witnesses came and said her husband is dead, we would believe them. Therefore, *chad nami meheiman* — perhaps we should believe a single witness as well, just as we do in the analogous cases of *tevel*, *hekdesh*, and *konamot*.

### Tevel, Hekdesh, Konamot

The *Gemara* now examines each of these three parallel cases in detail to see if any of them can actually serve as the source or model for the woman's case.

Regarding *tevel* — grain from which tithes have not yet been separated — suppose we have a basket that may not have been tithed, and a single witness comes and says it has been properly separated. The *Gemara* asks: *ei dideih* — if the witness is the owner himself, then we understand why he is believed: *mishum d'beyado l'takno* — it is within his power to fix it at any moment, and since he could always tithe it, we treat his assertion that he already did so as credible. But the case that actually resembles ours must involve a witness speaking about *someone else's* property. In that case, the analysis depends on a *machloket*: can one tithe on behalf of another person using one's own grain, without the owner's consent? If so, then *mishum d'beyado l'takno* — this witness himself could effect the remedy at any moment, and so he has a degree of standing analogous to ownership. But if the owner's knowledge is required, then the witness is simply making an assertion about a fact he did not himself create — and we are back to the question of where we get the authority to believe him.

The same difficulty recurs with *hekdesh*. If something has been consecrated to the Temple and a witness says it has been redeemed, we can explain the case of *kedushat damim* — monetary consecration — by saying *mishum deyadei liptoto*: the owner can always effect a redemption. And even for *kedushat haguf* — where the animal itself has been consecrated for a *korban* — if it is the owner's own animal, *mishum deyadei leishalei alei*, he can always go to a *talmid chacham* and have the *hekdesh* annulled. But once again, the case that resembles ours is where a third party comes and says the owner went to a *talmid chacham* and cleared the animal of its consecrated status. And *hi gufa minlan* — from where do we derive that such a witness is believed?

The same pattern plays out with *konamot*. A *konam* can operate in two ways: it can render something forbidden to oneself, or it can render one's own property forbidden to others. In either case, if it is one's own *konam*, the owner can seek annulment at any time, which explains why a single witness who happens to be the owner is credited. But the moment we move to a witness testifying about someone else's *konam* — that the owner has already sought annulment — we once again reach *hi gufa minlan*. Each time the analysis is brought to the point that actually resembles our case, the underlying logic collapses.

It is notable that all of this reasoning has been *stama d'Talmuda* — anonymous throughout. Every one of these exchanges represents the back-and-forth of the *Gemara*'s own anonymous layer of analysis, and no solution has been found.

### Agunah Prevention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מִתּוֹךְ חוֹמֶר שֶׁהֶחְמַרְתָּ עָלֶיהָ בְּסוֹפָהּ — הֵקַלְתָּ עָלֶיהָ בַּתְּחִלָּה. לָא לַיחְמַיר וְלָא לַיקֵּיל! מִשּׁוּם עִיגּוּנָא אַקִּילוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Due to the stringency that you were stringent with her, the woman who married on the basis of a single witness, at the end, i.e., if it turns out that the testimony was incorrect and the husband is still alive, the halakha is very severe with her and she loses out in all regards, you are lenient with her at the beginning, by accepting the testimony of a single witness to enable the woman to marry. The Gemara suggests: If so, let us not be stringent at the end and not be lenient at the beginning.** **The Gemara answers: Due to the case of a deserted wife, the Sages were lenient with her.**

Now, for the first time, a named *amora* steps forward with a direct answer. *Amar Rabbi Zeira: mitoch chomer she'hichmarta aleha besofah hekaltah aleha betechilah.* Because the consequences at the end are so severe — because she must leave both husbands, forfeit her *ketubah*, and face all the attendant penalties — the rabbis did not need to make life so difficult for her at the beginning. Knowing full well that she stands to lose everything if she acts precipitously, she can be trusted to wait until she is genuinely certain that her husband is dead before proceeding to remarry. Therefore the rabbis permitted reliance on a single witness.

The *Gemara* responds tartly: *lo tachmir velo takal* — do not make it harder at the end and thereby justify making it easier at the beginning. Leave well enough alone; the cost is too high.

But the final answer is: *mishum aguna ikilu bah rabanan*. Ultimately, the rabbis were lenient with her out of concern for the *agunah*. Without this leniency, a woman whose husband disappears and is presumed dead would have no legal recourse and would remain chained for the rest of her life. It is important to note that this type of *igun* — where a husband has actually died or disappeared — is categorically different from the *agunah* crisis of the modern era, which involves husbands who are known to be alive yet refuse to deliver a *get*. The halakhic solutions that have been developed for the former have far less application to the latter.

## Husband Returns

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּצֵא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּיסֵּת בְּעֵד אֶחָד, אֲבָל נִיסֵּת עַל פִּי שְׁנֵי עֵדִים — לֹא תֵּצֵא. מַחֲכוּ עֲלֵיהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא: אֲתָא גַּבְרָא וְקָאֵי, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לֹא תֵּצֵא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּלָא יָדְעִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: They taught this halakha only if she married by virtue of the testimony of one witness, but if she married on the basis of the testimony of two witnesses, she does not have to leave him. They laughed at him in the West, Eretz Yisrael: The man, the first husband, has come and stands before us, and yet you say she does not have to leave her second husband. The Gemara explains: No, it is necessary in a situation when we do not know the man who comes before us claiming to be the first husband.**

The *Mishnah* continues: if the husband returns after she has remarried, *tetze mizeh umizeh* — she must leave both the second husband and the first. At this point, *Rav* makes a striking statement: *lo shanu ela she'niset be'ed echad, aval niset al pi shenei edim lo tetze.* Rav holds that the rule of *tzet mizeh umizeh* applies only when she remarried on the basis of a single witness. But if two witnesses came and testified that her husband was dead, and she married on that basis — she does not have to leave her second husband.

In *Eretz Yisrael*, they laughed at this: *machku aleha bema'arava.* The man is standing right in front of you, and you are telling her she may remain with the second husband?

### Gemara's Objection

The *Gemara* formulates the objection directly: *ata gavra veka'i* — the man has come and is standing before you. *Ve'at amart lo tetze?* And you are saying she does not need to leave?

### Identity Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּלָא יָדְעִינַן לֵיהּ, בְּעֵד אֶחָד אַמַּאי תֵּצֵא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲתוֹ בֵּי תְרֵי וְאָמְרִי: אֲנַן הֲוֵינַן בַּהֲדֵיהּ מִכִּי נְפַק וְעַד הַשְׁתָּא, וְאַתּוּן הוּא דְּלָא יָדְעִיתוּ לֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּכֵּר יוֹסֵף אֶת אֶחָיו וְהֵם לֹא הִכִּירוּהוּ״, וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיָּצָא בְּלֹא חֲתִימַת זָקָן, וּבָא בַּחֲתִימַת זָקָן. סוֹף סוֹף תְּרֵי וּתְרֵי נִינְהוּ, וְהַבָּא עָלֶיהָ בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי קָאֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **If we do not know him, even if she married by one witness, why should she leave? The testimony of the witness who says the husband is dead should be accepted. The Gemara answers: No, it is necessary for a case when two others came and said: We were with him from when he left until now, and it is you who do not recognize him, as his appearance has changed over the course of time. This is as it is written: "And Joseph recognized his brothers but they did not recognize him," and Rav Ḥisda said that this verse teaches that Joseph left his brothers without a full beard, and he came with a full beard, which is why they failed to recognize him.** **The Gemara asks: Even in this case, ultimately they are two against two. Initially, two witnesses testified that the man was dead, and now another pair arrives saying he is alive. Why should the testimony of the witnesses who say he is dead be accepted, allowing her to remain with the second husband, while other witnesses claim he is still alive? And as this is an uncertain case, one who has intercourse with her stands obligated to bring an uncertain guilt-offering.**

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzericha* — Rav's ruling is still necessary. The case is one of *d'la yad'ina leh* — we do not recognize this person. Someone is claiming to be the long-lost husband, but there is no positive identification. The *Gemara* presses: if we do not recognize him at all, why would she even need to leave her second husband in the case where she married on the basis of a single witness? It answers: the ruling is needed for the case where two witnesses come and say, *anan havainan bahadei mikai nafak v'ad hashta* — "We have been with him continuously from the time he left until this very moment; it is you who fail to recognize him."

The *Gemara* supports this with the verse describing Yosef's reunion with his brothers: *vayaker Yosef et echav ve-hem lo hikiruhu* — Yosef recognized his brothers but they did not recognize him. *Rav Hisda* explains: he left before the seal of a beard had formed on his face, and he returned with it. The broader point is clear: it is entirely plausible that a person could return after a long absence and not be recognized, whether because of physical changes, the passage of time, or the disappearance of those who originally knew him.

### Conflicting Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְאֶחָד מֵעֵדֶיהָ. הִיא גּוּפַהּ בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי קָיְימָא! בְּאוֹמֶרֶת ״בָּרִי לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: We are dealing with a case where that woman married one of her witnesses, who testified that her husband had died. Since the witness himself has no doubt as to the truth, he is not liable to bring an uncertain guilt-offering.** **The Gemara asks: Even so, she herself stands obligated to bring an uncertain guilt-offering, as she has no personal knowledge of the matter and relied on the witness. The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case where she says: It is clear to me.**

The *Gemara* then raises a further difficulty. Even granting all of this — even accepting that the two witnesses who accompany the returning husband are credible — we now have *trei v'trei ninhu*: two witnesses who said the husband was dead against two witnesses who say he is alive and present. This creates a *safek*, and anyone who now sleeps with this woman would be obligated in an *asham talui* — the conditional guilt offering for uncertain sin. How then can Rav say *lo tetze*, that she remains with the second husband, when every act of marital relations generates a *safek* requiring an *asham talui*?

### Rav Sheshet's Solution

*Rav Sheshet* offers a solution: *kegon she-nisset l'achad me-adeha* — the case is one where the woman married one of the original witnesses who testified to her husband's death. This is relevant because an *asham talui* is not an offering that comes from external legal pressure; it reflects a person's own internal doubt about whether they have sinned. If she married the very witness who established the fact of the husband's death, that witness himself has no doubt whatsoever — he was there. He does not feel any uncertainty, and therefore an *asham talui* simply does not apply to him.

The *Gemara* pushes further: even if the man does not have a doubt, *hi gufa be'asham talui* — what about her? Does she not still feel uncertain? The answer is: *be'omeret bari li* — she herself says, "I am certain that he is dead." Rashi explains that the witnesses produced convincing signs — an item that only the husband would have had, for example — that left her with no personal doubt.

### Temporal Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיסֵּת, אֲבָל נִיסֵּת וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים — לָא אָמַר. דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״מֵת״ וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לָא מֵת״, שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״ וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא נִתְגָּרְשָׁה״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא, וְאִם נִיסֵּת — לֹא תֵּצֵא. רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר: תֵּצֵא. אָמַר רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵימַת אֲנִי אוֹמֵר תֵּצֵא? בִּזְמַן שֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיסֵּת. אֲבָל נִיסֵּת וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תֵּצֵא!
>
> **Translation:** **If so, what is the purpose of stating this? That is, if Rav is referring only to this particular case, he has not taught anything new, as even Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, stated his opinion only with regard to a case where witnesses came and afterward she married, but in the case where she married and afterward witnesses came, he did not state his halakha with regard to this case.** **To what ruling is the Gemara referring? As it is taught in a *baraita*: If two witnesses say that the husband is dead and two say he is not dead, or if two say that this woman was divorced and two say she was not divorced, this woman may not marry; and if she married regardless, she need not leave her new husband, as there is no uncontroverted testimony that she is forbidden to him. Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: She must leave him. Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, further said: When do I say she must leave him? When the witnesses who contradicted the first pair by claiming she is still married came and afterward she married, despite their testimony. However, if she married and afterward the second pair of witnesses came, this woman need not leave her second husband.**

The *Gemara* then raises the question of *novelty*: if the case requires both that she married one of her witnesses and that she herself is certain the husband is dead, what is the *chiddush* of Rav's ruling? Even *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* — who takes the strictest position — confines his ruling to a specific scenario. *Afilu Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi lo ka'amar ela sheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset* — even he only rules strictly when both sets of witnesses appeared *before* she married. But if she married first and only then did contradicting witnesses emerge, *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* would also not require her to leave.

### Baraita Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רַב נָמֵי, בִּזְמַן שֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיסֵּת. לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמַר: טַעְמָא דְּנִיסֵּת וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים, אֲבָל בָּאוּ עֵדִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיסֵּת — תֵּצֵא. כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav spoke too, he was referring to a case when the second pair of witnesses came and testified that this is her husband, and afterward she married. Rav claims that even in that case she need not leave her second husband. His ruling serves to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, in favor of that of the first *tanna*. And some say that Rav's teaching should be understood as follows: The reason is that she married and afterward witnesses came; however, if witnesses came and afterward she married, she must leave him. According to whose opinion is this ruling of Rav? It is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei.**

*Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* clarifies his own position: *eimatai ani omer tetzei? Bizman sheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset.* He requires her to leave only when both sets of contradicting witnesses appeared *before* she married. If she knew going in that the situation was a *safek*, and she married anyway, she cannot now claim the protection of *lo tetze*. But if she married first and only afterward did contradicting witnesses arrive, even he would agree she need not leave.

Rav's ruling is therefore not merely a restatement of what all *Tannaim* agree on. There are two ways to read Rav. According to the first reading (*yesh omrim*), Rav's case is one where she married *after* the contradicting witnesses appeared, and he rules *lo tetze* — unlike *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi*. On this reading, Rav is announcing that the *halacha* does not follow *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi*. According to the second reading, Rav's ruling applies to the case where she married *before* the contradicting witnesses appeared, meaning Rav actually holds *like* Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi — his *chiddush* being simply that the *halacha* follows that position.

### Kohen Case Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא רָצָה — דׇּפְנוֹ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתּוֹ״, בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ. הֵיכִי דָמֵי, אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא נִיסֵּת לְאֶחָד מֵעֵדֶיהָ וְלֹא קָאָמְרָה ״בָּרִי לִי״, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּדׇּפְנוֹ? אֶלָּא לָאו, דְּנִיסֵּת לְאֶחָד מֵעֵדֶיהָ, וְקָאָמְרָה ״בָּרִי לִי״, וְקָתָנֵי דׇּפְנוֹ. אַלְמָא, מַפְּקִינַן לַהּ מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection from a *baraita*: From where is it derived that if a priest does not want to observe the strictures against disqualified women, that the court forces him [*dafno*] by flogging him, and it sanctifies him despite his wishes? The verse states, at the end of the chapter that deals with the prohibitions of the priesthood: "And you shall sanctify him…he shall be sacred for you," which indicates that this is performed even against his will.** **Rava analyzes this *baraita*: What are the circumstances? Assuming that this is referring to a priest who married a woman whose status as a divorced woman or a *zona* is uncertain, if we say that she did not marry one of her witnesses and did not say: It is clear to me, that the opposing witnesses were speaking the truth, need this be said that the court forces him? Since both of them are clearly in violation of a transgression, it is obvious that they must be separated. Rather, is it not referring to a case when she married one of her witnesses and she says: It is clear to me, and even so the *baraita* is teaching that the court forces him? Apparently, the halakha is that they remove her from him, which contradicts Rav's opinion that one who married based on the testimony of two witnesses need not leave her second husband.**

*Rava* raises an objection to Rav from a different *baraita*. The case is a *kohen* who has married a woman forbidden to him — a *gerushah*, for example. The *baraita* asks: from where do we know that if he refuses to divorce her, we compel him? The verse says *ve-kidashto* — "you shall sanctify him": he is made holy whether he wishes it or not. Now, *heichi dami* — what is the scenario of this *baraita*? If it is the simple case without the complications of contradicting witnesses and personal certainty, the *kohen*'s obligation to divorce is obvious; no *baraita* is needed. It must therefore be referring to the more complex case — one where she did marry one of her witnesses and she herself declared certainty about her husband's death. And yet the *baraita* says we compel him. This seems to be a direct challenge to Rav, who said *lo tetze*.

### Kohen Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר כְּהוּנָּה שָׁאנֵי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי דׇּפְנוֹ — דׇּפְנוֹ בְּעֵדִים. וְאִבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כְּשֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיסֵּת, וְרַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **A prohibition with regard to the priesthood is different, as uncertainties with regard to priests are treated stringently, as though they were definite. And if you wish, say: What is the meaning of the phrase: The court forces him? It means that it forces him by witnesses. In other words, only if another pair of witnesses comes and clarifies the matter does the court prevent the marriage *ab initio*. However, if she has already married she need not leave her husband. And if you wish, say instead that it is referring to a situation when witnesses came and afterward she married, and this *baraita*, which claims that the court forces him to divorce her in that case, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei.**

The *Gemara* offers several possible resolutions. First: *issur kehunah shani* — the *kohen* case is categorically different. A *kohen* faces additional prohibitions that do not apply to a regular *Yisrael*, and therefore even a doubt that would not require dissolution of an ordinary marriage is sufficient to require dissolution of a *kohen*'s marriage.

Second: *mai dafnu? Dafnu be'eidim.* What does it mean to "compel" him in the *baraita*? Rashi explains that this refers not to forcing him to give a *get* after the fact, but rather to preventing the marriage in the first place — by producing witnesses who will complicate the situation and demonstrate that she may in fact still be married, thereby deterring him from marrying her at all. But once the marriage has occurred, we would not necessarily dissolve it.

Third: *kasheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset ve-Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi hi* — perhaps this *baraita* represents the position of *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* specifically, in the scenario where the contradicting witnesses appeared before she married. Rav may simply hold differently.

## Rav Ashi & Shmuel

### Rav Ashi's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אוֹמֵר: מַאי ״לֹא תֵּצֵא״ דְּקָאָמַר רַב — לֹא תֵּצֵא מֵהֶיתֵּירָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן. הָא אַמְרַהּ רַב חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דִּתְנַן: נִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ. וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אֲמַר רַב: הָכִי הִלְכְתָא! חֲדָא מִכְּלָלָא דַּחֲבֶרְתַּהּ אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: What is the meaning of the phrase: She need not leave him, that Rav said? It means that she need not leave her state of being permitted to her first husband.** **The Gemara asks: If that is what he meant, Rav already said it once. As we learned in the mishna: If she married without the consent of the court, but rather by witnesses' testimony, it is permitted for her to return to him. And Rav Huna said that Rav said: That is the halakha.** **The Gemara answers: One ruling was stated by inference from the other.**

*Rav Ashi* now offers a fundamentally different reading of Rav's original ruling. *Mai lo tetze deka'amar Rav? Lo tetze meheterah harishon.* When Rav said *lo tetze*, he did not mean she stays with the second husband. He meant she does not lose her permitted status with respect to the *first* husband. In other words, when two witnesses testified that her husband was dead and she married on that basis, and the husband subsequently returns — she is not *asurah* to the first husband. She may go back to him. She is *asurah* to the second husband, whose marriage has been nullified, but her original marriage is very much intact.

### Mishna's Case

The *Gemara* then asks: but Rav already said this once — why does he need to say it a second time? *Detnan: niset shelo birshut muteret lachzor lo* — the *Mishnah* states that if she married *shelo bi-reshut* (understood here to mean on the basis of two witnesses, without needing the permission of *beit din*), she is permitted to return to the first husband. And *Rav Huna* reported that Rav ruled that the *halacha* follows this *Mishnah*. So Rav has already told us she can return to the first husband. Why restate it?

The answer is: *chada miklala de-chavreita itmar* — one ruling was derived from the other. Once we know the *halacha* follows the end of the *Mishnah* — that she can return to her first husband — it follows as a corollary that she does not forfeit her original *heter*.

### Shmuel's Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא מַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ, אֲבָל מַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ — לֹא תֵּצֵא. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּבֵי תְרֵי, כִּי מַכְחֲשָׁא לֵיהּ מַאי הָוֵי! אֶלָּא בְּחַד, טַעְמָא דְּמַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ, הָא שָׁתְקָה — תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: They taught that she must leave her second husband only if she does not contradict the witness who claims her first husband is alive. However, if she contradicts him, she need not leave her second husband.** **The Gemara inquires: With what are we dealing here? If we say we are dealing with two witnesses who testified that her husband is still alive, even if she contradicts him, what of it? The testimony of the two witnesses is fully accepted. Rather, it must be referring to one witness, from which it may be inferred that the reason that she need not leave her second husband is that she contradicts him, which indicates that if she remains silent and does not contradict his testimony, she must leave him.**

*Shmuel* now enters with his own formulation: *lo shanu ela shelo machchishto, aval machchishto lo tetze.* What we have been discussing applies only when the woman does *not* contradict the returning man. If the husband returns and she contradicts him — she says, "You are not my husband; I do not know you" — then she does not have to leave her second husband and may remain with him.

### One vs. One Witness

The *Gemara* asks: *bemai askinan* — what is the scenario? If two witnesses come and say the husband is alive, then her denial is irrelevant; two witnesses cannot be contradicted by one person's claim. It must therefore be that a *single* witness comes and says the husband is alive. So the *Gemara* concludes: *ta'ama demachishatei* — the reason she stays with the second husband is that she contradicted the single witness. *Ha shetaka teitzei* — but if she had been silent, she would have had to leave, even on the basis of this single witness.

### Ulla's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד, הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּפְסוּלֵי עֵדוּת, וְכִדְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת. וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Ulla say that wherever you find that the Torah relies on one witness, this is a full testimony equal to that of two witnesses, and the statement of one witness has no bearing in a place where it is contradicted by two witnesses? If so, there is no difference between one witness and two witnesses in this case.** **The Gemara answers: With what are we dealing here? With a case where the pair who said he was alive were disqualified from giving testimony, and this is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya.** **As it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Neḥemya says: Wherever you find that the Torah relies on one witness, follow the majority of opinions.** **And they established the testimony of two women against one man like the testimony of two men against one man.**

This prompts the *Gemara* to invoke *Ulla*'s principle: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad harei kan shnayim, ve-ein devarav shel echad bimkom shnayim.* Anywhere that the Torah has granted the reliability of a single witness, the Torah has not simply lowered the bar — it has elevated that witness to the status of two. So when the original single witness said the husband was dead and the woman was permitted to remarry on that basis, that witness now carries the weight of two. A single witness who subsequently says the husband is alive cannot override that, because one witness cannot stand against two.

One might ask: the *Gemara* earlier concluded that it was the *rabbis* who permitted reliance on a single witness, out of concern for the *agunah* — not the Torah itself. How then can *Ulla* invoke the language of *he'emina Torah*? The force of the objection is real, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it fully here. What seems to be operating is the understanding that even the rabbinic permission was grounded in a Torah-level framework, and that the leniency — once granted — confers the full status that the Torah accords to a single witness in contexts where it is permitted.

### Disqualified Witnesses

The *Gemara* then asks: what exactly is the case where someone comes after the fact and says the husband is alive? If it is a *kosher* single witness against the original *kosher* single witness who now has the status of two — the later witness can obviously not override. So the *Gemara* answers: *bipsulei edut* — the case is where two disqualified witnesses, such as two women or two relatives, come and say the husband is alive. *Ucherabbi Nechemiah* — and this is in accordance with the view of *Rabbi Nechemia*.

### Rabbi Nechemia's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָרְצַהּ לִדְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הָכִי: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת, וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת — כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד אֲבָל שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד — כְּפַלְגָא וּפַלְגָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And you must accordingly amend the statement of Rabbi Neḥemya so that it reads as follows: Rabbi Neḥemya says: Wherever you find that the Torah relies on one witness, follow the majority of opinions.** **And they established two women against one woman like two men against one man. However, in a case involving two women against one man who is a valid witness, this is like half against half, i.e., they are equal.**

*Rabbi Nechemia* holds: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad halach achar rov de'ot* — in all cases where the Torah accepts a single witness, one follows the majority of opinions. Since the original single witness has the status of two, and the two disqualified witnesses also count as two opinions, one follows the larger number. *Ve-asu shtei nashim ke-ish echad* — and by this principle, two women count like one man in terms of testimony.

But this rule applies symmetrically only when the original witness was also a woman. *Aval shtei nashim be-ish echad — ke-palga u-palga dami* — if two women testify against a single male witness (who has the Torah-granted status of two), the result is half against half — a standoff. On the alternative reading proposed by the *Gemara*, the case is that the original witness was a woman from the outset, and the principle of *Rabbi Nechemia* is applied accordingly: two women testifying against one woman is treated like two men against one man.
