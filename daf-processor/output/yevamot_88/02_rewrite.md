# Yevamot 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Single Witness Testimony

### Mishna Setup

The perek of *Ha-Ishah Rabbah* is one of the most perplexing and challenging *perakim* in all of Shas — not only analytically, but in terms of *rabbinic* justice. The central difficulty is understanding how a woman who appears to have done everything right ends up in such severe legal jeopardy: she must leave both husbands, and she loses her rights from each of them.

The *Talmud* begins by identifying the precise situation described in the *Mishnah*. A single witness came and told the woman that her husband was dead. She went to the *beit din*, received permission to remarry, and did so. Because the remarriage was predicated on the testimony of a single witness, all of these harsh consequences follow. Had two witnesses come and told her that her husband was dead, the picture would be entirely different — she could return to her first husband, the second marriage would be treated as a nullity, and so on. What the *Mishnah*'s structure implies, then, is that a single witness is in fact *believed* in such a case.

### Korban Chatas Source

This leads the *Gemara* to a further implication. It is established that if one witness comes and says *achalta chelev* — "you ate forbidden fat" — and the accused responds *lo achalti chelev*, he is *patur* from bringing a *korban*. But if he remains silent, he would be *chayav*. This means that on the basis of a single witness, one is obligated to bring a *korban chatas* — not merely an *asham talui*, the offering brought in cases of genuine doubt, but the *chatas* itself, which implies that the sin is treated as established. This in turn implies that the single witness has been believed *m'doraisa* — a striking conclusion.

The *Gemara* asks: *minalan* — from where do we derive that a single witness can be believed at the Torah level? The source is a *baraita* interpreting the verse *o hoda eilav chataso*: his sin must become known *to him*, and not through others informing him. The *baraita* raises the possibility that even if he fails to contradict someone who accuses him, he might still be *patur* — since another person informed him, perhaps that does not satisfy the requirement of *o hoda eilav*. The verse is therefore read broadly: *mikol makom*, from any situation, he can come to know of his sin.

### Ne'emanut vs. Shtika

The *Gemara* now asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is this case? If two witnesses came and the accused failed to contradict them, why would we need a verse at all? Two witnesses establish a fact; the *korban* obligation would be obvious. It must therefore be that the *baraita* is discussing a single witness — *chad* — and the inference is that if one fails to contradict a single witness, one is *chayav*. This would mean that the single witness is actually believed, *shema mina eid echad ne'eman*.

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *umimai demishum de'meiman*? Perhaps this has nothing to do with the reliability of the witness at all. Perhaps the reason for the *chiyuv* is *mishum de'kashtik* — because the accused was silent. And *shtika ke'hoda'ah damya* — silence functions as a form of admission. This is confirmed by the *seifa* of the *baraita*.

### Two Witnesses Problem

The end of the *baraita* states: if two witnesses say *achalta chelev* and the accused says *lo achalti*, he is *patur* — even against two witnesses. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees and holds him *chayav*. This is a striking passage. Our initial assumption was that two witnesses obviously should be believed — *lama li kra*, why would we need a verse to establish what is self-evident? But the *seifa* reveals that the *Rabbanan* do not convict even on the basis of two witnesses if the accused denies it. This suggests that the *Rabbanan* are not operating in the realm of *ne'emanut* — of witness reliability — at all.

### Shtika K'Hoda'ah

*Rabbi Meir* argues by way of a *kal vachomer*: if two witnesses can bring a person to the death penalty, surely they should be sufficient to obligate a *korban*, which is far less severe. The *Rabbanan* respond: the accused is not without recourse even against two witnesses, because he could always say *mezid hayiti* — I acted intentionally — in which case no *korban* would be due. This is a form of *migo*: since he has available to him a more extreme claim that would exempt him, we credit his denial as well. The deeper point is that a *korban chatas* depends not on whether a person sinned, but on whether they *know* they sinned. No external testimony can substitute for a person's own internal certainty about what occurred and what their intentions were.

### Rabbi Meir's Kal Vachomer

Returning to the opening of the *baraita*, the *Gemara* asks: *reisha mai ta'ama kemachayvei Rabbanan* — why do the *Rabbanan* hold that silence in the face of a single witness creates *chiyuv*? If it were because the single witness is *ne'eman*, then we would be back to a contradiction — here are two witnesses who are *ne'eman* in all circumstances, yet the *Rabbanan* exempt the accused even against their testimony. The answer must be that the *Rabbanan* are not working within the framework of *ne'emanut* at all. Rather, *ela lav mishum de'ishtik* — it is entirely because the accused was silent. *Shtika ke'hoda'ah damya*: silence is treated as an admission.

### Internal Knowledge Model

The *Gemara* therefore proposes to abandon the verse entirely and seek a *sevara* — a logical basis. It cites the known case of *safek chelev safek shuman*: a piece of meat that might be *chelev* or might be permitted fat, where a single witness comes and declares *bari li d'shuman hu* — I am certain it is permitted — and is believed. A brief aside is warranted here: it is striking that the *Talmud* reaches for the analogy of the piece of meat to describe the legal status of a wife. Feminist scholars of the *Talmud* have noted this kind of comparison, as the *din* of the woman in this case is treated as formally identical to the *din* of a cut of meat.

### Chelev Analogy

Can this analogy actually sustain the case of the woman? The *Gemara* objects: *hatam lo itchazek issura* — in the meat case, the prohibition was never established; we simply did not know what category the piece fell into. *Hacha itchazek issura d'eshet ish* — here we know for certain that this woman was married. Furthermore, *ein davar she-be'erva pachot mishnayim* — in cases that touch on sexual prohibitions, we require no fewer than two witnesses. These two objections are related: once we say *itchazek issura d'eshet ish*, we are in the domain of *arayot*, and therefore we cannot rely on a single witness.

The objection can be sharpened further. Our case does not resemble *safek chelev safek shuman* at all. It more closely resembles a case of *vadai chelev* — a piece of fat that is definitely forbidden — where a single witness comes and says it is actually permitted. In that case, *d'lo meheiman* — the single witness is not believed.

One might ask: is it not the case that even where *itchazek issura* applies, a single witness would be ineffective? Yes — and this is precisely the point. Even the case of *safek chelev* allows a single witness only because there is no established prohibition. The moment something is *vadai chelev* or *itchazek issura*, no number of witnesses can overturn the established fact through testimony alone. The only exception would be personal knowledge — if you yourself saw a piece of meat cut from the animal's kidney, no hundred witnesses can tell you otherwise, because it is your direct knowledge, not testimony at all, that establishes the *issur*.

This leads to a further observation from the *Gemara*: *hatam afilu bei mea lo meheimni* — in the case of *vadai chelev*, even a hundred witnesses cannot permit it. But *hacha keivan d'ki atu bei trei meheimni* — in the case of the woman, if two witnesses came and said her husband is dead, we would believe them. Therefore, *chad nami meheiman* — perhaps we should believe a single witness as well, just as we do in the analogous cases of *tevel*, *hekdesh*, and *konamot*.

### Tevel, Hekdesh, Konamot

The *Gemara* now examines each of these three parallel cases in detail to see if any of them can actually serve as the source or model for the woman's case.

Regarding *tevel* — grain from which tithes have not yet been separated — suppose we have a basket that may not have been tithed, and a single witness comes and says it has been properly separated. The *Gemara* asks: *ei dideih* — if the witness is the owner himself, then we understand why he is believed: *mishum d'beyado l'takno* — it is within his power to fix it at any moment, and since he could always tithe it, we treat his assertion that he already did so as credible. But the case that actually resembles ours must involve a witness speaking about *someone else's* property. In that case, the analysis depends on a *machloket*: can one tithe on behalf of another person using one's own grain, without the owner's consent? If so, then *mishum d'beyado l'takno* — this witness himself could effect the remedy at any moment, and so he has a degree of standing analogous to ownership. But if the owner's knowledge is required, then the witness is simply making an assertion about a fact he did not himself create — and we are back to the question of where we get the authority to believe him.

The same difficulty recurs with *hekdesh*. If something has been consecrated to the Temple and a witness says it has been redeemed, we can explain the case of *kedushat damim* — monetary consecration — by saying *mishum deyadei liftoto*: the owner can always effect a redemption. And even for *kedushat haguf* — where the animal itself has been consecrated for a *korban* — if it is the owner's own animal, *mishum deyadei leishalei alei*, he can always go to a *talmid chacham* and have the *hekdesh* annulled. But once again, the case that resembles ours is where a third party comes and says the owner went to a *talmid chacham* and cleared the animal of its consecrated status. And *hi gufa minlan* — from where do we derive that such a witness is believed?

The same pattern plays out with *konamot*. A *konam* can operate in two ways: it can render something forbidden to oneself, or it can render one's own property forbidden to others. In either case, if it is one's own *konam*, the owner can seek annulment at any time, which explains why a single witness who happens to be the owner is credited. But the moment we move to a witness testifying about someone else's *konam* — that the owner has already sought annulment — we once again reach *hi gufa minlan*. Each time the analysis is brought to the point that actually resembles our case, the underlying logic collapses.

It is notable that all of this reasoning has been *stama d'Talmuda* — anonymous throughout. Every one of these exchanges represents the back-and-forth of the *Gemara*'s own anonymous layer of analysis, and no solution has been found.

### Agunah Prevention

Now, for the first time, a named *amora* steps forward with a direct answer. *Amar Rabbi Zeira: mitoch chomer she'hichmarta aleha besofah hekaltah aleha betechilah.* Because the consequences at the end are so severe — because she must leave both husbands, forfeit her *ketubah*, and face all the attendant penalties — the rabbis did not need to make life so difficult for her at the beginning. Knowing full well that she stands to lose everything if she acts precipitously, she can be trusted to wait until she is genuinely certain that her husband is dead before proceeding to remarry. Therefore the rabbis permitted reliance on a single witness.

The *Gemara* responds tartly: *lo tachmir velo takal* — do not make it harder at the end and thereby justify making it easier at the beginning. Leave well enough alone; the cost is too high.

But the final answer is: *mishum aguna ikilu bah rabanan*. Ultimately, the rabbis were lenient with her out of concern for the *agunah*. Without this leniency, a woman whose husband disappears and is presumed dead would have no legal recourse and would remain chained for the rest of her life. It is important to note that this type of *igun* — where a husband has actually died or disappeared — is categorically different from the *agunah* crisis of the modern era, which involves husbands who are known to be alive yet refuse to deliver a *get*. The halakhic solutions that have been developed for the former have far less application to the latter.

## Husband Returns

### Rav's Position

The *Mishnah* continues: if the husband returns after she has remarried, *tetze mizeh umizeh* — she must leave both the second husband and the first. At this point, *Rav* makes a striking statement: *lo shanu ela she'niset be'ed echad, aval niset al pi shenei edim lo tetze.* Rav holds that the rule of *tzet mizeh umizeh* applies only when she remarried on the basis of a single witness. But if two witnesses came and testified that her husband was dead, and she married on that basis — she does not have to leave her second husband.

In *Eretz Yisrael*, they laughed at this: *machku aleha bema'arava.* The man is standing right in front of you, and you are telling her she may remain with the second husband?

### Gemara's Objection

The *Gemara* formulates the objection directly: *ata gavra veka'i* — the man has come and is standing before you. *Ve'at amart lo tetze?* And you are saying she does not need to leave?

### Identity Recognition

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzericha* — Rav's ruling is still necessary. The case is one of *d'la yad'ina leh* — we do not recognize this person. Someone is claiming to be the long-lost husband, but there is no positive identification. The *Gemara* presses: if we do not recognize him at all, why would she even need to leave her second husband in the case where she married on the basis of a single witness? It answers: the ruling is needed for the case where two witnesses come and say, *anan havainan bahadei mikai nafak v'ad hashta* — "We have been with him continuously from the time he left until this very moment; it is you who fail to recognize him."

The *Gemara* supports this with the verse describing Yosef's reunion with his brothers: *vayaker Yosef et echav ve-hem lo hikiruhu* — Yosef recognized his brothers but they did not recognize him. *Rav Hisda* explains: he left before the seal of a beard had formed on his face, and he returned with it. The broader point is clear: it is entirely plausible that a person could return after a long absence and not be recognized, whether because of physical changes, the passage of time, or the disappearance of those who originally knew him.

### Conflicting Testimony

The *Gemara* then raises a further difficulty. Even granting all of this — even accepting that the two witnesses who accompany the returning husband are credible — we now have *trei v'trei ninhu*: two witnesses who said the husband was dead against two witnesses who say he is alive and present. This creates a *safek*, and anyone who now sleeps with this woman would be obligated in an *asham talui* — the conditional guilt offering for uncertain sin. How then can Rav say *lo tetze*, that she remains with the second husband, when every act of marital relations generates a *safek* requiring an *asham talui*?

### Rav Sheshet's Solution

*Rav Sheshet* offers a solution: *kegon she-nisset l'achad me-adeha* — the case is one where the woman married one of the original witnesses who testified to her husband's death. This is relevant because an *asham talui* is not an offering that comes from external legal pressure; it reflects a person's own internal doubt about whether they have sinned. If she married the very witness who established the fact of the husband's death, that witness himself has no doubt whatsoever — he was there. He does not feel any uncertainty, and therefore an *asham talui* simply does not apply to him.

The *Gemara* pushes further: even if the man does not have a doubt, *hi gufa be'asham talui* — what about her? Does she not still feel uncertain? The answer is: *be'omeret bari li* — she herself says, "I am certain that he is dead." Rashi explains that the witnesses produced convincing signs — an item that only the husband would have had, for example — that left her with no personal doubt.

### Temporal Sequence

The *Gemara* then raises the question of *novelty*: if the case requires both that she married one of her witnesses and that she herself is certain the husband is dead, what is the *chiddush* of Rav's ruling? Even *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* — who takes the strictest position — confines his ruling to a specific scenario. *Afilu Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi lo ka'amar ela sheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset* — even he only rules strictly when both sets of witnesses appeared *before* she married. But if she married first and only then did contradicting witnesses emerge, *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* would also not require her to leave.

### Baraita Dispute

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* to clarify this. Two witnesses say the husband is dead; two witnesses say he is not. Two witnesses say she was divorced; two witnesses say she was not. *Harei zo lo tinasei* — she should not marry, since she is a *safek*. *Ve'im niset, lo tetze* — but if she did marry, she need not leave, say the *Rabbanan*. *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi omer tetzei* — Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi says she must leave.

*Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* clarifies his own position: *eimatai ani omer tetzei? Bizman sheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset.* He requires her to leave only when both sets of contradicting witnesses appeared *before* she married. If she knew going in that the situation was a *safek*, and she married anyway, she cannot now claim the protection of *lo tetze*. But if she married first and only afterward did contradicting witnesses arrive, even he would agree she need not leave.

Rav's ruling is therefore not merely a restatement of what all *Tannaim* agree on. There are two ways to read Rav. According to the first reading (*yesh omrim*), Rav's case is one where she married *after* the contradicting witnesses appeared, and he rules *lo tetze* — unlike *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi*. On this reading, Rav is announcing that the *halacha* does not follow *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi*. According to the second reading, Rav's ruling applies to the case where she married *before* the contradicting witnesses appeared, meaning Rav actually holds *like* Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi — his *chiddush* being simply that the *halacha* follows that position.

### Kohen Case Challenge

*Rava* raises an objection to Rav from a different *baraita*. The case is a *kohen* who has married a woman forbidden to him — a *gerushah*, for example. The *baraita* asks: from where do we know that if he refuses to divorce her, we compel him? The verse says *ve-kidashto* — "you shall sanctify him": he is made holy whether he wishes it or not. Now, *heichi dami* — what is the scenario of this *baraita*? If it is the simple case without the complications of contradicting witnesses and personal certainty, the *kohen*'s obligation to divorce is obvious; no *baraita* is needed. It must therefore be referring to the more complex case — one where she did marry one of her witnesses and she herself declared certainty about her husband's death. And yet the *baraita* says we compel him. This seems to be a direct challenge to Rav, who said *lo tetze*.

### Kohen Exception

The *Gemara* offers several possible resolutions. First: *issur kehunah shani* — the *kohen* case is categorically different. A *kohen* faces additional prohibitions that do not apply to a regular *Yisrael*, and therefore even a doubt that would not require dissolution of an ordinary marriage is sufficient to require dissolution of a *kohen*'s marriage.

Second: *mai dafnu? Dafnu be-eidim.* What does it mean to "compel" him in the *baraita*? Rashi explains that this refers not to forcing him to give a *get* after the fact, but rather to preventing the marriage in the first place — by producing witnesses who will complicate the situation and demonstrate that she may in fact still be married, thereby deterring him from marrying her at all. But once the marriage has occurred, we would not necessarily dissolve it.

Third: *kasheba'u eidim ve'achar kach niset ve-Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi hi* — perhaps this *baraita* represents the position of *Rabbi Menachem Berabbi Yosi* specifically, in the scenario where the contradicting witnesses appeared before she married. Rav may simply hold differently.

## Rav Ashi & Shmuel

### Rav Ashi's Reinterpretat…

*Rav Ashi* now offers a fundamentally different reading of Rav's original ruling. *Mai lo tetze deka'amar Rav? Lo tetze meheterah harishon.* When Rav said *lo tetze*, he did not mean she stays with the second husband. He meant she does not lose her permitted status with respect to the *first* husband. In other words, when two witnesses testified that her husband was dead and she married on that basis, and the husband subsequently returns — she is not *asurah* to the first husband. She may go back to him. She is *asurah* to the second husband, whose marriage has been nullified, but her original marriage is very much intact.

### Mishna's Case

The *Gemara* then asks: but Rav already said this once — why does he need to say it a second time? *Detnan: niset shelo birshut muteret lachzor lo* — the *Mishnah* states that if she married *shelo bi-reshut* (understood here to mean on the basis of two witnesses, without needing the permission of *beit din*), she is permitted to return to the first husband. And *Rav Huna* reported that Rav ruled that the *halacha* follows this *Mishnah*. So Rav has already told us she can return to the first husband. Why restate it?

The answer is: *chada miklala de-chavreita itmar* — one ruling was derived from the other. Once we know the *halacha* follows the end of the *Mishnah* — that she can return to her first husband — it follows as a corollary that she does not forfeit her original *heter*.

### Shmuel's Condition

*Shmuel* now enters with his own formulation: *lo shanu ela shelo machchishto, aval machchishto lo tetze.* What we have been discussing applies only when the woman does *not* contradict the returning man. If the husband returns and she contradicts him — she says, "You are not my husband; I do not know you" — then she does not have to leave her second husband and may remain with him.

### One vs. One Witness

The *Gemara* asks: *bemai askinan* — what is the scenario? If two witnesses come and say the husband is alive, then her denial is irrelevant; two witnesses cannot be contradicted by one person's claim. It must therefore be that a *single* witness comes and says the husband is alive. So the *Gemara* concludes: *ta'ama demachishatei* — the reason she stays with the second husband is that she contradicted the single witness. *Ha shetaka teitzei* — but if she had been silent, she would have had to leave, even on the basis of this single witness.

### Ulla's Principle

This prompts the *Gemara* to invoke *Ulla*'s principle: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad harei kan shnayim, ve-ein devarav shel echad bimkom shnayim.* Anywhere that the Torah has granted the reliability of a single witness, the Torah has not simply lowered the bar — it has elevated that witness to the status of two. So when the original single witness said the husband was dead and the woman was permitted to remarry on that basis, that witness now carries the weight of two. A single witness who subsequently says the husband is alive cannot override that, because one witness cannot stand against two.

One might ask: the *Gemara* earlier concluded that it was the *rabbis* who permitted reliance on a single witness, out of concern for the *agunah* — not the Torah itself. How then can *Ulla* invoke the language of *he'emina Torah*? The force of the objection is real, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it fully here. What seems to be operating is the understanding that even the rabbinic permission was grounded in a Torah-level framework, and that the leniency — once granted — confers the full status that the Torah accords to a single witness in contexts where it is permitted.

### Disqualified Witnesses

The *Gemara* then asks: what exactly is the case where someone comes after the fact and says the husband is alive? If it is a *kosher* single witness against the original *kosher* single witness who now has the status of two — the later witness can obviously not override. So the *Gemara* answers: *bipsulei edut* — the case is where two disqualified witnesses, such as two women or two relatives, come and say the husband is alive. *Ucherabbi Nechemiah* — and this is in accordance with the view of *Rabbi Nechemia*.

### Rabbi Nechemia's Rule

*Rabbi Nechemia* holds: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad halach achar rov de'ot* — in all cases where the Torah accepts a single witness, one follows the majority of opinions. Since the original single witness has the status of two, and the two disqualified witnesses also count as two opinions, one follows the larger number. *Ve-asu shtei nashim ke-ish echad* — and by this principle, two women count like one man in terms of testimony.

But this rule applies symmetrically only when the original witness was also a woman. *Aval shtei nashim be-ish echad — ke-palga u-palga dami* — if two women testify against a single male witness (who has the Torah-granted status of two), the result is half against half — a standoff. On the alternative reading proposed by the *Gemara*, the case is that the original witness was a woman from the outset, and the principle of *Rabbi Nechemia* is applied accordingly: two women testifying against one woman is treated like two men against one man.
