# Shekalim 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Friend's vs Own…

### Opening Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: הַנּוֹתֵן שִׁקְלוֹ לַחֲבֵירוֹ לִשְׁקוֹל עַל יָדוֹ וּשְׁקָלוֹ עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ אִם נִתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה מָעַל. הַשּׁוֹקֵל שִׁקְלוֹ מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ וְנִתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה וְקָרְבָה הַבְּהֵמָה מָעַל. מִמַּעֲשֵׁר שֵׁנִי וּמִדְּמֵי שְׁבִיעִית יֹאכַל כְּנֶגְדָּן:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: If one gives his *sheqel* to another person to deliver it for him but that one uses it for himself, if the sums were disbursed he committed *me`ilah*29Sacrilegious larceny. As explained in Note 3, the money used for public sacrifices, disbursed three times a year (Mishnah 3:1) is computed taking into account monies collected but not yet delivered. These therefore are Temple money wherever they be at the moment (Note 22). Dishonest use of these monies therefore is not stealing from a private person but *me`ilah* from Heaven's property.. If somebody pays his *sheqel* from Temple money, if the sums were disbursed and the animal sacrificed he committed *me`ilah*30Temple money in general is money intended for the upkeep of the Temple. *Sheqalim* are mainly used to buy sacrifices. Using one for the other is alienation from the original intent but not *me`ilah*, which is committed only if *sancta* are used for profane purposes. If the money which he took from Temple money is actually used to buy a sacrifice, he does not have to take from his profane money to pay the *sheqel*; using the corresponding profane money makes the act larceny.. From Second Tithe or Sabbatical money, he shall eat correspondingly31Second Tithe is produce to be eaten in Jerusalem in purity. The produce may be redeemed and the money taken to Jerusalem (*Deut*. 14:22–27). Similarly, Sabbatical produce has to be eaten, not used in industry. If such produce was sold, the proceeds are to be used to buy produce of the Land to be consumed in the Land. If such money was alienated from its original use, the situation can be repaired in replacing it with new money to be used for the original purpose.

We begin *daf* 6 with the *mishna* found on 5b. The *mishna* opens: *ha-nosei shekalo le-chavero ve-shaklo al yado* — one who carries his friend's *shekel* and gives it on his behalf. The phrase *al yado*, which we have encountered before, here means that you entrusted your *shekel* to a friend so that he could deliver it to wherever *shekalim* were being collected — at the local square or village — on your behalf.

### Me'ila from Friend's She…

The scenario continues: *ve-shaklo al yedei atzmo* — and he forgot, through an honest mistake, and deposited the *shekel* into the collection box for himself, intending it to discharge his own obligation rather than yours. This brings us immediately to the issue addressed on the previous *daf*: once a *shekel* is set aside, it has the status of *kodesh* — it is *hekdesh*. The *Gemara* there raised the question of how one could swear regarding *hekdesh*, since oaths do not apply to consecrated property. So when Reuven gave Shimon the *shekel*, Reuven had already sanctified it. When Shimon then deposited it toward his own obligation, he was taking a coin that was *hekdesh* belonging to Reuven and using it — not maliciously, not intentionally, but through honest error — to discharge his own financial duty. Here the *halacha* of *me'ila* (misuse of consecrated property) enters the picture.

The *mishna* rules: *im nitharma ha-trumah ma'al* — once the *terumah* is taken, *me'ila* applies. The *terumah* here refers to the moment when the *Beis HaMikdash* removes funds from the collection box and begins applying them. We learned previously that this act of *terumah* functions as the moment the *Beis HaMikdash* formally receives the money. For *me'ila*, the critical question is when was the money actually *used* — when was benefit accrued from it? The *mishna* teaches that this occurs at the point of *terumah*, when the funds are removed from the large collection box and put into active use. The *me'ila* does not take effect at the moment the misdirected *shekel* was placed in the box, but later, when the *Beis Din* draws the *terumah* from the collected *shekalim*.

### Own Hekdesh Funds

The *mishna* then turns to a second case: *ha-shokel shiklo min ha-hekdesh* — one who paid his *shekel* from *hekdesh* funds. Here the person did not use a friend's *shekel* but rather his own money that happened to be *hekdesh* — money he had consecrated to the *Beis HaMikdash*, or *ma'aser sheni* money, or *shmittah* money — and inadvertently used it to pay his *shekel* obligation.

### Me'ila Timing

Interestingly, for this second case the *mishna* does not focus on the *terumah* as the critical moment but instead on actual application of the funds: *ve-nitharma ha-trumah ve-karvah ha-behemah* — the *terumah* was taken and the animal was purchased and offered. Only at that point, *ma'al* — *me'ila* takes effect.

The *mishna* then raises the possibility that the funds in question were *mi-ma'aser sheni mi-dmei shvi'is* — from *ma'aser sheni* money or *shemittah* money. In that scenario, if one realizes soon enough, there is a remedy: *yokhal kenegdan* — one may eat corresponding food. As the *Gemara* will clarify, one can transfer the *kedushah*: wherever the *ma'aser sheni* money is, its sanctity is transferred to a different coin or food, and the corresponding food may then be eaten. The *shekel* obligation has effectively been discharged through ordinary funds once the *kedushah* is redirected.

One might ask why the same transfer mechanism is not available when one used *hekdesh* funds rather than *ma'aser sheni* funds. If one realizes before it is too late that one gave a *hekdesh* coin, why not simply declare that wherever the *hekdesh* money is, its *kedushah* should be transferred to another coin? The answer turns on the nature of the consecration: once the animal has been purchased and the funds have been concretely applied — reaching the level of *kedushas haguf*, intrinsic sanctity of an object designated as a *korban* — there is no redemption without a *mum* (blemish). But at the stage of money, which is one level removed from the actual *korban*, value-based *hekdesh* can be transferred. The *mishna*'s silence on this remedy for *hekdesh* funds therefore remains somewhat unclear, yet it is what we have.

### Terumah vs Korban Offeri…

A striking feature of the *mishna* as we have it is the apparent inconsistency between the two cases: the first hinges on *nitrama ha-trumah* as the critical moment, while the second hinges on *karvah ha-behemah*. This asymmetry will be the subject of the *Gemara*'s opening discussion.

The *Gemara* opens by noting the apparent tension directly. *Anan teninan* — we teach in our *mishna* that the critical moment is *im karva ha-behemah*, when the animal is brought. But *detani devei Rebbi* — the school of Rebbi teaches differently: *im nitharma ha-trumah*, when the *terumah* is taken.

Here we encounter the broader question of what *mishna* the *Yerushalmi* itself had before it. It is worth noting — though it has not been stated until now — that the *Yerushalmi* is a complete misnomer. It is more accurately called *Talmuda de-vnei ma'arava* or *Talmuda shel Eretz Yisrael*; it was not written in Yerushalayim but somewhere in the Land of Israel. The *girsa* questions surrounding this passage are substantial. As we have our text, the first case of the *mishna* (your friend's *shekel*) identifies the *terumah* as the critical moment, while the second case (your own *hekdesh* funds) identifies the offering of the animal. The *Yerushalmi*'s *mishna*, it appears, was fully consistent — both cases were defined by when the animal was brought — and the tradition from Rebbi's school identifying the *terumah* moment was recognized as a separate variant. The *Yerushalmi* accordingly saw no internal contradiction in its *mishna*.

The *Gemara* then asks: *man tana* — who is the author of the position that the critical moment is when the *terumah* is taken? The answer: *Rebbi Shimon hi* — it is Rabbi Shimon. For Rabbi Shimon holds that the *kohanim* are meticulous (*zerizim hem*), and that the Temple pays the animal suppliers immediately upon delivery rather than waiting until the animal is actually offered. According to this position, money is considered "used" — and thus subject to *me'ila* — as soon as the *terumah* is drawn and the funds change hands to the supplier. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that payment is deferred until the animal is actually brought as a *korban*, out of concern that a problem might arise with the animal in the interim; the *Beis HaMikdash* therefore prefers to wait. Accordingly, in their view, the money is not "used" until that later moment.

One might press: why would the moment of *nitrama ha-trumah* specifically be when the supplier is paid? After all, the *terumah* is removed from the large collection box and the *Beis HaMikdash* only needs a handful of animals at any given time — not hundreds all at once. The *Gemara* nonetheless operates with the assumption that everyone agrees *me'ila* takes effect when the money is actually transferred to the supplier, and the dispute concerns only when that transfer occurs: immediately upon *terumah* according to Rabbi Shimon, or only upon the offering of the animal according to the *chachamim*. It is also worth noting the distinction between the financial liability question we examined yesterday — where the key moment for whether the donor has discharged his obligation was *nitrama ha-terumah*, agreed upon by all — and the *me'ila* question here, which requires not merely delivery but actual *use* of the funds.

### Ganav u'Lesof Chavero

The *Gemara* then raises a sharp difficulty: *ganav u'lesof chavero* — suppose I stole an *olah* belonging to someone else and brought it to the *Beis HaMikdash* and it was slaughtered without explicit designation. Whose *korban* would it be? Clearly the original owner's, since *stama leshem ba'alim ha-rishonim mishtakacha* — an undesignated offering is presumed to be for the original owners. The *Gemara* applies this principle to the *shekel* case: if Reuven put Shimon's money into the collection box without any explicit designation, the money was in effect Shimon's, and when the Temple eventually used it, it was Shimon's money being used. Where then is the *me'ila*? What exactly did Reuven misuse? If the funds automatically revert to their original owner's account, Reuven derived no benefit from them and committed no misuse.

### Mesuyam and Rabban Gamli…

The *Gemara* resolves this with a key concept: *amar Rav Yehuda tifshot bi-mesuyam* — we are dealing with a case where the coin is *mesuyam*, designated and identifiable. If the coin was somehow marked or distinguishable — scratched with a distinctive mark, for example — then when the Temple used it, the very act of using that identifiable coin constituted using it *on behalf of Reuven*, not Shimon. It is through this designation that the misuse becomes concrete: that identifiable coin was treated as Reuven's when in fact it belonged to Shimon.

The *Gemara* then cites a practice of *Beis Rabban Gamliel*: *mishel Beis Rabban Gamliel she-haya mitkaven ve-dochef le-toch ha-kuppa* — the house of Rabban Gamliel would not deposit their coins into the *shofar* and wait for the standard *terumah* collection. Instead, they would wait until the moment of collection and press their coins directly into the *kuppa* (basket) as the funds were being drawn. The *shofar* was a container shaped like an inverted funnel, with a narrow slot preventing anyone from reaching in once the money was deposited; coins dropped in anonymously could lie there for months until collected. The *kuppa*, by contrast, was the basket into which the collectors poured the funds during the *terumah* process — a process comparable to emptying a vending machine.

By placing the coin directly into the collectors' hands as they gathered the funds, the Rabban Gamliel household ensured their coin was identifiably theirs at the moment of use. This serves a dual purpose in the *Gemara*'s discussion: it explains what makes a coin *mesuyam* (immediate personal designation at the moment of collection), and it will also address the *shiyuraim* question that follows.

### Shiyuraim Concern

The *Gemara* presses further: *dechash lomar shema li-shiyuraim hanofku* — perhaps the concern is that even when the *terumah* is taken or the animal is brought, my particular coin might have been left behind as *shiyuraim* (leftovers not included in that collection cycle). The collectors did not necessarily empty the *shofar* completely; some coins might remain. If my coin was among those left behind, then even once an animal was purchased with other funds, my coin has not been "used" at all — so where is the *me'ila*?

The *Gemara* offers two responses. The first: *ella ke-Rabbi Meir* — it could be following Rabbi Meir, who holds *mo'alin be-shiyuraim*: *me'ila* applies even to the leftover coins, as if the very act of taking the *terumah* extends to encompass everything in the collection, including what was not removed. This is admittedly a difficult position to fully explain, and we will encounter Rabbi Meir's reasoning later. The second response: *o hi be-mesuyam* — or, as before, we are dealing with a designated coin, specifically following the practice of *Beis Rabban Gamliel* who deposited coins directly into the *kuppa*. Since the coin goes straight into what is actively being used, it cannot be among the *shiyuraim* by definition.

It is notable that the two answers — the *mesuyam* point and the Rabban Gamliel practice — appear in the *Gemara* in a somewhat inverted order relative to the two questions they address. What makes the coin mine rather than my friend's? The designation. What ensures it is not among the leftovers? Placing it directly into the *kuppa*. The *Gemara* here has presented both answers in service of both questions, even if the fit is somewhat reversed.

### Practical Benefit of Me'…

Having established that *me'ila* can in principle apply to this scenario, the *Gemara* turns to a further question: *ma neheneh?* — what practical benefit did Reuven actually receive? He never put the money in his pocket. He delivered it to the *Beis HaMikdash*; he simply called it his own. If there is no tangible gain, what is the *me'ila*?

Rabbi Avin answers in the name of the Rabbis: *mikivan she-Beis Din re'uya le-meshkino ve-lo mashkino ke-mo she-neheneh* — because if Reuven had not paid, *Beis Din* would have seized his property. The practical benefit of escaping that seizure constitutes the *hana'ah* that triggers *me'ila*. This raises a natural question: if this is the operative benefit, why was the *mesuyam* answer even necessary? Even if the coin were not designated — even if, as far as heaven is concerned, it was Shimon's *shekel* that was used — as far as *Beis Din* is concerned, it was Reuven's *shekel*, and Reuven walks away without anyone coming to collect from him. That practical benefit exists regardless of the *mesuyam* designation. This is a genuine difficulty with how the *Gemara*'s answers interlock, and the question of what exactly constitutes the act of *geneiva* in this case remains pointed.

## Transfer of Sanctity

### Ma'aser Sheni Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אַךְ בְּכוֹר אֲשֶׁר יְבֻכַּר לַיי בִּבְהֵמָה לֹא יַקְדִּישׁ אִישׁ אוֹתוֹ. כָּל שֶׁהוּא קוֹדֶשׁ אֵין קְדושָּׁה חָלָה עָלָיו. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. מֵבִיא סֶלַע שֶׁלְחוּלִין וְאוֹמֵר. מָעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁינִי בְּכָל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֵן מְחוּלָלִין עַל סֶלַע זוֹ וְאוֹתָן נִתְפַּסִּין לְשֵׁם שֵׁינִי וְהַשְּׁאָר נַעֲשִׂין שְׁקָלִים.

Turning to the *mishna*'s ruling regarding *ma'aser sheni* money — that one may eat corresponding food — the *Gemara* explains the mechanism of transfer. The verse teaches *kol she-hu kodesh ein kedusha chala alav* — one cannot impose a second *kedushah* on something already sanctified. Therefore one cannot directly consecrate *ma'aser sheni* money for *machatzis ha-shekel*. But the solution the *mishna* provides is: bring a coin of *chulin* and declare — *ma'os ma'aser sheni be-chol makom she-hen harei hen mechulalin al sela zo* — wherever my *ma'aser sheni* money is, let its value be transferred onto this coin. That coin then takes on the *kedushah* of *ma'aser sheni*, and the original money is freed to serve as *shekalim*. The same applies to *shemittah* funds. One may similarly effect this transfer for both categories.

The *mishna* on 6a opens with a new case: *ha-machnis ma'os ve-omer harei elu le-shekali* — one who saves coins over the course of the year, placing them into a box and declaring "these are for my *shekel*," accumulating penny by penny toward the *machatzis ha-shekel* obligation.

### Beis Shammai: Motar Neda…

When it comes time to pay, the person counts his coins and finds he has more than a half *shekel*. What is the status of the excess? Beis Shammai rules: *mosran nedava* — the leftover is *nedavah*, consecrated property used by the *Beis HaMikdash* for voluntary *olos* offered when the altar is not otherwise occupied. This reflects Beis Shammai's general position that *hekdesh b'ta'us* — consecration made in error — nonetheless takes effect. When one declared "these are for my *shekel*," the extra coins were unknowingly sanctified, and even though it was inadvertent, the *kedushah* holds.

## Transfer of Sanctity…

### Hekdesh Cannot Transfer

Why does the *mishna* offer a transfer remedy for *ma'aser sheni* but not for *hekdesh* funds? The verse states *ach bechor asher yivakar ba-behema lo yakdish ish oso* — do not sanctify a *bechor* that is already sanctified. The principle derived is *kol she-hu kodesh ein kedusha chala alav*: a second *kedushah* cannot take effect on something already consecrated. Therefore one cannot consecrate *ma'aser sheni* money toward the *machatzis ha-shekel* by imposing a new *kedushah* directly upon it. The transfer remedy works for *ma'aser sheni* not by adding a new *kedushah* but by redirecting the existing one. *Hekdesh* funds, as general consecrated property, presumably pose similar structural issues — though exactly why the *mishna* omits the parallel remedy for *hekdesh* is not fully clarified here.

### Shea'avi Mehen

If, however, one said not "these are my *shekel*" but rather *she-avi me-hen shekali* — "I will draw from these funds for my *shekel*" — then both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree: *she-ha-motar chulin*, whatever remains is *chulin*. In this formulation one never declared all the coins sanctified; one merely indicated that from within this pool of money a *shekel*'s worth would be used. The excess never entered any category of *hekdesh* at all, and the question of *hekdesh b'ta'us* does not arise.

### Chatat Comparison

A parallel: if one declared "these coins are for my *chatat*" (*korban* of sin-offering), both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree that the leftover is *nedavah* — not *chulin*. Why does the *chatat* case not produce the same debate as the *shekel* case? Because the *chatat* has no fixed price. When one sets aside one hundred dollars for a *chatat* and finds an animal for ninety, it was never clear at the outset how much would be needed; therefore the entire sum was sanctified. The ten-dollar remainder was fully consecrated and must go toward *nedavah*. (If one said *she-avi me-hen chatasi* — "I will draw from these for my *chatat*" — then again everyone agrees the leftover is *chulin*.)

### Shekalim vs Chatat

Rabbi Shimon explicates the distinction directly: *ma bein shekalim le-chatat? ### Rabbi Shimon Replies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַף עַל פִּי כֵן יַד כּוּלָּם שָׁוָוה. אֲבָל חַטָּאת זֶה מֵבִיא בְּסֶלַע וְזֶה מֵבִיא בִּשְׁתַּיִם וְזֶה מֵבִיא בְּשָׁלוֹשׁ.

Rabbi Shimon responds to Rabbi Yehuda's objection: *af al pi chen yad kullan shava* — even so, everyone's contribution is equal. Yes, the absolute amount has varied across different historical periods; but at any given moment, a fixed amount applies uniformly to every Jew. This is fundamentally different from the *chatat*, where at any single point in time *ze mevi be-sela ve-ze mevi be-shtayim ve-ze mevi be-shalosh* — one person may bring a *chatat* worth a *sela*, another worth two, another worth three, depending on their means and circumstance. Rabbi Chiya offers proof from our *mishna* in support of Rav Bibi's position (that when one says *eilu* with a fistful, the leftover is *chulin*). The proof runs through Rabbi Shimon's statement: *ma bein shekalim le-chatat? Ella she-shekalim yesh lahem kitzva ul'chatat ein lahem kitzva* — in what case is Rabbi Shimon distinguishing *shekalim* from *chatat*? It cannot be the case where one said *she-avi me-hen*, "I will draw from these," because in that case everyone already agrees the leftover is *chulin* for both *shekalim* and *chatat*. Similarly, there would be nothing to distinguish if the case were the same formulation for both. The *Gemara* reasons: *man anan kayamin* — what case are we actually discussing? It must be the case where one said *eilu shekali* — the fistful scenario. And Rabbi Shimon says that in that case, since the *shekel* has a fixed amount, *motar chulin* — the leftover is *chulin*; whereas by *chatat*, which has no fixed amount, *motar nedava*. This proves that when one grabs a fistful and says *eilu*, the leftover is *chulin* — even Beis Shammai agrees — which is Rav Bibi's position.

The *Gemara* then notes that a later *mishna* states *mosar shekalim chulin* — leftover *shekel* money is *chulin* — and asks whether this too proves Rav Bibi's position. The answer is that this does not constitute a strong proof: one can read that *mishna* as dealing with the standard penny-collection case and as going according to Beis Hillel. It is not a *chiddush* that the *mishna* rules like Beis Hillel; the question of whether Beis Shammai would agree in the fistful-of-coins case remains separate.

## Hekdesh B'Ta'ut Cases

### Set Aside Shekel Wrongly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ שִׁקְלוֹ וְסָבוּר שֶׁהוּא חַיָיב וְנִמְצָא שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָיב. לֹא קָדַשׁ. הַמַּפְרִישׁ שְׁנַיִם וְסָבוּר שֶׁהוּא חַיָיב שְׁנַיִם וְנִמְצָא שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָיב אֶלָּא אֶחָד. אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁינִי מָה אַתְּ עֲבַד לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If somebody put aside his *sheqel* in the belief that he owed it and it turned out that he did not owe, it was not dedicated51The entire argument follows the House of Hillel who in Mishnah *Nazir*5:1 stated that dedication in error is void, against the opinion of the House of Shammai.. If he put aside two in the belief that he owed twice and it turned out that he owed only once, how do you treat the second?

The *Gemara* now turns to cases of *hekdesh b'ta'ut* — consecration made in error. The first case: *ha-maphrish shekalo ve-savar she-hu chayav ve-nimtza she-eino chayav* — one sets aside a coin as one's *shekel*, believing oneself obligated, and it turns out one was not. Perhaps one's wife had already given on one's behalf, or one had forgotten that one had already given. The ruling: *lo kadash* — it was not sanctified. This is the position of Beis Hillel: *hekdesh b'ta'us* does not take effect.

### Two Shekalim, One Owed

The second case: *ha-maphrish shenayim ve-savar she-hu chayav shenayim ve-nimtza she-eino chayav ela echad* — one set aside two *shekalim*, believing one owed for two years (last year's and this year's), and it turns out one had in fact paid last year's obligation. One is only *chayav* one. One declared "these two are for my *shekalim*." The first coin is clearly valid. What of the second? The *Gemara* asks: *ha-shaini mah avid lei?*

### Chatat Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [נִשְׁמְעִינָהּ מִן הָדָא. הִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ] סָבוּר שֶׁהוּא חַיָיב וְנִמְצָא שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָיב. לֹא קָדְשָׁה. הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתַּיִם וְסָבוּר שֶׁהוּא חַיָיב שְׁתַּיִם וְנִמְצָא שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אוֹתָהּ הַשְּׁנִיָיה מָה אַתְּ עֲבַד לָהּ. (בָּאוֹמֵר) [אֶלָּא רוֹעָה. הָכָא נַמֵּי אֵלּוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** As one who believed that he owed it and it turned out that he did not owe, or as one who said "these"52Following those who hold that dedicating "these coins" is a full dedication even if the value exceeds that of a *sheqel*?

The *Gemara* seeks to derive an answer from the parallel *chatat* case: *hifrish chataso ve-savar she-hu chayav ve-nimtza she-eino chayav* — one set aside a *chatat* animal and was not in fact obligated; *lo kidsha* — it was not sanctified, consistent with Beis Hillel. But: *hifrish shenayim ve-savar she-hu chayav shenayim ve-nimtza she-eino chayav ela echad* — one set aside two *chatat* animals and was only obligated for one. The second animal: *ella ro'eh* — it must graze (i.e., it retains a degree of sanctity and grazes until it develops a *mum*, at which point it can be redeemed). The *Gemara* applies the same reasoning to the *shekel* case: *hacha nami* — so here too the second *shekel* would have some sanctified status. The *Yerushalmi*'s language here is striking: rather than bringing a textual proof, it advances a *svara* (logical reasoning) by drawing on the *chatat* scenario. Some commentators emend the *girsa* to present an explicit text rather than a logical inference.

### Partial Effect Logic

What is the underlying logic that distinguishes setting aside one item in error from setting aside two where only one is valid? The *mefarshim* explain: if one sanctifies a single coin and the *hekdesh* is entirely mistaken, Beis Hillel says it does not take effect at all. But if one sanctifies two simultaneously and the *kedushah* validly applies to one of them, then even where one's intention regarding the second was mistaken, perhaps the partial effectiveness of the consecration causes it to take hold across the entire declared set. Since the *kedushah* is working for one, it works for both — even the erroneous portion.

The *Gemara* then connects this directly to the earlier fistful-of-coins debate. When one grabs a fistful and says *eilu shekali*, and the *kedushah* takes effect for the half-*shekel*'s worth contained within, does it also take effect for the excess on the grounds that a partial *hekdesh* has been activated? This is exactly the question the earlier dispute addresses. If the answer from the two-*shekalim* case proves that partial *hekdesh* extends to the whole, then even in the *eilu* scenario the leftover would be *nedavah*, not *chulin* — which cuts against Rav Bibi's position. The *Yerushalmi* thus weaves the two debates together: *heicha at omerta elu* — how could you have said before that when one declares *eilu* the leftover is *chulin*, given that partial consecration apparently extends to the whole?

## Collection Practices and…

### Three Yearly Collections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אַבִּין. מִיכָּן לְשָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין. מִיכָּן לְשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת. מִיכָּן לְשָׁלֹשׁ אַפְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abbin said, from here the three *se`ah*, from here the three boxes, from here the three collection times68Mishnah 3:1–2: Money was disbursed from the *sheqalim*3 times a year; each disbursement yielded three boxes of *sheqalim, three se`ah each.*.

From the verse in Nechemya — *shlishit ha-shekel ba-shanah* — Rav Acha derives that one is required to give one's *shekalim* three times per year. The initial large donation at the time of the return from exile — a *darkon* — can be understood as three half-*shekalim* spread across three collection seasons, totaling a *shekel* and a half. The verse's phrasing encodes a triple rhythm of giving. This connects to the broader *Beis HaMikdash* practice: three times annually the *terumah* was drawn from the *shofrot*, three baskets (*kuppot*) each holding three *se'in* were used, and three collection removals were made — a pervasive theme of three running through the entire *shekel* system.

### Appeal Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיכָּן שֶׁאֵין מַטְרִיחִין עַל הַצִּיבּוּר יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה פְעָמִים בַּשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** from here that one does not importune people more than three times a year.

From the same source the *Gemara* derives: *she-ein matrichin al ha-tzibur yoser mi-shalosh pe'amim ba-shanah* — one should not burden the community with more than three appeals per year. This principle speaks directly to communal fundraising practice. It is an open question how many contemporary synagogues observe this guidance.

### Theme of Three

Rabbi Avin notes that the number three is embedded throughout the *shekel* institution, all traceable back to this verse: three *se'in* per basket, three baskets, three times of *hafrashos* (removals) from the *shofrot*. The verse's reference to "a third" — or "three" — becomes the symbolic and structural backbone of the entire collection system, from the baskets used to the frequency of draws to the number of times the community is approached.

## Symbolic Meaning of Half…

### Noon Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב זֶה יִתְּנוּ כָּל הָעוֹבֵר עַל הַפְּקוּדִים וגו׳. רִבִּי יוּדָה וְרִבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. חַד אָמַר. לְפִי שֶׁחָטְאוּ בְמַחֲצִית הַיּוֹם יִתְנוּ מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל. וְחָרָנָה אָמַר. לְפִי שֶׁחָטְאוּ בְשֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת יִתְנוּ מַחֲצִית הַשֶּׁקֶל דַּעֲבַד שִׁיתָּא גְרַמָּסִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is written69*Ex.* 30:13, reading הַפְּקוּדִים "the census" as Mishnaic Hebrew הַפִּקּוּדִים "the Commandments".: *This they shall give, everybody who violated the Commandments*. One scruple is two *oboloi*, a third of a denar, approximately 1.2 metric grams, used here as name of a silver coin..

The *Gemara* closes with a series of *derashot* on the symbolic significance of the *machatzis ha-shekel*. Why a half? One *tanna* answers: *lefi she-chatu bi-machatzit ha-yom yitnu bi-machatzit ha-shekel* — because Israel sinned at midday, they give a half-*shekel* in atonement. The sin of the golden calf (*cheit ha'egel*) occurred at the sixth hour, midday, as derived from the verse *ki boshesh Moshe* — read as *ba shesh*, "the sixth hour came." The half-*shekel* mirrors the half-day at which the sin occurred. It should be noted that in the order of the Torah, *Ki Tisa* and the *machatzis ha-shekel* command appear before the account of *cheit ha'egel*; Rashi and others understand the passage as being out of chronological sequence, and all these *derashot* presuppose that the *machatzis ha-shekel* was instituted as a *tikkun* for the *cheit ha'egel*, even if textually it precedes it.

### Six Geraim

A second *derasha*: *lefi she-chatu be-shesh sha'os ba-yom yitnu machatzit ha-shekel de-avei sheisha garmisin* — because they sinned in the sixth hour, they give a *machatzis ha-shekel*, which equals six *garmisin*. This formulation emphasizes not the "half" aspect but the number six, corresponding to the six hours of daylight at which the sin occurred. One practical difference noted between these two approaches: since we know one may give *more* than a *machatzis ha-shekel* when circumstances require a larger denomination, the symbolic emphasis differs. According to the first *derasha*, the symbolic goal is always a "half" of whatever coin is current. According to the second, the goal is that whatever is given should equal six of some smaller unit.

### Ten Dibrot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֵּירִבִּי נְחֶמְיָה בְשֵׁם רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּיי. לְפִי שֶׁעִיבְּרוּ עַל עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּיבְּרוֹת יְהֵא נוֹתֵן כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד עֲשֶׂרֶת גֵּרָה.

> **Translation:** Rebbi Joshua the son of Rebbi Neḥemiah in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan ben Zakkai: Because they transgressed the Ten Commandments, each of them shall give ten *gerah*69*Ex.* 30:13, reading הַפְּקוּדִים "the census" as Mishnaic Hebrew הַפִּקּוּדִים "the Commandments"..

### Sale of Yosef Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי בֶּרֶכְיָה רִבִּי לֵוִי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. לְפִי שֶׁמָּכְרוּ בְכוֹרָהּ שֶׁל רָחֵל בְּעֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף יְהֵא כָל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד [פוֹדֶה אֶת] בְּנוֹ בְכוֹרוֹ בּעֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Berekhiah, Rebbi Levi in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: Because they sold Rachel's firstborn for twenty pieces of silver; each of them shall redeem his firstborn with twenty pieces of silver72*Num*. 3:47. The firstborn is redeemed by 5 *sheqalim*. Since it is held that the *sheqel* in the Torah is equal to a Roman tetradrachma, *Qiddušin*1:3 Note 339, 5 *sheqalim* are 20 *denarii*. The generic expression "pieces of silver" is taken to mean the most common silver coin, the denar. The homily is mentioned as introduction to the following one.

Rabbi Berachia and Rabbi Levi in the name of Rabbi Shimon Lakish offer a striking *derasha*: *lefi she-machru bechora shel Rachel be-esrim kesef* — because the brothers sold the firstborn son of Rachel (Yosef) for twenty *kesef*, everyone gives and redeems his firstborn with twenty *kesef*. The *pidyon ha-bechor* is five *shekalim*, and this *derasha* identifies the twenty *kesef* of Yosef's sale with twenty *dinarim* (since a *dinar* is a *kesef*), which at four *dinarim* to a *shekel* equals five *shekalim*. The brothers sold Yosef — Yaakov's (through Rachel's) firstborn — for five *shekalim*, and we pay five *shekalim* to redeem our firstborn sons.

### Ten Brothers' Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי פִּינְחָס בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי לֵוִי. לְפִי שֶׁמָּכְרוּ בְכוֹרָהּ שֶׁל רָחֵל בְּעֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף וְנָפַל לְכָל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מֵהֶן טִבַּע לְפִיכַךְ יְהֵא כָל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן שִׁקְלוֹ טִבַּע.
