# Ketubot 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon & Shtar Mav…

### Rabbi Shimon Overview

The daf opens by resuming the discussion of Rabbi Shimon's position, which had been introduced at the bottom of 78b. The husband is entitled to the *peirot* — the income and produce — from the wife's property that she brings into the marriage, and we had surveyed a series of debates concerning the extent of his right to block the sale of that property. Clearly, this right to block a sale could only apply once there had been *nissuin*, not merely *erusin*. Where property came to the wife after *nissuin*, the husband could intervene; where it arrived before *nissuin* and there is now *nissuin*, the matter was subject to debate. But in any event, the husband receives the *peirot*, and in some circumstances he could not even block the sale.

Rabbi Shimon introduces a distinction not only regarding the *peirot* themselves but also regarding when he may block a sale: he may do so only with respect to property he knew about, not property of which he was unaware — for instance, property located overseas. Rashi explains that the basis for this distinction is that the husband cannot claim he was relying upon or anticipating those particular properties. Consequently, at least with respect to property that predates the marriage, he is not entitled to block the sale of assets that were unknown to him.

### Woman Hides Property

With this framework in place, the *sugya* now turns to the practical question of what legal devices a woman might employ to protect her property, given that the husband has been granted these substantial rights. The Gemara presents a case story: *hahi ita de-ba'ya d'tavrichinah l'nichseh mi-gavrah* — a certain woman wanted to spirit away her property from her husband. Rashi supplies additional background: she was a widow who wished to remarry, and in anticipation of the marriage she transferred her property to her daughter — *kedei l'avruchey zechus ba'al meihem*, so that the husband-to-be would not acquire rights in it. This is the well-known phenomenon of transferring property into another's name to protect it from creditors, here applied to protect it from a future husband's *peirot* rights.

The transfer was executed before the marriage, since had it been done afterward the husband might have been able to block it. She then married, and subsequently was divorced. The problem that brings this to a court arose after the divorce: the daughter, having received a valid *shtar*, refused to return the property to her mother. During the marriage the mother could not demand the property back — she needed it to remain formally in her daughter's name — but once divorced she wanted it returned, and the daughter declined.

The woman came before Rav Nachman, who tore up the gift document — *kara Rav Nachman l'shetara*. The clear implication is that he regarded the entire transaction as a legal fiction, a mere ruse to shield the property from the husband. This is a striking ruling, because the daughter possessed a valid *shtar*, and Rav Nachman acted based on the circumstances alone. Rashi suggests that witnesses had testified explicitly that the gift was not genuine. Tosafot, however, finds this difficult: the Gemara is about to record a challenge to Rav Nachman's conduct, which would make little sense if there had been explicit witnesses. Tosafot concludes instead that Rav Nachman exercised a judgment call based entirely on contextual evidence, without any formal *moda'ah* — declaration of protest — having been delivered to witnesses. As Tosafot states: *v'nireh d'af al gav d'lo masrah moda'ah l'shum edim, d'mimaila hu nikar* — it was self-evident from the circumstances. *V'lo hayu devarim shebalev*, it was not a case of unexpressed inner intent that the law would ordinarily disregard, because the intent here was unmistakably manifest: *nikar hadavar shehu lo nitkavnah ela lehavriach* — it was evident that her only intention was to chase the property away.

This analysis is reinforced by the principle — attributed to Ramban and Tosafot ha-Rosh — that the rule of *devarim shebalev einam devarim* applies most forcefully in bilateral transactions, where the other party is paying consideration and cannot be expected to bear the risk of the transferor's secret reservations. In a unilateral gift, by contrast, we look more carefully to the state of mind of the one giving the gift.

### Shtar Mavrachat Concept

Rav Anan witnessed this and went to complain to Mar Ukva, saying: *chazi Mar Nachman Chakla'ah heichi makra shtarei d'inshei* — did you see how Rav Nachman the farmer tears up people's documents? Mar Ukva, rather than responding to the rhetorical dismissal of Rav Nachman, asked to hear the full facts of the case: *eini izi gufa d'uvda heichi hava* — tell me in truth, how did the whole matter actually unfold? Once Rav Anan recounted the story, Mar Ukva responded: *shtara mavrachat ka'amrat?* — are you telling me about a *shtar mavrachat*, a document to chase away property? There was already a recognized name for this device. And Mar Ukva then cited what Rav Chanilai said in the name of Shmuel: *moreh hora'ah ani* — I am a duly authorized decisor, with authority from the Reish Galuta — *yoreh yoreh* — and if such a *shtar mavrachat* ever came before me, *ekra'enu*, I would tear it up as well.

This establishes that the practice of tearing up a *shtar mavrachat* was not an idiosyncratic ruling by Rav Nachman but a recognized position of Shmuel, one of the foremost authorities of the Babylonian amoraim. The court must assess the circumstances, and where the context makes clear that the document was a legal fiction, it may be voided.

### Shmuel & Rava Debate

Rava then posed a question to Rav Nachman: *ta'ama mai* — what precisely is the reasoning behind tearing up the document? The presumed justification for Shmuel's ruling is the principle that *lo shavik inish nafshiah v'yahiv l'achrinei* — a person does not simply abandon their own interests and give their property to someone else. An unexplained *stam* transfer of one's entire property to a third party is highly suspicious and provides reasonable grounds to conclude that the gift was not sincere.

### Self-Interest Principle

Rava's question, however, is that this is not a transfer to a stranger but to the woman's own daughter. It is not uncommon for people to transfer property to their children even while alive and not on their deathbed. Does the presumption of self-interest still operate in such a case? The Gemara responds: *afilu hachi bimkom barta nafshai adifa* — even so, when it comes to one's daughter, one's own self-interest takes precedence. One does not simply sign away one's entire estate to one's children without reservation, even beloved children. *Chayecha v'chaye d'samchi chayecha kodmin* — even in matters of family, one's own life comes first.

The Gemara thus maintains the principle even in the case of a daughter, establishing that an unqualified wholesale transfer of all one's property to one's child is presumed to be insincere absent a credible contrary explanation. Naturally, this raises the question of whether the same presumption applies if only part of the property was transferred; that distinction, as we will see, becomes relevant shortly.

## Tosefta Remedies

### Fake Documents Debate

Having established the concept of the *shtar mavrachat* and Rav Nachman's authority to void it, the Gemara now presents a challenge from a *Tosefta* that appears to lay out legitimate mechanisms by which a woman may protect her property from her husband's rights — with apparent rabbinic sanction. The *Tosefta* states: *harotzah shetavriach nechasia miba'alah* — a woman who wishes to transfer her property away from her husband, *keitzad hi osah* — what may she do? *Kotevet shtar pasim l'acherim* — she writes a fictitious document to others. That is the position of Shimon ben Menasya. Rashi explains the term *pasim* as *piyusim* — a kind of enticement or persuasion document — essentially meaning a document whose purpose is to give the appearance of a genuine transfer while in fact being a legal fiction.

The *Tosefta* continues with the position of the Sages: *v'chachamim omrim ratza mesachek bah* — the Sages say that if she does this without more, the recipient may simply laugh in her face and walk away with the property. There is thus a tension: Rav Nachman voids a document that looks real but is secretly a *shtar mavrachat*, while the Sages here acknowledge that a *shtar mavrachat* can work — provided it is structured properly.

### Conditional Language

The Sages' requirement is explicit: *ad shetichtov lo mehayom u'lechisheerze* — she must write into the document a specific conditional formulation: the transfer is effective from today, but subject to my right to revoke it whenever I wish. This is precisely the kind of *tenai* that makes the protection viable — the recipient cannot simply walk away with the property because the right of revocation is built in from the outset.

This addresses the concern raised above: why did the mother in the original case not simply use such a conditional formulation? The answer is that with an explicit *tenai* of this kind, if the wife exercises her right to revoke upon divorce, the husband might argue that since the transfer was retroactively annulled, he is owed compensation for the *peirot* he would have received over the entire duration of the marriage. Rashi's comment is revealing in this regard: *bishtarei zeh mehayom yesumacha ucheshertzeh yesh kan hazvacha mefurash* — the explicit abandonment is contained within the document itself, so that when she declares *eini rotzeh* — I no longer want this — the question of what that does retroactively is not entirely settled. One could imagine a creditor-recipient colluding with the husband: the recipient assigns his rights to the husband under a power of attorney, the husband collects the fruits, and they divide the proceeds. These complications are real.

### Fake Transfer Problem

The Gemara now presses the logical difficulty: *ta'ama d'chasav lei hachi* — it is only because the conditional language was written in that the wife retains the property. *Ha lo chasav lei hachi kanyei lo-keach* — but if it was not written, the recipient acquires outright. This seems to contradict Rav Nachman, who simply tore up the *shtar* without requiring any explicit language. The Gemara's response, through Rava, is that there is no real contradiction: *lo kashya ha b'kula ha b'miktzata* — in the case of an entire property transfer, the circumstance is so inherently suspicious that explicit conditional language is unnecessary; the court can infer from context alone that it was a *shtar mavrachat*. This is the *anan sahadi* — we ourselves are witnesses — that *lo shavik inish* the principle that no one signs away all their property without reason. But where only part of the property is transferred, the inference is no longer so obvious and more explicit language is required.

Even so, the Gemara immediately raises a more fundamental problem: if the transfer was a legal fiction in any event — whether because it was the entire property and thus obviously fake, or because the explicit conditional language renders it revocable — then the wife does not truly own the property during the marriage. But *v'likniyah ba'al* — if it is not genuinely owned by the transferee, should the husband not then be entitled to it? How can the wife have it both ways: on the one hand, the document is not genuine, so she recovers the property after divorce; and on the other hand, the husband is excluded from it during the marriage?

### Unknown Property Doctrine

Abaye provides the resolution: *asu-un k'nichasim she-ein yeduyim l'baal v'aliba d'Rabbi Shimon* — they treated this as equivalent to property that is not known to the husband, in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's position. This is a striking application of Rabbi Shimon's principle. We had understood Rabbi Shimon's distinction between known and unknown property as referring primarily to the husband's right to block a sale. But here it emerges that Rabbi Shimon's position extends to the *peirot* themselves: if the husband does not know of the property, he is not entitled to its *peirot*. The existence of a *shtar* — even one that is technically fictitious — operates formally to make the property appear as though it is not hers, and by extension not known as her property to the husband. That formal appearance suffices, according to this analysis, to bring the property within the category of *nichsaim she-einam yeduyim* and to exclude the husband from its *peirot*.

This is a remarkable legal construction. The rabbis gave the husband sweeping rights to the *peirot* of his wife's property, and then simultaneously furnished her with a vehicle to shield her property from those very rights. The device is frankly a legal fiction, and the Gemara acknowledges this openly. And yet the rabbis validated it, apparently because they recognized that a woman is not always in a position of sufficient power to negotiate an explicit stipulation with her husband withdrawing his *peirot* rights — though such a stipulation was theoretically available in a later *perek*. The *shtar mavrachat*, properly structured, allows her to accomplish the same result unilaterally.

After the divorce she recovers the property in full, since the transfer was never binding. She is entitled to the income during that period as well. She need not have had explicit witnesses, nor formal *tenai* language in every case, to achieve this protection.

### Rabbeinu Tam Limits

Tosafot records that a comparable case came before Rabbeinu Tam — a case not of husband and wife but of a debtor who wished to protect property from a creditor's lien, or from his wife's *ketubah* lien, by transferring it fictitiously to a third party before incurring the debt or the marriage. Rabbeinu Tam ruled that in such cases the *shtar* was genuine and the transferee actually acquired the property.

His reasoning is crucial: *dedavka hacha dehavei mavrachet lo kani ba'al* — the *shtar mavrachat* works only in the wife-husband context, and only because there exists a legally recognized category — *nichsaim she-einam yeduyim* — within which a wife can own property that is formally inaccessible to her husband. The fictitious transfer is given effect because it mimics this established category: the property is on paper not hers, and the husband therefore has no legal basis to claim *peirot* from something that does not appear to be his wife's. The legal fiction operates within an existing legal framework that can accommodate it.

But *aval bish'ar oirvin* — in all other cases, no such category exists. A creditor's rights attach to all property genuinely owned by the debtor; there is no recognized exception for property that is secretly the debtor's but officially in another's name. Therefore, in those cases, either the transfer is genuine — in which case the transferee truly owns the property — or it is not, in which case it remains exposed to the creditor. There is no middle ground. As Rabbeinu Tam states: *i lo havei kani loke'ach havei kani lei leba'al chovo ule'isha* — if the transferee does not acquire, then the creditor or wife acquires. The *shtar mavrachat* device, which allows one to own something without a particular party having rights to it, is specific to the case of a wife and her husband under Rabbi Shimon's framework. Extend it beyond that relationship and the logic collapses.

Tosafot notes two positions on whether we actually rule like Rabbi Shimon: one view holds that we do not rule like him but invoke his logic specifically for this protective purpose — which makes the protection even more striking, since it rests on a principle we do not formally adopt. The other view holds that the *chachamim* do rule like Rabbi Shimon. Either way, the result is a powerful expression of rabbinic concern for protecting the wife's financial position even within a legal system that otherwise granted the husband extensive rights over her property.

## Property After Betrothal

### Mishnah Intro

The discussion now turns to a new *mishna*. The topic is property that comes to the wife and what happens to it once the husband is entitled to the *peirot*. Rather than dealing with real estate from the outset, the *mishna* considers what happens when the property takes forms other than fields, and what obligations flow from that. If she inherited money — *nafal lah kesafim* — and had a non-interest-bearing sum, *yilkach bahen karka v'hu ochel peirot*: they purchase land with it and he eats the fruits. The same rule applies to detached produce: if she inherited cut grain or other harvested goods, they are liquidated and the proceeds used to purchase land which then generates *peirot* for the husband. The point is that the husband is entitled to insist that the assets be converted into a form that is income-generating — specifically *karka* — so that the principal is preserved and the *peirot* flow to him continuously.

### Rabbi Meir Valuation

The *mishna* then addresses the case of *peirot hamichubarim lakarka* — produce still attached to the land. Here the question is more subtle. Suppose the wife inherited an orchard with fruit on the trees. Is the value of the fruit on the trees considered part of her *karka* — part of the principal she retains title to — or is it already considered *peirot* that will flow to the husband once harvested?

Rabbi Meir rules: *shamim otam kamah hi yafah im peirot ve-kamah hi yafah belo peirot* — one assesses how much the property is worth with the fruit on the trees and how much it is worth without them. The difference represents the value of the attached produce. That differential is not treated as *karka*; rather, *yilkach bahen karka v'hu ochel peirot* — it must be used to purchase additional land, and the husband eats the *peirot* from that new land.

To illustrate: suppose the orchard with fruit on the trees is worth a thousand. Once the fruit is picked and brought to market, it sells for fifteen hundred. The extra five hundred — the increase in value from picking and bringing to market — is *peirot* generated by the *karka* during the marriage, and it goes directly to the husband. But the original thousand that represented the value of the fruit on the trees at the time she inherited it — that is part of her principal. When the fruit is sold and that original value is recovered, she uses it to purchase more *karka*, retaining title, and that *karka* continues to generate *peirot* for the husband going forward.

### Fruit Distribution

The *mishna* then states a general rule about which fruits belong to whom when the wife enters and exits the marriage: *eilu hein hanichasim shenichnasim veyotzim imah* — these are the assets that enter and exit the marriage with her. *Peirot hamichubarim lakarka umotar* — the value of fruit attached to the land and the surplus from their sale — *yilkach bahen karka v'hu ochel peirot*: this is part of her principal, used to purchase land, with the husband eating the *peirot* from that land.

*Vahatlushin min hakarka shelo* — produce that was already detached from the land at the time she inherited it belongs to the husband. When that harvested grain or cut fruit is sold, the proceeds are his *peirot*. The *karka* itself — now bare of that detached produce — reverts to the category of principal she holds title to, and future produce from it will again be his *peirot*.

### Rabbi Shimon Entry/Exit

Rabbi Shimon offers a symmetric formulation: *makom sheyafeh kochah bichnisatah hura kochah biyitziasah* — where her position is stronger at entry into the marriage, it is weaker at exit; where it is weaker at entry, it is stronger at exit.

Concretely: *peirot hamichubarim lakarka bichnisatah shelo* — if when she enters the marriage the fruit is attached to the trees, that is his. The attached fruit is treated as part of the *karka*, and when picked during the marriage it is entirely his *peirot*. But *biyitziasah* — when they divorce and she leaves the marriage with fruit still on the trees — that attached fruit is hers. By the same logic that attached fruit is treated as *karka*, the fruit on the trees at the time of divorce is part of the *karka* she takes back; she is not required to pay him for it.

Conversely, *vahatlushin min hakarka bichnisatah shelah* — detached produce that she had when she entered the marriage is hers, not yet his *peirot*. But *uvitziasah shelo* — produce that was detached during the marriage and is in his possession when she leaves: that is his, because it became his *peirot* at the moment it was detached from the land.

## Investment Choices

### Field vs. Property

The Gemara now takes up the question of investment choices. Where the wife's property must be converted into income-generating assets, who decides what form that investment takes? The opening case: *ar'a u-vatei* — should the funds be invested in agricultural fields or in rental property? The Gemara rules: *ar'a* — agricultural land.

Rashi's reasoning is that rental property provides a more certain income stream and a more secure principal. Tosafot reads the case differently and more interestingly. According to Tosafot, fields maintain the value of the principal but generate comparatively modest income, while houses generate higher income but require ongoing investment for repairs and maintenance, causing the principal to erode over time. Naturally, the husband prefers the higher-income option — rental property — while the wife prefers the option that better protects her principal — the field. The *halacha* sides with the wife: she is entitled to insist on the investment that best protects the *keren*, even at the cost of lower income for the husband. This makes sense conceptually: the principal is ultimately hers and reverts to her upon divorce or widowhood; the husband's interest is in the *peirot*, not in the *keren* itself.

One might ask whether this principle is directional — that is, whether it always favors the wife's preference — or whether it specifically favors whichever party wants to protect the principal. If, for some reason, the husband preferred the more conservative investment and the wife preferred the riskier higher-income option, the calculus according to Tosafot's reading would be that her preference for principal protection governs. But what if it is the reverse? That remains an open question that the Gemara does not directly address.

### Asset Hierarchy

The Gemara then runs through an ascending hierarchy of investment alternatives. *Batei u-diklei* — houses or palm trees? *Batei* — houses, because they offer more secure principal protection relative to palm trees. *Diklei u-ilanei* — palm trees or other fruit trees? *Diklei* — palm trees, for the same reason: the principal in palm trees is more secure than in other varieties of fruit-bearing trees. *Ilanei degufni* — other trees or vines? *Ilanei* — the other trees.

The pattern throughout follows Tosafot's principle: at each decision point, the more conservative investment — the one that better preserves the principal — is favored, reflecting the wife's protected interest in the *keren*.

### Timber & Fish Rule

The Gemara then presents a harder case: *arza de-veita u-feira de-chifrei* — a stand of trees from which timber is cut, and a pond or river stocked with fish. Here the question is whether the timber and the fish are to be categorized as *peirot* — income generated by the underlying asset — or as *keren* — part of the principal itself.

Some say they are *peirot*: the husband chops down the trees and takes all the timber as his income. Others say they are *keren*: since the trees and fish do not regenerate automatically — unlike a field that reliably produces grain each season — selling the timber is actually depleting the principal, not enjoying its yield. Under this view, the proceeds of selling timber or fish belong to the wife as principal, to be reinvested in something that will generate *peirot* for the husband.

The Gemara formulates the general principle: *d'giz'o machlif peirot* — if the thing replenishes itself, then what it produces is *peirot*; *ein giz'o machlif keren* — if it does not replenish itself, then what it produces is *keren*. This rule extends to non-regenerating resources more broadly: whatever is extracted from a source that does not regenerate is treated as principal, not income.

## Offspring & Fruit of Fru…

### Animal vs. Slave Offspri…

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is cited — some attribute the tradition to Rabbi Ami, others to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba — with the following rule: one who steals the offspring of a *milog* animal — that is, an animal that a wife brought into the marriage — pays the double penalty (*tashlumei kefel*) to the wife. This implies that the wife owns the offspring. But this is puzzling: why should not the offspring of her animal be *peirot*, belonging to the husband?

The Gemara observes that this ruling follows neither the Rabbanan nor Chananyah. A *baraita* is cited: *vlad behemah milog l'baal* — the offspring of a wife's *milog* animal belongs to the husband, as *peirot*; *vlad shifchah milog l'ishah* — but the offspring of a wife's *milog* female slave belongs to the wife. Chananyah ben Akaviah disagrees and holds that the offspring of the slave is treated just like the offspring of the animal: it belongs to the husband as *peirot*.

### Fruit of Fruit Doctrine

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *afilu teima divrei hakol* — even according to all opinions, the *kefel* paid to the wife makes sense. The reason is the principle of *peirot tiktinu lei Rabbanan, peira de-peira lo tiktinu lei Rabbanan* — the rabbis established that the husband receives *peirot*, but they did not establish that he receives *peirot* of *peirot*. The *kefel* is a secondary benefit — an income generated by the lamb, which is itself already the husband's *peirot* — and therefore it falls into the category of *peira de-peira*, which was never granted to him. The husband owns the lamb, but the *kefel* for the lamb — a kind of income generated by what is already his *peirot* — belongs to the wife.

### Tosafot Irony

Tosafot points out a striking asymmetry. First, Tosafot clarifies that the principle of *peira de-peira* applies specifically to an anomalous benefit like *kefel*, which comes from outside rather than being a natural product. If the husband's lamb grows up and gives birth to more lambs, those subsequent generations are entirely his — *peira* of *peira* in a natural biological sense belongs to him indefinitely, once he owns the original *peirot*. It is only the artificial windfall of a theft penalty that is withheld as *peira de-peira*.

Tosafot then notes the irony: if someone steals the original ewe — the mother, which the wife owns as *keren* — the husband receives the *kefel*, because the *kefel* is *peirot* of the wife's principal, and the husband receives all *peirot*. But if someone steals the lamb — which the husband owns as his *peirot* — the husband does not receive the *kefel* for the theft of his own property; instead, the wife receives it, as *peira de-peira*. The result is genuinely bizarre: the husband collects the *kefel* when his wife's property is stolen, but not when his own *peirot* are stolen.

### Principal Protection Log…

The Gemara then returns to the debate about the offspring of the slave. The question is posed: if the Rabbanan are concerned about the death of the animals — that is, if they recognize that animals, unlike *karka*, can die and thereby erode the wife's principal — then they should also not give the husband the offspring of the animals. The logic is that the offspring should be retained by the wife and used to reinvest in the principal, compensating for the inevitable attrition of the flock over time. Just as the dividends of a depreciating asset might be reinvested to maintain its value, so too the lambs born to the flock should replenish the principal rather than flowing to the husband as pure *peirot*.

Conversely, if they are not concerned about the death of the animals and simply treat the entire flock as durable principal, then the husband should receive the offspring of the slave as well, since by the same logic she is durable principal producing *peirot*.

The Gemara answers: *ve-le-olam chayshi le-misa* — the Rabbanan are indeed concerned about death of the animals. The reason the wife still does not receive the offspring of the animals is distinct, as we shall see immediately below.

### Animal Exception

The Gemara explains: *ve-shani beheima de-ika oros* — animals are different because there are hides. Even if an animal dies, the wife retains something: she can sell the hide and recover a minimal residual value. This is enough to distinguish the case from the slave. Because the wife is not entirely exposed to total loss of principal when an animal dies — there is some minimal recovery from the hide — the rabbis did not consider the principal sufficiently at risk to justify withholding the offspring from the husband. In the case of the slave, no such residual exists: if the slave dies, the wife recovers nothing, and the principal is entirely lost. Consequently, the offspring of the slave is retained by the wife to protect and reinvest in her principal.

The daf ends with a final note from the Gemara: *amar Rav Zevid mishmiya de-Abimi halacha ke-Chananya* — Rav Zevid rules in the name of Abimi that the *halacha* follows Chananyah, who holds that the offspring of the slave is treated like the offspring of the animal and belongs to the husband. The discussion of the implications of this ruling will continue on the following daf.
