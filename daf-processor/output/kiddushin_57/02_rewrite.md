# Kiddushin 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shor Haniskal

### Mishna Overview

The *daf* before us — Kiddushin 57 — opens at the bottom of 56b, dealing with the *mishna* that lists items forbidden in benefit (*issur hana'ah*), and therefore if a man attempted to betroth a woman using any of them, she is not *mekudeshet*, since such items carry no legally transferable value. Among the cases discussed is the *shor haniskal* — the ox condemned to stoning because it killed a person — which remains forbidden in benefit even if it was subsequently *shechted*, rather than actually stoned.

### Mar Zutra Challenge

The *gemara* raises a challenge — *matkif lah* Mar Zutra: when the *Torah* says that even its flesh (*besoro*) is forbidden, perhaps that applies only where one performed something akin to stoning itself — for instance, where one sharpened a stone into a blade and used it to slaughter the animal, *damechzei kisekila*, it resembles stoning. But if one simply used an ordinary knife, perhaps that would render the animal permitted.

### Slaughter Methods

The *gemara* responds on two grounds. First, *mi saki b'oraisa kesiv* — the *Torah* nowhere specifies a knife, and therefore there is no halakhic basis for distinguishing between one method of *shechita* and another; if one type renders the animal permitted, so should any other. Second, there is an explicit *braisa*: one may *shecht* with any implement — *bein betzur bein bezechuchis bein bekurmis shel kano* — whether with a rock, a piece of glass, or a reed splinter. The methods are legally equivalent, and the challenge collapses.

## Et Particle Debate

### Dual Prohibitions

The *gemara* now observes that both the prohibition of eating (*issur achila*) and the prohibition of benefit (*issur hana'ah*) are derived from the same phrase — *lo ye'achel es besoro*, its flesh shall not be eaten. Given that, what further need is there for the phrase *u'va'al hashor naki*, "and the owner of the ox shall be clean" — which has also been used to teach that the owner derives no benefit?

### Skin Benefit

The answer is that *u'va'al hashor naki* is needed to establish that one may not derive benefit even from the hide. Without it, one might have reasoned from *lo ye'achel besoro* — the word *besaro*, its flesh, is what is written — that the flesh is forbidden but the skin is permitted, and one could therefore produce leather from it. The phrase *u'va'al hashor naki* closes that possibility.

But what of those *tannaim* who deduce something else from *u'va'al hashor naki* — for instance, that one does not pay half-damages (*chatzi kofer*) or compensate for miscarried fetuses (*demei vlados*) in the case of a goring ox — and thus do not use that phrase to teach the prohibition on the skin? From where do they derive that the skin too is forbidden in benefit?

The answer is: from the word *es* — *es besoro* — the particle *es* coming to include *hamatfel lebesoro*, something that is secondary or attached to the flesh, namely the skin. And the opposing position — the one that does derive the skin prohibition from *u'va'al hashor naki* — does not employ the *et* particle hermeneutically at all.

### Shimon Ha'amsuni

This introduces one of the most striking passages in the *gemara*. At the top of 57a, a famous *braisa* is cited: Shimon Ha'amsuni — and some say Nechemia Ha'amsuni — would systematically interpret every instance of the particle *es* in the *Torah* to indicate an inclusion, something additional being taught. This was his organizing principle across the entire *Torah*.

*Keivan shehigia le'es Hashem Elokecha tira* — until he reached the verse commanding fear of God — *perash*, he withdrew. He could not bring himself to say that anything could be compared to or included alongside fear of God. His students immediately raised the obvious problem: *kol esin shedarashta mah tehei aleihem* — if this particular *es* teaches nothing, does that not undermine every other instance you have interpreted? Is the hermeneutical principle now invalidated retroactively?

His response was remarkable: *keshem shekibalti sachar al hadrisha kach kibalti sachar al haprisha* — just as I received reward for the interpretations, so too I will receive reward for the retraction. There is a question of how far this concession actually goes. One reading is that he means: I still believe all those other interpretations are true; I simply cannot in good conscience force this one, and if my inability to answer here casts doubt in people's minds about my other teachings, so be it — my intellectual honesty demands no less. The other, more radical reading is that *sachar al haprisha* means he was willing to surrender all those teachings entirely, rather than compromise on a *drasha* he did not believe. That would make the gesture even more extraordinary — the full sacrifice of a lifetime's learning in service of *emes*.

### Rabbi Akiva Resolution

*Ad sheba Rabbi Akiva v'limed* — until Rabbi Akiva came and taught that *es Hashem Elokecha tira l'rabos talmidei chachamim*: the *es* particle in that verse comes to include *talmidei chachamim*, whose fear and awe bears at least an analogical relationship — obviously not equivalence — to the awe of God, inasmuch as they represent and transmit His *Torah*. One elegant reading of this episode is that it was precisely Shimon Ha'amsuni's act of *perisha*, his public withdrawal rather than offering a forced answer, that made possible Rabbi Akiva's insight: only a tradition that demonstrates such radical commitment to *emes* could produce *talmidei chachamim* genuinely worthy of being mentioned alongside the awe of God.

## Egla/Metzora Analogy

### Egla Arufah

The *gemara* proceeds to ask: from where do we know that the *egla arufa* — the heifer whose neck is broken as part of the unsolved murder ritual — is forbidden in benefit? The answer given in the name of the school of Rabbi Yannai is: *kapar kesiv bah k'kodshim* — the *Torah* uses the language of atonement (*kapar*) in connection with the *egla arufa*, and therefore it operates on the same principle as sacrifices, which are forbidden in benefit.

### Tzippurei Metzora

Regarding the *tzippurei metzora* — the birds brought as part of the purification process for a *metzora* — the source is derived from a *braisa* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The *braisa* formulates a parallel structure: inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the *Torah* mentions something that is *machshir* (changes the status of the *metzora*, namely the *asham* brought by the *metzora*) alongside something that is *mechaper* (provides atonement, meaning all other *korbanos*). The same pairing appears outside the *Beis Hamikdash*: something *machshir* (the birds of the *metzora*) alongside something *mechaper* (the *egla arufa*).

### Parallel Structure

The logical structure is a *gezeira shava* of principle: just as inside the *Beis Hamikdash* the *machshir* — the *metzora's asham* — is treated like the *mechaper* and is forbidden in benefit, so too outside the *Beis Hamikdash* the *machshir* — the *metzora's* birds — is treated like the *mechaper*, and since the *egla arufa* is forbidden in benefit, so too the birds are forbidden in benefit.

## Metzora Birds Timeline

### Yochanan/Lakish

An *amoraic* dispute now turns on the question of precisely *when* the *tzippurei metzora* become forbidden in benefit. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *mishe'as shechita* — from the moment of *shechita*. One bird is slaughtered and the other is dipped in its blood and water and released; the act of *shechita* is what triggers the prohibition. Reish Lakish holds: *mishe'as lekicha* — from the moment of purchase.

It is worth noting — as *Rashi* and most *Rishonim* understand — that the sent bird also acquires forbidden status at some point, but ultimately becomes permitted once sent free. According to Reish Lakish, both birds become forbidden from the moment of purchase; the released bird subsequently regains its permitted status upon release. According to Rabbi Yochanan, both become forbidden from the moment of *shechita*, and the released bird then regains permitted status. *Rashi*'s alternative reading — that according to Rabbi Yochanan only the slaughtered bird ever becomes forbidden while the sent bird was never forbidden — does not accord easily with the plain language of the *mishna*, which lists "the birds of the *metzora*" without distinction.

Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning is simply that *shechita hi d'asra lah* — it is the act of *shechita* that creates the forbidden status. Reish Lakish's reasoning is that we derive the prohibition of the *metzora* birds from *egla arufa*. The *egla arufa* is forbidden *michayim* — while still alive — just as sacrifices become forbidden while still alive. Therefore, the *metzora* birds are likewise forbidden while still alive, and since they are alive at the time of purchase, from that point the prohibition takes effect.

## Egla Timeline

### Yannai Memory

The *gemara* now turns to the *egla arufa* itself: when precisely does it become forbidden? Rabbi Yannai says: *gvul shamati bah v'shachachti* — I recall that there is a significant point in the process that triggers the prohibition, but I no longer remember which point.

### Wadi Descent

The colleagues — *chavraya* — propose: *yeridasah lenachal eisan hi osartah* — it is the descent into the *nachal eitan*, the wadi, that renders it forbidden. This is a concrete, explicit moment described in the *Torah*, making it the natural candidate for the triggering event.

### Consistency Test

The *gemara* then asks: if the *egla arufa* is not forbidden from the moment of purchase, should we apply that reasoning consistently to the *metzora* birds — and say that they too are not forbidden from the time of purchase?

### Two Moments

The *gemara* rejects this: *hachi hashta? hasam is lei gvul achrina hacha leis lei gvul achrina* — the situations are not parallel. The *egla arufa* has its own distinct moment — the descent to the wadi — which is explicitly referenced in the *Torah*. The *metzora* birds have no such equivalent moment. And as *Rashi* points out, while the *Torah* says nothing analogous to the descent of the wadi by the birds, it does say explicitly that the birds must be purchased (*tikach*), thereby establishing the moment of acquisition as the operative trigger. Each has its own moment, but both become forbidden while still alive.

## Metzora Birds Timeline…

### Braisa Proof

Rabbi Yochanan now challenges Reish Lakish from a *braisa*. The *braisa* expounds: *kol tzippor tehorah tocheilu lerabos es hameshulachas* — "every pure bird you may eat" includes the sent-out bird, teaching that it is permitted in benefit. And *vezeh asher lo socheilu meihem lerabos es hashechutah* — the extra formulation teaches that the slaughtered bird is forbidden in eating — and by extension, given the *mishna*, in benefit.

Now, Rabbi Yochanan presses: if according to Reish Lakish the bird was already forbidden from the time of purchase — *michayim asura* — why does the *braisa* derive the prohibition specifically from the moment of *shechita*? If it was already forbidden before *shechita*, why would the text focus on that moment?

Reish Lakish's response is that this teaching is necessary nonetheless: one might otherwise have thought that the *metzora* birds are like *kodashim*, where the animal begins forbidden while alive but then *shechita* comes and renders it permitted (*mashchita umachshira*). The *braisa* therefore teaches that no — the *shechita* does not change its status to permitted; the bird remains forbidden even after the ritual has been performed on it.

### Status Resolution

The *gemara* then brings another challenge to Reish Lakish, this time from a *mishna*: *shachat venimtzah tereifah* — if one of the *metzora* birds was slaughtered and found to be a *tereifah*, one takes a replacement pair for the second bird, *veharishonah muteres bahana'ah* — and the first bird (the one found to be *tereifah*) is permitted in benefit. Like any *tereifah*, it does not carry the forbidden status of the *metzora* birds, because ultimately it was not used.

The challenge to Reish Lakish is sharp: if the bird was forbidden from the time of purchase, it was already forbidden before being slaughtered. Why, then, when its *shechita* fails because it is a *tereifah*, would it revert to being permitted? It was already forbidden!

The *gemara* resolves this: the case here is where the *tereifah* defect was found *bevnei me'eha* — in its internal organs — meaning a congenital defect, an organ that was missing from birth. Such an animal was never fit to serve as one of the *metzora* birds at all, and therefore *kedushah* never took effect upon it — *delo chalah kedushah klal*. Even according to Reish Lakish, who holds that the prohibition takes effect at purchase, if at the very moment of purchase the bird was already a *tereifah* by virtue of a congenital defect, the *kedushah* never attached. This is different from a case where the animal became *tereifah* only later — whether at the moment of *shechita* or subsequently — in which case, since it was whole at the time of purchase, the prohibition would have taken effect then and would remain in force even if the *shechita* ultimately failed.

### Improper Shechita

A further *tosefta* challenges Reish Lakish: if one performed the *shechita* without having the cedar wood, the hyssop, and the red string present — *shelo be'ezov ushelo be'erez ushelo bishni tola'as* — Rabbi Shimon rules that since it was slaughtered without fulfilling its commandment properly (*shelo kemitzvato*), the bird is permitted in benefit.

The *gemara* explains the dispute: *ad kan lo pligi ela* — the entire disagreement turns on whether *shechita she'eina re'uya* — a *shechita* that is not properly constituted — counts as a *shechita* for purposes of conferring the forbidden status, or not. Crucially, both disputants agree that it is *shechita* that gives the bird its status; they disagree only about whether this particular defective *shechita* accomplishes that. One holds that *shechita she'eina re'uya shema shechita* — even an improper *shechita* is still called *shechita* and triggers the prohibition, since the animal was set aside (*huktza*) for the *mitzvah*. The other holds that *shechita she'eina re'uya lo shema shechita* — such a *shechita* does not count and therefore does not confer the status.

The *gemara* concludes that this is a *tannaitic* dispute (*tannai hi*), and Reish Lakish aligns himself with the school of Rabbi Yishmael, which — as cited earlier — derives the birds' prohibition from *egla arufa* and therefore holds that they are forbidden *michayim*, from the time they are alive. That position belongs to Rabbi Yishmael's school, while those *tannaim* who ground the prohibition in *shechita* form the opposing view. The existence of that *tannaitic* disagreement means the challenge to Reish Lakish from the *tosefta* does not compel him.

## Sent-Out Bird Status

### Living Animals

The *gemara* now turns to a different question: is the sent-out bird permitted in benefit? The starting premise, offered in the name of Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, is that *lo matzinu ba'alei chayim she'asurin* — we do not find that living animals acquire a status of *issur hana'ah*. The ordinary rule is that forbidden statuses attach when an animal dies or is slaughtered, not while it is still alive. On that basis, since the sent-out bird remains alive, it should be permitted.

### Exceptions

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak immediately challenges this: what about an animal that was *muktza* or *ne'evad* — set aside for or actually worshiped as *avodah zarah*? These are living animals and they are forbidden. Rabbi Yirmiyah adds: what about an animal that performed *biah* in the presence of witnesses (*rovei'a venirba be'eidim*)? That too is a living animal which the *Torah* requires be put to death — and presumably it is forbidden in benefit even while alive. One could equally have cited the *shor haniskal* from our own *mishna*, which is forbidden from the time of *g'mar din*.

### Most vs. All

The *gemara* therefore qualifies the principle. The formulation is adjusted: *lo matzinu rov ba'alei chayim she'asurin* — it is not that no living animal ever acquires forbidden status, but that *most* living animals do not. These exceptional cases exist, but they are exceptions. The operative assumption, where we have a choice between deeming the living bird permitted and deeming it forbidden, is that living animals ordinarily do not carry *issur hana'ah*, and that is why we default to permitting the sent-out bird.

### Field Analogy

Rabbi Yishmael's school provides a textual basis: the *Torah* says *v'shilach al pnei hasadeh* — and he shall send it out over the face of the field. *Mah sadeh mutar, af hai nami mutar* — just as a field is free for use, so too this bird is permitted.

### Geographic Requirement

The *gemara*, however, questions whether the word *sadeh* really comes to teach that the bird is permitted. Perhaps the term is needed for a different *braisa*: *sadeh* means you cannot stand in Yafo and throw it into the Mediterranean, nor stand at the edge of the wilderness and fling it into the desert, nor stand outside a city and send it back into the city. Rather, you must stand within a city and send it out beyond the city wall, with an open field beyond. The word *sadeh* teaches a geographic requirement for how and where the bird must be sent, not that it is permitted in benefit. The *gemara* replies: if that were all, the text would not need the definite article — *hasadeh*. The extra *hei* teaches two things: *shema mina tarti*, both the geographic requirement and the permissibility of the bird.

### Rava Answer

Rava offers a simpler and more elegant answer: *lo amra Torah shlach l'takalah* — the *Torah* does not issue a command that would create a stumbling block. If the sent-out bird were forbidden in benefit, then after the ceremony no one would know which specific bird it was, and someone might find it and eat it, unknowingly transgressing. The *Torah* would not design a commandment that predictably leads people into transgression.

## Examples Series

### Nezir Hair

The *gemara* continues its survey of cases of *issur hana'ah*. Regarding the hair of a *nazir* — *bsar nazir* — the source is the verse *kadosh yihiyeh gadel pera sear rosho*, "holy shall be the growth of his hair." From the word *kadosh*, one derives that the hair is forbidden in benefit.

One might ask: perhaps *kodesh tofes es damav v'yotze l'chulin* — perhaps holiness works here the way it does with sanctified objects, where one can redeem them so that the money becomes holy and the object returns to ordinary status? The *gemara* answers: *mich'dinan kodesh kadosh k'rinan*. Though we read the word as *kodesh* for purposes of the *drasha*, the actual word written is *kadosh* — and here *Rashi* identifies a crucial distinction between a noun and an adjective. *Kodesh* is a noun, a status of sanctification that can in principle be transferred to money through redemption. *Kadosh* is an adjective, a descriptive quality inherent to the thing itself. An adjective cannot be redeemed away from the object it describes; it adheres to the thing and cannot be detached by transferring it to money. The hair of the *nazir* is intrinsically described as holy — it is not that holiness was assigned to it as a transferable status.

### Peter Chamor

Regarding the *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey that was not redeemed — our *mishna* appears not to accord with the view of Rabbi Shimon. A *braisa* records a *tannaitic* dispute: Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *peter chamor* is forbidden in benefit; Rabbi Shimon permits it. The *mishna* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position.

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha clarifies: this dispute — and the *mishna*'s ruling of forbidden benefit — applies specifically *l'achar arifa*, after the donkey's neck has been broken because the owner did not redeem it with a lamb. On that everyone agrees it is forbidden. The dispute apparently concerns its status prior to that point.

### Basar B'Chalav

For *basar b'chalav*, the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives the *issur hana'ah* from the fact that the verse *lo tivashel gdi b'chalev imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk — appears three times in the *Torah*. One occurrence teaches the prohibition of eating, one teaches the prohibition of cooking, and one teaches the prohibition of deriving benefit.

### Shimon Yehuda

The *mishna*'s ruling does not accord with the view of Rabbi Shimon Yehuda, who holds that *basar b'chalav* is forbidden in eating but permitted in benefit. His proof is from a juxtaposition: the *Torah* places *lo tivashel gdi b'chalev imo* adjacent to the verse *v'anshei kodesh tihiyun li*, "you shall be holy people to me," followed immediately by *u'vasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu lakelev tashlichun oto* — do not eat meat of a *tereifah* in the field; throw it to the dog. The *tereifah* is forbidden in eating but permitted in benefit — you may throw it to a dog, meaning you may derive benefit from it. Since the passage about *basar b'chalav* is juxtaposed to the *tereifah*, we learn: *ma lehalan assur b'achila u'mutar b'hana'ah, af kan assur b'achila u'mutar b'hana'ah* — just as *tereifah* is forbidden to eat but permitted for benefit, so too *basar b'chalav*. The *halacha* does not follow this view; we rule *basar b'chalav* is forbidden even in benefit.

## Chulin B'Azara

### Parallel Argument

The *gemara* now arrives at what is arguably the most complex case in the *mishna*: *chulin shenishchatu b'azara*, ordinary (*chulin*) animals slaughtered in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The source for this prohibition is not immediately obvious, since no verse explicitly addresses it.

Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Meir offers a structural parallel: the *Torah* implies *shchot li besheli v'shelcha beshelcha* — slaughter My things in My place, and your things in your place. We know from the prohibition of *shchutei chutz* that *kodshim* must be slaughtered inside the *Beis Hamikdash*; slaughtering them outside renders them forbidden in benefit. By parallel, *chulin* must be slaughtered outside the *Beis Hamikdash*: *ma sheli beshelcha assur, af shelcha besheli assur* — just as My things [slaughtered] in your place are forbidden, so too your things [slaughtered] in My place are forbidden.

### Karet Distinction

The *gemara* immediately probes whether *karet* applies to *chulin b'azara* by the same analogy: *ma sheli beshelcha anush kares, af shelcha besheli anush kares?* The *gemara* rejects this by citing the verse *v'el petach ohel moed lo hevio l'hakriv korban l'Hashem v'nichrat* — one who fails to bring a *korban* to the *Beis Hamikdash* and instead slaughters it outside incurs *karet*. The verse specifies *korban* — a sacrifice. *Al korban anush kares v'ein al chulin shenishchatu b'azara anush kares*: *karet* applies only to a *korban* slaughtered outside, not to *chulin* slaughtered inside.

But this creates a difficulty for the original analogy: since *chulin b'azara* does not carry *karet* while *shchutei chutz* does, the parallel is imperfect — *ika l'mifrach* — the analogy can be challenged. One can argue that the severity of the *kodshim* case (which includes *karet*) warrants the *issur hana'ah* there, but the absence of *karet* for *chulin b'azara* means the parallel does not compel us to apply *issur hana'ah*.

### Abaye Derivation

Abaye therefore offers an independent derivation. The *Torah* uses the formulation *v'shachat... v'shachato... v'shachat oso* — three separate mentions of *shechita* in connection with *shelamim* (peace offerings). Given that a single mention suffices to teach the law, why the repetition?

### Five-Step Logic

Because the *Torah* also states *ki yirchak mimcha hamakom v'zavachta* — when the *Beis Hamikdash* is distant from you, you may slaughter and eat. The implication: *berichuk makom ata zove'ach v'ee ata zove'ach bemakom karov* — it is when you are far from the *Beis Hamikdash* that you slaughter *chulin*; you do not slaughter *chulin* when you are close to the *Beis Hamikdash*, meaning inside it. *Prats le-chulin shelo yishchatu ba-azarah* — this excludes *chulin* from being slaughtered in the *azarah*. But from this verse alone, one might have thought the prohibition applies only to *tmimim* — unblemished animals that are fit to be brought as *korbanos*. And even if one would extend it to animals with blemishes (*ba'alei mumim*), on the grounds that they belong to the same general category (*min hamachshir*), one might not extend it to wild animals (*chaya*), and certainly not to birds, since birds are not brought as *shelamim* and are slaughtered differently.

### Shechut Repetitions

The three-fold repetition of *shechita* by *shelamim* — *v'shachat, v'shachato, v'shachat oso* — comes to close each of these gaps progressively: only *shelamim* are slaughtered in the *azarah*, not *chulin*, not blemished animals, not *chaya*, and not birds. And the phrase *ki yirchak... v'zavachta v'achalta* — when far away, you shall slaughter and eat — implies further: *mah she'ata zove'ach berichuk makom ata ochel, v'ee ata ochel mah she'ata zove'ach bemakom karov* — what you slaughter far from the *Beis Hamikdash* you may eat; what you slaughter inside the *Beis Hamikdash* you may not eat. This establishes the prohibition on eating *chulin b'azara*. The same three-fold repetition applies again to extend this eating prohibition to all the same categories — blemished animals, wild animals, and birds.

### Tereifa Parallel

Finally, the question remains: perhaps *chulin b'azara* is merely forbidden in eating, but one may derive benefit — for example by throwing it to dogs? Here the *gemara* derives from the verse *lakelev tashlichun oso*, referring to *tereifah*: *oso ata mashlich lakelev v'ee ata mashlich lakelev chulin shenishchatu ba-azarah* — it is the *tereifah* that you throw to dogs; you do not throw *chulin b'azara* to dogs. The juxtaposition with *tereifah* — which may be fed to dogs and thus is permitted in benefit — teaches by exclusion that *chulin b'azara* may not. That is how we derive that *chulin b'azara* is forbidden not only in eating but in benefit entirely.
