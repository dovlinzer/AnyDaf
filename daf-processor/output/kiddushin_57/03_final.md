# Kiddushin 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shor Haniskal

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּעׇרְלָה, בְּכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, וּבְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, בְּצִפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע, וּבִשְׂעַר נָזִיר, וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, וּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מְכָרָן וְקִידֵּשׁ בִּדְמֵיהֶן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *orla*,** i.e., fruit grown during a tree's first three years, or **with diverse kinds in a vineyard,** i.e., grain and grapes planted together, or **with an ox that is** sentenced to be **stoned, or with a heifer whose neck is broken,** or **with** the **leper's birds** which are designated for his offering, **or with a nazirite's hair, or** with **a firstborn donkey, or** with **meat** cooked **in milk, or** with **non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard,** if he betroths her with any of these items, the woman **is not betrothed,** since it is prohibited to derive benefit from any of these items. By contrast, if **one sold them and betrothed** a woman **with** the **money** received from **their** sale, **she is betrothed,** as in these cases one may derive benefit from the money he receives in exchange for the forbidden item.

The *daf* before us — Kiddushin 57 — opens at the bottom of 56b, dealing with the *mishna* that lists items forbidden in benefit (*issur hana'ah*), and therefore if a man attempted to betroth a woman using any of them, she is not *mekudeshet*, since such items carry no legally transferable value. Among the cases discussed is the *shor haniskal* — the ox condemned to stoning because it killed a person — which remains forbidden in benefit even if it was subsequently *shechted*, rather than actually stoned.

### Mar Zutra Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר זוּטְרָא: וְאֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּבָדַק צוּר וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ, דְּמִיחֲזֵי כִּסְקִילָה, אֲבָל שַׁחְטֵיהּ בְּסַכִּין – לָא! מִידֵּי סַכִּין בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא כְּתִיב? וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: בַּכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, בֵּין בְּצוֹר, בֵּין בִּזְכוּכִית, בֵּין בִּקְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra objects to this: But say** that **this** prohibition against eating the ox **applies** only **where he examined** a sharp **stone** to ensure that it had no nicks, **and slaughtered with it** in a halakhically acceptable manner. That action could **be considered like stoning,** since in any event the ox was killed by a stone. The Torah teaches that even if it was killed in this manner, one is prohibited from eating it. **But** if **he slaughtered it with a knife,** he should **not** be prohibited from eating it. The Gemara expresses surprise at this reasoning: **Was a knife written in the Torah?** If one slaughters the animal in a halakhically acceptable manner, this is called slaughtering, not stoning. **And moreover, it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ḥullin* 1:2): One may **slaughter with anything** that cuts cleanly, **whether with a stone, whether with glass, whether with the stalk of a reed.** Consequently, there is no difference in the *halakha* with regard to using a knife or a sharp stone.

The *gemara* raises a challenge — *matkif lah* Mar Zutra: when the *Torah* says that even its flesh (*besoro*) is forbidden, perhaps that applies only where one performed something akin to stoning itself — for instance, where one sharpened a stone into a blade and used it to slaughter the animal, *damechzei kisekila*, it resembles stoning. But if one simply used an ordinary knife, perhaps that would render the animal permitted.

The *gemara* responds on two grounds. First, *mi saki b'oraisa kesiv* — the *Torah* nowhere specifies a knife, and therefore there is no halakhic basis for distinguishing between one method of *shechita* and another; if one type renders the animal permitted, so should any other. Second, there is an explicit *braisa*: one may *shecht* with any implement — *bein betzur bein bezechuchis bein bekurmis shel kano* — whether with a rock, a piece of glass, or a reed splinter. The methods are legally equivalent, and the challenge collapses.

## Et Particle Debate

### Dual Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּנָפְקָא לַן אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה וְאִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִ״לֹּא יֵאָכֵל״, הַאי ״בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״ לְמַאי אֲתָא? לַהֲנָאַת עוֹרוֹ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״ כְּתִיב, בְּשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר וְעוֹרוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that we have derived** both **the prohibition** against **eating and the prohibition** against **benefiting** from an ox sentenced to be stoned **from: "It shall not be eaten," what** *halakha* **does that** other phrase: **"But the owner of the ox shall be clear," come** to teach? The Gemara answers: It comes **to** teach the prohibition against deriving **benefit** from **its hide** after it has been killed. Were it not for this phrase, it might **enter your mind to say** that since **it is written: "Its flesh shall not be eaten,"** only **its flesh is forbidden, but its hide is permitted.** Therefore, the words "and the owner of the ox is clean" teach that its hide is forbidden as well.

The *gemara* now observes that both the prohibition of eating (*issur achila*) and the prohibition of benefit (*issur hana'ah*) are derived from the same phrase — *lo ye'achel es besoro*, its flesh shall not be eaten. Given that, what further need is there for the phrase *u'va'al hashor naki*, "and the owner of the ox shall be clean" — which has also been used to teach that the owner derives no benefit?

### Skin Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהָנָךְ תַּנָּאֵי דְּמַפְּקִי לֵיהּ הַאי ״בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי״ לַחֲצִי כּוֹפֶר וְלִדְמֵי וַלְדוֹת, הֲנָאַת עוֹרוֹ מְנָא לְהוּ? מֵ״אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״, אֶת הַטָּפֵל לִבְשָׂרוֹ. וְאִידַּךְ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to those *tanna'im* who expound this** phrase: **"The owner of the ox shall be clear," to** teach that the owner of an innocuous ox, i.e., one that is not known to cause damage with the intent to injure, is exempt from the payment of **half** of the **indemnity** incurred if that ox killed a person, or that he is exempt from **payment** of damages **for** miscarried **offspring** if his ox gores a pregnant woman and causes her to miscarry; **from where do they** derive this prohibition against **benefiting from** the ox's **hide?** The Gemara answers: They derive it **from** the wording: **"Its flesh** may not be eaten [*velo ye'akhel **et besaro*****]."** The verse could have been formulated: *Velo ye'akhel besaro*, which already means: And its flesh shall not be eaten. The addition of the word "*et*" teaches that the prohibition applies also to **that which is secondary to the flesh,** i.e., the hide. The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, who derives the prohibition against benefiting from the hide from the verse: "But the owner of the ox shall be clear," what does he learn from the additional word "*et*"?

The answer is that *u'va'al hashor naki* is needed to establish that one may not derive benefit even from the hide. Without it, one might have reasoned from *lo ye'achel besoro* — the word *besaro*, its flesh, is what is written — that the flesh is forbidden but the skin is permitted, and one could therefore produce leather from it. The phrase *u'va'al hashor naki* closes that possibility.

But what of those *tannaim* who deduce something else from *u'va'al hashor naki* — for instance, that one does not pay half-damages (*chatzi kofer*) or compensate for miscarried fetuses (*demei vlados*) in the case of a goring ox — and thus do not use that phrase to teach the prohibition on the skin? From where do they derive that the skin too is forbidden in benefit?

### Shimon Ha'amsuni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: שִׁמְעוֹן הָעַמְסוֹנִי וְאָמְרִי לַהּ נְחֶמְיָה הָעַמְסוֹנִי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ כׇּל אֶתִּין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״, פֵּירַשׁ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי, כׇּל אֶתִּין שֶׁדָּרַשְׁתָּ מָה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶם? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקִּבַּלְתִּי שָׂכָר עַל הַדְּרִישָׁה, כָּךְ קִבַּלְתִּי עַל הַפְּרִישָׁה. עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד: ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״ – לְרַבּוֹת תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Shimon HaAmasoni, and some say** that it was **Neḥemya HaAmasoni, would interpret all** occurrences of the word **"*et*" in the Torah,** deriving additional *halakhot* with regard to the particular subject matter. **Once he reached** the verse: **"You shall fear the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 6:13), which is written with the added word "*et*," **he withdrew** from this method of exposition, as whose fear could be an extension of the fear of God? **His students said to him: Our teacher, what will be with all** the occurrences of the word **"*et*" that you interpreted** until now? **He said to them: Just as I received reward for the exposition, so I received reward for** my **withdrawal** from using this method of exposition. The word "*et*" in this verse was not explained **until Rabbi Akiva came and expounded: "You shall fear the Lord your God":** The word "*et*" serves **to include Torah scholars,** i.e., that one is commanded to fear them just as one fears God. In any event, Shimon HaAmasoni no longer derived additional *halakhot* from the word *et*.

This introduces one of the most striking passages in the *gemara*. At the top of 57a, a famous *braisa* is cited: Shimon Ha'amsuni — and some say Nechemia Ha'amsuni — would systematically interpret every instance of the particle *es* in the *Torah* to indicate an inclusion, something additional being taught. This was his organizing principle across the entire *Torah*.

*Keivan shehigia le'es Hashem Elokecha tira* — until he reached the verse commanding fear of God — *perash*, he withdrew. He could not bring himself to say that anything could be compared to or included alongside fear of God. His students immediately raised the obvious problem: *kol esin shedarashta mah tehei aleihem* — if this particular *es* teaches nothing, does that not undermine every other instance you have interpreted? Is the hermeneutical principle now invalidated retroactively?

His response was remarkable: *keshem shekibalti sachar al hadrisha kach kibalti sachar al haprisha* — just as I received reward for the interpretations, so too I will receive reward for the retraction. There is a question of how far this concession actually goes. One reading is that he means: I still believe all those other interpretations are true; I simply cannot in good conscience force this one, and if my inability to answer here casts doubt in people's minds about my other teachings, so be it — my intellectual honesty demands no less. The other, more radical reading is that *sachar al haprisha* means he was willing to surrender all those teachings entirely, rather than compromise on a *drasha* he did not believe. That would make the gesture even more extraordinary — the full sacrifice of a lifetime's learning in service of *emes*.

### Rabbi Akiva Resolution

*Ad sheba Rabbi Akiva v'limed* — until Rabbi Akiva came and taught that *es Hashem Elokecha tira l'rabos talmidei chachamim*: the *es* particle in that verse comes to include *talmidei chachamim*, whose fear and awe bears at least an analogical relationship — obviously not equivalence — to the awe of God, inasmuch as they represent and transmit His *Torah*. One elegant reading of this episode is that it was precisely Shimon Ha'amsuni's act of *perisha*, his public withdrawal rather than offering a forced answer, that made possible Rabbi Akiva's insight: only a tradition that demonstrates such radical commitment to *emes* could produce *talmidei chachamim* genuinely worthy of being mentioned alongside the awe of God.

## Egla/Metzora Analogy

### Egla Arufah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה: מְנָלַן? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: ״כַּפָּרָה״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ, כְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman **with a heifer whose neck is broken,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara clarifies: **From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a heifer whose neck is broken? **The school of Rabbi Yannai said:** An expression of **atonement was written with regard to it.** The verse: "Atone for Your people Israel" (Deuteronomy 21:8), was written with regard to a heifer whose neck was broken, **as** was also written with regard to **sacrificial animals.** Therefore, one is prohibited from deriving benefit from it, just as one may not benefit from an offering.

The *gemara* proceeds to ask: from where do we know that the *egla arufa* — the heifer whose neck is broken as part of the unsolved murder ritual — is forbidden in benefit? The answer given in the name of the school of Rabbi Yannai is: *kapar kesiv bah k'kodshim* — the *Torah* uses the language of atonement (*kapar*) in connection with the *egla arufa*, and therefore it operates on the same principle as sacrifices, which are forbidden in benefit.

### Tzippurei Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע״ מְנָלַן? דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: נֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר מִבִּפְנִים, וְנֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman **with the leper's birds,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara clarifies: **From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a leper's birds? **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** A mitzva that **enables is stated** in the verses with regard to a leper, **and** the Torah also discusses a mitzva that **atones,** both of which are performed **inside** the Temple. Here, a mitzva that enables is a reference to the leper's guilt-offering, which enables him to partake of offerings; and a mitzva that atones is a reference to all other offerings. **And** a mitzva that **enables is stated** in the verses with regard to a leper, **and** the Torah also discusses a mitzva that **atones,** both of which are performed **outside** the Temple. Here, a mitzva that enables is a reference to the leper's birds, which permit him to reenter the camp; and a mitzva that atones is a reference to the heifer whose neck is broken, which atones for the inhabitants of the city nearest to an unsolved murder.

Regarding the *tzippurei metzora* — the birds brought as part of the purification process for a *metzora* — the source is derived from a *braisa* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The *braisa* formulates a parallel structure: inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the *Torah* mentions something that is *machshir* (changes the status of the *metzora*, namely the *asham* brought by the *metzora*) alongside something that is *mechaper* (provides atonement, meaning all other *korbanos*). The same pairing appears outside the *Beis Hamikdash*: something *machshir* (the birds of the *metzora*) alongside something *mechaper* (the *egla arufa*).

### Parallel Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר הָאָמוּר בִּפְנִים עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַכְשִׁיר כִּמְכַפֵּר – אַף מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר הָאָמוּר בַּחוּץ עָשָׂה בּוֹ מַכְשִׁיר כִּמְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** in the case of the **enabling and atoning** rites **stated** in the Torah that are performed **inside** the Temple the Torah **made** the item of **the enabling** rite, i.e., the leper's guilt-offering, from which one is prohibited from deriving benefit, **like** the item of **the atoning** rite, i.e., offerings in general, **so too,** in the case of the **enabling and atoning** rites **stated** in the Torah that are performed **outside** the Temple the Torah **made** the item of **the enabling** rite, i.e., the leper's birds, from which one is prohibited from deriving benefit, **like** the item of **the atoning** rite, i.e., the heifer whose neck is broken.

The logical structure is a *gezeira shava* of principle: just as inside the *Beis Hamikdash* the *machshir* — the *metzora's asham* — is treated like the *mechaper* and is forbidden in benefit, so too outside the *Beis Hamikdash* the *machshir* — the *metzora's* birds — is treated like the *mechaper*, and since the *egla arufa* is forbidden in benefit, so too the birds are forbidden in benefit.

## Metzora Birds Timeline

### Yochanan/Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: צִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע מֵאֵימָתַי אֲסוּרִים? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִשְּׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִשְּׁעַת לְקִיחָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר מִשְּׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה, שְׁחִיטָה הוּא דְּאָסְרָה לַהּ. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִשְּׁעַת לְקִיחָה, מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה נָפְקָא, מָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה מֵחַיִּים – אַף צִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע מֵחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disputed the following issue: **From when** is one **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **the leper's birds? Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From the moment of** their **slaughter; and Reish Lakish says: From the moment** they are **taken** and designated to be a leper's birds. The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From the moment of** their **slaughter,** because **it is the slaughter that prohibits them,** since they are not consecrated beforehand. **Reish Lakish says: From the moment** they are **taken,** since this *halakha* **is derived from the heifer whose neck is broken. Just as** one is prohibited from deriving benefit from **a heifer whose neck is broken during** its **lifetime, so too,** one is prohibited from deriving benefit from the **leper's birds during** their **lifetime.**

An *amoraic* dispute now turns on the question of precisely *when* the *tzippurei metzora* become forbidden in benefit. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *mishe'as shechita* — from the moment of *shechita*. One bird is slaughtered and the other is dipped in its blood and water and released; the act of *shechita* is what triggers the prohibition. Reish Lakish holds: *mishe'as lekicha* — from the moment of purchase.

It is worth noting — as *Rashi* and most *Rishonim* understand — that the sent bird also acquires forbidden status at some point, but ultimately becomes permitted once sent free. According to Reish Lakish, both birds become forbidden from the moment of purchase; the released bird subsequently regains its permitted status upon release. According to Rabbi Yochanan, both become forbidden from the moment of *shechita*, and the released bird then regains permitted status. *Rashi*'s alternative reading — that according to Rabbi Yochanan only the slaughtered bird ever becomes forbidden while the sent bird was never forbidden — does not accord easily with the plain language of the *mishna*, which lists "the birds of the *metzora*" without distinction.

Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning is simply that *shechita hi d'asra lah* — it is the act of *shechita* that creates the forbidden status. Reish Lakish's reasoning is that we derive the prohibition of the *metzora* birds from *egla arufa*. The *egla arufa* is forbidden *michayim* — while still alive — just as sacrifices become forbidden while still alive. Therefore, the *metzora* birds are likewise forbidden while still alive, and since they are alive at the time of purchase, from that point the prohibition takes effect.

## Egla Timeline

### Yannai Memory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה גּוּפַהּ מֵאֵימָתַי? אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: גְּבוּל שָׁמַעְתִּי בָּהּ וְשָׁכַחְתִּי, וְנָסְבִין חַבְרַיָּא לוֹמַר: יְרִידָתָהּ לְנַחַל אֵיתָן הִיא אוֹסַרְתָּהּ. אִי מָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה מִשְּׁעַת לְקִיחָה לָא מִיתַּסְרָא, אַף צִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע נָמֵי מִשְּׁעַת לְקִיחָה לָא מִיתַּסְרִי! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם אִית לֵיהּ גְּבוּל אַחֲרִינָא, הָכָא מִי אִית לֵיהּ גְּבוּל אַחֲרִינָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to it, **the heifer whose neck is broken, itself, from when** is it a forbidden item? **Rabbi Yannai said: I heard** the **boundary,** i.e., stage, beyond which **it** is forbidden, **but I have forgotten** what it is. **But the group** of scholars **were inclined to say** that **its descent to a hard valley,** where its neck is broken, **is** the action that **renders it forbidden.** The Gemara asks: **If** so, **just as** with **a heifer whose neck is broken, it is not forbidden from the moment** it is **taken** but only afterward, **so too, the leper's birds should also not be forbidden from the moment** they are **taken.** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the heifer, **it has another boundary** that can render it forbidden, namely its descent to the valley; **here,** in the case of the leper's birds, **does it have another boundary?** It is taken and immediately slaughtered.

The *gemara* now turns to the *egla arufa* itself: when precisely does it become forbidden? Rabbi Yannai says: *gvul shamati bah v'shachachti* — I recall that there is a significant point in the process that triggers the prohibition, but I no longer remember which point.

The colleagues — *chavraya* — propose: *yeridasah lenachal eisan hi osartah* — it is the descent into the *nachal eitan*, the wadi, that renders it forbidden. This is a concrete, explicit moment described in the *Torah*, making it the natural candidate for the triggering event.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *egla arufa* is not forbidden from the moment of purchase, should we apply that reasoning consistently to the *metzora* birds — and say that they too are not forbidden from the time of purchase?

The *gemara* rejects this: *hachi hashta? hasam is lei gvul achrina hacha leis lei gvul achrina* — the situations are not parallel. The *egla arufa* has its own distinct moment — the descent to the wadi — which is explicitly referenced in the *Torah*. The *metzora* birds have no such equivalent moment. And as *Rashi* points out, while the *Torah* says nothing analogous to the descent of the wadi by the birds, it does say explicitly that the birds must be purchased (*tikach*), thereby establishing the moment of acquisition as the operative trigger. Each has its own moment, but both become forbidden while still alive.

## Metzora Birds Timeline (continued)

### Braisa Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״כׇּל צִפּוֹר טְהֹרָה תֹּאכֵלוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמְשׁוּלַּחַת, ״וְזֶה אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֹאכְלוּ מֵהֶם״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשְּׁחוּטָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מֵחַיִּים אֲסוּרָה, לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה מִיבַּעְיָא?! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקֳּדָשִׁים, דְּמֵחַיִּים אֲסִירִי, וְאָתְיָא שְׁחִיטָה וּמַכְשְׁרָה לְהוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Of all clean birds you may eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:11). The superfluous word "all" is stated **to include** one of the leper's birds, which is **sent away** to freedom, while the words: **"But these are they of which you shall not eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:12), are stated **to include** in the prohibition the other, **slaughtered** bird. Rabbi Yoḥanan asks: **And if it enters your mind** to say that the bird **is forbidden from** when it is **alive, is** a verse **necessary** to teach that it is forbidden **after** its **slaughter?** The Gemara answers: The verse is necessary, **lest you say: Just as it is in** the case **of sacrificial animals, where** one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **them when** they are **alive, and** the act of **slaughter comes and renders them fit** to be eaten, so too with regard to the bird. The verse **teaches us** that with regard to the leper's bird this is not the case, and it remains forbidden even after it has been slaughtered.

Rabbi Yochanan now challenges Reish Lakish from a *braisa*. The *braisa* expounds: *kol tzippor tehorah tocheilu lerabos es hameshulachas* — "every pure bird you may eat" includes the sent-out bird, teaching that it is permitted in benefit. And *vezeh asher lo socheilu meihem lerabos es hashechutah* — the extra formulation teaches that the slaughtered bird is forbidden in eating — and by extension, given the *mishna*, in benefit.

Now, Rabbi Yochanan presses: if according to Reish Lakish the bird was already forbidden from the time of purchase — *michayim asura* — why does the *braisa* derive the prohibition specifically from the moment of *shechita*? If it was already forbidden before *shechita*, why would the text focus on that moment?

Reish Lakish's response is that this teaching is necessary nonetheless: one might otherwise have thought that the *metzora* birds are like *kodashim*, where the animal begins forbidden while alive but then *shechita* comes and renders it permitted (*mashchita umachshira*). The *braisa* therefore teaches that no — the *shechita* does not change its status to permitted; the bird remains forbidden even after the ritual has been performed on it.

### Status Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: שְׁחָטָהּ וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה – יִקַּח זוּג לַשְּׁנִיָּה, וְהָרִאשׁוֹנָה מוּתֶּרֶת בַּהֲנָאָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מֵחַיִּים אֲסוּרָה, הָרִאשׁוֹנָה אַמַּאי מוּתֶּרֶת בַּהֲנָאָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה בִּבְנֵי מֵעֶיהָ, דְּלָא חָל עֲלַהּ קְדוּשָּׁה כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** Reish Lakish: The mishna (*Nega'im* 14:5) teaches that if one **slaughtered** one of the leper's birds **and it was found** to be **a bird with a condition that would have caused it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], a partner is taken for the second,** i.e., remaining, bird, **while** with regard to **the first** bird, i.e., the *tereifa*, one **is permitted to** derive **benefit** from it. **And if it enters your mind** that the bird **is forbidden from** when it is **alive, why is** one **permitted to** derive **benefit from the first one,** if the prohibition took effect before it was slaughtered? Reish Lakish **said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the slaughtered bird **was found** to be **a *tereifa* in its inner organs,** so **that** the **consecration did not take effect at all,** as the bird was not fit to be used for this purpose.

The *gemara* then brings another challenge to Reish Lakish, this time from a *mishna*: *shachat venimtzah tereifah* — if one of the *metzora* birds was slaughtered and found to be a *tereifah*, one takes a replacement pair for the second bird, *veharishonah muteres bahana'ah* — and the first bird (the one found to be *tereifah*) is permitted in benefit. Like any *tereifah*, it does not carry the forbidden status of the *metzora* birds, because ultimately it was not used.

The challenge to Reish Lakish is sharp: if the bird was forbidden from the time of purchase, it was already forbidden before being slaughtered. Why, then, when its *shechita* fails because it is a *tereifah*, would it revert to being permitted? It was already forbidden!

The *gemara* resolves this: the case here is where the *tereifah* defect was found *bevnei me'eha* — in its internal organs — meaning a congenital defect, an organ that was missing from birth. Such an animal was never fit to serve as one of the *metzora* birds at all, and therefore *kedushah* never took effect upon it — *delo chalah kedushah klal*. Even according to Reish Lakish, who holds that the prohibition takes effect at purchase, if at the very moment of purchase the bird was already a *tereifah* by virtue of a congenital defect, the *kedushah* never attached. This is different from a case where the animal became *tereifah* only later — whether at the moment of *shechita* or subsequently — in which case, since it was whole at the time of purchase, the prohibition would have taken effect then and would remain in force even if the *shechita* ultimately failed.

### Improper Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: שְׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּאֵזוֹב וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּעֵץ אֶרֶז וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁנִי תוֹלַעַת, רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְהוּקְצָה לְמִצְוָתָהּ – אֲסוּרָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְנִשְׁחֲטָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתָהּ – מוּתֶּרֶת. עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא מָר סָבַר: שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה – שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, וּמָר סָבַר: שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה – לֹא שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה. דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא מֵחַיִּים לָא מִיתַּסְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised** another **objection to** Reish Lakish from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nega'im* 8:8): If he **slaughtered** the bird **without** bringing **a hyssop, or without** bringing **cedar wood, or without** bringing **a scarlet thread,** which were all used in the rite, **Rabbi Ya'akov says: Since** the bird **was set aside for its mitzva,** it is **forbidden** anyway. **Rabbi Shimon says: Since it was not slaughtered in accordance with its mitzva,** it is **permitted.** Reish Lakish infers: **They disagree only with regard to** this issue, that one **Sage,** Rabbi Ya'akov, **holds** that an act of **slaughter that is not fit** for accomplishing its full ritual purpose **is** nevertheless **considered** an act of **slaughter,** and the bird is therefore forbidden; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that an act of **slaughter that is not fit** for accomplishing its full ritual purpose **is not considered** an act of **slaughter** at all, and therefore one is permitted to derive benefit from the bird. **But everyone agrees at least** that one **is not prohibited from** deriving **benefit from it** when it is **alive,** but only after it has been slaughtered.

A further *tosefta* challenges Reish Lakish: if one performed the *shechita* without having the cedar wood, the hyssop, and the red string present — *shelo be'ezov ushelo be'erez ushelo bishni tola'as* — Rabbi Shimon rules that since it was slaughtered without fulfilling its commandment properly (*shelo kemitzvato*), the bird is permitted in benefit.

The *gemara* explains the dispute: *ad kan lo pligi ela* — the entire disagreement turns on whether *shechita she'eina re'uya* — a *shechita* that is not properly constituted — counts as a *shechita* for purposes of conferring the forbidden status, or not. Crucially, both disputants agree that it is *shechita* that gives the bird its status; they disagree only about whether this particular defective *shechita* accomplishes that. One holds that *shechita she'eina re'uya shema shechita* — even an improper *shechita* is still called *shechita* and triggers the prohibition, since the animal was set aside (*huktza*) for the *mitzvah*. The other holds that *shechita she'eina re'uya lo shema shechita* — such a *shechita* does not count and therefore does not confer the status.

The *gemara* concludes that this is a *tannaitic* dispute (*tannai hi*), and Reish Lakish aligns himself with the school of Rabbi Yishmael, which — as cited earlier — derives the birds' prohibition from *egla arufa* and therefore holds that they are forbidden *michayim*, from the time they are alive. That position belongs to Rabbi Yishmael's school, while those *tannaim* who ground the prohibition in *shechita* form the opposing view. The existence of that *tannaitic* disagreement means the challenge to Reish Lakish from the *tosefta* does not compel him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: נֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר מִבִּפְנִים, וְנֶאֱמַר מַכְשִׁיר וּמְכַפֵּר בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish replied: I concede that this *baraita* does not accord with my opinion, but this issue **is** a dispute between the ***tanna'im*. As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** A mitzva that **enables is stated** in the verses with regard to a leper, **and** the Torah also discusses a mitzva that **atones,** both of which are performed **inside** the Temple. **And** a mitzva that **enables is stated** in the verses with regard to a leper, **and** the Torah also discusses a mitzva that **atones,** both of which are performed **outside** the Temple.

## Sent-Out Bird Status

### Living Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: לֹא מָצִינוּ בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים שֶׁאֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** The reason to expound the verse as the *baraita* does is that **we have not found** kosher **living creatures that are** permanently **forbidden** with regard to eating. Therefore, it stands to reason that the slaughtered bird is forbidden, not the living one.

The *gemara* now turns to a different question: is the sent-out bird permitted in benefit? The starting premise, offered in the name of Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, is that *lo matzinu ba'alei chayim she'asurin* — we do not find that living animals acquire a status of *issur hana'ah*. The ordinary rule is that forbidden statuses attach when an animal dies or is slaughtered, not while it is still alive. On that basis, since the sent-out bird remains alive, it should be permitted.

### Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: וְלָא הֲרֵי מוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד, דְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִינְהוּ וַאֲסִירִי! כִּי אֲסִירִי – לְגָבוֹהַּ, לְהֶדְיוֹט – מִישְׁרֵא שְׁרֵי. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הֲרֵי רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע בְּעֵדִים, דְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִינְהוּ וַאֲסִירִי! אֶלָּא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: לֹא מָצִינוּ רוֹב בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים שֶׁאֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak objects to this** explanation: **But haven't** we found kosher living animals that are permanently forbidden? **But there are** the cases of an animal **set aside** as an offering to an idol, **and** an animal that was itself **worshipped** as an idol, **which are living creatures and** yet **are** permanently **forbidden.** The Gemara answers: There is a difference, as **when** they are **forbidden,** they are forbidden only to be used **for the Most High,** i.e., to be used as offerings in the Temple service, but it is permitted **for a common** Jew to derive benefit from them. **Rabbi Yirmeya objects to** the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **But** an animal that **copulated** with a woman, and an animal that **copulated** with a man, **in** the presence of **witnesses, they are living creatures and** yet they **are** permanently **forbidden,** as the *halakha* is that these animals are killed, and one is prohibited from deriving benefit from them once they have been sentenced. **Rather,** the above explanation should be emended to say: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: We have not found most** kosher **living creatures that are** permanently **forbidden** while they are still alive, and it can be assumed that the inclusion of the verse is referring to that which is generally forbidden, even if there are exceptions.

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak immediately challenges this: what about an animal that was *muktza* or *ne'evad* — set aside for or actually worshiped as *avodah zarah*? These are living animals and they are forbidden. Rabbi Yirmiyah adds: what about an animal that performed *biah* in the presence of witnesses (*rovei'a venirba be'eidim*)? That too is a living animal which the *Torah* requires be put to death — and presumably it is forbidden in benefit even while alive. One could equally have cited the *shor haniskal* from our own *mishna*, which is forbidden from the time of *g'mar din*.

The *gemara* therefore qualifies the principle. The formulation is adjusted: *lo matzinu rov ba'alei chayim she'asurin* — it is not that no living animal ever acquires forbidden status, but that *most* living animals do not. These exceptional cases exist, but they are exceptions. The operative assumption, where we have a choice between deeming the living bird permitted and deeming it forbidden, is that living animals ordinarily do not carry *issur hana'ah*, and that is why we default to permitting the sent-out bird.

### Field Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשִׁלַּח עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה״ – כְּשָׂדֶה, מָה שָׂדֶה מוּתֶּרֶת – אַף הַאי נָמֵי מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another answer to the question of how the *baraita* knew which bird the verse is permitting. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The verse states: "And shall let go** the living bird **into the open field"** (Leviticus 14:7), which indicates that the bird is **like a field: Just as a field is permitted, so too, this** bird **is also permitted.**

Rabbi Yishmael's school provides a textual basis: the *Torah* says *v'shilach al pnei hasadeh* — and he shall send it out over the face of the field. *Mah sadeh mutar, af hai nami mutar* — just as a field is free for use, so too this bird is permitted.

### Geographic Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״שָׂדֶה״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״שָׂדֶה״ – שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲמוֹד בְּיָפוֹ וְיִזְרְקֶנָּה לַיָּם, בִּגְבָת וְיִזְרְקֶנָּה לַמִּדְבָּר, וְשֶׁלֹּא יַעֲמוֹד חוּץ לָעִיר וְיִזְרְקֶנָּה בְּתוֹךְ הָעִיר. אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁעוֹמֵד בָּעִיר וְיִזְרְקֶנָּה חוּץ לַחוֹמָה. וְאִידַּךְ? אִם כֵּן נִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״שָׂדֶה״, מַאי ״הַשָּׂדֶה״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is **that** word **"field" coming to** teach **this? That** word **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The word **"field"** teaches **that one may not stand in Jaffa and throw** the bird that is set free **to the sea,** or stand **in Gevat and throw it to the desert, and that he may not stand outside the city and throw it inside the city. Rather, any** manner in **which he is standing in the city and throws it outside the wall** to the field is valid. The word "field" teaches that one must set it free only to the field and nowhere else, not to teach that the bird is permitted. **And the other** *tanna*, the school of Rabbi Yishmael, who derives that one is permitted to derive benefit from the bird based on the word "field," replies: **If so,** that this was the only *halakha* the word is teaching, **let the Torah write "field"; what** is the significance of **"the field"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** i.e., both the place to throw the bird and the permission to derive benefit.

The *gemara*, however, questions whether the word *sadeh* really comes to teach that the bird is permitted. Perhaps the term is needed for a different *braisa*: *sadeh* means you cannot stand in Yafo and throw it into the Mediterranean, nor stand at the edge of the wilderness and fling it into the desert, nor stand outside a city and send it back into the city. Rather, you must stand within a city and send it out beyond the city wall, with an open field beyond. The word *sadeh* teaches a geographic requirement for how and where the bird must be sent, not that it is permitted in benefit. The *gemara* replies: if that were all, the text would not need the definite article — *hasadeh*. The extra *hei* teaches two things: *shema mina tarti*, both the geographic requirement and the permissibility of the bird.

### Rava Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לֹא אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״שַׁלַּח״ לְתַקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** a different answer to the question of how the *baraita* knew which bird the verse is permitting: **The Torah did not say "let go" for** it to serve as a **stumbling block.** If the bird sent free was forbidden, the Torah would not have commanded him to send it away, since people might eat it unwittingly.

Rava offers a simpler and more elegant answer: *lo amra Torah shlach l'takalah* — the *Torah* does not issue a command that would create a stumbling block. If the sent-out bird were forbidden in benefit, then after the ceremony no one would know which specific bird it was, and someone might find it and eat it, unknowingly transgressing. The *Torah* would not design a commandment that predictably leads people into transgression.

## Examples Series

### Nezir Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׂעַר נָזִיר. מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״קָדֹשׁ יִהְיֶה גַּדֵּל פֶּרַע שְׂעַר רֹאשׁוֹ״ – גִּידּוּלוֹ יִהְיֶה קָדוֹשׁ. אִי מָה קֹדֶשׁ תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו, וְיוֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין – אַף שְׂעַר נָזִיר תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו וְיוֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין! מִי קָרֵינַן ״קוֹדֶשׁ״? ״קָדוֹשׁ״ קָרֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman **with a nazirite's hair,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a nazirite's hair? The Gemara answers: **As the verse states** with regard to a nazirite: **"He shall be holy [*kadosh*], he shall let the locks of the hair of his head grow long"** (Numbers 6:5), which teaches: **His** hair **growth shall be holy.** The Gemara asks: **If** the hair of a nazirite can be compared to consecrated property by use of the term "holy," then **just as** with regard to **consecrated** property, it **transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed **and** it **becomes desacralized, so too, a nazirite's hair should transfer** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed **and** the hair itself should become **desacralized.** This is not the *halakha*. The Gemara answers: **Do we read holy [*kodesh*]** in this verse, which is the term the verse uses for a consecrated item (see Leviticus 22:14)? **We read "holy [*kadosh*]."** Since a different conjugation of the term is used, the *halakhot* of the hair of a nazirite are not derived from those of consecrated property.

The *gemara* continues its survey of cases of *issur hana'ah*. Regarding the hair of a *nazir* — *bsar nazir* — the source is the verse *kadosh yihiyeh gadel pera sear rosho*, "holy shall be the growth of his hair." From the word *kadosh*, one derives that the hair is forbidden in benefit.

One might ask: perhaps *kodesh tofes es damav v'yotze l'chulin* — perhaps holiness works here the way it does with sanctified objects, where one can redeem them so that the money becomes holy and the object returns to ordinary status? The *gemara* answers: *mich'dinan kodesh kadosh k'rinan*. Though we read the word as *kodesh* for purposes of the *drasha*, the actual word written is *kadosh* — and here *Rashi* identifies a crucial distinction between a noun and an adjective. *Kodesh* is a noun, a status of sanctification that can in principle be transferred to money through redemption. *Kadosh* is an adjective, a descriptive quality inherent to the thing itself. An adjective cannot be redeemed away from the object it describes; it adheres to the thing and cannot be detached by transferring it to money. The hair of the *nazir* is intrinsically described as holy — it is not that holiness was assigned to it as a transferable status.

### Peter Chamor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר. נֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּתַנְיָא: פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לְאַחַר עֲרִיפָה, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman **with a firstborn donkey,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a firstborn donkey,** deriving **benefit** from it **is prohibited;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And Rabbi Shimon permits** it. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** The mishna can be referring to one who betrothed a woman with a firstborn donkey **after** it has had its **neck broken, and everyone agrees** that it is prohibited to derive benefit from the donkey once its neck is broken.

Regarding the *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey that was not redeemed — our *mishna* appears not to accord with the view of Rabbi Shimon. A *braisa* records a *tannaitic* dispute: Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *peter chamor* is forbidden in benefit; Rabbi Shimon permits it. The *mishna* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position.

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha clarifies: this dispute — and the *mishna*'s ruling of forbidden benefit — applies specifically *l'achar arifa*, after the donkey's neck has been broken because the owner did not redeem it with a lamb. On that everyone agrees it is forbidden. The dispute apparently concerns its status prior to that point.

### Basar B'Chalav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים: אֶחָד אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה, וְאֶחָד אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, וְאֶחָד אִיסּוּר בִּישּׁוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman with **meat** cooked **in milk,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from meat cooked in milk? **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The Torah states **three times: "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk"** (Exodus 23:19; Exodus 34:26; Deuteronomy 14:21). **One** verse serves to teach **the prohibition** against **eating** meat cooked in milk, **and one** verse serves to teach **the prohibition** against deriving **benefit** from meat cooked in milk, **and one** verse serves to teach **the prohibition** against **cooking** meat in milk.

For *basar b'chalav*, the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives the *issur hana'ah* from the fact that the verse *lo tivashel gdi b'chalev imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk — appears three times in the *Torah*. One occurrence teaches the prohibition of eating, one teaches the prohibition of cooking, and one teaches the prohibition of deriving benefit.

### Shimon Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאַנְשֵׁי קֹדֶשׁ תִּהְיוּן לִי״. מָה לְהַלָּן אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה – אַף כָּאן אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says:** It is **prohibited to eat meat** cooked **in milk but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it, **as** it **is stated: "For you are a holy people to the Lord your God. You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk"** (Deuteronomy 14:21), **and it states there** with regard to the prohibition of an unslaughtered animal carcass: **"And you shall be holy men to Me"** (Exodus 22:30). Since both verses employ the term "holy" he derives: **Just as there,** in the case of an animal carcass, it is **prohibited to eat** it **but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it, as the Torah explicitly states that it may be sold to a gentile, **so too here,** with regard to meat cooked in milk, it is **prohibited to eat** it **but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it.

The *mishna*'s ruling does not accord with the view of Rabbi Shimon Yehuda, who holds that *basar b'chalav* is forbidden in eating but permitted in benefit. His proof is from a juxtaposition: the *Torah* places *lo tivashel gdi b'chalev imo* adjacent to the verse *v'anshei kodesh tihiyun li*, "you shall be holy people to me," followed immediately by *u'vasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu lakelev tashlichun oto* — do not eat meat of a *tereifah* in the field; throw it to the dog. The *tereifah* is forbidden in eating but permitted in benefit — you may throw it to a dog, meaning you may derive benefit from it. Since the passage about *basar b'chalav* is juxtaposed to the *tereifah*, we learn: *ma lehalan assur b'achila u'mutar b'hana'ah, af kan assur b'achila u'mutar b'hana'ah* — just as *tereifah* is forbidden to eat but permitted for benefit, so too *basar b'chalav*. The *halacha* does not follow this view; we rule *basar b'chalav* is forbidden even in benefit.

## Chulin B'Azara

### Parallel Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: שְׁחוֹט לִי – בְּשֶׁלִּי. וְשֶׁלָּךְ – בְּשֶׁלָּךְ. מַה שֶׁלִּי בְּשֶׁלָּךְ – אָסוּר, אַף שֶׁלָּךְ בְּשֶׁלִּי – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman with the items it enumerated, **or** with **non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** i.e., that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a non-sacred animal that was slaughtered in the Temple courtyard, derived? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir: The Torah said: Slaughter for Me,** i.e., for offerings to God, **in My** place, inside the Temple courtyard, **and your** non-sacred animals that are intended for eating should be slaughtered **in your** place, outside the Temple courtyard. **Just as** one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **My** consecrated animals if they were slaughtered **in your** place, as one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a consecrated animal slaughtered outside the Temple, **so too,** one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **your** non-sacred animals if they were slaughtered **in My** place.

The *gemara* now arrives at what is arguably the most complex case in the *mishna*: *chulin shenishchatu b'azara*, ordinary (*chulin*) animals slaughtered in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The source for this prohibition is not immediately obvious, since no verse explicitly addresses it.

Rabbi Yochanan citing Rabbi Meir offers a structural parallel: the *Torah* implies *shchot li besheli v'shelcha beshelcha* — slaughter My things in My place, and your things in your place. We know from the prohibition of *shchutei chutz* that *kodshim* must be slaughtered inside the *Beis Hamikdash*; slaughtering them outside renders them forbidden in benefit. By parallel, *chulin* must be slaughtered outside the *Beis Hamikdash*: *ma sheli beshelcha assur, af shelcha besheli assur* — just as My things [slaughtered] in your place are forbidden, so too your things [slaughtered] in My place are forbidden.

### Karet Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה שֶׁלִּי בְּשֶׁלָּךְ עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, אַף שֶׁלָּךְ בְּשֶׁלִּי עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ לְהַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּן לַה׳... וְנִכְרַת״, עַל קׇרְבָּן – עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, עַל חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה – אֵין עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** so, **just as** the act of slaughtering **My** consecrated animals **in your** place is **punishable by *karet*, so too,** to the act of slaughtering **your** non-sacred animals **in My** place should be **punishable by *karet*.** To counter this logic, **the verse states:** "If a man from the house of Israel slaughters an ox or lamb…**and has not brought it to the door of the Tent of Meeting to sacrifice an offering to the Lord…and that man shall be cut off"** (Leviticus 17:3–4). This teaches that it is only **for an offering** that one slaughtered outside the Tent of Meeting of the Tabernacle, or the Temple courtyard, that **he is punishable with *karet*,** but **for non-sacred** animals that one slaughtered **in the** Temple **courtyard he is not punishable with *karet*.**

The *gemara* immediately probes whether *karet* applies to *chulin b'azara* by the same analogy: *ma sheli beshelcha anush kares, af shelcha besheli anush kares?* The *gemara* rejects this by citing the verse *v'el petach ohel moed lo heviro l'hakriv korban l'Hashem v'nichrat* — one who fails to bring a *korban* to the *Beis Hamikdash* and instead slaughters it outside incurs *karet*. The verse specifies *korban* — a sacrifice. *Al korban anush kares v'ein al chulin shenishchatu b'azara anush kares*: *karet* applies only to a *korban* slaughtered outside, not to *chulin* slaughtered inside.

But this creates a difficulty for the original analogy: since *chulin b'azara* does not carry *karet* while *shchutei chutz* does, the parallel is imperfect — *ika l'mifrach* — the analogy can be challenged. One can argue that the severity of the *kodshim* case (which includes *karet*) warrants the *issur hana'ah* there, but the absence of *karet* for *chulin b'azara* means the parallel does not compel us to apply *issur hana'ah*.

### Abaye Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵהָכָא: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ״ ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ״ ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ״. תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי יַתִּירֵי, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye quotes a lengthy *baraita* that serves as a source for the *halakha* that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a non-sacred animal slaughtered in the Temple courtyard. **Rather, Abaye said** it is derived **from here:** The Torah states **three verses that** have **a superfluous** element with regard to the various species from which one may bring a peace-offering: The verse **"and he slaughters it"** (Leviticus 3:2), the verse **"and he shall slaughter it"** (Leviticus 3:8), and the verse **"and he shall slaughter it"** (Leviticus 3:13). The Torah could simply have stated: "And he shall slaughter," without adding: "It." **Why** must **the verse state** the term "it" these three times?

Abaye therefore offers an independent derivation. The *Torah* uses the formulation *v'shachat... v'shachato... v'shachat oso* — three separate mentions of *shechita* in connection with *shelamim* (peace offerings). Given that a single mention suffices to teach the law, why the repetition?

### Five-Step Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִרְחַק מִמְּךָ הַמָּקוֹם... וְזָבַחְתָּ״, בְּרִחוּק מָקוֹם אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ וְאִי אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ בְּמָקוֹם קָרוֹב, פְּרָט לְחוּלִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִשָּׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא תְּמִימִים הָרְאוּיִם לִיקָּרֵב, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִים, שֶׁכֵּן: מִין הַמַּכְשִׁיר. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַחַיָּה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת הַחַיָּה, שֶׁהִיא בִּשְׁחִיטָה כִּבְהֵמָה. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעוֹפוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ״ ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתוֹ״ ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The source for the *halakha* is **because it is stated** with regard to the ritual slaughter of animals for meat consumption: **"If the place** which the Lord your God shall choose to put His name there **be too far from you, then you shall slaughter** of your herd and of your flock" (Deuteronomy 12:21), from which it is derived: **When** you are **far from the place,** i.e., the Temple, **you may slaughter** non-sacred animals for meat consumption, **but you may not slaughter** non-sacred animals **in a near place,** i.e., in the Temple. The verse serves to **exclude non-sacred** animals, thereby teaching **that they may not be slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard.** However: **And I have** derived the prohibition against slaughtering a non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard **only** with regard to **unblemished** animals, which are fit to be sacrificed. **From where** do I know **to include** even **blemished** animals, which are not fit to be sacrificed, in this prohibition? **I include blemished** animals **since** they are at least of **the type that is fit** to be sacrificed. **From where** do I know **to include the undomesticated animal,** which is never sacrificed as an offering, in this prohibition? **I include the undomesticated animal since it** is rendered fit for consumption **by** means of **slaughtering, like a domesticated animal. From where** do I know **to include fowl,** as the Torah does not mention slaughter with regard to fowl, in this prohibition? **The verse states: "And he slaughters it," "and he shall slaughter it,"** as well as **"and he shall slaughter it,"** employing the additional term "it" each time. These three verses teach that one may not slaughter any non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard.

Because the *Torah* also states *ki yirchak mimcha hamakom v'zavachta* — when the *Beis Hamikdash* is distant from you, you may slaughter and eat. The implication: *berichuk makom ata zove'ach v'ee ata zove'ach bemakom karov* — it is when you are far from the *Beis Hamikdash* that you slaughter *chulin*; you do not slaughter *chulin* when you are close to the *Beis Hamikdash*, meaning inside it. *Prats le-chulin shelo yishchatu ba-azarah* — this excludes *chulin* from being slaughtered in the *azarah*. But from this verse alone, one might have thought the prohibition applies only to *tmimim* — unblemished animals that are fit to be brought as *korbanos*. And even if one would extend it to animals with blemishes (*ba'alei mumim*), on the grounds that they belong to the same general category (*min hamachshir*), one might not extend it to wild animals (*chaya*), and certainly not to birds, since birds are not brought as *shelamim* and are slaughtered differently.

The three-fold repetition of *shechita* by *shelamim* — *v'shachat, v'shachato, v'shachat oso* — comes to close each of these gaps progressively: only *shelamim* are slaughtered in the *azarah*, not *chulin*, not blemished animals, not *chaya*, and not birds.

### Tereifa Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, וְאִם שָׁחַט יְהֵא מוּתָּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי יִרְחַק מִמְּךָ הַמָּקוֹם... וְזָבַחְתָּ... וְאָכַלְתָּ״, מַה שֶּׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ בְּרִחוּק מָקוֹם – אַתָּה אוֹכֵל, וְאִי אַתָּה אוֹכֵל מַה שֶּׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ בְּמָקוֹם קָרוֹב, פְּרָט לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה. יָכוֹל לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, וְאִם שָׁחַט יַשְׁלִיכֶנּוּ לִפְנֵי כְלָבִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לַכֶּלֶב תַּשְׁלִכוּן אֹתוֹ״ – אוֹתוֹ אַתָּה מַשְׁלִיךְ לַכֶּלֶב, וְאִי אַתָּה מַשְׁלִיךְ חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **one may not slaughter** *ab initio* **but if he did slaughter it would be permitted for him** to eat it. Therefore, **the verse states: "If the place** which the Lord your God shall choose to put His name there **be too far from you, then you shall slaughter** of your herd and of your flock, which the Lord has given you, as I have commanded you, **and you shall eat** within your gates, after all the desire of your soul" (Deuteronomy 12:21), which is expounded as follows: **That which you slaughter in a far place,** outside the Temple, **you may eat, but you may not eat that which you slaughter in a near place,** which **excludes non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard.** Therefore, it is prohibited to eat a non-sacred animal slaughtered in the Temple courtyard even after the fact. The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **he may not slaughter** a non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard *ab initio*, **but if he did slaughter** it, **he may cast it before the dogs,** i.e., derive benefit from it. **The verse states:** "Therefore you shall not eat any flesh that is torn of beasts in the field; **you shall cast it to the dogs"** (Exodus 22:30), which teaches that although one is prohibited from eating the meat of a *tereifa*, one may derive benefit from it. The word "it" serves to emphasize and exclude: **You may cast it,** i.e., a *tereifa*, **to the dogs, but you may not cast non-sacred** animals **that have been slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard,** as it is prohibited to derive any benefit from them.

Finally, the question remains: perhaps *chulin b'azara* is merely forbidden in eating, but one may derive benefit — for example by throwing it to dogs? Here the *gemara* derives from the verse *lakelev tashlichun oso*, referring to *tereifah*: *oso ata mashlich lakelev v'ee ata mashlich lakelev chulin shenishchatu ba-azarah* — it is the *tereifah* that you throw to dogs; you do not throw *chulin b'azara* to dogs. The juxtaposition with *tereifah* — which may be fed to dogs and thus is permitted in benefit — teaches by exclusion that *chulin b'azara* may not. That is how we derive that *chulin b'azara* is forbidden not only in eating but in benefit entirely.
