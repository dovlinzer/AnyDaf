# Berakhot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Onain Exemption

### Rashi: Osek B'Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁמֵּתוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — פָּטוּר מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִן הַתְּפִלָּה וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין, וּמִכָּל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One whose deceased** relative **is laid out** unburied **before him is exempt from the recitation of *Shema*, from** the *Amida* **prayer, and from** the mitzva to don **phylacteries, as well as all** positive **mitzvot mentioned in the Torah,** until the deceased has been buried.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 17a opens a broader discussion about the dead and their awareness, but before entering that terrain, it is worth pausing to summarize the halachic debate concerning the *onain* — one who has suffered an immediate loss and whose dead has not yet been buried — and his exemption from *krias Shema* and related obligations.

Rashi's position, as he presents it in his first comment on *mi she-meiso*, is that the *onain* is exempt on the basis of *osek b'mitzvah*: he is distracted with concerns about the burial of the dead, analogous to the case of the bridegroom. This raises, of course, the same questions that arise in the bridegroom's case — how much is the exemption rooted in distraction, and how much in the formal halakhic category of *osek b'mitzvah*? Nevertheless, Rashi classifies the *onain* within that general framework.

What immediately stands out, however, is the sheer breadth of the *onain*'s exemptions. He does not *daven*, does not recite *Birkas Hamazon*, does not recite *Hamotzi* — a scope of exemption we never find in any ordinary case of *osek b'mitzvah*. Rashi, noting the difficulty, says the *onain* is "not required" to perform these acts but retains the option to do so if he chooses. Still, the thoroughgoing nature of the exemption is striking, and Rashi's explanation seems strained by the sheer breadth of what is being set aside.

### Tosfos: Kavod Hamet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו, וְאֵין מְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו, וּפָטוּר מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִן הַתְּפִלָּה וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין וּמִכׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there is no need for others **to recite a blessing** beforehand **on his behalf, nor do others invite him** to join in Grace after Meals, as he cannot be a member of the quorum of three required to recite the formula. **He is exempt from the recitation of *Shema*, from** the *Amida* **prayer and from phylacteries, and from all mitzvot mentioned in the Torah.**

Tosfos, drawing on the *Yerushalmi*, rejects Rashi's framework entirely. The exemption, Tosfos argues, is not *osek b'mitzvah* but *kavod hamet* — the honor owed to the dead. To turn one's attention to anything else while responsible for an unburied relative is itself a form of disrespect to the *mes*. This is why the exemption is more thoroughgoing than any ordinary case of *osek b'mitzvah*: it is not simply that one's hands are occupied with a *mitzvah*, but that the very act of engaging in other religious performances would constitute a turning away from one's sole obligation in that moment.

A significant practical consequence follows. Rashi holds that reciting *berachos* before and after eating is optional for the *onain*. Tosfos, by contrast, rules that it is actually *forbidden* — not merely unnecessary. Turning one's attention to a *beracha* is itself a departure from the required focus on the *mes*, and therefore the prohibition is active, not merely waived. This is the position we rule like, and it goes considerably beyond anything the category of *osek b'mitzvah* would generate.

### Rav Soloveitchik

Even within the framework of *kavod hamet*, a question remains. What exactly is the disrespect in reciting a *beracha* before eating, especially when one is in another room and the *mes* is not literally before one's eyes? The *kavod* concern alone does not fully explain the depth of the exemption.

Rav Soloveitchik offered a profound philosophical reading that addresses this directly. He argued that the issue is not about attention allocation or the appearance of disrespect, but about the appropriate psychological and religious state of a person who has just experienced an immediate loss. Conscious engagement in *mitzvos*, he explained, is a life-affirming act — a statement about meaning, purpose, and the significance of one's actions before God. To engage deliberately in *mitzvos* is to assert that life is purposeful.

But an *onain* is a person who may, at that very moment, be in a state of existential questioning — questioning God's justice, questioning whether anything makes sense, whether there is meaning in what has just happened. To demand that such a person immediately perform life-affirming religious acts would be a kind of violation of the authentic inner state that the situation calls for. The *halacha* therefore permits — and perhaps even requires — the *onain* to remain in that state of shock and questioning, rather than moving prematurely into affirmation. It is only after burial that the transition can begin. Even then, the mourner is still exempt from *talmud Torah* and from *tefillin* in the early period of *aveilus*, reflecting a gradual return toward the life-affirming mode. Rav Soloveitchik's reading thus explains why the *onain*'s exemption is more radical than any standard category — it is rooted in a halakhic recognition of the human condition at its most vulnerable moment.

## Met Mutal Lefanav

### Rabbeinu Tam's Sister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — אִין, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — לָא. וּרְמִינְהִי: מִי שֶׁמֵּתוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — אוֹכֵל בְּבַיִת אַחֵר...כְּמוּטָל לְפָנָיו דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that one whose deceased relative is laid out before him is exempt from the recitation of *Shema* and other positive mitzvot. The Gemara deduces: When the corpse is **laid out before him, yes,** he is exempt, but when the corpse is **not** physically **laid out before him, no,** he is not exempt from these mitzvot. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **One whose deceased** relative **is laid out before him eats in another room...** **Since it is incumbent upon him to bury him,** it is **as he is laid out before him.**

Two additional halachic points require attention before proceeding to the *Gemara*'s main discussion. The first concerns the definition of *met mutal lefanav*. The *Gemara* clarifies that the *onain*'s obligation need not rest on a *mes* literally lying before him; it suffices that it be *mutal lekovro* — resting upon him in the sense that he bears responsibility for the burial. Tosfos raises the practical question of what happens when one is in a different town and another relative is already handling all the arrangements. Is that still *mutal lekovro*?

Tosfos recounts that when Rabbeinu Tam's sister died and Rabbeinu Tam was in a different city, he did not treat himself as an *onain*. His reasoning was that his sister was married, her husband bore the primary legal responsibility for her burial, and therefore Rabbeinu Tam's own obligation was not triggered. It is possible that Rabbeinu Tam's conclusion rested not merely on practical considerations about who was arranging the burial, but on a halakhic assumption that a spouse holds primary responsibility — analogous to the *kohen*'s laws of *tumah*, where the obligation concerning a married sister (*achoto ha-betulah asher lo haytah l'ish*) is qualified by her status. Whether the same reasoning would apply if an unmarried sibling died with another sibling present and a third sibling absent remains unclear, and Tosfos does not elaborate.

### Maharam Rothenburg

Tosfos also raises a related difficulty that was tragically relevant in the period of the *Ba'alei haTosafot*: what if one has no access to the body at all? The *Maharam mi-Rothenburg* was famously imprisoned and died in captivity, his body held hostage as ransom. In such a case, where the body exists but cannot be reached or buried, at what point does a relative assume *onain* status? Tosfos raises these questions without fully resolving them, recognizing that the circumstances of the time made them practically urgent.

### Modern Rulings

Given that, as established above, the *onain* exemption is rooted more in the psychological and emotional state of the mourner than in the formal category of *osek b'mitzvah*, the practical tendency in contemporary rulings is toward a broad definition of *mutal lekovro*. The key exception is the combination of two factors: being out of the country and not attending the funeral, with someone else handling all the arrangements entirely. In that case, the person may be sufficiently removed from the situation that a different mode applies. But if one is traveling to attend the funeral, one is generally considered an *onain* even if another relative is managing the preparations locally, since this person remains meaningfully involved.

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשַׁבָּת מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמְבָרֵךְ וּמְזַמֵּן, וּמְבָרְכִין עָלָיו וּמְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו, וְחַיָּיב בְּכָל הַמִּצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב בְּאֵלּוּ — נִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ — תַּשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **On Shabbat,** however, **he reclines** at the meal as per his custom, **and eats meat and drinks wine, and recites blessings and** recites the formula to **invite** the participants in the meal to join together in the Grace after Meals, **and others may recite blessings on his behalf and invite him** to join in Grace after Meals, **and he is obligated in all mitzvot mentioned in the Torah. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Just as he is obligated** on Shabbat **to** fulfill **these** mitzvot associated with Shabbat meals, **he is obligated to** fulfill **all** mitzvot. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is** the practical difference **between them** the apparently identical statements of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the first *tanna*? **The** practical difference **between them is** with regard to **conjugal relations.**

The *Gemara* records a debate concerning Rabban Gamliel and the status of an *onain* on *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* suggests that *tashmish hamita* — marital relations — is normally forbidden for an *onain*, a ruling that is itself surprising. The exemptions associated with *aninut* have generally focused on the suspension of positive religious obligations; we have not previously seen any indication that the *onain* is forbidden from eating meat and wine, or from marital relations. This is not anywhere stated in the *Gemara* as a routine restriction.

### Onain and Aveilus

This opens a broader debate among the *Rishonim* about the relationship between *aninut* and *aveilus*. A number of *Rishonim* understand these as overlapping states — the *onain* is in some sense already in a preliminary form of mourning, and most of the laws that apply to an *avel* (bathing, laundering, marital relations, and so on) apply to the *onain* as well. Many other *Rishonim* dispute this, and the *Shulchan Aruch* does not codify additional restrictions for the *onain* beyond the marital question. The *Rema*, however, does include these restrictions. The practical reality is that rabbis today do not typically communicate this list of additional prohibitions to bereaved families, making this a significant gap between the *Rema*'s position and common practice.

## Cemetery Encounter

### The Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי וְאָזְלִי בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת. הֲוָה קָשַׁדְיָא תְּכֵלְתָּא דְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: דַּלְיַיהּ, כְּדַי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ: לְמָחָר בָּאִין אֶצְלֵנוּ, וְעַכְשָׁיו מְחָרְפִין אוֹתָנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Yonatan were walking in a cemetery and the sky-blue string** of **Rabbi Yonatan's** ritual fringes **was cast** to the ground and dragging across the graves. **Rabbi Ḥiyya said** to him: **Lift it, so** the dead **will not say: Tomorrow,** when their day comes, they will **come to** be buried with **us, and now** they are **insulting us.**

We now turn to what will be the major topic of the daf — not *halacha* but a series of striking *aggadic* passages about the dead and their awareness of the living world. The relevant *Gemara* begins near the bottom of 18a.

Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Yonatan were walking through a cemetery. Rabbi Yonatan's *tzitzis* — referred to by the *Gemara* as *tcheiles* — was trailing out of his garments, dragging across the graves. Rabbi Chiyya said to him: lift them up, so that the dead should not say, "tomorrow they are going to come and join us, and now they are mocking us" — displaying their *mitzvos* before us while we cannot fulfill them.

Rabbi Yonatan replied: do they know that much? And he invoked the verse from *Kohelet*: *ve-ha-mesim einam yod'im me'umah* — "the dead know nothing."

### Kohelet Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם קָרִיתָ — לֹא שָׁנִיתָ. אִם שָׁנִיתָ — לֹא שִׁלַּשְׁתָּ. אִם שִׁלַּשְׁתָּ — לֹא פֵּירְשׁוּ לְךָ. ״כִּי הַחַיִּים יוֹדְעִים שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ״ — אֵלּוּ צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּמִיתָתָן נִקְרְאוּ חַיִּים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶן יְהוֹיָדָע בֶּן אִישׁ חַי רַב פְּעָלִים מִקַּבְצְאֵל הוּא הִכָּה אֵת שְׁנֵי אֲרִאֵל מוֹאָב וְהוּא יָרַד וְהִכָּה אֶת הָאֲרִי בְּתוֹךְ הַבּוֹר בְּיוֹם הַשָּׁלֶג״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: If you read** the verse, **you did not** read it **a second time, and if you** read it **a second time, you did not** read it **a third time, and if you** read it **a third time, they did not explain it to you** properly. The meaning of the verse: "For the living know that they will die, and the dead know nothing and have no more reward, for their memory has been forgotten" (Ecclesiastes 9:5): **For the living know that they will die, these are the righteous, who even in their death are called living.** An allusion to this is **as it is stated: "And Benayahu, son of Yehoyada, son of a valiant man of Kabze'el, who had done mighty deeds, he smote the two altar-hearths of Moab; he went down also and slew a lion in the midst of a pit in time of snow"** (II Samuel 23:20).

Rabbi Chiyya's response is pointed. If you have learned *Torah* once without truly absorbing it, twice without fixing it, three times without someone having explained it to you — then you have missed the meaning of the very verse you are quoting. The verse does not mean what you think it means.

*Ki ha-chayyim yod'im she-yamutu* — "the living know that they will die." And *ve-ha-mesim einam yod'im me'umah* — "the dead know nothing." What does "the living" mean here?

### Tzaddikim vs. Reshaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֶּן אִישׁ חַי״: אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּנֵי מֵתֵי נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא: ״בֶּן אִישׁ חַי״ — שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּמִיתָתוֹ קָרוּי חַי. ״רַב פְּעָלִים מִקַּבְצְאֵל״ — שֶׁרִיבָּה וְקִבֵּץ פּוֹעֲלִים לַתּוֹרָה. ״וְהוּא הִכָּה אֵת שְׁנֵי אֲרִאֵל מוֹאָב״ — שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחַ כְּמוֹתוֹ לֹא בְּמִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי. ״וְהוּא יָרַד וְהִכָּה אֶת הָאֲרִי בְּתוֹךְ הַבּוֹר בְּיוֹם הַשָּׁלֶג״, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דְּתַבַּר גְּזִיזֵי דְבַרְדָּא, וּנְחַת וּטְבַל. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דִּתְנָא סִיפְרָא דְבֵי רַב בְּיוֹמָא דְסִיתְוָא. ״וְהַמֵּתִים אֵינָם יוֹדְעִים מְאוּמָה״ — אֵלּוּ רְשָׁעִים, שֶׁבְּחַיֵּיהֶן קְרוּיִין ״מֵתִים״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה חָלָל רָשָׁע נְשִׂיא יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** He was referred to in the verse as **son of a living man.** The Gemara wonders: **Is that to say,** the fact that the Bible referred to him with that appellation, **that all others are children of the dead? Rather,** the verse should be explained as follows: **The son of a living man** who lives forever, **who even in death is referred to as living. Man of Kabze'el who had done mighty deeds, as he accumulated and gathered many workers for** the sake of the **Torah. Who killed the two lion-hearted men [*Ariel*] of Moab, as** after his death **he left no one his equal, in either the First Temple or the Second Temple** periods, as the Temple is called *Ariel* (see Isaiah 29:1), and the two *Ariel* refers to the two Temples. The Sages disagreed over the interpretation of the rest of the verse: **"And who descended and slew the lion in the pit on the snowy day." Some say** that this means **that he broke blocks of hail and descended and immersed himself** in the water to purify himself. **Others say that he learned** all of **the *Sifra*,** the *halakhic* midrash on the book of Leviticus **of the school of Rav,** on a **winter's day.** In contrast to the righteous, who are referred to as living even after their death, the verse states explicitly: **"The dead know nothing." These are the wicked, who** even **during their lives are called dead, as** the prophet Ezekiel **said** in reference to a king of Israel who was alive: **"And you are a slain, wicked prince of Israel"** (Ezekiel 21:30).

*Elu tzaddikim* — these are the righteous, who *she-ve-mitatan nikre'u chayyim* — even in their death are called alive. And "the dead"? *Elu reshaim* — these are the wicked, who *she-be-chayyeihem keru'im mesim* — even while alive are called dead. They walk through the world oblivious, unaware of the meaning of their existence, like the walking dead.

The proof that the righteous are called alive even after death comes from the verse about *Bnayahu ben Yehoyada*: *ben ish chai* — the son of a living man. This raises the obvious question: is not everyone the son of a living man? The *Gemara* therefore derives from here that this is a special designation — one who even in death is still called alive.

### Ben Ish Chai

The verse describes Bnayahu ben Yehoyada as *rav pe'alim mi-Kabtze'el* — a man of great deeds from Kabtze'el. This is interpreted as meaning he *ribba ve-kibbetz po'alim la-Torah* — he gathered many workers for the study of *Torah*. *Hu hikka es shnei Ariel Moav* — he left no one like himself in terms of *Torah* achievement, neither in the period of the first *Beit ha-Mikdash* nor the second, as the word *Ariel* is associated with the altar and the Temple, particularly in *Yechezkel*. Rashi also notes an association with *Moav* and King David, who descends from *Rut ha-Moaviyah*.

*Ve-hu yarad ve-hikka es ha-ari be-toch ha-bor be-yom ha-sheleg* — he descended and struck the lion in the pit on the day of the snow. One explanation is that he broke the ice and immersed in the freezing *mikvah* waters — the *Gemara* noting this as a mark of his extraordinary piety. Another explanation offered by some: *de-tana Sifra Devei Rav be-yoma de-sitva* — he studied the *Sifra*, the Tannaitic commentary on *Vayikra* edited by Rav, on a winter's day. The *Sifra* is considered among the most demanding of Tannaitic works, particularly its material on *Kodshin*, and studying it is itself compared to breaking ice.

Returning to the central argument: the verse *ve-ha-mesim einam yod'im me'umah* does not mean the literally dead are unaware. It means the *reshaim* — those who are oblivious to their responsibilities — are, even while living, considered dead. The *reshaim* are called *mes* while still alive: *ve-ata chalal rasha nesi Yisrael* — "you are a corpse, you wicked prince of Israel." And the verse *yummat ha-mes* — "the dead shall be put to death" — the *Gemara* asks, can the dead be put to death? Rather, the person is called *mes* already in his lifetime because of his wickedness.

The argument therefore is: you cannot prove from that verse that the literally dead lack awareness. The verse is speaking about the *reshaim* of the living and the *tzaddikim* of the living; it says nothing definitive about whether the actually deceased know what happens in this world. That question remains open, and the *Gemara* will now pursue it through a series of narratives.

One point worth noting about the category of *reshaim* as used here: it seems to be a remarkably broad designation, encompassing anyone who passes through life mindlessly, without the conscious awareness of meaning that the *tzaddik* maintains. This is not a narrow category of evildoers. The *tzaddikim* are described as the exception — those who cultivate an active awareness of their mortality and therefore live purposefully. Everyone else falls broadly into the category of *reshaim* in the sense of the oblivious. This reading connects meaningfully to the point raised earlier about Rav Soloveitchik — that the conscious engagement in *mitzvos* is itself a statement about the meaning of existence.

## Dead Communication

### Sons of Rabbi Chiyya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא נְפוּק לְקִרְיָיתָא. אִייַּקַּר לְהוּ תַּלְמוּדַיְיהוּ. הֲווֹ קָא מִצַּעֲרִי לְאִדְּכוֹרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ חַד לְחַבְרֵיהּ: יָדַע אֲבוּן בְּהַאי צַעֲרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: מְנָא יָדַע? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״יִכְבְּדוּ בָנָיו וְלֹא יֵדָע״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: וְלָא יָדַע? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״אַךְ בְּשָׂרוֹ עָלָיו יִכְאָב וְנַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו תֶּאֱבָל״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: קָשָׁה רִמָּה לַמֵּת כְּמַחַט בַּבָּשָׂר הַחַי! אָמְרִי: בְּצַעֲרָא דִידְהוּ — יָדְעִי, בְּצַעֲרָא דְאַחֲרִינָא — לָא יָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a story on this topic: **The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya went out to the villages** to oversee the laborers. **They forgot what they had learned and were struggling to recall it. One** of them **said to the other:** Does **our** deceased **father know of** our **anguish? The other said to him: From where would he know? Isn't it written: "His sons are honored yet he shall not know it,** they come to sorrow and he shall not understand them" (Job 14:21)? The dead do not know. **The other said** back **to him: And** do the dead truly **not know? Isn't it written: "Only in his flesh does he feel pain, in his soul does he mourn"** (Job 14:22)? Based on this verse **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** Gnawing **maggots are as excruciating to the dead as** the stab of **a needle to the flesh of the living.** The dead must have the capacity to feel and know. **In order to reconcile this contradiction they said:** They **know of their own pain but do not know of the pain of others.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a series of stories aimed at testing whether the dead in fact know what is happening in this world. The discussion begins at 18b.

The sons of Rabbi Chiyya went out to the villages to attend to their investments and property. As a result, their learning became heavy upon them — *aikru lehu talmudaihu* — and they began to forget what they had learned, struggling to recall their *Torah*. One said to his fellow: does our father — our deceased father — know of the suffering we are going through in struggling to remember? His companion replied: how could he know? The verse says *yichbedu vanav ve-lo yeda* — "his sons will be honored and he will not know" — and by a play on the word *yichbedu* (weighted down, like their learning had become heavy upon them), the verse seems directly applicable: the father does not know.

The first son replies: is that so certain? The verse also says *ach besaro alav yich'av ve-nafsho alav te'eval* — "his flesh will pain him and his soul will mourn for him." And Rabbi Yitzchak explains: what is meant by "his flesh will pain him"? The answer is *kasha rima l'mes k'machat bivsar ha-chai* — the worms that consume the corpse are as painful to the dead as a needle piercing living flesh. So the dead are aware of what happens to their own bodies.

To this the counter is offered: *b'tzara d'idhu yad'ei* — they are aware of afflictions happening to themselves, *b'tzara d'achrina lo yad'ei* — but they are not aware of what is happening to others. This remains unresolved: one cannot prove from the verse about the *rima* that the dead have broader awareness, but neither has it been disproven.

### Two Spirits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד שֶׁנָּתַן דִּינָר לְעָנִי בְּעֶרֶב רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, וְהִקְנִיטַתּוּ אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְהָלַךְ וְלָן בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת. וְשָׁמַע שְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת שֶׁמְסַפְּרוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ. אָמְרָה חֲדָא לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, בּוֹאִי וְנָשׁוּט בָּעוֹלָם, וְנִשְׁמַע מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַפַּרְגּוֹד מַה פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּא לָעוֹלָם? אָמְרָה לָהּ חֲבֶרְתָּהּ: אֵינִי יְכוֹלָה, שֶׁאֲנִי קְבוּרָה בְּמַחְצֶלֶת שֶׁל קָנִים. אֶלָּא לְכִי אַתְּ, וּמַה שֶּׁאַתְּ שׁוֹמַעַת אִמְרִי לִי. הָלְכָה הִיא וְשָׁטָה וּבָאָה. וְאָמְרָה לָהּ חֲבֶרְתָּהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, מַה שָּׁמַעְתְּ מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַפַּרְגּוֹד? אָמְרָה לָהּ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁכָּל הַזּוֹרֵעַ בִּרְבִיעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה בָּרָד מַלְקֶה אוֹתוֹ. הָלַךְ הוּא וְזָרַע בִּרְבִיעָה שְׁנִיָּה. שֶׁל כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לָקָה, שֶׁלּוֹ — לֹא לָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **And** is it so that the dead do **not** know of the pain of others? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving a pious man who gave a poor man a dinar on the eve of Rosh HaShana during drought years, and his wife mocked him** for giving so large a sum at so difficult a time? **And** in order to escape her incessant mockery, **he went and slept in the cemetery.** That night in his dream (Ritva, *HaKotev*, Maharsha), **he heard two spirits conversing with each other. One said to the other: My friend, let us roam the world and hear from behind the heavenly curtain [*pargod*],** which separates the Divine Presence from the world, **what calamity will befall the world. The other spirit said to her:** I **cannot** go with you, **as I am buried in a mat of reeds, but you go, and tell me what you hear. She went, and roamed, and came** back. **The other spirit said: My friend, what did you hear from behind the heavenly curtain? She replied: I heard that anyone who sows during the first rainy season** of this year, **hail** will fall and **strike his** crops. Hearing this, the pious man **went and sowed** his seeds **during the second rainy season.** Ultimately, the crops of **the entire world were stricken** by hail **and his** crops **were not stricken.**

The *Gemara* now brings the famous story of the *chasid* and the two spirits. A certain righteous man gave a *dinar* — a substantial sum — to a poor person on *erev Rosh Hashanah*, during years of famine. His wife was furious. He went and spent the night in the cemetery. He heard two spirits conversing with one another.

One said to the other: my friend, *chaverti*, let us fly about the world and hear from behind the *paroged* — the curtain that divides the divine counsel from the world — what afflictions will come upon the world this year. The *paroged* represents the place where the heavenly decisions for the year, made on *Rosh Hashanah*, are being discussed and determined. Her companion replied that she could not come, for she was buried in a mat of reeds — *machatzelet shel kanim* — which apparently bound her spirit, preventing her from flying freely. This detail likely reflects both shame (being buried without proper burial provisions) and a kind of magical constraint associated with an inadequate burial.

The free spirit went alone, flew about, and returned. She told her companion: I heard that whoever plants in the first rains will be smitten by hail. The *chasid* overheard this. He went and planted only in the second rain, and when the hail came and smote everyone else's crops, his were spared.

### Righteous Man

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לַשָּׁנָה הָאַחֶרֶת הָלַךְ וְלָן בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת וְשָׁמַע אוֹתָן שְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת שֶׁמְסַפְּרוֹת זוֹ עִם זוֹ. אָמְרָה לָהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, לֹא כָּךְ אָמַרְתִּי לָךְ, אֵינִי יְכוֹלָה שֶׁאֲנִי קְבוּרָה בְּמַחְצֶלֶת שֶׁל קָנִים?! אֶלָּא לְכִי אַתְּ, וּמַה שֶּׁאַתְּ שׁוֹמַעַת בּוֹאִי וְאִמְרִי לִי. הָלְכָה וְשָׁטָה וּבָאָה. וְאָמְרָה לָהּ חֲבֶרְתָּהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, מַה שָּׁמַעְתְּ מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַפַּרְגּוֹד? אָמְרָה לָהּ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁכָּל הַזּוֹרֵעַ בִּרְבִיעָה שְׁנִיָּה שִׁדָּפוֹן מַלְקֶה אוֹתוֹ. הָלַךְ וְזָרַע בִּרְבִיעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁל כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ נִשְׁדַּף וְשֶׁלּוֹ לֹא נִשְׁדַּף. אָמְרָה לוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֶשְׁתָּקַד שֶׁל כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לָקָה וְשֶׁלְּךָ לֹא לָקָה, וְעַכְשָׁיו שֶׁל כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ נִשְׁדַּף וְשֶׁלְּךָ לֹא נִשְׁדַּף? סָח לָהּ כָּל הַדְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ. אָמְרוּ: לֹא הָיוּ יָמִים מוּעָטִים עַד שֶׁנָּפְלָה קְטָטָה בֵּין אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ חָסִיד וּבֵין אִמָּהּ שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ רִיבָה. אָמְרָה לָהּ: לְכִי וְאַרְאֵךְ בִּתֵּךְ שֶׁהִיא קְבוּרָה בְּמַחְצֶלֶת שֶׁל קָנִים. לַשָּׁנָה הָאַחֶרֶת הָלַךְ וְלָן בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת וְשָׁמַע אוֹתָן רוּחוֹת שֶׁמְסַפְּרוֹת זוֹ עִם זוֹ. אָמְרָה לָהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, בּוֹאִי וְנָשׁוּט בָּעוֹלָם וְנִשְׁמַע מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַפַּרְגּוֹד מַה פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּא לָעוֹלָם. אָמְרָה לָהּ: חֲבֶרְתִּי, הֲנִיחִינִי, דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינִי לְבֵינֵךְ כְּבָר נִשְׁמְעוּ בֵּין הַחַיִּים. אַלְמָא יָדְעִי. דִּילְמָא אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא שָׁכֵיב, וְאָזֵיל וְאָמַר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The following year,** on the eve of Rosh HaShana, the same pious man **went and slept in the cemetery** at his own initiative, and again **he heard the two spirits conversing with each other. One said to the other: Let us roam the world and hear from behind the heavenly curtain what calamity will befall the world. She said to her: My friend, have I not** already **told you** that **I cannot, as I am buried in a mat of reeds? Rather, you go, and tell me what you hear. She went, and roamed, and returned. The other** spirit **said to her: My friend, what did you hear from behind the curtain? She said to her: I heard that those who sow during the second rainy season blight will strike his** crops. That pious man **went and sowed during the first rainy season.** Since everyone else sowed during the second rainy season, ultimately, the crops **of the entire world were blighted and his** crops **were not blighted.** The pious man's **wife said to him: Why is it that last year,** the crops **of the entire world were stricken and yours were not stricken, and now** this year, the crops **of the entire world were blighted and yours were not blighted? He related to her the entire story. They said: It was not** even **a few days** later that **a quarrel fell between the pious man's wife and the mother of the young woman** who was buried there. The pious man's wife **said to her** scornfully: **Go and I will show you your daughter,** and you will see that **she is buried in a mat of reeds.** **The following year, he** again **went and slept in the cemetery, and heard the same spirits conversing with each other. One said** to the other: **My friend, let us roam the world and hear from behind the heavenly curtain what calamity will befall the world. She said to her: My friend, leave me** alone, as **words that** we have privately exchanged **between us have already been heard among the living. Apparently,** the dead **know** what transpires in this world. The Gemara responds: This is no proof; **perhaps another person,** who heard about the conversation of the spirits secondhand, **died and** he **went and told them** that they had been overheard.

The following year, the *chasid* returned to the cemetery and slept there again. He heard the same two spirits. The free one again flew and returned with word from behind the *paroged*: this year, whoever plants in the second rains will be smitten by a harsh wind — *shidafon*. He went and planted in the first rain. His crops were spared while everyone else's were ruined.

His wife, now twice having observed that her husband had uncannily anticipated which planting season to use, pressed him to explain. He recounted the entire story of the spirits. Shortly thereafter, a quarrel broke out between the wife of this *chasid* and the mother of the girl who had been buried in the mat of reeds. The wife, in an act of cruelty, said to the mother: come, I'll show you that your daughter is buried in a mat of reeds — evidently a public shaming, implying either that the mother was unaware of how her daughter had been buried, or that the *chasid*'s wife wished to humiliate her.

The following year, undeterred, the *chasid* went again and slept in the cemetery. He heard the same spirits. This time, however, when the free spirit proposed their annual foray, the spirit in the mat of reeds said: leave me alone. *Devarim she-beini le-veinech nishmeu bein ha-chayyim* — things spoken between us have been heard among the living.

What is striking here is the nature of what triggered the spirit's awareness. It was not, apparently, the *chasid* telling his wife — it was the subsequent fight, when word of the mat of reeds reached the dead girl's mother. Whether this means the spirits have access to everything said in the world, or only to things directly relevant to them, remains ambiguous.

The *Gemara* draws the obvious conclusion: *alma yad'ei* — so you see they know what is happening in the world. But the *Gemara* immediately deflects: *dilma inish achrina shachiv ve-azil ve-amar lehu* — perhaps in the interim someone had died, and that newly departed spirit had brought word of what had been happening among the living. The dead may not have direct access; they may simply be informed by the newly arrived. The story thus fails to decisively prove that the dead possess independent knowledge of the world.

### Zeiri's Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דִּזְעֵירִי הֲוָה מַפְקֵיד זוּזֵי גַּבֵּי אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָתֵיהּ. עַד דְּאָתֵי וְאָזֵיל לְבֵי רַב, שְׁכִיבָה. אֲזַל בָּתְרַהּ לַחֲצַר מָוֶת, אֲמַר לַהּ: זוּזֵי הֵיכָא? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִיל שַׁקְלִינְהוּ מִתּוּתֵי צִנּוֹרָא דְּדָשָׁא בְּדוּךְ פְּלָן, וְאֵימָא לַהּ לְאִימָּא, תְּשַׁדַּר לִי מַסְרְקַאי וְגוּבְתַּאי דְּכוּחְלָא בַּהֲדֵי פְּלָנִיתָא דְּאָתְיָא לִמְחַר. אַלְמָא יָדְעִי! דִּלְמָא דּוּמָה קָדֵים וּמַכְרֵיז לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the deceased's knowledge of what transpires, **come** and **hear** a proof, as it is told: **Ze'iri would deposit** his **dinars with his innkeeper. While he was going and coming to** and from **the school of Rav, she died,** and he did not know where she had put the money. **So he went after her to** her grave in **the cemetery and said to her: Where are the dinars? She replied: Go and get them from beneath the hinge of the door in such and such a place, and tell my mother** that **she should send me my comb and a tube of eyeshadow with such and such a woman who will** die **and come here tomorrow. Apparently,** the dead **know** what transpires in this world. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Perhaps** the angel **Duma,** who oversees the dead, **comes beforehand and announces to them** that a particular individual will arrive the next day, but they themselves do not know.

Another story is brought. Zeiri had left money with his innkeeper for safekeeping. By the time he returned from the *yeshiva* of Rav, the innkeeper had died. He went to the grave site and addressed her: *zuzei heicha* — where is my money? She told him: go take it from beneath the door hinge in such-and-such a place. And then — by the way, she added — tell my mother to send me my haircomb and my eye-shadow applicator, together with so-and-so who is going to die tomorrow. That, evidently, is how you get items delivered to the dead: someone who is about to die takes them along.

The narrative detail about the personal items for the grave connects to a broader discussion in *Masechet Smachot*, which warns against the practice of placing personal articles into the grave — a practice that draws on deeply rooted beliefs about the dead continuing to need their possessions in the afterlife. The resonance with Egyptian burial customs is apparent.

What the *Gemara* takes from this story for its dialectic is the detail that the innkeeper knew who was going to die the next day. *Alma yad'ei* — so they have knowledge. But again, the *Gemara* responds: *dilma Duma kadim u-machrezei* — perhaps Dumah, the angel appointed over the dead, had already announced in heaven who would be dying tomorrow, and the spirit's knowledge derives from that heavenly announcement, not from any direct awareness of the living world.

## Yochanan's Reversal

### Dumah's Role

Rashi identifies Dumah as *malach she-hu memuneh al ha-mesim* — the angel appointed over the dead. The name appears in a different sense in *Tehillim* (*lo kol yordei dumah*), but the *Gemara* here uses it as a proper name for a personalized angelic figure, which is characteristic of the Babylonian Talmud's angelology — a development that, as noted, reflects a Persian influence, with personal names for angels entering the tradition from *Bavel*.

The upshot of all these stories, taken together, is that no proof has yet been brought that the dead directly perceive what happens in the living world. Each narrative admits of an alternative explanation: perhaps the dead learn from recently deceased intermediaries, or from heavenly announcements made by Dumah. This sustained skepticism is itself notable — what one finds surprising is not that the *Gemara* affirms belief in the dead's awareness, but that it so persistently questions it.

## Shmuel's Father

### Shmuel's Search

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הֲווֹ קָא מַפְקְדִי גַּבֵּיהּ זוּזֵי דְיַתְמֵי. כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ לָא הֲוָה שְׁמוּאֵל גַּבֵּיהּ. הֲווֹ קָא קָרוּ לֵיהּ: ״בַּר אָכֵיל זוּזֵי דְיַתְמֵי״. אֲזַל אַבָּתְרֵיהּ לַחֲצַר מָוֶת. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בָּעֵינָא אַבָּא! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַבָּא טוּבָא אִיכָּא הָכָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בָּעֵינָא אַבָּא בַּר אַבָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַבָּא בַּר אַבָּא נָמֵי טוּבָא אִיכָּא הָכָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בָּעֵינָא אַבָּא בַּר אַבָּא אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הֵיכָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ סְלֵיק לִמְתִיבְתָּא דִּרְקִיעָא. אַדְּהָכִי חַזְיֵיהּ לְלֵוִי דְּיָתֵיב אַבָּרַאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי יָתְבַתְּ אַבָּרַאי? מַאי טַעְמָא לָא סָלְקַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְאָמְרִי לִי: כָּל כִּי הָנָךְ שְׁנֵי דְּלָא סְלֵיקְתְּ לִמְתִיבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אַפָּס וְאַחְלֵישְׁתֵּיהּ לְדַעְתֵּיהּ, לָא מְעַיְּילִינַן לָךְ לִמְתִיבְתָּא דִרְקִיעָא. אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אֲתָא אֲבוּהּ, חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא בָכֵי וְאַחֵיךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא קָא בָכֵית? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּלַעֲגָל קָא אָתֵית. מַאי טַעְמָא אַחֵיכְתְּ? דַּחֲשִׁיבַתְּ בְּהַאי עָלְמָא טוּבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי חֲשִׁיבְנָא — נְעַיְּילוּהּ לְלֵוִי. וְעַיְּילוּהוּ לְלֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another proof: **Come** and **hear,** as it is told: **They would deposit the money of orphans with Shmuel's father** for safekeeping. **When Shmuel's father died, Shmuel was not with him,** and did not learn from him the location of the money. Since he did not return it, Shmuel was called: **Son of** him who **consumes the money of orphans.** Shmuel **went after** his father **to the cemetery and said to** the dead: **I want Abba.** The dead **said to him: There are many Abbas here. He told them: I want Abba bar Abba. They said** to him: **There are also many people named Abba bar Abba here. He told them: I want Abba bar Abba, the father of Shmuel. Where** is he? **They replied: Ascend to the yeshiva on high. Meanwhile, he saw** his friend **Levi sitting outside** the yeshiva, away from the rest of the deceased. **He asked him: Why do you sit outside? Why did you not ascend** to the yeshiva? **He replied: Because they tell me** that for **all those years** that **you didn't enter the yeshiva of Rabbi Afes, and** thereby **upset him, we will not grant you entry to the yeshiva on high.** **Meanwhile,** Shmuel's **father came** and Shmuel **saw that he was crying and laughing.** Shmuel **said to** his father: **Why are you crying?** His father **replied:** Because **you will come here soon.** Shmuel continued and asked: **Why are you laughing?** His father replied: Because **you are extremely important in this world.** Shmuel **said to him: If I am important, then let them grant Levi entry** to the yeshiva. **And** so it was **that they granted Levi entry** to the yeshiva.

The *Gemara* brings one final story bearing on this question. Shmuel's father had been entrusted with the money of orphans for safekeeping. When the father died, Shmuel was not present, and the father had no opportunity to inform him where the money was held. As a result, people began calling Shmuel *bar achil zuzei deyatmi* — the son of the one who consumes the money of orphans — impugning both Shmuel's reputation and his father's.

Shmuel went to the gravesite. He asked the assembled spirits for *Aba* — his father, whose personal name was indeed Aba. The spirits mocked him: we have many *Abas* buried here. He specified: *Aba bar Aba*. Still they mocked: we have many of those too. He finally identified his father fully: *Aba bar Aba Avuhu deShmuel*.

### Heavenly Yeshiva

He was told that his father had ascended to the *metivta de-rakiya* — the heavenly *yeshiva*. In the meantime, he noticed the spirit of Levi sitting outside the main group in the cemetery. He asked Levi: why are you sitting outside? Why have you not ascended to the heavenly *yeshiva*? Levi explained: all those years during which you did not attend the *yeshiva* of Rabbi Afis — and thereby caused Rabbi Afis anguish through your absence — you will have to wait that same number of years before being admitted to the heavenly *yeshiva*.

Presently, Shmuel's father came down, and Shmuel observed that he was simultaneously crying and laughing. He asked why the crying: *de-la'agya ka'atit* — because soon you will be coming here, you will be dying. And why the laughing: *de-chashivat b'hai alma tuba* — because of the great honor that awaits you in the world of the afterlife.

### Orphan Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוּזֵי דְיַתְמֵי הֵיכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שַׁקְלִינְהוּ בְּאַמְתָא דְרִחְיָא. עִילָּאֵי וְתַתָּאֵי — דִּידַן, וּמִיצְעֵי דְּיַתְמֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַדְתְּ הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי גָּנְבִי גַּנָּבֵי — מִגַּנְבוּ מִדִּידַן. אִי אָכְלָה אַרְעָא — אָכְלָה מִדִּידַן. אַלְמָא דְּיָדְעִי! — דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲשִׁיב קָדְמִי וּמַכְרְזִי: פַּנּוּ מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Shmuel **said to** his father: **Where is the orphans' money? He said to him: Go** and **retrieve it from the millhouse,** where you will find **the uppermost and the lowermost** money **is ours, and** the money **in the middle** belongs to **the orphans.** Shmuel **said to him: Why did you do that? He replied: If thieves stole, they would steal from our** money on top, which the thief would see first. **If the earth swallowed up** any of it, it would swallow **from our** money, on the bottom. **Apparently,** the dead, in this case Shmuel's father, **know** when others will die. Since Shmuel did not die the next day, clearly the angel Duma could not have informed them (*Tosafot*). The Gemara responds: **Perhaps Shmuel is different,** and **because he is so important they announce beforehand: Clear place** for his arrival.

Shmuel then interceded for Levi — since I am so important, he asked, let Levi be admitted. And they brought Levi in. Then Shmuel turned to the matter at hand: *zuzei deyatmi heicha* — where is the orphans' money? His father told him: go take it from the hold of the millstone. The money on top and on the bottom belongs to us; the money in the middle belongs to the orphans. The reason for this arrangement: if thieves steal, they will steal from the outer layers first — ours — and the orphans' money is protected. If the earth consumes from below, it will consume ours first. The orphans' money is maximally protected in the middle.

This detailed account of where the money is hidden and how it is protected serves to vindicate the father's reputation entirely. As for the proof-value of the story: the father knew that Shmuel would soon be dying. The *Gemara* entertains the objection that perhaps Dumah had announced this — but the father speaks of death as imminent though not immediate, not "tomorrow," suggesting this was not a last-minute announcement. The *Gemara* counters: *dilma shani Shmuel keivan de-chashiv kadmu u-machrezei* — perhaps Shmuel is so important that the announcement of his coming was made far in advance, *panu makom* — make room — so that even his imminent death was proclaimed in heaven long before it happened.

## Yochanan's Reversal…

### Original Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן לַמֵּתִים שֶׁמְסַפְּרִים זֶה עִם זֶה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֵלָיו זֹאת הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי לְאַבְרָהָם לְיִצְחָק וּלְיַעֲקֹב לֵאמֹר״. מַאי ״לֵאמֹר״? — אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם לְאַבְרָהָם לְיִצְחָק וּלְיַעֲקֹב: שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּעְתִּי לָכֶם כְּבָר קִייַּמְתִּיהָ לִבְנֵיכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, with regard to the crux of the issue, **Rabbi Yonatan also reconsidered** his opinion, **as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** is it derived **that the dead converse with each other? As it is stated: "And the Lord said to him, this is the land that I swore to Abraham, Isaac and Jacob, saying:** I will give it to your offspring" (Deuteronomy 34:4). **What is** the meaning of **"saying"?** It means that **God told Moses: Go and tell Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob,** that **the oath that I swore to you I have already fulfilled for your descendants.**

Despite all these stories, no definitive proof has been brought that the dead possess direct knowledge of what transpires in the living world. Every narrative has admitted of an alternative explanation. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* now tells us that Rabbi Yochanan — who had apparently maintained that the dead know nothing at all — in the end reversed his position.

### Moshe's Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּלָא יָדְעִי, כִּי אָמַר לְהוּ מַאי הָוֵי? אֶלָּא מַאי — דְּיָדְעִי? לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר לְהוּ?! לְאַחְזוֹקֵי לֵיהּ טֵיבוּתָא לְמֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it should enter your mind that** the dead **do not know, then what of it if he tells them?** The Gemara rejects this: **Rather what** will you say, **that** they **know?** Then **why does he** need **to tell them?** The Gemara replies: This is not difficult, as he is telling them so that **they will give credit to Moses.**

The proof that convinced him comes from a verse. *De-amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yochanan*: from where do we know that the dead speak with one another? From the verse: *vayomer Hashem eilav zos ha-aretz asher nishbati le-Avraham le-Yitzchak u-le-Yaakov leimor* — "And God said to him: this is the land I swore to Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, saying." The word *leimor* ordinarily implies "to say [to someone else]." But Moshe is at the very moment of his death. To whom is he supposed to say this?

The *Gemara* answers: God is telling Moshe — go and tell Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov. *She-shvu'ah she-nishbati lachem kiyamtiha le-vaneichem* — the oath I swore to you, I have now fulfilled for your children. Moshe, who saw the Land with his own eyes, is to go and report this to the *avot*.

The objection arises: if the dead already know what is happening in the world, why would Moshe need to report this to the patriarchs? *Ei salka da'atach de-la yad'ei, ki amru lehu mai hava* — if they don't know anything, what is accomplished by telling them? The point is that they must have conscious awareness; otherwise the message is meaningless. Rashi notes that Rabbi Yochanan's original position was that the dead have no awareness whatsoever — *einam mevinim klum ela tza'ara de-gufa* — and this verse proves that they are in fact conscious beings capable of receiving and understanding information.

The *Gemara* responds that the reason Moshe is sent is *le-achzukei le-tivuta le-Moshe* — so that the *avot* should be able to express gratitude to Moshe, recognizing his role in bringing about the fulfillment of the promise. This addresses why Moshe specifically must report it, but does not undermine the underlying premise that the *avot* are conscious and communicative.

## Speaking of the Dead

### Stone Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַמְסַפֵּר אַחֲרֵי הַמֵּת, כְּאִלּוּ מְסַפֵּר אַחֲרֵי הָאֶבֶן. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי דְּלָא יָדְעִי, וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי דְּיָדְעִי וְלָא אִיכְפַּת לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** On this subject, **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Anyone who speaks** negatively **after the deceased it is as if** he **speaks after the stone.** The Gemara offers two interpretations of this: **Some say** this is because the dead **do not know, and some say** that **they know,** but **they do not care** that they are spoken of in such a manner.

The *Gemara* now closes with a series of related statements. Rabbi Yitzchak says: *kol ha-mesaper acharei ha-mes ke-ilu mesaper acharei ha-even* — whoever speaks about the dead, it is as if he is speaking about a stone. This seems to echo Rabbi Yochanan's original position.

### Awareness Question

Two interpretations are offered. Some say: *de-la yad'ei* — the dead are simply not aware of what is being said about them. Others say: *de-yad'ei ve-lo ichpas lehu* — they are aware, but they do not care. They are dead. Either way, on this view, speaking ill of the dead has no consequence for the dead themselves.

### Divine Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: חַד אִישְׁתַּעִי מִילְּתָא בָּתְרֵיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל וּנְפַל קַנְיָא מִטְּלָלָא וּבְזַעָא לְאַרְנְקָא דְמוֹחֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי צוֹרְבָא מֵרַבָּנָן, דְּקוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא תָּבַע בִּיקָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Pappa say:** There was once **someone who spoke** disparagingly **after** the death of **Mar Shmuel and a reed fell from the ceiling, fracturing his skull?** Obviously, the dead care when people speak ill of them. The Gemara rejects this: This is no proof that the dead care. Rather, a **Torah scholar is different, as God** Himself **demands** that **his honor** be upheld.

But then Rav Pappa reports: someone spoke disparagingly of Shmuel after his death. A beam fell from the ceiling and split his skull. Does that not suggest the dead do care? The *Gemara* responds: *shani tzurba m'rabanan* — a *talmid chacham* is different. *De-Kudsha Brich Hu tavei biy'karei* — God Himself defends the honor of Torah scholars. It is not that the dead are aware and react; it is that God actively protects the dignity of *talmidei chachamim*, even after their deaths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַמְסַפֵּר אַחַר מִטָּתָן שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַמַּטִּים עֲקַלְקַלּוֹתָם יוֹלִיכֵם ה׳ אֶת פּוֹעֲלֵי הָאָוֶן שָׁלוֹם עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **ben Levi said** similarly: **One who speaks** disparagingly **after the biers of Torah scholars** and maligns them after their death will **fall in Gehenna, as it is stated: "But those who turn aside unto their crooked ways, the Lord will lead them away with the workers of iniquity; peace be upon Israel"** (Psalms 125:5).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi extends this: *kol ha-mesaper acharei mitatan shel talmidei chachamim nofel b'Gehennom* — whoever speaks disparagingly after the *mita* of *talmidei chachamim* — their deeds or conduct — falls into *Gehennom*. The verse is cited: *ve-hamatim akalkalotam yolichem Hashem es po'alei ha-aven shalom al Yisrael* — those who weigh the deeds of the righteous and render a perverse verdict, God will lead those sinners — even when peace rests upon Israel, even at the moment of their own death — to their reckoning.

### Judging Favorably

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אִם רָאִיתָ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה בַּלַּיְלָה — אַל תְּהַרְהֵר אַחֲרָיו בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁמָּא עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה. ״שֶׁמָּא״ סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא וַדַּאי עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי — בִּדְבָרִים שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ. אֲבָל בְּמָמוֹנָא — עַד דְּמַהְדַּר לְמָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **it was taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: If you saw a** Torah **scholar transgress a prohibition at night, do not think** badly **of him during the day; perhaps he has repented** in the meantime. The Gemara challenges this: **Does it enter your mind** that only **perhaps** he has repented? Shouldn't he be given the benefit of the doubt? **Rather, he has certainly repented.** The Gemara notes: **The idea** that one must always give a Torah scholar the benefit of the doubt and assume that he has repented refers specifically to **matters** affecting **himself, but,** if one witnesses a Torah scholar committing a transgression **involving the property** of another, one is not required to give him the benefit of the doubt. Rather, he should not assume that he has repented **until** he sees him **return** the money to **its owner.**

The *Gemara* concludes with a ruling from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *im ra'ita talmid chacham she'avar aveira ba-layla al teharher acharav ba-yom* — if you saw a Torah scholar sin at night, do not think ill of him by day. Why? *Shema asa teshuva* — perhaps he repented. And the *Gemara* sharpens this: not "perhaps" — *ela vadai asa teshuva* — one may assume with certainty that he did.

This applies specifically, however, to *devarim she-begufo* — sins between man and God, matters of personal religious conduct. But *aval be-mamono* — in matters involving money, financial dealings with others — *ad de-mahadar l'marei* — one must wait until one actually sees the scholar reform his conduct before extending the benefit of the doubt. This distinction is important: *dan l'chaf zechut* applies classically to an ambiguous action, where we interpret a possible wrong favorably. Here, the case is one where something clearly wrong was observed, and the question is how we regard the person going forward. The *Gemara* rules that in matters between man and God, we assume *teshuva* has been done. But in financial matters, the concern for protecting others' interests means that charitable assumption must await demonstrated change.
