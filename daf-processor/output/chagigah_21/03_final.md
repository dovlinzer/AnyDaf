# Chagigah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten/Eleven Levels

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בַּקֹּדֶשׁ מִבִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁמַּטְבִּילִין כֵּלִים בְּתוֹךְ כֵּלִים לִתְרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא לַקֹּדֶשׁ. אֲחוֹרַיִים וָתוֹךְ וּבֵית הַצְּבִיטָה בִּתְרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא בַּקֹּדֶשׁ. הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַמִּדְרָס, נוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ. בִּגְדֵי אוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה, מִדְרָס לַקֹּדֶשׁ. לֹא כְּמִדַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִדַּת הַתְּרוּמָה. שֶׁבַּקֹּדֶשׁ: מַתִּיר וּמְנַגֵּב וּמַטְבִּיל, וְאַחַר כָּךְ קוֹשֵׁר. וּבִתְרוּמָה: קוֹשֵׁר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַטְבִּיל. כֵּלִים הַנִּגְמָרִים בְּטָהֳרָה, צְרִיכִין טְבִילָה לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לִתְרוּמָה. הַכְּלִי מְצָרֵף מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לִתְרוּמָה. הָרְבִיעִי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ פָּסוּל, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִתְרוּמָה. וּבִתְרוּמָה, אִם נִטְמֵאת אַחַת מִיָּדָיו — חֲבֶירְתָּהּ טְהוֹרָה, וּבַקֹּדֶשׁ — מַטְבִּיל שְׁתֵּיהֶן, שֶׁהַיָּד מְטַמֵּא אֶת חֲבֶירְתָּהּ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ אֲבָל לֹא בִתְרוּמָה. אוֹכְלִין אוֹכָלִים נְגוּבִין בְּיָדַיִם מְסוֹאָבוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא בַּקֹּדֶשׁ. הָאוֹנֵן וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים צְרִיכִין טְבִילָה לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Concerning several matters there is greater **stringency with regard to sacrificial** food **than with regard to *teruma*,** a portion of the produce designated for the priest. This expresses itself in many ways, the first being **that one may immerse vessels inside** other **vessels** to purify them **for *teruma*; but not for sacrificial** food, for which one must immerse each vessel separately. Another difference is that the *halakhot* of **the back** of a vessel **and** its **inside and** its **place for gripping** apply **to** vessels used for ***teruma*,** meaning that each part of the vessel has its own use and is considered a separate vessel in that it does not convey impurity to the other parts of the vessel when it contracts impurity; **but not to sacrificial** food, for which an impure section of the vessel does convey impurity to all the other sections. Likewise, **one who carries an object trodden** on by a *zav*, a man suffering from gonorrhea, **may carry *teruma*** at the same time, if he is careful that neither he nor the impure object should come into contact with the *teruma*, **but** this may **not** be done with **sacrificial** food. **The garments of those who eat *teruma*** are like an object trodden on by a *zav* **with regard to sacrificial** food. The mishna lists other stringencies that apply to sacrificial foods but not to *teruma*: **The characteristics of *teruma* are not like the characteristics of sacrificial** food, **as in** the case of vessels that are used with **sacrificial** food, if one has a garment or vessel that is tied up **he must untie** it **and dry** it if there was any moisture on it, as both a knot and absorbed moisture are considered interpositions that prevent the water of the ritual bath from reaching the entire garment. **And he may** then **immerse** them, **and afterward he may tie** them up again if he wishes. **But with regard to *teruma* he may,** if he so desires, **tie** up the garment **and then immerse** it without any concern that the knot might be considered an interposition. **Vessels** that were fashioned and **completed in purity** nevertheless **require immersion** to be considered pure **for sacrificial** food**s, but not for *teruma*. A vessel combines** all the food **that is in it** with regard **to sacrificial** food, meaning that if one piece of food becomes impure all the other pieces become impure as well; **but not** with regard **to *teruma*,** concerning which each piece is treated independently. The mishna continues the list of differences between sacrificial food and *teruma*. **Sacrificial** food **that is impure with fourth-degree** impurity **is disqualified,** meaning that the sacrificial food is rendered impure but it does not impart impurity to other items. ***Teruma* is disqualified when it is impure with third-degree** impurity; it is not susceptible to fourth-degree impurity at all. **And with regard to *teruma*, if one of one's hands became impure** by rabbinic law that renders only the hands impure, **its counterpart,** i.e., the other hand, remains **pure. But with regard to sacrificial** food, if one hand becomes impure **he must immerse them both, as one hand renders its counterpart impure with regard to sacrificial** food **but not with regard to *teruma*. One may eat dry foods,** i.e., foods that have never come into contact with liquid and are therefore not susceptible to impurity, **with impure hands when** it is ***teruma*, but not** when it is **sacrificial** food. **An acute mourner [*onen*],** i.e., someone who has experienced the loss of a close relative on that day, who had not come into contact with the deceased; **and one who is lacking atonement,** i.e., someone who still needs to bring an offering to complete his purification procedure, such as a *zav* or a woman after childbirth, both **require immersion** in order **to** eat **sacrificial** food. The *onen* would immerse after the day has passed and the one lacking atonement would immerse after the requisite offering is brought. **However,** immersion in these cases is **not** necessary **for** eating ***teruma*.

The *mishnah* on this *daf* lists ten — or, as we shall see, perhaps eleven — ways in which *kodesh* is treated with greater stringency than *terumah*. The *gemara* proceeds to examine these distinctions one by one. The broader context of these *mishnayot* is the stratification of Jewish society according to levels of *taharah* observance: *chullin*, *ma'aser*, *terumah*, *kodesh*, and so forth. As the *mishnayot* in this chapter will ultimately demonstrate, these distinctions translate into the creation of distinct social groupings — a reality that becomes particularly fraught during the *regalim*, when all of Israel gathers together and those distinctions must somehow be managed or suspended.

### Stratifications

Before turning to the *gemara*'s analysis, it is worth noting that the *mishnah*'s primary focus is on the differential treatment of various categories of food and the vessels used in their preparation and service. The question of how a *kohen* or an Israelite bringing a *shelamim* would prepare meat, or how wine and oil for *korbanos* would be handled, makes the question of vessel purity directly practical. A *kohen* cooking sacrificial meat, or an individual bringing wine and oil for the *niskei korbanos*, would need vessels whose status of *taharah* had been properly established — and the rules for establishing that status differ markedly between *terumah* and *kodesh*.

## Kelim b'Kelim

### Vessels Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אִילָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכְּבֵידוֹ שֶׁל כְּלִי חוֹצֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is taught in the mishna that one may not immerse one vessel inside another if they will be used for sacrificial food, though this may be done for *teruma*. The Gemara asks: **With regard to sacrificial** food, **what is the reason** that one may **not** immerse vessels in this manner? **Rabbi Ila said: Because the weight of the** inner **vessel causes an interposition** between the water and the vessels. That is, the innermost vessel weighs down on the bottom one, not allowing the water to reach the two vessels' point of contact.

The *gemara* opens its analysis with the first distinction listed in the *mishnah*: for *terumah*, one may immerse vessels within other vessels in the *mikvah* simultaneously — stacked or nested together — whereas for *kodesh*, one may not. Practically speaking, this is a familiar concern to anyone who has brought *kelim* to the *mikvah*: vessels must be separated so that no one vessel rests heavily upon another in a way that might prevent water from reaching every surface. The question the *gemara* addresses is: what is the precise rationale for the stricter standard that applies to *kodesh*?

### Rabbi Ila: Weight

*Amar Rabbi Ila mishum shekivedo shel kli chotzetz* — Rabbi Ila offers a straightforward explanation: the weight of the inner vessel causes it to press against the outer vessel, preventing water from passing between the two. This is a *chatzitza* concern — the inner vessel interposes between the water of the *mikvah* and the surface of the outer vessel (or vice versa), invalidating the immersion for *kodesh* purposes, though not for *terumah*.

### Two Chatzitza Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּסֵיפָא מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה, רֵישָׁא לָאו מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה! דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְלֹא כְּמִדַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִדַּת הַתְּרוּמָה, שֶׁבַּקֹּדֶשׁ מַתִּיר וּמְנַגֵּיב וּמַטְבִּיל וְאַחַר כָּךְ קוֹשֵׁר, וּבַתְּרוּמָה קוֹשֵׁר וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַטְבִּיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question on this explanation: **But is it not so that since the latter clause** of the mishna mentions that sacrificial foods and *teruma* differ **with regard to** matters of **interposition, the first clause** of the mishna must **not** be **with regard to** matters of **interposition,** but is referring to a different consideration? **As it teaches in the latter part** of the mishna: **The characteristics of *teruma* are not like the characteristics of sacrificial** food, **as in** the case of **sacrificial** food, if one has a garment or vessel that is tied up **he must untie** it **and dry** it, **and he may** then **immerse** them, **and afterward he may tie** them up again if he wishes; **but with regard to *teruma* he may tie** up the garment **and then immerse** it. This latter clause shows that there the Sages were concerned for any possibility of interposition with regard to sacrificial food, so most likely the earlier clause of the mishna is due to a different reason.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge. If Rabbi Ila's rationale for the vessels prohibition is a *chatzitza* concern, why does the *mishnah* list separately, later on, another distinction between *terumah* and *kodesh* that also appears to rest on a *chatzitza* concern — namely, that for *kodesh*, one must untie any knots in a garment before immersing it (*matir umangiv umatbil*), whereas for *terumah*, one may immerse a garment even while it remains knotted? *Midekattani seifa mishum chatzitza, hani lav mishum chatzitza?* Since the *mishnah* presents a limited number of distinctions, it would seem strange to list two separate items whose underlying rationale is the same heightened *chatzitza* concern.

### Chatzitza Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה, וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן רֵישָׁא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּלְקֹדֶשׁ לָא — מִשּׁוּם כְּבֵידוֹ שֶׁל כְּלִי דְּאִיכָּא, אֲבָל סֵיפָא, דְּלֵיכָּא כְּבֵידוֹ שֶׁל כְּלִי — אֵימָא לְקֹדֶשׁ נָמֵי לָא הָוֵי חֲצִיצָה. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן סֵיפָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּלְקֹדֶשׁ לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקִיטְרָא בְּמַיָּא אִהַדּוֹקֵי מִיהַדַּק, אֲבָל רֵישָׁא, דְּמַיָּא אַקְפּוֹיֵי מַקְפּוּ לֵיהּ לְמָנָא — לָא הָוְיָא חֲצִיצָה, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: No, this is not necessarily so. In both **the first clause and the latter clause** of the mishna the concern is **due to interposition, and** it is nevertheless **necessary** to teach us both cases. **For had** the mishna **taught us** only **the first clause,** i.e., that one may not immerse one vessel within another, **I would have said** that **this is the reason** one may **not** do so **for sacrificial** food: **Because there is the weight of** the interior **vessel** that must be taken into account. **But** in **the latter clause, where there is no weight of a vessel** to be considered, **I will say** that **it is not** considered an interposition **for sacrificial** food **either. And had it taught us** only **the latter clause** dealing with the knotted garments, **I would have said** that **this is the reason one may not** do so **for sacrificial** food: **Because** it is the nature of **knots** to **tighten** even more **in water,** creating an interposition that bars the water from entering all the way, **but** in the case of **the first clause** of the mishna, which deals with one vessel inside another and **where water** by nature **causes the** top **vessel to** lighten and **float** away from the lower vessel rather than weigh down on it, I would have said that **it is not** considered an **interposition.** It is therefore **necessary** for the *halakha* to be stated in both cases.

The *gemara* answers: *reisha veseifa mishum chatzitza — utzricha*. Both the vessels case and the knotted garments case are indeed rooted in a heightened concern for *chatzitza* by *kodesh*, but neither is derivable from the other, and therefore both must be listed independently.

Had the *mishnah* taught only the vessels case, one might have reasoned as follows: the prohibition there is because a metal or ceramic inner vessel, resting against the outer vessel, literally blocks water from reaching the intervening surface. But in the case of a knotted garment, I might have said: a garment is porous and will absorb water, so even if it is knotted, the water will soak through and there is no genuine *chatzitza*. Therefore, I would not have applied the same stringency to knotted garments for *kodesh*.

Conversely, had the *mishnah* taught only the knotted garments case, one might have explained the prohibition there as follows: *mishum d'kitra bemaya hiduki mehadek* — a knot tightens further when submerged in water, making it even harder for water to penetrate. But in the case of one vessel inside another, one might have said: *demaya achlei matzpu* — the water causes the inner vessel to float upward, naturally creating a gap between the two, so there is no *chatzitza*. I would therefore have permitted immersing nested vessels even for *kodesh*.

Since each case cannot be inferred from the other, both must be stated. And since for *terumah* neither prohibition applies, the implicit explanation is that for *terumah*, the optimistic reasoning in each case is relied upon — the water causes the inner vessel to float, and the garment absorbs water — whereas for *kodesh* we are more stringent and do not rely on these assumptions.

### Ten Levels Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אִילָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִילָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: עֶשֶׂר מַעֲלוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן, חָמֵשׁ רִאשׁוֹנוֹת — בֵּין לַקֹּדֶשׁ, בֵּין לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אַחֲרוֹנוֹת — לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Ila** here conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning** in considering these two issues as one, **as Rabbi Ila said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa said: They taught ten stringencies** of sacrificial food **here** in this mishna, rather than the apparent eleven. The **first five** stringencies apply **both to the sacrificial** foods themselves and **to non-sacred** food **that was prepared according to** the standards of **purity of sacrificial** food, whereas the **last** five apply only **to** actual **sacrificial** food **but not to non-sacred** food **that was prepared according to** the standards of **purity of sacrificial** food. The fact that Rabbi Ila counts only ten cases in the mishna shows that he considered the two cases discussed above to be of the same category, and therefore they are counted together as one stringency.

This answer, which treats the vessels case and the knotted garments case as two distinct items, both grounded in *chatzitza* concerns, is in tension with a position attributed to Rabbi Ila elsewhere. *Rabbi Ila l'ta'ameih* — Rabbi Ila is consistent with his own view, as reported in the name of Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa: *eser ma'alot shanu kan* — the *mishnah* enumerates ten distinctions, not eleven. By treating the vessels case and the knotted garments case as variations of the same underlying *chatzitza* concern rather than as two fully independent items, Rabbi Ila is able to collapse them into a single entry, yielding a count of ten rather than eleven.

### Eleven Distinctions

To appreciate this fully, it is worth enumerating all the distinctions the *mishnah* lists. First, vessels within vessels — the focus of the current discussion. Second, the question of whether *tum'ah* affecting one part of a vessel (the inside, outside, or handle) renders the entire vessel *tamei*, or only that section — a distinction that applies differently for *terumah* and *kodesh*. Third, whether carrying a *midras* object — the seat or couch of a *zav* — on one shoulder renders *terumah* on the other shoulder *tamei*. Fourth, that the garments of one who eats *terumah* are considered *midras* for one who eats *kodesh*. Fifth, the matter of undoing knots before immersion — as discussed above. Sixth, that even a vessel fresh from the potter's kiln, with which nothing *tamei* has ever come in contact, must be immersed before use for *kodesh*. Seventh, that a vessel combines all its contents into a single unit for purposes of *tum'ah* by *kodesh*, but not by *terumah*. Eighth, that *tum'ah* is transmitted by *kodesh* down to a fourth-level derivative (*revi'i*), whereas *terumah* only transmits down to a third level (*shelishi*). Ninth, that if one hand becomes *tamei*, the other is not thereby rendered *tamei* for *terumah*, but it is for *kodesh*. Tenth, that even food that has never come into contact with liquid — and is therefore not susceptible to *tum'ah* biblically — must nevertheless have one's hands washed before handling for *kodesh*, but not for *terumah*. Eleventh, that an *onen* (one in the first stage of mourning before burial) and a *mechusar kippurim* (one who has completed the purification process but has not yet brought the concluding sacrifice) must immerse in a *mikvah* before eating *kodesh*, but not before eating *terumah*.

That gives us eleven. Rabbi Ila, by treating items one and five — the nested vessels and the knotted garments — as a single *chatzitza* concern, reduces the count to ten.

## Five-Five Division

### First Five Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא: חֲמֵשׁ קַמָּיָיתָא, דְּאִית לְהוּ דְּרָרָא דְטוּמְאָה מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן בֵּין לְקֹדֶשׁ בֵּין לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, בָּתְרָיָיתָא, דְּלֵית לְהוּ דְּרָרָא דְטוּמְאָה מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן לְקֹדֶשׁ, לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rabbi Ila's statement. **What is the reason** for this distinction? With regard to the **first five** stringencies, **which have a connection to impurity** as defined **by Torah law** because ignoring them can lead to a case of impurity by Torah law as opposed to merely rabbinic law, **the Sages decreed these** stringencies **both for** actual **sacrificial** food **and for non-sacred** food **prepared according to** the standards of **purity of sacrificial** food. However, with regard to the **last** five, **which do not have a connection to impurity by Torah law,** as their entire impurity is based on a rabbinic decree, **the Sages decreed these** stringencies only **for** actual **sacrificial** food. But **with regard to non-sacred** food **made according to** the standards of **purity of sacrificial** food, **the Sages did not decree** these stringencies **for** such foods.

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa's position, as transmitted by Rabbi Ila, goes further than a simple count. Not only are there ten distinctions, but those ten are divided into two conceptually distinct groups of five. *Chamesh rishonot bein l'Kodesh bein l'chullin shena'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh* — the first five stringencies apply not only to actual *kodesh* (consecrated food and *korbanos*) but also to *chullin she'na'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh* — ordinary, unconsecrated food that someone has taken upon himself to handle with the same standard of care required for *kodesh*.

This category — *chullin she'na'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh* — was a central concern of yesterday's discussion. The question it raises is: if one consistently treats his ordinary food as though it were a *korban*, does that food acquire the halachic status of *kodesh*? The answer, as *Tosafot* notes, is that the position here assumes *chullin she'na'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh lav k'kodesh dami* — such food is generally not treated as equivalent to *kodesh* in all respects; it is perhaps treated more like *terumah* or like elevated *chullin*, but not as full *kodesh*. And yet, the first five of the ten stringencies apply to it.

### Last Five Kodesh Only

*Achronot l'kodesh aval lo l'chullin shena'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh* — the last five stringencies apply only to actual *kodesh*. Food that is merely treated with *kodesh*-level care, but which does not possess genuine *kedushah*, is not subject to these final five.

### Biblical Tumah

The *gemara* explains the rationale: *chamesh kamaisa d'is l'hu drara d'tumah m'de'oraisa gazru b'hu rabanan bein l'kodesh bein l'chullin shena'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh* — the first five stringencies all have some connection, however indirect, to a sphere where biblical *tum'ah* is a genuine concern. Because they are rooted in (or at least linked to) *tumah de'oraita*, the Rabbis extended these stringencies even to *chullin she'na'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh*.

### Rabbinic Only

*Basraisa d'lais l'hu drara d'tumah m'de'oraisa gazru b'hu rabanan l'kodesh* — the last five stringencies do not have such a connection to biblical *tum'ah*. They are therefore purely rabbinic enactments that the Rabbis applied only to actual *kodesh*, but not to *chullin* that is merely treated as *kodesh*.

### Category Logic

It must be said that figuring out on one's own which items belong in which group is not intuitive, and *Tosafot* must intervene to make the classification coherent. In brief: the *chatzitza* concern in immersion is clearly connected to *tum'ah de'oraita*, since an invalid immersion due to *chatzitza* is a biblical concern. The case of the inner and outer parts of a vessel — while the division itself is rabbinic — involves *tum'ah* derived from liquids associated with a *zav* or *zavah*, which are themselves biblical sources of *tum'ah*; that tenuous connection is apparently sufficient. Carrying a *midras* on one's shoulder alongside *terumah* obviously implicates the biblical *tum'ah* of *midras*. The garments of one who eats *terumah* being considered *midras* for one who eats *kodesh* is connected to the possibility that a *niddah* sat upon them — again, a biblical *tum'ah*. And even the requirement to immerse a vessel fresh from the kiln is connected, by the *gemara*'s later discussion, to the concern that something *tamei* may have come in contact with it during manufacture — a concern with some biblical grounding.

For the last five: *tum'at yadayim* (hand impurity) is entirely rabbinic. The requirement to wash hands before handling food that never came in contact with liquid is similarly of purely rabbinic origin, since such food cannot become biblically *tamei*. The concern about an *onen* or *mechusar kippurim* likewise lacks a clean biblical *tum'ah* parallel for the purposes at hand. *Tosafot* adds an important clarification: what distinguishes the last five is not merely that they lack a biblical *tum'ah* connection in the abstract, but that any biblical *tum'ah* concern they might involve does not arise with *chullin* at all. This is why they could not be extended to *chullin she'na'asu al taharat ha-Kodesh*.

As the *gemara* itself seems to acknowledge, the precise mapping of each item onto its category is not fully transparent, and the assertion about which items belong where is clearer than the underlying logic that assigns them there.

## Rava: Narrow Opening

### Rava Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא הָוֵי מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה, רֵישָׁא לָאו מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה. וְרֵישָׁא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁלֹּא יַטְבִּיל מְחָטִין וְצִינּוֹרוֹת בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֵין בְּפִיו כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** disagreed with Rabbi Ila. He **said** that **since** the reason for the stringency in **the latter clause is due to** concern for interposition, this implies that the reason for the stringency in **the first clause is not due to interposition,** but to a different reason. **And** with regard to the stringency in **the first clause** that one may not immerse one vessel within another, **this is the reasoning:** It is **a** rabbinic **decree** to ensure **that one not immerse** small vessels, such as **needles and hooks, inside a vessel whose mouth** is less than the width of **the tube of a wineskin.**

Rava takes a fundamentally different approach. He disputes Rabbi Ila's conflation of the vessels case and the knotted garments case as two variations of a *chatzitza* concern. *Rava amar midseifa havei mishum chatzitzah reisha lav mishum chatzitzah* — yes, the case of knotted garments (the *seifa*, the latter clause of the *mishnah*) is indeed a *chatzitza* concern, but the first case, of vessels within vessels (the *reisha*), is not a *chatzitza* concern at all, and the two must not be conflated.

### Needles Concern

What, then, is the reason behind the prohibition against immersing nested vessels for *kodesh* purposes? *Gezeirah shelo yatbil machtin u'tsinorot bikli she'ein befiv kishfoferet hanod* — it is a rabbinic decree lest one come to immerse needles and other small items in a vessel whose opening is smaller than the width of a *shfoferet hanod*.

The practical scenario Rava envisions is this: one wishes to immerse small objects — needles, pins, and similar tiny items — in the *mikvah*. The difficulty is obvious: loose needles will scatter in the water, sink to the bottom, or otherwise become impossible to manage. The natural solution is to place them inside a vessel and immerse the vessel. But if the vessel has a narrow opening — say, a jug or flask — the needles cannot float out, making it a convenient container for immersion. The problem, however, is whether the water inside such a narrow-mouthed vessel is considered part of the *mikvah* water at all, or whether it is merely water that has entered a sealed container and is no longer meaningfully connected to the *mikvah*.

### Shfoferet Hanod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִתְנַן: עֵירוּב מִקְווֹאוֹת, כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד, כְּעוֹבְיָהּ וְכַחֲלָלָהּ, בִּשְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת חוֹזְרוֹת לִמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** In such a case the water in the bottle would not be considered attached to the rest of the ritual bath, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 6:7): **The joining of** different bodies of water in cases of **ritual baths** takes place if the opening between the two bodies is at least as wide **as** the width of **the tube of a wineskin,** counting both the **thickness** of the wall of the tube **and its space, which is equivalent to** the width of **two fingers going around in their place,** i.e., a space large enough to insert two fingers and twist them around inside.

The answer depends on the size of the opening. *She'ein befiv kishfoferet hanod* — if the opening is smaller than a *shfoferet hanod*, the water inside is not considered a continuation of the *mikvah* water, and the needles are not considered to have been immersed in the *mikvah* at all. The *shfoferet hanod* — the tube (*shfoferet*) of a leather water flask (*nod*) — defines the minimum opening required for the water inside a vessel to be considered connected to, and part of, the surrounding *mikvah* water. This measurement, cited in a *mishnah* elsewhere (*eruv mikvaot kishfoferet hanod — ke-ovya u-ke-chalala — bi-shtei etzbaot chozrot limkoman*), is the width of two fingers in diameter, referring to the full circumference of the tube. This is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### Modern Mikvah

This principle is not merely historical. It is the very foundation of how modern *mikvaot* are constructed. The standard contemporary *mikvah* operates through a dual-pool system. One pool — the *bor mei geshamim* — collects rainwater on the roof and feeds it into a reservoir below. This pool, filled exclusively with *mei geshamim*, is halachically valid on its own, containing at least forty *se'ah* of rain-collected water. The second pool, the one into which people actually immerse, is filled with clean, drawn water (*mayim she'uvim*) — which, on its own, would be invalid as a *mikvah* — and is regularly changed for hygienic reasons. The two pools are connected by a pipe. Because the valid *mikvah* and the pool of drawn water are connected, the drawn water is considered part of the valid *mikvah* and the entire system is *kasher*. But that connecting pipe must meet the standard of *shfoferet hanod* — it must have an opening of at least two finger-widths in diameter. Without that, the two pools are not considered a single body of water, and the drawn water remains invalid. The same principle Rava invokes to explain the rabbinic decree about nested vessels is precisely what underlies the architecture of every contemporary *mikvah*.

## Basket Difference

### Basket Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין דְּרָבָא לִדְרַבִּי אִילָא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ סַל וְגַרְגוּתְנִי שֶׁמִּילְּאָן כֵּלִים וְהִטְבִּילָן. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה — אִיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַטְבִּיל מְחָטִין וְצִינּוֹרִיּוֹת בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֵין בְּפִיו כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד — סַל וְגַרְגוּתְנִי שֶׁאֵין בְּפִיהֶן כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד, לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The** practical difference **between them** is in the case of **a basket or** a wicker **bin [*gargutni*] that one has filled** with smaller **vessels and** has **immersed them** all together. **According to** Rabbi Ila, **the one who said** it is prohibited to immerse one vessel inside another **due to** the concern of **interposition,** in this case as well **there is** such a concern, as the inner vessels might weigh down against the basket and prevent the water from touching the contact points. But **according to** Rava, **the one who said** it is prohibited **due to** a rabbinic **decree lest one immerse needles and hooks inside a vessel whose mouth does not have** the width of **the tube of a wineskin, there are no** such things as **baskets and** wicker **bins whose mouth does not have** the width of **the tube of a wineskin,** and therefore the decree would not apply to them.

Having established the two competing explanations — Rabbi Ila's *chatzitza* rationale and Rava's narrow-opening rationale — the *gemara* asks what practical difference (*nafka mina*) exists between them. The answer: *ika beiniyeihu sal ve-gargutni she-milan kelim ve-hitbilan* — the case of a basket (*sal*) or large woven container (*gargutni*) that is filled with vessels and immersed in the *mikvah*.

### Ila: Baskets Problem

According to Rabbi Ila, whose concern is *chatzitza* — the weight of an inner vessel preventing water from circulating between the vessels — the prohibition applies regardless of what the outer container looks like. A basket, no less than a ceramic pot, can have an inner vessel resting against it, and the weight of the inner vessel is just as problematic in a basket as in any other container. Therefore, according to Rabbi Ila, immersing a basket filled with vessels remains a concern even for *kodesh* purposes.

### Rava: Baskets Okay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: סַל וְגַרְגוּתְנִי שֶׁמִּילְּאָן כֵּלִים וְהִטְבִּילָן — טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rava follows his** line of **reasoning, as Rava said: A basket or** wicker **bin that one has filled** with smaller **vessels and** has **immersed them** all together, **they are pure** in all regards, even for sacrificial food.

According to Rava, however, whose concern is specifically the case of narrow-mouthed vessels in which one might place needles, baskets are categorically excluded from the decree. The very nature of a basket — the feature that makes it a basket rather than some other kind of vessel — is that it has a wide opening. There is no basket with a narrow mouth; if it had a narrow mouth, it would not be called a basket. Since the *gezeirah* was enacted only against immersing objects in vessels with openings narrower than a *shfoferet hanod*, and since a basket by definition cannot have such a narrow opening, the decree never applied to baskets in the first place.

This is a meaningful categorical distinction rather than a case-by-case assessment of whether any particular vessel triggers the underlying concern. Such case-by-case reasoning — *lo palug* considerations aside — would be unworkable in practice. But here the division between baskets and other vessels is clean enough to be drawn at the categorical level, and Rava draws it accordingly.

## Vessels in Vessels

### Porous Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִקְוֶה שֶׁחֲלָקוֹ בְּסַל וְגַרְגוּתְנִי — הַטּוֹבֵל שָׁם לֹא עָלְתָה לוֹ טְבִילָה. דְּהָא אַרְעָא כּוּלַּהּ חַלְחוֹלֵי מְחַלְחֲלָא, וּבָעֵינַן דְּאִיכָּא אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rava stated a second teaching with regard to baskets and bins as well: **And** in the case of **a ritual bath that one divided** into two sections **by** inserting **a basket or** wicker **bin,** so that each section is left with less than the required forty *se'a*, if **one immerses there, his immersion is ineffective for him.** Despite the certainty that water seeps through the basket or bin, this is not enough to join the two incomplete sections of the ritual bath to be counted as one. We know that this is so, **for the earth is entirely porous, and** nevertheless **we** do not rely on this to allow several adjacent, small ditches full of water to add up to forty *se'a*, but rather **require that there be forty *se'a*** together **in one place.**

The discussion of baskets leads naturally into a related question: can a basket or similarly porous vessel actually divide a *mikvah*? If one places a wicker basket inside a *mikvah* — imagine a large laundry-style basket made of hemp, riddled with openings — does it divide the *mikvah* into two separate bodies of water?

The intuitive answer might be: obviously not, since the basket is so thoroughly perforated that water passes freely through it in every direction. The *gemara*, however, reaches a more surprising conclusion. Rava states: *u-mikveh she-hilko be-sal ha-gargustani ha-tovel sham lo alta lo tevilah* — if a *mikvah* was divided by such a basket, one who immerses in one of the resulting sections has not achieved a valid *tevilah*. The basket does divide the *mikvah*.

The reason Rava gives is striking: *de-ara kullah chalchulei mechalchalah* — the entire earth is porous. If we were to say that porousness alone is sufficient to prevent division, then any body of water would be connected to every other body of water through the porous earth, and we could never speak of a divided *mikvah* at all. Since that conclusion is absurd, porousness as such cannot be the criterion. What matters is whether there is a formal *mechitzah* — a partition — and the basket, however full of holes, constitutes such a partition.

This is a surprisingly formalist position. Even something as open and porous as a woven basket, which allows water to flow through it freely, is treated as a genuine wall for purposes of dividing a *mikvah*. One might push further and ask: what about a *mechitzah* made of three or four strings, each within three *tefachim* of the next, which is halachically valid as a partition in other contexts? Would such a construction divide a *mikvah*? Rava's reasoning seems to point in that direction, though the question is acknowledged as requiring further analysis.

This discussion of the basket's porousness also reinforces, from a different angle, why Rava's *gezeirah* does not apply to baskets. Not only does a basket have a wide opening by definition, but its thoroughly porous nature means that it is a fundamentally different type of vessel — one in which the concern about water failing to penetrate simply does not arise.

### Purity Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּכְלִי טָהוֹר, אֲבָל בִּכְלִי טָמֵא, מִיגּוֹ דְּסָלְקָא טְבִילָה לְכוּלֵּיהּ גּוּפֵיהּ דְּמָנָא, סָלְקָא לְהוּ נָמֵי לְכֵלִים דְּאִית בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this** *halakha*, that small vessels such as needles cannot be immersed inside a vessel with a narrow opening, **applies** only if he immersed them **in a pure vessel,** which does not require purification on its own. **But** if he did so **in an impure vessel,** which requires purification in its own right, **since the immersion is effective for the whole of the** outer **vessel,** including its inside, **it is also effective for** the **vessels that are inside of it.** Since the water that enters the outer vessel is considered attached to the rest of the ritual bath for purposes of purification of the outer vessel, so is it considered attached with regard to the purification of the inner vessels.

*Amar Rav Papa:* the analysis now turns to a more refined question involving the relationship between an outer vessel and the objects placed within it. The rule, it turns out, depends critically on the initial *taharah* status of the outer vessel.

### Outer Tamei

*Migu de-salka tevilah le-kula gufa de-mana, salka le-hu nami le-kelim de-it bah* — if the outer vessel was *tamei* and requires immersion, then when it is immersed and becomes *tahor*, the same *tevilah* that purifies the outer vessel also renders *tahor* whatever is inside it. The inner vessels benefit from the *tevilah* of the outer vessel by virtue of being contained within it at the moment of the status change. They need not themselves be considered to be immersed in the *mikvah* water in any direct sense; the transformation of the outer vessel's status carries over to them automatically.

### Outer Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: כֵּלִים שֶׁמִּילְּאָן כֵּלִים וְהִטְבִּילָן — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ טְהוֹרִין. וְאִם לֹא טָבַל, מַיִם הַמְעוֹרָבִים — עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד. מַאי קָאָמַר וְאִם לֹא טָבַל? הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאִם אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַטְבִּילוֹ, וּמַיִם הַמְעוֹרָבִים עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מְעוֹרָבִים כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** We know this, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 6:2): With regard to **vessels that one filled with** other **vessels and immersed them** all together, **they are pure,** regardless of the width of the opening of the outer vessel. **And if he did not immerse, the joining of waters** is not effective **until they are joined like the** width of the **tube of a wineskin.** This second sentence of the mishna is unclear, and the Gemara seeks to clarify it. **What is** the mishna **saying** here when it says: **And if he did not immerse?** The Gemara explains. **This is what** the mishna **is saying: And if he has no need to immerse** the outer vessel, as it was already pure, **and** similarly in a case of two bodies of **water** that are **joined** together by means of a hole, it is not valid **until** the water **is joined** through a space as wide **as the tube of a wineskin.**

But if the outer vessel was already *tahor* and did not itself need immersion, then the inner vessels only become *tahor* if the water inside is connected to the *mikvah* water through an opening of at least *shfoferet hanod* size. Without that opening, the water inside the vessel is not part of the *mikvah* water, and the inner vessels are not considered to have been immersed at all.

### Mishna Case

This framework allows Rav Papa to explain a *mishnah* that, at first glance, seems puzzling: *kelim she-milan kelim — ve-hitbilan — harei elu tehorim* — fill vessels with vessels and immerse them, and they are *tahor*. The *mishnah* then continues: *ve-im lo taval, mayim ha-me'uravin ad she-ye-u me'uravin ke-shfoferet hanod* — and if it was not immersed, the water must be combined through an opening of *shfoferet hanod* size.

The phrase "if it was not immersed" initially seems incoherent — how can you speak of immersion not having occurred if we just said they were immersed? The *gemara* reinterprets: *ve-im eino tzarich litvilah* — "if it does not require immersion" — that is, if the outer vessel was already *tahor* and did not need to be immersed. In that case, the inner vessels only become *tahor* if the opening connecting the interior water to the *mikvah* water is of sufficient size. But if the outer vessel was *tamei* and needed to be immersed, the *tevilah* of the outer vessel carries through to the inner vessels regardless of the size of the opening.

This principle opens up some intriguing theoretical possibilities. One might wonder whether a similar logic could apply in other immersion scenarios — for instance, where something cannot itself come into direct contact with *mikvah* water. If placed inside a *tamei* vessel that undergoes a valid *tevilah*, might the interior object benefit from the status change of the vessel? The halachic parameters of such a question — particularly whether the principle applies to persons as well as vessels, and whether vessels of sufficient size retain the identity of *keli* — remain to be worked out.

It should be noted that earthenware vessels (*klei cheres*) are a special case: they cannot be purified through *mikvah* immersion at all, so these considerations do not apply to them.

## Basket Difference…

### Tannaic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרָבָא וּדְרַבִּי אִילָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: סַל וְגַרְגוּתְנִי שֶׁמִּילְּאָן כֵּלִים וְהִטְבִּילָן בֵּין לַקּוֹדֶשׁ בֵּין לַתְּרוּמָה, טְהוֹרִין. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: לַתְּרוּמָה אֲבָל לֹא לַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara notes: **And this** dispute between **Rava and Rabbi Ila is** also a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a basket or a** wicker **bin that one filled** with **vessels and** then **immersed them, whether for** purposes of **sacrificial** food **or for** purposes of ***teruma*, they are pure.** This is identical to Rava's opinion. **Abba Shaul says:** They are pure **for** purposes of ***teruma* but not for** purposes of **sacrificial** food. This is identical to Rabbi Ila's opinion.

The *gemara* now observes that the dispute between Rava and Rabbi Ila is not simply an *amoraic* debate; it mirrors a *tannaitic* disagreement. *Ditnaya: sal ha-gargustani she-milan kelim ve-hitpilan — bein la-kodesh bein la-trumah — tehorim* — the anonymous first *tanna* of the *baraita* holds that a basket filled with vessels and immersed is *tahor* for both *terumah* and *kodesh* purposes. This aligns with Rava's position: since the *gezeirah* was never about baskets (which have wide openings), there is no restriction even for *kodesh*. *Abba Shaul omer: la-trumah aval lo la-kodesh* — Abba Shaul disagrees, holding that for *kodesh* the restriction applies even to a basket. This aligns with Rabbi Ila's position: since the concern is *chatzitza* — the weight of an inner vessel — it applies regardless of the shape or construction of the outer container.

The *gemara* thus grounds the *amoraic* debate in a *tannaitic* precedent, with the first *tanna* corresponding to Rava and Abba Shaul corresponding to Rabbi Ila.

## Am Ha'aretz Example

### Stringency Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי! לְמַאן קָאָמְרִינַן — חֲבֵרִים, חֲבֵרִים מִידָּע יָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** in light of these two reasons we have given for concern with regard to immersing vessels inside other vessels, this should **not** be permitted for ***teruma* either.** The Gemara responds: **For whom do we say** the principle that one vessel may not be immersed inside another? For ***ḥaverim*,** individuals devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, especially *halakhot* of ritual purity. Others do not carefully follow these *halakhot* in any event. And ***ḥaverim* know** such things **well,** that water is considered detached from the ritual bath if it is separated by a narrow opening, and that if one vessel is weighing down on another, preventing the water from reaching that spot, the top vessel must be lifted to allow the water to touch all parts of the vessel. Therefore, there is no need to apply these concerns and stringencies to the case of *teruma*.

Having established the various distinctions between *kodesh* and *terumah*, the *gemara* raises an obvious question. For *terumah* as well, there are people who are scrupulous about *taharah* — *chaverim*, those who take upon themselves careful observance of the laws of purity. If *chaverim* are knowledgeable enough to immerse their vessels properly for *terumah*, handling each vessel separately and ensuring that no *chatzitza* intervenes, why do we not rely on the same knowledge and care when it comes to *kodesh* — why impose additional stringencies at all?

### Imitation Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, קוֹדֶשׁ נָמֵי! חָזֵי לֵיהּ עַם הָאָרֶץ וְאָזֵיל מַטְבִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters with another question. **If so,** we should say the same thing in the case of **sacrificial** food **too,** i.e., that all these *halakhot* are for *ḥaverim*, who meticulously follow ritual purity for sacrificial food and inquire about such *halakhot*. Why, then, did the Sages apply these concerns and stringencies to the case of sacrificial food? The Gemara responds: With regard to sacrificial food they were concerned that a **common person** [***am ha'aretz***], who is not meticulous about ritual purity, may **see** the *ḥaver* immersing small vessels inside of large vessels, **and** will then **go and immerse** vessels of his own in this manner. But he will not take the same precautions as the *ḥaver* would, ensuring that the outer vessel has a wide opening and that the vessels on top do not weigh down on the lower ones.

The *gemara*'s answer: *chazi lei Am Ha'aretz ve'azil matbil* — we are concerned that an *am ha'aretz* will observe what the *chaver* does and imitate it. When the *chaver* immerses nested vessels for *terumah*, the *am ha'aretz* sees this and concludes that this is how things are done. When he later immerses vessels for *kodesh* — where the rules are stricter — he will simply do what he saw done for *terumah*, without realizing that the standard must be higher. If the *chaver* himself demonstrates no visible difference in how he handles *kodesh* versus *terumah*, the *am ha'aretz* will have no way of knowing there should be one. Therefore, the *chaver* must behave differently with *kodesh* — not for his own sake, since he knows the law, but to ensure that his observable practice does not mislead others.

### No Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי, חָזֵי לֵיהּ עַם הָאָרֶץ וְאָזֵיל מַטְבִּיל! לָא מְקַבְּלִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But the same concern could be raised with regard to ***teruma* as well.** It is possible that **an *am ha'aretz*** may **see** the *ḥaver* immerse vessels for *teruma* in this manner, **and** he will then **go and immerse** his vessels this way, without taking the precautions that the *ḥaver* would take. The Gemara answers: **We do not accept** *teruma* **from** *amei ha'aretz*, as they are not trustworthy with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual purity, and therefore it does not matter if the vessels he uses for *teruma* are not immersed properly. Therefore, the Sages were not concerned that the *am ha'aretz* may come to a misunderstanding when observing a *ḥaver* immersing vessels within vessels.

One might object: the same concern should apply to *terumah*. If the *am ha'aretz* watches the *chaver* immerse nested vessels for *terumah* without separating them, he will do the same when handling his own *terumah*. So why are we not equally stringent about *terumah*, to prevent this imitation?

The *gemara*'s response: *lo mikablinan mineihu* — we do not accept *terumah* from an *am ha'aretz* in the first place. A *chaver kohen* who receives *terumah* will simply decline the offering of the *am ha'aretz* and direct him to give it to another *kohen* who is himself an *am ha'aretz*. Since the *terumah* of the *am ha'aretz* will never reach the *chaver*'s table regardless of how it was handled, the practical harm of the *am ha'aretz* imitating improper *terumah* practice is contained.

### Kodesh No Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹדֶשׁ נָמֵי, לָא נְקַבֵּיל מִינַּיְיהוּ! הָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its line of questioning. If so, **we should likewise not accept sacrificial** food **from** *amei ha'aretz*, since they are not sufficiently meticulous with ritual purity, and we should therefore not care if they immerse their vessels improperly. The Gemara responds: The *am ha'aretz* **will have** feelings of **antagonism** if sacrificial food is not accepted from him, and this would lead to internal discord and conflict within Israel.

But this answer only works if there is an alternative destination for the rejected item. With *terumah*, there is one: give it to another *kohen*. With *korbanot* and *kodshim*, there is none. There is only one *Beit Hamikdash*. One cannot tell an *am ha'aretz* who brings wine or oil for his *korban* to bring it to a different *Mikdash* — no such alternative exists. If his offering is rejected, he has nowhere else to go.

### Proper Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי, הָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה! לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ, דְּאָזֵיל יָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ חַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, in the case of ***teruma* he will also have** feelings of **antagonism** if *teruma* is not accepted from him. Why were the Sages concerned about this factor only with regard to sacrificial food and not *teruma*? The Gemara replies: An *am ha'aretz* **does not care** if his *teruma* is not accepted by *ḥaverim*, **as** he can always **go** and **give** his *teruma* **to an *am ha'aretz* priest** who is **his friend** and who will accept it from him. In the case of sacrificial food, however, there is only one Temple, and care must be taken not to make the *amei ha'aretz* feel they are being rejected.

Therefore, *chaverim* must take particular care to set the correct example in all matters relating to *kodesh*. The *am ha'aretz* will observe how *kodesh*-related *kelim* are handled and imitate that practice when he brings his own offerings. Since there is no way to exclude him from participation in the *avodah* — no alternative *kohen* to send him to, no alternative *Mikdash* — the standard of practice must itself be made exemplary, so that imitation produces correct behavior. This is the reason for the additional stringencies applied to *kodesh* over and above those applied to *terumah*.

## Wine/Oil Acceptance

### Wine Oil Trusted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דְּחָיֵישׁ לְאֵיבָה — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִפְּנֵי מָה הַכֹּל נֶאֱמָנִים עַל טׇהֳרַת יַיִן וָשֶׁמֶן כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הוֹלֵךְ וּבוֹנֶה בָּמָה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְשׂוֹרֵף פָּרָה אֲדוּמָּה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this sensitivity of not causing offense to the *am ha'aretz* is expressed elsewhere as well: **And who is the *tanna* that is concerned for** such **antagonism** of *amei ha'aretz*? **It is Rabbi Yosei, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei said: For what** reason are **all** people, i.e., even *amei ha'aretz*, **trusted with regard to the purity of** their **wine and oil** that they bring to the Temple for sacrificial purposes **throughout the year?** Why is the status of these items not investigated to determine that they were prepared with the necessary regard for ritual purity? In order to avoid schisms among the people, **so that each and every individual should not go off and build a** private **altar for himself and burn a red heifer for himself.** Were the Sages to reject sacrificial wine and oil from *amei ha'aretz*, they would become alienated and go off and create schisms, going so far as to build their own separate temples and bring their own private offerings.

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* that seems, at first glance, to undercut everything just established. *Amar Rabbi Yossi: mipnei mah hakol ne'emanim al taharat yayin veshemen kol yemot hashanah* — Rabbi Yossi asks: why are all people believed regarding the purity of wine and oil brought to the *Beit Hamikdash* throughout the entire year? The irony is striking. The *mishnah* has just catalogued a long list of stringencies applied to *kodesh* that go beyond even those applied to *terumah*; the *am ha'aretz* concern has just been articulated as a reason for even greater vigilance around *kodesh* — and yet here, precisely when it comes to the holiest context of all, bringing offerings to the *Beit Hamikdash*, everyone is trusted unconditionally.

### Private Altar

Rabbi Yossi's answer: *kedei shelo yehei kol echad ve'echad holech uvoneh bamah le'atzmo* — so that no one should go and build himself a private altar. If a person comes to bring wine or oil as part of his *korban* and is told that his offering cannot be accepted because he is not trusted regarding its *taharah*, he has nowhere else to go and nothing else to do — except, perhaps, to build his own *bamah* and bring his offerings there. The impulse to bring a *korban*, to participate in the service of God, is real and powerful; if the only *Mikdash* rejects it, the alternative is not abandonment of the impulse but private, unauthorized worship — which is prohibited and spiritually dangerous.

### Eiva Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּמַאן מְקַבְּלִינַן הָאִידָּנָא סָהֲדוּתָא מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ — כְּמַאן כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: In accordance with whose** opinion **do we accept testimony nowadays from an *am ha'aretz*,** despite the concern of some Sages that their carelessness with regard to observance of *halakha* might also lead to personal untrustworthiness? **In accordance with whom** is this done? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.**

This is the principle of *eiva* — avoiding hatred and enmity. The *gemara* identifies Rabbi Yossi as the *tanna* who gives decisive weight to *eiva*, allowing it to override what would otherwise be valid grounds for rejection. *U-man tana dechaish le'eiva — Rabbi Yossi* — it is Rabbi Yossi who holds that the concern for *eiva* is weighty enough to override the normal standards of trustworthiness we would otherwise apply.

### Modern Testimony

Rav Papa draws an explicit contemporary application: *keman mekablinan idna sahadusa mi'am ha'aretz — ke'Rabbi Yossi* — according to whom do we nowadays accept testimony from an *am ha'aretz*? Like Rabbi Yossi. In the time of the *Beit Hamikdash*, rejection on grounds of *am ha'aretz* status meant: your offering is not accepted here. In later times, rejection on grounds of *am ha'aretz* status in the context of *eidut* means: your testimony is not accepted in the *beit din*. Both represent forms of complete social and religious exclusion. Rabbi Yossi's principle — that the concern for *eiva*, for the feeling of total rejection, overrides the grounds for exclusion — carries forward from the *Beit Hamikdash* into the rabbinic court.

### Unity Theme

The broader theme that emerges from this *gemara* is a profound tension within rabbinic legal culture. On the one hand, the entire discussion of the *mishnah* in this chapter has been building an elaborate structure of stratified standards: *chullin*, *ma'aser*, *terumah*, *kodesh*, with multiple distinctions and sub-distinctions at each level, and a parallel social structure of *chaverim* versus *amei ha'aretz*. On the other hand, the *gemara* here — and, as we shall see when we reach the discussions around the *regel* — repeatedly returns to the recognition that these stratifications carry a cost. When they produce a sense of total exclusion, when they threaten to rupture the unity of *Klal Yisrael* or to drive people toward their own private forms of worship, the calculus changes.

It is worth noting that most of these distinctions have, in practice, receded into history. The absence of the *Mikdash* has made *tum'ah ve-taharah* concerns largely theoretical, and the *regel*-period suspension of the stratifications has given way to a more permanent softening. But the principle Rabbi Yossi articulates — and that Rav Papa applies to everyday testimony — has not receded. The question of how to maintain standards without producing a sense of complete exclusion, of how to insist on integrity in communal institutions without making people feel that those institutions are not theirs, remains as urgent as ever. The *gemara* answers it, at least in part, by invoking *eiva*: the cost of rejection is sometimes too high to pay.
