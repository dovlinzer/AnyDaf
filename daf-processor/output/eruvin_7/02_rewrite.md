# Eruvin 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv Basics & Mavoi

### Eruv Challenge

The fundamental challenge addressed throughout this section of the *gemara* is a practical one: how can one permit carrying by constructing an *eruv* in, or adjacent to, a *reshus harabim*? The word *eruv* itself derives from *me'arvin* — the act of joining together through a shared loaf of bread — but the term has come to denote the entire enterprise of establishing the legal enclosure that makes carrying permissible. In a genuine *reshus harabim*, the requirements are so demanding as to render an *eruv* nearly impossible. The *gemara* had already established that a true *reshus harabim* requires, among other conditions, that its entrances be fitted with doors that are actually closed during the day — a standard virtually no modern thoroughfare meets. The *gemara* therefore shifts its focus to the *meva'os hamefulashim l'reshus harabim*: the alleyways and side streets that feed into the major arteries, rather than the arteries themselves.

### Tzurat Hapetach vs Door

The *gemara* records a dispute concerning the *mavoi mefulash* — the alleyway open at both ends into a *reshus harabim*. At the end that connects to the *reshus harabim*, what form of closure is required? The *tanna kamma* holds that a *tzurat hapetach* suffices — a symbolic doorframe consisting of two posts and a lintel, which need not be functional. According to the understanding attributed to Chananya, however, reflecting the position of Beit Hillel, an actual door is required. This debate between the *tanna kamma* and Chananya maps onto a parallel dispute between Rav and Shmuel: Rav rules like the *tanna kamma* that a *tzurat hapetach* is adequate, while Shmuel rules like Chananya that an actual door is necessary.

### Mavoi Akum Case

The *gemara* now introduces a specific case: *hahu mavoi akum de-hava be-Nahardea* — a certain bent alleyway that existed in Nahardea. A *mavoi akum* is an alleyway that does not run straight between two points of *reshus harabim* but instead bends, so that one must turn a corner to travel from one *reshus harabim* to the other. The shape can be visualized as two separate dead-end alleyways joined at a bend, each opening into a different *reshus harabim*. The *gemara* reports that in practice, the authorities of Nahardea applied to this bent alleyway both the stringency of Rav and the stringency of Shmuel — and as a result required an actual door.

### Rav's Position

To understand how both stringencies combine to produce this result, each must be examined independently. Rav's position — *torato ke-mufalash* — is that the bent alleyway is legally treated as though it runs straight through. Even though traffic must navigate a corner to pass from one *reshus harabim* to the other, Rav considers the alleyway's legal character equivalent to that of a straight *mavoi mefulash*, one that runs directly between two public domains. This is the *chumra* of Rav: imposing the more demanding classification of *mefulash* even upon the bent configuration.

### Both Stringencies

Once Rav's stringency is applied and the *mavoi akum* is treated as a straight *mavoi mefulash*, a second question arises: what does a *mavoi mefulash* require at its *reshus harabim* end? Here Shmuel's stringency enters. Shmuel rules that a *mavoi mefulash* requires not merely a *tzurat hapetach* but an actual door. The *gemara* therefore records that the authorities combined these two independent stringencies: treating the bent alley as straight (following Rav) and demanding a door for a straight *mavoi mefulash* (following Shmuel). The striking irony is that neither Rav nor Shmuel, taken individually, would have reached this conclusion. Rav himself says a *tzurat hapetach* is sufficient for a straight *mavoi mefulash*, so even under his own classification he would require no door. Shmuel says the bent alleyway is legally a dead end — *torato ke-satum* — so he would not require a door there either. Neither authority alone would have mandated a door, yet by combining each one's stringency on a different question, the practice of Nahardea arrived at precisely that result.

## Stringencies Debate

### Two Chumrot?

The practice of Nahardea prompts the *gemara* to raise a pointed methodological question: *u-mi avdinan ke-trei chumrei* — do we really adopt two *chumrot* simultaneously in this manner? Tosafot clarifies the precise difficulty. There is nothing unusual in ruling stringently on each of two independent disputes. The problem here is that neither Rav nor Shmuel would personally have endorsed the outcome produced by combining their respective stringencies. Neither authority, standing alone in both aspects of the question, would ever have required a door in the *mavoi akum* case. Is it proper to arrive, through a combination of stringencies, at a conclusion that no single *posek* would have reached?

### Beit Hillel Rule

The *gemara* cites a *brayta* to address this question. The *brayta* states: *le-olam halacha ke-Veit Hillel* — the ruling is always like Beit Hillel. Yet the same *brayta* continues: *ve-ha-rotzeh la'asot ke-divrei Beit Shammai ose, ke-divrei Beit Hillel ose* — one who wishes to follow Beit Shammai may do so, and one who wishes to follow Beit Hillel may do so. The *gemara* initially uses this *brayta* to address the question of consistency across rulings, leaving the internal contradiction within the *brayta* itself to be resolved shortly.

### Cherry-Picking Issue

The *brayta* continues with a crucial qualification. If one picks selectively — taking the *kulot* of Beit Shammai on some questions and the *kulot* of Beit Hillel on others — such a person is called a *rasha*, a wicked person. This is *kula* shopping: adopting only the lenient positions of each school without submitting to either school's overall approach. The *gemara* elsewhere offers a pointed *derasha* on the verse *ve-ami maklo yagid lo* — "my people, their staff will instruct them" — reading it as *kol ha-meikel yagid lo*, a reference to the one who always seeks out the leniency. To adopt only the leniencies of competing authorities, without genuine commitment to any coherent position, is considered a form of moral failure.

### Inconsistent Chumrot

The opposite extreme is addressed as well. If one adopts the *chumrot* of both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — always seeking the more stringent position from each school — *alav ha-katuv omer, ha-ksil ba-choshech holech*: of such a person the verse says, "the fool walks in darkness." The one who, out of ignorance rather than principled reasoning, piles stringency upon stringency from every direction, groping without a coherent framework, is characterized not as pious but as foolish.

### Consistent Choice

The *brayta* therefore demands consistency: *ei ke-Veit Shammai ke-kuleihem u-che-chumreihem, ei ke-Veit Hillel ke-kuleihem u-che-chumreihem* — either one follows Beit Shammai in all their positions, both lenient and stringent, or one follows Beit Hillel in all their positions. The underlying principle is that *pesak* requires genuine submission to a coherent legal authority or methodology, not a patchwork of convenient selections.

## Bat Kol & Exceptions

### Internal Contradiction

The *gemara* immediately recognizes that the *brayta* is internally inconsistent: *ha-gufa kashya*. On the one hand, it declares *le-olam halacha ke-divrei Veit Hillel* — we always rule like Beit Hillel. On the other hand, it says *ha-rotzeh la-asot ke-divrei Veit Shammai ose* — one may still rule like Beit Shammai. How can both statements coexist within the same *brayta*?

### Before/After Bat Kol

The *gemara* resolves this by positing that the two statements reflect two distinct historical periods: *kan kodem bat kol, kan le-achar bat kol* — one clause was stated before the heavenly voice declared the *halacha* to follow Beit Hillel, and the other was stated after. During the period before the *bat kol*, it was genuinely contested whether to rule like Beit Shammai — who were acknowledged as sharper in their reasoning — or like Beit Hillel, who represented the majority. In that period, choosing either school was a legitimate option. After the *bat kol* established *halacha ke-Veit Hillel*, the option of ruling like Beit Shammai was foreclosed.

### Rabbi Yehoshua's Stand

The *gemara* offers a second resolution: *ve-i ba'it eima, ha ve-ha le-achar bat kol* — both clauses may be read as applying after the *bat kol*, and the possibility of ruling like Beit Shammai even after the *bat kol* reflects the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, who holds that we do not follow a *bat kol* in *halachic* matters. This is the famous principle of *lo va-shamayim hi* — "it is not in heaven." In the celebrated episode of the *tannur shel achnai*, Rabbi Eliezer invoked a series of miracles to support his ruling, culminating in a heavenly voice declaring him correct. It was Rabbi Yehoshua who stood and declared that we do not heed a *bat kol* — the Torah was given to the human realm and its decisions belong there. Since Rabbi Yehoshua categorically rejects the authority of a *bat kol*, he might equally have rejected the *bat kol* that established *halacha ke-Veit Hillel*, leaving the question of Beit Shammai open even after its proclamation.

### Tosafot's Logic

Tosafot raises a difficulty with this analysis. On the one hand, the *gemara* in *Bava Metzia* affirms *lo va-shamayim hi* and the irrelevance of the *bat kol* to *halachic* adjudication; on the other hand, the *halacha* is in practice established by the *bat kol* of Beit Hillel versus Beit Shammai. Tosafot resolves this by drawing a critical distinction. In the case of the *tannur shel achnai*, the established procedure was already known: *halacha* is determined by majority vote, and the majority had already ruled against Rabbi Eliezer. The *bat kol* there was attempting to override a known and operative process, and it was therefore properly rejected. By contrast, in the dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai, no clear procedure existed for deciding between two competing schools of equal standing. With no established process to appeal to, the *bat kol* served as the legitimate and necessary arbiter. Once a process is known, a *bat kol* is irrelevant; only where process is absent does the *bat kol* fill the void.

### Analogous Debates

The *gemara* presents yet a third reading: *ve-i ba'it eima hachi ka'amar* — the *brayta* need not be read as referring to Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel at all. Rather, it addresses any situation analogous to their dispute: *kol heicha de-mishkachas trei tannai o trei amoraim de-faligi ahadadi ke-ein machlokes Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel* — whenever two *tannaim* or two *amoraim* debate in a manner structurally similar to that of the two great schools, the same rule applies. In such cases — where two coherent, comprehensive opposing positions are articulated by competing authorities — one may not selectively adopt the *kulot* of each side or the *chumrot* of each side. One must commit to the positions of one authority throughout: *ela ei ke-kulei de-mar ke-chumrei avid, o ke-kulei de-mar ke-chumrei avid* — follow all the *kulot* and *chumrot* of one, or all of the other.

## When Combine Chumrot

### Rav Yehuda's Practice

Returning now to the practical question of the *mavoi akum*, the *gemara* records that Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the difficulty by explaining that the authorities of Nahardea did in fact rule entirely according to Rav. The key is the statement of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *halacha ve-ein morin kein* — while the *halacha* is technically that a *tzurat hapetach* suffices, this ruling is not publicly taught, out of concern that people will be careless. In practice, therefore, Rav himself adopted Shmuel's requirement of a door — not as a theoretical capitulation to Shmuel's position, but as a practical measure. Rav Adda bar Abba, however, reports that Rav said *halacha u-morin kein* — the ruling is stated publicly and a *tzurat hapetach* is indeed taught as sufficient. This dispute about whether Rav considered his ruling fit for public dissemination reintroduces the question of why a door was demanded in Nahardea.

### Mutually Contradictory

Rav Shizbi then articulates the governing principle that resolves the entire discussion: *ki lo avdinan ke-chumrei de-bei trei, heicha de-sasri ahadadi* — we do not adopt the *chumrot* of two separate positions only when those *chumrot* mutually contradict one another. The prohibition against combining stringencies is not absolute; it applies specifically to cases where holding both stringencies simultaneously produces an internal logical contradiction.

### Spine/Skull Example

The *gemara* illustrates this with the case of *shidra ve-gulgoles* — the spine and skull. A *brayta* records a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding how much of a spinal column or skull, when missing, prevents the skeleton from conveying *tumah* under a tent (*ohel*). For the spine: Beit Shammai requires that two vertebrae be missing before *tumah* is no longer transmitted; Beit Hillel holds that the absence of a single vertebra suffices to disqualify the spine. For the skull: Beit Shammai holds the threshold is a hole the size made by a drill (*ke-mlo makde'ach*); Beit Hillel holds it must be large enough that its removal from a living person would cause death. Tosafot notes that even Beit Shammai grounds the threshold in what would prove fatal, but Beit Hillel sets the bar at a larger and more certain lethal size. Rav Yehuda then reports in the name of Shmuel that this same debate applies *ve-chen le-inyan tereifa* — the identical question governs when a living animal is missing vertebrae or skull bone sufficient to render it a *tereifa*.

### Contradiction Illustrated

The problem of combining stringencies in this case becomes clear upon examination. When it comes to a dead skeleton transmitting *tumah* through *ohel*, Beit Hillel's position that a single missing vertebra suffices to disqualify the spine is a *kula* — it raises the threshold for *tumah* transmission, so that more complete spines stop transmitting *tumah* earlier. But that same single-vertebra threshold, when applied to a living animal, is a *chumra* — the animal becomes a *tereifa* at a lower threshold. If one were to be strict in both directions — ruling like Beit Shammai in the *tumah* case (two vertebrae must be missing) and like Beit Hillel in the *tereifa* case (one vertebra suffices) — one would be applying the identical underlying debate in directly opposite directions across the two contexts. This is the paradigmatic case of *sasrei ahadadi* — positions that contradict each other, because the very same legal quantity is being treated as significant in one direction in one context and insignificant in the opposing direction in another.

### Mavoi Akum Revisited

With this principle established, the *gemara* returns to vindicate the practice of Nahardea. The two stringencies applied there — Rav's rule that the *mavoi akum* is treated as straight, and Shmuel's rule that a straight *mavoi mefulash* requires a door — are logically independent of one another. There is no internal contradiction in holding both simultaneously. A person could coherently believe that a bent alleyway should be treated as straight *and* that straight alleyways require a door. These positions do not cancel each other out across different contexts. The fact that neither Rav nor Shmuel individually happened to hold both stringencies is a biographical contingency, not a logical necessity. Where the *chumrot* do not contradict, combining them is permissible.

### Rabbi Akiva's Etrog

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a striking case involving Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva picked an *esrog* on the first of *Shevat* and observed two distinct stringencies regarding it — one according to Beit Shammai and one according to Beit Hillel. The background is the dispute concerning when *Rosh Hashanah* for trees falls: Beit Shammai holds it is *Rosh Chodesh Shevat*, while Beit Hillel holds it is the fifteenth of *Shevat* — *Tu B'Shvat*. The relevance concerns the agricultural tithes: in the second year of the *shemitta* cycle one gives *maaser sheni*, while in the third year one gives *maaser ani*. A fruit picked in the interval between the two positions — after *Rosh Chodesh Shevat* but before *Tu B'Shvat* — could belong to either year, depending on which school one follows. Rabbi Akiva gave both *maaser ani* and *maaser sheni*, even though giving both simultaneously is logically contradictory — the fruit can only belong to one year. The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that *Rabbi Akiva gemara istapkah lei* — Rabbi Akiva was uncertain what the tradition of Beit Hillel actually was. He did not know whether Beit Hillel placed the new year at *Rosh Chodesh Shevat* or at *Tu B'Shvat*, and therefore he acted stringently in both directions out of genuine doubt, not out of principled *pesak*. This resolution ties back to the broader discussion: actively ruling one way and then ruling the opposite way in a different case is problematic; but when one genuinely does not know which position to adopt, taking the stringent approach in all directions out of *safek* is permissible. The *gemara* thus distinguishes between a deliberate *pesak* that produces contradictory rulings and a practical response to uncertainty.

## Practical Resolution

### Summary

After the extended digression on the combination of stringencies, the *gemara* recapitulates its conclusions. The prohibition on combining *chumrot* applies when they are mutually contradictory — as in the *shidra ve-gulgoles* case, where taking both stringencies produces directly opposing determinations about the same underlying legal quantity. When the *chumrot* are logically independent and non-contradictory, as in the *mavoi akum* case with Rav and Shmuel, combining them is entirely legitimate. And when the combined stringencies are adopted not as a principled *pesak* but as a response to genuine *safek* — as with Rabbi Akiva and the *esrog* — even apparent contradictions may be tolerated. With the digression complete, the *gemara* returns to the central practical question: what precisely is required for a *mavoi mefulash*, and when does a *tzurat hapetach* suffice?

### Rav's Key Point

The *gemara* records a pivotal statement: Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav that the entire dispute — whether a *mavoi mefulash* requires a *tzurat hapetach* or a door — applies only under a specific condition. *Machlokes be-sratya mi-kan u-sratya mi-kan* — the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Chananya, and by extension between Rav and Shmuel, applies only when a major *reshus harabim* opens at both ends of the alleyway. This is the paradigmatic *mavoi mefulash*: a street with a genuine *reshus harabim* on each side, like a cross-street running between two major thoroughfares. The same applies, Rav Yehuda specifies, if both ends open into major public squares (*platya mi-kan u-platya mi-kan*). Only under those conditions is the question of *tzurat hapetach* versus door a live dispute.

### Mixed Openings

If, however, one end of the alleyway opens into a *reshus harabim* and the other opens into a valley — *sratya mi-kan u-vikah mi-kan* — the requirement is already reduced. In that case, a *tzurat hapetach* on the *reshus harabim* side and a *lechi* or *korah* on the valley side is sufficient. The full weight of the *mavoi mefulash* requirements, including the debate over whether a door is needed, pertains only to the most demanding configuration.

### Valley Case

If both ends open into valleys — *vikah mi-kan u-vikah mi-kan* — neither end connects to a genuine *reshus harabim* at all. In that case, the *gemara* affirms that a *tzurat hapetach* on one side and a *lechi* or *korah* on the other unquestionably suffices, and there is no debate. This ruling is the practical foundation for the institution of modern *eruvin*. Since the *rishonim* — prominently Rashi and the *geonim* — established that a genuine *reshus harabim* requires six hundred thousand people traversing the street daily, virtually no modern urban street qualifies as a *reshus harabim*. Our streets are effectively treated as *karmelis*, not *reshus harabim*. Once neither end of an alleyway opens into a true *reshus harabim*, the more demanding requirements drop away, and a *tzurat hapetach* suffices — which is precisely what modern *eruvin* employ.

### Courtyard Case

At the end of the same passage, Rav Yehuda adds a further ruling, though this time without attributing it to Rav — it is stated simply in the name of Rav Yehuda himself. *Im haya mavoi kala le-rachavah ein tzarikh klum* — if the alleyway terminates into a *rachavah*, a walled backyard, nothing is required at that end. The case, as Rashi clarifies, presupposes that the opposite wall of the backyard is breached and opens into a *reshus harabim* — so the alleyway technically connects through the *rachavah* to a *reshus harabim*. Nevertheless, because the *rachavah* itself is a walled enclosure, the end of the *mavoi* that faces the *rachavah* requires no additional closure. One would still place a *lechi* or *korah* on the far side, but at the *rachavah* end, the existing walls are sufficient.

## Abaye Challenge

### Attribution Correction

Abaye takes issue with Rav Yosef's presentation. The final ruling — that a *mavoi* terminating in a walled *rachavah* requires nothing at that end — was cited by Rav Yehuda without specifying whose ruling it was. Abaye argues that this must be the position of Shmuel, not Rav: *ha-din de-Rav Yehuda de-Shmuel hi*. His reason is that if attributed to Rav, it would contradict another ruling that Rav himself stated.

### Rav's Parallel Case

The contradicting statement of Rav is preserved in a report by Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba in Rav's name: *mavoi she-nifratz bi-mlo'o le-chatzer, ve-nifretzah chatzer kenegdo* — a *mavoi* that has been broken open across its entire width into a *chatzer*, and the *chatzer* in turn is breached on its opposite side. This is structurally identical to the *rachavah* case, substituting *chatzer* for *rachavah*. In this scenario, Rav says: *chatzer muteret u-mavoi assur* — the *chatzer* is permitted for carrying but the *mavoi* is forbidden.

### Rav's Case Explained

If the *rachavah* case requires nothing at that end and permits carrying in the *mavoi*, how can the nearly identical *chatzer* case forbid carrying in the *mavoi*? This apparent contradiction is what leads Abaye to conclude that the *rachavah* ruling cannot be Rav's. The initial assumption, before the *gemara* fully resolves the matter, is that the *mavoi* is forbidden because it remains legally connected to the *reshus harabim* through the breach in the *chatzer's* far wall — and Rav's ruling that the *chatzer* is permitted but the *mavoi* is not suggests that Rav regards the *mavoi* as exposed to the *reshus harabim* in a way that the *chatzer* is not.

### Perspective Difference

Abaye's reasoning about why the *chatzer* is permitted but the *mavoi* forbidden rests on a difference of perspective and inclusion. From the *chatzer's* standpoint, the opening into the *mavoi* is only a partial breach — the *chatzer* retains walls on the *mavoi* side except for the doorway-sized opening. The *chatzer* does not feel fully merged with the *mavoi*; its residents can reasonably view themselves as enclosed and separate. From the *mavoi's* standpoint, however, the entire wall separating it from the *chatzer* has collapsed — *nifratz bi-mlo'o*. The *mavoi* is entirely open to the *chatzer* and cannot claim any separation from it. This asymmetry — full openness from one direction, partial openness from the other — produces the asymmetric ruling: the *chatzer* is permitted, the *mavoi* is not.

### Precedent Cited

Rav Yosef's response is disarmingly candid: *ana lo yadana* — I do not know the full reasoning. He does not possess the theoretical explanation for why Rav's case comes out differently. What he does know is the practical precedent: *uvda hava be-Dura di-Re'uta* — there was an actual case in the village of shepherds, a *mavoi* that terminated in a *rachavah*, and it came before Rav Yehuda, who ruled that nothing was required. That much Rav Yosef asserts confidently. As for whether it is consistent with Rav's other ruling — if not, let it be attributed to Shmuel. The precedent stands regardless: *tehavei mi-shmeih de-Shmuel ve-lo kashya midi*.

### Rav Sheshet's Resolution

Rav Sheshet then provides a resolution that vindicates both rulings as consistent with Rav. He addresses it to Rav Shmuel bar Abba — some say to Rav Yosef bar Abba — with the words *asbera lach*, "let me explain it to you." The key is a distinction between two entirely different types of problems.

### Walls vs Eruv

The *rachavah* case and the *chatzer* case differ not in their wall configurations but in whether the residents of the *mavoi* and the adjacent space have made a joint *eruv*. *Kan she-irvu bnei chatzer im bnei mavoi, kan she-lo irvu* — in one case the residents of the *chatzer* and the *mavoi* made an *eruv* together, and in the other they did not. When an *eruv* has been made jointly, the wall configuration of the *rachavah* is sufficient to permit carrying; nothing additional is needed at the *rachavah* end of the *mavoi*. In the *chatzer* case, no joint *eruv* was made. The *chatzer* is permitted because from the *chatzer's* perspective, the *mavoi* is not fully merged with it — its opening into the *mavoi* is only partial, so the *chatzer* residents are unaffected. The *mavoi*, however, is entirely open into the *chatzer*, and since the *mavoi* residents have not joined in an *eruv* with the *chatzer* residents, the *mavoi* residents are prohibited from carrying. The problem in the *mavoi* is not a deficiency of walls — the walls of the *rachavah* or *chatzer* are adequate from a structural standpoint — but rather the absence of a joint *eruv* joining the two groups. There is thus no contradiction between Rav's rulings: the *rachavah* case assumes a joint *eruv* was made, and the *chatzer* case assumes it was not.

## Three-Walled Alley

### Three-Wall Case

The *gemara* now transitions to a new case: the *mavoi ha-asui ke-nadli* — the alleyway shaped like a centipede, that is, a rectangular enclosure with three complete walls and one side entirely open. Having resolved the questions surrounding the *mavoi mefulash* and its variants, the *gemara* turns to this distinct configuration where the issue is not two open ends but rather a single open face on what is otherwise a three-sided enclosure.

### Rav vs Shmuel

The dispute is stated directly. Rav holds that the *mavoi asui ke-nadli* requires nothing on the fourth, open side — the three existing walls are sufficient to permit carrying within. Shmuel holds that a *lechi* or *korah* is required on the open fourth side. The *gemara* will proceed to examine the reasoning underlying this disagreement.
