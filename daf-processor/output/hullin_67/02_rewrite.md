# Hullin 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Water Insects Intro

### Perek Overview

Perek *elu tereifos* has been a remarkable chapter, covering not only the laws of *treifos* but also the permitted and forbidden species of birds, fish, and grasshoppers. Today's daf is 67, and we pick up at the very bottom of 66b, where the Gemara turns to the subject of water insects.

### Oceans vs. Wells

The Gemara had been discussing the rule that the *kosher simanim* — fins and scales — are required only for creatures found in oceans and rivers. When it comes to water insects found in standing water, such as well water, the matter is different: because such water does not correspond to the biblical categories of oceans and rivers, it is irrelevant whether the creatures in it possess *kosher* or non-*kosher simanim*. The standing water exemption applies to small insects that grow naturally within still water, and the key dividing line, as we will see, is whether the water in question is flowing or standing.

One might wonder: what if a creature from running water found its way into a well through an underground aquifer — a cave fish, for instance, in a still pond? The answer, which the daf will clarify, is that the permission applies only to creatures that originally formed in the standing water itself. If an organism grew in an ocean or river and subsequently entered a well, it remains problematic.

It is worth noting in passing that *mayim chayim* — water fit for use in certain ritual contexts — does not mean running water in the sense relevant here. Water from an underground spring qualifies as *mayim chayim*, yet well water is nonetheless considered standing water for our purposes. The distinction the Gemara draws is between flowing and non-flowing water, not between natural and artificial sources.

### Bug Analogies

The discussion here is in some respects an instantiation of the broader principle concerning *sheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz*. We know from earlier in the perek that a bug found inside a fruit or vegetable is surprisingly not problematic, provided it grew within the fruit without ever being exposed to the ground — without ever having *shoretz al ha'aretz*. The current discussion follows a similar logic: creatures that grow within standing water and are never separated from it are not forbidden.

There is, however, an important distinction. By land, the prohibition is *sheretz ha'aretz* — something that creeps on the ground. A bug that grew inside a fruit and never emerged is simply not in that category. By water, the creatures involved are *sheretz hamayim*, and the Torah does use that term for everything in the water, whether we would colloquially call it an insect or a sea creature. Still, the parallel holds to a degree: the permission is tied to the creature never having separated from the medium in which it grew, just as the fruit-bug is permitted only so long as it never emerged from the fruit.

### Insect Origins

One further point bears mentioning before turning to the Gemara itself. Modern science understands that insects appearing inside a fruit grew there because eggs were laid within it — the bug did not arise spontaneously from the fruit's own substance. The Gemara, however, proceeds from the assumption that these creatures often *grow from* the material in which they appear — that the water itself, or the flesh, generates the insect. This assumption will play a meaningful role later in the daf, particularly in the discussion about worms found in animal flesh and in fish.

## Hermeneutics

### Vessels Eima

The Gemara opens with a surprising interpretive possibility. Since the verse says "this you may eat from all that is in the water," one might read the subsequent qualification of oceans and rivers not as a restriction but as an expansion: perhaps in oceans and rivers, creatures require *simanim* — but in vessels, *keilim*, even creatures lacking *simanim* are permitted. On this reading, the Torah is more lenient about vessels.

The Gemara then entertains the reverse: perhaps the verse is actually *stricter* about vessels. Perhaps one should read it as follows — in oceans and rivers, creatures with *simanim* may be eaten, but creatures in vessels may never be eaten regardless of *simanim*. The Gemara dismisses this: *lo salka daatach* — it cannot be right. The verse framing the prohibition says *vechol asher ein lo snapir vekaskeset bayamim uvanachalim* — anything lacking fins and scales *in the oceans and rivers* is forbidden. The implication of the prohibition verse is that only creatures in oceans and rivers lacking *simanim* are forbidden. Creatures in other waters — by implication — may always be eaten, regardless of whether they have *simanim*.

So the fundamental principle emerges: the only category of forbidden water-creature is one found in running water that lacks the *kosher simanim*. Everything in other waters is permitted. This has an immediate and entirely practical consequence: one may drink directly from a well without worrying about whatever tiny organisms may live in it. The *rosh* articulates this precisely: one need not be concerned that one might swallow worms or insects that grow in the water of wells and cisterns.

### Klal U-Prat

The Gemara does not stop there, however. It asks: granted that we derive from the prohibition verse that standing water is always permitted — but how far does the restriction on running water extend? Perhaps it should be read with perfect specificity: the Torah mentions only oceans and rivers (*yamim venachalim*), and one should not extrapolate beyond those precise categories. Streams, gullies, trenches, and ravines — *nitzim vecharitzim* — would then be unproblematic even if the water runs.

The Gemara addresses this through the hermeneutical principle of *klal u-prat u-klal*: general, specific, general. The verses contain the word *bamayim* (in the water) twice — once before and once after the specific mention of *bayamim uvanachalim* — creating the structure needed for this *drasha*. A *klal u-prat u-klal* permits us to expand beyond the literal *prat* and include anything similar to it. Since the specific is flowing water — *mayim nov'im* — we include all flowing water in the restriction, not only oceans and rivers proper.

### Ravina's Fix

A difficulty arises: the two instances of *bamayim* in the verse do not appear in the order A-B-A (general, specific, general) but rather A-A-B (general, general, specific). This structure does not naturally support a *klal prat u-klal drasha*. Ravina resolves this by citing a rule transmitted in the west, in *eretz yisrael*: whenever one encounters two consecutive *klalot* followed by a *prat* — that is, the pattern A-A-B rather than A-B-A — one rearranges the order mentally, placing the *prat* between the two *klalot*, and then applies the standard *klal u-prat u-klal* analysis. This is a recognized hermeneutical technique, and Ravina's application of it here allows the standard *drasha* to proceed.

### Water Classification

With the *klal u-prat u-klal drasha* in place, the Gemara establishes that the restriction applies to all flowing water (*mayim nov'im*), while standing water is consistently permitted. The Gemara then considers an alternative drawing of the line: perhaps the *prat* of oceans and rivers teaches that the relevant category is not flowing water but *natural* water — *mayim gedelim al gabei karka* — water that grows upon the ground. On this reading, even wells and cisterns, which are natural formations, would be forbidden, and only creatures growing in artificial vessels (*keilim*) would be permitted.

The Gemara rejects this more stringent position with a pointed observation: *im kein, tochlu mai ahani lei* — if that were so, what purpose does the verse serve when it says "this you may eat from all that is in the water"? That verse is expanding the category of what is permissible. If the *klal u-prat* analysis, left to itself, would only permit creatures in artificial vessels, the additional verse saying "you may eat from anything in the water" comes to widen that permission to include standing water in natural settings — wells, cisterns, and caves. This resolves the tension and establishes the final ruling: running water is forbidden (the *simanim* requirement applies), while all standing water — whether vessels or natural cisterns — is permitted.

The Gemara elaborates further. *mai ribei* — what does the general term include in the forbidden category? *charitzim une'itzim le'issura* — even channels and ditches that carry running water are included in the prohibition. *u'mai mi'et* — what does the *miyut* exclude from the prohibition? *borot shichin ume'arot leheira* — cisterns, wells, and caves, all of which are permitted. The reason is articulated by the *tanna* Matya bar Yehuda: wells and cisterns are permitted because their water is *atzur*, gathered and standing, like vessels — and thus sufficiently unlike the specific of oceans and rivers. Ditches and channels, by contrast, are not gathered but running, and are therefore included in the prohibition.

## Ribbuy U-Miyut

### R. Yishmael

The Gemara now presents an alternative hermeneutical derivation of the same conclusion, attributed to the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The school of Rabbi Yishmael observes that the word *bamayim* appears twice in the relevant verses, in the sequence A-A-B rather than A-B-A. This pattern, they say, is not to be read as a *klal u-prat* — general and specific — but rather as *ribbuy u-miyut*: inclusive and exclusive. The method of *ribbuy u-miyut* yields different results than *klal u-prat u-klal* and is associated with a more expansive interpretive stance.

### Menorah Example

The distinction between the two methods is best illustrated by a classic example involving the *menorah*. The relevant verse uses the pattern: a general term (make it of anything), then gold as a specific, then a general term again (make the *menorah*). Under *klal u-prat u-klal*, one concludes that the *menorah* must be made of something similar to gold — that is, metal, though not necessarily precious metal. Under *ribbuy u-miyut ve-ribbuy*, the analysis is more expansive: one includes everything except the item most dissimilar to gold. The most dissimilar material to gold would be earthenware pottery — the cheapest and most fragile material. Everything else, including wood, would be acceptable. This explains the historical question of whether the *chashmonaim* fashioned the *menorah* from wood when gold was unavailable.

### Rashi Explains

The deeper difference between the two methods, as Rashi explains, lies not only in the outcome at the A-B-A stage but in what the interpreter is doing at the A-B stage itself. Under *klal u-prat*, when one has only the general followed by the specific, one treats the specific as the *totality* of what is meant — the general statement is entirely collapsed into the specific example. Only when a second general statement follows does one expand slightly beyond the specific to include things *similar* to it.

Under *ribbuy u-miyut*, however, the interpreter never treats the specific as exhaustive even at the A-B stage. From the outset, the specific is understood as an *example* — an illustration — not as the complete definition. The *miyut* is already being interpreted abstractly, as pointing toward a category rather than listing its members. Consequently, at the A-B stage of *ribbuy u-miyut*, one already includes anything *like* the specific. When the final *ribbuy* then appears, it expands even further, including everything except what is most dissimilar from the specific.

In the present context, this means that Rabbi Yishmael's school, though normally inclined toward the *klal u-prat* method, treats the doubled *bamayim* — the A-A-B pattern — as a signal to apply the more expansive *ribbuy u-miyut* analysis. The doubled general term at the outset indicates that the restriction should be read broadly and inclusively.

### Results

Applied to the case of water insects, the *ribbuy u-miyut* analysis runs as follows. *bamayim* — water — is an inclusive term (*ribbuy*). *bayamim uvanachalim* — oceans and rivers — is a limiting term (*miyut*). *bamayim* again — a second inclusive term (*ribbuy*). The sequence is *ribbuy, miyut, ribbuy*, which includes almost everything. What does it include in the forbidden category? *charitzim une'itzim le'issura* — even gullies and ditches, even if they are not oceans or rivers, are included in the requirement for *simanim*. What does the *miyut* exclude from the prohibition? *borot shichin ume'arot le-heira* — wells, cisterns, and caves are permitted.

Tosafot argues that this method, being more expansive, actually yields a *stricter* result than the *klal u-prat u-klal* approach in one respect: it would include gullies and ditches even if the water in them is standing, whereas the earlier analysis emphasized the quality of flowing water as the operative criterion. Rashi, however, understands both methods as arriving at the same practical conclusion. In any case, the Gemara again notes that one might have argued the *ribbuy u-miyut* logic should be even stricter — including wells and cisterns as forbidden — and once again answers that the verse permitting eating from things in the water (*tochlu kol asher bamayim*) pulls the line back and allows standing water.

The Gemara then asks: why not reverse the categories — permit gullies and forbid wells? The answer, supplied by Matya bar Yehuda, is that wells and cisterns are *atzurim kekeilim* — their water is gathered and contained, like vessels — and this structural similarity to vessels, which are clearly permissible, is what places them in the permitted category. Gullies and channels, where water runs rather than stands, lack this quality and remain forbidden.

## Meforash/Satum

### Rav Acha/Ravina

With the substantive ruling established, the Gemara turns to a question it raised somewhat earlier: since one verse permits eating from things in the water and another verse forbids eating certain water creatures, which of these verses is the *meforash* — the explicit source — for the rule that standing water is always permissible, and which is the *satum* — the implicit or less clearly communicating source? Rav Acha and Ravina disagree. One holds that the eating verse is the *meforash* source and the prohibition verse is *satum*; the other reverses this.

### Verse Analysis

The two verses in question are as follows. The first is the positive verse: *v'et zeh tokhlu mikol asher bamayim, kol asher lo snapir ve'kaskeset bamayim, bayamim uvanachalim, otam tokhlu* — this is what you may eat from all that is in the water; anything possessing fins and scales, in the water, in the oceans and the rivers, those you may eat. This verse mentions both *bamayim* and *bayamim uvanachalim* in the context of what is permitted, making it at least plausible that one derives the standing-water permission directly from here.

The second is the prohibition verse: *vekhol asher ein lo snapir ve'kaskeset bayamim uvanachalim mikol sheretz hamayim umikol nefesh hachayah asher bamayim sheketz hem lachem* — anything lacking fins and scales in the oceans and the rivers, from all the swarming things of the water and from all living beings in the water, they are an abomination to you. Note that this verse declares something an abomination in the context of oceans and rivers, but the actual prohibition — *mibsaram lo tokhlu*, do not eat from their flesh — appears in the following verse, no longer directly coupled with the qualifier *bayamim uvanachalim*.

The one who says the eating verse is the *meforash* reasons that from that verse one directly learns the permission for vessels: since the eating verse says *bayamim uvanachalim* as the relevant locus, vessels and standing water are implicitly distinguished as permitted. The one who says the prohibition verse is the *meforash* reasons that if one had only the eating verse, one might actually have read it *more strictly* — understanding that creatures in oceans and rivers with *simanim* may be eaten, but that creatures in other waters may not be eaten at all. It is precisely the prohibition verse, which says that the only forbidden category is creatures in oceans and rivers lacking *simanim*, that teaches us standing water is always permissible. On this view, the prohibition verse is the anchor and the eating verse is clarified by it rather than the reverse.

### Practical Conclusion

Both positions agree on the bottom line: insects and water creatures that grow in wells, cisterns, and other standing water are permitted, and one may drink from such water without concern. The question of which verse is *meforash* and which is *satum* is ultimately a technical hermeneutical point. As the Gemara notes, we need both verses working together: without the prohibition verse, the eating verse alone might have been read to restrict rather than expand permission for standing water; without the eating verse, the prohibition verse alone might have been read to restrict even wells. Together they establish the ruling.

A practical postscript arose in discussion: the question of whether modern municipal water — which may flow in from rivers and streams before being impounded in a reservoir — falls under the category of running or standing water. The Croton Reservoir, which supplies New York City, is in fact fed by tributaries of the Croton River, which flows into the Hudson. That the water ultimately sits in a reservoir does not fully resolve the question of its original character. This practical matter is not addressed in the Gemara but illustrates how the distinction between flowing and standing water retains real relevance.

## Practical Cases

### Mead & Chips

Rav Huna rules: a person should not pour mead through small chips of wood — which were used as a filter — at night. The concern is that an insect naturally growing within the mead might be separated out onto the chips, and then fall back into the cup. If the separation occurred during the day and one saw it happen, one would either refrain from drinking or remove the insect. At night, however, if one later sees an insect in the mead, one will assume it grew there naturally and never left its original medium — and thus conclude it is permitted — unaware that it had in fact separated and returned.

The critical principle at work here is that an insect which grew naturally in a liquid such as mead is permitted so long as it remains in its natural environment. The moment it separates — even onto a filter, even briefly — it becomes subject to the prohibition of *sheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz*, a creeping thing that has crept upon the ground. The term *al ha'aretz* does not require literal contact with the earth; it means simply that the creature is no longer within the liquid medium in which it grew.

### Separation Issue

The Gemara raises an obvious extension of this concern: even without sifting, when one pours from a pitcher into a cup, might one not worry that an insect got caught on the rim of the pitcher and then fell into the cup, having thus briefly separated? The Gemara dismisses this: *hainu rivisa* — that is where it grows. When an insect is in a vessel containing the liquid in which it originated, the natural situation includes contact with the vessel's walls and rim; this does not constitute meaningful separation. Only a genuine act of separation — such as being sifted out onto the chips — crosses the threshold.

### Well Water

A *braisa* confirms this framework directly in the context of well water. The verse *tochlu mikol asher bamayim* — you may eat from all that is in the water — is the source for drinking from wells despite the presence of naturally occurring organisms. One might have worried: as one draws water in a bucket and pours it into a cup, might an insect cling to the rim of the bucket and fall back in? Again the answer is *hainu rivisa*: the bucket is the organism's natural habitat at that moment, and contact with its wall is not a separation that triggers *sheretz al ha'aretz*.

### Sifting Rule

Rav Chisda offers a supporting *braisa*: the verse *kol hasheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz* — all the swarming things that swarm upon the earth — comes to include even the *yavchushin*, a type of small insect commonly found in wine, that have been sifted out. The language is precise: *ta'ama de-sinenam* — the reason they are forbidden is specifically that they were sifted out. *ha lo sinenam, shari* — if they were not sifted out, they are permitted. This confirms that naturally occurring insects in wine or mead remain permitted so long as they are never separated from their host medium, and become forbidden the moment they are extracted.

The conceptual picture that emerges: insects naturally growing in a liquid are categorized as *sheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz* in a latent sense, but do not yet incur the prohibition because they have not yet crept upon the ground. Once separated from their medium, even momentarily, that potential prohibition is actualized.

## Shmuel's Rule

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel introduces a remarkably strict position. He rules that a cucumber — or by extension any fruit or vegetable — in which bugs formed *while it was still attached to the ground* renders those bugs forbidden as *sheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz*, even though the bugs themselves never left the fruit. The rationale is that while the fruit was connected to the ground, the insects' movement within it is considered movement upon the ground.

This is an extremely far-reaching stringency. Without Shmuel's rule, one could say: find a bug inside a vegetable, confirm there is no exit hole visible from the outside, conclude it grew within and never emerged, and permit it. With Shmuel's rule, one must also determine *when* the bug formed — whether before or after the fruit was picked. This determination is often practically impossible.

### Supporting Braisot

The Gemara attempts to find support for Shmuel from two *braisos*. The first *braisa* lists bugs that are permitted: the *zizin* in lentils, the small insects in chickpeas (*kalisin*), the worms in dates, and the worms in pressed figs — all permitted because they grew inside the fruit without having crept on the ground. The second *braisa* includes as forbidden the worms at the stems of olives (*ikrei zeitim*) and at the stems of grapevines (*ikrei gefanim*). One might read these two *braisos* as distinguishing between bugs that formed while the fruit was attached to the ground (forbidden, per Shmuel) and bugs that formed after detachment (permitted).

### Rejection Logic

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading. *lo* — no; *idi ve-idi be-aveha* — both *braisos* could be speaking of bugs that formed while the fruit was still attached. The distinction is not about when the bug formed but about *where* in the plant it grew. Bugs inside the fruit itself — in the flesh of the date, within the lentil, inside the fig — are permitted regardless of when they formed, because they were enclosed and never exposed to the open air. The bugs at the stems of olives and grapevines are forbidden because those stems are exposed to the outside. *dika nami* — this reading is linguistically confirmed by the *braisa* itself, which specifies *ikrei zeitim* and *ikrei gefanim* — the stems — which are indeed external and exposed. The *braisos* thus do not support Shmuel; to the contrary, they suggest that the operative criterion is exposure to air, not the timing of formation relative to detachment from the ground.

### Tosafot Leniency

This leaves the question of how to rule. Tosafot cites Rabbenu Tam, Rabbenu Netanel, and the Riva as concluding that the *halacha* does not follow Shmuel. However, Tosafot also notes that *halachot gedolot* and the *she'iltot* of Rav Achai rule in accordance with Shmuel, creating a genuine dispute with significant practical consequences.

Tosafot then offers a crucial leniency that applies even according to Shmuel. Speaking of certain legumes and peas in which small insects are commonly found — insects whose formation causes the outer shell to darken — Tosafot rules that even according to Shmuel, such insects are not forbidden. The reason: *kol zman shelo richash* — as long as the insect has not yet moved. If the bug grew beneath the shell and never moved while the fruit was attached to the ground, it does not qualify as *hashoretz al ha'aretz*, even on Shmuel's stringent view. Shmuel's prohibition applies when the insect is actively moving within the fruit while it is attached; an insect that formed but remained immobile under the shell has not yet crept. When one removes the shell and finds the bug, it may begin to move at that moment — but that is after detachment. This leniency was practically essential in an era when perfectly bug-free produce was often unavailable.

## Rav Yosef's Questions

### Separated Dead Bug

Rav Yosef poses a series of unresolved questions, all circling around the precise conditions under which an insect transitions from permitted to forbidden. The first: *pirsha u-mesa mahu* — what if an insect separated from the fruit but died at the very moment of separation, before having a chance to move? Is the act of separation itself sufficient to incur the prohibition, or must the insect actually move while outside the fruit?

### Partial Exposure

*Miktzasah mahu* — what if only part of the insect emerged from the fruit? Does partial separation suffice to trigger the prohibition?

### Air Exposure

*La'avir ha'olam mahu* — what if the insect came out of the fruit but did not actually touch the ground, only the open air? This question connects to the earlier discussion of the wood-chip filter: the chips are not the ground, yet contact with them was apparently sufficient to constitute separation. Does exposure to open air alone — without any contact with a surface — complete the prohibition?

### Date Roof

Rav Ashi adds further questions. *legag timrah mahu* — what of an insect that emerges to the very top surface of a date — the *gag*, the roof — but remains there, neither departing into open air nor re-entering the body of the fruit? A date has a small indentation near where the stem was, and the question envisions an insect that pokes out into that indentation. Is this already an *avir ha'olam* exposure, or is it still within the fruit's natural boundary?

### Internal Movement

*Legar'inusa mahu* — what of an insect that moves not outward but from the pit of the date into its surrounding flesh? The assumption had been that anywhere within the fruit is permissible, but for dates the Gemara appears to assume the insect normally grows within the pit specifically. If it then migrates from pit to flesh, has it left its original habitat in a way that changes its status?

### Fruit-to-Fruit

*Mitimrei letimrei mahu* — what if an insect passes from one date into an adjacent date, without ever passing through open air, when two dates were pressed together?

### Teiku Conclusion

The Gemara answers all of these questions with a single word: *teiku* — they remain unresolved. These cases illustrate just how difficult it is to draw precise lines once one commits to the principle that separation from the original medium is the operative trigger. In an era when produce inspection is easier and stricter standards are routinely applied, these unresolved questions carry correspondingly greater weight; when the easier path was simply to find a basis for permission, the ambiguities were tolerable.

## Animal Worms

### Kokiani Forbidden

Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi rules: *kokiani assiri* — the *kokiani* worms, found in the lungs and liver of an animal, are forbidden. The reason: *me'alma asu* — they came from outside the animal; they did not grow within it originally.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

Rav Ashi challenges this immediately. If the *kokiani* truly came from outside — presumably swallowed with the animal's food — why are they found in the lungs and liver? They should have passed through the digestive tract and be found in the intestines or the stomach. Their presence in the lungs and liver is difficult to explain if they entered through the mouth.

### Formed From Flesh

An alternative view is then presented: some say Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi originally ruled *kokiani shari* — the *kokiani* are permitted. Why? *minei gavli* — they are formed from the flesh of the animal itself. Here the Gemara makes explicit the ancient assumption about spontaneous generation: the flesh literally becomes the bug. Since these organisms arise from the animal's own tissue and not from any external source, they would — on the analogy of bugs formed within a fruit — be permitted.

### Nostril Entry

Rav Ashi then provides the resolution that leads to the final ruling. The entry route is neither the mouth nor spontaneous generation from the flesh: *minyiraya ayili be'ushi* — they enter through the nostrils during the animal's breathing. When the animal inhales, these tiny organisms enter through the nose, travel through the passages connected to the lungs, and from there reach the liver — which the Gemara treats as connected to the lungs. This explains why they are found in the lungs and liver and not in the digestive tract.

### Final Ruling

*Vehilchesa kokiani assiri* — the final *halacha* is that *kokiani* are forbidden. Because they enter from outside via breathing, they are external organisms that have entered the animal, not creatures formed from the animal's own substance. They are therefore subject to the prohibition applicable to external insects.

## Meat vs. Fish

### Beast Worms

*Toloy darna de-bisra assiri* — worms found in the flesh of a beast are forbidden. This ruling will require explanation, since one might have supposed that such worms, growing within the flesh of a properly slaughtered animal, should be analogous to bugs within a fruit — and thus permitted.

### Fish Worms OK

By contrast, *dichvurei de-chayei ha-yamim* — worms found in the flesh of fish — *sharyin*, are permitted.

### Hide & Eat

Ravina offered his mother practical guidance on this point: if you find such worms in the fish, hide them from my sight — I would rather not see them and be disgusted — but I will eat the fish, because *halacha* permits it.

### Origin Theory

Rav Mesharsheya son of Rav Aha explains the distinction by posing the question: how is this different from what a *braisa* teaches, that *et nivlatam tishketzu* — you shall treat their carcasses as abominable — comes to include the *darnim* found in the flesh of a beast, which are forbidden? Why are worms in beast flesh forbidden while worms in fish flesh are permitted?

### Shechita Theory

The answer introduces an entirely new conceptual framework, distinct from the question of whether the organism came from outside or grew within. *behema bi-shechita hu de-mishtaryia* — a beast becomes permitted only through *shechita*. These worms, the Gemara assumes, are formed from (*gavlin* — grew out of) the flesh of the beast itself. They began as beast meat. Meat of a beast requires *shechita* to be permitted. At the moment *shechita* was performed, these organisms were already in their worm-form and distinct from the meat — and so *shechita* did not render them permitted. *be-issuraiyu kaimi* — they remain in their forbidden state: not forbidden as insects, but forbidden as beast meat that was never subject to *shechita*. They are, in effect, a piece of the beast that was transformed *after* the slaughter had already taken effect on the rest of the animal, leaving them outside the scope of the *shechita*.

Fish, however, do not require *shechita*; fish flesh is permitted through mere gathering — *asifa*. Thus even if the worms grew from (*gavlin*) the fish's own flesh, that flesh was already entirely permitted at the moment of gathering. *ki gavlin be-hetera gavlin* — they grew into being within something that was already permitted, and so they too are permitted.

## Creeping Things

### Belly Crawlers

The Gemara now turns to a systematic enumeration of the other categories of forbidden creeping things on land. *holech al gachon* — that which goes on its belly — this is the snake (*nachash*). The verse's inclusive language (*kol*) extends to other creatures similar to the snake, *de-domeh le-shilshul* — anything resembling a slithery crawling thing, including various types of worms.

### Four-Legged

*Al arba* — that which goes on four legs — this refers to the scorpion (*akrav*), which moves in its distinctive shuffling gait. The inclusive language again extends to *hachipushit* and anything similar to it. Rashi honestly acknowledges that he does not know precisely what a *chipushit* is; in modern Hebrew the word is used for a ladybug.

### Multi-Legged

*Marbe raglayim* — that which has many legs — this is the *nadal*. Rashi explains: *me'at raglayim korin lo* — they call it the hundred-legged creature — that is, the centipede.

### Inclusive Rule

*Lerabot et ha-domeh ve-et ha-domeh le-domeh* — to include anything similar and anything similar to what is similar. The principle is a sweeping one: any creature not falling within the categories of a *kosher* mammal (split hooves, chewing cud), a *kosher* fish (fins and scales), or a *kosher* bird is forbidden. Every other creeping, crawling, or multi-legged creature is included within these prohibitions.

## Livyatan

### R. Yossi Opinion

A final *braisa* closes the perek with an aggadic teaching. Rabbi Yossi ben Durmaskit declares: the *livyatan* — the great sea creature whose flesh will be served at the banquet for the righteous in the World to Come — *dag tahor hu*, is a *kosher* fish.

### Biblical Support

The source is a verse from Job: *ga'ava afikei maginim tachtav chadudei cheres* — pride of the channels of shields is upon it, underneath it are the sharp shards of pottery. *afikei maginim* — the channels of shields — *ellu kaskasim* — these are the scales; the things that protect it like shields are its scales. *tachtav chadudei cheres* — under it are the sharp shards of pottery — *ellu snapirim* — these are the fins; they are like shards of pottery in the way they cut through the water, enabling the creature to swim. Since the *livyatan* has both scales and fins, it possesses the *simanim* of a *kosher* fish, and the righteous may eat it at the great feast to come.

With that, the *hadran* is recited and we close on *elu tereifos*.

## Treifot Intro

### Beast Treifot

The third perek opens with its *mishna*: *elu treifot be-behema* — these are the *treifot* in a large domesticated animal.

### Key Defects

The Mishna lists the foundational cases of *treifa*. *nekuvat ha-veshet* — a perforation of the gullet. *pesukat ha-gargeret* — a severed windpipe. *nikav krum shel moach* — if the membrane of the brain was punctured. *nikav ha-lev le-veit chalalo* — if the heart was punctured to its internal cavity. *nishberah ha-shedrah ve-nifsek ha-chut shelah* — if the spinal column was broken and its spinal cord was severed.

### Liver Rule

*Nitlah ha-kaved ve-nishtayer heimena kezayit, harei zu kesheira* — if the liver was removed and there remains of it at least a *kezayit*, the animal is *kosher*. The rule is not merely that some liver must remain; the minimum is a *kezayit*. If even that much remains intact, the animal is valid.

### More Defects

*Nikav ha-mases u-veit ha-kosot la-chutz* — if the omasum or the reticulum were punctured through to the outside. *naflah min ha-gag* — if the animal fell from a roof. *nishtabru rov tzaloteha* — if the majority of its ribs were broken.

### Mauling Rules

*U-derusat ha-ze'ev* — and the mauling of a wolf renders the animal a *treifa*. Rabbi Yehuda qualifies: *derusat ha-ze'ev ba-dekah* — the mauling of a wolf applies to small cattle, for that is within a wolf's power to inflict lethal damage. *u-derusat ha-ari ba-gasah* — the mauling of a lion applies to large cattle. *derusat ha-netz be-of ha-dak* — the mauling of a hawk in small birds. *u-derusat ha-gas be-of ha-gas* — the mauling of a larger predatory bird in large birds. The criterion, in other words, is scaled to the relative strength of predator and prey.

### General Rule

*Zeh ha-klal: kol she-ein kamohah chayyah, treifah* — this is the general rule: any animal that cannot survive as it is, is a *treifa*. This overarching principle encompasses all the specific cases enumerated, and serves as the conceptual foundation for the entire perek to follow.
