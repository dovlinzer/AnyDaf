# Shabbat 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Spices & Tziruf

### Spice Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּבְלִין שְׁנַיִם בִּגְמִיל שְׁמוֹס, מִין אֶחָד אוֹ מִשְׁלוֹשָׁה מִינִים אֲסוּרִים וּמִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a baraita: **Spices** that are **two** or three in number, whether of **one type** or **of three different types, are forbidden** and **combine with one another.**

The dominant theme of this section of the *masechta* continues to be minimum sizes required to be *chayav* for carrying on *Shabbos*. The *mishna* states: *tavlin kidei letaveil beitza kalla* — spices sufficient to season a light egg, which Rashi identifies as a hen's egg prepared scrambled or fried. That much is the threshold for *chiyuv*. The *gemara* then raises a contradiction from a *braisa*: *tavlin shnayim b'gimmel shemos mimin echad o mishlosha minim assurim umitztarfin zeh im zeh* — spices with two or three different names, whether of one type or of three different types, are forbidden and combine with one another. The plain implication is that different types of spices combine to reach a minimum amount.

### Tosafos: Combining Prohi…

Tosafos has a lengthy discussion on this point. He focuses on the phrase *shnayim b'gimmel shemos*, understanding it to refer not merely to different varieties of the same spice, but potentially to spices with different underlying *issurim*. The key line in Tosafos runs: *d'gimmel minei issur k'gon orlah kilayim kerem v'shel asher v'shel ir nidachas kari gimmel shemos* — that there are times the *gemara* uses the word *shem* to refer to the nature of the *issur* itself. From here Tosafos is drawn into a broader discussion: if one has a small amount of spices from *avoda zara*, a small amount from *orlah*, and a small amount from *kilayim*, and their combined volume equals a *k'zayis*, does he receive *malkus*? He has not consumed a minimum of any single category — yet Tosafos entertains the possibility of a generic *lav* that would combine across different prohibited categories, making one *chayav* even on a mixture of distinct *issurim*. That larger question can be set aside for now. The operative point is that different types of spices combine — both for the purpose of reaching the minimum *shiur*, and for the purpose of *bitul*, where the combined flavor of multiple forbidden spices in a dish prevents any one of them from being considered *batel*.

### Chizkiya: Similar Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמִינִים מְתִיקִים שָׁנוּ חוֹיִל וְרְאוּיִים לְמַתִּיק כִּדְיָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **In similar types,** they taught this ruling only **with respect to sweet spices, those that are fit to sweeten a pot.**

Chizkiya qualifies this principle significantly: *biminin metika shanu hoil v'reuyin l'mateik k'deirah* — the *braisa* was taught only with respect to sweet spices, those that share the function of sweetening the pot. Only when all the spices operate in the same direction, so to speak, do they combine. Spices of different characters — one sweet, one savory, one sharp — do not combine with one another, since they do not work together toward a unified effect on the dish.

### Gemara: Why Similar?

The *gemara* then challenges: if only similar spices combine, how does the *mishna* permit combining different types of spices for the purpose of seasoning an egg? The *gemara* resolves this by saying *hacha nami chazu l'mateik* — in the *mishna*'s case as well, all the spices in question are of the sweet-spice category and function together.

This is a curious *gemara* in two respects. First, Tosafos himself asks why the *braisa* says *reuyin l'mateik* — "suited to sweeten" — rather than simply *mamtikin*, "they sweeten." He then raises a deeper question: if the spices, taken in their combined quantity, do produce a discernible taste, why should it matter whether they are all of the sweetening type? And if they do not produce a discernible taste, why should their being sweet help? Tosafos answers that if they work in different directions — some sweetening, some sharpening, some souring — then even if something is technically tasted, it is a *nosein ta'am lifgam*, a taste that diminishes rather than enhances, and is therefore halakhically disregarded.

Another way to understand the underlying issue is through the question of whether a distinct taste must be identifiable. If multiple spices all contribute to sweetness, then even if one cannot identify the particular sweetness of cinnamon versus honey versus sugar as distinct contributions, the combined sweetness is a recognizable and meaningful flavor. But if they are pulling in different directions, no single recognizable flavor emerges from the combination, and the mixture does not register halakhically as carrying any one of their tastes.

What remains striking is that elsewhere in the *gemara*, when it comes to *tziruf* — combining different items to reach a minimum *shiur* — the rule is entirely formal: as long as all items are measured by the same standard, they combine, even if no animal or person would consume that particular hybrid mixture. The *gemara* never asked, for example, whether a combination of camel food and sheep food would actually be eaten together. Here, by contrast, the *gemara* insists that the spices must actually function together as a unified flavor agent. Why the *gemara* demands functional cooperation in this case but not in others is itself an interesting question that the discussion leaves open.

## Dyes & Coloring

### Plant Dyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא סַמָּמִין, קְלִיפֵי אֱגוֹזִים, קְלִיפֵי רִמּוֹנִים, סְטִיס וּפוּאָה — כִּדֵּי לִצְבּוֹעַ בִּגֶד קָטָן כִּפְתַּח הַצְּנִיף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **plant materials used to produce dye, such as shells of nuts, shells of pomegranates, *stis* and *puah*,** is **enough to dye a small piece of cloth used at the opening of a hairnet.**

The *mishna* lists plant materials used to produce dye: shells of nuts (*klipei egozim*), shells of pomegranates (*klipei rimonim*), *stis*, and *puah* — each sufficient to dye a small piece of cloth used at the opening of a hairnet. The standard is *kidei litzbo'a beged katan*, enough to color that minimal piece of fabric.

### Raw vs. Processed Dyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא סַמָּמִים שְׁרוּיִים — כִּדֵּי לִצְבּוֹעַ בָּהֶן דוּגְמָא לְאִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** If one takes out dyes that have already been **steeped** and converted into liquid form — the minimum is only **enough to dye a sample swatch placed on the warp of a loom.**

The *gemara* raises a contradiction from a *braisa*: *hamotzi samamanim shruyim* — if one takes out dyes that have already been steeped and converted into liquid form — the minimum is only *kidei litzbo'a bahen dugma l'irah*, enough to color a sample swatch placed on the warp of a loom, which is obviously smaller than the amount needed for the hairnet opening. Why does the processed dye require a smaller minimum?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן שְׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר אָבוּהָ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ לִשְׁרוֹת סַמָּמִין לִצְבּוֹעַ בָּהֶן דוּגְמָא לְאִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha explained: Because a person does not go to the trouble of steeping and processing raw plant material just to dye so tiny a sample.**

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha explains: *lefi she'ein adam tore'ach lishros samamanim litzbo'a bahen dugma l'irah* — a person does not go to the trouble of steeping and processing raw plant material just to dye so tiny a sample. Once the dye has already been prepared as a liquid, however, even a very small amount is useful and significant — one would take even a drop of ready liquid to color a swatch. The distinction, articulated earlier in the *masechta* as well, is one of processing stage: the significance of a substance depends on what form it is in at the time it is carried.

## Cleansers

### Urine & Natron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא מֵי רַגְלַיִם. וּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: עַד בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם. הַמּוֹצִיא נֶתֶר — כִּדֵּי לִמְחוֹק בִּגֶד קָטָן כִּפְתַּח הַצְּנִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **urine.** **And the Sages taught in a baraita: Until** the age of **forty days.** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **natron** — a mineral cleanser — is **enough to remove a stain from the small cloth used at the opening of a hairnet.**

The *mishna* turns to cleansing agents. *Mei raglayim* — urine — was used in the laundering process. A *braisa* adds the qualification *ad ben arba'im yom* — until forty days. Tosafos raises several possibilities for what this means: whether it refers to the age of the infant whose urine is in question, whether it means urine that has been aged forty days, or whether the effective period begins or ends at forty days. The *mishna* also includes *neter* — natron, a mineral cleanser. A distinction is drawn between *neter Alexandrit*, Alexandrian natron, and *neter Antipantiron*, which appears to be of a different provenance — Rashi suggests the latter derives from a plant source rather than a mineral one. The standard measure for all these cleansers is the amount needed to remove a stain from the small cloth used at the opening of a hairnet.

### Borit & Shevi'is

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא בּוֹרִית. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בּוֹרִית — חוֹל. גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהִי: הַבּוֹרִית וְהַחוֹל! מִכָּאן שֶׁבּוֹרִית אֵינוֹ חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* also mentions **borit,** commonly translated as soap. **Rav Yehuda said: *Borit* is sand.** GEMARA: **And the Gemara raised a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **Borit and sand** are listed as **two separate items! From here** we see that **borit is not sand.**

The *mishna* also mentions *borit*, commonly translated as soap. Rav Yehuda identifies *borit* as sand, but the *gemara* immediately challenges this: a *braisa* explicitly lists *borit* and sand (*haborit vehakhol*) as two separate items, so clearly *borit* is not sand. The *gemara* then proposes that *borit* is *kavrita* — sulfur (*gafrit*), as Rashi identifies it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בּוֹרִית — גַּפְרִית. וּרְמִינְהִי: בּוֹרִית נִכְנְסָה לִשְׁמִטִּין! וּמָה שֶׁאִית לוֹ עִקָּר יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁמִטִּין, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עִקָּר אֵין לוֹ שְׁמִטִּין. וּגַפְרִית — אֵין לוֹ עִקָּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: *Borit* is sulfur.** **And the Gemara raised a contradiction:** A *baraita* states that **borit was added to the list of items that carry *shevi'is*—sabbatical year—sanctity. And the rule is: only something that grows from a root possesses *shevi'is* status; something that does not grow from a root does not.** **And sulfur does not grow from a root!**

But this too is challenged. A *braisa* states that *borit* was added to the list of items that carry *shevi'is* — sabbatical year — sanctity. The rule is *kol sheyesh lo ikkar yesh lo shevi'is, she'ein lo ikkar ein lo shevi'is*: only something that grows from a root possesses *shevi'is* status; a mineral does not. Since sulfur is mined rather than grown, it cannot have *shevi'is* status, and therefore *borit* cannot be sulfur.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בּוֹרִית — אָהָלָה. וּרְמִינְהִי: בּוֹרִית וְאָהָלָה! תְּרֵי גַּוְנֵי אָהָלָה, בּוֹרִית חַד גַּוְנָא, וְאָהָלָה לְשִׁמָּהּ חַד גַּוְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: *Borit* is *ahala*.** **And the Gemara raised a contradiction:** A *baraita* lists **borit and *ahala* as two separate items.** The Gemara answers: **There are two varieties of *ahala*; *borit* refers to one variety, while the other name refers to another.**

The *gemara* then proposes that *borit* is *ahala* — the same substance listed elsewhere, identified by some as aloe, though this identification was discussed and questioned in an earlier *gemara* as well, since different types of plants with that name have quite different properties. When the *gemara* points out that the *braisa* appears to list *borit* and *ahala* as two separate items, the answer is *trei gavni ahala* — there are two varieties of *ahala*, and *borit* refers to one variant while the other name refers to another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא כִּימוּלִיָּה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כִּימוּלִיָּה — שְׁלוֹף דוּץ. הַמּוֹצִיא אַשְׁלַג. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁאַלְתִּי אֶת כׇּל הַיּוֹרְדִים לַיָּם וְאָמְרוּ לִי שׁוּנָנָא הִיא, וְהִיא מְצוּיָה בְּנִקְּבֵי הַפְּנִינִים וְתָלוּשָׁה בִּבְרָזֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* includes **a type of earth** called ***kimuliya*,** which **Rav Yehuda identified as *shlof dutz*—a loose, easily-removed type of earth.** **And with regard to *ashlag:* Shmuel said: I asked all those who travel by sea and they told me *shunana* is the name used for it, and it is found in the aperture of a pearl and extracted with an iron pick.**

Beyond these, the *mishna* includes *kimuliya*, which Rav Yehuda identifies as *shlof dutz* — a loose, easily-removed type of earth. *Ashlag* is identified by Shmuel, who reports he asked all who traveled by sea and they told him it is called *shunana*, found in the aperture of a pearl and extracted with an iron pick — though precisely what this substance is remains somewhat unclear. All of these cleansers are measured by the same standard: the amount needed to cleanse a small garment.

## Sacred Items & Geniza

### Idolatry Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא מִן הַחֵרֶם כׇּל שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְלֹא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם."
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **items relating to *avoda zara*—appurtenances of idol worship—is any amount, as the verse states: "And nothing of that which is banned shall stick to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18).

The *mishna* states that even the smallest amount of items relating to *avoda zara* — appurtenances of idol worship — is significant when carried, as the verse states *velo yidbak beyadcha me'umah min hacherem*: not even the tiniest portion may remain in one's possession; it must be destroyed. Since even the smallest amount carries an obligation to act — namely, to destroy it — even the smallest amount is halakhically significant. This stands in contrast to the general principle, articulated explicitly in an earlier *gemara*, that items subject to *issurei hana'ah*, from which benefit is forbidden and which one desires only to be rid of, were excluded from the category of things that are *matzninin kamohen* — things people wish to preserve. Rabbi Yehuda here takes the position that the obligation to destroy the item is itself sufficient to confer significance on even the smallest amount.

## Fragrances & Oils

### Pepper (Pilpeles)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא פִּלְפְּלֵס כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. גְּמָ׳ לְמַאי חָזֵי? אָמַר: לְרֵיחַ הַפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out ***pilpeles*—pepper—is any amount.** GEMARA: **What is it used for?** The Gemara answered: **To place in the mouth to mask bad breath.**

*Pilpeles kol shehu* — the smallest amount of *pilpeles* — is listed in the *mishna*. The *gemara* asks *lemai chazi*, what is it used for, and answers *lereiach hapeh* — to place in the mouth to mask bad breath. Rashi notes in the *mishna* that this is not the same as our pepper, *eino pilpel shelanu*, though the *gemara* must explain what it is used for. The exchange raises an interesting question: if *kol shehu* is truly the smallest discernible amount, could one tiny crumb of a peppercorn have any meaningful effect on bad breath? The answer remains somewhat unclear, though presumably a ground or crumbled amount, however small, was considered sufficient in the context of a time before modern dental hygiene.

### Tar & Fragrances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא עִתְרָן כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. לְמַאי חָזֵי? אָמַר: לְצִיחוֹסָא. מִינֵי בְּשָׂמִים. הַמּוֹצִיא רֵיחַ רַע כׇּל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out ***itran*—tar or pitch—is any amount.** **What is it used for? Rashi explains: for a headache.** The measure for carrying out **various fragrances** is any amount. The measure that determines liability for carrying out **bad-smelling substances** is **any amount.**

*Itran kol shehu* — the smallest amount of *itran*, a type of tar or pitch — is used, as Rashi explains, for *tzichosa*, a headache. Commentators identify this with a migraine specifically, noting that the strong odor of tar — whether smelled directly or burned — was used as a remedy. *Minei besamim*, various fragrances, were used to cover bodily odors. The *gemara* adds *hamotzi reiach ra kol shehu* — even a bad smell, in the smallest amount. Rashi suggests bad-smelling substances were used for treatment of the sick, and one explanation is that they were used to drive away *shedim* and *mazikim*.

### Oils, Purple, Metals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא שֶׁמֶן טוֹב כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, אַרְגָּמָן כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, וּבְשׁוֹלַת הַוֶּרֶד אַחַת. מִינֵי מַתְּכוֹת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. גְּמָ׳ לְמַאי חָזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁכֵּן רָאוּי לַעֲשׂוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ דׁוֹרְבָּן קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **fine oil** (*shemen tov*), **royal purple dye**, and **one fresh rose petal** is **any amount.** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **various metals** is **any amount.** GEMARA: **What is it used for?** **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Even a tiny piece of metal is enough to fashion a small animal prod or a needle.**

*Shemen tov kol shehu* — the smallest amount of fine oil, identified earlier in the *gemara* as myrrh oil. *Argaman kolshehu* — royal purple dye, even the smallest amount; Rashi acknowledges that the reason is not spelled out clearly, *ta'ama lo nishparash*, though he suggests it too may have been used for its fragrance. *U'besolat havered achat* — one fresh rose petal, or according to the *Aruch*, a rose-shaped embroidered ornament used as decoration on a garment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרֵי עָלַי בַּרְזֶל — אַחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִפְחוֹת מֵאַמָּה עַל אַמָּה. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לְקָלַח אוֹרֵיב. הַמּוֹצִיא נְחוֹשֶׁת — לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִדְּמֵי סֵלַע שֶׁל כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* discusses the minimum amounts relevant for vows to the Temple. **If someone says: I pledge iron, the Sages say: Not less than a square *amah* by *amah*.** **Rav Yosef explained:** this is used for **a spike-studded panel placed on the roof** to deter ravens. **For copper, the minimum implicit in a vow is not less than the value of a silver *ma'ah*.**

*Minei matechot kolshen* — the smallest amount of various metals. The *gemara* asks *lemai chazi* and cites Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *sheken ra'ui la'asot mimenu dorban katan* — even a tiny piece of metal is enough to fashion a small animal prod or a needle.

A *braisa* then discusses the minimum amounts relevant not for *Shabbos* but for vows to the *Beit Hamikdash*. If someone says *harei alai barzel* — I pledge iron — *acherim omrim lo yiphos me'amah al amah*, not less than a *amah* by a *amah* square. Rav Yosef explains this is used for *kaleh oreiv* — a spike-studded panel placed on the roof of the *Beit Hamikdash* to deter ravens attracted by the burning sacrificial meat. The second formulation given in the *gemara* arrives at the same dimension from the opposite direction: the minimum amount pledged is what is needed to construct this device, which is a *amah* by a *amah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִמְצִינוֹרָה קְטַנָּה שֶׁל נְחוֹשֶׁת. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁמְּחַתְּכִין בָּהֶן אֶת הַפִּתִּילוֹת וּמְקַנְּחִין אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer specifies: Not less than a small fork made of copper.** **Abaye explained its use: One uses it to adjust the wicks and clean out the lamps.**

For copper — *nechoshet* — the minimum implicit in a vow is not less than the value of a silver *ma'ah*. Tosafos raises the obvious question: for gold the minimum is a gold *dinar*, for silver a silver *dinar* — consistent with the smallest unit of each metal's standard coin. Why then for copper does the minimum jump to the value of a silver *ma'ah* rather than a copper *peruta*, which is the smallest copper coin? The likely answer is that a *peruta*'s worth of copper is so negligible an amount that one simply cannot do anything meaningful with it, whereas a *ma'ah*'s worth is at least sufficient to work with.

Rabbi Eliezer specifies: *lo yiphos metzinora ktana shel nechoshet* — no less than a small fork made of copper. Abaye explains its use: *shemechatkin bahen et hapitilot umekanchin et hanerot* — one uses it to adjust the wicks and clean out the lamps of the *menorah*. This tiny delicate implement represents the minimal useful object fashionable from copper, and thus defines the minimum for the vow.

## Sacred Items & Geniza…

### Torah Waste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא מַכַּךְ סְפָרִים וּמַכַּךְ מִטְפַּחוֹת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, שֶׁמַּצְנִיעִים אוֹתָן לִגְנִיזָה.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **the waste matter of sacred books and the cloths used to wrap them** is **any amount, because one sets them aside to place them in *geniza*.**

The *mishna* lists *makkak sefarim umakkak mitpachot* — the waste matter of sacred books and the cloths used to wrap them — as items for which even the smallest amount is significant, because *shematzninin osan ligenzan*, one sets them aside to place them in *geniza*. Even crumbling fragments, the residue left after the material has deteriorated, must be treated with the requisite care and buried rather than discarded.

### Worms & Pests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מַכַּךְ — מַה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל אֶת הַסְּפָרִים. תַּחְתָּךְ דִּשִּׁירָא — מַה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל אֶת הַשִּׁיר. אִילָא דְּעִנְבֵי — מַה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל אֶת הָעֲנָבִים. פֵּה דְתִּאֵינִים — מַה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל אֶת הַתְּאֵנִים. הָהָא דִּרִמּוֹנִין — אוֹכֵל אֶת הַרִמּוֹן. כׇּלּוּ סַכָּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda provides a taxonomy:** ***Makkak* is the name for what eats away at books and parchment. *Takhakh deshira* is what eats silk. *Ila de'invei* is what eats grapes. *Peh dete'einei* is what eats figs. *Haha derimonei* eats pomegranates. All of them are dangerous** to one's health if consumed.

Rav Yehuda provides a taxonomy of the creatures or waste associated with these materials. *Makkak* is the name for what eats away at books and parchment. *Takhakh deshira* is what eats silk. *Ila de'invei* is what eats grapes. *Peh dete'einei* is what eats figs. *Haha derimonei* eats pomegranates. Rashi understands these as the creatures themselves. *Kulehu sakanta* — all of them are dangerous to one's health if consumed.

### Peh Worm Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָא תַבְרְנָא בָּעֵי מִן רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: "רְבִּי, הַוְיָא בִּתְרוּמִיתָא דָּא קוֹצִים." אֲמַר לֵיהּ: "קְטָלַת פָּא לְדָדֵן הָאִישׁ!"
>
> **Translation:** A student was sitting before Rabbi Yochanan eating figs. He said: "Rabbi, there are thorns in these figs." Rabbi Yochanan replied: "The *peh* worm of the figs has killed this person."

The *gemara* illustrates the danger with a story. A student was sitting before Rabbi Yochanan eating figs. He said: *Rebbi*, it seems there are thorns in these figs — something sharp and pokey. Rabbi Yochanan replied: *katlei pa ledaden* — the *peh* worm of the figs has killed this person. The student had unwittingly swallowed the worm and taken it to be a thorn. Rabbi Yochanan was alerting him — none too reassuringly — that he was in real danger.

## Box of Fragrances

### One Act, Many Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא קֻפַּת הָרוֹכְלִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מִינִים הַרְבֵּה — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת אַחַת, שֶׁהוֹצִיאָהּ בִּמְלָאכָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who carries out a peddler's box of fragrances,** and **even though there are many different types within the box, he is only liable for a single sin offering, because he carried it out as a single prohibited labor.**

The final *mishna* of the *perek* introduces a case of *hamotzi kuppat harochlin* — one who carries out a peddler's box of fragrances. Such a box contained multiple small compartments, each holding a different fragrance. Even though there are many different types within the box — *af al pi she'yesh bah minim harbei* — one is only *chayav chattas achas*, a single *sin offering*, because the entire box was taken out in one act. Tosafos acknowledges that this seems obvious: even multiple separate acts of carrying within a single lapse of awareness combine for one *sin offering*, and certainly a single act does. Nevertheless, the *mishna* makes the point explicit, establishing that the multiplicity of compartments and varieties does not transform a single act of carrying into multiple acts.

## Seeds & Plants

### Garden Seeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְרוֹנֵי גִינָה — כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּסֵירוּת אוֹמֵר: חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **vegetable garden seeds** is **any amount.** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira says: Five seeds** constitute the minimum.

*Zeronei gina* — vegetable garden seeds — are measured at less than a *grogeris* (pressed fig), since these are not being carried as food. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira says five seeds is the minimum for *chiyuv*. This minimum reflects the practical standard for planting; one does not ordinarily take out a single seed to plant.

### Specific Seeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶרַע קִישּׁוּאִין — שְׁנַיִם. זֶרַע דִּילּוּעִין — שְׁנַיִם. זֶרַע פּוֹל הַמִּצְרִי — שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **cucumber seeds** is **two.** For **gourd seeds,** the measure is **two.** For **Egyptian bean seeds,** the measure is **two.**

For cucumber seeds (*zera kishu'in*), gourd seeds (*zera dilu'in*), and Egyptian bean seeds (*zera pul hamitzri*), the minimum is two. These larger seeds — taken individually in pairs — are of sufficient significance that even two constitutes a meaningful amount for planting purposes.

## Locusts

### Kosher Locust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָגָב חַי טָהוֹר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. מֵת — כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A live kosher **grasshopper,** even the smallest size, **renders one liable.** **If** the grasshopper is **dead,** it is treated as food and requires **the standard food measure** of **a pressed fig-bulk.**

*Chagav chai tahor kol shehu* — a live kosher grasshopper, even the smallest size, renders one *chayav*. If the grasshopper is dead — *meis* — it is treated as food and requires the standard food measure of *k'gerogeris*, the size of a pressed fig.

### Non-Kosher Vine Locust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִפּוֹרֶת כְּרָמִים, בֵּין חַיָּה בֵּין מֵתָה — כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, שֶׁמַּצְנִיעִין אוֹתָהּ לִרְפוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** A non-kosher **vine grasshopper** is **any amount whether alive or dead.** **Because one sets it aside for medicinal use.**

*Tzippores keramim* — a non-kosher vine grasshopper — is *kol shehu* whether alive or dead. Since it is not kosher and thus not food, the ordinary food measure does not apply. Instead, even the smallest amount is significant because *shematznino lah l'refuah* — one sets it aside for medicinal use, as will be elaborated later in the *gemara*.

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Ye…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹצִיא חָגָב חַי טָמֵא כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, שֶׁמַּצְנִיעִין אוֹתוֹ לְקָטָן לִשְׂחוֹק בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who carries out a live non-kosher locust,** the smallest amount renders one **liable. Because one sets it aside for a child to play with.**

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af hamotzi chagav chai tamei kol shehu* — even a live non-kosher locust, the smallest amount renders one *chayav*. His reasoning: *shematznino lekatan litzchok bah* — one sets it aside for a child to play with. The Tanna Kamma, by contrast, does not assign significance to a live non-kosher locust. This sets up the question: if a live *kosher* locust is *kol shehu* because children play with it, why is a live *non-kosher* one any different? And conversely, why does the kosher/non-kosher distinction matter for the living creature at all, since one does not eat a live grasshopper? These questions are taken up in the *gemara*.

## Seeds & Plants…

### Why Minimum Seeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ זֶבֶל וָחוֹל הַדַּק — כְּדֵי לְזַבֵּל קֶלַח שֶׁל כְּרוּב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי לְזַבֵּל כְּרֵישָׁא! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא דִּזְרִיעַ, הָא דְּלָא זְרִיעַ — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ לְהוֹצִיא נִימָא אַחַת לִזְרִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna the measure that determines liability for carrying out garden seeds. **And the Gemara raised a contradiction** from what we learned: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **compost and fine sand** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to fertilize a cabbage stalk.** **And Rav Pappa said: This** applies to a case where the seed **was** already **planted** and growing. **That** applies to a case where the seed **was not** yet **planted, because a person does not** go to the **trouble to carry out a single seed for planting.**

The *gemara* now addresses the apparent tension between the *mishna*'s minimum of five (or two) seeds for planting and the rule from a *braisa* that taking out manure or fine fertilizing material sufficient for even one head of cabbage — or according to the Sages, one leek — constitutes a *chiyuv*. If even one plant's worth of fertilizer is significant, why are multiple seeds required?

Rav Papa resolves this: *ha d'zari'a ha d'la zari'a* — the two cases describe different stages. The fertilizer case concerns something already planted and growing: once a plant is in the ground, one will indeed go to the trouble of fertilizing a single stalk. Seeds, however, have not yet been planted, and *ein adam tore'ach lehotzi nima achat lezri'ah* — a person does not bother taking out one single seed to plant. The effort of the planting act is only undertaken when one has at least a small meaningful quantity. The same principle applied earlier to dyes: the minimum depends on the stage at which the material finds itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא גַּרְעִינִין, אִם לִנְטִיעָה — שְׁתַּיִם, אִם לַאֲכִילָה — כִּמְלֹא פִּי חֲזִיר, וְכַמָּה מְלֹא פִּי חֲזִיר — אַחַת. אִם לְהַסִּיק — כְּדֵי לְבַשֵּׁל בֵּיצָה קַלָּה. אִם לְחֶשְׁבּוֹן — שְׁתַּיִם. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* is then cited: **One who carries out** date **pits,** **if** he did so in order **to plant,** he is liable for carrying out **two** pits. **If** he did so in order for the animals **to eat,** he is liable for carrying out **enough to fill a pig's mouth. And how much is enough to fill a pig's mouth? One** date pit. **If** he did so in order **to burn** the pits, the measure that determines liability is **equivalent** to that which is used **to cook an easily** cooked **egg. If** he carried them out in order to use them **to count,** the measure for liability is **two** pits. ***Aḥerim* say** the measure for liability is **five** pits.

A *braisa* is then cited: *hamotzi gar'inim* — one who takes out pits or seeds. If for planting (*im lentiyah*) — two. If for eating (*im lachilah*) — *kemelo pi chazir*, enough to fill the mouth of a pig. Rashi clarifies this refers specifically to a date pit. One might wonder how a single date pit fills a pig's mouth; likely the phrase does not mean literally filling the entire oral cavity, but rather that one is sufficient for the animal to chew on — a single unit constitutes a meaningful portion. Tosafos adds that the phrase envisions selling or giving to a non-Jew for his pig, since there are specific rabbinic concerns about Jews raising pigs that go beyond ordinary restrictions on non-*kosher* items and which arise from other *gemarot*.

If for burning as fuel (*im lehasik*) — *kidei levashel beitzah kalah*, enough to cook a light egg. If for use as counting markers (*im lecheshbon*) — two according to one opinion, five according to *acherim*. Rashi explains the five: up to four items, a person can track mentally without a mnemonic aid, but once the count reaches five, one begins to forget and needs a marking system. The two who say five is the threshold and two is insufficient apparently differ on when external counting aids become necessary.

## Hair & Fibers

### Tail Hairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא שְׁנֵי נִימִין מִזְּנַב הַסּוּס וּמִזְּנַב הַפָּרָה — חַיָּיב, שֶׁמַּצְנִיעִין אוֹתָן לְנִישְׁבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who carries out two hairs from a horse's tail** or **from a cow's tail** is liable, **because people store them to** use in **traps.**

*Hamotzi shnei nimim miznav hasus umiznav hapara* — one who takes out two hairs from a horse's tail or a cow's tail is *chayav*, because *shematkinah lenishvin* — one puts them aside to use as the trigger element of a snare or trap.

### Pig Hair & Palm Fibers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקְשֶׁה שֶׁל חֲזִיר — אַחַת. צוּרֵי דֶקֶל — שְׁתַּיִם. תּוֹרֵי דֶקֶל — אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The measure for carrying out **stiff hair of a pig** is **one** hair. The measure that determines liability for carrying out **palm fronds** is **two** fronds. The measure for **palm vines** is **one** vine.

A single pig bristle (*michsha shel chazir*) suffices, as it is harder and is used in stitching. For the fibrous material wound around the trunk of a palm tree (*tzurei dikla*), two are needed, as they are woven together in basket-making. For the thinner vine-like growth (*turei dikla*), one is sufficient, though Rashi himself acknowledges he does not know what it is used for — *velo yadati lemah chazi*.

## Locusts…

### Locust for Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי צִיפּוֹרֶת כְּרָמִים? אָמַר רַב: פַּלְיָא בִּיאָרִי. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וּמִשְׁתַּכְחָא בְּדִיקְלָא דְּחַד נְבָארָא וְעָבְדִי לַהּ לְחוּכְמָא. אָכֵיל לֵיהּ לְפַלְגָא דְיַמִּינֵיהּ, וּפַלְגָא דִשְׂמָאלֵיהּ רָמֵי לֵהּ בְּגוּבְתָּא דִנְחָשָׁא וְחָתֵים לֵהּ בְּשִׁיתִּין גּוּשְׁפַּנְקֵי, וְתָלֵי לֵהּ בְּאִיבְרָא דִשְׂמָאלָא. וְסִימָנָיךְ: "לֵב חָכָם לִימִינוֹ וְלֵב כְּסִיל לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ", וְחָכֵים כַּמָּה דְּבָעֵי, וְגָמַר כַּמָּה דְּבָעֵי, וְאָכֵיל לֵיהּ לְאִידַּךְ פַּלְגָא, דְּאִי לָא — מִיעֲקַר תַּלְמוּדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: With regard to carrying out the locust called ***tzipporet keramim*, whether dead or alive,** the measure for liability is **any amount.** The Gemara asks: **What is *tzipporet keramim*? Rav said:** It is the locust called ***palya biari*. Abaye said:** **It can be found in a palm tree that** has only **one vine, and it is used** as a talisman **for wisdom.** One **eats its right half,** and **casts its left half into a copper tube, and seals it with sixty seals, and hangs it on his left arm.** **The mnemonic reminding you** which half to eat and which half to hang on the arm is the verse: **"A wise heart to his right and a foolish heart to his left"** (Ecclesiastes 10:2). **One** then **grows wise as much as he wants, and studies as much as he wants, and** then **eats the other half, as if** he does **not** do so, **his learning** will **be forgotten.**

The *gemara* now identifies *tzippores keramim* as *palya be'eri* according to Rav, and Abaye adds that it is found in a very young palm tree that has not yet developed its full growth of fronds and vines. It is used *lechuchmah* — to enhance learning and retention. The procedure described: eat the right half of the creature. Place the left half in a copper tube, seal it with sixty seals — Rashi notes this is not literally sixty but means it is well sealed — and suspend it on one's left arm. The mnemonic for this arrangement: *lev chacham limino velev ksil lismolo* — the heart of the wise is to his right, the heart of the fool to his left — so one eats the right half. One then learns as much as one wishes. Afterward, one must eat the other half to consolidate the learning; failing to do so will cause everything learned to be forgotten, *me'akar talmudo*.

Whether this locust is kosher or not remains somewhat ambiguous in context. Ordinarily one might invoke the principle that *refuah* use permits a non-*kosher* item under certain conditions, but here the purpose is enhancement of learning rather than treatment of illness, which may not satisfy the conditions for that dispensation. It is possible the *gemara* assumes the creature is in fact kosher.

### Rav Kahana Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָא תַבְרְנָא בָּעֵי מִן רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וְהָוָה קָאֵים קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וַהֲוָה קַמְעַבַּר שׁוֹשִׁיבָא אַפּוּמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁקְלֵיהּ, דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ מֵיכַל קָאָכֵיל לֵיהּ, וְקָעָבַר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תְּשַׁקְּצוּ אֶת נַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם"!
>
> **Translation:** A story is introduced: **Rav Kahana was standing before Rav when a** live **kosher locust was flying near his mouth as he spoke. Rav told him: Move it away from you, so that people will not** mistakenly **say: He is eating it and violating** the prohibition **of "You shall not make yourselves detestable."**

The *gemara* then returns to the dispute between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehuda regarding the live non-kosher locust. A story is introduced: Rav Kahana was standing before Rav when a *shushiva* — a type of non-kosher locust — was flying near his mouth as he spoke. Rav told him: *shaklei* — move it away from you. Why? *De-lo limru meichal ka achal* — lest onlookers see the creature near his mouth, with it perhaps even entering his mouth, and think he is eating it.

### Alive vs. Dead Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי טַעְמָא? וְקָעָבַר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תְּשַׁקְּצוּ אֶת נַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם". אֶלָּא דִילְמָא מָיֵית וְאָכֵיל לֵיהּ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִי מָיֵית — קָטָן מִיסְפָּד סָפֵיד לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Why?** **Because eating a live creature, even a kosher one, violates the principle of** the prohibition **against making oneself disgusting.** But **perhaps** the real concern is that **the creature will die and** the child **will eat it.** **And Rabbi Yehuda,** who permits giving a non-kosher locust to a child for play, does not share that concern. **If** the locust **dies,** the **child will eulogize it** and mourn its death, not eat it.

But why should it matter if people think he is eating a kosher locust? The *gemara* answers: *ve-ka'avar mishum bal teshaktzu et nafshoteichem* — because eating a live creature, even a kosher one, violates the principle of *bal teshaktzu*, the prohibition against making oneself disgusting. Whether this constitutes a formal technical transgression or is more broadly a violation of the spirit of that *pasuk* is a matter of parameters, but the upshot is clear: one should not eat a live creature. This explains why even a live *kosher* locust cannot be eaten without concern.

Returning to the dispute: the Tanna Kamma holds that a live non-kosher locust has no significance because parents would not let children play with it. The concern is *dilma achlei* — the child may eat it, and since it is non-kosher, that is a problem. One might ask: if even a live *kosher* locust risks the child eating it alive and transgressing *bal teshaktzu*, why does the Tanna Kamma permit children to play with the kosher one? The *gemara* resolves this: the concern is not that he will eat it alive — *ella dilma mayis ve-achlei* — but that it will die and then he will eat it. If it is a kosher locust and it dies, the child eats it and there is no *kashrus* violation. If it is non-kosher, the parent cannot allow the child to play with it since the death of the creature would lead the child to eat it, a non-*kosher* food.

### Rashi: Direct Feeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנִסְכָּא. וּמִדְּאִיכָּא שְׁנָצִין, מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ עַד פּוּמֵּיהּ וְשָׁרֵי וְשָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** [From Shabbat 92a] **The Gemara explains:** Here, it is referring **to** long **metal strips.** **And since** the purse **has laces** to close its opening, to be liable for theft it is sufficient **that he carry it out so** that **its mouth** is in the public domain, as he can **untie** the straps.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *ai mayis, katan mispad safid lei* — if it dies, the child will eulogize it, not eat it. Children treat their pets as pets, they do not eat them when they die. Therefore the parent has no concern about non-*kosher* consumption, and a live non-*kosher* locust may be given to a child to play with.

Rashi's comment here has significant implications beyond the immediate case. He writes: *modeh hu de-lo yavinan lei be-yadayim* — even the opinion that a minor eating non-*kosher* food does not obligate the *Beit Din* to intervene (*katan ochel nevelot ein Beit Din metzuvin alav le-hafrisho*) would agree that one may not directly feed the child non-*kosher* food — *de-tanya lo tochlum lo tachilum le-hazhir gedolim al haktanim*. Now, the parent here is not literally feeding the child the locust; he is giving it to the child as a toy. Yet Rashi categorizes the scenario — handing the child a live locust that will likely die and be eaten — as *ma'achil be-yadayim*, tantamount to direct feeding. This raises profound questions about the parameters of the prohibition of *ma'achil be-yadayim*: does placing a child in a situation where non-*kosher* consumption is a foreseeable consequence constitute direct feeding? The *Chasam Sofer* has a celebrated *teshuvah* engaging precisely this question in the context of a parent placing a child in an institution where the child would inevitably be fed non-*kosher* food — whether the act of placement constitutes *ma'achil be-yadayim* given that Rashi extends the concept this far. That *teshuvah* and the broader discussion it generates belongs to a longer conversation, but Rashi's formulation here provides an important foundation for it.

## Matznia Principle

### Setting Aside Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּצְנִיעַ לְזֶרַע וּלְדוּגְמָא וְלִרְפוּאָה וְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא. וְכׇל אָדָם — אֵין חַיָּיב עָלָיו אֶלָּא כְּשִׁיעוּרוֹ. חָזַר וְהִכְנִיסוֹ — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא כְּשִׁיעוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who stores** a seed **for sowing, or as a sample, or for medicinal purposes and carried it out on Shabbat is liable** for carrying out **any amount.** **And any other person is only liable** for carrying it out on Shabbat **if** he carries out **its measure** for liability. If one stored the seed, carried it out, **and then brought it back in, he is only liable if** he brought in **its measure** for liability.

The new *perek* begins with *ha'matznia* — one who formally sets aside an item. *Ha'matznia le'zera u'li'dugma u'li'refuah ve'hotzio be'Shabbos chayav be'chol she'hu* — if one sets aside something for planting, for use as a sample, or for medicinal purposes, and then carries it out on *Shabbos*, one is *chayav* even for the smallest amount. The act of setting aside confers subjective significance on even a negligible quantity.

A normal person who did not perform this act of *matznia* — *eino chayav alav ela ki'shiuro* — is *chayav* only upon the standard minimum measures discussed throughout this *perek*. Furthermore, if the person who had set aside the item subsequently changed his mind and brought it back indoors — *chazar ve'hichniso* — he is again *eino chayav ela ki'shiuro*. The return undermines the significance he originally conferred. The seed had its special status because of his intent and that he was carrying it out to use it; bringing it back signals abandonment of that intent, and the subjective significance dissolves with it.

### Gemara: Why Matznia?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִיתְנֵי "הַמַּצְנִיעַ"? לִיתְנֵי: הַמּוֹצִיא לְזֶרַע וּלְדוּגְמָא וְלִרְפוּאָה — חַיָּיב בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why does** the mishna **teach: One who stores** a seed for a specific purpose and carried it out? **Let** the mishna simply **teach: One who carries out** a seed **for planting, or as a sample, or for medicinal purposes is liable** for carrying out **any amount.**

The *gemara* raises a sharp question: *lama li le'masni ha'matznia? leisni ha'motzi le'zera u'li'dugma u'li'refuah* — why does the *mishna* need to specify that one *set aside* the item? It should have simply said: one who *takes out* something for planting, for sampling, or for healing is *chayav kol shehu*. The very fact that the *gemara* poses this challenge is itself remarkable. The implicit assumption of the question is that the act of *matznia* — physically setting aside ahead of time — is not what generates significance. Rather, the intent at the moment of carrying out is itself sufficient to elevate even the smallest amount into a significant object. If I take out one seed with the intention to plant it, that intent, expressed in the act of carrying, is what matters — regardless of whether I set it aside beforehand.

This is quite a radical claim. It means that any item carried out with a specific use in mind becomes significant at *kol shehu*, without any prior formal act of designation. And it immediately raises a problem: if intent at the moment of carrying is always sufficient, then why do the *mishna* and *braisa* we just discussed specify different minimum amounts for seeds depending on whether they are for planting, for eating, or for burning? If I take out one seed intending to plant it, should not *kol shehu* apply universally?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִצְנִיעוֹ וְשָׁכַח לָמָּה הִצְנִיעוֹ, וְהַשְׁתָּה קָא מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ סְתָמָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלַהּ לְמַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה, עַל דַּעַת רִאשׁוֹנָה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where one stored it** for a specific purpose **and forgot why he stored it, and now he carries it out** for **no** specific **purpose. Lest you say** that **the object's original significance is nullified,** the mishna **teaches us** that **anyone who performs** an action with an object **performs** the action **with the original intention in mind.**

Abaye resolves the first question by reframing the *mishna*'s case. The *mishna* is speaking of a person who *set aside* a single seed — *kegon she-hitznio* — and then *forgot why he had set it aside* — *veshachach lama hitznio* — and took it out simply to bring it somewhere, without any particular use in mind at the moment of carrying. In this case, the intent at the moment of carrying provides no subjective elevation — the person is just moving the seed. One might have thought that since he has no intent now, and has even forgotten his prior intent, the earlier act of setting aside has been fully negated — *bitulei batlei machshavto*. Abaye teaches: *kol ha-oseh al da'at rishonah hu oseh* — what one does is defined by one's original intent. The prior act of setting aside survives the forgetting and continues to define the significance of the object. Therefore, even without a current use-intent, the object remains significant at *kol shehu*.

### Use-Intent Significance

But the *gemara*'s larger implication — that use-intent at the moment of carrying is by itself sufficient to create *kol shehu* significance, even without any prior *matznia* — is still on the table. According to this principle, any time a person takes out even the smallest amount of anything with the specific intent to use it, he has rendered it significant and is *chayav kol shehu*. This eliminates the need for formal prior designation entirely and shifts the operative factor entirely to the moment of carrying.

### Tosafos Challenge

Tosafos presses the obvious difficulty: if intent at the moment of carrying is sufficient to create *kol shehu* significance, then why do we have different minimum amounts for seeds depending on their intended use — two for planting, *melo pi chazir* for animal feed, *beitzah kalah* for firewood? If intent alone determines significance, one seed taken out for planting should be *chayav* regardless of quantity.

Tosafos's resolution is that those measures are not about what is in any individual's mind at the moment of carrying. Rather, they reflect objective use-categories. If the *type* of seed or pit is one that is ordinarily used for planting, the minimum is two. If it is the kind used for animal feed, the minimum is *melo pi chazir*. These standards are defined by what the object is generally used for — its objective category — and not by the subjective intent of the person carrying it. Subjective individual intent generates *kol shehu* significance only in the *matznia* case or when the person carries it out specifically to use it himself. The standard minimum measures, by contrast, represent a formal, category-based threshold that operates independently of any individual's purpose on a given occasion.
