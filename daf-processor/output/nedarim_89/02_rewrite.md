# Nedarim 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Widow/Divorcée Vows

### Introduction

The final *perek* of *Nedarim* arrives at the end of a sustained treatment of vow annulment that began with the father's authority over his daughter, moved through the complex case of the *na'arah me'orasah* who stands under the joint control of father and *arus*, and then devoted considerable attention to the husband's power of *hafara* over his wife. Now, three *dapim* from the end of the tractate, the *mishnah* turns to the case that the Torah addresses in what appears to be the most straightforward fashion: the woman who is entirely her own person, subject to no man's authority.

### Torah Source

The *pasuk* in question is *v'neder almanah u'grushah kol asher asra al nafshah yakum aleha* — the vow of a widow or divorcée, whatever she has forbidden upon herself, shall stand. At first glance, this seems to require no derivation at all: if there is no father and no husband, there is obviously no one to annul her vow. The *mishnah* therefore signals, by opening with this verse, that the *pasuk* is coming to teach something less obvious. The *chiddush* is not the bare case but a more nuanced one — and it is in unpacking that nuance that the *perek* begins.

### Delayed Vow Case

The case the *mishnah* presents is the following. A woman who is independent — a *bogeret*, an *almanah*, a *gerushah*, any woman not under a man's control — declares: *hareini nezirah achar shloshim yom*, I will become a *nezirah* after thirty days. Before those thirty days elapse, she gets married. The vow then takes effect when she is already married to a husband who, in principle, has the power to annul her vows. Can he annul it?

The *mishnah* answers: *af al pi shenisset b'toch shloshim yom eino yachol l'hafer* — even though she married within the thirty days, he cannot annul it. The *chiddush* of the *pasuk* is precisely this: the husband's authority to annul is not determined by when the vow takes effect but by when the vow was made. Since she was an *almanah* or *gerushah* at the moment of utterance, the vow is established as hers alone, and no subsequent marriage can strip it of that character.

### Reverse Case

The reverse case follows naturally. A married woman — *nadrah v'hi b'reshut habaal* — declares while under her husband's authority: *hareini nezirah achar shloshim yom*. He has the power to annul her vow. The *mishnah* rules that he may in fact annul it, *af al pi shenit'almanah o nitgarshah b'toch shloshim yom* — even if she is widowed or divorced within those thirty days, before the vow ever takes effect. He need only perform the annulment during the period of their marriage; the fact that when the thirty days finally expire she is no longer his wife is irrelevant. He cannot, of course, annul her vows after she is widowed or divorced, but he can annul before it takes effect during the marriage, and that annulment holds. Again, the controlling moment is the time of utterance, not the time of effect.

### Same-Day Rule

The *mishnah* then presents a compressed and striking scenario: *nadrah bo bayom v'nitgarshah bo bayom v'hechezirah bo bayom* — she took a vow, was divorced, and was then remarried to the same man, all on the same day. Can he annul it, given that it was made while she was his wife and she is his wife again at the moment he seeks to annul? The *halachah* is *eino yachol l'hafer* — he cannot. The reasoning draws on two related principles. First, a husband cannot be *meifer* for vows that predate him — *ein ba'al meifer b'kodmin* — and the second marriage, even to the same man, is a new marriage. From the perspective of that second marriage, the vow was made prior to it and is therefore beyond his reach. Second, the divorce, even for a moment, granted her independence, and that moment is legally transformative.

### Governing Principle

The *mishnah* closes with the formulation *zeh haklal: kol sheyatzah l'reshut atzmah sha'ah achat eino yachol l'hafer* — the governing rule is that once she enters her own domain, even for a single moment, he loses the power to annul. This serves as the practical articulation of the *pasuk*'s teaching. The independence of the *almanah* and *gerushah* is not merely a biographical state but a legal threshold: once crossed, it cannot be erased by subsequent remarriage. The possibility raised in discussion — that his act of divorcing her might itself function as a kind of *kiyum*, a ratification of the vow through the very act that created her independence — is worth considering, even if the *halachah* does not depend on it.

## Yishmael vs. Akiva

### Baraita Case 1

A *baraita* now presents the underlying *machloket tanna'im* in explicit form. The first case: an *almanah* or *gerushah* — a woman fully independent — declares *hareini nezirah l'cheshe'inasei*, I will become a *nezirah* when I get married. She then does get married.

### Yishmael: Effect

*Rabbi Yishmael omer yafer* — Rabbi Yishmael holds that the husband can annul. His reasoning: the determinative moment is when the vow takes effect, i.e., when it becomes operative. The vow takes effect upon marriage, which means it takes effect precisely when she is under his authority. The fact that it was formulated before the marriage is irrelevant; what matters is the moment at which the prohibition becomes real. Rabbi Yishmael thus focuses on the *sha'at chal* — the time of effect.

### Akiva: Utterance

*Rabbi Akiva omer lo yafer* — Rabbi Akiva holds that the husband cannot annul it, because the controlling moment is *sha'at amira* — the time of utterance. She uttered the vow when she was independent, and that character attaches to the vow permanently. The *seiman* introduced here — *yelali* — encodes the alternating pattern of *yafer / lo yafer* in Case 1 and *lo yafer / yafer* in Case 2, since the positions reverse in the second case.

### Baraita Case 2

The second case is the mirror image: a married woman declares *hareini nezirah l'cheshe'etgaresh*, I will become a *nezirah* when I get divorced. The husband, cooperatively or otherwise, then divorces her, and the vow takes effect at the moment of divorce.

### Positions Reversed

The positions now reverse. *Rabbi Yishmael omer lo yafer*: since the vow takes effect when she is divorced and no longer under his authority, his annulment is meaningless — he had no power over it at the moment it became operative. *Rabbi Akiva omer yafer*: since the vow was made during the marriage, the husband had the power to annul it on the day she made it, and it does not matter that the effect only came later. The reversal is not surprising once the underlying logic is clear: each *tanna* is applying a single consistent principle across both cases, and the two cases simply instantiate the tension between the two principles in opposite directions.

### Torah Exegesis

The Talmud then presents the scriptural foundations for each position. Rabbi Yishmael derives his view from the phrase *veneder almanah u'grushah* — the word *veneder*, he argues, refers not to the act of vowing but to the actual standing reality of the vow itself. The focus of *veneder almanah u'grushah* is on when the vow *exists* as an operative prohibition: is it in existence when she is an *almanah u'grushah*, i.e., independent, or when she is married? That is the question. *Ad she-hi neder besha'at almanut v'gerushin* — we look to the state she is in when the vow is in effect.

Rabbi Akiva derives his view from the phrase *kol asher asra al nafshah* — the word *asra*, she forbade, focuses attention not on the standing reality of the prohibition but on the act of prohibiting. *Ad she-isur neder besha'at almanut v'gerushin* — was she independent at the moment she performed the act of forbidding? That is the question, and it is answered by looking at the *sha'at amira*, the moment of utterance.

### Mishna Attribution

Rav Chisda states the obvious conclusion: *matnitin Rabbi Akiva hi* — our *mishnah*, which consistently governs by the time of utterance, reflects Rabbi Akiva's position.

## Abaye's Resolution

### Thirty-Day Vow

Abaye, however, challenges this. He argues: *afilu teima Rabbi Yishmael* — one might say the *mishnah* is consistent even with Rabbi Yishmael's position. The key distinction lies in how the vow is formulated. In the *mishnah*, the vow is time-indexed: the woman says it will take effect after thirty days. The date of effect is fixed independently of her marital status. It is possible that over the course of those thirty days, nothing changes: she remains in exactly the same legal position she was in when she uttered the vow. The change in her status — the marriage or divorce — happens to occur incidentally within that window, but it is not built into the structure of the vow itself.

### Event-Based Vow

In the *baraita*, by contrast, the vow is status-indexed: *l'cheshe'inasei*, when I get married; *l'cheshe'etgaresh*, when I get divorced. The event of taking effect is definitionally tied to a change in legal status. By the very structure of the vow, the act of utterance and the moment of effect are categorically different moments — the vow was designed to span a transition from one legal domain to another. It is this intrinsic opposition between the two moments — utterance in one status, effect in another status — that Rabbi Yishmael focuses on and that triggers his rule of *sha'at chal*.

### Incidental Change

But in the *mishnah*'s thirty-day case, Abaye argues, even Rabbi Yishmael might agree that the *sha'at amira* governs. The vow was expressed as taking effect after a certain number of days — *shalmu yomei velo nitgarsha, shalmu yomei velo mitnasba* — and it was entirely possible that those thirty days would run out without any change in her status at all. The change that happened to occur was adventitious, not intrinsic to the vow. Since Rabbi Yishmael's principle focuses on cases where the two moments are by design in opposition, when the status change is merely incidental he may concede that the original status, the one in which the vow was made, is the one that counts. This is the *ra'n*'s formulation: *keivan shebitchilato haya raui haneder lachul beoto reshut shehaya beshaat amira* — since at the outset the vow could have taken effect within the same legal domain as the utterance, the original domain defines it even in Rabbi Yishmael's view. The *halachah* in any case follows Rabbi Akiva, both because *halacha ke-Rabbi Akiva mechavero* and because the *mishnah* is a *stam mishna* — though whether it is in fact a *stam mishna* depends on whether one accepts Abaye's resolution.

## Leaving Father

### Ketubot Precedent

The *gemara* now turns to the closing line of the *mishnah* — *zeh haklal kol sheyatzah l'reshut atzmah sha'ah achat eino yachol l'hafer* — and reads it alongside a parallel *zeh haklal* from tractate *Ketubot*, which dealt with the *na'arah me'orasah*. That *klal* in *Ketubot* establishes the following: as long as the daughter has not fully left her father's authority, the father retains his right to participate in the annulment of her vows jointly with the *arus*. The formulation *zeh haklal kol shelo yatzah mereshut ha'av aviah u'vaalah mefirin nedareha* is broad: even if her departure from the father's domain is partial or ambiguous — for example, if the father or his messengers accompany the husband's messengers on the way — she is still considered within the father's authority, and the father may still annul.

The *leatuyei* — the case the *klal* in *Ketubot* comes to include — is the scenario where the father accompanies, or sends his representatives to accompany, the husband's emissaries. Even in that borderline situation, since she has not fully exited her father's control, he remains in the picture. The *klal* reaches broadly in favor of maintaining the father's presence so long as any connection remains.

### Our Perek Klal

The *zeh haklal* of our *perek* — *Elu Naarot* — operates in the opposite direction and addresses the threshold from the other side. Once she has been handed over to the husband's messengers without the father or his representatives present — *masar ha'av lishluchei habaal o shemasru shluchei av lishluchei habaal* — she has left her father's control, and any prior vows can no longer be annulled by the husband under the rule of *ein ba'al meifer b'kodmin*. Interestingly, at this precise moment she has not yet entered *reshut habaal* in the full sense — she has not seen her husband, there has been no *chuppah*, she is simply in transit with his messengers. Yet since the father has stepped back, the opportunity for the husband to annul vows from the betrothal period is now gone.

The *mishnah*'s language of *yatzah l'reshut atzmah* is therefore somewhat forced in this reading, since she has not become independent in any straightforward sense — she is on her way to *reshut habaal*. The better reading seems to be *yotzet mereshut ha'av*: she is leaving the father's domain, not entering her own. But the practical consequence is the same: once the father is no longer present, the window for annulling prior vows closes.

### Betrothal Status

The question this raises is worth pressing: vows she made during *erusin* — the betrothal period — were subject to annulment by the father and the *arus* jointly. Once she transitions to full *nisuin*, those vows from the betrothal period become prior to the husband's status as full husband and cannot be annulled. The moment she is handed over to his messengers without the father functions, in this analysis, as a kind of threshold event equivalent to *chuppah* for purposes of the annulment window. The husband had the opportunity to annul betrothal-period vows while she was still betrothed and still partially in her father's domain; once that threshold is crossed, the father's authority is gone, and from the husband's vantage point, those vows preceded his authority and are beyond his reach.

## Nine Cases

### Three by Three

The *mishnah* now presents what appears to be an elaborate multiplication of cases, though the underlying structure is simple: there are three ways a woman becomes *yatzah l'reshut atzmah* — independent — and the *mishnah* takes each of those three and shows that various other circumstances that might seem to add complexity are in fact irrelevant. The *Yerushalmi* is cited to note that Rabbi Yehuda taught this *mishnah* *l'chahed bo et hatalmidim* — to sharpen the students — and indeed the exercise has the quality of a deliberate puzzle rather than a necessary elaboration of the law.

The three fundamental categories are: a woman who has been married and divorced (*yetomah b'chayei ha'av*, an emancipated minor despite her father's being alive); a true orphan (*yetomah*, whose father has died); and a *bogeret*, a woman who has reached adulthood. Within each of these categories, the *mishnah* varies circumstances that make no legal difference — whether she is a *bogeret* or a *na'arah*, whether the father is alive or dead — to arrive at nine cases. The key point is that once the essential condition is met, the additional factors are legally superfluous.

An important clarification: for a girl to achieve independence through marriage and divorce, the marriage must have been *nisuin*, full marriage, not merely *erusin*. If she became widowed or divorced during *erusin*, she never left her father's authority in the first place. Only genuine *nisuin* — *masi bito* — fully removes her from the father's domain, such that a subsequent divorce or widowhood renders her truly independent.

### Divorced Category

The first group focuses on the *yetomah b'chayei ha'av* — the woman who has been married and divorced while her father is still alive. Within this group, three variations are presented: she is a *bogeret* and also happens to be a *yetomah b'chayei ha'av* (emancipated through divorce); or she was a *na'arah*, became a *bogeret*, and is a *yetomah b'chayei ha'av*; or she is a *na'arah* who has not yet become a *bogeret* but is a *yetomah b'chayei ha'av*. In all three cases, the controlling factor is simply that she has been married and divorced. The other circumstances are irrelevant.

### Orphan Category

The second group focuses on the woman whose father has actually died — the true *yetomah*. Again, three variations: she is a *bogeret* and her father has died; she was a *na'arah*, became a *bogeret*, and her father died; or she is a *na'arah* who has not become a *bogeret* and whose father has died. In each case, what renders her vows binding is simply that there is no father.

### Adult Category

The third group focuses on the *bogeret* — the woman who has attained adulthood. Here the variation is somewhat different, since by definition this group is about the *bogeret* status itself. The cases are: she is a *bogeret* and her father is alive; she was a *na'arah* who became a *bogeret* and her father is alive. One might note that one could have generated a third variation — adding whether she was married and divorced — to produce a full twenty-seven case matrix, but the *mishnah* (and *Rabbi Yehuda*) stops at nine.

### Rabbi Yehuda

After presenting the nine cases, the *mishnah* adds a ruling attributed to Rabbi Yehuda: *af hamasi bito haktana v'nitalmana v'nitgarsha v'chazra etzlo* — even a man who marries off his minor daughter, and she becomes widowed or divorced and returns to him, *adayin hi na'arah* — even if she is still a *na'arah* and not a *bogeret*, since she has had *nisuin* and is now divorced, she is independent. Her vows are binding. This is not adding a new principle but rather illustrating what *yetomah b'chayei ha'av* means — the father is alive, she is still a minor, but the experience of full *nisuin* followed by divorce has emancipated her in the eyes of the law.

### Chachamim Simplify

Rav Yehuda cites Rav: *zo divrei Rabbi Yehuda, aval Chachamim omrim shalosh naarot nidreihen kayamim* — this elaborate enumeration is Rabbi Yehuda's teaching, but the Sages state the matter simply: three categories of women have binding vows — a *bogeret*, a *yetomah* (in the literal sense), and a *yetomah b'chayei ha'av* (divorced after *nisuin*). The *ra'n* quotes the *Yerushalmi* explicitly: *she'af Rabbi Yehuda lo amar ella l'chahed et hatalmidim* — Rabbi Yehuda's nine cases were pedagogical, not substantive. The Sages' three categories contain all nine within them.

## Konam Cases

### Konam 1

The following *mishnah* presents two *konam* formulations that test the edges of the husband's annulment authority. The first: *konam she'eini nehenet l'abba u'l'avicha im ose ani al picha* — a vow that the wife will receive no benefit from her own father or from her father-in-law, conditional on her husband benefiting from her. The two parts of the package work in opposite directions: if the husband eats what she produces, the *tnai* is triggered and she cannot benefit from either father — which constitutes *inui nefesh*; but if he does not eat, the *tnai* is not triggered, yet the vow sits over him as a reason not to eat from what she makes, which affects *devarim shebeino l'veina*.

### Package Analysis

One way or the other, this vow touches on something within the husband's domain: either it will produce *inui nefesh* for his wife, or it will constrain the marital dynamic between them. The first *mishnah* rules that he can annul it.

The second part of the *mishnah*'s first case raises a harder question. The wife says: *she'eini nehenet l'abba u'l'avicha im ose ani al pi abba u'al pi avicha* — I will receive no benefit from you (my husband) if my father or your father benefit from anything I do. Now, if the *tnai* is triggered, the result is clearly *devarim shebeino l'veina* and *inui nefesh* — she cannot benefit from her husband. But one might ask: can she simply go through life ensuring that neither father benefits from her, thereby never triggering the vow? If so, there is no *inui nefesh* and no *devarim shebeino l'veina* in the untriggered state — the husband cannot derive marital benefit from whatever she gives to her father or father-in-law, and they do not benefit from her. Why should he be able to annul?

The *Ra'n* explains that since it is so highly likely that the fathers will at some point benefit from her activity, the vow is treated as effectively anticipating a result that is *devarim shebeino l'veina* and *inui nefesh*, and therefore he may annul it. One might add — building on what we already saw in the previous *mishnah* — that the *mishnah* there already established that a husband can annul a vow before it takes effect. If we know that once the *tnai* is triggered the vow would be squarely within the categories he may annul, then the question of whether it is certain to be triggered may not be the threshold question at all; it is enough that if it takes effect it would be his to annul, and he may act on that potential.

Other *Rishonim* — the *Rosh* among them — prefer a different reading: the package as a whole, even in its untriggered state, is *devarim shebeino l'veina*. The *tnai* itself is structurally tied to the marital relationship — the triggering event is him benefiting from her, and the consequence is her being forbidden to benefit from him — and this interconnection places the entire construct within the scope of *beino l'veina*, not merely as an indirect annoyance but as something that directly configures the relationship between the two of them. On this reading, the *mishnah* is not relying on probabilistic anticipation but on a structural analysis of the vow itself.

A previous *mishnah* established that if she makes a vow about her father or his father's not benefiting from her, that is not *beino l'veina* — he may be indirectly upset, but it does not directly touch the marital relationship. Here, the difference is that her inability to give her father benefit is linked by *tnai* to a vow that is explicitly about the two of them. That linkage transforms the package into something *beino l'veina*, at least according to these *Rishonim*.

### Konam 2

The second *konam* is the *mishnah*'s bolder case: *menu'ah ani min haYehudim im meshameshetcha* — the benefit of marital relations with any Jewish man is forbidden to me if I have marital relations with you. This is clearly *beino l'veina* and *inui nefesh*. The *mishnah* rules he may annul it. The mechanism is worth noting: the vow does not directly say she will not have marital relations with her husband; it says the *hana'at tashmish* of all men — which, since it excludes no one, includes him — is forbidden to her. This formulation is permitted, whereas directly forbidding marital relations with him would be *batel* outright. As a vow of *hana'at tashmish*, the husband is included among those she may not be with if the *tnai* is triggered. So one way or another, the vow affects *tashmish* with her husband.

### Baraita Dispute

A *baraita* records a debate. The case is the first *konam* — the wife will not benefit from her father or father-in-law if the husband benefits from her. *Rabbi Natan omer lo yafer* — Rabbi Natan says the husband cannot annul it. *Chachamim omrim yafer* — the Sages say he can.

The *Ra'n* explains Rabbi Natan's position strikingly: Rabbi Natan holds that *hafara* — the annulment of a vow — can only be performed after the vow has actually taken effect. *Eina ba'al meifer ela im kein chal haneder* — the husband cannot be *meifer* unless and until the *neder* has *chal*. Therefore, in the first *konam* case, where the *tnai* has not yet been triggered and the vow has not yet taken effect, Rabbi Natan says he cannot annul it. The *chachamim* disagree and hold that the husband may be *meifer* even before the *neder* takes effect.

This position of Rabbi Natan is striking in context, because it seems to echo the view of Rabbi Yishmael that annulment timing should track the time of effect. Yet Rabbi Natan was not drawn into that earlier discussion. The two principles are related but not identical: Rabbi Yishmael was speaking about which party has the *power* to annul (father vs. husband) based on the woman's status at the time of effect; Rabbi Natan is speaking about whether annulment can occur at all before the vow takes effect. They are parallel intuitions operating in different analytical registers.

## Worldly Deprivation Story

### The Vow

The *gemara* now introduces a story that illuminates the debate between Rabbi Natan and the *chachamim* and then leads into a broader question about *hatarat nedarim*. A certain man forbade himself all benefit from the world — *asar hana'at d'alma alei* — conditional on his getting married before he had had the opportunity to learn *halachah*. His intent was to motivate himself: he wanted to spend a period of genuine study in *yeshiva* before marrying, and he took this vow as a spur to his own commitment. He was not merely saying he would try; he set a substantive goal of *taninan hilcheta* — mastering the essential corpus of legal learning.

### Temptation

He tried with every possible effort — *rehat b'agpei v'tuvlei*, running with his staff and flask, or a ladder and pulley, depending on the reading; all idioms for maximum exertion — but he was unable to accomplish the learning he had set out to do. He could not find the right teacher, or could not succeed in understanding, or the circumstances simply did not permit it. He remained unmarried, bound by the social and personal pressures of not having fulfilled his vow's condition. Then Rav Acha bar Rav Huna came and, as *piteihu* — he misled him — told him that since he had tried as hard as he could, he had satisfied his vow and could proceed to marry. The man did so.

Subsequently — and this is where the tar or mud comes in — Rav Acha bar Rav Huna apparently caused the man to realize, through some kind of practical intervention, that he might in fact be bound by his vow after all. The exact nature of the intervention is debated among the *Rishonim*: one reading has him throwing dirt on the man's garments, making him unable to get them cleaned without deriving benefit from the world; another reading has him applying tar to make the man unrecognizable. One plausible reading is that having gotten the man to marry, Rav Acha bar Rav Huna then made him feel that his vow had actually taken effect — i.e., that the marriage had triggered it — so that the man would now be in a position to seek a formal *hatarat nedarim*. The man's embarrassment in coming to Rav Chisda may explain why he concealed his identity.

### Appeal

The man came before Rav Chisda and laid out the situation: he had tried as hard as he could, he had taken a wife, and he was now uncertain whether his vow had taken effect. He asked to be released from it.

### Strategy

Rava then reflects on what Rav Acha bar Rav Huna had done and praises it: *man hakim le-mevad ki hai milta i lav Rav Aha bar Rav Huna* — who but Rav Acha bar Rav Huna would have been clever enough to set this up? The genius of the strategy, Rava explains, was that Rav Acha bar Rav Huna understood that the same *machloket* between Rabbi Natan and the *chachamim* that applies to *hafara* — whether the vow must take effect before annulment — *hacha nami paligi b'she'eila*, might also apply to *she'eilat chacham*, the process of a sage releasing someone from a vow. Rabbi Natan might hold that just as a husband cannot be *meifer* until the *neder* takes effect, so too a *chacham* cannot release a vow until it has taken effect. Rather than leave the man in the position of seeking release from a vow that had not yet definitively triggered, Rav Acha bar Rav Huna arranged for the vow to take effect — by having the man get married and thereby trigger the condition — so that the release could proceed cleanly even according to Rabbi Natan's view.

### Hatarat Debate

This raises the explicit question: is the debate between Rabbi Natan and the *chachamim* limited to *hafara* by the husband, or does it extend to *hatarat nedarim* by a *chacham*?

### Textual Proofs

Rav Papa answers: *mahloket b'hafara* — the debate between Rabbi Natan and the *chachamim* is only about *hafara*. Rabbi Natan holds *eina ba'al meifer ela im kein chal haneder*, and derives this from the verse *ve-hafrah ha-levanah* — playing on the similarity between *ve-hafrah* and *hafara*, and reading *ha-levanah* as *chal* the *bamah* (since *kol ha-noder ke-ilu boneh bamah*, one who vows is as if he builds a *bamah*): *hafara* operates when *chal* the vow, i.e., when the vow has taken effect. The *rabanan*, for their part, derive that *hafara* may occur even before the vow takes effect from the verse *mefer mahshevot arumim* — God annuls the schemes of the devious — meaning one can annul even a thought, even something not yet actualized.

### Universal Rule

However, when it comes to *she'eilah* — releasing through a *chacham* — *divrei ha-kol ein chacham matir klum ela im kein chal haneder*: everyone agrees, both Rabbi Natan and the *chachamim*, that a sage cannot release a vow until it has taken effect. The source is the verse *lo yahel devaro* — he may not violate his word — from which we derive that *others* may release his word for him; but the word *yahel* contains within it the word *chal*, teaching that the release operates only once there is *chal devaro*, once the vow has taken effect.

The logic of this is admittedly difficult. The mechanism of *hatarat nedarim* is that the *chacham* retrospectively identifies the vow as having been made *b'ta'ut* — in error — as though the person would not have wanted to take it had he foreseen the circumstances. Retroactive recharacterization of this kind should not, intuitively, require the vow to have taken effect. Yet the formal requirement stands: the process of *hatarat nedarim* has an independent formal structure, and that structure, according to all opinions, requires *chal* as a prerequisite. This is why Rav Acha bar Rav Huna's strategy was not merely playing it safe with respect to Rabbi Natan's position on *hafara* but was in fact necessary from the perspective of *hatarat nedarim* according to everyone.
