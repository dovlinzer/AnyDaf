# Yevamot 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Marriage & Wife's Value

### Context & Review

Today's daf is 63, and we pick up at the very bottom of 62b. Before doing so, a brief recap is in order. Yesterday's discussion covered the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* and specifically Rabbi Yehoshua's position about not stopping at two children — along with the broader question of how much of that represents a formal obligation versus *minhag derech eretz*. The *gemara* is now in the middle of a discussion about the importance of marriage, presented from a man's perspective. We have already seen two schools attributed to Bavel and Eretz Yisrael respectively: the Babylonian position frames marriage as an intrinsic good — a source of *simcha*, wholeness, and blessing — while what is attributed to Eretz Yisrael is more instrumental, focusing on the prevention of sin and the husband's ability to learn Torah. It is worth noting the irony that most other sources suggest the actual schools were reversed, with the differing attitudes relating to leaving home to study Torah in Bavel versus Eretz Yisrael, and to questions of polygamy and monogamy.

### Tanu Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹהֵב אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ כְּגוּפוֹ, וְהַמְכַבְּדָהּ יוֹתֵר מִגּוּפוֹ, וְהַמַּדְרִיךְ בָּנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו בְּדֶרֶךְ יְשָׁרָה, וְהַמַּשִּׂיאָן סָמוּךְ לְפִירְקָן — עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְיָדַעְתָּ כִּי שָׁלוֹם אׇהֳלֶךָ״. הָאוֹהֵב אֶת שְׁכֵינָיו, וְהַמְקָרֵב אֶת קְרוֹבָיו, וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת בַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One **who loves his wife as** he loves **himself, and who honors her more than himself, and who instructs his sons and daughters in an upright path, and who marries them off near the time** when **they** reach maturity, **about him the verse states: And you shall know that your tent is in peace.** As a result of his actions, there will be peace in his home, as it will be devoid of quarrel and sin. One **who loves his neighbors, and who draws his relatives close, and who marries the daughter of his sister,**

The *gemara* opens with a *tanu rabbanan*: one who loves his wife as himself — *ha'ohev et ishto k'gufo* — and respects her even more than himself — *u'mechabda yoter m'gufo* — where "respect" here is understood similarly to how *kibbud em* is understood: attending to the person's physical needs such as clothing, feeding, and care, though it appears to carry a broader meaning as well. The *baraita* continues: one who instructs his sons and daughters to walk on the right path — *ve'hamadrich banav u'venotav b'derech yeshara* — and who marries them off close to their age of maturity — *ve'hamassian samuch l'pirkan* — regarding him the verse says: *v'yadata ki shalom ohalecha u'pakadta navecha v'lo techeta*, "you will know that your tent is at peace, and you will see that your dwelling is without sin." What is added here beyond the earlier discussion is the dimension of children: educating them on the right moral path and marrying them off while young, so that once they are sexually mature they are already married and protected from sin. This includes even the notion of marrying off a young son while he is quite young.

### Neighbors & Charity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמַּלְוֶה סֶלַע לְעָנִי בִּשְׁעַת דׇּחְקוֹ — עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״אָז תִּקְרָא וַה׳ יַעֲנֶה תְּשַׁוַּע וְיֹאמַר הִנֵּנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **and who lends a *sela* to a pauper at his time of need, about him the verse states: "Then shall you call, and the Lord will answer; you shall cry, and He will say: Here I am"** (Isaiah 58:9).

The *gemara* then continues with further obligations: one who loves his neighbors — *ha'ohev et shechenav* — one who draws his relatives close — *ve'hamakarev et kerovav*, a nicely phrased formulation acknowledging that relatives are not always actually close — one who marries his niece (*ve'hanosei et bat achoto*, though there is some discussion as to whether this refers specifically to his sister's daughter or also his brother's daughter), and one who lends money to a poor person in a moment of particular distress — *ve'hamalveh sela l'ani b'shat dochko* — regarding all of these the verse says: *az tikra v'Hashem ya'aneh tishva v'yomar hineni*, "then you will call out and God will answer, you will cry out and He will say: here I am." As Rashi points out, this verse follows the passage *ki tireh arom v'chisito u'mibesarcha lo titalam* — seeing a naked person and dressing him — where covering the naked corresponds to lending to the poor in their distress, and "not hiding from your own flesh" corresponds to taking care of relatives, marrying one's niece, and so forth.

### Marriage Essential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אִשָּׁה — אֵינוֹ אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בְּרָאָם וַיִּקְרָא אֶת שְׁמָם אָדָם״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קַרְקַע — אֵינוֹ אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשָּׁמַיִם שָׁמַיִם לַה׳ וְהָאָרֶץ נָתַן לִבְנֵי אָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Any man who does not have a wife is not a man, as it is stated: "Male and female He created them…and called their name Adam"** (Genesis 5:2). **And Rabbi Elazar said: Any man who does not have** his own **land is not a man, as it is stated: "The heavens are the heavens of the Lord; but the earth He has given to the children of men"** (Psalms 115:16).

Rabbi Elazar offers a striking teaching: *kol adam she'ein lo isha eino adam* — any man without a wife is not a man — as it is written, *zachar u'nekeva bera'am vayikra shemam Adam*: only when they were both male and female together was the name "Adam" applied to them. This clearly reflects the message of Bereishit — the sense of wholeness and oneness that is created through marriage.

Rabbi Elazar then adds: *kol adam she'ein lo karka eino adam* — any person without land is likewise not a real man. The introduction of land into the discussion is surprising, and one is prompted to reconsider what he means with the earlier statement about a wife. The scholar Satlow, in his study of marriage in Jewish antiquity, argues that Rabbi Elazar's point is primarily about a person's social standing — that having a home and being married is what it takes to be recognized as a person within society. Seen this way, the two statements complement each other: they are not existential claims about human completeness in a philosophical sense, but rather a description of what constitutes full membership in the community. This reading is supported by the sociological reality that in many Jewish communities, even now, unmarried individuals are sometimes not viewed as fully integrated members — a reality that may not be ideal but is nonetheless real.

### Helper Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אֶעֱשֶׂה לּוֹ עֵזֶר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ״: זָכָה — עוֹזַרְתּוֹ, לֹא זָכָה — כְּנֶגְדּוֹ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר רָמֵי, כְּתִיב ״כְּנַגְּדוֹ״, וְקָרֵינַן ״כְּנֶגְדּוֹ״. זָכָה — כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, לֹא זָכָה — מְנַגַּדְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of **that** which **is written: "I will make him a helpmate for him [*kenegdo*]"** (Genesis 2:18)? If one is **worthy** his wife **helps him;** if he is **not worthy** she is **against him. And some say** a slightly different version: **Rabbi Elazar raised a contradiction: It is written** in the Torah with a spelling that allows it to be read: **Striking him [*kenagdo*], and we read** it as though it said: **For him [*kenegdo*].** If he is **worthy** she is **for him** as his helpmate; if he is **not worthy** she **strikes him.**

The *gemara* continues with Rabbi Elazar: *mai d'chtiv e'eseh lo ezer k'negdo* — what is the meaning of the verse "I will make him a helpmate opposite him"? *Zacha* — if he merits — *ozarto*, she will be his help. *Lo zacha* — if he does not merit — *k'negdo*, she will be against him. A second tradition presents the same idea through a textual discrepancy: the word is written *k'negdo* but read *k'nigdo*. The homiletical reading derives: if he merits — *k'negdo*, meet for him, appropriate for him; if he does not merit — *minagdato*, from the Aramaic for "lashing" — she lashes him.

### Wife's Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְאֵלִיָּהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ, כְּתִיב: ״אֶעֱשֶׂה לוֹ עֵזֶר״, בַּמָּה אִשָּׁה עוֹזַרְתּוֹ לָאָדָם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָדָם מֵבִיא חִיטִּין — חִיטִּין כּוֹסֵס? פִּשְׁתָּן — פִּשְׁתָּן לוֹבֵשׁ? לֹא נִמְצֵאת מְאִירָה עֵינָיו וּמַעֲמִידָתוֹ עַל רַגְלָיו?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yosei encountered Elijah** the prophet and **said to him: It is written: I will make him a helpmate. In what** manner **does a woman help a man?** Elijah **said to him:** When **a man brings wheat** from the field, does he **chew** raw **wheat?** When he brings home **flax,** does he **wear** unprocessed **flax?** His wife turns the raw products into bread and clothing. Is his wife **not found** to be the one who **lights up his eyes and stands him on his feet?**

Rabbi Yossi found Elijah and asked him: the verse says "I will make him a helpmate" — in what way does a woman help a man? Elijah responded: when a man brings wheat home from the field, does he chew wheat directly? When he brings home flax, does he wear raw flax? Rather, the wife grinds the wheat into flour and bakes bread from it; she beats the flax and fashions it into linen and clothing. Thus: *nimtzeit me'ira einav u'ma'amidato al raglav* — she lights up his eyes and makes him stand on his legs. This is, admittedly, a very concrete, material understanding of the wife's contribution — placing her in what we might call the kitchen or the workroom — and it must be acknowledged that not all of these framings reflect modern sensibilities.

### Bone & Flesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת הַפַּעַם עֶצֶם מֵעֲצָמַי וּבָשָׂר מִבְּשָׂרִי״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבָּא אָדָם עַל כׇּל בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה, וְלֹא נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ עַד שֶׁבָּא עַל חַוָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of **that** which **is written: "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh"** (Genesis 2:23)? This **teaches that Adam had intercourse with each animal and beast** in his search for his mate, **and his mind was not at ease,** in accordance with the verse: "And for Adam, there was not found a helpmate for him" (Genesis 2:20), **until he had intercourse with Eve.**

Rabbi Elazar then addresses the verse *zot hapa'am etzem me'atzami u'vasar mibsari* — "this time, bone of my bone, flesh of my flesh." He reads it as teaching that Adam had relations with every animal and creature — *melamed sheba adam al kol behema v'chaya* — but *lo nitkarera da'ato*, his mind was not put at ease, until he came to Chava. Only then did he recognize, "this is my own species." The reading is motivated by the interpretive question: what exactly prompted Adam's declaration? Merely seeing her did not seem sufficient to explain the force of the words.

### Abraham's Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנִבְרְכוּ בְךָ כֹּל מִשְׁפְּחֹת הָאֲדָמָה״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאַבְרָהָם: שְׁתֵּי הַבְרָכוֹת טוֹבוֹת יֵשׁ לִי לְהַבְרִיךְ בְּךָ: רוּת הַמּוֹאֲבִיָּה, וְנַעֲמָה הָעַמּוֹנִית. ״כֹּל מִשְׁפְּחוֹת הָאֲדָמָה״ — אֲפִילּוּ מִשְׁפָּחוֹת הַדָּרוֹת בָּאֲדָמָה אֵין מִתְבָּרְכוֹת אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל. ״כׇּל גּוֹיֵי הָאָרֶץ״ — אֲפִילּוּ סְפִינוֹת הַבָּאוֹת מִגַּלְיָא לְאַסְפַּמְיָא אֵינָן מִתְבָּרְכוֹת אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of **that** which **is written: "And in you shall all the families of the earth be blessed [*nivrekhu*]"** (Genesis 12:3)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Abraham: I have two good shoots to graft** [***lehavrikh***] **onto you: Ruth the Moabite,** the ancestress of the house of David, **and Naamah the Ammonite,** whose marriage with Solomon led to the ensuing dynasty of the kings of Judea. **"All the families of the earth"** means: **Even families that live in the earth,** i.e., who have land of their own, **are blessed only due to the Jewish people.** Similarly, when the verse states: **"All the nations of the earth** shall be blessed in him" (Genesis 18:18), it indicates that **even ships that come from Galia to Hispania are blessed only due to the Jewish people.**

Rabbi Elazar continues: what is the meaning of *v'nivrechu becha kol mishpachot ha'adamah* — "and through you all the families of the earth will be blessed"? God said to Avraham: I have two graftings to graft onto you — *shtei brachot tovot yesh li l'havrich becha*. The word *l'havrich* carries a double meaning: not only "blessing" (*berachah*) but also *havrachah*, the grafting of a branch from one tree onto another. The two graftings are Ruth the Moabite and Na'amah the Ammonite. From Ruth will come David; Na'amah, too, produces children who enter the royal lineage. This is a particularly significant statement within the context of Yevamot, where questions of lineage and the status of converts have been and will continue to be central themes. Here the *gemara* is emphasizing, rather pointedly, the wonderful blessing that comes specifically from outside one's own gene pool.

### Israel's Centrality

The *gemara* continues: *kol mishpachot ha'adamah* — all the families of the earth — *afilu mishpachot hadarot ba'adamah*, even the families dwelling out in the countryside, far from cities — *ein berachah ella bishvil Yisrael*, all their blessing derives from Israel. *Kol goyei ha'aretz* — all the nations of the earth — even the ships sailing from Gaul to Spain, entirely remote from the land of Israel and not even on solid ground but on the sea — *einan mitbarechot ella bishvil Yisrael*, all their blessing is by virtue of Israel. Interestingly, there is actually a rabbi from Gaul mentioned in the Yerushalmi, suggesting that the Jewish world extended even to that distant region.

## Labor Professions

### Future Agriculture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲתִידִים כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי אוּמָּנִיּוֹת שֶׁיַּעַמְדוּ עַל הַקַּרְקַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיָרְדוּ מֵאֳנִיּוֹתֵיהֶם כֹּל תֹּפְשֵׂי מָשׁוֹט מַלָּחִים כֹּל חֹבְלֵי הַיָּם עַל הָאָרֶץ יַעֲמֹדוּ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין לְךָ אוּמָּנוּת פְּחוּתָה מִן הַקַּרְקַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיָרְדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: All craftsmen are destined to stand upon and work the land, as it is stated: "And all that handle the oar, the mariners, and all the pilots of the sea, shall come down from their ships, they shall stand upon the land"** (Ezekiel 27:29). **And Rabbi Elazar said: There is no occupation lowlier than** working **the land, as it is stated: "And they shall come down,"** implying that one who works the land is of lower stature than even a sailor.

Rabbi Elazar continues with a series of statements. He teaches: *atidin kol ba'alei umanut sheya'amdu al hakarka* — in the future, all craftspeople will stand upon the land, meaning they will ultimately turn to farming. The verse cited is *veyardu me'oniyotayhem kol tofsei mashot malachim kol chovlei hayam al ha'aretz ya'amdu* — all those who work the sails, all the mariners, will descend to the land.

### Agriculture Lowly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַזְיַהּ לְהַהִיא אַרְעָא דְּשָׁדֵי בַּיהּ כְּרָבָא לְפוּתְיָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תִּשְׁדְּיֵיהּ לְאוּרְכָּיךְ — הַפּוֹכֵי בְּעִיסְקָא טָב מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara similarly relates: **Rabbi Elazar saw land that was plowed** across **its width. He said to it:** Even **if they plow you** once more **lengthwise,** for further improvement, **conducting business is better than** farming with **you,** as the potential profits gained by selling merchandise are far greater than those from working the land.

Rabbi Elazar then immediately declares: *ein lecha umanut pechutah min hakarka* — there is no craft more lowly than working the land. This is striking: if in the future all craftspeople will leave their trades and turn to the land, one might expect that to be presented as a positive development. Yet here Rabbi Elazar seems to be saying the opposite. One possible resolution is that the vision of the future involves the non-Jews becoming the farmers, freeing others — perhaps, to learn Torah — but the tension remains worth noting. The verse cited is *veyardu* — they will descend — understood as a downward movement, not merely from their boats but from their station.

### Against Farming

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב עָל לְבֵינֵי שׁוּיבְלֵי, חֲזַנְהוּ דְּקָא נָיְיפָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי נָיְיפַתְּ — אִיתְנוֹפֵי. הַפּוֹכֵי בְּעִיסְקָא טָב מִינָּךְ. אָמַר רָבָא: מְאָה זוּזֵי בְּעִיסְקָא — כֹּל יוֹמָא בִּשְׂרָא וְחַמְרָא, מְאָה זוּזֵי בְּאַרְעָא — מִילְחָא וַחֲפוּרָה. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא מַגְנְיָא לֵיהּ אַאַרְעָא, וּמַרְמְיָא לֵיהּ תִּיגְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **Rav entered between the sheaves** in a field and **saw them waving** in the wind. **He said to them: If you** want to **wave** go ahead and **wave,** but **conducting business is better than** farming with **you. Rava** similarly **said:** One who has **a hundred dinars** that are invested **in** a **business** is able to eat **meat and wine every day,** whereas he who has **a hundred dinars** worth **of land** eats only **salt and vegetables. And what is more,** working the land **causes him to lie on the ground** at night in order to guard it, **and** it **draws quarrels upon him** with other people.

The *gemara* then offers two anecdotes illustrating this attitude. Rabbi Elazar saw a particular plot of land in which a furrow — or, in another reading, a head of cabbage — was being planted widthwise. He said to it: even if you were planted lengthwise, it would be better to be involved in business than to worry about working you. Rav walked between stalks of grain and saw them swaying in the wind. He said to them: wave all you like — it is still far better to be involved in business than to farm.

### Business Economics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: זְרַע וְלָא תִּזְבֹּין. אַף עַל גַּב דְּכִי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ, הָנֵי מִבָּרְכָן. זַבֵּין וְלָא תִּיזּוֹל. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּיסְתַּרְקֵי, אֲבָל גְּלִימָא — לָא מִיתְרַמְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Sow** your own produce **and do not buy** it. **Even though they are equal to each other** in value, **these** that you sow **will be blessed.** Conversely, **buy** your clothes **rather than weave [*teizul*]** them yourself. The Gemara comments: **This applies** only to **mats [*bistarkei*], but** with regard to the **cloak** one wears, perhaps **he will not find it** precisely to his liking, and therefore he should make his own cloak, which fits his measurements.

Rava offers an economic analysis. If one invests a hundred *zuz* in business — *me'ah zuzei be'iska* — every day one eats meat and drinks wine. If one invests the same hundred *zuz* in farming — *me'ah zuzei be'ara* — one eats vegetables with salt. Moreover, one has to sleep on the land to guard the crops — *maganya leh a'ara* — and gets into disputes with neighbors — *uramya leh tigrei*. The conclusion is that it is better to be a businessman.

### Plant vs. Purchase

Rav Papa, however, offers a counter-position: *zara velo tazbin* — plant rather than purchase. Even if the cost works out the same, the work of one's own hands is blessed, so one should produce one's own food rather than go buy it in the market. Then he adds: *zabin velo tazor* — purchase rather than weave. The precise interpretation of this second clause is disputed. Rashi reads it as: sell your property before you become impoverished, though with the caveat that this applies to certain objects like mats; for a cloak, one would not sell it — *lo mistramlei* — out of concern that one would not be able to find another that fits as well. Tosafot, working from a variant text, reads the contrast differently: while one should grow one's own food, one should purchase one's own clothes rather than weave them — except that for a garment like a cloak, custom tailoring may be preferable. This raises the natural question of how Rav Papa's endorsement of personally working the land sits alongside the earlier denigration of farming — a tension the *gemara* leaves in place.

## Suffering & Justice

### Punishment Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר אֲבִינָא: אֵין פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִכְרַתִּי גוֹיִם נָשַׁמּוּ פִּנּוֹתָם הֶחֱרַבְתִּי חוּצוֹתָם״, וּכְתִיב: ״אָמַרְתִּי אַךְ תִּירְאִי אוֹתִי תִּקְחִי מוּסָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar bar Avina said: Calamity befalls the world only due to** the sins of **the Jewish people, as it is stated: "I have cut off nations, their corners are desolate; I have made their streets waste"** (Zephaniah 3:6), **and it is written: "I said: Surely you will fear Me, you will receive correction"** (Zephaniah 3:7). This indicates that other nations were punished so that the Jewish people would mend their ways.

Rabbi Elazar bar Avina teaches: *ein puraniyot ba'ot la'olam ella bishvil Yisrael* — bad things happen to the world for the sake of Israel. The verse cited is *hikratti goyim nashammu pinnotam, hecherevti chutzotam* — I have cut off nations, their corners are desolate, I have laid waste their courtyards — followed by: *amarti ach tir'i oti tikchi musar*, "I said: perhaps you will fear Me and take heed." The implication is that all the devastation visited upon the entire world is ultimately so that Israel should take heed and return.

### Rav's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הֲוָה מִיפְּטַר מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַחֲמָנָא לַיצְּלָךְ מִמִּידֵּי דְּקָשֵׁה מִמּוֹתָא. וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּקָשֵׁה מִמּוֹתָא? נְפַק, דָּק וְאַשְׁכַּח: ״וּמוֹצֶא אֲנִי מַר מִמָּוֶת אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וְגוֹ׳״. רַב הֲוָה קָא מְצַעֲרָא לֵיהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ, כִּי אָמַר לַהּ ״עֲבִידִי לִי טְלוֹפְחֵי״, עָבְדָא לֵיהּ חִימְצֵי. ״חִימְצֵי״, עָבְדָא לֵיהּ טְלוֹפְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites more statements with regard to wives. When **Rav was taking leave of** his uncle and teacher, **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** upon his return from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: May the Merciful One save you from something that is worse than death.** Rav was perplexed: **Is there anything that is worse than death? He went, examined** the sources, **and found** the following verse: **"And I find more bitter than death the woman, etc."** (Ecclesiastes 7:26). Rabbi Ḥiyya was hinting at this verse, and indeed, **Rav's wife would** constantly **aggravate him. When he would say to her: Prepare me lentils, she would prepare him peas;** if he asked her for **peas, she would prepare him lentils.**

The *gemara* introduces a transition. Rav was departing from Rabbi Chiyya, and Rabbi Chiyya said to him: *rachamana litzlan mimmidi de-kashi mimota* — may God save us from something that is worse than death. Rav wondered to himself: is there anything worse than death? He went and searched and found the verse: *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah* — I find more bitter than death the woman. We now enter a section of the *gemara* that is, it must be acknowledged, written entirely by men discussing wives. What came before was also from the male perspective — the value of a wife for the husband, whether intrinsically or instrumentally, the husband's obligations — but now the *gemara* turns to a frank and mixed assessment of marriage from the man's vantage point. The question of when we get to hear how wives feel about their husbands is a pointed one, and we will return to it.

The *gemara* reports: *Rav hava ka metzatra leih devitehu* — Rav's wife was causing him a great deal of anguish. When he would ask her to make *tluphi* — lentils — she would make *chimtzi* — a type of pickled or brined dish, not radically different but not what he asked for. When he asked for *chimtzi*, she would make *tluphi*. She would invert his requests.

### Food Requests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי גְדַל חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ, אֲפֵיךְ לַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיעַלַּיָא לָךְ אִמָּךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הוּא דְּקָא אָפֵיכְנָא לַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הַיְינוּ דְּקָא אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: דְּנָפֵיק מִינָּךְ, טַעְמָא מַלְּפָךְ. אַתְּ לָא תַּעֲבֵיד הָכִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִמְּדוּ לְשׁוֹנָם דַּבֶּר שֶׁקֶר הַעֲוֵה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ḥiyya, his son, grew up, he would reverse** the requests Rav asked him to convey **to her,** so that Rav would get what he wanted. Rav **said to** his son Ḥiyya: **Your mother has improved** now that **you** convey my requests. **He said to** Rav: **It is I who reverse** your request **to her.** Rav **said to him: This is** an example of the well-known adage **that people say:** He **who comes from you shall teach you wisdom;** I should have thought of that idea myself. **You,** however, **should not do so,** i.e., reverse my request, **as it is stated: "They have taught their tongue to speak lies, they** weary themselves to **commit iniquity, etc."** (Jeremiah 9:4). If you attribute such a request to me, you will have uttered a falsehood.

When his son Chiyya grew up, he began relaying his father's requests to his mother — but inverting them, so that Rav ultimately received what he actually wanted. When Rav noticed that his meals were now matching his requests, he said to his son: your mother has improved! The son replied: no — *ana hu de-ka-afichna lah* — I was the one inverting her responses so that you got what you wanted. Rav then said to him: that is exactly what people mean when they say *de-nafik minach ta'ama malfach* — the one who comes from you will teach you. You learn wisdom from your children. Nevertheless, Rav continued, *at lo ta'avid hachi* — do not do this — citing the verse: *limdu leshonam daber sheker* — "they have taught their tongues to speak falsehood." Do not teach deception, even for a worthy end.

### Wife's Perspective

At this point it is worth pausing to read a contemporary *midrash* by Rivkah Lubitch, who wrote the wife's perspective on this story. Her account asks: why did Rav's wife do this? Not, she writes, because Rav would sometimes tell her he was coming home early from the *beit midrash* and would arrive only after dark — leaving her sitting in distress, waiting, until anger and resentment finally overtook her. Not because he would sometimes say he was coming home late and would arrive early, leaving her scrambling to prepare the meal. Not because he would sometimes signal that they would dine together privately — only for him to arrive from the *beit midrash* with many students, all of whom would sit, eat, and enjoy themselves in Torah discussion while she labored to serve them all. Not because he would sometimes say he was bringing guests and then come home alone.

Rather, Lubitch writes, it was because when Rav knew she had cooked *chimtzi*, he would ask for *tluphi*; and when she had cooked *tluphi*, he would ask for *chimtzi*. And she — she was silent. She said nothing. She simply served him what she had already prepared in the pot. And he never knew why she was doing this. And she, this poor woman, did not know why her husband was treating her this way — he who was her entire world and everything she worked for. And so she was bitter all her days, and nearly longed for death. And it is for this that the verse says: *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah*.

This rendering gives a profound sense of what the same story looks like from a very different vantage point, and it is with this in mind that we turn to the rest of the *gemara*.

## Marriage Complexity

### Rabbi Chiyya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא הֲוָה קָא מְצַעֲרָא לֵיהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ. כִּי הֲוָה מַשְׁכַּח מִידֵּי, צָיַיר לֵיהּ בְּסוּדָרֵיהּ וּמַיְיתֵי נִיהֲלַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: וְהָא קָא מְצַעֲרָא לֵיהּ לְמָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּיֵּינוּ שֶׁמְּגַדְּלוֹת בָּנֵינוּ, וּמַצִּילוֹת אוֹתָנוּ
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar story. **Rabbi Ḥiyya's wife would** constantly **aggravate him.** Nevertheless, **when he would find something** she would appreciate, **he would wrap it in his shawl and bring** it **to her. Rav said to him: Doesn't she** constantly **aggravate you?** Why do you bring her things? Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: It is enough for us** that our wives **raise our children and save us**

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Chiyya. His wife, too, caused him much anguish — *Rabbi Chiya havah ka metzaara lei devitehu*. Yet when he found a nice thing in the marketplace, he would wrap it in his handkerchief and bring it to her as a small gift. Rav asked him: but she causes you such suffering! Rabbi Chiyya responded: *dayeinu* — it is enough for us — *she-megadlot baneinu u-matziylot otanu min ha-chet*: that they raise our children and save us from sexual sin. Even through all the difficulty, one must remain appreciative.

### First Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַחֵטְא. מַקְרֵי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ ״וּמוֹצֶא אֲנִי מַר מִמָּוֶת אֶת הָאִשָּׁה״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן מַאן? כְּגוֹן אִמָּךְ. וְהָא מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצֵא קוֹרַת רוּחַ אֶלָּא מֵאִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְהִי מְקוֹרְךָ בָרוּךְ וּשְׂמַח מֵאֵשֶׁת נְעוּרֶיךָ״, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן מַאן? כְּגוֹן אִמָּךְ! מִתְקָיף תְּקִיפָא, וְעַבּוֹרֵי מְיעַבְּרָא בְּמִלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **from sin.** We should therefore show our gratitude to them. The Gemara cites a related incident: **Rav Yehuda** was **teaching** Torah **to Rav Yitzḥak, his son,** and they encountered the verse: **"And I find more bitter than death the woman"** (Ecclesiastes 7:26). His son **said to him: For example, whom?** His father replied: **For example, your mother.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't Rav Yehuda teach Rav Yitzḥak, his son,** the following *baraita*: **A man finds peace of mind only with his first wife, as it is stated: "Let your fountain be blessed, and have joy from the wife of your youth"** (Proverbs 5:18), **and** his son **said to him: For example, whom,** and his father responded in this case as well: **For example, your mother.** This indicates that Rav Yehuda did find peace of mind with his wife. The Gemara answers: She was aggressive and **forceful, but** she was **easily appeased.**

The *gemara* then presents what appears to be a contradiction. Rav Yehuda was teaching his son Rav Yitzchak the verse *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah* — I find bitterer than death the woman. Rav Yitzchak asked: who is an example of such a woman? Rav Yehuda replied: your mother. But elsewhere Rav Yehuda taught his son the verse *yehi mekorecha baruch u'smach me-eshet neurecha* — "may your source be blessed and rejoice with the wife of your youth" — with the interpretation *ein adam motze korat ruach ella me-ishto rishona*, a man finds true satisfaction of spirit only with his first wife. And when asked for an example of this, he again said: your mother.

One wonders about the wisdom of these conversations with one's children about their other parent. But the *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *matkif takifa ve-avurei mi-avurei be-milei* — she would get very angry but could also be calmed down and pacified with words. In other words, the marriage was genuinely mixed — at times very difficult, at times a source of deep satisfaction. This is, of course, true of many marriages. It is neither all one thing nor all another.

### Good vs. Bad Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי אִשָּׁה רָעָה? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְקַשְּׁטָא לֵיהּ תַּכָּא וּמְקַשְּׁטָא לֵיהּ פּוּמָּא. רָבָא אָמַר: מְקַשְּׁטָא לֵיהּ תַּכָּא וּמַהְדְּרָא לֵיהּ גַּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** when a woman is considered **a bad wife? Abaye said: She arranges a table for him and arranges** her **mouth for him** at the same time. In other words, although she prepares food for him, she verbally abuses him while he eats. **Rava said: She arranges a table for him and** then **turns** her **back to him,** displaying her lack of interest in his company.

The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami isha ra'ah* — what would be an example of a bad wife? Abaye says: *mekashta lei taka u-mekashta lei puma* — she prepares the dish and at the same time prepares her mouth — she makes his meals while simultaneously getting ready to berate him during the meal. Rava says: *mekashta lei taka u-mahadra lei gava* — she prepares the dish and turns her back to him. She feeds him while shunning him.

### Wife's Value & Respect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּשָׂא אָדָם אִשָּׁה עֲוֹנוֹתָיו מִתְפַּקְּקִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מָצָא אִשָּׁה מָצָא טוֹב וַיָּפֶק רָצוֹן מֵה׳״. בְּמַעְרְבָא כִּי נָסֵיב אִינָשׁ אִיתְּתָא, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ הָכִי: ״מָצָא״ אוֹ ״מוֹצֵא״? ״מָצָא״ דִּכְתִיב: ״מָצָא אִשָּׁה מָצָא טוֹב״, מוֹצֵא דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמוֹצֶא אֲנִי מַר מִמָּוֶת אֶת הָאִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina said: Once a man marries a woman his iniquities crumble [*mitpakekin*], as it is stated: "Whoever finds a wife finds good, and obtains [*vayafek*] favor of the Lord"** (Proverbs 18:22). **In the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **when a man married a woman they would say to him as follows: *Matza* or *motze*?** In other words, they would ask the groom if the appropriate passage for his wife is that verse, which begins with the word ***matza*, as it is written: Whoever finds [*matza*] a wife finds good,** or whether the more appropriate verse is the one beginning with the word ***motze*, as it is written: "And I find [*motze*] more bitter than death the woman"** (Ecclesiastes 7:26).

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina then offers a more positive teaching: *keivan she-nasa adam isha, avonotav mitpakkekim* — when a man marries a woman, his sins are sealed up or covered over, as derived from the verse *matza isha matza tov ve-yafek ratzon me-Hashem* — "one who finds a wife finds good and draws favor from God," where *vayafek* is understood as related to *pitpuk*, closing or sealing. And in Eretz Yisrael, when a man would marry a woman, they would ask him: *matza o motze?* — is your wife a *matza* or a *motze*? A *matza*, as in *matza isha matza tov* — one who finds good — or a *motze*, as in *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah* — one who is bitterer than death? This was an ingenious way of asking how the marriage was working out, while simultaneously acknowledging that marriages exist on a wide spectrum — sometimes good, sometimes mixed, sometimes genuinely difficult.

A beautiful *drasha* on this exchange suggests that the question was not about the woman at all but about the man: *matza o motze* — what kind of person are you? Are you a *matza*, someone who, having found something, is content — who has a *metzia* and stops searching? Then *matza isha matza tov*. Or are you a *motze* — someone who, even after finding, keeps looking around, wondering if something better is out there? Then *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah* — you will never be satisfied.

### Divorce Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִשָּׁה רָעָה מִצְוָה לְגָרְשָׁהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״גָּרֵשׁ לֵץ וְיֵצֵא מָדוֹן וְיִשְׁבּוֹת דִּין וְקָלוֹן״, וְאָמַר רָבָא: אִשָּׁה רָעָה וּכְתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה — צָרָתָהּ בְּצִדָּהּ. דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בַּחֲבִרְתַּהּ, וְלָא בְּסִילְתָּא. וְאָמַר רָבָא: קָשָׁה אִשָּׁה רָעָה כְּיוֹם סַגְרִיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דֶּלֶף טוֹרֵד בְּיוֹם סַגְרִיר וְאֵשֶׁת מִדְיָנִים נִשְׁתָּוָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is a mitzva to divorce a bad wife, as it is written: "Cast out the scorner and contention will depart; strife and shame will cease"** (Proverbs 22:10). **And Rava said: A bad wife whose marriage contract** settlement **is** too **large** for her husband to pay in the event of a divorce, **her rival wife is at her side.** In other words, the only way for him to improve matters is to take another wife. **As people say** in the well-known adage: The way to trouble a woman is **with her peer and not with a thorn. And Rava said: A bad wife is as troublesome as a day of heavy rain, as it is stated: "A continual dropping on a very rainy day and a contentious woman are alike"** (Proverbs 27:15).

Rava teaches: *isha ra'ah mitzvah le-garsha* — if a man has a bad wife, it is a *mitzvah* to divorce her, as derived from the verse *garesh letz ve-yeitzei madon ve-yishbot din ve-kalon* — "drive out the mocker and strife departs, quarreling and disgrace will cease." This is a significant ruling in light of a tendency in some *batei din* to resist any action toward divorce under the blanket assumption — invoked under the name of *shalom bayit* — that it is always better to remain married. Rava's position pushes back clearly against that assumption.

### Bad Marriage Remedies

Rava adds: if the wife is bad but her *ketubah* is large and the husband cannot afford to divorce her, the remedy is to marry another wife — the presence of a competitor will change the dynamic. The popular saying is invoked: *be-chavarta v'lo b'silta* — better to change someone's behavior through a friend (i.e., a rival) than with a thorn.

### Wife Comparisons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה טוֹבָה אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה, וְכַמָּה רָעָה אִשָּׁה רָעָה. כַּמָּה טוֹבָה אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״מָצָא אִשָּׁה מָצָא טוֹב״, אִי בְּגַוַּהּ מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא — כַּמָּה טוֹבָה אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה, שֶׁהַכָּתוּב מְשַׁבְּחָהּ. אִי בַּתּוֹרָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא — כַּמָּה טוֹבָה אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה, שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִמְשְׁלָה בָּהּ. כַּמָּה רָעָה אִשָּׁה רָעָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמוֹצֶא אֲנִי מַר מִמָּוֶת אֶת הָאִשָּׁה״. אִי בְּגַוַּהּ מִשְׁתַּעֵי קָרָא — כַּמָּה רָעָה אִשָּׁה רָעָה, שֶׁהַכָּתוּב מְגַנֶּהָ. אִי בְּגֵיהִנָּם מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא — כַּמָּה רָעָה אִשָּׁה רָעָה, שֶׁגֵּיהִנָּם נִמְשְׁלָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: Come and see how good a good wife is and how bad a bad wife is. How good is a good wife? As it is written: Whoever finds a wife finds good. If the verse speaks of her,** a wife, this demonstrates **how good a good wife is, as the Bible praises her. If the verse speaks** metaphorically **of the Torah,** it nevertheless indicates **how good a good wife is, as the Torah is compared to her.** Conversely, **how bad is a bad wife? As it is written: "And I find more bitter than death the woman." If the verse speaks of her,** this demonstrates **how bad a bad wife is, as the Bible condemns her. If the verse speaks** metaphorically **of Gehenna,** it still demonstrates **how bad a bad wife is, as Gehenna is compared to her.**

Rava then offers a comparative hermeneutic: *bo u're'ei kamah tovah isha tovah v'kamah ra'ah isha ra'ah* — come and see how good is a good wife and how bad is a bad wife. If the verse *matza isha matza tov* is read literally, then the simple meaning testifies to the goodness of a good wife. If it is read metaphorically — as *isha* referring to Torah or wisdom, as is common in Mishlei — then how good is a good wife that the Torah itself is likened to her. Either way, the goodness of a good wife is established. Conversely, if *u-motze ani mar mimmavet et ha-ishah* is read literally, it testifies to how terrible a bad wife is. If it is read metaphorically — *isha* as a reference to *Gehinnom* — then how bad is a bad wife that *Gehinnom* itself is compared to her.

### Escape Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנְנִי מֵבִיא רָעָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא יוּכְלוּ לָצֵאת מִמֶּנָּה״, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: זוֹ אִשָּׁה רָעָה וּכְתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה. ״נְתָנַנִי ה׳ בִּידֵי לֹא אוּכַל קוּם״, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא בַּר חִיָּיא: זוֹ אִשָּׁה רָעָה וּכְתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה. בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרוּ: זֶה שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתָיו תְּלוּיִן בְּכַסְפּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites further statements on the same issue. The verse states: **"Behold, I will bring evil upon them, which they shall not be able to escape"** (Jeremiah 11:11). **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: This is a bad wife whose marriage contract is large.** Similarly, with regard to the verse: **"The Lord has given me into the hands of those against whom I cannot stand"** (Lamentations 1:14), **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Mar Ukva bar Ḥiyya said: This is a bad wife whose marriage contract is large. In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they said this** verse is referring to one **whose food is dependent on his money.** He is forced to purchase his food with cash, as he does not possess land of his own.

The *gemara* continues with several verses interpreted through the lens of the bad wife with a large *ketubah*. *Hineni meivi ra'ah she-lo yuchlu latzes mimena* — "behold I bring an evil from which they cannot escape" — Rav Nachman quoting Rabbah bar Avuha says: this refers to a bad wife with a large *ketubah*. *Nesannani Hashem b'yadei lo uchal kum* — "God has delivered me into the hands of those before whom I cannot stand" — this too, say Rav Chisda quoting Mar Ukva bar Chiyya, refers to the same situation. In Eretz Yisrael, the reading is: *zo she-mezonoseha teluyim b'chaspa* — one whose food supply depends entirely on purchasing, who cannot grow her own food, always uncertain whether what she needs will be available in the market. This also echoes the earlier passage about the wife who makes her husband *stand on his feet* and *lights up his eyes* — *me'iras eineinu u'mekimas ragleinu* — suggesting that the counterimage is the wife whose household cannot function without her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּנֶיךָ וּבְנוֹתֶיךָ נְתוּנִים לְעַם אַחֵר״, אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: זוֹ אֵשֶׁת הָאָב. ״בְּגוֹי נָבָל אַכְעִיסֵם״, אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: זוֹ אִשָּׁה רָעָה וּכְתוּבָּתָהּ מְרוּבָּה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ הַמִּינִים. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אָמַר נָבָל בְּלִבּוֹ אֵין אֱלֹהִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"Your sons and your daughters shall be given to another people"** (Deuteronomy 28:32), **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said** that **Rav said: This is** a reference to the children's **father's wife,** their stepmother. With regard to the verse: **"I will provoke them with a vile nation"** (Deuteronomy 32:21), **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said** that **Rav said: This is a bad wife whose marriage contract is large. Rabbi Eliezer says** that **these are apostates, and so too** the verse **states: "The vile man has said in his heart: There is no God,** they have dealt corruptly" (Psalms 14:1), which proves that an apostate is called vile.

The *gemara* then reads *banecha u'venotav nesunim l'am acher* — "your sons and daughters shall be given to another people" — as referring to a stepmother: one's children are under the authority of someone who is not their parent. And *b'goy naval ach'isaim* — "I will anger them with a degenerate nation" — is interpreted by Rav Chanan bar Rava in the name of Rav as again referring to a bad wife with a large *ketubah*, though the precise connection to *naval* is not spelled out. Rabbi Eliezer (with the correct reading being *eilu ha-minim* rather than *Tzedukim*, as found in the Steinsaltz edition) says: these are the *minim*, the heretics. The connection may relate to the pattern of verbal argumentation and debate associated with the *minim* — and thus the verse *amar naval belibo, ein Elokim* — "the fool has said in his heart: there is no God."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אֵלּוּ אַנְשֵׁי בַּרְבַּרְיָא וְאַנְשֵׁי מַרְטְנַאי, שֶׁמְּהַלְּכִין עֲרוּמִּים בַּשּׁוּק. שֶׁאֵין לְךָ מְשׁוּקָּץ וּמְתוֹעָב לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם יוֹתֵר מִמִּי שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ בַּשּׁוּק עָרוֹם. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵלּוּ חַבָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*** with regard to the verse: "I will provoke them with a vile nation," that **these are the inhabitants of Barbarya and the inhabitants of Martenai, who walk naked in the marketplace, as none is more despised and abominable before the Omnipresent than one who walks naked in the marketplace. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: These are** the ***Ḥabbarim*,** a sect of Persian priests.

A *beraita* adds: *elu anshei Barbarya ve-anshei Martanai* — these are the Barbarians and the people of Mauritania — *she-mehalchin arumim ba-shuk*, who walk mostly unclothed in the marketplace — *she-ein lecha meshukatz u-metoav lifnei ha-Makom yoter mimi she-mahalech ba-shuk arum* — for there is none so disgusting and abhorrent before God as one who walks naked in the marketplace. Here *naval* points not to speech but to degenerate conduct.

## Chabarim Persecution

### Denying God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲתוֹ חַבָּרֵי לְבָבֶל, שְׁגָא נְפַל. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מְקַבְּלִי שׁוּחְדָּא, תָּרֵיץ יְתֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: When **they said to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The ***Ḥabbarim* have come to Babylonia, he shuddered and fell** off his chair, out of concern for the Jews living there. **They said to him:** There is a way to deal with their persecution, as **they accept bribes.** Upon hearing that not all was lost, **he straightened himself** and **sat** in his place once again.

Rabbi Yochanan says: *eilu Chaverin* — these are the *Chabarim*. Now *chaver* can refer to the class of people in the Second Temple period who ate *chulin be-tahara*, but here it refers to a specific group in Babylonia — Zoroastrian priests who came to Bavel in the time of Rav and later and were notably oppressive, showing no deference to *talmidei chachamim* as other Babylonian authorities had.

### Three Decrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּזְרוּ עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה מִפְּנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה. גָּזְרוּ עַל הַבָּשָׂר — מִפְּנֵי הַמַּתָּנוֹת, גָּזְרוּ עַל הַמֶּרְחֲצָאוֹת — מִפְּנֵי הַטְּבִילָה. קָא מְחַטְּטִי שָׁכְבֵי — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׂמֵחִים בְּיוֹם אֵידָם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיְתָה יַד ה׳ בָּכֶם וּבַאֲבוֹתֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the *Ḥabbarim*, the Gemara cites the following statement of the Sages: The *Ḥabbarim* were able to **issue decrees against** the Jewish people with regard to **three** matters, **due to three** transgressions on the part of the Jewish people. **They decreed against meat,** i.e., they banned ritual slaughter, **due to** the failure of the Jewish people to give the priests **the gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw. **They decreed against** Jews bathing in **bathhouses, due to** their neglect of ritual **immersion.** Third, **they exhumed the dead** from their graves **because** the Jews **rejoice on** the **holidays** of the gentiles, **as it is stated: "Then shall the hand of the Lord be against you and against your fathers"** (I Samuel 12:15).

When Rabbi Yochanan was told that *Chabarai le-Bavel* — the *Chabarim* had come to Babylonia — *shaga ve-nafal*, he collapsed in distress, recognizing what this would mean for the rabbis there. When told that they could be bribed, *taritz ve-yativ* — he straightened up and recovered, understanding that as long as they were susceptible to bribery, matters were not hopeless.

The *Chabarim* issued three decrees against the Jews of Bavel corresponding to three sins. First: *al bisra mipnei ha-matanot* — they prohibited eating kosher meat, understood as punishment for the Jews' failure to give the priestly gifts — *matanot* — from slaughtered animals, a discussion with implications for whether those obligations apply outside Eretz Yisrael. Second: *gazru al ha-merchatza'ot mipnei ha-tevila* — they shut down the bathhouses, understood as punishment for laxity in attending the *mikveh*. Third: *ve-ka-machtati shichvei* — they exhumed the dead. The Zoroastrian theological position held that burying bodies in the earth defiles the ground; their practice was to expose the body in a covered outdoor structure where birds of prey would strip the flesh from the bones.

### Body Exhumation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: (זוֹ) חַטּוֹטֵי שָׁכְבֵי. דְּאָמַר מָר: בַּעֲוֹן חַיִּים — מֵתִים מִתְחַטְּטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Shmuel said: This** verse is referring to **exhuming the dead,** which upsets both the living and the dead, **as the Master said:** Due **to the iniquity of the living, the dead are exhumed.**

The penalty of exhumation brings with it a theological difficulty. The verse says *vahayta yad Hashem bachem uv'avoteichem* — "the hand of God will be against you and against your fathers." How can punishment reach one's fathers? The *gemara* answers: only in the case of exhumation of the bodies — *davar ze, chitutei shichvi*. Rabbah bar Shmuel explains: *ba'avon chayim metim mitchatetin* — because of the sins of the living, the dead are exhumed. The *gemara* continues with an exchange between Rabbah and Rav Mari about two apparently contradictory verses on the subject of burial: one says the dead *lo ye'asfu velo yikaveru, ledomen al pnei ha'adamah yihiyu* — will not be gathered or buried, left like dung on the earth — while the other says *venivchar mavet mechayim* — death will be preferred to life. The resolution offered is that the second verse applies to the *resha'im*: for the wicked, it is better that they die than that they continue to live in this world, sin further, and fall into *Gehinnom*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָארִי, כְּתִיב: ״לֹא יֵאָסְפוּ וְלֹא יִקָּבֵרוּ לְדוֹמֶן עַל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה יִהְיוּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְנִבְחַר מָוֶת מֵחַיִּים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִבְחַר מָוֶת לָרְשָׁעִים, שֶׁלֹּא יִחְיוּ בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְיֶחֶטְאוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ בְּגֵיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: It is written: "They shall not be gathered nor buried; they shall be for dung upon the face of the earth"** (Jeremiah 8:2), **and it is written: "And death shall be chosen rather than life"** (Jeremiah 8:3). If death will be so indecent that their bodies will not even be buried, why would people choose death over life? Rabba bar Mari **said to him:** The latter verse does not refer to the previously described state of affairs, but rather it means: **Death is preferable for the wicked,** as it is better **that they not live in this world and sin and** consequently **descend into Gehenna.**

### Ben Sira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב בְּסֵפֶר בֶּן סִירָא: ״אִשָּׁה טוֹבָה מַתָּנָה טוֹבָה לְבַעְלָהּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״טוֹבָה בְּחֵיק יְרֵא אֱלֹהִים תִּנָּתֵן״. ״אִשָּׁה רָעָה צָרַעַת לְבַעְלָהּ״, מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ — ״יְגָרְשֶׁנָּה וְיִתְרַפֵּא מִצָּרַעְתּוֹ״. ״אִשָּׁה יָפֶה אַשְׁרֵי בַּעְלָהּ, מִסְפַּר יָמָיו כִּפְלַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites more statements concerning women. **It is written in the book of Ben Sira: A good wife is a good gift for her husband. And it is written: A good one will be placed in the bosom of a God-fearing man; a bad wife is a plague to her husband. What is his remedy? He should divorce her and he will be cured of his plague. A beautiful wife, happy is her husband; the number of his days are doubled.** His pleasure in her beauty makes him feel as though he has lived twice as long.

The *gemara* now turns to the book of Ben Sira, citing it with the word *katuv* — "it is written" — as though it carries scriptural weight, despite Ben Sira being classified among the *sefarim chitzoniyim*. A selection of passages is quoted. *Isha tova matana tova leva'alah* — a good wife is a good gift to her husband — *becheik yerei Elokim tinaten* — she will be given into the bosom of one who fears God. *Isha ra'ah tzara'at leva'alah* — a bad wife is like leprosy to her husband — and what is the remedy? *Yegareshena miyad vetitraper mitzara'ato* — divorce her immediately and be healed.

*Isha yafa ashrei ba'alah* — a beautiful woman: happy is her husband — *mispar yamav kiflayim*, the number of his days will be doubled. But: *he'alem eineicha me'ishet chen* — hide your eyes from a charming woman who belongs to another — *pen tilached bimtzudatah*, lest you be caught in her net. Do not incline toward her husband — *al tetei etzel ba'alah limsoch imo yayin veshichar* — do not join him in drinking wine and mead. For in the beauty of another man's wife, *rabbim hushchatu* — many have been destroyed — *va'atzumim kol harugeiha* — and great are the numbers of those she has killed. *Rabbim hayu pitzei rochel* — great are the wounds of the spice merchant, the seller of fragrances — *ha-margilim lidvar aveira* — who habituate people to transgression, since the fragrances lead people to sin — *kenitzotz mav'ir gachelet* — like a spark upon a coal. *Kechluv malei of ken bateihem mele'im mirma* — as a bird trap full of birds, so their houses are filled with deceit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַעְלֵם עֵינֶיךָ מֵאֵשֶׁת חֵן פֶּן תִּלָּכֵד בִּמְצוּדָתָהּ. אַל תֵּט אֵצֶל בַּעְלָהּ לִמְסוֹךְ עִמּוֹ יַיִן וְשֵׁכָר, כִּי בְּתוֹאַר אִשָּׁה יָפָה רַבִּים הוּשְׁחֲתוּ, וַעֲצוּמִים כׇּל הֲרוּגֶיהָ״. ״רַבִּים הָיוּ פִּצְעֵי רוֹכֵל״. ״הַמַּרְגִּילִים לִדְבַר עֶרְוָה כְּנִיצוֹץ מַבְעִיר גַּחֶלֶת. כִּכְלוּב מָלֵא עוֹף — כֵּן בָּתֵּיהֶם מְלֵאִים מִרְמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Turn your eyes from a graceful woman** who is married to another man, **lest you be caught in her trap. Do not turn to her husband to mix wine and strong drink with him,** which can lead to temptation. **For on** account of **the countenance of a beautiful woman many have been destroyed, and her slain is a mighty host.** Furthermore, **many have been the wounded peddlers.** This is referring to men who travel from place to place to sell women's jewelry. Their frequent dealings with women lead their husbands to harm the peddlers. **Those who accustom** themselves **to licentious matters are like a spark that ignites a coal. As a cage is full of birds, so are their houses full of deceit.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל תֵּצַר צָרַת מָחָר, כִּי לֹא תֵדַע מַה יֵּלֶד יוֹם. שֶׁמָּא מָחָר בָּא וְאֵינֶנּוּ, נִמְצָא מִצְטַעֵר עַל הָעוֹלָם שֶׁאֵין שֶׁלּוֹ״. ״מְנַע רַבִּים מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתֶךָ, וְלֹא הַכֹּל תָּבִיא בֵּיתֶךָ״. ״רַבִּים יִהְיוּ דּוֹרְשֵׁי שְׁלוֹמֶךָ. גַּלֵּה סוֹד לְאֶחָד מֵאָלֶף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes additional statements from the book of Ben Sira: **Do not suffer from tomorrow's trouble,** that is, do not worry about problems that might arise in the future, **as you do not know what a day will bring. Perhaps** when **tomorrow comes,** the individual who was so worried will **not** be among the living, and **he** was **consequently upset over a world that is not his. Prevent a crowd from inside your house,** do not let many people enter, **and do not** even **bring all** your friends into **your house.** Make sure, however, that **a crowd seeks your welfare,** and that you have many allies. **Reveal a secret to** only **one in a thousand,** since most people are unable to keep a secret.

The selection then pivots away from the subject of women: *al tetzar tzarat machar* — do not grieve over tomorrow's troubles — *ki lo teda mah yeled yom* — for you do not know what the day will bring — *shema machar ba ve'einenu* — perhaps tomorrow will come and none of your fears will have materialized — *nimtza mitzta'er al olam she'eino shelo* — and you will have been suffering over a world that was never yours to begin with. Finally: *mine'a rabbim mitoch beitecha* — keep many people out of your home — *velo hakol tavi leveitecha* — do not bring everyone in — *rabbim yihiyu dorshei shelomecha* — many will present themselves as seeking your welfare — *vegale sod leachad me'alef* — but reveal your secrets to only one in a thousand.

## Procreation Mitzvah

### Mashiach Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ כׇּל הַנְּשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּגּוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי רוּחַ מִלְּפָנַי יַעֲטוֹף וּנְשָׁמוֹת אֲנִי עָשִׂיתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said: The** Messiah, **son of David, will not come until all the souls of the body have been finished,** i.e., until all souls that are destined to inhabit physical bodies will do so. **As it is stated: "For the spirit that enwraps itself is from Me, and the souls that I have made"** (Isaiah 57:16).

Rabbi Assi teaches: *ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu kol neshamot shebaguf* — Ben David does not come until all the souls stored in the heavenly repository have been sent into the world — as the verse says: *ki ruach milifnai ya'atof uneshamot ani asiti* — "for the spirit that passes before Me, and the souls that I have made." The coming of *Mashiach* is thus linked to the exhaustion of the reservoir of souls, which in turn depends on the fulfillment of *pru u'revu*.

### Three Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹסֵק בִּפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹפֵךְ דַּם הָאָדָם בָּאָדָם דָּמוֹ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְאַתֶּם פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ״. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: כְּאִילּוּ מְמַעֵט הַדְּמוּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי בְּצֶלֶם אֱלֹהִים עָשָׂה אֶת הָאָדָם״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְאַתֶּם פְּרוּ וְגוֹ׳״. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים וּמְמַעֵט הַדְּמוּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתֶּם פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who does not engage in** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply** is considered **as though he sheds blood, as it is stated: "Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed"** (Genesis 9:6), **and it is written** immediately **afterward: "And you, be fruitful and multiply"** (Genesis 9:7). **Rabbi Ya'akov says: It is as though he diminishes the** Divine **Image, as it is stated: "For in the image of God He made man"** (Genesis 9:6), **and it is written** immediately **afterward: "And you, be fruitful** and multiply" (Genesis 9:7). **Ben Azzai says:** It is **as though he sheds blood and** also **diminishes the** Divine **Image, as it is stated: "And you, be fruitful and multiply,"** after the verse that alludes to both shedding blood and the Divine Image.

A *tanna* teaches: Rabbi Eliezer says: whoever does not engage in *firya verivya* — procreation — it is as though he has spilled blood — *ke'ilu shofech damim* — as derived from the juxtaposition of *shofech dam ha'adam ba'adam damo yishafach* with *ve'atem pru urevu*. The one who takes life and the one who fails to create life are placed side by side. Rabbi Yaakov says: it is as though he has diminished the divine image — *ke'ilu mema'et hadmut* — since *ki betzelem Elokim asa et ha'adam*, humans are made in the image of God; not bringing human beings into the world therefore diminishes God's presence in the world, not merely in biological terms but in terms of the person as a bearer of *tzelem Elokim*. Ben Azzai combines both: *ke'ilu shofech damim umema'et hadmut* — as though he has both spilled blood and diminished the divine image.

### Ben Azzai's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְבֶן עַזַּאי: יֵשׁ נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ וְנָאֶה מְקַיֵּים, נָאֶה מְקַיֵּים וְאֵין נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ, וְאַתָּה נָאֶה דּוֹרֵשׁ, וְאֵין נָאֶה מְקַיֵּים. אָמַר לָהֶן בֶּן עַזַּאי: וּמָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁנַּפְשִׁי חָשְׁקָה בַּתּוֹרָה, אֶפְשָׁר לָעוֹלָם שֶׁיִּתְקַיֵּים עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to ben Azzai: There** is a type of scholar who **expounds well and fulfills** his own teachings **well,** and another who **fulfills well and does not expound well. But you,** who have never married, **expound well** on the importance of procreation, **and** yet **you do not fulfill well** your own teachings. **Ben Azzai said to them: What shall I do, as my soul yearns for Torah,** and I do not wish to deal with anything else. **It is possible for the world to be maintained by others,** who are engaged in the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply.

They said to Ben Azzai: *yesh na'eh doresh vena'eh mekayem* — some expound well and fulfill well; *na'eh mekayem ve'ein na'eh doresh* — some fulfill even without eloquence. But you — *ata na'eh doresh ve'ein na'eh mekayem* — you speak beautifully but do not practice what you preach. You are not married. Ben Azzai replied: *uma e'ese shenafshi chashka baTorah* — what can I do? My soul is bound up in Torah. *Efshar la'olam sheyitkayem al yedei acherim* — let others fulfill the obligation. This raises a fascinating question that generates much subsequent discussion: can one who devotes himself entirely to Torah study be exempt from *pru u'revu*? And is there a sense in which Torah study serves as some kind of analog or substitute?

### Torah Priority

The *gemara* records a second *beraita* with the same essential structure — Rabbi Eliezer: *shofech damim*; Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: *mema'et hadmut*; Ben Azzai: both — followed again by the challenge to Ben Azzai and his reply. The repetition prompts a closer look at what is really at stake in the debate between *shofech damim* and *mema'et hadmut*. Are these not both ultimately saying the same thing — that failing to bring life into the world is a grave failure?

A possible reading is that the distinction tracks the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel on the minimum requirement for *pru u'revu*. Beit Shammai requires two boys; Beit Hillel requires a boy and a girl. The logic behind Beit Shammai's position — why boys specifically? — has never been fully explained, and the usual answer that boys were simply more valued in rabbinic culture, while sociologically accurate, does not resolve the internal halakhic logic. But if the emphasis falls on *betzelem Elokim* — on bringing into the world beings who will bear and enact the divine image through Torah and *mitzvot* — and if in the halakhic framework of the *gemara* men are the primary bearers of Torah obligation, then one could argue that Beit Shammai's preference for boys flows directly from the *mema'et hadmut* framework. The point of procreation, on that view, is not merely biological perpetuation of life — that would be captured by *shofech damim* — but the amplification of God's presence in the world through Torah-observant persons. That framing might genuinely favor boys over girls in Beit Shammai's understanding. Whether or not one accepts this reasoning normatively, it offers a coherent internal logic for an otherwise puzzling position.

### Divine Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּבְנֻחֹה יֹאמַר שׁוּבָה ה׳ רִבְבוֹת אַלְפֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין הַשְּׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה עַל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנֵי אֲלָפִים וּשְׁנֵי רְבָבוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים וּשְׁנֵי רְבָבוֹת חָסֵר אֶחָד, וְזֶה לֹא עָסַק בִּפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — לֹא נִמְצָא זֶה גּוֹרֵם לַשְּׁכִינָה שֶׁתִּסְתַּלֵּק מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply: **"And when it rested, he would say: Return, Lord, to the ten thousands of the thousands of Israel"** (Numbers 10:36). This **teaches that the Divine Presence does not rest upon less than two thousands and two ten-thousands of the Jewish people,** as the terms thousands and ten-thousands are both in the plural. Consequently, if **there were two thousands and two ten-thousands of the Jewish people, less one, and this** man **did not engage in** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply, is he not found** to have **caused the Divine Presence to be depart from the Jewish people?**

A *tanu rabbanan* is cited: *uvenucho yomar shuva Hashem rivvot alfei Yisrael* — "and when it rested, he said: return, O God, to the myriads of thousands of Israel." This teaches that the *Shechina* does not rest on fewer than two thousand and two myriads — twenty-two thousand — of Israel. Suppose Israel numbered exactly one fewer than that — 21,999. And suppose one person among them had not engaged in *pru u'revu* — *nimtza ze gorem la-Shechina shetistalek mi-Yisrael*: that one person's failure to bring the missing child into the world is what caused the *Shechina* to depart from Israel. This is the fullest expression of *mema'et hadmut*: not an abstract diminishment but a direct causal link between one individual's failure to procreate and the withdrawal of divine presence.

### Lineage Continuity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: גּוֹרֵם לַשְּׁכִינָה שֶׁתִּסְתַּלֵּק מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִהְיוֹת לְךָ לֵאלֹהִים וּלְזַרְעֲךָ אַחֲרֶיךָ״, בִּזְמַן שֶׁזַּרְעֲךָ אַחֲרֶיךָ — שְׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה, אֵין ״זַרְעֲךָ אַחֲרֶיךָ״ — עַל מִי שׁוֹרָה? עַל הָעֵצִים וְעַל הָאֲבָנִים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Others say:** He **causes the Divine Presence to depart from the Jewish people, as it is stated: "To be a God to you and to your seed after you"** (Genesis 17:7). **When your seed is after you,** i.e., when you have children, **the Divine Presence rests** upon the Jewish people, but if **your seed is not after you, upon whom** can the Divine Presence **rest? Upon wood and stones?**

Others say: *gorem la-Shechina shetistalek mi-Yisrael*, derived from the verse *lihyot lecha l'Elokim u'l'zaracha acharecha* — "to be your God and the God of your offspring after you." *Bi-zman she-zaracha acharecha, ha-Shechina shorah* — when you have offspring after you, the *Shechina* rests. *Ein zaracha acharecha* — if there is no offspring — *al mi shorah? al ha-eitzim v'al ha-avanim* — upon whom will it rest? Upon the sticks and stones?

It is worth noting that the phrase *al ha-eitzim v'al ha-avanim* likely carries an additional resonance. There is a *gemara* elsewhere that speaks of wasting seed *al ha-eitzim v'al ha-avanim* — upon the sticks and the stones — in the context of improper emission of seed. The echo here seems intentional: if one's seed does not produce children and thereby provide a vessel for the *Shechina*, the implicit question is where exactly that seed was going, and where the divine presence could possibly be expected to reside. The emphasis in both passages on *Yisrael* — that the *Shechina* requires Israel specifically, not humanity in general — also connects to the earlier point about *betzelem Elokim* and the patrilineal continuity of *zaracha acharecha*, which in a patriarchal framework focuses on the male line: Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov. This in turn further illuminates the Beit Shammai position: the continuity of *Yisrael* as the bearer of God's presence in the world depends on sons.
