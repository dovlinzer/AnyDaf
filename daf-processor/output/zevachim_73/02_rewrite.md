# Zevachim 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Davar Chashuv & Figs

### Intro & Rabbi Meir

The Gemara on daf 73 opens with a *beraita* invoked to identify a *tanna* who holds that even items belonging to the category of *kol she-darko limnot* — things that are normally or occasionally counted — are not *batel*. To situate this properly, recall the range of opinions concerning *davar chashuv*: Rabbi Meir's position addresses things that are exclusively or generally counted and holds they are not *batel*; the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Akiva restrict the category to a small enumerated list of six or seven items. Now the Gemara introduces a still more expansive definition — *kol davar she-yesh bo minyan*, any item that can be or is occasionally counted — which represents the broadest possible category of things that resist nullification.

### Pressed Figs Case

The *beraita* presents the following scenario: a *litra* of pressed figs of *terumah* — *litra ketsiot she-darsa al pi iggul* — were pressed into a round in a device used for making fig-rounds, but the owner no longer knows in which round he pressed them. The figs of *terumah* were pressed into one such round among many, but the question is which container it ended up in. The case is examined across three types of vessels: *al pi iggul*, pressed into one of these rounded devices; *al pi chavit*, placed into one of many barrels where each barrel contains multiple rounds stacked one upon another, and the *terumah* round is at the top — *al pi kaveret*, placed into a similar container of yet another type. In each case, *ve-eini yode'a be-eizeh darsah* — the owner does not know which container holds the *terumah*.

### R. Eliezer vs. R. Yehosh…

According to Rabbi Meir's version of the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, the positions are as follows. Rabbi Eliezer takes the lenient position: *ro'in et ha-elyonot ke-illu hen perudot ve-ha-tachtonot ma'alot et ha-elyonot* — we regard the top layer as though it were dispersed throughout the entire barrel, mixed together with all the rounds below it, and the lower contents effectively raise and mix with the upper. Even though we know that the *terumah* is at the top, Rabbi Eliezer allows us to treat the entire contents of all the containers as one aggregate. Since *terumah* is *batel* in a ratio of one in a hundred, and if the total rounds across all containers add up to a hundred, it is *batel*. This is a strikingly lenient position — and it is justified, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, by the fact that *terumah* nowadays is only *de-rabbanan*.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and adopts the more straightforward analysis: only the top layers are relevant, since we know the *terumah* is specifically among the top rounds. Therefore, *im yesh sham me'ah pumot ya'alu* — if there are a hundred top layers, a hundred barrels, then among those hundred top rounds the *terumah* is *batel*. But if not — *ve-im lav ha-pumot asurim ve-ha-shulayim muttarin* — then the top round of each barrel is forbidden while everything below is certainly permitted, since there is no question that the *terumah* is confined to the top layer.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Version

Rabbi Yehuda presents a different version of the same debate, with both positions shifted to a stricter register. In Rabbi Yehuda's version, Rabbi Eliezer already adopts what had been Rabbi Yehoshua's position in Rabbi Meir's version: Rabbi Eliezer says *im yesh sham kof pumot ya'alu* — one must find a hundred top layers for the *terumah* to be *batel*; if not, the tops are forbidden. This means that even the more lenient position in Rabbi Yehuda's version begins one level higher than Rabbi Meir's version. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehoshua's position in Rabbi Yehuda's version is stricter still: *afilu yesh sham shelosh me'ot pumot lo ya'alu* — even if there were three hundred top layers, the *terumah* is not *batel*.

### Not Batel Conclusion

Why would three hundred not suffice, when *terumah* is ordinarily *batel* at one in a hundred? The answer is precisely the principle under discussion: this *litra* of pressed figs belongs to the category of items that are *sometimes* counted. A round of pressed figs could be sold by weight, as part of a full barrel, or in various other ways — but it can also be sold as a round unto itself, and that occasional counting confers upon it the status of *davar chashuv*. Because it is occasionally counted, it is not *batel*, even by *terumah*, which is rabbinic. The *beraita* concludes with an additional case: *daras be-igul ve-eini yode'a be-eizeh makom be-igul darsah* — if the *terumah* was pressed somewhere within a single device but the owner cannot remember where — whether in the northern section or the southern, *o litzfonah o lidromah* — then *divrei hakol ya'alu*, everyone agrees that all the figs are combined together and the *terumah* is *batel*. In that case there is no identifiable layer and the mixture is genuinely uniform.

What emerges is a spectrum of positions on *davar chashuv*: from the six or seven items of Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*, to things sold exclusively by count, to things sold usually by count, to things sold only occasionally by count. The Gemara's implication is that cows — which are certainly sometimes if not exclusively sold by number — would fall within at least the most expansive of these views, and therefore are not *batel* in the case of the *mishnah*. The argument from *davar chashuv* thus provides one basis for why the mixed herd cannot be nullified.

## Ba'alei Chayim Never Bat…

### Rav Ashi's Answer

Rav Ashi offers a more satisfying and broader answer. *Afillu teima Rabbanan* — even according to the *Rabbanan* or Rabbi Akiva, who do not accept the category of *darko limnot*, they would nonetheless maintain that there is no *bittul* in the *mishnah*'s case. The reason is independent of counting and significance in the marketplace: *ba'alei chayim chashivei ve-lo batli* — live animals are inherently significant and are never nullified. A living creature always retains its individual identity and can never be subsumed into an aggregate. This is why *bittul* simply does not apply to the herd in the *mishnah*, regardless of one's position on *davar chashuv*.

## Rov vs. Bittul

### Individual Cows & Rov

Having established that *bittul* does not operate here, the Gemara shifts to a fundamentally different mode of analysis. It is worth pausing to appreciate the conceptual move being made. There are two distinct ways in which a mixture of dry items — *yavesh be-yavesh* — can be resolved: one is collective nullification, where the minority loses its identity and is absorbed by the majority; the other is individual statistical determination, where we take each item separately and ask what the statistics say about its identity. The Gemara now abandons the first approach entirely, given that *ba'alei chayim* resist nullification, and instead proposes the second.

The Gemara asks: *ve-nimshach ve-nikrav echad minaihu* — why not simply take one cow out of the mixture and offer it? Not because the forbidden cow has been nullified, but because we can invoke *kol de-parish me-rubba parish* — anything that has separated, separated from the majority. Of a hundred cows, ninety-nine are permitted. If one wanders out, statistics dictate a 99-in-100 probability that it is a permitted animal. This is *azlinan batar rov* — following the majority — not as a tool of nullification but as a mechanism for determining the status of an individual item based on probability. Why not proceed this way, taking each cow one by one and offering it up on the *mizbe'ach*?

### Kol Kavua Principle

The Gemara rejects this proposal on the basis of the principle *kol kavua ke-mechtzah al mechtzah dami* — anything that is fixed is treated as though it were fifty-fifty. The cows in the mixture are *kavua*, fixed in place; they have not separated on their own. When we go to take one from the mixture, we are not looking at a separated item and asking where it came from; we are standing before the entire mixture, aware that the forbidden animal is somewhere among them, and selecting from that known aggregate. In that situation we cannot statistically discount the forbidden animal, because it is right there before us.

### Stores Example

The classic illustration clarifies the distinction. Suppose there are nine kosher butcher shops and one *treifah* shop on a street, and a piece of meat is found lying in the street with no indication of its origin. The *halacha* is that we apply *kol de-parish me-rubba parish*: the meat has separated from the group, and nine out of ten of those shops are kosher, so we assume it came from a kosher shop and it is permitted. But if a person went to one of the shops, purchased meat, and then could not remember which shop he had entered, the question has changed. He is no longer looking at a piece of meat and asking about its origin abstractly; he is looking at all the shops together, including the *treifah* shop, and asking which one was it. Each shop is *kavua*, fixed in its location, and the forbidden store is visibly part of the set. The principle of *kol kavua ke-mechtzah al mechtzah dami* applies, and the meat must be treated as though it is fifty-fifty.

This is the problem with taking a cow from the mixture. One is confronting the full group, which includes the forbidden animal, and selecting from it directly. The forbidden cow's presence cannot be statistically discounted in that moment. We are not looking at an individual cow and asking about its identity in isolation — we are looking at the herd and reaching in. That is *kavua*, and *kavua* is treated as fifty-fifty.

## Scattering & Priests

### Scatter Proposal

The Gemara then proposes a creative solution that would dissolve the *kavua* problem: *nichbishinhu de-naidi* — scatter them. If one throws a stone among the herd or startles them so that they disperse, each cow then wanders off on its own. At that point, no individual cow is any longer part of a fixed group; it has separated from the mixture. One could then approach each cow individually and say: here is a single animal, ninety-nine out of a hundred of these animals are permitted — *kol de-parish me-rubba parish* — and this one is permitted. This is not *kavua*; it is a separated item assessed on its own terms. So scatter the herd and then take each cow one at a time.

### Rava's Simultaneity Conc…

Rava responds with a concern. *Gezeirah shema yavo'u asarah kohanim be-vat achas ve-yakrivu* — there is a rabbinic decree based on the fear that ten *kohanim* might come simultaneously, each take a cow, and then bring them all to the *Beis Hamikdash* at the same time. If, say, the problematic animal is one of ten, and all ten *kohanim* arrive at the *mizbe'ach* together with their animals — or with their blood collected in basins — then collectively we know with certainty that one of the ten is invalid. At that point we can no longer look at each *kohen*'s offering in isolation; we are confronted with the aggregate again, and the statistics become unfavorable. Rashi's scenario involves a case of fifteen cows where the majority likelihood actually favors the problematic blood being present among what is brought.

### Basin Objection

One of the *rabanan* objects to this reasoning: *ela me-atah megisat asira* — does the collection basin then render it forbidden? The point of the objection is that once each *kohen* took his cow individually and its status was ruled upon at the moment of selection — *de-chezkinhu*, we assessed it as *kasher* — that ruling does not get revisited simply because the blood of multiple animals later appears together. The status of each animal was determined individually at the time of taking. Once individually assessed as *kasher*, the later aggregation of the blood should not reopen the question.

### Gemara's Reformulation

The Gemara therefore reformulates Rava's concern. The problem is not the simultaneous presentation of blood at the altar — it is the simultaneous *taking* of the animals. *Mishum shema yavo'u asarah kan be-vat achas ve-yikchu* — the concern is that ten *kohanim* will come simultaneously and each grab a cow from the scattered herd at the exact same moment. If they all take at the same time, then even though each one has before him an individual scattered cow, we are in effect looking at all ten together — and knowing that one of those ten is forbidden, we cannot tell each *kohen* that his animal is likely permitted. The analogy is to the case in *halacha* where two people walk two different roads, one of which has *tumah*, and ask separately about their status: each is ruled *tahor* individually. But if they come together simultaneously and ask, one of you must be *tamei*, and both are rendered *tamei*. The simultaneous confrontation prevents individual assessment.

### Likelihood Question

The Gemara then questions how realistic this concern is. *Asarah kohanim be-vat achas mi efshar* — is it truly plausible that ten *kohanim* would scatter the herd and each seize a different cow at the exact same instant? Even defining the moment of "taking" — is it when the hand touches the animal? When the question is posed to the decisor? — is itself unclear. This seems too unlikely a scenario to drive legal policy. And yet, this line of reasoning will lead to the Gemara's more definitive answer.

## Rava's Fear Answer

### Rava's Gezerah

Rava ultimately reformulates his answer more precisely: *mishum kavua*. The real concern is not that ten *kohanim* will literally act simultaneously — that is admittedly implausible. Rather, the rabbinic decree is that we do not permit one to scatter the herd and take individually, because if we were to allow that, people would reason: why bother scattering them? And they would then proceed to take directly from the undispersed mixture, where *kavua* genuinely applies and the taking is clearly forbidden. The *gezeirah* is therefore a fence: to prevent taking from the fixed mixture, the rabbis prohibit even the seemingly permissible act of scattering and then taking individually.

### Not Accepted

Rava then adds a further ruling: *hashta de-amri rabanan lo nakriv — ei nakriv lo martzi* — now that the *rabanan* have said one may not offer these animals, if one goes ahead and ignores the rabbinic ruling and offers one anyway, the *korban* will not be accepted. It is worth noting, as *Tosafos* points out, that the principle of *davar chashuv* not being *batel* is itself likely only *de-rabbanan*. So even if *de-orayta* the offering would be valid — since one could in principle follow the *rov* — once the rabbis have said it is forbidden, the violation of their ruling renders the *korban* invalid and not accepted. This sets up the debate about *nadchin* that the Gemara is about to address.

## Mixed Birds Challenge

### Mixed Birds Mishna

At this point, Rav Huna bar Yehuda challenges Rava from the *mishnah* in the tractate of *Kinnim*. The *mishnah* states: *chatas she-nit'arva be-olah ve-olah she-nit'arva be-chatas afilu achas be-ribbu yamus kulam* — if a *chatas* bird became mixed with an *olah* bird, or vice versa, even one forbidden bird among ten thousand causes them all to die. They are *ba'alei chayim* and are not *batel*; one cannot offer them all as *olot* lest one *chatas* be offered above, nor all as *chatat* lest one *olah* be offered below. The *halacha* is that they all must be left to die.

### Priest Consults

However, this ruling applies only in a specific circumstance: *be-kohein nimlach* — when the *kohen* consults the authority and asks what he should do. In that case, the ruling is clear: you may not offer any of them, and they must all die.

### Priest Acts Alone

But if the *kohen* does not consult — *be-kohein she-eino nimlach* — and simply proceeds on his own initiative, the *halacha* differentiates by what he does. If he performs *melika* on all of them above, which is the location for the *olah*: *mechtza kasher mechtza pasul* — one of them was legitimately offered as an *olah*, one was not, but he has at least validly offered whichever one was the *olah*. Similarly, if he does all of them below, the *chatas* is validly offered. If, however, he tries to resolve the mixture himself by doing one above and one below — *achas le-mata ve-achas le-ma'ala* — the ruling is *sheneihem pesulim*, both are invalid, because we say *she-ani omer chatas kerva le-ma'ala ve-olah kerva le-mata* — perhaps the *chatas* was done above and the *olah* below, and each was offered in the wrong location.

The challenge to Rava is clear: here is a case where the rabbis say you may not offer the mixed birds, but if the *kohen* ignores that ruling and goes ahead anyway, some of the offerings are valid. The rabbinic prohibition does not, after the fact, invalidate what was objectively a valid offering. This is a direct contradiction to Rava's ruling that *ei nakriv lo martzi* — if you violate the rabbinic decree and offer, it is not accepted.

## Nadchin Debate

### Two Nadchin Views

The Gemara resolves this contradiction by situating the two rulings within an existing *tannaic* dispute: *ha ke-man de-amar ba'alei chayim nidchin, ve-ha ke-man de-amar ba'alei chayim einam nidchin* — the case of the cows follows the opinion that *ba'alei chayim* are *nidchin*, while the case of the birds follows the opinion that *ba'alei chayim* are not *nidchin*.

### Nadcha V'Nir'ah

The concept of *nidchin* requires explanation. When an animal that has been consecrated as a *korban* enters a state in which it temporarily cannot be offered — it is described as *nidcha*, pushed away. When it subsequently becomes eligible again, the state is called *nidcha ve-nir'ah* — it was pushed away and then becomes fit once more. The question is whether the period of disqualification leaves a permanent mark. For inanimate objects — blood, for instance — there is broad consensus that once *nidcha*, the disqualification is permanent even when the original obstacle is removed. For *ba'alei chayim*, however, there is a debate: perhaps the living, dynamic nature of an animal means that labels do not adhere permanently, and when circumstances change and it becomes fit, that fitness is genuine and operative.

According to Rava, the rabbinic ruling that forbids offering the animals from the mixture renders them all *nidcha*. Once off the table, they remain off the table — even if one then scatters the herd and statistically the offering would be valid, the prior status of *nidcha* persists. This is the opinion that *ba'alei chayim nidchin*. The ruling in the *Kinnim mishnah*, where the *kohen* who acted without consulting finds some of his offerings valid, reflects the opposing view — *ba'alei chayim einam nidchin* — that a live animal does not permanently carry the label of *nidcha* and can recover its valid status.

## Rabbi Eliezer Heads

### One Head Offered

The Gemara then introduces a further case — drawn from the *mishnah* at the top of daf 74a — to probe the application of *nidchin* to slaughtered items, where the debate about *ba'alei chayim* is no longer relevant, since *kol alma nidche* — everyone agrees that once something slaughtered becomes unusable, the disqualification is permanent for inanimate matter.

The *mishnah* involves the heads of *olot* that became mixed with one another when one of the animals was a *baal mum*. The *kohen* is told he may not offer any of the heads because of the unknown *pasul* one among them. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *im karev harosh shel echad meihem yikrevu kol haroshim* — if one head was in fact brought up on the *mizbe'ach*, then all the remaining heads may now be offered as well. The reasoning is that once one was brought up, we are entitled to assume — retroactively — that the problematic one was the one already offered, and the remaining heads are all presumed valid.

This ruling is striking for our discussion. The heads were initially forbidden by rabbinic ruling — *lo nakriv*. The *kohen* ignored this and brought one up anyway. Not only is that offering not invalidated retroactively, but the act of bringing one up *licenses* the remaining heads to be offered. According to Rava, who holds that ignoring the rabbinic prohibition renders the offering not accepted — *lo martzi* — this should not work. The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Eliezer follows the view of *Chanan HaMitzri*.

## Chanan HaMitzri View

### Two Goats Case

Chanan HaMitzri holds a uniquely permissive position: he does not recognize the principle of *nidche* even for slaughtered items. The *beraita* states: *Chanan HaMitzri omer afilu dam be-kos meivi chaveiro u-mezaveg lo* — even if the blood is already collected in a cup, one may bring a new partner animal and pair it with that blood. The reference is to the two goats of Yom Kippur — the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* and the *sa'ir* whose blood is sprinkled inside the *Beis Hamikdash*. If the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* died and the blood of its slaughtered partner is sitting in the cup, one might think the blood is now *nidcha* — it cannot be used because it has no partner. Chanan HaMitzri says: simply bring a new live goat as the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*, and that blood in the cup can still be used and the service can proceed. Even though there was a period during which that blood was unusable, it is not permanently *nidcha*.

### Rava vs. Others

Rava's ruling — that once the *rabanan* say *lo nakriv*, any offering made in violation is *lo martzi* — reflects the position that *nidche* applies even when the disqualification stems from a rabbinic restriction. Once rabbinically pushed off the table, the animal or its blood remains off the table, and acting in violation does not rehabilitate it. Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in the case of the mixed heads, by contrast, assumes that *nidche* does not apply — that a temporary rabbinic restriction does not permanently disqualify the offering — and this aligns with the view of Chanan HaMitzri, who rejects *nidche* across the board, even for slaughtered items.

The practical upshot is vivid: one who holds *ba'alei chayim nidchin* would say that ignoring the rabbis and offering from the forbidden mixture avails nothing — the *korban* is not accepted. One who holds *ba'alei chayim einam nidchin* would say that if it was done, the offering stands where it objectively was valid. The *sugya* leaves us with this dispute intact, mapping it onto the broader question of when rabbinically imposed disqualification becomes permanent and when the underlying fitness of an item can reassert itself.
