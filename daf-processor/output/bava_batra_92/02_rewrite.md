# Bava Batra 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Machlon v'Chilyon

### Market Ethics Overview

The preceding discussion in *perek* 91 encompassed a range of interconnected concerns: the prohibition against manipulating and cornering markets, particularly with respect to food staples, and the special heightened concern that applies when such commerce occurs in the Land of Israel. Woven into that discussion was the story of *Machlon v'Chilyon* and *Elimelech*, who abandoned the people of Israel during a time of famine. One of the central explanations offered for their punishment was that one is not permitted to leave *Eretz Yisrael* even in difficult times unless there is truly no alternative, and their departure represented a betrayal of the community at its most vulnerable.

### Names Debate

The *Gemara* on 91b takes up the question of names. The *pasuk* in *Rus* refers to them as *Machlon v'Chilyon*, whereas in *Divrei Hayamim* the same figures are called *Yoash v'Saraf*. Both sets of names carry evocative connotations of destruction and doom. *Rav v'Shmuel* debate the relationship between the two. One holds that *Machlon v'Chilyon* were their given names, and the names *Yoash v'Saraf* were applied as descriptors: *Yoash* from the root of *yiush*, despair, because they abandoned hope in redemption when they left the land, and *Saraf* because they became deserving of burning for their sin of forsaking the people. The other opinion reverses this: their given names were *Yoash v'Saraf*, and *Machlon v'Chilyon* were the names applied to them by way of reproach. *Machlon* derives from *chulin*, meaning they desacralized themselves — the *Rashbam* connects this to *chutz la'aretz*, which is itself abbreviated *chul* in Israeli Hebrew — and *Chilyon* from *klayah*, destruction, as they became deserving of annihilation.

### Etymology & Sin

A *baraita* is cited in support of the opinion that *Machlon v'Chilyon* were their given names. The etymological readings in both directions are morally freighted: whether their names from birth already encoded their fate or whether their sins earned them new names, the tradition insists that the names and the deeds are deeply intertwined.

### Biblical Connection

The *baraita* introduces a verse from *Divrei Hayamim* to anchor the discussion: *v'Yokim v'anshei Chozeva v'Yoash v'Saraf asher ba'alu l'Moav v'yoshvei Lechem v'hadvarim atikim* — and the men of *Chozeva*, and *Yoash* and *Saraf* who had relations with *Moav*, and those who dwelt in *Lechem*, and these are ancient matters. The *Gemara* proceeds to unpack this famously cryptic verse.

### Verse Exegesis

The *Gemara* parses each element in turn. *V'Yokim* — this refers to *Yehoshua*, who established and upheld his oath to the people of *Giv'on*. *V'anshei Chozeva* — these are the people of *Giv'on* themselves, who deceived *Yehoshua* and did not fully keep their side of the agreement. The *Rashbam* notes that the verse is set in the context of descendants of *Yehudah*, which raises the question of why *Yehoshua*, who was not from the tribe of *Yehudah*, appears here.

*Asher ba'alu l'Moav* — they took Moabite wives, which applies directly to *Machlon v'Chilyon*. *V'yoshvei Lechem* — those who dwell in *Lechem*, meaning *Beit Lechem* — this refers to *Rus HaMoaviya*, who returned and cleaved to the inhabitants of *Beit Lechem Yehudah*. The word *shava* is embedded: she returned and was bound. *V'hadvarim atikim* — these are ancient matters, for God had already ordained it from of old, as it says *matzati David avdi*, "I have found David my servant," and elsewhere *shtei bnotecha hanimtzot*, "your two daughters who are found," connecting *matza* to *Lot*'s daughters, ancestors of *Moav*, through whom God had already been orchestrating the lineage of David from the very beginning.

### Oath Contrast

The *Maharsha* explains the deeper purpose of grouping all these figures in a single verse. The organizing theme is fidelity — the keeping or breaking of oaths and commitments. *Yehoshua* maintained his oath to *anshei Giv'on* even though they had deceived him. *Rus*, though she came from a foreign land, chose to cleave to the people and the land of Israel. These figures stand in sharp contrast to *Machlon v'Chilyon*, who abandoned their faith, their people, and their land. The verse juxtaposes those who maintained their commitments against those who did not.

*Rav Yaakov Emden* adds a further dimension: all the figures in this verse were involved in some fashion with non-Jewish women, yet each of the oath-keepers did so only through the permitted path of conversion, whereas *Machlon v'Chilyon* acted *be-issur*. The contrast is thus not only between fidelity and betrayal, but between those who found a legitimate path and those who did not bother to seek one.

### Continuation Parse

The verse continues: *Hemah hayotzrim v'yoshvei neta'im v'gederah im ha-melech bimlachto yashvu sham* — they are the fashioners, those who dwell among the plantings and the enclosure, who sat there with the king in his work. The *Gemara* parses further. *Hemah hayotzrim* — these are the descendants of *Yonadav ben Rechav*, who kept their father's oath. *V'yoshvei neta'im* — this is *Shlomo*, who is compared to a planting that grows tall and flourishes in his kingdom. *V'gederah* — this is the *Sanhedrin*, who fenced in the breaches of Israel, the word *gederah* evoking the closing of gaps. *Im hamelech bimlachto yashvu sham* — this is *Rus HaMoaviya*, who lived to see the kingdom of *Shlomo*, her descendant many generations removed. *Rabbi Elazar* reads the verse in *Shlomo*'s palace that he made a throne for his mother not as referring to *Shlomo*'s literal mother but as referring to *l'imah shel malchut*, the foremother of kingship — which is *Rus*.

The *Maharsha* or *Rav Yaakov Emden* also notes the mention of the *Sanhedrin* in connection with the *Rus* story, alluding to the *midrash* that when *Boaz* sought to marry *Rus*, there was widespread objection on the grounds of *lo yavo Amoni u'Moavi*, and *Boaz* convened a *beit din* that established the ruling of *Moavi v'lo Moavit*, thereby enabling the marriage — a ruling subsequently validated by the *Sanhedrin*.

### Rechabites Narrative

The *baraita* continues the theme of oath-keeping by focusing on the descendants of *Rechav*. In *Yirmiyahu*, God instructs the prophet to bring the *bnei Yonadav ben Rechav* into the *Beit HaMikdash*, seat them in a chamber, and offer them wine. *Yirmiyahu* does so. They refuse, explaining that their ancestral father had made them swear never to drink wine, never to purchase land, and to remain nomadic, and that they had upheld this oath across many generations without fail. God then uses them explicitly as a foil for *Bnei Yisrael*, who have not kept faith with Him — and it is on account of this failure that Israel is sent into *galut*. The connection to *Rechav* is associated in the *midrash* with *Yitro*, though the precise association is a matter of the tradition.

### Shemitah Promise

A *baraita* is now introduced that pivots the discussion toward the divine promise of agricultural sustenance during *shemitah*, circling back to the themes of the land and its provisions that opened the *perek*. The verse promises *v'achaltem min hatvuah yashan* — you will eat from the old grain. The question is what precisely this divine guarantee entails. The worry is twofold: will there be enough old grain to last until the new harvest, and will that old grain remain edible?

### Grain Preservation

The *baraita* references the term *belo salmotone*, and the *Rashbam* explains this as referring to a type of rot or infestation, the natural deterioration through which stored grain might become wormy or decay. *Rav Nachman* interprets the guarantee as *belo rtzinta*, without becoming wormy or rotting. *Rav Sheshet* reads it as *belo shadifa*, without being blasted — though this is harder to apply once the grain has already been harvested. The *Rashbam* accordingly explains that this refers to the grain not becoming heated or otherwise compromised in storage. The *Gemara* brings a *tanna* who supports *Rav Nachman*'s reading and another who supports *Rav Sheshet*'s.

### Grain Sustenance

The *Gemara* elaborates on the verse's promise. One might have thought the worry is that there will not be enough old grain to last until the new harvest is fully ripe. The verse therefore says *ad bo tvuatah* — you will eat the old grain until the new harvest reaches its own maturity without your intervention. There will be no anxious gap between the exhaustion of the old and the arrival of the new.

### Aging Benefit

A further *baraita* draws on the doubled language of *yashan noshan* — both forms of the word for "old" — to teach that produce actually improves with age. The doubled expression extends this principle beyond things that ordinarily improve with age; even produce not normally improved by aging will, in the context of the *shemitah* blessing, grow better over time.

### Storage Capacity

The verse concludes with the remarkable image of *veyashan mipnei chadash totzi'u* — you will be removing old grain to make room for the new. *Totzi'u* teaches that your warehouses will still be overflowing with old grain when your threshing floors are full of the newly harvested crop, such that you must remove the old to make room. *Rav Papa* adds the caveat that while the rule *kol mili atika ma'alya* — all old things are good — generally holds, there are exceptions: dates, beer, and fish hash do not improve with age. This is a fitting conclusion to a *perek* that began with concerns about hoarding and manipulation of food staples, ending instead on a note of miraculous abundance and divine provision.

*Hadran alach hamocher et hasfinah*.

## Seeds Selling Case

### Perek Intro

We now begin the sixth *perek* of *Bava Batra*, *hamocher peirot*. The preceding *perek*, *hamocher et hasfinah*, was itself exceptionally rich in conceptual material, encompassing questions about the mechanics of *kinyanim* and the classic issues of *bitul mekach* and overcharging — *sugyot* that occupy a central place in yeshiva study of *Bava Batra*. The sixth *perek* continues within this general territory of *mekach ta'ut* and the obligations of buyer and seller, though in some respects the cases here are more concrete than those of *perek* five, even as they intersect directly with the same underlying conceptual issues.

### Mishnah Seeds

The *Mishnah* opens: *hamocher peirot l'chaveiro v'zaran v'lo tzamchu* — one who sells grain to his friend, who goes ahead and plants it, and the grain does not sprout. *Peirot* here carries its broad meaning, encompassing grain and all produce. The seller is not liable. The buyer's grievance — that he purchased the grain for planting and it proved unfit — does not create a valid claim against the seller. The seller never undertook to guarantee the grain's fitness for planting. Indeed, the seller might well have assumed the buyer was purchasing the grain for consumption. Since the buyer did not specify his purpose, the seller bears no responsibility. Buyer beware. The *Mishnah* adds *v'afilu zera pishtan* — even if what was sold was flaxseed, which most people in an agricultural context would presumably purchase for planting rather than eating. Even so, the seller is exempt: *eino chayav b'achrayuto*.

### Flaxseed Question

*Tosafot* raises a question even in the case where it is clearly understood that the grain is being sold for planting: how does the buyer know to hold the seller responsible rather than himself? Perhaps the failure to sprout reflects something the buyer did wrong in planting. *Tosafot* resolves this by confining the *Mishnah*'s case to circumstances where it became evident retroactively that the seeds were never fit for planting at all — not that something went wrong in the field, but that the seller's goods were inherently unsuitable. Even in such a case, says the *Mishnah*, if the buyer did not explicitly specify his purpose, the seller bears no liability.

### Rabban Shimon

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* introduces an exception: *zar'onei ginah she'einan ne'echalin chayav b'achrayutan* — with regard to vegetable seeds that are not edible, the seller is responsible. The *chiddush* here is straightforward: if the seeds in question have no use other than planting — nobody purchases them to eat — then the context of the sale makes the purpose of the transaction self-evident. The seller cannot claim he assumed the buyer intended some other use. The conceptual principle that emerges is that absent explicit clarification, responsibility follows the most plausible reading of the transaction; but where only one reading is possible, no clarification is needed and the seller is fully accountable.

## Mekach Taut Ox

### Goring Ox

The *Gemara* now introduces a classic debate of *Rav v'Shmuel*: *hamocher shor l'chavero v'nimtza nagchan* — one who sells an ox to his fellow, and it turns out the ox is a *nagchan*, a gorer. In an agricultural society, the overwhelming reason one buys an ox is for plowing. A goring ox is useless for that purpose; the only thing one can practically do with it is slaughter it for meat. And as the *Rashbam* makes clear, and as the *Gemara* will elaborate, the ox's value as meat is generally less than its value as a plowing animal.

*Rava* rules: *harei zeh mekach taut* — this is a *mekach ta'ut*, a mistaken transaction. The buyer was entitled to assume, given the context, that he was purchasing a plowing ox, and having received something unfit for plowing, he received the wrong item entirely. This is not an *ona'ah* dispute about overcharging; it is not a question of whether too much was paid. The buyer received something categorically different from what the transaction implied. It is comparable to purchasing first-quality grain and receiving second-quality, or ordering wine and receiving vinegar. The remedy is return of the purchase price.

*Shmuel* disagrees: *yachol lomar lo l'shchita macharti lach* — the seller can say, "I sold it to you for slaughter." The seller is not obligated to account for the buyer's unstated assumptions. If the buyer wanted to ensure he was purchasing an ox fit for plowing, he should have said so explicitly. *Shmuel*'s position aligns with the *Mishnah*: absent specification, the seller is not responsible. This raises the question of how *Rav* would address the *Mishnah*, which seems to support *Shmuel* directly. One might note that even if the seller knew the ox was a *nagchan* — which seems likely — *mekach ta'ut* does not require deception. Even where the seller genuinely believed the goods were sound and they proved otherwise, it remains a *mekach ta'ut* if the buyer received something other than what the transaction implied. *Mekach ta'ut* is about the object, not the intent to deceive.

### Buyer Identity

The *Gemara* probes the conditions under which *Rav*'s position would even be plausible as a disputed question. *Lachzei ee gabei d'zavin l'nichtsa l'nichtsa ee l'radya l'radya* — let's simply look at who the buyer is. If the *Rashbam*'s condition applies — that the parties are known to each other — and the buyer is the local butcher, then the transactional context makes it implicit that the purchase was for slaughter, giving the seller a legitimate claim that no *mekach ta'ut* occurred. If the buyer is the local farmer, it is equally obvious that he was purchasing for plowing. The *Gemara* therefore situates the doubt in the case of *b'gavra d'zavin l'hachi u'l'hachi* — a person who might buy for either purpose: perhaps he has a side business, or he is a middleman, or he is simply unknown to the seller. In such a case, the buyer's identity cannot resolve the question.

### Price Determination

The *Gemara* next suggests: *v'lechzei damei heichi damei* — let us look at how much was paid. This is the principle of *damim modiyim*, that the price paid reveals the nature of the transaction. If the buyer paid the going rate for a plowing ox — say ten thousand — and the going rate for a meat ox is five thousand, then the price itself establishes that the buyer was purchasing for plowing. Both the *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* note that this principle was debated earlier in the tractate, where *Rabbi Yehuda* endorsed *damim modiyim* and the *Chachamim* rejected *ein damim modiyim*. The *Rashbam* further observes that if the price paid were conclusive, the argument would work in either direction: either the buyer was clearly purchasing a plowing ox and there is a *mekach ta'ut*, or if the seller insists it was a sale for meat, the price differential constitutes *ona'ah*, overcharging — a *mimanafshach* of sorts. The *Gemara*, however, does not develop this line of reasoning.

### Price Fluctuation

The *Gemara* neutralizes the price evidence by setting the case in conditions where *d'yakar bisra v'ka'ei bidmei radya* — meat has become very expensive and now a meat ox and a plowing ox fetch the same price. Under these conditions, the amount paid reveals nothing about the buyer's intended use, and *damim modiyim* cannot resolve the question.

### Practical Difference

With neither the buyer's identity nor the price paid available to resolve the ambiguity, the *Gemara* asks: *l'mai nafka mina*? If the ox is worth the same for slaughter as for plowing, why does it matter whether the transaction is a *mekach ta'ut* or not? If the buyer cannot use it for plowing, let him simply sell it to a butcher and recover his money that way.

### Money Back Issue

The *Gemara* answers: *nafka mina l'tircha* — the practical difference is the burden of inconvenience. The buyer is not in the business of selling meat to butchers. He does not want to go searching for a buyer, negotiating a sale, and arranging a transaction for an animal he never wanted for that purpose. He wants his money back directly from the seller. If it is a *mekach ta'ut*, he is entitled to demand precisely that.

### Cash Requirement

The *Gemara* presses further. Even assuming *mekach ta'ut*, can the seller not simply return the ox itself as payment? If the ox is worth as much as what the buyer paid, let the seller give the ox back as equivalent payment in lieu of cash, invoking the principle that a debtor may discharge his obligation through goods of equivalent value. The *Gemara* resolves that the case requires *d'ika l'ishtalumei minei* — the seller has cash on hand. Once cash is available, the buyer is entitled to insist on being repaid in cash rather than being handed back an animal he has no use for.

### Payment Mode

*Tosafot* addresses the broader question of when payment in kind may be substituted for cash, citing *Rabbeinu Tam*'s formulation of three distinct categories. A creditor who extended a cash loan may demand repayment in cash if the debtor has it; if not, the debtor may pay in goods of equivalent value, and we do not compel him to raise cash specifically. In the case of damages, even if the tortfeasor has cash, he may pay in equivalent goods, since he never received cash from the injured party and the obligation to restore is not tied to the specific medium. The third category is the worker: even if the employer has no cash at all, the worker may insist on cash payment and the employer must go and raise it. Employment carries an implicit promise of cash wages, and one who cannot pay in cash should not hire workers. The *Gemara* in *Bava Basra* explicitly states that if a worker is to be paid in straw — *tol mah she-asita b'scharecha* — the court does not permit this even if the employer is entirely without funds.

## Rov v'Chazakah

### Rav's Position

Having established why the distinction matters, the *Gemara* now frames the underlying dispute between *Rav* and *Shmuel* as a general question of jurisprudential principle. *Rav amar mekach ta'ut* — *Rav* rules it is a *mekach ta'ut* — *basar ruba azlinan*, because we follow the majority, *v'rubba l'radya zvinay*, and the majority of buyers purchase oxen for plowing. Therefore the buyer's assumption is vindicated by the *rov*, and he may claim *mekach ta'ut*.

### Shmuel's Position

*Shmuel* disagrees: *ki azlinan basar ruba b'issura, b'mamona lo* — we follow the *rov* in matters of *issur*, forbidden and permitted, but not in monetary matters. The principle of *holchin bemamon achar harov* does not apply; one cannot use a statistical majority to extract money from another person.

### Issur vs Mamona

The distinction between *issur* and *mamona* in the application of *rov* is a fundamental one. In ritual law, if nine out of ten shops sell kosher meat and one sells *treif*, we may follow the majority and assume a found piece of meat is kosher. The question in *mamona* is whether the same logic entitles a claimant to extract money from a defendant.

It is worth noting that one could have framed the entire debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel* as something far more local: a question about the conventions governing commercial transactions. Who bears the burden of clarifying the purpose of a sale — the buyer or the seller? This is not necessarily a question about *rov* at all; it is simply a question about market conventions, about who must speak up in cases of ambiguity. Under this framing, even if the *beit din* knew with certainty that the buyer purchased for plowing, that would not necessarily make it the seller's responsibility to have known. But the *Gemara* frames it as a general rule about whether *rov* is sufficient to establish monetary claims — not limited to transactions but applicable wherever there is a disputed fact that bears on a monetary obligation.

### Creditor Presumption

*Shmuel*'s position is typically explained through the interaction of *rov* with *chazakah*. The seller has money in his pocket. The buyer wants it out of his pocket. *Hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the one who seeks to extract from another bears the burden of proof. A *rov* may tell us what is statistically probable, but it does not constitute the kind of definitive proof required to overcome a person's presumptive right to what is in his possession. The seller is *muchzak* in the money, and *chazakah* of possession stands against the *rov* of the buyer. It is not that *rov* is being discarded; rather, in the monetary context, it cannot overcome the *chazakah* of the one holding the funds.

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* raises a striking difficulty. If *rov* is insufficient to take money out of someone's pocket because the person is *muchzak* in his life as well — why would *rov* be sufficient to execute someone? The *Gemara* elsewhere establishes that one follows the *rov* in capital cases: if the victim might have been a *treifa*, or if there is a question whether the condemned's father was truly his halachic father — both determinative for liability in capital law — the *rov* is relied upon. If anything, the standard of proof required to take a person's life should be *at least* as high as the standard required to take money from his pocket. Why then does *rov* suffice in capital cases but not in monetary ones?

The answer lies in distinguishing between categories of *rov*. There is the *rov* of sixty or seventy percent — a statistical majority that tells us what is probable when we genuinely do not know. And there is the *rov* of ninety or ninety-five percent — a preponderance so overwhelming that *halacha* treats it as equivalent to *vaday*, definitive knowledge. In capital cases, the *rov* relied upon operates at this latter level: we do not merely say we don't know but follow the majority; we say that for *halachic* purposes we treat the fact as known. It is in this sense that *rov* can sustain a capital ruling, while a weaker *rov* against a *chazakah* of possession cannot overcome the monetary presumption. We will continue with the evidence the *Gemara* brings to test this framework in tomorrow's session.

## Ketubah Status

### Woman's Claim

The *Gemara* now introduces a *meitivei*, a challenge to the framework of *holchin bemamon achar harov*. The case: a woman who has been widowed or divorced seeks to collect her *kesubah*. She does not have the *kesubah* document — in communities where a *kesubah* was not written, the *get* itself served as the document entitling her to the base payment. The question is what that base payment is. A woman married as a *besula* collects 200 *zuz*; a woman who was a widow or divorcée at the time of her marriage collects 100 *zuz* as *almana*. The woman claims *besula nisasi* — I was married as a virgin, I am entitled to 200. The husband — this must be a case of divorce, since the husband speaks — counters: *almana nisasich*, I married you as a widow, you are entitled only to 100. She is *hamotzi mechavero*, the one seeking to extract from him, and the burden of proof is on her.

### Proof Methods

If there are witnesses — *im yesh eidim sheyatz'sa behinuma* — that she went out to her wedding with a *hinuma*, which the *Rashbam* understands as a type of veil or canopy associated specifically with a first wedding, *verasha parua*, and with her hair uncovered — that is, she appeared as a young woman going to her first marriage with the customary accoutrements and display associated with a *besula* — then *kesubasah masayim*, she collects 200. This raises an interesting observation in passing: the uncovering of hair at a first wedding implies that at least in certain periods and communities, a divorced or widowed woman would already have been covering her hair, such that the state of a *besula* at her wedding was visibly distinguishable.

### Rov Question

The *Gemara* draws its inference: *ta'ama de'ika eidim* — the reason she succeeds is because there are witnesses. *Ha lika eidim lo* — without witnesses, she would not succeed. But why not? *Leima halech achar rov nashim* — let us simply follow the majority of women, *v'rov nashim besulos nisa'os*, and the majority of women in that society were married as virgins. Why does she need witnesses at all? This challenge, the *Gemara* recognizes, bears directly on *Shmuel*'s principle: if *ein holchin bemamon achar harov*, then *rov* cannot suffice even where the statistical majority strongly favors the claimant. But then what exactly is the reason, and does this support *Shmuel* universally or is there something specific to this case?

### Kol Answer

*Ravina* answers: *rov nashim besulos nisa'os u'miyut almanos* — true, most women marry as virgins and a minority as widows or divorcées. *V'chol hanisa'os besulos yesh la kol* — but every woman who marries as a *besula* has a *kol*, a matter of public knowledge. The wedding of a *besula* is an event; it is known in the community. *V'zo ho'il v'ein la kol*, but this woman — since she has no *kol* — *itra lei ruba*, the weight of the *rov* is undermined. The absence of *kol* erodes the *rov*'s presumptive force.

At this stage of the *Gemara*, it remains possible to read the *eidim* and the *kol* as related: perhaps the *eidim* are precisely establishing that there was a *kol*, that there was sufficient public awareness at the time of the wedding. On this reading, without *eidim* we lack evidence of a *kol*, and without *kol* the *rov* is undermined.

### Kol v'Edim

The *Gemara* complicates this reading by asking: *kol hanei de-psulot she-yesh lahen kol, ki ika eidim mai havei* — if all *besulot* have a *kol*, then what does it add that there are *eidim*? If the *kol* already existed, why should *eidim* be needed at all? And conversely, if there is no *kol* — *mide-la ka lei kol* — then the *eidim* who testify to the details of the wedding ceremony are testifying in the face of an absent *kol*, which the *Gemara* treats as remarkable: *sahadei shikra ninhu* — the witnesses would effectively be contradicted by the absence of communal memory. This makes explicit that the *Gemara* regards *kol* and *eidim* as distinct categories of evidence rather than as the same phenomenon.

### Kol Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves: *ela rov nishot besulot she-yesh lahen kol* — not every *besula* who marries has a *kol*, but most do. Where a *kol* did exist, the *rov* is reinforced by the public knowledge and the woman can succeed. Where no *kol* is present, the *rov* is undermined — not because the absence of *kol* proves she was not a *besula*, but because it removes the corroborating support that would have made the *rov* compelling. *V'zu ho'il v'ein la kol itra lah ruba* — for this woman, since there is no *kol*, the *rov* has been weakened. But once *eidim* are produced, their testimony establishes the fact directly, independent of the *rov*, and she collects 200.

The upshot is that the *Gemara* here is using the *kol* not to prove the fact in question, but to explain why the *rov* does or does not carry sufficient weight in a monetary claim. This continues the exploration of the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — that the standard of proof required to extract money from another is higher than simple statistical probability — and the question of under what conditions *rov* can or cannot meet that standard. This discussion will continue in the next *shiur*.
