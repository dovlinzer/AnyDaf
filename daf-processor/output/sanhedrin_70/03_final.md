# Sanhedrin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Age of Puberty Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח בְּיַד נָתָן הַנָּבִיא וַיִּקְרָא אֶת שְׁמוֹ יְדִידְיָהּ בַּעֲבוּר (כִּי) ה׳ (אֲהֵבוֹ)״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי לִשְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים וַיִּהְיוּ גֹזְזִים לְאַבְשָׁלוֹם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַבְשָׁלוֹם בָּרַח וַיֵּלֶךְ גְּשׁוּרָה וַיְהִי שָׁם שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב אַבְשָׁלוֹם בִּירוּשָׁלִַים שְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים וּפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ לֹא רָאָה״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵלְכָה נָּא וַאֲשַׁלְּמָה אֶת נִדְרִי אֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתִּי לַה׳ בְּחֶבְרוֹן״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲחִיתֹפֶל רָאָה כִּי לֹא נֶעֶשְׂתָה עֲצָתוֹ וַיַּחֲבֹשׁ אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וַיָּקׇם וַיֵּלֶךְ אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וְאֶל עִירוֹ וַיְצַו אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וַיֵּחָנַק״. וּכְתִיב: ״אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים וּמִרְמָה לֹא יֶחֱצוּ יְמֵיהֶם״. וְתַנְיָא: כׇּל שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל דּוֹאֵג אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאַרְבַּע, וְשֶׁל אֲחִיתֹפֶל אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ. כַּמָּה הָוְיָא לְהוּ? תְּלָתִין וּתְלָת. דַּל שְׁבַע דַּהֲוָה שְׁלֹמֹה, פָּשׁ לְהוּ עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית. דַּל תַּרְתֵּי שְׁנֵי לִתְלָתָא עִבּוּרֵי, אִשְׁתְּכַח דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written** with reference to the birth of Solomon: **"And he sent by the hand of Nathan the prophet, and he called his name Jedidiah, for the Lord's sake"** (II Samuel 12:25). **And** later **it is written: "And it came to pass after two years, that Absalom had sheepshearers"** (II Samuel 13:23), and at that time Amnon was killed (see II Samuel 13:23–29), this being at least two years after Solomon was born. **And** afterward **it is written: "So Absalom fled, and went to Geshur, and was there three years"** (II Samuel 13:38), so that this was five years after Solomon was born. **And** it is further written: **"So Absalom dwelt two years in Jerusalem, and did not see the king's face"** (II Samuel 14:28), bringing the tally to seven years after Solomon was born. **And** at some point during the rebellion **it is written: "And it came to pass after forty years, that Absalom said to the king, I pray you, let me go and pay my vow, which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron"** (II Samuel 15:7). This was the beginning of Absalom's rebellion against David. Accordingly, at that time Solomon was at least seven years old. **And** at some point during the rebellion **it is written: "And when Ahithophel saw that his counsel was not followed, he saddled his donkey, and arose, and went to his house, to his city, and put his household in order, and strangled himself** and died" (II Samuel 17:23). **And it is written: "Bloody and deceitful men shall not live out half their days"** (Psalms 55:24). **And** in keeping with this verse, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All of Doeg's years were only thirty-four and Ahithophel's were only thirty-three.** Neither reached the age of thirty-five, half of the normal life span of seventy years. Based on this, one can calculate: **How many** years did Ahithophel live? **Thirty-three. Subtract seven** years, **Solomon's** age at the time of Ahithophel's death, which **leaves** Ahithophel **twenty-six** years old at the time of Solomon's birth. **Subtract two** more **years for three pregnancies,** one preceding the birth of Eliam the son of Ahithophel, one preceding the birth of Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, and one preceding the birth of Solomon, son of Bathsheba. **It turns out** that three generations were born in twenty-four years, and **that each and every** parent **begot** a child **at** the age of **eight.**

### Tanakh Fertility Age

The sugya of *ben sorer u-moreh* raises the question of when, precisely, a boy first becomes capable of siring children. The Gemara has already established that the relevant window begins at thirteen, with the appearance of two pubic hairs, and closes three months later — a limit derived from the principle that once a boy can impregnate someone, the pregnancy would become visible within that period, at which point he could no longer be called *ben* without the connotation of fatherhood. The underlying theoretical framework is that he is *nidon al shem sofo* — judged on account of what he will become — a glutton and drunkard who will turn to robbery and even murder to feed his habit. The Rambam, it should be noted, shifts the emphasis away from the individual's anticipated trajectory and toward the verse *ve-khol Yisrael yishme'u ve-yira'u*: the execution serves to protect the institutions of communal authority — divine, judicial, and parental — just as it does in the cases of the *zaken mamreh*, the false prophet, and *edim zomemim*.

At this juncture, however, the Gemara introduces a striking historical claim: in the generations of the *Tanakh*, boys were capable of fathering children at the age of eight. This is not merely an antiquarian curiosity but bears directly on the *halakhic* debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding what age constitutes a legally significant act of intercourse. The Gemara now undertakes to prove, through a series of genealogical calculations, that the men of earlier biblical generations were indeed capable of procreation at eight.

### Shlomo Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר זְעֵירִי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁבִּיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה, פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה שֶׁאֵין בִּיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֶן שְׁמֹנֶה, דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: גָּמְרִינַן מִדּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: לָא גָּמְרִינַן מִדּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים. וְדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים מְנָלַן דְּאוֹלִיד? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״הֲלוֹא זֹאת בַּת שֶׁבַע בַּת אֱלִיעָם אֵשֶׁת אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי״, וּכְתִיב: ״אֱלִיעָם בֶּן אֲחִיתֹפֶל הַגִּלֹנִי״
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabba bar Naḥmani, says** that **Rav Ḥisda says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ze'eiri says: All,** i.e., both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **concede with regard to** a boy **nine years and one day old that his intercourse** is regarded as **intercourse** and disqualifies a woman from marrying into the priesthood as well as results in her liability to receive the death penalty, even though he himself is not liable to receive it. And they also all concede concerning a boy **less than eight years old that his intercourse** is **not** regarded as **intercourse** vis-à-vis these *halakhot*. **They disagree only about** a boy who is **eight years old, as Beit Shammai maintain** that **we learn from earlier generations,** when people were able to father children at that age, and we apply that reality to the present; **and Beit Hillel maintain** that **we do not learn from earlier generations.** And the Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** in **earlier generations** men **fathered** children at this age? **If we say** that we know this **from** the following calculation: **It is written: "Is this not Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, wife of Uriah the Hittite?"** (II Samuel 11:3). **And it is** also **written: "And Eliam, son of Ahithophel the Gilonite"** (II Samuel 23:34), which teaches that Bathsheba was the granddaughter of Ahithophel.

The first proof proceeds from the relationship between Shlomo, Bat Sheva, Eliam, and Achitophel. The Gemara cites the verse identifying Bat Sheva as the daughter of Eliam (*halo zot Bat Sheva bat Eliam eshet Uriah ha-Chiti*), and cross-references this with the identification of Eliam as the son of Achitophel the Gilonite. It then notes the verse recording Shlomo's birth (*vayishlach be-yad Natan ha-navi vayikra shemo Yedidyah*) and proceeds to reconstruct the timeline from Shlomo's birth to Achitophel's death.

Rashi observes that Shlomo is Bat Sheva's second son — the first having died — and the chain of verses that follows tracks the years of Avshalom's movements after Shlomo's birth.

### Absalom Timeline

The calculation unfolds through a sequence of *pesukim*. After Shlomo's birth, two years pass (*vayehi li-shnatayim yamim*) before the sheep-shearing incident involving Avshalom. Avshalom then flees to Geshur and remains there for three years (*vayehi sham shalosh shanim*), bringing the total to five years since Shlomo's birth. He then returns and dwells in Jerusalem for two more years (*vayeishev Avshalom bi-Yerushalayim shnatayim yamim*), for a cumulative total of seven years.

It is at this point — seven years after Shlomo's birth — that Achitophel, seeing that his counsel has not been followed, returns home and takes his own life (*vayachvosh et ha-chamor vayeilech el beito vayetzav el beito vayichanek*). The verse *anshei damim u-mirma lo yechetzu yemeihem* establishes that the wicked die before half their years, with the standard lifespan reckoned as seventy. A *baraita* confirms: Doeg's total years were thirty-four; Achitophel's were thirty-three.

### Eight-Year Calculation

Achitophel died at thirty-three, seven years after Shlomo was born. He was therefore twenty-six years old when Shlomo was born. Shlomo, however, was Achitophel's great-grandson: Achitophel fathered Eliam, Eliam fathered Bat Sheva, and Bat Sheva gave birth to Shlomo. Three generations of begetting thus had to occur within those twenty-six years.

Accounting for the time of the three pregnancies — rounding to approximately two years total for three gestations, assuming seven-month pregnancies where possible — the Gemara subtracts two years from twenty-six, leaving twenty-four years to be divided across three generations. The arithmetic yields eight years per generation: *ishtakach de-khol chad ve-chad be-tamnei olid* — each one fathered a child at age eight.

### Age Nine Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בִּתְשַׁע אוֹלֵיד, וּבַת שֶׁבַע אוֹלִידָא בְּשֵׁית, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתְּתָא בָּרְיָא. תֵּדַע, דְּהָא הֲוַי לַהּ וָלָד מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **From where** do you prove this? **Perhaps both** Ahithophel and his son Eliam **fathered** children **when** they were each **nine** years old, **and Bathsheba gave birth** to Solomon **when** she was **six, because a woman is stronger** and can conceive at an earlier age. **Know** that this is true that women conceive at an earlier age, **as** Bathsheba **had already** given birth to **a child** from David before giving birth to Solomon (see II Samuel 11:27). Therefore, no proof can be derived from here.

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: *dilma tarvaihu be-tisha olidu* — perhaps the two men in this chain (Achitophel and Eliam) each fathered a child at nine rather than eight. This would be marginally more comfortable, since *halakha* already recognizes nine as the age at which intercourse becomes legally significant, even if we no longer hold that boys can father children at that age. The response is that if the men were nine, then Bat Sheva would have had to give birth at six, which is implausible. But the Gemara notes that *ishta barya* — a woman is constitutionally more vigorous — and moreover, Shlomo was not Bat Sheva's first child, which would push her age at first conception even lower, to seven. The chain therefore cannot be used to establish the minimum age of paternity for the men, since the variable of Bat Sheva's age cannot be fixed with sufficient precision.

### Abraham Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״אֵלֶּה תּוֹלְדֹת תֶּרַח תֶּרַח הוֹלִיד אֶת אַבְרָם אֶת נָחוֹר וְאֶת הָרָן״. וְאַבְרָהָם גָּדוֹל מִנָּחוֹר שָׁנָה, וְנָחוֹר גָּדוֹל מֵהָרָן שָׁנָה. נִמְצָא אַבְרָהָם גָּדוֹל שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים מֵהָרָן. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח אַבְרָם וְנָחוֹר לָהֶם נָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״יִסְכָּה״ זוֹ שָׂרָה, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״יִסְכָּה״? שֶׁסּוֹכָה בְּרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר תֹּאמַר אֵלֶיךָ שָׂרָה שְׁמַע בְּקֹלָהּ״. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״יִסְכָּה״ – שֶׁהַכֹּל סָכִים בְּיוֹפְיָהּ. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפֹּל [אַבְרָהָם] עַל פָּנָיו וַיִּצְחַק וַיֹּאמֶר בְּלִבּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. כַּמָּה קַשִּׁישׁ אַבְרָהָם מִשָּׂרָה? עֲשַׂר שְׁנִין. וְקַשִּׁישׁ מֵאֲבוּהּ תַּרְתֵּין שְׁנִין. אִשְׁתְּכַח כִּי אוֹלְדַהּ הָרָן לְשָׂרָה בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלְדַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **from here** that one can deduce that in earlier generations men fathered children at the age of eight, as it is written: **"And these are the generations of Terah; Terah begot Abram, Nahor, and Haran"** (Genesis 11:27). **And Abraham was** at least **one year older than Nahor, and Nahor was one year older than Haran,** so **it turns out** that **Abraham was two years older than Haran. And it is written: "And Abram and Nahor took wives for themselves;** the name of Abram's wife was Sarai and the name of Nahor's wife was Milka, daughter of Haran, father of Milka and father of Iscah" (Genesis 11:29). **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Iscah is** in fact **Sarah. And why was she called Iscah? Because she envisioned [*shesokha*]** hidden matters **by means of divine inspiration. And this** explains **what is written: "In all that Sarah has said to you, hearken to her voice"** (Genesis 21:12). **Alternatively,** Sarah was also called **Iscah, because all gazed [*sokhim*] upon her beauty. And it is written: "And Abraham fell upon his face, and laughed, and said in his heart:** Shall a child be born to him that is a hundred years old? And shall Sarah, who is ninety years old, give birth?" (Genesis 17:17). **How much older was Abraham than Sarah?** He was **ten years** older than her **and,** as stated above, he was **two years older than her father,** Haran. **It turns out** then that **when Haran begot Sarah, he begot her at** the age of **eight.**

The Gemara therefore turns to a second proof, drawn from the family of Avraham. The verse records that Terach fathered Avram, Nachor, and Haran (*Terach holid et Avram et Nachor ve-et Haran*). A tradition holds that Avram was one year older than Nachor and Nachor one year older than Haran, making Avraham two years older than Haran.

The verse then records that Avram and Nachor took wives — Avram taking Sarai, and Nachor taking Milkah the daughter of Haran, *avi Milkah va-avi Yiskah*. Rabbi Yitzchak identifies Yiskah as Sarai: she was called Yiskach (*sokhah*) because she perceived with *ruach ha-kodesh* — as the verse confirms: *kol asher tomar elecha Sarah shema be-kolah* — or, alternatively, because all gazed upon her beauty. Thus Avraham married his niece, the daughter of his younger brother Haran.

A subsequent verse establishes that Avraham was one hundred years old when he laughed at the prophecy of a son, and that Sarah was ninety (*ha-le-ven me'ah shanah yivaled ve-im Sarah ha-bat tishim shanah teled*) — a difference of ten years. Since Avraham was two years older than Haran, and ten years older than Sarah, it follows that Sarah was born when Haran was eight years old: Avraham was ten when Sarah was born, and Avraham was two years older than Haran, so Haran was eight at Sarah's birth. *Mishtakach ki oladah Haran le-Sarah be-tamnei olidah* — Haran fathered Sarah at eight.

### Birth Order Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא אַבְרָהָם זוּטָא דַּאֲחוּהּ הֲוָה, וְדֶרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ. תֵּדַע דְּקָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ קְרָא דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי נֹחַ בֶּן חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה וַיּוֹלֶד נֹחַ אֶת שֵׁם אֶת חָם וְאֶת יָפֶת״. שֵׁם גָּדוֹל מֵחָם שָׁנָה, וְחָם גָּדוֹל מִיֶּפֶת שָׁנָה. נִמְצָא שֵׁם גָּדוֹל מִיֶּפֶת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים. וּכְתִיב: ״וְנֹחַ בֶּן שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה וְהַמַּבּוּל הָיָה מַיִם עַל הָאָרֶץ״. וּכְתִיב: ״(וְאֵלֶּה) תּוֹלְדוֹת שֵׁם שֵׁם בֶּן מְאַת שָׁנָה וַיּוֹלֶד אֶת אַרְפַּכְשָׁד שְׁנָתַיִם אַחַר הַמַּבּוּל״. בֶּן מְאָה שָׁנָה? בַּר מְאָה וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין הֲוָה! אֶלָּא, דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב זְבִיד מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא. אָמַר לִי: אַתּוּן מֵהָכָא מַתְנִיתוּ, וַאֲנַן מֵהָכָא מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ: ״וּלְשֵׁם יֻלַּד גַּם הוּא אֲבִי כׇּל בְּנֵי עֵבֶר אֲחִי יֶפֶת הַגָּדוֹל״. יֶפֶת הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּאֶחָיו הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **From where** do you prove this? **Perhaps Abraham was the youngest of the brothers, and** not the oldest among them. The fact that Abraham is listed first is no proof that he was the oldest, as perhaps the verse **listed them in the order of their wisdom** and therefore Abraham, being the wisest, was mentioned first. **Know** that it is true **that the verse** sometimes **lists** brothers not according to their birth order, but **in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom, as it is written: "And Noah was five hundred years old; and Noah begot Shem, Ham, and Japheth"** (Genesis 5:32). According to this, **Shem was** at least **one year older than Ham, and Ham one year older than Japheth,** so **it turns out** that **Shem was two years older than Japheth. And it is written: "And Noah was six hundred years old when the flood of waters was upon the earth"** (Genesis 7:6). **And it is written: "These are the descendants of Shem; Shem was one hundred years old, and begot Arpachshad two years after the flood"** (Genesis 11:10). If Shem was the oldest brother, how could he be only **100 years old? He must have been** at least **102 years old,** as Noah was 500 years old when his third son was born, and he was 600 years old at the time of the flood. **Rather,** the verse **listed them in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom,** Shem being the wisest. With regard to his age, Shem was the youngest of the brothers, having been born when Noah was 502 years old. Shem begot his son 100 years later, which was two years after the flood. **Here too,** then, with regard to the sons of Terah, it can be argued that the verse **lists them in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom.** **Rav Kahana says: I stated this discussion before Rav Zevid of Neharde'a.** When he heard it, **he said to me: You learn** that Shem was not Noah's oldest son **from there, and we learn it from here: "And to Shem, the father of all the children of Eber, the brother of Japheth the elder, to him also were children born"** (Genesis 10:21). This verse indicates that **Japheth,** rather than Shem, **was the oldest of the brothers.**

The Gemara immediately challenges this proof: *dilma Avraham zuta de-akhva hava* — perhaps Avraham was not listed first because he was the oldest, but rather because the Torah lists children in order of wisdom or importance rather than birth order. If Avraham was actually the youngest of the three brothers, the age calculation collapses.

The Gemara supports this possibility from the sons of Noach. The verse lists them as Shem, Cham, and Yefet (*vayoled Noach et Shem et Cham ve-et Yafet*), which would imply Shem is the eldest. But a subsequent verse states that Shem was one hundred years old when he fathered Arpachshad two years after the flood (*Shem ben me'ah shanah vayoled et Arpachshad shnatayim achar ha-mabul*). Since the flood began when Noach was six hundred years old and Noach's first child was born when he was five hundred, Yefet should have been ninety-eight at the flood — yet Shem is said to be one hundred two years after the flood, making him ninety-eight at its onset, not older. The conclusion is that the verse *eleh toldot Shem: Shem ben me'ah shanah* means Shem was actually the youngest, listed first only because of his importance. It is further confirmed by the verse *achi Yafet ha-gadol* — Yefet was the eldest. *Derech chochmatan kachshiv lehu* — the Torah orders by wisdom, not birth.

Rav Kahana reports that he raised this entire discussion before Rav Zvid of Neharda'a, who responded that he did not rely on the proofs from Avraham's genealogy or from Shlomo and Bat Sheva. Rather, the demonstration comes from Betzalel.

### Betzalel Mishkan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְנָלַן? מֵהָכָא: ״וּבְצַלְאֵל בֶּן אוּרִי בֶן חוּר לְמַטֵּה יְהוּדָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַתָּמׇת עֲזוּבָה אֵשֶׁת כָּלֵב וַיִּקַּח לוֹ כָלֵב אֶת אֶפְרָת וַתֵּלֶד לוֹ אֶת חוּר״. וְכִי עֲבַד בְּצַלְאֵל מִשְׁכָּן בַּר כַּמָּה הָוֵי? בַּר תְּלֵיסַר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ אֲשֶׁר הֵמָּה עֹשִׂים״. וְתַנְיָא: שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה עָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה מִשְׁכָּן, שְׁנִיָּה הֵקִים מִשְׁכָּן וְשָׁלַח מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, from where do we** derive that in earlier generations men fathered children at the age of eight? **From here,** as it is written: **"And Bezalel, son of Uri, son of Hur, of the tribe of Judah,** made all that the Lord commanded Moses" (Exodus 38:22). **And it is written: "And Azubah died, and Caleb took for himself Ephrat, who bore him Hur.** And Hur begot Uri, and Uri begot Bezalel" (I Chronicles 2:19–20). **And when Bezalel made the Tabernacle how old was he?** He must have been at least **thirteen years old, as it is written:** "And all the wise men that carried out all the work of the sanctuary, came **every man from his work that they did"** (Exodus 36:4), and one who is less than thirteen is not called a man. **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **In the first year** following the exodus from Egypt **Moses made the Tabernacle; in the second** year **he erected the Tabernacle and sent out the spies.**

The third proof begins with the verse *u-Vetzalel ben Uri ben Chur le-mateh Yehudah*, identifying Betzalel as the craftsman of the *mishkan*. A related verse identifies Chur as the son of Kalev and Efrat (*vayikach lo Kalev et Efrat va-teled lo et Chur*), and we proceed on the assumption that this is the same Chur.

The Gemara establishes that Betzalel must have been at least thirteen when he worked on the *mishkan*, derived from the verse *ish ish mi-melakhto asher hemah osim* — *ish* implies a full-grown man, i.e., a *bar mitzvah* of thirteen. A *baraita* records that in the first year after the Exodus the *mishkan* was constructed, and in the second year it was erected and the spies were sent.

### Caleb Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי בִּשְׁלֹחַ מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״, ״וְעַתָּה הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם בֶּן חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה״. כַּמָּה הָוְיָא לְהוּ? אַרְבְּעִין. דַּל אַרְבֵּיסַר דַּהֲוָה בְּצַלְאֵל, פָּשָׁא לְהוּ עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית. דַּל תַּרְתֵּי שְׁנֵי דִּתְלָתָא עִיבּוּרֵי, אִשְׁתְּכַח דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written** that Caleb, Bezalel's great-grandfather, said to Joshua: **"I was forty years old when Moses the servant of the Lord sent** me from Kadesh Barnea to spy out the land" (Joshua 14:7). And he added: **"And now, behold, I am this day eighty-five years old"** (Joshua 14:10). **How many** years old **was** Caleb when he was sent off with the spies? He was **forty. Subtract fourteen** years, **as Bezalel was** at least fourteen years old when Caleb was sent to spy out the land. This is known because that mission took place a year after the Tabernacle was erected. This **leaves twenty-six** years. **Subtract two** more **years for three pregnancies,** one preceding the birth of Hur, son of Caleb, one preceding the birth of Uri, son of Hur, and one preceding the birth of Bezalel, son of Uri. **It turns out** that three generations were born in twenty-four years, and **that each and every** parent **begot** a child **at** the age of **eight.**

The calculation proceeds as follows. Kalev declares at the time of the conquest of Canaan: *ben arba'im shanah anochi bi-shloach Moshe eved Hashem oti* — I was forty years old when Moshe sent me as a spy (*ve-ata hinei anochi ha-yom ben chamesh u-shmonim shanah*). He was eighty-five at the time of the declaration; the spies were sent in the second year of the Exodus. The *mishkan* was built in the first year. Therefore, when the *mishkan* was being built, Kalev was thirty-nine years old.

Subtracting Betzalel's age of thirteen yields twenty-six: Kalev was twenty-six when Betzalel was born. Now, between Kalev and Betzalel stand two generations: Kalev fathered Chur, Chur fathered Uri, and Uri fathered Betzalel. Accounting for approximately two years of pregnancies across three gestations, and dividing the remaining twenty-four years by three, each generation must have fathered a child at eight. *Ishtakach de-olid be-tamniya olid* — it is established that they gave birth at eight.

It is noted that the calculation is, if anything, more compelling given that Kalev had already had children by a previous wife before this chain, which would only tighten the arithmetic further.

### Tosafot Age Shift

This entire discussion occasions a remarkable comment by Tosafot on the previous *daf* (69a). The *mishna* had stated that if a minor *ger* dies it is known he has no heirs, since he could not have fathered children. But if, as the Gemara has now demonstrated, boys in earlier generations could father children at eight — and if *bar mitzvah* was always thirteen — then a boy who died as a minor at, say, twelve-and-a-half could theoretically have had children in the earlier period. Why then would we assume he had no heirs?

Tosafot answers: *de-af al pi she-be-dorot ha-acharonim nasnu chachamim shiur be-vas yud beis shanah ve-yom echad u-ve-katan ben yud gimmel shanah ve-yom echad* — even though in later generations the Sages fixed the age of majority at twelve for a girl and thirteen for a boy with two pubic hairs, in the earlier generations when puberty arrived much younger (*she-hayu memaharim lehavi sa'arot*), the *zeman gadlut* — the legal threshold of adulthood — was correspondingly earlier. The *halakhic* definition of adulthood was not a fixed number but tracked the biological reality of puberty in each era.

This is a striking position. The suggestion that the *halakhic* age of majority itself changed with the historical shifting of the age of puberty — and that this is not a rabbinic innovation but a direct consequence of the Gemara's own analysis — is the kind of observation that might seem heretical if stated carelessly. Yet here is Tosafot stating it plainly, and deriving it directly from the *gemara* we have just learned. Tosafot goes on to address the apparent tension with Betzalel's requirement of thirteen, working out the resolution, though we need not enter into every detail of that answer here.

## Daughter Exclusion

### Shimon's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּן, וְלֹא בַּת. תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּדִין הוּא שֶׁתְּהֵא בַּת רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת כְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, שֶׁהַכֹּל מְצוּיִין אֶצְלָהּ בַּעֲבֵירָה
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the penalty for rebelliousness is imposed upon **a son, but not** upon **a daughter. It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: It would be reasonable** that **a daughter should be fit to be** treated **like a stubborn and rebellious son,** and to be punished like him if she sins in the same way as he does. The reason is **that all are found** frequently **with her in sin,**

The *mishna* specifies *ben* — a son — to the exclusion of a daughter. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Shimon argued that by rights a daughter should be subject to the law of *ben sorer u-moreh* just as a son is. His reasoning: *she-ha-kol metzuyin etzelah ba-aveirah* — a wayward daughter is even more likely to draw others into sin, making herself sexually available to feed whatever habit she has developed. This danger to society is at least as grave as the concern that a son, addicted to gluttony and drunkenness, will turn to robbery and murder.

### Gezeirat HaKatuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גְּזֵירַת הַכָּתוּב הִיא: ״בֵּן״, וְלֹא בַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **But it is a Torah edict** that the penalty for rebelliousness is imposed only upon **a son, and not** upon **a daughter.**

The response to Rabbi Shimon is categorical: *ella gezeirat ha-katuv hi ben ve-lo bat* — this is a *gezeirat ha-katuv*, a scriptural decree, limiting the law to sons only. As the Gemara has ruled in several prior contexts within this sugya, the verse says *ben* and the analysis ends there, regardless of how logically compelling the argument for extension might be.

## Gluttony Definition

### Measure Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵאֵימָתַי חַיָּיב? מִשֶּׁיֹּאכַל תַּרְטֵימָר בָּשָׂר, וְיִשְׁתֶּה חֲצִי לוֹג יַיִן הָאִיטַלְקִי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מָנֶה בָּשָׂר, וְלוֹג יַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From when** is a stubborn and rebellious son **liable? From when he eats a *tarteimar* of meat and drinks a half-*log* of Italian wine. Rabbi Yosei says:** From when he eats **a *maneh* of meat and** drinks **a *log* of wine.**

The *mishna* on *Sanhedrin* 70a turns to the question of when the conduct of the *ben sorer u-moreh* triggers liability. The operative verse characterizes him as *zolel ve-sovei* — a glutton and a drunkard. The *mishna* defines this quantitatively: *mi-she-yokhal tartimar basar ve-yishteh chatzi log yayin* — he must have eaten a *tartimar* of meat and drunk half a *log* of wine. Rabbi Yosi disputes and doubles both amounts: a *maneh* of meat and a *log* of wine.

### Mitzvah Gatherings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל בַּחֲבוּרַת מִצְוָה, אָכַל בְּעִיבּוּר הַחֹדֶשׁ, אָכַל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, אָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים
>
> **Translation:** The mishna now lists a series of conditions concerning his eating and drinking. If **he ate** these items **with a group** assembled for the performance **of a mitzva,** or **he ate** them **at** a meal celebrating **the intercalation of a month,** or **he ate** the items when they had **second tithe** status, **in Jerusalem,** he does not become a stubborn and rebellious son because each of these circumstances involves some aspect of a mitzva. If **he ate** the meat of unslaughtered **animal carcasses or animals that had wounds that would have caused them to die within twelve months [*tereifot*]** or **repugnant creatures or creeping animals,**

Eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* — a gathering convened for a religious purpose, such as a wedding feast — does not create liability, even if the boy eats the requisite amounts there. Similarly exempt are those who eat *ma'aser sheni* in Jerusalem, since that too is a *mitzvah* context. Also listed is eating at the gathering for *ibbur ha-chodesh*, the intercalation of the month, to which we will return.

### Both Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִצְוָה, וְדָבָר שֶׁהוּא עֲבֵירָה, אָכַל כׇּל מַאֲכָל וְלֹא אָכַל בָּשָׂר, שָׁתָה כׇּל מַשְׁקֶה וְלֹא שָׁתָה יַיִן – אֵינוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר וְיִשְׁתֶּה יַיִן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא״. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תְּהִי בְסֹבְאֵי יָיִן בְּזֹלְלֵי בָשָׂר לָמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna summarizes: If **he ate an item that** involves performing **a mitzva or an item that** involves committing **a transgression,** or if **he ate any food** in the world **but did not eat meat,** or if he **drank any beverage but did not drink wine, he does not become a stubborn and rebellious son, unless he** actually **eats meat and** actually **drinks wine, as it is stated:** "This son of ours is stubborn and rebellious; he does not listen to our voice; he…**is a glutton [*zolel*] and a drunkard [*vesovei*]"** (Deuteronomy 21:20). One is not called a glutton and a drunkard unless he eats meat and drinks wine. **And although there is no** explicit **proof to the matter** that the reference in the Torah is to meat and wine, **there is an allusion to the matter** in another verse, **as it is stated: "Be not among wine drinkers [*besovei*], among gluttonous eaters [*bezolelei*] of meat"** (Proverbs 23:20).

The *mishna* further specifies that eating every kind of food in great quantity, but without meat, or drinking every intoxicating beverage, but without wine, does not qualify. The verse *zolel ve-sovei* requires both: *ad she-yokhal basar ve-yishteh yayin*. The *mishna* acknowledges that there is no strict proof (*ein ra'ayah la-davar*) that *zolel* means specifically meat and *sovei* means specifically wine, but there is a *zeikher la-davar* — a textual resonance — in the verse *al tehi be-sov'ei yayin be-zolelei basar lamo*, which pairs the two. Rashi notes that this is perhaps simply one illustration of *zolel ve-sovei*, not a definitional limitation; nevertheless, the *halakha* requires both.

### Tartemar Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: תַּרְטֵימָר זֶה אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהוּ, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁכָּפַל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּיַיִן, נִמְצָא כּוֹפֵל אַף בַּבָּשָׂר, וְנִמְצָא תַּרְטֵימָר חֲצִי מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Zeira says:** Concerning **this *tarteimar*** that is mentioned in the mishna, **I do not know what** its measure **is. But since Rabbi Yosei** is found to have **doubled** the measure **of the wine,** as the unattributed opinion in the mishna speaks of a half-*log* whereas Rabbi Yosei requires a *log*, he presumably **is found** to have **doubled** the measure of **the meat as well.** Therefore, it **turns out** that **a *tarteimar*** is equivalent to **one-half of a *maneh*.**

Rav Zeira confesses that he does not know independently what a *tartimar* is. His solution is to reason from context: since Rabbi Yosi doubles the *tanna kamma*'s amount for both meat and wine — a *log* versus half a *log* — and Rabbi Yosi specifies a *maneh* for meat, it follows by inference that the *tanna kamma*'s *tartimar* is half a *maneh*. *Nimtza tartimar chatzi maneh.*

### Cheap Purchase Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר מוֹלָדָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּקַּח בָּשָׂר בְּזוֹל וְיֹאכַל, יַיִן בְּזוֹל וְיִשְׁתֶּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Molada says** that **Rav Huna says:** A stubborn and rebellious son **is not liable unless he purchases inexpensive [*bezol*] meat and eats** it, and he buys **inexpensive wine and drinks** it, **as it is written:** "He is **a glutton [*zolel*] and a drunkard."**

Rav Chana bar Molda said in the name of Rav Huna: *eino chayav ad she-yikach basar be-zol ve-yishteh yayin be-zol* — he is not liable unless he purchased the meat and wine cheaply. The concern underlying *ben sorer u-moreh* is not mere gluttony at a given moment but the fear of addictive behavior that will lead him toward a life of crime. Rashi makes this explicit: if a person only consumes very expensive wine and meat, *lo shakhia'h le-himashekh* — he is unlikely to be drawn after it compulsively, since he cannot sustain such expenditure. The word *bezol* also echoes the word *zolel*, yielding a pun that the Gemara notes (*dichsiv zolel ve-sovei*). The concern, then, is not the individual act of overindulgence but the pattern it presages.

### Dilution Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר מוֹלָדָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר חַי וְיִשְׁתֶּה יַיִן חַי. אִינִי? וְהָא רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: אָכַל בָּשָׂר חַי וְשָׁתָה יַיִן חַי אֵינוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה! אָמַר רָבִינָא: יַיִן חַי – מְזִיג וְלָא מְזִיג, בָּשָׂר חַי – בְּשִׁיל וְלָא בְּשִׁיל, כִּבְשַׂר כִּיבָא דְּאָכְלִי גַּנָּבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥanan bar Molada says** that **Rav Huna says:** A stubborn and rebellious son **is not liable unless he eats raw meat and drinks undiluted wine.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Is that so? But don't Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** If **he ate raw meat or drank undiluted wine he does not become a stubborn and rebellious son? Ravina said:** The two conflicting statements can be reconciled. If he ate totally raw meat or drank totally undiluted wine, he is in fact exempt. The **undiluted wine** for which he is liable is wine that is **diluted but not diluted** properly. And the **raw meat** for which he is liable is meat that is **cooked but not cooked** properly, **like the scorched meat that thieves** are wont to **eat,** due to the hasty manner in which they must prepare their food.

Ravina clarifies the nature of the wine and meat required. *Yayin chai mazug ve-lo mazug* — the wine must be diluted but not too diluted, retaining sufficient potency. *Basar chai bashil ve-lo bashil* — the meat must be cooked but not fully cooked, like the meat eaten hurriedly by thieves over coals. This intermediate state — *ke-vasar kiba de-akhli ganvei* — is itself evocative: it is the food of a man already on the path of crime, eating in haste.

### Seduction Theory

Rashi explains the underlying logic of both requirements. With respect to wine: *mamshikhah she-meshubakh hu* — the wine must be of a quality that draws a person after it. If the wine is either too raw and harsh or too diluted and weak, it will not create the compulsive pull that is the real concern. Similarly for meat: if it is fully and properly cooked, then the person who insists on such refined preparation will never adapt to the lifestyle of a thief, who eats on the run. The intermediate states — semi-cooked meat and wine diluted to a pleasant but potent degree — are precisely the conditions under which addictive behavior is most likely to take hold. *Lo mamshikhah yayin mazug* — over-diluted wine does not draw a person after it; *aval bashil shapir lo mamshikhah* — perfectly cooked meat does not either.

### Salted/Fresh Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: אָכַל בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ וְשָׁתָה יַיִן מִגִּיתּוֹ, אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה. תְּנַן הָתָם: עֶרֶב תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב לֹא יֹאכַל אָדָם שְׁנֵי תַבְשִׁילִין, וְלֹא יֹאכַל בָּשָׂר, וְלֹא יִשְׁתֶּה יַיִן. וְתָנָא: אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן מִגִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** If **he ate** heavily **salted meat or drank wine from his winepress,** i.e., wine that has not finished fermenting, he **does not become a stubborn and rebellious son.** And on a related topic **we learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Ta'anit* 26b): On **the eve of the Ninth of Av, a person may not eat two cooked dishes** in one meal. **And** furthermore, **he may neither eat meat nor drink wine. And** a *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **But one may eat** heavily **salted meat,** as it is not considered meat, **and one many drink wine from his winepress** before it has properly fermented.

Rabba and Rav Yosef both rule that if he ate salted meat (*basar maliach*) and drank wine directly from the pressing floor (*yayin mi-gitto*), he does not become a *ben sorer u-moreh*. These represent the opposite extremes: salted meat is preserved and lacks the freshness that makes meat genuinely seductive; wine straight from the press is essentially grape juice, barely fermented and not yet intoxicating in any meaningful way.

A *baraita* from the laws of *erev Tisha be-Av* is adduced in support: one may eat salted meat and drink newly pressed wine on *erev Tisha be-Av*, because these do not constitute the pleasurable indulgence that is prohibited on that day.

### Duration Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבָשָׂר מָלִיחַ, עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהוּא כִּשְׁלָמִים. וְיַיִן מִגִּיתּוֹ, עַד כַּמָּה? כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהוּא תּוֹסֵס. וְהָתַנְיָא: יַיִן תּוֹסֵס אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי. וְכַמָּה תְּסִיסָתוֹ? שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים. הָכָא מַאי? הָתָם מִשּׁוּם שִׂמְחָה הוּא, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהוּא כִּשְׁלָמִים נָמֵי אִית בֵּיהּ שִׂמְחָה. הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִימְּשׁוֹכֵי הוּא, וּבְכָל שֶׁהוּא לָא מִימְּשִׁיךְ. וְיַיִן – עַד אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **salted meat** on the eve of the Ninth of Av, **how long** must this meat remain in salt before it is permitted? **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Kahana says: As long as it is like a peace-offering,** which could be eaten for two days and one night after it was sacrificed. After this time has passed, it is no longer the type of meat that one may not eat during that meal. Therefore, if it was salted for longer than this, it may be eaten on the eve of the Ninth of Av. The Gemara inquires further: **And** with regard to **wine from his press** before it has properly fermented, **until when** is wine considered in this category? **As long as it is** still **fermenting. And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Fermenting wine is not subject to** the prohibition of **exposed** liquids, as there is no concern that a snake will leave its venom in that wine. **And how long** is **its** initial **fermenting** period? **Three days** from the time the grapes were pressed. The Gemara clarifies: These definitions of salty meat and wine from his winepress were stated with regard to the prohibitions applying on the eve of the Ninth of Av. **Here,** concerning a stubborn and rebellious son, **what** is considered salty meat and wine from his press? The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to the eve of the Ninth of Av, the prohibition **is due to joy; as long as** the meat **is like a peace**-**offering, there is still joy.** But **here,** with regard to a stubborn and rebellious son, **it is due to** the son **becoming drawn** to it, **and** if the taste of the meat is flawed only **slightly he will not be drawn** to it. **And** with regard to **wine,** there is no concern that he will be drawn to it **until** it is **forty days** old.

Rabbi Chanina bar Kahana rules that meat is considered *basar maliach* — and thus no longer fresh enough to create concern — once it has sat in salt for as long as *shlamim* meat may be eaten: two days and an intervening night. Wine is considered *yayin mi-gitto* as long as it is still *toses* — still bubbling after pressing — which a *baraita* establishes as three days.

The Gemara then probes whether the same thresholds apply to *ben sorer u-moreh* as to *erev Tisha be-Av*. The answer is that the two laws rest on different principles. The prohibition on *erev Tisha be-Av* turns on *simcha* — joy — and even somewhat aged salted meat or slightly fermented wine still imparts a certain pleasure. *Ben sorer u-moreh*, by contrast, turns on *imshuchei* — the addictive pull. *U-ve-khol she-hu lo mimshakh* — even a brief period of salting suffices to eliminate the seductive quality for purposes of this law. Wine, by the same logic, may not create the relevant pull until it has fermented for forty days, by which point it is fully wine and no longer *yayin mi-gitto*.

## Wine Ethics

### Wine Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן: לֹא נִבְרָא יַיִן בָּעוֹלָם אֶלָּא לְנַחֵם אֲבֵלִים, וּלְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר לָרְשָׁעִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּנוּ שֵׁכָר לְאוֹבֵד וְיַיִן לְמָרֵי נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara's discussion turns to wine in general. **Rav Ḥanan says: Wine was created in the world only to comfort mourners** in their distress, **and to reward the wicked** in this world so that they will have no reward left in the World-to-Come, **as it is stated: "Give strong drink to him that is ready to perish, and wine to the bitter of soul"** (Proverbs 31:6). "Him that is ready to perish" is referring to the wicked, who will perish from this world, while "the bitter of soul" denotes mourners.

Having treated the technical requirements for liability in *ben sorer u-moreh*, the Gemara opens an extended *aggadic* discussion on wine — its dangers, its proper uses, and its moral valence. Rabbi Chanan states: *lo nivra yayin ba-olam ella le-nachem avelim u-le-shalem sakhar la-resha'im* — wine was created in this world only to comfort mourners and to pay out reward to the wicked.

### Wicked Reward

Rashi explains the second purpose carefully. The statement is not ironic — not that the wicked will drown themselves in wine and thus seal their own destruction. Rather, Rashi reads it straightforwardly: wine brings pleasure (*she-mit'adnim bo ba-olam ha-zeh*), and for the wicked, this pleasure constitutes the *sakhar* — the reward for whatever *mitzvot* they performed — being cashed out in this world, leaving them with nothing for *Olam Ha-ba*. The verse cited is *tenu shekhar le-oved u-yayin le-marei nafesh* — give strong drink to the one who is lost, and wine to the bitter of soul.

### Wine Color Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן כִּי יִתְאַדָּם״? אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן שֶׁמַּאֲדִים פְּנֵיהֶם שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וּמַלְבִּין פְּנֵיהֶם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. רָבָא אָמַר: ״אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן כִּי יִתְאַדָּם״ – אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן שֶׁאַחֲרִיתוֹ דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Look not upon wine when it is red"** (Proverbs 23:31)? **Look not upon wine that reddens the faces of the wicked in this world** when they drink it, **and whitens their faces,** i.e., embarrasses them, **in the World-to-Come. Rava says** that this is how the verse should be understood: **"Look not upon wine that reddens [*yitaddam*]"** means: **Look not upon wine, as it leads to bloodshed [*dam*],** indicating that one who drinks wine will end up committing an act of killing or will be killed because of it.

Rabbi Yitzchak interprets the verse *al tere yayin ki yitadam* — do not look at wine because it is ruddy. The *derashah* reads: do not be seduced by the ruddiness that wine brings to the faces of the wicked in this world (*she-ma'adim pneihem shel resha'im ba-olam ha-zeh*), for it will whiten their faces — shame them — in the world to come (*u-malbin pneihem le-Olam Ha-ba*). The lesson is that wine's apparent blessing to the wicked is in truth part of the mechanism by which their reward is exhausted in this life.

Rava offers a different reading of the same verse: *al tere yayin she-acharito dam* — do not run after wine, because its end is blood. The pursuit of wine leads to the shedding of blood — whether one becomes a *ben sorer u-moreh* and eventually turns to violence, or simply destroys oneself through dissipation.

### Head/Poor Pun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״תִּירָשׁ״ וְקָרֵינַן ״תִּירוֹשׁ״. זָכָה – נַעֲשֶׂה רֹאשׁ, לֹא זָכָה – נַעֲשֶׂה רָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raises a contradiction:** The verse states: "Therefore, they shall come and sing in the height of Zion, and shall flow to the bounty of the Lord, for wheat, and for wine, and for oil, and for the young of the flock and of the herd" (Jeremiah 31:12). The word for wine **is written *tirash*,** without the letter *vav*, **but we read** it as ***tirosh*,** with the letter *vav*. The matter can be explained as follows: If **one merits** and drinks a moderate amount **he becomes a leader [*rosh*],** whereas if **he does not merit** and drinks excessively **he becomes poor [*rash*].**

Rav Kahana notes a *ketiav u-keri* — a discrepancy between how a word is written and how it is read — in the word *tirosh*, the Torah's term for wine. Written without a *vav* it can be read *tirash*. *Zakha — na'aseh rosh*: if one merits, wine makes one a head, a leader. *Lo zakha — na'aseh rash*: if one does not merit, wine impoverishes. The same substance elevates or destroys depending on the character of the one who uses it.

### Joy/Desolation Pun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״יְשַׁמַּח״ וְקָרֵינַן ״יְשַׂמַּח״. זָכָה – מְשַׂמְּחוֹ, לֹא זָכָה – מְשַׁמְּמֵהוּ. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא וְרֵיחָנֵי פַּקַּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** a similar **contradiction:** It **is written:** "And wine that **gladdens** the heart of man" (Psalms 104:15). The word for gladdens could be read as *yeshamaḥ*, meaning that wine makes one crazy, **but we read** it as ***yesamaḥ*,** gladdens the heart. The matter can be explained as follows: If **one merits** and drinks a moderate amount the wine **gladdens him [*mesameḥo*],** whereas if **he does not merit** and drinks excessively **it makes him crazy [*meshamemehu*]. And that is what Rava** meant when he **said: Wine and** fragrant **spices have made me wise;** that is to say, the controlled drinking of wine is beneficial to the drinker.

Rava finds a parallel *ketiav u-keri* in *yismach*, the word for joy in *yayin yismach levav enosh*. Written one way it could be read as *yishma*, evoking *shemamah* — desolation. *Zakha — mesamcho*: wine makes one joyous. *Lo zakha — meshammehu*: it makes one desolate. Again, the same reality, bifurcated by merit.

Rava adds a personal testimony: *chamra ve-reichani pakhin* — wine and fragrances sharpened my intellect. Used in moderation and at appropriate moments, these sensory pleasures were not distractions but aids to the clarity of mind needed for *Torah* learning. This serves as the Gemara's clearest affirmation that the discussion is not about prohibition but about proper use.

### Noah's Thirteen Woes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵישׁ עוֹבֵר גָּלִילָאָה: שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה וָוִין נֶאֱמַר בַּיַּיִן: ״וַיָּחֶל נֹחַ אִישׁ הָאֲדָמָה, וַיִּטַּע כָּרֶם, וַיֵּשְׁתְּ מִן הַיַּיִן, וַיִּשְׁכָּר, וַיִּתְגַּל בְּתוֹךְ אׇהֳלוֹ, וַיַּרְא חָם אֲבִי כְנַעַן אֵת עֶרְוַת אָבִיו, וַיַּגֵּד לִשְׁנֵי אֶחָיו בַּחוּץ, וַיִּקַּח שֵׁם וָיֶפֶת אֶת הַשִּׂמְלָה, וַיָּשִׂימוּ עַל שְׁכֶם שְׁנֵיהֶם, וַיֵּלְכוּ אֲחֹרַנִּית, וַיְכַסּוּ אֵת עֶרְוַת אֲבִיהֶם וּפְנֵיהֶם וְגוֹ׳״, ״וַיִּיקֶץ נֹחַ מִיֵּינוֹ, וַיֵּדַע אֵת אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה לוֹ בְּנוֹ הַקָּטָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **A visitor from the Galilee expounded: The** conversive ***vav* is stated thirteen** times **in** the passage concerning **wine,** as it is stated: **"And Noah began [*vayyaḥel*] to be a farmer, and he planted [*vayyita*] a vineyard, and he drank [*vayyesht*] of the wine, and was drunk [*vayyishkar*]; and he was uncovered [*vayyitgal*] within his tent. And Ham, the father of Canaan, saw [*vayyar*] the nakedness of his father, and told [*vayyagged*] his two brothers outside. And Shem and Japheth took [*vayyikaḥ*] the garment, and laid it [*vayyasimu*] upon both their shoulders, and went [*vayyelekhu*] backward, and covered [*vaykhassu*] the nakedness of their father; and their faces** were backward, and they did not see their father's nakedness. **And Noah awoke [*vayyiketz*] from his wine, and knew [*vayyeda*] what his younger son had done to him"** (Genesis 9:20–24). All thirteen instances of the conversive *vav* here are followed by the letter *yod*. Together they form the word *vay*, meaning woe, and allude to the suffering and misfortune caused by uncontrolled drinking.

The Galilean preacher known as *Over Galila* made a *derashah*: *yud gimmel vavim ne'emru be-yayin* — thirteen *vavim* are stated in connection with wine in the narrative of Noach. Each *vav* represents *vai* — woe. The thirteen verses run in sequence: *vayachel Noach ish ha-adamah* (1), *vayita karem* (2), *vayesht min ha-yayin* (3), *vayishkar* (4), *vayitgal be-tokh ohalo* (5), *vayar Cham* (6), *vayaged li-shnei echav ba-chutz* (7), *vayikach Shem va-Yefet et ha-simlah* (8), *vayasimu al shekhem shneihem* (9), *vayeilekhu achoranit* (10), *va-yekhasu et ervat avihem* (11), *u-fneihem achoranit* (12), *vayikatz Noach mi-yeino* (13). One thing led inexorably to the next: the pattern of descent from the first act of planting the vineyard to the final exposure and violation was unbroken. This chain is the clearest illustration in the *Torah* of the dynamic the Gemara has been describing — the seductive quality of wine drawing a person further and further down a path from which there is no easy return.

### Noah's Violation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר סֵרְסוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר רְבָעוֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר סֵרְסוֹ: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקִּלְקְלוֹ בָּרְבִיעִי, קִלְּלוֹ בָּרְבִיעִי. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר רְבָעוֹ: גָּמַר ״וַיַּרְא״ ״וַיַּרְא״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיַּרְא חָם אֲבִי כְנַעַן אֵת עֶרְוַת אָבִיו״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַיַּרְא אוֹתָהּ שְׁכֶם בֶּן חֲמוֹר וְגוֹ׳״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר סֵרְסוֹ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קִלְּלוֹ בָּרְבִיעִי. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רְבָעוֹ, מַאי שְׁנָא רְבִיעִי? נִלְטְיֵיהּ בְּהֶדְיָא! הָא וְהָא הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Having cited the passage discussing Noah, the Gemara enters into a discussion about what was actually done to him by his younger son, Ham. **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed: **One says** that Ham **castrated** Noah **and one says** that Ham **sodomized him.** The Gemara explains: **The one who says** that Ham **castrated** Noah adduces the following proof: **Since he injured** Noah **with** respect to the possibility of conceiving **a fourth** son, which Noah wanted but could no longer have, therefore Noah **cursed him by means of** Ham's **fourth son.** Ham's sons were Cush, Mizraim, Put, and Canaan (see Genesis 10:6), and of all of these, it was Canaan whom Noah cursed (see Genesis 9:25–28). **And the one who says** that Ham **sodomized** Noah **learned** this from a verbal analogy between the words **"and he saw"** and **"and he saw." Here it is written: "And Ham, the father of Canaan, saw the nakedness of his father"; and there it is written: "And Shechem, son of Hamor** the Hivite, prince of the country, **saw her,** and he took her, and lay with her, and afflicted her" (Genesis 34:2). This indicates that the term "saw" alludes to sexual intercourse. The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that Ham **castrated** Noah, it is **due to that** reason that Noah **cursed** Ham **by means of** Ham's **fourth** son. **But according to the one who says** that Ham **sodomized him, what is different** about his **fourth** son? **He should have cursed** Ham **directly.** The Gemara answers: This Sage holds that both **this** offense **and that** offense **were** committed. All agree that Ham castrated Noah, and some say that Ham also sodomized him.

The Gemara then addresses what precisely Cham did to Noach. Rav and Shmuel dispute: one says he castrated him, the other says he sodomized him. The support for castration is the fact that Noach cursed Cham's fourth son Canaan — *arur Kena'an* — because Cham prevented him from having a fourth son by castrating him: *mitokh she-kilkelo ba-revi'i killelo ba-revi'i*. The support for the other view is the *gezeirah shavah* between *vayar Cham* here and *vayar otah Shekhem ben Chamor* by the violation of Dinah — the same language of seeing implies the same act. The Gemara resolves that perhaps both occurred: *chada ke-ha ve-chada ke-ha* — one as well as the other. The castration would explain why Noach cursed the fourth son, and the violation is derived from the linguistic parallel.

### Adam Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּחֶל נֹחַ אִישׁ הָאֲדָמָה וַיִּטַּע כָּרֶם״. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מָר עוּקְבָא אָמַר רַבִּי זַכַּאי: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְנֹחַ: נֹחַ, לֹא הָיָה לְךָ לִלְמוֹד מֵאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלֹּא גָּרַם לוֹ אֶלָּא יַיִן? כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אוֹתוֹ אִילָן שֶׁאָכַל מִמֶּנּוּ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן – גֶּפֶן הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the passage relating to Noah. The verse states: **"And Noah began to be a farmer, and he planted a vineyard."** In explanation of this matter, **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Ukva says, and some say** that **Mar Ukva says** that **Rabbi Zakkai says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Noah: Noah, shouldn't you have learned from Adam the first** man, **whose** banishment from the Garden of Eden **was caused only by wine?** The Gemara notes: This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **the tree from which Adam the first** man **ate was a grapevine.**

The Gemara then reports a remarkable statement: Rav Chisda said in the name of Rav Ukva, and some say Mar Ukva said in the name of Rav Zakai, that the Holy One Blessed be He said to Noach: *lo hayah lekha lilmod me-Adam ha-Rishon* — did you not have from whom to learn? Could you not have learned from Adam the First? The parallel is powerful. Just as Noach emerged to begin civilization anew upon a devastated earth, so Adam had emerged into a pristine world. And just as Adam's first sin brought ruin, Noach's first act of planting a vineyard brought ruin upon himself and his family. God's rebuke is: you had the precedent before you; you knew that wine — the fruit of the vine — was precisely what had led the first man astray.

### Fruit Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, אוֹתוֹ אִילָן שֶׁאָכַל אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן מִמֶּנּוּ – גֶּפֶן הָיָה, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁמֵּבִיא יְלָלָה לְאָדָם אֶלָּא יַיִן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חִטָּה הָיָה, שֶׁאֵין הַתִּינוֹק יוֹדֵעַ לִקְרוֹא אַבָּא וְאִימָּא עַד שֶׁיִּטְעוֹם טַעַם דָּגָן. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: תְּאֵנָה הָיָה, שֶׁבַּדָּבָר שֶׁקִּלְקְלוּ בּוֹ נִתְקְנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתְפְּרוּ עֲלֵה תְאֵנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir says: The tree from which Adam the first** man **ate was a grapevine,** **as,** even today, **nothing except wine brings wailing** and trouble **upon a person;** most sins are caused by drunkenness. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The Tree of Knowledge **was** the **wheat** plant. This is proven by the fact **that,** even today, **an infant does not know how to call out** to his **father or mother until he tastes the taste of grain,** and for this reason wheat is called "the Tree of Knowledge." **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** The Tree of Knowledge **was a fig** tree, **because** it was **with the matter with which they sinned** that **they were rehabilitated, as it is stated: "And they sewed together fig leaves,** and made for themselves loincloths" (Genesis 3:7).

This brings the Gemara to a three-way *machloket* among the *Tanna'im* regarding what the forbidden fruit of the *etz ha-da'at* actually was.

Rabbi Meir holds it was the grapevine: *she-ein lekha davar she-mevi yelala le-adam ella yayin* — there is nothing that brings woe upon a person like wine. The connection to our entire discussion is obvious, and the *derashah* of God's rebuke to Noach assumes this view.

Rabbi Yehudah holds it was wheat: *she-ein ha-tinok yode'a likro aba ve-ima ad she-yit'om ta'am dagan* — a child does not know how to say father and mother until it has tasted grain. The *etz ha-da'at* is an *etz* of knowledge and intellect; wheat, which develops a child's capacity for speech and thought, is the substance that corresponds to *da'at tov ve-ra*. Furthermore, the verse describes the tree as desirable for eating and beautiful to the eyes — a description that suits a stalk of wheat.

Rabbi Nechemya holds it was a fig tree: *she-ba-davar she-kilkelu vo nitkanu* — the very thing through which they sinned became the thing that repaired them, for after eating they sewed together fig leaves to cover themselves (*vayitperu aleh te'enah*). Rashi reads this as *midah ke-neged midah* in the mode of divine providence: the same instrument God uses to wound, He uses to heal.

Tosafot, however, does not accept Rashi's formulation and offers a different explanation. When Adam and Chavah sinned, they sought shelter among all the trees of the garden, but every tree refused to receive them — *berukh zeh ha-ganav she-ganav da'atei de-Vara'ei*, blessed is this thief who tried to steal the mind of his Creator — each tree distancing itself from association with the sin. Only the fig tree, which had itself been the instrument of the sin, opened its doors to them in a kind of shared complicity: *al yedei she-akhlah mimenu patkha lo delaseiha ve-kibblah oto*. It was not divine *midah ke-neged midah* from above, but the natural consequence of the fig tree's own involvement.

## Bathsheba's Teaching

### Solomon's Youth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דִּבְרֵי לְמוּאֵל מֶלֶךְ מַשָּׂא אֲשֶׁר יִסְּרַתּוּ אִמּוֹ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכְּפָאַתּוּ אִמּוֹ עַל הָעַמּוּד וְאָמְרָה לוֹ, ״מַה בְּרִי וּמַה בַּר בִּטְנִי וּמֶה בַּר נְדָרָי״. ״מַה בְּרִי״ – הַכֹּל יוֹדְעִים שֶׁאָבִיךָ יְרֵא שָׁמַיִם הֲוָה, עַכְשָׁיו יֹאמְרוּ אִמּוֹ גָּרְמָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of wine. Referring to the verse that states: **"The words of King Lemuel, the burden with which his mother corrected him"** (Proverbs 31:1), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** This **teaches that** when Solomon's **mother** Bathsheba saw Solomon engaged in excessive drinking, she **bound him to a pillar** to have him flogged. **And she said to him: "What, my son? And what, son of my womb? And what, son of my vows?"** (Proverbs 31:2). She meant: **"What, my son?" Everyone knows that your father,** David, **was a God-fearing** man, and **now,** when they see you sin, **they will** all **say** that **his mother caused him** to drink, i.e., that you engage in these behaviors because you are my son.

The Gemara now reads the opening of *Mishlei* 31 as a record of Bat Sheva's *mussar* to the young Shlomo. The passage begins: *divrei Lemuel melech, massa asher yisartu imo* — the words of Lemuel the king, a moral teaching with which his mother chastised him. Rashi explains *massa* here as a *parashat tochechah*, a section of remonstrance. Rav Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: the verse teaches that his mother bound him to a post (*kafato imo al ha-'amud*) and subjected him to physical discipline.

The scenario implied is that Shlomo, in his youth — before assuming the full weight of kingship — had been consorting with other kings and princes and falling into their habits of drinking wine. His mother took it upon herself, in the most direct manner, to correct this.

### David's Piety

The verse *mah bri u-mah bar bitni u-mah bar nedari* — what, my son, and what, the son of my womb, and what, the son of my vow — is read as three separate complaints. The first, *mah bri*, is interpreted: everybody knows that your father Dovid was God-fearing. If you conduct yourself this way, people will say *imo garemah lo* — it is his mother's fault.

### Conception Sacrifice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמַה בַּר בִּטְנִי״ – כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים שֶׁל בֵּית אָבִיךָ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁמִּתְעַבְּרוֹת, שׁוּב אֵינָן רוֹאוֹת פְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ. וַאֲנִי דָּחַקְתִּי וְנִכְנַסְתִּי, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא לִי בֵּן מְזוֹרָז וּמְלוּבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **"And what, son of my womb?"** That is to say: With regard to **all of the women of your father's house, once they conceive they no longer see the face of the king, but I pushed** myself in **and entered** the king's chamber while I was pregnant, **so that I might have a son who is strong and fair-skinned.** There are times during a woman's pregnancy when intercourse is beneficial for the development of the fetus. Bathsheba was telling Solomon: I did my utmost to ensure that you have extra strength and beauty, and now you use that strength and appeal to pursue drink.

The second complaint, *mah bar bitni*, is more intimate. All the other wives of your father's household, once they became pregnant, were no longer permitted to enter the king's presence. But I (*ve-ani dechakti ve-nikhnasti*) forced my way in, insisting on continuing marital relations even while pregnant, in order to produce a son who would be *mezuraz u-meluban* — vigorous and pure. The *midrashic* tradition held that marital relations during pregnancy strengthened the child. Bat Sheva made a personal sacrifice of propriety and comfort in order to produce the most capable possible son. And now, she implies, that very vigor is being squandered on wine.

### Mother's Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמָה בַּר נְדָרָי״ – כָּל נָשִׁים שֶׁל בֵּית אָבִיךָ הָיוּ נוֹדְרוֹת: ״יְהֵא לִי בֵּן הָגוּן לַמַּלְכוּת״, וַאֲנִי נָדַרְתִּי וְאָמַרְתִּי: ״יְהֵא לִי בֵּן זָרִיז וּמְמוּלָּא בַּתּוֹרָה וְהָגוּן לִנְבִיאוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And what, son of my vows?"** That is to say: With regard to **all of** the **women of your father's house, they would take vows** while they were pregnant, saying: **Let me have a son who is fit to be king. But I,** by contrast, **took a vow and said: Let me have a son who is diligent and filled with** knowledge of **the Torah and fit for prophecy.**

The third complaint, *mah bar nedari*, is addressed to the divergence between Bat Sheva's hopes and Shlomo's behavior. All the other wives of the king made vows that they might produce a son fit for kingship. But Bat Sheva's vow was different: *yehei li ben zariz u-mimalei ba-Torah ve-hagun li-nevuot* — let me have a son who is swift, filled with Torah, and worthy of prophecy. She did not merely want a king; she wanted a king who was a prophet and a *talmid chakham*. To see such a son pursuing wine was to see her deepest aspirations betrayed.

### Kings' Folly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל לַמְלָכִים לְמוֹאֵל, אַל לַמְלָכִים שְׁתוֹ יָיִן״. ״אַל לַמְלָכִים״ – אָמְרָה לוֹ: מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל מְלָכִים שֶׁשּׁוֹתִים יַיִן וּמִשְׁתַּכְּרִים וְאוֹמְרִים לָמָּה לָנוּ אֵל? ״וּלְרוֹזְנִים אֵי שֵׁכָר״ – מִי שֶׁכׇּל רָזֵי עוֹלָם גְּלוּיִים לוֹ יִשְׁתֶּה יַיִן וְיִשְׁתַּכֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** It is further stated there: **"It is not for kings, O Lemoel, it is not for kings to drink wine;** nor for princes to say: Where is strong drink?" (Proverbs 31:4). The Gemara provides an explanation of the meaning of each part of this verse. **"It is not for kings":** Bathsheba **said to** her son Solomon: **What have you to do with kings who drink wine and become intoxicated and say: Why [*lamma*] do we need God [*El*]?** The Gemara continues to explain the verse. **"Nor for princes [*rozenim*] to say: Where is strong drink?"** This means that **one** like Solomon, **to whom all the secrets [*razei*] of the world are revealed, should he drink wine and become intoxicated?**

The verse continues: *al la-melakhim Lemuel, al la-melakhim sheto yayin* — not to kings, Lemuel, not to kings to drink wine. The Gemara reads *Lemuel* as Shlomo himself and understands the verse as Bat Sheva's direct address: why are you spending your time with those kings who drink wine and get drunk and say *lama lanu El* — what do we need God for? The name *Lemuel* is broken down as a hint: he who has God before him (*le-El*) should not behave as those kings behave. Similarly, *ve-roznim iy shekhar* — those to whom all the secrets of the world are revealed (*mi she-kol razei olam galuyim lo*) — should they drink wine and become drunk? The one who will be given divine wisdom has no business imitating the dissipation of ordinary kings.

### Solomon's Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִין שֶׁחָזַר שְׁלֹמֹה וְהוֹדָה לְאִמּוֹ? דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בַעַר אָנֹכִי מֵאִישׁ וְלֹא בִינַת אָדָם לִי״. ״כִּי בַעַר אָנֹכִי מֵאִישׁ״ – מִנֹּחַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּחֶל נֹחַ אִישׁ הָאֲדָמָה״. ״וְלֹא בִינַת אָדָם לִי״ – זֶה אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** can it be learned **that Solomon repented and admitted to his mother** that she was justified in her rebukes? **As it is written: "For I am more foolish than a man, and have not the understanding of a man"** (Proverbs 30:2). This should be understood as follows: **"For I am more foolish than a man [*ish*]";** that is, I am more foolish than Noah, who sinned with wine and is called "a man," **as it is written: "And Noah began to be a farmer [*ish ha'adama***]" (Genesis 9:20). **"And have not the understanding of a man [*adam*]"; this** is a reference to **Adam the first** man, who also sinned with wine, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who says that the Tree of Knowledge was a grapevine.

Rabbi Yitzchak then asks: where do we see that Shlomo ultimately accepted his mother's rebuke? From the verse *ki va'ar anochi me-ish ve-lo binat adam li* — I am more brutish than any man and I have not the understanding of men. The Gemara reads *me-ish* as *mi-Noach* — I was as foolish as Noach — and *binat adam li* as referring to *Adam ha-Rishon* — I lacked the wisdom of Adam the First. Shlomo confesses that he repeated the very mistakes of Noach and Adam, following wine into self-destruction. His mother's words reached him, his youth of dissipation ended, and he became *Shlomo ha-Melech* — the wisest of all men.

## Exemptions

### Mitzvah Company

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל בַּחֲבוּרַת מִצְוָה. אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל בַּחֲבוּרָה שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ סְרִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the son **ate** these items **with a group** assembled for the performance **of a mitzva,** he does not become liable as a stubborn and rebellious son. **Rabbi Abbahu says: He is not liable unless he eats with a group that is entirely** made up of **idlers.** This seems to indicate that if he eats and drinks in the company of decent people, even if he consumes the required amounts that would otherwise make him liable, he does not become a stubborn and rebellious son.

The *mishna* had stated that eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* does not create liability for *ben sorer u-moreh*. Rav Abbahu now specifies a condition for the non-*mitzvah* case: *eino chayav ad she-yokhal be-chavura she-kulah serikin* — he is not liable unless he ate in a gathering that is entirely composed of worthless people. This implies that if even one respectable person is present, there is no liability.

### Mitzvah Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: אָכַל בַּחֲבוּרַת מִצְוָה – אֵינוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה. טַעְמָא דְּמִצְוָה, הָא לָאו מִצְוָה – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָאו כּוּלָּהּ סְרִיקִין? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּכוּלָּהּ סְרִיקִין, כֵּיוָן דִּבְמִצְוָה קָא עָסֵיק – לָא מִמְּשִׁיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: If the son **ate** and drank the requisite amounts of meat and wine **with a group** assembled for the performance **of a mitzva he does not become** liable as **a stubborn and rebellious son?** A precise reading of the mishna indicates that **the reason** that he does not become liable is that he ate and drank with a group assembled for the performance **of a mitzva. But** if they were **not** assembled for the performance of **a mitzva** he would be liable **even if** the group **is not entirely** made up of **idlers.** The Gemara answers: There is no contradiction between the mishna and the statement of Rabbi Abbahu. In fact, the son is liable only if he eats with a group of whom all are idlers. And the mishna **teaches us this: That even if** the group **is entirely** composed of **idlers, since they are occupied with a mitzva,** there is **no** concern that he **will be drawn** to sin.

The Gemara challenges this from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* states that eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* exempts him. At such a gathering there are presumably decent people present — yet the *mishna* still exempts him. If the reason for exemption in the case of a fully-*sarikin* gathering is the presence of worthless company, then the reason in the *chavurat mitzvah* case must be something else — namely, the *mitzvah* nature of the gathering itself, not the quality of the attendees.

The Gemara resolves: *ta'ama de-mitzvah* — the reason for the *mishna*'s exemption is purely the *mitzvah* context, and this applies regardless of who is present. If a boy ate at a *chavurat mitzvah* even among entirely worthless people, he would still be exempt because *keivan de-be-mitzvah ka-asik lo mimshakh* — since the occasion is a *mitzvah*, the fear of compulsive addictive behavior developing from this particular meal is absent. The exemption for a gathering of *serikin* without a *mitzvah* context requires that the company all be worthless; in the *mitzvah* context the company is irrelevant.

### Ibur Chodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל בְּעִיבּוּר הַחֹדֶשׁ. לְמֵימְרָא דְּבָשָׂר וְיַיִן מַסְּקוּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ אֶלָּא בְּפַת דָּגָן וְקִטְנִית בִּלְבַד! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: אַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ אֶלָּא בְּפַת וְקִטְנִית, וְאִיהוּ אַסֵּיק בָּשָׂר וְיַיִן וַאֲכַל, כֵּיוָן דִּבְמִצְוָה קָא עָסֵיק – לָא מִמְּשִׁיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the son **ate** and drank the requisite amounts of meat and wine **at** a meal celebrating **the intercalation of the month** he does not become liable as a stubborn and rebellious son. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that meat and wine are brought up** to the upper chamber where the month is intercalated? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **They ascend** to intercalate the month **only with** a meal consisting of **bread** made of **grain and legumes?** The Gemara answers: The mishna **teaches us this: Even though they** ordinarily **ascend to** the upper chamber **only with bread and legumes, and he brought up meat and wine and ate** them, **since they are occupied with a mitzva,** there is **no** concern that he **will be drawn** to sin.

The *mishna* also exempts eating at the gathering for *ibbur ha-chodesh* — the gathering at which the court would announce that the preceding month was a full thirty-day month. The Gemara observes that this gathering apparently involved eating meat and drinking wine (*u-meimra de-vasar ve-yayin maski*). But a *baraita* states that the participants at such a gathering ate only bread and legumes (*ein olim lah ella be-pat dagan u-kitnit bilvad*). The resolution: even if everyone else was eating only bread and legumes and the boy brought his own meat and wine and ate those separately, since *keivan de-be-mitzvah ka-asik lo mimshakh* — the *mitzvah* context insulates even his separate indulgence from the *din* of *ben sorer u-moreh*.

### Night/Day Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין עוֹלִין בְּעִיבּוּר הַחֹדֶשׁ פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ אֶלָּא בְּפַת דָּגָן וְקִטְנִית, וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ אֶלָּא לְאוֹר עִיבּוּרוֹ, וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ בַּיּוֹם אֶלָּא בַּלַּיְלָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין עוֹלִין לָהּ בַּלַּיְלָה אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם! כְּדַאֲמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לִבְנֵיהּ: אַחְרִיפוּ וְעוּלוּ, אַחְרִיפוּ וּפוּקוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׁמְעוּ בְּכוּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **No fewer than ten men ascend** to the upper chamber **for the intercalation of the month; and they ascend** to intercalate the month **only with bread** made of **grain and legumes; and they ascend only on the night of** the month's **intercalation; and they ascend not by day, but only at night.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **They ascend not at night, but only by day?** The Gemara explains: **As Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to his sons:** When you come to intercalate the month, **ascend early and leave early so that people should hear your** comings and goings, and thereby know that you have been addressing this matter. The proper time for this is at daybreak, between night and day.

A final *baraita* teaches the parameters of the *ibbur ha-chodesh* gathering: it requires a minimum of ten people, it is conducted with bread and legumes only, and — *ein olim lah ella le-or iburo* — it takes place at nightfall leading into the new month. The verse supports a nighttime gathering. However, a contradictory *baraita* states that it is conducted by day and not by night. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's instruction to his sons — *achifu ve-ulu, achifu u-feku* (go early and arrive, go early and depart) — is cited as evidence of a daytime schedule, implying that they would gather during the day while beginning celebrations the night before. The resolution appears to be that the primary gathering occurred during the day, with the celebration commencing the night prior.

Under any interpretation, the *mitzvah* context of the *ibbur ha-chodesh* gathering suffices to exempt a boy who eats meat and wine there from becoming a *ben sorer u-moreh*.
