# Sanhedrin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Age of Puberty Proofs

### Tanakh Fertility Age

The sugya of *ben sorer u-moreh* raises the question of when, precisely, a boy first becomes capable of siring children. The Gemara has already established that the relevant window begins at thirteen, with the appearance of two pubic hairs, and closes three months later — a limit derived from the principle that once a boy can impregnate someone, the pregnancy would become visible within that period, at which point he could no longer be called *ben* without the connotation of fatherhood. The underlying theoretical framework is that he is *nidon al shem sofo* — judged on account of what he will become — a glutton and drunkard who will turn to robbery and even murder to feed his habit. The Rambam, it should be noted, shifts the emphasis away from the individual's anticipated trajectory and toward the verse *ve-khol Yisrael yishme'u ve-yira'u*: the execution serves to protect the institutions of communal authority — divine, judicial, and parental — just as it does in the cases of the *zaken mamreh*, the false prophet, and *edim zomemim*.

At this juncture, however, the Gemara introduces a striking historical claim: in the generations of the *Tanakh*, boys were capable of fathering children at the age of eight. This is not merely an antiquarian curiosity but bears directly on the *halakhic* debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding what age constitutes a legally significant act of intercourse. The Gemara now undertakes to prove, through a series of genealogical calculations, that the men of earlier biblical generations were indeed capable of procreation at eight.

### Shlomo Genealogy

The first proof proceeds from the relationship between Shlomo, Bat Sheva, Eliam, and Achitophel. The Gemara cites the verse identifying Bat Sheva as the daughter of Eliam (*halo zot Bat Sheva bat Eliam eshet Uriah ha-Chiti*), and cross-references this with the identification of Eliam as the son of Achitophel the Gilonite. It then notes the verse recording Shlomo's birth (*vayishlach be-yad Natan ha-navi vayikra shemo Yedidyah*) and proceeds to reconstruct the timeline from Shlomo's birth to Achitophel's death.

Rashi observes that Shlomo is Bat Sheva's second son — the first having died — and the chain of verses that follows tracks the years of Avshalom's movements after Shlomo's birth.

### Absalom Timeline

The calculation unfolds through a sequence of *pesukim*. After Shlomo's birth, two years pass (*vayehi li-shnatayim yamim*) before the sheep-shearing incident involving Avshalom. Avshalom then flees to Geshur and remains there for three years (*vayehi sham shalosh shanim*), bringing the total to five years since Shlomo's birth. He then returns and dwells in Jerusalem for two more years (*vayeishev Avshalom bi-Yerushalayim shnatayim yamim*), for a cumulative total of seven years.

It is at this point — seven years after Shlomo's birth — that Achitophel, seeing that his counsel has not been followed, returns home and takes his own life (*vayachvosh et ha-chamor vayeilech el beito vayetzav el beito vayichanek*). The verse *anshei damim u-mirma lo yechetzu yemeihem* establishes that the wicked die before half their years, with the standard lifespan reckoned as seventy. A *baraita* confirms: Doeg's total years were thirty-four; Achitophel's were thirty-three.

### Eight-Year Calculation

Achitophel died at thirty-three, seven years after Shlomo was born. He was therefore twenty-six years old when Shlomo was born. Shlomo, however, was Achitophel's great-grandson: Achitophel fathered Eliam, Eliam fathered Bat Sheva, and Bat Sheva gave birth to Shlomo. Three generations of begetting thus had to occur within those twenty-six years.

Accounting for the time of the three pregnancies — rounding to approximately two years total for three gestations, assuming seven-month pregnancies where possible — the Gemara subtracts two years from twenty-six, leaving twenty-four years to be divided across three generations. The arithmetic yields eight years per generation: *ishtakach de-khol chad ve-chad be-tamnei olid* — each one fathered a child at age eight.

### Age Nine Alternative

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: *dilma tarvaihu be-tisha olidu* — perhaps the two men in this chain (Achitophel and Eliam) each fathered a child at nine rather than eight. This would be marginally more comfortable, since *halakha* already recognizes nine as the age at which intercourse becomes legally significant, even if we no longer hold that boys can father children at that age. The response is that if the men were nine, then Bat Sheva would have had to give birth at six, which is implausible. But the Gemara notes that *ishta barya* — a woman is constitutionally more vigorous — and moreover, Shlomo was not Bat Sheva's first child, which would push her age at first conception even lower, to seven. The chain therefore cannot be used to establish the minimum age of paternity for the men, since the variable of Bat Sheva's age cannot be fixed with sufficient precision.

### Abraham Genealogy

The Gemara therefore turns to a second proof, drawn from the family of Avraham. The verse records that Terach fathered Avram, Nachor, and Haran (*Terach holid et Avram et Nachor ve-et Haran*). A tradition holds that Avram was one year older than Nachor and Nachor one year older than Haran, making Avraham two years older than Haran.

The verse then records that Avram and Nachor took wives — Avram taking Sarai, and Nachor taking Milkah the daughter of Haran, *avi Milkah va-avi Yiskah*. Rabbi Yitzchak identifies Yiskah as Sarai: she was called Yiskah (*sokhah*) because she perceived with *ruach ha-kodesh* — as the verse confirms: *kol asher tomar elecha Sarah shema be-kolah* — or, alternatively, because all gazed upon her beauty. Thus Avraham married his niece, the daughter of his younger brother Haran.

A subsequent verse establishes that Avraham was one hundred years old when he laughed at the prophecy of a son, and that Sarah was ninety (*ha-le-ven me'ah shanah yivaled ve-im Sarah ha-bat tishim shanah teled*) — a difference of ten years. Since Avraham was two years older than Haran, and ten years older than Sarah, it follows that Sarah was born when Haran was eight years old: Avraham was ten when Sarah was born, and Avraham was two years older than Haran, so Haran was eight at Sarah's birth. *Mishtakach ki oladah Haran le-Sarah be-tamnei olidah* — Haran fathered Sarah at eight.

### Birth Order Debate

The Gemara immediately challenges this proof: *dilma Avraham zuta de-akhva hava* — perhaps Avraham was not listed first because he was the oldest, but rather because the Torah lists children in order of wisdom or importance rather than birth order. If Avraham was actually the youngest of the three brothers, the age calculation collapses.

The Gemara supports this possibility from the sons of Noach. The verse lists them as Shem, Cham, and Yefet (*vayoled Noach et Shem et Cham ve-et Yafet*), which would imply Shem is the eldest. But a subsequent verse states that Shem was one hundred years old when he fathered Arpachshad two years after the flood (*Shem ben me'ah shanah vayoled et Arpachshad shnatayim achar ha-mabul*). Since the flood began when Noach was six hundred years old and Noach's first child was born when he was five hundred, Yefet should have been ninety-eight at the flood — yet Shem is said to be one hundred two years after the flood, making him ninety-eight at its onset, not older. The conclusion is that the verse *eleh toldot Shem: Shem ben me'ah shanah* means Shem was actually the youngest, listed first only because of his importance. It is further confirmed by the verse *achi Yafet ha-gadol* — Yefet was the eldest. *Derech chochmatan kachshiv lehu* — the Torah orders by wisdom, not birth.

Rav Kahana reports that he raised this entire discussion before Rav Zvid of Neharda'a, who responded that he did not rely on the proofs from Avraham's genealogy or from Shlomo and Bat Sheva. Rather, the demonstration comes from Betzalel.

### Betzalel Mishkan

The third proof begins with the verse *u-Vetzalel ben Uri ben Chur le-mateh Yehudah*, identifying Betzalel as the craftsman of the *mishkan*. A related verse identifies Chur as the son of Kalev and Efrat (*vayikach lo Kalev et Efrat va-teled lo et Chur*), and we proceed on the assumption that this is the same Chur.

The Gemara establishes that Betzalel must have been at least thirteen when he worked on the *mishkan*, derived from the verse *ish ish mi-melakhto asher hemah osim* — *ish* implies a full-grown man, i.e., a *bar mitzvah* of thirteen. A *baraita* records that in the first year after the Exodus the *mishkan* was constructed, and in the second year it was erected and the spies were sent.

### Caleb Timeline

The calculation proceeds as follows. Kalev declares at the time of the conquest of Canaan: *ben arba'im shanah anochi bi-shloach Moshe eved Hashem oti* — I was forty years old when Moshe sent me as a spy (*ve-ata hinei anochi ha-yom ben chamesh u-shmonim shanah*). He was eighty-five at the time of the declaration; the spies were sent in the second year of the Exodus. The *mishkan* was built in the first year. Therefore, when the *mishkan* was being built, Kalev was thirty-nine years old.

Subtracting Betzalel's age of thirteen yields twenty-six: Kalev was twenty-six when Betzalel was born. Now, between Kalev and Betzalel stand two generations: Kalev fathered Chur, Chur fathered Uri, and Uri fathered Betzalel. Accounting for approximately two years of pregnancies across three gestations, and dividing the remaining twenty-four years by three, each generation must have fathered a child at eight. *Ishtakach de-olid be-tamniya olid* — it is established that they gave birth at eight.

It is noted that the calculation is, if anything, more compelling given that Kalev had already had children by a previous wife before this chain, which would only tighten the arithmetic further.

### Tosafot Age Shift

This entire discussion occasions a remarkable comment by Tosafot on the previous *daf* (69a). The *mishna* had stated that if a minor *ger* dies it is known he has no heirs, since he could not have fathered children. But if, as the Gemara has now demonstrated, boys in earlier generations could father children at eight — and if *bar mitzvah* was always thirteen — then a boy who died as a minor at, say, twelve-and-a-half could theoretically have had children in the earlier period. Why then would we assume he had no heirs?

Tosafot answers: *de-af al pi she-be-dorot ha-acharonim nasnu chachamim shiur be-vas yud beis shanah ve-yom echad u-ve-katan ben yud gimmel shanah ve-yom echad* — even though in later generations the Sages fixed the age of majority at twelve for a girl and thirteen for a boy with two pubic hairs, in the earlier generations when puberty arrived much younger (*she-hayu memaharim lehavi sa'arot*), the *zeman gadlut* — the legal threshold of adulthood — was correspondingly earlier. The *halakhic* definition of adulthood was not a fixed number but tracked the biological reality of puberty in each era.

This is a striking position. The suggestion that the *halakhic* age of majority itself changed with the historical shifting of the age of puberty — and that this is not a rabbinic innovation but a direct consequence of the Gemara's own analysis — is the kind of observation that might seem heretical if stated carelessly. Yet here is Tosafot stating it plainly, and deriving it directly from the *gemara* we have just learned. Tosafot goes on to address the apparent tension with Betzalel's requirement of thirteen, working out the resolution, though we need not enter into every detail of that answer here.

## Daughter Exclusion

### Shimon's Logic

The *mishna* specifies *ben* — a son — to the exclusion of a daughter. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Shimon argued that by rights a daughter should be subject to the law of *ben sorer u-moreh* just as a son is. His reasoning: *she-ha-kol metzuyin etzelah ba-aveirah* — a wayward daughter is even more likely to draw others into sin, making herself sexually available to feed whatever habit she has developed. This danger to society is at least as grave as the concern that a son, addicted to gluttony and drunkenness, will turn to robbery and murder.

### Gezeirat HaKatuv

The response to Rabbi Shimon is categorical: *ella gezeirat ha-katuv hi ben ve-lo bat* — this is a *gezeirat ha-katuv*, a scriptural decree, limiting the law to sons only. As the Gemara has ruled in several prior contexts within this sugya, the verse says *ben* and the analysis ends there, regardless of how logically compelling the argument for extension might be.

## Gluttony Definition

### Measure Requirements

The *mishna* on *Sanhedrin* 70a turns to the question of when the conduct of the *ben sorer u-moreh* triggers liability. The operative verse characterizes him as *zolel ve-sovei* — a glutton and a drunkard. The *mishna* defines this quantitatively: *mi-she-yokhal tartimar basar ve-yishteh chatzi log yayin* — he must have eaten a *tartimar* of meat and drunk half a *log* of wine. Rabbi Yosi disputes and doubles both amounts: a *maneh* of meat and a *log* of wine.

### Mitzvah Gatherings

Eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* — a gathering convened for a religious purpose, such as a wedding feast — does not create liability, even if the boy eats the requisite amounts there. Similarly exempt are those who eat *ma'aser sheni* in Jerusalem, since that too is a *mitzvah* context. Also listed is eating at the gathering for *ibbur ha-chodesh*, the intercalation of the month, to which we will return.

### Both Required

The *mishna* further specifies that eating every kind of food in great quantity, but without meat, or drinking every intoxicating beverage, but without wine, does not qualify. The verse *zolel ve-sovei* requires both: *ad she-yokhal basar ve-yishteh yayin*. The *mishna* acknowledges that there is no strict proof (*ein ra'ayah la-davar*) that *zolel* means specifically meat and *sovei* means specifically wine, but there is a *zeikher la-davar* — a textual resonance — in the verse *al tehi be-sov'ei yayin be-zolelei basar lamo*, which pairs the two. Rashi notes that this is perhaps simply one illustration of *zolel ve-sovei*, not a definitional limitation; nevertheless, the *halakha* requires both.

### Tartemar Amount

Rav Zeira confesses that he does not know independently what a *tartimar* is. His solution is to reason from context: since Rabbi Yosi doubles the *tanna kamma*'s amount for both meat and wine — a *log* versus half a *log* — and Rabbi Yosi specifies a *maneh* for meat, it follows by inference that the *tanna kamma*'s *tartimar* is half a *maneh*. *Nimtza tartimar chatzi maneh.*

### Cheap Purchase Rule

Rav Chana bar Molda said in the name of Rav Huna: *eino chayav ad she-yikach basar be-zol ve-yishteh yayin be-zol* — he is not liable unless he purchased the meat and wine cheaply. The concern underlying *ben sorer u-moreh* is not mere gluttony at a given moment but the fear of addictive behavior that will lead him toward a life of crime. Rashi makes this explicit: if a person only consumes very expensive wine and meat, *lo shakhia'h le-himashekh* — he is unlikely to be drawn after it compulsively, since he cannot sustain such expenditure. The word *bezol* also echoes the word *zolel*, yielding a pun that the Gemara notes (*dichsiv zolel ve-sovei*). The concern, then, is not the individual act of overindulgence but the pattern it presages.

### Dilution Standards

Ravina clarifies the nature of the wine and meat required. *Yayin chai mazug ve-lo mazug* — the wine must be diluted but not too diluted, retaining sufficient potency. *Basar chai bashil ve-lo bashil* — the meat must be cooked but not fully cooked, like the meat eaten hurriedly by thieves over coals. This intermediate state — *ke-vasar kiba de-akhli ganvei* — is itself evocative: it is the food of a man already on the path of crime, eating in haste.

### Seduction Theory

Rashi explains the underlying logic of both requirements. With respect to wine: *mamshikhah she-meshubakh hu* — the wine must be of a quality that draws a person after it. If the wine is either too raw and harsh or too diluted and weak, it will not create the compulsive pull that is the real concern. Similarly for meat: if it is fully and properly cooked, then the person who insists on such refined preparation will never adapt to the lifestyle of a thief, who eats on the run. The intermediate states — semi-cooked meat and wine diluted to a pleasant but potent degree — are precisely the conditions under which addictive behavior is most likely to take hold. *Lo mamshikhah yayin mazug* — over-diluted wine does not draw a person after it; *aval bashil shapir lo mamshikhah* — perfectly cooked meat does not either.

### Salted/Fresh Wine

Rabba and Rav Yosef both rule that if he ate salted meat (*basar maliach*) and drank wine directly from the pressing floor (*yayin mi-gitto*), he does not become a *ben sorer u-moreh*. These represent the opposite extremes: salted meat is preserved and lacks the freshness that makes meat genuinely seductive; wine straight from the press is essentially grape juice, barely fermented and not yet intoxicating in any meaningful way.

A *baraita* from the laws of *erev Tisha be-Av* is adduced in support: one may eat salted meat and drink newly pressed wine on *erev Tisha be-Av*, because these do not constitute the pleasurable indulgence that is prohibited on that day.

### Duration Limits

The Gemara then establishes the precise durations. Rabbi Chanina bar Kahana rules that meat is considered *basar maliach* — and thus no longer fresh enough to create concern — once it has sat in salt for as long as *shlamim* meat may be eaten: two days and an intervening night. Wine is considered *yayin mi-gitto* as long as it is still *toses* — still bubbling after pressing — which a *baraita* establishes as three days.

The Gemara then probes whether the same thresholds apply to *ben sorer u-moreh* as to *erev Tisha be-Av*. The answer is that the two laws rest on different principles. The prohibition on *erev Tisha be-Av* turns on *simcha* — joy — and even somewhat aged salted meat or slightly fermented wine still imparts a certain pleasure. *Ben sorer u-moreh*, by contrast, turns on *imshuchei* — the addictive pull. *U-ve-khol she-hu lo mimshakh* — even a brief period of salting suffices to eliminate the seductive quality for purposes of this law. Wine, by the same logic, may not create the relevant pull until it has fermented for forty days, by which point it is fully wine and no longer *yayin mi-gitto*.

## Wine Ethics

### Wine Purpose

Having treated the technical requirements for liability in *ben sorer u-moreh*, the Gemara opens an extended *aggadic* discussion on wine — its dangers, its proper uses, and its moral valence. Rabbi Chanan states: *lo nivra yayin ba-olam ella le-nachem avelim u-le-shalem sakhar la-resha'im* — wine was created in this world only to comfort mourners and to pay out reward to the wicked.

### Wicked Reward

Rashi explains the second purpose carefully. The statement is not ironic — not that the wicked will drown themselves in wine and thus seal their own destruction. Rather, Rashi reads it straightforwardly: wine brings pleasure (*she-mit'adnim bo ba-olam ha-zeh*), and for the wicked, this pleasure constitutes the *sakhar* — the reward for whatever *mitzvot* they performed — being cashed out in this world, leaving them with nothing for *Olam Ha-ba*. The verse cited is *tenu shekhar le-oved ve-yayin le-marei nafesh* — give strong drink to the one who is lost, and wine to the bitter of soul.

### Wine Color Warning

Rabbi Yitzchak interprets the verse *al tere yayin ki yitadam* — do not look at wine because it is ruddy. The *derashah* reads: do not be seduced by the ruddiness that wine brings to the faces of the wicked in this world (*she-ma'adim pneihem shel resha'im ba-olam ha-zeh*), for it will whiten their faces — shame them — in the world to come (*u-malbin pneihem le-Olam Ha-ba*). The lesson is that wine's apparent blessing to the wicked is in truth part of the mechanism by which their reward is exhausted in this life.

### Wine Blood

Rava offers a different reading of the same verse: *al tere yayin she-acharito dam* — do not run after wine, because its end is blood. The pursuit of wine leads to the shedding of blood — whether one becomes a *ben sorer u-moreh* and eventually turns to violence, or simply destroys oneself through dissipation.

### Head/Poor Pun

Rav Kahana notes a *ketiav u-keri* — a discrepancy between how a word is written and how it is read — in the word *tirosh*, the Torah's term for wine. Written without a *vav* it can be read *tirash*. *Zakha — na'aseh rosh*: if one merits, wine makes one a head, a leader. *Lo zakha — na'aseh rash*: if one does not merit, wine impoverishes. The same substance elevates or destroys depending on the character of the one who uses it.

### Joy/Desolation Pun

Rava finds a parallel *ketiav u-keri* in *yismach*, the word for joy in *yayin yismach levav enosh*. Written one way it could be read as *yishma*, evoking *shemamah* — desolation. *Zakha — mesamcho*: wine makes one joyous. *Lo zakha — meshammehu*: it makes one desolate. Again, the same reality, bifurcated by merit.

### Rava's Wisdom

Rava adds a personal testimony: *chamra ve-reichani pakhin* — wine and fragrances sharpened my intellect. Used in moderation and at appropriate moments, these sensory pleasures were not distractions but aids to the clarity of mind needed for *Torah* learning. This serves as the Gemara's clearest affirmation that the discussion is not about prohibition but about proper use.

### Noah's Thirteen Woes

The Galilean preacher known as *Over Galila* made a *derashah*: *yud gimmel vavim ne'emru be-yayin* — thirteen *vavim* are stated in connection with wine in the narrative of Noach. Each *vav* represents *vai* — woe. The thirteen verses run in sequence: *vayachel Noach ish ha-adamah* (1), *vayita karem* (2), *vayesht min ha-yayin* (3), *vayishkar* (4), *vayitgal be-tokh ohalo* (5), *vayar Cham* (6), *vayaged li-shnei echav ba-chutz* (7), *vayikach Shem va-Yefet et ha-simlah* (8), *vayasimu al shekhem shneihem* (9), *vayeilekhu achoranit* (10), *va-yekhasu et ervat avihem* (11), *u-fneihem achoranit* (12), *vayikatz Noach mi-yeino* (13). One thing led inexorably to the next: the pattern of descent from the first act of planting the vineyard to the final exposure and violation was unbroken. This chain is the clearest illustration in the *Torah* of the dynamic the Gemara has been describing — the seductive quality of wine drawing a person further and further down a path from which there is no easy return.

### Noah's Violation

The Gemara then addresses what precisely Cham did to Noach. Rav and Shmuel dispute: one says he castrated him, the other says he sodomized him. The support for castration is the fact that Noach cursed Cham's fourth son Canaan — *arur Kena'an* — because Cham prevented him from having a fourth son by castrating him: *mitokh she-kilkelo ba-revi'i killelo ba-revi'i*. The support for the other view is the *gezeirah shavah* between *vayar Cham* here and *vayar otah Shekhem ben Chamor* by the violation of Dinah — the same language of seeing implies the same act. The Gemara resolves that perhaps both occurred: *chada ke-ha ve-chada ke-ha* — one as well as the other. The castration would explain why Noach cursed the fourth son, and the violation is derived from the linguistic parallel.

### Adam Parallel

The Gemara then reports a remarkable statement: Rav Chisda said in the name of Rav Ukva, and some say Mar Ukva said in the name of Rav Zakai, that the Holy One Blessed be He said to Noach: *lo hayah lekha lilmod me-Adam ha-Rishon* — did you not have from whom to learn? Could you not have learned from Adam the First? The parallel is powerful. Just as Noach emerged to begin civilization anew upon a devastated earth, so Adam had emerged into a pristine world. And just as Adam's first sin brought ruin, Noach's first act of planting a vineyard brought ruin upon himself and his family. God's rebuke is: you had the precedent before you; you knew that wine — the fruit of the vine — was precisely what had led the first man astray.

### Fruit Identity

This brings the Gemara to a three-way *machloket* among the *Tanna'im* regarding what the forbidden fruit of the *etz ha-da'at* actually was.

Rabbi Meir holds it was the grapevine: *she-ein lekha davar she-mevi yelala le-adam ella yayin* — there is nothing that brings woe upon a person like wine. The connection to our entire discussion is obvious, and the *derashah* of God's rebuke to Noach assumes this view.

Rabbi Yehudah holds it was wheat: *she-ein ha-tinok yode'a likro aba ve-ima ad she-yit'om ta'am dagan* — a child does not know how to say father and mother until it has tasted grain. The *etz ha-da'at* is an *etz* of knowledge and intellect; wheat, which develops a child's capacity for speech and thought, is the substance that corresponds to *da'at tov ve-ra*. Furthermore, the verse describes the tree as desirable for eating and beautiful to the eyes — a description that suits a stalk of wheat.

Rabbi Nechemya holds it was a fig tree: *she-ba-davar she-kilkelu vo nitkanu* — the very thing through which they sinned became the thing that repaired them, for after eating they sewed together fig leaves to cover themselves (*vayitperu aleh te'enah*). Rashi reads this as *midah ke-neged midah* in the mode of divine providence: the same instrument God uses to wound, He uses to heal.

Tosafot, however, does not accept Rashi's formulation and offers a different explanation. When Adam and Chavah sinned, they sought shelter among all the trees of the garden, but every tree refused to receive them — *berukh zeh ha-ganav she-ganav da'atei de-Vara'ei*, blessed is this thief who tried to steal the mind of his Creator — each tree distancing itself from association with the sin. Only the fig tree, which had itself been the instrument of the sin, opened its doors to them in a kind of shared complicity: *al yedei she-akhlah mimenu patkha lo delaseiha ve-kibblah oto*. It was not divine *midah ke-neged midah* from above, but the natural consequence of the fig tree's own involvement.

## Bathsheba's Teaching

### Solomon's Youth

The Gemara now reads the opening of *Mishlei* 31 as a record of Bat Sheva's *mussar* to the young Shlomo. The passage begins: *divrei Lemuel melech, massa asher yisartu imo* — the words of Lemuel the king, a moral teaching with which his mother chastised him. Rashi explains *massa* here as a *parashat tochechah*, a section of remonstrance. Rav Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: the verse teaches that his mother bound him to a post (*kafato imo al ha-'amud*) and subjected him to physical discipline.

The scenario implied is that Shlomo, in his youth — before assuming the full weight of kingship — had been consorting with other kings and princes and falling into their habits of drinking wine. His mother took it upon herself, in the most direct manner, to correct this.

### David's Piety

The verse *mah beri u-mah bar bitni u-mah bar nedari* — what, my son, and what, the son of my womb, and what, the son of my vow — is read as three separate complaints. The first, *mah beri*, is interpreted: everybody knows that your father Dovid was God-fearing. If you conduct yourself this way, people will say *imo garemah lo* — it is his mother's fault.

### Conception Sacrifice

The second complaint, *mah bar bitni*, is more intimate. All the other wives of your father's household, once they became pregnant, were no longer permitted to enter the king's presence. But I (*ve-ani dechakti ve-nikhnasti*) forced my way in, insisting on continuing marital relations even while pregnant, in order to produce a son who would be *mezuraz u-meluban* — vigorous and pure. The *midrashic* tradition held that marital relations during pregnancy strengthened the child. Bat Sheva made a personal sacrifice of propriety and comfort in order to produce the most capable possible son. And now, she implies, that very vigor is being squandered on wine.

### Mother's Vow

The third complaint, *mah bar nedari*, is addressed to the divergence between Bat Sheva's hopes and Shlomo's behavior. All the other wives of the king made vows that they might produce a son fit for kingship. But Bat Sheva's vow was different: *yehei li ben zariz u-mimalei ba-Torah ve-hagun li-nevuot* — let me have a son who is swift, filled with Torah, and worthy of prophecy. She did not merely want a king; she wanted a king who was a prophet and a *talmid chakham*. To see such a son pursuing wine was to see her deepest aspirations betrayed.

### Kings' Folly

The verse continues: *al la-melakhim Lemuel, al la-melakhim sheto yayin* — not to kings, Lemuel, not to kings to drink wine. The Gemara reads *Lemuel* as Shlomo himself and understands the verse as Bat Sheva's direct address: why are you spending your time with those kings who drink wine and get drunk and say *lama lanu El* — what do we need God for? The name *Lemuel* is broken down as a hint: he who has God before him (*le-El*) should not behave as those kings behave. Similarly, *ve-roznim iy shekhar* — those to whom all the secrets of the world are revealed (*mi she-kol razei olam galuyim lo*) — should they drink wine and become drunk? The one who will be given divine wisdom has no business imitating the dissipation of ordinary kings.

### Solomon's Repentance

Rabbi Yitzchak then asks: where do we see that Shlomo ultimately accepted his mother's rebuke? From the verse *ki va'ar anochi me-ish ve-lo binat adam li* — I am more brutish than any man and I have not the understanding of men. The Gemara reads *me-ish* as *mi-Noach* — I was as foolish as Noach — and *binat adam li* as referring to *Adam ha-Rishon* — I lacked the wisdom of Adam the First. Shlomo confesses that he repeated the very mistakes of Noach and Adam, following wine into self-destruction. His mother's words reached him, his youth of dissipation ended, and he became *Shlomo ha-Melech* — the wisest of all men.

## Exemptions

### Mitzvah Company

The *mishna* had stated that eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* does not create liability for *ben sorer u-moreh*. Rav Abbahu now specifies a condition for the non-*mitzvah* case: *eino chayav ad she-yokhal be-chavura she-kulah serikin* — he is not liable unless he ate in a gathering that is entirely composed of worthless people. This implies that if even one respectable person is present, there is no liability.

### Mitzvah Reason

The Gemara challenges this from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* states that eating at a *chavurat mitzvah* exempts him. At such a gathering there are presumably decent people present — yet the *mishna* still exempts him. If the reason for exemption in the case of a fully-*sarikin* gathering is the presence of worthless company, then the reason in the *chavurat mitzvah* case must be something else — namely, the *mitzvah* nature of the gathering itself, not the quality of the attendees.

The Gemara resolves: *ta'ama de-mitzvah* — the reason for the *mishna*'s exemption is purely the *mitzvah* context, and this applies regardless of who is present. If a boy ate at a *chavurat mitzvah* even among entirely worthless people, he would still be exempt because *keivan de-be-mitzvah ka-asik lo mimshakh* — since the occasion is a *mitzvah*, the fear of compulsive addictive behavior developing from this particular meal is absent. The exemption for a gathering of *serikin* without a *mitzvah* context requires that the company all be worthless; in the *mitzvah* context the company is irrelevant.

### Ibur Chodesh

The *mishna* also exempts eating at the gathering for *ibbur ha-chodesh* — the gathering at which the court would announce that the preceding month was a full thirty-day month. The Gemara observes that this gathering apparently involved eating meat and drinking wine (*u-meimra de-vasar ve-yayin maski*). But a *baraita* states that the participants at such a gathering ate only bread and legumes (*ein olim lah ella be-pat dagan u-kitnit bilvad*). The resolution: even if everyone else was eating only bread and legumes and the boy brought his own meat and wine and ate those separately, since *keivan de-be-mitzvah ka-asik lo mimshakh* — the *mitzvah* context insulates even his separate indulgence from the *din* of *ben sorer u-moreh*.

### Night/Day Dispute

A final *baraita* teaches the parameters of the *ibbur ha-chodesh* gathering: it requires a minimum of ten people, it is conducted with bread and legumes only, and — *ein olim lah ella le-or iburo* — it takes place at nightfall leading into the new month. The verse supports a nighttime gathering. However, a contradictory *baraita* states that it is conducted by day and not by night. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's instruction to his sons — *achifu ve-ulu, achifu u-feku* (go early and arrive, go early and depart) — is cited as evidence of a daytime schedule, implying that they would gather during the day while beginning celebrations the night before. The resolution appears to be that the primary gathering occurred during the day, with the celebration commencing the night prior.

Under any interpretation, the *mitzvah* context of the *ibbur ha-chodesh* gathering suffices to exempt a boy who eats meat and wine there from becoming a *ben sorer u-moreh*.
