# Sanhedrin 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ir Nidachas Definition

### Yatzu v'lo Sheluchim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יָצְאוּ״ – הֵן, וְלֹא שְׁלוּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** an interpretation of the verse: "Certain men, wicked persons, are gone out from your midst, and have subverted the inhabitants of their city saying: Let us go and let us worship other gods that you have not known" (Deuteronomy 13:14). From the term **"are gone out"** it is derived that these *halakhot* apply only if **they,** the subverters themselves, subverted the residents, **but not agents** whom they designated to subvert.

The *gemara* opens directly into the *halachah* with a *baraita*: *yatzu anashim b'nei b'liya'al mi-kirbecha* — "men, wicked people, went forth from its midst." The word *yatzu*, "they went," establishes that the enticers must act personally, not through agents. It must be the people themselves who are drawing others toward *avodah zarah*, not representatives acting on their behalf.

### Anashim Pacht Mishnayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲנָשִׁים״ – אֵין אֲנָשִׁים פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״אֲנָשִׁים״ – וְלֹא נָשִׁים, ״אֲנָשִׁים״ – וְלֹא קְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** From the term **"men"** it is derived that **men** in the plural means **no less than two. Alternatively,** it is derived: **Men, but not women; men, but not children.**

The word *anashim*, "men" or "people," yields two separate *darshanim*. First, *ein anashim pachas mi-shnayim* — there must be at least two enticers. Second, *davar acher*: *anashim v'lo nashim* — men, not women — and *anashim v'lo k'tanim* — not minors.

### Bne Beliyaal Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּנֵי בְּלִיַּעַל״ – בָּנִים שֶׁפָּרְקוּ עוֹל שָׁמַיִם מִצַּוְּארֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"wicked persons [*benei veliyya'al*]"** is an abbreviation of *banim beli ol*, meaning: **Sons who have cast off the yoke of Heaven from their necks.**

The phrase *b'nei b'liya'al* is explained as *banim she-porku ol shamayim mi-tzavareihem* — people who have cast off the yoke of Heaven from their necks — *b'li ol*, without a yoke. In terms of adding any independent *halachic* weight, this is less clear; it appears primarily as an explication of the word's meaning.

### Chutz Lasafar Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״ – וְלֹא מִן הַסְּפָר.
>
> **Translation:** From the term **"from your midst"** it is derived that these *halakhot* apply only to a city located in the center of the country, **but not** to a city located **on the border.**

*Mi-kirbecha* — from its midst — excludes *min ha-s'far*, a city on the border. A border city cannot become an *ir nidachas* because declaring it so would endanger the surrounding territory.

### Madichiha Mitochah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יֹשְׁבֵי עִירָם״ – וְלֹא יוֹשְׁבֵי עִיר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** From the phrase **"inhabitants of their city"** it is inferred: **But not inhabitants of another city.**

*Yoshvei iram* — they seduced the dwellers of their city — *v'lo yoshvei ir acheres*, not of another city. This establishes that the *madichim*, the enticers, must themselves be from within the city they are seducing.

### Eidim v'Hasraah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֵאמֹר״ – שֶׁצְּרִיכִין עֵדִים וְהַתְרָאָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The term **"saying"** alludes to the *halakha* **that** in order to convict them **one requires witnesses and forewarning for each and every one** who worshipped idols.

The word *lemor* — "to say" — implies that valid testimony and formal warning are required. *Rashi* derives this from the end of the verse, *nelcha v'na'avdah*, "let us go and worship," reasoning that if the speech must be reportable, there must be witnesses, and where there are witnesses there must be *hasra'ah*. An alternative reading is that *lemor* itself signals a formal declaration requiring warning. This stands in contrast to cases of *masis u-madiach* — seducing an individual to *avodah zarah* — where the practice is to conceal witnesses. Here, to render a city an *ir nidachas*, proper *eidim v'hasra'ah* are required for each individual worshipper, *l'chol echad v'echad*, until the majority of the city has worshipped *avodah zarah* and the city as a whole becomes subject to the laws of *ir nidachas*.

## Tribes & City Division

### Yochanan: Cholkin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹלְקִין עִיר אַחַת לִשְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים; וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵין חוֹלְקִין עִיר אַחַת לִשְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One divides one city into** the tribal territory of **two tribes; and Reish Lakish says: One does not divide one city into** the tribal territory of **two tribes.**

A dispute is recorded: Rabbi Yochanan holds *cholkin ir achas l'shnei shevatim* — it is possible for a single city to be divided between two different tribes in the land of Israel.

### Reish Lakish: Lo Cholkin

Reish Lakish disagrees: *ein cholkin ir achas l'bei shevatim* — a city is not divided between two tribes. The general background assumption of the *gemara* is that Jerusalem was divided between Yehudah and Binyamin, but Reish Lakish may regard that as an exception. His position is that no other city was divided between two tribal territories.

### Meditichah Meoso Shevet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מַדִּיחֶיהָ מֵאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר וּמֵאוֹתוֹ שֵׁבֶט – מַאי לָאו, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמַדִּיחֶיהָ מֵאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר, אִי אִיכָּא מֵאוֹתוֹ שֵׁבֶט – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חוֹלְקִין עִיר אַחַת לִשְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from the mishna, which states: **Unless its subverters are from that city and from that tribe; what, is it not that although its subverters are from that city, if they are from that tribe, yes,** they render the city an idolatrous city, and **if not, no,** they do not render the city an idolatrous city? **Conclude from it: One divides one city into** the tribal territory of **two tribes,** as otherwise, by definition, a resident of that city is a member of that tribe.

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from our *mishnah*, which states that the enticers must be *mitocheha u-me'oso shevet* — from within the city and from the same tribe as those they seduce. If the enticers are already required to be from within the city, what does the additional criterion of tribal identity add? It must be addressing a case where someone physically inside the city belongs to a different tribe than the majority of its residents. How could someone dwell within a single city yet be of a different tribe? This would require that the city itself be divided between two tribes — the northern section belonging to one tribe and the southern to another. If a member of, say, Reuven were to entice the residents of the Shimon section of the same city, they would fail the *me'oso shevet* requirement and it would not be an *ir nidachas*. This implies that Rabbi Yochanan's position — that cities can be divided between tribes — is presumed.

### Nafla Leh Biy'rushah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ בִּירוּשָׁה. אִי נָמֵי, דְּיַהֲבוּהָ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בְּמַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** even if a city is not divided between two tribes, the scenario in the mishna is possible in a case **where** the property in the city **fell to him as an inheritance** from the family of his mother, who was from a different tribe. **Alternatively,** it is possible in a case **where they gave him** that property **as a gift.**

The *gemara* rejects this proof. Reish Lakish can maintain that even a city entirely within one tribe's territory might contain an individual of a different tribe, either through inheritance — *d'naflah leh biy'rushah* — for example, if his father was from Shimon but his mother from Reuven and he inherited her portion — or through a gift or sale. An individual might be a resident of a city that belongs entirely to one tribe while personally being affiliated with another.

### Arim Tesha Mei'et

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״עָרִים תֵּשַׁע מֵאֵת שְׁנֵי הַשְּׁבָטִים הָאֵלֶּה״. מַאי לָאו: אַרְבַּע וּפַלְגָא מֵהַאי, וְאַרְבַּע וּפַלְגָא מֵהַאי? וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חוֹלְקִין עִיר אַחַת לִשְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים! לָא, אַרְבַּע מֵהַאי וְחָמֵשׁ מֵהַאי. אִי הָכִי, לִפְרוֹשׁ פָּרוֹשֵׁי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised an objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish from a verse written with regard to the cities designated for the Levites: **"Nine cities from those two tribes"** (Joshua 21:16). **What, is it not** that those nine cities were equally divided; **four-and-a-half from this** tribe, **and four-and-a-half from that** tribe, **and conclude from it** that **one divides one city into** the tribal territory of **two tribes?** The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** those nine cities were divided in this manner: **Four** cities **from this** tribe **and five** cities **from that** tribe. The Gemara asks: **If so, let** the verse **state explicitly** from which tribe the additional city was taken.

A further attempt to resolve the dispute is made from a verse in Yehoshua describing *arim tesha mei'et shnei ha-shevatim ha-eilu* — nine Levitical cities given from these two tribes, Yehudah and Shimon. The *gemara* asks: does this not imply that one city was shared between the two, meaning four and a half from Yehudah and four and a half from Shimon, proving that a single city can be divided between two tribes? The *gemara* replies that the verse may simply mean that between the two tribes they gave nine cities — four from one and five from the other, with no city shared. However, the *gemara* pushes back: if that were so, the verse should have specified how many each tribe gave. The very fact that it presents a joint total of nine suggests at least one city was held in common. The *gemara* concludes *kashya* — the difficulty stands, but the proof is not absolute.

## Shehudchu Mei'aleihen

### Enticers Required?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הוּדְּחוּ מֵאֲלֵיהֶן, מַהוּ? ״וַיַּדִּיחוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שֶׁהוּדְּחוּ מֵאֲלֵיהֶן? אוֹ דִילְמָא, אֲפִילּוּ הוּדְּחוּ מֵאֲלֵיהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the inhabitants of an idolatrous city **subverted themselves** to idol worship and were not subverted by others, **what is** the *halakha*? Since **the Merciful One states: "And have subverted,"** perhaps it may be inferred: **But not if they subvert themselves; or perhaps even** if **they subverted themselves** to idol worship, the city can be deemed an idolatrous city.

The *gemara* raises a formal question: *shehudchu mei'aleihen mahu* — what is the *halachah* if the city required no enticers at all, and the residents simply, on their own initiative, turned to *avodah zarah*? Is it derived from the verse *vayidichu* — "they enticed" — that there must be active enticers for the laws of *ir nidachas* to apply? Or does it perhaps make matters worse when people sin entirely of their own volition?

### Nashim v'Ktanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִדִּיחוּהָ נָשִׁים וּקְטַנִּים, אַמַּאי? לֶיהֱוֵי כְּהוּדְּחוּ מֵאֲלֵיהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of the dilemma from the mishna: If **women or children subvert** the inhabitants of the city, these idol worshippers are judged as individuals. **Why** is that so? **Let** their status **be** considered **as** though **they subverted themselves.**

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishnah* itself: *hidichuah nashim u-ktanim* — if women or minors served as the enticers, the city does not become an *ir nidachas*. The *gemara* asks: why not? If we were to maintain that the city can achieve *ir nidachas* status even without qualified enticers — that is, even through self-motivated *avodah zarah* — then why should enticement by women or children matter? Let the case of women and children simply be treated as equivalent to a city that went on its own. The fact that the *mishnah* distinguishes the case of women and children from full *ir nidachas* suggests that these cases are categorically different from self-motivated idolatry. But the question then becomes: how?

### Batar Nafshaihu Griri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ בָּתַר נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ גְּרִידִי, הָנֵי בָּתַר נָשִׁים וּקְטַנִּים גְּרִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** Apparently, if the inhabitants subverted themselves the city is not deemed an idolatrous city. The Gemara rejects this proof: These cases are not comparable, as **these,** who subverted themselves, **are drawn after their own** initiative and worship idols wholeheartedly, and therefore perhaps the city is rendered an idolatrous city. **Those,** who are subverted by women and children, **are drawn after women and children** and are not committed to idol worship, and therefore, perhaps the city is not rendered an idolatrous city.

The *gemara* answers that the two cases may indeed differ in severity. *Hanech batar nafshaihu griri* — those who act on their own initiative are following their own deeply held desire; they are genuinely committed to *avodah zarah*. By contrast, *hanech batar nashim u-ktanim griri* — those who were persuaded by women or children may be much more superficially attached to their transgression. People led astray by women or children may be more easily reversible, less fully committed — they might eventually dismiss the enticement as beneath their serious consideration. It could therefore actually be worse, and more irreversible, when people act entirely on their own. This reasoning reflects significant societal assumptions embedded in the *gemara*'s analysis.

## Judicial Process

### Ad Sheyudach Rubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיּוּדַח רוּבָּהּ. הֵיכִי עָבְדִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a city is not deemed an idolatrous city **unless most of** the inhabitants of the city **are subverted.** The Gemara asks: **How do we act** in order to determine if most of the inhabitants have been subverted?

For the laws of *ir nidachas* to apply, the majority of the city must have worshipped *avodah zarah*: *ad sheyudach rubah*. This immediately raises a procedural difficulty — one cannot know whether the majority has worshipped until every case has been processed, yet the judicial process must begin long before the final tally is known.

### Danin v'Chovshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: דָּנִין וְחוֹבְשִׁין, דָּנִין וְחוֹבְשִׁין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא: נִמְצָא אַתָּה מְעַנֶּה אֶת דִּינָן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** The court **judges** each inhabitant suspected of idol worship, **and** if he is found liable **imprisons** him in a jail, and it **judges and imprisons** each succeeding idolater until a majority of the city has been convicted. **Ulla said to him:** That will **result in you delaying justice for those** already sentenced and awaiting execution, and it is prohibited to delay justice.

Rav Yehudah's approach: *danin v'chovshin danin v'chovshin* — you judge each individual, find them liable for *sekila*, and imprison them, then judge the next, imprison them, and continue until the full tally is in. If the imprisoned total ultimately constitutes a majority of the city, the ruling changes to *ir nidachas* and they are all executed by the sword rather than stoned, and the city's property is destroyed.

### Ulla's Challenge

Ulla objects to this approach: *mishum d'me'ansinan dinan shel elu* — you are thereby causing *innui ha-din*, the oppression of their judgment. Once a capital verdict has been rendered, one cannot simply hold the condemned in jail indefinitely. The obligation to carry out judgment cannot be postponed.

### Danim v'Soklim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אָמַר עוּלָּא: דָּנִין וְסוֹקְלִין, דָּנִין וְסוֹקְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ulla says:** The court **judges** each inhabitant **and stones** him if he is found liable, then **judges and stones** each succeeding idolater until half of the city is found guilty, and all future convicted idolaters are executed by the sword.

Ulla therefore proposes an alternative: *danim v'soklim danim v'soklim* — judge each case individually and carry out the execution of *sekila* as you go, while maintaining a running tally. Once you reach the person whose verdict would make the worshippers a majority of the city, you recognize that you are now dealing with an *ir nidachas*, and from that point forward the penalty shifts to the sword and the city's property is subject to destruction.

### Marbin Lahem Batei Dinim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: דָּנִין וְסוֹקְלִין, דָּנִין וְסוֹקְלִין, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מַרְבִּין לָהֶן בָּתֵּי דִינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a parallel amoraic dispute. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The court **judges and stones** the idolater, then **judges and stones** each succeeding idolater. **And Reish Lakish says: One increases** the number of **courts for them** so that all the inhabitants of the city can stand trial at the same time and the means of their execution will be determined with no delay of justice.

Reish Lakish proposes a different solution: *marbin lahem batei dinim* — convene many courts simultaneously to process all cases on a single day. This avoids the problem of *me'aneh et ha-din* because no verdict goes unexecuted for any meaningful time, and it avoids the inconsistency of applying *sekila* to some and *sayif* to others. *Rashi* notes that while it is ordinarily prohibited to judge two capital cases on the same day, that restriction does not apply when the cases are all part of the same matter.

### Sanhedrin HaGedolah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר יוֹסֵי אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״וְהוֹצֵאתָ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא אוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה הָהִיא״ – אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹצִיא כָּל הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rabbi Ḥama bar Yosei say** that **Rabbi Oshaya says** that it is written: **"And you shall take that man or that woman out…**to your gates" (Deuteronomy 17:5), from which it is derived: **You take a man and a woman out to your gates** and they are judged by the local court, **but you do not take the entire city out to your gates;** instead, they stand trial before the Great Sanhedrin.

The *gemara* challenges all these approaches by citing a ruling of Rabbi Chama bar Yosi in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *v'hotzeisa es ha-ish hahu o es ha-isha hahi* — "You shall bring out that man or that woman" to your gate, referring to individual cases of *avodah zarah* judged within the city. *Ish v'isha atah motzi l'sharecha* — a single man or woman you bring to the city gate, *v'ei atah motzi kol ha-ir kulo l'sharecha* — but you do not bring an entire city to its own gate. An entire city must be judged by the *sanhedrin* in the *beis ha-mikdash*, the national *beis din ha-gadol*. This creates a fundamental problem: any procedure that involves local courts issuing verdicts on individual residents — whether following Rav Yehudah, Ulla, or Reish Lakish — may leave the *sanhedrin ha-gedolah* with no genuine role in the process, having only a marginal function after the local *beis din* has already effectively determined all the facts.

### Me'aynin B'Dineihem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מַרְבִּין לָהֶן בָּתֵּי דִינִין וּמְעַיְּינִין בְּדִינֵיהֶן, וּמַסְּקִינַן לְהוּ לְבֵית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל, וְגָמְרִי לְהוּ לְדִינַיְיהוּ, וְקָטְלִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the procedure is that **one increases** the number of **courts for them and** those courts **analyze their cases,** and when they conclude that a majority of the inhabitants are guilty of idolatry they are not sentenced; instead, **we take them to the High Court [Sanhedrin] and** the court **issues** the **verdict of** the idolaters **and executes them.**

The *gemara* resolves this by combining Reish Lakish's framework with an important qualification: *marbin lahem batei dinim u-me'aynin b'dineihem* — convene many courts and investigate all the cases thoroughly, but do not reach *gmar din*, the final verdict, in any of them. Since no final judgment has been rendered, there is no *innui ha-din* if the proceedings are temporarily held open. *U-maskinan lehu l'beis din ha-gadol* — once the investigation reveals that a majority of the city is implicated, the entire matter is referred to the *beis din ha-gadol*. There, the *beis din ha-gadol* completes the *gmar din* for each case and carries out the executions: *v'gmiri lehu l'dayanei l'dineihu v'katli lehu*. In this way the *sanhedrin ha-gedolah* is genuinely involved in the dispositive stage of the proceedings.

## Residency Shiurim

### Shloshim Yom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֵּה תַכֶּה אֶת יֹשְׁבֵי הָעִיר וְכוּ׳״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַחַמֶּרֶת וְהַגַּמֶּלֶת הָעוֹבֶרֶת מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, לָנוּ בְּתוֹכָהּ וְהוּדְּחוּ עִמָּהּ – אִם נִשְׁתַּהוּ שָׁם שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, הֵן בְּסַיִיף וּמָמוֹנָן אָבֵד. פָּחוֹת מִיכָּן, הֵן בִּסְקִילָה וּמָמוֹנָן פָּלֵט.
>
> **Translation:** § It is written: **"You shall smite the inhabitants of that city** with the edge of the sword, destroy it utterly, and all that is in it and its animals, with the edge of the sword" (Deuteronomy 13:16). **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The caravan of donkeys and the caravan of camels that move from place to place,** that **lodged in** the city **and were subverted with it, if they had stayed there thirty days they** are executed **by the sword and their property is destroyed.** If they had stayed there **less than that, they** are executed **by stoning,** as individual idolaters, **and their property is spared.**

The verse commands striking *yoshvei ha-ir*, the dwellers of the city. The *mishnah* had noted that even a passing camel driver or donkey driver could potentially affect the calculation of whether a majority of the city worshipped *avodah zarah*, without specifying the precise threshold. A *baraita* now fills this in: *ha-chamar v'ha-gamal ha-over mi-makom l'makom* — a donkey driver or camel driver passing through from place to place — *lan b'sochah v'nehidchu imah* — who slept in the city and then worshipped *avodah zarah* along with its residents — if they were there *shloshim yom*, thirty days, they are considered *yoshvei ha-ir hahi*, dwellers of that city. This threshold operates on two levels. First, it affects the population count used to determine whether those who sinned constitute a majority of the city. Second, it determines the individual's own fate: if they dwelled there for thirty days, they are treated as city residents — executed by the sword and their property destroyed along with the city. If they were there less than thirty days, they are treated as individuals who committed *avodah zarah* on their own, subject to *sekila* and their property is spared.

### Yud Bet Chodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: כַּמָּה יִהְיֶה בָּעִיר וְיִהְיֶה כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר? שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **How long shall** one **be in the city and** as a result, his status **will be like** that of **the residents of the city** with regard to giving charity and paying taxes? He is obligated if he remained there **twelve months,** and not thirty days.

The *gemara* raises a challenge from Bava Batra: *kama yehei ba-ir v'yehei k'anshei ha-ir* — how long must one reside in a city to be considered among its members for purposes of civic obligation, such as taxes for communal walls and defense? The answer there is twelve months — *yud-bet chodesh*. Is the threshold thirty days or twelve months?

### Yoshvei v'Bnei Ha'ir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא לְמִיהְוֵי מִבְּנֵי מָתָא, הָא לְמִיהְוֵי מִיָּתְבֵי מָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** This contradiction is **not difficult,** and it may be resolved: **This** period of twelve months is the period required **to become** one **of the citizens of the city; this** period of thirty days is the period required **to become** one **of the inhabitants of the city.**

Rava resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha l'mehavi mi-bnei mata, ha l'mehavi mi-yoshvei mata*. There is a distinction between being a *ben ha-ir*, a full member of the city, and a *yoshev ha-ir*, a dweller in the city. After thirty days of residence one becomes a *yoshev*, a dweller, which is the relevant status for *ir nidachas*. After twelve months one becomes a *ben ha-ir*, a full civic member, which is what Bava Batra requires for the obligation to contribute to communal infrastructure. A passing remark is also made that the threshold for the enticers themselves — whether *madicheiha mitochah* requires that they be *yoshvei ha-ir* or specifically *bnei ha-ir* — is not made explicit in the sources.

### Mudar Hana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מִבְּנֵי הָעִיר, אִם יֵשׁ אָדָם שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּהָא שָׁם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ – אָסוּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ, פָּחוֹת מִיכָּן – מוּתָּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ. בְּיוֹשְׁבֵי הָעִיר, אִם נִשְׁתַּהָא שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – אָסוּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ, פָּחוֹת מִיכָּן – מוּתָּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from the citizens of the city, if there is a person who had stayed there twelve months, it is prohibited** for the one for whom deriving benefit is forbidden **to derive benefit from him.** If a person had stayed there **less than that, it is permitted** for the one for whom deriving benefit is forbidden **to derive benefit from him.** With regard to one prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit **from the inhabitants of the city, if** there is a person who **had stayed there thirty days, it is prohibited** for the one for whom deriving benefit is forbidden **to derive benefit from him.** If a person had stayed there **less than that, it is permitted** for the one for whom deriving benefit is forbidden **to derive benefit from him.** In the context of an idolatrous city, the reference is to the inhabitants of the city, which includes anyone who had stayed there for thirty days.

The *gemara* confirms this distinction from a *baraita*. If someone vows not to derive benefit from *bnei ha-ir*, the members of the city: someone who has been there twelve months is included in the vow and benefit from him is forbidden; less than that, it is permitted. But if the vow was formulated as *yoshvei ha-ir*, the dwellers of the city: someone who has been there thirty days is included, and less than that is permitted. This distinction — thirty days for *yoshvei ha-ir*, twelve months for *bnei ha-ir* — precisely supports Rava's resolution.

## Hacharem Otah

### Kol Asher Bah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַחֲרֵם אֹתָהּ וְאֶת כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בָּהּ״ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַחֲרֵם אוֹתָהּ וְאֶת כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בָּהּ״ – פְּרָט לְנִכְסֵי צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּחוּצָה לָהּ. ״וְאֶת כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בָּהּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת נִכְסֵי צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"Destroy it utterly, and all that is in it"** (Deuteronomy 13:16). **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to that which is written: **"Destroy it utterly, and all that is in it";** this serves **to exclude the property of the righteous that is** located **outside** the city, which is not destroyed. **"And all that is in it";** this serves **to include the property of the righteous that is** located **inside** the city; it is also destroyed.

The verse commands *hacharem otah v'et kol asher bah* — destroy it and everything that is in it. The *baraita* derives from *otah*, "it," that this excludes *nichsei tzadikim she-be-chutzah lah* — property of the righteous that is located outside the city. Although the word *kol* suggests comprehensiveness, the qualifier *bah* — "in it" — limits the destruction to what is physically inside the city. Property of those who did not sin, if situated outside the city, is spared.

### Kol Shelalah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת כׇּל שְׁלָלָהּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת נִכְסֵי רְשָׁעִים שֶׁחוּצָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"and** you shall gather **all of its spoils"** serves **to include the property of the wicked that is** located **outside** the city; it is also destroyed.

At the same time, the verse continues *v'et kol shlalah tikbotz el toch rechovah* — gather all its spoil into the public square. This phrase is interpreted as *l'rabot nichsei resha'im she-be-chutzah lah* — to include the property of the sinners even if it is located outside the city. Unlike *kol asher bah*, which refers to what is physically present within the walls, *kol shlalah* refers to all that belongs to the city — property that is the *shalal*, the wealth, of the city's residents, regardless of where it is situated. *Rashi* adds the further nuance that this applies specifically to property that was once in the city but has since been moved outside; something that was never in the city at all would be more difficult to characterize as *shlalah*. The resulting framework distinguishes *tzadikim* from *resha'im*: property of the righteous is included only if it is physically inside the city; property of the wicked is included even if outside, as long as it can fairly be called the city's *shalal*.

### U'venikpatzim Betocha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּבְנִקְבָּצִים לְתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: And** this is the *halakha* only **with regard to** property that can be **gathered into** the city.

Rav Chisda limits the scope of *kol shlalah* with respect to property of the wicked situated outside the city: *u-venikpatzim betocha* — only property that could be gathered back into the city, which *Rashi* understands as property retrievable within a single day, is included. Property too remote to be gathered in falls outside the reach of *shlalah*.

### Migram Lahem Sheyeduru

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה נִכְסֵי צַדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ יֹאבֵדוּ? מִי גָּרַם לָהֶם שֶׁיָּדוּרוּ בְּתוֹכָהּ? מָמוֹנָם. לְפִיכָךְ מָמוֹנָם אָבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: For what** reason **does the Torah say** that **the property of the righteous that is in it shall be destroyed?** Why must they suffer for the sins of others? The reason is: **Who caused** the righteous **to live in** this city inhabited by wicked people? It is **their property** that tied them to this city; **therefore, their property is destroyed.**

Rabbi Shimon asks: *mipnei mah amrah Torah nichsei tzadikim she-be-tocha yovdu* — why does the Torah decree that the property of the righteous located inside the city is destroyed? His answer: *migram lahem she-yeduru be-tocha* — it was their financial interest that caused them to dwell among the wicked. Surely it was not social or religious affinity that drew the righteous into this city of evildoers — it must have been financial considerations. *V'al ken mammonam avad* — therefore their money is lost. Since it was their money that led them to remain among the *resha'im*, it is fitting that their money is what they forfeit.

### Pikdonot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פִּקְדוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַנְשֵׁי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת מוּתָּרִין. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי לֵימָא דְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת וְאִיתַנְהוּ בְּגַוַּהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא דְּמוּתָּרִין, לָאו שְׁלָלָהּ הוּא! וְאֶלָּא דִּידְהוּ וְאִיתַנְהוּ בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת. אִי דְּנִקְבָּצִין לְתוֹכָהּ – אַמַּאי מוּתָּרִין? וְאִי אֵין נִקְבָּצִין לְתוֹכָהּ – הָא אַמְרַהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא! לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת דְּמַפְקְדִי בְּתוֹכָהּ. וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּקַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ אַחְרָיוּת. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּקַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ אַחְרָיוּת, כְּדִידֵיהּ דָּמֵי? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Deposits of the inhabitants of an idolatrous city are permitted,** i.e., they are not destroyed. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that Rav Ḥisda is referring to property belonging to inhabitants **of another city and** that property **is** deposited **within** the idolatrous city, it is **obvious that** the deposits are **permitted,** as the deposits are **not "its spoils";** they are the property of others. **But rather,** say that Rav Ḥisda is referring to property **belonging to** the inhabitants of the idolatrous city, **and** it **is** deposited **within another city.** If so, the status of that property should be like any other property belonging to the inhabitants of the idolatrous city; **if** it is property **that** can be **gathered into** the city, **why are** the deposits **permitted?** It was already established that the property of the wicked is destroyed. **And if** it is property that **cannot** be **gathered into** the city, **didn't** Rav Ḥisda already **say once** that this property is not burned? The Gemara answers: **No,** Rav Ḥisda is **actually** referring to property belonging to inhabitants **of another city that is deposited within** the idolatrous city; **and what are we dealing with here?** It is a case **where** an inhabitant of the idolatrous city **assumes** financial **responsibility for** the deposit in his possession. **Lest you say: Once he assumes** financial **responsibility for** the deposit in his possession, the status of the deposit is **like** that of **his own** property and it should be destroyed, therefore Rav Ḥisda **teaches us** that deposits are permitted and not destroyed.

Rav Chisda rules: *pikdonot shel anshei ir ha-nidachat mutarim* — entrusted property associated with the *ir nidachas* is permitted. The *gemara* asks what case this refers to. If it is property belonging to residents of another city that happens to be held by someone in the *ir nidachas*, the answer is obviously that it is permitted, since *peshita de-mutarim* — it is manifestly not the city's *shalal*. The *gemara* therefore frames the case as *d'didhu v'inhu b'ir acheres* — property owned by residents of the *ir nidachas* that is being held for them in another city. The obvious implication is that such property is included in *kol shlalah* — it is the city's wealth even though it is located outside. But Rav Chisda's ruling is that it is permitted, and the *chiddush* lies in the specific circumstance: *dekibbel aleih achrayus* — the watchman in the other city accepted financial liability for the entrusted goods. Given that principle elsewhere — for example in the context of *chametz b'Pesach*, where accepting liability renders something legally one's own — one might have thought this liability suffices to make the goods the watchman's property, thus rendering them *shalal* of the *ir nidachas*. The ruling teaches that even so, they remain permitted, because the entrusted goods do not fundamentally belong to the *ir nidachas*, and accepted liability does not change their essential ownership.

Rav Chisda then addresses jointly owned property. An animal half-owned by a resident of the *ir nidachas* and half by a resident of another city: *asurah* — it is entirely forbidden. An animal is an indivisible unit; once the *halachah* of *ir nidachas* attaches to any portion of it, it applies to the whole. Dough, however, that is half owned by the *ir nidachas* and half by another city: *muteres* — it is permitted, in the sense that one may divide it and the portion belonging to the other city may be freely used.

### Behema v'Isah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּהֵמָה חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת וְחֶצְיָהּ שֶׁל עִיר אַחֶרֶת – אֲסוּרָה. עִיסָּה חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת וְחֶצְיָהּ שֶׁל עִיר אַחֶרֶת – מוּתֶּרֶת. מַאי טַעְמָא? בְּהֵמָה כְּמַאן דְּלָא פְּלִיגָא דָּמְיָא, עִיסָּה כְּמַאן דִּפְלִיגָא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: An animal** that is **half** the property **of an idolatrous city and half** the property **of another city** is **forbidden.** By contrast, with regard to **dough** that is **half** the property **of an idolatrous city and half** the property **of another city,** the half that belongs to the other city is **permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the difference between them? The Gemara explains: **An animal is like an entity that is not divided,** as it is possible to eat part of an animal only through the slaughter of the entire animal. Therefore, if any portion of the animal is forbidden, the entire animal is forbidden. **Dough is like an entity that is divided.** Therefore, the fact that one portion of the dough is forbidden does not render the entire dough forbidden.

The rationale for this distinction: *behema k'man d'lo pliga damya* — an animal is treated as though it cannot be divided; it is a single integral unit. Since the status of *ir nidachas* applies to part of it, it applies to all of it. *Isah k'man d'pliga damya* — dough, however, is naturally divisible, treated as though it is already divided; therefore the prohibited status attaches only to the portion that genuinely belongs to the *ir nidachas*.

## Behemas Ir Nidachas

### Shechita L'Taharah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב חִסְדָּא: בֶּהֱמַת עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת מַהוּ דְּתִיתְהֲנֵי בַּהּ שְׁחִיטָה לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵילָה? לְפִי חֶרֶב אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, לָא שְׁנָא שַׁחֲטַהּ מִשְׁחָט, לָא שְׁנָא קַטְלַהּ מִקְטָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raises a dilemma:** With regard to **the animal of an idolatrous city, what is** the *halakha*; **is ritual slaughter effective** at least **in purifying it from** the ritual impurity **of an unslaughtered animal carcass?** If the animals of the city are killed but not ritually slaughtered, they are impure with the impurity of a carcass and transmit impurity by means of contact as well as if they are lifted. Will the ritual slaughter of those animals prevent the transmission of that impurity? Is it so that since **the Merciful One states:** "And its animals, **with the edge of the sword,"** indicating that **it is no different** if **one ritually slaughtered** the animal, and **it is no different** if **one killed** the animal in another way, its status is that of a carcass and it transmits impurity?

Rav Chisda raises a question: *behemas ir ha-nidachat mahu d'tehani bah shechitah l'taharah midei neveilah* — does *shechitah* performed on an animal of *ir nidachas* prevent the animal from acquiring the status of *neveilah*? An animal that dies without *shechitah* is a *neveilah* and conveys *tumah*. An animal properly slaughtered — even if found to be *treifah* and thus forbidden for consumption — does not acquire *tumas neveilah*. The question here is: even though the animal of *ir nidachas* is *assur b'hana'ah* and cannot be eaten, does the act of *shechitah* at least spare it from *tumas neveilah*?

### Lefi Charev Amar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁחְטַהּ מַהְנְיָא לַהּ שְׁחִיטָה? מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps, since one ritually slaughtered** the animal, **ritual slaughter is effective. What** is the *halakha*? The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* invokes the verse that mandates the animals of *ir nidachas* be killed *lefi charev* — by the edge of the sword. The verse does not distinguish between killing by direct stabbing and cutting the neck in the manner of *shechitah*: *lo shna shachtei u-mashchat v'lo shna katlei u-maktal* — it makes no difference whether you slaughter it in the manner of *shechitah* or simply kill it; either way it constitutes the fulfillment of *lefi charev*. The act is therefore not considered *shechitah* in the operative sense — it is an execution mandated by law. Accordingly, the animal does not lose its status of *neveilah* and does convey *tumah*. The same reasoning would apply to the question of whether slaughtering such an animal would violate the prohibition of *oso v'es bno lo tishchatu b'yom echad* — since the act is not considered *shechitah*, the prohibition would not apply. The *gemara* leaves the primary question as *teiku*.

## Kodashim v'Heqdesh

### Kodshei Mizbe'ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הָיוּ בָּהּ קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ – יָמוּתוּ. וְאַמַּאי יָמוּתוּ? יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ דְּמֵיהֶן לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to analyze the *baraita*. **The Master said: If there were offerings of the most sacred order in it,** if they were animals **consecrated for** sacrifice on **the altar, they shall die.** The Gemara asks: **But why shall they die? They should graze until they become unfit, and** then **they should be sold and their value should be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

The *gemara* turns to the *mishnah*'s ruling that *shlalah v'lo shlal shamayim* — the city's spoil is destroyed but not that which belongs to Heaven. The *baraita* now provides the detailed treatment of each category of sanctified property. If the city contained animals consecrated as *kodshei mizbe'ach* — sacrificial offerings destined for the altar — *yamusu*, they must be left to die.

The immediate question is why. The verse excludes *shlal shamayim* from destruction; why then are these animals not simply removed from the city and offered on the altar? And if they cannot be offered, why not let them acquire a blemish, redeem them, and use the proceeds for other communal sacrifices? The *gemara* will address both.

### Bedek Habayis Yifdu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת – יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Items **consecrated for Temple maintenance must be redeemed.**

By contrast, *kodshei bedek ha-bayis yipadu* — property consecrated for Temple maintenance purposes may simply be redeemed. This makes sense straightforwardly: such property is *shlal shamayim* and entirely exempt from the laws of *ir nidachas*, so the only question is how to extract it from the city's context, and redemption accomplishes that cleanly. *Rashi* on the *mishnah* suggests one might have thought the property could simply be walked out of the city since it is *shel shamayim*, but the implication is that it must be properly redeemed, perhaps because of some residual contamination from the city's context.

### Zevach Reshaim To'eva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״זֶבַח רְשָׁעִים תּוֹעֵבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** They shall die, as it is written: **"The offering of the wicked is an abomination"** (Proverbs 21:27), and it is unacceptable for sacrifice on the altar.

Rabbi Yochanan explains why *kodshei mizbe'ach* must be left to die rather than offered or redeemed: *zevach resha'im to'evah* — "the sacrifice of the wicked is an abomination" (Proverbs 21:27). These animals are owned by the condemned wicked, and therefore they cannot be brought as a valid *korban*. This is not because *kodshei mizbe'ach* fall within the scope of *ir nidachas* — for Rabbi Yochanan, they are genuinely exempt as *shlal shamayim*. The reason they cannot be used is entirely separate: the ownership of *resha'im* disqualifies them as offerings. Moreover, this disqualification extends even to redemption: if the proceeds of redemption would derive from the sacrifice of the wicked, those proceeds are tainted as well. Rabbi Yochanan thus presents a two-tiered analysis: the animals escape the *halachah* of *ir nidachas* entirely, but a separate principle — *zevach resha'im to'evah* — prevents them from being used in their normal sanctified capacity.

### Mamon Ba'alim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים הוּא. וְהָכָא בְּקָדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא דְּאָמַר: מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** They shall die because the animal **is the property of its owner** and is not exclusively consecrated property. **And** the reason it is considered the property of the owner is that **here,** the *tanna* is referring **to offerings** with regard to **which one bears** financial **responsibility for their** replacement. That responsibility renders the status of these consecrated animals like that of his property. **And** this **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says:** An offering with regard to which one bears financial responsibility for its replacement **is the property of** its **owner.**

Reish Lakish takes a fundamentally different approach: *mamon ba'alim hu* — the *korban* is in fact considered the property of its owner. This position rests on a principle attributed to Rabbi Shimon: when the owner of a *korban* accepts personal financial liability for it — *chayav b'achrayuso* — the offering is considered like his own property. Since the *ir nidachas* resident has such liability, the *korban* is legally treated as the resident's property and therefore does fall within the scope of *ir nidachas*. The reason it is left to die rather than actively destroyed by the sword is not because it escapes *ir nidachas*, but because its status as *kodshim* calls for a softer treatment: we do not actively destroy sacred property, but we allow it to perish.

### Kodshim Kalim v'Kodshei…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, רֵישָׁא לָאו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי דְּאָמַר: קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים. אֲבָל קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים מַאי? יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **From** the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** one may infer that **the first clause is not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara suggests a new explanation: Rather, the animals that shall die are **offerings of lesser sanctity, and** the ruling of the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says: Offerings of lesser sanctity are the property of** their **owner.** The Gemara infers: **But offerings of the most sacred order, what** shall be done with them? **They shall be redeemed.**

The *gemara* refines this position by noting that the opening clause of the *baraita* — which mandates leaving *kodshei mizbe'ach* to die — cannot in fact be attributed to Rabbi Shimon, since Rabbi Shimon himself appears at the end of the *baraita* as a dissenting voice distinguishing *bechor* and *ma'aser*. Accordingly, the *gemara* attributes the opening clause to a different authority, reaching the *mamon ba'alim* result through Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's principle regarding *kodshim kalim*. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili holds that *kodshim kalim*, lesser sanctified items such as *shelamim* that the owners themselves eat, are considered *mamon ba'alim* — the owner's property. As opposed to *kodshei kodshim*, the most holy offerings which the *kohanim* eat off, as it were, God's table, and which are fundamentally *mamon gavo'ah* — Heavenly property.

Applying this principle: *kodshim kalim* of the *ir nidachas* residents are *mamon ba'alim* and genuinely subject to the *halachah* of *ir nidachas*, but are left to die rather than actively destroyed because they are *kodshim*. *Kodshei kodshim*, being truly *shlal shamayim*, would in principle be exempt and redeemable — with one critical exception. A *chatas* whose owners are executed — *chatas she-mesu be'aleha* — has a specific *halachah* that it must be left to die and cannot be redeemed even with a blemish. Since the owners of the *ir nidachas* are being executed, any *chatas* among the *kodshei kodshim* would fall into this category and must also perish. This mixed situation within *kodshei kodshim* — some redeemable, some not — explains why the *baraita* does not attempt to draw the distinction internally within *kodshei mizbe'ach*, but instead contrasts the entire category of *kodshei mizbe'ach* with *kodshei bedek ha-bayis*.

The two approaches of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish thus represent fundamentally different frameworks for understanding the middle status of sanctified property in *ir nidachas* — property that is neither actively destroyed nor simply walked out of the city. For Rabbi Yochanan, such property is genuinely exempt from *ir nidachas* but subject to some other restriction that prevents its normal use. For Reish Lakish, such property genuinely carries the *halachah* of *ir nidachas*, but its sanctified status earns it a less direct form of destruction.

## Bechor v'Ma'aser

### Behemtecha Lo Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״ – וְלֹא בֶּהֱמַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Shimon says** that the term **"its animals"** serves to **exclude a firstborn animal and** animal **tithe.**

Rabbi Shimon rules: *behemtecha* — "your animals" — *v'lo bechor u-ma'aser* — excludes *bechor* and *ma'aser* from the destruction mandated for the animals of *ir nidachas*. The *gemara* asks: under what circumstances? If we are speaking of unblemished *bechor* and *ma'aser* — *tmimim* — they are *shlal shamayim* and would be exempt in any case. If we are speaking of blemished animals — *ba'alei mumin* — then once blemished they can simply be eaten at home and are entirely in the owner's domain, in which case they should be destroyed along with the rest of the owner's property. Under which scenario does the *psuek* of *behemtecha* actually do meaningful work?

### Mi She'ne'echal Betorat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּתְמִימִין – שְׁלַל שָׁמַיִם הוּא! אֶלָּא בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין – שְׁלָלָהּ נִינְהוּ? אָמַר רָבִינָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין, וּמִי שֶׁנֶּאֱכָל בְּתוֹרַת בְּהֶמְתָּהּ. יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִין בְּתוֹרַת בְּהֶמְתָּהּ, אֶלָּא בְּתוֹרַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** animals **are we dealing with? If we say** that Rabbi Shimon is speaking **with regard to unblemished animals,** like all other offerings **they are the spoils of Heaven** and not the spoils of the inhabitants of the city. **Rather,** Rabbi Shimon is speaking **with regard to blemished** animals, which belong to their owners. If so, **they are the spoils of** the city, and what is the reason that Rabbi Shimon says that there is no obligation to destroy these animals? **Ravina says: Actually,** Rabbi Shimon is speaking **with regard to blemished** animals, **and** his statement should be understood as follows: The mitzva is to destroy that **which is eaten as its animals,** i.e., as the property of an inhabitant of an idolatrous city. **Excluded are those** firstborn and tithe animals, **which,** even when blemished, **are not eaten as its animals; rather,** they are eaten **as firstborn and tithe** animals. They are given as gifts to priests, and **are** considered **the spoils of Heaven.**

Ravina resolves this: *le'olam be-va'alei mumin* — indeed we are speaking of blemished animals, and yet they are exempt. The verse *behemtecha* conveys that only something eaten entirely in the manner of one's ordinary animal — *betorat behemtecha* — is subject to the *ir nidachas* destruction. *Yatzu elu she-einam ne'echalin be-torat behemtecha ella be-torat bechor u-ma'aser* — these are excluded because even when consumed outside the *beis ha-mikdash*, they are still eaten in the capacity of *bechor* and *ma'aser*, not as ordinary meat.

### Betorat Bechor u'Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַכֹּל קָרֵב וְהַכֹּל נִפְדֶּה. מַאי קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל שֶׁקָּרֵב כְּשֶׁהוּא תָּם, וְנִפְדֶּה כְּשֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם – מִ״שְּׁלָלָהּ״ אִימְּעִיט, וְכׇל שֶׁקָּרֵב כְּשֶׁהוּא תָּם וְאֵינוֹ נִפְדָּה כְּשֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם, כְּגוֹן בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר – מִ״בְּהֶמְתָּהּ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this *halakha* **disputes** the statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says: Everything is sacrificed and everything is redeemed.** The Gemara asks: **What is he saying?** The Gemara explains that **this** is what **he is saying: Any** offering **that is sacrificed** on the altar **when it is unblemished and is redeemed when it is blemished is excluded from** the term "its **spoils,"** since it is considered the spoils of Heaven. **And** the *halakha* concerning **any** offering **that is sacrificed** on the altar **when it is unblemished and is not redeemed when it is blemished, e.g., a firstborn** animal **and** animal **tithe, is derived from** the term "its **animals,"** as they are not the city's animals and they are not included in the city's property.

*Rashi* explains this as referring to how one characterizes the animal when eating it — it retains the name and status of *bechor* or *ma'aser* even when consumed at one's own table. A *gemara* elsewhere adds that there are restrictions on how the meat of *bechor* and *ma'aser* may be sold even when blemished, indicating that these animals do not become fully unrestricted property even after acquiring a blemish. The owner does not have complete dominion over them. They therefore do not qualify as *behemtecha* in the full sense, and are consequently excluded from the *ir nidachas* destruction even in their blemished state.

This reading of Ravina stands in tension with how *Shmuel* understood Rabbi Shimon's position. *Shmuel* explained Rabbi Shimon as follows: *kol she-karev k'she-hu tam v'nifdeh k'she-hu ba'al mum mi-shalal yatza* — anything that, when unblemished, is offered as a *korban*, and when blemished must be redeemed, falls into the category of *shlal shamayim* and is exempt from *ir nidachas*. On this reading, the entire basis for exempting a *korban* is that it is *shlal shamayim*, not the *behemtecha* verse. Rabbi Shimon needed *behemtecha* specifically to exempt *bechor* and *ma'aser* even when *tmimim* — because *kol she-karev k'she-hu tam v'eino nifdeh k'she-hu ba'al mum*, something that is offered when unblemished but does not require redemption when blemished, is not truly *shlal shamayim* in the full sense. Such an offering has a weaker *kedushah* — when blemished it can simply be eaten without any formal process of redemption — and therefore even when unblemished it is not sufficiently *shel shamayim* to be categorically exempt. The *behemtecha* verse is thus needed to exclude it from *ir nidachas* on independent grounds. The practical difference between Ravina and *Shmuel*'s reading: for *Shmuel*, blemished *bechor* and *ma'aser* would in fact be subject to *ir nidachas* destruction since they are no longer *shlal shamayim* and *behemtecha* was needed only for the unblemished case. For Ravina, even blemished *bechor* and *ma'aser* are exempt because they are never eaten as fully ordinary meat.

## Terumah Yirakev

### Terumah B'yad Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמוֹת יֵרָקְבוּ. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, כֵּיוָן דְּמָמוֹנֵיהּ הוּא, תִּשָּׂרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* continues: ***Terumot* must** be left to **decay. Rav Ḥisda says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to ***teruma*** that is still **in the possession of an Israelite,** who has not yet given it to a priest. **But** concerning ***teruma*** that is already **in the possession of a priest** who lives in the idolatrous city, **since it is his property, it shall be burned.**

The *baraita* states that *terumah* in the *ir nidachas* should be left to rot — *yerakevu*. The question is why this middle path is necessary. If *terumah* is *shlal shamayim*, it should simply be removed from the city and given to a *kohen*; if it is not, it should be actively destroyed.

### Terumah B'yad Kohen

Rav Chisda provides a distinction. In the version first presented by the *gemara*: the ruling that *terumah* is left to rot applies only when it is *terumah b'yad Yisrael* — in the hands of a *Yisrael* who has not yet given it to a *kohen*. But *terumah b'yad kohen* — once it has been given to a *kohen* who can eat it — is considered *mamona hu*, the *kohen*'s actual property, and therefore it would be burned and actively destroyed like other property of *ir nidachas* residents. *Rashi* explains the middle status of *terumah b'yad Yisrael* as follows: since the *terumah* might eventually have been given to a *kohen* in the *ir nidachas* itself, thereby becoming that *kohen*'s property and subject to destruction, it cannot simply be walked out of the city. Yet since it is currently *shlal shamayim*, it is not actively destroyed either — hence it is left to rot.

### Tinasein L'Kohen Acheres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ יִגָּנְזוּ. וְהָא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל כִּתְרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן דָּמֵי, וְקָתָנֵי יִגָּנְזוּ! אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל תִּנָּתֵן לְכֹהֵן שֶׁבְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection** from the mishna: **Second tithe and sacred scrolls must be interred. But isn't** the status of **second tithe in the possession of an Israelite like** that of ***teruma* in the possession of a priest,** as he may partake of it and benefit from it in Jerusalem; **and** nevertheless, **it is taught:** They **must be interred? Rather, if** the statement of Rav Ḥisda **was stated, this is what was stated: Rav Ḥisda says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to ***teruma*** that is already **in the possession of a priest** who lives in the idolatrous city, as even though it is his property, it maintains the sanctity of *teruma*. **But *teruma*** that is still **in the possession of an Israelite,** which is not his property at all, **shall be given to a priest who is in another city,** and should not be left to decay.

The *gemara* then challenges this from a comparison with *ma'aser sheni*: the *baraita* states that *ma'aser sheni* and sacred scriptures must be buried. Since *ma'aser sheni* in the hands of a *Yisrael* is analogous to *terumah* in the hands of a *kohen* — the *Yisrael* who holds *ma'aser sheni* is the one entitled to eat it — yet the *baraita* says to bury it rather than burn it, it seems that even property that is in the hands of its rightful consumer does not automatically fall into the category of property to be actively destroyed. The *gemara* resolves this by reversing Rav Chisda's formulation. The corrected version: *lo shanu ella terumah b'yad kohen* — the ruling that *terumah* must be left to rot applies specifically when it is in the hands of a *kohen*; *aval terumah b'yad Yisrael*, when it is still in the hands of a *Yisrael*, it is entirely *shlal shamayim* and entirely exempt, and *tinasein l'kohen she-be-ir acheres* — it should simply be given to a *kohen* in another city.

### Middle Status Ambiguity

The question of what justifies the middle status — letting something rot or bury rather than either removing it freely or actively destroying it — remains genuinely unresolved in the text. Whether it is because property is fundamentally exempt but practically compromised, as in Rabbi Yochanan's framework, or because it genuinely falls under *ir nidachas* but is treated more gently because of its sanctified character, as in Reish Lakish's framework, applies equally here as it did in the discussion of *kodshei mizbe'ach*. The *gemara* breaks off before reaching a final resolution of these questions, leaving them open for the continuation on the following *daf*.
