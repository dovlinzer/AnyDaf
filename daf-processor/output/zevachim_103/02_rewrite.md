# Zevachim 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Skin & Meat Entitlement

### Zachah Principle

The daf opens at the top of 103a, where the *mishnah* pivots from the question of who is entitled to portions of the meat to the closely related question of entitlement to the skin — the hide of the animal, which clearly represented significant monetary value. The *mishnah* establishes a foundational principle: *kol she-lo zachah ha-mizbeiach bi-vesarah, lo zachu ha-kohanim be-orah* — whenever the altar does not merit the flesh, the *kohanim* do not merit the skin.

The underlying assumption, which the *gemara* will make explicit, is that the skin is distributed together with the meat: just as a *kohen* who was *tamei* or otherwise disqualified from receiving meat loses his share in the meat, so too does he lose his share in the skin. The *mishnah* is therefore addressing a more fundamental question — namely, when do the *kohanim* as a class forfeit their collective entitlement to the skin entirely? In such cases, the skin would presumably be sold, with the proceeds going to the *Beis HaMikdash*.

The vehicle through which the question is understood is the *olah*, where the flesh belongs to the *mizbeiach* rather than to the *kohanim*. If something occurred to invalidate the sacrifice, so that the meat may not be placed on the altar at all, then the *kohanim* have no claim to the skin either.

### Psul Before Zerikah

Rashi clarifies the operative principle with precision: *kegon she-ira bo pasul kodem zerikah* — for instance, if an invalidation occurred before the blood was thrown. The paradigm case is *shechita* performed with a *piggul* intention. The concept of *zachah* here does not mean that the flesh was actually placed on the fire; it means that the flesh merited being placed on the fire. That determination is made at the moment of *zerikah* — for it is the throwing of the blood that renders the flesh eligible to be burned on the *mizbeiach*. If at the moment the blood was thrown the sacrifice was valid, then the *mizbeiach* was *zocheh* in the flesh — even if the flesh was never actually burned, as in a case of *nosar* where the meat was left over past its time. Conversely, if the sacrifice was invalidated before the blood was thrown, there was never a moment at which the meat could have ascended to the altar, and the *kohanim* therefore have no claim to the skin.

### Tame & Neveilah Cases

One might ask about cases where the invalidity is not inherent to the *korban* as such but has affected it contingently. For example: what if the entire flesh became *tamei* before the blood was thrown? That is not a structural defect in the *korban* in the way that *piggul* is — one could have imagined that only part of the meat became *tamei*, leaving the remainder valid. The *gemara* does not resolve this question here, and it is left aside.

The clearer cases are those of all-or-nothing invalidity. If the animal died on its own before *shechita* and became a *neveilah*, the meat was never eligible for the *mizbeiach*, and the *kohanim* do not receive the skin. On the other hand, if the entire meat became *tamei* after *zerikah*, the *mizbeiach* had already merited the flesh at the moment the blood was thrown. Remarkably, in such a case the *kohanim* do receive the skin. The procedure would be to first flay the animal — giving the skin to the *kohanim* — and only then burn the invalid meat. Similarly, if the meat became *nosar* after *zerikah*, the *mizbeiach* had already been *zocheh* in the flesh, and the *kohanim* retain their entitlement to the skin.

### Olat Ish Concept

The *mishnah* then derives this from the verse (Vayikra 7:8): *vehakohen hamakriv et olat ish or ha-olah asher hikriv lakohein lo yihyeh* — the skin of the *olah* that the *kohen* brings shall be his. The phrase *olat ish* — a person's *olah* — implies that the offering must have *counted for someone* at the time of *zerikah*.

However, this *derashah* sits in some tension with the subsequent ruling: *af al pi shelo altah libe'alim,orah la-kohanim* — even if the sacrifice was not counted toward the owner's obligation, the *kohanim* still receive the skin. This means that an *olah* slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — not for its proper designation — which is *kasher* but does not fulfill the owner's duty, still grants the *kohanim* their claim to the skin. This appears to cut against the *derashah* that the *korban* must have "counted for someone." What the *derashah* is really targeting is whether the *korban* was valid — not whether it was attributed to a specific individual. The *gemara* will explain how this is all reconciled through the interplay of the phrase *olat ish* with the alternative formulation *or ha-olah*.

The *mishnah* continues: *echad olat ha-ish ve-echad olat ha-ishah, oram la-kohanim* — whether it is a man's *olah* or a woman's, the *kohanim* receive the skin. The word *ish* in the verse is not to be read restrictively. *Orot kodashim kalim li-ve'alim* — skins of *kodashim kalim* such as *shelamim* go to the owners, who receive the meat. *Orot kodshei kodashim la-kohanim* — and the skins of *kodshei kodashim* go to the *kohanim*.

## Korban Types & Skin

### Distribution by Type

Having established that the *kohanim* receive the skin of an *olah* — despite the fact that the flesh itself goes entirely to the *mizbeiach* — the *mishnah* proceeds to address the other categories of *korbanot*. The rule is crisp: *orot kodashim kalim li-ve'alim* and *orot kodshei kodashim la-kohanim*. The owners of *kodashim kalim*, such as *shelamim*, receive the skin together with their portion of the flesh. The *kohanim*, who receive the flesh of *kodshei kodashim* such as *chatat* and *asham*, likewise receive the skin.

### Lev 7:8 Citation

The source for the *kohanim*'s entitlement to the skin of the *olah* is the verse in Vayikra 7:8: *vehakohen hamakriv et olat ish, or ha-olah asher hikriv, lakohein lo yihyeh* — the *kohen* who offers a person's *olah*: the skin of the *olah* that he has brought shall be his. The verse specifies only the *olah*, yet the *mishnah* extends the ruling to *kodshei kodashim* as well. This extension requires justification, which the *mishnah* provides through a *kal vachomer*.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

The *kal vachomer* reasoning embedded in the *mishnah* is striking for appearing in this form in a *mishnah* itself: *umah im olah shelo zachah bi-vesarah zachah be-orah* — if even by an *olah*, where the *kohanim* do not receive the flesh (since it goes entirely to the *mizbeiach*), they nonetheless receive the skin, then *kodshei kodashim* — where the *kohanim* do receive the flesh of a *chatat* or *asham* — is it not all the more so clear that they should receive the skin?

### Mizbeiach Counterpoint

One might attempt to refute this *kal vachomer* by pointing to the *mizbeiach* itself: the altar receives the flesh of the *olah* but not the skin — does this not demonstrate that one can receive flesh without necessarily receiving the corresponding skin? The *mishnah* preempts this: *ain mizbeiach yokhiach* — the *mizbeiach* is no proof. The *mizbeiach* never receives the skin under any circumstances. There is no halachic category in which the skin of an animal is burned on the *mizbeiach*. Therefore, the *mizbeiach* can say nothing about the relationship between entitlement to flesh and entitlement to skin. When we ask whether someone entitled to the flesh is also entitled to the skin, we consider only entities that can in principle receive the skin — and there the *kal vachomer* holds: if the *kohanim* receive the skin even where they have no claim to the flesh, they certainly receive it where they do have a claim to the flesh.

## Olat Ish Exclusions

### Olat Hekdesh Exclusion

A *baraisa* introduces two cryptic exclusions from the rule that the *kohanim* receive the skin. Rabbi Yehuda derives from the phrase *olat ish* — a person's *olah* — that the *halacha* applies only to a private individual's *olah*, excluding *olat ha-hekdesh*, an *olah* belonging to the Temple itself. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda reads the phrase differently and excludes *olat geirim* — the *olah* of converts. Both readings appear puzzling at first glance, and the *gemara* will clarify them.

Regarding *olat ha-hekdesh*: Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef explains that the case in question is an *olah* that comes from *motarot* — surplus funds originally designated for an *asham* or *chatat*. The scenario is familiar: someone sanctified an *asham*, it became lost or otherwise unavailable, and they brought another; when the original was found, it was allowed to develop a blemish, redeemed, and the proceeds used for an *olah*. Similarly, surplus *chatat* funds — where money designated for a *chatat* was not entirely used, or where replacement funds were used and the original funds were found — would be redirected into an *olah*. These *olahs* are communal, belonging to the *Beis HaMikdash* treasury rather than to any private individual, and therefore the *kohanim* do not receive the skin from them; instead, the *Beis HaMikdash* retains it.

### Motarot Funds Debate

The *gemara* immediately notes a complication: this explanation works well according to the opinion that *motarot* — surplus *asham* and *chatat* funds — go toward communal *nidvas tzibbur* offerings. But what of the opinion that such funds go toward a private *nidvas yachid* offering? In that case, the *olah* brought from these repurposed funds is the individual's own private *olah*, not a communal one. If so, what is there to exclude?

Rava resolves this: the exclusion can still function, but it is derived not from the word *ish* contrasted with *hekdesh* (private versus communal), but from the word *ha-olah* — with the definite article. A parallel *derashah* exists: *ha-olah — olah rishonah*, meaning only the primary designation as an *olah* qualifies, not something that has been converted into an *olah* from redirected funds. Just as there is a *derashah* that the definite article in *ha-olah* indicates the primacy of the *olat tamid*, so here it teaches that the *kohanim*'s entitlement to the skin applies only to an *olah* originally sanctified as such — not one that is an *olah* only because the designated funds were repurposed. Even if the repurposed *olah* is technically that person's private offering, since it derives from funds diverted from a different purpose, the *halacha* granting the *kohanim* the skin does not apply. In such a case, the *Beis HaMikdash* retains the skin.

The *gemara* also confirms: when the *baraisa* discusses six of the Temple's thirteen donation chests being used for such *motarot* funds, Rabbi Yehuda adds parenthetically that the *kohanim* do not receive the skin of those communal *olahs*. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

However, Rabbi Nechemia — or, according to another version, Rabbi Shimon — challenges Rabbi Yehuda: if so, you have nullified the *midrash* of Yehoyada HaKohen, the ruling derived from the verse in the narrative of Yehoyada the *kohen* during the First Temple period. The verse reads: *kesef asham ve-chesef chatat lo yuva Beis Hashem, la-kohanim yihyu* — the money of the *asham* and the money of the *chatat* shall not be brought into the House of God; it shall belong to the *kohanim*. This implies that even when the funds of an *asham* or *chatat* are redirected, the *kohanim* still receive a portion.

The *gemara* provides the *midrash* of Yehoyada HaKohen: *asham hu asham asham la-Hashem* — the verse in the Torah speaks of the *asham* as both belonging to a person and as an *asham* to God. When the *asham* funds are repurposed into an *olah*, the flesh becomes God's (*asham la-Hashem*), but the skin — *orah la-kohanim*. The communal *olahs* funded by *motarot* are described as *basar la-Shem* and *or la-kohanim*, affirming that even communal *olah* offerings generated from repurposed *asham* or *chatat* funds grant the *kohanim* the skin.

In the face of this challenge, Rabbi Yehuda apparently fell silent and retracted his position. Rav Nachman accordingly needs a different reading of *olat ha-hekdesh*.

### Bedek Habayis Sanctifica…

Rabbi Abba in the name of Rabbi Yannai offers a distinct case. *Prat l'matfis olah l'vedek ha-bayis* — the exclusion applies to a case where someone sanctified an animal as an *olah* and then, on top of that, additionally sanctified it as a gift to the Temple treasury (*bedek ha-bayis*). This seems paradoxical: if the animal is already an *olah*, what remains to give to the temple treasury? Everything is going onto the *mizbeiach*. Is the second sanctification even meaningful? The *gemara* explains that the standard practice is to redeem the animal before it is slaughtered — the person would pay the animal's value to the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the animal would then proceed to be offered as an *olah*. But the question is whether, biblically, the second sanctification accomplished anything at all.

### Skin Ownership After Red…

The *gemara* now analyzes the skin's status with particular care. There are two positions regarding whether *kodshei vedek ha-bayis* — the sanctification for the Temple treasury — takes halachic effect when the animal was already an *olah*:

According to the view that *kedushas bedek ha-bayis* does take hold biblically (*tafsei mid'oraisa*), then the animal genuinely belongs to the Temple Treasury at some point, and this is sufficient to remove the skin from the *kohanim*'s entitlement.

But even according to the view that *kedushas bedek ha-bayis* does not take hold biblically in such a case, the *gemara* points out: *hani mili basar, aval or tafis* — that limitation applies only to the flesh, which was already consecrated as an *olah* and heading to the *mizbeiach*. The skin, however, is a different matter. When an animal is consecrated as an *olah*, it is the interior — the flesh — that is the *olah*. The skin surrounding it is not itself the *olah*; it is the skin of an *olah*, which will be given to the *kohanim* once the sacrifice is completed. Since the skin is not yet the *olah* at the time the animal is walking around before *shechita*, it can be sanctified to *bedek ha-bayis* even according to the more restrictive view. Therefore, when this person subsequently sanctified the animal to *bedek ha-bayis*, the skin — still part of the live animal — was validly acquired by the Temple Treasury.

The striking implication, as the *gemara* develops it, is that even after the animal has been redeemed, slaughtered, and offered — even after the act of redemption that preceded the *shechita* — since there was a moment at which the skin was owned by the Temple Treasury, the *kohanim* do not receive it. This is the *chiddush*: it is not only that one could slaughter the animal with the skin already designated for *bedek ha-bayis* (which would be obvious), but that even once the formal process of redemption and *shechita* has occurred, the prior ownership by *bedek ha-bayis* is sufficient to deny the *kohanim* their usual entitlement.

Rav Nachman accordingly locates *olat ha-hekdesh* — the *olah* whose skin goes to the Temple rather than the *kohanim* — in this second scenario: an animal sanctified as an *olah* with a subsequent *hekdesh* to *bedek ha-bayis*. The first scenario (communal *olahs* from *motarot*) is disqualified as a reading, since we have established that even communal *olahs* grant the *kohanim* the skin, per the *midrash* of Yehoyada HaKohen.

In fact, as the *gemara* makes clear, Rav Nachman concludes that the correct reading is altogether elsewhere: *mukmina lah b'makdish nechasav v'aliba d'Rabbi Yehoshua* — we locate the case in the scenario of one who sanctifies all his property (*hamakdish nechasav*), following Rabbi Yehoshua's position. That scenario is the subject of the next section.

## Property Sanctification

### Hamakdish Nechasav

The *mishnah* introduces the case of *hamakdish nechasav* — one who sanctifies all his property. If among his possessions were unblemished animals capable of serving as *korbanot*, what is their status? On the one hand, the general declaration of sanctification (*hakdashah*) is conventionally understood as dedicating property to *bedek ha-bayis* — the Temple treasury. On the other hand, these animals are fit for the *mizbeiach*, and one might interpret the sanctification differently with respect to them.

### R. Eliezer View

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *zecharim yimachru li-tzorchei olos* — the males shall be sold to those who need to bring *olahs*, and *nekevos yimachru li-tzorchei zivchei shelamim* — the females shall be sold to those who need to bring *shelamim*. The animals remain under the Temple's ownership, but because they are unblemished and consecrable, the *hekdesh* will not simply sell them for non-sacrificial use. Instead, the Temple will sell them at a discount to individuals already obligated to bring those respective *korbanot*, with the condition that the purchaser commits to using them for the appropriate offering. The Temple thus receives the sale proceeds into its treasury, and the animals still end up being offered on the *mizbeiach* as intended. *Ve-demeihem yiplu im she'ar nechasim le-vedek ha-bayis* — the proceeds of these sales join the rest of the dedicated funds in the Temple treasury.

### R. Yehoshua View

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and reads the act of sanctification contextually. Although the same word — *makdish* — is used for all the property, its meaning is determined by the nature of the object being sanctified. For one's house, furniture, or other chattels, *makdish* means dedicating to the treasury. For unblemished male animals, Rabbi Yehoshua reads *makdish* as sanctifying them as an *olah* outright: *zecharim atzman yikrevu olos* — the males themselves shall be offered as *olahs*. This contextual interpretation tracks the principle that the word *makdish* always conveys dedication to the Temple in some sense; for an *olah*, however, the property goes entirely to the Temple through the *mizbeiach* rather than through the treasury.

The females present a difficulty. One might suggest they are sanctified as *shelamim*, but that would mean the owner retains much of the meat — which is insufficiently "Temple-oriented" to be consistent with the declared intent to sanctify everything. Rabbi Yehoshua therefore holds: *yimachru li-tzorchei shelamim*, they are sold to those needing to bring *shelamim*. But unlike Rabbi Eliezer, who deposits those proceeds in the treasury, Rabbi Yehoshua holds *ve-yavi bi-demeihem olos* — the proceeds are themselves used to bring *olahs*. The word *makdish*, even for the females, carries the sense of directing toward an *olah* in the fullest possible way: the females cannot be *olahs* themselves, but their proceeds become *olahs*. The rest of the property — the house, the car, the non-animal assets — *yiplu le-vedek ha-bayis*, falls to the Temple treasury.

### Skin as Bedek Habayis

Even according to Rabbi Yehoshua, who reads the sanctification of unblemished males as an *olah*, there is a significant qualification: *hani mili basar, aval or tafis* — that reading applies to the flesh, but the skin is still captured by *bedek ha-bayis*.

The logic is as follows. When the person said "I am sanctifying everything," every item defaulted to *bedek ha-bayis* unless contextual interpretation overrode it. For unblemished males, the contextual interpretation redirected the flesh to the *mizbeiach* as an *olah*. But as noted above in the context of Rabbi Yannai's ruling, the skin of a living animal designated as an *olah* is not itself the *olah* — it is the skin surrounding the *olah*. Therefore, the original force of the declaration — *bedek ha-bayis* — extends as far as it can, and it reaches the skin. Only when the contextual interpretation is at its strongest — with respect to the flesh heading to the *mizbeiach* — does it override the default. The skin, which was never explicitly covered by the contextual reinterpretation, remains dedicated to *bedek ha-bayis*.

This, then, is the case of *olat ha-hekdesh* that excludes the *kohanim* from receiving the skin: not a communal *olah* from *motarot* (which the *midrash* of Yehoyada HaKohen shows does grant the *kohanim* the skin), not merely an animal with a double sanctification (though that works too under Rabbi Yannai's ruling), but specifically the case of *hamakdish nechasav* according to Rabbi Yehoshua, where the default dedication to *bedek ha-bayis* applies to the skin even as the flesh is redirected toward the *mizbeiach*.

Summarizing the three possible accounts of *olat ha-hekdesh*: the simplest reading — that it refers to communal *olahs* from surplus *chatat* and *asham* funds — was rejected because Yehoyada HaKohen's *midrash* shows that even those *olahs* grant the *kohanim* the skin. The remaining two positions are variations on a single theme: an animal is an *olah* but the skin is *bedek ha-bayis*. This can arise either because an already-consecrated *olah* was subsequently dedicated to *bedek ha-bayis* (per Rabbi Yannai), or because a general sanctification of all property was contextually interpreted as an *olah* for the flesh but as *bedek ha-bayis* for the skin (per Rabbi Yehoshua's reading of *hamakdish nechasav*).

## Ish Filtering & Merit

### Ish Inclusivity

A *baraisa* now addresses the word *ish* in *olat ish* directly. The verse specifies an *olat ish* — a man's *olah*. Does this exclude women, slaves, and converts from granting the *kohanim* their skin entitlement? The *baraisa* states: *ein li ela olat ish* — I might have thought only a man's *olah* qualifies. How do I include women's and slaves' *olahs*? The verse adds *or ha-olah* — the skin of the *olah* — using the definite article and dropping the word *ish*, thereby broadening the scope: any *olah*, regardless of who brought it, grants the *kohanim* the skin. The word *ish* is thus not a restriction on who brings the *olah* in terms of the person's category — women, slaves, and converts are all included.

### Olah She'alah L'ish

If so, what work does the word *ish* do? The *baraisa* answers: *olah she-altah le-ish* — the *olah* must have counted as a valid offering for a person. The *ish* language introduces a qualitative criterion, not a categorical one. It must have been a legitimate, valid *korban* at the time of *zerikah*. *Prat she-nishchata chutz li-zmanah ve-chutz li-mkomah* — this excludes an *olah* slaughtered with an intention that renders it *piggul*. Such an offering, though it goes through the motions, never truly "counted" for anyone, and the *kohanim* do not receive the skin.

### Shelo Lishmah Challenge

This raises a further question: if *olah she-altah le-ish* is the operative criterion, then perhaps an *olah* slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — not for its designated name — should likewise be excluded? Such an *olah* is *kasher* and fulfills the technical requirements of the service, but it does not count toward the owner's obligation. One might argue that it too has not "counted" for the person in the relevant sense.

### Or Ha'olah Principle

The *baraisa* counters with the verse's second formulation: *or ha-olah* — the skin of the *olah*, without the limiting word *ish*. This phrase teaches that any valid *olah*, regardless of whether it was attributed to a specific person's obligation, grants the *kohanim* the skin. The interplay between *olat ish* and *or ha-olah* is thus precise: the former excludes genuinely invalid *korbanot* (like *piggul*); the latter restores entitlement for offerings that are valid but not credited to anyone. The real exclusion, in other words, is invalidity — not lack of attribution. As long as the *korban* was a legitimate *olah*, the *kohanim* receive the skin, even if it was offered *shelo lishmah*.

## Rabbi Yishmael Position

### Asher Hikriv Drasha

A further *baraisa* addresses the derivation of *kohanim*'s skin rights for *kodshei kodashim*. The *tanna kamma* derives this from the phrase *asher hikriv* — "that he brought" — which appears in the verse alongside *or ha-olah*. The force of *asher hikriv* broadens the rule beyond *olah* to any *korban* one brings. This is then balanced against the word *olah* to limit the extension to offerings that are *olah*-like in status — specifically, *kodshei kodashim*.

### Natural Unity Assumption

Rabbi Yishmael proposes instead a *kal vachomer*: if the *kohanim* receive the skin of an *olah* — where they have no entitlement to the flesh — then *kodshei kodashim*, where they do receive the flesh, certainly grant them the skin as well. This does not require *asher hikriv* as a textual source; logic alone suffices. Rabbi Yishmael then uses *asher hikriv* for a different purpose, which we will encounter shortly.

### Flesh-Skin Unity Examples

Rabbi (Rebbi) goes further still. For him, the premise that skin follows flesh is not derived at all — it is simply the natural baseline. The *Torah* treats the skin as part of the same unit as the flesh unless it explicitly separates them. This assumption is bolstered by the cases of *parim ha-nisrafim* — the inner *chata'ot* whose carcasses are burned outside the *Beis HaMikdash* — where the *Torah* explicitly states that they are burned along with their skins. For *chatat*, *asham*, and *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* — offerings where the *matanah* goes to the *kohen* — since the *Torah* never instructs flaying, the skin goes together with the flesh as a matter of course. The *kohanim* receive the entire carcass and may choose whether to flay it themselves or eat it as-is off the skin.

Similarly for *kodashim kalim* belonging to the owners: since the *Torah* never mandates flaying, the owners receive the full animal including its skin and may handle it as they see fit.

### Rabbi's Specification Vi…

According to Rabbi, the only case requiring explicit *Torah* specification is the *olah*, where the verse *ve-hifshit et ha-olah ve-nitteach otah li-netacheha* — flay the *olah* and cut it into its pieces — actively separates the skin from the flesh. Once separated, one might not have known what to do with the skin. Therefore the *Torah* states explicitly: *or ha-olah asher hikriv la-kohen ha-makriv otah lo yihyeh* — the skin of the *olah* that was brought belongs to the *kohen* who offered it. That verse was needed only for the *olah*. Everything else follows automatically from the baseline assumption that skin accompanies flesh.

## Service Fitness Requirem…

### Excluded Categories

The section of the *baraisa* that addresses who among the *kohanim* is entitled to the skin is anchored in the verse *ve-ha-kohen ha-makriv et olat ish* — the *kohen* who brings the *olah*. This phrase links the entitlement to the capacity to perform the service (*avodah*). A *tuvul yom* (one who immersed that day but whose purification is not yet complete), an *onen* (one in acute mourning before burial), and a *mechusar kipurim* (one who still lacks atonement) are excluded from receiving their portion.

### Consumption vs. Non-Cons…

One might have thought the exclusion applied only to the meat — since the disqualification of these *kohanim* from service is related to their inability to eat consecrated food — but that perhaps they retain a claim to the skin, which is not for consumption. The *baraisa* counters with the phrase *lo yihyeh*: it is the *kohen ha-makriv* — the one fit to perform the service — who receives the skin, just as he receives the meat. The fitness requirement for the skin tracks the fitness requirement for the *avodah*, not the fitness for *achilah*. A *kohen* unfit to perform the service receives neither meat nor skin.

### Bones & Skin Rights

Rabbi Yishmael, who uses *asher hikriv* to exclude *tuvul yom*, *mechusar kipurim*, and *onen* (rather than deriving this from *lo yihyeh*), has a use for *lo yihyeh* instead. Rabbi Yochanan reports in his name: *ne'emar be-olah lo yihyeh ve-ne'emar be-asham lo yihyeh* — the phrase *lo yihyeh* appears in the verse about the *olah* and also in the verse about the *asham*. This verbal parallel creates a *gezeirah shavah* linking the two. Just as with an *asham* the *kohen* receives the flesh and therefore obviously keeps the bones as well, so too with the *olah* — even though the flesh goes to the *mizbeiach* — *atzmotav mutarim*, the bones are permitted to the *kohen*. The *kohen* may take home the bones of the *olah* and make use of them.

The *lo yihyeh* in the *olah* verse is indeed superfluous (*mufni*), since the verse already establishes the *kohen*'s entitlement to the skin. Were it not superfluous, a logical challenge would block the *gezeirah shavah*: the *asham* entitles the *kohen* to eat the flesh, and naturally its bones too; the *olah*, where the *kohen* receives no flesh, should presumably not entitle him to the bones. But since *lo yihyeh* is an extra word, it forges the connection despite the logical asymmetry — teaching that not only does the *kohen* receive the skin of the *olah*, but the bones as well.

## Timing of Skin Entitleme…

### Psul Before/After Flaying

The subsequent *mishnah* introduces a different framework for determining when the *kohanim* lose their skin entitlement. Rather than focusing on whether the invalidity occurred before or after *zerikah* — which was the implicit standard of the earlier *mishnah* — this *mishnah* makes the pivotal moment the act of *hefshot*, the flaying itself. *Kol ha-kodashim she-ira bahem pasul kodem le-hefshetan* — all consecrated animals that became invalid before they were flayed: *einam la-kohanim*, the *kohanim* do not receive the skin. *Le-achar hefshatan* — if they became invalid after they were flayed: *hareiy hen shel kohanim*, the skin belongs to the *kohanim*.

The logic underlying this framework is that as long as the skin is still attached to the flesh, they form a single unit. If the *korban* becomes invalid while the skin is still on, the skin shares the fate of the flesh. Once the skin has been physically separated through flaying, it is an independent entity, and any subsequent invalidity of the flesh does not retroactively affect the skin's status.

### R. Chanina Sgan View

Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim — the deputy *kohen gadol* — testifies: *miyamai lo ra'iti or she-yetzei le-veis ha-sreifah* — in all my days in the Temple, I never saw a flayed skin taken out to be burned. This testimony is significant because it implies that the *mishnah*'s framework is correct: once a skin has been flayed, it always goes to the *kohanim*, never to the fire. The expected consequence of an invalid *korban* — burning the flesh — never extended to a separately flayed skin.

One might assume Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim is simply affirming the *mishnah*'s position. The *gemara*, however, identifies a deeper *chiddush* in his testimony. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* holds that if an invalidity is discovered after flaying that was actually present from *before* the flaying — for instance, an animal was flayed and only then discovered to have been a *treifah* — the skin would be burned, since the invalidity predates the flaying. Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim disagrees: once a skin has been physically flayed and separated, the *kohanim* receive it regardless of when the invalidity occurred. Rabbi Akiva makes this explicit: *midvarav lamadnu she-ha-mafshit et ha-bechor ve-nimtza treifah, she-yoshu ha-kohanim ha-or* — from his words we derive that if one flays a *bechor* and it is then discovered to have been a *treifah*, the *kohanim* may still use the skin.

Thus the *mishnah* and Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim represent two distinct positions: the *mishnah* focuses on when the invalidity *existed* (before or after flaying), while Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim focuses on the physical act of separation — once separated, the skin is always the *kohanim*'s.

### Chachamim Counterpoint

The *chachamim* push back against Rabbi Chanina Sgan Hakohanim's proof from his personal experience: *ein lo ra'inu re'aya* — absence of evidence is not proof of absence. The fact that he never witnessed a separately flayed skin being burned does not establish that burning such a skin is halachically impossible or never occurred. Perhaps the scenario simply never arose during his tenure. The *chachamim* therefore maintain that if the invalidity was present before the flaying — even if discovered only after — the skin is burned along with the flesh: *yetzei le-veis ha-sreifah*.

### Zerikah vs. Flaying

This *mishnah*'s framework — invalidity before or after flaying — stands in apparent tension with the earlier *mishnah*'s framework, which made *zerikah* the operative moment. The earlier *mishnah* held: if the *korban* was invalid at the time the blood was thrown, the *kohanim* do not receive the skin; if valid at *zerikah*, they do. This suggests that even if the skin had already been flayed before *zerikah* — a theoretically possible though practically unlikely sequence — the skin's fate is still determined by the validity of the *korban* at *zerikah*. The later *mishnah*, however, makes flaying the operative moment: if flayed before invalidity, the *kohanim* receive it.

The *gemara* formulates the contradiction directly: according to the earlier *mishnah*, even if the skin was flayed before *zerikah*, an invalid *zerikah* (such as *piggul*) would deny the *kohanim* the skin — *af al gav d'afshateih le-or kodem zerikah*. This is consistent with the position that the blood-throwing's validity or invalidity determines the skin's status, irrespective of flaying. The later *mishnah*, by contrast, would grant the *kohanim* the skin once it is flayed, even if the *zerikah* is then performed invalidly. How are these two *mishnahs* to be reconciled?

## Mishna Reconciliation

### Abaye's Synthesis

Abaye resolves the contradiction by reading both *mishnahs* as following the opinion of Rabbi (Rebbi). The truly decisive moment, according to Rabbi, is the flaying — and specifically, whether the invalidity occurred before or after the skin was physically separated. This is the position of the later *mishnah* taken at face value.

The earlier *mishnah*'s formulation — focusing on the time of *zerikah* — does not contradict this. Rabbi concedes (*u-modeh Rebbi*) that flaying does not in practice precede *zerikah*. Flaying an animal is a time-consuming process; *zerikah* is essentially instantaneous. It would be entirely inappropriate to catch the blood and then wait for the animal to be flayed before throwing it. In the real world of Temple service, *zerikah* always comes first. Therefore, for all practical purposes, the time of *zerikah* and the question of whether the animal has been flayed are coextensive: if it was invalid at *zerikah*, it had not yet been flayed and the skin is not the *kohanim*'s; if it was valid at *zerikah*, flaying will follow and the skin is theirs.

But the *ikar* — the underlying halachic principle — is that the flaying is what truly matters. There exists a theoretical scenario where someone threw the blood only after the animal had been flayed; in such a case, if the *zerikah* was invalid, the *kohanim* would nevertheless receive the skin, since the invalidity occurred after flaying. That scenario, while virtually impossible in practice, is the logical implication of Rabbi's true position.

### Rav's Synthesis

Rav reads both *mishnahs* as following Rabbi Eliezer (Rabbi Eliezer ben Shimon), for whom the blood-throwing is the genuinely decisive moment. According to Rav, the language of the later *mishnah* — *kodem le-hefshetan* (before flaying) and *le-achar hefshetan* (after flaying) — is not to be taken literally. The phrases do not actually refer to the physical act of flaying. Rather, *kodem le-hefshetan* means before the *korban* reached the stage of readiness for flaying — that is, before *zerikah* — and *le-achar hefshetan* means after it reached that stage, i.e., after *zerikah*. The later *mishnah* is thus simply another way of expressing the same standard as the earlier one: validity at the time of *zerikah*.

The dispute therefore reduces to: does the truly operative moment track the physical separation of skin from flesh, or the legal moment of *zerikah* that renders the animal eligible for flaying? Abaye (following Rabbi) reads it as the former — with *zerikah* serving only as a practical proxy. Rav (following Rabbi Eliezer ben Shimon) reads it as the latter, with the language of flaying being metaphorical shorthand for *zerikah*-readiness. The *gemara* notes that this question will continue on the following daf.
