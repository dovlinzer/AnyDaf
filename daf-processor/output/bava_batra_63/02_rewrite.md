# Bava Batra 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shchiv Mera & Chelek

### Opening

The fourth *perek* of Bava Batra resumes on daf 63 at a somewhat unusual juncture. The *sugya* opens with the word *pshita* — a term that ordinarily functions as an interjection mid-discussion to push an argument forward, not as an opening move. Its precise function here is not entirely clear, but the substance that follows illuminates the context.

### Shchiv Mera Definition

The *Rashbam*, along with other *rishonim*, clarifies that this *sugya* concerns a *shchiv mera* — a person who is gravely ill — dividing up property. There is a conceptual question regarding what exactly defines a *shchiv mera*: is it simply someone so ill as to be confined to bed, or must the person be specifically on their deathbed? The practical stakes of that distinction are significant when dealing with inheritances and end-of-life property arrangements. In reality, one can rarely know at the time of a serious illness whether it will prove fatal — and thus the legal structure of *shchiv mera* transactions is built around conditional gifts or conditional sales. The *shchiv mera* declares, in effect, "I want so-and-so to have this property," and the transaction takes effect upon death.

A further distinctive feature of *shchiv mera* transactions is that they take effect entirely through verbal declaration, without any formal *kinyan*. This stands in contrast to virtually every other mode of acquisition in *halacha*. A major question — to be treated more fully in the ninth *perek* — is whether this power operates *mide'oraita*, perhaps rooted in the laws of inheritance, or whether it is a rabbinic enactment designed to allow people a more equitable and responsive mechanism for end-of-life estate planning.

### Yachlok vs Chelek

The *Gemara* opens with what it presents as obvious: if the *shchiv mera* says *yachlok ploni benichsay* — "so-and-so should split my properties" — then that person receives *palga*, a half. The term *yachlok* implies a precise division into two equal parts. (As the *Gemara* established two days prior, *nichsay* encompasses all manner of real property, including houses and even slaves.)

The difficulty arises when the language shifts. If instead the dying person says *tnu chelek liploni benichsay* — "give a portion of my property to so-and-so" — then *mai*, what does that mean? The word *chelek* (portion) is inherently ambiguous: it could indicate a twelfth, a hundredth, a half, or anything in between. The problem is closely analogous to the ambiguities of gift-giving and sales examined in the preceding discussion, where imprecise language created significant halachic complications.

### Ravina bar Kissi Braita

To address this question, *Ravina bar Kissi* cites a *braita* with the formula *ta shema*. Before examining its content, a critical framing caveat must be noted: this *braita* operates within the position of *Sumchos*, whose general ruling we do not adopt as *halacha*. *Sumchos*, a student of *Rabbi Meir*, holds that in cases of *mamon hamutal besafek* — doubtful monetary disputes — the property should always be split equally. The rabbinic consensus, by contrast, follows the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: whoever seeks to extract property from the one currently in possession of it bears the burden of proof, preserving the status quo. The *braita* cited by *Ravina bar Kissi* reflects *Sumchos*'s framework, and its usefulness here is confined to illuminating what *chelek* means as a quantity — not to affirm *Sumchos*'s broader methodology.

## Chelek Context & Scale

### Chelek b'Bor

The *braita* opens: *haomer tnu chelek liploni b'vor* — if a person says "give so-and-so a *chelek* in the cistern (*bor*)." *Sumchos omer*: *ein pachot m'revia* — it cannot be less than a quarter.

The reasoning proceeds as follows. The word *chelek* is inherently ambiguous — it could mean the most minimal sliver or it could mean as much as half. The floor of the range is some negligible minimum; both parties agree that at least that tiny amount was intended. The ceiling of the range, for a *bor*, is a full half. The entire span between the minimum and a half is therefore *besafek* — in dispute. Following *Sumchos*'s principle of *yachloku*, one splits that disputed range, yielding a quarter. Adding the undisputed minimal amount produces the result: a quarter plus a little bit. This is what *Sumchos* means by *ein pachot m'revia* — not less than a quarter, with a small addition.

### Chelek L'Chavit

The next case: *l'chavit* — water rights described as a *chelek* to fill a *chavit* (barrel). Here *Sumchos* rules *ein pachot m'shminit* — not less than an eighth (plus a small additional amount). The reasoning is parallel. When the *chelek* is described in terms sufficient to fill a *chavit*, the working assumption is that such an amount constitutes a quarter of the *bor*. That quarter is now the ceiling of the disputed range. Splitting it yields an eighth, plus the undisputed minimum.

### Chelek L'Kdeira & Tafiach

The *braita* continues with progressively smaller vessels. For *l'kdeira* — water rights to fill a cooking pot — *Sumchos* rules *ein pachot mi-yod bet*, not less than 1/12. Here the assumption is that the relevant quantity constitutes a sixth of the *bor*, and splitting that sixth yields 1/12. Finally, for *letafiach* — a drinking glass — the rule is *ein pachot mi-shisha asar*, not less than 1/16, since the relevant quantity is taken to be an eighth of the *bor*, half of which is 1/16.

The *Rashbam* explains that a *tafiach* is a *kli katan lishtot bo bnei adam*, a small vessel used by people for drinking, and notes that it is *v'shiura zuta mikulhu* — the smallest quantity of all.

### Water Usage Context

What emerges from this *braita* is that the meaning of *chelek* is not fixed but contextual — it scales with the frame of reference provided by the surrounding language. The hierarchy moves from large-scale usage (the *bor* itself, potentially for agriculture or animals) down through intermediate quantities (a barrel) to domestic cooking and finally personal drinking. This mirrors a familiar pattern in water allocation: most water consumption in any agricultural society goes not to drinking but to irrigation, livestock, manufacturing, and large-scale use. The *braita*, within *Sumchos*'s framework, captures that intuition formally — the intended portion is understood in light of what the water is actually for.

*Ravina bar Kissi*'s point is thus established: *chelek* can be quite ambiguous, ranging from a minimal amount up to substantially different quantities depending entirely on the context in which it is used.

## Ben Levi & Terumah

### Ben Levi Sells Field

A new *braita* is introduced: *tanu rabanan*. The case is that of a *ben Levi* — a Levite — who sells his field to a *Yisrael* and stipulates: *al menat she-ma'aser rishon sheli* — "on condition that the *ma'aser rishon* (first tithe) from this field shall be mine."

A preliminary observation: while the *Torah* envisions the *Levi* as lacking a hereditary ancestral portion in the Land of Israel, a *Levi* can nonetheless own land through purchase or inheritance from a maternal grandfather, and in principle could be a substantial landowner. The *Torah*'s assumption that *Levi'im* would be relatively poor and therefore in need of the social safety net of *ma'aser rishon* does not preclude the possibility of a wealthy *Levi* who happens to own land. The present case takes precisely such a situation as its premise.

### Davar Shelo Ba Olam

The case poses a genuine halachic dilemma with two equally problematic poles. On one extreme: if the *Levi* is understood to have retained outright property rights to one-tenth of the field, that cannot generate valid *ma'aser rishon*. The entire mechanism of *ma'aser rishon* requires that the produce belong to the *Yisrael*, who then separates the tithe and hands it to the *Levi*. A *Levi* who owns the land himself cannot receive *ma'aser rishon* from it, because the produce was never the *Yisrael*'s to separate.

On the other extreme: if the *Levi* is understood to have retained some future claim to the tithe — the produce has not yet grown, the harvest has not occurred, the separation has not taken place — then this appears to be *davar shelo ba l'olam*, a transfer of something that does not yet exist, which *halacha* generally does not permit.

It is worth noting a subtle but important distinction raised in the course of discussion: the initial transaction may not be problematic in the same way. When the *Levi* sells the field and retains a right, he is not selling a *davar shelo ba l'olam* — he is *keeping* something. The halachic complications around *davar shelo ba l'olam* arise most acutely when one attempts to *sell* or *transfer* such a right. This distinction illuminates the later cases in the *braita*.

The parallel case of *kohen hamisayeia b'veit hagranot* — a *kohen* who assists at the threshing floor hoping to be given *terumah* — reflects a related set of concerns: the field owner normally retains the right to designate which *kohen* receives the *terumah*, and any attempt to manipulate that right is halachically fraught.

### Zechut Resolution

The resolution adopted by the *Rashba* and the *Ritva* (with similar reasoning) is that the *Levi* is not retaining a property interest in the land or in any specific fruit. Rather, he is retaining an abstract legal right — a *zechut* — to be the designated recipient of the *ma'aser rishon* when the *Yisrael* separates it in the future. The *Rashbam* articulates this explicitly: *ma'aser rishon shelo va'afilu machra lokeiach l'acher d'zechuto shel levi eino yachol limkor l'acher d'achti birshuto ka'amar* — the *ma'aser rishon* is his, and even if the buyer sells the field to a third party, the *zechut* of the *Levi* cannot be transferred by that sale, because the field remains, in a sense, within the *Levi*'s claim.

The critical halachic weight is placed on the words *al menat* — "on condition." The *Gemara* explains: *keivan d'amar lei al menat shema'aser rishon sheli, shiyurei shayreia limkom ma'aser* — because he used the language of *al menat*, he has "retained the place of the *ma'aser*." This is understood not as ownership of the physical land producing the tenth, but as a retained legal right to the produce that will grow there. The *rishonim* acknowledge that this is not entirely airtight — it is not a fully tangible right, and yet it is treated as legally cognizable precisely because it is articulated through the *al menat* formulation in the contract.

### Variations & Conditions

The *braita* continues with several variations. First: if the *Levi* said *li ulvanai* — "for me and for my sons" — then should he die, the right passes to his descendants (*met yitein livanav*). This is halachically significant: it means that an intangible legal right of this nature — the right to receive *ma'aser rishon* — can be inherited. This might not have been assumed. One might have thought that while property transfers by inheritance, intangible *shibudim* (liens) or abstract legal entitlements to future produce do not. The *braita* indicates otherwise.

Second variation: if the *Levi* specified *kol zman shehasadeh zo biyadecha* — "as long as this field remains in your possession" — then if the buyer sells the field to a third party and subsequently repurchases it, the *Levi*'s right is extinguished. Although the field returned to the original buyer's ownership, it did so under new conditions. The prior stipulation is not revived automatically.

The *Gemara* then presses: *amai? ein adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam* — why does any of this work? One cannot transfer something that does not yet exist. The answer returns to the *al menat* formulation: the *Levi* did not sell a *davar shelo ba l'olam* — he retained it. The problems arise when a third party subsequently attempts to transfer that right, which is something that cannot be done. This is why when the field is sold to another and returned, the right cannot travel with it: one cannot transfer what one never properly acquired.

What this *sugya* opens up is a remarkably sophisticated conception of ownership — one that moves well beyond tangible real estate into the realm of rights, futures, and intangible entitlements to produce. The legal right to *ma'aser rishon* must be clearly articulated, distinct from property ownership and distinct from a futures arrangement, since most transfers of non-existent things are not recognized in *halacha*. The *rishonim* struggled to define precisely what kind of legal ground this right stands on, and the tension is honestly acknowledged: the answer may not be fully airtight, but the framework is coherent enough to be operative.

## Reish Lakish & House

### Diyuta Elyona

*Reish Lakish* draws an inference from the *braita* about the *ben Levi*: *zot omeret hamocher bayit l'chavero v'amar lo al menat shediyuta ha'elyona sheli, diyuta ha'elyona shelo* — this implies that when someone sells a house and stipulates *al menat* that the upper course of bricks (*diyuta elyona*) shall be his, then indeed the upper course remains his.

The *diyuta elyona* refers to the uppermost layer of bricks — a course, in the architectural sense — at the top of the structure. In a one-story house, this corresponds to the air rights above the roof, or more precisely, the right to continue construction from the roofline upward. The question the *Gemara* immediately raises is: what is the *chiddush* here? Why does this need to be said? That question will drive the discussion in the section that follows.

## Reish Lakish & House (II)

### Zizin Rights

*Rav Zevid* interprets the force of *Reish Lakish*'s ruling narrowly but concretely: *she'im ratza lehotzi bah zizin motzi* — if the seller wishes to extend small protrusions or ledges (*zizin*) from the upper structure, he may do so. One might have assumed that retaining the *diyuta elyona* meant only retaining it as it stands, with no right to alter or add to it. *Rav Zevid* teaches that the *al menat* language conveys actual construction rights, including the right to extend *zizin*.

### Building Aliyah

*Rav Pappa* goes further: *she'im ratza livnot aliyah al gabah boneh* — if the seller wishes to build an entirely new upper story (*aliyah*) on top of the existing structure, he may. According to *Rav Pappa*, the construction rights conveyed by the *al menat* language are quite expansive, encompassing even major structural additions.

### Rav Pappa Difficulty

The *Gemara* notes an asymmetry. *Rav Zevid*'s position fits naturally within the rhetorical structure of *Reish Lakish*'s statement: *zot omeret* ("this implies") typically introduces a corollary that is analogous and proportionate to the original case. Extending *zizin* is a modest inference from retaining the *diyuta elyona*, and the parallel to the *ben Levi* retaining the *zechut* to *ma'aser rishon* is reasonably apt.

*Rav Pappa*'s position, however, is more difficult. Adding an entire additional story substantially alters the nature of the property sold — the ground-floor buyer's situation is materially affected by the seller constructing a second story above him. Whether this is truly comparable to what *Reish Lakish*'s *zot omeret* implies is questionable. The *Gemara* simply states *kashya* — the difficulty stands; the inference does not sit comfortably with the rhetorical framework *Reish Lakish* employed.

## Umka V'Ruma

### Explicit Language

*Rav Dimi of Neharde'a* states: *hai man de-mizban leh beisa le-havreh, af al gav de-chasav leh umka ve-ruma, tzarich le-michatav leh kani lach me-tehom ar'a ve-ad rum reki'a* — when someone sells a house to his neighbor, even if the deed of sale (*shtar*) includes the phrase *umka ve-ruma* (depths and heights), that is insufficient. One must explicitly write that the buyer acquires from the depths of the earth (*tehom ar'a*) to the heights of heaven (*rum reki'a*).

### Umka V'Ruma Logic

The reasoning is that *umka ve-ruma* by itself, written in isolation, conveys nothing beyond what a standard sale of a house already includes. When one sells a house, of course the floor is the floor and the ceiling is the ceiling — those are the natural limits of the structure. Writing *umka ve-ruma* without further elaboration merely restates what is already implied by a plain *stam* sale. To actually convey excavation rights below the house and air rights above it, one must use the more expansive language: *me-tehom ar'a ve-ad rum reki'a*, from the depths of the earth to the heights of heaven. That formula makes explicit what the generic *umka ve-ruma* leaves ambiguous.

### Bor V'Dut Proof

The *Gemara* considers whether this position is supported by the *mishna* that will appear on the following *daf*. That *mishna* states: *velo et habor velo et hadut af al pi shekatav lo umka veruma* — when selling a house, the sale does not include the *bor* (cistern) or the *dut* (water tank) even if the seller wrote *umka ve-ruma* in the deed. A *bor* is a subterranean cistern, a *dut* a water tank, and *michilot* are tunnels beneath the structure. This *mishna* would seem to confirm *Rav Dimi*'s position: writing *umka ve-ruma* alone fails to transfer even the underground features, let alone full excavation and air rights.

### Mishna Reinterpretation

The *Gemara* declines to accept this as a straightforward proof, however. The objection is that if *umka ve-ruma bistama lo kani* — if plain *umka ve-ruma* acquires nothing — then one should expect the *mishna* to say that writing the phrase still fails to transfer the *bor* and *dut*. But by that logic, the *mishna*'s case becomes unremarkable. The *Gemara* therefore proposes a radical reinterpretation — a *hachi ka'amar*: the *mishna* should be read not as "even if you wrote *umka ve-ruma* you do not get the *bor* and *dut*," but rather as "even if you did not write *umka ve-ruma*, it is as though you did." That is, *af al pi shelo katav lo, kimi shekatav dami* — even without explicitly writing the phrase, it functions as though written, thereby conveying the *bor*, *dut*, and *michilot*.

This is a complete reversal of the plain reading. What once appeared to limit the effect of *umka ve-ruma* now becomes evidence that *umka ve-ruma*, even when absent from the text, operates by convention as if present. The legal intuition underlying this is that certain formulaic phrases in contracts carry established semantic content within their legal context, even when not written explicitly — the conventions of contractual drafting supply the meaning. Thus, under this reinterpretation: if one writes *umka ve-ruma*, one acquires; if one does not write it, convention treats it as though written for purposes of the *bor*, *dut*, and *michilot*, though the broader range of *tehom ar'a ve-ad rum reki'a* still requires explicit specification.

## Maakaeh & Roof Rights

### Roof Retained

The *mishna* under discussion states: *velo et hagag bizman sheyesh lo ma'akeh gavoha asara tefachim* — when a house is sold, the sale does not include the roof (*gag*) when the roof has a parapet (*ma'akeh*) at least ten *tefachim* high.

### Chashiv Status

The reasoning behind this rule is that a *ma'akeh* ten *tefachim* high renders the roof a distinct and independently usable space — *chashiv*, significant enough to be treated as a separate area. A low parapet, insufficient to be halachically valid, signals that the rooftop is not safely or practically usable and therefore would be transferred along with the house in a plain *stam* sale. But when the *ma'akeh* reaches ten *tefachim*, the roof becomes, functionally, a separate area — comparable in some respects to a parking space in a building that is considered distinct from the apartment above it. In such a case, the seller retains the roof as a separate usable space even though the house below is sold.

### Al Menat Necessity

This *mishna* connects back to *Reish Lakish*'s ruling. *Reish Lakish* taught that the seller must use the language *al menat* — "on condition that" — to retain the *diyuta elyona*, the upper construction rights. The question naturally arises: if a plain *stam* sale already excludes air rights (because the seller retains the roof when it has a ten-*tefachim* *ma'akeh*), why would *Reish Lakish* need the *al menat* formulation at all? The *stam* case would seem to accomplish the same result on its own.

### Ravina-Rav Ashi

This precise challenge is posed by *Ravina* to *Rav Ashi*: *ta shema de-amar Reish Lakish zot omeret hamocher bayit lechavero... v'amrinan lemai hilchata* — and we already discussed *Reish Lakish*'s ruling and asked what it comes to teach. If *bistama lo kani* — if a plain sale without mentioning *umka ve-ruma* does not convey the roof rights — then *lama li al menat*? What work is the *al menat* language doing? The *stam* sale would already exclude the roof in the relevant case (where the *ma'akeh* makes it *chashiv*). Why would the seller need to make an explicit condition?

### Liability Interpretation

The *Gemara*'s resolution is: *ahani lei al menat de-ei nafal hadar banei lei* — the *al menat* formulation accomplishes something distinct from simply retaining the roof. It establishes a condition of ongoing liability: if the upper course (*diyuta*) should collapse, the seller is obligated to rebuild it. In a plain *stam* sale where the seller implicitly retains the roof (by virtue of the *ma'akeh* making it *chashiv*), there is no construction or repair obligation attached. The *al menat* language creates that additional duty. Thus, what distinguishes the *al menat* case from the *stam* case is not the question of who owns the roof, but the question of who bears the liability to maintain and rebuild it.

### Rashbam Caveat

The *Rashbam* is not fully satisfied with any of the interpretations on offer. Relying in part on the commentary of *Rabbeinu Chananel*, he proposes that the ruling of *Rav Pappa* should be understood more narrowly — not as granting the right to build an entirely new second story, but rather as addressing the obligation to rebuild the *diyuta* itself if it collapses. He reads the clause *ahanei de-ei nafla deyuta hadar banei lah* as a limitation, not an expansion, of the seller's construction rights. In his formulation: *le-olam mi-stama lo kani lokeiach ruma*, the buyer in a plain sale simply does not acquire the air rights, and the *al menat* clause functions only to specify the seller's repair obligations regarding the upper course.

The *Rashbam* concludes, however, with a note of honest uncertainty: *ki nira shitat Shmuel zot be-einai ve-yesh shitot acherot ve-ein ba-hen mamash* — this interpretation seems correct to him, but there are other readings, and he is not fully convinced by any of them. The issue, in his view, remains unresolved. That candor is itself instructive: the interplay between *stam* sales, *al menat* conditions, and the scope of construction rights in vertically shared structures is genuinely complex, and the *Gemara* here leaves room for ongoing interpretation.
