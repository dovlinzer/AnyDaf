# Bava Kamma 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Second Half Overview

### Transition to Theft

We now move from the first half of *Bava Kamma* to the second half, though in terms of *perakim* it is slightly more than half. The first half of the tractate concerns *nizkei mamon* — damage caused by one's property, as well as damage inflicted upon the property of others — in short, the laws of damages. The second half shifts to an entirely different set of topics: *gneiva* and *gzeila*, and also personal injury, when a person harms another person's body. This material, though touched upon earlier in the tractate, now receives its own sustained treatment. *Meruba*, the chapter we have now begun, is central to this second half.

### Gneiva vs Gzeila

Two terms require precise definition at the outset. *Gneiva* is burglary — when one surreptitiously enters another's domain and takes his property without direct confrontation. *Gzeila* is theft — the outright seizure of property from another. The distinction will prove consequential throughout the chapter, though *gzeila* will be addressed more fully in a later *perek*. For now, the comparison between the two recurs as an ongoing motif.

### Kefel Penalties

When one commits *gneiva*, one pays *kefel* — double the value of the stolen object. This is the central penalty that *Meruba* explores. The obligation intensifies in certain cases: if the stolen object was an ox or a sheep, and the burglar subsequently slaughtered or sold it, the payment rises to four or five times the value respectively.

### Klal u'Prat Structure

*Meruba* is considered a classic *yeshivishe masechta*, and one will soon understand why. The opening two *dafim*, however, are given over largely to *drashot* employing the hermeneutical method of *klal u'prat u'klal* — general, specific, general — which establishes which categories of objects are subject to *kefel*. The Torah's *psukim* mention only specific animals; the derivation extends liability to the entire universe of movable goods. These pages are traditionally skipped in the *yeshivot*, and one should be forewarned that the material, while important to understand, is not the most analytically stimulating. What follows is the *sugya* of today and the next *daf*.

## Torah Sources

### Ki Yiten Ish Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְרוּבָּה מִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל מִמִּדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. שֶׁמִּדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בְּדָבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים וּבֵין בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, וּמִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹר וָשֶׂה בִּלְבַד – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִגְנֹב אִישׁ שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה וּטְבָחוֹ אוֹ מְכָרוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The principle of double payment** applies **more broadly than the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment, as the principle of double payment applies both to** the theft of **something that is alive and to** the theft of **something that is not alive, but the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment applies only to** the theft of **an ox or a sheep, as it is stated: "If a man steal an ox or a sheep, and slaughter it or sell it,** he shall pay five oxen for an ox and four sheep for a sheep" (Exodus 21:37).

The *psukim* in the Torah underlying the law of *kefel* require careful examination. The first passage, *ki yiten ish el re'ehu kesef o kelim lishmor* — "when a man gives to his fellow silver or vessels to guard" — establishes the context of a *shomer*, a custodian who receives property to watch. The *pasuk* continues: *ve'gunav mibeit ha'ish im yimatze haganav yeshalem shnayim* — "and it was stolen from the man's house; if the burglar is found, he shall pay double." The objects mentioned here — *kesef o kelim* — are quite general. This is the foundational *pasuk* establishing that one who burgles pays *kefel*.

### Im Lo Yimatze Source

Immediately following comes a second passage. *Im lo yimatze haganav* — "if the burglar is not found" — *venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim* — the custodian is brought before the judges. The *pasuk* then says *im lo shalach yado bimleches re'ehu* — "if he did not misuse his fellow's property" — meaning the *shomer* takes an oath that he did not misuse the entrusted property. The passage then lists the objects over which such an oath applies: *al shor, al chamor, al seh, al salmah, al kol aveidah asher yomar ki hu zeh* — "concerning an ox, a donkey, a sheep, a garment, any lost object that one claims is his" — *ad ha'elohim yavo dvar shneihem asher yarshi'un elohim yeshalem shnayim l're'ehu* — "the matter of both shall come before the judges; whom the judges condemn shall pay double to his fellow."

This second passage is puzzling. Who exactly is paying double? And what is meant by the object being called *aveidah* if it was given to the *shomer* to watch? The resolution offered by *Chazal* is that this passage describes a case of *toenei ta'anat ganav* — one who falsely claims that the object was stolen. The *shomer* swears that the object was burglarized; he did nothing wrong. In truth, he himself took it. The *halacha* is that if one takes a false oath that property was stolen — that it was subject to *gneiva* — and one is in fact the one who kept it, one is treated as though one committed the burglary oneself, and one pays *kefel*. This, then, is how *Chazal* read the second half of the *pasuk*: *toenei ta'anat ganav venimtza shehu genavo* — one who claims the object was stolen and it turns out he himself took it.

### Gneiva vs Claim

The structure of these two *psukim* thus reveals two separate categories. In the first *pasuk*, the objects are described simply as *kesef o kelim* — quite general. In the second, where the case concerns a false claim of *gneiva*, the list is much longer and more specific: *shor, chamor, seh, salmah, kol aveidah*. The goal of the *sugya* is to establish that *kefel* extends to virtually all objects, and that this longer list — with its *klal u'prat u'klal* structure — is the basis for that derivation. A small number of things are excluded, but the general category is broad.

## Daf 62b Analysis

### Metaltel Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאִילּוּ ״מִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בְּגַנָּב בֵּין בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, וּמִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בְּגַנָּב בִּלְבַד״ – לָא קָתָנֵי. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״ – כָּלַל, ״עַל שׁוֹר, עַל חֲמוֹר, עַל שֶׂה וְעַל שַׂלְמָה״ – פָּרַט, ״עַל כׇּל אֲבֵדָה״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל; כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches one particular difference between double payment and fourfold or fivefold payment, **while it does not teach** this difference between them: **The principle of double payment applies both in** the case of **a thief and in** the case of a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit entrusted to him and who takes an oath to that effect. **But the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment applies only in** the case of **a thief,** not in the case of one who falsely claims that a deposit was stolen. From where are these matters derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse that discusses double payment: "For any matter of trespass, whether it be for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment, or for any manner of lost thing about which one shall say: This is it, the claims of both of them shall come before the judges, the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8). **"For any matter of trespass"** is **a generalization; "whether it be for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment"** is **a detail.** And when the verse states: **"Or for any manner of lost thing," it then generalizes** again. Consequently, this verse contains **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** and one of the thirteen rules of exegesis states that in such a case **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Therefore, **just as** each of the items mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as an item that is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value, **so too** double payment is practiced with regard to **any item that is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value.

The *Gemara* on 62b opens: *minah hanei milei* — from where do we derive these laws? *D'tanu rabbanan* — the *Rabbanan* taught: *al kol dvar pesha* — "concerning any matter of wrongdoing" — that is the *klal*, the general category. *Al shor, al chamor, al seh, al salmah* — those are the *prat*, the specific examples. *Al kol aveidah* — "concerning any lost item" — that is the *klal* again. This gives us a full *klal u'prat u'klal*. The rule of *klal u'prat u'klal* is *ee atah dan ela k'ein ha'prat* — you include only things similar in kind to the specific examples. The specific examples are: *davar hametaltel v'gufo mamon* — something that is movable and has intrinsic monetary value. Therefore: *af kol davar hametaltel v'gufo mamon* — anything that is movable and has intrinsic value falls under *kefel*.

### Exclusions Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ קַרְקָעוֹת – שֶׁאֵינָן מִטַּלְטְלִין; יָצְאוּ עֲבָדִים – שֶׁהוּקְּשׁוּ לְקַרְקָעוֹת; יָצְאוּ שְׁטָרוֹת – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמִּטַּלְטְלִין, אֵין גּוּפָן מָמוֹן; יָצָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ – ״רֵעֵהוּ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Land is excluded, as it is not movable property.** Canaanite **slaves are excluded, as they are compared to land** in many areas of *halakha*. Financial **documents are excluded, as, although they are movable property, they do not** have **intrinsic monetary** value. The value of the material on which the document is written is negligible; documents are valuable only because they serve as proof for monetary claims. Finally, **consecrated** property **is excluded** because **it is written** in the verse that the one found liable shall pay double to **"his neighbor,"** i.e., to another person, rather than to the Temple treasury.

From this derivation, several categories are excluded. *Yatz'u karka'ot she'einan metaltelin* — real estate is excluded because it is not movable. *Yatz'u avadim shehukshu l'karka'ot* — slaves are excluded because they are legally equated with land. *Yatz'u shtarot* — documents, specifically IOU notes, are excluded *she'af al pi shemetaltelin ein gufan mamon* — even though they are physically movable, their intrinsic value lies not in the document itself but in the debt it represents, and therefore they lack *gufo mamon*. And finally, *yatz'a hekdesh* — consecrated property is excluded, because the *pasuk* says *ki yiten ish el re'ehu*, invoking *re'ehu*, one's fellow, and consecrated property does not belong to a private individual.

### Gneiva Land Tosafot

One might ask how one can commit *gneiva* — burglary — on land at all, since *gneiva* by its nature is a surreptitious act, not an open seizure. *Tosafot* raises this question explicitly: *gneiva b'karka heichi shaychah?* — what relevance does *gneiva* have to land? Two answers are offered. First, *b'massig gevul* — by moving a boundary marker, one covertly takes another's land without the owner even knowing. The analogy to sneaking into someone's house is apt: the owner has no idea the theft occurred. Second, *i nami b'mechubar l'karka* — or it refers to something attached to the ground, such as grapevines ripped out of the soil.

## Kal Ribuy Debate

### Rami Kal Ribuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אֲבָל עוֹפוֹת לָא! וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָא ״שַׂלְמָה״ כְּתִיב! אָמְרִי: אֲנַן בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים קָאָמְרִינַן; אֵימָא בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים, דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אִין, דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין נִבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – לָא, דְּהָא כֹּל חַד וְחַד כְּלָל וּפְרָט בְּאַפֵּי נַפְשֵׁיהּ דָּרְשִׁינַן לֵיהּ; אֲבָל עוֹפוֹת – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *halakha* of double payment is limited to cases similar to the details mentioned in the verse, it should also be derived that **just as** each of the items mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as an item whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying, so too any item whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying** it is subject to double payment. **But birds,** whose carcasses do not transmit impurity through contact or carrying, should **not** be subject to double payment. The Gemara objects to the question: **But how can you say this? But isn't "garment" written** in the list of specified items (Exodus 22:8)? It is impossible to speak of the impurity of carcasses with regard to clothing. The Gemara responds: **Say,** in response to this objection: **We are speaking** specifically **of animals,** and this is the question that was asked: Why not **say** that **with regard to animals,** an animal **whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying, yes,** it is subject to double payment, whereas an animal **whose carcass does not transmit impurity through contact and carrying,** such as a bird, **no,** a thief would not pay double payment for stealing it? **This line of reasoning is correct, because we expound each one** of the listed items in the **generalization, and detail,** and generalization **by itself.** Each item is treated individually as representing a category, but the different items are not grouped together into one broad category. Since several animals are listed, it is concluded that if the stolen item is an animal it must resemble the listed animals. **But** since **birds** do not transmit impurity through contact or carrying, they are **not** subject to double payment.

The *Gemara* now raises a potential objection to the scope of the *klal u'prat u'klal* derivation. After establishing that the *prat* consists of movable goods with intrinsic value, a challenge arises: perhaps within the category of animals, we should limit *kefel* only to animals whose carcasses convey *tumah* through contact and carrying — *she'neveilato metamei b'maga u'v'masa*. This would exclude birds, since birds do not convey *tumah* through direct contact. The word *kol* appearing in the second *klal* — *al kol aveidah* — seems positioned to resolve this by functioning not as an ordinary *klal* but as a *ribuy*, a more expansively inclusive term. Rami bar Chama argues precisely this: the word *kol* here is a *ribuy*, making the category broader than a standard *klal* would permit.

### Ma'aser Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל הֵיכָא דִּכְתַב ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא? וְהָא גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר, דִּכְתִיב ״כֹּל״, וְקָא דָרְשִׁינַן לֵיהּ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט! דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתָּ הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ״ – כָּלַל, ״בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ – פָּרַט, ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאׇלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל; כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, אַף כֹּל פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is it so that **anywhere that** the Torah **wrote** the word ***kol* it is an amplification? But isn't it** so that **with regard to** second **tithe,** the word ***kol* is written** in the verse: "And you shall bestow the money for whatever [*bekhol*] your soul desires, for oxen, or for sheep, or for wine, or for strong drink, or for whatever your soul asks of you" (Deuteronomy 14:26)? **And** yet **we expound** that verse **as a generalization, and a detail,** and a generalization. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"And you shall bestow the money for whatever your soul desires,"** is **a generalization,** as no particular type of food is specified. **"For oxen, or for sheep, or for wine, or for strong drink,"** is a **detail,** as specific types of food are mentioned. When the verse concludes with **"or for whatever your soul asks of you," it has generalized again.** Since the verse is formulated as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** This indicates that **just as** each of the items mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as the produce of produce,** i.e., they grow from a parent organism, e.g., agricultural produce or animals, **and** they are **grown from the ground,** i.e., their sustenance comes from the ground, **so too** the category of items one may purchase with second-tithe money includes **all** items that are **the produce of produce and are grown from the ground.**

The *Gemara* challenges this by noting that by *ma'aser sheni*, the Torah also uses the word *kol* — *b'chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — and yet there the *Gemara* reads it as a standard *klal* in a *klal u'prat u'klal* construction. A *braisa* is cited: *v'nasata hakesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* is the *klal*; *b'vakar u'v'tzon u'v'yayin u'v'shachar* is the *prat*; *u'v'chol asher tishalcha nafshecha* is the second *klal*. The conclusion there is *ee atah dan ela k'ein ha'prat* — you may only use *ma'aser sheni* money for things that are *pri mi'pri ve'gidulei karka* — the fruit of a fruit and that grow from the ground. The word *kol* there is read narrowly, as a *klal*, not as a *ribuy*.

### Kal Ribuy Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: ״בַּכֹּל״ – כְּלָלָא, ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״כֹּל״ – כְּלָלָא הוּא, מִיהוּ ״כֹּל״ דְּהָכָא – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא. מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ״ – כָּלַל, ״כֶּסֶף אוֹ כֵלִים״ – פָּרַט, ״לִשְׁמוֹר״ – (הֲדַר) [חָזַר] וְכְלָל; וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הַאי ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״ נָמֵי לִכְלָל וּפְרָט הוּא דַּאֲתָא, נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא לְהָנֵי פְּרָטֵי גַּבֵּי הַאיְךְ כְּלָל וּפְרָט; ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רִיבּוּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Say** in answer to this question that the term ***bekhol*** is **a generalization,** whereas the term ***kol* is an amplification. And if you wish, say** an alternate answer: The word ***kol*** is usually **a generalization. But** the word ***kol* that is** written **here,** in the verse concerning double payment (Exodus 22:8), is an exception. It is regarded as **an amplification,** as the Gemara will explain. **After all,** there is another **generalization, and a detail, and a generalization written at the beginning** of this passage, **as it is written:** "If a man gives his neighbor money or vessels to safeguard and it was stolen from the house of the man, if the thief shall be found he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:6). **"If a man gives his neighbor"** is **a generalization. "Money or vessels" is a detail.** When the verse concludes **"to safeguard," it has generalized again.** **And if it enters your mind** to say that **this** later verse: **"For any matter of trespass"** (Exodus 22:8), **is also coming** to state **a generalization, and a detail,** and a generalization, **let the Merciful One write these details,** i.e., ox, donkey, sheep, and garment, which are cited in the later verse, **together with that** previous **generalization, and detail,** and generalization. **Why do I** need the latter verse beginning with **"for any matter of trespass"? Conclude from it** that the word *kol* **is an amplification** in this instance, and it includes all animals.

Rami bar Chama's answer distinguishes between *b'chol* — "with all," as used by *ma'aser sheni* — and *kol* — simply "all," as used here. The former is a standard *klal*; the latter is a *ribuy*. The *Gemara* offers an additional resolution: *ve'ee ba'it eima* — one could also say that even if *kol* is generally a standard *klal*, here specifically it functions as a *ribuy*. The reason is that we already have a complete *klal u'prat u'klal* structure in the earlier *pasuk* — *ki yiten ish el re'ehu kesef o kelim lishmor*, where *kesef* is the *klal*, *o kelim* the *prat*, and *lishmor* the second *klal*. If the second list were meant to serve the same *klal u'prat u'klal* function, the Torah would have simply incorporated these specifics into the same sentence. The fact that the Torah repeats a separate list here signals that *kol dvar pesha* and *kol aveidah* are functioning differently — not as a standard *klal u'prat u'klal* but as a *ribuy*.

### Ribuy u'Mi'ut Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא, כֹּל הָנֵי פְּרָטֵי לְמָה לִי? חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי קַרְקַע, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי עֲבָדִים, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁטָרוֹת. ״שַׂלְמָה״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְסוּיָּים, ״עַל כׇּל אֲבֵדָה״ – לְכִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now that you said** that the word ***kol*** is **an amplification, why do I** need **all these details** listed in the verse, i.e., ox, donkey, sheep, and garment? The Gemara answers: As for the three animals listed, **one** is mentioned **to exclude land, one to exclude** Canaanite **slaves, and one to exclude** financial **documents.** The example of **a garment** is mentioned **to exclude an item that is not** clearly **delineated** in size or quantity. **"Or for any manner of lost thing"** is written **to** teach that **which Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** says, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole

This recognition produces a methodological shift. If *kol* here is a *ribuy* rather than a *klal*, then the entire structure changes: instead of *klal u'prat u'klal* — which begins with the general, narrows to the specific, and then expands back — we are now operating under *ribuy u'mi'ut*: we begin with everything included and use the specific examples to identify particular exclusions. This brings the analysis full circle: rather than using the *prat* as the template for what is included, we use the specific terms to identify the narrow categories that are *excluded* from the otherwise universal application of *kefel*.

## Four Exclusions

### Shor Chamor Seh

Under the *ribuy u'mi'ut* framework, each of the specific items in the list serves to exclude one category. *Shor, chamor*, and *seh* — ox, donkey, and sheep — together exclude three categories: real estate, slaves, and documents (*shtarot*). One of these three animals corresponds to each exclusion.

### Salmah Non-Specific

The garment — *salmah* — excludes a fourth category: *davar she'eino mesuyam*, something that is not well-defined or not clearly specified.

### Rashi Identification

*Rashi* explains *davar she'eino mesuyam* as referring to something that the owner cannot identify as his own — *she'ein la'be'alim siman bo she'patur mi'kefel*. The parallel *Rashi* draws is to the law of returning *aveidah* in *Bava Metzia*, where the word *salmah* appears there as well, and the *Gemara* derives from it that one is only obligated to return a lost object if it is identifiable. By the same logic, if the owner cannot identify the stolen item as his, there is an exemption from *kefel*.

### Tosafot Half-Items

*Tosafot* takes issue with *Rashi's* explanation. The question *Tosafot* poses is: *mah li yesh bo siman u'mah li ein bo siman?* — what difference does it make whether the owner has a *siman* or not? The fact that the item is his is not in question; the absence of *siman* does not diminish his ownership. It would be strange to say that because you cannot identify it as yours, the burglar goes free from *kefel*. *Tosafot* therefore proposes an alternative reading: *davar she'eino mesuyam* refers to something inherently partial or incomplete — citing the example from the chapter *ha'zahav* of a half-pomegranate or a half-walnut. Precisely what this category encompasses remains somewhat unclear, and *Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty.

## Aveidah Application

### Chiya Aveidah Kefel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּאֲבֵידָה, מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כׇּל אֲבֵדָה אֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר״.
>
> **Translation:** **a lost item** that he had found, which he had been obligated to safeguard until it could be returned to its owner, **he pays double payment, as it is stated: "For any manner of lost thing about which one shall say:** This is it…the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8).

The *Gemara* then asks what purpose the phrase *al kol aveidah* serves in the *pasuk*, given that the *ribuy* and the exclusions have already been derived from the preceding items. The answer is: *l'chderebbi Chiya bar Abba*, for the teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — *ha'to'ein ta'anat ganav be'aveidah meshalem tashlumei kefel* — one who falsely claims that a lost object was stolen pays *kefel*.

### Wallet Scenario

The scenario is as follows. The *pasuk* speaks of someone who was given an object to watch — a *shomer pikdaon* — who then falsely swears it was burglarized. Now consider a variant: one finds another's wallet, an *aveidah*, and when the owner comes asking, one says — falsely — that a burglar came and took it. The finder is also a *shomer*, a *shomer aveidah*, and may be subject to a *shvua* regarding what happened to the object. If it then emerges that the finder kept the wallet himself, does he pay *kefel*? He was never formally entrusted with the object; it came to him because he found it.

The answer is yes — *al kol aveidah* tells us that even in the context of a found object, if one takes a false oath claiming it was stolen, one pays *kefel*. The mechanism is this: one who swears that an object was burglarized, and who is in fact the one who kept it, takes on the full legal status of a *ganav* through the substance of one's own oath. As the *Gemara* puts it elsewhere, you are what you *nishba* — you are treated as having committed the very act you falsely attested to. The same logic applies whether the object was deposited with you or whether you found it.

## Lost vs Stolen

### Pikdoni Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי?״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אָבַד״, ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״; וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן. הוֹדָה עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (108b) about a case where an owner of an item said to the bailee: **Where is my deposit?** The bailee **said to him:** It **was lost.** The owner said: **I administer an oath to you** that it was actually lost, **and** the bailee **said: Amen,** thereby accepting the oath; **and** subsequently **the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he** himself **consumed** the deposit. In this case, the bailee **pays the principal,** i.e., the value of the deposit, to the owner. If the bailee **admitted on his own** that he stole the deposit before any witnesses testified to this effect, **he pays the principal and** an additional **one-fifth** of the principal amount to the owner, **and** he brings **a guilt-offering** to atone for his sin (see Leviticus 5:20–26).

The *Gemara* at this point cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Shavuot* to anchor these distinctions in an authoritative source. The case is: someone asks a *shomer*, "Where is the object you were watching for me?" — *heichan pikdoni?*

### Lost Oath

In the first scenario, the *shomer* responds that it was lost. The owner makes him take a *shvua*, and he does so. Witnesses then testify that he in fact kept it for himself. The ruling: *meshalem et ha'keren* — he pays only the base amount, *keren*. He does not pay *kefel*, because his oath claimed *aveidah*, not *gneiva*.

### Stolen Oath Kefel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״נִגְנַב״, ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״; וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁגְּנָבוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues with another case: The owner said to the bailee: **Where is my deposit?** The bailee **said to him: It was stolen.** The owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen; and the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he stole** the deposit. In this case, the bailee **pays double payment.** If he **admitted** his theft **on his own, he pays the principal and** an additional **one-fifth** to the owner, **and** he brings **a guilt-offering** to atone for his sin.

In the second scenario, the *shomer* says the object was stolen — *nignav*. The owner makes him take a *shvua* to that effect, and he swears it was so. Witnesses then testify that he himself took it — *venimtza shehu genavo*. In this case: *meshalem tashlumei kefel* — he pays double. This is the paradigmatic case of *toenei ta'anat ganav bishvuah*.

### Admission Payment

A third scenario: the *shomer* voluntarily comes forward and admits, without witnesses forcing the matter, that he kept the object. In this case, *kefel* does not apply — because *kefel* is a *kenas*, a punitive fine, and one who admits of his own accord is not subject to a *kenas*. Instead, the mechanism of atonement applicable here is the *korban asham gezelot* — the guilt offering brought by one who swears falsely in order to retain another's property. This requires payment of *keren ve'chomesh* — the principal plus an additional fifth — and bringing the *asham* sacrifice. This reflects the category of *kefel* exemption upon voluntary admission, not our primary focus, but it completes the picture of the three-way comparison.

## Gneiva Oath Requirement

### Gneiva Aveidah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב דִּמְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל – אֲבָל בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת אָבַד לָא מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. וַאֲפִילּוּ טוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב – בִּשְׁבוּעָה הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **In any event,** the mishna **teaches** that **in** the case of a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **he pays double payment, but in** the case of a bailee **who** falsely **claims that** a deposit **was lost, he does not pay double payment. And** it also teaches that **even** with regard to **one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, it is only **by** taking **an oath** to substantiate his claim **that he pays double payment, but** for simply lying **without** taking **an oath he does not pay double payment.**

The *Gemara* now states the categorical rule explicitly: *to'en ta'anat ganav bishvuah* — one who claims *gneiva* and takes an oath — *meshalem tashlumei kefel*. But *to'en ta'anat aveidah* — one who claims the object was merely lost and takes an oath — *lo meshalem tashlumei kefel*. The specific character of the false claim matters: only a sworn false claim of *gneiva* triggers *kefel*.

### Oath Requirement

A further refinement: even when the claim is one of *gneiva*, *kefel* applies only *bishvuah* — when accompanied by an oath. A mere unsworn claim, without a formal *shvua*, does not suffice to impose *kefel*. Both conditions must be present: the claim must be of *gneiva* specifically, and it must be made under oath.

## Two Bratot

### First Braita Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״ – בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב – אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם לֹא יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״ – בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states: "If a man gives his neighbor money or vessels to safeguard and it was stolen from the house of the man, **if the thief shall be found** he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:6). **The verse is speaking of** a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole. The *baraita* continues: Do **you say** that the verse is speaking **about one who** falsely **claims that** a deposit **was stolen, or is it** speaking **only about the thief himself,** teaching that if the actual thief is caught he must pay double payment? **When** the Torah **says** in the following verse: **"If the thief shall not be found**…the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:7–8), **the verse is speaking of one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, as it states that no other thief was found. Since the latter verse is speaking of one who falsely claims that a deposit was stolen, it stands to reason that the earlier verse is speaking of this case as well.

The *Gemara* now presents two *bratot* that interpret the two halves of the *psukim* — *im yimatze haganav* and *im lo yimatze haganav* — differently. The first *braita* reads both halves as addressing *toenei ta'anat ganav*: *beto'en ta'anat ganav ha'katuv medabber*. This is a striking read, since the plain sense of *im yimatze haganav* — "if the burglar is found" — seems straightforwardly to describe an actual burglar being caught and paying double. The *braita* asks: perhaps the first half refers to the *ganav* himself? No, says the *braita* — since the second half of the *pasuk* is clearly about a claim of *gneiva*, the first half must also be about a claim. It is an argument by parallel structure.

### Second Braita Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״אִם יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״ – בְּגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם לֹא יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״ – הֲרֵי טוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים: ״אִם יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״? בְּגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: When the Torah states: **"If the thief shall be found** he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:6), **the verse is speaking of the thief himself.** Do **you say** that it is speaking **about the thief himself, or is it** speaking **only about one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole? If so, then **when** the verse then **says: "If the thief shall not be found**…the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:7–8), the case of **one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit **is** already **stated. How,** then, **do I realize** the first verse about paying double: **"If the thief shall be found,"** so that it not be superfluous? It must be that **the** first **verse is speaking of the thief himself.**

The second *braita* adopts the more intuitive reading. *Im yimatze haganav* — the first half of the *pasuk* — *beganav atzmo ha'katuv medabber*: it speaks of the actual burglar. The *braita* asks: perhaps the first half is also about a claim? No — because the second half, *im lo yimatze haganav*, is precisely the scenario where the *ganav* is not found and a claim about what happened is made. That being so, the first half must be the contrasting case — where the *ganav* actually is found, and he is the real burglar.

### Consensus Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת, ״אִם לֹא יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״ – בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב כְּתִיב. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר רָבָא: ״אִם לֹא יִמָּצֵא״ כְּמָה שֶׁאָמַר, אֶלָּא שֶׁהוּא עַצְמוֹ גְּנָבוֹ – יְשַׁלֵּם שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Although the two *baraitot* disagree about the meaning of the earlier verse, **in any event everyone** agrees that the latter verse, which states: **"If the thief be not found [*im lo yimmatze hagannav*]**…shall pay double to his neighbor," is referring to a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit. **From where** is this interpretation **inferred** from the verse? **Rava said** that the verse should be understood as follows: **If it is not found [*im lo yimmatze*]** to be **as he said,** i.e., if his claim that the deposit was stolen is found to be untrue, **but he himself stole it, he shall pay double** to his neighbor.

Notwithstanding their disagreement about how to read the first half of the *pasuk*, both *bratot* agree on the essential point: *im lo yimatze haganav* in the second half of the *pasuk* is read as a case of *toenei ta'anat ganav* — a false sworn claim of *gneiva* — and that such a claim generates an obligation of *kefel*.

## Im Lo Interpretation

### Rava You're Ganav

The *Gemara* asks: how exactly do we read *toenei ta'anat ganav* into the words *im lo yimatze haganav*? Rava offers the reading: *im lo yimatze haganav k'mo she'amar ela she'hu atzmo genavo* — the burglar was not found as the *shomer* claimed, because there was no real burglar; rather, it turns out he himself was the *ganav*. The phrase *im lo yimatze haganav* is read not simply as the burglar being physically absent, but as the burglar not being findable because he is standing right there. The one who made the claim is the burglar.

### Oath Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּבִשְׁבוּעָה? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים״ – לִשְׁבוּעָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לִשְׁבוּעָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְדִין? נֶאֱמַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֱמַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לְמַעְלָה; מָה לְהַלָּן לִשְׁבוּעָה, אַף כָּאן לִשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** This raises a further question: **And from where do we** derive **that** this double payment of one who falsely claims that the deposit was stolen applies only **when** the bailee has taken **an oath** that it was stolen? The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "If the thief shall not be found, **the homeowner shall approach the judges** to determine if he laid his hand [*shalaḥ yado*] on his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:7). This means that he shall come to court **for** the purpose of taking **an oath.** Do **you say** he comes to court **for** the purpose of taking **an oath, or is it only for** the purpose of facing **judgment?** The meaning may be determined by means of a verbal analogy. **Laying** the **hand [*shliḥut yad*],** referring to misappropriation, **is stated later,** in the verse: "The oath of the Lord shall be between them both, to see whether he has not laid his hand [*shalaḥ yado*] on his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:10), **and laying the hand is stated above,** i.e., Exodus 22:7. **Just as laying** the **hand later** is referring explicitly **to an oath, so too** laying the hand **here** is referring **to an oath.**

This raises a further question: where exactly does the text signal that a *shvua* was taken? The *pasuk* says the owner was brought before the judges and testified that he did not misuse the property — but there is no explicit mention of an oath in this passage.

### Shomer Parallel

The derivation comes from a parallel. Later in the same section of *psukim* dealing with *shomrim*, the Torah states explicitly: *shvuat Hashem tihye bein shneihem im lo shalach yado bimleches re'ehu* — "an oath of God shall be between them that he did not misuse his fellow's property." The phrase *im lo shalach yado* appears both earlier, in our passage, and later, in the explicit oath context. The *braisa* derives: *ma le'halan l'shvua — af kan l'shvua* — just as there the formula involves an oath, so here too the formula involves an oath. This *gezeirah shavah* by analogy of language is the textual basis for requiring a *shvua* in order to incur *kefel* in the case of *toenei ta'anat ganav*.

## First Braita Logic…

### First Establishes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיבִי תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the two *baraitot* cited earlier: **Granted, according to the one who says** in the second *baraita* that **one** verse about double payment is speaking **about** the **thief and one** verse is speaking **about** a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **this is** why **two verses are written,** as each verse teaches a different *halakha*. **But according to the one who says** in the first *baraita* that **both** of the verses **are** speaking **about** a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **why do I** need **two verses?** One verse should be sufficient.

The *Gemara* then addresses a structural question about the first *braita's* position. If, according to the first *braita*, both halves of the *pasuk* address *toenei ta'anat ganav*, what work does the first half do independently? The answer: the first *pasuk* — *im yimatze haganav yeshalem shnayim* — establishes the fundamental *halacha* of *toenei ta'anat ganav*: that one who falsely swears the object was stolen and turns out to be the taker himself pays *kefel*.

## True Ganav Kefel

### True Ganav Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי טַעֲנַת אָבַד. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דְּלָא מְיַיתַּר, לְמַעוֹטֵי טַעֲנַת אָבַד מְנָא לֵיהּ? מִ״גַּנָּב–הַגַּנָּב״. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דְּמַיעֵט לֵיהּ טוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת אָבַד, ״גַּנָּב–הַגַּנָּב״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? אָמַר לָךְ: מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דְּהַאי ״גַּנָּב–הַגַּנָּב״ אַפְּקֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי טַעֲנַת אָבַד, דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say:** Both verses are necessary because **one** verse serves **to exclude** from double payment the case of one who falsely states **the claim** that the item has been **lost.** Double payment is paid only when the bailee falsely claims that the item under his care was stolen. The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **one** verse is speaking **about the thief and one** verse is speaking **about** a bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, so that **there is no superfluous** verse, **from where does he** learn **to exclude** from double payment a bailee who falsely states **the claim** that the item has been **lost?** The Gemara answers: He derives it **from** the fact that the verse could have stated: If **a thief [*gannav*]** shall not be found, but it states instead: "If **the thief [*hagannav*]** shall not be found." The Gemara asks: **And** since **according to the one who says** that **both** verses are speaking **about** a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit one of the verses **excludes** the case of a bailee who falsely **claims that** the deposit **was lost,** there seems to be no source to teach the *halakha* stated by Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba. Accordingly, **from where does he** derive the *halakha* taught by **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba,** that if the bailee slaughtered or sold the animal he pays a fourfold or fivefold payment?

The *Gemara* then confronts the challenge facing the first *braita* directly. If both *psukim* discuss only *toenei ta'anat ganav*, from where does the first *braita* derive that a *real ganav* — one who actually commits burglary — pays *kefel*? There is no explicit *pasuk* remaining to establish this.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לֵיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב; דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן, מָה לְהַלָּן בִּשְׁבוּעָה – אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁבוּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let it be derived by an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of the bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, because if one is obligated to pay double payment for falsely claiming that a deposit was stolen, which constitutes passive theft, all the more so must a thief himself pay double payment, this derivation is not possible. The reason is that **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source.** In other words, a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference cannot be more stringent than the *halakha* of the source from which it is derived. Consequently, on the basis of this *a fortiori* inference, it would have to be concluded that **just as there,** in the source case, the double payment is required only when the guilty party took **an oath, so too here,** when the thief himself pays double, it is only when he took **an oath** that he did not steal it.

One might suppose the answer lies in a *kal vachomer*: if someone who merely makes a false *claim* of *gneiva* pays *kefel*, surely the actual *ganav* who committed the act should pay at least as much. The *Gemara* entertains this possibility.

---

## First Braita Logic…

### Second Excludes

The second *pasuk* — *im lo yimatze haganav* — according to the first *braita*, serves a different function: it excludes the case of *toenei ta'anat aveidah* from the obligation of *kefel*. The repeated treatment of *toenei ta'anat ganav* in a second *pasuk* is used to draw the contrast: this applies only when one claims *gneiva*, not when one claims *aveidah*.

---

## True Ganav Kefel…

### Dayo Rule

However, the *kal vachomer* cannot be used to derive that the real *ganav* pays *kefel*. The principle of *dayo* — the rule of limitation — holds that a conclusion derived by *kal vachomer* cannot exceed the case from which it is drawn. Here, the base case of *toenei ta'anat ganav* requires a *shvua*. Were one to extend this to the actual *ganav* via *kal vachomer*, one would be forced by *dayo* to say the real *ganav* also pays *kefel* only if he takes an oath — which is absurd and contrary to *halacha*. The *kal vachomer* is therefore blocked, and the first *braita* cannot derive *kefel* for the real *ganav* from these *psukim* alone.

## Tana d'Bei Source

### Intact Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: יֵאָמֵר ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״ – וְהַכֹּל בַּכְּלָל! אִילּוּ כָּךְ, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר הַקָּרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אַף כֹּל הַקָּרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. מָה יֵשׁ לְךָ לְהָבִיא – שֶׂה, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״שֶׂה״ – הֲרֵי שֶׂה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״גְּנֵיבָה״? לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **He derives** the obligation of a thief to pay double payment without having taken an oath from a third verse: "If the theft shall be found in his possession alive, whether it is an ox, or a donkey, or a sheep, he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:3). The double payment in this verse applies to all items, as is derived **from what the school of Ḥizkiyya taught. As the school of Ḥizkiyya taught: Let** the verse **state** only **"ox" and "theft," and all** items **would be included.** Why was it necessary to also mention "donkey" and "sheep"? **Had** the verse been written in **this** manner, without "donkey," **I would have said: Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail,** "ox," are clearly **defined as an item that is sacrificed on the altar, so too everything that can be sacrificed on the altar** is subject to double payment, but other items. **What is there for you to include** in this category that is not already listed? **A sheep. When** the verse **states "sheep," a sheep is thereby stated** explicitly. Therefore, the details of "ox" cannot be written to include specifically items that can be sacrificed on the altar, e.g., a sheep. **How** then **do I realize** the meaning of **"theft,"** i.e., what is the category of items derived from the detail and generalization? It must be that it serves **to include all** types of **items** in the requirement of double payment.

The resolution comes from a different source entirely — *tana d'vei Chizkiyah*. The *pasuk* in question is: *im hematzei timatzei be'yado ha'gneivah* — "if the stolen object is found in his possession" — *mi'shor ad chamor ad seh chayim shnayim yeshalem* — "from ox to donkey to sheep, alive, he shall pay double." This *pasuk* is describing a case where the *ganav* still has the stolen animal in his possession — *be'yado*, in his hand; *chayim*, alive, meaning he has not yet slaughtered it. If the stolen animal is found with him intact and alive, he pays double. The contrast is with the case of slaughter or sale, where he pays four or five. This is the explicit *pasuk* establishing that a real *ganav* pays *kefel* — independently of any oath, independently of any claim.

### Slaughtered Sold

The contrast within the same section is with the case where the burglar slaughtered or sold the animal, in which case the Torah mandates payment of four or five times the value, as the case may be.

### Multiple Animals

This leads to a fresh inquiry: if the *pasuk* was simply trying to establish *kefel* for all stolen objects, why did it need to list *shor, chamor, seh*, and *chayim* — multiple specific animals plus an additional modifier? Would it not have sufficed to mention one? The *Gemara* takes up this question in the next section.

---

## Final Klal Prat

### Shor Alone

If the *pasuk* had mentioned only *shor* — ox — one might have concluded that *kefel* applies only to animals that can be brought as sacrifices on the altar, since the ox belongs to that category.

### Chamor Addition

The addition of *chamor* — donkey — which cannot be brought as a sacrifice, extends the category beyond animals offered on the *mizbeiach*. But one might now say: perhaps it is limited to animals sanctified as firstborn, *kedosh bivchorah* — a category that includes the donkey.

### Seh Broadens

The further addition of *seh* — sheep — goes beyond even that. But even with all three animals listed, one might still argue that *kefel* is limited to the category of *ba'alei chayim* — living creatures — and does not extend to inanimate objects.

### Chayim Ribuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאָמֵר ״שׁוֹר״, וַ״חֲמוֹר״ וָ״שֶׂה״ וְ״חַיִּים״ – וְהַכֹּל בַּכְּלָל! אִילּוּ כֵּן, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, אַף כֹּל בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים. מָה יֵשׁ לְךָ לְהָבִיא – שְׁאָר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״חַיִּים״ – הֲרֵי חַיִּים אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״גְּנֵיבָה״? לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר; ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* then asks: **Let** the verse **say** only **"theft," and "ox," and "donkey" and "sheep" and "alive," and** in that manner **all** items would be **included.** The *baraita* answers: **Had** the verse been written in **this** manner, **I would say: Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail,** "ox," "sheep," and "donkey," are clearly **defined as** items that are **animals, so too all animals** are subject to double payment, but not inanimate objects. **What is there for you to include** in this category that is not already listed? **Animals other** than oxen, sheep, and donkeys. **When** the verse **states "alive," animals are thereby stated** explicitly. Therefore, the details of "ox," "sheep," and "donkey" cannot be written to include animals. **How** then **do I realize** the meaning of **"theft,"** i.e., what is the category of items derived from the detail and generalization? It must be that it serves **to include all** types of **items** in the requirement of double payment. If so, **why do I** need the phrase **"if** the theft **shall be found [*himmatze timmatze*]"?**

The word *chayim* — "alive" — in the *pasuk* resolves this final ambiguity. One might have thought *chayim* was simply describing the condition of the animal: it is still alive because the *ganav* has not yet slaughtered it. But the *Gemara* reads it as the final *ribuy*: had the Torah wanted only to limit *kefel* to the animal kingdom, it would have sufficed to say *chayim* alone without listing *shor, chamor*, and *seh*. The fact that it says all of those specific animals forces us to read *chayim* as expanding the category — *l'rabos kol davar*, to include the entire universe of things. The word *gneivah* in the same *pasuk* thus serves as the broadest possible inclusion: *kefel* applies to all stolen objects, not merely to animals or to any particular subset of them. This is the source, from *tana d'vei Chizkiyah*, that establishes *kefel* for the actual *ganav* across the full range of property.
