# Ketubot 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sale of Debt & Mechilah

### Wife's Debt & Ketubah Sa…

Daf 86 continues the Gemara's extended treatment of a husband's rights to his wife's property — and, in particular, the legal complications that arise when a wife holds debts owed to her by third parties. Can she perform *mechilah*, waiving those debts? And what happens when a woman sells her *ketubah* to a third party, subsequently dies, and her daughter seeks to waive that same debt?

These questions expose a fundamental tension in *halacha* regarding the transfer of abstract obligations.

### Abstract Debt Transfer

The problem is this: if Reuven sells to Charlie the debt that Michael owes him, it is not the case that Michael now directly owes Charlie anything. A debt is an abstract thing — how does one transfer ownership of an abstraction? Tosafot raises exactly this difficulty. One possible answer is that where there are liens on land, the transfer is coherent, because liens represent a kind of partial ownership of a physical asset that can be conveyed. But what about a debt with no land attached, no lien at all?

The honest answer is that in *halacha*, it is exceedingly difficult to transfer a debt in a fully direct sense. What actually happens is closer to this: Michael still owes the money to Reuven; what Charlie has acquired is the exclusive right to collect and pocket those funds. The money is still, in a meaningful sense, owed to Reuven — Charlie merely has a kind of automatic-transfer arrangement, as though Reuven's account has been linked to Charlie's. One might contrast this with secular law, which handles non-physical objects — intellectual property, corporate shares, assigned receivables — with considerably greater conceptual ease. *Halacha* demands something more concrete, and it is precisely this concreteness that makes pure debt transfer so difficult to execute cleanly.

The implication is striking: since the money is still fundamentally owed to Reuven, Reuven retains the legal capacity to perform *mechilah* — to waive Michael's debt entirely. The *Gemara* states this explicitly: *HaMocher Shtar Chovo LeChavero VeChazar UMichalo, Machul* — if the seller subsequently forgives the debt, the forgiveness is effective. *VeAfiliu Yoresh* — even the heir of the seller can waive the debt before Charlie has had the opportunity to collect. This follows from the principle that an heir is not a transferee of an object but rather a continuation of the person himself; the money is still owed to that person's estate, and the heir steps into that identity rather than receiving a transferred asset.

This arrangement is, as one would readily notice, a gaping loophole. No rational actor in a functioning economy would purchase a debt that the seller could annul at any moment. The *Gemara* is about to turn to several attempts to close this gap.

### Shmuel: Waiver Post-Sale

The *Gemara* cites Shmuel's ruling directly: *HaMocher Shtar Chov LeChaveiro VeChazar UMichalo, Machul* — it is waived — *VeAfiliu Yoresh Mochal* — and even the heir can waive it. This is Shmuel's *chiddush*: counterintuitive as it may seem, even after the sale, the original obligee or his heir retains the power of *mechilah*. The *Gemara* now proceeds to address the obvious commercial problem this creates.

### Rav Huna's Workaround

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua offers a practical workaround. If Charlie wants to protect his investment, he should go to Michael, *mekarkesh lei zuzei* — jingle some coins in front of him — and induce Michael to write a new debt document directly in Charlie's name. The mechanics are straightforward: Charlie approaches Michael and proposes to tear up the old debt document — which reflects the thousand dollars Michael owes Reuven and which Charlie purchased — and in exchange for some cash payment, say one hundred dollars, Michael agrees to write a fresh *shtar* directly obligating himself to Charlie for nine hundred dollars. Charlie spends the hundred dollars but he has now cut Reuven out of the picture entirely. Michael's new debt runs straight to Charlie, not through Reuven's estate.

This solution, however, is imperfect in several respects. Charlie must spend money to execute it. The old liens attached to the original debt are extinguished — the new *shtar* is dated from now and carries no prior lien priority. And crucially, the arrangement requires Michael to be somewhat unaware that he could achieve a better outcome by approaching Reuven directly, offering Reuven, say, five hundred dollars in exchange for full *mechilah* of the thousand, cutting Charlie out entirely. Charlie must act before Michael realizes his leverage. One might also ask: what if Michael simply pays Reuven directly, since the debt is still technically owed to him? The answer is that while the *mechilah* would be effective, a direct payment to Reuven by Michael would not discharge Michael's obligation to Charlie — the rights to collect and pocket have been conveyed, and Michael cannot unilaterally reroute them. That asymmetry is, as noted, a peculiar feature of this mechanism.

### Dinei d'Garmi Doctrine

A second, more systematic attempt to close the loophole comes from Meimar. *Amar Meimar: man d'dan dinei d'garmi magbi bei d'mei shetar mealya* — Meimar says: the one who adjudicates the laws of *garmi* can collect from the offender the full value of the debt document. That is to say: if Reuven performs *mechilah* on Michael's debt after having sold the *shtar* to Charlie, causing Charlie a direct financial loss, then under the doctrine of *dinei d'garmi*, Charlie can sue Reuven for the full value of what was waived.

### Causation Continuum

What is *garmi*? It is related conceptually to the word *grama* — indirect causation — but the addition of the *yud* functions as an intensifier, denoting a higher degree of directness. The broader framework of tort liability in *halacha* requires, at minimum, both a direct physical act (*ma'aseh mazik*) and actual damage to a physical object. Two separate problems arise in borderline cases: the causation may be indirect (*grama*), or the damaged "thing" may not be a physical object at all — as when someone tears up a debt document, destroying not a valuable physical item but an abstract legal entitlement. In the case of the forged or waived *shtar*, both concerns apply: Reuven's act of *mechilah* is not a physical act against Charlie's property, and what Charlie lost was not a tangible object but an abstract right to collect.

Logically, both *grama* and the destruction of non-physical value should be exempt from *mazik* liability. But the Rabbis recognized a substantial zone of practical injustice: there are acts where the causation is so proximate and the loss so definite that exempting the actor entirely would be untenable. They therefore identified a portion of the causation continuum — those cases most closely resembling direct *mazik* — and designated them *garmi*, applying liability even though the formal requirements of classical tort are not met. There is a debate whether this is *d'oraissa* or *derabbanan*, but proceeding on the assumption that it is rabbinic, the principle is that cases falling into this category — high causal proximity, definite financial loss, even without physical damage to an object — are actionable.

Tosafot raises a further question: how much does Reuven owe Charlie if *dinei d'garmi* applies? Charlie purchased the debt for, say, seven hundred dollars on a thousand-dollar obligation. The phrase *d'mei shetar mealya* — full value of the debt document — suggests Charlie recovers the full thousand dollars. If so, the system is genuinely protected: whether Charlie collects from Michael or whether Reuven performs *mechilah* and Charlie sues Reuven, Charlie is made whole for a thousand dollars. That makes purchasing the debt commercially rational. If Charlie could only recover his purchase price of seven hundred dollars, the protection would be weaker — he would still have to absorb litigation costs and uncertainty to recover what he paid, and the market for debt sales would function poorly.

### Rafram v. Rav Ashi

The *Gemara* records a concrete application of this principle: *Hava uvda ve-khafyei Rafram le-Rav Ashi ve-agbi be-khitsurei de-tzalmei* — there was an actual case, and Rafram compelled Rav Ashi to pay, like full-value wooden planks — an idiom meaning he made him pay in full.

Two interpretations appear in Rashi, one here and one in *Bava Kamma* where the same incident is recorded in parallel. The Rashi here understands that Rav Ashi was inclined to let a defendant go free and Rafram persuaded him to adjudicate the case as *dinei d'garmi*, ruling that the party had caused real, compensable damage rather than mere *grama*. The Rashi in *Bava Kamma* takes a more dramatic approach, suggesting that Rav Ashi himself, as a child, once destroyed someone's debt documents (*shtaros*), and Rafram — when Rav Ashi had grown — compelled him to pay for it under *dinei d'garmi*. This second interpretation opens a fascinating subsidiary question about a minor's culpability for damages: even though minors are generally exempt from monetary liability, there may be some form of *dinei shamayim* obligation that follows them into adulthood — a full discussion that belongs to *Bava Kamma*. In any event, the operative conclusion is that *dinei d'garmi* is the accepted doctrine and constitutes a genuine mechanism for closing the *mechilah* loophole.

## Creditor vs. Wife Priori…

### Payment Distribution Rule

With this digression into *Bava Kamma*-territory behind us, the *Gemara* returns to its main track. Meimar cites a ruling in the name of Rav Chama: *Hai man de-ika lei kesuvas isha ve-baal chov* — if someone has against him both his ex-wife's *ketubah* claim and a creditor's debt — *ve-is lei ara ve-is lei zuzei* — and he possesses both land and cash — then *le-baal chov mesalkinan lei be-zuzei, le-isha mesalkinan lah be-ara* — to the creditor we pay cash, and to the wife we give land. *Hai ke-dinei ve-hai ke-dinei* — each according to her own legal category.

### Three Obligation Types

The reason is as follows: a creditor lent cash, and is therefore entitled to receive cash in return. The *ketubah*, by contrast, is not grounded in a cash transaction; it begins as an abstract matrimonial obligation with liens on land, and that is how it is fundamentally conceptualized. Rashi notes that even though part of the *ketubah* includes the *nedunya* — the dowry, which may well have been cash or moveable objects — this is bundled into the general framework, and the *ketubah* as a whole is understood as a land-based lien.

Tosafot, building on Rabbeinu Tam, elaborates a three-way taxonomy. For the *malveh* — the lender — if cash is on hand, the debtor cannot pay him with land; you borrowed money, you repay money, as the *Gemara* rules here. For the *nizak* — one who suffered property damage — even if cash is available, the tortfeasor may satisfy the debt with land. This is counterintuitive: the innocent injured party receives something less liquid than cash, with no apparent benefit to him. Yet that is the rule. For the *sechir* — the hired laborer — even if the employer has no cash at hand, he must go ahead, liquidate assets, and produce cash to pay wages: *zil terach ve-zavin ve-aisi zuzei*. Three scenarios, three different obligations — the first requiring cash only if available, the second permitting land even when cash is available, the third requiring the party to actively acquire cash even when none is on hand.

### Single Asset Priority

The next complication arises when there is only a single plot of land. *Ve-i lo ika ella chada ara ve-lo chazya ella le-chad* — if the land is sufficient to pay only one creditor — *le-baal chov yahavinan lei, le-isha lo yahavinan lah* — we give it to the creditor and not to the ex-wife. This holds even if, as one might expect, the wife's lien is the older one.

### Tosafot Policy Logic

*Mai taama?* — what is the reason? *Yoseir mi-mah she-ish rotzeh lisa, isha rotzah le-hinasei* — women are more eager to marry than men. This is not a legal analysis in the narrow sense; it is a frank policy determination, as Tosafot explains. The rule cannot simply track lien priority, because the policy consequences of doing so would be intolerable for the credit market. People remain married for many years — often the wife's lien will predate any creditor's. If we consistently gave the single asset to the earlier lienor, which would frequently be the wife, creditors would have strong reason to hesitate before extending loans. The alternative — giving the land to the creditor — might give women some pause before marrying, but Tosafot's conclusion is that women will marry regardless. The more pressing systemic concern is *ne'ilat delet* — the closing of the door to credit. Someone must be the disadvantaged party in this zero-sum scenario; the policy judgment is that the risk of chilling the credit market outweighs the risk of deterring marriage.

Tosafot also notes the apparent tension with an earlier *mishnah* — *Lakushot shebahim*, where the ruling seems to favor the woman over the creditor — and works to distinguish the circumstances. The concern there is for the woman's welfare where a creditor has alternative recourse and the woman may not. Here, where there is a single asset and the question is purely one of priority, the credit-system rationale governs.

## Payment as Mitzvah

### Forced Land Sale Case

Rav Pappa addresses a question posed in the name of Rav Chama: if someone owes money and has land, and the creditor comes to collect, and the debtor says "go ahead and take my land" — *amrinan lei lo*, we tell him no. He cannot simply hand over the land; he must sell it and give cash. This seemed to establish a rule that even when the debtor has only land, he must liquidate it and pay money.

### Hidden Cash Exception

However, Rav Chama clarifies: that ruling applies only to the specific case where the debtor had cash on hand but artificially removed it from his own possession — *tola maosei be-oved kochavim hava* — he transferred or entrusted his money to a non-Jew so that when the creditor arrived he could claim he had no cash. *Hu asa shelo k'hogan lifichach asu vo shelo k'hogen* — because he acted improperly toward the creditor, the court treats him improperly in return, compelling him to sell the land and produce cash. But if the debtor genuinely has no cash and has not played any such games, he may pay the creditor with land. The forced-liquidation rule, in other words, is not a general principle but a punitive response to bad-faith evasion.

### Mitzvah Framework

Rav Pappa further teaches: *prias ba'al chov mitzvah* — paying off a creditor is a *mitzvah*. This is not merely a matter of monetary rights and liens; it carries the additional dimension of a positive religious obligation.

### Minor Heirs Exception

The *nafka mina* from this characterization is significant: if a debtor dies leaving minor heirs who are now responsible for the estate, can the creditor collect from the estate while the children are under age? Under a purely monetary-rights framework, the creditor holds a *shtar mekuyam*, his lien is established, and one would expect him to be able to exercise it against the estate regardless of the heirs' ages. But if the mechanism of collection depends in part on the obligor's being commanded in the *mitzvah* of repayment — if that religious dimension is part of what enables compelled collection — then minors, who are not yet obligated in *mitzvot*, cannot be compelled. Even with valid liens, the creditor must wait until the heirs reach majority before collecting.

### Refusal Challenge

Rav Kahana presses Rav Pappa: according to your position that paying is a *mitzvah*, what if an adult debtor simply says he does not want to perform the *mitzvah*? Does the entire enforcement mechanism collapse?

### Compulsion Authority

Rav Pappa replies: *tanina* — we have learned this. The rule is that lashes apply to transgressions of negative commandments. But for positive commandments — *aseh sukkah ve-eini oseh, lulav ve-eini oseh* — if someone refuses to perform a positive obligation while still able to, *makin oto ad she-tetze nafsho* — one may beat him to the very verge of death. The court may use physical compulsion to enforce the performance of *mitzvot*. The refusal of a debtor to pay is therefore no escape; he is subject to *kfiyah datit* — coercive religious enforcement — precisely because repayment is a *mitzvah* and courts may compel its performance. The one exception remains minors, who are not yet bound by the *mitzvah* and therefore cannot be compelled under this mechanism.

## Get After 30 Days

### Get Placement Case

The *Gemara* now pivots in a seemingly abrupt direction, though the conceptual thread — control, *kinyan*, and deferred effect — connects directly to earlier discussions in the *mishnah*. Rami bar Chama poses a question to Rav Chisda: a man says to his wife, *Harei zeh gittech* — here is your *get* — *velo tigarshi bo ela achar sheloshim yom* — but you may not be divorced through it until after thirty days. She takes the document and places it *betzidei reshut harabbim* — in the side areas of the public thoroughfare. Does the *get* take effect after thirty days?

The question turns on what degree of control over the *get* is required at the moment it takes effect. For a *get* to be valid when its effect is deferred, there must be some residual sense of possession or control at the time of activation. Is placement in the *tzidei reshut harabbim* — the sidewalk-like margins of the public domain — sufficient to establish that control?

### Rav & Shmuel Position

The *Gemara* answers first from Rav and Shmuel, who both rule: *einah megureshet* — she is not divorced. Their basis is an earlier ruling about a debtor's cattle found in the *reshut harabbim* at the time of his death: the Gemara there established that property in the public domain is not under the owner's control — that was precisely the condition enabling creditors or the wife to seize it. Since *reshut harabbim* is defined as space outside one's control, the *get* placed there is not under the wife's control, and therefore the *kinyan* cannot take effect. Rav and Shmuel apparently treat *tzidei reshut harabbim* as equivalent to *reshut harabbim* itself.

### Rav Nachman Analogy

Against this, the *Gemara* proposes Rav Nachman's ruling in the name of Rabba bar Avuha as a counter-source: *meshokh parah zu ve-lo tehei kenuyah lekha ad achar sheloshim yom, kanah* — draw this cow and you will own it only after thirty days — the acquisition works, *va-afilu omedet ba-agam* — even if after thirty days the cow is standing in a swamp. A swamp is like an alleyway (*simta*): not private property, but not a public thoroughfare either. Being in such a semi-private space is sufficient to count as being under the buyer's control for the *kinyan* to take effect later. Might not *tzidei reshut harabbim* — the side areas of the public domain — be analogous to a swamp?

### Swamp-Sides Distinction

The *Gemara* rejects the analogy: *lo, agam le-chud ve-tzidei reshut harabbim le-chud* — the swamp is one category, the sides of the public domain are another. The *tzidei reshut harabbim* are so proximate to the thoroughfare that they are treated as part of it, as outside personal control. A swamp, by contrast, is sufficiently removed from public traffic to remain under the individual's domain. The underlying principle is clear: for a deferred *kinyan* to take effect, the object must be under the recipient's control at the moment of activation. Alleyways and swamps qualify; the margins of the public thoroughfare do not.

### Multiple Positions

The *Gemara* then presents the two versions of this dispute in reverse order: one version begins from Rav Nachman's ruling and uses Rav and Shmuel to rebut it; the other begins from Rav and Shmuel and uses Rav Nachman as the challenge. In each version, the same resolution applies: the swamp and the *tzidei reshut harabbim* are distinct categories. Tosafot adds the important clarification that this entire discussion assumes the husband said *me-hayom* — from today — since without that formulation a deferred *get* of this type would not function at all. This case thus draws together several threads from the earlier *mishnah* — the *kinyan le-achar zman*, the significance of *reshut harabbim*, and the condition of ongoing control — and resolves them through the combined weight of Rav, Shmuel, and Rav Nachman.

## Wife's Oath Right

### Storekeeper Oath Rule

The *Gemara* moves now to a new *mishnah*. *Ha-moshiv es ishto chanvanis* — if a man set his wife up as a storekeeper — *o she-minah apitropya* — or appointed her as an *apitropya*, a guardian or agent with power of attorney over his estate — *harei zeh mashbia kol zman she-yirtze* — he may make her take an oath whenever he wishes. The mechanism is not an audit or an accounting but a sworn declaration that she has not skimmed from the business, not pocketed money. This oath-compulsion right follows from the ordinary rules applicable to an employer and employee.

### Rabbi Eliezer Expansion

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: *afillu al pilchah ve-al issatah* — even regarding her spindle and her dough. That is, even without any formal commercial arrangement, even when the wife is engaged only in ordinary domestic tasks — spinning wool, preparing bread — the husband may still compel an oath. His reasoning: there will always be some moment in the course of domestic life when the wife is handling the husband's property, *she-ein lecha isha she-lo na'asit apitropya sha'a achat be-chayei ba'alah* — there is no woman who has not served at some point as a de facto steward of her husband's property. Since she is effectively always in some *apitropya* relationship, the oath right always applies.

### Snake Proverb

The Sages respond: *ein adam dar im nachash bi-kfifa* — a person cannot live under the same roof with a snake. Allowing the husband to demand an oath at any time, over the grocery money or the dough, creates an intolerable atmosphere of suspicion. The *Gemara* works through the logical implications: if Rabbi Eliezer means the husband may do this entirely *le-chatchila* — that is, without any pre-existing oath context — then the Sages' response is apt and forceful. If he means it only as *gilgul* — the bundling doctrine — then one might ask why the wife should care.

### Gilgul Bundling

The *Gemara* answers: she cares even about the *gilgul* case. *Gilgul* means that once a party is already required to take an oath about one matter, additional obligations may be packaged into that same oath. Even this limited application of Rabbi Eliezer's position is psychologically devastating: *keivan de-daikat batrai kulei hai* — you are so exacting with me — *lo matzina le-meidar bahadach* — I cannot continue to live with you. It is one thing to be subject to oath-compulsion in the context of running your million-dollar business. It is another to have the same distrust extended to the few coins spent on groceries. The *ta shema* from the *baraita* confirms that Rabbi Eliezer does indeed mean the husband may act *le-chatchila*, and *shema mina* — we learn accordingly — that this is his explicit position.

## Oath Waiver Mechanics

### Wife Exemption

The new *mishnah* introduces the mechanism by which the husband may waive his oath rights entirely. *Katav lah neder u-shvu'a ein li alayich* — if he writes to her that he has no right to impose a *neder* or *shvu'a* upon her — *eino yachol le-hashbia* — he can no longer compel her to swear. A *neder* would take the form: "If I stole anything from you, let the fruits of the world be forbidden to me." A *shvu'a* is a direct oath: "I swear I did not steal from you." By writing this waiver — whether in the *ketubah* or as a separate document — he relinquishes the right to demand either.

### Heir Limitations

However, the waiver is limited to what was in his power to waive. *Aval mashbia hu et yoresheha ve-et ha-ba'im bi-rshuta* — but he may still compel her heirs and those who come through her authority to take an oath. When the heirs come to collect the *ketubah*, for instance, he may make them swear they have not already been paid. The exemption he granted runs only to her personally, not automatically to all who derive rights through her. This closely parallels the earlier *mishnah*'s discussion of *mechilah*: just as a seller cannot fully transfer a debt because the obligation runs to him and not to the buyer, so too a waiver of oath rights is interpreted narrowly unless its language is explicitly expansive.

### Explicit Language Rule

The *Gemara* establishes that the narrowest interpretation governs by default. A general waiver of the husband's own oath rights does not, without explicit language, extend to his heirs or to those to whom the wife sells her *ketubah*. The language must be spelled out.

### Complete Waiver

To foreclose all avenues, the husband must state: *neder u-shvu'a ein li alayich ve-al yoreshaich ve-al ha-ba'im bi-rshutaich* — I have no oath right over you, your heirs, or those who come through your authority — and further, *ve-lo ani ve-lo yorshai ve-chol ha-ba'im bi-rshuti* — neither I, nor my heirs, nor anyone acting through my rights. *Eino yachol le-hashbia* — in that case, none of these parties can compel an oath — *lo hu ve-lo yorshav ve-lo ha-ba'im bi-rshuto* — not from her, her heirs, or those who come through her.

### Post-Mortem Status

What happens after the husband's death? *Halcha mikever ba'alah le-beit aviha* — she went from her husband's grave back to her father's house — or she returned to live in the home of her father-in-law — *ve-lo na'asit apitropya* — and no new stewardship arrangement was established. In that case, *ein ha-yorshim mashbi'in otah* — the heirs cannot compel her to take an oath — because Reuven, the husband, had waived all oath rights in his lifetime, and her current role involves nothing beyond what was covered by that prior waiver.

### New Stewardship

But *ve-im na'asit apitropya* — if after her husband's death the heirs actively appoint her to continue managing the estate — *ha-yorshim mashbi'in otah al ha-atid lavo, ve-ein mashbi'in otah al ma she-avar* — the heirs may make her swear regarding future dealings but not past ones. The new appointment creates a fresh legal relationship with fresh oath rights; it is entirely unaffected by the deceased husband's prior waiver, which pertained only to obligations arising from her relationship with him. What he waived was his own authority and that of those acting through him — once that relationship ends and a new independent arrangement begins, the waiver does not reach it.

The general principle running through all of these cases is consistent: a person may waive only what is within his authority to waive. Additional parties, additional relationships, and future obligations require explicit language and cannot be assumed from a general grant. Absent such explicit language, the most restrictive interpretation prevails.
