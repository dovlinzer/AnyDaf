# Sukkah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gazul, Yavesh, Hadar

### Previous: Gazul, Yavesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּבְנֵי כְרַכִּין, שֶׁהָיוּ מוֹרִישִׁין אֶת לוּלְבֵיהֶן לִבְנֵי בְנֵיהֶן! אָמְרוּ (לָהֶם): מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? אֵין שְׁעַת הַדְּחָק רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **involving city dwellers** who lived in an area distant from the region where the four species grow, **who would bequeath their *lulavim* to their grandchildren,** even though they were completely dry. The Sages **said to him:** Is there **proof from there** that species that are dry remain fit for use? Actions taken **in exigent circumstances are not proof.** In typical circumstances, it would be prohibited to use those species.

Today's *daf* is 32, and we pick up near the bottom of 31b, three lines in, moving on from the various invalidities of the *lulav*. We have already covered *gazul* and *yavesh* — two of the principal disqualifications. *Gazul* raised all the issues of ownership and the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. *Yavesh* opened into a central discussion of the requirement of *hadar* — beauty or splendor — a concept derived from the *esrog* but applied across the *arba minim*.

### Hadar Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְגָדוֹל כְּדֵי שֶׁיֶּאֱחוֹז שְׁנַיִם בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵי הָדָר? לָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: לוּלָב בְּיָמִין וְאֶתְרוֹג בִּשְׂמֹאל, זִימְנִין דְּמִחַלְּפִי לֵיהּ וְאָתֵי לְאַפּוּכִינְהוּ וְאָתֵי לְאִיפְּסוֹלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And in a large** *etrog*, the maximum measure is **so that one could hold two in his one hand;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says:** It is fit **even** if it is so large that he can hold only **one in his two hands.** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda? Is it **not due to** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda **requires beauty** in an *etrog*? The Gemara answers: **No,** the rationale is **as Rabba said:** One holds **the *lulav* in the right** hand **and the *etrog* in the left. Sometimes,** when one is handed the four species, **they will exchange them for him,** placing the three species in his left hand and the *etrog* in his right, **and** then **he will come to switch them** and place each in the appropriate hand. However, if the *etrog* is too large, he will be unable to hold the *etrog* and the *lulav* together, and he will **come to render** the *etrog* **unfit,** as it is apt to fall.

Rabbi Yehuda, at the conclusion of that sugya, rejects the requirement of *hadar* as applied both to *lulav* and to *esrog*. Even the *esrog*, where the word appears in its original context, is governed by other criteria not rooted in *hadar* per se — criteria related to the fullness of the fruit and its being a fully developed specimen — rather than beauty in the strict aesthetic sense.

### Baked Lulavim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֶתְרוֹג הַיָּשָׁן — פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes: **Come** and **hear** another proof that, with regard to an *etrog*, Rabbi Yehuda does not require beauty: **An old *etrog* is unfit. Rabbi Yehuda deems it fit.** This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rava, who holds that everyone agrees that an *etrog* requires beauty. The Gemara concludes: It is, indeed, **a conclusive refutation** of Rava's opinion.

The other important issue that emerged was the *gemara* discussing *b'kafiffin* — dried-up *lulavim* — and whether one may recite a *bracha* over them. The Rabbis concluded with the principle *ein sha'as ha'dechak ra'aya* — the fact that something is permitted in a time of emergency is not proof that it is valid under normal conditions. This raised questions among the *rishonim*: can one use otherwise invalid *arba minim* and recite a *bracha* over them, in a situation analogous to the *sha'as ha'dechak* described in the *gemara*?

## Asheira & Ir Nidachas

### Asheira, Ir Nidachas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. וְשֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה — פָּסוּל? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: לוּלָב שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The *lulav* **of a tree worshipped as idolatry [*asheira*] and** a *lulav* **from a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry, which must be burned along with all the city's property, are unfit. **And is** a *lulav* **of an *asheira* unfit? But didn't Rava say** with regard to **a *lulav* of idolatry: One should not take it** to fulfill the mitzva *ab initio*; **however, if he took it, it is fit** and he fulfills his obligation after the fact? Apparently, a *lulav* from an *asheira* is fit.

We now move on from *gazul* and *yavesh* to the next category of invalidities: *shel asheira veshel ir nidachas*. The *gemara* states that a *lulav* taken from an *asheira* — a tree that is worshiped as an idol — or from an *ir nidachas* — a city whose inhabitants worshiped *avodah zarah* and which must therefore be destroyed and burned in its entirety — is *pasul*, invalid.

### Rava: Lo Yitol

But this immediately raises a difficulty. The *gemara* cites Rava: *lulav shel avodah zarah lo yitol, ve'im natal kasher* — a *lulav* of idolatry, one should not take it, but if one did take and use it, one has fulfilled the *mitzvah*. Why, then, does the *mishna* categorically invalidate a *lulav* of *asheira*? Should it not follow the same rule — do not take it, but if you did, you are *yotzei*?

### Avodah Zarah Definition

Before addressing the resolution, an important clarification is in order. *Avodah zarah* in this context does not mean an idolatrous religion. It means a specific physical object — an idol — that has been worshiped. The legal framework here concerns not the religion of *avodah zarah* or what constitutes forbidden worship, but rather: when worship is directed toward an object, that object becomes an idol and becomes *assur b'hana'ah* — forbidden to derive benefit from. The further question is what else, connected to that object, also becomes *assur b'hana'ah* by extension.

To illustrate: if someone picks up a hymnbook from a Hindu religious service, that alone does not make it *tashmishei avodah zarah*. The operative question for *issur hana'ah* is whether the object was used in direct connection with the idol itself, because the idol is *assur b'hana'ah* and objects connected to it become *assur b'hana'ah* by extension.

### Tashmishei AZ

Rashi gives two scenarios in which a *lulav* can become *shel avodah zarah*. The first: *sheshimeshu bo l'avodah zarah l'chabed lefaneha karka* — it was used as a broom to sweep the ground in front of the idol. This is the category of *tashmishei avodah zarah* — an appurtenance used in the service of the idol. The second scenario Rashi raises: *she'avodatah b'lulav l'ha'avir lefaneha ul'zorkah bah* — the *lulav* itself was used in an act of worship before the idol, such as waving it in front of the idol. This also falls under *tashmishei avodah zarah*, as distinct from *tikroves avodah zarah*, which would apply if the *lulav* were actually offered up as a sacrifice to the idol.

It is worth noting that *tikroves avodah zarah* does not even require a physical idol to be present: if someone slaughters an animal and offers it to Zeus, even without any idol in the room, it becomes forbidden as an offering to a pagan deity. But in our case, what matters is the *lulav*'s use as an appurtenance of the idol's service, which renders it *assur b'hana'ah*.

Given all of this, the *gemara* asks: if this *lulav* is *assur b'hana'ah* and you use it on *Sukkos*, it seems you should not take it. But *ve'im natal kasher* — if you did, you fulfilled the *mitzvah*. How so, if it is forbidden in benefit?

### Mitzvot Lav Lehenot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בַּאֲשֵׁרָה דְמֹשֶׁה עָסְקִינַן, דְּכַתּוֹתֵי מְיכַתַּת שִׁיעוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Here,** in the mishna, **we are dealing with the *asheira* of Moses,** depicted in the Torah. The mishna is not referring to a tree planted in deference to idolatry, but rather to a tree that was itself worshipped as an idol. There is an obligation to burn idolatry and destroy it. Therefore, legally, the latter tree is considered as if it were already burned. The requisite **measure** of the *lulav* **was crushed,** and it is therefore unfit for use in fulfilling the mitzva. Rava's ruling does not apply to an *asheira* of that kind.

This is the classic principle of *mitzvot lav lehenot nitnu* — *mitzvot* are not given for physical benefit. Rashi explains here: *ve'af al gav de'isura de'hana'ah hu* — even though it is forbidden in benefit — *demitzvot lav lehenot nitnu* — for *mitzvot* are not given for benefit. He elaborates: *ein kiyum mitzvot hana'at ha'guf* — fulfilling a *mitzvah* is not a physical benefit to the body — *ela avodat eved l'rabo* — it is the service of a servant to his master. In other contexts Rashi expresses this even more sharply, saying that *mitzvot* were given *le'ol tzavareihem nitnu* — as a yoke upon one's neck — a formulation noticeably harsher than the familiar *ratzah Hakadosh Baruch Hu lezakot et Yisrael*.

### Halacha This-Worldly

The simpler and more fundamental point, however, has nothing to do with whether we experience *mitzvot* as spiritually fulfilling. It is a precise *halachic* point: *halacha* deals with physical, this-worldly realities. If something is *assur b'hana'ah*, benefit is measured strictly by concrete, this-worldly benefit. Otherworldly benefit — reward in *olam ha'ba*, divine favor, spiritual fulfillment — is not relevant within the framework of *halacha*. This is, in a sense, one of the surprising and philosophically rich features of *halacha*: though it is religious law, it is profoundly grounded in the measurable, this-worldly dimension of human experience.

One might ask: even setting aside *olam ha'ba*, surely a person derives personal satisfaction and enjoyment from performing a *mitzvah* — does that not constitute *hana'ah*? This question connects to the broader principle stated in the *gemara*: *kol kol u'mareh v'reiach ein bahem mishum me'ilah* — aesthetic pleasures of sound, sight, and smell are not technically classified as forbidden benefit in the biblical sense. Visual or aesthetic pleasure is not considered a physical benefit; smell is treated somewhat differently because it engages the body more directly. So too the internal satisfaction of performing a *mitzvah* does not rise to the level of concrete, physical *hana'ah* that *halacha* recognizes. Hence, if one took the *lulav* of *avodah zarah* and used it, one did not technically derive forbidden benefit, and one has fulfilled the *mitzvah*. The reason not to take it in the first place, as Rashi states in two words, is *dema'is ligvoah* — it is disgusting before God. One should not bring something associated with idolatry into the service of God. But as a matter of strict law, if one did, one is *yotzei*.

### Stolen vs. AZ Lulav

This raises a pointed question: why is a *stolen* *lulav* invalid — and not merely ill-advised — while a *lulav* used for *avodah zarah* is only disgraceful, not technically invalid? Surely something used in idolatrous worship carries a more severe stigma than something merely stolen. Shouldn't *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* disqualify it?

Tosafot addresses this earlier in the sugya of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* and offers two important points. First, he argues that the *aveirah* of worshiping *avodah zarah* was entirely unrelated to the *mitzvah* of *lulav*. He draws an analogy: if someone used a *lulav* to dig a hole on *Shabbat*, or carried it through *reshut ha'rabim* on *Shabbat*, would the *lulav* thereby become invalid for a *mitzvah*? Clearly not. The *aveirah* of *Shabbat* desecration is not intertwined with the *mitzvah* of *lulav*. Similarly, even though worshiping the *lulav* as *avodah zarah* is a severe *aveirah*, it did not cause the *mitzvah* to come about — the *mitzvah* is entirely independent. When one *steals* a *lulav*, by contrast, the very act of stealing is what enabled one to perform the *mitzvah*; the *aveirah* and the *mitzvah* are intertwined.

That said, one must acknowledge the force of the difficulty here. If the problem of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* lies not only in the intertwining of actions but also in the stigmatization of the *object* — if using something for *aveirah* taints it as a *mitzvah* vehicle — then surely worshiping something as *avodah zarah* would seem to place it far beyond the pale of any *mitzvah* use. It is genuinely striking that the *gemara* does not invoke *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* here. Tosafot's answer focuses on the action, not the object — the *aveirah* did not enable the *mitzvah* — but the intuitive difficulty remains.

### Chitutei Mechatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: דּוּמְיָא דְּעִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The formulation of the mishna **is also precise** and indicates that the reference is to an *asheira* of Moses, **as** the juxtaposition of the *halakha* of an *asheira* to the *halakha* of a city whose residents were incited to idolatry **teaches** that the legal status of the *asheira* **is similar to** that of **a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry, in which all the property must be burned. In both cases, the *lulav* is considered already burned and lacking the requisite measure. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** that this is the reason that the *lulav* is unfit.

So the *gemara* resolves the original question differently. The *lulav* of *asheira* is invalid — not because it is *assur b'hana'ah*, but for an entirely different reason. The *gemara* states: *hacha b'asherah d'Moshe askinan* — we are speaking of a *Moshe-type asheira* — *d'chitutei mechatat shiurei* — because its *shiur*, its legal size and standing, is ground up and destroyed.

The principle of *chitutei mechatat shiurei* holds that an object which is obligated to be destroyed is treated as though it were already burnt and annihilated, and therefore cannot serve as a *mitzvah* object. The logic is this: the entire concept of a *shiur* — a minimum size or quantity — is a measure of significance. An object that is commanded to be destroyed has no significance; its obligatory destruction undermines any weight it could have as a *mitzvah* object. Hence a *lulav* from an *ir nidachas* — where the *lulav* itself was not used for any forbidden purpose, but is merely located in a city that must be destroyed — is invalid precisely because it is subject to destruction. The same applies to the *asheira*: not because of *issur hana'ah*, but because the obligation to destroy it means it is treated *halachically* as though already destroyed.

### Moshe's Asheira

But this raises a further question: which *asheiros* fall under the obligation of destruction? The *gemara* seems to distinguish between different categories of idols, identifying the relevant case as *asherah d'Moshe* — Moshe's *asheira*. What does this mean?

Rashi explains: *otam she'hayu bi'sha'at kibbush Eretz Yisrael* — those *asheiros* that existed at the time of the conquest of the land, *she'hitzrichan ha'katuv sereifah* — which the Torah required to be burned, as it is written *va'ashereihem tisrefun ba'esh*. This is a striking Rashi. He appears to limit the *mitzvah* to destroy *avodah zarah* to the idols present during the conquest of Eretz Yisrael under Moshe and Yehoshua. After that period, according to this reading of Rashi, there is no *mitzvah* to destroy *avodah zarah*.

This is remarkable, because the received understanding in most *poskim* and *rishonim* is that the obligation to destroy *avodah zarah* is not limited to the conquest period. And yet, if one surveys the *mishnayot* and *gemarot* of *Masechet Avodah Zarah* — which discusses *yayin nesech*, objects *assur b'hana'ah*, forbidden forms of worship, and much else — one will search in vain for a sustained discussion of the *mitzvah* to destroy *avodah zarah*. Scholars have noted the likely explanation: the *Mishnah* was edited in Eretz Yisrael under Roman rule, which was saturated with pagan idols. Under those conditions, any discussion of a practical obligation to destroy the idols of the surrounding culture would have been not only impractical but potentially dangerous. The topic was therefore set aside. This has resonance for contemporary discussions as well, where questions about *avodah zarah* in the land of Israel are sometimes raised; it is instructive to note which topics were, for purely practical reasons, effectively bracketed by the Tannaim and Amoraim.

Rashi's formulation actually renders this bracketing *halachically* operative — limiting the *mitzvah* to destroy to the conquest period — which is indeed a remarkable position.

Tosafot does not accept Rashi's approach. Tosafot explains the distinction differently: *Moshe* refers not to a time period but to a type of ownership — Jewish-owned *avodah zarah*. A non-Jew who owns and worships an idol can annul it — be *mevatel* it — declare it no longer sacred, and it reverts to being a permissible object. But once an idol is owned or worshiped by a Jew, its status becomes permanently fixed and cannot be reversed. Therefore Tosafot says: the distinction is between *avodah zarah* that can still potentially be nullified — in which case it need not be destroyed and *chitutei mechatat shiurei* does not apply — and *avodah zarah* owned by a Jew that can never have its status changed and must be destroyed. Only the latter falls under the principle of *chitutei mechatat shiurei* and is therefore invalid as a *mitzvah* object.

The conclusion of this section, then, is striking: mere prohibition of *hana'ah* — even if the object was used for *avodah zarah* — is not sufficient to disqualify the *lulav* as a *mitzvah* object, because *mitzvot lav lehenot nitnu*. What does disqualify it is the obligation of destruction, which strips the object of all *halachic* standing. A stolen *lulav* fails because of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*, the intertwining of the *aveirah* with the *mitzvah* act. An *avodah zarah lulav* that lacks the destruction obligation fails only in the moral-aesthetic sense — *dema'is ligvoah* — but technically one would be *yotzei*. Only when the destruction obligation attaches does it become categorically invalid.

## Lulav Defects

### Niktam vs. Nisdak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִקְטַם רֹאשׁוֹ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא נִקְטַם, אֲבָל נִסְדַּק — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If **the top** of the *lulav* **was severed** it is unfit. **Rav Huna said: They taught** that it is unfit **only** when it was completely **severed; however,** if the top merely **split,** the *lulav* **is fit.**

We turn now from these questions of origin and ownership to physical defects of the *lulav* itself. The *mishnah* states that *niktam rosho* — if the head of the *lulav* is cut off — it is invalid. Rav Huna qualifies: *lo shanu ela niktam* — this applies only if it is cut off — *aval nisdak kasher* — but if it is split, it remains *kasher*. The case of *nisdak*, as Rashi explains, refers to the splitting of the leaf-tips themselves. Without getting into the full practical *halacha*, the basic point is a valuable leniency: split leaves do not, in and of themselves, constitute a defect.

### Braita: Bent, Thorny

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִסְדַּק כָּשֵׁר? וְהָתַנְיָא: לוּלָב כָּפוּף, קָווּץ, סָדוּק, עָקוֹם דּוֹמֶה לְמַגָּל — פָּסוּל. חָרוּת — פָּסוּל. דּוֹמֶה לְחָרוּת — כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דַּעֲבִיד כְּהֶימְנֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is a split** *lulav* **fit? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A *lulav*** that is **bent** at the top, that is **thorny, split,** or **curved** to the extent that it is shaped **like a sickle** is **unfit.** If it became **hard** as wood it is **unfit.** If it merely appears **like hard** wood but is not yet completely hardened, it is **fit.** Apparently, a split *lulav* is unfit. **Rav Pappa said:** The split *lulav* in the *baraita* is so split that **it is shaped like a fork [*heimanak*],** with the two sides of the split completely separated, and it appears that the *lulav* has two spines.

The *gemara* then poses a challenge from a *braita*, which lists multiple forms of invalid *lulavim*: *lulav kafuf* — bent; *kavatz* — thorny; *saduk* — split; *akum domeh lemagal* — bent like a scythe, meaning severely curved; *charut* — hardened like a stick, where the leaves have fused; all of these are *pasul*. However, *domeh lecharut* — resembling hardened but where the leaves are still separable — is *kasher*. Rav Papa resolves the apparent contradiction between the *braita* which lists *saduk* as *pasul* and Rav Huna's ruling that *nisdak* is *kasher* by specifying that the *braita* refers to a severe split — one that effectively produces two separate spines, analogous to a scribal tool with two separate tips used to make simultaneous copies. A *lulav* whose spine bifurcates into two distinct heads is invalid. Ordinary splitting of the leaves is not a problem.

### Bent Lulav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָקוֹם דּוֹמֶה לְמַגָּל, אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְפָנָיו, אֲבָל לְאַחֲרָיו — בִּרְיָיתֵיהּ הוּא. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לִצְדָדִין — כִּלְפָנָיו דָּמֵי. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: כִּלְאַחֲרָיו דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If it is **curved** to the extent that it is shaped **like a sickle,** it is unfit. **Rava said: We said** that it is unfit **only** when it is curved **forward** away from the spine; **however,** if it is curved **backward,** toward the spine, it is fit for use because that **is its nature,** and that is the way a *lulav* typically grows. **Rav Naḥman said:** The legal status of a *lulav* that is curved **to** either of **the sides is like** that of a *lulav* curved **forward,** and it is unfit. **And some say:** Its legal status **is like** that of a *lulav* curved **backward,** and it is fit.

Regarding *akum domeh lemagal* — bent like a scythe — Rava offers a nuanced distinction: *lo amran ela lifanav* — the problem applies only if the *lulav* bends forward, meaning toward the side of the leaves rather than toward the spine. *Aval le'achorav briya hi* — if it bends backward toward the spine, that is the natural growth pattern of the *lulav* and does not constitute a defect. The precise application of this distinction in practice — since *lulavim* typically tend to curve in one direction when they bend — is a genuine question, and the further teaching of Rav Nachman adds complexity: if the *lulav* bends to the side, one opinion equates that with bending forward (invalid), and another opinion equates it with bending backward (valid). The question of how much curvature is tolerable, and in which direction, is directly relevant to the examination of *lulavim* before *Sukkos*.

### Symmetry Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי לוּלַבָּא דְּסָלֵיק בְּחַד הוּצָא — בַּעַל מוּם הוּא, וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: This *lulav* that grew with one leaf,** i.e., leaves on only one side of the spine, **is blemished and unfit.**

Rava adds a further ruling: *hai lulava desalik bechad hutza* — a *lulav* whose leaves all emerge from only one side of the spine — *ba'al mum hu pasul* — is like a blemished specimen and is invalid. This is not stated explicitly in the *mishnah*, but reflects a broader aesthetic principle: symmetry. The concept of symmetry is, in fact, central to aesthetics going back to classical philosophy, and its application here — to the requirement that leaves be present on both sides of the spine — gives concrete *halachic* expression to the general requirement that the *lulav* be a proper specimen.

### Nifretzu vs. Nifredu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְרְצוּ עָלָיו כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: נִפְרְצוּ — דְּעָבֵיד כִּי חוּפְיָא. נִפְרְדוּ — דְּאִיפָּרוּד אִפָּרוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **If the** palm **leaves were severed** from the spine of the *lulav*, it is unfit; if its leaves were spread, it is fit. **Rav Pappa said: Severed** means that the leaves are completely detached from the spine, and one ties them to the *lulav*, so **that** the *lulav* **is made like a broom. Spread** means that the leaves remain attached but are merely **separated** from the spine in that they jut outward.

The *mishnah* lists two further conditions: *nifretzu alav* — invalid — and *nifredu* — valid. *Nifredu* is not difficult to understand: the leaves are fanned out, separated from the spine but still attached to it; they have simply spread open rather than lying flat. Rabbi Yehuda rules that such a *lulav* is *kasher* but one must bind the leaves flat against the spine. The case of *nifretzu* — which is invalid — requires clarification.

### Nifretzu as Broom

Rav Papa explains: *nifretzu de'avid ke'hufya* — *nifretzu* means it has come to look like a broom. Rashi understands this as referring to a case where the leaves actually fell off and were tied back onto the spine, producing a broom-like appearance. Tosafot disagrees, arguing that this is too obvious to require stating; Tosafot understands *nifretzu* as referring to leaves that have been split fully open, their two halves completely separated — which produces the broom-like appearance. The practical and theoretical disagreement about what exactly constitutes *nifretzu* remains unresolved between Rashi and Tosafot.

### Split Tip Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: נֶחְלְקָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר (רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן) אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נִיטְּלָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת — פָּסוּל. מַאי לָאו, הוּא הַדִּין נֶחְלְקָה? לָא, נִיטְּלָה שָׁאנֵי, דְּהָא חֲסַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: **What is** the *halakha* if **the** central **twin-leaf split?** The Gemara cites proof to resolve the dilemma. **Come** and **hear** that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** If **the** central **twin-leaf was removed,** the *lulav* **is unfit. What, is it not** that **the same is true if** the twin-leaf **split?** The Gemara answers: **No,** the case where it **was removed is different, because** the result is that **it is lacking,** and an incomplete *lulav* is certainly unfit. However, if the leaf remains in place, even though it is split, it does not necessarily render the *lulav* unfit.

Rav Papa then poses the most practically significant question of the entire section: *nechleka hatayomes mahu* — what is the law if the tip of the *lulav* is split? This is the question most people have in mind when carefully examining their *lulav* before purchase. One might think that since ordinary split leaves are permissible, a split tip would be similarly treated. But the tip is uniquely significant because it is the crown of the *lulav*.

The *gemara* brings the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan (or in another version, Rav Matna citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi): *nitlah hatayomes pasul* — if the tip is removed, it is invalid. The first approach in the *gemara* argues that a split tip (*nechleka*) is not equivalent to a removed tip (*nitlah*): removal is worse because the tip is actually missing (*de'chasar lei*), and a split alone would be permissible. A second version of the tradition states the opposite: *nechleka hatayomes na'aseh kemi shenitlah hatayomes u'pasul* — a split tip is treated as equivalent to a removed tip and is therefore invalid.

### Rashi: Full Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר (רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן) אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נֶחְלְקָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁנִּיטְּלָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת וּפְסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** If **the** central **twin-leaf split, it becomes as** a *lulav* whose central **twin-leaf was removed, and** it is **unfit.** According to this version of the statement, the dilemma is resolved.

This second version is what generates the intense scrutiny of *lulav* tips that every consumer of *lulavim* before *Sukkos* knows well. However, Rashi's definition of what constitutes a problematic split offers significant relief. Rashi states: the *nechleka* refers to the case where the two uppermost central leaves — at the point where the spine terminates — split apart from one another, *venisdekah hashidrah ad ha'alim shelematta mehem* — and the spine itself splits all the way down until it reaches the leaves below. In other words, according to Rashi, a truly problematic split is one that runs the full length of the terminal section, all the way down to where the next leaves emerge. A small split at the very tip alone does not constitute *nechleka hatayomes* under Rashi's definition. According to this view, the stringent behavior many people adopt when examining *lulav* tips goes beyond what Rashi requires. Other authorities, however, take the position that even a minimal separation at the top is a disqualifying *nechleka*, which explains why this issue generates such careful attention.

## Kapot Tmarim, Hadas ID

### Binding Fanned Leaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: ״כַּפּוֹת תְּמָרִים״ — כְּפוֹת, אִם הָיָה פָּרוּד יִכְפְּתֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If the leaves were spread, one should bind the *lulav* from the top. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Tarfon** that the verse states: **"Branches [*kappot*] of a date palm."** The Sages interpret the term to mean **bound [*kafut*],** indicating that **if** the leaves of the *lulav* **were spread, one should bind it.**

The *gemara* now records the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda: if the leaves of the *lulav* are spread out (*nifraду*), one must bind them so that they are flat against the spine. A *braita* attributes this teaching to Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon, connecting it to the term *kapot tmarim* in the Torah: *kapot* — implying bound — means that if the leaves are spread apart, one must bind them together against the spine.

### Kapot Tmarim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״כַּפּוֹת תְּמָרִים״ דְּלוּלַבָּא הוּא? אֵימָא חֲרוּתָא! בָּעֵינָא כְּפוֹת וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: From where** is it ascertained **that this** term, **"branches of a date palm," is** referring to the branches **of the *lulav*? Say** it is referring to **the hardened branch** of the date palm. Rav Ashi answered: That cannot be, as **we require** the *lulav* **to be bound, and there is no** binding, since at that stage the hardened leaves point outward, and binding them is impossible.

Ravina poses a foundational question to Rav Ashi: *mimai de'hai kapot tmarim de'lulav hu* — how do we know that the phrase *kapot tmarim* refers specifically to the *lulav* as we know it? The word *tmarim* does derive from the palm tree, but the palm tree has many distinct components. Perhaps *kapot tmarim* refers to a different part of the palm tree entirely.

### Charuta Problem

Ravina's first suggestion: *eima cheruta* — perhaps it means the *cheruta*, the stage at which young palm branches have hardened and the leaves have fused into stick-like extensions. Could the Torah mean to take a larger palm branch bearing multiple such hardened offshoots? The *gemara* rejects this: *be'inan kaput ve'leika* — we need something that is actually *bindable* — *kapot* implies there are separate elements that can be drawn together. A hardened stick has no separate elements to bind; it is simply a stick.

### Uptzala Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא אוּפְתָּא! ״כְּפוֹת״ — מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא פָּרוּד, וְהַאי כָּפוּת וְעוֹמֵד לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the fundamental requirement of the mitzva is a *lulav* that appears as one unit, **say** that one takes **the trunk** of the date palm. The Gemara answers: The term **bound,** from which it is derived that the branch should appear as one unit, **indicates that there is** the possibility that it could be **spread. However, this** trunk **is perpetually bound,** as it can never become separated.

The second suggestion: *ve'eima uptzala* — what about the fiber of the trunk itself, a single unified structure? The *gemara* rejects this as well: *kaput miklal de'ika parud* — *kaput* implies that there is a plurality of elements that, although separate, are situated together. Something that is entirely a single, undifferentiated unit cannot be described as *kaput* — bound together — because there is nothing to bind. The *lulav* must have both features: separate elements (the individual leaves) and a unity that encompasses them (the spine along which they are arranged).

### Kufra Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כּוּפְרָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״דְּרָכֶיהָ דַרְכֵי נוֹעַם וְכׇל נְתִיבוֹתֶיהָ שָׁלוֹם״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** the verse is referring to **the branch of the date palm [*kufra*]** that has not yet hardened completely and could still be bound, albeit with difficulty. **Abaye said** that **it is written** in praise of the Torah: **"Its way are ways of pleasantness and all its paths are peace"** (Proverbs 3:17). At that stage of development, some of the leaves are thorns that potentially wound. The Torah would not command to use that type of branch in fulfilling the mitzva.

A third suggestion: *ve'eima kufra* — what about the stage just before full hardening, when branches are still young enough that one could physically force them together with binding? Rashi describes this as branches roughly one or two years old whose wood has not yet fully thickened, such that one could still fold the offshoots together by tying. Could this be *kapot tmarim*?

### Pleasant Ways

Abaye dismisses this on a different ground: *deracheha darchei noam vechol nesivoseha shalom* — the Torah's ways are pleasant and all its paths are peace. At this stage of development, the branches bear thorns that would scratch and injure the hand. It is inconceivable that the Torah would mandate taking something in hand that would cause injury. This is a principled *halachic* inference from the general character of the Torah, not merely an aesthetic preference.

### Kapot Singular

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא תּוֹסְפָאָה לְרָבִינָא: וְאֵימָא תַּרְתֵּי כַּפֵּי דְתַמְרֵי? ״כַּפַּת״ כְּתִיב. וְאֵימָא חֲדָא? לְהָהוּא — ״כַּף״ קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava, the expert in *Tosefta*, said to Ravina:** Since the verse states "branches of a date palm" in the plural, **say** that one is obligated to take **two palm branches** in fulfilling the mitzva of the four species. Ravina answered: Although the word is vocalized in the plural, based on tradition ***kappot* is written** without the letter *vav*, indicating that only one is required. The Gemara suggests: **And say** that one is required to take only **one** leaf? The Gemara answers: If that were the intention of the Torah, it would not have written *kappot* without a *vav*. **That** single leaf **is called *kaf*.** *Kappot* without the *vav* indicates both plural, i.e., multiple leaves, and singular, i.e., one branch.

Rava Tosfa'ah raises another possibility before Ravina: *ve'eima tarti kapei de'timarei* — perhaps *kapot tmarim* means two palm-clusters, i.e., a cluster of dates. A cluster of dates is, after all, a unified whole (*kapot*) composed of separate elements. The *gemara* responds: *kapat kesiv* — the word is written without a *vav*, in a form that indicates singular rather than plural. Thus it cannot mean multiple clusters. But then perhaps one cluster of dates — *ve'eima chada*? The *gemara* answers: *la'hu kaf kari lei* — a single cluster would simply be called a *kaf*, not *kapot*. The specific form *kapot* implies one thing that is inherently multiple — a single unit composed of many separate elements — and the only candidate from the palm tree that fits this description is the *lulav* itself: its individual leaves are distinct, yet they all emerge from and are unified along a single spine.

### Lulav Identified

This is the conclusion: *kapot tmarim* refers to the *lulav*, precisely because it uniquely combines separate elements (the leaves) within a single unified structure (the spine). The other parts of the palm tree each fail on one or the other dimension — either they lack separate elements or they lack the essential unity.

At this point it is worth noting the observation of the Rambam in his *Perush Hamishnayot*. These derivations — similarly to those we will encounter shortly regarding *anaf etz avot* and *pri etz hadar* — can appear somewhat forced. Did the Sages truly not know which plant was meant, and only figure it out by parsing the grammar of *kapot*? The Rambam argues that in reality, the identities of the *arba minim* were always known by tradition — by an oral culture that transmitted the meaning of these terms from generation to generation. The *gemara*'s etymological and botanical derivations are, in his view, not the actual source of knowledge but rather retroactive demonstrations of how the verse can be seen to support what was already known. The Rambam classifies this traditional knowledge as a form of *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* — not derived from the verse, but received as transmitted tradition.

## Shiur: Tefachim

### Halacha Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוּלָב שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שִׁיעוּר הֲדַס וַעֲרָבָה — שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְלוּלָב — אַרְבָּעָה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא לוּלָב יוֹצֵא מִן הַהֲדַס טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **A *lulav* that has three handbreadths** in length, sufficient to enable one to wave with it, is fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The** minimum **measure of a myrtle branch and a willow branch is three** handbreadths. **And** the minimum measure of **a *lulav* is four** handbreadths. The difference between the measures is **so that the *lulav* will extend** at least one **handbreadth from the myrtle branch.**

The Rambam's point connects to the broader question of *shiurim* — minimum measurements — which the *gemara* now begins to address. The measurement requirements for *lulav*, *hadas*, and *aravah* are not, in his view, derived analytically from the verses, but represent received knowledge about what the Torah always meant. Just as the identification of the species was a matter of transmitted knowledge, so too the specific minimum dimensions required for each of the *minim* belong to a category of *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* — tradition rather than derivation.

## Tzinei Har Habarzel

### Leaf Overlap Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִינֵּי הַר הַבַּרְזֶל — כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה מַגִּיעַ לְצַד עִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, אֲבָל אֵין רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה מַגִּיעַ לְצַד עִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: A *lulav* from the **palms of the Iron Mountain is fit.** It has few leaves on its spine, and those leaves are not crowded together like the leaves on a standard *lulav*. **Abaye said:** The Sages **taught that** this type of *lulav* is fit **only** in a case in which **the top of this** leaf **reaches the base of that** leaf above it on the spine. **However,** if there are so few leaves that **the top of this** leaf **does not reach the base of that** leaf, **it is unfit.**

Before proceeding to the main discussion of *shiurim*, the *gemara* addresses the ruling of the *mishnah* that *tzinei har habarzel* — *lulavim* from the iron mountain — are *kasher*. These are *lulavim* whose leaves are unusually short. Abaye establishes the operative distinction: *lo shanu ella she'rosho shel zeh magi'a le'tzad ikaro shel zeh* — the ruling of *kasher* applies only when the tip of one leaf reaches at least to the base of the next leaf. *Aval ein rosho shel zeh magi'a le'tzad ikaro shel zeh pasul* — if the leaves are so short that the tip of one does not even reach the base of the next, the *lulav* is invalid.

### Mishna-Braita Harmony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: צִינֵּי הַר הַבַּרְזֶל פְּסוּלָה. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן כְּשֵׁרָה! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כְּאַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **That was taught** in a *baraita* **as well:** A *lulav* from the **palms of the Iron Mountain are unfit.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that it is **fit? Rather, learn from it in accordance with** the statement of **Abaye,** that there is a distinction based on the configuration of the leaves on the *lulav*. Indeed, **learn from it.**

This distinction resolves an apparent contradiction. The *mishnah* states that *tzinei har habarzel* is *kasher*, while a *braita* states it is *pasul*. Abaye's ruling harmonizes the two: the *mishnah* speaks of the case where each leaf reaches the base of the next — *kasher*; the *braita* speaks of the case where leaves are so stunted they do not even reach the base of the next — *pasul*. *Ela shema mina ke'Abaye* — this confirms Abaye's resolution.

### Gei Ben Hinnom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּרָמֵי לַיהּ מִירְמֵא: תְּנַן צִינֵּי הַר הַבַּרְזֶל — כָּשֵׁר, וְהָתַנְיָא: פְּסוּלָה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — שֶׁרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה מַגִּיעַ לְצַד עִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, כָּאן — שֶׁאֵין רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה מַגִּיעַ לְצַד עִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה. אָמַר רַבִּי מָרִיּוֹן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: שְׁתֵּי תְמָרוֹת יֵשׁ בְּגֵיא בֶּן הִנֹּם, וְעוֹלֶה עָשָׁן מִבֵּינֵיהֶם. וְזֶהוּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: צִינֵּי הַר הַבַּרְזֶל כְּשֵׁרוֹת, וְזוֹ הִיא פִּתְחָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And others raise it as a contradiction. We learned in the mishna:** A *lulav* from the **palms of the Iron Mountain is fit. But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: It is **unfit? Abaye said:** This is **not difficult: Here,** in the mishna, where the *lulav* is fit, it is referring to a case **where the top of this** leaf **reaches the base of that** next leaf, whereas, **there,** in the *baraita*, where the *lulav* is unfit, it is referring to a case **where the top of this** leaf **does not reach the base of that** next leaf. The Gemara describes the location of these *lulavim*. **Rabbi Maryon said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said, and some say that Rabba bar Mari taught** this *baraita* **in the name of Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: There are two date palms in the valley of ben Hinnom, and smoke arises from between them. And this is** the place about **which we learned** in the mishna: A *lulav* from the **palms of the Iron Mountain is fit. And that** site **is the entrance of Gehenna.**

The *gemara* then appends an intriguing aggadic tradition. Rabbi Miryon citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and in a parallel tradition Rabba bar Mari citing Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, teach: *shtei tmarot yesh b'gei ben Hinnom* — there are two palm trees in the Valley of Ben Hinnom, which lies outside Jerusalem. *Ve'ole ashan mi'beineihem* — smoke rises from between them. This is the valley whose name gives us the word *Gehennom*. The *gemara* identifies these palm trees as the *tzinei har habarzel* referred to in the *mishnah*, explaining that the *mishnah* speaks of the distinctive *lulavim* that grow from these particular trees in this valley. And the *gemara* adds: *v'zo hi pitcha shel Gehennom* — the space between those two smoke-emitting palm trees is the gateway to *Gehennom*. The image is striking — the two palm trees stand like pillars framing an archway, and from between them smoke rises from below. That the *lulav* from such trees is nonetheless *kasher* is itself a remarkable detail.

## Shiur: Tefachim…

### Three and Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי פַּרְנָךְ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁדְרוֹ שֶׁל לוּלָב צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִן הַהֲדַס טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Parnakh** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The spine of the *lulav*,** and not merely its leaves, **must** be at least four handbreadths long, so that it will **extend from the myrtle branch** at least one **handbreadth.**

The *gemara* now turns to the primary discussion of minimum sizes. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel states: *shiur hadas va'aravah shlosha* — the minimum size of the *hadas* and *aravah* is three *tefachim* — *ha'lulav arba'ah* — and the *lulav* is four *tefachim*, *kedai she'yehei lulav yotzei min ha'hadas tefach* — so that the *lulav* protrudes one *tefach* above the *hadas*. It is worth recalling the broader context: the reason the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *aravah* are bound together as a single unit derives from the way the *pasuk* is written — *pri etz hadar*, then *kapot tmarim* without a connecting *vav*, then *ve'anaf etz avot ve'arvei nachal* — the three non-*esrog* species are thus bound together. This also explains why we recite the *bracha* as *al netilat lulav* rather than *al netilat arba minim* or *al netilat esrog*: the *lulav* is the tallest and most dominant member of the bundle, and the *pasuk* centers on *kapot tmarim* with the other two appended to it.

### Spine Protrusion

Rav Parnach citing Rabbi Yochanan adds a further specification: *shidra shel lulav tzarich she'yeitzei min ha'hadas tefach* — not merely the top of the *lulav*'s leaves, but the *spine* of the *lulav* must protrude a full *tefach* above the *hadas*. This is a more demanding requirement: it is not enough that the leaves of the *lulav* extend above the *hadas*; the spine itself must reach a full *tefach* higher. Practically, this is something to attend to when assembling the *lulav*: one should verify not merely that the general tip of the *lulav* is higher, but specifically that the spine protrudes.

### Three vs. Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לוּלָב שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים כְּדֵי לְנַעְנֵעַ בּוֹ כָּשֵׁר. אֵימָא: וּכְדֵי לְנַעְנֵעַ בּוֹ, כָּשֵׁר. מָר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ, וּמַר כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Didn't **we learn** in the mishna: **A *lulav* that has three handbreadths** in length, **sufficient** to enable one **to wave with it,** is **fit** for use in fulfilling the mitzva? That indicates that a *lulav* three handbreadths long is fit. The Gemara answers: Emend the language of the mishna and **say:** A *lulav* that has three handbreadths **and** an additional handbreadth that is **sufficient** to enable one **to wave with it** is **fit.** This emendation is understood by each *amora* according to his opinion. It is understood by **this Sage,** Shmuel, **as per his** opinion that only one additional handbreadth is required including the leaves; **and** it is understood by **this Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **as per his** opinion that the additional handbreadth must be in the length of the spine of the *lulav*, and the leaves are not taken into consideration.

The *mishnah* says that a *lulav* of three *tefachim* — *kedai lenane'ah bo kasher* — sufficient to shake it — is valid. But Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel just stated that the *lulav* must be four *tefachim*. The resolution: read the *mishnah* as *u'khedai lenane'ah bo kasher* — meaning three *tefachim plus* enough to shake it, i.e., three *tefachim* plus one more. *Mar ki'islei u'Mar ki'islei* — each Amora read the *mishnah* in accordance with his own position. According to Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel, the extra *tefach* brings the overall *lulav* to four *tefachim* measured including the leaves. According to Rav Parnach citing Rabbi Yochanan, who demands that the spine itself protrude a *tefach*, the four *tefachim* are measured along the spine alone, yielding a still more demanding standard.

### Five-Tefach Amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שִׁיעוּר הֲדַס וַעֲרָבָה — שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְלוּלָב — אַרְבָּעָה. מַאי לָאו בַּהֲדֵי עָלִין! לָא, לְבַד מֵעָלִין. גּוּפָא: שִׁיעוּר הֲדַס וַעֲרָבָה — שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְלוּלָב — אַרְבָּעָה. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof from a *baraita*. **Come** and **hear: The** minimum **measure of a myrtle branch and of a willow branch is three** handbreadths, **and** that of **a *lulav* is four** handbreadths. **What, is it not** that this measure is calculated **with the leaves,** in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel? The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** it can be understood that the measure is calculated **without the leaves.** Apropos the *baraita* cited above, the Gemara discusses **the matter itself. The** minimum **measure of a myrtle branch and of a willow branch is three** handbreadths, **and** that of **a *lulav* is four** handbreadths. **Rabbi Tarfon says: With a cubit of five handbreadths.** The preliminary understanding of Rabbi Tarfon's opinion is that the minimum measure of a myrtle branch is five handbreadths, not three.

Rabbi Tarfon's position, as taught in the *braita*: *be'amah bat chamisha tefachim* — measured with a five-*tefach amah*. One reading of this would be that Rabbi Tarfon demands five *tefachim* for the *hadas* and *aravah* and a proportionally greater amount for the *lulav*. Rava reacts sharply: *shara lei marei l'Rabbi Tarfon* — may his Master forgive Rabbi Tarfon for such a requirement! *Hashta avud shlosha lo meshkachinan* — we struggle to find even three *tefachim* of properly braided leaves; *bat chamisha miba'aya* — could we possibly find five?

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael he offered a different reading. Rabbi Tarfon does not disagree with the quantities of three and four; he agrees that it is three for *hadas* and *aravah* and four for *lulav*. His innovation concerns the *size of the tefach* used to measure. He says: take a normal six-*tefach amah* and divide it into five parts instead of six. Each resulting unit is therefore one *tefach* plus one-fifth — that is, 1.2 normal *tefachim*. Use those enlarged *tefachim* as your measuring units: three of them for *hadas* and *aravah* (equaling 3.6 normal *tefachim*), four for *lulav* (equaling 4.8). *Tsei meihem shlosha la'hadas ve'hashar la'lulav* — three units for the *hadas* and the remainder for the *lulav*.

This raises a further difficulty: *kashya de'Shmuel a'd'Shmuel* — Shmuel contradicts himself. Rav Yehuda cited Shmuel as saying the *shiur* of *hadas* and *aravah* is three *tefachim*; yet Rav Huna cited Shmuel as ruling *halacha k'Rabbi Tarfon* — we follow Rabbi Tarfon. If Rabbi Tarfon means three units of 1.2, requiring 3.6 normal *tefachim*, how can Shmuel also say simply "three"? The *gemara* answers: *lo dak* — Shmuel was not being precise when he said three; he was approximating. But the *gemara* objects: surely we would only accept imprecision in the direction of stringency (*l'chumra*), not leniency (*l'kula*). Rounding down from 3.6 to 3 would be a leniency, which is not how we apply the principle of *lo dak*.

### 5/6 Tefach Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁרָא לֵיהּ מָרֵיהּ לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. הַשְׁתָּא עָבוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה לָא מַשְׁכְּחִינַן, בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה מִבַּעְיָא! כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: אַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים עֲשֵׂה אוֹתָהּ בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה, צֵא מֵהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה לַהֲדַס, וְהַשְּׁאָר לַלּוּלָב. כַּמָּה הָווּ לְהוּ — תְּלָתָא וּתְלָתָא חוּמְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: May his Master,** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **forgive Rabbi Tarfon** for this extreme stringency. **Now, we do not find** even **a dense-leaved** myrtle branch **three** handbreadths long; is it **necessary** to say that finding one **five** handbreadths long is nearly impossible? **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that this is the correct understanding of the statement of Rabbi Tarfon: Take **a cubit of six handbreadths,** and **render it** a cubit of **five** handbreadths. Rabbi Tarfon is saying that for the purpose of measuring the myrtle branch, willow branch, and *lulav*, the standard six-handbreadth cubit is divided into five handbreadths, each slightly larger than the standard handbreadth. **Take three of** these large handbreadths **for the myrtle branch, and** three of these handbreadths plus **the extra** handbreadth **for the *lulav*.** The Gemara calculates: **How many** standard handbreadths **are there** in the minimum measure of a myrtle branch or willow branch? There are **three and three-fifths** standard handbreadths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שִׁיעוּר הֲדַס וַעֲרָבָה — שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְהָתָם אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן! לָא דַּק. אֵימַר דְּאָמְרִינַן לָא דַּק לְחוּמְרָא, לְקוּלָּא מִי אָמְרִינַן לָא דַּק?!
>
> **Translation:** However, on that basis, there is **a difficulty,** as one statement **of Shmuel** contradicts another statement **of Shmuel. Here, Rabbi Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The minimum **measure of** the **myrtle branch and** of **the willow branch is three** handbreadths, **and there, Rav Huna said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon,** who requires a larger handbreadth. There is a discrepancy of three-fifths of a handbreadth between the measures. The Gemara answers: When Shmuel said that the measure is three handbreadths, he **was not precise** and merely approximated the measure. The Gemara asks: **Say that we say: He was not precise when** the approximation leads **to stringency,** but when it leads **to leniency, do we say: He was not precise?** That would result in using an unfit myrtle branch in performing a mitzva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר: אַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים עֲשֵׂה אוֹתָהּ שִׁשָּׁה. צֵא מֵהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה לַהֲדַס, וְהַשְּׁאָר לַלּוּלָב. כַּמָּה הָוֵי לְהוּ — תְּרֵי וּפַלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that this is the correct understanding of the statement of Rabbi Tarfon: Take **a cubit of five handbreadths,** and **render it** a cubit of **six** handbreadths. Rabbi Tarfon said that for the purpose of measuring the myrtle branch, willow branch, and *lulav*, a five-handbreadth cubit is divided into six handbreadths, each slightly smaller than the standard handbreadth. **Take three of** these smaller handbreadths **for the myrtle branch, and** three of these handbreadths plus **the extra** handbreadth **for the *lulav*.** The Gemara calculates: **How many** standard handbreadths **are there** in the minimum measure of a myrtle branch or willow branch? There are **two and a half** standard handbreadths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף קַשְׁיָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל! לָא דַּק, וְהַיְינוּ לְחוּמְרָא לָא דַּק. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately,** there remains **a difficulty,** as one statement **of Shmuel** contradicts another statement **of Shmuel.** In one statement he said the minimum measure of a myrtle branch is two and a half handbreadths, and in another he said that the measure is three handbreadths. The Gemara answers: When Shmuel said that the measure is three handbreadths, he **was not precise** and merely approximated the measure. **And this** is a case of: **He was not precise,** where the approximation leads **to a stringency, as Rav Huna said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon.** Shmuel holds that the actual measure required is two and a half handbreadths, and he rounded it off to three, which is a more stringent measure.

This is a significant leniency, particularly for the *hadas*: the minimum requirement for the braided-leaf portion of the *hadas* reduces to two and a half normal *tefachim*, rather than three. Given how difficult it is to find *hadasim* with properly braided leaves running for an extended length, this becomes an important practical *halacha*.

Now *Shmuel*'s statement that the *shiur* is three makes sense: *lo dak l'chumra* — he was approximating upward, rounding 2.5 to 3. That is acceptable imprecision since it is in the direction of stringency. And the ruling *halacha k'Rabbi Tarfon* means: we use the 5/6 *tefach* standard, so the effective minimums are 2.5 *tefachim* for *hadas* and *aravah* and a proportionally adjusted amount for the *lulav*.

The practical takeaways from this entire section are substantial: the standard regarding split leaves, the question of bending (forward versus backward, and how much), the critical issue of the *nechleka hatayomes* and Rashi's relatively lenient definition of it, the question of whether the *lulav* spine must protrude above the bundle, and finally the issue of minimum sizes and the use of the 5/6 *tefach* standard to make the *hadas* requirement more achievable.

## Hadas Specifics

### Hadas Invalid Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֲדַס הַגָּזוּל וְהַיָּבֵשׁ — פָּסוּל. שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת — פָּסוּל. נִקְטַם רֹאשׁוֹ, נִפְרְצוּ עָלָיו, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ עֲנָבָיו מְרוּבּוֹת מֵעָלָיו — פָּסוּל. וְאִם מִיעֲטָן — כָּשֵׁר, וְאֵין מְמַעֲטִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A myrtle branch that was stolen or that is** completely **dry is unfit. A myrtle branch of a tree worshipped as idolatry [*asheira*] or** a myrtle branch **from a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry **is unfit.** If **the top** of the myrtle branch **was severed,** if **the leaves were severed** completely, or if **its berries were more numerous than its leaves, it is unfit. If one diminished their** number by plucking berries so that they no longer outnumbered the leaves, the myrtle branch is **fit. But one may not diminish** the number **on the Festival** itself.

We turn now to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the *hadas*. The structure of the *mishnah* mirrors that of the *lulav mishna* with adaptations for the particular characteristics of each species. *Hadas ha'gazul ve'ha'yavesh pasul* — if it is stolen or dried, it is invalid. *Shel asherah v'shel ir ha'nidachat pasul* — if it comes from an *asheira* or from an *ir nidachas*, it is invalid. *Niktam rosho, nifretzu alav* — if its head was cut off or its leaves fell off, it is invalid. These applications require no additional discussion, as they follow directly from what was established regarding the *lulav*.

### Berries Problem

The *mishnah* then introduces a disqualification unique to the *hadas*: *o she'hayu anavav merubot me'alav pasul* — if there were more berries than leaves on the *hadas*, it is invalid. The *hadas* can grow berries, and if the berries come to outnumber the leaves, the character of the branch changes — it is no longer predominantly a leafy branch of the type required. *Ve'im mi'atan kasher* — if one removes enough berries to restore the predominance of leaves, it is valid. However, *ve'ein mematin b'Yom Tov* — one may not pluck the berries on *Yom Tov* itself. The reason for this restriction will require further discussion.

### Anaf Etz Avot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עֲנַף עֵץ עָבוֹת״ — שֶׁעֲנָפָיו חוֹפִין אֶת עֵצוֹ. וְאֵי זֶה הוּא? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה הֲדַס. וְאֵימָא זֵיתָא! בָּעֵינַן ״עָבוֹת״, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** It is written: **"Boughs of a dense-leaved tree"** (Leviticus 23:40); this is referring to **a tree whose leaves obscure its tree. And which tree is that? You must say it is the myrtle tree.** The Gemara suggests: **And say** it is **the olive** tree, whose leaves obscure the tree. The Gemara answers: **We require a "dense-leaved"** tree, whose leaves are in a chain-like configuration, **and** that **is not** the case with an olive tree.

The *gemara* then moves to the identification of the *hadas* from the verse *anaf etz avot* — a branch of braided wood. The *gemara* derives that the *anaf* must be one whose leaves cover the branch — *she'anavav chofin et etzo* — and from this concludes: *hevei omer zeh hadas* — this must be the *hadas*. But how do we know that? Why not another tree?

### Eliminating Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא דּוּלְבָּא! בָּעֵינַן עֲנָפָיו חוֹפִין אֶת עֵצוֹ, וְלֵיכָּא. וְאֵימָא הִירְדּוּף! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״דְּרָכֶיהָ דַרְכֵי נוֹעַם״, וְלֵיכָּא. רָבָא אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״הָאֱמֶת וְהַשָּׁלוֹם אֱהָבוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And say it is the Oriental plane tree,** whose leaves are in a braid-like configuration. The Gemara answers: **We require** a tree whose **leaves obscure its tree, and** that **is not** the case with **an Oriental plane tree.** The Gemara suggests: **And say** the verse is referring to **oleander,** which has both characteristics. **Abaye said:** It is written with regard to the Torah: **"Its ways are ways of pleasantness"** (Proverbs 3:17), **and** that **is not** the case with the oleander tree, because it is a poisonous plant and its sharp, thorn-like leaves pierce the hand of one holding it. **Rava said:** The unfitness of the oleander is derived **from here: "Love truth and peace"** (Zechariah 8:19), and poisonous plants that pierce are antithetical to peace.

The *gemara* runs through several candidates and eliminates them. *Teima zeita* — perhaps an olive branch? No: *be'inan avot ve'leika* — we require braided leaves, and the olive does not produce them. *Ve'teima dulba* — what about the *dulba*, which Rashi identifies as the chestnut (*etz armon*)? Here the problem is the opposite: *be'inan anafav chofin et etzo ve'leika* — the chestnut may produce braided leaves but they do not cover the branch. *Ve'teima hirduf* — what about the *hirduf*, which does have braided leaves that cover the branch, but whose leaves are spiky and sharp and will cut the hand? Abaye responds: *deracheha darchei noam ve'leika* — the Torah's ways are pleasant, and such a branch would injure those who hold it; the Torah could not demand this. Rava adds a further scriptural grounding: *ha'emet ve'hashalom ehavu* — love truth and peace — and Rashi connects this to the requirement that what one holds in hand be something one can hold in peace, without injury. Whether the Sages truly reasoned to the identification of the *hadas* through these eliminations, or whether — as the Rambam maintains — they already knew it from transmitted tradition and were simply showing how it can be derived from the verses, is the underlying question that runs through this entire series of discussions.
