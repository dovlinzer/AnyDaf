# Sukkah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stolen Sukkah

### Lulav to Sukkah

The *gemara* on 31a opens with a *tanna de-vei rabanan* that shifts our focus from the *lulav* to the *sukkah*. In the preceding discussion, we had examined the problems presented by a stolen *lulav*: first, the concern of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah*, a *mitzvah* performed through the agency of sin, and second, the requirement of *lachem* — that the *arba minim* must belong to the one performing the *mitzvah*. The latter concern explains why even a borrowed *lulav* is problematic on the first day, and it led us into a further discussion about purchasing *hadassim* growing on stolen land — at what point the buyer is considered the owner, and at what point the taint of *gezeilah* is sufficiently in the past that it no longer clouds the *mitzvah*. With that background in place, the *gemara* now turns to the parallel issues in the context of *sukkah*.

### Eliezer vs. Rabbis

The *baraita* states: *sukkah gezulah* — a stolen *sukkah* — and one who constructs a *sukkah* in *reshut ha-rabim*, public space, are cases in which Rabbi Eliezer *posel*, invalidates the *sukkah*, while the *chachamim* hold it to be *machshir*, valid. The case of *reshut ha-rabim* is instructive: by building in public space, one is not stealing portable objects but rather seizing land one does not own — land belonging to the public. The *gemara* uses the word *mesachech* deliberately, indicating one who actually builds and covers a *sukkah* in that space, effectively stealing the public's land by occupying it.

### Two Problems

Rav Nachman steps in to clarify the precise parameters of the debate. *Machlokes shetokaf chavero ve-hotzio mi-sukkato* — the dispute pertains specifically to the case where one physically seized one's fellow's *sukkah* by ejecting him from it. The thief did not carry the *sukkah* away; he stole the land, or more precisely, he seized control of it. This brings us immediately to the fundamental question underlying the entire discussion: *karka einah nigzelet* — can land be considered legally stolen? The general principle in *halacha* is that land does not acquire the status of stolen property, because the land itself has not been physically removed or transformed. Even so, there is a position in *Bava Kamma* — notably attributed to Rabbi Eliezer himself — that *karka nigzelet*, that land can indeed take on the status of stolen property.

### Land Status

Rav Nachman explains Rabbi Eliezer's position as follows: *Rabbi Eliezer le-taimei de-amar ein adam yotzei chovaso be-sukkato shel chaveiro* — Rabbi Eliezer famously holds, as we saw at the end of the previous chapter, that one can only fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* in one's own *sukkah*, not in a borrowed one. Given that principle, Rabbi Eliezer invalidates this *sukkah* under either of two possible characterizations of the seized land. *I karka nigzeles* — if land can be legally stolen, then the *sukkah* is a *sukkah gezulah* and one is not *yotzei* with a stolen *sukkah*. *Ve-inami karka einah nigzeles* — and if land cannot be considered stolen, then the person's relationship to the *sukkah* is akin to that of a borrower; it is a *sukkah she'ulah*, and according to Rabbi Eliezer, borrowing likewise does not suffice. Either way, the *sukkah* is not his, and since Rabbi Eliezer demands ownership, one does not fulfill the *mitzvah*.

### Rabbis' Position

The *rabanan*, by contrast, hold *adam yotzei yedei chovato be-sukkato shel chaveiro* — one can fulfill the obligation in another's *sukkah*. For the *rabanan*, the only remaining concern would be if the *karka* were actually labeled as stolen, which would introduce a distinct problem of *gezeilah*. But since *karka einah nigzelet* — since land does not acquire the legal identity of stolen property — the *sukkah* is simply one that the person does not own. And since the *rabanan* do not require ownership of the *sukkah*, there is no problem at all. This neatly maps onto the two problems we identified by *lulav*: the ownership concern and the *gezeilah* label. For Rabbi Eliezer, both are in play. For the *rabanan*, neither is.

### Stolen Schach

Having established the parameters of the debate over land, the *gemara* turns to a categorically different case: *aval gazal eitzim ve-sichach bahen* — if one actually stole the wood and used it as *schach*. Here one might have expected a unanimous ruling of invalidity, since the *schach* itself now carries the full legal identity of stolen property. Yet the *gemara* says *divrei ha-kol ein lo ela dmei eitzim* — by universal agreement, the thief owes only the monetary value of the wood, and the *sukkah* is valid. The reason is a combination of two related principles. First, placing the wood as *schach* may constitute a *shinuy* or at least a *shinuy ha-shem* — a transformation of the object's name and character, as what was "wood" is now called "*schach*." Second, and more decisively, there is the concept of *takkanat marish*, a rabbinic enactment discussed in *Bava Kamma*: if one steals a beam and builds it into one's house, even if the construction could technically be undone, the *rabanan* enacted that the thief need not dismantle the structure — payment suffices. The rationale is *takkanat ha-shavim*, a *takkana* designed to facilitate *teshuva*. Rather than demand that the wrongdoer undo every physical consequence of his sin — an obstacle that might deter repentance altogether — the Rabbis preferred a regime that demands restitution but not demolition. The analogy to modern amnesty programs is apt: the goal is reintegration and compensation, not perpetual liability. Once the *rabanan* enacted that the thief becomes the legal owner of the stolen *schach* upon building it into his *sukkah*, the object is no longer *gezulah* — it sheds both the problem of lacking ownership and the label of *gezeilah* — and the *sukkah* is fully valid.

## Schach vs. Land

### Gemara's Question

The *gemara* now asks: *mi-mai* — how do we know that the *machlokes* between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* pertains only to the case of *karka* and not to stolen *schach*?

### Dumya Argument

The answer is *mi-dekatani dumya de-reshut ha-rabim* — because the *baraita* presents the stolen *sukkah* as comparable to a *sukkah* built in *reshut ha-rabim*. *Mah reshut ha-rabim karka lav didei hu* — in the case of *reshut ha-rabim*, the problem is not the wood but the land, which does not belong to the builder. *Sukkah nami karka lav didei hu* — so too in the disputed case of the stolen *sukkah*, the problem is the land, not the *schach*. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* is therefore confined to the scenario where only the *karka* is problematic.

### Schach Consensus

When the *schach* itself is stolen, the picture is different in both directions. Biblically, one should universally agree that such a *sukkah* is invalid, since the *schach* genuinely carries the *gezeilah* label. Rabbinically, however, the *takkanat marish* takes effect, and one becomes the owner of the stolen *schach* once it is placed — and both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* therefore agree the *sukkah* is valid post facto. This explains the otherwise puzzling formulation: *divrei ha-kol ein lo ela dmei eitzim*. It is not that the theft is unproblematic; it is that the rabbinic enactment transforms the property status and thereby resolves the *mitzvah* concern, even as it leaves intact the obligation to compensate the original owner.

It is worth noting that by *sukkah*, unlike by *lulav*, the *gemara* never invokes *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah* as an independent concern. The problem of *gezeilah* is addressed on its own terms, and once the *takkana* renders the thief the legal owner, the issue is simply closed — the *aveira* recedes into the past without further consequence for the *mitzvah*.

## Woman's Complaint

### Servants' Theft

The *gemara* now presents a striking narrative. Rav Nachman was closely associated with the *Reish Galuta* — the political head of Babylonian Jewry — serving as his rabbinic authority in residence. The *Reish Galuta*'s household was not always a model of restraint, and the servants who went to procure *schach* for the *Reish Galuta*'s *sukkah* on the eve of *Sukkot* apparently helped themselves to wood from the property of others.

### Woman Complains

An elderly woman appeared before Rav Nachman with a complaint: *Reish Galuta ve-chulhu rabbanan de-vei Reish Galuta be-sukkah gezulah atitu* — the *Reish Galuta* and all the rabbis gathered in his house were sitting in a stolen *sukkah*, because the servants had cut down trees from her property without permission to use as *schach*. *Tzavcha ve-lo ashgechu bah* — she cried out, and no one paid her any attention. Remarkably, even Rav Nachman himself did not respond.

### Abraham Reference

Stung by the dismissal, the woman addressed Rav Nachman directly: *itata de-hava leh le-avuh tlas me'a ve-samni sarei avdei tzavcha kammaichu ve-lo ashgechisun bah* — a woman whose father had three hundred and eighteen servants is crying out before you and you ignore her? The reference is unmistakable: the number three hundred and eighteen is the count of Avraham's servants who accompanied him in the pursuit to rescue Lot. She was invoking her lineage from Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and insisting on her dignity as a Jew and a person: how could she be so casually dismissed?

### Payment Suffices

Rav Nachman responded to those present in the *sukkah*: *pita hi da* — this woman is a chatterbox — *ve-ein lah ela dmei eitzim bilvad*. Yes, the servants stole the *schach*, but the *Reish Galuta* owns it by virtue of the *takkanat marish*, and the woman is owed only monetary compensation. The *mitzvah* obligation is discharged.

What makes this narrative genuinely disquieting is the mismatch between the woman's complaint and Rav Nachman's response. She was not primarily raising a *halakhic* objection about the validity of the *sukkah*; she was raising a moral complaint about theft and being ignored. The technically correct answer — that you fulfill the *mitzvah* regardless, and she need only be compensated — is not actually an answer to her grievance. Rav Nachman could at minimum have said: you are right that it was wrong, you will be paid. The *gemara* does not editorialize clearly, leaving the reader to observe this gap between legal sufficiency and moral responsiveness.

## Takkanat Marish

### Ravina's Statement

Ravina now formally restates the underlying principle: *hai kishura de-matalalta de-gezulah avdu leh rabbanan takanta mishum takkanat marish* — for a beam of a *sukkah* that has been stolen, the Rabbis applied the same *takkana* as that of the *takkanat marish* from *Bava Kamma*, whereby monetary restitution suffices and the stolen material need not be physically returned.

### Common vs. Uncommon

The *gemara* objects: *pshita* — is this not obvious? We already established this principle for ordinary *schach* material. Why would a beam be any different? The answer is that *eitzim shechichi* — ordinary branches and sticks are common and readily available. If I owe you money instead of returning your sticks, you can easily go purchase replacements. But a beam — *lo shchicha* — is not so common. A custom-cut beam may be difficult to replace, and the original owner may rightly prefer the return of the actual object rather than a cash substitute. One might therefore have thought the *takkana* would not extend to items that are hard to replace, since money is a less adequate substitute. The *gemara* teaches that even so, *kol mashma lan* — the *takkana* applies universally: we still require only monetary restitution, because the broader social and religious benefit of facilitating *teshuva* outweighs the relative inconvenience to the owner.

### Seven-Day Rule

The *gemara* adds an important qualification: *hanei milei be-go shiva* — this *takkana* applies only during the seven days of *Sukkot*. If the thief performs *teshuva* and wishes to make restitution while *Sukkot* is ongoing, money suffices. *Aval le-vatar shiva* — but if *Sukkot* has passed and the beam is still in place, *hadar be-einei* — the thief must return the actual beam. The reasoning is that the *sukkah* was only meant to endure for the week; once the week is over, the construction has no ongoing purpose analogous to a house, and the force of the *takkana* dissolves.

### Permanence Factor

There is one exception: *ve-i chibro be-tina* — if the beam was secured with plaster, rendering the construction more permanent, then even after *Sukkot* the thief acquires the beam and owes only money. The underlying principle is that a *shinuy ha-chozer li-vriyato* — a reversible change — is insufficient to effect a *kinyan* under normal rules of property. Only because of the special rabbinic *takkana* does the thief acquire ownership during the week. Once the week ends and the temporary structure is no longer serving its purpose, the *takkana*'s force lapses — unless the permanence of the construction through plaster brings the situation back within the spirit of the original *takkanat marish* as applied to permanent buildings.

## Gezeilah Conclusion

### Summary

With this, the *gemara* concludes its extended treatment of *gezeilah* in the context of the *arba minim* and *sukkah*. By *lulav*, the analysis turned on whether the problem is one of ownership — relevant only on the first day, when *lachem* applies — or one of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah*, which would persist all seven days. By *sukkah*, the concept of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah* does not arise; instead, the analysis focuses entirely on the *gezeilah* label and the ownership question. Because *karka einah nigzelet*, land does not acquire the status of stolen property, and because the *rabanan* do not require ownership of the *sukkah*, most scenarios are resolved without invalidating the *mitzvah*. And in the case of stolen *schach*, the *takkanat marish* effectively eliminates the problem by transferring ownership to the thief, leaving only the monetary obligation to the original owner.

### Yaavesh Intro

Having set aside the *gezeilah* discussion, the *gemara* returns to the opening phrase of the *mishna*: *lulav ha-gazul ve-ha-yavesh* — a stolen *lulav* and a dried-out *lulav*. The *gezeilah* side has been extensively analyzed; now comes the turn of *yavesh*, the dried-out *lulav*, and the question of what role aesthetic quality plays in the *mitzvah* of the *arba minim*.

---

## Hadar Debate

### Rava's Claim

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *t'na yavesh pasul* — a dried-out *lulav* is invalid; *Rabbi Yehuda machshir* — Rabbi Yehuda permits it. Rava then offers an analysis of the underlying dispute. The word *hadar* appears in the *pasuk*: *pri eitz hadar* — the fruit of a beautiful tree. Everyone agrees that this word *hadar* is rooted in the *esrog*, describing the character of the fruit and its tree. The question is whether this aesthetic criterion extends to the other *minim* as well.

### Rabbis Connect

Rava states: *machlokes be-lulav* — the dispute concerns only a dried-out *lulav*. The *rabanan* hold *makshinan lulav le-esrog*: we draw an analogy between the *lulav* and the *esrog*, and just as *hadar* is a requirement for the *esrog*, so too it applies to the *lulav*, the *hadas*, and the *arava*. A dried-out *lulav* therefore fails the *hadar* standard.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds *lo makshinan lulav le-esrog* — the analogy is not drawn. For him, *hadar* is an *esrog* concern only, not applicable to the other three *minim*. Those *minim* may be dried out and still be valid. And *aval be-esrog*, says Rava, *divrei ha-kol hadar ba'inan* — regarding the *esrog*, everyone agrees that *hadar* is required. Rava is thus making two claims simultaneously: that Rabbi Yehuda in principle rejects any aesthetic demand for the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *arava*, and that even Rabbi Yehuda concedes the *hadar* requirement for the *esrog*. Both claims, as we will see, will be challenged.

---

## Challenge Rava

### Bound Leaves

The *gemara* challenges the first of Rava's claims. *Ve-lo ba'i Rabbi Yehuda hadar?* — do you really mean that Rabbi Yehuda has no *hadar* demands for the *lulav*? But our *mishna* states: Rabbi Yehuda says that if the leaves of the *lulav* are fanned out, one must bind them together against the spine from above. Surely this is an aesthetic demand — the *lulav* must look a certain way. Is this not because *ba'i hadar*?

The *gemara* rejects this inference. *Ki-dekatani ta'ama* — a *baraita* supplies the reason explicitly: Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Tarfon that the word *kapot temarim* means *kafut* — bound. If the *pasuk* describes the *lulav* as bound, and in practice it is spread out, *yachftenu* — one must bind it. The demand that the leaves be aligned and gathered is not an aesthetic preference but a formal definitional requirement derived from the word *kapot*: the *lulav* must be bound. Rabbi Yehuda has a specific structural requirement, not a general aesthetic one.

### Palm Binding

The *gemara* presses further. Our *mishna* also states: *ein ogdin et ha-lulav ela be-mino divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — when one binds together the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *arava*, Rabbi Yehuda requires that the binding itself be from the same species, specifically from the palm. One cannot use a string or a rubber band. Is this not an aesthetic concern — that it looks more pleasing when everything is from the same species?

### Rava's Response

Again the *gemara* rejects this. Rava explains: *afilu be-siva ve-afilu be-ikra de-dikla* — Rabbi Yehuda permits even ugly parts of the palm, even fibrous bark or material from the base of the trunk. If his concern were aesthetic, he would not permit ugly palm material. Clearly, aesthetics are not his motivation.

### Bal Tosif

*Ela mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda* — what then is the reason? Because Rabbi Yehuda holds *lulav tzarich egged* — the *lulav* requires binding as a formal Torah requirement. Binding the four *minim* together is not merely a nice custom; it is what constitutes the *mitzvah* object. If that is so, *ve-im meivi min achrin* — if one introduces a foreign material as the binding, *havei lehu chamisha minim* — one has effectively added a fifth component to the *mitzvah* object. This is a *bal tosif* concern, and it redefines what one is holding. For the *rabanan*, binding is only a practical convenience or aesthetic enhancement, and one may use any material. But for Rabbi Yehuda, the binding is constitutive of the *mitzvah* object, and its composition matters.

This distinction — between an aesthetic criterion and a formal definitional one — is the heart of Rava's defense. Rabbi Yehuda demands things of the *lulav*, but his demands arise from the legal definition of the object, not from any preference for beauty. He has no *hadar* requirement.

---

## Etrog Challenge

### City Dwellers

The *gemara* now turns to challenge the second limb of Rava's claim: that even Rabbi Yehuda requires *hadar* for the *esrog*. A *baraita* is cited that discusses all four *minim* together. Among its rulings: *kemushin kesheirim* — wilting *minim* are valid; *yeveshim pesulim* — dried-out ones are invalid; *Rabbi Yehuda omer af yeveshim* — Rabbi Yehuda permits even dried-out ones. Rabbi Yehuda then offers a proof: *ma'aseh bi-vnei krakhin* — there was a practice among city dwellers who, lacking easy access to fresh *arba minim*, *she-hayu morishim et lulaveihem li-vnei veneihem* — would bequeath their *lulavim* and *etrogim* to their children and grandchildren. By that point, the objects would long have dried out — and they used them anyway. Clearly, says Rabbi Yehuda, dried-out is acceptable.

### Urgency Objection

The *chachamim* respond: *misham re'aya? Ein she'at ha-dechak re'aya* — is that a proof? A situation of pressing necessity does not serve as a legal precedent. Under normal circumstances, when alternatives are available, a dried-out *lulav* remains invalid. The behavior of the city dwellers proves only that one may make concessions in extremis, not that the *mitzvah* is regularly fulfilled with dried-out *minim*.

### Rava Defended

But the *gemara* notes that Rabbi Yehuda says *af yeveshim* — even dried-out ones are valid — in a *baraita* that appears to encompass all four *minim*. *Mai lav esrog* — does this not include the *esrog*? In defense of Rava, the *gemara* answers *lo, alulav* — no, the statement refers only to the *lulav* (and presumably the *hadas* and *arava*). When Rabbi Yehuda invokes the practice of bequeathing dried-out *arba minim*, he means the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *arava*, not the *esrog*. Rava's position is thus preserved at this stage: Rabbi Yehuda still concedes that the *esrog* requires *hadar* and cannot be dried out.

### She'at Implications

The exchange about *she'at ha-dechak* carries significant *halakhic* weight. If the *chachamim* concede that under pressing circumstances one uses a dried-out *lulav* — and, as the *rishonim* point out, those city dwellers were presumably reciting a *beracha* — this implies that even a *pasul* object may be used with a *beracha* under *she'at ha-dechak*. The *Tosafot* on 31a draws this inference: *im lo matza kasher, mevarekh be-pasul* — if one cannot find a *kasher lulav* or *esrog*, one may use a *pasul* one and recite the *beracha*.

### Ashkenazi-Sefardi

This question was not merely theoretical. There were historical periods in which access to *kasher arba minim* was genuinely difficult, and different communities responded differently. The *chakhmei Sefarad* held that the *psulim* of *hadar* and *yavesh* apply only to the first day, and on *chol ha-moed* these concerns are absent — so at minimum, one fulfills the *mitzvah* on *chol ha-moed* even with such objects. The *chakhmei Ashkenaz* took a stricter view: these *psulim* apply all seven days — but, they held, *be-she'at ha-dechak* one may use *pasulim* and make a *beracha* even on the first day. A notable point of difference emerges: the Ashkenazi position enables use of *pasulim* with a *beracha* on the first day — which the Sefardi position, rooted in the first-day *lachem* requirement, would not permit. Some *rishonim* raise an additional consideration: the category of *yavesh* may be partially relative. If fresh *lulavim* are unknown in a community and only dried ones exist, perhaps a dried *lulav* is *hadar* by the standards of that environment. But other *rishonim* resist this, noting that the relativity argument cannot be extended to other types of *psulim* that are more absolute in character.

## Rava Rejected

### Old Etrog Proof

The *gemara* now adduces a definitive proof against Rava's position regarding the *esrog*. *Ta shma: esrog ha-yashan pasul* — an old *esrog* is invalid. *Rav Yehuda machshir* — Rav Yehuda permits it. This is explicit: the *baraita* is discussing an *esrog*, and Rav Yehuda validates it even when old. Rava claimed that on this point all agree — that an *esrog* requires *hadar* — and Rabbi Yehuda differs only regarding the *lulav*. This *baraita* directly contradicts that claim.

### Tyufta

*Tyufta de-Rava tyufta* — Rava's position is refuted. The *gemara* accepts the refutation. Rava was correct that Rabbi Yehuda rejects *hadar* as a principle, but he was wrong to limit that rejection to the *lulav*. Rabbi Yehuda rejects aesthetic demands for all four *minim*, including the *esrog*. A dried-out or old *esrog* is as valid as a dried-out *lulav*, according to Rabbi Yehuda.

---

## Non-Aesthetic Criteria

### Green Etrog

Having established that Rabbi Yehuda has no *hadar* requirement, the *gemara* works through several apparent counterexamples — cases where Rabbi Yehuda does impose demands on the *esrog* — and demonstrates that each arises from non-aesthetic considerations. First: our *mishna* states that an *esrog* that is green like a leek — *yarok ke-karti* — is invalid according to Rav Yehuda, while Rabbi Meir permits it. Is this not because Rav Yehuda demands *hadar* — that the *esrog* must look a certain way? The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-lo gamar peira* — because it has not yet ripened. An *esrog* that is still green has not completed its growth and is therefore not yet a fruit in the full sense. *Pri eitz hadar* requires a *pri*, and a fully ripened fruit is definitional. This is not an aesthetic judgment but a factual one about the fruit's developmental stage.

### Minimum Size

A second apparent counterexample: the *mishna* records a dispute about the minimum size of a valid *esrog*. Rabbi Meir holds *ke-egoz* — the size of a walnut. Rav Yehuda holds *ke-veitzia* — the size of an egg, a stricter and larger minimum. Does Rav Yehuda's higher minimum not reflect a *hadar* concern, a sense that an aesthetically appropriate *esrog* must meet a certain scale? The *gemara* again rejects this. The minimum size derives not from aesthetics but from the definition of what constitutes the fruit. Even if a particular *esrog* specimen will never grow larger, the legal standard looks at what a recognizable, fully formed *esrog* is — and something smaller than an egg does not meet that definition. As the *Tosafot* observe, even a small-variety fruit that typically grows to this size would still be subject to this standard, because the criterion is the definition of an *esrog* as a legal category, not its individual growth.

### Maximum Size

A third challenge involves the upper size limit. Rav Yehuda holds that an *esrog* is valid only if two can be held in one hand simultaneously — implying a maximum size beyond which the *esrog* is invalid. Rav Yosi disagrees and permits even an *esrog* so large that a single one requires both hands. Is Rav Yehuda's upper limit not an aesthetic one — that an excessively large *esrog* departs from the normative image of beauty?

### Teimani Etrogim

This question is practically significant given the existence of the large *Teimani esrogim*, which can be quite massive. According to Rav Yosi, even these are valid; there is no upper limit.

### Hand-Switching

Rava explains Rav Yehuda's concern with larger sizes on purely practical grounds: *lulav be-yamin ve-esrog be-smol* — the *lulav* is held in the right hand and the *esrog* in the left, because the *lulav* contains three *minim* and one generally favors the right hand for the primary object. *Zimnin de-michalflei* — sometimes one will accidentally reverse the arrangement, and then one must switch hands. At the moment of switching, one hand briefly holds both objects simultaneously. If the *esrog* is too large, it cannot fit alongside the *lulav* in the same hand even momentarily, and it will drop. Therefore Rav Yehuda requires that the *esrog* be small enough so that two of them could fit in one hand — meaning one *esrog* plus a *lulav* can fit together during the switch. This is an entirely practical concern, not an aesthetic one.

## Hadar Linguistics

### Tree Identifier

The *gemara* now reaches a resolution. Having established that Rabbi Yehuda has no *hadar* requirement by any of the four *minim* — not by *lulav*, not by *esrog* — it asks: *ve-ela le-Rav Yehuda, hai ketiv hadar* — what does Rabbi Yehuda do with the word *hadar* in the *pasuk*? If it does not create an aesthetic criterion, what does it mean?

### Perennial Meaning

The *gemara* answers: *hahu hadar be-ilano mi-shana le-shana* — the word *hadar* identifies the character of the tree, not an aesthetic demand on the fruit. Specifically, *hadar* is related to the root meaning "to dwell" — the fruit of an *esrog* tree dwells on its branches from year to year. The *esrog* tree is a perennial fruit-bearing tree, on which the fruit remains across growing seasons. The word *hadar* in *pri eitz hadar* tells us what type of tree to look for — one whose fruit persists year-round — not that the fruit must meet a standard of beauty. The *Ramban* on the *Torah* advances a parallel reading, suggesting that *esrog* itself derives from an Aramaic root meaning attractive or appealing, and that *hadar* may function as a proper descriptive name for the fruit rather than an operative *halakhic* criterion. In either reading, the word *hadar* serves an identifying or classifying function, not a prescriptive aesthetic one — and this is the foundation of Rabbi Yehuda's position throughout.
