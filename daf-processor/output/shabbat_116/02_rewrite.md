# Shabbat 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kitvei Kodesh & Muktze

### Mishna: Sefer & Tik

The daf begins on 116b with the continuation of a *mishna* that addresses saving *kitvei kodesh* from a fire on *Shabbat*. The *mishna* states: *matzilin tik hasefer im hasefer* — one may save the container or mantle of a *sefer* together with the *sefer* itself, and similarly the bag of *tefillin* together with the *tefillin*. The *mishna* adds: *ve'af al pi she'yesh betokhan ma'ot* — even if there is money inside the container. This permits the incidental movement of *muktzeh* as part of the salvage operation.

The *mishna* then asks: *uleheikhan matzilin otan* — to where may these items be brought? The answer is *lemavoi she'eino mefulash* — to a *mavoi*, a courtyard, that is not open at both ends. *Mefulash* denotes a thoroughfare open on both sides. The implication is that there remains some rabbinic problem with carrying in this *mavoi*, though it is not entirely forbidden. Ben Beteira, however, says *afilu lemefulash* — even to a *mavoi* that is open at both ends, which is a considerably more permissive position and presumably entails a more serious rabbinic concern about carrying.

Two explicit allowances emerge from this *mishna* for *sefarim*: first, one may bring them into a courtyard where carrying is not fully rabbinically sanctioned; second, one may incidentally move *muktzeh* in the process. A third issue — whether and how much one may save before the concern arises of coming to extinguish the fire — is addressed more fully in the subsequent *mishna* dealing with food.

### Mavoi: Mefulash Debate

The *gemara* initially focuses on the *muktzeh* aspect. It then introduces a *baraita* that, unexpectedly, concerns the fourteenth of Nisan falling on *Shabbat* — a digression that will ultimately connect back to the *mishna*'s allowances. Before turning to that, however, the debate in the *mishna* between the majority and Ben Beteira turns on the definition of the *mavoi* into which one may carry. The *gemara* will explore at length what precisely *mefulash* and *eino mefulash* mean, a discussion that will be taken up in full later.

### Ben Beteira Identity

A question arises regarding the identity of Ben Beteira — specifically, why he is referred to by the patronymic alone, without a personal name, and why this figure may be unfamiliar. Ben Beteira is a *tanna* who appears from time to time in rabbinic literature. He features notably in discussions surrounding the question of bringing the *korban Pesach* on *Shabbat* — the famous episode in which *Bnei Beteira* stood in opposition, or at least in tension, with Hillel's ruling, where Hillel invoked a tradition received from Shemaya and Avtalyon. Ben Beteira may also appear in discussions involving Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ruling to blow the *shofar* in Yavne after the *churban*. In any case, he is a recognized *tanna* whose opinions appear at various points in the Talmud.

## Pesach Flaying Debat (II)

### Rabbi Yishmael vs. Sages

The *gemara* unexpectedly introduces a *baraita*: *arba'ah asar she'chal lihyot be-Shabbat* — if the fourteenth of Nisan falls on *Shabbat*. The *korban Pesach* overrides *Shabbat*, but the question is how far that override extends. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka rules: *mafshitin et hapesach ad hachazeh* — one may flay the *pesach* only up to the chest, working from the legs upward. This allows removal of the *emurim*, the innards designated for the *mizbe'ach*, which may indeed be offered on *Shabbat* as part of bringing the *korban Pesach*. However, one may not flay it completely, since complete flaying would serve the meat — and the meat will not be eaten until *motzaei Shabbat*. The Sages disagree: *mafshitin et kulo* — one may flay the entire *pesach*.

The *gemara* asks: the position of Rabbi Yishmael is intelligible — *dehai avid letzorech gavo'ah*, what you do is for the sake of Heaven, for what is needed on the altar, and since that is permitted on *Shabbat*, so is this. But what is the reasoning of the Sages? Why permit flaying that serves only the meat, which is not needed until after *Shabbat*? Rabbah bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan invokes a verse: *kol pa'al Hashem le-ma'anehu* — all that God has done is for His sake (Proverbs 16:4). The idea, however counterintuitive it may seem to derive a practical *halacha* from this verse in *Ketuvim*, is that all aspects of the *korban Pesach* process, including preparing the meat for consumption, are ultimately in the service of God. One therefore does not draw a fine line between what is strictly needed for the *mizbe'ach* and what is needed for the *hedyot* — it is all part of bringing the *korban Pesach*, whose very purpose is to be eaten. The *gemara* here operates in a mode of *giluy milta* — the verse is not directly *doche Shabbat*, but rather illuminates a general perspective on the latitude one may take.

### Three Explanations

Within the Sages' position, two distinct explanations are offered for permitting complete flaying. Rav Yosef says: *kedei shelo yasriach* — so that the meat does not spoil. Since the entire purpose of the *korban Pesach*, more than any other *korban*, is the *achilah*, the eating, at night, one is permitted to protect that meat even on *Shabbat*. Rava offers a different rationale: *shelo yehei kodshei shamayim mutalim ke-nevelah* — so that sanctified objects of Heaven should not lie about like a carcass, half-flayed and unseemly. For Rava, it is not the practical concern of meat spoiling but rather the inappropriateness, the indignity, of leaving a sacred animal in such a state that justifies complete flaying.

### Meat vs. Kavod Kodashim

The practical difference between Rav Yosef and Rava can be illustrated in two cases. First: *de-manach aphtora de-dahava* — if the carcass is laid on a gold table, treated with great dignity, no one could claim it resembles a *nevelah*. This would resolve Rava's concern but not Rav Yosef's. One might ask, then, why according to Rava it would ever be necessary to flay completely — simply placing it on a gold table would solve the problem. Second: *yoma de-astana* — a day with a strong cold wind, as Rashi explains a north wind, that keeps the meat cool and prevents spoilage. This resolves Rav Yosef's concern but not Rava's.

A parallel is worth noting here. In the chapter of *Perek Rabbi Eliezer de-milah*, the *gemara* discusses performing a *brit milah* on *Shabbat*. The ruling there is that if the *mohel* is still in the process of performing the *milah*, he may continue removing small additional pieces of skin even if they would not render the *milah* invalid — because he is still mid-process. Once he has stopped, he may not return for those pieces. Similarly, here, once the broader activity of preparing the *korban Pesach* is sanctioned on *Shabbat*, even peripheral steps within that process may be included under certain conditions.

The *gemara* then asks: according to Rabbi Yishmael, what does he do with the verse *kol pa'al Hashem le-ma'anehu*? The verse must give him some latitude — he explains it as permitting flaying to the extent necessary for removing the *emurim*: *shelo yotzi imurim kodem she-yafshit ha-or* — one does not remove the innards before partially flaying the hide, because the wool or hair of the animal's skin would contaminate the innards if drawn out without first flaying. Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan explains the technical reason: the fleece or hair would get into the innards and render them unseemly. So Rabbi Yishmael grants latitude to flay insofar as it facilitates removing the *emurim* for the *mizbe'ach*, but no further.

## Sefer Torah Precedent

### Basis Distinction

Rav Ashi cites Mar Ukva: *mai hadru lei chavrayya le-Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* — what did the colleagues say to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka to argue for greater latitude in the *korban Pesach* flaying? The tradition is that they invoked the *mishna* about saving *kitvei kodesh*, reasoning: *im matzilin tik hasefer im hasefer* — if we allow saving the container of a *sefer Torah* together with the *sefer Torah*, should we not permit *nafshit et hapesach meirosho*, flaying the *pesach* from its head completely?

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *mi dami* — how can these be compared? *Hasam tiltul* — there, by the *sefer Torah*, at most we are dealing with moving something, perhaps *muktzeh*, perhaps carrying into an imperfectly sanctioned *mavoi*, but in any case a rabbinic concern about *tiltul*. *Hacha melacha* — here we are discussing *melacha*, actual flaying, which would be a genuine biblical prohibition absent the override of the *korban Pesach*.

Rav Ashi therefore explains: *bitartei pligi* — actually there are two parallel debates by the *korban Pesach*. There is a *muktzeh* dimension — *pligi bitiltul* — and there is the *melacha* dimension — *pligi bimelacha*. The argument from the *mishna* is thus reformulated: *im matzilin tik hasefer im hasefer, lo nitaltel ora agav basar* — if one may carry the *sefer Torah* in its container, should one not be permitted to carry the skin together with the meat? According to Rashi, this addresses the case where the skin is still attached to the meat: the skin is *muktzeh* since it is not usable on *Shabbat*, and the question is whether it may be moved together with the meat. Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's framing, preferring a case where the skin is fully detached, with the argument being that one would use the flayed skin as a kind of carrier or wrapper for the meat — analogous to the *tik* of the *sefer Torah*.

The implication is that Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka was arguing not only against complete flaying, but also against using the flayed skin to carry the meat. The colleagues countered from our *mishna*.

The *gemara* rejects this comparison: *hasam na'asa basis ledavar hamutar* — there, the container of the *sefer Torah* serves as a base for something permitted, the *sefer Torah* itself, which is not *muktzeh* at all. *Hacha na'asa basis ledavar ha'asur* — here the skin serves as a base for something forbidden. According to Rashi, the flesh is not edible on *Shabbat* since it is raw. Tosafot goes further: when *Shabbat* began, the animal was still alive, rendering its flesh *muktzeh* from that moment forward. The two cases are therefore fundamentally different.

### External Container

The *gemara* attempts yet another formulation of the argument: *ela hachi ka'amrei lei* — here is what they were actually saying. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is not simply that one carries the *sefer Torah* in its container together with the money that happened to be inside. Rather, consider this scenario: one had no container around the *sefer Torah*, went to another room where there was a *sefer Torah* container that happened to have money in it, brought that container to the *sefer Torah*, placed the *sefer Torah* inside, and carried it out. In this case, when *Shabbat* began, the container held only money — not a *sefer Torah*. It was therefore a pure *basis ledavar ha'asur*. Yet the *gemara* assumes this too would be permitted, even though the *mishna* does not explicitly state it. And the argument to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka would be: if even this is permitted, should one not be allowed to carry the skin together with the meat, even though the meat is a *davar ha'asur*?

The *gemara* again asks: *ve-hi gufa minalan* — who told you this external-container scenario is actually permitted? If you derive it from the fact that in our *mishna* we did not require the person to shake the money out before saving the *sefer Torah*, that is because *ade-hachi ve-hachi nafla de-leika* — by the time you would do all that, the fire would have consumed everything. But in the external-container case, *ade-hachi ve-hachi lishtaiya* — before you got back with the container, all the money could simply have been tipped out. So the *gemara* at least implies that one should dump the money before bringing the container to the *sefer Torah*. If there is an alternative, one would be expected to use it.

This is why Tosafot insists the comparison must be to a case where the skin is fully flayed off and no longer attached: the argument is precisely that even when there is no other option for carrying the meat — just as there was no other option for saving the *sefer Torah* quickly — one should be permitted to use the skin even though it is a *basis ledavar ha'asur*.

### Rabbinical Labor

After these multiple failed attempts at comparison, the *gemara* arrives at its final resolution: *ella amar bar Abba Ashi le-olam ke-da-amran me-ikara* — really it was as originally said, focusing on the comparison between the *tiltul* allowance by the *sefer Torah* and the flaying of the *pesach*. How does the comparison work despite the objection that *tiltul* and *melacha* are categorically different?

The answer: *kegon de-lo kafid ella a-or* — the case is one where the person has no intention to use the hide, has no interest in the skin whatsoever. Flaying is only a biblical *melacha* when one intends to preserve the skin for use as leather. When one is solely interested in protecting the meat — or maintaining its dignity, in Rava's formulation — the flaying is only a rabbinic prohibition. And the *gemara* then adds: *de-shakil lei bevarzei* — furthermore, the flaying is done in tiny pieces, scraping off small bits of skin rather than removing one continuous piece. This eliminates even the possibility of *psik reisha*, since no usable piece of hide results. Abaye and Rava both rule that Rabbi Shimon concedes the rule of *psik reisha ve-lo yamut* — that even unintended but inevitable results are prohibited — but if no usable result is produced at all, that concern evaporates.

The rough comparison thus emerges: just as certain rabbinic concerns — some *muktzeh* considerations, some issues with carrying in a *mavoi* — are set aside to preserve sacred *sefarim*, so too certain rabbinic concerns around flaying are set aside to preserve the *korban Pesach*. The two cases remain distinguishable in many ways — not all rabbinic prohibitions are equivalent, and the contexts differ. But the general principle that we ease certain *derabbanans* to preserve something sacred is shared between them.

A further question is raised regarding the hide of the *korban Pesach* in general: since by other *korbanot* the *kohanim* receive the hides, what happens to the hide of the *pesach*, which goes entirely home with the owners? The answer is that the owner receives the hide as well, just as the owner receives the meat.

## Pesach Flaying Debat (II)

### Shinu'i: Small Pieces

As noted in the final resolution above, the *gemara* establishes that the flaying in the case of the *korban Pesach* must be performed *be-varzei* — by scraping off tiny pieces rather than removing the skin in one continuous motion. This *shinu'i* ensures that no usable piece of hide is produced and therefore eliminates the question of *psik reisha*. Since Abaye and Rava both hold that Rabbi Shimon concedes in a case of *psik reisha ve-lo yamut*, it is essential that the method of flaying produce no usable result. With this modification, the flaying is purely rabbinic in character, enabling the comparison to the *derabbanans* permitted by the *mishna* for saving *kitvei kodesh*.

## Mavoi Definitions

### Rav Chisda: Dual Lechaim

Returning to the *mishna*'s debate about where one may carry *kitvei kodesh*, the *gemara* now defines *mefulash* and *eino mefulash* in earnest. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami mefulash, heichi dami she'eino mefulash* — what exactly do these terms mean? Rav Chisda offers a surprisingly restrictive definition. *Shalosh mechitzot u-shnei lechayayim* — three walls and two *lechaim* (vertical posts at the open end): that is a *mavoi she'eino mefulash*, a closed *mavoi*, one that is entirely in order for carrying. *Shalosh mechitzot ve-lechi echad* — three walls and only one *lechi*: that is a *mavoi mefulash*, an open *mavoi*. This is shocking on its face, since three walls with a single *lechi* is the standard requirement for rabbinic carrying in a *mavoi* according to the normative view of Beit Hillel.

The *gemara* explains that both positions in the *mishna* are *aliba de-Rabbi Eliezer*. Rabbi Eliezer, as taught in the *mishna* in Eruvin, requires *shenei lechayayim* — two posts, one on each side of the opening — in order to permit carrying in a *mavoi*. Beit Hillel requires only one *lechi* or a *korah* (a horizontal beam). So the debate in our *mishna* is understood entirely within Rabbi Eliezer's framework: the majority holds that even for *sifrei kodesh* the *mavoi* must meet Rabbi Eliezer's full standard — three walls and two *lechaim*. There is thus no great innovation, since carrying there is fully permitted. Ben Beteira then says he will be lenient even with only one *lechi*, which Rabbi Eliezer normally would not consider sufficient. The *gemara* is thus pushing the *mishna* to the most restrictive possible reading, anxious to limit the cases in which a rabbinic leniency is being granted on account of *kitvei kodesh*.

### Rava: Two Walls Model

Rava objects to Rav Chisda's interpretation on two grounds. First, the language: *gimmel mechitzot ve-lechi echad mefulash karyat lei* — you call three walls and one *lechi* a *mefulash*, an open-ended *mavoi*? That language suggests a space wide open on both ends, like a thoroughfare. Three walls and a single *lechi* is far from open-ended; calling it *mefulash* is a serious stretch. Second, according to the Sages who require two *lechaim*, carrying into a *mavoi* with two *lechaim* is permitted for anyone — there is nothing special about *sifrei kodesh* that would need to be mentioned. The *mishna* would be stating a trivial point.

Rava therefore proposes a different framework: *shetei mechitzot ve-shnei lechayayim ze mavoi she'eino mefulash* — two *mechitzot* with a *lechi* on each end is the *mavoi she'eino mefulash*. *Shetei mechitzot ve-lechi echad ze mavoi mefulash* — two *mechitzot* with only one *lechi* is the *mefulash*. Now the language *mefulash* is fully appropriate — a space with only two real walls and one post is genuinely open. This framework operates *aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds an extremely permissive position. As taught in a *baraita*: if someone has two houses on two sides of a *reshut ha-rabim*, he may place a *lechi* or *korah* on each end — north and south — and carry in the middle, treating the space as enclosed despite it being a full thoroughfare. The Sages respond: *ein me'arvin reshut ha-rabim be-kach* — that does not constitute an *eiruv* for a *reshut ha-rabim* with only two real walls. But Rabbi Yehuda's position is that two walls with a *lechi* on each side are sufficient. Within that framework, Rava explains the debate: the majority requires two *lechaim* (one on each open end), while Ben Beteira permits even with only one *lechi*.

### Rav Ashi: Three Walls

Abaye presses Rava: even granting the language of *mefulash*, according to Rabbi Yehuda who permits carrying with two walls and a *lechi* on each side, if that is sufficient, why does the *mishna* present this as a special allowance for *sifrei kodesh* alone? According to the Sages within Rabbi Yehuda's framework, carrying would be permitted there for anyone. Tosafot anticipates this: Rava presumably holds that Rabbi Yehuda means two walls and two *lechaim* are sufficient only *de-oraita* but not necessarily *de-rabbanan*, so there remains a rabbinic concern that the leniency for *sifrei kodesh* addresses. Nevertheless, the objection stands that the *mishna*'s framing is peculiar.

Rav Ashi offers the final resolution: *shalosh mechitzot u-lechi echad ze mavoi she'eino mefulash* — three *mechitzot* with one *lechi* is the *eino mefulash*. *Shalosh mechitzot belo lechi ze mavoi ha-mefulash* — three walls with no *lechi* at all is the *mefulash*. This operates *afilu le-Rabbi Eliezer* — even within Rabbi Eliezer's framework. Rabbi Eliezer normally requires two *lechaim*. The allowance for *kitvei kodesh* is: *aval le-Sefer Torah be-chad lechi sagi* — for saving a *Sefer Torah*, one *lechi* suffices. And the debate with Ben Beteira is whether even no *lechi* at all — three bare walls with a fully open end — is permitted for saving *kitvei kodesh*.

The resolution is elegant on the linguistic level: *mefulash* naturally describes a space with three walls and nothing at the fourth end — genuinely open. And the debate remains within a three-wall framework throughout, since it is difficult to imagine allowing carrying without three walls absent the radical position of Rabbi Yehuda. Even Ben Beteira requires three walls; he only dispenses with the *lechi* requirement entirely.

What is striking about this entire discussion is how modest the actual allowance for *kitvei kodesh* turns out to be. Carrying the *sefer Torah* with its container that happened to have money: that was *tiltul min hatzad* and *basis ledavar mutar u-ledavar asur*, which under normal circumstances is not really problematic. The innovation with respect to carrying is limited to one fewer *lechi* than normally required, or possibly no *lechi* at all — but always with three walls. The *gemara* resists giving any dramatic license for *kitvei kodesh* and works hard to minimize the rabbinic deviation involved. The deeper point will emerge as the discussion turns to food: the real concern is not how much rabbinic latitude is extended but rather the fear that the person, in his anxiety, will cross a line and extinguish the fire.

## Food Salvage Rules

### Mishna: Three Seudos

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishna*: *matzilin mazon shalosh se'udot* — one may save food for three meals. Human food is saved for a person, animal food for animals. The three-meal rule reflects the *Shabbat* obligation of eating three meals, a topic that will be developed further.

The *mishna* specifies: *naflah deleikah beleilei Shabbat* — if the fire broke out on Friday night, one may save food for three meals. *Beshacharit* — if it broke out in the morning, only two meals, since one meal has already passed. *Bemincha* — if it broke out at *Mincha* time, only one meal, since only one remains before *Shabbat* ends. The assumption is that once *Shabbat* is over one can prepare fresh food. Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *le'olam matzilin mazon shalosh seudos* — regardless of when the fire breaks out, one may always save food for three meals, since three meals is the sanctioned *Shabbat* food quantity in all circumstances.

### Gezerah: Overzealousness

The *gemara* asks a pointed question: since the food is not *muktzeh* and, as Rashi emphasizes in contrast to the *kitvei kodesh* case, the courtyard into which the food is brought is fully kosher for carrying — why limit how much can be saved? *Nitzil tfei* — let one save as much as possible. Rava answers: *mitoch she'adam bahul al mamono* — because a person is so anxious and pressured about his monetary loss, *ai sharis lei asi le-chabbuyei* — if you permit him too broadly, he will become so consumed with salvaging everything that he will come to extinguish the fire. The restriction to three *seudos* is a *gezerah* to prevent this. If you tell him: grab three *seudos* and leave, he will hopefully not lose sight of *Shabbat* in his panic.

This creates an interesting asymmetry: on one side, we extend certain leniencies — permitting carrying into a *mavoi* that lacks the normally required *lechaim* — and on the other side, we impose restrictions — limiting food salvage — all animated by the same concern about the person's behavior during a crisis.

A brief note on contemporary practice: *lehalacha* we are considerably more permissive regarding extinguishing fires on *Shabbat* due to *sakana*, the danger to life, which raises the question of why those concerns were apparently not operative in the *gemara*'s context. One plausible answer is that stone houses with dirt floors, which were the norm in the ancient *Eretz Yisrael* setting and perhaps even more so under Roman occupation where fire brigades existed, simply did not burn in the way that later wooden structures in *Ashkenaz* did.

### Abaye's Objection

Abaye raises a parallel: a *baraita* teaches that if a barrel of wine broke on one's roof on *Shabbat*, one may place a single vessel underneath to catch the spilling wine — *mavi kli u-maniach tachteha* — but one may not hold a vessel to catch the drip, nor bring additional vessels to collect what is flowing off the roof. One may perform this one-time act of placing a vessel, but not engage in an ongoing salvage operation. Abaye asks: here there is no fire, nothing being extinguished — what *gezerah* is at work? The *gemara* answers: *gezeira shema yavi kli derech reshut ha-rabim* — we are concerned that in the course of a full salvage operation, the person will bring vessels through a *reshut ha-rabim*, constituting a biblical carrying violation. The parallel underscores a broader rabbinic concern about letting people engage in extended emergency salvage operations, lest they inadvertently commit more serious violations in their haste.

## Wine Barrel Salvage

### One vs. Multiple Vessels

The *gemara* now examines the barrel case in its own right. *Nishbera lo chavis be-rosh gago* — a barrel broke on one's roof. *Mevi kli u-maniach tachtav* — one may bring a vessel and place it underneath. But one may not bring another vessel to catch the drip, nor a second vessel to position at the edge of the roof so the wine streams into it as it flows. Placing a vessel beneath the barrel is a single discrete act; anything that looks like an ongoing, organized salvage effort is prohibited.

### Guest Exception

The *baraita* continues: *nizdamnu lo orchim* — if guests unexpectedly arrive, one may now bring additional vessels and collect the wine, since one is ostensibly doing so to serve guests, not to conduct a salvage operation. But: *lo yiklot ve-achar kach yazmin* — one may not first collect and then invite guests. Rather, *yazmin ve-achar kach yiklot* — one must first invite the guests and then collect. Even then, the *baraita* says: *ve-ein maarimin be-kach* — one may not scheme. That is, one may not invite guests as a calculated ruse to justify collecting wine.

### Scheming Debate

Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda disagrees: *maarimin* — one may scheme in this case. The wine is going to waste, and since the underlying concern is merely rabbinic, scheming to prevent monetary loss is acceptable here.

The *gemara* observes that this seems to parallel a broader *tannaitic* debate about *ha'arama* — scheming — in *halacha*, a significant topic that appears across various contexts, from selling *chametz* before *Pesach* to transferring a firstborn animal's ear to a non-Jew. The question of when *halacha* permits, ignores, or prohibits such schemes is a recurring theme.

## Pit Animals Analogy

### Mother & Offspring

The *gemara* wonders whether the debate about *ha'arama* in the barrel case mirrors the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding animals that fell into a pit on *Yom Tov*. The *baraita*: *oso ve-et beno she-naflu le-vor be-Yom Tov* — a mother and its offspring fell into a pit on *Yom Tov*. Under normal circumstances, one may not slaughter both on the same day. Rabbi Eliezer says: *ma'aleh et ha-rishon al menat le-shochto* — bring up the first one with the intention of slaughtering it. Then the second one: one may lower food to it where it is, *bishvil she-lo yamut*, so it does not die, but one may not raise it out of the pit, since there is no justification for *shechting* it that day. Rabbi Yehoshua says: bring up the first one declaring intent to slaughter it, then once it is out of the pit, say: actually, I prefer the second one — and scheme to bring the second one up as well. *Ratzah zeh shochet, ratzah zeh shochet* — then one may slaughter either one. Rabbi Yehoshua even goes so far as to permit changing one's mind back to the first animal after the second is already out.

### Alternatives Analysis

The *gemara* cautions against a simple equation of these two debates. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer's objection to scheming by the animals is specific to that case: *de-efshar be-parnasa* — there is a reasonable alternative, namely lowering food to the animal. Since an alternative exists that prevents any loss to the animal, there is no justification for the scheme. *Aval hacha de-lo efshar* — but in the barrel case, where the wine will simply be destroyed with no alternative, even Rabbi Eliezer might permit scheming. Conversely, Rabbi Yehoshua may permit scheming by the animals not only because of monetary loss but because of *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — the prohibition against causing suffering to animals, who are sitting at the bottom of a pit. *Aval hacha de-leika tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — in the barrel case, where there is no animal suffering involved, Rabbi Yehoshua might not permit scheming. The two debates thus cannot simply be collapsed into one. Different *tannaim* may take different positions depending on the particular factors at play, and one cannot assume consistency across all cases of *ha'arama*.

## Food Salvage Details

### Bread Quality Hierarchy

The *gemara* returns to the salvage of food from a fire and addresses a further *baraita*. *Hitzeipa nikiya* — if one saved fine bread first, *ein matzipin pat hadraah* — one may not subsequently go back to save coarser bran bread. Once you have fine bread for your three meals, who would eat bran bread? There is no purpose in saving it. However: *aval pat hadraah, matzipin nikiya* — if one first saves bran bread, one may then go back and save fine bread as well, since the three meals of bran bread are insufficient, and one may reasonably claim to want better-quality bread for *Shabbat*.

### Holiday Boundaries

The *baraita* continues: *matzilin mi-Yom ha-Kippurim le-Shabbat* — one may save food on *Yom Kippur* for *Shabbat*, in the case where *Yom Kippur* falls on Friday and *Shabbat* immediately follows, since one will have no opportunity to prepare *Shabbat* food and *hachana* from *Yom Kippur* to *Shabbat* is not problematic in this direction. *Aval lo mi-Shabbat le-Yom ha-Kippurim* — but one may not save from *Shabbat* for *Yom Kippur*, since one cannot eat on *Yom Kippur*, and for *motzaei Yom Kippur* there will be time to prepare. *Ve-ein tzarich lomar mi-Shabbat le-Yom Tov* — needless to say, one may not save from *Shabbat* for *Yom Tov*, since *Yom Tov* permits food preparation. *Ve-lo mi-Shabbat le-Shabbat ha-ba'ah* — nor from one *Shabbat* to the following *Shabbat*, since six intervening days allow for preparation. One may, however, save on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat*, since one cannot prepare food on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* may be *mechin le-Shabbat*.

Tosafot raises a question: why would one think it is permitted to save from *Shabbat* to *motzaei Yom Kippur*? After all, *motzaei Yom Kippur* is an ordinary weekday and one does not save from *Shabbat* to a weekday — just as one does not save from *Shabbat* to *motzaei Shabbat*. Tosafot answers: there is a *mitzvah* to eat immediately upon the conclusion of *Yom Kippur*, and one might have thought that this *mitzvah* constitutes sufficient justification. Moreover, given the fast, there is an added concern about delaying the resumption of eating. Alternatively, the case concerns saving food *for Yom Kippur itself* — not for after it — to feed children, who must be fed even on *Yom Kippur*.

### Forgotten Bread

Another *baraita*: *shachach pat be-tanur ve-kiddesh alav ha-yom* — one forgot bread in the oven and *Shabbat* came in. Taking bread out of an oven — *rediyat ha-pat* — is rabbinically prohibited. The *tanna de-bei Rabbi Yishmael* teaches that *rediyat ha-pat* is a *chochma* — a skilled activity — but not a *melacha*, and therefore the prohibition is purely rabbinic. Nevertheless, normally this rabbinic restriction is in force on *Shabbat*.

The question is: does the imperative to have food for *Shabbat* override this rabbinic prohibition? The *baraita* rules: *matzilin mazon shalosh seudos* — yes, one may remove bread from the oven to save three *seudos* worth. This is quite striking, going considerably further than the *mishna*, which never sanctioned the violation of a *derabbanan*. Furthermore: *ve-omer la-acherim, bo'u ve-hatzilu lachem* — one may even announce to others that they may come and save food for themselves. People may thus come and collectively save large quantities of bread, each taking for themselves three meals' worth.

However: *u-keshe-hu rodeh, lo yirdeh be-mardeia* — when removing the bread, do not use the normal baker's shovel that is the standard implement. *Ela be-sakin* — use a knife instead. This *shinu'i* is required. The *gemara* asks: *eini* — is the *shinu'i* really necessary? Since *rediyat ha-pat* is only rabbinic, is it not already permitted in its normal form? The *gemara* answers: *kama de-efshar li-shnuyei meshaninnan* — wherever possible, we prefer to introduce a *shinu'i*. Even when a rabbinic prohibition is being overridden for a legitimate reason, one does not do it in the normal forbidden manner if an alternative mode is available. The *gemara* is thus quite strict in demanding the *shinu'i* even where the underlying activity is already rabbinic. Notably, the very idea that one might not require the *shinu'i* — since the entire prohibition is only rabbinic — is itself a striking position that has no explicit precedent in the *mishna*.

## Shabbat Meals

### Friday Preparation

From the salvage discussion the *gemara* transitions naturally to the laws of eating on *Shabbat*. Rav Chisda teaches: *le-olam yashkim adam le-hotza'at Shabbat* — a person should always rise early to spend money on *Shabbat* expenses. The verse states: *ve-haya ba-yom ha-shishi ve-hachinu et asher yavi'u* — on the sixth day they prepared what they would bring. The word *ve-haya* implies immediacy: the very first activity of Friday morning should be preparing for *Shabbat*.

### Lechem Mishneh

Rabbi Abba teaches: *be-Shabbat chayav adam livtzoa al shtei kikarot* — on *Shabbat* one is obligated to break bread over two loaves, the *lechem mishneh*. The source is the *manna*, where the verse records that a double portion fell on Friday. The biblical precedent of *lechem mishneh* thus mandates that one hold two loaves when making *hamotzi* on *Shabbat*.

### Rav Kahana Practice

Rav Ashi reports: *chazina lei le-Rav Kahana de-nakit tartei u-vatza chada* — I observed Rav Kahana hold both loaves when reciting *hamotzi* but break into only one of them. He explained his reasoning from the verse: *liktot ksiv* — the word used is *liktot*, to gather. One is required to hold the two loaves, not necessarily to break into both. This is the common practice of holding both loaves while breaking only one, saving the other for a subsequent meal.

### Rabbi Zeira Custom

Rabbi Zeira had a different custom: *hava vatza akula sheirusa* — he would break bread on every item he was serving at the beginning of the meal, and Rashi explains further that he would cut a portion large enough to last the entire meal. Ravina asked Rav Ashi: *ve-amichzei ke-ravtanusa* — does this not look like gluttony, to have this enormous chunk of bread before him? Rav Ashi replied: *keivan de-chol yoma lo avid, ve-ha'idna hu de-ka avid, lo michzei ke-ravtanusa* — since this is not what one normally does on other days and is done specifically now, it does not look like gluttony. It appears, rather, as something done for *kavod Shabbat*, as part of the lavish and distinguished character of the *Shabbat* meal.

### Eiruv Bread Use

Rav Ammi and Rav Assi had another practice: *ki matlu lehu rifta de-eiruva* — when the bread used for making an *eiruv* was brought to them — whether *eiruv chatzeirot* or, by analogy for our practice, *eiruv tavshilin* — *sharu lei*, they would eat it and begin their meal with it. Their reasoning: *ho'il ve-is'avid bah chada mitzvah, lis'avid bah mitzvah achrina* — since one *mitzvah* has already been performed with this bread, let another *mitzvah* be performed with it as well. They would therefore use the *eiruv* bread specifically for their *Shabbat* meal. Some maintain this practice specifically, using the *eiruv* bread for *shalosh seudos* or some other *oneg Shabbat* purpose, in order to honor the bread that performed one *mitzvah* by giving it another. The discussion of related *mitzvot* of eating on *Shabbat* will continue.
