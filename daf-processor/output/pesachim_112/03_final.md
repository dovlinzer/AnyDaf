# Pesachim 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Health & Poverty Omens

### Causes of Blindness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי יָהֵיב אַרְבּוֹנָא לִנְהוֹרָא: מַן דְּסָרֵיק רֵישֵׁיהּ יַבִּשׁ, וּמַן דְּשָׁתֵי טִיף טִיף, וּמַן דְּסָיֵים מְסָנֵי אַדְּמִייתִנְיָה כַּרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: These three matters cause blindness to the light** of one's eyes: **One who combs his hair** when it is **dry, one who drinks** wine that is **dripping** from the barrel, **and one who puts on shoes when his feet are wet** after being washed.

The daf begins at the bottom of 111b with a teaching of Rav Yosef: *hani tlata milei yahiv arvona l'nehora* — these three things give a pledge toward blindness, meaning they make a person blind. The first is combing one's hair dry, without wetting it beforehand. The second is drinking in a drip-by-drip fashion — placing one's mouth directly to a dripping barrel. The third is putting on one's shoes while one's feet are still wet. These are presented as genuine practical concerns, even if their precise physiological rationale is not elaborated upon.

### Poverty Omens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּלַאי בְּבֵיתָא קְשֵׁי לְעַנְיוּתָא. כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: תְּלָא סִילְתָּא — תְּלָא מְזוֹנֵיהּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא רִיפְתָּא, אֲבָל בִּישְׂרָא וְכַוְורֵי — לֵית לַן בַּהּ, אוֹרְחֵיהּ הִיא. פָּארֵי בְּבֵיתָא — קְשֵׁי לְעַנְיוּתָא. נִשְׁוָרָא בְּבֵיתָא — קְשֵׁי לְעַנְיוּתָא. בְּלֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת וּבְלֵילֵי רְבִיעִית — שָׁרוּ מַזִּיקִין עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If a food is **hanging in one's house,** it **causes poverty.** This is **as people say** in a popular proverb: **He who hangs the basket hangs his sustenance,** i.e., he loses it. **And we said this only** about hanging **bread; however,** if one hangs **meat and fish, we have no** problem **with it.** The reason is that **it is the common practice** to hang meat and fish. **Bran [*parei*] in the house causes poverty.** Bread **crumbs in the house cause poverty.** If these crumbs are spread throughout the house on **Shabbat nights,** i.e., Friday nights, **or on Tuesday nights,** when demons are present, **harmful spirits rest on them.**

The Gemara then turns to a series of behaviors said to lead to poverty. If one suspends objects in the house — food hanging from the ceiling in baskets, for instance — *kashi l'aniyuta*, this is difficult for poverty, meaning it causes poverty. As people say: *tala silta tala mazonei* — if you hang your basket, you have suspended your *mezonot*, your livelihood. This applies specifically to bread; if one hangs meat or fish to dry, *orcheih hu*, that is its normal manner of preservation, and there is no concern.

Similarly, having bran in the house is said to lead to poverty, perhaps because bran is symbolic of the food of the poor. And crumbs of bread left around the house also lead to poverty — reflecting a general rabbinic sensitivity toward the proper treatment of food, particularly bread. The Gemara elsewhere is quite exacting on questions of respect for food, and the disregard signaled by allowing crumbs to accumulate carries weight here as well.

### Demons & Spirits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסָּרָא דִמְזוֹנֵי ״נְקִיד״ שְׁמֵיהּ. אִיסָּרָא דְעַנְיוּתָא ״נָבָל״ שְׁמֵיהּ. צָעָא אַפּוּמָּא דְחַצְבָּא — קְשֵׁי לְעַנְיוּתָא. מַאן דְּשָׁתֵי מַיָּא בְּצָעֵי — קְשֵׁי לִבְרוּקְתִּי. דְּאָכֵיל תַּחְלֵי וְלָא מָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ — מְפַחֵיד תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The administering angel** appointed over **food is called Nakid,** i.e., he is clean [*naki*] and particular about cleanliness. **The administering angel** appointed over **poverty is called Naval.** The angel appointed over food will not stay in a dirty place, while the angel appointed over poverty will thrive there. **A plate** placed **on a jug causes poverty. One who drinks water from a plate causes** eye **pain.** One **who eats cress without washing his hands will be afraid for thirty days.**

The Gemara further notes that on Friday nights and on the nights leading into Wednesday, *mazikin* — demons or harmful spirits — hover over these crumbs and bran. Two spirits are named. The spirit presiding over food is called *Nakid*, a name the Rashbam connects to *naki*, cleanliness, linking it to the theme of treated food with care. The spirit presiding over poverty is called *Navel*, a word connoting degeneracy, which fits here as the spirit associated with the mistreatment and neglect of things.

### Uncleanliness & Poverty

The Gemara continues: *tza afuma d'chatzva*, if there is dirt on the mouth of a barrel or pitcher, *kashi l'aniyuta*, this leads to poverty. This connects to the *Navel* spirit and the theme of general uncleanliness. It is worth noting that while in other contexts the Gemara's concerns about dirt and hygiene may have an implicit connection to illness and what we would today understand as germ theory, here the Gemara is treating this specifically as a cause of poverty, not of sickness.

One might ask how we even know the meanings of the unusual vocabulary appearing in these passages — words that are uncommon even within the broader corpus of *Shas*. Sometimes meaning is recovered from context, sometimes from parallels elsewhere in the Talmud, and sometimes from traditions preserved by the *Geonim*, which are then cited by Rashi or the Rashbam. The *Aruch*, composed around the year 1000, represents a significant lexicographic effort, drawing on traditions that had been maintained within those roughly five centuries since the close of the Talmud. One cannot assume one hundred percent accuracy in every case, but these traditions deserve respect.

### Vision Hazards

One who drinks water from a dish rather than from a proper cup risks *bruktai* — a difficulty with eyesight, something like cataracts.

## Hand-Washing Practices

### Cress & Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּמְסוֹכַר וְלָא מָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ — מְפַחֵיד שִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי. דְּשָׁקֵיל מַזְיֵיהּ וְלָא מָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ — מְפַחֵיד תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי. דְּשָׁקֵיל טוּפְרֵיהּ וְלָא מָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ — מְפַחֵיד חַד יוֹמָא, וְלָא יָדַע מַאי קָא מְפַחֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** One **who lets blood and does not wash his hands will be afraid for seven days.** One **who cuts his hair and does not wash his hands will be afraid for three days.** One **who cuts his nails and does not wash his hands will be afraid for one day, and he will not know what is frightening** him.

Moving to the top of 112a, the Gemara states that one who eats *tachli* — watercress — and does not wash his hands afterwards will suffer *mafchid tlatin yomei*, an unexplained anxiety and fear lasting thirty days.

### Bloodletting Rules

One who undergoes *mesuchar* — bloodletting from the shoulders — and does not wash his hands afterwards will be fearful for seven days. This is clearly a reference to hand-washing after the procedure, and as we will see, it gives rise to a practice that is actually codified.

### Hair & Nail Care

One who gets a haircut — *shakil mazyei* — and does not wash his hands afterwards will be fearful for thirty days. One who has his fingernails removed and does not wash his hands afterwards will be fearful for one day — *v'lo yada mai kamafchid*, and will not even know why he is afraid.

These concerns are cited as the basis for actual practice. As Dr. Elman has demonstrated, anxiety about hair, nails, and demonic vulnerability was a prevalent motif in Zoroastrian and broader Persian culture, the environment in which the Babylonian *Chazal* lived, and this context helps explain why such concerns enter the Talmudic discussion.

### Shulchan Aruch Rules

The Shulchan Aruch, *Orach Chayim*, Siman 4, codifies the requirement of *netilat yadayim* in a broad range of circumstances:

> אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים צְרִיכִים נְטִילָה בְּמַיִם: הַקָּם מֵהַמִּטָּה, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא, וּמִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ, וְהַנּוֹטֵל צִפָּרְנָיו, וְהַחוֹלֵץ מִנְעָלָיו, וְהַנּוֹגֵע בְּרַגְלָיו, וְהַחוֹפֵף רֹאשׁוֹ... מִי שֶׁעָשָׂה אַחַת מִכָּל אֵלּוּ וְלֹא נָטַל, אִם תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הוּא תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִשְׁתַּכַּח, וְאִם אֵינוֹ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם יוֹצֵא מִדַּעְתּוֹ.

The Shulchan Aruch then continues:

> וְהַמַּקִּיז דָּם מֵהַכְּתֵפַיִם וְלֹא נָטַל יָדָיו מְפַחֵד שִׁבְעָה יָמִים. הַמְגַלֵּחַ וְלֹא נָטַל יָדָיו מְפַחֵד ג׳ יָמִים. הַנּוֹטֵל צִפָּרְנָיו וְלֹא נָטַל יָדָיו מְפַחֵד יוֹם אֶחָד וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מִמָּה מְפַחֵד.

This is essentially a direct quotation of the Gemara's language, incorporated into the Shulchan Aruch. Notably, the Rambam does not include this material. This raises a broader and genuinely fascinating question about which elements of Talmudic sugyas dealing with *shedim* and *ruach ra'ah* enter the halachic literature and which do not.

The Shulchan Aruch did not phrase this as *chayav litol yadav* — one is obligated to wash — or as an *issur* not to wash. Nevertheless, he did record the consequence: *mafchid shlosha yamim*, fearful for three days; *mafchid shiva yamim*, fearful for seven days. The question of what makes it into halachic literature is thus in part a question of the author's sensibilities. Did Rav Yosef Karo feel this was important *hadracha* — practical guidance for living — even if not strict *halacha* in the narrow technical sense? And was the fact that he already had a *siman* on *netilat yadayim*, treating the halachic requirements of hand-washing, an occasion to include adjacent material relating to washing hands in other contexts? That too cannot be determined with certainty.

What is striking is how much of what now carries practical weight is a function of certain editorial decisions — by the Rambam and by Rav Yosef Karo — about what belongs within halachic discourse. One sees this even in practice today: people wash their hands after a haircut, but not necessarily after scratching their head, even though the Shulchan Aruch mentions both in the context of *tefillah*. The difference in uptake reflects exactly this process of selective reception.

### Rambam vs. Demons

A great deal of the material about *ruach ra'ah* and *shedim* does not appear in the Rambam. The various Gemaras that speak of the *ruach ra'ah* resting on the hands in the morning, or on food left uncovered, or on water placed near an open barrel — none of this is found in the Rambam. Rav Ovadia Yosef, *zichrono livracha*, addresses this explicitly in the context of whether food that was left under a bed while one slept constitutes a *safek sakana*. He cites the Rambam's implicit rejection of these concerns, invoking the principle of *nishtanu hateva* — nature has changed. Rather than saying the Gemara was mistaken, this approach holds that in the Rambam's era, the relevant natural or spiritual conditions no longer obtained in the same way. This reading was not original to Rav Ovadia; the *Lechem Mishnah* in *Hilchos Netilas Yadayim* makes a similar observation.

The question then becomes: how much does the Rambam's non-codification of this material function as an authoritative *shita* — a legitimate position that can weigh in halachic discussions, even when other authorities have codified the practice? This is a live question, particularly around *negel vasser* and the various *ruach ra'ah* concerns associated with morning hand-washing, food left under the bed, and so on. The Gemara we are studying concerns food left under the bed that becomes unfit for consumption because *ruach ra'ah shoreh aleihen* — an evil spirit rests upon it — even if it is covered with an iron vessel. The Gemara references *Tur* and *Shulchan Aruch*, *Yoreh Deah*, and points toward Rambam, *Perek* 12 of *Hilchos Rotzeach V'Shemiras Nefesh* — and, as one might expect, it is not there.

## Night Dangers

### No Water Nights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם לֹא בְּלֵילֵי רְבִיעִיּוֹת וְלֹא בְּלֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת, וְאִם שָׁתָה — דָּמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ מִפְּנֵי סַכָּנָה. מַאי סַכָּנָה? רוּחַ רָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A person** should **not drink water on Tuesday nights or on Shabbat nights,** i.e., Friday nights. **And if he drinks** water, **his blood is upon his** own **head, due to** the **danger.** The Gemara asks: **What is** this **danger?** The Gemara answers: The danger of the **evil spirit** that rules on these days.

The Gemara teaches: *lo yishte adam mayim lo beleilei revi'iyot velo beleilei shabbatot* — a person should not drink water on Tuesday nights or Friday nights. This is an immediately striking statement; not drinking water at all on those nights would be a serious hardship in a world without alternative beverages readily available. The Rashbam immediately qualifies the prohibition, explaining it applies only when there is no light — drinking in the dark on those particular nights is forbidden. This qualification is the Rashbam's insertion to render the ruling intelligible, since the Gemara's text itself does not state this condition. *Damo berosho*, his blood is on his own hands, if he drinks. *Mipnei hasakana* — because of danger. *Mai sakana?* — *Ruach Ra'ah*, an evil spirit.

### Food Under Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין תַּחַת הַמִּטָּה, אֲפִילּוּ מְחוּפִּין בִּכְלִי בַּרְזֶל — רוּחַ רָעָה שׁוֹרָה עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught:** If **food and drink** are **under** one's **bed, even if they are covered with iron vessels, an evil spirit rests upon them.**

A *baraita* teaches that food and drink placed under a bed — even if covered with an iron vessel — have *ruach ra'ah shoreh aleihen*, an evil spirit resting upon them, and may not be consumed.

### Protective Rituals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם צָחֵי מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? (נֵימָא) שִׁבְעָה קוֹלוֹת שֶׁאָמַר דָּוִד עַל הַמַּיִם, וַהֲדַר נִישְׁתֵּי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קוֹל ה׳ עַל הַמָּיִם אֵל הַכָּבוֹד הִרְעִים ה׳ עַל מַיִם רַבִּים קוֹל ה׳ בַּכֹּחַ קוֹל ה׳ בֶּהָדָר קוֹל ה׳ שׁוֹבֵר אֲרָזִים וַיְשַׁבֵּר ה׳ אֶת אַרְזֵי הַלְּבָנוֹן קוֹל ה׳ חוֹצֵב לַהֲבוֹת אֵשׁ קוֹל ה׳ יָחִיל מִדְבָּר יָחִיל ה׳ מִדְבַּר קָדֵשׁ קוֹל ה׳ יְחוֹלֵל אַיָּלוֹת וַיֶּחֱשׂוֹף יְעָרוֹת וּבְהֵיכָלוֹ כּוּלּוֹ אוֹמֵר כָּבוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if he is thirsty, what is his remedy?** What should he drink? The Gemara answers: **He** should **say** the **seven voices that David said over the water, and afterward he** may **drink. As it is stated: "The voice of the Lord is upon the waters; God of glory thunders, even the Lord upon many waters. The voice of the Lord is powerful; the voice of the Lord is full of majesty. The voice of the Lord breaks the cedars; the Lord breaks in pieces the cedars of Lebanon.** He makes them also skip like a calf; Lebanon and Sirion like a young wild ox. **The voice of the Lord hews out flames of fire. The voice of the Lord shakes the wilderness; the Lord shakes the wilderness of Kadesh. The voice of the Lord makes the hinds to calve, and strips the forests bare; and in His temple all say: Glory"** (Psalms 29:3–9).

The Gemara asks: *v'ei tzechi mai takanteih* — if one is thirsty on one of these nights, what is the remedy? One option is to recite the seven *kolot* that David uttered regarding water — the passage from Psalms 29 beginning *kol Hashem al hamayim*, in which the phrase *kol Hashem* appears seven times — before drinking.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא, (נֵימָא) הָכִי: ״לוּל שָׁפָן אֲנִיגְרוֹן אֲנִירָדְפִין בֵּין כּוֹכְבֵי יָתֵיבְנָא, בֵּין בְּלִיעִי שַׁמִּינֵי אָזֵילְנָא״. וְאִי לָא, אִי אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ בַּהֲדֵיהּ — נַיתְעֲרֵיהּ וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״פְּלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנִתָא צָחֵינָא מַיָּא״, וַהֲדַר נִישְׁתֵּי. וְאִי לָא — מְקַרְקֵשׁ נִכְתְּמָא אַחַצְבָּא וַהֲדַר נִישְׁתֵּי. וְאִי לָא — נִישְׁדֵּי בַּהּ מִידֵּי, וַהֲדַר נִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** he does **not** remember that verse, **he** should **say as follows: Lul, Shafan, Anigron, Anirdafin,** which are names of demons, **I sit between the stars, I walk between thin** and **fat** people, take any of them if you wish but leave me alone. **And if** he does **not** recall this incantation, **if there is** another **person with him, he** should **wake him and say to him: So-and-so, son of so-and-so, I thirst for water; and then** he may **drink. And if** there is **no** other person with him, he should **knock the cover on the cup and then drink. And if** he is **not** able to do this, **he** should **throw some** object **in it and then drink.**

If one does not know that passage by heart, an alternative incantation is provided: *lu shafan anigron anirdafin* — terms the Rashbam does not even attempt to translate, apparently some form of incantation, perhaps the names of *shedim*. The speaker then declares: *bein kochvei yativna* — I sit among the stars — *bein balya lishmina azilna* — I go between the thin and the fat. The Rashbam explains this as a way of saying to the *shedim*: there are many people around you, thin ones and fat ones — leave me alone.

If neither option is available, one may wake a companion and say: *Planya bar Planta* — so-and-so son of so-and-so (notably using the mother's name as the second identifier) — *tzachina maya*, I am thirsty for water. Once one has declared this to another person, one may then drink. Alternatively, one may bang on the lid of the pitcher to frighten away whatever is lurking, or throw something into the water before drinking. The Gemara seems to present these in descending order of efficacy, though it does not say so explicitly.

### Rivers & Pools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם לֹא מִן הַנְּהָרוֹת וְלֹא מִן הָאֲגַמִּים בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאִם שָׁתָה — דָּמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה. מַאי סַכָּנָה? סַכָּנַת שַׁבְרִירֵי. וְאִי צַחֵי מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? אִי אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ בַּהֲדֵיהּ — לֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״פְּלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנִתָא, צָחֵינָא מַיָּא״. וְאִי לָא, (נֵימָא) אִיהוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ: ״פְּלָנְיָא, אֲמַרָה לִי אִימִּי אִיזְדְּהַר מִשַּׁבְרִירֵי שַׁבְרִירֵי בְּרִירֵי רִירֵי יְרֵי רֵי — צָחֵינָא מַיָּא בְּכָסֵי חִיוָּרֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A person** should **not drink water from rivers or from ponds at night. And if he drank, his blood is upon his** own **head due to the danger.** The Gemara explains: **What is** this **danger? The danger of blindness.** The Gemara asks: **And if he is thirsty, what is his remedy? If there is** another **person with him, he** should **say to him: So-and-so, son of so-and-so, I thirst for water. And if** there is **no** one else with him, **he** should **say to himself: So-and-so, my mother said to me** to **beware of *shavrirei*,** the demon of blindness. He should continue to say the following incantation, in the first part of which the demon's name gradually disappears: ***Shavrirei berirei rirei yiri ri*; I thirst for water in white** earthenware **cups.** This is an incantation against those demons.

A second *baraita* states: *lo yishte adam mayim lo min haneharot velo min ha'agamim balaila* — do not drink water from rivers or pools at night. *Mipnei hasakana* — because of danger. *Mai sakana?* — *Shavriri*, the spirit of blindness, which both causes blindness and refers to a particular spirit by that name.

If one is thirsty and in the field with no other water available, and there is a companion present, one says: *Planya bar Planta, amar li immi* — so-and-so son of so-and-so, my mother told me — *hizdaher mi-Shavriri* — beware of Shavriri. One then recites a diminishing incantation: *Shavriri, Briri, Riri, Yiri* — each iteration dropping a letter from the name, as if to make the spirit progressively disappear. One then declares *tzachina maya bekasi chivri* — I am thirsty for water in white vessels — and may then drink.

## Four Cups Obligation

### Tamchoi & Poor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ מִן הַתַּמְחוּי וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the statement of the mishna that on Passover one must drink no less than four cups of wine: **And** this *halakha* applies **even** if the poor person accepts funds **from the charity plate.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case. If there is a mitzva to drink these four cups, they must be provided for him.

The Gemara returns now to the *Mishnah*, specifically the ruling that even the poorest person — even one supported by the *tamchoi*, the communal soup kitchen that distributed food on a daily basis to those who could not afford two meals of their own — must nonetheless have *arba kosot* at the Seder. The *tamchoi* was primarily a food-based institution, not a monetary one, and its recipients were those most deeply without means. The Gemara's reading of the *Mishnah* is that even such a person must find a way to obtain four cups of wine regardless.

The Gemara asks: *pshita* — is this not obvious? After all, this is a rabbinic *mitzvah*, and even for biblical *mitzvot* there is a limit to how much a person is obligated to spend. For a positive biblical *mitzvah*, one need not spend more than a third or a fifth of one's assets. Why then is it obvious that even the destitute person must obtain the four cups?

### Akiva's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: עֲשֵׂה שַׁבַּתְּךָ חוֹל וְאַל תִּצְטָרֵךְ לַבְּרִיּוֹת — הָכָא מִשּׁוּם פַּרְסוֹמֵי נִיסָּא (מוֹדֵי).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is necessary only** to teach that this *halakha* applies **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said: Make your Shabbat** like an ordinary **weekday and do not be beholden to** other **beings.** If one is unable to honor Shabbat without financial help from others, it is better for him to save money and eat his Shabbat meals as he would on a weekday rather than rely on other people. **Here,** in the case of the four cups, Rabbi Akiva **concedes** that it is appropriate for a poor person to request assistance from the community, **due to** the obligation **to publicize the miracle.**

The Gemara answers: *lo nitzrecha ela afilu le-Rabbi Akiva* — this ruling is needed specifically to address Rabbi Akiva's position.

Rabbi Akiva taught: *aseh Shabbatecha chol ve-al titztarech la-beriyot* — better that your Shabbos should be like a weekday than that you become dependent on others. The principle reflects a profound concern for financial independence and human dignity. The state of dependency, once entered, can be self-perpetuating; it can affect a person's self-image in ways that are lasting and damaging. Better to have no special food for Shabbos than to be forced into a position of taking loans and relying on others.

One might think that Rabbi Akiva would apply this same logic to *arba kosot*: if obtaining the four cups requires borrowing money or becoming dependent, one should forgo them. The *Mishnah*'s ruling comes to tell us that even according to Rabbi Akiva, the four cups must be obtained — even if it means taking loans. This is striking, and raises the question of what makes *arba kosot* different. The Rambam applies the principle of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle — to explain this. The *arba kosot* are a fundamental vehicle for *pirsumei nisa*, and that mandate demands universal, unconditional participation. This is the same logic that underlies the inclusion of women in the obligation of the four cups — *af hen hayu be-oto ha-nes* — and as the Rav Soloveitchik points out, *pirsumei nisa* is the context in which such inclusive, near-absolute obligations are especially characteristic.

It is worth pausing to appreciate the tension here. On the one hand, we are told that *pirsumei nisa* breaks all normal limits and insists on complete universality. On the other hand, the Gemara earlier discussed who reclines at the Seder — and noted that a woman in the presence of her husband, or a student before his *rav*, may not recline. So the universality is not quite total. Nevertheless, the *arba kosot* represent a point at which the community's obligation to ensure participation is paramount — indeed, the Mishnah's first line establishes that the community must provide.

## Four Cups Obligation (II)

### D'vei Eliyahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: עֲשֵׂה שַׁבַּתְּךָ חוֹל וְאַל תִּצְטָרֵךְ לַבְּרִיּוֹת, אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה הוּא דָּבָר מוּעָט בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. מַאי נִינְהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כָּסָא דְהַרְסָנָא. כְּדִתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא אוֹמֵר: הֱוֵי עַז כַּנָּמֵר וְקַל כַּנֶּשֶׁר, רָץ כַּצְּבִי וְגִבּוֹר כָּאֲרִי לַעֲשׂוֹת רְצוֹן אָבִיךָ שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **the school of Eliyahu taught** that **although Rabbi Akiva said: Make your Shabbat** like **a weekday and do not be beholden to** other **beings; however, one** should nevertheless **perform some small** alteration **in his house** to distinguish Shabbat from a weekday. The Gemara asks: **What** is **this** alteration? **Rav Pappa said:** For example, one should serve **small, fried fish. As we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima says: Be bold like a leopard, light like an eagle, run like a deer, and** be **strong like a lion to perform the will of your Father in Heaven.** This statement teaches that one should exert every effort to perform a mitzva.

*Tana D'vei Eliyahu* teaches: even though Rabbi Akiva says one should make one's Shabbos like a weekday rather than become dependent, he would nonetheless agree that one must make *something* a little different — some small gesture to mark the day. The Gemara cannot fully come to peace with the idea of *aseh Shabbatecha chol* in its totality. *Mai nihu* — what would this small gesture be? Rav Pappa says: *kasa d'harsena* — a small dish of fish hash. Not a large expenditure; perhaps just a few extra pennies. The point is that one scrapes together what one can and does something, however small, to make Shabbos distinct.

The Shulchan Aruch, in the very first *siman* of *Hilchos Shabbos*, Siman 242, codifies this tension carefully: one who is already dependent on others for *tzedakah* or loans must still make every effort to honor Shabbos with what little he has of his own. The statement *aseh Shabbatecha chol* applies only to one who is in extremely straitened circumstances. Even then, the prescription is to be economical during the week in order to set something aside for Shabbos. The *Mishnah Berurah* attempts to harmonize the various statements in the Gemara and the competing values at stake; the Shulchan Aruch's bottom line seems to be that if the real choice is between doing nothing special and taking out loans, one should do nothing — but if one can scrape a few pennies together, one must do so.

## Akiva's 7 Teachings

### Commands 1-3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים צִוָּה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּנוֹ: בְּנִי, אַל תֵּשֵׁב בְּגוֹבְהָהּ שֶׁל עִיר וְתִשְׁנֶה, וְאַל תָּדוּר בְּעִיר שֶׁרָאשֶׁיהָ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the full source of Rabbi's Akiva statement with regard to Shabbat preparations. **The Sages taught: Rabbi Akiva commanded Rabbi Yehoshua, his son,** about **seven matters: My son, do not sit at the high point of a city,** where many people pass, **and study** there, as the passersby will interrupt you. **And do not live in a city whose leaders are Torah scholars,** as they are too busy studying to govern properly.

The Gemara now presents a *baraita*: *shivah devarim tzivah Rabbi Akiva et Rabbi Yehoshua beno* — Rabbi Akiva commanded seven things to his son Rabbi Yehoshua. Interestingly, we rarely hear of Rabbi Akiva's children. Rashi suggests that this Rabbi Yehoshua may be Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, a *Tanna* mentioned frequently elsewhere, on the basis of a Gemara that refers to Rabbi Akiva by the epithet *kereach*, bald — but Tosafos strongly disagrees. The phenomenon of the children of great men living in relative obscurity, always in the shadow of a towering parent, is poignant here.

The first teaching: *beni al teshev begovhah shel ir vesishaneh* — do not sit at the highest point of the city and try to learn there. The gathering place at the city heights will distract from study. The second: *ve-al tadur be-ir sherhosheha talmidei chachamim* — do not live in a city whose leaders are Torah scholars. The Rashbam explains: *detaridi begirsa velo taridi bemilei ditzibura* — they are too busy with their learning and not sufficiently occupied with communal needs. The third: *ve-al tikanes leveisecha pit'om, kol shekein leveit chaveircha* — do not enter your own house suddenly, and certainly not your friend's house. Even with respect to one's own household, one must respect the privacy of other members; both Rashi and the Rashbam make this point explicitly. The teaching applies to all members of the household, not merely in situations where one suspects wrongdoing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל תִּכָּנֵס לְבֵיתְךָ פִּתְאוֹם, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן לְבֵית חֲבֵירְךָ. וְאַל תִּמְנַע מִנְעָלִים מֵרַגְלֶיךָ. הַשְׁכֵּם וֶאֱכוֹל, בַּקַּיִץ מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה, וּבַחוֹרֶף מִפְּנֵי הַצִּינָּה. וַעֲשֵׂה שַׁבַּתְּךָ חוֹל וְאַל תִּצְטָרֵךְ לַבְּרִיּוֹת. וֶהֱוֵי מִשְׁתַּדֵּל עִם אָדָם שֶׁהַשָּׁעָה מְשַׂחֶקֶת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva continued: **And do not enter your house suddenly,** without knocking first; **all the more so** do not enter **the house of another,** as he might not be ready to receive you. **And do not withhold shoes from your feet,** as it is disgraceful to go barefoot. **Wake up and eat, in the summer due to the heat,** as it is best to eat before it grows hot, **and in the winter due to** the strength you will need to tolerate **the cold. And make your Shabbat** like **a weekday and do not be beholden to** other **beings. And exert yourself** to join together **with a person upon whom the hour smiles,** i.e., a successful person.

### Shoes & Breakfast

The fourth teaching: *ve-al timna min'alim meraglecha* — never go without shoes on your feet. The Gemara considers the wearing of shoes important, with implications for dignity and self-presentation that go beyond mere physical protection. The fifth: *hashkeim ve-echol bakayitz mipnei hachamah uvachoref mipnei hatzinah* — rise early and eat in the summer because of the sun and in the winter because of the cold. Whatever the precise logic of "because of the sun" or "because of the cold," the basic message is to have an early breakfast — the most important meal of the day.

## Akiva's 7 Teachings (II)

### Shabbos vs. Dependence

The sixth teaching brings us back to the principle already discussed: *va-aseh Shabbatecha chol ve-al titzatarech labriyos* — make your Shabbos like a weekday rather than become dependent on others. This is precisely why the Gemara cited this principle in the context of *arba kosot* — it is part of Rabbi Akiva's teachings to his son.

### Fortune & Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא לְמִיזְבַּן מִינֵּיהּ וְלָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא לְמֶעְבַּד שׁוּתָּפוּת בַּהֲדֵיהּ. וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״מַעֲשֵׂה יָדָיו בֵּרַכְתָּ״ — כׇּל הַנּוֹטֵל פְּרוּטָה מֵאִיּוֹב מִתְבָּרֵךְ, אֲפִילּוּ לְמִיזְבַּן מִינֵּיהּ וּלְזַבּוֹנֵי לֵיהּ שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** in explanation of this last statement: **Do not buy from him and do not sell to him.** If he is the beneficiary of good fortune, he will profit from any business transaction and you will suffer from it. **Rather, form a partnership with him. And now** we have heard **that Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "You have blessed the work of his hands"** (Job 1:10)? This means that **anyone who took a *peruta* from Job would be blessed,** even if he received it via a business transaction. This shows that one should engage in business with a person who is blessed, for **even if** he wishes **to buy from him or to sell to him it is well,** i.e., he will share in the good fortune of the other.

The seventh teaching: *vehevei mishtadel im adam shehasha'ah mesacheket lo* — make efforts to associate with someone who is enjoying great fortune, so that perhaps some of it will rub off on you. This seems, on the surface, somewhat less pragmatic than the preceding advice — more in the realm of luck and mystical contagion than practical wisdom.

The Gemara, however, offers two readings. Rav Pappa says: this is not about buying from him or selling to him — that alone accomplishes little. Rather: *ela lemiavad shutafus bahadei* — try to go into business with him. This is very practical advice: if someone is having remarkable success, they may well know what they are doing, and becoming a partner in their venture is sound strategy.

But then Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak adds a deeper dimension by citing the verse from Iyov: *ma-aseh yadav berachta* — you have blessed the work of his hands. The principle derived is: *kol hanotel perutah mei-Iyov misbarech* — whoever received even a single *perutah* from Iyov was blessed. Anything that passed through his hands transferred blessing. On this reading, even buying from him or selling to him — any commercial contact that results in receiving something from the fortunate person — can transfer blessing. The advice thus has both a sober, pragmatic dimension and a more mystical, supernatural one. One may note the irony that Iyov ultimately lost everything — but the Gemara brackets that, since Iyov's reversal was caused by the *Satan*, not by any failure of his own fortune.

## Akiva to Shimon

### Great Tree Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים צִוָּה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי כְּשֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, לַמְּדֵנִי תּוֹרָה, אָמַר: אֵינִי מְלַמְּדֶךָ. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם אֵין אַתָּה מְלַמְּדֵנִי אֲנִי אוֹמֵר לְיוֹחַי אַבָּא וּמוֹסֶרְךָ לַמַּלְכוּת. אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁהָעֵגֶל רוֹצֶה לִינַק פָּרָה רוֹצֶה לְהָנִיק. אָמַר לוֹ: וּמִי בְּסַכָּנָה? וַהֲלֹא עֵגֶל בְּסַכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to cite similar advice dispensed by Rabbi Akiva. **Rabbi Akiva commanded Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai** to do **five matters when** Rabbi Akiva **was imprisoned.** Beforehand, Rabbi Shimon **said to him: Rabbi, teach me Torah.** Rabbi Akiva **said** to him: **I will not teach you,** as it is dangerous to do so at the present time. Rabbi Shimon **said to him** in jest: **If you will not teach me, I will tell Yoḥai** my **father, and he will turn you over to the government.** In other words, I have no means of persuading you; you are already in prison. Rabbi Akiva **said: My son,** know that **more than the calf wishes to suck, the cow wants to suckle,** but I am afraid of the danger. Rabbi Shimon **said to him: And who is in danger? Isn't the calf in danger,** as you are in jail and I am the one at risk?

The Gemara now shifts to five things Rabbi Akiva commanded to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — *keshehuyu chavush beveit ha-asurim*, when Rabbi Akiva was imprisoned by the Romans for teaching Torah.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai came to him and said: *Rebbi, lamdeni Torah* — Rebbi, teach me Torah. Rabbi Akiva refused, saying *eini melamdecha* — I will not teach you, I have already gotten myself into enough trouble. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai responded with a remarkable threat: if you do not teach me, I will tell my father Yochai — we almost never hear of Yochai himself, only of *bar Yochai* — and he will hand you over to the government. The audacity of this threat against a teacher already in Roman custody is startling. The Maharsha notes that Rabbi Akiva was already in prison, so the threat to make things worse for him carries its own irony. Further, what could Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai possibly report to the Romans? That Rabbi Akiva was teaching Torah — which is precisely why he was imprisoned. The very means of the threat thus defeats itself.

Rabbi Akiva responded: *yoter mi-mah sheha-egel rotzeh linok, haparah rotzah lehanik* — more than the calf wants to suckle, the cow wants to provide milk. He was not refusing out of lack of desire to teach; he was merely trying to be cautious. And Rabbi Shimon pressed the metaphor further: *u-mi al hasakana?* — and in your parable, who is in danger if it does not happen? *Vehalo egel hasakana* — it is the calf who is in danger, the one who will perish without the milk. The allusion is clear: Torah study for Rabbi Shimon was as vital as water for a fish — and this argument is precisely what Rabbi Akiva had used elsewhere to justify his own Torah teaching in the face of Roman prohibition. Rabbi Akiva conceded, and what follows is less formal Torah teaching than ethical instruction — perhaps a deliberate choice to avoid further legal jeopardy.

### Checked Books

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אִם בִּקַּשְׁתָּ לֵיחָנֵק — הִיתָּלֵה בְּאִילָן גָּדוֹל. וּכְשֶׁאַתָּה מְלַמֵּד אֶת בִּנְךָ — לַמְּדֵהוּ בְּסֵפֶר מוּגָּהּ. מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: בְּחַדְתָּא, שַׁבֶּשְׁתָּא כֵּיוָן דְּעָל — עָל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** If so, I will tell you a few matters. First of all, **if you wish to strangle yourself, hang yourself on a tall tree.** This proverb means that if one wants others to accept what he has to say, he should attribute his statement to a great man. **And when you teach your son, teach him from a corrected text.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **that** statement? **Rava said, and some say Rav Mesharshiya** said: Rabbi Akiva was referring **to** learning **a new** topic, for **once a mistake enters** one's mind, it has **entered** there and is difficult to put right.

The first teaching: *im bikashta leichanek, hitaleh be-ilan gadol* — if you want to go strangle yourself, hang from a big tree. The graphic image means: if you wish to take a bold or radical halachic position, find a great authority to support yourself from. This expression is found throughout Rabbinic literature; when an author is *mehadesh* something novel in a *teshuvah*, he will write *u-matzati ilan gadol litlot alav* — I found a great tree to support myself with — and cite the authority behind him.

The second teaching: *u-keshehatah melamed et bincha, lamdehu besefer muga* — when you teach your son, teach him from a corrected book. In an era when all texts were copied by hand, scribal error was a constant hazard. *Shabeshta kivan de-al al* — once a mistake enters a text, it stays. The Gemara asks: what does *muga* mean in this context? Rava (or, in another version, Rav Mesharshiya) says: *bechadta* — in a new book. The Rashbam adds that this may also mean the first time one is learning a new topic, when one is especially susceptible to absorbing errors uncritically.

Tosafos raises a striking objection: Rava himself holds that mistakes eventually correct themselves — *shabeshta mimmela nafka*, errors come out on their own. Is this not a contradiction? Tosafos resolves it: when a student is absorbing large quantities of material rapidly without yet being able to analyze it carefully, errors may self-correct once the student matures and begins to analyze more precisely. But when learning from a book, one tends to defer to the authority of the written text and never revisit one's initial understanding. The book becomes the authority, and the error is frozen in place. This is a genuinely insightful piece of educational psychology: the authority we assign to written texts makes their errors far more durable than errors absorbed in other ways.

The Maharsha adds his own note on the phrase *sefer she-eino muga* — he understands *chadta* as meaning a book that has simply never been proofread by anyone at all, making the primary concern the importance of at least one pass of editorial correction. This is a simpler reading than the "first time learning" interpretation. One might also ask: what kinds of books were being discussed in this period? Given that almost nothing was written down — other than *sifrei Torah* — the *sefer* in question may well be a *Sefer Torah*, which is interesting in its own right, since we tend to think of the *Sefer Torah* primarily in ritual terms — *kriat ha-Torah* — and not as a learning text. Yet in the Talmudic period, the *Sefer Torah* was evidently also the medium from which one began formal Torah study.

### Used Pot Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא תְּבַשֵּׁל בִּקְדֵירָה שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל בָּהּ חֲבֵירְךָ. מַאי נִיהוּ — גְּרוּשָׁה בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, דְּאָמַר מָר גָּרוּשׁ שֶׁנָּשָׂא גְּרוּשָׁהּ — אַרְבַּע דֵּעוֹת בַּמִּטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva further told Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: **Do not cook in a pot in which your colleague cooked** his food. The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **this** statement? The Gemara explains: Rabbi Akiva is referring to marrying **a divorced woman in the lifetime of her** former **husband. As the Master said:** If **a divorced man marries a divorced woman, there are four minds in the bed** during intimacy. Each person thinks about his current and former spouse, which verges on illegitimacy.

The third teaching: *lo tevashel be-kederah she-bishel bah chavercha* — do not cook in the pot your friend already used. *Mai nihu?* — what does this mean? *Grushah be-chayei ba-alah* — do not marry a divorced woman while her husband is still alive. The image of the "used pot" is, admittedly, an objectifying metaphor for women; it treats the divorced woman as a vessel that retains residue of its previous use.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַלְמָנָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כׇּל אֶצְבָּעוֹת שָׁווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that this advice holds true **even with regard to** marrying **a widow, as** not **all fingers are equal.** It is possible that intimate relations with her second husband might not be as pleasing as with the first, leading her to disparage and even hate him.

The Gemara itself, however, significantly tempers this reading. *De-amar mar garush shenasa grushah arba de-ot ba-mitah* — if a divorced man marries a divorced woman, there are four minds in the bed: both parties are preoccupied with their former spouses. The concern is thus not asymmetrical — it applies equally to divorced men. The Gemara then asks whether the concern extends even to widows, who might be presumed less likely to actively think about their deceased husbands. The answer: *lefi she-ein kol etzba-ot shavot* — all fingers are not equal, a euphemism meaning that she will inevitably compare her new husband to the one she lost. This is at least a partial reversal of the objectification: here it is the man who is the object of comparison, not the woman. That said, one cannot ignore the practical consequence: divorced and widowed women are rendered even harder to remarry in a society that already diminishes their prospects, and one must acknowledge that reality honestly.

### Mitzvah & Profit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה וְגוּף גָּדוֹל — אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת וְלֹא שָׂכָר. מִצְוָה וְגוּף טָהוֹר — נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה וְלוֹ בָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva continued to offer instruction: It is **a mitzva and a great** material benefit to one's **body** to **eat fruits without payment.** That is, when one lends money and takes land as collateral, deducting from the loan the value of the fruit he eats, both the borrower and the lender benefit from this practice. One who both performs **a mitzva and** retains a **pure body** is one who **marries a woman,** as his thoughts will remain pure **and he will** merit to **have children.**

The fourth teaching: *mitzvah beguf gadol ochel peirot v'lo sachar* — a *mitzvah* with a large body: one eats the fruit and not for a fee. The Rashbam understands this as referring to lending money in a manner that is halachically permissible while still yielding profit — specifically, taking land as collateral and eating the fruit of that land while the loan remains outstanding, in a way that avoids the biblical prohibition of *neshech*. The virtue is in doing well while doing good: performing the *mitzvah* of lending to one who needs funds, and simultaneously making a legitimate profit.

### Pure Body Marriage

The fifth teaching: *mitzvah beguf tahor, nosei ishah v-lo banim* — a *mitzvah* with a pure body: marry a woman even if you already have children. This might seem paradoxical — what does marriage have to do with bodily purity in the ascetic sense? The Gemara's implicit reasoning appears to be the concern that a man who has already fulfilled *peru urvu* and remains unmarried will be chronically preoccupied with illicit sexual temptation. Marriage — even without the imperative of procreation — keeps one's *guf tahor*, one's bodily conduct in a sanctioned framework. The word *tahor* here thus refers not to ritual purity but to moral-behavioral propriety.

## HaKadosh's Commands

### Avoid Mockers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים צִוָּה רַבֵּינוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ אֶת בָּנָיו: אַל תָּדוּר בְּשַׁכְנְצִיב מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיצָנֵי הָווּ, וּמָשְׁכוּ לָךְ בְּלֵיצָנוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites more instructions issued by a Sage to his heirs. **Our holy rabbi,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **commanded his sons** to do **four matters: Do not live in Shekhantziv** in Babylonia, **because they are mockers. And they will draw you in to their mockery** and lead you to abandon your Torah studies.

The Gemara now presents four things that Rabbeinu HaKadosh commanded his sons. The first: *al tadur b-shichnat tiv* — do not dwell in the place called *Shichnat Tiv*. Why? *Mishum d-leitzanei havu* — because they are mockers and skeptics, and *u-mashchu lach b-latzanusa* — they will draw you along into their culture of mockery and cynicism. This attitude is deeply corrosive to a life of Torah and to seriousness of purpose.

### Aramaic Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל תֵּשֵׁב עַל מִטַּת אֲרַמִּית. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דְּלָא תִּיגְנֵי בְּלָא קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע. וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דְּלָא תִּינְסַב גִּיּוֹרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi further commanded his sons: **Do not sit on the bed of an Aramean woman.** This advice is explained in different ways. **Some say** it means **that you** should **not go to sleep without reciting *Shema*,** as a Jew who does this acts like a gentile. **And some say** it means **that you** should **not marry a convert,** i.e., a Jewish woman who was once an Aramean.

The second command: *v-al teshiv al mitah arammit* — do not sit on an Aramaic bed. Several readings are offered. One interpretation: *lo tigni b-lo kriyas shema* — do not go to sleep in the manner of a non-Jew, that is, without reciting *Shema*; one's sleep should begin with *tefillah* and *kriyas shema al ha-mitah*. The word *aramai* is used here in its generic sense of "non-Jew." Another reading: *lo tinsa giyurta* — do not marry a female convert, i.e., one whose bed was previously an Aramaic bed. A third reading takes the phrase literally: do not sit on a bed belonging to a non-Jewish woman — this stems from the story of Rav Pappa, who came to collect a debt from a non-Jewish woman; she allegedly killed her young child, placed the body in the bed, and when Rav Pappa rose from having sat there, accused him of the child's death in order to avoid repaying her debt. The concern, on this reading, is about practical danger and vulnerability to false accusation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אֲרַמָּאִית מַמָּשׁ, וּמִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה דְרַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi meant the **actual** bed of **an Aramean woman, and** this is **due to** the concern for a similar outcome to the later **incident involving Rav Pappa.** The incident in question was as follows: Rav Pappa entered the house of a gentile woman to collect a debt. The woman asked him to sit on her bed until she brought the money. As it turned out, she had placed her dead baby under the bed. Rav Pappa was subsequently accused of killing the baby and was forced to flee the district.

### Pay Taxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶל תַּבְרִיחַ עַצְמְךָ מִן הַמֶּכֶס, דִּילְמָא מַשְׁכְּחוּ לָךְ וְשָׁקְלִי מִנָּךְ כׇּל דְּאִית לָךְ. וְאַל תַּעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי הַשּׁוֹר בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹלֶה מִן הָאֲגַם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשָּׂטָן מְרַקֵּד בֵּין קַרְנָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר, וּבְיוֹמֵי נִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi continued: **And do not avoid paying taxes, lest they find you and confiscate everything you own. And do not stand before an ox when it emerges from the marsh because Satan dances between its horns,** i.e., an ox is particularly menacing at that time. **Rabbi Shmuel said:** This is referring **to a black ox, and** specifically **during the days of Nisan,** when the ox is most dangerous.

The third command: *velo tavriach atzmecha min ha-machas* — do not try to escape your taxes. The *machas* was a specific form of tax — perhaps an import or transit duty — levied in the context of Roman governance. It is worth noting that certain *mishnayot* treat evading tax collectors without censure, and the *Yerushalmi* does not reverse this. But the *Bavli* introduces *dina d-malchuta dina* — the law of the kingdom is law — and with it, a fundamentally different attitude toward civic financial obligations. Rabbeinu HaKadosh, as the political head of the Jewish community and dependent on Roman goodwill, had strong reasons of his own for issuing this instruction. But the Gemara grounds it in entirely pragmatic terms: *dilma mishk-chu lach* — perhaps they will catch you — *veshakli minach kol de-it lach* — and take everything you have. As history has repeatedly demonstrated, tax evasion tends to be expensive when discovered.

## HaKadosh's Commands (II)

### Ox Danger

The fourth command: *ve-al ta-amod bifnei ha-shor she-oleh min ha-agam* — do not stand before an ox emerging from a swamp. *De-it beih satan mirakeid bein karnav* — the Satan dances between its horns. This is clearly a metaphor: the ox emerging from a swamp is agitated, unpredictable, and looking for trouble. Do not be the trouble it finds.

## Animal & Health Hazards

### Black Oxes Spring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַרְחִיקִין מִשּׁוֹר תָּם — חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה, מִשּׁוֹר מוּעָד — כִּמְלֹא עֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya teaches** with regard to the same issue: **One distances himself fifty cubits from an innocuous ox [*shor tam*],** an ox with no consistent history of causing damage with the intent to injure. **From a forewarned ox [*shor muad*],** an ox whose owner was forewarned because his ox already gored a person three times, one distances himself until it is beyond **eyeshot.**

Rabbi Shmuel qualifies the preceding warning: *beshor shachor* — this applies specifically to black oxen, which are particularly aggressive — *uveyomei Nissan* — and in the spring, when the natural world is in bloom and the animal's hormones are at their height.

Rav Oshaya taught: one keeps a distance of fifty *amot* — approximately seventy-five feet — from an ox that has never gored before, a *shor tam*. From an ox that has gored previously, a *shor mu-ad*, one should not even be within its line of sight. And in the name of Rabbi Meir: even if the head of the ox is buried in a basket eating its grain, *sak le-igra* — climb to the roof — *veshadi darga mitotach* — and kick the ladder away from beneath you. No ox is going to climb a ladder; the hyperbole is the point. Oxen are dangerous; maintain maximum distance.

### Animal Sounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: רֵישׁ תּוֹרָא בְּדִיקּוּלָא — סַק לְאִיגָּרָא וּשְׁדִי דַּרְגָּא מִתּוּתָךְ. אָמַר רַב: נִיזְהָא דְתוֹרָא — ״הֵן הֵן״. נִיזְהָא דְאַרְיָה — ״זֶה זֶה״. נִיזְהָא דְגַמְלָא — ״דָּא דָּא״. נִיזְהָא דְאַרְבָּא — ״הֵילֵנִי הַיָּיא הִילָא וְהִילּוּק הוּלְיָא״.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught citing the name** of **Rabbi Meir,** in an exaggerated vein: Even if **the head of the ox** is in its food **basket, go up to the roof and kick the ladder out from underneath you** to escape from it. **Rav said: The cry** that one says **to** lead **an ox** is ***hen hen*. The cry to** lead **a lion** is ***zeh zeh*. The cry to** lead **a camel** is ***da da*. The cry to** laborers using ropes to pull **a ship** along a river is ***heleni*, *hayya*, *hela*, *vehilook*, *hulya*.**

Rav teaches the proper vocalizations for managing various animals and situations. The call for oxen is *hein hein*. The call for a lion — though why one would need to call a lion is not obvious — is *ze ze*. The sound for moving camels is *da da*. And for pulling a boat, the Gemara offers *hayomei hi hi hileich vehileich chulya* — something like a rhythmic heave-ho, using the *h*-sound to coordinate effort. The Gemara's inclusion of these practical details is curious but evidently considered worth preserving.

### Leprosy Hazards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: עוֹר, דָּג, וְכוֹס, חַמִּין, וּבֵיצִים, וְכִנִּים לְבָנִים — כּוּלָּן קָשִׁין לְדָבָר אַחֵר. עוֹר — מַאן דְּגָנֵי אַמַּשְׁכָּא דְצַלָּא. דָּג — שִׁיבּוּטָא בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן. כּוֹס — שִׁיּוּרֵי כָּסָא דְהַרְסָנָא. חַמִּין — חַמִּימֵי דְחַמִּימֵי מְשַׁדְּרוּ עִילָּוֵיהּ. בֵּיצִים — מַאן דִּמְדָרֵךְ אַקְּלִיפִּים. כִּינִּים לְבָנִים — מַאן דִּמְחַוַּור לְבוּשֵׁיהּ וְלָא נָטֵיר לֵיהּ תְּמָנְיָא יוֹמֵי וַהֲדַר לָבֵישׁ לֵהּ — בָּרְיָין הָנָךְ כִּינִּים, וְקַשְׁיָן לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Hide, fish, and a cup, hot water, and eggs, and white lice all cause** the **other matter,** i.e., leprosy. The Gemara elaborates: **Hide** is referring to **one who sleeps on a tanner's hide** before it has been tanned. **Fish** is referring to the ***shibuta*** fish **in the days of Nisan.** The **cup** is referring to one who eats **the leftovers of small fried fish. Hot water** is referring to **very hot water that** one **pours on himself. Eggs** means **one who steps on eggshells. White lice** is referring to **one who washes his garment but does not keep it for eight days before wearing it again,** a habit which **creates these lice. And** all of these practices **cause** the **other matter,** leprosy.

Abaye teaches that certain things lead to *davar acher* — leprosy. These are: *or* — untanned leather, specifically lying on hides that have not been properly processed; *dag* — specifically the fish called *shivuta* eaten in the spring; *kos chamim* — the remnants of a cup of *harshena* soup or porridge; *chamim* — scalding hot water thrown on oneself; *beitzim* — walking on eggshells; and *kinim levanim* — linen garments that have been washed and then left hanging for eight days without being laundered again, so that lice develop in them, and then worn. Each item in this list has its own Talmudic elaboration. The linen case is described as follows: *man demachvar livushei velo natir lei tamnaya yomei vehadar lavush leh, biryan hanach kinim, vekashin ledavar acher* — one who cleans linen garments and then leaves them for eight days without washing them, and then wears them, thereby produces lice — *vekashin ledavar acher*, and this leads to leprosy.

### Cats & Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בֵּיתָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ שׁוּנָּרָא לָא נֵיעוּל בַּהּ אִינִישׁ בְּלָא מְסָנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּשׁוּנָּרָא קָטֵיל לְחִיוְיָא וְאָכֵיל לֵיהּ, וְאִית בֵּיהּ בְּחִיוְיָא גַּרְמֵי קַטִּינֵי, וְאִי יָתֵיב לֵהּ גַּרְמָא דְחִיוְיָא אַכַּרְעֵיהּ — לָא נָפֵיק וְאִסְתַּכַּן לֵיהּ. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: בֵּיתָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ שׁוּנָּרָא — לָא נֵיעוּל בֵּיהּ אִינִישׁ בְּהַכְרָא, מַאי טַעְמָא — דִילְמָא מִיכְּרִיךְ בֵּיהּ חִוְיָא וְלָא יָדַע וּמִסְתַּכַּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** With regard to **a house in which there is a cat, a person** should **not enter there barefoot. What is the reason? Because the cat** might **kill a snake and eat it, and the snake has small bones, and if** a small **bone gets into one's foot it cannot be removed, and he will be in danger. Some say** that Rav Pappa said: With regard to **a house in which there is no cat, a person** should **not enter there in the dark. What is the reason?** Since there is no cat to hunt snakes, **perhaps a snake will wrap itself around him without** him **knowing and he will be in danger.**

Rav Pappa teaches: *beita deit bei shunra, lo neil bei inshi belo masanei* — in a house that has a cat, one should not walk around barefoot. Why? *Mishum deshunra katel lechivya* — because cats kill snakes. *Ve-achal lei* — and eats the snake. *Ve-it bei dechivya garmei katekini* — but there are small bones within the snake, and *veyativ hei garma dechivya akarei* — if such a bone becomes lodged in one's foot, *lo nafik* — it cannot be extracted — *ve-istaken* — and one will be in mortal danger from the poison.

Conversely, in a house without a cat, one should not put on one's shoes in the dark. *Dilma mirach bei chivya* — a snake may have coiled inside the shoe — *velo yada ve-istaken* — and one will not know it is there until too late. In a place without cats to control the snake population, snakes in shoes were a genuine and life-threatening hazard. The story of a scorpion found nesting in a shoe on *erev Yom Kippur* at a yeshiva in Israel is a vivid reminder that for much of human history, and in much of the world today, such concerns are anything but theoretical.
