# Arakhin 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bayit Rishon & Sheni

### Twin Destructions

The previous *daf* was largely concerned with calculating how the *churban habayit* could have occurred on *Motzaei Shevi'it* — the conclusion of a *shemitah* cycle. That discussion began with *Bayit Rishon* and worked through a range of proofs. The *baraita* that continues this inquiry appears on 11b. Rabbi Yose states: *megalgelim zechut l'yom zakai v'chovah l'yom chayav* — merit is brought about on an auspicious day and liability on a day already marked for punishment. The *baraita* then records that when the First Temple was destroyed, that day was Tisha B'Av, *Motzaei Shabbat*, and *Motzaei Shevi'it* — the conclusion of the seventh year of the *shemitah* cycle. The *mishmar* of Yehoyariv was on duty, and the *kohanim* and *levi'im* were standing on the *duchan* chanting *shirah*. And then the *baraita* adds: *v'chen bashniyah* — and the same was true of the Second Temple.

### 420-Year Puzzle

The problem, taken up on 12b, is mathematical. We have a received tradition that the Second Temple stood for 420 years. Four hundred years constitute eight fifty-year *yovel* cycles. Adding two *shemitah* cycles yields fourteen years, leaving a remainder of six — which falls short of completing a *shemitah* cycle. How, then, could the Second Temple have been destroyed at the conclusion of a *shemitah* year? One resolution offered on 12b invokes the *shita* of Rabbi Yehuda, who counts *yovel* differently. Another approach simply holds that *Bayit Sheni* was destroyed on Tisha B'Av and *Motzaei Shabbat*, and sets aside the *Motzaei Shevi'it* component altogether in the context of the Second Temple. It is at this point that the *Gemara* on 13b introduces Rav Ashi's solution.

### Six Years Excluded

*Rav Ashi amar*: *hanehu sheit shanei ad d'salik Ezra u'mekadesh, la kachashiv lehu* — those six years from when the construction of the Temple began until Ezra made *aliyah* with the majority of the *golah* were not counted toward *shemitin* and *yovelot*. The premise is that the counting of *shemitah* and *yovel* did not apply during that initial construction phase, and therefore those years do not disturb the calculation. The obvious question is: from where do we know that the gap was precisely six years?

### Koresh to Darius

The *Gemara* cites a series of verses to establish the six-year figure. The background is well known: after Koresh issued his decree permitting the Jews to return and rebuild the Temple, the local inhabitants — traditionally identified with the peoples settled in the land by the Assyrian empire — obstructed the work until it was halted altogether. The verse in Ezra, written in Aramaic, records: *ba'dayin batelat avidat beit elaha di bi'Yerushlem* — the work of the House of God in Jerusalem was stopped.

Construction resumed in the second year of Darius. The *Gemara* then cites a later verse in Ezra: *v'shetzei beita dena ad yom tlata leyerach Adar tehi shnat sheit lemalchut Daryavesh* — this Temple shall be completed by the third day of the month of Adar in the sixth year of the reign of Darius. And a *baraita* teaches: *beoto zman leshana habaa ala Ezra v'galuto* — the following year from that time, in the seventh year of the king's reign, Ezra and his exiles came up to *Eretz Yisrael*, as it is written: *vayavo Yerushalayim bachodesh hachamishi hi hashana hasheviit lamelech* — he came to Jerusalem in the fifth month, which was the seventh year of the king. Counting from the second year of Darius through the seventh year — second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh — comes to six years. Rav Ashi's claim is that those six years of Temple construction were not counted for *shemitah* and *yovel*, and that this resolves the arithmetic of the 420-year Second Temple period concluding on *Motzaei Shevi'it*.

## Rav Ashi's Stance

### 'Amar' vs 'Amar'

There is a terminological point worth noting here. The *Gemara* introduces this solution with the formulation *Rav Ashi amar* — with Rav Ashi's name preceding the verb — rather than the more common *amar Rav Ashi*. This distinction is not merely stylistic. When a statement appears as *amar Rav* so-and-so, it is typically an independent statement: that *amora* determined he had something important to say and said it. When a statement appears as *Rav* so-and-so *amar*, with the name first, it is in the vast majority of cases a responsive formulation — that *amora* is disagreeing with a position already put forward by another *amora*. One sometimes sees both forms in close proximity: *Rava amar* X and *Abaye amar* X, where each is responding to the other.

In this instance, however, looking back at 12b, there is no named *amora* whose position Rav Ashi could be responding to. The preceding discussion on that entire *amud* is essentially anonymous, with the only named figures being Rabbi Yehuda and *Rabbanan* — both *Tannaim*, far earlier than Rav Ashi. The *Rabbanan* position concerns the counting of *yovel*: *Rabbanan* hold that the forty-ninth year, which is the *shemitah* year, concludes the cycle, and the fiftieth year is *yovel*, after which the first year of the next *shemitah* count begins. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *yovel* year simultaneously counts as the first year of the next *shemitah* cycle. Rav Ashi's formulation here seems to be engaging with the anonymous voice of the *Gemara* — the *stam d'gemara* — which proposed that the resolution could work according to *Rabbanan*. It is Rav Ashi, with his *Rav Ashi amar*, who now offers a different resolution, one that works without invoking Rabbi Yehuda's position at all.

### Stam d'Gemara

This brings us to a significant question in the scholarship of the *Talmud*: the nature and dating of the *stam d'gemara*, the anonymous voice. The traditional position, and the view of the *yeshivot*, is that Rav Ashi and Ravina were the ones who compiled and edited the *Talmud*, and therefore the anonymous material is Rav Ashi's own voice — it presents other positions, and then Rav Ashi states his own view. On this reading, there is no difficulty: Rav Ashi can respond to an anonymous layer because the anonymous layer is also his.

In academic Talmudic scholarship, however, the position associated most prominently with Professor Shamma Friedman and Professor David Weiss Halivni holds that all anonymous material is later than the named *amoraim*, even later than Rav Ashi. In its more extreme form, this position becomes nearly axiomatic: if a statement is anonymous, no named *amora* could possibly be responding to it, because the anonymous layer postdates all named figures. More conservative voices in the field allow for the possibility that what is now anonymous once had an attribution that was lost in transmission, or that in certain instances a named *amora* does engage with formulations that ended up in the *stam*. This passage appears to be one such case: Rav Ashi seems to be responding to an anonymous voice. Whether one explains that as Rav Ashi responding to his own prior anonymous framing, or as a later anonymous layer that preceded Rav Ashi's ruling in the editorial arrangement, the passage at minimum suggests that whoever placed the *Rav Ashi amar* formulation here understood him to be in dialogue with — and disagreeing with — an unnamed position.

## 17 Jubilees

### Yechezkel's 14 Years

The *Gemara* now returns to a matter raised in yesterday's learning. *Gufa*: Israel counted seventeen *yovelot* — seventeen fifty-year cycles — from when they entered the land until they were exiled. The *Gemara* notes immediately that one cannot say they began counting from the very moment they crossed the Jordan. We already know that there is a fourteen-year gap. If counting had begun immediately upon entry, then working back from the destruction of the Temple, the Temple would have been destroyed at the very beginning of a new *yovel* year. But we know from the book of Yechezkel that Yechezkel received his great vision fourteen years after the Temple's destruction — and that vision occurred in a *yovel* year. This means the Temple's destruction must have fallen fourteen years *before* a *yovel*, not at its beginning. The only way to reconcile seventeen *yovelot* with the required fourteen-year gap is to say that the counting did not begin immediately, but was delayed by fourteen years — seven years to conquer the land and seven years to divide it.

### Kalev's Age

The *Gemara* now asks: from where do we know that it took seven years to conquer the land? From what Kalev ben Yefuneh tells us about himself. In Sefer Yehoshua, Kalev says: *ben arbaim shanah anochi bishloch Moshe eved Hashem oti mi-Kadesh Barnea leraggel et ha-aretz* — I was forty years old when Moshe sent me from Kadesh Barnea to scout the land. And then: *ve'attah hinneh anochi hayom ben chamesh u'shmonim shanah* — and now, at the time of the *chalukah*, the division of the land, I am eighty-five.

The *Gemara* also cites the tradition that in the first year in the *midbar* Moshe built the *mishkan*, and in the second year the *meraglim* were sent. That means the spies were dispatched in the second year — making Kalev forty years old in year two of the *midbar*. The total time in the *midbar* is forty years, of which two preceded the sin of the spies and thirty-eight followed. Therefore, when *Bnei Yisrael* crossed the Jordan, Kalev was seventy-eight years old — *bar tamnin sari* — seventy-eight. By the time the *chalukah* began, he was eighty-five. The difference is seven years: *ishtakach d'sheva kibshu* — it emerges that they spent seven years in conquest.

### Division of Land

The *Gemara* then asks: and from where do we know that it took seven years to divide the land? Two answers are offered. The first: *i ba'it eima mi d'sheva kibshu sheva chilku* — if you like, say: just as it took seven years to conquer, it took seven years to divide. The second answer, however, is a striking one: *i ba'it eima mi d'la mishkachat lehu arba esrei shanah achar shehukta ha'ir* — or alternatively, since we know from Yechezkel's testimony that his vision fell precisely fourteen years after the Temple's destruction and that this was a *yovel* year, and since we have already established seven years for conquest, there must be seven years for division — because that is the only way the numbers work. This is transparent circular reasoning, and the *Gemara* is entirely candid about it. We need fourteen years to fill a gap, we know seven of them, so the remaining seven are assigned to the division of the land. The *Talmud* here is unusually direct about its interpretive method: a seven-year gap exists and must be accounted for, so it is attributed to the most plausible historical process available.

## Six Goats

### Mishnah's Six

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnayot*, which concern the roles and minimum numbers associated with Temple service. The *Mishnah* states: *ein pochatin mishisha tela'im hamevukarim b'lishkat hatela'im k'dei l'Shabbat ul'yamim tovim* — one does not have fewer than six lambs that have been examined for *mumim* kept in the chamber of lambs, sufficient for *Shabbat* and *yamim tovim*. The *Mishnah* specifies *yamim tovim* and *Rosh Hashanah* — note that in the period of the *Mishnah* there was no biblical obligation of two days *yom tov*, so the only case of two consecutive *yamim tovim* would be *Rosh Hashanah*. If *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashanah* were to coincide, one might need two animals for each day, amounting to six. The *Mishnah* continues: *u'mosifin ad olam* — and one may add as many as one wishes, so long as space permits.

However, the *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: surely when *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashanah* coincide there are also *mussaf* offerings to be brought, which would require far more than six animals. The answer given is *tana b'alma ka'ei* — the *Mishnah* is speaking generally, not specifically about that combination of days. What, then, is the significance of *k'dei l'Shabbat ul'yamim tovim*? The *Gemara* proposes that this phrase is *simana b'alma* — merely a mnemonic marker — and the real teaching of the *Mishnah* is a different one: *ein pochatin mishisha tela'im hamevukarim b'lishkat hatela'im arba'ah yom kodem shechitah* — one does not have fewer than six examined lambs in the chamber, and this is because they must be available four days before the *shechitah*. The six and the four days are the substance of the law; the reference to *Shabbat* and *yom tov* simply illustrates the scenario that makes the requirement visible.

### Rashi's System

*Rashi* addresses the four-day inspection requirement by describing the operational system in place from the time of *Chanukat HaBayit*. When the Temple was inaugurated, six inspected lambs were placed in the *lishkat hatela'im* four days before the inauguration. On the first day, two were taken for the *korban hatamid* and two replacements were introduced and examined. On the second day, two more of the remaining original six were used for the *tamid* and another two replacements were added. On the third day, the final two of the original group were used and two more brought in. By the fourth day, the first pair of replacements — those added on day one — had now been in the chamber for four days and were ready for *shechitah*. Thus a continuous cycle maintained itself: *v'chen le'olam notnim shenayim v'notlim shenayim* — one always adds two and removes two — ensuring that every animal offered had been checked at least four days before it was slaughtered.

*Rashi* raises a difficulty with this system in the context of the four-day count: when *Pesach* takes a lamb on the tenth of Nisan and slaughters it on the fourteenth, that is a gap of four clear days excluding the endpoints — the day of selection is not counted, and the fourteenth itself is the *shechitah* day. The arithmetic of Rashi's *Chanukat HaBayit* system, if one counts the day of entry into the chamber and the day of *shechitah* together, likewise arrives at four, but the precise counting of endpoints requires care. *Rashi* writes: *vekashye li* — this troubles me — noting that the parallelism between the *Pesach* four-day rule and the *tamid* four-day rule is imprecise if the endpoints are counted differently.

*Rashi* then offers an alternative: *deshmonah nitnu sham beyom chinuch* — actually eight animals were placed in the chamber at the time of the inauguration, not six. The *Mishnah*'s statement that one must have no fewer than six is to be understood as the minimum that remains *after* the two daily offerings have already been removed. Any time two are taken out, six must still be present. Before the next day's night begins, two more are examined and added. The result is that the number on hand fluctuates but never falls below six. *Rashi* then introduces a *lishna achrina* — an alternative formulation — in which he abandons the count of six altogether as connected to the four-day inspection period, and instead reads the *Mishnah*'s six as an independent minimum, with the *Shabbat*-and-*yom tov* language functioning solely to illustrate the four-day necessity: when *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashanah* fall together, one cannot go out to acquire and inspect a new animal on those days, so the four-day pre-inspection must be arranged in advance. It is this general need for a four-day buffer that the six-animal minimum is designed to ensure.

The verbosity of this *Rashi*, and especially the presence of a *lishna achrina*, is worth noting. *Rashi* as we know him is terse, precise, and direct. A *Rashi* that raises difficulties, argues extensively, and then offers an entirely alternative reading under the heading *lishna achrina* raises a textual question. Professor Yaakov Nachum Epstein, in the first issue of *Tarbitz* — the journal of academic Talmud of the Hebrew University — demonstrated that the *Rashi* on *Masechet Horayot* is not actually Rashi's own commentary. His first argument was stylistic: the *Rashi* on *Horayot* is verbose, convoluted, and unclear — entirely unlike the real *Rashi*. He adduced more substantive evidence as well, but the stylistic instinct itself is meaningful. When one sees *Rashi* becoming unusually long and complicated, or when one encounters a *lishna achrina* indicating that a second, contradictory formulation has been appended, there is good reason to suspect that what one is reading is not entirely *Rashi*'s own hand, or that additions from a *talmid* have been incorporated into the text.

### Ben Bag Bag

The *Gemara* identifies the source of the four-day pre-inspection rule as a *Tanna* who appears only rarely: Ben Bag Bag, known primarily from *Pirkei Avot* — *hafoch bah v'hafoch bah* — turn it and turn it, for everything is in it. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Ben Bag Bag asks: from where do we know that the *korban tamid* requires examination for *mumim* four days before its *shechitah*? He answers from a *gezerah shavah*. In the *parasha* of the *tamid*, the Torah says *tishmeru lehakriv li bemo'ado* — you shall guard it to offer it to Me in its appointed time — using the root *sh-m-r*, guarding or watchfulness. And elsewhere, in the passage establishing the *Pesach* sacrifice, it says: *v'hayah lachem lemishmeret ad arba'ah asar yom lachodesh harishon* — it shall be kept by you until the fourteenth day of the first month — again the root *sh-m-r*, this time in the noun form *mishmeret*. Since the *Pesach* lamb is selected on the tenth and slaughtered on the fourteenth, the *mishmeret* entails a four-day period of pre-slaughter inspection. Ben Bag Bag applies this via *gezerah shavah* to the *tamid*: just as the *Pesach* requires four days between selection and *shechitah*, so too does the *tamid*.

*Rashi* himself raises the precise objection that a student might raise: the *Pesach* case involves taking the animal on the tenth and slaughtering it on the fourteenth — is that four days or three? If one counts the day of taking and the day of slaughter as endpoints, the span between them is four intervals but five points. The *Gemara* and *Rashi* are both aware of this endpoint question, and *Rashi* flags it explicitly in the phrase *vekashye li*.

### General Minimum

Having established Ben Bag Bag's derivation, the *Gemara* confirms that the *Mishnah*'s language supports the reading of *simana b'alma*. The *Mishnah* says *k'dei Shabbat* rather than *k'dei l'Shabbat* — "as for Shabbat" rather than "in order to suffice for Shabbat" — and the *Gemara* notes: *v'katani k'dei Shabbat v'lo k'dei l'Shabbat, shema mina* — this language implies that *Shabbat* is being used as a reference point or illustration, not as the specific purpose of the six-animal minimum. The conclusion is that six inspected animals in the *lishkat hatela'im* is a standing minimum at all times — not a number calibrated to any particular *Shabbat* or *yom tov* combination — and that the *Shabbat*-*yom tov* language is representative of the general principle that one must always have lambs pre-inspected with a four-day buffer. This reading is also consistent with the broader context of the *mishnayot* on this *daf*, which are uniformly concerned with permanent minimum quantities of Temple appurtenances, not with case-specific provisions.

## Instruments

### Trumpets

The *Mishnah* states: *ein pochatin mishtei chatzotzrot* — there must be no fewer than two trumpets — *u'mosifin aleihem* — and one may add as many as one wishes. The *Gemara* asks: up to how many? Rav Huna — and some say this was transmitted as Rav Zavdi said in the name of Rav Huna — answers: up to 120, as it is written regarding the dedication of Shlomo's Temple in *Divrei HaYamim*: *v'imahem kohanim l'me'ah v'esrim machatzrim b'chatzotzrot* — with them were *kohanim*, 120 of them, blowing trumpets. The dedication ceremony of *Chanukat HaBayit d'Shlomo* thus establishes 120 as the upper bound for trumpeters.

### Harps & Tosa

The *Mishnah* further states: *mitisha kinorot* — no fewer than nine harps — *u'mosifin aleihem* — and one may add to them. *Tosafot*, on 13a, raises the question of how there can be a maximum at all when the *Mishnah* explicitly says one may add as many as one wishes. The answer *Tosafot* gives is *mitzvah min hamuvchar* — performing the commandment in the most excellent manner. The optimal number is 120, corresponding to the dedication ceremony of Shlomo's Temple. *Tosafot* adds that beyond 120 one begins to worry about *ibuv kolot* — a confusion or cacophony of voices — meaning that once the ensemble exceeds that number, the harmonious blending of sounds can no longer be maintained. Therefore 120 represents both the historical precedent and the ideal.

### Cymbals

The *Mishnah* states: *v'hatziltzal levad* — and the cymbal alone. The *Gemara* asks: from where is this derived? *Amar Rav Assi*: from the verse in *Divrei HaYamim*, where David brings the *aron Hashem* back into the tent — the *aron* having spent a period in the field, returned at the time of David's celebrated and somewhat controversial dance — and the verse lists the musicians present, identifying one individual, Asaf, as playing the cymbals: *v'Asaf bimtziltayim*. Since there is only one person playing the cymbals, that establishes the minimum: one.

The *Gemara* immediately notes a grammatical tension: *metziltayim* is in the dual form — like *yadayim*, *raglayim*, *shtayim* — suggesting two. The answer is: *keivan d'chada avidta avdi, l'chad gavra avdi, kari lehu chad* — since the two cymbals perform a single function, executed by a single person, they are counted as one. A cymbal by itself cannot produce music by crashing; the two halves of the instrument are a single unit, like a pair of trousers — one item referred to in the dual because it consists of two pieces. Thus *metziltayim* denotes a single instrument that happens to be dual in form.

## Twelve Levites

### Mishnah's 12

The *Mishnah* states: *ein pochatin mishneim asar Levi'im omdim al haduchan u'mosifin ad olam* — there must be no fewer than twelve *levi'im* standing on the *duchan* singing, and one may add indefinitely. Twelve is the minimum for the Levitical choir.

### Minors' Role

The *Mishnah* continues: *ein hakatan nichnas la'azarah la'avodah ela b'sha'ah shehaLevi'im omrim b'shir* — a minor does not enter the *Azarah* to perform *avodah* except at the time when the *levi'im* are singing. According to *Rashi* at that passage, the minors referred to are young *levi'im*. The implication is that even though minors are generally excluded from Temple service, they are permitted entry specifically in the context of the Levitical *shir*. Crucially, they are not permitted to play instruments: *v'lo hayu omrim b'nevel v'chinnor, ela b'peh* — they did not sing with *nevel* or *kinnor*, only with their voices — *k'dei litein tevel b'neimah* — in order to add a seasoning or spice to the melody.

### R. Eliezer b. Yaakov

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds: *ein olin l'minyan* — these minors do not count among the twelve. He may be understood as clarifying rather than disputing: the *Mishnah*'s requirement of twelve is a minimum of adult *levi'im*; the minors who participate in the singing are supplementary. Moreover, *v'ein olin l'duchan* — they do not stand on the stage itself — *ela ba'aretz hayu omdim v'rasheihem bein raglei haLevi'im* — rather they stood on the ground, their heads at the level of the feet of the adult *levi'im* standing on the platform above them. These minors were called *tza'arei haLevi'im* — a term whose meaning will be explained shortly.

### Rav Pappa's Math

One might ask: where does the number twelve come from? *Amar Rav Pappa*: the twelve *levi'im* correspond to nine *kinnorot*, two *nevalim*, and one *tziltzal* — nine plus two plus one equals twelve. Rav Pappa derives this not from the *Mishnah* but from the verses in *Divrei HaYamim*, where in the context of Shlomo's *Chanukat HaBayit*, groups of musicians are listed by name with the recurring formulation *hu v'echav shneim asar* — he and his brothers, twelve. These twelve-person groupings, when analyzed according to the instruments involved — *nevalim*, *kinnorot*, and *tziltzal* — yield the total of twelve that the *Mishnah* requires. The *chatzotzrot*, it should be noted, are sounded by *kohanim*, not *levi'im*, and are therefore not included in Rav Pappa's count.

One might wonder about the *chalilim* — the flutes — which appear in the *Mishnah* on 13a (*ein pochatin mishnei chalilin*). The answer is that the *chalilim* were played only on specific days throughout the year — twelve days, in fact, according to the principle of *hachalil makeh lifnei hamizbe'ach* — and since they were not a daily fixture in the way the harps and *nevalim* were, Rav Pappa does not incorporate them into his accounting of the permanent minimum twelve.

### Divrei HaYamim

The *Gemara* also addresses the question of whether minors were permitted in the *Azarah* for the *shir* at all. Rabbi Yochanan derives this from a verse in *Ezra*: *v'yamod Yeshua banav v'echav Kadmiel u'vanav bnei Yehudah k'echad lenatzeach al osei hamelacha* — the repeated formula of *banav v'echav*, his sons and his brothers, appears again with Chenadad and his descendants. *Tosafot* asks: how does the repeated mention of *banav v'echav* prove that those mentioned are minors? The answer is that once the verse has said *v'echav haLevi'im* — his brothers who are *levi'im* — one should not need to name yet another *levi* with his own *banav v'echav*. The fact that the formula repeats suggests that each instance of *banav v'echav* signals a distinct category, and the context implies that the sons in question are minors — young boys included specifically for the purpose of the *shir*.

## Singing Debate

### Voices Only

The *Mishnah* states: *lo hayu omrim lo b'nevel v'lo b'chinnor ela b'peh* — they did not sing with *nevel* or *kinnor*, only with their voices. This formulation distinguishes between *nevel* and *kinnor* as two separate instruments. The *Gemara* asks: does this mean our *Mishnah* does not follow the position of Rabbi Yehuda? — *leima matnitin dela k'Rabbi Yehuda*.

### R. Yehuda's Strings

A *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehuda holds: the *kinnor* of the Temple had seven strings, as it is written *sova semachot et panecha* — do not read *sova* but rather *sheva*, seven, yielding a homiletical derivation of seven strings from the association with joy. In the Messianic era, the *kinnor* will have eight strings, derived from the psalm heading *lamnatzeach al hashminit* — for the eighth, understood as referring to the eighth string. And in *olam haba*, the *kinnor* will have ten strings, as it is written: *alei asor v'alei nevel alei higayon b'chinnor* — here the *asor*, the ten-stringed instrument, and the *nevel* are both mentioned alongside the *kinnor*. This seems to indicate that the *kinnor* of *olam haba* will incorporate the *asor* — that is, will have ten strings — and that Rabbi Yehuda equates the *nevel* and the *kinnor* as variants of the same instrument. If so, our *Mishnah*, which treats them as distinct, would be in tension with Rabbi Yehuda's view.

### Nevel vs Kinnor

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda — l'olam haba agav d'nafish nimi didei nafish kalei k'nevel* — even according to Rabbi Yehuda, since the *kinnor* of *olam haba* has so many strings, its sound becomes fuller and richer, resembling that of a *nevel*. Therefore it is called a *nevel* — *kari lei nevel* — not because it is identical to a *nevel*, but because its tonal quality approximates one. The underlying distinction between *nevel* and *kinnor* that Rabbi Yehuda would recognize is that the *nevel* has more strings and produces a thicker, broader sound, while the *kinnor* is a lighter instrument with fewer strings. When the *kinnor* of *olam haba* gains enough strings to become acoustically similar to a *nevel*, Rabbi Yehuda calls it by that name. This resolution preserves Rabbi Yehuda's awareness that the two instruments are in principle distinct — and thus our *Mishnah*'s differentiation between them is perfectly consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's view as well.

## Child Singers

### Soadei vs Tza'arei

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov refers to the minor *levi'im* participants as *tza'arei haLevi'im* — a term rooted in the word *tza'ar*, distress or annoyance. However, a *baraita* — or perhaps an alternate textual version of the *Mishnah* — uses a different term: *soadei haLevi'im*, from the root *sa'ad*, meaning to support or aid. On this reading, the children were helpers or supporters of the adult Levites.

### Pure Voices

The *Gemara* explains why our *Tanna* uses the term *tza'arei* rather than *soadei*: *keivan d'hanei katin kaleihu v'hanei av kaleihu* — because these ones, the children, had thin, pure voices, while the adults had thick, heavier voices. The children's voices were higher and clearer; they could push their voices to greater heights — *hanei mechatei v'hanei la mechatei* — while the adults could not reach as high. *Kari lehu tza'arei* — they were called *tza'arei*, annoying, because they perpetually outdid the adult *levi'im* in the register and purity of their singing. The image is of boy choristers whose clear, soaring voices consistently outshone the rougher sound of their adult counterparts — and so, with a mixture of grudging acknowledgment and mild exasperation, they earned the name *tza'arei haLevi'im*: the ones who gave the *levi'im* grief.

*Hadran alach elu ne'erachin* — and with that we complete another chapter of *Maseches Arachin*.
