# Menachot 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Night & Tefillin

### Night as Zman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מְצַלּוּ בְּהוּ בְּאוּרְתָּא? הָהוּא פְּלִיגָא. וּמִי אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: סָפֵק חֲשֵׁיכָה סָפֵק (לָא) [אֵין] חֲשֵׁיכָה – לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מַנִּיחַ, הָא וַדַּאי חֲשֵׁיכָה – חוֹלֵץ! הָתָם בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna would pray in the evening with** phylacteries. The Gemara explains: **That** opinion represented in this incident **disagrees** with the ruling of Rav Naḥman. **And did Rabba bar Rav Huna** actually **say this,** that the mitzva of phylacteries applies at night? **But doesn't Rabba bar Rav Huna say:** If it is **uncertain** whether it is **nightfall** or whether it is **not nightfall,** one **neither removes** his phylacteries, as it is not yet definitely night, **nor dons** them *ab initio*. **This** indicates that if it is **definitely nightfall,** one **removes** his phylacteries. The Gemara answers: Rabba bar Rav Huna's ruling **there was stated with regard to Shabbat eve,** as one may not don phylacteries on Shabbat, when the mitzva does not apply.

The discussion on 36b continuing into 37 concerns the question of whether night constitutes a designated time for wearing *tefillin*. The *gemara* had introduced a range of opinions regarding precisely when one must remove *tefillin*: some hold that removal is required as soon as the sun sets — which is the *halachik* definition of night — while others point to *tichleh regel min hashuk*, the time when people finish returning from work, or the onset of the time for sleeping. These latter two criteria are not *halachik* definitions of night at all. Rather, they reflect a rabbinic concern that a person may fall asleep while wearing *tefillin*, which could lead to a violation involving bodily awareness — *pacha gas*. The question of whether night is in principle *layla lav zman tefillin*, not a time for *tefillin*, thus hinges on whether the prohibition is rooted in Torah law or in this rabbinic concern about losing bodily control during sleep.

### Chasheicha Levels

A distinction must be carefully maintained here. The two later criteria — *tichleh regel min hashuk* and the time for sleeping — are not statements about when night halakhically begins. They are expressions of a rabbinic concern that one might doze off. The first criterion, sunset, is a genuine *halachik* definition. Understanding which category a given opinion belongs to determines whether we are dealing with a Torah-level question about *zman tefillin* or a rabbinic precaution against inadvertent sleep.

### Torah vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, שַׁבָּת נָמֵי זְמַן תְּפִילִּין. אִי קָסָבַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, לַיְלָה נָמֵי לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, דְּמֵהֵיכָא דְּמִמַּעֲטָא שַׁבָּת מֵהָתָם מִמַּעֲטִי לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: **What does** Rabba bar Rav Huna **hold? If he holds** that **night** is **a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries,** then **Shabbat** is **also a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries. If he holds** that **Shabbat** is **not a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries,** then **night** is **also not a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries.** The reason for this statement is **that from** the source **where Shabbat is excluded** from the mitzva of phylacteries, **nights are excluded from there** as well.

A natural question arises: even granting that night is not the designated time for *tefillin*, why does that fact generate an active prohibition to continue wearing them? After all, many *mitzvot* are not operative at night without any suggestion that one is forbidden to perform them nocturnally. It is not a *mitzvah* to take *lulav* at night — but is it prohibited to hold one's *lulav* at night? There may be a concern of *bal tosif* if one holds it as part of an act of performing the *mitzvah*, but it is not self-evidently *assur* merely because the daytime obligation has passed.

### Lulav & Tzitzit

*Tzitzit* presents an even more instructive comparison. We are not scrupulous about removing *tzitzit* at nightfall — and indeed there is debate whether one might in fact be *chayav* to wear them at night as *mitzvot beged yom*, an obligation that attaches to the garment by day. Part of the answer for *tefillin* may indeed involve a form of *bal tosif*, but part of it may be something deeper — that even the Torah's definition of *tefillin* as a *mitzvah* only during the day, and not at night, is connected to the concept of awareness and consciousness that the *mitzvah* requires.

### Action vs State

There is a deeper conceptual dimension here that connects to a fundamental question about the nature of the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*: is the *mitzvah* one of *ukshartem* — an act of binding — or of *vehayu* — a state of being? The language of the *pesukim* invokes both. If the *mitzvah* is primarily a *state*, then the Torah is effectively defining a bounded period during which that state must be maintained. A state without temporal limits is almost a contradiction in terms — it collapses the structure that gives it meaning.

### Kedusha Boundaries

This connects to a broader principle about *kedusha*. For something to possess sacred status, it requires definition — beginning and end, inside and outside. If a *mitzvah* could be performed at all times without limit, it would paradoxically lose the very character that makes it significant. Boundaries are not merely restrictions; they are constitutive of the *kedusha* itself. The limitation of *tefillin* to daytime is thus not simply a technical exclusion but part of what makes the wearing of *tefillin* meaningful as an *ot*, a sign.

## Rabba bar R. Huna

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁמַרְתָּ אֶת הַחֻקָּה הַזֹּאת לְמוֹעֲדָהּ מִיָּמִים יָמִימָה״ – ״יָמִים״ וְלֹא לֵילוֹת, ״מִיָּמִים״ וְלֹא כׇּל יָמִים, פְּרָט לְשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֶמְרָה חוּקָּה זוֹ אֶלָּא לְפֶסַח בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the end of the passage of the Torah that discusses both the mitzvot of the Paschal offering and phylacteries: **"And you shall observe this ordinance in its season from year [*miyamim*] to year"** (Exodus 13:10). This indicates that these mitzvot apply during the **days [*yamim*] but not** during the **nights.** Furthermore, the letter *mem*, meaning from, in the term: **"From year [*miyamim*],"** teaches: These mitzvot apply on some days, **but not** on **all days.** This **excludes *Shabbatot* and Festivals,** on which phylacteries are not worn. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says: This** verse, mentioning **an ordinance, is stated only with regard to the Paschal** offering, and it is not referring to phylacteries at all. Evidently, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says that at night one is exempt from the obligation of donning phylacteries, says that on Shabbat one is exempt as well.

The *gemara* now presses a pointed question: the *gemara* reports that Rabba bar Rav Huna held that one who was already wearing *tefillin* during the day could continue wearing them into the night — suggesting that night is in some sense a permissible time. Yet the *gemara* cites another ruling by the very same Rabba bar Rav Huna: *safek chasheicha safek lo chasheicha — lo choleitz velo mani'ach*. If it is *bein hashemashot*, the twilight period, one neither removes the *tefillin* nor puts on new ones. The implication of this ruling is *ha vadai chasheicha choleitz* — if it is definitively night, he must remove them. This appears to contradict his apparent leniency in a prior story where he wore his *tefillin* when it came time to daven *Ma'ariv*.

### Erev Shabbat Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַנִּיחַ אָדָם תְּפִילִּין בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וּבְיָמִים טוֹבִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה לְאוֹת עַל יָדְךָ וּלְטוֹטָפוֹת בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״ – מִי שֶׁצְּרִיכִין אוֹת, יָצְאוּ שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁהֵן גּוּפָן אוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabba bar Rav Huna **derives** the exemption from the obligation to don phylacteries on Shabbat **from** a different source, the source **where Rabbi Akiva derives it from, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **a person should don phylacteries on *Shabbatot* and Festivals.** To counter this, **the verse states: "And it shall be for a sign for you on your arm, and for a remembrance between your eyes,** so that God's law shall be in your mouth; for with a strong arm God brought you out of Egypt" (Exodus 13:9). This teaches that the obligation to don phylacteries applies when the Jewish people **require a sign** to assert their status as God's nation, i.e., during the week. This serves to **exclude *Shabbatot* and Festivals, as they themselves are signs** of the Jewish people's status as God's nation and a remembrance of the exodus from Egypt. Consequently, no further sign is required on these days.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hatam be'Erev Shabbat itamar* — that ruling about definitive nightfall requiring removal was stated specifically in the context of *Erev Shabbat*. What Rabba bar Rav Huna requires one to remove on Friday night is not rooted in the principle that night is *lav zman tefillin*. Rather, it is because *Shabbat* itself — which everyone agrees is *lav zman tefillin* — has arrived. If night were simply another period governed by the same rule as *Shabbat*, the distinction would collapse. Instead, the *gemara* treats the two as categorically different: *Shabbat* definitively excludes *tefillin*, while ordinary nighttime does not carry that same absolute exclusion.

### Safek Chasheicha

The *gemara* then asks directly: *mai kasavar?* — what did Rabba bar Rav Huna actually hold? If he held *layla lav zman tefillin*, then *Shabbat*, which is derived from the same *pasuk*, should also be excluded. If he held *Shabbat lav zman tefillin*, then by the same logic night should be excluded as well, since *demeheicha d'memaatet Shabbas me-hasam memaatet layla* — the same source that excludes *Shabbat* excludes night. His leniency in leaving *tefillin* on into the evening demonstrates that he held *layla* to be a permitted time; and his stringency on *Erev Shabbat* demonstrates that *Shabbat* itself is a separate and independent category of exclusion.

## Parashat Kadesh

### Source Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל הַמַּנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין אַחַר שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: עוֹבֵר בְּלָאו. לֵימָא בְּרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ ״פֶּן״ וְ״אַל״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Anyone who dons phylacteries after sunset violates a positive mitzva. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He violates a prohibition.** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that these Sages **disagree with regard to** the principle that **Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a says. As Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a says: Any place where it is stated** in the Torah any of the terms: **Observe,** or: **Lest, or: Do not,** this means **nothing other than a prohibition,** as these are negative terms.

The *gemara* now turns to the underlying scriptural derivation. It cites a *braita* built on the *pasuk* in *Parashat Kadesh*: *ve-shamarta et ha-chuka ha-zot l'moada mi-yamim yamima*. This verse appears in the context of *Pesach* and also in proximity to the *parshiyot* of *tefillin*. The phrase *m'yamim yamima* is parsed as follows: *yamim* — days and not nights; *mi-yamim* — some days and not all days, to exclude *Shabbatot* and *yamim tovim*. This exclusion is understood to apply to *tefillin*.

### R. Yosi vs. R. Akiva

This reading is the position of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, who understands the *pasuk* as addressing *tefillin*. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *lo ne'emar chuka zu ela le-Pesach bilvad* — this *chuka* refers exclusively to the *korban Pesach*, not to *tefillin*. However, Rabbi Akiva nonetheless holds that *tefillin* are not worn on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* — he derives this from a different source entirely.

### Reconciling Views

The *gemara* therefore notes: *nafka lei me-heicha d'nafka lei l'Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva's exclusion of *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* from the time for *tefillin* comes from elsewhere. A second *braita* records Rabbi Akiva's own derivation: *yachol yani'ach adam tefillin be-Shabbatot u-ve-yamim tovim?* — perhaps one should wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*? The Torah says *ve-haya l'ot al yadecha u-le-totafot bein einecha* — they shall be a sign. The principle follows: *mi she-tzrichin ot* — only when a sign is needed. *Yatze'u Shabbatot ve-yamim tovim she-hen gufan ot* — excluded are *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, which are themselves signs, and therefore do not require this additional marking.

## Ot Framework

### R. Akiva Ot

Rabbi Akiva's derivation rests on a compelling conceptual logic: *tefillin* serve as an *ot*, a sign. The function of a sign is to mark and remind. When days already carry their own inherent significance as signs — as *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* do — the wearing of an additional *ot* does not supplement that significance; it undermines it. For something to function as a sign, it must stand out. If everything is always a sign, nothing is. Thus the very nature of the *ot* limits when it is appropriate.

The comparison to the *tzitz* of the *Kohen Gadol* is illuminating. The *Kohen Gadol* wears the *tzitz* — engraved *Kodesh l'Hashem* — on his forehead at all times as a reminder of God's presence and as an instrument of atonement. Yet on *Yom HaKippurim*, during the *avodat Yom HaKippurim*, he does not wear the *tzitz*; he wears *bigdei lavan* and enters the *Kodesh Kodashim*. The reason is precisely analogous: when you are in the immediate presence of the Divine, you do not need a reminder of that presence. The *tzitz* is for ordinary moments of service; the encounter in the *Kodesh Kodashim* transcends it. Similarly, *Shabbat* itself is an encounter of such depth that the *ot* of *tefillin* would be not merely superfluous but somewhat incongruous.

### Sign Function

There is a further dimension to this. The prohibition is not merely that one *need not* wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat* — one is *not allowed* to. This follows from the *pasuk*: if *tefillin* are excluded by Torah law from *Shabbat*, then wearing them would constitute a violation. The idea of the *ot* reinforces this: wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbat* would imply that *Shabbat* is insufficient as a sign on its own — a kind of *pgiya* in the *kedushat Shabbat*. Neither sign functions properly when the other is present simultaneously, because the presence of one appears to compensate for a deficiency in the other, and no such deficiency exists.

### Tosafot Ot

*Tosafot* on this *sugya* grapple with how to extend the *ot* logic to *Yom Tov*. The *Torah* does not explicitly call *Yom Tov* an *ot* in the same way *Shabbat* is called *os hi beini u-veineichem*. *Tosafot* therefore articulate that the *ot* of *Yom Tov* is not constituted by the prohibition of *melacha* — because even *Chol HaMoed*, on which *melacha* is permitted in various forms, can still be considered to carry an *ot*. Rather, *Tosafot* ground the *ot* of *Yom Tov* in the *mitzvot asei* that characterize those days — on *Pesach*, the prohibition of eating *chametz*; on *Sukkot*, the obligation to dwell in the *sukkah*. These *mitzvot* function as inherent commemorations of what the *yamim tovim* represent historically, and they constitute the *ot* that makes wearing *tefillin* unnecessary.

### Chol HaMoed

This analysis bears directly on *Chol HaMoed*, which is one of the major *machloket Rishonim* — a debate that runs through the medieval authorities and remains unresolved in the *gemara* itself. The *gemara* simply does not address *Chol HaMoed* explicitly; the entire discussion belongs to the *Rishonim*, particularly the *Hagahot Mordechai*. According to *Tosafot*, since *Chol HaMoed* carries its own *ot* — through the *mitzvas hayom*, the special and unique character of those days — one does not wear *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed*. Other *Rishonim* strongly disagree, tying the exclusion more directly to the *issur melacha*, which is less operative on *Chol HaMoed*. The dispute is serious on both sides: on one hand, *mitzvas tefillin* is a *mitzvas asei d'Oraita*; on the other, the *gemara*'s logic about the *ot* suggests that there are days when this *ot* must be withheld.

## Practices & Minhag

### Gemara Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: וְאִם לְשׇׁמְרָן – מוּתָּר. וְאָמַר רָבִינָא: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי וְחָשַׁךְ, וְהִנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וַאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: לְשׇׁמְרָן קָא בָעֵי לְהוּ מָר? וַאֲמַר לִי: אִין. וַחֲזֵיתֵיהּ לְדַעְתֵּיהּ דְּלָאו לְשׇׁמְרָן הוּא בָּעֵי, קָסָבַר: הֲלָכָה וְאֵין מוֹרִין כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: And** although it is prohibited to don phylacteries at night, **if** one does so in order **to safeguard them** from theft and the like, it is **permitted. And Ravina said: I was sitting before Rav Ashi and** it **grew dark, and he donned phylacteries. And I said to him: Does the Master need to safeguard them? And he said to me: Yes. But I saw that his intention** in donning them was **not** that he **needed to safeguard them;** rather, Rav Ashi **holds:** This is the ***halakha*,** that night is an appropriate time for phylacteries, **but** a public **ruling is not issued to that effect.**

The *gemara*'s clear position is that *tefillin* are not worn on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. The question of *Chol HaMoed* is left unresolved within the *gemara* itself and belongs entirely to the realm of *Rishonim* and later *poskim*.

### Ashkenazi Minhag

The Ashkenazic *minhag* is to refrain from wearing *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed*. Sephardic communities have different practices, with variations regarding whether a *bracha* is recited if *tefillin* are worn. One approach among those who do wear *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed* is to do so without a *bracha*, on the grounds that the *halacha* is genuinely unresolved and one should not risk a *bracha l'vatala*.

### No-Bracha Option

The logic of wearing *tefillin* without a *bracha* on *Chol HaMoed* is that one thereby fulfills the *mitzvah* while acknowledging the *safek* — the unresolved question — and thus avoids either neglecting the *mitzvas asei* entirely or desecrating the day through an unauthorized *bracha*. In practice, however, the challenge with this approach is the risk of reverting to *autopilot*: as soon as the *tefillin* goes on, the *bracha* can come out automatically, creating the further complication of what to do when one reaches the *shel rosh*.

## Awareness Obligation

### R. Elazar's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בַּתְּפִילִּין בְּכׇל שָׁעָה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִצִּיץ, וּמָה צִיץ שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא אַזְכָּרָה אַחַת, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״וְהָיָה עַל מִצְחוֹ תָּמִיד״, שֶׁלֹּא (תַּסִּיחַ) [יַסִּיחַ] דַּעְתּוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ, תְּפִילִּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן אַזְכָּרוֹת הַרְבֵּה, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says: A person is obligated to touch** his **phylacteries** regularly for **the entire time** that he is wearing them. This is derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the **frontplate** of the High Priest, as follows: **And if** with regard to the **frontplate, which has only one mention** of God's name, **the Torah states: "And it should be always upon his forehead"** (Exodus 28:38), which means that the High Priest must always be aware that the frontplate is placed on his head and **that he should not be distracted from it,** then with regard to **phylacteries, which have numerous mentions** of God's name, **all the more so** one must always be aware of them.

The *gemara* states: *kol ha-meniach tefillin achar shki'at ha-chama over b'asei* — anyone who puts on *tefillin* after sunset has transgressed a positive commandment. This is Rabbi Elazar's position, grounded in the *pasuk* of *ve-shamarta*. Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *over b'lav* — one transgresses a negative prohibition, based on the use of *shimur* in the sense of *lo ta'aseh*, comparable to *shamarta et yom ha-Shabbat*. The *gemara* then considers whether Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan are in fact disputing the principle of *Rabbi Avin* citing *Rabbi Ila'a* — namely, that wherever the Torah uses the language of *hishamer*, *pen*, or *al*, it signifies a negative prohibition. The *gemara* concludes that this is not the crux of their dispute; rather, they differ on whether *hishamer d'asei* — a *shimur* that introduces a positive obligation — also carries the force of a *lav*.

### Lishmoran

There is an important exception: *d'im lishomram mutar* — if one keeps *tefillin* on after nightfall in order to protect them, it is permitted. If one is traveling at night and fears that removing the *tefillin* will result in their loss or damage — that they cannot be safely kept except by remaining on the body — then wearing them into the night is permitted under these circumstances.

### Halacha Without Moring

*Ravina* recounts that he once sat before Rav Ashi when it grew dark, and Rav Ashi put on his *tefillin*. When asked whether he was doing so *lishmuran* — to protect them — Rav Ashi confirmed that he was. But Ravina could discern from his manner — *ve-chazinan be-da'atei d'lav lishmuran hu ba'ei* — that protecting the *tefillin* was not in fact his intent. The explanation offered is that Rav Ashi held *halacha v'ein morin kein* — this is the *halacha*, but we do not teach it publicly. He genuinely held that *layla zman tefillin*, that night is a permitted time for *tefillin*, but he did not wish to broadcast this ruling lest it lead people to sleep while wearing them and thereby come to *pacha gas*. There is a textual variant — the *Tzeidah Laderech* switches the *girsa* to indicate that Rav Ashi simply did not remove *tefillin* he was already wearing, rather than actively putting on new ones — which would bring the story into conformity with earlier statements that everyone agrees one does not actively put on *tefillin* anew at night, even according to those who are lenient about keeping them on.

The category of *halacha v'ein morin kein* raises profound questions about the rabbinic communication of *halacha*. The approach is not to misrepresent the *halacha*, but to decline to publicize it — a kind of deliberate reticence — when the concern is that broader knowledge of the lenient ruling will lead to laxity and violation. A parallel case appears in *Beitzah*, where an *Amora* asked about turning off a light on *Yom Tov* for a couple seeking privacy, and kept deflecting with alternative suggestions until cornered into declaring the act *assur* — which some *Rishonim* understand as a case of *halacha v'ein morin kein* in which he held a lenient view but could not bring himself to state it openly.

### Mashmesh Concept

The *gemara* records: *chayav adam le-mashmesh be-tefillin be-chol sha'ah* — a person is obligated to constantly be in awareness of his *tefillin* at every moment. The proof comes from a *kal va-chomer* from the *tzitz*: the *tzitz* contains only one mention of God's name, *Kodesh l'Hashem*, yet the Torah says *ve-haya al mitzcho tamid* — it shall be on his forehead constantly. From the word *tamid* we learn *she-lo tassiach da'atenu mimenu* — he must not let his awareness wander from it. *Tefillin*, which contain many mentions of God's name, must certainly require this constant consciousness. The term *mashmesh* — touching or feeling — is a technique, a physical gesture that reinforces mental awareness; the underlying obligation is to remain consciously present to the fact that one is wearing *tefillin* and to what that wearing signifies. This connects back to the *ot* framework: the *tzitz* is not worn when entering the *Kodesh Kodashim* because at that moment one is in the direct presence of what the *tzitz* is meant to remind one of. Similarly, on *Shabbat*, the day itself accomplishes what *tefillin* are designed to accomplish.

## Which Hand

### Yad Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יָדְךָ״ – זוֹ שְׂמֹאל. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שְׂמֹאל, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יָמִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַף יָדִי יָסְדָה אֶרֶץ וִימִינִי טִפְּחָה שָׁמָיִם״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יָדָהּ לַיָּתֵד תִּשְׁלַחְנָה וִימִינָהּ לְהַלְמוּת עֲמֵלִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״לָמָּה תָשִׁיב יָדְךָ וִימִינֶךָ מִקֶּרֶב חֵיקְךָ כַלֵּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "And it shall be for a sign for you on **your arm [*yadkha*]"** (Exodus 13:9), that **this** is referring to the **left** arm. **Do you say** it means the **left** arm, **or is it only** the **right** arm? **The verse states: "Even My hand [*yadi*] has laid the foundation of the earth, and My right hand [*vimini*] has spread out the heavens"** (Isaiah 48:13). **And** another verse **states: "Her hand [*yadah*] she put to the tent pin, and her right hand [*viminah*] to the workmen's hammer"** (Judges 5:26), **and** another verse **states: "Why do You withdraw Your hand [*yadkha*], even Your right hand [*viminekha*]? Draw it out of Your bosom and consume them"** (Psalms 74:11). All these verses employ the term *yad* with regard to the left hand, and use the term *yamin*, literally, right, without the term *yad*, to indicate the right hand.

The *gemara* turns to the specific *halachot* of how *tefillin* are worn. The first question is which hand. A *braita* establishes: *yadcha — zo smol* — "your hand" means the left. The *gemara* offers scriptural support: *af yadi yis'dah aretz vi-ymini tipchah shamayim* — my "hand" founded the earth, while my "right" formed the heavens — demonstrating that *yad* and *yamin* are distinct terms, with *yad* denoting the left. A second proof comes from the account of *Yael* killing *Sisra*: *yadah layated tishlachnah vi-yminah le-halmut amelim* — her *yad* reached for the tent-peg while her right hand held the hammer. Since both hands are occupied simultaneously, they must be different hands, and since one is specified as *yemin* (right), the *yad* must be the left.

Rabbi Yose HaChareim objects: we find *yamin* itself called *yad*, as in *vayar Yosef ki yashit aviv yad yemino* — Joseph saw that his father placed his right *yad* on Ephraim. The *gemara* responds: *yad yemino ikrei* — with the modifier "right," *yad* can mean the right hand. But *yad stam* — the unmodified word *yad* — would not on its own denote the right hand.

### R. Natan Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״ ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ – מָה כְּתִיבָה בְּיָמִין, אַף קְשִׁירָה בְּיָמִין, וְכֵיוָן דִּקְשִׁירָה בְּיָמִין – הַנָּחָה בִּשְׂמֹאל הִיא. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַחוֹרָם, הַנָּחָה דְּבִשְׂמֹאל מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says:** This proof is **not necessary,** as **it says: "And you shall bind them** for a sign upon your arm" (Deuteronomy 6:8), and then it states: **"And you shall write them** upon the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:9). This teaches that **just as writing is with** the **right** hand, as most people write with their right hands, **so too,** the **binding** of phylacteries must be performed **with** the **right** hand. **And since binding is with** the **right** hand, this means that **donning is on** the **left** arm, as one cannot bind the phylacteries with the same hand upon which he is donning them. The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei HaḤorem,** who holds that the right hand is also called *yad* in the Torah, **derive that donning** phylacteries is **on** the **left** arm? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from where Rabbi Natan derives it.**

Rabbi Natan takes a different approach: *eino tzarich* — one need not derive it from those verses at all. The *pasuk* itself says *u-keshartem u-chesavtem* — you shall bind and you shall write. Just as writing (*kesiva*) is done with the right hand — since most people are right-handed — so too the binding (*keshira*) is done with the right hand. If binding is performed with the right hand, then *hanacha bi-smol* — the placement must be on the left, because you are binding it *onto* your left arm with your right hand. This derivation is strikingly different from the others because it shifts the emphasis: the point is not primarily which body part *receives* the *tefillin*, but rather which hand *performs* the act of binding. The *mitzvah* is framed as an act of *kesharim* — tying — performed with one's dominant hand.

### R. Akiva Spelling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִ״יָּדְכָה״ כְּתִיב, בְּהֵ״י כֵּהָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֵימָא ״יָדְךָ״ שֶׁבְּכֹחַ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי כְּתִיב בְּחֵי״ת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The requirement that phylacteries be donned on the left arm is derived **from** the verse: "It shall be for a sign upon **your arm [*yadkha*]"** (Exodus 13:16), which **is written with** a letter ***heh*** at the end. This is expounded as though it stated: Your **weak [*keha*]** arm. **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi: But** one can **say** that *yadkha* should be interpreted as *yadko'aḥ*, with a letter *ḥet* at the end instead of a *heh*. If so, this would mean: **Your arm that is of strength [*shebeko'aḥ*],** which is the right arm. Rav Ashi **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Is** this word **written with a *ḥet*?**

Rabbi Akiva derives the placement from the spelling of *yadcha*: *mi-yadcha*. The word is written *kaf hey* — with a *heh* — rather than *kaf* alone. Reading the *heh* as indicating *kehah* — weakness — the verse implies *yad kehah*, the weak hand. Since most people are right-handed, their weak hand is the left, and that is where *tefillin* are placed.

The *gemara* records a challenge from Rava to Rav Ashi: perhaps the *kaf hey* should be read as if written *kaf chet* — implying *koach*, strength — your strong hand? Rav Ashi responds: *mi ksiv be-chet?* — it is not written with a *chet*; the spelling with a *heh* is deliberate and indicates weakness.

### Lefty Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִטֵּר מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין בִּימִינוֹ, שֶׁהוּא שְׂמֹאלוֹ. וְהָתַנְיָא: מַנִּיחַ בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ שֶׁהוּא שְׂמֹאלוֹ שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם! אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא בְּשׁוֹלֵט בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A left-handed person dons phylacteries on his right** arm, **which is** equivalent to **his left** arm, i.e., his weaker arm. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that a left-handed person **dons** phylacteries **on his left** arm, **which is the left** arm **of every** other **person? Abaye said: When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is referring **to one who has** equal **control with both his hands,** i.e., an ambidextrous person. Since such an individual also uses his right hand, he dons phylacteries on his left arm.

A *tanna'itic* dispute follows. One *braita* rules that a left-handed person (*itter*) places *tefillin* on his right hand — which is *his* weak hand — while another *braita* appears to say that even a left-handed person places *tefillin* on the left hand, as is the norm for everyone. Abaye resolves the contradiction: *ki tanya hi bi-ktu'ei bishtei yadav* — the latter *braita* speaks of an ambidextrous person. Since such a person has no genuinely weak hand, we default to the conventional norm — the objective left hand — rather than attempting to identify a subjectively weak one. For a true left-handed person, the ruling of the first *braita* applies: *tefillin* go on the right, because that is his *yad kehah*.

### Tosafot Balance

*Tosafot* raise a nuanced complication. The *gemara* has two partially overlapping derivations — *u-keshartem* (bind with the right hand, place on the left) and *yad kehah* (the weak hand). For a pure lefty, both lead to the same result: he writes with the left, so his binding hand is the left, and his weak hand is the right, meaning *tefillin* go on the right. But what about a person who writes with his right hand but performs most other tasks with his left? *Tosafot* note the uncertainty: from the perspective of *u-keshartem*, such a person would be treated as right-handed and would place *tefillin* on the left. From the perspective of *yad kehah*, the question of which is actually his weaker hand is less clear. *Tosafot* suggest that since the determination based on *u-keshartem* is unambiguous — writing is the primary indicator of handedness — and since even from the perspective of *yad kehah* the answer is debatable, one tips toward the conventional ruling and treats this person as right-handed, placing *tefillin* on the left. The harder case is the reverse: someone who writes with the left but does most other activities with the right, since there *u-keshartem* would point toward the left hand as dominant.

### Action vs Wearing

This entire discussion crystallizes a fundamental conceptual question: is *tefillin* primarily a *mitzvah* of *kesharim* — binding — or a *mitzvah* of *levisha* — wearing? Framing it as a *mitzvah* of *u-keshartem* shifts the focus: the real *ma'aseh mitzvah* is the act of tying, performed with the dominant hand, and the placement on the opposite arm is a consequence of that act. Framing it as *yad kehah* places the emphasis on the wearing — on which body part the *tefillin* must be worn. Most of the time these two framings converge on the same practical result. The divergence emerges precisely in the edge cases — the ambidextrous person, the person who writes with one hand but functions dominantly with the other — and in those cases the conceptual orientation becomes *halachically* determinative.

## Missing Limbs

### Gidem Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״יָדְכָה״ בְּהֵ״י – זוֹ שְׂמֹאל, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״יָדְךָ״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגִּידֵּם. תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין לוֹ זְרוֹעַ – פָּטוּר מִן הַתְּפִילִּין, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״יָדְכָה״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגִּידֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that Rav Ashi's opinion, that the *halakha* that phylacteries are donned on the left arm is derived from the term *yadkha*, is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: ***Yadkha*** is written **with a *heh*,** indicating weakness, and **this** is referring to the **left** arm. **Others say: "Your arm,"** i.e., *yadkha*, serves **to include one without** a complete **arm,** i.e., one whose arm ends at the elbow, in the obligation to don phylacteries, as the remaining part is also categorized as a weak arm. It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If one **does not have** a left **arm,** i.e., not even above the elbow, he is **exempt from the** mitzva of **phylacteries. Others say: *Yadkha*** serves **to include one without** a left **arm** even above the elbow, teaching that he must don phylacteries on his right arm.

Two *beraitot* are cited with apparently conflicting rulings: one holds *ein lo zeroa patur min ha-tefillin* — if one lacks an arm, one is exempt from *tefillin*; another position (cited as *acherim omerim*) derives from the spelling of *yadcha* with a *kaf heh* that even a *gidem* remains *chayav* — since the verse expressly includes those lacking a hand, the obligation persists wherever there is still a viable *makom hanachah*.

### Rema Ruling

The *Rema* in *Siman* 27 rules: *gidem she-ein lo yad rak zeroa — yani'ach be-lo berachah* — a person who lacks the hand but retains the upper arm places *tefillin* without a *bracha*. The *Mishnah Berurah* clarifies the limits of this ruling: if the person does not have even the part of the upper arm muscle (*kiboret*) where *tefillin* are normally placed, then placing *tefillin* on the remaining stump is not meaningful, and the person does not wear *shel yad* at all. The underlying logic is that without a viable *makom hanachah*, there is no obligation to place, and placing without such a location serves no purpose.

### Shel Rosh Pivot

If the weak arm is entirely absent, the *halacha* pivots to *shel rosh*. Since *shel yad* and *shel rosh* are *einan me'akvin zeh et zeh* — they are not interdependent — the person still has a full *mitzvah* of *shel rosh*, and wears it with both *brachot*. This itself is a significant ruling: the loss of the weak arm creates an *ones* with respect to *shel yad* — the person is exempt from what they cannot perform — but does not diminish the obligation regarding the head *tefillin*.

### Rav Moshe Detail

Rav Moshe Feinstein was asked about a person with paralysis in the weak arm. The question is whether paralysis renders the arm legally absent — equivalent to the *gidem* case — or whether it is treated as an arm that is fully present despite its dysfunction. Rav Moshe reasons that paralysis in the arm is typically not caused by a pathology *in the arm itself* — *ela mitsad machala she-ba-moach* — but rather in the brain or spinal cord. The arm retains its blood supply — *barur she-motzi dam harbeh* — and is therefore not comparable to a shriveled limb that has lost circulation. Where a limb has truly atrophied to the point of having no blood flow, Rav Moshe holds that even physical presence does not constitute a *zeroa* for purposes of this *mitzvah*; one would not make a *bracha*. But paralysis — where the arm is present, healthy in terms of circulation, and simply cannot be moved — does not reduce the arm to the status of *chasar* (absent). Such a person remains *chayav* in *tefillin shel yad* and wears them with a *bracha*.

### Father's Testament

In the final lines of this responsum, Rav Moshe adds a deeply personal note: *zecharono zecharuni* — his memory came back to him. His father, *hagaon zatzal*, was paralyzed on his left side during the last seven months of his life — *machalat ha-paraliz* — and nonetheless wore *tefillin* on his paralyzed left arm throughout that period. This personal testimony undergirds and authenticates the ruling: the principle that presence with loss of function still obligates was not merely a theoretical position for Rav Moshe, but one that his own father embodied in the most intimate circumstances.

## Body Placement

### Hand Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״יָדְךָ״ זוֹ קִיבּוֹרֶת, מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עַל יָדְךָ״ – זוֹ גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁבַּיָּד. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: זוֹ גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁבַּיָּד, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא עַל יָדְךָ מַמָּשׁ? אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הַנַּח תְּפִילִּין בַּיָּד וְהַנַּח תְּפִילִּין בָּרֹאשׁ, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּגוֹבַהּ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ – אַף כָּאן בְּגוֹבַהּ שֶׁבַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of the *baraita*: **The Master says: "On your arm"; this** is the **bicep.** The term *yad* can mean either hand or arm. Therefore, the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As the Sages taught: "On your arm [*yadkha*]"; this** is the **upper** part **of the arm. Do you say** that **this** is the **upper** part **of the arm, or** is it **only literally on your** actual **hand,** i.e., on the palm of the hand? **The Torah says: Don phylacteries on the *yad* and don phylacteries on the head; just as there,** with regard to the head, it means **on** the **upper** part **of the head,** as will be explained, **so too here,** it means **on** the **upper** part **of the arm.**

The *gemara* establishes where precisely on the arm *tefillin* are placed. *Al yadecha — zu govah she-ba-yad* — "your hand" in the context of *tefillin* refers to the elevated part of the hand, namely the upper arm muscle, the *kiboret*. The *gemara* raises the possibility that *al yadecha mamash* might mean literally the palm, but rejects this through a *hekkesh*: the same way the placement on the head is at its height — a point to be established from the *pasuk* on head *tefillin* — so too placement on the hand is at its height, which is the upper arm. Rabbi Yitzchak offers a corroborating derivation: *ve-samtem et devarai eileh al levavchem — u-keshartem* — it should be placed *kineged ha-lev*, adjacent to the heart. This is another convergent reason why *tefillin* go on the upper arm, on the left side, closest to the heart.

### Two Heads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ פְּלֵימוֹ מֵרַבִּי: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי רָאשִׁים, בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹ קוּם גְּלִי, אוֹ קַבֵּל עֲלָךְ שַׁמְתָּא. אַדְּהָכִי אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיתְיְלִיד לִי יָנוֹקָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּרֵי רֵישֵׁי, כַּמָּה בָּעֵינָא לְמִיתַּב לְכֹהֵן? אֲתָא הָהוּא סָבָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ: חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sage **Peleimu raised a dilemma before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: In the case of **one who has two heads, on which of them does he don phylacteries?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Either get up** and **exile** yourself from here **or accept upon yourself excommunication** for asking such a ridiculous question. **In the meantime, a certain man arrived** and **said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: A firstborn **child has been born to me who has two heads. How much** money **must I give to the priest** for the redemption of the firstborn? **A certain elder came** and **taught him: You are obligated to give him ten *sela*,** the requisite five for each head.

The *gemara* presents one of its more striking exchanges in a discussion of where *tefillin* are placed on the head. *Pleimo* — whose name may itself derive from the Aramaic root *plai*, meaning to jest or mock — asked Rebbi: *mi she-yesh lo shenei rashim, be-eizeh meihem meniach tefillin?* If someone had two heads, on which does one place *tefillin*? Rebbi was deeply offended by the question, offering *Pleimo* the choice of either going into exile (*goleh*) or accepting *niddui*, and declaring it a disgraceful question unworthy of serious treatment.

The irony is that almost immediately a man arrived and reported to Rebbi: *islyid li yenuka de-is lei terei reishi* — he had just had a newborn with two heads. He wished to know how much to give the *kohen* for *pidyon ha-ben*, since the *pasuk* specifies payment *la-gulgolet* — per head — and a *bechor* with two heads might seem to require ten *shekalim* rather than five. An elder appeared and taught a *braita*: such a person is *chayav litten asara zuz*. Rebbi was left without a response: the case he had dismissed as absurd had both occurred in practice and been codified.

The *gemara* then notes a *braita* of Rami bar Chama raising whether a multiply-anomalous infant might be considered a *treifa* and thus exempt from *pidyon*, citing the verse *padot tifdeh et bechor ha-adam* with the limitation *ach* — implying not in all cases. The *gemara*'s response is *shani hacha de-vegulgolet tela Rachmana* — the Torah tied the obligation to the *gulgolet*, the head, and if there are two viable heads, one pays per head. The fate of the original question — which of two heads receives *tefillin* — remains technically unresolved.

### Head Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ, מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״ – זוֹ גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר זוֹ גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ מַמָּשׁ? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״לֹא תָשִׂימוּ קׇרְחָה בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם לָמֵת״. מָה לְהַלָּן בְּגוֹבַהּ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ, מְקוֹם שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה קׇרְחָה – אַף כָּאן בְּגוֹבַהּ שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, מְקוֹם שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה קׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement of the *baraita* that the phylacteries of the head are donned on the **upper** part **of the head,** the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As the Sages taught: "Between your eyes"** (Exodus 13:9); **this** is the **upper** part **of the head. Do you say** that **this** is the **upper** part **of the head, or is it only literally between your eyes? It is stated here: "Between your eyes," and it is stated there:** "You shall not cut yourselves, **nor make any baldness between your eyes for the dead"** (Deuteronomy 14:1), **Just as there,** the phrase "between your eyes" is referring to a place **on** the **upper** part **of the head,** as that is **a place where** one can **render** himself **bald** by removing his hair, **so too,** the place where phylacteries are donned is **on** the **upper** part **of the head, a place where** one can **render** himself **bald.**

The *gemara* establishes where on the head *tefillin* are placed. *Bein einecha — zu govah she-ba-rosh* — "between your eyes" means the height of the head, not literally the space between the eyes. The derivation proceeds through *gezeirah shavah*: the same phrase *bein einecha* appears in the prohibition of making a *korcha* (*lo tasimu korcha bein eineichem la-met*). Since the *korcha* prohibition refers to making a bald spot where hair grows from — which is on the top of the skull, not at eye level — so too the placement of *tefillin bein einecha* is at the height of the head, at the place where hair grows. *Mah le-halan be-govah she-be-rosh, mekom she-oseh korcha, af kan be-govah she-be-rosh, mekom she-oseh korcha.*

The *gemara* further grounds this in the observation attributed to the academy of Rabbi Yannai: the location is *makom she-moach shel tinok rofes* — where the fontanelle of an infant pulsates, which is higher up on the skull. The common practice is to place the bottom edge of the *tefillin shel rosh* at the hairline, with the body of the *tefillin* resting above it.

## Privacy & Visibility

### R. Eliezer Ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה לְךָ לְאוֹת״, לְךָ לְאוֹת וְלֹא לַאֲחֵרִים לְאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** This proof is **not necessary, as** the verse **states: "And it shall be for a sign for you** upon your arm" (Exodus 13:9), which teaches: It shall be **a sign for you, but not a sign for others,** i.e., one must don the phylacteries of the arm in a place where they are not seen by others. This is the arm, which is usually covered, whereas the hand is usually visible.

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Eliezer's view in the context of establishing the placement of *tefillin shel yad* on the upper arm. *Ve-haya lecha le-ot — lecha le-ot ve-lo le-acherim le-ot* — it shall be a sign for *you*, not a sign for others. This principle militates against the palm as a placement location: the palm, being naturally visible to others, would make the *ot* public, whereas the Torah frames it as a personal sign. The upper arm, by contrast, is positioned against the body and is less inherently visible, making it the appropriate location for a sign intended primarily for the wearer's own consciousness.

### Covering Custom

This principle of *lecha le-ot* generates the widespread *minhag* to cover *shel yad* when wearing it. In practice, one typically either pulls the sleeve down over the *tefillin* or places a small protective cover — a plastic casing — over the *shel yad*. The reason for the casing is pragmatic: pulling a sleeve repeatedly over the *tefillin* can rub away the sharp edges and compromise the required *ribuah* (squareness). The casing preserves the *tefillin* while still keeping them from being prominently visible. Strictly speaking, based on the continuation of the *sugya*, covering the *tefillin* is not a formal *halachic* obligation — it reflects the location principle of *lecha le-ot* — but it is the recognized and accepted *minhag*.

### Wound Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְשַׂמְתֶּם אֶת דְּבָרַי אֵלֶּה עַל לְבַבְכֶם ... וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״, שֶׁתְּהֵא שִׂימָה כְּנֶגֶד הַלֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** This proof is **not necessary, as** the verse **states: "Therefore you shall place these words in your heart** and in your soul, **and you shall bind them"** (Deuteronomy 11:18). This teaches **that placing** the words, i.e., donning the phylacteries, **shall be opposite the heart,** on the bicep.

The *gemara* records that Rav Ashi was once sitting before *Ameimar* and had a wound on his arm, causing him to tear his sleeve to relieve pressure on the injury. As a result, his *tefillin* became visible. *Ameimar* asked: does the Rav not hold by *lachem le-ot ve-lo le-acherim le-ot*? Rav Ashi responded: *be-makom "lachem le-ot" itmar* — the principle refers to the *place* where *tefillin* are worn, not to an independent obligation to cover them at all times. The requirement is that the *tefillin* be located in a spot that is inherently private — the upper arm — not that they be covered at every moment regardless of circumstance. When visibility occurs incidentally, as through a torn sleeve necessitated by a wound, no violation has occurred.

## Body Placement…

### R. Yehuda Negaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״הַנַּח תְּפִילִּין בַּיָּד״, ״הַנַּח תְּפִילִּין בָּרֹאשׁ״, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּמָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לִיטָּמֵא בְּנֶגַע אֶחָד, אַף כָּאן בְּמָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לִיטָּמֵא בְּנֶגַע אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This proof is **not necessary,** as **the Torah says: Don phylacteries on the arm** and **don phylacteries on the head. Just as there,** with regard to the phylacteries of the arm, it is referring **to a place which is fit to become ritually impure with** only **one** type of leprous **mark,** that of the skin, **so too here,** with regard to the phylacteries of the head, it is referring **to a place which is fit to become ritually impure with** only **one** type of leprous **mark,** that of a place of hair (see Leviticus 13:29–37).

Rabbi Yehuda offers an alternative derivation for the placement on the head, distinct from the *korcha* analogy. He reasons through an implicit *hekkesh* between the arm and the head: just as *tefillin* on the arm are placed where skin is subject to a single category of *negaim* — *or ha-basar*, the skin alone — so too *tefillin* on the head must be placed where only one category of *negaim* applies. If *tefillin* were placed literally between the eyes, the site would encompass both skin (*or ha-basar*) and the hair of the eyebrows, generating two distinct categories of *negaim*. The proper location on the head — where only *negei se'ar* apply — is the place where hair grows on the skull, which is the upper part of the head, not the forehead between the eyes. Rabbi Yehuda thus arrives at the same halakhic conclusion through a different *limud*, using the laws of *Negaim* as the terminological framework for defining which anatomical zones are in play.

## Conclusion & Next

### Tefillin Summary

With this we conclude the discussion of *tefillin* in Menachot. The *sugya* has traversed a remarkable range of questions: when in the day *tefillin* may be worn and when they must be removed; whether the operative prohibition at night is *de-Oraita* or rabbinic; how *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* are excluded through the *ot* principle; the precise placement on the body — arm and head — derived through comparison to *negaim*, *korcha*, and the heart; which hand bears the obligation, and how handedness affects the ruling for the lefty and the ambidextrous; the status of the *gidem*; the requirement of continuous *mashmesh* — mental awareness — while wearing *tefillin*; and the principle of *lecha le-ot*, a sign for the self rather than for public display. Running through all these discussions is the recurring tension between *tefillin* as a *mitzvah* of *kesharim* — an act of binding — and *tefillin* as a *mitzvah* of *levisha* — a state of wearing.

### Tzitzit Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתְּכֵלֶת אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת הַלָּבָן, וְהַלָּבָן אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַתְּכֵלֶת. תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל יָד אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, וְשֶׁל רֹאשׁ אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת שֶׁל יָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The absence of **the sky-blue [*tekhelet*]** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of ritual fringes with **the white** strings, **and the** absence of **white** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with **the sky-blue** strings. If one has only one, he wears it without the other. Absence of the **phylacteries of** the **arm does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries **of** the **head, and** absence of the phylacteries **of** the **head does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries **of** the **arm.** If one has only one, he dons it without the other.

This *Mishnah* introduces the concept of *me'akev* — interdependence. Normally, when a *mitzvah* consists of multiple components, the absence of one component might invalidate the others. Here the *Mishnah* teaches that the pairs listed are *einan me'akvin zeh et zeh* — each can stand independently. The first pair, *techelet* and *lavan*, are the two threads of *tzitzit*: the blue-dyed thread and the white. Since the identity of the *chilazon* — the creature from which *techelet* was produced — has been lost, the ruling that *techelet* is not *me'akev lavan* is of profound practical importance, enabling the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* to be fully observed today with white threads alone. The second pair reaffirms what the *sugya* has just established about *tefillin*: *shel yad* and *shel rosh* are independent obligations, and if one is unavailable for any reason — whether through absence of a limb or any other circumstance — the other retains its full obligatory force.
