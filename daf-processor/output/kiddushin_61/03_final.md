# Kiddushin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Al Menat Conditions

### From Now Until 30 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶתֵּן לְךָ מִכָּאן וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָאו תְּנָאָה הוּא, וּלְזָרוֹזֵיהּ קָאָמְרָ[ה], קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me **on the condition that I will give you** a particular sum of money **from now** and until thirty days have elapsed, if he gives her the money within the allotted time she is betrothed. The Gemara questions the need for this ruling: It is **obvious** that this is the case. The Gemara explains: This ruling is necessary, **lest you say** that the specified time period **is not a** proper **condition, and he said** so only **to motivate himself** to prove the sincerity of his intentions. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the time period is an essential part of his condition, and if he fails to give her the money within this period the betrothal is void.

The *daf* opens toward the bottom of 61b, a little before the *mishnah*, picking up a theme established in the preceding discussion. There we had been dealing with a man who says "this is your *get* from now and after thirty days," where a range of possibilities arose concerning whether the formula meant the *get* took effect immediately, whether there was ambiguity about the intended moment, or whether it described some ongoing mode of effectuation. The comparison was to *kiddushin*, not *get*, and the preceding *mishnah* had established the principle *kol ha'omer al menat ke'omer me'achshav damei*: whoever uses the *al menat* formulation is treated as though he said "from now." That position is clearly associated with Rebbe, and while there is a minority voice suggesting the matter is subject to debate, the overwhelming weight of evidence treats it as a consensus ruling.

We now turn to the next case: *al menat she'etayn lecha mi'kan v'ad shloshim yom* — "I am betrothing you on the condition that I give you two hundred *zuz* from now until thirty days." He must give the money within thirty days, or the *kiddushin* does not take effect. The *Gemara* raises the possibility that this is so obvious it need not be stated — *peshita*. The response: one might have thought that the phrase "within thirty days" is not strictly part of the condition itself but is rather an expression of self-encouragement, a kind of personal pledge to act promptly, while the operative *tnai* remains only the giving of two hundred *zuz*. The *Gemara* therefore clarifies that the time limit is indeed part of the condition itself, and failure to perform within thirty days leaves her not *mekudeshet*.

### Ownership of Zuz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז וְכוּ׳. וְנֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא יֵשׁ לוֹ! וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא יֵשׁ לוֹ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי וַדַּאי, הָא בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that if he says to her: You are hereby betrothed to me **on the condition that I have two hundred dinars,** if he in fact possesses the specified amount, she is betrothed, but if he does not, she is not betrothed. The Gemara asks: **And let us be concerned** that **perhaps he has** this sum, although he fails to show it to her. How can his betrothal be nullified if there is no conclusive evidence that he does not have the money? **And furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita* that **we are concerned** that **perhaps he has** the money. The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult,** as **this** case, in the mishna, is referring **to a definite betrothal,** which is not established in this case, as he has failed to demonstrate that he has the funds. By contrast, **that** case, in the *baraita*, is referring to **an uncertain betrothal.** The betrothal cannot be entirely disregarded, as he might have the money after all.

The *mishnah* then takes up: *al menat she'yesh li matayim zuz* — "on the condition that I have two hundred *zuz*." If he does indeed have two hundred *zuz*, she is married. The plain reading of the *mishnah* implies that if he does not have it, she is definitely not *mekudeshet*. But this raises an immediate difficulty: how does one prove a negative? If you look in his wallet and find nothing, how can you be certain there is no money hidden elsewhere? A *braita* makes this explicit — *chashinan shema yesh lo*: we are concerned that perhaps he does have two hundred *zuz* tucked away somewhere.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha b'kiddushei vadai, ha b'kiddushei safek* — the *mishnah* means she is not *definitely* married, while the *braita* clarifies she is not definitely *unmarried* either. When one cannot establish whether a *tnai* has been satisfied, the result is *safek mekudeshet* — a state of doubtful betrothal, which is of course the worst of both worlds. She cannot freely remarry without a *get*, yet she is not definitively bound to this man.

### Showing Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁאַרְאֵךְ מָאתַיִם זוּז וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא: לֹא נִתְכַּוְּנָה אֶלָּא לִרְאוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. וְאִם הֶרְאָה לָהּ עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּנָקֵט דְּמֵי בְּעִיסְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if a man says to her: You are hereby betrothed to me **on the condition that I will show you two hundred dinars,** she is betrothed and he must show her the money. A Sage **taught** (*Tosefta* 3:6): This woman **intended to see only his own** money, not simply to be shown any two hundred dinars. With regard to the subsequent ruling of the mishna: **And if he showed her** the money **on the table she is not betrothed,** the Gemara comments: This is **obvious,** as the woman wants to see his own money. The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach **that even if he holds** somebody else's **money as** part of a **joint** business **venture,** nevertheless, as the money does not actually belong to him he has failed to fulfill the condition.

The *mishnah* next addresses *al menat she'arech matayim zuz* — "on the condition that I show you two hundred *zuz*." If he shows her the money, she is married. But the *Gemara* immediately adds: *tana lo niskaven ela l'harot mishelo* — the implicit meaning of "show you" is "show you that I have it," specifically from his own funds. He cannot show her money belonging to others and count that as fulfillment of the condition.

This is a significant interpretive point. Technically the words "show you" were satisfied — he showed her money. Yet the *Gemara* reads in an implicit qualification that he never explicitly stated: the money must be his own. This is not *devarim shebalev* — a subjective, unexpressed intention that the law generally disregards — because there is an objectively understood context that any normal person would appreciate. When someone says "I will show you two hundred *zuz*" in the context of betrothal, the universal understanding is that he is demonstrating his capacity to provide, not merely producing coins that happen to be nearby.

The *Gemara* then rules: *v'im her'ah al hashulchan einah mekudeshet* — if he showed her money on the table of a money-changer, she is not *mekudeshet*. *Peshita* — obviously. The case that actually requires the ruling is the following: *d'af al gav d'nakat damei b'iska* — he is holding money given to him to invest. Here there is a genuine *hava amina*: the money is not wholly unrelated to him; he has investment rights, will earn a share of the profits, and in a sense manages the funds. Nevertheless, since it was never his money to begin with — it was entrusted to him by others for investment purposes — showing it does not satisfy the condition. His demonstrating that he oversees a large investment portfolio does not constitute showing that he has two hundred *zuz* of his own.

### Land as Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר וְכוּ׳. עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וּנֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא יֵשׁ לוֹ! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אָמַר לַהּ: מַאי נָפְקָא לָךְ מִינַּהּ, אֲנָא טָרַחְנָא וּמַיְיתֵינָא! קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me **on the condition that I possess** tillable land of **a *beit kor* of earth, she is betrothed,** provided that **he possesses** such land. If he said to her that the betrothal is: **On the condition that I possess** land **in such and such a place, if he possesses** land **in that place she is betrothed, but if not she is not betrothed.** The Gemara questions the need for this ruling: It is **obvious** that if he does not possess land in that place she is not betrothed, as that was precisely his condition. The Gemara explains that this ruling is necessary **lest you say** that he can **say to her: What difference does it make to you** whether the field is in that location or elsewhere, as even if it is farther away **I will go to the trouble of bringing** the produce from the field to our house, so what loss have you incurred? The mishna therefore **teaches us** that despite this claim the betrothal is invalid, due to his failure to fulfill the condition.

The parallel case for land follows: *al menat she'yesh li beit kor afar* — on the condition that he has a plot of land measuring a *beit kor*, which *Rashi* defines as seventy-five thousand square *amot*. If he has it, she is *mekudeshet*; if he does not, she is not. When he specifies a location — *al menat she'yesh li b'makom ploni* — she is only married if the land is in that precise location. Land elsewhere does not satisfy the condition. The *Gemara* addresses the possibility that he might argue: the land is functionally equivalent, the only difference is distance, and he pledges to exert the extra effort in traveling to it — *ana tarchana u'maitina*. The *Gemara* rejects this: the location was part of the explicit condition, and he cannot discount what he stated clearly.

This raises a broader question that runs throughout this section: when does context allow us to *add* something unstated, and when does it allow us to *discount* something that was stated? When he says "show you" and we read in "mine," we are supplementing an implicit understanding that nobody would contest. But when something was stated explicitly — a specific location, a specific time frame — context alone does not suffice to say he did not really mean it or that no one should care. There is a meaningful distinction between using context to fill a gap and using it to override an express term. The latter requires a far higher threshold, and even if the deviation seems trivial in practice, the condition as stated remains binding.

### Showing Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁאַרְאֵךְ בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר וְכוּ׳. וְאִם הֶרְאָהּ בַּבִּקְעָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּנָקֵיט בְּדִיסְתּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches: If a one says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me **on the condition that I will show you a *beit kor* of earth,** she is betrothed, provided that he possesses such land. A Sage **taught** (*Tosefta* 3:6): The woman **intended to see only his own** land. The mishna further teaches: **And if he showed** her a plot of land **in a valley, she is not betrothed.** The Gemara asks: This is **obvious;** after all, this land does not belong to him. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where he maintains** the field **as a sharecropper [*distora*],** i.e., he owns a share in it. Nevertheless, as the land does not belong to him she did not have this field in mind, and consequently she is not betrothed.

The *mishnah* also takes up *al menat she'arecha beit kor afar* — on the condition that he shows her a *beit kor* of land — with exactly the parallel structure to the money case. *Tana: lo niskaven ela leharot mishelo* — when he says "show you," he means show you mine. If he shows her land in a valley that he does not own, she is not *mekudeshet*. The operative *hava amina* here involves the case of a *distura* — a sharecropper. This is a stronger case than the money analogy, because the man never said "I'll show you my land" — he said "I'll show you a *beit kor* of land." One might argue that a sharecropper demonstrating that he has means of agricultural support for the family has in substance fulfilled the promise. Yet the *Gemara* rules that even here, the implicit meaning of "show you" is that it must be land he fully owns.

What is striking is the degree to which we read the word "mine" into a statement that never used it. He said he would show her; we infer he meant to demonstrate that he possesses. And the inference is so powerful that even partial ownership — sharecropping rights, investment stakes — does not suffice. The *tnai* was for full ownership, even though those exact words were never spoken.

## Beit Kor Definition

### Hekdesh & Crevices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי הֶקְדֵּשׁ תְּנַן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ שָׂדֵהוּ בִּשְׁעַת הַיּוֹבֵל, נוֹתֵן בְּזֶרַע חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים חֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף. הָיוּ נְקָעִים עֲמוּקִּים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים אוֹ סְלָעִים גְּבוֹהִים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים – אֵין נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – נִמְדָּדִין עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who consecrates his field during the time of the Jubilee** Year, i.e., in an era when the *halakhot* of the Jubilee Year are observed, if he wishes to redeem it from the Temple treasury **he gives fifty silver shekels for** an area that yields **a *ḥomer*,** i.e., a *kor*, of **barley seed.** If the field **had crevices,** i.e., deep fissures in its surface, **ten handbreadths deep, or boulders ten handbreadths high, they are not measured with it,** i.e., in the calculation of land that requires redemption. If the crevices or boulders are **less than that, they are measured with it.**

Having established that a *beit kor* is the relevant land measurement in the *kiddushin* case, the *Gemara* takes up a more fundamental question: what exactly constitutes a *beit kor*? The answer is less obvious than it first appears, because land is rarely perfectly flat, and the treatment of irregular topographical features — crevices and rocks — differs depending on the legal context in which the measurement arises.

The *Gemara* opens with the *hekdesh* case. When one sanctifies a field during the *yovel* period, the *Torah* establishes a fixed redemption rate: fifty *shekel kesef* for every plot of land on which one plants a *chomer* of barley seed — that is, for every *beit kor*. The relevant rule from the *mishnah* is: if the field contains crevices ten *tefachim* deep or rocks ten *tefachim* tall, they are not measured together with the field — *ein nimdadim imah*. If they are less than that, they are measured with it.

*Rashi* understands *nimdadim imah* to mean that the measurement literally follows the contour of the terrain — up and over the rock, down into the crevice — so that the total measured area includes those features. Others understand it more simply: features below the threshold are treated as if they are flat and included in the overall area, while those above the threshold are subtracted from the total.

The *Gemara* asks: why does it matter whether the crevices and rocks are included in the measurement or not? Even if they are excluded from the main field and treated as separate sanctified spaces, the owner said "I am sanctifying this land," and everything within the field's borders is in principle sanctified — either as part of the field or as its own unit. What is the *nafka mina*?

The answer lies in the redemption structure. The fixed fifty *shekel* rate applies per *beit kor*. If the field was exactly a *beit kor* and you subtract the crevice, you no longer have a *beit kor* subject to the fixed rate — you have a slightly-less-than-*beit kor* field and a separate crevice that is not a field at all. The slightly-smaller field gets redeemed at the proportional rate (which follows from the *Torah*'s use of the word *sadeh*, teaching that even partial plots are assessed proportionally). But the rock or water-filled crevice, as a non-plantable space, is not a *sadeh* and gets redeemed not at the standard rate but at market value.

*Mar Ukva bar Chama* then specifies: the entire discussion of *hekdesh* concerns crevices filled with water — *nikaymei d'maya* — which are not plantable. The comparison with rocks supports this: just as rocks cannot be planted, we are dealing with features that are inherently non-agricultural.

### Plantability Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן נָמֵי! הָנְהוּ נְאגָנֵי דְאַרְעָא מִיקְּרוּ, שִׁדְרָא דְאַרְעָא מִקְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the crevices, like the boulders, are unfit for sowing, then **even** if there is a disparity of **less than** ten handbreadths **as well,** the crevices and boulders should likewise not be measured as part of the field. The Gemara answers: If they are separated from the field by less than ten handbreadths, **these** crevices **are called the cracks in the ground.** Similarly, boulders less than ten handbreadths high **are called the spine of the ground.** They are considered regular features of fields, which typically have a few pits and mounds.

This introduces the governing principle for the *hekdesh* case: **plantability**. Whether a topographical feature is included in the *beit kor* or treated as a separate unit depends on whether it can be farmed. A crevice filled with water cannot be planted; if it is deep enough to constitute its own space (ten *tefachim*), it is not a *sadeh* and is excluded. If it is shallower, the *Gemara* explains: *hani nagnei d'ara mikru* — it is treated as a basin of the land, an integral topographical feature of an otherwise cultivable field. Similarly, a shorter rock is called *shidra d'ara* — the spine of the land — a natural feature within a plantable field, not a separate space.

The logic is coherent: a field need not be perfectly uniform in every square *amah* to be called a *sadeh*. Minor topographical variation is part of what a field is. But once a feature becomes deep or tall enough to constitute its own independent space — its own *reshut* — it is evaluated on its own terms. If those terms yield a non-plantable space, it falls outside the category of *sadeh* and is redeemed differently.

### Mechira Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי מֶכֶר תְּנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״בֵּית כּוֹר עָפָר אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״, וְהָיוּ שָׁם נְקָעִים עֲמוּקִּים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים אוֹ סְלָעִים גְּבוֹהִים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים – אֵין נִמְדָּדִים עִמָּהּ. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – נִמְדָּדִים עִמָּהּ. וְאָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מְלֵאִים מַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to a sale** of a field, **we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 102b): In the case of **one who says to another: I am selling you a *beit kor* of earth,** if **there were crevices ten handbreadths deep or boulders ten handbreadths high** in the field, **they are not measured with it;** if the crevices or boulders were **less than that, they are measured with it. And Mar Ukva bar Ḥama says: Even if they are not filled with water,** nevertheless they are not included.

The *Gemara* then turns to the corresponding rule in *Bava Batra* for a sale — *mechira* — which, as anticipated, follows a meaningfully different standard. The *mishnah* there addresses the case where one says *beit kor afar ani mocher lach* — "I am selling you a *beit kor* of land" — and the field contains crevices ten *tefachim* deep or rocks ten *tefachim* tall. There too, they are *ein nimdadim imah* — not measured with the land, and the seller must make up the shortfall with additional flat land.

But *Ukva bar Chama* rules: *af al pi she'ein mele'im mayim* — even if the crevices are not filled with water. Unlike the *hekdesh* case, where plantability is the criterion, here the exclusion applies even to a dry, theoretically plantable crevice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֶת מְעוֹתָיו בְּשָׂדֶה אַחַת וְיֵרָאֶה לוֹ כִּשְׁנַיִם וְכַשְּׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this difference between the two rulings of Mar Ukva bar Ḥama? Why in the case of a sale are crevices not considered part of the field even if they are not filled with water? **Rav Pappa says: Because a person** who buys a field **does not want to give his money for one field and** yet **it appears to him like two or three places.** When purchasing a parcel of land, it is important to the purchaser that the land be one contiguous unit so as to enable farming it without difficulty. Therefore, these areas of ten handbreadths are not measured as part of the field regardless of whether or not they are filled with water.

*Pappa* explains the rationale: *lafi she'ein adam rotzeh sheyiten et ma'otav b'sadeh achat v'teira'eh lo kishnayim ushlosha mekomot* — a person does not want to spend money on what he understands to be one field and then find himself working two or three separate spaces. Even if the deep crevice is technically cultivable, it requires separate attention, separate plowing, separate harvesting — a distinct agricultural operation. The buyer was expecting to purchase a coherent, unified working unit, and a field fragmented by deep crevices does not deliver that. The standard here is not formal plantability but the **reasonable expectation of the buyer** — what a person of ordinary sense would have meant and understood when agreeing to purchase a *beit kor*.

The underlying distinction between *hekdesh* and *mechira* is therefore conceptual. Redemption from *hekdesh* is governed by the *Torah*'s formal definition of a *sadeh*: so long as the land as a whole qualifies as a field — meaning it is predominantly plantable — the technical definition controls. There is no "meeting of minds," no subjective expectation to assess; the question is whether the land fits the Torah's category. A *mechira*, by contrast, is an agreement between two parties, and it is therefore governed by what each party reasonably understood the transaction to include. Economy of use, ease of harvest, and the coherence of the agricultural unit are all part of what the buyer was expecting to receive.

### Kiddushin Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי? לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ מְדַמֵּינַן לַהּ, אוֹ לְמֶכֶר מְדַמֵּינַן לַהּ? מִסְתַּבְּרָא לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ מְדַמֵּינַן לַהּ, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: אֲנָא טָרַחְנָא וְזָרַעְנָא וּמַיְיתֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed the *halakhot* of a field with regard to consecration and sales, the Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* **here,** with regard to measuring a field to see if it fulfills the condition stipulated by one who betroths a woman, if it contains large crevices that are not filled with water? Do **we compare it to** the *halakha* of **consecrated** property and include these places, **or do we compare it to** the *halakha* of **a sale,** which means that they are not included? The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **we compare it to** the case of **consecrated** property, **as** the husband can **say to her: I will go to the trouble of sowing and bringing** the produce from the lower or higher areas as well. Although the labor requires additional effort, he does possess a *beit kor* of land.

This sets up the *Gemara*'s question regarding our case: *hacha mai* — which standard applies when a man says "I will show you a *beit kor*" as a condition of *kiddushin*? Do we compare it to *hekdesh* or to *mechira*?

One might argue that *kiddushin* resembles *mechira*, since it too involves a meeting of minds between two parties, and the woman might reasonably have expected a field that she could work as a coherent unit rather than one fractured by deep crevices. This argument mirrors the one the *Gemara* just made on behalf of the buyer: she could say she did not anticipate the extra difficulty. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* rules that as long as the crevice is not filled with water and is therefore plantable, the condition is satisfied. The man can say: *ana tarchana u'zarna u'maitina* — I will invest the extra effort, I will plant the crevice, I will bring in the yield. If the land is plantable — even if demanding — he has demonstrated that he has a *beit kor* of agricultural land to his name, and the *tnai* is met.

This result is instructive alongside what was said earlier about location. When the condition was not satisfied — when he claimed land in location A and the land was actually in location B — he could not say "don't worry, I'll do the extra traveling." That is a case where the condition was genuinely unmet and he was proposing to compensate for its non-fulfillment. Here, by contrast, the question is definitional: *is* this a *beit kor* or not? Under the applicable standard — plantability — it is. He is not waiving or compensating for a failed condition; he is asserting that the condition has in fact been met under the correct measure, and that he will personally absorb whatever extra labor that entails.

## Double Condition

### Rabbi Meir Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל תְּנַאי שֶׁאֵינוֹ כִּתְנַאי בְּנֵי גָּד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן – אֵינוֹ תְּנַאי. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם אִם יַעַבְרוּ בְנֵי גָד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן״. וּכְתִיב: ״וְאִם לֹא יַעַבְרוּ חֲלוּצִים״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Meir says: Any condition that is not** doubled, i.e., which does not specify both the result of fulfilling the condition and the result of the condition remaining unfulfilled, **like the condition** Moses stipulated with **the children of Gad and the children of Reuben** who sought to settle on the eastern side of the Jordan, **is not** a valid **condition** and is not taken into account at all. **As it is stated: "And** Moses **said to them, if the children of Gad and the children of Reuben pass over** the Jordan with you, every man armed for battle before the Lord, and the land shall be subdued before you, then you shall give them the land of Gilead for a possession" (Numbers 32:29). **And it is written** afterward: **"But if they will not pass over armed** with you, they shall receive a possession among you in the land of Canaan" (Numbers 32:30).

The *Gemara* now moves to one of the most consequential debates in the laws of *tnai*: the doctrine of *Rabbi Meir* concerning the proper formulation of a conditional. *Rabbi Meir omer: kol tnai she'eino k'tnai bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven, eino tnai* — any condition that is not formulated like the condition that *Moshe* made with the tribes of Gad and Reuven is not a valid condition. If the condition is defective, it is simply ignored and the underlying act — the *kiddushin*, the sale, the *get* — takes effect unconditionally.

The biblical source is the negotiation in *Bemidbar* where *Moshe* tells the tribes of Gad and Reuven: if you go armed before your brothers, you will receive the land of *Gil'ad*. And it is written: *v'im lo ya'avru chalutzim* — and if the armed men do not cross over, then they will receive with you in the land of *Kena'an*. The positive side of the condition was stated, and then the negative side was explicitly stated as well. *Rabbi Meir* derives from this that a valid *tnai* requires the formulation of **both** consequences — the positive outcome if the condition is met and the negative outcome if it is not. This is the *tnai kaful*, the doubled condition.

### Logic Explanation

The logical basis for this requirement is genuine and worth noting. In propositional logic, "if P then Q" does not entail "if not P then not Q." What it entails is the contrapositive: "if not Q then not P." The two are not equivalent. If one says "you are betrothed to me if I give you two hundred *zuz*," that statement alone does not logically imply that if he fails to give the money she is not betrothed. Strictly speaking, the non-fulfillment of the antecedent leaves the consequent indeterminate. Only by stating the negative explicitly — "and if I do not give you two hundred *zuz*, you are not betrothed" — does the condition operate symmetrically and unambiguously govern both outcomes.

This is not how most people speak in ordinary conversation, but *Rabbi Meir* holds that when it comes to legal acts as powerful as *kiddushin* — where the *ma'aseh* carries enormous weight and its undoing requires extensive formulation — only a rigorously complete statement of the condition suffices to make the *ma'aseh* contingent.

### Three Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ הַדָּבָר לְאוֹמְרוֹ, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא כֵּן, יֵשׁ בַּמַּשְׁמָע שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן לֹא יִנְחָלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says:** One cannot derive the requirements of conditions in general from that particular case, as with regard to the nullification of the condition of the children of Gad and Reuben it was **necessary to state the matter, as otherwise,** if the verse had not specified both sides of the condition, **it might have** been thought it **meant that they will not inherit even in the land of Canaan.** One might have thought that if the tribes of Gad and Reuben would not fulfill the condition, they would forfeit their right to inherit anywhere. It was therefore necessary to specify that they would not lose their portion in Eretz Yisrael. Consequently, it is possible that with regard to a standard condition, where no such misunderstanding is likely to take place, it is not necessary to mention both sides.

*Rashi* identifies three requirements derived from the *tnai b'nei Gad u'vnei Reuven*, of which the *mishnah* places primary emphasis on the *tnai kaful*:

The first is *tnai kaful* — the double condition, as described. Both the positive and negative outcomes must be articulated.

The second is *tnai kodem l'ma'aseh* — the condition must be stated before the act. If he says "you are betrothed to me — if I give you two hundred *zuz*," the act (*kiddushin*) is stated first and the condition appended afterward. Since primacy has been given to the act, the condition is not strong enough to prevent it from taking effect. The underlying principle is that the *ma'aseh* is so powerful a legal event that in order for a condition to subordinate it completely, the conditionality must be established from the very outset. Stating the act first gives it an unqualified primacy that a subsequent condition cannot overcome.

The third requirement is *hein kodem l'lav* — the affirmative outcome must be stated before the negative. One must say "if I give you, you are betrothed; if I do not give you, you are not betrothed" — not the reverse. If one ends by stating the positive outcome, the act is again given the last word, imparting to it a kind of finality that undermines the conditionality. *Rabbi Meir* requires that the condition be framed so that the act's contingency is expressed in every way possible: it comes second, its positive outcome is stated first, and the negative consequence is made explicit.

The formula that satisfies all three: *im etayn lach matayim zuz, harei at mekudeshet li; v'im lo etayn lach, einech mekudeshet li* — "if I give you two hundred *zuz*, you are betrothed to me; and if I do not give you, you are not betrothed to me." The *tnai* precedes the *ma'aseh*, the *hein* precedes the *lav*, and both sides are stated.

It is notable that every case in the preceding *mishnayot* — formulated as *al menat she'etayn lach matayim zuz*, *titkadshi li* — places the act before the condition, violating *Rabbi Meir*'s rule of *tnai kodem l'ma'aseh*, and none of them include a *tnai kaful*. Those *mishnayot* are not according to *Rabbi Meir*, and it is a legitimate question whether we *pasken* like him across all three requirements or only in some of them — particularly *tnai kaful*, which has stronger reason to be followed.

### Rabbi Hanina Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר! אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לָאו לִתְנַאי כָּפוּל הוּא דַּאֲתָא, לִכְתּוֹב: ״וְאִם לֹא יַעַבְרוּ וְנֹאחֲזוּ בְתֹכְכֶם״, ״בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel is saying well,** i.e., presenting a reasonable objection, **to Rabbi Meir.** He apparently refuted Rabbi Meir's opinion entirely. How would Rabbi Meir respond? The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Meir** could have **said to you: If it enters your mind** that the verse **does not come** to teach the requirement of **a compound condition** to stipulate both positive and negative outcomes, **let it** merely **write: But if they will not pass over they shall receive a possession among you,** which would indicate that they have a portion in the land. The verse actually proceeds to state: **"In the land of Canaan."**

*Rabbi Hanina ben Gamliel* disagrees with the premise that the doubled formulation in *Bemidbar* was necessary to teach the doctrine of *tnai kaful*. He holds: *tzarich hadavar l'omro* — there was a specific reason *Moshe* had to state the negative side, but it was not to establish a general principle requiring repetition. Had *Moshe* not stated "and if they do not cross over," one might have inferred that the tribes would receive nothing at all — *afilu b'eretz Kena'an lo yinchalu*. The explicit statement was necessary not to articulate the consequence of non-fulfillment of the positive *tnai* (that was already implicit), but to inform them that they would nonetheless retain a portion in *Eretz Kena'an*. The repetition was doing positive work — granting a consolation benefit — not negative work establishing a rule of formulation.

On *Rabbi Hanina*'s view, therefore, a single-sided *tnai* is perfectly valid. The non-fulfillment of a stated condition implicitly negates the act; explicit repetition is not required.

### Pessukim Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אָמַר: אִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא ״וְנֹאחֲזוּ בְתֹכְכֶם״ – בְּאֶרֶץ גִּלְעָד, אֲבָל אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן – כְּלָל לָא. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר? ״בְתֹכְכֶם״ – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִית לְכוּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says: If the Merciful One had not written: "In the land of Canaan," I would say** that the requirement: **"They shall receive a possession among you"** (Numbers 32:30) is referring **to the land of Gilead,** i.e., this land must be shared with the other tribes. **But** they would **not** inherit in **the land of Canaan at all.** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Meir** respond to this claim? He maintains that the expression: **"Among you," means anywhere that you have** taken possession, including the land of Canaan. Therefore, the subsequent phrase "the land of Canaan" is superfluous and serves to teach that the condition must be doubled.

The *Gemara* examines this dispute through a careful reading of the relevant *pesukim*. *Rabbi Meir* challenges *Rabbi Hanina*: if the purpose of the negative formulation was merely to grant them a portion in *Eretz Kena'an*, it would have sufficed to say *im lo ya'avru v'ne'echzu b'tochachem* — if they do not cross over, they will be settled among you. That alone would establish their entitlement to a share among the rest of *Bnei Yisrael*. Why does the verse need to add *b'eretz Kena'an*? The extra words, says *Rabbi Meir*, are limiting: they specify *only* the land of *Kena'an*, thereby explicitly negating the earlier promise of *Gil'ad*. This demonstrates that without such explicit negation, the original promise would have remained in effect — proving that a *tnai kaful* is necessary.

*Rabbi Hanina* responds: had the *Torah* not said *b'eretz Kena'an*, the word *b'tochachem* would have been understood to mean among you in *Gil'ad* — a shared portion in the Transjordanian territory — and would not have conveyed any entitlement to *Kena'an* at all. The phrase *b'eretz Kena'an* was therefore necessary to specify that they receive a portion in *Kena'an* proper, not merely a share in *Gil'ad*. The non-receipt of all of *Gil'ad* in the case of non-fulfillment was already obvious from the simple logic of the condition and required no statement.

*Rabbi Meir* counters: *b'tochachem kol heicha d'ato lachu mashma* — "among you" means wherever you have, encompassing both *Gil'ad* and *Kena'an*. Had it said only *im lo ya'avru v'ne'echzu b'tochachem*, one would have understood that they receive equal shares everywhere — in *Gil'ad* and *Kena'an* alike. *B'eretz Kena'an* is therefore restricting: they get *Kena'an* and not *Gil'ad*. That restriction constitutes an explicit rejection of the original promise of *Gil'ad*, and without it, the original *tnai* would not have been negated.

### Meir vs Hanina

The fundamental disagreement can now be sharpened. *Rabbi Meir* holds that without the explicit negation embedded in *b'eretz Kena'an*, the original promise of *Gil'ad* would have remained in force even if the condition went unfulfilled. The entire doubled formulation was therefore necessary to negate the original *tnai* — establishing the general rule of *tnai kaful*. *Rabbi Hanina ben Gamliel* holds that the negation of the original *tnai* was already implicit; the affirmative statement of the *tnai* is sufficient to generate, by implication, its negative consequence. The doubled language served the entirely different purpose of conferring a positive benefit — a portion in *Kena'an* — that would not otherwise have been granted.

### Inheritance Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה – לְאָדָם שֶׁהָיָה מְחַלֵּק נְכָסָיו לְבָנָיו, אָמַר: פְּלוֹנִי בְּנִי יִירַשׁ שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי בְּנִי יִירַשׁ שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי בְּנִי יִתֵּן מָאתַיִם זוּז וְיִירַשׁ שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית, וְאִם לֹא יִתֵּן – יִירַשׁ עִם אֶחָיו בִּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים. מִי גָּרַם לוֹ לִירַשׁ עִם אֶחָיו בִּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים – כְּפֵילוֹ גָּרַם לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to this issue that **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel said:** Hear **a parable: To what is this matter,** i.e., the condition of the children of Gad and Reuben, **comparable?** It is comparable **to a person who was dividing up his property among his sons,** and **said: My son so-and-so shall inherit such and such a field; and my son so-and-so shall inherit such and such a field; and my son so-and-so shall give two hundred dinars and inherit such and such a field, and if he does not give** the money **he will inherit** a part **of the remainder of the property with his brothers. What causes** the last brother **to inherit** a part **of the remainder of the property with his brothers?** The father's **double** formulation of the condition **causes him** to inherit in this manner. It was therefore necessary for the father to state both sides and explain what will happen if the third brother fails to give the money. Had the father not repeated the condition, upon failing to give the two hundred dinars the son would not have received any portion of the property.

To illustrate his position, *Rabbi Hanina ben Gamliel* offers a *mashal*. A man on his deathbed distributes his estate among his sons. He says: Reuven shall inherit such-and-such a field; Shimon shall inherit such-and-such a field; and Levi, if he gives two hundred *zuz* to the estate, shall inherit field number three. If he had stopped there, one might have worried that Levi is disinherited entirely if he fails to give the money. The father therefore adds: if he does not give, he shall inherit together with his brothers among the remaining assets. *Mi garam lo l'yerash im echav b'shar nechasim? Kefelo garam lo* — what enabled him to share with his brothers in the remainder? The repetition — the doubled statement — did that. The parable's point: the doubled language was needed not to prevent the original promise from standing in the absence of fulfillment, but affirmatively to confer a benefit that would not otherwise have existed.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא דָּמְיָא מָשָׁל לְמַתְנִיתִין! הָתָם קָתָנֵי: יֵשׁ בַּמַּשְׁמָע שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן לֹא יִנְחָלוּ, אַלְמָא כְּפֵילָה לְאֶרֶץ גִּלְעָד נָמֵי מַהֲנֵי. וְהָכָא קָתָנֵי: מִי גָּרַם לוֹ לִירַשׁ עִם אֶחָיו בִּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים – כְּפֵילוֹ גָּרַם לוֹ, אַלְמָא כְּפֵילָה לִשְׁאָר נְכָסִים הוּא דְּקָמַהֲנֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא. הָא – מִקַּמֵּי דְּנֵימָא לֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר ״וְנֹאחֲזוּ״, הָא – לְבָתַר דְּנֵימָא לֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר ״וְנֹאחֲזוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** the **parable is not similar to the mishna,** as **there** the mishna **teaches: It might have** been thought it **meant that** if they do not fulfill the condition **they will not inherit even in the land of Canaan,** and certainly not in the land of Gilead. This **apparently** indicates that the **double** formulation **is also effective for** them to inherit **the land of Gilead** with the other tribes. Otherwise, the children of Gad and Reuben would not receive any part of the Gilead either. **And** yet **here** the *baraita* **teaches: What causes** the last brother **to inherit** a part **of the remainder of the property with his brothers?** The father's **double** formulation **causes him.** This **apparently** indicates that the **double** formulation **is effective for the rest of the property,** whereas he would have received that portion of the field linked to the condition in any case. According to this reasoning, the children of Gad and Reuben would have been granted a portion in the land of Gilead even without the double formulation. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** case, referring to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel's ruling in the mishna, was stated **before Rabbi Meir said to him** that the verse could simply have stated: **"They shall receive a possession** among you." At that stage, Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel maintained that if the two tribes did not fulfill the condition they would not inherit even in the land of Gilead, as indicated by his use of the term: Even, in the mishna. Whereas **that** case, referring to the parable in the *baraita*, was taught **after Rabbi Meir said to** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel that when the phrase: **"They shall receive a possession,"** appears by itself it is referring to the land of Canaan. As stated previously, Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel responded by explaining that had the verse not stated: "In the land of Canaan," one would have said that the requirement: "They shall receive a possession among you" (Numbers 32:30), is referring to the land of Gilead, and they would not inherit in the land of Canaan. In other words, even without the compound condition they would have received a portion in Gilead, which is similar to the parable.

The *Gemara* notes a tension between the parable and the earlier language of the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* had stated that without the repetition, one might have inferred *she'afilu b'eretz Kena'an lo yinchalu* — that they would not have received *any* share in *Eretz Kena'an*. This suggests that *venachazu b'tochachem* alone would have given them a share in *Gil'ad* (not *Kena'an*), and only *b'eretz Kena'an* extended that to *Kena'an* as well. But the parable implies the repetition only helped with *shar nechasim* — the remainder — not with the primary promised asset.

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha mikamei denamlei Rabbi Meir venachazu, ha levatar denamlei Rabbi Meir venachazu*. The answer turns on the phrase *venachazu* itself. Before the specification *b'eretz Kena'an*, the phrase *venachazu b'tochachem* already established, on its own, that they would receive a share in *Gil'ad*. Then *b'eretz Kena'an* added that they would also receive a share in *Kena'an*. For *Rabbi Hanina*, the logic of a single *tnai* already negated their exclusive claim to all of *Gil'ad* without any need for the doubled language; both subsequent phrases served only to specify and expand the positive benefits they would retain. For *Rabbi Meir*, this entire scaffolding was necessary precisely to overcome the force of the original unrepeated promise — which, without explicit negation, would have continued to entitle them to all of *Gil'ad*. The *sugyah* on the parsing of the *pesukim* is thus complete, and the *shiur* will continue tomorrow with the working out of *Rabbi Meir*'s doctrine of *tnai kaful* in its practical applications.
