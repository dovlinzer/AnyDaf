# Sukkah 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Sukkah

### Masechet Structure

We begin today with a new *masechet*: Sukkah. The tractate spans five *perakim* and is, on the whole, logically organized. One might have expected it to open with a discussion of the *mitzvah* of sitting in a *sukkah*, but in fact the first two chapters are devoted to the structure of the *sukkah* itself. Only toward the end of the second *perek* does the tractate turn to the *mitzvah* of dwelling within it. The third *perek* then addresses *lulav* and *esrog*, and the fourth and fifth *perakim* treat *Hallel* and related topics. The first three chapters are certainly the primary ones in terms of the objects of the *mitzvah* and the *mitzvos* that apply on Sukkos.

### Rishonim Sources

Unlike Yoma, where much of the material is not practically relevant today, Sukkah deals with laws that are entirely operative, and accordingly there is a considerably richer body of Rishonim available. Beyond Rashi and Tosafot, there is a Ritva on Sukkah — as there is on Yoma — but there is also extensive discussion by the Rif, the Ramban, and the Ba'al HaMaor. On the third *perek*, concerning *lulav* and *esrog*, entire independent monographs were composed: the Ra'avad wrote a *Hilchos Lulav* and the Ramban wrote a *Hilchos Lulav v'Esrog* treating the *halacha* of *arba minim*. The wealth of Rishonim on this *masechet* is appreciably greater than what we encountered in Yoma, as one would expect given the practical relevance of the material.

### Opening Mishnah

We begin at 2a. The *Mishnah* states: *sukkah she-hi gevoha lemala me-esrim amah pesula* — a *sukkah* that is higher than twenty *amos* is invalid. *Rabbi Yehuda machshir* — Rabbi Yehudah declares it valid. Furthermore, a *sukkah* that is not at least ten *tefachim* in height is invalid, one that lacks three walls is invalid, and one in which the sun exceeds the shade — *she-chamasa meruba me-tzilata* — is invalid.

These three criteria are the ones the *Mishnah* enumerates: the height must fall between ten *tefachim* and twenty *amos*, there must be a minimum of three walls, and the *sukkah* must provide more shade than sun. What the *Mishnah* conspicuously omits are the other dimensions — whether there is a minimum area or a minimum length and width — and any direct discussion of the materials valid for walls or for *sechach*. The absence of any explicit mention of *sechach* is itself quite striking, particularly since the final criterion — that the shade must exceed the sun — raises an important question: is this condition part of the definition of what valid *sechach* is, or is it rather a description of a factual state of affairs in the *sukkah* at any given moment? Does the condition speak to the quality of the *sechach* itself, or to the reality of the sunlight on a particular day at a particular angle?

The *Gemara* then opens by citing the first *Mishnah* in Eruvin — *mavoi she-hu gavoah me-esrim amah yima'et*, an alleyway whose cross-beam (*korah*) is higher than twenty *amos* must be lowered — to which Rabbi Yehudah there likewise says it need not be lowered. To understand the structure of a *mavoi*: multiple houses open into a courtyard, multiple courtyards open into the *mavoi*, which is like a dead-end alley. The *mavoi* has three walls and opens onto the public domain. To permit carrying within it, the residents must establish a *shituf mevo'os* and there must be either a *lechi* — a vertical post demarcating the fourth wall — or a *korah*, a horizontal beam spanning the opening. That beam must be positioned within twenty *amos*; if it is higher, it must be lowered.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai shena gabei sukkah de-tani pesula, u-mai shena gabei mavoi de-tani takanta* — why by *sukkah* does the *Mishnah* say "invalid," while by *mavoi* it says "lower it"? Why not use the same language in both cases? The *Gemara* answers: *sukkah de-oraisa tani pesula, mavoi de-rabbanan tani takanta* — *sukkah* is a biblical law and so it says "invalid"; *mavoi* is rabbinic and so it tells you how to correct the problem.

What does this distinction mean in substance? Rashi offers a conceptually rich explanation. Regarding *sukkah*, Rashi says the twenty-*amah* limit is a *halakha* received from Sinai — *de-esrim amah hilkheta gemiri lah mi-de-oraisa* — and the measure was stated from Sinai. The *Mishnah* is therefore not legislating but reporting an objective fact: a *sukkah* of this height is, in the biblical scheme, simply invalid. The word *pesula* reflects an objective metaphysical state of affairs. By contrast, the *mavoi*, as Rashi explains, has no pre-existing objective invalidity. Biblically, three walls suffice and no *korah* is needed at all; the *korah* is entirely a rabbinic requirement. The rabbis cannot therefore declare a too-high *korah* "invalid" as though they are reporting a pre-existing halakhic reality — the reality is of their own creation. What they can say is: we require this, and here is how to satisfy our requirement. Hence: *takanta*, a corrective instruction, rather than *pesula*, a statement of invalidity.

This Rashi touches on something profound — the notion, reminiscent of Rav Soloveitchik's concept of halakhic man, that *halacha* creates objective realities, platonic structures in the world of law. The contrast here is between a pre-existing halakhic truth that the *Mishnah* merely reports, and a rabbinic enactment that the rabbis themselves are in the act of creating. It is worth noting, however, that this principle is not applied consistently throughout the Talmud, and Rashi himself is not claiming a universal rule.

Tosafot offers a more practical explanation. When the *Mishnah* says *pesula*, there is no ambiguity: the structure is invalid and one has not fulfilled the *mitzvah*. But if it were to say "lower it," one might reason: I was supposed to lower it, but since I did not, perhaps it is still acceptable *bedi'eved*. For a *de-oraisa* requirement, we cannot tolerate that possible misreading and so we say *pesula* — unambiguous invalidity. For a rabbinic rule, we are willing to risk the misinterpretation, because using the word *yima'et* ("lower it") has the practical advantage of telling you how to address the situation. Where the *Mishnah* faces multiple problems to enumerate — too high, too low, insufficient shade, too few walls — saying *pesula* is also more concise than specifying a corrective for each scenario. In the *mavoi* case, only one scenario is presented, so the corrective language is workable.

A second answer offered by the *Gemara* is that the distinction between *de-oraisa* and *de-rabbanan* is not actually the controlling factor — sometimes *de-oraisa* cases use corrective language and sometimes *de-rabbanan* cases use *pesula*. Rather, by *sukkah*, since there are many conditions enumerated, *tani pesula* — it is concise. By *mavoi*, where only one condition is at issue, the *Mishnah* can afford to say *yima'et*, telling you how to fix it.

An additional and quite notable point: the fact that Sukkah and Eruvin open with essentially the same *Mishnah* — the same twenty-*amah* limit, the same dissent of Rabbi Yehudah — is striking. It strongly suggests some common underlying reason for the requirement in both cases and for the debate in both cases, even though the first series of explanations the *Gemara* will offer is entirely particular to *sukkah* concerns and has no bearing on *mavoi*. That question — whether there is a shared source for the two debates — will occupy us later.

### Savoraic Openings

Before proceeding to the main discussion, it is worth pausing to observe a characteristic feature of how *masechtos* begin. Based on a letter of Rav Sherira Gaon, many scholars believe the openings of tractates are *Savoraic* in composition — that is, they postdate the finalization of the *Gemara* proper. Whether or not that is so, these openings tend to share a common quality: they contain no named Amoraic statements, everything is *stam*, and the focus tends to be on technical, linguistic, or structural matters rather than new legal analysis. Think of the opening of Kiddushin, which spends considerable time analyzing why the *Mishnah* uses the term *niknis* rather than *kiddushin*, why it speaks from the woman's perspective rather than the man's, why it uses *lashon zachar* versus *lashon nekeivah*, and so forth. These openings ease one into the *masechet* and simultaneously introduce conceptual themes that will recur throughout it. Here, the opening has achieved something important: it has introduced us to the striking parallel between Sukkah and Eruvin and has begun to prepare us for the fundamental question of why twenty *amos* is the operative threshold in each case.

## Height Limit Basis

### Rava: Vision

The *Gemara* now asks *mina hani mili* — what is the source of this twenty-*amah* requirement? And implicitly, one begins to wonder whether the answer will be local to *sukkah* or whether it might illuminate the parallel case of *mavoi* as well. As we will see, the first series of explanations is entirely particular to the concerns of *sukkah*.

Rava derives the rule from the verse: *lema'an yedu doroseichem ki va-sukkos hoshavti et Bnei Yisrael* — "so that your generations shall know that I caused the children of Israel to dwell in *sukkos*" (Vayikra 23:43). *Ad esrim amah adam yodea she-hu dar ba-sukkah* — up to twenty *amos*, a person is aware that he is dwelling in a *sukkah*. *Lemala me-esrim amah ein adam yodea she-hu dar ba-sukkah* — above twenty *amos*, he is no longer aware of this. Why? *Mishum de-lo shalta bei eina* — because the eye does not perceive it. And what does the eye fail to perceive? Not the walls — those he can see. What he cannot naturally see is the *sechach*.

This introduces us to a foundational principle that will run throughout the entire *masechet*: for *Chazal*, the *ikar* of the *sukkah* is the *sechach*. Rashi makes this explicit in a brief but pointed remark just before the *Gemara* begins: *ve-al shem ha-sechach keruya sukkah* — the *sukkah* takes its name from the *sechach*. Consequently, any verse that speaks of *sukkah* is assumed to refer primarily to the *sechach*, not to the walls.

For Rava, the verse *lema'an yedu doroseichem* is not merely describing the purpose of the *mitzvah* — awareness of the historical fact that God sheltered Israel in *sukkos* — but is actually legislating a requirement about how the *mitzvah* must be performed. The *mitzvah* must be done in a manner in which one is aware that one is sitting under *sechach*. If the *sechach* is so high that one must crane one's neck to see it, and in fact would not naturally look up to see it at all, then one lacks the awareness required by *lema'an yedu*. The *sechach* effectively does not register. And this — the absence of awareness of the *sechach* — is what invalidates the *sukkah*.

One might observe the irony — noted in the course of discussion — that if the *sukkah* commemorates the *ananei kavod*, the Clouds of Glory, one would naturally imagine something high overhead. But that is a tension we will return to later; for now, the point is Rava's reading of the verse.

### R. Zeira: Shade

Rabbi Zeira derives the rule from a different verse, one with a markedly different character. The verse is from Yeshayahu (4:6): *ve-sukkah tihyeh le-tzel yomam me-chorev* — "and there shall be a *sukkah* for shade by day from the heat." It is immediately significant that this is a Messianic verse, not a verse about the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* at all. The broader passage in Yeshayahu evokes the redemption and speaks of a cloud by day and a fire by night — an explicit evocation of the *Exodus* from Egypt — and describes how in that future time God's glory will be canopied (*al kol kavod chuppah*), with a *sukkah* providing shade from heat and shelter from rain. By invoking this verse, Rabbi Zeira is subtly introducing into the discussion the theme of *sukkah* as divine protection, of the connection between *Sukkos* and the Messianic era, themes that resonate through the *haftaros* and through certain *Tannaim* such as Rabbi Akiva. That the verse uses the word *sukkah* while the verse immediately before uses *chuppah* is, as we will see, itself a point Rabbi Zeira will exploit in defending his position.

How does Rabbi Zeira use this verse? *Ad esrim amah adam yoshev be-tzel sukkah* — up to twenty *amos*, a person is sitting in the shade of the *sukkah*, meaning the *sechach* is actually providing shade. *Lemala me-esrim amah ein adam yoshev be-tzel sukkah ela be-tzel defanot* — above twenty *amos*, he is no longer sitting in the shade of the *sechach* but in the shade of the walls, because walls of such height shade the interior by virtue of their own height, and the *sechach* far above contributes nothing practically. The invalidity of the *sukkah* above twenty *amos* is thus, for Rabbi Zeira, an invalidity rooted in the *sechach*'s failure to provide shade in practice.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye immediately challenges Rabbi Zeira. *Ela me'ata osseh sukkato be-ashrot karnayim, hachi nami de-lo havei sukkah?* — If one builds a *sukkah* in a valley between two high mountains, do you mean to say it is not a valid *sukkah*? After all, the mountains themselves are shading the interior, not the *sechach*. Rabbi Zeira responds: *hatam, dal ashrot karnayim, ika tzel sukkah* — there, if you were to remove the mountains, the *sechach* would still provide shade. The *sechach* of that *sukkah*, considered on its own, is constructed in a way that it is capable of providing shade. *Hacha, dal defanot, leika tzel sukkah* — here, by contrast, if you remove the walls, the *sechach* still does not provide shade, because the *sechach* is so high that regardless of the walls, it effectively never shades the interior below it.

The reasoning is physical: a small area of *sechach* situated at an extreme height, when the sun is at any angle other than directly overhead — which may in fact never occur — will not cast meaningful shade on the area below. The sunlight reaches under it from multiple directions. Thus the failure of the *sechach* to provide shade is not a function of the walls' shading; it is intrinsic to the *sechach*'s elevation. The *sechach* itself, independent of the walls, is situated in a manner that it cannot provide shade under virtually any normal circumstance. It is worth acknowledging that one might ask whether this depends on the area of the *sechach* — a very wide *sechach* at the same height might behave differently — and the *Gemara* will address this when it gets to the qualifications of the debate. But as a general claim, Rabbi Zeira's principle stands: it is about whether the *sechach* in practice provides shade.

Both Rava's explanation and Rabbi Zeira's thus far locate the problem in the *sechach* — for Rava, the *sechach* is not perceived; for Rabbi Zeira, the *sechach* does not practically shade.

### Rava: Temporary

Rava offers a second explanation, this one from a different verse: *ba-sukkos teshevu shivat yamim* — "you shall dwell in *sukkos* for seven days." *Amra Torah, kol shivat ha-yamim, tze mi-dirat keva ve-shev be-dirat arai* — for all seven days leave your permanent home and dwell in a temporary structure. Rava sees in the word *sukkah* an implicit contrast to the house: the *mitzvah* is conceptually built on leaving the permanent and entering the temporary. This introduces another fundamental theme of the *masechet* — *sukkah* as *dirat arai*, a temporary dwelling. *Ve-ad esrim amah, adam osseh dirato dirat arai* — up to twenty *amos*, the dimensions are such that a person can construct something temporary. *Lemala me-esrim amah, de-adam osseh dirato dirat keva* — above twenty *amos*, those are dimensions only of a permanent structure; nobody builds something so tall as a temporary dwelling.

The invalidity thus is not about the *sechach* per se, but about the overall character of the structure: a very tall structure cannot qualify as *dirat arai* and therefore cannot function as a *sukkah*.

### Abaye's Second Challenge

Abaye challenges this explanation as well. *Asu mechitzot shel barzel ve-sichech gaben, hacha nami de-lav sukkah?* — If one built metal walls — or, to extend the scenario, went out after Yom Kippur and constructed four solid walls of brick and mortar and placed *sechach* on top — does that mean one would not be *yotzei*, because the structure is *dirat keva*? Are we really saying that the building material determines whether a *sukkah* is valid?

Rava responds: *hachi ka-amina lach* — this is what I am actually saying. *Ad esrim amah, de-adam osseh dirato dirat arai* — up to twenty *amos*, these are dimensions within which a person can construct a *dirat arai*. Those dimensions are compatible with a temporary structure. Therefore, *ki avid dirat keva, nami nafik* — even if one uses materials associated with a permanent structure, one has still fulfilled the *mitzvah*, because the dimensions are those of a temporary dwelling. *Lemala me-esrim amah, de-adam osseh dirato dirat keva* — above twenty *amos*, these are dimensions exclusively associated with permanent construction. Therefore, *ki avid dirat arai, nami lo nafik* — even if one constructs it out of the most flimsy materials imaginable, since the dimensions are those of a permanent structure, one has not fulfilled the *mitzvah*.

In other words, the distinction between *dirat arai* and *dirat keva* is not determined by building material but by dimension. This is characteristic of *halacha* generally: the law translates abstract concepts into quantifiable, measurable terms. The building material is not the issue; the dimensions are.

This said, a further question naturally presents itself. What if one takes normal *sukkah* dimensions but constructs a perfectly sealed, weather-tight, permanent-looking wood roof as the *sechach* — planks nailed together and plastered? Is there not some sense in which a practical, fully permanent roof structure crosses a line independent of quantifiable dimensions?

### Tosafot: Schach

Tosafot addresses precisely this question. Tosafot notes: could one nail planks to the roof so that no rain enters — *she-lo yerdu geshamim ba-sukkah* — and would that be acceptable? Regarding a fully permanent house with a wood ceiling, Tosafot says it is clearly invalid: *desukkah amar Rachmana ve-lo beita she-kavu'a kol ha-shanah* — the Torah said *sukkah*, not a structure that is fixed all year round. But what about a newly built structure with a permanent wood roof? Tosafot says: *nehi de-lo chaishinan bi-defanot i avid lehu keva* — while we do not invalidate a *sukkah* because its walls are permanent, *mikol makom ba-sechach she-ikar ha-sukkah al shem ha-sechach* — when it comes to the *sechach*, which is the *ikar* of the *sukkah* — *lo mishtarya ha-sukkah ad de-avid lah arai* — the *sukkah* is not valid unless the *sechach* itself is made in a temporary manner.

What constitutes a *sechach* that is not *arai*? Tosafot does not fully quantify it, but among the factors he raises is the following: if the *sechach* is impervious to rain — if rain cannot penetrate it — then the *sechach* has become effectively a permanent roof, and that disqualifies the *sukkah*. This connects to the well-known principle that rain on *Sukkos* is a *siman kelalah ba-chag* — a sign of curse on the festival — which only makes sense if the *sukkah* is structurally capable of letting rain in. If it were possible to construct a rain-proof *sukkah*, there would be no such sign. Tosafot thus concludes that part of the definition of valid *sechach* — and specifically, of *sechach* as *arai* — is that it not form a sealed, rain-impervious roof.

This is a striking point: while the walls of the *sukkah* may be permanent without concern, the *sechach* — because it is the *ikar* of the *sukkah*, the very thing that makes it a *sukkah* — cannot be constructed in the manner of a permanent roof. This requirement for the *sechach* to remain *arai* may not always be fully quantifiable, but Tosafot insists it is real and operative at the biblical level. And there is a certain irony here: the verse in Yeshayahu that Rabbi Zeira cited speaks of a *sukkah* that provides shade *from the rain* — yet it is precisely the incapacity to be rain-proof that defines the valid *sechach*.

## Explanations Evaluated

### Against Rava

Having laid out the three explanations — Rava's argument from *lema'an yedu*, Rabbi Zeira's argument from providing shade, and Rabbah's argument from *dirat arai* — the *Gemara* now evaluates each in turn, noting that none commands universal agreement.

Regarding Rava's explanation — that the *sechach* must be visible, and above twenty *amos* the eye does not perceive it — the *Gemara* objects: *hahu yedia le-dorot hi* — that phrase, *lema'an yedu doroseichem*, speaks to the historical awareness that God sheltered Israel, not to a requirement of how one must sit in the *sukkah* right now. It is not *peshat* in the verse to read it as mandating that one be visually aware of the *sechach* during the performance of the *mitzvah*.

### Against R. Zeira

Regarding Rabbi Zeira's explanation — that the *sechach* must provide practical shade — the *Gemara* objects: *hu li-yemot ha-mashiach*, that verse in Yeshayahu is a Messianic verse. It is not speaking about the *sukkah* of the *mitzvah* at all. One cannot derive from a vision of end-time Divine shelter the practical requirements of the *sukkah she-mitzvah*.

Now, nobody actually disputes that *sechach* must inherently be capable of blocking sunlight — that is why it is called *sechach*, that is definitional to it. But the question is whether the *sechach* must actually provide shade in the particular physical circumstances in which the *sukkah* stands. The majority position is that the *sechach* needs the quality of blocking sunlight, but it need not actually do so in practice given its position. A *sechach* that is too high to provide shade is a problem of positioning, not of material quality, and the *sechach* itself is not thereby invalidated.

Rabbi Zeira might counter: *im ken leima kra ve-sukkah tihyeh le-tzel yomam* — if the verse wanted only to give a Messianic vision without teaching anything about the *mitzvah sukkah*, it should have used a different word. The very previous verse used *chuppah*; why shift to *sukkah*? The use of *sukkah* specifically suggests *shema mina tarti* — learn from it two things: a Messianic vision, and something about the actual *sukkah* of the *mitzvah*. This is Rabbi Zeira's defense. But the other authorities are not persuaded.

### Against Rabbah

Regarding Rabbah's explanation — that a *sukkah* above twenty *amos* is invalid because those dimensions are those of a *dirat keva*, not a *dirat arai* — the *Gemara* objects on account of Abaye's challenge about brick walls and metal walls. If the concept of *dirat arai* were the operative principle, one would expect building material to matter as much as or more than dimension. The fact that we do not invalidate a *sukkah* for permanent materials — while Rabbah himself explains that dimension substitutes for material in defining *arai* versus *keva* — is not entirely satisfying to the other authorities. They do not accept that the abstract concept of *dirat arai* translates into a dimensional requirement of the kind Rabbah proposes.

## Qualifications

### R. Yoshiyah

The *Gemara* now asks *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rabbi Yoshiyah?* — according to which of the three explanations does the following qualification of Rabbi Yoshiyah fit? Rabbi Yoshiyah, cited by Rav, states: *machlokes be-she-ein defanos megi'os la-sechach* — the debate in the *Mishnah* between the Sages and Rabbi Yehudah applies only when the walls do not reach all the way up to the *sechach*, i.e., there is a gap between the top of the walls and the *sechach*. *Aval defanos megi'os la-sechach, afilu lemala me-esrim amah kesherah* — but if the walls reach all the way up to the *sechach*, everyone would agree the *sukkah* is valid even above twenty *amos*.

The *Gemara* asks: *ke-man?* — according to whom does this make sense? *Ke-Rava* — it fits specifically with Rava's explanation, that the problem is the eye not perceiving the *sechach*. *De-amar mishum de-lo shalta bei eina* — the problem is the eye's failure to see the *sechach*. If the walls reach all the way up to the *sechach*, the eye naturally follows the wall until it reaches the intersection of wall and *sechach*, and so one's gaze is directed to the *sechach*. But if the walls end in a gap before the *sechach*, the eye stops at the top of the wall where there is only air, and does not continue upward to the *sechach*. Thus, whether the walls meet the *sechach* is relevant only to the question of whether the *sechach* is perceived — it cannot be relevant to the question of temporary versus permanent structure, and it also cannot meaningfully affect whether the *sechach* provides shade.

### Rav Huna

The next qualification: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rav Huna amar Rav?* — according to which explanation does the following statement of Rav Huna in the name of Rav fit? *Machlokes be-she-ein bah ela arba amos* — the debate applies only when the *sukkah* is no larger than four *amos* by four *amos*. *Aval yesh bah yoter me-arba amos al arba amos, afilu lemala me-esrim amah kesherah* — but if it is larger than four by four *amos*, even above twenty *amos* it is valid.

*Ke-man?* — according to whom does this fit? The *Gemara* answers: *ke-Rabbi Zeira* — it fits Rabbi Zeira's explanation, *de-amar mishum tzel*, that the problem is whether the *sechach* provides shade in practice. And providing shade depends on the area of the *sechach*: *ve-keivan de-rivcha, ika tzel sukkah* — since it is very wide, even very high up it provides shade. A tiny patch of *sechach* at extreme height provides no shade regardless of its material quality. But a large expanse of *sechach* — even at the same height — will cast meaningful shade because the angle of sunlight reaching the interior is blocked by the breadth of the covering.

One might push back: shouldn't the threshold be proportional? If four by four *amos* justifies going above twenty *amos*, shouldn't ten by ten *amos* justify going to twenty-five *amos*, and so on — with a whole graduated chart? Tosafot acknowledges this possibility: according to this approach, as the *sechach* grows, proportionally greater heights might be permissible. But Tosafot also offers what is probably the more standard rabbinic-legal answer, by analogy to a principle stated in Yoma regarding minimum quantities: once a minimum threshold is met, the law draws a bright line. The Rabbis knew that beyond four by four *amos*, some shade will always reach the interior even at extreme heights — *i efshar she-lo yikanes tzel sechach purta*, it is impossible that no shade at all enters. Below four by four *amos*, the Rabbis knew that above twenty *amos* no practical shade is achieved. The law quantifies and fixes the threshold, even if a more proportional scheme might seem logically demanded.

### R. Chana bar Rabbah

The third qualification: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rav Chana bar Rabba amar Rav?* — according to whom fits the statement of Rav Chana bar Rabbah in the name of Rav? *Machlokes be-she-einah machzeket ela kedei rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano* — the debate applies only to a *sukkah* that holds no more than the minimum: one's head, most of one's body, and one's table. *Aval machzeket yoter mi-kedei rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano, afilu lemala me-esrim amah kesherah* — once it is larger than the absolute minimum, even above twenty *amos* it is valid.

A word on this minimum size: since one eats in the *sukkah* in a reclining position, the *sukkah* must accommodate one's head, most of one's body, and the small table from which one eats. The *Gemara* will establish later that this corresponds to approximately seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*. The precise dimensions of the minimum *sukkah* — whether it is this seven-by-seven or the larger four *amos* by four *amos* — is itself a debate that will appear later in the *masechet*. To get a rough sense of scale: an *amah* is roughly eighteen inches and comprises six *tefachim*, so four *amos* by four *amos* is approximately thirty-six square feet, while seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim* is roughly four square feet — about one-sixteenth the area.

*Ke-man?* — according to whom does this fit? *De-lo ke-chad* — it does not go like any of the three explanations. One might try to fit it with Rabbi Zeira's shade argument: once it is slightly larger than the absolute minimum, the *sechach* can provide some shade even when high. But the *Gemara* rejects this: if the concern is shade, the shade argument would demand *at least* four by four *amos*; the incremental difference from seven *tefachim* to anything larger than seven *tefachim* is not meaningful enough to solve the shade problem at extreme heights.

### Tosafot: Chicken Coop

Tosafot offers an alternative rationale for Rav Chana bar Rabbah's qualification. A structure that is both extremely tiny and extremely tall is not something anyone would recognize as a *sukkah*. It is *lul shel tarnigolim* — a chicken coop. A tiny, short structure might pass as a miniature dwelling; but a tiny structure that is extraordinarily tall is simply an absurd configuration that no longer registers as any kind of dwelling at all, neither temporary nor permanent. Once the *sukkah* is even slightly larger than the minimum — no longer so small as to be bizarre — this objection falls away and the height limit is no longer disputed.

### Gemara Resolution

The *Gemara* now addresses the apparent tension between the qualifications of Rav Huna and Rav Chana bar Rabbah. One might think these two authorities are simply debating what the minimum size of a *sukkah* is — both agreeing that the twenty-*amah* debate is limited to a minimum-size *sukkah*, and disagreeing only about what constitutes that minimum: is it four *amos* by four *amos* (Rav Huna) or *rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano* — seven *tefachim* (Rav Chana bar Rabbah)?

The *Gemara* resists this reading. *De-kulei alma hechsher sukkah be-rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano* — everyone agrees the minimum valid *sukkah* is defined by *rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano*, seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*. The debate between Rav Huna and Rav Chana bar Rabbah is not about what constitutes a minimum *sukkah*. Rather, it is about the scope of the twenty-*amah* debate: *de-mar savar, machzeket rosho ve-rubo ve-shulchano pligi* — one holds that the twenty-*amah* debate applies only to the absolute minimum *sukkah*, seven by seven *tefachim*; once it is any larger, everyone agrees it is valid even above twenty *amos*. *U-mar savar* — the other holds that the debate extends to any *sukkah* from the minimum up to four *amos* by four *amos*; only once it exceeds four by four does everyone agree it is valid above twenty *amos*.

This framing is more satisfying, because it keeps the focus on the nature of the twenty-*amah* problem — what it is about a *sukkah* that twenty *amos* disrupts — rather than collapsing into a debate about minimum size. The *Gemara* insists on preserving that focus: the qualifications are driven by the underlying rationale for the twenty-*amah* limit, not merely by an independent question about minimum dimensions. If the concern is shade (Rabbi Zeira / Rav Huna), then the shade problem is resolved once the *sukkah* exceeds four by four *amos*, because at that size the *sechach* will provide some shade even at great height. If some other consideration governs (Rav Chana bar Rabbah, in Tosafot's reading: the structure looks like a chicken coop), then once the *sukkah* is any larger than the absolute minimum, the concern is already resolved.

## Queen Hilni Case

### Hilni's Sukkah

The *Gemara* now cites a *Mishnah* that presents a practical case testing the scope of the twenty-*amah* rule. The *Mishnah* states: a *sukkah* that is higher than twenty *amos* — *lama pesulah?* — why is it invalid? And Rabbi Yehudah *machshir arba'im va-chamishim amah* — Rabbi Yehudah declares valid a *sukkah* forty or fifty *amos* high, which effectively means there is no upper height limit. In support of his position, Rabbi Yehudah adduces: *ma'aseh be-Hilni ha-malka be-Lod* — there was an incident involving Queen Hilni in Lod, *she-haysa sukkasah gevoha me-esrim amah* — whose *sukkah* was higher than twenty *amos*, *ve-hayu zekeinim nichnasim ve-yotzim la-sham ve-lo amru lah davar* — and the Sages would enter and exit there and said nothing to her. The Sages' silence, Rabbi Yehudah argues, is an implicit endorsement: they saw a *sukkah* above twenty *amos* and raised no objection.

### Woman Exemption

The Sages respond: *misham ra'aya?* — do you bring proof from there? *Isha haysa* — she was a woman, and women are *peturot min ha-sukkah*, exempt from the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*, which is a time-bound positive commandment. The Sages remained silent not because they endorsed the *sukkah*'s validity but because there was nothing to say to her — she was not obligated.

### Seven Sons Argument

Rabbi Yehudah counters: *ve-lo shiva banim havu lah?* — did she not have seven sons who were with her in the *sukkah*? And furthermore, *kol ma'aseh lo asta ela al pi chakhamim* — she conducted herself in all matters according to the rulings of the Sages; she was known for her exceptional *frumkeit* and would have built her *sukkah* precisely as the Sages instructed. So the Sages' silence cannot be dismissed as irrelevant: they saw her sons in the *sukkah* and still said nothing.

### Chinuch Obligation

The *Gemara* interjects: why is the additional point needed — that she did everything according to the Sages — once Rabbi Yehudah has already established that she had seven sons? The *Gemara* explains: *hachi ka-amar lehu* — this is what Rabbi Yehudah is saying to the Sages. If you respond that *banim ketanim hayu* — all seven sons were small children, and *ketanim peturim min ha-sukkah* — minor children are exempt from *sukkah* — Rabbi Yehudah's reply is: *keivan de-shiva havu, i efshar de-lo hava behu chad she-eino tzarich le-imo* — with seven sons, it is statistically impossible that none of them had reached the age of no longer needing his mother in the night. That age of independence is the criterion the *Gemara* establishes later for when a minor becomes obligated in *sukkah* by rabbinic law as part of *chinuch* — the obligation to educate children in the performance of *mitzvos*.

If the Sages then counter that a minor's obligation is only *mi-derabbanan* and perhaps Queen Hilni was not concerned about rabbinic obligations on behalf of her sons — *ve-i derabbanan lo mashgicha* — Rabbi Yehudah's further point addresses this: *ve-od kol ma'aseh lo asta ela al pi chakhamim* — she was scrupulous even in rabbinic requirements; she did everything the Sages told her.

It is worth noting a technical point about the *Gemara*'s method here. Rabbi Yehudah, who knew the actual historical case, did not need to reason probabilistically from the number seven to establish that at least one son had reached *chinuch* age. He knew who the sons were and how old they were. The *Gemara* itself, reasoning post-hoc from the text, establishes the point through probability: with seven sons, surely one was old enough. Rabbi Yehudah knew the facts directly; the *Gemara*'s probabilistic reasoning is the *Gemara*'s own contribution to establishing the argument's logical foundation. We will see tomorrow how the case of Queen Hilni is then used to test the various theories about the twenty-*amah* limit that we have been developing throughout this *sugyah*.
