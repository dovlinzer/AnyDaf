# Temurah 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hekdesh Iluy & Death

### Hekdesh Iluy Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם עָבַר וּשְׁחָטָן — מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** If he transgressed and slaughtered it before redeeming it — **what he did is done** and he is not required to redeem it.

The daf begins at the bottom of 32b, picking up where the previous shiur left off. The *mishna* had covered two distinct topics. The first was *hekdesh iluy* — the concept that an animal already sanctified as a *korban* can have an additional layer of *kedusha* added to it by the owner. As the *gemara* explored, there is a debate as to whether this additional layer constitutes an actual *kedushas bedek habayis*, a sanctification for the temple treasury, or whether it merely generates a personal financial obligation. Two *nafka minas* turned on this question. First, if the *korban* was brought without first redeeming this additional layer, does it retroactively invalidate the offering? Second, can the animal subsequently be made *cherem* to the *kohanim*? If the additional layer is a genuine sanctification of the object, then *bedieved* the *korban* would be invalid, but a *cherem* to the *kohanim* would be possible. If it is only a personal obligation, then *bedieved* the *korban* is valid, but a *cherem* to the *kohanim* would not attach — since *cherem* requires sanctification of an object, not merely the creation of a personal duty. The *gemara*'s conclusion leaned toward the additional layer being only a personal obligation, with any increment of *kedusha* being purely rabbinic. There might therefore be a *cherem* to the *kohanim*, but only at a rabbinic level, and *bedieved* the *korban* remains valid.

### Mishna: Dead Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מֵתוּ יִקָּבְרוּ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cites a dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis with regard to animals consecrated for the altar and animals consecrated for Temple maintenance. The Rabbis hold that **if they died** and were not redeemed, they cannot be redeemed and **they must be buried,** whereas Rabbi Shimon maintains that this is the *halakha* only with regard to animals consecrated for the altar; animals consecrated for Temple maintenance can be redeemed.

The second topic of the *mishna* now takes center stage: what happens when an animal that was sanctified — whether as a *korban* or for *bedek habayis* — dies? The operative question is whether such an animal can be redeemed after death, or whether it must simply be buried.

### Tanna Kamma & R. Shimon

The *tanna kamma* rules that a dead sanctified animal cannot be redeemed. *Rabbi Shimon* disagrees in part, holding that if the animal was sanctified for *bedek habayis*, it may be redeemed even after death. The underlying dispute, as the *gemara* explains, concerns whether these animals fall within the category of *yeshno b'klal ha'amadah ve'ha'arachah* — whether they are governed by the Torah's requirement that "the *kohen* shall stand the animal and assess it before God," which implies the animal must be alive at the time of redemption. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, this requirement applies only to *kodshei mizbe'ach*, to animals dedicated as sacrifices, and not to animals dedicated to the temple treasury. His reasoning is compelling: since one may sanctify a dead animal for *bedek habayis* in the first place, what difference should death make at the point of redemption? The *chachamim*, by contrast, apply the living requirement to all sanctified animals — though, as the *gemara* will show, precisely how they do so is subject to a major dispute between *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Reish Lakish*.

## Ha'amadah V'ha'aracha

### Living Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבָּנַן: אֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ וְאֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: According to the Rabbis, both** animals **consecrated for the altar and** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were included in the** *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** as it states in the Torah with regard to the redemption of consecrated animals: "And if it be any unclean animal, of which they may not bring an offering unto the Lord, then he shall set the animal before the priest. And the priest shall value it, whether it be good or bad; as you the priest values it, so shall it be" (Leviticus 27:11–12). Any consecrated animal that cannot be stood before the priest, e.g., one that died, is not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and must be buried.

The *gemara* now directly engages the debate about *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* — the requirement that the animal be standing and alive when evaluated for redemption. The question is whether this requirement applies across the board, to both *korbanot* and *bedek habayis*, or whether it is more limited in scope.

### R. Yochanan's View

*Rabbi Yochanan* reads the *tanna kamma* as holding that both *kodshei mizbe'ach* and *kodshei bedek habayis* are subject to *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* — every sanctified animal must be alive to be redeemed. The only exception, as will emerge, is an animal that was already blemished from the outset when it was sanctified as a *korban*, a case known as *ba'al mum me'ikaro*. Since such an animal was never suitable for the altar in the first place, it never bore a genuine *kedushas haguf*, and the normal redemption rules do not apply to it in the same way.

### Reish Lakish's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לְרַבָּנַן, קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says: According to the Rabbis,** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** as that verse is discussing animals consecrated for Temple maintenance. Accordingly, if an animal consecrated for Temple maintenance died and was not redeemed, it must be buried without redemption. But animals **consecrated for the altar were not included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** as the verse is not referring to such animals. Accordingly, if they died they are redeemed.

*Reish Lakish*, remarkably, inverts this. He reads the *tanna kamma* as requiring the living condition only for *kodshei bedek habayis* — for animals sanctified to the temple treasury — and not for *kodshei mizbe'ach*. According to *Reish Lakish*, the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Shimon* are essentially debating only the treasury category, and both agree that sacrificial animals do not require *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*. This position, as the *gemara* will show, is counterintuitive and difficult to sustain.

## Tanna Kamma Logic

### General vs. Specific

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁמֵּתוּ יִפָּדוּ״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר לְרַבָּנַן אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְפָרוֹשֵׁי ״קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁמֵּתוּ יִפָּדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to the explanation of Reish Lakish: **We learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Shimon says** that animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance that died can be redeemed.** The Gemara analyzes this statement of Rabbi Shimon: **Granted, according to** the explanation of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that **according to the Rabbis, both this and that,** i.e., animals consecrated for the altar and for Temple maintenance, **were included** in the *halakha* of **standing and valuation** and if they died they are not redeemed; **this is** why it **was necessary** for **Rabbi Shimon to explain** that specifically in the case of animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance that died, they can be redeemed.** This serves to emphasize that Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the Rabbis only with regard to animals consecrated for Temple maintenance, not those consecrated for the altar.

*Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading of the *mishna* is elegant. The *tanna kamma* states a general, unqualified prohibition against redeeming dead sanctified animals. *Rabbi Shimon* then comes along and carves out an exception: *kodshei bedek habayis she'meisu yipadu* — if it was for the treasury, it may be redeemed. This is a natural *machloket*: the *tanna kamma* applies the prohibition universally, and *Rabbi Shimon* narrows it.

### Reish Lakish Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְפָרוֹשֵׁי? לֵימָא ״אִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the explanation of **Reish Lakish,** that the Rabbis hold that only animals consecrated for Temple maintenance may not be redeemed if they died, **why must** Rabbi Shimon **explain** that he is referring to animals consecrated for Temple maintenance? **Let him say** simply: **If they died they can be redeemed,** and it would be obvious that he is speaking of animals consecrated for Temple maintenance, for if he were referring to animals consecrated for the altar, even the Rabbis agree that they may be redeemed.

*Reish Lakish*'s reading, however, poses an immediate textual difficulty. If the *tanna kamma* was only ever talking about *bedek habayis* — as *Reish Lakish* claims — then why did *Rabbi Shimon* need to specify *kodshei bedek habayis*? He could simply have said "they may be redeemed" and been done with it.

### Rabbi Shimon's Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא הֲוָה יָדַע מַאי דְּאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ — מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ, בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — אִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reish Lakish** could have **said to you** that **Rabbi Shimon did not know what the first *tanna* said,** i.e., he was unsure whether the statement of the first *tanna*, the Rabbis, that the animal must be buried without redemption, is referring to animals consecrated for Temple maintenance or to animals consecrated for the altar. **And** therefore, **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying to** the first *tanna*: **If** your statement is referring **to** animals **consecrated for the altar,** that they are included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and must be buried if they died without redemption, then **I concede to you.** By contrast, if your statement is referring **to** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** then I disagree with you and rule that **if they died they can be redeemed.**

*Reish Lakish* has an answer, though it is admittedly strained: *Rabbi Shimon* himself was uncertain about the scope of the *tanna kamma*'s statement. He could not tell whether the *tanna kamma* was forbidding redemption of *kodshei mizbe'ach* or *kodshei bedek habayis*. So *Rabbi Shimon* responded to both possibilities: if you are talking about sacrificial animals, I agree with you; if you are talking about the treasury, I disagree — those may be redeemed. Notably, *Reish Lakish*'s own admission that *Rabbi Shimon* could not parse the *tanna kamma* is somewhat self-undermining, since it acknowledges that the *tanna kamma*'s statement reads as broadly categorical. Nevertheless, *Reish Lakish* insists that the *tanna kamma*'s intent was narrow, limited to requiring that treasury animals be alive.

The upshot, as the lecturer notes, is that *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading is clearly the stronger one. The *tanna kamma*'s unqualified language sounds like a universal rule.

## Sifra Baraita

### Pasuk Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״אִם בְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ מִמֶּנָּה״ — בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין שֶׁיִּפָּדוּ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The verse states: "And **if** it be any non-kosher **animal, of which they** may not **bring an offering** unto the Lord, then he shall set the animal before the priest. And the priest shall value it" (Leviticus 27:11–12). The Sages teach that **the verse is speaking of blemished** animals, **that they can be redeemed.** The verse indicates that one redeems a blemished animal by means of standing and valuation.

A *baraita* is then cited in support of *Rabbi Yochanan*. The *gemara* opens with the *pasuk* (Vayikra 27): *ve'im bechol behemah tme'ah asher lo yakrivu mimena korban l'Hashem* — "if it is an animal from which one does not bring a *korban* to God." The question is what kind of animal this *pasuk* describes. The *baraita* explains: *b'ba'alei mumin shenistau hakatuv medaber* — it is speaking of animals with blemishes that are thereby redeemed. A blemished animal cannot be brought as a *korban* and so falls into the category of "an animal from which one does not bring." One might think the *pasuk* is talking about a *behemah tme'ah*, a literally non-kosher animal. But a separate *pasuk* later — *ve'im bivehemah hatme'ah ufadah be'erkecha* — already addresses non-kosher animals straightforwardly. So the emphasis of this first *pasuk* falls on the phrase *asher lo yakrivu mimena korban* — an animal that cannot be brought as a *korban*. That animal is a kosher one with a permanent blemish. Further, the verse specifies: *yachol yipadu al muman over* — one might have thought the rule applies even to a transitory blemish. The *pasuk* therefore qualifies the case as *she'einah kreivah kol ikar* — an animal that can never be brought under any circumstances. Something with a passing blemish that will heal cannot be redeemed; only a permanently blemished animal qualifies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אִם בִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה וּפָדָה בְעֶרְכֶּךָ״ — הֲרֵי בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה אָמוּר,
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Do you say** that the verse is **speaking of blemished animals; or** perhaps **it is** referring **only to a non-kosher animal,** and it is teaching that a non-kosher animal that was consecrated for Temple maintenance is redeemed through standing and valuation. The *baraita* answers: **When it says** later in that passage: "And **if it be of a non-kosher animal, then he shall redeem it according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:27), **a non-kosher animal** is thereby **stated** as subject to redemption.

The *pasuk* then continues with the remedy: *ve'he'emido lifnei hakohen ve'he'erich oto hakohen* — stand it before the *kohen* and have the *kohen* assess it. This is the *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* requirement: the animal must be alive and present when evaluated.

### Korbanot Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: עֲבֵיד לַהּ הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its reasoning: **And the Merciful One states** that one should **perform standing and valuation for** a blemished animal, in accordance with the verse "Then he shall set the animal before the priest. And the priest shall value it." Since the verse requires that the animal have a permanent blemish to be redeemed, it must be referring to an animal consecrated for the altar, as all animals consecrated for Temple maintenance may be redeemed, even unblemished ones. Furthermore, as this is an unattributed *baraita* that appears in the *Sifra*, it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the disputant of Rabbi Shimon, and therefore it follows the opinion of the Rabbis of the mishna. It may therefore be concluded from the *baraita* that the Rabbis hold that one redeems an animal consecrated for the altar with standing and valuation, as maintained by Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The significance of this *baraita* is clear. It is discussing a blemished *korban* — an animal that was sanctified as a sacrifice and then developed a permanent *mum* — and it applies to such an animal the requirement of *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*. This directly supports *Rabbi Yochanan*: the *tanna kamma* holds that even animals sanctified as *korbanot* must be alive in order to be redeemed. *Reish Lakish*'s claim that the *tanna kamma* exempts *kodshei mizbe'ach* from this requirement is contradicted by the *baraita*, which places *korbanot* squarely within *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*.

### Attribution

There is an additional reason to attribute this *baraita* to the *tanna kamma* who argues against *Rabbi Shimon*. The *baraita* appears in the *Sifra*, the *Torat Kohanim* — the Tannaitic midrash on Vayikra — and the *stam* of the *Sifra* is attributed to *Rabbi Yehuda*. *Rabbi Yehuda* is the classic *bar plugta* of *Rabbi Shimon* throughout *Shas*, and since our *mishna* presents a *machloket* between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *chachamim*, the presumed *chachamim* is *Rabbi Yehuda*. The *baraita* in the *Sifra* thus reflects *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position — the *tanna kamma* — and it clearly requires *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* for *korbanot*. This further strengthens *Rabbi Yochanan*.

## Tov Ve'ra Source

### Bein Tov U'vein Ra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר לְרַבָּנַן קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהֶעֱרִיךְ הַכֹּהֵן אוֹתָהּ בֵּין טוֹב וּבֵין רָע״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the opinion of Reish Lakish. **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: What is the reasoning of Reish Lakish, who said** that according **to the Rabbis,** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were included** in the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** whereas animals **consecrated for the altar were not included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation?** The reasoning is **that the verse states:** "Then he shall set the animal before the priest. **And the priest shall value it, whether it be good or bad"** (Leviticus 27:12).

Despite the weight of evidence favoring *Rabbi Yochanan*, the *gemara* now presents *Reish Lakish*'s textual basis. The motivation for *Reish Lakish*'s position comes from the *pasuk* itself. When describing the process of *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*, the verse states: *ve'he'erich hakohen otah bein tov uvein ra* — the *kohen* shall assess it, whether good or bad. In the technical language of *Temurah*, "good or bad" refers to blemished or unblemished. *Reish Lakish* reads this as implying that the distinction between blemished and unblemished is irrelevant — you assess it regardless. And what type of animal is it irrelevant whether it has a *mum* or not?

### Bedek Habayit Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ חָלוּק בֵּין טוֹב לְרַע? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְאָמַר קְרָא ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav explained: **What is an item** for **which** the *halakha* **is not divided between good and bad,** i.e., where there is no difference between unblemished and blemished animals? **You must say** that **this** is an item **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** which has the same sanctity whether blemished or unblemished. **And the verse states:** "And the priest shall value **it."** The restrictive term "it" serves **to exclude** animals **consecrated for the altar** from the *halakha* of standing and valuation.

The answer, argues *Reish Lakish*, is *kodshei bedek habayis* — animals sanctified for the temple treasury. For *kodshei mizbe'ach*, the distinction between blemished and unblemished is critical: an unblemished animal cannot be redeemed at all, and a blemished one can. The dichotomy between "good" and "bad" is not irrelevant there — it is decisive. But for the treasury, you can in principle redeem whether blemished or unblemished. The phrase *bein tov uvein ra*, suggesting indifference to that distinction, therefore points to *bedek habayis*. The word *otah* — "it" — then serves to exclude *kodshei mizbe'ach* from the requirement.

### Atem Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who says that according to the Rabbis, animals consecrated for the altar are also included in the obligation of standing and valuation, the term **"it"** serves **to exclude what?** The Gemara responds: It serves **to exclude** from the *halakha* of standing and valuation an animal that was **blemished from the outset** and was consecrated for the altar.

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds: granted that *bein tov uvein ra* points to *bedek habayis*, what does *otah* exclude? *Leima'utei mai*? The *gemara* answers: *otah* excludes *ba'al mum me'ikaro* — an animal that was already blemished when it was sanctified as a *korban*. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading of the *pasuk* thus covers both *korbanot* (from the phrase *asher lo yakrivu*) and *bedek habayis* (from *bein tov uvein ra*), while using *otah* to carve out the exceptional case of an animal that bore a *mum* from the very beginning of its consecration. That animal — never having been fit for the altar, never having achieved *kedushas haguf* — does not require the living condition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּלְתַנָּא דְּבֵי לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר דַּאֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, וְכֵן תָּנֵי לֵוִי בְּמַתְנִיתֵיהּ: וַאֲפִילּוּ חַיָּה, וַאֲפִילּוּ עוֹפוֹת, וְהָכְתִיב ״אוֹתָהּ״! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** this is problematic **according to the *tanna*** of the **school of Levi, who said that even** an animal that was **blemished from the outset** is **included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation. As Levi taught: Everything was included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation, even** an animal **blemished from the outset. And Levi taught likewise in his *baraita*: Even an undomesticated animal, and even birds** that are not brought as offerings, e.g., geese, are included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation. But **isn't it written "it,"** which must serve to exclude some animal from the obligation of standing and valuation? If so, how can Levi teach that everything is included? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this poses **a difficulty.**

The *gemara* then mentions a third, more extreme opinion: that of the *tanna d'vei Levi*, who says *hakol hayu bichlal ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* — everything requires *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*, even an animal that was already blemished from the outset when sanctified as a *korban*, and even a non-kosher animal sanctified in some fashion. According to this view, every sanctified animal must be alive to be redeemed. The word *otah*, which on any normal reading must exclude something, stands as a difficulty — *kashya* — that this extreme view cannot resolve.

## R. Shimon's Logic

### R. Shimon's Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת לֹא הָיוּ? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהֶעֱרִיךְ הַכֹּהֵן אוֹתָהּ בֵּין טוֹב וּבֵין רָע״, אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁחָלוּק בֵּין טוֹב וּבֵין רָע? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְאָמַר קְרָא ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the opinion of Rabbi Shimon in the mishna. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon, who said** that animals **consecrated for the altar were included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** and therefore if they died without redemption they are buried, but animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were not** included in this *halakha*? **As the verse states:** "Then he shall set the animal before the priest. **And the priest shall value it, whether it be good or bad"** (Leviticus 27:11–12). **What is an item** for **which** the *halakha* **is divided between good and bad,** i.e., between an unblemished and blemished animal? **You must say** that **this** is an animal **consecrated for the altar, and the verse states:** "And the priest shall value **it,"** which serves **to exclude** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance** from the *halakha* of standing and valuation.

The *gemara* then turns to *Rabbi Shimon*'s independent derivation. *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* presents it: *mai ta'amei d'Rabbi Shimon* — what is *Rabbi Shimon*'s reasoning? The *pasuk* states *bein tov uvein ra*. *Rabbi Shimon* reads this not as "regardless of whether blemished or unblemished," but as indicating an animal for which the distinction between blemished and unblemished *matters*. Which sanctified animal has a consequential *tov/ra* distinction? Only one where the presence or absence of a *mum* changes the *halacha* entirely.

### Kodshei Mizbe'ach

That animal, says *Rabbi Shimon*, is *kodshei mizbe'ach*. For a sacrifice, the question of whether it is blemished or unblemished is of the utmost significance — it determines whether the animal can be brought at all. The *pasuk* saying it should be assessed *bein tov uvein ra* therefore signals that it is referring to *kodshei mizbe'ach*.

### Atem Excludes Treasury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִי הָכִי, ״בֵּין טוֹב לְרָע״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the verse is referring to a consecration for which there is a distinction between good and bad, then it **should have** stated: And the priest shall value it **between good and bad,** to indicate that this distinction is made. The term "whether it be good or bad" indicates that the two types have the same *halakha*, as claimed by the Rabbis. The Gemara concludes: This poses **a difficulty** for the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

*Otah*, on *Rabbi Shimon*'s reading, then excludes *bedek habayis* — it is *kodshei mizbe'ach* that must be alive, while animals sanctified for the treasury are exempt from *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*. The *gemara* notes the objection to *Rabbi Shimon*'s hermeneutic: if *bein tov uvein ra* is meant to signal a type of animal where the distinction *matters*, the text should have said *bein tov lera* — "distinguishing between good and bad." The conjunction *u'vein* suggests both are equally valid, pointing toward indifference. *Taku*, the *gemara* leaves this as a difficulty against *Rabbi Shimon*.

## Meitivei Baraita

### Temimim & Mumim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מֵיתִיבִי: מֵתוּ תְּמִימִין — יִקָּבְרוּ, וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין — יִפָּדוּ. בַּמֶּה דְבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — בֵּין תְּמִימִין בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִים יִקָּבֵרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: With regard to consecrated animals that **died,** if they were **unblemished** they are not redeemed and **shall be buried, and** if they were **blemished they can be redeemed. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to** animals **consecrated for the altar. But with regard to** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance, both unblemished and blemished** animals **shall be buried.** This is the statement of the Rabbis.

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that generates significant complexity. The *baraita* states: animals that were sanctified and died — if they are unblemished (*temimim*), they must be buried; if they are blemished (*ba'alei mumim*), they may be redeemed. When does this apply? When they are *kodshei mizbe'ach*. But if they are *kodshei bedek habayis*, whether blemished or unblemished, they must be buried.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ וְאֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, תְּמִימִים — יִקָּבְרוּ, בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין — יִפָּדוּ. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרֵישָׁא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: Both** in a case where the animals that died were **consecrated for the altar and** in a case where they were **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** if they were **unblemished they can be buried,** and if they were **blemished they can be redeemed.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, from the first clause** of the *baraita*, as Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that according to the Rabbis, both animals consecrated for the altar and those consecrated for Temple maintenance are included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and are buried if they died.

This *baraita* presents a notable structure. The *tanna kamma* it reflects says that for *kodshei mizbe'ach*, blemished animals can be redeemed but unblemished ones must be buried. For *bedek habayis*, everything must be buried. *Rabbi Shimon* then adds his ruling: for both *kodshei mizbe'ach* and *kodshei bedek habayis*, unblemished must be buried and blemished may be redeemed.

Several important ideas are being introduced here. First, the rule that unblemished animals cannot be redeemed even for the treasury — even though in principle, according to *Rabbi Shimon*, the treasury category does not require the living condition. The reason, as will become clear, is that unblemished animals could theoretically have served as *korbanot*, and we therefore treat them as though they were sacrificial animals. *Rashi* views this as a rabbinic concern about the impropriety of redeeming what could have been a *korban*. *Tosafot* offers a distinct technical explanation: death does not constitute a *mum*, and therefore an unblemished animal that died cannot be redeemed without a *mum* — even if death itself has rendered it useless.

### First Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could **say to you: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing **with** an animal that was **blemished from the outset.** In such a case, the Rabbis rule that the animal was not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and is therefore redeemed if it dies. But a consecrated animal that subsequently developed a blemish and died must be buried.

The *gemara* initially reads the *baraita* as supporting *Reish Lakish*. The phrase *ba'alei mumim yipadu* — blemished animals may be redeemed — is initially understood as referring to animals that acquired their *mum* after consecration. If that reading is correct, then the *tanna kamma* is permitting the redemption of *korbanot* that died with a *mum* that came upon them later — which is precisely *Reish Lakish*'s position, that *kodshei mizbe'ach* do not require *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* according to the *tanna kamma*. This, the *gemara* says, appears to contradict *Rabbi Yochanan*: *tiyuvta de'Rabbi Yochanan meireisha*.

### Ba'al Mum Me'ikaro

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds: the *baraita* is discussing *ba'al mum me'ikaro* — an animal that bore its *mum* from the very beginning. That is the case where everyone, including the *tanna kamma*, agrees the animal may be redeemed even after death. And indeed, this interpretation is confirmed by the logic of *Rabbi Shimon*'s own position. *Rabbi Shimon* holds unambiguously that *kodshei mizbe'ach* must be alive to be redeemed. If the *baraita* were discussing an animal that acquired its *mum* after consecration, *Rabbi Shimon* would have explicitly disagreed with the *tanna kamma*'s permission to redeem it. The fact that *Rabbi Shimon* does not dispute this ruling in the *baraita* demonstrates that the *mum* must be *me'ikaro* — present from the start — since that is the only scenario in which *Rabbi Shimon* concedes that a dead *korban* may be redeemed.

### Alignment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי תֵּימָא שֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּישָׁן אֶת מוּמָן — לִיפְלוֹג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן עֲלַהּ, אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינָּה בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **So too, it is reasonable** that the *baraita* is discussing animals blemished from the outset, **as, if you say that** it is referring to animals **whose consecrations preceded their blemishes,** then **Rabbi Shimon should disagree with** the Rabbis on this point and maintain that such blemished animals consecrated for the altar should be buried, not redeemed. After all, Rabbi Shimon holds that animals consecrated for the altar are included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** this that the *baraita* is dealing **with** an animal that is **blemished from the outset?**

The *gemara* confirms this inference: *ela lav shema mina* — we learn from here that *ba'alei mumim* in the *baraita* means *ba'al mum me'ikaro*, because only then do *Rabbi Shimon* and the *tanna kamma* align. This supports *Rabbi Yochanan*'s framework perfectly.

## Alt. Text & Shitur

### Reish Lakish Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא, לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּשֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּישָׁן אֶת מוּמָן, אִי הָכִי, נִיפְלוֹג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן עֲלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if the *baraita* is referring to animals blemished from the outset, and for this reason the Rabbis hold that the animal is redeemed, then it may be inferred that the Rabbis maintain that if the animal developed a blemish after its consecration and died, it must be buried. If so, **let us say** that the *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** who maintains that according to the Rabbis, animals consecrated for the altar were not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and are redeemed if they died. The Gemara responds: **Reish Lakish interprets** the *baraita* as referring **to** animals **whose consecrations preceded their blemishes.** Consequently, the statement of the Rabbis is in accordance with his opinion that animals consecrated for the altar were not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and are therefore redeemed if they died. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then the earlier question recurs, as **Rabbi Shimon should disagree with** the statement of the Rabbis and say that such blemished animals consecrated for the altar are buried.

At this point, *Reish Lakish* is said to have a different *girsa* — a different version of the *baraita* — which is somewhat more ambiguous about *Rabbi Shimon*'s position. In this alternative text, *Rabbi Shimon* is not explicit about whether the blemished case he permits refers to a *korban* where the blemish came later or where it was present from the start. This ambiguity gives *Reish Lakish* more room to maneuver.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵיפָךְ אָפֵיךְ, וְהָכִי מוֹתֵיב מִמְּכִילְתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי: מֵתוּ, בֵּין תְּמִימִין בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין — יִקָּבֵרוּ. [בַּמֶּה דְבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — יִפָּדוּ.]
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reish Lakish reverses** the text of that *baraita*, i.e., he maintains that in that *baraita*, Rabbi Shimon explicitly states that animals consecrated for the altar are buried, whether they are unblemished or blemished. **And** he **raises** his **objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan **from another *baraita*, like this:** With regard to consecrated animals that **died, whether** they were **unblemished or blemished, they shall be buried. With regard to what** case is this ruling **stated?** It is stated **with regard to** animals **consecrated for the altar, but in** the case of animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance, they can be redeemed.** This is the statement of the Rabbis.

With this different version of the text, *Reish Lakish* can argue as follows. The *tanna kamma* permits redeeming a blemished *korban* even when the blemish came later — demonstrating that the *tanna kamma* does not require the living condition for *kodshei mizbe'ach*. And *Rabbi Shimon*, in *Reish Lakish*'s reading of this alternative *baraita*, does not explicitly endorse the *tanna kamma*'s ruling in the *korban* case; he is only distinguishing between blemished and unblemished, which could be a reference limited to *bedek habayis*. Therefore, on this reading, *Rabbi Shimon* is silently disagreeing with the *tanna kamma* about the case where a *korban* acquired its *mum* later. The *gemara* notes, however, that even this alternative version ultimately runs into the same difficulties, and the stronger textual case remains with *Rabbi Yochanan*.

## Dead Korbanot Issue

### Rav Yirmiyah Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר לְרַבָּנַן: קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, וְקָתָנֵי ״קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין יִפָּדוּ״, וּמוֹקְמִינַן לָהּ בְּשֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּישָׁן אֶת מוּמָן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further discusses the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish. **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira: According to Reish Lakish, who says** that **according to the Rabbis,** animals **consecrated for the altar were not included** in the *halakha* of **standing and valuation, and it is taught** with regard to animals **consecrated for the altar** that **blemished** animals **can be redeemed; and we established** that according to Reish Lakish, the *baraita* is dealing **with** animals **whose consecrations preceded their blemishes,** which means that although the animal has inherent sanctity because it was consecrated when unblemished, nevertheless it can be redeemed; let us **conclude from** the *baraita* that **one may redeem sacrificial** animals in order **to feed them to dogs.**

*Rav Yirmiyah* poses a pointed question to *Rabbi Zeira*. According to *Reish Lakish*, the *rabbanan* — the *tanna kamma* — permit redeeming *kodshei mizbe'ach* even when dead, since they hold that the living requirement does not apply to sacrificial animals. But this generates a serious problem.

### Keluvim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּשֶׁעָבַר וּשְׁחָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: One cannot arrive at this conclusion from the *baraita*, since when the *baraita* states that the animals died it is not referring to deaths due to natural causes. Rather, **what are we dealing with here? With** a case **where** one **transgressed** the prohibition against slaughtering the blemished animals **and slaughtered them** before they were redeemed, an act that nevertheless renders them permitted for human consumption after redemption.

If a *korban* dies a natural death and then is redeemed, what does the owner do with it? He feeds it to the dogs. And that is precisely the problem of *podim et hakodshim le'ha'akhilam la'klavim* — redeeming sanctified animals for the purpose of feeding them to dogs — which is explicitly prohibited in the context of *korbanot*. So even if we grant that there is no technical requirement of *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* for *kodshei mizbe'ach* according to *Reish Lakish*, how do we avoid this problem?

### Shechut Chutz Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּדְתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁנָּפַל בָּהֶן מוּם, וּשְׁחָטָן, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יִקָּבֵרוּ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **all sacrificial** animals **that developed a blemish and** one **slaughtered them, Rabbi Meir says** that **they shall be buried,** as he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon that animals consecrated for the altar were included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and must be buried if they died. **And the Rabbis say:** Since an animal consecrated for the altar is not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation, even if one slaughtered it, **it can be redeemed.**

The *gemara* answers: *hacha bemai askinan — beshe'avar ve'shachtan* — we are dealing with a case where the animal was already *shechted*, albeit improperly. The person violated the prohibition and *shechted* the *korban* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Now that it has been *shechted*, redeeming it will not result in feeding it to dogs — once redeemed, it can actually be eaten. This is precisely the case discussed in the subsequent *baraita*: *kol hakodashim she'naflu bahem mum ve'shachtan* — a *korban* that acquired a *mum* and was then *shechted* without having been redeemed first. *Rabbi Meir* says it must be buried, likely as a *knas* for the improper slaughter. *Ve'chachamim* say it may be redeemed — and this represents the explicit permission of the *chachamim* to redeem a dead *korban*, which is the view consistent with *Reish Lakish*.

## Blemished Korban Baraita

### Ba'al Mum Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּלְדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ פָּטוּר, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **their offspring and their milk are permitted after their redemption. And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **exempt** from *karet*, even if the animal was not yet redeemed, **and** those animals **do not render** an animal exchanged for them **a substitute. And if** these animals **died** before they were redeemed, **they may be redeemed.**

A further *baraita* is then introduced, spelling out the full set of *halachot* governing an animal that was sanctified as a *korban* while already bearing a permanent *mum* — *ba'al mum me'ikaro*. The *baraita* describes a comprehensive picture of how the complete removal of *kedushas haguf* in this case affects every applicable *halacha*.

### Permissibility Details

Since the *kedusha* in this case was never a genuine *kedushas haguf*, the animal upon redemption sheds its *kedushah* entirely. Therefore: its firstborn is subject to the laws of *bechor*; if *shechted*, the *matanot* — the *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keiva* — are given as usual; it may be shorn and worked (*yotzim le'chulin le'gozez ula'avod*); and its offspring and milk (*velad ve'chalav*) are permitted without restriction. These rules stand in sharp contrast to an animal that was sanctified and later acquired a *mum*, where shearing, working, and the milk and offspring remain bound by the *kedusha* even after redemption.

### Temura & Redemption

The *baraita* further specifies: *ein osim temurah* — such an animal cannot serve as the basis for *temurah*. This is consistent with the principle established throughout *Temurah* that *temurah* requires *bein tov lera bein ra letov* — a genuine *kedushas haguf* that at one point was fit for the altar. Since *ba'al mum me'ikaro* never had such fitness, no *temurah* can be made from it. And then, directly relevant to our discussion: *ve'im meisu yipadu* — if such an animal dies, it may be redeemed.

## Attribution Dispute

### Rav Yehuda's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת לֹא הָיוּ, כְּדִתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** with regard to this mishna: **This** statement, that one who consecrates a blemished animal for the altar may redeem it if it dies, is in accordance with **the statement of Rabbi Shimon, who says** that animals **consecrated for the altar were included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** but animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were not** included. **As we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Shimon says: If** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance died, they can be redeemed,** since they were not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation. But animals consecrated for the altar that died are buried, as they were included in this *halakha*.

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* asserts: *zu divrei Rabbi Shimon* — this is the position of *Rabbi Shimon* alone, not the *tanna kamma*. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, a *ba'al mum me'ikaro* is effectively like *bedek habayis* since it never bore *kedushas haguf*, and it therefore does not require *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha*. Hence it can be redeemed after death. The *pasuk*'s word *otah*, on *Rabbi Shimon*'s reading, excludes *bedek habayis* from the requirement — and *ba'al mum me'ikaro* falls into that same excluded category.

### Attribution Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁנִּפְדֶּה, מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּאֲמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹתָהּ״, ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ הָיָה בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon concedes with regard to** an animal that was **blemished from the outset,** which was consecrated for the altar and died, **that it is redeemed. What is the reason? As the verse states:** "Then he shall set the animal before the priest. And the priest shall value **it"** (Leviticus 27:11–12). The emphasis of **"it"** serves **to exclude** an animal that was **blemished from the outset** from the *halakha* of standing and valuation. **But the Rabbis say:** Every animal consecrated for the altar, **even** one that was **blemished from the outset, was included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** and must be buried if it dies. This apparently contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says that according to the Rabbis an animal that was blemished from the outset was not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation.

This attribution to *Rabbi Shimon* alone raises an immediate question: why is the ruling ascribed only to *Rabbi Shimon*? The *tanna kamma* would equally appear to allow redeeming a dead *ba'al mum me'ikaro*. Should the *baraita* not have said *zu divrei Rabbi Shimon u'machlokto* — the view of *Rabbi Shimon* and the *tanna kamma* together?

### Reish Lakish Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן חֲכָמִים? תַּנָּא דְּבֵי לֵוִי. אִי הָכִי, ״זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן״ וְתוּ לָא? ״זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וּמַחְלוּקְתּוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Pappa, or Rava: The statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav does not pose a difficulty for Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rav was not referring to the Rabbis of the mishna, who disagree with Rabbi Shimon. Rather, **who is** the *tanna* referred to here as **the Rabbis?** It is **a *tanna* of the school of Levi.** The other rabbi questioned Abaye's answer: **If so,** that even the Rabbis of the mishna hold that an animal that was blemished from the outset is redeemed, why did Rav Yehuda say that Rav said that **this** mishna in tractate *Bekhorot* is in accordance with **the statement of Rabbi Shimon, and nothing more?** This statement indicates that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon alone. **He should have** said that **this** mishna is in accordance with the **statement of Rabbi Shimon and** those who **disagree** with **him,** i.e., the Rabbis.

The reason the *baraita* attributes the ruling only to *Rabbi Shimon* is that the compiler held like *Reish Lakish*. And according to *Reish Lakish*, the *tanna kamma* permits redeeming *kodshei mizbe'ach* even when dead — even when the *mum* came later. So what the *baraita* says about *ba'al mum me'ikaro* being redeemable is something the *tanna kamma* trivially agrees to in any case, since the *tanna kamma* allows redeeming dead *korbanot* generally. Therefore the ruling in the *baraita* — that a *ba'al mum me'ikaro* may be redeemed when dead — is not distinctive of the *tanna kamma*'s position. It is only *Rabbi Shimon*'s position that requires this specific clause, because *Rabbi Shimon* normally insists that *kodshei mizbe'ach* must be alive — but carves out the exception of *ba'al mum me'ikaro*.

### Tanna Kamma Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי דְּלָא קָתָנֵי הָכִי, מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב סָבַר לַהּ כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר לְרַבָּנַן: קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him:** The reason **that** Rav Yehuda **does not teach** in **this** manner, i.e., he does not say that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, is **because Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, who said** that **according to the Rabbis** of the mishna, only animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation;** animals **consecrated for the altar were not** included, and are therefore redeemed.

The underlying logic of the attribution is thus as follows. Had the *baraita* been written according to *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading of the *tanna kamma*, the attribution should have covered both opinions. But since the *baraita* is being read through *Reish Lakish*'s lens — where the *tanna kamma* broadly permits redemption of dead *korbanot* — only *Rabbi Shimon*'s position requires the explicit qualifier about *ba'al mum me'ikaro*. Hence the attribution to *Rabbi Shimon* alone.

## Temimim Restriction

### Unblemished Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרֵישָׁא קָתָנֵי: ״וְאִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ״, וְסֵיפָא קָתָנֵי: ״אִם מֵתוּ יִקָּבֵרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** admittedly **the first clause** of the mishna can be explained in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, as it **teaches** that **if they died they can be redeemed.** This ruling is in accordance with the Rabbis, as an animal that was blemished from the outset is considered as one consecrated for the altar, since as it was consecrated in order to be sold to purchase a valid offering. But **the latter clause** of the mishna, which discusses a case where the consecration of the animal preceded the development of a blemish, is not in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, as it **teaches** that **if they died they shall be buried.** According to Reish Lakish and Rav, the Rabbis hold that in such a case the animal is redeemed. Consequently, the mishna cannot be in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis.

With the *ba'al mum* side of the *baraita* now resolved, the *gemara* turns to a new question. Why does the *baraita* prohibit redeeming unblemished animals — *temimim* — even when they were sanctified only for the temple treasury? According to *Rabbi Shimon*, *bedek habayis* does not require *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* at all. Why then should an unblemished *bedek habayis* animal that dies be subject to burial?

### Mizbe'ach Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ: משום דַּחֲזוּ לְהַקְרָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira responds: It is **because they are fit for sacrifice** upon the altar.

The answer, as *Rabbi Zeira* explains to *Rav Yirmiyah*, is: *mishum de'chazu le'hakrava* — because unblemished animals are fit for sacrifice. Even though this particular animal was dedicated to the treasury rather than the altar, its physical fitness for sacrifice means we cannot simply discard it as ordinary property. We treat it as though it were a *korban*, and since a dead *korban* cannot be redeemed according to *Rabbi Shimon*, the same rule applies here.

### Treasury to Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּדְתַנְיָא: הַמַּתְפִּיס תְּמִימִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, כְּשֶׁהֵן נִפְדִּין — אֵינָן נִפְדִּין אֶלָּא לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, שֶׁכׇּל הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִידֵי מִזְבֵּחַ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the significance of the fact that these animals, which were consecrated for Temple maintenance, are fit for the altar. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who associates unblemished** animals, i.e., consecrates them by means of associating their sanctity with sanctified items, **for Temple maintenance,** when they are redeemed by the treasurer **they are redeemed only for the altar,** i.e., they are sold to those who must bring an offering, and the animals are sacrificed upon the altar. They may be redeemed only for the altar, not for any non-sacred use, **as any** consecrated item **that is fit** to be sacrificed **on the altar may never leave** the custody of the **altar.**

This principle is supported by a further *baraita*: *ha'makdish temimim le'bedek habayis* — one who dedicates unblemished animals to the temple treasury. When they are to be redeemed, *einam nifdim ela le'mizbe'ach* — they are sold only to a buyer who will bring them as a *korban*. The treasury does not simply sell them to the highest bidder; they must pass to someone who will use them for sacrifice.

### Perpetual Claim

The *baraita* articulates the principle explicitly: *she'kol ha'ra'uy le'mizbe'ach eino yotzei mi'yedei mizbe'ach le'olam* — anything fit for the altar never completely exits the altar's domain. Once an unblemished animal has been sanctified — even for the treasury — it can never be fully diverted from its potential as a *korban*. It must always be channeled back to the altar. This is the reason that even for *bedek habayis*, an unblemished animal that dies must be buried rather than redeemed: to redeem it for secular use would be to permanently remove from the altar's domain an animal that could have been a *korban*.

## Final Resolution

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רָבָא: לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּמוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, וְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ דְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ דְּלָא הָוֵי בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה. וְלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to analyze the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish. **Rav Pappa said to Abaye, and some say** that **Rava** said to Abaye: **According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who interprets** the above *baraita* as referring **to** an animal **blemished from the outset, and** who further claims that **everyone,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis, **holds that** an animal **blemished from the outset was not included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** one may ask: **And** do they both agree that an animal blemished from the outset was **not** included in that *halakha*?

*Rav Pappa* now challenges the assumption that has quietly underpinned the entire preceding discussion. The *gemara* had been working with the premise that everyone — both *Rabbi Shimon* and the *tanna kamma* — agrees that *ba'al mum me'ikaro*, when sanctified as a *korban* and subsequently dying, may be redeemed. *Rav Pappa* challenges this directly.

### Mishna Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתְנַן: כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁקָּדַם מוּם קָבוּעַ לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן וְנִפְדּוּ, חַיָּיבִין בַּבְּכוֹרָה וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְיוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין לִיגָּזֵז וְלֵיעָבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 14a): **All the sacrificial** animals in **which a permanent blemish preceded their consecration** are not sanctified as offerings; rather, they are sold and the money received is used to purchase valid offerings. **And** once **they are redeemed,** they **are obligated in,** i.e., subject to accounting their offspring, **a firstborn; and in the** priestly **gifts** of the foreleg, jaw, and maw; **and they emerge** from their sacred status and assume complete **non-sacred** status in order **to be shorn and to be utilized for labor.**

The *mishna* in *Temurah* explicitly lists the distinctive *halachot* of an animal sanctified as a *korban* while already bearing a permanent *mum*: its firstborn may be taken, its *matanot* given, it may be shorn and worked, and if it dies — *ve'im meisu yipadu* — it may be redeemed. If this were a unanimous position, why would it need to be stated at all?

### R. Shimon Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת לֹא הָיוּ, כְּדִתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** once **they are redeemed,** they **are obligated in,** i.e., subject to accounting their offspring, **a firstborn; and in the** priestly **gifts** of the foreleg, jaw, and maw; **and they emerge** from their sacred status and assume complete **non-sacred** status in order **to be shorn and to be utilized for labor. And** if these animals **died** before they were redeemed, **they may be redeemed. And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** with regard to this mishna: **This** statement, that one who consecrates a blemished animal for the altar may redeem it if it dies, is in accordance with **the statement of Rabbi Shimon, who says** that animals **consecrated for the altar were included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** but animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance were not** included. **As we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Shimon says: If** animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance died, they can be redeemed,** since they were not included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation. But animals consecrated for the altar that died are buried, as they were included in this *halakha*.

*Rav Yehuda* responds: *zu divrei Rabbi Shimon* — this is only *Rabbi Shimon*'s position. It is *Rabbi Shimon* who holds that *ba'al mum me'ikaro* sanctified as a *korban* can be redeemed after death, because *Rabbi Shimon* limits the *ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* requirement strictly to genuine *kodshei mizbe'ach* and excludes anything that was never fit for the altar.

### Chachamim Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁנִּפְדֶּה, מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּאֲמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹתָהּ״, ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ הָיָה בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon concedes with regard to** an animal that was **blemished from the outset,** which was consecrated for the altar and died, **that it is redeemed. What is the reason? As the verse states:** "Then he shall set the animal before the priest. And the priest shall value **it"** (Leviticus 27:11–12). The emphasis of **"it"** serves **to exclude** an animal that was **blemished from the outset** from the *halakha* of standing and valuation. **But the Rabbis say:** Every animal consecrated for the altar, **even** one that was **blemished from the outset, was included in** the *halakha* of **standing and valuation,** and must be buried if it dies.

The *chachamim* — the *tanna kamma* — disagree even in this case. According to the *chachamim*, even a *ba'al mum me'ikaro* that was sanctified as a *korban* requires the living condition. The sanctification, however imperfect, was a *korban*-type dedication, and the *chachamim* do not carve out this exception.

### Tanna d'bei Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן חֲכָמִים? תַּנָּא דְּבֵי לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Pappa, or Rava: The statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav does not pose a difficulty for Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rav was not referring to the Rabbis of the mishna, who disagree with Rabbi Shimon. Rather, **who is** the *tanna* referred to here as **the Rabbis?** It is **a *tanna* of the school of Levi.**

This stringent *chachamim* position is identified with the *tanna d'bei Levi*, the extreme opinion encountered earlier. *Tanna d'bei Levi* holds that *hakol hayu bichlal ha'amadah ve'ha'aracha* — everything must be alive — and it is this view that insists even *ba'al mum me'ikaro* cannot be redeemed after death. This is not the mainstream *tanna kamma* of the *mishna*, but the outlying, maximalist position.

### Final Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, רַב כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ ״זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וּמַחְלוּקְתּוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, אֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that the Rabbis maintain that all consecrations were included in the *halakha* of standing and valuation and are buried if they died, and the mishna is in fact in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And** as for **that** which posed **a difficulty for you,** that Rav Yehuda **should have** said that **this** mishna is in accordance with **the statement of Rabbi Shimon and** those who **disagree** with **him, say: Indeed** he said so, that the mishna is in accordance with **the opinion of** Rabbi Shimon and those who disagree with him.

The *gemara* ultimately resolves that *ve'ibayeit eima* — one may also say: *Rav s'veira lei k'Rabbi Yochanan* — Rav holds like *Rabbi Yochanan*. And according to *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading of the *tanna kamma*, the *tanna kamma* does agree that *ba'al mum me'ikaro* can be redeemed. The ruling in the *mishna* therefore represents the position of *Rabbi Shimon* and *the tanna kamma* together. The difficulty of the attribution — why did it say only *Rabbi Shimon* and not both? — is resolved by noting that the language can simply be adjusted: *eima hachi nami* — so understand it that way too. The only opinion that stands in genuine isolation is the extreme position of *tanna d'vei Levi*, who would not permit any redemption of a dead *korban*, even *ba'al mum me'ikaro*. The normative ruling, consistent with *Rabbi Yochanan* and the *tanna kamma*, is that an animal which bore its *mum* from the very beginning of its consecration as a *korban* may indeed be redeemed after death. With that, the *daf* concludes — and nearly so does the *masechet*.
