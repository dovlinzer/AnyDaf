# Menachot 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dried vs Fresh Figs

### Measurement Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: תּוֹרְמִין תְּאֵנִים עַל הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת, בְּמִנְיָן. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן מְשַׁעֲרִינַן – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ כְּמוֹת שֶׁהֵן – הָוֵה לֵיהּ מַרְבֶּה בְּמַעַשְׂרוֹת. וּתְנַן: הַמַּרְבֶּה בְּמַעַשְׂרוֹת – פֵּירוֹתָיו מְתוּקָּנִים, ומַעְשְׂרוֹתָיו מְקוּלְקָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute over whether food is to be measured in its current volume or according to its initial volume. **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 4:2): **One may separate *teruma*** and tithes from fresh **figs for dried figs,** which have shrunk and are now smaller than they were when they were fresh. In other words, one may designate fresh figs as *teruma* and tithe to exempt the dried figs, despite the difference between these two types of figs. This separation may be performed only **by number,** e.g., ten fresh figs for ninety dried figs. One may not set aside this *teruma* by volume, i.e., by separating fresh figs with a volume of one-tenth of the measure of dried figs. The reason is that the volume of the fresh figs is greater than that of the dried figs, so he would set aside fewer fresh figs than he would if he calculated by number.
>
> The Gemara analyzes this *halakha*. **Granted, if you say** that **one measures** food items **as they were** initially, then since when the obligation to separate *teruma* began, the volume of the dried figs was the same as the fresh ones, then it is **well;** the amount of figs to be separated as *teruma* should be calculated based on number, disregarding their current volume. **But if you say** that foods are to be measured **as they** currently **are,** then since the volume of the dried figs is smaller than that of the fresh figs, he will separate a larger amount than necessary, and **this** case is an example of one who **increases** his **tithes.**
>
> **And we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Demai* 8:10): In the case of **one who increases** his **tithes,** i.e., he designates more than one-tenth of the produce as tithe, the remainder of **his produce is rendered fit** for consumption, as it has been properly tithed. **But his tithes are ruined,** as the amount over one-tenth is not tithe, and it was not itself tithed, so it remains untithed produce. If so, how can the fresh figs be considered proper *teruma* and tithes in this case?

The Gemara on 54b continues a broader debate that had been unfolding around the question of how one measures flour for a *mincha* offering when some transformation has occurred — specifically, whether sourdough affects the measurement. The discussion had already expanded into a more general inquiry: do we measure produce according to its original state or its current state? Rava had entered to limit the scope of that debate, insisting that it cannot be that we go by the size of something as it now stands if that size is insufficient, and further that the current state would not satisfy the requirement unless the item had that status to begin with. Rava sought to confine the debate to what happens when something shrinks in the interim and then grows again. But now the Gemara pushes even further, attempting once more to generalize the question. A *ta shema* is introduced.

### Baraita on Figs

The *baraita* concerns *te'einim* (fresh figs) and *grogrot* (dried figs). Drying figs was a common practice — beyond any preference in taste, the practical benefit of a pre-refrigeration world was that dried figs could be stored for extended periods. Dried figs, however, occupy considerably less volume than their undried counterparts. This creates a real measurement problem when one wishes to separate *teruma* or *ma'aser* from a mixture containing both fresh and dried figs, particularly when separating from one type on behalf of the other.

The *baraita* states: *tormin te'einim al ha-grogrot be-minyan* — one may separate fresh figs on behalf of dried figs, and this is done by number. The natural question is why we use number rather than volume. *Ma'aser* is understood to be measured by volume, not by count. If one has a hundred dried figs and takes ten fresh figs as *ma'aser*, the ten fresh figs represent substantially more than ten percent of the volume of the hundred dried figs — since fresh figs are considerably larger. If *ma'aser* is defined by volume, this would seem to be giving far too much.

### Fresh on Dried

The Gemara presses this point: *ee amrat bishlama lekamoshehayu misha'arinan* — if we say we measure according to the way things originally were, then this works out well. The dried figs were once fresh figs, and ten fresh figs taken by number represents a genuine ten percent of what a hundred dried figs once were. *Ela ee amrat kamoshehen* — but if we determine the proportion by the way things are now, then taking ten fresh figs over a hundred dried figs gives too much *ma'aser*, since the volume of ten fresh figs far exceeds ten percent of the volume of a hundred dried figs. *Havei leh marbe bema'aser* — one is taking more than the required tenth. And a *baraita* teaches that if one takes too much *ma'aser*, the fruit itself may be rendered properly tithed, but the *ma'aser* is compromised — it contains *ma'aser* mixed together with *chullin*, which creates its own set of problems, particularly for the *levi* when he comes to separate *terumat ma'aser* from it. So the first half of the *baraita* implies we go by the original state.

### Dried on Fresh Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ כְּמוֹת שֶׁהֵן – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן – מַרְבֶּה בְּמַעַשְׂרוֹת הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *halakha*. **Granted, if you say** that one measures foods **as they are** currently, it is **well. But if you say** that one measures foods **as they were** initially, when the dried figs were fresh, it should be enough to set aside a smaller number of dried figs corresponding to the fresh ones. Since the *baraita* instructs him to separate a larger number of dried figs than required, this too **is** an example of **one who increases** his **tithes.**

The *baraita* then states the reverse: *grogrot al te'einim bemida* — when separating dried figs on behalf of fresh figs, one measures by volume, not by number. This is puzzling. If we consistently go by the original state, then here too we should simply take ten dried figs for a hundred fresh figs — representing an honest ten percent of what all of them once were. Yet the *baraita* says to use measure, meaning one would have to take a larger quantity of dried figs to equal ten percent of the volume of the hundred fresh figs. *Ee amrat lekamoshehayu, marbe bema'aser havei* — if we are going by the way things originally were, the person giving dried figs on fresh figs would be taking too much *ma'aser* by measuring volumetrically, since they'd end up taking roughly twenty dried figs (representing the same volume as ten undried) rather than simply ten.

The *baraita* thus seems to flip its logic depending on direction: in one case it goes by number, in the other by volume. This is internally inconsistent.

### Teruma vs Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָכָא בִּתְרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה עָסְקִינַן, וְרֵישָׁא בְּעַיִן יָפָה, וְסֵיפָא בְּעַיִן יָפָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, this *baraita* cannot serve as proof for either opinion. Since the two statements of the *baraita* appear contradictory, it must be that this *baraita* is actually not discussing tithes, which must be separated according to a precise measure. **Rather, here we are dealing with standard *teruma*.** By Torah law there is no fixed measure for standard *teruma*; a single kernel of grain exempts the entire crop. The Sages established a range of measures: One-fortieth for a generous gift, one-fiftieth for an average gift, and one-sixtieth for a miserly gift. Accordingly, one who wishes to give generously should give slightly more than the exact measure. **And** therefore, **the first clause** of the *baraita* is speaking of one who wishes to separate *teruma* **generously, and** in **the latter clause,** where he also gives more than necessary, **it** is also referring to one who wishes to separate his *teruma* **generously.**

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by recharacterizing what the *baraita* is about. *Hacha biteruma gedola askinan* — the *baraita* is not discussing *ma'aser* at all; it concerns *teruma gedola*. The word used is *tormin*, which indeed derives from *teruma*, not *ma'aser*. This distinction is critical: *ma'aser* must be precisely ten percent, whereas *teruma gedola* has no specified biblical amount. The *rabbanan* set a general guideline of roughly two percent, with a somewhat more generous person giving a bit more and a less generous person giving a bit less, but there is no single exact required amount.

### Generous Measure

Given that *teruma gedola* has this built-in flexibility, the Gemara explains the logic of the *baraita* as follows: *vereisha be'ayin yafa veseifa be'ayin yafa* — both halves of the *baraita* are operating according to the same principle: always use the measure that results in giving more. When separating fresh figs on dried figs, measuring by number will yield more (since ten fresh figs are volumetrically greater than ten percent of the dried). When separating dried figs on fresh figs, measuring by volume will yield more (since one will end up giving twenty dried figs rather than ten). The *baraita* is not inconsistently switching between different standards; it is consistently applying the principle of the more generous measure — *ayin yafa* — which is appropriate when the amount is flexible, as it is with *teruma gedola*.

One might still object: if this is truly *teruma gedola*, what is the significance of the specific figure of ten over ninety?

### Rabbi Elazar's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַבָּא הָיָה נוֹטֵל עֶשֶׂר גְּרוֹגְרוֹת שֶׁבַּמַּקְצוּעַ עַל תִּשְׁעִים שֶׁבַּכַּלְכַּלָּה, וְאִי בִּתְרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, עֶשֶׂר מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so, say the latter clause: Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: Father,** i.e., Rabbi Yosei, **would set aside ten dried figs that** were **in a vessel for ninety** fresh figs **that** were **in a basket. And if** this *baraita* is referring to **standard *teruma*,** then with regard to this mention of **ten** dried figs, **what is its purpose?** This proportion was greater by orders of magnitude than even the amount of a generous gift established by the Sages.

That objection is anticipated by the *baraita* itself, which continues with the statement of Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosi. He reports that *abba* — his father — would take ten dried figs (*eser grogrot sheb'miktzoa*) for ninety figs in the basket (*tishim shebakalkala*). The *miktzoa* was the instrument used for processing the figs into dried form. This clearly describes a calculation of ten out of one hundred — one takes ten for the remaining ninety — which is the logic of *ma'aser*, a precise tenth. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosi's father was not estimating; he was going by number and taking exactly ten percent based on what the items originally were, even though the ten dried figs represent less volume than ten percent of the undried.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בִּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עָסְקִינַן, וְאַבָּא אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן גּוֹמֵל הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: אַבָּא אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן גּוֹמֵל אוֹמֵר: ״וְנֶחְשַׁב לָכֶם תְּרוּמַתְכֶם״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי תְּרוּמוֹת הַכָּתוּק מְדַבֵּר, אַחַת תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וְאַחַת תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר. כְּשֵׁם שֶׁתְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה נִיטֶּלֶת בְּאוֹמֶד וּבְמַחְשָׁבָה, כָּךְ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר נִיטֶּלֶת בְּאוֹמֶד וּבְמַחְשָׁבָה, מָה תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה בְּעַיִן יָפָה, אַף תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר בְּעַיִן יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, here we are dealing with *teruma* of the tithe,** which the Levite separates from his tithe and gives to a priest. This *teruma* is one-tenth of the first tithe. **And** this ruling **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Elazar ben Gomel. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Elazar ben Gomel says** with regard to the verse: **"And your *teruma* [*terumatkhem*] shall be reckoned to you** as though it were the grain of the threshing floor" (Numbers 18:27), that in using a plural term of the word "your," **the verse speaks about two *terumot*. One is standard *teruma*,** i.e., the grain of the threshing floor, **and** the other **one is *teruma* of the tithe.** The verse equates these two *terumot*.
>
> Abba Elazar ben Gomel explains: **Just as standard *teruma* is taken by estimate,** as there is no requirement for the amount separated to be measured precisely; **and** it can be taken **by thought,** as one is not required to physically separate it before consuming the remaining produce, **so too, *teruma* of the tithe may be taken by estimate and by thought. And** this comparison also teaches that **just as** in the case of **standard *teruma*** one should give **generously, so too,** with regard to ***teruma* of the tithe** one should give **generously.** Therefore, one who separates *teruma* of the tithe from fresh figs for dried figs should do so generously, e.g., ten fresh figs for ninety dried ones, as though the volume of the dried figs was as large as that of fresh ones.

The Gemara therefore challenges the *teruma gedola* interpretation: *v'ee b'trumah gedola eser mai avidteih* — if we are talking about *teruma gedola*, what is this business of ten over ninety? *Teruma gedola* does not require a specific proportion; you give what you give. So clearly, at least Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosi is talking about *ma'aser*. And his position is that you always go by the original state, so ten dried figs for ninety undried correctly represents a genuine ten percent.

The Gemara then introduces yet another possibility: *ela hacha b'trumat ma'aser askinan* — the *baraita* is discussing *terumat ma'aser*. *Terumat ma'aser* is the portion that the *levi*, after receiving the *ma'aser* from the *yisrael*, must in turn separate and give to the *kohen* — a tenth of the tenth. There is a specific amount, namely a tenth. However, as the Gemara will explain, *terumat ma'aser* has a degree of latitude in how that tenth is estimated, because it too bears the title of *teruma* and is given to a *kohen*.

This is supported by the position of Aba Elazar ben Gomel (some read: Gamla), cited in a *baraita*: the verse *venechshav lachem trumatchem* — addressing the *leviim* as they give their *teruma* to the *kohanim* — compares *terumat ma'aser* to *teruma gedola*. *Keshem shetrumah gedola be-omed uv-machshava* — just as *teruma gedola* may be taken by estimation (*omed*) and by mere mental designation (*machshava*, i.e., without verbal declaration), so too *terumat ma'aser nitalas be-omed uv-machshava*. And just as *teruma gedola be-ayin yafa* — with a generous measure — so too *terumat ma'aser be-ayin yafa*.

This is the key move: by linking *terumat ma'aser* to *teruma gedola* through a *gezeira shava*, the Gemara establishes that although *terumat ma'aser* is nominally a fixed tenth, one may use an estimated and somewhat generous measure in calculating it. Tosafos notes the logical difficulty in this comparison: *teruma gedola* has no defined amount at all, and comparing it to *terumat ma'aser* — which does have a defined amount — seems to stretch the analogy. Tosafos responds that this is precisely what the *gezeira shava* teaches, as a matter of derivation rather than pure logic. However, there is another approach that fits more naturally with the *pshat* of this Gemara: some hold that *teruma gedola* does have a biblical dimension of substantive giving — not merely a symbolic grain, but something meaningful as a proportion of the whole — and that this proportion is not rigidly fixed but is subject to generous estimation. On this view, the comparison to *terumat ma'aser* is conceptually coherent, because both involve a proportion that must be substantial but need not be calculated with arithmetical precision.

The Gemara accordingly returns to the first *tanna*'s position in the *baraita*: since we are discussing *terumat ma'aser*, which permits estimation and the generous measure, one always selects whichever method of measuring — by number or by volume — produces the more generous result.

A brief but significant digression arises here regarding the term *machshava*. Rashi explains, as Tosafos notes on 55a (about four to five lines into the wide lines), that *be-machshava* means two things: first, one need not physically separate the grain from the pile, and second, one need not verbally declare the designation — mental intention alone suffices. The verse *venechshav* supports the permissibility of eating without physical separation. Tosafos, however, raises the possibility that *machshava* here does not go so far as to mean pure mental thought. Rather, it may mean that no physical separation is required, but verbal declaration is still necessary. This debate connects directly to the broader question running through Zevachim from its very first pages: when the Gemara speaks of a *machshava shelo lishma*, does it mean a thought that occurs entirely in the mind, or does it mean something verbalized? Rashi and Tosafos consistently debate whether the *machshava* that determines the validity of a *korban* is pure cognition or speech, and the same question applies here to the designation of *teruma*.

### Dried Figs Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שָׁאנֵי גְּרוֹגְרוֹת, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְשׁוֹלְקָן וּלְהַחְזִירָן לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that one cannot extrapolate from the example of dried figs to other cases. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Elazar says** the following reason for that particular *halakha*: **Dried figs are different, since one can boil** dried figs in water **and return them to** their previous state; in other words, **as they were** when they were fresh. Consequently, one may separate them for fresh figs as though they too were fresh. One cannot extrapolate from here a principle with regard to other items.

With the *baraita* and the position of the first *tanna* explained, the Gemara returns to Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosi, who maintains that one always goes by the way things originally were and takes ten dried figs over ninety undried. The Gemara confirms that this makes sense if we follow the original-state principle: *ee amart kemos she-hayu amrinan shapir*. The Gemara further asks: *ee amart kemos she-hein, batshul'hu* — if we go by the way things are now, ten dried figs do not represent ten percent of the volume of a hundred fresh figs, and one is giving too little.

But when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *shani grogrot, ho'il ve-yachol lesholkan u-lehachziran le-chamus she-hein* — dried figs are different, because one can boil them and restore them to their original state. They are, in a sense, always capable of reverting. This is analogous to the earlier discussion about dehydrated meat that, when wetted by rain, returns to its original volume. Because dried figs can always be rehydrated, Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosi would naturally regard them according to their original condition. One therefore cannot derive from his ruling a general principle that we always follow the original state of any item; his position may be specific to cases where restoration is practically available.

## Fresh or Dried Figs

### Baraita on Which to Give

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תּוֹרְמִין תְּאֵנִים עַל הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת, בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁרְגִילִין לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּאֵנִים גְּרוֹגְרוֹת, וְלֹא גְּרוֹגְרוֹת עַל תְּאֵנִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁרְגִילִין לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּאֵנִים גְּרוֹגְרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the possibility of separating fresh figs as *teruma* for dried ones. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may separate *teruma*** from fresh **figs for dried figs** by number, e.g., ten fresh figs for ninety dried ones, **in a place where they are accustomed to make** fresh **figs** into **dried figs,** and therefore the fresh figs can be preserved by processing them into dried figs. **But** one may **not** set aside *teruma* from **dried figs for** fresh **figs even in a place where they are accustomed to make** fresh **figs** into **dried figs.**

A new *baraita* is introduced, still on the topic of dried and fresh figs but now addressing a different question entirely: not how to measure the ten percent, but what one actually gives the *kohen* — fresh figs (*te'einim*) or dried figs (*grogrot*). The *baraita* states: *tormin te'einim al grogrot bemakom sheragilim la'asos* — one may separate fresh figs on behalf of dried figs in a place where people customarily process figs into dried form. However, *velo grogrot al te'einim*, one may not separate dried figs on behalf of fresh figs, *va'afilu bemakom sheragilim la'asos te'einim grogrot* — even in a place where people regularly convert figs into dried figs.

The underlying issue is a familiar tension: fresh figs taste better, but dried figs last longer. In the absence of a *kohen* to receive the *teruma* immediately, one wants to separate something with longevity. Conversely, if a *kohen* is present, one wants to give him the finer-quality fruit.

### Contradictory Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: תּוֹרְמִין תְּאֵנִים עַל הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁרְגִילִין לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּאֵנִים גְּרוֹגְרוֹת. בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁרְגִילִין – אִין, בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין רְגִילִין – לָא. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא כֹּהֵן, מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָגִיל – אַמַּאי לָא? וְהָתְנַן: מְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ כֹּהֵן תּוֹרֵם מִן הַיָּפֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*. **The Master said: One may separate *teruma*** from fresh **figs for dried figs in a place where they are accustomed to make** fresh **figs** into **dried figs.** This indicates that **in a place where they are accustomed** to make dried figs, **yes,** one may set aside *teruma* in this manner. But **in a place where they are not accustomed** to make dried figs, one may **not** separate from fresh figs for dried ones, as the fresh figs are liable to spoil before they can be used.
>
> The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** it is referring to a situation **where there is a priest** present, and the owner of the produce can give him the *teruma* without delay, then even in **a place where he is not accustomed** to make dried figs, **why** may he **not** set aside fresh figs for dried ones? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 2:4): In **a place where there is a priest** present, the owner of the produce **separates *teruma* from the** best-quality produce? In this case, the fresh figs are superior in quality to the dried ones, despite the fact that dried figs last longer.

The *baraita*, however, is puzzling. The first half seems to say: give fresh figs if they can easily be made into dried figs — suggesting the goal is dried figs with longevity. The second half says: do not give dried figs, even where conversion is common. The two halves seem to contradict one another. What is the operative logic?

### Kohen Available or Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּלֵיכָּא כֹּהֵן, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְלֹא גְּרוֹגְרוֹת עַל הַתְּאֵנִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁרָגִיל לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּאֵנִים גְּרוֹגְרוֹת. וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא כֹּהֵן, אַמַּאי לָא? וְהָתְנַן: מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כֹּהֵן – תּוֹרֵם מִן הַמִּתְקַיֵּים! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאִיכָּא כֹּהֵן. רֵישָׁא דְּלֵיכָּא כֹּהֵן, סֵיפָא דְּאִיכָּא כֹּהֵן? אִין, רֵישָׁא דְּלֵיכָּא כֹּהֵן, סֵיפָא דְּאִיכָּא כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is referring to a situation **where there is no priest** present, and by the time a priest is found the fresh figs might spoil. If so, **say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **But** one may **not** set aside *teruma* from **dried figs for** fresh **figs even in a place where they are accustomed to make** fresh **figs** into **dried figs. And if** this is referring to a situation **where there is no priest** present, **why** may one **not** set aside dried figs, which can be preserved for a lengthy period, for fresh ones? **But didn't we learn** in the same mishna (*Terumot* 2:4): In **a place where there is no priest** present, the owner of the produce **separates *teruma* from that which will endure,** not from the best-quality produce? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that this clause is referring to a situation **where there is a priest** present.
>
> The Gemara challenges: If so, **the first clause** of the *baraita* addresses a case **where there is no priest** present, whereas **the latter clause** addresses a case **where there is a priest** present. The Gemara explains: **Yes, the first clause** of the *baraita* addresses a case **where there is no priest** present, and **the latter clause** addresses a case **where there is a priest** present.

The Gemara begins to work through the cases. Regarding the first half — separating fresh figs on dried figs only where people customarily convert — the Gemara asks: *ee de'ika kohen* — if there is a *kohen* readily available to receive the *teruma*, why should it matter whether the town is accustomed to converting? A *mishna* teaches *bemakom sheyesh kohen torem min hayaffe* — where there is a *kohen* around, one gives from the better-quality fruit. If the *kohen* is present, give him fresh figs; there is no obligation to worry about preserving the *teruma* for him. So the condition about customary conversion cannot be relevant when a *kohen* is present. *Ela peshita deleika kohen* — the first half must be addressing a case where there is no *kohen* available. In that case, ordinarily one should not separate from figs that will spoil before a *kohen* can receive them. The *baraita* permits separating fresh figs only where conversion is easy — meaning the fresh figs can readily be made into dried figs, thereby preserving them until a *kohen* comes.

Then *eima seifa* — consider the second half. It says one may not separate dried figs on fresh figs, even where conversion is common. If there is no *kohen* present, why not? A *mishna* teaches *makom she-ein kohen, toreim min hamiskayeim* — where there is no *kohen* available, one separates from the produce with greater longevity. Dried figs certainly have greater longevity. So the prohibition on separating dried figs in the second half must be addressing a case where there is a *kohen* present. *De'ika kohen* — and when there is a *kohen*, you give him the fresher, finer fruit.

The resolution thus requires reading the two halves of the *baraita* as addressing entirely opposite scenarios: the first half speaks of a case where there is no *kohen*, and the second half speaks of a case where there is a *kohen*. *Reisha deleika kohen, seifa de'ika kohen.* The *baraita* never states this explicitly, and its silence on what is in fact the crucial variable — whether a *kohen* is available — is quite striking. Yet this is the Gemara's answer.

### Methodological Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, דָּחֲקִינַן וּמוֹקְמִינַן מַתְנִיתִין בִּתְרֵי טַעְמֵי, וְלָא מוֹקְמִינַן בִּתְרֵי תַּנָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Learn from** this discussion that **we exert** ourselves **and interpret the mishna according to two reasons,** i.e., two different situations in accordance with the opinion of one *tanna*, **but we do not interpret** it as being in accordance with the opinions **of two *tanna'im*.** An interpretation that maintains a single authorship of a mishna is preferable even if it requires explaining the mishna as discussing two different situations.

Rav Pappa draws a methodological conclusion from the Gemara's resolution. *Shma mina dachkinan umokminan matnisin bitrei ta'amei* — the Gemara preferred to strain and explain the *baraita* as unified, with two different contexts (the presence or absence of a *kohen*), *velo mokminan bitrei tannai* — rather than ascribing the contradiction to two different *tannaim* whose conflicting opinions were edited together.

This methodological point is worth pausing over, because it is arguably the hermeneutic principle that underlies the entire enterprise of Gemara study and the work of the *rishonim* generally. The *geonim* had a perfectly serviceable answer to internal contradictions: this is Gemara Menachos and that is Gemara Bava Kamma, and they do not share the same assumptions. Effectively, they attributed contradictions to different voices — different *tannaim* or *amora'im* — without pressing further. That approach may well be more historically accurate. But it does not yield interesting learning, because every problem resolves into "different opinions, end of story."

The *rishonim*, and Tosafos above all, operate on a different assumption: treat the text as a single voice wherever that can be sustained, and press to reconcile. Even if the reconciliation requires discovering that this law only applies by day, that one only by night, this passage when a *kohen* is present, that one when no *kohen* is present — the exercise sharpens the halakhic concepts themselves. By forcing a reconciliation, the analyst is compelled to articulate the limiting conditions of each rule, and the ideas become far more precise.

Haym Soloveitchik has noted that precisely the same enterprise — the intentional search for contradictions followed by the systematic effort to reconcile rather than assign different authors — was occurring simultaneously in the high medieval study of Roman law and canon law in Europe. There the method was called the dialectic. Whether or not there was any direct influence in either direction, the parallel is striking. What the *geonim* said was probably more historically correct. But when a text becomes canonized and authoritative, the operative question shifts from "what did this originally mean?" to "what are our interpretive principles?" — and those principles demand reading for coherence wherever coherence can be found.

Rav Pappa is making exactly this point. It is not a claim about historical authorship; it is a claim about interpretive method. When forced to choose between treating a *baraita* as one voice that can be reconciled through contextual differentiation, or treating it as two voices mechanically combined, one should prefer the former.

## Chametz in Menachot

### Mishna on Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת נִילּוֹשׁוֹת בְּפוֹשְׁרִין, וּמְשַׁמְּרָן שֶׁלֹּא יַחְמִיצוּ, וְאִם הֶחְמִיצוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ – עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כׇּל הַמִּנְחָה אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״, וְחַיָּיב עַל לִישָׁתָהּ, וְעַל עֲרִיכָתָהּ, וְעַל אֲפִיָּיתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the meal-offerings** that come as *matza* **are** to be **kneaded with lukewarm** water so that the dough will bake well, as only a small amount of oil is added. **And one must watch over them** to ensure **that they do not** become **leaven** while kneading and shaping them, **and if** a meal offering or even only **its remainder becomes leaven, one violates a prohibition, as it is stated: "No meal offering that you shall bring to the Lord shall be made with leaven;** as you shall burn no leaven nor any honey as an offering made by fire to the Lord" (Leviticus 2:11). **And one is liable** to be flogged **for kneading** the meal offering, **and for shaping** it, **and for baking** it, if the meal offering becomes leaven.

The *mishna* opens: *kol hamenachos nilochos befoshirin* — all *menachos* are kneaded with lukewarm water. The Torah speaks of *shemen* (oil) in connection with *menachos*, and discusses baking various offerings, but never explicitly mentions water as an ingredient. The *mishna* simply assumes it. This is worth noting because the Gemara never provides an explicit derivation for the addition of water. Logically, one cannot bake bread-like dough without a liquid medium, so its presence is inferred. The Rambam, interestingly, reads *kol hamenachos hane'efos* — only the *menachos* that are baked — suggesting that offerings mixed directly with oil and prepared in a pan involve no bread-like substance and therefore no water. But the *mishna* as we have it applies this to all *menachos*.

Lukewarm — rather than hot — water is specified because hot water accelerates fermentation. The concern for *chametz* is paramount: all *menachos* must be *matzah*, and anything that hastens the onset of leavening must be avoided. *Umeshamerin shelo yachmitzu* — and one is vigilant to ensure they do not become *chametz*.

### Remnant Chametz

The *mishna* then states: *v'im hechmitsu she'areha, over belo ta'aseh* — if the remnant of the *mincha* (the *shirayim*, the portion eaten by the *kohanim* after the *kometz* is burned) became *chametz*, one has transgressed a negative commandment. This requires some explanation. Once a *mincha* has been baked, it is generally not possible for it to become *chametz* — baking halts fermentation. The concern therefore applies primarily to the *minchat chotet* (the sinner's *mincha*), which is pure flour without oil, or the *minchat solet* (the simple *mincha* offering), which is kneaded into dough but not necessarily baked until after the *kometz* is separated and burned. In such cases, the dough is still in a state where fermentation is possible, and the *kohanim* must ensure the remaining *shirayim* are baked promptly and not allowed to sit and ferment.

The operative concern is to keep working the dough — kneading it continuously — or to watch it closely so that fermentation does not begin. If it begins to show signs of leavening, one must work it again immediately. Once it has in fact become *chametz*, it is already too late to avoid transgression.

### Reish Lakish Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ חֶלְקָם״, אֲפִילּוּ חֶלְקָם לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that one who allows the remainder of a meal offering to become leavened violates a prohibition. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Reish Lakish said: The verse states: "It shall not be baked with leaven.** I have given it as **their portion** of My offerings made by fire" (Leviticus 6:10). This section of the verse can be read as a single sentence, to indicate: **Even their portion** of meal offerings, i.e., the remainder eaten by priests after the removal of the handful to be burned on the altar, **shall not be baked with leaven.**

The Gemara asks: *m'na hani milei* — from where do we know that the prohibition against *chametz* extends to the *shirayim*, the remnant? Reish Lakish derives it from a *pasuk*: *lo te'afeh chametz chelkam natati otah me'ishay* — do not bake it *chametz*, [it is] their portion that I have given from My fire-offerings. By shifting the parsing of the verse to read *lo te'afeh chametz chelkam* — do not let the *kohanim*'s portion become *chametz* — Reish Lakish derives that even the *shirayim* fall under the prohibition.

### Make vs Bake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״! לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **this** verse **come for this** purpose? It cannot, as **it is necessary** as the source for a different *halakha*, **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"It shall not be baked with leaven"** (Leviticus 6:10). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** this? **Isn't** this requirement **already stated** earlier: **"No** meal offering that you shall bring to the Lord **shall be made with leaven;** as you shall burn no leaven nor any honey as an offering made by fire to the Lord" (Leviticus 2:11)? Rather, the phrase "it shall not be baked with leaven" serves to teach a different *halakha*. **Since** the prohibition concerning leaven **is** first **stated** in general terms: **Shall not be made with leaven,** without specification, one **might** have thought that one who causes a meal offering to become leaven **will be liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes **for all of** his actions, i.e., kneading, shaping, and baking the dough. Therefore, **the verse states: "It shall not be baked** with leaven," which teaches that one who causes a meal offering to become leaven is liable separately for baking it, and for each stage of its preparation.

The Gemara immediately challenges this *drasha*: *hai l'hachi hu d'ata?* — is that verse needed for this? Surely the verse has a more basic function, as demonstrated by a *baraita*. The *baraita* notes that there is already a verse in Vayikra stating *lo te'aseh chametz* — do not make it *chametz*. "Make" is comprehensive and includes every stage of processing. Why then does another verse (in Tzav) say *lo te'afeh chametz* — do not bake it *chametz*? If "make" already covers everything, "bake" is redundant.

### Separate Transgressions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִיָּיה בַּכְּלָל הָיְתָה, לָמָּה יָצָאת לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ? מָה אֲפִיָּיה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁהִיא מַעֲשֶׂה יְחִידִי, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא לִישָׁתָהּ וַעֲרִיכָתָהּ. וְכׇל מַעֲשֶׂה יְחִידִי שֶׁבָּהּ, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קִיטּוּף, שֶׁהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה יְחִידִי, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains this derivation: **Baking** leaven **was** included **in the general** prohibition incorporating all of the stages involved in preparing the meal offering. **Why** did **it emerge** from the generalization to be mentioned explicitly? It emerged in order **to compare** the other stages **to it: Just as** the act of **baking is notable** in **that it is a single,** i.e., separately defined, **action, and one is liable** to receive lashes **for it by itself** if the dough is leaven, **so too, I will include** the other stages of the preparation of a meal offering, i.e., **kneading it and shaping it,** and conclude that one is liable separately for each of these actions if the dough is leavened.
>
> **And** the same applies to **any single action involved in** the preparation of a meal offering. This statement serves **to include** the act of **smoothing** the surface of the dough with water. The reason this act is included is that although it is not a significant stage in the preparation of the dough, **it is a single,** independent **action, and** therefore **one is liable** to receive lashes **for it by itself.** This *baraita* demonstrates that one cannot derive the prohibition against allowing the remainder of a meal offering to become leavened from the verse: "It shall not be baked with leaven," as this verse is the source of a different *halakha*. If so, from where is that prohibition derived?

The *baraita* answers: one might have thought that even if one performs multiple prohibited acts — kneading *chametz*, shaping it (*aricha*), baking it — these all constitute a single transgression under the prohibition of "making." How would one know that each separate act of processing is its own distinct transgression? *Lamah yatzas lehakish aleha* — the verse singles out baking to connect it to the larger category. Just as baking is a discrete stage in the process and one transgresses for that stage independently, so too every individual stage of processing — kneading, shaping, slapping the dough smooth (*kituf*) — is a separate transgression. *She'hu ma'aseh yechidi v'chayav aleha bifnei atzman* — each individual act is independently actionable.

### Two Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן מֵ״חֶלְקָם״ קָאָמְרִינַן. וְאֵימָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב ״חֶלְקָם לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ״, מַאי ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ חֶלְקָם״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The verse: "It shall not be baked with leaven," is required for the principle stated earlier. **We say** that the prohibition against allowing the remainder of a meal offering to become leavened is derived **from** the subsequent phrase: "I have given it as **their portion** of My offerings made by fire" (Leviticus 6:10). The remainder is the portion of the meal offering eaten by the priests.
>
> The Gemara challenges: **But** once it has been determined that the term "their portion" teaches the prohibition against leavening the remainder of a meal offering, one can **say** this **entire** section of the verse **comes** only **for this** purpose, which would mean that there is no source for the *halakha* that one is liable separately for each stage of the preparation of a meal offering with leaven.
>
> The Gemara answers: One cannot say that this teaches only the prohibition against leavening the remainder of a meal offering, as **if so, let** the verse **write: Their portion shall not be baked with leaven. What** is meant by the fact that the verse stated it in a different order: **"Shall not be baked with leaven.** I have given it as **their portion"?** This indicates that one should **learn from this two** *halakhot*, i.e., that there is a prohibition against leavening the remainder of a meal offering and that one is liable to receive a separate set of lashes for each stage of preparation performed with leavened dough.

The Gemara then raises an alternative: perhaps the entire verse is devoted exclusively to teaching the prohibition of *chametz* in the *shirayim*, with nothing left over to teach the principle of multiple transgressions. The Gemara rejects this: *im kein lichtov chelkam lo te'afeh chametz* — had the Torah intended only to address the remnant, it would have written it explicitly in that order. The fact that the verse first states the general prohibition *lo te'afeh chametz* and only then appends *chelkam* teaches two things at once: the general prohibition (from which we derive multiple-stage liability), and the extension to the *kohanim*'s portion specifically. *Shamas minah tarti* — from this we learn two things.

### Klal U'Prat Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה״ – כָּלַל, ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה״ – פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט – אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט; אֲפִיָּיה – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further challenges: **But** one can **say** that the phrase: **Shall not be made** with leaven, is **a generalization,** as it does not mention any specific acts, and the phrase: **"It shall not be baked** with leaven," is **a detail,** as it specifies one particular stage of the preparation; and there is another standard hermeneutic principle: When there is **a generalization and a detail, the generalization** is referring **only** to **that which** is specified **in the detail.** In this case, that would mean that **baking, yes,** is included in this prohibition, but **other matters,** e.g., kneading and shaping, are **not** included.

The Gemara then raises a new hermeneutic objection. Why not read *lo te'aseh* (do not make) as a *klal* — a general statement — and *lo te'afeh* (do not bake) as a *prat* — a specifying particular? If so, the rule of *klal uprat ein baklal ela ma shebaprat* would apply: the general category is limited to what the particular specifies, and only baking would be prohibited. All the other stages of processing would fall away.

## Distant Pesukim Rules

### Rav Aftoriki View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי: מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה כְּלָל וּפְרָט הַמְרוּחָקִין זֶה מִזֶּה, וְכׇל כְּלָל וּפְרָט הַמְרוּחָקִין זֶה מִזֶּה – אֵין דָּנִין אוֹתָן בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aptoriki said:** That hermeneutic principle is not relevant here, **because** this **is** a case of **a generalization and a detail that** appear in the Torah **distanced from one another,** as the phrase: Shall not be made with leaven (Leviticus 2:11), is far from the expression: "It shall not be baked with leaven" (Leviticus 6:10). **And** for **any** instance of **a generalization and a detail that** appear in the Torah **distanced from one another, one cannot derive** a *halakha* from **them by** analyzing them as **a generalization and a detail.**

Rav Aftoriki responds: *mishum dehavei klal uprat hamiruchakim zeh mizeh* — this *klal uprat* reading is unavailable because the two verses are not adjacent. The verse *lo te'aseh chametz* appears in Vayikra, and the verse *lo te'afeh chametz* appears in Tzav — separated by several *parashiot*. *Dechol klal uprat hamiruchakim zeh mizeh ein danin oso beklal uprat* — when a *klal* and a *prat* appear far apart from one another, we do not apply *klal uprat* hermeneutics. Adjacency is a prerequisite for this type of interpretation.

### Nasi Goat Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּדִי: וּכְלָל וּפְרָט הַמְרוּחָקִין זֶה מִזֶּה אֵין דָּנִין אוֹתָן בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט הָעֹלָה לִפְנֵי ה׳ חַטָּאת הוּא״ – הֵיכָן עוֹלָה נִשְׁחֶטֶת? בַּצָּפוֹן, אַף זֶה בַּצָּפוֹן. וְכִי אָנוּ מִכָּאן לְמֵידִין? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר תִּשָּׁחֵט הָעֹלָה תִּשָּׁחֵט הַחַטָּאת״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava raises an objection, and some say** that this objection is **unattributed [*kedi*]: And** is it correct that in the case of **a generalization and a detail that** appear in the Torah **distanced from one another, one cannot derive** a *halakha* from **them by** analyzing them as **a generalization and a detail? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a goat brought by a king as a sin offering: The verse states: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the goat, **and slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering before the Lord; it is a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:24). **Where is the burnt offering slaughtered? On the northern** side of the Temple courtyard, as it is stated: "And he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar northward before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:11). **This** sin offering of a king must consequently **also** be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard.
>
> The *baraita* asks: **And do you learn** this *halakha* **from here? But isn't it already stated:** "Speak to Aaron and to his sons, saying: This is the law of the sin offering: **In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered shall the sin offering be slaughtered** before God; it is most holy" (Leviticus 6:18)? If so, **to what** purpose **was this singled out?** Why does the Torah state explicitly that the sin offering of the king requires slaughter in the north?

A challenge is raised — the Gemara attributes it to Rav Ada bar Abba, and some attribute the core formulation anonymously (*amrei lei kadi*). The challenge: is it truly the case that *klal uprat* cannot be applied to distant verses? A *baraita* is cited from the laws of *kodshim*. The verse in Vayikra, concerning the goat brought as a *chatat* by the *nasi*, states: *veshachat oso bimkom asher yishchat et haolah lifnei Hashem chatas hu* — it shall be slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered before God, it is a *chatat*. Since the *olah* is slaughtered in the north, this tells us the *chatat* of the *nasi* is also slaughtered in the north. But then the *baraita* notes that a separate verse in Tzav already states *bimkom asher tishachat haolah tishachat hachatas* — where the *olah* is slaughtered, the *chatat* is slaughtered. These two verses appear in different *parashiot*.

The *baraita* explores the function of the specific verse about the *nasi*'s goat. *Halama yatza lekovo* — perhaps it comes to establish this rule as absolutely binding: if the *chatat* of a *nasi* is not slaughtered in the north, it is invalidated (*psalo*). Alternatively — and this is the *baraita*'s *hava amina* — perhaps it comes to limit: only the *chatat* of a *nasi* requires the north, and no other *chatat* does. The *baraita* rejects this limitation by citing a third verse (*ushachat et hachatas bimkom haolah*), which establishes *binyan av* — a paradigm — that all sin-offerings must be slaughtered in the north.

### Proof for Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לְמָה זֶה יָצָא? לְקוֹבְעוֹ, שֶׁאִם לֹא שָׁחַט אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן – פְּסָלוֹ. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְכָךְ יָצָאת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה טָעוּן צָפוֹן וְאֵין אַחֵר טָעוּן צָפוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט אֶת הָעֹלָה״, זֶה בָּנָה אָב לְכׇל חַטָּאוֹת שֶׁטְּעוּנוֹת צָפוֹן. טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת״, הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא שֶׁזֶּה טָעוּן צָפוֹן, וְאֵין אַחֵר טָעוּן צָפוֹן. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה כְּלָל וּפְרָט, וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּמְרוּחָקִין זֶה מִזֶּה, דָּנִין אוֹתָן בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: It is **to fix** a place **for it,** that this is the only place where a sin offering may be slaughtered, teaching **that if he did not slaughter it in the north** of the Temple courtyard, **he has disqualified it** even after the fact.
>
> The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that it is **singled out for this** purpose, to teach that even after the fact a sin offering slaughtered anywhere other than in the north is disqualified? **Or** perhaps **it is only** to teach **that this** goat sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north, but no other** goat sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north.** The *baraita* answers: **The verse states** elsewhere: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the sin offering, **and slaughter the sin offering in the place of burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:29), and **this established a paradigm for all sin offerings, that they require** slaughter in the **north.** Therefore, the additional verse stated with regard to the sin offering of a king teaches that if he did not slaughter it in the north it is disqualified.
>
> The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. The **reason** that all sin offerings must be slaughtered in the north is **that the Merciful One wrote: "And slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering,"** from which it can be inferred that if **not for this** verse **I would say that** only **this** sin offering, the male goat brought by a king, **requires** slaughter in the **north, but no other** type of sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north. What is the reason** for this? After all, the verse: "In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered shall the sin offering be slaughtered," appears to be referring to all types of sin offerings.
>
> **Isn't** it **because** this verse **is a generalization and a detail,** as the verse first generalizes about all sin offerings, and then the verse concerning the sin offering of a king: "And slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering," is a detail, as it is referring to a specific sin offering? **And even though** the verse concerning sin offerings and the verse concerning the sin offering of a king are **distanced from one another,** nevertheless **we** would **derive** a *halakha* from **them by** means of the principle of **a generalization and a detail.** This appears to disprove the explanation of Rabbi Aptoriki.

What is significant for our purposes is the *baraita*'s *hava amina*: one might have thought only the *nasi*'s *chatat* requires the north, with no other *chatat* doing so — despite the verse in Tzav that explicitly extends the rule to all *chataot*. The Gemara infers that the only way to entertain that *hava amina* is if one were reading the verse in Tzav as a *klal* and the verse in Vayikra about the *nasi* as a *prat*, thereby limiting the general to the specific example — a *klal uprat* reading applied to verses from different *parashiot*. This would seem to refute Rav Aftoriki's position that *klal uprat* requires adjacent verses.

*Ha lav hachi hava amina shezeh taun tzafon ve-ein acher taun tzafon* — had that third verse not been written, one would have concluded only the *nasi*'s goat requires north, and nothing else. *Lav mishum dehavei klal uprat, ve-af al gav demiruchakim zeh mizeh danin oso* — is it not because the *baraita* was willing to apply *klal uprat* even to distant verses?

### Rav Ashi Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי כְּלָל וּפְרָט הוּא?! פְּרָט וּכְלָל הוּא, וְנַעֲשָׂה כְּלָל מוֹסִיף עַל הַפְּרָט, (וְאִיתְרַבִּי) [וְאִיתְרַבּוֹ] לְהוּ כֹּל מִילֵּי. אֶלָּא תַּנָּא, ״אֹתוֹ״ קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה טָעוּן צָפוֹן וְאֵין אַחֵר טָעוּן צָפוֹן, דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this** claim raised by Rav Adda bar Ahava: Is **this a generalization and a detail? It is** in fact **a detail and a generalization,** as the verse: "And slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:24), appears in the Torah earlier than the verse: "In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered the sin offering shall be slaughtered" (Leviticus 6:18). A hermeneutic principle states that in this case **the generalization adds to the detail, and includes all matters.**
>
> **Rather,** the reason that if not for the specific textual derivation we would have thought that only a sin offering brought by a king requires slaughter in the north is that the word **"it,"** which is an exclusion, is **difficult for the *tanna*** of the *baraita*, as it is unclear what this term serves to exclude. **And this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: Or** perhaps the verse **is** teaching that **only this** sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north, but no other** type of sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north, as the Merciful One writes "it,"** which is an exclusion. Therefore, the additional verse: "And slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering," teaches that this *halakha* applies to all burnt offerings.

Rav Ashi rejects this proof. *Hai klal uprat hu? Prat u-klal hu!* — the *baraita* is not a case of *klal uprat* at all; the order is reversed. The verse in Vayikra about the *nasi*'s goat is specific — that is the *prat*. The verse in Tzav that covers all *chataot* is general — that is the *klal*. So we have *prat u-klal*, not *klal uprat*. And when the *prat* precedes the *klal*, the rule is not restriction but expansion: *ve-na'aseh ha-klal mosif al ha-prat, ve-israbu lehu kol mili* — the general adds to the specific and everything would be included. So the *baraita*'s *hava amina* that only the *nasi*'s goat requires north cannot be based on *klal uprat* reasoning, because on *prat u-klal* reasoning the result would be the exact opposite — maximum inclusion.

What, then, generated the *baraita*'s *hava amina*? *Tanna oso kashya leh* — the *tanna* was exercised by the word *oso* in the verse about the *nasi*'s goat: *veshachat oso*. The word *oso* — "it," i.e., that specific one — implies exclusivity. One might therefore have thought: only *this* *chatat* (the *nasi*'s goat) requires the north, and no other. It was not *klal uprat* reasoning at all, but the limiting force of the word *oso*, that generated the possibility of restricting the rule to the *nasi*'s case alone. Accordingly, the *baraita* provides no evidence that *klal uprat* can be applied to distant verses, and Rav Aftoriki's position stands: when a *klal* and a *prat* appear far apart from one another, we do not apply *klal uprat* hermeneutics.

### Word Oso Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת״, ״אֹתוֹ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי (נַחְשׁוֹן, וְשָׁחַט, עוֹף, בַּפֶּסַח סִימָן). ״אוֹתוֹ״ בַּצָּפוֹן, וְאֵין שָׂעִיר נַחְשׁוֹן בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **derives it from** the phrase: **"And slaughter the sin offering** in the place of the burnt offering," **to exclude what** does the term **"it"** serve? The Gemara answers: It serves **to exclude** the case that emerges from the following discussion, summarized by the **mnemonic: Nahshon; and slaughter; bird; on Passover.**
>
> The Gemara explains the first suggestion: **It,** the goat sin offering of a king, is slaughtered **in the north, but** the **goat** offered by **Nahshon,** prince of the tribe of Judah, was **not** slaughtered **in the north** of the Tabernacle. He, along with all the other princes of the tribes, brought offerings to inaugurate the altar and the Tabernacle, as recorded in the Torah (see Numbers, chapter 7). The sin offerings brought at this time were unique because they were not brought to atone for any sin. The term "it" teaches that even though they had some characteristics of a sin offering, the offerings of the princes did not require slaughter in the north.
