# Chullin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Samaritan Shechita

### Hakol Shochtin Overview


Daf 4 opens by returning to the Mishna's phrase *hakol shochtin* — "all may slaughter" — and the range of interpretations that phrase invites. The most consequential cases are those involving two categories of people whose trustworthiness is not assured: the Samaritan (*kuti*) and the Jew who is indifferent to *kashrut*. Beyond the question of trustworthiness, there is also the prior question of basic qualification: must a slaughterer be tested for knowledge? Must he have demonstrated prior experience? These questions will be taken up in turn.

### Samaritan Slaughter Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁחִיטַת כּוּתִי מוּתֶּרֶת, בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? כְּשֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו, אֲבָל בָּא וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט – חוֹתֵךְ כַּזַּיִת וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, אֲכָלוֹ – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The slaughter** performed by **a Samaritan is permitted** *ab initio*. **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** there is **a Jew standing over him** and supervising to ensure that the slaughter was performed properly. **But** if the Jew **came and found that** the Samaritan already **slaughtered** the animal, the Jew **cuts an olive-bulk** of meat from the slaughtered animal **and gives it to** the Samaritan to eat. If the Samaritan **ate it,** it is **permitted** for the Jew **to eat** meat **from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered. But if** the Samaritan **did not** eat the meat, it is **prohibited to eat from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.**

The Gemara opens with a *tanu rabbanan*: *shechitas kuti muteres* — the slaughter of a Samaritan is permissible. This is qualified immediately: *bameh devarim amurim? keshe-yisrael omeid al gabav* — when does this hold? Only when a Jew is directly standing over him and supervising. But if a Jew came after the fact and found that the Samaritan had already slaughtered — *avo u'metza'o sheshachat* — then a test is required. The operative concern is that a Samaritan may slaughter properly for his own consumption but may not trouble himself to do so when slaughtering for others. To resolve this, one takes a *kezayis* of the meat and offers it to the Samaritan himself. If he eats it, one may eat from his *shechita*; if he refuses, one may not.

### Testing Samaritan Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, מָצָא בְּיָדוֹ דְּקוּרְיָא שֶׁל צִפֳּרִים, קוֹטֵעַ רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. אֲכָלוֹ – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly,** if the Jew **found a string of birds in the possession of** a Samaritan, and the Jew does not know whether they were properly slaughtered, **he severs the head of one of them and gives it to** the Samaritan to eat. If the Samaritan **ate it,** it is **permitted** for the Jew **to eat** the meat **from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered. But if** the Samaritan did **not** eat the meat, it is **prohibited to eat from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.**

The *braisa* then extends this principle to a parallel case: *matza beyado dikuy shel tzipporim* — if a Jew finds in the Samaritan's possession a bundle of slaughtered birds. Here too, one cuts off the head of one of the birds and offers it to the Samaritan. If he eats it, the entire batch is permitted; if not, it is forbidden. On its surface this seems identical to the meat case, but as the Gemara will explain, the bird case introduces complications that make it considerably more interesting.

## Abaye-Rava Debate

### Excluded Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי דָּיֵיק מֵרֵישָׁא, רָבָא דָּיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא. אַבָּיֵי דָּיֵיק מֵרֵישָׁא: טַעְמָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** In arriving at their respective interpretations of the mishna, **Abaye inferred from the first clause** of the *baraita* and **Rava inferred from the latter clause** of the *baraita*. **Abaye inferred from the first clause:** The slaughter performed by a Samaritan is permitted in a case where there is a Jew actively supervising to ensure that the slaughter was performed properly, that **the reason** it is permitted is **that the Jew is standing over him. But** if the Jew **exits and enters,** then **no,** it is prohibited to eat from what the Samaritan slaughtered.

The *braisa* has now established two clear cases: direct supervision (*yisrael omeid al gabav*), which renders the *shechita* permissible, and no supervision at all, which requires the eating-test. But a middle case is conspicuously absent — what about a Jew who pops in and out periodically (*yotzei venichnas*) without standing over the Samaritan continuously? The *braisa* addresses the two extremes and leaves the middle open. This is what Aristotle might call the excluded middle: the *braisa* says nothing about the intermediate case, and that silence becomes the ground for dispute between Abaye and Rava.

### Abaye: Direct Only


*Abaye dayik mei'reisha* — Abaye draws his inference from the beginning of the *braisa*. The *reisha* states that the *shechita* is permitted only *keshe-Yisrael omeid al gabav*, when a Jew is directly standing over the Samaritan. From this, Abaye infers: *ta'ama deYisrael omeid al gabav, aval yotzei venichnas lo* — the reason it is permitted is specifically because of direct standing supervision; anything less, even *yotzei venichnas*, would not suffice.

### Rava: Periodic OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא דָּיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא, טַעְמָא דְּבָא וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס – שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava inferred from the latter clause:** If the Jew came and found that the Samaritan already slaughtered the animal, the Jew cuts an olive-bulk of meat from the slaughtered animal and gives it to the Samaritan to eat. **The reason** that it is necessary to administer this test is due only to the fact **that** the Jew **came and found that** the Samaritan already **slaughtered** the animal. **But** in a case where the Jew **exits and enters,** it is **permitted** to eat from what the Samaritan slaughtered *ab initio*.

*Rava dayik mei'seifa* — Rava draws his inference from the end of the *braisa*. The *seifa* tells us that a problem arises only when a Jew *came and found* the meat already slaughtered — *ba u'metza'o sheshachat* — with no prior oversight whatsoever. From this, Rava infers: *ta'ama deba u'metza'o sheshachat, aval yotzei venichnas shapir dami* — only total absence of supervision is disqualifying; anything better than that, including periodic supervision, is acceptable.

### Abaye Explains Seifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא, אָמַר לָךְ: יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס נָמֵי ״בָּא וּמְצָאוֹ״ קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **And according to Abaye, the latter clause** is **difficult.** The Gemara answers that Abaye could have **said to you:** The *tanna* **also characterizes** the case where a Jew **exits and enters** as a case of: If the Jew **came and found** the Samaritan.

The Gemara challenges each position in turn. *ul'abaye kasha seifa* — how does Abaye account for the *seifa*, which seems to imply that anything better than no supervision would be acceptable? Abaye responds: *yotzei venichnas nami bo matzo kare lei* — popping in and out is effectively equivalent to having come and found the meat already slaughtered. It is, in Abaye's view, tantamount to no supervision at all, and that is precisely why the *seifa* does not need to address it separately.

### Rava Explains Reisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, אָמַר לָךְ: יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס נָמֵי כְּעוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **And according to Rava, the first clause** is **difficult.** The Gemara answers that Rava could have **said to you:** The case where a Jew **exits and enters** is **also** considered **like** a case where the Jew **is standing over him,** and it is included in that *halakha*.

*Ul'Rava kasha reisha* — how does Rava account for the *reisha*, which speaks of *omeid al gabav* and seems to require direct supervision? Rava responds symmetrically: *yotzei venichnas nami k'omed al gabav dami* — periodic supervision is equivalent to direct standing supervision. The *reisha* did not need to enumerate *yotzei venichnas* explicitly because that case is obviously subsumed within the category of adequate supervision. In the end, the *braisa* describes only the two poles. Each *amora* reads the missing middle through his own interpretive lens: Abaye assimilates it to the bottom, Rava to the top.

This dispute has practical resonance for questions of *hashgacha* in the contemporary world. *yotzei venichnas* and *mashgiach temidi* represent two distinct models of supervision, and which is required depends on the level of concern — both intentional and unintentional — in any given setting. The distinction between *fleishig* and *milchig* restaurant supervision, for instance, tracks this same logic, even if no single line in the Gemara maps directly onto that contemporary ruling.

## Bird Head Test

### Birds Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, מָצָא בְּיָדוֹ דְּקוּרְיָא שֶׁל צִפֳּרִים, קוֹטֵעַ רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. אֲכָלוֹ – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ. אַמַּאי? לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא הַאי הוּא דַּהֲוָה שָׁחֵיט שַׁפִּיר!
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* continues: **Similarly,** if the Jew **found a string of birds in the possession of** a Samaritan, and the Jew does not know whether they were properly slaughtered, **he severs the head of** one of them and gives it to the Samaritan to eat. If the Samaritan ate it, it is permitted for the Jew to eat from what the Samaritan slaughtered. But if the Samaritan did not eat the meat, it is prohibited to eat from what the Samaritan slaughtered. The Gemara asks: **Why** is that a reliable indication? **Let us be concerned** that **perhaps** it **is this** bird alone, whose head the Jew severed, **that** the Samaritan **slaughtered properly,** and the rest are unslaughtered carcasses.

Returning now to the case of the bundle of birds: *kiyotzei bo* — similarly, if a Jew finds a Samaritan holding a bundle of slaughtered birds, one cuts off the head of one and offers it to him to eat. If he eats it, the entire bundle is permitted.

### One Head Problem


The Gemara raises an obvious difficulty: *leichush dilma hai hu dehavi shachit shapir* — perhaps the Samaritan slaughtered some birds properly, intending them for himself or his family, and slaughtered others improperly, intending them for others. If so, how can eating one head tell us anything reliable about the rest of the batch? The Samaritan could simply know which bird he slaughtered properly and eat that one strategically.

### Rav Menasha Hiding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְנַשֶּׁה: בְּמַכְנִיסָן תַּחַת כְּנָפָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashe said** an answer to this question. Before presenting his answer, the Gemara cites **a mnemonic** for the three statements of Rav Menashe cited in this tractate, this one and two others: **Inserts, a scalpel** (see 31a), **into rams** (see 51a). Rav Menashe's answer is as follows: The case in the *baraita* is one **where** the Jew **inserts** the string of birds **under the corners of his garment** and hands the Samaritan the head of one of the birds. In that way, the Samaritan has no way of knowing from which bird the head was taken. If he ate it, apparently all the birds were slaughtered properly.

*Amar Rav Menasha* — Rav Menasha resolves the difficulty: *bemachnisam tachat kenafav* — one hides all the birds under one's cloak, concealing them from the Samaritan's view, then extracts a single head without the Samaritan seeing which one was selected. Since he cannot know which bird is being tested, if he is willing to eat it, one can infer that all the birds were slaughtered properly.

### Mark Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא סִימָנָא הֲוָה יָהֵיב לֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּיהּ? אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: דִּמְמַסְמֵס לֵיהּ מַסְמוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And perhaps** the Samaritan **placed a distinguishing mark in** that bird, indicating to him that it is the kosher one. **Rav Mesharshiyya said:** The case in the *baraita* is one **where** the Jew **crushed** the head that he gave the Samaritan, thereby rendering it indistinguishable from the others.

The Gemara presses further: *vedilma simana hava yaiv leh begaveh* — perhaps the Samaritan anticipated this and placed a hidden mark on the properly slaughtered birds, allowing him to identify the good ones even when their heads are presented to him. The concealment alone would then be insufficient.

### Mashing Solution


*Amar Rav Mesharshaya* — Rav Mesharshaya provides the final answer: *demimasmas leh mimusmei* — one mushed the head thoroughly into an undifferentiated mass before presenting it. Any mark the Samaritan may have made would be obliterated, and only then, having eliminated the possibility of strategic identification, can one present the head to him and draw a reliable inference from his willingness to eat it.

## Samaritan Standards

### Written vs. Oral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא קָסָבְרִי כּוּתִים: אֵין שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **And perhaps the Samaritans hold there is no** source for the **slaughter of a bird in the Torah.** Therefore, the fact that the Samaritan ate the bird's head is no proof that the bird was properly slaughtered.

The Gemara now shifts to a deeper question underlying all of the above. The assumption throughout has been that if a Samaritan slaughters for himself, he does so correctly — the only concern was whether he would extend that care to meat intended for others. But what is the basis for that assumption? A new challenge is raised: *vedilma kasavri Kutim ein shechitah le'of min HaTorah* — perhaps Samaritans believe there is no biblical requirement of *shechita* for birds at all. The concern is rooted in the nature of Samaritan authority: they defer to the written *torah* rather than to rabbinic tradition. With respect to large animals, the *torah* explicitly uses the verb of slaughter — *vezavachta*. But for birds, no such verb appears. Given that Samaritans are thought to be more careful about matters that are written than those that depend on oral tradition, perhaps they do not recognize any laws of *shechita* for birds at all — even when slaughtering for their own consumption.

### Five Defects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, שְׁהִיָּיה, דְּרָסָה, חֲלָדָה, הַגְרָמָה, וְעִיקּוּר – מִי כְּתִיבָן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that possibility: **And according to your reasoning,** those actions that disqualify the slaughter of an animal: **Interrupting** the slaughter, **pressing** the knife, **concealing** the knife in the course of an inverted slaughter, **diverting [*hagrama*]** the knife from the place of slaughter, **and ripping** the *simanim* from their place before cutting them, **are they written** in the Torah?

The Gemara's rebuttal begins by taking stock of the five major ways in which a *shechita* can be rendered invalid: *shehiyah* — pausing mid-stroke before the cut is complete; *drasah* — applying downward pressure rather than allowing the sharpness of the blade to do the cutting; *chaladah* — tunneling, meaning inserting the knife between the trachea and esophagus rather than cutting them from the outside; *hagramah* — slaughtering too high or too low along the neck; and *ikkur* — uprooting, tearing out one of the *simanim* before severing it. These are the essential technical requirements of valid *shechita*.

### All Unwritten


The Gemara's rebuttal is then put sharply: *aboch veta'amech* — if your concern is that Samaritans disregard laws that are not explicitly written in the *torah*, then this concern applies universally. *shehiyah, drasah, chaladah, hagramah, ve'ikkur — *mi ketivan*?* Are any of these written? None of these laws appear explicitly in the *torah*. Yet we assume that Jews who accept the oral tradition observe them. If the concern about the unwritten renders all *shechita* laws suspect for Samaritans, then we could never trust their *shechita* for any animal whatsoever.

### Embrace Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְזִיקוּ בְּהוּ – אַחְזִיקוּ בְּהוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְזִיקוּ – אַחְזִיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** even though the details are not all written in the Torah, **once** the Samaritans **embraced** those disqualifications, **they embraced them,** and a Jew may rely on their slaughter; when they eat from the meat, it is permitted for a Jew to eat the meat as well. **Here too,** although the requirement of ritual slaughter for a bird is not written in the Torah, **once** the Samaritans **embraced** the mitzva of ritual slaughter, **they embraced** it in the same manner that it is performed by Jews.

The resolution is: *keivan de'achziki behu achziki* — once a community has embraced a practice, the fact of that embrace is what grounds our trust, regardless of whether the practice is explicitly written. The principle is applied here: just as Samaritans have embraced the general laws of *shechita* for animals, so too, if they have embraced *shechita* for birds, that embrace is sufficient grounds for trusting them. The question thus becomes not whether something is written, but whether it has been adopted in practice — and that question, the Gemara now reveals, is itself the subject of a *tannaic* dispute.

## Samaritan Matza Debate

### Matza Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחְזוּק וְלָא אַחְזוּק בִּדְלָא כְּתִיבָא, תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: מַצַּת כּוּתִי מוּתֶּרֶת, וְאָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהּ יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to** mitzvot **that are not written** explicitly in the Torah that Samaritans embraced, the question of whether they are **presumed** to fulfill them in the manner that Jews fulfill them **or** they are **not presumed** to do so **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is **permitted** to eat **the *matza* of a Samaritan** on Passover, **and a person fulfills his obligation** to eat *matza* **on** the first night of **Passover with it.**

The Gemara cites a *braisa*: *matzas Kuti muteres* — Samaritan *matza* is permissible — *ve'adam yotzei bah yedei chovaso b'Pesach* — and one may even fulfill the biblical obligation of eating *matza* on the *seder* night with it. This is not as obvious as it sounds. One might have thought that while Samaritan *matza* is not *chametz*, it nevertheless lacks the *shimur* — the intentional watching to prevent leavening — that is required for *matza* used to fulfill the *mitzvah* on the *seder* night. The *tanna kamma* holds that their *matza* has even this quality and may be used *l'chatchila*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין בְּדִקְדּוּקֵי מִצְוֹת כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar prohibits** the consumption of the *matza* of a Samaritan on Passover, **because** the Samaritans **are not experts in the details of mitzvot like Jews** and do not know the precise nature of leaven prohibited by the Torah.

*Rabbi Eliezer oser* — Rabbi Eliezer forbids it outright. His reasoning is that Samaritans are not expert in the precise details of *mitzvah* observance — *lfi she'ein beki'in bedikdukei mitzvot* — and therefore one cannot trust that they exercise the scrupulousness necessary to prevent the *matza* from becoming *chametz* in the first place. The Gemara notes that the formulation *lo beki'ei* — they are not expert — is technically imprecise as a characterization of Rabbi Eliezer's position. His actual view, as consistently understood, is more sweeping: even in areas Samaritans do observe, one can never be assured they observe them with the full level of care required by *halacha*, precisely because they operate outside the rabbinic system and do not submit to its authority. The problem is not ignorance of specific rules; it is the inability to verify scrupulousness in any instance.

### Rabban Shimon Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בָּהּ כּוּתִים, הַרְבֵּה מְדַקְדְּקִין בָּהּ יוֹתֵר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** On the contrary, with regard to **any mitzva that the Samaritans embraced** and accepted upon themselves, **they are more exacting in its** observance **than** are **Jews.** Therefore, one may assume that they prepared the *matza* properly.

Then comes the most striking position in the *braisa*: *rabban shimon ben gamliel omer, kol mitzvah she'hechziku bah kutim *harbeh medakdekin* bah yoter mi'Yisrael* — any *mitzvah* that the Samaritans have embraced as their own, they observe with greater scrupulousness than Jews. This is a remarkable statement. The explanation is not difficult to understand psychologically: when a community does not observe everything, the areas it does observe can take on heightened significance. Those who observe fewer things may observe each one with an intensity that those who observe everything cannot always maintain. The phenomenon is recognizable.

### Core Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מַצַּת כּוּתִי מוּתֶּרֶת, וְאָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהּ יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָא בְּקִיאִי בְּשִׁימּוּר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין בְּדִקְדּוּקֵי מִצְוֹת. קָסָבַר: לָא בְּקִיאִי בְּשִׁימּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to analyze that *baraita*. **The Master said:** It is **permitted** to eat **the *matza* of a Samaritan** on Passover, **and a person fulfills his obligation** to eat *matza* **on** the first night of **Passover with it.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that if the *matza* is permitted one fulfills his obligation with it on Passover? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that Samaritans **are not expert in** the mitzva of **guarding** the *matza* for the sake of the mitzva, the *tanna* **teaches us** that they are expert. **Rabbi Elazar deems it prohibited** to eat the *matza* of Samaritans on Passover, **due to** the fact **that** the Samaritans **are not experts in the details of mitzvot. He holds** that Samaritans **are not expert in** the mitzva of **guarding** the *matza* for the sake of the mitzva.

The Gemara notes that the positions of the *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appear at first to be identical — both permit Samaritan *matza* — and asks what distinguishes them. The answer requires the Gemara to construct the underlying logic of each position.

### Tanna Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בָּהּ כּוּתִים, הַרְבֵּה מְדַקְדְּקִין בָּהּ יוֹתֵר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּכְתִיבָא וְלָא אַחְזִיקוּ בַּהּ, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיבָא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אַחְזִיקוּ בַּהּ, וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: אִי אַחְזוּק – אִין, אִי לָא אַחְזוּק – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** With regard to **any mitzva that Samaritans embraced** and accepted upon themselves, **they are** more **exacting in its** observance **than** are **Jews.** The Gemara raises an objection: **That is** identical to the opinion of **the first *tanna*.** The Gemara explains: **There is** a practical difference **between** their opinions with regard to a mitzva **that is written but** with regard to which the Samaritans **did not embrace it. The first *tanna* holds: Once** the mitzva **is written** in the Torah, **even if** there is **no** knowledge **that they embraced it,** Samaritans can be relied upon to perform it properly. **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds:** Even with regard to a mitzva written in the Torah, **if they embraced** its observance, **yes,** one may rely on the Samaritans, but **if they did not embrace** its observance, **no,** one may not rely on them.

*Ika beinaihu delo ketiva ve'achziku bah* — the difference lies in a case where a law is not written in the *torah* but Samaritans are known to have embraced it, or conversely, where it is written but they are not known to have embraced it. *tanna kamma savar keivan dechtiva, af al gav delo achziku bah* — the *tanna kamma* holds that what is written in the *torah* may be trusted even without confirmed embrace; since it appears in the written text, one may assume Samaritans observe it. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds the opposite: *ei achziku ein, ei lo achziku lo* — only what is demonstrably embraced may be trusted, and whether it is written is irrelevant. Conversely, something not written but demonstrably embraced may be trusted according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel but not according to the *tanna kamma*.

The Gemara thus frames the core dispute: the *tanna kamma* anchors trust in the written *torah*, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel anchors it in demonstrable practice.

### Why Clear Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בָּהּ כּוּתִים״ – ״אִם הֶחְזִיקוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If** it is **so** that this is the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the formulation of his statement is imprecise. He said: With regard to **any mitzva that Samaritans embraced** and accepted upon themselves, they are more exacting in its observance than are Jews; this indicates that one may rely upon Samaritans to observe those mitzvot even if they are not written in the Torah. Therefore, **he should have** said: **If they embraced,** which addresses the statement of the first *tanna*. Contrary to the first *tanna*, who said that one may rely upon Samaritans with regard to any mitzva that is written, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that one may rely upon them only if they embraced the mitzva.

This raises an obvious challenge: once one is prepared to trust Samaritans at all — as the *tanna kamma* clearly is — why not simply do the fact-finding and determine what they actually embrace, as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel proposes? The empirical approach seems more accurate. One possible answer is that *halacha* often prefers clear, stable rules over case-by-case fact-finding. What Samaritans embrace can vary from community to community and from generation to generation. A random sample may not be representative. Written *torah* provides a fixed, verifiable criterion. The trade-off is that the rule will not always track the facts, but clear rules have their own value in a legal system. It may even be the case that the *tanna kamma* requires both: that a law be written *and* that Samaritans be known to have embraced it — a reading that would make the *tanna kamma*'s position somewhat more intelligible, combining the clarity of the written criterion with the empirical grounding of the embrace criterion.

## Mumar Ochel Nevelot

### Rava: Check Knife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא אָמַר רָבָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד, אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת לְתֵיאָבוֹן – בּוֹדֵק סַכִּין וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, וּמוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the statement of Rava cited earlier (3a), the Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Rava says:** In the case of **a Jewish transgressor** whose transgression is that he **eats** unslaughtered **animal carcasses to** satisfy his **appetite,** if he seeks to slaughter an animal, **one examines a knife** to ensure that it is perfectly smooth with no nicks **and gives** it **to** the transgressor, **and it is permitted to eat from** what **he slaughtered.**

*Gufa* — the Gemara now turns to the other major case: the Jew who is personally indifferent to *kashrut*. *amar rava: yisrael mumar ochel nevelot l'teiavon* — a Jew who has abandoned observance of *kashrut* not out of ideological rejection but out of appetite and indifference, eating non-*shechted* meat when it is convenient or cheaper — *bodek sakin venosen lo* — one checks his knife and gives it to him, and *mutar le'echol mishchitato* — one may eat from his *shechita* without even standing over him.

### Heter vs. Issur Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא הֶתֵּירָא וְאִיסּוּרָא, לָא שָׁבֵיק הֶתֵּירָא וְאָכֵיל אִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason? Since** in this case **there is** the option to slaughter the animal in **a permitted** manner **or** to slaughter the animal in **a prohibited** manner, such a transgressor **would not** intentionally **forsake the permitted** manner **and eat** food slaughtered in a **prohibited** manner. Since he has a knife that was examined and the majority of those associated with slaughter are experts, the food is presumed to be permitted, and there is no concern that perhaps he intentionally sabotaged the slaughter.

*Mai ta'ama?* — what is the reason? *keivan d'ika heter ve'issura, lo shavek heiteira ve'achil issura* — since there are two options before him, one permitted and one forbidden, and all other things being equal he has no reason to prefer the *issur* over the *heter*, we assume he will not abandon the *heter* for the *issur* without some incentive. This man eats non-*shechted* meat when it is cheaper or more convenient. But when there is no advantage to doing so — when the *shechita* itself costs him no additional effort — we assume he will do it correctly.

### Effort Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי לָא בְּדַק נָמֵי, מִיטְרָח לָא טָרַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then **even** in a case **where** the Jew **did not examine** the knife, it should be permitted to eat from the animal slaughtered by the transgressor. The Gemara answers: It is prohibited because if the transgressor discovers that the knife is flawed, **he does not exert** himself to replace it with a knife with a smooth blade.

The Gemara presses: if the principle is that all things being equal he will prefer *heter*, then why is a knife-check necessary at all? Why not simply trust him even without it? The answer: *mitrach lo tarach* — he will not exert himself. Without being given a pre-checked knife, he may not take the trouble to ensure his knife is sharp and nick-free. That is an effort, and if an effort is required, the *lo shavek heiteira* logic no longer applies. But given a properly checked knife and no added burden of care, the correct *shechita* is essentially the default — once one knows how to do it properly, there is no greater muscular or practical effort involved in doing it correctly than incorrectly. Under those conditions, we rely on the presumption that he will choose the *heter*.

## Chametz After Pesach

### Chametz Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ, חֲמֵצָן שֶׁל עוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה אַחַר הַפֶּסַח מוּתָּר מִיָּד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן מַחְלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rava:** A *baraita* **is taught that supports your** opinion: With regard to **the leavened bread of transgressors,** who do not eradicate their leavened bread before Passover, **after Passover it is permitted immediately, due to** the fact **that they exchange** the forbidden leavened bread in their possession with permitted leavened bread belonging to gentiles immediately after Passover.

The students bring a *braisa* as support for Rava's position: *chamtzan shel ovdei aveirah achar haPesach mutar* — the *chametz* belonging to Jews who kept *chametz* in their homes throughout *pesach* is permitted after *pesach*. This is striking because *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* — *chametz* that remained in Jewish possession over *pesach* — is in principle prohibited after the fact. Yet the *braisa* declares it permitted.

### The Swap Logic


The reason given is: *mipnei shehein machlifin* — because these violators exchange their *chametz* for a neighbor's *chametz* after *pesach*. The reasoning reveals a remarkable psychology. These are Jews who transgressed *chametz* on *pesach*, a violation carrying the penalty of *kares*. Yet after *pesach*, when the stake is considerably lower — perhaps only a rabbinic prohibition — they go out of their way to swap their *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* for ordinary post-*Pesach chametz*, so that what they eat carries no halachic taint at all. The *lo shavek heiteira* principle operates here with no effort required: swapping *chametz* costs nothing additional, and so they do it. This corroborates Rava's position that those who are indifferent to *halacha* in one context will nonetheless choose the *heter* over the *issur* when no effort is required.

### Gemara Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ, הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵץ אַחַר הַפֶּסַח דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְקָתָנֵי: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן מַחְלִיפִין, אַלְמָא לָא שָׁבֵיק הֶתֵּירָא וְאָכֵיל אִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages who cited this proof **assumed** that **this** *baraita* is in accordance with **whose** opinion? They assumed it **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: Leavened bread** that was not eradicated before Passover is forbidden **after Passover by Torah law.** The Gemara comments: **And** nevertheless **it is taught** that it is permitted **due to** the fact **that** the transgressors **exchange** their leavened bread with that of gentiles. **Apparently,** even one who performs transgressions **does not** intentionally **forsake the permitted and eat forbidden** food, where the permitted food is easily accessible.

The Gemara initially assumes this *braisa* follows Rabbi Yehuda's view that *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* is a biblical prohibition, and reasons that if we trust these violators even for a biblical matter, this supports Rava all the more. The Gemara then considers whether the *braisa* might follow Rabbi Shimon, who holds this prohibition is only rabbinic, and asks whether the *lo shavek heiteira* principle would then be limited to rabbinic matters only. The Gemara responds that if that were so, the text would have employed the phrase *shani omer* — "I would say" — indicating a permissive assumption despite uncertainty. Instead, the *braisa* states *mipnei shehein machlifin* — *because* they swap — asserting it as a known fact, not a convenient presumption. Since it says they *definitely* swap, and they do so even for what might be a rabbinic prohibition, this establishes the principle a fortiori for biblical matters: *bedioraita lo kol sheken* — how much more so for a biblical prohibition will a person prefer the *heter* when no effort is required.

### Knowledge vs. Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵץ אַחַר הַפֶּסַח דְּרַבָּנַן, וְכִי מְקִילִּינַן – בִּדְרַבָּנַן, בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – לָא מְקִילִּינַן! וְתִיהְוֵי נָמֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מִי קָתָנֵי ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר הֶחְלִיפוּ״? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּחְלִיפִין קָתָנֵי, דְּוַדַּאי מַחְלִיפִין. וּמָה בִּדְרַבָּנַן לָא שָׁבֵיק הֶתֵּירָא וְאָכֵיל אִיסּוּרָא, בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** in the *baraita* can this be proven? **Perhaps** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says: Leavened bread** that was not eradicated before Passover is prohibited **after Passover by rabbinic law. And** accordingly, **when we are lenient it is with regard to** prohibitions **by rabbinic law,** whereas **with regard to** prohibitions **by Torah law,** e.g., the prohibition of an unslaughtered carcass, **we are not lenient.** The Gemara answers: **And let** the *baraita* **be even** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. Does** the *tanna* **teach: As I say** that **they exchanged** their leavened bread for the leavened bread of a gentile, which would indicate that it is an assumption? **He teaches: Due to** the fact **that they exchange,** as a statement of fact, **as they certainly exchange. And if in** cases involving prohibitions **by rabbinic law** the transgressor **does not** intentionally **forsake the permitted and eat forbidden** food, then **in** prohibitions **by Torah law** is it **not all the more so** reasonable that he would not forsake the permitted in favor of the forbidden?

A distinction emerges from this analysis. There is a difference between saying *shani omer* — I will presume without certainty — and saying *machlifin* — they factually do this. In the former, the leniency would rest on a legal fiction adopted for practical purposes; in the latter, we are asserting something we genuinely hold to be true. The Gemara here insists we are doing the latter. Whether or not an actual investigation was conducted in any individual case, we are asserting as a matter of established fact that this is the behavior, not merely granting ourselves permission to assume it without knowledge.

## Tosefta Cases

### Tosefta List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוּתִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ עָרֵל, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rava: **Everyone slaughters, and even a Samaritan, and even an uncircumcised** man, **and even a Jewish transgressor.**

The Gemara now cites a *tosefta* that reads as a direct commentary on the Mishna's *hakol shochtin*: *hakol shochtin, *ve'afilu Kuti*, ve'afilu arel, ve'afilu Yisrael mumar* — all may slaughter, including a Samaritan, including an uncircumcised Jew, and including a Jewish *mumar*. The *tosefta* thus enumerates three of the cases the Gemara has been analyzing. What it adds is the category of the *arel* — the uncircumcised Jew.

### Arel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי עָרֵל הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא מֵתוּ אֶחָיו מֵחֲמַת מִילָּה – הַאי יִשְׂרָאֵל מְעַלְּיָא הוּא! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא מְשׁוּמָּד לַעֲרֵלוּת, וְקָא סָבַר מְשׁוּמָּד לְדָבָר אֶחָד לָא הָוֵי מְשׁוּמָּד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This uncircumcised** man, **what are the circumstances? If we say** that he is an uncircumcised man **whose brothers died due to circumcision** and the concern is that he might suffer a similar fate, clearly he may slaughter, as **he is a full-fledged Jew** and not a transgressor at all. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that he is **a transgressor with regard to** remaining **uncircumcised,** as he refuses to be circumcised, **and** the *tanna* **holds** that he may nevertheless slaughter an animal since **a transgressor concerning one matter is not a transgressor concerning the entire Torah.**

*Hai arel heichi dami?* — what type of uncircumcised Jew does the *tosefta* mean? If it refers to someone whose brothers died as a result of circumcision — a family pattern of what we would today recognize as hemophilia — then he was halachically exempt from circumcision, and *hai Yisrael maya hu* — he is a perfectly ordinary Jew whose *shechita* raises no question at all. The *tosefta* must therefore be speaking of someone who deliberately remains uncircumcised despite having no medical exemption — a *mumar l'areilus*.

### One Area Principle


The implicit reasoning of the *tosefta* is: *mumar l'davar echad lo havei mumar l'chol ha-Torah kullah* — rejection of one area of *halacha* does not render a person a *mumar* with respect to the whole *torah* or with respect to any other specific area. The principle reflects a realistic understanding of human psychology: people compartmentalize. A known violator in one domain may be entirely reliable in another, even if the violated domain is technically more severe. The *tosefta* therefore permits this person's *shechita lechatkhila*.

### Mumar L'Kashrut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד. הַאי מְשׁוּמָּד הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי מְשׁוּמָּד לְדָבָר אַחֵר – הַיְינוּ מְשׁוּמָּד לַעֲרֵלוּת, אֶלָּא לָאו מְשׁוּמָּד לְאוֹתוֹ דָּבָר, וְכִדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **And even a Jewish transgressor. This transgressor, what are the circumstances? If he is a transgressor concerning another matter** besides eating unslaughtered animal carcasses, **that is** identical to the case of **a transgressor with regard to** remaining **uncircumcised. Rather, is it not** that he is **a transgressor concerning the same matter** of eating unslaughtered carcasses, **and** the *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava,** who said that one may even rely on the slaughter of a transgressor with regard to eating unslaughtered animal carcasses to satisfy his appetite, *ab initio*?

The Gemara then asks what the *tosefta* adds by mentioning the *yisrael mumar* separately, since that case would appear to be subsumed under *mumar l'davar echad*. The answer is that the *tosefta* must be referring to a *mumar l'oso davar* — someone who has specifically rejected *kashrut* itself. This is then read as a support for Rava's position: that even a Jew who himself eats non-*shechted* meat may be trusted to slaughter for others, provided the knife is checked and no additional effort is required.

## Sin Becomes Heter

### Repeat Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: מְשׁוּמָּד לְאוֹתוֹ דָּבָר לָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּדָשׁ בֵּיהּ – כְּהֶתֵּירָא דָּמֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof: **No, actually I will say to you:** The slaughter of **a transgressor concerning the same matter** is **not** valid. **What is the reason?** It is that **since he has become accustomed to** performance of that transgression, it is **like a permitted** act **for him,** and the concern is that he is not at all careful to slaughter the animal properly.

The Gemara rejects this reading of the *tosefta* as support for Rava. *le'olam eima lach *mumar l'oso davar* lo* — in fact, a *mumar* who has rejected *kashrut* specifically cannot be trusted for *shechita*, and the *tosefta* must be referring only to rejection of an unrelated area. The Gemara's objection to Rava strikes at the psychological premise underlying his entire position.

### Two Sins Erase


*Keivan d'dash bei k'heteira dami leh* — once a person has repeatedly transgressed, the transgression begins to feel like a *heter* to him. Rava's logic assumed that even the person who eats *neveilot* still maintains some internal distinction between *issur* and *heter* and, all things being equal, will choose the *heter*. But the Gemara says this is not psychologically accurate. The Gemara elsewhere states: *aveirah d'adam over bah ve'shonah, na'aseis lo k'heter* — a sin that a person commits twice becomes like a *heter* in his mind. Once the categories of *issur* and *heter* have collapsed in this domain, there is no longer a preference for *heter*, because he no longer perceives the act as *issur* at all.

### Salanter Coda


Rav Yisrael Salanter added a coda to this principle: do it twice and it becomes like a *heter*; do it three times and it becomes like a *mitzvah*. The capacity of human rationalization is such that repeated behavior is not merely normalized but eventually embraced as positive and proper.

### Cannot Trust Mumar


The consequence for *halacha*: one cannot trust the *shechita* of a *mumar ochel nevelot* precisely because, in his own mental framework, the distinction between kosher and non-kosher meat no longer exists as *issur* and *heter*. He is not a person choosing *heter* over *issur* when the choice is costless — he is a person for whom the category of *issur* in this domain has ceased to exist. Rava's premise therefore does not apply to him.

## Mumar Avodas Kochavim

### Idolater Trusted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְשׁוּמָּד לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְכִדְרַב עָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַב עָנָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the transgressor mentioned in the *baraita* is **a transgressor with regard to idol worship, and** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Anan, as Rav Anan says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **a Jew** who is **a transgressor with regard to idol worship, it is permitted to eat from** what **he slaughters.**

*Ella mumar l'avodas kochavim* — the *tosefta*'s reference to a *mumar* whose *shechita* is trusted must be referring to a Jew who practices idolatry. *ve-ki-d'rav anani, d'amar rav anani amar shmuel: yisrael mumar l'avodas kochavim mutar* — as Rav Anani said in the name of Shmuel, a Jew who is a *mumar* with respect to *avodas kochavim* may still have his *shechita* trusted. This is a striking ruling: a Jew who worships idols may be trusted for *kashrut* purposes.

### Separate Areas


The logic follows the *mumar l'davar echad* principle in its most striking application. Rejection of one religious domain — even one as fundamental as the rejection of idolatry — does not necessarily entail any change in behavior or attitude in another domain. A Jew who practices idolatry may nonetheless keep a fully kosher home, not because idolatry is treated lightly, but because the categories are conceived as genuinely separate. Just as rejecting *brit milah* does not make one suspect regarding *kashrut*, rejecting monotheism in practice does not in itself alter one's relationship to the laws of *shechita*.
