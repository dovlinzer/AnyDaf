# Shabbat 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mekoshesh Identity

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְקוֹשֵׁשׁ זֶה צְלָפְחָד, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּהְיוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר וַיִּמְצְאוּ אִישׁ וְגוֹ׳״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אָבִינוּ מֵת בַּמִּדְבָּר״, מַה לְּהַלָּן צְלָפְחָד, אַף כָּאן צְלָפְחָד — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The wood gatherer** mentioned in the Torah was **Zelophehad, and it says: "And the children of Israel were in the desert and they found a man** gathering wood on the day of Shabbat" (Numbers 15:32), **and below,** in the appeal of the daughters of Zelophehad, **it is stated: "Our father died in the desert** and he was not among the company of them that gathered themselves together against the Lord in the company of Korah, but he died in his own sin, and he had no sons" (Numbers 27:3). **Just as below** the man in the desert is **Zelophehad, so too, here,** in the case of the wood gatherer, the unnamed man in the desert is **Zelophehad;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The daf opens five lines from the bottom of 96b with a *tanna rabbanan* that addresses a question carried over from the previous daf: who was the *mekoshesh*, the wood-gatherer of the wilderness, and what precisely was his sin? The *baraita* states: *mekoshesh zeh Tzelafchad* — this gatherer was Tzelafchad. The proof comes from a *gezeirah shavah*: the verse describing the *mekoshesh* reads *vayihyu bnei Yisrael bamidbar vayimtze'u ish mekoshesh etzim* — they were in the wilderness and found a man gathering wood — and elsewhere the daughters of Tzelafchad declare *avinu met bamidbar* — our father died in the wilderness. The shared word *bamidbar* links the two passages: just as the latter speaks of Tzelafchad, so does the former. This is the view of Rabbi Akiva.

### Ben Beteira Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא: עֲקִיבָא, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אַתָּה עָתִיד לִיתֵּן אֶת הַדִּין: אִם כִּדְבָרֶיךָ — הַתּוֹרָה כִּיסַּתּוּ, וְאַתָּה מְגַלֶּה אוֹתוֹ?! וְאִם לָאו — אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira said to him: Akiva, in either case you will be judged in the future** for this teaching. **If** the truth is **in accordance with your statement** that the wood gatherer was Zelophehad, **the Torah concealed** his identity, **and you reveal it. And if** it the truth is not in accordance with your statement, **you are** unjustly **slandering that righteous man.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא גָּמַר גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה! גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לָא גָּמַר. אֶלָּא מֵהֵיכָא הֲוָה? מִ״וַּיַּעְפִּילוּ״ הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, didn't** Rabbi Akiva **derive** this by means of **a verbal analogy?** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira **did not learn a verbal analogy.** Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira had no tradition of this verbal analogy from his teachers, and therefore he disagreed with Rabbi Akiva's conclusion. The Gemara asks: **However,** according to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, **from where was** Zelophehad's liability derived? Why was he executed? The Gemara answers: Zelophehad was among those who **"presumed** to ascend to the top of the mountain" (Numbers 14:44) in the wake of the sin of the spies.

Rabbi Yehuda Ben Beteira objects sharply. He tells Rabbi Akiva: *bein kach u'vein kach atah atid litein et hadin* — whether you are right or wrong, you will be called to account for saying this. If you are correct, then *ha-Torah kissato v'atah megaleh oto* — the Torah deliberately concealed his identity to protect his honor, and you are exposing it. If you are wrong, *atah motzi la'az al oto tzaddik* — you are spreading a false and damaging report about a righteous person. Either way, the identification serves no constructive purpose.

The Gemara then raises the obvious rejoinder: but Rabbi Akiva had a *gezeirah shavah* — surely he is bound to follow where it leads? Rashi sharpens this point: *v'ela gamar gezeirah shavah im kein lo chissato ha-Torah shavei leh k'meforash* — if the Torah itself provides the *gezeirah shavah*, it is as though the Torah stated it explicitly; the Torah has not hidden anything. The Gemara resolves the tension by explaining that Rabbi Yehuda Ben Beteira simply did not accept this *gezeirah shavah* — *gezeirah shavah lo gamar* — and was therefore not impressed.

Tosafot raises an additional question: even if Ben Beteira lacked this tradition himself, why not simply accept it from Rabbi Akiva? Tosafot answers that Ben Beteira had a contrary tradition. The understanding among the *rishonim* is that each *gezeirah shavah* valid for derivation was transmitted as part of a fixed set, and Ben Beteira knew this particular linkage was not among them. This touches on broader questions the *rishonim* discuss — the Ramban, for instance, is troubled when the Gemara asks why one makes a *gezeirah shavah* from these *pesukim* rather than others, since the entire institution is by tradition. The Ramban suggests that while the underlying *halacha* and the existence of a *gezeirah shavah* were transmitted, the precise textual identification of the supporting verses was sometimes left to the Sages to work out. At any rate, the upshot here is that Ben Beteira rejected the *gezeirah shavah* entirely and therefore found Rabbi Akiva's identification unjustifiable.

### Ye'afilu Connection

Having set aside Rabbi Akiva's identification, the Gemara itself asks in Aramaic — signaling that this is the Gemara's own voice, not Ben Beteira's — *ela meheicha hava*: very well, but what then was Tzelafchad's sin, given that the Torah says he died *b'cheto* — by his own sin? The Gemara answers: *vayapilu* — he was among those who insisted on ascending to Eretz Yisrael after the *chet ha-meraglim*. The passage describing the *mekoshesh* appears immediately after the *chet ha-meraglim* in the portion of *Shelach*, and the episode of the *mekoshesh* occurs in the same textual neighborhood as the account of those who defiantly attempted to enter the land after God had decreed against it. Interestingly, the Gemara connects Tzelafchad to the *ye'afilu* group — those who said, in effect, we sinned, let us reverse course and go up to the land — even while Moshe warned them that God would not be with them.

### Religious Motivation

The Gemara thus identifies both plausible candidates for Tzelafchad's sin — the *mekoshesh* and the *ye'afilu* — as acts rooted in some form of religious earnestness, however misguided. The *ye'afilu* were attempting a form of *teshuvah*, trying to undo the damage of the spies' sin and return to the intended trajectory of entering Eretz Yisrael. As for the *mekoshesh*, while the Gemara does not spell it out here, it is noted elsewhere that the motive attributed to him was to demonstrate, in the wake of the decree that Israel would not enter the land, that the Torah's obligations remained fully in force. The reasoning attributed to him: lest people conclude that since Israel would not be entering Eretz Yisrael, the covenant had somehow lapsed and sins would go unpunished. He violated Shabbat publicly to prove by his own execution that the Torah retained its full authority even in the wilderness. The Gemara here does not articulate this explicitly, but it is worth noting that both identifications — *ye'afilu* and *mekoshesh* — frame Tzelafchad's death as flowing from an act of misplaced religious zeal rather than mundane wickedness. This, one might suggest, is partly what motivates the Gemara's effort to connect the unnamed figure to a positive context, even after Ben Beteira has declined to do so.

## Tzara'at of Innocents

### Choshed Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר, אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּחַר אַף ה׳ בָּם וַיֵּלַךְ״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאַף אַהֲרֹן נִצְטָרַע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא: עֲקִיבָא, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אַתָּה עָתִיד לִיתֵּן אֶת הַדִּין. אִם כִּדְבָרֶיךָ — הַתּוֹרָה כִּסַּתּוּ וְאַתָּה מְגַלֶּה אוֹתוֹ?! וְאִם לָאו — אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **On a similar note,** Rabbi Akiva revealed an additional matter not explicitly articulated in the Torah. **You say** that when Aaron and Miriam spoke against Moses, both Aaron and Miriam were struck with leprosy, as it written: **"And God became angry at them and He left,** and the cloud departed from above the tent, and behold, Miriam was leprous like snow. And Aaron turned toward Miriam, and behold, she was leprous" (Numbers 12:9–10). The verse's statement that God became angry at both of them **teaches that Aaron, too, became leprous;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira said to him: Akiva, in either case you will be judged in the future** for this teaching. **If** the truth is **in accordance with your statement, the Torah concealed** Aaron's punishment **and you reveal it. And if** the truth is not in accordance with your statement, **you are** unjustly **slandering that righteous man.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָכְתִיב ״בָּם״! הַהוּא בִּנְזִיפָה בְּעָלְמָא. תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אַף אַהֲרֹן נִצְטָרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפֶן אַהֲרֹן אֶל מִרְיָם וְהִנֵּה מְצֹרָעַת״, תָּנָא: שֶׁפָּנָה מִצָּרַעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, didn't** Rabbi Akiva **derive** this from the plural pronoun **them,** meaning that God was angry with both of them? The Gemara answers: God's anger in **that** verse was manifest in **a mere rebuke,** not in leprosy. A *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, **who said** that **Aaron also became leprous, as it is written: "And Aaron turned toward Miriam, and behold, she was leprous"** (Numbers 12:10), and it was **taught:** This teaches **that he turned,** i.e., he was healed, **from his leprosy,** as he too had been afflicted.

The Gemara then moves to a parallel discussion involving similar methodology — identifying an unnamed figure with a named one — but now in the context of Aharon and Miriam. The verse says that after God's anger was kindled against Miriam and Aharon, *vehinei Miriam metzora'at kashaleg* — behold Miriam was leprous as snow. Rabbi Akiva derives from the general expression of divine anger — *vayichar af Hashem bam* — that Aharon, too, was afflicted with *tzara'at*, not just Miriam. Once again Rabbi Yehuda Ben Beteira objects with exactly the same formulation: whether right or wrong, you will be called to account, because either the Torah concealed it to protect Aharon's honor and you have revealed it, or you are spreading a false report about a righteous person. In this case the Gemara notes that the word *bam* implies both of them were affected, and a tradition is cited to support the position that Aharon indeed had *tzara'at*, derived from the verse *vayifen Aharon el Miriam vehinei metzora'at* — Aharon turned to Miriam and behold she was leprous. The word *vayifen*, he turned, is read as indicating he turned away from his own *tzara'at* to observe hers. Rashi understands this as *kadam v'nisrapa* — his *tzara'at* departed first, and having recovered, he then turned to see that Miriam's remained. Aharon's affliction, then, was real but momentary, less prolonged than Miriam's.

Before arriving at Moshe's *tzara'at*, the Gemara sets up the relevant principle:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַחוֹשֵׁד בִּכְשֵׁרִים — לוֹקֶה בְּגוּפוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֵן לֹא יַאֲמִינוּ לִי וְגוֹ׳״, וְגַלְיָא קַמֵּי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא דִּמְהֵימְנִי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן מַאֲמִינִים בְּנֵי מַאֲמִינִים, וְאַתָּה אֵין סוֹפְךָ לְהַאֲמִין.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of Miriam's leprosy, the Gemara cites that which **Reish Lakish said: One who suspects the innocent** of indiscretion **is afflicted in his body, as it is written:** "And Moses answered and said: **But they will not believe me** and will not hearken to my voice, for they will say, God did not appear to you" (Exodus 4:1), **and it is revealed before the Holy One, Blessed be He, that the Jewish people would believe.** The Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to** Moses: **They are believers, the children of believers; and ultimately, you will not believe.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵן מַאֲמִינִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּאֲמֵן הָעָם״. בְּנֵי מַאֲמִינִים — ״וְהֶאֱמִין בַּייָ״. אַתָּה אֵין סוֹפְךָ לְהַאֲמִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַעַן לֹא הֶאֱמַנְתֶּם בִּי וְגוֹ׳״. מִמַּאי דִּלְקָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ לוֹ עוֹד הָבֵא נָא יָדְךָ בְּחֵיקֶךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **They are believers, as it is written: "And the people believed** once they heard that God had remembered the children of Israel, and that He saw their affliction, and they bowed and they prostrated" (Exodus 4:31). **The children of believers,** as it says with regard to Abraham our Patriarch: **"And he believed in God,** and He counted it for him as righteousness" (Genesis 15:6). **Ultimately, you will not believe, as it is stated:** "And God said to Moses and to Aaron: **Because you did not believe in Me** to sanctify Me in the eyes of the children of Israel" (Numbers 20:12). **From where** do we know that Moses **was afflicted** in his body? **As it is written: "And God said to him further: Bring your hand to your bosom,** and he brought his hand to his bosom and he took it out and behold, his hand was leprous like snow" (Exodus 4:6).

*ha-choshed bi-ksherim lokeh b'gufo* — one who suspects innocent people is smitten in his very body. The verse invoked is Moshe's own statement at the *sneh*: *vehein lo ya'aminu li* — but they will not believe me. This, the Gemara observes, was an unjust suspicion. The Israelites' actual response once Moshe delivered God's message was immediately *vaya'amen ha'am* — the people believed. God knew they were believers: *hem ma'aminim bnei ma'aminim* — they are faithful, the children of the faithful, alluding to the faith of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Ya'akov. And God tells Moshe: *v'atah sofcha lo leha'amin* — in the end, you yourself will fail to believe fully, a reference to the later sin at *Mei Merivah*, where the verse states *ya'an lo he'emantem bi* — because you did not have faith in me.

### Moshe's Tzara'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִדָּה טוֹבָה מְמַהֶרֶת לָבֹא מִמִּדַּת פּוּרְעָנוּת. דְּאִילּוּ בְּמִדַּת פּוּרְעָנוּת כְּתִיב: ״וַיּוֹצִיאָהּ וְהִנֵּה יָדוֹ מְצֹרַעַת כַּשָּׁלֶג״, וְאִילּוּ בְּמִדָּה טוֹבָה כְּתִיב: ״וַיּוֹצִיאָהּ מֵחֵיקוֹ וְהִנֵּה שָׁבָה כִּבְשָׂרוֹ״ — מֵחֵיקוֹ הוּא דְּשָׁבָה כִּבְשָׂרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** On this topic, **Rava said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** who said: The divine **attribute of beneficence takes effect more quickly than the** divine **attribute of punishment.** From where is this derived? **While, with regard to the** divine **attribute of punishment, it is written, "And he took it out and behold, his hand was leprous like snow"** (Exodus 4:6), **with regard to the** divine **attribute of beneficence it is written:** "And He said: Return your hand to your bosom, and he returned his hand to his bosom **and he took it out from his bosom and behold, it had returned to be like his original flesh"** (Exodus 4:7). The Gemara analyzes this as follows: It was already **from his bosom** that **it returned to be like his original flesh.** Moses' hand was healed even before he took his hand out.

The Gemara asks: *mimai delaka* — from where do we know that Moshe was smitten? The answer: *dechativ vayomer lo Hashem od hava na yadcha b'cheikecha* — God immediately instructed Moshe to insert his hand into his chest and take it out, and it emerged *metzora'at kashaleg*, leprous as snow. The punishment was delivered at once and in the body — exactly the principle of *ha-choshed bi-ksherim lokeh b'gufo*. The *midrash* here is compact and elegant: it plays on the root *he'emin* — Moshe said *lo ya'aminu*, the people in fact responded with *vaya'amen*, and Moshe himself later failed the test of *he'emantem* — and it locates the *tzara'at* of Moshe within this single thematic arc rather than simply attributing it to *lashon hara*. While one could invoke *lashon hara* — after all, Miriam's *tzara'at* is paradigmatically linked to *lashon hara* — the Gemara here frames the cause differently: it was the act of suspecting the innocent that drew the physical punishment.

### Mida Tova Speed

Rava — and some say Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina — derives from this passage an additional teaching: *mida tova memaheret* from *puraniot* — the attribute of divine beneficence acts faster than the attribute of punishment. The proof: when Moshe's hand came out of his chest leprous, the verse reads *vayotzi'ah vehinei yado metzora'at kashaleg* — he drew it out and behold it was leprous. But when God restored it, the verse says *vayotzi'ah me-cheiko vehinei shava k'visaro* — and it returned to the flesh color. The *midda tova* is derived from the word *mecheiko*: as soon as he began to remove his hand from within his clothing — before it had fully emerged — it had already returned to healthy flesh. The healing was faster than the affliction. This is presented as a demonstrable inference from the precise wording of the text.

### Nes B'toch Nes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּבְלַע מַטֵּה אַהֲרֹן אֶת מַטֹּתָם״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to discuss another miracle that transpired at that time. With regard to the verse, "And each man threw down his staff and they became serpents, **and Aaron's staff swallowed their staffs"** (Exodus 7:12), **Rabbi Elazar said:** This was **a miracle within a miracle.** It was Aaron's staff, not his serpent, that swallowed the other staffs.

The discussion of Moshe, Aharon, and the signs naturally draws in one more observation, still within the thematic orbit of the signs given at the *sneh* and subsequently performed before Pharaoh. Rabbi Elazar states: *nes b'toch nes* — a miracle within a miracle. The verse says Aharon's staff swallowed the staffs of the Egyptian sorcerers — and this occurred after the staff had already returned to being an inanimate staff, no longer in the form of a snake. That a wooden staff should swallow other staffs is itself a miracle of a different order than a serpent swallowing serpents — hence the miracle nested within the miracle.

## Zorek P.D. to P.D.

### Height of Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that there is a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis in a case where one threw an object **from the private domain to the** other **private domain** through the public domain between the two. Rabbi Akiva deems him liable, as one who threw an object from the private domain to the public domain, and the Rabbis deem him exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבָּה: לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה פְּלִיגִי, וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי — דְּמָר סָבַר אָמְרִינַן קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה, וּמָר סָבַר לָא אָמְרִינַן קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה. אֲבָל לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר, וְלָא יָלְפִינַן זוֹרֵק מִמּוֹשִׁיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba raised a dilemma** with regard to their dispute: **Are they disagreeing** with regard to a case where the object traveled **below ten** handbreadths from the ground? **And,** if so, **it is with regard to this** point **that they disagree: As this Master,** Rabbi Akiva **holds: We say** that **an object in airspace is considered at rest.** The object is considered as if it was actually placed in the public domain after being lifted from the private domain. **And this Master,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds: We do not say** that **an object in airspace is considered at rest. However,** with regard to a case where the object traveled **above ten** handbreadths from the ground, **everyone agrees** that one is **exempt, and we do not derive** the legal status of **throwing from** the legal status of **passing.** Although everyone agrees that one who passes an object from a private domain to another private domain via a public domain is liable, even if it was passed above ten handbreadths, as that was the service of the Levites, one who throws an object in that manner is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא: לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה פְּלִיגִי, וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי — דְּמָר סָבַר יָלְפִינַן זוֹרֵק מִמּוֹשִׁיט, וּמָר סָבַר לָא יָלְפִינַן זוֹרֵק מִמּוֹשִׁיט. אֲבָל לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? — קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps, they are disagreeing** with regard to a case where the object traveled **above ten** handbreadths from the ground, and **it is with regard to this that they disagree: As this Master,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds: We derive** the legal status of **throwing from** the legal status of **passing.** Therefore, one who throws an object that passes through the airspace of a public domain higher than ten handbreadths from the ground is liable. **And this Master,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds: We do not derive throwing from passing. However,** with regard to a case where the object traveled **beneath** the **ten** handbreadth airspace of the public domain, **everyone agrees** that he is **liable. What is the reason** for that? **An object in airspace is considered at rest.**

The Gemara now returns to its central halachic discussion. The *Mishnah* has stated that if one throws an object from a *reshut ha-yahid* to another *reshut ha-yahid* with a *reshut ha-rabim* in between, Rabbi Akiva holds one is *chayav* and the *chachamim* hold one is *patur*. The Gemara seeks to understand the precise nature of this debate. Rava poses the question: is the debate occurring when the object passes *lematta me'asara* — within ten *tefachim* of the ground, i.e., through the airspace of the *reshut ha-rabim* — or is it occurring *lema'ala me'asara*, above ten *tefachim*?

If the debate is within ten, then what separates Rabbi Akiva from the *chachamim* is whether we apply the principle of *kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — that an object passing through the airspace of a *reshut ha-rabim* is considered as though it came to rest there, thereby creating liability for transferring from *reshut ha-yahid* to *reshut ha-rabim*. Rabbi Akiva would hold *kluta k'mi she'huncha damya*, the Rabbis would not. Above ten *tefachim*, everyone would agree one is *patur*, since above ten is not the airspace of the *reshut ha-rabim*, and no one extends the special case of *moshit* — handing from one balcony to the next — to throwing.

If the debate is above ten, then the question is whether the special category of *moshit* — carrying on a raised surface alongside the *reshut ha-rabim* — can be extended to throwing. Rabbi Akiva holds *yafinan zorek mi-moshit*, the Rabbis hold *lo yafinan*. And below ten, everyone would agree one is *chayav* — *divrei hakol chayav* — since *kluta k'mi she'huncha* would be universally accepted within the airspace.

### Atzmah Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מִילְּתָא אִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב חִסְדָּא, וּפַשְׁטַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא מֵהָא: מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְעוֹבֵר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עַצְמָהּ — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים. מִדְּקָאָמַר ״בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עַצְמָהּ״, פְּשִׁיטָא לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Rav Ḥisda had a dilemma** with regard to **this matter, and Rav Hamnuna resolved it for him from this** *baraita*: With regard to an object that travels **from the private domain to the** other **private domain, and it passes through the public domain itself, Rabbi Akiva deems** one **liable and the Rabbis deem** one **exempt. From the fact that it says** in the *baraita*: **Through the public domain itself, it is obvious** that it is **with regard to** a case where the object traveled below **ten** handbreadths from the ground **that they disagree.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּמַעֲבִיר, לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — הוּא דִּמְחַיֵּיב, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לָא מְחַיֵּיב? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמּוֹצִיא מַשּׂוֹי לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — חַיָּיב, שֶׁכֵּן מַשָּׂא בְּנֵי קְהָת. אֶלָּא לָאו בְּזוֹרֵק, וּלְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה הוּא דִּמְחַיֵּיב, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לָא מְחַיֵּיב, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּקְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה פְּלִיגִי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to what** form of transfer is the *baraita* dealing? **If you say** it refers to **passing** an object in his hand, is it only when he passes it **below ten** handbreadths that **he is liable?** When he passes it **above ten** handbreadths **is he not liable? Didn't Rabbi Elazar say: One who carries out a load** from a private domain to a public domain **above ten** handbreadths from the ground **is liable, as that was the** manner in which **the descendants of Kehat,** from whom we derived the laws of carrying, carried their **burden** in the Tabernacle? **Rather, isn't** this *baraita* referring to a case **of throwing, and it is** in a case where the object travels **below ten** handbreadths from the ground **that one is liable,** and **above ten** handbreadths from the ground **one is not liable? Learn from it** that it is **with regard to** whether or not **an object in airspace is considered at rest** that **they disagree.** The Gemara summarizes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the crux of their dispute.

Rav Chana reported in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *mechayev haya Rebbi shtayim* — in this case Rebbi would hold one is *chayav* twice, once for *hotza'ah* — removing the object from the *reshut ha-rabim* into the house — and once for *hachnasah* — bringing it from the house back out to the *reshut ha-rabim*. Rav Chana then raises an objection: to say Rebbi holds *chayav* twice implies *mechayev Rebbi atoldah bimkom av* — that Rebbi holds one brings a separate *korban* for a *toldah* when done together with an *av*. But is that not contrary to Rebbi's own position? The *baraita* teaches that Rebbi derives the number thirty-nine *melachot* from the words *eileh ha-devarim* — and elsewhere the Gemara draws from this that thirty-nine is the maximum number of sin offerings corresponding to the thirty-nine *avot*, implying that doing an *av* and *toldah* together yields only one *korban*, not two. Rebbi explicitly holds that *toldah bimkom av* is only one *chiyuv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עַצְמָהּ״ — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this conclusion **disagrees** with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said: Rabbi Akiva deems** one **liable even** if the object travels **above ten** handbreadths. **And that** term that **was taught** in the *baraita*, **the public domain itself,** is **to convey to you the far-reaching** nature of the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who deem one exempt even if the object traveled in the public domain itself, and all the more so if it traveled above ten handbreadths, which is no longer within the bounds of the public domain.

Rav Hamnuna reads it as an emphasis that the object passed specifically within ten *tefachim*, i.e., through the *reshut ha-rabim* proper. Now, if the *baraita* were discussing ordinary carrying (*ma'avir*), there would be no need to specify *lematta me'asara*: Rabbi Elazar had already taught that *ha-motzi masa'uy lema'ala me'asara chayav*, one who carries an object above ten *tefachim* and then brings it to rest is also *chayav*, because the *bnei Kehat* carried the sacred vessels in that fashion. Hence for ordinary carrying, whether within or above ten *tefachim*, one is *chayav*, and there would be no reason to emphasize the *reshut ha-rabim atzmah* — within ten. The only scenario where it matters to specify *within ten* is throwing, where the object flies through the air and the question is whether its passage through the airspace constitutes a halachic resting. Therefore *atzmah* signals that the *baraita* — and hence the *Mishnah*'s debate — concerns throwing specifically within ten *tefachim*. *Shema mina*, the Gemara concludes: the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* is about *kluta k'mi she'huncha*.

Rabbi Elazar then offers a dissenting position: Rabbi Akiva would hold one *chayav* even above ten *tefachim*, and the word *atzmah* in the *baraita* serves not to define the scope of the debate but *lehodi'acha kochan de-rabbanan* — to demonstrate the full strength of the Rabbis' exempting position. According to this reading, the debate spans both within ten and above ten; the Rabbis exempt in both cases, and the *baraita* chose to describe the case within ten to show that even there, where the argument for liability is strongest, the Rabbis exempt.

### Gimmel Tefachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִי, דַּאֲמַר רַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִי: תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב. לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר. מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה — בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** This opinion of Rabbi Elazar **disagrees** with the opinion of **Rav Ḥilkiya bar Tovi, as Rav Ḥilkiya bar Tovi said:** If the thrown object traveled **within three** handbreadths from the ground, **everyone agrees** that one is **liable** because the Sages established the principle of *lavud*. *Lavud* means that any object within three handbreadths of another object is considered to be attached to it. Therefore, an object that traveled within three handbreadths of the ground is considered to have come to a complete rest. If the thrown object traveled **above ten** handbreadths from the ground, **everyone agrees** that one is **exempt.** If the thrown object traveled **between three** handbreadths **and ten** handbreadths from the ground, **we have come to the dispute** between **Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת. וְאִם הָיוּ רְשׁוּיוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ — מוּתָּר. מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **Within three** handbreadths of the ground, **everyone agrees** that one is **liable; above ten** handbreadths from the ground, **everyone agrees** that one is exempt by Torah law, and **it is only** prohibited **due to rabbinic decree.** The Sages prohibited throwing or passing an object from the private domain of one person set to the private domain of another person unless a joining of the courtyards is set. **And if both of** the private **domains were his it is permitted.** If the thrown object traveled **between three** handbreadths **and ten** handbreadths from the ground, **Rabbi Akiva deems** one **liable and the Sages deem** him **exempt.**

Rav Hilkiya bar Tuvi then introduces a tripartite structure that clarifies the landscape of the debate. Within three *tefachim* of the ground, *divrei hakol chayav* — everyone agrees one is *chayav*. Above ten *tefachim*, *divrei hakol patur* — everyone agrees one is exempt. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* occupies the middle range: *mi-gimmel v'ad yud* — from three *tefachim* up to ten.

A *baraita* cited immediately thereafter reinforces this: within three, *divrei hakol chayav*; above ten, it is only a rabbinic prohibition — *mishum shevut* — and if both rooftops belong to the same person, it is even permitted outright. This last point is striking: if one owns both private domains and throws above the ten-*tefach* airspace of the *reshut ha-rabim*, the prohibition is purely rabbinic, and self-ownership of both sides removes even that. The practical force of this ruling may be appreciated by considering the case of two rooftops on opposite sides of a *reshut ha-rabim*: the sole obstacle to throwing between them at roof height is the rabbinic concern about the appearance of carrying between two distinct private domains — a concern dissolved when they belong to a single owner. The concern about crossing the *reshut ha-rabim* itself, above ten *tefachim*, generates no biblical prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִם הָיוּ רְשׁוּיוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ — מוּתָּר. לֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב, דְּאִיתְּמַר: שְׁנֵי בָתִּים בִּשְׁנֵי צִדֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לִזְרוֹק מִזֶּה לָזֶה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מוּתָּר לִזְרוֹק מִזֶּה לָזֶה. וְלָאו מִי אוֹקֵימְנָא לְהַהִיא כְּגוֹן דְּמִידְּלֵי חַד וּמִתַּתֵּי חַד — דְּזִימְנִין נָפֵל, וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited above: **And if both of** the private **domains were his,** i.e., they belonged to the same person, **it is permitted. Let us say** that this **is a conclusive refutation** of **Rav's** opinion, **as** an amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to the following case: Concerning **two houses on two** opposite **sides of the public domain,** even if they belong to the same person, **Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: It is prohibited to throw** an object **from this** private domain **to that** private domain. **And Shmuel said: It is permitted to throw from this** private domain **to that** private domain. The Gemara rejects this and states: **Didn't we** already **establish that** Rav's statement is referring to a case **where one** of the houses **was elevated and one was low?** Due to the disparity in height, the concern is that **at times** the object **will fall** into the public domain, **and** one **will come to bring it** in from there and thereby violate a Torah prohibition.

The Gemara addresses an apparent conflict with a ruling of Rav: Rabbah bar Rav Huna reported in the name of Rav that one may not throw from one house to the other across a *reshut ha-rabim*, while Shmuel ruled it is permitted. The Gemara resolves this: the case of Rav's prohibition involves one building taller than the other, creating a significant likelihood that the thrown object will miss and fall into the *reshut ha-rabim*. Were it to fall, one might then go down and retrieve it — carrying in the *reshut ha-rabim*. It is that consequential risk, rather than the throw itself, that Rav prohibits. Rashi notes that the missed throw itself would not even constitute a *melacha b'shogeg*: if an object does not go where one intended, it lacks the character of *melekhet machshevet* — purposive, intentional craft — and is therefore not a *melacha* at all. The concern is the subsequent retrieval.

### Three Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: כׇּל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה — כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לָהּ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים שֶׁתִּילָּקֵט בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Hamnuna, and some say** that **Rav Hamnuna** said **to Rav Ḥisda:** From **where is this matter that the Sages stated: Any** objects **less than three** handbreadths apart **are considered to be *lavud*,** attached? **He said to him: Because it is impossible for the public domain to be made level with planes.** Since the space cannot be completely smooth, even the minor differences in the ground level throughout the public domain must be taken into consideration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שְׁלֹשָׁה נָמֵי? וְתוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: הַמְשַׁלְשֵׁל דְּפָנוֹת מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, אִם הֵן גְּבוֹהִין מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — פְּסוּלָה. הָא פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** He asked him: **If so,** if that is the reason, objects within **three** handbreadths should **also** be considered *lavud*. Why is it that only objects within less than three handbreadths are considered attached? **And furthermore,** an inference can be made from **that which we learned** in the mishna with regard to the *halakhot* of *sukka*: If **one lowers the walls** of a *sukka* **from the top to the bottom, if** the bottom of the wall **is above three handbreadths** from the ground, the *sukka* **is invalid** because it is considered to be lacking walls. By inference, if one lowers the walls so that the bottom of the wall is **below three** handbreadths from the ground, it is **valid.** In this case, the rationale that it is impossible for the public domain to be made level does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ מְחִיצָה שֶׁהַגְּדָיִים בּוֹקְעִין בָּהּ. תִּינַח לְמַטָּה, לְמַעְלָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא כׇּל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי — הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** He rejects this: **There, the reason** that a space larger than three handbreadths is not considered to be part of the wall is **because it,** i.e., the wall, **is a partition that goats pass through.** Therefore, it is a partition incapable of serving its function. Once a partition is below three handbreadths, it will obstruct the passage of the goats. Furthermore, according to this explanation, **it works out well** when the measure of three handbreadths is **below,** adjacent to the ground. If any more than three handbreadths of space are between the ground and the wall, it is not considered a wall. However, there are several *halakhot* in which *lavud* applies **above** and not near the ground, e.g., when the roofing of the *sukka* is not connected to the walls. **What,** then, **can be said** to explain that *halakha*? **Rather,** the conclusion is that the *halakha* which states that **anything that is less than three is considered to be *lavud* is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **learned through tradition.**

The Gemara asks — and this exchange is attributed to either Rav Chisda or Rav Hamnuna — where does this principle of *kol pachas mi-gimmel kelavud dami* — anything within three *tefachim* is considered connected — come from? The answer offered is a functional rationale: *lefi she-ee efshar le-reshut ha-rabim she-tisleket b'malet uv'rehatni* — it is impossible to perfectly level a *reshut ha-rabim* using a plane and roller. Any public thoroughfare will have natural undulations, and therefore the ground effectively extends, in practical terms, for up to three *tefachim* above the theoretical floor. Anything within three is therefore halachically part of the ground.

This explanation, however, immediately runs into difficulties. If the reasoning is purely about the physical character of the *reshut ha-rabim*, it should not apply elsewhere — yet the principle of *lavud* is applied broadly, including to the walls of a *sukkah*. The Gemara cites the *Mishnah* in *Sukkah*: a wall suspended from above is valid if it comes within three *tefachim* of the ground, invalid if it is higher than that — and there, the *reshut ha-rabim* rationale is plainly irrelevant. The Gemara responds that the *sukkah* case operates on a different principle: a gap of more than three *tefachim* at the base of a wall is invalid because *gedayim bokin bah* — goats can pass through, and it is not practically a wall. Less than three, goats cannot pass through, so it functions as a wall.

But the Gemara presses further: *tainach lematah* — that explains the bottom; but what about above? The *lavud* principle is applied also at the top of a wall or between the wall and the *sechach*: if the top of the wall falls within three *tefachim* of the *sechach*, it is treated as if the wall reaches all the way up. That scenario cannot be explained by the *reshut ha-rabim* rationale or by the *gedayim* consideration. The Gemara therefore concedes: *kol pachas mi-shelosha kelavud dami hilcheta gemiri lah* — the principle that anything within three is treated as connected is a received tradition, a *halacha* without a fully generalizable logical basis.

### Lavud Tradition

The Gemara's admission that *lavud* ultimately rests on *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* rather than logic is worth pausing over. The discussion had proposed an elegant functional explanation — the natural unevenness of public thoroughfares — but successive applications of the principle outside the *reshut ha-rabim* context could not be accommodated by that reasoning. The *sukkah* cases, particularly the top-of-wall application, forced the Gemara to acknowledge that *lavud* is a received tradition that transcends any single rationale. It had to be anchored somewhere, and this passage is where the Gemara explicitly does so.

### Bayis Kluta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד בָּאֶמְצַע — רַבִּי מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לֹא חִיֵּיב רַבִּי אֶלָּא בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד מְקוֹרֶה, דְּאָמְרִינַן בֵּיתָא כְּמַאן דְּמַלְיָא דָּמֵי, אֲבָל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקוֹרֶה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a case in a *baraita* similar to the one discussed in the mishna: One who throws an object **from the public domain to the** other **public domain and** the object passes through **the private domain between** the two, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems him liable** for carrying into the private domain, **and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.** With regard to this, **Rav and Shmuel both said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** him **liable only** if **the private domain** between the two public areas **is covered** with a roof. In that case, **we say** that **the house is considered full** and an object that passes through it is considered as if it landed upon an actual object. **However,** if the private domain **is not covered,** he **is not** liable even according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

Having analyzed the case of throwing from *reshut ha-yahid* to *reshut ha-yahid* across a *reshut ha-rabim*, the Gemara turns to the reverse: throwing from *reshut ha-rabim* to *reshut ha-rabim* with a *reshut ha-yahid* in between. The *baraita* states: *reshut ha-rabim le-reshut ha-rabim u-reshut ha-yahid ba'emtza* — one threw from one public domain to another with a private domain in between — *Rebbi mechayev ve-chachamim potrin*. Rabbi — Rebbi Yehuda ha-Nasi — holds one is *chayav*; the *chachamim* exempt.

Rava and Shmuel both clarify that Rebbi imposes *chiyuv* only when the intervening *reshut ha-yahid* is *mekura* — roofed, like a house — not when it is an open yard or courtyard. The reason: *bayis k'man d'malya dami* — a roofed enclosure is treated as though it were completely filled. The thrown object, passing through a window, traversing the enclosed space, and exiting through another window, is treated as though it came to rest within the house, because the house, conceptually, is a full, bounded box. When an object enters that box, the principle of *kluta k'mi she'huncha* applies with particular force: the complete enclosure — floor, walls, and roof — creates the most persuasive case for treating the airspace as equivalent to rest. An open courtyard, lacking a roof, does not generate this conceptual filling, and Rebbi would agree with the *chachamim* there.

### Av Toldah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי שְׁתַּיִם — אַחַת מִשּׁוּם הוֹצָאָה, וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם הַכְנָסָה.
>
> **Translation:** On this topic, **Rav Ḥana said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **would deem him liable** to bring **two** sin-offerings in this case, **one for carrying out** from the private domain into the second public domain, **and one for carrying in,** when the object initially entered the private domain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב חָנָא וְקָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ḥana was sitting, and** the following point **was difficult for him:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּמְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי אַתּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב?
>
> **Translation:** **Is that to say** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** one **liable** for **a subcategory** of prohibited labor when performed **with a primary category** of prohibited labor? After all, carrying out and carrying in constitute a primary category of prohibited labor and its subcategory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״דְּבָרִים״ ״הַדְּבָרִים״ ״אֵלֶּה הַדְּבָרִים״ — אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים וָתֵשַׁע מְלָאכוֹת שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרוּ לְמֹשֶׁה בְּסִינַי!
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that Shabbat is mentioned in the verse: "These are the things [*eleh hadevarim*] that God has commanded to perform them" (Exodus 35:1)? Several points are derived from the superfluous emphases in this verse. The Torah could simply have stated: This is a thing [*davar*]. When it states **things [*devarim*]** in the plural, it teaches at least two points. The addition of the definite article the in the term **the things [*hadevarim*]** adds at least a third point. The numerological value of letters of the word *eleh*, which are *alef*, one; *lamed*, thirty; and *heh*, five, is thirty-six. The phrase: **These are the things,** alludes to three plus thirty-six derivation, i.e., the **thirty-nine prohibited labors that were stated to Moses at Sinai.** Since Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that there are a fixed number of primary categories of labor, he would certainly hold a person liable for the primary categories but not for the subcategories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: מָר אַהָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ, וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַדְּרַבִּי. אֲנַן אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתְנִינַן — וְלָא קַשְׁיָא לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to him: The Master taught** Rav Yehuda's statement **with regard to this, and** consequently, **he** encounters **a difficulty.** One statement of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi contradicts another statement of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **We learn** the statement of Rav Yehuda **with regard to the** opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** therefore **there is no difficulty for us.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְעָבַר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who threw an object **from the private domain to the public domain, and it traveled four cubits in the public domain, Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שְׁתַּיִם — אַחַת מִשּׁוּם הוֹצָאָה וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם הַעֲבָרָה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ חֲדָא הוּא דִּמְחַיֵּיב, מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן פָּטְרִי לִגְמָרֵי? הָא אַפֵּיק לַהּ מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה חֲדָא הוּא דִּמְחַיֵּיב, וְרַבָּנַן פָּטְרִי לִגְמָרֵי, וְהֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר עַד דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים תְּנוּח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Yehuda would deem him liable** to bring **two** sin-offerings in this case, **one for carrying out** from the private domain into the public domain **and one for carrying** the object four cubits through the public domain. The Rabbis deem him exempt for carrying four cubits in the public domain. And it must be interpreted that way **because if it would enter your mind** to say that Rabbi Yehuda **deems** him **liable** to bring only **one** sin-offering, **by inference, the Rabbis deem** him **completely exempt.** How is that possible? Didn't **he carry** an object out **from the private domain into the public domain?** This proof is rejected: **And from where** do you draw that conclusion? **Perhaps I could actually say to you** that **Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable** to bring **one** sin-offering **and the Rabbis deem** him **completely exempt, and how do you find that** circumstance? In a case **where he said:** My intention is that **as soon it,** the object, **goes out into the public domain it will** immediately **come to rest.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: אָמְרִינַן קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה, וְאִיתְעֲבִידָא לֵיהּ מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה, וְלָא אִיתְעֲבִידָא לֵיהּ מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ! אֲבָל אַתּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב לָא מְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And they disagree with regard to this: Rabbi Yehuda maintains** that **we say: An object in airspace is considered at rest, and** therefore **his intention was fulfilled.** As soon as the object enters the airspace of the public domain it is considered to have come to rest. **And the Rabbis maintain** that **we do not say: An object in airspace is considered at rest, and** therefore **his intention was not fulfilled** and he is exempt. **However, Rabbi Yehuda does not hold** one **liable** for **a subcategory** of labor performed together with **a primary category** of labor.

Rav Yosef then clarifies: the statement that one is *chayav* twice was not said in explanation of Rebbi's ruling about throwing through the house. It was said in explanation of a different *baraita*, dealing with the debate of Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*: *reshut ha-yahid le-reshut ha-rabim v'avar arba amot be-reshut ha-rabim* — one threw from a *reshut ha-yahid* into a *reshut ha-rabim* and the object traveled four *amot* within the *reshut ha-rabim*. Rabbi Yehuda holds *chayav* and the *chachamim* exempt. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel explains that this is a debate about *shtayim* — whether one is *chayav* twice. The reason the *chachamim* cannot be understood as exempting entirely is that obviously one has transferred from a private to a public domain — one would be *chayav* at least once. Therefore the debate must be: is one *chayav* an additional time for traversing four *amot* in the *reshut ha-rabim*? Rabbi Yehuda holds yes — twice — and the *chachamim* hold only once.

The attribution of *chayav shtayim* to this debate of Rabbi Yehuda rather than to Rebbi resolves the problem: Rabbi Yehuda is not bound by Rebbi's position against holding *toldah bimkom av* liable independently. Rabbi Yehuda could hold like Rabbi Eliezer that one does bring two *korbanos* for an *av* and *toldah* together.

### Holtz Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסִיף אַף הַשּׁוֹבֵט וְהַמְדַקְדֵּק. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שׁוֹבֵט הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל מֵיסֵךְ, מְדַקְדֵּק הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל אוֹרֵג. מַאי לָאו דְּעַבְדִינְהוּ לְתַרְוַויְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַתּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **It could not enter your mind** to say so, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda adds even lining up** the threads of the warp **and beating** the threads of the woof to the list of primary categories of labor. The Rabbis **said to him: Lining up** is a subcategory **subsumed under** the primary category of **stretching** the threads of the warp within the loom, and **beating is subsumed under** the primary category of **weaving. Is this not** referring to a case where **one performed both** lining up and beating **together, and learn from it** that **Rabbi Yehuda deems** one **liable** for both **a subcategory** and **a primary category** of labor when they are performed together?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְעוֹלָם דְּעַבְדַּהּ לְהָא לְחוּדַּהּ וְהָא לְחוּדַּהּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַתּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב לָא מְחַיֵּיב, וּבְהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר הָנֵי אָבוֹת נִינְהוּ, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי הָנֵי תּוֹלְדוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And from where** do you draw that conclusion? **Perhaps** it is **actually** referring to a case where **one performed this** action **alone and this** action **alone, and Rabbi Yehuda does not deem** one **liable for a subcategory** of labor performed together with **a primary category** of labor. **And** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to this. Rabbi Yehuda maintains** as follows: **These** actions of lining up and beating **are** additional **primary categories** of labor, **and the Rabbis maintain** as follows: **These are subcategories.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּדַע, דְּקָתָנֵי ״רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסִיף״. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אָבוֹת, מַאי מוֹסִיף — מוֹסִיף אָבוֹת. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ תּוֹלָדוֹת, מַאי מוֹסִיף? אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לֹא חִיֵּיב רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Know** that this is so, **as** the *baraita* **teaches: Rabbi Yehuda adds.** The Gemara explains this quote from the *baraita*: **Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Yehuda meant that these are **primary categories** of labor, **what** is the meaning of: **He adds?** It means **he adds primary categories** of labor. **However, if you say** that he meant that these are **subcategories, what** is the meaning of: **He adds? It was also stated** that it was **Rabba and Rav Yosef who both said: Rabbi Yehuda deemed** him **liable** to bring **only one** sin-offering.

The Gemara then probes the precise structure of the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*. One possibility: the Rabbis do not fully exempt — rather, they agree one is *chayav* once but dispute only the second *chiyuv*. The object entered the *reshut ha-rabim*, and then — applying *kluta k'mi she'huncha* — one is *chayav* for that entry; then it traveled four *amot*, yielding a potential second *chiyuv*. Rabbi Yehuda imposes both; the Rabbis impose only one. This reading naturally accounts for why the *baraita* specifies four *amot*: the four *amot* represent the second act.

### One vs Two

An alternative reading is proposed: perhaps the Rabbis exempt entirely, and the debate concerns whether *kluta k'mi she'huncha* applies at all. The scenario: one explicitly intended the object to come to rest as soon as it entered the *reshut ha-rabim*, but it flew on and came to rest four *amot* later. Rabbi Yehuda holds *kluta k'mi she'huncha damya* — it is treated as having rested at the moment of entry — and therefore his intention was satisfied; one is *chayav*. The Rabbis hold *lo amrinan kluta k'mi she'huncha* — we do not treat the airspace as rest — and therefore the object never landed where one wanted, the intention was unfulfilled, and one is *patur* entirely. On this reading, there is no second *chiyuv* in the picture at all; it is simply a dispute about whether the action was completed.

The Gemara dismisses this alternative: *lo salka dach-tach* — it cannot hold. Proof comes from the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda on the *melachot*: *Rabbi Yehuda mosif af ha-shovet veha-medakdek* — Rabbi Yehuda added *shovet* and *medakdek* to the list of prohibited labors. The *chachamim* responded that both are *toldot* — derivatives — of existing *avot*. Significantly, the debate is framed as Rabbi Yehuda *adding* these labors. If the question were simply whether Rabbi Yehuda holds thirty-nine or forty-one *avot*, the debate would be stated differently. The word *mosif* — he added — implies he agreed they were *toldot* and was adding them nonetheless, meaning the debate is whether one is *chayav* for a *toldah bimkom av*. And *shema mina* — we derive — that Rabbi Yehuda does hold one is *chayav* for a *toldah bimkom av*, confirming that the debate in the carrying case is indeed about one *chiyuv* versus two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וּלְמַאי דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא דִּמְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שְׁתַּיִם? אִי לְהָכָא קָבָעֵי לַהּ — לְהָכָא לָא קָבָעֵי לַהּ. אִי לְהָכָא קָבָעֵי לַהּ — לְהָכָא לָא קָבָעֵי לַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁתִּרְצֶה — תָּנוּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: And according to what originally entered our mind** that **Rabbi Yehuda deemed** him **liable** to bring **two** sin-offerings, how could he be liable for both carrying out from the private domain and for carrying four cubits in the public domain? **If one** only **wanted** the object to land **here** at the beginning of the public domain, **he did not want** it to land **here,** four cubits into the public domain. Conversely, **if one** only **wanted** the object to land **here,** four cubits into the public domain, **he did not want** it to land **here,** at the beginning of the public domain. Rav Ashi **said to** Ravina: It is possible in a case where **one says: Any place that it wants to** come to rest, let it come to **rest.** One indicated that his intention would be fulfilled wherever the thrown object lands.

Returning now to the question of *chiyuv shtayim*: the Gemara asks, on the assumption that Rabbi Yehuda holds one *chayav* twice, how can that even work here? If one intended the object to land at point A — the moment of entry into *reshut ha-rabim* — one did not intend it to land at point B, four *amot* later. And if one intended point B, one did not intend point A. How can two separate *chiyuvim* attach to a single throw aimed at a single destination? The Gemara resolves: *be'omer kol makom she-tirtze tanu'ach* — the case is where one said explicitly: I do not care where it lands, wherever it stops is fine with me. Since one expressed complete indifference to the landing point, halachically two distinct acts are recognized: the object entered the airspace of *reshut ha-rabim*, and — because of *kluta k'mi she'huncha* — that constitutes act one; it then traveled further and came to rest four *amot* later, constituting act two. Since one had no preference for either location over the other, both are attributed to one's intention, and both generate *chiyuv*.

### Indifference Case

What is conceptually remarkable about this conclusion is the superimposition of two discrete acts onto a single, continuous motion. The object never actually stopped moving between entry into the *reshut ha-rabim* and its final resting point four *amot* away. Yet halakha treats it as though it stopped and then resumed — by virtue of *kluta k'mi she'huncha* at the moment of entry, and then by virtue of the actual stop four *amot* later. One did not initiate two throws; one threw once. And yet, because the *halacha* treats the airspace entry as equivalent to rest, and because one declared indifference to outcome, two acts of *hotza'ah* are recognized, yielding two *chiyuvim* according to Rabbi Yehuda. Rashi observes that if the object had instead missed and fallen into the *reshut ha-rabim* against one's intent, that would not even constitute a *melacha b'shogeg* — the lack of alignment between intent and result in that scenario would be so fundamental as to negate the characterization of the act as *melekhet machshevet* entirely.

## Intent Precision

### Intent Subset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא נִתְכַּוֵּון לִזְרוֹק שְׁמֹנֶה וְזָרַק אַרְבַּע — הֲרֵי כָּתַב ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן. נִתְכַּוֵּון לִזְרוֹק אַרְבַּע וְזָרַק שְׁמֹנֶה — מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הָא אַפֵּיק לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי, הָא לָא נָח. וְלָאו הַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁתִּרְצֶה תָּנוּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning throwing an object on Shabbat from one domain to another and within a single domain, the Gemara raises several issues with regard to intention when throwing. It is **obvious** that **one who intended to throw** an object **eight** cubits in a public domain **and** actually **threw** it only **four** cubits is liable **because** that case is similar to a case where **one wrote** the word ***shem*,** the first two letters **of** the name **Shimon.** In the case of writing *shem*, the individual performed the prohibited labor of writing a two-letter word, even though he did not complete the word that he originally intended to write. The question is as follows: **What** is the *halakha* if **one intended to throw** an object **four** cubits **and threw** it **eight? Do we say he did** indeed **carry** the object, **or perhaps** we say that ultimately the object **did not land where he wanted** it to land? **But** is **that not** precisely what **Ravina said to Rav Ashi,** as mentioned above? **And** Rav Ashi **said** in response that it is referring to a case **where one says: Any place that it wants to** come to rest, let it come to **rest.** In such a scenario one is liable, because he expressed the fact that he is contented with any labor that will be performed with the object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ הֲרֵי כָּתַב ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן, מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם כַּמָּה דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״שֵׁם״ לָא מִכְּתִיב לֵיהּ ״שִׁמְעוֹן״, הָכָא כַּמָּה דְּלָא זָרֵיק אַרְבַּע — לָא מִיזְדַּרְקִי לֵיהּ תַּמְנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, the first case, which seems obvious, also requires clarification. **And that which you said,** that this is similar to a case where **one wrote** the word ***shem*,** the first two letters **of** the name **Shimon, is** it in fact **similar? There, as long as** the letters of ***shem*,** *shin* and *mem*, **are not written,** the name **Shimon cannot be written. Here,** where one intended to throw the object eight cubits and he threw it only four, is it true that **as long as it was not thrown four** cubits **it cannot be thrown eight?** An object can be thrown eight cubits without first landing after four cubits. The question remains unresolved.

The Gemara's final passage, with which the daf concludes, turns explicitly to the question of how precisely one's *kavanah* must match the outcome in order to generate *chiyuv* — specifically in the context of carrying and throwing. The Gemara states: *peshita* — it is obvious — that if one intended to throw something eight *amot* and it only went four, one is *chayav*. The analogy: one who intended to write the name *Shimon* but wrote only as far as *shin-mem* — the word *shem* — is *chayav*, because writing *shem* is included within writing *Shimon*. The intended act subsumes the actual act: four *amot* is contained within the intention to go eight.

### Intent Superset

The harder case — *niskaven lizrok arba v'zarak shmona mai* — is the reverse: one intended to throw four *amot* but the object traveled eight. Two ways of analyzing this are proposed. On one view, *ha-apik lei* — you removed the object from its original four-*amah* domain, and that removal is what constitutes the *melacha* of carrying four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabim*; one is therefore *chayav* regardless of whether it went further. On another view, the landing point matters: *heicha d'ba'ei lo nach* — where one wanted it to land, after four *amot*, it did not land — it traveled further. One's intention was for a specific endpoint, and that endpoint was not realized.

The Gemara then notes the connection to what was just established above. We concluded that one is *chayav* twice in the four-*amot* case only when one explicitly declares indifference to the landing point — *kol makom she-tirtze tanu'ach*. This framing now implies that without such explicit indifference, if one intended a specific result and the object exceeded that intention, one is *patur* — because *heicha d'ba'ei lo nach*, it did not land where one wanted. This is the question the daf leaves open: when the actual outcome is a superset of the intended outcome — not a subset — does *chiyuv* attach? The particularly stringent standard of *kavanah* that governs the *melacha* of carrying, where even carrying an object the wrong direction can exempt, suggests the answer may be no. The resolution of this question is deferred to the following daf.
