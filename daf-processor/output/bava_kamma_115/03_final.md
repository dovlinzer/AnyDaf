# Bava Kamma 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stolen Vessels Framework

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּכִּיר כֵּלָיו וּסְפָרָיו בְּיַד אַחֵר, וְיָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם גְּנֵיבָה בָּעִיר – יִשָּׁבַע לוֹ לוֹקֵחַ כַּמָּה נָתַן, וְיִטּוֹל. וְאִם לָאו – לָאו כֹּל הֵימֶנּוּ, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: מְכָרָן לְאַחֵר, וּלְקָחָן זֶה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who recognizes his** stolen **vessels and scrolls in another's possession, and a rumor had spread in the city** that the former had been the victim of **theft, the purchaser,** i.e., the one in possession of these items, **must take an oath to** the victim as to **how much** money **he spent** on the purchase, **and he** then **takes** that sum of money in exchange for returning the items. **And if no** such rumor had spread, **it is not in** the purported victim's **power** to assert that the items were stolen, and he is not entitled to demand their return, **as I** could **say:** The items were never stolen; rather, the purported victim **sold them to another, and this** individual who currently possesses the item **purchased them from** that other person.

The daf opens with the *mishna* beginning approximately fifteen lines from the bottom of 114b. The *mishna* presents the following case: a person recognizes his vessels or books — at that time, of course, books were scrolls and extraordinarily expensive — in the possession of another. He sees someone walking around with his objects. The *mishna* then adds a critical condition: *veyatza lo shem geneiva ba'ir* — there was a known report in town that he had been burgled. Given that presumption, the *mishna* rules that *yishava lo loke'ach kama natan veyitol* — the purchaser swears how much he paid and collects that sum from the original owner, who then retrieves his object.

The underlying assumption is that this all takes place before *ye'ush* — before the owner has despaired of recovering his property. Prior to *ye'ush*, the stolen object legally remains his, and he retains the right to reclaim it. But here a complication arises: why should the purchaser be entitled to any reimbursement at all? He is in possession of a stolen object. The thief — not the original owner — is the one who wronged him. The owner's obligation to compensate the purchaser flows entirely from *takanat hashuk*, a special enactment designed to facilitate commerce. Without such protection, every buyer would face the specter of having a purchased item claimed as stolen and taken from him, leaving him no recourse but to chase down a thief who has long since absconded. The constraint this would place on commerce was deemed intolerable, and so the Sages enacted that even if an item turns out to have been stolen, the innocent purchaser may recover his payment from the original owner. Once the owner retrieves his object, he in turn may pursue the thief for reimbursement.

The *mishna* closes with the converse: *ve'im lav* — if there was no such report of a burglary — *lav kol heimenu*, the owner is not believed. The reason is *she'ani omer machran le'achar velakan zeh mimenu*: one might simply say the owner sold the object, regretted it, and is now attempting to reclaim it through a fabricated story. Without something to substantiate the claim of theft, the owner cannot compel the purchaser to return the item.

### Takanat Hashuk

Two distinct legal interests are in tension throughout this *mishna*. On one hand, *takanat hashuk* protects the innocent purchaser from suffering a loss through no fault of his own. On the other hand, the *mishna* is also being somewhat lenient toward the original owner: it credits his claim of theft on the basis of a community report, which is a considerably lower evidentiary threshold than one might expect when forcibly retrieving an object from someone else's possession. One way to understand this is that the two considerations reinforce each other: precisely because the purchaser will be compensated, the threshold for crediting the owner's theft claim may be lowered. Since the purchaser is not left empty-handed, there is less injustice in crediting a claim supported merely by community rumor.

### Report Insufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכִי יָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם גְּנֵיבָה בָּעִיר – מַאי הָוֵי? לֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא זַבְּנִינְהוּ, וְהוּא נִיהוּ קָא מַפֵּיק שְׁמָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And if a rumor spread in the city** that he had been the victim of **theft, what of it? Let us suspect** that **perhaps he sold the items and he himself is spreading the rumor** that they were stolen so that he will be able to buy them back.

The *gemara* immediately challenges the *mishna*'s reliance on the community report. *V'chi yatza lo shem geneiva ba'ir mai havei?* — so what if there is a rumor? Perhaps the owner sold the item, regretted the sale, and is himself the one spreading the rumor to manufacture the appearance of a burglary. The *gemara* presses the point: not only is a mere report not direct evidence, but the same person whose word we would need to trust in order to credit the report is the very person who has a motive to fabricate it. A rumor that can just as easily have been engineered by the claimant offers no genuine evidentiary weight.

### Objective Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּאוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְעָמַד וְהִפְגִּין בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאָמַר: נִגְנְבוּ כֵלַיִי. כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן עִילָּא מָצָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where people,** i.e., guests, **came into his house, and he arose at night and shouted [*vehifgin*], and said: My vessels have been stolen,** in which case it seems clear that he was the victim of theft. The Gemara rejects this rationale: On the contrary, the suspicion that he is lying should apply **all the more so,** as **he has found a pretext** for claiming that his property was stolen, and it should be suspected that he is taking advantage of the situation dishonestly.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav that what is required is objective, independently generated evidence — for example, people who had actually slept in the owner's house that night, and the owner rose in the night screaming *nignevu chelai* — my belongings have been stolen. (The lecturer notes that *hifkin* is the root of the modern Hebrew word *hafganah*, a protest.) The *gemara*, however, dismisses even this: the *gemara* responds *kol sheken ee lo matza* — this is even more reason for suspicion, since all of that could be a performance staged by the owner himself. There is still no truly objective evidence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה מַחְתֶּרֶת חֲתוּרָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וּבְנֵי אָדָם שֶׁלָּנוּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – יָצְאוּ, וְאַנְבּוּרְקְרָאוֹת שֶׁל כֵּלִים עַל כִּתְפֵיהֶם, וְהַכֹּל אוֹמְרִים: ״נִגְנְבוּ כֵּלָיו שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana** would **conclude this** *halakha* by stating **in the name of Rav:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** a group of people spent the night in his house. And **there was a tunnel** that had been **burrowed into his house, and the people that stayed overnight** as guests **in his house left with bundles of vessels on their shoulders, and everyone says: So-and-so's vessels were stolen.** In such a case, there is clear evidence that the homeowner was the victim of theft.

Rav Kahana then added in the name of Rav a further element that substantially strengthens the showing: *kegon shehayta machteret chatura betoch beito* — there was a tunnel dug under the house. People had been seen leaving the house with bundles of objects on their shoulders, *tzrorot shel keilim al kitfeihem*, and the entire town was saying that this particular person's belongings had been stolen — *hakol omrim nignevu keilav shel ploni*. This is not evidence the owner could have manufactured: there are eyewitnesses, physical evidence in the form of a tunnel, and a community-wide consensus.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא כֵּלִים הֲווֹ, סְפָרִים לָא הֲווֹ! אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן דְּקָאָמְרִי נָמֵי סְפָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But perhaps** only **vessels were** stolen but **scrolls were not.** Why must the purchaser return the scrolls as well as the vessels? The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** everyone **is saying** that **scrolls** were **also** stolen.

The *gemara* then raises another concern: even granted that the owner was burgled, how do we know that the specific objects now in the purchaser's possession are the very objects stolen? The *mishna* mentions both vessels and books — perhaps only one category was reported stolen. Moreover, how do we match the particular item claimed to the item actually taken? Perhaps the stolen object was old and the owner is now claiming a new one; perhaps it was small and he is claiming a large one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא זוּטְרֵי, וְקָא טָעֵין רַבְרְבֵי! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, דְּקָאָמְרִי: סֵפֶר פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let us suspect** that **perhaps** only **small** scrolls were taken, while **he is claiming** that **large** scrolls were stolen. If this is the case, he is claiming scrolls that do not belong to him. The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** The mishna is discussing a case where people **are saying: Such and such a scroll and such and such** a scroll were stolen, i.e., it is known which scrolls were taken from the homeowner.

Rav Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the report must have specifically mentioned books as well. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina added that the report must have identified the specific items. Rav added further that witnesses must directly testify that the items now in the purchaser's hands — *halalu kelim shel ploni, halalu sefarim shel ploni* — are the very ones belonging to the original owner. This is a dramatic escalation of evidentiary requirements far beyond anything the *mishna* itself seemed to demand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הֲווֹ עַתִּיקֵי, וְקָא טָעֵין חַדְתֵי! אָמַר רַב, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמְרִי: ״הַלָּלוּ כֵּלָיו שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי, הַלָּלוּ סְפָרָיו שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this as well: **But perhaps** the scrolls that were stolen **were old** and had therefore depreciated in value, while **he is claiming** that **new** scrolls were taken. If so, he is claiming scrolls that do not belong to him. **Rav said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** people **say** explicitly: **These are so-and-so's vessels** and **these are so-and-so's scrolls.** In this case it is known exactly which items were stolen.

The *gemara* also addresses the *ba bamachteret* ruling of Rav — the principle in Sanhedrin that one who tunnels into a house and steals thereby *acquires* the objects, since by exposing himself to being killed (*masar nafshia likatla*), he is treated as having "paid" for them (*b'damei kaninhu*). The *gemara* distinguishes: that principle applies when the thief tunneled *in*, exposing himself to the homeowner's lethal defense. Here, the intruders merely slept in the house and tunneled *out* — they never assumed the mortal risk entailed by a true *ba bamachteret*, and therefore Rav's acquisition principle does not apply and the objects must be returned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּקְנָנְהוּ – בְּבָא בַּמַּחְתֶּרֶת, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מְסַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִקְטָלָא; אֲבָל הָנֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מְסַרוּ נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ לִקְטָלָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This matter, that** one who steals **acquires** what he stole, **applies** only **to a burglar, as he initially subjected himself to** be **killed** when he broke into the house. **But** concerning **these** thieves, who stayed overnight as guests of the homeowner, **since they did not** break into the house and thereby **subject themselves to** being **killed,** they are **not** exempt from returning the stolen item.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת הֶעָשׂוּי לִמְכּוֹר כֵּלָיו, אֲבָל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁאֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי לִמְכּוֹר כֵּלָיו –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Rava says: We taught** that all of these conditions must be met before the purchaser can be forced to return the item **only** with regard to **a homeowner who is wont to sell his vessels, but** with regard to **a homeowner who is not wont to sell his vessels,**

The *gemara* then records that Rava qualified all the preceding demands: *lo shanu ela bebaal habayis ha'asuy limkor kelav* — these heightened evidentiary requirements apply only when the owner is the sort of person who regularly sells his household items. If he is not someone who typically sells his belongings, the threshold drops considerably, and we return closer to the simple reading of the *mishna*. The *gemara* raises the objection that even someone who normally does not sell might occasionally need money and do so, to which Rav Ashi responds that this is precisely why some substantiating report is needed — but once such a report exists, combined with the low likelihood that this particular owner would sell, that suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צָרִיךְ לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי עֲלֵיהּ כּוּלֵּי הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** one **does not need to pursue** the matter in **all this** detail.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, וְזַבֵּין! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הֲרֵי יָצָא לוֹ שֵׁם גְּנֵיבָה בָּעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this ruling: **Perhaps he needed money, and he** therefore **sold** the items despite the fact that he does not generally sell his personal belongings. The Gemara answers that **Rav Ashi said: A rumor spread in the city** that he had been a victim of **theft.** It is reasonable to assume that he is the rightful owner, as he does not usually sell his belongings and it is common knowledge that he was the victim of theft.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot raises a difficulty with the final evidentiary requirement as it appears in the *gemara*: if witnesses directly testify that what the purchaser holds is the owner's property, there seems to be no gap at all — it would be direct proof, not a matter of community report and inference. Tosafot therefore reads the statement differently: what the witnesses saw and reported was that the thieves' bags contained a specific item — say, a particular silver *iPhone* — and the purchaser is now holding an item matching that description exactly. There remains a tiny inferential step, but an extraordinarily small one. This reading preserves the *mishna*'s framework of substantiated community report while explaining why the *gemara* required such a close correspondence between the described stolen item and the item actually claimed.

## Multiple Parties

### Chanan Bisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: גָּנַב וּמָכַר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב – רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר: הַדִּין עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: הַדִּין עִם הַשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** § Having assessed the mishna's ruling with regard to a case where the thief remained unidentified, the Gemara discusses a case where the thief was found. **It was stated:** In a case where a thief **stole** an item **and sold** it, **and later the thief was identified, Rav says in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya:** The homeowner's **claim** can be pursued only **with the first** one, i.e., the thief, but he has no claim against the purchaser. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** The homeowner's **claim** can also be pursued **with the second** one, i.e., with the purchaser.

The *gemara* introduces a new scenario: *ganav umachar ve'achar kach hukar haganav* — the thief stole, sold the stolen item to a purchaser, and then was subsequently identified. Unlike the *mishna*'s case, where the thief's identity may be entirely unknown, here all three parties — the original owner, the thief, and the purchaser — are potentially before us. The question is how to structure the legal claims among them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continues: **And both** Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan have accepted as *halakha* **the ruling of Rav Ḥisda** (111b), that if the owner had not yet despaired of retrieving his item, he can press his claim against either the thief or the purchaser.

Before examining that dispute, the *gemara* cites the incident of *Chanan Bisha*, a notorious figure who stole a cloak and sold it. Rav Huna, a student of Rav, ruled that the original owner must pay the purchaser — a classic application of *takanat hashuk* — even though the thief was identified. The *gemara* explains that this case is exceptional: *Chanan Bisha*, despite being known, was effectively untouchable, a sort of mafioso from whom the purchaser could never realistically collect. Since the practical ability to recover from the thief did not exist, even the opinion that holds there is no *takanat hashuk* when the thief is known would concede that in this case the purchaser must be protected and compensated by the owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַב לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק? וְהָא רַב הוּנָא תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲוָה, וְחָנָן בִּישָׁא גְּנַב גְּלִימָא וְזַבְּנַהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא: זִיל שְׁרִי עֲבִיטָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this answer: **And did Rav hold** that the Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case? **But wasn't Rav Huna a student of Rav, and** yet when a certain thief known as **Ḥanan the Wicked stole a cloak and** then **sold it,** and the case **came before Rav Huna, he said to that man** from whom the cloak was stolen: **Go redeem your pledge** from the purchaser, i.e., reimburse the purchaser for the garment that he returned to you. Evidently, Rav's own student held that the Sages did allow a purchaser to claim reimbursement from the owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי חָנָן בִּישָׁא, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא לְאִישְׁתַּלּוֹמֵי מִינֵּיהּ – כְּלֹא הוּכַּר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Ḥanan the Wicked is different. Since** he has no money, **there is no** way for the purchaser **to collect payment from him,** and the case is therefore **comparable** to a case where the thief **was not identified,** with regard to which case the Sages implemented the provision ensuring the integrity of the marketplace.

### Rav vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר הַדִּין עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן – דִּינָא דְּלוֹקֵחַ דְּלִישְׁקוֹל זוּזֵי מִגַּנָּב, וְלֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר הַדִּין עִם הַשֵּׁנִי – דִּינָא דְּלוֹקֵחַ דְּלִישְׁקוֹל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְעָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya** that **the claim** can be pursued only **with the first** one, i.e., the thief, meaning that **the law with regard to the purchaser** is **that,** after returning the item to its owner, **he can collect money** only **from the thief, and** the Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yannai** that **the claim** of the purchaser can be pursued **with the second one,** meaning that **the law with regard to the purchaser** is that **he can** also **collect** the **money from the owner** when he returns the item to him, **and** the Sages **did implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case.

The core dispute is stated as follows. Rav, in the name of Rabbi Chiyya, says *hadin im harishon* — the owner must deal with the first party. Rabbi Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Yannai, says *hadin im hasheni* — he deals with the second. The initial assumption is that "first" and "second" refer to the thief and the purchaser respectively: Rav holds the owner must pursue the thief for compensation and cannot bring the purchaser into the dispute, while Rabbi Yochanan holds the owner may go to the purchaser and retrieve his object from him.

### Ye'ush Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא פְּלִיגִי; כָּאן לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ – הַדִּין עִם הַשֵּׁנִי, כָּאן לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ – הַדִּין עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **do not disagree. Here,** in the case about which Rabbi Yoḥanan issues his ruling, the item was purchased from the thief **before** the victim of the theft **despaired** of recovering the stolen item. Consequently, **the claim** can be pursued even **with the second** one, i.e., the purchaser. Conversely, **there,** in the case about which Rav issues his ruling, the item was purchased **after** the victim **despaired,** and therefore **the claim** can be pursued only **with the first** one, i.e., the thief.

Rav Yosef attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction by arguing that the two positions actually do not conflict: *kan lifnei ye'ush hadin im hasheni, kan l'achar ye'ush hadin im harishon*. Before *ye'ush*, the object legally remains the owner's and he may retrieve it from the purchaser — that is Rabbi Yochanan's case. After *ye'ush*, the purchaser has acquired the object and the owner's recourse is only against the thief — that is Rav's case. This appears tidy and uncontroversial.

### Rav Chisda

The *gemara* adds that both positions depend on the principle of Rav Chisda from earlier in the chapter: if a thief steals an item and a third party comes and consumes it, the original owner may collect from *either* the thief *or* the consumer — *ratza mizeh goveh, ratza mizeh goveh*. This principle establishes that even after the stolen object has passed out of the owner's control, his legal claims against subsequent parties remain intact before *ye'ush*. In our case, this principle is what enables the owner to retrieve his object from the purchaser before *ye'ush*: without Rav Chisda's ruling, one might have argued that once the object was stolen and sold, the owner's rights were too attenuated to compel the purchaser — who, after all, had done nothing wrong — to give it up. Rav Chisda establishes that they are not.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְלָא פְּלִיגִי? הָא מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה כְּלִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי! דִּתְנַן, אָמַר לוֹ: ״מְכוֹר לִי מֵעֶיהָ שֶׁל פָּרָה״, וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן מַתָּנוֹת – נוֹתְנָן לְכֹהֵן, וְאֵינוֹ מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים. לָקַח הֵימֶנּוּ בְּמִשְׁקָל – נוֹתְנוֹ לְכֹהֵן, וּמְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **And** is it so that Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **do not disagree? Isn't** a case involving **gifts** to which members **of the priesthood** are entitled **comparable** to a case of a stolen item that was sold **before** the onset of the owner's **despair,** as the priest still hopes to receive the gifts, **and** yet Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree** with regard to their legal status? This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 132a): If **one said to** a butcher: **Sell me the innards of a cow, and the gifts of the priesthood were in them,** the purchaser **must give** the gifts **to a priest, and** he **may not deduct** the value of the gifts **from the money** he agreed to pay the butcher. By contrast, if **he purchased** the innards **from** the butcher **by weight, he must give** the gifts **to a priest and** he **may deduct** the value of the gifts **from the money** he agreed to pay the butcher.

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef's reconciliation. He contends that the dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan is genuine and that there exists a case analogous to "before *ye'ush*" in which they nonetheless disagree: the case of *matnot kehunah*.

### Matnot Kehunah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁשָּׁקַל לְעַצְמוֹ, אֲבָל שָׁקַל לוֹ הַטַּבָּח – הַדִּין עִם הַטַּבָּח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only where** the purchaser **weighed** the innards **for himself, but** if **the butcher weighed** the innards **for him, the** priest's **claim** may be pursued only **with the butcher.** The butcher is comparable to a thief when he sells the gifts of the priesthood to a non-priest, and Rav holds that the priest can press his claim only against the butcher. Similarly, in the case of the mishna he would hold that the owner can press his claim only against the thief and not against the purchaser.

The *mishna* governing *matnot kehunah* presents the following scenario: a purchaser tells a butcher, sell me the innards of this cow. The innards include the *keivah* — the stomach — which constitutes one of the priestly gifts that must be given to a *kohen*. The parallel to our case is precise: the butcher is the thief, effectively stealing the *kohen*'s portion; the purchaser is the one who ends up with the stolen item in his possession; and since *matnot kehunah* are owed by religious obligation and there is no specific *kohen* who has yet acquired a personal claim to them, the *gemara* treats this as analogous to a pre-*ye'ush* situation.

If the purchaser bought the innards without explicit weighing, the assumption is that he did not knowingly pay for the *keivah* specifically — he never intended to purchase something he knew was not the butcher's to sell. He therefore gives the *keivah* to the *kohen* without seeking reimbursement. If, however, the purchaser weighed the innards and paid by weight, he has unwittingly paid for the *keivah* as well. In that case he gives the stomach to the *kohen* and recovers its value from the butcher.

### Ye'ush Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים בְּיַד לוֹקֵחַ, וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ בְּיַד גַּנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another explanation of the dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Rav Zevid said** that both agree that if the owner has not yet despaired of retrieving his item, he can press his claim against either party. They disagree in a case **where the owners despaired** of retrieving it only after the item was already **in the possession of the purchaser, but they did not** yet **despair** when it was **in the possession of the thief.**

Rav Zvid interprets the dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan differently. He maintains that both agree before *ye'ush* the object is returned from the purchaser, and both agree after *ye'ush* the owner deals with the thief. The dispute, in his reading, concerns a middle case: *ye'ush* occurred after the purchaser took possession — that is, *shinuy reshut* preceded *ye'ush*. One opinion holds that *shinuy reshut she'achar ye'ush* — a change of possession followed by *ye'ush* — does confer ownership; the other holds it does not. If the purchaser acquired the object, the owner must deal with the thief; if not, the owner may retrieve the object from the purchaser.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי – מָר סָבַר: יֵאוּשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ שִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת – קָנֵי, שִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת וְאַחַר כָּךְ יֵאוּשׁ – לָא קָנֵי; וּמָר סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is with regard to this** that **they disagree:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that if there is **despair** on the part of the owner **and afterward** there is **a change in possession** of a stolen item, e.g., it leaves the possession of the thief and enters the possession of a purchaser, the purchaser **acquires** the item. By contrast, if there is first **a change in possession** of a stolen item **and afterward** there is **despair** on the part of the owner, then the purchaser does **not acquire** the item. Since in this case the owner did not despair until after the change of possession from the thief to the purchaser, the purchaser did not legally acquire the item, and the owner can press his claim against him. **And** one **Sage,** Rav, **holds** that there **is no difference** whether the despair or change of possession came first. As long as the owner has despaired of recovering his item, it becomes the property of the purchaser, and the owner cannot demand that he return it.

### Thief Known

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בִּגְלִימָא – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּהָדַר לְמָרֵיהּ, וְהָכָא בְּעָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a third explanation of the dispute: **Rav Pappa said** that **with regard to** a stolen **cloak** itself, **everyone agrees that it** must be **returned** by the purchaser **to its owner. But here, it is with regard to** whether the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case that Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree.**

Rav Pappa offers yet another reading. He argues that when it comes to the actual physical garment, all authorities agree it is returned to the original owner — *biglima dechulei alma lo pligi dehadar lemarei*. The dispute in the *baraita* is not about *ye'ush* at all, but rather about whether *takanat hashuk* was enacted in a case where the thief has been identified. Rav holds: *dina deloke'ach delishkol zuzei migannav velo asu batakkanat hashuk* — the purchaser's remedy is to collect from the thief, and no *takanat hashuk* was enacted obligating the owner to pay the purchaser when the thief is known and available. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *asu bah takkanat hashuk* — the enactment protects the purchaser even here, because he did nothing wrong by buying in the marketplace, and it would be unfair to force him to run after the thief.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר הַדִּין עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן – דִּינָא דְּלוֹקֵחַ דְּלִישְׁקוֹל זוּזֵי מִגַּנָּב, וְלֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר הַדִּין עִם הַשֵּׁנִי – דִּינָא דְּלוֹקֵחַ דְּלִישְׁקוֹל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְעָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya** that **the claim** can be pursued only **with the first** one, i.e., the thief, meaning that **the law with regard to the purchaser** is **that,** after returning the item to its owner, **he can collect money** only **from the thief, and** the Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yannai** that **the claim** of the purchaser can be pursued **with the second one,** meaning that **the law with regard to the purchaser** is that **he can** also **collect** the **money from the owner** when he returns the item to him, **and** the Sages **did implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case.

### Well-Known Thief

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם גַּנָּב מְפוּרְסָם הוּא, לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק. וְהָא חָנָן בִּישָׁא, דִּמְפוּרְסָם הֲוָה, וְעָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק! נְהִי דִּמְפוּרְסַם לְבִישׁוּתָא, לִגְנִיבוּתָא לָא מְפוּרְסַם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: If he is a well-known thief,** the Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case. The purchaser should have been aware that the item may be stolen and should not have purchased it. The Gemara asks: **But wasn't Ḥanan the Wicked well known, and** yet, according to Rav Huna, the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case? The Gemara answers: **Although** he was **well known for villainy,** he was **not well known for theft.**

Rava articulates a principle that moves beyond the *hukar haganav* debate entirely: *im ganav mefursam hu, lo asu bo takkanat hashuk* — if the thief was notorious and well-known as a thief, the Sages did not enact *takanat hashuk* to protect the purchaser. The purpose of the enactment was to enable and protect ordinary commerce, not to subsidize transactions with known criminals. If a buyer purchases from a *ganav mefursam*, he should have known better, and the Sages will not shield him from the consequences.

The *gemara* immediately notes the apparent tension with the *Chanan Bisha* case, in which *takanat hashuk* was applied even though he was notoriously dangerous. Rava distinguishes: *Chanan Bisha* was renowned for his violent character, but was not specifically known as a thief — *lignivusei lo mefursam*. There was no particular reason for the purchaser to suspect he was buying stolen goods. The principle of *ganav mefursam* therefore requires that the person's reputation as a thief specifically — not merely as a bad actor — be publicly established.

## Special Circumstance (II)

### Debt Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּחוֹבוֹ, גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּהֶיקֵּיפוֹ – לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק, דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָנְהוּ יְהַבְתְּ לֵיהּ מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to examine the provision ensuring the integrity of the marketplace. **It was stated:** With regard to one who **stole and repaid his debt** with the stolen item, or one who **stole and repaid his** debt from **credit** that was extended to him with the stolen item, the Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case, **as they say** to the creditor: **It was not with the expectation of** receiving **these** stolen items **that you gave** the debtor **anything.**

The *gemara* now examines *takanat hashuk* in a series of special transactional configurations. The first: a thief used the stolen object to pay off a pre-existing debt he owed the current possessor. *Ganav ufara bechovo, ganav ufara beheikeifo* — whether he paid an outstanding debt or a running tab, *lo asu bo takkanat hashuk*. The rationale is *lav ada'ata d'hanach yahavit lei midei* — the possessor did not shell out money for the sake of this object. The money he lent was lent earlier, unconnected to this particular item. When the owner retrieves the object, the possessor is simply returned to his prior position — the thief still owes him the original sum — and no special protection is warranted.

### Collateral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא; שָׁוֵי מָאתַן בִּמְאָה – עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק. שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה – אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק, מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the integrity of the marketplace, it was also stated that if a thief provided a stolen item **worth two hundred** dinars as **collateral for** a loan of **one hundred** dinars, the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case. Consequently, the creditor is entitled to reimbursement from the owner for returning the collateral. If the collateral and the loan were **of equal value, Ameimar said:** The Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case, while **Mar Zutra said:** The Sages **did implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case.

The next case involves collateral: the thief gave the stolen object to a lender as security for a loan. Where the collateral is worth 200 and the loan is 100 — *shavi masan be'me'ah* — the lender clearly extended the loan *because of* the collateral, and *takanat hashuk* applies: the lender laid out money on the faith of this object, and the owner must compensate him upon retrieval. Where the collateral and loan are equal in value — *shaveh be'shaveh* — Amemar holds *lo asu bah takkanat hashuk*: since you would not normally lend money against collateral of only equal value, the inference is that the loan rested primarily on personal trust in the borrower rather than on the object itself. Mar Zutra holds that even in this case *takanat hashuk* applies, since money was after all extended on account of the object. The final ruling — *ve'hilchesa bekhlei asu bah takkanat hashuk* — sides with Mar Zutra.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And the *halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Mar Zutra that the Sages **did implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case.

### Pricing Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבִינָא; שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה – עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק. שָׁוֵי מְאָה בְּמָאתַן – רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: לֹא עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק, רָבָא אָמַר: עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines other applications of this provision: In **a sale,** if the stolen item was purchased **for an amount equal** to its actual market value, the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case. If a stolen item **worth one hundred** dinars was purchased **at** the price of **two hundred** dinars, **Rav Sheshet says:** The Sages **did not implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case, while **Rava says:** The Sages **did implement the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case.

Ordinary purchase at fair market value — *zabna shavei be'shavei* — is the paradigm case for *takanat hashuk*, and it applies without question. Two divergent scenarios are then examined. If the purchaser paid substantially *less* than the object's fair value — *shavi me'ah be'ma'san*, worth 200 but bought for 100 — *takanat hashuk* still applies: he shelled out money for the item and is entitled to protection. If, on the other hand, the purchaser grossly *overpaid* — *shavi me'ah be'masayim*, worth 100 but bought for 200 — Rav Sheshet holds *lo asu bah takkanat hashuk*: as Rashi explains, when one so dramatically overpays, it is evident that the excess was essentially a gift to the seller, not a genuine commercial transaction in this object. Rava disagrees and holds *asu bah takkanat hashuk* — and Rashi notes that on Rava's view the owner must reimburse the full 200. The final ruling — *ve'hilchesa bekhlei asu bah takkanat hashuk* — adopts Rava's position, extending protection across all the debated scenarios except the case of payment of a pre-existing debt, where the *gemara* never entertained any doubt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: בְּכוּלְּהוּ עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק, לְבַר מִגָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּחוֹבוֹ, גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּהֶיקֵּיפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **And the *halakha*** is that **in all these** cases the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace, except** with regard to one who **stole and repaid his debt** with the stolen item and one who **stole and repaid his** debt from **credit** that was extended to him with the stolen item.

### Avimi bar Nazi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבִימִי בַּר נָאזִי חֲמוּהּ דְּרָבִינָא הֲוָה מַסֵּיק בְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי. גְּנַב גְּלִימָא אֵתְיַאּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, אוֹזְפֵיהּ אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי אַחֲרִינֵי. לְסוֹף הוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts a related incident: **Avimi bar Nazi, the father-in-law of Ravina, was owed four dinars by a certain man,** who then **stole a cloak and brought it to** Avimi as repayment for the loan. Avimi then **lent him four additional dinars. In the end, the thief was identified** as the debtor, and the case **came before Ravina** to determine whether or not the owner would have to pay Avimi for returning the cloak.

A real case is then recorded. Avimi bar Nazi, the father-in-law of Ravina, had lent a certain man four *zuz*. That man stole a cloak and gave it to Avimi in payment of the debt. With the debt cleared, Avimi then lent the man another four *zuz*. The thief was subsequently identified. The question: does *takanat hashuk* protect Avimi with respect to either or both loans?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: קַמָּאֵי – גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּחוֹבוֹ, וְלָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי; הָנָךְ אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי אַחֲרִינֵי – שְׁקוֹל זוּזָךְ, וְ[אַ]הְדַּר גְּלִימֵי[הּ].
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said** to Avimi: With regard to the **first** four dinars that were lent, the thief is viewed as one who **stole and repaid his debt, and** the owner is **not required to give anything** in exchange for the garment's return as the Sages' ordinance ensuring the integrity of the marketplace does not apply in such a case. With regard to **those four additional dinars, take your money** from the owner of the cloak **and return the cloak** to him. Since Avimi lent the second set of dinars only because he had received the cloak, Ravina ruled that the Sages' ordinance applied to him.

Ravina ruled that the first four *zuz* constitute a clear case of *ganav ufara bechovo* — the cloak paid a pre-existing debt, and no *takanat hashuk* applies to that amount. Regarding the second four *zuz*, however, Ravina argued that Avimi extended this loan precisely because his trust in the borrower had been established by the repayment of the first debt — and that repayment was effectuated through the stolen cloak. The second loan was therefore causally connected to the cloak, and *takanat hashuk* should apply.

### Rabbi Abbahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב כֹּהֵן: וְדִלְמָא גְּלִימָא – בְּהָנֵי זוּזֵי קַמָּאֵי יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, ״גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּחוֹבוֹ, גָּנַב וּפָרַע בְּהֶיקֵּיפוֹ״; וְאַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי בָּתְרָאֵי – הֵימוֹנֵי הֵימְנֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּהֵימְנֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, מְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר: הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kohen objects to this: But perhaps the cloak was given** to Avimi only in payment **for these first** four **dinars,** and it was a case of a thief who **stole and repaid his debt** or who **stole and repaid his** debt from **credit** that was extended to him with the stolen item. **And the latter four dinars** were loaned because Avimi **trusted the debtor, just as he trusted him initially,** when he lent the first four dinars. The cloak was not collateral for the second loan, and therefore the Sages' ordinance should not apply. **The matter circulated** until **it came before Rabbi Abbahu,** who **said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Kohen.**

Rav Kahana challenged Ravina's reasoning on the second loan: perhaps Avimi lent the second four *zuz* simply out of the same trust that led him to extend the first loan — *ki heichi d'heimanye mei'ikara* — entirely independent of having received the cloak. The matter was deliberated and brought before Rabbi Abbahu, who ruled *hilchasa k'Rav Kahana*: the second loan is treated as independent, and the entire transaction is governed by the rule of *ganav ufara bechovo uve'hekefo*. Avimi must return the cloak and receives no reimbursement from the original owner.

## Special Circumstance (II)

### Narsha Sefer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַרְשָׁאָה גְּנַב סִפְרָא, זַבְּנֵיהּ לְפַפּוּנָאָה בִּתְמָנַן זוּזֵי. אֲזַל פַּפּוּנָאָה, זַבְּנֵיהּ לְבַר מָחוֹזָאָה בִּמְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִין זוּזֵי. לְסוֹף הוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֵיזִיל מָרֵי דְסִפְרָא וְיָהֵב לֵיהּ לְבַר מָחוֹזָא תְּמָנַן זוּזֵי וְשָׁקֵיל סִפְרֵיהּ, וְאָזֵיל בַּר מָחוֹזָאָה וְשָׁקֵיל אַרְבְּעִין מִפַּפּוּנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts another incident: **A resident of Naresh stole a scroll** and **sold it to a resident of Pappunya for eighty dinars. The resident of Pappunya** then **went and sold it to a resident of Meḥoza for one hundred and twenty dinars. In the end, the thief was identified** and **Abaye said: The** original **owner of the scroll should go and give the resident of Meḥoza eighty dinars and take his scroll** in return. **And** afterward, **the resident of Meḥoza should go and take** the remaining **forty** dinars **from the resident of Pappunya.**

A final case involves multiple purchasers in sequence. A person from Narsha stole a *sefer* and sold it to a man from Papuna for eighty *zuz*. The Papuna man sold it to a man from the city of Mechuza for a hundred and twenty. The thief was then identified. Now there are two purchasers: the man from Papuna, who paid eighty, and the man from Mechuza, who paid a hundred and twenty — the current holder of the book. How should the owner's payment obligations and rights of recovery be structured?

### Abaye Solution

Abaye ruled as follows: the owner goes and pays the man from Mechuza eighty *zuz* — the amount the book originally fetched from the thief — and retrieves his book. The man from Mechuza then recovers the remaining forty *zuz* of what he paid from the Papuna man, who had profited by forty on the transaction. The reasoning is that the original price of eighty is the measure of what was genuinely "shelled out" for the item at the point it left the thief's hands, and that anchors the owner's obligation.

### Rava Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא לוֹקֵחַ מִגַּנָּב – עָשׂוּ בּוֹ תַּקָּנַת הַשּׁוּק, לוֹקֵחַ מִלּוֹקֵחַ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this: Now** that it has been established that with regard to one who **purchases from a thief,** the Sages **implemented the provision** ensuring **the integrity of the marketplace in this** case despite the fact that he dealt with the thief directly, **is** it **necessary** to teach that the same would apply to one who **purchased** a stolen item **from a purchaser?** Accordingly, the final purchaser is entitled to receive from the original owner the full amount that he paid for the item.

Rava challenged this analysis on policy grounds: *hashta loke'ach mi'ganav asu bo takkanat hashuk, loke'ach miloke'ach miba'ya* — if we protect a direct purchaser from a thief, certainly we should protect a purchaser who bought from another purchaser, since he had even less reason to suspect anything was amiss. It would be unfair to force the man from Mechuza to chase after two parties; he should be made whole in a single transaction with the owner. Rava therefore ruled: the owner pays the man from Mechuza the full hundred and twenty *zuz* and takes back his book. The owner then recovers forty from the Papuna man — who unjustly profited from stolen goods — and eighty from the Narsha thief. This is the ruling that is adopted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לֵיזִיל מָרֵיהּ דְּסִפְרָא וְיָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְבַר מָחוֹזָאָה מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִין זוּזֵי וְשָׁקֵיל סִפְרֵיהּ, וְלֵיזִיל מָרֵי[הּ] דְסִפְרָא וְלִישְׁקוֹל אַרְבְּעִין מִפַּפּוּנָאָה, וּתְמָנַן מִנַּרְשָׁאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that **the** original **owner of the scroll should go and give the resident of Meḥoza one hundred and twenty dinars and take his scroll** in return. **And** afterward **the** original **owner of the scroll should go and collect forty** dinars **from the resident of Pappunya and eighty** dinars **from the resident of Naresh,** as each profited from the sale in these amounts, respectively.

## Unilateral Lifesaving

### Wine Saves Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זֶה בָּא בְּחָבִיתוֹ שֶׁל יַיִן וְזֶה בָּא בְּכַדּוֹ שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ, נִסְדְּקָה חָבִית שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ, וְשָׁפַךְ זֶה אֶת יֵינוֹ וְהִצִּיל אֶת הַדְּבַשׁ לְתוֹכוֹ –
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a situation where **this** individual **came with his barrel of wine, and that** individual **came with his jug of honey,** if **the barrel of honey cracked and this** first individual **poured** out **his wine and salvaged the** other's **honey,** which is worth more than the wine, by collecting it **into** his wine barrel,

The *mishna* presents a new scenario: two men are walking, one carrying a barrel of wine and the other a barrel of honey. The honey barrel cracks and the honey is about to spill and be lost. The man with the wine pours out his wine and saves the honey by transferring it into his now-empty barrel. *Ein lo ela secharo* — he is entitled only to payment for his service, the market rate for someone hired to perform such a rescue. He may not demand reimbursement for the value of the wine he spilled. No one hired him; no one asked him to sacrifice his wine. Gratitude does not create a legal obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ. וְאִם אָמַר: ״אַצִּיל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ, וְאַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי דְּמֵי שֶׁלִּי״ – חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** the owner of the wine **has** the right to collect **only his wage,** i.e., compensation for the effort he put into salvaging the honey. He is not, however, entitled to compensation for the wine itself. **But if** the owner of the wine **said: I will salvage your** honey **and you will pay me the value of my** wine, the owner of the honey is **obligated to pay him** compensation for the wine.

### Stipulated Agreement

If, however, prior to acting, the rescuer said to the honey owner: "I will save your honey if you agree to pay me for the value of my wine," and the owner agreed, then *chayiv litein lo* — the owner is obligated to pay. The explicit stipulation creates a contractual obligation.

### Donkey Wadi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁטַף נַחַל חֲמוֹרוֹ וַחֲמוֹר חֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁלּוֹ יָפֶה מָנֶה וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ מָאתַיִם, וְהִנִּיחַ זֶה אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ וְהִצִּיל אֶת שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״אֲנִי אַצִּיל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ, וְאַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי אֶת שֶׁלִּי״ – חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if **a river washed away his donkey and the donkey of another,** and **his** donkey was **worth one hundred dinars and** the donkey **of the other** was **worth two hundred, and** the individual with the less valuable donkey **abandoned his** donkey **and** instead **salvaged** the donkey **of the other, he has** the right to collect **only his wage,** i.e., compensation for the effort he put into salvaging his fellow's donkey. **But if he said to** the owner of the more valuable donkey: **I will salvage your** donkey **and you will pay me the** monetary value **of mine** in exchange, the owner of the more valuable donkey is **obligated to pay** the rescuer compensation for his donkey.

The *mishna* presents a parallel case: a flash flood sweeps through a wadi, carrying away both a man's donkey — worth a hundred — and his neighbor's donkey — worth two hundred. The man can save only one. He abandons his own donkey and saves his neighbor's more valuable animal. Again, *ein lo ela secharo* — he collects only the service fee for saving the donkey, not compensation for the donkey he sacrificed. And again, if he stipulated in advance and the neighbor agreed to compensate him for his lost donkey, *chayiv litein lo* — the neighbor must pay.

### Hefker Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: מֵהֶפְקֵירָא קָא זָכֵינָא! מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה טָעוּן כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן, וְרָאָה שֶׁהֵן מִשְׁתַּבְּרוֹת, לֹא יֹאמַר: ״הֲרֵי זֶה תְּרוּמָה וּמַעֲשֵׂר עַל פֵּירוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי״, וְאִם אָמַר – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And why** does one who pours out wine have the right to collect only this wage? **Let him say to** the owner of the honey: **I have acquired** your honey **from ownerless** property. **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who was laden** with **jugs of wine and jugs of oil and saw that they were breaking** and their contents were leaking out should **not say: This is hereby** separated as ***teruma* and tithe for** the **produce that I have in my house. And** even **if he said** this, it is as though **he did not say anything.** The fact that one cannot separate produce that is about to be lost as *teruma* or tithe indicates that such property is considered ownerless.

The *gemara* raises a striking challenge to the *mishna*'s ruling: why is the rescuer of the honey entitled to only a service fee? Granted that the honey owner did not ask him to act, but consider the state of the honey at the moment of rescue — it was entirely about to spill and be destroyed, *holech le'ibbud*. Something in the process of complete destruction and loss should be considered ownerless, *hefker*. If the honey was effectively *hefker* at the moment the rescuer acted, then he should be regarded as having taken possession of ownerless property — in which case he owns all of it, not merely a service fee!

### Terumah Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּשֶׁעֵקֶל בֵּית הַבַּד כָּרוּךְ עָלֶיהָ; הָכִי נָמֵי, כְּשֶׁעֵקֶל בֵּית הַבַּד כָּרוּךְ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it is **as Rabbi Yirmeya says** in a different context, that the ruling discussed there is referring to a case **where the basket of the olive press** was **wrapped around it** so that the barrel would not break completely, and some of the contents would remain inside. **So too,** here, the mishna issued its ruling only **when the basket of the olive press** was **wrapped around** the honey barrel so that it would not break completely, and the contents are therefore not rendered ownerless.

The *gemara* supports the *hefker* premise by citing a *baraita*: if someone is carrying jugs of wine and oil and sees them breaking, he may not declare that the spilling wine or oil should serve as *terumat ma'aser* on equivalent produce stored in his home. The purpose of such a declaration would be transparent: the wine is doomed, so let it count as the *kohen*'s share — the owner saves himself from having to separate from his good produce, and it costs him nothing since the spilling wine was going to waste anyway. Not only is this not permitted *lechatchila*, but if he made such a declaration, *lo amar klum* — it is legally void. And the reason it is void is precisely because at that moment of complete loss, the produce is considered ownerless. One cannot consecrate or separate *terumah* from *hefker* property.

### Robbery Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם? וְהָתַנְיָא: מִי שֶׁבָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּמָעוֹת בְּיָדוֹ וְאַנָּס כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, לֹא יֹאמַר: ״הֲרֵי פֵּירוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי מְחוּלָּלִים עַל מָעוֹת הַלָּלוּ״, וְאִם אָמַר – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the *baraita* that stated: **And** even **if he said** that the wine or oil is separated as *teruma* or tithe, it is as though **he did not say anything. But isn't it taught** in a different *baraita*: If **one was traveling on the road and had money in his possession, and** he saw **a ruffian** moving **toward him,** he **should not say: The** second-tithe **produce that I have in my house is hereby desacralized onto these coins,** i.e., the sanctity of the produce is hereby transferred to the coins. **But if he said** so, **his statement stands,** and the produce is desacralized while the coins attain the sanctity of the second tithe.

The *gemara* then cites a seemingly contradictory *baraita*: if a person is traveling and a highway robber confronts him to take his money, he should not declare that the produce in his home should be redeemed through this money for purposes of *ma'aser sheni*. Yet if he does make such a declaration, *divrei kayamin* — it takes legal effect. This appears to contradict the previous case: here too the property is about to be completely and irrecoverably lost, yet the declaration is valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַצִּיל. אִי בְּשֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַצִּיל, לְכַתְּחִלָּה אַמַּאי לֹא יֹאמַר? בְּשֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where he is capable of saving** his money from the ruffian and that is why he may transfer the sanctity of the second tithe to the coins. The Gemara asks: **If** it is a case **where he is capable of saving** the money, **why should he not say** that the produce should be desacralized ***ab initio*?** The Gemara answers: It is a case **where he is capable of saving** the money only **with difficulty.** Consequently, he should not transfer the sanctity of the second tithe to the coins, as it may be lost; but if he does so, the transfer takes effect, as it is not certain that the money will be lost.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: the case of the robber is different because *b'sheyachol lehatzil* — it is possible that the person could save himself, perhaps by running away or otherwise evading the robber. Since recovery of the money is not entirely foreclosed, the property is not treated as *hefker*. The *gemara* refines this further: it is only possible to escape through considerable effort — *al yedei had'chak*. And precisely because escape is difficult but not impossible, the property retains its ownership status for purposes of the declaration, but the Sages nonetheless counseled against making it *lechatchila* — since once you have transferred the *ma'aser sheni* status onto the money, you may have even less motivation to exert yourself to recover it.

### Netting Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶפְסֵידָא, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לֹא יֹאמַר? וְהָתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ עֶשֶׂר חָבִיּוֹת שֶׁל טֶבֶל טָמֵא, וְרָאָה אַחַת מֵהֶן שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְגַּלְּתָה, אוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵי הִיא תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר עַל תֵּשַׁע חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ״. וּבְשֶׁמֶן לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי הֶפְסֵד כֹּהֵן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that **wherever there is** a potential **loss one should not say** that the sanctity is transferred to the money ***ab initio*? But isn't it taught** otherwise in the following *baraita*: If **one had ten barrels of ritually impure, untithed** wine, i.e., first-tithe wine from which the *teruma* of the tithe had not yet been separated, **and he saw that one of them had broken** and that its contents were leaking out, **or that it had been exposed** and is forbidden for consumption, **he may say: This** barrel **is hereby** separated as **the *teruma* of the tithe for the other nine** barrels. **But with regard to oil, he should not do so due to the priest's loss.**

Having established the theoretical framework, the *gemara* returns to Rabbi Yirmiyah's distinction. The resolution to why the honey in the *mishna* is not *hefker* is that the barrel was not cracking catastrophically and spilling all at once. Rather, it was encased in a netting — *kesha'aka bisa bar karcha lah* — such that the honey was dripping but not freely pouring out and being entirely destroyed. Since not everything was inevitably lost, since there remained some possibility of partial recovery or containment, the honey did not attain the status of *hefker*. The owner retained his legal ownership, and the rescuer therefore holds the honey subject to returning it upon receipt of a service fee.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּשֶׁעֵקֶל בֵּית הַבַּד כָּרוּךְ עָלֶיהָ. בִּשְׁלָמָא שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה – חַזְיָא, אֶלָּא נִתְגַּלְּתָה – לְמַאי חַזְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The *baraita* issued its ruling only **when** the **basket of the olive press** was **wrapped around** the barrel so that some of its contents would remain inside. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** this rationale applies to a barrel **that was broken,** as the remaining contents are still **fit** for use, **but** with regard to a barrel that was **exposed, for what** use **is it fit?**

### Tafel Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא חַזְיָא לְזִילּוּף, וְהָתַנְיָא: מַיִם שֶׁנִּתְגַּלּוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשְׁפְּכֵם בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; וְלֹא יְגַבֵּל בָּהֶן אֶת הַטִּיט; וְלֹא יְרַבֵּץ בָּהֶן אֶת הַבַּיִת; וְלֹא יַשְׁקֶה מֵהֶם אֶת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, וְלֹא בֶּהֱמַת חֲבֵירוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the exposed wine is **fit for sprinkling,** in order to spread its pleasant aroma, **isn't it taught** otherwise in the following *baraita*? With regard to **water that was exposed, one may not pour it** out **in the public domain, nor mix clay with it, nor settle** dust **with it** by sprinkling it in **a house, nor give it to his animal to drink, nor** give it **to the animal of another.** This proves that exposed liquids may not be used even for sprinkling.

The *gemara* then cites another *baraita* to test the boundaries of the *hefker* principle. If a person has ten barrels of *tevel tamei* — produce that is ritually impure and from which *terumah* has not yet been separated — and one of the barrels cracks or becomes exposed, he may declare that broken barrel to be *terumat ma'aser* on the other nine. This seems to permit exactly what was prohibited in the earlier case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּעַבַּר לָהּ בִּמְסַנֶּנֶת, כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – דְּתַנְיָא: מְסַנֶּנֶת, יֵשׁ בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי. אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַתַּחְתּוֹנָה מְגוּלָּה, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַתַּחְתּוֹנָה מְכוּסָּה – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהָעֶלְיוֹנָה מְגוּלָּה, אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי; לְפִי שֶׁאֶרֶס שֶׁל נָחָשׁ דּוֹמֶה לִסְפוֹג, וְצָף וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The wine is fit for use **because he passed it through a strainer,** and it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A vessel covered with **a strainer is subject to** the *halakha* of **exposure** if the vessel is left unsupervised. **Rabbi Neḥemya said: When** is this so? It is so **when the lower vessel,** in which the liquid collects after passing through the strainer, **is exposed. But when the lower vessel is covered, even if the upper vessel is exposed, it is not subject to** the *halakha* of **exposure, because the venom of a snake is like a sponge** in that **it floats and stays in place.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּבְשֶׁמֶן לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי הֶפְסֵד כֹּהֵן. מַאי שְׁנָא שֶׁמֶן – דְּרָאוּי לְהַדְלִיק? יַיִן נָמֵי רָאוּי לְזִילּוּף! וְכִי תֵּימָא זִילּוּף לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שׁוֹתִין מִלּוֹג בְּסֶלַע, וּמְזַלְּפִין מִלּוֹג בִּשְׁתַּיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the continuation of the *baraita* cited previously: **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **But with regard to oil, he should not do so, due to the priest's loss.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about a leaking barrel of impure **oil,** which one may not separate as *teruma*, because if he does so the priest will suffer a loss? That case is different **because** the impure *teruma* oil **is fit** to be used **to kindle** a fire, and the priest will now not receive that oil. The Gemara challenges: But **wine is also fit** to be used, as the priest is able **to sprinkle** it in order to impart a pleasant aroma. Why is there is no concern that the priest will suffer a loss? **And if you would say** that **sprinkling is nothing,** i.e., it is insignificant, **doesn't Shmuel say in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya: One drinks from a *log*** of wine worth one ***sela*, and one sprinkles from a *log*** of wine worth **two** *sela*, which indicates that there is greater benefit in sprinkling wine than in drinking it?

The *gemara* distinguishes on the basis of the *kohen*'s loss. Since the wine is *tamei*, a *kohen* cannot consume it; the only use would be minimal, such as burning it for fragrance. Because the *kohen*'s practical loss from this *terumat ma'aser* declaration is negligible, the Sages permitted it *lechatchila* — or at minimum did not forbid it so severely. However, *uveshemen lo ya'aseh kein mipnei hefsed kohen* — in the case of *tamei* oil, the same declaration should not be made *lechatchila*, because a *kohen* can use *tamei* oil for lighting, and his loss from this declaration would be real and substantial. Yet in both cases, the declaration takes effect *bedi'avad*. This shows that the concern is not that the property is *hefker* — because if it were ownerless, the declaration would be entirely void — but rather a *lechatchila* policy consideration about fairness to the *kohen*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּחָדָשׁ. וְהָא רָאוּי לְיַשְּׁנוֹ! אָתֵי בֵּיהּ לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with new** wine that has not yet developed an aroma and is therefore not fit for sprinkling. The Gemara counters: **But isn't it fit to** allow it to **age** so that it will become fit for sprinkling? The Gemara responds: If one waits for it to age, he might **come to** experience **a mishap with it,** as he will forget that it is impure *teruma*, which is forbidden for consumption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמֶן נָמֵי אָתֵי בֵּיהּ לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה! דְּמַנַּח [לֵיהּ] בִּכְלִי מָאוּס. יַיִן נָמֵי מַנַּח לֵיהּ בִּכְלִי מָאוּס! הַשְׁתָּא לְזִילּוּף קָא בָּעֵי לֵיהּ, בִּכְלִי מָאוּס קָא מַנַּח לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Oil** should **also** be subject to the concern that one might **come to experience a mishap with it,** as he might forget that it is forbidden and mistakenly drink it. The Gemara explains: The halakha is **that** the priest must **place** the oil **in a foul vessel,** and there is no concern that he will consume it, as such oil is used only for kindling. The Gemara asks: **Let the wine also be placed in a foul vessel,** in which case there would be no concern that he would drink it. The Gemara responds: **Now that he desires** to use **it for sprinkling, would he place it in a foul vessel?** This would ruin its aroma. Consequently, the wine may not be kept due to concern over a possible mishap, while the oil may be kept.

Rabbi Yirmiyah resolves this as well: in this case too, the barrel is wrapped in the pressing-house netting — *keshehakeivah shel beit habad keruchah aleha* — and the contents are therefore not catastrophically and irreversibly spilling out all at once. The property is not *hefker*, the declaration takes effect, and the only question is one of *lechatchila* policy regarding the *kohen*'s loss. We will continue from here tomorrow.
