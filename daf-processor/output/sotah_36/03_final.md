# Sotah 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stones & Torah Writing

### Pesukim Review

We begin at the bottom of 35b, in the midst of a debate concerning the details surrounding the erection of the stones and the writing of the Torah. Before proceeding, a correction is in order regarding what was said in the previous session. A more careful reading of the relevant *pesukim* clarifies considerably what the *gemara* is about to do.

### Three Stone Sets

The *gemara* identifies three distinct sets of stones. The first are those that Moshe erected on his side of the *Yarden* — a fact not stated explicitly in the *pesukim* but derived by the *gemara* through a *gezeira shava* on the word *be'er*, "well." The second are the stones that were set in the *Yarden* itself: *vayusham sham ad hayom hazeh* — they were erected there and remain there to this day, hidden beneath the water, their erection symbolic, their presence known but unseen. These are distinct from the third set, the twelve stones taken *out* of the *Yarden* and subsequently erected in *Gilgal*.

### Deuteronomy 27 Analysis

It is important to examine the *pesukim* in Devarim 27 carefully, because they generate a significant textual ambiguity. The Torah commands: *ve-hayah bayom asher tavru et ha-Yarden... ve-hakemota lecha avanim gedolot ve-sata otam ba-sid* — on the day you cross the *Yarden*, you shall erect large stones and plaster them with lime. It then commands: *ve-katavta aleihem et kol divrei ha-Torah ha-zot* — and you shall write upon them the entire Torah. So far, it appears the Torah is to be written on the erected stones. The text then continues: *ve-hayah be-ovrechem et ha-Yarden takimu et ha-avanim ha-eleh... be-Har Eival ve-sata otam ba-sid* — you shall erect these stones on Har Eival and plaster them. Then follows the command to build a *mizbeiach* of whole, unhewn stones, offer *olot* and *shelamim*, eat, and rejoice.

The ambiguity is this: the Torah speaks of writing on the stones and then speaks of building a *mizbeiach* from *avanim shelemot*, and then concludes with the instruction to write *et divrei ha-Torah ha-zot ba'er hetev* — the words of this Torah, explained well. Are we returning to the erected stones, or is this writing upon the *avnei ha-mizbeiach*? The *pesukim* are genuinely ambiguous.

### Tosafot: Malon vs. Mizbe…

*Tosafot* on 35b, *keitzad katvu Yisrael et ha-Torah*, addresses this directly. A *Tosefta* records that Rabbi Yehudah holds *al gabi ha-mizbeiach katvuha* — it was written on the *mizbeiach*. But the *Yerushalmi* preserves a different tradition: *al avnei ha-malon nichtevu* — it was written on the stones of the lodging place, i.e., the erected stones. The practical difference is significant. According to the view that it was written on the *avnei ha-malon*, the nations of the world could send their scribes day after day to transcribe the Torah, which was written in seventy languages, from this enduring public monument. According to the view that it was written on the *avnei ha-mizbeiach* — *lo le-sha'ah hayu ve-nignazu*, they were not permanent and were eventually hidden away. The *gemara* there resolves that even so, God placed understanding in the heart of every nation and they somehow acquired the Torah that was written in seventy languages.

*Tosafot* also raises the linguistic difficulty: for those who say it was written on the *avnei ha-mizbeiach*, what does *ve-sata otam ba-sid* — and you shall plaster them — refer to? The answer given is *bein kol even va-even* — the plaster between each stone, i.e., the mortar binding the *mizbeiach* together, not a surface coating for writing.

### Two Separate Events

There is a second layer of complexity that emerges from the narrative in *Yehoshua*, namely the apparent separation of the stone erection and the Har Gerizim/Har Eival ceremony into two distinct events. The Torah in Devarim seems to present everything — erecting the stones, writing the Torah, building the *mizbeiach*, and the *brachot* and *klalos* — as one unified event at Har Gerizim and Har Eival. But in *Yehoshua*, these come apart.

### Geographic Timeline

A look at the geography and the narrative sequence in *Yehoshua* reveals the full extent of this problem. *Gilgal*, where the Israelites camped after crossing the *Yarden*, was right at the eastern edge of *Yericho*: *vayachanu ba-Gilgal biktzeh mizrach Yericho*. Har Gerizim and Har Eival are far to the interior — the *gemara* later says more than sixty *mil* away, a journey of multiple days. And the narrative in *Yehoshua* does not conflate these events at all.

Perek 4 of *Yehoshua* records that *Yehoshua* erected the twelve stones taken from the *Yarden* in *Gilgal*, with no mention of writing Torah and no mention of building a *mizbeiach*: *ve-et shteim esrei ha-avanim ha-eleh asher lakechu min ha-Yarden hekim Yehoshua ba-Gilgal*. The purpose stated for the stones is purely commemorative — so that future generations would know that Israel crossed the *Yarden* on dry land, *lema'an da'at kol amei ha-aretz ki yad Hashem ki chazaka hi*. Then the narrative proceeds to the wars of *Yericho* and *Ha'Ai*, and the episode of *Achan*, spanning multiple *perakim* of wars and battles. Only at the end of Perek 8 does the text say: *az yivneh Yehoshua mizbe'ach l'Hashem Elokei Yisrael be-Har Eival* — and there, in Perek 8, it describes the building of the *mizbeiach*, the writing: *vayichtav sham al ha-avanim mishne Torat Moshe*, and the performance of the *brachot* and *klalos*.

This means that according to the plain reading of *Yehoshua*, the erection of the stones happened immediately after crossing the *Yarden*, and the *mizbeiach*, the writing of the Torah, and the *brachot* and *klalos* happened weeks or months later, well into the land, after several significant military campaigns. The Torah's account in Devarim, by contrast, presents it all as a single integrated sequence of events occurring upon entry. The *gemara* is faced with the task of reconciling these two very different presentations.

### Malbim's Theory

One approach to the ambiguity of the sequence of plastering and writing is that of the *Malbim*, who proposes that the writing of the Torah on the plaster occurred at multiple stages. His view is that first, while the plaster was just beginning to dry — a state when one can still inscribe into it — they wrote the Torah. This would correspond to what occurred at or near the *Yarden*. Subsequently, when they built the *mizbeiach* and the *olot* were offered, the stones were plastered again, the plaster dried somewhat, and they wrote the Torah yet again. This approach attempts to honor both the Devarim and the *Yehoshua* accounts by distributing the writing across different moments. However, this is a complex theory and we must set it aside for now to turn to what the *gemara* itself requires.

## Teshuvah & Seven Nations

### Rava on Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ עַמִּים מִשְׂרְפוֹת סִיד״ — עַל עִסְקֵי סִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Sheila said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon? As it is written: "And the peoples shall be as the burnings of plaster"** (Isaiah 33:12). This is homiletically interpreted to mean that the nations were punished **on account of matters of plaster,** i.e., they did not study the Torah that was written on plaster.

Returning now to the main *gemara*: the discussion concerns a debate between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon regarding the nations who witnessed the writing of the Torah on the stones. Six lines from the bottom of 35b, *Rava bar Shila* explains the reasoning behind Rabbi Shimon's position. The verse states: *ve-hayu amim misrofot sid* — the nations shall be as burning lime. Rabbi Shimon reads this *al iskei sid* — as referring to the matter of the lime, the plaster. Had the nations learned from what was written on the plaster, they would not have been destroyed. The implication of Rabbi Shimon's view is that the option of *teshuvah* and acceptance was genuinely open to them.

### Rabbi Yehudah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כִּי סִיד, מָה סִיד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא שְׂרֵיפָה — אַף אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם אֵין לָהֶם תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that the Torah was not written on plaster, interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: He explains that the gentiles are **like plaster; just as plaster has no remedy but burning,** i.e., it is created by burning stone, **so too, the nations of the world have no remedy other than burning** in Gehenna.

Rabbi Yehudah, by contrast, reads *kesid* — just as lime has no remedy other than burning, so too these nations have no remedy other than destruction. For Rabbi Yehudah, their fate is unconditional; *teshuvah* would not have altered it.

### Yefat Toar & Canaanites

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּתַנְיָא ״וְשָׁבִיתָ שִׁבְיוֹ״ — לְרַבּוֹת כְּנַעֲנִים שֶׁבְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, שֶׁאִם חוֹזְרִין בִּתְשׁוּבָה מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן?
>
> **Translation:** **In accordance with whose** opinion is **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When you go forth to battle against your enemies, and the Lord your God delivers them into your hands, **and you take them captive"** (Deuteronomy 21:10), implying that there is no obligation to destroy them, **to include** not only gentiles who are not Canaanites, but even **Canaanites that are** living **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **as, if they repent, they are accepted** and allowed to live in Eretz Yisrael.

A *baraita* is cited that connects to this debate: *ve-shavita shivyo* — the laws of *yefat toar* — is read as including *Kena'anim* who were outside the land of Israel, meaning that if they were to do *teshuvah* they would be accepted: *im chozrin be-teshuvah mekabelin otam*. The *gemara* concludes that this *baraita* follows the position of Rabbi Shimon, since it is Rabbi Shimon who holds that *teshuvah* would have been effective for the seven nations. Rabbi Yehudah, by contrast, maintains that the obligation to destroy the *shiva umot* applies regardless, even outside of Israel, even if they do *teshuvah*.

### Tosafot on Rachav

*Tosafot* raises a sharp question: *teima heich kibelu la l'Rachav* — how was *Rachav* accepted, given that she was from *Kena'an* within the land, as is established in the *Sifri*? This is a serious difficulty for any reading that would accept *Kena'anim* under certain conditions.

### Yefat Toar Override?

*Tosafot* addresses both this question and a related difficulty. Regarding *Rachav*, *Tosafot* explains: *im hayu chozrin az be-teshuvah be-sha'ah she-kasav et ha-Torah, afilu Kena'anim she-be-tocha hayu mekabelin* — had the *Kena'anim* done *teshuvah* at the time the Torah was written on the stones, even those within the land would have been accepted, and this holds even according to Rabbi Yehudah. The critical distinction is the timing. The debate about whether *teshuvah* is effective pertains to the period after the war had already begun. *Rachav*'s acceptance thus occurred within the pre-war window, before the conflict was joined in earnest. Once war commenced, that is where the dispute between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon becomes operative, and Rabbi Yehudah holds that subsequent *teshuvah* would not exempt them — whereas the *Kena'anim* outside the land remain under discussion.

The second difficulty *Tosafot* raises is whether *yefat toar* could itself constitute an exception to the obligation to destroy the *shiva umot*: *dilma be-yefat toar afilu Rabbi Yehudah modeh* — perhaps even Rabbi Yehudah would agree that the laws of *yefat toar* override the obligation to destroy them, given that the *gemara* holds *yefat toar* waives even the prohibitions of *kehunah*. *Tosafot* responds that the laws permitting sexual relations with a captive woman do not carry with them an exemption from the separate affirmative *mitzvah* to destroy the seven nations. The *mitzvah* to eliminate the *shiva umot* outside of Israel, according to Rabbi Yehudah, would not be overridden by the *heter* of *yefat toar*. These are, in sum, several intertwined questions: the degree to which there is an obligation to destroy the *shiva umot* outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, whether *teshuvah* would have constituted a remedy, how to handle the case of *Rachav*, and whether *yefat toar* could serve as a basis for exception.

## One Day Narrative

### Sixty Mil Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה נִסִּים נַעֲשׂוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם: עָבְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן, וּבָאוּ לְהַר גְּרִיזִים וּלְהַר עֵיבָל יָתֵר מִשִּׁשִּׁים מִיל, וְאֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵיהֶם, וְכׇל הָעוֹמֵד בִּפְנֵיהֶם מִיָּד נִתְרָז, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת אֵימָתִי אֲשַׁלַּח לְפָנֶיךָ וְהַמֹּתִי אֶת כׇּל הָעָם אֲשֶׁר תָּבֹא בָּהֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the entrance of the Jewish people into Eretz Yisrael: **Come and see how many miracles were performed on that day: The Jewish people crossed the Jordan, and they arrived at Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal,** which are **more than sixty *mil*** from the river, **and** on that day **no entity was able to stand before them. And anyone who stood before them was immediately struck with diarrhea, as it is stated: "I will send My terror before you, and will confound all the people that you encounter"** (Exodus 23:27).

The *gemara* now returns to the central narrative and its harmonization of the *Yehoshua* account with the Devarim commands. *Bo re'eh* — come and see — *kama nisim na'asu be-oto ha-yom*: how many miracles were performed on that very day they crossed the *Yarden*. The *gemara* states that *Bnei Yisrael* crossed the *Yarden* and then came to Har Gerizim and Har Eival — *yoter mi-shishim mil*, more than sixty *mil* away, which is approximately sixty kilometers. This is a remarkable claim. The point is precisely that the *gemara* is taking the events of Perek 8 of *Yehoshua* — the *mizbeiach*, the writing of the Torah, the *brachot* and *klalos* — and asserting they occurred on the same day as the crossing of the *Yarden*.

### Bracketing Battles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר ״תִּפֹּל עֲלֵיהֶם אֵימָתָה וָפַחַד ... עַד יַעֲבֹר עַמְּךָ ה׳״ — זוֹ בִּיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, ״עַד יַעֲבֹר עַם זוּ קָנִיתָ״ — זוֹ בִּיאָה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, the verse **says: "Terror and dread falls upon them;** by the greatness of Your arm they are as still as a stone; **till Your people pass over, Lord"** (Exodus 15:16). **This** alludes to the **first arrival** of the Jewish people in Eretz Yisrael, during the period of Joshua. The verse continues: **"Till the people that You have gotten pass over"** (Exodus 15:16). **This** alludes to the **second arrival,** when the Jews returned to Eretz Yisrael from Babylonia after the destruction of the First Temple.

This requires bracketing the battles described in Perakim 6 and 7 of *Yehoshua* — the conquest of *Yericho* and the campaign against *Ha'Ai* — and treating them as narratively displaced from their chronological position. This is essentially the principle of *ein mukdam u-me'uchar ba-Torah* applied to the book of *Yehoshua*: the battles of *Yericho* and *Ha'Ai* did not occur on that first day but are recorded out of sequence. *V'ein kol beryah yechola la'amod bifneihem* — no creature was able to stand against *Bnei Yisrael*; *ve-chol ha-omed bifneihem haya nisraz* — anyone who tried was immediately, let us say, undone.

### Fear Before Them

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱמוֹר מֵעַתָּה: רְאוּיִין הָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לַעֲשׂוֹת לָהֶם נֵס בְּבִיאָה שְׁנִיָּה כְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁגָּרַם הַחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, **say from now** that **the Jewish people were worthy of having a miracle performed for them during the second arrival, just like** they were worthy of having miracles performed for them during **the first arrival, but** their **sin caused** them to enter Eretz Yisrael through a natural process, with the permission of the kings of other nations.

The *gemara* draws upon a verse: *ve-es emasi ashalach lefanecha ve-hamoti et kol ha-am* — I will send My fear before you and I will disturb all the people. *Rashi* explains *ve-hamoti* as *lashon mehumah ve-irvuv ha-guf mah she-betocho* — a language of confusion and disordering of the inner body, i.e., causing the enemy to lose control of his bowels. And the verse continues: *tipol aleihem eimatah va-fachad* — fear and trembling will fall upon them.

The *gemara* then applies a *derasha* to the verse *ad ya'avor amcha Hashem / ad ya'avor am zu kanita* — the two halves of the verse are read as referring to two distinct entries into the land. The first half, *ad ya'avor amcha Hashem*, refers to *bi'ah rishonah*, the first entry in the time of *Yehoshua*. The second half, *ad ya'avor am zu kanita*, this nation that You have acquired, refers to *bi'ah sheniyah*, the second entry in the time of *Ezra*. The *gemara* observes: *amru me'atah re'uyim hayu Yisrael la'asot lahem nes be-vi'ah sheniyah ke-vi'ah rishonah* — it would have been fitting for miracles to accompany the second entry as they had the first. *Ela she-garam ha-chet* — but sin caused otherwise. *Rashi* makes the important observation that it was not merely the absence of miracles that was at stake, but the fundamental nature of the entry itself: in the second entry, *Bnei Yisrael* entered as subjects of the Babylonian kingdom, *mishta'abdim le-malchut*, settling the land by permission rather than conquering it as a sovereign nation.

### Stones Round Trip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ הֵבִיאוּ אֶת הָאֲבָנִים, וּבָנוּ אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְסָדוּהוּ בְּסִיד, וְכָתְבוּ עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת כׇּל דִּבְרֵי הַתּוֹרָה בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּאֵר הֵיטֵב״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its description of the entrance into Eretz Yisrael during the period of Joshua: **And afterward they brought the stones and built the altar** on Mount Ebal, **and plastered it** over **with plaster, and wrote on** the stones **all of the words of the Torah in seventy languages, as it is stated:** "And you shall write on the stones all of the words of this law **clearly elucidated"** (Deuteronomy 27:8).

The *gemara* continues: *ve-achar kach hevi'u et ha-avanim u-vanu et ha-mizbe'ach ve-sadu va-sid ve-chatvu aleihem et kol divrei ha-Torah be-shivim lashon*. This is the *gemara*'s resolution of the ambiguity in the *pesukim*: the same stones that had been erected in *Gilgal* were transported to Har Gerizim and Har Eival, where they were used to build the *mizbeiach*, plastered, and the Torah was written upon them in seventy languages. The *olot* and *shelamim* were offered, they ate, drank, rejoiced, performed the *brachot* and the *klalos* — *ve-kiplu et ha-avanim* — and then folded up, so to speak, the stones. This phrase raises a question: if the stones were brought all the way to Har Gerizim and Har Eival, when did they get erected in *Gilgal*, as *Yehoshua* describes? The answer is that after all this was done, they brought the stones back. *Ve-ba'u va-lanu be-Gilgal* — and they came and lodged in *Gilgal*. As the verse says, *ve-ha'avartem otam imachem ve-hinachtem otam ba-malon* — you shall carry them with you and place them at the lodging place. One might ask: perhaps any lodging place would do? The *gemara* derives from the phrase *asher talinu bo ha-lailah* — where you sleep that night — that it refers to one specific place. And then the verse in *Yehoshua* records: *ve-et shteim esrei ha-avanim ha-eleh asher lakechu... hekim Yehoshua ba-Gilgal* — the twelve stones were erected in *Gilgal*. According to the *gemara*'s reading, this erection in *Gilgal* happened after the round trip to Har Gerizim and Har Eival, not before.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֶעֱלוּ עוֹלוֹת וּשְׁלָמִים, וְאָכְלוּ וְשָׁתוּ וְשָׂמְחוּ, וּבֵרְכוּ וְקִלְּלוּ, וְקִיפְּלוּ אֶת הָאֲבָנִים, וּבָאוּ וְלָנוּ בַּגִּלְגָּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַעֲבַרְתֶּם אוֹתָם עִמָּכֶם וְהִנַּחְתֶּם אוֹתָם בַּמָּלוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And they sacrificed burnt-offerings and peace-offerings, and they ate and drank and celebrated, and they uttered** the **blessings, and they uttered** the **curses, and they took [*kippelu*] the stones** with them, **and they arrived and slept in Gilgal, as it is stated: "And carry them over with you, and lay them down in the lodging place,** where you shall lodge this night" (Joshua 4:3).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל בְּכׇל מָלוֹן וּמָלוֹן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר תָּלִינוּ בוֹ הַלָּיְלָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה הָאֲבָנִים הָאֵלֶּה אֲשֶׁר לָקְחוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that they were required to place these stones **at each and every lodging place** where they stayed. Therefore, **the verse states: "Where you shall lodge this night,"** meaning only on that night. **And it is written: "And those twelve stones, which they took** out of the Jordan, Joshua set up in Gilgal" (Joshua 4:20).

### Synthesis & Resolution

It is worth pausing to appreciate the full scope of what the *gemara* has accomplished here. There were two distinct problems in the *pesukim*. The first was the internal ambiguity in Devarim 27: was the Torah written on the erected stones or on the *avnei ha-mizbeiach*? The *gemara*'s answer is that these were the same stones — the stones erected in *Gilgal* became the stones of the *mizbeiach* at Har Gerizim and Har Eival, and the Torah was written on them in that dual capacity. The second problem was the apparent disjunction between Devarim, which presents the erection of the stones, the building of the *mizbeiach*, the writing of the Torah, and the *brachot* and *klalos* as one integrated event, and *Yehoshua*, which places the erection of the stones in *Gilgal* immediately after the crossing and the *mizbeiach* and *brachot u-klalos* many chapters and months later in Perek 8. The *gemara* resolves this by collapsing both accounts into a single miraculous day: the stones were taken from the *Yarden*, transported more than sixty *mil* to Har Gerizim and Har Eival, used for the *mizbeiach* and the writing of the Torah, and then brought back to *Gilgal* — all on the same day — while the battles of *Yericho* and *Ha'Ai* are treated as occurring at a later time, bracketed out of chronological sequence. This is a *gemara* that is solving problems in the *pesukim* that one would not have recognized without reading the narrative of *Yehoshua* alongside Devarim with great care.

### Tzira Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: צִרְעָה לֹא עָבְרָה עִמָּהֶם. וְלָא? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְשָׁלַחְתִּי אֶת הַצִּרְעָה לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:10): The **hornet [*tzira*] did not cross** the Jordan **with them.** The Gemara asks: **And** did it **not? But isn't it written: "And I will send the hornet before you,** which shall drive out the Hivites, and the Canaanites" (Exodus 23:28)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: עַל שְׂפַת יַרְדֵּן עָמְדָה, וְזָרְקָה בָּהֶן מָרָה. וְסִימְּתָה עֵינֵיהֶם מִלְּמַעְלָה, וְסֵירְסָתַן מִלְּמַטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָנֹכִי הִשְׁמַדְתִּי אֶת הָאֱמֹרִי מִפְּנֵיהֶם אֲשֶׁר כְּגֹבַהּ אֲרָזִים גׇּבְהוֹ וְחָסֹן הוּא כָּאַלּוֹנִים וָאַשְׁמִיד פִּרְיוֹ מִמַּעַל וְשׇׁרָשָׁיו מִתָּחַת וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** The hornet **stood on the banks of the Jordan and threw its venom at** the inhabitants of the land **and it blinded their eyes from above and castrated them from below, as it is stated: "Yet I destroyed the Amorites before them, whose height was like the height of the cedars, and they were strong as the oaks; yet I destroyed their fruit from above, and their roots from beneath"** (Amos 2:9).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: שְׁתֵּי צְרָעוֹת הֲוַאי, חֲדָא דְּמֹשֶׁה וַחֲדָא דִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ. דְּמֹשֶׁה לָא עֲבַר, דִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ עֲבַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: There were two hornets. One** was the hornet **of Moses,** which helped conquer the eastern side of the Jordan, **and one** was the hornet **of Joshua.** The hornet **of Moses did not cross** the Jordan, but the hornet **of Joshua did cross.**

The *gemara* cites a *tanna*: *tzira lo avra imahen* — the *tzira*, the wasps, did not cross over with them into *Eretz Yisrael*. The verse had promised: *ve-shalachti et ha-tzira lefanecha* — I will send the wasps before you. So why does the *baraita* say they did not cross? Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish explains: *al sfat ha-Yarden amda* — they stood at the bank of the *Yarden*. They did not enter the land but remained hovering at its border, and from there they launched their attack. *Ve-zarka bahen mara* — they spat their venom across, blinding the enemy: *ve-simta eineihem mi-l'mala* — blinded their eyes above — *ve-sersatan mi-l'mata* — and castrated them below. This was, in effect, a long-range assault — a kind of guided weapon that operated from outside the border of *Eretz Yisrael*. The verse in Amos confirms both aspects: *va-ashmid piryo mi-ma'al* — I destroyed the fruit from above — *ve-sharashav mi-tachat* — and the roots below.

Rav Papa offers a different resolution: *shtei tzirot havai* — there were two distinct *tzirot*. One was associated with Moshe, and one with *Yehoshua*. The *tzira* of Moshe did not cross over into *Eretz Yisrael*, while the *tzira* of *Yehoshua* did cross and operated within the land, fulfilling the verse *ve-shalachti et ha-tzira lefanecha*. What problem is Rav Papa solving by positing two? It is difficult to say with certainty, and a closer examination of the *Maharsha* would be required. The *Maharsha* does observe that the verse referring to the *tzira* specifies only the *Kena'ani* and the *Chiti* and does not mention the other nations — which may be part of what the *gemara* is working through. The matter requires further study.

## Division of Tribes

### Six on Each

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁשָּׁה שְׁבָטִים עָלוּ לְרֹאשׁ הַר גְּרִיזִים כּוּ׳. מַאי ״וְהַחֶצְיוֹ״? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁחֲלוּקִין כָּאן — כָּךְ חֲלוּקִין בְּאַבְנֵי אֵפוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: **Six tribes ascended to the top of Mount Gerizim,** as it is stated: "Half of them in front of Mount Gerizim and the half of them in front of Mount Ebal" (Joshua 8:33). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term **"and the half of them"?** It seems to be referring to a division that already existed. **Rav Kahana says: The same way that** the tribes **were divided up here** on the mountains, **so** too **were they divided on the stones of the ephod,** a garment of the High Priest.

The *gemara* now turns to the actual events at Har Gerizim and Har Eival. Six tribes went up to the top of Har Gerizim and six to Har Eival. The verse in *Yehoshua* states: *chetzyo el mul Har Gerizim ve-ha-chetzyo el mul Har Eival* — half facing Har Gerizim and the [other] half facing Har Eival. What is the significance of the *ve-ha-chetzyo*, the particular phrasing of "the half"?

### Ephod Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שְׁתֵּי אֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת הָיוּ לוֹ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עַל כְּתֵיפָיו, אַחַת מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִכָּאן, וּשְׁמוֹת שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים כָּתוּב עֲלֵיהֶם, שִׁשָּׁה עַל אֶבֶן זוֹ וְשִׁשָּׁה עַל אֶבֶן זוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁשָּׁה מִשְּׁמוֹתָם עַל הָאֶבֶן הָאֶחָת וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this answer: **The High Priest had two precious stones** on the part of the ephod that rested **on his shoulders, one on this side and one on that side, and the names of the twelve tribes were written on them, six on this stone and six on that stone, as it is stated: "Six of their names on the one stone,** and the names of the six that remain on the other stone, according to their birth" (Exodus 28:10).

Rav Kahana answers: *ke-derech she-chalukim kan, kach chalukim be-avnei efod* — just as they are divided here on the mountains, so are they divided on the stones of the *efod*. The *gemara* challenges this with a *baraita*.

### Ephod Letter Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנִיָּה כְּתוֹלְדוֹתָם, וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹנָה כְּתוֹלְדוֹתָם — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיְּהוּדָה מוּקְדָּם. וַחֲמִשִּׁים אוֹתִיּוֹת הָיוּ, עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ עַל אֶבֶן זוֹ וְעֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ עַל אֶבֶן זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from the verse that only the names on the **second** stone were written **according to** the order of **their birth:** Gad, Asher, Issachar, Zebulun, Joseph, Benjamin. **But** the names on the **first** stone were **not** written **according to** the order of **their birth, as Judah was** written **first,** and afterward came Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Dan, and Naphtali. **And there were fifty letters** on the two stones of the ephod, **twenty-five** letters **on this stone and twenty-five on that stone.**

The *baraita* teaches: *shtei avanim tovot hayu lo le-Kohen Gadol al ketfav* — the *Kohen Gadol* had two precious stones on his shoulders, one on each side. On them were written the names of the twelve tribes: six names on one stone and six on the other. The verse states: *shishah mi-shemotam al ha-even ha-achat ve-et shemot ha-shishah ha-no'tarim al ha-even ha-sheniyah ke-toldotam*. The *baraita* notes that *ke-toldotam* — by their birth order — applies to the second stone but not the first. Why? Because on the first stone, *Yehudah* is listed first even though he is the fourth-born, since he is the leader — *mipnei she-Yehudah mukdam*. The *baraita* further states: *va-chamishim otiyot hayu* — there were fifty letters in total — *esrim va-chamishah al even zo ve-esrim va-chamishah al even zo* — twenty-five on each stone, a perfectly equal division.

### Rabbi Chanina View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁחֲלוּקִין בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים, חֲלוּקִין בְּאַבְנֵי אֵפוֹד, אֶלָּא כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁחֲלוּקִין בְּחוֹמֶשׁ שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says:** The names of the tribes were **not divided on the stones of the ephod the** same **way that they were divided in** the list found at the beginning of **the book of Numbers** (Numbers 1:1–15). **Rather** they were divided **the way that they were divided in the second book,** i.e., Exodus (Exodus 1:2–4).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? בְּנֵי לֵאָה כְּסִידְרָן, בְּנֵי רָחֵל אֶחָד מִכָּאן וְאֶחָד מִכָּאן, וּבְנֵי שְׁפָחוֹת בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **How** were they written? On one stone, the names of **the sons of Leah** were written **in the order of their** birth: Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah, Issachar, and Zebulun. On the other stone **the sons of Rachel** were written. **One,** Benjamin, was written **on this side,** i.e., at the bottom of the list, **and one,** Joseph, was written **on that side,** i.e., at the top of the list. **And the children of the handmaids,** i.e., Bilhah and Zilpah, who were Dan, Naphtali, Gad, and Asher, were written on the second stone **in the middle.**

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel disagrees with the first opinion. He holds that the division on the *avnei efod* did not follow the order found in *Chumash ha-Pekudim*, i.e., *Bamidbar*, but rather the order found in *Chumash Sheni*, i.e., *Shemot*. The opening of *Shemot* lists: *Reuven, Shimon, Levi, Yehudah, Yissachar, Zevulun, u-Vinyamin* — and then: *Dan, Naftali, Gad, ve-Asher* — and the section concludes with *Yosef*. The *gemara* explains that in this listing, *benei Leah* appear in their birth order together, while *benei Rachel* appear one at the beginning (*Binyamin*) and one at the end (*Yosef*), with the children of the *shefachot* in the middle: *benei Rachel echad mi-kan ve-echad mi-kan, u-venei shefachot be-emtza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״כְּתוֹלְדֹתָם״ — כִּשְׁמוֹתָן שֶׁקָּרָא לָהֶן אֲבִיהֶן, וְלֹא כְּשֵׁמוֹת שֶׁקָּרָא לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה. ״רְאוּבֵן״, וְלֹא ״רְאוּבֵנִי״, ״שִׁמְעוֹן״, וְלֹא ״שִׁמְעוֹנִי״, ״דָּן״ וְלֹא ״הַדָּנִי״, ״גָּד״ וְלֹא ״הַגָּדִי״. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** if their names were not written in the order of their births, then **how do I establish** the meaning of the phrase: **"According to their birth"** (Exodus 28:10)? It means that their names were written **according to** the **names that their father,** Jacob, **called them, and not according to the names that Moses called them.** On the stones it said **Reuben, and not Reubenites; Simeon, and not Simeonites; Dan, and not Danites; Gad, and not Gadites.** This *baraita* contradicts Rav Kahana's opinion, as according to all of the opinions in the *baraita*, the division of the names on the ephod is not identical to the division of the tribes on Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal. The Gemara confirms: **The refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Kahana is** in fact **a conclusive refutation.**

But this raises a difficulty: the verse says the names were inscribed *ke-toldotam* — and the second stone in this arrangement is not in birth order at all. The *gemara* resolves: *ki-shmos she-kara lahem avihen, ve-lo ki-shmos she-kara lahem Moshe* — it means by the names their father gave them at birth, not the tribal appellations used by Moshe later. Thus *Reuven* and not *Re'uveni*, *Shimon* and not *Shimoni*, *Dan* and not *ha-Dani*, *Gad* and not *ha-Gadi*. The intent is not a sequential birth order but rather personal names given at birth. This is a subtle and meaningful distinction: the *avnei efod* bore not the names of the tribes as national entities — as they appear in the census of *Bamidbar* — but the names of the twelve sons of *Yaakov* as individual human beings, as they appear in the opening of *Shemot*. There is something deeply personal about this understanding: the *Kohen Gadol* carried these twelve men on his shoulders, not twelve tribes in the abstract.

### Ve'hachetzyo Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי ״וְהַחֶצְיוֹ״? תָּנָא: — חֶצְיוֹ שֶׁל מוּל הַר גְּרִיזִים מְרוּבֶּה מֵחֶצְיוֹ שֶׁל הַר עֵיבָל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֵּוִי לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** is meant by the phrase: **"And the half of them** facing Mount Ebal" (Joshua 8:33)? It is **taught** in a *baraita*: The use of the definitive article in the verse indicates that the smaller half of the Jewish people was on Mount Ebal. **The half that was facing Mount Gerizim was larger than the half on Mount Ebal, because** the tribe of **Levi** was included in the group that was facing Mount Gerizim, and they remained **on the bottom** between the two mountains.

The *gemara* therefore returns to the question: if *ve-ha-chetzyo* does not refer to the division of the stones of the *efod*, what does it mean? The answer comes from a *tanna*: *hatziyah shel mul Har Gerizim merubah me-hatziyah shel Har Eival* — the half facing Har Gerizim was larger in number than the half on Har Eival. The *ve-ha-chetzyo* on Har Eival highlights that it was the lesser half.

### Levi Below Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֵּוִי לְמַטָּה בָּצְרִי לְהוּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֵּוִי לְמַטָּה — בְּנֵי יוֹסֵף עִמָּהֶם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְדַבְּרוּ בְּנֵי יוֹסֵף אֶת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לֵאמֹר מַדּוּעַ נָתַתָּה לִּי נַחֲלָה גּוֹרָל אֶחָד וְחֶבֶל אֶחָד וַאֲנִי עַם רָב ... וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵיהֶם יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אִם עַם רַב אַתָּה עֲלֵה לְךָ הַיַּעְרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is puzzled by this statement: **On the contrary, because** the tribe of **Levi** remained **on the bottom they were fewer** in number. The Gemara answers: **This is what** the *baraita* **is saying: Although** the tribe of **Levi was on the bottom, the descendants of Joseph were among them,** and the tribe of Joseph was numerous, **as it is stated: "And the children of Joseph spoke to Joshua, saying: Why did you give me a single lot and one part for an inheritance, seeing I am a great people,** forasmuch as the Lord has blessed me thus, **and Joshua said to them: If you are a great people, you should go up to the forest"** (Joshua 17:14–15).

The reason for this disparity: *mipnei she-Levi lemata* — because *Levi* was below, not on top of Har Gerizim. Since the *Levi'im* and *Kohanim* stood below in the valley rather than ascending the mountain, the side of Har Gerizim was diminished by their absence. One might object: *adaraba*, since *Levi* was below, that should make the Har Gerizim side smaller — yet the *gemara* says it was larger. The *gemara* clarifies: even though *Levi* was not fully counted in the Har Gerizim side, nevertheless the Har Gerizim side was still numerically greater.

### Yosef's Population

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְהַחְבִּיאוּ עַצְמְכֶם בַּיְּעָרִים, שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁלוֹט בָּכֶם עַיִן הָרָע. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זַרְעֵיהּ דְּיוֹסֵף לָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא בִּישָׁא, דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּן פֹּרָת יוֹסֵף בֵּן פֹּרָת עֲלֵי עָיִן״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַל תְּהִי קוֹרֵא ״עֲלֵי עָיִן״, אֶלָּא ״עוֹלֵי עָיִן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Joshua **said to them: Go and hide yourselves in the forests, so that the evil eye will not have dominion over you,** as you are such a large number of people. The tribe of Joseph **said to him: The evil eye does not have dominion over the offspring of Joseph, as it is written: "Joseph is a fruitful vine, a fruitful vine by a fountain"** (Genesis 49:22), **and Rabbi Abbahu says: Do not read** the verse as saying: **"By a fountain [*alei ayin*]"; rather,** read it as: **Those who rise above** the evil **eye [*olei ayin*],** teaching that Joseph and his descendants are not susceptible to the evil eye.

The reason is *bidei Yosef imahem* — because *benei Yosef* were on Har Gerizim, and *Yosef*'s population was enormous, more than enough to compensate for the absence of *Levi*. The proof is from *Yehoshua*: *vayidabru bnei Yosef et Yehoshua lemor, madua natata li nachalah goral echad ve-chevel echad va-ani am rav* — the children of *Yosef* complained to *Yehoshua* that he had given them only one portion even though they were so numerous. *Yehoshua* replied: *aleh lecha ha-ya'arah* — go up into the forest and take more land. The *gemara* interprets this exchange with a remarkable aside: *amar lahem lechu ve-hachvi'u atzmechem ba-ye'arim she-lo tishlot bachem ayin ha-ra* — *Yehoshua* was in effect telling them: you are so many, go hide yourselves in the forests so the *ayin ha-ra* will not afflict you. They responded: *zar'eih de-Yosef lo shaltah beih eina bisha* — the seed of *Yosef* is not subject to the evil eye. The verse itself confirms: *ben porat Yosef, ben porat alei ayin* — Rabbi Abbahu reads *alei ayin* not as "by the spring" but as *olei ayin*, rising above the evil eye. Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina derives the same from the *berachah* of *Yaakov*: *ve-yidgu la-rov be-kerev ha-aretz* — they shall multiply like fish within the land. Just as fish in the sea are covered by water and the eye has no power over them, so too the descendants of *Yosef* are not subject to the *ayin ha-ra*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אֲמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְיִדְגּוּ לָרֹב בְּקֶרֶב הָאָרֶץ״, מָה דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם — מַיִם מְכַסִּין עֲלֵיהֶן וְאֵין הָעַיִן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶן, אַף זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף — אֵין הָעַיִן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternative source for the assertion that the evil eye holds no sway over Joseph and his descendants: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that it is derived **from here:** Jacob blessed Joseph's children and said: **"And let them grow [*veyidgu*] into a multitude in the midst of the earth"** (Genesis 48:16). **Just as** with regard to **fish [*dagim*] in the sea, waters cover them and the** evil **eye** therefore **has no dominion over them, so too,** with regard to **Joseph's descendants, the** evil **eye has no dominion over them.**

### Census Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי חֲמִשִּׁים אוֹתִיּוֹת? חֲמִשִּׁים נְכֵי חֲדָא הָוְיָין! אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יוֹסֵף הוֹסִיפוּ לוֹ אוֹת אַחַת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵדוּת בִּיהוֹסֵף שָׂמוֹ בְּצֵאתוֹ עַל אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the *baraita* that contradicts Rav Kahana's opinion: **Are these** names of the tribes, which were written on the ephod, composed of a total of **fifty letters?** There **are** only **forty-nine. Rabbi Yitzḥak says: They added one letter to** the name of **Joseph [*Yosef*]** when it was written on the ephod, **as it is stated: "He appointed it in Joseph [*Yehosef*] for a testimony when He went forth against the land of Egypt"** (Psalms 81:6).

*Tosafot* on 36a, *mahi ve-ha-chetzyo*, takes the discussion further by actually performing a tabulation from the census at the end of *Bamidbar* — the census taken at the end of the forty years in the wilderness, after the generation of the desert had died, as is established by *Seder Olam*. *Tosafot* computes the actual numbers tribe by tribe and concludes: *timtza be-minyan shvatim she-amdu al Har Gerizim odef al otam she-amdu al Har Eival tish'at alafim chasar kuf-lamed* — the tribes on Har Gerizim exceeded those on Har Eival by nine thousand minus 130. So the Har Gerizim side was indeed larger, consistent with the *gemara*. Crucially, *Levi* numbered approximately eight thousand. If one removes *Levi* from the Har Gerizim side, the difference narrows to only about one thousand, rendering the two sides nearly equal. This is entirely consistent with the *gemara*'s statement that it was *Levi*'s absence below that nearly equalized the sides, and that *Yosef*'s large numbers made the difference in keeping Har Gerizim the larger side.

### Fifty Letters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״כְּתוֹלְדֹתָם״ בָּעֵינַן! אֶלָּא: כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ ״בִּנְיָמִין״ כְּתִיב, וְהָכָא ״בִּנְיָמִין״ שָׁלֵם, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וְאָבִיו קָרָא לוֹ בִנְיָמִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak objects to this: We require** the names to be written **"according to their birth,"** and Joseph was not called *Yehosef* from birth. **Rather,** the explanation is as follows: Throughout **the entire Torah,** the name of **Benjamin is written** without a second letter *yod* between the letters *mem* and *nun*, **and here,** where he is born, **Benjamin** is written **in full,** spelled with a second *yod*. **As it is written: "But his father called him Benjamin"** (Genesis 35:18). Therefore, his name was written on the ephod with a second *yod*, "according to his birth."

The *gemara* now raises an arithmetical problem. The *baraita* had stated that there were fifty letters across the two stones of the *efod*, twenty-five on each. But when one actually counts the letters in the twelve tribal names, the total is forty-nine, not fifty — and the division is twenty-five and twenty-four, not twenty-five and twenty-five. *Hamishim ve-ki chada havei* — fifty? It is forty-nine.

### Yehosef Solution

Rabbi Yitzchak offers the first solution: *Yosef hosifu lo ot achat* — an additional letter was added to *Yosef*'s name. The verse states: *edut be-Yehosef samo be-tzeto al eretz Mitzrayim* — there is a verse in *Tehillim* where *Yosef* appears with an extra *heh*, written *Yehosef*. It was this spelling that was inscribed on the *efod* stone, adding one letter to reach fifty total, with exactly twenty-five on each stone.

### Binyamin Shalem

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenges this: the *baraita* says *ke-toldotam* — the inscription must reflect the names as given at birth. *Yehosef* was not a name by which he was known during his lifetime in that form; it appears only in a late verse in *Tehillim*. Therefore, the name *Yehosef* does not satisfy the requirement of *ketoldotam*. Instead, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes a different solution: throughout the entire Torah, *Binyamin* is written without a *yud* — *Binyamin chaser* — but when he was born and his father named him, the verse reads *va-aviv kara lo Binyamin* — and this spelling is with the full *yud*, *Binyamin shalem*. It is precisely this birth spelling that satisfies *ke-toldotam* — the name as called at birth — and that provides the fiftieth letter, yielding twenty-five letters on each stone.

## Yosef's Temptation

### Yosef Sanctifies Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: יוֹסֵף שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בַּסֵּתֶר — הוֹסִיפוּ עָלָיו אוֹת אַחַת מִשְּׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, יְהוּדָה שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — נִקְרָא כּוּלּוֹ עַל שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥana bar Bizna says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says: Joseph, who sanctified the name of Heaven in private, had one letter of the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** the letter *heh*, **added to his** name. Whereas in the case of **Judah, who sanctified the name of Heaven in public [*befarhesya*]** at the Red Sea during the exodus from Egypt, merited that **his entire** name **is called by the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** as the entire four-letter name of God can be found within Judah's name.

Since we have been speaking of *Yosef* and the extra letter added to his name, the *gemara* now tells us why he merited this distinction. Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida teaches: *Yosef she-kidesh Shem Shamayim be-seter, hosifu lo ot achat mi-shmo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — *Yosef*, who sanctified God's name in private, was rewarded with one letter from God's name: the *heh* of *Yehosef*.

### Yehudah Public Sanctific…

The contrast is drawn immediately: *Yehudah she-kidesh Shem Shamayim be-farhesia, nikra kulo al shemo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — *Yehudah*, who sanctified God's name publicly, merited that his entire name reflects God's name. Indeed, *Yehudah* contains the letters *yud, heh, vav, heh* — the four letters of the divine name — with only an additional *dalet*. We will return tomorrow to the public sanctification of *Yehudah*, which the *gemara* will identify not with the *Yehudah* of this week's *parsha* but with the story of *Nachshon ben Aminadav* at the *Yam Suf*. For now, the focus is on *Yosef*.

### Temptation Scene

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹסֵף מַאי הִיא — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי כְּהַיּוֹם הַזֶּה וַיָּבֹא הַבַּיְתָה לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלַאכְתּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה נִתְכַּוְּונוּ. ״וַיָּבֹא הַבַּיְתָה לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלַאכְתּוֹ״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלַאכְתּוֹ מַמָּשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: לַעֲשׂוֹת צְרָכָיו נִכְנַס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is** the situation where **Joseph** sanctified God's name in private? **As it is written: "And it came to pass on a certain day, when he went into the house to do his work"** (Genesis 39:11). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This **teaches that both** Joseph and Potiphar's wife stayed in the house, as they **intended to** perform **a matter of sin.** With regard to the phrase **"when he went into the house to do his work," Rav and Shmuel** engage in a dispute with regard to its meaning. **One says:** It means that he went into the house **to do his work, literally. And one says: He entered** the house **in order to fulfill his** sexual **needs** with her.

The *gemara* cites the verse: *va-yehi ke-hayom ha-zeh va-yavo ha-bayta la'asot melachto* — it was on such a day and he came to the house to do his work. Rabbi Yochanan says: *melamed she-shneihem li-dvar aveirah nitkavnu* — both of them, *Yosef* and the wife of *Potiphar*, intended that day to sin. What pushes Rabbi Yochanan to this unflattering reading about *Yosef*? *Tosafot* explains that it is the juxtaposition of *va-yavo ha-bayta* — "and he came to the house" — a word *va-yavo* associated with *bi'ah*, with *la'asot melachto*, doing his work. On this reading, the text hints that his coming was charged with intent. *Rav u-Shmuel* debate this directly: one says *la'asot melachto mamash* — to do his actual work — and the other says *la'asot tzorachav nichnas* — he entered to fulfill his desires.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֵין אִישׁ מֵאַנְשֵׁי הַבַּיִת וְגוֹ׳״, אֶפְשָׁר בַּיִת גָּדוֹל כְּבֵיתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע לֹא הָיָה בּוֹ אִישׁ?! תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם יוֹם חַגָּם הָיָה, וְהָלְכוּ כּוּלָּן לְבֵית עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלָּהֶם, וְהִיא אָמְרָה לָהֶן חוֹלָה הִיא, אָמְרָה: אֵין לִי יוֹם שֶׁנִּיזְקָק לִי יוֹסֵף כַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And there was none of the men of the house** there within" (Genesis 39:11). The Gemara asks: **Is it possible** that in such **a large** and important **house like the house of that wicked man** that **no one was in there? The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: That day was their festival day and they all went to their house of idol worship; and she told them** that **she was sick** and could not go, as **she said** to herself: **I have no day on which Joseph will attend to me like this day.**

*Ve-ein ish me-anshei ha-bayit sham ba-bayit* — none of the household staff was present. How could such a large household be completely empty? *Tana de-bei Rabbi Yishmael* explains: *oto ha-yom yom chagam haya* — it was a festival day of theirs, *ve-halchu kulam le-veit avodah zarah shelahem* — and all of them went to their house of idol worship. She told them she was ill and remained behind. *Amra*, she said to herself: *ein li yom she-nizkak li ke-hayom ha-zeh* — I will never have an opportunity like today.

### Yaakov's Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּתְפְּשֵׂהוּ בְּבִגְדוֹ לֵאמֹר וְגוֹ׳״, בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בָּאתָה דְּיוֹקְנוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו וְנִרְאֲתָה לוֹ בַּחַלּוֹן, אָמַר לוֹ יוֹסֵף! עֲתִידִין אַחֶיךָ שֶׁיִּכָּתְבוּ עַל אַבְנֵי אֵפוֹד וְאַתָּה בֵּינֵיהֶם, רְצוֹנְךָ שֶׁיִּמָּחֶה שִׁמְךָ מִבֵּינֵיהֶם, וְתִקָּרֵא רוֹעֶה זוֹנוֹת? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרֹעֶה זוֹנוֹת יְאַבֶּד הוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And she caught him by his garment, saying:** Lie with me" (Genesis 39:12). **At that moment his father's image [*deyokeno*] came and appeared to him in the window.** The image **said to him: Joseph,** the names of **your brothers are destined to be written on the stones of the ephod, and you** are to be included **among them. Do you desire your name to be erased from among them, and to be called an associate [*ro'eh*] of promiscuous women? As it is written: "But he who keeps company with harlots wastes his riches"** (Proverbs 29:3), as he loses his honor, which is more valuable than wealth.

At that critical moment, *va-titpesehu be-vigdo lemor*, she grabbed him by his garment. It was at this moment that *ba'ah demut dyukno shel aviv* — the image of his father appeared to him — *ve-nirah lo ba-chalon*, in the window. And the image spoke. *Yaakov*'s image said to him: *Yosef, atidim achecha she-yikkatvu al avnei efod* — your brothers are destined to be inscribed on the *avnei efod*, *ve-ata beneihem* — and you are among them. *Retzoncha she-yimacheh shimcha mi-beneihem* — do you want your name erased from among them? This warning carries a profound resonance, given the context in which the *gemara* has placed it. We have spent considerable time discussing the writing of names on the *avnei efod* and the erasure of names — and now the threat to *Yosef* is precisely the erasure of his name from among his brothers on those very stones. One might also detect here a deeper echo: the concept of *mechiyat ha-shem*, the erasure of the divine name, is particularly associated with grave sexual transgression. The warning is simultaneously about physical erasure from the *efod* and about the theological weight of such a sin.

*Ve-tikara ro'eh zonot* — and you will be called a shepherd of prostitutes. The verse in Mishlei says: *ro'eh zonot ye'abed hon* — one who shepherds prostitutes wastes great treasure. This was the warning. People dissipate immense reservoirs of dignity and honor by following their desires.

### Physical Resistance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד — ״וַתֵּשֶׁב בְּאֵיתָן קַשְׁתּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שֶׁשָּׁבָה קַשְׁתּוֹ לְאֵיתָנָהּ. ״וַיָּפֹזּוּ זְרוֹעֵי יָדָיו״ — נָעַץ יָדָיו בַּקַּרְקַע וְיָצְאָה שִׁכְבַת זַרְעוֹ מִבֵּין צִיפּוֹרְנֵי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately: "And his bow abode [*teishev*] firm"** (Genesis 49:24). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** This means **that his bow,** i.e., his penis, **returned [*shava*] to its strength,** as he overcame his desire. The verse about Joseph continues: **"And the arms of his hands were made supple"** (Genesis 49:24), meaning that **he dug his hands into the ground and his semen was emitted between his fingernails.**

*Miyad* — immediately — *va-teshev be-eitan kashto* — he placed his strength in his bow. The bow is transparently a phallic metaphor for the tension of sexual arousal. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Meir reads: *she-shav kashto le-eitano* — his bow returned to its strength, or alternatively, his *keri* returned to its source. *Va-yaphozu zero'ei yadav* — and the sinews of his arms were scattered. He pressed his fingers into the ground to prevent ejaculation. *Va-yetze shichvat zera mi-bein tzippornei yadav* — the seminal emission came out through his fingernails. This graphic account is conveying something important about the nature of what *Yosef* was resisting: he had come to a point where resistance was physiologically nearly impossible, where the body's drive was so powerful that even in holding back, the seed had to discharge somehow. He redirected it at enormous personal cost.

### Shepherd Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִידֵּי אֲבִיר יַעֲקֹב״, מִי גָּרַם לוֹ שֶׁיֵּחָקֵק עַל אַבְנֵי אֵפוֹד — אֶלָּא אֲבִיר יַעֲקֹב. ״מִשָּׁם רֹעֶה אֶבֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל״, מִשָּׁם זָכָה וְנַעֲשָׂה רוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רוֹעֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל הַאֲזִינָה נֹהֵג כַּצֹּאן יוֹסֵף״.
>
> **Translation:** **"By the hands of the Mighty One of Jacob"** (Genesis 49:24): **Who caused his** name **to be etched onto the stones of the ephod?** It was **only the might of Jacob. "From there, from the Shepherd, the Stone of Israel"** (Genesis 49:24) means: **From there,** because of Joseph's ability to withstand this trial, **he merited to become a shepherd [*ro'eh*]** of the Jewish people, **as it is stated: "Listen, O Shepherd of Israel, who leads like the flock of Joseph"** (Psalms 80:2).

*Midei avir Yaakov* — from the power of *Yaakov*. Who caused it that his name was ultimately inscribed on the *avnei efod*? *Avir Yaakov* — the strength of *Yaakov*, whose image appeared and pulled him back. *Misham ro'eh* — the *gemara* now returns to the image of the shepherd. Because he resisted and became worthy of having his name inscribed on the *efod*, *misham zachah ve-na'aseh ro'eh* — from there he merited and became a shepherd. *She-ne'emar: ro'eh Yisrael ha'azinah, noheig ke-tzon Yosef* — the verse says, Shepherd of Israel, hear, who leads like the flock of *Yosef*. *Yosef*, who might have become a *ro'eh zonot*, a shepherd of prostitutes, instead became the shepherd of *Bnei Yisrael*.
