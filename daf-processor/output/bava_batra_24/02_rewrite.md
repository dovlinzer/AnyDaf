# Bava Batra 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rov vs. Karov

### Mishna Overview

The daf continues a discussion that has been unfolding across several pages concerning the tension between two competing principles of attribution: *rov* (majority) and *karov* (proximity). The *mishna* presented a case involving a dove found near a particular dovecote, where the ruling was that the dove is attributed to the owner of that nearby cote — an application of *karov*. This stands in apparent tension with the position of Rabbi Chanina, who holds that one always follows *rov*, the majority, rather than mere proximity. The Gemara has been working systematically to respond to each piece of evidence that might support *karov*, in order to preserve and defend Rabbi Chanina's principle.

### Gemara Defense

With this framework in mind, the Gemara continues its sustained effort to shore up Rabbi Chanina's position, deflecting each apparent counterexample and insisting that under normal circumstances, *rov* governs.

## Blood Braita

### Abaye's Proof

The Gemara now records that Abaye offers his own support for Rabbi Chanina. *Af anan nami tanina* — we too have taught a *braita* that corroborates the principle of following *rov*. The *braita* states: *dam she-nimtza ba-prozdor sefeko tamei she-chazakato min ha-makor* — blood found in the *prozdor* (the antechamber or canal) is treated as doubtfully impure, and the reason given is that its presumptive origin is the *makor* (the source, i.e., the uterus). This ruling, Abaye argues, demonstrates that we follow *rov* even when *karov* points elsewhere.

### Anatomy Explained

To understand this *braita*, the Gemara invokes a well-known anatomical metaphor attributed to the Sages: *mashlu mashal chachamim be-isha, ha-cheder ve-ha-prozdor ve-ha-aliya* — the Sages compared the female anatomy to a house consisting of three parts: the *cheder* (inner room), the *prozdor* (antechamber), and the *aliya* (upper story). In halachic terms, the *cheder* represents the uterus; only blood originating there is considered *dam niddah* and renders a woman *tamei*. The *prozdor* is the passage through which such blood would travel. The *aliya* is a third, less clearly identified source, connected to the *prozdor* by a kind of opening or tube (*lul patuach*), from which blood of a different and non-*tamei* character could potentially emerge. The question, then, is this: if blood is found in the *prozdor*, which source do we attribute it to — the *cheder* (which is the *rov*, since the great majority of blood comes from there) or the *aliya* (which, being connected by the *lul*, is *karov*, or more immediately proximate)?

The *braita*'s ruling is that we follow *rov* and assume the blood comes from the uterus. This is Abaye's proof for Rabbi Chanina's principle.

It is worth noting that there is genuine difficulty in identifying what precisely the *aliya* refers to anatomically. One compelling approach — evident in some *acharonim*, though ultimately rejected — draws on a parallel passage in which the Gemara notes that a cow does not have a *prozdor*. What a cow lacks is the external labia. This suggests that the *prozdor* in the human female context might refer not to the inner vaginal canal but to the external space formed by the labia. If that reading is adopted, the *aliya* becomes far more intelligible: it would refer to the urethra, and the concern would be that blood found externally might have come from blood in the urine rather than from the uterus. The *lul patuach* would then describe the urethal opening. This interpretation makes the physiological picture coherent in a way that internal readings struggle to achieve. Nevertheless, other evidence in the sources points toward the *prozdor* being an internal structure, and that reading is generally maintained, leaving the *aliya* as an anatomical puzzle. Either way, the Gemara's underlying point is clear: we go by *rov* and not by *karov*.

### Rov vs. Matzui

Rava responds to Abaye's proof with a pointed objection. *Rov u-matzui ka'amart?* — Are you speaking of a case that is not merely *rov* but also *matzui*, meaning not only quantitatively majority but practically prevalent and established by pattern? These are two distinct concepts. *Rov* in the ordinary sense means simply that more of something comes from one source than another — as when a distant dovecote has five hundred birds while the nearby cote has only a few. *Matzui*, by contrast, reflects a known, observed pattern: not just that there is more blood in the uterus, but that in practice virtually all blood passing through the *prozdor* is known to flow from the *cheder*. It is not merely a numerical majority; it is the established, natural way things work.

Rava's point is that *rov u-matzui leit de-man d'palig* — when you have both majority and established prevalence together, no one disputes that you follow this combined indicator. The woman's case is such an instance: the blood is not just probabilistically more likely from the uterus, but experientially and practically it is the source under normal circumstances. Therefore, Abaye's proof is less than it appears — it is not a proof that *rov* alone defeats *karov*, but rather that *rov* combined with *matzui* does so, and that is uncontroversial.

### Rava's Inferences

Having established that the *braita* of Rabbi Chiya — who rules that blood found in the *prozdor* is treated as certainly *tamei* rather than merely doubtfully so — implies that we follow *rov* and treat the ruling as definitive, Rava proceeds to draw three inferences (*shma mina mid'Rabbi Chiya tlat*). First: when there is a conflict between *rov* and *karov*, the *halacha* follows *rov*. Second: *ruba de'oraita* — the principle of following *rov* and treating the outcome as a *vadai* (certainty) rather than a *safek* (doubt) is a biblical-level rule, as evidenced by the fact that Rabbi Chiya treats the woman as definitively *tamei*, making her liable for *karet* should she enter the *Beit HaMikdash*, and making *terumah* she touches subject to burning — rulings befitting a *vadai* determination. Third: the ruling supports the position of Rabbi Zeira.

### Rabbi Zeira

Rabbi Zeira's position, invoked in the third inference, addresses a classic case: a piece of meat found in the street whose origins are unknown. If there are nine stores in the city selling kosher meat and one selling *treif*, one may rely on the *rov* and eat the meat — even if, as Rabbi Zeira holds, the gates of the city are closed, meaning there is no external *rov* from surrounding communities to add to the equation. Some opinions had required a double *rov* — the *rov* within the city plus the broader *rov* of the surrounding region — in order to permit the meat. Rabbi Zeira maintains that a single *rov* suffices, and the ruling is *vadai* — permissive without qualification. The case of blood in the *prozdor* supports this: the woman is compared to a city with closed gates (*daltot ha-medina ne'ulot*), since no blood is entering from outside her body, and yet we rule with certainty based on the single internal *rov*. This shows that one *rov* is sufficient, that it is treated as *vadai*, and that it overrides *karov*.

### Meat Case

The meat case merits further elaboration, as it raises a number of important questions. One might ask whether the size of the *rov* matters — must the kosher stores vastly outnumber the non-kosher one, or is even a small majority sufficient? The general principle is that *rov* applies even when the majority is not overwhelming; one does not need nine-to-one. What matters more is the nature of the *miut* (minority): is it a *miut ha-matzui* (a prevalent minority) or a *miut she-eino matzui* (a negligible minority)? A *miut she-eino matzui* is generally understood as less than ten percent or perhaps less than five percent. When the *miut* is non-trivial — a *miut ha-matzui* — there is an obligation to investigate further if investigation is possible (*chiyuv levarer*). When the *miut* is negligible, one may rely on the *rov* even if investigation is possible (*efshar levarer*). This is why, for example, after *shechita* one must check the lungs of an animal for *treifos* — because *treifos* in the lungs constitute a *miut ha-matzui* — but need not check every other organ, where *treifos* are a *miut she-eino matzui*. On the question of whether the proximity of the meat to the non-kosher store is relevant: based on everything established about *rov* versus *karov*, even if the meat is found closer to the non-kosher store, the *rov* of kosher stores would in principle prevail, though this is not explicitly stated in the Gemara's discussion here.

## Rabba Retracts

### Retraction Question

Having cited Rava's challenge — that the case of blood in the *prozdor* involves not mere *rov* but *rov u-matzui*, and therefore cannot serve as proof for the general principle of *rov* against *karov* — the Gemara notes: *hadar beih Rabbah meihai* — Rabbah retracted. The question is which position Rabbah retracted. Did he retract his earlier characterization of the case as one of *rov u-matzui*, or did he retract the use of Abaye's *braita* as a proof for the general principle?

### Interpretation

The Gemara's language suggests that Rabbah retracted the objection to using Abaye's proof — that is, he accepted that the *braita* does in fact demonstrate the principle of *rov* against *karov*, without requiring the added weight of *matzui*. This is, however, somewhat puzzling. Rava's original point had real force: once a pattern has been empirically established, as it is in the case of blood flowing from the uterus, this constitutes an even stronger basis for attribution than a mere numerical *rov*, and anyone who would argue for *karov* against a simple *rov* would face an even steeper climb against *rov u-matzui*. The conclusion that Rabbah ultimately treated the case as a standard *rov* — assimilating *rov u-matzui* back into the ordinary category — implies that those who would argue for *karov* could theoretically do so even when a practical pattern has been established, though this is very difficult to understand once such a pattern is clear. The Gemara does not elaborate further on this point, leaving it as a somewhat unresolved tension.

## Wine Barrel

### Rav's View

The Gemara now presents a new case. *Chavit she-tzafah ba-nahar* — a barrel of wine floating in the river. No one is chasing it, so it may be claimed as *aveidah*. The question is whether it is permitted to drink — i.e., whether it is treated as Jewish wine or as *stam yeinam*. Rav rules: if the barrel is found opposite a city that is majority Jewish, it is permitted, because we attribute it to the nearby city. If it is found opposite a city that is majority non-Jewish, it is forbidden, for the same reason of proximity. Rav's ruling is thus explicitly based on *karov* — he attributes the barrel to whatever city it is nearest.

### Shmuel's View

Shmuel disagrees. Even if the barrel is floating past a city that is majority Jewish, Shmuel rules it is forbidden — because the majority of cities along the river are non-Jewish, and one should follow that broader *rov* rather than the nearby city. Shmuel thus sides with *rov* even against *karov*.

### Reconciliation

The Gemara considers whether Rav and Shmuel debate the principle of Rabbi Chanina itself — whether Shmuel accepts it and Rav rejects it. But consistent with the Gemara's sustained effort to defend Rabbi Chanina, it declines this reading: *de-kulei alma it lehu de-Rabbi Chanina* — in fact, both Rav and Shmuel accept the principle of following *rov*. Their dispute is more specific. Rav reasons that if the barrel had truly come from a distant upstream city, the obstacles of the river — the protrusions (*akulei*) and the still waters (*pshurei*) that Rashi describes — would have stopped or diverted it before it arrived here. Therefore, the distant *rov* is practically inaccessible to this location and should be discounted. Since the access of the distant majority is obstructed, Rav falls back on *karov*. Shmuel responds: *charifa de-nahara nakit ve-atei* — the powerful current of the river carried the barrel past all obstacles. Therefore, the distant *rov* remains relevant and governs.

Both agree that obstacles can in principle reduce the weight of a distant *rov* — just as the Gemara elsewhere, in the context of *eglah arufah*, speaks of physical barriers that limit access from other cities. The question is simply whether the river's current overcomes those obstacles. The Gemara thus reaffirms that everyone holds of Rabbi Chanina; the practical disagreement is only about whether a particular impediment is sufficient to discount the distant *rov*.

## Orlah Vineyard

### Ravina Ruling

The Gemara introduces another case. A barrel of wine (*chatzva de-chamra*) was found in a vineyard planted with *orlah* grapes. The circumstances suggest the barrel had been stolen. Ravina ruled the wine permissible — one need not assume it came from the *orlah* grapes in this particular vineyard. This is striking, since the vineyard is *karov* — the barrel is right there in it.

### Evidence Explanation

The Gemara explains that this ruling does not reflect a principled application of *rov* over *karov*. Rather, *shani hatam* — that case is different, because the evidence itself points away from the nearby vineyard. The reason: *demignav minei atsnuyei begaveih lo matsnei* — a thief does not hide stolen goods in the same place from which he stole them. Rashi explains: thieves are afraid that the workers who tread the grapes (*dorchei ha-anavim*) will discover the hidden barrel. Therefore, a stolen barrel found within the vineyard almost certainly came from elsewhere — the thief would not have concealed it in the very location where discovery is most likely. Ravina's leniency is thus not a statement about *rov* triumphing over *karov* in the abstract; it is a specific evidential inference that the very presence of the barrel in this vineyard makes attribution to it less plausible, not more.

### Barrel vs. Grapes

The Gemara qualifies this reasoning. *Ve-hanei milei chamra* — the logic of the thief not hiding the goods at the scene applies only to a barrel of wine, which would be recognized as coming from a particular vineyard and thus discovered. *Aval invei* — if what was found were a bunch of stolen grapes, the Gemara indicates that a thief might indeed hide them within the same vineyard, since loose grapes are less identifiable. The distinction is thus between a recognizable, attributed container (the barrel) and undistinguishable produce (the grapes themselves). The ruling follows accordingly.

## Wine Flasks

### Rava's Ruling

A further case: *hanehu zikki de-chamra de-ishtakach bei kufai* — flasks of wine found between the vine rows of a vineyard. Here the relevant question is not *orlah* but *stam yeinam*. The vineyard belongs to Jews. Rava ruled the wine permissible, attributing the flasks to the Jewish owners of the vineyard even though, as the Gemara clarifies, the majority of those who pass through the area are non-Jews.

### Jewish Sellers

The Gemara asks whether Rava's ruling implies he rejects Rabbi Chanina — following *karov* (the Jewish vineyard) over *rov* (the majority non-Jewish passersby). The answer is again: *shani hatam* — no, this is different. *De-ruba de-shafochei Yisrael ninhu* — the majority of those who actually fill and sell wine flasks in this area are Jews. Rashi explains that the Jews operate small wine-filling stations along the road, serving as what one might picture as provisioners for travelers. So although most of the travelers passing through are non-Jews, when it comes to the specific activity of filling wine flasks, the majority of the vendors doing so are Jewish. The non-Jewish travelers are getting their flasks filled with kosher Jewish wine. Thus, Rava's ruling does not rest on *karov* alone but on a *rov* specific to the type of transaction at hand — who fills the flasks.

This raises an interesting question: once a non-Jewish traveler receives a flask of wine and handles it, does the wine remain permitted? The Gemara does not address this directly, but the implication of the case, as presented, is that the flask has not been handled in a way that would render it *stam yeinam* — perhaps because it was sealed or because the evidence indicates it had not been opened.

### Flask Sizes

The Gemara further qualifies the ruling. *Ve-hanei mili be-ravrevi* — this applies only to large flasks. A large flask is the kind one gets filled at a provisioning station, not something carried from home. *Aval be-zutrei* — small personal flasks are presumed to have come from the traveler's own supply (*eima mi-drachei naflu*, they fell from those traveling the road), and those would be suspect as *stam yeinam*. The Gemara adds one more refinement: *ve-i ika ravrevi be-hadayihu* — if a large flask and a small flask are found together, one assumes the small flask was also filled at the Jewish provisioner alongside the large one, for the purpose of balancing the load on the donkey. Rashi notes this practice: when one side of a donkey carries a large, heavy wine container, smaller flasks are loaded on the other side to balance the weight. If found together with a large flask, the small flask is therefore also attributed to the Jewish source.

## Trees & Cities

### Mishna Rules

The Gemara now transitions to a new *mishna* on a more classically *Bava Batra* theme: the required distance between trees and a city. *Marchikin et ha-ilan min ha-ir esrim v-chamesh amah* — a fruit-bearing tree must be kept at least twenty-five *amot* from the edge of a city. *U-v'charuv u-v'shikma chamishim amah* — for the *charuv* (carob) and the *shikma* (sycamore), the required distance is fifty *amot*.

### Abba Shaul

Abba Shaul extends the rule: *kol ilan srak chamishim amah* — any non-fruit-bearing tree requires fifty *amot* of distance from the city. Rashi explains this on the grounds that a non-fruit-bearing tree is aesthetically unpleasant near a city (*ilan srak gnai hu la-ir*), and therefore a greater buffer is required. Rashi also explains that the *charuv* and *shikma* specifically require fifty *amot* because of their wide and sprawling branches, which would crowd the space adjacent to the city.

### City Priority

The *mishna* then addresses which party has the prior claim when a conflict arises. *Ve-im ha-ir kadma* — if the city was established first and the tree was planted afterward within the prohibited zone, the city may have the tree cut down *and does not owe payment*, since the planter was in violation from the outset. Notably — and this is a surprising ruling — even if *the tree was there first* (*ve-im ilan kadam*), the city may nonetheless have it cut down; in that case, however, the city *must pay* the owner, since the tree owner was not in violation.

### Doubt Cases

In a case of doubt about which came first (*safek zeh kadam safek zeh kadam*), the *mishna* rules that the tree is cut down and no payment is made. The bias in doubt cases thus favors the city.

## Noy Ha'ir Reason

### Ulla's Reason

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the rationale for requiring trees to be kept distant from a city? Ulla answers: *mishum noy ha-ir* — for the beauty of the city. The open, unplanted space immediately adjacent to a settlement is aesthetically desirable, and this aesthetic interest is a recognized halachic basis for requiring the twenty-five *amot* buffer.

### Migrash Question

The Gemara challenges this: why invoke *noy ha-ir* at all? Let the ruling derive instead from the Torah's principle of *migrash* — the open space surrounding the cities of the Levites. The Torah speaks of a thousand *amot* of green space (*migrash*) that must be maintained outside such cities, which cannot be converted into fields, and conversely, fields cannot be converted into *migrash*. If this is a general principle about maintaining open space near settlements, it would provide a much stronger (and Torah-based) source for the tree-distance rule — though notably at a thousand *amot*, far exceeding our *mishna*'s twenty-five.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

The Gemara responds: the *mishna* is operating according to the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the laws of *migrash* — maintaining the green belt and not converting it — apply only to the cities of the Levites as specified in the Torah. For ordinary cities, there is no biblical mandate to maintain such open space, and a city may arrange its surroundings as it chooses. Hence the need for a different rationale: *noy ha-ir*.

### Trees in Migrash

The Gemara then addresses the position of the *rabbanan*, who hold that the *migrash* rules do apply more broadly — that one cannot convert a *migrash* into a *sadeh* (field) or vice versa. Even according to them, the *mishna*'s rule is independently necessary, because the prohibition on converting *migrash* applies only to planting it with *zeraim* (seeds, i.e., grain crops). *Aval ilanos avdinan* — trees, however, may be planted within the *migrash*, since trees are consistent with the character of open, habitable green space. A park with trees still functions as a *migrash*; a wheat field does not. Accordingly, even if a city must maintain a thousand-*amah* *migrash* under the *rabbanan*'s view, trees within that *migrash* are permitted — yet our *mishna* still requires that the tree line begin no closer than twenty-five *amot* from the city, for *noy ha-ir*.

## Enclosed Spaces

### Karpef Rules

The Gemara supports the distinction between seeds and trees by citing a *braita* from the laws of *eruv*. If one has a *karpef* — an enclosed space — larger than *beis sa'atayim* (fifty *amot* by a hundred *amot*, or five thousand square *amot*) that was enclosed for the purpose of habitation (*hukaf le-dira*), it qualifies as a *reshus ha-yachid* in which one may carry on *Shabbos*.

### Seeds vs. Trees

The *braita* then addresses what happens if the majority of this space is subsequently used for agricultural purposes. If the majority is planted with seeds — converting it effectively into a grain field — it loses its status as a habitable space and carrying within it is prohibited, since a grain field is not the kind of space in which people dwell and conduct their lives. However, if the majority is planted with trees, it retains the character of a courtyard or garden (*harei hu ke-chatzer u-mutar*) — people walk among trees, gather in parks, and use such spaces for living activities. Trees, unlike grain, do not strip a space of its quality as a human habitation. This distinction in *hilchos eruv* provides the conceptual parallel: just as trees are consistent with the *migrash* character of open habitable space, while grain crops are not, so too in the context of a city's green belt, trees are a more acceptable presence — yet the immediate twenty-five *amot* must still remain entirely unplanted for the sake of *noy ha-ir*.

---

## Enclosed Spaces…

### City Spacing

Applying this logic back to the city context: even if trees may be planted within the *migrash* under the *rabbanan*'s view without transforming it into a prohibited *sadeh*, the *mishna* still establishes that the tree line cannot begin immediately adjacent to the city. A buffer of twenty-five *amot* of completely unplanted space must be maintained for the aesthetic quality of the city — *noy ha-ir* — regardless of what the broader *migrash* rules permit.

## Tree vs. Well

### Well Mishna

The Gemara now directs attention to an upcoming *mishna* on 25b that deals with the required distance between a tree and a *bor* (cistern or well): *marchikin et ha-ilan min ha-bor esrim ve-chamesh amah* — twenty-five *amot*, and fifty for the *charuv* and *shikma*, measured both laterally and downward. The parallel to our *mishna* is explicit, and the Gemara compares the two in detail. The outcomes, however, differ in a revealing way. In the *bor* case: if the well came first (*im ha-bor kadmah*), the tree is cut down and the city pays; if the tree came first (*im ha-ilan kadam*), the tree is not cut down at all; and in a case of doubt (*ve-im safek*), the tree is also not cut down. In the city case, by contrast, the tree is *always* cut down regardless of which came first — the only variable is whether payment is owed.

### Payment Query

This asymmetry prompts the Gemara's question. When the well has priority and the tree owner was in violation, why must the well owner *pay* to have the tree removed, whereas when the city has priority and the tree owner was in violation, the city need *not* pay? Why is the city treated more favorably than a private well owner?

### Committee Pot

Rav Kahana offers one answer: *kidra de-bei shutfei lo chamimei ve-lo kerirei* — a pot under the care of partners is neither hot nor cold. A responsibility shared by many is executed by none. Because the city's obligation to pay would be distributed among many people, no individual would feel the urgency to contribute, and the collective would never get around to reimbursing the tree owner. If payment were required, the practical result would be that the tree would never get cut down. To ensure the city can actually accomplish the removal, the law spares it the payment obligation. There is a certain irony here: the city's collective dysfunction winds up operating to its legal advantage. The Gemara itself notes that this answer, while functional, is not the most satisfying one.

### City Rights

The better answer is simply that the city has greater rights than a private individual. The *hezek de-rabbim* — damage suffered by the many — is treated with greater legal weight than *hezek de-yachid*, harm to an individual. The collective interest of the city in maintaining its proper character and spacing is a more powerful legal interest than that of a single well owner, and it therefore entitles the city to have the tree removed without owing compensation, even when the city is asserting a right that a private party in the same position would have to purchase.

The Gemara then clarifies that Rav Kahana's *kidra de-bei shutfei* reasoning was actually meant to address a different part of the *mishna*: the clause that says when the tree came first, the city cuts it down *and then* pays. One might have expected the tree owner to demand payment upfront before permitting removal. Rav Kahana explains: because it is so difficult to get the collective to pay, and because we want the removal to actually happen, the law allows the city to cut first and pay later. This means the tree owner must wait for his money — a disadvantage — but the alternative, requiring payment in advance, would mean the tree never gets removed. The law therefore prioritizes the outcome the city needs while preserving the tree owner's right to compensation, however delayed.

## Tree First Case

### Cut First Issue

The *mishna*'s ruling that when the tree came first, the city cuts it down and pays (*kotzetz ve-noten damim*), raises an immediate procedural question: why should the city be permitted to cut the tree down before paying? The tree owner was not in violation; it seems entirely reasonable that he should demand his money before surrendering his property.

### Logistical Answer

Rav Kahana's answer, as established above, is logistical: *kidra de-bei shutfei* — shared responsibility among the many members of the city means that if payment must precede removal, the city will never actually organize and execute the payment, and the tree will remain standing. Since the city has a legitimate interest in having the tree removed, the law permits them to act first and settle the debt afterward. The tree owner retains his right to be paid, but cannot hold the tree hostage to that right.

---

## Doubt Case

### Burden Shift

The final clause of the *mishna* — that in a case of doubt, the city cuts without payment — stands in stark contrast to the parallel *mishna* on the well, where doubt results in the tree not being cut at all. The Gemara explains the divergence through the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof lies with the one who seeks to extract something from another's possession.

### Presumptive Right

In the *bor* case, if the tree came first with certainty, the tree would not be cut down. Therefore, in a case of doubt, the well owner cannot demand removal: since there is a scenario in which he would have no right to cut the tree, he must prove that scenario does not apply. Absent such proof, the tree remains — the burden falls on him to demonstrate he has the right to cut.

In the city case, however, the tree is cut down regardless of which came first; the only question is whether payment accompanies the removal. Since the city is always entitled to remove the tree, it does so in the doubt case as well. The remaining question — must it pay? — is now governed by *hamotzi me-chavero alav hara'aya* in the opposite direction: the tree owner must prove that his tree preceded the city in order to collect payment. Since he cannot, the city cuts and owes nothing. The city's presumptive right to remove the tree, which exists in all scenarios, anchors the doubt case in its favor — both as to the act of removal and as to the absence of payment.
