# Ketubot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rape & Rov in Yichus

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתִינוֹקֶת שֶׁיָּרְדָה לְמַלּאוֹת מַיִם מִן הָעַיִן, וְנֶאֱנָסָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: אִם רוֹב אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר מַשִּׂיאִין לִכְהוּנָּה, הֲרֵי זוֹ תִּינָּשֵׂא לִכְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yosei said:** There was **an incident involving a young girl who descended to fill** her jug with **water from the spring, and she was raped,** and the identity of the rapist was unknown. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: If the majority of the people of the city marry** their daughters **to** members of the **priesthood, this young girl may be married to** a member of the priesthood.

The Mishnah presents the case of a young girl who was raped. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri rules that if most of the people in the city are those whose intercourse with the woman would leave her *ksheira l'kehunah* — that is, they are Jewish and not *mamzeirim* or *chalalim* — then she remains *ksheira* for marriage to a *kohen*. With this ruling, the Mishnah introduces the principle of *rov* (majority) into questions of *yichus*.

### Rov & Earlier Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי דְּאָמַר כְּמַאן? אִי כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב פְּסוּלִין נָמֵי מַכְשַׁר. אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב כְּשֵׁרִים נָמֵי פָּסֵיל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: In accordance with whose** opinion is it **that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri stated** his opinion? **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel, even in** a case where **the majority** of the people **are** of **flawed lineage as well, he deems** her **fit** to marry into the priesthood. **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, even in** a case where **the majority** of the people **are** of **unflawed lineage, he deems** her **unfit** to marry into the priesthood. Rav Naḥman **said to** Rava that **this** is what **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:**

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty. Until now, the tractate has been preoccupied with the debate between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua, a dispute centered entirely on the woman's claim and her credibility — the percentage composition of the surrounding population played no role. Rava therefore poses the question to Rav Nachman: according to whom does Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri rule? If he follows Rabban Gamliel, then Rabban Gamliel permits her even when the majority of the neighborhood consists of *psulin* — non-Jews or those otherwise disqualifying — provided she identifies the man and attests to his fitness. If he follows Rabbi Yehoshua, then even when the majority are *ksheirim*, she is *pesulah*, because Rabbi Yehoshua does not accept her unilateral claim. Tosafot works through this tension and suggests that perhaps the Gemara assumes the woman did identify her attacker; Rashi, however, reads the entire discussion as proceeding according to Rabbi Yehoshua — a reading that will become significant as the *sugya* develops.

### Caravans of Tzipori

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְכִדְרַבִּי אַמֵּי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: וְהוּא שֶׁהָיְתָה סִיעָה שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם כְּשֵׁרִין עוֹבֶרֶת לְשָׁם, וְכִדְרַבִּי יַנַּאי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: נִבְעֶלֶת בִּקְרוֹנוֹת כְּשֵׁרָה לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The **incident** transpired **among the wagons [*keronot*]** in the marketplace **of Tzippori** on the market day, and this *halakha* is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Ami, as Rabbi Ami said: And this is** the ruling only in a case **where there was a contingent of men of unflawed lineage passing there.** Due to the fact that both the lineage of the majority of the people of the city where the girl was raped and the lineage of the majority of the passersby is unflawed, the rape is attributed to a man of unflawed lineage. **And** this ruling is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yannai, as Rabbi Yannai said: If she engaged in intercourse in the wagons, she is fit to** marry a member of the **priesthood.**

The Gemara's answer comes in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav: the story in the Mishnah involved the caravans (*kronot*) of Tzippori. Rabbi Ami qualifies the Mishnah accordingly — it applies only when a group of *ksheirim* was passing through the area. Rabbi Yannai adds that if she had intercourse at the time when the caravans were passing through, she is *ksheira l'kehunah*. The Gemara then introduces a complication: if, on the other hand, one man separated from Tzippori and had intercourse with her — even though the majority of Tzippori's residents are *ksheirim* — the child is a *shtuki*, a foundling of uncertain paternal lineage, whose status is therefore suspect. The question is: why should the presence of caravans make any difference at all?

## Kavuah vs. Parush

### Caravans Always Parush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְרוֹנוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא נִבְעֶלֶת בִּשְׁעַת קְרוֹנוֹת, כְּשֵׁרָה לַכְּהוּנָּה. אֲבָל פֵּירַשׁ אֶחָד מִצִּיפּוֹרִי וּבָעַל — הַוָּלָד שְׁתוּקִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it **enter your mind** to say that the woman was raped **in the wagons,** in the crowded area of the marketplace where business is conducted? **Rather,** Rabbi Yannai is saying that if **she** was forced to **engage in intercourse at the time of the wagons,** i.e., when the convoys pass, **she is fit to** marry a member of the **priesthood. However,** if **one** individual, whose lineage is unknown, **left Tzippori and engaged in intercourse** with a woman, **the child is a *shetuki*** and deemed unfit to marry into the priesthood even though the majority of the inhabitants of city are of unflawed lineage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר זְעֵירִי, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הוֹלְכִין אַחַר רוֹב הָעִיר, וְאֵין הוֹלְכִין אַחַר רוֹב סִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: That is **similar** to **this** statement **that when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **Ze'eiri said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said, and some say** directly that **Ze'eiri said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: One follows the** flawed lineage of the **majority of** the people in **the city and one does not follow** the unflawed lineage of **the majority of the** traveling **contingent,** and the woman who engaged in intercourse with a member of the itinerant contingent is deemed unfit to marry into the priesthood.

The answer arrives when Rav Dimi brings a tradition from Eretz Yisrael in the name of Zeiri citing Rabbi Chanina: we follow the majority of the city (*rov ha-ir*), not the majority of the caravan (*rov siya*). This seems to contradict what was just said — the caravan is supposed to help! The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *hani naydi v'hani kvi v'kaimi* — the caravan is moving, while the townspeople are fixed in place. The actual ruling is that one follows the majority of the city *combined with* the majority of the caravan. Both majorities are required simultaneously.

### Gezeirah Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא?! הָנֵי נָיְידִי, וְהָנֵי קְבִיעִי וְקָיְימִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary [*kelapei layya*],** the opposite is logical. **These** members of the contingent **are moving,** and in that case the principle is that one follows the majority. **And these** people of the city **are fixed and standing** in one place, and in that case the principle is that even if the lineage of the majority is unflawed, the uncertainty is treated as if it were equally balanced.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הוֹלְכִין אַחַר רוֹב הָעִיר, וְהוּא דְּאִיכָּא רוֹב סִיעָה בַּהֲדַהּ, וְאֵין הוֹלְכִין אַחַר רוֹב הָעִיר גְּרֵידְתָּא, וְלֹא אַחַר רוֹב סִיעָה גְּרֵידְתָּא. מַאי טַעְמָא? גְּזֵרָה רוֹב סִיעָה אַטּוּ רוֹב הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this is what Rabbi Yannai is saying: **One follows the** lineage of the **majority of the city, and that is** specifically in a case **where there is a majority** of men of unflawed lineage in the passing **contingent** together **with** the city's unflawed majority. In that case, from both perspectives the intercourse can be attributed to a man of unflawed lineage. **However, neither does one follow the** unflawed lineage of the **majority of the city alone, nor** does he **follow the** unflawed lineage of the **majority of the contingent alone. What is the reason** that one does not follow the lineage of the majority of the moving contingent? It is because the Sages issued **a decree** not to follow **the majority of the contingent due to the majority of the city,** where one does not follow the majority.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרוֹב הָעִיר נָמֵי, אִי דְּקָא אָזְלִי אִינְהוּ לְגַבַּהּ: כׇּל דְּפָרֵישׁ — מֵרוּבָּא פָּרֵישׁ! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָא אָזְלָא אִיהִי לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ קָבוּעַ, וְאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל קָבוּעַ כְּמֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** aren't there cases where one follows **the majority of the city as well,** as, **if** one of the residents of the city **goes** out of the city **to her** and rapes her, the principle is: The legal status of **any** item **that is separated** from the group is that of one **separated from the majority?** Therefore, there is no reason to issue a decree. The Gemara answers: The decree is **necessary only** due to a case **where she went** into the city **to them,** in which case the rapist **is** an indistinguishable member of a **fixed** set, **and Rabbi Zeira said:** The legal status of uncertainty with regard to **any** item **fixed** in its place is that of an uncertainty that is **equally balanced,** and one does not follow the majority.

The reason a double *rov* is necessary is rooted in a *gezeirah* — a protective decree. If one were permitted to rely solely on the majority of the caravan (which is always *parush*, separated, because the travelers are not in their home location), one might come to permit reliance on the majority of the city even in circumstances where the encounter took place in someone's home — a case of *kavuah*. Conversely, if one relied solely on the majority of the city, one might permit the case even when the woman went to the man's home rather than meeting him in the field, which transforms the situation from *parush* to *kavuah*. The requirement for both *rov ha-ir* and *rov siya* together prevents any such slide.

### Kol d'Parish & Kavuah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי בָּעֵינַן תְּרֵי רוּבֵּי? וְהָתַנְיָא: תֵּשַׁע חֲנוּיוֹת כּוּלָּן מוֹכְרוֹת בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה, וְאַחַת מוֹכֶרֶת בְּשַׂר נְבֵלָה, וְלָקַח מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵי זֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח — סְפֵיקוֹ אָסוּר. וּבַנִּמְצָא — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the statement of Rabbi Ami: **And do we require two majorities** to overcome the minority? **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **nine stores** in a city, **all of which sell** kosher **meat** from **a slaughtered** animal, **and one** other store that **sells meat** from unslaughtered **animal carcasses, and** a person **bought** meat **from one of** the stores **and he does not know from which** store **he bought** the meat, in this case of **uncertainty,** the meat **is prohibited.** The legal status of uncertainty with regard to any item fixed in its place is that of an uncertainty that is equally balanced, and one does not follow the majority. This *baraita* continues: **And in** the case of meat **found** in the street, outside the stores, **follow the majority** of stores that sell kosher meat. In other words, the meat is kosher.

This is the occasion for the Gemara to articulate the foundational principles at stake: *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza dami* — anything in a fixed location must be treated as a fifty-fifty doubt — and *kol d'parish me-rubah parish* — anything that has separated from a group is judged by the majority. These principles are formally strange; they resist simple probabilistic logic. The intuition behind *kavuah*, however imperfect, seems to be that when one confronts a bounded, fixed set of objects — among which one is definitively problematic — and asks about the identity of a particular member of that set, one cannot simply ignore the definite presence of the problematic element. One is forced to acknowledge a genuine fifty-fifty doubt, as opposed to the case of *parush*, where one approaches an object of unknown provenance and asks what the majority would indicate about its identity.

### Meat Analogy

The classic paradigm is the case of meat. If one finds a piece of meat lying in the street and wishes to know its status, and in the city there are nine *kosher* butchers and one non-*kosher* butcher, one follows the majority: the meat is *kosher*. The meat has *parish* — it has separated from its source — and the inquiry is about its status, not about which fixed location it came from. However, if one returns home and finds meat there without remembering where it was purchased, the question becomes: which store did I enter? Those stores are fixed in their locations. The inquiry is now directed at a fixed set of objects, and therefore the ruling is *safek asur* — a doubtful prohibition — even though nine out of ten stores are *kosher*. That is *kavuah*: *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza dami*.

### Rape Case Application

Applying these principles to the rape case: if the man left Tzippori and encountered the woman in the field, he is *parush* — he has separated from his fixed location — and we therefore follow *rov*, which in Tzippori is *ksheirim*. But if the woman went to the man's home, that is *kavuah*: she is asking, in effect, which of these fixed domiciles did I enter? In that case, even a ninety-percent majority cannot help her; we treat it as fifty-fifty. The caravan is always *parush* by definition, because travelers are never in their fixed home location. The genius of requiring both *rov ha-ir* and *rov siya* together is that the caravan component guarantees a *parush* framework, preventing any confusion with a *kavuah* scenario. In principle, a single *rov* of the city would suffice when the encounter is genuinely *parush*, but because the circumstances can blur — sometimes she comes to him, sometimes he comes to her — the Rabbis required the additional safeguard of the passing caravan, ensuring the case always carries the character of *parush*. This is the *gezeirah*: insist on both majorities so no one mistakenly permits a *kavuah* case.

## Kol D'Kavuah Principle

### Nine Stores Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁאֵין דַּלְתוֹת מְדִינָה נְעוּלוֹת, דְּקָא אָתֵי לַהּ רוּבָּא מֵעָלְמָא — וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁדַּלְתוֹת מְדִינָה נְעוּלוֹת! מַעֲלָה עָשׂוּ בְּיוּחֲסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that one follows the majority only in a case **where the gates of the city are unlocked, where** the meat could have **come** to the city **from the majority** of kosher meat **outside** the city and only by combining that majority with the majority of kosher meat stores inside the city, creating two majorities, is the meat ruled kosher; **but didn't Rabbi Zeira say: Even if the city gates are locked,** one follows the majority and the meat is kosher even without a double majority? The Gemara answers: The Sages require two majorities only in cases such as establishing the identity of the child's father, because they **established a higher standard with regard to** matters of **lineage.** However, in other cases, e.g., concerning kosher meat, a single majority is sufficient.

The Gemara now turns to examine the principle of *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* more rigorously. It opens with the classic *baraita*: nine stores all sell *shechutah* (properly slaughtered meat) and one sells *neveilah* (non-slaughtered meat). One purchased from one of the stores but cannot remember which. The ruling is *safek asur* — forbidden, because the doubt is framed around which store was entered, and the stores are fixed (*kavuah*). If, on the other hand, meat is found in the street, one follows *rov* — the question is about the meat's status, not the store's identity, and so it is *parush*.

This paradigm, the Gemara notes, only demonstrates the principle operating *le-chumra* — to generate stringency. Even though nine out of ten stores are *kosher*, if the doubt is *kavuah*, we treat it as fifty-fifty and forbid. But the formulation cited earlier in the name of Rabbi Zeira was *bein le-kula bein le-chumra* — the principle of *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* applies whether toward leniency or stringency. Where does Rabbi Zeira get this? The nine-stores case only shows stringency.

### Single vs. Double Rov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל קָבוּעַ — כְּמֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה דָּמֵי, בֵּין לְקוּלָּא בֵּין לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § In terms of the matter **itself, Rabbi Zeira said:** The legal status of uncertainty with regard to **any** item **fixed** in its place is that of an uncertainty that is **equally balanced, both** when it leads to **leniency,** e.g., in a case where, were one to follow the majority, the ruling would be stringent, and when it leads to **stringency,** e.g., in a case where, were one to follow the majority, the ruling would be lenient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי זֵירָא הָא? אִילֵּימָא מִתֵּשַׁע חֲנוּיוֹת, כּוּלָּן מוֹכְרוֹת בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה, וְאַחַת בְּשַׂר נְבֵלָה, וְלָקַח מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח — סְפֵיקוֹ אָסוּר, וּבַנִּמְצָא — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב; הָתָם לְחוּמְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does Rabbi Zeira** learn that the legal status of uncertainty with regard to any item fixed in its place is that of an uncertainty that is equally balanced, both when it leads to leniency and when it leads to stringency? **If we say** that it is derived **from** the case of **nine stores** in a city, **all of which sell** kosher **meat** from **a slaughtered** animal, **and one** other store that sells **meat** from unslaughtered **animal carcasses, and** a person **bought** meat **from one of** the stores **and he does not know from which** store **he bought** the meat, in this case of **uncertainty** the meat **is prohibited. And in the** case of meat **found** in the street, outside the stores, **follow the majority** of stores that sell kosher meat, and therefore the meat would be kosher. **There,** in the first case, ruling it an equally balanced uncertainty is **a stringency,** as there is a majority of kosher stores.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִתִּשְׁעָה צְפַרְדְּעִים וְשֶׁרֶץ אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְנָגַע בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶם, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶם נָגַע — סְפֵיקוֹ טָמֵא. הָתָם נָמֵי לְחוּמְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Zeira learns that *halakha* **from** a different *baraita*. If there were **nine frogs,** which is a creeping animal that does not impart ritual impurity while alive or when dead, **and one** ritually impure **creeping animal,** whose carcass imparts ritual impurity, **among them, and** a person **touched one of the** ten creatures, **and he does not know which of them he touched,** in this case of **uncertainty** the person is **ritually impure. There too,** ruling it an equally balanced uncertainty is **a stringency,** as the majority of the creeping animals do not impart impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִתִּשְׁעָה שְׁרָצִים וּצְפַרְדֵּעַ אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְנָגַע בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן נָגַע. בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — סְפֵיקוֹ טָמֵא. בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — סְפֵיקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Zeira learns that *halakha* **from** the continuation of that *baraita*. If there are **nine creeping animals,** whose carcasses impart ritual impurity, **and one frog among them, and** a person **touched one of them, and he does not know which of them he touched,** if it was **in the private domain,** in this case of **uncertainty** the person is **ritually impure,** as all cases of uncertainty with regard to ritual impurity are ruled impure in the private domain. This is derived from the case of *sota*. However, **in the public domain,** in that case of **uncertainty** the person is **ritually pure.** Although contact with a creeping animal from the majority would render him ritually impure, since the uncertainty is with regard to a fixed group, its legal status is that of an equally balanced uncertainty, and in the public domain he is ritually pure. Rabbi Zeira learns that *halakha* from this case of leniency.

The question that drives the entire inquiry is whether the meat case — the classic paradigm of *parush* — requires a single *rov* or a double *rov*. The Gemara suggests: perhaps we should reconstruct the meat case as involving a double *rov*, on the grounds that the gates of the city were open, meaning meat might have arrived from travelers passing through, just as in the caravan case. But Rabbi Zeira explicitly ruled that even when the gates of the city are locked — guaranteeing the meat came from one of the city's stores and nowhere else — one still follows *rov*. The meat case, therefore, operates on a single *rov*. This stands in contrast to the rape case, where a double *rov* was required.

### Yichus Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מְנָא לַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָרַב לוֹ וְקָם עָלָיו״, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּין לוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: פְּרָט לְזוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְגָו.
>
> **Translation:** After citing a tannaitic source for Rabbi Zeira's opinion, the Gemara asks: **And from where in the Torah do we** derive that the legal status of any item fixed in its place is like that of an uncertainty that is equally balanced, both when it leads to leniency and when it leads to stringency? It is derived from **the verse** that **states** with regard to a murderer: **"And lie in wait for him, and rise up against him"** (Deuteronomy 19:11), indicating that one is liable **only** in a case **where he intends to** kill **him.** One who intended to kill one person and inadvertently killed another is exempt from punishment. **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who hold that one is liable in that case, interpret that verse? The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say:** It **excludes** the case **of** one who **throws a stone into a crowd** and did not intend to kill a specific person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה גּוֹיִם וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּינֵיהֶם — תִּיפּוֹק לִיה דְּרוּבָּא גּוֹיִם נִינְהוּ. אִי נָמֵי, פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא — סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the case where he threw the stone and is exempt? **If we say that there are nine gentiles** in the crowd **and one Jew among them,** even without the verse, **let him derive** the exemption from the fact **that they are a majority of gentiles. Alternatively,** even if we say that **half** of the ten people are considered gentiles **and half** are considered Jews, let him derive the exemption from the principle: In a case of **uncertainty** concerning a **life-threatening** situation, the *halakha* **is lenient.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה יִשְׂרְאֵלִים וְגוֹי אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶם, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ גּוֹי קָבוּעַ, וְכׇל קָבוּעַ — כְּמֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the verse is **necessary** only in a case where **there are nine Jews and one gentile among them.** Were the ruling to follow the majority the one who threw the stone would be liable. But in that case, **because the gentile is fixed** among them, **and** the legal status of **any** item **fixed** in its place is **like** that of an uncertainty that is **equally balanced,** he is exempt, based on the principle: In a case of uncertainty concerning a life-threatening situation, the *halakha* is lenient. Apparently, even in cases of Torah law in which the result would be a leniency, i.e., exemption from the death penalty for murder, the legal status of any item fixed in its place is like that of an uncertainty that is equally balanced.

The Gemara resolves the disparity with a single phrase: *ma'alah asu be-yuchsin* — the Rabbis enacted an extra stringency in matters of *yichus*, fitness for marriage to a *kohen*. Under ordinary *halakhic* principles, when the situation is one of *parush*, a single *rov* suffices. In the domain of *yichus*, however, the Rabbis demanded the additional layer of a second *rov*. This accounts for why the rape case requires both *rov ha-ir* and *rov siya*, while the meat case does not. The normal rules of *rov* — *kol d'parish me-rubah parish* — would have resolved even the rape case with a single majority, but the elevated standard of *yichus* necessitates more.

### Rodents/Frog Case

Returning to Rabbi Zeira's claim that *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* applies even *le-kula*, the Gemara works through the candidates. The nine-stores case shows stringency: *kavuah* overrides a nine-to-one majority in the direction of leniency. The case of nine frogs and one *sheretz* (a dead rodent that conveys *tumah*, while frogs do not), where one touches one of the ten without knowing which, is also *le-chumra*: even though nine of the ten are harmless, the doubt is *kavuah* and the ruling is *safek tamei*. Neither case demonstrates leniency.

The Gemara then cites a *Tosefta* in Taharot with the reverse configuration: nine dead *sheratzim* and one frog. One touches one of the ten without knowing which. Now ninety percent of the set conveys *tumah*. Under ordinary probability, one would expect a ruling of *vadai tamei* — certain impurity. Yet because the doubt is *kavuah*, the principle *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* converts it into a fifty-fifty *safek*. And once it is a *safek*, the general rule of *safek tumah bi-reshut ha-rabbim tahor* applies: a doubt of *tumah* arising in a public domain leaves one *tahor*. Here, then, is the *le-kula* application — the *kavuah* principle generates leniency even when the majority would point toward impurity.

### Reshus Ha-Yachid/Rabbim

The ruling that *safek tumah bi-reshut ha-yachid tamei* and *safek tumah bi-reshut ha-rabbim tahor* is itself one of the striking features of this *sugya*. The distinction between private and public domain for doubts of *tumah* is not intuitively obvious, and the Gemara acknowledges it by tracing the rule to its source: the Torah describes a *sotah* — a woman suspected of infidelity — with the language *ve-hi nitma'ah*, she became *temeiah*. The moment of suspected *tumah* for a *sotah* is her act of seclusion with the man, which occurs in a private space. From this the rule is derived: a doubt of *tumah* that arises in a private domain (analogous to the secluded space of the *sotah*) is treated as *tamei*; a doubt arising in a public domain is treated as *tahor*. This framework then interacts with *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza*: by converting a ninety-percent likelihood into a fifty-fifty *safek*, *kavuah* places the case within the category of *safek*, which then triggers the *reshut ha-rabbim / reshut ha-yachid* distinction.

### Biblical Source

The Gemara then asks for a biblical source for *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* as a Torah-level principle. The verse adduced is from the laws of murder: *ve-arav lo ve-kam alav* — he lay in wait for him and rose against him. This phrase teaches, according to Rabbi Shimon in Sanhedrin, that criminal liability for murder requires that one kill the specific individual one intended to kill. If one aimed at Reuven and killed Shimon, Rabbi Shimon holds this is *shogeg*, not *meizid*. The Rabbis disagree: intention to kill Reuven and killing Shimon is still murder. But what does the phrase *ve-arav lo* exclude, according to the Rabbis? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: it excludes one who throws a stone into a group (*lego*) — that is, fires randomly into a crowd.

The Gemara works through the case. If the group consists of nine non-Jews and one Jew and one shoots randomly, one is obviously not liable — the majority are non-Jews, and even at fifty-fifty, the principle of *safek nefashot le-hakel* (leniency in capital matters) would apply. The novel case, the *chiddush*, is nine Jews and one non-Jew. One might think that shooting into such a group constitutes an act directed against a Jew, and that whoever was killed — even if it turned out to be the non-Jew — should generate liability on the analogy of intending to kill Reuven and killing Shimon. The Torah excludes this: the non-Jew is fixed within the group (*kavuah*), and *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza dami* — the act is therefore treated as fifty-fifty, not as definitively aimed at a Jew. The shooter is exempt. This is the biblical derivation of the principle.

## Mishnah Reconciliation

### Horat Sha'ah View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְרַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the matter of following the majority in cases of lineage, it **was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute: **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi** said that **Rav said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** in the mishna, and it is permitted for the young girl who was raped to marry a priest. **And Rav Ḥanan bar Rava** said that **Rav said:** That **was a provisional edict** issued in exigent circumstances. However, typically, with regard to matters of lineage two majorities are required.

Having established the principle of *kol d'kavuah k'mechtza al mechtza* and its implications for *yichus*, the Gemara now returns to the Mishnah. Two *amoraim* take different positions. Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav rules *le-halakha* in accordance with the Mishnah — one does follow *rov* in the rape case. Rav Chanan bar Rava, by contrast, says *hora'at sha'ah hayta* — that ruling was a special circumstance, not a normative *halakhic* precedent.

### Yirmiya's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: וּלְיוּחֲסִין לָא בָּעֵינַן תְּרֵי רוּבֵּי? וְהָתְנַן:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya raised an objection** to the ruling of Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi, who apparently ruled that even in cases where there is one majority the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei: **And in matters of lineage, do we not require two majorities,** a majority of the city's inhabitants and a majority of the passing contingent? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Makhshirin* 2:7):

Rabbi Yirmiya challenges the first position. If Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi rules in accordance with the Mishnah, he is saying that for *yichus* a single *rov* suffices. But the entire preceding *sugya* has been premised on the assumption that *yichus* demands a double *rov*. How can one rely on a single *rov* for something as consequential as permitting a woman to marry a *kohen*?

### Foundling Infant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא בָּהּ תִּינוֹק מוּשְׁלָךְ, אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — גּוֹי. אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — יִשְׂרָאֵל. מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** If there is a city in which both Jews and gentiles reside, and **one found** an unidentified, **abandoned baby in** the city, **if** there is a **majority of gentiles** in the city the baby is deemed **a gentile. If** there is a **majority of Jews** in the city the baby is deemed **a Jew.** If **half** the population is gentile and **half** Jewish, the baby is deemed **a Jew.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ, אֲבָל לְיוּחֲסִין — לֹא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְפַקֵּחַ עָלָיו אֶת הַגַּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said** with regard to this mishna: The Sages **taught** that if there is a majority of Jews in the city the baby is deemed a Jew **only** with regard **to sustaining him; however,** with regard **to lineage,** e.g., marrying him to a Jewish woman, **no,** he is not deemed a Jew based on the majority and would require conversion. **And Shmuel said:** It was taught that he is deemed a Jew in order **to create an opening** in **a heap** of debris **on his** behalf on Shabbat, i.e., desecrating Shabbat in order to save his life. Apparently, contrary to the ruling of Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi, Rav holds that a single majority is insufficient to deem him Jewish in matters of lineage.

To sharpen the point, Rabbi Yirmiya cites the Mishnah at the top of 15b: if a foundling infant (*tinok mushlach*) is discovered and the majority of the city's residents are non-Jewish, the infant is presumed non-Jewish; if the majority are Jewish, the infant is presumed Jewish; and if the population is evenly split, the infant is still presumed Jewish. Rav qualifies this ruling: the presumption of Jewish status operates only *le-hachyoto* — in order to fulfill our obligation to sustain and care for him (*ve-chai achicha imach*) — but not for purposes of *yichus*. For *yichus*, even a majority of Jews in the city is insufficient. This shows that the Gemara is consistent: *yichus* demands more than a single *rov*, and therefore one cannot rule like the Mishnah as if a single *rov* were enough.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בִּקְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל צִפּוֹרִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Yirmeya **overlooked that which Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said** with regard to the mishna: The **incident** of the rape of the young girl transpired **among the wagons** in the marketplace **of Tzippori,** and there were two majorities; the majority of the inhabitants of the city and the majority of the passing contingent. Therefore, when Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi ruled that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, i.e., that the young girl may marry a priest, it was in a case of two majorities.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא, דְּאָמַר: הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה, קַשְׁיָא הָא? מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי הָא, לָא מַתְנֵי הָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the case in the mishna is one of two majorities, according **to Rav Ḥanan bar Rava who said** in the name of Rav: **That was a provisional edict** issued in exigent circumstances, meaning that two majorities were required in that case but typically one majority is sufficient, it is **difficult.** Didn't Rav say that in matters of lineage one majority is insufficient? The Gemara answers: **That** is not difficult. **The one who teaches this,** that the ruling in our mishna was a provisional edict, **does not teach that** statement that Rav Yehuda said that Rav said that the incident took place among the wagons in the marketplace of Tzippori. Rather, he holds that there was a single majority and nevertheless, due to exigent circumstances, the girl was permitted to marry into the priesthood, although generally two majorities are required in cases of lineage.

The Gemara resolves the tension by noting: *ishtamitei'eihu* — whoever was ruling in accordance with the Mishnah had simply forgotten the tradition of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, that the Mishnah's case specifically involved the caravans of Tzippori — a double *rov* situation. Once reminded, the apparent contradiction dissolves.

The Gemara then addresses a further difficulty: according to Rav Chanan bar Rava, who says *hora'at sha'ah hayta*, what is the meaning of calling it a special circumstance if in fact the case involved a double *rov*? Should a double *rov* not suffice as a matter of normal *halakha*? The Gemara's answer is clean: *man d'matni ha lo matni ha* — the two traditions are not held by the same authority. The one who says the Mishnah is ruled *le-halakha* because it was a double *rov* case (the caravans of Tzippori) would not say it was *hora'at sha'ah*. The one who says it was *hora'at sha'ah* holds that it was only a single *rov* — and precisely because it was only a single *rov*, it cannot serve as a normative precedent for *yichus*. Either way, the conclusion is the same: do not draw from this Mishnah the inference that a single *rov* suffices for *yichus*. Whether the case was ruled permissibly because of a double *rov*, or whether the ruling was an unrepeatable special circumstance, the general principle stands — *yichus* demands a double *rov*.

## Foundling Infant

### Mishnah Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפַהּ: מָצָא בָּהּ תִּינוֹק מוּשְׁלָךְ, אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — גּוֹי, אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — יִשְׂרָאֵל, מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ, אֲבָל לְיוּחֲסִין — לֹא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְפַקֵּחַ עָלָיו אֶת הַגַּל.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the case of the abandoned baby, the Gemara analyzes the matter **itself:** If there is a city in which both Jews and gentiles reside, and **one found** an unidentified, **abandoned baby in** the city, **if** there is a **majority of gentiles** in the city the baby is deemed **a gentile. If** there is a **majority of Jews** in the city the baby is deemed **a Jew.** If **half** the population is gentile and **half** Jewish, the baby is deemed **a Jew. Rav said:** The Sages **taught** that the baby is deemed a Jew **only** with regard **to sustaining him; however,** with regard **to lineage, no. And Shmuel said:** It was taught that he is deemed a Jew in order **to create an opening** in **a heap** of debris **on his** behalf on Shabbat.

The Gemara now turns in full to the *gufah* — the body of the Mishnah about the foundling infant. If the majority of the city's residents are non-Jewish, the infant is treated as a non-Jew. If the majority are Jewish, the infant is treated as a Jew. If the population is evenly divided, the infant is treated as a Jew.

### Rav's Limits

Rav qualifies the Mishnah: *lo shanu ela le-hachyoto* — the assumption of Jewish status operates only for purposes of sustaining the infant's life, fulfilling the obligation of *ve-chai achicha imach*. For *yichus* — whether this child may marry a *kohen*, whether there is concern for *mamzerut* — a single *rov* is not enough. The Mishnah's ruling does not extend to genealogical status determinations.

### Shmuel & Pikuach Nefesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הוֹלְכִין בְּפִיקּוּחַ נֶפֶשׁ אַחַר הָרוֹב! אֶלָּא כִּי אִיתְּמַר דִּשְׁמוּאֵל — אַרֵישָׁא אִתְּמַר: אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — גּוֹי, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וּלְפַקֵּחַ עָלָיו אֶת הַגַּל אֵינוֹ כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Shmuel say that? But didn't Rav Yosef say** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: One does not follow the majority in matters** involving **saving a life?** Even if there is the slightest concern that the life of a Jew may be in danger, one takes all steps necessary to save him, even on Shabbat. **Rather, when the statement of Shmuel was stated** with regard to saving a life **it was stated concerning the first clause** of the mishna: **If** there is a **majority of gentiles** in the city the baby is deemed **a gentile. Shmuel said: And** with regard **to creating an opening** in **a heap** of debris **on his** behalf **[*lefake'aḥ alav et hagal*]** on Shabbat, **that is not so.** Even if there is a gentile majority in the city, one does not follow the majority in cases involving the saving of a life.

Shmuel goes further in a different direction: *le-fake'ach alav et ha-gal* — the basis for treating the infant as Jewish is not merely the general obligation of *hachyasah* but the imperative of *pikuach nefesh*, saving life on *Shabbat*. Shmuel's broader principle, transmitted by Rav Yosef in the name of Rav Yehuda, is this: *ein holchin be-pikuach nefesh achar ha-rov* — when it comes to saving a life, we do not follow majority reasoning. Even if there is only a small likelihood that the person is Jewish, one violates *Shabbat* to save them. Shmuel's qualification therefore applies not to the case where the majority is Jewish, but to the opening case of the Mishnah — where the majority is non-Jewish. Even there, *Shmuel* says, we do not withhold life-saving on *Shabbat*.

This is a striking position. The Gemara's implication is that even if for years one has treated this individual as a non-Jew for every other purpose — using him as a *Shabbat goy*, feeding him *treifot* — the original uncertainty of his birth is never fully extinguished. When the building collapses and he may be underneath, we remember that his parentage was never conclusively established, and we violate *Shabbat* to save him.

### Long-term Doubt

This has a counterintuitive quality that the Gemara itself highlights. Normally, once a *halakhic* status has been determined and acted upon, subsequent doubts do not revive the old uncertainty. The *safek* does not survive across generations of rulings. Yet in this case, the Gemara is prepared to say: even after twenty years of treating this person as a non-Jew in every practical sense, we remember for the purposes of *pikuach nefesh* that his identity was never resolved. The *safek* of his birth remains live — but only for that one purpose. This is precisely Shmuel's position: when the majority is non-Jewish and we have treated the infant accordingly, we have nonetheless never truly closed the file on his Jewish identity, and that open file obligates us to violate *Shabbat* on his behalf.

### Dinei Mamonot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — גּוֹי, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְהַאֲכִילוֹ נְבֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **If** there is a **majority of gentiles** the baby is deemed **a gentile.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** was this stated? **Rav Pappa said:** It was stated in order **to feed** the baby **animal carcasses,** i.e., non-kosher food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְהַחְזִיר לוֹ אֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** And it is taught in the mishna: **If** there is a **majority of Jews** the baby is deemed **a Jew.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** was this stated? **Rav Pappa said:** It was stated in order **to return lost property to him,** as one is required to return lost property to a Jew.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לִנְזָקִין. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא דְּנַגְחֵיהּ תּוֹרָא דִידַן לְתוֹרָא דִידֵיהּ — לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אַתְּ וּשְׁקוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** And it is taught in the mishna: If **half** the population is gentile and **half** Jewish, the baby is deemed **a Jew.** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** was this stated? **Reish Lakish said:** It was stated with regard **to damages.** In terms of payment of damages, the courts judge him as a Jew. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say that our ox,** one belonging to a Jew, **gored his ox,** one belonging to a person of uncertain status, and he claims that he should be compensated for the damages as a Jew, **let the** owner of the ox that gored **say to him: Bring proof that you are a Jew, and take** payment. Due to the uncertainty surrounding his status, he is unable to produce any proof.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: דְּנַגְחֵיהּ תּוֹרָא דִידֵיהּ לְתוֹרָא דִידַן, פַּלְגָא מְשַׁלֵּם. וְאִידַּךְ פַּלְגָא — אָמַר לְהוּ: אַיְיתֵי רְאָיָה דְּלָאו יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲנָא, וְאֶתֵּן לְכוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, this *halakha* is **necessary only** in a case **where his ox gored our ox,** one belonging to a Jew. In that case, there is no question that **he pays half** the damage, which is the payment when an innocuous ox belonging to a Jew gores an ox belonging to a Jew. **And** with regard to **the other half,** which the owner of the gored ox is claiming, asserting that this person of uncertain status is a gentile and therefore liable to pay full damages, the owner of the ox that gored can **say to** the claimants: **Bring proof that I am not a Jew and I will give you** payment of the other half of the damages. It is with regard to that case that Reish Lakish ruled that in a case of uncertainty, the baby has the presumptive status of a Jew, and it is incumbent upon the claimant to prove otherwise.

Rav Papa then enumerates the practical applications of these distinctions. When the majority of the city is non-Jewish and the infant is presumed non-Jewish: *le-ha'achilo neveilot* — one may feed him non-*kosher* meat, use him as a *Shabbat goy*, and treat him in every active sense as a non-Jew. When the majority is Jewish and the infant is presumed Jewish: *le-hachazir lo aveidah* — one must return lost objects to him. The *nafka mina* between the fifty-fifty case (where one already treats him as *safek Yisrael* for *hachyasah*) and the clear majority case emerges specifically in monetary law. At fifty-fifty, the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav hara'aya* applies: since the claim to Jewish status has not been established by majority, one may say to him, "bring proof that you are Jewish," and in the absence of such proof, one is not obligated to return the lost object. Once there is a majority of Jews, however, no such demand is necessary.

The *halakha* assumes different rules for an ox goring depending on whether owner and victim are Jewish or non-Jewish. The Gemara works through the case: if our ox gored his ox and his status is uncertain (fifty-fifty), we can tell him, "bring proof that you are a Jew and then collect." If, however, his ox gored our ox — a *tam* (first-time gorer) — he pays *palga*, half damages, which is what he would owe under the assumption he is Jewish. But if we then demand he pay more (as we would if he were non-Jewish and therefore always liable in full), he can respond: "bring proof that I am not Jewish, and I will pay you more." The burden of proof operates symmetrically, cutting in both directions, always governed by the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav hara'aya*. The general rule holds throughout: we follow *rov* in all matters except *yichus*, where a double *rov* is demanded.

### Marriage Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you "A virgin is wedded."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה. הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: בְּתוּלָה נְשָׂאתַנִי, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא אַלְמָנָה נְשָׂאתִיךְ. אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁיָּצָאת בְּהִינוּמָא וְרֹאשָׁהּ פָּרוּעַ — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מָאתַיִם. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אַף חִילּוּק קְלָיוֹת רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who was widowed or divorced,** and is now claiming payment of her marriage contract that is not before the court, and **she says: You married me** as **a virgin,** who is entitled to two hundred dinars, **and he says: No; rather, I married you** as **a widow,** who is entitled to one hundred dinars, then, **if there are witnesses that she went out** of her father's house to her wedding **with a *hinnuma* or** with **her hair uncovered,** in a manner typical of virgins, payment of **her marriage contract is two hundred** dinars. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: Even** testimony that there was **distribution of roasted grain,** which was customary at weddings of virgins, constitutes **proof** that she is a virgin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: שָׂדֶה זוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיךָ הָיְתָה וּלְקַחְתִּיהָ הֵימֶנּוּ, שֶׁהוּא נֶאֱמָן,
>
> **Translation:** Several disputes between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua were cited previously with regard to the credibility accorded to the respective claims of parties to a dispute. Based on one of those disputes, the *tanna* adds: **And Rabbi Yehoshua concedes in** a case where **one says to another: This field,** which is currently in my possession, **belonged to your father and I purchased it from him, that he is deemed credible,** and his entire claim is accepted. The court accepts not only his admission that it once belonged to the other's father, but also his statement that he purchased it.

With *hadran alach Betula Nisat* — the conclusion of the chapter — the Gemara introduces the opening Mishnah of the next *perek*. The shift is significant. The previous chapter dealt with *ta'anat betulim* — the claim regarding virginity on the wedding night. The new Mishnah addresses a different question entirely: not whether the woman was a virgin when married, but whether the marriage was a first marriage or a second marriage, a distinction that determines whether her *ketubah* is two hundred *zuz* or one hundred. Suppose the couple is divorcing or the woman is widowed twenty years after the wedding, and neither party has preserved the original *ketubah*. She claims it was a first marriage (two hundred); he claims she was already a widow at the time of marriage (one hundred). How is this resolved?

### First Marriage Signs

The Mishnah identifies the evidentiary markers of a first wedding. If there are witnesses that she went out *be-hinuma* — in a wedding carriage — and that her head was uncovered (*rosho parua*), with her hair flowing down on her shoulders as Rashi describes, these are recognized signs that it was a first marriage. When such evidence exists, her *ketubah* is two hundred.

### Parched Grain Sign

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka adds a further sign: *af chiluk kalyot raya* — even the distribution of roasted or parched grain to the guests is sufficient evidence of a first wedding. This was a custom specific to first marriages, analogous perhaps to other celebratory practices that distinguish the first wedding from subsequent ones.

### Peh She'asar

The Mishnah concludes with the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua — presumably the same Rabbi Yehoshua from the previous *perek*, who consistently required external corroboration and refused to accept unilateral claims. Here, however, Rabbi Yehoshua concedes. The principle he invokes is *peh she'asar hu peh she'hitir* — the same mouth that created the prohibition is the mouth that permits it. The illustration is from monetary law: if one says to another, "this field was your father's and I purchased it from him," he is believed, because without his own statement we would have had no grounds to question his title to the field. The very mouth that introduced the claim of the other's father's ownership is the mouth that asserts the subsequent purchase — and therefore that mouth is trusted. This is the limiting principle: Rabbi Yehoshua does not believe a woman who makes a claim *stam* to extract money. But when the claimant's own statement is what generates the doubt in the first place, the same statement can resolve it. If witnesses establish that the field originally belonged to the father, however, then the man who admits ownership and claims to have purchased it is no longer in a *peh she'asar peh she'hitir* position — external evidence has created the doubt, and he is not believed. The Gemara will explore in the next session how *peh she'asar peh she'hitir* applies to the woman's claim in this new Mishnah.
