# Bava Batra 136b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Son's Sale After Death

### Intro: Father's Transfer

The Mishnah concludes with the case of *hakosav nechasav livno le'achar moso* — one who writes the title of his property to his son while retaining the *peirot* (fruits, i.e., usufruct) for himself during his lifetime. As the Rashbam notes, the motivation may be to shield assets from a second wife and her children, or any number of similar concerns. Whatever the underlying purpose, the legal structure is clear: the father transfers title but continues to enjoy the benefits of the property. The Mishnah teaches that no one can sell more than he possesses. If the father sells the property, he has sold only his right to the *peirot* for the duration of his life — the underlying title is no longer his to convey. If the son sells the property, the purchaser acquires nothing until the father dies, since the father's right to the *peirot* remains intact in the interim.

### Central Question

The *gemara* now raises a sharper question: suppose the son sold the property while the father was alive — transferring to Reuven whatever title he possessed — and then the son predeceased the father. When the father subsequently dies, does Reuven acquire the property?

On first reflection, the answer seems obvious. The son sold Reuven his title to the property, subject to the father's right to *peirot* until death. Reuven accepted that limitation and took the risk that the chain of events would unfold favorably. Even if the son is no longer alive, Reuven already acquired the title, and once the father's *peirot* rights terminate at death, Reuven's ownership should fully actualize. But the *gemara* is not so certain.

### R. Yochanan Opinion

Rabbi Yochanan says: *lo kanah lokeach* — Reuven acquires nothing.

### Reish Lakish Opinion

Reish Lakish says: *kanah lokeach* — Reuven does acquire and gains full ownership of the property.

### R. Yochanan Explains

Rabbi Yochanan's position rests on the principle of *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf dami* — the right to the fruits of a property is considered equivalent to ownership of the body of the property itself. Here is the structure: the father transferred the *kinyan haguf* (title) to the son but retained the *kinyan peirot* (the right to all practical benefit). If we say that *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf*, then it is the father who is effectively perceived as the real-world owner, even though the son holds technical title. The father is not the registered owner, but he is the functional owner — the one with actual control and benefit.

From this perspective, the son, though technically holding title, has no actualized ownership. What he possesses is a future interest — a right that will materialize only when the father dies and the *peirot* rights extinguish. When the son purports to sell to Reuven, he is not transferring actual ownership of property; he is, at most, transferring his contingent stake — essentially saying: "You will receive this through me, when I receive it." If the son dies before that moment arrives, he never receives it, and there is nothing to pass through to Reuven. The sale therefore cannot take effect.

It is worth pausing to note, as the Rashba makes explicit, that the son's technical title does carry some real consequences. A second wife the father marries would have no lien on the property, since it is no longer legally registered in the father's name. And if the son were to die without having sold the property, it would pass to the son's own heirs — because it *is* registered in the son's name. The distinction that *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf* draws is more nuanced: the son has title in the registered sense, but he lacks the powers and status of an *owner* in the functional sense. He cannot exercise dominion over it, cannot transfer it as a true owner would. One might frame it this way: under whose name is the property registered? The son's. But who holds the owner's practical power and role? The father. Since the son lacks the owner's role, his purported sale transfers only his contingent future interest — and if he predeceases the father, that interest evaporates.

### Reish Lakish Explains

Reish Lakish holds *kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — the right to *peirot* is not equivalent to ownership of the body of the property. On this view, the father's retention of the *peirot* does not elevate him to the status of owner; the son is the genuine owner. When the son sold Reuven the property, he conveyed actual title. Reuven takes subject to the father's *peirot* rights, which will terminate at the father's death. At that point, Reuven's ownership — already fully acquired — is no longer encumbered, and he possesses the property outright. The son's death is irrelevant: he had already transferred ownership, and ownership once transferred does not revert simply because the seller is no longer alive.

## Kinyan Peirot Debate

### Case: Field for Fruit

The *gemara* questions why Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish needed to articulate their dispute here. This is, after all, a well-known debate between them — *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf* or not — that was already stated in another context. Specifically, the prior formulation of the debate involves the case of *hamocher sadeh l'peirot*: one who sells his field not outright but for the sake of its fruits. The seller retains the title (*kinyan haguf*) while the buyer, call him Michael, acquires the right to all benefit from the land — its usufruct. That prior case sufficiently establishes the general dispute. Why restate it in the context of the father-son transfer?

### Bikkurim: R. Yochanan

In the *hamocher sadeh l'peirot* case, the *gemara* had recorded that Rabbi Yochanan says Michael *meivi v'korei* — he must bring his first fruits (*bikkurim*) to the Temple and recite the declaration of *mikra bikkurim*, including the phrase *al ha'adama she'nasata li*, "the land which You gave me." Even though Michael does not hold title, since *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf dami*, Michael's ownership of the *peirot* is tantamount to ownership of the land itself. He is therefore treated as the owner of the land for purposes of *bikkurim* and can truthfully declare it as his land.

### Bikkurim: Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish, who holds *kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf dami*, says Michael *meivi v'eino korei* — he brings the *bikkurim* but does not recite the declaration. Since the *kinyan peirot* does not make him the owner of the land, he cannot say *al ha'adama she'nasata li*. The Rashbam and Tosafot debate the basis for the obligation to bring at all under Reish Lakish's view: the Rashbam suggests there is an intermediate level at which Michael's connection to the land suffices to require bringing, though not to permit the declaration; Tosafot holds that the obligation to bring in this case is only rabbinic, since without *kinyan haguf* there is no biblical basis to treat it as his land.

### R. Yochanan Clarifies

Despite this prior statement of the dispute, Rabbi Yochanan explains why he needed to restate his position in the father-son case. Even granting that he generally holds *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf*, one might have thought that in the specific context of a father giving property to his son, the father would be *mochel* — would willingly relinquish — his status as effective owner, deferring to his son. The logic would be: since the son will ultimately receive everything when the father dies anyway, perhaps the father does not insist on retaining the functional title of owner. He is willing to let the son step into that role even while the father still holds the *kinyan peirot*.

This is a genuinely difficult point to articulate. The father's *kinyan peirot* is a real legal right — not a mere social preference — so it is not obvious what exactly he would be relinquishing by being *mochel*. One cannot simply confer the "title of owner" as though it were a separate transferable thing; it would seem to be a natural outgrowth of legal ownership, not something independently conferred. Nevertheless, the concern was real: perhaps when it is one's own son, the father's willingness to be *mochel* effectively means he no longer insists on being perceived as the functional owner, and therefore the son's ownership would be more substantial. Rabbi Yochanan therefore needs to state that even in the father-son case, the *kinyan peirot* is *k'kinyan haguf*, and this does not change merely because the relationship is familial. The father retains his functional status as owner regardless.

### Reish Lakish Clarifies

Reish Lakish similarly explains why he needed to restate his position. Even granting that he holds *kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf* in general, one might have thought that a distinct principle applies when the original owner divests himself partially: whichever element of ownership the original owner retains, he remains considered the owner. Since the original owner started with everything, any portion he keeps should keep him identified as the owner. In the *bikkurim* case, where I sell the *kinyan peirot* to Michael and retain title, I should still be considered the owner — not because of the general rule that *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf*, but simply because I started with full ownership and never completely divested myself. On this reasoning, Michael is not the owner precisely because the original owner retained something.

But one might then think the reverse also follows: when the father retains the *kinyan peirot* and gives the son the *kinyan haguf*, perhaps the father should still be considered the owner, again because whoever retains any element of original ownership remains the owner. Reish Lakish therefore needs to clarify that the identity of "owner" follows the person who holds the *kinyan haguf* — full stop. It does not depend on who held everything originally. If I sell you the *kinyan peirot* and retain the *guf*, I am the owner. If I give over the *guf* and retain only the *peirot*, the one who holds the *guf* is the owner. The principle is consistent and directional: ownership is identified with the *guf*, regardless of the original starting position. Together, the two cases establish that Reish Lakish's view is a general principle and not an artifact of the seller-retains-something scenario.

## Conditional Inheritance

### Gift Sequence Formula

The *gemara* now introduces the formula *nechasai lecha ve'acharecha yirash ploni ve'acharav yirash ploni* — "my property is yours, and after you, so-and-so shall inherit, and after him, so-and-so shall inherit." This formula creates a cascading chain of ownership. If everyone dies in the proper sequence — if the first recipient dies, the second acquires; if the second dies, the third acquires — the arrangement works neatly. The *gemara* notes this explicitly: *meis rishon kana sheni, meis sheni kana shlishi*.

Understanding the mechanism of such a chain requires some care. How does the original giver control the disposition of property after he himself is dead? Once property is transferred to Reuven, it is Reuven's property — ordinarily, the giver cannot dictate where it goes after Reuven's death. One might think of this as some form of *kinyan le-zman* (a time-limited acquisition) or a *tenai* (conditional gift), but both of those frameworks raise their own difficulties. The most intuitive conceptualization, prior to the *gemara*'s clarification, would be that the *guf* was actually transferred to the final recipient — Levi — while the intermediate parties received only *kinyan peirot* for the duration of their lives. On this model, Reuven and Shimon have the usufruct and Levi has the title, and the whole dispute about *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf* becomes directly relevant to this chain.

### Chain Breaking Question

A problem arises: what if Shimon, the second in line, dies before Reuven, the first? Shimon never received the property from Reuven, so Levi cannot receive it through Shimon. The chain breaks. To whom does the property now belong?

If we operated on the initial conceptualization — where Levi holds the *kinyan haguf* and Reuven holds only the *kinyan peirot* — then under Rabbi Yochanan's view (*kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf*), Reuven functions as the virtual owner of the *guf*, and since the chain cannot continue to Levi, the virtual title would revert and effectively consolidate in Reuven. It would then pass to Reuven's heirs upon his death. Under Reish Lakish's view (*kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf*), Reuven's *peirot* right is not *kinyan haguf*, so Reuven is never the owner; Levi, as the holder of the *kinyan haguf*, is the owner, and the property should go to Levi even when the chain breaks. But if the conditions under which Levi was to receive it cannot be met, one could argue instead that the property should revert to the original giver.

The *gemara* raises this latter possibility as a challenge: the *gemara* notes *leyorshei noten mibaei lei* — if Reuven is not an owner (under Reish Lakish's view), and the proper recipient cannot receive, should the property not revert to the original giver's heirs rather than Reuven's?

### Acharecha Mechanism

The response, transmitted in the name of Rav Oshaya in Bavel and confirmed by Rami bar Chama before Rava, is: *acharecha shani* — the word *acharecha* is categorically different. The use of the word *acharecha* ("after you"), particularly in conjunction with *yirash* ("shall inherit"), fundamentally reconceives the nature of the transfer. It is not a case of *guf* to Levi and *peirot* to Reuven and Shimon. Rather, the word *acharecha* means that each party receives full and complete ownership — *guf* and *peirot* together — for the duration of his life, and only thereafter does the full ownership transfer completely to the next person.

This is, as the Rashbam emphasizes, the force of the word *yirash*: the next party *inherits* — receives complete ownership — at the moment the prior party's ownership ends. Whatever the precise legal mechanism by which the original giver can structure this chain (and the *gemara* does not fully articulate it), the outcome is that the formula *acharecha* confers genuine, complete ownership on each person in sequence. Reuven holds the property fully. Shimon is to hold it fully after Reuven. Levi is to hold it fully after Shimon.

With this understanding, the question of what happens when Shimon predeceases Reuven resolves differently. Reuven had full ownership. Shimon, who would have been the next full owner, died without receiving. Since the chain cannot continue through Shimon to Levi, the property simply remains in the full ownership of Reuven and passes, upon Reuven's death, to Reuven's heirs. There is no reversion to the original giver, because Reuven was a genuine owner — not a mere usufructuary — and full owners pass their property to their heirs. The *gemara* acknowledges that not everyone accepts *acharecha shani*, and this is indeed a dispute among *tannaim*, as the subsequent *baraita* will demonstrate.

## First Heir's Power

### Baraita: Sale Effect

A *baraita* is cited: in a case of *nechasai lach ve'acharecha l'ploni* — the property is given to Rachel with the instruction that after her, it goes to Sarah — the first recipient sold the property. What is the legal effect? Does the second heir, Sarah, reclaim the property from the purchaser, or is Sarah left with nothing?

The *gemara* notes that a second *baraita* appears to state the direct opposite ruling, creating an apparent contradiction: *kashya deRabbi aDeRabbi u'deRabban Shimon ben Gamliel aDeRabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. Both Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel seem to contradict themselves across the two *baraitot*.

### Tannaic Debate

In the first *baraita*, Rabbi says *hasheni motzi miyad halakochot* — Sarah can reclaim the property from the purchaser. Rachel was not a full owner with the power to sell the underlying property away from Sarah; her sale of the *guf* is therefore void with respect to Sarah's entitlement. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *ein lashen ela ma sheshiyer rishon* — Sarah receives only what Rachel chose to leave over for her. If Rachel sold the property, the sale is valid and Sarah receives nothing.

In the second *baraita*, the positions appear reversed: Rabbi permits the first recipient to sell and benefit, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel restricts the first recipient to nothing more than the *achilas peirot* — the right to consume the fruit.

### Resolution: Fruit vs. La…

The *gemara* resolves the internal contradiction for each sage. For Rabbi: the two statements are not contradictory — *ha begufa, ha bepeiros*. When Rabbi ruled that Rachel could sell freely, he meant she could sell the *peirot*, the fruits of her entitlement, which she genuinely owns. When Rabbi ruled that Sarah could reclaim the property from purchasers, that was a case where Rachel had attempted to sell the *guf* itself, the underlying property — which Rachel, under Rabbi's view, lacked the power to convey away from Sarah.

### Rabban Shimon View

For Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the *gemara* resolves the contradiction as follows: *ha l'chatchila, ha di'eved* — one ruling addresses the permissible norm, the other the post-facto outcome. *L'chatchila*, Rachel should not sell the underlying *guf*; that would contravene the intention of the original giver who wanted Sarah to receive it. But if Rachel went ahead and sold it *di'eved* — after the fact — the sale takes effect and Sarah is left with nothing. How can this be? Because *acharecha shani*: the word *acharecha* transferred genuine, full ownership to Rachel. Sarah's entitlement exists, but it has not yet actualized — the original giver divested himself of the property entirely, and no one is currently standing as its owner in a way that blocks Rachel's power. Since Rachel holds the full *guf* by virtue of *acharecha shani*, and since there is no explicit stipulation in the gift that would invalidate a sale, Rachel's sale — though contravening the giver's wishes — is legally operative. It was not the most upright thing to do. No one would award Rachel a gold star. But there was no explicit condition that would render the transaction void.

The distinction from the father-son case discussed at the outset is instructive. There, the father *retained* the property and transferred only the title; since he had not fully divested himself, no one could step in and claim uncontested ownership. Here, the giver divested himself entirely, Sarah's ownership has not yet actualized, and Rachel holds what amounts to the only fully actualized title. This creates the space for Rachel to exercise full ownership rights — including the ability to sell.

## Ethics & Wickedness

### Advisor is Rasha

The *gemara* now turns to the ethical dimension of this arrangement. Abaye asks: *eizehu rasha arum* — who is the scheming wicked person? It is *ze hamashi eitza limkor benechasim k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the one who advises Rachel to sell the property, invoking the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel to enable her to break the chain and prevent Sarah from ever receiving the property.

### First Heir's Motivation

Remarkably, Rachel herself is not deemed the *rasha*. Even though she is the one who actually carries out the sale that deprives Sarah of her entitlement, the *gemara* declines to apply that designation to her. The reason is that Rachel acts out of self-interest: she is protecting her own family, benefiting her own children, doing what people naturally do when they look after their own. She has a legal right to do it. It may not reflect the highest standard of *yoshrus* (ethical rectitude), and we would not commend it, but acting within one's legal rights out of self-interest does not make one a *rasha*.

### Advisor's Pure Harm

The *rasha* is the external advisor — the one who has no personal stake in the transaction and whose only contribution is to walk up to Rachel and say: "Did you know that according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, you can sell this property a moment before you die, break the chain, and leave Sarah with nothing?" This person stands to gain nothing. The only effect of his counsel is to harm Sarah. It is this quality — intervening in a dispute that is not one's own, causing harm to another party with no benefit to oneself — that the *gemara* identifies as the hallmark of the *rasha arum*. The *gemara* elsewhere captures a related idea with the phrase *over al riv lo lo* — one who inserts himself into a quarrel that does not belong to him. There is something deeply troubling about deploying one's knowledge of Torah law not to protect a right one holds, but to arm someone else against a third party whom one has no reason to harm. Paradoxically, it is precisely *because* the advisor acts without personal self-interest that he is more culpable, not less: he cannot even claim the partial justification of looking after his own needs.

### Halachic Ruling

The *gemara* records that Rav Yochanan rules *halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: the first heir may indeed sell the underlying property and thereby break the chain. The Rashbam notes that this ruling simultaneously reflects the position that *kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — and that the operative mechanism enabling Rachel to sell is *acharecha shani*, the word *acharecha* having conferred upon her genuine and complete ownership of the *guf*.

## Matnat Shachiv Mera

### Competing Claims

The *gemara* now introduces a closely related scenario that carries significant practical implications. In the same *acharecha* framework, rather than selling the property to an outside purchaser during her lifetime, Rachel makes a *matnat shachiv mera* — a deathbed gift — conveying the property to a third party, Devorah. The question is: when Rachel dies, does the property go to Devorah by virtue of the *matnat shachiv mera*, or does it go to Sarah by virtue of the *acharecha* formula?

Both claims purport to take effect at or upon Rachel's death. This is the tension.

### Timing Distinction

Abaye rules: *matnat shachiv mera lo kanah ela l'achar mita, v'kodam acharecha* — the *matnat shachiv mera* takes effect only after death, and the *acharecha* transfer has already preceded it. The property goes to Sarah. Devorah's *matnat shachiv mera* does not override the *acharecha*.

### Moment vs. After

This ruling requires an important conceptual distinction. Did both transfers not take effect "after Rachel's death"? How can one precede the other? Abaye draws the line between *im gmar mita* — at the very moment of death — and *l'achar mita* — after death. The *acharecha* formula takes effect *im gmar mita*, at the precise moment Rachel ceases to be alive. The *matnat shachiv mera*, by contrast, takes effect *l'achar mita*, after that moment has passed.

Why should *acharecha* be read as occurring at the moment of death rather than after? Because *acharecha* means "after you" — after you are no longer among the living. At the instant of death, Rachel is no longer the subject "you"; the moment the living state ends, *after you* has arrived. By contrast, *l'achar mita* — the framework for both *matnat shachiv mera* and ordinary *yerusha* — refers to after the death has already occurred, a beat later. One can visualize it as: here is Rachel alive, here is the moment of death, and here is the moment after death. *Acharecha* occupies the threshold moment itself — the transition from alive to dead. *L'achar mita* occupies the moment after that threshold has been crossed. Sarah's entitlement under *acharecha* attaches at the threshold; Devorah's *matnat shachiv mera* attaches only once the threshold has been passed. Sarah therefore takes priority.

### Deathbed Gift Timing

The *gemara* immediately questions whether Abaye himself actually held this position consistently. A *baraita* records a dispute: *matnat shachiv mera — aimasai kanah?* — when does a deathbed gift take effect? Abaye says *im gmar mita* — at the moment of death. Rava says *l'achar gmar mita* — after the moment of death.

This internal tension within Abaye's position creates a difficulty. If Abaye holds that *matnat shachiv mera* takes effect *im gmar mita* — at the very moment of death — then Devorah's claim through the *matnat shachiv mera* and Sarah's claim through *acharecha* would be simultaneous, arising at precisely the same moment. The earlier ruling, in which Abaye seemed to say the *acharecha* precedes the *matnat shachiv mera*, would be undermined. Whether Abaye can reconcile these two positions, and what the practical *nafka mina* between *im gmar mita* and *l'achar gmar mita* would be in other cases — whether, for instance, a *matnat shachiv mera* takes effect at the same moment as an ordinary *yerusha* or a beat after it — is where the *gemara* will continue.
