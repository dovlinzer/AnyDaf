# Shabbat 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Poletet Shichvat Zera

### Three Topics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנַּיִן לְפוֹלֶטֶת שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁתְּהֵא טְמֵאָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֱיוּ נְכֹנִים לִשְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים״. מִנַּיִן שֶׁמַּרְחִיצִין אֶת הַמִּילָה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיוֹתָם כֹּאֲבִים״. מִנַּיִן שֶׁקּוֹשְׁרִין לָשׁוֹן שֶׁל זְהוֹרִית בְּרֹאשׁ שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם יִהְיוּ חֲטָאֵיכֶם כַּשָּׁנִים כַּשֶּׁלֶג יַלְבִּינוּ״. מִנַּיִן לְסִיכָה שֶׁהִיא כִּשְׁתִיָּיה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים? אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתָּבֹא כַמַּיִם בְּקִרְבּוֹ וְכַשֶּׁמֶן בְּעַצְמוֹתָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues to cite a series of unrelated *halakhot* based upon biblical allusions. **From where** is it derived **that** a woman who **discharges semen** even **on the third day** after relations **is ritually impure,** just like one who touches semen (see Leviticus 15:17)? Because the semen remains fit for insemination, it can transmit impurity, **as it is stated** prior to the revelation at Sinai: "And he said to the people, **prepare yourselves for three days,** do not approach a woman" (Exodus 19:15). This three-day separation period ensured that even a woman who discharged semen would be pure. The mishna cites another *halakha* based on a biblical allusion: **From where** is it derived **that one may wash the circumcision on the third day,** meaning the third day after the circumcision, even if **it occurs on Shabbat? As it is stated: "And it came to pass on the third day when they were in pain"** (Genesis 34:25). The pain of circumcision lasts at least three days, and as long as the child is in pain he is considered to be in danger. The mishna cites another *halakha* with an allusion in the Bible: **From where** is it derived **that one ties a scarlet strip** of wool **to the head of** the **scapegoat** that is dispatched to Azazel? **As it is stated: "If your sins be like scarlet, they will become white like snow"** (Isaiah 1:18). Since the goat is offered to atone for sins, red wool is tied to its horns. The mishna cites another allusion. **From where** is it derived **that smearing** oil on one's body **is like drinking** and is similarly prohibited **on Yom Kippur? Although there is no proof for this, there is an allusion to it, as it is stated: "And it comes into his inward parts like water and like oil into his bones"** (Psalms 109:18). The verse appears to equate smearing oil on one's body with drinking water.

Daf 86 opens a new *mishna* — or rather, what the editors of the printed Gemara have collected as a single *mishna* — that is, in truth, a compilation of three entirely unrelated topics. The *perek* as a whole operates this way: each *mishna* addresses a distinct subject, most of them deriving a practical law from a scriptural verse. We are moving here from questions of *tumah* and *keilim* to territory that begins with *tahara* but quickly opens into an entirely different discussion.

The three topics bundled together are: (1) the case of a woman who expels semen from her body on the third day after intercourse and her status of *tumah*; (2) the permissibility of bathing a circumcised infant on the third day even when that day falls on Shabbat; and (3) the tying of a crimson thread on the head of the *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach* — the scapegoat — on Yom Kippur. The *mishna* appends to this a further Yom Kippur law: that anointing the body (*sichah*) is treated like drinking (*shtiyah*) for purposes of the Yom Kippur prohibitions.

The source for the red thread is the verse *im yihyu chata'eichem kashanim kasheleg yalbinu* — "if your sins are like crimson, they shall become white as snow" — the logic being that since the ritual is one of expiation, one wishes to see the red visibly turn to white. The sourcing for *sichah kishtiyah* rests on the verse *vatava kamayim bekirbo vechashemen be'atzmotav* — "it shall come like water into his innards and like oil into his bones" — thereby comparing anointing to drinking in the way both are absorbed by the body. The *mishna* itself notes *af al pi she'ein ra'aya ledavar zecher ledavar* — that while there is no full proof, there is at least an allusion. It is worth noting that the *mishna* is not entirely consistent in deploying this phrase: some proofs that appear equally weak are simply introduced with *she'ne'emar*, while others that seem comparably tenuous receive the more modest framing of *zecher ledavar*. The pattern does not fully resolve.

Our focus for this daf, however, is entirely on the first topic: *poletet shichvat zera*.

### Day Three Impurity

The *mishna* asks: *mina'in lapoletet shichvat zera bayom hashlishi she'hi tmeia* — from where do we know that a woman who expels semen from her body on the third day after intercourse is *tmeia*? The source cited is the verse *hayu nechonim lishloshet yamim* — "be prepared for three days" — followed by the command *al tigshu el isha* — "do not draw near to a woman." The occasion is, of course, the preparation for receiving the Torah at *ma'amad Har Sinai*.

The logic works as follows: if God commanded a three-day separation before the giving of the Torah, and if we understand this to mean that the Torah was given on the *fourth* day, then the concern that motivated the separation must have extended through day three. That concern, as the Gemara understands it, was the *tumah* of a woman who might still be expelling semen. Hence, even on day three, a woman remains *tmeia* as a *poletet shichvat zera*.

### Three-Day Separation

This at once raises a textual question that will occupy most of the daf: does *lishloshet yamim* mean "for three days" — i.e., prepare for three full days, after which the Torah is given on day four — or does it mean "for the third day" — i.e., be ready *by* day three, which is when the Torah is given? The *mishna* assumes the former reading, and we will see this assumption heavily contested in the *gemara* that follows.

### Women at Sinai

The plain sense of the verses is striking. God instructs Moshe to prepare *Bnei Yisrael* for the giving of the Torah and says *al tigshu el isha* — "do not draw near to a woman." On the surface, this seems to be addressed exclusively to men, and the Torah is being given, so to speak, to men. Indeed, the whole framing of the verses addresses men.

And yet *Chazal*, in more than one place, underscore that women were very much part of *ma'amad Har Sinai*. The famous *drasha* on the verse *koh tomar l'veit Ya'akov v'tageid livnei Yisrael* distinguishes the two verbs: *tageid*, rooted in *gid* — sinew, something hard — refers to the men, while *tomar*, a softer form of address, refers to *elu hanashim* — the women. This is also the source for the name *Beis Ya'akov*. The very institution of women's Torah education draws on this *drasha* of *Chazal*.

Relevant to our *mishna*, *Chazal* explain that the three-day separation was not instituted for the sake of the men. The men who had a seminal emission were *tamei* for one day — they could immerse in a *mikvah* and be *tahor* by nightfall. So why three days? The answer, as the *gemara* understands it, is that the separation was required so that the women would no longer be in a state of *poletet shichvat zera*. That is why the men were prohibited from having intercourse: so that the women's bodies would have sufficient time to clear the *tumah* arising from the act. Since a woman may continue to expel semen for days after intercourse, and since that expelled semen renders her *tmeia*, the three-day (or four-day) separation ensured that by the time the Torah was given, the women would be *tehorot* and fit to stand at *Har Sinai*.

This is a remarkable reading. The stated reason for the commandment *al tigshu el isha* — addressed to men — turns out to be entirely for the sake of the women. The men are a secondary concern; the women are the primary beneficiaries of the preparation. Far from being excluded from *Sinai*, the women were the reason the separation was mandated at all.

### Sex & Tumah

Before turning to the debate among *tanna'im* about exactly when *poletet* status ends, one must address a threshold difficulty: at the time of *Matan Torah*, no *halachot* had yet been given. There was no such reality as *tumah* before the Torah was received. Moreover, even if one accepts that it was somehow operative in advance, there are many forms of *tumah* that are far weightier than *poletet shichvat zera* — *tumas meis*, *zav*, *niddah* — seven-day *tumot* requiring elaborate processes of purification. Why was this particular form of *tumah* the operative concern?

This question is addressed not in our *gemara* but in the *gemara* in *Berachot*, where the *takkanah* of Ezra prohibiting a *ba'al keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission — from learning Torah or *davvening* is discussed at length. At its core, the reasoning there is rooted in the verse *hoda'atam levanecha u'vney vanecha* paired with the verse *yom asher amadta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev* — comparing all future Torah learning to the experience of *ma'amad Har Sinai*, which was conducted *be-eimah u've-yirah u've-retet u've-zei'ah* — in awe, in fear, in trembling, and in a sweat. It follows from this that the *ba'al keri* is in a state incompatible with that awe — not because the act itself is forbidden or shameful, but because the state of having just engaged in intercourse represents a fundamentally different *mindset*, one not consonant with standing before the divine presence in that posture of complete awe and trepidation.

Significantly, the *gemara* in *Berachot* rules that *zavim*, *metzora'im*, *bo'alei niddot*, and women who are *niddot* are all *mutarim likrot ba-Torah ba-Nevi'im u-va-Ketuvim u-lishnot ba-Mishnah u-va-Midrash u-va-Halachot u-va-Aggadot* — they may learn all of Torah, written and oral, without restriction. Only the *ba'al keri* is restricted. The reasoning offered is *d'machmat kalut rosh hu ba* — that this state arises from a kind of lightness, a frame of mind fundamentally at odds with the gravity of Torah engagement. It is not, then, a matter of abstract metaphysical *tumah* being more or less severe; it is a question of what *kind* of experience creates the incompatibility with Torah.

This broader framing is important. The *gemara*'s concern here should not be read as expressing a negative attitude toward marital relations as such. The *gemara* elsewhere — notably in the context of *birchat eirusin* and *birchat nisu'in* in *Ketuvot* — frames the union of husband and wife in terms of *kedushah*: *assar lanu et ha-arusos ve-hitir lanu et ha-nesu'ot*, to acknowledge that the divine will is for husband and wife to come together in this way. The *berachah* before the act, as it were, elevates it from the merely physical, framing the intimacy within a context of divine sanction and holiness. That this same experience, however holy in its proper context, creates a kind of *transition state* incompatible with the immediate engagement in Torah — this is the point the *gemara* is making. The transition is formalized through immersion in the *mikvah*, which changes one's *tumah* status and marks the shift from one mode of experience to another.

So the *tumah* of *poletet shichvat zera* becomes, by parallel logic, the female counterpart of the male *ba'al keri*. The concern at *Sinai* was not about *niddah* or *metzora* or *zav* — weightier as those *tumot* are in other respects — but specifically about the *tumah* arising from the sexual act, extending as it does in the woman's body for days afterward.

### Tosafot's Logic

*Tosafot* addresses the difficulty at the top of the page: *af al pi she-be-otah sha'ah adayin lo nitnah Torah* — even though at that time the Torah had not yet been given — *mikol makom nistamcha Torah al davar she-atid lihyot tumah achar matan Torah* — nonetheless, the Torah rested upon something that would become *tumah* after *Matan Torah*. The logic, similar to other cases of anticipatory legal categories (as with Noach and the clean and unclean animals), is that the future halakhic significance was operative even in advance of the formal giving of the law.

*Tosafot* further engages the question of why *poletet shichvat zera* and not the other, weightier *tumot*: *af al gav d-lo hikpidah al zavim u-metzora'in u-bo'alei niddot* — even though the Torah at *Sinai* was not concerned with *zav*, *metzora*, or *bo'el niddah*, all of which are more serious *tumot* — the reason is precisely the one derived from the *gemara* in *Berachot*: it is the connection to the act of intercourse specifically, and the incompatibility of that mindset with the awe of *ma'amad Har Sinai*, that singled out this *tumah* as the operative concern. The other *tumot*, however grave, do not carry that particular association.

## Tannaitic Debate

### Day Three Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רֵישָׁא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, סֵיפָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה? דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה — טְהוֹרָה שְׁמַעְנָא לֵיהּ. מַאן דְּלָא מוֹקֵי כְּתַנָּאֵי, תָּנֵא רֵישָׁא ״טְהוֹרָה״, וּמוֹקֵי לַהּ לְכוּלַּהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, וּמַאן דְּמוֹקִים כְּתַנָּאֵי, רֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן וְסֵיפָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments on the *halakhot* cited in the mishna: **The first clause** in the mishna with regard to discharged semen **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. The latter clause** in the mishna with regard to circumcision **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. As, if** one was to assert that the first clause is also **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, we heard him** say that in that case the woman is **ritually pure.** The Gemara explains: **He who does not establish** the mishna as reflecting the opinions of two ***tanna'im*,** has a variant reading of the mishna; he **teaches** the *halakha* in **the first clause:** The woman is **ritually pure, and establishes the entire** mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. And he who establishes** the mishna as reflecting the opinions of two ***tanna'im*,** holds that the **first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and the latter clause is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

With this background established, the *gemara* turns to the internal structure of the *mishna*. The *gemara* notes: *reisha d'lo k'Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya* — the opening of the *mishna*, which says a woman is *tmeia* on day three, does not accord with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. A *braita* — actually a *mishna* in *Mikvaot* (8:3), though the *gemara* here attributes it to a *Tosefta* of *Mikvaot* — presents Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's position: *poletet shichvat zera bayom hashlishi tehora* — a woman who expels semen on day three is *tehora*. Day three is not a problem; only days one and two are.

At the same time, the *seifa* of our *mishna* — the ruling that one may bathe an infant on the third day after circumcision even if that day falls on Shabbat — does align with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who is precisely the *tanna* who rules that one may perform such acts for the infant's sake on day three after *milah*.

### Textual Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פּוֹלֶטֶת שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בְּיוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי טְהוֹרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע עוֹנוֹת, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהֵן חָמֵשׁ עוֹנוֹת, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ עוֹנוֹת. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם חָמֵשׁ, וְאִם יָצָאתָה מִקְצָת עוֹנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, נוֹתְנִין לָהּ מִקְצָת עוֹנָה שִׁשִּׁית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the matter of a woman who discharges semen, citing that which **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: A woman who **discharges semen** at any point **on the third day is ritually pure;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. Rabbi Yishmael says:** Even on the third day she is ritually impure. In addition, the only relevant tally is the number of days. The number of twelve-hour periods of night and day that passed is not relevant. **Sometimes there are four** twelve-hour **periods** that elapsed between cohabitation and discharge. If she had relations just before nightfall on Wednesday and discharged on Friday night at the beginning of Shabbat, it is considered as if three days passed, meaning Wednesday, Thursday and Friday; and four twelve-hour periods, meaning Wednesday night, Thursday day, Thursday night and Friday day. **Sometimes there are five** twelve-hour **periods** that elapsed, in a case where she discharged semen at the end of Friday night. **Sometimes there are six** twelve-hour **periods** that elapsed, if she had relations at the beginning of Tuesday night and discharged at the end of the third day. **Rabbi Akiva says:** The *halakha* is that it is **always five** twelve-hour **periods. And if** she had relations after **part of the first period passed, she is given part of the sixth period** to complete the requisite five twelve-hour periods, so that sixty hours will have elapsed between cohabitation and discharge.

This creates an internal tension in the *mishna*: the opening goes against Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya and the conclusion goes with him. The *gemara* addresses this by presenting two methodological approaches.

The first approach — *man d'lo moki k'tana'ei* — holds that one should not attribute consecutive lines of a *mishna* to different *tanna'im* without explicit indication. If the *mishna* reads as a unified whole, it should express a single consistent opinion. For those who take this approach, the solution is to emend the text: read the opening as *tehora* rather than *tmeia*, and the entire *mishna* aligns with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. *Tosafot* notes that this would not be an arbitrary emendation — there were apparently manuscript versions in circulation that did read *tehora*, and those who preferred the unified reading would simply adopt that textual tradition.

The second approach — *man d'mooki k'tanna'ei* — has no objection to a *mishna* reflecting different *tanna'im* in consecutive clauses without attribution. For those who take this approach, *reisha Rabbanan v'seifa Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya*: the first part, holding that day three is still *tmeia*, represents the majority Rabbinic view, while the end, permitting bathing on day three, represents Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's position.

### Three Positions

This sets up the formal presentation of the *tannaitic* debate, which is the subject of the *braita* that follows. There are three distinct positions on when *poletet* status ends: Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya holds it ends on day three; Rabbi Ishmael and the *Rabbanan* hold it ends on day four; and Rabbi Akiva calculates not by calendar day but by the passage of sixty hours — five *onot*.

### Rabbi Elazar Day 3

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: *poletet shichvat zera bayom hashlishi tehora* — on day three, the woman is *tehora*. He holds, as will emerge from the *gemara*'s subsequent discussion, that the Israelites separated from their wives on Thursday before *Matan Torah*. Thursday, Friday, and Shabbat: the Torah is received on day three, and since it was given to women who were *tehorot*, day three cannot be a problem. Hence, Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya concludes that already on day three the semen has lost its potency and the woman is *tehorah*.

### Day Four Position

Rabbi Ishmael, whose position the *gemara* treats as identical to that of the *Rabbanan* in our *mishna*, holds that the relevant marker is not the number of elapsed hours but the transition to the fourth calendar day. A woman remains *tmeia* on day three but is *tehorah* on day four, whenever that arrives.

Rabbi Ishmael's language, as recorded in the *braita*, is: *pe'amim shehen arba onot, pe'amim shehen chamesh onot, pe'amim shehen shesh onot* — sometimes four *onot*, sometimes five, sometimes six. An *onah* is a twelve-hour half-day unit — either from sunrise to sunset or from sunset to sunrise. The point is that "day four" does not represent a fixed quantum of elapsed time. If a woman had intercourse at the very end of Sunday, just before sunset, then Sunday night counts as the first *onah*, Monday day as the second, Monday night as the third, and Tuesday day is still day three — she becomes *tehorah* on Tuesday night, which is halachically Wednesday, day four. That would be only four *onot*. Whereas if intercourse occurred near the beginning of Saturday night — halachically the start of Sunday — then day four begins only on Wednesday, after six *onot* have elapsed. The point is that the day-count, not the hour-count, is determinative for Rabbi Ishmael.

### Rabbi Akiva 60 Hours

Rabbi Akiva disagrees and measures by elapsed time, not by calendar day. He says *le'olam hei* — always five *onot*, sixty hours. Moreover, he is precise: *v'im yatza miktzas onah rishonah notnin lah miktzas onah shishit* — if only part of the first *onah* elapsed before intercourse began, one gives her a corresponding partial *onah* at the end. In other words, the measure is strictly sixty hours from the moment of intercourse, not from the beginning of whatever *onah* was in progress at the time.

The biological rationale underlying all three positions — explicit in Rabbi Akiva's formulation — is that semen loses its potency after a certain period of time. Once it can no longer fertilize, it is no longer halakhically classified as *shichvat zera*, and a woman who expels it is no longer a *polelet*. Rabbi Akiva's position has the advantage of logical consistency: if the operative principle is the biological loss of potency, the measure ought to be elapsed time, not the accident of which calendar day happens to begin. As will emerge when we turn the page, the biological reality — as observed — accords with seventy-two hours, which corresponds to the *Rabbanan*'s position of six full *onot*.

## Matan Torah Timeline

### Rav Pappa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה כְּרַבָּנַן — דְּאָמְרִי בְּחַמְשָׁא עֲבוּד פְּרִישָׁה, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — דְּאָמַר בְּאַרְבְּעָה עֲבוּד פְּרִישָׁה, אֶלָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כְּמַאן? לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, כִּדְאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מֹשֶׁה בְּהַשְׁכָּמָה עָלָה וּבְהַשְׁכָּמָה יָרַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said this before Rav Pappa, and some say** that **Rav Pappa** said this **to Rava: Granted, Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** with regard to the revelation at Sinai that Moses **instituted separation** between husbands and wives **on** the **fifth** day of the week. Since everyone agrees that the Torah was given on Shabbat, husbands and wives were separated for two days. **And Rabbi Yishmael** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who **said** that Moses **instituted separation on** the **fourth** day of the week, meaning that husbands and wives were separated for three days. **However, in accordance with whose** opinion did **Rabbi Akiva** state his opinion? Ritual impurity, in this case, is not based on the passage of days but on the passage of twelve-hour periods, which do not correspond to either opinion mentioned with regard to the revelation at Sinai. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** Rabbi Akiva holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** However, his understanding is based on that which **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Moses ascended** Mount Sinai **early in the morning, and he descended early in the morning** and related to them the mitzva of separation.

The *gemara* records that in front of Rav Pappa — and some say Rav Pappa posed the question to Rava — the following difficulty was raised. Everyone agrees that the Torah was given on Shabbat; the debate is only about when the separation occurred. *Bishlama* Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya and Rabbi Ishmael are intelligible: one says the Israelites separated on Thursday (giving us day three as Shabbat), the other says they separated on Wednesday following Rabbi Yossi, who holds that the separation was on the fourth of Sivan. *Ela Rabbi Akiva keman?* — but whose position does Rabbi Akiva follow? If the determinant is five *onot*, how does that map onto either of these narratives? From which verse or textual moment does one derive five *onot* rather than four or six?

### Moshe's Instructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהַשְׁכָּמָה עָלָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּשְׁכֵּם מֹשֶׁה בַבֹּקֶר וַיַּעַל אֶל הַר סִינַי״. בְּהַשְׁכָּמָה יָרַד, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֶךְ רֵד וְעָלִיתָ אַתָּה וְאַהֲרֹן עִמָּךְ״, מַקִּישׁ יְרִידָה לַעֲלִיָּיה: מָה עֲלִיָּיה בְּהַשְׁכָּמָה, אַף יְרִידָה בְּהַשְׁכָּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the source of this opinion: **He ascended** Mount Sinai **early in the morning, as it is written: "And Moses rose up early in the morning, and went up to Mount Sinai,** as the Lord had commanded him" (Exodus 34:4). And **he descended** the mountain **early in the morning, as it is written: "Go descend and you shall ascend together with Aaron"** (Exodus 19:24). The Torah **juxtaposes descent to ascent** to establish that **just as** Moses' **ascent** was **early in the morning, so too,** his **descent** was **early in the morning.** Moses told the people to separate in the early morning so that there would be five complete periods of separation over the course of the three days.

The *gemara* resolves this through a tradition cited in the name of Rav Adda bar Ahava: *Moshe behashkamah alah u'behashkamah yarad* — Moshe ascended the mountain and descended the mountain always at first light, *behashkamah*. The proof: *vayashkem Moshe baboker vaya'al el har Sinai* — "Moshe rose early in the morning and ascended Har Sinai." And for the descent: the verse *lech red... v'alita ata ve'aharon imach* — "go down, and you and Aaron will come up" — the juxtaposition of descent and ascent links them together, and since the ascent was in the morning, the descent was as well. *Makish yerida le'aliyah.*

### Wednesday Start

The *gemara* assumes that Moshe told the people to separate on Wednesday morning, first thing. The Torah was received on Shabbat morning. The question now becomes clear: from first light Wednesday morning until Shabbat morning is a count of five *onot* — Wednesday day, Wednesday night, Thursday day, Thursday night, Friday day — and then Shabbat morning arrives.

### Five Onot Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימְרָא לְהוּ, וְהָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשִׁים הֵן וְאֵין מְשַׁמְּשִׁין מִטּוֹתֵיהֶן בַּיּוֹם! הָא אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הָיָה בַּיִת אָפֵל — מוּתָּר. וְאָמַר רָבָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: תַּלְמִיד חָכָם מַאֲפִיל בְּטַלִּיתוֹ, וּמוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why did he need to tell them** to separate during the morning hours? **Didn't Rav Huna say: The Jewish people are holy and do not have relations during the day?** It was not necessary to command them until night. The Gemara replies: It was necessary to tell them in the morning, **as Rava said: If it was a dark house, it is permitted** to have relations during the day. **And** similarly, **Rava said,** and **some say** that **Rav Pappa** said: **A Torah scholar obscures** the light in the room **with his cloak and is** thereby **permitted** to have relations during the day. Therefore, it was necessary to command the people to separate even during the daytime hours.

This gives Rabbi Akiva his five *onot*. The emphasis on *behashkamah* — that Moshe came down and issued the instruction at first light on Wednesday — is essential, because it anchors the starting point precisely. Had Moshe issued the instruction at midday Wednesday, the interval would still have been five *onot* before Friday night (when they might have immersed) — but the *gemara* pushes back on this. Does it even matter when exactly Moshe told them, since *Yisrael kedoshim hem ve'ein meshamshin mitoteihem bayom* — Israel is holy and does not engage in intercourse during the daytime? If they don't have sex during the day anyway, then even a midday announcement on Wednesday would mean they had already stopped by Wednesday morning.

The *gemara* counters: Rava (and some say Rav Pappa) rules that *im haya bayit afel mutar* — if the house is dark, intercourse is permitted during the day — and further, that a *talmid chacham b'afel be-talito mutar* — a Torah scholar may engage in intercourse under the covers even without a darkened room. So intercourse during daylight hours was possible under certain circumstances, and the *behashkamah* instruction was therefore necessary to remove all ambiguity about the starting point of the five *onot*.

This incidentally raises a question about the pre-*Matan Torah* context: can one invoke these norms before the Torah was given? The *gemara*'s implicit answer is that the principle *Yisrael kedoshim hem* is not a formal *halakhic* ruling but a statement about the moral character and expected comportment of the people — grounded in God's earlier declaration *atem tihiyu li mamleches kohanim vegoy kadosh* — and therefore does not depend on the formal giving of the law.

## Tevul Yom

### Friday Night Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא טְבוּלֵי יוֹם נִינְהוּ? אַבָּיֵי בַּר רָבִין וְרַב חֲנִינָא בַּר אָבִין דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לִטְבוּל יוֹם. יָתֵיב מָרִימָר וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְמָרִימָר: נִיתְּנָה קָאָמְרַתְּ, אוֹ רְאוּיָה קָאָמְרַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רְאוּיָה קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't** some of the people have status of **those who immersed themselves during the day** when they received the Torah? Some of the women immersed themselves on Shabbat evening to purify themselves from the discharge of semen. Even after immersion, the purification process is not complete until sunset. It was **Abaye bar Ravin and Rav Ḥanina bar Avin who both said** in response: **The Torah was given to those who immersed themselves during the day,** and that in no way diminishes the magnitude of the revelation. The Gemara relates that **Mareimar sat and stated this *halakha*. Ravina said to Mareimar: Did you say** that the Torah **was** actually **given** to those who immersed themselves during the day, **or did you say** that **it was fit** to be given to those who immersed themselves during the day, but, in reality, it was not? He **said to him: I said** that the Torah **was fit** to be given, but in actuality the nation was ritually pure, and the women did not discharge semen on the third day.

The *gemara* now introduces a further technical complication. Even granting that five *onot* have passed and the semen is no longer potent, when exactly would the women have immersed? If they immersed after nightfall Friday — once the five *onot* were complete — they would have been *tevulot yom*: they had immersed, but the full completion of their *taharah* required waiting until the following sunset. Shabbat morning, when the Torah was given, would then find them still in a state of *tevul yom*.

### Torah to Tevulei Yom

The *gemara* answers: *Abaye bar Avin ve-Rav Hanina bar Avin de'amri tarvaihu: nitna Torah l'tvulei yom.* Both authorities state that the Torah could be given to those who were *tevulei yom*. The reasoning is that *tevul yom* represents a fundamentally diminished level of *tumah*: the person has already immersed, the primary *tumah* has been removed, and only the formal completion of *taharah* — which occurs automatically at nightfall — remains outstanding. Given that the concern motivating the entire three-day separation was the connection to the act of intercourse and the particular incompatibility of that experience with *kabbalat haTorah*, a *tevul yom* who has already immersed has sufficiently transitioned. The *tumah* that remains is a generic, low-level residual, not the same in character as the original *tumah* arising from the act.

This connects to a broader question with practical halakhic implications. Those who today wish to ascend Har HaBayit are instructed to immerse in a *mikvah* beforehand. But if a person had intercourse the previous night and immersed in the morning, they remain a *tevul yom* until sunset and technically retain some vestige of *ba'al keri* status. Whether a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* is forbidden on Har HaBayit — which has the status of *machaneh Leviyah* and not the *makom hamikdash* proper — is in fact debated in the *gemara* and among the *rishonim*. The question of whether the old status carries over into the *tevul yom* period or whether immersion converts it into a generic low-level *tumah* is not fully resolved.

### Theory vs. Reality

Marymar was sitting and presenting this teaching — that the Torah was given to *tevulei yom* — when Ravina challenged him: *nitna kamarta o re'u'ya kamarta?* — are you saying the Torah *was* given to those who were *tevulei yom*, or merely that it *could have been* given to them?

The distinction matters. If the timing worked out so that the women immersed before sunset on Friday and were fully *tehorot* by nightfall, there would have been no actual *tevul yom* issue at all. The question is purely theoretical: had the five *onot* not been complete until after nightfall, and had they immersed Friday night, they would have been *tevulei yom* the next morning — and the answer is that this would have been acceptable. But in practice, *Baruch Hashem*, the timing was favorable. Marymar confirms: *re'u'ya kamina* — I was speaking theoretically, about what could have been. In practice, they were fully *tehorot*.

### Rabbi Yitzchak's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטְבְּלוּ בֵּינֵי שִׁימְשֵׁי, וְלִיקַבְּלוּ תּוֹרָה בֵּינֵי שִׁימְשֵׁי? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״לֹא מֵרֹאשׁ בַּסֵּתֶר דִּבַּרְתִּי״. וְלִיטְבְּלוּ בְּצַפְרָא דְשַׁבְּתָא, וְלִיקַבְּלוּ תּוֹרָה בְּצַפְרָא דְשַׁבְּתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא הַלָּלוּ הוֹלְכִין לְקַבֵּל תּוֹרָה וְהַלָּלוּ הוֹלְכִין לִטְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let them immerse** during **twilight** on Shabbat eve **and receive** the **Torah** just after that during **twilight.** Why was it necessary to delay revelation until Shabbat morning? **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that the verse said in that regard: **"From the first, I did not speak in concealment"** (Isaiah 48:16). God did not give the Torah under the cloak of night, but rather in the light of day. The Gemara asks: **And let them immerse themselves on Shabbat morning and receive the Torah on Shabbat morning.** In that case, according to all opinions, the period of separation could have begun one twelve-hour period later. **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** This was not done **so that** there would **not** be a situation where **these,** one segment of the people, **would be going to receive the Torah while those,** another segment of the people, **would be going to immerse themselves.** Optimally, the entire nation should go to receive the Torah together.

But then the *gemara* asks a sharper question. According to Rabbi Akiva's five-*onah* calculation — starting from Wednesday morning — the five *onot* are complete by Friday night. Why not let the women immerse Friday night, be *tevulei yom*, and receive the Torah Shabbat morning without any issue? The *gemara* has already established that *tevul yom* is acceptable. So why was it necessary for Moshe to issue the instruction at first light on Wednesday, and why wait until Shabbat morning to give the Torah? Why not shift everything by one *onah* — instruct them Wednesday night, complete five *onot* by Shabbat morning, and avoid the *tevul yom* question altogether?

Rabbi Yitzchak answers: *shelo yehu halalu holchin lekabel Torah ve-halalu holchin li'tvila* — so that it should not be that some people are going to receive the Torah while others are running off to immerse in the *mikvah*. The concern is not that they would miss *Matan Torah* entirely — presumably they would still arrive in time. The concern is that while the community gathered to receive the Torah in a moment of collective grandeur, some women would be arriving late, rushing back from the *mikvah*, marginalized from the very experience the entire three-day preparation was designed to enable. One might even say: it is not only that the separation was for the sake of the women — the timing of the instruction was similarly calibrated so that not a single woman would find herself excluded, even in a minor way, from the moment of *kabbalat haTorah*.

## Six Onot Standard

### Six Full Onot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שֵׁשׁ עוֹנוֹת שְׁלֵמוֹת בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabbi Abba, said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: These are the statements of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. However, the Rabbis say: We require** that **six complete** twelve-hour **periods** elapse between cohabitation and discharge. If a woman discharged semen less than seventy-two hours after having relations, the semen renders her ritually impure.

The *gemara* concludes by presenting the majority Rabbinic position: *shesh onot shelemot ba'inan* — we require six complete *onot*, seventy-two hours, for semen to lose its potency. This is *amar Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the positions of Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva both operate within the framework of day four, whether that means five or sometimes six *onot* — but the *Chachamim* say six *onot* in every case. That means from Wednesday morning until Shabbat morning — Wednesday day, Wednesday night, Thursday day, Thursday night, Friday day, Friday night — six full *onot* — and then the Torah is given Shabbat morning, after a full seventy-two hours have elapsed.

As it happens, this matches the biological reality: semen loses its viability after approximately seventy-two hours. The *Chachamim*'s position is thus not only the majority halakhic ruling but one that aligns with the observed facts of reproductive biology.

### Rambam vs. Gemara

This makes the *Gra*'s marginal note all the more significant. The *Gra* notes that the variant reading of the *Geonim* — and the ruling of the *Rambam* — is not *shesh onot* but *shalosh onot*, three *onot*, thirty-six hours. This is a substantial textual dispute with major practical consequences. The *Gra* indicates that the *girsat ha-Geonim* diverges from the text before us, and the *Rambam* rules accordingly.

### Niddah Counting

The practical halakhic stakes are significant. Women do not begin counting their seven *nekiyim* — the seven clean days required before immersion — until after the *poletet* concern has passed. If a woman became a *niddah* on Sunday and had intercourse shortly before she began to bleed, she cannot begin counting her clean days until the semen from that intercourse is no longer potent, since any day on which she might expel semen is considered *tamei* for counting purposes. According to the seventy-two-hour standard — three full days — she cannot begin counting on Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, or Wednesday: the semen might still be potent. She can only begin counting on Thursday, day five. (Ashkenazic practice adds yet another day beyond this, for reasons not developed here.) The thirty-six-hour position of the *Rambam*, by contrast, would allow counting to begin significantly earlier. This dispute between the *Geonim* and *Rambam* on one side and the standard Talmudic text on the other is therefore not an abstract question of *mesorah* — it has profound monthly implications for the laws of *niddah*.

## Semen Sources

### Woman vs. Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת שֶׁפֵּירְשָׁה מִן הָאִשָּׁה, אֲבָל פֵּירְשָׁה מִן הָאִישׁ — טְמֵאָה כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא לַחָה. מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״וְכׇל בֶּגֶד וְכׇל עוֹר אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה עָלָיו שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״ — פְּרָט לְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁהִיא סְרוּחָה. מַאי לָאו שֶׁפֵּירְשָׁה מִן הָאִישׁ? לָא, שֶׁפֵּירְשָׁה מִן הָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The **dispute** over how long semen renders one ritually impure is only with regard to semen that **is discharged from a woman; however,** if it **is discharged from a man,** e.g., it was on a garment, **it is impure as long as it is moist,** regardless of how much time passed since its discharge. **Rav Sheshet raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *baraita*: The Torah states: **"And every garment, and every hide on which there is semen** shall be immersed in water, and be impure until evening" (Leviticus 15:17). And the Sages taught: This **excludes semen which is foul. What, is it not** referring to semen **discharged from a man,** indicating that even moist semen becomes foul after a certain period of time and no longer transmits impurity? The Gemara rejects this: **No,** this *halakha* is referring to semen **that was discharged from a woman.**

Having established the framework for *poletet shichvat zera*, the *gemara* introduces a distinction made by Rav Hisda. *Machloket she-pirshah min ha-isha* — the entire debate above applies specifically to semen that has been expelled from a woman's body after intercourse. *Aval pirshah min ha-ish tmeia* — but semen that came directly from a man, such as semen found on a garment after seminal emission, is governed by a different rule.

### Moisture vs. Chemistry

For semen on a garment, the operative measure is not seventy-two hours or any fixed time period but simply moisture: *tmeia kol zman she-hi lachah* — it is *tamei* as long as it remains moist. Once it dries completely, it is no longer *tamei*, regardless of how many hours have passed.

Rav Sheshet challenges this from a *braita*: *vechol beged vechol or asher yihyeh alav shichvat zera* — the verse speaks of any garment upon which there is semen — *prat l'shichvat zera she'srucha* — this excludes semen that has become putrid, i.e., has lost its potency. If the verse is speaking of semen on a garment, and the exclusion is for semen that has become *srucha*, does this not indicate that even for semen on a garment, the determinant is potency — seventy-two hours — rather than moisture? The *gemara* answers: *lo she'pirsha min ha'isha* — the verse is speaking about semen that came from a woman's body, not about semen directly on a garment. The two categories remain distinct.

This points to something deeper: it is not merely the woman's *tumah* status that changes after seventy-two hours. The semen itself undergoes a different process inside the body than it does on an exposed surface. Inside the woman's body, it is transformed — its potency is affected differently than when it lies on a garment open to the air. The *gemara* will now explore what exactly causes this difference.

### Jewish/Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּמְעֵי גוֹיָה מַהוּ? יִשְׂרָאֵל דִּדְאִיגִי בְּמִצְוֹת — חֲבִיל גּוּפַיְיהוּ, גּוֹיִם דְּלָא דְּאִיגִי בְּמִצְוֹת — לָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאָכְלִין שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים חֲבִיל גּוּפַיְיהוּ. וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר כֵּיוָן דְּאָכְלִי שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים חֲבִיל גּוּפַיְיהוּ: בִּמְעֵי בְּהֵמָה מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **semen of a Jew in the womb of a gentile woman?** Do we say that since **Jews are concerned about** fulfilling **mitzvot,** due to that concern, **their body** temperature **is hot** and semen that is not absorbed becomes foul faster, in contrast to **gentiles who are not concerned about** fulfilling **mitzvot** for whom that is **not** the case? **Or, perhaps, since** gentiles **eat detestable creatures and creeping animals their body** temperature **is** also **hot? And if you say that since** they **eat detestable creatures and creeping animals, their body** temperature **is hot,** another dilemma can be raised: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to semen of a Jew **in the womb of an animal?**

Rav Pappa poses a remarkable question: *shichvat zera shel Yisrael b'mi kutit mahu?* — what is the status of semen from a Jewish man that was expelled from a non-Jewish woman's body? For purposes of *tumah*, a non-Jewish woman does not have the same *tumah* status as a Jewish woman. The semen of a non-Jewish man is not *tamei*. But here the semen is from a Jewish man — which is inherently *tamei* — and has been inside a non-Jewish woman's body. When it is expelled from her body, does it lose its potency by the seventy-two-hour rule, as semen from a Jewish woman's body does, or does it remain governed by the moisture rule, as semen directly on a garment would be?

### Body Temperature

The *gemara* frames the question in terms of internal body temperature. *Yisrael d'da'igei b'mitzvot chavivei gufaihu* — Jews, who are constantly concerned with their obligations and anxious about fulfilling the *mitzvot*, have a higher internal body temperature. It is this elevated internal heat, the *gemara* suggests, that accelerates the transformation of semen inside the body, rendering it non-potent within seventy-two hours. *Akkum d'lo da'igei b'mitzvot* — non-Jews, who do not occupy themselves with *mitzvot*, do not share this internal intensity, *lo chavivei gufaihu* — their bodies are not as warm. If that is the operative factor, then semen inside a non-Jewish woman's body would not be subject to the same transformation, and the seventy-two-hour rule would not apply.

On the other hand: *keivan d'achli shekatzim u'remashim chavivei gufaihu* — non-Jews eat rodents and other forbidden foods, and perhaps this too has a biological effect, raising their internal body temperature through a different mechanism. If so, non-Jewish bodies might also be sufficiently warm to transform the semen in the same way.

### Animal vs. Human

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה הִיא דְּאִית לַהּ פְּרוֹזְדוֹר — מַסְרְחָא, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה דְּלֵית לַהּ פְּרוֹזְדוֹר — לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Do we say that in **a woman, who has a** long **corridor [*perozdor*]** to her womb, the semen becomes **foul; but** in **an animal, which does not have** as long **a corridor,** it does **not? Or perhaps** it is **not different,** and the period of time that the semen renders one ritually impure is the same in both cases? No resolution was found for these dilemmas. Therefore, **let them stand** unresolved.

*V'ei timtzei lomar* — and if one were to conclude that the eating of *shekatzim u'remashim* does indeed raise body temperature, one must then ask about animals: *b'mi behemah mahu?* — what is the status of semen from a human male expelled from an animal's body? The question brackets the ethical gravity of the act entirely and focuses on the narrow *tumah* question: does an animal's body transform semen the way a human woman's body does?

### Prozozdor

The *gemara* introduces the decisive anatomical factor. *Ishah d'it lah prozdor masrechet* — a woman who has a *prozdor* causes the semen within her to become *srucha*, to lose its potency. *Aval behemah d'leit lah prozdor lo?* — but an animal, which does not have a *prozdor*, perhaps the same process does not occur? Rashi explains that the *prozdor* refers not to an internal structure — as it is sometimes understood in *Masechet Niddah* — but to the external labia, the *labia externa*, which human females possess and animals do not. This anatomical feature closes off the interior from the outside air and, together with the body's internal heat, creates the conditions under which semen loses its potency within the defined time period. An animal, lacking this structure, is open to the air in a way that a human woman is not, and therefore the same transformation may not occur within the animal's body.

The question is left as *teiku* — unresolved.

What is striking is the *gemara*'s sustained engagement with questions of biology and body chemistry as operative categories in *halakhah*. Internal body heat — attributed to the spiritual intensity of *mitzvah* observance or to the effects of diet — the anatomical difference between human and animal, and the chemical transformation of semen inside versus outside the body: all of these function as genuine halakhic variables. The semen's status is not simply a function of time elapsed but of the specific physiological environment in which it resides. And this opens onto questions — left in *teiku* — that the *gemara* acknowledges it cannot fully answer.
