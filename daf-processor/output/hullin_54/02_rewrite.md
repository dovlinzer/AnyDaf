# Hullin 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Derusa & Inspection

### Derusa Overview

The daf opens at the very bottom of 53b, in the midst of the *gemara*'s treatment of *derusa* — the notion that certain animals inject a harmful substance through their claws, rendering the prey a *treifa*. The *gemara*'s underlying assumption, that this clawing mechanism transmits something lethal through the wound site, does not correspond to modern zoological knowledge; indeed, only the platypus possesses a genuinely venomous claw, and even that is confined to a hind spur. Attempts to reconcile the *gemara*'s framework with contemporary science — whether by invoking rabies or the bacteriological dangers of cat-scratch fever — founder on the internal details of the *sugya* itself, which stipulates that the problem arises only when the predatory animal acts willingly and retracts its claws, not merely when they make contact. Whatever the underlying reality, that is the halakhic framework we are working within.

### Skin Inspection

The *gemara* has been discussing how *derusa* is investigated. The operative principle established in the preceding discussion is that if one inspects the wound site and finds no redness of the skin, there is no problem. The assumption is that the absence of reddening at the site indicates that no harmful substance was injected, and the animal is therefore permitted. The case of the trachea and esophagus, however, is treated differently: for those organs, the organs themselves must show redness, since a hole in either — however small — constitutes a *treifa* in its own right. Reddening of those organs indicates that the clawing has created an effective problem at that location.

### Trachea vs Esoph.

With this framework in place, Rav Pappa reports a question raised by Rav Bibi bar Abaye. The starting point is a known distinction between the two *simanim*: a hole in the esophagus, however minute — *b'mash'hu* — renders the animal a *treifa*. It follows that if *derusa* affected the esophagus and redness appears upon inspection, even the smallest red spot constitutes a problem. But the trachea operates by a different standard: a hole in the trachea is a *treifa* only if it amounts to a majority of the trachea's circumference, or if an actual cutaway hole reaches the size of an *issar*, a coin of specific dimensions. The question Rav Bibi bar Abaye raises is therefore: when *derusa* manifests on the trachea — not a hole, but a red spot on the outer surface of the trachea discovered upon inspection — what is the minimum size of that red spot to create a *treifa*? Should it mirror the standard for a hole in the trachea, requiring a full *issar*-sized marking? Or does some other consideration apply?

### Poison Burns

After raising the question, Rav Pappa then resolves it. The answer is that both the esophagus and the trachea share the same standard in the context of *derusa*: even a minimal red spot — *b'mash'hu* — is problematic. The reasoning is captured in the phrase *zihara mikla kali v'azil*: the harmful substance burns and continues to spread. The *gemara*'s model here is not poison in the systemic, bloodstream sense, but something more akin to an acid — a locally acting corrosive agent. Rashi explains that the assumption is one of local, progressive destruction. A small site of redness on the trachea is not merely a small injury; it is an ongoing process of burning that will continue to erode the tissue until a significant hole results. Therefore, even a tiny red spot on the trachea is sufficient to render the animal a *treifa*, because that small mark represents the beginning of what will inevitably become a larger lesion. This comparison to flesh-eating bacteria — a locally destructive process that spreads inexorably from its point of origin — captures something of what the *gemara* appears to be describing.

It is also worth noting that the *gemara*'s treatment of *derusa* in general significantly limits the practical scope of this *treifa* category. Just as the case of *nefila* — an animal that falls from a roof — was progressively limited by the conditions that the animal must have jumped rather than fallen passively, and must subsequently be able to stand and walk, so too *derusa* is hemmed in at every turn: questions of which predatory animals are included, whether the action was intentional, whether it involved the insertion or the retraction of the claws, and now the entire inspection mechanism by which redness at the wound site can exculpate the animal. For those troubled by these two categories that sit uneasily with modern understanding, it is worth observing that the *gemara*'s cumulative effect is to restrict their application considerably.

### Abdominal Check

The *gemara* continues: Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta was sitting before Rav Nachman and stated that when a case of *derusa* arises, the required inspection — *bedika* — must be conducted opposite the *benei mei'ayim*, the abdominal organs. The logic is that *derusa* creates a *treifa* specifically by causing holes in internal organs, and the organs most vulnerable are those of the gastrointestinal tract, which sit below the ribcage with less bony protection than the heart or lungs. The inspection is therefore targeted: one examines the outer skin of the animal in the area of the *benei mei'ayim*, looking for those telltale red spots. Rashi and Tosafot both understand *benei mei'ayim* to refer primarily to the gastrointestinal organs — stomach, intestines, and the like — rather than to the entire inner cavity. If no redness appears at that location, one may assume that no harmful substance was injected into those organs and the animal is permitted. Clawing elsewhere on the body — the limbs, for instance — would not in any case produce a *treifa* even if redness appeared there, because *treifa* is generated by injury to the internal organs, and those areas do not directly threaten the vulnerable organs.

### Full Body Check

Rav Nachman, upon hearing this teaching, responded with an emphatic exclamation — *ha Elokim!* — expressing surprise or even mild reproach. He cited Rav's teaching that the inspection must extend *mikafo ad atama*, from the *kaf* to the *atama*, the thigh socket. The *gemara* immediately raises a question: what does *kaf* mean here? If it means the palm of the hand, the range described — shoulder to thigh — would be essentially equivalent to the *benei mei'ayim* area, adding nothing. Rashi understands *kaf* here not as the palm of the hand but as the shoulder joint, a large bony structure that can loosely be described by that term. But even understood as the shoulder, Rashi and Tosafot both note the problem: the range from shoulder to thigh encompasses the entire internal cavity including the lungs and heart, which the *gemara* elsewhere refers to not as *benei mei'ayim* but as *kol hachalal kulo*, the entire inner cavity. Tosafot suggests that in bovine anatomy this bone may extend further, such that the shoulder-to-thigh range in cattle aligns with what the *gemara* means by *benei mei'ayim*. The *gemara* ultimately resolves that *kaf* here refers to *kafa demoucha* — the brain pan, the bony cup that houses the brain — making the range extend from there all the way down to the thigh. Crucially, this range includes the neck area, which contains the trachea and esophagus. What Rav's teaching therefore adds, beyond the earlier statement about *benei mei'ayim*, is that the neck region — where those *simanim* are located — must also be inspected, even though as we discussed, those organs are somewhat more resistant and have their own separate inspection standards.

The practical upshot of this discussion is a clarification of which regions of the body require inspection when the site of clawing is unknown. The answer is not the entire body, since clawing in many areas would never produce a *treifa* regardless. But neither is it limited to the abdominal area alone. The inspection must cover the full range from the brain pan down to the thigh socket, including the neck.

## Shmutat/Shchitat

### Rav's Position

The *gemara* now shifts to a teaching reported in connection with Rav. The narrative that follows involves Rav Chiya bar Yosef's journey from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, where he found Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish sitting together and articulating the rule that a *derusa* requires inspection opposite the *benei mei'ayim*. Rav Chiya bar Yosef informed them that Rav had extended this range from the *kaf* to the *atama*. Resh Lakish's response was striking: *manu Rav u'manu Rav?* — "Who is this Rav? I don't know anyone named Rav." Rabbi Yochanan then intervened to remind Resh Lakish of that same student who had sat in their presence in the *yeshiva* — who had actually occupied the more prestigious position of sitting while the younger Rabbi Yochanan himself had stood — and who had been great in all respects, in Torah and in *chesed* alike. Once Resh Lakish was reminded, he conceded: *beram zachor oto ha'ish l'tov*, "that person should indeed be remembered favorably," for at least people had reported teachings in his name — she'amru shmuah mipiv.

It is worth noting how reluctant Resh Lakish's acknowledgment is: he refers to Rav throughout as *oto ha'ish*, "that man," and frames his concession around the fact that others have cited teachings in his name, rather than offering any direct tribute to the greatness of those teachings. There may be a geographic dimension to this reluctance — the feeling, perhaps, that Rav had taken his formidable Torah to Babylonia and built it up there when he might have remained in Eretz Yisrael. And the fact that Resh Lakish did not know him at all likely reflects the chronology: Rav had already departed for Babylonia by the time Resh Lakish came onto the scene, and if Rav was indeed an honorific rather than a given name, Resh Lakish may genuinely have been encountering this appellation for the first time.

The teaching that Rav is credited with — and that Resh Lakish acknowledges for this reason — is the following: *shmuta v'shchuta keshera*. If an animal's *siman* — let us say the trachea — was cut during *shechita* and was subsequently found to be dislodged from its proper position, the animal is nevertheless *keshera*. The reasoning is *she'i efshar lishmuta she'teaseh shchuta*: it is impossible for a *siman* that was dislodged before *shechita* to have been cut by the *shechita*, because if the trachea had already been displaced from its anatomical position, the blade of the *shochet* would never have reached it. Therefore if we see that the trachea has been cut, we know that the cut occurred while it was in its proper place; the dislocation must have happened afterward, and is therefore not a disqualifying defect.

### Yochanan's Test

Rabbi Yochanan, however, disagrees with this simple inference. He rules that one cannot rely on that logical argument alone — *yatvi v'yakbi*, one must sit and establish through a comparison test. The procedure Rashi describes is this: one makes a second cut in the trachea in its now-dislodged position and compares the appearance of that test cut with the appearance of the original *shechita* cut. The underlying assumption is that a cut made while the trachea is in its normal anatomical position will look different from a cut made while it is displaced. If the original *shechita* cut resembles the test cut made when the *siman* is dislodged, one must conclude that the original cut also occurred when the *siman* was out of position — meaning the *shechita* was invalid. Only if the two cuts look different can one accept that the original cut occurred properly.

### Nachman's Limit

Rav Nachman then qualifies Rav's teaching further. He states: *lo shanu ela shelo tafas b'simanin* — Rav's ruling applies only in the case where the *shochet* was not gripping the *simanim* during the act of *shechita*. In that case, the logic holds: if the trachea was displaced before the cut, the blade would not have reached it. But *aval tafas b'simanin v'shachat* — if the *shochet* was holding the *simanim* together in his hand while performing the *shechita*, then it is entirely possible that the trachea was already displaced before the cut and the *shochet* was holding it in place manually, such that the cut reached it even though it was not in its natural anatomical position. In that case, one cannot make Rav's inference and the animal may well be a *treifa*. The effect of these two qualifications — Rabbi Yochanan's comparison test and Rav Nachman's limitation to cases where the *shochet* was not gripping the *simanim* — is that by the time Resh Lakish has begrudgingly acknowledged Rav's teaching as worth remembering, two immediate and significant restrictions have already been placed upon it.

## Tradition vs Science

### Enumerated List

The final line of the first *mishna* had stated: *zeh ha-klal, kol she'ein kamohah chayah, treifa* — "This is the general rule: any animal with such an injury that it cannot live, is a *treifa*." The *gemara* asks what this *zeh ha-klal* comes to include. The answer given is that it comes to include the seven teachings — *sheva shmaitata* — of *amoraim* who added to the list of *treifot*. This seems to suggest that the category of *treifa* is open-ended: any injury the sages demonstrate will cause the animal's death can be added to the list.

### Hunter Evidence

The *gemara* now challenges this apparent openness with two stories. First, from the house of a man known as Yosef the hunter: hunters would strike animals in the *gid hanasha*, the sciatic nerve region, and those animals would die as a result of that wound. Rashi suggests the hunters would hit the hindquarters to bring the animal down and then *shecht* it, but if they failed to do so in time, the animal would perish from that wound alone. The *gid hanasha* injury, however, is not on the list of *treifot*. They brought this evidence to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira. His response was categorical: *ve'chi l'hosif al treifot yesh? Ein lecha ela mah she'manu chachamim* — Are you trying to add to the list of *treifot*? You have only what the sages enumerated.

A parallel case is then presented. Rav Pappa bar Abba was also a hunter. He would strike animals in the kidneys and they would die. Notably, the very next *mishna* will state explicitly that an animal whose kidneys are removed is not a *treifa*. Yet here is direct evidence that a blow to the kidneys kills the animal. They brought this observation to Rabbi Abba, who responded identically: *ve'chi l'hosif al treifot yesh? Ein lecha ela mah she'manu chachamim*.

### Medicine Response

The *gemara* then presses the point: *ve'ha ka'chazinan de'ka meita* — but we see with our own eyes that the animal dies! How can one simply invoke the authority of the enumerated list and dismiss the empirical evidence? The *gemara*'s response is: *gemiri, ee badri lah samma chayah* — it is a tradition that if one were to apply a certain medicine to that wound, the animal could survive. That is to say, the animals in question did not die because the injury is inherently lethal; they died only because no treatment was applied. Were the appropriate remedy administered, the animal would live. Therefore, these wounds do not actually belong on the list of injuries from which an animal cannot survive.

### Philosophy

This is a striking *gemara* that raises significant questions about the relationship between the list of *treifot* and empirical reality. The tension is immediately apparent: the *gemara* began by saying that *zeh ha-klal* comes to include the *sheva shmaitata*, implying that the *amoraim* could and did add to the list. Yet now the *gemara* insists the list is closed and cannot be augmented by new observations. How are these reconciled?

The resolution lies in understanding how the *gemara* itself treated the *sheva shmaitata* when they were introduced at the beginning of the *perek*. The *gemara* went to considerable lengths to fit those seven teachings within the original enumerated eighteen — not simply to acknowledge them as independent additions, but to demonstrate that they were subsumed within categories already present. This treatment reveals the *gemara*'s understanding that even those *amoraic* additions were not novel discoveries made by individual rabbis based on observation, but rather long-standing traditions that happened to be transmitted through the *amoraim*. The *gemara* thus presents a coherent position: the list of *treifot* is based entirely on tradition — *halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — and not on empirical determination. Empirical observation cannot add to or subtract from the list. As for injuries not on the list that appear to cause death, the *gemara* responds that some remedy exists, whether or not we know what it is. And as for injuries that are on the list where an animal might appear to survive, one does not revise the list on that basis either.

This framework provides protection against the constant challenge of new scientific discovery. Every generation might observe something new that seems to argue for revising the list. By grounding the list entirely in tradition, one insulates it from that pressure. The cost of this position, however, is a degree of tension with the very principle underlying *treifa* — that the animal cannot survive — since the list and the underlying rationale no longer perfectly track one another.

### Rishonim Views

The Rambam and the Rashba both grappled with this tension and ultimately came down on the side of protecting the list. When confronted with evidence that animals survive injuries that are on the *treifot* list, or die from injuries that are not, they maintained the list's authority and sought ways to discount the empirical challenge rather than yield to it. The Chacham Tzvi, by contrast, took a notably different position in at least one famous case — involving an animal believed to have had no heart — arguing that it would be absurd and even harmful to the integrity of the halakhic system to consistently reject what we demonstrably know about how the world works. The Chacham Tzvi's concern was that a system that cannot engage with observable reality becomes intellectually untenable.

The discussion naturally extends to the broader question of how *halakha* relates to scientific knowledge in other areas. Rav Moshe Feinstein addressed a striking case: a man who underwent a testicular biopsy for the purpose of treating infertility, and who, according to the explicit language of the *gemara*, would be classified as a *krut shofcha* — a status that would bar him from marriage. The *gemara* is unambiguous that even the removal of a tiny amount of tissue creates this status. Rav Moshe argued, however, that the category of *krut shofcha* is grounded in the assumption of resultant infertility. Since we now know that a limited testicular biopsy does not produce infertility, the underlying rationale is absent and the man is not in fact a *krut shofcha*. Crucially, Rav Moshe was explicit that this mode of reasoning — revisiting a halakhic category in light of updated scientific understanding of its underlying rationale — is in principle applicable across *halakha*. What makes *treifot* different and immune to such reinterpretation is precisely the halakha's own insistence that the list is *halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, not derived from the principle of non-survival but transmitted as a fixed tradition that the principle of non-survival only approximates.

This position is not without its difficulties. The *gemara*'s response that medicine might exist does not sit entirely comfortably as a categorical assertion — one could read it not as a blanket dismissal but as a specific factual claim that in these particular cases, effective treatment does exist. Under that reading, the *gemara* would not be foreclosing revision of the list in principle, but only contesting whether these particular cases truly meet the standard of inevitable death. That reading was not, however, the general approach adopted by the *poskim*. The dominant tradition in this area — specific to *treifot* — is that the list is authoritative as received, not subject to revision based on new observation.

## Kosher Conditions

### Full List

The *gemara* now turns to the second *mishna*, which enumerates conditions that do not render an animal a *treifa*. *v'eilu kashrot b'beheimah* — the following are kosher in a large domesticated animal.

A hole in the trachea — *nikva hagargeret* — that does not sever the majority of the trachea is not a *treifa*. Nor is a crack — *nisdeka* — running along its length, provided it does not extend all the way through. If material is actually removed from the trachea, how large a lacuna is problematic? Rabban Gamliel says: the size of an Italian *issar*, a specific Roman coin. Anything smaller is not a *treifa*.

If the skull is cracked open — *niftecha hagulgoles* — but the membrane surrounding the brain is not pierced, the animal can survive and is not a *treifa*. A hole in the heart — *nikav halev* — that does not penetrate the inner chamber is not a *treifa*. The spine — *hashidra* — may be broken, provided the spinal cord itself is not severed. The liver may be substantially removed, provided a *k'zayit* of liver tissue remains — the *gemara* ultimately rules in favor of this *mishna* against an earlier suggestion that any remaining amount suffices. The two middle stomachs — *hemases u'veit hakosos* — may have a hole in the wall between them without creating a *treifa*, since food passes through a permeable membrane between them in any event. The spleen may be removed; the kidneys may be removed — though this is medically remarkable, since survival without kidneys requires dialysis today, a consideration the ancients could not have anticipated, and there is a possibility that kidneys were understood more metaphorically, as the seat of wisdom, which an animal does not need. The lower jaw may be removed provided the *simanim* remain in place. The womb — *ha'eim*, literally "mother," a striking usage — may be removed. If the lung is hardened but the hardening occurred through natural causes rather than human intervention — *charuta bidei shamayim* — the animal is not a *treifa*.

Finally, the *mishna* records the case of *hagluda* — an animal whose skin has been removed while alive, whether through disease or human action. Rabbi Meir rules the animal *machshir*, kosher; the Sages rule it invalid. According to the Sages, this case thus falls into the category of *treifot*, even though it does not appear on the first *mishna*'s list.

### Excluded Middle

The *gemara* now raises what is essentially the excluded middle problem. The two lists — the first *mishna*'s enumeration of *treifot* and this *mishna*'s enumeration of kosher conditions — together do not obviously exhaust all possibilities. There will be cases that appear on neither list. The question is which list is *davka* — exclusive and definitive — such that anything not explicitly included in it falls into the opposite category.

*Itmar*: a dispute is reported. Rabbi Yochanan says the first *mishna* is *davka* — these are the *treifot*, and only these. Anything not on the first list is therefore not a *treifa*. Resh Lakish says the second *mishna* is *davka* — these are the kosher conditions, and only these. Anything not on the second list is therefore a *treifa*. Note the logic: whichever list is *davka* is the smaller, more restrictive category, and everything outside it falls into the other. Rabbi Yochanan, by treating the first list as *davka*, makes the category of *treifa* smaller and the category of *kosher* open-ended — a *kula*. Resh Lakish, by treating the second list as *davka*, makes the category of *kosher* smaller and the category of *treifa* open-ended — a *chumra*.

### Dislocated Thigh

The practical case that falls between the two lists, and thus determines which position is correct, is the statement of Rav Masna: *hai buka d'atama d'shaf miduchtei, treifa* — an animal whose thigh socket has been dislocated from the hip joint is a *treifa*. This case appears on neither *mishna*. Under Rabbi Yochanan's position, since it is not on the first *mishna*'s list of *treifot*, it cannot be a *treifa*; the first list is *davka*, so Rav Masna's case must be kosher. Under Resh Lakish's position, since it is not on the second *mishna*'s list of kosher conditions, it must be a *treifa* — consistent with Rav Masna's ruling.

The *gemara* explains each position's internal reasoning. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the first *mishna* taught the *treifot* and then stated *zeh ha-klal*, anticipating that people would want to include cases like Rav Masna's under the general principle that "an animal which cannot survive is a *treifa*." After all, a dislocated thigh is somewhat similar to cases of organ removal — *nitulei* — in that the thigh has been removed from its proper position. The *tanna* therefore stated *eilu treifot* — these and no others — to exclude Rav Masna's case. Even if someone wanted to include it under *zeh ha-klal*, the word *eilu* signals that the list is limited and this case falls outside it.

Resh Lakish holds that the second *mishna* anticipated that Rav Masna's case was a gray area — it was not clearly similar enough to any of the established *treifot* categories to be automatically included under *zeh ha-klal*. It is not quite like a hole in an organ — *nikuv*; not quite like a severed organ — *psukei*; and not quite like a removed organ — *nitulei* — because the thigh has not been fully removed, just displaced. Since it was ambiguous whether it belonged on the *treifot* list, the *tanna* of the second *mishna* stated *eilu kashrot* — these are the kosher conditions, and only these — signaling that anything ambiguous falls into the category of *treifa*, not kosher. Rav Masna's case, being ambiguous, is therefore a *treifa*.

### Ligament Severance

The *gemara* then returns to Rav Masna's case directly. Rav Masna ruled it a *treifa*; Rava rules it *kesheira*. Rava draws a distinction, however: *ifsak nivei* — if the ligament connecting the thigh to the hip socket is cut — that is a *treifa*. The *gemara* then rules: *v'hilkhasa, ifsak nami kesheira* — even if the ligament is cut, the dislocated hip is not a *treifa*, until — *ad d'mis'achla it'achulei* — the ligament is completely dissolved or decomposed. The *gemara* may be operating with the assumption that even a severed ligament can heal unless it has fully disintegrated. This distinction also clarifies why the dislocated thigh is not simply assimilated to the category of *nitulei*, removed organs: the thigh is never fully removed, it is roughly in position and may even retain some ligamentous connection, making it genuinely distinct from an organ that has been extracted entirely.

## Work & Respect

### Craftsmen & Sages

At this point the *gemara* records an account that begins with an incidental observation by Rabbi Chanana, a money changer, who reported the following. Rabbi Bar Nafcha came to his table and requested a *dinar kurdina* — a specific coin from Babylonia that he wished to use to gauge the size of a hole in a trachea for *treifa* determinations. As Bar Nafcha approached the table, Rabbi Chanana wished to rise and honor him. Bar Nafcha stopped him and said: *sheiv beni sheiv* — sit, my son, sit. He then articulated the principle: *ein ba'alei umniyot resha'im la'amod bifnei talmidei chachamim b'sha'ah she'oskim b'melachtam* — craftsmen are not permitted to stand before Torah scholars while they are engaged in their work.

The word *resha'in* here does not mean "forbidden" in an absolute sense but, as Tosafot explains, means "not obligated." The question of why is addressed differently by Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi invokes a *tosefta* that limits the case to someone working in a partnership arrangement — where the worker receives a commission but a partner or employer is also depending on the proceeds. Since the craftsman is not solely working for himself, he cannot choose to interrupt his work, even for the honor of a sage, without effectively stealing from his partner. Rashi extends this to say that even a shopkeeper who merely sits inattentively rather than engaging customers is derelict in his duty to his employer. Tosafot suggests the case may apply even to someone working solely for himself, reading *ein resha'in* as "not obligated." Even without an employer's interests at stake, the person engaged in productive work is not obligated to interrupt it to stand for a sage — the honor is waived by the sage, as the sage understands the importance of one's livelihood.

This reflects a noteworthy ethic present elsewhere in the *gemara* as well — the *mishna* in *berakhot* shortens the permitted length of prayer for workers during working hours, out of respect for their responsibility to their employer.

### Bikkurim Honor

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge from a *mishna* about *bikkurim*. When the people of Israel would travel from their towns to bring their first fruits to Jerusalem, they would pass through villages along the way. At those villages, *kol ba'alei umniyot omdim mipneihem* — all the craftsmen would stand before the *bikkurim* pilgrims, and would greet them warmly: *acheinu bo'achem b'shalom*, "our brothers, come in peace." This standing was clearly done during working hours, yet no objection is raised in the *mishna*. How, then, does Bar Nafcha say one need not stand?

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent contradiction: *mipneihem omdim* — yes, one stands for the *bikkurim* pilgrims, *mipnei talmidei chachamim ein omdim* — but one need not stand for Torah scholars in the same circumstances. A Torah scholar's honor can be waived; the scholar understands the craftsman's situation and releases him from the obligation. But the *bikkurim* occasion is different.

### Mitzva Timing

Rav Yosef bar Abba explains why: *kama chaviva mitzvah b'sha'atah* — how beloved is a *mitzva* performed at its proper time. The *bikkurim* pilgrims are doing something that happens at a specific, designated moment — the appointed season, the single annual opportunity to bring firstfruits. This is not something the scholar can do for them at another time; it is a *mitzva* that, if not performed now, cannot be performed at all for another year, and perhaps not by that same individual ever again. The uniqueness and time-bound urgency of the *mitzva* elevates it above the ordinary honor due to a scholar, who will always be there to be honored another time. One rises not for the person but for the act — for the *mitzva* being performed in its proper moment.

### Communal Welcome

The *gemara* then offers a second, more pragmatic explanation. Perhaps the craftsmen rose not purely from a sense of halakhic honor, but *kedei shelo tehei nimtza machshilan l'atid lavo* — so as not to be found causing them to stumble in the future. If the pilgrims traveled great distances, making significant effort to bring their *bikkurim*, and were met along the way with indifference or silence, they might conclude that the effort was not worth repeating and decline to make the journey in subsequent years. But if the villages rose to greet them warmly, asking after their welfare and welcoming them as brothers, the pilgrims would feel the communal significance of what they were doing and would be encouraged to return. The warmth of communal welcome thus serves not only as an expression of present honor but as an investment in future *mitzva* performance. It is left open whether the primary reason is the intrinsic value of the time-bound *mitzva* or this pragmatic communal consideration.

## Ad v'Lo Ad B'Chlal

### Exact = Upper

The *gemara* concludes the daf with a brief but important principle articulated by Rav Nachman. The context is the various size-thresholds encountered throughout the laws of *treifot*: a hole in the trachea the size of an *issar* is a *treifa*; a hole in the skull the size of a *sela* creates a problem; and so on. The question arises: what is the ruling when a hole measures exactly the stated size — neither more nor less? Is it treated as belonging to the upper (prohibited) category or the lower (permitted) category?

Rav Nachman rules: *k'sela kiyetar misela* — if it is the size of a *sela*, it is considered like more than a *sela*. *k'issar kiyetar me'issar* — if it is exactly the size of an *issar*, it is considered like more than an *issar*. In both cases, the exact measurement falls into the upper, prohibited category. An animal with a hole in its trachea exactly the size of an *issar* is a *treifa*, not because it exceeds the threshold but because the exact threshold itself is included in the prohibited category.

### Until Not Incl.

The underlying principle is *ad v'lo ad b'chlal*: when the sages say that something is permitted "until" a certain size, they mean up to but not including that size. The moment the object reaches the stated threshold, it is already beyond the zone of permission. The word "until" in the sages' usage excludes the limit point itself from the permissive ruling. This *sugya* of *ad v'ad b'chlal* versus *ad v'lo ad b'chlal* — whether boundary measurements are included within the stated limit or fall just beyond it — will continue to be developed on the next daf.
