# Bava Batra 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wall Height & Beit HM

### Wall Height Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ כְּלִילָא דְּיָילֵי, נַקְּרִינְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד אֲתָא וִיתִיב קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: חֲזִי מַר, הַאי עַבְדָּא בִּישָׁא מַאי קָא עָבֵיד! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִלְטְיֵיהּ מָר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּתִיב ״גַּם בְּמַדָּעֲךָ, מֶלֶךְ אַל תְּקַלֵּל״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי לָאו מֶלֶךְ הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיהְוֵי עָשִׁיר בְּעָלְמָא, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְחַדְרֵי מִשְׁכָּבְךָ אַל תְּקַלֵּל עָשִׁיר״; וְלֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא נָשִׂיא, וּכְתִיב: ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** He **placed a garland** made **of porcupine** hide **on** Bava ben Buta's head, which **pricked his eyes out. One day** Herod **came and sat before him** without identifying himself in order to test him. **He,** Herod, **said: See, Master, what this evil slave** Herod **is doing.** Bava ben Buta **said to him: What should I do to him?** Herod **said to him:** The **Master should curse him.** Bava ben Buta **said to him:** But **it is written: "Do not curse the king, not even in your thoughts"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20). Herod **said to him: He is not a king,** since he rules illegally. Bava ben Buta **said to him: And** even if **he were merely a rich man** I would not curse him, as **it is written: "And do not curse a rich person in your bedchamber"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20). **And** even **were he only a leader** I would not curse him, as **it is written: "And you shall not curse a leader among your people"** (Exodus 22:27).

The *gemara* on this *daf* arrives in the middle of a digression. The immediate backdrop is a discussion of the minimum height of a shared wall — whether the obligation to build stems from *hezek re'iya* (visual damage) or from a prior agreement, how high a stone wall must be (four *amot*), and how wide it must be to support that height. From there, the *gemara* raised the case of the *Beit HaMikdash*, whose walls were extraordinarily high yet not proportionally wide — and it is that question that opens the discussion here.

### Teardown Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ כְּלִילָא דְּיָילֵי, נַקְּרִינְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד אֲתָא וִיתִיב קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: חֲזִי מַר, הַאי עַבְדָּא בִּישָׁא מַאי קָא עָבֵיד! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִלְטְיֵיהּ מָר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּתִיב ״גַּם בְּמַדָּעֲךָ, מֶלֶךְ אַל תְּקַלֵּל״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי לָאו מֶלֶךְ הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיהְוֵי עָשִׁיר בְּעָלְמָא, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְחַדְרֵי מִשְׁכָּבְךָ אַל תְּקַלֵּל עָשִׁיר״; וְלֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא נָשִׂיא, וּכְתִיב: ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאֹר״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ, וְהַאי לָאו עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתְּפֵינָא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּאָזֵיל דְּלֵימָא לֵיהּ, דַּאֲנָא וְאַתְּ יָתֵיבְנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, כְּתִיב: ״כִּי עוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם יוֹלִיךְ אֶת הַקּוֹל וּבַעַל כְּנָפַיִם יַגֵּיד דָּבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** He **placed a garland** made **of porcupine** hide **on** Bava ben Buta's head, which **pricked his eyes out. One day** Herod **came and sat before him** without identifying himself in order to test him. **He,** Herod, **said: See, Master, what this evil slave** Herod **is doing.** Bava ben Buta **said to him: What should I do to him?** Herod **said to him:** The **Master should curse him.** Bava ben Buta **said to him:** But **it is written: "Do not curse the king, not even in your thoughts"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20). Herod **said to him: He is not a king,** since he rules illegally. Bava ben Buta **said to him: And** even if **he were merely a rich man** I would not curse him, as **it is written: "And do not curse a rich person in your bedchamber"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20). **And** even **were he only a leader** I would not curse him, as **it is written: "And you shall not curse a leader among your people"** (Exodus 22:27). **He said to him: That *halakha* stated** with **regard to** "a leader among your people," that is, to a fit Jew who **acts as** a member of **your people,** i.e., in accordance with Torah law, **and this one does not do the deeds of your people.** Bava ben Buta **said to him:** Nevertheless, **I am afraid of him.** Herod **said to him: There is nobody who will go and tell him, since you and I are sitting** here alone. Bava ben Buta **said to him:** Nevertheless, **it is written: "For a bird of the sky shall carry the sound, and that which has wings shall tell the matter"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20).

Before arriving at the *Beit HaMikdash* proper, the *gemara* had been exploring a related set of laws: may one tear down a *shul* in order to build a better one in its place? The answer is generally no, for two distinct reasons. First, there would be no place to pray in the interim. Second, and perhaps more practically, there is a concern of *pshia* — negligence — that one might tear down the old building and then simply never get around to rebuilding. The *gemara* had noted some possible exceptions to this rule.

Now the *gemara* uses this framework to set up a striking question: *Bava ben Buta*, a *tanna* who lived during the time of the second *Beit HaMikdash* and who, as we will see, played a role in advising Herod regarding its rebuilding — how could he have given Herod counsel to tear down the *Beit HaMikdash* and build a more magnificent one in its place? As *Rav Chisda* rules: *lo listarei inish beis knishta ad dbanei beis knishta achrita* — one may not tear down a *beis knesset* until another has been built.

Several observations arise here. First, in the case of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the entire premise of the rule becomes complicated: you cannot build on an alternative plot of land — if ever there is a building that must occupy a specific location, it is the *Beit HaMikdash*. Second, there is the historical question of what exactly happened during Herod's reconstruction. It is not immediately obvious whether *korbanot* continued to be offered throughout the construction or whether there was a full cessation. The *gemara* seems to assume significant disruption, though the principle of *makrivim af al pi she-ein bayit* — that offerings may be brought even without a complete structure — might have provided some relief. Third, and perhaps most striking, is the *gemara*'s framing of the *Beit HaMikdash* simply as a type of *beis knesset*. This is a characteristically rabbinic retrojection — applying later institutional categories to an earlier reality — and it reflects a broader rabbinic tendency to present themselves, rather than the *kohanim*, as the dominant religious authority even during the Temple period.

### Herod's Rebuilding Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הוּא, אִי הֲוַאי יָדַעְנָא דִּזְהִרִי רַבָּנַן כּוּלֵּי הַאי, לָא הֲוָה קָטֵילְנָא לְהוּ; הַשְׁתָּא מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּא כִּבָּה אוֹרוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי נֵר מִצְוָה וְתוֹרָה אוֹר״, יֵלֵךְ וְיַעֲסוֹק בְּאוֹרוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָהֲרוּ אֵלָיו כָּל הַגּוֹיִם״. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, הָכִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּא סִימֵּא עֵינוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה אִם מֵעֵינֵי הָעֵדָה״, יֵלֵךְ וְיִתְעַסֵּק בְּעֵינוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי מְחַלֵּל אֶת מִקְדָּשִׁי, גְּאוֹן עֻזְּכֶם מַחְמַד עֵינֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Herod **said to him: I am he. Had I known that the Sages were so cautious I would not have killed them. Now, what is that man's remedy,** i.e., what can I do to repent for my sinful actions? Bava ben Buta **said to him: He** who **extinguished the light of the world** by killing the Torah Sages, **as it is written: "For the mitzva is a lamp, and the Torah is light"** (Proverbs 6:23), **should go and occupy himself with the light of the world,** the Temple, **as it is written** with regard to the Temple: **"And all the nations shall flow [*venaharu*] unto it"** (Isaiah 2:2), the word *venaharu* alluding to light [*nehora*]. **There are** those **who say** that **this** is what **he said to him: He** who **blinded the eye of the world, as it is written** in reference to the Sages: **"And if** it be committed through ignorance **by the eyes of the congregation"** (Numbers 15:24), **should go and occupy himself with the eye of the world,** the Temple, **as it is written: "I will desecrate my Temple, the pride of your strength, the delight of your eyes"** (Ezekiel 24:21).

The *gemara*'s question is pointed: *heichi aspirei lei eitza leHordus lemisterei leBeis Hamikdash?* — how did *Bava ben Buta* give Herod advice to tear down the *Beit HaMikdash*?

At this point it is worth pausing to consider the historical reality. Herod did not merely renovate the existing structure. He undertook a massive engineering project: leveling the top of *Har HaBayis*, constructing massive retaining walls (the *Kotel* being the most famous), and filling in the space with enormous quantities of earth on three of the four sides to create the vast flat plaza of the *Har HaBayis*. The scale of this project was staggering — a literal uprooting and reshaping of a mountain. This context heightens the *gemara*'s question considerably: this was not a simple reconstruction but a complete destruction and rebuilding of the entire site.

### Possible Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתְּפֵינָא מִמַּלְכוּתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁדַּר שְׁלִיחָא; וְלֵיזִיל שַׁתָּא, וְלִיעַכַּב שַׁתָּא, וְלֶהְדַּר שַׁתָּא; אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי סָתְרַיתְּ [לֵיהּ] וּבָנְיַית [לֵיהּ]. עֲבַד הָכִי. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: אִם לֹא סָתַרְתָּה – אַל תִּסְתּוֹר; וְאִם סָתַרְתָּה – אַל תִּבְנֵי; וְאִם סָתַרְתָּה וּבָנִיתָ – עַבְדֵי בִּישָׁא, בָּתַר דְּעָבְדִין מִתְמַלְכִין. אִם זַיְינָךְ עֲלָךְ, סִפְרָךְ כָּאן – לָא רֵכָא וְלָא בַּר רֵכָא, הוֹרְדוֹס [עַבְדָּא] קָלָנְיָא מִתְעֲבִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Herod **said to him: I am afraid of the** Roman **government,** that they will not permit me to make changes in the Temple. Bava ben Buta **said to him: Send a messenger** who will **travel** there for **a year, and remain** there for another **year, and** take yet another **year** to **return. In the meantime, you can demolish** the Temple **and rebuild it. He did so.** Eventually, **they sent** a message **to** Herod from Rome: **If you have not** yet **demolished it, do not demolish it; and if you have** already **demolished it, do not rebuild it; and if you have demolished it and** already **rebuilt it,** you shall be counted among **those who act wickedly, seeking counsel** only **after they have** already **acted.** Even **if you are armed** and in command of a military force, **your book,** i.e., your genealogical record, **is here.** You are **neither a king [*reikha*] nor the son of a king,** but rather **Herod the slave who has made himself a freeman [*kelonya*].**

The *gemara* offers two answers. The first: *tivya chaza bei* — perhaps they found cracks and structural failures in the existing building, which, as discussed earlier, provides a special license to tear down even a sacred structure. The second: *malchuta shani* — when it is a royal decree or a decision of the king, we do not worry about negligence, because kings have the resources and the will to see their projects through. The *gemara* cites *Shmuel*: *ei amar malchusa akrana turi* — if a king says he will uproot mountains, he will uproot mountains and not retract. Given what we know about Herod's actual feat of reshaping *Har HaBayis*, the phrase *akrana turi* — uprooting mountains — carries more than a little resonance here.

## Herod's Story

### Herod's Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָבָא בֶּן בּוּטָא, הֵיכִי אַסְּבֵיהּ לֵיהּ עֵצָה לְהוֹרְדוֹס לְמִיסְתְּרֵיהּ לְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ? וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא לִיסְתּוֹר אִינִישׁ בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא עַד דְּבָנֵי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא אַחְרִיתָא! אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: תִּיוְהָא חֲזָא בֵּיהּ, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַלְכוּתָא שָׁאנֵי, דְּלָא הָדְרָא בֵּיהּ; דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, אִי אָמַר מַלְכוּתָא: ״עָקַרְנָא טוּרֵי״ – עָקַר טוּרֵי וְלָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ. הוֹרְדוֹס עַבְדָּא דְּבֵית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי הֲוָה. נָתַן עֵינָיו בְּאוֹתָהּ תִּינוֹקֶת. יוֹמָא חַד, שְׁמַע הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בַּת קָלָא דְּאָמַר: כָּל עַבְדָּא דְּמָרֵיד הַשְׁתָּא, מַצְלַח. קָם קַטְלִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ מָרְוָתֵיהּ, וְשַׁיְּירַהּ לְהַהִיא יָנוֹקְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How could Bava ben Buta have advised Herod to raze the Temple** and build another in its place, as will be described later? **But doesn't Rav Ḥisda say** that **a person must not demolish a synagogue unless he** first **builds another synagogue** to take its place? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that **he saw cracks in** the old Temple structure. And **if you wish, say** that actions taken by **the government are different, as** the government **does not go back** on its decisions. Therefore, there is no need to be concerned about negligence, as there is in the case of ordinary people. **As Shmuel says: If the government says** it will **uproot mountains, it will uproot mountains and not retract** its word. § **Herod was a slave in the house of the Hasmoneans. He set his eyes upon a certain young girl** from the house of the Hasmoneans. **One day that man,** Herod, **heard a Divine Voice that said: Any slave who rebels now will succeed. He rose up** and **killed all his masters, but spared that girl.**

With the *Beit HaMikdash* as the backdrop, the *gemara* launches into a remarkable aggadic account of Herod's rise. *Hordos avda de'bei Chashmonai hava* — Herod was a slave in the house of the *Chashmonaim*. He cast his eyes upon one of the daughters of the *Chashmonai* family, developing an intense desire for her. One day he heard a *bas kol* — understood here as a heavenly voice, though *Saul Lieberman* has noted that the phrase can sometimes mean simply an echo or rumor — declaring: *kol avda de'marid hashta matzlach* — any slave who rebels today will succeed. Seizing the moment, Herod rose up and killed all of his masters — *kam katlinhu le'khulu marosei* — wiping out the entire house of the *Chashmonaim*, save for the one young woman he desired. Whether his primary motivation was lust or political ambition, the *gemara* presents it as the former: his driving impulse was his desire for the girl, and retaining her would simultaneously satisfy that desire and lend his reign a degree of legitimacy.

### Chashmonai Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי חֲזָת הָהִיא יָנוֹקְתָּא דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִינְסְבַהּ, סְלִיקָא לְאִיגָּרָא, וּרְמָא קָלָא אֲמַרָה: ״כֹּל מַאן דְּאָתֵי וְאָמַר: מִבֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי קָאָתֵינָא – עַבְדָּא הוּא, דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּיַירָא מִינַּיְיהוּ אֶלָּא הַהִיא יָנוֹקְתָּא, וְהַהִיא יָנוֹקְתָּא נְפַלָה מֵאִיגָּרָא לְאַרְעָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **When that girl saw that he wanted to marry her, she went up to the roof and raised her voice,** and **said: Whoever comes and says: I come from the house of the Hasmoneans, is a slave, since only that girl,** i.e., I, **remained from them. And that girl fell from the roof to the ground** and died.

When the young woman realized that Herod intended to marry her, she went up to the rooftop, raised her voice, and made a public proclamation: *kol man de'ati ve'amar mibeis Chashmonai kasina avda hu* — anyone who hereafter claims descent from the house of the *Chashmonaim* is in fact a slave. She declared herself the sole surviving true descendant of that house, and then threw herself from the roof to her death. Her act was both a declaration of the illegitimacy of Herod's rule and a final, dramatic embodiment of the downfall of her dynasty.

### Body Preservation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַמְנַהּ שְׁבַע שְׁנִין בְּדוּבְשָׁא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בָּא עָלֶיהָ, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: לָא בָּא עָלֶיהָ. דְּאָמְרִי לַהּ בָּא עָלֶיהָ, הָא דְּטַמְנַהּ – לְיַתּוֹבֵיהּ לְיִצְרֵיהּ. וּדְאָמְרִי לַהּ לֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ, הַאי דְּטַמְנַהּ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵאֱמְרוּ: בַּת מֶלֶךְ נְסַב.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that Herod **preserved** the girl's body **in honey for seven years** to prevent it from decaying. **There are** those **who say** that **he engaged in necrophilia with her** corpse and **there are** those **who say he did not engage in necrophilia with her** corpse. According to those **who say he engaged in necrophilia with her** corpse, the reason **that he preserved her** body was **to gratify his** carnal **desires. And** according to those **who say he did not engage in necrophilia with her** corpse, the reason **that he preserved her** body was **so that** people **would say he married a king's daughter.**

Herod preserved her body in honey for seven years. The *gemara* records a dispute about his motivation: *ika de'amri ba'ala* — some say he continued to have intercourse with her even after her death; *ika de'amri lo ba'ala* — others say he did not. According to those who say he did not, he preserved the body either to satisfy his desire in some other manner, or, more politically, to maintain the appearance that he was still married to a *Chashmonai* princess — lending his rule an aura of legitimacy.

### Slave Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: מַאן דָּרֵישׁ ״מִקֶּרֶם אַחֶיךָ תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ״? רַבָּנַן. קָם קַטְלִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, שַׁבְקֵיהּ לְבָבָא בֶּן בּוּטָא לְמִשְׁקַל עֵצָה מִנֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Herod **said** to himself: **Who expounds** the verse: **"One from among your brothers you shall set as king over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15) as meaning that he who is appointed as king must come from a Jewish family and cannot be an emancipated slave or a convert? It is **the Sages** who expound the verse in this manner, insisting that a king must have Jewish roots. **He** then **rose up and killed all the Sages,** but **spared Bava ben Buta in order to take counsel with him.**

Having seized power, Herod turned to the question of his own legitimacy. He asked: *man darish mikerev achicha tasim aleicha melech* — who are those who expound the verse that one must place over oneself a king "from among your brothers," *veha demi'at lemididi avda* — and who thereby exclude me as a slave? The answer: *rabbanan* — the rabbis. Once again the *gemara* presents the rabbis as the dominant religious authorities of the period, a retrojection of later realities. *Tosafos* raises the obvious question: if it is an explicit verse, why do you need the rabbis to expound it? The answer is that while the verse's plain meaning excludes non-Jews, it is the rabbis who extend the exclusion to include slaves. The rabbis, despite their partial obligation in *mitzvos*, made themselves, in Herod's view, the source of his political vulnerability.

### Slaughter of Rabbis

*Kam katlinu lechulu rabbanan* — Herod went and killed all the rabbis. *Tosafos* notes that this cannot be taken literally, since we know that the *Bnei Beseira* were still present when *Hillel* arrived from *Bavel* shortly thereafter. Rather, it means the vast majority were killed. The one he spared was *Bava ben Buta* — *shavkei leBava ben Buta lemishkal eiza minei* — he kept him alive because he recognized his wisdom and wanted to retain him as an advisor.

### Herod-Bava Encounter

Having blinded *Bava ben Buta* by means of a crown of thorns pressed inward against the eyes, Herod one day came and sat before him without identifying himself. The blind *tanna* could not see who was speaking to him. Herod presented himself as an ordinary person and posed a question: *chazi mar hai avda bisha mai ka avid* — have you seen what this evil slave is doing? — meaning Herod himself, to test how the rabbi would respond.

### Bava's Responses

*Bava ben Buta*'s responses unfold in several stages. First, when Herod suggests cursing this figure, *Bava* declines on halachic grounds: *gam bemada'acha melech al tekalel* — even in your private chamber, do not curse a king. Herod pushes back: but he is not a real king. *Bava* responds: even if he is merely wealthy, *uvechadrei mishkavcha al tekalel ashir* — do not curse a wealthy man. And even if he is simply a *nasi*, a communal leader, *venasi ve'amecha lo ta'or* — do not curse a prince among your people.

Herod then presses further: but the verse says *be'amecha* — "among your people" — implying one who acts as a member of the community, not one who does evil. So perhaps cursing is permitted? *Bava* responds: *mistafina minei* — I am afraid of him. This is a notably different register — no longer a principled halachic argument, but honest self-preservation. Herod continues: there is no one here to carry your words back to him. *Bava* replies: *ki of hashamayim yolich et hakol u'va'al knafayim yagid davar* — the birds of heaven will carry the sound, and a winged creature will tell the matter. I will say nothing.

At this point Herod reveals himself: *ana hu* — I am he. And he says: *i hava yadana de'zehirei rabanan kulei hai, lo hava katilina lehu* — had I known that the rabbis were this careful, this guarded in their speech and conduct, I would not have killed them. It is worth noting that *Bava*'s final motivation was caution rather than loyalty — he was not swearing allegiance to Herod, only expressing that it was imprudent to speak. And yet the point stands: whatever the motivation, the rabbis' behavior toward a ruler, however illegitimate, was circumspect, and Herod regrets not having understood that before.

### Herod's Regret

Herod now asks: *hashta mai takantei de'hu gavra* — what is his remedy? What can he now do to make amends? *Bava ben Buta* responds: *hu kiva oro shel olam* — you extinguished the light of the world, *de'khitiv ki ner mitzvah ve'torah or* — as it is written, a commandment is a candle and Torah is light. The rabbis were the world's source of illumination. Therefore: *yelech ve'ye'asok be'oro shel olam* — go and occupy yourself with the light of the world, a reference to the *Beit HaMikdash*, for *venaharu elav kol hagoyim* — all the nations will stream toward it, and the word *venaharu* echoes the word for light.

### Light & Eyes

A second version is also recorded. Some say *Bava ben Buta* framed it differently: *hu suma eino shel olam* — you blinded the eye of the world. The *gemara* cites the verse *haya me'einei ha'edah* — from the eyes of the community, which the rabbis read as referring to the *Sanhedrin*. Therefore: *yelech v'yitasek be'eino shel olam* — go and occupy yourself with the eye of the world, the *Beit HaMikdash*, for *hineni mechalel et mikdashi ge'on uzchem machmad eineichem* — behold I am desecrating my sanctuary, the glory of your strength, the desire of your eyes.

The imagery here is layered and striking. *Bava ben Buta* himself has been blinded, and the language of light and sight runs through the entire passage. But there is also a historical irony worth noting: in the actual sequence of Jewish history, the *Beit HaMikdash* was the center of religious life and was later replaced, after its destruction, by rabbinic Judaism. Here the *gemara* inverts that relationship — the rabbis are the primary source of light and sanctity, and the *Beit HaMikdash* is offered as a kind of substitute or compensation for their loss.

### Roman Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״רֵכָא״? מַלְכוּתָא – דִּכְתִיב: ״אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם רַךְ וּמָשׁוּחַ מֶלֶךְ״. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, מֵהָכָא: ״וַיִּקְרְאוּ לְפָנָיו אַבְרֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the word ***reikha*?** It denotes **royalty, as it is written: "I am today a tender [*rakh*] and anointed king"** (II Samuel 3:39). **And if you wish, say** that the meaning of the word is learned **from here,** from the term describing Joseph after he was appointed viceroy to the king: **"And they cried before him, *Avrekh*"** (Genesis 41:43).

Having decided to rebuild, Herod faces an obstacle: *mistafina mimachusa* — I am afraid of the Roman government. I cannot act without their permission. *Bava ben Buta* advises: *shadar shlicha* — send a messenger, let him take a year traveling there, *v'leakev shata* — spend a year waiting for an audience, *v'leihadar shata* — and take a year to return. That gives you three years. *Adei hachi v'hachi* — by the time he comes back, *sastis lei v'banistas lei* — you will have already torn it down and rebuilt it. Act first; obtain permission after. This reflects a recognizable principle: it is easier to secure forgiveness than permission.

The *gemara* notes that *Rashi* in the last comment on the relevant passage references this story as written in *Yosifon ben Goryon*. It should be noted that *Yosifon ben Goryon* is not the Greek Josephus but a medieval Hebrew work that presented itself as a translation of Josephus — essentially a literary forgery, which explains why many *rishonim* who cite it find parallels to the *gemara*'s accounts but not to Josephus directly.

### Rome's Response

The response eventually came back: *im lo satarta al tistur* — if you have not yet torn it down, do not. *V'im satarta al tivni* — if you have already torn it down, do not rebuild it. *V'im satarta oovanita* — and if you have torn it down and rebuilt it: *avda bisha batar d'avdin mis'malkhin* — a bad slave, who acts first and then asks. The Roman response then turns contemptuous: *im zayanakh alakh* — so you think your weapons give you authority? *Siphrakha kan* — we have your records here in Rome. *Lo racha v'lo bar racha* — not a king and not the son of a king. *Hordis avda kalnya mit'avid* — Herod the slave has made himself into a free man. The *gemara* then clarifies the term *racha*: *dichtiv anochi hayom racha umashuach melech* — I today am young and have been anointed king — indicating *racha* means a king, linguistically cognate with the Latin *Rex*. An alternative derivation is also offered: *v'yakre'u lefanav avrech*, the proclamation before Yosef in Egypt.

## Beit HM Construction

### Construction Beauty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מִי שֶׁלֹּא רָאָה בִּנְיַן הוֹרְדוֹס, לֹא רָאָה בִּנְיָן נָאֶה [מִיָּמָיו]. בְּמַאי בַּנְיֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּאַבְנֵי שִׁישָׁא וּמַרְמְרָא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּאַבְנֵי כּוּחְלָא, שִׁישָׁא וּמַרְמְרָא. אַפֵּיק שָׂפָה וְעַיֵּיל שָׂפָה, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּנְקַבֵּיל סִידָא. סְבַר לְמִשְׁעֲיֵיהּ בְּדַהֲבָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁבְקֵיהּ, דְּהָכִי שַׁפִּיר טְפֵי, דְּמִיחְזֵי כִּי אִידְווֹתָא דְיַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say: One who has not seen Herod's building has never seen a beautiful building in his life.** The Gemara asks: **With what did he build it? Rabba said: With stones of white and green marble [*umarmara*]. There are** those **who say** that he built it **with stones of blue, white, and green marble.** Alternate rows of stones **sent out an edge** a bit **and drew in an edge** a bit, **so that they would** better **receive** and hold **the plaster. He considered covering it with gold,** but **the Rabbis said to him: Leave it,** and do not cover it, **since it is more beautiful this** way, **as it looks like the waves of the sea.**

Having recounted Herod's rise to power, his slaughter of the rabbis, his encounter with *Bava ben Buta*, and his decision to rebuild, the *gemara* now turns to the construction itself. *Mi shelo ra binyan Hordis, lo ra binyan na'eh miyamav* — whoever did not see Herod's building has never seen a beautiful edifice in their life. With what did he build it? *Raba* says: with stones of *shaisha u'marmara* — according to *Rashi*, a kind of green and white marble. Some say *kuchla shaisha u'marmara* — blue and white marble. *Apheik sapha v'ayeil sapha* — the stones were laid so that the facing protruded and receded in alternating rows, creating a wave-like pattern across the facade. The purpose given is *ki heichi d'nikabel saida* — so that it could receive plaster or some kind of bonding material between the rows. As is now known from archaeology, Herodian masonry is indeed characterized by its distinctive embossed framing, with each stone slightly recessed at the face for stability.

### Wave Pattern

When those supervising the construction considered smoothing over the front entirely with gold — *savar l'mishayei b'dahava* — the rabbis intervened: *shavkei* — leave it as it is. *D'hachi shapir tvei* — it looks better this way. *D'mechzei ki avata d'yama* — it looks like the waves of the sea. The blue and white marble undulating in and out across the facade created a visual effect of ocean waves, and that, the rabbis ruled, was more beautiful than gold plating.

### Bava's Complicity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָבָא בַּר בּוּטָא הֵיכִי עֲבַד הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ דָּנִיֵּאל? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִשִּׂיא עֵצָה לִנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָהֵן מַלְכָּא, מִלְכִּי יִשְׁפַּר עֲלָךְ, וַחֲטָאָיךְ בְּצִדְקָה פְרֻק, וַעֲוָיָתָךְ בְּמִחַן עֲנָיִן, הֵן תֶּהֱוֵי אַרְכָא לִשְׁלֵוְתָךְ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״כֹּלָּא מְּטָא עַל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר מַלְכָּא״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְלִקְצָת יַרְחִין תְּרֵי עֲשַׂר וְגוֹ׳״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how did Bava ben Buta do this,** i.e., give advice to Herod the wicked? **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** who says: **For what** reason **was Daniel punished? Because he offered advice to Nebuchadnezzar, as** after sharing a harsh prophecy with him, **it is stated: "Therefore, O king, let my counsel be acceptable to you, redeem your sins with charity and your iniquities with graciousness to the poor, that there may be a lengthening of your prosperity"** (Daniel 4:24). **And it is written: "All this came upon King Nebuchadnezzar"** (Daniel 4:25). **And it is written: "And at the end of twelve months"** (Daniel 4:26). Only after a year was the prophecy fulfilled but not before that, apparently because Nebuchadnezzar heeded Daniel's advice.

This brings the *gemara* back to the question that was deferred: *u'Baba Ben Buta heichi avid hachi* — how did *Bava ben Buta* become complicit in advising and cooperating with Herod, an evil king, even for a noble purpose? The *gemara* raises a ruling: *Rava bar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — and some say it was *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — asks why Daniel was punished. The answer: *mipnei she'hisi eitza l'Nevuchadnezzar* — because he gave advice to Nebuchadnezzar. When Nebuchadnezzar had his disturbing dream, Daniel counseled him: *lehein malka malchi yishpar alach v'chatayach b'tzidka perok* — your sins should be redeemed through charity, *v'avayasach b'mikan anayin* — and your iniquity through kindness to the poor. This was good advice, intended to benefit others, and it did succeed — the dream's fulfillment was delayed twelve months. And yet Daniel was punished for it.

The lesson the *gemara* draws is that one should not give advice to wicked kings even when the advice itself is beneficial. Merely being complicit in an evil king's enterprise — even in a positive capacity — carries moral risk. The evidence that Daniel was in fact punished: he appears later in the book of Esther as *Hatach*, and *Rav* identifies *Hatach* as Daniel. If the word *Hatach* indicates he had been cut down from his former greatness — *shehatkhuhu migedulato* — then his degraded status as a mere messenger demonstrates he was punished. If instead *Hatach* means he was the one through whose mouth all royal decrees were sealed, then the evidence of his punishment comes from elsewhere: *deshadyuhu leguva de'aryavata* — he was thrown into the lions' den.

## Beit HM Construction…

### Justifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי עַבְדָּא, דְּאִיחַיַּיב בְּמִצְוֹת. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, דְּאִי לָא מַלְכוּת – לָא מִתְבְּנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that **a slave** like Herod **is different since he is obligated in the mitzvot,** and therefore Bava ben Buta had to help him repent. **And if you wish, say the Temple is different, as without** the help of **the government it would not have been built.**

Two answers are offered to resolve how *Bava ben Buta* could have done what Daniel was punished for. First: *shani avda dechayev bemitzvot* — it is different because Herod, as a slave, is obligated in *mitzvos*, and therefore we take an interest in helping him fulfill them. This answer is admittedly difficult to understand — surely the concern should be the benefit to the broader population, not the spiritual welfare of a murderous king. Second, and more compelling: *shani beis hamikdash de'i la malkhuta la mivni* — the *Beit HaMikdash* is different, because without royal involvement and resources, it simply could not have been built. This warrants a level of engagement and complicity that other situations would not.

The broader tension running through this entire passage is, as one might put it, the deep ambivalence about how to respond to illegitimate political power. It is dangerous to engage; there is also an ethical and religious imperative to do so in certain circumstances; there may be exceptions; and the danger remains. The *gemara* holds all of these tensions without resolving them neatly.

## Wall Ownership Markers

### Custom Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה. ״הַכֹּל״ – לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי אַתְרָא דִּנְהִיגִי בְּהוּצָא וְדַפְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In a place where it is customary to build a wall of non-chiseled stone, or chiseled stone, or small bricks, or large bricks, they must build the partition with that material. **Everything is in accordance with the regional custom.** The Gemara asks: **What** does the word **everything** serve **to add?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add a place where it is customary** to build a partition **out of palm and laurel branches.** In such a place, the partition is built from those materials.

Returning from the lengthy digression about Herod and the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *gemara* turns back to the *mishna*'s foundational rule: *hakol keminhag hamedina* — everything follows the local custom. The *gemara* asks what the word *hakol* ("everything") comes to include beyond the basic cases. The answer: *le'atuyei atra denehigi behutza vedafna* — to include a place where the custom is to build walls from branches or wood. Even such a wall, despite not being made of stone or brick, is considered a legitimate fulfillment of the obligation if that is the local norm.

### Ownership Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ, אִם נָפַל הַכּוֹתֶל – הַמָּקוֹם וְהָאֲבָנִים שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּנְפַל לִרְשׁוּתָא דְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, אִי נָמֵי דְּפַנִּינְהוּ חַד לִרְשׁוּתָא דִידֵיהּ; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נִיהְוֵי אִידַּךְ ״הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה״, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Therefore, if the wall** later **falls,** the assumption is that **the space** where the wall stood **and the stones belong to both of them,** to be divided equally. The Gemara questions the need for this ruling: Isn't it **obvious** that this is the case, since both neighbors participated in the construction of the wall? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* for a case **where** the entire wall **fell into the domain of one of them. Alternatively,** it is necessary in a case **where one of them** already **cleared** all the stones **into his** own **domain. Lest you say** that **the other** party **should be** governed by the principle that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant,** that is, if the other party should have to prove that he had been a partner in the construction of the wall, the mishna **teaches us** that they are presumed to have been partners in the building of the wall, and neither requires further proof.

The *mishna* then addresses what happens when a jointly built wall collapses. The *makom veha'avanim shel shneihem* — the space and the stones belong to both of them equally, since the presumption is that any wall between two properties was built jointly and stands on both parties' land. The *gemara* says this is not obvious: the case where this principle is needed is *denafal lerushuta dechad mineihu* — where all the stones fell into one person's domain, or where one party moved them all to his own side. In that case one might think we should treat it as a *safek* (doubt) and rule that the one in possession has the advantage. The *gemara* rules: *lo tzricha* — no, because the standing presumption is that the wall was always jointly owned, and that presumption overrides the physical fact of where the stones have landed.

### Garden vs Valley

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּגִינָּה, מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִגְדּוֹר – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ: וְכֵן בְּגִינָּה, מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִגְדּוֹר – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; הָא סְתָמָא – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל בִּקְעָה, מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִגְדּוֹר – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; הָא סְתָמָא – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And similarly with regard to a garden,** in **a place where it is customary to build a partition** in the middle of a garden jointly owned by two people, and one of them wishes to build such a partition, the court **obligates** his neighbor to join in building the partition. The Gemara comments: **This** matter **itself** is **difficult.** On the one hand, **you said: And similarly with regard to a garden,** in **a place where it is customary to build a partition** in the middle of a garden jointly owned by two people, and one of them wishes to build such a partition, the court **obligates** his neighbor to join in building the partition. One can infer **that ordinarily,** where there is no custom, the court **does not obligate him** to build a partition. But **say the latter clause** of the mishna: **But** with regard to an expanse of **fields,** in **a place where it is customary not to build a partition** between two people's fields, and one person wishes to build a partition between his field and that of his neighbor, the court **does not obligate** his neighbor to build such a partition. One can infer **that ordinarily,** where there is no custom, the court **obligates him** to build a partition.

The *mishna* states: *vechein begina makom shenahagu ligdor mechayvin oto* — similarly, in a garden, in a place where it is the practice to build a fence, one is obligated to do so. The simple reading of this would imply that if it is not a place where the practice is to build a fence, one is not obligated. Looking at the *seifa* of the *mishna*: *aval bebika makom shenahagu shelo ligdor ein mechayvin oto* — but in a valley, where it is the practice not to build a wall, one is not obligated. The implication of this *seifa* would be that in a valley without such a practice, one is obligated. But this makes no sense: if in a garden — where there is far more reason for a wall — the default is not to require one, how could the default in an open valley be to require one? The *gemara* declares: *hagufa kashya* — the text itself is difficult.

### Mishnah Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכֵן סְתָם גִּינָּה, וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִגְדּוֹר בְּבִקְעָה – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, מַאי ״אֲבָל״? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְכֵן סְתָם גִּינָּה – כִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִגְדּוֹר דָּמֵי, וּמְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. אֲבָל סְתָם בִּקְעָה – כִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא נָהֲגוּ דָּמֵי, וְאֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying: And similarly** with regard to **an ordinary garden, and** also **in a place where it is customary to build a partition in** an expanse of **fields,** the court **obligates him** to build a partition. **Rava said to him: If so, what** is the point of the word: **But,** mentioned afterward in connection with an expanse of fields, which seems to indicate that the issue of fields had not yet been addressed? **Rather, Rava said** that **this** is what the *tanna* **is teaching: And similarly an ordinary garden is** treated **like a place where it is customary to build a partition, and** therefore the court **obligates him** to build a partition. **But an ordinary** expanse of **fields is** treated **like a place where it is customary not** to build a partition, **and** therefore the court **does not obligate him** to build one.

*Abaye* offers one reading: *stam gina* — a standard garden carries an obligation, and *uvmakom shenahagu ligdor bebika* — in a valley where there is a practice to build a wall, you are also obligated. This reads the two clauses as addressing two separate cases. *Rava* objects: *im kein mai aval* — if that is so, what is the force of the word "but" in the *seifa*? You would have already addressed the valley case in the first clause. *Rava* therefore proposes his own reading: *stam gina kemakom shenahagu ligdor dami* — a standard garden is treated as a place where the practice is to build a wall, and therefore you are obligated; *aval stam bika kemakom shelo nahagu dami* — but a standard valley is treated as a place where the practice is not to build a wall, and therefore you are not obligated. Rather than reading the *mishna* as conditional ("if there is a practice"), we read it as definitional: a garden simply is the type of place where the norm is to build, and a valley simply is the type of place where it is not.

### Unilateral Construction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִם רָצָה, כּוֹנֵס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וּבוֹנֶה, וְעוֹשֶׂה חֲזִית. מַאי ״חֲזִית״? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אַכְפְּיֵהּ לֵיהּ לְקַרְנָא לְבַר. וְנַעֲבֵיד מִלְּגָיו! עָבֵיד חַבְרֵיהּ נָמֵי מִלְּבַר, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – גָּיֵיז לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ, וַאֲמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא! גִּיזּוּזָא מִידָּע יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rather, if** one person **wishes** to erect a partition, **he must withdraw into his own** field **and build** the partition there. **And he makes** a border **mark on the outer side** of the barrier facing his neighbor's property, indicating that he built the entire structure of his own materials and on his own land. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of a border **mark? Rav Huna said: He bends the edge** of the wall **toward the outside.** The Gemara suggests: **Let him make it on the inside.** The Gemara explains: In that case, **his neighbor might also make** a mark **on the outside,** that is, on the side facing his own property, **and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara responds: **If so,** that is, there is a concern about such deception, **now also** when the person who builds the wall makes a border mark on the outer side of the wall, the neighbor **might cut it off and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara answers: Such **a cut is noticeable** and the deception will not work.

After establishing when an obligation to build a wall exists and when it does not, the *mishna* turns to a scenario where one party wants to build but cannot obligate the other: *ela im rotze kones letoch shelo bone ve'ose hazit* — if he wishes, he may recede into his own property, build the wall entirely on his land with his own materials, and create a *chazit* — a marking or facing that signals the wall's exclusive ownership. The concern is that a hundred years hence, when the wall collapses and no one remembers its history, the presumption will be that it was a jointly owned wall. The *chazit* is the mechanism for preserving proof of sole ownership.

### Chazit Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִיכְפָּא לְקַרְנָא מִלְּגָיו. וְנֶעְבַּד מִלְּבַר! גָּיֵיז לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי לָיֵיף לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא! לִיפּוּפָא מִידָּע יְדִיעַ. וְהָא ״מִבַּחוּץ״ קָתָנֵי! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that in answer to the question: What is the meaning of a border mark, **Rav Huna said: He bends the edge** of the wall **toward the inside.** The Gemara suggests: **Let him make it on the outside.** The Gemara explains: In that case, **his neighbor might cut it off and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that is, there is a concern for such deception, **now also** when the person who builds the wall makes a border mark toward the inside, **his neighbor might add** a border mark on his own side **and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara answers: **An addition is noticeable** and the deception will not work. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the mishna **teach** that he makes the border mark **on the outside** and not on the inside? The Gemara comments: This is **a difficulty.**

The *gemara* explores what exactly constitutes a valid *chazit*. *Rav Huna* says: *kofya lekarna levar* — you turn the corner of the wall outward, so that rather than a flat corner it protrudes on the neighbor's side. The *gemara* asks: why not turn it inward? Because then the neighbor could simply add a corresponding corner on his side and claim joint ownership. And if you turn it outward, can't the neighbor simply cut the corner off? No, *gezuyei meida yedia* — the cutting will be visible.

A second version reverses the positions: *Rav Huna* says turn the corner inward, *miligav*. The *gemara* asks: why not outward? Because the neighbor could cut it off. And inward — won't he just add an inward corner on his side? No, *lifufa meida yedia* — the addition will be visible. The *gemara* notes a tension with the *mishna*, which appears to specify that the *chazit* goes on the outside, *milivar* — and rules: *kashya*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁעֲיֵיהּ בְּאַמְּתָא מִלְּבַר. וְנֶיעֱבַד מִלְּגָיו! עָבֵיד חַבְרֵיהּ מִלְּבַר, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – דְּקָפֵיל לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא! קִילּוּפָא מִידָּע יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The party who builds the wall **should smear it** with clay **up to a cubit** at the top of the wall **on the outside.** The Gemara suggests: **Let him do** this **on the inside.** The Gemara explains: In that case, **his neighbor might** also **do** it **on the outside,** that is, on the side facing his own property, **and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara responds: **If so,** that is, if there is a concern for such deception, **now also** when the person who builds the wall smears it with clay on the outside, **his neighbor can peel it off and say:** The wall **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara explains: **Peeling** clay **is noticeable** and the deception will not succeed.

*Rabbi Yochanan* offers a different method: *meshayaya be'amah milivar* — you plaster over an additional layer on the neighbor's side of the wall. This indicates the wall is yours. Why not plaster on your own side? Because then the neighbor could plaster on his side and claim it is jointly his. If you plaster on his side, can't he just remove it? He could, but *kelifa meida yedia* — the removal of plaster will be visible, though this argument may be harder to sustain over long periods of time.

### Branch Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּצָא – אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: סַינּוֹפֵי יְרֵיכֵי מִלְּבַר. וְנֶיעְבַּד מִלְּגָיו! עָבֵיד נָמֵי חַבְרֵיהּ מִלְּבַר, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – גָּיֵיז וְשָׁדֵי לֵיהּ, וְאָמַר: דִּידִי וְדִידֵיהּ הוּא! מַשְׁרֵיק לֵיהּ טִינָא. הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – אָתֵי חַבְרֵיהּ וְקָלֵיף לֵיהּ! קִילּוּפָא מִידָּע יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If he builds a partition of **palm branches,** how does he make a border mark? **Rav Naḥman said: He directs the tips of the branches to the outside.** The Gemara suggests: **Let him do** this **to the inside.** The Gemara states: If he so directs the branches, **his neighbor might also do** the same **to the outside,** that is, to the side facing his own property, **and say:** The partition **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara objects: **If so,** that is, if there is concern for such deception, **now also** when the one who builds the partition directs the tips of the branches to the outside, the neighbor **can cut off** the tips **and throw** them away, **and say:** The partition **is** both **mine and his.** The Gemara counsels: **He should smear** the tips of the palm branches **with clay.** The Gemara comments: **Now also, the neighbor might come and peel** the clay off. The Gemara answers: **Peeling is noticeable.**

For walls made of branches or wood — *hutza* — the *gemara* considers parallel methods: bending the branches outward so that the points face the neighbor's side, indicating ownership. Similar arguments about tampering and visibility are made.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: הוּצָא לֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** One who builds a partition of **palm branches has no remedy** to prove who erected it, **except with** a written **document.** The neighbor should draw up a document stating that he has no claim to the space or to the partition, because they belong exclusively to the other neighbor.

*Abaye* concludes: *hutza leit lei takanta ela beshtara* — for a branch wall, none of these physical markers will be sufficient as a permanent proof. The only adequate protection is a *shtar* — a legal document — signed by the other party, acknowledging sole ownership.

### Joint Markers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם עָשׂוּ מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִפַּרְזִיקָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לָא יַעֲשׂוּ לֹא זֶה וְלֹא זֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּקְדֵים חַד מִנַּיְיהוּ וַעֲבַד דִּידֵיהּ, וְאִי לָא עָבֵיד חַבְרֵיהּ – אָמַר: דִּידֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Nevertheless, in a place where it is not customary to build a partition between two people's fields, **if they made** such a partition **with the agreement of the two of them,** they build it in the middle, i.e., on the property line, and make a border mark on the one side and on the other side. Therefore, if the wall later falls, the assumption is that the space where the wall stood and the stones belong to both of them, to be divided equally. **Rava from Parzika said to Rav Ashi: Neither this one nor that one should make** a border mark. Rav Ashi **said to him: No,** this ruling **is necessary** in a case **where one of them went ahead and made** a border mark **for himself,** so that **if his neighbor does not make** one as well, the first one **will say** that **it is** entirely **his.**

The *mishna* then introduces the case where both parties agreed to build a wall together in a place where there would otherwise be no obligation — such as in an open valley. In that case, *simanim* are made on both sides. The *gemara* asks: why not simply make no *simanim* at all? The answer: the provision is needed for a scenario where one party has already gone ahead and made a mark on his side. At that point, the other party must respond by marking his side as well; otherwise, the first party could claim exclusive ownership.

### Anti-Fraud Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא – תַּקַּנְתָּא לְרַמַּאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְרֵישָׁא – לָאו תַּקַּנְתָּא לְרַמַּאי הוּא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rava from Parzika asked him: **And is the *tanna*** of the mishna **teaching us a remedy** to be used **against a swindler?** Rav Ashi **said to him: But isn't the former clause** of the mishna's ruling also **a remedy** to be used **against a swindler?** That clause teaches that one who builds a wall should make a border mark to indicate that the wall is his.

*Rav Papa mi-Prazika* asks *Rav Ashi*: in the case of the jointly built wall, why not simply make no *simanim* on either side? *Rav Ashi* explains: we are addressing the case where one party has already marked his side, necessitating a response.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא – תְּנָא דִּינָא, וּמִשּׁוּם דִּינָא תְּנָא תַּקַּנְתָּא; אֶלָּא סֵיפָא, דִּינָא קָתָנֵי – דְּקָתָנֵי תַּקַּנְתָּא?! אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָכָא בְּהוּצֵי עָסְקִינַן, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּאַבַּיֵּי – דְּאָמַר: הוּצָא לֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָרָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּבַחֲזִית סַגִּיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava from Parzika **said to him: Granted,** in **the former clause** the *tanna* **taught the *halakha*** that if one wishes to build a partition between his own field and that of his neighbor, he does so at his own expense and on his own land, **and due to** the need to teach that ***halakha* he** also **taught a remedy** to be used against a swindler. **But does he teach a *halakha*** in **the latter clause,** so **that he** also **teaches a remedy** to be used against a swindler? **Ravina said: Here,** in the latter clause, **we are dealing with** a barrier made of **palm branches.** This is **to the exclusion** of the opinion **of Abaye, who said:** One who builds a partition of **palm branches has no remedy** to prove who erected it, **except with** a written **document. He teaches us that** a border **mark suffices.**

*Rav Papa* then raises a meta-question: *ve-tani takanta le-ramai?* — is it the *Tanna*'s business to be teaching people how to protect themselves against cheats? Perhaps the *mishna* should only be teaching law, not practical protective measures against fraud.

The *gemara* responds: *bishlama reisha tana dina* — in the *reisha*, the primary teaching is the *halacha* itself: in a valley, one cannot be compelled to build; if one builds unilaterally, it must be entirely on one's own property. Having established that *din*, the *mishna* naturally follows through with how one can protect oneself. *ומשום de-dina tana takanta* — because of the underlying *din*, the protective measure is mentioned as well. But in the *seifa*, where both parties have agreed to build jointly, there is no novel *din* being taught — of course a joint wall may be built jointly. So the *seifa* appears to exist solely in order to teach the protective measure, which seems out of place.

### Extra Clauses

This puzzle — why the *Tanna* includes an apparently redundant or extra clause — reflects a broader method of *mishna* interpretation. Just as the rabbis *dorshen* extra letters or words in a verse to derive halachic lessons, so too an extra clause in a *mishna* is assumed to be teaching something beyond what might be logically derived otherwise.

### Ravina's Explanation

This brings the *gemara* back to the question that was deferred: *u'Baba Ben Buta heichi avid hachi* — how did *Bava ben Buta* become complicit in advising and cooperating with Herod, an evil king, even for a noble purpose? The broader tension running through this entire passage is, as one might put it, the deep ambivalence about how to respond to illegitimate political power. It is dangerous to engage; there is also an ethical and religious imperative to do so in certain circumstances; there may be exceptions; and the danger remains. The *gemara* holds all of these tensions without resolving them neatly. *Ravina* resolves the issue: *hacha be-huzia askinan le-afukei mi-de-Abaye* — the *seifa* is speaking specifically about a branch wall, and it comes to reject *Abaye*'s position. *Abaye* ruled that for a branch wall there is no effective *chazit* and one needs a *shtar*. The *mishna*'s extra clause comes to teach: *de-be-chazi sagia* — a *chazit* suffices even for a branch wall. The *seifa* is not primarily there to advise against fraud; rather, it is there to extend the category of walls for which a physical *chazit* is valid, contrary to *Abaye*'s more restrictive ruling. The extra clause teaches through its very redundancy: since we did not need it to teach the basic *din*, it must be providing new information — namely, that a branch wall can indeed be marked with a *chazit*.

## Surrounding Walls

### Three & Fourth Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּקִּיף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ רוּחוֹתָיו, וְגָדַר אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה וְאֶת הַשְּׁלִישִׁית – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אִם עָמַד וְגָדַר אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who surrounds another on three sides,** that is, he owns parcels of land on three sides of the other person's field, **and he built a partition** on **the first, the second, and the third** sides, the court **does not obligate** the neighbor who owns the inner field to contribute to the construction of the partition if he does not wish to do so. **Rabbi Yosei says: If he arose and built a partition** on **the fourth** side of the field, the court **imposes upon** the owner of the inner field the responsibility to pay his share for **all** of the partitions.

The *gemara* now moves to a new *mishna* on a related but distinct topic: *hamakif et chaveiro mishalosh ruchotav* — one who surrounds his neighbor's field on three sides and builds walls on each of those three sides. This scenario, familiar from *Bava Kamma*, involves a situation where no *hezek re'iya* claim exists and no agreement to build was made. The one who built acted entirely on his own initiative. The *mishna* rules: *ein mechayvin oto* — the neighbor in the middle (the *nikaf*) cannot be compelled to pay for any of it.

The reasoning is straightforward: he was never obligated to build, and the outer party acted unilaterally. But the *mishna*'s language also invites an inference: when only three walls have been built, there is no obligation. What happens when a fourth wall is added, fully enclosing the neighbor's field? At that point, something qualitatively changes. The surrounded party's field is now genuinely protected — from animals, trespassers, and other intrusions — and this creates a case of *yored letoch sadeh chaveiro*, benefiting someone's property without their request, for which there may be some obligation to reimburse the benefactor.

It should be noted that there is a debate among the *rishonim* — already discussed in *Bava Kamma* — about which walls the *mishna* is referring to: the walls that are literally adjacent to the neighbor's field, or the outer party's own walls which happen to enclose the central field by virtue of their configuration. Either way, once a fourth wall completes the enclosure, the legal situation changes.

### Rabbi Yosi's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: אִם עָמַד וְגָדַר אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל. לָא שְׁנָא עָמַד נִיקָּף, לָא שְׁנָא עָמַד מַקִּיף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says** that **if he arose and built a partition** on **the fourth** side of the field, the court **imposes upon** the owner of the inner field the responsibility to pay his share for **all** of the partitions. The Gemara comments: It **is no different** if it was the owner of the **surrounded** field who **arose** and built a partition on the fourth side, and it **is no different** if it was the owner of the **surrounding** field who **arose** and built a partition on the fourth side. The *halakha* is the same in both cases.

*Rabbi Yosi* addresses this directly: *im amad vegadar et harevi'it megalgelin alav et hakol* — if the surrounded party himself rose up and built the fourth wall, then we impose upon him payment for all the walls. By voluntarily building the fourth wall, *Shimon* (the *nikaf*) demonstrates that he genuinely wants and values the enclosure. That demonstration of desire is sufficient to obligate him retroactively for his share of all four walls, not merely the one he built himself.

### Gemara Expansion

The *gemara* significantly broadens *Rabbi Yosi*'s ruling: *lo shena amad nikaf lo shena amad makif* — it does not matter whether it was the *nikaf* (the surrounded party) who built the fourth wall or the *makif* (the one who had built the first three) who built it. Once the enclosure is complete, the *nikaf* benefits from all four walls regardless of who built the last one. Even without the *nikaf*'s personal demonstration of desire, the objective reality of benefit is present, and that is sufficient to obligate him to participate in payment. This, in effect, aligns with the position that might be attributed by implication to the *Tanna Kamma*, who had not directly addressed the fourth wall scenario.

### Cost vs Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי מַה שֶּׁגָּדַר. חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the following point. **Rav Huna says** that when Rabbi Yosei said the court obligates the owner of the inner field to pay his share for **all** of the partitions, he pays **in accordance with what** the other person actually spent when he **built the partitions.** That is, the owner of the inner field must contribute his share according to the cost of the partition his neighbor built. **Ḥiyya bar Rav says:** He must pay his share for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds,** and no more. The owner of the surrounded field can claim he had no desire for a more substantial partition.

A dispute now emerges about how much the *nikaf* must pay. *Rav Huna* says: *hakol lefi mah shegadar* — the payment is calculated based on what was actually built. If the outer party erected a stone wall, the *nikaf* pays his share of a stone wall. *Chiya bar Rav* disagrees: *lo hakol lefi dmei kanim bezol* — no, one calculates based on the cheapest possible wall that would have served the purpose, like a minimal fence of reeds. The reasoning behind *Chiya bar Rav*'s position is that the *nikaf* never agreed to any of this from the outset. He cannot be made to pay for a grand construction he never wanted. His obligation is limited to the benefit he actually derives, and that benefit is measured by the minimum cost of achieving equivalent protection. The *gemara* draws an analogy to a familiar situation: if someone installs an expensive lock on a shared door, a co-tenant who never agreed to it can reasonably say, "I would have bought a cheap lock" — and his obligation is accordingly limited.
