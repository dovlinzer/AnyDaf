# Ketubot 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

### R. Yehuda's Mechila

The *gemara* on 56b opens with a discussion of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position on *kesuvah* modification. *Rabbi Yehuda* permits a woman to be *mochel* — to waive — a portion of her *kesuvah*, so that the effective obligation falls below the base amounts of 200 or 100 *zuz*. What he does not permit is an initial stipulation that the *kesuvah* should begin at a lesser amount. The *gemara* presses this distinction: if one may be *masneh al mah she-kasuv ba-Torah* — stipulate against the monetary parameters of biblical obligations in marriage, such as *she'er kesut ve-onah* — why should it be impossible to modify a rabbinic institution? *Rabbi Yehuda*'s answer is that the rabbis wished to strengthen their own institution of *kesuvah* more than the biblical monetary obligations: *asu chizuk le-divreihem yoter mi-shel Torah*.

### Chizuk LeKesubah

The principle underlying *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is therefore that certain rabbinic institutions receive a heightened level of protection — a *chizuk* — such that direct modification at the outset is disallowed, even where modification of analogous biblical obligations would be permitted. The base obligation must remain intact; only afterward may the woman be *mochel*.

### Chamorim v. Demai

The *gemara* raises an apparent tension from a *mishnah* concerning donkey drivers who enter a city. One driver says of his own grain that it is new — inferior in quality — while his companion's is old, and that his own has not had *terumot u-ma'asrot* separated while his companion's has. The ruling in that *mishnah* is that they are not believed, the concern being that in the next city they reverse their claims as part of a scheme — with *Rabbi Yehuda* ruling that they are believed. This seems problematic: if *Rabbi Yehuda* is so committed to reinforcing rabbinic institutions, why does he not protect the rabbinic institution of *demai* — the requirement to separate *terumot u-ma'asrot* from produce of *amei ha-aretz*?

The *gemara* itself notes that the case is not quite parallel: the *kesuvah* concerns a definitive rabbinic financial obligation, whereas *demai* involves a doubt about whether *terumot u-ma'asrot* were separated in the first place.

### Abaye & Rava

*Abaye* offers the following principle: *vadai de-divreihem avdu rabbanan chizuk* — a definite rabbinic institution, such as the *kesuvah* obligations, the rabbis reinforced and said cannot be directly modified. *Safek de-divreihem lo avdu rabbanan chizuk* — a rabbinic institution that is itself predicated on a doubt, such as the obligation to tithe in *demai* cases, has a weaker foundation and was not subject to the same reinforcement. *Rava* offers a slightly different framing: it is not merely the doubt-based structure of *demai* that explains *Rabbi Yehuda*'s leniency there, but rather the fact that *demai* as a category is inherently treated with numerous leniencies, since in principle we believe that *terumot u-ma'asrot* were in fact separated. Either way, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s willingness to be lenient about *demai* does not undercut his position of strengthening the *kesuvah*.

### R. Meir Tenai Batel

*Rabbi Meir* takes a more stringent position. The *mishnah* states that anyone who stipulates less than 200 for a *betulah* or 100 for an *almanah* renders the relations *bi'as zenut* — more akin to fornication than to the protected institution of marriage. One might initially read *Rabbi Meir* as saying that the obligation can in fact be reduced, but the consequence is that one thereby transgresses; the act of living together in that condition is spiritually compromised.

The *gemara*, however, goes significantly further. From the language *kol ha-pocheis* — anyone who "reduces" — the *gemara* infers that *Rabbi Meir* holds not merely that a transgression occurs, but that the attempt to reduce the amount entirely fails: *alma kasavar tenai batel* — the stipulation is void. She retains the full amount; the attempt at reduction does not succeed.

This raises an immediate difficulty: if the *tenai* is *batel* and she in fact has the full 200, how can it be called *bi'as zenut*? The *gemara* answers: *lo samcha da'atah* — even though she actually retains the full amount, because the husband declared *lays lich ella maneh* — you are only entitled to a hundred — she subjectively does not feel the protections of marriage are in place. That subjective insecurity is sufficient to render the relationship *bi'as zenut*.

### Kesuvah De'Oraisa

The *gemara* then asks why *Rabbi Meir* holds the *tenai* to be *batel*. It is well established that *Rabbi Meir* rules that one who stipulates against a biblical monetary obligation — *kol ha-masneh al mah she-kasuv ba-Torah tenao batel* — fails to achieve the stipulation. But the *gemara* assumes that this specifically concerns biblical obligations; if a rabbinic obligation is involved, the stipulation would be binding even for *Rabbi Meir*. So why cannot one stipulate against the *kesuvah*, a rabbinic institution?

The *gemara*'s answer is striking: *kasavar Rabbi Meir kesuvah de'oraisa* — *Rabbi Meir* may hold that the *kesuvah* is a biblical obligation. Therefore the condition fails as an instance of *masneh al mah she-kasuv ba-Torah*, and the woman retains the full amount. The *gemara* thus transforms the *mishnah*: rather than reading *Rabbi Meir* as saying you can reduce the amount but transgress in doing so, the *gemara* reads him as saying the reduction never takes effect, the full biblical obligation remains, yet the subjective harm to the woman's sense of security still produces *bi'as zenut*.

This is presented as a genuine *chidush* on the *gemara*'s part. One might have simply said that *Rabbi Meir* reinforces the *kesuvah* institution more vigorously than other rabbinic obligations. Instead the *gemara* grounds it in *Rabbi Meir*'s view of *kesuvah* as *de'oraisa*, which provides the principled basis for why the *tenai* is *batel* and why even an attempt at *mechila* would presumably be ineffective under *Rabbi Meir*'s position as well.

## Meir, Yehuda, Yossi

### Tanya 3 Views

A *baraita* presents three positions on the question of *kesuvah* modification. *Rabbi Meir* states that any attempt to stipulate less than 200 for a *betulah* or 100 for an *almanah* constitutes *bi'as zenut*. *Rabbi Yossi* says one is *rashai* — permitted — to stipulate a lesser amount from the outset. *Rabbi Yehuda* occupies the middle position: one may write a *shtar* of 200 and the woman writes a receipt saying she has already received 100, or, in the case of a widow, she writes a receipt for 50 of the 100. Thus *Rabbi Yehuda* requires the base amount to be formally present but allows it to be immediately reduced through a receipt.

### R. Yossi Rashai

*Rabbi Yossi*'s position — that the parties may stipulate a lesser amount from the outset — is the most permissive of the three. He goes beyond *Rabbi Yehuda* in dispensing with the requirement to begin formally with the full base amount.

### Metaltelim

The *gemara* challenges *Rabbi Yossi*'s permissive stance by citing a separate *baraita* that appears to show him as protective of a woman's interests: *ein osim kesuvat isha metaltelin mipnei tikkun ha'olam* — one does not designate chattel to serve as the lien for a woman's *kesuvah* for reasons of *tikkun ha'olam*. The concern is that chattel, unlike real estate, can deteriorate or break — the Wedgwood china, as it were — leaving the woman unable to collect her full *kesuvah*. *Rabbi Yossi* in that *baraita* questions what *tikkun ha'olam* is served, given that the chattel's value is not fixed and may diminish.

At first glance *Rabbi Yossi* seems to be asking why the designation is even permitted — pointing to the very concern the *Tanna Kamma* used as a reason to prohibit it. The *gemara* clarifies that both authorities say designation of chattel alone is prohibited. The debate is about a case where the husband has accepted *achrayus* — personal liability — for the chattel's value. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that once the husband accepts *achrayus*, the designation is permitted, since if the chattel breaks he remains liable for the full amount from his other property. *Rabbi Yossi* counters that this still does not fully protect the woman.

### Achrayus

*Rabbi Yossi*'s objection is that even if the husband has accepted *achrayus* against the chattel's destruction, the chattel may not break — it may simply decline in value. If the wife is limited to collecting from the designated china, and the china is worth less when she comes to collect, she loses out. Accepting liability for the item's destruction is not sufficient; it does not address the risk of depreciation. Thus *Rabbi Yossi* holds that even with *achrayus*, one may not designate chattel for the *kesuvah*.

### Hadaat L'Isha

Now the *gemara* poses the sharpest question on *Rabbi Yossi*'s permissive position regarding the initial stipulation. Over there, in the chattel case, *Rabbi Yossi* is concerned even about a potential decrease in value — he is protecting the woman against a scenario she did not agree to and may not have anticipated. Here, by contrast, where the parties are stipulating at the outset for a lesser *kesuvah* amount, the *gemara* asks: *hashta u'mah hasam d'dilma pochasi chayesh Rabbi Yossi* — there, where the concern is only a possibility of diminution, *Rabbi Yossi* worries; *hacha d'vadai kapachta* — here, where the woman is definitely receiving less — how much more should he object?

The answer resolves the tension cleanly: *hasam lo yada d'simchol* — in the chattel case, the woman does not know she is consenting to a risk of loss. She wants her full 200 *zuz* and the husband is creating a scenario in which she may not collect it. That is a unilateral compromise of her interests. *Aval hacha yad'ah ve-kamachla* — but here, the lesser amount was agreed to, she was a party to the stipulation. *Rabbi Yossi*'s concern is exclusively with unilateral undermining of the woman's position; where she knowingly and consensually agrees to the lesser amount, he permits it.

The three-way structure thus stands: *Rabbi Yossi* permits a lesser amount with the woman's consent; *Rabbi Yehuda* requires formal commencement with the full amount followed by a receipt; *Rabbi Meir* holds that no reduction can be achieved and that any attempt renders relations *bi'as zenut*.

## Kesuvah Irkesa

### Irksa Kesuvah

The practical implications of *Rabbi Meir*'s position are illustrated through a case involving the sister of *Rami bar Chama*, who was married to *Rabbi Avya*. Her *kesuvah* was lost — not that she was divorcing, but simply that the document had gone missing. This is in fact a real-world occurrence. A replacement document for a lost *kesuvah* is known as a *kesuvah d'irkesa*, from the Aramaic *irkas*, meaning lost. Such a document is rewritten by the original witnesses, who attest that the original *kesuvah*, which has been lost, was signed by them and contained such-and-such terms.

The practical question is whether the couple may continue to live together — including having marital relations — while the replacement has not yet been written.

### Rav Yosef Dinur

They came before *Rav Yosef*. *Rav Yosef* cited a ruling transmitted by *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel*: this prohibition — that living without the documentation of the *kesuvah* constitutes *bi'as zenut* — represents *divrei Rabbi Meir*, the position of *Rabbi Meir* alone.

### Chachamim Permit

The sages, however, hold that a person may go *shtayim ve-shalosh shanim b'lo kesuvah* — two or three years without a written *kesuvah*. The formulation of two or three years is understood as an idiom for an extended but not indefinite period; it expresses the principle that there is no urgent imperative to immediately replace the document.

One might initially wonder why the absence of the written document should matter at all. The obligation itself — 200 or 100 *zuz* — exists regardless of whether a document is present; we have already learned that even without a written *kesuvah* the liability stands. However, the *gemara* applies *Rabbi Meir*'s logic: just as he holds that a woman's subjective belief that she has only 100 *zuz* creates *bi'as zenut* even when she actually has 200, so too the absence of the physical document creates a subjective sense in the woman that she is unprotected. She may know the debt exists in principle, but without the document she may fear he will claim he already paid. The document provides proof of non-payment. Without it, she does not feel fully secured within the institution of marriage, and for *Rabbi Meir* that subjective insecurity is sufficient to classify the relations as *bi'as zenut*. The sages disagree.

### Abaye Gezerot

*Abaye* raises a challenge: we have a tradition in the name of *Rav Nachman* citing *Shmuel* that *halacha k'Rabbi Meir bi-gzeirosav* — we rule like *Rabbi Meir* in his *gzeiros*. This is a well-known principle. It is worth noting that the *gemara* refers to this ruling as a *gzeira*, which is somewhat striking, since *gzeiros* are typically rabbinic safeguards against potential violations. Here, the *gemara* treats *Rabbi Meir*'s classification of living without a written *kesuvah* as *bi'as zenut* as a type of *gzeira* — a protective measure in service of the sanctity of the marital institution rather than a safeguard against a separate prohibition. *Abaye*'s implication is clear: if we rule like *Rabbi Meir* in his *gzeiros*, go write her a substitute *kesuvah*.

### Halacha Ma'aseh

The practical ruling emerges from this discussion: *l'maaseh*, one should move promptly to write a substitute *kesuvah*. This applies not only when a *kesuvah* is lost at a wedding — a real scenario that occurs — but also in cases where a couple married decades ago can no longer locate their original document, or where the signatures on the original have faded and become illegible. In all such cases, a new *kesuvah d'irkesa* must be written.

A question arises regarding duplicate documents: if the original witnesses rewrite the identical *kesuvah*, the concern is that the woman might now possess two documents evidencing the same debt and attempt to collect twice. For this reason the replacement *kesuvah* uses a different *nusach*, indicating that the original was lost and that the replacement document attests to the contents of the original. Once payment is eventually made and the document is surrendered, that concern dissolves.

### Rama TRG

The *Rama* addresses the question of whether, according to *Rabbi Meir*'s position, a couple must actually cease living together — sleep in separate quarters — until the replacement *kesuvah* is written. The *Rama* rules that in our era, after *takanas Rabbeinu Gershom*, which prohibits a husband from divorcing his wife against her will, the concern underlying *Rabbi Meir*'s *bi'as zenut* is fundamentally ameliorated. *Rabbi Meir*'s concern is that without the protection of a *kesuvah*, the woman feels that the relationship lacks the full safeguards of marriage — that the husband might callously divorce her the next morning. The *kesuvah* protects against that fear. Now that *takanas Rabbeinu Gershom* prevents unilateral divorce entirely, the woman knows she cannot be dismissed against her will; her sense of security within the marriage is not dependent on the written document in the same way. Therefore, the *Rama* holds that today we do not require the couple to cease living together before the replacement is written, though the substitute *kesuvah* should still be produced as quickly as practicable.

## Techila v. Sof

### R.Y. ben Levi

When *Rav Dimi* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted the following in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi* citing *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* in the name of *Bar Kappara*: the three-way dispute among *Rabbi Meir*, *Rabbi Yehuda*, and *Rabbi Yossi* as to whether the *kesuvah* amount may be changed applies only *be-techilah* — at the beginning, meaning before *chuppah*. *Aval be-sof* — but at the end, meaning after *bi'ah*, all agree that she cannot waive the base amount. Once the full obligation has been activated through consummation, even *Rabbi Yossi* would hold that modifying the base amount below 200 or 100 is impermissible.

### R. Yochanan Both

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and holds *bein b'zo u-vein b'zo machlokes* — the three-way dispute applies both at the beginning and at the end.

### R. Abbahu Harmony

*Rabbi Abbahu* reports that this apparent dispute between *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* and *Rabbi Yochanan* was actually clarified for him personally by *Rabbi Yochanan*, who said that he and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* do not in fact disagree.

To understand the harmonization, one must recognize that three stages are in play: before *chuppah*, during *chuppah*, and *bi'ah*. When *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* said the debate is at *techilah*, he meant *techilas chuppah* — prior to the *chuppah*. When he said that at *sof* all agree the amount cannot be changed, he meant *sof bi'ah* — after consummation. *Rabbi Yochanan*, when he said the debate applies in both *zo u-vein zo*, meant that the dispute extends through *techilas chuppah* and *sof chuppah* — the beginning and end of the *chuppah* event — which is the same as saying the debate extends from before the *chuppah* through the *chuppah* itself and up until *bi'ah*. Both authorities therefore agree: the debate among the *Tannaim* is relevant through and including the *chuppah* stage; once *bi'ah* has occurred, all agree the full obligation is locked in.

### Rav Dimi Alt

*Ravin* arrived with an alternative transmission of these same positions. In his version, *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* said the debate is *le-vasof* — at the end — while at *techilah* — the beginning — all agree that a modification is permissible. And *Rabbi Yochanan* again says the debate applies in both cases. *Rabbi Abbahu* then harmonizes this version as well, again in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: he and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* do not disagree. What *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* called *le-vasof* in this version is *sof chuppah* — after the *chuppah* but before *bi'ah*. What all agree is permissible at *techilah* is *techilas chuppah* — before the *chuppah* begins. And when *Rabbi Yochanan* says both cases are in dispute, he means *techilas bi'ah* and *sof bi'ah* — the stages surrounding consummation.

The two transmissions thus present mirror images. In *Rav Dimi*'s version, all agree before the *chuppah* that a modification is possible and all agree after *bi'ah* that it is not, with the dispute situated in the middle stages. In *Ravin*'s version, the arrangement is the reverse: all agree the stages prior to *chuppah* allow modification, and the dispute concerns the stages from *chuppah* onward. In both cases *Rabbi Abbahu*'s harmonization collapses the apparent disagreement between *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* and *Rabbi Yochanan* by showing that what one called *techilah* the other called *sof* and vice versa, depending on which referent — *chuppah* or *bi'ah* — they used as their anchor.

### Rav Pappa Sevara

*Rav Pappa* remarks that had *Rabbi Abbahu* not transmitted the clarification from *Rabbi Yochanan* that he and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* were saying the same thing, he himself would have preferred to read their positions differently: he would have said that *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* are genuinely disagreeing — and therefore that *Rav Dimi* and *Ravin* are not disagreeing with each other at all, but are simply using different language to convey the same transmission.

The *gemara* asks: what practical difference does it make which pair we prefer to be in actual disagreement? *Rav Pappa*'s answer, cited by both *Rashi* and *Tosafos*, is a principle of transmission: *d'pligi trei amorai ta'ama d'nafshaihu v'lo pligi trei amorai aliba d'chad amora* — it is preferable that two *Amoraim* be in genuine disagreement with each other based on their respective *sevara* — their independent reasoning — than that two *Amoraim* be in dispute about what a third *Amora* actually said. Two *Amoraim* reasoning differently about where the *machlokes* among the *Tannaim* logically falls is the normal stuff of *Shas* — each has a view about which stage of the marital process is the more plausible location for the dispute. But when the disagreement is about what a named *Amora* actually transmitted, that implies a problem in the chain of transmission: one of them got it wrong. *Rav Pappa* says that if he had to choose which debate to collapse, he would have preferred to keep *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* and *Rabbi Yochanan* as genuinely arguing from *sevara* and reconcile *Rav Dimi* and *Ravin* as expressing the same position in different language. Since *Rabbi Abbahu* ruled otherwise, we follow his report, but *Rav Pappa*'s methodological preference is noted.

## Mezonot & Trousseau

### Shneim Asar

The *mishnah* introduces the following rule: *notnim le-vesulah shneim asar chodesh mishetav'ah ha-ba'al l'farnes es atzmah* — one gives a *betulah* twelve months from the time the husband demands that she prepare herself, meaning from when he indicates his readiness to proceed from *kiddushin* to *nissuin*. This is the period she is entitled to assemble her trousseau and prepare for marriage. Importantly, the twelve months is not counted from the moment of *kiddushin* — the betrothal may have taken place years earlier — but from the moment the husband presses for the marriage to proceed. *Ve-cheshem she-notnin le-isha kach notnin le-ish* — and just as she is entitled to twelve months, so too he may claim twelve months to prepare his household and resources for the marriage, *l'farnes es atzmo*.

### Almana 30 Yom

For an *almanah* — a widow entering a second marriage — the preparation period is shortened to thirty days. The assumption is that she is more independent, more prepared, and that the second marriage is a less elaborate event. The question of whether the groom similarly receives only thirty days in the case of an *almanah* is presumed to follow in parallel.

### Ochelet Mishelo

Once the twelve-month clock expires and the parties have not yet proceeded to *nissuin*, the husband's obligation of financial support begins. He must now pay for her *mezonot* — her food and expenses — even though the *nissuin* has not yet taken place. *Ochelet mishelo* — she eats from his resources. The beginning of this tractate had addressed the related question of whether fault matters when the deadline is missed; the *mishnah* here does not enter that discussion but simply establishes that from the expiration of the deadline onward, he bears the cost.

### Teruma Kohen

If the husband is a *kohen*, the *mishnah* adds that once she begins eating from his resources, she may eat *teruma*. This teaches two things simultaneously: that an *arusa* — a betrothed woman, not yet fully married — of a *kohen* is halachically entitled to eat *teruma*, and that his obligation of financial support may be discharged by providing *teruma*, which is less costly.

### Tarfon v. Akiva

*Rabbi Tarfon* holds that the husband may satisfy his entire food obligation by providing *teruma*. *Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: at least half must be *chullin* — ordinary food that she may eat at all times. The reason is that for a substantial portion of the month she is *tamei* as a *niddah*, and during that period she cannot eat *teruma*. It would therefore be inequitable to give her only *teruma*, since she would be forced to sell it and receive in exchange a lesser quantity of *chullin*. *Rabbi Akiva* requires that at minimum half of his support obligation be discharged in *chullin* to ensure she is not disadvantaged during the periods of her *tum'ah*.

### Yavam Teruma

The *mishnah* then introduces a case that might appear analogous but is treated differently: *ha-yabam eino ma'achil bi-teruma* — a *yabam* cannot feed his *yevamah* *teruma* even if he is a *kohen*. A woman whose husband — a *kohen* — has died and whose brother-in-law has not yet performed *yibbum* is a *shomeret yabam*. Despite her pending obligation to marry the *yabam*, and despite the existence of a *mitzvah* to perform *yibbum*, she does not gain entitlement to eat *teruma* through that waiting status. There is no twelve-month clock that runs after which she begins eating *teruma*; the category is simply different.

### Months Yabam

The *mishnah* then considers a case where a woman has spent part of her twelve-month waiting period as the *arusa* of the original husband and part as *shomeret yabam*. For instance, six months elapsed as the *arusa* of the *kohen* and six months waiting for the *yabam*. Or the extreme cases: eleven months and twenty-nine days as the *arusa* and one final day as *shomeret yabam* before the deadline, or one day as the *arusa* and then the remainder as *shomeret yabam*. In all these configurations, she does not eat *teruma*. The *mishnah* notes that the last case — nearly all twelve months waiting for the *yabam* — is in a sense obvious, since even if one waited five years as a *shomeret yabam* she would never acquire entitlement to eat *teruma*; *Rashi* characterizes that case as somewhat of a rhetorical inclusion.

The *mishnah* then preserves an earlier ruling — introduced with *zu mishna rishona* — and reports a subsequent revision by a later *beis din*: *ein ha-isha ochelet bi-terumah ad she-tikanes la-chuppah* — a woman may not eat *teruma* until she enters the *chuppah*. This revision delays the entitlement to eat *teruma* until full *nissuin*, which actually increases the husband's food costs, since he must provide *chullin* throughout the betrothal period.

## Rivka Source

### Rav Chisda Rivka

The *gemara* asks: *mina hani mili* — what is the biblical source for the twelve-month waiting period? *Rav Chisda* derives it from the verse describing the departure of *Rivka*: *vayomer achiha ve-immah teshev ha-na'arah ittanu yamim o asor* — her brother and mother said, let the girl stay with us *yamim* or ten. The request was made to delay her departure to marry *Yitzchak*, and one side requested a year while the other requested ten months.

### Yamim Zman

The *gemara* analyzes the word *yamim*. If it means merely two days — the minimum implied by a plural — that is not how people speak; no one bargains for "two days or ten days" in that order, since one normally bargains downward. Alternatively, could *yamim* mean a month? The *gemara* rules that out through a *gezeira shava*: we derive the meaning of unqualified *yamim* from the case in which the same word appears unqualified, as in the verse regarding the redemption period of a house in a walled city — *yamim tihyeh ge'ulato* — which is established as a year. One does not derive unqualified *yamim* from a case where the word is qualified by *chodesh*, as in *chodesh yamim*, since the modifier there shapes the meaning. Therefore *yamim* alone means a year. The brother and mother were requesting a year, or alternatively ten months, and the servant pressed them; but in principle the right to request a year is established from this narrative.

### Ketana Delay

The *gemara* cites *Rabbi Zeira*'s transmission of a *baraita*: *ketannah bein hi u-vein aviha yecholim le'akev* — when the girl is still a *ketannah*, either she or her father can delay the *nissuin*. The twelve-month clock is designed for a case where she is no longer a *ketannah*; while she remains a *ketannah*, the right to postpone is broader — they may hold back for the entire duration of her *ketannah* status.

One might ask what practical force this right has: can a *bet din* force a woman into a *chuppah* against her will regardless? *Tosafos* addresses this by noting that if the withholding party did not have a recognized right to delay, financial consequences would follow — the recalcitrant party could be penalized, for example by reducing the *kesuvah* amount over time, which is the mechanism the later *gemaros* discuss for enforcing marital obligations. The right to delay removes the applicability of such penalties.

### Av Achrayut

The *gemara* immediately questions one aspect of this *baraita*: *bishlama ei ihi me'akva* — it is understandable that she herself can delay, since she has a personal stake in her readiness for marriage. *Ela aviha, ei nicha lah, aviha mai nafka lei mina* — but if she herself is willing to proceed to *nissuin*, what standing does the father have to object? Once *kiddushin* has taken place and she is ready, what legitimate claim does the father have to prevent the marriage from proceeding to its next stage?

### Av Tima'as

The *gemara* answers that the father's concern is the following: *hashta lo yade'ah* — right now, as a *ketannah*, she does not fully appreciate what she is agreeing to. *Le-machar mimreda* — tomorrow, once she is married, she may rebel, grow despondent with her husband, *ve-nafka* and he will divorce her. *Ve-asya ve-nafala ilavai* — and she will return and become my responsibility. The father foresees that a premature marriage may end badly and that he, socially if not legally, will bear the burden of the fallout. It is notable that the *gemara* could have invoked the father's financial entitlements during *arusah* — his rights to her *ma'aseh yadeha* — as his basis for objection. Instead the *gemara* chose to anchor his right in paternal concern for the daughter's welfare and the anticipation of practical consequences he will face if the marriage fails.

## Ein Poskin Ketana

### Lo Nisuin Ketana

*Rabbi Abba bar Levi* is cited with an explicit prohibition: *ein poskin al ha-ketanah le-hasi'ah keshehi ketanah* — one does not make an arrangement to proceed to *nissuin* with a *ketanah* while she is still a *ketanah*. This goes beyond saying she has a right to delay; it is an affirmative prohibition against arranging the *nissuin* itself while she remains a minor. The *gemara* here is effectively signaling that *nissuin* during *ketanah* status is undesirable. *Kiddushin* during *ketanah* status is legally valid; they may be betrothed at a very young age and remain so for years. But the *gemara* here is drawing a firm line at the consummation of the marriage in the full sense of *nissuin*: we do not want that happening while she is a *ketanah*. This is worth noting as a significant statement by the *gemara* about the proper age at which marriage in its full sense should take place.

### Poskin Lehashia

However, the *gemara* continues: *aval poskin al ha-ketanah le-hasi'ah keshehi gedolah* — but one may make an arrangement now, while she is still a *ketanah*, that the *nissuin* will take place once she becomes an adult. So the father may plan ahead and commit to a *nissuin* date that falls after *bat mitzvah*, even while she is currently still a minor.

### Mifchada Chalsha

One might argue that even this should be prohibited: perhaps informing the girl now, while she is still young, that she is scheduled to be married on a specific date after her *bat mitzvah* will cause her distress. *Dilma mifchada me-hashta ve-chalsha* — perhaps she will be frightened from this moment onward and grow despondent. The *gemara* dismisses this concern. *Ka mashma lan* — we are taught that we are not worried about this. The arrangement for a future *nissuin* after she reaches adulthood is permitted.

### R. Huna Bogeret

*Rav Huna* introduces a further ruling: *bogeres yom echad ve-niskadshah notnim lah shloshim yom ke-almanah* — if a woman has reached *bagrus* — the age of 12 and a half — and then entered into *kiddushin*, she is given only thirty days rather than twelve months to prepare for *nissuin*. In *Rav Huna*'s view, a *bogeres* is treated like an *almanah* in this respect.

### Baraita Bogeret

The *gemara* immediately challenges this from a *baraita*: *bogeres harei hi ke-tavu'ah* — a *bogeres* is treated as if the husband had already demanded that the marriage proceed. *Tavu'ah* is the terminology for when one party says it is time for *nissuin*, which triggers the twelve-month clock for a *betulah*. The *baraita* seems to say that reaching *bagrus* automatically triggers that clock — and it is a twelve-month clock, not thirty days. This constitutes a direct *tiyuvta* of *Rav Huna*'s position. The *gemara* attempts to deflect this by suggesting that *ke-tavu'ah* here means like the demand-triggered clock for an *almanah*, which is thirty days — but this reading is rejected as too forced.

## Mishna Reconciled

### Tva'ah Eidun

A *baraita* clarifies the underlying mechanics: after *eirusin* has taken place, either party — husband or wife — may demand that *nissuin* proceed. *Bein she-tav'ah ha-ba'al ve-hi me'akeves* — whether the husband demanded and she sought to delay, *bein she-tav'ah hi u-va'al me'akev* — or she demanded and he sought to delay: in both cases, the clock for the preparation period is triggered and the other party is given time to prepare.

### Shas Tvuah

The *baraita* further specifies: *notnim lo shneim asar chodesh mi-shas tevi'ah* — twelve months are granted from the time of the demand, *aval lo mi-shas eirusin* — not from the time of *eirusin* itself. The *eirusin* may have taken place years before; the clock begins only when one of the parties actively presses for the transition to *nissuin*.

### Bogeret Ktavuah

The *baraita* then states: *u-vogeres harei hi ke-tavu'ah* — and a *bogeres* is treated as equivalent to having been demanded of. This means that the moment a woman attains *bagrus*, it is as if the demand for *nissuin* has been made, and her twelve-month preparation period begins automatically from that point. This directly contradicts *Rav Huna*'s ruling of thirty days and is declared *tiyuvta de-Rav Huna tiyuvta* — a definitive refutation of *Rav Huna*.

## Ein Poskin Ketana…

### Rav Papa Resolve

*Rav Pappa* resolves the tension by distinguishing between two scenarios involving a *bogeres*. The ruling that a *bogeres* gets twelve months — because her reaching *bagrus* functions as an automatic demand — refers to a woman who has just entered *bagrus* and whose *kiddushin* was contracted around that time. The ruling of thirty days, akin to an *almanah*, applies to a *bogeres* who has already been in *bagrus* for twelve months before entering into *kiddushin* — that is, a woman who is 13 and a half years old at the time of *kiddushin*. By that point, the automatic demand that came with *bagrus* has long since been triggered; she has already had the full benefit of the twelve-month window. When *kiddushin* is contracted now, after that window has elapsed, she receives only thirty days, like an *almanah*, since the delay is no longer attributable to preparation for an imminent marriage but to a situation that has been waiting far beyond the normal timeframe. *Rav Huna*'s ruling was therefore not refuted but rather correctly limited to this specific scenario.

## Mishna Reconciled…

### Tosafos Naara

*Tosafos* raises a structural difficulty with the entire framework. The *mishnah*'s twelve-month rule — triggered by a demand for *nissuin* — appears to apply to the paradigmatic case. But when we examine the cases on either end: if the woman is a *ketanah*, the right to delay extends beyond twelve months for the entire duration of her *ketanah* status; if she is a *bogeres*, the clock starts automatically without any demand. The case where the twelve-month clock triggered by a *tevi'ah* — a demand — is the operative rule is specifically when she is a *na'arah*, the period between twelve and twelve and a half years old.

This creates a complication: if she was a *na'arah* when the demand was made and the twelve-month clock began ticking, but she transitions to *bogeres* six months into that period, what happens? At the moment of *bagrus* the automatic demand kicks in independently — but she is already mid-way through a twelve-month period. *Tosafos* works through the implications of this timing, examining whether the clock that began during her *na'arah* status continues or is superseded by the automatic *tevi'ah* created by *bagrus*, and at what stage the various rules intersect. The *gemara*'s basic architecture, as *Tosafos* articulates it, reserves the twelve-month period for the *na'arah* stage specifically; the *ketanah* has more than twelve months and the *bogeres* has twelve months counted differently — from the moment of *bagrus* itself, without the need for any explicit demand.
