# Yevamot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Yibum & Arayot

### Seder Nashim Overview

We begin *Seder Nashim*. The word is generally translated as "women," but a compelling case can be made that the more precise translation is "wives." The word *isha* in the plural *nashim* does not always mean women in the generic sense — when we say Shlomo had many *nashim*, we mean wives. And indeed, the tractates of *Seder Nashim* — *Yevamot*, *Gittin*, *Kiddushin*, even much of *Nedarim* — are overwhelmingly concerned with the relationship between a husband and wife, or a father and his daughters. This framing raises interesting questions about how women are treated as a subject of legal discourse throughout the *seder*.

It is worth noting that *Yevamot* is also an extraordinarily challenging tractate with which to open a *seder*, and for *Daf Yomi* purposes — where *masechtot* are ordered by number of *prakim*, largest first — it would have been far more pedagogically friendly to build gradually toward such complexity rather than beginning here. We will be in this *masechet* for many months, finishing around President's Day.

While a substantial portion of *Yevamot* does deal directly with the *mitzvah* of *yibum*, it is important to frame the tractate more broadly. An equally significant section addresses general issues of personal status: *mamzer*, *ptzu daka*, *krus shepcha*, questions of uncertain Jewish lineage, categories of women *kohanim* may not marry, *gerus*, and the laws of *agunah* — including the question of trusting a single witness to testify that a husband has died. Rather than treating these as digressions from the main topic, one should understand *Yevamot* as the tractate that comprehensively addresses personal status after the *mitzvah* of *yibum* has provided the opening framework.

### Yibum Etymology & History

The literal meaning of *yabam* or *yevama* is simply brother-in-law and sister-in-law. Although the *mitzvah* of *yibum* is translated into English as "levirate marriage" — a Latin term, *levir* meaning "husband's brother" — the Hebrew terms carry their ordinary relational meaning as well as their technical legal sense.

*Yibum* clearly predates *Matan Torah*. We find it in the story of Yehuda and Tamar, and elements of it appear in the story of Ruth as well, though that case does not involve brothers. This leads the Ramban to suggest that *yibum* existed in ancient cultures before *Matan Torah* and was practiced with any male relative, not only a brother — a reading he finds echoed in the Ruth narrative. The Torah then came along and, because of the prohibition of *arayot*, restricted this practice to the single remaining case of brothers.

This raises the question of what *lehakim shem achiv hamet* — "to establish the name of his dead brother" — actually means. It clearly does not mean literally naming the child after the deceased father; we find no such practice in the Torah or in the story of Yehuda and Tamar. The deeper question is why the act of *yibum* itself is required to accomplish this. The Ramban hints at an idea involving *gilgul neshamot*, a form of reincarnation of the dead brother's soul, suggesting that when conception occurs through a family member, there is some metaphysical continuity that allows the child to serve as a genuine extension of the deceased — even setting aside the question of whether one believes in reincarnation literally. For *halakha*, the practical implications have much to do with inheritance of the dead father's property, though we will see that this is not universally agreed upon.

### Yibum vs. Arayot Paradox

The central conceptual tension of this *masechet* is the apparent paradox at its heart: how does the *mitzvah* of *yibum* override the *issur arayot* of *eshet ach*, which carries the punishment of *kares*? For a man to have sexual relations with his brother's wife is an *issur kares*. Yet *yibum* explicitly requires exactly that. This is not a trivial collision.

Non-rabbinic Jewish sects dealt with the tension in different ways. The *Karaites* argued that the *Torah* only permits *yibum* in a case where the parties are *not* actually brothers — pointing to the use of *achim* in a loose sense, as when Avraham says to Lot, *halo anashim achim anachnu*, which does not mean literal brothers. On this reading, *yibum* applies only in a case like Ruth, where there is family relation but no *issur erva*, thereby avoiding the collision entirely. Others — apparently the *Tzadukim* or possibly the *Eseiyim* — suggested that *yibum* applied only to *eirusin*, not *nissu'in*, as another way of managing the tension.

For *Chazal*, however, this collision is precisely the *chiddush* of *yibum*. It is the very fact that *yibum* runs up against an *issur erva* that makes it so remarkable.

### Asei Doche Lo Ta'asei

The halakhic mechanism at work is the principle of *asei doche lo ta'asei* — a positive commandment overrides a negative one. But the *Gemara* suggests that *yibum* may represent something even more radical than the ordinary application of that principle. The paradigm here is the idea of *ne'emru be-dibbur echad* — two commands stated as one. Sometimes this concept applies to commands that are not contradictory but rather complementary, as with *shamor* and *zachor* regarding *Shabbat*. But the more striking application is when two commands are genuinely contradictory, and yet both are simultaneously true. The clearest example of this — and one of the most striking *ne'emru be-dibbur echad* formulations in the *Gemara* — is the prohibition of *eshet ach* on one hand and the command *yevama yavo aleha* on the other. Both are true, both are stated as one, and the tension between them is not resolved but rather held in productive suspension. This is the fascinating and paradoxical core of *yibum*.

## Opening Sugya: Order & L…

### Why Daughter First?

We turn now to the opening of the *masechet* itself, beginning with what is characteristically found at the start of many *masechtot*: a technically dense *stam* passage that is hyper-focused on questions of language and the phrasing of the *Mishnah*. According to a letter of Rav Sherira Gaon, these opening *sugyot* represent one of the latest layers of the *Gemara*, possibly added after the general *stam* editing was complete. They typically introduce no new conceptual content, but rather allude to material that will arise in later discussions and probe the precise wording of the *Mishnah*.

The first question is about the order of the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* opens its list of the fifteen women exempt from *yibum* with *bito* — a man's daughter. It then continues through granddaughter, wife's daughter, wife's granddaughter, mother-in-law, and so on. What is the organizing principle behind beginning with daughter?

The *Gemara* suggests: *keivan de'ati midrasha chaviva lei* — because it is derived from a *drasha* rather than from an explicit verse, it is dear to the *Tanna*. The *Tannaim* had a tendency to favor citing legal derivations they themselves uncovered through interpretation over those that are stated explicitly in the text.

Why, though, is the prohibition of a man's biological daughter not explicit in the *pesukim*? The *Torah* does speak about the prohibition of a woman and her daughter, but Rashi notes that the relevant verse — *ervat isha u'vitah lo tegaleh, et bat bnah v'et bat bitah lo tikach legalot ervatah* — is understood by the Rabbis to refer to *one's wife* and her daughter, not to one's biological daughter from a non-marital union. The prohibition of *bito* — a man's biological daughter who is not also his wife's daughter — is never stated explicitly in the *Torah*. It is only derived through *drasha*. Hence the *Gemara* notes that the fundamental *issur* in this case, *ikkar issura midrasha*, comes entirely from rabbinic interpretation.

The *Gemara* then quotes Rava in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi: *atya heina heina, atya zima zima* — the prohibition is established through a *gezeira shava* on the words *heina* and *zima*.

### Gezeira Shava: Daughter

The *gezeira shava* works as follows. In the verse about biological granddaughter, *ervat bat bincha o bat bitecha lo tegaleh ervatan ki ervat-cha heina*, the word *heina* appears. In a separate verse concerning a wife's daughter and granddaughter, *v'sh'eira heina zima hi*, the word *heina* also appears. Through the verbal analogy linking these two uses of *heina*, we derive that just as the wife's granddaughter passage mentions daughter as well, so too the prohibition by biological relatives extends to the daughter. The *heina*-*heina* connection imports the inclusion of *bat* — daughter — into the biological lineage prohibition.

The *zima*-*zima* component then serves a further purpose: linking the case of biological daughter to the punishment of *sreifa* — death by burning. The verse by the wife's daughter says *zima hi ba-esh yisrefu oto v'et-hen*, establishing that the punishment is burning. Since *heina* connects the biological case to the wife's case, and *heina* in turn connects to *zima*, and *zima* connects to the punishment of *sreifa*, the chain runs: biological granddaughter → through *heina* → wife's granddaughter and daughter → through *zima* → the punishment of burning. In this way, the prohibition of biological daughter, and its equivalence in severity to the wife's-line cases, is derived not from any explicit verse but from a sequence of interpretive links.

This is why the *Tanna* chose to begin with *bito*: it is the one *erva* whose fundamental prohibition is derived entirely from *drasha*, rather than being explicitly written in a *pasuk*.

### Organization by Kinship

The *Gemara* then asks a sharper question. If the principle is that cases learned through *drasha* are placed first, then *achot ishto* — wife's sister — which is the *one* case whose exemption from *yibum* is more explicitly derived from a *pasuk*, should be listed *last* in the *Mishnah*. Why is it not?

The *Gemara* answers: *aidei de'ayei be'issur achvasa*, since we had already begun discussing sibling-based prohibitions, we included wife's sister in that grouping. But the *Gemara* pushes back — even granting that, why not move the entire sibling section to the end, after the wife section, so as to preserve the principle of *drashot* first?

At this point the *Gemara* abandons the *drasha* framework entirely: *ela, tana korvei korvei nakat* — the *Mishnah* is simply organized according to degree of kinship. This is plainly the correct reading. The *Mishnah* first lists one's own descendants: *bito u'vat bito u'vat bno* — daughter, granddaughter through daughter, granddaughter through son. These are the closest relatives. Then it lists wife's descendants — the same downward generations, but through the wife: *bat ishto*, *bat bitah*, *bat bnah*. The ordering moves downward through one's own line, then downward through the wife's line.

*Aidei de'tana shlosha dorot lemata didei, tana nami shlosha dorot lemata didah* — having listed three generations going down on his side, the *Mishnah* lists three generations going down on her side. And having gone down three generations on her side, it then goes up three generations on her side: mother-in-law, grandmother-in-law. Then, having covered vertical relationships — both downward and upward — the *Mishnah* moves to collateral relationships: *achota*, then *achot ima*, and so on. Once we are in the domain of sisters and sibling-based relations, *achot ishto* naturally appears there, even though she is not a blood relative of the husband, because she belongs thematically to the sisters grouping.

The passage then addresses why *kallato* — daughter-in-law, which is *eshet bno* — appears where it does in the list. It should have preceded *eshet achiv shelo haya be-olamo*, since *eshet achiv shelo haya be-olamo* is not really a case rooted in *korva* at all. The case of *eshet achiv shelo haya be-olamo* — the wife of a brother who was not yet born at the time of the first brother's death — is not forbidden because of a familial proximity relationship; rather, it is forbidden because there was never a *mitzvas yibum* to begin with. The scenario is this: Shimon was married to a woman, Shimon died, Reuven performed *yibum*, and only afterward was Levi born. When Reuven later dies, Levi has no *mitzvas yibum* with Reuven's first wife — the one who had been Shimon's — because he was not yet in the world at the time of Shimon's death. The prohibition is structural, not relational.

One might therefore expect *kallato* to precede this case in the list, since *kallato* is genuinely a *korva*-based prohibition. The *Gemara* explains: *aidei de'ayrei be-issur achva, tani eshet achiv shelo haya be-olamo* — since we had moved into the domain of wives-of-siblings, the logic of the grouping pulled *eshet achiv shelo haya be-olamo* forward, and *kallato* follows after. *Ve-hadar tani kallato* — and then the *Mishnah* returns to daughter-in-law. This resolution is entirely satisfying as *pshat*: vertical, then horizontal by blood, then horizontal through marriage, and the internal logic of each grouping accounts for the precise sequence within it.

### Potrot vs. Osrot

The *Gemara* moves on to the next linguistic question: why does the *Mishnah* say *potrot* — they exempt their co-wives — rather than *osrot* — they forbid their co-wives?

The answer is both elegant and substantively important. If the *Mishnah* had said *osrot*, one might have understood that the mechanism at work is that an *erva* directly forbids her co-wife. If that were the case, then even if the *erva* were married to a stranger — someone entirely unrelated to the surviving brother — her co-wife should also be forbidden to that brother. But that conclusion would be wrong.

The true mechanism is that the *erva* removes the *mitzvas yibum*. It is not that she makes the co-wife forbidden; rather, she eliminates the *mitzvah* that would have otherwise applied to the co-wife. And since that *mitzvah* is gone, the co-wife reverts to her default status as a brother's wife, who is prohibited to the surviving brother in the ordinary way. The prohibition of the co-wife is a consequence of the removal of the *mitzvah*, not an independent effect of the *erva*.

*Kevan de-bimakom mitzvah hu de-assira tzara, ve-shelo bimakom mitzvah sharya* — the co-wife is only forbidden in the context of a *mitzvah*, meaning when her co-wife was the wife of the man's brother. If the *erva* had been married to a stranger, there would be no *mitzvas yibum* in the first place, and therefore the co-wife would remain permissible to the surviving brother. *Mishum hachi tana potrot* — this is precisely why the *Mishnah* uses the language of *exemption*: it is describing the mechanism accurately. The *erva* exempts her co-wife from *yibum*, rather than forbidding the co-wife directly.

This is a genuinely good answer, and it explains why the language of *potrot* was the only appropriate choice.

### Chalitza Before Yibum

The *Gemara* now raises one final linguistic question about the *Mishnah*: why does it say *min ha-chalitza u-min ha-yibum* — exempt from *chalitza* and from *yibum* — in that order? The primary *mitzvah* is *yibum*; *chalitza* is the release mechanism. Shouldn't *yibum* come first? And indeed, why mention both at all? If one is exempt from *yibum*, shouldn't that be sufficient?

The *Gemara* addresses both questions in sequence. On the first: if the *Mishnah* had said only *min ha-yibum*, one might have thought that while *yibum* is forbidden, *chalitza* is still required — perhaps out of concern that there remains some biblical attachment that needs to be formally dissolved. This is not an unreasonable concern, since there are categories where *yibum* is not performed but *chalitza* is still required, either because of *safek* or because of certain rabbinic considerations. To avoid this misunderstanding, the *Mishnah* states that these women are exempt from *both*. The principle is: *kol ha'oleh le-yibum oleh la-chalitza, ve-chol she-eino oleh le-yibum eino oleh la-chalitza* — whoever is subject to *yibum* is subject to *chalitza*, and whoever is not subject to *yibum* is not subject to *chalitza*. Biblically, they are a package deal.

This does not mean there are no women for whom *chalitza* is performed but *yibum* is not — those cases exist due to *safek* or rabbinic reasons. But at the biblical level, exemption from one means exemption from both. Hence the need to state both explicitly.

As for the ordering — why *chalitza* before *yibum* — the *Gemara* says: *Abba Shaul hi*, this *Mishnah* follows the position of Abba Shaul. Rashi, citing the chapter of *ha-choletz*, quotes Abba Shaul directly: *ha-kones yevamto le-shum noy le-shum ishut ke-ilu pogea ba-erva* — one who marries his *yevama* for the sake of her beauty or out of genuine desire for marriage is as though he has violated an *erva*. And further: *karov be-einai lihyot ha-vlad mamzer* — in his view, the child is practically a *mamzer*. The reason is that *yibum*, to be valid as a *mitzvah*, must be performed *leshem shamayim*, for the sake of Heaven and the fulfillment of the commandment, not because the man actually desires this woman as a wife. If his motivation is personal attraction rather than fulfillment of the *mitzvah*, the act loses its sanctified character and begins to resemble an ordinary violation of *eshet ach*. This is a striking position, one that might seem counterintuitive — we might think that genuine mutual desire to build a marriage is a feature, not a flaw. Yet Abba Shaul holds that any motivation other than pure *leshem mitzvah* undermines the *yibum* entirely.

Following this view, the *Mishnah* lists *chalitza* before *yibum* to signal that *chalitza* is the preferred mode. In Ashkenazic communities this became the normative practice: even when the surviving brother is unmarried and *yibum* would technically be permitted, *chalitza* is strongly encouraged or required. In some Sephardic communities, the primary *mitzvah* of *yibum* is still emphasized, and the practice differs accordingly.

### Fifteen Women & Exclusio…

The *Gemara* finally asks about the enumeration itself: *minyana derisha lema'utei mai, minyana deseifa lema'utei mai* — why does the *Mishnah* specify "fifteen" at the beginning, and why does it repeat the formulation at the end? An exact count implies that something is being deliberately excluded. What is being excluded?

The *Gemara* answers: *lema'utei de-Rav u-de-Rav Assi* — to exclude the positions of Rav and Rav Assi, who each proposed additional cases in which the principle of *tzara* — the co-wife's exemption — should apply.

Rav's case involves *tzarat sotah*. A man whose wife has committed adultery is prohibited from continuing to have sexual relations with her; their union is no longer viable in the relevant sense. If such a man dies, his brother has no *mitzvas yibum* with this woman, since the relationship between her and the deceased husband was effectively broken — it was not a union capable of producing the kind of continuation that *yibum* is meant to effect. So far so good. But what about her co-wife? Rav argues that since there is no *yibum* with the *sotah*, her co-wife is also exempt. The *Mishnah*, by specifying exactly fifteen, rejects this view: in the case of *sotah*, the *sotah* herself is not an *erva*, and so her co-wife is *not* exempt. The co-wife would still be subject to *yibum*.

Rav Assi's case is *tzarat ailonit* — the co-wife of a woman who cannot bear children. Again, the issue is not that the *ailonit* is an *erva*, but that there is something fundamentally deficient about that marriage in terms of its procreative purpose. Rav Assi says this too should exempt the co-wife. The *Mishnah* says no.

The *Gemara* then asks the reverse question: *u-l'Rav u-l'Rav Assi lema'utei mai* — granting that Rav and Rav Assi believe their cases are not rejected by the *Mishnah*, what does the *Mishnah*'s enumeration exclude according to them?

The *Gemara* offers two possibilities, depending on whether Rav and Rav Assi agree with each other. If they do, then the *Mishnah*'s count excludes two other cases on which they *agree* that the *tzara* principle does not apply: *tzarat mema'enet* and *tzarat machzir gerushato*. If they do not agree with each other, then one of the exclusions is the other's case, and the remaining exclusion is one of the other two.

The case of *mema'enet* involves a girl who was married off in a rabbinic marriage by her mother or brothers while still a minor. She retains the right of *mi'un* — refusal — by which she can annul the marriage. If her husband dies and a brother-in-law acquires a *zika* — the quasi-attachment transferred from the deceased husband — she can exercise *mi'un* to dissolve that attachment and free herself from the obligation of *yibum* or *chalitza*. Since her entire marriage and the *zika* were rabbinic in nature, her exercise of *mi'un* severs the relationship entirely. In this case, if the brother-in-law has a second wife, one might ask whether that co-wife of the *mema'enet* is also exempt. The answer is no — the case of *mema'enet* does not carry the co-wife along with it into exemption.

The case of *machzir gerushato* is simpler. If a man divorced his wife, she subsequently married another man, and then the first husband attempted to take her back, the *Torah* forbids this remarriage. If this man — who is in an illicit remarriage — also has a second wife, and he then dies, the *machzir gerushato* is not a candidate for *yibum* because of the prohibition on the relationship. But the second wife, the *tzara*, would still be subject to *yibum*. The *issur* of *machzir gerushato* differs in character from the *issur* of *sotah*: it is more about the prohibition of his *remarrying* her than about an ongoing *issur* of sexual relations per se, and therefore it does not carry the co-wife along into exemption.

The *Gemara* then addresses one further issue. If Rav and Rav Assi are correct that the principle of *tzara* applies in their cases, why are those cases not listed in the *Mishnah*? The answer: *lefi she-eino be-tzaras tzara* — because even in those cases, the exemption does not extend to *tzaras tzara*, the co-wife of a co-wife. In the *Mishnah*'s fifteen cases, the exemption extends indefinitely — *tzaras tzara ad sof ha-olam*. Rashi on the *Mishnah* notes, interestingly, *b'masnisin meforesh* — it is the *Mishnah* itself, later on, that will spell out what this means; not the *Gemara*, but a subsequent *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* is in some sense self-annotating, explaining its own terms as it proceeds.

With this, the opening *sugya* has introduced, in compressed form, the major categories that will occupy us throughout the tractate: the nature of *tzara*, the question of what relationships create an exemption from *yibum* even absent an *erva*, the cases of *mema'enet* and *machzir gerushato*, and the chain of *tzaras tzara*. All of this complexity lies ahead.
