# Avodah Zarah 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rami bar Chama - Ne'evad

### Intro & Grain Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְקָמַת חִטִּים, מַהוּ לִמְנָחוֹת? יֵשׁ שִׁינּוּי בְּנֶעֱבָד, אוֹ אֵין שִׁינּוּי בְּנֶעֱבָד?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows to a stalk of wheat, what is** the *halakha* with regard to using it **for meal-offerings?** Does the wheat lose its forbidden status after it is ground into flour, or not? **Does a change in** the form of **a worshipped** object revoke its forbidden status, **or does a change in** the form of **a worshipped** object **not** revoke its forbidden status?

The daf opens by continuing a series of questions posed by Rami bar Chama relating to the status of *ne'evad* — the status of something that has been worshipped — as it applies to objects that are *mechubar*, attached to the ground. The broader background is as follows: we have established that one cannot render something forbidden as *avodah zarah* if it is attached to the ground. A mountain, for example, cannot be made into *avodah zarah*. A tree sits in a somewhat more complex position — the Gemara earlier discussed a rock that dislodged itself — but the general principle is that *mechubar* objects cannot acquire the forbidden status of *avodah zarah*. The question that has been driving the discussion, however, is a narrower one: even if such an attached object does not become forbidden in *hana'ah*, does it acquire the status of *ne'evad* — that of something worshipped — so as to disqualify it from use in the *Beis Hamikdash*?

Rami bar Chama now poses the following question: *hamishtachaveh lekamas chittin mahu lemenachos* — if someone bows down to grain while it is still growing in the ground, may that grain be used for a *minchah* offering? On the surface, this seems identical to the question discussed on the previous daf: does *mechubar* acquire the status of *ne'evad* for purposes of the *Beis Hamikdash*? But there is an important dimension that distinguishes this case.

### Shinuy be-Ne'evad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: תָּא שְׁמַע, כׇּל הָאֲסוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ — וַלְדוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִים, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which is taught in a mishna (*Temura* 30b): The principle is that concerning **all** items **that are forbidden with regard to** sacrificing them the **altar, their offspring,** i.e., whatever products are derived from them, **are permitted. And it is taught with regard to** this mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer deems** the offspring **forbidden.**

The distinguishing issue is *shinuy*: *yesh shinuy bene'evad o ein shinuy bene'evad* — does a transformation or change in the object's form affect its *ne'evad* status? For *avodah zarah* that was fully forbidden in *hana'ah*, we know that even subsequent changes do not help — if you burn it, the ashes remain forbidden. But here, since the grain is *mechubar* and therefore never acquired the full status of *avodah zarah*, the question is whether the change it undergoes — being harvested, ground into flour, and otherwise processed — is sufficient to eliminate whatever degree of *ne'evad* status it may have had. That is the new layer introduced by this question.

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman suggests a proof. The Mishnah teaches: *kol ha'asurin legabei hamizbei'ach veladosehen mutarin* — all animals that are forbidden for the *mizbei'ach* (including animals that were worshipped, *ne'evad*, or that had an act of bestiality performed upon them), their offspring are permitted. And a *baraisa* adds: *v'tanya alah Rebbi Eliezer osser* — Rabbi Eliezer forbids. The case under discussion is one where the animal was already pregnant at the time the forbidden act took place, so that the *ne'evad* status attached to the animal while the fetus was in utero. Now there is a debate whether the fetus, once born, may be brought as a *korban*. If this were simply a question of whether *shinuy* — the transformation from fetus to born animal — eliminates the *ne'evad* status, then the existence of this debate would itself constitute evidence that *shinuy bene'evad* is indeed a live dispute.

### Fetus Proof & Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁנִּרְבְּעוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף עִיבְּרוּ, אֲבָל עִיבְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִרְבְּעוּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֲסוּרִין, וְהָכָא נָמֵי כְּעִיבְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִרְבְּעוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it stated with regard to** this dispute that **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **dispute** is with regard to a case **where** it became prohibited to use the animals as offerings because a person **engaged in bestiality** with them **and later they became pregnant.** **But** if **they became pregnant and later** a person **engaged in bestiality** with them, **all agree** that the offspring **are forbidden** because the offspring were involved in the act of bestiality, albeit in their fetal state. **And here too,** the case of the wheat is **similar to** the case where **they became pregnant and later** a person **engaged in bestiality** with them, because the flour was also worshipped, albeit in the form of a stalk of wheat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם, מֵעִיקָּרָא בְּהֵמָה וְהַשְׁתָּא בְּהֵמָה, דַּשָּׁא הוּא דַּאֲחִידָא בְּאַנְפַּהּ. הָכָא, מֵעִיקָּרָא חִיטֵּי וְהַשְׁתָּא קִמְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of offspring that it is prohibited to bring as an offering, it was **initially an animal,** in its fetal state, **and now** it is still **an animal.** A fetus is treated as a full-fledged animal, and the opening of the womb is compared to a closed **door that holds** it **in its place. Here,** in the case of the flour, it was **initially wheat and now** it is **flour;** it is in a totally new form. Therefore, no proof can be derived from the mishna.

The Gemara rejects this proof on a critical factual point. Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha clarifies: *machlokes keshenirbe'u velisof ivru* — the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Tanna Kamma* is only in the case where the forbidden act occurred first and the animal became pregnant afterward, leaving it an open question whether that status passes on to the subsequently conceived fetus. However, *aval ibru velisof nirbe'u divrei hakol asurin* — if the animal was already pregnant when the forbidden act took place, all agree the fetus is forbidden. This means the debate is not about whether *shinuy* (transformation from fetus to born animal) eliminates the *ne'evad* status, but rather about whether the status is transmitted to a fetus conceived after the fact. And given that universal agreement that a fetus *in utero* at the time of the forbidden act is forbidden, this would actually prove *ein shinuy bene'evad* — that transformation does not remove the *ne'evad* status — for the same reason.

The Gemara then presents an alternative reading (*ikka d'amri*) in which this clarification was already incorporated into the original proof: the one who cited the fetal debate already acknowledged that the context was *keshenirbe'u velisof ivru* and was drawing precisely on the point of universal agreement (*divrei hakol asurin*) to prove that *ein shinuy bene'evad* — that the fetus, despite being born and transformed from the original state at the time of worship, remains forbidden. This, he argued, demonstrates that the transformation undergone is not considered a *shinuy* sufficient to erase *ne'evad* status. And therefore the same should apply to our case of grain: even after harvesting and grinding, the *ne'evad* status persists.

The Gemara, however, rejects the comparison: *hachi hashta* — how can you compare these cases? In the case of the fetus: *hasam me-ikar beheimah ve-hashta beheimah* — it was an animal then and it is an animal now. The birth is merely the removal of a barrier — *dasha hu d'achiza b'appah*, a door that was in its way, the door of the womb. *Hacha me-ikar hitti ve-hashta kimcha* — here, it was wheat and now it is flour. These are categorically different things bearing different names. The natural biological progression from fetus to born animal, no matter how significant developmentally, is still treated as a continuous organic process. The man-made transformation of wheat into flour — yielding a product with an entirely different identity — may well constitute a *shinuy* of a different order. Therefore, one cannot derive from the fetal case that such a transformation would not constitute a *shinuy bene'evad*. The question remains unresolved.

## Reish Lakish - Lulav

### Mitzvah & Dichui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַדֶּקֶל, לוּלָבוֹ מַהוּ לְמִצְוָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows to a palm tree, what is** the *halakha* with regard to **its *lulav*?** Is it permitted to use a *lulav* cut from it **for the mitzva** or not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ מִתְּחִלָּה לְכָךְ לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְהֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי אָסוּר. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ בְּאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ. וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְהֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי אָסוּר. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, לְעִנְיַן מִצְוָה מַאי? מִי מְאִיס כְּלַפֵּי גָּבוֹהַּ אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara narrows down the dilemma: **With regard to a tree that one initially planted for such** an idolatrous purpose, **do not raise the dilemma.** In that case the *halakha* is clear, **as it is prohibited** to use it **even for an ordinary,** non-mitzva, purpose. Rather, **raise the dilemma with regard to a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped.** **And do not raise the dilemma according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda,** who holds **that even for an ordinary** purpose **it is forbidden** (see 45b). Rather, **when should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who hold that such a tree does not become forbidden: **What** is the *halakha* **with regard to** using a *lulav* from such a worshipped tree for **a mitzva? Is it** nevertheless forbidden because it is considered **repulsive** to use such a *lulav* **for** the **Most High,** i.e., for a mitzva, **or not?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: בַּאֲשֵׁירָה שֶׁבִּיטְּלָהּ קָמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, יֵשׁ דִּחוּי אֵצֶל מִצְוֹת אוֹ אֵין דִּחוּי אֵצֶל מִצְוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he presented a different understanding of Reish Lakish's dilemma. **He said:** Reish Lakish **raises the dilemma with regard to an *ashera*** whose status as an object of idol worship **was revoked.** His dilemma is as follows: **Is there disqualification with regard to mitzvot or is there no disqualification with regard to mitzvot?** This *lulav* was once unfit for the mitzva, since it was worshipped while attached to the tree. Is it permanently disqualified, i.e., can it never be rendered fit? Or is the *halakha* that there is no permanent disqualification with regard to mitzvot, and consequently when the tree's status as an object of idol worship was revoked, the *lulav* became fit for the mitzva?

The Gemara now presents a related but distinct question from Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish asked: *hamishtachaveh l'dekel lulavo mahu l'mitzvah* — if one bows down to a palm tree, can the *lulav* be used for the *mitzvah* of the four species on Sukkot?

The Gemara first delimits the precise parameters of the question. If the tree was originally planted for the sake of being worshipped — *ilan she-netao mi-tchilah l'kach* — there is no question: it is *assur behana'ah* even for ordinary use (*afilu l'hedyot nami assur*), and certainly cannot be used for a *mitzvah*. Similarly, according to Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehudah, who holds that even bowing to a tree that was planted for other purposes and subsequently worshipped is sufficient to render it forbidden in *hana'ah*, there is likewise no question. The question arises specifically according to the *Rabbanan*, who hold that bowing to a tree that was not originally planted for worship does not give it a *tfisas yad* sufficient to render it *assur behana'ah*. In such a case, the tree is *mechubar* and is not forbidden in benefit — but the question remains: *le-inyan mitzvah mai* — does the *ne'evad* status that exists even without full *assur behana'ah* render it *ma'is klapei gavo'ah*, too tainted to be used in the service of God?

The Gemara then introduces a further dimension through the report of Rav Dimi who came from Eretz Yisrael: *ba'asheirah she-bitlah kamiba'ei lei* — Reish Lakish's question in fact presupposes that even without *bittul*, the palm tree worshipped while still attached to the ground would be considered unfit. The question is whether, if a non-Jew performed *bittul* on the tree — nullifying its *avodah zarah* status — that *bittul* would restore the *lulav* to fitness for use. This introduces two subsidiary concepts: *bittul* and *dichui*.

It is worth pausing to note what is striking about this formulation. *Bittul* normally operates to remove the status of *issur hana'ah*. Here, however, since the tree was *mechubar* and never acquired full *assur behana'ah* status, it never technically had the *avodah zarah* prohibition that *bittul* normally dissolves. The Gemara nonetheless assumes that *bittul* can affect the residual *ne'evad* status for purposes of *mitzvah* and *Mikdash* — which is a remarkable *chiddush*. Furthermore, as Tosafot observes, the entire question of *ne'evad* rendering the *lulav* problematic is only a *l'chatchilah* concern. *Bedi'eved*, one who used it would be *yotzei*, because *mitzvos lav lihanous nitnu* — the fulfillment of a *mitzvah* is not considered *hana'ah*, so even if the object were fully *assur behana'ah*, after the fact the obligation would be discharged. Tosafot invokes the ruling of Rabbah in Tractate Sukkah that *issurei hana'ah* can serve for a *mitzvah bedi'eved*.

With this in mind, the Gemara introduces the concept of *dichui* in the context of *mitzvos*: *yesh dichui etzel mitzvos o ein dichui etzel mitzvos* — does an object that was once unfit for a *mitzvah* and later becomes fit again remain permanently disqualified by virtue of having been pushed off? The concept of *dichui* is well established in the laws of *korbanos*, where an animal that was once invalid cannot be restored to fitness. The question is whether an analogous principle applies to objects used for *mitzvos*.

### Blood Covering Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדִּתְנַן: כִּיסָּהוּ וְנִתְגַּלָּה — פָּטוּר מִלְּכַסּוֹת, כִּיסָּהוּ הָרוּחַ — חַיָּיב לְכַסּוֹת. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁחָזַר וְנִתְגַּלָּה, אֲבָל לֹא חָזַר וְנִתְגַּלָּה — פָּטוּר מִלְּכַסּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve** the dilemma **from that which we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 37a): With regard to one who slaughtered an undomesticated animal or a bird and is obligated to cover the blood, if **he covered** the blood **and it was** then **uncovered** he is **exempt from** the obligation **to cover** it a second time. But if **the wind** blew dust and **covered** the blood and no person was involved, he is **obligated to cover** it. **And Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna **taught** that one is obligated to cover the blood after the wind covered it **only** in a case **where** the blood **was then exposed. But** if **it was not then exposed, he is exempt from** the obligation **to cover** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בָּהּ: כִּי חָזַר וְנִתְגַּלָּה מַאי הָוֵי? הוֹאִיל וְאִידְּחִי אִידְּחִי!
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this issue and asked: **When** the blood **was then exposed, what of it?** Why is one obligated to cover it a second time? **Once it was disqualified, it** should remain **disqualified.** When the wind covered the blood, the person was exempted from covering the blood. If so, even if the blood is subsequently uncovered, he should remain exempt. Why, then, is he obligated to cover the blood in that case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: אֵין דִּיחוּי אֵצֶל מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa says: That is to say** that **there is no disqualification with regard to mitzvot.** Once the cause of the exemption from the obligation is removed, one is once again obligated to fulfill the mitzva. If so, Reish Lakish's dilemma is resolved.

To resolve the question of *dichui* by *mitzvos*, the Gemara suggests a proof from the law of covering blood (*kisui hadam*). The Mishnah teaches: if one slaughtered a bird or a wild animal and then covered the blood, and subsequently the covering blew away (*kisehu venitgalah*) — one is exempt from having to cover it again. But if the wind itself blew dirt over the blood and covered it without any human action, and then blew the covering away, one is still *chayav* to cover the blood. Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan clarifies: this latter ruling applies only in the case where the wind blew the covering away again (*shechazar venitgalah*); but if the wind-blown covering remains in place, one is exempt from any further obligation.

The Gemara then recalls that a question was raised: *ki chazar venitgalah mai havai* — once the blood is re-exposed, why does it matter? *Ho'il v'idchi idchi* — since the *mitzvah* of covering was once pushed off (as the blood was never properly covered by a human act and the wind-blown covering dissolved), perhaps that *dichui* is permanent and the *mitzvah* can never be fulfilled. To this, Rav Pappa responds: *zos omeres ein dichui eitzel mitzvos* — this teaches that the concept of *dichui* does not apply to *mitzvos*. The fact that one must still cover the blood when it is re-exposed demonstrates that a *mitzvah* cannot be permanently pushed away.

One might note that the analogy is not entirely clean: the blood is not the *mitzvah* object in the strict sense — if anything, the dirt used for covering is more analogous to the *lulav* in our case. The Gemara nonetheless draws the comparison, inferring from the blood case that a *lulav* whose *mitzvah*-fitness was suspended due to *ne'evad* status should similarly not be permanently disqualified once *bittul* is performed.

### Teiku on Bittul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַב פָּפָּא גּוּפֵיהּ אִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, מִפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַב פָּפָּא דְּאֵין דִּיחוּי אֵצֶל מִצְוֹת, לָא שְׁנָא לְקוּלָּא וְלָא שְׁנָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: It is with regard to **Rav Pappa's** resolution **itself** that Reish Lakish **raised the dilemma.** Is it **obvious to Rav Pappa,** based on the discussion with regard to the blood, **that there is no disqualification with regard to mitzvot;** and there **is no difference** whether that ruling leads **to leniency,** as in the case of the worshipped tree whose status as an object of idol worship was revoked, thereby permitting its *lulav* branch to be used for a mitzva, **and** there **is no difference** whether that ruling leads **to stringency,** as in the case of the blood, where one is obligated to cover it anew?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, וּלְחוּמְרָא אָמְרִינַן, לְקוּלָּא לָא אָמְרִינַן? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps is he uncertain, and** therefore, when that ruling leads **to stringency, we say** that there is no disqualification with regard to mitzvot, and one must perform the mitzva. But when that ruling leads **to leniency, we do not say** that there is no disqualification with regard to mitzvot. The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara rejects this proof, however, on a subtle ground. *DeRav Pappa gufeh miba'ya leh* — the question of Reish Lakish about the *lulav* was actually a question *within* Rav Pappa's very inference from the blood case. That is, Reish Lakish was asking whether Rav Pappa's conclusion — that there is no *dichui* by *mitzvos* — was stated as an absolute and certain principle, applying both to *stringencies* and to *leniencies*, or whether Rav Pappa only derived from the blood case that we apply the principle stringently (requiring one to re-cover the blood, not dismissing the obligation as permanently lapsed) but would not apply it leniently to *permit* a previously disqualified *lulav*. If the latter, then even though *dichui* does not permanently eliminate the obligation in the stringent direction, *bittul* might still not suffice to resuscitate the *lulav* for *l'chatchilah* use.

The Gemara leaves this as a *teiku*. We thus conclude that there appears to be no *dichui* by *mitzvos* in general, but whether *bittul* on a *ne'evad mechubar* object is sufficient to permit its use for a *mitzvah l'chatchilah* remains unresolved.

## Rav Pappa - Wool & Instruments

### Wool for Techeiles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לִבְהֵמָה, צַמְרָהּ מַהוּ לִתְכֵלֶת? תְּכֵלֶת דְּמַאי? אִי תְּכֵלֶת לְכֹהֲנִים — הַיְינוּ בַּעְיָא דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, וְאִי תְּכֵלֶת לְצִיצִית — הַיְינוּ בַּעְיָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא הֲוָה לְמִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּקָא בָעֵי לֵיהּ הָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מִילֵּי אַחְרָנְיָיתָא: צַמְרָהּ מַהוּ לִתְכֵלֶת? קַרְנֶיהָ מַהוּ לַחֲצוֹצְרוֹת? שׁוֹקֶיהָ מַהוּ לַחֲלִילִין? בְּנֵי מֵעֶיהָ מַהוּ לְפֹארוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows to an animal, what is** the *halakha* with regard to **its wool?** May it be used **for** the **sky-blue wool?** The Gemara asks: The **sky-blue wool** in question is **for which** mitzva? **If** it is the **sky-blue wool for** the ritual garments worn by **priests,** then Rav Pappa's dilemma **is** the same as the **dilemma** raised **by Rami bar Ḥama** earlier (46b) with regard to the use in the Temple of worshipped items that have since changed in form. **And if** the **sky-blue wool** in question is **for** the mitzva of **ritual fringes,** this **is** the same as the **dilemma** raised **by Reish Lakish** with regard to the use of a branch of a worshipped palm tree for performing the mitzva of *lulav*. **Yes, it is indeed so that** Rav Pappa **did not** need **to raise** this **dilemma,** as it was previously addressed. **And** the reason **that he raises this dilemma** is **due to other matters** that he needed to clarify, concerning the use of parts of a worshipped animal in the Levites' ritual song. With regard to **its wool, what is** the *halakha* concerning using it **for sky-blue wool** used in the performance of a mitzva? With regard to **its horns, what is** the *halakha* concerning their use **for trumpets?** With regard to the bones of **its thighs, what is** the *halakha* concerning their use **for flutes?** With regard to **its innards, what is** the *halakha* concerning their use **for harp strings [*leforot*]?**

The Gemara now introduces a question posed by Rav Pappa: *hamishtachaveh l'vehema tzimrah mahu l'techeiles* — if one bows down to an animal, can its wool be used for *techeiles*, the blue dye for *tzitzis*? At first glance, this question appears to duplicate everything already discussed. The Gemara itself acknowledges this: *techeiles d'mai* — *techeiles* for what purpose? If the question is about *techeiles* for the priestly garments (*bigdei kohanim*), that is identical to Rami bar Chama's question about *ne'evad mechubar* and *tashmishei korban* — and also implicates the *shinuy* question, since the wool would need to be shorn and dyed. If the question is about *techeiles* for *tzitzis*, that is identical to Reish Lakish's question about using *ne'evad mechubar* for the fulfillment of a *mitzvah*.

The Gemara concedes: *ein hachi nami* — indeed, Rav Pappa need not have asked this question, *d'lo hava leh l'mibaye*. The reason he formulated his question in terms of wool for *techeiles*, however, is that he wished to proceed to additional questions involving parts of the same animal.

### Animal Parts & Instruments

Following the question about wool, Rav Pappa poses three further questions about other parts of the worshipped animal: *karnaim mahu lachatzotzros* — can the horns be used for trumpets? *Shokayim mahu lachalilim* — can the leg-bones, hollowed out, be used for flutes? *Benei mei'aya mahu lipe'aros* — can the intestines be used as strings for lyres? The specific context here is the instruments of the Levites, used to accompany the *shira* sung during the bringing of *korbanos*. Tosafot notes that the silver *chatzotzros* of the Torah were a priestly instrument; these questions concern Levitical instruments that accompanied the *avodah*.

The question about these instruments would seem to reduce straightforwardly to the question of *tashmishei korban* — accessories or instruments of the sacrificial service. But the Gemara identifies a distinction worth exploring.

### Instruments - Essential?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: עִיקַּר שִׁירָה בִּכְלִי, לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — דְּוַדַּאי אֲסִיר. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: עִיקַּר שִׁירָה בַּפֶּה, בַּסּוֹמֵי קָלָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא וּמַיְיתִינַן, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי אֲסִיר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the opinion **of the one who says** that the **primary** element of **song** in the Temple is the accompaniment **by** musical **instruments, do not raise the dilemma, as it is certain** that use of a worshipped animal is **prohibited,** because the musical instruments are items used to facilitate an offering. **When should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of the one who says** that the **primary** element of **song** in the Temple is singing **with the mouth,** and that the musical instruments are used **merely** to **sweeten the sound,** i.e., to accompany and enhance the singing. **And** accordingly, **can one bring** musical instruments made of a worshipped animal's parts, as they are not an essential element of the Temple service? **Or perhaps** is it **nevertheless forbidden** since it is repulsive to use such instruments in the course of the Temple service? The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara draws a distinction based on a fundamental dispute: what is the primary element of the *shira* that accompanied the *korban* — the vocal singing (*shira bepeh*) or the playing of musical instruments (*shira bikli*)? *Aliba demand amar ikar shira bikli lo tiba lach devadai assur* — according to the view that the primary element of the *shira* is the music of the instruments, there is no question: the instruments are essential to the *avodah* and their status as *tashmishei korban* is clear, rendering the use of a *ne'evad* animal's parts forbidden just as Rami bar Chama established.

The question arises according to the view that *ikar shira bepeh* — the primary *shira* is the vocal singing. If that is the case, *bisumei kola be'alma hu maisinan* — the instruments serve merely to sweeten and enhance the sound but are not themselves doing something essential. Does that subordinate role mean it is permissible to use parts of a *ne'evad* animal for them, since they are not central to the *avodah*? Or, perhaps, even such an ancillary role is still sufficiently connected to the *korban* to be forbidden? Rashi adds that one might even construct a *kal vachomer*: if even a *lulav* — which is used for an independent *mitzvah* entirely outside the *Mikdash* — may be problematic when it is *ne'evad*, then certainly an instrument used in the *Beis Hamikdash*, even in an ancillary capacity, should be forbidden. The question is left as a *teiku*.

## Rava - Wellspring Water

### Wellspring Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבָּה: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְמַעְיָן, מֵימָיו מַהוּ לִנְסָכִים? מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא לְבָבוּאָה קָא סָגֵיד, אוֹ דִלְמָא לְמַיָּא קָא סָגֵיד? וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ סֵפֶל לְהֶדְיוֹט! לְעוֹלָם לְמַיָּא קָא סָגֵיד, וְהָכִי קָמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: לְמַיָּא דְּקַמֵּיהּ קָא סָגֵיד, וְקַמָּאֵי קַמָּאֵי אֲזַדוּ, אוֹ דִּלְמָא לְדַבְרוּנָא דְּמַיָּא קָא סָגֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows to a spring** of water, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to using **its water for libations?** The Gemara asks: **What is the dilemma he is raising? If we say** that the dilemma concerns whether **he is bowing to** his own **reflection or perhaps he is bowing to the water,** why then is this a dilemma only with regard to libations in the Temple? **Let him raise the dilemma** with regard to the use of **a pail** of water that a person bowed to, even **for ordinary** purposes. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is clear that **he is bowing to the water. And this is the dilemma** Rava **raises: Is he bowing to the water before him, and** therefore it follows that **the first** waters, i.e., those that were present at the time of his bowing, **are gone,** and the water that is currently flowing is permitted, **or perhaps is he bowing to the flow of the water,** which would render it prohibited to use all the water that flows in that spring for libations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מִיתַּסְרִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: מַיִם שֶׁל רַבִּים אֵין נֶאֱסָרִין! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָא נָבְעִי מֵאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** the water actually **rendered forbidden** by his worship? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: Water that belongs to the public cannot become forbidden?** The Gemara answers: **No,** the dilemma is not with regard to a public spring. Rather, it is **necessary** with regard to a spring **that flows from the ground** in one specific location that is privately owned.

Rava now poses his question: *hishtachava lema'ayan meimav mahu lenisachim* — if one bows down to a wellspring, can the water be used for the *nisachim* — the libations (whether the water libations of Sukkot or the water used for *menachos*)? The Gemara first probes what exactly is being asked. Perhaps the question is: when one bows to a wellspring, is one worshipping one's own reflection (*lebabu'a kasagid*) or is one worshipping the water itself (*lemaya kasagid*)? If it is the former, the water would not become *ne'evad* at all. But if that were the question, one need not ask it specifically about a wellspring — one could simply ask about a bucket of water in which one sees one's reflection, and whether water in that ordinary context becomes *assur behana'ah*. Since the question is specifically formulated as involving a *mechubar* wellspring, clearly the question of reflection versus water is not what is being asked.

The Gemara therefore reframes the question: *le'olam lemaya ka sagid* — we may assume the worshipper was worshipping the water. The question then is: *lemaya de-kamei ka sagid ve-kamei kamei azlu leh* — was the worshipper worshipping the specific water that stood before him at that moment (which has since flowed on), such that new water coming from the same spring would be entirely permissible? Or perhaps — *o dilma ledivrona de-maya ka sagid* — the worshipper was worshipping the *source* of the water, in which case all water emanating from that spring would be tainted.

### Public Water & Ownership

The Gemara raises an immediate objection: *umi missasra* — can such water even become forbidden? Rabbi Yochanan teaches in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: *mayim she-rabim ein ne'asarin* — water that is publicly owned cannot be rendered forbidden. Just as one cannot worship another person's property and make it *assur*, one cannot worship public goods and render them forbidden. A wellspring is presumably public, so its water should not become forbidden regardless.

The Gemara resolves this: *lo tzericha dekanvei mei ara* — the case under discussion is a wellspring that flows through private property. In that context, there is no obstacle from the principle of public ownership, and Rava's question about whether one is worshipping the current water or its source remains a genuine and live question — to which the Gemara provides no resolution.

## Mishnah - Adjacent House

### House Fell Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיָה בֵּיתוֹ סָמוּךְ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְנָפַל — אָסוּר לִבְנוֹתוֹ. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? כּוֹנֵס בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וּבוֹנֶה. שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — נִידּוֹן מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one whose house was adjacent to** a house of **idol worship and** the dividing wall **fell,** it is **prohibited to rebuild it. What should he do?** He should **withdraw into his own** property **four cubits and build** the wall there. In talmudic times, external walls to homes were often built in two parts, with a functional space between them. If the space between the two parts of the wall **belonged to him and to** the house of **idol worship,** the area **is treated** as **half and half**  with regard to withdrawing into his property before rebuilding the wall; he may build the wall four cubits from the middle of that space.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָנָיו, עֵצָיו וַעֲפָרוֹ — מְטַמְּאִין כְּשֶׁרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שַׁקֵּץ תְּשַׁקְּצֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The stones of** the fallen wall, **its wood, and its dust, transmit impurity like a creeping animal,** i.e., one who touches them becomes impure like one who touches a creeping animal, **as it is stated:** "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house, and be accursed like it; **you shall detest it [*shakketz teshakketzennu*],** and you shall abhor it; for it is a proscribed item" (Deuteronomy 7:26). The term *shakketz* is used in a different form, *shekketz*, with regard to creeping animals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּנִדָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּזְרֵם כְּמוֹ דָוָה צֵא תֹּאמַר לוֹ״, מָה נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא, אַף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** Those items transmit impurity **like a menstruating woman, as it is stated: "You will put them far away as a menstruating woman; you shall say to it: Go away"** (Isaiah 30:22). **Just as a menstruating woman transmits impurity by carrying,** as one who moves a menstruating woman, even without touching her, becomes impure, **so too,** objects of **idol worship transmit impurity by carrying.**

The Gemara moves to a new Mishnah. *Mi shehaya beito samuch l'avodat kochavim venafal, assur livnoto* — if one had a house adjacent to a house of *avodah zarah* and one's own house fell down, it is forbidden to rebuild it. The background assumption, as Rashi and Tosafot explain based on how the Gemara develops, is that the adjacent structure was not merely housing an idol but was itself worshipped alongside the idol. Furthermore, the two houses shared a common wall. One might ask how it was permissible to benefit from this shared wall while it stood, given that the *avodah zarah* property is *assur behana'ah*. The answer follows from the principle we have been discussing: one cannot make another person's property forbidden by worshipping it. The non-Jews worshipping the house made forbidden only what was theirs — at most the half of the shared wall on their side of the property line — but not the portion on the Jewish owner's side. Thus, while the wall stood, using one's half was permissible.

Once the wall fell, however, rebuilding it is forbidden — since doing so would construct a wall that supports and benefits the *avodah zarah* property and would thus aid its status as *avodah zarah*.

### Four Cubits Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָא קָא מַרְוַוח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! אָמַר רַב חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: דְּעָבֵד לֵיהּ בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא. וְהָא בָּעֵי צְנִיעוּתָא! דְּעָבֵד לֵיהּ בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא דְּלַיְלָה. וְהָא אָמַר מָר: אֵיזֶהוּ צָנוּעַ? הַנִּפְנֶה בַּלַּיְלָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁנִּפְנָה בַּיּוֹם, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא בִּכְדֶרֶךְ, מִיהוּ צְנִיעוּתָא בָּעֵי לְמֶעְבַּד! דְּעָבֵד לֵיהּ לְתִינוֹקוֹת. אִי נָמֵי, דְּגָדַיר לֵיהּ בְּהִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one must rebuild his wall at a distance from the house of idol worship. The Gemara asks: **But** how may one do so? **Isn't he creating more space for the** house of **idol worship? Rav Ḥanina of Sura said:** This is not a problem, **as he converts** the empty space into **a bathroom.** Since the mishna teaches that no wall may be built in that space, the bathroom is apparently not enclosed. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** a bathroom **need** to be in a place of **modesty?** The Gemara answers: This is not a problem, **as he converts it** into **a bathroom for nighttime,** when no one can see him. The Gemara asks: **But didn't the master say** in a *baraita*: **Who is a modest person? One who defecates at night in** the same **place that he defecates during the day,** i.e., in a place where no one can see him? **And although we interpreted** the *baraita* as referring not to location but to conduct, teaching that one must conduct himself at night **in the same manner,** i.e., with the same degree of modesty, as he does during the day with regard to removing his clothing when defecating (see *Berakhot* 62a), **nevertheless,** it can be inferred from here that even at night one **is required to observe** the dictates of **modesty.** The Gemara answers: This is not a problem, **as he converts it** into a bathroom **for children,** who are not required to observe the same level of modesty. The Gemara suggests a different answer to the problem of enlarging the space used for idolatry: **Alternatively,** one can answer **that he fences** in the four-cubit space **with thorns and shrubs,** thereby preventing its use.

The Mishnah provides a solution: *yachnis letoch shelo arba amot uvoneh* — one must set one's new wall back four *amot* (approximately six feet) into one's own property from the property line before building. This is necessary so that the non-Jew cannot build his wall directly up against the newly constructed wall and benefit from it.

The Mishnah further addresses the case where the original wall straddled the property line — partly on the Jewish owner's side and partly on the non-Jew's side: *nidon mechtza al mechtza* — it is adjudicated half and half. Rashi understands this to mean that the Jewish owner's half of the old wall — whatever portion fell on his side of the property line — counts toward the four *amot* he must set back. So if the wall was two *amot* thick and one *amah* of it was on his property, that *amah* counts, and he need only move back an additional three *amot* before building.

The Mishnah then states that the stones, wood, and dirt of the fallen wall convey *tumah* like a *sheretz*: *avanav ve'etzav va'afaro metame'im kesheretz*. The verse *shakketz teshakztzenu* is cited — you shall hold it as an abomination — and the word *shakketz* connects to *sheretz*, establishing contact-*tumah*. Rabbi Akiva holds it is *tamei* like a *niddah* — *keniddah* — deriving from the verse *tizrem kmo davah*, cast it out like a menstruant: just as a *niddah* conveys *tumah* through *massa* (carrying), so too does *avodah zarah*. This sudden introduction of the laws of *tumah* is striking, given that the entire preceding discussion has focused on *issur hana'ah* — and, as we shall see, the Gemara will in fact pass over this portion entirely and focus on a different aspect of the Mishnah.

### Benefit Question & Solution

The Gemara focuses on a practical difficulty in the Mishnah's ruling: if the Jewish owner sets his wall back four *amot*, he is leaving a gap on his side of the property line — and that gap effectively gives the non-Jew a pleasant open space or yard adjacent to the *avodah zarah* property. Isn't that a prohibited benefit to the *avodah zarah*?

Rav Chanina from Sura answers: *d'avid lei beit hakisei* — one uses that four-*amah* gap as a latrine. The space then provides no benefit to anyone. The Gemara objects: *v'ha ba'ei tzniusa* — using a latrine requires privacy; one cannot expose oneself right on the property line of the non-Jew. The response: *d'avid lei beit hakisei d'leila* — use the space only as a latrine at night, when the cover of darkness provides sufficient privacy. A further objection is raised from the teaching that a modest person relieves himself at night in the same fashion as he does in the day — meaning one should maintain the same degree of modesty at night as during the day, which would preclude exposing oneself near a neighbor's property line even at night. The Gemara clarifies that this teaching was re-interpreted to refer not to the physical location but to the manner in which one comports oneself — one need not go to the same place, but the degree of modesty (i.e., how much of oneself one exposes) must be the same. Nevertheless, *tzniusa ba'ei lemeevad* — one must still maintain proper modesty, making the latrine solution awkward.

The Gemara therefore offers two further solutions: *d'avid lei letinokos* — use the space for small children to relieve themselves, for whom the full requirements of modesty do not apply. Alternatively, *degadrei lei behizmei vehigi* — plant thorns and thistles in the gap so that neither the non-Jew nor anyone else can use the space for any beneficial purpose.

## Three Houses Teaching

### House Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה בָּתִּים הֵן — בַּיִת שֶׁבְּנָאוֹ מִתְּחִלָּה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר. סִיְּידוֹ וְכִיְּידוֹ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְחִידֵּשׁ — נוֹטֵל מַה שֶּׁחִידֵּשׁ. הִכְנִיס לְתוֹכָהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהוֹצִיאָהּ — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to idol worship **there are three** types of **houses,** each with its own *halakhot*. **A house that one built initially for** the purpose of **idol worship is forbidden,** i.e., it is prohibited to derive benefit from such a house. If one **plastered** a house **or cemented it for** the purpose of **idol worship, and he** thereby **added** a layer to the walls of the house, one **removes that which he added,** and the rest of the house is permitted. If one **brought** an object of **idol worship into** a house temporarily **and** then **removed it,** the house **is** then **permitted.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְבַיִת — אֲסָרוֹ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר תָּלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִבְּרוֹ כְּתָלוּשׁ דָּמֵי, וְהָאֲנַן ״בְּנָאוֹ״ תְּנַן! בְּנָאוֹ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לוֹ, הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא בְּנָאוֹ. אִי הָכִי, הָנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה — אַרְבָּעָה הָווּ! כֵּיוָן דִּלְעִנְיַן בִּיטּוּל, בָּנָה וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה חַד קָא חָשֵׁיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if a house was initially **built** for the purpose of idol worship, it is forbidden. **Rav says: One who bows to a house has rendered it forbidden.** The Gemara infers: **Evidently,** Rav **holds** that an item that was **detached and** that **one subsequently attached is considered as** if it were still **detached.** Consequently, a house, which is attached to the ground but was built with materials that were detached from the ground, can become forbidden through worship. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that if a house was initially **built** for the purpose of idol worship, it is forbidden? This indicates that the house becomes forbidden only when it is initially constructed for idolatrous purposes. The Gemara answers that there are two cases where the house is rendered forbidden: If one **built it** for purposes of idol worship, **even if he did not bow to it,** or if **he bowed to it, even if he did not build it** for purposes of idol worship; it is forbidden in either case. The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does the mishna list only **these three** types of houses, when **there are** really **four?** The Gemara answers: **Since with regard to** the **revocation** of their idolatrous status there is no difference between a house that one **built** for idolatrous purposes **and** a house that one **bowed** down to, the mishna **considers them** as **one.**

The Gemara cites Rav: *hamishtachaveh levayit assur* — one who bows down to a house renders it forbidden. Rav's reasoning is that *talush ulevasof chibur ketalush dami* — something that was unattached and subsequently affixed to the ground retains the legal status of an unattached object. A house, even though it rests upon the ground, was constructed piece by piece from materials that were originally detached — stones, bricks, timber. It therefore never truly acquires the status of *mechubar* in the way that a tree does, which was planted and grew organically as part of the earth. Accordingly, one who bows down to a house can render it forbidden through *avodah zarah*, just as one can render any detached object forbidden.

This is a striking ruling in contrast to a tree, which once planted grows into the landscape in a way that resists the full imposition of *avodah zarah* status. Rav holds that a house never achieves that level of organic attachment, and thus *hishtachaveh* alone can make it forbidden.

The Gemara raises a difficulty: if bowing to a house renders it forbidden, the Mishnah's three categories are actually four — since building a house for *avodah zarah* and bowing to a pre-existing house are two distinct paths to the same prohibition, yet the Mishnah presents them as a unified first category. The Gemara resolves this: *keivan dile'inyan bittul benao vehishtachaveh chad kachashiv* — for the purposes of *bittul*, building a house for *avodah zarah* and bowing down to a house are treated as a single category. In both cases, the entire house is forbidden and requires *bittul* of the whole structure. This distinguishes both from the case of plastering (where only the added plaster is forbidden and the rest of the house is unaffected) and from the case of placing and removing the idol (where no permanent status is conferred at all). Though the Gemara's resolution is not entirely without difficulty — the Mishnah really does seem to be saying that building is the only way to render the house fully forbidden — this is how the Gemara reconciles Rav's teaching with the Mishnah's structure.

## Three Stones Teaching

### Stone Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁלֹשׁ אֲבָנִים הֵן — אֶבֶן שֶׁחֲצָבָהּ מִתְּחִלָּה לְבִימוֹס — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה. סִיְּידָהּ וְכִיְּידָהּ לְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — נוֹטֵל מַה שֶׁסִּיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד, וּמוּתֶּרֶת. הֶעֱמִיד עָלֶיהָ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְסִילְּקָהּ — הֲרֵי זוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to idol worship **there are three** types of **stones,** each with its own *halakhot*. **A stone that** one **initially hewed for** use in **a platform [*bimos*]** for an idol **is forbidden.** If one **plastered** a stone **or cemented it for the sake of idol worship, one removes that which he plastered or cemented and** the stone **is permitted.** If one **erected** an object of **idol worship upon** a stone **and** subsequently **removed it,** the stone **is** then **permitted.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: וְהוּא שֶׁסִּיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל אֶבֶן. וְהָא דֻּומְיָא דְּבַיִת תְּנַן, וּבַיִת לָאו בְּגוּפֵיהּ הוּא, וּמִיתְּסַר! בַּיִת נָמֵי אִיכָּא בֵּינֵי אוּרְבֵי. מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּשִׁייעַ, וַהֲדַר שַׁיְיעֵיהּ? אֶלָּא כִּי אִתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי — לְעִנְיַן בִּיטּוּל אִתְּמַר, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּסִיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל אֶבֶן, כִּי נָטַל מַה שֶּׁחִידֵּשׁ — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. דְּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁסִּיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל אֶבֶן, כְּאֶבֶן שֶׁחֲצָבָהּ מִתְּחִלָּה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דָּמְיָא, וְתִיתְּסַר כּוּלַּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one may not derive benefit from a stone plastered or cemented for the sake of idol worship. **Rabbi Ami says: And** that **is** the *halakha* provided **that one plastered or cemented within the stone itself,** by carving or engraving into it. If the plaster or cement was merely appended to the outside of the stone as an external ornament, the stone is not forbidden. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna the *halakha* with regard to stones immediately following the *halakha* with regard to a house utilized for idol worship? This indicates that the *halakha* of a stone is **similar to** the *halakha* of **a house. And** in the case of **a house** that was rendered forbidden when it was plastered or cemented for idol worship, that plastering was **not** done **within the house itself,** but on the exterior of its walls, **and** even so it is **rendered forbidden.** Accordingly, even external plastering should render the stone forbidden. The Gemara answers: In the case of **a house** it can **also** be explained that the plaster or cement was added to the house itself, as **there is** space **in between the bricks** that can be filled in. The Gemara asks: As the mishna does not specify, **are we not dealing** even with a house **that one plastered** with no idolatrous purpose **and** then **plastered again** on top of that layer for the sake of idol worship, rendering the house forbidden even without affecting the house itself? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ami was not explaining at what point a stone plastered for idolatry becomes forbidden; **rather, when Rabbi Ami's** statement **was stated, it was stated with regard to revocation** of the stone's idolatrous status, **and** he taught that **even if one plastered or cemented within the stone itself, when one removes that which was added,** it is **permitted.** The Gemara explains Rabbi Ami's ruling: **Lest you say** that **since one plastered or cemented within the stone itself, it is equivalent to a stone that one initially hewed for idol worship and the entire** stone is **forbidden,** Rabbi Ami **teaches us** that the stone can become permitted by the removal of the additional layer of plaster.

It is worth noting that the *bimus* case is conceptually a case of *tashmish* — a platform for the idol — rather than something itself worshipped. This would ordinarily mean it becomes forbidden only when actually put to use. Rashi and Tosafot nonetheless understand, based on how the Gemara develops, that the platform too was worshipped directly alongside the idol.

Rabbi Ami makes a statement that at first seems to narrow the second category significantly: *vehu she'siad b'kiur b'gufo shel aven* — for the plastering to render the stone (or by extension, the house) forbidden, the plaster must have penetrated into the body of the stone itself, filling its crevices and fissures. The implication seems to be that if the plaster merely sits atop the stone's surface without penetrating its substance, it does not become forbidden.

The Gemara challenges this: *vahadumya d'bayis tnan* — but the analogous ruling in the Mishnah concerns a house, where plaster by its nature sits between the bricks and on the surface of walls. *Bayis lav b'gufei hu mitzar* — in a house, the plaster does not actually penetrate the body of any single brick. Yet the Mishnah's ruling about plastering a house for *avodah zarah* clearly renders the plaster forbidden. The Gemara responds: *bayis nami ika beini urbei* — in a house too there are gaps between the bricks where plaster can penetrate. So Rabbi Ami's principle would apply there as well.

But the Gemara presses further: *mi lo askinan d'shaya v'hadar shaya* — does the Mishnah not also encompass a case where the stone or house was already plastered once and then plastered again for *avodah zarah*, with none of the new plaster penetrating the stone itself? Reading Rabbi Ami's statement as a threshold condition for the Mishnah's ruling seems to exclude too many cases and contradicts the plain sense of the Mishnah.

### Plaster Clarification

The Gemara therefore reframes Rabbi Ami's statement entirely: *ella ki itmar d'Rabi Ami l'inyan bittul itmar* — what Rabbi Ami was actually speaking about is the question of *bittul*, not the threshold condition for the plaster to become forbidden in the first place.

Rabbi Ami's *chiddush* is as follows: even in a case where the plaster did penetrate into the crevices of the stone itself, *keivan d'siad b'kiur b'gufo shel aven* — one might have thought that such deep penetration transforms the entire stone, rendering it as though it were hewn from the outset for *avodah zarah* and therefore wholly forbidden, with *bittul* of the whole stone required. Rabbi Ami teaches otherwise: *ki mato shapir dami* — even when the plaster has penetrated the stone, when one removes the plaster, the stone reverts to being *muttar*. The only thing that was ever forbidden was the plaster itself. *D'mahu d'teima keivan d'siad b'kiur b'gufo shel aven k'man d'chatzvei mitchilah l'avodas kochavim dami v'issar kulei* — one might have assumed that deep penetration is tantamount to having hewn the stone originally for *avodah zarah*, making the whole stone forbidden. Rabbi Ami's statement forecloses this: the added material is what was consecrated to *avodah zarah*, and removing it restores the original object fully.

As noted at the outset, throughout both the houses and stones discussions, the precise character of what is worshipped — whether the structure is itself the *avodah zarah* or merely a *tashmish* — remains somewhat underdetermined by the Mishnah's language. Rashi and Tosafot, drawing on the Gemara's own reasoning, conclude that these Mishnayos are speaking of structures that were directly worshipped as *avodah zarah* rather than merely serving as its appurtenances — though the logic of the *bimus* in particular would most naturally suggest a *tashmish* case.
