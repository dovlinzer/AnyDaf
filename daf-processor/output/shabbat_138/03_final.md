# Shabbat 138 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sieve on Vessel

### R. Eliezer vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: תּוֹלִין אֶת הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְנוֹתְנִין לַתְּלוּיָה בַּשַּׁבָּת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין תּוֹלִין אֶת הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין לַתְּלוּיָה בַּשַּׁבָּת, אֲבָל נוֹתְנִין לַתְּלוּיָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Eliezer says: One may suspend** and stretch over a base **the strainer** through which sediment is filtered from wine, **on a Festival. And one may place** wine **through** a strainer that was already **suspended** the day before; however, one may not suspend the strainer **on Shabbat. And the Rabbis say: One may not suspend the strainer on a Festival, and one may not place** wine for filtering **through a suspended** strainer **on Shabbat; however, one may place** wine **through a suspended** strainer **on a Festival.**

The daf opens at the very bottom of 137b, two lines from the bottom, picking up a new topic that was introduced after the extensive treatment of *brit milah*. The subject is the act of placing a sieve on top of some type of jar or vessel. Rabbi Eliezer permits doing so on *Yom Tov*, while the *Chachamim* forbid it. The Gemara frames this as a case of *ohel arai* — a temporary tent-like structure — which is striking, since it would never obviously occur to us that stretching something over the top of a jar constitutes an *ohel*. Yet that is precisely the Gemara's framing.

### Ohel Arai & Machshirei

The reason Rabbi Eliezer permits it is that he allows even *machshirei ochel nefesh* on *Yom Tov* — preparatory activities related to food, including things that could have been done *erev Yom Tov*. This framing of the case as *machshirei ochel nefesh* implies that stretching the sieve over the jar could involve a genuine biblical concern of *ohel*, such that Rabbi Eliezer must invoke a specific permissive principle to allow it. The *Chachamim*, however, maintain that it may not be done on *Yom Tov*.

### Tala Mai—Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תָּלָה מַאי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תָּלָה — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say:** One may not suspend the strainer on a Festival. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **he suspended** a strainer unwittingly, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rav Yosef said:** If **he suspended** it, **he is liable** to bring **a sin-offering,** like anyone who unwittingly performs a labor prohibited by Torah law on Shabbat.

The Gemara now poses the question *tala mai* — what is the legal status if one actually did place this sieve stretched over the vessel on *Yom Tov*? Is this a genuine *de'oraita* violation, or something else? This question is characteristic of these later chapters of *Shabbat*, where the Mishnah lists prohibited activities without clarifying whether each prohibition is biblical or rabbinic, leaving it to the *Amoraim* to work out the underlying status.

### Rav Yosef: Chayav

Rav Yosef rules: *tala chayav chatas* — if one stretched the sieve over the vessel, he is liable for a sin-offering. This is a strong position, asserting that the act constitutes a genuine biblical violation.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּלָא כּוּזָא בְּסִיכְּתָא הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיחַיַּיב?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But if** that is **so,** that an action of that sort constitutes performance of the prohibited labor of building by Torah law, then if **one suspended a jug on a peg, is he also liable** for building a tent?

Abaye challenges this: *of ohel*? Are we really talking about a true *ohel* here? The force of his objection is that an *ohel* is ordinarily understood as a structure, whereas what we are dealing with is essentially a vessel. Even if one could technically identify a roof-like element in the act of covering a jar — and the analogy to wrapping something tightly in a membrane is useful here — it hardly resembles the kind of independent structure one normally associates with *ohel*. He further presses: does it follow that hanging a jar from a nail would similarly make one liable? Obviously, the mere act of suspending something creates no *ohel*; his point is that something so trivial cannot properly be called a *melacha*.

### Midrabbanan & Uvda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּרַבָּנַן הִיא, שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂה כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה בַּחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: It is a rabbinic** decree issued so **that one will not conduct** himself on Shabbat **in the manner that he conducts** himself **during the week.**

Abaye resolves the question: *midrabbanan hi* — the prohibition is entirely rabbinic. And notably, he does not even characterize it as a rabbinic *ohel*. Instead, he frames it as *uvda d'chol* — a weekday-type activity, the kind of thing one does in the course of ordinary labor on a weekday, which is inappropriate on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* even absent any actual *melacha* category. What constitutes *uvda d'chol* is itself an open question; here it may be associated with the activity of sifting wine, which borders on *borer*, but the concept does not always map neatly onto a specific *melacha*. The upshot of Abaye's position is that when Rabbi Eliezer permits this on *Yom Tov*, there is no great novelty: since it is merely *uvda d'chol* and involves food preparation, it falls within the broad permission to engage in food-related activities on *Yom Tov*.

This leads naturally into a broader contemporary application. The *Shulchan Aruch*, drawing on the *Terumat HaDeshen*, rules that certain uses of specific implements on *Shabbat* are prohibited as *uvda d'chol*. Based on this, some *Acharonim* — including the *Shemirat Shabbat* — extend the principle to forbid the use of labor-saving kitchen gadgets on *Shabbat* as *uvda d'chol*, reasoning that any device reminiscent of large-scale production is inconsistent with the spirit of *Shabbat*. Rav Moshe, however, disagrees, limiting the prohibition to implements associated with large-scale commercial production, and ruling that ordinary small-scale kitchen devices used domestically do not constitute *uvda d'chol*. The parameters of this category thus remain genuinely contested.

## Temporary Structures

### Four Prohibited Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנַקֵּיט אַבָּיֵי חוּמְרֵי מַתְנְיָתָא וְתָנֵי: הַגּוֹד וְהַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת, כִּילָּה וְכִסֵּא גַלִּין — לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, וְאִם עָשָׂה — פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר. אׇהֳלֵי קְבַע — לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, וְאִם עָשָׂה — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת. אֲבָל מִטָּה וְכִסֵּא טְרַסְקָל וְאַסְלָא — מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתָן לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye would consolidate the principles of the *baraitot*** pertaining to the construction of a tent on Shabbat **and teach:** With regard to **a large wineskin, a** wine **strainer, a canopy** hung over a bed, **and a folding chair** whose cover is detached from its legs, **one may not assemble** them due to the prohibition against making a temporary tent. **If one did** so unwittingly, **he is exempt** by Torah law from bringing a sin-offering, **but** it is **prohibited** by rabbinic decree. With regard to **permanent tents, one may not make** them, **and if he did** so, **he is liable to** bring **a sin-offering** for performing the prohibited labor of building. **However,** with regard to **a bed, and a folding chair [*teraskal*]** whose cover is attached to its legs, **and a** collapsible **toilet, it is permissible to open them *ab initio*,** since they are prepared for use from before Shabbat.

Abaye gathered together various *Baraitot* and *Mishnayot* — the word *chumrei* here means bundles, not stringencies — and organized them into groupings. He taught the following: *ha-god, ha-mishmeret, kila, v'chisei galen lo yaaseh* — four prohibited structures. The *god* is a leather flask or canteen used for storing wine, which travelers would stretch over poles so that the wind would keep it cool; suspending this large leather pouch creates something resembling an *ohel*. The *mishmeret* is precisely the sieve-over-vessel case of our Mishnah. The *kila* is a canopy over a bed. And *chisei galen* is a type of chair whose leather seat is stretched over an open frame — picture opening the legs of a director's chair and then stretching the leather seat across the top, creating both a functional surface and a kind of roof-like covering. All four of these one should not do on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*.

### Tefach & Liability

If one did perform any of these, the ruling is *patur aval assur* — one is exempt from a sin-offering but it remains forbidden. None of these constitutes a genuine biblical *ohel*; they lack the permanence required and, critically, they are not even made to function as an *ohel* in any meaningful structural sense. These are rabbinic prohibitions only. By contrast, *ohel keva* — a permanent structure — one may not build, and if one did, one would be *chayav chatas*. Two factors distinguish the rabbinic cases from the biblical one: the structures are temporary, and they are not made to serve as genuine *ohalim*.

### Hinged Structures

The Gemara then introduces the category of what is fully permitted: a bed, a *chisei traskal* (a folding chair, like a director's chair, where everything is attached on hinges and one merely opens it), and an *asla* (a similar chair with an opening in the seat used as a toilet). Rashi explains that the bed case involves a bed that was standing vertically against the wall — like a Murphy bed — which one simply lowers down to the horizontal position. When one does so, an *ohel*-like space is created beneath it, yet one has not erected anything; one has only returned something to its natural position. The *traskal* similarly involves a structure that is entirely pre-made on hinges, requiring nothing more than opening. All of these are *mutarin letasam lechatchila* — permitted to be set up at the outset.

The question of an umbrella is directly relevant here. The historical consensus among *poskim* treats umbrellas as prohibited, despite the fact that a fresh analysis might well permit them given these principles, and that tradition carries normative weight.

### Pre-made Principle

The governing principle is important: something already constructed and on hinges, which merely requires opening, is not considered a new act of *ohel* creation. An additional factor that favors all these permitted cases is that none of them are being made *for the purpose of* an *ohel* — a chair, a bed, a table exists to serve the person using it, not to protect the space beneath it. This further distinguishes these cases from genuine *ohel* creation. However, caution is warranted before extending these rulings expansively; a canopy over a baby carriage, for instance, does serve to protect the space beneath it in the manner of an *ohel*, which complicates any straightforward application of the pre-made principle — though it remains true that the structure is pre-assembled and requires only opening. The question of pop-up tents and collapsible cribs follows a similar logic, though the concern of *boneh* may be raised in those cases as well, particularly where legs must be snapped into place.

## Wine Sifting

### Wine in Sieve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין לַתְּלוּיָה בַּשַּׁבָּת. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שִׁימֵּר מַאי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: שִׁימֵּר — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna: **One may not place** wine for filtering even **into** a **suspended** strainer **on Shabbat. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **one strained** wine, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rav Kahana said:** If **one strained** wine, **he is liable to** bring **a sin-offering.**

The Gemara transitions back to a case involving *borer*-type separation before returning to the *ohel* discussion. The Mishnah states that one may not place wine in a sieve on *Shabbat*. The Gemara asks *shimmer mai* — what is the halakhic status if one actually did sieve the wine? Rav Kahana rules: *ha-shimmer chayav chatas* — one who sifted the wine is liable for a sin-offering, making it a biblical violation.

### Challenge: Rav Sheshes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּרַבָּנַן מְחַיְּיבִי חַטָּאת, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שָׁרֵי לְכַתְּחִילָּה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet strongly objects to this: Is there something for which the Rabbis render one liable** to bring **a sin-offering and Rabbi Eliezer permits** its performance ***ab initio*?** Extreme differences of opinion of that kind are rarely found in one mishna.

Rav Sheshes challenges this forcefully: how is it possible that the two positions in the dispute are at such extreme opposites? Rabbi Eliezer permits sieving wine *lechatchila* — even at the outset — while the *Chachamim* prohibit it. If the *Chachamim's* prohibition were merely rabbinic, that would be understandable. But to say that Rabbi Eliezer permits something outright while the *Chachamim* consider it a biblical *chiyuv chatas* — can such diametrically opposed rulings really coexist within a single dispute?

### Golden Tiara Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אַלְּמָה לָא? הֲרֵי עִיר שֶׁל זָהָב, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב חַטָּאת, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שָׁרֵי לְכַתְּחִילָּה. מַאי הִיא — דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה בְּעִיר שֶׁל זָהָב, וְאִם יָצְאָה — חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא תֵּצֵא, וְאִם יָצְאָה פְּטוּרָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יוֹצְאָה אִשָּׁה בְּעִיר שֶׁל זָהָב לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to** this question: **Why not? Isn't** there an analogous dispute with regard to a woman who wears **a city of gold** ornament from one domain to another on Shabbat, **as Rabbi Meir renders** her **liable** to bring **a sin-offering, and Rabbi Eliezer permits** it even ***ab initio*?** **What is** that dispute? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman may not go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **with a city of gold** ornament. **And if she did go out** with it into the public domain, **she is liable to bring a sin-offering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: She may not go out** with it *ab initio*, **and if she went out, she is exempt. And Rabbi Eliezer says: A woman may go out with a city of gold** ornament ***ab initio*.** Apparently, there is precedent for a dispute in which one opinion maintains that an action incurs liability to bring a sin-offering, while another opinion rules that it is permitted *ab initio*.

Rav Yosef replies: why not? There is a precedent. Consider the *ir shel zahav* — the golden tiara or golden city ornament — where we find precisely this pattern. Rabbi Meir rules that a woman who goes out wearing this ornament into the public domain on *Shabbat* is *chayav chatas*. The *Chachamim* rule *patur aval assur* — she is exempt but it remains forbidden. And Rabbi Eliezer rules she may go out wearing it *lechatchila* from the outset. In that dispute, Rabbi Meir's position of *chiyuv chatas* and Rabbi Eliezer's position of *mutar lechatchila* coexist, despite being extreme opposites. The reasoning: Rabbi Meir holds that this type of ornament is not recognized as standard clothing or jewelry that women typically wear, so wearing it constitutes a form of carrying rather than wearing; Rabbi Eliezer holds that it is genuine jewelry and that there is no concern she will remove it to show her friends.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי סָבְרַתְּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר קָאֵי, דְּאָמַר חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת? אַדְּרַבָּנַן קָאֵי, דְּאָמְרִי פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ אִיהוּ מוּתָּר לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Do you hold that Rabbi Eliezer is relating to** the statement of **Rabbi Meir, who said** that **she is liable** to bring **a sin-offering? He is relating to** the statement of **the Rabbis, who said one is exempt but it is prohibited, and he said to them** that he holds that it is **permitted *ab initio*.** Had there not been the intermediate opinion of the Rabbis, an argument with such extreme opinions would not have been possible.

Abaye responds to this parallel with a sharp observation: *mi savrat Rabbi Eliezer ad Rabbi Meir kai*? Who says Rabbi Eliezer in that case is even responding to Rabbi Meir? Perhaps he is responding only to the *Chachamim*, who say *patur aval assur*, and he is telling them it is *mutar lechatchila* — so the opposition is between *assur* and *mutar*, not between *chayav chatas* and *mutar*. Tosafot here admits genuine difficulty. The Tosafot notes that even according to Abaye's reframing, the actual halakhic positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer remain at extreme opposites — one holding *chayav chatas* and the other holding *mutar lechatchila* — even if they are not directly disputing one another. Tosafot openly acknowledges not fully understanding what the Gemara's answer accomplishes.

### Meraqed vs. Borer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם מַאי מַתְרִינַן בֵּיהּ? רַבָּה אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בּוֹרֵר. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם מְרַקֵּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: One is liable to bring a sin-offering for straining. **Due to** performance of **what** category of prohibited labor **do we forewarn him? Rabba said:** It is **for** the category of **selecting,** as one is selecting the wine from the sediment. **Rabbi Zeira said:** It is **for** the category of **sifting,** as straining is similar to sifting flour in a sifter, which is a form of selecting.

The Gemara proceeds apparently satisfied that Rabbi Eliezer can hold something is *mutar* even when others hold it warrants a sin-offering. It therefore now asks: *mishum mai matrin bei* — when one warns the person who sieves wine of the category of *melacha* he is about to violate, what *av melacha* is invoked?

### Analysis & Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, כְּווֹתִי דִּידִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא: מָה דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹרֵר — נוֹטֵל אוֹכֵל וּמַנִּיחַ הַפְּסוֹלֶת, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי: נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאוֹכֵל וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַפְּסוֹלֶת. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, כְּווֹתִי דִּידִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא: מָה דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל מְרַקֵּד — פְּסוֹלֶת מִלְּמַעְלָה וְאוֹכֶל מִלְּמַטָּה, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי: פְּסוֹלֶת מִלְּמַעְלָה וְאוֹכֶל מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: According to my opinion, it is reasonable. What is the manner of one who selects?** He **takes** the **food and leaves the refuse; here too,** when straining wine, **one takes the food and leaves the refuse.** **Rabbi Zeira said: According to my opinion,** that this is not typical selection but rather a specific type of selection, **it is reasonable,** as **what is the manner of sifting?** The **refuse** remains **atop** the sifter **and the food is below. Here too,** when straining wine, the **refuse** remains **atop** the strainer **and the food is below.**

Raba says the relevant *melacha* is *borer*. This might seem to stand to reason. *Borer* is the act of taking food while leaving the waste behind. Here too, the wine passes through the sieve and comes out — that is the food — while the lees and grape remnants remain stuck above — that is the *pesolet*. One is, in effect, extracting the food from the mixture.

Rabbi Zeira disagrees and says the *melacha* is *meraked* — sifting. The distinguishing feature of *meraked* is that the *pesolet* is on top and the food comes out below. Here too, the grape sediment remains on top of the sieve while the pure wine falls through beneath — *pesolet mil'ma'ala v'ochel mil'mata*.

At first glance this debate seems almost self-answering: the case of wine sieving is obviously more similar to *meraked* than to *borer*, since *meraked* involves a sieve and the same spatial arrangement — refuse on top, food below — whereas *borer* typically involves manually removing food from a pile without a sieve. Rashi makes this explicit: he explains that according to Raba, the act would also qualify as *meraked* — Raba is not denying it resembles *meraked* — but he holds it is additionally classified as *borer* and one could warn the violator on that basis as well. Rabbi Zeira, however, denies that it qualifies as *borer*: for *borer*, the refuse sits at the bottom of the pile and the food is taken out from above by hand, as with legumes. Since the spatial arrangement here is inverted — refuse above, food below — it does not sufficiently resemble *borer* to constitute a proper *hatra'ah* on that basis. Everyone agrees it is *meraked*.

A broader halakhic point worth noting here, though it is not developed in full at this juncture: there is a significant leniency that applies to liquids but not solids in the laws of *borer*. With respect to liquids, if the mixture was drinkable before being sieved, one is permitted to pour it through a sieve on *Shabbat*. This leniency — applied, for instance, to water filters and to orange juice with pulp (permitting its straining for someone who would drink it with pulp anyway) — has no parallel in the laws of *borer* as applied to solid foods. Part of the reason may be that passing liquid through a sieve happens somewhat passively, unlike the active manual sorting characteristic of *borer* with solids. But regardless, the Gemara's conclusion is that one who sieves wine is indeed *chayav* — the act is most clearly analogous to *meraked*, and may also implicate *borer*.

## Tallit as Canopy

### Folded Tallit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: טַלִּית כְּפוּלָה לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, וְאִם עָשָׂה — פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר. הָיָה כָּרוּךְ עָלֶיהָ חוּט אוֹ מְשִׁיחָה — מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתָהּ לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Yeḥezkel taught:** With regard to **a doubled cloak, one may not make** a covering on Shabbat by taking the cloak and placing it over a rope and extending the two sides in order to form something similar to a canopy beneath which one could lie (*ge'onim*; Rif). **And if one made** it, **he is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering by Torah law, **but it is prohibited** by rabbinic decree. If **there was a string or a cord wrapped around it** before Shabbat, and the cloak was attached to the string while folded, **it is permitted to** spread it and **stretch it *ab initio*.**

Rami bar Yechezkel taught: *tallit kfulah lo yaaseh* — one should not spread a folded *tallit* over posts to create a canopy on *Shabbat*. Rashi explains that *kfulah* means a cloth doubled over, such that it not only covers the top but falls over the sides, creating walls as well — making it function even more fully as an *ohel*. The ruling: *patur aval assur* — if one did so, one is exempt from a sin-offering but the act is rabbinically forbidden. It remains a temporary *ohel* and does not carry biblical liability, but it is prohibited.

### Drawstring Allowance

The greater novelty in Rami bar Yechezkel's teaching is what he permits. If the *tallit* has a string or drawstring (*chut o meshichah*) attached to it, it is *mutar limotcho lechatchila* — one may unfurl it at the outset on *Shabbat*. The rationale is analogous to the hinged structures discussed earlier: if a string is already attached and the structure need only be pulled open, one is not creating something new. Even a drawstring mechanism is sufficient to render the act one of opening rather than erecting.

### Tosafos: Tefach Rule

Tosafot introduces an important qualification that distinguishes the drawstring case from the hinged-structure case. With a hinged object like a bed or folding chair, the entire structure is pre-made and one merely opens it — no additional criterion is required. But with a *tallit* and a drawstring, which is not on hinges and involves a more active spreading, Tosafot holds that there must already be a first *tefach* of the *ohel* constructed before one may spread it further. The principle is that one may add to a pre-existing *ohel arai* — this is permitted — but one may not *create* an *ohel arai* from scratch even with a drawstring. If the first *tefach* is already in place, then spreading the remainder merely extends an existing structure rather than originating one. This requirement of a pre-existing first *tefach* applies to the drawstring case specifically; hinged structures do not require even this.

## Canopies & Beds

### Rav & Rav Kahana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא מֵרַב: כִּילָּה מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף מִטָּה אֲסוּרָה. מִטָּה מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף כִּילָּה מוּתֶּרֶת. כִּילָּה וּמִטָּה מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּילָּה אֲסוּרָה וּמִטָּה מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** On a related issue, **Rav Kahana raised a dilemma before Rav:** In the case of **a canopy, what** is the *halakha*? Is it permitted to spread it on Shabbat? **He said to him: Even a bed is prohibited.** Rav Kahana asked: With regard to **a bed, what** is the *halakha*? **He said to him: Even a canopy is permitted.** Rav Kahana again asked: In the case of **a bed and a canopy, what** is the *halakha*? **He said to him: A canopy is prohibited, and a bed is permitted.**

The Gemara records a playful exchange between Rav Kahana and Rav. Rav Kahana asked: *kilah mahu* — what is the law regarding a canopy over a bed? Rav replied: *af mitah asurah* — why are you asking about the canopy? Even putting up a bed is forbidden. Rav Kahana then asked: so what about the bed? Rav reversed: *af kilah muteret* — why are you asking about the bed? Even a canopy is permitted. Rav Kahana, seeing he was being toyed with, asked plainly about both. Rav then gave the actual ruling: *kilah asurah u'mitah muteret* — the canopy is forbidden, the bed is permitted.

### Carmonite Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא קַשְׁיָא. הָא דְּקָאָמַר אַף מִטָּה אֲסוּרָה — כִּדְקַרְמֹנָאֵי. הָא דְּקָאֲמַר לֵיהּ אַף כִּילָּה מוּתֶּרֶת — כִּדְרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל. כִּילָּה אֲסוּרָה וּמִטָּה מוּתֶּרֶת — כִּדְדִידַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this is **not difficult,** as the responses do not in fact contradict one another. Rather, **when he said: Even a bed is prohibited,** this is referring to folding beds **like those of the Carmanians.** Unfolding them is considered like making a tent. **When he said: Even a canopy is permitted,** this is referring to spreading the canopy in the manner explained by **Rami bar Yeḥezkel.** The canopy was bound by a string from before Shabbat. When he said: **A canopy is prohibited, and a bed is permitted,** this is referring to beds and canopies **like ours,** which do not fold. A bed of that kind involves no building. However, spreading canopies is performed in a manner similar to constructing a tent.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing cases. When Rav said "even a bed is forbidden," he was referring to the Carmonian (*karmonai*) bed — a type of bed that is not simply lowered from a vertical to a horizontal position, but requires one to snap the legs into place. This raises serious concerns not merely of *ohel* but of genuine *boneh* — the act of assembling a functional vessel — and also of *makkeh b'patish*, since one might end up banging the legs firmly into their sockets to secure them. When Rav said "even the canopy is permitted," he was referring to the case described by Rami bar Yechezkel — a canopy with a drawstring already attached, and as Tosafot notes, with a first *tefach* already in place, such that one is only extending a pre-existing structure. The straightforward ruling for normal cases is: making a canopy from scratch is forbidden; lowering a bed that was leaning vertically is permitted.

### Rav Huna's Canopies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: חֲזֵינָא לְהוּ לְכִילֵּי דְּבֵי רַב הוּנָא דְּמֵאוּרְתָּא נְגִידָא וּמִצַּפְרָא חֲבִיטָא רַמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: I saw the canopies of the house of Rav Huna that were spread out in the evening, and in the morning they were cast off and lying** on the ground. This indicates that it is permitted to dismantle and spread them on Shabbat.

Rav Yosef reports an empirical observation: *chazina lehu l'kilta d'bei Rav Huna* — I observed the canopies in the household of Rav Huna. They were spread in the evening and thrown down in the morning — *me'orta negidi, umitzfra chavita ramya*. Rashi understands this as evidence that if canopies can be taken down they can presumably also be put up, and that these too were cases involving drawstrings or similar pre-made elements that permitted the act.

### Curtains Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: וִילוֹן מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתוֹ וּמוּתָּר לְפוֹרְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya:** With regard to **a curtain, it is permitted to spread it, and it is permitted to dismantle it.** Since a curtain has no roof, neither action constitutes establishing a tent.

Rav says in the name of Rabbi Chiya: a *vilon* — a curtain serving as a temporary wall — *mutar linototo* — may be hung on *Shabbat*, and *mutar l'parko* — may also be taken down. This introduces the broader question of whether the *melacha* of *ohel* applies to walls as well as roofs. The general assumption is that making a temporary wall, so long as it is not being attached to a permanent structure, does not constitute a prohibited act. A free-standing temporary curtain — a *mechitzah shel tzniusa*, for instance — does not raise an *ohel* concern. The issues that arise with *mechitzot* on *Shabbat* typically involve situations where the partition is being erected to serve a specific halakhic function, which is a separate discussion.

### Wedding Canopies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כִּילַּת חֲתָנִים מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתָהּ וּמוּתָּר לְפוֹרְקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya:** With regard to **a bridegroom's canopy,** which has no roof but is entirely sloped, **it is permitted to spread it and it is permitted to dismantle it** on Shabbat.

Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Chiya: *kilas chasanim* — the wedding canopy used in the bridal suite — *mutar linotosah* — may be erected on *Shabbat*, and *mutar l'parkah* — may be taken down. These wedding canopies, unlike standard bed canopies with flat roofs, came to a point like a teepee. A flat roof that is a *tefach* wide is what creates an *ohel*; a canopy that comes to a point has no such roof and is therefore classified entirely as walls. Since walls are permitted, the wedding canopy may be erected.

### Rav Sheshes: Tefach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין בְּגַגָּהּ טֶפַח, אֲבָל יֵשׁ בְּגַגָּהּ טֶפַח — אֲסוּרָה. וְכִי אֵין בְּגַגָּהּ טֶפַח נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לְגַגָּהּ טֶפַח, אֲבָל יֵשׁ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לְגַגָּהּ טֶפַח — אָסוּר. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין בְּשִׁיפּוּעָהּ טֶפַח, אֲבָל יֵשׁ בְּשִׁיפּוּעָהּ טֶפַח — שִׁפּוּעֵי אֹהָלִים כְּאֹהָלִים דָּמוּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא נָחֵית מִפּוּרְיָא טֶפַח, אֲבָל נָחֵית מִפּוּרְיָא טֶפַח — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, said: We only said** that it is permitted in a case where **its roof is not a handbreadth** wide; **however,** if **its roof is a handbreadth** wide, **it is prohibited.** Furthermore, even **when its roof is not a handbreadth** wide, **we only said** that it is permitted **where there is not** the width of **a handbreadth within three** handbreadths **of its roof; however, if it** expands to the width of **a handbreadth within three** handbreadths **of its roof, it is prohibited. And we only stated** that it is permitted **where there is not in its incline** the width of **a handbreadth; however,** if **there is in its incline** the width of **a handbreadth, it is prohibited.** This *halakha* is in accordance with the principle that **the inclines of tents,** even though they are not actual roofs, **are considered like tents. And we only said** that this canopy is permitted **where** no part of the canopy **descends** to **a handbreadth** below the bed; **however,** if part of the canopy **descends** to **a handbreadth** below the bed, **it is prohibited,** as the bed itself becomes a roof, and the curtain is considered a wall.

Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi refines this: *lo amran ela she'ein b'gagah tefach* — the permission applies only when the top of the canopy does not reach a *tefach* in width. If the very apex of the canopy is a *tefach* wide, it is forbidden because there is now a genuine roof. But even when the apex is not a *tefach* wide, the permission is further limited: *ela she'pachut mi'shlosha samuch l'gagah* — the top must not reach a *tefach* width within three *tefachim* down from the apex. This is because the principle of *lavud* — that anything within three *tefachim* is considered legally connected — would cause the *tefach* width three *tefachim* below the apex to be treated as if it were the roof itself. One must therefore ensure that no *tefach* of width is found within the top three *tefachim* of the structure.

The Gemara adds a further restriction: *lo amran ela she'ein b'shipua tefach* — even in the slope of the walls, there must not be a *tefach* of width. If there is a *tefach* width in the incline, then *shipuei ohalim ke-ohalim dami* — the inclined walls of tents are treated as the tent roof itself. Rashi acknowledges that taken to its logical conclusion, this rule creates difficulty: if the entire bottom of the canopy is two *tefachim* wide, it seemingly would always be forbidden. Rashi resolves this by suggesting the canopy was purely decorative and not actually placed over the bed. Tosafot offers the alternative that the walls angle outward in a manner that avoids creating a *tefach* of horizontal width at any point, though this reading is difficult. The most straightforward resolution, adopted by Tosafot, is simply that this final line does not belong in the Gemara text at all.

A final condition: *delo necha mipuraya tefach* — the canopy must not hang down a *tefach* below the edge of the mattress. If the canopy extends beyond the edges of the bed and hangs down a *tefach*, then those hanging portions are considered walls and the top portion of the canopy becomes a roof — at which point the structure is forbidden. The entire permission depends on the canopy being understood as consisting entirely of walls converging to a point, with no portion functioning as a roof.

## Hats & Clothing

### Felt Hat Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: הַאי סְיָאנָא שָׁרֵי. וְהָאִיתְּמַר: סְיָאנָא אָסוּר! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ טֶפַח, הָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet, son** of **Rav Idi,** also **said:** Wearing **this felt hat is permitted** on Shabbat, even though it has a wide brim and is similar to a tent. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Wasn't it stated** elsewhere that **it is prohibited** to wear **a felt hat** on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: This** latter statement, which prohibited wearing the hat, is referring to a case **where its** brim **has** the width of **a handbreadth** wide and is similar to a tent. **That** statement by Rav Sheshet, which permits wearing the hat, is referring to a case **where its** brim **does not have** the width of **a handbreadth.**

Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi continues: *hai siana shari* — this *siana*, a felt hat, is permitted on *Shabbat*. Rashi identifies the *siana* as *kova shel levad* — a felt hat. The reasoning is analogous to the bed and the folding chair: the entire structure is pre-made, and placing it on one's head does not involve any act of spreading or construction. One is not making something new.

### Brim Width Debate

The Gemara immediately complicates this: but is it not also taught that a *siana* is forbidden? The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha de'ika tefach, ha de'leika tefach* — it depends on whether the brim is a *tefach* wide. A hat with a brim wider than a *tefach* — four inches — presents an *ohel* concern, since the *tefach* threshold is what transforms a covering into a halakhically significant *ohel*. What makes this case somewhat different from the bed or chair is that a hat's brim genuinely functions as an *ohel* — its purpose is to provide shade beneath it — whereas the bed and chair are not made to protect the space underneath them. Still, it remains somewhat anomalous that something entirely pre-made and worn constantly should be treated differently.

### Cloak Tightness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, שַׁרְבֵּיב בִּגְלִימָא טֶפַח הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיחַיַּיב? אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּמִיהַדַּק, הָא דְּלָא מִיהַדַּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if** that is **so,** if **one extended** his **cloak a handbreadth** beyond his head, would you **also** say **that he is liable** for making a tent? **Rather,** this is **not difficult.** The reason the hat is prohibited is not due to making a tent, but due to concern that the wind might blow the cap off one's head and he will come to carry it by hand. The conflicting statements can be resolved as follows: **This** statement of Rav Sheshet, which permits wearing the hat, is referring to a case **where it is** fitted **firmly** on his head. **That** statement, which prohibits wearing the hat, is referring to a case **where it is not** fitted **firmly.**

The Gemara presses further: if a wide-brimmed hat is forbidden, then by the same logic, should one not also be forbidden from pulling one's cloak over one's head — creating a *tefach* of covering above? The Gemara resolves this: *lo kashya — ha de'mahedik, ha de'lo mahedik* — the distinction is between holding it tightly and not tightly. Rashi explains that the issue has nothing to do with *ohel* at all. When one holds the cloak loosely over one's head, the concern is that the wind may blow it off and one will rush to retrieve it and end up carrying it in a *reshut harabbim*. When one holds it tightly, that fear does not apply. Under this reading, the *ohel* concern is simply not the operative issue with a cloak held over the head.

### Tosafos: Stiff Brim

Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Chananel, offers a different reading of *de'mahedik* and *de'lo mahedik*. Tightly (*mahedik*) refers to a brim that is stiff and does not easily bend — *she'eino nichpaf* — which gives the appearance of an *ohel* and is therefore forbidden. Loosely (*lo mahedik*) refers to a flexible brim that bends easily — *she'hu nichpaf* — which does not look like an *ohel* and is therefore permitted. According to this reading, the prohibition is appearance-based: it is not a true *ohel* but *looks* like one, and that is sufficient to forbid it. This position is cited in the *Shulchan Aruch*. In practice, hats are not treated as problematic, which follows Rashi's approach; but the discussion around stiff-brimmed hats based on Rabbeinu Chananel's reading remains a genuine consideration in the sources.

## Rami's Letter

### Two Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל לְרַב הוּנָא: אֵימָא לַן אִיזִי הָנָךְ מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא דַּאֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב, תַּרְתֵּי בְּשַׁבָּת וַחֲדָא בְּתוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Yeḥezkel sent to Rav Huna: Say to us, please, those excellent statements that you said to us in the name** of **Rav, two with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat, and one with regard to the Torah.**

The Gemara now relates that Rami bar Yechezkel sent a letter to Rav Huna asking him to transmit two particular teachings that Rav Huna had previously taught in the name of Rav — *chad beShabbat v'chad beTorah* — one pertaining to the laws of *Shabbat* and one relating to Torah. The request was not exploratory but confirmatory: Rami bar Yechezkel already knew the substance of these teachings and wished them formally recorded, having sent a written inquiry to elicit a written reply.

### God by Two People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: הָא דְּתַנְיָא גּוֹד בְּכִיסָנָא מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת — אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם, אֲבָל בְּאָדָם אֶחָד — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **sent to him** in response: With regard to **that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **It is permitted to spread a large wineskin** and suspend it **by its straps on Shabbat, Rav said: They only taught** that it is permitted if it is performed **by two people** together. They do not stretch the wineskin like a tent; rather, they place it without stretching it. **However, it is prohibited for one person** to do so by himself, due to the concern that he may establish a tent of sorts.

The first teaching relates to the *god* — the leather flask discussed earlier, which when stretched over poles can create a rabbinic concern of *ohel*. The Baraita states: *god bekisna mutar linototo beShabbat* — a *god* held by its straps may be spread on *Shabbat*. Rav qualifies: *lo shanu ela be'bei terei bnei adam* — this was only said when two people are spreading it. *Aval be'adam echad assur* — if one person alone spreads it, it is forbidden. Rashi, citing the *Geonim*, explains the mechanical difference: when one person must spread the leather flask alone, he must tie one end firmly to a post and then stretch it taut to secure the other end, resulting in a tightly-stretched covering that more closely resembles an *ohel*. When two people work together, each holding one end, the flask can be suspended more loosely, and it does not carry the same appearance of *ohel* construction.

### Kila Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְכִילָּה אֲפִילּוּ בַּעֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם אָסוּר. אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא מִימַּתְחָא פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: And it is prohibited** to spread **a canopy** on Shabbat **even with ten people.** The reason for this is that **it is impossible that it will not be stretched a bit** for a certain period of time, which would establish a temporary tent.

Abaye adds: *u'vekila afilu bei assura bnei adam assur* — and with a *kila* — a true canopy structure, one actually made to function as a roof — even ten people working together cannot permit it, because *i efshar delo mimtacha purta* — it is impossible to spread it without it becoming stretched at least a little. The significance of stretching, as opposed to mere placement, is that a tautly stretched covering has the character of a genuine *ohel*, and no number of people can avoid imparting that quality to a true canopy.

### Oven Leg Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִידַּךְ מַאי הִיא דְּתַנְיָא: כִּירָה שֶׁנִּשְׁמְטָה אַחַת מִיַּרְכוֹתֶיהָ — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ. שְׁתַּיִם — אָסוּר. רַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חַד נָמֵי אָסוּר, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִתְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** *halakha* with regard to Shabbat, **what** is it? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a stove, one of whose legs fell, it is permitted to move it** on Shabbat. Since it remains a vessel, it may be moved if it is taking up a space that is needed. However, if **two** of its legs fell, **it is prohibited,** since it is then a broken vessel. **Rav said: Even** if only **one** leg fell, **it is also prohibited** to handle it, due to **a decree lest one fasten** the leg in place forcefully and be liable for preparing a vessel for use.

The second *Shabbat* teaching involves an oven. *Kira she'nishmeta achat miyarkoteiha* — if a tripod oven has one of its legs slip out of place, *mutar letaltela* — one may still move it on *Shabbat*, since it remains functional and is not *muktzeh*. *Shtayim assur* — if two legs have slipped, it may not be moved, presumably either due to *muktzeh* or due to concern that one will come to fix it.

### Rava: One Leg

Rava takes a more stringent position: *afilu chad nami assur* — even if only a single leg has slipped, the oven may not be moved. His reasoning: *gezeira shema yigsof* — there is a rabbinic decree out of concern that if one picks it up to move it, one will be tempted to push the leg back into place and fix it, which would constitute either *boneh* or *makkeh b'patish*. The *muktzeh* question is not the operative concern for Rava; the concern is the act of repair that might follow from handling it.

## Torah Forgetting

### Rav's Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרָה — דְּאָמַר רַב: עֲתִידָה תּוֹרָה שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּכַּח מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִפְלָא ה׳ אֶת מַכֹּתְךָ״, הַפְלָאָה זוֹ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהִי. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לָכֵן הִנְנִי יוֹסִיף לְהַפְלִיא אֶת הָעָם הַזֶּה הַפְלֵא וָפֶלֶא״ — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: הַפְלָאָה זוֹ תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Torah,** Rav Huna related **that Rav said: The Torah is destined to be forgotten from the Jewish people. It is stated** at the conclusion of the curses in the Torah's reproof: **"And the Lord will make your plagues astonishing,** and the plagues of your seed, great plagues of long continuance, and evil diseases of long continuance" (Deuteronomy 28:59). **This** term of **astonishment,** mentioned in the verse in addition to the explicit punishments, **I do not know what it is.** But **when** the verse **states** elsewhere: **"Therefore, behold, I will continue to astonish this people with wondrous astonishment,** and the wisdom of its wise will be lost, and the understanding of its men of understanding shall be hidden" (Isaiah 29:14), you must **say: This astonishment** is referring to forgetting the **Torah.**

The Gemara now transitions to an extended *aggadic* teaching connected to the second item Rami bar Yechezkel requested from Rav Huna — a matter relating to Torah. Rav states: *atidah Torah she'tishtakach mi'Yisrael* — there will come a time when Torah will be forgotten from the Jewish people. The verse adduced is from the *tochacha*: *v'hifla Hashem et makotecha* — God will make your smitings wondrous. The word *hafla'ah* is ordinarily associated with wondrous or extraordinary things. Rav reads a second verse — *lachen hineni yosif l'hafli et ha'am hazeh haflei va'fela* — as identifying this *hafla'ah* specifically with Torah, since the verse concludes *v'avdah chochmat chachamav* — the wisdom of its sages will be lost. The wondrous punishment, in other words, is the disappearance of Torah wisdom.

### Kerem B'Yavne

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ רַבּוֹתֵינוּ לַכֶּרֶם בְּיַבְנֶה אָמְרוּ, עֲתִידָה תּוֹרָה שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּכַּח מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה יָמִים בָּאִים נְאֻם ה׳ אֱלֹהִים וְהִשְׁלַחְתִּי רָעָב בָּאָרֶץ לֹא רָעָב לַלֶּחֶם וְלֹא צָמָא לַמַּיִם כִּי אִם לִשְׁמוֹעַ אֵת דִּבְרֵי ה׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְנָעוּ מִיָּם עַד יָם וּמִצָּפוֹן וְעַד מִזְרָח יְשׁוֹטְטוּ לְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת דְּבַר ה׳ וְלֹא יִמְצָאוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a similar idea in the *Tosefta*: **When our Sages entered the vineyard in Yavne, they said: The Torah is destined to be forgotten from the Jewish people,** as **it is stated: "Behold, days are approaching, says the Lord God, and I will send forth a hunger in the land, not a hunger for bread and not a thirst for water, but for hearing the words of the Lord"** (Amos 8:11). **And it states: "And they will drift from sea to sea, and from north to east they will roam to find the word of the Lord, but they will not find it"** (Amos 8:12).

The Gemara cites a *Baraita*: when our rabbis entered the *kerem b'Yavne* — the rabbinic assembly at Yavne after the destruction of the Temple, called a vineyard because the scholars were arranged in rows — they said: *atidah Torah she'tishtakach mi'Yisrael* — Torah will be forgotten from Israel. The verse they cited is from Amos: *hinei yamim ba'im ne'um Hashem Elokim v'hishlachti ra'av ba'aretz, lo ra'av lalechem v'lo tzama lamayim, ki im lishmoa et divrei Hashem* — a hunger will come, not for bread or water, but to hear the word of God. And further: *v'na'u mi'yam ad yam... levakesh et devar Hashem v'lo yimtza'u* — they will wander seeking the word of God and will not find it.

This fear of Torah's loss was not abstract for the scholars at Yavne; it was the lived reality of the post-*Churban* generation. Their urgent effort to gather testimony — the process that produced *Massechet Eduyot* and ultimately led toward the crystallization of the Mishnah — was motivated precisely by this concern. The fear that *Torah she'ba'al peh* would be lost drove the consolidation of traditions, even at the cost of writing down what was traditionally transmitted orally.

### What is Lost?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דְּבַר ה׳״ — זוֹ הֲלָכָה, ״דְּבַר ה׳״ — זֶה הַקֵּץ, ״דְּבַר ה׳״ — זוֹ נְבוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** "The word of the Lord" in this context bears many meanings. **"The word of the Lord"; that is *halakha*. "The word of the Lord"; that is the end** of days. **"The word of the Lord"; that is prophecy.** All these will be lost from the Jewish people.

The Gemara identifies what *devar Hashem* means in this verse. Three interpretations are offered: *halacha*, knowledge of the messianic end-time, or prophecy. The most striking of these is the first — that what will be lost is *halacha* itself — though the latter two, the end of prophetic guidance and loss of knowledge of redemption, are similarly sobering.

### Woman's Quest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״יְשׁוֹטְטוּ לְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת דְּבַר ה׳״? אָמְרוּ: עֲתִידָה אִשָּׁה שֶׁתִּטּוֹל כִּכָּר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה וְתַחֲזוֹר בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת לֵידַע אִם טְמֵאָה הִיא וְאִם טְהוֹרָה הִיא, וְאֵין מֵבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** is the meaning of: **"They will roam to find the word of the Lord,** but they will not find it"? **They said: It is destined that a woman will take a loaf of *teruma*** bread **and circulate among the synagogues and study halls to ascertain whether it is ritually impure or whether it is ritually pure, and there** will be **none** who **understands.**

The Gemara explains what the verse's description of wandering means in practice. The image offered: *atidah ishah she'titol kikar shel terumah v'tachzor b'vatei knesiyot u'vatei midrashot* — a woman will take a loaf of *terumah* and wander from synagogue to synagogue and from study hall to study hall, *leda'at im tamei hi v'im tahor hi* — seeking to know whether it is pure or impure. *V'ein mevin* — and no one will be able to tell her. This is the image of wandering to find *devar Hashem* and not finding it.

### Gemara Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם טְהוֹרָה הִיא וְאִם טְמֵאָה הִיא בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״מִכׇּל הָאוֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״! אֶלָּא: לֵידַע אִם רִאשׁוֹנָה הִיא וְאִם שְׁנִיָּה הִיא, וְאֵין מֵבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How is it possible that they will be unable to understand **whether** the loaf **is ritually pure or whether it is ritually impure? It is explicitly written** in the Torah **with regard to this: "All food that is eaten** upon which water falls shall contract impurity, and all liquid drunk in any vessel shall contract impurity" (Leviticus 11:34). There can be no doubt as to the question of whether or not the loaf can become impure. **Rather,** the Gemara explains: The woman seeks **to ascertain whether it** assumes **first-**degree ritual impurity status **or whether it** assumes second-degree ritual impurity status; **and there** will be **none** who **understands.**

The Gemara presses back sharply: *behedya ketiv bei* — but the answer is written explicitly in the Torah! The verse *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel* explicitly addresses the transmission of *tumah* to food. How could something so plainly stated in Scripture be forgotten? The implication of the Gemara's challenge is that the scenario being described — total ignorance of whether something is *tamei* or *tahor* — is implausible given that the basic categories are written in the Torah itself.

### Tumah Mechanisms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין הִיא? כְּדִתְנַן: הַשֶּׁרֶץ שֶׁנִּמְצָא בַּתַּנּוּר — הַפַּת שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ שְׁנִיָּה, שֶׁהַתַּנּוּר תְּחִילָּה. מִסְתַּפְּקָא לְהוּ הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: לֶיחְזְיֵיהּ לְהַאי תַּנּוּרָא כְּמַאן דִּמְלֵי טוּמְאָה וְתִיהְוֵי פַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא אָמְרִינַן לֶיחְזְיֵיהּ לְהַאי תַּנּוּרָא כְּמַאן דִּמְלֵי טוּמְאָה. דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהוּ כׇּל הַכֵּלִים מִיטַּמְּאִין בַּאֲוִיר כְּלִי חֶרֶס, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר בְּתוֹכוֹ יִטְמָא״, ״מִכׇּל הָאוֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״ — אוֹכָלִין מִטַּמְּאִין בַּאֲוִיר כְּלִי חֶרֶס, וְאֵין כֵּלִים מִטַּמְּאִין בַּאֲוִיר כְּלִי חֶרֶס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **That too is an** explicit **mishna,** and how is it that none will know an explicit mishna? **As we learned** in a mishna: If **the** carcass of a **creeping animal was found in** the airspace of **an oven, the bread inside it** assumes **second-**degree ritual impurity status, **as the** creeping animal, which is a primary source of impurity, renders the **oven** impure with **first-**degree ritual impurity. The oven then renders the bread impure with second-degree ritual impurity. **They are uncertain** with regard to **that which Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava: Let us view this oven as one filled with impurity, and** the **bread will** then assume **first-**degree ritual impurity status. In other words, the legal status of food in the airspace of an earthenware vessel that also has the carcass of a creeping animal in its airspace is that of food that came into contact with the creeping animal, even if the food does not come into contact with the carcass of a creeping animal. **He said to him** that **we do not say: Let us view the oven as one filled** with **ritual impurity, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** think that **all vessels should become ritually impure in the airspace of an earthenware vessel** that has the carcass of a creeping animal in its airspace; therefore, **the verse states:** "And any earthenware vessel in which any of them falls, **all that is in it shall be impure,** and you shall break it. **All food that is eaten,** upon which water comes, shall be impure; and all drink that may be drunk, in any vessel, shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:33–34). The *baraita* learns from the juxtaposition of these verses that **foods become ritually impure in the airspace of earthenware vessels, but vessels do not become ritually impure in the airspace of earthenware vessels.** Apparently, the airspace of an oven is not considered filled with the impurity of the carcass of a creeping animal. If that were the case, even vessels would become ritually impure.

The Gemara resolves the challenge by redefining what will be forgotten. The woman is not asking whether the loaf is *tamei* or *tahor* in a basic sense — she knows it is *tamei*. What she will not be able to determine is *im rishona hi v'im shniya hi* — whether it is a *rishon le'tumah* or a *sheni le'tumah*. The Gemara immediately challenges again: but this is explicitly taught in a *Mishnah*! The Mishnah in *Avot ha-Tumot* states: *ha-sheretz she'nimtza ba-tanur, ha-pat she'betocho shniya* — if a dead rodent is found in a pottery oven, the bread inside the oven is a *sheni le'tumah*, because the rodent (*av ha-tumah*) renders the oven a *rishon*, and the oven renders the bread a *sheni*. This is standard Mishnaic content; how could it be forgotten?

The Gemara answers with the question that Rav Adda bar Ahava once posed to Rava: why is the bread a *sheni* and not a *rishon*? The reasoning that treats the bread as a *sheni* depends on understanding that *tumah* travels from the rodent to the oven, and from the oven to the bread — two steps. One could mount a logical argument that the rodent, being in the same enclosed space as the bread, should directly render the bread *tamei* as if the entire vessel were filled with *tumah*, making the bread a *rishon*. What will be forgotten is the answer to this question: the understanding that *ochlin mitamin be'avir kli cheres, v'ein kelim mitamin be'avir kli cheres* — food becomes *tamei* through the airspace of a pottery vessel, but other vessels do not. This rule — derived from the verse's specification that food and not vessels becomes *tamei* through the airspace — is what establishes that the rodent does not directly render the bread *tamei*, but does so through the oven as an intermediary. And the proof that the bread is only a *sheni* — not a *rishon* — is that if the rodent directly transmitted *tumah* to the bread, the bread would be a *rishon*; the fact that the verse implies only food (and not other vessels) becomes *tamei* in the airspace shows that the *tumah* travels through the oven, making the bread a *sheni*. It is this level of reasoning — the mechanism by which *tumah* transmits through a *kli cheres* — that will be forgotten. Not the basic facts, but the internal logic that resolves apparent contradictions.

### R. Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּכַּח תּוֹרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא תִשָּׁכַח מִפִּי זַרְעוֹ״. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״יְשׁוֹטְטוּ לְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת דְּבַר ה׳ וְלֹא יִמְצָאוּ״? שֶׁלֹּא יִמְצְאוּ הֲלָכָה בְּרוּרָה וּמִשְׁנָה בְּרוּרָה בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** An opposing view **was taught** in another *baraita*. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: Heaven forfend that the Torah should be forgotten from the Jewish people, as it is stated:** "And this song shall answer to him as a witness, **for it shall not be forgotten from his seed"** (Deuteronomy 31:21). **Rather, how do I explain: "They will roam to find the word of God, but they will not find it"?** It means **that they will not find clear *halakha* and clear teaching together,** but rather there will be disputes among the Sages.

The Gemara closes this passage with a dissenting *Baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai says: *chas v'shalom she'tishtakach Torah mi'Yisrael* — God forbid that Torah should ever be forgotten from Israel, as the verse states *ki lo tishkach mi'pi zaro* — it will never be forgotten from his descendants. How then does Rabbi Shimon understand the verse about wandering and not finding *devar Hashem*? *Shelo yimtza'u halacha berurah u'mishnah berurah b'makom echad* — what will not be found is a clear *halacha* and a clear ruling in any single place. Torah will not disappear, but clarity will. Every matter will be disputed; nothing will be settled; scholars will disagree on every detail. The *Mishnah Berurah* famously takes its title from precisely this phrase — *mishnah berurah* — clear teaching. This, Rabbi Shimon holds, is what the assembly at *kerem b'Yavne* feared and why they worked so urgently to consolidate testimony: not that Torah itself would vanish, but that the clarity and unity of its transmission would be lost, leaving an endless proliferation of dispute with no resolved and authoritative ruling in any one place.
