# Niddah 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam Betulim

### Mishnah to Amoraic shift

The present daf opens with two final observations on the topic of *dam betulim* before the Gemara moves forward. As noted in the preceding discussion, the Mishnah treated *dam betulim* as a basis for leniency, but beginning in the period of Rav and Shmuel a stricter approach began to take shape — one that was not yet fully consolidated in the Amoraic era but became solidified in the post-Geonic period — treating *dam betulim* uniformly as *dam niddah*.

### Geonic practice

An early Geonic work, the *Chilukim Bein Bnei Mizrach Livnei Ma'arav* — a text recording differences in practice between the communities of Eretz Yisrael and Bavel, paralleling in some respects debates between the Bavli and the Yerushalmi — already records a practice of women manually rupturing the hymen before the wedding night. This is noteworthy in light of other Gemarot that discuss the first act of intercourse.

### Tosafos on Bias Mitzvah

The question of why the Mishnah refers to the first act of intercourse as *bias mitzvah* has been raised several times in this masechta. Tosafos — specifically in Kesubot, not in this daf — explains that this act is one through which a woman's body undergoes a profound physical transformation in her connection to her husband, and through which she becomes, in a physical sense, a fully sexual being. It should be noted, parenthetically, that this designation does not require the couple to consummate the marriage on the wedding night itself; sometimes the couple is simply not ready, and the term refers to the first act of intercourse whenever it occurs.

### Modern medical solutions

Against this backdrop, the historical practice of manual rupture before the wedding night finds a modern analogue: some women today opt to have the hymen surgically removed by a gynecologist precisely in order to avoid the halachic challenges posed by *dam betulim*, chief among them the requirement that the couple separate immediately after their first act of intercourse. If the hymen has been surgically removed, or if the woman is not a *besulah* and does not have an intact hymen, these *halachos* do not apply.

### Full penetration standard

One further clarification is essential. The later *Rishonim* and certainly the *Shulchan Aruch* and *Poskim* establish that even in the absence of blood, if there has been full penetration (*gmar biah*), the woman must be treated as if there was blood and as if it was uterine blood. However, this rule applies specifically when full penetration has been achieved. In cases where a couple — particularly an inexperienced couple on their wedding night — is genuinely uncertain whether full penetration occurred, and there is no blood, they need not be concerned. Blood triggers the obligation; full penetration triggers the obligation even without blood. But uncertainty about whether penetration was completed, combined with the absence of blood, does not require any further action.

## Tanu Rabbanan bleeding

### Women categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁהָיוּ שׁוֹפְעוֹת דָּם וּבָאוֹת, מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת לְאַחַר אַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת, מִתּוֹךְ הַלַּיְלָה לְאַחַר הַלַּיְלָה — כּוּלָּן צְרִיכוֹת לִבְדּוֹק אֶת עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **And** with regard to **all of them,** i.e., all the women who are given four nights, **if they were discharging blood continually, from four nights** until **after** those **four nights,** or in the case of those women who are given only one night, if they discharge blood **from that** one **night** until **after that night,** they may not attribute the blood to the torn hymen. Rather, **all of them must examine themselves,** in the manner that will be explained.

The Gemara now presents a *Tana Rabbanan* passage that addresses several categories of women who experience consecutive bleeding and the checking requirements that apply to them. The baseline scenario concerns women who bleed from one four-night period to after that period, or from one night to after that night — the variation depending on the woman's age and status as previously established. Once the bleeding extends beyond the permitted window, all these women are required to check themselves: they cannot simply assume that blood appearing after the designated period is still hymenal bleeding without actively verifying it.

### Rabbi Meir stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכוּלָּן, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַחְמִיר כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to all of** these women, **Rabbi Meir is stringent in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai,** and therefore a minor girl who bleeds for more than four nights must examine herself, despite the fact that Beit Hillel say she remains ritually pure until the wound has healed. Similarly, Rabbi Meir holds that with regard to a young girl who has reached puberty, if she continues to bleed after the first night she must examine herself.

With respect to all these categories, Rabbi Meir is strict like Beit Shammai. Whereas Beit Hillel grants a four-night window in certain cases, and Beit Shammai grants only one night or the first act of intercourse, Rabbi Meir follows Beit Shammai's more limited allowance. Yet here is a striking feature of Rabbi Meir's position: even though he is strict like Beit Shammai regarding the time window, he introduces a leniency of his own based on the *appearance* of the blood. Beit Shammai himself, having given a narrow window, would not extend leniency after that window even if the subsequent blood resembled the blood from within the window — except in a case of continuous flow. Rabbi Meir, however, despite adopting Beit Shammai's strictness about the time window, would permit a woman to rely on the continuity of appearance: if the blood seen after the window looks the same as the blood seen during the permitted period, that similarity is a basis for leniency beyond what Beit Shammai would allow.

### Blood distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מַרְאֵה דָמִים מְשׁוּנִּים הֵן זֶה מִזֶּה. כֵּיצַד? דַּם נִדָּה — אָדוֹם, דַּם בְּתוּלִים — אֵינוֹ אָדוֹם. דַּם נִדָּה — זִיהוּם, דַּם בְּתוּלִים — אֵינוֹ זִיהוּם. דַּם נִדָּה — בָּא מִן הַמָּקוֹר, דַּם בְּתוּלִים — בָּא מִן הַצְּדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Meir would say: The appearances of** impure and pure **blood differ from one another. How so?** The **blood of a menstruating woman is red,** whereas **blood** that comes from a torn hymen, indicating that she was **a virgin, is not red.** The **blood of a menstruating woman is cloudy; blood** that indicates that she was **a virgin is not cloudy.** Finally, the **blood of a menstruating woman comes from the uterus; blood** that indicates that she was **a virgin comes from the sides** of the vaginal wall.

Rabbi Meir supports this position with a substantive observation about the distinct characteristics of different blood types. He states: *dam niddah adom, dam betulim eino adom* — menstrual blood is red, hymenal blood is not red in the same way; the latter is brighter and more purely sanguinous, while the former, being the discharge of the uterine lining, contains other matter and therefore has a distinctly different hue. Similarly, *dam niddah zehum, dam betulim eino zehum* — menstrual blood has an odor, hymenal blood does not, again for comparable reasons. And most significantly: *dam niddah ba min hamakor, dam betulim ba min hatzdadim* — menstrual blood comes from the uterus, while hymenal blood comes literally from the sides, meaning from the vaginal canal at the site of the hymen, not from the uterus itself. These distinctions establish that blood appearance, odor, and anatomical source are all meaningful evidentiary factors in assessing whether a woman is dealing with hymenal or uterine bleeding.

### Appearance debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים כׇּל מַרְאֵה דָמִים אַחַת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Rabbi Yosei says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the statement of Rabbi Meir.** According to Rabbi Meir, it is possible to examine the color of blood to determine whether it is menstrual blood or hymenal blood. **But the Rabbis say** that **all appearances of blood are one,** i.e., there is no distinction between the appearance of menstrual blood and blood indicating one's loss of virginity.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Rav Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan confirms that the position just described — namely, that one may assess and distinguish between blood types — is the view of Rabbi Meir alone: *zu divrei Rabbi Meir*. The Sages, however, disagree: *kol mareh damim achat hen* — all appearances of blood are treated as one and the same. Whether this means that the Sages literally believed the bloods are indistinguishable, or more likely that they ruled one may not rely on such distinctions in practice, the effect is the same: a move away from case-by-case empirical assessment toward more formal, quantifiable criteria such as the woman's age and the number of nights, rather than a color-by-color evaluation. This halachic tendency — trading granular accuracy for clearly defined categories — is a recognizable feature of legal development, and it is this formalistic approach of the Sages that ultimately prevails.

## Bleeding from intercourse

### First husband protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאָה דָּם מֵחֲמַת תַּשְׁמִישׁ — מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה, וּשְׁנִיָּה, וּשְׁלִישִׁית, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — לֹא תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ עַד שֶׁתִּתְגָּרֵשׁ
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: A woman **who sees blood due to sexual intercourse may engage in intercourse** before the **first time** this occurs, before the **second** time this occurs, **and** before the **third** time this occurs. **From this** point **forward,** i.e., after three consecutive occurrences of bleeding due to intercourse, **she may not engage in intercourse until she is divorced** from her husband

The Gemara now transitions to a related but distinct topic of central importance in *hilchos niddah*: *haroeh dam machamat tashmish*, a woman who bleeds as a result of intercourse. This is not a question about the first act of intercourse and hymenal bleeding; the situation is one occurring in the middle of a marriage, where the act of intercourse itself appears to be causing bleeding. The concern is that the intercourse may be inducing actual *niddah* bleeding rather than bleeding from a wound or irritation, and if that is the case, the couple faces a severe ongoing problem.

The Gemara's initial ruling is that she may have intercourse up to three times. The logic is that of *chazakah*: until a pattern has been established three times, one does not need to assume that the bleeding reflects a recurring problem. After the third occurrence, however, she may no longer continue with this husband. The ruling is stark: *mikan va'eilach lo tishamesh ad shetitgaresh* — from that point on she may not have intercourse until she receives a divorce.

### Second husband pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִנָּשֵׂא לְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **and is married to another** man. She is permitted to engage in intercourse with her second husband because it is possible that the bleeding was caused by engaging in intercourse with her first husband, and the issue will not reoccur when she engages in intercourse with a different man.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיסֵּת לְאַחֵר, וְרָאֲתָה דָּם מֵחֲמַת תַּשְׁמִישׁ — מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לֹא תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ עַד שֶׁתִּתְגָּרֵשׁ וְתִנָּשֵׂא לְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If **she married another** man **and** again **saw blood due to sexual intercourse, she may engage in intercourse** before the **first time** this occurs, before the **second** time this occurs, **and** before the **third** time this occurs. **From this** point **forward she may not engage in intercourse until she is divorced** from her second husband **and is married to** yet **another** man.

Once divorced from the first husband, she may marry someone else. With the second husband, the same protocol applies: she may have intercourse three times, and only after a third occurrence of bleeding must she separate and divorce. The reasoning here is that the problem may be specific to the particular husband — anatomy varies, and what causes this kind of irritation or induced bleeding with one man may not occur with another.

### Third husband universal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיסֵּת לְאַחֵר, וְרָאֲתָה דָּם מֵחֲמַת תַּשְׁמִישׁ — מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לֹא תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ עַד שֶׁתִּבְדּוֹק עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **she married another** man **and** again **saw blood due to sexual intercourse, she may engage in intercourse** the **first time** this occurs, the **second** time this occurs, **and** the **third** time this occurs. **From this** point **forward** there is a presumption that she always bleeds due to engaging in intercourse, and therefore **she may not engage in intercourse** or marry someone else **until she examines herself.**

When she marries a third husband and the same pattern repeats itself, the situation has now changed fundamentally. Three different men, three separate patterns — the problem can no longer be attributed to any individual husband's anatomy. It must now be assumed to lie with the woman herself. At this point she cannot simply divorce again and try a fourth time; she must instead establish through examination that the bleeding is not uterine in origin. This is what the Gemara means when it says *mikan va'eilach lo tishamesh ad shetivdok atzmah* — she may not have intercourse until she has checked herself.

### Shoferet test method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד בּוֹדֶקֶת אֶת עַצְמָהּ? מְבִיאָה שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת, וּבְתוֹכָהּ מִכְחוֹל, וּמוֹךְ מוּנָּח עַל רֹאשׁוֹ. אִם נִמְצָא דָּם עַל רֹאשׁ הַמּוֹךְ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמִּן הַמָּקוֹר הוּא בָּא. לֹא נִמְצָא דָּם עַל רֹאשׁוֹ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמִּן הַצְּדָדִין הוּא בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does **she examine herself? She brings a tube, inside of which** she places a cosmetic **brush** so that it is long enough to reach deeply into her vagina, **and** an absorbent **cloth is placed on the tip** of the brush. She inserts the tube with the brush and cloth within herself and then removes it. **If blood is found on the top of the** absorbent **cloth, it is known that** the blood **comes from the uterus** and she is ritually impure. If **blood is not found on the top** of the cloth, **it is known that** the blood **comes from the sides** of the vaginal walls and she is ritually pure, and she may resume engaging in intercourse with her husband.

The checking procedure the Gemara prescribes is this: she brings a *shoferet* — a tube — and inside it is a *michol*, a stick or applicator, with a pad (*moch*) placed on the head of the applicator within the tube. The tube is inserted internally, and the applicator is passed through it until the pad comes into contact with the cervix. The pad does not touch the vaginal walls at all; it goes directly to the cervix. If blood is found on the head of the pad, it is established that the bleeding is coming from the uterus — *beyadu'a shemin hamakor hu*. If no blood is found on the pad, the bleeding is coming from the sides, from the vaginal walls — *beyadu'a shemin hatzdadim hu* — and is not uterine blood and therefore not a halachic problem.

### Test limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת — (אַפְגּוֹרֵי מַפְגְּרָא) [אַפְסוֹדֵי מַפְסְדָא] לַהּ! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת שֶׁל אֲבָר, וּפִיהָ רָצוּף לְתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *baraita* that states that the test for a woman who experiences bleeding due to sexual intercourse is to insert **a tube,** the Gemara asks: But won't a tube **scratch her** and cause her to bleed regardless? **Shmuel said:** The *baraita* is referring **to a tube of lead, and the mouth,** i.e., the end that is inserted, **is folded inward** so that it will not scratch her.

Even granting the test its evidentiary role, several important questions arise. First, the test does not replicate the conditions that produce the bleeding: intercourse was the trigger, and the tube procedure is not intercourse. The absence of blood on the pad does not straightforwardly prove that no uterine bleeding would occur during intercourse. Second, a question arises about how frequently the woman must perform this test. The plain reading suggests it is done once, but Rashi and Tosafos, following a development in the Gemara's discussion, indicate she may need to perform it regularly — perhaps before each act of intercourse. These are real evidentiary and practical limitations, though the Gemara continues to engage with the test as a meaningful diagnostic tool.

## Toleh B'Maka framework

### Uterine wounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם יֵשׁ לָהּ מַכָּה בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם — תּוֹלָה בְּמַכָּתָהּ, וְאִם יֵשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת — תּוֹלָה בְּוִסְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if she has a wound in that place,** i.e., her vagina, **she attributes** the blood **to her wound,** and she is ritually pure, as it is assumed to not be uterine blood. **And if she has a** fixed menstrual **cycle,** i.e., she does not bleed every time she engages in intercourse with her husband, but only at fixed times, **she attributes** the blood **to her** fixed menstrual **cycle,** and she is permitted to engage in intercourse at other times.

Beyond the tube test, the Gemara introduces another and in many respects more significant basis for leniency: *toleh b'maka* — attributing the bleeding to a wound. The text states: *u-ma im yesh la maka b'oto makom, toleh b'makatah* — if she has a wound in that location, she may attribute the bleeding to the wound. The implication is that even if blood appears to be coming from the uterus, it need not be menstrual blood; it could be blood from an actual wound located in the uterus itself, which does sometimes occur. This is a principle of broad application throughout *hilchos niddah*, not only in this specific case of bleeding during intercourse: whenever it can be reasonably established that a woman's bleeding originates from a wound — whether in the vaginal canal or in the uterus — that is a basis to rule that she is not a *niddah*. The bleeding is *maka* blood, wound blood, not *dam niddah*.

The practical significance of *toleh b'maka* extends in two directions. First, it is a reason not to require divorce — the bleeding is wound-related and not a sign that intercourse is causing *niddah* bleeding. Second, and more fundamentally, it is a basis to rule that the woman is not a *niddah* at all in the first instance. Both conclusions depend on the quality of the evidence available, and the determination of whether bleeding qualifies as *maka* bleeding involves a constellation of factors: whether it is accompanied by pain, what color it is, what the woman reports about her own sensations, and whether her cycle suggests the bleeding is cyclical rather than wound-related. The practical approach today in cases of bleeding during intercourse is to bypass the three-husband progression and the *shoferet* test altogether and instead to consult a gynecologist directly, relying on modern medical assessment to establish whether the bleeding is wound-related — a direct application of the *toleh b'maka* framework through contemporary diagnostic tools.

It is also worth noting that *toleh b'maka* and the tube test are not mutually exclusive. Even if the tube test yields a positive result — indicating uterine origin — if the woman can additionally establish that she has a wound in the uterus, the bleeding may still be attributed to the wound. The wound blood is coming from the uterus, yes, but it is *maka* blood, not *dam niddah*.

### Veset and timing

Another mitigating factor is the *veset* — the woman's regular menstrual cycle. If the problematic bleeding during intercourse occurs only at specific points in her cycle, say on days near the end of the cycle when *niddah* onset would be expected, then it is possible to manage the situation without divorce: simply avoid intercourse on those particular days, and the fact that intercourse at other times causes bleeding can be tolerated, since the bleeding at those times can be attributed to the proximity of her *veset*. She can remain married; the couple works around the timing.

### Color analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה דַּם מַכָּתָהּ מְשׁוּנֶּה מִדַּם רְאִיָּיתָהּ — אֵינָהּ תּוֹלָה, וְנֶאֱמֶנֶת אִשָּׁה לוֹמַר ״מַכָּה יֵשׁ לִי בַּמָּקוֹר שֶׁמִּמֶּנָּה דָּם יוֹצֵא״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the blood of her wound differed from the blood that she sees** due to sexual intercourse, **she may not attribute** the blood to her wound. **And a woman is deemed credible to say: I have a wound in** my **uterus, from where** the **blood is emerging.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

Color of the blood is another relevant factor. If the blood that appears during intercourse looks different from the blood of the suspected wound — wound blood tends to be a brighter red, being pure blood rather than the discharge of the uterine lining — that discrepancy is disqualifying: *v'im hayah dam makatah meshuneh mi'dam re'iyatah, einah tolah* — if the wound blood's color differs from the blood of the bleeding episode, she cannot attribute the latter to the wound. Conversely, if the colors match, that is a point in favor of the *maka* explanation. The question of whether a woman is required to actively examine the color or only loses the leniency if she discovers a discrepancy is a separate discussion, but the color of the blood is clearly part of the evidentiary picture.

### Woman's testimony

Regarding the woman's own testimony: the Gemara rules *v'ne'emenet ishah lomar, maka yesh li b'makor she'mimenah dam yotze* — a woman is believed when she states that she has a wound in her uterus from which blood is emerging. This presumably reflects some subjective sensation — a particular cramping, localized pain, or other physical awareness. It is a striking ruling: on the basis of her report alone, without external medical verification, the Rabbis accept her testimony. This is the position attributed to *Rabbi* (Rebbi).

### Wound blood purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: דַּם מַכָּה הַבָּא מִן הַמָּקוֹר — טָמֵא, וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ הֵעִידוּ עַל דַּם הַמַּכָּה הַבָּא מִן הַמָּקוֹר שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Blood of a wound that comes from the uterus is ritually impure** as a primary category of impurity. Although this blood does not render it prohibited for her to engage in intercourse with her husband, it does render her impure with regard to eating ritually pure food. **But our Sages testified** that they had a tradition **with regard to** the **blood of a wound that comes from the uterus, that it is ritually pure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר עוּלָּא: מָקוֹר מְקוֹמוֹ טָמֵא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the opinion of those Sages and the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? **Ulla said:** The difference **between them is** whether the **place of** a woman's **uterus is impure,** which means that any blood that passes through there is impure, even if it is blood from a wound. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, even the blood of a wound becomes impure if it passes through the uterus, whereas those Sages hold that only blood that originates in the uterus is impure.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees, but not about the woman's status. His position is: *dam maka ba min hamakor tamei* — if wound blood comes from the uterus, the blood itself is *tamei*, even though it is clearly not *niddah* blood and the woman herself is not a *niddah*. The Rabbis counter: *dam maka ba min hamakor she'hu tahor* — even if it comes from the uterus, wound blood is *tahor*. The Gemara clarifies, via Ulla, that what Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis are debating is whether the uterus as a location has the capacity to render blood *tamei* even when that blood is not menstrual — *makor mekomo tamei ika beinaihu*. But this is a question in *hilchos tumah v'taharah* regarding the blood itself, not a question about the woman's status as a *niddah*. Even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel agrees the woman is not a *niddah*; only the blood, having passed through the uterus, may carry *tumah*. This distinction between the woman's personal status and the ritual status of the blood itself is the crux of the disagreement.

## Test critique analysis

### Shoferet abrasion issue

Resolved above in the *Test limitations* section, which included the blockquote covering Shmuel's answer about the lead tube with the folded-inward mouth.

### Timing of test question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְתִבְדּוֹק עַצְמָהּ בְּבִיאָה שְׁלִישִׁית שֶׁל בַּעַל הָרִאשׁוֹן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כׇּל הָאֶצְבָּעוֹת שָׁווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* says that if a woman experiences bleeding on three occasions due to intercourse with her husband he must divorce her. **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: But let her examine herself after the third** act of **intercourse with her first husband,** so that he need not divorce her. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** It is preferable for her not to test herself and risk becoming forbidden to all men and instead to be divorced and remarry another, **because not all fingers,** i.e., penises, **are equal.** Since it is possible that sexual intercourse with her second husband might not cause her to bleed, she should not risk becoming forbidden to all men by performing the examination.

Reish Lakish poses to Rabbi Yochanan the question everyone has been asking: *v'tivdok atzmah b'viah shlishit shel ba'al rishon* — why wait until the third husband? Why could she not have performed the tube test with the first husband already on the occasion of the third act of intercourse?

### Anatomical variation

Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *lefi she'ein kol etzba'ot shavot* — not all fingers are alike. Anatomical variation between men means that even after establishing a pattern with the first husband, the situation might resolve itself with the second husband simply because different anatomy produces different results. The woman therefore has a legitimate reason not to perform the test with the first husband. Rashi explains that if she wanted to, she could; but the problem is twofold. First, she would have to repeat the test before each act of intercourse — a significant burden. Second, and more critically: as other *Rishonim* explain, if the test yields a positive result, she could not marry anyone at all, since the test would establish definitively that the bleeding is uterine. She therefore reasonably prefers not to perform the test and instead allows the possibility of a second husband whose different anatomy may resolve the problem without the risk of a universally prohibitive result. If she does choose to perform the test with the first husband, she may. But the Gemara recognizes why she might not want to, and therefore the structure of trying a second husband without a test is halachically legitimate. In practice today, all of this is bypassed in favor of direct gynecological assessment to establish whether the bleeding is *maka* in origin.

## Case studies

### Rabbi's shock case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְאַבָּדָן: זִיל בַּעֲתַהּ. אֲזַל בַּעֲתַהּ, וְנָפַל מִמֶּנָּה חֲרָרַת דָּם. אָמַר רַבִּי: נִתְרַפְּאָה זֹאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** woman who experienced bleeding due to sexual intercourse **who came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and asked him what she should do. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** the Sage **Abdon,** who was present at the time: **Go** and suddenly **frighten** this woman. Abdon **went** and **frightened her, and a mass** of congealed **blood fell from her** vagina. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: This** woman is now **cured.** She will no longer experience bleeding due to sexual intercourse, as this mass of blood was the source of the blood.

The Gemara now presents several case narratives, each illustrating a different approach to the problem of women who bleed at intercourse or after immersion. A woman came before Rabbi with this problem. Rabbi said to his assistant Avdan: *zil ba'asah* — go startle her. Avdan went and startled her, *v'nafal minah k'charos dam* — and a small clot of blood fell from her. Rabbi declared: *nitrapa zo* — this woman has been healed. The assumption is that what had been causing her bleeding during intercourse was some kind of internal mass or accumulation — not menstrual bleeding at all — and that the shock, by causing an involuntary physical response, expelled the mass and resolved the problem.

### Shmuel's hopeless case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהִיא אִתְּתָא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב דִּימִי בַּר יוֹסֵף: זִיל בַּעֲתַהּ! אֲזַל בַּעֲתַהּ, וְלָא נְפַל (מִמֶּנָּה) [מִינַּהּ] וְלָא מִידֵּי. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ מְמַלְּאָה וְנוֹפֶצֶת הִיא, וְכׇל הַמְמַלְּאָה וְנוֹפֶצֶת — אֵין לָהּ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: There was **a certain** woman who experienced bleeding due to sexual intercourse **who came before Shmuel.** Shmuel **said to Rav Dimi bar Yosef: Go** and **frighten** this woman. Rav Dimi **went** and **frightened her, but nothing fell from her at all. Shmuel said: This** woman **is filled** with blood, **which falls** out of her during intercourse. **And any** woman who **is filled** with blood **that falls** out during intercourse **has no cure.**

A woman came before Shmuel with the same complaint. He instructed Rav Dimi bar Yosef to go and startle her. Rav Dimi did so — *v'lo nafal minah v'lo midi* — and nothing fell out. Shmuel's verdict: *zu memaleh v'nofetzes hi* — this is a woman who fills with blood and releases it. *V'chol hamemaleh v'nofetzes ein lah takanah* — for a woman of this type there is no remedy, because the bleeding is not based on an incidental mass but on an actual physiological pattern of uterine bleeding triggered by intercourse. The logic of the test here cuts in the opposite direction from Rabbi's case: no clot means no mass to expel, meaning no *maka* explanation, meaning the bleeding must be uterine. This is, as noted, a somewhat stark conclusion — the failure of one improvised test does not exhaustively rule out other explanations — but this is how Shmuel reads it.

### Yochanan evil eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּכֹל אֵימַת דַּהֲוָת סָלְקָא מִטְּבִילַת מִצְוָה, הֲוָת קָחָזְיָא דְּמָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: שֶׁמָּא דִּימַת עִירִיךְ עָלְתָה בִּיךְ, לְכִי וְהִבָּעֲלִי לוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַנָּהָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain** woman **who came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and told him **that every time she emerged from immersion** in a ritual bath, after completing the **mitzva** of purifying herself for her husband, **she would see blood** before she engaged in intercourse with him. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to her: Perhaps the gossip** of the women **in your city,** who are jealous of the love between you and your husband, **has reached you,** and this evil eye causes you to bleed before you engage in intercourse. **Go and** immerse in the river and **engage in intercourse with** your husband **on the bank of the river,** so that the other women will not see you leaving the ritual bath and gossip about you.

A woman came before Rabbi Yochanan with a different but related problem: every time she emerged from the mikveh after her immersion, she immediately saw blood, making it practically impossible for her to be with her husband. Rabbi Yochanan said to her: *shema divas ircha alsah bach* — perhaps the gossip of your city has come upon you, meaning people are casting an *ayin hara* upon you, jealous of your relationship, and that *ayin hara* is causing the bleeding every time you emerge from the *mikveh*. His remedy: *lechi v'heichave'i al gabei hanahar* — go and immerse in a river instead, and be with your husband near the river. If people cannot see that she has gone to the *mikveh*, no one will know to cast an *ayin hara*, and the problem will be resolved.

### Alternative remedies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמַר, אֲמַר לַהּ: תְּגַלִּי לְחַבְרוֹתִיךְ. כִּי הֵיכִי (דתהוו) [דְּתָוְוהָן] עֲלַיךְ לְהָךְ גִּיסָא, נִתְוְוהוּ עֲלַךְ לְהָךְ גִּיסָא. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמַר, אֲמַר לָהּ: גַּלִּי לְחַבְרוֹתִיךְ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִבְעוֹ עֲלַיךְ רַחֲמִים, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְטָמֵא טָמֵא יִקְרָא״ — צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ צַעֲרוֹ לָרַבִּים, וְרַבִּים מְבַקְּשִׁים עָלָיו רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to her: Reveal** this fact **to your friends, so that** those women **who were** against **you on this side,** and gossiped about you, **will be** with **you on that side,** and be kind to you. **And some say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to her: Reveal** this fact **to your friends, in order that they will pray for mercy for you, as it is taught** in a *baraita* discussing the verse: "And the leper in whom the mark is, his clothes shall be ripped and the hair of his head shall grow long and he will put a covering upon his upper lip **and will cry: Impure, impure"** (Leviticus 13:45). The leper publicizes the fact that he is ritually impure, as he **must announce his pain to the masses, and** then **the masses will pray for mercy on his** behalf.

The Gemara offers two alternative versions of Rabbi Yochanan's counsel. One version: *tzili v'chavrateych* — tell your friends about your situation. The logic, in this version, is that once her friends know she is suffering — that she is never able to be with her husband — they will feel compassion rather than jealousy, and the compassion will neutralize the *ayin hara*. Tosafos, in a notably more cynical vein, suggests a different mechanism for the same advice: tell your friends that you bleed heavily, because the Gemara elsewhere notes that women who bleed heavily tend to have many children. The friends will then become jealous of *that*, which will counteract the original jealousy — manipulating the *ayin hara* rather than eliminating it. A third version of Rabbi Yochanan's counsel: *gali l'chavrateych ki hichei d'libo alach rachamei* — tell your friends so that they will pray for you. This version invokes the principle derived from the *metzora*: *v'tamei tamei yikra* — the *metzora* is required to publicize his condition so that the public will pray on his behalf. Here too, publicizing one's suffering brings the community to intercede through prayer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וְאִתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: There was** a similar **incident in Pumbedita** of a woman who experienced bleeding immediately after immersing in a ritual bath, and she followed the advice given by Rabbi Yoḥanan **and she was cured.**

Rav Yosef adds that there was in fact a case in Pumbedita in which a woman was healed, presumably through one of these means.

What these case studies collectively illustrate is that the Gemara brings together multiple frameworks for addressing the problem of unexplained bleeding: medical and physical assessment (*maka* attribution, the clot expelled by shock), psycho-social intervention (managing the *ayin hara* by controlling who knows what and when), and spiritual remedy (*tefillah*).

## Rabbi's Takkanah

### Field backdrop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: הִתְקִין רַבִּי בְּשָׂדוֹת, רָאֲתָה יוֹם אֶחָד — תֵּשֵׁב שִׁשָּׁה וְהוּא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **decreed** that **in the fields,** i.e., in those distant places where there were no Torah scholars and whose residents were not well versed in the *halakhot* of menstruating women and did not know how to distinguish between the days of menstruation and the days of *ziva*, if **she saw** blood on **one day, she must sit** and count **six** days **and that** first day. She must observe six clean days without a discharge despite the possibility that she might have experienced bleeding only in her period of *ziva*, in which case she would be impure for only one day.

The Gemara now turns to what is perhaps the most consequential passage of the entire daf for contemporary *hilchos niddah*. Rav Yosef, citing Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, transmits: *hitkin Rebbi basadot* — Rebbi enacted a *takkanah* in the fields, or according to an alternate reading, in a place called Shavzit — a location identified in the tradition as a place where the women were not learned, not capable of carefully tracking the distinctions between *niddah* and *zavah* status. The backdrop is this: if a woman cannot reliably track when her bleeding began relative to her menstrual cycle, she may not be certain whether a given episode of bleeding makes her a *niddah* (whose blood is cyclical, after a period of days) or a *zavah* (whose blood is abnormal, appearing outside the normal cycle). Further complicating the assessment, the distinctions between blood colors that determine whether a given sighting is halachically significant can be difficult to apply without knowledge.

### One day rule

Rebbi's *takkanah* establishes a simplified waiting-period formula. If a woman bleeds for one day: *teshvu shiv'ah vehu* — she waits that day and six more, seven days total including the day of bleeding. This accounts for the worst-case scenario: the bleeding might have begun her *niddah* period, requiring seven days including the first.

### Two days rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם — תֵּשֵׁב שִׁשָּׁה, וְהֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה — תֵּשֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** If she experiences bleeding for **two** days, **she must sit** and count **six** days **and** both of **those** first two days, for a total of eight days, in case the first day on which she bled was the last day of *ziva*, while the next day was the first day of her menstruation period. If she experiences bleeding for **three** days **she must sit** and count **seven clean** days, as she might be a greater *zava*, who must count seven clean days.

If she bleeds for two days: *teshvu shiv'ah vehein* — those two days plus six more, eight days total. This might seem counterintuitive — why not two plus five, which would be seven from the first day of *niddah*? The answer is that there is a scenario in which the first day was *zavah* bleeding and the second day was the first day of *niddah*. If the second day begins the *niddah* count, she needs six clean days after it, making the total two plus six.

### Three days rule

If she bleeds for three days: *teshvu sheva neki'im* — she must count seven clean days. Three consecutive days of bleeding triggers the concern that she may be a *zavah gedolah*, whose requirement is seven clean days. Even if she might have thought those three days constitute *niddah* bleeding — which would require only four more days, not necessarily clean — she cannot know this with confidence, and so Rebbi requires the *sheva neki'im* of a *zavah gedolah*. This *takkanah* of Rebbi applies a maximum stringency at each level, but it is important to note that it may have been limited to the specific communities mentioned (*basadot*), and it never required *sheva neki'im* for fewer than three consecutive days of bleeding. That standard is about to change.

## Rabbi Zeira stringency

### One drop rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל הֶחֱמִירוּ עַל עַצְמָן, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ רוֹאוֹת טִפַּת דָּם כְּחַרְדָּל — יוֹשְׁבוֹת עָלֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related statement. **Rabbi Zeira says: Jewish women were stringent with themselves** to the extent **that even if they see a drop of blood** the size **of a mustard** seed, they **sit seven clean** days **for it.** By Torah law, a woman who experiences menstrual bleeding waits seven days in total before immersing, regardless of whether she experienced bleeding on those days. If she experiences bleeding during the eleven days when she is not expected to experience menstrual bleeding, she is a lesser *zava* and waits one day without bleeding and then immerses. The Jewish women accepted upon themselves the stringency that if they experience any bleeding whatsoever, they treat it as the blood of a greater *zava*, which obligates one to count seven clean days before immersing (see Leviticus 15:25).

Rabbi Zeira's statement marks the decisive turning point of this daf and, arguably, of all contemporary *hilchos niddah*. *Benot Yisrael himkiru al atzman she'afilu ro'ot tipat dam kachardal yoshevet aleha sheva neki'im* — the daughters of Israel have been strict upon themselves such that even if they see a drop of blood as small as a mustard seed — and some *Rishonim* add: *va'afilu pachot mikan*, even less than that — they sit and observe seven clean days.

### Logic problem

The logical problem with this practice is immediately apparent and worth stating plainly. There is no halachic scenario under which a single drop of blood mandates *sheva neki'im*. A *niddah* requires seven days, but they need not be clean; a *zavah* who sees blood only once requires only one clean day, not seven. Seven clean days is the requirement only of a *zavah gedolah*, who has seen blood on three consecutive days outside her *niddah* period. A single drop of blood, no matter how it is categorized, does not by itself obligate *sheva neki'im*. And that is why Rabbi Zeira specifies *kachardal* — the tiniest drop — because if it were a substantial flow, one might try to argue that perhaps it represents accumulated blood from three days inside the body that was not discharged until now. Even that argument is foreclosed by specifying the mustard-seed drop: there is no rational halachic basis for this practice.

### Justifications offered

The *Rishonim* nonetheless attempt to identify a scenario that gives the practice some halachic grounding. The scenario they identify is this: a woman who is already counting *sheva neki'im* after a *zavah gedolah* episode, and in the middle of those seven clean days she sees even one drop of blood — she must begin the seven clean days again from the start. In that case, a single drop does lead to *sheva neki'im*. The argument is that by treating every instance of blood as if it were this case, the *benot Yisrael* have adopted the most stringent possible application across the board. More straightforwardly, however, this appears to reflect what one might call the *chumra* approach — rather than navigating the complex distinctions between *niddah*, *zavah ketanah*, and *zavah gedolah*, apply the strictest law uniformly and eliminate the need for case-by-case assessment.

### Hybrid status

The internal logic of the practice can be described as the creation of a hybrid *niddah-zavah* status. The strictest aspect of *niddah* law is that even a single drop of blood renders a woman a *niddah* — there is no minimum quantity required. The strictest aspect of *zavah* law is the requirement of *sheva neki'im*. The practice of *benot Yisrael* combines these two strictures: take the *niddah* rule (one drop is enough) and apply the *zavah gedolah* consequence (*sheva neki'im*). Every woman who sees any blood at all thereby becomes, in practical terms, a hybrid — she is treated as *niddah* in terms of the trigger, and as *zavah gedolah* in terms of the remedy. This flattening of all distinctions into one uniform standard is precisely the *keep-it-simple* tendency that recurs in halachic development, particularly in Ashkenaz, though as we will see it originates here in the Gemara itself.

### Benot Yisrael reasons

The attribution of this stringency to *benot Yisrael* rather than to a rabbinic decree raises a significant question: what does it mean for women's own practice to establish halachic norms of this magnitude? Several possibilities are worth considering. One cynical reading is that a stringency is being imposed upon women and then attributed to them — the women are blamed for their own burden. A more sympathetic reading, along the lines suggested by some in the tradition, is that this reflects the one domain of the marriage relationship in which women hold unilateral authority: a woman's declaration of *tumah* is not subject to her husband's approval, and her insistence on the full seven clean days before resuming relations represents a form of agency within the sexual relationship. The observation has also been made that certain configurations of the five-day waiting period (before counting seven days) combined with the seven clean days could, depending on a woman's individual cycle length, create a window that regularly bypasses the period of peak fertility — leading some to speculate that an early form of birth control may have been one motivation at the grassroots level, though this was not a rabbinic calculation.

What is notable, and will be developed further in the next daf, is that the Gemara does not leave this as a purely voluntary *minhag* of *benot Yisrael*. The process by which this practice is transformed from a self-imposed stringency into a rabbinic *takkanah* with universal force is the story the Gemara continues to tell.

## Rava vs. Pappa

### Miscarriage case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְבְּרֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל, וְדָרֵשׁ: קִשְּׁתָה שְׁנֵי יָמִים, וְלַשְּׁלִישִׁי הִפִּילָה — תֵּשֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים. קָסָבַר: אֵין קָשָׁוֵי לִנְפָלִים, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava authorized Rav Shmuel, and he taught:** If a pregnant woman experienced **labor pains** for **two days, and on the third** day **she miscarried, she must sit** and count **seven clean** days. The Gemara explains that Rava **holds** that the principle that blood that emerges while the woman experiences **labor pains** is not ritually impure does **not** apply **to miscarriages. And** Rava further maintains that **it is impossible for the womb to open without blood** emerging. Therefore, when she miscarried she must have experienced a flow of blood, even if she did not notice it.

Rava sent Rav Shmuel to deliver a public *drasha* on the following ruling: if a woman experienced labor pains accompanied by bleeding for two days and miscarried on the third day, she must count *sheva neki'im*. The reasoning involves two principles. First, *ein kishul u-nefel*: the dispensation that treats labor pains as discounting *zavah* bleeding applies when a live birth follows; for a miscarriage, no such dispensation applies, and the two days of bleeding count fully as *zavah* days. Second, *i efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam*: the uterus cannot open for a miscarriage without blood, and therefore the third day of miscarriage must be assumed to have involved blood even if none was visibly observed. With three consecutive days of bleeding thus established, the woman is a *zavah gedolah* and requires *sheva neki'im*.

### Pappa's challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: מַאי אִרְיָא קִשְּׁתָה שְׁנֵי יָמִים? אֲפִילּוּ מַשֶּׁהוּ בְּעָלְמָא! דְּהָא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל הֶחֱמִירוּ עַל עַצְמָן, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ רוֹאוֹת טִפַּת דָּם כְּחַרְדָּל — יוֹשְׁבוֹת עָלֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava:** For **what** reason did you teach this *halakha* **specifically** with regard to a woman who experiences **labor pains** for **two days? Even** if she **merely** discharges **any amount** of blood she should be impure, **as Rabbi Zeira says: The Jewish women were stringent with themselves** to the extent **that even if they see a drop of blood of** the size of **a mustard** seed, that woman **sits seven clean** days **for it.** Since it is impossible for the womb to open without the emergence of blood, when this woman miscarried there must have been blood, and therefore she must count seven clean days.

Rav Pappa challenged Rava: *mai irya kashta shnei yamim* — why build this ruling on the specific case of two days of prior bleeding? Based on the teaching of Rabbi Zeira — *benot Yisrael himkiru al atzman she'afilu ro'ot tipat dam kachardal yoshevet aleha sheva neki'im* — even a single drop of blood during the miscarriage should trigger *sheva neki'im*. Why construct the elaborate three-day analysis at all?

### Rava's response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רבא: אָמֵינָא לָךְ אִיסּוּרָא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מִנְהָגָא?! הֵיכָא דְּאַחְמוּר — אַחְמוּר, הֵיכָא דְּלָא אַחְמוּר — לָא אַחְמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Pappa: **I speak to you** of **a prohibition,** i.e., that by Torah law a woman who experiences difficulty in childbirth for two days and on the third miscarries must count seven clean days as a greater *zava*, **and you speak to me** of **a custom,** a mere stringency. The stringency you mention does not apply in this case. **Where** the Jewish women **were stringent, they were stringent,** i.e., if they saw a drop of blood the size of a mustard seed. **Where they were not stringent,** i.e., in a case of blood due to labor, **they were not stringent.** By contrast, in the case I described the woman is obligated to count seven clean days by Torah law.

Rava responded sharply: *amina lach issura, at amrat minhaga* — I am speaking to you about real prohibition, actual *halakha*; you are quoting me a *minhag*. The teaching of Rabbi Zeira represents a custom — a practice that *benot Yisrael* took upon themselves — not established *halakha*. A *minhag* operates where it operates and does not displace halachic analysis. *Hecha d'achmir achmir, hecha d'lo achmir lo achmir* — where communities have adopted this stringency they have it, and where they have not, they do not. Rava refuses to allow the *minhag* of *benot Yisrael* to pre-empt the actual halachic discussion. Rav Pappa, by contrast, quotes Rabbi Zeira as settled law. This exchange is a snapshot of the process by which Rabbi Zeira's statement is in transit — moving from voluntary *minhag* toward universal *halakha* — and Rav Pappa is a key driver of that transition, as the next daf will make clear.

## Sudden chuppah case

### Rava statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תְּבָעוּהָ לִינָּשֵׂא וְנִתְפַּיְּיסָה — צְרִיכָה שֶׁתֵּשֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** With regard to **one who proposed marriage to** a woman **and she accepted it,** the emotional excitement might have caused her to have a flow of menstrual blood, which would render her ritually impure and prohibit her from engaging in intercourse. Even if she was unaware of any flow, she must consider the possibility that it occurred. Therefore, to purify herself **she must wait seven** consecutive days that are **clean** from any flow of menstrual blood and then immerse in a ritual bath. Only after that process may she marry.

Rava introduces a further extension of the *sheva neki'im* requirement: *tavuha linasei v'nispaysa* — if a man who has been betrothed (*kiddushin*) but has not yet begun living together with his wife suddenly announces they will begin married life together — *nisuin*, the *chuppah* — and the woman agrees, she must count *sheva neki'im* before they can consummate the marriage, even though no blood has been observed.

### Arousal spotting

The Gemara's assumption is that the sudden announcement produces sexual excitement in the woman, and that arousal may cause some spotting even without a visible flow. The speculation in the *Rishonim* is that this may reflect a transference or analogy from the male experience — sexual excitement can cause seminal emission — applied to a female physiology where excitement is assumed to produce some discharge or spotting. What is remarkable about this ruling, and what marks the trajectory of the entire daf, is that *sheva neki'im* is now required even in the *absence* of any observed blood at all. Rabbi Zeira required *sheva neki'im* for a mustard-seed drop of blood; Rava here requires it when there is no blood whatsoever, only a presumption of possible spotting from arousal. The *Rishonim* debate whether a prompt examination that yields nothing might address this, but the working assumption follows the direction of cumulative stringency. One sees how rapidly the standard has shifted from the nuanced, evidence-based approach of the Mishnah to a position in which even the theoretical possibility of a drop triggers the full *zavah gedolah* response.

## Ravina son case

### Four to seven days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אֶיעְסֹק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַב חֲנִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סָבַר לֵיהּ מָר לְמִכְתַּב כְּתוּבָה לְאַרְבָּעָה יוֹם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. כִּי מְטָא לְאַרְבָּעָה, נְטַר עַד אַרְבְּעָה אַחֲרִינָא, אִיעַכַּב שִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי בָּתַר הָהוּא יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related incident: **Ravina arranged for his son** to marry into **the family of Rav Ḥanina,** i.e., to marry Rav Ḥanina's daughter. Rav Ḥanina **said to** Ravina: **Does the Master hold** that it is appropriate **to write the marriage contract** as stating that the wedding will take place **in four days,** i.e., on Wednesday? Ravina **said: Yes. When** the **fourth** day, i.e., Wednesday **arrived, he waited until another fourth** day before marrying her, until the following Wednesday, i.e., **he delayed seven days after that day** when he had planned to marry her.

Ravina was arranging the marriage of his son to the daughter of Rabbi Chanina. He was asked whether they should write the *ketubbah* dated four days hence — meaning set the *chuppah* for four days from now. He agreed. When the fourth day arrived, however, he announced that an additional four days would be required before the *chuppah* — effectively extending the wait to seven days total from the original announcement.

### Minor distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: תְּבָעוּהָ לִינָּשֵׂא וְנִתְפַּיְּיסָה צְרִיכָה לֵישֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימַר דְּאָמַר רָבָא בִּגְדוֹלָה דְּקָחָזְיָא דְּמָא, אֲבָל בִּקְטַנָּה דְּלָא חַזְיָא דְּמָא — מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥanina **said to** Ravina: **What is this?** Why did you delay the wedding by an extra week? Ravina **said to** Rav Ḥanina: **Doesn't the Master hold in accordance with this** statement **of Rava, as Rava said:** With regard to **one who proposed marriage to** a woman **and she accepted it, she must wait seven** consecutive days that are **clean** from any flow of menstrual blood and then immerse in a ritual bath? Rav Ḥanina **said to** Ravina: One can **say that Rava said** this statement **with regard to an adult woman, who has seen** menstrual **blood. But did** Rava actually **say** this **with regard to a minor girl, who has not** yet **seen** menstrual **blood?**

His interlocutor was puzzled: the original plan was to have the *chuppah* in four days, and now there is a further delay. Ravina explained that he was applying Rava's ruling — *tavuha linasei v'nispaysa tzericha leishev sheva neki'im* — once the *chuppah* date was announced and the bride accepted it, she needed to count seven clean days. The response was: when would Rava's ruling apply? Only to a *gedolah d'chazya dama* — a woman who has reached puberty and experiences menstrual bleeding. In such a case, sexual excitement might trigger actual bleeding. But this bride is a *ketanah*, a minor who has not yet had her first period — surely Rava's ruling was never intended to apply to a girl who has not yet reached menarche.

### No minor exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּפֵירוּשׁ אָמַר רָבָא: לָא שְׁנָא גְּדוֹלָה, לָא שְׁנָא קְטַנָּה. גְּדוֹלָה טַעְמָא מַאי? מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵחֲמָדָא, קְטַנָּה נָמֵי מֵחֲמָדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to** Rav Ḥanina: **Rava said explicitly** that there is **no difference** whether she is **an adult woman** and **no difference** whether she is **a minor girl. What is the reason** that **an adult woman** must wait for seven days? She must wait **because she desires** to marry her husband, and this might cause her to have a flow of blood. **A minor girl also desires** to marry her husband, which could cause a flow of blood.

Ravina replied that Rava had explicitly stated: *lo shna gedolah v'lo shna ketanah* — it makes no difference whether the bride is an adult or a minor.

### Minor sexual desire

The Gemara then provides Rava's reasoning: *gedolah ta'ama mai? mishum d'michamda* — why does the ruling apply to an adult woman? Because she experiences sexual desire, and that desire may cause bleeding. *Ketanah nami michamda* — even a minor girl also experiences desire. This conclusion is striking and, to a contemporary reader, deeply troubling. Setting aside the already disturbing reality reflected throughout this Gemara that marriages were being consummated with girls who had not yet reached puberty, the Gemara here goes a step further: not only does it assume that such consummation is occurring, but it attributes sexual desire to these girls as the halachic basis for the ruling. This ties into the broader thread that Ravina has been raising throughout this masechta regarding the assumption embedded in these laws about the experience and interiority of very young brides. The Gemara will continue this discussion on the following daf.
