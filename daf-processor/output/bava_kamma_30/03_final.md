# Bava Kamma 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Negligence & Bor Ownership

### Picture Breaking Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹפֵךְ מַיִם בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקוֹ. הַמַּצְנִיעַ אֶת הַקּוֹץ וְאֶת הַזְּכוּכִית; וְהַגּוֹדֵר אֶת גְּדֵרוֹ בְּקוֹצִים; וְגָדֵר שֶׁנָּפַל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; וְהוּזְּקוּ בָּהֶן אֲחֵרִים – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who pours water in the public domain, and another** person **incurred damage due to it,** the one who poured water **is liable** to pay **for his damage.** In the case of **one who conceals a thorn or a piece of glass** in his wall adjacent to the public domain, **or one who puts up a fence of thorns, or** one who puts up **a fence that** subsequently **fell into the public domain, and others incurred damage due to** any of **these, he is liable** to pay **for their damage.**

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 29b, continuing the discussion of whether one is liable when a pitcher breaks and causes damage. The *mishnah* presents a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda: Rabbi Meir holds one is *chayav*, while Rabbi Yehuda exempts unless there was *kavanah*. The straightforward explanation of this debate is the question of *nitkal poshea* — whether tripping, or the object flipping out of one's hand, constitutes negligence. If *nitkal poshea*, one is considered at fault; if *nitkal ones*, one is not.

### Mafkir Nezikav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: מַפְקִיר נְזָקָיו פָּטוּר; וּמְצַמְצֵם מַאי טַעְמָא פָּטוּר – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהִתְחַכֵּךְ בַּכְּתָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, actually I** could **say to you** that in principle, **one who renounces ownership of his hazardous property is exempt.** Nevertheless, one who puts up a fence of thorns is liable, because he does not renounce ownership of the thorns that protrude into the public domain. **And** in a case where **he restricts** the thorns to his own property, **what is the reason** he is **exempt?** The reason is not that he is not liable to pay for the damage of a pit in his own property, but rather **because it was stated about** this case that **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** He is exempt **because it is not the** typical **manner of people to rub against walls,** but to keep a small distance from them. Therefore, if a pedestrian is injured by the thorns, it is considered an unusual accident, for which the owner of the fence is not liable.

The *gemara* also discusses the related question of *mafkir nezikav* — abandoning ownership of a hazard one has created. This issue could operate on one of two levels. The first, more sweeping level would be a claim that, technically, one is never *chayav* on a *bor* one does not own — which would produce the radical implication that if one digs a pit in *reshus harabbim* one is *patur* because one does not own it. The second, more immediately pressing level concerns the requirement that there be a moment of negligence in order to incur *chiyuv* for a *bor*.

### Moment of Negligence

Even if one was not negligent at the moment the object broke — and therefore may be exempt for that instant — one does not necessarily remain exempt indefinitely. If, in the aftermath of the breaking, the person had an opportunity to clean up the hazard and failed to do so, that failure itself becomes the act of negligence. Critically, however, this obligation to clean up arises only if one continues to own the object. If one was not at fault when it broke (*ones*), and then became *mafkir* the object so that it was no longer one's responsibility to clear it away, one remains *patur*. But if one retained ownership, that ongoing ownership generates a duty to clean it up, and the failure to do so supplies the requisite moment of negligence. This is a fundamental conceptual point: *bor* liability requires not just creating a hazard, but a traceable moment of culpable neglect.

### Yochanan/Elazar Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר חַיָּיב – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר פָּטוּר?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים אֵינָן בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, וַעֲשָׂאָן הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הֵן בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ; וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְחָמֵץ מִשֵּׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from** the fact **that Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that he is **liable,** is it evident that **Rabbi Elazar said** that he is **exempt? But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** There are **two entities** that **are not in a person's** legal **possession and** nevertheless **the verse rendered them as though they were in his possession** with regard to certain halakhic responsibilities. **And these are: A pit** that he dug **in the public domain and leavened bread** remaining in his possession on the eve of Passover **from six hours,** i.e., noon, **onward.**

The *gemara* now takes up the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Elazar on the basic question of whether ownership of the *bor* is a prerequisite for *chiyuv*. The prior discussion had established that Rav Elazar, citing his teacher (Rabbi Yishmael), held that two things one does not technically own nonetheless generate liability as if one did: *chametz* on Pesach, and a *bor bireshus harabbim*. Accordingly, it had been assumed that Rabbi Yochanan takes the opposing view — that *chiyuv* for *bor* requires actual ownership.

### Thorny Fences Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּמַפְרִיחַ, אֲבָל בִּמְצַמְצֵם – פָּטוּר. מְצַמְצֵם מַאי טַעְמָא פָּטוּר – לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוְיָא לֵיהּ בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to one who puts up a fence of thorns, **they taught** that he is liable **only in** a case where he **projects** the thorns out into the public domain, **but in** a case where he **restricts** them to his own property, he is **exempt.** The Gemara infers: In a case where he **restricts** them, **what is the reason** that he is **exempt? Is it not because it is** considered **a pit on his** own **property?**

The *gemara* challenges this assumption. It cites a *mishnah* that addresses one who embeds thorns or broken glass in a wall (*hamatznia hakotz vehazechuchit*), or one who builds a fence from thorn-bushes, and whose wall or fence then falls into *reshus harabbim*, injuring someone — the ruling is *chiyuv*. Rabbi Yochanan comments: this applies only if the fence *mafriyach* — protrudes or spreads outward into the *reshus harabbim*. But *bimetzamtzem* — if the fence is carefully contained entirely within one's own property line — one is *patur*.

### Metzamtzem Exemption

Why is one exempt in the *metzamtzem* case? Rav Acha son of Rav Ika explains: *lefi she'ein darchan shel bnei adam lehischachech bekosalim* — because people naturally do not walk flush against walls. When moving through a public thoroughfare, people instinctively give walls a buffer zone, much as a driver allows clearance when pulling through a narrow passage. Accordingly, a hazard that is precisely on one's side of the property line and against a wall does not realistically threaten passersby, who will anyway maintain that natural distance. The *bor*, by contrast, is invisible from the surface; the pedestrian walking along the edge of the property line has no reason to give a buffer, and may fall in without warning.

### Chiyuv Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלַל דְּחִיּוּבָא דְבוֹר – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים הוּא; אַלְמָא מַפְקִיר נְזָקָיו חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** By inference, the liability** in the category **of Pit,** according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, **is in the public domain,** where a pit generally does not belong to the one who dug it. **Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that **one who renounces ownership of his hazardous property is liable.**

The *gemara* draws an inference from Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. If one holds that *chiyuv* for *bor* requires ownership, then when is one ever actually *chayav*? If the *bor* is in the middle of *reshus harabbim*, one does not own it and is *patur*. If the *bor* is in one's own domain, people should not be there. The only scenario in which *chiyuv* would exist would be a *bor* dug precisely at the edge of one's property, technically owned, yet functioning as a hazard because it abuts *reshus harabbim* directly. But this is precisely the scenario of the thorny fence placed *metzamtzem* — on one's own side of the line — and Rabbi Yochanan rules *patur* there. The exemption in the wall case is explained not by lack of ownership but by the buffer-zone principle: because people do not rub against walls, a hazard flush against one's wall at the property line does not create a realistic danger for the public. However, an invisible *bor* at the property line does, because pedestrians have no occasion to avoid it.

This leads the *gemara* to conclude: *michlal dechiyuva debor bireshus harabbim hu* — the *chiyuv* of *bor* in the Torah is grounded in a *bor* in *reshus harabbim*, implying that one need not own the *bor* to be *chayav*. *Alma*, then, *mafkir nizko chayav* — abandoning the hazard does not eliminate liability.

### Resolution: Stam Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר וָמֵת – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in an unattributed mishna (50b): In the case of **one who digs a pit in the public domain and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **liable.**

The *gemara* ultimately resolves the question by appealing to a principle of Rabbi Yochanan himself: *halakha kesam mishnah* — we rule in accordance with an unattributed *mishnah*. A later *mishnah* states plainly that one who digs a *bor bireshus harabbim* and into which an ox or donkey falls is *chayav*. This *stam mishnah* assumes *chiyuv* for a *bor* one does not own. Since Rabbi Yochanan rules like a *stam mishnah*, he clearly holds that ownership is not a prerequisite for *bor* liability.

The positions are thus reversed from what had been initially assumed. Rabbi Yochanan holds that one is *chayav* for a *bor bireshus harabbim* even without owning it — the classic case of *bor* requires no ownership. Rav Elazar, citing Rabbi Yishmael, had said that a *bor bireshus harabbim* is treated *as if* you owned it — which implicitly establishes that ownership is the conceptual foundation of *bor* liability. Rav Elazar himself then took that framework one step further, ruling that actual ownership is required, and that for a *bor bireshus harabbim*, which one genuinely does not own, one would in fact be *patur*. The *gemara* resolves the apparent tension in Rav Elazar's position — that he had himself cited the tradition treating *bor bireshus harabbim* as owned — by noting: *lo kashya ha didei ha derabeih* — that was the position of his teacher, Rabbi Yishmael; Rav Elazar's own view extended the underlying ownership logic to its conclusion.

In sum: Rabbi Yochanan holds that *bor* liability is fundamentally about creating a hazard and causing damage, independent of ownership. Rav Elazar holds that *bor* is fundamentally a category of *mamon hamazik* — damage by one's property — and therefore requires ownership; the *bor bireshus harabbim* case in the Torah may be treated constructively as owned, but conceptually, ownership remains the operative criterion.

## Adam Hamazik Tripping

### Ramban: No Negligence

Before turning to the next *mishnah*, an important conceptual thread requires attention. The entire preceding discussion of *nitkal poshea* and *nitkal ones* has been framed in terms of *bor* liability. But one of the ways the *gemara* framed the issue earlier was the case in which, as a person trips, a pitcher flies from one's hand and strikes someone. The question of *nitkal poshea* applies there as well. This creates a tension with the view of the Ramban, who holds that *adam hamazik* — direct bodily damage by a person — generates liability even without negligence.

### Body vs. Object

The distinction that resolves this tension is the following. If a person trips and actually falls upon another person or that person's property, even without any negligence on the tripper's part, one is *chayav* — that is *adam hamazik*, which, like *moed l'olam*, does not require fault. It is one's own body that caused the damage, and one is always responsible for what one's body does.

### Negligence & Agency

The case of the flying pitcher is categorically different. When a person trips and something flies out of one's hand and strikes another person's object, the question becomes: whose *koach* is that? The pitcher's impact is not the person's body breaking the object — it is a thing the person was holding that broke the object. Whether that act can be attributed to the person — whether it counts as *the person's* act — may depend on whether the person was negligent in the first place. Without a requisite degree of negligence, the flying pitcher may not constitute the person's *koach* at all; it is simply a flying object that caused damage. With negligence, the act of the object can be attributed back to the person who allowed it to fly free. This is why, for the Ramban, who exempts *adam hamazik* from the negligence requirement, the *nitkal poshea* analysis remains relevant specifically for the case where not one's body, but an object that left one's hands, caused the harm.

## Water Spilling & Hazards

### Spilling Water Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּנִטְנְפוּ כֵּלָיו בְּמַיִם, אֲבָל הוּא עַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר; קַרְקַע עוֹלָם הִזִּיקַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the case of one who pours water in the public domain, **Rav says: They taught** that he is liable **only when the clothes** of one who slipped **were soiled by the** dirty **water, but** if the one who slipped **himself** was injured, the one who poured the water **is exempt,** as it is the impact with **the ground** that **injured him,** not the water.

The next *mishnah* continues seamlessly from the prior discussion. Where the earlier case involved accidental breakage of a vessel, this *mishnah* moves to a more deliberate act: *hashofech mayim bireshus harabbim* — one who spills water into the public domain. This was not an accident; one was simply disposing of collected rainwater or wastewater. *Vehuzak bahen acher chayav benizko* — if someone is damaged, one is *chayav*. Given the deliberate nature of the act, fault and negligence are clear.

The *mishnah* continues with the cases already discussed — embedding thorns or glass in a wall, building a fence from thorns — and the liability when such things cause harm in *reshus harabbim*. These cases span a wide spectrum: from a pitcher that breaks accidentally, to water intentionally spilled, to a wall that falls perhaps without the owner's knowledge or fault. The absence of any explicit negligence analysis in the *mishnah*'s formulation itself explains why an earlier *amora* could read the *mishnah* as supporting liability even in a case of total *ones* — because the *mishnah* seems indifferent to the question of how deliberate the hazard-creation was. The *gemara*, however, has been insisting throughout that some degree of negligence is required, which means these cases must be understood as involving either anticipation of risk, notice that a hazard had been created, or an opportunity to remedy the situation.

### Rav: Water as Shor

Rav limits the *chiyuv*: *lo shanu ela denitkalu kelav bemayim* — one is only *chayav* when the person's vessels were damaged by the water. The water, in Rav's view, remains the property of the one who spilled it and functions both as *shor* (causing damage as a moving force) and as *bor* (a stationary hazard). Damage to vessels by the water itself is thus *chayav*. However, *aval hu atzmo patur* — if the person slipped on the water and injured himself upon the ground, one is exempt: *karka olam hizikato* — it was the ground, the *karka olam*, that injured him, not the water. The water may have caused him to slip, but the actual harm was inflicted by the ground below, which is not one's property. Strange as this may appear, it is Rav's position.

### Rav Huna: Mud Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב: לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא כְּרִפְשׁוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said to Rav:** Why should he be exempt from paying restitution for the injury? Even if the water that he poured is considered **only like his filth** that he tossed in the public domain, he should be liable. Since the muddy ground caused the injury, and the mud belongs to him, as it results from the addition of his water to the dirt, he should be liable.

Rav Huna challenges Rav: *lo yehei ela kevifsho* — let it be treated at least like one's waste or dung. His argument proceeds as follows. If the spilled water mixed with the earth and transformed it into mud, one can no longer say the person injured himself on *karka olam*. The mud is, in some sense, the *spiller's* mud — one's water transformed the ground and, in doing so, made that patch of ground one's own. Once the ground becomes mud, and it is one's mud, it is no longer *karka olam* that injures the passerby — it is one's transformed property.

### Water on Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ דְּלָא תַּמּוּ מַיָּא?! בִּדְתַמּוּ מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav responded: **Do you maintain** that this is a case **where the water was not absorbed** into the ground? It is a case **where the water was absorbed,** leaving only moist dirt. Since there is no mud there that can be deemed as belonging to the one who spilled the water, he is exempt from liability.

The *gemara* responds by questioning the factual premise. *Mi savrat dela tamu maya* — Tosafot notes that the superior textual reading reverses the formulation: what makes you think the water was absorbed into the earth and turned it to mud? The case in question is water sitting *on top of* the ground, not absorbed into it. It has not transformed the earth; the earth below remains *karka olam* exactly as it was. The water rests on the surface; one is *chayav* for what the water does, but not for what the ground beneath it does. Only if the water genuinely entered the earth and produced mud would Rav Huna's argument apply, because only then would the ground itself become, in a meaningful sense, one's property.

### Summer/Rainy Seasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, וַחֲדָא בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ פּוֹתְקִין בִּיבוֹתֵיהֶן וְגוֹרְפִין מְעָרוֹתֵיהֶן – בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה אֵין לָהֶן רְשׁוּת, וּבִימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים יֵשׁ לָהֶם רְשׁוּת. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת – אִם הִזִּיקוּ, חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the mishna's ruling refers only to the soiling of the pedestrian's clothes, **why do I** need **two** *mishnayot* to state this *halakha*? **One** *halakha* was stated with regard to a case where this occurred **in the summer,** the dry season, **and one** with regard to a case where it occurred **in the rainy season. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **all those** people **who** engage in activities that the Sages **stated** are permitted, i.e., those who **open [*potkin*] their gutters** and drain the sewage from their houses into the public domain, **and** those who **flush out** the water from **their caves,** where foul-smelling water was stored, into the public domain, **during the summer they do not have permission** to do so, while **during the rainy season they have permission** to do so, since the street is rained upon in any event and thereby washed. **And although** all these people perform their actions **with permission, if they cause damage they are liable to pay** for it.

One might ask: why does the *mishnah* need two cases involving someone slipping on water — the earlier case (the broken pitcher) and this one (*hashofech mayim*)? The *gemara* answers: one case addresses the summer season, the other the rainy season. A *baraita* teaches that those who wish to open their drainage pipes and empty their collected waste into *reshus harabbim* — *kol elu she'amru potkin bivoteihen vegofrin me'aroteihen* — during the summer (*biyemot hachama*) have no such right: *ein lahem reshus*. In the rainy season (*biyemot hageshamim*), they do have such a right: *yesh lahem reshus*. The two cases of the *mishnah* thus address different seasonal contexts and the distinct legal question of whether one had permission.

### Permission ≠ Exempt

This brings the *gemara* to an important principle: *ve'af al pi shebireshus im heiziku chayavim leshalem* — even when one has the legal right to dispose of one's water or waste into *reshus harabbim*, if damage results, one must pay. This might seem counterintuitive. The classic *bor* case, after all, involves someone who clearly had no right to leave the hazard; one might expect that having explicit permission would eliminate liability. But the *gemara* teaches otherwise. Permission to perform an act does not confer immunity from the consequences of that act. One retains the obligation to execute it in a manner that does not expose others to danger. If the manner of disposal creates a hazard and someone is harmed, liability remains — regardless of whether the underlying act was permitted.

This is exactly the point of the second *mishnah* case: *hashofech mayim bireshus harabbim* refers precisely to the scenario where one may have had a legal right to spill the water, yet remains *chayav* because permission to act does not mean permission to harm.

## Glass in Walls

### Glass Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּצְנִיעַ קוֹצוֹתָיו וּזְכוּכִיּוֹתָיו לְתוֹךְ כּוֹתֶל שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וּבָא בַּעַל כּוֹתֶל וְסָתַר כּוְתְלוֹ וְנָפַל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיב הַמַּצְנִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 2:6): With regard to **one who conceals his thorns or his pieces of glass in another's wall, and the owner of the wall came and demolished his wall and it fell into the public domain,** and the thorns or glass **caused damage, the one who concealed them is liable.**

A *baraita* presents a further scenario. Shimon placed his thorns and broken glass (*kotzav uzechuchisav*) inside Reuven's wall. Reuven then demolished his wall (*baal hakosel vesasar koslo*), and it fell into *reshus harabbim*, with the glass causing injury. The ruling: *chayav hamatznia* — Shimon, who hid the glass, is *chayav*. The *gemara* probes the conditions of this liability.

### Rickety Wall Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּכוֹתֶל רָעוּעַ, אֲבָל בְּכוֹתֶל בָּרִיא – הַמַּצְנִיעַ פָּטוּר, וְחַיָּיב בַּעַל הַכּוֹתֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** this **only in** the case of **an unstable wall,** since the one who concealed his items should have anticipated that the owner of the wall would soon demolish it, **but in** the case of **a stable wall, the one who concealed** his items **is exempt, and the owner of the wall is liable.**

Rabbi Yochanan qualifies: *lo shanu ela bekosel raua* — Shimon's liability applies only when the wall was rickety (*raua*), such that he could reasonably have anticipated that it would collapse or be torn down. *Aval bekosel bari, hamatznia patur* — if the wall was structurally sound, Shimon is exempt. Placing glass inside a solid wall is comparable to *ruach she'einah metzuyah* — an unforeseeable wind. Shimon may have violated Reuven's property rights by secreting his glass there, but from the standpoint of *nezek* — creating a hazard for the public in *reshus harabbim* — there was no negligence. Under all normal circumstances, a strong wall would not have fallen and no danger would have materialized. Reuven may have a separate grievance about the misuse of his wall, but Shimon's liability toward the *rabim* is absent.

### Shimon/Reuven Paradox

This produces a striking result. If the wall was *raua*, Shimon is *chayav* because he should have anticipated the wall's collapse. If the wall was *bari*, Shimon is *patur* — but in that case, *vechayav baal hakosel*, Reuven, the owner of the wall, is *chayav*. The reversal is striking: the stronger the wall, the less culpable Shimon is, and the more culpable Reuven becomes.

### Reuven's Liability

Why is Reuven *chayav* when the wall was solid? This is, as the *gemara* itself does not fully explain, the more puzzling of the two rulings. Rashi suggests that one who tears down one's own wall bears responsibility for all consequences of that demolition — once Reuven undertakes to knock down the wall, he must investigate and account for everything that might be dislodged or dispersed. Yet the critical explanatory step is absent from the *gemara*: how is Reuven liable when he had no knowledge that Shimon's glass was embedded there? One must either assume he did know, or hold that whoever demolishes a structure is expected to anticipate any consequences of that demolition — a demanding standard whose precise rationale the *gemara* leaves without full articulation.

### Bucket Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: הַמְכַסָּה בּוֹרוֹ בְּדׇלְיוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וּבָא בַּעַל דְּלִי וְנָטַל דׇּלְיוֹ – חַיָּיב בַּעַל הַבּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: That is to say** that in the case of **one who covers his pit with another's bucket, and the owner of the bucket came and took his bucket,** and the pit causes damage, **the owner of the pit is liable.**

Ravina draws an analogy to make the rickety-wall ruling feel less surprising. The principle is: *hamechaseh bor bedal'yo shel chaveiro* — if Shimon used Reuven's bucket as a manhole cover for his pit, and Reuven came and reclaimed his bucket, *chayav baal habor* — Shimon is *chayav* for the now-uncovered pit. The comparison illuminates Shimon's position: when Shimon puts his glass into Reuven's rickety wall, knowing that wall is liable to come down, it is as though he is covering his hazard with something he has every reason to expect will be removed. The eventual removal of the "cover" — whether Reuven takes back his bucket or the wall collapses — was foreseeable, and therefore the hazard's reemergence into *reshus harabbim* remains Shimon's responsibility.

The *gemara* notes that this analogy, while clarifying, may be somewhat beside the point. It explains readily why Shimon is *chayav* when the wall was rickety — a point already derived from the foreseeable-collapse analysis. What the *gemara* is more interested in, and less successful at explaining, is why Reuven bears liability in the strong-wall scenario where Shimon could not have anticipated the collapse. That asymmetry — Reuven liable despite ignorance, Shimon exempt despite having placed a dangerous object in another's wall — remains the more intellectually arresting aspect of this *baraita*.

## Chasidim & Excellence

### Burying Hazards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הָיוּ מַצְנִיעִים קוֹצוֹתֵיהֶם וּזְכוּכִיּוֹתֵיהֶם בְּתוֹךְ שְׂדוֹתֵיהֶן, וּמַעֲמִיקִים לָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְעַכֵּב הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת שָׁדֵי לְהוּ בְּנוּרָא. רָבָא שָׁדֵי לְהוּ בְּדִגְלַת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: The early pious people would conceal their thorns and their pieces of glass in their fields, and would dig to the depth of** at least **three handbreadths** in order to bury them, **so that they would not obstruct the plow. Rav Sheshet would toss** his thorns **into fire,** so they would not cause damage to others. **Rava would toss them into the Tigris [*Diglat*]** River.

The *gemara* brings a *baraita* that transitions from the legal analysis to the domain of ethical aspiration: *chasidim harishonim* — the original righteous ones — would take their thorns and broken glass (*kotzoteihem vezechuchiyoteihem*) and bury them *betoch sadoteihem*, within their own fields, three *tefachim* deep in the ground (*mema'akim lahem gimmel tefachim*), so that the plow would not be impeded — and, according to another version, so that the plow would not expose them and inadvertently recreate the hazard. Rav Sheshet would burn such debris. Rava would throw it into the Tigris River. The common thread is that these figures went well beyond what the law required in order to ensure that no person could possibly be harmed by their discarded materials.

### Three Paths Chasidus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמֶהֱוֵי חֲסִידָא – לְקַיֵּים מִילֵּי דִּנְזִיקִין. רָבָא אָמַר: מִילֵּי דְאָבוֹת. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: מִילֵּי דִּבְרָכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: One who wants to be pious should observe the matters of** tractate ***Nezikin*,** so as to avoid causing damage to others. **Rava said** he should observe **the matters of** tractate ***Avot*. And some say** he should observe **the matters of** tractate ***Berakhot*.**

From this the *gemara* derives a general principle, introduced by Rav Yehuda: *hai man deba'i lemehei chasida, lekayem milei denezikin* — whoever wishes to be truly righteous should fulfill the obligations of *nezikin* — Bava Kamma, Bava Metzia, and Bava Batra, covering the full range of interpersonal obligations: damages, theft, lost property, and the prohibition of *ribis*. Rava adds: *milei de'avos* — Pirkei Avos. And some say: *milei debrachos* — Maseches Brachos.

The Maharsha notes that *milei denezikin* encompasses all three *Bava* tractates. But what does *lekayem* mean here? Does it merely mean fulfilling one's baseline legal obligations — knowing the laws and keeping them precisely — and that this alone constitutes *chasidus*? Or does it mean something more? Rabbeinu Chananel adds the phrase *afilu lirshus harabbim*, suggesting that *chasidus* in *nezikin* entails carrying one's conscientiousness even into contexts where no strict legal obligation applies.

### Vigilance Over Law

The three paths — *nezikin*, *avos*, and *brachos* — may represent three distinct approaches to the underlying question of what it means to achieve ethical excellence rather than mere legal compliance. The *nezikin* path focuses on interpersonal obligations: the legal framework of rights and responsibilities toward others. Rabbeinu Chananel's comment suggests this path leads not just to meeting legal requirements but to a posture of constant vigilance, ensuring one never causes harm even in situations where liability would not technically attach.

The Maharsha (channeling the Ran) understands the three options as addressing three distinct spheres of human responsibility. *Avos* is concerned primarily with *middos* — character development and self-cultivation. The ethical tradition represented by *Pirkei Avos* asks whether right conduct flows from right character: if one cultivates the proper disposition, the correct behavior will follow naturally. *Brachos*, as Rabbeinu Chananel reads it, is grounded in the recognition that *assur l'adam leihanos min ha'olam hazeh belo bracha* — nothing in the world is ultimately one's own; everything belongs to God. If that recognition is internalized deeply, it transforms one's orientation toward property — both one's own and others' — in a way that no legal framework alone can achieve. The person who truly understands that their possessions are not ultimately theirs will be far more careful about how those possessions affect others. These are thus three pathways to the same destination: *chasidus* in interpersonal behavior — not in the particularistic or ritualistic sense of the term, but in the universal ethical sense of taking seriously one's impact on other people and the world around them.

## Straw/Hay & Rights (II)

### Straw/Hay Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת תִּבְנוֹ וְקַשּׁוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִזְבָלִים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקוֹ, וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמְקַלְקְלִין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם, וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who takes out his straw [*teven*] and his hay [*kash*] to the public domain to** use afterward as **fertilizer and another** person **incurred damage due to them,** he is **liable** to pay **for his damage, and whoever** takes possession of the hay and straw **first acquires** them for himself. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** With regard to **anyone who places obstacles in the public domain and they cause damage,** he is **liable to pay** damages, **and whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them.

The *mishnah* introduces a new case: *hamotzi es tivno vekasho lereshus harabbim lizvalim* — one who puts out straw (*teven*) and hay (*kash*) in *reshus harabbim* in order to have it trodden upon and converted into usable fertilizer. The goal is improvement — one wants to derive benefit from the act of placing it there. *Vehuzak bahen acher chayav benizko* — if someone is harmed, one is *chayav*. Once again the *mishnah* does not specify the basis for fault, but the implication is clear: one should be able to foresee that depositing material in a public thoroughfare exposes people to risk, and that foreseeability supplies the negligence.

*Vechol hakodem bahen zachah* — and whoever seizes the straw first acquires it. This is a novel *halakha*: not only is the person who put the straw there liable for any harm, but members of the public acquire the right to take possession of the straw. Whether this is a general rule or a targeted response to the particularly common and slippery hazard of straw in the public domain is a question the *gemara* explores. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel broadens the rule: *kol hamekalkelin bireshus harabbim vehiziku chayavin leshalem, vechol hakodem bahem zachah* — anyone who damages the public thoroughfare (*mekalkel* in *reshus harabbim*) must pay, and anyone who seizes the material has acquired it.

The term *mekalkel* — literally, one who ruins or corrupts — is significant. It does not describe simply leaving any object in a public space; it refers to actually altering or degrading the public thoroughfare itself. Leaving a knapsack in the road, which could be a momentary tripping hazard, is not yet *mekalkel*. Spreading straw across the street so that it is everywhere, or scattering broken glass so that it lies there indefinitely — these alter the landscape of the *reshus harabbim* and constitute *mekalkel*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel thus appears to be expanding upon the *tanna kamma*: where the *tanna kamma* cited the concrete case of straw and hay, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel articulates a broader principle covering anyone who degrades the public thoroughfare.

## Principle/Profit Deb (II)

### Mekalkel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה. אָמַר רַב: בֵּין בְּגוּפָן, בֵּין בְּשִׁבְחָן. וּזְעֵירִי אָמַר: בְּשִׁבְחָן אֲבָל לֹא בְּגוּפָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated in the mishna that if one puts his straw and hay out in the public domain, whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them. The Sages disagreed with regard to the extent of this acquisition. **Rav says:** He acquires them **both with regard to** the items **themselves and with regard to** the value of **their enhancement,** which took place while they were in the public domain. **And Ze'eiri says:** He acquires them **with regard to** the value of **their enhancement but not with regard to** the items **themselves,** and he must pay the owner of the straw according to their value at the time he placed them in the public domain.

The debate over *kol hakodem bahem zachah* — whether one may seize only the *shevach* (the improved product, i.e., the fertilizer) or also the *guf* (the principal, i.e., the original straw) — leads the *gemara* to examine what *mekalkel* means and what the logic of the forfeiture rule actually is. Rav holds that one may seize *bein begufan bein beshivchan* — both the principal and the improved product. Ze'iri holds that one may only seize *bishvachan velo vegufan* — the improvement, not the original straw.

The *gemara* frames the disagreement: *Rav savar knesu gufan mishum shvachan* — Rav agrees in principle that the *shevach* is the conceptual focus of the forfeiture, but holds that since we are fining the *shevach*, we extend the fine to include the principal as well. Ze'iri holds that the fine tracks the wrong precisely: since the problem is the improvement, one may only seize the improvement.

This framing raises an honest difficulty. The straw itself is the immediate hazard — it is what causes people to slip. Why, then, is the analysis centered on the *shevach*? The *gemara*'s implicit answer seems to be that the motive for placing the straw in *reshus harabbim* in the first place is the anticipated improvement — one wants fertilizer. It is that motive, and its anticipated product, that the rabbis wish to penalize and remove as an incentive. Whether that fully explains why the principal straw is treated as conceptually secondary to its improved form is a question the *gemara* itself acknowledges but does not fully resolve.

## Straw/Hay & Rights (II)

### Rabbi Yehuda Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁעַת הוֹצָאַת זְבָלִים, אָדָם מוֹצִיא זִבְלוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְצוֹבְרוֹ כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא נִישּׁוֹף בְּרַגְלֵי אָדָם וּבְרַגְלֵי בְּהֵמָה. שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that the ruling in **the mishna** that one is liable to pay for damage caused by straw or hay that he put in the public domain **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: During the period when fertilizer is taken out, a person may take his fertilizer out to the public domain and let it accumulate** there **for a full thirty days, so that it gets trampled by people's feet and by animals' feet, as** it was **on this condition** that **Joshua bequeathed Eretz** Yisrael to the Jewish people (see 80b). In other words, people do not have the right to prevent someone from taking out his straw to the public domain, since they received their portion of Eretz Yisrael following Joshua's conquest on this condition. Apparently, since one has the right to take out his straw, he is not held liable to pay for damage caused by it.

The *gemara* now asks whether the *mishnah*'s rule of liability is consistent with the view of Rabbi Yehuda. A *baraita* teaches: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, bish'as hotza'as zvalim, adam motzi zavlo lireshus harabbim vetzovro kol shloshim yom* — at the designated time for removing dung, a person may bring one's dung into *reshus harabbim* and pile it there for thirty days, *kedei sheyei nishof beraglei adam uveraglei behema* — so that it may be trodden by people and animals and transformed into usable fertilizer. *Al menat ken hinchil Yehoshua es ha'aretz* — this right, Rabbi Yehuda says, was one of the conditions under which Yehoshua allocated the land of Israel; it is embedded in the social compact of the *aretz*.

### Permission/Liability Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאִם הִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** since **Rabbi Yehuda** possibly **concedes that if** the fertilizer **caused damage,** he is liable to **pay for the damage it caused,** although he acted within his rights.

The *gemara* responds: even if one holds like Rabbi Yehuda that there is a right to dispose of waste in *reshus harabbim* at the appropriate time, *modeh Rabbi Yehuda she'im hizik meshalem mah shehizik* — Rabbi Yehuda concedes that if damage results, one must pay. The language of *al menat ken hinchil Yehoshua es ha'aretz* might suggest something stronger — that the permission is absolute, conferring immunity — but the *gemara* reads it as permitting the act while not immunizing its harmful consequences.

A *baraita* then challenges this reading. It cites the principle of *ner Chanukah*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer bener Chanukah patur mipnei shehu reshus mitzvah* — if a shopkeeper placed his Chanukah *menorah* outside and it ignited a passing camel's flax, Rabbi Yehuda exempts him because it is a *reshus mitzvah*, a *mitzvah*-permitted act. This appears to state a general principle: when one has a right (*reshus*) to do something, one is not liable for resulting damage. The *gemara* distinguishes: the exemption applies specifically to *reshus mitzvah* — permission rooted in a *mitzvah* obligation — not to a general license granted by *beit din*. A merely legal permission (*reshus beit din*), without the backing of a *mitzvah*, does not generate impunity.

A further *baraita* settles the matter explicitly: *kol elu she'amru mutarim lekalkel bireshus harabbim — im hiziku chayavim leshalem, veRabbi Yehuda poter* — all those who have a right to damage the *reshus harabbim*: if they cause harm, they are *chayav* — but Rabbi Yehuda exempts them. This *baraita* makes clear that the *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Yehuda.

Rav Nachman preserves the possibility of consistency: *matnitin shelo bish'as hotza'as zvalim veRabbi Yehuda hi* — our *mishnah* may indeed reflect Rabbi Yehuda's view, but addresses a case where the person acted outside the permitted time period, when no license existed at all. Rav Ashi offers a further resolution: even Rabbi Yehuda would agree that straw and hay — *mishum demashreki*, because they are extraordinarily slippery — remain subject to liability even when placed during the permitted season, because the degree of potential harm is so high that no license can absolve it.

The upshot is a clear principle that runs through all these cases: having a right to perform an act does not exempt one from liability for the harm that act causes. Liability exists independently of permission. One who enjoys a *reshus* to dispose of materials in *reshus harabbim* must still ensure the disposal is not conducted in a manner that exposes others to harm; failure to do so leaves the full weight of liability intact.

### Principal vs. Shevach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַב סָבַר: קָנְסוּ גּוּפָן מִשּׁוּם שִׁבְחָן, וּזְעֵירִי סָבַר: לֹא קָנְסוּ גּוּפָן מִשּׁוּם שִׁבְחָן.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what do they disagree? Rav holds** that the Sages **penalized** the owner by revoking his ownership of the items **themselves due to** the value of **their enhancement** that he stands to earn by putting them in the public domain, **whereas Ze'eiri holds** that **they did not penalize** him by revoking his ownership of the items **themselves due to** the value of **their enhancement.** Rather, he loses only the increase in their value due to their enhancement.

Returning to the question of *kol hakodem bahem zachah*, the *gemara* examines Rav's position that one may seize even the principal. *Rav savar bein begufan bein beshivchan* — both the original straw and the improved fertilizer may be seized. Ze'iri holds that only the *shevach* — the improvement — is available for seizure. The *gemara* frames the underlying debate: *Rav savar knesu gufan mishum shvachan* — Rav agrees the conceptual focus is the improvement, but extends the fine to the principal; Ze'iri holds the fine is precisely calibrated to the wrong — only the improvement may be seized.

### Shevach Motive

The *gemara*'s framing centers the analysis on the *shevach* as the person's motive for placing the straw in *reshus harabbim*. The straw is placed precisely because it will improve into fertilizer; that anticipated improvement is what incentivizes the behavior. The forfeiture penalty is therefore directed at removing that incentive: by making the improved product available for seizure, the law strips the person of the benefit that motivated the act. Under Rav's view, this logic extends to the principal itself; under Ze'iri's view, the penalty is limited to exactly what caused the misbehavior.

### Dung Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַהוֹפֵךְ אֶת הַגָּלָל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who turns over dung in the public domain and another** person **incurred damage due to it,** the former **is liable** to pay **for his damage.**

The *mishnah* then records: *hahofech es hagalal bireshus harabbim vehuzak bahen acher chayav benizko* — one who overturns dung in *reshus harabbim* and another person is harmed is *chayav*. Significantly, the *mishnah* does not say *kol hakodem bahem zachah* — there is no right of seizure stated in the dung case. This appears to support Ze'iri: since dung has no *shevach* — it is already in its improved state and cannot improve further — there is no improvement to fine, and therefore no forfeiture right attaches. The *gemara* initially suggests this proves Ze'iri's position.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds: *davar sheyesh bo shevach knesu gufo mishum shivcho* — for something that can generate improvement, like straw, Rav's rule applies and one may seize even the principal because of the potential *shevach*. *Davar she'ein bo shevach* — something that can never improve, like dung that is already fully processed — *lo kenasu*: even Rav would not extend the forfeiture right to the principal, because without any *shevach* in the picture at all, the rationale for fining the principal does not apply. This means Rav's rule that one may seize the principal is itself conditional on the presence of a *shevach* dimension; it is not a blanket rule about anything left in *reshus harabbim*.

### Timing Question: Immediate or After Improvement?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר קָנְסוּ גּוּפָן מִשּׁוּם שִׁבְחָן; לְאַלְתַּר קָנְסִינַן, אוֹ לְכִי מַיְיתִי שְׁבָחָא קָנְסִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to the statement of** Rav, **who says that they penalized** him by revoking his ownership of **the items themselves because of its** added value due to **enhancement, do we impose** this **penalty immediately** when he takes the straw out, allowing one to take possession of it even before it has time for its value to become enhanced, **or do we impose the penalty** only **when its enhancement** in value **emerges?**

A further question is then raised: *le'altar kansinan* — if it is something that can generate improvement, may the straw be seized immediately upon placement, before any improvement has occurred? Or — *o lechi mashvei shvacha kansinan* — only once some actual improvement has taken place and the straw has begun to become fertilizer? The *gemara* does not reach a clean resolution of this question. It observes that the challenge from the dung case was mounted before Rav Nachman's distinction was articulated — i.e., when the questioner assumed Rav entitled one to seize the principal *on its own terms*, without reference to *shevach*. Now that Rav's rule is understood to be conditional on *shevach*, the dung case no longer serves as a counterargument, and the timing question — whether seizure rights vest immediately or only upon actual improvement — remains open.

## Seizing & Announcement

### Taking/Stealing Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא תִּבְנוֹ וְקַשּׁוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִזְבָלִים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקוֹ, וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה, וּמוּתָּר מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. וְהַהוֹפֵךְ אֶת הַגָּלָל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב, וְאָסוּר מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל!
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who takes out his straw and his hay to the public domain to** use afterward as **fertilizer and another** person **incurred damage due to them, he is liable** to pay **for his damage. And** furthermore, **whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them, **and is permitted** to do so **with regard to** the prohibition against **robbery. And** in the case of **one who turns over dung in the public domain and another incurred damage due to it,** he **is liable, but it is forbidden** to take possession of it **due to** the prohibition against **robbery.**

The *gemara* turns to what appears to be an internal contradiction in the *baraita*. It reads: *hamotzi tivna vekasha lireshus harabbim lizivalim, vehuzak bahen acher chayav benizko, vechol hakodem bahem zachah, ve'assur mishum gezel* — one who takes straw to *reshus harabbim* for fertilizer, who causes damage is liable, whoever seizes first acquires it, yet it is *prohibited* on account of stealing. How can the same passage say both that one who seizes acquires and that taking it is forbidden?

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction by distinguishing the two halves of the ruling. *Kol hakodem bahem zachah* — the seizure right — refers to the *shevach*: one may take what has already improved into fertilizer. *Ve'assur mishum gezel* — the prohibition — refers to the *guf*, the principal straw. The *tanna kamma* thus permits seizure only of the improvement while prohibiting taking the original straw, treating the principal as still protected by laws of *gezelah*.

### Rabban Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי: הַמּוֹצִיא תִּבְנוֹ וְקַשּׁוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִזְבָלִים, וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר – חַיָּיב בְּנִזְקוֹ, וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה, וַאֲסוּרִין מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמְקַלְקְלִין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם, וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה, וּמוּתָּרִין מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests alternatively: **Let us say** that the dispute between Rav and Ze'eiri is **parallel to** a dispute between **these *tanna'im*,** as it is stated in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who takes his straw and his hay out to the public domain to** use afterward as **fertilizer, and another incurred damage due to them,** he is **liable** to pay **for his damage. And whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them, **but they are forbidden due to** the prohibition against **robbery. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In the case of **anyone who places obstacles in the public domain and they cause damage,** he is **liable to pay. And whoever** takes possession of **them first acquires** them **and they are permitted** to him **with regard to** the prohibition against **robbery.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees. His formulation — *kol hamekalkelin bireshus harabbim vehiziku chayavin leshalem, vechol hakodem bahem zachah, umuttar mishum gezel* — uses *muttar mishum gezel* rather than *assur*. He holds that even the principal may be seized; it is not protected by the laws of *gezelah*. On this reading, *muttar mishum gezel* means the straw is fully available to anyone who takes it, principal included.

### Rav Huna: Silent Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה, וַהֲדַר קָאָמַר אֲסוּרִין מִשּׁוּום גָּזֵל! אֶלָּא לָאו הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכׇל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה – בְּשִׁבְחָן, וַאֲסוּרִין מִשּׁוּום גָּזֵל – אַגּוּפָן; וַאֲתָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר: אֲפִילּוּ גּוּפָן נָמֵי – כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This matter itself is difficult.** First **you said** that **whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them, **and then it says that they are forbidden due to** the prohibition against **robbery,** indicating that one may not take possession of them. **Rather, is this not what** the *baraita* **is saying:** This statement: **And whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them, is stated **with regard to** the value of **their enhancement.** And this statement: **But they are forbidden due to** the prohibition against **robbery,** is stated **with regard to** the items **themselves? And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel came to say** that **even** with regard to the items **themselves, whoever** takes possession **of them first acquires** them. Therefore, this tannaitic dispute apparently relates to whether the penalty applies to the items themselves or just to the value of their enhancement.

Rav holds like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that one is entitled to seize even the principal. But how does Rav reconcile this with *assur mishum gezel* in the first half of the *baraita*? Rav Huna citing Rav explains: *halakha ve'ein morin ken* — it is the *halakha* that one may take the principal, but this ruling is not publicly announced. The *assur mishum gezel* formulation reflects what is told to the public; in practice, however, one who seizes the principal has acquired it lawfully.

### Rav Adda: Public Announcement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִזְעֵירִי – וַדַּאי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. לְרַב – מִי לֵימָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? אָמַר לְךָ רַב: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – קָנְסוּ גּוּפָן מִשּׁוּם שִׁבְחָן; וְהָכָא, בַּהֲלָכָה וְאֵין מוֹרִין כֵּן קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – דְּאִתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה, וְאֵין מוֹרִין כֵּן. רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: הֲלָכָה, וּמוֹרִין כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **According to Ze'eiri, it is certainly** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel explicitly deems it permitted for one to take possession of the items. According **to Rav, shall we say** that **it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? The Gemara responds that **Rav** could have **said to you:** According **to all** opinions, the Sages **imposed a penalty** revoking his ownership of the items **themselves due to** the value of **their enhancement. And here** in the *baraita* the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** whether or not it is ruled that this is the ***halakha* but** a public **ruling is not issued to that effect, as it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed as to whether the *halakha* revoking his ownership of the items themselves should be taught to the public. **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says:** This is the ***halakha*, but** a public **ruling is not issued to that effect.** In other words, although it is permitted for one to take possession of the straw, halakhic authorities should not publicly rule that people may do so, lest people become accustomed to taking items that belong to others. This is the opinion of the first *tanna* in the *baraita*. **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** This is the ***halakha* and** a public **ruling is issued to that effect.** This, Rav could have said, is the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Rav Adda bar Ahavah disagrees: *halakha umorin ken* — the *halakha* is as Rav holds, and it is also announced publicly. People are told openly that they may seize even the principal. On this view, *assur mishum gezel* and *muttar mishum gezel* are not positions about what we tell the public versus what is technically true; they reflect a genuine substantive dispute between *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel about whether the principal forfeiture applies.

### Repeat Offenders

A question arises from the behavior of Rav Huna and Rav Adda bar Ahavah themselves, both of whom are reported to have publicly declared materials in *reshus harabbim* available for seizure. If Rav Huna holds that the *halakha* is not announced (*ein morin ken*), why did he make such announcements? The *gemara* answers: *hani mussarin havu* — those were cases involving *mussarin*, persons who had been forewarned and were repeat offenders. For habitual violators who continued to leave hazardous materials in *reshus harabbim* despite prior warning, public announcement of seizure rights was an appropriate and targeted deterrent measure, not a general-policy announcement of the *halakha*.

### Synthesis Summary

The discussion of these final *mishnayot* and *baraitot* yields two interlocking principles. First, permission to perform an act — whether by rabbinic license, seasonal right, or social compact — does not exempt one from liability for harm caused by that act. The obligation to protect others from one's activities is independent of whether the activity itself was permitted. Second, when one does more than create an incidental hazard and instead actively degrades the public thoroughfare (*mekalkel bireshus harabbim*), special forfeiture rules may apply, allowing members of the public to seize the hazardous material. Whether that seizure right extends to the principal or only the improved product, and whether such a rule should be publicly announced or maintained as a known-but-unstated *halakha*, are themselves matters of significant dispute — disputes that resolve differently depending on whether one is dealing with a first-time offender or a habitual violator, and on which of the competing *tannaitic* and *amoraic* frameworks one applies.
