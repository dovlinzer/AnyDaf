# Shevuot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Akiva vs. Tanna Kama

### Eating Defined

The daf opens in the middle of a *tashma* passage, picking up a discussion that concerns one of the foundational disputes of the tractate: when a person takes an oath not to eat, does the word "eat" carry its halachically defined meaning — a *kezayit*, an olive's bulk — or does it mean something else? The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *tanna kama* turns on precisely this question. The *tanna kama* holds that since an oath is grounded in language, and the halachic definition of eating throughout the Torah is a *kezayit*, that definition applies here as well. Rabbi Akiva disagrees. Since a *shevua* is fundamentally about a person's words and intentions, and since one could theoretically take an explicit oath not to eat even the tiniest amount — a *kol shehu* — the very model of a *shevua* applying to a *kol shehu* exists. Therefore, even when unstated, that is how we interpret the oath.

### Rabbi Shimon

The Gemara raises a complication. There is a position, familiar from Makkot, associated with Rabbi Shimon, who holds that in general, the Torah's prohibitions on eating apply even to a *kol shehu* — a small amount — at least for the purposes of *malkus*, lashes. The amount of a *kezayit* is reserved, in his view, only for the obligation to bring a *korban*. The Gemara therefore asks: perhaps Rabbi Akiva's position that even a *kol shehu* triggers an oath violation is not rooted in anything unique about *shevuot* at all — perhaps he simply follows Rabbi Shimon's general approach that a *kol shehu* suffices for transgression.

This raises an immediate difficulty, noted by both Rashi and Tosafot. The context of our Mishnah is entirely about when one brings a *korban* for violating an oath — not about *malkus*. And even Rabbi Shimon himself agrees that a *korban* requires a *kezayit*. So what does Rabbi Shimon's position regarding *malkus* have to do with the question of a *korban* for a *shevua*?

### Rashi's Word-Breaking

Rashi offers what may be called an incisive structural reading. He reasons as follows: when a person eats a prohibited substance, eating more of it represents a greater violation. A small amount earns lashes; a larger amount — a *kezayit* — triggers *karet* if done willfully, or a *korban* if done inadvertently. There is a sliding scale, and the *kezayit* marks a qualitative threshold. But the violation of a *shevua* is categorically different. The wrong being committed is not the eating of the forbidden substance itself — it is the breaking of one's word. And breaking one's word is binary: either one has broken it or one has not. Eating more of the forbidden substance does not constitute a greater breaking of the word. The moment one eats, the word is broken. That is why, according to Rashi, the *kezayit* threshold does not map onto the *shevua* context in the same way.

### Tosafot on Karet

Tosafot takes a different approach. Rabbi Shimon normally links the *kezayit* requirement for a *korban* to the associated punishment of *karet*: since *karet* is a severe divine sanction, one must have committed a substantial transgression — a *kezayit* — to warrant it, and the *korban* mirrors that standard as the substitute for unintentional *karet*-level violations. However, the *korban* brought for violating a *shevua* has no *karet* attached to it at all — even a willful false oath does not incur *karet*. This is itself a peculiarity: one brings a *korban* for something that carries no *karet*. But Tosafot draws from this an important inference: precisely because there is no *karet* associated with a *shevua*, the *kezayit* threshold — which is calibrated to *karet* — need not apply. If a *kol shehu* suffices for *malkus* in ordinary prohibitions (per Rabbi Shimon), then in the domain of *shevuot*, where the *korban* is untethered from *karet*, a *kol shehu* should similarly suffice for the *korban*.

### Challenge to Akiva

The Gemara then challenges the assumption that Rabbi Akiva follows Rabbi Shimon in general. When the *tanna kama* asked Rabbi Akiva, "Where do we ever find that one is liable for eating a *kol shehu*?" — why didn't Rabbi Akiva simply respond, "I follow Rabbi Shimon throughout the entire Torah"? The fact that he did not say this implies that Rabbi Akiva does not in general hold like Rabbi Shimon. Rather, the Gemara explains, Rabbi Akiva was arguing within the framework of the *Rabbanan*'s own position — *ledivreihem*. He was saying: even according to you, who normally require a *kezayit*, you should concede that *shevuot* are different and that a *kol shehu* should apply. The *Rabbanan*, tersely and unabashedly, respond: no.

### Nazir Proof

The Gemara adduces further proof that Rabbi Akiva does not generally follow Rabbi Shimon. The Mishnah elsewhere teaches: a *nazir* who dipped his bread in wine and there was enough wine — when combined with the bread — to constitute a *kezayit*, is liable. Rabbi Akiva requires that the combination reach a *kezayit*. If Rabbi Akiva normally followed Rabbi Shimon that even a *kol shehu* suffices, why would the *nazir* need the wine to combine with the bread up to a *kezayit* at all? Even a small amount of wine should have been sufficient. It is therefore clear that Rabbi Akiva's standard position is that a *kezayit* is required, and *shevuot* represent a deliberate exception in his view.

### Non-Kosher Foods

A Mishnah is cited: if someone takes an oath "I will not eat" and then eats *neveilot*, *treifot*, *shekatzim*, or *remasim*, he is liable. Rabbi Shimon exempts. The Gemara asks the obvious question: why should the oath take effect on non-kosher food at all? The principle of *mushba ve'omeid me-Har Sinai* holds that one is already foresworn from eating these things by virtue of the covenant at Sinai — a *shevua* cannot take effect upon a pre-existing *shevua*.

### Three Answers

Three *Amoraim* — Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan — answer in agreement: the oath takes effect because the person said "I will not eat anything," encompassing all food in the world. Since the oath validly applies to the permitted foods, it simultaneously attaches to the forbidden foods as well — *bekolel devarim hamutarim im ha-asurim*. The forbidden foods are caught within the broader net of the oath.

Reish Lakish disagrees sharply. Even the all-inclusive formulation would not help; the oath still would not take effect on prohibited foods. The only ways the oath can take effect on non-kosher food, he says, are two: either one explicitly takes a *shevua* not to eat even a *chatzi shiur* — a half-*kezayit* — in which case the oath targets an act that the Torah itself does not explicitly forbid at that measure; or, according to Rabbi Akiva, one uses the generic formulation "I will not eat," which Rabbi Akiva interprets as applying to even a *kol shehu*, and since the Torah's prohibition of non-kosher food attaches only at the *kezayit* level, the *shevua* creates an additional prohibition at the sub-*kezayit* level where none previously existed.

### Akiva Implicit

This last point illuminates Rabbi Akiva's position sharply: according to him, any oath phrased using the word "eating" — even without explicit specification — is interpreted to mean a *kol shehu*. This is because the model of a *shevua* applying to a *kol shehu* inherently exists (since one could always have said it explicitly), and Rabbi Akiva understands the implicit oath to carry that meaning. The Gemara then confirms this reading: if Rabbi Akiva followed Rabbi Shimon in general, then by eating non-kosher food even in a small amount, one would already be foresworn from Sinai — there would be nothing for the *shevua* to add. The fact that Rabbi Akiva says the *shevua* does take effect demonstrates that normally even Rabbi Akiva requires a *kezayit*, and only in the domain of *shevuot* does he hold that the *kol shehu* applies.

### Explicit vs. Implicit

Rava summarizes the scope of the debate: the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *tanna kama* applies only when one used the generic formulation "I will not eat" without specifying an amount. But if one explicitly said "I will not eat even a tiny amount," all opinions would agree that even a *kol shehu* creates a violation. And further: if one used the word *"taam"* — "I will not taste" — all would agree that even a *kol shehu* is intended, since "taste" carries no fixed halachic definition. One might have thought otherwise — perhaps "taste" is used colloquially to mean a substantial sample — but Rava maintains that since there is no established halachic measure for "taste," we interpret the word in its most minimal sense. "Eat," however, carries a well-established halachic definition, and the *tanna kama* applies it.

## Eating Exceptions

### Briah Exception

Having established the general rule that a *kezayit* is the default measure for an oath of eating, the Gemara proceeds to identify apparent exceptions that the *Rabbanan* must address when they claim there is no precedent for liability with less than a *kezayit*. The first is the *briah* — a whole creature. One who eats a whole ant is liable even though an ant is far smaller than a *kezayit*. The Gemara responds: *birya shani* — a complete organism is different.

There are two explanations for this. First, just as a *kezayit* confers quantitative significance on an act of eating, a whole creature confers qualitative significance — it represents a complete act. Second, the verse explicitly prohibits eating a *sheretz*, a *sheketz*, and so on; when one eats an entire specimen of that creature, one has violated precisely what the verse says, even if it is smaller than a *kezayit*. By contrast, when the Torah prohibits eating meat generically, the question of "how much?" requires external definition.

### Hekdesh Measure

The Gemara raises a second apparent exception: *hekdesh*, consecrated property. One who derives benefit from consecrated property is liable for *meilah* even for an amount less than a *kezayit*. The Gemara responds: there is no absence of a minimum amount here — the measure is simply different. *Meilah* is calibrated not to acts of eating but to acts of deriving benefit, and the threshold for benefit is a *shaveh prutah*, a penny's worth. A different scale applies, but the principle of requiring a minimum amount remains intact.

### Explicit Tiny Amount

The third exception raised is the case of explicit specification. If someone says "I will not eat even a tiny amount," he is liable for a *kol shehu* — and the *Rabbanan* themselves would concede this. So there exists a case where liability attaches below a *kezayit*. Why, then, do the *Rabbanan* claim there is no such precedent?

The Gemara responds that an explicit oath of this kind is comparable to a *briah*: just as a whole creature is qualitatively significant despite its small size, an explicit statement lends weight and significance to an otherwise trivial act. By default, eating a tiny amount is a meaningless act, beneath the threshold of halachic significance. But if a person explicitly designates that tiny amount as the subject of his oath, he has given it weight — he has made it significant. On the alternative explanation of *briah*, the parallel is more direct: the verse says "do not eat a rat," and if one eats a rat, one has done exactly what the verse prohibits; similarly, if one says "I will not eat a tiny amount" and then eats a tiny amount, one has violated precisely what one said. But the *Rabbanan*'s point is that this is an exception created by explicit designation, not the default interpretation of the word "eat."

### Dirt Question

Rava raises a question: if someone takes a *shevua* that he will not eat dirt and then eats dirt — what is the measure? On one hand, he used the word *achila* — eating — which might import the standard *kezayit* measure. On the other hand, eating dirt is not a normal act of eating in any conventional sense; people do not eat dirt as food. Perhaps in this context, *achila* is simply shorthand for "putting in one's mouth and swallowing," and no standard halachic definition of eating applies, making even a *kol shehu* sufficient. The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

The Gemara notes that this question is relevant to a broader principle. When the *Rabbanan* say there is never liability for a *kol shehu*, they are speaking of normal foodstuffs subject to normal acts of eating. Dirt, which is not *bar achila* — not something that is biologically eaten — may occupy a different category entirely, and the *Rabbanan*'s categorical statement need not address it.

### Konamot

The Gemara then raises *konamot* as a possible exception. A *konam* is a form of *neder* in which a person borrows the language of *korbanot* to prohibit something to himself — "this bread is *konam* to me" means roughly "this bread is to me as a *korban*." Because the word *konam* itself is not a word of eating — it does not say "I forbid myself to eat this" but simply "this is forbidden to me" — the status it creates is not defined by an act of *achila*. Rashi notes that the person is not saying "*konam* this bread from eating" but simply declaring the bread *konam*, which means utterly prohibited in all ways.

As Rav Pappa argues, *konamot* do not require a *kezayit* even according to the *tanna kama*: since no word of *achila* was used, the standard halachic definition of eating does not apply, and even a tiny benefit constitutes a violation. The Gemara characterizes this as *k'mefaresh dami* — equivalent to an explicit specification — because by using the language of *konam* rather than *achila*, the person has, in effect, specified that his prohibition is not of eating in the halachic sense but of something broader.

### Konam Meilah

This raises the question of what measure *does* govern a *konam*. Since the language of *konam* parallels the language of *hekdesh*, it might follow that the relevant measure is a *shaveh prutah*, as in *meilah* from consecrated property. Tosafot presses this point: even if *konam* does not require a *kezayit*, it should not have *less* structure than *hekdesh*, which still requires a minimum of a *shaveh prutah*. The Gemara seems to imply that there is no minimum measure for a *konam* in some respects, which Tosafot finds difficult to resolve.

The deeper issue is whether there is *meilah* by *konamot* at all. *Meilah* normally applies to items that are objectively *hekdesh* — sanctified for all persons. A *konam* creates only a subjective status, forbidding the item to the one who declared it but not to others. Can such a subjective prohibition generate *meilah*? This question drives the subsequent discussion.

## Combination Rule

### Baraita Rule

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* that presents a striking asymmetry: two *konamot* combine to reach the minimum threshold, while two *shevuot* do not combine. If a person says "*konam* this apple is forbidden to me in eating, and *konam* this apple is forbidden to me in eating," and eats a half-*kezayit* from each, the two amounts combine and he has violated his *konam*. But if a person says "*shevua* I will not eat this, and *shevua* I will not eat this," and eats a half-*kezayit* from each, the two amounts do not combine and he has not violated either oath. The question is why this difference exists.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir dissents from the *baraita*: he holds that *konamot* are like *shevuot* — presumably meaning they too do not combine. This requires explanation, since the logic behind each position is not immediately apparent. The Gemara explores whether Rabbi Meir means that *konamot* are like *shevuot* in that they do not combine, or whether the text should be reversed — that *shevuot* are like *konamot* and do combine.

### When Konamot Combine

The Gemara first establishes why *konamot* combine. Rav Pappa explained earlier that when one says *"konam"* without using the word *achila*, there is no minimum amount at all. But the *baraita* speaks of a case where one did use the word *achila* — "the eating of this is *konam* to me, and the eating of this is *konam* to me" — and therefore a *kezayit* is indeed required. The question then is: if each *konam* specifies a separate object, why should amounts from each combine? Eating a half-*kezayit* of the first apple did not violate the *konam* on the first apple, and eating a half-*kezayit* of the second apple did not violate the *konam* on the second apple. Where is the violation?

The Gemara resolves this: the person said "the eating of these two is *konam* to me" — formulating them as a single unit. Since both apples are grouped under one prohibition, any eating from either one, or from both together, counts toward that single prohibition, and the amounts combine.

### Oath Separation

Why, then, do *shevuot* not combine even when formulated similarly — "I swear I will not eat from this one and from this one"? Rav Pinchas explains that *shevuot* are subject to the rule of *chaluka lekorbanos* — they are divided into separate sin offerings. When one says "*shevua* I will not eat from this and from this," halacha treats this as two separate oaths, each governing its own object. The source is the verse *"vehaya ki yesham le'achas me'eleh"* — "and he shall be guilty regarding one of these" — from which the *Sifrei* derives that even a single formulation covering two items is reckoned as two distinct oaths. This means that eating a *kezayit* from each incurs two separate *korbanos*, as though one had violated two different prohibitions, like eating *chelev* and *dam*, which never combine.

### Rav Pinchas

The implication of Rav Pinchas's rule is that two *shevuot* covering different objects are always treated as entirely separate, regardless of how they were phrased. If one eats a half-*kezayit* from each, one has not completed the act required to violate either oath individually, and since they are separate they cannot combine. This is the fundamental structural difference between *konamot* and *shevuot*: a *konam* covering two items remains one *konam*; a *shevua* covering two items is treated as two *shevuot*.

Rabbi Meir's position, on the Gemara's revised reading, is that *shevuot* are like *konamot* — that is, *shevuot* do combine. Rabbi Meir does not deny that there may be two *korbanos* in principle, but he holds that the rule of *chaluka lekorbanos* does not preclude combination when assessing whether the minimum amount for liability has been reached.

### Ravina's Answer

Ravina offers an alternative approach to the entire question. He proposes that Rav Pappa's principle — that *konamot* require only a *kol shehu* — was stated specifically with regard to *malkus*, not with regard to a *korban*. The *baraita* that discusses combination, however, concerns a *korban*, not *malkus*. And what *korban* would be relevant to a *konam*? Since a *konam* borrows the language of *hekdesh*, the applicable *korban* is the *korban me'ila* — brought for improperly benefiting from consecrated property. A *korban me'ila* requires a *shaveh prutah*, not a *kezayit*. Therefore, the *baraita* teaches that two *konamot* combine to reach a *shaveh prutah*'s worth for the *korban me'ila*, while two *shevuot* do not combine to reach the *kezayit* needed for a *korban shevua*.

### Ravina Challenged

This interpretation requires that the *Rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Meir hold that there is *meilah* by *konamot* — otherwise there would be no *korban* to discuss. The Gemara therefore examines a *baraita* on this point.

### Konam vs. Hekdesh

The *baraita* distinguishes between two cases. If one says "this loaf is *hekdesh*," the item is objectively consecrated — both the person and others would transgress *meilah* by deriving benefit from it, and it can be redeemed, since it has genuine *hekdesh* status. If one says "this loaf is *hekdesh* for me" — a formulation that parallels *konam* — the status is subjective: the person himself transgresses *meilah* by eating it, but others do not, and it cannot be redeemed, since the *hekdesh* is not an objective metaphysical reality. This is Rabbi Meir's position. The *Chachamim*, however, hold that in the case of "this loaf is *hekdesh* for me," neither the person nor others transgress *meilah*, because the status is not sufficiently objective to constitute real *hekdesh*.

The problem is that the *Chachamim*'s position — no *meilah* for *konamot* — is the opposite of what Ravina's explanation of the *baraita* requires. Ravina needs the *Rabbanan* (who hold that *konamot* do combine) to affirm *meilah* by *konamot*, but the *Chachamim* here deny any *meilah* by *konamot*.

### Resolution

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by inverting the attribution in the *baraita* on *meilah*. It is Rabbi Meir who holds there is no *meilah* by *konamot* at all — and when Rabbi Meir says in the earlier *baraita* that *konamot* are like *shevuot* and do not combine, he is consistent: there is no *meilah*, hence no *korban*, hence no reason to speak of combination for a *korban* threshold. The *Chachamim*, on the other hand, hold that there is *meilah* for the person himself — "he transgresses, his fellow does not" — and it is therefore the *Rabbanan* who affirm combination for *konamot*, since combining to a *shaveh prutah* enables the *korban me'ila*.

When Rabbi Meir argues *ledivreihem* — within the *Rabbanan*'s framework — he says: even granted that you hold there is *meilah* by *konamot*, you should still agree that *konamot* are like *shevuot* and do not combine. The *Rabbanan* reject this, maintaining that the rule of *chaluka lekorbanos*, which prevents *shevuot* from combining, does not apply to *konamot*, which remain a single unified prohibition over all covered objects.

## Rava's Questions

### Dirt Measure

Returning now to the unresolved questions left earlier, the Gemara formally records Rava's question: *shevua shelo ochel afar — bechama?* If someone takes an oath not to eat dirt and then eats dirt, what is the operative measure? Is it a *kezayit*, because he used the word *ochel* — eating — which normally carries a halachic definition? Or is it a *kol shehu*, because dirt is not a normal foodstuff and therefore "eating" in this context cannot carry its standard halachic weight? The Gemara leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

### Grape Seeds

Rava then raises a second question: if someone takes a *shevua* not to eat *chartzan* — grape seeds — what is the measure? Two considerations pull in opposite directions. On one hand, grape seeds are eaten — they are consumed as part of a grape — and in that sense they belong to the category of normal food. This would suggest that "eating grape seeds" should be interpreted as a *kezayit*. On the other hand, people do not eat grape seeds on their own; they are not independently consumed as a food. This might place them in the same category as dirt — something not genuinely *bar achila* in the standalone sense — and the measure would be a *kol shehu*. This question, too, is left unresolved: *teiku*.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi poses a further question related to these themes: what is the law for a *nazir* who takes a *shevua* not to eat grape seeds? A *nazir* is already biblically prohibited from eating grape seeds. The *shevua* would therefore be redundant at the level of a *kezayit* — the Torah already forbids that. For the *shevua* to add anything meaningful, it would have to apply at the sub-*kezayit* level, a *kol shehu*. Should we therefore interpret the *nazir*'s oath as implicitly targeting the *kol shehu*, in order to give his words meaning?

### Non-Redundancy

The question is whether halacha adopts a principle of charitable or purposive interpretation — reading an oath to mean whatever it would take for the oath to be non-redundant and effective — or whether we simply take the word "eat" at face value, apply its standard measure, and accept that the oath turns out to be redundant and ineffective. This is a genuinely significant structural question about how *shevuot* are interpreted.

### Neveilah Biology

The Gemara brings a *tashma* to resolve the issue. The Mishnah teaches that one who takes a *shevua* "I will not eat" and then eats *neveilot* and *treifot* is liable. We have already seen this passage and the debate about *mushba ve'omeid*. But the Gemara now highlights Reish Lakish's position: the *shevua* takes effect on non-kosher food only if one explicitly specified "I will not eat a *chatzi shiur*," or, according to Rabbi Akiva, by default because *shevua* always means *kol shehu*. And crucially, Reish Lakish does not say: "since you're already prohibited from *neveilah* at the *kezayit* level, we will naturally interpret your *shevua* to mean *chatzi shiur* so that it should be meaningful." He does not do so. We require an explicit statement.

### Dirt Not Edible

This resolves Rav Ashi's question about the *nazir*. We do not adopt a principle of non-redundancy that would re-interpret an oath to give it effect. If a *nazir* says "I will not eat grape seeds" without specifying a smaller amount, his words mean a *kezayit*, and since he is already foresworn at that level, the *shevua* is redundant and does not take effect. The parallel to *neveilah* is direct: one who says "I will not eat *neveilah*" means a *kezayit*, and we do not creatively re-interpret the oath to save it from redundancy.

However, the Gemara distinguishes between *neveilah* and dirt precisely on the question of biological edibility. *Neveilah* is *bar achila* — it is genuinely edible in the physical sense. The vast majority of humanity eats it. There is, as the Gemara puts it, a lion crouching upon it — meaning the Torah is the sole external force prohibiting it — but the food itself is edible. Therefore, when one says "eat *neveilah*," one is speaking of eating something that is biologically food, and the standard *kezayit* measure applies. Dirt, by contrast, is *lav bar achila hu klal* — it is not edible at all as a biological matter. When one says "eat dirt," one is not invoking the halachic act of eating in any normal sense, and it remains unresolved what measure, if any, applies. The Gemara's *teiku* on dirt thus stands, while Rav Ashi's question about the *nazir* and grape seeds is answered: we read "eat" at its face value, do not rescue the oath from redundancy by creative interpretation, and the *shevua* of the *nazir* regarding grape seeds does not take effect. The Gemara closes here, leaving the continuation for the following Mishnah.
