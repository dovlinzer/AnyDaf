# Gittin 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bifnei Nichtav Requireme…

### Bifnei Nichtav Basics

The daf begins at the bottom of 6b, picking up a discussion about the practical scope of the *shaliach*'s obligation to declare *bifnei nichtav uvifnei nechtam* — that the *get* was written and signed in his presence. The Gemara had recently established that even within *medinat hayam* one must reassess circumstances: if the population is highly transient or signatures are not recognizable, the declaration is required. This leads Tosafot and the *rishonim* to conclude that this is one of those *takanot* which do not remain fixed but must be continuously reevaluated based on prevailing conditions.

### Ramban's Question

The Ramban raises a question that bears noting here. The Gemara's reassessment is framed specifically in terms of *medinat hayam*. The Ramban asks: could one ever imagine a scenario in which even in Eretz Yisrael — if conditions deteriorated to the point where Torah knowledge was scarce and travel was limited, as was the case in the time of the *rishonim* — one would be required to state *bifnei nichtav uvifnei nechtam*? Or do we say that the question of whether the declaration is needed simply never applies to Eretz Yisrael, which is always categorically exempt? This remains an open question that the Ramban raises.

### Bavel's Special Status

The Gemara has already granted Bavel a special status comparable to Eretz Yisrael, such that the *shaliach* need not declare *bifnei nichtav uvifnei nechtam* within Bavel, owing to the regularity of travel and the widespread familiarity with *lishmah*. A further ruling attributed to Rabbi Avyasar held that when traveling between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael the declaration is likewise unnecessary.

### Rabbi Avyasar Authority

This ruling prompted the Gemara to challenge Rabbi Avyasar's reliability. The critique rested on the observation that in a letter he wrote — a blistering denunciation of the practice in Bavel of leaving home, wife, and children to study in Eretz Yisrael — he quoted a *pasuk* without scoring the parchment beneath it. In a *Sefer Torah*, the letters are suspended from scored lines indented into the parchment above them; the letters hang from the scoring rather than resting upon it. Failing to score a *pasuk* when writing it was taken as evidence that Rabbi Avyasar did not know this *halacha*, and therefore his authority could be questioned. The Gemara responds, however, that the absence of this practice is not necessarily disqualifying — not everyone is expected to know it. Indeed, this is likely the origin of the English word "underscore": to place the scoring beneath. In a *Sefer Torah* the scoring appears above, but the general concept of marking beneath a passage to highlight it flows from the same tradition.

## Rabbi Avyasar Defens (II)

### Avyasar Divine Alignment

The Gemara adds a further defense of Rabbi Avyasar. The verse states: *vatizneh alav pilegsho* — his concubine acted faithlessly against him — which is the opening of the story of *pilegeish b'Givah*. Rabbi Avyasar interpreted *vatizneh* not as literal adultery but as referring to a fly (*z'vuv*) found in the food — the word *z'vuv* possibly connecting etymologically to *mezonot*, as Tosafot suggests. Rabbi Yonatan, by contrast, said he found a strand of hair (*nima*).

Rabbi Avyasar then encountered Eliyahu and asked: what is God occupied with at this moment? Eliyahu replied that God is engaged in the *sugya* of *pilegeish b'Givah*. And what does He say? *Avyasar bni kach hu omer* — My son Avyasar says thus; *Yonatan bni kach hu omer* — My son Yonatan says thus. Rabbi Avyasar recoiled: *chas v'shalom* — can there really be a doubt before Heaven? Eliyahu answered: *eilu v'eilu divrei Elokim chayim* — both are the words of the living God.

### Fly vs. Hair Debate

The Gemara then clarifies how both positions can be simultaneously correct: *hein z'vuv matza v'lo hikpid* — he found the fly and was not particularly disturbed; *nima matza v'hikpid* — but he found the hair and became angry. The fly was disgusting but understandable; the hair provoked a sharper reaction. Even though Rabbi Yonatan's position was ultimately more precise, Rabbi Avyasar was nonetheless aligned with something genuinely true, and God's engagement with his view demonstrates that he cannot simply be dismissed.

### Negligence Theory

The Gemara elaborates. Rav Yehuda teaches: *z'vuv b'ke'ara v'nima b'oto makom* — the fly was in the bowl of food, but the hair was found in an intimate location. Based on a certain passage in the Gemara, it appears there was a practice of removing pubic hair, and the presence of such a hair there indicated either a potential physical danger (*sakanta*) or that the matter had not been properly attended to. A second opinion holds that both were found in the food (*ika d'amri idy v'idy b'ke'ara*): the fly was an *ones* — an unavoidable accident, for what can one do about a fly landing in food — while the hair reflected negligence (*pshiyuta*). The husband's disproportionate anger over a strand of hair, or his fury at finding a fly, drove his *pilegesh* from the house and set in motion a catastrophe.

### Undue Fear Lesson

From here the Gemara draws a broader moral lesson. Rav Chisda teaches: *le'olam al yatil adam eimah yeteirah b'toch beito* — a person should never impose excessive fear upon his household. The husband of the *pilegeish b'Givah* imposed such fear upon her, and as a result many myriads of Israel fell in the wars that followed.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav extends this further: anyone who imposes excessive fear upon his household will ultimately come to three sins — *gilui arayot*, *shfichut damim*, and *chilul Shabbat*. Rashi explains the mechanism of each. *Gilui arayot*: the wife, afraid to admit that she did not immerse in the *mikvah* because the water was freezing cold — and one should not underestimate how arduous those immersions were historically, descending into deep wells in the dead of night in darkness and bitter cold — remains silent and her husband unknowingly cohabits with her while she is a *niddah*. *Shfichut damim*: as happened here, she flees from him in terror, falls into a pit or off a bridge, and dies. Or, as Rashi says, it is what the *pilegeish b'Givah* story itself exemplifies. *Chilul Shabbat*: she did not light candles and did not cook before nightfall, and out of fear of his reaction she lights the candles and cooks after dark — and he eats the food cooked on Shabbat without knowing it was forbidden.

Tosafot notes that *Seder Olam* records that the day of *pilegeish b'Givah* was itself a Shabbat, which would connect *chilul Shabbat* directly to that narrative. Rabbeinu Chananel has a variant reading: the three sins are *gilui arayot*, *shfichut damim*, and *chilul Hashem* — noting that *chillul Shabbat* and *chillul Hashem* are nearly the same word, and that all three were indeed present in the *pilegeish b'Givah* story.

It is striking that this *aggadata* appears at the opening of *Masechet Gittin*, a tractate deeply concerned with the husband's unilateral power over the *get* and the need to protect the wife's interests. From the very beginning the Gemara raises the specter of the husband who manufactures claims to make the wife's life impossible — and here too the story presents a husband whose disproportionate anger over a fly or a hair sets an entire catastrophe in motion.

## Domestic Ethics (III)

### Pre-Shabbat Statements

Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the Rabbis have said that a man must say three things in his household — to his wife — just as it begins to grow dark on *erev Shabbat*: *issartem* — have you taken *ma'aser*? *eiravtem* — have you made the *eiruv chatzeiros* or *eiruv techumin* (which is itself a dispute between Rashi and Tosafot)? *hadliku es haner* — light the candles. These three must be said *benichusa* — pleasantly, gently, without anger or yelling. The purpose is *ki heichi delikablu minei* — so that the members of the household will actually listen and act accordingly, rather than acting out of fear or being driven to sin precisely through the harshness of the tone.

### Abaye's Derivation

Abaye adds: *ana lo shmiyena le'ha de'Rabbah bar bar Chana* — I never heard that teaching — *v'kiyamtiyah misvara* — yet I arrived at the same conclusion independently through my own reasoning.

## Rabbi Avyasar Defens (II)

### Rabbi Chanina's Fear

Rabbi Abbahu continues the theme: *le'olam al yatil adam eimah yeseirah besoch beiso* — a person should never impose excessive fear upon his household. A great man once had his household so terrified of him that they nearly fed him something of enormous forbidden consequence: *eiver min hachai*, a limb torn from a living animal, because circumstances forced an emergency and they were too afraid to tell him. The Gemara asks: is it really possible that God would allow a *tzaddik* to stumble into such a sin? After all, the Gemara teaches elsewhere that *behemtan shel tzaddikim* — the very animals of the righteous — are protected by God from causing them to stumble, as with the donkey of Pinchas ben Yair, which was prevented from eating produce from which *terumos u'ma'asros* had not been separated. How much more so would God protect the *tzaddikim* themselves?

The answer: *ela bikshu le'ha'achilo davar gadol* — they sought to feed him, they were on the verge of it, but did not actually succeed. God intervened. The great man in question is identified as Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel.

Tosafot offers an important clarification here. It is not that *tzaddikim* can never stumble into sin. Rather, there is a specific tradition regarding food — *de'gnai hu le'tzaddik she'yochal davar issur* — that it would be a particular disgrace for a *tzaddik* to have a forbidden substance actually enter his body. It is for this reason, Tosafot explains, that the Gemara invokes divine protection specifically in cases of accidentally consuming *treif* food, while not making the same argument for other inadvertent transgressions. This concern connects to a broader sensibility about the singular gravity of what enters the body: the idea that feeding even an infant non-*kosher* food before the age of *chinuch* carries a kind of spiritual weight, the tradition of *Moshe Rabbeinu* refusing to nurse from an Egyptian woman, and the considerable hesitation found in various *teshuvot* regarding commerce in non-*kosher* goods, even in the difficult circumstances of the medieval period.

## Silence vs. Adversaries

### Mar Ukva's Question

Mar Ukva sent a question to Rabbi Elazar: *bnei adam ha'omedim alai* — there are people who stand against me and cause me harm — *u'veyadi le'mosram la'malchut* — and I have the means to hand them over to the government, as I possess information about them. What may I do? This is, of course, the question of *mesirah*. He wants to know whether protecting himself through this means is permitted.

### Guard Your Mouth

Rabbi Elazar scored the parchment and wrote him the following verse — and here the Gemara notes again the practice of scoring before writing a *pasuk*: *Amarti eshmerah derachai me'chato bi'leshoni, eshmerah le'fi machsom be'od rasha le'negdi* — I said I would guard my path from sinning with my tongue; I will watch my mouth, keeping it muzzled, while the wicked stands against me. Even though the wicked person is arrayed against you, guard your mouth and do not speak out against him.

### Anguish Concern

Mar Ukva wrote back: *ka mitza'ari li tuva* — he causes me tremendous anguish — *v'la mitzina de'eikum behu* — and I have no other means of protecting myself against him.

### Divine Silence Path

Rabbi Elazar wrote back again, scoring and writing: *dom l'Hashem v'hitcholel lo* — be silent to God and long for Him. Do not speak out; remain silent; God will cause them to fall, *v'hu yapilem lecha chalalim chalalim*. And if you wish to do something constructive: *hashkem v'haarev aleihem le'veit ha'midrash* — rise early and stay late over them at the *Beit ha'Midrash* — *v'inan kalin me'aleihen* — and they will disappear on their own. Tosafot connects *dom* here to *dimdumei chamah*, the early and late light of dawn and dusk, as a reading of *hashkem v'haarev*.

### Gneiva's Punishment

The Gemara then records: *ha'davar yatza mi'pi Rabbi Elazar v'natnuhu le'Gneiva be'kolar* — the matter had barely left the mouth of Rabbi Elazar when Gneiva — whose very name is suggestive — was put in chains. The parallel to *Haman chapu* — the moment in *Megillat Esther* when Haman was immediately seized as the words left the king's mouth — is notable. God acts swiftly when the righteous are silent and trust in divine justice.

## Music Prohibition

### Music Source

Mar Ukva was then asked a question: *zimra minalan de'assir* — from where do we know that playing music is forbidden? He scored parchment and wrote: *al tismach Yisrael el gil ba'amim* — do not rejoice, O Israel, in the rejoicing of nations — particularly after the *Churban ha'Bayit*. This verse was written even while the first Temple still stood, by the prophet Hoshea.

### Voice vs. Instrument

The Gemara asks: why did he not instead send the verse *be'shir lo yishtu yayin, yemar shechar le'shotav* — they shall not drink wine with song; the drink shall be bitter to those who drink it? The answer: had he sent that verse alone, one might have thought the prohibition applies only to *zimra de'mana* — instrumental music — while *de'puma*, vocal singing, would be permitted. By sending the verse from Hoshea instead, the Gemara establishes *ka mashma lan* — it teaches us — that even purely vocal music is included in the prohibition.

### Drinking Houses Context

Tosafot clarifies what is actually prohibited. Rashi explains that the prohibition is specifically directed at *shir be'veit ha'mishta'ot* — singing in drinking establishments. This reading is supported by the Gemara's very choice to ask why the verse about wine was not cited, and by the *mishnah* at the end of *Masechet Sotah* which states: *mishe'batla Sanhedrin batel ha'shir be'veit ha'mishta'ot* — once the Sanhedrin was abolished, song in the drinking houses was abolished. Tosafot notes, however, that the Yerushalmi speaks of one who rises and falls asleep to music — *dehavei kaei u'damich be'zimra* — suggesting the prohibition extends beyond the drinking house context to music used purely for pleasure and self-indulgence. *Shir shel mitzvah shari* — music in the context of a *mitzvah*, such as at a *chuppah* to gladden the groom and bride, or the recitation of *zemirot*, remains permitted. This area generated considerable literature among the *rishonim* and *poskim* regarding precisely where the prohibition applies and how strictly it is to be observed, with added complexity today given the difference between live and recorded music.

## Music Prohibition (II)

### Rabbinic Origin

Rav Huna bar Rav Natan asked Rav Ashi the meaning of the *pasuk* in *Yehoshua* listing the cities *Kinah, Dimonah, v'Ad'adah*. Rav Ashi replied that these are simply the cities of Eretz Yisrael being enumerated. Rav Huna responded that of course he knew they were cities — he was looking for a *derasha*. Rav Geviha from Argiza, he said, would offer one: *kol sheyesh lo kinah al chaveiro v'domeh* — anyone who has a grievance against his fellow and yet remains silent — *Kinah v'Dimonah* — and is silent, *shochney adei ad oseh lo din* — the One who dwells forever will execute judgment for him. This connects directly to the story of Gneiva: remain silent, do not hand him over to the government, and God will act. Rav Ashi then challenged Rav Huna bar Rav Natan to extend the *derasha* to the next verse: *Tziklag, Madmannah, v'Sansannah*. He replied that if Rav Geviha were present, he would have a *derasha* for that too. Rav Acha from Bei Choza'ah then supplied one: *kol mi sheyesh lo tza'akas legima al chaveiro* — anyone who has a cry of *legima* against his fellow, meaning his livelihood has been compromised to the point where he cannot put food on the table — *v'domeh* — and is silent and does not attack — *shocheyn ba'sneh oseh lo din* — the One who dwells in the bush will come to his defense.

## Wedding Crowns

### Crown Source

Reish Galuta asked Rav Huna: *kelila minalan de'assur* — from where do we know that the crown worn by a *chassan* at his wedding is forbidden? Rav Huna answered: it is *miderabbanan* — rabbinic — citing the explicit *mishnah* in *Masechet Sotah*: *be'fulmus shel Aspasyanos gazru al ataros chassanim v'al ha'irus* — in the wars of Vespasian they decreed against the crowns of grooms and against the drums.

### Mitre Crown Link

While Rav Huna stepped away momentarily, Rav Chisda was left with Reish Galuta and offered a biblical grounding. He cited the verse in *Yechezkel*: *koh amar Hashem Elokim, hasir ha'mitznefet v'hareim ha'atarah* — thus says the Lord God, remove the mitre and lift off the crown. *Zos lo zos* — this, not even this. *Ha'shpalah ha'gavoha v'ha'gavoha ha'shpil* — raise the lowly and bring down the exalted. What is the connection between the *mitznefet*, the High Priest's mitre, and the *atarah*, the crown? The verse teaches: *bizman she'mitznefet b'rosh kohen gadol, atarah b'rosh kol adam* — when the *mitznefet* rests upon the head of the *Kohen Gadol*, when the *Beit ha'Mikdash* stands, then crowns may rest upon the heads of all people at their weddings. *Nistalka mitznefet me'rosh kohen gadol, nistalka atarah me'rosh kol adam* — but once the *mitznefet* has been removed from the head of the *Kohen Gadol*, once the *Churban* has occurred, no one may wear such a crown.

### Huna's Objection

When Rav Huna returned and was told what Rav Chisda had said, he declared emphatically: *ha'elohim, miderabbanan* — by God, it is rabbinic, not a *pasuk*. The connection to *Yechezkel* is a support and a beautiful one, but it does not transform this into a biblical prohibition. He then added graciously: *ella chizer shimcha v'chazyan milach* — your name indeed suits you and your words are very pleasing. The point stands: the verse provides an *asmachta* and a conceptual underpinning, but the prohibition itself is a rabbinic decree. Tosafot notes that Rashi understood the *atarah* as a literal metal crown, but Tosafot challenges this from the first chapter of *Sotah*, which indicates the crowns in question were made of roses or myrtle — that is, wreaths.

### Braiding Wreath

Ravina found Mar bar Rav Ashi braiding a wreath for his daughter for her wedding — an *ateret kallah*, a crown for the bride. Ravina challenged him: does the master not hold by the verse *hasir ha'mitznefet v'hareim ha'atarah*, which would seem to prohibit crowns for women as well?

### Gender Distinction

Mar bar Rav Ashi replied: *dumya d'kohen gadol b'chasanei* — the prohibition is drawn by analogy to the *Kohen Gadol*, and therefore applies only to grooms (*chasanim*), not to women (*benashi lo*). Tosafot elaborates: what is prohibited for *kallos* according to the *mishnah* in *Sotah* is specifically an *ir shel zahav* — a golden tiara — while a wreath made of natural materials is a different matter entirely. The prohibition on the *kallah* wearing an *ir shel zahav* when she goes out in public derives from the general laws of jewelry on Shabbat treated in the chapter *bameh ishah*, not from the specific decree about wedding crowns — and even there, the prohibition is limited to the wedding context out of concern for *simcha yeteirah*, excessive festivity.

## Angels Plea Derasha

### Angels' Argument

Rav Avira expounded — sometimes citing the tradition in the name of Rav Ami, sometimes in the name of Rav Assi — on the phrase *zos lo zos*. When God declared to Israel *hasir ha'mitznefet v'hareim ha'atarah*, the ministering angels, the *malachei ha'shareis*, spoke before God: *Ribbono shel Olam, zos lahem l'Yisrael she'hikdimu lefanecha b'Sinai na'aseh v'nishma* — is this what Israel deserves, the very people who preceded *nishma* with *na'aseh* at Sinai?

### Sinai Connection

This is deeply evocative. The *midrashim* describe how at Sinai, when Israel declared *na'aseh v'nishma*, the *malachei ha'shareis* descended and crowned each Jew with two crowns — one for *na'aseh* and one for *nishma*. When they subsequently sinned with the *eigel*, the verse records *vayisnazlu b'nei Yisrael es adyam me'Har Choreiv* — Israel stripped off their ornaments from Har Choreiv — which the *midrashim* interpret as the removal of those very crowns. Here, in discussing the removal of the wedding crowns on account of the *Churban*, the *malachei ha'shareis* invoke the merit of *na'aseh v'nishma* as a reason to retain them.

### Crowns Removal Reason

God's response to the angels: *lo zos im zos* — is it not this very Israel who *hishpila es ha'gavoha v'higbiha es ha'shafel* — who brought low what was exalted and raised up what was lowly? They engaged in *avodah zarah*, they brought down the glory of the *Beit ha'Mikdash*, and they even erected an image in the *Heichal*. Therefore the crowns must be removed.

## Livelihood Derasha

### Limited Livelihood

Rav Avira expounded again — sometimes in the name of Rav Ami, sometimes in the name of Rav Assi — on the verse: *koh amar Hashem im shleimim v'chen rabbim v'chen nagozu v'avar*. If a person sees that his livelihood is *mtzumtzamin* — limited and barely sufficient — he should give *tzedakah* from it; and all the more so if he has abundant resources. The word *nagozu* is the key: it suggests shearing, and from it the Gemara derives the obligation to give charity even from constrained means.

## Domestic Ethics (III)

### Charity Derasha

*Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* teaches: anyone who shears from his property — *kol ha'gozez mi'nechasav* — and gives *tzedakah* from it, is saved from the judgment of *Gehennom*, and the word *v'avar* — he passes through — indicates passage to safety.

---

## Livelihood Derasha (II)

### Shearing Property

*Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* continues: anyone who shears from his property and gives *tzedakah* is saved from the judgment of *Gehennom*.

### Sheep Parable

This is illustrated through a parable of two sheep crossing a body of water. One is shorn and one is not. The shorn sheep passes through without difficulty; the unshorn sheep is weighed down by its waterlogged wool and cannot cross. So too: the one who has given away his wealth is unencumbered and can pass through, while the one who has hoarded it all is dragged down.

## Domestic Ethics (III)…

### Poor Giving Charity

The verse concludes *v'iniisicha* — and I have afflicted you. Mar Zutra teaches: *afilu ani ha'misparnes min ha'tzedakah ya'aseh tzedakah* — even a poor person who himself subsists on charity must give *tzedakah*. He too should recognize the blessing of whatever he possesses and share it with those in even greater need.

### Poverty Elimination

Rav Yosef teaches from the word *oni* in *lo a'aneich od* — I will no longer afflict you: *shuv ein marin lo simanei aniyus* — one who gives *tzedakah*, even from poverty, will no longer be shown the signs of poverty. With this concludes the lengthy *aggadata* that began from the attempt to vindicate Rabbi Avyasar and the principle that one need not declare *bifnei nichtav* when traveling between Bavel and *chutz la'aretz*.

## Eretz Yisrael Borders

### Yehuda's Boundaries

The Gemara now turns to the question of the boundaries of Eretz Yisrael as they pertain to *bifnei nichtav*. It is worth noting at the outset — and Tosafot's extensive discussion earlier in the *perek* addresses this — that the question of boundaries here might either reflect a completely independent definition tailored to the concerns of *get* delivery and *lishmah*, or it might assume that the boundaries used for *terumos u'ma'asros* and *kedushah* apply here as well. As will become clear, this *sugya* appears to adopt the latter approach, at least as a working framework.

Rabbi Yehuda sets the boundaries: from Rekem in the east, Ashkelon in the south, and Akko in the north.

### Tithes Boundaries

These boundaries map onto the standard halakhic definitions used for the laws of *terumot u'ma'asrot* and *shevi'it*. The Gemara appears to assume, or at least explore, the premise that the same area defined as Eretz Yisrael for those purposes defines it for the laws of *gittin* as well — not necessarily because they are identical definitions by principle, but because the areas of settlement with *kedushat Eretz Yisrael* and the areas relevant to *bifnei nichtav* can be assumed to overlap unless otherwise demonstrated.

### Akko vs. Kziv

The Gemara raises a difficulty: if Akko is the northern boundary of Eretz Yisrael, how do we account for the following *baraita*? One who walks from Akko northward to Kziv — with Kziv being further north than Akko — is told that the road to his east (*mi'yemino le'mizrach*, which confirms he is walking northward) is *tamei mishum Eretz ha'Amim* — impure as foreign territory — and exempt from *ma'aser* and *shevi'it*, while the road to his west is *tehorah* and *chayevet b'ma'aser u'v'shevi'it*, until Kziv is reached. This implies that the northern boundary extends to Kziv — beyond Akko. The traveler is walking north from Akko; to his right is the east, which is outside Eretz Yisrael; to his left is the west, a narrow strip between the road and the Mediterranean shore, which retains the sanctity of Eretz Yisrael. The picture becomes clearer when one considers the geography: Akko sits on the sea; the coastal path to Kziv runs along the shore, and to the left of the traveler is indeed a strip of land between the path and the sea that remains within the borders of Eretz Yisrael.

### Strip Explanation

Abaye answers: *retzu'ah nafka* — a small strip extends northward. The primary and substantial settlement of Eretz Yisrael ends at Akko; from Akko to Kziv there is only a narrow coastal strip remaining. When we define the northern border as Akko, we are identifying where the *ikkar yishuv* — the main inhabited region — ends. The strip beyond, while technically part of Eretz Yisrael, is too marginal to serve as the defining boundary marker. Tosafot raises the question of whether it is appropriate to devote an entire *mishnah* to describing the halakhic status of such a narrow strip. The Gemara answers that even a path can serve as a geographic marker, as demonstrated by a verse in the book of *Shoftim* that gives directions to Shiloh by reference to a road — *li'msila ha'olah mi'Beit El Shechema* — and Rav Pappa confirms that *lemizrachah shel mesila* means precisely to the east of the path, with the path itself serving as the boundary marker.

The underlying tension throughout this *sugya* is worth noting: some discussions in the first *perek* tailor the definition of Eretz Yisrael specifically to the concerns of *get* delivery — familiarity with signatures, reliability of witnesses, knowledge of *lishmah* — while this *sugya* appears to simply borrow the standard definitions from *terumot u'ma'asrot*. These two approaches do not seem to be in direct dialogue with one another.

## Get by Boat

### Boat Equivalence

The Gemara presents two contradictory *beraitot*. One teaches: *ha'meivi get bi'sfina kemevi b'Eretz Yisrael* — one who brings a *get* in a boat is like one who brings it in Eretz Yisrael, and need not declare *bifnei nichtav*. The other teaches: it is like one who brings it in *chutz la'aretz*, and the declaration is required. The case envisions the *get* being written or delivered while the *shaliach* is aboard a boat, without having disembarked — the question being whether his location counts as Eretz Yisrael or not.

### Foreign Soil Dispute

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by invoking a parallel dispute regarding *terumot u'ma'asrot*: the case of foreign soil brought in a boat into Eretz Yisrael — *afar chutz la'aretz haba bi'sfina l'aretz* — with something growing from that soil aboard the boat. Is the produce *chayav b'ma'aser u'v'shevi'it*? This is a dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rashi's Interpretation

Rashi understands the Gemara's case as involving a boat in one of the rivers of Eretz Yisrael — not on the open sea. The *get* was written in the boat while it was in such a river. The question is whether a boat in a river is considered "in Eretz Yisrael" in the relevant sense: *k'ilu mivi derech yavasha* — as if he were bringing it overland — or whether *ha'nahar eino tor yavasha l'chol toras ha'aretz* — the river does not count as dry land for all purposes of the Land's laws.

### Scraping Bottom

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *emasy? Bizman she'ha'sfina gosheshes* — when does the boat count as being in Eretz Yisrael? Only when the boat is scraping (*gosheshes*) along the riverbed — that is, when there is physical contact between the boat and the ground of Eretz Yisrael. In that case, the boat is considered connected to the land and the produce is *chayav*. If the boat is not scraping the bottom, the produce is *patur*.

Rabbi Yirmiah resolves the contradiction between the two *beraitot* accordingly: one *beraita* reflects the view of the *Tanna Kamma*, who holds that a boat in Eretz Yisrael is treated as part of the land regardless, while the other reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position, which requires contact with the riverbed. Abaye sharpens this: the two *beraitot* correspond to the two conditions — when the boat is scraping the bottom, and when it is not.

### Perforated Pot

Rabbi Zeira draws a comparison to a *perforated pot* (*atzitz nakuv*) suspended above the ground: just as there is a debate about whether such a pot, with its contents disconnected from the ground by air space, retains the agricultural sanctity of the land (*palugta de'Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbanan*), so too the boat case mirrors that same structural question. The boat, like the *atzitz nakuv*, may be treated as porous — either literally, if Rashi's reading of the boat as made of pottery is accepted, or conceptually — such that contact with the ground below determines its status.

### Boat Movement

Rava draws a distinction between the two cases. One cannot simply assume they are parallel. Rabbi Yehuda's condition of *gosheshes* may apply specifically to a boat — *sefina ha'asuya livroach* — because a boat is designed to move; it is never truly settled in place. Since it is inherently mobile, the only moment when it can be treated as part of the land is when it is actively dragging along the bottom and thus physically connected. But an *atzitz* that stays in one location — *she'eino asui livroach* — Rabbi Yehuda might treat differently, granting it the status of connection to the land even without direct contact.

Alternatively, Rava suggests, the *Rabbanan* themselves might distinguish between the cases in the opposite direction. When they said the boat counts as part of Eretz Yisrael even without contact, their reasoning was that water is not truly a separation: *demaya de'ar'a semichta damya* — water is like thick, dense ground, still physically continuous with the earth beneath. Air, however, is a genuine separation; an *atzitz* suspended above ground by an air gap might be treated differently even by the *Rabbanan*. The irony throughout this entire *sugya* — and it is worth noting — is that while the debate about foreign soil in a boat is technically a dispute in the laws of *terumos u'ma'asros*, the Gemara is applying its resolution directly to the question of *get* delivery, even though the conceptual concerns underlying *bifnei nichtav* — familiarity with witnesses, knowledge of *lishmah* — bear no obvious relationship to whether a boat's hull is scraping the riverbed.
