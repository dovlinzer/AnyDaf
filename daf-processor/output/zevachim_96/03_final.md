# Zevachim 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blia'ah vs. Bishul

### Iruy & Blia'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רָבִּי זֵירָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין כִּבְשׁוֹנוֹת בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** The pots cannot be returned to kilns **because,** as taught in a *baraita* (see *Bava Kamma* 82b), **kilns are not built in Jerusalem** because of the great quantity of smoke they produce.

The daf opens by returning to the conceptual distinction explored in the previous session. The Torah prescribes two distinct responses when a *chattat* is cooked in a vessel: a pottery vessel must be broken, while a metal vessel must be scalded and rinsed (*merika u'shtifa*). The question at stake is whether these rituals are triggered by the vessel's *absorption* of the sacrificial food's taste (*blia'ah*), or merely by the act of *cooking* (*bishul*) having taken place within it.

A preliminary case was considered: what if one pours boiling hot liquid from a different pot into a vessel, a process known as *iruy*? The working assumption is that *iruy* does not constitute *bishul* — it is not cooking *in* that vessel, a point with direct implications for laws of *Shabbos* and *basar b'chalav*. Yet *iruy* does produce *blia'ah*: the taste is absorbed into the vessel's walls. So the case demonstrates that *blia'ah* can occur without *bishul*.

### Oven: No Blia'ah

The converse case follows: what if food is suspended inside an oven, cooking in the ambient heat without actually touching the walls? The *Gemara*'s assumption — significant for discussions of oven use generally — is that in such a scenario there is *bishul* but no *blia'ah*. The food is cooked, but the vessel's walls absorb nothing. This question was left unresolved in the prior session.

### Pottery Oven Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַעְבֵּיד הֶסֵּקָה מִבִּפְנִים! חָיֵיס עֲלַיְיהוּ, דְּמִתַּבְרִי. הִילְכָּךְ, הַאי כּוּבְיָא – הֶסֵּיקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ הוּא, וַאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let us** also **perform** the **kindling** of the pot **from the inside,** in order to cleanse that which has been absorbed. The Gemara answers: This solution is not feasible; the owners of such pots might be **concerned for them, as they** are apt to **break** if the heat becomes too great. Consequently, the owners will not apply sufficient heat to ensure that the absorbed flavor will be completely cleansed. The Gemara concludes: **Therefore,** with regard to **this earthenware tile [*kuvya*],** which is used on the fire as a baking pan and **its kindling is from the outside,** it becomes **prohibited** for subsequent use by the flavors absorbed within, which cannot be cleansed.

This leads to a crucial observation: if food is actually cooked inside a pottery oven — touching the walls — can that oven be *kashered*? The *Gemara* establishes that yes, in principle it can: by introducing fire to the inside of the oven, one can purge the absorbed taste, in a manner analogous to a kiln. This is distinct from a standard pottery pot, where introducing internal fire is impractical and would be done so poorly that the Rabbis do not rely on it. In an oven, however, where the fire naturally burns on the inside, this constitutes a legitimate mode of *kashering* pottery.

## Pottery Breaking

### Kasher vs. Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קְדֵירוֹת שֶׁל מִקְדָּשׁ, אַמַּאי אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא יִשָּׁבְרוּ? נַהְדְּרִינְהוּ לְכִבְשׁוֹנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** according to the opinion that earthenware vessels can be cleansed of their absorbed substances by the process of kindling, with regard to **pots** used in **the Temple, why does the Merciful One state** in the Torah that **they should be broken? Let us** simply **return them to** the **kilns** in which pots are made to be sure that the pots will be cleansed by the extreme heat of the kilns.

It is important to distinguish two layers of this question. One is practical: why, in the *Beis Hamikdash*, did the *Kohanim* not simply kiln-fire their pottery vessels rather than break them? The other is textual: why does the Torah itself prescribe only breaking, without offering kiln-firing as an alternative? These require separate answers.

### Rashi: Blia'ah Model

Before addressing the practical answer, a prior difficulty must be resolved. Even if we accept that kiln-firing can *kasher* pottery, who says that *kashering* is what the Torah demands here at all? The Torah prescribes a specific ritual — scalding and rinsing for metal, breaking for pottery. Perhaps these are self-contained rituals that do not admit of substitution, even if the underlying concern is absorption.

Rashi addresses this by reading the prior discussion as having established a definitive conclusion: the operative principle is *blia'ah*. Since the ritual is fundamentally anchored in the idea of absorption — the vessel absorbed the sanctified taste and must therefore be treated — any method that genuinely removes or nullifies that absorption should, in principle, suffice. Rashi accordingly reads the *Gemara*'s question as coherent: since the whole system is about *blia'ah* and *kashering*, a kiln is a legitimate mode of *kashering*, and the *Gemara* is right to ask why it was not used.

This also explains why the Torah's ritual of scalding and rinsing for metal is understood not as an arbitrary requirement but as a method of *kashering*. And it explains why, for pottery, the Torah says to break: since breaking is sufficient when one will not reuse the vessel — the absorbed taste remains in the walls, but it no longer matters — the Torah allows breaking as a practical solution. The core demand remains the neutralization of the absorbed taste.

### Tosafot: Include Bishul

*Tosafot* is not prepared to accept that the earlier discussion definitively resolved the question about cooking without absorption. It remains possible, in *Tosafot*'s view, that even food cooked in a vessel without touching the walls — the suspended-in-an-oven scenario — triggers the same ritual obligation.

Yet *Tosafot* reaches a similar conclusion to Rashi regarding the kiln question. Even if that case is included in the ritual's scope, *Tosafot* argues that it is included only because the conceptual model remains *blia'ah*. It is analogous to the principle of *shomeia k'oneh* — listening counts as speaking, but the model is still speaking, and listening is subsumed into it. Cooking without absorption is included *as if* it were absorption, because the governing model is absorption. Since the model is absorption, *kashering* is the operative concept, and a kiln — which genuinely *kashers* pottery — would in principle suffice.

*Tosafot* thus agrees with Rashi that the system is fundamentally about absorption and *kashering*, even while disagreeing that cooking without absorption is exempt from the ritual. The different treatment of pottery and metal in the Torah itself — breaking versus scalding — proves the point: these distinct rituals only make sense in light of the different absorption properties of the two materials. Pottery absorbs more deeply and cannot be un-absorbed through the usual means; metal can be scalded. This material distinction confirms that the underlying concern is absorption throughout.

*Tosafot* further offers a third reading: returning pottery to a kiln is tantamount to making it into a completely new vessel. Even if one were to argue that the ritual is not strictly about *kashering*, kiln-firing so thoroughly reconstitutes the vessel that the old vessel — with its absorbed taste — simply ceases to exist as a halachic object. On this view too, the *Gemara*'s question holds.

### No Kilns in Yerushalayim

For the practical question — why did the *Beis Hamikdash* not simply kiln-fire its pottery vessels — the *Gemara* provides a direct answer. Rav Zeira states: *l'fi she'ein osin kivshonos b'Yerushalayim* — kilns are not operated in Yerushalayim. The smoke and pollution generated by kilns was deemed incompatible with the sanctity and cleanliness of the city, and so no kilns were maintained there. Since *kashering* pottery by kiln required kilns that were simply not present in Yerushalayim, it was not a practical option, and the vessels were broken instead.

### Breaking as Leniency

Regarding the textual question — why did the Torah not list kiln-firing as an option — Rashi explains that the Torah's prescription was written in the context of the wilderness period, when the people were traveling through *Sinai* and had no fixed kilns. Kiln-firing was therefore simply not a viable option at the time of the Torah's writing, and so it was not enumerated.

*Tosafot*, however, offers a strikingly different reading. *Tosafot* argues that the Torah's instruction to break pottery is not a *stringency* but actually a *leniency* — and this is counterintuitive. If the whole point is to eliminate the absorbed taste from the world, then merely breaking the pot does not accomplish that. The taste remains embedded in the shards. One might think the Torah would demand that the taste be actively extracted and destroyed. By telling us that *breaking suffices*, the Torah is being lenient: you need not actually extract the taste; destroying the vessel as a functional object is enough. On this reading, kiln-firing is an even more complete solution and remains implicitly permitted — the Torah is simply giving us the minimum sufficient act.

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי עוֹשִׂין אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת בָּעֲזָרָה?! אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּתָנֵי שְׁמַעְיָה בְּקַלְנְבוֹ: שִׁבְרֵי כְּלֵי חֶרֶס נִבְלָעִין בִּמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an objection to Rabbi Zeira's answer. **Abaye said: But** if, as the *baraita* teaches, there are no kilns in Jerusalem, **are scrap heaps** of earthenware **assembled in the Temple courtyard?** The same *baraita* also teaches that there are no scrap heaps in Jerusalem. What, then, is done with the shards of earthenware vessels that must be broken in the courtyard? The Gemara dismisses the question: Abaye raised that objection only because **that which Shemaya taught in Kalnevo escaped him;** Shemaya taught there: In the Temple, **shards of earthenware vessels were** miraculously **absorbed in their place.**

At this point, Abaye raises a further difficulty against the practical explanation. If the reason the *Beis Hamikdash* did not kiln-fire its pottery was to avoid kilns, why were the vessels broken inside the *Azarah*? Breaking pottery creates shards — a mess entirely unbefitting the sacred courtyard. Surely a small kiln inside the *Azarah* would be less objectionable than leaving shards strewn about.

### Shards Absorbed

The *Gemara* answers Abaye's question by citing a teaching of Rav Shmaya: *shivrei klei cheres nivlaim bimkonam* — when pottery vessels were broken in the Temple, the shards were miraculously absorbed into the ground on the spot. No mess, no shards lying about. This miracle resolved the practical difficulty. (One might add that even without invoking the miraculous, a continuous kiln operation is more disruptive than the occasional sweeping away of shards, but the *Gemara* rests its answer on the miracle.)

## Metal vs. Pottery

### Metal Oven in Mikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: תַּנּוּר שֶׁל מִקְדָּשׁ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת הֲוָה – נֶעְבֵּיד דְּחֶרֶס, דְּהֶסֵּיקוֹ מִבְּפָנִים הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the topic of kindling earthenware vessels and asks: **But** if kindling from within cleanses everything absorbed in an earthenware oven, what is the reason for **that which Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: The oven in the Temple was** fashioned **of metal? Let us fashion** it **of earthenware, as** an oven's **kindling is from the inside,** and, accordingly, it would be possible to cleanse it?

A related ruling now comes into view. Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, states: *tanur shel Mikdash shel mateshes hayah* — the oven of the Temple was made of metal, not pottery.

### Why Metal Only?

The question this raises is immediate. Given everything just established — that a pottery oven *can* be *kashered* by virtue of the fire burning on its inside — why not use a pottery oven? When the *chattat* or other meat is cooked in it, simply fire the oven at high temperature and the *kashering* is accomplished without breaking anything. There is no need to maintain a separate kiln; the oven itself provides the interior fire that constitutes the *kashering*. Why, then, did the Temple specifically require a metal oven?

### Kli Shares Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִיכָּא שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים – דַּאֲפִיָּיתָן בְּתַנּוּר וּקְדוּשָּׁתָן בְּתַנּוּר; הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּלִי שָׁרֵת, וּכְלִי שָׁרֵת דְּחֶרֶס לָא עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The reason the oven must be fashioned of metal is **because there are the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering on *Shavuot* of two loaves from the new wheat, **and the shewbread,** i.e., the bread baked each week in a special form and displayed for the duration of one whole week on the table in the Sanctuary, **whose baking is** done **in the oven, and** also **whose sanctification** occurs **in the oven.** Because these offerings are not kneaded in a service vessel, they are sanctified only by being placed in the oven, and therefore the oven **is a service vessel; and we do not make a service vessel of earthenware.**

The answer is that the oven's function was not limited to cooking sacrificial meat. Certain communal offerings — the *shtei halechem*, the *lechem hapanim*, and the *minchas ma'afeh tanur* — are baked directly in the oven, and the oven itself serves as their *kli shareis* (sacred utensil). Unlike other meal offerings that are mixed with oil in a separate *kli shareis* before baking, these breads are sanctified by being placed into the oven. The oven is therefore not merely a cooking appliance but the sacred vessel through which these offerings attain their consecrated status: *kiddushan batanur* — their sanctification occurs in the oven itself, making the oven a *kli shareis*.

### Pottery Not Kli Shares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא דְּעֵץ, אֲבָל דְּחֶרֶס לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And even Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says only** that a service vessel may be fashioned **of wood,** which is a somewhat significant material, **but** with regard to a service vessel fashioned **of earthenware,** he holds that this is **not** valid.

And here is the decisive point: a *kli shareis* may not be made from pottery. There is a debate among the *Rishonim* regarding whether a *kli shareis* may be made of wood — even Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda, who permits wood, extends the dispensation no further. But everyone agrees that pottery is too humble a material to serve as a sacred utensil. The verse from which we derive the requirements of a *kli shareis*, anchored in the *Menorah* and its associated vessels, places the minimum threshold above pottery.

Consequently, the Temple required a metal oven not because pottery could not be *kashered* — it could — but because the oven needed to function as a *kli shareis*, and a *kli shareis* must be metal (or at minimum, wood according to one opinion). Pottery is categorically excluded from this role.

This cleanly resolves the question while reaffirming the prior conclusion: pottery *can* in principle be *kashered*; the choice of metal was driven entirely by the *kli shareis* requirement, not by any inability to purify pottery.

## Svara vs. Text

### Leaving Rami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יְהוּדָה הֲוָה רְגִיל קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, שַׁבְקֵיהּ וַאֲזַל לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת. יוֹמָא חַד פְּגַע בֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַלְקַפְטָא נַקְטַן, רֵיחָא אָתֵי לַהּ לְיָד?! מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזְלַתְּ לָךְ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, הָוֵית לָךְ כִּי רַב שֵׁשֶׁת?!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates an incident related to the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing. **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yehuda was** initially **accustomed** to study Torah **before Rami bar Ḥama.** After some time, **he left him and went to** study before **Rav Sheshet. One day** Rami bar Ḥama **met him** and **said to him** colloquially: Did you assume, as many do, that when **the chief of taxes [*alkafta*] grasped me** by the hand, **the fragrance** of his hand **came to** my **hand?** Do you think that **because you went** away from me in order to study **before Rav Sheshet, have you become like Rav Sheshet** merely by association?

The *Gemara* now introduces a memorable anecdote. Rav Yitzchak bar Yehuda had been a regular student of Rami bar Chama, but he left him and went instead to study under Rav Sheshet. One day, Rami bar Chama encountered his former student. With a touch of irony, Rami asked him: *alakpeta naktat reicha ati leyad?* — roughly, did you shake hands with a powerful official hoping his prestige would rub off on you? The implication was that Rav Yitzchak had left for reasons of social status, to attach himself to the more prominent Rav Sheshet.

### Logic Contradicted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו מִשּׁוּם הָכִי; מָר – כִּי בָּעֵינָא מִילְּתָא, פָּשֵׁיט לִי מִסְּבָרָא; כִּי מַשְׁכַּחְנָא מַתְנִיתָא – פָּרְכָא לַהּ. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת – כִּי בָּעֵינָא מִילְּתָא מִינֵּיהּ, פָּשֵׁיט לִי מִמַּתְנִיתָא; דְּכִי נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ מַתְנִיתָא וּפָרְכָא – מַתְנִיתָא וּמַתְנִיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yitzḥak bar Yehuda **said to him:** It is **not due to that** reason that I went to study before Rav Sheshet, but for another reason. As for you, **Master, when I ask** with regard to any **matter,** Master **resolves** the question **for me through reasoning.** Consequently, **when I find a mishna** that opposes that reasoning, it **refutes** Master's proposed resolution. As for **Rav Sheshet, when I ask of him** a question concerning any **matter,** he **resolves** the question **for me by** citing **a mishna.** Consequently, **when I also find a mishna, and** that mishna **refutes** the proposed resolution, **it is** a dispute between one **mishna and** another **mishna,** which does not necessarily refute the mishna that he cited.

Rav Yitzchak denied this entirely. His reason for leaving, he explained, was purely pedagogical — and not what a teacher wants to hear. Whenever he posed a question to Rami bar Chama, Rami would answer from *svara* — from pure logical reasoning — without grounding his answer in any transmitted text. Then Rav Yitzchak would produce a *braita* that contradicted the position, and Rami's answer would simply collapse. The exchange would end there, with Rav Yitzchak having learned nothing firm, because his teacher's position had been flatly refuted by a tannaitic source. The problem was not that Rami's logic was poor; the problem was structural. An answer built on *svara* alone is always vulnerable to being overturned by a text, and in practice this happened repeatedly.

### Rav Sheshet & Texts

By contrast, Rav Sheshet would answer questions by producing *braitot* — he grounded his positions in textual sources. Even when Rav Yitzchak subsequently found a counter-*braita*, the result was a collision of two tannaitic sources, which is a fundamentally different situation. Two competing texts create a genuine dispute that can be analyzed, and Rav Sheshet could then offer reasons why his *braita* should be favored over the contradicting one. At worst, the matter ends in a draw. But Rav Sheshet's position is never simply *refuted* — it retains legitimacy. Rav Yitzchak walked away from each session having learned something durable. With Rami, he never knew whether what he had learned would survive contact with a source. That uncertainty was the decisive factor in his departure.

### Rami's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּעִי מִינַּי מִילְּתָא, דְּאִיפְשִׁיט לָךְ כִּי מַתְנִיתָא. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ: בִּישֵּׁל בְּמִקְצָת כְּלִי – טָעוּן מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה, אוֹ אֵין טָעוּן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ טָעוּן, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַהַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama **said to him: Ask me** about **a matter, which I** will **resolve for you in accordance with a mishna.** Rav Yitzḥak bar Yehuda **asked him:** If one **cooked** a sin offering **in** only **part of a vessel, does** the entire vessel **require scouring and rinsing, or does it not require** scouring and rinsing? Rami bar Ḥama **said to him:** The entire vessel **does not require** scouring and rinsing, **just as it is** taught concerning **sprinkling** the blood of a sin offering upon a garment. In the latter case, the mishna teaches (93b) that one must launder only the part of the garment on which the blood sprayed.

Rami bar Chama, characteristically, was undaunted. He told Rav Yitzchak: go ahead, ask me anything. I will answer from *svara*, and you will see that my *svara* will turn out to be consistent with the *braitot*. My logical conclusions are sound; the texts will support them. This exchange sets the stage for what follows — a direct test of that claim.

## Bishul BeMiktzat

### Whole vs. Part

The section continues with Rami bar Chama posing a specific question. He asks: *bishul bimiktzas kli taun merika u'shtifa o ein taun?* — if food was cooked in only part of a vessel (say, only the bottom portion), must the scalding and rinsing be performed on the entire vessel, or only on the part where the cooking took place? This is not an abstract puzzle; it has direct practical implications for the *chattat* and related *korbanos* whose cooking triggers the *merika u'shtifa* ritual.

Rami answers from *svara*: only the place where the cooking occurred requires treatment.

### Blood Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי?! דָּם לָא מְפַעְפַּע, בִּישּׁוּל מְפַעְפַּע! וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: חוֹמֶר בְּהַזָּאָה מִמְּרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה, וְחוֹמֶר בִּמְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה מִבְּהַזָּאָה:
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yitzḥak bar Yehuda **said to him: Are** the situations **comparable? Blood does not spread** and penetrate all parts of the garment, but in the case of **cooking,** the flavor of the meat **spreads** throughout the entire vessel. **Additionally,** your reasoning opposes that which **is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 10:15): A certain **stringency** applies **to sprinkling** more **than** it applies to **scouring and rinsing; and** a certain **stringency** applies **to scouring and rinsing** more **than** it applies to **sprinkling.**

He supports this by analogy to the law of blood on a garment. When blood from a *chattat* spatters onto a garment, the verse requires laundering — but only the affected portion, not the entire garment. The *svara* is: since the blood touches only a specific area, only that area requires attention. By parallel reasoning, since the cooking occurred only in part of the vessel, only that part need be scalded and rinsed.

Rami also frames this as an indirect anchor in a tannaitic source: the blood-and-garment rule is stated in a *Mishna*, and his *svara* bridges from that *Mishna* to the case of the pot. In his view, this gives his position at least partial textual grounding.

### Taste Spreads

Rav Yitzchak challenges the analogy directly: *mi dami? dam lo mefapeia, bishul mefapeia.* The cases are not comparable. Blood does not spread through a garment — it remains localized where it falls, which is precisely why only that spot requires laundering. But taste *does* spread throughout a pot when food is cooked in it. Even if the liquid only reached the bottom of the vessel, the heat and the absorbed flavor permeate the walls of the entire pot. Therefore, the analogy to blood is inapt on a purely logical level, and Rami's *svara* already fails before any text is introduced.

### Braita: Whole Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בִּמְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה – שֶׁהַמְּרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים בֵּין בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, בִּישֵּׁל בְּמִקְצָת הַכְּלִי – טָעוּן מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה כָּל הַכְּלִי; מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּהַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The **stringency** that applies **to scouring and rinsing** is **that the scouring and rinsing** of vessels **is practiced both for offerings of the most sacred order and for offerings of lesser sanctity;** and even if one **cooked in** only **part of the vessel, the entire vessel requires scouring and rinsing, which is not so in** the case of errantly **sprinkling** blood onto a garment, for which one must launder only the place on which the blood sprayed.

Moreover, Rav Yitzchak produces an explicit *braita* that settles the question directly. The *braita* enumerates the ways in which the blood-spattering law (*haza'ah*) is stricter than *merika u'shtifa*, and vice versa. Among the ways *merika u'shtifa* is stricter, the *braita* states: *bishal bemiktzas hakli taun merika u'shtifa kol hakli* — if cooking occurred in only part of the vessel, *merika u'shtifa* must be performed on the entire vessel. This is precisely contrary to Rami's position and is stated as an explicit tannaitic ruling.

This, Rav Yitzchak notes, is exactly why he left Rami. Rami's *svara* was elegant, it was supported by an analogy to a *Mishna*, and yet it was directly contradicted by a *braita*. This is the recurring pattern: *svara* is vulnerable in a way that text-based reasoning is not.

### Rami Concedes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תַּנְיָא תַּנְיָא. וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם בִּכְלִי נְחֹשֶׁת בֻּשָּׁלָה״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּמִקְצָת כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama **said to him: If** this *baraita* **is taught, it is taught,** and I cannot take issue with it. The Gemara then clarifies: **And what is the reason** that an entire vessel requires scouring and rinsing even if one cooked the meat of an offering in only part of the vessel? The reason is that **the verse states: "And if it be cooked in a copper vessel,** it shall be scoured and rinsed in water" (Leviticus 6:21). From the phrase "in a copper vessel" it is derived that **even** if the meat is cooked **in** only **part of a vessel,** the entire vessel must be scoured and rinsed.

Rami's response is a classic expression of his orientation: *ei tanya, tanya* — if there is a *braita*, there is a *braita*. It is a concession, but a begrudging one. He accepts the textual authority of the *braita*, but his tone makes clear that this does not alter his fundamental approach. He will continue reasoning from *svara*; when a text contradicts him, he will yield to the text. But seeking out the texts in advance is not his method. The episode serves as a perfect illustration of the pedagogical contrast Rav Yitzchak had described — and of why the student found Rav Sheshet's approach more educationally reliable.

## Verse Basis

### Verse Foundation

Having established from the *braita* that cooking in part of a vessel obligates *merika u'shtifa* on the whole vessel, the *Gemara* now asks: *vetaama mai?* — what is the actual reason for this rule? Notably, rather than simply articulating the logical reason — that taste spreads throughout the pot when cooking occurs — the *Gemara* turns to a verse. This itself reflects a preference for grounding law in text over grounding it in *svara*, consistent with the thematic dynamic of the preceding passage.

### Nechoshes Verse

The verse cited is *veim bekli nechoshes bushala* — "if it was cooked in a copper vessel." The *Gemara* reads *bekli* — "in a vessel" — as implying even partial use of the vessel: *vaafilu bemiktzas kli*. Even if cooking occurred in only part of the vessel, the entire vessel is subject to the law. The textual basis for requiring treatment of the whole pot is thus located in the verse's phrasing, rather than in the logical inference about taste spreading. This is entirely consistent with the lesson of the prior exchange: even when a logical *svara* is available, the *Gemara* here anchors the ruling in scripture.

## Scope of Offerings

### Yehuda vs. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״חַטָּאת״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַטָּאת, כׇּל קָדָשִׁים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Whether** the meat is from **offerings of the most sacred order** or whether it is from offerings of lesser sanctity, the vessels in which it is cooked must be scoured and rinsed. The Gemara cites a related *baraita*: **The Sages taught:** The Torah introduces the mitzva of scouring and rinsing with the qualifying statement: "This is the law of the **sin offering"** (Leviticus 6:18). From this verse **I have** derived **only** that the *halakha* with regard to scouring and rinsing applies to vessels in which **a sin offering** was cooked. **From where** do I derive that this *halakha* applies to vessels used for **all sacrificial** meat? **The verse states:** "Every male among the priests may eat of it; **it is most sacred"** (Leviticus 6:22), to teach that this *halakha* applies to vessels used for all of the sacrificial meat that the priests eat.

The *Gemara* now turns to a tannaitic dispute about the scope of the *merika u'shtifa* obligation — which categories of sanctified food, when cooked in a vessel, trigger this ritual? The baseline case is the *chattat*. The *braita* asks: since the verse speaks specifically of a *chattat*, how do we know to extend the obligation to other *kodshim*?

The verse concludes with the phrase *kodesh kodashim hi* — "it is holy of holies" — which is read as broadening the application beyond the specific case of the *chattat*. But how far does this extension reach? Rabbi Yehuda holds that it extends to all sacred offerings including *kodashim kalim* (lesser-sanctity offerings), but not to *terumah*. Rabbi Shimon holds that it is limited to *kodshei kodashim* (highest-sanctity offerings) and excludes even *kodashim kalim*.

### Otah Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָהּ״ – פְּרָט לִתְרוּמָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים טְעוּנִין מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים אֵינָן טְעוּנִין מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה. דִּכְתִיב: ״קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים״ – קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים אֵין, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought **that I** should **include** vessels used for cooking ***teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, as well, as it is also sacred and may be eaten only by a priest (see Leviticus 22:14). To counter this, **the verse states:** "Every male among the priests may eat **of it;** it is most sacred" (Leviticus 6:22). The emphatic qualifier "of it" **excludes *teruma*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: Offerings of the most sacred order require scouring and rinsing,** but **offerings of lesser sanctity do not require scouring and rinsing, as it is written: "Most sacred."** Accordingly, with regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, yes,** scouring and rinsing is required; but for **offerings of lesser sanctity, no,** it is not required.

Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning begins with the word *otah*, which appears in the verse (*yochal otah* — "he shall eat it") and functions as a limiting term. Rabbi Yehuda asks: if *kodesh kodashim hi* were meant literally — referring only to *kodshei kodashim* — there would be no need for *otah* to limit anything further, since the category would already be narrow. The very presence of *otah* as an exclusionary term implies that *kodesh kodashim* is being used in an expansive sense, to include all consecrated things. *Otah* then draws the outer boundary, excluding *terumah*. What remains between that broad reading of *kodesh kodashim* and the exclusion of *terumah* is *kodashim kalim* — and those are therefore included in the obligation.

### Shimon's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי תְּרוּמָה, מִכְּלָל דְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים טְעוּנִין מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר לָךְ: ״אוֹתָהּ״ כִּדְאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The *baraita* explains Rabbi Shimon's reasoning; **what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara answers: **Since** the qualifying term **"of it" was necessary to exclude *teruma*, by inference,** it must be **that** vessels used for **offerings of lesser sanctity require scouring and rinsing.** If even offerings of lesser sanctity are excluded from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, it would be self-evident that the vessel used for *teruma* is exempt from scouring and rinsing. Accordingly, the direct exclusion of *teruma* indicates that the vessels used for offerings of lesser sanctity are not excluded. **And Rabbi Shimon** could have **said to you:** The term **"of it"** teaches a different *halakha* and excludes a disqualified sin offering from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, **as we say** earlier in this chapter (93a).

Rabbi Shimon reads the *otah* differently. He does not apply it to this context at all. Rather, *otah* was already used in an earlier law — the law concerning blood of the *chattat* — to exclude certain categories of *chattat* from that obligation, specifically a *chattat* that lacked a *sha'at ha'kosher* (a moment of fitness). In Rabbi Shimon's reading, *otah* is already "spent" on that earlier derivation and is not available to perform any limiting function in the cooking context. Consequently, *kodesh kodashim hi* is taken at face value, literally referring to *kodshei kodashim* alone. *Terumah* is excluded simply because it is not in that category, and *kodashim kalim* are equally excluded for the same reason. Rabbi Shimon thus requires no special limiting word here because the literal phrase itself already draws the line.

## Ritual vs. Kasher

### Terumah Kashering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְרוּמָה לָא בָּעֲיָא שְׁטִיפָה וּמְרִיקָה?! וְהָתַנְיָא: קְדֵרָה שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל בָּהּ בָּשָׂר לֹא יְבַשֵּׁל בָּהּ חָלָב, וְאִם בִּישֵּׁל – בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם. תְּרוּמָה לֹא יְבַשֵּׁל בָּהּ חוּלִּין, וְאִם בִּישֵּׁל – בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it correct that with regard to a copper vessel used to cook ***teruma*, it does not require rinsing and scouring? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 8:16): With regard to **a pot in which** one **cooked meat,** one **may not cook milk in it; and if** one **cooked** milk in it, the meat absorbed in the pot renders the milk forbidden if it **imparts flavor** to it. Similarly, if one cooked ***teruma*** in a pot, one **may not cook non-sacred** food **in it; and if** one **cooked** non-sacred food in it, the absorbed *teruma* renders the mixture sacred if it **imparts flavor** to it. Therefore, a pot requires purging with boiling liquid in order to expel the flavor of *teruma* from it.

Both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon agree that *terumah* is not subject to the specific Torah ritual of *merika u'shtifa*. But this agreement raises an immediate difficulty: does that mean a pot used to cook *terumah* requires no treatment at all before being used for something else? That seems untenable.

### Absorption Principle

A *braita* makes the concern concrete. It states the general principle of vessel absorption in the context of everyday *kashrus*: a pot used to cook meat may not be used to cook milk, and vice versa, lest absorbed taste transfers and affects the new contents. The same applies to *terumah*: a pot used to cook *terumah* may not be used to cook non-consecrated *chullin*, because the absorbed *terumah* flavor may transfer and render the *chullin* restricted to consumption by a *kohen* only — if the transferred taste is detectable (assessed by the standard of *noten ta'am*, roughly one-in-sixty). The *terumah* pot thus clearly requires some form of treatment. So what distinguishes the ritual prescribed by the Torah from the ordinary *kashering* that any absorbed pot requires?

### Ritual Differs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לִדְאָמַר מָר: בִּישֵּׁל בְּמִקְצָת כְּלִי – טָעוּן מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה כָּל הַכְּלִי. הָא תְּרוּמָה – לָא צְרִיךְ אֶלָּא מְקוֹם בִּישּׁוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Three *amora'im* address the apparent inconsistency that while the Torah excludes vessels used for *teruma* from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, the *baraita* teaches that these vessels must be purged. **Abaye said:** When the verse excludes *teruma* from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, this **is necessary only for** that **which the Master said:** If one **cooked in** only **part of** the **vessel, the entire vessel requires scouring and rinsing.** By contrast, in **this** case, if ***teruma*** was cooked in only part of a vessel, one **must** perform scouring and rinsing **only** in **the place of** the **cooking,** and not in the whole vessel.

Abaye resolves this by identifying three specific ways in which the Temple ritual of *merika u'shtifa* differs from ordinary vessel *kashering*. These distinctions explain why the Torah's ritual is something over and above what would be required for any absorbed vessel. Understanding these differences also clarifies why the question of whether *terumah* is "included" in the ritual is meaningful: *terumah* still requires ordinary *kashering*, but it is not subject to the additional stringencies of the ritual.

### Entire vs. Used

The first difference, established by the *braita* cited earlier: the ritual requires that *merika u'shtifa* be performed on the entire vessel, even if cooking occurred in only part of it. Normal *kashering*, according to Abaye, requires treatment only up to the level to which the vessel was actually used. If the soup reached only halfway up the pot, ordinary *kashering* requires heating water only to that level — because the absorbed taste spread inward as far as the liquid went, and reversing the process by heating liquid to the same level should extract it.

This is counterintuitive in light of the *Gemara*'s earlier argument that taste spreads throughout the pot during cooking. Abaye's implicit logic seems to be that even if taste spreads during cooking, the *kashering* process itself — by replicating the same conditions that caused the absorption — can reverse it to the same degree. So practically, one fills to the level of use. The ritual, however, demands the whole vessel regardless. (In practice, current *halacha* requires filling the entire vessel for ordinary *kashering* as well, as a safeguard — the principle established here is a theoretical floor, not the final practical ruling.)

### Water Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לִדְאָמַר מָר: ״בַּמָּיִם״ – וְלֹא בְּיַיִן, ״בַּמָּיִם״ – וְלֹא בְּמָזוּג. הָא – אֲפִילּוּ בְּיַיִן וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּמָזוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** When the verse excludes *teruma* from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, that **is necessary only for** that **which the Master said:** The verse specifies: "It shall be scoured and rinsed **in water"** (Leviticus 6:21), **but** the vessel is **not** to be scoured and rinsed **in wine.** It must be scoured and rinsed **"in water," but not in diluted** wine. By contrast, in **this** case, i.e., the vessel in which *teruma* was cooked, it may be scoured and rinsed **even in wine, and even in diluted** wine.

The second difference: the ritual explicitly requires water — *b'mayim* — and water alone. The *Gemara* derives that the ritual may not be performed with wine, even diluted wine. By contrast, ordinary *kashering* does not require water specifically. Any liquid of comparable efficacy can in principle serve. This produces a surprising implication: if a fleishig pot was used to cook vegetable soup before it was used for milk, one might argue that the vegetable soup constituted an intervening *kashering* of the pot — since *kashering* with non-water liquids is theoretically valid. Whether this actually works *lehalacha* is a separate discussion that the *Rishonim* address, but the principle — that ordinary *kashering* is not limited to water — is established here.

### Cold Rinse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר, לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לִדְאָמַר מָר: מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בְּצוֹנֵן. הָא – אֲפִילּוּ בְּחַמִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla said:** When the verse excludes *teruma* from the *halakha* of scouring and rinsing, this **is necessary only for** that **which the Master said:** One must perform **scouring and rinsing with cold** water, in addition to purging a vessel of its absorbed flavors with boiling water. By contrast, in **this** case, i.e., with regard to the vessel in which *teruma* was cooked, one may cleanse the vessel **even** by performing only the purging **with boiling** water, which removes the residue of the forbidden food, and omitting the cold water processes entirely.

The third difference concerns the concluding step of the ritual. According to one opinion in the *Mishna* (presented shortly), *merika u'shtifa* consists of two cold-water treatments: a rinse of the inside and a rinse of the outside, both in cold water. This cold rinse at the conclusion of the process is part of the specific ritual. Ordinary *kashering*, by contrast, consists of a hot-water treatment (*hagala*) and does not include a mandated cold rinse afterward.

The *Gemara* notes that this third distinction is opinion-dependent. If one holds that *merika* refers to a hot treatment and *shtifa* to a cold rinse, then both the ritual and ordinary *kashering* involve hot water — but even so, the cold rinse (*shtifa yetirta*) remains distinctive to the ritual. If one holds that both *merika* and *shtifa* are cold, as the *Mishna* states, then the entire ritual is cold water, which is clearly different from ordinary *kashering*.

It is worth noting that in current practice, all three of these distinctions have been incorporated into standard *Pesach* *kashering* procedure by rabbinic enactment or *minhag*: we fill the entire vessel, we use water, and we follow the hot *hagala* with a cold rinse. These practices are modeled on the Temple ritual even though they are not technically required by the basic logic of *kashering* alone.

## Mishna: Methods

### Tarfon: Holiday Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּישֵּׁל מִתְּחִילַּת הָרֶגֶל – יְבַשֵּׁל בּוֹ כָּל הָרֶגֶל. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד זְמַן אֲכִילָה. מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה – מְרִיקָה כִּמְרִיקַת הַכּוֹס, וּשְׁטִיפָה כִּשְׁטִיפַת הַכּוֹס. מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה בְּצוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Tarfon says:** If one **cooked** a sin offering in a copper vessel **from the beginning of the** pilgrimage **Festival,** one **may cook in it** for **the entire** pilgrimage **Festival;** he need not scour and rinse the vessel after every use. **And the Rabbis say:** One may not continue using it in this manner; rather, one must perform **scouring and rinsing before** the end of the **period** during which **partaking** of the particular cooked offering is permitted. **Scouring** is **like the scouring of** the inside of **a cup,** the cleaning done when wine sticks to the cup, **and rinsing** is **like the rinsing of** the outside of **a cup. Scouring and rinsing** are both performed **with cold** water.

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishna*, which presents several practical rules about how and when *merika u'shtifa* is performed. Rabbi Tarfon holds that if one began using a pot at the outset of a *regel* (a pilgrimage festival), one may continue using it throughout the entire festival without performing *merika u'shtifa* between uses. The reasoning relates to the status of the meat during the festival period; we will see this explored further in the subsequent *Gemara*.

### Sages: End Eating

The Sages disagree. They hold that the festival status is irrelevant. Once the time during which the sacrificial meat may be eaten has elapsed, the vessel must be processed — whether by *merika u'shtifa* for a metal vessel or by breaking for a pottery vessel. The deadline is defined by when the permitted eating window closes, not by the festival calendar.

### Cup Analogy

The *Mishna* then describes the physical manner of *merika* and *shtifa*. *Merika k'merikat ha'kos* — *merika* is like rinsing the inside of a cup: the interior of the vessel is treated. *Shtifa k'shtifat ha'kos* — *shtifa* is like rinsing the outside of a cup: the exterior is treated. So the two actions together constitute a complete treatment of the vessel — inside and outside.

### Cold Water

Both *merika* and *shtifa*, according to this *Mishna*, are performed with cold water (*tzonen*). This is the view referred to earlier when Abaye discussed the third distinction between the ritual and ordinary *kashering*. The *Mishna* thus describes the ritual as entirely a cold-water procedure, distinct in character from the hot-water *hagala* of ordinary *kashering*.

### Spits & Hagala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשַּׁפּוּד וְהָאַסְכָּלָא – מַגְעִילָן בְּחַמִּין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the spit and the** metal **grill [*askela*],** one **purges them in hot water.**

For spits (*hashapud*) and grills (*ha'askala*) — implements placed directly over the fire rather than used as containers — neither *merika* nor *shtifa* applies. Instead, the *Mishna* states *magilan b'rotchin*: they are subjected to *hagala*, immersion in boiling water. This is the first appearance of the term *hagala* in this context, and it is striking. Implements used directly over open flame are typically *kashered* by *libun* — direct exposure to fire — not by *hagala*. Why does the *Mishna* prescribe *hagala* for spits and grills? This question, with its implications for the relationship between these Temple procedures and the broader laws of *kashering* vessels, is left open — a tantalizing problem that the continuation of the *Gemara* will address.
