# Nazir 44b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Remarks

### Greeting

Our hearts and *tefillot* go out to the injured and to all of Israel. May we know only security and peace.

### Tumah to Taharah

We begin on 44b with the *mishnah*. We are moving from the section dealing with *tumah* — which covered the *issurim* of *tumah*, *yayin*, and *tiglachat* — into the rituals of the *nazir* associated with shaving at the end of the *tumah* process, and then, in the next *mishnah*, the shaving at the end of the *taharah* process. It is worth noting in passing that each of those three earlier topics turns out to serve as a basis for important areas of *halacha* elsewhere: *tumah* for *tumas kohanim*, *tiglachat* for the general issue of shaving, and *yayin* as a paradigm for the principle of *taam ke'ikkar* in *kashrus*.

---

## Tiglachat Tumah

### Mishnah Text

*Tiglachat tumah keitzad?* — How is the shaving of *tumah* performed? The *mishnah* describes the procedure: one sprinkles the ashes of the *parah adumah* on days three and seven, shaves on day seven, and brings the *korbanos* on day eight. This is precisely what the *Torah* prescribes. The relevant verse in *Bamidbar* reads: *v'chi yamut meis alav b'fesa pitoam v'timei rosh nizro v'gilach rosho b'yom taharato b'yom hashvii yegalchenu* — he shaves on the seventh day, the day of his purification — *u'vayom hashmini yavi shtei torim o shnei b'nei yonah* — and on the eighth day he brings the *korbanos*.

### Purification Timeline

The purification here follows the standard *parah adumah* process from *Bamidbar*: sprinkling on days three and seven renders the *nazir* *tahor*, and on the eighth day he brings his *korbanos* in order to restart the *nezirus taharah*. This is not the conclusion of the *nezirus*; it is the beginning of a fresh count.

### Rabbi Akiva's View

The *mishnah* continues: *ve'im gilach bashmini mevi korbanosav bo bayom* — if for some reason he postponed the shaving until day eight, he may nonetheless bring the *korbanos* on that same day eight. The *halacha* is not that the *korbanos* are brought the day after shaving; the *halacha* is that they are brought on day eight. If shaving was delayed, that delay does not push the *korbanos* to day nine. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva.

### Rabbi Tarfon's Objection

Rabbi Tarfon challenged: *u'mah ma bein zeh l'metzora?* — how is the *nazir* different from the *metzora*? Rabbi Tarfon takes it as given that a *metzora* who delays shaving until a day later than prescribed must bring his *korban* one day later still — in that case on day nine rather than day eight. By the same logic, he argues, a *nazir* who postpones shaving until day eight should have to wait until day nine to bring his *korbanos*. (In the *metzora*'s case the relevant delay is not about day eight per se, but about waiting the full seven days until the *kohen* revisits and the subsequent shaving cycle is completed — the structure is parallel even if the precise day count differs.)

### Akiva's Response

Rabbi Akiva replied with a fundamental distinction: *zeh taharato teluyah b'yamav* — by the *nazir*, his becoming *tahor* is dependent on the days themselves; *umetzora taharato teluyah b'tiglachto* — by the *metzora*, the shaving itself is part of the *taharah* process. What is the basis for this distinction? *Tosafos* points out that it derives from a close reading of the respective *pesukim*.

### Process Comparison

By the *nazir*, the *Torah* simply says *v'gilach rosho b'yom hashvii* — he shaves on day seven — and then *u'vayom hashmini yavi*. The shaving is mentioned, but there is no requirement to immerse in a *mikvah* afterward. By contrast, the *metzora*'s purification is described in detail in *Parshas Tazria*. First the *kohen* observes that the affliction has healed — *v'ra'ah hakohen v'hineh nirpa nega hatzaraat min hatzarua* — and the two-bird ritual is performed. Then: *v'chibes hamitaher begadav v'gilach kol searo v'rachatz bamayim v'taher v'achar yavo el hamachaneh* — he launders his garments, shaves all his hair, immerses in water (*mikvah*), becomes *tahor*, and only then may return to the camp. After a seven-day wait, on day seven he shaves again — *yegalach et kol searo et rosho ve'et zekano ve'et gabot einav ve'et kol searo yegaleach* — and then again: *vechibes et begadav verachatz et besaro bamayim vetaher*. Once more, shaving is followed by immersion in the *mikvah*.

### Mikvah Requirement

The critical difference is that by the *metzora*, the shaving is structurally integrated into the *taharah* process, and it is followed by a required immersion in the *mikvah*. This means that if the *metzora* postpones shaving until day eight, even if he had gone to the *mikvah* on day seven, that *mikvah* immersion is ineffective — it must come after the shaving. He therefore must shave on day eight and immerse again, rendering him a *tevul yom* on day eight. Since the *Torah* clearly intends for him to be fully out of his *tumah* status before bringing his *korban*, he must wait until day nine.

By contrast, the *nazir*'s *taharah* process consists entirely of the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes on days three and seven. He is *tamei meis* like any person who has come into contact with a corpse — no unique *taharah* process beyond the standard one. There is no *mikvah* requirement following the shaving. He immerses on day seven after the sprinkling, becomes a *tevul yom*, and by day eight he is fully *tahor*. The shaving is a ritual of *nezirus*, not a component of *taharah*. Postponing the shaving changes nothing about when he is *tahor*.

### Akiva's Conclusion

Rabbi Akiva thus concludes: *zeh taharato teluyah b'yamav umetzora taharato teluyah b'tiglachto* — the *nazir*'s *taharah* is tied to days; the *metzora*'s *taharah* is tied to his shaving. And since the *metzora* must immerse after shaving: *ve'ein meivi korban ela im kein he'eriv shimsho* — one can only bring a *korban* after *ha'arev shemesh*, meaning after the *tevul yom* period has passed. If shaving was postponed to day eight, the subsequent *mikvah* makes him a *tevul yom*, and the *korban* cannot be brought until day nine. The *nazir* faces no such constraint — shaving is a ritual act of *nezirus*, entirely separate from the *taharah* mechanism, and its postponement does not delay the *korbanos*.

## Tarfon & Akiva Debate

### Did Tarfon Concede?

The *Gemara* raises a pointed question: *kiblah minei o lo?* — did Rabbi Tarfon ultimately accept Rabbi Akiva's answer, or did he not? The text of the *mishnah* leaves this unresolved. To address it, the *Gemara* introduces a *braisa* attributed to the *Amora* Hillel (not Hillel Hazaken).

### Hillel's Baraita

The *braisa* states: *gilach bashmini mevi korbanosav batshi'i* — if he shaved on day eight, he brings his *korbanos* on day nine. *Tosafos* notes that there was a *masorah* that this *braisa* refers to a *nazir*, not a *metzora* — since everyone already agrees that a *metzora* who postpones shaving to day eight must wait until day nine. If the *braisa* is indeed discussing a *nazir*, then it asserts that even the *nazir* who delays shaving must bring his *korbanos* one day later. The only authority who holds that position is Rabbi Tarfon. Thus the *Gemara* infers that Rabbi Tarfon did not concede to Rabbi Akiva — this *braisa* reflects his view. Or at minimum, it reflects his position as it stood before the dispute was resolved.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava proposes a different resolution: *ha detaval bashvii ha delo taval bashvii* — the distinction in the *braisa* is not about whether shaving was postponed, but about whether the *nazir* immersed in the *mikvah* on day seven or day eight. Even for a *nazir tamei meis*, going to the *mikvah* is required, and immersion renders one a *tevul yom* who cannot bring a *korban* until after *ha'arev shemesh*. If the *mikvah* immersion was delayed until day eight, one is not *tahor* until day nine — and that, says Rava, is what the *braisa* is describing.

### Critique of Rava

This resolution is difficult to sustain. If the *braisa*'s case were about delaying the *mikvah* immersion, it should have said *taval bashmini* — he immersed on day eight. Instead it says *gilach bashmini* — he shaved on day eight. The very choice of language indicates that the shaving is the operative issue, not the timing of immersion. The simple reading of the *braisa*, then, supports the inference that Rabbi Tarfon did not accept Rabbi Akiva's distinction.

## Tevul Yom Status

### Tamei & Korbanos

This leads to a broader question: can a *tamei* person have *korbanos* brought on his behalf? The general *halacha* — *arel v'tamei hameshalachin korbonoseihem* — is that a *tamei* person may indeed have someone else bring his *korbanos* for him, even though he cannot enter the relevant areas of the *Beis Hamikdash* himself. However, this rule applies only to lighter forms of *tumah*, such as *sheretz* or *neveilah*. A *tamei meis*, *zav*, *zava*, or *metzora* cannot have another person bring a *korban* on his behalf — that is the *halacha*.

### Tosafot's Argument

Beyond this general rule, *Tosafos* argues that even if the precise *halacha* about *tamei* persons sending *korbanos* were in some degree of dispute, it is implicit from the *pesukim* themselves that for the specific *korbanos* of the *metzora*'s purification process — and for the *korbanos* of the *nazir* restarting his *nezirus taharah* — the *Torah* requires the person to be fully out of his state of *tumah* before bringing the relevant *korban*. This is evident from the structure of the verses: the *Torah* prescribes shaving and *mikvah* on day seven and bringing the *korban* the following day. Why not bring it that same day? Clearly because the *Torah* requires the person to no longer be a *tevul yom*. The specific *korbanos* tied to the *taharah* process demand full *taharah* — not merely *tevul yom* status — before being brought.

One might ask whether a *tevul yom* cannot in principle have a *korban* brought for him at all. The answer is that *lehalacha* this is probably true, but *Tosafos*'s point is a more focused one: even setting aside that broader question, the *pesukim* governing these specific *korbanos* make clear that the *Torah* demands the person be completely free of his *tumah* state before proceeding.

### Abaye's Quote

The *Gemara* now turns to a direct discussion of *tevul yom* status. Abaye reports: *ashkechinhu l'chavrei d'Rav Nosson bar Oshaya d'yasvin v'kamrin* — I found the colleagues of Rav Nosson bar Oshaya sitting and discussing the following *braisa*.

### Zav's Approach

The *braisa* derives from the *pesukim* concerning a *zav*. The *Torah* describes the *zav*'s purification in *Vayikra*: *v'chi yithar hazav mizovo v'safar lo shivas yamim l'taharato v'chibes begadav v'rachatz b'saro b'mayim chayim v'taher* — he counts seven days, launders his garments, immerses in living water, and becomes *tahor*. On day eight he comes to the *mishkan*. The *braisa* asks: when may he come? *Bizman shehu taval v'asah ha'erev shemesh* — only after he has immersed in the *mikvah* and *ha'arev shemesh* has passed, meaning he is no longer a *tevul yom*. If he has not done so — *lo taval v'asah ha'arev shemesh* — he may not enter the *mishkan* grounds.

### Zav Status Inference

From this *braisa*, the colleagues of Rav Nosson bar Oshaya inferred: *alma kasavar tevul yom shel zav kezav dami* — this *braisa* holds that a *tevul yom* of a *zav* retains the status of a *zav*. The significance of this inference is considerable. A *zav* is prohibited from entering *Machaneh Leviyah*. The *halakhic* translation of *Machaneh Leviyah* in post-*mishkan* terms is *Har HaBayis*. The question therefore is: if the *zav* has completed his seven days, immersed in the *mikvah*, and needs only nightfall to be fully *tahor* — does his status as a *tevul yom* still carry the prohibitions of a *zav*, including the prohibition against entering *Machaneh Leviyah*/*Har HaBayis*?

## Modern Har Habayit

### Har Habayit Tours

This question has direct practical relevance. There are tours conducted on *Har HaBayis* that are carefully routed to avoid the *Makom HaMikdash* proper, where one would be prohibited regardless of *tumah* status. The justification for such tours is that *Tamei Meis* — which we must assume describes virtually everyone today — is permitted in *Machaneh Leviyah*, i.e., on *Har HaBayis*, and only prohibited within the *Makom HaMikdash* itself.

### Tamei Yotzei Migufo

However, a more stringent category of *tumah* is that of *tamei yotzei migufo* — *tumah* that originates from within one's own body. This includes a woman who is a *niddah* and a man who has had a seminal emission (*ba'al keri*). These individuals are restricted from *Har HaBayis* entirely.

### Tevul Yom & Har Habayit

A practical difficulty arises. Suppose such a man goes to the *mikvah* before arriving at *Har HaBayis* — he is now a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri*. Is a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* treated like a *ba'al keri* for purposes of *Har HaBayis* access? Is he still prohibited? This is precisely the question that turns on whether *tevul yom shel zav kezav dami* — whether a *tevul yom* who derives from one of these more severe *tumah* categories retains that category's restrictions.

---

## Abaye's Challenge

### Abaye's Argument

Abaye pushes back against the colleagues' inference. He argues: if *tevul yom shel zav kezav dami* is truly the principle, then the same logic should apply to the *nazir tamei*. The verse concerning the *nazir* who has become *tamei meis* similarly says he comes to *petach ohel moed* on day eight — *yavish shtei torim el hakohen el petach ohel moed*. One should be able to make the same inference: *eimatai hu ba? bizman shetaval v'asah ha'erev shemesh* — he may only come after having immersed and *ha'arev shemesh* has passed. This would imply that until day eight, the *nazir tamei* — as a *tevul yom* of *tamei meis* — was prohibited from entering those grounds.

But this inference cannot be correct. The *nazir tamei* is *tamei meis* — not a *zav*. And *tumah* from contact with a corpse does not restrict one from *Machaneh Leviyah*.

### Tamei Meis Permitted

The *Gemara* cites a *braisa* that makes this explicit: *tamei meis mutar l'hikaenes l'Machaneh Leviyah* — one who is *tamei meis* is permitted to enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. And not only that — *afilu meis atzmo* — even a corpse itself may be present in *Machaneh Leviyah*. The proof: *vayikach Moshe es atzmos Yosef imo* — Moshe took the bones of Yosef *imo*, meaning with him, *imo b'Machaneh Leviyah*, in the very camp of the *Leviyim*. Since the *nazir tamei* was permitted in *Machaneh Leviyah* even at the peak of his *tumah*, the *pesukim* that speak of waiting until day eight cannot be about permission to enter the grounds.

### Korban Timing Issue

Therefore the *pesukim* are addressing something else entirely: the timing of the *korban*, not the permission to enter the space. The day-eight designation tells us that the *korban* cannot be brought until the *nazir* is no longer a *tevul yom* — but it says nothing about when he may enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. This undermines the inference the colleagues sought to draw from the *zav* passage.

---

## Zav vs. Metzora

### Mechusar Kapara Status

Rava now offers a crucial distinction. It is not that *tevul yom shel zav* is simply *kezav dami* across the board. Rather, the specific *tevul yom* discussed in the *braisa* is one who is also *mechusar kapara* — who must still bring a *korban* the following day. This *tevul yom* is categorically different from an ordinary *tevul yom*. An ordinary *tevul yom* has completed everything; he need only wait for nightfall and he will be fully *tahor*. But a *tevul yom* who is also *mechusar kapara* is not in a merely transitory state. His *taharah* is not complete until he brings the *korban*. His waiting is not simply the passive passage of time — it is waiting to perform a remaining act of *kapara*. This more substantial residual status is what gives him a *zav*-like character.

The distinction within the *zav* category itself clarifies this. A *zav* who has seen a flux twice does not need to bring a *korban*; a *zav* who has seen three times must bring one. The *tevul yom* of the *zav* who saw twice — or analogously, a *ba'al keri* who has simply immersed and needs only nightfall — is *lav kezav dami* according to *Tosafos* and the *Rosh*'s reading. That is, his *tevul yom* status does not carry the *zav*'s restrictions on *Machaneh Leviyah*. But the *tevul yom* who must still bring a *korban* the next day occupies a different position.

### Tamei Yihyeh Source

The *Gemara* asks: where do we derive that a *mechusar kapara* who is also a *tevul yom* is prohibited from *Machaneh Leviyah*? The source is the verse *tamei yihyeh od tumato vo* — he shall remain *tamei*, his *tumah* still upon him. The *drashah*: *tamei yihyeh* comes to include a *tevul yom*, and *od tumato vo* comes to include a *mechusar kapara*. From this verse we know that both statuses carry continuing restrictions. It is taken as axiomatic that any residual *tumah* — even the weakest, even simply being a *tevul yom* waiting for nightfall — bars one from *Makom HaMikdash*. The question unique to *Machaneh Leviyah* is whether *tevul yom* of a *zav* carries *zav*-like restrictions there as well.

### Petach Ohel Moed Meaning

Why do the *pesukim* describe the *zav*'s entry as coming to *petach ohel moed*? The *Gemara* explains: since a *zav* as a full *zav* is already prohibited not just from *Makom HaMikdash* but from *Machaneh Leviyah* itself, the phrase *petach ohel moed* cannot be referring to *Makom HaMikdash* proper — he could not have been there before day eight simply on account of *Machaneh Leviyah* restrictions. So when the *Torah* says he comes on day eight to *petach ohel moed*, it is describing his entry into *Machaneh Leviyah*. The use of the phrase *petach ohel moed* to describe what is functionally *Machaneh Leviyah* access teaches us by analogy: just as one who is *mechusar kapara* cannot enter *Makom HaMikdash* (the *ohel moed* proper), so too a *zav* who is *mechusar kapara* — still a *tevul yom* with a *korban* obligation pending — cannot enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. The language of *petach ohel moed* applied to *Machaneh Leviyah* teaches that the restriction on a *mechusar kapara* vis-à-vis *Makom HaMikdash* finds a parallel restriction on a *tevul yom mechusar kapara* vis-à-vis *Machaneh Leviyah*.

The reasoning is admittedly somewhat circular, as Abaye's own challenge forces a concession. But the conclusion is that the *pesukim* about waiting until day eight are about bringing the *korban* — not about entering the grounds — and the *zav mechusar kapara* who is a *tevul yom* is restricted from *Machaneh Leviyah* not because the *pesukim* imply entry restrictions per se, but because the description of entry as *petach ohel moed* teaches a parallel *din*.

### Conclusion on Tevul Yom

The upshot, according to *Tosafos* and the *Rosh*, is: a normal *tevul yom* of a *zav* — one who has no outstanding *korban* obligation — is *lav kezav dami* and is not restricted from *Machaneh Leviyah* on that account. Only a *tevul yom* who is simultaneously *mechusar kapara*, who still has a *korban* to bring the next day, retains a *zav*-like status that prohibits him from *Machaneh Leviyah*. A *mechusar kapara* who has passed through *ha'arev shemesh* and is simply waiting to bring his *korban* the following day is of course permitted in *Machaneh Leviyah* — that is how he goes to bring the *korban* in the first place, approaching the entrance of *Makom HaMikdash*. The restriction on a *tevul yom mechusar kapara* applies specifically while he is still a *tevul yom*, not after.

## Modern Har Habayit…

### Significance

The resolution of this *sugya* has direct bearing on the question of *Har HaBayis* access in contemporary times. The key question was whether a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* retains the status of a *ba'al keri* — and by extension, whether going to the *mikvah* before ascending *Har HaBayis* is sufficient to resolve the restriction, or whether daytime *tevul yom* status still carries a prohibition. According to the reading of *Tosafos* and the *Rosh*, a *tevul yom* who is not *mechusar kapara* is not *kezav dami* and does not carry the *ba'al keri*'s restrictions. However, it should be noted that this is not the unambiguous *psak*, and other *sugyos* address the same question, with the *halacha* not entirely settled. Those who permit ascent to *Har HaBayis* under careful conditions are presumably relying on the position that *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* is not like a *ba'al keri*.

## Tiglachat Taharah

### Three Animals

We now turn to the next *mishnah*: *tiglachat hataharah keitzad?* — what is the shaving of *taharah*? This is the shaving that concludes the full thirty-day *nezirus*. The *mishnah* states: *haya meivi gimel behemot* — he brings three animals — *chatat, olah, u'shelamim*. The *Torah* in *Bamidbar* then says: *veshachat et hashelamim umigaleach aleihem* — he slaughters the *shelamim* and shaves in connection with them.

### Which Korban?

The *mishnah* asks: to which *korban* is the shaving conceptually linked? The *pesukim* list all three *korbanos* and then describe the shaving, without explicitly tying the shaving to any one of them. The *mishnah* therefore debates this point.

### Shelamim Opinion

The first opinion holds that the shaving is linked to the *shelamim*, based on the juxtaposition in the verse: the *Torah* says *veshachat et hashelamim* immediately before the description of shaving. Additionally, the hair is placed under the pot cooking the *shelamim*, which further connects the act of shaving to the *shelamim* ritual.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *lo haya migaleach ela al hachatat shehachatat kodemet bechol makom* — the shaving is linked to the *chatat*, because the *chatat* is brought first in all contexts involving multiple *korbanos*, and therefore the shaving is conceptually tied to the first *korban* brought.

### Bedieved Validation

The *mishnah* adds that *b'diavad*, if one shaved in connection with any of the three *korbanos*, one has fulfilled the obligation. The dispute between the opinions is only *lechatchila*.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel's view is brought as a practical illustration: if one brought three animals without specifying which is which — simply designating them as his *korbanos* for *nezirus* — then *ha'ra'uya l'chatat tikarev chatat, l'olah tikarev olah, l'shelamim tikarev shelamim*. Since the three animals appropriate for the respective *korbanos* are physically different — *Tosafos* notes: a female lamb (*kisbah*) for the *chatat*, a male lamb (*keves*) for the *olah*, and a ram (*ayil*) for the *shelamim* — the *kedushah* of each animal sorts itself out accordingly. Each animal is inherently suited to one of the three *korbanos*, so even without explicit designation the *korbanos* are valid.

## Where Shaving Occurs

### Baraita Text

The *Gemara* cites a *braisa*: *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed* — the *nazir* shaves at *petach ohel moed*. The *braisa* connects this with the *shelamim*: since the *shelamim* is described as being brought *pesach ohel moed* — *u'shchato pesach ohel moed* — and the *nazir*'s shaving is described with the same language, the shaving is being conceptually linked to the ritual of the *shelamim*. Indeed, beyond the *nazir* context, there is a *halacha* that if the doors of the *ohel moed* are not yet open when the *shelamim* is slaughtered, it is *pasul* — the phrase *pesach ohel moed* carries substantive weight in the *shelamim* ritual specifically. Applying that same phrase to the *nazir*'s shaving therefore connects the two.

### Literal vs. Metaphoric

One might ask: perhaps *pesach ohel moed* is not a metaphoric association but a literal geographic description — that the *nazir* performs his shaving literally inside *Makom HaMikdash*, in *Ezras Yisrael*. For a *nazir tahor* completing his thirty days, this is not a *taharah* problem — he is fully *tahor* and permitted in *Makom HaMikdash*. So conceivably the *Torah* is directing that the shaving literally take place at *pesach ohel moed*. The *Gemara* addresses and rejects this possibility.

### Rabbi Yoshaya

Rabbi Yoshaya argues that the literal reading cannot be correct, on grounds of *bizayon* — disrespect to the sanctity of *Makom HaMikdash*. He derives this from a *kal v'chomer*: the *Torah* says *lo ta'aleh b'ma'alos al mizbachi* — do not ascend the altar by stairs — because even the legitimate *avodah* of a *kohen* walking up steps might involve some slight exposure that is considered disrespectful. If even the *avodah* itself must be performed with such care regarding dignity, then surely the act of shaving — which is not an act of *avodah* — must not be performed within *Makom HaMikdash* itself, where it would constitute a *bizayon*. Therefore the phrase *pesach ohel moed* cannot mean literally there; it must be describing the connection to the *shelamim*. A parallel version of this *drashah* is cited from a *Midrash* in *Parshas Naso*, with slightly different wording but the same conclusion.

### Rabbi Yitzchak

Rabbi Yitzchak offers a different textual argument for the same conclusion. The *Torah* says *v'lakach et se'ar rosh nizro v'natan al ha'esh* — he takes the hair of his head and places it on the fire. The implication is *mi she'eino mechusar ela lekicha u'netina* — that all that intervenes between taking and placing is the act of taking and placing themselves. If the shaving took place in *Makom HaMikdash*, the *nazir* would then have to carry his hair out to where his *shelamim* is cooking — creating additional steps of *hava'ah* between the taking and the placing. The *Torah*'s language implies they occur in the same location. Therefore the shaving must take place where the *shelamim* is being cooked — which is outside *Makom HaMikdash*, in *Ezras Nashim* or even elsewhere in *Yerushalayim*. (Indeed, *lehalacha* there was an actual *lishkas nezirim* — a chamber in the *Ezras Nashim* — where the *nazir* would shave and cook his *shelamim* together.) A parallel formulation of this argument is cited in an alternative version: *makom shehu mevashel sham hayah megaleach* — where he cooks, there he shaves.

### Abba Chanan

Abba Chanan, in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, offers a position somewhat closer to the literal reading: *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed kol zman she'ein pesach ohel moed pasuach eino megaleach* — the *nazir* shaves at *pesach ohel moed*, and if the doors of the *ohel moed* are not open, he may not shave. This takes the phrase *pesach ohel moed* not as a pure metaphor but as imposing a real condition: the shaving is only valid when the doors are open, wherever the shaving physically occurs. This is analogous to the rule about *shelamim* — a *shelamim* slaughtered when the doors are not yet open is *pasul*.

### Shimon Shizuri

Rabbi Shimon Shizuri offers a distinct *drashah* that moves in a different direction entirely. He reads: *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed v'lo nezira* — a male *nazir* shaves at *pesach ohel moed*, but a female *nazirah* does not. According to *Tosafos*, what this means practically is that the *nazir* shaves in the vicinity of the grounds — in *Ezras Nashim* or wherever the *shelamim* is being cooked — an area that is frequented by *kohanim*, while the *nazirah* shaves in a private, modest location (*makom tzanua*) elsewhere in *Yerushalayim*. The reason: *shema yisgaru ba pirchei kehuna* — lest the young *kohanim* be aroused by her, since her hair is uncovered during the shaving.

### Sotah Objection

The *Gemara* raises an objection: the *sotah* also stands before the *kohanim* with her hair uncovered — *v'he'emidah lifnei Hashem* — and we do not invoke any concern of *shema yisgaru ba pirchei kehuna*. Why should the *nazirah* be treated differently?

### Sotah Response

Rabbi Shimon Shizuri responds: *zu kocheles u'pokeses* — the *nazirah* wears cosmetics and presents herself attractively, and it is in that state that she is shaving and her hair is uncovered. *Zu eina kocheles u'pokeses* — the *sotah*, by contrast, is being subjected to a public, degrading process; she is disheveled, not adorned. A woman in an attractive state with uncovered hair raises a concern of arousal that does not apply to a woman undergoing the humiliating *sotah* ordeal. It is for this reason that the *nazirah*, unlike the *nazir*, must shave in a modest, private location.
