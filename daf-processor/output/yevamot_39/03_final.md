# Yevamot 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomeret Yavam Property

### Rachel's Property Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְלוּ לָהּ נְכָסִים מִשֶּׁנִּתְאָרְסָה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תִּמְכּוֹר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר. אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם מָכְרָה וְנָתְנָה — קַיָּים. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זִיקַּת אֲרוּסָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק אֲרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** But didn't we learn in a mishna (*Ketubot* 78a): If **property was bequeathed to** a woman **after she was betrothed, Beit Shammai say: She may sell** that property, **and Beit Hillel say: She may not sell** that property. However, **both agree that if she sold** it **or gave** it **away,** the transfer **is valid.** The mishna clearly states that according to Beit Hillel, a woman who is definitely betrothed may not sell the property *ab initio*. **Rather, conclude from here,** from the fact that here Beit Hillel permit the *yevama* to sell her property *ab initio*, that a levirate **bond** formed **with a betrothed woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **betrothed.**

Today's daf is 39, and we pick up in the middle of 38b. The *gemara* is engaged with the cases presented in the *mishnah*, where a woman — call her Rachel — is a *shomeret yavam*, waiting for her brother-in-law Shimon to either perform *yibum* or *chalitzah*. During this period, Rachel inherits property, which functions in the manner of *nichsei milug*: she holds title, and were she fully married, her husband would be entitled to the *peirot*. The *mishnah* establishes that while Rachel is alive, she has full control — she may buy and sell freely — and both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree to this. The dispute arises only when she dies, at which point there is a question of who inherits: Lavan, her father, or Shimon, the *yavam*.

### Safek vs. Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: נָפְלוּ לָהּ נְכָסִים מִשֶּׁנִּישֵּׂאת, אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם מָכְרָה וְנָתְנָה — שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זִיקַּת נְשׂוּאָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 78a): If **property was bequeathed to** a woman **after she was married, both** Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai **agree that if she sold** the property **or gave** it **away,** then **the husband repossesses** it **from the purchasers. Rather, conclude from here,** from the fact that here Beit Shammai assume the rights of the *yavam* are limited, that a levirate **bond** formed **with a married woman affords** her a status equivalent to **a woman** for whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **married.**

The question the *gemara* presses is this: if Beit Shammai holds that upon Rachel's death the estate is split between Shimon and Lavan — a ruling that appears to reflect Shimon's *safek* claim of quasi-marital status through *zika*, a doubtful *nisuin* — why does Shimon receive nothing while Rachel is alive? His potential entitlement to *peirot* exists regardless of whether she is alive or dead, yet during her lifetime Beit Shammai concedes entirely to her position. Why does his *safek* claim not entitle him to at least something?

### Ein Safek Motzi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַבָּה: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּנָפְלָה כְּשֶׁהִיא נְשׂוּאָה, וְזִיקַּת נְשׂוּאָה עוֹשָׂה סְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה. רֵישָׁא דְּאִיהִי קָיְימָא — הָוְיָא לַהּ אִיהִי וַדַּאי וְאִינְהוּ סָפֵק, וְאֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי. סֵיפָא דְּמֵתָה, הַלָּלוּ בָּאִין לִירַשׁ, וְהַלָּלוּ בָּאִין לִירַשׁ — וְיַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba said** a different resolution to the apparent inconsistency in Beit Shammai's rulings: Both **this** first clause **and that** latter clause of the mishna concern a case in **which she happened** before her *yavam* for levirate marriage **once she was** already **a married woman, and** a levirate **bond** formed **with a married woman affords** her a status equivalent to that of **a woman** about whom there is an **uncertainty** whether she is **married.** The distinction between the two clauses is as follows: In the **first clause, where she is alive, she has a certain** claim to the property, **while they,** i.e., the *yavam*, are considered to have only **an uncertain claim** to the property, as she has the status of a woman for whom there is an uncertainty whether she is married. **And** since one who has **an uncertain claim cannot extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it, she therefore retains full possession of the property. In **the latter clause,** however, **where she died,** neither party has a definite claim; rather, **these** heirs of the father **come to inherit, and those** heirs of the husband **come to inherit, and** therefore **they should divide up** the property.

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle of *ein safek motzi midei vadai*: an uncertain claim cannot displace a certain one. While Rachel lives, she unquestionably holds title. Shimon's claim — whether or not *zika* gives him a quasi-marital status that would entitle him to *peirot* — is at most a *safek*. Since she is a *vadai* possessor of the property, even Beit Shammai concedes that his *safek* cannot dislodge her. When she dies, however, the question of who holds title to the estate becomes genuinely open, and that is where Beit Shammai says to divide.

### House Collapse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּלְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵין סָפֵק מוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי? וְהָתְנַן: נָפַל הַבַּיִת עָלָיו וְעַל אָבִיו, עָלָיו וְעַל מוֹרִישָׁיו, וְהָיוּ עָלָיו כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה וּבַעַל חוֹב. יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב אוֹמְרִים: הַבֵּן מֵת רִאשׁוֹן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת הָאָב, וּבַעַל חוֹב אוֹמֵר: הָאָב מֵת רִאשׁוֹן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת הַבֵּן — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נְכָסִים בְּחֶזְקָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba's opinion: **But** is it true that **according to Beit Shammai,** one with **an uncertain claim cannot extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 157a): In a case where **a house collapsed upon** a person **and upon his father,** or **upon him and upon those** from **whom he stood to inherit, and there were** outstanding debts **against** that person from his **wife's marriage contract and** to **a creditor,** but he had no money with which to pay those debts, and it is not known who died first, the following situation arises: If the father died first, then before the son died he had already inherited the father's property and therefore the son's creditors gained a lien over that property and have the rights to collect their debts from that property even after the son's death. Accordingly, the father's heirs and the creditor offer opposing claims: **The father's heirs say: The son died first and** only **afterward the father died.** Therefore, the creditor never gained any rights to collect from the property. **And the creditor says: The father died first and** only **afterward the son died.** Therefore, the father's property was liened to the son's debts, and the creditor has a right to collect. The mishna continues: **Beit Shammai say: They should divide up** the property between them. **And Beit Hillel say:** The **property** retains **its** previous ownership **status,** which in this case means that since the last known possessor was the father, so the father's heirs gain full rights to it. Abaye explains his proof: **Isn't** it the case **here** that **the father's heirs** have a **definite** claim **and the creditor** has only **an uncertain** claim? Therefore, since Beit Shammai rule that the property should be divided up, it is apparent that they hold that one with **an uncertain claim can extract** property **from** one who has **a definite** claim to it.

A difficulty arises, however, from another ruling associated with Beit Shammai: the case of a house collapsing on a minor and his father, where the question is whether creditors may claim a portion of the estate alongside the heirs. There, Beit Shammai appears to say that we divide despite someone being in presumptive possession — which would seem to contradict the principle just established, that a *safek* cannot displace a *vadai*.

### Shtar Ligvot Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: שְׁטָר הָעוֹמֵד לִגְבּוֹת כְּגָבוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba rejects the proof: Beit Shammai's ruling in this case cannot be adduced as a proof because **Beit Shammai hold:** A debt recorded in **a bill** of debt **that is awaiting collection is as though it was already collected** to the extent that the creditor is considered to be in possession of the debt. Therefore, the creditor is considered to be in possession of the property to the same extent as the father's heirs; consequently, the property is divided between them.

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that Beit Shammai holds *shtar ha-omed ligvot k'gavuy dami* — a document whose time for collection has arrived is treated as though collection has already taken place. In that case, therefore, it is not a matter of the creditors' *safek* claim attempting to extract money from heirs who are *vadai* possessors. Because the debt instrument was ripe for collection, both parties stand in an equal posture of possession. Neither party is more *muchzak* than the other. The two doctrines thus operate in tandem: Beit Shammai accepts *ein safek motzi midei vadai* as a general principle, but carves out an exception when the *safek* claimant holds a mature *shtar*, which grants them a co-equal standing rather than the position of an outsider.

## Sotah & Ketubah

### Sotah Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא, דִּתְנַן: מֵתוּ בַּעֲלֵיהֶן עַד שֶׁלֹּא שָׁתוּ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּתָן וְאֵינָן שׁוֹתוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ שׁוֹתוֹת אוֹ לֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say** that Beit Shammai hold this opinion? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Sota* 24a): A married woman who secluded herself with another man after her husband had warned her not to do so is suspected of having committed adultery. To establish her guilt or innocence she is brought to the Temple, where she drinks the bitter waters. With regard to such women, if **their husbands died before they drank** the bitter waters, **Beit Shammai say: They collect** the money assured to them in **their marriage contracts and do not drink** the waters. **And Beit Hillel say: Either they drink,** and if they survive they collect their marriage contracts, **or** they do not drink and **they cannot collect their marriage contracts,** and all the husband's property passes to his heirs.

The *gemara* now asks: from where do we know that Beit Shammai holds *shtar ha-omed ligvot k'gavuy dami*? It cites the *mishnah* in the laws of *sotah*: a woman who was warned by her husband and secluded with another man, and who therefore must drink the *sotah* waters — if her husband dies before she is able to drink, what is the status of her *ketubah*? The *mishnah* records a dispute. Beit Shammai says: *notlot ketuvatam ve-ein shosot* — they collect their *ketubah* in full. Beit Hillel says: *o shosot o lo notlot ketuvatam* — either they drink, or they do not collect. The *gemara* clarifies that Beit Hillel cannot literally mean they should drink, since the verse states the husband must bring his wife to drink — and if the husband is dead, that is impossible. Rather, Beit Hillel means: *mitoch she-lo shosot, lo notlot ketuvatam* — since they cannot drink, they cannot collect.

### Sotah Safek Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שׁוֹתוֹת?! ״וְהֵבִיא הָאִישׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֵיכָּא! אֶלָּא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא שׁוֹתוֹת לֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּתָן. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּסָפֵק הוּא: סָפֵק זַנַּאי סָפֵק לָא זַנַּאי, וְקָאָתֵי סָפֵק וּמוֹצִיא מִידֵי וַדַּאי! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שְׁטָר הָעוֹמֵד לִגְבּוֹת כְּגָבוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the statement of Beit Hillel: Did Beit Hillel really mean: **Either they drink,** which implies they may actually choose to drink? But doesn't **the Merciful One state: "And the man shall bring his wife"** (Numbers 5:15), which indicates that the ritual of drinking the bitter waters applies only when the husband is still alive, **and** in this case **there is no** husband to do so; consequently, she should not be able to drink. **Rather,** Beit Hillel's intent is as follows: The only means by which a suspected adulteress is able to collect her marriage contract is by drinking the bitter waters and proving her innocence. Therefore, where this is not possible due to the death of the husband, **since** the wives **do not drink, they cannot collect their marriage contracts.** Rabba explains his proof: **Isn't** it the case **here that** the wife's claim to her marriage contract **is uncertain** because there is **uncertainty whether she was unfaithful or whether she was not unfaithful, and** so it would appear that one with **an uncertain** claim **is coming and undermining** the **definite** claim of the husband's heirs? This is untenable, as even were one to hold that someone with an uncertain claim can extract property from someone who has a definite claim to it, that would only allow for the money to be divided between the two sides, whereas in this case Beit Shammai rule that the creditor collects the entire debt. **Rather, conclude from** that mishna that Beit Shammai hold that a debt recorded in **a bill** of debt **that is awaiting collection is** considered **as though it were already collected** to the extent that the one who is owed the money is considered to be in possession of the debt. It is due to this reason that she is empowered to be able to collect her marriage contract.

The *sotah*'s status is genuinely doubtful. She may have committed adultery, or she may not have — that is precisely why the entire *sotah* procedure exists. Her *ketubah* entitlement is therefore a *safek*. The heirs of the deceased husband, meanwhile, are in presumptive possession of the estate. A woman coming to collect her *ketubah* is, in the ordinary framing, a creditor seeking to extract money from people who hold title. Beit Hillel's position is therefore perfectly consistent with *ein safek motzi midei vadai*: her doubtful right cannot override the heirs' certain possession, so she collects nothing. Beit Shammai's position, however, requires explanation. Why does he allow collection despite her *safek* status?

### Shtar Proves Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, לוֹתְבֵיהּ מֵהָא! דִּלְמָא כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה שָׁאנֵי, מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why did **Abaye** object to Rabba's opinion based on the mishna in tractate *Bava Batra*? **Let him object to** Rabba's opinion **based on this** mishna in tractate *Sota* since based on Abaye's assumption that a bill of debt is not considered as though it were already collected, this mishna perforce demonstrates that Beit Shammai hold that one with an uncertain claim can extract property from one who has a definite claim to it. The Gemara answers: Abaye did not object based on this mishna because he reasoned that **perhaps a woman's marriage contract is different** from a regular bill of debt in that the Sages uniquely reinforced a woman's hold over the debt in her marriage contract **due to** the increased **desirability** that this would bring her when trying to remarry. This would ensure that she would bring some money with her into a new marriage.

The *gemara* answers: *shma mina shtar ha-omed ligvot k'gavuy dami*. A *ketubah* is a *shtar* — a legal instrument — and since it was ripe for collection at the moment of the husband's death, it is treated as though collection has already occurred. The *sotah* is not to be understood as a *safek* claimant reaching into the heirs' certain possession. Rather, both she and the heirs stand in an equal posture: her *ketubah* instrument grants her standing co-equal to the heirs, not subordinate to them. This is why Beit Shammai allows collection.

A further subtlety demands attention here. In the house collapse case, Beit Shammai splits the disputed property between the heirs and the creditors. But in the *sotah* case, Beit Shammai allows the woman to collect the *entire* *ketubah*, not merely half. Why the asymmetry?

Several distinctions apply. In the house collapse case, there is a pure epistemic vacuum: no one knows in which order the deaths occurred, and neither party has any prior claim to certainty. In the *sotah* case, the heirs are asserting that she committed adultery, but they have no actual knowledge of this — she, by contrast, is in a position to know whether she was faithful and is asserting her entitlement from a position of personal knowledge. Beyond that, the *ketubah* debt pre-existed in a fully established form: at an earlier point she was unquestionably entitled to it. That certain, prior entitlement distinguishes her claim from that of a creditor in the collapse case, where one cannot say the debt obligation ever attached beyond doubt. These distinctions must be worked through on a case-by-case basis, but the governing principle is clear: when both parties hold equal standing by virtue of the *shtar* doctrine, it is not a case of *safek motzi midei vadai*, and the particulars of each situation determine whether one party collects entirely or the two divide.

### Why House Case?

One might ask: given that the *sotah* case also demonstrates Beit Shammai's position on *shtar ha-omed ligvot k'gavuy dami*, why did the *gemara* not derive the principle from there in the first place? Since we are already in the legal domain of women and *ketubot*, why was it necessary to invoke the case of a collapsing house?

### Chinah Exception

The *gemara* answers: *dilma kesuvas isha shani mishum china*. One cannot necessarily generalize from the *ketubah* to all *shtarot*, because the Rabbis may have accorded special legal strength to a woman's *ketubah* that they would not extend to an ordinary debt instrument. The reason is *china* — a concept of attractiveness or desirability, reflecting the Rabbis' interest in strengthening the *ketubah*'s enforceability in order to make marriage an attractive and secure institution for women. Since the *ketubah* might occupy a uniquely privileged category, one cannot draw a general rule from it about *shtarot* as a whole. The case of the collapsing house, by contrast, involves ordinary creditors and ordinary debt instruments, making it the proper vehicle for establishing the general principle. And there, the answer is that a ripe *shtar* of any kind grants co-equal standing — not merely a *ketubah*.

## Ketubah Split Debate

### Mishnah Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָא קָתָנֵי: מֵתָה, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ וּבִנְכָסִים הַנִּכְנָסִים וְיוֹצְאִין עִמָּהּ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נְכָסִים בְּחֶזְקָתָן. הָכִי קָאָמַר: מֵתָה, מָה יַעֲשֶׂה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְשַׁבְקַהּ. נְכָסִים הַנִּכְנָסִים וְהַיּוֹצְאִים עִמָּהּ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נְכָסִים בְּחֶזְקָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this claim: Do they **not** disagree? **But it is** explicitly **taught** in the mishna that they disagree in that case: If the widow waiting for her *yavam* **died, what should be done with** the money assured to her in **her marriage contract, and with** her **property that enters and leaves** the marriage **with her? Beit Shammai say: The husband's heirs should divide up** the property **together with the father's heirs. And Beit Hillel say:** The **property** retains **its** previous ownership **status.** The Gemara answers: **This is what** that mishna **is saying:** If she **died, what should be done with** the money assured to her in **her marriage contract? And** the *tanna* then **left** this question unanswered, and addressed an additional case: What should be done with her **property that enters and leaves** the marriage **with her? Beit Shammai say: The husband's heirs should divide up** the property **together with the father's heirs. And Beit Hillel say:** The **property** retains **its** previous ownership **status.**

With the framework of Beit Shammai's positions now established, the *gemara* turns to a tension internal to the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* addresses the scenario in which Rachel dies as a *shomeret yavam*, and it raises the question: *mah ya'aseh biktuvatah u-va-nekhasim ha-nikhnasim ve-ha-yotzim imah* — what should be done with her *ketubah* and with her property? The focus of the preceding discussion has been on the *nekhasim* — the *nichsei milug* — and the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel over how to divide that estate. But what about the *ketubah* — the 200 *zuz* Rachel held against Reuven, which by operation of *yibum* now effectively rests against Shimon's estate? The *mishnah* appears to say that Beit Shammai divides everything down the middle. Yet if Shimon is clearly *muchzak* in the estate he inherited from Reuven, why would Beit Shammai say that a *safek* claim to the *ketubah* can extract any portion from him?

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** In truth, Beit Shammai **do not disagree** on that point.

Rava resolves this by offering a bold re-reading of the *mishnah*: *lo palig* — Beit Shammai does not in fact disagree regarding the *ketubah*. Beit Shammai concedes that Shimon retains the *ketubah* money in full. The *mishnah*'s question — *mah ya'aseh biktuvatah* — is not answered by the subsequent ruling; it stands as an unanswered question, followed by a period. What follows — *nekhasim ha-nikhnasim ve-ha-yotzim imah* — is a separate statement about the property alone, and it is only regarding the property that Beit Shammai says *yachloku*. The *mishnah*, on this reading, rules explicitly only on the *nekhasim*, leaving the *ketubah* without a stated ruling from Beit Shammai — because Beit Shammai concedes the *ketubah* to Shimon entirely. This is admittedly a strained reading of the *mishnah*, and the *gemara* acknowledges as much, but it then attempts to find support from the *mishnah*'s own language.

### Language Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא — דְּקָתָנֵי: יַחְלוֹקוּ יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב. וְלָא קָתָנֵי: יוֹרְשֵׁי הָאָב עִם יוֹרְשֵׁי הַבַּעַל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The language of **the mishna is also precise** according to this interpretation, **as it teaches:** Beit Shammai say that **the husband's heirs should divide up** the property **together with the father's heirs,** which implies that the father's heirs had de facto possession of the property and the husband's heirs then came and divided that property with them. This is true with regard to her property that enters and leaves the marriage with her. **And** the mishna **does not teach** using the reverse formulation: Beit Shammai say that **the father's heirs should divide up** the property **together with the husband's heirs,** which would imply that the husband's heirs had *de facto* possession of the property; this is true with regard to the payment of the marriage contract. **Conclude from here** that Beit Shammai did not rule what should be done with the payment of the marriage contract, as the Gemara claimed.

Rav Ashi observes that the *mishnah*'s language can itself be read as supporting this interpretation. The *mishnah* says *yachloku yorshei ha-ba'al im yorshei ha-av* — the heirs of the husband come and divide *with* the heirs of the father. This implies that the father's family is already in possession, and Shimon is entitled to come and take his portion from them. That formulation naturally describes the *nekhasim*, where the father's family holds the default position. Had the *mishnah* also intended to address the *ketubah*, the language would have been reversed: the father's heirs come and take their portion from the husband's estate. Since the language is oriented in only one direction, it appears that only the *nekhasim* are being discussed, and the *ketubah* was never part of Beit Shammai's ruling of *yachloku* at all.

### Conclusion Admits

The upshot of Rava's analysis is that Beit Shammai is in full agreement with *ein safek motzi midei vadai* across the board. Beit Shammai concedes that when Rachel is alive, her certain possession of the *nichsei milug* cannot be overridden by Shimon's *safek* claim to *peirot*. And Beit Shammai likewise concedes that when Rachel dies, Shimon's certain possession of the estate cannot be disturbed by a *safek* claim to the *ketubah* on behalf of her heirs. The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerns only the *nichsei milug* upon Rachel's death, where the question of title itself becomes genuinely uncertain — and there Beit Shammai says to divide, because at that point neither side is more *vadai* than the other.

## Abaye's Timing

### When Inherited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: רֵישָׁא דְּנָפְלוּ לָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, סֵיפָא דְּנָפְלוּ לָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא תַּחְתָּיו דְּבַעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a third resolution to the apparent inconsistency in Beit Shammai's rulings in the mishna: **Abaye said: The first clause** concerns a case in **which** property **was bequeathed to her when she was** still **a widow waiting for her *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, and **the latter clause** concerns a case in **which** property **was bequeathed to her when she was** still **under,** i.e., married to, **her** first **husband,** before he died.

Abaye offers an alternative explanation of the *mishnah* that shifts the axis of analysis entirely. Rather than locating the critical variable in whether Rachel is alive or dead, Abaye proposes that the real distinction is *when* she came to own the property. The *reisha* of the *mishnah* — the case in which Beit Shammai agrees that Rachel retains full control — concerns property that fell to Rachel *after* Reuven had already died, that is, while she was already a *shomeret yavam*. The *seifa* — the case in which Beit Shammai says to divide — concerns property that fell to Rachel *while she was still married to Reuven*.

### Ownership Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָסָבַר אַבָּיֵי יָדוֹ כְּיָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye holds** that according to Beit Hillel, with regard to the husband's rights to his wife's property, **his hand is like her hand,** but not any stronger.

This distinction carries significant legal weight. When Rachel inherits property after Reuven's death, there is no real husband in the picture. Her *zika* to Shimon is at most a quasi-relationship. In this posture, Rachel establishes herself as the undisputed, uncompromised owner of the property. Her title is clean. Shimon's quasi-claim through *zika* is not strong enough to cloud what is, in effect, sole ownership. Beit Shammai therefore concedes she retains full control — while she lives, and presumably also in terms of her family's inheritance claim.

When, however, Rachel acquired the property while she was still married to Reuven, the situation is fundamentally different. At that moment, Reuven — as her real, full husband — had a recognized and substantial position in the property by virtue of his entitlement to *peirot*, which carries a quasi-proprietary character (*k'kinyan haguf*). Her title was never clean in the way it is in the first case; from the moment of acquisition, she and Reuven stood in a kind of co-ownership. When Reuven dies and Shimon steps in through *zika*, Shimon steps into a relationship with property that never belonged exclusively to Rachel. Since she never fully established herself as sole owner, Shimon's position — though weaker than Reuven's had been — is sufficient to generate a genuine dispute, and Beit Shammai therefore says to divide.

### Co-Equal Partner

A key question in Abaye's framework is precisely how to characterize the shift from Reuven's position to Shimon's. If Reuven had a strong position in the property — and his entitlement to *peirot* gives him at least a quasi-proprietary claim — and Shimon's position through *zika* is admittedly weaker, then why does Shimon's weaker position suffice to generate a *yachloku*? Abaye's answer is that while Shimon's claim is indeed weaker than Reuven's was, the key point is that Rachel's title had already been compromised before Reuven's death. She never fully owned the property in an unencumbered sense. When Reuven leaves the picture and Shimon enters at a somewhat lower level of entitlement, the result is that Shimon's claim and Rachel's claim are now roughly co-equal — neither clearly superior — which is precisely the condition under which Beit Shammai says *yachloku*.

Beit Hillel disagrees with this analysis. Beit Hillel holds that Reuven's position in the property, though significant, was co-equal to Rachel's rather than superior. Under that view, when Reuven dies and Shimon steps in at the lower level of *zika*, Rachel's title effectively reasserts itself as the dominant one, and it is her heirs — Lavan's family — who receive the property. The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, on Abaye's reading, thus turns on whether a husband's position in his wife's *nichsei milug* is stronger than, or merely equal to, hers.

### Yadav Keyidah

Abaye's formulation of Beit Hillel's position invokes the phrase *yado k'yidah* — his hand is as her hand, meaning the husband and wife occupy equal positions in relation to the property. It is precisely because Beit Hillel holds *yado k'yidah* that they do not say *yachloku* in the *seifa*. When Reuven — who was merely co-equal — exits the picture, and Shimon — who is at the still-lower level of *zika* — enters, Rachel's position is now unambiguously superior, and Beit Hillel therefore awards the property to her family.

## Rava's Rejoinder

### Husband Stronger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי דְּנָפְלִי לָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא תַּחְתָּיו דְּבַעַל — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּיָדוֹ עֲדִיפָא מִיָּדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him:** I hold that **if** the property **was bequeathed to her when she was** still **under the** first **husband, everyone,** i.e., both Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai, **agrees that his hand is stronger than her hand,** and therefore the hand of the *yavam* will be equally as strong as that of the *yevama*, and if she dies the property will be divided between the two sides.

Rava now engages with Abaye's framework, accepting the basic structural insight — that the timing of Rachel's acquisition is the relevant variable — but disagreeing sharply on the characterization of the husband's position. Rava holds that there is no debate: *dechulei alma lo pligi deyado adifa miyidah* — everyone agrees that the husband's position in *nichsei milug* property is stronger than the wife's, not merely equal. The husband's entitlement to *peirot*, understood at the level of *kinyan ha-guf* or reinforced by rabbinic enactment, makes him the senior property partner. This is not a disputed premise; it is a baseline legal reality that even Beit Hillel would accept.

If this is true, then Abaye's proposed distinction between the two cases — one where she inherited while a *shomeret yavam* versus one where she inherited while fully married — would actually yield the wrong result for Beit Hillel. If the husband was the senior partner and Shimon steps in at a co-equal level, even Beit Hillel ought to say *yachloku*. There would be no case in which Beit Hillel grants Rachel's family the full property under Abaye's framework.

### Maamar Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּנָפְלוּ לָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם. רֵישָׁא דְּלָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר, סֵיפָא דַּעֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר. וְקָסָבַר רָבָא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי עוֹשֶׂה וַדַּאי אֲרוּסָה וּסְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה. וַדַּאי אֲרוּסָה — לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה, וּסְפֵק נְשׂוּאָה — לַחְלוֹק בַּנְּכָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, Rava presents his own resolution to the apparent inconsistency in Beit Shammai's rulings in the mishna: **Rather, both this** first clause **and that** latter clause concern cases in **which** property **was bequeathed to her when she was a widow waiting for her *yavam*. The first clause** concerns a case in **which** the *yavam* **had not performed a levirate betrothal with her,** and **the latter clause** concerns a case in **which he had performed a levirate betrothal with her.** The Gemara explains the rationale behind Rava's explanation: **And Rava holds** that **according to Beit Shammai a levirate betrothal** with a *yevama* **affords** her a status equivalent in some aspects to a woman who is **definitely betrothed and** in other aspects to a woman about whom there is **uncertainty** whether she is **married.** The Gemara elucidates: She is similar to a woman who is **definitely betrothed** with regard **to nullifying** the levirate bond of **her rival wife,** so that the rival wife need not perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*. **And** she is similar to a woman about whom there is **uncertainty** whether she is **married** with regard to empowering the *yavam* **to divide up the property** that she attained while she was waiting for her *yavam*.

Rava therefore proposes a different variable to distinguish the *reisha* from the *seifa*: whether or not Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel. The *reisha* — where Beit Shammai concedes that Rachel retains full control, and by extension that Lavan retains the estate upon her death — is the case in which no *maamar* was performed. In this scenario, Shimon's connection to Rachel remains at the level of *zika* alone — a purely halakhic, quasi-marital bond that is real but limited. Beit Shammai concedes that this is insufficient to generate a competing claim to the property while Rachel lives, and that upon her death her family takes the estate.

The *seifa* — where Beit Shammai says *yachloku* — is the case in which Shimon did perform *maamar*. The *maamar* introduces a new and significant factor, elevating Shimon's relationship with Rachel beyond the baseline *zika*.

### Maamar Effect

According to Rava, *maamar* for Beit Shammai accomplishes the following: *oseh vaday arusah ve-safek nisu'ah* — it renders Rachel a definite *arusah* and a doubtful *nisu'ah* vis-à-vis Shimon. The status of definite *arusah* has implications for *erva* and *tzarat erva* cases — it is enough to push away co-wives in the context of *yibum* eligibility. But the status of *safek nisu'ah* has implications for property: as a doubtful *nisu'ah*, Shimon acquires a *safek* claim to the *nichsei milug*. When Rachel then dies, her family's claim to the property is a *vadai* in one sense, but Shimon's *safek nisu'ah* status generates a competing claim that is not simply a *safek* coming to displace a *vadai* from the outside. Rather, both claims are now of a character that justifies division — and Beit Shammai accordingly says *yachloku*.

## Maamar Dispute

### Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְאִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: An interpretation of the mishna **was stated in the name of Rabbi Elazar** that is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava. And** an interpretation of the mishna **was stated in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina** that is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye.**

The *gemara* notes that the two *amoraim* of the earlier generation map onto the positions of Rava and Abaye: the tradition in the name of Rabbi Elazar follows Rava's position, while the tradition in the name of Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina follows Abaye's position.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה בִּלְבָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And did Rabbi Elazar** actually **state this** interpretation that is in accordance with the opinion of Rava? **But didn't Rabbi Elazar say: According to Beit Shammai a levirate betrothal acquires** the *yevama* **only** with regard **to nullifying** the levirate bond of **a rival wife** so that she need not perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*? This implies that it does not acquire her with regard to enabling the *yavam* to inherit from her. This, then, stands in contrast to Rava's interpretation.

A challenge is immediately raised against the attribution to Rabbi Elazar: did Rabbi Elazar not himself say that *maamar* for Beit Shammai only operates *lidchos b'tzarah bilvad* — that its sole halakhic effect is to push away co-wives in the context of *erva* and *tzarat erva*, implying that it has no relevance to property claims? If so, how can Rabbi Elazar be aligned with Rava, who holds that *maamar* generates a *safek nisu'ah* status with direct property implications?

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ — אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כִּי אֲמַרִי אֲנָא, דְּלָא סַגִּי לַהּ בְּגֵט אֶלָּא דְּבָעֵי נָמֵי חֲלִיצָה. לַחְלוֹק בַּנְּכָסִים דְּלָא קָנֵי — מִי אֲמַרִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers two possible solutions: **Reverse** the opinions, so that Rabbi Elazar is in accordance with Abaye, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, is in accordance with Rava. **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually, do not reverse** the opinions, since Rabbi Elazar can be understood to be in accordance with the opinion of Rava, as **Rabbi Elazar** could have **said to you: When I said** the statement that implies that a levirate betrothal acquires in a very limited sense, the intention was only that its acquisition is limited in **that** giving her **a bill of divorce** is still **insufficient for her** in order to release her from the levirate bond; **rather, she also needs** to perform ***ḥalitza*.** But to suggest **that he does not acquire** her with regard **to dividing up** the **property** she attained, **did I say that?** Certainly not, as in fact a levirate betrothal acquires her to that extent as well, as Rava assumes.

The *gemara* resolves this apparent contradiction by clarifying what Rabbi Elazar's statement actually meant. Rabbi Elazar can maintain that *maamar* creates both an *arusa* status and a *safek nisu'ah* status — including for property purposes — consistent with Rava's position. What his earlier statement was saying is something different: that *maamar* does not fully replace the woman's status as a *yevamah*. Even after *maamar*, she remains in some sense a *shomeret yavam*, and therefore if Shimon subsequently wishes to separate from her, a *get* alone is insufficient — *chalitzah* is also required. The *chiddush* of Beit Shammai on Rabbi Elazar's account is not that *maamar* is legally weak, but rather that it adds a layer of status without eliminating the pre-existing *yevamah* relationship. Shimon has done *kiddushin* through *maamar*, and she may even acquire a quasi-*nisu'ah* status for monetary purposes — but since *yibum* was never performed, the *yevamah* relationship remains simultaneously in force, and *chalitzah* is still needed to sever it. Both statuses coexist; *maamar* adds without displacing.

## Mishnah Language

### Rav Papa Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי: נְכָסִים הַנִּכְנָסִים וְיוֹצְאִים עִמָּהּ, מַאי ״נִכְנָסִין״ וּמַאי ״יוֹצְאִין״? לָאו ״נִכְנָסִין״ — לִרְשׁוּת הַבַּעַל, וְ״יוֹצְאִים״ — מֵרְשׁוּת הַבַּעַל לִרְשׁוּת הָאָב, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקַשְׁיָא ״מֵתָה״, אַדְּמִפַּלְגִי בְּגוּפַהּ וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה, לִפַּלְגוּ בְּחַיֶּיהָ וּלְפֵירוֹת. וְתוּ לָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The precise formulation of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Abaye, **as it teaches:** Her **property that enters and leaves** the marriage **with her. What** is the meaning of: **That enters, and what** is the meaning of: **That leaves? Is it not: That enters into the domain of the husband and leaves the domain of the husband** when she dies, and then enters **into the domain of the father?** Interpreted in this way, the mishna explicitly considers the period in which she was still married to her first husband, and likewise, the case in which she came into the possession of property should be understood as referring to a case in which she did so while still married. This is consonant with Abaye's interpretation, but not with Rava's. And when Rav Pappa said: **Even though** according to his opinion **it is difficult** that the mishna discusses the case in which **she died,** he meant as follows: **Instead of disputing** who has the rights to the property **itself,** which necessitates considering the case **after** her **death, let** Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai **dispute** the more immediate case **when she is** still **alive and** dispute who has the rights to the use and **produce** of the property. The Gemara concludes: **And there is nothing more** to say concerning this matter, i.e., as Rav Pappa noted, while it is undeniable that the mishna's formulation supports Abaye's interpretation, it is equally true that the case discussed by the mishna would appear to challenge his interpretation.

Rav Papa now offers linguistic support from the *mishnah* itself for Abaye's reading. The *mishnah* refers to the disputed property as *nekhasim ha-nikhnasim ve-ha-yotzim imah* — property that "comes in and goes out with her." This phrase demands explanation: where is this property going? Rav Papa argues that it must mean: property that entered into the domain of the husband — *nikhnasim le-reshut ha-ba'al* — and went out with her from the husband's domain — *yotzim mi-reshut ha-ba'al le-reshut ha-av* — at his death. In other words, the *mishnah* is describing property that Rachel acquired *while she was married to Reuven*, such that it entered into the marital estate and then reverted when Reuven died. This phrasing appears only in the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, and Rav Papa argues that it therefore indicates that the *seifa* is specifically discussing property acquired during the marriage to Reuven — precisely the scenario Abaye identified as the relevant distinction.

### Death Emphasis

This support is real, but a difficulty remains. The plain reading of the *mishnah* structures its two cases around whether Rachel is alive or dead: in the *reisha* she is alive, in the *seifa* she is dead and they are dividing the estate. If, as Abaye holds, the real distinction is when she acquired the property rather than whether she is alive or dead, why does the *mishnah* frame the *seifa* around her death at all? One could have discussed the implications of having acquired property during the marriage even while Rachel is still alive — for instance, in terms of *peirot* rights. The fact that the *mishnah* introduces the case by telling us she died and asks what to do with the estate suggests, on its face, that the critical variable is her death, not the timing of acquisition.

### Support Despite

Despite this problem, the *gemara* concludes that the linguistic evidence from *nikhnasim ve-ha-yotzim imah* lends meaningful support to Abaye's reading. Rashi's comment at this point is precise: *ein lahashiv al kach, devadai dika kavatei, ve-kashya le-meiti* — there is nothing to refute the linguistic support for Abaye, yet the emphasis on death in the *seifa* remains a genuine difficulty against him. The *mishnah* thus offers simultaneous support and challenge to Abaye's framework, and the *gemara* leaves the matter with a *ve-tulo midai*, noting that there is nothing more to resolve the tension definitively.

## After Yibum

### Full Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּנָסָהּ הֲרֵי הִיא כּוּ׳. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: לוֹמַר שֶׁמְּגָרְשָׁה בְּגֵט וּמַחְזִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: If the *yavam* **consummated the levirate marriage with her, then** her legal status is that of his wife in every sense. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this said? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** It is **to say that** once they have consummated the levirate marriage, the only way to legally dissolve the marriage is if **he divorces her with a bill of divorce,** but *ḥalitza* is of no avail, **and** after divorcing her, he is permitted to **take her back** to be his wife, as the prohibition against engaging in relations with one's brother's wife does not apply to her.

The *mishnah* now addresses what happens after Shimon performs *yibum* with Rachel. The ruling is straightforward in principle: once *yibum* is accomplished, Rachel becomes Shimon's full wife in every respect.

### Get Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְגָרְשָׁה בְּגֵט, פְּשִׁיטָא? סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב: ״וּלְקָחָהּ לוֹ לְאִשָּׁה וְיִבְּמָהּ״ — אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: וַעֲדַיִן יִבּוּמֵי הָרִאשׁוֹן עָלֶיהָ, בַּחֲלִיצָה — אִין, בְּגֵט — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the first clause of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina's statement: **Isn't** it **obvious** that to sever his relationship with her, **he divorces her with a bill of divorce?** The Gemara explains: It is necessary to teach this because **it could enter your mind to say** that **since it is written: "And take her to him to be his wife and consummate the levirate marriage with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), continuing to refer to the marriage as a levirate marriage even after the verse has stated that he took her as a wife, one might claim **the Merciful One is saying** that the **original levirate** bond **continues to be upon her,** and therefore, **with *ḥalitza*, yes,** they can sever the relationship, but **with a bill of divorce, no,** the relationship would not be severed. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that she is considered to be his wife to the extent that the relationship can be severed with a bill of divorce alone.

The first practical consequence is that if Shimon subsequently wishes to separate from Rachel, he must give her a *get*. The mechanism of *chalitzah* — which was the operative mode of separation while Rachel was a *yevamah* — is no longer available or relevant. She is no longer a *yevamah*; she is simply a wife.

### Remarry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחְזִירָהּ. פְּשִׁיטָא? סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, מִצְוָה דְּרַמְיָא רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ — עַבְדַּהּ, הַשְׁתָּה תֵּיקוּם עֲלֵיהּ בְּאִיסּוּר אֵשֶׁת אָח, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the second clause: **Isn't** it **obvious** that after divorcing her, he is permitted to **take her back** to be his wife? The Gemara explains: It is necessary to teach this because **it could enter your mind to say** that the **mitzva that the Merciful One imposes upon him, he has performed,** and therefore **now** that there is no longer any mitzva to marry her, **she should** once again **be established** as forbidden **to him by the prohibition** against engaging in relations with one's **brother's wife.** To dispel this notion, the mishna **teaches us** that she is considered to be his wife to the extent that there is no longer any prohibition against remarrying her.

The second consequence is that if Shimon divorces Rachel and she subsequently marries another man and is widowed or divorced from him, Shimon may remarry Rachel through ordinary *kiddushin*. The relationship has been entirely normalized.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּלְקָחָהּ לוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁלְּקָחָהּ — הֲרֵי הִיא כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this ruling: **But** why not **say** that this **is indeed so,** that she should be forbidden to him? The Gemara explains: **The verse states: "And take her to him to be his wife"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), indicating that **once he has taken her,** her legal status is **that of his wife in every sense.**

The *gemara* challenges the apparent obviousness of the first ruling. Surely it goes without saying that after *yibum* one gives a *get* rather than performs *chalitzah*? The *gemara* responds that this needed to be stated. One might have thought — *salka de-atach amina* — that since the Torah says "take her for a wife and perform *yibum*," the act of *yibum* initiates the relationship but does not fully extinguish her *yevamah* status. Perhaps she retains the status of sister-in-law throughout the marriage, such that separating from her would require both a *get* and *chalitzah*.

### Complete Change

The *mishnah* therefore teaches that this is not so. Once *yibum* is performed, *amar kra u-lakachah lo l'ishah* — the Torah describes the act as taking her as a wife. *Keivan she-lakachah, harei hi k'ishto le-chol davar* — once he has taken her, she is fully his wife in every respect. All prior statuses — sister-in-law, *yevamah*, *shomeret yavam* — are entirely dissolved. The act of *yibum* is not merely a marital act that coexists with her former status; it transforms her status completely.

The same principle resolves the concern about remarriage. One might have thought that if Shimon divorces Rachel and she marries another man, she cannot return to Shimon — not because of any general prohibition, but because the act of *yibum* functioned as a divinely mandated override of the normal prohibition of a brother's wife, and that override applied only within the specific context of the *mitzvah*. Once divorced, a remarriage would not be a continuation of *yibum* but a new marriage — and a new marriage to a brother's wife would reinstantiate the original prohibition. The *mishnah* teaches that this concern is unfounded. The *yibum* act comprehensively changes her status, and she is simply his wife; the prohibition of *eishet ach* has no further purchase.

### Ketubah Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתוּבָּתָהּ כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא — אִשָּׁה הִקְנוּ לוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. וְאִי לֵית לַהּ מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן — תַּקִּינוּ לַהּ מִשֵּׁנִי, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: The **only** exception to this is that her **marriage contract will** still **be** payable from the property of her first husband and not from the property of the *yavam*. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara explains: **A wife was acquired to him by Heaven,** and since he did not choose her as a wife, he did not obligate himself to pay her marriage contract. The Gemara qualifies the mishna's ruling: **And if she has no** ability to collect her marriage contract **from the first** husband, since he died without leaving any property with which to pay it, the Sages **instituted an ordinance for her** benefit that she must receive a new marriage contract **from** her **second** husband, i.e., the *yavam*. This was instituted **so that she will not be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her,** since doing so would incur a financial burden on his part.

One qualification attaches to this full-wife status in the domain of *ketubah*. If Shimon dies after *yibum*, Rachel collects her *ketubah* primarily from Reuven's estate — because the *ketubah* obligation originated with Reuven, and Shimon effectively stepped into Reuven's position along with Reuven's estate. The *gemara* notes: *ishah hiknu lo min ha-shamayim* — a wife was given to him by Heaven, meaning that the *ketubah* obligation tracks the estate that was transferred to Shimon from Reuven, and thus remains anchored there. However, if Reuven's estate is insufficient to satisfy the *ketubah*, Rachel may collect from Shimon's own estate — *tiknu lah mi-sheini* — because without recourse to Shimon's estate she would be left without protection, and the Rabbis were concerned that absent such protection a husband might be too quick to divorce his wife: *kedei she-lo tehei kalah be-einav le-hotzi'ah*. As Tosafot observes, since Shimon in any event now possesses Reuven's property, the practical question of which estate she collects from involves careful analysis of what the distinction amounts to — but the *halakha* establishes a clear ordering of primary and secondary sources of collection.

## Mitzvah Begadol

### Oldest Obligated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִצְוָה בְּגָדוֹל לְיַיבֵּם. לֹא רָצָה — מְהַלְּכִין עַל כׇּל הָאַחִין. לֹא רָצוּ — חוֹזְרִין אֵצֶל גָּדוֹל וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ, עָלֶיךָ מִצְוָה: אוֹ חֲלוֹץ, אוֹ יַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The **mitzva** of levirate marriage **is for the eldest** of the brothers **to consummate the levirate marriage.** If the eldest **does not want** to do so, the court **goes to each of the** other **brothers** and requires them to do so. **If they do not want** to do so, the court **returns to** the **eldest** brother **and says to him:** The **mitzva is** incumbent **upon you; either perform *ḥalitza* or consummate the levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* now turns to a topic that, the *gemara* suggests, probably should have appeared at the very opening of the *masechet*: the primary obligation to perform *yibum* falls on the oldest brother. *Mitzvah begadol leyabem* — the *mitzvah* of *yibum* is incumbent first and foremost upon the eldest brother. This principle carries two implications: first, that *yibum* is preferred over *chalitzah*; and second, that among the brothers, the eldest bears the primary obligation.

### Go to Others

If the oldest brother is unwilling to perform *yibum*, the *mishnah* directs that one should go to the other brothers — not necessarily in order of age — to see whether any of them is prepared to perform *yibum*. The preference for *yibum* over *chalitzah* means that a younger brother's willingness to do *yibum* is worth pursuing even at the cost of bypassing the older brother's readiness to do *chalitzah*. Notably, the *mishnah* frames this entire process from the perspective of the brothers; the woman's preference is not foregrounded, a point to which the discussion will return.

### Force Oldest

If none of the brothers wish to perform *yibum*, one returns to the oldest brother and tells him directly: *alecha mitzvah* — the obligation is yours. He must either perform *yibum* or perform *chalitzah*; the obligation cannot remain unresolved. He is the one to be pressured and, if necessary, compelled.

### No Waiting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּלָה בְּקָטָן עַד שֶׁיַּגְדִּיל, אוֹ בַּגָּדוֹל עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, אוֹ חֵרֵשׁ אוֹ שׁוֹטֶה — אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, אֶלָּא אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: עָלֶיךָ מִצְוָה, אוֹ חֲלוֹץ אוֹ יַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** If a brother **made** his decision **dependent** upon the possibility that one of his other brothers will eventually consummate the levirate marriage, saying that he will do so only if they do not, then whether he makes it dependent **upon** a brother who is currently **a minor,** meaning that the *yevama* should wait **until he matures, or upon** his **eldest brother,** who is not currently present, meaning the *yevama* should wait **until he comes from overseas, or** upon a brother who is **a deaf-mute or an imbecile,** as perhaps they will recover from their disability, the court **does not listen to him; rather,** the judges of the court **say to him:** The **mitzva is** incumbent **upon you; either perform *ḥalitza* or consummate the levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* further specifies that there are cases in which one might be tempted to delay: if there is a younger brother who is a minor and might grow up and wish to perform *yibum*; if the oldest brother is overseas and one might wait for his return; or if one of the brothers is a *cheresh* or *shoteh* who might conceivably recover. In all these cases, *ein shom'in lo* — we do not listen to arguments for delay. The response is: *alecha mitzvah, o chalotz o yabem* — you are the one obligated, act now. The *mishnah* emphasizes that the present situation, with the brothers who are present and capable, is what determines the obligation. One does not suspend action in hopes that a better outcome might materialize.

## Yibum vs. Chalitzah

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: בִּיאַת קָטָן, וַחֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל — פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, חַד אָמַר: בִּיאַת קָטָן עֲדִיפָא, וְחַד אָמַר: חֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל עֲדִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: An amoraic dispute **was stated: Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagree with regard to** a case in which there is the choice between **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **[*katan*] or *ḥalitza*** performed **by** the **eldest** brother. **One said:** The **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **is preferable, and** the other **one said: *Ḥalitza*** performed **by** the **eldest** brother **is preferable.**

The *gemara* now poses a specific case that tests the priorities established by the *mishnah*: a younger brother wishes to perform *yibum*, while the older brother is prepared to perform *chalitzah*. The question is which takes precedence. Based on the *mishnah*'s plain reading — which sends us to the younger brothers only when the oldest refuses to do anything — one might think the answer is obvious. But the *gemara* shows that the matter is in fact disputed.

### Yibum Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר בִּיאַת קָטָן עֲדִיפָא — דְּהָא מִצְוָה בְּיִיבּוּם. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר חֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל עֲדִיפָא: בִּמְקוֹם גָּדוֹל — בִּיאַת קָטָן לָאו כְּלוּם הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **the one who said** that **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **is preferable,** this is due to the fact **that** the **mitzva** of levirate marriage **is through** the actual **consummation of the levirate marriage,** and therefore preference is always given to consummation over the performance of *ḥalitza*. **And the one who said *ḥalitza*** performed **by** the **eldest** brother **is preferable,** this is because **in the presence of** the **eldest** brother, the **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **is** considered **nothing.**

Rabbi Yochanan holds *biat katan adifa* — the act of *yibum* by the younger brother is preferable. His reasoning: *yibum* is a greater *mitzvah* than *chalitzah*, and therefore if *yibum* can be achieved through any brother, that outcome is to be preferred over *chalitzah* even by the oldest.

### Obligation Priority

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds *chalitzat gadol adifa* — the action of the oldest brother takes precedence. His reasoning does not deny that *yibum* is the more significant *mitzvah*. Rather, he argues that *b'makom gadol, biat katan lav klum hu* — when the oldest brother is present and prepared to act, the younger brother's act is of no legal significance. The oldest brother is the one upon whom the obligation devolves; his willingness to do *something* means that he is the relevant actor, and one does not look past him to the other brothers.

### Logic

The underlying disagreement, then, is between two frameworks. Rabbi Yochanan focuses on the quality of the *mitzvah* being performed: if *yibum* is better, find the best person to do it. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi focuses on the identity of the obligated party: once the oldest brother is ready to fulfill his obligation in any manner, he is the man, and the question of which *mitzvah* is greater is secondary.

## Mishnah Interpretation

### First Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לֹא רָצָה — מַחְזִירִין עַל כׇּל הָאַחִין. מַאי לָאו: לֹא רָצָה לְיַיבֵּם אֶלָּא לַחְלוֹץ, וְקָתָנֵי: מְהַלְּכִין אֵצֶל הָאַחִין, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּיאַת קָטָן עֲדִיפָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests proof for the first opinion: **We learned** in the mishna: If the eldest **does not want** to consummate the levirate marriage, the court **goes to each of the** other **brothers** and requires them to do so. The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** that **he does not want to consummate the levirate marriage but** is willing **to perform *ḥalitza*? And** yet the mishna **teaches:** The court **goes to each of the** other **brothers** and requires them to consummate the levirate marriage. If so, **conclude from here** that the **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **is preferable.**

The *gemara* attempts to read the *mishnah* as settling this dispute. The *mishnah* says that if the oldest brother *lo ratza* — did not want — one goes to the other brothers. A natural reading of *lo ratza* is that he refused to perform *yibum* but was prepared to perform *chalitzah*. If so, the *mishnah* is teaching that even though the oldest brother is willing to do *chalitzah*, one still goes to the other brothers to seek *yibum* — proving that *yibum* of a younger brother takes precedence over *chalitzah* of the oldest, in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lo Ratza Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לֹא רָצָה לַחְלוֹץ וְלֹא לְיַיבֵּם, דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי הָאַחִין: לֹא רָצוּ לֹא לַחְלוֹץ וְלֹא לְיַיבֵּם. אַמַּאי חוֹזְרִים אֵצֶל הַגָּדוֹל? לְמִיכְפְּיֵיהּ (לִכְפְּיֵיהּ) [לִיכְפִּינְהוּ] לְדִידְהוּ! כֵּיוָן דְּמִצְוָה עֲלֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ רַמְיָא — לְדִידֵיהּ כָּיְיפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** the mishna might concern a case where the eldest **does not want** either **to perform *ḥalitza* or to consummate the levirate marriage,** and it is only because he is unwilling to do either that the other brothers are considered. The Gemara asks: If this is so, then **in the corresponding situation** in the mishna in which **the brothers do not want** to do so, the case must be that they do not want either **to perform *ḥalitza* or to consummate the levirate marriage.** But if so, **why should** the court invest the extra effort to **return to the eldest** brother **to force him** to fulfill his duty? **Let** the court **force** the other brothers to fulfill their duty. The Gemara responds: **Since** the **mitzva is incumbent upon** the eldest brother *ab initio*, **it is he who is forced.**

The *gemara* rejects this reading. *Lo ratza* must mean that the oldest brother refused to do anything — neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah*. It is this total refusal that prompts the *mishnah* to send us to the other brothers.

### Force Issue

This corrected reading creates its own difficulty. If all the brothers ultimately refuse to do anything — neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah* — and compulsion becomes necessary, why does the *mishnah* direct us to compel the oldest brother specifically? Once we are in the realm of compulsion, the question of who bears the primary obligation seems less relevant. We simply need someone to act — so why not compel a younger brother?

### Mitzvah Priority

The *gemara* answers without hesitation: *keivan de-mitzvah le-didei ramya, le-didei kafnan* — since the *mitzvah* is primarily incumbent upon the oldest brother, he is the one we compel. Even when force is necessary, the legal structure of obligation has not changed. The oldest bears the primary responsibility, and that responsibility means he is the one subjected to compulsion.

## Waiting Cases

### Wait for Young

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: תָּלָה בְּקָטָן עַד שֶׁיַּגְדִּיל אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. וְאִי בִּיאַת קָטָן עֲדִיפָא, אַמַּאי אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ? נִינְטַר דִּלְמָא גָּדֵיל וּמְיַיבֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **We learned** in the mishna: If a brother makes his decision **dependent upon** a brother who is currently **a minor,** the court **does not listen to him.** The Gemara suggests: **And if** the **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a minor is preferable, why shouldn't** the court **listen to him? Let** the court **wait,** as **perhaps he will mature and consummate the levirate marriage.** Rather, it would appear that the mishna assumes that consummation by a younger brother is not preferable.

Having established that *lo ratza* means refusing both *yibum* and *chalitzah*, the *gemara* presses a further question. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that *yibum* is always preferable even when performed by a younger brother — why does the *mishnah* say we do not wait for a minor brother to grow up? If we could obtain *yibum* by waiting for the younger brother to come of age, should we not do so?

### Wait for Old

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, וּבְגָדוֹל עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, אַמַּאי? נִינְטַר דִּלְמָא אָתֵי וְחָלֵיץ? אֶלָּא כֹּל שַׁהוֹיֵי מִצְוָה לָא מְשַׁהֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But** even **according to your reasoning** that *ḥalitza* performed by an elder brother is preferable, what about the next case in the mishna, where a brother asks to wait **until the eldest brother comes from overseas?** In that case, as well, the mishna rules: **They do not listen to him;** but **why shouldn't** they listen to him? **Let** the court **wait,** as **perhaps he will come and** at the very least **perform *ḥalitza*. Rather,** it is clear that the reason for the mishna's ruling is that **we do not delay the performance of any mitzva;** therefore, if one of the brothers is currently unable to perform the mitzva, he is not considered at all. Consequently, no proof can be derived from the mishna.

The same challenge can be posed in the opposite direction, now aimed at Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who holds that the oldest brother's action takes precedence. If so, why does the *mishnah* say we do not wait for the oldest brother to return from overseas? If the oldest brother is the obligated party and his action is what we prefer, we should be willing to wait for him.

### No Delay

The *gemara* answers that neither challenge lands, because both rest on the same false premise — that our hierarchy of preferences justifies delay. The *gemara* states: *ella kol she-hu mitzvah lo mashinan* — wherever there is a *mitzvah* to be done, we do not postpone it. No matter what one's priorities are regarding who should perform the *mitzvah* or in what form, the overriding imperative is that the *mitzvah* be discharged promptly with the people who are present and capable right now.

### Act Now

The two questions thus cancel each other out, and the answer is the same in both directions: it is not a question of which preference takes precedence, but of the principle that a *mitzvah* cannot be held in abeyance while we hope for a more optimal actor to become available. Whatever our series of preferences — *yibum* over *chalitzah*, oldest over youngest — those preferences operate among present options, not as grounds for indefinite delay.

## Chalitzah Preference

### Chalitzah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִית דְּאָמְרִי: בְּבִיאָה, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבִיאַת קָטָן עֲדִיפָא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בַּחֲלִיצַת קָטָן. וְהָכִי אִיתְּמַר: חֲלִיצַת קָטָן וַחֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. חַד אָמַר: חֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל עֲדִיפָא, וְחַד אָמַר: כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that the dispute is more limited: **When** there is the possibility of **consummating** the levirate marriage, **everyone agrees that** the **consummation of** the levirate marriage by **a younger** brother **is preferable** to the *ḥalitza* of the eldest brother. **When they disagree** it is **with regard to the** significance of the ***ḥalitza* of a younger** brother. **And this** is how the dispute **was stated: Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagree with regard to** a case in which there is choice between the ***ḥalitza* of a younger** brother **or** the ***ḥalitza* of** the **eldest** brother. **One said:** The ***ḥalitza* of** the **eldest** brother **is preferable. And** the other **one said:** The two options **are equivalent.**

A second version of the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi now emerges, one that sharpens the question. In the first version, the debate concerned *yibum* by a younger brother versus *chalitzah* by the older brother. Now the question is narrower: if *yibum* is entirely off the table and the only available outcome is *chalitzah*, does the principle of *mitzvah begadol* still apply? That is, when choosing between *chalitzat katan* and *chalitzat gadol*, is there a preference for the older brother to perform *chalitzah*?

### Mitzvah Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר חֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל עֲדִיפָא — דְּהָא מִצְוָה בַּגָּדוֹל. וְאִידַּךְ — כִּי אָמְרִינַן מִצְוָה בַּגָּדוֹל לְעִנְיַן יִבּוּם, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן חֲלִיצָה — כַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **the one who said** that the ***ḥalitza* of** the **eldest** brother **is preferable,** this is due to the fact **that** the **mitzva** of levirate marriage and *ḥalitza* **is** incumbent **upon** the **eldest** brother. **And the other** Sage would respond to this claim by saying that **when we say** that the **mitzva is** incumbent **upon** the **eldest** brother, that is only **with regard to** the consummation of the **levirate marriage; however, with regard to** performing ***ḥalitza*,** all the brothers **are equivalent.**

One opinion holds *chalitzat gadol adifa* — the *mitzvah begadol* principle extends even to *chalitzah*, and therefore the older brother should be the one to perform it.

### Equivalent

The other opinion holds *ke-hadadi ninhu* — once we are operating in the *chalitzah* domain, the brothers are equivalent. The reasoning behind the *mitzvah begadol* principle was connected to the role of *yibum* in perpetuating the dead brother's name and legacy, an act for which the oldest brother's position is especially meaningful. *Chalitzah*, however, is simply an act of severing the levirate bond, with no such resonance — and for a mere act of severance, age confers no special significance.

### Mishnah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לֹא רָצוּ — חוֹזְרִין אֵצֶל גָּדוֹל. מַאי לָאו: לֹא רָצוּ לְיַיבֵּם, אֶלָּא לַחְלוֹץ. וְקָתָנֵי: חוֹזְרִין אֵצֶל גָּדוֹל, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חֲלִיצַת גָּדוֹל עֲדִיפָא! לָא, לֹא רָצוּ לֹא לַחְלוֹץ וְלָא לְיַיבֵּם, דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי גָּדוֹל: לֹא רָצָה לֹא לַחְלוֹץ וְלֹא לְיַיבֵּם. אֶלָּא אַמַּאי חוֹזְרִין אֵצֶל גָּדוֹל? לְמִכְפְּיֵיהּ (לִכְפְּיֻיהוּ) [נִכְפִּינְהוּ] לְדִידְהוּ! כֵּיוָן דְּמִצְוָה עֲלֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ רַמְיָא — לְדִידֵיהּ כָּיְיפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof for the first opinion: **We learned** in the mishna: If the younger brothers **do not want** to consummate the levirate marriage, the court **returns to** the **eldest** brother and demands that he at least perform *ḥalitza*. The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** that the other brothers **did not want to consummate the levirate marriage but** are willing **to perform *ḥalitza*? And** yet the mishna **teaches:** The court **returns to** the **eldest** brother so that he can perform *ḥalitza*. If so, **conclude from here** that the ***ḥalitza* of** the **eldest** brother **is preferable.** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** the mishna might concern a case where the other brothers **did not want** either **to perform *ḥalitza* or to consummate the levirate marriage,** and it is only because they are unwilling to do either that the court returns to the eldest brother. The Gemara asks: If this is so, then **in the corresponding** situation in which the mishna states: The **eldest** brother **does not want** to do so, the case must be that he does not want either **to perform *ḥalitza* or to consummate the levirate marriage. But** if so, when the younger brothers also refuse, **why should** the court invest the extra effort to **return to** the **eldest** brother **to force him** to fulfill his duty? **Let** the court **force them,** i.e., the younger brothers, to fulfill their duty. The Gemara responds: **Since the mitzva is incumbent upon** the eldest brother *ab initio*, **it is he who is forced.**

The *gemara* attempts again to resolve the dispute from the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* says *lo ratzu, chozrin eitzel gadol* — if the brothers did not want to act, one returns to the oldest brother. The *gemara* reads *lo ratzu* as meaning the younger brothers refused to do *yibum* but were prepared to do *chalitzah*. If so, we now have a situation where multiple brothers are prepared to do *chalitzah*, and the *mishnah* directs us to return to the oldest — proving that even in the *chalitzah* domain the older brother's action is preferred.

### No Proof Yet

Once again, however, the *gemara* corrects this reading: *lo ratzu* means the brothers refused to do anything at all — neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah*. The same logic as before applies: we go to the oldest because the *mitzvah* obligation remains on him, and we compel him because he is the obligated party. But this tells us nothing about whether, when *chalitzah* is the only option and the brothers are cooperating, there is a preference for the oldest to perform it. The *mishnah* provides no resolution to this question, and the dispute remains unresolved.
