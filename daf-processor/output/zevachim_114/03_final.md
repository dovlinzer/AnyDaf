# Zevachim 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechutei Chutz Overview

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאַקְדְּשִׁינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, וַהֲדַר (רבעו) [אִירְבַעוּ]. אֶלָּא מוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד – אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ! בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – דְּאָמַר: קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** with regard to **an animal that** actively **copulated with a person or an animal that was the object of bestiality, you find** circumstances in which the exemption for one who slaughters it outside the Temple courtyard cannot be based on the fact that it is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, e.g., a case **where one initially consecrated it,** at which point it was fit to be brought to the Temple courtyard, **and then engaged in bestiality with it.** Since it was initially fit to be brought to the Temple courtyard, another verse is needed to exclude it. **But** with regard to an animal that was **set aside** for idol worship **or** one that was **worshipped,** this explanation is not tenable, since an animal that was already consecrated would not become disqualified because **a person does not render forbidden an item that is not his.** The Gemara responds that it is possible to disqualify a consecrated item **in** the case of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** such as a peace offering, **and in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says: An offering of lesser sanctity is the property of the owner.**

Daf 114 opens the final *perek* of the tractate, which continues the discussion of *shechutei chutz* — the prohibition of slaughtering outside the designated sacred space — while expanding into the question of *bamot*, the private altars that were permissible or forbidden at different stages of Jewish history. The immediate concern, however, is narrower: which categories of animal, when slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, result in a transgression, and which do not.

### Parah & Seir Exceptions

The *perek* had already established that the *Parah Adumah* and the *Seir Hamishtaleach* are exceptions to the general rule of *shechutei chutz*. Since neither animal is designated for the *petach ohel moed* — their proper place is outside the *Beit HaMikdash* by design — slaughtering them elsewhere does not constitute *chutz*, and one is therefore *patur*. Tosafot notes, however, that if the *Seir Hamishtaleach* is slaughtered before the lottery has been drawn — before the lots determine which goat goes *la-Shem* and which goes to be sent away — one would in fact be *chayav*, because at that stage the animal has not yet been designated and could just as easily have become the goat offered inside.

### Mishna Invalidities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע – דְּבַר עֶרְוָה. מוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד – עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים. אֶתְנַן, וּמְחִיר, כִּלְאַיִם, יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן – בִּוְלָדוֹת קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara summarizes: **Therefore,** all of the cases listed in the mishna are cases in which the animal was initially fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting but was subsequently disqualified as an offering. **An animal that** actively **copulated with a person and an animal that was the object of bestiality** are disqualified after having been consecrated, due to **a matter of forbidden sexual intercourse.** An animal that was **set aside** for idol worship **or** one that **was worshipped** as an object of **idol worship** becomes forbidden after it was consecrated in the case **of an offering of lesser sanctity,** which according to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is the property of its owner. In the case of an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as the **price** of a dog, or an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds,** or an animal **born by caesarean section,** none of which could have occured at a time that the animal was fit to be sacrificed, the mishna is referring **to the offspring of sacrificial** animals that were given as payment to a prostitute or as the price of a dog while in utero. These animals were fit to be brought as a sacrifice while they were still part of a consecrated animal, and only following birth are they considered to be unfit for sacrifice.

The *mishna* proceeds to discuss other categories of invalidity: animals used in the context of bestiality, animals used in idolatrous worship, and *baalei mumin* — animals with blemishes. In all these cases one is *patur* for slaughtering them outside. But Rabbi Shimon introduces a crucial distinction: if the animal is currently unfit but has the potential to become fit at a later time, one does not incur *karet* — the standard punishment for *shechutei chutz* — but one does transgress a negative prohibition. The *rabanan* disagree, maintaining that if the animal cannot be brought now, there is no transgression at all; for them, the absence of *karet* and the absence of any *lav* are linked.

## Rabbi Shimon & Mechusar…

### Shimon's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין וְכוּ׳, אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ וְכוּ׳. וּצְרִיכִי; דְּאִי תְּנָא בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיסִי; אֲבָל תּוֹרִין, דְּלָא מְאִיסִי – אֵימָא לָא, דְּמוֹדוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן;
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cites a disagreement between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon with regard to temporarily **blemished** animals: Rabbi Shimon holds that one who sacrifices them outside the Temple courtyard violates a prohibition, as they will be fit for sacrifice after the passage of time, whereas the Rabbis hold that one is exempt. The mishna cites two similar disagreements: With regard to doves whose time of fitness has not yet arrived that are slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, and with regard to one who slaughters an animal **itself and its offspring** on one day, where the latter, which is not fit for being sacrificed until the next day, is slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard. The Gemara comments: **And** all of these cases **are necessary. As, if** the mishna had **taught** the disagreement only in the case of temporarily **blemished** animals, one would think that the Rabbis deem exempt one who sacrifices outside the Temple courtyard only in that case, **because they are repulsive; but** with regard to **doves** whose time of fitness has not yet arrived, **which are not repulsive** and which will be fit when their time arrives, **I will say** that this is **not** the *halakha*, and **that** the Rabbis **concede to Rabbi Shimon** that one does violate a prohibition.

Rabbi Shimon's position establishes a middle ground: something that cannot be offered today but will be capable of being offered in the future occupies an intermediate halachic space. It is close enough to the status of a valid *korban* to generate a *lav*, yet not sufficiently fit at the present moment to warrant *karet*.

### Mechusar Zman Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא תּוֹרִין – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא (חזו) [אִיחֲזוֹ] וְאִידְּחוֹ; אֲבָל בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין, דְּאִיחֲזוֹ וְאִידְּחוֹ – אֵימָא לָא, דְּמוֹדֵה לְהוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְרַבָּנַן; וְאִי תְּנָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי – מִשּׁוּם דִּפְסוּלָא דְּגוּפַיְיהוּ; אֲבָל אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, דִּפְסוּלָא מֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לַהּ – אֵימָא מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna had **taught** the disagreement only in the case of **doves** whose time of fitness has not yet arrived, one could say that it is only in this case that Rabbi Shimon holds that one who sacrifices them outside violates a prohibition, **because they are not** defined as: **Fit** for sacrifice **and rejected;** their time of fitness simply has not arrived. **But** with regard to temporarily **blemished** animals, **which were fit** for sacrifice **and** then **disqualified, I will say** that this is **not** the *halakha*, **as Rabbi Shimon concedes to the Rabbis** that one who sacrifices them outside the Temple courtyard does not violate a prohibition, since they are not fit to be sacrificed as offerings. **And if** the mishna had **taught** only **these two** cases, i.e., temporarily blemished animals and doves whose time of fitness has not yet arrived, I would say that the Rabbis hold that one who slaughters them outside the Temple courtyard is not liable **because their disqualification** is **inherent. But** in the case of the animal **itself and its offspring,** where **the disqualification comes to** the offspring **from an external** factor, i.e., that its parent was slaughtered that day, **I will say** that **the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Shimon** that one who slaughters an animal and its offspring outside the Temple courtyard does violate the prohibition. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach the disagreement in each case.

The *mishna* enumerates several examples of *mechusar zman* — offerings that lack the proper time. A passing blemish falls into this category: today the animal is *pasul*, but the blemish may heal. The prohibition of *oto v'et beno* provides another case: if the animal's mother or offspring was slaughtered today, this animal cannot be slaughtered today, though tomorrow it will be fully permitted. Similarly, a bird that is not yet old enough, or any animal that has not yet reached its eighth day, is *mechusar zman* — its time has simply not yet arrived.

A distinct subcategory arises when the problem lies not with the animal but with the owner: a *zav*, a *zavah*, or a *yoledet* who has not yet completed the days required before bringing their obligatory *korbanot* renders those sacrifices *mechusar zman be-baalim*, lacking the proper time because of the owner's status rather than the animal's.

### Three Cases Analysis

The *gemara* asks why the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *rabanan* must be stated explicitly in all three cases — *baalei mumin*, *oto v'et beno*, and the case of young birds. The answer is that each case carries a distinct reason why one might have thought the ruling would differ from the others.

Had the *mishna* stated only the case of *baalei mumin*, one might have reasoned that the *rabanan* rule *patur* specifically because the blemished animal is *me'isay* — repugnant — and therefore more thoroughly rejected from sacred service, even if the rejection is transitory. In that case, one might have assumed that for birds that are merely too young, with nothing repugnant about them, the *rabanan* would concede to Rabbi Shimon and at least acknowledge a *lav*.

Conversely, had the *mishna* stated only the case of young birds, one might have concluded that the *rabanan* rule *patur* because those birds were never fit in the first place and were thus entirely pushed away — *lo chazi ve-idchi*. But *baalei mumin*, which were once fit and only later became disqualified, might present a different picture. There is an argument that something *chazi ve-idchi* — fit and then rejected — is considered more thoroughly displaced than something that was never fit, which would be a reason for the *rabanan* to say *patur* even by Rabbi Shimon's logic. Or alternatively, one might reason in the opposite direction: since *baalei mumin* were once valid, they retain a stronger connection to *kedushah*, and Rabbi Shimon might agree to the *rabanan* that the repugnance of a blemish overrides that residual fitness.

Finally, had the *mishna* stated only these two cases, one might have concluded that the *rabanan* rule *patur* exclusively when the deficiency is intrinsic to the animal — whether due to its physical state or its developmental stage. But the case of *oto v'et beno* is different: the animal itself is entirely sound; the problem originates from something external, namely that its mother or offspring was slaughtered today. One might therefore have thought the *rabanan* concede to Rabbi Shimon in this case, since the animal's own fitness is unimpeachable. The *gemara* therefore concludes *tzrichi* — all three cases are necessary — because no matter the nature or degree of the disqualification, the *rabanan* hold that the inability to bring the offering today is decisive, while Rabbi Shimon maintains that future fitness generates a *lav* in every instance.

## Lo Ta'asun Verse Source

### Devarim 12:8 Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר אֲנַחְנוּ עוֹשִׂים פֹּה הַיּוֹם״ – אֲמַר לְהוּ מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: כִּי עָיְלִיתוּ לָאָרֶץ – יְשָׁרוֹת תַּקְרִיבוּ, חוֹבוֹת לֹא תַּקְרִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches **that Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of any sacrificial animal that is fit to be sacrificed after the passage of time, if one sacrificed it outside the courtyard, he is in violation of a prohibition but there is no liability for *karet*. Rabbi Shimon did not specify what prohibition is violated. The Gemara therefore asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon? Rabbi Ile'a says** that **Reish Lakish says that the verse states: "You shall not do all that we do here this day,** every man whatsoever is right in his own eyes. For you have not as yet come to the rest and to the inheritance, which the Lord your God gives you" (Deuteronomy 12:8–9). **Moses said** the following **to the Jewish people: When you enter Eretz** Yisrael, **upright** offerings, i.e., offerings that one believes are proper to bring due to one's own generosity, such as vow offerings and gift offerings, **you may sacrifice,** but **obligatory** offerings **you may not sacrifice,** even in the Tabernacle in Gilgal, until you arrive at "the rest," i.e., Shiloh, at which point you may sacrifice them.

The *gemara* now turns to the question of Rabbi Shimon's textual basis. Rabbi Ilai citing Reish Lakish identifies the source in Devarim 12:8: *lo ta'asun ke-chol asher anachnu osim po ha-yom ish kol ha-yashar be-einav* — "Do not do as we are doing here today, each man whatever is right in his eyes."

### Lo Ta'asun Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגִלְגָּל לְגַבֵּי שִׁילֹה מְחוּסַּר זְמַן הוּא, וְקָאָמַר לְהוּ מֹשֶׁה: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** since obligatory offerings during the period of **Gilgal, in relation to** the period of **Shiloh,** are considered offerings **whose time has not yet** arrived, **and Moses said to** the Jewish people concerning them: **"You shall not do,"** during that period, it follows that one who sacrifices an offering whose time has not yet arrived is in violation of the prohibition: "You shall not do."

The plain sense of the verse is that in the wilderness, *Bnei Yisrael* were offering *korbanot* in various locations, and Moshe warns them that upon entering the land there will be a central, designated place. The difficulty, of course, is that during the period of the *mishkan* in the desert there already was a single central location, and private offerings elsewhere were forbidden. How then does the verse imply that what they were doing at that time was bringing offerings *everywhere*?

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the verse as follows: Moshe tells the Jewish people that upon entering the land, during periods when private *bamot* are permitted (*heter bamot*), only *nedavot* — voluntary offerings such as *nedarim* and *nedavot* like *olot* and *shelamim* — may be brought on a *bamah*. *Chovot* — obligatory sacrifices like *chatas* and *asham* — may not. The phrase *ish kol ha-yashar be-einav* refers specifically to voluntary offerings brought according to one's own judgment and desire. The verse thus prohibits bringing obligatory sacrifices on a private *bamah*, even during periods when *bamot* are generally permissible.

### Nedavot vs Chovot

This reading gives rise to the foundational *halacha* that only *nedarim* and *nedavot* are *karev ba-bamah* — eligible to be brought on a *bamah* — while *chovot* are not. When there is a central *mishkan* or *Beit HaMikdash*, both *nedavot* and *chovot* are brought there. During *heter bamot*, one may bring *nedavot* on a private *bamah*, but *chovot* must wait until a central location is established.

### Gilgal & Shilo Analysis

How does this connect to Rabbi Shimon's principle of *mechusar zman*? The *gemara* draws the following analogy: Gilgal — the first settlement in the Land of Israel where a large communal *bamah* existed — is considered *mechusar zman* relative to Shilo. That is, during the period of Gilgal, obligatory *korbanot* such as *chatas* and *asham* could not yet be brought because the time of Shilo — the era of the central *mishkan* — had not yet arrived.

This is an admittedly unusual application of the concept of *mechusar zman*. One might more naturally describe the inability to bring *chovot* at Gilgal as a restriction arising from the laws of *heter bamot* rather than as a temporal deficiency in the *korban* itself. The *gemara* is effectively translating a restriction about the *categories* of sacrifice permissible during *heter bamot* into the language of *mechusar zman*: the *chatas* during the period of Gilgal is not yet the right time. From this, Rabbi Shimon derives that *mechusar zman* creates a *lav* even when the offering is not presently fit — since the verse *lo ta'asun* prohibits bringing obligatory *korbanot* even at Gilgal, where one might have thought the large communal *bamah* sufficed.

## Aseh vs Lo Ta'aseh Debate

### Yirmya's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: אִי הָכִי, מִילְקֵי נָמֵי לִילְקֵי! אַלְּמָה אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַכָּתוּב נִתְּקוֹ לַעֲשֵׂה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira:** Therefore, it is prohibited to sacrifice offerings whose time has not yet arrived, even if they are sacrificed in the Tabernacle as was the case in Gilgal. **If so,** anyone who sacrifices an offering whose time has not yet arrived, even if he sacrifices it inside the Temple courtyard, **should also receive lashes** for sacrificing it, just as one would for violating other Torah prohibitions. **Why did Rabbi Zeira say** elsewhere that one who slaughters, inside the Temple courtyard, an offering whose time has not yet arrived does not receive lashes for having violated the prohibition of: "It shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 22:23), which is the general prohibition against sacrificing animals that are not fit to be sacrificed. Rabbi Zeira explains that he does not receive lashes because **the verse has transmuted** the negative precept **into** a prohibition that is stated as **a positive mitzva,** in the verse: "But from the eighth day forward it may be accepted" (Leviticus 22:27). There is no punishment of lashes for violating such a prohibition. Rabbi Yirmeya is asking that one should still receive lashes for having violated the prohibition of: "You shall not do."

Rabbi Yirmya raises a pointed objection: if *lo ta'asun* constitutes a genuine *lo ta'aseh*, then anyone who violates it should receive *malkot* — lashes. This follows from the standard rule that transgressing a negative prohibition is punishable by lashes.

### Zeira's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְרַבָּנַן, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **That statement** of Rabbi Zeira applies only **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Shimon in the mishna and hold that the verse that states: "You shall not do," does not indicate that one who slaughters an animal whose time has not yet arrived is in violation of a prohibition. But **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** one would **indeed** receive lashes for slaughtering an animal whose time has not yet arrived inside the Temple.

Rabbi Zeira responds that the verse itself transforms the prohibition into an *aseh* only — *ha-katuv nitko la-asei*. It is not merely an *aseh nitak la-lo ta'aseh* in the conventional sense (where a negative prohibition is linked to a positive commandment that repairs the violation), but rather the verse converts the prohibition entirely into a positive commandment, removing the status of a *lo ta'aseh* altogether.

### Chullin Parallel

This principle has a parallel in *Chullin*. When one slaughters a *mechusar zman* animal inside the *Beit HaMikdash* — for example, offering an animal whose mother was slaughtered that same day — one might have thought that in addition to the prohibition of *oto v'et beno*, one also transgresses the prohibition of slaughtering a *pasul korban* in the *Beit HaMikdash* (*lo yiratzu*). The *gemara* in *Chullin* establishes, however, that the prohibition against slaughtering *mechusar zman* in the *Beit HaMikdash* is only an *aseh*, not a *lo ta'aseh*, and therefore one does not receive lashes for it. This is the principle that Rabbi Zeira invokes: *mechusar zman* is treated as an *aseh* violation, not a full *lo ta'aseh*.

### Shimon's Lashes

For Rabbi Shimon, who derives from *lo ta'asun* that there is a genuine *lo ta'aseh* associated with *mechusar zman*, the calculus is different. According to him, one who brings an obligatory *korban* at Gilgal — where bringing *chovot* is the equivalent of *mechusar zman* — would in fact receive *malkot*, since for Rabbi Shimon the prohibition retains its character as a *lo ta'aseh*.

### Gilgal as Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: פְּנִים דְּגִלְגָּל לְגַבֵּי שִׁילֹה – כְּחוּץ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Rabbi Zeira's statement is even in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who also holds that one would not receive lashes for slaughtering inside the Temple courtyard an offering whose time has not yet arrived. A prohibition cannot be derived from the prohibition stated with regard to the Tabernacle in Gilgal, since **inside** the Tabernacle **in Gilgal, in relation to** the Tabernacle in **Shiloh, is considered like outside,** and the prohibition: "You shall not do," pertains only to sacrificing an offering whose time has not yet arrived outside the Temple courtyard.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a different resolution to the apparent contradiction. He proposes that the rule exempting *mechusar zman* from *malkot* — treating it only as an *aseh* — applies specifically when the *shechita* takes place *bifnim*, inside the *azarah* of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The inner sanctum of the *Mikdash* has a unique status that transforms the violation into a lesser *aseh* category. Gilgal, however, is not the inner sanctum. In relation to Shilo, Gilgal is considered *chutz* — outside. Therefore, bringing *mechusar zman* at Gilgal does not benefit from the rule that applies inside the *Mikdash*, and the violation retains its full character as a *lo ta'aseh* subject to *malkot*.

This leads to a broader conceptual picture, which the *gemara* works through with some difficulty. On one hand, there is the *halacha* from *Chullin* that *mechusar zman* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* is only an *aseh*. On the other hand, Rabbi Shimon's derivation from *lo ta'asun* treats bringing *chovot* at a *bamah* — or at Gilgal — as a *lo ta'aseh* with *malkot*. The resolution is either that Rabbi Shimon simply disagrees with the rule in *Chullin* and maintains it is a *lo ta'aseh* even inside the *Mikdash*, or — following Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — that the *aseh*-only rule is limited to actions performed inside the *azarah*, and Gilgal, being treated as *chutz* relative to Shilo, falls outside that domain.

## Pesach Before Time Source

### Pesach Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַזּוֹבֵחַ פֶּסַח בְּבָמַת יָחִיד בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, שֶׁהוּא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תוּכַל לִזְבֹּחַ אֶת הַפָּסַח״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The reason of Rabbi Shimon** is not based upon: "You shall not do," as Reish Lakish claims, but upon another verse. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: From where** is it derived **that one who slaughters** his **Paschal offering on a private altar at a time** when it is **prohibited** to sacrifice offerings on private **altars** violates **a prohibition? The verse states: "You may not sacrifice the Paschal offering** within any of your gates; but at the place that the Lord your God shall choose to cause His name to dwell in, there you shall sacrifice the Paschal offering" (Deuteronomy 16:5–6).

Rava offers an alternative and more direct source for Rabbi Shimon's principle. A *baraita* records Rabbi Shimon himself deriving from the laws of *korban Pesach* that *mechusar zman* during a period of *issur bamot* generates a *lav*.

### Lo Tuchal Pesach

The verse in Devarim 16 states: *lo tuchal lizbo'ach et ha-Pesach be-achad she'arecha* — "You may not slaughter the Pesach offering in any of your gates." This is a centralization mandate: the *korban Pesach* must be brought at the designated national location. Rabbi Shimon asks: from where do we know that slaughtering a *korban Pesach* on a private *bamah* during a period of *issur bamot* constitutes a *lo ta'aseh*? The answer is from this verse.

### Pesach Timing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאַחַד שְׁעָרֶיךָ״ – לֹא אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ אֶלָּא בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל נִכְנָסִין בְּשַׁעַר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **even at a time** when it is **permitted** to sacrifice offerings on private **altars** this is **so.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Within any [*be'aḥad*] of your gates,"** which indicates that **I said** this prohibition **to you only when all of the Jewish people enter** the Temple **through one [*eḥad*] gate** in order to sacrifice their offerings. When there is no permanent communal altar, it is permitted to slaughter the Paschal offering on a private altar.

One might immediately object: why is a special verse necessary? At a time of *issur bamot*, anyone who slaughters a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is already *chayav karet* for the standard violation of *shechutei chutz*. The prohibition of bringing a *korban Pesach* on a private *bamah* should be self-evident.

The *gemara* resolves this by clarifying the precise scenario. If we are speaking about slaughtering the *korban Pesach* after *chatzot* — after midday on the fourteenth of Nisan, when its time has properly arrived — then indeed one would be *chayav karet* for the classic *shechutei chutz*, and no special verse is needed. The special derivation must therefore address the case before *chatzot*, when the *korban Pesach* is still *mechusar zman* — its proper time has not yet arrived. In that scenario, the *rabanan* would say one is *patur* entirely, since the offering cannot yet be brought, while Rabbi Shimon argues that the verse *lo tuchal lizbo'ach et ha-Pesach* teaches that even then one transgresses a *lav*.

### Before Chatzot Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִי נֵימָא אַחַר חֲצוֹת – כָּרֵת נָמֵי מִחַיַּיב! אֶלָּא לָאו קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת? לְעוֹלָם לְאַחַר חֲצוֹת, וּבִשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba analyzes the *baraita*: **When** was this Paschal offering, for which one violates a prohibition for slaughtering it during a time when it is forbidden to sacrifice on private altars, slaughtered? **If we say** that it was **after midday** on the fourteenth of Nisan, which is the proper time for sacrificing the Paschal offering in the Temple, then one who sacrifices it then violates not only a prohibition, he should also **be deemed liable** to receive ***karet* as well,** as would anyone who slaughters a fit offering outside the Temple courtyard. **Rather, is it not** discussing one who slaughtered the Paschal offering on a private altar on the fourteenth of Nisan **before midday,** when its time had not yet arrived? The Gemara rejects Rabba's explanation: **Actually,** the Paschal offering may have been sacrificed on a private altar **after midday** of the fourteenth of Nisan, **and** it is **referring to a time** when it is **permitted** to sacrifice on private **altars,** i.e., the periods of Gilgal, Nov, and Gibeon. The verse teaches that although it was permitted to sacrifice voluntary vow offerings and gift offerings on a private altar, the Paschal offering may be sacrificed only on a great public altar.

The *gemara* entertains a counter-reading: perhaps the case is indeed after *chatzot*, but the verse operates in a context of *heter bamot* rather than *issur bamot*. Under this reading, *lo tuchal lizbo'ach et ha-Pesach* would be establishing that even during a period when *bamot* are generally permissible, a *korban Pesach* — being an obligatory sacrifice — may not be brought on a private *bamah*. This is then the verse from which one learns that bringing a *chovah* during *heter bamot* is prohibited.

### Pesach Always Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת קָאָמַר! אִיסּוּר בָּמָה לוֹ, הֶיתֵּר בָּמָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *baraita* **state: At a time** when it is **prohibited** to sacrifice offerings on private **altars?** The Gemara responds: The *baraita* means that it is **prohibited for** one to sacrifice the Paschal offering on a private **altar,** but it is **permitted** to use a private **altar for another** offering, i.e., a voluntary vow offering or gift offering.

The *gemara* resolves the ambiguity in the *baraita* by noting that the two readings are not necessarily in conflict. The *korban Pesach* occupies a peculiar position: it has certain characteristics of a *korban yachid* yet is broadly obligatory. During a period of *heter bamot*, it remains forbidden to bring the *korban Pesach* on a private *bamah* precisely because it is a *chovah*. The verse *lo tuchal lizbo'ach et ha-Pesach be-achad she'arecha* functions as the textual anchor establishing this: the prohibition applies when *kol Yisrael nichnasim b'sha'ar echad* — when all of Israel enters through one gate, meaning when a central location exists — and in that context, the *Pesach* must be brought only at that center.

The first, simpler reading of the *baraita* — that it is addressing a case of *issur bamot* with the *korban Pesach* brought before *chatzot* — remains the most natural reading and provides Rabbi Shimon with the clearest and most direct textual basis for his principle. From this case, where there is no *karet* because the time has not fully arrived yet there remains a *lav*, Rabbi Shimon derives the general rule: during *issur bamot*, bringing something *mechusar zman* still carries a *lo ta'aseh*, even absent the full penalty of *karet*.

## Mechusar Zman Be'Bealim

### Owner Status Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסַּר זְמַן כּוּ׳. וְהָנֵי בְּנֵי אֲשָׁמוֹת נִינְהוּ?! אָמַר זְעֵירִי: תְּנִי מְצוֹרָע בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that with regard to an offering **whose time has not yet** arrived because it is premature for its owner, one who sacrifices it outside the Temple courtyard is exempt. This category includes a *zav*, a *zava*, and a woman after childbirth, any of whom sacrificed a sin offering or guilt offering outside the Temple courtyard during the days that they are counting toward purification. The Gemara asks: **And are these** individuals **subject to** the obligation to bring **guilt offerings? Ze'eiri said:** The text of the mishna should **teach: Leper, together with** the *zav*, *zava*, and woman after childbirth. A leper brings a guilt offering as part of his purification process.

The *mishna* now turns to cases where the *mechusar zman* arises not from a deficiency in the animal but from the status of the owner. A *zav*, a *zavah*, and a *yoledet* — individuals who have experienced a form of *tumah* requiring a process of purification and waiting — are obligated to bring specific *korbanot* once their period of purification is complete. Before that time, their *korbanot* are *mechusar zman be-baalim*: the animal may be entirely fit, but the owners have not yet reached the stage at which they may bring the offering.

### Yoledet's Sacrifices

The *mishna* specifies further distinctions: if a *yoledet* slaughters her *olah* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, one is *chayav*; if she slaughters her *chatas*, one is *patur*. This is because an *olah* is a type of offering that anyone, at any time, may bring. Hence an *olah*, even one designated for a *yoledet* in particular, is always *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed* and slaughtering it outside constitutes a transgression of *shechutei chutz*. A *chatas*, however, functions only within the parameters of its specific obligation: before the *yoledet* reaches the point of her obligation, this particular *chatas* has no path into the *Beit HaMikdash*, and it is therefore not *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed*. Whoever slaughters it outside is *patur*.

### Metzora Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹלוֹתֵיהֶן וְשַׁלְמֵיהֶן. וְהָנֵי בְּנֵי שְׁלָמִים נִינְהוּ?! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: תְּנִי נָזִיר. דְּזְעֵירִי קַבְעוּהָ תַּנָּאֵי, דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת לָא קַבְעוּהָ תַּנָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches that if those whose time has not yet arrived sacrifice **their burnt offerings or their peace offerings** outside the Temple courtyard, they are liable. The Gemara asks: **And are these** individuals **subject to** the obligation to bring **peace offerings? Rav Sheshet said: Teach** the case of **a Nazirite** as part of the list in the mishna. A nazirite brings a peace offering at the conclusion of his term of naziriteship. The Gemara notes that the addition **of Ze'eiri** to the text of the mishna, i.e., the case of a leper, was **fixed** by **the *tanna'im*** in the version of the mishna that they would teach, while the addition **of Rav Sheshet,** i.e., the case of a nazirite, was **not fixed** by **the *tanna'im*** in the mishna that they would teach.

A difficulty arises in the *mishna*'s original formulation. The *mishna* refers to people who have an *asham* as part of their obligatory *korbanot*, but the individuals listed — *zav*, *zavah*, *yoledet* — do not bring an *asham*. Similarly, it refers to an *olah* and *shelamim*, yet not all of those listed bring a *shelamim*. Zeiri therefore instructs that *metzora* should be added to the list, since a *metzora* does indeed bring an *asham* as part of the *korbanot* required upon purification. This emendation was accepted and incorporated into the text of the *mishna*. Rav Sheshet additionally proposed adding *nazir* to address the *shelamim*, since a *nazir* brings a *shelamim* upon completing the *nezirut* period. However, *nazir* was not incorporated, as Tosafot explains: all the others on the list are *tamei*, whereas a *nazir* who has not yet completed the *nezirut* period does not share the same category of *tumah*-based disqualification. The *metzora* belongs organically among those who are *tamei* and awaiting purification; the *nazir* does not.

## Asham Shelo Lishmo

### Chilkiya's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִלְקִיָּה (דְּבֵי) רַב טוֹבִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוֹ; אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – חַיָּיב, הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one whose days of purification are not complete, e.g., a leper, slaughters his guilt offering outside the courtyard, he is exempt, since the offering is not fit for sacrifice at that time. With regard to this, **Rabbi Ḥilkiya,** a Sage **from the school of Rav Tovi, says: They taught** this **only** with regard to one who slaughters a guilt offering outside the Temple courtyard **for its own sake. But** if he slaughtered it outside the Temple courtyard **not for its own sake** but for the sake of a different offering, he is **liable** for having sacrificed outside the courtyard. This is **because** it was **fit to** be sacrificed **not for its own sake inside** the Temple courtyard, as a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its own sake is fit for sacrifice (see 2a).

Rabbi Chilkiya be-Rabbi Tuvi introduces a striking ruling regarding the case of a *metzora's asham* slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash* before its time. The *mishna* had established that slaughtering such an *asham* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — since it is *mechusar zman* and not *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed* — makes one *patur*. Rabbi Chilkiya limits this ruling: it applies only when the animal is slaughtered *lishmo* — with the correct intention, as an *asham*. If, however, it is slaughtered *shelo lishmo* — with a different intention, such as for the sake of an *olah* — one is *chayav*.

### Identity Change Logic

The reasoning is as follows: an *asham* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* would be *kasher*, albeit without fulfilling the owner's obligation. Since it would be *kasher bifnim* when slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, it is *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed* in that modality. Therefore, when one slaughters this *mechusar zman asham shelo lishmo* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, one is *chayav*. The rationale is that the fitness of the offering — its *ra'uy* status — is evaluated by asking whether it *could* be brought inside the *Beit HaMikdash* in some valid capacity, and slaughtering it as an *olah* inside would have been valid.

### Initial Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לִשְׁמוֹ נָמֵי נִיחַיַּיב – הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים! בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** one who slaughtered the guilt offering **for its own sake should also be liable** for having slaughtered it outside the Temple courtyard, **since** it was **fit** to be slaughtered **not for its sake inside** the Temple courtyard. The Gemara answers: In order for a guilt offering that was slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard to be considered fit to be sacrificed inside it, it first **requires uprooting** of its status, i.e., the one who slaughters it should intend explicitly that it be a different sacrifice. If its status as a guilt offering has not been uprooted, it is not considered fit to be sacrificed inside.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this position. If slaughtering the *asham lishmo* outside is *patur* because it is *mechusar zman*, why should slaughtering it *shelo lishmo* be *chayav*? The argument for *chiyuv* is that had it been slaughtered *shelo lishmo* inside, it would have been *kasher*. But the same logic would seem to apply when it is slaughtered *lishmo* outside: even then, one could say that had it been slaughtered *shelo lishmo* inside, it would have been *kasher*, and therefore there exists a path by which this animal is *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed*. Why, then, is the *lishmo* case *patur*?

The *gemara* answers: *ba'i akira* — identity change requires actual performance, not mere potential. Until the animal is in fact slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, its identity has not changed; it remains an *asham*. As an *asham*, it is *mechusar zman* and not *ra'uy le-fesach ohel moed*, and therefore slaughtering it *lishmo* outside yields *patur*. One cannot invoke a hypothetical reclassification: the animal's current identity as an *asham* is what determines its status. If, however, one actually proceeds to slaughter it *shelo lishmo* — thereby enacting the identity shift — then in that moment its status changes, and the *chiyuv* applies. As the *gemara* pointedly illustrates, one cannot say "if grandma had wheels she'd be a trolley car": the animal is what it is until the act of reclassification is actually performed.

### Rav Huna's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא: וְכִי יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna objects to** Rabbi Ḥilkiya's statement that a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived is fit to be sacrificed inside if it is slaughtered not for its own sake: **And is there anything that is not fit** if its action is performed **for its own sake, but is fit** if its action is performed **not for its sake?** The Gemara replies: **And** is there **not? But** there is

Rav Huna poses a fundamental challenge to the underlying assumption of Rabbi Chilkiya's ruling: is there any situation in which something is *not kasher lishmo* but *kasher shelo lishmo*? The challenge targets the claim that an *asham mechusar zman*, which is *pasul* when slaughtered as an *asham* (*lishmo*) due to *mechusar zman*, somehow becomes *kasher* when slaughtered as an *olah* (*shelo lishmo*). This seems paradoxical: slaughtering something with the wrong intention is generally a lesser form of validity, not a greater one. How can doing it *shelo lishmo* produce a more *kasher* result than doing it *lishmo*?

### Pesach Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא?! וַהֲרֵי פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – דְּאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ! פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – שְׁלָמִים נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** the **Paschal offering during the rest of the days of the year,** i.e., not on the fourteenth of Nisan after midday, when it is fit to be sacrificed, **which is not fit** if it was sacrificed **for its sake, but is fit** if it was sacrificed **not for its sake.** The Gemara responds: The **Paschal offering during the rest of the days of the year is** considered to be **a peace offering,** not a Paschal offering that was slaughtered not for its sake.

The *gemara* responds by pointing to the case of *korban Pesach* brought outside its season — on any day of the year other than the fourteenth of Nisan. In that case, a *Pesach* slaughtered *lishmo*, as a *Pesach*, is *pasul*, since it is not the time for a *Pesach*. But a *Pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* — not with *Pesach* intent — is *kasher*, and it functions as a *shelamim*. Here, explicitly, *shelo lishmo* produces a *kasher* result while *lishmo* produces a *pasul* result.

The *gemara*, however, qualifies this answer: the reason this works is that a *Pesach* brought outside its season undergoes a full identity transformation — it *becomes* a *shelamim* by operation of *halacha*. Therefore, when it is slaughtered *shelo leshem Pesach*, it is not really *shelo lishmo* in the usual sense; it is simply being slaughtered for what it already is: a *shelamim*. This qualification sets up the difficulty that will be explored further: whether the *asham mechusar zman* undergoes a comparable identity transformation when slaughtered as an *olah*, or whether — unlike the *Pesach* outside its season, whose identity has already shifted — the *asham* retains its identity as an *asham* unless and until the act of *shelo lishmo shechita* is actually performed. The implications for the broader laws of *shelo lishmo* throughout the tractate will be taken up in the following session.
