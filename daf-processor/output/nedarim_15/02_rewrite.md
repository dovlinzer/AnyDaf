# Nedarim 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Yehuda vs. Rav Nachm…

### Mishna on Konam

Today's *daf* is 15. We pick up where the *gemara* says *Ravina amar*, beginning with the word *le'olam*, approximately fifteen lines from the bottom of the page. By way of orientation, it is worth recalling what has been under discussion.

The *mishna* at the end of the previous *perek* dealt with a case in which someone said *konam pi medaber imach* — a *neder* directed at one's mouth as a physical body part, which constituted a *davar sheyesh bo mamash*, a prohibition bearing on something concrete. The *mishna* before us now, by contrast, speaks of a *konam* formulated as follows: my sleeping is *konam*, my speech is *konam* — which would seem to be a *davar she'ein bo mamash*, a prohibition directed at an entirely abstract entity.

### Condition-Dependent Neder

The *gemara*, however, opened not with this question about abstract objects but with a distinct debate that will occupy us through the better part of this *daf*: the disagreement between *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* and *Rav Nachman*. The case is one in which a person declares a *neder* — making something forbidden to himself today — conditional upon some action he will or will not take tomorrow. The question is whether we permit the person to act upon the *neder* today, trusting that he will be careful tomorrow not to trigger the *tnai* (the condition), or whether we are concerned that once the matter turns on the *tnai*, he will not treat it with the same weight he would give to a direct violation of the *neder* itself.

To clarify the mechanics: if the person does trigger the condition, then the *neder* takes retroactive effect, meaning that in hindsight he has violated the *neder*. But because the violation is established only retroactively and was uncertain at the time of the act, perhaps he will not take the *tnai* seriously enough. *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* holds that we do not permit the person to act today, precisely because he will not treat the *tnai* with the gravity he would apply to the *neder* itself. *Rav Nachman*, by contrast, holds that a person will indeed treat the *tnai* with the same seriousness and may therefore be trusted to observe it.

### Connecting Mishna to Deb…

The *gemara* attempted, somewhat forcibly, to read our *mishna* as a special case of this debate — as addressing the situation in which what one does today depends on what one will do tomorrow. The *gemara* itself acknowledges that this is a strained reading. One reason cited for why the *mishna* could not simply be taken at face value was a *mimanashach* argument: if the *mishna* is discussing making sleep forbidden as an abstract idea, a *neder* to that effect would not work; and if it means making one's eyes forbidden to sleep, that was already taught in a prior *mishna*. Since it cannot straightforwardly be either, the *gemara* proposed that it must be addressing the *tnai* case. That is where the previous *daf* concluded.

## Abstract Neder Derabbanan

### Ravina's Solution

The *gemara* now offers a different and important answer. *Ravina amar*, beginning with *le'olam kedekatani*: the *mishna* means exactly what it says — that the *neder* was formulated as *konam* on the sleeping itself, as an abstract act. The natural question is how *bal yachel* can apply to a *davar she'ein bo mamash*. The *gemara*'s answer is *umai bal yachel mi-derabbanan* — the *bal yachel* that applies here is *mi-derabbanan*.

### Derabbanan Scope

This is a critically important point that was touched upon earlier but now receives explicit formulation: even though a *neder* takes effect at the biblical level only on something concrete, the rabbis extended the category of *neder* to encompass abstract matters as well. Accordingly, *konam* on my sleeping, *konam* on my walking — these take effect *mi-derabbanan*. From a conceptual standpoint, a significant gap remains between saying *shvua* I will not walk (which bears on the person himself) and saying *konam* on my walking (which is directed at the abstract act). But many of the practical distinctions collapse once we accept that both function *mi-derabbanan*. According to *Ravina*, then, the *chiddush* of our *mishna* is precisely this: even a *neder* formulated against an abstract action takes effect *mi-derabbanan*, and it is that rabbinic *bal yachel* which the *mishna* invokes.

### Bal Yachel Derabbanan

This leads the *gemara* to pose a sharp question: *umi ika bal yachel mi-derabbanan*? Would we ever apply the language of *bal yachel* — which is a *pasuk* in the *Torah* — to something that is only *derabbanan*? The *gemara* is not suggesting there are no rabbinic *nedarim*; there is ample evidence otherwise. The question is specifically about invoking the *pasuk* of *bal yachel* in this context. To illustrate the strangeness of the proposition: one would not say that eating chicken cooked in milk violates *lo tivashal gedi be-halev imo* — one would say it is *asur mi-derabbanan*. Would we similarly apply a *pasuk* of *bal yachel* to what is only a *derabbanan*? That is the *gemara*'s challenge.

## Minhag as Neder

### Baraita Minhag Issur

The *gemara* answers *in* — yes — and cites a *baraita* in support: *devarim ha-muttarim va-aherim nahagu bahen issur* — things that are permitted, which others have treated as forbidden through practice — *i attah rashai le-hattiran bifnehem*, you may not go ahead and tell them that their practice is baseless and that what they are doing is really entirely permissible, *she-ne'emar lo yachel devaro*, because it says, "his word shall not be violated." The *baraita* thus establishes that a pattern of religious practice creates a binding commitment, and that the verse of *lo yachel devaro* applies to it — even though no one imagines that this is a formal *neder* in the technical sense. There is therefore a category of *bal yachel mi-derabbanan*.

### Types of Minhagim

This passage opens onto an extraordinarily important domain of thinking about *nedarim* that is, interestingly, almost entirely absent from the explicit discussions of our *masechet*, which consistently focuses on the power of *neder* as it operates on objects. Yet it surfaces here and there, and the implications are far-reaching.

The *neder* that most of us think of in everyday life — when we say *bli neder*, for instance — tends to invoke the idea of personal religious commitment, of obligating oneself to do something. This is closer to the concept of *nidrei mitzvah*, such as *ashne ha-shana perek ze* — the commitment to study a particular chapter — and even there some *rishonim* held it was really a *shvua* rather than a *neder*. The biblical concept of *neder*, as in *vayiddor Yaakov neder lemor*, carries both the personal obligation and the dedication of an object. But for most people in practice, the concept of *neder* centers on that sense of personal religious obligation one takes upon oneself.

One classic formulation is what is said during *hattarat nedarim*: *kol davar tov she-nahagti shalosh pe'amim ve-lo amarti she-ye-he davar ze bli neder* — that performing some positive religious act three times in a row, without having explicitly exempted it from the status of a *neder*, constitutes a kind of *kabbalat neder* to continue the practice going forward. This is not about formal *mitzvah* obligations, which would not generate a *neder*-related consequence, but about voluntary religious practices.

### Hatam Sofer Minhag

The *Hatam Sofer*, who devoted considerable energy to combating religious reform and opposing the introduction of changes into traditional practice, drew heavily on precisely this idea. Even when a proposed change could be defended on technical halachic grounds, one powerful tool for resistance was the argument of *minhag* — that this is simply not the practice, and our practice is not the practice. The obvious weakness of this argument, standing alone, is that *minhagim* do change, and it is not in itself an overwhelming response if something is *halachically* permissible.

What the *Hatam Sofer* did to strengthen the force of *minhag* was to identify it with *neder*: every *minhag* is essentially a *neder* to act in that way, making every *minhag de-oraita* in force. In one *tshuva*, discussing what may have been a case of *bishul akum* or something similar, he reasons: even if the practice in question falls only in the *derabbanan* category, or does not fall in the *derabbanan* category at all and is therefore purely a matter of *minhag*, the *minhag* itself elevates it to *de-oraita* status through the force of *neder*. This is a strikingly powerful application of the *gemara*'s principle here.

It is worth pausing to appreciate how significant a *chiddush* this represents, and how far it takes us from what one might expect to find discussed in *Masechet Nedarim*. One opens this *masechet* expecting a discussion of vows about objects — and indeed that is what fills most of the tractate. And yet, through passages like this one, the concept of *neder* quietly encompasses the entire domain of adopted personal religious practices and communal *minhagim*, with consequences reaching into major halachic controversies of the modern period.

One might note in this context that *Tosafot* actually resists reading the *baraita* as an argument by mere analogy to our case of *neder* on abstract acts. *Tosafot* argues — see the line beginning *vachen nahagu bo issur teima mah inyan zeh le-nahagu bo issur*, followed by *yesh lomar de-hai namei she-nadar ve-nitkaven le-esor atzmo gam be-davar she-ein bo mamash, ein lecha minhag gadol mi-zeh* — that making a *neder* to forbid oneself from something abstract is itself the greatest possible form of *nahagu bo issur*; they are thus not two separate things but the same phenomenon. The simpler reading, however, is that the *gemara* is reasoning by analogy: just as a religious *minhag* generates a *derabbanan bal yachel*, so too does a rabbinic extension of *neder* to abstract matters.

### Minhag Issur vs. Heter

It is also important to note that the specific language of the *baraita* — *devarim ha-muttarim va-aherim nahagu bahen issur* — is carefully limited. It speaks of treating something as a *davar asur*, and this brings the discussion much closer to the world of *nedarim*. Consider, for example, someone who refrains from eating certain foods that mimic the taste or feel of *chametz* on *Pesach* even though they are technically permitted, or who does not travel on Fridays out of concern for arriving home before *Shabbos* in time, as the *gemara* in *Pesachim* discusses. In both cases one is treating something as *asur* — and whether the subject is an object (the pseudo-*chametz* food) or an abstract action (traveling on Friday), the prohibition is structured analogously to the world of *nedarim*, including the concept of *davar she'ein bo mamash*.

This stands in contrast to the other major category of *minhag*, which involves positive religious acts — taking on a practice of performing a *mitzvah*-adjacent act on a regular basis. That category is more analogous to the discussions of *ashne ha-shana perek ze* type commitments, which shade toward the *shvua* end of the spectrum. The *gemara* here, with its invocation of *bal yachel*, is addressing specifically the *minhag issur* form. The *gemara*'s conclusion, in any case, is that a *bal yachel mi-derabbanan* does exist, and this suffices to answer the challenge to *Ravina*'s position.

## Wife's Hana'ah Case

### Pesach Timeline Mishna

With the question of *bal yachel mi-derabbanan* resolved, the *gemara* returns to the unfinished debate between *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman*. The *gemara* introduces a *mishna* — one we will be studying in approximately a month — and attempts to derive from it a ruling in the debate.

The *mishna* presents the following case: a husband declares to his wife, *she'at nehenis li ad haPesach im telchi le-veit avicha ad heChag* — you may not derive benefit from me through *Pesach* if you go to visit your father's house at any point through *Succos*. The chronological structure is clear: *Pesach* falls first in the calendar year, followed by *Succos*. So the *issur hana'ah* runs from now until *Pesach*, and the *tnai* — the condition that would trigger it — is whether she visits her father at any point from now through *Succos*.

To lay this out concretely: if she visits her father's house during the period before *Pesach*, she has triggered the *tnai* and the prohibition on *hana'ah* takes effect, meaning she cannot derive benefit from her husband through *Pesach*. But the more interesting question is what happens during the period before she has triggered the *tnai*: may she be deriving *hana'ah* from her husband in the meantime? That is precisely the question of *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman*. *Rav Yehuda* would say no — we cannot trust her to refrain from visiting her father, so we do not let her derive *hana'ah* now. *Rav Nachman* would say yes — she can be trusted, and she may derive *hana'ah* with the expectation that she will not trigger the *tnai* later.

### Inference Hana'ah Permit…

The *gemara* proceeds to examine the language of the *mishna*. It says: *halcha lifnei haPesach asurah behana'aso ad haPesach* — if she went to her father before *Pesach*, she is forbidden from deriving benefit through *Pesach*. The *gemara* infers: *halcha lifnei haPesach asurah* — only if she has gone do we say she is forbidden; *lo halcha lo* — if she has not yet gone, it is permitted for her to derive *hana'ah*, and we simply trust her not to trigger the *tnai* later. This inference would appear to support *Rav Nachman*.

*Rabbi Abba*, however, rejects this reading. *Halcha lifnei haPesach asurah ve-loka* — the *mishna* means: if she has already gone to her father's house before *Pesach*, then the *tnai* is triggered, the prohibition is clearly in force, and if she were then to derive *hana'ah*, she would receive lashes. *Lo halcha* — if she has not yet gone, then *asurah be'alma* — she is simply forbidden, but without lashes, because we do not yet know whether the *tnai* will ultimately be triggered. The *mishna*'s statement *halcha asurah* is thus not saying it is only when she has gone that we prohibit; it is specifying the more severe consequence. Even when she has not yet gone, *Rav Yehuda* would hold that we do not trust her to maintain the *tnai* and would therefore not permit her to derive *hana'ah* — though the prohibition would not carry lashes at that stage, since it would have the character of *hatra'at safek*.

### Lashes Question

At this point a critical question arises: who, in fact, receives lashes? He made the vow; she derived the *hana'ah* — why should she bear the burden of his *nedarim* and be the one who faces lashes?

The *Ran* addresses this question directly. He says: *lokah hi* — it is she who receives lashes. *Aval ihu eino loke she-harei lo asah shum issur* — he has not done anything wrong, since *she-hedirah*, he merely made the vow; it was she who violated it. *Ude-tana nami hashmi'enu me-agav orchei* — perhaps part of the point of the *mishna* is to tell us that *de-alah rechiv issura* — the prohibition rests on her, *aval lo alav klal* — not on him at all.

### Ran vs. Rambam

The *Rambam* disagrees. *Loke hu she-hedirah* — he is the one who gets lashes, but only if *im he-henah otah* — if he actively caused her to derive *hana'ah*, say, by giving her a massage or directly providing the benefit himself. In that case, he has violated his own *neder*. *Aval hi eina loka* — she does not receive lashes, because *de-kivan de-lo nadrah klal* — she never took a *neder*. *Lo shayach bah lo yachel devaro* — the verse says do not violate *your* word; this is not her word, so even if she acts against it, she has not violated the biblical prohibition of *bal yachel*.

The *Ran*, however, argues that the *Rambam*'s position is untenable, citing a *sugya* in the later chapter *Ein Bein ha-Mudar* regarding *yesh milah be-konamos*. He arrives at the following principle: *ein ha-oser over klal* — the one who creates the *issur* does not transgress by virtue of the other person's violation — *ve-ha-mudar nami vadai avar* — and the one against whom the *neder* was directed is indeed the one who transgresses. The reasoning turns on the key phrase *lo yachel devaro*: *lo yachel a-dibur ka-amar* — what cannot be violated is the *word*, the *dibur*, as an abstract entity. It does not matter ultimately whose word it is; what matters is that a prohibition-status was created through speech, and anyone who violates that status violates that *dibur*.

This framing is entirely consistent with the central emphasis of our *masechet* on *neder* as creating an objective status in an object. I can make my coffee *asur* on you; the coffee acquires that status. If you then drink it, you have violated that status — you have violated the word that created it, *lo yachel devaro* — even though it was not your word. Just as one can sanctify an animal as a *korban*, and if another person misuses it they transgress, so too here: the person who crosses the line that should not be crossed is the one who transgresses, even though they did not draw that line.

The *Rambam*, by contrast, does not fully shift the focus away from the person who took the vow. Consistent with his view (discussed in the previous *daf*) that a *davar ha-nadur* must be the kind of thing one can bring as a free-will offering to God — connecting *neder* to the classical biblical act of obligating oneself to give something to the Divine — the *Rambam* understands *lo yachel devaro* as addressed first and foremost to the one who made the vow. For the *Rambam*, getting lashes requires that both conditions be met: the objective status of the object be violated, and the violation be committed by the very person who took the vow. Without both elements, there may still be a biblical prohibition, but the formal penalty of lashes does not apply.

The practical consequence is striking. If I declare *konam* on this coffee regarding you, and you drink it: according to the *Ran*, you receive lashes, because you violated the *dibur*-created status. According to the *Rambam*, you do not receive lashes because it was not your *dibur*, and I do not receive lashes because I did not perform the violating act — unless I actively caused you to drink it. In that case, the *Rambam* would say I get lashes as the one whose vow has now been violated by my own hand. If neither actively violated — if you simply drank what I passively left for you — then according to the *Rambam*, no one receives lashes, even though there may well be a biblical *issur* in play.

## Later Condition Violation

### Shaatnez Alternative

The *gemara* returns to the debate and presents what amounts to a mirror-image *mishna*. The case here is: *shaatnez li al hachag im telchi le-veit avicha ad haPesach* — she cannot derive benefit through *Succos* if she goes to her father's house before *Pesach*. Here the *issur hana'ah* extends all the way to *Succos*, and the *tnai* is limited to the period before *Pesach*. The structure is the reverse of the earlier case: the *tnai* window comes first, and the *issur* period extends far beyond it.

The *mishna* states: *halcha lifnei haPesach asurah behana'ah ad haChag* — if she went to her father's house before *Pesach*, she is forbidden from deriving benefit all the way through *Succos*. *U-muteres leilech achar haPesach* — and she is permitted to go to her father's house after *Pesach*. The *rishonim* discuss what the *chiddush* of this latter clause is; one reading is that we are not concerned that permitting her to go after *Pesach* will somehow lead retroactively or by extension to a prohibited going before *Pesach*. Then the *mishna* says: *halcha asurah, lo halcha lo* — if she went, she is forbidden; if she did not go, she is not.

### Rava's Clarification

The *gemara* again presses: *lo halcha lo* — does this mean that as long as she has not triggered the *tnai*, she is permitted to derive *hana'ah*? That would support *Rav Nachman*.

*Rava* answers: *hu hadin afilu lo halcha asurah* — the same logic applies here: even if she has not yet gone to her father's house, she is not permitted to derive *hana'ah*. *Halcha asurah ve-loka* — if she has already gone and it is before *Pesach*, then the *tnai* is triggered, the *issur* is in full force, and if she derives *hana'ah* she will receive lashes — whether she or he, depending on one's reading of the *Ran* and *Rambam*. *Lo halcha asurah be'alma* — if she has not yet gone, she is still forbidden from deriving *hana'ah*, but it is a lesser prohibition without lashes, because the *tnai* has not yet been triggered and we do not yet know its ultimate status. The *mishna*'s *lo halcha lo* is therefore not saying she is permitted to derive *hana'ah* in the interim; it is saying she will not face lashes in that period. Whether she may derive *hana'ah* in the interim remains the open question between *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman*, and the *pshat* of the *mishna* remains difficult for *Rav Yehuda*'s position.

## Meisivei Bread Case

### Bread Condition Case

The *gemara* raises a *meisivei* — a challenge from a *mishnaic* source. The case is: *kikar zo alai hayom im eilech le-makom ploni le-machar* — this loaf of bread is forbidden to me today if I go to Philadelphia tomorrow. The *mishna* then states: *achal, harei zeh be-val eilech* — if he ate the bread, he is now under a prohibition against going to Philadelphia tomorrow, as though there were a new biblical prohibition created by the *neder*.

### Permit or Forbid Eating

The *gemara* notes with some amusement the formulation *harei zeh be-val eilech* — as if there were now a new *lo ta'aseh*, "do not go to Philadelphia," which he has generated through his *neder*. But the *gemara*'s question is pointed: *mi katani ochel?* — does the *mishna* say he is *permitted* to eat the bread? It says *achal katani* — it says "if he ate," not "he may eat." The *mishna* is simply describing the consequences of having already eaten the bread; it never addresses whether eating the bread was permitted in the first place. This distinction is crucial for the debate between *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman*.

### Rav Yehuda Response

*Rav Yehuda* explains accordingly: the *mishna* sets up the case by assuming the bread was already eaten — not because it was permitted to eat, but because, from *Rav Yehuda*'s perspective, he was not supposed to eat the bread to begin with. Since the *mishna* could not say "you are permitted to eat" — because *Rav Yehuda* would say you are not — it framed the case as *achal*, taking the act as a fait accompli. Once the bread has been eaten, regardless of whether it should have been, the *mishna* then addresses the consequence: you are now under *bal eilech*, do not go to Philadelphia. *Ela ayadi de-tana reisha achal, de-lo mistani leh ochel* — since the first clause had to be framed around the fact of having eaten rather than the permission to eat, the *seifa* continues in kind, addressing what follows from that fact. Nothing can be inferred from this *mishna* about whether one was originally permitted to eat the bread before the *tnai* could be determined.

The *gemara*'s *maskana* is thus that the debate between *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman* remains unresolved by the *mishnaic* sources. Many *rishonim* observe that the *gemara* works so hard to deflect challenges to *Rav Yehuda*'s position — consistently providing him with answers — that one might infer the *gemara* is inclined to rule with him. The *Ran* leans toward *Rav Yehuda*: where a *neder* has been taken with a condition whose fulfillment is uncertain, we tell the person not to transgress the *neder* out of concern that he will trigger the *tnai* later.

## Neder for Action Control

### Indirect Action Control

This *sugya* invites us to step back and appreciate a broader phenomenon at work throughout these cases. A *neder* is about an object — it creates a status in a physical or semi-physical entity, making it *asur*. A *shvua*, by contrast, is about the person who swears. And there is a strong reluctance to take *shvuot* precisely because of *lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa et shemo lashav* — the gravity of invoking God's name in a false or unfulfilled oath. How, then, does one obligate oneself to do or to avoid doing a particular action, when one wants to avoid the weightier instrument of *shvua*?

The answer is to use *neder* instrumentally, directing it at an object in such a way that the *neder* will create indirect pressure on one's actions. The *neder* does not forbid the action; it makes the object *asur*, and then the stakes of the *neder* bear upon whether the action is taken.

### Fruit Trip Example

A concrete example: suppose I am trying to ensure that I actually make a long-deferred trip to Philadelphia. Rather than swear that I will go — which would invoke *shem shamayim* — I can say: all fruit in the world shall be *asur* to me if I fail to make this trip. Now I am strongly motivated to make the trip, not because the act of not going is directly forbidden but because I do not want to suffer the consequence of losing access to all fruit. The *neder* has translated into behavioral compulsion through the object.

The case of the loaf of bread works the same way, though it introduces a twilight zone of uncertainty. I said: this loaf is *asur* to me today if I go to Philadelphia tomorrow. Now, during this period, I do not know whether the *neder* will ultimately kick in. This is what generates the entire debate of *Rav Yehuda* and *Rav Nachman*: may I eat the bread and trust myself not to go tomorrow? The phenomenon is perfectly natural — using *nedarim* on objects to push oneself toward or away from certain actions — but the *tnai* structure introduces complexities about how much we can trust the person to maintain the condition.

## Husband Forbids Sex

### Konam Sex Mishna

The *gemara* now turns to a *mishna* that asks whether a husband can use *konam* to forbid conjugal relations: *ha-omer le-isha konam she-ani mishamshech harei zeh be-val yachel dvarav* — if the man says to his wife *konam* that I will not have sex with you, this is subject to *bal yachel*. Here we are operating, as the *mishna* established, in the *derabbanan* realm — the husband is forbidding not his body per se but the *hana'ah* of the act, the abstract pleasure of the sexual encounter, which is a *davar she'ein bo mamash*.

### She'ar Kesut Obligation

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *ve-ha mishtabed lah mid'oraita* — but he is biblically indebted to her, as it says *she'erah kesutah ve'onatah lo yigra* — he cannot withhold her food, clothing, or conjugal relations. One might think that this biblical obligation simply overrides any *neder* he could take. But that cannot be the full answer — after all, a person can make a *neder* that *tefillin* be *konam* on him, thereby indirectly undermining his biblical obligation to wear *tefillin*, since the *neder* forbids the object rather than directly confronting the *mitzvah*. The same logic would seem to apply here: let him forbid the *hana'ah* on her, and then when it comes time for the *mitzvas onah*, he simply cannot fulfill it because the object — as it were — is *asur*.

### Wife's Property Rights

The reason the *gemara* rejects this approach, however, is not the existence of an obligation but the existence of a *shibud* — a property claim. The *Ran* formulates this with precision: *de-lo matzi avid de-mid'oraita meshubed lah ve-havei ke-oser nichsei chavero al chaveiro* — he cannot make this *neder* because biblically he is *meshubed* to her, and his attempt to make his sexual pleasure *asur* on her is like trying to forbid a friend's property to that same friend. She has a form of property rights, a lien, on his body with respect to conjugal relations. It is hers, in a meaningful sense. And one cannot make *asur* on another person what already belongs to that person.

The distinction is important. Obligation alone would not block the *neder*; the indirect structure of *neder*-on-object routinely allows a person to undermine his own obligations. What blocks the *neder* here is *ownership*: she effectively owns a claim to his conjugal attention, and he therefore cannot make it *asur* to her.

### Pleasure Forbidden on Se…

The *gemara* therefore specifies the loophole: *be-omer hana'as tashmishech alai* — if instead the husband says, "the pleasure I would derive from you is *konam* on *me*," that formulation works. He is not forbidding her property to her; he is forbidding her property to himself. Anyone may make anything *asur* to themselves, including another person's property. He can make her car *asur* to himself; he can similarly make her sexual pleasure *asur* to himself. The *neder* thus takes effect. Indirectly, this prevents him from fulfilling *mitzvas onah*, but as we have established, indirect undermining of a *mitzvah* through *neder*-on-object does not invalidate the *neder*. They could not sustain the marriage under these conditions and would be required to divorce, but the *neder* itself is valid.

## Wife Forbids Sex

### Wife Forbids Pleasure

The *gemara* then turns to the reverse case. *Rav Kahana* states: if a wife says *tashmishi alecha* — my sexual pleasure is *konam* on you — then *kofin oso u-meshamshaseto* — we compel her nonetheless to have conjugal relations with her husband. She cannot make this *neder* effective against him.

### Husband's Shibud

The reasoning is exactly parallel to the earlier case. *De-shibuda meshubada lei* — her body, with respect to conjugal relations, is *meshubed* to him. Just as she has a property claim on him for *onah*, he has a corresponding claim on her. Therefore, when she attempts to forbid her sexual pleasure on him, she is in the same position as the husband who tried to forbid his sexual pleasure on his wife: she is attempting to make *asur* on the other person something that already belongs to that person. It cannot be done.

However, if she were to say *hana'as tashmishecha alai* — the sexual pleasure I would derive from you is *konam* on *me* — that formulation would work, on the same logic as the husband's case. She is making something *asur* to herself, and *she'ein machilin lo le-adam davar ha-asur lo* — one does not cause a person to receive what is forbidden to them. At that point, she is forbidden to have conjugal relations, and they would be required to divorce.

### Equal Marital Rights

This *gemara* is, in the context of the broader *masechet*, extraordinarily significant — and not only for the halachic mechanics of *nedarim*. What is striking is that the *gemara* begins with the husband's case, and there it posits not merely that he has a *mitzvah* to have sex with his wife, but that she holds an actual *shibud* on him — a property claim on his conjugal attentions. That framing is then replicated precisely and symmetrically in the wife's case: his *shibud* on her mirrors hers on him.

The conventional framing of marital sexuality in halachic literature centers on the verse *she'erah kesutah ve'onatah lo yigra* — his *mitzvah* to provide for her conjugal needs — and the wife's right to demand sex at regular intervals. What receives far less explicit attention is his corresponding right to have sex with her, and whether that right is personal obligation or property-claim. This *gemara* answers that question by using the exact same language in both directions: *meshubed lah* and *meshubeda lei*. The *shibud* is equal and parallel. One spouse cannot make forbidden to the other what that other person has a property claim upon, and this principle applies identically regardless of which direction the *neder* is attempted.

In a broader cultural context where woman as sexual property of the man has been the dominant framework for millennia, this *sugya* — encountered here in *Masechet Nedarim* of all places — quietly posits the man as equally the sexual property of the woman within the marriage, using precisely the same legal language in both cases. It is a remarkable conceptual statement about the nature of marital rights, and it emerges through the very particular lens of how *nedarim* interact with the property structure of conjugal obligations.
