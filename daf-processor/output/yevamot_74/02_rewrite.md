# Yevamot 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arel & Maaser Sheni

### Two Maasrot

The question before the *gemara* is whether an *arel* — an uncircumcised man — is permitted to eat *maaser sheni*. A *braita* had already been cited stating that an *arel* is forbidden in *maaser*, and the *gemara* attempted to reinterpret this as referring to *maaser behema* — the tithe of animals — rather than the grain tithe. That reading was clearly strained. The *gemara* then fell back on a second escape: the *braita* might be referring to *maaser rishon* according to the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that *maaser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim* and is thus treated analogously to *teruma*. This is admittedly a radical position of Rabbi Meir, but it is the vehicle the *gemara* uses to avoid conceding that an *arel* is forbidden in *maaser sheni*.

### Chiya Bar Rav

The *gemara* presses further: come and hear. Rabbi Chiya bar Rav of Difti teaches a *braita*: an *arel* is forbidden in two *maasrot* — *shtei maasrot*. The natural reading is that one of these is *maaser dagan*, the grain tithe, and the other is *maaser behema*, the animal tithe — the latter being *kodshim* and thus clearly forbidden. If so, the prohibition on the grain tithe establishes that an *arel* is forbidden in *maaser sheni*.

### Maaser Rishon Defense

The *gemara* deflects once more: here too, the reference is to *maaser rishon* and *maaser behema*, invoking Rabbi Meir's position. The *braita* still does not compel the conclusion that *maaser sheni* is forbidden to an *arel*.

### Maaser Rishon Again

The same move is made a second time: whenever a *braita* appears to establish the prohibition of an *arel* in the grain tithe, the *gemara* reasserts that the reference is to *maaser rishon* according to Rabbi Meir. Each iteration of this defense holds off the proof but is clearly makeshift.

### Onen Pattern

The *gemara* now introduces a structurally elegant *braita* that compares three categories of persons — *onen*, *tvul yom*, and *mechusar kippurim* — against three categories of sanctity: *maaser* (which, as will become clear from the cases, can only mean *maaser sheni*), *teruma*, and *para aduma*. The pattern is a rotating one: each of the three persons is forbidden in exactly one of the three categories and permitted in the other two, and the forbidden category differs for each. This creates a kind of three-way matrix in which every combination is covered.

### Onen Rules

An *onen* — a mourner before the burial of his close relative — is forbidden in *maaser*, as the verse states *lo achalti be'oni mimenu*, and permitted in *teruma* and *para aduma*. The permission to eat *teruma* was established earlier in the *sugya*. The permission to perform the *avoda* of the *para aduma* — including the burning and the sprinkling — follows from the fact that, although an *onen* cannot eat *kodshim*, the *para aduma* is not technically a *korban*; it belongs to the category of *bedek habayit* and is not treated as a sacrificial offering in the usual sense.

### Tosafot Onen

Rashi notes parenthetically that the permission of an *onen* to perform the *para aduma* follows by *kal vachomer*: if a *tvul yom* can do so, certainly an *onen* can. Tosafot pushes back on the logic of this inference: it does not always follow that leniency in one area implies leniency in another, given that the *onen* is actually more restricted than the *tvul yom* regarding *maaser*, while the *tvul yom* is more restricted than the *onen* regarding *teruma*. Tosafot therefore clarifies that the only prohibition on an *onen* in the context of *maaser sheni* is eating it — *le'achilah* — whereas touching *maaser sheni* is permitted for both; the *onen*'s permission to perform the *para aduma* speaks to physical involvement, not consumption, and the two categories therefore cannot be simply compared.

### Onen Logic

Why should an *onen* be more restricted regarding *maaser sheni* than *teruma*, given that *teruma* is generally the more stringent category? The answer lies in the character of *maaser sheni*: it must be brought to Jerusalem and consumed there, making it structurally analogous to a *korban*. Just as an *onen* is forbidden in a *korban*, he is forbidden in *maaser sheni*. *Teruma*, by contrast — though possessing greater *kedusha* — is eaten anywhere, and thus lacks the *korban*-like quality that triggers the *onen*'s restriction.

### Tvul Yom Rules

A *tvul yom* — one who has immersed in a *mikvah* but for whom nightfall has not yet arrived — is forbidden in *teruma*, because a *tvul yom* has the status of a *sheni* and would render *teruma* a *shlishi*, disqualifying it. He is, however, permitted to perform the *avoda* of the *para aduma* — this is in fact the famous *halakha* that the *para aduma* was specifically performed by a *tvul yom*, demonstrating that *tahor min hakhlal de'ika tamei*. He may also eat *maaser sheni*.

### Mechusar Kippurim

A *mechusar kippurim* — one who has immersed and for whom nightfall has passed, but who has yet to bring the required *korban* — represents a weaker degree of residual impurity than the *tvul yom*. He is forbidden in *para aduma* but permitted in *teruma* and *maaser sheni*. This requires explanation: if the *tvul yom* — who is more restricted than the *mechusar kippurim* in most respects — can perform the *para aduma*, why can the *mechusar kippurim* not?

### Tvul Yom Para

The resolution is that the *tvul yom* who may perform the *para aduma* is one whose remaining purification process consists entirely of waiting — no additional action, no *korban*, is pending. For instance, someone who touched a carcass, immersed, and need only wait for nightfall: that person may perform the *para aduma*. But a *tvul yom* who still has a *korban* to bring — someone in a longer purification process such as a *zavah* — cannot perform the *para aduma* even after immersion, and cannot do so even after nightfall until the *korban* is brought. When we say the *tvul yom* can perform the *para aduma*, we mean specifically the person whose entire remaining process will resolve on its own within a matter of hours.

### Missing Pattern

Now the *gemara* turns to use this *braita* as a proof regarding the *arel*. If it were true that an *arel* can eat *maaser sheni*, the *braita*'s rotating pattern would have included a fourth configuration: an *arel* is forbidden in *teruma* — which we know — permitted in *para aduma* — since there is no reason to exclude him — and permitted in *maaser sheni*. This configuration would have fit perfectly into the pattern being elaborated, yet it is conspicuously absent. The omission implies that an *arel* cannot be described as forbidden in *teruma* but permitted in *maaser sheni*, because in fact he is forbidden in *maaser sheni* as well.

### Rabbi Akiva Defense

The *gemara* deflects once more: this *braita* might reflect the position of Rabbi Akiva, who — as will be elaborated — treats an *arel* as equivalent to a *tamei* person in all matters where personal status creates a prohibition. According to Rabbi Akiva, an *arel* would also be forbidden in *para aduma*, since the verse states *ve'asaf ish tahor*, and the *arel* is equated to one who is not *tahor*. If so, the configuration of *arel* being permitted in *para aduma* and *maaser sheni* but forbidden only in *teruma* was never available to the *braita* — and its absence therefore proves nothing about *maaser sheni*.

## Rabbi Akiva Arel

### Ish Ish

With Rabbi Akiva's position now brought into focus, the *gemara* cites his derivation explicitly: *Rabbi Akiva omer, ish ish le'rabos es ha'arel* — the doubled language of *ish ish* in the verse about the *tamei* person who may not eat *teruma* is used by Rabbi Akiva to include the *arel* in that prohibition. The broader implication drawn from this is that wherever a *tamei* person is forbidden due to his personal status — not merely because of the risk of contaminating food, but because of who he is — an *arel* is likewise forbidden.

### Yosef HaBavli

The *gemara* now asks: who is the *tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi Akiva and holds that an *arel* is not equivalent to a *tamei*? The answer is the *tanna kamma* of the *braita* of Rabbi Yosef haBavli.

### Para Srifa

The relevant *braita* states: *sreifat onen u'mechusar kippurim kesheira* — the *tanna kamma* holds that both the *onen* and the *mechusar kippurim* are valid performers of the burning of the *para aduma*. Rabbi Yosef haBavli, however, distinguishes: an *onen* is valid (*kesheira*), but a *mechusar kippurim* is invalid (*pesulah*). Both positions concern which residual-impurity categories are excluded from the *para aduma* by the verse's requirement of *ish tahor*.

### Rashi Inference

Rashi infers from this debate a parallel controversy about the *arel*: just as the *tannaim* disagree about whether a *mechusar kippurim* may perform the *para aduma*, so too they would disagree about whether an *arel* — given his contested equivalence to a *tamei* — may perform it.

### Tosafot Object

Tosafot objects to Rashi's inference on logical grounds: the dispute over *mechusar kippurim* concerns the extent of his residual impurity, while the dispute over the *arel* concerns a categorically different question — whether he is classified as *tamei* at all. These are independent considerations, and one does not follow from the other. Indeed, Tosafot appears to have had a different text of the *braita* that explicitly mentioned *arel* rather than leaving it to inference by analogy. Our standard text does not mention *arel* directly, and Rashi's inference by analogy is what fills the gap — but it is an inference Tosafot explicitly rejects.

## Gezeira Shava

### Mimenu Link

Beyond the series of *braitot*, Rabbi Yitzchak independently establishes that an *arel* is forbidden in *maaser*. He derives this through a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *mimenu*: in *maaser* the verse states *lo achalti be'oni mimenu*, and in *pesach* the Torah states *al tochlu mimenu na* and *lo totiru mimenu ad boker*. Just as *mimenu* at *pesach* implies an *arel* is forbidden — since he may not eat the *korban pesach* — so too *mimenu* at *maaser* implies an *arel* is forbidden there as well.

### Weight Challenge

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge: if the word is not free — *mufneh* — at both ends of the *gezeirah shavah*, the analogy is vulnerable to a logical rebuttal. In this case, the rebuttal is compelling: *pesach* is weightier than *maaser*, since one is liable for *pesach* meat on account of *pigul*, *notar*, and *tumah* — none of which apply to *maaser sheni*. If the *gezeirah shavah* is not ironclad — because the word is not free — one could argue that the severity of *pesach* accounts for the restriction and it cannot be extended to the lighter *maaser*.

### Free Words

The resolution lies in establishing that the word *mimenu* appears three times at *pesach*, and at least one of those appearances is free — not required by the grammatical or legal demands of the verse. It is the *mufneh* — free — word that enables a valid *gezeirah shavah*.

### Tochlu Use

The first occurrence of *mimenu* at *pesach* is in the phrase *al tochlu mimenu na* — do not eat of it raw. This is grammatically required; the verse cannot function without *mimenu* and it therefore does no independent legal work beyond what the verse demands.

### Free for Gezeira

The second occurrence is the word free to anchor the *gezeirah shavah*, available precisely because the first was required by plain grammar and the third is dealt with separately.

### Notar Clause

The third occurrence of *mimenu* at *pesach* appears in the clause *ve'hanotar mimenu ad boker ba'esh tisrofu* — what remains of it until morning you shall burn with fire. The question is whether this *mimenu* is itself doing independent legal work, or whether it is merely incidental to the larger phrase.

### Notar Purpose

There is a dispute about what this third clause accomplishes. One view holds that the phrase *ba'esh tisrofu* following *lo totiru mimenu* creates a *mitzvat asei* linked to the *lo ta'aseh* of not leaving the *pesach* overnight — making the prohibition *nitak la'asei* — and thus the word *mimenu* was simply carried along for linguistic flow, doing no independent work. Another view holds that the purpose of this verse is to establish that if *notar* remains, one does not burn it on the morning of the 15th of Nisan — which is *yom tov* — but waits for the morning of the 16th. Either way, the *mimenu* in this third clause is not doing independent legal work; it was written along with the phrase that was needed, whether for *nitak la'asei* or for the teaching about a second morning of burning. The conclusion is the same: the *mimenu* at the end is written only because the larger phrase was needed, and is therefore genuinely free, available to serve as one node of the *gezeirah shavah*.

### Maaser Mimenu

The *gemara* turns to the three appearances of *mimenu* in the *maaser* context: *lo vi'arti mimenu be'oni*, *velo vi'arti mimenu be'tamei*, and *velo natati mimenu le'met*.

### Maaser Grammar

The first *mimenu* in the *maaser vidui* passage is grammatically required — it is the natural mode of expression and does no more than what the text demands.

### Abbahu Teaching

The second *mimenu* — *velo vi'arti mimenu be'tamei* — is the source for the teaching of Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: this prohibition of burning *tamei maaser sheni* applies specifically to *maaser sheni*, whereas *teruma* that has become *tamei* may indeed be burned. The *mimenu* at *maaser* thus derives a specific law about *maaser sheni*, and is not free.

### Anointing Tamei

The third *mimenu* — *velo natati mimenu le'met* — is the source for Reish Lakish's teaching, cited in the name of Rabbi Samya: how do we know that *maaser sheni* which has become *tamei* may be used for *sichah* — anointing the body — even though it may not be eaten? From the word *mimenu*: I did not give *of it* to the dead. The inference is that what I did not do for the dead — anoint them — I may do for the living. What is something equally relevant to both the living and the dead? Anointing. The living person may therefore use *tamei maaser sheni* for *sichah*. This is a striking *halakha*: even though you cannot burn *tamei maaser sheni* for fuel or light, you may anoint your body with it, because *sichah* is a form of bodily consumption that does not constitute eating or drinking and therefore remains legitimate even in a state of *tumah*.

### Sichah Debate

The *gemara* challenges this derivation. Mar Zutra asks: perhaps the verse means that one should not use the proceeds of selling the *maaser sheni* to buy a coffin or shrouds for the dead — implying that one may sell it and buy clothes or other items for the living? Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua responds: *mimenu migufo* — *of it* means direct use of the item itself. You do not make shrouds from produce; therefore, the verse must be speaking of direct bodily application, not indirect commercial use. Rav Ashi adds a parallel argument: *lo natati* is analogous to *lo achalti* — just as eating is a direct consumption of the object itself, so too the giving spoken of here must be a direct use of the *maaser sheni* itself on the body.

### One Side Free

The analysis reveals an asymmetry: at *pesach*, one of the three *mimenu* words is genuinely free. At *maaser*, however, all three words are occupied — none is free. The *gezeirah shavah* therefore has only one free node, at *pesach*, and is technically a *gezeirah shavah mitzad echad* — free on only one side. According to the view that such a one-sided *gezeirah shavah* can be challenged, the weight-of-*pesach* argument remains a valid rebuttal and the proof is not ironclad.

### Abaye Free Sides

Abaye resolves the problem by drawing on a teaching of Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha. If the teaching that *tamei teruma* may be burned — which Rabbi Abbahu derived from the second *maaser mimenu* — can actually be derived from a different source entirely, then that second *mimenu* is genuinely free after all, and we have *mimenu* free on both sides of the *gezeirah shavah*.

### Teruma Burning

The alternative source is Rav Nachman's teaching in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: the verse states *va'ani hineh natati lecha et mishmeret terumotai* — I have given you the guardianship of my *terumot*, in the plural. The Torah speaks of two *terumot* — one *tehorah* and one *teme'ah*. Even *tamei teruma* has a use: *lehasikah tachat tavshilcha* — you may burn it to fuel your cooking fire. Since the permission to burn *tamei teruma* can be derived from this verse about *teruma* directly, the second *mimenu* at *maaser* — which Rabbah bar Avuha used for the same teaching — is freed from that obligation. With the second *maaser mimenu* now available as a free word, both sides of the *gezeirah shavah* are free, and the analogy between *pesach* and *maaser sheni* regarding the *arel* is established on firm ground.

## Conclusion & Tamei

### Summary Arel

The *gemara*'s sustained treatment of the *arel* and *maaser sheni* draws to a close. Multiple *braitot* — each of which the *gemara* attempted to neutralize through various reinterpretations — consistently pointed toward the same conclusion: an *arel* is forbidden in *maaser sheni*. Rabbi Yitzchak then provided an independent derivation via *gezeirah shavah* linking *pesach* and *maaser* through the word *mimenu*, and Abaye secured both sides of that *gezeirah shavah* by showing that the relevant *maaser* word is genuinely free. The overall conclusion is that an *arel* is forbidden in *pesach*, forbidden in *teruma*, and likewise forbidden in *maaser sheni*.

### All Tamei

With the *arel* question resolved, the *mishnah* moves on to what would seem to be the more self-evident clause: *ve'chol ha'tmei'im* — all *tamei* persons are likewise forbidden in *teruma*. This transition shifts the discussion from a category defined by a physical status (*arel*) to the broader world of ritual impurity.

## Tamei Teruma

### Yochanan Source

The *gemara* asks: where is the source that all *tamei* persons are forbidden in *teruma*? Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Yishmael, identifies the verse: *ish ish mi'zera aharon ve'hu tzaru'a o zav ba'kodashim lo yochal ad asher yithar* — any man of the seed of Aharon who is afflicted with *tzara'at* or is a *zav* shall not eat of the holy things until he becomes *tahor*. The verse continues with additional categories of *tamei* persons. The operative question is: what do *kodashim* — holy things — refer to here?

### Zera Aharon

The key to the identification lies in the phrase *mi'zera aharon* — of the seed of Aharon. The *gemara* asks: what holy thing is shared equally by all members of the seed of Aharon? *Kodshei kodashim* cannot be the answer, because those may only be eaten by male *kohanim* — women of priestly lineage cannot eat them. *Teruma*, on the other hand, may be eaten by both male and female members of a *kohen*'s household. The *gemara* therefore identifies *kodashim* in this verse as *teruma*: *havei omer zo terumah*.

### Women Exclusion

A possible alternative is raised: perhaps the reference is to *chazeh ve'shok* — the breast and thigh of the *shelamim* offering, which are *kodshim kalim* that women of priestly families may eat. The *gemara* rejects this: a *bat kohen* who marries an *Yisrael* and is subsequently widowed without children returns to eating *teruma*, but cannot return to eating *kodshim kalim* of her father's house — this is derived from a separate verse. So *kodshim kalim* is not equally accessible to all *zera aharon* once they enter various marital situations. *Teruma* remains the most universally shared form of *kodesh* for all of *zera aharon*.

One might further argue that even *teruma* is not truly universal: a *chalalah* — a woman born of a disqualified priestly union — cannot eat *teruma*. The *gemara* resolves this: a *chalalah* is not considered *zera aharon* in the relevant halachic sense. Although she is obviously his biological descendant and carries all obligations of *kibbud av va'em*, her lineage is no longer considered *meyuchas* in the priestly sense; a male in her position would not be a *kohen*. The phrase *zera aharon* therefore points precisely to *teruma*.

### Ad Asher Yithar

The verse states these *tamei* persons may not eat *kodashim* — now established as *teruma* — *ad asher yithar*, until they become *tahor*. A question arises: how do we know this phrase means specifically until nightfall following immersion — which would exclude a *tvul yom* but permit a *mechusar kippurim*? Perhaps *ad asher yithar* means until one has completed the entire purification process, including bringing a *korban*? If so, even a *mechusar kippurim* would be forbidden in *teruma*.

### Tanna Kamma

The *gemara* cites the school of Rabbi Yishmael: the verse is speaking specifically of a *zav ba'al shtei re'iyot* — a *zav* who experienced only two sightings — and a *metzora musgar* — a leper in the confined stage but not the declared stage. These two cases are cited as parallel to the case of one who becomes *tamei* through contact with a corpse (*tmei nefesh*).

### Sunset Meaning

The logic is that one who is *tamei* through corpse contact does not bring a *korban* at all. Since the verse draws these cases together, when we read *ad asher yithar* in this context, we read it as applicable to persons who likewise are not in a *korban*-bearing process. A *zav* with only two sightings does not bring a *korban*; a *metzora musgar* does not bring a *korban*. For such people, *tahor* means: go to the *mikvah* and wait for nightfall. Hence *ad asher yithar* signifies sunset alone.

### Korban Extension

The *gemara* resists the inference: perhaps this means only that those without a *korban* obligation need wait only for nightfall — but those who are obligated to bring a *korban* must wait until they have done so, even for *teruma*. This is a reasonable position and is not automatically refuted by the reading of the verse. The *gemara* presses: what then is the source that permits a *mechusar kippurim* to eat *teruma* the night before bringing the *korban*?

### Mishna Hierarchy

The *gemara* brings the *mishna*: one who immerses and emerges may eat *maaser*; once the sun sets (*he'erev shemesh*), one may eat *teruma*; once the *korban* is brought, one may eat *kodshim*. This neatly establishes three ascending levels of purity required for three ascending levels of sanctity.

### Mechusar Position

The *gemara* asks: where is the proof that specifically the *mechusar kippurim* — one who still needs to bring a *korban* — is permitted in *teruma* the night before? The source for this precise ruling requires its own derivation.

## Three Psukim (IV)

### Rava Teaching

Rava, citing Rav Chisda, explains that three verses underlie this three-tier hierarchy, each corresponding to one level of sanctity.

### Mikvah Pesach

The first verse states *lo yochal min ha'kodashim ki im rachatz besaro ba'mayim* — he shall not eat of the holy things unless he has bathed his flesh in water. This establishes that immersion in a *mikvah* alone is sufficient to permit eating something. This is the lightest level, corresponding to *maaser*.

### Korban Kodshim

The third verse states *ve'chipper aleiha ha'kohen ve'tahera* — the *kohen* shall atone for her and she shall be *tahor*. This establishes that bringing a *korban* is required to achieve the fullest *tahara*, corresponding to *kodshim*.

### Sunset Teruma

The second verse — *uva ha'shemesh ve'taher ve'achar yochal min ha'kodashim* — establishes that sunset marks the purity threshold for the intermediate level, corresponding to *teruma*.

### Teruma Weight

How do we know *teruma* is heavier than *maaser* and lighter than *kodshim*? A mnemonic is employed: *mechpaz*. *Mem* stands for *mita bidei shamayim* — one who eats *teruma* in a state of impurity incurs death at the hands of Heaven; *chet* is *chomesh* — one who eats *teruma* accidentally must add a fifth; *peh* is the absence of *pidyon* — *teruma* has no redemption, whereas *maaser sheni* does; *zayin* is *zar* — *teruma* is forbidden to non-*kohanim*. Against this, one might argue that *maaser* is weightier, represented by the mnemonic *hadastav*: *hei* is *hava'as makom* — *maaser sheni* must be brought to Jerusalem; *dalet* is *vidui* — a declaration is required; *samech* represents that an *onen* is forbidden in it; *tet* is *tumah* — *tamei maaser sheni* may not be burned for benefit; and *bet* is *biur* — one must eliminate *maaser* from the house every three years. The *gemara* resolves the question: *mita bidei shamayim* tips the balance — the consequence of death at the hands of Heaven makes *teruma* demonstrably weightier. Rava adds that even without invoking *mita*, the first verse — which speaks of *nefesh* — itself points to *maaser* as the lightest category.

### Nefesh Logic

The verse that requires only immersion reads: *nefesh asher tiga bo ve'tame'ah, ki im rachatz besaro ba'mayim*. The word *nefesh* — person — implies something available to every person. *Teruma* is restricted to *kohanim* and their households; it is not *shaveh bechol nefesh* — equal for all people. The only form of *kodesh* that all people may eat is *maaser sheni*. Therefore the verse permitting eating after immersion alone must be speaking of *maaser sheni*.

## Yoledet Case (III)

### Yoledet Status

The remaining question — whether a *mechusar kippurim* who is obligated to bring a *korban* may nevertheless eat *teruma* before bringing it — is answered through the case of a *yoledet*, a woman who has given birth. Her purification process spans forty days for a boy (and eighty for a girl). At the end of seven days she immerses in the *mikvah*. From that point, any blood she produces is *dam tohar* and does not render her *tamei*. However, she is not fully *tehorah* until she brings her *korban* on day forty.

### Long Tvul Yom

After her immersion on day seven, she enters a prolonged *tvul yom* status — she has immersed but her effective nightfall does not arrive until the night before day forty. During these thirty-three intermediate days she is called a *tvulas yom aroch* — a *tvul yom* of extended duration. In that period she may eat *maaser* but not *teruma*.

### Night Before

On the night before day forty — the evening preceding the morning on which she brings her *korban* — she transitions to *mechusar kippurim* status. She is now permitted to eat *teruma*, even though she has not yet brought her *korban*. The following morning, when she brings the *korban*, she may eat *kodshim* as well.

## Three Psukim (IV) (II)

### Kodshim Weight

*Kodshim* is weightier than *teruma*. The mnemonic is *pinkakas*: *peh* is *pigul*, *nun* is *notar*, *kaf* is *karet*, *ayin* is *meila*, and *samech* is the general prohibition of *assur*. None of these — with the exception of some overlap — apply to *teruma*, which instead carries *mita bidei shamayim* rather than *karet*. While one might object that *teruma*'s features from *mechpaz* are more numerous, the *gemara*'s conclusion is that the *kodshim* characteristics as a collection outweigh those of *teruma*.

## Yoledet Case (III)

### Milos Yamim

Abaye demonstrates the two-stage purity of the *yoledet* from two verses. The first verse states *ad milos yemei tahara* — until the completion of the days of purity. This verse establishes that once the days of purity are complete — that is, when nightfall arrives on the night before day forty — she is in some sense *tehorah*.

## Three Psukim (IV) (III)……

### Rabba Language

Rabba offers an alternative derivation: even without counting features, the language of the verse *ve'chipper aleiha ha'kohen ve'tahera* itself establishes that *kodshim* is weightier. The language of *kapparah* — atonement — is uniquely associated with *korban*-bearing, and the fact that the verse uses this formulation to mark the point of *tahara* for *kodshim* demonstrates that *kodshim* occupies a categorically higher level of sanctity.

## Yoledet Case (III)…

### Vechiper

The second verse states *ve'chipper aleiha ha'kohen ve'tahera* — after the *kohen* brings the atonement-*korban* for her, she becomes *tehorah* for *kodshim*. This verse marks the moment of full *tahara*.

### Resolution

Two verses therefore govern the *yoledet*'s purity: the first — *ad milos yemei tahara* — applies to *teruma*, marking the night before the *korban* as her point of *tahara* for purposes of eating *teruma*; the second — *ve'chipper aleiha* — applies to *kodshim*, marking the bringing of the *korban* as her point of *tahara* for eating sanctified offerings.

### Inference

From this we draw the critical inference: a woman who is *mechusar kippurim* — who still has a pending *korban* obligation — is nonetheless *tahor* for *teruma* from the night before the *korban* is brought. This is the halachic proof that a *mechusar kippurim* is not forbidden in *teruma* even when a *korban* is required.

## Three Psukim (IV) (III)……

### Tamei Status

The second verse's formulation — *ve'chipper aleiha ha'kohen ve'tahera*, signaling that she becomes *tehorah* only through the *korban* — implies that before the *korban* is brought, she retains a personal status of *teme'ah* with respect to *kodshim*. The verse *ve'habasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo ye'achel* is explicit: one with a personal *tamei* status may not eat the meat of *kodshim*. Therefore, even after nightfall and even after becoming permitted in *teruma*, she remains forbidden in *kodshim* until the *korban* is brought. It would be untenable to say that one still considered *teme'ah* in any degree could eat *kodshim* — the prohibition is categorical.

## Sheshet Challenge

### Non-Priestly

Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, raises a powerful challenge against the entire framework. He notes that a *braita* on the laws of *yoledet* cites the verse *daber el bnei Yisrael*, and asks: I know only that Jewish women by birth are subject to *tumas leida* — how do I know a *giueres* and a *shifcha meshucheres* are also subject to it? The answer given in the *braita* is that the verse *isha ki tazria* — when a woman conceives — comes to include any woman. The *braita* therefore explicitly establishes that the *tahara* laws of the *yoledet* apply even to a *giueres* and a freed slave woman.

### Contradiction

Rav Sheshet presses: if the verses we have been using to derive the hierarchy of *maaser*, *teruma*, and *kodshim* are speaking about *teruma* — as the *gemara* has concluded — how can they apply to a *giueres* or a *shifcha meshucheres*? These women cannot eat *teruma*. Paradoxically, a *giueres* may eat *shelamim* — she can eat *kodshim kalim* if she wishes, as a full member of the Jewish people — but she has no access to *teruma*, which is restricted to priestly households. If the verses regarding her purity are about *teruma*, the *braita*'s inclusion of a *giueres* is incoherent.

### Multiple Segments

Rava responds that the verse operates in distinct segments — *kra milei milei kachashiv* — different parts of the verse address different matters and different audiences. It is not the case that every clause of the passage speaks to every person mentioned. The *braita*'s inclusion of a *giueres* establishes only that she is subject to *tumas leida* as such, not that every specific law derived from the passage applies to her.

### Inclusion Logic

The *tahara* laws regarding *teruma* — derived from the same verses — continue to apply to *kohen* women who are the primary subjects of the passage. The fact that the *braita* also applies *tumas leida* to a *giueres* does not transform the purity thresholds for *teruma* into laws for non-priestly women. The two teachings coexist within the same passage without conflict: one clause teaches universality of *tumas leida*, while another teaches the specific *teruma*-based purity thresholds applicable to those who can eat *teruma*.

## Teruma Purity

### Three Needed

The *gemara* now asks why three separate verses are required to establish the *teruma* hierarchy. Could we not have derived everything from one or two?

### Process Req

The first verse — *ad asher yithar* — establishes that there is some purification process required before eating *teruma*. Without it, we would not know that any formal *tahara* step is necessary at all.

### Sunset Spec

The second verse — *uva ha'shemesh ve'taher* — specifies that the relevant moment of *tahara* is sunset. Without this verse, knowing only that *tahara* is required, we would not know when that threshold is crossed.

### Ambiguity

Even with both of these verses, an ambiguity would remain: perhaps the sunset rule applies only when no *korban* is pending, but for a *bar kapparah* — one who must bring a *korban* — perhaps the requirement extends until the *korban* is brought, even for *teruma*. Without a third verse, this question would remain unresolved.

### Yoledet Proof

The third verse — the *yoledet* passage with *ad milos yemei tahara* — resolves this ambiguity. It demonstrates that even a woman in the *korban*-bearing process becomes *tehorah* for *teruma* once nightfall arrives before the day of her offering. The *mechusar kippurim* status, even when a *korban* is required, does not extend the *teruma* restriction beyond nightfall.

### Why Yoledet

Had we had only the *yoledet* verse — *ad milos* — without the intermediate sunset verse, we might have concluded that the completion of the purification *days* is the determinative factor, and that immersion in a *mikvah* alone, without waiting for nightfall, would suffice. It is the second verse (*uva ha'shemesh ve'taher*) that corrects this, insisting that nightfall is necessary. The *yoledet* verse alone would have been insufficient to teach that.

### Summary Three

Together, the three verses establish the complete framework: *ad asher yithar* teaches that a purification process is required; *uva ha'shemesh ve'taher* specifies that nightfall is the critical moment; and the *yoledet* verses demonstrate that even in the presence of a pending *korban* obligation, nightfall suffices to permit *teruma*, while full *tahara* for *kodshim* awaits the bringing of the *korban* itself. This three-verse architecture corresponds precisely to the three-tier hierarchy of *maaser*, *teruma*, and *kodshim* — each requiring a progressively more complete form of *tahara*.
