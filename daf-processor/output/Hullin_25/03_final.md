# Chullin 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pottery Vessels Interior

### Pottery vs Other Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ מְלֹא חַרְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** that it renders impure everything within it, **and** this is the *halakha* **even** if it is **full of mustard** seeds, in which case most of the seeds do not come in contact with the sides of the vessel, and nevertheless all the mustard seeds become impure.

The daf opens at 25a with the declaration of Rav Adda bar Ahava to Rava. Before engaging the new sugya, it is worth setting out the foundational distinction that frames the entire discussion. Normal vessels become tamei when a source of tumah — such as the carcass of a dead animal — touches them, whether it touches their exterior or interior. The operative mechanism is contact. A kli cheres, however, is governed by an entirely different principle. The Torah states, *asher yippol mehem el tocho* — "that which falls into its interior." For a pottery vessel, what matters is the presence of a source of tumah within the interior. If the tumah-source is suspended inside the vessel without touching it, the vessel becomes tamei; if it touches the pottery from the outside, without entering the interior, the vessel does not become tamei.

The Gemara demonstrates this from the very opening of 25a: *veafilu malei chardal* — even if the vessel is filled with mustard seed, everything inside it becomes tamei. This is striking. If tumah transmission in a pottery vessel operated through contact, only the mustard seeds directly touching the walls of the jar would become tamei; the remainder, separated from the vessel's surface by intervening seeds, could not be reached. Since the Torah declares that everything inside becomes tamei, the mechanism must be presence in the interior rather than physical contact. This is how a kli cheres differs: it receives and transmits tumah through interiority, not touch.

### Kal Vachomer Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: וִיהֵא כְּלִי חֶרֶס מִיטַּמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁאֵין מִיטַּמְּאִין מֵאֲוִירָן – מִיטַּמְּאִין מִגַּבָּן, כְּלִי חֶרֶס שֶׁמִּיטַּמֵּא מֵאֲוִירוֹ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּטַמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava: And let** it be derived that **an earthenware vessel becomes impure from** contact of an impure item with **its outer side by means of an *a fortiori*** inference: **If all the** other types of **vessels, which do not become impure from** the presence of an impure item in **their airspace, become impure from** contact of an impure item with **their outer side,** then with regard to **an earthenware vessel, which becomes impure from** the presence of an impure item in **its airspace, isn't it logical that it will become ritually impure from** contact of an impure item with **its outer side?**

Having established this distinction, the Gemara immediately faces a challenge from Rav Adda bar Ahava. He poses a *kal vachomer*: let a kli cheres become tamei *migabbo* — from its back, meaning its exterior, through contact — by the following reasoning. All other vessels, which do not receive tumah through their interior, do nevertheless become tamei through contact with their exterior. A pottery vessel, which has the stronger capacity of becoming tamei through something merely present in its interior, should *a fortiori* also become tamei through direct contact from the outside.

The argument is not without force. Contact is the most natural and obvious mode of tumah transmission — it is how virtually everything else in the world of tumah operates. If a pottery vessel possesses the enhanced capacity to receive tumah through presence in the interior, without even touching, one might indeed expect it to receive tumah through the more basic mechanism of contact as well.

### Sealed Vessels Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכֹל כְּלִי פָתוּחַ אֲשֶׁר אֵין צָמִיד פָּתִיל עָלָיו״, אֵיזֶהוּ כְּלִי שֶׁטּוּמְאָתוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לְפִתְחוֹ? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה כְּלִי חֶרֶס, וְכִי אֵין צָמִיד פָּתִיל עָלָיו הוּא דְּטָמֵא, הָא יֵשׁ צְמִיד פָּתִיל עָלָיו – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Therefore, **the verse states: "And every open vessel that has no sealed cover upon it** is impure" (Numbers 19:15), indicating that its impurity is dependent upon the mouth of the vessel. **Which is the vessel whose impurity hastily** takes effect just after the impure item enters **into its mouth? You must say** that is **an earthenware vessel. And** it is **when there is no sealed cover on it that** the vessel becomes **impure. But** when **there is a sealed cover on it,** the vessel is **pure,** as the earthenware vessel does not become impure from contact of an impure item with its outer side.

The Gemara responds by citing a verse: *vechol kli patuach asher ein tzamid patil alav tamei hu* — "every open vessel upon which there is no tightly sealed cover, it shall be tamei." This verse concerns vessels found in an ohel hamet, a dwelling in which a human corpse lies. Under a roof shared with a corpse, everything present becomes tamei. Yet the Torah declares that a pottery vessel with a tightly sealed cover remains tahor.

The Gemara asks: how can a sealed cover protect a pottery vessel from tumah? Everything under the roof becomes tamei. The answer is clear: if the pottery vessel receives tumah exclusively through its interior, and the seal prevents access to the interior, then the interior is not exposed to the ohel hamet and the vessel remains tahor. But if the pottery vessel's exterior is also exposed — which it obviously is — and if exterior contact sufficed to convey tumah, the seal would be meaningless. The fact that the Torah attributes complete protection to the sealed cover demonstrates that a pottery vessel is not susceptible to tumah from its exterior at all. The exterior of the pottery vessel, even in an ohel hamet, simply does not count.

The Gemara draws this conclusion through a play on language: *eizu kli shetumato kodemet lepitcho* — which type of vessel is it that tumah arrives, as it were, at its opening? The answer: *hevei omer zeh kli cheres* — this is the pottery vessel. The tumah enters specifically through the opening, through the interior, and through no other route.

### Tumah Nature Debate

*[Continued from above]*

This discussion naturally prompts a deeper question: why should pottery vessels operate so differently from other vessels? One notes that in the laws of kashrut, the porous nature of pottery — its capacity to absorb taste and its inability to be fully purified through conventional means — provides a rationale for special treatment. But for tumah, the distinction seems to lack any physical justification. Tumah appears to be a purely ritualistic designation.

A further consideration: the Torah does not permit pottery vessels to be purified in a mikvah. Other vessels — wood, metal — can be immersed and rendered tahor. For pottery, once it becomes tamei, it must be broken. This consequence is far more severe. It may therefore be that the Torah, precisely because the consequences of tumah for pottery vessels are so severe, sought to limit the avenues through which they become tamei. Restricting tumah reception to the interior — and specifically reading *tocho* as an alternative to contact rather than an addition to it — serves a protective function. Sealing the interior is far simpler than guarding every exterior surface. This gives practical relief to anyone in whose home a person died, which in ancient times would have been a common occurrence.

This, of course, raises the inverse question: why cannot pottery vessels be purified in a mikvah in the first place? That remains without a fully satisfying answer. But at minimum, the two features — restricted modes of tumah reception and inability to purify — form a coherent pair, even if neither is individually explained from first principles.

The question of whether tumah is a purely ritualistic designation or something with metaphysical or quasi-physical reality is one the Gemara itself does not resolve, and it reflects two fundamentally different orientations. One approach understands tumah as a kind of real, spreading phenomenon — something that actually moves through space, that bursts upward from a corpse, spreads under a roof, and reaches whatever lies beneath it. On this view, the laws of tumah describe a kind of physics of impurity. The other approach treats tumah not as a thing in itself but as a description of the relationship between a person or object and a source of impurity. One becomes tamei not because something called tumah physically reached him, but because of his proximity or relational exposure to the source. Both approaches have their proponents, and neither is without difficulty. The case of a kohen flying at great altitude over a cemetery is equally hard to explain on either account — tumah spreading thirty thousand feet upward is as difficult to conceptualize as a relationship with a source thirty thousand feet below.

### Reversed Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִהְיוּ כׇּל הַכֵּלִים מִיטַּמְּאִין מֵאֲוִירָן, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה כְּלִי חֶרֶס שֶׁמִּיטַּמֵּא מֵאֲוִירוֹ – אֵינוֹ מִיטַּמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ, כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁמִּיטַּמְּאִין מִגַּבָּן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵין מִיטַּמְּאִין מִגַּבָּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** it be derived that **all the** other **vessels become impure from** the presence of an impure item in **their airspace by means of an *a fortiori*** inference: **If an earthenware vessel, which does not become impure from** contact of an impure item with **its outer side, becomes impure from** the presence of an impure item in **its airspace,** then with regard to **all the** other **vessels, which become impure from** contact of an impure item with **their outer side, isn't it logical that they will become ritually impure from** the presence of an impure item in **their airspace?**

Having established that pottery vessels receive tumah exclusively from their interior, the Gemara attempts the reverse *kal vachomer*. If pottery vessels, which do not become tamei from their exterior, do become tamei from their interior — then all other vessels, which do become tamei from their exterior, should *a fortiori* also become tamei from their interior, through mere presence of a tumah-source within them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״תּוֹכוֹ״, תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְלֹא תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the carcasses of creeping animals: "And every earthenware vessel **into which [*tokho*]** any of them falls" (Leviticus 11:33), from which it is inferred: ***Tokho*,** i.e., the airspace, **of this** earthenware vessel renders the vessel impure, **and not *tokho*,** the airspace, **of** any **other** kind of vessel.

The Gemara rejects this by appeal to the word *tocho* — "its interior" — used in the Torah in the context of pottery vessels. *Tocho shel zeh*, the interior of this one — the pottery vessel — *ve-lo tocho shel acher*, and not the interior of any other type of vessel. The word *tocho*, with its possessive construction, teaches that interior-based tumah reception is the exclusive property of the pottery vessel and is not extended to metal, wood, or any other class of vessel.

One clarification is worth making explicit. Although the Gemara frames the contrast as interior versus exterior, this is a simplification. Other vessels, if something touches their interior, do also become tamei — but that is because of the touch, not because of presence within the interior. The Gemara is distinguishing between two modes of reception: contact (which governs other vessels) and presence-in-the-interior (which governs pottery alone).

There is then a further attempted argument: perhaps other vessels require both conditions simultaneously — contact with the interior specifically, so that neither contact with the exterior alone nor presence in the interior alone would suffice. The Gemara rejects this as well by returning to *vechol kli patuach*. That verse demonstrates that in an ohel hamet, sealed pottery vessels are tahor and all other vessels are tamei regardless of whether they are sealed — meaning other vessels become tamei through their exterior exposure, with no interior requirement whatsoever.

### Four Derashot Tocho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי ״תּוֹכוֹ״, הָא דַּרְשִׁינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: How can the *halakha* be derived from the term *tokho* in that verse? **But didn't** the Sages **interpret these** instances of ***tokho*** that appear in that verse and derive: Just as in the case of *tokho* that is stated with regard to transmitting impurity, the food is impure even though it did not come into contact with the vessel, so too, in the case of *tokho* that is stated with regard to the vessel becoming impure, the vessel is impure even though the impure item did not come into contact with it.

At this point the Gemara raises an objection: if *tocho* is being used to teach that interior-based tumah applies only to pottery and not to other vessels, how can that usage be legitimate? The word *tocho* has already been deployed for other purposes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבְּעָה ״תּוֹכוֹ״ כְּתִיבִי: ״תּוֹכוֹ״, ״תּוֹךְ״; ״תּוֹכוֹ״, ״תּוֹךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Four** instances of the term ***tokho*** from which *halakhot* can be derived **are written: "*Tokho*"** is written, and ***tokh*** could have been written; those are two instances. Then, later in that verse, once again **"*tokho*"** is written, and ***tokh*** could have been written.

The Gemara answers: *arba tocho ketivei* — effectively, we extract four derashot from the word *tocho*. The Torah uses the root *toch* twice in the relevant passages, and since the Torah could have expressed the idea differently — Tosafot notes that the text could have said simply *bo*, "into it," rather than *tocho*, "into its interior" — the specific language of *toch* is itself an addition that licenses interpretation. Thus we have four derashot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד לְגוּפֵיהּ, וְחַד לִגְזֵרָה שָׁוָה, וְחַד ״תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וְלֹא תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל אַחֵר״, וְחַד ״תּוֹכוֹ וְלֹא תּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּלִי שֶׁטֶף.
>
> **Translation:** **One** instance is **to** teach the *halakha* **itself,** that the vessel renders the food in its airspace ritually impure, **and one** instance is **to** teach the **verbal analogy** from which it is derived that the vessel becomes impure without contact with the impure item; **and one** source is to teach that ***tokho*,** the airspace, **of this** earthenware vessel renders the vessel impure, **and not *tokho* of** any **other** kind of vessel; **and one** source is to teach ***tokho*,** food in the airspace of an impure earthenware vessel becomes impure, **but not *tokh tokho*,** not food that is in the airspace of a vessel that is within an earthenware vessel, **and even** if that inner vessel is one of the other types of **vessel** purified through **rinsing** in the water of a ritual bath.

The first, *chad l'gufei*, is the peshat meaning: a tumah-source that falls into the interior of a pottery vessel renders it tamei. The second, *chad l'gezeirah shavah*, teaches through a parallel derivation that just as the vessel transmits tumah to everything inside it — hence the case of mustard seeds where every single seed becomes tamei — so too it receives tumah from whatever is present in its interior, even without contact. The third, *chad* — *tocho shel zeh ve-lo tocho shel acher* — teaches that interior-based tumah is exclusive to the pottery vessel and does not apply to other vessels. The fourth remains.

### Vessel Within Vessel

*[Continued from above]*

The fourth derashah is *tocho ve-lo toch tocho* — the interior, but not the interior of the interior. This teaches a principle of spatial containment: *ve-afilu kli shetef* — and this applies even with respect to non-pottery vessels (*kli shetef* being the term for vessels that can be purified through immersion in a mikvah, that is, all non-pottery vessels).

The principle operates as follows. If one vessel is placed inside another — imagine a smaller vessel sitting within a larger pottery vessel — then what is inside the inner vessel is considered to be located in the inner vessel and not in the outer one, provided that the walls of the inner vessel extend higher than those of the outer vessel. This has two practical consequences. First, if a tumah-source is in the inner vessel, the outer pottery vessel does not become tamei, because the tumah-source is considered to be located in the inner vessel, not in the pottery vessel. Second, if the outer pottery vessel is tamei, food inside the inner vessel remains tahor, because that food is considered to be in the inner vessel, not in the outer one.

Why does the inner vessel not itself become tamei when surrounded by a tamei outer vessel, thereby passing tumah to its contents? Rashi explains that pottery vessels communicate tumah only to foods; they do not make other vessels tamei. So the inner vessel itself remains tahor, it operates as an independent location, and what is inside it is protected accordingly.

One might ask: why should the food in the inner vessel care? Even if the inner vessel is tahor, once a tamei outer pottery vessel surrounds everything, should the food not be considered to be in the outer vessel as well? The derashah answers precisely this. Even though the inner vessel is physically inside the outer one, what is inside the inner vessel — when its walls extend higher — is considered to be in the inner vessel only, not in the outer one. The principle of *tocho ve-lo toch tocho* is specifically about the food and other contents, not about the vessel itself.

This question of how to locate an object that is in one thing which is itself inside another thing recurs throughout the Gemara. A well-known parallel involves the principle of *keilav shel lokeach*: if a buyer's vessel is sitting in the seller's courtyard and the seller places the merchandise inside the buyer's vessel, does the buyer acquire it? The object is in the buyer's vessel, but the vessel is in the seller's domain — so which is the operative location? The pottery vessel case resolves its version of this problem by applying the rule of *tocho ve-lo toch tocho*: when the inner vessel's walls stand higher, what is inside it is exclusively in that inner vessel.

## Wood vs Metal Vessels

### Wood/Metal Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ טָהוֹר בִּכְלֵי עֵץ, טָמֵא בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת; טָהוֹר בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת, טָמֵא בִּכְלֵי עֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: That which is **ritually pure in wooden vessels** is **ritually impure in metal vessels;** that which is **ritually pure in metal vessels** is **ritually impure in wooden vessels.**

The Mishnah now introduces a new pairing. *Tahor b'khlei etz tamei b'khlei matachot; tahor b'khlei matachot tamei b'khlei etz* — what is pure for wood vessels is impure for metal vessels, and vice versa. The subject here has shifted from the mode by which tumah is received to the question of what constitutes a vessel in the first place — that is, what state of completion a vessel must achieve before it becomes susceptible to tumah at all.

The distinction is this: wood vessels require a *kibbul* — a receptacle, a hollowed-out interior capable of containing something — in order to be susceptible to tumah. This is derived from the verse *kol kli etz o beged o or o sak* — just as a sack is defined by its capacity to contain things, so a wood vessel must have a receptacle. A wood knife, for example, is entirely functional but has no receptacle; it does not become tamei. Metal vessels, by contrast, do not require a receptacle — a metal knife does become tamei. What metal vessels require instead is *gemar melacha*, the finishing of the work, the completion of every last step of manufacture.

### Unfinished Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גּוֹלְמֵי כְּלִי עֵץ טְמֵאִין, פְּשׁוּטֵיהֶן טְהוֹרִין; גּוֹלְמֵי כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת טְהוֹרִין, פְּשׁוּטֵיהֶן טְמֵאִין. נִמְצָא טָהוֹר בִּכְלֵי עֵץ – טָמֵא בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת, טָהוֹר בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת – טָמֵא בִּכְלֵי עֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in explanation of the mishna: **Unfinished [*golmei*] wooden vessels** that are receptacles and are fit for use but work remains to complete their crafting are susceptible to becoming **impure. Flat** wooden utensils are **not susceptible to impurity. Unfinished metal vessels** are **not susceptible to impurity. Flat** metal utensils are susceptible to becoming **impure. It is found** that that which is **ritually pure in wooden vessels** is **ritually impure in metal vessels;** that which is **ritually pure in metal vessels** is **ritually impure in wooden vessels.**

The Gemara elaborates with a Baraita. *Golemi klei etz* — the word *golem* here means an unfinished product, evoking the image of the famous unformed creature of Jewish legend, a lump not yet fully made. Unfinished wood vessels are nonetheless tamei, provided they have a receptacle. *Peshuteihen* — flat wood vessels without a receptacle — are tahor. For wood, the question of whether the object is finished or unfinished is irrelevant; what matters is only whether it has a hollowed interior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן גּוֹלְמֵי כְּלִי עֵץ: כֹּל שֶׁעָתִיד לָשׁוּף, לְשַׁבֵּץ, לְגָרֵר, לְכַרְכֵּב, לְהָטִיחַ בְּטוּנָס, מְחוּסָּר כַּן אוֹ אוֹגֶן אוֹ אוֹזֶן – טָמֵא, מְחוּסָּר חֲטִיטָה – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the **unfinished wooden vessels: Any** vessel **that** one plans in the **future to smooth, to set** gems or ornaments in it, **to plane** it, **to adorn** it with grooves and protuberances, **to rub** it and smooth it **with** the skin of **a tuna [*tunas*],** or if it is **lacking a base or a rim or a handle,** the vessel is susceptible to becoming **impure.** If the vessel is **lacking** the **hollowing** necessary to render it a receptacle, it is **not susceptible to impurity.**

The Gemara then provides examples of what "unfinished" means for a wood vessel: anything that still requires polishing (*lashuf*), the addition of filigree or engraving (*leshabetz*), scraping or sanding (*legarer*), carving (*lekarkev*), or sealing with an oil or fish-skin coating (*lehatiach betunas*). Furthermore, a vessel lacking a base (*mechusar kan*), or without a lip (*mechusar ogen*), or without handles (*mechusar ozen*) — all of these are still considered tamei if they have a receptacle, because for wood, it is the receptacle that defines vessel status.

### Receptacle Incomplete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסָּר חֲטִיטָה, פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּחַק קְפִיזָא בְּקַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that if the vessel is **lacking hollowing,** it is not susceptible to becoming impure, as it is clearly not a vessel? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** only in a case **where one hollowed three *log* [*kefiza*] in** a receptacle that he intends to hold **one *kav*,** which contains six *log*.

A question arises: if a wood vessel is lacking the carving-out altogether — *mechusar chatita* — it is tahor. This seems obvious. The Gemara clarifies that the case is not obvious: *lo tzericha dechaka kafiza bekava* — the vessel is intended to hold a full kav, but so far only a smaller receptacle has been carved. The vessel does have some receptacle, but the intended receptacle has not yet been completed. In this case, the incomplete intention matters. Even though a functioning, if shallower, receptacle exists, the fact that the maker intends a deeper receptacle and has not yet finished carving it out means the vessel is not yet complete as a receptacle and remains tahor.

This introduces an element of intentionality even into the wood vessel analysis, where ordinarily we emphasize objective function. It is a notable qualification.

Tosafot raises a tension with another Gemara, in the second chapter of Sanhedrin, which states that a kneading trough and a bed — *ha'arisa vehamitah* — become susceptible to tumah only after being smoothed with fish skin (*misheyishufam be'or hadag*). This seems to contradict the Baraita here, which says that even if a wood vessel has not yet been smoothed, it is tamei if it has a receptacle. Tosafot resolves this cleanly: the smoothing referred to in Sanhedrin means removing large splinters that would injure someone using the vessel. Without that, the object is not yet truly usable and so has not achieved vessel status at all. The polishing mentioned here in our Gemara, however, refers to purely aesthetic finishing — making it look nicer and smoother — which does not affect usability. Thus: removing hazardous splinters is a prerequisite for function; decorative polishing is not.

### Unfinished Metal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן גּוֹלְמֵי כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת: כֹּל שֶׁעָתִיד לָשׁוּף, לְשַׁבֵּץ, לְגָרֵר, לְכַרְכֵּר, לְהַקִּישׁ בְּקוּרְנָס. מְחוּסָּר כַּן אוֹ אוֹגֶן אוֹ אוֹזֶן – טָהוֹר, מְחוּסָּר כְּסוּי – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the **unfinished metal vessels: Any** vessel **that** one plans in the **future to smooth, to set** gems or ornaments in it, **to plane** it, **to adorn** it, **to strike** it **with a hammer,** or if it is **lacking a base or a rim or a handle,** the vessel is **not susceptible to impurity.** If the vessel was complete and was **lacking a cover,** the vessel is susceptible to becoming **impure.**

For metal vessels the rule is reversed. *Golemi klei matachot* — unfinished metal vessels — are tahor. A vessel missing its polishing, its filigree, its final engraving, its final hammer blow (*lehakish bekurnas*), is not yet susceptible to tumah. The concept of *makeh bepatish* — the final hammer blow — is well known from the laws of Shabbat as the paradigm of the finishing touch, and it applies here directly. A metal vessel that has not received this final completion, this last stage of manufacture, is not yet a vessel for purposes of tumah.

Noteworthy is what does not matter for metal: *mechusar kan o ogen o ozen* — if a metal vessel is missing its base, its lip, or its handles, it is nonetheless tamei already, because those attachments are considered separate objects, not integral parts of the vessel itself. Conversely, *mechusar kisuy* — if it is merely missing a cover — it is tamei, because the cover is similarly a separate object and not part of the vessel's completion.

### Honor vs Expense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וּלְכָבוֹד עֲשׂוּיִין. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וּדְמֵיהֶן יְקָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **these** unfinished wooden vessels, with regard to which the *halakha* is that provided they are fit for use they are susceptible to impurity, **and what is different** about **those** unfinished metal vessels, with regard to which the *halakha* is that even if they are fit for use they are not susceptible to impurity until their crafting is complete? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The difference is that **since** metal vessels **are crafted for** uses of **honor,** they are not considered vessels until their completion. **Rav Naḥman said:** The difference is that **since the worth of** metal vessels **is expensive,** they cannot be sold at that price unless they are complete.

The Gemara now poses a question that is unusual in this type of sugya: *mai shna hani u-mai shna hani* — what is the reason for this distinction? Why does metal require finishing but not receptacle, and wood require receptacle but not finishing? Two amoraim offer competing rationales.

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *ho'il u-lechavod asuyin* — metal vessels are made for use in contexts of honor and respect. One brings out metal vessels when receiving distinguished guests; metal belongs to the domain of the elevated and the ceremonial. Because of this association, a metal vessel must be in a fully finished, presentable state before it attains vessel status. An unfinished metal vessel is incongruous with the honored purpose for which metal is used; it has not yet become what it is meant to be. Wood vessels, being common and ordinary, carry no such expectation, and thus their mere functionality — having a receptacle — suffices.

Rav Nachman disagrees. His reasoning is *hovu de-damei yakarim* — they are expensive. Metal costs more, and someone paying a significant price for a metal vessel expects to receive a finished product. The consideration is not the social context of use but the economic investment and the expectation that comes with it. An unfinished expensive item is not yet worth what it should be worth; it has not yet been completed in the sense that matters to its owner.

### Bone Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ כְּלֵי עֶצֶם, וְאַזְדָּא רַב נַחְמָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּלֵי עֶצֶם כִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between their** opinions? The Gemara answers: The difference **between them is** with regard to **bone vessels** crafted from horns, which are expensive but are not crafted for uses of honor. **And Rav Naḥman follows his** line of **reasoning, as Rav Naḥman says:** The halakhic status of **bone vessels** with regard to impurity is **like** that of **metal vessels.**

The practical difference between these two rationales is identified through the test case of bone vessels (*klei etzem*). Bone vessels were presumably expensive — worked bone is a costly material. However, bone vessels are not specifically associated with contexts of honor and ceremony in the way metal vessels are; they are used by ordinary people in ordinary contexts.

On Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning, bone vessels would not require finishing, since they are not vessels made for honored occasions. On Rav Nachman's reasoning, bone vessels would require finishing, since they are expensive items and the criterion is cost rather than social context.

The Gemara notes that Rav Nachman follows his own reasoning: *ve'azal Rav Nachman leta'amei* — Rav Nachman rules that bone vessels are like metal vessels and require finishing before they become susceptible to tumah.

### Bone Biblical Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דִּכְלֵי עֶצֶם מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה? אִין, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה עִזִּים תִּתְחַטָּאוּ״? לְהָבִיא דָּבָר הַבָּא מִן הָעִזִּים, מִן הַקַּרְנַיִם וּמִן הַטְּלָפַיִם. שְׁאָר בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עִזִּים״? פְּרָט לְעוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is that to say **by inference that bone vessels are susceptible to ritual impurity?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "And all work of goats…you shall purify"** (Numbers 31:20)? It is **to include** vessels **that come from the goats, from the horns and from the hooves,** and indicate that they are susceptible to impurity. And **from where** is it derived that vessels that come from the horns and the hooves of **other domesticated animals and undomesticated animals** are susceptible to impurity? It is derived from the verse, as **the verse states: "And all work,"** where the term "all" is an amplification. **If so, why** must **the verse state: "Goats?"** It serves **to exclude birds,** as vessels crafted from the bones of birds are not susceptible to impurity.

The Gemara observes that this entire discussion implies — *miklal* — that bone vessels are susceptible to tumah at the biblical level, since the debate about their status appears to be conducted at the level of biblical law. A Baraita then confirms this. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka asks: what does the verse *vechol ma'aseh izim titchata'u* — "all things made of goats you shall purify" — teach? The straightforward reading would limit biblical tumah susceptibility to things made from goat hair. But the Baraita expands: *lehavi davar haba min ha'izim* — to include things that come from other parts of the goat, *min hakarnayim* — from the horns — and *umin hatalpayim* — from the hooves. Worked horn and hoof, which are essentially bone-like materials, are thus biblically susceptible to tumah.

How do we know this extends beyond goats to other animals? The verse also says *vechol ma'aseh* — "all acts," in a broader formulation. If so, why mention goats specifically? *Perat le'ofot* — to exclude bird bones. Something made from bird bones would not become tamei, either because they are too fragile or too insignificant. Only things made from the bones, horns, or hooves of land animals are included.

## Agriculture & Commerce

### Nuts Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחַיָּיב בִּשְׁקֵדִים הַמָּרִים – פָּטוּר בַּמְּתוּקִים, הַחַיָּיב בַּמְּתוּקִים – פָּטוּר בַּמָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the obligation of separating *teruma* and tithes, the stage of development **that** is **obligated in bitter almonds** is **exempt in sweet** almonds; and the stage in development **that** is **obligated in sweet** almonds is **exempt in bitter** almonds.

The Mishnah now shifts entirely, moving from the domain of tumah and tahara to that of Zeraim — agricultural law. The pairing follows the same structural pattern: *hachayav bishkedim hamarin patur bimetukin, *hachayav bimetukin* patur bamarin* — one who is obligated with respect to bitter almonds is exempt with respect to sweet ones, and vice versa. The question is when something is considered food and thus obligated in *trumot u'ma'asrot*.

Two varieties of nuts are contrasted. Bitter almonds are edible and palatable when they are small and immature; once they reach full maturity they become bitter to the point of inedibility. Sweet almonds follow the opposite trajectory: when immature they are not yet edible, but once fully developed they are desirable food. Obligation in *trumot u'ma'asrot* tracks edibility. Thus the small, immature bitter almonds are obligated; the mature bitter almonds are exempt. The mature sweet almonds are obligated; the immature ones are exempt. What makes something obligated in one case exempts it in the other, and vice versa.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁקֵדִים הַמָּרִים, קְטַנִּים – חַיָּיבִין, גְּדוֹלִים – פְּטוּרִין. מְתוּקִים, גְּדוֹלִים – חַיָּיבִין, קְטַנִּים – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in explanation of the mishna: In the case of **bitter almonds,** when they are **small** one is **obligated** to separate *teruma* and tithes, as they are not yet bitter and are fit for consumption; when they are **large** one is **exempt** from separating *teruma* and tithes, because they are bitter and unfit for consumption. In the case of **sweet** almonds, when they are **large** one is **obligated** to separate *teruma* and tithes, because the almonds are ripe and fit for consumption; when they are **small** one is **exempt** from separating *teruma* and tithes, because they are not fit for consumption.

The Gemara cites a Baraita elaborating this principle: *shkedim hamarin ktanim chayavin, gdolim pturin; metukin gdolim chayavin, ktanim pturin*. Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yossi then introduces a dissent in the name of his father: *zeh vezeh liphtur* — both forms of the bitter almond are exempt — and some say *zeh vezeh lachiyuv* — both are obligated. The view that both forms of the bitter almond are exempt is intelligible: even the immature bitter almond, though subjectively edible, is not yet an objectively developed fruit and might therefore be exempt on grounds of not having reached a recognizable stage of agricultural completeness. Rabbi Chanina is cited as ruling in practice according to the view that both are exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: זֶה וָזֶה לִפְטוּר, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: זֶה וָזֶה לְחִיּוּב. אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא: הוֹרָה רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בְּצִפּוֹרִי כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר זֶה וָזֶה לִפְטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to bitter almonds, **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father to exempt** both **this** small almond **and that** large almond. **And some say to obligate** both **this** small almond **and that** large almond. **Rabbi Ela said: Rabbi Ḥanina issued a ruling in Tzippori in accordance with the statement of the one who says to exempt** both **this** small almond **and that** large almond.

### Processing via Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר זֶה וָזֶה לְחִיּוּב, גְּדוֹלִים לְמַאי חֲזוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְמַתְּקָן עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says to obligate** both **this** small almond **and that** large almond, **for what** use **are** the **large** almonds **fit?** They are bitter and unfit for consumption. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Since one can sweeten them by means of** roasting them on **the fire,** this renders them fit for consumption.

The Gemara asks: according to the opinion that both forms of the bitter almond are obligated — including the mature, bitter ones — how can the mature bitter almonds possibly be considered food? They are inedible in their raw state.

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *ho'il veyachol l'matkan al yedei ha'or* — since they can be rendered edible through cooking, through the application of fire, they are considered food. The potential for edibility through processing is sufficient to confer the status of food and trigger the obligation in *trumot u'ma'asrot*. Even something that is not presently edible raw may be obligated if it can be made edible through a recognized culinary process.

### Temed Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתֶּמֶד, עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֶחְמִיץ – אֵינוֹ נִיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וּפוֹסֵל אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה. מִשֶּׁהֶחְמִיץ – נִיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: ***Temed*,** a beverage produced from grape residue soaked in water, **until it fermented, may not be purchased with** second-**tithe money** to be drunk in Jerusalem, because it is not wine. **And** if three *log* of it fall into a ritual bath, its halakhic status is that of drawn water and **it invalidates the ritual bath. Once it fermented, it** is wine, and therefore it **may be purchased with** second-**tithe money and it does not invalidate the ritual bath.**

The Mishnah moves to another case of threshold classification: *ha-temed*. Temed is produced by adding water to grape lees — the residual grape matter remaining after pressing — and allowing the water to absorb the remaining juice. The result is a diluted, weaker form of wine-like liquid.

The question is whether temed is classified as water or as wine, and the Mishnah identifies the moment of fermentation as the decisive threshold. *Ad shelo hechmit* — before it has fermented — temed is considered water. As water, it has two legal consequences. First, *eino nikach b'kesef ma'aser* — it cannot be purchased with *ma'aser sheni money*, since ma'aser sheni funds may only be spent on food and drink products of agricultural origin, not on mere water. Second, *u-posel et ha-mikvah* — if drawn temed is added to a mikvah that has not yet reached its required measure of forty se'ah, it invalidates the mikvah, since it is drawn water rather than rainwater or spring water.

*Mishe-hechmit* — once it has fermented — temed is reclassified as wine. It may then be purchased with *ma'aser sheni money*, and if it flows into a mikvah, it does not invalidate it, since wine is not drawn water for these purposes.

This connects to a broader structural point about modern mikvah construction. Standard mikva'ot maintain a sealed pit (*bor*) of forty se'ah of rainwater that no one ever enters, connected to the bathing pool from which water is regularly changed and cleaned. The entire legitimacy of the usable pool depends on its connection to that sealed bor, which never falls below the required minimum. Once the minimum is fully present, the addition of drawn water does not invalidate.

### Brothers Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאַחִים הַשּׁוּתָּפִין, כְּשֶׁחַיָּיבִין בַּקָּלְבּוֹן – פְּטוּרִין מִמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה; כְּשֶׁחַיָּיבִין בְּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה – פְּטוּרִין מִן הַקָּלְבּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **brothers who are partners** in the inheritance of their father, **when they are obligated to** add **the premium [*kalbon*]** to their annual half-shekel payment to the Temple, **they are exempt from animal tithe; when they are obligated to** separate **animal tithe, they are exempt from** adding **the premium.** Partners who pay the half-shekel are required to add the premium and are exempt from animal tithe. If they are not true partners, but their inheritance remains the property of the father, the sons are exempt from paying the premium, and they are obligated to separate animal tithe.

The final Mishnah takes yet another turn, now addressing a question of civil and communal law that bears on the concepts of partnership and corporate unity. *Ha-achim ha-shutfim* — brothers who are partners — can exist in two distinct legal configurations, and their obligations differ accordingly.

When brothers inherit a father's estate and then divide it between themselves before re-entering into a partnership, they are thereafter two separate individuals who happen to be in a joint business arrangement. In this configuration, each brother giving his annual half-shekel contribution is treated as a separate person giving a separate coin. They are therefore *chayavin b'kalbon* — obligated in the kalbon, the small additional fee paid to the money changer for breaking a full shekel into a half-shekel. However, because they are treated as independent individuals whose flocks are jointly owned through partnership, they are *pturim mi-ma'aser behemah* — exempt from the animal tithe, since *ma'aser behemah* applies to individually owned flocks, not to flocks held in partnership.

When brothers have not divided the estate and thus hold it as an undivided inheritance, the estate itself functions as a unified entity. One entity gives one full shekel — no half-shekel transaction, no money changer's fee. They are therefore *pturim min hakalbon*. But precisely because the flock belongs to a single unified entity rather than to separate individual owners, they are *chayavin b'ma'aser behemah* — obligated in the animal tithe.

This Mishnah touches on a conceptual question of considerable practical importance. Halacha recognizes partnership — a joint arrangement between two individuals who each retain their separate identities — but does not generally recognize the corporation as a legal entity distinct from its owners. The closest analogue in classical halachic thinking to what we would call a corporation is the undivided inherited estate. In that case, the estate itself is treated as a unified juridical entity, and the brothers are its owners but not individual partners in the ordinary sense. This distinction — between partnership and unified estate — is directly relevant to contemporary questions about the halachic status of corporations, where the inherited estate model serves as the primary point of comparison.

A concluding note from yesterday's discussion. Both Rashi and the Rambam — though the Gemara itself does not state it explicitly — assume that the twenty-four rotational groups (*mishmarot*) used for kohanim also applied equally to levi'im. The Mishnah in Ta'anit indicates that levi'im served on the same rotation, and the implication is that they were similarly divided into twenty-four groups, each serving roughly two weeks per year, with further subdivision by day within each week. In practice, any individual levi might have served as few as two days per year. On the separate question of a levi whose voice had deteriorated due to age or infirmity — and whether such a levi could continue in any role — the Rambam, employing the phrase *yeiraeh li* (indicating a conclusion without an explicit source), rules that a levi who can no longer sing may still serve in the other functions of the levitical role, such as gatekeeping. This accords with the principle discussed yesterday: the levi'im are defined by their service and their roles, and the loss of capacity in one role does not disqualify them from others.
