# Kiddushin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Amah Ivriyah & Proratio

### Reish Lakish's Model

The daf opens by returning to the question that has occupied the preceding discussion: what is the basis for Beis Shammai's requirement that *kiddushin* be effected with no less than a *dinar*? The Gemara has already considered and set aside two earlier explanations — one grounded in the presumption (*ada'ata*) that women would not consent to *kiddushin* for less than a *dinar*, and another rooted in the association of *kesef* in the Torah with *kesef tzuri*, standard silver coinage. That second explanation was shown to be insufficient: when the Torah uses *kesef* generically, without specifying an amount, it does not mandate a particular denomination and could in principle encompass even a *perutah*. The rule that *kesef* implies *kesef tzuri* — a specific, weighty coinage — applies only where the Torah specifies a precise sum, such as thirty or fifty shekels. With those explanations behind us, the Gemara turns to a third and more analytically compelling account.

Reish Lakish cites a teaching in the name of Chizkiyah. The verse *ve-hefdah* — "and she shall be redeemed" — written in the context of the *amah ivriyah*, the Hebrew maidservant sold by her father, teaches that the maidservant may redeem herself through a pro-rated reduction of her purchase price: she pays back only the portion corresponding to the years of service that remain, having already worked off the rest. The Gemara has elsewhere used the *amah ivriyah* as the model from which the laws of the father's right to receive *kiddushin* money on behalf of his daughter are derived, so the two institutions are linked by analogy.

### Proratio Challenge

The implication of this teaching is immediate and consequential. If the redemption of an *amah ivriyah* requires the ability to pro-rate the original purchase price, then that purchase price must have been large enough to permit pro-rating in the first place. If the father sold her for a *dinar* — close to 192 *perutot* — then one can indeed reduce that sum incrementally as she works off portions of her term. But if the father had sold her for only a single *perutah*, there is nothing to reduce: *miprutah mee megara* — you cannot subtract from a *perutah*. Therefore the original sale price must have been more than a *perutah*, and by analogy, so must the *kesef kiddushin*.

The Gemara raises an objection. Perhaps the Torah's instruction regarding pro-rating is simply conditional: where a *dinar* was paid, pro-rating is available and may proceed down to a *perutah* minimum; but where only a *perutah* was paid, no pro-rating applies and the sale remains valid as a simple transaction with no reduction option. On this reading, the institution of the *amah ivriyah* itself would not require that a minimum above a *perutah* be paid — it would only mean that in cases where more was paid, the pro-rating mechanism is available.

### Yi'ud Analogy

The Gemara responds by invoking a parallel principle drawn from the laws of *yi'ud* — the right of the master or his son to designate the *amah ivriyah* as a wife. This designation is not mandatory; the master may choose not to exercise it. One might therefore argue that if *yi'ud* is merely optional, a sale should be valid even in cases where *yi'ud* is structurally impossible — for instance, where the buyer is related to the girl in a degree that would prohibit marriage, or where he has already married her sister. Yet the law is otherwise: *kol heicha de-la matzi miyad, lav zvini zvini* — wherever *yi'ud* cannot take place at all, the sale itself is invalid. The entire institution of selling a daughter as an *amah ivriyah* was permitted by the Torah only because the hope and expectation of *yi'ud* is inherent to it. If *yi'ud* is off the table entirely, the sale is void. Here there is a meaningful difference, one worth noting: the argument for *yi'ud* rests on the idea that the entire rationale for permitting the sale was the prospect of marriage, so without that prospect the sale lacks its foundational justification. The argument for *megara* — pro-rating — is less obviously essential to the institution. Why should the absence of an ability to reduce the price retroactively invalidate a sale? The Gemara is nonetheless drawing the comparison, asserting *hacha nami*: just as *yi'ud* must be possible for the sale to be valid, so too the pro-rating option must be structurally available. Since a *perutah* cannot be reduced, a sale for a *perutah* cannot support the pro-rating mechanism, and therefore is not a valid sale.

### Perutah to Dinar

From this the Gemara concludes that the purchase price of an *amah ivriyah* must exceed a *perutah*, and by analogy, so must the *kesef kiddushin* for a woman. And by the same analogy, Beis Hillel's position that a *perutah* suffices for *kiddushin* is derived by its own logic, while Beis Shammai, learning from the *amah ivriyah*, requires an amount capable of pro-rating.

## Why Dinar Not Perutah

### Intermediate Coins

The Gemara immediately presses a further question. Even granting that the minimum must exceed a *perutah*, why does Beis Shammai land on a *dinar* specifically? Between a *perutah* and a *dinar* there is an enormous range of values — why not half a *dinar*, or two *perutot*, or any of the many intermediate denominations?

### Next Level Coinage

The Gemara's answer is terse but conceptually significant: *keivan de-afiktaih mi-perutah, okmah a-dinar* — once we have established that a *perutah* is insufficient, we move directly to the next serious level of coinage, which is the *dinar*. The analogy offered is an informal one: if you rule out a penny, you do not land on a nickel or a dime — you go to a dollar. There is a qualitative leap from the smallest denomination to the next major coinage level. A *ma'ah* or a half-*dinar* might seem like natural stopping points, yet the Gemara considers the *dinar* to be the next genuinely substantial coin once the *perutah* is ruled out. This may be unsatisfying in its precision, but that is the Gemara's position.

### Rava: Women's Status

Rava offers an entirely independent rationale for Beis Shammai's *dinar* requirement. *She-lo yehu benot Yisrael ke-hefker* — so that Jewish women should not be treated as ownerless property, as something of negligible worth. If a woman could be acquired in marriage for a *perutah* — a coin of minimal value — it sends a societal message of degradation. The institution of marriage carries with it a statement about the dignity of the person entering it. A *perutah* is too small a sum to convey that dignity; it symbolizes something cheap. A *dinar*, by contrast, carries weight as a serious monetary unit.

There is an irony worth pointing out. The Gemara elsewhere grounds the entire institution of *kiddushin* by analogy in the sale of a daughter as a maidservant — a framework that treats women in explicitly transactional, almost commercial terms. Rava's reasoning here pulls in precisely the opposite direction, insisting on a minimum that protects women's social standing and dignity. Both are operative; they simply reflect different layers of the legal and moral reasoning at work.

### Rashi: Rabbinic Enactment

Rashi's commentary makes an important clarification about the status of Rava's reasoning. Rashi, in explaining the phrase *ke-hefker*, states explicitly that this is a *derabbanan* enactment: *min haTorah* a *perutah* should technically suffice, but the Rabbis enacted that anything less than a *dinar* would not take effect — *afkinu rabbanan lekiddushin minei* — they uprooted the *kiddushin* retroactively. This is a significant ruling. Rava's reasoning, in Rashi's reading, is not about deriving a *d'oraita* minimum from a biblical source; rather, it is a rabbinic protection layered over the Torah's framework, motivated by the concern for women's dignity in society.

## Beis Hillel: Perutah

### Beis Hillel Intro

The Gemara now turns to Beis Hillel, who holds that *kiddushin* is effected with a *perutah* or the equivalent value of a *perutah* in goods. This raises the question of what exactly a *perutah* is and whether its value is fixed or variable.

### Fixed vs. Relative Value

Rav Yosef suggests that when we say a *perutah*, we mean *kol shehu* — whatever constitutes the minimal coinage in a given society at a given time. The value of a *perutah*, on this view, is not fixed in absolute terms but is relative to the monetary norms of the era and place.

Abaye challenges this immediately. The Mishnah goes out of its way to define a *perutah* precisely as one eighth of an *issar ha'italki* — one eighth of an Italian *issar*. If the *perutah* were simply whatever counts as minimum coinage in any given context, why would the Mishnah specify this concrete ratio? One might respond that the Mishnah was simply describing the situation as it obtained in its own time — in the days of Moshe the minimum coinage may have been something else entirely, and in each generation the minimum coinage of that generation would apply. On this reading the Mishnah is not locking in a fixed definition; it is simply illustrating the concept for its era.

The Gemara, however, rejects this flexibility. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that Rabbi Simai in his generation had assessed the value of a *perutah* using precisely the standard of the Mishnah — one eighth of an Italian *issar*. Rashi explains the context: a man had betrothed a woman with a *perutah*, and Rabbi Simai needed to determine whether the *kiddushin* was valid. He did so by checking whether the *perutah* in question was still worth one eighth of an Italian *issar* by the standards of his time. The Mishnah's definition was being used as an objective and continuing benchmark, not merely as a historical description.

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported a related assessment by Rabbi Dostai, Rabbi Yannai, and Rabbi Oshaya: they assessed a *perutah* as one sixth of an Italian *issar*. This seems to contradict the Mishnah's figure of one eighth. But the critical point for this discussion is that these *Amoraim* too were using an objective, externally fixed standard — not a shifting social convention. Whether one eighth or one sixth is the correct figure, the *perutah* has a determinate value that does not simply track whatever happens to be the smallest coin in circulation.

### Sifra Calculation

Rav Yosef now presses back. If the *perutah* has a stable and fixed value, how does one account for a passage in the *Sifra*? The *Sifra* discusses the case of one who benefits from *hekdesh* — consecrated property — and thereby incurs the obligation of *me'ilah* and must bring an *asham*. The Torah requires that the *asham* be worth two *shekalim* — that is, two *sela'im*. The *Sifra* offers a striking moral observation: one who derived even a *perutah*'s worth of benefit from *hekdesh* must bring a *korban* worth over 2,000 *perutot* — underscoring the severity of the transgression and the disproportionate weight of atonement required. The *Sifra* says: *tse vachashov kama prutos beshnei sla'im* — go and calculate how many *perutot* are in two *sela'im*. And the *Sifra* states that this number exceeds 2,000.

The difficulty is arithmetical. A *sela* is a *shekel*, a *shekel* is two *zuz* (i.e., two *dinarim*), a *dinar* is 192 *perutot*, and therefore two *sela'im* equals four *dinarim* equals 768 *perutot* — wait, let us be more careful. A *dinar* is 192 *perutot*; two *sela'im* is four *dinarim*, giving 768 *perutot* — no, that is also not right. Working from the breakdown given in the Gemara itself: a *dinar* is six *ma'ot*, a *ma'ah* is two *pundyon*, a *pundyon* is two *issarin*, an *issar* is two *musmasin*, a *musmas* is two *kuntrakin*, a *kuntrak* is two *perutot* — giving 192 *perutot* per *dinar*. Two *sela'im* is four *dinarim*, which is 768 *perutot* — yet this is even further from 2,000. Even taking two *sela'im* as two *dinarim* (if *shekel* here means *dinar*), one gets 384, still far short. Let us take the numbers the Gemara actually works with: two *shekalim* at the rate of 768 *perutot* per *shekel* gives approximately 1,536. The *Sifra* calls this "more than 2,000," which Rav Yosef argues implies that a *perutah* in the *Sifra*'s reckoning was worth less than the one-eighth-of-an-*issar* standard would dictate. If *perutot* were smaller units than the Mishnah assumes, the count would rise, and could exceed 2,000.

The Gemara reports that a certain elder (*hahu saba*) had a different text of the *Braisa*: not *yoter me-alpayim* — "more than 2,000" — but *karov le-alpayim* — "close to 2,000." The Gemara asks, even so, how is 1,536 close to 2,000? The answer: *keivan de-nafku mi-palga* — since 1,536 has already passed 1,500, it is rounded to the nearest thousand, making it "close to 2,000." That is, of the two thousands that bracket it — 1,000 and 2,000 — it is closer to 2,000. With the correct *girsa* of the *Braisa* in hand, the apparent challenge dissolves. The *Sifra* does not require a *perutah* of lesser value than the Mishnah's definition. The value of the *perutah* is indeed stable and consistent.

### Perutah-Dinar Ratio

Having established that the *perutah* has a fixed value, the Gemara returns to the apparent contradiction between Rav Dimi's report (one eighth of an *issar*) and Ravin's report (one sixth of an *issar*). Abaye asks Rav Dimi whether their dispute mirrors the *Tannaitic* debate between the *Tanna Kamma* — who defines a *perutah* as one eighth of an Italian *issar*, deriving this from the chain of subdivisions in the Mishnah: six *ma'ot* per *dinar*, two *pundyon* per *ma'ah*, two *issarin* per *pundyon*, yielding 24 *issarin* per *dinar* and therefore 192 *perutot* per *dinar* and 8 *perutot* per *issar* — and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who uses a different set of denominations. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's coinage system yields 144 *perutot* per *dinar*: six *ma'ot* per *dinar*, three *hadrasin* per *ma'ah* giving 18, two *hanatzim* per *hadras* giving 36, two *shemanim* per *hanatz* giving 72, and two *perutot* per *shaman* giving 144. If one assumes the same 24-*issar*-per-*dinar* ratio, 144 divided by 24 gives 6 *perutot* per *issar* — i.e., one sixth.

Rav Dimi responds that in fact both he and Ravin agree with the *Tanna Kamma*: the ratio of *perutah* to *dinar* is fixed at 192 to 1. What fluctuates is not the *perutah*-to-*dinar* ratio but the *issar*-to-*dinar* ratio, because the value of the *issar* was not stable and changed over time. When the *issar* was of higher value — *de-ikur issarei* — there were 24 *issarin* to a *dinar*, producing a *perutah*-to-*issar* ratio of 1 to 8. When the *issar* was devalued — *de-zul issarei* — there were 32 *issarin* to a *dinar*, producing a *perutah*-to-*issar* ratio of 1 to 6. The *perutah* itself never changed in value relative to the *dinar*; only the *issar* fluctuated.

The practical upshot is significant. The *perutah* and the *dinar* are the stable anchors of the system. The *issar* is an intermediate unit whose purchasing power was historically variable — the *issar* was indeed known to fluctuate radically in value across the Roman period. When someone in one generation describes a *perutah* as one eighth of an *issar* and someone in another generation describes it as one sixth, they are not disagreeing about the value of a *perutah*; they are reflecting different economic realities of the *issar* in their respective eras. Both are measuring the *perutah* against a *dinar*, and both arrive at the same underlying value.

This has broad application. Whenever the *halacha* invokes a *perutah* — whether for *kiddushin*, for *me'ilah*, for *pidyon ha-ben*, or for anything else — we are not speaking of whatever happens to be the smallest coin in circulation at a given time and place. We mean an objective, stable monetary unit defined as 1/192nd of a *dinar*. In contemporary terms, with a *dinar* (i.e., a *zuz*) valued at approximately $3 based on its precious metal weight in silver, a *perutah* works out to roughly two cents. This is the precious-metal-based valuation, which is the standard the *halacha* employs — not a purchasing-power equivalence, which would be far harder to calculate consistently across time.

A brief but illuminating digression: the *ketubah* is denominated in 200 *zuz*, which once represented approximately a year or two of living expenses. By the Middle Ages, devaluation had rendered 200 *zuz* nearly worthless in terms of actual purchasing power, leaving women without meaningful financial protection upon divorce. The *Takanah* was therefore enacted to denominate the *ketubah* in *zekukim* — 100 *zekukim* for the *mohar* and 100 *zekukim* for the *tosefet* — a *zekuk* being a far larger weight of silver than a *zuz*. Today 200 *zekukim* corresponds to approximately $27,000 in silver value. The *zuz*, meanwhile, is now worth approximately $3 in silver, and 200 *zuz* correspondingly about $600. The point is that the system is anchored in precious metal weight, not in buying power, and the historical trajectory of coinage devaluation is precisely why the *ketubah* denomination had to be reformulated.

## Objects of Uncertain Val…

### Shmuel: Madai Concern

The Gemara now introduces a related but distinct problem: what is the *halacha* when *kiddushin* is effected not with money but with an object of uncertain value? Shmuel states: *kiddasha bitmara* — if a man betrothed a woman with a date — *afilu omid kor tmarin bedinar* — even if an entire *kor* of dates is currently selling for a *dinar*, which would imply that a single date is worth far less than a *perutah* — *chashinan shema shaveh prutah bemadai* — we are nonetheless concerned, lest this particular date be worth a *perutah* somewhere far away, in a place like *Madai*. Shmuel's position is striking: even an object whose local market value is clearly negligible creates a *safek kiddushin* — an uncertainty of marriage — because one cannot rule out that it might fetch a *perutah* somewhere on the globe.

The implication for the Mishnah's formulation is profound. Beis Hillel says *kiddushin* is valid with a *perutah* or *shaveh perutah* — the equivalent of a *perutah*. One might have assumed that if an object is worth less than a *perutah* locally, *kiddushin* is definitively invalid. Shmuel is saying no — there is no case where one can be entirely certain that an object is not worth a *perutah* somewhere in the world.

### Definite vs. Safek

The Gemara reconciles this with Beis Hillel's Mishnah as follows: *lo kashya, ha bekiddushei vadai, ha bekiddushei safek* — there is no contradiction. When Beis Hillel says *shaveh perutah*, he means that if the object is worth a *perutah* in this locale, the *kiddushin* is *vadai* — definitively — valid. If it is not worth a *perutah* here, the *kiddushin* is *safek* — uncertain. Beis Hillel was not saying that a sub-*perutah* object creates no *kiddushin* at all; he was establishing the threshold for *vadai kiddushin*. This is a significant *chumra* embedded in Shmuel's ruling: virtually every case of non-monetary *kiddushin* with an object of minimal value becomes at least a *safek kiddushin*.

### Mother's Testimony

The Gemara illustrates this with a series of cases. A man betrothed a woman with a bundle of rags — essentially a bundle of *shmattes*. Rav Simi bar Chiya sat before Rav and examined the case: *ei beih shaveh prutah, in; ei lo, lo* — if worth a *perutah*, she is married; if not, not. The Gemara objects: even if not worth a *perutah*, according to Shmuel there would still be a *safek kiddushin*. The Gemara responds: *lo kashya, ha bekiddushei vadai, ha bekiddushei safek* — the same resolution applies.

Then comes the case of the colored stone — a man betrothed a woman with what appears to be a decorative stone of the kind sold inexpensively by the handful. Rav Chisda sat and assessed: *ei it bah shaveh prutah, in; ei lo, lo*. Again the Gemara asks: even if not worth a *perutah*, shouldn't Shmuel's principle apply? Here, however, the Gemara gives a different answer: *Rav Chisda lo savar lah de-Shmuel* — Rav Chisda simply does not hold of Shmuel's position.

The narrative continues. The mother of one of the parties — the text is ambiguous — testified that on the day of the betrothal, the stone was worth a *perutah*: *dehahu yoma dekadshah havah bei shaveh prutah*. Rav Chisda dismisses her testimony: *lav kol kaminach de'asrat lah abatra* — you have no standing to render her forbidden to the second man. The dismissal rests on the principle that *ein davar shebe'erva pachos mishnayim* — matters of personal status (*davar shebe'erva*) require two valid witnesses. A single woman's testimony, even if believed in ordinary matters of *issurin*, is insufficient to establish or challenge a marriage bond, especially because the ruling has direct implications for third parties — the second man's status and rights are at stake, not merely an abstract prohibition.

Rav Chisda draws a parallel to the case of Yehudis, the wife of Rabbi Chiya. Yehudis, suffering through the anguish of yet another difficult labor, said to her husband that her mother had once told her that her father had betrothed her to another man when she was a minor. Was she then forbidden to Rabbi Chiya? Rabbi Chiya responded: *lav kol keminach de'imach, de'asrat lich alavai* — your mother has no halakhic standing to make you forbidden to me. The testimony of one woman, carrying no corroboration and implicating a marriage whose consequences fall on others, is entirely dismissed. The ruling in the colored stone case follows the same logic: the stone is assessed on the evidence available, the mother's testimony is dismissed, and the conclusion is that the stone was not worth a *perutah*, so the first *kiddushin* was not valid. She is definitively married to the second man and requires no *get* from the first.

### Witnesses Elsewhere

The rabbinic colleagues then challenge Rav Chisda: *amai, hei ika sahadi be-Idis deyad'ei dehahu yoma hava bei shaveh prutah* — why rule so definitively? There are witnesses in a distant place — somewhere called Idis — who know that on the day of the betrothal the stone was in fact worth a *perutah*. Surely their knowledge creates at least a *safek*? Rav Chisda replies: *hashata miha lo leisnu kaman* — at this moment they are not before us. They are unavailable. One cannot indefinitely suspend a ruling because of witnesses who are elsewhere and have not come forward.

He invokes the principle stated by Rabbi Chanina: *eidei b'tzad istin ve-tinaser* — there are witnesses over there, but she may still remarry. Rabbi Chanina's ruling concerned the daughters of Shmuel who were taken captive. The *halacha* permits them to remarry on their own testimony that they were not violated, because there were no witnesses who directly observed their captivity. Even if one knew abstractly that witnesses existed somewhere, that knowledge does not override the absence of actual testimony before the court.

Abaye and Rava, however, disagree with Rav Chisda's application of this principle. They hold *lo savari leh l'ha deRav Chisda*. The analogy to the captive woman, they argue, is imperfect. In the case of the *shvuyah*, leniency was granted because the concern about her violation is rabbinic in nature, not a *d'oraita* prohibition in the fullest sense — and even there, only because there were no witnesses present. But here, where there are actual witnesses who positively know that the stone was worth a *perutah*, and whose existence is not merely hypothetical, the *d'oraita* concern of *eishet ish* cannot be dismissed so casually.

Tosafot steps in to clarify the parameters of the case. He objects that two extremes are each implausible. If the witnesses' existence is purely conjectural — a simple *shema yesh eidim* with no concrete basis — it is obvious one cannot require a *get* for such a remote possibility, and Rav Chisda would clearly be lenient. On the other hand, if someone had actually heard those witnesses say that the stone was worth a *perutah*, it would be equally obvious that Rav Chisda could not dismiss this so leniently, and even he would require a *get*. Tosafot therefore concludes that the case must be a middle scenario: there was a *kol* — a rumor, a report — that witnesses in a distant location knew the stone's value, but nothing more concrete than that. Rav Chisda was willing to dismiss an unverified rumor of witnesses elsewhere; Abaye and Rava were not. The Gemara adds that in Sura, descendants from the family of that case remained — some married into the general community following Rav Chisda's ruling, but the rabbis themselves were more stringent and avoided marrying into that family. The Gemara clarifies: their stringency was not because they held of Shmuel's principle (that any object might be worth a *perutah* in *Madai*), but because they followed the position of Abaye and Rava — that the rumored existence of witnesses elsewhere creates a genuine *safek* that cannot be set aside.

The practical conclusion that emerges is this: if an object is clearly and definitively worth less than a *perutah* locally, and there is no concrete knowledge of witnesses who know otherwise, we rule that the *kiddushin* is not valid. But where there is some genuine — even if uncertain — basis for believing witnesses exist who know the object was worth a *perutah*, we cannot simply dismiss the possibility of *kiddushin*.

### Myrtle Stick Case

The final case in this sequence: a man betrothed a woman in the marketplace with a stick of myrtle — *shatisa de-asa* — clearly plucked from the ground and worth nothing. Rav Acha bar Huna sent the question to Rav Yosef. The response: *nagdi ke-Rav, ve-itzterich get ke-Shmuel* — he is to receive lashes in the manner of Rav, and she requires a *get* in accordance with Shmuel. The lashes follow from Rav's enactment against *kiddushin* conducted in a marketplace, as we shall see. The *get* follows from Shmuel's principle: however obvious it may be that a myrtle stick is worthless locally, Shmuel requires that we treat it as a *safek kiddushin* on the possibility that it might be worth a *perutah* somewhere far away.

## Rav's Lashes

### Marketplace Kiddushin

Rav enacted that a man who effects *kiddushin* in a marketplace — spontaneously, in an unplanned manner — receives rabbinic lashes. The reason is that marriage is a serious institution requiring deliberate forethought and preparation. Even if the *kiddushin* is fully valid *mid'oraita* — witnesses were present, the woman accepted — the manner in which it was done reflects an attitude toward marriage as something casual and unplanned, like a market transaction. Rav sought to prevent this.

### Without Shiduchim

Similarly, Rav enacted lashes for one who effects *kiddushin* without any prior *shiduchim* — without an engagement process preceding the betrothal. This is closely related to the marketplace *kiddushin* concern: in both cases, the problem is the absence of the deliberateness and gravity that ought to characterize entry into the institution of marriage.

### Kiddushin BeViah

Rav also enacted lashes for one who effects *kiddushin be-viah* — *kiddushin* through intercourse. The deeper concern here is that the structure of Jewish marriage, with its two distinct stages of *kiddushin* and *nisuin*, is designed to communicate that marriage is not reducible to sexuality. The binding commitment — the *kiddushin* — precedes the physical intimacy. When a man makes the act of intercourse itself the vehicle of *kiddushin*, he inverts this structure and reduces the institution to its physical dimension. This sends the wrong message about what marriage is: a commitment that endures independently of the couple's physical life together, a legal and covenantal bond that does not dissolve when circumstances become difficult. Beginning with the physical collapses this distinction. Technically the *kiddushin* may be valid, but Rav required lashes because it reflects a misunderstanding — or a deliberate rejection — of the institution's moral architecture.

### Other Lash Cases

Rav's enactments extend beyond these three cases. One who *mevatel* a *get* — invalidates a bill of divorce that is already in transit — also receives lashes, since the woman may be left in a state of uncertainty about her status, potentially leading to serious harm. One who deposits a *moda'a* against a *get* — who testifies before witnesses that he is being compelled to give the *get* against his will, thereby preemptively undermining it — is also subject to lashes, since this is a strategy to circumvent the court's authority and leave the woman as a *mesurevet get*. One who abuses a rabbinic emissary sent to summon him to court receives lashes for rejecting rabbinic authority. One who allows a *niduy* to remain upon him for thirty days without any effort toward reconciliation shows contempt for rabbinic censure and is escalated to lashes. Finally, a son-in-law who lives in his mother-in-law's home — where there is no father-in-law present — is subject to Rav's enactment, because of the suspicion such an arrangement may generate.

The Gemara clarifies that this last enactment applies only when the son-in-law is actually dwelling there (*de-dayer*), not when he merely visits. However, the case of a son-in-law who regularly passed through the door of his mother-in-law's home and was given lashes by Rav Sheshet is reconciled: in that particular case, *hahu midam havei daima chamsei minei* — there were concrete suspicions circulating about him, not merely the structural concern created by residence.

### Nahardai Limitation

The scholars of Nehardea (*Nahardai*) limit Rav's enactment of lashes to a single category: *kiddushin be-viah belo shiduchim* — *kiddushin* through intercourse without any prior engagement. On this reading, only the combination of these two factors — making intercourse the mode of *kiddushin* and doing so without a prior engagement process — triggers Rav's sanction. The other cases in the list, on the *Nahardai* view, do not.

A further opinion in the Gemara holds that even *kiddushin be-viah with* prior *shiduchim* is subject to lashes — *mishum pritzuta* — on account of immodesty. Tosafot discusses what exactly the *pritzuta* is. One opinion (cited in the name of Rabbeinu Tam) is that it consists in requiring witnesses to the act of intercourse itself, which is inherently immodest. Tosafot's own preferred reading, attributed to the Ri, is simpler and more direct: the *pritzuta* lies in making the inception of the marriage bond — the foundational moment of *kinyan* — consist entirely in an act of intercourse. Even if witnesses to the act are somehow avoided, the message conveyed is one in which marriage is grounded in and defined by sexuality rather than by commitment. This, the Ri argues, is the real *pritzuta*, and it is what Rav sought to uproot through his enactment of lashes.
