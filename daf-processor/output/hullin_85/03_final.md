# Hullin 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Choleh on Shabbat

### Rav Eina Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב עֵינָא אַפִּתְחָא דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת חַיָּיב לְכַסּוֹת. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבָּה: אֶשְׁתּוֹמָא קָאָמַר, לִישְׁמְטוּהּ לְאָמוֹרֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כּוֹי אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאִם שְׁחָטוֹ אֵין מְכַסִּין אֶת דָּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Eina taught at the entrance to the house of the Exilarch: One who slaughters** an undomesticated animal or a bird **for** a critically **ill** person **on Shabbat,** for whom it is permitted to slaughter, is **obligated to cover** its blood. **Rabba said to** those present: Rav Eina **is saying an astonishing** statement; **remove his interpreter from** before **him. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: One may not slaughter a *koy* on a Festival, but if he slaughtered** it, **one does not cover its blood** until after the Festival.

The *daf* begins, actually a few lines before the formal start of 85, on 84b, where *Rav Eina* expounded before the doorstep of the house of the *Resh Galuta* — the political head of the Jewish community in *Bavel*. His teaching concerns a scenario of immediate practical significance: *ha-shochet l'choleh b'Shabbos chayav l'chasot* — if one slaughters an animal for a sick person on *Shabbos*, he is obligated to cover the blood. The premise is that the *shechita* itself was entirely legitimate, performed because the ill person required fresh meat and there was *sakanat nefashot*, a genuine threat to life. The question is whether, having performed a legally sanctioned *shechita*, one then proceeds to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *kissui hadam* — covering the blood — despite the fact that *Shabbos* is in force.

### Three Concerns

To appreciate the force of this ruling, one must first survey the rabbinic concerns that ordinarily would militate against covering blood on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*. Three distinct concerns emerge.

The first and most serious is the problem of digging fresh earth. To cover the blood one typically needs loose dirt, and obtaining it by digging constitutes a classic case of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufah* — a prohibited labor performed without requiring the labor itself as an end, since one wants the dirt, not the hole. Digging a hole for the sake of the dirt one extracts is the paradigmatic example of this category; it is rabbinically prohibited, not biblically. The second concern is *muktzah*: if the earth was not designated before *Yom Tov*, it cannot be handled on *Yom Tov*. The third concern is purely social: if one is seen covering the blood of a doubtful animal — the *koy* — on *Yom Tov*, observers will assume it must be a definite *chayah*, and this will lead them to permit the *chelev* of the *koy*, treating it as a fully kosher *chayah* whose forbidden fat one need not avoid.

### Koy vs. Chaya

These three concerns are not uniformly applicable. The third — the concern about misleading observers — applies only to the *koy*, a creature of uncertain status. It would not apply to a definite *chayah* or *of* slaughtered on *Shabbos* for a *choleh*, where there is no risk of sending a false signal about the animal's classification. The *muktzah* concern applies on *Shabbos* as well, since no earth would have been designated for any purpose on *Shabbos* itself. And the *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufah* concern is present in both contexts, though it represents only a rabbinic prohibition.

Now the question comes into sharper relief. On *Yom Tov*, one slaughters a *koy* and does not cover its blood. What are the reasons? One is the misleading appearance, unique to the *koy*. The other two — *muktzah* and the quasi-*melacha* of digging — represent rabbinic restrictions. By contrast, when one slaughters a definite *chayah* or *of* on *Shabbos* for a *choleh*, the unique-to-*koy* concern is entirely absent, and the remaining concerns are equally rabbinic in character. *Rav Eina*'s ruling is that since all the objections are rabbinic and since the *shechita* itself was fully permitted, we extend the permission to encompass *kissui hadam* as well. The logic is that once the *shechita* is sanctioned, the elements that accompany it are sanctioned as part of that package — even though *kissui hadam* is technically a separate *mitzvah* from *shechita*, not necessary for the welfare of the *choleh*, and not *me'akeiv* any aspect of the *shechita* itself.

One might ask: given that one could simply cover the blood after *Shabbos*, why would any rabbinic restriction be overridden at all? The answer is that *kissui hadam*, while a separate *mitzvah*, is associated with the act of *shechita*, and since all of the restrictions in play are rabbinic, we do not demand that one forgo the *mitzvah* because of rabbinic concerns when the underlying act was itself fully permitted.

### Rabbah's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מִילָה שֶׁוַּדָּאָהּ דּוֹחֶה שַׁבָּת, אֵין סְפֵיקָהּ דּוֹחֶה יוֹם טוֹב, כִּסּוּי שֶׁאֵין וַדָּאוֹ דּוֹחֶה שַׁבָּת, אֵין דִּין שֶׁאֵין סְפֵקוֹ דּוֹחֶה יוֹם טוֹב?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei elaborates: This can be derived **from an *a fortiori*** inference: **And if** with regard to the mitzva of **circumcision,** concerning **which a definite** obligation **overrides Shabbat,** nevertheless **its uncertain** obligation **does not override a Festival;** then with regard to the mitzva of **covering** the blood, concerning **which** even **a definite** obligation **does not override Shabbat,** is it **not logical that its uncertain** obligation, e.g., covering the blood of a *koy*, **would not override a Festival?**

*Rabbah* reacted sharply to this ruling — *ishtomam ka'amar*, he called it an astounding and untenable statement. He adduced a *baraisa* in which *Rabbi Yose* says: one does not slaughter a *koy* on *Yom Tov*, and if one did slaughter it, one does not cover its blood — and the *baraisa* supports this by means of a *kal v'chomer*.

### Kal V'Chomer Milah

The *kal v'chomer* of the *baraisa* proceeds as follows: *milah*, circumcision, when it is definitely required, overrides *Shabbos*. Yet a doubtful *milah* — where, for instance, the child was born at *bein ha-shmashot* and one does not know whether *Yom Tov* is the eighth day or not — does not override even *Yom Tov*. From this the *baraisa* draws an analogy: covering blood, where even a definite obligation to cover does not override *Shabbos*, certainly should not override *Yom Tov* in a case of doubtful obligation, as with the *koy*.

It must be acknowledged that this is a loose *kal v'chomer*. The entire discussion is within the domain of rabbinic restrictions, and the underlying considerations are not perfectly parallel. Nevertheless, the *baraisa* uses this as an analogical framework to define how far rabbinic restrictions should be maintained or relaxed in the face of doubtful obligations.

The critical phrase in the *baraisa* is *kissui she'ein vad'o docha Shabbos* — a definite obligation to cover the blood does not override *Shabbos*. The *gemara* recognizes that this bears directly on *Rav Eina*'s case: it seems to assume that even when *shechita* was performed legitimately for a *choleh*, one still does not cover the blood on *Shabbos*.

### Shofar Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: תְּקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר בִּגְבוּלִים תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵין וַדָּאָהּ דּוֹחֶה שַׁבָּת, וּסְפֵיקָהּ דּוֹחֶה יוֹם טוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said to him** in rebuttal: **The sounding of the shofar in the provinces,** i.e., outside the Temple, **will prove** that this *a fortiori* inference is incorrect. This is **because its definite** obligation **does not override Shabbat** and it is prohibited to sound the shofar on Rosh HaShana that falls on Shabbat; **and** yet **its uncertain** obligation **overrides** the **Festival.**

The *baraisa* continues with a counter-argument from the rabbis: *tekiat shofar bigvulin tochiach* — the blowing of *shofar* proves the contrary. *Shofar* is not blown on *Shabbos* even when there is a definite obligation, yet a doubtful obligation does override *Yom Tov*. This seems to show that doubtful obligations can indeed override *Yom Tov*, which would argue that covering the blood of a *koy* should likewise be permitted.

The *gemara* asks: what is the doubtful case of *shofar* that overrides *Yom Tov*? If one were to say it refers to the second day of *Rosh Hashanah* — *sfeka d'yoma*, uncertainty about which day is truly *Yom Tov* — that is not apposite, because the doubt there is about whether it is even *Yom Tov* at all, not about whether the obligation to blow is strong enough to override *Yom Tov*. Indeed, the *gemara* notes that if blowing overrides a definite *Yom Tov*, it obviously overrides a doubtful one — that proves nothing about the force of a doubtful *mitzvah*.

The correct understanding of the doubtful *shofar* case is *safek ish safek isha* — a *tumtum* or *androgynos*, someone of uncertain sex. There is genuine doubt whether this person is obligated to blow *shofar*, since women are assumed for the moment to be exempt such that blowing for them on *Yom Tov* would constitute playing a musical instrument without a *mitzvah* purpose, which is rabbinically prohibited. Yet for the person of uncertain sex we override this rabbinic restriction because of the doubtful obligation. This then provides a genuine parallel: a doubtful obligation overrides *Yom Tov*'s rabbinic restrictions, and perhaps the same should apply to covering the blood of a *koy*.

### Elazar HaKappar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵשִׁיב רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר בְּרִיבִּי תְּשׁוּבָה: מָה לְמִילָה שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת בְּלֵילֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים, תֹּאמַר בְּכִסּוּי שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בְּלֵילֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar the Distinguished responded** with another **refutation** of the *a fortiori* inference: It cannot be inferred from circumcision that an uncertain obligation of the mitzva of covering the blood does not override a Festival. **What** is notable **about circumcision?** It is notable in **that it is not in effect on Festival nights,** but is performed only during the daytime. Will **you** then **say with regard to** the mitzva of **covering** the blood, **which is in effect on Festival nights,** that it does not override a Festival? Perhaps, since the mitzva of covering the blood is in effect during both day and night, its uncertain obligation overrides a Festival as well.

*Rabbi Elazar HaKappar BeRabbi* offered a further response to *Rabbi Yose*'s position. Even granting the *kal v'chomer* from *milah*, one can distinguish *milah* from *kissui hadam*: *mah l'milah she-kein einah noheget b'leilei yamim tovim* — *milah* is weaker in that it does not apply at night. *Kissui hadam*, by contrast, *noheget b'leilot k'vayamim* — applies at night just as by day. Since *kissui hadam* is in some respects a stronger obligation than *milah*, the *kal v'chomer* cannot be applied, and one might argue that a doubtful *kissui hadam* should override *Yom Tov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: זֶה אֶחָד מִן הַדְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא ״אֵין לִי עֲלֵיהֶם תְּשׁוּבָה״, וְהֵשִׁיב רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר בְּרִיבִּי תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In reference to Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's refutation, **Rabbi Abba says: This** *a fortiori* inference drawn by Rabbi Yosei is **one of the matters** with regard to **which Rabbi Ḥiyya says: I do not have any refutation for them, and Rabbi Elazar HaKappar the Distinguished** successfully **responded** with **a refutation.**

*Rabbi Abba* then records that *Rabbi Chiya* himself acknowledged he had no adequate rejoinder to *Rabbi Yose*'s original argument against covering the blood of a *koy* on *Yom Tov*, while *Rabbi Elazar HaKappar* had provided a cogent counter-argument.

### Baraita on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת, כִּסּוּי שֶׁאֵין וַדָּאוֹ דּוֹחֶה שַׁבָּת. מַאי וַדָּאוֹ דְּכִסּוּי דְּלָא דָּחֵי שַׁבָּת? לָאו הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba concludes his refutation of the statement of Rav Eina: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches that definite** obligations with regard to the mitzva of **covering** the blood **do not override Shabbat.** Now, **what** is a case of **a definite** obligation **of covering** the blood **that does not override Shabbat?** Is it **not** a case where **one slaughters** an undomesticated animal or a bird **for** a critically **ill** person **on Shabbat?**

Returning now to the assumption embedded in the *baraisa*, the *gemara* focuses on the phrase *kissui she'ein vad'o docha Shabbos* — even a definite covering of blood does not override *Shabbos*. The *gemara* asks: what is this definite case of *kissui* that does not override *Shabbos*? Is it not precisely the case of one who slaughtered legitimately for a *choleh* on *Shabbos*, who unquestionably must cover the blood — and yet the *baraisa* assumes this is not done on *Shabbos*? That would directly contradict *Rav Eina*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דְּעָבַר וְשָׁחַט?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps** the *baraita* is referring to an instance **where** one **transgressed** Shabbat **and slaughtered** an animal for a healthy person.

The *gemara* attempts to deflect: perhaps the *baraisa* speaks of one who transgressed and slaughtered on *Shabbos* without permission — in which case, of course, covering the blood is not permitted. But the *gemara* rejects this, because the *baraisa* draws a comparison to *milah*: just as *milah* when definitively required overrides *Shabbos* by permission, and when doubtfully required does not override *Yom Tov*, so too the analogy concerning *kissui* must be dealing with a legitimate, permitted *shechita*. Once the comparison is built on *birshus*, permission, the *baraisa* must mean a case of legitimately permitted *shechita* — and yet it assumes that even then, covering the blood on *Shabbos* is not done.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוּמְיָא דְּמִילָה: מָה מִילָה בִּרְשׁוּת, אַף כִּסּוּי נָמֵי בִּרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The *baraita* cannot be referring to such a case, since Rabbi Yosei compares it with circumcision. Therefore, it must be **similar to** the case of **circumcision,** in that **just as** the **circumcision** was performed **with permission,** as it is a mitzva to perform circumcision even if the eighth day falls on Shabbat, **so too,** the **covering** of the blood must be referring to a case where the slaughter was done **with permission,** i.e., for an ill person.

The conclusion is therefore that one does not cover the blood on *Shabbos* even when the *shechita* was entirely legitimate. *Kissui hadam* is a distinct *mitzvah*; it is not required for the benefit of the *choleh*; it can be done after *Shabbos*. The rabbinic concerns — *muktzah* and the quasi-*melacha* of digging — are not overridden simply because the *shechita* was permitted. One waits until after *Shabbos* and covers the blood then, assuming it remains possible to do so.

A practical concern is worth noting: one cannot wait indefinitely, as there is a risk that the blood will be covered inadvertently or absorbed, and the opportunity for the *mitzvah* will be lost. But the normative ruling is clear: the *shechita* permission does not carry over into permission to perform *kissui hadam* on *Shabbos* itself.

The *gemara* also clarifies that covering the blood of a *koy* on *Yom Tov* is ultimately not permitted — not because the *Yom Tov* restrictions are categorically insurmountable, since all relevant restrictions are rabbinic, but largely because of the concern articulated in *Beitzah*: observers will conclude that since the blood is being covered the *koy* is a definite *chayah*, and will come to permit its *chelev*. The innate rabbinic restrictions might have been overridden for a doubtful *mitzvah*, but this distinct social concern — the risk of erroneous conclusions about the *koy*'s status — is sufficient to maintain the prohibition. The *stam* of the *Mishnah* thus follows *Rabbi Yose* that one does not cover the blood of a *koy* on *Yom Tov*.

## Shechita Invalid

### Invalid Shechita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה, וְהַשּׁוֹחֵט לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְהַשּׁוֹחֵט חוּלִּין בִּפְנִים וְקָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ, חַיָּה וָעוֹף הַנִּסְקָלִים – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים. הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְנִתְנַבְּלָה בְּיָדוֹ, הַנּוֹחֵר וְהַמְעַקֵּר – פָּטוּר מִלְּכַסּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters** an undomesticated animal or a bird **and it is discovered to be an animal with a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*];** and in the case of **one who slaughters** an undomesticated animal or a bird **for** the sake of **idol worship; and** in the case of **one who slaughters a non-sacred** animal or bird **inside** the Temple courtyard **or a sacrificial** bird **outside** the Temple courtyard; or in the case of one who slaughters **an undomesticated animal or a bird that** was sentenced to **be stoned,** e.g., for killing a person; in all these cases, even though it is prohibited to eat any of these animals or birds, **Rabbi Meir deems** one **obligated** to cover their blood, **and the Rabbis deem** one **exempt** from doing so because, in their opinion, slaughter that is not fit to render the meat permitted for consumption is not considered an act of slaughter. **One who slaughters** an animal or bird **and it became a carcass by his hand,** i.e., the slaughter was performed incorrectly, and **one who stabs** the animal or bird, **and one who tears loose** the windpipe and the gullet, are **exempt from covering** the blood, as no act of slaughter took place, and one is obligated to cover blood only after a valid slaughter.

The *Mishnah* opens a new discussion with the following cases of *shechita she'eina re'uya* — *shechita* that does not render the meat permissible. One slaughtered and discovered the animal was a *treifa*; one slaughtered as an act of worship to *avodah zarah*; one slaughtered *chulin bifnim* — non-consecrated animals inside the *Beit HaMikdash*; or *kodashim bachutz* — a *korban* slaughtered outside. The *Mishnah* also includes a *chayah* or bird destined to be stoned, having caused a human death. All of these involve a genuine act of *shechita* that nonetheless fails to make the meat permissible — either because the animal is forbidden to be eaten, or because benefit from it is entirely forbidden.

It is worth noting that the case of *kodashim bachutz* appears somewhat redundant, since we have already been taught that *kissui hadam* does not apply to *korbanot* in the first place. Its inclusion here seems to reflect a formulaic listing.

### Rabbi Meir vs. Sages

The dispute in this *Mishnah* is between *Rabbi Meir* and the sages. *Rabbi Meir* holds that one is still obligated to cover the blood even in all of these cases of *shechita she'eina re'uya*, while the sages exempt. The final clause of the *Mishnah* presents a point of consensus: one who slaughtered and inadvertently produced a *neveilah* — botching the *shechita* — or who strangled or tore out the *simanim* without any act of *shechita*, is exempt from *kissui hadam* by everyone's account. A formal act of *shechita* is required; the dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and the sages concerns only what happens when there is a genuine act of *shechita* but it fails to render the meat permissible.

### Rabbi's Split Vote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רָאָה רַבִּי דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, וּשְׁנָאוֹ בִּלְשׁוֹן חֲכָמִים, וּדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּכִסּוּי הַדָּם, וּשְׁנָאוֹ בִּלְשׁוֹן חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the dispute in the mishna about whether an act of slaughter that is not fit to render the meat permitted renders one obligated to cover the blood, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the redactor of the Mishna, **saw** as correct **the statement of Rabbi Meir,** that ineffective slaughter is considered an act of slaughter, **with regard to** the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day, **and taught** that *halakha* in the mishna (81b) **using the term: The Rabbis,** so that it would be accepted. **And** he saw as correct the statement **of Rabbi Shimon,** that ineffective slaughter is not considered an act of slaughter **with regard to** the mitzva of **covering the blood, and taught** that *halakha* in the mishna here **using the term: The Rabbis.**

This *Mishnah* generates an immediate tension with the material of the preceding *perek*. Earlier, in discussing *oto v'et beno*, the position that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* counts as *shechita* was attributed to the sages — that is, *Rabbi* in that *perek* sided with the view that such a *shechita* counts. Here, in this *perek*, the sages say it does not count for *kissui hadam*. How is this resolved?

*Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* explains: *ra'ah Rebbi devarav shel Rabbi Meir b'Oso Ve'et Beno* — *Rabbi* saw merit in *Rabbi Meir*'s position for the purposes of *oto v'et beno*, and therefore in that *perek* he taught it over as the position of the sages. *V'shanah bilshon chachamim* — he attributed it to the sages because he endorsed it. Here, in the *perek* of *kissui hadam*, *Rabbi* saw merit in *Rabbi Shimon*'s position — that *shechita she'eina re'uya* does not count — and accordingly attributed that position to the sages. In each *perek*, the position *Rabbi* endorses becomes the voice of the sages.

### Rabbi Meir Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: גָּמַר שְׁחִיטָה שְׁחִיטָה מִשְּׁחוּטֵי חוּץ. מָה הָתָם – שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי – שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason that Rabbi Meir** holds that ineffective slaughter is considered an act of slaughter in the case of **a mother and its offspring? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** He **derives** his opinion by means of a verbal analogy of the terms **slaughter** and **slaughter, from** the case of sacrificial animals **slaughtered outside** the Temple. The verse states with regard to a mother and its offspring: "It and its offspring you shall not slaughter [*lo tishḥatu*] in one day" (Leviticus 22:28), and the verse states with regard to sacrificial animals: "Or that is slaughtered [*yishḥat*] outside the camp" (Leviticus 17:3). Accordingly, **just as there,** with regard to one who slaughters an offering outside the Temple it is a case of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted, as one is prohibited from deriving any benefit from such meat, and it **is** nevertheless **considered** an act of **slaughter** to render one liable for slaughtering it outside the Temple, **so too here,** in the case of a mother and its offspring, an act of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted **is considered** an act of **slaughter,** and one is liable.

The *gemara* now unfolds the underlying reasoning of each position. What is *Rabbi Meir*'s basis for saying that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* still counts as *shechita*?

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* explains: *gemar shechita shechita mishchutei chutz* — he derives it through a shared verbal form from the laws of *shechutei chutz*, the slaughter of a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The Torah uses the verb *ve-shachat* in the context of *shechutei chutz*, a case where the meat is entirely not fit for consumption. Yet the Torah still uses the language of *shechita*. The same verb appears in the prohibition of *oto v'et beno*. Just as *shechita* there refers to an act even when it produces meat that cannot be eaten, so too by *oto v'et beno* an act of *shechita* that does not make the meat fit is still called *shechita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּכִסּוּי הַדָּם? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: גָּמַר שְׁפִיכָה שְׁפִיכָה מִשְּׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, מָה לְהַלָּן – שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, אַף כָּאן – שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion of the dispute in the mishna: **What is the reason that Rabbi Meir** holds one is obligated **in** the mitzva of **covering the blood** in a case of ineffective slaughter? **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** Rabbi Meir **derives** this from a verbal analogy between the terms **pouring** and **pouring, from** the case of a sacrificial animal that was **slaughtered outside** the Temple courtyard. One verse states with regard to covering the blood: "And he shall pour out its blood" (Leviticus 17:13), and one verse states with regard to sacrificial animals slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard: "He has poured blood" (Leviticus 17:4). Accordingly, **just as there,** with regard to offerings slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, it is a case of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted but **is** nevertheless **considered** an act of **slaughter, so too here,** with regard to covering the blood, an act of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted **is considered** an act of **slaughter.**

The *gemara* also explains *Rabbi Meir*'s reasoning for *kissui hadam* specifically. *Reish Lakish* derives it from a *gezerah shavah* of *shfichah* — spilling. Regarding *shechutei chutz*, the Torah says *dam shafach* — it is as if one spilled blood. Regarding *kissui hadam*, the Torah says *ve-shafach et damo* — and he shall spill its blood. Just as by *shechutei chutz* the meat is not fit but it is still called *shechita*, so too by *kissui hadam*, even if the *shechita* does not make the meat fit, it is still called *shechita* and coverage is required.

### Shimon Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״ כְּתִיב, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: הָהוּא לְמַעוֹטֵי עוֹף טָמֵא הוּא דַּאֲתָא, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: עוֹף טָמֵא מַאי טַעְמָא – דְּלָאו בַּר אֲכִילָה הוּא, טְרֵפָה נָמֵי – לָאו בַּר אֲכִילָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And Rabbi Shimon** would respond: It is **written** with regard to covering the blood: "An undomesticated animal or bird **that may be eaten"** (Leviticus 17:13), indicating that the verse is referring specifically to slaughter fit to render the meat permitted for consumption. **And Rabbi Meir** holds: **That** phrase **comes to exclude a ritually impure bird** from the mitzva of covering the blood. **And Rabbi Shimon** would respond: **What is the reason an impure bird** is not included in the mitzva of covering the blood? It is **because it is not fit for consumption.** If so, **a *tereifa*** should **also** not be included in the mitzva of covering the blood, since **it is** also **not fit for consumption.**

*Rabbi Shimon* counters *Rabbi Meir*'s general argument differently for each context. By *oto v'et beno*, he derives from the verse *tvo'ach tevach ve-hachein* — the instruction to *Yosef*'s steward to slaughter the meat for the midday meal — that *shechita* inherently implies meat that will be eaten. The *gemara* challenges this: why use that verse, which employs a different verb (*tavach* rather than *shachat*), when *shechutei chutz* uses the identical verb *shachat*? The answer is a principle from the academy of *Rabbi Yishmael*: when there is an identical word available, one derives from the identical word. *Ve-shav ha-kohein u-va ha-kohein* — returning and coming — are similar enough to link two passages concerning *tzara'at*, but only where no identical word is available. Since *shachat* appears in *shechutei chutz*, one must derive from there, not from the *tavach* of *Yosef*'s household. *Rabbi Shimon*'s response is that *chulin* must be derived from *chulin*, not from the world of *kodashim* — *danin chulin mi-chulin ve-ein danin chulin mi-kodashim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ בְּקָדָשִׁים מִי לָא נָהֵיג? הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: רָאָה רַבִּי דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, וּשְׁנָאוֹ בִּלְשׁוֹן חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And Rabbi Meir** would respond: **Is this to say** that the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day **does not apply to sacrificial** animals? Rather, since the prohibition also applies to sacrificial animals, one can derive its *halakha* from the case of sacrificial animals that were slaughtered outside the Temple. The Gemara comments: **This is** what **Rabbi Ḥiyya** was referring to when he **said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **saw** as correct **the statement of Rabbi Meir with regard to** the *halakha* of **a mother and its offspring, and taught it** in the mishna **using the term: The Rabbis.**

*Rabbi Meir* in turn replies: *oto v'et beno* applies even to *kodashim*, so there is no objection to deriving from *shechutei chutz*.

For *kissui hadam* specifically, *Rabbi Shimon* has a powerful textual argument. The verse states *asher yatzud tzeid chayah o of asher ye'achel* — whoever traps a *chayah* or bird *that will be eaten*. The phrase *asher ye'achel* — that will be eaten — explicitly conditions the obligation on the *shechita* producing meat that is fit for consumption. *Rabbi Meir*'s response is that *asher ye'achel* comes only to exclude a non-kosher animal by species — *of tamei* — not to require that the *shechita* make the meat fit. *Rabbi Shimon* counters: the very reason we exclude a non-kosher bird is that it is *lav bar achilah* — not edible. The same logic then applies to any case where the meat is not edible, including a *treifa* or a *shechita l'avodah zarah*.

Thus, the simpler way to understand *Rabbi*'s split vote: as a general principle, *shechita she'eina re'uya* counts as *shechita*, which is why *oto v'et beno* is triggered even when the *shechita* is not fit. But for *kissui hadam*, the Torah's explicit phrase *asher ye'achel* introduces a specific requirement that the *shechita* produce meat that may be eaten — and therefore only *Rabbi Shimon*'s position, which insists on this condition, applies here.

### Fetus in Womb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: לֹא לַכֹּל אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁאֵין מַתִּירָתָהּ בַּאֲכִילָה; וְלֹא לַכֹּל אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לֹא שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁמְּטַהַרְתָּהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Not with regard to all** *halakhot* did **Rabbi Meir say** an act of **slaughter that is not fit,** e.g., where one slaughters a *tereifa*, **is considered** an act of **slaughter,** as **Rabbi Meir** does **concede that** such an act of slaughter **does not render** the meat of the animal **permitted for consumption. And not with regard to all** *halakhot* did **Rabbi Shimon say** an act of **slaughter that is not fit is not considered** an act of **slaughter,** as **Rabbi Shimon** does **concede that** such an act of slaughter **renders** the animal **pure from** having the impure status of an unslaughtered **animal carcass.**

The *gemara* now turns to a fascinating *hava amina* arising from *Rabbi Meir*'s position. *Rabbi Abba* states: *lo l'chol amar Rabbi Meir shechita she'eina re'uya shema shechita* — even *Rabbi Meir* did not say that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* counts for all purposes. Specifically, *Rabbi Meir* concedes that it does not render the meat permissible for eating — *she'einah matirta b'achilah*. The *gemara* notes this is nearly self-evident: whoever thought that slaughtering a *treifa* makes its meat permitted?

The real case the *gemara* has in mind is more subtle. Suppose one slaughters an animal that is a *treifa*, and finds within its womb a live nine-month fetus — a *ben pekuah* with a degree of independent status. Might one argue: since *Rabbi Meir* holds that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* still counts as an act of *shechita*, let the *shechita* of the *treifa* mother count for the fetus in the womb, thereby permitting the fetus without requiring its own separate *shechita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, לְשׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה חַי; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל דְּאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, תַּהֲנֵי לֵיהּ שְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ וְלָא לִיבְעֵי שְׁחִיטָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this statement of Rabbi Abba **is necessary in** a case where **one slaughtered a *tereifa* and found a living nine-month-old** fetus **inside it.** It might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Meir said** an act of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted **is considered** an act of **slaughter, the slaughter of its mother should be effective to** render the meat of the fetus permitted for consumption, as it normally does when the mother is not a *tereifa*; and the fetus, after it is born, **should not require** its own **slaughter.** Rabbi Abba therefore **teaches us** that according to Rabbi Meir, slaughter that does not render the meat of the mother permitted for consumption does not render the fetus permitted either.

This is indeed a sophisticated *hava amina*. The fetus is not itself a *treifa* — its mother's condition does not apply to it as it would to a limb. The fetus has its own independent vitality. One might reason: the act of *shechita* was performed; *Rabbi Meir* says that even a non-fitting *shechita* is still an act of *shechita*; the fetus, being its own independent entity, can have that act of *shechita* applied to it even though it did not render the mother's meat permissible.

### Heter Through Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בֶּן פְּקוּעָה טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how can **you understand** that without Rabbi Abba's statement, one would have thought that according to Rabbi Meir, the fetus does not require its own slaughter? **But doesn't Rabbi Meir say** that **a fetus removed from its mother's womb after the mother was properly slaughtered [*ben pekua*], requires slaughter,** as the slaughter of the mother does not permit the fetus?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן. סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: שְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ מְטַהַרְתּוֹ. כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן שְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ מְטַהַרְתּוֹ, תַּהֲנֵי לֵיהּ שְׁחִיטַת אִמּוֹ וְלָא לִיבְעֵי שְׁחִיטָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The statement of Rabbi Abba **is not necessary** for the opinion of Rabbi Meir, but it is for the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. This is **because Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** in one instance, **and holds in accordance with** the opinion of the Rabbis in another. In a case where one slaughtered a mother and its offspring on the same day **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** an act of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted **is considered** an act of **slaughter. And** with regard to a *ben pekua*, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say the** proper **slaughter of the mother renders** the fetus **kosher,** i.e., the fetus does not require its own ritual slaughter. Therefore, **since the Rabbis say the** proper **slaughter of the mother renders** the fetus **kosher,** one might conclude that with regard to a *tereifa* as well, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds **the slaughter of the mother should be effective to** render the meat of the fetus permitted for consumption, **and it should not require** its own **slaughter.** In light of this, Rabbi Abba **teaches us** that although Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees with Rabbi Meir that an ineffective slaughter is considered an act of slaughter, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that in the case of a *tereifa* the slaughter of the mother is not effective for the fetus.

*Rabbi Meir*'s answer — and this is the *gemara*'s clarification of where he draws the line — is that the *heter* of the fetus comes through the *heter* of the mother. It is not that the act of *shechita* is applied directly and independently to the fetus. Rather, when the *shechita* renders the mother's meat permissible, that permissibility extends to whatever is inside her. The mechanism is the *heter shechita* working through the mother, not an independent application of the act to the fetus. Since the *shechita* did not render the mother's meat permissible — she was a *treifa* — the *heter* cannot flow through to the fetus. *Rabbi Meir* would agree: there is no standalone application of the act of *shechita* to the fetus. The fetus's *heter* is derivative of the mother's *heter*, and where the mother's *heter* fails, so does the fetus's.

The *gemara* also notes that the *hava amina* is anyway complicated by *Rabbi Meir*'s own view: he holds that a live nine-month-old fetus — *ben pekuah* — requires its own *shechita*, meaning the mother's *shechita* does not work for it even in the standard case. So for *Rabbi Meir* himself, the question is moot. The *gemara* therefore reformulates: this *kamashmalan* is directed at *Rabbi* — who generally follows the sages that the mother's *shechita* does work for the fetus, and who follows *Rabbi Meir* that *shechita she'eina re'uya* counts. Where these two positions intersect — a *treifa* mother whose *shechita* would ordinarily extend to her fetus — *Rabbi* would draw the line: the *heter* flows through the mother, and a failed *heter* at the mother produces no *heter* for the fetus.

### Neveilah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא לַכֹּל אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לֹא שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁמְּטַהַרְתָּהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״, מִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה מְטַמְּאָה וּמִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה, וְאֵי זוֹ זוֹ – טְרֵפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses the second statement of Rabbi Abba: **And not with regard to all** *halakhot* did **Rabbi Shimon say** an act of **slaughter that is not fit is not considered** an act of **slaughter,** as **Rabbi Shimon concedes that** such an act of slaughter **renders** the animal **pure from** having the impure status of an unslaughtered **animal carcass.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*:** The verse states with regard to an animal carcass: **"And if there dies of the animals**…he that touches its carcass shall be unclean" (Leviticus 11:39). The word "of" indicates that **some animals transmit impurity** as a carcass **and some animals do not transmit impurity. And which** animal does not? **That is a *tereifa* that** one **slaughtered.**

The *gemara* turns to the corresponding concession from *Rabbi Shimon*: *lo l'chol amar Rabbi Shimon shechita she'eina re'uya lav shema shechita* — not for all purposes did *Rabbi Shimon* say a non-fitting *shechita* counts for nothing. *Modeh Rabbi Shimon shemetaharto midei neveilah* — he concedes that even a non-fitting *shechita* removes the *neveilah* status from the animal.

This is not difficult to understand. The distinction between a *treifa* and a *neveilah* is precisely whether an act of *shechita* was performed. A *treifa* is an animal that had *shechita* but whose meat is forbidden due to a pre-existing physical defect; a *neveilah* is an animal that died without *shechita*. Even *Rabbi Shimon*, who says that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* is meaningless for purposes of creating prohibitions like *kissui hadam* or *oto v'et beno*, concedes that it was nonetheless an act of *shechita* and therefore the animal did not die the death of a *neveilah*.

The *gemara* draws on the teaching of *Rav Huna* in the name of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* — or, as some say, taught in a *baraita* — that the verse *veki yamut min ha-behemah* — when an animal dies — introduces *neveilah* tumah; but *miktzat behemah* only some animal deaths convey tumah. The death that does not is: *zo treifa she-shachtah* — a *treifa* that was slaughtered. Everyone agrees that the act of *shechita*, however ineffective otherwise, suffices to prevent *neveilah* status.

### Treifa in Azarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, לְשׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה וְהִיא חוּלִּין בַּעֲזָרָה. דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה, וְכֵן הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה, זֶה וָזֶה חוּלִּין בַּעֲזָרָה – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר בַּהֲנָאָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** Rabbi Abba's statement **is necessary in** the case of **one who slaughters a *tereifa*, and it is a non-sacred** animal **in the Temple courtyard. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who slaughters** a known ***tereifa*, and likewise one who slaughters** an animal **and it was found** to be **a *tereifa*,** and **this and that** were **non-sacred** animals slaughtered **in the Temple courtyard, Rabbi Shimon permits** one **to** derive **benefit** from them, as one is not considered to have transgressed the prohibition against slaughtering a non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard. **And the Rabbis prohibit** one to derive benefit from them.

The *gemara* identifies a less obvious case in which *Rabbi Shimon*'s concession becomes halachically significant: *shochtet et ha-treifa vehi chulin ba'azarah* — one slaughtered a *treifa* and it happened to be a non-consecrated animal inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, i.e., *chulin ba'azarah*. This case combines two forms of *shechita she'eina re'uya*: the animal's *treifa* status and the prohibited location.

A *baraita* teaches: whether one knew it was a *treifa* from the outset or discovered it afterward — *zeh va-zeh chulin ba'azarah*, and it constitutes *chulin ba'azarah* in both cases. The question is whether performing *shechita* on a *treifa* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* constitutes the forbidden act of *chulin ba'azarah* — bringing non-consecrated meat into the Temple precincts through the act of *shechita*.

### Vote Splitting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַלְמָא לָאו שְׁחִיטָה הִיא כְּלָל, אֵימָא: מִידֵי נְבֵלָה נָמֵי לָא מְטַהֲרָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, it might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Shimon says** it is **permitted to derive benefit** from such an animal, **evidently** slaughtering a *tereifa* **is not considered slaughter at all.** Therefore, **say** that slaughter **does not** have the ability to **purify** the *tereifa* **from** having **the status of** a ritually impure **animal carcass either.** Rabbi Abba therefore **teaches us** that even according to Rabbi Shimon, the slaughter is effective to remove it from the status of a carcass.

This is precisely where the dispute between *Rabbi Shimon* and the sages — here meaning *Rabbi Meir* — plays out practically. *Rabbi Shimon*, who says *shechita she'eina re'uya lav shema shechita*, holds that since the animal was a *treifa* the *shechita* was not a valid act of *shechita*, and therefore one did not transgress *chulin ba'azarah* — *muttar bahana'ah*, the animal is still permissible in benefit. The sages — *Rabbi Meir* — hold that it counts as *shechita* and therefore one did violate *chulin ba'azarah* and the meat is forbidden in benefit.

Now the *gemara* raises a striking *hava amina* regarding *Rabbi Shimon*'s own position. Since *Rabbi Shimon* says it was not a *shechita* — thus exempting from *chulin ba'azarah* — one might conclude that he has committed fully to calling it a non-act: if it was no *shechita* at all for purposes of *chulin ba'azarah*, then perhaps it should also fail to remove *neveilah* status. The *kamashmalan* of the *gemara* is that one may split the vote. *Rabbi Shimon* can say: from the perspective of *chulin ba'azarah*, the act was not a valid *shechita* — the prohibition is not triggered; but from the perspective of *neveilah*, the animal did undergo an act of *shechita* and is not a *neveilah*. Whenever an act of *shechita* is performed on an animal, it removes the animal from the category of *neveilah*, even when that same act of *shechita* is too defective to activate the prohibition of *chulin ba'azarah* and too defective to make the meat permissible.

The conceptual point with which the *daf* concludes is thus a refined account of what *shechita she'eina re'uya* actually means: it was an act of *shechita* — the animal was not killed by natural death or violence — yet the *heter shechita*, the permissive effect of *shechita*, was not realized. For purposes that require only the act, even *Rabbi Shimon* recognizes its significance. For purposes that require the *heter*, even *Rabbi Meir* would draw a line. The *gemara* will continue to develop these distinctions in the discussion that follows.
