# Zevachim 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melikah & Bird Offerings

### Melikah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹלַת הָעוֹף כֵּיצַד נַעֲשֵׂית? עָלָה לַכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב, בָּא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית. הָיָה מוֹלֵק אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ מִמּוּל עׇרְפָּה, וּמַבְדִּיל, וּמִיצָּה אֶת דָּמָהּ עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. נָטַל אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְהִקִּיף בֵּית מְלִיקָתוֹ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, סְפָגוֹ בְּמֶלַח, וּזְרָקוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The sacrifice of **the bird burnt offering, how was it performed?** The priest **ascended the ramp and turned to the surrounding ledge and came to** the **southeast corner** of the altar. He **would** then **pinch** off **the** bird's **head** by cutting **at its nape** with his thumbnail **and separate** the bird's head from its body, **and** would **squeeze out its blood on the wall of the altar. He took the head and neared the place of its pinching,** i.e., its nape, **to the altar** in order to squeeze the blood from the head. **He would** then **absorb** the remaining blood **with salt and throw** the head **onto the fire** on the altar.

Daf 65 opens in the middle of a very long *mishnah* that began on the previous daf. After discussing where on the *mizbeach* the various bird sacrifices are performed, the *mishnah* turns to the mechanics of how one carries out those sacrifices — specifically, the process of *melikah*, the severing of the bird's head with the thumb. The *gemara* on the previous daf went into considerable detail about how the *kohein* actually holds the bird in order to perform this act.

### Olah vs. Chatas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לַגּוּף, וְהֵסִיר אֶת הַמּוּרְאָה וְאֶת הַנּוֹצָה וְאֶת בְּנֵי הַמֵּעַיִים הַיּוֹצְאִין עִמָּם, וְהִשְׁלִיכָן לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן. שִׁיסַּע, וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל. וְאִם הִבְדִּיל – כָּשֵׁר. סוֹפְגוֹ בְּמֶלַח, וְזֹרְקוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **He** then **arrived at the body and removed the crop and the feather** attached to it **and the innards that emerge with them and he tossed them to the place of the ashes. He** then **ripped** the bird lengthwise **and did not separate** the two halves of the bird; **but if he separated** them, the offering is **valid.** He would then **absorb** the remaining blood **with salt and throw** the body of the bird **onto the fire** on the altar.

The *mishnah* also distinguishes between the *olah* and the *chatas* in terms of where each is performed and how the blood is handled. The *olah* is performed at the top of the *mizbeach* and the *chatas* at the bottom — the reverse of the arrangement for mammal sacrifices. By the *olah*, the head is fully severed, both *simanim* (trachea and esophagus) are cut through, and the blood is squeezed out directly — there is no squirting. The head is salted and thrown onto the fire, and the body is salted and thrown onto the fire separately. By the *chatas*, the head is not fully severed; only one *siman* is cut, and the blood squirts out before the remainder is squeezed out. The *olah* involves burning the bird; the *chatas* does not.

### Lishmah & Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֵסִיר הַמּוּרְאָה וְלֹא הַנּוֹצָה וְאֶת בְּנֵי מֵעַיִים הַיּוֹצְאִין עִמָּם, וְלֹא סְפָגוֹ בְּמֶלַח – כֹּל שֶׁשִּׁינָּה בָּהּ מֵאַחֵר שֶׁמִּיצָּה אֶת דָּמָהּ, כְּשֵׁירָה. הִבְדִּיל בַּחַטָּאת וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל בָּעוֹלָה – פָּסוּל. מִיצָּה אֶת דַּם הָרֹאשׁ וְלֹא מִיצָּה דַּם הַגּוּף – פְּסוּלָה. דַּם הַגּוּף וְלֹא מִיצָּה דַּם הָרֹאשׁ – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he neither removed the crop, nor the feather and the innards that emerge with them, nor absorbed** the blood **with salt,** with regard to **any** detail **that he changed in** the sacrificial rites **after he squeezed out its blood,** the offering is **valid.** If **he separated** the head from the body **in** sacrificing **the sin offering, or** if **he did not separate** the head from the body **in** sacrificing **the burnt offering,** the offering is **disqualified.** In a case where **he squeezed out the blood of the head and did not squeeze out the blood of the body, it is disqualified.** If he squeezed out the **blood of the body and did not squeeze out the blood of the head,** the offering is **valid.**

The *mishnah* then reviews the familiar framework of *lishmah* and *pigul* as it applies to bird sacrifices — noting, as we have seen in prior discussions about mammal sacrifices, what the critical junctures are. Whereas by a mammal sacrifice there are four critical stages (slaughter, catching of the blood, transporting, and throwing), by a bird sacrifice there are effectively only two: the *melikah* itself, and either the squirting of the blood (for a *chatas*) or the squeezing out of the blood (for an *olah*).

## Chatas Shelo Lishmah

### Chatas Invalidation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף שֶׁמְּלָקוֹ [שֶׁמְּלָקוֹ] שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וּמִיצָּה דָּמוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ – זֶהוּ שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** or in the case of **a bird sin offering where one pinched** its nape **not for its sake and squeezed out** its **blood** with the intent of consuming it or burning it **beyond its** designated **time, or** in a case **where he pinched** its nape with the intent to consume it or burn it **beyond its** designated **time and squeezed out** its **blood not for its sake, or** in a case **where he pinched** its nape **and squeezed out its blood not for its sake, that is** a case of a bird offering **whose permitting factor is not sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

The *gemara* at the top of 65a addresses the case of a bird *chatas* whose *melikah* was performed *shelo lishmah*. Just as with an animal *chatas*, performing the *melikah* *shelo lishmah* invalidates the bird *chatas* — though it does not create *pigul* and the owner receives no atonement. By contrast, a bird *olah* performed *shelo lishmah*, like its animal counterpart, is not invalidated outright; it simply does not count for the owner.

### Mixed Thoughts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר, כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ, כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר, כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** If one pinched the nape of the bird and squeezed out its blood with the intent **to eat an olive-bulk** of the offering **outside** its designated area and **an olive-bulk the next day,** or **an olive-bulk the next day** and **an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, or **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area and **half an olive-bulk the next day,** or **half an olive-bulk the next day** and **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, the offering is **disqualified and** it **does not include** liability to receive ***karet*.**

The *gemara* then addresses the case in which a *shelo lishmah* thought is combined with a *pigul* thought — that is, where the *melikah* was performed *shelo lishmah* and the subsequent squeezing of the blood was intended *chutz lizmano* (outside its proper time). In such a case, by the *chatas*, the *shelo lishmah* thought derails the sacrifice from becoming *pigul*, since the *korban* was already invalidated through a different avenue. By the *olah*, however, this does not prevent *pigul* from taking effect.

### Separate Kezaysim

A further scenario is introduced: what if, rather than improper thoughts occurring at different stages of the *avodah*, the *kohein* had two different improper thoughts about two distinct *kezaysim* — intending, for example, to eat one *kezayit* *chutz limkomo* (outside its proper place) and another *kezayit* *chutz lizmano* (outside its proper time)? These are not thoughts about different stages of the *avodah* but rather thoughts about different portions of the animal. The *gemara* cycles through several permutations: one *kezayit* outside its location and one outside its time; the same in reverse order; half a *kezayit* outside its location and half outside its time; and again in reverse. In all these cases, the ruling is *pasul v'ein bo kares* — invalid, but without *kares* and without *pigul*.

In the last two cases, the absence of *pigul* follows clearly from the fact that the *chutz lizmano* thought encompassed only half a *kezayit*, which is insufficient to create *pigul* on its own. In the first two cases, the reasoning is that even though there was a full *kezayit* of *chutz lizmano* intent, the simultaneous intent to eat a *kezayit chutz limkomo* — a different type of invalidating thought — prevents the sacrifice from becoming *pigul*.

### Rav Yehuda's Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, זֶה הַכְּלָל: אִם מַחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. וְאִם מַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda** disagreed and **said** that **this is the principle: If the** improper **intent** with regard to **the time preceded the intent** with regard to **the area,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating **it. And if the intent** with regard to **the area preceded the intent** with regard to **the time,** the offering is **disqualified and** it **does not include** liability to receive ***karet*.**

Rav Yehuda disagrees with this blanket ruling. He holds that the order of the thoughts is determinative. If the *chutz lizmano* thought came first (*machsheves hazman kadma l'machsheves hamakom*), the sacrifice becomes *pigul* and one is *chayav kares*. In his view, once *pigul* has taken effect, a subsequent *chutz limkomo* thought about a different *kezayit* does not undo it. If, however, the *chutz limkomo* thought came first, the sacrifice has already been invalidated through that avenue, and a subsequent *pigul* thought cannot take hold — the ruling is *pasul v'ein bo kares*. This principle parallels what was established in prior discussions regarding *nesachim*.

### Chachamim Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.]
>
> **Translation:** [**And the Rabbis say:** In both **this** case where the intent with regard to time came first **and that** case where the intent with regard to area came first, the offering is **disqualified and** it **does not include** liability to receive ***karet*.**]

The *Chachamim* reject Rav Yehuda's distinction based on order. In their view, whichever thought came first, the combination of the two different invalidating thoughts prevents *pigul* from applying — in either sequence, the ruling is *pasul v'ein bo kares*.

### Eating & Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶאֱכוֹל כַּחֲצִי זַיִת וּלְהַקְטִיר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁאֵין אֲכִילָה וְהַקְטָרָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** If his intent was **to eat half an olive-bulk and to burn half an olive-bulk** not at the appropriate time or in the appropriate area, the offering is **valid, because eating and burning do not join together.**

A final case is noted: if one had a thought to eat half a *kezayit* at the wrong time and to burn half a *kezayit* at the wrong time, this does not constitute *pigul*, because eating and burning do not combine to form a single *pigul* thought. The *gemara* observes that this review of the rules of *pigul* and *lishmah* as applied to bird sacrifices is not substantially new — all of it follows from what was established regarding larger animals. The *mishnah* could theoretically have stated simply that all the *pigul* laws applicable to animal sacrifices apply equally to bird sacrifices; it chose to record the *machlokes* explicitly.

## Pesukim Analysis

### Olah Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִקְרִיבוֹ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** a *baraita* with regard to the verse pertaining to a bird burnt offering: "And the priest shall bring it to the altar, and pinch off its head, and make it smoke on the altar" (Leviticus 1:15). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: And** the priest **shall bring it** to the altar? Since the previous verse states that "he shall bring his offering," this verse should have started with the phrase "And he shall pinch off its head."

The *gemara* now turns to the *pesukim* in *Vayikra* that describe the procedures for bird sacrifices, since some of the laws discussed in the *mishnah* are derived from careful reading of these verses. The *pesukim* for the bird *olah* appear in the opening of *Vayikra* (1:14–17), where the Torah transitions from animal offerings to bird offerings. The relevant passage reads: *v'im min ha-of olah korbano la-Hashem v'hikriv min ha-torim o min bnei ha-yonah et korbano* — and if his offering is a bird *olah* to God, he shall bring from the turtle-doves or from the young pigeons. *V'hikrivo ha-kohein el ha-mizbeach* — the *kohein* shall bring it close to the altar. *U-malak et rosho v'hiktir ha-mizbei'ach* — he shall perform *melikah* on its head and burn it on the altar. *V'nimtza damo al kir ha-mizbeach* — and its blood shall be squeezed out on the wall of the altar.

The *gemara* notes immediately that this sequence is puzzling: it says first that the *kohein* burns it and then that he squeezes out the blood. Obviously, squeezing out the blood must precede burning the bird. The out-of-order presentation will prove significant for *derash*.

The passage continues: *v'hesir et murato b'notzatah v'hishlich otah etzel ha-mizbeach kedmah el mekom ha-deshen* — he removes the crop and throws it near the altar to the place of ashes. *V'shisa oto bichnafav lo yavdil* — he tears it open through its wings without fully separating it. *V'hiktir oto ha-kohein ha-mizbeicha al ha-eitzim asher al ha-eish* — and the *kohein* burns it on the fire on the altar. This is the second instance of *v'hiktir* in the passage, a duplication the *gemara* will exploit.

One further observation: the *olah* passage uses *v'nimtza* — the blood is squeezed out — and does not use the language of *v'hizah* (sprinkling). This stands in contrast to the *chatas* passage.

### Chatas Pesukim

The *pesukim* for the bird *chatas* appear at the end of *Parshas Vayikra* (5:8–9), in the section dealing with *korban oleh v'yored*. The passage describes a person bringing two birds — one as a *chatas* and one as an *olah*. For the *chatas*, it states: *v'hikriv et asher la-chatas rishona u-malak et rosho mimul orpo v'lo yavdil* — he shall bring forward first the one that is the *chatas*, perform *melikah* from opposite the nape of its neck, and not fully separate it. *V'hizah midam ha-chatas al kir ha-mizbeach* — he shall sprinkle (*hizah*) from the blood of the *chatas* on the wall of the altar. *V'ha-nishar ba-dam yimatzei el yesod ha-mizbeach* — and the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out (*yimatzei*) at the base of the altar.

The key differences between these two passages thus emerge: the *chatas* specifies *mimul orpo* (from the nape), *v'lo yavdil* (without fully separating), *v'hizah* (sprinkling), and *yimatzei el yesod* (squeezing at the base). The *olah* specifies squeezing (*nimtza*) — no sprinkling — and its blood goes to the wall of the altar.

### Verb Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב מִן הַתֹּרִים אוֹ מִן בְּנֵי הַיּוֹנָה״ – יָכוֹל הַמִּתְנַדֵּב עוֹף לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִשְּׁנֵי פְּרִידִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיבוֹ״ – אֲפִילּוּ פְּרִידָה אַחַת יָבִיא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Since it is stated** in the preceding verse: "And if his offering to the Lord be a burnt offering of birds, **then he shall bring** his offering **of doves, or of pigeons"** (Leviticus 1:14), one **might** have thought that **one who donates a bird** as an offering **may not** donate **fewer than two birds.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And** the priest **shall bring it,"** indicating that **one may bring even one bird to** be sacrificed on **the altar.**

Returning to the *olah* passage, the *gemara* examines the verbs with care. The sequence *u-malak* — *v'hiktir* — *v'nimtza* presents the burning before the squeezing of blood, which cannot reflect the actual order of operations. As the *gemara* will explain, this inversion is intentional and carries *halachic* weight: it serves to link the squeezing of blood (*nimtza*) to the burning (*hiktir*) and thereby derive that the squeezing must take place at the same location as the burning.

The *olah* passage also contains *v'hiktir* twice — once immediately after *melikah* and once at the end of the passage after the crop is removed and the body is torn open. This duplication, as the *gemara* will develop, teaches that the head and body of the *olah* are offered separately, and correspondingly that the *melikah* itself must completely separate the head from the body.

## Kohein & Melikah

### Kohein Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַכֹּהֵן״? לִקְבּוֹעַ לוֹ כֹּהֵן. שֶׁיָּכוֹל וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה בֶּן צֹאן, שֶׁקָּבַע לוֹ צָפוֹן – לָא קָבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן; עוֹף, שֶׁלָּא קָבַע לוֹ צָפוֹן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִקְבַּע לוֹ כֹּהֵן?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״(אֶל) הַכֹּהֵן״ – לִקְבּוֹעַ לוֹ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues analyzing the verse: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** that **"the priest** shall bring it to the altar" (Leviticus 1:15)? It is **to establish** that only **a priest** may pinch its nape, **as** one **might** have thought that even a non-priest may perform this procedure. **Could this not** be derived through **logical inference: And if** with regard to **a sheep** burnt offering, with regard to **which** the verse **established** that **it** must be slaughtered in the **north** (see Leviticus 1:11), it did **not establish** that **it** must be slaughtered by a **priest** (see 32a), then in the case of **a bird** burnt offering, with regard to **which** the verse **did not establish** that its nape must be pinched in the **north, is it not logical that** the verse does **not establish** that its nape must be pinched by **a priest?** To counter this, **the verse states** that **"the priest** shall bring it to the altar," in order **to establish** that its nape must be pinched specifically by **a priest.**

The *gemara* now proceeds to a *baraita* that methodically derives the specific laws of *melikah* from the *pesukim*. The *baraita* begins: *v'hikrivo* — why does the verse need to say "he shall bring it"? The *baraita* first addresses the word *min ha-torim o min bnei ha-yonah*, which is in the plural. One might have thought that just as obligatory bird sacrifices typically involve two birds (one *chatas* and one *olah*), even a voluntary bird *olah* must consist of two birds. Therefore the verse says *v'hikrivo* in the singular — *afilu perida achat yavi*, even a single bird suffices.

The *baraita* then asks: why does the verse say *v'hikrivo ha-kohein el ha-mizbeach* — why is *ha-kohein* mentioned? After all, *shechita* of animal sacrifices does not require a *kohein*; a *zar* (non-*kohein*) may perform *shechita*. One might reason by *kal vachomer*: if a sheep, which is restricted to the north of the altar, does not require a *kohein* for slaughter, then a bird, which has no such geographic restriction, should certainly not require a *kohein*. Therefore, the verse must state *ha-kohein* explicitly — *likboa lo kohein*, to establish that *melikah* requires a *kohein*.

### No Knife Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִמְלְקֶנּוּ בְּסַכִּין? וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה אִם שְׁחִיטָה, שֶׁלֹּא קָבַע לָהּ כֹּהֵן – קָבַע לָהּ כְּלִי; מְלִיקָה, שֶׁקָּבַע לָהּ כֹּהֵן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּקְבַּע לָהּ כְּלִי?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֹּהֵן... וּמָלַק״. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁזָּר קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ?! אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֹּהֵן״? שֶׁתְּהֵא מְלִיקָה בְּעַצְמוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that the priest should **pinch its** nape **with a knife. And** one could derive this through **a logical inference: And if** with regard to **slaughtering,** with regard to **which** the verse **did not establish** that **it** must be performed by **a priest,** it **established** that **it** must be performed with **an instrument,** i.e., a knife (see 97b); then in the case of **pinching, which** the verse **established** must be performed by **a priest, is it not logical that** the verse **establishes** that **it** must be performed with **an instrument?** To counter this, **the verse states:** "And the **priest** shall bring it near the altar **and pinch off** its head." In explanation of this verse, **Rabbi Akiva said: Could it enter your mind that a non-priest may approach** the **altar** in order to sacrifice an offering? Since this is impossible, the verse does not need to state that the sacrificial rite is performed by a priest. **Rather, what** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "The **priest"?** It means **that the pinching must be** performed **with the very** body of the **priest.**

The *baraita* proceeds to a second *kal vachomer*: *melikah* requires a *kohein* but no knife; *shechita* requires a knife but no *kohein*. One might therefore reason that since *shechita*, which does not require a *kohein*, nevertheless requires a knife — surely *melikah*, which requires a *kohein*, should also require a knife. The *gemara* answers from *talmud lomar kohein u-malak*: the *kohein* performs the *melikah* — implying with his own body, specifically with his thumbnail — and no knife is required.

Rabbi Akiva refines the *derash*. He asks: is it not already obvious that a *zar* cannot perform *melikah*? After all, *melikah* is performed directly at the *mizbeach* itself, in the immediate context of offering the blood — how could one imagine a non-*kohein* approaching the altar to perform such an act? Rabbi Akiva argues that the self-evident prohibition on a *zar* approaching the altar in the context of an *avodah* is understood without the verse needing to state it. The real teaching of *ha-kohein* must therefore be something else: *she-tehei melikah b'atzmo shel kohein* — that the *melikah* must be performed with the *kohein*'s own body, i.e., with his thumbnail, and not with any instrument.

### Why Kohein Matters

This raises a broader question: why is it that *shechita* of animal sacrifices does not require a *kohein* and is performed with a knife, while *melikah* of bird sacrifices requires a *kohein* and is performed with the finger? A conceptual explanation suggests itself. When a *kohein* is not required for *shechita*, the sanctifying element of the act is the instrument — the knife, which is a *kli shareit*, a sanctified temple vessel. The knife serves as the sacred object that effects the transformation of the animal's status. When, however, the act is performed with the finger — with no knife involved — there is no *kli shareit* present. The *kohein*'s own person substitutes: the finger of the *kohein* is, as it were, the sanctified instrument. This also connects to Rabbi Akiva's earlier point: the *melikah* occurs in the very context of the altar and the offering of the blood simultaneously, whereas by animal *shechita* the blood is caught, transported, and only then placed on the altar. That remove from the immediate altar context is what permits a non-*kohein* to perform *shechita*; the intimacy of *melikah* with the altar demands the *kohein* himself.

### Top of Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִמְלְקֶנָּה בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמָלַק... וְהִקְטִיר״ – מָה הַקְטָרָה בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אַף מְלִיקָה בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to analyze the verse: One **might** have thought that the priest **may pinch** the bird's nape **whether above** the red line of the altar **or below** it. To counter this, **the verse states:** "And the priest shall bring it unto the altar **and pinch off** its head, **and make it smoke** on the altar." The verse juxtaposes the pinching to the burning of the bird on the altar to teach that **just as** the **burning** occurs **atop the altar, so too,** the **pinching** occurs **on** the **top** part of the wall of **the altar.**

The *baraita* continues: perhaps *melikah* — and specifically the squeezing of the blood — may be performed either at the top half or the bottom half of the *mizbeach*. The verse teaches otherwise: *u-malak v'hiktir* — just as *haktara* (burning) takes place at the top of the altar where the fire is, so too *melikah* takes place at the top of the altar. The *chatas*, correspondingly, is at the bottom — the reverse of the arrangement for mammal sacrifices.

### Head Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמָלַק״ – מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִן הַצַּוָּאר? וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״וּמָלַק״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וּמָלַק״; מָה לְהַלָּן מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף, אַף כָּאן מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The verse states that the priest shall **"pinch off** its head," which must be performed **at the nape. Do you say** that the pinching occurs **at the nape, or** is it **only at the throat? It** can be derived through **a logical inference:** It **is stated here,** with regard to a bird burnt offering: **"And pinch off** its head" (Leviticus 1:15), **and** it **is stated there,** with regard to a bird sin offering: **"And pinch off** its head opposite its nape, but shall not separate it" (Leviticus 5:8). **Just as there,** the head is pinched **at the nape, so too here,** the head is pinched **at the nape.**

The *baraita* then considers where on the bird's neck the *melikah* is performed. The verse says *mimul orpo* — from opposite the nape of the neck, i.e., the back of the neck rather than the throat. This is explicit in the *chatas* passage; the *baraita* links the *olah* passage to it by *gezeirah shavah* on the word *u-malak*.

However, the *chatas* passage also says *v'lo yavdil* — one does not fully sever the head. Should we derive from the *gezeirah shavah* that the *olah* also should not fully sever? No — here the other *derash* intervenes:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן מוֹלֵק וְאֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל, אַף כָּאן מוֹלֵק וְאֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמָלַק וְהִקְטִיר״; מָה הַקְטָרָה – הָרֹאשׁ לְעַצְמוֹ וְהַגּוּף לְעַצְמוֹ, אַף מְלִיקָה – הָרֹאשׁ לְעַצְמוֹ וְהַגּוּף לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the *halakha* of a bird burnt offering is derived from that of a bird sin offering, perhaps it should also be derived that **just as there,** the priest **pinches** off the head **but does not separate** it completely from the body, **so too here,** with regard to a bird burnt offering, he **pinches** off the head **but does not separate** it from the body. To counter this, **the verse states** with regard to a bird burnt offering: **"And pinch off** its head, **and make it smoke** on the altar" (Leviticus 1:15). This indicates that **just as** with regard to the **burning** of the bird burnt offering, **the head** is burned **by itself and the body** is burned **by itself, so too** with regard to the **pinching, the head** is **by itself and the body** is **by itself,** i.e., the head is completely detached from the body.

*u-malak v'hiktir*, and since *haktara* involves head and body separately (*rosh l'atzmo v'guf l'atzmo*), so too *melikah* must fully separate head from body. How do we know *haktara* is separate? Because the verse says *v'hiktir* twice — once immediately after *melikah* and once at the end of the passage after the body is prepared. The first *v'hiktir* refers to burning of the head; the second *v'hiktir* refers to burning of the body. Therefore the two are offered separately, and correspondingly the *melikah* must achieve that separation. By the *chatas*, the *lo yavdil* means one need not — though need not is not the same as may not; we will return to that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁהַקְטָרַת הָרֹאשׁ בְּעַצְמוֹ וְהַגּוּף בְּעַצְמוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִקְטִיר אוֹתוֹ״ – הֲרֵי הַקְטָרַת הַגּוּף אֲמוּרָה; הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְהִקְטִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחָה״? בְּהַקְטָרַת הָרֹאשׁ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And from where** is it derived **that the burning of the head** is **by itself and the body** is burned **by itself? As it is stated:** "And he shall rend it by its wings…**and** the priest **shall make it smoke** upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:17). Since **the burning of the body is stated** in this verse, **how do I realize** the meaning of the earlier phrase: **"And make it smoke on the altar"** (Leviticus 1:15)? One must conclude that **the verse is speaking about the burning of the head.**

## Blood Location

### Top Half Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִמְצָה דָּמוֹ״ – כּוּלּוֹ. ״עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – וְלֹא עַל קִיר הַכֶּבֶשׁ, וְלֹא עַל קִיר הַהֵיכָל; וְאֵיזֶה זֶה? זֶה קִיר הָעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to expound the verse stated with regard to a bird burnt offering: "And its blood shall be drained out on the wall of the altar" (Leviticus 1:15). When the verse states: **"And its blood shall be drained out,"** it means that **all of it** must be drained out. The continuation of the verse indicates that it must be drained out **"on the wall of the altar," and not on the wall of the ramp, nor on the wall of the Sanctuary. And which** wall of the altar **is this? This is the upper wall,** i.e., above the red line.

Having established that both the *melikah* and the squeezing of blood must occur at the top half of the altar, the *baraita* explains how this is derived for the blood-squeezing specifically. The verse states *u-malak v'hiktir v'nimtza damo al kir ha-mizbeach* — but this sequence is impossible as a description of the actual procedure, for one cannot squeeze out blood after the bird has already been burned. The only purpose of placing *v'nimtza* after *v'hiktir* is therefore to teach that the squeezing of the blood must occur at the same place as the *haktara*: just as *haktara* takes place at the top of the altar (*b'rosh ha-mizbeach*), so too *mitzui* (squeezing) takes place at the top of the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא קִיר הַתַּחְתּוֹן? וְדִין הוּא: מָה בְּהֵמָה, שֶׁחַטָּאתָהּ לְמַעְלָה – עוֹלָתָהּ לְמַטָּה; עוֹף, שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ לְמַטָּה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁעוֹלָתוֹ לְמַטָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps the verse is referring **only** to **the lower wall,** i.e., below the red line; **and this** can be supported by **a logical inference:** And **if** with regard to **an animal** offering, **where** the blood of **its sin offering** is applied **above** the red line, the blood of **its burnt offering** is applied **below** the red line, then with regard to **a bird** offering, **where** the blood of **its sin offering** is sprinkled **below** the red line, **is it not logical that** the blood of **its burnt offering** is sprinkled **below** the red line?

One might have thought otherwise: by mammal sacrifices, the *chatas* goes above and the *olah* below; by bird sacrifices, the *chatas* goes below — perhaps the *olah* should also go below? The *kal vachomer* would suggest this. But the verse, by juxtaposing *haktara* and *nimtza*, refutes it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמָלַק... וְהִקְטִיר... וְנִמְצָה דָּמוֹ״ – וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ לְאַחַר שֶׁהִקְטִיר חוֹזֵר וּמְמַצֶּה?! אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה הַקְטָרָה בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אַף מִיצּוּי בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. הָא כֵּיצַד? הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וּפוֹנֶה לַסּוֹבֵב, וּבָא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית; הָיָה מוֹלֵק אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ מִמּוּל עׇרְפָּה וּמַבְדִּיל, וּמְמַצֶּה מִדָּמָהּ עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. אִם עֲשָׂאָהּ לְמַטָּה מֵרַגְלָיו, אֲפִילּוּ אַמָּה – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this, **the verse states: "And pinch off** its head, **and make it smoke** on the altar; **and its blood shall be drained out." Could it enter your mind** that **after he has burned** the bird, the priest **goes back and drains out** its blood? **Rather,** the verse serves **to tell you: Just as** the **burning** occurs **atop the altar, so too,** the **draining** occurs **atop the altar. How so?** The priest **would ascend the ramp and turn to the surrounding ledge and arrive at** the **southeast corner. He would pinch off the** bird's **head at its nape, and separate** it from its body. **He would** then **squeeze out its blood on the wall of the altar** beside him. Nevertheless, **if** the priest **performed** the squeezing **below his feet,** i.e., below the surrounding ledge, **even** one **cubit** beneath the ledge, it is **valid.**

The *baraita* then describes the actual procedure: the *kohein* ascends the ramp (*kevesh*), turns to the *soveiv* (the walkway that encircles the altar at its midpoint), and arrives at the southeast corner. There he performs the *melikah* and squeezes out the blood against the wall of the altar.

The verse specifies *al kir ha-mizbeach* — on the wall of the altar. This means all the blood, and specifically the wall of the altar itself, not the wall of the *kevesh* (ramp) and not the wall of the *heichal*.

An allowance is noted: the *kohein* may squeeze out the blood up to one *amah* below his feet and it remains valid (*u-mamtzeh l'matta me-raglav afilu amah kesheira*). This is because the *soveiv* is at the six-*amah* mark of the altar's height, and the midpoint of the altar — the boundary between its upper and lower halves — is one *amah* below the *soveiv*. The *kohein* standing on the *soveiv* can squeeze the blood downward as far as that midpoint and still be within the upper half.

### Location Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל עַצְמָהּ אֵין נַעֲשֵׂית אֶלָּא בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes by citing a dissenting opinion: **Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov say: The entire** rite of a bird burnt offering **is performed only atop the altar.** The Gemara asks: Since the first *tanna* and Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov agree that the bird must be burned atop the altar and its blood must be squeezed on the top part of the wall of the altar, **what is** the difference **between them?**

Rabbi Nechemia and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagree: they hold that the entire act must be performed at the very top of the altar (*b'rosho shel mizbeach*), with no allowance for squeezing blood below the *kohein*'s feet.

### Fire & Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲרָכָה עַל גַּבֵּי סוֹבֵב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye and Rava both say: There is** a difference **between them** with regard to whether one may **form an arrangement** of wood **on** the **surrounding ledge** and burn the bird there. According to the first *tanna*, this is permitted when needed, and therefore it is valid if the priest squeezes out the blood on the wall up to a cubit below the surrounding ledge. According to Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, one may never burn the bird on the surrounding ledge, and therefore it is not valid if the priest squeezed out the blood below the surrounding ledge.

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what is the underlying point of this debate? Abaye and Rava both answer: the dispute hinges on whether one may place a fire (*maaracha*) on the *soveiv* itself. The reason the squeezing must relate to the burning — as derived from the juxtaposition of *v'hiktir* and *v'nimtza* — means that the squeezing must be in proximity to where the burning occurs. If a fire may be placed on the *soveiv*, then squeezing the blood below one's feet and placing it directly onto that fire is considered sufficiently proximate. If no fire may be placed on the *soveiv*, then the only genuine proximity to the fire is above the *soveiv*, and squeezing below the feet is too distant. This is not merely a question of which half of the altar one is in; it is a question of the functional juxtaposition between the squeezing of the blood and the burning.

## Crop Removal

### Crop Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהֵסִיר אֶת מֻרְאָתוֹ בְּנֹצָתָהּ״ – זוֹ זֶפֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse pertaining to a bird burnt offering: **"And he shall take away *murato* with its feathers"** (Leviticus 1:16). **This** word, *murato*, is referring to the **crop.**

The *gemara* turns to the removal of the crop (*murah* / *zefek*). The verse says *v'hesir et murato b'notzatah* — he removes the crop *b'notzatah*. One possibility is that one cuts around the crop with a knife and removes only the crop itself; the word *b'notzatah* would then be read as meaning "with its feathers."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יַקְדִּיר בְּסַכִּין וְיִטְּלֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּנֹצָתָהּ״ – נוֹטֵל אֶת הַנּוֹצָה עִמָּהּ. אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן חָנָן אוֹמֵר: נוֹטְלָהּ וְנוֹטֵל קוּרְקְבָנָהּ עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Had the verse written only "its crop," one **might** have thought that the priest may **cut** the skin **with a knife and take** the crop without any other part of the bird. Therefore, **the verse states: "With its feathers [*benotzatah*],"** which teaches that **he takes the feathers with it,** i.e., he must remove the skin with the feathers still attached. The Gemara presents another opinion: **Abba Yosei ben Ḥanan says: He takes** the crop **and he** also **takes its gizzard with it.**

Abba Yosi ben Chanan holds that one takes it together with the *korkevan* (intestine).

### Surgical Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״בְּנֹצָתָהּ״ – בְּנוֹצָה שֶׁלָּהּ; קוֹדְרָהּ בְּסַכִּין כְּמִין אֲרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **from the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The word ***benotzatah*** is a contraction of the words ***benotza shelah,** meaning its feathers. This teaches that the priest **cuts it with a knife,** creating an opening **like a window** opposite the crop. This allows him to remove the crop with only the feathers and skin directly opposite it, and that is what he brings to the place of the ashes.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael reads *b'notzatah* differently: one uses a knife to cut surgically around the spot on the outside of the bird's body directly opposite the crop, forming a circular incision (*k'min arubah*), so that when the crop is removed it comes out with only the skin and feathers directly adjacent to it — without taking excess body tissue.

### Tearing Open

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיסַּע וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשִׁסַּע״ – אֵין שִׁיסַּע אֶלָּא בַּיָּד, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְשַׁסְּעֵהוּ כְּשַׁסַּע הַגְּדִי״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that after removing the crop and the feathers and innards that emerged with it, the priest **ripped** the bird lengthwise **and did not separate** the two halves of the bird. In this regard **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he shall rend"** (Leviticus 1:17). The act of **rending is** performed **only by hand, and so too,** the verse **states** with regard to Samson: **"And he rent it as one would have rent a kid,** and he had nothing in his hand" (Judges 14:6).

The verse also says *v'shisa oto bichnafav lo yavdil* — he tears it open through its wings, not fully severing it. The *baraita* derives from the word *shisa* that this must be done by hand: *ein shisa ela b'yad*. A proof is brought from the verse in the context of Shimshon, *va-yishaseihu k'shesa ha-gedi* — "he tore it as one tears a young goat" — a tearing done with the hands. The *lo yavdil* here refers to not fully severing the body when tearing it open, which is distinct from the *lo yavdil* earlier in the *chatas* passage that refers to not fully severing the head.

### Post-Blood Acts

A further principle is established: if the *kohein* failed to remove the crop or to carry out these post-*melikah* procedures, the sacrifice remains *kasher*. The critical acts are those involving the blood; procedures performed after the blood stage do not invalidate the sacrifice if omitted.

## Both Simanim

### Mishnah Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֵסִיר אֶת הַמּוּרְאָה כּוּ׳. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁמַּבְדִּילִין בְּחַטַּאת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the priest **did not remove the crop,** or he changed any detail of the sacrificial rite after he squeezed out the blood, the offering is valid. If he separated the head from the body of a bird sin offering, it is disqualified. The Gemara says: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, said: I heard that one may separate** the head from the body **of a bird sin offering.**

The *gemara* now notes that the *mishnah* is not in accordance with the position of Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon. The *mishnah* states — in the context of its earlier discussion — that if the *kohein* performed *melikah* on a bird *chatas* and severed both *simanim*, the *chatas* is invalid.

A *baraita* records Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon's dissenting view: *shamati she-mavdilin b'chatat ha-of* — "I have heard that one may fully sever [both *simanim*] in the case of a bird *chatas*," and it remains valid.

### R.E. Valid

### Squeezing Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב; וְכֵיוָן דִּמִצּוּי דָּם, קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֵׂה עוֹלָה בְּחַטַּאת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for the difference **between them,** i.e., what is the basis of the disagreement? **Rav Ḥisda says:** The difference **between them** concerns the question of whether **squeezing out** the blood of **a bird sin offering** after its blood has been sprinkled is **indispensable. The first *tanna*,** whose opinion is cited in the mishna, **holds** that **squeezing out** the blood of **a bird sin offering** is **indispensable. And since squeezing out** the **blood** is indispensable, the priest will have to squeeze out the blood after separating the head from the body, and he will have **performed the rite of** a bird **burnt offering on a bird sin offering,** which disqualifies the offering (see 66a).

Rav Chisda explains the underlying point of the debate. The *tanna kamma* holds that *mitzui chatat ha-of me'akev* — squeezing out the blood of a bird *chatas* is an indispensable requirement. Given this, if the *kohein* severs both *simanim* and then squeezes out the blood, he has performed both the distinctive acts of the *olah* procedure: fully severing the head and squeezing the blood.

### Like Olah

The operative assumption is that what renders the *chatas* invalid in this case is not the violation of the verse *v'lo yavdil* per se, but rather the fact that the *kohein* has treated the *chatas* exactly as he would treat an *olah* — *ma'aseh olah b'chatat ha-of*. This is what invalidates it. One might ask: by animal sacrifices, the same act of *shechita* is performed for both *chatas* and *asham*, yet this does not invalidate — why should it be different here? The *gemara* accepts the premise that here it does invalidate, and proceeds to analyze why.

### Flesh Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף לֹא מְעַכֵּב, וּמְחַתֵּךְ בָּשָׂר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds** that **squeezing out** the blood of **a bird sin offering is not indispensable.** Consequently, if the priest would separate the bird's head from its body, he would refrain from squeezing out the blood, in which case the rite is dissimilar to that of a bird burnt offering. **And** the separation of the head **is** considered as though the priest was **merely cutting flesh,** and the offering is valid.

Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon holds that *mitzui chatat ha-of lo me'akev* — squeezing out the blood is not an indispensable requirement. If so, even if the *kohein* severed both *simanim*, he has not replicated the procedure of the *olah* in a meaningful sense, because the *olah* procedure requires both the severing and the squeezing, and the squeezing here is not obligatory. Under this view, what the *kohein* has done is nothing more than cutting through flesh — *u-me-chattech basar b'alma hu* — which does not render the *chatas* invalid. Rashi notes that in practice, in such a case, the *kohein* should take care not to squeeze out the blood, so as not to replicate the *olah* procedure.

## Siman Status

### One Act vs. Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: שְׁהִיָּיה בְּסִימָן שֵׁנִי בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: שְׁהִיָּיה בְּסִימָן שֵׁנִי בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף – לֹא מְעַכֵּב; וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּשְׁהִיָּיה, קָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֵׂה עוֹלָה בְּחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** there is an alternative explanation of the dispute: It is possible that all agree that squeezing the blood of a bird sin offering is indispensable, and **there is** a difference **between them** with regard to whether **interrupting** the pinching before severing the **second *siman*,** i.e., the gullet or windpipe, one of the organs that must be cut in the ritual slaughter, **of a bird burnt offering disqualifies** the offering. **The first *tanna* holds** that **interrupting** the pinching before severing the **second *siman* of a bird burnt offering does not disqualify** the offering, **and** therefore, if the priest separates the head of a bird sin offering from its body, **even though he interrupted** the pinching before severing the second *siman*, **he has performed the rite of** a bird **burnt offering on** a bird **sin offering,** which disqualifies it.

Rava offers a different framework for understanding the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon. He frames it as a conceptual question about the nature of the requirement to sever both *simanim* by the bird *olah*: is the second *siman* part of one continuous act of *melikah* (comparable to how both *simanim* of a mammal constitute a single act of *shechita*), or is it a separate, distinct requirement that follows after the primary *melikah* act is already complete?

This question has practical implications. In standard *hilchos shechita*, a pause (*shehiya*) in the middle of the *shechita* act invalidates it. If severing the second *siman* of a bird *olah* is part of a single unified act of *melikah*, then a pause between the first and second *siman* would invalidate the *olah*. If, however, the second *siman* is a distinct, subsequent requirement — not part of the *shechita* act itself — then a pause would not invalidate.

### Pause Relevance

This conceptual question is directly relevant to the *chatas*. By the *chatas*, there is a structural pause built into the process: after cutting the first *siman*, the *kohein* must complete cutting through the majority of the flesh of the neck (*rov basar*), and only then could he proceed to the second *siman*. This intervening step necessarily creates a pause between the two *simanim*. The question then becomes: does this pause make the *chatas* procedure look like a valid *olah* or an invalid one?

### Melikah Process

The physical mechanics of *melikah* illuminate why the pause is inherent. The *kohein* comes from the back of the neck with his thumbnail. He first cuts through a minority of the flesh of the neck, then cuts through the *siman*, then finishes cutting through the majority of the flesh of the neck. He cannot cut through most of the flesh first, because doing so before reaching the *siman* would effectively kill the bird before the *melikah* itself. Thus the sequence is: minority of flesh → first *siman* → majority of flesh → (optionally) second *siman*. The majority of flesh necessarily falls between the two *simanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק; חַד אָמַר: שְׁהִיָּיה בְּסִימָן שֵׁנִי בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, וְחַד אָמַר: רוֹב בָּשָׂר מְעַכֵּב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **And** Rava and Abaye disagree **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak. One says** that the difference **between** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, concerns whether **interrupting** the pinching before severing the **second *siman* of a bird burnt offering disqualifies** the offering. **And one says** that the difference **between them** concerns whether cutting the **majority of** the **flesh** of the nape of a bird sin offering is **indispensable.**

### Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּבָעֵינַן דְּרוֹב בָּשָׂר בַּתְּחִילָּה?! אִין; וְהָתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד מוֹלְקִין חַטַּאת הָעוֹף? חוֹתֵךְ שִׁדְרָה וּמַפְרֶקֶת בְּלֹא רוֹב בָּשָׂר, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לַוֶּושֶׁט אוֹ לַקָּנֶה. הִגִּיעַ לַוֶּושֶׁט אוֹ לַקָּנֶה – חוֹתֵךְ סִימָן אֶחָד אוֹ רוּבּוֹ, וְרוֹב בָּשָׂר עִמּוֹ. וּבָעוֹלָה – שְׁנַיִם אוֹ רוֹב שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since there is a dispute concerning whether cutting the majority of the flesh of the nape of a bird sin offering is indispensable, can it be deduced **by inference** that all agree **that we require** the priest to cut **the majority of the flesh *ab initio*?** The Gemara responds: **Yes; and** similarly it **was taught** in *baraita*: **How does one pinch** the nape of **a bird sin offering?** Using his thumbnail, the priest **cuts** the **spine and nape, without** cutting through the **majority of** the **flesh, until he reaches** either **the gullet or the windpipe.** Upon **reaching the gullet or the windpipe, he cuts one *siman*** entirely, **or** at least **the majority of it, and** he cuts **the majority of the flesh with it. And in** the case of a bird **burnt offering,** he cuts the **two** *simanim* **or the majority of the two** *simanim*.

The *tanna kamma* holds that a pause between the two *simanim* does not invalidate the *olah*: *shehiya b'siman sheini b'olas ha-of lo me'akev*. Under this view, the second *siman* is a separate requirement, not part of the *shechita* act, so a pause is of no consequence. Given this, if a *kohein* performing a *chatas* cuts through both *simanim* — even with the intervening pause — he has replicated what is entirely valid by an *olah*. Therefore, the *chatas* is invalid because it has been treated just like an *olah*.

### R.E. Safe

Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon holds the opposite: *shehiya b'siman sheini b'olas ha-of me'akev* — a pause between the two *simanim* would invalidate the *olah*. Under this view, both *simanim* constitute a single unified act of *melikah*, and any interruption breaks that act. By the *chatas*, however, the *kohein* necessarily pauses between the two *simanim* in order to cut the majority of the flesh. At the moment that pause occurs, the *melikah* can no longer be considered a valid *olah*-type act. From that point on, cutting through the second *siman* is nothing more than cutting through flesh (*meichtichas basar b'alma*), and the *chatas* remains valid.

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: רוֹב בָּשָׂר מְעַכֵּב אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says** there is a third explanation of the dispute between the *tanna'im*: It is possible that all agree that interrupting the pinching before severing the second *siman* disqualifies a bird burnt offering, and **there is** a difference **between them** with regard to whether cutting the **majority of** the **flesh** of the nape of a bird sin offering is **indispensable.**

Abaye proposes a variation on this analysis. Rather than framing the debate around whether a pause invalidates the *olah*, Abaye frames it around whether cutting through the majority of the flesh (*rov basar*) is itself a required step in the process. Under Abaye's reading, everyone agrees that a pause would invalidate the *olah*. The debate is whether, when one cuts both *simanim* in a *chatas*, there is necessarily a pause at all. If one is required to cut the majority of the flesh between the two *simanim*, there is an inevitable pause and the *chatas* is safe. If, however, cutting the majority of the flesh is not required and one could proceed directly from the first to the second *siman* without stopping, then it would be possible to replicate the *olah* procedure exactly — and that would invalidate the *chatas*.

### Gemara Synthesis

The *gemara* notes that the debate between Rava and Abaye itself reflects a dispute between two *amora'im* — one of whom says that the core point of disagreement between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon is whether a pause between the two *simanim* invalidates the *olah*, and the other of whom says that all agree a pause would invalidate and the debate is instead about whether *rov basar* is a required intermediate step.

To summarize the final analysis: the reason cutting both *simanim* of a bird *chatas* would be invalid is not primarily a violation of *v'lo yavdil* but rather that doing so makes the *chatas* indistinguishable from an *olah*. The point of contention is how to define "indistinguishable." If an *olah* tolerates a pause between the two *simanim* (and the pause that necessarily occurs in a *chatas* does not differentiate it), the *chatas* is invalid. If an *olah* does not tolerate such a pause (and the *chatas* necessarily has one), then cutting both *simanim* in a *chatas* is not truly replicating the *olah* procedure, and the *chatas* remains valid. Alternatively, if the question is whether one must pause at all — that is, whether *rov basar* is required — then the debate shifts to whether the *chatas* can in principle be performed without any differentiating pause.
