# Nedarim 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wellspring Dispute

### Two Towns Setup

The daf picks up at the bottom of 80b, continuing the larger discussion of what constitutes a *neder inuy nefesh* — a vow of self-affliction that a husband is entitled to annul — alongside the category of *beino l'veina*, matters that pertain specifically to the dynamic between husband and wife. The Gemara introduces a *baraita* (found in the Tosefta to Bava Metzia) with the formula *v'rammi d'Rabbi Yosi ad d'Rabbi Yosi* — a contradiction is raised between two rulings of Rabbi Yosi.

The *baraita* presents the case of a *ma'ayan shebnei ha'ir*: a natural wellspring that originates in one town and flows toward another. The question is how to adjudicate competing water needs between the two towns across a hierarchy of uses.

### Life & Animals

The hierarchy proceeds as follows. First: *chayeihem v'chayei acheirim* — the lives of the townspeople who possess the wellspring take precedence over the lives of the people in the other town. This is entirely unsurprising; it reflects the foundational halachic principle of *v'chai achicha imach* — *chayecha kodmin l'chayei achicha*, your own life takes priority over that of another. Second: *behemtam u'vehemat acheirim* — their animals take precedence over the other town's animals. Third: *kvisatan u'kvisat acheirim* — their laundry takes precedence over the other town's laundry. All three of these rulings follow naturally from the principle of self-priority.

### Laundry Priority

The surprising ruling comes next. The *baraita* poses the case of *chayei acheirim u'kvisatan*: what if the other town's people need the water for basic sustenance — their lives — while the town with the wellspring needs it only for laundry? The anonymous first voice of the *baraita* rules *chayei acheirim kodmin l'kvisatan*: the other town's lives take precedence over laundry, which seems obvious. But then: *Rabbi Yosi omer, kvisatan kodemes l'chayei acheirim* — Rabbi Yosi rules that their laundry precedes the other town's lives.

This is, on its face, a startling ruling. Someone in the next town needs water to survive, and Rabbi Yosi permits the first town to keep the water for laundry?

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot offers an important qualification. He reads *kodemet* not as a categorical denial of water to the other town, but as a question of temporal priority. The wellspring flows naturally from town A to town B. Town A is permitted to first pause or dam the water in order to complete their laundry, after which the water continues downstream to town B. On this reading, nobody in the second town ultimately goes without — they simply have to wait. The *chiddush* is only about the order of access, not about a permanent withholding, and Tosafot presumably frames it this way because the assumption is that no one will actually die from the delay.

### Ethics & Charity

The Ran, by contrast, appears to favor a more literal reading, which creates genuine moral tension. One might try to defend the position with reference to the principle that even a limb of one's own body takes precedence over another person's life — the *psak halacha* being that one is not obligated to risk a limb to definitively save another's life, even though objectively a life outweighs a limb. If even one's own limb takes precedence, perhaps something central to one's bodily dignity and well-being — clothing one wears, the state of one's physical environment — might similarly precede a more distant person's claim on one's resources. This is admittedly harsh, and the lecturer does not endorse a literal reading. But it invites comparison to Peter Singer's well-known critique: every day people spend significant sums on dinners, consumer goods, and personal luxuries while lives elsewhere could be saved with those same funds. The distance and abstraction of the need makes it feel different — but morally, is it? Here, the context is less abstract: these are fellow Jews in a neighboring town. The tradition of *pidyon shvuyim* illustrates that when Jews are in acute need nearby, significant personal sacrifice is expected. A mitigating interpretation of *chayei acheirim* is also possible — perhaps it refers not to imminent death but to a lower level of well-being, a harder life rather than a life-threatening emergency.

### Tzedakah Priority

This ruling opens onto a broader question about the competing metrics within *tzedakah* obligations. Halacha articulates two distinct hierarchies: one based on proximity (*aniucha ve'aniyei ircha, aniucha kodmin* — your own poor take precedence over those of another city) and one based on degree of need (*ani ve'ashir, ani kodem* — the poor take precedence over the wealthy even in loan contexts). What happens when proximity and need pull in opposite directions? The poor of one's own city need education, while the poor of a neighboring city need food — to whom does one's primary obligation run?

The Chasam Sofer holds that need always governs first: one must ensure that basic needs everywhere are met before ascending to higher-level needs, beginning with those closest. The Aruch Hashulchan, by contrast, emphasizes that primary obligation always tracks proximity. Something of this tension plays out in the *baraita*: even granting that we should not imagine people literally dying, one can still ask whether one town's laundry precedes another town's general welfare. These are genuinely unsettled questions in the application of *tzedakah* priorities, and the Gemara surfaces them here without fully resolving them.

## Medical Dangers

### Shmuel's Health

Having established that Rabbi Yosi elevates laundry to a remarkable degree of importance, the Gemara now presents a medical teaching from Shmuel that contextualizes this. *Arvuvita dereisha* — the tangling and filth of the hair from not washing — *mayti lidei avira*, leads to blindness. *Arvuvita demani* — the filth of one's garments from not doing laundry — *mayti lidei sha'amimuta*, leads to a kind of mental disturbance or psychological deterioration. *Arvuvita degufa* — bodily filth from not bathing — *mayti lidei shichney vekivi*, leads to sores and blisters.

### Contradiction Resolved

This teaching sharpens the original contradiction. If not bathing causes sores and blisters, how can Rabbi Yosi say bathing is not *inuy nefesh*? And if not laundering causes psychological deterioration — seemingly worse — why does Rabbi Yosi use laundry to such great rhetorical effect while dismissing bathing? The Gemara's resolution is that Rabbi Yosi does not doubt Shmuel's medical claims. Rather, his position is that not laundering is genuinely weighty — enough that one may prioritize laundry over other competing claims — yet that still does not automatically make the *deprivation* of laundering the kind of *inuy nefesh* that rises to the level activating a husband's annulment power. The distinction is between what is medically serious and what crosses the formal halachic threshold of *nidrei inuy nefesh*.

## Three Warnings

### Hygiene Warning

The Gemara records that *shalchu mitam* — they sent from Eretz Yisrael — three warnings. The first: *hizahru b'arvuvsa* — be careful regarding filth, the neglect of bathing and laundering. Given Shmuel's medical teachings, the community has a responsibility to ensure that even those without means have access to the basic hygiene that protects health.

### Chavruta Warning

The second warning: *hizahru b'chavura* — be attentive to community, which the *rishonim* explain as a reference to learning in *chavruta*, in partnership. Torah study is not meant to be a solitary enterprise; the maintenance of a learning community is presented as a communal priority on a par with the first warning.

### Poor Children

The third and most extensively discussed warning: *hizahru bivnei aniyim* — take special care of the children of the poor, for *shemehem taitzei Torah* — from them Torah will ultimately go out. The verse cited is *yizal mayim midalyav* — water will flow from his lowly ones — where *dal* (lowly) is read as the source of *mayim* (water), and water is Torah.

### Torah Transmission

This leads the Gemara to pose a question: *umipnei mah einam metzuyin talmidei chachamim l'taitzei talmidei chachamim mibeineihen?* Why do we not consistently see the children of *talmidei chachamim* themselves becoming great *talmidei chachamim*? Logic would seem to favor it — Torah is spoken in the home, learning is prioritized, the culture is saturated with it. And indeed, one can point to dynasties like that of Rashi — Rashbam, Rabbeinu Tam, the Ri — where Torah did pass with remarkable depth across generations. Yet the Gemara treats the absence of such dynasties as the norm requiring explanation.

### Three Explanations

Three *amoraim* offer explanations. Rav Yosef says: *shelo yomru Torah yerusha hi lahem* — so that no family should come to feel that Torah is their *yerusha*, their inheritance. A *yerusha* is something received without effort; it arrives by default. The children of *talmidei chachamim*, if Torah passed automatically from father to son, would have no incentive to labor for it. The *bnei aniyim*, by contrast, receive nothing for free and must work for every opportunity. Their Torah therefore comes from genuine effort and urgent appreciation.

Rav Sheshet bar Rav Idi adds: *k'dei shelo yitgadru al hatzibur* — so that they should not become haughty over the community. If one family maintained a sustained monopoly on Torah learning across generations, the inevitable result would be a kind of aristocratic elevation — the family of Torah, set above and apart, a protected hierarchy. God arranges matters so this does not solidify.

Mar Zutra frames the same point as consequence rather than prevention: *mishum shehen mitgabrim al hatzibur* — it is because such families, when Torah does concentrate in them, do become overbearing toward the community. The loss of the dynasty is recompense for that pattern.

Rav Ashi offers a final reason: *mishum d'karu l'inshei chamrei* — because they call other people donkeys. When a family comes to regard itself as the sole bearers of Torah, they come to view laypeople as inherently inferior — *am ha'aretz hadomeh k'veheimah* — and that contempt, when rooted in a sense of innate superiority rather than earned distinction, generates its own punishment.

### Torah Universality

There is a deeper point underlying all three explanations. The *Beit HaMikdash* was hierarchical by design: physical proximity to it mattered, access was stratified, and *kehunah* was exclusively hereditary — a genuine *yerusha* for the *bnei Aharon*. Torah, by contrast, is the one of the *shlosha k'tarim* of which the Gemara says *kol harotzeh yavo v'yitol* — whoever wishes may come and take it. Chazal created the institution of universal education. The warning to care for *bnei aniyim* is in part a reminder that the universality of Torah is not self-maintaining; it requires active communal protection. Even something that should be as radically open and non-hierarchical as Torah can drift toward becoming a protected, centralized institution if the community is not vigilant.

## Blessing Over Torah

### Verse & Blessing

The Gemara now cites the verse: *mi ha'ish ha'chacham v'yaven et zot — al mah avdah ha'aretz* — who is the wise person who will understand this: why was the land destroyed? The Gemara records that the sages could not answer, the prophets could not answer, until God himself answered: *al azvam et Torati asher nasati lifneihem velo sham'u bekoli velo halchu bah* — because they abandoned My Torah and did not listen to My voice and did not walk in it.

### Voice & Blessing

The apparent redundancy of *velo sham'u bekoli* and *velo halchu bah* — not listening to God's voice and not walking in the Torah seem to describe the same failure — occasions the teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *she'ein mevarchin baTorah techila* — they did not make the *bracha* over Torah first. "Not listening to My voice" refers to the failure to make the blessing before learning.

### Torah Business

What is the connection between not making a *bracha* and the problem of Torah becoming a family inheritance? A *bracha* is not merely a formality. *Asher kideshanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu* — it is an act of recognizing that one learns because God commanded it, that Torah is a gift received from outside oneself, a *mitzvah* being performed in response to divine demand. The danger for a family in which Torah has become the family business across generations is that this sense evaporates. Learning becomes what one's father did, what one's grandfather did, the professional substrate of one's life. When Torah is simply the family trade, one does not make a *bracha* over it — not because one is impious, but because it no longer registers as a treasure being encountered. One is not *listening to God's voice*; one is simply doing what one does. This loss of orientation — the failure to experience Torah as a commanded gift rather than a familial possession — is, according to Ravina, the deeper cause behind both the abandonment of Torah and the failure of dynasties to sustain genuine Torah scholarship.

## Vardimus Question

### Vardimus Confused

Isi bar Yehuda stopped attending Rabbi Yosi's *beit midrash* for three days. When Vardimus bar Rabbi Yosi — Rabbi Yosi's son, bearing a name apparently of Greek derivation — encountered him and asked why he had not come, Isi bar Yehuda replied: *ki ta'ameh de'avuch lo yad'ana heichi asina* — if I cannot understand the reasoning of your father, how can I keep attending? Vardimus asked him to state what he did not understand, saying perhaps he could explain it. Isi bar Yehuda cited the ruling: *Rabbi Yosi omer kvisatan kodemet lechayei acheirim* — that one's laundry precedes the other town's lives. This, he said, makes no sense, and he could not continue attending a *beit midrash* where he heard such things without comprehension.

### Levite Suburbs

Vardimus then offers a textual derivation. The verse states: *umigrasheihem yihyu livhemtam ulechol chayasam* — the surrounding fields of the Levitical cities shall be for their animals and for all of their *chayasam*. The question is: what does *chayasam* mean? If it means wild animals — deer and the like — that is already included in *behemtam*. The verse would be redundant.

### Chayasam Meaning

Therefore, *chayasam* must refer to something that sustains life — *chayusa mamash*. But if it means literal life-sustaining activities, that too seems obvious and need not be stated. The derivation therefore pushes further: it must refer to something that comes next in the hierarchy of needs after *behema* — and consulting the order of the *baraita* (lives, then animals, then laundry), that next category is laundry. Laundry is thus included in the term *chayasam* — things that sustain life — because *de'ika tzaar degivshusa*, the neglect of laundry brings real physical harm (the filth, the associated ailments discussed by Shmuel). This is how Vardimus grounds his father's ruling: laundry is classified as life-sustaining activity and therefore may, under the framework of *chayecha kodmin*, be prioritized even against the competing needs of others.

## Bathing & Makeup

### Not Inuy Nefesh

The Gemara returns to the central case. *Amar Rabbi Yosi: ein elu nidrei inui nefesh* — Rabbi Yosi rules that vows involving the prohibition of bathing or the application of makeup do not fall within the category of *nidrei inuy nefesh*. The husband, therefore, cannot exercise his annulment power on that basis.

### Beino L'Veina Question

A question is then raised: even if these vows are not *inuy nefesh*, does Rabbi Yosi hold that they qualify as *devarim shebeino l'veina* — matters pertaining to the direct relationship between husband and wife — for which the husband also has annulment authority? The Gemara attempts to answer from Rabbi Yosi's own phrasing: *ein elu nidrei inui nefesh* sounds as though he is making a categorical ruling about the classification, not simply saying he cannot annul them. This might imply that they are not even in the *beino l'veina* category. But the Gemara deflects: perhaps Rabbi Yosi is only addressing the Rabbis who placed these in the *inuy nefesh* category, and his statement is *l'didchu* — addressed to those who take that position. He is saying: at minimum concede they are not *inuy nefesh*. His own view may go further and remove them from *beino l'veina* as well. Whether they belong there is therefore left open by this exchange.

### Rav Adda vs. Huna

Two *amoraim* dispute the matter. Rav Adda bar Ahava holds that the husband can annul — these vows do fall within *devarim shebeino l'veina*. Rav Huna holds he cannot annul.

### Fox Metaphor

Rav Huna's reasoning is captured by the saying: *shelo matzinu shual shemet b'afar pir* — we have never found a fox that died from the dust of its own burrow. A creature is not harmed by the familiar environment it has grown accustomed to. Applied here: if a husband's wife does not bathe or apply makeup, that is part of the physical reality of their shared life together. People adapt to and tolerate what becomes their normal environment. On this view, the husband has no genuine complaint, and therefore the vow does not rise to the level of *beino l'veina* in a way that would justify annulment.

## Annulment Categories

### Inuy Nefesh Rule

The *baraita* then sets out the governing categories systematically. Vows that carry *inuy nefesh* — genuine self-affliction — the husband may annul *bein beino l'veina bein beino l'veina ve'acheirim*: whether the vow directly concerns their relationship or involves her deprivation by a third party. The operative question for *inuy nefesh* is entirely about the nature and degree of harm to her, not about who is the subject of the vow. If her being deprived of something constitutes real *inuy nefesh*, it does not matter whether that thing comes from her husband, from another person, or from the world at large.

### Examples List

This is illustrated concretely. If she says *konam peirot eilu alai* — all fruit is forbidden to me — he can annul it. It does not matter whose fruit. If she says she is forbidden from benefiting from a particular person — her father, her brother, his brother, his father — or says she will not give straw before his animals or water to his cattle, he *cannot* annul. The reason: even though such vows may cause him annoyance or inconvenience, and even though they affect his household and family relationships, they are not directly about the intimate dynamic between the two of them. Since they do not rise to *inuy nefesh* either, neither basis for annulment applies. The *chiddush* here is that vows which create indirect irritation or hardship for the husband — even regarding his animals, his family members — fall outside both categories.

### Beino L'Veina Examples

By contrast, if she vows not to apply *kechol* or *afros* (the various forms of eye and face makeup), or not to have sexual relations — *shelo eshamesh mitatecha* — he can annul, *mishum devarim shebeino l'veina*, because these directly concern their relationship. The vow implicates something between the two of them in a direct and personal way.

### Obligatory Acts

But certain acts she cannot effectively vow away at all, because they fall within her existing obligations to him: *shelo atzia lecha mitatecha* (making his bed), *shelo emzog lecha et hakos* (mixing his wine), *shelo erchatz lecha panecha yadecha veraglecha* (washing his face, hands, and feet). As established in Ketubot, even a wife who brings a hundred maidservants into the household is still obligated to perform these acts of intimate service for her husband personally. Since she lacks the halachic standing to vow away something she is already obligated to provide, such a vow is simply ineffective — *ein tzarich lehafer*, the husband does not even need to formally annul.

## Rabban Gamliel

### Lo Yachel Devaro

That said, Rabban Gamliel holds that even with respect to vows that are technically ineffective — those covering acts she is already obligated to perform — the husband should nonetheless go through the process of annulment. The basis is *lo yachel devaro*: he should not let his word become violated. The Ran observes that the verse does not say *lo yachel nidro* or *shvuaso* — it says *devaro*, his *word*. The implication is that even speech that is not technically binding as a *neder* carries weight. Dismissing it entirely, treating it as though it never happened, is itself a form of disregard. The process of annulment, even where not strictly required, honors the gravity of speech and restores clarity.

### Son's Oath

This resonates practically. A son returned from camp having made some kind of conditional declaration — not technically a *neder* under strict halachic analysis — but he was distressed by it and wanted resolution. The technically correct answer that "it means nothing" was insufficient; he needed the formal process. The father said *mutar lach, mutar lach*, and only then did the son feel genuinely released. The words had weight, and the weight needed to be formally addressed. That is the thrust of Rabban Gamliel's ruling.

### Self-Annulment

A digression is prompted by the verse *lo yachel devaro*: *mikan lechacham she'ein matir nidro atzmo* — a Torah scholar cannot annul his own vow. The verse implies that one can *violate* one's words — meaning the words retain binding force on the person who spoke them. Someone else can annul, but the person himself cannot. As the aphorism goes: a prisoner cannot release himself from jail.

## Bathing & Makeup…

### Baraita Attribution

Returning to the *baraita*: who is the *tana* who placed the prohibition of applying *kechol* and *afros* in the category of *devarim shebeino l'veina*? The question matters because the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* places bathing and makeup in the *inuy nefesh* category. The *baraita* placing these in *beino l'veina* — a lesser category — can only reflect the view of Rabbi Yosi, who holds they are not *inuy nefesh*. This is therefore strong evidence that Rabbi Yosi, despite saying these do not qualify as *inuy nefesh*, does hold they belong in *beino l'veina* — resolving the earlier question in favor of Rav Adda bar Ahava.

## Sexual Relations

### Forbidden Pleasure

The Gemara now turns to the case of sexual relations. The *baraita* said that if she vows *shelo eshamesh mitatecha* — she will not have sexual relations with him — he can annul, *mishum devarim shebeino l'veina*. But the Gemara presses: what exactly is the case? If she says *hana'at tashmishi alecha* — the pleasure of my sexual service is forbidden to you — why would annulment even be necessary? *Hameshubdedet leih* — she is obligated to him in this regard; it is not hers to forbid.

### Rav Kahana

The case must therefore be, the Gemara concludes, *k'Rav Kahana*. Rav Kahana rules: if a woman says *hana'at tashmishi alecha* — my sexual service is forbidden to you — *kofin u'meshamshto*: she is compelled to fulfill her obligation regardless. The vow does not take effect, because it targets something that is not hers to withhold. But if she says *hana'at tashmishcha alai* — the pleasure of your sexual service is forbidden to me — then *yafer*: he must annul. This version of the vow does take effect.

### Wife Obligation

The reason the second formulation takes effect while the first does not relates to the principle *she'ein ma'achilin et adam davar ha'asur lo* — one may not force a person to consume something forbidden to them. She did not say she would not fulfill her duty; she said she would not benefit from him. Nevertheless, when the time comes to fulfill her obligation, she cannot be placed in the position of performing a transgression. Her obligation is therefore effectively neutralized by the vow, even though it was structured indirectly. And since this creates a genuine disruption in the dynamic between them, it falls within *beino l'veina* and he has the power to annul.

The Gemara takes for granted that a wife is *meshubdedet* — obligated — to have sexual relations with her husband. A wife who refuses is classified in halacha as a *moredet*, a rebel. The Rashba grounds this obligation not in a specific biblical verse but in the implicit structure of the marital bond: when a couple stands under the *chuppah*, the commitment they undertake includes mutual sexual fidelity and availability. It is not merely a contractual arrangement but a fundamental dimension of what marriage is.

### Rambam Problem

The Rambam offers a different account, one rooted in the *kinyan* model of *kiddushin*. On the Rambam's view — as elaborated by the Netziv and implicit in the Rambam's own formulation — the act of *kiddushin* involves an acquisition of the woman for marital purposes; her body, in the context of sexual relations, is in a sense her husband's domain. Therefore, she cannot make forbidden to herself what does not, on this account, fully belong to her to make forbidden. This reasoning is halachically potent but ideologically fraught, and can be read as providing halachic cover for marital coercion. The meaning of *kofin* in this context — whether it means legal compulsion or physical force — is itself disputed, and there is a documented history of women bringing complaints to rabbinic authorities and receiving inadequate redress on exactly these grounds.

### Custom Practice

The Gemara closes with a teaching about the weight of established practice: *devarim hamutarim ve'acheirim nahagu bahem issur, ee ata rashai linhog bahem heiter kedei levatlam* — if something is genuinely permitted but a community has adopted a practice of treating it as forbidden, one may not simply act in defiance of that practice to demonstrate its technical permissibility. The basis is once again *lo yachel devaro* — even informal speech and practice carry a kind of moral weight that demands respect. This is in the same register as Rabban Gamliel's ruling: even when there is no binding *neder*, the words and practices that people have taken seriously are not to be dismissed.

## Husband Annulling

### Matir vs. Meifer

Finally, the Ran raises a distinct and important question: can a husband serve as the one who is *matir* — who permits — his wife's vows, rather than simply being *meifer*, the one who annuls? The two roles are conceptually different. *Hafarah* is the husband's specific statutory power to annul within the marriage framework. *Hatarat nedarim* is the rabbinically prescribed process of releasing a vow through a sage or panel. The question is whether a husband can function in the latter capacity for his wife.

### Vested Interest

The Ran's analysis turns on the principle of *nogea badavar* — an interested party. If the husband has a personal stake in the vow being dissolved — because it is a *beino l'veina* matter, or because it implicates him in some way — he cannot serve as *matir*, since an interested party is disqualified from serving in that role. But if the vow does not affect him, the question becomes whether his status as her husband — governed by the principle of *ishto k'gufo*, that a wife is as one's own body — disqualifies him from serving as *matir* even when he has no direct interest. This is an open dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda in a *mishnah*, and the question of how we *pasken* on it remains unresolved.
