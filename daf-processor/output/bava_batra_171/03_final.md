# Bava Batra 171 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rewriting After Partial Payment

### Mishna Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁפָּרַע מִקְצָת חוֹבוֹ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יַחְלִיף, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יִכְתּוֹב שׁוֹבָר. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נִמְצָא זֶה צָרִיךְ לִהְיוֹת שׁוֹמֵר שׁוֹבָרוֹ מִן הָעַכְבָּרִים! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כָּךְ יָפֶה לוֹ, וְלֹא יֵרַע כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a debtor **who repaid part of his debt, Rabbi Yehuda says:** The creditor **should exchange** the promissory note for a new one stating the current balance and tear up the first promissory note. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The creditor may keep the original promissory note, and he **should write a receipt** for the payment he has received and give it to the debtor as proof of his partial payment of the sum recorded in the old note. **Rabbi Yehuda said** with regard to this arrangement: It is **found** that **this** debtor **must** now **guard his receipt against** being destroyed by **mice,** as if he no longer has the receipt, he will have to pay the entire sum recorded in the promissory note. **Rabbi Yosei said to him: This** situation is **fitting for him;** it is better that this procedure be followed, **and the strength of** the claim of **this** creditor **not be weakened.**

The daf opens with a question that arises from a common practical situation: a *shetar chov* has been written for a thousand dollars, and the *loveh* pays back two hundred. What is to be done with the existing document? The *mishna* records a debate on precisely this point. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the original *shetar* should be replaced with a new one reflecting the remaining balance of eight hundred. Rabbi Yosi disagrees and instead advocates writing a *shovar* — a receipt — for the amount paid.

### Positions Explained

The reasoning on each side flows from distinct concerns about who bears the burden of risk. Rabbi Yehuda objects to the *shovar* solution because it places an unfair responsibility on the *loveh*: he must now safeguard his receipt against loss, and if it is destroyed or stolen — proverbially, eaten by mice — he may find himself obligated to pay the full original sum again. Rabbi Yosi counters that this is not unreasonable: *kach yafeh lo*, it is appropriate that the *loveh* bear this responsibility, since after all it is he who incurred the debt. More importantly, Rabbi Yosi argues that rewriting the *shetar* in the manner Rabbi Yehuda proposes would damage the interests of the *malveh*. The implicit assumption underlying Rabbi Yosi's concern is that Rabbi Yehuda's new *shetar* would bear today's date — the date of rewriting — rather than the original date of the loan. Were that the case, the *malveh* would forfeit all the liens that had attached to the borrower's property from the time of the original transaction, a potentially devastating loss.

### Rav's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אֵין הֲלָכָה לֹא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלֹא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; אֶלָּא בֵּית דִּין מְקָרְעִין הַשְּׁטָר, וְכוֹתְבִין לוֹ שְׁטָר אַחֵר מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, nor** is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rather,** the *halakha* is that **the court tears up the** original promissory **note and writes a different** promissory **note for** the creditor, listing the new sum owed, dated **from the time** of the **first** document.

The *gemara* introduces Rav's ruling, transmitted through Rav Huna: the *halacha* follows neither Rabbi Yehuda nor Rabbi Yosi as they had been understood. Instead, *beis din* tears up the old *shetar* and writes a new one *mizman rishon* — dated as of the original loan. This solution is elegant: the *malveh* loses nothing, retaining the full force of his liens from the original date, while the *loveh* is not burdened with the responsibility of guarding a receipt. The apparent third position introduced by Rav reflects his understanding that Rabbi Yehuda had meant only a *shetar* dated as of today, leaving the *malveh* worse off — and thus Rav stepped in to articulate a better path.

### Braita Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב הוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא לְרַב הוּנָא: אִי שְׁמִיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַב הָא [בָּרַיְיתָא] דְּתַנְיָא: עֵדִים מְקָרְעִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר, וְכוֹתְבִין לוֹ שְׁטָר אַחֵר מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן – הֲוָה הָדַר בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, וְלָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Rav Huna, and some say** it was **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba** who said this **to Rav Huna: If Rav would have heard this** following ***baraita*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that according to Rabbi Yehuda the proper procedure is that the **witnesses tear the** original promissory **note and write another** promissory **note for** the creditor listing the new sum owed, dated **from the time** of the **first** document, **he would have retracted** his statement that the court tears the promissory note and writes a new one. Rav Huna **said to him:** Rav **heard** the *baraita*, **and** nevertheless **he did not retract** his statement.

At this point a challenge is raised. Rav Nachman — and some say Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba — asked Rav Huna: had Rav actually heard the *braita* that addresses this very dispute? For the *braita* teaches explicitly: *eidim* tear up the *shetar* and write a new one *mizman rishon*, stating clearly that even Rabbi Yehuda's position requires the original date. Had Rav known this *braita*, would he not simply have ruled like Rabbi Yehuda without introducing anything new?

The *gemara* responds that Rav had in fact heard the *braita* — and still did not retract. Why? Because the *chiddush* of Rav's ruling is not the *mizman rishon* principle, which the *braita* already attributes to Rabbi Yehuda. The *chiddush* is that writing a predated *shetar* — even in this legitimate case — requires the authority of a *beis din*. The *braita* had permitted the *eidim* themselves to rewrite the *shetar* from the original date. Rav rules that this is insufficient: only a *beis din* may authorize it.

### Beis Din vs. Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּי דִינָא – אַלִּימֵי לְאַפְקוֹעֵי מָמוֹנָא. אֶלָּא עֵדִים שֶׁעָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתָן – חוֹזְרִין וְעוֹשִׂין שְׁלִיחוּתָן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, a court** can write a new document, because the court **has the power to confiscate money.** A creditor has the right to seize any property that the debtor had owned on the day the promissory note was written, even if that property was subsequently sold to others; the earlier the date on the note, the more properties it applies to. By dating the new document on the date of the original loan the creditor is once again given the right to seize properties from those who had purchased land from the debtor in between the date of the original loan and the date the new promissory note is actually written. The court has such confiscatory power. **But** with regard to **witnesses, who** already **performed their agency,** i.e., fulfilled their assigned role, the first time they wrote the promissory note, can **they return and perform their agency** again by writing a second promissory note?

The *gemara* articulates why this distinction matters. *Bishlama beis din alima leafkuei mamona* — a *beis din* possesses the institutional authority to, in effect, extract monetary obligations and reconstitute them; *hefker beis din hefker*. But witnesses who have already signed a *shetar* and fulfilled their role — can they simply return and execute another? Writing a *shetar* dated as of today would present no difficulty: both *malveh* and *loveh* could agree to treat the original debt as discharged and execute a fresh $800 loan right now, no special authority needed. The problem arises precisely when the new *shetar* is to bear an earlier date. Creating liens retroactively — even to reflect a reality that genuinely existed — is not something witnesses and the parties can accomplish on their own. That is a form of predating a document, a *shtar mukdam*, and requires *beis din* to do it properly.

### Tosfot's Policy

Tosfot acknowledges the force of the conceptual question here: if the liens already exist, if the witnesses are not fabricating anything but merely re-attesting to a known fact, what is truly objectionable about allowing the *eidim* to rewrite the *shetar* themselves? Tosfot concedes that from a purely logical standpoint, this would not be invalid. The same witnesses who saw the original thousand-dollar loan can truthfully write that the *loveh* now owes eight hundred — that is not a lie. No new liens are being invented out of whole cloth; the liens have existed all along.

But Tosfot frames Rav's requirement of *beis din* as a matter of policy rather than strict legal impossibility. Allowing *eidim* on their own to write a predated *shetar*, even in a case where it reflects the truth and both parties consent, opens a dangerous door. Predating documents is inherently risky business, and Rav determined that it should only be done under the formal auspices of *beis din* — not because the liens cannot otherwise be said to exist, but because permitting private predating without judicial supervision invites abuse.

Tosfot also addresses a related scenario discussed earlier in the *gemara*: the case of a *shetar* being erased and requiring replacement. In that case as well, the question of whether *beis din* is needed — and at what level — turns on an additional factor. There, the *loveh* has not consented. He has not authorized the *eidim* to rewrite his *shetar*. Under ordinary circumstances, a *shetar* cannot be written against a party without that party's authorization — what the *gemara* calls *da'as mis'chayiv*, the consent of the one who is being bound. Here, in the partial payment case, both sides agree: the *loveh* has every reason to prefer a *shetar* for eight hundred over a *shetar* for a thousand, and he authorizes the *eidim* accordingly. That bilateral consent is what makes it comparatively manageable. But even so, Rav requires *beis din*, as a matter of policy, given the inherent danger of predating any document that carries liens.

## Deeds Without Liens

### Ten Deeds Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עֵדִים כּוֹתְבִין אֲפִילּוּ עֲשָׂרָה שְׁטָרוֹת עַל שָׂדֶה אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that witnesses are **not** empowered to do so? **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Witnesses** who were commissioned to write a deed for the transfer of property **may write even ten deeds for one field?** If the original deed is lost or destroyed, the witnesses may write a replacement deed for the purchaser, even if the deed is lost many times.

The *gemara* now presses on its own conclusion with a question. Rav himself — the very authority who just ruled that *eidim* cannot rewrite a predated *shetar* without *beis din* — is also cited by Rav Yehuda as saying that *eidim kosvin afilu asara shtaros al sadei achas*: witnesses may write even ten replacement deeds on the same plot of land. How can both statements be Rav's? If *eidim* cannot rewrite a *shetar* independently, how can they produce ten replacement deeds?

### No Liens Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בִּשְׁטַר מַתָּנָה, וְרַבָּה אָמַר: בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** an answer to this question: Rav Yehuda's statement was concerning **a deed of gift,** not a bill of sale. A gift of land has no guarantee; if it is repossessed from the recipient by the giver's creditor to repay the giver's debt, the recipient has no recourse and cannot collect any money from anyone. Therefore, in this case no one is jeopardized by the writing of many replacement deeds, which are intended only to serve as a proof that the recipient is actually entitled to the land. **And Rabba says:** It is possible to say that Rav Yehuda's statement applied even **with regard to a bill** of sale **that does not have a guarantee in it.** In that case as well, there is no potential loss to any purchasers of land. By contrast, in the case of a promissory note, which can be used to repossess property from those who have purchased land from the debtor, witnesses do not have the authority to predate a document and thereby subject more purchasers to possible land seizures due to the predating of the document.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction through two answers, one attributed to Rav Yosef and one to Rabba. Rav Yosef says the case of the ten deeds refers to a *shtar matana* — a gift deed. Rabba says it refers to a *shtar she'ein bo achrayus* — a deed that explicitly excludes the seller's liability. In either case, the key feature is the absence of *achrayus*.

The significance of this distinction is substantial. A standard deed of sale carries with it an implicit guarantee: if the property is subsequently seized by a prior creditor, the seller is obligated to compensate the buyer from his other assets — this is *achrayus*. It is this guarantee that generates the dangerous lien structure. A deed without *achrayus* — whether because it is a gift (where no such insurance is assumed by default) or because the parties explicitly waived it — cannot be used to seize property from third parties who purchased land in the interim. Therefore, even if such a deed is predated, there is no risk of defrauding earlier purchasers. The concern about *shtar mukdam* is precisely the concern that fabricated early liens will be used to dispossess innocent buyers. Where that risk does not exist, *eidim* may freely write replacement documents — even ten of them, even predated — without requiring the authority of *beis din*.

### Tosfot Caveat

Tosfot raises a subtle difficulty here. Earlier in the *gemara* there was a discussion of concerns about fraudulent schemes when replacement documents are involved — the worry being that a person might claim he "lost" a *shetar* when in fact he is setting up a double collection. How, Tosfot asks, could we even have entertained the idea that *eidim* would rewrite a document with *achrayus*, given all the concerns raised there?

Tosfot resolves this by drawing a crucial distinction: the earlier concern about schemes applied to a case where the original *shetar* was allegedly lost and its loss was unverifiable. In the case of the ten deeds, however, the situation is different — it is known with certainty that the original document was destroyed. Either the witnesses themselves observed it being burned, or its destruction is otherwise established beyond doubt. In that scenario, there is no concern that the *eidim* are participating in a scheme; the only question is whether they are technically authorized to produce a replacement. The answer, for documents without *achrayus*, is yes. It is only the loan document with liens — where predating creates real risk of prejudicing third parties — that Rav restricts to *beis din* supervision.

## Lost Document Case

### Lost Shetar Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף הֲוָה מַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ זוּזִי בְּרַבִּי אַבָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פַּפֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי זוּזַיי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי שְׁטָרַאי, וּשְׁקוֹל זוּזָךְ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״שְׁטָרָךְ אִירְכַס לִי, אֶכְתּוֹב לָךְ תְּבָרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves its assertion: This is **like that** case **where Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef was owed money by Rabbi Abba.** Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef **came before Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi,** and **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Give me my money.** Rabbi Abba **said to him:** First **give me my** promissory **note, and** then **take your money.** Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef **said to him: I lost your** promissory **note;** instead, **I will write a receipt for you.**

The *gemara* now turns to a related case involving a lost *shetar* and a dispute about whether a *shovar* must be written in its place. The narrative centers on Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef, who had lent money to Rabi Abba. The case came before Rabi Chanina bar Pappa. Rav Yitzchak demanded payment; Rabi Abba responded: *hav li shtarai* — return my *shetar* to me, and I will pay you. Rav Yitzchak said he had lost the *shetar*, and offered instead to write a *shovar* — a receipt — acknowledging that the debt had been discharged.

### Bavel vs. EY

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״הָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר!״ אֲמַר: ״מַאן יָהֵיב לַן מֵעַפְרֵיהּ דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, רָמִינַן בְּעַיְינִין; הָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְהָא רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר״. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba **said to him:** **Isn't** there the opinion of **Rav and Shmuel, who both say** that the *halakha* is that one does **not write a receipt?** Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi, the judge, thereupon **said: Who will give us** some **of the dust of Rav and Shmuel,** and **I will place it on my eyes,** so highly do I regard them. Nevertheless, **isn't** there the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, who both say** that one **writes a receipt?** **And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Ilai says:** The *halakha* is that one **writes a receipt.**

Rabi Abba refused, citing the ruling of Rav and Shmuel, who both hold that *ein kotvin shovar* — one does not write a receipt. Under that position, the *loveh* is entitled to insist: if you want your money, produce the *shetar* and allow me to tear it up. The *loveh* should not be forced to accept a receipt and take on the burden of guarding it for the rest of time. Without the original *shetar*, the *malveh* cannot collect.

The *gemara* then introduces the opposing view: *man yeiv lan me-afrei de-Rav u-Shmuel ve-raminan be-einain* — who would give us the dust of Rav and Shmuel to place in our eyes, as it were. The formulation is striking: it acknowledges the towering authority of the Babylonian masters while also suggesting, gently, that their teaching may have obscured other valid positions. For Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — the great Eretz Yisrael *Amoraim* — both hold that *kotvin shovar*, one does write a receipt. And when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael he transmitted in the name of Rabbi Ela'a that *kotvin shovar* as well. There is thus a genuine *machlokes* between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael on this point.

### Malveh's Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּכוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּא אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר, אָבַד שְׁטָרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה – יֹאכַל הַלָּה וְחָדֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And it stands to reason that** one **writes a receipt. As, if** it would **enter your mind that** one does **not write a receipt,** then **if the** promissory **note of this** creditor **is lost, should this** debtor **eat and rejoice?** Is it fair that he should keep money that he knows he owes the creditor?

The *gemara* presses to establish which position ought to be followed. It brings a pointed argument in favor of writing the *shovar*: *avad shtaro shel zeh yochel haleh veyechadi?* — Because this person's *shetar* was lost, should the *loveh* consume the *malveh*'s money and rejoice? The *malveh* lent real money. He is owed it. If we say that without the *shetar* he cannot collect, then the loss of a piece of parchment destroys a legitimate debt — and the *loveh* profits from pure happenstance. That cannot be just. Therefore, the *malveh* must be able to write a *shovar* and collect even without the original document.

### Loveh's Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְאֶלָּא מַאי, כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר?! אָבַד שׁוֹבָרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה – יֹאכַל הַלָּה וְחָדֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִין, ״עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this** line of reasoning: **Rather, what** should the *halakha* be, that one **writes a receipt** when the creditor cannot find the promissory note? If **the receipt of** the debtor **is lost, should this** creditor **eat and rejoice?** Once the debtor has lost his receipt, there is nothing to prevent the creditor from collecting the debt a second time. This, too, is unfair. In either case, whether a receipt is written or not, an injustice could result. Why should it be preferred that the potential of suffering an injustice should be borne by the debtor rather than the creditor? **Rava said to** Abaye: **Yes,** it is preferable, because, as it is written: **"The borrower is a servant to the lender"** (Proverbs 22:7). The debtor's interests are subordinated to those of the creditor.

Abaye responds with a sharp counter-argument that mirrors the structure of the original complaint. Very well — but if we write the *shovar* and the *loveh* subsequently loses it, *avad shovro shel zeh yochel haleh veyechadi?* — should the *malveh* now go and rejoice and collect twice? The *malveh* still holds his original *shetar* — or claims it is "lost" while secretly retaining it — and the *loveh*, having paid and lost his receipt, stands exposed to a second demand for payment. The same logic that protects the *malveh* from the inequity of losing his *shetar* could equally be deployed to protect the *loveh* from the inequity of losing his *shovar*.

One must note the asymmetry in the two concerns, however. The scenario where the *malveh* "lost" his *shetar* and then produces it to collect twice requires the assumption that he is lying. The *loveh* who refuses to pay despite acknowledging the debt is not doing anything covert — he is openly exploiting the *malveh*'s misfortune. The moral weight of the two cases is not identical, even if their formal structure appears parallel.

### Loveh's Burden

Rava resolves the tension with a principled ruling. If a risk must be borne by one party, it should fall on the *loveh* — because *eved loveh l'ish malveh*, the *loveh* is a slave to the *malveh*. This is not a casual expression; it reflects a genuine *halachic* orientation. The entire network of *mitzvot* governing the creditor-debtor relationship — the prohibitions against pressuring the borrower, against entering his home to take collateral — exists precisely because the *malveh* holds structural power over the *loveh*, and the Torah seeks to check that power. Yet the underlying truth remains: the *loveh* is indebted. He received money he has not repaid. The *malveh* is out his funds and has received nothing in return. In any allocation of risk between the two, it is more appropriate that the *loveh* bear it.

The practical upshot is as follows. The *malveh*, if he has genuinely lost his *shetar*, may produce a *shovar* in its place and collect. We do not say that the accident of his loss extinguishes a legitimate debt. The *loveh*, once he pays, must guard his receipt — and if he fails to do so, the loss is his. From this case the *gemara* also establishes, against the view of Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua who had suggested that a *shovar* applies only to partial payment, that we write a *shovar* even for the *full* amount of the debt.

## Postdated Documents

### Predated Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב הַמּוּקְדָּמִין – פְּסוּלִין, וְהַמְאוּחָרִין – כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Shevi'it* 10:5): **Promissory notes that are antedated are not valid, but** those **that are postdated are valid.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishna* from *Shevi'it* bearing on the validity of misdated documents. The rule stated there is crisp: *shtarei chov hamukdamin psulin v'hameucharim ksherin* — predated loan documents are invalid, postdated loan documents are valid. The predated document is disqualified because it falsely extends the *malveh*'s liens backward in time, allowing him to collect from property sold after the actual loan was made but before the fabricated date. The postdated document, by contrast, merely means that the *malveh* is forfeiting some of his lien rights — he cannot collect from property sold between the actual date of the loan and the written date. That is a loss to no one but himself, and therefore the document remains valid.

### Loan vs. Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שְׁטָרֵי הַלְוָאָה, אֲבָל שְׁטָרֵי מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר – אֲפִילּוּ מְאוּחָרִין נָמֵי פְּסוּלִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? זִימְנִין דִּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ אַרְעָא בְּנִיסָן וְכָתֵיב לֵיהּ בְּתִשְׁרִי, וּמִתְרְמֵי לֵיהּ זוּזֵי בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי וְזָבֵין לַיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, וְכִי מָטֵי תִּשְׁרִי – מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: הֲדַר זְבֵנְתַּהּ מִינָּךְ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **promissory notes, but** with regard to **deeds of buying and selling** property, **even postdated** documents **are not valid. What is the reason** for this stringency in the case of bills of sale? If postdated documents were allowed, there could be **times that** the seller **sells land to** the purchaser **in** the month of **Nisan** of a particular year **and writes** a postdated deed **for him** stating that the sale took place **in** the following **Tishrei, and** some **money happens to** reach the seller **between** Nisan and Tishrei **and he purchases** the land back **from** the purchaser. **And** then, **when Tishrei arrives,** the purchaser **takes out** the original bill of sale **and says to** the seller: It is true that you purchased the field from me, but **I then purchased it from you** again now, in Tishrei, as stated in this document.

Rav Hamnuna refines the ruling: *lo shanu ela shtarei chov* — this leniency for postdated documents applies only to loan documents, not to sale documents. A postdated deed of sale is also invalid. The reason is a specific abuse that becomes possible when a sale deed is postdated. If Shimon sells land to Reuven in Nissan but the deed is written as of Tishrei, and in the intervening months Shimon raises funds and buys the land back from Reuven, then when Tishrei arrives Reuven can produce his postdated deed and claim that he purchased the land *then* — after the repurchase — creating the appearance of a second, independent sale and allowing him to assert ownership a second time. The *Rashbam* notes that such arrangements may even have been formalized with buyback clauses, making the scheme all the more plausible.

This fraudulent use is specific to sale documents. With a loan document, the argument might seem parallel: could a *malveh* not lend money in Nissan, postdate the *shetar* to Tishrei, accept repayment in the interim, lose the *shetar*, and then in Tishrei produce it and claim it reflects a new loan? The *gemara* considers exactly this scenario.

### Double Collection Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שְׁטָרֵי הַלְוָאָה נָמֵי – זִמְנִין דְּיָזֵיף בְּנִיסָן וּכְתִיב לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא בְּתִשְׁרִי; וּמִתְרְמֵי לֵיהּ זוּזֵי בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי וּפָרַע לֵיהּ, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי שְׁטָרַאי, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִירְכַס לִי, וְכָתֵיב לֵיהּ תְּבָרָא; וְכִי מָטֵי זִמְנֵיהּ, מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דִּיזַפְתְּ מִינַּאי! קָסָבַר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** a similar scenario could occur with **promissory notes as well:** There could be **times that one borrows** money **in Nisan and writes** a promissory **note for** the creditor stating that the loan took place **in** the following **Tishrei, and** some **money happens to** reach the debtor **between** Nisan and Tishrei **and he repays** his debt **to** the creditor early **and says to him: Give me my** promissory **note. And** the creditor **says to him: I lost** it, **and he writes him a receipt** instead. **And** then, **when the time** written in the note **arrives,** the creditor **takes out** the supposedly lost promissory note **and says to** the debtor: It is true that you repaid me previously, but **it is now,** after you repaid that loan, **that you borrowed from me these** dinars recorded in this document, which is dated in Tishrei. The Gemara answers: Rav Hamnuna **holds that** one does **not write a receipt,** so that scenario cannot occur. Like Rabbi Yehuda, Rav Hamnuna maintains that if a creditor loses his promissory note the debtor does not have to repay the loan.

The *gemara* poses the parallel case with full force. Borrow a thousand dollars in Nissan; postdate the *shetar* to Tishrei. The *loveh* pays in the interim and asks for the *shetar* to be returned. The *malveh* claims he lost it and writes a *shovar*. Then when Tishrei arrives, the *malveh* takes out the *shetar* and says: you borrowed a thousand dollars from me again, just this month — this is a new debt. The *shovar* said he paid up a debt from months ago; this *shetar* is for a fresh loan. Pay up.

The *gemara* resolves this neatly: *kasavar ein kotvin shovar* — the concern only arises if we allow *shovarim* to be written. Under the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *shtaros* must be returned and torn up upon repayment and that we do not write *shovarim*, there is no risk. The *loveh* will simply refuse to pay until the original *shetar* is returned. The *malveh* cannot hold onto the postdated *shetar* in secret and then produce it as evidence of a new loan, because the *loveh* can demand: give me the *shetar* before I give you the money. Once the *shetar* is surrendered and torn up, the postdated loan document cannot be misused.

The same protection, however, does not apply to sale documents. When the *loveh* pays off a loan, he can insist on receiving the *shetar* in exchange. But when a buyer repurchases land he had previously sold, he does not demand the original deed — he simply receives a new deed in his name. The original postdated deed thus remains in the seller's hands, available for future misuse. This is why postdated deeds of sale cannot be permitted, while postdated loan documents, under the regime that prohibits *shovarim*, are safe.

### Rav Abba Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר לְרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּכָתְבִינַן שְׁטָרֵי מְאוּחָרֵי וְכָתְבִינַן תְּבָרָא, אַמַּאי קָעָבְדִינַן הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, בָּתַר דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְסָפְרֵיהּ: כִּי כָּתְבִיתוּ שְׁטָרֵ[י] מְאוּחָרֵי, כְּתֻבוּ הָכִי: ״שְׁטָרָא דְּנַן לָא בְּזִמְנֵיהּ כְּתַבְנֵיהּ, אֶלָּא אַחַרְנוֹהִי וּכְתַבְנוֹהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar said to Rav Kahana, and some say** it was **Rav Yirmeya of Difti** who said this **to Rav Kahana: And today, when we write postdated** promissory **notes, and we** also **write a receipt** in cases where the creditor loses his promissory note, **why do we do this?** The combination of allowing postdated promissory notes and allowing the writing of a receipt instead of producing the promissory note would enable double collection of the loan to take place. Rav Kahana **said to him:** The problem was rectified **after Rabbi Abba told his** court **scribe: When you write postdated** promissory **notes, write as follows: We did not write this document on its date,** i.e., on the date written within the document; **rather, we postdated it and wrote it.** Since it is clear from the text of the document that it was postdated, double collection of the loan is avoided.

The *gemara* arrives at a real-world problem. Rav Yeimar (and some say Rav Yirmiyah of Difti) raises the issue with Rav Kahana: *ve-haidna d'chatvinan shtarei meucharim ve-chatvinan shovar* — but in current practice, we write both postdated *shtaros* and *shovarim*. We have therefore returned to the very situation of potential double collection. Why do we do this?

The answer is that Rav Abba issued instructions to his scribes. When writing a postdated document, the *sofer* must include explicit language within the *shetar* itself: *shtara dnan lav b'zmanei chatavnuhu ela icharnu-hu u-chatavnu-hu* — this document was not written on its stated date; rather, we intentionally postdated it. This notation changes everything. Once it is explicit on the face of the *shetar* that the document is postdated, any subsequent *shovar* that the *loveh* holds can be matched to it. If the *malveh* tries to claim the *shetar* represents a new, later loan, the *loveh* simply points to the language within the *shetar* itself and says: look, it says right here that this was postdated. And since I have a *shovar* for a thousand dollars — the same amount — it must be going on this very *shetar*.

This is also why Rabbi Yosi, in the earlier *mishna*, permitted the Shabbos and Yom Kippur case: when it is manifest from the document itself that it is postdated — because no transaction could have been written on Shabbos or Yom Kippur — the *shovar* can be reliably correlated to it. The *Rashbam* adds an important practical note: once this system is in place, a *malveh* must be careful never to write another postdated *shetar* of the same amount for the same *loveh*, lest the *loveh* always be able to claim that any *shovar* he holds is matched to the new document.

## Practice Evolution

### Current Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְהָאִידָּנָא – דְּלָא קָא עָבְדִינַן הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: But** what about **today, when we do not do this,** i.e., we do not follow Rabbi Abba's instructions? How do we avoid double collection of the loan?

The discussion takes another turn when the practice evolves still further. Rav Ashi raises the next problem with Rav Kahana: *ve-haidna d'lo chatvinan hachi* — but nowadays we do not even write that explicit notation in the *shetar*. We write postdated documents without any statement that they are postdated, and we write dated *shovarim*. This once again opens the system to abuse.

### Rav Safra Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְסָפְרֵיהּ: כִּי כָּתְבִיתוּ הָנֵי תְּבָרֵי; אִי יָדְעִיתוּ זִימְנָא דִשְׁטָרָא – כְּתֻבוּ, אִי לָא – כְּתֻבוּ סְתָמָא, דְּכׇל אֵימַת דְּנָפֵיק – לַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana replied: The problem was rectified **after Rav Safra said to his** court **scribe: When you write these receipts** for debtors who pay debts without the original promissory note being torn, **if you know the date** written **in the** missing promissory **note, write** it into the receipt. But **if** you do **not** know the date written in the promissory note, **write** the receipt **without specification,** i.e., do not write any date at all in the receipt, so **that whenever** the creditor **produces** the promissory note, the undated receipt **can weaken it,** i.e., exempt the debtor from payment.

Rav Safra addressed this by instructing his scribes differently. Rather than annotating the *shetar*, he focused on the *shovar*. When writing a receipt, the scribes should follow this principle: *i yaditu zimna d'shtara kisvu* — if you know the date of the *shetar*, write it on the *shovar*. So if it is known that the *shetar* is postdated to Tishrei and the *loveh* pays in Tishrei, simply date the *shovar* as of Tishrei. The *shovar* and the *shetar* will then carry the same date, making them clearly correspondent, and the risk of double collection disappears.

But what if the date of the *shetar* is not known? Rav Safra's instruction then is: write an undated *shovar* — *shetama*, without a date. An undated receipt can serve as a perpetual defense: whenever the *malveh* produces his postdated *shetar* and claims it reflects a current loan, the *loveh* produces his undated *shovar* and says he has already paid.

This solution, however, introduces new dangers of its own. An undated receipt is an extraordinarily potent instrument — essentially a blank check in reverse. If the *malveh* ever lends the same *loveh* the same amount again on a later occasion, the *loveh* can produce his undated *shovar* and claim it satisfies the new debt. Even more troubling: if the *malveh* later lends a larger amount, the *loveh* can produce the undated receipt and claim it represents partial payment of the new obligation, reducing what he owes. The *Rashbam* makes the practical implication explicit: once an undated receipt for a certain amount has been written, the *malveh* must be vigilant never to extend a loan of that exact amount to the same borrower again.

### Modern Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְהָא הָאִידָּנָא – דְּלָא קָעָבְדִינַן הָכִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבָּנַן תַּקּוֹנֵי תַּקִּינוּ; מַאן דְּעָבֵיד – עָבֵיד, מַאן דְּלָא עָבֵיד – אִיהוּ הוּא דְּאַפְסֵיד אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi, and some say** it was **Rav Ashi** who said **to Rav Kahana: But today, when we do not do this** either when writing receipts, how can we avoid double collection of a loan? Rav Kahana **said to him: The Sages instituted** taking this precaution. **One who does** what the Sages instituted **does** it and protects himself from loss; and as for **one who does not do** so, **he has brought the loss upon himself,** and will suffer the consequences if the promissory note is found and presented in the future.

At the final stage of this development, Ravina poses the problem to Rav Ashi — and some attribute it to Rav Ashi addressing Rav Kahana — that current practice has reverted to a state where postdated *shtaros* are written alongside ordinarily dated *shovarim*, without any of the protective mechanisms described above. The system, as practiced, is exposed to the double collection risk that the entire preceding discussion was meant to prevent.

### Rabbis' Framework

Rav Ashi's response closes the discussion: *Rabanan takuni takuni* — the rabbis established the proper framework. They articulated the rules clearly: if you are going to write a postdated *shetar*, either explicitly annotate it as postdated within the document itself, following Rav Abba's instruction, or ensure that the *shovar* is undated, following Rav Safra's instruction. The system can work; the protections are available.

*Man d'avid avid* — one who follows the established procedure is protected. *Man d'lo avid ihu d'afsid anafshei* — one who does not follow it has caused his own loss. The *loveh* who pays a postdated debt, accepts a dated receipt, and fails to ensure that either the *shetar* is annotated or the *shovar* is undated has the tools of protection available to him and has simply not used them. The system places the responsibility squarely on the parties. The rabbis have done their part by establishing the framework; the *loveh* must do his part by insisting that the *sofer* follow it.
