# Bava Kamma 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shaminu al Pi Sadeh

### Field vs. Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשָּׁמִין עַל גַּב שָׂדֶה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana says: As the verse states: "And it feed in another field [*uvi'er bisde aḥer*]"** (Exodus 22:4). This **teaches that** the court **appraises** the damage **relative to another field,** i.e., relative to the damaged field as a whole and not an appraisal of only the specific garden bed that was damaged.

The daf opens in the middle of an expansion of the concluding line of the first *mishna* in the sixth *perek*, which established that when assessing damage done by *shein v'regel*, one employs the principle of *shaminu al pi sadeh acher* — the damage is assessed against another field. This principle contains two essential ideas. First, the focus falls on the depreciation of the field itself, not on the value of the fruit that was eaten — a figure that is substantially lower than what one would calculate by simply valuing the consumed produce. Second, the question arises of how to conduct that assessment, since the answer will differ depending on whether the owner's entire plot consists of the one patch where the animal ate, or whether that patch is but a small portion of a far larger holding.

The *gemara* responds to this second question with the formulation *shaminu al pi sadeh acher* — one assesses the damage against a reference field. This introduces the *shishim* calculation, though the precise mechanics of that figure — sixty *se'ah*, sixty *tirkav*, and how the arithmetic works — remain somewhat involved.

### Rashi's Sixty Model

Rashi understands this to mean that one imagines what the depreciation would have been had the damage occurred within a standardized field that is sixty times the size of the patch consumed. From Rashi's presentation, it sounds as though one constructs a generic model — a kind of hypothetical benchmark — rather than anchoring the calculation to the owner's actual field.

### Tosafot's Actual Field

Tosafot, however, emphasizes that the *mishna* itself reads *shamin beis se'ah be-osah sadeh* — one assesses a *beis se'ah* within *that* field. What Rashi's explanation loses, Tosafot argues, is precisely the word *be-osah sadeh*: the *chiddush* of the *mishna* lies in the insistence on the actual damaged field. One asks how much *this* person's field was depreciated. The *shishim* model still applies, but the reference field used in the calculation is imagined as sixty *se'ah* of this very field — with its particular location, soil quality, and market value — not some generic abstraction. The calculation thus becomes proportional: if the animal ate a half *se'ah*, one pays half the assessed depreciation; if it ate a quarter *se'ah*, one pays accordingly. The *chiddush* of *be-osah sadeh* is that one envisions this field expanded to sixty *se'ah*, not an interchangeable generic plot.

## Tannaic Fruit Valuation

### R. Yosi: Market Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. אָכְלָה חֲזִיז – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן בַּמְשׁוּיָּיר שֶׁבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Yishmael** both **said the same thing.** They are of the same opinion that the appraisal for damages is based on the value of the produce remaining in the field once it ripens. The opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** is **this that we stated** above in the *baraita*, i.e., that the damages paid for an animal eating unripe grain are assessed according to **what remains of it,** i.e., the court waits until the rest of the field ripens and then appraises the value of what was previously eaten.

The *gemara* now proceeds on 59a. Abaye states that Rabbi Yosi HaGelili and Rabbi Yishmael *amru davar echad* — they said the same thing. Rabbi Yosi HaGelili appears in a *beraita* introduced earlier: *nidon be-mishuyar she-ba* — the damage is assessed based on the value of the produce at the time it will be brought to market. According to this approach, if an animal ate a bushel of unripe grapes that would eventually sell for one hundred dollars per bushel, the owner pays one hundred dollars. The focus here shifts from the field's depreciation to the value of the fruit destroyed, and the mechanism for valuing unripe produce that has no current market price is to assess it according to what it will eventually fetch when fully ripened.

### R. Yishmael: Best Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – דְּתַנְיָא: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל נִיזָּק; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לִגְבּוֹת לַנִּיזָּקִין מִן הָעִידִּית.
>
> **Translation:** The opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael** is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"The best of his field and the best of his vineyard he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4), which means he must pay according to the **best**-quality **field of the injured** party **or the best**-quality **vineyard of the injured** party. **This is the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: The verse comes only** to allow **injured** parties **to collect** compensation **from** the **superior-quality** land of the one liable to pay for the damage.

Rabbi Yishmael, as derived from a separate *beraita*, expounds the verse *meitav sadehu u-meitav karmo yeshaleim* — "the best of his field and the best of his vineyard he shall pay." Rabbi Yishmael reads this as *meitav sadehu shel nizak* — the best of the *victim's* field. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, reads the verse as addressing the *mazik*'s obligation to pay from the best of his own holdings: when the *nizak* comes to collect the assessed damages, he may collect from the *mazik*'s best-quality land. If the *mazik* destroyed a hundred dollars' worth, the *nizak* collects that sum from the choicest field available. This reading is clear enough. But what does Rabbi Yishmael mean when he says one pays based on the best of the *nizak*'s field? Does it mean that if the animal destroyed a low-grade acre one must reimburse the owner as though a prime acre were destroyed — a position without precedent?

### Hamotzi Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא תֵּימָא כְּרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין – דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכְלָה עֲרוּגָה בֵּין הָעֲרוּגוֹת, וְלָא יָדְעִינַן אִי כְּחוּשָׁה הֲוַאי אִי שְׁמֵינָה הֲוַאי, דְּאָמַר: קוּם שַׁלֵּים שְׁמֵינָה בְּמֵיטַב דְּאִיכָּא הַשְׁתָּה; דְּהָכִי לָא אָמְרִינַן. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And do not say** that Rabbi Yishmael's statement should be interpreted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Idi bar Avin, as Rav Idi bar Avin says:** Here it concerns a case **where** the animal **ate** the produce of **one garden bed from among** several **garden beds, but we do not know whether** the garden bed it ate from **was lean or choice. Therefore,** the verse **says** to the owner of the animal: **Arise** and **pay choice** land, equivalent **to the best**-quality land **that there is now** remaining, and the court does not assume that the animal ate produce from the lesser-quality garden bed, **as we do not say this.** **What is the reason** that Rabbi Yishmael does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rav Idi bar Avin? It is because he accepts the principle that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant;** without such proof, the owner of the animal pays only the value of a lesser-quality garden bed.

The *gemara* responds to this second question with the formulation *shaminu al pi sadeh acher* — one assesses the damage against a reference field. This introduces the *shishim* calculation, though the precise mechanics of that figure — sixty *se'ah*, sixty *tirkav*, and how the arithmetic works — remain somewhat involved. One reading was proposed by Rav Idi bar Avin: the case involves a situation where the animal ate from a patch positioned at the boundary between average and prize produce, and it is genuinely unknown whether it consumed from the inferior section or the superior one. In that situation of *safek*, the *Torah* tips the doubt in favor of the *nizak* and one pays as though the best were destroyed.

The *gemara* rejects this reading — *de-hachi lo amrinan* — on the grounds that it violates a fundamental principle: *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*, the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract money. The *gemara* is willing to accept a *chiddush* in the *Torah*, even an arbitrary ruling, so long as it does not collide with a general operative principle of law. A ruling that tips the *safek* in favor of the *nizak* would do precisely that, and while it is not impossible for the *Torah* to legislate such an outcome, the *gemara* prefers not to read Rabbi Yishmael that way.

### Future Value Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּמֵיטַב דִּלְקַמֵּיהּ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – כִּי הַיְאךְ דְּסָלֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Yishmael interprets the Torah's expression "the best of his field" as requiring payment **with the best**-quality land **before him, and what is this?** It is **that which** remained in the field and subsequently **sprouted,** and the compensation is based on the value of this.

The correct reading of *meitav sadehu*, the *gemara* concludes, is *be-meitav de-lekamei* — in its best state, meaning its future state when it will be ready for harvest. Rabbi Yishmael is thus aligned with Rabbi Yosi HaGelili: when unripe produce is destroyed, one pays based on what it will eventually be worth when brought to market. The phrase *meitav sadehu* refers not to the best-quality location within the field, but to the produce at its optimal future stage — when it will have reached full ripeness.

This reading is striking in its own right. The damage to the *mazik* consisted in the destruction of produce that at the moment of the act was unripe and had no straightforward market value, yet he is made to pay based on a significantly higher figure — the mature crop's market price. One might argue, however, that since there is genuinely no available method to put a dollar value on an acre of unripe grapes — no one purchases bare unripe produce; one either buys the land or the finished goods — the *Torah* directs us to the future value as the only workable mechanism of assessment. Even so, the question of fairness to the *mazik* remains, not least because no deduction is yet made for the labor, irrigation, and other costs that would have been required to bring the produce to that eventual state of ripeness.

## Ripening Stages

### R. Shimon: Pre-Emergence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָכְלָה לוּלְבֵי גְפָנִים וְיִחוּרֵי תְאֵנִים, אֲבָל אָכְלָה פַּגִּים אוֹ בוֹסֶר – רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ עֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: In what** case **is this statement,** that the court appraises the vineyard or group of trees that were damaged, **said?** It is **when** the animal **ate young branches [*lulevei gefanim*] of vines or shoots of fig** trees, **but** where **it ate unripe figs or unripe grapes,** the court **views them** and appraises them **as if** they were **grapes ready to be picked.**

The *gemara* returns to a *beraita* in which Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda rules in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *bameh devarim amurim she-achlah lulavei gepanim ve-ikhrei te'einim* — when does one pay based on the depreciation of the field? When the animal ate what amounted to budding vine-shoots or budding fig-shoots, meaning the fruit had not yet meaningfully emerged at all.

### Smader Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכְלָה סְמָדַר – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ הֵן עֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רוֹאִין כַּמָּה הָיְתָה יָפָה וְכַמָּה הִיא יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **ate** the grapes while they were in the **budding** stage, **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The court **views** the grapes that were damaged **as if they were grapes about to be picked,** and appraise the damage based on this. **And the Rabbis say:** The court **views how much** the vineyard **was worth** before the animal ate the grapes, **and how much it is worth** now.

This implies that once the produce reaches the stage of *smadar* — when the fruit has just barely begun to emerge and ripen — one shifts to the future-value assessment rather than the field-depreciation model. Rashi clarifies that *lulavei gepanim* describes the very first emergence of the fruit, while *smadar* denotes a slightly more advanced stage of early ripening, though still far from completion. So if the animal ate produce at the *smadar* stage, one presumably applies the principle of *ro'ina oso ke-ilu anavim omdos le-vatzer* — one views the grapes as though they were already ready to be harvested.

The *gemara* then probes the end of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda's statement: *achlah pagim o boser* — if the animal ate produce at the *pagim* (unripe figs) or *boser* (unripe grapes) stage. These terms describe a stage more advanced than *smadar*, closer though still not at full ripeness. At this stage, one pays as though the produce were already ready for harvest. The implied tension is that the *beraita* addressed two extremes — before any emergence and close to ripeness — without addressing the intermediate stage of *smadar*, the classic problem of the excluded middle.

### Ravina's Unification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, כְּרוֹךְ וּתְנִי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָכְלָה לוּלְבֵי גְפָנִים וְיִחוּרֵי תְאֵנִים, אֲבָל אָכְלָה סְמָדַר פַּגִּין אוֹ בוֹסֶר – רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ עֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Bind** them together **and teach** them as a single statement, as follows: **In what** case **is this statement said** that the court appraises how much the field was worth when the animal ate from it? It is said in a case **when** the animal **ate young branches of vines or shoots of fig trees. But** if it **ate** grapes in the **budding** stage, **unripe figs, or half-ripe grapes,** the court **views them** and appraises them **as if** they were **grapes ready to be picked.**

Ravina resolves the ambiguity: *krukh ve-tani* — wrap them all together. The field-depreciation model applies only when the animal ate before the fruit had meaningfully begun to emerge (*lulavei gepanim* and *ikhrei te'einim*). But once the produce has entered any stage of ripening — whether *smadar*, *pagim*, or *boser* — one applies the future-value model and treats it as though the grapes were already ready for harvest. The *smadar* stage, even though it precedes *pagim* and *boser*, belongs in the second category.

### Position Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ כְּחַשׁ גּוּפְנָא; וְלָא מְסַיְּימִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda is** identical to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua** with regard to grapes in the budding stage. Why would the mishna state their opinions separately? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to **the weakening of the vine.** A vine is weakened by the grapes growing on it, as they draw nutrients from the roots and branches. Now that the grapes have been eaten, the vine is no longer weakened. Therefore, according to one opinion, the court takes into consideration this reduction of the weakening of the vine, and that amount is deducted when calculating the damages, whereas according to the other opinion, the court does not take this into account. **But** their respective opinions are **not defined,** and it is not clear which *tanna* takes this weakening into account and which does not.

This leads the *gemara* to observe that if Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda places *smadar* in the second category, his position is identical to that of Rabbi Yehoshua, who appears at the very top of 59a: *achla smadar Rabbi Yehoshua omer ro'in otah ke-ilu anavim omdot le-vatzer* — if the animal ate at the *smadar* stage, Rabbi Yehoshua says one views the grapes as ready to be harvested. If we are incorporating *smadar* into the future-value category for Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda as well, then the two positions are identical. The *gemara* answers: *ika beinayhu kachash gufna* — there remains a point of difference between them, concerning the question of *kachash gufna*.

## Kachash Gufna

### Nutrient Depletion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מְסַיְּימִי וּמְסַיְּימִי! מַאן תַּנָּא דְּחָיֵישׁ לִכְחַשׁ גּוּפְנָא – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא: אוֹנֵס אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַצַּעַר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסּוֹפָהּ לְהִצְטַעֵר תַּחַת בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Their respective opinions **are certainly defined,** and it is possible to know which Sage held which opinion, since **who is the *tanna*** who **is concerned for the weakening of the vine? It is Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya:** With regard to the indemnity payments that **a rapist** must pay his victim, who had been a virgin, he **does not pay** compensation for **the pain** caused by the rape. This is **due to** the fact that **she will ultimately suffer** the same pain during her first act of sexual intercourse **with her husband** when she marries.

The issue of *kachash gufna* introduces a significant complication into the future-value framework. When one pays the *mazik* as though the produce were already ripe and ready for harvest, does one deduct from that payment the degree to which the process of ripening — specifically the drawing of nutrients from the vine — would have depreciated the vine itself? Rashi explains: *u-menakes min ha-tashlumim mah she-yachishu ha-anavim ba-gephanim im ya'amdu sham ad ha-batir* — one deducts from the payment what the grapes would have drained from the vines had they remained on them until harvest, since the grapes continuously draw on the vine's nutrients and leave the vine somewhat depleted. The *gemara* focuses specifically on this cost to the vine as the representative carrying cost of bringing the produce to market. While one might also imagine factoring in the costs of labor or other agricultural inputs, the *gemara* specifically identifies the depletion of the vine's nutrients — *kachash gufna* — as the operative consideration. The idea is that the payment for future value must be offset by the costs the *mazik* would have saved the owner from incurring.

### R. Shimon ben Menasya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה נִבְעֶלֶת בְּרָצוֹן לְנִבְעֶלֶת בְּאוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** The pain of a woman who **has intercourse willingly is not comparable to** the pain of a woman **who has intercourse by rape.**

The *gemara* notes that while one of the two positions — Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda or Rabbi Yehoshua — must account for *kachash gufna*, it is not immediately clear which one does. Abaye asserts that it can be identified: *mesaymi* — the positions can be sorted out.

To demonstrate which *tanna* is the one who factors in future costs to be deducted, Abaye invokes a *beraita*: *Rabbi Shimon Yehuda omer mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* — Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda rules here in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. And in a separate case we know Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's approach to deducting costs that would have arisen in the future.

### Rape Case Parallel

The *beraita* adduced is deeply challenging. A rapist (*ones*) is obligated under *halacha* to pay damages — including *nezek* assessed as the diminution in value of the woman as a slave-market commodity (comparing the price of a virgin to a non-virgin), and *boshet* for humiliation. What he does *not* pay, according to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, is *tzaar* — pain and suffering — and the stated rationale is *she-sofa le-hitztayer tachat ba'ala*: she would have suffered this same pain in any case when she eventually had marital relations with her husband, and therefore no additional *tzaar* payment is owed for the loss of virginity.

The *gemara* responds that the rabbis objected to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's position on *tzaar*: *eino domeh nivalet be-ratzon le-nivalet be-ones* — the pain of a willing participant is categorically different from the pain of rape. The objection is obviously and entirely correct, and this is the accepted position. The reason the *gemara* invokes this deeply disturbing case at all is not to endorse Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's reasoning about *tzaar*, but to demonstrate that he operates on the principle that one deducts from a damage payment any cost of a similar nature that would have been incurred in the future in any event. It is this principle — deducting future-arising costs from present damage payments — that links him to the *kachash gufna* question.

### Miscarriage Costs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נְכֵי חַיָּה. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: נְכֵי מְזוֹנוֹת. מַאן דְּאָמַר נְכֵי חַיָּה, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן נְכֵי מְזוֹנוֹת. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר נְכֵי מְזוֹנוֹת, אֲבָל נְכֵי חַיָּה – לָא, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִתְּתָא דִּידִי פְּקִיחָה הִיא, וְלָא מִבַּעְיָא חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning the *halakha* of one who injured a pregnant woman, thereby causing her to miscarry, for which the Torah holds him liable to pay her husband compensation. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The court appraises the compensation for the miscarried offspring and **deducts** the amount they would have paid for **a midwife.** Since she miscarried, the husband no longer has to pay for a midwife, so that is deducted from the compensation. **Ben Azzai says:** The court **deducts** the value of the extra **sustenance** the husband would have been required to provide for his pregnant wife. **The one who says** that the court appraises the compensation for the miscarried offspring **less** the amount they would have paid for **a midwife** would **all the more so** concede that the appraisal is **less** the value of the extra **sustenance. But the one who says** that the appraisal is **less** the value of the extra **sustenance** does **not** necessarily hold that the court appraises the compensation for the miscarried offspring **less** the money they would have paid for **a midwife, since** the husband **can say to** the assailant: **My wife is capable and does not require a midwife** to assist her when giving birth.

The *gemara* further extends the comparison. In the case of striking a pregnant woman and causing a miscarriage, the *Torah* mandates payment of *dmei vladot* — compensation for the loss of the unborn child. The question is how this sum is assessed. The reference point appears to be what an unborn child would have been worth on the slave market — or more precisely, the difference in value between a pregnant slave and a non-pregnant one — which amounts to compensating the husband for a future asset not yet realized. This is analogous to the unripe fruit scenario.

A dispute then emerges. Rabbi Yose says one deducts *nechi chaya* — the cost of the midwife. Ben Azzai says one deducts *nechi mezonot* — the cost of the additional food the woman would have consumed during pregnancy. The *gemara* establishes that whoever holds one must deduct midwife costs *a fortiori* would also require deducting food costs, since the food cost is more certain. But the reverse does not follow: Ben Azzai, who requires only deducting food costs, would not require deducting midwife costs, because the husband can credibly argue *itteta didi pekicha hi* — my wife is skilled and capable and would not have needed a midwife. An uncertain future cost that the husband might have avoided cannot be subtracted from what the *mazik* owes, whereas the cost of the extra food during pregnancy is a certainty.

## Palm Tree Assessment

### Aramean Palms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עֲבוּד עוֹבָדָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בְּשִׁשִּׁים. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שָׁמוּ דִּקְלָא אַגַּב קַטִּינָא דְאַרְעָא. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּדִקְלָא (דַ)אֲרַמָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to the discussion of the appraisal of compensation for damage, the Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, acted in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Naḥman** and appraised a damaged date palm **relative to** an area **sixty** times greater than the particular area where the tree was standing. **A different version** of this *halakha* is also stated: **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, appraised** the damage caused to **a date palm relative to** the assessment of **the patch of land** where the tree stood, i.e., how much it was worth with the tree and how much it was worth without it. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, with regard to an Aramean date palm.**

The *gemara* reports: Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua ruled in practice like Rav Nachman that even when it is an *adam ha-mazik* — a person who destroys a *dekel* — one assesses the damage not solely by the tree's intrinsic value but in relation to the field, employing a *shishim*-style method. An alternative version of this ruling states that they assessed the value of a palm tree *agav katina d'ara* — in conjunction with a small parcel of land — which may not require the precise *shishim* model but still grounds the assessment in the depreciation of the land rather than isolating the tree as an object. The underlying question is when one destroys something growing on land, does one look to the diminution in the land's value, or does one focus on the thing destroyed? The answer may depend on whether the growing object is perceived in the market as integral to the land or as possessing independent significance.

The *halacha* is stated: one rules like Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua for *dikla d'Armia* — an Aramean palm tree.

### Persian Palms Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא בְּדִקְלָא פָרְסָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Exilarch with regard to a Persian date palm.**

However, the *halacha* is like the Reish Galuta for *dikla d'Parsa* — a Persian palm tree. Rashi explains that a Persian palm tree is sufficiently *chashuv* — significant and imposing — that people do not perceive it as merely a feature of the surrounding land. When they see it, they focus on the tree itself as the primary object of value. Accordingly, one assesses the damage to the Persian palm directly as the value of the tree, not as the depreciation of the surrounding field. This recalls the earlier complaint that the Reish Galuta was *dan dina d'Parsa* — judging by the Persian standard — which now proves to have been correct in the case of the *dikla d'Parsa* specifically.

## Eliezer Zeira Story

### The Black Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלִיעֶזֶר זְעֵירָא הֲוָה סָיֵים מְסָאנֵי אוּכָּמֵי, וְקָאֵי בְּשׁוּקָא דִנְהַרְדְּעָא. אַשְׁכְּחוּהּ דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי מְסָאנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Eliezer Ze'eira** **was wearing black shoes,** unlike the Jewish custom of that time, **and standing in the market of Neharde'a.** Officials **of the house of the Exilarch found him and said to him: What is different** about you that causes you to wear **these shoes?**

The *gemara* introduces a short narrative that connects to the Reish Galuta and to the theme of assessing future value. Eliezer Zeira — the younger or smaller Eliezer — *hava sayem masanei uchmei*, was wearing black shoes. He was standing in the marketplace of Nehardea when members of the Reish Galuta's household came upon him. They asked why he was wearing these unusual shoes. Tosafot notes some difficulty here, since other *gemarot* suggest black shoes were in fact the common practice, and Tosafot therefore suggests that perhaps it was the combination of black shoes with black laces that was distinctive. Whatever the precise nature of the practice, it evidently carried symbolic weight.

### Mourning Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: דְּקָא מִאֲבִילְנָא אַיְּרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them:** I am wearing them **because I am in mourning over** the destruction of the Temple and **Jerusalem.**

Eliezer replied: *de-ka-m'aveilna Yerushalayim* — because I am in constant *aveilus* over the loss of Jerusalem. He was not formally an *avel* in the technical sense, but he maintained a constant state of mourning for the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Yuhara Accusation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַתְּ חֲשִׁיבַתְּ לְאִיתְאֲבוֹלֵי אַיְּרוּשָׁלַיִם? סְבוּר יוּהֲרָא הֲוָה, אַתְיוּהּ וְחַבְשׁוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: Are you** a man of such **importance to** publicly **mourn over Jerusalem? They thought that it was** simply **presumptuousness** on his part. Since he was acting against the prevalent Jewish custom, **they brought him** to the prison **and incarcerated him.**

The members of the Reish Galuta's household were unimpressed. *At chashivta l'is-avulei al Yerushalayim?* — You are so important that you walk around in perpetual mourning for Jerusalem? They regarded this as *yuhara* — religious pretentiousness, presenting oneself as a great *chasid* who is continuously distressed over the destruction. One might also speculate that displaying such conspicuous mourning for Jerusalem carried an implicit critique of the present political condition — an awkward posture before the household of the Reish Galuta, who served as the lay political head of the Jewish community in Babylonia. Whatever the precise reasoning, they threw him into prison.

### Dates Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אֲנָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אוֹ אַתּוּן בְּעוֹ מִינַּאי מִילְּתָא, אוֹ אֲנָא אִיבְעֵי מִינַּיְיכוּ מִילְּתָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בְּעִי אַתְּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַאי מַאן דְּקַץ כּוּפְרָא, מַאי מְשַׁלֵּם? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מְשַׁלֵּם דְּמֵי כוּפְרָא. וְהָא הֲווֹ תַּמְרֵי! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מְשַׁלֵּם דְּמֵי תַמְרֵי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְהָא לָאו תַּמְרֵי שְׁקַל מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Eliezer Ze'eira **said to them: I am a great man,** a scholar, and it is fitting for me to mourn publicly over the destruction of Jerusalem. **They said to him: How do we know** that you are a scholar? **He said to them: Either you ask of me a matter** of *halakha* and I will answer you, **or I will ask you a matter** of *halakha* and you will answer me. **They said to him: You ask.** **He said to them:** With regard to **one who cuts** a cluster of flowers on **the stem** of a date palm belonging to another, **what** is he required to **pay? They said to him: He pays the value of the** date **stem.** He said to them: **But** ultimately **they will become** ripe **dates,** which are worth more. **They said to him:** If so, **he pays the value of** the future **dates. He said to them: But he did not take** ripe **dates from** the other person, so how can the court obligate him to pay for damage that he did not cause?

From jail, Eliezer proposed to demonstrate his caliber as a *talmid chacham*: either they would pose him a *halacha* question, or he would pose one to them. They told him to ask. He asked: *hai man d'katatz kufra mai m'shalem* — if someone cuts down a *kufra*, an unripe date, what does he pay? They answered: *m'shalem dmei kufra* — he pays the value of an unripe date. As Rashi notes, that value is essentially nothing, since no one purchases unripe dates. Eliezer then pressed: *hava tamrei* — but it would have been a ripe date; the owner has lost what would eventually have been a mature fruit. They responded: fine, pay the value of a ripe date. He countered: *ve'ha lav tamrei shakil minei* — but he didn't take a ripe date from him. The full tension of the assessment problem is exposed: the owner lost something real, but what was taken was not yet worth very much, and what it would have been worth lies in the future.

### Shishim Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן אַתְּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּשִׁשִּׁים. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאן אָמַר כְּווֹתָיךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָא שְׁמוּאֵל חַי וּבֵית דִּינוֹ קַיָּים. שַׁדַּרוּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לְהוּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר לְכוּ בְּשִׁשִּׁים, וְשַׁבְקוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Eliezer Ze'eira: **You tell us** the correct appraisal for the date stem. **He said to them:** The court appraises the damage relative to a similar piece of land **sixty** times the size. **They said to him: Who says** an opinion **as you** do, so that you can prove you are correct? **He said to them: Shmuel is alive and his court exists;** you can ask him. **They sent** the question **before Shmuel,** together with the ruling of Eliezer Ze'eira. Shmuel **said to them: He is saying well to you,** because the *halakha* is as he says; the appraisal is **relative to** an area **sixty** times greater. Upon hearing this, the officials of the Exilarch realized that he was a great man **and they released him.**

They asked him to provide the answer. He replied: *bishishim* — one applies the *shishim* computation. Rashi explains: if one cut an entire acre of unripe dates, the field's value — which is assessed in part by the quality of its produce — reflects a portion of that future value, and the *shishim* method captures this. The precise mechanics of how *shishim* resolves the ripe/unripe tension for a single date are not spelled out fully, but the principle is that the field-based computation provides a coherent framework for valuing something not yet at its mature stage.

When they asked who confirms this ruling, he cited Shmuel: *hashmua chai uveit dino kayam* — Shmuel is alive and his *beis din* is in session; go ask him. They sent to Shmuel, who confirmed: *shappir ka'amar lachon, bishishim* — he spoke correctly, it is *shishim*. Eliezer was released. The story is a vivid one, though the connection between demonstrating *Torah* scholarship and the right to wear unusual shoes as a sign of mourning remains somewhat elusive. What is clear is the narrative's integration of the *shishim* theme and the broader question of valuing future produce.

## Complete Fruit Rule

### R. Shimon's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אָכְלָה פֵּירוֹת גְּמוּרִים כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָא דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשָּׁמִין עַל גַּב הַשָּׂדֶה, הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִידֵּי דִּצְרִיךְ לְשָׂדֶה; הָנֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא צְרִיכִי לְשָׂדֶה – בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna (55b) teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says:** If the animal **ate ripe produce,** the owner pays the value of the ripe produce eaten. **What is the reason** for Rabbi Shimon's opinion? **This that the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And it feed in another's field"** (Exodus 22:4), which **teaches that** the court **appraises** the damage **relative to** another **field, this statement** applies specifically with regard to **produce** that **requires a field** to grow. For one's animal eating this **produce, which do not require the field** in order to ripen further, the animal's owner **must pay** their value **as they are.**

The *gemara* returns to the final ruling in the *beraita*: Rabbi Shimon holds that *achal peirot gemurim* — if the animal ate fully ripened fruit ready for harvest — one assesses the damage not by the field-depreciation method but by treating the produce as already-harvested fruit. The field-valuation model applies only so long as the fruit has not yet reached full completion.

### Independence Rationale

The *gemara* asks for the rationale: *amar Rachmana uvi'er b'sadeh acher melamed she-shamin al gav sadeh* — the verse establishes that one assesses in relation to the field context. But this holds only for produce that still requires the ground — *midi d'tzarich l'sadeh* — produce still drawing on the field's nutrients in order to ripen. Once the fruit is fully ripe and no longer needs to draw on the field's resources, it is considered as though already detached from the ground — *be-einayhu ba'ei shulumi* — and one pays for it as for harvested produce, without reference to the field at all.

### Rav's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: דָּן רַב כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וּפְסַק הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya says** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: Rav judged** a practical *halakha* on a certain issue **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** despite the fact that in general the *halakha* is not in accordance with his opinion. **And** furthermore, **he ruled** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** even though in that case his was a minority opinion.

Rav Huna bar Chiya reports in the name of Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba that Rav once ruled in an actual case like Rabbi Meir — and separately that he stated in principle, to his students, that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon on the question of *peirot gemurim*.

### Ketuba Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּן רַב כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר – דְּתַנְיָא: כָּתַב לָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא חָתְמָה לוֹ, לַשֵּׁנִי וְחָתְמָה לוֹ – אִבְּדָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: נַחַת רוּחַ עָשִׂיתִי לְבַעְלִי, אַתֶּם מָה לָכֶם עָלַי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya explains: **Rav judged** a practical *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of a field designated by its owner as a lien for his wife's marriage contract, which he subsequently wants to sell, if **he wrote** a document of sale **to a first** buyer, **but** his wife **did not sign for him** to endorse the sale, and subsequently the husband wrote a document of sale **to a second** buyer, **and** his wife **signed for him, she** thereby **loses** the lien of **her marriage contract,** since the sale is effective and she can no longer collect from this field; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** that **she can say: I did** it only to **please my husband,** but I did not mean it and never intended to forgo my rights. **What** claim **do you,** the purchasers, **have against me?** Therefore, the lien is still in effect. Rav judged a case in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

The ruling *dan Rav ke-Rabbi Meir* is then elaborated through a *beraita*. A husband who wishes to sell a field over which his wife holds a *ketuba* lien would normally need her consent, expressed by her signing the *shtar mechira*. However, *halacha* assumes that a wife who signs a document transferring property at her husband's initiative may be doing so merely to please him, not out of genuine acquiescence, and therefore her rights are not thereby extinguished.

Rabbi Meir holds that if the husband wrote a deed to a first buyer and the wife refused to sign, and the husband then wrote a deed to a second buyer and she did sign, she has lost her lien — *avda ktuvata*. The fact that she refused to sign for the first and only agreed when a different buyer was proposed demonstrates that her signature here was genuine, not coerced deference. Rav ruled in a real case in accordance with this position. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: even in that scenario she may argue *nachat ruach asiti le-va'ali* — I only did it to give my husband peace of mind — and her lien remains intact.

## Stacking Grain

### No Permission Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּגְדִּישׁ בְּתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וַאֲכָלָתַן בְּהֶמְתּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who stacks** his produce **in another's field without permission** from the owner of that field, **and an animal belonging to the owner of the field eats** the produce, the owner of the field is **exempt.**

The *mishna* concludes the discussion of *shein* with a case that recapitulates themes encountered before. *Ha-magdish be-toch s'deh chaveiro shelo bi-reshus* — if one stacks grain in a neighbor's field without permission, and the owner of the field's animal then eats it, the owner is *patur*. This is straightforward: *shein* liability attaches only in *reshus ha-nizak*, the victim's domain. An animal eating in its own owner's field — which is effectively *reshus ha-mazik* — is certainly not *chayav*.

### Animal Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הוּזְּקָה בָּהֶן – בַּעַל הַגָּדִישׁ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the animal **is injured by** the produce, **the owner of the stack** is **liable.**

The *mishna* continues: *ve-im huzka va-hen* — if the animal was injured by the piled grain, then the owner of the grain pile is *chayav*. The grain pile, placed without permission on someone else's property, constitutes a hazard analogous to a *bor*, and the one who created it bears liability for resulting injuries.

### Permission Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הִגְדִּישׁ בִּרְשׁוּת – בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **But if he stacked** them in that field **with permission, the owner of the field** is **liable** for damage caused to the produce.

*Ve-im higdish bi-reshus* — if, however, he stacked the grain with the field owner's permission — *ba'al ha-sadeh chayav* — the field owner is now *chayav* if his animal eats it. The *chiddush* here requires explanation: granting permission does not by itself transform the field into *reshus ha-nizak*. Even in *reshus ha-rabim*, where a person has a full right to be present, *shein* liability does not apply. Why should permission to stack grain in one's field be different?

One approach within the *shein* framework would be to say that by granting permission the field owner has effectively leased that portion of the land to the grain owner, temporarily making it *reshus ha-nizak*. Another framing, which several students raised, is that the owner by granting permission accepted *shmira* — a duty to watch the produce — which itself generates the obligation.

### Threshing Floor Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא תְּנַן דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי? דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי, הָאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל עָלָיו בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לִשְׁמוֹר! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא – בְּנָטַר בֵּי דָרֵי עָסְקִינַן, דְּכֵיוָן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַיֵּיל וּגְדוֹשׁ״ – ״עַיֵּיל וְאֶנְטַר לָךְ״ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **that which we learned** in the mishna **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **As, if** the mishna were **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **doesn't he say:** A homeowner who gives another permission to bring items into his courtyard is not responsible for them **unless the homeowner accepts on himself to safeguard** them? The mishna does not mention any such acceptance of responsibility. **Rav Pappa said: Here we are dealing with the supervisor of the threshing floors,** to whom people would entrust their produce. **As, since the supervisor says** to the owner of the produce: **Bring** it **in and stack** it, it is as though he had said to him: **Bring** it **in and I will supervise** it **for you.**

The *gemara* raises the objection: this *mishna* would then contradict the view of Rabbi, who holds that one is not liable until one has explicitly accepted *shmira*. Rav Pappa resolves the difficulty: *hacha bi-natrei bei darei askinan* — here the *mishna* is speaking about the context of grain-watching arrangements at threshing floors, where it was standard practice for field owners to accept communal grain and store it for threshing. In that institutional context, *keivan de-amar lei ayyel ve-agdash*, once the owner says "come in and pile your grain," it is tantamount to saying "come in and I will watch it" — the acceptance of *shmira* is implicit in the nature of the arrangement. Rabbi's requirement of explicit acceptance is thus not violated; in the *bei darei* setting, the invitation itself constitutes the explicit acceptance.

## Fire Exemption

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה בְּיַד חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – פָּטוּר בְּדִינֵי אָדָם, וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. שִׁלַּח בְּיַד פִּקֵּחַ – הַפִּקֵּחַ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sends a fire,** i.e., places a burning object, **in the hand of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor** is **exempt** for any damage later caused by the fire **according to human laws but liable according to the laws of Heaven.** If **he sent** it **in the hand of a** halakhically **competent** person, **the** halakhically **competent** person is **liable,** not the one who sent him.

The *gemara* now transitions to the third major *av nezek* of this *perek*: *esh*, fire. The *mishna* states: *Ha-meshale'ach et ha-be'eirah be-yad cheresh shoteh ve-katan* — one who sends a fire — or sends it via an errand — in the hands of a *cheresh* (deaf-mute), *shoteh* (mentally incompetent individual), or *katan* (minor) — *patur be-dinei adam ve-chayav be-dinei shamayim* — is exempt in human courts but liable in the court of Heaven.

### Action Detachment

The question is why. These individuals are not *bnei da'at* in a full legal sense — they lack full legal responsibility. One might expect the sender to be treated as though he had introduced a force like *ru'ach metzuyah* that carried the fire to someone else's field, which would render him liable. The answer, the *gemara* implies, is that even though a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* lacks full legal competence, he still makes choices and acts. This is sufficient to sever the causal attribution from the sender: it becomes *their* fire, not the sender's fire. Since they cannot be held liable — being exempt from consequences — the result is an unjust outcome, which is addressed by the pronouncement of liability *be-dinei shamayim*. Notably, this ruling implicitly ascribes a form of agency to the *cheresh shoteh ve-katan*: they are not treated as a mere natural force analogous to wind. Their holding of the fire constitutes an act, which transfers ownership of the fire to them.

### Competent Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד פִּקֵּחַ – הַפִּקֵּחַ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If **he sent** it **in the hand of a** halakhically **competent** person, **the** halakhically **competent** person is **liable.**

*Shol'ach be-yad pikei'ach ha-pikei'ach chayav* — if one sends the fire through a legally competent adult, the adult is liable. The fact that the sender instructed him provides no defense: *divrei ha-rav ve-divrei ha-talmid, divrei mi shom'im* — when the master's command and the student's action conflict with law, whose words does one heed? The agent who carries out the unlawful instruction bears personal liability.

## Fire Spreading

### Wood Bringer Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הֵבִיא אֶת הָאוּר וְאֶחָד הֵבִיא אֶת הָעֵצִים – הַמֵּבִיא אֶת הָעֵצִים חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** person **brought the fire, and one** other person subsequently **brought the wood,** causing the fire to spread, **the one who brought the wood** is **liable** for any damage caused.

*Echad heivi et ha'or ve-echad heivi et ha'eitzim, ha-meivi et ha'eitzim chayav* — if one person brought a fire and another brought the wood, the one who brought the wood is liable. The fire existed before the wood arrived; it was the addition of the wood that made the blaze capable of spreading and causing damage.

### Fire Bringer Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הֵבִיא אֶת הָעֵצִים וְאֶחָד הֵבִיא אֶת הָאוּר – הַמֵּבִיא אֶת הָאוּר חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, if **one** person first **brought the wood, and** subsequently **one** other person **brought the fire, the one who brought the fire** is **liable,** since it was he who actually kindled the wood.

The *mishna* then reverses the order: *echad heivi et ha'eitzim ve-echad heivi et ha'or, ha-meivi et ha'or chayav* — if the wood was already there and one person came and set it alight, the fire-bringer is liable. The interesting question not fully addressed by the *mishna* is what happens in borderline cases: where the pre-existing fire was not entirely negligible and Michael's wood enhanced the blaze to a new level of danger, is there any shared liability, analogous to the case of digging a well nine *tefachim* deep and a second party completing it to ten? The *mishna* does not address these intermediate cases.

### Blower Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא אַחֵר וְלִיבָּה – הַמְלַבֶּה חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If **another came and fanned** the flame, and as a result the fire spread and caused damage, **the one who fanned it** is **liable,** since he is the proximate cause of the damage.

*Ba acher ve-libah, ha-melabeh chayav* — if a third party came and blew on an existing fire, the one who blew it into a larger flame is *chayav*. Again, the *mishna* does not specify whether this applies only when the fire could not have spread without the blowing, or whether the last actor to materially enhance the fire always absorbs all liability.

### Wind Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיבְּתָה הָרוּחַ – כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **the wind fanned** the flames, **all** the people involved **are exempt,** since none of them actually caused the damage.

The most challenging case: *libata ha-ru'ach, kulam pturim* — if the wind blew the fire and spread it, all parties are *patur*. This presents a difficulty: if the *ru'ach* was a *ru'ach metzuyah* — a foreseeable, naturally occurring wind — how can anyone be *patur*? The entire principle of *esh* liability is premised on the owner being responsible for foreseeable spreading of fire.

## Wind Foreseeability

### Substantive Fire

Tosafot raises precisely this question and offers a first interpretation: the case involves a *ru'ach metzuyah*, and the reason all are *patur* is that what was in place before the wind arrived was not yet a genuine *aish* capable of spreading. It was a small, nascent fire that would have extinguished itself. The wind transformed it into a spreading fire, making it the wind's fire rather than the originator's. Even though the wind was foreseeable, the sender cannot be liable because he never created a hazard in the first place — an *aish* is defined as a fire capable of spreading, and this was not that.

### Unforeseeable Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ גַּחֶלֶת וְלִיבָּהּ, אֲבָל מָסַר לוֹ שַׁלְהֶבֶת – חַיָּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? מַעֲשָׂיו קָא גָרְמוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish says in the name of Ḥizkiyya: They taught** that one who sends fire in the hand of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor is exempt **only when he conveyed to him** a glowing **coal and** one of these people **fanned** it himself and set it alight. **But** if one **conveyed a torch to** a deaf-mute, imbecile, or minor, the one who gave it to him is **liable. What is the reason** for this *halakha*? **The action** of the one who gave it to him directly **caused** the fire to spread.

Tosafot, however, rejects this first interpretation based on other evidence elsewhere in the *gemara*. Even if one started a fire too small to spread on its own, if a *ru'ach metzuyah* then spreads it, one is still *chayav* — it is still one's *aish*. Therefore, Tosafot concludes, the *mishna*'s case of *libata ha-ru'ach ve-kulam pturim* refers specifically to a *ru'ach she-eina metzuyah* — a wind that was unforeseeable and unusual. When an extraordinary wind unexpectedly converts a fire into a spreading blaze, one is indeed *patur*. But where the wind was of the ordinary, foreseeable variety, one remains *chayav* regardless of the initial size of the fire.

## Ember vs. Flame

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מָסַר לוֹ שַׁלְהֶבֶת – פָּטוּר, מַאי טַעְמָא? צְבָתָא דְחֵרֵשׁ גָּרְמָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if **he conveyed a torch to him,** he is **exempt. What is the reason?** It is the **tongs of the deaf-mute that caused** the damage, since torches do not cause fires on their own.

Reish Lakish said in the name of Chizkiya: *lo shanu ela she-masar lo gacheles ve-libah* — the exemption granted when fire is handed to a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* applies only when one handed them an ember (*gacheles*) and they blew it into a flame. In that case, the *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* truly takes ownership of the fire — they transform a dormant coal into a live flame, and so it is genuinely their fire.

### Force Transfer

*Aval masar lo shalheves, chayav* — but if one handed them a live flame, the sender is *chayav*. *Mai ta'ama? Masrah koach gorem lo* — because the sender's own force and action are the direct cause: the fire was already active and capable of spreading when he transferred it. This is analogous to the principle underlying *ru'ach metzuyah* — when you introduce an already-operative hazardous force, the intermediary does not break the chain of causation. Handing a live flame to a *cheresh* is close enough to directly setting the fire that the sender remains liable.

### R. Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu masar lo shalheves, patur* — even if one handed them a live flame, the sender is *patur*. 

### Holding Intervenes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שָׁמוּ דִּקְלָא אַגַּב קַטִּינָא דְאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** A different version of this *halakha* is also stated: **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, appraised** the damage caused to **a date palm relative to** the assessment of **the patch of land** where the tree stood.

*Mai ta'ama? Tzafta d'cheresh ka garim lei* — it is the holding of the *cheresh* that caused the fire to spread. Even where the fire was already live, the act of the *cheresh* — however limited his legal capacity — constitutes an intervening act sufficient to break the sender's causal chain. The *cheresh*'s physical possession and handling of the flame is what ultimately brings about the spread, and therefore the fire becomes attributed to him rather than to the sender.

## Asha Obligation

### Act vs. Property

The *gemara* connects this dispute to a deeper conceptual question: *asha mishum chitzav* versus *asha mishum mamono* — is fire liability grounded in the category of a personal harmful act (like an arrow shot from one's bow, *chitzav*), or is it a property-based obligation, akin to liability for one's ox or *bor*?

### Agent Competence

This underlying conceptual question directly bears on the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan. The more fire liability is conceptualized as a personal act — *asha mishum chitzav* — the more one requires a direct, uninterrupted causal connection between the sender and the resulting harm. The interposition of another agent, even a legally incompetent one, severs that connection and removes the sender's liability. The more fire liability is conceptualized as a property obligation — *asha mishum mamono* — the less important it is that the causal chain passes through another agent. One's *mammon* (property or force) caused damage, and that alone suffices for liability, regardless of who physically carried it to the point of impact. Thus Rabbi Yochanan, for whom even a live flame transferred to a *cheresh* results in *petur*, may be working from a *chitzav*-oriented model in which the *cheresh*'s holding constitutes a genuine intervention. Reish Lakish, who holds one liable for transferring a live flame, may be working from a *mammon*-oriented model or from the intuition that a live flame leaves too little meaningful intervening action for the *cheresh* to perform.

## Ember vs. Flame (II)

### Minimal Acceleration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* סִלְתָּא, שְׁרָגָא, דְּהָהוּא וַדַּאי מַעֲשֶׂה דִידֵיהּ גָּרְמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **of thin wood** and **a candle [*sheraga*], since** in **that** case **his** own **action,** i.e., that of the one who sent the flame, **definitely caused** the fire to spread.

Rabbi Yochanan's exemption, even in the case of a live flame, has a limit. *D'la mechayaves ela she-masar lo guza silta sharga* — one is only *chayav* when one hands the *cheresh* something that amounts to flax, combustible material, and a flame together — essentially a conflagration already fully prepared to burst forth, where the *cheresh* need do almost nothing for it to cause damage. In that case, *d'hu vadai ma'aseh didei garmi* — it is unquestionably one's own act that causes the harm. The term *ma'aseh didei* itself resonates with the *chitzav* conceptualization of *asha*: when the sender's act is so complete and decisive that the *cheresh* contributes nothing beyond minimal transport, the fire remains the sender's act in the fullest sense, and liability is unambiguous.
