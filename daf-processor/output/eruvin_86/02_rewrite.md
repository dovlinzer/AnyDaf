# Eruvin 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv & Shituf Placement

### Eruv vs Shituf

The daf begins, technically at the bottom of 85b, with a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel concerning the placement of an *eruv* and a *shituf*. The underlying framework is already established: an *eruv chatzerot* must be placed in a *bayit*, a house within the *chatzer*, while the *shituf mevo'ot* can — and according to Tosafot, must — be placed in the *chatzer* rather than in a *bayit*. What types of spaces qualify as a *bayit* is itself a subject of discussion in the *mishna*, where the *Tanna Kamma* holds that even a barn or storage shed constitutes a sufficient *bayit* for purposes of *eruv*, since it constitutes a form of edifice related to habitation, even if no one actively lives there.

### Bread as Eruv

Into this framework, Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel introduces a striking practical case: if a group of people — *bnei chaburah* — were reclining together at a meal on Friday afternoon, and *Shabbat* entered before they had established an *eruv*, the bread already on the table before them can serve as their *eruv*. The phrasing is significant: it does not seem to require that anyone formally acquire the bread on behalf of all participants, nor that anyone explicitly designate it *leshem eruv*. The simple reality of shared participation in the meal appears to be sufficient.

This raises interesting questions. First, does the bread need to be a whole loaf? Various *mishnayot* suggest that a full, unbroken loaf is required. Since *Shabbat* entered while they were already eating, it is possible the loaf had already been broken. One might resolve this by assuming the ruling follows the view that a whole loaf is not strictly required, or perhaps the consideration is that the loaf was whole at the moment *Shabbat* began — though this deserves further investigation in the *posekim*.

The *Gemara* adds: *ve'amri lah mishum shituf* — some say the same bread can count as a *shituf* for the *mavoi*. This formulation, rather than contradicting the first, sets the stage for Rava's resolution.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava clarifies that these two opinions are not in dispute: *velo pligi*. The difference is purely situational. If the group is dining inside the *bayit*, the bread counts as an *eruv chatzerot*, since the *eruv* must be located within a *bayit*. If they are eating outside in the *chatzer* — a picnic of sorts — it cannot qualify as an *eruv* (since it is not in a *bayit*) but it can qualify as a *shituf* for the *mavoi*. The resolution is elegant and preserves both formulations.

### Baraita Support

Abaye then tells Rava that a *tanya* supports this position: *eiruvi chatzerot bachatzer veshitufai mevo'ot bemavoi*. But this support is immediately complicated, because the *baraita* as stated seems to place the *eruv chatzerot* in the *chatzer* itself — which contradicts the *mishna* that invalidates an *eruv* placed even in a gatehouse (*beit sha'ar*), porch (*achsadra*), or balcony (*mirpeset*), places that fall short of a proper *bayit*. Certainly the *chatzer* itself does not qualify.

The *Gemara* therefore amends the *baraita*: *ela eima eiruvi chatzerot bebayit shebachatzer veshitufai mevo'ot bachatzer shebamavoi* — the *eruv chatzerot* goes in a *bayit* that belongs to the *chatzer*, and the *shituf mevo'ot* goes in a *chatzer* that opens into the *mavoi*. What begins as a seeming support turns out to require textual emendation, yet even after that emendation, the amended reading does provide genuine support for Rava's position.

The upshot of this whole discussion: an *eruv chatzerot* must be placed in a *bayit* — a genuine domicile or structure associated with habitation — that belongs to the *chatzer* in question. Whether the owner must be actively present, or whether absentee ownership also creates a *halachic* problem, is a separate question taken up in the next *mishna*.

## Tfisat Yad

### Tfisat Yad Doctrine

Rabbi Yehuda introduces a qualification of great practical importance: *im yesh sham tfisat yad* — if the owner retains a "holding of the hand" in the space. The *halacha* is this: if someone rents out a barn or apartment, the tenant would ordinarily be considered a new, independent presence in the *chatzer*, obligating his participation in the *eruv*. However, if the owner retains objects in that unit — maintains a *tfisat yad* there — the unit is considered to remain fundamentally the owner's domain even while it is occupied by another. Consequently, it does not constitute a separate *reshut* requiring independent participation in the *eruv*.

### Ben Bunyas Example

The *Gemara* illustrates this with the paradigm of Ben Bunyas — a wealthy individual identified by Rashi as someone who rented out all the houses in his *chatzer* but furnished each one, keeping his own equipment and objects in every unit. Because he retained a *tfisat yad* throughout, all the units remained, in effect, a single unified domain belonging to one owner. No *eruv* was required among them.

### Honoring Wealthy

The *Gemara* digresses around this figure. Ben Bunyas came before Rebbi, who instructed his attendants to clear a place of honor for him — *pnu makom l'ven me'ah maneh*, for a man of great wealth. A second, apparently more distinguished visitor arrived, and Rebbi similarly directed his servants to honor him. Rabbi Yishmael b'Rebbi Yosi then informed Rebbi that the first visitor's father, the patriarch Bunyas himself, possessed a thousand ships at sea and a thousand villages on dry land — far greater wealth than his son's appearance suggested. Rebbi's response was instructive: he told Rabbi Yishmael b'Rebbi Yosi to convey to the father that when he sends his son to visit, he should dress him in a manner befitting his station, so that he receives his proper honor.

The episode may initially appear to reflect an unbecoming preoccupation with wealth. The *Gemara* directly addresses this by noting that both Rebbi and Rabbi Akiva made a point of honoring the wealthy. The verse cited is *yeshav olam lifnei Elokim chesed v'emes man yintzruhu* — the world endures before God when *chesed* and *emes* sustain it. Rava bar Mari expounds that wealthy individuals who support society through philanthropy, employment, and material sustenance are worthy of recognition. The juxtaposition of *chesed v'emes* may also carry a deeper resonance: *chesed* represents material benevolence, while *emes* often refers to *Torah*. The implication is that rabbinic leadership should not cultivate disdain for wealth or for those who pursue it, particularly when those individuals sustain both the poor and the institutions of *Torah* study.

### Tfisat Yad Definition

Returning to the *halacha*, Rabbah bar bar Chana specifies what constitutes a *tfisat yad*: even something as seemingly minor as the spike of a plow left in a rented barn is sufficient to maintain the owner's presence there.

Rav Nachman then transmits a principle from the school of Shmuel that sharpens the definition considerably: *davar hanitil b'Shabbat osser, davar she'eino nitil b'Shabbat eino osser*. An object that can be moved on *Shabbat* does not constitute a *tfisat yad* — because it is entirely possible that the owner could come and retrieve it on *Shabbat* itself, meaning it creates no permanent, binding presence. An object that cannot be moved on *Shabbat*, however — whether because it is *muktzah machamat chisron kis* (set aside due to monetary concern), or *tevel* (untithed produce), or some other category of *muktzah* — does constitute a *tfisat yad*, because its presence there is effectively immovable on *Shabbat* and thereby establishes an ongoing claim. A *baraita* confirms this principle.

### Modern Applications

This *halacha* is of striking practical importance. In cities without an *eruv*, the question of whether one may carry within an apartment building, a hotel, or a cruise ship often turns on this very principle. All of these structures are in a sense the modern equivalent of a *chatzer* with multiple separate units opening onto a shared space — precisely the paradigm case of *eruv chatzerot*. Yet in nearly all of these scenarios, the owner retains a *tfisat yad* of one kind or another.

In a hotel or on a ship, the guest typically has no absolute right to a specific room; management can reassign the guest to a different room if necessary. This means the guest never truly possesses an independent *reshut* — the owner's claim over each room is not displaced. In an apartment building, the owner may retain the major appliances in each unit, which would typically qualify as items not movable on *Shabbat*, and thereby establish a *tfisat yad* throughout the building. The result is that in many such cases, the entire structure is treated as a single owner's domain, and the *eruv chatzerot* concern does not arise. There are extensive contemporary *teshuvot* on the application of these principles to modern settings.

## Presence Requirements

### Absent Owner Case

The next *mishna* addresses the question of what level of presence is required for an owner's house to count as a problematic, independent domain in a *chatzer*. The case: *hamaniach beiso v'halach lishbot b'ir acheret* — a person left his house and went to spend *Shabbat* in another city. Does his house in the *chatzer* still create a *halachic* obstacle requiring participation in the *eruv*?

### Rabbi Meir View

Rabbi Meir says: yes, regardless of whether the absent owner is Jewish or non-Jewish, his house is a problem — *harei zeh osser*. For Rabbi Meir, ownership of a domicile within the *chatzer* is sufficient to create an obligation; active presence is irrelevant.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *eino osser*. If the owner is not actively present, his house does not create a problem. Presence itself is the operative criterion.

### Rabbi Yosi View

Rabbi Yosi agrees in principle with Rabbi Yehuda but introduces a crucial distinction: *nochri osser, Yisrael eino osser*. A non-Jew who has left his house is still a problem, because a non-Jew might return on *Shabbat* — there is no *halachic* inhibition preventing him from traveling. His potential, imminent return keeps him effectively "present." A Jew, however, would not normally travel back on *Shabbat*, and therefore — even if he is technically within *techum* and could theoretically return — his return is sufficiently unusual that we do not consider him imminently present. The relevant standard, for Rabbi Yosi, is not physical presence but imminent presence.

### Rabbi Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon refines and extends Rabbi Yosi's position. He rules that even if a Jew did not go to another city but merely went to spend *Shabbat* with his married daughter in the same city, he is nevertheless not considered present in his home: *shekvar hisiyach milibo* — he has already mentally and practically disengaged from his house for *Shabbat*. He is spending *Shabbat* elsewhere; his original home is, for this *Shabbat*, effectively unoccupied. Physical proximity does not substitute for presence.

### Rav Ruling

Rav rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon*. Even a Jew who has merely gone a few blocks to his daughter's house for *Shabbat* is not considered present in his original dwelling.

However, Rav adds a qualification: *v'davka bito* — this applies specifically when he is staying with his daughter. If he is staying with his son, *lo* — we cannot be equally confident that he will not return. The *Gemara* captures this in a folk expression: if a male dog barks at you, you can still enter the house; if a female dog barks, you had better leave. The point is that the relationship with a son-in-law is typically more fraught than with a daughter-in-law, and a man spending *Shabbat* at his son's home might feel compelled to leave if friction with his daughter-in-law becomes acute, making his return to his own home a live possibility.

### Conceptual Discussion

This raises a broader conceptual question: what is the underlying logic of the entire *eruv* framework? Halachically, the *chatzer* and all of its constituent spaces already form a single *reshut hayachid* — it is all walled, it is all private domain. The Rabbinic requirement of *eruv chatzerot* is not a biblical necessity but a Rabbinic enactment, driven by the experiential reality that even within a single *reshut hayachid*, multiple separate households create a fragmented sense of domain. The *eruv* reestablishes the unified character of the space at a practical and experiential level.

Given this, it makes considerable sense that an owner who is simply not present for *Shabbat* does not generate the friction or the sense of competing, separate domains that the *eruv* was designed to address. He is not an outlier; his absence removes any experiential disruption. If one can establish the *eruv* once for the year — as is standard practice, using a preserved food item that will not spoil — then the question of re-establishing it weekly only arises in unusual circumstances, and can in any case be arranged communally, with one individual taking formal responsibility each week on behalf of the others.

## Well Division

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishna* now introduces a new topic with its own distinct geometry: *bor shebein shtei chatzerot* — a well situated between two courtyards. The *mishna* rules: you may not draw water from it on *Shabbat* unless a *mechitza* ten *tefachim* high has been constructed — *bein milmata bein mitoch ogno*, whether lower in the well or at its lip.

### Physical Scenario

The physical scenario requires some visualization. Two separate, fully walled courtyards share a common wall between them. That shared wall runs directly over a well — the wall passes above the well at ground level, but the well itself is below, and the wall does not extend down through the water. Each courtyard thus has access to the same well from its own side. The problem: drawing water from one side effectively draws water that is shared with the other courtyard, making it a transfer of objects across courtyard boundaries in the absence of a joint *eruv*. A *mechitza* within the well itself is therefore required.

### Below vs Above

The *mishna* speaks of the *mechitza* being *milmata* or *lemala*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reports that this is actually a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel: Beit Shammai requires it to be below (*milmata*) and Beit Hillel allows it to be above (*lemala*). But what do "below" and "above" actually mean within the well?

### Rav Huna Reading

Rav Huna explains: *lamata mamash* and *lemala mamash* — both positions refer to locations entirely within the well itself, not above ground level. "Below" means near the waterline; "above" means near the lip of the well. Both are inside the well, differing only in their elevation within it.

### Rav Yehuda Reading

Rav Yehuda reads differently: "below" (*lamata min hamayim*) means within the water itself, and "above" (*lemala min hamayim*) means above the waterline. Notably, what Rav Yehuda calls "above" corresponds roughly to what Rav Huna calls "below."

Rashi understands Rav Yehuda's "within the water" to mean a *mechitza* constructed from the very floor of the well upward through the water, protruding at the top. Tosafot modifies this: the *mechitza* need not go to the floor, but must be substantially within the water and protrude slightly above the surface.

### Water Mixture Challenge

A pointed question follows. Rabbah bar bar Chana asks Rava: granted that Rav Yehuda requires the *mechitza* to be within the water — but what does that accomplish? The water still flows on both sides of the wall; the water intermingles regardless of where the partition is placed. And conversely, if the water mixes anyway, what is the advantage of placing the *mechitza* within the water as opposed to above it?

### Protrusion Requirement

The answer invokes a tradition cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, which Rav Yehuda traces through to Rabbi Hiyya: *tzarich she-yiru rosho kanim l'mala min hamayim tefach* — the *mechitza* must protrude a *tefach* above the waterline so that it is visible. According to Rashi, this means the *mechitza* must run the entire height of the water column and emerge above it — an imposing structure that actually does prevent water from mixing. According to Tosafot, the requirement of protruding a *tefach* above the water is not about preventing mixing but about visual symbolism: the *mechitza* must be substantially in the water (to symbolize the division of the water) and must also be seen above the water (to serve as a visual marker). On this view, the water continues to mix in practice, but the partition is considered symbolically effective.

A parallel formulation, cited by Yaakov Karchina, holds that even the "above the water" position of Rav Yehuda requires the partition to dip a *tefach* into the water. The logic is consistent: a *mechitza* that floats entirely above the waterline without touching the water does not symbolize a division of the water at all; it must at minimum make contact with the water to discharge its symbolic function.

## Beam Division

### Pi Tikra Principle

The *Gemara* turns to an alternative means of dividing the well — one that, counterintuitively, turns out to be more permissive than a physical *mechitza*. The relevant principle is *pi tikra yored v'soteim* — the edge of a ceiling is viewed as descending and sealing the space below it. The irony is significant: ordinarily, a suspended wall that does not reach the ground is *halachically* invalid, because we do not apply *gud achit* (the principle of imagining the wall extending downward) once the gap exceeds three *tefachim*. Yet a ceiling — which is not a wall at all — is treated as generating virtual walls from its edges, precisely because its horizontal surface communicates an enclosure more powerfully than a partial wall does. This principle applies when the beam is at least four *tefachim* wide and has straight, smooth edges.

### Beam Alternative

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, rules: *kora arba mataret bamayim* — a beam four *tefachim* wide placed over the well allows both courtyards to draw water from the well. By *pi tikra*, the edges of the beam are viewed as extending downward through the well, effectively dividing it. Each side may draw water from beneath "its" edge of the beam.

### Bucket Confinement

An obvious practical objection arises: even if the virtual wall is *halachically* effective, a bucket lowered into the well will float freely across to the other side. There is no physical barrier to keep it in place. The *Gemara* responds: the rabbis established that a bucket does not travel more than four *tefachim* laterally from where it is lowered — and since the beam itself is four *tefachim* wide, the bucket will remain within the confines of the beam's virtual wall.

### Water Leniency

Still, the question remains: in what sense is the well "divided" when the water itself mingles freely below? We have already seen that even the *mechitza* opinions concede that water mixing is not a complete disqualification. The *Gemara* invokes a broader principle: *hikilu chachamim bamayim* — the rabbis were lenient with regard to water. Access to drinking water is a pressing need, and the rabbis calibrated their standards accordingly.

Tosafot understands this leniency as specific to the beam case: the standard for a beam (*pi tikra*) is simply less demanding than the standard for a *mechitza*, particularly with respect to whether the divider must actually penetrate the water. Rashi draws a broader *posekim* conclusion: since the beam clearly works without any contact with the water, the opinion that a *mechitza* must be placed in the water cannot be the normative ruling. We rule like the more lenient position — the *mechitza* need not reach the water. This has significant practical implications regarding the permissibility of structures built over bodies of water, a question directly engaged by Tosafot in reference to the practice of constructing outhouses over rivers in medieval France and Germany.

### Wall Sufficiency

Rav Yehuda then goes further: the wall that already runs *above* the well at ground level is itself sufficient — there is no need for any additional *mechitza* within the well at all. *Lo tehei mechitza gedolah min hakotel shebeineihem* — the dividing wall between the courtyards is already a greater *mechitza* than anything placed inside the well. The *Gemara* subsequently characterizes this position as aligning with the view of Rabbi Yosi regarding suspended walls, a connection explored in the following section.

## Suspended Walls

### Position Separation

Rabbah bar bar Chana, citing Rabbi Yochanan, equates Rav Yehuda's leniency about the wall above the well with the position of Rabbi Yosi: *Rav Yehuda b'shitas Rabbi Yosi amra* — because Rav Yehuda relies on a wall that is suspended above the well (not penetrating it at all), and Rabbi Yosi, in the context of *Sukkah*, permits suspended walls to extend their effect downward even without touching the ground. The *Gemara* therefore identifies Rav Yehuda's ruling as an application of Rabbi Yosi's broader principle.

The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: *v'lo hi* — these positions are not necessarily identical, and one does not necessarily commit to the other.

### Rabbinic vs Biblical

The distinction the *Gemara* draws is one of *halachic* domain and gravity. Rav Yehuda's leniency about the wall above the well operates in the realm of *eruv chatzerot*, which is entirely Rabbinic — the underlying space is already a *reshut hayachid* biblically, and the entire *eruv* framework is a Rabbinic enactment. One might be lenient with suspended walls in a context that is *derabanan*. Rabbi Yosi's leniency in *Sukkah*, on the other hand, involves the biblical *mitzvah* of *Sukkah* — yet at worst, an invalid *sukkah* violates a positive commandment (*issur assei*), which is categorically less severe. One cannot automatically extend either position to the other's domain. The *Gemara* therefore holds: Rav Yehuda's permissiveness about the well does not commit him to Rabbi Yosi's position about *Sukkah*, and Rabbi Yosi's position about *Sukkah* does not commit him to leniency in the realm of *hilchot Shabbat* — where the underlying transgressions, at the biblical level, carry the gravest penalties. Even if Rabbi Yosi believes suspended walls are genuinely effective, he might impose stricter standards in a *halachic* domain whose severity warrants additional caution.

### Tzippori Case

An objection is raised: we know of a famous incident in Tzippori — Rabbi Yosi's own city — in which sheets were spread over pillars to create an ad hoc enclosure on *Shabbat* itself, enabling a *Sefer Torah* to be transported. This would suggest that Rabbi Yosi did apply his leniency regarding suspended walls to *Shabbat*, not merely to *Sukkah*.

The *Gemara* responds: that incident was not performed on the authority of Rabbi Yosi himself but on the authority of his son, Rabbi Yishmael b'Rebbi Yosi. The son may have extended the father's ruling from *Sukkah* to *Shabbat* — but the father's own position in the domain of *Shabbat* remains unstated, and one cannot automatically attribute to him a leniency he never explicitly applied there.

## Suspended Walls (II)

### Sefer Torah Case

The full account of the Tzippori incident: Rav Dimi reports that on one occasion, the community forgot to bring the *Sefer Torah* to the *shul* before *Shabbat*, and it remained in a private house. The following day — on *Shabbat* itself — they spread sheets over pillars, creating an improvised enclosure, and carried the *Sefer Torah* to the *shul* and read from it. Rashi notes that the purpose of the enclosure was not simply to create a *reshut hayachid* out of a *karmelit*, but specifically to address an *eruv chatzerot* concern: by constructing a walled corridor between the house and the *shul*, they effectively blocked the access of other houses to the shared *chatzer* space, thereby circumventing the need for a formal *eruv chatzerot* for that corridor.

### Creation on Shabbat

This raises an immediate question: how could they erect *halachic* partitions on *Shabbat* at all? It is universally agreed that one may not construct a temporary *ohel arai* on *Shabbat*, and a fortiori one may not create a new *halachic mechitza* on *Shabbat* for the purpose of defining a new domain.

### Repositioning Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this: *matzau sadinim pursuim al amudin* — they found sheets already hanging on pillars. The structure was not newly created on *Shabbat*; it pre-existed in folded or compressed form, and they simply repositioned it — moved the pillars, spread the sheets — on *Shabbat*. There was no act of construction, only the repositioning of an already-existing *mechitza*. The resulting enclosure was *halachically* effective because it was pre-formed, not newly made.

## Lenient Authorities

### Rava Connection

Rava draws an explicit connection between two lenient authorities: Rav Yehuda — whose position about the wall over the well we have already examined — and Rabbi Hananya ben Akavya, presenting them as essentially aligned in their treatment of virtual or suspended walls over water on *Shabbat*.

### Platform Rule

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* to establish Rabbi Hananya ben Akavya's position. A *gezustra* — a platform or protruding structure — that extends over a body of water and measures four *amot* by four *amot* (24 *tefachim* by 24 *tefachim*) poses a question of how one may draw water from below it on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Hananya ben Akavya rules: one opens a hole of four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* exactly in the center of the platform and draws water through it.

### Virtual Walls

The geometric reasoning is as follows. If the platform is 24 *tefachim* wide and the opening is four *tefachim* in the center, then there are ten *tefachim* of platform on each side of the opening. Rabbi Hananya ben Akavya holds that those ten *tefachim* on each side are viewed as bending downward — creating four virtual walls, each ten *tefachim* high, descending from the edge of the platform to the water. This establishes a *reshut hayachid* at the water's surface, permitting one to draw water through the opening.

This is a remarkable leniency: no physical wall exists at all. The entire structure of the domain is virtual, constructed entirely from the platform's own edges extended downward by *halachic* imagination. Rava's identification of this position with Rav Yehuda's ruling about the wall over the well underscores the common thread: both authorities are prepared, at least in the realm of water access on *Shabbat* — a domain treated with Rabbinic leniency — to rely on virtual or constructively extended walls where a physical *mechitza* would normally be required.
