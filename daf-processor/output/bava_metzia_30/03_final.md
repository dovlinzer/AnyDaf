# Bava Metzia 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Object Maintenance

### Found Scrolls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳: מָצָא סְפָרִים – קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן אֶחָד לִשְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְאִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לִקְרוֹת – גּוֹלְלָן. אֲבָל לֹא יִלְמוֹד בָּהֶן בַּתְּחִילָּה, וְלֹא יִקְרָא אַחֵר עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **found scrolls, he reads them once in thirty days** in order to ventilate them and prevent mold. **And if he does not know** how **to read, he rolls** and unrolls **them** in order to ventilate them. **But he shall not study** passages **in them for the first time,** as he would leave the scroll exposed to the air for a lengthy period, thereby causing damage. **And another** person **shall not read** the scroll **with him,** as each might pull it closer to improve his vantage point, which could cause the scroll to tear.

The *mishnah* on 29b opens a new topic: the maintenance obligations that apply to found objects beyond animals. The *mishnah* states: *matza sefarim, korei bahen l'echad lishloshim yom* — if one finds scrolls, one should read from them once every thirty days in order to air them out and prevent mold or moth damage. *Ve'im eino yodeiah likros* — and if one does not know how to read, then *golelan*, one simply rolls them from beginning to end, opening them up to expose the parchment to air. Reading is a convenient means of airing them out, and one may derive the incidental benefit of reading along the way, but even someone who cannot read fulfills the obligation by scrolling through them.

*Aval lo yilmad bahem batchilah* — one may not, however, learn something entirely new from them. If one is studying unfamiliar material, one tends to linger too long in a single location, which risks wearing down the parchment in that spot and fails to serve the airing-out purpose. *Velo yikra acher imo* — nor should one read together with another person, as this risks a tugging contest that could tear the scroll.

One might also ask whether *sefarim* here refers exclusively to *sifrei kodesh* or to any type of book — all of which, in the ancient world, were written as scrolls. The question is a genuine one, though the *gemara*'s subsequent discussion, which applies identical parameters to a *sefer Torah*, will bear on how we understand the scope of this *mishnah*.

### Found Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא כְּסוּת – מְנַעֲרָהּ אֶחָד לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם וְשׁוֹטְחָהּ לְצׇרְכָּהּ, אֲבָל לֹא לִכְבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If one **found a garment, he shakes it once in thirty days, and he spreads it** out **for its sake,** to ventilate it, **but** he may **not** use it as a decoration **for his** own **prestige.**

*Matza kesus mena'arah echad lishloshim yom* — if one finds a garment, one shakes it out once every thirty days, again to prevent moth damage and the accumulation of dust. *Veshotechah l'tzarchah* — one may spread it out for its own needs, meaning for the purposes of its maintenance, *velo lichvodo* — but not for one's own honor. The example would be someone who thinks: I have guests arriving, this is a fine occasion to display that impressive garment I found. That is prohibited. Notably, with the *sefer*, incidental benefit to oneself was permitted as one scrolled it; with the garment, the *mishnah* appears to draw a distinction. The *gemara* will return to this contrast in detail.

### Silver & Copper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלֵי כֶסֶף וּכְלֵי נְחוֹשֶׁת – מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן לְצׇרְכָּן, אֲבָל לֹא לְשַׁחֲקָן. כְּלֵי זָהָב וּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית – לֹא יִגַּע בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיָּבוֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one found **silver vessels or copper vessels,** he **may use them for their** own **sake** to prevent tarnish and rust, **but** he may **not** use them **to** the extent that he will **erode them.** If he finds **gold vessels or glass vessels,** which are not ruined by neglect, **he may not touch them until Elijah will come** and identify the owner.

*Klei kesef u'klei nechoshet mishtamesh bahen l'tzarchan velo l'shachkan* — silver and copper vessels may be used to maintain them, but not in a way that wears them out. The *mishnah*'s language — permitting use rather than merely polishing or rinsing — raises an implicit question about whether incidental benefit to oneself is permitted alongside the maintenance purpose. The *mishnah* does not say merely to rinse or shine them; it says to use them. The implication is that modest use keeps them in good condition and that some degree of personal benefit in the process is countenanced, so long as the use is not destructive.

### Gold & Glass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלֵי זָהָב וּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית – לֹא יִגַּע בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיָּבוֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If he finds **gold vessels or glass vessels,** which are not ruined by neglect, **he may not touch them until Elijah will come** and identify the owner.

*Klei zahav u'klei zechuchit lo yiga bahen ad sheyavo Eliyahu* — gold and glass vessels one should not touch at all, *ad sheyavo Eliyahu* — until Elijah comes, meaning indefinitely. As Rashi explains, gold does not oxidize or rust and therefore requires no use-based maintenance, while glass is too easily broken. In the case of gold, use is simply unnecessary; in the case of glass, it is too dangerous.

### Dignity Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא שַׂק אוֹ קוּפָּה וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לִיטּוֹל – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִטּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** If a person **found a sack or a basket or any** other **item that** it **is not his** typical **manner to take** and carry because it is beneath his dignity, **he** shall **not take** it, as one need not demean himself in order to return a lost item.

*Matza sak o kuppah vechol davar she'ein darko litol, harei zeh lo yitol* — if one finds a sack, a box, or anything not normally taken, one need not take it. One might initially think this refers to *safek hinuach* — objects that may have been intentionally left — but the *gemara* will clarify that this is not the correct reading. *Ein darko litol* does not mean the object is likely to have been placed there intentionally; it means that retrieving it would be beneath one's dignity. This is an important independent principle, and the *gemara* will elaborate on it at length in the closing section of the *daf*.

## Found Tefillin

### Shmuel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמּוֹצֵא תְּפִילִּין בַּשּׁוּק – שָׁם דְּמֵיהֶן וּמַנִּיחָן לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says: One who finds phylacteries in the marketplace** and is in need of phylacteries **assesses their value and immediately places** the money aside for the owner.

*Amar Shmuel: hamotzei tefillin bashuk, shamin demihen u'manichan l'alter* — Shmuel rules that if one finds *tefillin* in the marketplace, one assesses their value, sets aside the money — effectively purchasing them from the absent owner — and may then put them on immediately. The question the *gemara* will immediately raise is: why can one not simply set them aside and maintain them as one would any other found object?

### Ravina's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבִינָא: מָצָא סְפָרִים – קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן אֶחָד לִשְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וְאִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לִקְרוֹת – גּוֹלְלָן. גּוֹלְלָן – אִין, שָׁם דְּמֵיהֶן וּמַנִּיחָן – לָא, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תְּפִילִּין בֵּי בַּר חָבוּ מִשְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי, סְפָרִים לָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raises an objection** from the mishna: If one **found scrolls, he reads them once in thirty days; and if he does not know** how **to read, he rolls** and unrolls **them.** Ravina infers: To **roll** and unroll **them, yes,** he may do so, but **assess their value and place** the money aside, **no,** he may not. **Abaye said:** There is a difference between phylacteries and scrolls. **Phylacteries are available at the house of bar Ḥavu,** where they are produced in large quantities, but **scrolls are not available,** as Torah scrolls are not easily obtained.

*Metiv Ravina*: the *mishnah* states that if one finds scrolls, one reads from them once every thirty days, and if one cannot read, one rolls them. The implication is that found objects must be maintained and held, not liquidated. Why should *tefillin* be different? Why is one permitted to assess the value, pay it over, and simply use or sell the *tefillin* rather than preserve them for the owner?

### Availability Factor

The passage above contains Abaye's answer already: *tefillin bar bei bar chavu mishkach shchichi* — *tefillin* are readily available everywhere. *Sefarim lo shchichi* — actual scrolls are not. This reflects a striking historical reality: before the age of printed books, even a modest collection of scrolls represented an enormous and nearly irreplaceable treasure. The Alexandrian library — housing thousands of scrolls of the ancient world — was rightly considered an incalculable loss when destroyed. *Tefillin*, by contrast, were worn by every Jewish male and were produced in large quantities, creating a robust and accessible market.

This raises a further question, though: even granting that *tefillin* are easily replaceable, what is the justification for essentially acquiring them rather than simply setting them aside? One consideration may be that *tefillin* left locked in a box serve no one, potentially for years, if the owner never returns to claim them. Another is that *tefillin* that can be assessed and replaced with cash present an almost interchangeable object — unlike a *sefer Torah* or a broken-in animal, which the owner may have a strong personal attachment to. If one follows the view of Rabbi Tarfon regarding the use of found objects, the money can be set aside and returned if the owner eventually appears, while in the meantime someone actually benefits from the *tefillin*. These considerations together appear to animate Shmuel's ruling, though the precise formal justification remains somewhat elusive.

## Sefer Torah Care

### No Re-lending

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׁוֹאֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ לְאַחֵר, פּוֹתְחוֹ וְקוֹרֵא בּוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִלְמוֹד בּוֹ בַּתְּחִילָּה, וְלֹא יִקְרָא אַחֵר עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who borrows a Torah scroll from another, that** person **may not lend it to another,** i.e., a third person. He **may open it and read it, provided that he does not study** passages **in it for the first time,** lest the scroll be exposed for a lengthy period of time and sustain damage. **And another** person **shall not read** the scroll **with him,** lest the scroll tear.

*Tanu rabbanan: hashoel sefer Torah mechaveiro, harei zeh lo yashilenu l'acher* — if one borrows a *sefer Torah*, one may not lend it to a third party. The *gemara* immediately notes that this seems obvious: *Reish Lakish* elsewhere establishes the general rule that *ein hashoel rashai lehashil* — a borrower may not lend out what he has borrowed — and likewise *ein hasocher rashai lehashkir* — one who rents may not sublet. So why is it necessary to state this rule specifically for a *sefer Torah*?

The answer is that the *sefer Torah* case is actually a *chiddush*. One might have thought that *nicha lei le'inish detei'aved mitzvah bemamonei* — a person is genuinely pleased when others perform a *mitzvah* using their property. If I lend someone my *sefer Torah*, I may well be happy for him to lend it further so that more people can learn *Torah*. Therefore, one might argue that the owner implicitly permits re-lending. *Ka mashma lan* — the ruling teaches us that we do not apply this logic here, because the concern for potential damage overrides the assumption of implicit permission.

This connects to a broader practical reality: books are frequently borrowed and never returned, or are damaged in transit through spilled liquids, torn pages, or broken bindings. Some people therefore inscribe inside their books *mutar lehishtamesh bimkomo* — one may use it in this place — granting permission for in-place use while withholding permission to remove it. The general default, absent such explicit permission, is that *sefarim* may not be used without authorization, even when standing right next to them.

### Reading Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פּוֹתְחוֹ וְקוֹרֵא בּוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִלְמוֹד בּוֹ בַּתְּחִילָּה, וְלֹא יִקְרָא אַחֵר עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** He **may open it and read it, provided that he does not study** passages **in it for the first time,** lest the scroll be exposed for a lengthy period of time and sustain damage. **And another** person **shall not read** the scroll **with him,** lest the scroll tear.

*Potcho v'korei bo* — one who borrows a *sefer Torah* obviously opens and reads from it, since that is the entire purpose of borrowing it. *U'vilvad shelo yilmad bo batchilah* — but one may not use it to learn material entirely new to oneself. This is noteworthy: it confirms that *sifrei Torah* were in practice used for direct study and learning, not merely for ceremonial or ritual reading. The restriction against learning new material from a borrowed *sefer Torah* parallels the restriction on found scrolls discussed in the *mishnah*, applying the same logic — lingering in one location risks wearing down the parchment. *Velo yikra acher imo* — and a second person may not read together with the borrower.

### Entrusted Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הַמַּפְקִיד סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ, גּוֹלְלוֹ כׇּל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, פּוֹתְחוֹ וְקוֹרֵא בּוֹ, אִם בִּשְׁבִילוֹ פְּתָחוֹ – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** in the case of **one who deposits a Torah scroll with another,** the bailee **rolls it every twelve months,** and he **may open it and read it. If** it is **for himself** that **he opened it,** it is **prohibited.**

*Vechen hamaphkid sefer Torah etzel chaveiro, golelo kol shneim asar chodesh* — if one entrusts a *sefer Torah* to another person for safekeeping, the *shomer* must roll it once every twelve months, which is notably less frequent than the once-every-thirty-days standard of the *mishnah* for a found scroll. The *gemara* then states: *potcho v'korei bo* — one may open it and read from it. This seems surprising: the *shomer* is merely watching the scroll; he has not borrowed it for the purpose of reading. Why is reading permitted?

The *gemara* notes the internal tension: the *shomer* is required to roll the scroll every twelve months for its maintenance, but then the *baraita* adds that one may open and read from it — which sounds like permission for personal use. Yet it also states explicitly, *im bishvilo patcho assur* — if one opens it fundamentally for one's own purposes, that is forbidden. *Ha amart poteach v'korei bo* — but you just said one may open and read from it!

The *gemara* resolves this: *hachi ka amar* — what the *baraita* means is that while one is in the process of rolling and caring for the scroll — when the fundamental motivation is the maintenance of the *sefer Torah* — one may incidentally read from it along the way. But if the primary reason for opening the scroll is one's own desire to read from it, that is *assur*. The operative distinction is between incidental benefit derived in the course of fulfilling one's maintenance obligation, and using the *pikadon* as a vehicle for one's own purposes.

### Airing Frequency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: בְּחָדָשׁ – שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, בְּיָשָׁן – שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Sumakhos says: In** the case of **a new** Torah scroll, one rolls it every **thirty days** because the ink is not yet dry and must be more frequently ventilated. By contrast, **in** the case of **an old** Torah scroll, one rolls it every **twelve months. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** In the case of **both this** new Torah scroll **and** the case of **that** old Torah scroll, one rolls it every **twelve months.**

*Sumchos omer: bechadash shloshim yom, beyashan shneim asar chodesh* — Sumchos holds that a new *sefer Torah* must be aired every thirty days, while an old one requires airing only every twelve months. *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer: echad zeh ve'echad zeh shneim asar chodesh* — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says twelve months in both cases.

The *gemara* objects: isn't Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov simply repeating the *tanna kamma*, who also said twelve months? The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov would then be identical, which is halakhically redundant. The *gemara* therefore reframes: *ela eima Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer echad zeh ve'echad zeh shloshim yom* — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov in fact holds thirty days for both new and old scrolls. This revision conveniently identifies Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov as the author of our *mishnah*, which prescribed a thirty-day interval without distinguishing between new and old scrolls.

The *gemara* then returns to the *mishnah*'s further restrictions — *aval lo yilmad bo batchilah velo yikra acher imo* — and notes that a *baraita* elaborates: one may not read a *parasha* twice, and one may not read a *parasha* and then translate it, since both practices involve dwelling too long in a single location and serve one's own purposes more than the scroll's maintenance needs. One might note that reading the *parasha* twice and then translating it corresponds to the practice of *shnayim mikra ve'echad targum* — reflecting how ancient this practice is.

### Page Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִפְתַּח בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה דַּפִּין, וְלֹא יִקְרְאוּ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם בְּכֶרֶךְ אֶחָד – הָא שְׁנַיִם קוֹרִין!
>
> **Translation:** One should **not open** it **more than three columns** at a time, since a wider opening increases the risk of tearing. **Three people shall not read in it** together **from one volume. But two people may read** it together, contrary to the ruling in the mishna.

*Velo yiftach bo yoter mi'gimmel dapin* — one should not open more than three parchment sections at once, since a wider opening increases the risk of tearing. The *baraita* also specifies that three people should not read from it simultaneously, while the *mishnah* had already excluded two people reading together. The *gemara* asks whether these rulings are in conflict.

*Amar Abaye lo kashya: kan b'inyan echad, kan b'shnei inyanim* — there is no contradiction: the prohibition applies when multiple readers are focused on the same location, which risks a tugging contest; if readers are positioned at different sections of the scroll, each focused on their own portion, the concern is reduced. As Rashi explains, two readers at different locations — one reading *Nitzavim*, one reading *Vayeilech* — are each focused on their respective passages and are not competing for the same stretch of parchment. But if all readers are looking at the same place, even two creates a dangerous dynamic.

## Garment Maintenance

### Over-Shaking Harm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּנִיעוּר מְעַלֵּי לַהּ? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גַּרְדִּי אוּמָּן בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, יְנַעֵר כְּסוּתוֹ בְּכׇל יוֹם! אָמְרִי: בְּכׇל יוֹם – קָשֵׁי לַהּ, אֶחָד לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם – מְעַלֵּי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that shaking** a garment **is beneficial for it? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** Only **one who has** access to **a skilled weaver [*gardi*] in his house may shake his garment every day,** as the weaver can replace the damaged garments with new ones. The Sages **say:** Shaking a garment **every day is harmful to it,** but shaking it **once in thirty days is beneficial for it.**

The *mishnah* states that a found garment should be shaken out every thirty days. *L'meimra d'nicha lei* — this seems to imply that shaking is beneficial. Yet *Rabbi Yochanan* offers a pointed, somewhat sardonic observation: *mi she-yesh lo gardi uman b'toch beito, yenachasenu b'chol yom* — if you have a master tailor living in your house, go ahead and shake your garment every single day. His point, of course, is the opposite: excessive shaking damages garments and causes them to tear. There is a parallel in the modern advice of dry cleaners, who may post reminders to clean clothes regularly — though in practice, over-cleaning is itself harmful.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent tension straightforwardly: *b'chol yom kashi lei, achat lishloshim yom ma'alei lei* — every day is harmful, but once a month actually preserves the garment in good condition. These are not contradictory positions but applications to different frequencies.

### Method of Shaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּחַד, וְהָא בִּתְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: It is **not difficult.** In **this** mishna, where the ruling is that shaking a garment is beneficial, the reference is to a case **where one** person shakes the garment. **And that** statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who rules that shaking the garment causes damage, is referring to a case **where two** people shake the garment.

*Iba'is eima lo kashya: ha b'chad v'ha b'trei* — alternatively, the difference is between one person shaking the garment and two people shaking it. One person shaking is less vigorous and maintains the garment without excessive stress; two people shaking together is more vigorous, which may clean it more effectively but risks damage to its fibers and longevity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בִּידָא, וְהָא בְּחוּטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: It is **not difficult.** In **this** mishna, where the ruling is that shaking a garment is beneficial, the reference is to a case where one shakes the garment **by hand. And that** statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who rules that shaking the garment causes damage, is referring to a case where one shakes the garment **with a stick.**

*Iba'is eima lo kashya: ha b'yada v'ha b'chutra* — yet another resolution: shaking with one's hand is less forceful and gentler on the garment, while beating it with a stick — as one sometimes sees with rugs — is more effective for cleaning but obviously more destructive over time. It bears noting that this entire discussion concerns shaking out dust rather than laundering; a garment hanging in storage does not become soiled in the way that would require washing, but it does require periodic airing to remove dust accumulation.

### Fabric Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בִּדְעַמְרָא, הָא בִּדְכִיתָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: It is **not difficult.** In **this** mishna, where the ruling is that shaking a garment is beneficial, the reference is to a case **where** one shakes a garment made **of wool. And that** statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who rules that shaking the garment causes damage, is referring to a case **where** one shakes a garment made **of linen.**

*Iba'is eima lo kashya: ha b'damra v'ha d'chitna* — yet another distinction: the material matters. Is it wool or is it flax? Rashi comments that wool, being a fabric that stretches, *she-nimtechet v'nikre'et*, is more susceptible to tearing. Whether or not one agrees with Rashi's assessment of relative durability, the *gemara*'s point is clear: the appropriate frequency and vigor of maintenance depends on the specific fabric's properties.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּסָא דְחָרָשִׁין וְלָא כָּסָא דְפוֹשְׁרִין. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת. אֲבָל בִּכְלֵי חֶרֶשׂ – לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וּבִכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא צְוִיץ, אֲבָל דִּצְוִיץ – לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא שְׁדָא בַּהּ צִיבַיָּא, אֲבָל שְׁדָא בֵּיהּ צִיבַיָּא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional statements by Rabbi Yoḥanan providing practical advice. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is preferable to drink from **a cup of witches and not** to drink from **a cup of lukewarm** water, which is extremely unhealthy. Rabbi Yoḥanan qualifies his statement: **We said this only with regard to** lukewarm water **in metal vessels, but in earthenware vessels we have no** problem **with it. And even in metal vessels, we said this only** in a case **where** the water had **not** been **boiled, but** if the water had been **boiled we have no** problem **with it. And we said** that lukewarm water is unhealthy **only** in a case where **one did not cast flavorings into** the water, **but if he cast flavorings into** the water **we have no** problem **with it.**

The *gemara* then appends a series of practical observations attributed to *Rabbi Yochanan*, offered in the same vein of practical wisdom about caring for possessions. *Rabbi Yochanan* observes that he would prefer a cup over which witchcraft had been performed to a cup of lukewarm water sitting in a metal vessel, since lukewarm water in metal may be harmful. This concern does not apply to pottery. Similarly, the problem is mitigated if something — Rashi suggests herbs — has been added to the water, or if it has been brought to a boil. *Rabbi Yochanan* also notes that if a father leaves one a great deal of money and one wishes to dissipate it rapidly, one should wear linen garments — specifically *chitna deromita*, Roman linen — use white glass vessels, and hire many workers without supervising them. The *gemara* specifies that the workers in question are ox-drivers, *betorei*, whose unsupervised work causes disproportionate losses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי שֶׁהִנִּיחַ לוֹ אָבִיו מָעוֹת הַרְבֵּה וְרוֹצָה לְאַבְּדָן, יִלְבַּשׁ בִּגְדֵי פִשְׁתָּן, וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית, וְיִשְׂכּוֹר פּוֹעֲלִים וְאַל יֵשֵׁב עִמָּהֶן. יִלְבַּשׁ בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן – בְּכִיתָּנָא רוֹמִיתָא. וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית – בְּזוּגִּיתָא חִיוָּרְתָּא. וְיִשְׂכּוֹר פּוֹעֲלִים וְאַל יֵשֵׁב עִמָּהֶן – תַּרְגּוּמַאּ בְּתוֹרֵי דִּנְפִישׁ פְּסֵידַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **one whose father bequeathed him a great deal of money and he seeks to lose it,** he **should wear linen garments, and should use glass vessels, and should hire laborers and not sit with them** to supervise. The Gemara elaborates: **He should wear linen garments;** this is stated **with regard to Roman linen,** which becomes tattered quickly. **He should use glass vessels;** this is stated **with regard to** expensive **white glass. And he should hire laborers and not sit with them;** this applies to laborers who work **with oxen, whose** potential for causing **damage is great** if they are not supervised, as they will trample the crops.

## Motivation & Benefit

### Dual Purpose Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹטְחָהּ לְצוֹרְכָּהּ אֲבָל לֹא לִכְבוֹדוֹ וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְצוֹרְכּוֹ וּלְצוֹרְכָּהּ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who found a lost garment **spreads it for its sake** to ventilate it, **but** may **not** use it as a decoration **for his** own **prestige. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one spreads it both **for his sake and for its sake, what** is the *halakha*?

The *mishnah* having established that a found garment may be spread out *l'tzarchah* — for its own maintenance needs — but not *lichvodo* — for one's own honor — the *gemara* now poses the question: *l'tzarchah ul'tzarcho mai?* What if one intends the spreading both for the garment's needs and for one's own benefit simultaneously? Is that permitted?

It is worth pausing to note how this question relates to the *sefer Torah* case discussed earlier. There, incidental reading while scrolling through the *sefer Torah* was permitted. Why might the garment case be different? *Tosafot* argues that this is not an absolute formal rule about whether one may ever derive incidental benefit from a maintained object. Rather, it is a practical concern: if one's motivation is partly personal, one may leave the garment out longer than its maintenance requires, rationalizing that it is still "for the garment's needs." With the *sefer Torah*, specific restrictions were already in place — no two readers, no new material, no reading it twice — that constrain the use and prevent overuse. With the garment, no such safeguards exist, and the concern is that mixed motivation leads to misuse. Importantly, one might add that the question is not only whether damage will result, but whether one's initial engagement with the object is fundamentally motivated by personal interest. When one begins from personal motivation, even a use framed as maintenance may really be self-serving — and it is notoriously difficult to disentangle one's actual motivations from post-hoc rationalizations.

### Tosafot Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שׁוֹטְחָהּ לְצוֹרְכָּהּ״, לְצוֹרְכָּהּ – אִין. הָא לְצוֹרְכּוֹ וּלְצוֹרְכָּהּ לָא! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״אֲבָל לֹא לִכְבוֹדוֹ״, לִכְבוֹדוֹ הוּא דְּלָא, הָא לְצוֹרְכָּהּ וּלְצוֹרְכּוֹ – שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the mishna: **He spreads it for its sake.** The Gemara infers: **For its sake, yes,** he spreads it, **but** both **for his sake and for its sake,** he may **not** do so. The Gemara rejects the proof: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **But not for his** own **prestige.** The Gemara infers: It is **for his prestige** alone **that** he may **not** spread it, **but for its sake and for his sake,** one may **well** do so. **Rather, no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna, as there are conflicting inferences from the first clause and the latter clause.

*Tosafot* frames the question through the lens of *shomer* law: the finder is conceptualized as a *shomer*, and the question of whether he may derive incidental benefit while fulfilling his obligation is the operative issue. The *gemara* then attempts to resolve the question from the *mishnah* itself: *l'tzarchah in* — the *mishnah* says "for its needs," implying that this is the only permissible motivation. *Ha l'tzarchah ul'tzarcho lo* — mixed motivation would therefore be excluded.

But *eima seifa* — look at the end of the *mishnah*: *aval lo lichvodo* — but not for one's honor. The implication of the *seifa* is that the prohibition applies when the motivation is exclusively personal. *Lichvodo d'lo* — only that is forbidden, while *l'tzarchah ul'tzarcho shapir dami* — a mixed motivation would be fine. The two halves of the *mishnah* thus appear to contradict each other.

*Ela meha lika l'mishma mina* — the *gemara* concludes that no inference can be drawn from this *mishnah*, because the *mishnah* typically articulates the two extreme cases and leaves the middle ground unaddressed. This is a classic feature of *mishnaic* formulation: the *mishnah* states A and not-B without speaking to the intermediate cases, so one cannot infer the rule for those cases from either half of the *mishnah* alone.

### Guests Arriving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא יִשְׁטָחֶנָּה לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מַגּוֹד לְצׇרְכּוֹ, אֲבָל יִשְׁטָחֶנָּה עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה וְעַל גַּבֵּי מַגּוֹד לְצׇרְכָּהּ. נִזְדַּמְּנוּ לוֹ אוֹרְחִים – לֹא יִשְׁטָחֶנָּה לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מַגּוֹד, בֵּין לְצוֹרְכּוֹ בֵּין לְצוֹרְכָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If one finds a lost garment, **he may not spread it on a bed or on a hanger for his sake, but he may spread it on a bed or on a hanger for its sake.** If **guests happened** to visit, **he may not spread it, neither on a bed nor on a hanger** and **neither for his sake nor for its sake.** Apparently, it is prohibited to spread it for both his sake, to enhance his prestige before his guests, and for its sake.

A further *ta shma* presents the relevant rule more directly: one may not spread the garment on a bed or on a frame *letzarcho* — for one's own honor — but one may do so *l'tzarchah* — for the garment's needs. Again, only the two extremes are stated.

The *baraita* then adds: *nizdamnu lo orchin* — if guests happen to arrive, *lo yishtachena lo al gabi mita velo al gabi magod, bein letzarcho bein letzarcha* — one may not spread it out on a bed or on a frame, whether for one's own needs or for the garment's needs. This appears to be precisely the case of mixed motivation — one sincerely believes one is doing it for the garment's sake, yet guests are present, and therefore one may not do it at all. This would seem to establish that mixed motivation — *letzarcho uletzarcha* — is indeed forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִקְלָא קָלֵי לַהּ אִי מִשּׁוּם עֵינָא, אִי מִשּׁוּם גַּנָּבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: It **is different there, as** spreading it before his guests is tantamount to **burning it, either due to the** evil **eye** that will result, **or due to thieves,** as once the guests are aware of the valuable item in his possession they may be tempted to steal it.

The *gemara* rejects this inference, however: *shani hatam demikla kali lah* — the guest scenario is different, because spreading out the garment when guests are present is effectively destroying it. *Ei mishum eina* — either because the guests may cast an *ayin hara* on it, or *ei mishum ganvei* — from a more prosaic perspective, one is advertising its value and inviting theft. One might add that even setting aside *ayin hara*, a house full of guests increases the practical risk that someone will spill something on it or handle it carelessly. The prohibition in the guest scenario thus rests not on the mixing of motivations but on the specific danger that the circumstances create. Accordingly, nothing can be inferred from this case about the general rule of *letzarcho uletzarcha*.

### Parah Adumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִכְנִיסָה לְרִבְקָה וְדָשָׁה – כְּשֵׁירָה. בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּינַק וְתָדוּשׁ – פְּסוּלָה. וְהָא הָכָא דִּלְצוֹרְכּוֹ וּלְצוֹרְכָּהּ הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי פְּסוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If **one introduced** a calf **into a yoke** [***lirvaka***] so that it would suckle, **and it threshed** with the cows, **it is fit** for use in the ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken, because the owner did not intend for it to perform labor. But if the owner introduces it **so that it will suckle and it will thresh, it is unfit** for use in that ritual because his intent is for the calf to perform labor, and the intentional performance of labor disqualifies it. **And here, isn't** the placement of the calf in the yoke **for** both **his sake,** threshing, **and for its sake,** suckling, **and** the *baraita* **teaches** that the calf **is unfit?** Apparently, it is prohibited for one who found a lost garment to spread it for both his sake and for its sake.

A final *ta shma* addresses the question through an analogy. If one placed an animal intended for *parah adumah* or *egla arufa* into a barn, and while there it incidentally threshed grain, the animal remains *kshera* — valid — because one did not intend for it to do work. *Bishvil shetinak vetadush* — but if one placed it there specifically so that it should nurse from its mother and also thresh grain while doing so, it is *psula* — invalid. In the latter case, one desired both the animal's own need (nursing) and one's personal interest (threshing), and this dual motivation invalidates it. The *gemara* draws the analogy: here too, a mixed motivation of *letzarcho uletzarcha* would be problematic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא עֻבַּד בָּהּ״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof by citing a verse written with regard to the heifer whose neck is broken. It **is different there, as the verse states:** "The Elders of that city shall take a heifer of the herd **that has not been worked with** and that has not pulled a yoke" (Deuteronomy 21:3), indicating that the heifer is rendered unfit **in any case** of labor performed. Therefore, no conclusion can be drawn with regard to spreading the garment.

The *gemara* immediately deflects: *shani hatam de-amar kra asher lo uvad bah, mi-kol makom* — the *parah adumah* case is governed by the specific scriptural formulation, which prohibits any work having been done, regardless of motivation or circumstance. The standard there is distinct from the question of care for an *aveida*.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *parah adumah* standard is truly that absolute — excluding even fully incidental work — then why is the case where the animal incidentally threshes while being placed for another reason valid? The *gemara* answers that this is comparable to a *mishnah* about *parah adumah*: *shakhan aleha of, kshera* — if a bird rests on the cow, it is valid; *ala aleha zakhar, psula* — if a male mounted it, it is invalid. In both cases the work occurred without one's direct action. The difference lies in *Rav Pappa*'s principle: the verse is written *avad* (active, "one worked it") but read *uvad* (passive, "it was worked"). The dual formulation yields a combined standard: what invalidates is work that happened by itself — *mi-meila* — but that one would have been *nicha lei*, pleased to have happen.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״עָבַד״ וְקָרֵינַן ״עוּבַּד״ – בָּעֵינַן ״עוּבַּד״ דּוּמְיָא דְּ״עָבַד״. מָה ״עָבַד״ דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ, אַף ״עוּבַּד״ דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Now that** the word **is written** without an additional letter *vav* as ***avad* and we vocalize** the word with an additional letter *vav*, as ***ubbad*,** in order to render the heifer unfit **we require** the situation described by the word ***ubbad*** be **similar to** the situation described by the word ***avad*. Just as** the word ***avad*** indicates **that** the owner **is amenable** to the performance of that labor, **so too,** the word ***ubbad*** means **that** the owner **is amenable** to the performance of that labor. Since the owner is amenable to the heifer mating, the heifer is rendered unfit. So too, in the first clause of the *baraita*: If one introduced a calf into a yoke so that it may suckle, and it threshed with the cows, it is fit for use in the ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken, because the owner is not amenable to its performing labor.

*Ma avad de-nicha lei, af uvad de-nicha lei* — just as direct work presupposes something desired, so too passive work invalidates only if one would have been pleased by it. One would generally want one's cattle to copulate and produce offspring — so a male mounting the *parah adumah* invalidates even though one did not cause it. But one generally does not particularly care whether one specific animal threshes or not when other animals are threshing anyway, so incidental threshing does not invalidate.

*Tosafot* raises an obvious question: if the animal is being designated as a *parah adumah*, surely one would *not* want it to copulate, since this would destroy its status and its considerable value. How can one say one is *nicha lei*? *Tosafot* answers that we do not measure *nicha lei* based on the animal's specific status as a *parah adumah*; rather, we measure it by whether, under normal circumstances, this would be a desirable outcome. Since copulation is ordinarily desirable for cattle, it is treated as *nicha lei* regardless of the particular circumstances. This parallels discussions in the context of *Shabbat* about *psik reisha de-lo nicha lei* — where something definitely happens as a consequence of one's action but one is not pleased by the result. Some *poskim* have attempted to argue that various *Shabbat* violations fall into the category of *lo nicha lei* on the grounds that one would prefer the violation not occur — but as *Tosafot*'s reasoning here illustrates, the standard is what one would generally prefer under normal conditions, not what one would prefer given the halakhic consequences of the outcome.

## Vessel Maintenance

### Wooden Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצֵא כְּלֵי עֵץ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא יֵרָקְבוּ, כְּלֵי נְחוֹשֶׁת מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּחַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁחִיקָן. כְּלֵי כֶּסֶף מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּצוֹנֵן, אֲבָל לֹא בְּחַמִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁחִירָן. מַגְרֵיפוֹת וְקַרְדּוּמּוֹת מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּרַךְ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּקָשֶׁה מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּפְחִיתָן. כְּלֵי זָהָב וּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית לֹא יִגַּע בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one found **silver vessels or copper vessels,** he **may use them** for their own sake; and the same *halakha* applies to other vessels. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who finds wooden vessels uses them, so that they will not deteriorate** due to lack of use. If one found **copper vessels** he **uses them with hot** water, **but not** directly **on the fire, due to** the fact **that it erodes them.** If one found **silver vessels he uses them with cold** water, **but not with hot** water, **because it tarnishes them.** If one found **rakes or axes,** he **may use them with soft** substances **but not with hard** substances **because** using them with those substances **damages them.** If one found **gold vessels or glass vessels,** which do not deteriorate due to lack of use, **he may not touch them until Elijah will come** and identify the owner.

*Hamotzei klei eitz, mishtamesh bahen kedei shelo yirkavu* — if one finds wooden vessels, one uses them to prevent rotting. As with the *mishnah*'s general approach to vessel maintenance, the prescribed method is actual use rather than merely storing the items or polishing them.

### Copper Vessels

The passage above includes the rulings on copper vessels: *Klei nechoshet mishtamesh bahen be-chamin aval lo al yedei ha-ur* — copper vessels may be used with hot liquids or food but not placed directly over an open flame, since the direct heat would blacken and oxidize them. The best maintenance for copper is use with hot contents, but not the kind of vigorous, direct-flame use one might employ when cooking.

### Silver Vessels

The passage above also includes the rulings on silver vessels: *Klei kesef mishtamesh bahen be-tzonen, aval lo be-chamin, mipnei she-mashchiran* — silver vessels may only be used with cold water, not hot, since hot liquids cause them to blacken. Both copper and silver tarnish through oxidation under normal exposure to air; polishing removes a small layer of the metal itself, which makes periodic polishing a somewhat destructive form of maintenance. The *gemara*'s prescription of use according to these parameters thus reflects an attempt to keep the vessels functional while minimizing deterioration.

### Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַגְרֵיפוֹת וְקַרְדּוּמּוֹת מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בְּרַךְ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּקָשֶׁה מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּפְחִיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** If one found **rakes or axes,** he **may use them with soft** substances **but not with hard** substances **because** using them with those substances **damages them.**

*Magrefot ve-kardumos mishtamesh bahen be-rach aval lo be-kasha, mipnei she-pochasan* — shovels and picks may be used for soft tasks, such as shoveling ashes, but not for hard tasks such as breaking up logs, since heavy use damages them. *Tosafot* notes a general question running through all of these cases: why does maintenance require use at all? Why not simply polish or clean the objects? The answer, as *Tosafot* frames it, is that appropriate use actually keeps these objects in serviceable condition — as long as it falls within the parameters specified — whereas improper use or aggressive cleaning might itself cause damage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָמְרוּ בַּאֲבֵידָה כָּךְ אָמְרוּ בְּפִקָּדוֹן. פִּקָּדוֹן מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בְּפִקָּדוֹן שֶׁהָלְכוּ בַּעֲלֵיהֶן לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: In **the manner that** the Sages **said with regard to a lost item, so they said** with regard to **a deposit.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the bailee **doing with a deposit;** i.e., the owner should tend to his own item, why is the bailee using it at all? The Gemara answers: **Rav Adda bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Sheshet said:** It is referring **to a deposit whose owner went to a country overseas.** Therefore, it is incumbent upon the bailee to tend to the deposit until his return.

The *gemara* then turns back to gold and glass vessels: *klei zahav u'klei zechuchit lo yiga bahen ad she-yavo Eliyahu. Kederech she-amru ba-aveidah kach amru be-pikadon* — the same standards that apply to a found object apply equally to an entrusted object. A question arises: if the owner is present and could use the vessels himself, what business does the *shomer* have using them at all? *Amar Rav Huna bar Chama amar Rav Sheshet*: the rule of *pikadon* applies specifically when the owners have gone overseas — *she-halchu ba'aleihem li-medinas ha-yam* — and are therefore unavailable. In their absence, the *shomer* bears the maintenance obligation.

## When to Abandon

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא שַׂק אוֹ קוּפָּה [וְכׇל דָּבָר] שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לִיטּוֹל – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִטּוֹל. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִתְעַלַּמְתָּ״, פְּעָמִים שֶׁאַתָּה מִתְעַלֵּם וּפְעָמִים שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מִתְעַלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If a person **found a sack or a basket or any** other **item that** it **is not his** typical **manner to take** and carry because it is beneath his dignity, **he** shall **not take** it. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated with regard to the return of a lost item: "You shall not see your brother's ox or his sheep wandering **and disregard** them; you shall return them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1). The *tanna* explains that the phrase "and disregard them" means that there are **occasions** in **which you may disregard** lost items **and** there are **occasions** in **which you may not disregard** them.

Having established that one who finds *matza sak o kuppah she-ein darko litol, harei zeh lo yitol* — a sack or box that it is not customary to take — need not retrieve it, the *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — from where is this derived? *Detanu rabbanan*: the verse in Deuteronomy asks rhetorically, *ki tireh et shor achicha o et seio nidachim vehitalamta mehem* — when you see your brother's ox or sheep going astray, can you ignore them? — and answers *hashev teshivem*, you must return them. But the *gemara* reads the phrase *vehitalamta mehem* non-rhetorically: there are circumstances in which one *may* hide oneself from the obligation, *pe'amim she'ata mitalem* — and circumstances in which one may not. Three such circumstances are identified: *haya kohen ve-hi be-veit hakvarot* — if one is a *kohen* and the object is in a cemetery; *o she-haya zaken ve-eino lefi kevodo* — if one is an elderly or distinguished person and retrieving the object would be beneath one's dignity; and *o she-hayta melachto meruba mi-shel chaveiro* — if the income one would forfeit by retrieving the object exceeds the value of the object itself.

### Cost Consideration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? הָיָה כֹּהֵן וְהִיא בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה זָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה מְלָאכָה שֶׁלּוֹ מְרוּבָּה מִשֶּׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר: ״וְהִתְעַלַּמְתָּ מֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **How so;** under what circumstances may one disregard a lost item? One may do so in a case where **he was a priest and** the lost item **is in the graveyard** (Leviticus 21:1–4), **or** where **he was an elderly person and it is not in keeping with his dignity** to tend to the item, **or** where the value of **his labor was greater than** the value of the lost item of **the other** person, i.e., if the finder was to return the item, reimbursing him for his lost wages would cost more than the value of the item; **therefore, it is stated: "And disregard them."**

The loss-of-income exception — *leshelo umishelo shel chaveiro* — is derivable from a *drasha* of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* on the verse *effes ki lo yihyeh becha evyon*: the principle is *shelcha kodem leshel kol adam* — your own interests take precedence over those of others. This is a variation of *chayecha kodmin lechayei achicha* — your own life comes first — one of the foundational ethical principles of the Torah. It does not mean that all of one's wants override all of others' needs, but that one's basic welfare takes priority. In the monetary sphere specifically, one is not obligated to sustain a loss that exceeds what the other person would gain. These *mamonot* considerations carry a kind of built-in fungibility — money lost can, in principle, be reimbursed or replaced, in a way that a ritual violation cannot be undone. Therefore the calculus of financial tradeoffs is permitted in ways that it would not be in the ritual sphere.

### Kohen Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא? אִילֵימָא לְכֹהֵן וְהִיא בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת – פְּשִׁיטָא, הַאי עֲשֵׂה וְהַאי לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה, וְלָא אָתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְדָחֵי אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה, וְתוּ: לָא דָּחֵינַן אִיסּוּרָא מִקַּמֵּי מָמוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** case **was a verse necessary** to derive that one may disregard a lost item? **If we say** that the verse is necessary **for** the case of **a priest and** the lost item **in the graveyard,** it is **obvious** that he need not return the item, as **this** obligation to return the lost item **is a positive mitzva:** "You shall return them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1), **and that** entry of a priest into a graveyard is prohibited by both **a prohibition:** "To the dead among his people he shall not defile himself" (Leviticus 21:1), **and a positive mitzva:** "You shall be holy" (Leviticus 21:6); and there is a principle that **a positive mitzva does not override a prohibition and a positive mitzva. And furthermore, we do not override** a ritual **prohibition in the face of monetary matters.**

The *kohen*-in-cemetery case similarly does not require the verse, because the general rules of *asei docheh lo ta'asei* — a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition — along with its limits, already resolve it. The *mitzvah* of *hashev teshivem* is an *asei*, but the prohibition against a *kohen* becoming *tamei* is both a *lo ta'asei* and an *asei* of *kedoshim tihyu*. An *asei* does not override a *lo ta'asei* combined with another *asei*. Furthermore, the *lo ta'asei* of *lo tuchal lehis'alem* — one may not ignore a lost object — does not itself serve as a "pushing force" to override the *kohen*'s prohibition; only an *asei* can function that way. Thus the mechanics of the standard rules already exempt the *kohen*, without need of the special verse.

### Kevod Habriyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְשֶׁלּוֹ מְרוּבֶּה מִשֶּׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מִדְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: ״אֶפֶס כִּי לֹא יִהְיֶה בְּךָ אֶבְיוֹן״ – שֶׁלְּךָ קוֹדֵם לְשֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם, אֶלָּא לְזָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** say that the verse is necessary **to** derive the exemption from returning the lost item in the case where the value of **his labor was greater than** the value of the lost item of **the other.** The Gemara rejects that possibility: That *halakha* is **derived** not from the phrase: "And disregard them," but from that which **Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says. As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is written: **"Only so that there shall be no needy among you"** (Deuteronomy 15:4). This verse can be understood as a command, indicating that it is incumbent upon each individual to ensure that he will not become needy. Therefore, **your** assets **take precedence over** the assets **of any** other **person. Rather,** the verse is necessary **to** derive the exemption from returning the lost item in the case where he was **an elderly person and it is not in keeping with his dignity** to tend to the item.

What the verse *vehitalamta mehem* is specifically needed for, therefore, is *lezaken ve'eino lefi kevodo* — the case of the person of dignity for whom retrieval is beneath their station. Here, unlike the first two cases, there is no independent derivation. One is not protecting oneself from ritual violation, nor is one protecting one's own money against a greater loss. One is protecting one's dignity against the simple indignity of stooping in the road to retrieve another person's object. And yet the Torah says even *this* consideration — *kevod habriyot* — overrides the positive obligation to return the lost object. That is the *chiddush* that requires the verse.

### Dignity & Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? וְהָתְנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶפְקֵר לַעֲנִיִּים – הֶפְקֵר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ הֶפְקֵר עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הֶפְקֵר לַעֲנִיִּים וְלַעֲשִׁירִים, כִּשְׁמִיטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** property **rendered ownerless in a case like this? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 6:1) that **Beit Shammai say:** Property **declared ownerless for the poor is** thereby rendered **ownerless. And Beit Hillel say: It is not ownerless, until** the property **will be ownerless for the poor and for the rich, like** produce during **the Sabbatical Year,** which is available for all. As the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, how could Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, declare the wood ownerless selectively, excluding the prior owner of the wood?

*Tosafot* pursues the implications of *kevod habriyot* further. The principle is not limited to monetary obligations: there are cases where it overrides even ritual obligations. The paradigm case is *meis mitzvah* — an abandoned corpse with no one to bury it. The obligation to bury a *meis mitzvah* can override the obligation to bring a *korban Pesach* or to perform a *bris milah* for one's son. These are ritual *mitzvot*, not merely financial obligations, and yet *kevod habriyot* — specifically the profound affront to human dignity of leaving a body abandoned — takes precedence.

*Tosafot* accounts for this by pointing to degree: the *kevod habriyot* interest in burying an abandoned corpse is of an entirely different magnitude than the *kevod habriyot* interest in not having an important person stoop down in the road. When the dignity interest is sufficiently weighty, it can override even ritual obligations. When it is more modest — the ordinary case of *zaken ve'eino lefi kevodo* — it overrides monetary obligations such as *hashevat aveida*. The principle of *kevod habriyot* thus operates on a scale, and its force depends on how serious the affront to dignity actually is.
