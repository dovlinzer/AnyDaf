# Niddah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood in Tube

### Shofoferet Question

We begin in the middle of 26b, in the midst of a discussion in the new *perek* concerning the *mapelet* — women who have miscarried — and the question of what constitutes a fetus. A parallel and equally important thread has emerged: even when the expelled matter is not a fetus and does not itself render the woman *tamei*, does the very act of its expulsion from the uterus necessarily entail that blood came out, even if blood was not directly observed? This is the principle of *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam* — it is impossible for the uterus to open without blood. As noted previously, the *Noda B'Yehuda* extends this principle even to something inserted into the uterus from the outside, giving rise to a range of contemporary questions about gynecological examinations. Rav Moshe Feinstein largely renders those concerns moot by limiting the principle to instruments above a certain circumference — but the concept remains live and practically significant in a number of halachic contexts.

We now turn to another important question that flows from this discussion. The *Gemara* on 26b, approximately fifteen lines from the middle, records the following inquiry: *ba'ei minei Rav Yirmiyah me-Rav Zeira* — Rav Yirmiyah posed a question to Rav Zeira: *haro'ah dam b'shofoferet mahu* — what is the law if a woman sees blood in a tube? The scenario is one in which a tube is inserted up to the cervix — to the outer os, which opens into the vaginal canal — and blood flows directly into that tube, never making contact with the walls of the vaginal canal.

This may seem like a purely hypothetical situation, though it is worth noting that when this material was taught previously, a student identified a commercially available device — essentially a suction cup — designed to collect menstrual blood without it coming into contact with the vaginal canal. Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach also engages with a functionally similar scenario, as discussed below. The conceptual stakes are clear: a woman is understood to become a *niddah* when blood leaves the outer os of the cervix and enters the vaginal canal. What is the law if the blood never touches the vaginal canal at all?

The *Gemara* frames the question around the verse *dam yihiyeh zovah bivsarah* — her blood flows *b'vsarah*, meaning in her flesh. One reading of *b'vsarah* is that it requires the blood to literally touch the flesh, the walls of the vaginal canal — *bisarah*, in her flesh, and not *b'shofoferet*, not in a tube. The other reading is that *b'vsarah* conveys only the principle that a woman is *tamei* even before the blood exits her body, i.e., when blood is still within the vaginal canal — the foundational law that underlies all the laws of *bedikos* and the precise timing of *tum'ah*. On this second reading, *b'vsarah* tells us nothing about whether the blood must literally contact the skin of the vaginal wall.

Rav Zeira answers directly: *bisarah amar Rachmana velo b'shofoferet* — the verse says "in her flesh," not in a tube. He then proceeds to demonstrate that the additional information *b'vsarah* conveys is precisely this literal requirement: had the Torah wished only to teach that she is *tamei* while the blood is still internal, it could have written *b'vasar* without the *heh*. The form *b'vsarah* — "in her flesh" — teaches two things: that she is *tamei* before the blood exits, and that the blood must actually touch the walls of the vaginal canal. This *drasha* is notable in that it involves *amora'im* expounding directly on a verse — normally associated with *tanna'im* — to derive a practical halachic ruling.

### Akara Hilchatit

One practical application of this *shofoferet* discussion, unusual as the scenario may appear, emerges in a lengthy *teshuvah* by Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach. He addresses the question of the *akara hilchatit* — the "halakhically barren" woman — a woman who is biologically fertile but, due to the combined stringency of waiting for bleeding to cease and then observing seven clean days, finds that her fertile period consistently falls before she can become *tehorah*. This has become a serious and widely discussed issue in Israel in recent years.

Rav Shlomo Zalman's *teshuvah* grapples at length with whether the *chumra d'Rabbi Zeira* — the stringency of treating any blood stain, even one of unknown cause, as rendering a woman *tamei* — could ever be waived for the sake of *pru urvu*, the obligation to procreate. He works through the question extensively, and while one can sense his discomfort with the situation, he ultimately concludes that this cannot be done. What he does suggest, however, as a practical workaround in such cases, is the use of precisely such a device — inserting a cup-like implement to collect the blood so that it never contacts the vaginal canal and the woman would thus not become a *niddah* that month, enabling marital relations during the fertile period. Whether this has ever been implemented in practice is unclear. Notably, this is the same Rav Zeira whose stringency created the problem.

### Natural vs. Foreign

Having established the *shofoferet* question, the *Gemara* raises a challenge. Does not Rabbi Yochanan — citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — rule that if a woman expels a *chaticha*, a mass, and that mass is filled with blood on the inside, she is *tamei* if there is *dam agur*, collected blood within it? The blood here is enclosed within the hollow of the mass and has not touched the vaginal canal — yet the woman is *tamei* according to this view, which was already the most lenient of a range of opinions on the subject. This would seem to disprove the requirement that blood contact the walls of the vaginal canal.

The *Gemara* deflects: *hasam darchah shel isha lir'ot dam b'chaticha, hacha ein darchah shel isha lir'ot dam b'shofoferet* — there, it is natural for blood to be inside such a mass; here, it is not natural for blood to flow out through a tube. The significance of this *darchah* — "natural way" — language opens into two quite different readings, both of which will be developed extensively below.

Rashi understands the distinction to be about physical separation. The mass that contains the blood is itself a product of the woman's body — flesh in contact with flesh, *d'min b'mina lo chayatz*, like-in-like does not constitute a separation. It is therefore as if the blood is in contact with the walls of the vaginal canal. A tube, by contrast, is a foreign substance, a man-made object, which does constitute a separation between the blood and the walls. On Rashi's reading, what matters is whether the intervening material is natural to the body or foreign — not whether the situation itself is natural or unusual.

A second reading, the one that *Tosafot* inclines toward, is simpler: a tube, being foreign, creates a *chatzitzah*, a separation; a mass, being natural to the body, does not. The question of whether the mass constitutes a foreign body or not is what the *tanna'im* ultimately debate.

A third reading — that of the *Rosh* — will be explored shortly, and it carries the most far-reaching halachic implications.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita* and considers whether the *shofoferet* question reflects a genuine dispute among *tanna'im*. The *baraita* states: if a woman expelled a *chaticha* — a mass — even if it is filled with blood on the inside, she is *tamei* only if there is blood accompanying it on the outside; if not, she is *tehorah*. This is the position of the *Tanna Kama*. Rabbi Eliezer then states: *b'vsarah* — citing the verse, meaning in her flesh — not inside a *shilya* (embryonic sac) and not inside a *chaticha*. Blood inside even a natural body mass does not render her *tamei*; it must touch the flesh directly.

At first glance, this seems to be simply the same position as the *Tanna Kama*, and the *Gemara* notes as much: *Rabbi Eliezer hainu Tanna Kama*. But the *Gemara* then presents the position of the *Chachamim* as meaningfully distinct: *ein zeh dam niddah ella dam chaticha* — this is not *niddah* blood; it is the blood of the mass. The reason the woman is *tehorah* is not that the blood failed to touch the vaginal walls, but that this blood did not originate from the uterine lining in the first place. It is a categorically different substance.

The *Gemara* then identifies the *nafka minah* between these two approaches. Imagine a mass whose exterior surface is furrowed or cracked — *pluyei pluyei* — such that blood sitting in those crevices could potentially make contact with the walls of the vaginal canal as the mass passes through. On the *Tanna Kama*'s approach, which requires blood to touch the vaginal wall, such a case would render the woman *tamei*, since the blood in the crevices might well have touched the wall. If the surface is smooth — *d'shiya* — she is *tehorah*; if it is creviced, she is *tamei* because *b'vsarah kerina bei*, it counts as touching her flesh.

The *Chachamim*, by contrast, maintain that even if the mass is creviced and even if blood in those crevices contacts the vaginal wall, *ein zeh dam niddah ella dam chaticha* — this is still the blood of the mass and not *niddah* blood, and the woman remains *tehorah*.

The crucial difference for our purposes is the *shofoferet* case. If the concern is about touching the vaginal wall, then blood originating from the uterus and caught in a tube is genuinely problematic — it is *niddah* blood, and the only reason she might be *tehorah* is if we require it to touch the walls. If the concern is instead about whether the blood is *dam niddah* at all, then in the *shofoferet* case — where we know the blood came from the uterus — there is no escape: it is *niddah* blood. One approach produces *tum'ah* in the tube case, the other *tahorah*.

### Abaye's Resolution

Given this analysis, the *Gemara* seems to have established that the *shofoferet* question is in fact the subject of a *tanna'itic* dispute. Abaye, however, resists this conclusion. He asserts: *b'shofoferet kuli alma lo pligei d'tehorah* — regarding the tube itself, everyone would agree the woman is *tehorah*. The debate, he says, is confined to the case of the *chaticha* alone.

### Three Interpretations

The three readings of *Rishonim* part ways on how to understand this claim. Rashi interprets the phrase *darchah shel isha lir'ot dam b'chaticha* — it is natural for there to be blood within a mass — as a statement about whether we assess the blood as *niddah* blood. It is not that they are debating the naturalness of the occurrence per se; rather, they are debating whether the blood in the crevices of the mass is *dam niddah* or *dam chaticha*. This is somewhat awkward as an explanation of the *darchah* language, but it is consistent with Rashi's broader framework.

*Tosafot*'s reading, which is perhaps the most straightforward, is that the tube case is one where everyone agrees: a foreign object creates a *chatzitzah* and the blood does not render her *tamei*. The mass, however, sits at the boundary — is a body-produced mass sufficiently "natural" that it does not interpose as a separation? That is the dispute. One opinion holds that since it is natural for blood to be present within such a mass, the mass is not considered a foreign substance and does not separate between the blood and the walls. The other holds that even a mass is foreign enough to constitute a separation.

### Rosh's Approach

The *Rosh*'s interpretation departs most radically from the others and carries the most significant halachic implications. In his view, everyone agrees both that the blood in the crevices is *dam niddah* and that it touches the vaginal wall. The dispute is about something entirely different: whether *niddah* blood that is expelled in an unnatural manner — dragged out by an expulsion, rather than flowing naturally — creates *tum'ah* at all. One opinion holds that even blood expelled unnaturally renders her a *niddah*; the other holds that *niddah* blood must flow naturally, and blood dragged out by a foreign process is comparable to *dam makah* — blood from a wound — even if it originates from the uterus.

Crucially, on this reading, the *shofoferet* case is not about *chatzitzah* at all. The problem with blood caught in a tube is not that the tube separates the blood from the vaginal wall; it is that the blood is emerging through an unnatural process. And this principle, once articulated, has halachic reach far beyond the hypothetical tube.

This is developed explicitly in the *Tosafot HaRosh* on 21b, where he writes — approximately a third from the end — that *Rabbanan* hold the reason the *shofoferet* is *tehorah* is not *chatzitzah* but because *ein darchah shel isha lir'ot kederach kach* — this is simply not the natural way for a woman to see blood. The same applies to the creviced mass: even if the blood there is *dam niddah*, since it is not coming out in the natural manner, *ein derech nashim lir'os kach*. When the *Rabbanan* say *ein zeh dam niddah*, they mean that this is not the normal manner in which *niddah* blood emerges.

The *Tosafot HaRosh* then records a ruling by the *Sar mi-Kotzi*, one of the *Ba'alei Tosafot*, in the case of a woman whose uterus had been *ne'ekar* — uprooted — which is a description of what is today recognized as a prolapsed uterus, a condition in which part of the uterus collapses into the vaginal canal. This woman was bleeding continuously. The *Sar mi-Kotzi* ruled she was not a *niddah* — *k'ishah tehorah* — because *ein derech nashim lir'os kein*, this is not the natural manner of menstrual bleeding.

This framework has been invoked — contentiously — in discussions about uterine fibroids. Fibroids are internal growths around which the uterine lining forms; they involve no tear and no *makah* in the classic sense, but they can irritate the lining and cause sloughing. The blood that results is uterine blood, and it comes out — arguably — unnaturally, caused by this internal irritant. The *Tzitz Eliezer* attempts to draw on this *sugya* to argue that such bleeding might not create *niddah* status. These discussions are heavily debated in contemporary *poskim* as to scope and parameters, and the *Rosh*'s framework is their starting point.

Raba offers yet another angle on 22a, approximately three lines from the top. He suggests that in fact everyone might agree that neither the tube nor the mass renders the woman a *niddah* — all could hold *ein darchah lir'ot dam b'chaticha*. The dispute in the *baraita*, on his reading, is not about whether the woman is *tamei* as a *niddah*, but about whether the blood itself has *tum'ah* — the principle of *makor mekomo tamei*, that blood originating in the uterus retains a degree of *tum'ah* by virtue of its source, even if it did not render the woman personally a *niddah*. Rabbi Eliezer would hold *isha tehorah v'dam tamei d'asi derech makor* — the woman is *tehorah* but the blood itself is *tamei* since it passed through the uterus. The *Rabbanan* would hold *isha tehorah u'makor mekomo tahor* — once the blood does not create *niddah* status, it carries no independent *tum'ah* either, and it is treated no differently than blood from a wound.

The overall thrust of the *Gemara* is that the *shofoferet* case produces *tahorah* — there appears to be no dispute on this point. The various readings of the *tanna'itic* debate then sharpen the underlying principle: whether the issue is *chatzitzah*, naturalness of the substance, or naturalness of the expulsion — and each of these readings carries a different set of halachic implications for the cases that arise in practice.

## Baal Keri Status

### Semen Expulsion

The *Gemara* now moves to a parallel question in the domain of male *tum'ah*. Rabbah posed a question to Rav Huna: *haro'eh keri b'kisam mahu* — what is the law if a man had a seminal emission not through natural expulsion, but by inserting a small sliver into the urethral opening and thereby extracting semen? Would he be a *baal keri* and thus *tamei* for the purposes of *tum'ah v'taharah*?

The question is constructed in direct parallel to the *shofoferet* case. There, the blood was uterine blood caught before it touched the vaginal canal. Here, the semen was extracted, not expelled by the body itself. The *Gemara* introduces the possibility that *mimenu amar Rachmana* — the verse says "from him," *ad d'nafik mi-besaro* — only once it exits naturally from his flesh, and not *b'kisam*, not via a sliver. Alternatively, *mimenu* might mean only that it must exit the body at all — once it is outside, *afilu b'kisam nami*, even if extracted by a sliver.

This parallel reinforces a point that will become important: perhaps the problem with the *shofoferet* was not *chatzitzah* but unnaturalness of expulsion. If unnaturalness of expulsion is the core concept, the male case is its exact analogue.

Rav Huna, however, sidesteps the direct question. He says: *teipokei lei di-atzmo eino metamei ela b'chasimat pi amah* — leave that aside; the man only becomes *tamei* if there is enough semen to fill the urethral opening. If the sliver is occupying part of that space, perhaps the requisite amount is not present.

### Noge'a vs. Roeh

This response leads the *Gemara* to flag a deeper question: *l'meimra de-nogei'a havei* — does this imply that the man's *tum'ah* is the product of *touching* — that is, *nogei'a* — rather than an intrinsic personal status — *ro'eh*?

This distinction requires some background. There are two fundamental modes by which one contracts *tum'ah*. The first is *nogei'a*: an external source of *tum'ah* — a corpse, an animal carcass — is touched, and *tum'ah* is contracted from that external source. The second is *ro'eh*: something has occurred within one's own body, making the person himself or herself the source and carrier of *tum'ah*. A *niddah* is a *ro'ah*; a *zav* is a *ro'eh*; a *zavah* is a *ro'ah*. Their *tum'ah* is constitutive — it flows from their personal status.

The question regarding the *baal keri* is which category applies. On the one hand, there are strong indications toward *nogei'a*: the *tum'ah* of the *baal keri* is only for one day, far less severe than a *zav* or *niddah*; and more strikingly, a *baal keri* does not render people or vessels *tamei* — he can only render food *tamei*, making him function halachically as a *rishon l'tum'ah* rather than an *av*. This profile fits a man who is *tamei* not because of a personal status but because the semen itself is an *av ha-tum'ah* and his *tum'ah* derives from having been in contact with it as it exited.

### Baal Keri Nature

And yet there is compelling evidence in the other direction. The *Gemara* cites the verses in *Vayikra*: *v'ish ki teitzei mimenu shichvat zera v'rachatz ba-mayim et kol besaro v'tamei ad ha-arev* — a man from whom semen exits shall immerse all his flesh in water and be *tamei* until evening. The verse then addresses garments that have semen on them — they too are *tamei* — and the woman who has relations with a man: she too is *tamei*. The pattern suggests that what is *tamei* is the semen, and those who touch it contract *tum'ah* from it. This would strongly favor *nogei'a*.

However, there is a critical halachic datum that points the other way: the laws of who must leave the various *machanot* — the camps of Israel in the wilderness. A person who contracted *tum'ah* by touching a corpse may remain within *machaneh Leviyah*; only *machaneh Shechinah* is barred. But a *baal keri* is sent outside of *machaneh Leviyah* as well — placed together with the *niddah*, the *zav*, and the *zavah*. This exclusion from two camps is the mark of a *ro'eh*, a person whose personal status is *tamei*, not merely someone who contracted *tum'ah* from an external source.

### Zav's Clean Days

The *Gemara* probes this question through the case of seminal emission during the seven clean days of a *zav*. A *zav* must count seven clean days without any recurrence of *zivah* flow in order to purify himself — precisely as a *zavah* counts seven clean days. If during those seven days the *zav* has a further *zivah* emission, he must begin the count again. The question is: what if during those seven clean days he has a seminal emission — not a *zivah* flow, but ordinary semen? Does this interfere with his count?

The *Gemara* cites the verse: *zot torat ha-zav v'asher teitzei mimenu shichvat zara* — this is the law of the *zav* and the man from whom semen issues. The *drasha* drawn from this juxtaposition is: just as *zivah* flow disrupts the seven-day count, so does seminal emission.

One might think the reason seminal emission disrupts the count is that we are concerned — *lifi she-i efshar b'lo tzichtzuchei zivah* — that droplets of *zivah* fluid are inevitably mixed into the semen when a man in a state of *zivus* has a seminal emission. On this reading, it is not the semen that disrupts the count at all; the disruption is caused by those trace droplets of *zivah* that we assume are mingled in.

But this answer is immediately challenged: if the concern is actual *zivah* liquid mixed into the semen, then the seminal emission should disrupt all seven days — just as a fresh *zivah* emission would — since we are treating the mixture as real *zivah*. But the *halachah* is that seminal emission during the seven clean days disrupts only one day, not seven. The *baraita* derives this from the verse: *bah l'tamei bah* — its power to disrupt corresponds to its own level of *tum'ah*. A *zivah* emission creates seven days of *tum'ah*, and so it disrupts all seven clean days. A seminal emission creates one day of *tum'ah*, and so it disrupts only one day.

The response is that this is a *gezeirat hakatuv* — a scriptural decree: pure *zivah* flow, which produces seven-day *tum'ah*, disrupts all seven clean days; semen that contains mere droplets of *zivah* — *tzichtzuchei zivah d'areva b'shichvat zara* — disrupts only one day, because fundamentally the emission is semen and the *zivah* component is minor.

But this response essentially evacuates the earlier reasoning: if the semen disrupts the count only because of the *zivah* droplets within it, then the extent of disruption should map onto the severity of *zivah*, not of *keri*. The *Gemara* does not resolve this tension cleanly, and the question of whether the *baal keri* is fundamentally a *nogei'a* or a *ro'eh* remains formally unresolved. Both the *pasuk* and the *machaneh* rule point in different directions, and the halachic profile of the *baal keri* reflects a hybrid that does not fit neatly into either category.

### Tosafot View

*Tosafot* offer a clarification of what the original *kisam* question was actually about. The *Gemara*'s response — that the *kisam* would prevent there from being sufficient semen to fill the urethral opening — seemed to assume that a minimum quantity of semen is required. *Tosafot* observe that if the *baal keri*'s *tum'ah* were purely a matter of personal status, no minimum quantity would be required — the tiniest drop would suffice, as is the case for a *niddah* or *zav*. It is only when *tum'ah* is contracted externally that a minimum quantity is relevant.

But *Tosafot* reframe the question. The *Gemara*'s real point, they argue, is not about minimum quantity at all. Rather, it is about whether the semen touched the outer surface of the body. When *tum'ah* is contracted through *nogei'a*, the object must contact the outer surface of the person — not merely an internal surface. If the semen, extracted by a sliver, never contacts the external skin of the man's body but only traverses an internal passage, then on the *nogei'a* theory he would not be *tamei*. This is almost exactly parallel to the *shofoferet* discussion: blood that never touches the outer walls of the vaginal canal. The answer implicit in the *Gemara* — that it may not matter — reinforces the possibility that the man's *tum'ah* is *ro'eh*, a personal status, not dependent on external contact. And yet, as noted, the *Gemara* ultimately leaves the question open, and the question of a woman who has had relations and then has semen expelled from her body — and whether that affects her own status — remains for a later discussion.

## Dried Blood Test

### Dried Blood Status

The *Gemara* returns to the main theme. Rav Yosef bar Chanina posed a question to Rabbi Eliezer: *dam yavesh mahu* — what is the law regarding dried blood? Our *Mishnah* described a procedure of placing dried or hardened matter in water to determine if it was blood. That procedure assumes the blood became dried at some point after leaving the uterus, having originally emerged as liquid. But what if the blood was already dried when it passed from the uterus into the vaginal canal — would that make the woman a *niddah*?

The verse says *ki yazuv zov damah* — when her blood flows. Does this require the blood to be liquid at the critical moment — *lach in, yavesh lo*, only liquid, not dry? Or is the phrase merely descriptive of the typical case, and even dried blood creates *niddah* status?

### Water Test

The *Gemara* cites our *Mishnah*: *dam ha-niddah u'vsar ha-meis mitam'in lachin v'yaveshin* — the blood of a *niddah* and the flesh of a corpse transmit *tum'ah* whether moist or dried. But the questioner notes: that *Mishnah* addresses blood that began as liquid and subsequently dried — for instance, dried flesh of a corpse. That case is implicit in the *Mishnah*. The real question is *yavesh meikara* — what if the blood was dried from the very outset?

### Elazar bar Tzadok

The *Gemara* responds that this too is answered by our *Mishnah*: *hamapelet kemin klipa, kemin sa'ara, kemin afar, kemin yavchushim adumim* — if a woman expels matter that looks like a shell, a hair, dust, or tiny red insects, one places it in water; if it dissolves, she is *tamei*. Clearly this describes matter that emerged already in a dry state. So even blood dried from the outset renders her *tamei* once we establish that it is in fact blood.

The *Gemara* then raises the question: can such tiny things — shells, hairs — really be expelled from the body and not be dried blood? The answer is yes, and it is supported by testimony recorded by Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, who cites two cases that his father, Rabbi Tzadok, brought before the *beit din* in Yavneh — the center of Torah scholarship after the destruction of the Temple, where testimonies of all kinds were collected.

The first case involved a woman expelling small reddish shell-like substances. The *chachamim* consulted physicians, who determined that the woman had a *makah* — an internal wound — that was causing these to be expelled. They instructed that the matter be placed in water: if it dissolves, she is *tamei*; if not, she is *tehorah*. The second case involved a woman expelling reddish hair-like substances. Again the doctors were consulted and identified a *shuma* — a growth — inside her, which was producing these hair-like expulsions. Rashi suggests this might be a growth from which hair-like material could emerge. The same water test applied.

These cases demonstrate that one cannot assume everything expelled from the uterine area is blood. The water test is necessary precisely because the expelled matter may be of entirely different origin — wound-related debris, growth-related tissue — and only if it dissolves as blood would one conclude it is *niddah* blood.

### Dried Blood Conclusion

From these sources the *Gemara* concludes that even blood dried from the outset creates *niddah* status, so long as the water test confirms it is blood. The verse *ki yazuv damah* describes the normal case but does not restrict the law to flowing, liquid blood. This is significant also because the *Mishnah*'s cases of shells and hairs clearly parallel the two stories of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok — suggesting the *Mishnah* codified these real cases as its paradigm examples.

### Testing Methods

Reish Lakish rules that the water used for the test must be *poshrin* — lukewarm, neither too cold nor too hot, which might produce a false positive or false negative. A *baraita* confirms this requirement. The *Chachamim* offer an alternative procedure: the matter is placed on a fingernail with some saliva and pressed — if it liquefies under pressure, it indicates the presence of blood.

The *Gemara* asks what practical difference exists between these two approaches. Ravina answers: *me'uchah al yedei ha-dechak* — the dispute concerns whether material that liquefies only under manual pressure counts. The first opinion, requiring it to dissolve on its own in water, would hold that material requiring active compression is not confirmed as blood; the second opinion, using the spit-and-fingernail method, might hold that even dissolution under pressure suffices.

A further question arises from a parallel *Mishnah* about dried blood of a *niddah* or flesh of a corpse: in order to determine whether desiccated matter retains its *tum'ah* status, one immerses it in lukewarm water for *me'et la'et* — a full twenty-four-hour period. Must the same twenty-four hours apply here? The *Gemara* notes the cases differ: there, the question is whether matter that began moist has become sufficiently desiccated to lose its *tum'ah* status — one is testing the degree of desiccation of a *sheretz* or *neveilah* that begins hard. Here, the question is simply whether the substance is blood at all, and blood is naturally softer — perhaps less time suffices. The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku*, unresolved.

## Uterus Opening Principle

### Fish-Like Fetus Case

The *Gemara* returns to the central focus of the *perek*: what constitutes a legally significant fetus? The *Mishnah* ruled that if the expelled matter resembles a fish or other non-human creature, she is *tehorah* — it is not treated as a human fetus. The question now is: even if it is not a fetus for purposes of *yoledet* status, something was expelled from the uterus. Why not apply *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam* — it is impossible for the uterus to open without blood accompanying the expulsion — and render her *tamei* as a *niddah* on that basis?

Reish Lakish answers: *b'machloket shnuyah, rabbanan hee* — the *Mishnah* reflects the majority view. According to Rabbi Yehuda, however, even the expulsion of a fish-like form would cause the uterus to open and blood to accompany it, rendering her *tamei*.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with this analysis. He argues that even Rabbi Yehuda would concede the woman is *tehorah* in this case. The reason Rabbi Yehuda ruled *tamei* in the *chaticha* case — the expelled mass — was not because the opening of the uterus causes blood to flow, but because he understood the mass itself to be dried *niddah* blood: *chaticha d'avid dam d'karish* — a mass that is itself congealed blood. In the case of a fish-like form, however, even Rabbi Yehuda recognizes this is a biological entity, not dried blood, and he would agree she is *tehorah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Debate

This brings the two interpretations of Rabbi Yehuda's position into sharp relief. When Rabbi Yehuda ruled that a woman who expelled a mass is *tamei*, did he reason that the mass was itself dried *niddah* blood — *chaticha d'avid dam d'karish* — or did he apply the principle of *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam*, reasoning that whenever the uterus is opened to expel something, blood necessarily accompanies it?

If the former, then in the case of a fish-like form — which is clearly not dried blood — Rabbi Yehuda would agree with the majority: *tehorah*. If the latter, then Rabbi Yehuda should rule *tamei* whenever the uterus expels anything large enough to open it, regardless of whether the matter is blood.

The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan himself is credited elsewhere with the principle of *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam* as the basis of the debate. Under that reading — which is one version of his position — Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish would agree: the *Mishnah* is Rabbi Yehuda's opponents, and Rabbi Yehuda himself, applying *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam*, would require *tum'ah* even for the fish-like fetus.

### Size Requirements

A closely related question emerges from the earlier case of hairs and shells. If Rabbi Yehuda holds that any expulsion from the uterus necessarily brings blood with it, why did the *Gemara* not challenge those cases — why does not Rabbi Yehuda require *tum'ah* when a woman expels shell-like or hair-like matter?

*Tosafot* address this directly: *a'se'ara u'v'klipa lo parich* — the *Gemara* did not ask about hairs and shells because *d'muatin ninhu v'lo shayach behu petechat hakever* — these are minute, and such tiny matter does not cause the opening of the uterus. The principle of *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam* applies only when something of sufficient size is expelled.

This size threshold has immediate practical implications and is foundational to Rav Moshe Feinstein's approach. Even according to the *Noda B'Yehuda*, who extends *ee efshar lifteechat hakever b'lo dam* to instruments inserted from outside the body, Rav Moshe concludes that only an instrument of sufficient diameter — he specifies three-quarters of an inch — would trigger the concern. Since virtually no standard gynecological instrument approaches that circumference, the *Noda B'Yehuda*'s ruling is rendered almost entirely moot in practice. This size limitation is rooted precisely in the *Tosafot*'s observation here: small matter does not open the uterus, and therefore does not bring blood with it.
