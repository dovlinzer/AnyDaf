# Avodah Zarah 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## HaSocher Et HaPoel

### Intro & Chapter 5

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ בְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר. שְׂכָרוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ מְלָאכָה אַחֶרֶת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״הַעֲבֵר לִי חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן נֶסֶךְ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר. הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לְהָבִיא עָלֶיהָ יֵין נֶסֶךְ — שְׂכָרָהּ אָסוּר. שְׂכָרָהּ לֵישֵׁב עָלֶיהָ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִנִּיחַ גּוֹי לְגִינוֹ עָלֶיהָ — שְׂכָרָהּ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a gentile **who hires** a Jewish **laborer to work with wine** used for an idolatrous **libation with him, his wage** is **forbidden,** i.e., it is prohibited for the Jew to derive benefit from his wage. If the gentile **hired him to do other work with him, even if he said to him** while he was working with him: **Transport the barrel of wine** used for **a libation for me from** this **place to** that **place, his wage** is **permitted,** i.e., the Jew is permitted to derive benefit from the money. With regard to a gentile **who rents** a Jew's **donkey to carry wine** used for **a libation on it, its** rental **fee** is **forbidden.** If **he rented it to sit on it, even if a gentile placed his jug** of wine used for a libation **on it, its** rental **fee** is **permitted.**

We begin today the fifth *perek* of Masechet Avodah Zarah, on 62a. The chapter is entitled *Ha-socher Et Ha-poel* — "one who hires a worker" — and while it continues the *masechet*'s broader engagement with wine produced or owned or touched by non-Jews, its focus here shifts somewhat toward the monetary dimension of that prohibition. Additional topics arise over the course of the *daf* as well, including *shemittah* fruits and the prohibition of *etnan zonah* — the prohibition of bringing as a *korban* an animal given as payment for prostitution — a topic that surfaces in only a few places in the Talmud and is worth exploring carefully.

### Mishna: Worker

The *Mishnah* opens: *ha-socher et ha-poel la-asot imo be-yayin nesech — secharo assur.* If a non-Jewish employer hires a Jewish laborer to perform work associated with the production of *yayin nesech*, the wages are prohibited. Rashi clarifies this framing at the outset. However, if the worker was hired for some other task and was asked only incidentally to move a barrel of *yayin nesech* from one place to another, the wages are permissible — *secharo mutar* — because the agreed-upon terms of the contract concerned a different type of work.

### Mishna: Donkey

A parallel case follows: *ha-socher et ha-chamor lehavi aleha yayin nesech — secharah assur.* One who rents a donkey — or hires a donkey driver — to transport *yayin nesech* finds the wages prohibited. But if the donkey was rented for the owner to ride, and the non-Jew merely happened to place a wine flask on it, the wages remain permissible. The principle underlying both halves of the *Mishnah* is the same: if transporting or working with *yayin nesech* was the incidental byproduct of an arrangement made for other purposes, the wages are unaffected; if it was the core purpose of the hire, the wages are prohibited.

## Tofes Damav

### Mai Ta'ama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר? אִילֵּימָא, הוֹאִיל וְיֵין נֶסֶךְ אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, שְׂכָרוֹ נָמֵי אָסוּר — הֲרֵי עׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם דַּאֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה, וּתְנַן: מְכָרָן וְקִידֵּשׁ בִּדְמֵיהֶן — מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the first case of the mishna, where a gentile hires a Jew to produce wine used for a libation with him, **what is the reason** that **his wage** is **forbidden? If we say** that **since** it is **prohibited to** derive **benefit** from **wine** used for **a libation, his wage** is **also prohibited,** that is difficult: **There are** the cases of ***orla*** produce, i.e., produce grown during a tree's first three years, **and diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard,** from **which** it is also **prohibited to** derive **benefit, and** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 56b) that if a man **sold** this produce **and betrothed** a woman **with the money** received **for it,** she is **betrothed.** Evidently, money gained from a forbidden item is not itself forbidden, as otherwise the betrothal would not take effect.

The *Gemara* opens with the obvious question: *mai ta'ama secharo assur?* Why are the wages prohibited at all? One's initial reading of the *Mishnah* might assume it refers to *stam yeinam* — the rabbinic prohibition of ordinary non-Jewish wine — since throughout the *masechet* the term *yayin nesech* is often used to describe even the rabbinic-level prohibition of *stam yeinam*, so as to underscore its severity. But as will become clear, the *Gemara*'s first question points toward a distinction between the two: whether the stringency applied here extends only to *yayin nesech* proper or also to the rabbinic-level *stam yeinam*.

### Issur Hana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הוֹאִיל וְתוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וַהֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית דְּתוֹפֶסֶת אֶת דָּמֶיהָ, וּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לְפוֹעֵל ״הֵילָךְ דִּינָר זֶה, לְקוֹט לִי בּוֹ יָרָק הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר, ״לְקוֹט לִי יָרָק הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps the reason that the wage is forbidden is **since** the wine used for a libation **transfers to the money** its status **as** an object of **idol worship.** The Gemara challenges: **But there is** the *halakha* of **Sabbatical-**Year produce, **which transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed, **and** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 8:4): With regard to **one who says to** his **laborer** during the Sabbatical Year: **Here is this dinar** I give **to you; gather for me vegetables for its** value **today, his wage** is **forbidden,** i.e., the sanctity of the Sabbatical-Year produce is transferred to the wage, since it is as though he has purchased Sabbatical-Year produce in exchange for the dinar. But if the employer says to him: **Gather for me vegetables today,** without mentioning that it is for the value of the dinar, **his wage** is **permitted,** as he merely paid him for his labor. This should apply as well to the case of the wine used for a libation.

The *Gemara* proposes a first possible rationale: *ho'il ve-yayin nesech assur ba-hana'ah, secharo nami assur* — since the wine is forbidden to derive benefit from, perhaps the wages should be forbidden as well, as fruit of the poisonous tree. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *orlah* and *kil'ei hakerem* are likewise forbidden to derive benefit from, yet the *Mishnah* teaches that if one sold such fruits and used the proceeds for *kiddushin*, the betrothal is valid — *mekudeshet*. That is, the money received in exchange for *orlah* or *kil'ei hakerem* does not itself become prohibited; the prohibited status does not transfer to the monetary proceeds. If *issur hana'ah* alone were the operative principle, wages received for work with *yayin nesech* should similarly remain permissible, just as the *kiddushin* money derived from *orlah* is valid.

### Money Laundering

The *Gemara* therefore advances a different rationale: *ela ho'il ve-tofes damav ke-avodat kochavim* — because *yayin nesech* has the property of *tofes damav*, of latching its prohibited status onto the money exchanged for it, just as is the case with *avodah zarah* itself. If one sells an idol, the money received carries the prohibited status of *avodah zarah*. So too, the wages received for work involving *yayin nesech* carry the prohibited status of the wine. This is the principle at stake — not a generic *issur hana'ah*, but the specific capacity of certain prohibited items to transfer their status onto the money. Notably, not all prohibited items possess this quality; it is a feature of only certain categories of prohibition, and which categories are included becomes the central question the *Gemara* will pursue.

## Shemittah Fruits

### Le'ochla Lo Sechora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית דְּתוֹפֶסֶת אֶת דָּמֶיהָ, וּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לְפוֹעֵל ״הֵילָךְ דִּינָר זֶה, לְקוֹט לִי בּוֹ יָרָק הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר, ״לְקוֹט לִי יָרָק הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** **But there is** the *halakha* of **Sabbatical-**Year produce, **which transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed, **and** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 8:4): With regard to **one who says to** his **laborer** during the Sabbatical Year: **Here is this dinar** I give **to you; gather for me vegetables for its** value **today, his wage** is **forbidden,** i.e., the sanctity of the Sabbatical-Year produce is transferred to the wage, since it is as though he has purchased Sabbatical-Year produce in exchange for the dinar. But if the employer says to him: **Gather for me vegetables today,** without mentioning that it is for the value of the dinar, **his wage** is **permitted,** as he merely paid him for his labor. This should apply as well to the case of the wine used for a libation.

The *Gemara* raises a comparison: *harei shevi'it de-tofes dame'ha* — what about *shemittah* fruits, which similarly transfer their sanctity onto the proceeds of their sale? *Shemittah* fruits, those that grew naturally during the seventh year, are in principle *hefker* — ownerless — and available for anyone to take. The verse states *ve-hayeta shabbat ha-aretz lachem le-ochla*, and the Rabbis derive from this: *le'ochla ve-lo le-sechora* — the fruits are to be eaten, not sold. If one harvests and sells them in the marketplace, that constitutes forbidden commerce with *shemittah* produce.

### Shemittah Worker

The *Mishnah* presents the following case: if one says to a worker, *heilach dinar zeh ve-likot li yarak ha-yom* — "take this *dinar* and go pick vegetables for me today" (during the *shemittah* year) — the wages are prohibited, *secharo assur*, because the money is explicitly linked to the produce, and *tofes damav* applies. But if one simply says *likot li yarak ha-yom* — "go pick vegetables for me today" — without specifying any wage, the wages are permissible, *secharo mutar*. In this latter case there is room to construe the arrangement as a favor, compensated by a gift, rather than as hired labor in exchange for *shemittah* produce. The explicit monetary linkage is what triggers the *tofes damav* concern. This stands in contrast to *avodah zarah*, where the status latches onto the money whether the arrangement is explicit or implicit.

### Rambam vs Ramban

It is worth noting a related debate between *Rambam* and *Ramban* regarding the *mitzvah* to eat *shemittah* fruits. *Rambam* understands the verse *le'ochla* as primarily conveying the prohibition of commerce — it defines the permitted use (eating) in order to exclude the prohibited one (selling). *Ramban*, by contrast, counts in his addendum to *Rambam*'s *Sefer HaMitzvot* a separate positive *mitzvah* to eat the fruits of *shemittah*, implying a higher level of *kedushah* attached to those fruits. Some have accordingly suggested that one should wash one's hands before eating *shemittah* fruits, and there are discussions about whether a special *berachah* — *asher kidshanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu al achilat peirot shevi'it* — would be appropriate, all flowing from this underlying debate.

### Exports & Terumah

A related concern is the question of taking *shemittah* fruits out of the Land of Israel. This is generally prohibited until after the time of *bi'ur*, the point at which those fruits are no longer available in the field. After *bi'ur*, export becomes possible, but the issue remains live in practice: *shemittah* produce that makes its way to markets abroad raises questions of *kedushat shevi'it*, as does Israeli produce in general, which may carry obligations of *terumah* and *ma'aser* that consumers abroad must be aware of.

## Three Opinions

### Yochanan: Kenas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קְנָס הוּא שֶׁקָּנְסוּ חֲכָמִים בַּחֲמָרִין וּבְיֵין נֶסֶךְ. יֵין נֶסֶךְ — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, חַמָּרִין מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: הַחַמָּרִין שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית — שְׂכָרָן שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This **is a penalty that the Sages imposed upon donkey drivers and with regard to wine** used for **a libation.** The Gemara explains: With regard to **wine** used for **a libation,** the penalty is **as we said,** that the wage of one who is hired to work in the production of wine used for libation is forbidden. With regard to **donkey drivers, what is** this penalty? The penalty is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **the donkey drivers who were working in** the transportation of **Sabbatical**-Year **produce, their wage** is **Sabbatical**-Year produce.

With the comparison between *yayin nesech* and *shemittah* established, the *Gemara* presents three *amoraic* attempts to explain the differences between them. The first is from Rabbi Yochanan, as quoted by Rabbi Abahu: *kenas hu she-kans'u chachamim ba-chamarin u-ve-yayin nesech* — there is a special *kenas*, a rabbinic fine and deterrent, that the Rabbis imposed specifically with respect to *chamarin* (donkey drivers) and with respect to *yayin nesech*. As for *yayin nesech*, that is what was already stated in our *Mishnah*. As for *chamarin*, the *Gemara* cites a *Baraita*: donkey drivers who transported *shemittah* fruits have their wages take on the *kedushah* of *shemittah* — *secharan shevi'it*.

But what does it mean for wages to have *kedushat shevi'it*? The *Gemara* considers two possibilities. First, perhaps the wages would be paid with *shemittah* fruits themselves — but that would mean the employer is paying off a debt using *shemittah* produce, which is itself forbidden commerce, contrary to *le'ochla ve-lo le-sechora*. Second, perhaps the money acquires *kedushat shevi'it* — but then the *Gemara* cites a *Baraita* in which the case of the *dinar* being explicitly mentioned yields a permissible result, which seems to cut against this. The tension in Rabbi Yochanan's explanation at this point is left somewhat unresolved, which is precisely why the *Gemara* proceeds to Abaye and Rava.

Under Rabbi Yochanan's framework, the stringency applied to *yayin nesech* — and to the donkey drivers in the *shemittah* context — is not derived logically from a general principle but is simply a *kenas* that the Rabbis imposed as a deterrent. This explains why *orlah* and *kil'ei hakerem*, which are less common and generally more repugnant, do not require the same *kenas*, and why the *shemittah* case may follow a somewhat different logic.

### Abaye: B'tzad Heter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״שְׂכָרָן שְׁבִיעִית״? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָהֲבִינַן לְהוּ שָׂכָר מִפֵּירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית, נִמְצָא זֶה פּוֹרֵעַ חוֹבוֹ מִפֵּירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״לְאׇכְלָה״ וְלֹא לִסְחוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does it mean when it says that **their wage** is **Sabbatical**-Year produce? **If we say that we give them** their **wage** for their work **from Sabbatical**-Year **produce,** the employer **consequently is paying his debt from Sabbatical**-Year **produce, and** this violates that which **the Torah states:** "And the Sabbatical produce of the land shall be **for food** for you" (Leviticus 25:6), indicating that this produce is designated for food, **but not for commerce.**

Abaye accepts Rabbi Yochanan's framework that the donkey drivers' wages carry a special *kenas*, but offers his own explanation of what it means for the wages to be *shemittah*. He says: *le'olam yahavinan lei sechar mi-peirot shevi'it* — the wages are in fact paid using *shemittah* fruits. And when challenged that this amounts to using *shemittah* produce to discharge a financial obligation — which is *sechorah* — Abaye answers: *de-yahavinan lei be-tzad heter*, one must give the payment in a permissible manner. He draws a parallel from the laws of *ma'aser sheni*: one cannot instruct a friend to take *ma'aser sheni* fruits to Jerusalem *le-chalek*, meaning to receive a portion as payment for the transport — because that constitutes using the fruits to pay off a debt. But one may say "bring them to eat and drink in Jerusalem," and then give each other gifts in return. A gift exchange is not the same as a commercial transaction, even if it functions similarly in practice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דְּקָדוֹשׁ שְׂכָרָן בִּקְדוּשַּׁת שְׁבִיעִית. וּמִי קָדוֹשׁ? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְפוֹעֵל: ״הֵילָךְ דִּינָר זֶה וּלְקוֹט לִי יָרָק הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר, ״לְקוֹט לִי יָרָק בּוֹ הַיּוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **And** if it means **that their wage is sacred with the sanctity of Sabbatical-**Year produce, **is** that wage in fact **sacred? But isn't it taught** in a mishna that with regard to **one who says to a laborer: Here is this dinar** I give **to you and gather for me vegetables today, his wage** is **permitted,** but if he says to him: **Gather for me vegetables today for its** value, **his wage** is **forbidden?** The case of the donkey drivers is clearly similar to the former case, where the value of the dinar was not mentioned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ שָׂכָר מִפֵּירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ ״לְאׇכְלָה״ וְלֹא לִסְחוֹרָה, דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בְּצַד הֶיתֵּר, כְּדִתְנַן: לֹא יֹאמַר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ:
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually,** Rabbi Yochanan's statement should be interpreted as saying that **we give him** his **wage from Sabbatical**-Year **produce. And** as for **that which** appears to **pose a difficulty for you,** that the verse designates such produce **"for food" but not for commerce,** that can be resolved by explaining **that one gives him** his wage **in a permitted manner,** i.e., as a gift rather than as a wage. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 3:1) that **a person should not say to another:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַעֲלֵה לִי פֵּירוֹת הַלָּלוּ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם לְחַלֵּק״, אֲבָל אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הַעֲלֵם לְאוֹכְלָם וְלִשְׁתּוֹתָם בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם״, וְנוֹתְנִין זֶה לָזֶה מַתָּנָה שֶׁל חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Bring this produce** designated as second tithe **to Jerusalem for me** in exchange **for a share** of the produce, of which you may partake in Jerusalem. This is considered payment and is tantamount to conducting commerce with the tithe. **But he may say to him: Bring it** to Jerusalem **to eat it and drink it in Jerusalem,** as long as he does not specify that it is payment; **and** once in Jerusalem **they may give one another unrequited gifts.** This indicates that what may not be given as payment may be given as a gift, and therefore the donkey drivers may be compensated with Sabbatical-Year produce.

### Rava: Skilled Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם דְּקָדוֹשׁ בִּקְדוּשַּׁת שְׁבִיעִית, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ פּוֹעֵל — פּוֹעֵל דְּלָא נְפִישׁ אַגְרֵיהּ לָא קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן, חַמָּרִין דִּנְפִישׁ אַגְרַיְיהוּ — קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן בְּהוּ. וּמַתְנִיתִין — חוּמְרָא דְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: Actually,** Rabbi Yochanan means **that** the produce with which the drivers are paid is **sacred with the sanctity** of **Sabbatical**-Year produce, **and** as for **that which** poses **a difficulty for you** with regard to the *halakha* of the **laborer** cited in the mishna, which states that his wage is not sacred, that difficulty can be resolved as follows: There is a distinction between **a laborer, whose wage is not great,** and therefore **the Sages did not penalize him** by decreeing that his wage is sacred, and **donkey drivers, whose wages are great,** and therefore **the Sages penalized them. And** with regard to **the mishna** that deems forbidden even the laborer's wage in the case of one who produces wine designated for libation, **the stringency of wine** used for **a libation is different,** and it is treated more stringently than Sabbatical-Year produce.

Rava disagrees with Abaye and instead maintains: *le'olam de-kadosh bi-kdushat shevi'it* — the money does in fact take on *kedushat shevi'it*. The distinction Rava draws is between a *poel* — an ordinary day laborer who picks vegetables — and a *chamar*, a donkey driver. *Poel de-lo nafish agrei lo kansu rabanan* — since the day laborer earns relatively little, the Rabbis did not apply the *kenas* against him, because they wanted to protect his modest livelihood. *Chamarei de-nafish agraihu kansu rabanan* — donkey drivers, as a somewhat more skilled and better-compensated profession, did have the *kenas* applied against them. This is why the *Baraita* distinguishes between the cases: when the *poel* is hired without explicit mention of wages, the wages are permissible — not because the *kedushat shevi'it* does not attach to money in principle, but because the Rabbis chose not to extend the *kenas* to ordinary laborers.

### Yayin Nesech Chamura

The *Mishnah*'s ruling regarding *yayin nesech*, which prohibits wages even for a simple *poel*, deviates from the paradigm established by *shemittah*. The *Gemara* explains: *chumra d'yayin nesech shani* — the stringency applied to *yayin nesech* is of a different order. Because *yayin nesech* is so closely identified with *avodah zarah* itself, the Rabbis expanded the scope of the *kenas* beyond donkey drivers to encompass even ordinary laborers. The *shemittah* framework created by the Rabbis — applying the *kenas* to *chamarin* but not to simple *po'alim* — served as a template, but when it came to *yayin nesech*, that template was applied more broadly. This is the resolution of the *machlokes* among the three *amoraim*: all agree there is a special *kenas*, but they differ on exactly what it means for wages to carry *kedushat shevi'it* in the *chamarin* case, and on precisely why ordinary laborers were exempted from the *kenas* in *shemittah* but not in *yayin nesech*.

## Stam Yeinam

### Ibaia Lehu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שְׂכָרוֹ לִסְתָם יֵינָן, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיסּוּרָא חָמוּר כִּדְיֵין נֶסֶךְ — שְׂכָרוֹ נָמֵי אָסוּר, אוֹ דִלְמָא: הוֹאִיל וְטוּמְאָתוֹ (קיל) [קִילָא] — אַף שְׂכָרוֹ נָמֵי קִיל?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a gentile **hired** a Jew **to** work with him in the production of **nondescript wine** of gentiles, i.e., wine that was not used for libation, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since the prohibition** of deriving benefit from nondescript wine of gentiles is **as stringent as** the prohibition **of** deriving benefit from **wine** used for a **libation, his wage is also forbidden, or perhaps** should it be reasoned that **since** the *halakha* with regard to **its** capacity for imparting **ritual impurity** to one who comes into contact with it is more **lenient** than the *halakha* with regard to wine used for a libation, the *halakha* with regard to **its wage** is **also** more **lenient?**

Having established that the *Mishnah*'s prohibition refers specifically to *yayin nesech* proper and not merely to *stam yeinam*, the *Gemara* now raises the open question: *secharo le-stam yeinam mahu?* Does the same stringency of prohibited wages apply when one is hired to work with *stam yeinam* — ordinary non-Jewish wine, subject only to the rabbinic prohibition — as it does with *yayin nesech*? On one hand, since the Rabbis treated *stam yeinam* as equivalent in its level of *issur* to *yayin nesech*, perhaps the wages should be equally prohibited. On the other hand, perhaps when the Rabbis modeled the *kenas* they took into account the ways in which *stam yeinam* is more lenient than *yayin nesech*.

### Tumah Comparison

One metric of difference between *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam* is *tumah*. *Avodah zarah* itself conveys *tumah*, but wine merely handled or produced by a non-Jew does not carry the same *tumah*-transmitting status as wine actually consecrated for idolatrous libation. Thus *tuma'ato kil* — the *tumah* of *stam yeinam* is lighter than that of *yayin nesech* — and one might argue that *af secharo nami kil*, the wages too should be treated more leniently. The question, then, is whether the Rabbis, in constructing the *kenas* on wages, modeled it after the ways in which *stam yeinam* parallels *yayin nesech*, or after the ways in which it differs. The *Gemara* leaves the question unresolved, then pivots to a practical case that sheds light on the matter.

## Boat Rental

### Rav Chisda's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאֹגַר אַרְבֵּיהּ לִסְתָם יֵינָן, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ חִיטֵּי בְּאַגְרָא, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל קְלִינְהוּ וְקִבְרִינְהוּ (בקברי) [בֵּי קִבְרֵי].
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution: It is related **that** there was **a certain man who rented out his ship for** transporting **nondescript wine** of gentiles, and the gentiles **gave him wheat in payment. He came before Rav Ḥisda** to determine the status of the wheat. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Go burn it and bury it in a graveyard.** Evidently, payment for working with nondescript wine of gentiles is forbidden.

A certain man rented out his boat to transport *stam yeinam*. As payment, the non-Jews filled the boat with wheat — a seemingly generous arrangement. After the fact, the man came before Rav Chisda with his question about the status of the wheat.

### Burn & Bury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: בַּדְּרִינְהוּ! אָתוּ בְּהוּ לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה. וְלִיקְלִינְהוּ וְלִיבַדְּרִינְהוּ! דִּלְמָא מְזַבְּלִי בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to the method of eradication of the wheat in Rav Ḥisda's ruling. **But let him say to** the ship owner: **Scatter it.** The Gemara responds: If he scatters it, people might **be caused a mishap by it** if they find kernels of the scattered wheat and gather them for eating. The Gemara challenges: **But** then **let him burn it and scatter it.** Why should it be buried? The Gemara answers: **Perhaps** people will **fertilize** their fields **with it.**

Rav Chisda ruled: *zil kalinehu ve-kavrinehu be-vei kivrei* — go burn the wheat, and then bury the ashes in a cemetery. This is a striking ruling, requiring two separate acts of disposal, and the *Gemara* proceeds to interrogate each step.

### Temurah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִקְבְּרִינְהוּ בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ! מִי לָא תְּנַן: אֶחָד אֶבֶן שֶׁנִּסְקַל בָּהּ, וְאֶחָד עֵץ שֶׁנִּתְלָה עָלָיו, וְאֶחָד סַיִיף שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג בּוֹ, וְאֶחָד סוּדָר שֶׁנֶּחְנַק בּוֹ — כּוּלָּם נִקְבָּרִים עִמּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let him bury** the wheat **in its unadulterated form. Didn't we learn** in a *baraita* with regard to the instruments used for imposing capital punishment: **The stone with which** a condemned person is **stoned, and the tree on which** his corpse is **hung** after his execution, **and the sword with which** he is **killed, and the scarf with which** he is **strangled, all** of them **are buried** together **with him,** as it is prohibited to derive benefit from them. The *baraita* does not require that they be burned before they are buried.

To understand the ruling, the *Gemara* invokes the final *Mishnah* of Masechet Temurah, which establishes a general principle: *kol ha-nisrafin lo yikaveru* — everything required to be burned should not be buried; *ve-chol ha-nikbarin lo yisarefu* — everything required to be buried should not be burned. Each category of prohibited object has its proper method of disposal, and the two methods are not interchangeable. Rabbi Yehuda dissents, holding that one may choose to burn items that should technically be buried, but the other Rabbis reject this: *eino rashai le-shanot*, one may not deviate from the designated method. A further principle applies: when something is classified as *nisrafin*, once it has been burned the ashes — *afran mutar* — are permissible, because burning actually transforms the object. We say *panim chadashot ba'u le-chan* — a new form has come into being. By contrast, *nikbarin* objects, once buried, remain prohibited even in decomposed form — *afran assur* — because burial conceals but does not transform.

### Why Both Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם דְּקָא קָבְרִי בְּבֵי דִינָא, מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא דַּהֲרוּגֵי בֵּית דִּין נִינְהוּ. הָכָא לָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא, אֵימַר: אִינָשׁ גְּנַב וְאַיְיתִי קְבַר הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There, since they are buried in the court** graveyard, **the matter is clear** to all **that these were executed by the court,** so everyone knows that using the instruments of execution is prohibited. **Here, the matter is not clear** to all, as one might **say** to himself that **a person stole** the wheat **and brought** it and **buried** it **here,** and he might thereby come to use it.

Given this framework, Rav Chisda's dual requirement demands explanation. Why not simply scatter the wheat? Because *ata bi-hehu lidei takala* — people will find it and use it, not knowing it is prohibited, thereby violating the *issur*. Why not burn it and scatter the ashes? Because *dilma mizavlei behu* — people might use the ashes as fertilizer, since ash is rich in potassium. So the object needs to be buried — but burning it first creates a transformation (*panim chadashot*) that addresses one dimension of the problem, and burying the ashes in a cemetery ensures they will not be retrieved and used. Both steps together accomplish what neither alone can.

### Execution Tools

One might ask: the *Mishnah* in Sanhedrin teaches that the implements of capital punishment — the stone used for stoning, the beam for hanging, the sword for beheading, the cloth for strangulation — are all buried alongside the executed criminal, and we are not concerned that people will dig them up and use them. Why should our case be different? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam de-kakavrei be-vei dina mukeach milta de-harugei bei dina ninhu* — there, the implements are buried adjacent to the *beit din*, and it is therefore evident to everyone that those graves belong to court-executed criminals. People understand what those graves contain and do not disturb them. *Hacha lo mukeach milta* — in our case, if a sack of wheat is buried in the ground, a passerby who finds it will simply assume a thief hid it there and will help himself to it, entirely unaware of its prohibited status. This distinction — between a burial whose context makes the prohibition evident, and one that does not — is what justifies the additional step of burning the wheat before burying the ashes in a cemetery.

## Yannai's Loan

### Loan Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי יָזְפִי פֵּירֵי שְׁבִיעִית מֵעֲנִיִּים, וּפָרְעוּ לְהוּ בִּשְׁמִינִית. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: יָאוּת הֵן עָבְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai borrowed Sabbatical-**Year **produce from the poor and repaid them in the eighth** year. Others **came** and **said** this **to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** out of concern that by doing so they violated the prohibition against engaging in commerce with Sabbatical-Year produce. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to them: They are acting properly,** as this is not considered commerce.

The students of Rabbi Yannai had the practice of lending money against *shemittah* fruits. More precisely, they would borrow *shemittah* fruits from poor people during the seventh year and repay them — in money — in the eighth year. On its face, this arrangement appears to constitute commerce with *shemittah* produce, which is forbidden under *le'ochla ve-lo le-sechora*.

### Yochanan Approves

When this practice was brought before Rabbi Yochanan, he ruled: *yaot hem avdin* — they are doing it correctly. From a charitable standpoint, the arrangement represents a sophisticated form of *tzedakah*: the poor had gathered *shemittah* fruits for free, since the fruits are *hefker*, and then received a loan against those fruits. Being able to participate in what appears to be a regular business transaction preserves their dignity, making it a high-level form of *tzedakah*. But the question remains: is this not doing business with *shemittah* fruits? Rabbi Yochanan endorses the practice, and the *Gemara* connects his approval to the principle of delayed payment.

### Delayed Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְנֶגְדָּן בְּאֶתְנַן — מוּתָּר, דְּתַנְיָא: נָתַן לָהּ וְלֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ, בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ — אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And in the corresponding** case concerning **payment** to a prostitute for services rendered, it is **permitted** to sacrifice such an animal as an offering. Although the Torah prohibits the sacrifice of an animal used as a prostitute's payment (see Deuteronomy 23:19), in a case similar to this one, it is permitted; **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If the man **gave** the prostitute payment **but did not engage in intercourse with her,** or if **he engaged in intercourse with her but did not give her** payment, it is **permitted** for **her payment** to serve as an offering.

Rabbi Yochanan further states: *u-knegdan be-etnan mutar* — and the equivalent arrangement in the context of *etnan* is similarly permissible. The connecting principle is temporal disjunction: when payment is delayed rather than simultaneous — when the monetary exchange does not happen at the same moment as the transfer of the prohibited or sanctified item — the *kedushat shevi'it* does not latch onto the money in the way that it would in an immediate transaction. The loan structure of Rabbi Yannai's students, in which the fruits are received in the seventh year and repaid only in the eighth year, exploits this temporal gap. It is not merely the fact that repayment occurs outside the *shemittah* year, but rather that the very structure of a loan, with its inherent time delay between receipt and repayment, is categorically different from a direct commercial sale.

## Etnan Zonah

### Intro to Etnan

To understand the principle of *u-knegdan be-etnan mutar*, the *Gemara* turns to the laws of *etnan zonah* and *mechir kelev*, which are discussed at length in Masechet Temurah. The Torah prohibits bringing as a *korban* in the *Beit HaMikdash* two types of animals: *etnan zonah* — an animal given as payment to a prostitute — and *mechir kelev* — an animal received in exchange for a dog. The sixth *perek* of Masechet Temurah defines these terms. *Eizo hi etnan?* — What constitutes *etnan*? *Ha-omer le-zonah ha lach zeh be-sichrech* — one who says to a prostitute "take this sheep as your payment," even if a hundred sheep are given, all become prohibited as *korbanot*. Similarly, if someone says "take this sheep so that your slave woman will sleep with my slave," Rabbi Yehuda counts that as *etnan* while the other Rabbis agree.

*Mechir kelev* is defined as exchanging a sheep for a dog: *ha-omer le-chavero hach lach taleh zeh tachat kelev zeh*. If two partners divided nineteen sheep and one dog — one taking ten sheep and the other taking nine sheep and a dog — the ten sheep given in exchange for the dog are prohibited as *korbanot*, while the nine that accompany the dog are permissible since they were exchanged only for sheep. Importantly, *etnan kelev* — payment for a dog used for breeding — and *mechir zonah* — money paid as the price of a prostitute — are both *mutar*, permissible, since the verse specifies only *shnayim shnayim ve-lo arba*: only the two named prohibitions, not the reverse combinations.

### Braita Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לָהּ וְלֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ, בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ — אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If the man **gave** the prostitute payment **but did not engage in intercourse with her,** or if **he engaged in intercourse with her but did not give her** payment, it is **permitted** for **her payment** to serve as an offering.

A *Baraita* is cited: *natan la ve-lo ba'ala* — if a man paid the prostitute but never had intercourse with her — or *ba'ala ve-lo natan la* — if they had intercourse but he never paid her — *etnana mutar*, the payment is permissible. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: if he paid her but they were never together, *peshita* — obviously the payment is not *etnan*, *matanah be'alma hi*, it is simply a gift. And if they were together but he never paid her, obviously there is no prohibited payment because nothing was given. The *Baraita* as stated seems to address either an impossible scenario or a self-evident one.

### Braita Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא עָלֶיהָ, אוֹ בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָתַן לָהּ — אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If **he gave her** payment **and afterward,** after some time elapsed, **he engaged in intercourse with her, or** if **he engaged in intercourse with her and afterward,** after some time elapsed, **he gave her** payment, **her payment is permitted,** because the payment was not given proximate to the intercourse. This is also the *halakha* in the case of borrowing Sabbatical-Year produce, i.e., paying for it after time has elapsed is not considered commerce.

The *Gemara* therefore reinterprets the *Baraita*: *ela hachi kamar* — this is what it actually means. *Natan la ve-achar kach ba'ala* — he paid her first, and intercourse followed later. *O ba'ala ve-achar kach natan la* — or they were together first, and he paid her later. *Etnana mutar* — in both cases the payment is permissible. This is the operative content of the *Baraita*: time disjunction between payment and the act of prostitution removes the prohibition of *etnan*.

### Elazar's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵיחוּל עֲלַהּ אִיסּוּר אֶתְנַן לְמַפְרֵעַ! אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כְּשֶׁקָּדְמָה וְהִקְרִיבַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** the **prohibition** with regard to **payment** to a prostitute **should apply to** the payment **retroactively. Rabbi Eliezer says:** The *baraita* is referring to a situation **when she sacrificed** the payment in the Temple **first,** before the intercourse took place.

But this reading immediately invites a question: if he paid her in advance, why does the prohibition not take effect retroactively when intercourse eventually occurs? *Le-chi ba'ala le-chulei issur etnan le-mafre'a* — should we not say that once the act occurs, it retroactively renders the prior payment *etnan*? Rabbi Elazar answers: *ke-she-kadmah ve-hikravto* — the case in the *Baraita* must be understood as one where the prostitute took the animal before intercourse occurred and brought it as a *korban* before the act. Since the animal was already offered when the intercourse took place, there was no moment at which it could have become *etnan*. This raises a further structural question: how exactly does this work?

### Conditional Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר לַהּ: ״קְנַי לִיךְ מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ — פְּשִׁיטָא דִּשְׁרֵי, דְּהָא לֵיתֵיהּ בִּשְׁעַת בִּיאָה, וּמַתָּנָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of such a situation? **If** the circumstances are **that he said to her: Acquire** this payment **from now,** it is **obvious that** it is **permitted** to sacrifice it, **as it is not extant** as payment **at the time of the intercourse, and it is merely a gift that he gave her.** It would not be necessary for the *baraita* to state this.

The *Gemara* probes: *heichi dami?* How precisely must the transaction be structured? If the man said *kani lach me'achshav* — "acquire it from me now" — then *peshita de-shari*, it is obviously permissible, because *de-ha leita be-sha'at biah*, the sheep no longer exists at the time of intercourse — it has already been offered as a *korban* — and the initial transfer was simply a gift, *matanah be'alma*. If, on the other hand, he did not say "acquire it now," then *heichi matzi makriva* — how could she have offered it at all? She would not yet own it, and one cannot dedicate another's property as a *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי דְּלָא אָמַר לַהּ ״קְנַי לִיךְ מֵעַכְשָׁיו״, הֵיכִי (מצי) [מָצְיָא] מַקְרְבָה? ״וְאִישׁ כִּי יַקְדִּשׁ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ קֹדֶשׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מָה בֵּיתוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, אַף כֹּל בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the circumstances are **that he did not say to her: Acquire** this payment **from now, how can she** be allowed to **sacrifice it? The Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And when a man shall sanctify his house to be holy to the Lord"** (Leviticus 27:14), from which it is derived that **just as** one's **house is in his possession, so too, any** item that a person wishes to consecrate must be **in his possession.** The prostitute cannot consecrate an animal that is not in her possession.

The *Gemara*'s resolution: *heichi dami?* How precisely must the transaction be structured? If the man said *kani lach me'achshav* — "acquire it from me now" — then *peshita de-shari*, it is obviously permissible, because *de-ha leita be-sha'at biah*, the sheep no longer exists at the time of intercourse — it has already been offered as a *korban* — and the initial transfer was simply a gift, *matanah be'alma*. If, on the other hand, he did not say "acquire it now," then *heichi matzi makriva* — how could she have offered it at all? She would not yet own it, and one cannot dedicate another's property as a *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: ״לֶהֱוֵי גַּבִּיךְ עַד שְׁעַת בִּיאָה, וְאִי מִיצְטְרִיךְ לִיךְ קְנַי מֵעַכְשָׁיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to a situation **where he said to her: Let** the animal **be with you until the time of intercourse, and if you need it** in the meantime, **acquire** it **from now.** The *baraita* teaches that in this situation she may sacrifice her payment as an offering.

The *Gemara*'s resolution: *ela de-amar lah le-havei gavach ad sha'at biah* — the man must have told her to hold the animal as collateral, keeping it in her possession until the time of intercourse, at which point she would acquire it. But *im mitztarech lah kani me'hashta* — if she needs it in the meantime — for example, if she wishes to bring it as a *korban* right away — she may acquire it now. This conditional acquisition structure — hold as collateral, with the option to acquire immediately if needed — allows for a scenario in which she offers the animal before intercourse, the man follows through, and yet the prohibition of *etnan* never attaches, because at the moment of acquisition the act of prostitution lay either in the past or the future, never coinciding with the moment the animal was acquired.
