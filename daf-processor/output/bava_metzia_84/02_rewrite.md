# Bava Metzia 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Elazar as Policeman

### Aggadic Section Intro

Daf 84 plunges us into the middle of a large aggadic section of the Gemara — perhaps a reward, after all the difficult halachic material that preceded it. The transition is organic: since the Gemara had been discussing workers and their hours, particularly questions of what time people rise in the morning, it pivoted into a narrative involving Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. The story concerns a Jew who was appointed by the king to catch Jewish thieves and who had been offering advice on how to identify them — based, among other things, on who was nodding off at the tavern during lunchtime, which loops back to those very questions about workers' schedules.

### King's Appointment

Having demonstrated his expertise, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was assigned the task by the king himself: if you know what you are doing, you be the policeman.

### Proclamation Read

The Gemara records the moment of appointment with the phrase *karyana de-igarta ihu leihavei parvanka* — the reader of the letter should be the one who carries it out. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was thus brought forward and tasked with catching the thieves, *de-katafis ganvei*.

### Yehoshua's Rebuke

Upon hearing of this appointment, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha sent him a message: *chomets ben yayin* — vinegar, son of wine. You are a disappointment to your father. The full rebuke was: *ad matay ata moser amei shel eloheinu la-hariga* — how long will you continue handing over our people to be executed? The critique is sharp and obviously raises questions of perennial relevance: what does it mean to participate as a Jewish policeman, prosecutor, or officer in a non-Jewish government system, especially when the consequence of doing so is that fellow Jews are delivered to a system of justice whose punishments are far more severe than *halacha* would assign?

### Thorns Metaphor

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon sent back his defense: *kotzim ani mechaleh min hakerem* — I am removing thorns from the vineyard. These criminals are a danger within our own nation, and I am performing necessary work. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha responded in turn: *yavo ba'al hakerem ve-yechaleh et kotzav* — let the owner of the vineyard come and remove his own thorns. That is not your responsibility, and certainly not when the consequence is so harsh, so disproportionate to what *halacha* would actually prescribe. The exchange ends without resolution.

### Launderer Insult

One day, perhaps because Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's label had been circulating — even without the benefit of social media — a certain launderer, *hahu kovas*, encountered Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon and called him *chomets ben yayin* to his face. Even this ordinary commoner had picked up the epithet. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's reaction was immediate: *midei-chatzif kulei hai* — if this person has the audacity, the *chutzpah*, to address me this way, *shema mina rasha hi* — he must be wicked in other respects as well. He ordered his deputies to seize the man.

This moment crystallizes the danger the Gemara is illustrating. Even with the best intentions, once a person holds power, the line between legitimate judgment and abuse of that power becomes terrifyingly thin. He had just been insulted by this man. He rationalized the arrest by treating the insult as evidence of criminality — but the rationalization was, at least in part, driven by wounded pride.

### Realizes Error

After his anger subsided — *le-vatar denach daateih*, after his mind settled — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon realized he had acted rashly. He tried to go back and redeem the man, *azal batreih le-farukei*, but he could not; once turned over to the government, the matter was out of his hands. He recited about the situation: *shomer piv u-leshono shomer mi-tsarot nafsho* — one who guards his mouth and tongue guards himself from anguish. Rashi understands this as referring to the man who had been seized: had he guarded his tongue and not called Rabbi Elazar *chomets ben yayin*, this would not have happened to him. Yet one might equally read it as Rabbi Elazar reflecting on his own rashness: had he himself been more careful in how he acted upon what he heard, this catastrophe could have been avoided.

### Execution & Anguish

The man was strung up — *zakfu* — and Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon stood beneath, weeping.

### Man Was Wicked

Those around him attempted to console him. *Al ye-ra be-einecha*, do not blame yourself so much — this man truly was wicked. They told him: *she-hu u-veno ba-u al na-ara me-orasa be-Yom Ha-Kippurim* — he and his son had both had relations with a betrothed maiden on Yom Kippur. It is difficult to imagine a more severe accumulation of transgressions. He was, unquestionably, a *rasha*.

At that, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon placed his hands on his belly — and we will learn momentarily that he was a man of considerable girth — and addressed his innards directly: *sisu benei me-ai sisu* — rejoice, my innards, rejoice. Even in matters of uncertainty, *masfeika shelachem kach*, where I was not entirely sure, it turned out this way; *vada-i shelachem al achat kamma ve-kamma* — how much more reliable must you be in matters where you are certain. There is something striking about the image: his gut intuition, his *sevarat ha-kares*, as *poskim* sometimes call it — the instinct of the gut that functions without explicit textual proof — had proven correct.

He went further: *muftach ani bachem*, I am confident in you, my innards, *she-ein rimma ve-tole-ah sholetet bachem* — that no worm or maggot will have dominion over you at death, so pure is your intuition. Yet even having said all of this, *lo mityashev da-atei* — his mind was not put at rest. He remained anguished that he had caused this man's death.

### Fat Examination

The rabbis put him under a kind of anesthesia, *ashkyuhu samma de-shinta*, and brought him into a room of marble, *le-veita de-shisha* — a clean, protected space, like an operating theater — and performed what can only be described as a form of ancient liposuction. They cut open his stomach and removed *dikulei dikulei de-tarba* — baskets upon baskets of fat — and placed the fat in the sun during the months of Tammuz and Av, the hottest months of the year. *Ve-lo masriach* — it did not spoil.

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: *kol tarba nami la sariach* — ordinary fat does not spoil either. The distinction, the Gemara explains, is *shurakei sumkei* — fat that is streaked with red veins does spoil. Yet here, even the fat with red veins did not decompose. This was read as further validation: even his body bore witness that his gut instinct had been correct.

The power of this passage is worth pausing over. It is reminiscent of the problems in our own legal system — cases where people are pressured into confessions, where prosecutors push witnesses beyond what the evidence warrants. Here, perhaps he was not guilty of that particular crime, but he was guilty of something else. Yet that does not fully clear one's conscience when the process by which you acted was itself unjust. He had abused his power, at least as he understood the facts of the case, and he knew it.

### Finds Peace

With this biological evidence before him, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon finally recited the verse about himself: *af besari yishkon lavetach* — even my flesh will dwell in security. Even in death, he now believed, the worms would not consume him. The Gemara indicates that he was *mefuyas* — his mind was at last put to rest. Yet, as we shall see, the Gemara itself is not fully satisfied with the entire episode. The narrative as a whole makes clear that the Gemara does not endorse participation in such a role; the risks of executing the innocent, of abusing power even with good intentions, are simply too great.

## R. Ishmael Story

### Elijah Encounter

The Gemara continues: *v'af Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi*, even Rabbi Ishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi found himself in a similar situation — *ki mata ki hai ma'aseh l'yadeih* — the same circumstance came to his hand. He too was assigned to serve as a policeman, to catch thieves within the Jewish community. Elijah encountered him and posed the very same question that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha had posed to Rabbi Elazar: *ad matay ata moser amo shel Elokeinu la-hariga* — how long will you be handing over our people to execution?

### Elijah's Advice

Rabbi Ishmael ben Rabbi Yosi responded: *mai a'avid, harmana d'malka hu* — what can I do? These are the king's orders. Elijah's answer was not a halachic ruling but a practical directive: *avuch arak l'Asya* — your father fled to Asia Minor when confronted with such a demand. *At arak l'Ludkaya* — you can flee to Ludkaya. Run away rather than remain and send people to their deaths.

The lesson the Gemara draws is unambiguous: there are always alternatives. Even if in the end the story of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon suggested that he had, in some sense, acted correctly, the Gemara presents the risks as so severe — the danger of executing the innocent, the potential for abuse of power — that participation in such a role cannot simply be endorsed. The right path, when possible, is to find an exit. The disproportion between the punishment imposed by the non-Jewish government and what *halacha* would actually assign is not incidental; it is central to why the Gemara views this entire enterprise with such unease.

## Obesity Stories

### Two Obese Rabbis

Now that the Gemara has been speaking of both Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, it transitions to a passage about their physicality. *Ki havu mikla'ei Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi v'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon bahadi hadadi* — when these two would encounter one another, *havah ayili bikrei d'torei beinaihu v'lo havu nag'ei behu* — a team of oxen could pass through the airspace created between their protruding bellies without touching either of them. This is, of course, hyperbolic, and their immense size is clearly being conveyed through exaggeration.

### Matron's Challenge

A certain *matrona* — a woman of non-Jewish social standing — addressed them: *beneichem einam shelachem* — your children are not your own. How, given your obesity, were you capable of having intercourse with your wives?

### Responses to Matron

They responded: *shelahen gadol mishelanu* — our wives are larger than us. Rashi offers two readings: either that their wives' bellies were even larger than their own — which does not obviously answer the question — or that their wives' sexual desire was stronger than their own. The *matrona* was not satisfied. If the wives' bellies are even larger, *kal v'chomer* it would be even harder; if the wives' desire is stronger, perhaps they seek satisfaction elsewhere. *Ika d'amri*, another version of their response: *ki k'ish gvurato* — like a man is his strength, meaning that the sexual drive enables one to accomplish what needs to be accomplished regardless. Yet another version: *ahavah dochekes es habasar* — love itself compresses the flesh.

### Don't Answer Fool

The Gemara interjects: *d'lama lehu l'hadurei lah* — why did they bother responding at all? Does the verse not say *al taan k'sil k'ivalto* — do not answer a fool according to his foolishness? Engaging with such a taunt only pulls one down to its level — a principle that might serve as the governing rule for the overwhelming majority of online discourse as well.

### Defense of Legitimacy

The Gemara answers: *shelo l'hotzi la'az al beneihem* — to prevent slander from being cast upon their children. The *matrona* was not merely asking an indelicate physiological question; she was implicitly casting doubt on the paternity of their children. That required a response. And since Rashi explicitly glosses the response *ki k'ish gvurato* as referring to *ever tashmish* — the sexual organ — the Gemara proceeds naturally to the next discussion.

## Rabbis' Dimensions

### R. Yochanan Specs

Rabbi Yochanan said: *evrei d'Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi k'chamas bat tisha kabin* — the *ever* of Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yosi was like a water flask of nine *kavin*.

### Rav Pappa Compare

Rav Pappa added: *evrei d'Rabbi Yochanan k'chamas bat chamisha kabin* — the *ever* of Rabbi Yochanan was like a water flask of five *kavin*, and some say three *kavin*. As for Rav Pappa himself — *k'dikurei d'harfini* — like the baskets that were made in Harfini.

### Term Interpretation

The discussion raises an immediate interpretive question. The context that generated this passage was a discussion of obesity and sexual function — so some *rishonim* suggest that *ever* here refers to their girth and size generally, and not specifically to their sexual organ, which is its conventional meaning. *Tosafot* seems to take this view as well, adding that these words were written — *elu dvarim nichtvu shelo l'hotzi la'az al bnei adam shehem kach* — not to ridicule those who are of such dimensions. One reading of *Tosafot* is that the Gemara is explaining that even these enormously obese rabbis possessed the physical capacity for intercourse, and thus that no aspersions should be cast on their children. The Maharsha adds another dimension: even granting that this reflects a powerful sexual drive, the point is that these rabbis were nonetheless able to master and control that drive and did not sin in the sexual realm. Whatever the precise interpretation, it is a remarkable and striking passage.

## R. Yochanan Beauty

### Beauty Metaphors

From the discussion of Rabbi Yochanan's physical dimensions, the Gemara pivots to the theme of his extraordinary beauty. Rabbi Yochanan declared: *ana misharei deshifrei de-Yerushalayim* — I am a remnant of the beautiful people of Jerusalem, from the time when the *Beis Hamikdash* still stood. He then described what it would take for someone to get a sense of that beauty: *nayeiti kasa de-chaspa mibei salkei* — take a silver goblet fresh from the crucible; *u-nemalyei partzidei de-rimona sumka* — fill it with red pomegranate seeds; *ve-nahadar lei kelila de-varda sumka le-fumei* — encircle its rim with the petals of a red rose; *ve-notvei bein shimsha le-tula* — and set it in that space between direct sunlight and shade, in indirect light. *Hahu zaharurei me-ein shufrei de-Rabbi Yochanan* — that luminous glow gives you a sense of the radiance of Rabbi Yochanan.

### Chain of Beauty

But is this plausible? The Gemara challenges: was Rabbi Yochanan truly that beautiful? Is there not a tradition — attributed to *mar*, the master — that describes a chain of beauty descending through history? *Shufrei de-Rav Kahana me-ein shufrei de-Rabbi Abbahu* — the beauty of Rav Kahana offers a glimpse of the beauty of Rabbi Abbahu; *shufrei de-Rabbi Abbahu me-ein shufrei de-Yaakov Avinu* — the beauty of Rabbi Abbahu offers a glimpse of the beauty of our patriarch Jacob; *shufrei de-Yaakov Avinu me-ein shufrei de-Adam HaRishon* — and the beauty of Jacob offers a glimpse of the beauty of the first human. Rabbi Yochanan does not appear in this chain at all. How, then, can his beauty be the subject of such a description?

### Glory of Face

The Gemara answers: *shani Rabbi Yochanan de-hadrat panim lo havya lei* — Rabbi Yochanan was different because he did not possess *hadrat panim*. Rashi explains *hadrat panim* in a single word: *zakan* — a beard. Rabbi Yochanan apparently did not have one, and the chain of beauty catalogued above features men who possessed *hadrat panim*. Rabbi Yochanan's beauty was of a different, perhaps more ethereal character — the Maharsha suggests it was primarily the luminous aura or glow of his skin rather than conventional handsomeness of facial features. And one might note, in keeping with the earlier discussion, that Rabbi Yochanan was apparently quite portly — consistent with standards of beauty in the Babylonian Persian context of the Gemara's composition, where physical fullness was associated with health and prosperity.

### Mikvah Encounter

The Gemara relates that Rabbi Yochanan would go and seat himself at the gates where women would emerge from their immersion, *asha'arei tvilah*. His reasoning: *ki salkan bnot Yisrael mitvilat mitzvah lifge'u bi* — when the daughters of Israel come up from their immersion for the purpose of the *mitzvah* of *tahara*, let them encounter me first, *ki heichi delahavei lehu bnei shapiri kavasi* — so that they should have children as beautiful as I am, and *vegmiri oraisa kavasi* — as learned in Torah as I am. The underlying belief is that what a woman sees or thinks about at the time surrounding conception influences the child's appearance — a notion echoing Jacob's use of striped rods before the sheep mated.

This naturally raises a concern: does not the Gemara in Nedarim indicate that one should not think of someone other than one's spouse during intercourse? One possibility is that Rabbi Yochanan did not intend the impression to operate during the act itself, but rather immediately after emerging from the *mikvah* — and *Tosafot* cites another Gemara indicating that what a woman encounters right after *tevilah* can affect the child. A second possibility is that the relevant prohibitions in this area are not as categorical as they might initially appear. This is a larger discussion for another occasion.

### Evil Eye Defense

The rabbis challenged Rabbi Yochanan on a different ground: *lo mistafi mar me'ina bisha* — are you not concerned about the *ayin hara*, the evil eye? By displaying himself so prominently, was he not inviting misfortune upon his beauty? Rabbi Yochanan replied: *ana mizar'a diYosef kasina* — I descend from the tribe of Joseph, who is protected from the evil eye, *delo shalta bei eina bisha*. He cited the verse *ben porat Yosef ben porat alei ayin* — Joseph is a fruitful branch that rises above the eye. Rabbi Abbahu adds: *al tikrei alei ayin ela olei ayin* — do not read it as "over a wellspring" but as "rising above the evil eye." Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina brings a further proof: *veyidgu larov bekerev ha'aretz* — they shall multiply like fish in the midst of the land. Just as fish in the sea are covered by water and thus invisible to the harmful eye, so too the seed of Joseph is shielded: *ein ha'ayin sheletes bahen* — the eye has no dominion over them.

## Yochanan & Reish Lakish

### Jordan Jump

Having established the extraordinary beauty of Rabbi Yochanan, the Gemara arrives at the famous encounter with Reish Lakish. One day Rabbi Yochanan was bathing in the Jordan River — *hava ka sachi Rabbi Yochanan be-Yardenah* — and Reish Lakish saw him and leaped into the Jordan to go after him — *veshavar le-Yardenah avasrei*. Since Rabbi Yochanan had no beard, and given the extraordinary luminous beauty just described, it may well be that Reish Lakish mistook him for a woman.

### Torah Strength

Rabbi Yochanan spoke first: *cheilech le-oraisa* — your strength should go to Torah. You possess the athletic power to leap from a distance into the Jordan and swim after what you took to be a woman; that kind of strength belongs devoted to the study of Torah.

### Beauty Belongs Women

Reish Lakish responded: *shufrach le-nashei* — your beauty belongs to women. As my physical strength belongs to Torah, your beauty belongs to the realm of women.

### Sister Offer

Rabbi Yochanan then made his offer: *ei hadart bach, yahivna lach achtah de-shapira minai* — if you do *teshuvah*, I will give you my sister to marry, and she is more beautiful than I am. No one consulted the sister, but the offer was made.

### Strength Loss

Reish Lakish accepted upon himself to do *teshuvah* and to take upon himself the yoke of Torah. *Ba'ei lemehadar le-atuyei manei* — he went to return to the riverbank to retrieve his belongings — *velo matzi hadar* — and he could not. His physical strength had left him. Accepting the yoke of Torah had, as Rashi explains, channeled and exhausted the very power that had made his extraordinary physical feats possible.

### Teaching Talmud

Rabbi Yochanan taught him *mikra*, then *mishnah*, and ultimately made of him a great *talmid chacham* — *veshavyei gavra rabba*. What had been a gladiator of the body became one of the towering minds of the generation.

## Kelim Dispute

### Kelim Mishnah

One day the two were engaged in a dispute in the *beis midrash* over a *mishnah* in tractate Kelim. The *mishnah* lists: *ha-sayif ve-ha-sakin ve-ha-pigyon ve-ha-romach u-magal yad u-magal katzir* — the sword, the dagger, the *pigyon*, the spear, the hand-scythe, and the harvest-scythe. The question is *me-eimatai mekablin tuma* — from what point are they considered finished vessels susceptible to *tumah*? The answer: *mishe'as gmar melachtan* — from the time of their completion. But the further question is: what constitutes completion?

### Two Positions

Rabbi Yochanan held: *mishetzitchtzechun be-kivshan* — from the time they have been finished and polished in the furnace. Reish Lakish held: *mishetzitchtzechun ba-mayim* — not yet; you must then also polish them by rubbing with water, and only then are they fully finished and susceptible to *tumah*.

### Bandit Insult

Rabbi Yochanan responded to Reish Lakish's position with words that were not merely an argument but a wound: *ah lista be-listyutei yada* — ah, the bandit knows his banditry. Of course you know this — it is your field of expertise. It was not a kind thing to say. It was, at minimum, a reference to Reish Lakish's past, precisely the kind of remark about which the Gemara itself had spoken elsewhere under the heading of *ona'as devarim* — the prohibition of causing pain through words, and specifically the prohibition of reminding a person who has repented of who they once were. No one is saying Rabbi Yochanan did the right thing; it may have slipped out, or perhaps he meant it lightly, as a kind of compliment even — "you really know this area." But the effect was devastating.

### Wounded Response

Reish Lakish's response cuts to the heart: *u-mai ahant li* — what good did all of this do me? *Hasam Rabbi karu li* — there, among the bandits, I was also called "master," and here too I am called "master." So what exactly did your bringing me into the world of Torah accomplish? Now, *Tosafot* reads the story differently from Rashi, arguing that Reish Lakish had originally been a *talmid chacham* who had strayed and then returned, and that the reference to banditry is therefore not as it literally appears. This is admittedly a difficult reading given that Rabbi Yochanan had to teach him *mikra* and *mishnah* from the beginning. But Rashi's reading — that he had genuinely been a bandit before his *teshuvah*, and that the entire claim about banditry rests essentially on this single passage — is also worth noting: the image we carry of Reish Lakish as gladiator-turned-sage is based almost entirely on these few lines.

### Divine Closeness

Rabbi Yochanan's defense was: *amar lei hanei lach* — what do you mean, what good did I do for you? *D'karivtach tachas kanfei shechina* — I brought you under the wings of the divine presence. That is the benefit; not the honor, not the title, but the proximity to Torah and to God itself.

## Reish Lakish Death

### Anguish Causes Illness

*Chala daiteih d'Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan was deeply pained by Reish Lakish's words, and as a result, *chala Reish Lakish* — Reish Lakish fell ill. The causality flows from Rabbi Yochanan's anguish. Reish Lakish, in his wounded response, presumably did not mean it literally — he was reacting to an insult. But the damage was done. Rabbi Yochanan's pain over the exchange brought illness upon Reish Lakish.

### Wife's Pleas

Reish Lakish's wife — who was, of course, Rabbi Yochanan's sister — came to her brother weeping. *Asai bishvil beni* — pray for him for the sake of my son; do not leave my son without a father. Rabbi Yochanan answered with a verse from Jeremiah: *azva yasomayich ani achayeh* — leave your orphans, I will sustain them. Then she pleaded: *asai bishvil almanusi* — at least do something for the sake of my widowhood; do not let me become a widow. He replied again from that same verse: *ve-almanotayich alai tivtachu* — your widowhood too, trust in me, I will care for you. He would not relent. He was so consumed by the pain of having been apparently scorned that he was unmoved even by his own sister's tears.

### Reish Lakish Dies

Reish Lakish died. The parallel with the earlier story of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon is worth noting: in that case too, a rash reaction — driven by wounded feeling, a sense of having been disrespected — led to a person's death. There too there was deep regret and anguish afterwards.

### Consolation Attempt

*Va-hava kamitztayer Rabbi Yochanan basrei tuva* — Rabbi Yochanan was enormously anguished after Reish Lakish's death. The rabbis asked: *man lizei liyaseiv lidaiteih* — who should go and settle his mind, console him? Let us send Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat, *d'mechadid shmaiteih* — whose learning is very sharp; he will be a good intellectual partner for Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lament Challenge

Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat went and sat before Rabbi Yochanan. Every time Rabbi Yochanan said something, Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat said: *tanya d'mesaye'a lach* — there is a *baraita* that supports what you are saying. Rabbi Yochanan was not consoled. *At ke-Bar Lakisha?* — you think you are like Reish Lakish? When I would say something, *hava makshi li esrin v'arba kushi'ata* — he would challenge me with 24 questions. And I would answer — *v'hava esrin v'arba perukei* — 24 answers. *U-mimeila ravcha shmaiteih* — and through that, the learning expanded. And you — you bring me support? *Atu lo yadana de-shapir kamina?* — Do you think I do not know that what I said makes sense? Of course I know. That is not what I need.

### Value Opposition

The point is devastating. An intellectual companion who only confirms what you already believe teaches you nothing. The one who forces you to defend your position from 24 angles, to sharpen and refine and sometimes abandon your thinking — that person has given you something irreplaceable. *Higdil Torah*, as one might say: through genuine intellectual opposition, Torah itself is made greater.

## Teacher Lost

### Recognition

The Maharsha notes something profound about this moment: Rabbi Yochanan's lament reveals that he had come to understand he had not merely lost a student in Reish Lakish — he had lost a *teacher*. And since one is obligated to tear one's garments upon the death of one's *rav*, this recognition carries consequences.

### Rending Garments

Rabbi Yochanan rent his garments in mourning.

### Loses Mind

He went about crying — *va-hava kazil ve-ka bachi* — and calling out: *eicha Bar Lakish, eicha Bar Lakish* — where are you, Reish Lakish? Where are you? *Ad de-shaf da-ateih mineih* — until his mind became unseated. He lost his senses. He had lost not only his closest intellectual companion but, he now understood, the primary source through which his own Torah had grown and deepened.

### Prayer for Death

*Ba'u Rabbanan rachamei aleih* — the rabbis prayed for mercy on his behalf. But the mercy they prayed for was not recovery. To live without one's mind, in that state of derangement — that was not a life the rabbis could wish him. *Ve-nach nafsheih* — and he passed away. It is a grimmer resolution even than the first story. There, at least, the executed man was confirmed to be a *rasha*, and some measure of peace was found. Here, two great figures died, and there is no consolation.

## R. Elazar Regret

### Lost Trust

The Gemara now pivots back — and if one wondered whether the juxtaposition was intentional, the next words make clear that it was. *Va-afilu hachi lo samach Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon ada-ateih* — and even so, despite all the evidence that the executed man had been a *rasha*, despite the surgery that showed even his fat with red veins did not spoil in the summer sun, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon never trusted his own judgment again. He had caused a man's death on the basis of insufficient evidence, and no subsequent vindication could restore his confidence in himself as a reliable arbiter of guilt.

There is a powerful structural echo here: just as Rabbi Yochanan's angry, impulsive words led to irreversible consequences that left him inconsolable, so too Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon — acting out of wounded pride, rationalizing an arrest after being insulted — could never fully reckon with what he had done. Words said in haste, power exercised in anger: both stories are, among other things, meditations on *ona'as devarim* and on the terrible weight of holding authority. The application is not remote: the same pattern — an email written in rage, a message sent before the impulse subsides — continues to wound.

### Accepts Afflictions

*Kibel aleih yissurin* — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon accepted upon himself afflictions as an act of atonement for his role in that execution. This acceptance was not metaphorical; it was physical, ongoing, and extreme. The Gemara will describe exactly what it entailed.

### Boils Accept

Each night — *be-orta* — sixty cloth sheets, *namtei ahanamtei*, would be laid beneath him, and each morning — *le-tzafra* — sixty vessels of blood, pus, and fluid, *shittin mashchalei dama ve-chiva*, would be removed from underneath where he had lain. The sores covered his body through the night.

His wife, each morning, made him sixty types of a fig dish, *shittin minei lapda*, which Rashi identifies as a preparation believed to support healing, and he would eat it and recover enough strength to function. She refused to allow him to go to the *beis midrash*, *ki heichi de-lo lidchaku-hu rabbanan* — lest the rabbis cause him discomfort, whether by physical contact against his sores, or, as the story will suggest, by the anguish of their disapproval of his role as *parvanka*.

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, for his part, would address the afflictions themselves. Each night he would welcome them: *achai ve-re'ai bo* — my brothers and my friends, come. And each morning he would send them away: *zilu mikamei bittul Torah* — leave me now, on account of the Torah study you are taking from me.

### Wife Abandons

One night his wife overheard him welcoming his afflictions. Her response was blunt: *ad kabshat lehu illuyaich* — you bring these upon yourself? *Kilis mamon shel beis Abba* — you are destroying all of my father's wealth. She was the one with money in the family, and the care this required — sixty sheets a night, the medical attention, the fig preparations — was consuming it. *Imrada* — she rebelled against him and went to her father's house, or what was now called the women's house, *beis nasha*, since the patriarch was no longer alive.

---

## Sailors' Gift

### Sailors Arrive

With his wife gone, who would care for Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara provides the answer in dramatic fashion. *Seliku ve-asu hanahu nachshisin safunai* — sixty sailors came up from the sea. *Ayisu leih shittin avdei* — they presented him with sixty slaves. *De-nakiti shittin arnaki* — and they carried sixty wallets of silver and gold, which they gave him entirely.

### Storm Oath

*Tosafot*, expanding on Rashi, explains this by citing a *midrash*: these sailors had been caught in a terrible storm at sea and were about to be swamped. They prayed and took an oath to give their wealth to a great sage — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon — and the storm quieted. This is, in its essential form, a story very much like that of Jonah: the sea storm, the sailors, the oath, the calm. Having survived, they came to fulfill their vow.

### Care Received

The sixty slaves now provided the sixty sheets each night, the sixty types of *lapda* each morning, and all the care his wife had previously provided. He ate, drank, and recovered his strength.

### Wealth Reversal

When his wife sent their daughter to check on him, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon sent back a message: *shelanu gadol mishelahem* — our wealth is now greater than theirs. He had been accused of destroying her family's wealth through his medical expenses; he was now wealthier than they were. He recited about himself: *haysa k'oniyos socheir mimmerchak tavi lachmah* — she is like the ships of a merchant who brings bread from afar, the verse of *Eishes Chayil*. He applied it to himself, noting that his Torah — sometimes a metaphor for Torah itself — had brought boats from far away bearing sustenance and wealth.

## Niddah Rulings

### Blood Samples

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon went out to the *beis midrash* — now that his wife was no longer there to prevent him. The community brought before him sixty *maros*, sixty blood samples, on cloth, each requiring a ruling on whether the woman in question was a *niddah*. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon ruled them all *tahor*.

### Sixty Males

The rabbis murmured among themselves: *salkadaitach lavu chad safeik?* — is it possible that among sixty different samples there is not even one uncertain case, not a single one presenting a genuine problem? They were skeptical. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon responded with a declaration: *im kamosi hu* — if I am correct, *yichyu yiyu kulam zecharim* — every one of these women who will now go to the *mikvah* and be with her husband should conceive, and all the children born should be male. *Ve-im lav* — and if I am wrong, *tehei nekeiva achas beinehem* — let even one girl be born among them. The odds of sixty consecutive male births occurring by chance are staggeringly remote. *Hayu kulam zecharim* — they were all male.

### Named Elazar

*Ve-asku kulhu Elazar ashmay* — all sixty boys were given the name Elazar in his honor. It is worth pausing to note the resonance: Rabbi Yochanan had sat by the *mikvah* so that women emerging from *tevilah* would see him and conceive beautiful children in his image. Now Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, separated from his wife, rules the very *maros* that allow those women to be reunited with their husbands — and sixty children, all male, are named after him.

### Wife's Prevention

A *baraita* is cited: *amar Rabbi, kama pirya verivya bitla risha zo miyisrael* — Rabbi said, how much procreation did this wicked woman prevent from Israel? She had kept him from the *beis midrash*, and look what he accomplished in a single day. All those days he was kept away, women who might have been declared *tahor* were not, and the procreation that followed was lost. Rashi, perhaps wishing to soften this harsh characterization, suggests that *risha* — the wicked one — may refer to the evil government that conscripted him into the role of policeman, bringing the whole chain of suffering about. That reading is possible. The plain sense of the text, however, points to his wife.

## Death & Burial

### Attic Request

When Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was dying, he instructed his wife: *yadana be-rabanan de-risichi alai* — I know the rabbis are angry at me, Rashi explains, because of my role as policeman. *Ve-lo miaski bi shapir* — they will not attend to my burial as befitting my honor. *Ogni bi-aliyasi* — lay me in the attic. *Ve-lo tidcheli minai* — and do not be afraid of what you will find, do not fear the state of my body.

### Uncorrupted Body

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani transmitted: the mother of Rabbi Yonasan told him, having herself heard from the wife of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon: *lo pachos mi-tamni srei, ve-lo tfei me-esrin ve-tartei shanin* — for no fewer than eighteen years and no more than twenty-two years, she laid him in the upper chamber. When she would go up to check on him, she would look at his hair, and whenever a hair fell from his body, blood would emerge from the place it had been — meaning his body remained fully preserved, still vital in some profound sense, throughout all those years.

### Worm Appears

One day she saw a worm emerging from his ear, *richasha de-kanafik me-unei*, and was devastated — had his body begun to decay? He appeared to her in a dream and explained: *lo midi hu* — it is nothing to worry about. It happened because *yoma chad ashmia be-zilusa de-tzurba me-rabanan* — one day he heard someone disparaging a Torah scholar, *ve-lo mechai kidevayei lei* — and he did not protest as fully and forcefully as he should have. The worm from the ear was the consequence.

### Heavenly Judgment

Despite being dead and lying in the attic, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon continued to issue legal rulings. When two people came for a *din*, they would stand at the door of the house, each state their case in turn, and *naphik kala me-iliyasa* — a voice would emerge from the upper chamber, declaring: *ish ploni atah chayav, ish ploni atah zakai* — this party is liable, that party is acquitted. What to make of this is not entirely clear, but the Gemara records it without apparent skepticism.

### Proper Burial

One day his wife quarreled with a neighbor, and the neighbor cursed her: *tehei ke-va-ala shelo nitan le-kvura* — may you be like your husband who has not been given burial. The rabbis heard this and said: *kulei hai vadai lav orach ara* — clearly this entire situation is no longer becoming. Whatever the original intention, leaving a body unburied had now become, in the eyes of others, not a *nes* but a shame. It was time to bury him properly.

## Burial Obstacles

### Rabbis Intervene

The rabbis moved to take the body and arrange for burial. Some sources indicate that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — Rabbi Elazar's father — appeared to the rabbis in a dream: *peirada achas yesh li beineichem* — I have one dove among you, my son, and *ee attem rotzim le-havi-ah eitzli* — you do not wish to bring him to me? The time had come.

### Village Protection

*Azlu rabbanan le-asukei bei* — the rabbis went to take care of the body for burial. *Lo shavku bnei Akhbaraya* — the residents of Akhbaraya, the town where he had lived, would not let them remove him. Their reason: *de-kol shanei de-damich Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon be-iliyasa, lo sali chaya le-masayehu* — all the years that he lay sleeping in the attic, no harmful animal ever came to their village. He had been protecting them, and they were not prepared to give that up.

### Erev Yom Kippur

A window of opportunity presented itself. One day it was *erev Yom Kippur*, and the village residents were consumed with preparations — eating before the fast, attending to their affairs. The rabbis sent the people of a neighboring village, *bnei Biri*, and they slipped in, lifted his bier, *de-askiya le-arseih*, and carried it to the cave of his father, *le-me-arata d'avuha* — the burial cave of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai.

### Snake Miracle

When they arrived at the cave, they found a serpent encircling the entrance, blocking it — Rashi describes it as having swallowed its own tail, forming a ring. They said to it: *achana achana, pishach picha* — serpent, serpent, open your mouth, release your tail, open the way — *ve-yikanas beira eitzel aviv* — and let the son come in and be with his father. *Pachach lehu* — it opened.

### Final Burial

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was buried alongside his father, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai.

## HaNasi's Courtship

### Marriage Proposal

Now that Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was buried, *shlach Rebbi le-dabair be-ishah* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi sent someone to speak on his behalf to the widow, seeking to marry her.

### Vessel Metaphor

She sent back: *kli she-nishtamesh bo kodesh yishtamesh bo chol* — should a vessel that has served the holy now serve the profane? She was comparing herself to a sacred utensil: having been the wife of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, she was not available to be married to someone of lesser sanctity.

### Weapons Pouch

The Gemara notes that in *Eretz Yisrael* they expressed this idea differently: *be-asar demarei beisa tala zainei, kuba raya kulsei tala* — in the same peg where the master of the house hung his weapons, should the shepherd hang his pouch? The imagery is somewhat more pointed, but the message is the same.

### Greater Deeds

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was not deterred. He conceded the point about Torah — Rabbi Elazar was greater in Torah. But *be-maasim tovim* — in good deeds, in public service — was Rabbi Elazar truly greater? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was, after all, the *Nasi*, the head of the Jewish community, a man of enormous wealth who provided for his people.

### Afflictions Counter

The widow's response was sharp. First: you yourself have just acknowledged that he surpassed you in Torah. That alone is significant. But as for *maasim tovim* — *de-kibel alei yisurei* — he accepted upon himself afflictions. He chose to suffer. She understood that willingness to accept suffering as a form of personal spiritual greatness, a *maasim tovim* of internal character, that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, for all his public achievement, had not matched.

And there the shiur concludes, having traced the full arc of two intertwined aggadic narratives — the stories of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon and of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — both of which circle, in different registers, around the same essential questions: the weight of power, the permanence of words spoken in anger, and the courage it takes to face honestly what we have done.
