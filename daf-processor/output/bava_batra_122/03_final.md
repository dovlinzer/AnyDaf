# Bava Batra 122 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 13th Tribe & Nasi

### Atida Eretz Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲתִידָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁתִּתְחַלֵּק לִשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים – שֶׁבַּתְּחִלָּה לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים. וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא בְּכֶסֶף – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּין רַב לִמְעָט״.
>
> **Translation:** **Eretz Yisrael is destined to be divided among thirteen tribes** during the messianic era, unlike the division in the time of Joshua. **As, initially** the land was **divided only among twelve tribes,** as the Torah does not allot a portion to the tribe of Levi. The *baraita* continues: **And** the land was **divided only with money,** such that each tribe that received a portion more valuable than average compensated another tribe that had received a portion less valuable than average, **as it is stated: "Between the more and the fewer."**

The *gemara* opens with a striking statement from one of the *amoraim*: *atida Eretz Yisrael she-techalek l'shlosha asar shevatim* — in the future, the Land of Israel will be divided not among twelve tribes but among thirteen. The immediate question is: *l'man* — for whom is the thirteenth portion? Rav Chisda answers: *l'nasi* — it is for the *nasi*.

The *nasi* described in Yechezkel is a figure who functions somewhat like a king but is conspicuously not called one. Yechezkel depicts the *nasi* as having special *korbanot* to bring, a designated place in the *beit hamikdash*, and a particular gate through which he enters. Much of this appears in the *haftarot* as well — notably the *haftarah* for Parshat HaChodesh, which dwells at length on the *nasi*'s role in preparation for Pesach.

A central question surrounds whether Yechezkel's *nevuot* about the *beit hamikdash* were intended for *bayit sheni* or *bayit shlishi*. The conclusion reached in the Tannaitic period was that they cannot have been intended for *bayit sheni* — the Sages of that era saw *bayit sheni* with their own eyes and built it differently, closer to *bayit rishon* in structure. Therefore, Yechezkel's *nevuah* must be pointing toward *bayit shlishi*, and the *nasi* he describes must correspond in some way to *melech hamoshiach*. Yet this raises a genuine difficulty: why would a *navi* prophesying during the first *galus*, before *bayit sheni* had even arisen, be speaking about *bayit shlishi*? The general principle that *nevuah* can only be understood after the fact offers some comfort, though the full tension here is not easily resolved.

The broad consensus is that *bayit shlishi* and *yemei hamoshiach* are effectively coextensive. The underlying difficulty is that many components of the ideal *halachic* society are interdependent: you need *kohanim* who are *tahor*, a functioning *beit hamikdash*, a king, and a *sanhedrin* — and each presupposes the others in ways that create something of a logical loop. The solution is that *mashiach* will come, and a *navi* will be needed to identify the *kohanim meyuchasim*, to establish who descends from *Dovid HaMelech*, and to determine where the *mizbeach* belongs. The *nasi* of Yechezkel is, on this reading, the precursor or embodiment of the restoration of *malchus beit Dovid* — and his ancestral portion is to be a separate *nachala* from those of the twelve *shevatim*, with the inherited land of *Yerushalayim* itself remaining, as always, beyond the domain of any single tribe.

The question of which twelve tribes form the base count is itself non-trivial: do we count Yosef as a single tribe with Levi receiving no territorial portion, or do we count Ephraim and Menashe separately while Levi again goes without? Either arrangement yields twelve. In the Messianic context, the question of whether the *leviim* will receive a full *nachala* like the other tribes rather than being confined to designated cities is left open, though the closing chapters of Yechezkel address this at length.

It is worth noting that *chazal* were already acutely aware of internal tensions within Sefer Yechezkel — to the point that there were voices in the Tannaitic period calling for the book to be *gonez*, to be set aside as canonical. Various *drashot* were marshalled to defend its place, but that discussion belongs elsewhere.

### Nasi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: עֲתִידָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁתִּתְחַלֵּק לִשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים. אִידַּךְ לְמַאן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לְנָשִׂיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָעֹבֵד הָעִיר יַעַבְדוּהוּ מִכֹּל שִׁבְטֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to analyze the *baraita* just cited. **The Master says** above: **Eretz Yisrael is destined to be divided among thirteen tribes.** The Gemara asks: As to **the other,** thirteenth portion, **for whom** is it? **Rav Ḥisda said: For the king, as it is written: "And they that serve the city, out of all the tribes of Israel, shall till it"** (Ezekiel 48:19).

Rav Chisda's proof that the *nasi* receives a distinct territorial portion derives from the verse: *ve-ha-oved ha-ir ya'avduhu mikol shivtei Yisrael* — the city that serves him shall be worked by all the tribes of Israel. The force of the proof lies in the phrase *mikol shivtei Yisrael*: the area subject to the *nasi*'s authority is drawn from all the other tribes, implying it constitutes a reallocated portion carved out of the collective holdings. It is worth noting that other passages in Yechezkel might seem to offer more direct proof of the *nasi*'s special *nachala* — including passages read in the *haftarah* of Parshat HaChodesh — which makes the *gemara*'s choice of this particular verse somewhat surprising.

### Workers vs Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא רוּנְגָּר בְּעָלְמָא! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנּוֹתָר לַנָּשִׂיא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה, לִתְרוּמַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְלַאֲחֻזַּת הָעִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** Why is the verse interpreted in this way? **Say** that the verse speaks of **mere wages [*rongar*],** so that the king has rights to collect taxes, but not an actual portion of land. The Gemara answers: That possibility should **not enter your mind, as it is written: "And the residue shall be for the prince, on the one side and on the other of the sacred offering and of the possession of the city"** (Ezekiel 48:21).

Rav Pappa challenges the inference by raising an alternative reading: *eima rungar be-alma* — perhaps the verse is speaking of nothing more than hired laborers. The *Rashbam* explains *rungar* as *schirim be-alma la-avod bo ke-talmidim le-rav* — ordinary hired workers, akin to students who serve their teacher. On this reading, the verse describes workers from various tribes laboring in fields the *nasi* happens to own, without implying that he possesses a distinct ancestral *nachala* carved from the other tribes' portions. The *gemara* rejects this interpretation based on another verse in Yechezkel: *ve-hanosar l'nasi mizeh umizeh litrumat hakodesh v'la'achuzat ha'ir* — the remainder goes to the *nasi* from this side and that side, as a dedication to the *kodesh* and for control of the city.

This verse creates its own complexity, since it appears to give the *nasi* property rights over the *kodesh* and the city of *Yerushalayim*, which we would normally say was not apportioned to any tribe: *Yerushalayim lo nitchalka lishvatim*. The resolution seems to be that the *nasi*'s control does not violate the principle that *Yerushalayim* belongs to all the tribes collectively, but precisely how those two claims are reconciled remains a genuine difficulty in parsing Yechezkel.

### Yechezkel's Nasi

The broader picture that emerges is as follows: there are twelve tribal portions plus a thirteenth for the *nasi* and his family. The *nasi*'s holding is distinct from *shevet Yehudah*'s allocation and is not the same as *Yerushalayim* itself, which belongs to *kol hashevatim*. Whatever the *nasi*'s summer estate or seat of governance turns out to be, it is somewhere separate from the capital. These questions cannot be resolved fully from the *gemara*'s discussion alone.

### Land Valuation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא לִכְסָפִים – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּין רַב לִמְעָט״. לְמַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְשׁוּפְרָא וְסַנְיָא, אַטּוּ בְּשׁוּפְטָנֵי עָסְקִינַן? אֶלָּא לִקְרוֹבָה וּרְחוֹקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also states: **And** the land was **divided only with money, as it is stated: "Between the more and the fewer." With regard to what** is this said? **If we say** it is with regard **to beauty and ugliness,** i.e., that those who receive inferior-quality land received monetary compensation from the others, **is that to say we are dealing with fools [*beshufetanei*]** who would agree to take inferior-quality land in exchange for more money? **Rather,** it is said **with regard to** the difference between land that is **close** to Jerusalem **and** land that is **far** from Jerusalem.

The *gemara* then turns to the question of how compensation was managed when the land was divided — this now referring to the original division under Yehoshua. The principle stated is *velo nitchaleka ela bechasafim* — it was divided according to cash value. The question raised is: *lemai* — what precisely does this mean?

The *gemara* first considers whether the cash-value adjustment was meant to account for the superior or inferior quality of the land itself — better arable soil versus worse. But it dismisses this: *atu beshuftani askinan* — are we really dealing with people so foolish that they wouldn't automatically account for agricultural productivity when sizing portions? Of course the physical quality of the land factors in.

The real issue, the *gemara* concludes, is *lekurva velirchoka* — proximity to or distance from the city. A field located near an urban center commands a premium because of market access and ease of commerce. A more remote parcel of equivalent arable quality is simply worth less. It is this locational differential that requires some form of cash-value equalization. In modern terms: location, location, location.

The question then becomes what form the compensation takes — does the equalization happen in cash paid into some communal fund, or is the land itself simply re-partitioned until each person receives an equivalent value? The *gemara* poses this directly: *velo nitchaleka ela bechasafim* suggests it was denominated in cash terms rather than purely re-divided in land. This itself creates a tension, since land is regarded as intrinsically more valuable than cash — it is productive, permanent, and in the context of *nachalot* will belong to a family essentially forever. The best use of any cash compensation would be to reinvest it in land, yet land purchased in this era would revert at the *yovel*. These considerations suggest that the all-cash model leaves the compensated party with the short end of the deal.

## Goral & Exceptions

### Eliezer vs Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּכְסָפִים הֶעֱלוּהָ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בְּקַרְקַע הֶעֱלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: There **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to the manner by which this compensation was given: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The tribes **compensated** each other **with money. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The tribes **compensated** each other **with land** by giving extra land to those whose portions were in less advantageous locations.

The *gemara* notes that the dispute among the *amoraim* over cash versus land compensation mirrors a Tannaitic *machlokes*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer bechasafim he'aluha* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that equalization was effected through cash payments; *Rabbi Yehoshua omer becharka he'aluha* — Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the equalization was done through land itself, i.e., the parcels were re-subdivided until each was of equivalent value. Both agree, however, on the fundamental point: *velo nitchaleka ela begoral* — the division was ultimately determined by lottery.

### Ach Begoral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא בְּגוֹרָל – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַךְ בְּגוֹרָל״. תָּנָא: ״אַךְ בְּגוֹרָל״ – יָצְאוּ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב. לְמַאי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא שְׁקוּל כְּלָל; הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָאו דִּידְהוּ שְׁקוּל, דִּידְהוּ מִיבַּעְיָא?! אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא נָטְלוּ בְּגוֹרָל, אֶלָּא עַל פִּי ה׳. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ – דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל פִּי ה׳ נָתְנוּ לוֹ אֶת הָעִיר אֲשֶׁר שָׁאָל, אֶת תִּמְנַת סֶרַח בְּהַר אֶפְרָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also states: **And** the land was **divided only by a lottery, as it is stated: "Only by lot** shall the land be divided" (Numbers 26:55). The Sages **taught:** In the phrase **"only by lot,"** the term "only" indicates that **Joshua and Caleb** are **excluded** from this proviso. The Gemara asks: With regard **to what** were they excluded? **If we say that** they did **not take** portions **at all, now** that it has already been taught that they **took** the portions of the spies (118b) **that** were **not their** own, **is** it **necessary** to teach that they took **their own** portions? It goes without saying that they did collect their portions. **Rather,** the exclusion teaches **that** they **did not take** portions **by a lottery but according to** explicit designation by **the Lord.** With regard to **Joshua,** this is **as it is written: "According to the commandment of the Lord they gave him the city that he asked, even Timnath Serah in the hill-country of Ephraim"** (Joshua 19:50).

The *gemara* derives this from the verse: *ach begoral*. The word *ach* characteristically signals an exclusion. Who, then, is excluded from the *goral*? *Yatzu Yehoshua veCalev* — Yehoshua and Calev did not participate in the lottery. These were the only men who had been over the age of twenty at the time of the Exodus and were permitted to enter the land. All other men of that generation had died in the wilderness.

The *gemara* then asks: *l'mai* — what precisely does the exclusion mean? One possibility: they simply did not receive any portion at all — *d'lo shakil klal*. But the *gemara* dismisses this: *hashta d'lav didhu shakol didhu mibaya* — if even properties belonging to others were acquired for them, surely their own personal *chelekei meraglim* would be theirs. The earlier discussion in this chapter established that Yehoshua and Calev received the portions that would otherwise have gone to the ten *meraglim* who failed — a kind of providential reassignment. Just as the *bnot Tzelafchad* had to contend to receive their father's portion, here the two faithful *meraglim* received what the unfaithful ones forfeited.

The correct interpretation of *ach begoral* is therefore: *shelo natlu begoral* — they received their inheritances, but those inheritances were not assigned by random lottery. Rather, they were designated directly: *ela al pi Hashem* — by divine instruction.

### Joshua's City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״סֶרַח״, וּכְתִיב: ״חֶרֶס״! אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בַּתְּחִלָּה פֵּירוֹתֶיהָ כְּחֶרֶס, וּלְבַסּוֹף פֵּירוֹתֶיהָ מַסְרִיחִין. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בַּתְּחִלָּה מַסְרִיחִין, וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּחֶרֶס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects: **It is written** concerning Joshua's burial: "And they buried him in the border of his inheritance in Timnath **Serah"** (Joshua 24:30), **and it is written:** "And they buried him in the border of his inheritance in Timnath **Heres"** (Judges 2:9). Why is the name changed? **Rabbi Elazar says: Initially, its fruits** were as dry **as clay [*keḥeres*], and ultimately, its fruits** were so plump that they were **spoiling [*masriḥin*]. And there are** those **who say** the opposite: **Initially,** the fruits were **spoiling** prematurely, **and ultimately,** they lasted as long **as clay** without spoiling.

For Yehoshua, the *gemara* cites the verse: *al pi Hashem natnu lo et ha'ir asher sha'al et Timnat Serach b'har Ephraim* — God designated for Yehoshua the city of Timnat Serach in the hill country of Ephraim. This places Yehoshua's city within the tribal territory of Ephraim, in the Shomron region, where he would live and where his descendants after him would inherit.

The *gemara* then notes a textual problem: the city is called *Serach* in one place and *Cheres* in Sefer Shoftim. *Cheres* means pottery. How is the same city referred to by two different names?

Rabbi Elazar explains that the two names are anagrams of each other — the same letters, reversed. What the name-shift signals about the city's character is the subject of a further dispute. One view: *batchila peroteha k'cheres* — originally the fruits of the city were dry and inedible like pottery, *u'l'basof peroteha masrichin* — and eventually they rotted and spoiled. The other view reverses the order: initially the fruits were putrid, and ultimately they became dry like pottery.

There is a suggestion that *k'cheres* might actually be positive — fruit that is hard and dry-textured can be stored for a long time without spoiling — but this reads as somewhat apologetic. The more natural reading of both descriptions is that the fruits of Timnat Serach were consistently poor. This may carry a thematic resonance: the ten faithless *meraglim* brought back a negative report about the land, focusing precisely on its fruits. Yehoshua, who could see beyond the surface, inherited a city whose fruit was objectively unimpressive — yet he valued the *nachala* for what it was. The *gemara* in Berachot describes the fruits of Ginosar near the *Kineret* as so extraordinarily sweet that one had to eat bread afterward to neutralize the sweetness. The contrast with Yehoshua's city is striking.

### Caleb's Hebron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּלֵב – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְּנוּ לְכָלֵב אֶת חֶבְרוֹן כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר מֹשֶׁה, וַיּוֹרֶשׁ מִשָּׁם אֶת שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי הָעֲנָק״. חֶבְרוֹן עִיר מִקְלָט הֲוַאי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פַּרְוורַהָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת שְׂדֵה הָעִיר וְאֶת חֲצֵרֶיהָ נָתְנוּ לְכָלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה בַּאֲחֻזָּתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Caleb** also received his portion directly from God and not through the lottery, **as it is written: "And they gave Hebron to Caleb, as Moses had spoken; and he drove out from there the three sons of the giant"** (Judges 1:20). The Gemara asks about this verse: But **Hebron was a city of refuge** that belonged to the priests, as described in the book of Joshua (21:13); how could it have been given to Caleb? **Abaye said: Its outskirts [*parvaraha*],** i.e., only the fields and vineyards lying beyond the city limits, were given to Caleb. **As it is written: "But the fields of the city, and the villages thereof, they gave to Caleb the son of Jephunneh for his possession"** (Joshua 21:12).

For Calev, the verse states: *vayitnu leCalev et Hevron ka'asher diber Moshe vayoresh misham et shlosha bnei ha-anak* — they gave Calev the city of Hevron as Moshe had promised, and he drove out the three giants remaining there. It was called *Kiryat Arba* because four giants had once inhabited it; by the time Calev arrived, three remained, and he conquered them himself.

The *gemara* raises an apparent difficulty: *Hevron ir miklat havei* — was Hevron not a city of refuge? The six *arei miklat* were part of the forty-two or forty-eight cities assigned to the *leviim*. How, then, could Calev receive Hevron as his personal *nachala*?

The resolution, attributed to Abaye, is: *parvar ha* — it is the suburbs. The verse specifies: *ve-es sdei ha-ir ve-es chatzereha nasnu leCalev ben Yefuneh be-achuzaso* — the fields of the city and its environs were given to Calev as his holding. The city proper, including the *migrash* surrounding it as defined by *halacha*, belonged to the *leviim*. What Calev received was the broader suburban and agricultural zone surrounding those boundaries. This arrangement also has the elegant consequence that Calev, like Yehoshua, received his *nachala* within the tribal territory he would naturally have been assigned — Calev is of *shevet Yehudah*, and Hevron lies within *Yehudah*. Yehoshua's city of Timnat Serach is in *Ephraim*, which is his own tribe. Both men receive *nachalot* that are simultaneously exceptional — not assigned by *goral* — and fitting within the natural tribal geography.

Calev's connection to Hevron also carries midrashic resonance: the tradition holds that during the journey of the *meraglim*, Calev separated himself and went to pray at *Me'arat HaMachpelah* in Hevron, which became a source for the practice of visiting *kever avot*. His inheritance of that very city is a fitting reward.

## Son & Daughter Equal

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת בַּנַּחֲלָה; אֶלָּא שֶׁהַבֵּן נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם. וְהַבָּנוֹת – נִזּוֹנוֹת מִנִּכְסֵי הָאָב, וְאֵינָן נִזּוֹנוֹת מִנִּכְסֵי הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Both the son and the daughter** of the deceased are included **in** the *halakhot* of **inheritance. But** the difference is **that the** firstborn **son takes a double** portion **of the property of the father, and he does not take a double** portion **of the property of the mother. And** another difference is that **the daughters are sustained from the property of the father** after he dies, as it is a mandatory condition of their mother's marriage contract that they are to be sustained even before the estate is disbursed to the children, **but the** daughters **are not sustained from the property of the mother,** which is all inherited by the sons.

The next *mishna* opens with the statement: *echad ha-ben ve-echad ha-bat be-nachala* — a son and a daughter are equivalent in *nachala*. The *gemara* immediately identifies an apparent contradiction: have we not already learned in an earlier *mishna* that a son inherits before a daughter, and that *kol yotzei yerech shel ben kodmin labat* — every descendant of a son takes precedence over a daughter? How, then, can the *mishna* here declare them equivalent?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת לְנַחֲלָה״? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָרְתִי כִּי הֲדָדֵי, הָא תְּנַן: בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, כׇּל יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכוֹ שֶׁל בֵּן קוֹדְמִין לַבַּת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara analyzes the mishna: **What** is meant by the first clause of the mishna: **Both the son and the daughter** of the deceased are included **in** the *halakhot* of **inheritance? If we say that** they **inherit together, didn't we learn** in a mishna (115a): **A son precedes a daughter?** Additionally, **all descendants of a son precede a daughter.** It is clear that a daughter does not inherit together with a son.

The *gemara* then embarks on a four-part *sugya*, signaled by the mnemonic *nafsham* — *nun* for Rav Nachman, *peh* for Rav Pappa, *shin* for Rav Ashi, and *mem* for Mar bar Rav Ashi. Each *amora* proposes an interpretation of the opening clause of the *mishna*, and each attempt is blocked by the *ela* clause that follows. The structure is unusual — a four-part *sugya* of this kind is not common.

### Nachman's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: נַפְשָׁ״ם.) אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת נוֹטְלִין בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** ***Nafsham*** is **a mnemonic** for the names of the Sages cited in the following discussion: Naḥman; Pappa; Ashi; Mar. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: This** is what the mishna **is saying: Both the son and the daughter take in** inheritance the property **due** to their father **as** they would take **in** inheritance the property that he had **in** his **possession.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: *echad haben ve'echad habat notlin biraui kivemuchzak* — a son and a daughter inherit equivalently with respect to both *raui* and *muchzak* property. *Muchzak* property is what the father currently holds and controls. *Raui* property is what is due to him — property that would flow to him under standard inheritance rules from some other relative, even if not yet in his possession.

The *Rashbam* formulates it precisely: when the father is himself an heir to a more distant relative and that inheritance passes through him to his children, a son or daughter will receive it on equivalent terms. The concern the *mishna* addresses is that one might think a daughter's inheritance rights are a special exemption applicable only to ancestral land — to *nachala* in the narrow sense — but not to the full breadth of the father's estate. The *mishna* therefore establishes: *echad haben ve'echad habat* — they are equivalent across the board, whether the property is *raui* or *muchzak*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד נָטְלוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲלָקִים בַּנַּחֲלָה – חֵלֶק אֲבִיהֶן שֶׁהָיָה מִיּוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, וְחֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָיו בְּנִכְסֵי חֵפֶר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **We** already **learn** this **as well** in a mishna (116b): **Zelophehad's daughters took three portions** of land **in** the **inheritance** of Eretz Yisrael: **Their father's portion** that he received **because he was among those who left Egypt; and his portion** that he received **with his brothers in** the **property of Hepher,** their father, although Zelophehad predeceased his father and never was in possession of the inheritance from Hepher; and an additional portion that he received from Hepher because he was a firstborn. It is already taught in that mishna that property due to the deceased is inherited in the same manner as property possessed by the deceased.

This is supported by a *baraita*: *benot Tzelofchad natlu shloshah chalakim banachalah* — the daughters of Tzelofchad received three distinct portions: their father's share as one who left Egypt, his portion among his brothers in the inheritance from their grandfather Chefer, and a further portion from Tzelofchad himself. Three portions in total, demonstrating that daughters can inherit across the full range of *nachala* circumstances.

### Pappa's Bechorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מַאי ״אֶלָּא״? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת נוֹטְלִין חֵלֶק בִּבְכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** if the explanation of the mishna is as stated by Rav Naḥman, **what** is meant by the phrase: **But** the difference is that the firstborn son takes a double portion of the property of the father, and he does not take a double portion of the property of the mother? According to Rav Naḥman's explanation, what is the contrast between the two clauses in the mishna? **Rather, Rav Pappa said: This** is what the mishna **is saying: Both the son and the daughter** of the deceased **take a portion of the firstborn.**

Rav Pappa then proposes an alternative reading: *echad haben ve'echad habat notlin chelek bibechorah* — a son and a daughter can each receive a share of the firstborn's portion. A supporting *baraita* already states that the *bechor* takes a double portion. The implication here is that a daughter who inherits from her father can also participate in whatever the *bechor*'s double portion generates. But again the question arises: if a *baraita* already establishes this, why does our *mishna* need to say it? And the *ela* clause still has not been adequately accounted for.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל שְׁנֵי חֲלָקִים! וְעוֹד, מַאי ״אֶלָּא״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **We** already **learn** this in a mishna **as well** (116b), which explains the third portion taken by the daughters of Zelophehad: **And** they took an additional portion that he received from Hepher, **as he was a firstborn,** and a firstborn **takes two portions** of inheritance from his father. **And furthermore,** if the explanation of the mishna is as stated by Rav Pappa, **what** is meant by the phrase: **But** the difference is that the firstborn son takes a double portion of the property of the father, and he does not take a double portion of the property of the mother? According to this explanation as well, the first clause of the mishna has nothing to do with inheriting from the mother.

### Ashi's Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד בֵּן בֵּין הַבָּנִים וְאֶחָד בַּת בֵּין הַבָּנוֹת, אִם אָמַר: ״יִירַשׁ כׇּל נְכָסַי״ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: This** is what the mishna **is saying:** With regard to **both a son among the sons, and a daughter among the daughters, if** the father **says:** This particular child **shall inherit all my property, his statement stands.** A father can do so for any one son, or, when there are no sons, for any one daughter.

Rav Ashi proposes: *echad ben bein habanim ve'echad bat bein habanot* — a single son among other sons receives one portion, a single daughter among other daughters receives one portion, and in those respective contexts they are equivalent. If someone declares in a form of living will that a particular heir should receive all of his property — *im amar yerash kol nechasai devarav kayamin* — his words are upheld. The *gemara* identifies this position as that of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, as stated in a later *mishna*: if one designates someone *she-raui le-yorsho* — who is already a fitting heir — to inherit the entirety, his words stand; but if the designated person would not otherwise be a fitting heir — *she-eino raui le-yorsho* — the designation is ineffective.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן, כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא?! הָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ לְקַמַּן – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא אוֹמֵר: אִם אָמַר עַל מִי שֶׁרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין, עַל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ – אֵין דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion does Rav Ashi say this? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka?** The Gemara challenges: **But** the mishna **teaches this later** (130a), as **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: If** one **said about** another **who is fit to inherit** from **him** that the named individual should inherit all his property, **his statement stands,** but if one said it **about** another **who is unfit to inherit** from **him, his statement does not stand.** It is not reasonable to say that this mishna is stating the same *halakha* that is recorded in the later mishna in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty with Rav Ashi's reading: does this mean our *mishna* is a *stam mishna* that follows the minority position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka? The principle of *stam ve'achar kach machlokes* — an unattributed *mishna* followed later by a dispute — generally tells us that the position of the *stam* is the rejected one, since the *machlokes* that follows reveals it to be a contested view. The rule is that *ein halakha ke-stam* in such a case, and one must instead adjudicate by the rules of *klalei hafsak*. Normally we say *halakha ke-stam mishna*, but when a later *machlokes* bears on the same topic, the *stam* cannot be taken as definitive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: קָא סָתַם לַן כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא; סְתָם וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַחֲלוֹקֶת הִיא, וּסְתָם וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַחֲלוֹקֶת – אֵין הֲלָכָה כַּסְּתָם!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that the *tanna* here taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka,** in order to demonstrate that his opinion is accepted as *halakha*, this would not establish the *halakha* in accordance with his opinion. The reason is that this would be an instance of **an unattributed** mishna **and thereafter** a mishnaic **dispute** concerning the same matter, as in the later mishna there is a *tanna* who disagrees with the ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka; **and** in an instance of **an unattributed** mishna **and thereafter** a mishnaic **dispute, the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with the unattributed** mishna.

### Mar's Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מַאי ״אֶלָּא״? אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת שָׁוִין בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם וּבְנִכְסֵי הָאָב, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַבֵּן נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** if the explanation of the mishna is as stated by Rav Ashi, **what** is meant by the clause: **But** the difference is that the firstborn son takes a double portion of the property of the father, and he does not take a double portion of the property of the mother? According to this explanation as well, the first clause of the mishna has nothing to do with inheriting from the mother. **Rather, Mar bar Rav Ashi said: This** is what the mishna **is saying: Both the son and the daughter are equal** in their rights both **with regard to** the **property of the mother and with regard to** the **property of the father.** Sons and daughters can inherit from either fathers or mothers. **But** the differences are that **the** firstborn **son takes a double** portion **of the property of the father, and he does not take a double** portion **of the property of the mother,** and that the daughters are sustained from their father's estate before it is disbursed to the children, but they are not sustained from the property of their mother.

The final and accepted explanation comes from Mar bar Rav Ashi: *echad ha-ben ve'echad ha-bat shavin be-nichsei ha-em u-ve-nichsei ha-av* — a son and a daughter are equivalent in inheritance from their mother and from their father alike. The *ela* clause of the *mishna* then fits precisely: *ella she-ha-ben notel pi shnayim be-nichsei ha-av ve-eino notel pi shnayim be-nichsei ha-em* — the sole exception is that a *bechor* son collects a double portion from the father's estate but not from the mother's. The equivalence between son and daughter is the general rule; the *bechor*'s double portion from the father is the specific, named exception. This reading makes the *ela* clause integral and necessary rather than redundant, and it is Mar bar Rav Ashi's formulation that resolves the *sugya*.

## Pi Shnayim Debate

### Two Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם״ – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּכׇל הַנְּכָסִים?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"But he shall acknowledge the firstborn, the son of the hated, by giving him a double portion of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), this means the firstborn receives **double** the property received **by** any other **one** inheritor. The *baraita* analyzes this statement: **Do you say** the firstborn receives **double** the property received **by** any **one** inheritor, **or rather, is it a double** portion **of all the property,** such that the firstborn receives two-thirds of the entire estate, which is twice the portion left for the other inheritors to divide between themselves?

The *gemara* turns to a *baraita* analyzing the phrase *latet lo pi shnayim* from Parashat Ki Teitzei. The context is the passage forbidding a father from displacing his firstborn son in favor of a son born of a more beloved wife: *et ben ha-snu'ah yakir latet lo pi shnayim* — he must recognize the son of the less favored wife and give him the double portion. The question posed is: what exactly does *pi shnayim* mean?

Two interpretations are presented. The first is *pi shnayim ke'echad*: the *bechor* receives twice the share of any single one of his brothers. The second is *pi shnayim bechol hanechasim*: the *bechor* receives a double share of the total estate — meaning two-thirds of the entire inheritance, with the remainder split among all other heirs.

### Double Proportion

Under the *pi shnayim ke'echad* model, the inheritance is divided into one more share than the number of sons. With five sons, the estate is divided into six portions: the *bechor* takes two and each of the other four takes one. The *bechor*'s share is thus one-third of the total, and each other brother receives one-sixth. The *bechor* receives exactly double what any other single son receives.

### Two-Thirds Model

Under the *pi shnayim bechol hanechasim* model, the *bechor* always receives two-thirds of the total estate, regardless of how many brothers he has. With five sons, the four remaining brothers divide the remaining one-third among themselves, each receiving one-twelfth. His portion is double the combined sum of all the other heirs put together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד, וְחֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה; מָה חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד, אַף חֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** There may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with one** other brother, **and** there may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers, and there is no indication that the Torah differentiates between the manner in which the respective firstborns collect in each scenario. Therefore, **just as** when a firstborn takes **his portion,** dividing the estate **with one** other brother, he receives **double** the property received **by one** inheritor, **so too,** when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers, he receives **double** the property received **by one** inheritor.

The *gemara* notes that both models agree in the simplest case — two brothers. With two sons, the *bechor* receives two-thirds and the other receives one-third under either interpretation: two times one-third is two-thirds, and two-thirds of the whole is also two-thirds. The case of two brothers therefore cannot distinguish between the models, making it impossible to derive one from the other by simple analogy.

### Hanchilo Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״ – הַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה נַחֲלָה אֵצֶל אַחִין; הָא אֵין עָלֶיךָ לָדוּן כַּלָּשׁוֹן הָאַחֲרוֹן, אֶלָּא כַּלָּשׁוֹן הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** As the logical inference can lead to either conclusion, the *halakha* is determined by a derivation from a verse. **The verse states: "Then it shall be, in the day that he causes his sons to inherit"** (Deuteronomy 21:16). As the next verse spells out: "But he shall acknowledge the firstborn, the son of the hated, by giving him a double portion of all that he has" (Deuteronomy 21:17), the former verse is ostensibly redundant. The additional verse is therefore understood as an inclusion: **The Torah increased inheritance with regard to** the **brothers,** indicating that the more brothers there are, the greater the portion they collect from the entire estate. **Therefore, you should not reason in accordance with the final formulation,** in which the *baraita* suggests that the firstborn inherits twice as much as all the other brothers combined, **but in accordance with the first formulation,** according to which the firstborn inherits twice as much as each other brother.

The *gemara* resolves the ambiguity from the continuation of the verse: *vehaya beyom hanchilo et banav* — on the day he apportions to his sons. *HaTorah rivtah nachala etzel achim* — the Torah increases the *nachala* to be distributed among the brothers. The word *hanchilo et banav* implies that each son receives a meaningful *nachala* in his own right.

The *Rashbam* formulates this directly: *rivtah nachala shelo yitol pi shnayim bechol hanechasim heikha de'ika shnayim ushlosha achim milvado* — the Torah amplifies inheritance precisely so that the *bechor* does not take two-thirds of the whole when there are two or three other brothers. Under the two-thirds model, as the number of heirs grows, each non-*bechor* son is left with so small a portion that it barely constitutes a real *nachala* at all. The *pi shnayim ke'echad* model, by contrast, ensures that each son retains a proportionally fair share. The *halachah* therefore follows the first interpretation: the *bechor* receives double a single portion, not two-thirds of everything.
