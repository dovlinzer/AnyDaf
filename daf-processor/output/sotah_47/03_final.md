# Sotah 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levayah

### HaRav LeTalmid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרַב לְתַלְמִיד — עַד עִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A teacher** accompanies **a student until the outskirts of the city**

The daf begins on 46b, approximately twenty lines from the bottom, with a *baraita*: *tanu rabanan*, *harav letalmid* — even a rabbi who is hosting a student bears an obligation to accompany that student out of the city upon his departure. This is the *mitzvah* of *levayah*, rooted in the declaration of the *sanhedrin* in the *eglah arufah* ceremony: "our hands did not spill this blood" — meaning, we were not negligent in accompanying him out of the city. The *gemara* has been discussing this in the context of what declarations must be made in Hebrew, and now turns to the practical parameters of *levayah*.

### Distances: Ibburah, Tech…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָבֵר לְחָבֵר — עַד תְּחוּם שַׁבָּת
>
> **Translation:** **A friend** accompanies **a friend until the Shabbat boundary** of that city, which is two thousand cubits

How far must one accompany? The *baraita* specifies: *harav letalmid*, a rabbi accompanying a student, must go *ad ibburah shel ir* — until the outer houses of the city, the so-called "pregnancy" of the city, which corresponds to the measurement used for *techum shabbos*. *Chaver lechaver*, between equals, the obligation extends *ad techum shabbos* — two thousand *amot* beyond the city's edge, approximately one kilometer.

### Talmid LeRav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמִיד לְרַב — אֵין לוֹ שִׁיעוּר. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עַד פַּרְסָה.
>
> **Translation:** For **a student** who accompanies his **teacher, there is no measure** to the distance he accompanies him. The Gemara asks: **And how** far? **Rav Sheshet says: Up to a parasang [*parsa*],** which is four *mil*.

When the direction is reversed — a *talmid* accompanying his *rebbe* — the rule changes significantly. The *gemara* states *ein lo shi'ur*, there is no fixed limit. Yet immediately after asserting this, the *gemara* asks: *ein lo shi'ur vekama* — so how much really? Rav Sheshet answers: *ad parsah*, until a *parsah*, which equals four *mil*, roughly four kilometers — three times the distance of *techum shabbos*.

### Rebbe vs. Rabbo Muvhak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא רַבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּבְהָק, אֲבָל רַבּוֹ מוּבְהָק — שְׁלֹשָׁה פַּרְסָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And we said** this amount **only** with regard to one who is **not his most significant teacher, but** he accompanies **his most significant teacher,** who taught him most of his knowledge, **three parasangs.**

This limit of a *parsah* applies, however, only with respect to a *rebbe* who is not one's primary teacher. *Aval rabbo muvhak* — but for one's primary and most formative teacher — the obligation extends to *shalosh parsaot*, three *parsaot*, or twelve kilometers, approximately seven miles. This is a substantial distance, reflecting the depth of the relationship between a student and the teacher who shaped him most profoundly.

When they arrived there,** Rav Kahana **said to** Rav Shimi bar Ashi: Is it **true that you say** that **these palm trees** of **Babylonia have been** in this place **since the years of Adam the first** man?

### Adam HaRishon Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְכַּרְתַּן מִלְּתָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״בְּאֶרֶץ לֹא עָבַר בָּהּ אִישׁ וְלֹא יָשַׁב אָדָם שָׁם״? וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא עָבַר — הֵיכָן יָשַׁב (וּמֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא יָשַׁב — הֵיכָן עָבַר)? אֶלָּא: אֶרֶץ שֶׁגָּזַר עָלֶיהָ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן לְיִשּׁוּב — נִתְיַשְּׁבָה, אֶרֶץ שֶׁלֹּא גָּזַר עָלֶיהָ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן — לֹא נִתְיַשְּׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi **said to him:** By mentioning Adam the first man **you reminded me of something that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Through a land that no man passed through, and where no person [*adam*] dwelt"?** (Jeremiah 2:6). This verse is difficult: **Since it** is a land **through which no** man **has passed, where would he dwell? And if he did not dwell, where did he pass?** Why does the verse add that no person has dwelled there? **Rather,** this is the meaning: Any **land** concerning **which Adam the first** man **decreed that it would be a settled area, was settled;** but **a land** concerning **which Adam the first** man **did not decree that** it should be settled, **was not settled.**

Upon arriving at Bei Tinisa, Rav Shimi bar Ashi remarked that people say these palm trees of Bavel go back to the days of *Adam HaRishon*. Rav Kahana responded: *idkeran milta* — you have reminded me of a Torah teaching. He then cited Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chanina on the verse, *be'eretz lo avar bah ish velo yashav adam sham* — in a land that no person passed through and no *adam* ever settled. The question is a classic one: if no one passed through, it is obvious that no one settled — why repeat the point? The answer reads *adam* literally: only the land that *Adam HaRishon* decreed for human settlement was ever settled. The regions he did not designate remained uninhabited throughout history. Thus the two terms are not redundant; one speaks of ordinary travelers and the other of the primordial decree of Adam. The story illustrates how even the act of accompanying a *rebbe* — what might seem like mere travel — becomes an occasion for *divrei Torah*.

## Shofech Damim

### Rabbi Meir Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כָּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְלַוֶּה וּמִתְלַוֶּה — כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵי לִיוּוּהוּ אַנְשֵׁי יְרִיחוֹ לֶאֱלִישָׁע לֹא גֵּירָה דּוּבִּים לַתִּינוֹקוֹת
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Meir: Whoever does not accompany** another **or will not** allow himself to be **accompanied is like a spiller of blood** and is held responsible for any deaths that result from his inaction. The proof for this is **that had the inhabitants of Jericho accompanied Elisha, he would not have incited the bears to** attack **the children**

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir: *kol she-eino melaveh u-mitlaveh ke'ilu shofech damim* — anyone who neither accompanies nor is accompanied is as though he spilled blood. The inclusion of *mitlaveh* — the one being accompanied — is noteworthy: it implies that even the person refusing accompaniment bears responsibility if harm results. The connection to *eglah arufah* is direct: the failure to accompany leaves a person vulnerable, exposed to attack, and the community becomes implicated in whatever violence follows.

### Elisha & Bears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַל מִשָּׁם בֵּית אֵל וְהוּא עֹלֶה בַדֶּרֶךְ וּנְעָרִים קְטַנִּים יָצְאוּ מִן הָעִיר וַיִּתְקַלְּסוּ בוֹ וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ עֲלֵה קֵרֵחַ עֲלֵה קֵרֵחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is stated: "And he went up from there to Bethel, and as he was going up by the way, there came forth young lads out of the city and mocked him, and said to him: Go up, baldhead; go up, baldhead"** (II Kings 2:23).

The *gemara* now invokes a biblical narrative to drive this point home. *She'ilmalei livuhu anshei Yericho l'Elisha lo girah dubim batinokot* — had only the people of Yericho accompanied Elisha, he would not have sent the bears upon the young men. The verse (II Kings 2) recounts that Elisha traveled alone from Yericho to Beit El, at which point a group of young men emerged from the city, mocked him, and called out: *alei kerei'ach alei kerei'ach* — "get out of here, baldy." The *gemara* explains the word *kerei'ach* — baldy — as referring to: *shekeirachta lanu et hamakom*, you have stripped this place. These young men had been selling good water to the inhabitants; when Elisha performed a miracle sweetening Yericho's waters, he destroyed their livelihood. They were, in effect, accusing him of having impoverished them.

### Korach: Baldy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: עֲלֵה, שֶׁהִקְרַחְתָּ עָלֵינוּ אֶת הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: Go up,** away from here, **for you have made the place bald,** i.e., bare, **for us.**

What does the word *kerei'ach* — "baldy" — signify beyond the surface insult? The *gemara* connects it to *shekeirachta lanu et hamakom*, as just noted. The taunts were not random cruelty but a grievance rooted in their economic loss from Elisha's miracle.

### Ne'arim & Ketanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וּנְעָרִים קְטַנִּים״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁמְּנוֹעָרִים מִן הַמִּצְוֹת, ״קְטַנִּים״ — שֶׁהָיוּ מִקְּטַנֵּי אֲמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of: **"Young lads [*ne'arim ketannim*]"?** **Rabbi Elazar says:** The word "lads [*ne'arim*]" means that **they were shaken** empty **[*meno'arim*] of the mitzvot;** the word **"young [*ketannim*]"** means **that they were of little faith [*ketannei amana*],**

A deeper question surrounds the phrase *ne'arim ketanim* — literally "young lads who are small." Rabbi Eliezer explains: they are called *ne'arim* because they were *menue'arim min hamitzvot*, emptied of *mitzvot* — people with no serious religious commitment, which is why they could so callously insult the *navi*. They are called *ketanim* — small — because they were *m'katnei emunah*, of diminished faith, for rather than trusting that God would provide for them in other ways after the waters were sweetened, they attacked the man responsible.

Rav Yosef challenged this reading. Perhaps, he suggested, *ne'arim* simply designates their place of origin — there was a town called *Ne'urin*, and *ne'arim* would mean "people from Na'ar," not young men at all. He cites support from another verse (II Kings 5) describing a *na'ara ketana* — a young girl — captured from the land of Israel by Aram; perhaps there too, *na'ara* means she was from a place called *Ne'urin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא מְפָרַשׁ מְקוֹמָהּ, הָכָא מְפוֹרָשׁ מְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** There **does not specify her place** of origin, so "*na'ara*" could mean from the town of Ne'oran; but **here** the verse **specifies their place** of origin, namely Jericho.

The *gemara* responds: in the verse about the captive girl, her place of origin is not specified, so attributing *na'ara* to a geographical name is plausible. Here, however, the text explicitly states that these young men came from the city — from Yericho — *hacha mefareish mekumo*. The place is given; *ne'arim* therefore cannot be a geographical designation, and the original reading stands.

## Elisha's Curse

### Elisha's Ra'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּפֶן אַחֲרָיו וַיִּרְאֵם וַיְקַלְלֵם בְּשֵׁם ה׳״. מָה רָאָה? אָמַר רַב: רָאָה מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **"And he turned, he saw them, and he cursed them in the name of the Lord"** (II Kings 2:24). The Gemara asks: **What did he see?** **Rav says:** He **literally saw,**

The verse continues: *vayifen acharav vayar'em vayakalem beshem Hashem* — he turned, he saw them, and he cursed them in God's name. The *gemara* asks: *mai ra'ah* — what is meant by "he saw"? Was it simply that he looked at them?

Rav says: *ra'ah mamash* — he literally just saw them. Yet even this bare seeing is consequential, as we learn from a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel teaches that whenever the Sages directed their gaze against someone — *kol makom shenasnu chachamim eineihem* — that person either dies or becomes impoverished. A look from a *navi* of this stature carries devastating force.

### Mitigating Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּתְנוּ חֲכָמִים עֵינֵיהֶם — אוֹ מִיתָה, אוֹ עוֹנִי. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: רָאָה שֶׁכּוּלָּן נִתְעַבְּרָה בָּהֶן אִמָּן בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Wherever** it states **that the Sages placed their eyes** upon a certain person, they brought upon that person **either death or poverty. And Shmuel says: He saw** their essential nature, **that all their mothers became pregnant with them on Yom Kippur,** when conjugal relations are forbidden.

Shmuel offers a different reading: he saw that all of them had been conceived on *Yom Kippur* — their mothers had violated the sanctity of that day. This is the *gemara*'s attempt, however tentatively, to explain why such an extreme punishment befell them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: בְּלוֹרִית רָאָה לָהֶן, כְּגוֹיִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רָאָה שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה בָּהֶן לַחְלוּחִית שֶׁל מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says: He saw** that **they had plaited locks** grown on the back of their heads **like the gentiles. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He saw that they did not contain** even **a smidgen of a mitzva.**

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha adds another dimension: he saw that they wore their hair in a style called *blurei*, associated with the Amorites, suggesting involvement in idolatrous practices.

Rabbi Yochanan goes furthest in the prosecutorial direction: he saw that *lo hayta bahen lachluchit shel mitzvah* — there was not even a trace of moisture of *mitzvah* in them; they were entirely bereft of religious life.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא בְּזַרְעַיְיהוּ נִיהְוָה הֲוָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא בָּם וְלֹא בְּזַרְעָם עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** one will **come** to perform them **for their own sake.** He proves the value of a mitzva done not for its own sake: **As in reward for the forty-two offerings that Balak, king of Moab, brought, he merited that Ruth descended from him, from whom** King **Solomon descended, about whom it is written** that he brought many offerings: **"A thousand burnt-offerings did Solomon offer up"** (I Kings 3:4).

Yet even this does not fully satisfy. One might still argue: *vedilma bezar'aihu nihavi* — perhaps from their descendants something good might have come? Rabbi Eliezer answers: he saw that neither from them nor from any of their descendants through all generations would any good emerge — *lo bam velo bezaram ad sof kol hadorot*. The *gemara* is not celebrating what Elisha did; it is straining to make some peace with a troubling text by marshaling every possible mitigating consideration.

### Bears & Casualties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּצֶאנָה שְׁתַּיִם דֻּבִּים מִן הַיַּעַר וַתְּבַקַּעְנָה מֵהֶם אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁנֵי יְלָדִים״
>
> **Translation:** **"And two she-bears came out of the forest and tore forty-two children from them"** (II Kings 2:24).

*Vatetzeina shtayim dubim min haya'ar* — two bears emerged from the forest and mauled forty-two of the youths.

### Ness v. Ness B'Toch Ness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: נֵס. וְחַד אָמַר: נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס. מַאן דְּאָמַר נֵס — יַעַר הֲוָה, דּוּבִּים לָא הֲווֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס — לֹא יַעַר הֲוָה וְלָא דּוּבִּים הֲווֹ. וְלִיהְוֵי דּוּבִּים וְלָא לֶיהֱוֵי יַעַר! דִּבְעִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel** had a dispute with regard to this episode. **One says** there was **a miracle, and one says** there **was a miracle within a miracle.** The Gemara explains: **The one who says** there was **a miracle** claims that **there was** already **a forest** in that place but **there were no bears,** and the miracle was the appearance of bears. **The one who says** it was **a miracle within a miracle** claims **that neither was there a forest nor were there bears** in that area. The Gemara asks with regard to the second opinion: Why was a double miracle required? **And let there be bears and no forest;** the forest served no role in the story, so why was it created? The Gemara explains: The forest was necessary, **as** bears **are frightened** to venture into open areas but will attack people in their natural habitat, a forest.

A dispute between Rav and Shmuel: one said it was a single miracle; the other said it was a miracle within a miracle. According to the first view, there was a forest but no bears in it — the bears themselves were miraculous. According to the second, there was neither a forest nor bears — both were created for the occasion, making it a miracle layered upon a miracle.

This raises an obvious question: if both forest and bears had to be created miraculously, why create the forest at all? Why not simply produce the bears? The *gemara* answers: *de'vi'isi* — because the bears would have been afraid to attack without a place of refuge. Even within a miracle, the bears behave like natural bears; they will only charge if they have somewhere to retreat. The forest was necessary to make the miracle function through natural-seeming animal psychology.

### Arba'im u'Shnayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בִּשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁנַיִם קׇרְבָּנוֹת שֶׁהִקְרִיב בָּלָק מֶלֶךְ מוֹאָב הוּבְקְעוּ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁנַיִם יְלָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Due to forty-two offerings that Balak, king of Moab, brought** when he tried to have Balaam curse the Jewish people, **forty-two children were broken off from Israel,** in that incident involving Elisha.

Rabbi Chanina notes that forty-two children perished — and this corresponds precisely to the forty-two sacrifices that Balak king of Moab offered in his effort to bring a curse upon Israel through Bilaam. *Bishvil arbaim ushnayim korbanot shehikriv Balak melech Moav hufka miYisrael arbaim ushnayim yeladim*. Even Balak's evil sacrifices had some effect — not the effect he intended, but they registered sufficiently to ultimately account for these forty-two deaths.

## Shelo Lishma to Lishma

### Shelo Lishma Ba Lishma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲסוֹק אָדָם בְּתוֹרָה וּבְמִצְווֹת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ. שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ — בָּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: A person should always engage in Torah** study **and** in performance of **mitzvot, even** if he does so **not for their own sake, as through** such acts performed **not for their own sake,** one will **come** to perform them **for their own sake.**

This leads to a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: one should always engage in Torah and *mitzvot* even *shelo lishma* — not for the right reason — because from *shelo lishma* one comes eventually to *lishma*, acting for the proper motive.

### Balak & Rut Legacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבִּשְׂכַר אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁנַיִם קׇרְבָּנוֹת שֶׁהִקְרִיב בָּלָק מֶלֶךְ מוֹאָב, זָכָה וְיָצְתָה מִמֶּנּוּ רוּת שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנָּה שְׁלֹמֹה, שֶׁכָּתוּב בֵּיהּ ״אֶלֶף עֹלוֹת יַעֲלֶה שְׁלֹמֹה״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן חוֹנִי: רוּת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל עֶגְלוֹן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל בָּלָק הָיְתָה!
>
> **Translation:** **As in reward for the forty-two offerings that Balak, king of Moab, brought, he merited that Ruth descended from him, from whom** King **Solomon descended, about whom it is written** that he brought many offerings: **"A thousand burnt-offerings did Solomon offer up"** (I Kings 3:4). **And Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥoni** similarly **says: Ruth was the daughter of Eglon, son of Balak.**

The proof: *bishvil arbaim ushnayim korbanot shehikriv Balak melech Moav zacha veyatzta mimenu Rut* — in the merit of those forty-two sacrifices that Balak offered, he merited that Rut descended from him. Rav Yosef ben Choni specifies the lineage: Rut was the daughter of Eglon, who was the son of Balak. And from Rut came David and Shlomo — *elef olot ya'aleh Shlomo*, who brought a thousand burnt offerings to God for the purest of motives. Thus a chain beginning with Balak's deeply corrupted sacrifices ends with Shlomo's thousand offerings brought in perfect *lishma*.

### Ta'avato La'Kella

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּאֲוָתוֹ מִיהָא לִקְלָלָה הֱוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **His desire, in any event, was to curse** the Jewish people, and his reward for sacrificing his offerings was that the curse was fulfilled in the incident involving Elisha, as well.

One might object: surely the parallel is strained. Normally we say *shelo lishma* means a self-interested but not malicious motive. Balak's intent was thoroughly evil. Can we say even evil sacrifices lead to good? Moreover, did those evil intentions have no effect at all?

The *gemara* responds: *ta'avaso miya lekilela hava* — his desire *was* for curse, and that too had its impact. The forty-two deaths attributed to those sacrifices are the evil effect that his evil intention generated. The good that ultimately flowed from it — Rut, David, Shlomo — was the *shelo lishma* becoming *lishma* across generations, but his wicked intention was not without consequence either.

## Yericho & Chen

### Moshav HaIr Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר אֶל אֱלִישָׁע הִנֵּה נָא מוֹשַׁב הָעִיר טוֹב כַּאֲשֶׁר אֲדֹנִי רֹאֶה וְגוֹ׳״. וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּמַיִם רָעִים וְאֶרֶץ מְשַׁכֶּלֶת, אֶלָּא מָה טוֹבָתָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **"And the men of the city said to Elisha: Behold, please, the situation of this city is pleasant, as my lord sees,** but the water is bad and the land miscarries" (II Kings 2:19). The Gemara asks: **But if the water is bad and the land causes women to miscarry, what is pleasant** about **it?**

The *gemara* now circles back to the story of Elisha's arrival in Yericho. The people of the city addressed him: *hinei na moshav ha'ir tov ka'asher adoni ro'eh* — behold, the dwelling of the city is pleasant as my master can see. And yet the verse concludes that the waters are bad and the land causes miscarriage. The *gemara* presses: if the water is bitter and the land causes *shichlul*, what exactly is pleasant about the city? Why would they call it a good dwelling?

### Chen Makom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין: חֵן מָקוֹם עַל יוֹשְׁבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanin says: The grace of a place is upon its inhabitants,** i.e., people are fond of their hometown despite its shortcomings.

Rabbi Chanin answers: *chen makom al yoshvav* — a place has a certain charm upon those who dwell there. People who live somewhere develop a fondness for it that is not fully explicable from the outside. The inhabitants of Yericho found their city beautiful despite its obvious deficiencies, because familiarity and belonging produce an attachment that transcends objective assessment.

### Shlosha Chinot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, שְׁלֹשָׁה חִינּוֹת הֵן: חֵן מָקוֹם עַל יוֹשְׁבָיו, חֵן אִשָּׁה עַל בַּעְלָהּ, חֵן מִקָּח עַל (מִקָּחוֹ).
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There are three graces** that have a similar impact: **The grace of a place upon its inhabitants; the grace of a woman upon her husband,** despite her faults; and **the grace of a purchased** item **upon its buyer,** as one who has bought something views it in a positive light.

Rabbi Yochanan extends this into a general principle: *shlosha chinot hein* — there are three types of *chen*, charm, that operate similarly. *Chen makom al yoshvav* — the charm of a place upon its inhabitants. *Chen isha al ba'ala* — the charm of a woman upon her husband; what an outsider might not appreciate, a husband finds profoundly attractive in his wife, because *chen* is relational, not objective. And *chen mekach al mikcho* — the charm of a purchase upon its buyer; a person who has acquired something finds it wonderful in a way that someone who hasn't bought it may not see. All three share the same logic: intimacy and commitment generate a subjective beauty that is real even if not universally perceptible.

## Elisha's Illnesses

### Shlosha Cholaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲלָאִין חָלָה אֱלִישָׁע: אֶחָד שֶׁגֵּירָה דּוּבִּים בַּתִּינוֹקוֹת, וְאֶחָד שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְגֵחֲזִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, וְאֶחָד שֶׁמֵּת בּוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Elisha fell ill three times. One** was a punishment **for inciting** the **bears to** attack **the children; and one** was a punishment **for pushing Gehazi** away **with both hands,** without leaving him the option to return; **and one** was the sickness **from which he died**

A *baraita* teaches: *shlosha cholaim chala Elisha* — Elisha suffered three serious illnesses. The first was punishment for having sent the bears against the children. The second was for having pushed Geichazi away *bishtei yadayim* — with both hands — giving him only harsh rebuke without subsequently drawing him back. The third was simply the illness from which he ultimately died.

How does the *gemara* derive that there were three separate illnesses? From the verse: *ve'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo*. The *gemara* reads *chala*, *chalyo*, and *asher yamut bo* as three distinct expressions of illness — three episodes, three occasions of serious sickness.

### Smol Docha Yamin Mekarev…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא שְׂמֹאל דּוֹחָה וְיָמִין מְקָרֶבֶת, לֹא כֶּאֱלִישָׁע שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְגֵחֲזִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו, וְלֹא כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּרַחְיָה שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְיֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי מִתַּלְמִידָיו בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: It should always be** the **left,** weaker, hand that **pushes** another away **and** the **right,** stronger, hand that **draws** him **near.** In other words, even when a student is rebuffed, he should be given the opportunity to return. This is **not like Elisha, who pushed Gehazi** away **with both hands, and not like Yehoshua ben Peraḥya, who pushed Jesus the Nazarene,** one **of his students,** away **with both hands.**

From Elisha's error with Geichazi, the *gemara* derives a broader principle. *Le'olam tehei smol dochah ve'yamin mekarevet* — always let the left hand push away and the right hand draw close. When a person must rebuke and distance someone, the pulling close must ultimately be stronger than the pushing away. The *gemara* states explicitly: *lo ke'Elisha shedachafoto l'Geichazi bishtei yadav* — not like Elisha, who pushed Geichazi away with both hands. And the printed text says *velo ki-Yehoshua ben Perachiah shedachafoto le'echad mitalmidav bishtei yadav* — and not like Yehoshua ben Perachiah, who pushed away one of his students with both hands. The original text, which was censored to protect the Talmud from church persecution, names that student as Yeshu HaNotzri. The historical difficulty — Yehoshua ben Perachiah lived roughly a century before the common era, perhaps seventy years before Jesus — does not necessarily mean the *gemara* did not identify them; it may simply mean the *gemara*'s historical chronology does not align with ours.

## Geichazi Story

### Geichazi's Greed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלִישָׁע מַאי הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר נַעֲמָן הוֹאֵל קַח כִּכָּרָיִם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו לֹא לִבִּי הָלַךְ כַּאֲשֶׁר הָפַךְ אִישׁ מֵעַל מֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ לִקְרָאתֶךָ הַעֵת לָקַחַת אֶת הַכֶּסֶף וְלָקַחַת בְּגָדִים וְזֵיתִים וּכְרָמִים וְצֹאן וּבָקָר וַעֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **What was** that incident with **Elisha? As it is written: "And Naaman said: Pray, take talents"** (II Kings 5:23). Naaman offered Gehazi payment for the help Elisha had given him, **and** when the verse recounts Elisha's words to Gehazi, it **is written: "And he said to him: Did not my heart go, when the man turned back from his chariot to meet you? Is it a time to take money, and to take garments, and olives, and vineyards, and sheep, and oxen, and servants, and maidservants?"** (II Kings 5:26).

What precisely was Geichazi's sin? The *gemara* recounts: after Elisha had healed Naaman the Syrian general of his leprosy and explicitly refused any reward — establishing the principle that one does not take payment for such a *mitzvah* — Geichazi ran after Naaman in secret and took silver and garments in Elisha's name. When Geichazi returned, Elisha confronted him: *lo libi halach ka'asher hafach ish me'al merkavto likrasecha* — was my heart not with you when that man turned from his chariot to greet you? *Ha'eit lakachas et hakesef* — is now really the moment to accept such gifts?

### Shmonah Sheratzim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי שָׁקֵיל כּוּלֵּי הַאי? כֶּסֶף וּבְגָדִים הוּא דְּשָׁקֵיל! אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה הָיָה אֱלִישָׁע עוֹסֵק בִּשְׁמֹנָה שְׁרָצִים, אָמַר לוֹ: רָשָׁע, הִגִּיעַ עֵת לִיטּוֹל שְׂכַר שְׁמֹנָה שְׁרָצִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And did he take all that?** But **it was** only **money and garments that he took. Rabbi Yitzḥak says: At that time, Elisha was engaged in** the study of the topic of the **eight** impure **creeping animals. He said** to Gehazi: **Wicked one, it is time** for you **to receive** now, in this temporal world, **the reward** for studying the topic of the **eight** impure **creeping animals.**

The *gemara* notes that the verse mentions not only silver and garments but a long catalogue of goods: olives, vineyards, sheep, cattle, male and female slaves. But in fact Geichazi only took silver and garments — why does the verse enumerate so much more? Rabbi Yitzchak explains: *be'osa sha'ah hayah Elisha osek b'shmonah sheratzim* — at that very time, Elisha had been studying the laws of the eight *sheratzim*, the eight species of rodents that convey *tuma*. The eight items mentioned in the verse correspond to those eight categories. Elisha said to Geichazi: you have taken the reward for the *Torah* you studied — the reward you might have received in the World to Come for learning those laws you have now forfeited and will receive in this world in the form of material goods.

### Tzaraas Geichazi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְצָרַעַת נַעֲמָן תִּדְבַּק בְּךָ וּבְזַרְעֲךָ לְעוֹלָם״. ״וְאַרְבָּעָה אֲנָשִׁים הָיוּ מְצֹרָעִים״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֶה גֵּחֲזִי וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **"And the leprosy of Naaman shall cleave to you and to your descendants forever"** (II Kings 5:27), and that the verse later states: **"Now there were four leprous men"** (II Kings 7:3), about whom **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is** referring to **Gehazi and his three sons.**

The verse continues: *ve'tzaraas Naaman tidbak becha u-vezarcha le'olam* — the *tzaraas* of Naaman shall cleave to you and to your children forever. The *gemara* connects this to a later verse: *ve'arba'ah anashim hayu metzora'im petach hasha'ar* — four men afflicted with *tzaraas* sat at the gate. Rabbi Yochanan identifies them as Geichazi and his three sons.

### Elisha Seeks Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּבֹא אֱלִישָׁע דַּמֶּשֶׂק״. לָמָּה הָלַךְ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁהָלַךְ לְהַחְזִירוֹ לְגֵחֲזִי בִּתְשׁוּבָה, וְלֹא חָזַר. אָמַר לוֹ: חֲזוֹר בָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **"And Elisha came to Damascus"** (II Kings 8:7). The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose did **he go** there? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He went to help Gehazi in repentance, but** Gehazi **would not** agree to **repent** from his evil ways. Elisha **said to him: Return from your** sins.

A later verse records: *vayelech Elisha Damesek* — Elisha went to Damascus. Why did he go? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *shehalach lehachziro l'Geichazi b'teshuva* — he went to bring Geichazi back to *teshuvah*. Elisha had apparently come to feel some remorse about how he had handled his student and sought to repair it. He said to Geichazi: *chazor becha* — return, repent.

### Ein Maspikin B'Yado

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִמְּךָ, כׇּל מִי שֶׁחָטָא וְהֶחְטִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים — אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Gehazi **said to him: This is** the tradition that **I received from you: Whoever sins and caused the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent.**

Geichazi refused. He said: *kach mikublani mimcha kol mi shechata vehichti et harabbim ein maspikin b'yado la'asot teshuvah* — this is what I have received as tradition from you: anyone who sinned and caused others to sin does not receive the wherewithal to do *teshuvah*. It is not that *teshuvah* is metaphysically impossible, but that it becomes practically unattainable — the door feels permanently shut. One wonders, and the *gemara* implicitly raises this, whether teaching this principle so categorically to students is itself a form of pushing away with both hands. Geichazi took the teaching as license to give up entirely.

### Hichti Es HaRabim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עֲבַד? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֶבֶן שׁוֹאֶבֶת תָּלָה לוֹ לְחַטַּאת יָרָבְעָם, וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ בֵּין שָׁמַיִם לָאָרֶץ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: שֵׁם חֲקַק לַהּ אַפּוּמַּהּ, וְהָיְתָה אוֹמֶרֶת ״אָנֹכִי״ וְ״לֹא יִהְיֶה לְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Gehazi **do** that caused the masses to sin? **There are** those **who say** that **he hung a magnetic rock on Jeroboam's calf,** the golden calf that Jeroboam established as an idol, and used a magnet to pull the calf off the ground so that **he suspended it between heaven and earth,** i.e., caused it to hover above the ground. This seemingly miraculous occurrence caused the people to worship it even more devoutly. **And there are** those **who say: He engraved** the sacred **name on its mouth, and it would say: "I am** the Lord your God" **and: "You shall not have** other gods"** (Exodus 20:2).

In what way had Geichazi caused others to sin? The *gemara* records several traditions. One says he suspended the golden calves of Yerovam ben Nevat with lodestones — magnets arranged so the calves floated in midair — creating the illusion that they were genuine deities. Another says he engraved God's name on the mouth of the golden calves, so that they would supernaturally proclaim *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* and *Lo yiheyeh lecha*, mimicking the divine voice. Each of these represents an act of profound mass corruption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַבָּנַן דְּחָה מִקַּמֵּיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ בְנֵי הַנְּבִיאִים אֶל אֱלִישָׁע הִנֵּה נָא הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר אֲנַחְנוּ יוֹשְׁבִים שָׁם לְפָנֶיךָ צַר מִמֶּנּוּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּעַד הָאִידָּנָא לָא הֲוָה דְּחִיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say:** Gehazi **pushed the Sages** away **from** coming **before him,** preventing them learning from Elisha, **as it is written,** after the aforementioned incident: **"And the sons of the prophets said to Elisha, behold this place where we are staying before you is too cramped for us"** (II Kings 6:1). This proves **by inference that until that time** the place **was not cramped,** as Gehazi would turn people away.

A third tradition points in a slightly different direction: *rabbanan dacha mikamei* — he pushed away scholars from Elisha's presence, seeking to monopolize access to his master. How is this inferred? After Geichazi was sent away, the *bnei nevi'im* said to Elisha: *hinei na hamakom asher anachnu yoshvim sham lefanecha tzar mimenu* — the place where we sit before you has become too crowded. *Michlal de'ad idna lo hava dachik* — this implies that until then it had not been crowded, because Geichazi had been keeping people away. His removal opened the doors, and suddenly the place was full. His restricting access to Elisha constituted a form of *hichti et harabbim* — causing many to be deprived of Torah.

The broader lesson here is sobering. A small wrongdoing, handled with a heavy and unrelenting hand, can drive a person from occasional dishonesty into wholesale heresy and mass spiritual corruption. The consequences of pushing away entirely, without the right hand drawing close, are incalculable.

## Yehoshua ben Perachiah

### Yannai's Persecution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדַהֲוָה קָא קָטֵיל יַנַּאי מַלְכָּא לְרַבָּנַן, שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח (אַטְמִינֵהוּ) [אַטְמַרְתֵּיהּ] אֲחָתֵיהּ, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּרַחְיָה אֲזַל עֲרַק לַאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **When King Yannai was killing the Sages, Shimon ben Shataḥ was hidden by his sister,** Yannai's wife, while **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya went** and **fled to Alexandria of Egypt.**

The *gemara* now tells the parallel story. During the period when King Yannai was killing the Sages and Pharisees, Shimon ben Shetach was protected because his sister had married Yannai and hid him. Yehoshua ben Perachiah, who was the *nasi*, had no such protection and fled — *azal arek l'Alexandria shel Mitzrayim* — he ran away to Alexandria in Egypt.

### Return & Inn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה שְׁלָמָא, שְׁלַח לֵיהּ שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: מִנַּי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם עִיר הַקּוֹדֶשׁ לִךְ אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם: אֲחוֹתִי! בַּעֲלִי שָׁרוּי בְּתוֹכֵךְ, וַאֲנִי יוֹשֶׁבֶת שׁוֹמֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **When peace was made** between Yannai and the Sages, **Shimon ben Shataḥ sent him** the following letter: **From myself, Jerusalem the holy city, to you, Alexandria of Egypt. My sister, my husband dwells within you, and I am sitting desolate.**

When the persecutions subsided, Shimon ben Shetach sent a message to his colleague, composed as a poetic epistle: from me, Jerusalem the Holy City, to you, Alexandria of Egypt — my sister — my husband dwells within you and I sit desolate. The message was at once poetic, coded, and emotionally resonant. Yehoshua ben Perachiah understood: *shema minah hava lei shlama* — it must now be peaceful. He set out to return.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא, אִקְּלַע לְהָהוּ אוּשְׁפִּיזָא, קָם קַמַּיְיהוּ בִּיקָרָא שַׁפִּיר, עָבְדִי לֵיהּ יְקָרָא טוּבָא, יָתֵיב וְקָא מִשְׁתַּבַּח: כַּמָּה נָאָה אַכְסַנְיָא זוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **When he came** back to Eretz Yisrael, Rabbi Yehoshua **arrived at a certain inn.** The innkeeper **stood before him, honoring him considerably,** and overall **they accorded him great honor.** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya then **sat and was praising** them by saying: **How beautiful is this inn.**

On the journey back, he stopped at an inn. The innkeeper treated him with exceptional honor — *shapir avid lei yekara tuva*, with great and fitting respect. Yehoshua ben Perachiah sat and began praising the situation: *kama na'eh achsanya zo* — how beautiful is this inn. He meant, evidently, how beautiful is her manner of hosting.

### Student Rebuked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי: רַבִּי, עֵינֶיהָ טְרוּטוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע, בְּכָךְ אַתָּה עוֹסֵק? אַפֵּיק אַרְבַּע מְאָה שִׁפּוּרֵי וְשַׁמְּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Jesus the Nazarene,** one of his students, **said to him: My teacher,** but the **eyes** of the innkeeper's wife **are narrow [*terutot*].** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **said to him: Wicked one, is this what you are engaged in,** gazing at women? **He brought out four hundred *shofarot* and excommunicated him.**

One of his students — the text preserves this as Yeshu HaNotzri in the original *girsa* — heard this and replied: *Rabbi, eineiha trutot* — her eyes are round, they are not beautiful. The student had interpreted the remark as a comment on the innkeeper's physical appearance.

Yehoshua ben Perachiah reacted immediately and harshly: *rasha, bekach ata osek* — wicked one, is this what occupies your mind? He was incensed that his student's thoughts had gone in that direction. He then took out four hundred *shofarot* and placed the student under *cherem*, the ban of excommunication.

### Repeated Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל יוֹמָא אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ וְלָא קַבְּלֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָרֵי קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ. הֲוָה בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ לְקַבּוֹלֵיהּ, אַחְוִי לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ, סְבַר מִדְחָא דָּחֵי לֵיהּ, אֲזַל זְקַף לְבֵינְתָּא פַּלְחַאּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Every day** Jesus **would come before him, but he would not accept his** wish to return. **One day,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **was reciting *Shema*** when Jesus **came before him. He intended to accept him** on this occasion, so **he signaled to him with his hand** to wait. Jesus **thought he was rejecting him** entirely. He therefore **went and stood up a brick** and **worshipped it** as an idol.

Day after day the student returned, seeking to be readmitted. *Kol yoma ata lekamei velo kiblei* — every day he came and was not accepted. Then one day, as Yehoshua ben Perachiah was reciting *kri'at shema*, the student arrived once again. That very day, Yehoshua ben Perachiah had actually reached a change of heart — *hava be-da'ato lekablei* — he was prepared to accept him back. But as he was in the middle of *kri'at shema* he could not speak. He gestured with his hand — *achvei lei be-yadei*. The student interpreted the gesture as another rejection: *savar midche dachei lei* — he assumed he was being pushed away yet again.

### Stone Worship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזוֹר בָּךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִמְּךָ, כׇּל הַחוֹטֵא וּמַחְטִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים — אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **said to him: Return from your** sins. Jesus **said to him: This is** the tradition that **I received from you: Anyone who sins and causes the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent.**

### Ein Maspikin (Repeated)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר מָר: יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי כִּישֵּׁף וְהִסִּית וְהִדִּיחַ וְהֶחְטִיא אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **For the Master said: Jesus the Nazarene performed sorcery, and he incited** the masses, **and subverted** the masses, **and caused the Jewish people to sin.**

When Yehoshua ben Perachiah finally came to him and said *chazor becha* — return, repent — the student gave the same answer Geichazi had given: *kach mekublani mimcha, kol hachotei umachti et harabbim ein maspikin b'yado la'asot teshuvah* — this is what I have received as tradition from you: one who sins and causes others to sin is not given the opportunity to repent. The student invoked his teacher's own principle against his teacher's attempt at reconciliation.

As the *gemara* confirms: *kishef vehisit vehidiach vehichti et Yisrael* — he practiced sorcery, he seduced, he led astray, and he caused Israel to sin. This is how he had caused others to sin, making *teshuvah* practically foreclosed. And it confirms what the *gemara* already signaled: the dates in the story do not align historically with what we know of Jesus, yet the *gemara* clearly identifies the student as Yeshu HaNotzri and attributes to him the founding of a movement that led vast numbers away.

## Smol Docha Yamin

### Yeitzer, Tinok, Ishah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יֵצֶר, תִּינוֹק, וְאִשָּׁה — תְּהֵא שְׂמֹאל דּוֹחָה וְיָמִין מְקָרֶבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** With regard to the evil **inclination,** to **a child, and** to **a woman, the left** hand **should reject and the right** hand should **welcome.**

Having established the principle of *smol dochah ve'yamin mekarevet*, the *gemara* now extends it to other domains of personal life. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches: *yeitzer, tinok, ve'ishah — tehei smol dochah ve'yamin mekarevet* — with regard to one's *yeitzer* (sexual desire), one's children, and one's wife, always let the left hand push away and the right hand draw close.

This is a distinctively Jewish teaching. The *yeitzer* is not to be annihilated — Judaism does not embrace asceticism — but moderated. Yet the moderation must always be in the service of embrace rather than rejection. The right hand, which draws close, must be stronger. A man's relationship with his children — even when a father must set limits — should ultimately be defined by closeness and warmth. The same applies to the relationship with one's wife. The principle is the same throughout: never push away so hard that the right hand cannot draw close again.

## Eglah Arufah

### Horeg Ad Lo Neerfah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִמְצָא הַהוֹרֵג עַד שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶרְפָה הָעֶגְלָה — תֵּצֵא וְתִרְעֶה בָּעֵדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If the killer is found before the heifer's neck was broken,** the heifer **shall go out and graze among the herd.** It is not considered sacred at all, and it may rejoin the other animals.

The *gemara* now transitions to the *mishnah*. *Nimtza ha-horeig ad shelo neerfah ha-eglah* — if the murderer was discovered before the heifer's neck was broken, *teitzei vetireh ba-eider* — the heifer returns to the flock and may be used. It does not acquire the status of *eglah arufah* and is not forbidden. Rashi notes that there is an opinion in the *gemara* that once the heifer descends into the valley even before the neck is broken, it becomes forbidden — but the ruling here follows the view that it does not.

### Acharei Neerfah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנֶּעֶרְפָה הָעֶגְלָה — תִּקָּבֵר בִּמְקוֹמָהּ, שֶׁעַל סָפֵק בָּאתָה מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ — כִּיפְּרָה סְפֵיקָהּ, וְהָלְכָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If the killer is found **from** the time **when the heifer's neck was broken,** even if the rest of the ritual has not yet been performed, it is prohibited to benefit from the animal, despite the killer having been found; it **should be buried in its place.** This is **because** the heifer **initially came for uncertainty,** as the killer was unknown, and **it atoned** for **its uncertainty and left,**

If, however, the neck has already been broken — *misheneerfah ha-eglah tikaver bimkomah* — it is buried in place and remains permanently forbidden in benefit, even after the murderer is subsequently found. The reason is given in the next subsection.

### Kiphrah B'Safeik

The *gemara* explains the reason: *She'al safeik ba'ah mitchilatah* — it came originally on account of doubt. The entire purpose of the *eglah arufah* ritual is to achieve atonement for the community and the land during the period when no murderer is known and justice cannot be done. *Mitchilah kiphrah sefeika vehalchah lah* — it achieved its intended atonement during that period of uncertainty, and therefore it is not retroactively invalidated by the later discovery of the murderer. The ritual served its function; it may not be undone.

### Yehareg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶעֶרְפָה הָעֶגְלָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַהוֹרֵג — הֲרֵי זֶה יֵהָרֵג.
>
> **Translation:** If **the heifer's neck was broken and then the murderer is found, he is nevertheless executed.**

*Neerfah ha-eglah ve-achar kach nimtza ha-horeig — harei zeh yeihareig* — if the neck was already broken and then the murderer is found, he is nevertheless executed. The *eglah arufah* provided communal atonement for the land; it did not substitute for the murderer's punishment. The verse is explicit: *ula-aretz lo yechupar la-dam asher shupach bah ki im be-dam shofcho* — the land cannot be atoned for spilled blood except through the blood of the one who spilled it. Performing the ritual does not spare the murderer; it only addresses the communal dimension of the crime.

## Edim v. Eglah

### Ed Echad Ra'iti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״רָאִיתִי אֶת הַהוֹרֵג״
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one witness says: I saw the killer** — even though a single witness cannot convict in *beit din* — the *eglah arufah* is suspended.

The *mishnah* continues: *eid echad omer ra'iti et ha-horeig* — if one witness says "I saw the killer" — even though a single witness cannot convict in *beit din* — the *eglah arufah* is suspended. This parallels what we saw with *sotah*: a single witness testifying to the adultery suspends the *sotah* ordeal. The Torah grants *ed echad* a form of credibility that prevents proceeding with these rituals even without the formal requirement of two witnesses for conviction.

### Neutralization Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״רָאִיתִי אֶת הַהוֹרֵג״, וְעֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״לֹא רָאִיתָ״. אִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״רָאִיתִי״, וְאִשָּׁה אוֹמֶרֶת ״לֹא רָאִית״ — הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״רָאִיתִי״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא רָאִיתָ״ — הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״רָאִינוּ״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר לָהֶן ״לֹא רְאִיתֶם״ — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one witness says: I saw the killer, and one** other **witness says: You did not see** him; or if **a woman says: I saw, and** another **woman says: You did not see, they would break the neck** of the heifer, as without clear testimony about the identity of the killer the ritual is performed. Similarly, if **one witness says: I saw** the killer, **and two** witnesses **say: You did not see, they would break the neck** of the heifer, as the pair is relied upon. If **two** witnesses **say: We saw** the killer, **and one** witness **says to them: You did not see, they would not break the neck** of the heifer, as there are two witnesses to the identity of the killer.

The *mishnah* then presents a series of cases involving competing testimony. One witness says he saw and another says he did not: they neutralize each other and the neck is broken. A woman says she saw and another woman says she did not: the same, they neutralize. One witness says he saw and two say he did not: the two prevail, the neck is broken. Two witnesses say they saw and one says they did not: the two prevail, the neck is not broken.

### Mishe-Rabu Rotzchanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָרוֹצְחָנִים בָּטְלָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when murderers proliferated, the** ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken was nullified.**

*Mishe-rabu ha-rotzchanim batla eglah arufah* — when murders became rampant in Israel, the *eglah arufah* ritual was discontinued. The ritual presupposes a society where murder is an aberration that shocks the community into communal soul-searching. When murder had become routine — particularly associated with the period of the *sicarri* and the final decades of the Second Temple — the ritual could no longer carry its intended meaning. The *mishnah* mentions that this shift is associated with the rise of *Elazar ben Dinai*, who was originally known as *Tchina ben Perisha* and was later renamed *ben ha-ratzchan* — "son of the murderer."

## Batlu HaMitzvot

### Mishe-Rabu Menafim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הַמְנָאֲפִים — פָּסְקוּ הַמַּיִם הַמָּרִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי הִפְסִיקָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא אֶפְקוֹד עַל בְּנוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי תִזְנֶינָה וְעַל כַּלּוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי תְנָאַפְנָה כִּי הֵם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when adulterers proliferated,** the performance of the ritual of **the bitter waters of *sota* was nullified;** they would not administer the bitter waters to the *sota*. **And** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** who **nullified it, as** it **is stated: "I will not punish your daughters when they commit harlotry, nor your daughters-in-law when they commit adultery; for they** consort with lewd women"** (Hosea 4:14), meaning that when the husbands are adulterers, the wives are not punished for their own adultery.

*Mishe-rabu ha-menafim pasku ha-mayim ha-marim* — when adulterers became numerous, the bitter waters of *sotah* ceased to function. The *sotah* waters test a wife whose husband suspects her. But the waters only worked when the husband himself was innocent of adultery. Once men were routinely committing adultery, the waters would no longer have the power to check their wives.

### Yochanan ben Zakkai

*Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai hifsikan* — it was Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai who formally discontinued the *sotah* waters, citing the verse: *lo efkod al benoteichem ki tizneina ve'al kaloteichem ki tinafna* — I will not punish your daughters when they fornicate, nor your daughters-in-law when they commit adultery. When the men of the generation are themselves guilty, the divine mechanism of the *sotah* waters is suspended.

### Mishe-Meit Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁמֵּת יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בָּטְלוּ הָאֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵין אֶשְׁכּוֹל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּכּוּרָה אִוְּתָה נַפְשִׁי״.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosei ben Yehuda of Jerusalem died, the clusters ceased,** i.e., they were the last of the clusters, as explained in the Gemara, **as** it **is stated: "There is no cluster to eat; nor first-ripe fig that my soul desires"** (Micah 7:1).

*Mishe-meit Yosi ben Yoezer ish Tsereidah ve-Yosi ben Yehudah ish Yerushalayim batlu ha-eshkolot* — when Yosi ben Yoezer and Yosi ben Yehudah died — the earliest *zugot* recorded in *Pirkei Avot* — the *eshkolot* ceased. *Eshkol* means a cluster of grapes; the *gemara* uses it to describe Torah scholars who were complete, containing within themselves the totality of the tradition. With their deaths, gaps began to appear in the transmission. Debates multiplied because the tradition was no longer perfectly intact from teacher to student, and subsequent generations were simply not at the same level of comprehensive mastery. The verse cited is *ein eshkol le-echol bikurah ivta nafshi* — there is no longer a cluster to eat, no one of that completeness to be found.

### Yochanan Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹחָנָן כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל הֶעֱבִיר הוֹדָיַית הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. אַף הוּא בִּיטֵּל אֶת הַמְעוֹרְרִים וְאֶת הַנּוֹקְפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Yoḥanan the High Priest took away the declaration of the tithe.** After his time, no one recited the passage about the elimination of tithes that had previously been said at the end of a three-year tithing cycle. **He also nullified** the actions of **the awakeners and the strikers** at the Temple.

Yochanan Kohen Gadol instituted several reforms. He discontinued the *viduy ma'aser* — the declaration that the Torah requires be made every third year confirming that one has properly distributed all *ma'aser* from one's home. He also abolished the *me'orerim* and the *nokfim* — practices associated with the Temple which the *gemara* will explain further. He stopped the practice of metalworkers hammering and smelting in Jerusalem during *Chol HaMoed*, as the constant noise of work undermined the spirit of the festival and created a false impression for the populace that labor during *Chol HaMoed* was entirely permitted. And in his time, *ein tzarich lish'ol al demai* — one no longer had to inquire about *demai* produce, because he instituted the systematic taking of *terumot u-ma'asrot* from doubtfully-tithed produce, rendering the question practically resolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד יָמָיו הָיָה פַּטִּישׁ מַכֶּה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. וּבְיָמָיו אֵין צָרִיךְ לִשְׁאוֹל עַל הַדְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Until the days** of Yoḥanan the High Priest **the hammer** of smiths **would strike in Jerusalem,** but he banned the practice. **And** furthermore, **in his days a person did not need to inquire with regard to doubtfully tithed produce.**

## Gemara Analysis

### Lo Yichupuhr

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם נֶעֶרְפָה הָעֶגְלָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַהוֹרֵג שֶׁאֵין פּוֹטֶרֶת אוֹתוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלָאָרֶץ לֹא יְכֻפַּר לַדָּם אֲשֶׁר שֻׁפַּךְ בָּהּ כִּי אִם בְּדַם שׁוֹפְכוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that if the heifer's neck was broken and afterward the killer was found,** then the breaking of the neck **does not exempt him** from punishment? **The verse states: "And the land shall not be atoned, for the blood that was spilled in it, but by the blood of he who spilled it"** (Numbers 35:33).

The *gemara* now analyzes the *mishnah*'s rulings systematically. First, on the question of how we know that finding the murderer after the *eglah arufah* does not exempt him: *talmud lomar ula-aretz lo yechupar la-dam asher shupach bah ki im be-dam shofcho* — the land requires the blood of the murderer for atonement; the *eglah arufah* atones for the communal dimension but cannot replace the execution of the murderer.

### Ed Echad Ne'eman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר רָאִיתִי אֶת הַהוֹרֵג כּוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּמַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ, הָא לָא מַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ — עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that if **one witness says: I saw the killer,** and another testifies: You did not see him, they would break the heifer's neck. The Gemara infers: **The reason** they break the neck **is** because the second witness **contradicts him, but** if **no** one **contradicts him, one witness is relied upon,** and they do not break the heifer's neck. **From where are these matters** derived?

On the rule that a single witness saying "I saw" suspends the *eglah arufah* ritual: *ta'ama demachish leh ha lo machish leh ed echad ne'eman* — the reason that when one contradicts him they neutralize is that when the first witness stands uncontradicted, one witness is believed. *Minani mili* — from where do we derive this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא נוֹדַע מִי הִכָּהוּ״. הָא נוֹדַע מִי הִכָּהוּ, אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְסַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁרָאוּ אֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ וְאֵין מַכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעֵינֵינוּ לֹא רָאוּ״, וַהֲלֹא רָאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It states with regard to the heifer whose neck is broken: **"It is not known who has smitten him"** (Deuteronomy 21:1). **Consequently,** if **it was known who smote him, even** if it was only **one** person **at the end of the world** who knew, **they would not break the neck** of the heifer. **Rabbi Akiva says: From where** is it derived **that** if the members of the **Sanhedrin** themselves **saw one** person **kill someone, but they do not recognize him,** then **they would not break the neck** of the heifer? **The verse states: "Nor did our eyes see"** (Deuteronomy 21:7), **and** did they **not see?**

From the verse *lo noda mi hikahu* — the ritual is only done when it is not known who the killer was. *Ha noda afilu echad besof ha'olam lo hayu orfin* — if it is known at all, even through a single witness anywhere, the neck is not broken. This is the basis for *ed echad* being believed in this context. The *gemara* also cites Rabbi Akiva: from the elders' declaration *ve'eineinu lo rau* — our eyes did not see it — we derive that if the *sanhedrin* itself had witnessed the murder without identifying the killer, they too would not perform the *eglah arufah*, even without a named individual witness.

### Kashya on Neutralization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן, אִידַּךְ חַד הֵיכִי מָצֵי מַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **Now that you have said** that in this case **one witness is relied upon,** if so, **how is the other one able** to **contradict him? Didn't Ulla say: Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, there is** the equivalent of the testimony of **two** witnesses **here,** and **the statement of one** witness **has no** standing **in a place where** it is contradicted by **two** witnesses.

This creates a difficulty. If one witness is believed — and Ulla's principle is that *kol makom she-heimina Torah ed echad harei kan shnayim* (wherever the Torah believes one witness, that witness counts as two) — then how can a subsequent single witness come and neutralize him? The one who said he saw has the weight of two; the one who says he did not see has only the weight of one. They should not cancel each other out.

### Ulla's Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ עוּלָּא, תְּנִי: ״לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין״. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, תְּנִי: ״לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Ulla** could have **said to you** that the text of the mishna should be emended and **teach** the mishna in this way: **They would not break the neck** of the heifer. **And Rabbi Yitzḥak also said** to **teach: They would not break the neck.**

Ulla therefore emends the *mishnah*: *teni lo hayu orfin* — read the *mishnah* as "they would not break the neck" even in the case of one against one. The first witness, having been believed and having the weight of two witnesses, cannot be neutralized by a second who merely contradicts him. Rabbi Yitzchak agrees and makes the same emendation. The *mishnah* should be read: one against one — they do not break the neck, the first witness's testimony stands.

### Garsot HaMishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר, תְּנִי: ״הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין״. וּלְרַבִּי חִיָּיא קַשְׁיָא דְּעוּלָּא! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּבַת אַחַת, כָּאן בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya said** that one should **teach: They would break the neck.** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Ḥiyya,** the above ruling **of Ulla** is **difficult.** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **here,** in the mishna, the case is discussing when two witnesses came **simultaneously,** and therefore both of their testimonies are rejected; whereas **there,** with regard to the statement of Ulla, it is referring to a case when they testified **one after the other.**

Rabbi Chiya, however, maintains the standard reading of the *mishnah* — *hayu orfin*, one against one they do neutralize and the neck is broken. How does he resolve Ulla's principle?

The resolution: *lo kashya, kan be-vat achat, kan be-zeh achar zeh* — there is no contradiction. It depends on timing. When both witnesses testify simultaneously — or at least before the first has been formally accepted — they neutralize each other, and the *mishnah*'s ruling of *hayu orfin* applies. When the first witness testified and was already accepted and treated as credible, only then does Ulla's principle apply: the second witness cannot retroactively neutralize the first. Rashi understands simultaneity as *toch kidei dibbur*; Tosafot understand it more broadly as any testimony that occurred before the *beit din* had formally acted on the first witness's account.

## Mishnah Complications

### Mishnah Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: עֵד אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״רָאִיתִי אֶת הַהוֹרֵג״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״לֹא רָאִיתָ״ — הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. הָא חַד וְחַד — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **one witness says: I saw the killer, and two say: You did not see, they would break the neck.** This cannot be stated just to teach us this *halakha*, as the fact that two witnesses override one witness is well known. The Gemara assumes that it is stated for the following inference: **Therefore,** if **one** testified, **and** the other **one** then testified, **they would not break the neck.** This appears to be **a conclusive refutation of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who has the text of: They would break the neck.

The *mishnah* presents cases in both directions: one witness against two, and two witnesses against one — with different outcomes depending on which side has the majority. But the *mishnah* never explicitly states the baseline case of one against one. This creates an inferential problem, since each of the two cases involving two against one seems to imply something contradictory about what happens in the intermediate case.

### Psulei Edut Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַתְנִיתִין כּוּלַּהּ בִּפְסוּלֵי עֵדוּת, וְכִדְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the correct understanding is that **the entire mishna** is not dealing with valid witnesses and stating an obvious *halakha* in order to enable an inference, but instead it is dealing **with** people who are **disqualified** from **bearing witness** and is also teaching us a novel ruling. **And** the mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥemya**

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by observing: *ella matnitin kula bi-psulei edut* — the *mishnah*'s two-against-one cases are addressing witnesses who are *psulei edut*, disqualified witnesses. This connects to the principle of Rabbi Nechemya.

### Rabbi Nechemya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת, וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **who says: Wherever the Torah relied on one witness, when we move outside the realm of that specially-believed witness and deal instead with *psulei edut*, we follow a simple numbers game — majority of opinions. *Ve'asu shtei nashim be-ish echad ki-shnei anashim be-ish echad* — two women count against one man as two men count against one man.**

Rabbi Nechemya teaches: *kol makom she-heimina Torah ed echad halech achar rov deot* — wherever the Torah believed one witness with special standing, when we move outside the realm of that specially-believed witness and deal instead with *psulei edut*, we follow a simple numbers game — majority of opinions. *Ve'asu shtei nashim be-ish echad ki-shnei anashim be-ish echad* — two women count against one man as two men count against one man. Within the domain of *psulei edut*, the arithmetic applies straightforwardly: two beats one regardless of gender.

### Shnei Nashim v. Ish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כׇּל הֵיכָא דַּאֲתָא עֵד אֶחָד — כָּשֵׁר מֵעִיקָּרָא, אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה נָשִׁים כִּי אֶחָד דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** a different version of Rabbi Neḥemya's opinion: **Anywhere that one valid witness came at the outset, even one hundred women who later contradict him** are considered like one witness, and do not override his testimony.

An alternative reading, however, maintains: *kol heicha de-ata ed echad me-ikara afilu meah nashim ke-chad dami* — if a *kasher* male witness testified first, even a hundred women subsequently cannot neutralize him, because his testimony has the full weight of two *kasher* witnesses. In that reading, the *mishnah*'s cases of two against one must involve a *pasul* as the initial witness, so that the two *psulot* can effectively neutralize.

### Why Mention Zwei?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְתָרְצַהּ לִדְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הָכִי: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶאֱמִינָה תּוֹרָה עֵד אֶחָד — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת, וְעָשׂוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד, אֲבָל שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד — כִּי פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to this interpretation you must **emend the** statement **of Rabbi Neḥemya** so that it reads **like this: Rabbi Neḥemya says: Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, follow the majority of opinions. And they established** that **two women against one woman** are **like two men against one man. But two women** in opposition **to one man** who is a valid witness **is like half** of a pair of witnesses **and half** of a pair of witnesses, and the mishna did not address that case.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי פְּסוּלֵי עֵדוּת, לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוֹב דֵּעוֹת — לְחוּמְרָא, אֲבָל לְקוּלָּא — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question on these two interpretations of the mishna: **And why do I** need **two** cases in the mishna to teach the *halakha* that the majority opinion of **those disqualified** from **bearing witness** is followed? The Gemara explains: It is necessary, **lest you say** that **when we follow the majority opinion** in the case of invalid witnesses, this is when it results in a decision **to be stringent** and require the performance of the ritual. **But** when it results in a decision **to be lenient** and say that the ritual is not required, we do **not** follow the majority opinion, and the performance of the ritual is required even if there is one witness saying that the killer was not seen. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that there is no difference in this regard, and the majority opinion is followed in any case.

If the principle is simply that two *psulei edut* neutralize one, why does the *mishnah* need to state both directions — two saying they saw against one saying they didn't, and two saying they didn't against one saying they did? *Mahu de-teima ki azlinan batar rov deot le-chumra aval le-kula lo* — one might have thought that the majority rule among *psulei edut* is applied only stringently — that is, only in the direction that prevents breaking the neck — but not leniently, to permit breaking the neck when two *psulei edut* say the murderer was not seen. Therefore the *mishnah* states both cases explicitly to teach that the numbers game applies in both directions.

The *gemara*'s ultimate position can be summarized as follows. When a single *kasher* witness testifies that he saw the killer, he is believed to the extent that the *eglah arufah* ritual is suspended; this parallels the *ed echad* who is believed regarding *sotah*. If that witness testifies first and is accepted, a subsequent single witness cannot neutralize him. If they testify simultaneously, or before formal acceptance of the first, they neutralize. Two *kasher* witnesses subsequently can neutralize one *kasher* witness. When the witnesses involved are *psulei edut*, the rule follows simple numerical majority in either direction. Whether two *psulei edut* can neutralize a single *kasher* male witness who testified first depends on the dispute between the readings of Rabbi Nechemya — some holding that two women cannot overcome the specially-believed man, others holding that they can, provided the first witness was not of that specially-elevated status.
