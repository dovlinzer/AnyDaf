# Shevuot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Meir - Goats

### Intro to R. Meir

The Mishnah having laid out the various categories of atonement achieved by the *musaf* goats, the Gemara now turns to the position of Rabbi Meir: *kol seirei kapparotan shaveh* — all the goats effect the same atonement. It is important to clarify from the outset that "all" here does not mean every goat brought in the Temple, but specifically the *musaf* goats: the *musaf* of Yom Kippur, the *musaf* of the *regalim*, and the *musaf* of Rosh Chodesh. Rabbi Meir's claim is that all of these do identical work — they all atone for the same categories of sin. In this respect, he is embracing and extending what Rabbi Shimon had suggested, but collapsing any distinctions among them entirely. The three categories at issue — eating a *korban tamei*, unawareness at the beginning and end, and unawareness at the beginning with awareness at the end — are, in Rabbi Meir's view, handled equally by each of the *musaf* offerings.

### Vav Linking Offerings

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama de'Rabbi Meir* — what is Rabbi Meir's reasoning? Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina answers: *amar kra u'se'ir u'se'ir* — the Torah employs the conjunctive *vav* before the word *se'ir* (goat) in the passages describing the various *musaf* offerings in Parashat Pinchas (Bamidbar 28). The rule *vav mosif al inyan rishon* — the *vav* adds to and connects with the preceding matter — is the operative hermeneutic principle. By repeatedly attaching each offering to what preceded it with the letter *vav*, the Torah links all these goats together: *hukshu kol ha-seirim zeh la-zeh*, all the goats are placed in analogy with one another.

### Textual Patterns

A closer examination of the relevant passages in Parashat Pinchas reveals a consistent textual pattern. Almost every *musaf* sacrifice in the parasha concludes with a *vav* before the word *se'ir*: Rosh Chodesh has *u'se'ir izim echad l'chatat l'Hashem*; Pesach has *u'se'ir chatat echad*; Rosh Hashanah has *u'se'ir izim echad chatat l'chapair*; Sukkot and all the intermediate days of Sukkot similarly have *u'se'ir* with the *vav*. Two conspicuous exceptions stand out: Shavuot, where the text reads *se'ir izim* without the *vav*, and Yom Kippur, where the *musaf* similarly lacks the *vav*.

Setting those two exceptional cases aside for the moment, the presence of the *vav* in all the other passages creates a chain of association. We begin, as Rabbi Shimon had established, with the observation that Rosh Chodesh atones for *korban tamei*, the *regalim* atone for unawareness at beginning and end, and the Yom Kippur *musaf* atones for awareness at the end without awareness at the beginning. Because each of these offerings is connected to the others by the *vav*, whatever one does, the others also do — and Rabbi Meir reads this to mean they all do everything.

But the Gemara anticipates a problem in how this linkage works. If the connection operates sequentially — Pesach learns from Rosh Chodesh, Shavuot learns from Pesach, Rosh Hashanah learns from Shavuot, and so on (*kol chad m'chad gmar*) — one can imagine the properties of Rosh Chodesh eventually "rippling" all the way down to Sukkot and vice versa through a chain of derivations. However, this process runs into a formidable obstacle.

### Gezeirah Shavah

Rabbi Yochanan had established a general rule: *kol ha-Torah kulah lemedim lamed mi-lamad mi-lamad mi-lamad chutz mi-kodshim* — throughout the entire Torah, one may derive a *halacha* from another derived *halacha*, except in the realm of sacrificial law. In *kodshim*, one may not derive from a derivative; each inference must go back to an explicit source. This means the sequential ripple model fails: you cannot get Rosh Chodesh's atonement power all the way down to Sukkot through a chain of intermediate derivations.

The Gemara resolves this cleanly: *kuleihu me-hacha gamrei* — each one learns directly from the source. Because every offering in the list carries the *vav*, and because they all appear within a single *parasha*, each may be compared directly to any other without passing through intermediate steps. The *vav* linking them all effectively permits what would otherwise be impermissible derivative inference. Sukkot may learn directly from Rosh Chodesh; Rosh Chodesh may learn directly from the Yom Kippur *musaf* — without the chain, and without the problem of *lamed mi-lamed* in *kodshim*.

## Missing Vav Problem

### Challenge to Vav

The Gemara immediately confronts the obvious challenge: *teinach kol d'ketiv bei u'se'ir* — this works well for everything that actually has the *vav*. But what about Shavuot (*Atzeret*) and Yom Kippur, which lack the *vav* in their *musaf* passages? The very mechanism just described — the *vav* that links all the goats — cannot apply to them, since their texts do not contain it. How, then, are Shavuot and Yom Kippur connected to the rest?

### Eileh Ta'asu

The Gemara abandons the *vav* approach entirely as the primary basis and pivots to a different verse. Rabbi Yonah answers: *amar kra eileh ta'asu l'Hashem b'mo'adeichem* — "these shall you do for Hashem on your appointed times." This verse appears as a concluding summary statement at the end of all the *musaf* offerings in Parashat Pinchas, sweeping them all together under a single rubric. *Hukshu kol ha-mo'adot kulam zeh la-zeh* — all the *mo'adot* are placed in analogy with one another by virtue of this inclusive summary. Since the verse encompasses all the offerings together, the way any individual *korban chatat* functions for one applies equally to all the others.

### Rosh Chodesh as Mo'ed

But this solution raises its own question: the summary verse speaks of *mo'adeichem* — "your appointed times." Rosh Chodesh, strictly speaking, might not qualify as a *mo'ed* in the conventional sense, and if it falls outside the scope of *eileh ta'asu*, the entire chain of connection is broken.

The Gemara responds: *ibra Rosh Chodesh nami ikri mo'ed* — indeed, Rosh Chodesh is also called a *mo'ed*. The proof comes from Abaye. Abaye teaches that the month of Tammuz in the year the *meraglim* (spies) returned from Canaan was a full thirty-day month — *Tammuz d'hai shata miluyei malyuhi*. The significance of this is as follows: the spies were gone for forty days. Upon their return, the people wept. God wished to ordain that the night of weeping should fall on the ninth of Av, so as to fix that date permanently as a day of national mourning. This required the day of their return to be the eighth of Av. Whether a given day is called the eighth or the ninth of Av depends on when Rosh Chodesh Av falls, which in turn depends on whether the preceding month, Tammuz, had twenty-nine or thirty days. Had Tammuz been a twenty-nine-day month, Rosh Chodesh Av would have fallen one day earlier, and the day of the spies' return would have been labeled the ninth of Av — meaning the night of weeping would have been the tenth. To ensure the weeping occurred on the ninth, God arranged for Tammuz to be a full thirty-day month, which pushed Rosh Chodesh Av forward by one day and made the night of weeping fall precisely on the ninth.

This historical episode is then connected to the verse in Eichah: *kara alai mo'ed lishbor bachurai* — "He has called a *mo'ed* against me to shatter my young men." The use of the word *mo'ed* here in connection with the date of Tisha B'Av, which is fixed relative to Rosh Chodesh, demonstrates that Rosh Chodesh is itself called a *mo'ed*. Thus Rosh Chodesh falls within the scope of *eileh ta'asu b'mo'adeichem*, and the connection is complete.

## Yom Kippur Inner Goat

### R. Yochanan Concedes

With all the *musaf* goats now properly linked, Rabbi Yochanan makes an important concession: *modeh Rabbi Meir be-sa'ir ha-na'aseh bifnim* — even Rabbi Meir agrees that the inner goat of Yom Kippur stands apart. The inner goat — the one brought inside and whose blood is sprinkled in the Holy of Holies as part of the central *avodah* described in *Acharei Mot* — neither does what the *musaf* goats do, nor do they do what it does. This exclusive and reciprocal separation is grounded in the word *achas*: *kapara achas mechaper ve'eino mechaper shtei kaparot* — it performs one act of atonement and does not perform two. Correspondingly, *kappara zo lo tehe ela achas bashana* — this atonement is performed only once in the year; nothing else performs it.

## Rabbi Meir - Goats…

### Baraita Support

A *baraita* explicitly confirms Rabbi Meir's position: for unawareness at the beginning and end, for unawareness at the beginning but awareness at the end, and for a ritually pure person who ate from a *korban tamei*, the *se'ir ha-regalim*, the *se'ir rashei chodashim*, and the *se'ir ha-na'aseh bachutz* (the outer goat of Yom Kippur) all atone — *divrei Rabbi Meir*. Crucially, the *baraita* conspicuously omits the inner goat: *ve'ilu sa'ir ha-na'aseh bifnim shayrei* — the goat brought inside is not listed. And *ve-kapartan nami shayrei* — the work that those outer goats do is never attributed to the inner goat either. The *baraita* thus independently establishes that Rabbi Meir's equation of all goats applies only to the *musaf* offerings; the inner Yom Kippur goat retains its unique and exclusive role.

## Yom Kippur Inner Goat…

### Achas Bashanah

The phrase *achas bashana* — "once in the year" — does double duty in the Gemara's analysis of the inner Yom Kippur goat. On one hand, it means this atonement is performed once a year and nothing else performs it: nothing else can substitute for or replicate what the inner goat does. On the other hand, it means the inner goat itself performs only a single act of atonement — it does one job and not two. The verse *ve-chipper Aharon al karnotav achas bashana... midam chatat ha-kippurim achas bashana yechaper alav* (Shemot 30) contains the phrase *achas bashana* twice, and both appearances serve this twin function: exclusivity in both directions.

## R. Shimon - Functions

### Lost Goat Question

Having established the distinct function of each category of goat, the Gemara now turns to a practical extension of Rabbi Shimon's position: given that each goat performs a distinct kind of atonement, what happens if one of the designated goats is lost? If a replacement is found and used, and the original goat is subsequently recovered, may that original goat now be used for a different occasion's *musaf*? Rabbi Shimon answers: yes, because although each goat addresses something distinct, they all belong to the same genus — they all atone for *tum'at mikdash*. Since they share the same fundamental category, one may be substituted for another. The inner Yom Kippur goat, paired as it is with the *se'ir hamishtaleach*, presumably stands apart from this flexibility, though the Gemara acknowledges this as a genuine and interesting question.

### Rosh Chodesh Limits

Before addressing the substitution question directly, the Gemara first establishes precisely how we know each category is limited to its own function — so that Rabbi Shimon's position itself is coherent. How do we know Rosh Chodesh does not also do the work of the *regalim*? The answer is the word *avon*: the verse uses *avon ha-eidah* in connection with the Rosh Chodesh *musaf*, and from this we derive *avon echad hu nosei ve'eino nosei shenei avonot* — it bears one iniquity and not two. The Rosh Chodesh *se'ir* does its one thing and nothing more.

One might then ask from the other direction: perhaps the *regalim* do not only perform their own function but also pick up the work of Rosh Chodesh? After all, if Rosh Chodesh is limited, maybe something else does its job. The verse says *otah* — "it" — *noset avon*, and *ein acher noset avon*: only it bears this particular iniquity, and nothing else does. So both directions are closed: Rosh Chodesh does only its own work, and nothing else does Rosh Chodesh's work.

### Regalim Limits

The same question is posed for the *regalim* in relation to Yom Kippur: how do we know the *regalim* do not perform the atonement of the Yom Kippur *musaf*? The answer is *achas bashana*: this atonement is done only once in the year. *Kappara zo lo tehei ela achas bashana* — this particular atonement cannot be replicated by the *regalim*, because the verse makes clear that it is performed exclusively on Yom Kippur.

### Two Achot Problem

If the *regalim* cannot do Yom Kippur's work, one might think the reasoning cuts the other way as well: Yom Kippur cannot do the work of the *regalim*. After all, if *achas* — "one" — limits the scope of Yom Kippur's atonement to what it does alone, surely it should also limit the Yom Kippur *musaf* to performing only one category of atonement and not doing the work of the *regalim* as well. The Gemara states this tension explicitly: *achas k'siv, kappara achas mechaper ve'eino mechaper shtei kapparot* — doesn't the same word *achas* mean it only does one job, not two?

This creates a genuine dilemma: it seems impossible to use *achas bashana* to say that nothing else does Yom Kippur's work (which limits others from doing Yom Kippur's job) while simultaneously permitting Yom Kippur to do the work of the *regalim* (which would mean it is doing two jobs, not one).

The Gemara tries to escape by questioning whether *achas* is even operative in the context of the *musaf* goat at all — *de-chi k'siv achas, be-asiyah she-bifnim k'siv* — the word *achas bashana* in the relevant verses is written in connection with what is done on the inside, the inner Yom Kippur service, not the outer *musaf*. If *achas* is not applicable to the *musaf* goat, then neither the limitation ("only Yom Kippur does this work") nor the restriction ("Yom Kippur only does one job") applies there. But this immediately creates the opposite problem: if *achas* doesn't apply to the Yom Kippur *musaf*, then why can't the *regalim* do the Yom Kippur *musaf*'s work? The restriction that protects Yom Kippur's exclusivity has been removed.

The Gemara oscillates between these positions — *le-olam achas mashma lei*, fine, he does use *achas* — and is then immediately confronted again with the double-edged nature of the word.

### Achat Resolution

The resolution is elegant. The two instances of *achas bashana* in Shemot 30 are not treating the same subject with the same focus. The verse *ve-chipper Aharon al karnotav achas bashana* — "Aharon shall atone upon its horns once in the year" — is specifically about *karnotav*, the horns of the inner altar (*mizbe'ach ha-pnimi*). This *achas* says that the inner *mizbe'ach* receives this atonement only once in the year, and by extension, the *se'ir ha-na'aseh bifnim* — the inner goat — performs only one kind of atonement: it cleanses the inner altar and does nothing else. The tight contextual attachment to *karnotav* limits this particular usage of *achas* to the inner goat and its specific work.

The second *achas bashana* — *midam chatat ha-kippurim achas bashana yechaper alav* — is more general. It says that what gets done on Yom Kippur is done only once a year — meaning nothing else performs Yom Kippur's atonement. This applies to both the inner service and, by the linkage of the outer *musaf* to the inner (*milvad chatat ha-kippurim* having equated the outer goat to the inner in an earlier context), to the outer *musaf* as well.

The upshot: the first *achas* restricts the inner goat to doing only one job. The second *achas* says this atonement is unrepeatable — nothing else can do what Yom Kippur does. But the first *achas*, tied as it is to *karnotav* and the inner altar, does not extend to the outer *musaf* goat. Therefore, *ha-chitzon — afilu shtei kaparot* — the outer Yom Kippur *musaf* goat may perform two kinds of atonement. It can do its own unique work (protected by the second *achas*), and it can also do the work of the *regalim* and lower. This accounts for Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda's teaching in the name of Rabbi Shimon: the higher one goes up the hierarchy, the more the offering does — Yom Kippur covers everything below it, the *regalim* cover Rosh Chodesh's work as well, and Rosh Chodesh covers only its own.

With this, the Gemara has fully mapped out all the positions: Rabbi Meir holding all *musafim* do identical work; Rabbi Shimon holding they do distinct work but may substitute for one another by genus; Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon holding that higher offerings subsume the functions of lower ones without the reverse; and everyone agreeing that the inner Yom Kippur goat stands categorically apart.

## Year-End Tamidim

### Six Lambs Policy

The Gemara now turns to what appears at first to be an entirely new topic, though it will prove connected to the discussion of *kedushah* that follows. There is a standing requirement to maintain six lambs in the Temple chamber at all times. The reason is rooted in the law that a *korban tamid* — the daily lamb offering brought morning and afternoon — must be under supervision for four days prior to its offering, to ensure it is blemish-free. With six lambs available, two are offered on any given day while the remaining four are already undergoing their inspection period, ensuring that the offerings for the next several days are continuously prepared. This is an ordinary feature of Temple administration, but it creates a specific problem at the turn of the year.

### Ulla's Solution

Ulla, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, addresses this problem: *temidim shelo hutzrechu la-tzibur nifdin temimim* — *tamid* lambs that turn out to be no longer needed by the community may be redeemed even though they are unblemished.

The context is as follows. The Jewish fiscal year for Temple offerings begins on Rosh Chodesh Nissan, based on the principle of *olat chodesh be-chodsho* — which the Rabbis interpret to mean that when the year is renewed, new funds must be used. The *machatzit ha-shekel* contributions collected for that year fund all the *korbanot* of that year, and from Rosh Chodesh Nissan onward, new money is required. But in the days leading up to Rosh Chodesh Nissan, there will be four lambs in the chamber — acquired with last year's funds — which cannot be offered once the new year begins. They are perfectly unblemished and sanctified; there is simply no permissible use for them as *tamidim* from Rosh Chodesh Nissan onward.

Ulla's solution is to redeem them. The lambs are redeemed — their sanctity is removed and transferred — enabling that sanctity to be relocated onto new-year funds and the lambs themselves returned to ordinary status. The *chidush* here is significant: normally, a *korban* cannot be redeemed unless it has developed a blemish. An unblemished animal with the sanctity of a *korban* cannot simply be sold off. Yet Ulla asserts this is permitted in the case of communal *tamidim* at year's end.

### Rav Chisda Objects

Rabba was teaching this ruling when Rav Chisda challenged him sharply: *man tzait lach u-l'Rabbi Yochanan rabach* — who is going to accept this, from you or from your teacher Rabbi Yochanan? *Ve-chi kedusha she-bahen l'heichan halachah* — where does their *kedushah* go? The objection is not merely legal but conceptual: the *kedushah* of an unblemished animal cannot simply be dissolved. A blemish is precisely what creates the opening for *kedushah* to be transferred — it signals that the animal can no longer serve its intended function, which is what permits the sanctity to migrate elsewhere. Without a blemish, that opening does not exist, and the *kedushah* has nowhere to go.

Rabba responds by turning the question back to Rav Chisda: *ve-at lo tishbar d'lamina kedusha she-bahen l'heichan halachah* — don't you yourself face the same question? There is a parallel case that Rav Chisda would presumably accept, and it raises the identical difficulty.

## Keteret Analogy

### Keteret Problem

The parallel case involves *ketoret* — the incense. At the end of each year, there is typically a surplus of *ketoret* left over from the prior year's allocation. Just as with the *tamid* lambs, one cannot simply carry last year's *ketoret* into the new year; the new year requires new funds. Yet this leftover *ketoret* has been sanctified and, seemingly, cannot be redeemed without a blemish — it has none. The Mishnah (*u'tnan: motar ha-ketoret — mah hayu osin bah?*) addresses precisely this problem, and the procedure it mandates involves what appears to be exactly the kind of *kedushah* transfer that Rav Chisda found objectionable.

### Payment & Repurchase

The procedure as described in the Mishnah: *mafrishim mimenu s'char ha-umanim u-mechallelin otah al ma'ot ha-umanim v'notnin otah la-umanim b'scharan* — one separates from the *ketoret* the equivalent of the wages owed to the craftsmen, transfers the *kedushah* of the *ketoret* onto money belonging to those craftsmen, and then gives them the now-unsanctified *ketoret* as their payment.

The mechanics work as follows. A family of *kohanim* was designated to work on the *ketoret* and was compensated for their labor from Temple funds. That compensation money, having been designated for Temple use, itself bore *kedushah*. The solution exploits a fascinating halakhic principle: when sanctified money is paid to a worker for their labor, the *kedushah* is transferred not to the money received by the worker but to the abstract value of the labor performed. The worker's money is thus freed of its sanctity. Using this principle, the Temple pays the craftsmen with sanctified funds, allowing the *kedushah* to migrate into the value of their labor. The *ketoret* is then given to the craftsmen as payment — but now, since their money is no longer sanctified, it is simply an exchange of goods for labor compensation. New-year funds then repurchase the *ketoret* from the craftsmen, at which point the new money's *kedushah* transfers to the *ketoret*, making it new-year *ketoret*. The craftsmen receive the payment money, which is now ordinary funds since the *kedushah* transferred at the point of repurchase.

In all of this, the central operation — transferring the *kedushah* off the *ketoret* in the first step — is precisely what Rav Chisda said cannot be done with an unblemished *korban*. The *ketoret* has no blemish. It is fit to be used. Yet its *kedushah* is transferred. So Rabba asks: *ve-chi kedusha she-bahen l'heichan halachah* — where did the *kedushah* of the *ketoret* go? You face the same problem you leveled at me.

### Kedushah Types

Rav Chisda's response introduces a foundational distinction: *shani ketoret, de-kedushat damim hi* — the *ketoret* is different, because it has only *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*.

This distinction is conceptually significant and deserves careful articulation. *Kedushat damim* — sanctity of monetary value — is a status in which an object is holy because it is owned by the Temple and its relationship to the *mikdash* is purely one of worth. The object itself is not going to be used in the Temple's service; it is simply an asset whose value belongs to the *hekdesh*. One can think of *kedushat damim* as the Temple treating an object entirely as a monetary unit — what matters is what it is worth, not what it is. *Kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity — is a fundamentally different category: the object itself, in its very being, is designated to serve a Temple function. It is not about its monetary equivalent; it is about the thing itself being used.

The most obvious example of *kedushat haguf* is a *korban*: the animal itself is going to be slaughtered and offered. But *kedushat haguf* can also apply to materials that are themselves going to be used in the Temple's sacred service, including, eventually, *ketoret*. And it can apply even to construction materials that will physically become part of the Temple structure.

### Blemish Requirement

The distinction between *kedushat damim* and *kedushat haguf* directly explains why a blemish is required in one case but not the other. *Kedushat damim* is all about converting the object into monetary value — that is its very purpose. Of course one can redeem it without a blemish; redemption is the entire point. There is no conceptual difficulty in transferring the *kedushah* elsewhere, because the *kedushah* was never about the object itself in the first place. It was about its value, and value can always be transferred.

*Kedushat haguf*, by contrast, is about the thing itself. Its identity is to serve in the Temple. That identity cannot simply be dissolved by an act of the will or a legal mechanism — the object's very being is dedicated to its Temple function. It can only be removed when the object demonstrably can no longer fulfill that function — which is precisely what a blemish establishes. A blemish shows that the animal can no longer be brought on the altar, and that demonstration is what permits the *kedushah* to migrate. Without a blemish, the object's identity remains fully intact, and *kedusha lehiychan halachah* — there is nowhere for the *kedushah* to go.

This is why Rav Chisda objects to the *tamid* lambs: they have *kedushat haguf*, and without a blemish, there is no mechanism for removing their sanctity. But the *ketoret*, he maintains, still has only *kedushat damim* at the stage when the year-end transfer is performed — and therefore the question does not arise.

### Keteret's Stages

This answer, however, immediately raises an obvious difficulty. *Ketoret* is itself offered on the *mizbe'ach ha-zahav* in the Temple! It is not merely an asset; it is itself going to be used in the *avodah*. How can it be characterized as *kedushat damim* only?

The resolution lies in the stages of *ketoret*'s preparation. An animal that is sanctified as a *korban* is immediately usable — one need only slaughter it, and the slaughter itself is the beginning of the *avodah*. Its *kedushat haguf* attaches from the moment of sanctification. The *ketoret*, however, undergoes an extensive process of preparation: the raw ingredients must be obtained, ground, compounded, and eventually placed in a sacred vessel (*kli shares*) before they are ready to be offered. Until they reach that final stage — until they have been placed in the *kli shares* and are ready for actual use — they remain in process. And because they are still in process, still not fully transformed into the finished, ready-to-use *ketoret*, they retain the character of *kedushat damim*. They have not yet acquired *kedushat haguf*.

It is only when the *ketoret* is placed in the *kli shares* and the *kohen* is ready to offer it that *kedushat haguf* is conferred. Before that moment — including, apparently, even when the *ketoret* is already compounded and in storage — it is still characterized as *kedushat damim*, and the year-end transfer procedure is therefore permissible. The Gemara will continue to explore the precise contours of this analysis in the sugya that follows.
