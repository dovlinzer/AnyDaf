# Zevachim 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach Exception

### Mishna Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פֶּסַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ – לִשְׁמוֹ כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ פָּסוּל; וּבִשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – לִשְׁמוֹ פָּסוּל, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** When **a Paschal offering** is sacrificed **at its** appointed **time,** on Passover eve after noon, if it is sacrificed **for its sake,** it is **fit;** if it is sacrificed **not for its sake,** it is **unfit,** as stated in the mishna. **And** when an offering consecrated as a Paschal offering is sacrificed **during the rest of the days of the year,** if it is sacrificed **for its sake,** it is **unfit;** if it is sacrificed **not for its sake,** it is **fit.**

The *mishna* established a fundamental *halacha* regarding the *korban pesach* and the *chatat*: unlike other *zevachim*, which if slaughtered with incorrect intent — either for the wrong *korban* or for the wrong owner — are at least *kosher* post-facto even if they fail to discharge the owner's obligation, these two offerings are exceptions. The *korban pesach* brought *shelo lishmah* is disqualified. This much was established early in the *sugya*, and the remainder of the discussion pivots to a second point: what happens when a *korban pesach* is brought outside its proper time.

### Sugya Outline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר קְרָא: "וְאִם מִן הַצֹּאן קׇרְבָּנוֹ לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים לַה׳" – דָּבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן יְהֵא לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, i.e., that if a Paschal offering is sacrificed during the rest of the year it is fit if sacrificed not for its sake? **Shmuel's father says: The verse states: "And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings unto the Lord is of the flock"** (Leviticus 3:6), indicating that an offering **that is brought** only **from the flock will be a sacrifice of peace offerings.** The Paschal offering fulfills this criterion.

The governing principle under scrutiny is that a *korban pesach* brought *shelo lishmo shelo bizmano* — without correct intent and outside the proper season — reverts to being a *korban shelamim*, regardless of what other *korban* the slaughterer had in mind. The *sugya* has mounted a series of challenges to this principle, each framed by the word *eima* — "one might suggest" — proposing alternative *korbanot* to which the *pesach* might revert instead of *shelamim*. We now rejoin the discussion in the middle of the *ma'aser* challenge.

### Ma'aser Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא שַׁחְטֵיהּ לְשֵׁם מַעֲשֵׂר – לֶיהֱוֵי מַעֲשֵׂר! לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? דְּלָא לִיבְעֵי נְסָכִים, וּלְמִלְקֵא עֲלֵיהּ בְּ"לֹא יִגָּאֵל". אָמַר קְרָא: "הָעֲשִׂירִי יִהְיֶה קֹדֶשׁ" – זֶה מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵין אַחֵר מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If so, **say** that if one **slaughtered** a Paschal offering **for the sake of** the animal **tithe, let it be** rendered animal **tithe,** which, like a Paschal offering, is an offering of lesser sanctity. The Gemara adds: **For what *halakha*** does it matter, practically speaking, that a Paschal offering has been rendered an animal tithe rather than a peace offering? It matters **because** if it is rendered an animal tithe, **it should not require libations. And** furthermore, if one sells it, he **should be flogged for** transgressing the prohibition: **"It shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:33), which applies to the animal tithe even after its slaughter. The Gemara responds: **The verse states** with regard to the animal tithe: **"The tenth shall be sacred"** (Leviticus 27:32). The definite article indicates that **this,** the tenth animal, is the **tithe, but another** animal slaughtered for its sake is **not a tithe.**

*Ma'aser behema* is classified as *kodshim kalim*, like *shelamim*, but occupies a lower level of sanctity: it requires no *nesachim* and has only a single *matana* of the blood rather than the full *shtayim she'hen arba* of the *shelamim*. The *ma'aser behema* is designated by the owner, who drives the newborn animals of his flock through a narrow passageway and counts them — the tenth animal to pass through becomes *ma'aser*. It is precisely this counting process that underlies the rich imagery of the *Rosh Hashana* and *Yom Kippur* liturgy, which draws on the language of the shepherd tallying his flock.

## Reversion Alternatives

### Bechor Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: שַׁחְטֵיהּ לְשֵׁם בְּכוֹר – לֶיהֱוֵי כִּבְכוֹר! לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? דְּלָא לִיבְעֵי נְסָכִים, אִי נָמֵי דְּלִיתְּבֵיהּ לְכֹהֲנִים. בְּכוֹר נָמֵי, "עֲבָרָה"–"עֲבָרָה" מִמַּעֲשֵׂר גָּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Still, **say** that if one **slaughtered** a Paschal offering **for the sake of a firstborn** animal, **let it be** rendered **like a firstborn.** The Gemara adds: **For what *halakha*** does it matter, practically speaking, that it has been rendered a firstborn rather than a peace offering? It matters **because** if it is rendered a firstborn, **it should not require libations. Also,** this means **that one should give it to the priests.** The Gemara answers: With regard to **a firstborn too,** the verse states: "That you shall set apart [*veha'avarta*] unto the Lord all that opens the womb" (Exodus 13:12). Since with regard to the animal tithe it is stated: "Whatever passes [*ya'avor*] under the rod, the tenth shall be holy" (Leviticus 27:32), it is **derived** by verbal analogy between the **passing [*avara*]** stated with regard to a firstborn and the **passing [*avara*]** stated with regard to the animal **tithe** that a Paschal offering cannot become a firstborn either.

Having treated *ma'aser*, the *gemara* turns to the *bechor*, which shares a close affinity with *ma'aser*. Unlike *ma'aser*, which the owner designates, the *bechor* is *kadosh meirechem* — the firstborn animal of the flock or herd becomes sanctified automatically at birth. Notably, many *amoraim* hold that there is nonetheless a *mitzvah lehakedisho*, a positive obligation to actively designate and acknowledge the animal's sanctity — a concept structurally parallel to *kiddush* on *Shabbat*, where the day is made holy by the Almighty regardless of human action, yet we are nonetheless commanded to declare and acknowledge that holiness. Rav Soloveitchik develops this parallel at length in the *Shiurim L'zecher Abba Mari*.

The *gemara* proposes: *eima shachatei l'shem bechor* — perhaps if one slaughtered a *korban pesach* with the intent that it be a *bechor*, it should revert to a *bechor* rather than to a *shelamim*. The *halachic* import would be twofold: first, that the offering would not require *nesachim* (which a *korban shelamim* does require but a *bechor* does not); and second, that it would have to be given to the *kohanim* to eat, not to the owner. The *gemara* rejects this by invoking a *gezeira shava* linking *bechor* and *ma'aser*, which appear together at the end of *Parshat Bechukotai* in *Sefer Vayikra*.

### Ma'aser Challenge

The *gemara*'s rejection of the *ma'aser* reversion rests on the same textual proximity: *bechor* and *ma'aser* are linked by *gezeira shava*, and the same logic that dispenses with one applies equally to the other.

### Temurah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: שַׁחְטֵיהּ לְשֵׁם תְּמוּרָה – לֶיהֱוֵי תְּמוּרָה! לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? לְמִלְקֵא עֲלֵיהּ, אִי נָמֵי לְמֵיקַם עֲלֵיהּ בְּ"לֹא יִמָּכֵר וְלֹא יִגָּאֵל". אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר קְרָא: "וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ" – זוֹ תְּמוּרָה, וְאֵין אַחֵר תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But say** that if one **slaughtered** a Paschal offering **for the sake of a substitute, let it be** rendered **a substitute.** The Gemara adds: **For what *halakha*** does it matter, practically speaking, that it has been rendered a substitute rather than a peace offering? The one who slaughtered it **should be flogged for** transgressing the prohibition of effecting substitution. **Also,** its sale **should be determined to** violate the prohibition: **"It shall not be sold or redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:28). **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, says:** It is derived from that which **the verse states** with regard to substitution: **"Then both it and that for which it is changed** shall be sacred" (Leviticus 27:33). The verse teaches that only **it,** the animal substituted, is **a substitute, but another** animal is **not a substitute.**

The *gemara* then asks: *ve'eima shachatei l'shem temurah* — what if one slaughtered the animal with intent that it be a *temurah*? All three of *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *temurah* appear together at the end of *Parshat Bechukotai*, which is itself a striking fact worth noting. *Temurah* is not an independent category of *korban* in the usual sense; rather, it is the prohibition against transferring the *kedusha* of a consecrated animal onto a different animal. The *halachic* consequences would be: *lemilka alei* — the slaughterer would be liable to *makkot* for having performed the prohibited exchange; and alternatively, *l'meikam alei b'lo yamir v'lo yiga'el* — the animal would be subject to the rule that once a *temurah* is made, the resulting animal can neither be sold nor redeemed.

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman derives from the *pasuk* — *v'haya hu utmurato yihyeh kodesh*, "it and its exchange shall be holy" — that this applies only to a genuine *temurah*, not to the *korban pesach*. The reading is striking: the *pasuk* on its face affirms that the *temurah* takes effect, yet Mar Zutra uses it to limit *temurah* only to cases of *hu* — the paradigmatic case — excluding the *korban pesach*, which instead reverts to *shelamim*. The *pasuk* is, in effect, being read almost in the opposite direction from its plain sense.

### Toda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: שַׁחְטֵיהּ לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – לֶיהֱוֵי כִּי תוֹדָה! לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? לְהַטְעִינוֹ לֶחֶם. מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּפֶסַח גּוּפֵיהּ לָא בָּעֵי לֶחֶם, וּמוֹתָרוֹ בָּעֵי לֶחֶם?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But say** that if one **slaughtered** a Paschal offering **for the sake of a thanks offering, let it be** rendered **like a thanks offering.** The Gemara adds: **For what *halakha*** does it practically matter that it has been rendered a thanks offering rather than a peace offering? If so, **it should require** a meal offering of forty loaves of **bread.** The Gemara answers: **Is there anything,** i.e., any situation, comparable to this suggestion **that a Paschal offering itself does not require bread, but its leftover,** an animal consecrated as a Paschal offering but ultimately not sacrificed on Passover eve, **requires bread** when slaughtered not for its sake? Such a suggestion is implausible.

The *gemara* next raises the *toda*: *eima shachatei l'shem Toda* — perhaps the *korban pesach* should revert to a *korban toda*. The suggestion is admittedly strange from the outset, since a *korban pesach* must come from sheep or goats, while a *korban toda* must come from cattle. Any *machshava* to bring a sheep or goat as a *toda* is thus peculiar. Nonetheless the *gemara* entertains it: *d'lehavyei k'Toda*, that it should be treated as a *toda*, the practical consequence being *l'asuyei lechem* — an obligation to bring the forty loaves of bread that accompany the *korban toda*.

### Toda Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי – מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּפֶסַח גּוּפֵיהּ לָא בָּעֵי נְסָכִים, וּמוֹתָרוֹ בָּעֵי נְסָכִים?! אֲנַן הָכִי קָאָמְרִינַן: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמוֹתַר תּוֹדָה עַצְמָהּ – לָא בָּעֵי לֶחֶם, וְאִילּוּ מוֹתָר דְּאָתְיָא לְהוּ מֵעָלְמָא – בָּעֲיָא לֶחֶם?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, now** that a leftover Paschal offering slaughtered for the sake of a peace offering becomes a peace offering, this objection can **also** be raised: **Is there anything** comparable to the suggestion **that a Paschal offering itself does not require libations, but its leftover requires libations** as a peace offering? The Gemara explains its answer: **This** is what **we are saying: Is there anything** comparable to the suggestion **that the leftover of a thanks offering itself does not require bread, but** the **leftover** of an offering **from** some **other** category **requires bread?**

The *gemara* rejects this sharply: *mi ika midei d'Pesach gufeih lo ba'ei lechem umotaro ba'ei lechem*? Can it really be that the *pesach* itself requires no bread, yet its *motar* — the leftover animal being brought at the wrong time — would require bread? This is wholly absurd. And notably, the absurdity runs even deeper here, since the *korban toda* includes among its forty loaves ten that are *chametz*, which cannot coexist with any *pesach* context whatsoever.

There is something philosophically interesting lurking beneath this rejection. The question of how much the mistakenly-offered sacrifice retains its original identity versus how much it becomes the new, intended *korban* runs throughout the *sugya*. Here the *gemara* suggests that the original identity is not entirely extinguished — it is absurd to say that a *korban pesach* should suddenly generate a *chiyuv lechem*. The original and the new cannot simply coexist in that way.

The *gemara* then tightens the reasoning further: *mi ika midei d'motar todah atzmah lo ba'ei lechem?* Even the *motar* of a *toda* itself — an animal left over from a *toda* that subsequently reverts to a *shelamim* — does not require bread. All the more so would something associated with a *toda* only by one degree of removal be free from any *chiyuv lechem*. This closes the door on the *toda* suggestion.

The *gemara* then notes the parallel objection regarding *nesachim*: the *pesach* itself does not require *nesachim*, yet once it reverts to *shelamim*, its *motar* does require *nesachim*. The answer offered is *anan hachi ka'amrinan* — this is precisely what we mean. The logic governing *toda* and its *lechem* is not parallel to the logic governing *shelamim* and their *nesachim*, and the two cases must be distinguished.

## Three Pesukim

### Asham Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יֵימַר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הִילֵּל: וּמִמַּאי דִּבְמוֹתַר פֶּסַח כְּתִיב? דִּילְמָא בְּמוֹתַר אָשָׁם כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** It was taught that the verse: "And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings unto the Lord is of the flock" (Leviticus 3:6), teaches that a Paschal offering sacrificed not on Passover eve is rendered a peace offering if sacrificed not for its sake. **Rav Yeimar, son of Rav Hillel, objects to this: But from where** is it derived **that** this verse **is written with regard to a leftover Paschal offering? Perhaps it is written with regard to a leftover guilt offering;** a guilt offering is also brought only from the flock.

Rav Yeimar the son of Rav Hillel challenges the foundational proof itself. The *gemara* has been relying on the *pasuk* *ve'im min ha'tzon korbano le-zevach shelamim* as establishing that a *korban pesach* reverts to a *korban shelamim*. But Rav Yeimar asks: *u'mimai de-be-motar pesach ketiv* — how do we know that this *pasuk* is speaking about *motar pesach* at all? *Dilma be-motar asham ketiv* — perhaps it is speaking about *motar asham*, an animal designated as a *korban asham* that is being brought outside its proper context. The *korban asham*, after all, also comes exclusively from the flock — specifically, a male ram, an *ayil* — so the *pasuk*'s reference to *min ha'tzon* would fit the *asham* just as well as the *pesach*.

This is a genuinely interesting challenge, since the *korban asham* is in some ways a *korban* without a settled identity: it shares features with the *chatat*, the *olah*, and the *shelamim*, and is frequently in play when the *gemara* tries to determine to which category a *korban* is most closely related. The suggestion here is that the *asham* is most naturally compared to the *shelamim*, and that the *pasuk* might be teaching that a *motar asham* reverts to *shelamim*.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: "וְאִם מִן הַצֹּאן קׇרְבָּנוֹ לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים" – דָּבָר הַשָּׁוֶה בְּכׇל הַצֹּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The verse states: "And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings** unto the Lord **is of [*min*] the flock,"** where the term "*min*" indicates **that is** brought **equally from all** species of the **flock,** i.e., from both sheep and goats. A guilt offering, by contrast, is brought only from rams, i.e., male sheep.

Rava responds by reading the *pasuk* more carefully: *ve'im min ha'tzon korbano le-zevach shelamim* — *davar ha-shaveh be-chol ha'tzon*. The *pasuk* must be speaking of something that applies equally to all types of flock, both sheep and goats. The *korban asham*, which can only be brought from a male ram — not from a goat — is far too restricted to fit this characterization. The *korban pesach*, by contrast, may be brought from either sheep or goats, and thus qualifies as something truly *shaveh be-chol ha'tzon*. The *pasuk* therefore cannot be speaking of the *asham*.

### Min as Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר חִיָּיא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר כָּהֲנָא: בְּכֹל אֲתַר אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ "מִן" – לְהוֹצִיא, וְכָאן "מִן" – לְרַבּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin bar Ḥiyya, and some say Rabbi Avin bar Kahana, objects to this: You say everywhere** that the word **"*min*"** is stated **to exclude** items, **but here** you claim that the word **"*min*"** is stated **to include** all species of the flock? You should say the opposite: The verse indicates an offering that is brought only from some species of the flock, i.e., a guilt offering.

Rav Avin bar Chiya — and some say Rav Avin bar Kahana — raises a different challenge: throughout *halacha*, the word *min* in a *pasuk* serves *lehotsi* — to exclude, to narrow. Here, in *ve'im min ha'tzon*, we are being told that the word *min* serves *lerabot* — to include the *korban pesach* within the *din* of *shelamim*. This is a methodological inconsistency that troubles the *drasha*.

### Min Excludes Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי: הָכָא נָמֵי "מִן" לְהוֹצִיא – דְּלָא אָתֵי בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, וְלָא אָתֵי בִּנְקֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Mani says: Here too,** the word **"*min*"** is stated **to exclude** certain categories of the flock, **as** a Paschal offering **is not brought** from an animal that is **in its second year, and is not brought from** the **female** animals of the flock.

Rav Mani defends the *drasha*: *hakha nami min lehotsi* — here too, *min* is functioning as an exclusion. Specifically, it excludes *ben shtei shanim* — an animal that is two years old — and *nekevah* — a female animal. A *korban shelamim* may be brought from an older animal or from a female, but a *korban pesach* may not. This *halacha* of the *korban pesach* — that it must be a first-year male — is in fact being derived from this very *pasuk*, which ostensibly discusses *shelamim*. It is a remarkable hermeneutic move: the exclusionary force of *min* teaches a *din* about the *korban pesach* from a *pasuk* that appears to be about something else entirely.

### Rav Chana Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּכִי כְּתִיב הַאי – בְּמוֹתַר פֶּסַח כְּתִיב?! וְהָא מִדִּכְתִיב "אִם כֶּשֶׂב", "אִם עֵז" – מִכְּלָל דְּלָאו בְּמוֹתַר פֶּסַח כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another objection. **Rav Ḥana of Baghdad objects to this: How can you say that when this** verse **is written, it is written with regard to a leftover Paschal offering? But from** that **which is written** in the subsequent verses: **"If** he brings **a lamb"** (Leviticus 3:7), and **"if** his offering is **a goat"** (Leviticus 3:12), one learns **by inference that** the passage is **not written with regard to a leftover Paschal offering;** the Torah states elsewhere, in Exodus 12:5, that a Paschal offering is brought from both lambs and goats, and it is unnecessary to teach this again in Leviticus.

Rav Chana of Baghdad presses a further challenge: if this *pasuk* is truly speaking about *motar pesach*, one must contend with another *pasuk* in *Vayikra* that addresses the *shelamim* by saying *im kesev im ez* — whether a sheep or a goat. The use of *im* in that context should signal that we are simply describing the normal *korban shelamim*, not any reversion from a *korban pesach*.

### Sheep/Goat Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: "כֶּבֶשׂ" – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַפֶּסַח לְאַלְיָה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: "אִם כֶּבֶשׂ" – לְרַבּוֹת פֶּסַח שֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ; וּשְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח – לְכׇל מִצְוַת שְׁלָמִים, שֶׁיִּטָּעֲנוּ סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וְשׁוֹק. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: "אִם עֵז" – הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, לִימֵּד עַל הָעֵז שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה אַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Those** verses **are necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The word **"lamb"** is written **to include the Paschal offering in** the requirement that the **tail** be offered on the altar, which is written subsequently with regard to a peace offering (Leviticus 3:9), since this *halakha* is not mentioned in the verses concerning the Paschal offering. The *baraita* continues: **When** the verse **states: "If** he brings **a lamb,"** it is **to include in all the mitzvot of peace offerings a Paschal offering whose** first **year has passed** and is therefore too old to be sacrificed as a Paschal offering, **and peace offerings brought due to a Paschal offering.** Specifically, this indicates **that they require placing hands** on the head of the offering, **libations, and the waving of** the **breast and thigh. And when** the verse **states:** "And **if** his offering is a **goat," it interrupted the** previous **matter** and **taught that the** sacrificing of **a goat does not require** that the **tail** be burned on the altar.

The *gemara* resolves this by assigning independent *derashot* to each element. *Kesev* — sheep — teaches that if the *korban pesach* comes from a sheep, its *alyah* (the characteristic fat tail of certain breeds of sheep, which constitutes a form of *chelev*) must also be burned on the *mizbeach* as one of the *chalavim*, just as with any *korban* that comes from a sheep. The *gemara* notes that this is a *halacha* about the *korban pesach* derived from a *pasuk* that appears to be discussing *shelamim*. The word *im kesev* in a second context then teaches that a *pesach* that aged out — that is, became more than a year old — is disqualified. And *ushelamim ha-ba'im machmat pesach* — the *shelamim* that accompanies the *pesach*, namely the *korban chagigah* of the fourteenth of Nisan, the *chagigat arba'ah asar* — is included in all the obligations of a standard *shelamim*: *semikhah*, *nesachim*, *tnufat chazeh va'shok*.

*Im ez* — goats — then serves to break the passage and teach that goats, which lack the distinctive fat tail of sheep, are not subject to the requirement of *alyah* at all. This is a natural exclusion from the rule about sheep.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא – מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא?! מִדַּאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: "וְאִם מִן הַצֹּאן קׇרְבָּנוֹ לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים" – דָּבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן יְהֵא לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים! וְאַכַּתִּי מִדְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ נָפְקָא! דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מִנַּיִן לְמוֹתַר פֶּסַח שֶׁקָּרֵב שְׁלָמִים? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ צֹאן וּבָקָר". וַהֲלֹא אֵין פֶּסַח בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן הָעִזִּים! אֶלָּא מִכָּאן לְמוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח שֶׁיְּהֵא לְדָבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן וּמִן הַבָּקָר; וּמַאי נִיהוּ – שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it derived from here** that a Paschal offering that is too old to be sacrificed as a Paschal offering is sacrificed as a peace offering? **It is derived from** the verse **that Shmuel's father** cites, **as Shmuel's father says:** The verse: **"And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings** unto the Lord **is of the flock"** (Leviticus 3:6), teaches that an offering **that is brought** only **from the flock,** i.e., the Paschal offering, **will be a sacrifice of peace offerings.** The Gemara responds: **But** even without the *baraita*, Shmuel's father's statement is **still** superfluous, as that *halakha* is **derived from that which Rav Naḥman** says that **Rabba bar Avuh says. As Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: From where** is it derived **that a leftover Paschal offering is sacrificed as a peace offering? As it is stated: "And you shall sacrifice the Passover offering unto the Lord, your God, of the flock and the herd"** (Deuteronomy 16:2). Why is the herd mentioned? **But isn't a Paschal offering brought only from the lambs and from the goats? Rather,** it is derived **from here that a leftover Paschal offering should be** sacrificed as an offering **brought** both **from the flock and from the herd. And what is that?** It is **a peace offering.**

The *gemara* then notes a potential redundancy: this teaching — that whatever comes from the flock reverts to a *korban shelamim* — also appears to emerge from a teaching of Shmuel's father (*avuh d'Shmuel*), who derives from *ve'im min ha'tzon korbano le-zevach shelamim* that whatever comes from the flock becomes a *shelamim*, specifically including the *korban pesach*. And Rav Nachman in the name of Rabba bar Avuh derives the same *halacha* from a *pasuk* in *Devarim* — *ve-zavachta pesach la-Hashem Elohecha tzon uvacar* — which says that the *korban pesach* may be from sheep or from cattle, even though in fact a *korban pesach* can only be brought from sheep or goats, not cattle. The *gemara* infers that the mention of cattle must be teaching that *motar ha-pesach* reverts to a *korban* that can be brought from cattle — namely, a *shelamim*.

### Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי, חַד לְעִיבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, וְחַד לְעִיבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא שְׁנָתוֹ, וְחַד לְלֹא עִיבְּרָה לֹא זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא שְׁנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** none of these derivations are superfluous, as **three verses are written** that teach the *halakha* that a Paschal offering that is sacrificed not on Passover eve is sacrificed as a peace offering. **One** verse teaches this *halakha* in a case where **its time** of sacrifice, Passover eve, **has passed, and its** first **year has** also **passed,** disqualifying it for sacrifice as a Paschal offering. **And one** verse teaches the *halakha* in a case where **its time** of sacrifice **has passed, but not its** first **year. And** the third **one** teaches a case where **neither its time** of sacrifice **nor its** first **year has passed,** but it was sacrificed before Passover eve.

The *gemara* now notes that three distinct *pesukim* teach the same principle. Rather than treating this as redundancy, it concludes that each *pasuk* addresses a different scenario of *pesach* disqualification: *chad l'avar zemano* — one for the case where the appointed time has passed; *ve'avar shenato* — and the animal has also aged beyond its first year; *ve'chad l'avar zemano velo shenato* — one for the case where the time has passed but the animal has not yet aged out; and *ve'chad d'lo avar lo zemano velo shenato* — one for the case where neither the time has passed nor the animal has aged out.

### Case Breakdown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חַד, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הֵיכָא דְּעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ וּזְמַנּוֹ – דְּאִידְּחִי מִפֶּסַח לִגְמָרֵי; אֲבָל עִיבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא שְׁנָתוֹ, דַּחֲזֵי לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִידְּחִי לְהוּ מִמִּילְּתַיְיהוּ; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא עִבֵּר לֹא זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא שְׁנָתוֹ, דַּחֲזֵי לְפֶסַח – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** all these verses **are necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** only **one** of the verses, **I would say** that it is referring to a case **where** both **its** first **year and its time** of sacrifice **have passed.** Only such a Paschal offering should be sacrificed as a peace offering, **as it was completely rejected from** its status as **a Paschal offering. But** in a case where **its time** of sacrifice **has passed, but its** first **year** has **not** passed, in **which** case it is still **fit to** be sacrificed as a Paschal offering on the **second *Pesaḥ*, I would say** that it is **not** sacrificed as a peace offering. **And had the Merciful One written** only **these two** verses, one might assume that only these Paschal offerings are sacrificed as peace offerings, **as they were** both **rejected from their status** as Paschal offerings. **But** in a case **where neither its time** of sacrifice **nor its** first **year have passed,** in **which** case it is still **fit to** be sacrificed as **a Paschal offering** on Passover eve, **I would say** that it is **not** sacrificed as a peace offering. Therefore, all three verses **are necessary.**

The necessity of all three is demonstrated through a *tzrichi* argument. Had the Torah written only the first case — time passed and the animal aged out — one might have thought that reversion to *shelamim* applies only where the animal is doubly disqualified (*tartei l're'uta*) and can serve no further *pesach* purpose whatsoever. Had the Torah written the first two cases together, one might still have excluded the third case: an animal that is still within its first year even after the *pesach* has passed remains eligible for *Pesach Sheni* and retains some connection to its *pesach* identity, so perhaps it does not revert to *shelamim*. And had the Torah addressed only cases where the time has passed, one might have thought that the rule does not apply when the animal is brought *before* the proper time for the *korban pesach* — for instance, on the twelfth of Nisan, when the animal could still be used as a *korban pesach* on the fourteenth. All three *pesukim* are therefore necessary.

Rashi explains that the third case — *d'lo avar lo zemano velo shenato* — refers precisely to a situation where the *korban pesach* animal is slaughtered before *Pesach* as a *shelamim*. The *kohen*, encountering the animal a few days early, brings it as a *shelamim* and it is valid. What the *mishna* means when it says that the *korban pesach* must be brought *lishmah* is therefore quite limited in scope: only on the fourteenth of Nisan must the *korban pesach* be brought with correct intent. At every other time, an animal designated as a *korban pesach* that is slaughtered *shelo lishmah* reverts automatically to a valid *shelamim*. The *halacha* is, in a sense, protective: bring your *pesach* on *Pesach* properly and you fulfill the *mitzvah*; bring it at any other time without the correct intent and it still counts as a valid *shelamim*. Either way the offering is not lost.

One might also ask about redesignation: an owner could simply perform *akirah* — uprooting the *pesach* designation — and formally redesignate the animal as a *shelamim*, a procedure discussed in *Pesachim*. If one was *tamei* and could not bring the *korban pesach* at the proper time, one may use that animal for *Pesach Sheni*, or alternatively bring it as a *shelamim* and bring a different animal on *Pesach Sheni*.

## Nesi'im Korbanot

### Nesi'im Problems

The *gemara* now pivots to a discussion prompted by the *korbanot* brought by the *nesi'im* — the tribal princes — during *Chanukat HaMizbeach*, as recorded in *Parshat Naso*. The twelve *nesi'im* brought their offerings on twelve consecutive days. Three difficulties trouble the *mefarshim* and *Chazal* alike. First: how could they have brought personal *korbanot* on *Shabbat*, since some of those twelve days must have fallen on *Shabbat*? Second: how could each *nasi* bring a *korban chatat* if he had not committed any sin obligating him to bring one? Third: how could an individual bring *ketoret* as a personal offering, when *ketoret* is a communal obligation brought twice daily and has no individual counterpart?

One approach — and a compelling *peshat* reading associated with Jacob Milgrom — is that *makriv* in this context means not that the offerings were actually sacrificed on those days, but that they were donated to the *Beit HaMikdash* to stock its stores for the *korbanot* that would follow. This resolves all three problems simultaneously. The word *makriv* usually connotes actual sacrifice, but the reading has considerable contextual merit.

The *gemara* here focuses specifically on the *korban chatat*, each *nasi* having brought one alongside his other offerings during the twelve days of *Chanukat HaMizbeach*. The question is: what is the nature of a *chatat* brought as a *nedava* — as a free-will offering — by someone who has no sin requiring atonement?

### Rav's First Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָבוֹג: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשׁוּם חַטַּאת נַחְשׁוֹן – כְּשֵׁירָה; דְּאָמַר קְרָא: "זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַחַטָּאת" – תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הַחַטָּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says in the name of Mavog: A sin offering that** one **slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering of Nahshon,** i.e., a sin offering similar to those brought by the princes during the dedication of the Tabernacle (see Numbers, chapter 7), not to atone for a sin but as a gift, is **fit** and satisfies its owner's obligation; **as the verse states: "This is the law of the sin offering"** (Leviticus 6:18), indicating that there is **one law for all the sin offerings.**

Rav, quoting from Mavog — an otherwise obscure figure, apparently a city name, quoted by Rav who was himself a student of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — taught: a *chatat* that was slaughtered *leshem chatat Nachshon*, with intent that it be like the *chatat* of Nachshon ben Aminadav (the *nasi* of the tribe of Yehuda, who brought the very first such offering), is *kesheira*. The proof-text is *zot torat ha-chatat* — this is the law of the *chatat* — from which is derived *Torah achat le-chol ha-chata'ot*: a single law governs all *chataot*. On the face of this teaching, it would seem that a free-will *chatat* — not associated with any specific sin — is valid at least *bedi'avad*, and perhaps even beyond the exceptional circumstances of the desert.

## Mavog Attempts

### Menachot Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רָבָא וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא; אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא לְרָבָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁנִּקְמְצוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, וְעָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה; לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַמְּנָחוֹת דּוֹמוֹת לִזְבָחִים. שֶׁהַקּוֹמֵץ מַחֲבַת לְשֵׁם מַרְחֶשֶׁת – מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ שֶׁהִיא מַחֲבַת, חֲרֵיבָה לְשֵׁם בְּלוּלָה – מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין שֶׁהִיא חֲרֵיבָה;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava sat and stated this *halakha*** of Mavog. **Rav Mesharshiyya raised an objection to Rava** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: All meal offerings from which a handful was removed not for their sake** but for the sake of another type of meal offering **are fit, and satisfied the obligation of the owner,** in contrast to animal offerings, which if slaughtered not for their sake do not satisfy their owners' obligations. This is **because meal offerings are not similar to slaughtered offerings. As, if one removes a handful** from a meal offering prepared in a shallow **pan for the sake of** a meal offering prepared in **a deep pan,** his intention is demonstrably incorrect, as **its** mode of **preparation proves that it is** a meal offering prepared in a shallow **pan.** Likewise, if one removes a handful from **a dry** meal offering **for the sake of** a meal offering **mixed** with oil, his intention is demonstrably incorrect, as **its** mode of **preparation proves that it is a dry** meal offering. The demonstrably incorrect nature of the intention prevents it from affecting the status of the meal offering in any way.

Rav Mesharshiya challenges Rava, who was transmitting this teaching of Rav. He cites the parallel *mishna* in Menachot — which mirrors the opening *mishna* of Zevachim — where Rabbi Shimon rules that all *menachot* whose *kmitza* was performed *shelo lishmah* are *kesheirot* and even discharge the owner's obligation, precisely because *menachot* are not comparable to *zevachim*: *she-ein ha-menachot domot la-zevachim*.

### Menachot Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בִּזְבָחִים אֵינוֹ כֵּן – שְׁחִיטָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן, קַבָּלָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן, זְרִיקָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן. טַעְמָא דְּמַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין, הָא אֵין מַעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין – לָא; אַמַּאי? לֵימָא: "זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה" – תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to slaughtered offerings this is not so.** There is **one** mode of **slaughter for all of** the offerings, **one** mode of **collection** of the blood **for all of them,** and **one** mode of **sprinkling for all of them.** Therefore, improper intention affects the status of the offering; if a rite is performed for the sake of the wrong type of offering, the offering does not satisfy its owner's obligation. Rav Mesharshiya inferred from the *baraita*: **The reason** a meal offering satisfies its owner's obligation even when one removed a handful for the sake of another type of meal offering **is that its** mode of **preparation proves** its true nature. **But** if **its** mode of **preparation does not prove** its nature, it does **not** satisfy its owner's obligation. **Why** not? If Mavog's derivation is correct, **let us say** likewise that the verse: **"This is the law of the meal offering"** (Leviticus 6:7), indicates that there is **one law for all of the meal offerings,** regardless of their mode of preparation.

The *gemara* explains the distinction: in *menachot*, the specific type of *mincha* being offered is identifiable from the *ma'asim* themselves — the actions taken. A *mincha machavat* (griddle-baked) versus a *mincha meracheshet* (pan-baked) are distinguishable by the manner of preparation; a dry *mincha* (*charivah*) versus one mixed with oil (*belulah*) is similarly self-evident from how it is made. Thus *ma'aseha mochi'chin aleha* — the actions testify to the nature of the offering, even if the stated intent was for a different type of *mincha*. In *zevachim*, by contrast, all animal sacrifices share the same *shechita*, the same *kabala*, and ostensibly the same *zrika* — and so the intent of the slaughterer governs in a way it does not for *menachot*. The *gemara* acknowledges — and this is somewhat difficult — that in practice different *zevachim* do have different *zrikot*, but the principle stands for purposes of this discussion.

The upshot of Rav Mesharshiya's challenge is that the *pasuk* *zot torat ha-chatat* parallels *zot torat ha-mincha*, and if the latter does not establish a blanket equivalence between all *menachot* (since we know there are *halachic* differences depending on intent), then the former cannot establish a blanket equivalence between all *chataot* either. The proof offered by Mavog is therefore flawed.

### Wrong Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָבוֹג: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּתְכַּפֵּר בָּהּ נַחְשׁוֹן – כְּשֵׁירָה; אֵין כַּפָּרָה לְמֵתִים. וְלֵימָא מֵת בְּעָלְמָא! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: טַעְמָא דְּמֵת; הָא דְּחַי דּוּמְיָא דְּנַחְשׁוֹן – פְּסוּלָה. וּמַאי נִיהוּ? חַטַּאת נָזִיר וְחַטַּאת מְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** Mavog's statement **was stated, it was stated like this: Rav says in the name of Mavog: A sin offering that one slaughtered on the condition that Nahshon,** the prince of Judah, **be atoned for by it is fit;** this is not considered a change of owner, as **there is no atonement for the dead.** The Gemara challenges: **But** if this was Mavog's statement, why did he mention Nahshon? **Let him state** this *halakha* in reference to **a dead** person **in general.** The Gemara answers: **This** statement of Mavog **teaches us** that **the reason** for the fitness of a sin offering slaughtered for the atonement of Nahshon **is that he is dead. Consequently,** if it was slaughtered for the sake of **a living** person obligated to bring a sin offering **similar to** that of **Nahshon,** it is **unfit. And what is that?** It is **a nazirite's sin offering or a leper's sin offering,** which are brought not to atone for a sin but to become ritually pure. Consequently, if one sacrifices a standard sin offering for the sake of one obligated to bring the sin offering of a nazirite or a leper, it is considered a change of owner, and the offering is unfit.

Second version: Rav, quoting Mavog, taught that a *chatat* slaughtered *al m'nat she-yitkaper bah Nachshon* — with intent that it effect atonement for Nachshon ben Aminadav — is *kesheira*. This is a case of *shinui be'alim* — the *kohen* had in mind the wrong owner. Here, crucially, the real owner is alive and has sinned, and the *kohen* during the *shechita* had in mind Nachshon ben Aminadav, who is long dead. Since *ein kapara la-metim* — the dead cannot achieve *kapara* — the intended *shinui be'alim* to a dead person is effectively no *shinui be'alim* at all, because the *ba'al* one is attempting to designate it for does not exist in any meaningful *halachic* sense. The offering therefore remains valid, though it does not achieve *kapara* for the actual owner, who must bring a new *korban chatat*. The *kohanim* may still eat it.

One might ask why Mavog did not simply say *met* — a dead person generally — rather than specifically Nachshon. The *gemara* explains: had he said *met* generically, one might have inferred that only in the case of a dead person does the *shinui be'alim* fail to disqualify, but that if the mistaken person were living — like Nachshon — the *korban* would be *pasul*. By specifying Nachshon, Mavog implies that the case parallels Nachshon in some way, which leads the *gemara* to ask: what makes someone *chai dumya d'Nachshon*? A living person who had no sin requiring a *chatat* — as was the case with Nachshon's offering. And if such a person were the intended (but erroneous) owner, it would also render the *korban pasul*. The examples of such a person are *chatat Nazir* and *chatat Metzora* — chataot that are not brought for a sin in the usual sense, but for a process of purification, and which the *gemara* accordingly characterizes as comparable to *olot* (*hanei olot ninhu*).

### Obligated Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי עוֹלוֹת נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָבוֹג: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת כְּנַחְשׁוֹן – כְּשֵׁירָה; חַטַּאת נַחְשׁוֹן – עוֹלָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: Why is the offering disqualified? **These** sin offerings of a nazirite and a leper **are** equivalent to **burnt offerings,** since they are not brought for atonement. Sacrificing a sin offering for the sake of one obligated to bring a burnt offering is not considered deviation with regard to the owner and does not disqualify it (see 3b). **Rather, if** Mavog's statement **was stated, it was stated like this: Rav says in the name of Mavog: A sin offering that one slaughtered for one who is obligated to bring a sin offering similar to** that of **Nahshon** is **fit; Nahshon's sin offering is** equivalent to **a burnt offering.**

Third version: Rav, quoting Mavog, taught that a *chatat* slaughtered *al mi she-mechuyav chatat keNachshon* — on behalf of someone obligated in a *chatat* comparable to that of Nachshon — is *kesheira*. This formulation is difficult to parse: the whole premise of the discussion is that Nachshon was *not* obligated to bring a *chatat* in the conventional sense, having committed no sin. The text here is acknowledged to be obscure. What seems to be meant is that this version attempts to identify a person whose obligation in a *chatat* is structurally unusual — analogous to the nature of Nachshon's *chatat* — and teaches that bringing a *chatat* on such a person's behalf is nonetheless valid. The objection raised is that *chatat Nachshon olah hi* — Nachshon's *chatat* functioned like an *olah*, a gift rather than an atonement — and since *chatat Nazir* and *chatat Metzora* are similarly characterized as *olot*, one could have derived this ruling from those cases instead. The *gemara* responds that Mavog preferred to cite a paradigmatic *chatat* (*ikar chatat nakat*) rather than the unusual edge cases of Nazir and Metzora.

### Wrong Sin Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמַר, אָמַר רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָבוֹג: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת נַחְשׁוֹן – פְּסוּלָה; חַטַּאת נַחְשׁוֹן – עוֹלָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **There is** one **who says** that **Rav says in the name of Mavog: A sin offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering of Nahshon** is **unfit.** This is because **a sin offering of Nahshon is** equivalent to **a burnt offering,** and he is therefore considered to have deviated from the type of offering.

Fourth version — and the *gemara* introduces this as *ika de'amri*, a variant tradition: Rav, quoting Mavog, taught the opposite — that a *chatat* slaughtered *leshem chatat Nachshon* is *pesulah*. This version resolves all the difficulties that burdened the previous three attempts. The *chatat* of Nachshon, having been brought as a free-will offering without an underlying obligating sin, is characterized as *chatat Nachshon olah hi* — it is functionally an *olah*, not a genuine *chatat*. An offering brought *leshem chatat* but for what is really an *olah* is *pasul*. This is clean and consistent, and dispenses with the need for the elaborate explanations required by the first three versions.

## Chatat Interchanges

### Chatat Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת דָּם, לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – כְּשֵׁירָה. לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת נָזִיר, לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת מְצוֹרָע – פְּסוּלָה; הָנֵי עוֹלוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: A sin offering** brought for consuming forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering** brought for consuming **blood,** or **for the sake of a sin offering** brought for engaging in **idol worship, is fit;** it is not considered deviation from the type of offering. But if it was slaughtered **for the sake of a nazirite's sin offering,** or **for the sake of a leper's sin offering,** it is **unfit; these** sin offerings **are** equivalent to **burnt offerings,** as they are not brought for atonement.

The *gemara* records a second teaching of Rav: *chatat cheilev she-shachatah l'shem chatat dam* — a *chatat* brought for the sin of eating forbidden fat, slaughtered with intent that it be for the sin of eating blood; or *l'shem chatat avodat kochavim* — for the sin of idolatry committed unwittingly (which obligates a *korban*) — is *kesheira*. Interchanging one sin-*chatat* for another sin-*chatat* does not disqualify the offering.

### Nazir/Metzora

However, *l'shem chatat Nazir v'chatat Metzora* — if one brought a *chatat cheilev* with intent that it be the *chatat* of a *nazir* or *metzora* — it is *pesulah*. This is because, as established, *hanei olot ninhu*: those *chataot* function like *olot*, not like sin-*chataot* in the full sense, and interchanging a real *chatat* with what is effectively an *olah* is a disqualifying *shinui kodesh*.

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: חַטָּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת דְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כָּרֵת כְּמוֹתָהּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא, אֵין קָבוּעַ כְּמוֹתָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of **a sin offering** brought for consuming forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering** brought **for** the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that the offering is fit, as the latter type of sin offering atones for a transgression punishable by ***karet*** when committed intentionally, **similar to** the transgression of consuming forbidden fat? **Or perhaps** is it unfit, as a sin offering brought for defiling the Temple is a sliding-scale sin offering, the contents of which vary with the financial situation of the transgressor (see Leviticus 5:3–13), and **not a set** sin offering, which is always an animal, **like** a sin offering brought for consuming forbidden fat?

Rava poses a question: *chatat cheilev she-shachatah l'shem chatat d'tumat mikdash ve-kodashav* — what is the law if one brought a *chatat cheilev* with intent that it be a *chatat* for the sin of *tumat mikdash ve-kodashav* (entering the *Beit HaMikdash* or eating consecrated food while *tamei*)? The latter sin is atoned for by a *korban oleh v'yored* — a variable offering whose form depends on the owner's financial circumstances, as discussed in Masechet Shavuot. The question is: *mi amrinan karet k'motah* — do we say that since both *chatat cheilev* and *tumat mikdash* carry liability for *karet*, they are sufficiently equivalent that the interchange does not disqualify? Or do we say *ein kevua k'motah* — a fixed-form *chatat* and a variable *korban oleh v'yored* are structurally different enough that interchanging them constitutes a disqualifying *shinui kodesh*?

### Shinuy Be'alim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי כּוּלְּהוּ לִפְסוּלָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? "וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת" – לְשֵׁם אוֹתָהּ חַטָּאת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: בַּעְיָא דְּרָבָא הֵיכִי מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנַן בְּשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ [וְהָכִי מַתְנֵינַן לָהּ], אָמַר רָבָא: חַטָּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת דָּם וְחַטַּאת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – פְּסוּלָה. עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטַּאת נָזִיר וְחַטַּאת מְצוֹרָע – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches** a version of Rav's statement according to which in **all of** the cases he mentioned, including one where the sin offering was slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering brought for consuming blood or for engaging in idol worship, the offering is **unfit. What is the reason?** It is derived from the verse: **"And slaughter it [*otah*] for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33), that a sin offering must be slaughtered **for the sake of that [*otah*] sin offering,** and no other. **Rav Ashi said to Rav Aḥa, son of Rava: How do you teach Rava's dilemma?** Rava appears to assume that if one slaughters a sin offering for the sake of some other types of sin offering it remains fit. How is this compatible with your version of Rav's statement, according to which slaughter for the sake of any other type of sin offering disqualifies it? Rav Aḥa **said to him: We teach** Rava's dilemma **with regard to deviation** with regard to the **owner** rather than from the type of offering. **And this is how we teach it:** First, **Rava says: A sin offering** brought for consuming forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for** the sake of one **who is obligated** to bring **a sin offering** for consuming **blood, or** for the sake of one who is obligated to bring **a sin offering** for engaging in **idol worship,** is **unfit.** Since the other person is also obligated to bring a sin offering, this is considered deviation with regard to the owner. But if it was slaughtered **for one who is obligated** to bring **a nazirite's sin offering or a leper's sin offering,** it is **fit,** as these sin offerings are not brought for atonement. Deviation with regard to the owner disqualifies an offering only if the other owner is obligated to bring a similar offering.

Rav Ashi asks Rav Acha the son of Rava how his father Rava's question was being transmitted. Rav Acha responds that when he taught the preceding material, he taught it entirely as a matter of *shinui be'alim* — change of owner — not *shinui kodesh*. That is, Rava's teaching about the various *chatat* interchanges concerned cases where the *korban* was brought on behalf of the wrong person, not cases where one *korban* type was substituted for another. Accordingly, Rava's question was also about *shinui be'alim*: if a *chatat cheilev* was brought *al mishum mechuyav chatat dam* or *chatat avodat kochavim* — on behalf of a person obligated in one of those *chataot* — it is *pesulah*; but if brought *al mishum mechuyav chatat Nazir ve-chatat Metzora* — on behalf of a person whose obligation is of the *olah*-like variety — it is *kesheira*.

### Karait Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָעֲיִין לַהּ הָכִי – בָּעֵי רָבָא: חַטָּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת דְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כָּרֵת כְּמוֹתָהּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא, אֵין קָבוּעַ כְּמוֹתָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And** then **we pose the dilemma like this: Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of **a sin offering** brought for consuming forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for** the sake of **one who is obligated** to bring **a sin offering for** the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that the offering is unfit, as the one who defiled the Temple or its sacrificial foods is obligated to bring a sin offering for a transgression punishable by ***karet*, similar to** the transgression of the owner? **Or perhaps** it remains fit, as a sin offering brought for defiling the Temple is **not a set** sin offering **like** a sin offering brought for consuming forbidden fat?

Rava's question, reframed in terms of *shinui be'alim*, becomes: if a *chatat cheilev* is brought on behalf of someone obligated in a *chatat* for *tumat mikdash ve-kodashav*, what is the law? *Mi amrinan karet kevosah* — do we say that since both offenses carry *karet*, the persons obligated in each are sufficiently equivalent that there is no meaningful *shinui be'alim*? Or does the fact that one *chatat* is *kevuah* (fixed) and the other is not (*ein kevuah kevosah*) mean that even in terms of *be'alim* these persons are categorically different?

### Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The question was left unresolved in the time of Rav Ashi. What the discussion does clarify is that Rav Acha the son of Rava reframed his father's question from *shinui kodesh* to *shinui be'alim*, and it is in that reframing that the *teiku* stands.
