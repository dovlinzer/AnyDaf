# Pesachim 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach vs. Proceeds

### Nidcheh Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁתְּמוּרַת פֶּסַח קְרֵיבָה, וּתְמוּרַת פֶּסַח אֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה, וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲנִי אֲפָרֵשׁ: הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו שְׁלָמִים. וְכֵן תְּמוּרָתוֹ. אַחַר שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח — קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים. וְכֵן תְּמוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I heard two rulings from my teachers: One ruling was that the substitute of a Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering after Passover, and another ruling was that the substitute of a Paschal lamb is not sacrificed as a peace-offering after Passover; and I cannot explain these two rulings.** **Rabbi Akiva said: I will explain: With regard to a lamb that is separated as a Paschal lamb and is then lost, leading the owner to separate another animal as its replacement, and is later found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, it is left to graze until it becomes unfit and disqualified for use as a sacrifice. It is then sold and becomes unconsecrated, and the owner must bring a peace-offering with its proceeds. And so too, the same is true with regard to its substitute. On the other hand, if the lost lamb is found after the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, it itself is sacrificed as a peace-offering, and so too, its substitute is sacrificed,** which explains the ruling that the substitute of a Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering.

The *mishnah* opens with a framework that has occupied the *sugyah* throughout: when a *pesach* that was not brought on *Pesach* may be offered itself as a *shelamim*, and when one may only wait for it to develop a *mum*, redeem its value, and use the proceeds. The organizing distinction turns on the concept of *nidcheh* — whether the animal was in some sense "pushed away," its availability rejected.

### Lost Before/After

The *mishnah*'s governing case involves an animal that was lost before *Pesach*. When the owner designates a replacement and subsequently slaughters it, the question is whether the original animal was available at the moment of *shechita*. If the original was found before *shechita*, then the act of *shechita* constitutes a rejection of that animal — one had it as an option and chose to slaughter the replacement anyway. In that case the original is considered *nidcheh*, stigmatized, and cannot be offered; only its monetary proceeds may be used. If, however, the original was found only after *shechita*, it was never available to be pushed away. The owner had no choice, and therefore the original retains its full *kedushah* and may be offered as a *shelamim*.

### Temurah Status

The *mishnah* extends this framework to *temurah* — the attempted transfer of *kedushah* from a sanctified animal to a non-sanctified one. Such a transfer does not dissolve the *kedushah* of the original; rather, both animals become *kadosh*. The *temurah*, the *mishnah* teaches, inherits the status of the original *pesach*. Whether the *temurah* itself may be offered or only its proceeds may be used tracks the status of the original — and this is the explanation of Rabbi Akiva.

## Rava on Temurah

### Found After Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלֵימָא: פֶּסַח קָרֵב וּפֶסַח אֵינוֹ קָרֵב. הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאִיכָּא תְּמוּרַת הַפֶּסַח דְּלָא קָרְבָה. אִיתְּמַר, רַבָּה אָמַר: קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וּלְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה שָׁנִינוּ. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וּלְאַחַר חֲצוֹת שָׁנִינוּ. וּלְרַבִּי זֵירָא, הָא קָתָנֵי קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח? אֵימָא: קוֹדֶם זְמַן שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח. כְּתַנָּאֵי. הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — יִרְעֶה, לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — יִקְרַב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — יִרְעֶה, לְאַחַר חֲצוֹת — יִקְרַב. אַחַר שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח יָבִיא שְׁלָמִים וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה. אֲבָל נִמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — תְּמוּרָתוֹ מִכֹּחַ קְדוּשָּׁה דְּחוּיָה קָא אָתָא, וְלָא קָרְבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Gemara questions the explanation of Rabbi Akiva: If so, let the mishna say these *halakhot* more simply, as follows: The Paschal lamb itself is sacrificed and the Paschal lamb is not sacrificed. According to Rabbi Akiva's explanation, the same rule applies to both the lamb and its substitute. What additional point is Rabbi Yehoshua making by speaking about the substitute? The Gemara answers: This comes to teach us through this formulation that there is a case of a substitute Paschal lamb that is not sacrificed.**
>
> **It was stated that there was a dispute between *amora'im* about the proper text of the mishna. Rabba said: We learned the opinion of Rabbi Akiva in the mishna as referring to the distinction of whether the lamb is found before the slaughter of the replacement lamb or after the slaughter of the replacement lamb. Rabbi Zeira said: We learned that it depends upon whether it is found before midday or after midday. Midday is the time that determines the status of an animal as a Paschal lamb; therefore, if the lamb is found after midday, even if it is found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, its identity as a Paschal lamb is not firmly established and it may be sacrificed as a peace-offering. And according to Rabbi Zeira, isn't the mishna teaching explicitly that it depends upon whether the lamb is found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb? How can he disagree with the mishna? The Gemara answers: Say that according to Rabbi Zeira, the mishna must be interpreted as referring not to the act of the slaughter itself but to a lamb that is found before the earliest time for the slaughter of the Paschal lamb, which is midday. The Gemara notes that this dispute among the *amora'im* is like the following dispute between *tanna'im* in a mishna: The Paschal lamb that is found before the slaughter of its substitute shall be left to graze until it becomes unfit; it is then sold, and the proceeds are used to purchase an animal that will be sacrificed as a peace-offering. If it is found after the slaughter, it itself is to be sacrificed as a peace-offering. This is parallel to the explanation of Rabba. Rabbi Eliezer says that if it is found before midday it shall be left to graze, but if it is found after midday it is to be sacrificed as a peace-offering, which is parallel to the explanation of Rabbi Zeira.**
>
> **It was stated in the mishna that if the original lamb is found after the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, one should bring the original lamb as a peace-offering, and the same applies to its substitute. Rava said: They taught that the substitute may be sacrificed as a peace-offering only when the original lamb was found after the slaughter of the replacement lamb and one performed the substitution after the slaughter, when the original lamb itself was fit to be offered as a peace-offering. But if the original lamb was found before the slaughter, even if one performed the substitution after the slaughter of the replacement lamb, the substitute lamb may not be sacrificed as an offering. The reason for this is that the original lamb may no longer be sacrificed as an offering and must be left to graze until it develops a blemish. Therefore, the sanctity of its substitute comes on the strength of deferred sanctity, and the substitute may not be sacrificed.**

Rava refines the *mishnah*'s treatment of *temurah* with precision. He teaches: *lo shanu ella shenimtza achar shechita vehimur bo achar shechita* — the rule permitting the *temurah* to be brought applies only when the original *pesach* was found after *shechita*, meaning it was never *nidcheh*, and the act of *temurah* was also performed after *shechita*. In that scenario the *temurah* may be brought as a *shelamim*.

### Inherited Kedusha

The more complex case arises when the original was found before *shechita* — making it *nidcheh* — and the act of *temurah* was performed only afterward. One might reason that the *temurah* itself was never pushed away, since the *temurah* act postdates the *shechita*; the *temurah* animal, one might argue, came into its *kedushah* in an unencumbered state. Rava rejects this. *Temurah mikoch kedusha dechuya kaasai* — the *temurah* derives its sanctity from the original, and since the original's *kedushah* was *dechuya*, stigmatized, the *temurah* inherits that stigmatized status. It cannot be offered.

### Timeline Diagram

The distinction can be mapped visually. At ten in the morning the original sheep is lost. A new animal is then sanctified as *pesach*. At one in the afternoon *shechita* is performed on the replacement. Where was the original at the moment of *shechita*? If it was present — *nimtza kodem shechita* — then the act of *shechita* on the replacement simultaneously constitutes a rejection of the original; the owner had both animals available and slaughtered only the new one. That act of *shechita* is therefore a *dechiyah*, and the original becomes *nidcheh*. If an act of *temurah* is then performed on yet another animal, that new animal is a copy of a *nidcheh* original, and cannot escape the stigma. If, by contrast, the original surfaced only after *shechita*, it was never subject to rejection, and a *temurah* performed thereafter is likewise untainted.

## Abaye Challenges

### Im Kesev Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: ״אִם כֶּשֶׂב״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִם כֶּשֶׂב״? לְרַבּוֹת תְּמוּרַת הַפֶּסַח אַחַר הַפֶּסַח שֶׁקְּרֵבָה שְׁלָמִים. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — פְּשִׁיטָא! לְמָה לִי קְרָא? אֶלָּא לָאו, שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to Rava based on a *baraita*: With regard to the verse: "If he brings a lamb for his offering" (Leviticus 3:7), what is the meaning when the verse states: "If he brings a lamb"? What is added by the word "if"? It comes to include the substitute of a Paschal lamb after the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb and teaches that it is offered as a peace-offering. Abaye clarifies the meaning of this *baraita*: What are the circumstances of the case under discussion? If we say it is a case in which the lamb was found after the slaughter and one performed the substitution after the slaughter, it is obvious, as any Paschal lamb found after the slaughter of its replacement becomes a peace-offering; in that case, why do I need a verse to teach this *halakha*? Rather, is it not addressing a case in which the original lamb was found before the slaughter of its replacement and one performed the substitution after the slaughter of the replacement, and nonetheless the substitute lamb may be offered as a peace-offering, in opposition to Rava's ruling?**

Abaye challenges Rava from a *braitta* interpreting the phrase *im kesev* in the *parashah* of *shelamim*. The *braitta* asks: *ma talmud lomar im kesev*? What is this phrase adding? Rashi notes that either the word *im* or the word *kesev* appears to be superfluous, since subsequent verses already clarify the species. The *braitta* answers that the phrase comes *lerabos tmuras hapesach achar pesach shekreiva shelamim* — to include the *temurah* of a *pesach*, once the original *pesach* has been offered, in the category of what may be brought as a *shelamim*.

### Pasuk Teaching

The *braitta* thus establishes as a matter of derivation from the *pasuk* that a *tmuras pesach* — a *temurah* of a *pesach* — may be brought as a *shelamim* after the original *pesach* has been sacrificed.

### Non-Obvious Case

Abaye then presses the logical implication. He asks: *heichi dami* — what scenario is this *pasuk* actually addressing? If it is speaking of the case where the original was found after *shechita* and the *temurah* was also performed after *shechita* — *peshita*! Of course the *temurah* may be brought as a *shelamim*; the original was never *nidcheh*, and there is no stigma whatsoever. *Lama li kra* — why would a *pasuk* be needed for something so obvious? If the *pasuk* is teaching a *chiddush*, it must be addressing a scenario where there is reason to think the *temurah* cannot be brought — namely, Rava's scenario, where the original was found before *shechita* but the *temurah* was performed afterward. And if the *pasuk* says it can be brought, that would directly contradict Rava.

### Asmachta Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No; actually it can be explained as referring to a case in which the original lamb was found after the slaughter of the replacement, and one performed the substitution after the slaughter. It is indeed obvious and no verse is necessary to teach this *halakha*; the verse cited is not the source for this ruling but is a mere support.**

The *Gemara* deflects this challenge. It says: *lo* — the *pasuk* can indeed be speaking of the simple case, where both the discovery and the *temurah* occurred after *shechita*. Even though that case is obvious, the *pasuk* is merely cited as an *asmachta*, a textual support, rather than as the actual source of the law. The *Gemara* thus insulates Rava's position from this line of attack.

## Pesach Pasuk Purpose

### Conversion to Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קְרָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״כֶּשֶׂב״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַפֶּסַח לְאַלְיָה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אִם כֶּשֶׂב״, לְרַבּוֹת פֶּסַח שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, וּשְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח לְכׇל מִצְוַת שְׁלָמִים, שֶׁטָּעוּן סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק. וּכְשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאִם עֵז״ — הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, לִימֵּד עַל הָעֵז שֶׁאֵין טְעוּנָה אַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara asks: But then for what purpose does this verse come? The Gemara answers: As it was taught in a *baraita*: The word "lamb" comes to include the Paschal lamb in the requirement that the fat tail be offered on the altar. When it says: "If he brings a lamb," which alludes to additional types of offerings it comes to include a lamb consecrated as a Paschal lamb whose first year has passed and is therefore too old to be offered as a Paschal lamb, or peace-offerings that come due to a Paschal lamb, both those separated as a substitute for a Paschal lamb and those that were actually consecrated as a Paschal lamb but the owner fulfilled his obligation with a different Paschal lamb, in all the mitzvot of peace-offerings. These offerings are no longer considered Paschal lambs and are treated as peace-offerings in every way. Therefore, they require leaning and libations and the waving of the breast and thigh. And when it says: "And if a she-goat" — it interrupted the subject, and from this interruption the *braitta* derives that a she-goat does not require the fat tail.**

Since the *pasuk* is an *asmachta* in this context, the *Gemara* asks: *kra lama li ata* — if the phrase *im kesev* is not needed to teach the *temurah* rule, what is it actually teaching? The *Gemara* answers from a separate *braitta*. The word *kesev* comes *lerabos es hapesach l'alyah* — to include the *pesach* in the law of the fat tail. But first the *braitta* establishes the more fundamental teaching: a *pesach* that cannot be offered as a *pesach* is converted into a *shelamim*, and it follows all the laws of *shelamim*. The *Torah* never provides explicit detail about what is done with the fat of a *pesach*; we have only very general verses — one instructing that the blood of sacrifices be sprinkled on the *mizbeach* and the flesh eaten, and another commanding that the fat be burned — neither of which focuses specifically on the *pesach*. The *pasuk* in the *shelamim* section therefore teaches that the fat of a *pesach* is treated in all respects as the fat of a *shelamim*.

### Fat Tail Law

The word *kesev* specifically — "a lamb" — teaches that when a *pesach* that is a lamb is converted into a *shelamim*, the *alyah*, the fat tail, is included in what is offered on the *mizbeach*. This is a *chiddush*, since the fat tail is specific to sheep and is included in the *shelamim* service for lambs; the *pasuk* teaches that the same applies to a lamb *pesach* brought as a *shelamim*.

### Shelamim Laws

The word *im* teaches that all the full apparatus of *shelamim* obligations applies to a *pesach* that becomes a *shelamim*: *smicha* (the laying on of hands, which is not performed on a *pesach* itself), *nesachim* (the accompanying wine and flour offerings), and *tenufas chazeh ve-shok* (the waving of the breast and thigh). None of these apply to a regular *pesach*, but once the animal is converted into a *shelamim*, every *mitzvah* of *shelamim* applies to it.

### Goat Exclusion

The phrase *ve-im ez* — "and if a goat" — then interrupts the subject, and from this interruption the *braitta* derives that a goat does not require the fat tail. *L'me'at es ha-ez she-ein teuna alyah* — the goat is excluded from the fat tail requirement. This is relevant because a *pesach* may be either a lamb or a goat; if the *pesach* is a goat, the fat tail is not offered. One might note, as is evident to any observer of livestock, that goats do not in fact possess a substantial fat tail as sheep do. Rashi, aware of this, suggests that the *Gemara* seems to assume goats do have a fat tail, and uses this as evidence to that effect — though the resolution remains unclear.

## Rava Reversed

### Temurah Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא: הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטַת הַפֶּסַח — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו שְׁלָמִים. וְכֵן תְּמוּרָתוֹ. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל נִמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה — תְּמוּרָתוֹ קְרֵיבָה שְׁלָמִים. מַאי טַעְמָא — כִּי קָבְעָה שְׁחִיטָה מִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ. מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ — לָא קָבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some teach this dispute with regard to the first clause of the mishna, which states that a Paschal lamb which is first lost and then found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb must be left to graze until it becomes unfit, and it is then sold, and one must bring a peace-offering with its proceeds; and likewise, the same is the case with regard to its substitute. With regard to this *halakha*, Rava said: They taught this *halakha* only in a case where the original lamb was found before the slaughter and one performed the substitution before the slaughter; but if the original lamb was found before the slaughter and one performed the substitution after the slaughter of the replacement lamb, its substitute is offered as a peace-offering. What is the reason for his opinion? The slaughter of the replacement establishes a halakhic reality only with regard to something which is fit for it; in other words, it is only with regard to a Paschal lamb itself that it matters whether it was found before or after the slaughter. However, with regard to something that was not fit for it, such as the substitute, which cannot be offered as a Paschal lamb in any event, the slaughter does not establish its status. Therefore, it can later be sacrificed as a peace-offering.**

The *Gemara* notes that some authorities teach Rava's statement not in connection with the end of the *mishnah* — which addresses when the *pesach* itself may be brought — but in connection with the beginning, which addresses when only proceeds may be used. In this reversed formulation, Rava's statement works as follows: *amar Rava lo shanu ella shenimtza kodem shechita vehimur bo kodem shechita* — the rule that the *temurah* may not be used itself applies when the original was found before *shechita* and the act of *temurah* was also performed before *shechita*.

### Shechita Effect

In this version, the reasoning is equally precise. If the *temurah* was performed before *shechita*, then at the moment of *shechita*, both the original and the *temurah* were present and available; the act of *shechita* on the replacement pushes away both of them. But if the original was found before *shechita* — making it *nidcheh* — while the act of *temurah* was performed only after *shechita*, the *temurah* can actually be brought. The reasoning: *ki kava shechita mi-de-chazi lei, mi-de-lo chazi lei lo kava* — *shechita* only establishes the status of *nidcheh* with respect to an animal that was actually available and fit at the time of *shechita*. An animal that did not yet exist as a *temurah* when *shechita* was performed was not something *shechita* could push away. Therefore, even though the *temurah* derives its *kedushah* from the original *nidcheh* animal, in this reversed version Rava holds that the *temurah* itself was never pushed off and may be brought.

## Pesach Hu Pasuk

### Pesach Hu Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף לִפְנֵי הַפֶּסַח כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הוּא״ — הוּא קָרֵב, וְאֵין תְּמוּרַת הַפֶּסַח קְרֵיבָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — פְּשִׁיטָא! לְמָה לִי קְרָא? אֶלָּא לָאו, שֶׁנִּמְצָא קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** One **might have thought that even a substitute Paschal lamb that was found before the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb should have the same status, and it should be permitted to sacrifice such a lamb as a peace-offering. Therefore, the verse states: "It," to emphasize that it, a valid Paschal lamb, is sacrificed, and the substitute of a Paschal lamb is not sacrificed. What are the circumstances of the case under discussion? If we say it is a case in which the lamb is found before the slaughter and one performed the substitution before the slaughter — it is obvious! Why do I need a verse to teach this *halakha*? Rather, is it not addressing a case in which the original lamb was found before the slaughter and one performed the substitution after the slaughter of the replacement, and the *baraita* ruled that the substitute lamb may not be sacrificed as a peace-offering, in opposition to the ruling of Rava? The Gemara concludes that the refutation of the opinion of Rava is indeed a conclusive refutation, and his opinion is rejected according to this version.**

Abaye now challenges the reversed version of Rava's statement from a different *braitta*. This *braitta* focuses on the *pasuk* *pesach hu l'Adonai* — "it is a *pesach* to God" — and derives from the word *hu* that only the original *pesach* itself can be brought in certain circumstances, *ve-ein tmuras pesach kriva* — and in some cases the *temurah* of a *pesach* cannot be brought.

### Non-Obvious Teaching

Abaye applies the same logical methodology he used earlier. *Heichi dami* — what scenario does this exclusion address? If both the discovery of the original and the *temurah* act occurred before *shechita*, *peshita* — of course the *temurah* cannot be brought, since the *shechita* rejected both the original and the *temurah* simultaneously. *Lama li kra* — no *pasuk* is needed for that. If the *pasuk* teaches an exclusion, it must be addressing a case where one might have thought the *temurah* could be brought.

### Found Before, Temurah Af…

The non-obvious case is precisely Rava's reversed scenario: the original was found before *shechita* — making it *nidcheh* — and the act of *temurah* was performed after *shechita*. One might think the *temurah* is valid since it was never itself pushed away. The *pasuk* *pesach hu* comes to teach that even in this scenario, the *temurah* cannot be brought, because its *kedushah* originates from a *dechuya* source.

### Refutation Accepted

Unlike in the first version of the exchange, where the *Gemara* deflected Abaye's challenge by invoking *asmachta*, here the *Gemara* accepts the argument. *Tiyuvta de-Rava tiyuvta* — this is a knockdown refutation of Rava's reversed position. The *Gemara* does not reach for *asmachta* here because, as it observes, there was no other available interpretation of the *pasuk* — it had to be teaching this non-obvious case. And if it is teaching that case, Rava's reversed position is wrong: the *temurah* cannot be brought even when the *temurah* act postdates *shechita*, because it inherits the *nidcheh* status of the original. This outcome aligns with Rava's first version of the statement and the conclusion of the earlier analysis.

## Shmuel's Principle

### Chatas Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת מֵתָה — בַּפֶּסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים, וְכֹל שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת רוֹעֶה — בַּפֶּסַח נָמֵי רוֹעֶה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין הַפֶּסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel stated a principle pertaining to the *halakhot* of offerings: With regard to any animal that was consecrated as an offering and becomes unfit such that a sin-offering in its condition would be placed in isolation for it to die, meaning that it would be caused to die, if it is a Paschal lamb in that condition it is sacrificed as a peace-offering. And with regard to any animal that becomes unfit such that a sin-offering in its condition is left to graze until it develops a blemish, if it is a Paschal lamb it is also left to graze. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering only when the lost lamb was found after the slaughter of the replacement Paschal lamb, but if it was found before the slaughter, there is no instance in which it is brought as a peace-offering.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a sustained and initially cryptic teaching of Shmuel that will occupy the rest of the *daf*. Shmuel states: *kol she-b'chatas mesa, be-pesach kareiv shelamim* — any scenario that, if it occurred to a *chatas*, would require the animal to be left to die, if the same scenario occurred to a *pesach*, the *pesach* would be brought as a *shelamim*. And: *ro'eh, be-Pesach nami ro'eh* — any scenario that, if it occurred to a *chatas*, would require it to graze until it developed a *mum* and its value be transferred, the same would apply to a *pesach*.

It must be noted at the outset, as Rashi observes, that there is no logical necessity connecting the laws of *chatas* to the laws of *pesach*. The *pesach* framework centers on whether an animal is *nidcheh*, while a *chatas* presents entirely different halachic dynamics. Rashi therefore characterizes Shmuel's principle as *simana be-alma* — a mnemonic organizing device, not a logical derivation. Shmuel is not saying that the laws are structurally connected; he is saying that, as a matter of fact, the same scenarios that yield death in a *chatas* happen to yield *shelamim* status in a *pesach*, and similarly for grazing. If you want to know what to do with a *pesach* in a given situation, ask yourself what you would do with a *chatas* in the same situation.

Alongside Shmuel's opaque principle, Rabbi Yochanan states the matter with characteristic directness: *ein pesach kareiv shelamim ela she-nimtza achar shechita* — a *pesach* is brought as a *shelamim* only if it was found after *shechita*. Before *shechita* it was *nidcheh* and cannot be brought itself. This is precisely what the *mishnah* and all prior analysis have established. The question, then, is what Shmuel could possibly be adding.

### Chatas Meitot Cases

To unpack Shmuel's principle, one must first identify the cases in which a *chatas* is left to die. There is a well-known mnemonic: *Vitinnah* — recalling the verse that Timna was a concubine to Elifaz son of Amalek. The letters of *Vitinnah* encode five cases. Vav stands for *vlad chatas* — the offspring of a *chatas*, which carries the same *kedushah* but cannot be offered. Tav stands for *tmuras chatas* — the *temurah* of a *chatas*. Mem stands for *mesu be-aleha* — the owner died. Nun stands for *nichperu be-acher* — atonement was achieved through a different animal, the equivalent of the *avud* case we have been discussing throughout. And ayin stands for *avar shnaso* — the animal became more than a year old and is therefore no longer fit for a *chatas*.

### Pesach-Relevant Cases

Not all five of these cases are applicable to a *pesach*. A *pesach* must be male, and a *chatas* is female; therefore *vlad pesach* is impossible, and similarly the *temurah* scenario in Shmuel's discussion is not about *temurah* of the *pesach* itself as a separate category. The cases that are relevant to *pesach* are: *avud* (the lost animal, which is precisely the case the *mishnah* discusses), *mesu be-aleha* (the owner died), and *avar shnaso* (the animal exceeded a year of age). With this inventory in hand, Shmuel seems to be saying that in all these scenarios — anywhere a *chatas* would die — the *pesach* is brought as a *shelamim*.

## Avar Shnaso Problem

### Reish Lakish View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וּכְלָלָא הוּא? וַהֲרֵי חַטָּאת שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ, דְּלִרְעִיָּה אָזְלָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: חַטָּאת שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ — רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא עוֹמֶדֶת בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, וְרוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to Shmuel's statement: Is that an established principle in every possible circumstance? Isn't there the case of a sin-offering whose first year has passed and is therefore no longer fit to be offered as a sin-offering, which goes to graze until it develops a blemish? As Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: A sin-offering whose first year has passed, it is viewed as though it were standing in a cemetery where a priest may not enter in order to retrieve it; therefore, it grazes until it develops a blemish.**

Rav Yosef challenges Shmuel's principle. He asks: *kellala hu* — is this really a universal rule? He targets the *avar shnaso* case. The standard teaching is that *avar shnaso* belongs to the five cases where a *chatas* dies; this is the ayin of the *Vitinnah* mnemonic. However, the *Gemara* now introduces a dissenting opinion. Reish Lakish holds that *chatas she-avar shnaso ro'in osah ke-illu hi omedes be-veis ha-kevaros* — a *chatas* that has exceeded a year of age is treated as if it were standing in a graveyard that a *Kohen* cannot enter. There is nothing inherently wrong with the animal; it is simply inaccessible. Therefore, rather than being left to die, it grazes until it develops a *mum* and its value is transferred. Reish Lakish thus removes *avar shnaso* from the category of *chatas* that dies and places it in the category of *chatas* that grazes.

### Pesach Avar Shnaso

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים, דְּתַנְיָא: ״כֶּשֶׂב״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַפֶּסַח לְאַלְיָה, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם כֶּשֶׂב״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַפֶּסַח שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתוֹ וּשְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח לְכׇל מִצְוַת שְׁלָמִים, שֶׁטְּעוּנִים סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** **However, a Paschal lamb in a case like this is sacrificed as a peace-offering, as it was taught in a *baraita*: The word "lamb" comes to include the Paschal lamb in the requirement that the fat tail be sacrificed on the altar. When it says: "If he brings a lamb," it comes to include a lamb consecrated as a Paschal lamb whose first year has passed and peace-offerings that come due to a Paschal lamb. These are considered peace-offerings rather than Paschal lambs, and they are included in all the laws of peace-offerings in that they require leaning and libations and the waving of the breast and thigh.**

The problem for Shmuel's principle is that by *pesach*, the *avar shnaso* scenario yields the opposite result from what Reish Lakish says it yields by *chatas*. The *braitta* already studied — the one derived from *im kesev* — explicitly states that *pesach she-avra shnaso* is brought as a *shelamim*. The *braitta* lists it among the cases converted into a full *shelamim*, with *smicha*, *nesachim*, and *tenufas chazeh ve-shok*. So when the *pesach* exceeds a year, it is brought as a *shelamim*.

### Principle Fails

The collision is direct. According to Reish Lakish, a *chatas* that exceeds a year grazes. According to the *braitta*, a *pesach* that exceeds a year is brought as a *shelamim*. These are different dispositions in the same scenario. Shmuel's principle — that the *pesach* always follows the *chatas* — fails in this case. Rav Yosef's challenge stands.

## Avud Only

### Limited Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, בַּאֲבוּדִין. בִּדְחוּיִין לָא אָמַר. וְאָבוּד מִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וַהֲרֵי אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה, לְרַבָּנַן, דְּלִרְעִיָּה אָזְלָא. דִּתְנַן: הִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת — אַחַת מֵהֶן תִּקְרַב וּשְׁנִיָּה תָּמוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַטָּאת מֵתָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים. הָא קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים — רוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: When Shmuel stated his principle, it was specifically with regard to Paschal lambs that were lost; he did not state his principle with regard to Paschal lambs that were deferred because they had become unfit for use. The Gemara continues its line of questioning: With regard to lost sacrifices do you find Shmuel's principle to be correct? But what about the case of a sin-offering that was already lost at the time of the separation of a replacement to take its place, and the original animal was found before the second was sacrificed? According to the Rabbis, this animal goes for grazing, as we learned in a mishna: If one separated his sin-offering and it was lost, and he separated another in its place and the first was found, and therefore both are available, then one of them, whichever he chooses, is sacrificed, as he may bring only one offering, and the second shall be caused to die; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. And the Rabbis say: A sin-offering is caused to die only when it is found after the owners gained atonement through another offering. Therefore, according to the Rabbis, if the animal was found before the owners gained atonement, it grazes.**

The *Gemara* answers: *ki kamar Shmuel be-avudim, bi-dchuyim lo amar* — Shmuel was speaking only about cases of *avud*, of a lost animal. He was not making a claim about every possible problem that could befall a *pesach* or *chatas*. The cases of *avar shnaso* and *mesu be-aleha* are outside the scope of his principle. His principle applies only to the scenario where the *pesach* was lost, a replacement was designated, and then the original was found — the scenario the *mishnah* is addressing throughout.

### Rabbi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח, הֵיכָא דְּאָבַד וְנִמְצָא אַחַר חֲצוֹת קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים. שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר אֲבוּדָה לְמִיתָה אָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And yet with regard to the Paschal lamb, in a situation where it is found after midday before the slaughter, it is sacrificed as a peace-offering. Consequently, Shmuel's principle is not correct even with regard to offerings that were lost. The Gemara answers: Shmuel holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who said that a lost sin-offering goes to its death.**

Once the scope is narrowed to *avud* cases, the *Gemara* examines a *braitta* that sets out the law of a lost *chatas*. The *braitta* states: *hifrish chattaso ve-avda* — one designated his *chatas* and it was lost. He separated a second *chatas* in its place, and then the original was found; both are now standing before him. Rabbi holds: *achas meihen tikareiv ve-hashniyah tamus* — one is brought and the second is left to die. This is regardless of the timing; Rabbi makes no distinction about when the original was found.

### Chakhamim's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כֹּל אֲבוּדָה לְרַבִּי מֵתָה, וְאִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח הֵיכָא דְּאָבַד קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וְנִמְצָא קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — רוֹעֶה! קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת לָאו אָבוּד הוּא, כִּדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: אֲבֵידַת לַיְלָה לָאו שְׁמָהּ אֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But every lost sin-offering is placed in isolation and left to die according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, whereas with regard to the Paschal lamb, when it was lost before midday and found before midday but after a replacement animal had been separated, it grazes. The Gemara responds: A Paschal lamb lost before midday is not considered lost, in accordance with the opinion of Rava, as Rava said: A sin-offering lost at night and found by the morning is not called lost, and the *halakhot* of lost sin-offerings do not apply because a sin-offering cannot be sacrificed at night in any case. Similarly, if a Paschal lamb is lost before midday on the eve of Passover, since it could not be sacrificed at that time, it does not attain the status of a lost sacrifice even if one separates a replacement. In such a case, even Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would concede that the original animal would be left to graze rather than being left to die.**

The *chakhamim* disagree and introduce a temporal distinction. *Ein chatas mesa ella she-nimtzas le-achar she-nis-kapru ha-baalim* — a *chatas* is left to die only if it was found after the owners had already achieved atonement through the other animal. But — and this is somewhat counterintuitive — if the original was found before the second one was brought, a different rule applies. In that case, the owner had both animals available and chose one, thereby pushing the other aside. This very act of *dechiyah*, paradoxically, helps: because the animal was pushed away rather than simply abandoned, it does not carry the full unimpaired *kedushah* that would demand its death. Instead it grazes. So: found before atonement achieved — *ro'eh*; found after — *meisa*.

## Chatas-Pesach Mapping

### Chatas Scenarios

The *Gemara* maps the *chatas* framework onto the *pesach* question. By *chatas*, according to the *chakhamim*: if the original was found before the new one was brought and was pushed away, it grazes; if found after, it dies.

### Pesach Translation

Translating into *pesach* terms requires one conversion: *meisa* by a *chatas* corresponds to bringing the *pesach* as a *pesach* — meaning in the context of our discussion, the *pesach* may be brought as a *shelamim* without restriction. And *ro'eh* by a *chatas* — the animal grazes until it develops a *mum* — corresponds to the *pesach* being brought only through proceeds, the mode of using the money value rather than the animal itself.

### Perfect Match

The correspondence appears perfect. If the original *pesach* was found before *shechita* — analogous to the *chatas* being found before atonement — it was pushed away, it is *nidcheh*, and only the proceeds may be used. If found after *shechita* — analogous to the *chatas* found after atonement — it was never *nidcheh* and may be brought as a *shelamim*. Shmuel's principle, applied to *avud* cases with the *chakhamim*'s framework, perfectly recapitulates everything the *mishnah* and Rabbi Yochanan have been saying.

## Redundancy Problem

### Says Same Thing

This perfect correspondence is, in fact, the problem. If Shmuel's principle, applied carefully, yields exactly the same ruling as Rabbi Yochanan's straightforward before-and-after-*shechita* rule, then Shmuel is saying nothing new. He has simply restated the same position in a far more elaborate and obscure way. And yet the *Gemara* presents them as disagreeing. This cannot be right.

### Chatzot Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רוֹעָה לְרַבִּי הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כִּדְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת — מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וּשְׁנִיָּה תִּרְעֶה. וְהָא אִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים! אֶלָּא שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מֵתוֹת. וְהָא רוֹעָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ כְּלָל! שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי חֲדָא קָאָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁבַּחַטָּאת מֵתָה — בְּפֶסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים. וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אֵין הַפֶּסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — לָא. אַלְמָא: שְׁחִיטָה קָבַע. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: חֲצוֹת קָבַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if so, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi there is a case in which a sin-offering is left to graze, in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Oshaya, as Rabbi Oshaya said: If one separated two sin-offerings from the outset as a guarantee, so that if one is lost he may gain atonement with the other, he gains atonement with one of them and the second is left to graze.** **The Gemara challenges this: But with regard to a Paschal lamb, in a case like this the second animal would be sacrificed as a peace-offering. This, too, does not follow Shmuel's principle. Rather, it can be explained that Shmuel held in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who said there are five sin-offerings that are left in isolation to die, including all those which are lost or deferred. The Gemara asks: But Rabbi Shimon does not concede in any case at all that a sin-offering is left to graze, as he holds that any sin-offering which is deferred for any reason is left to die, while Shmuel referred to sin-offerings left to graze. The Gemara answers: Shmuel also said only one case. He did not mention sin-offerings that are left to graze; he said only that with regard to any offering that became unfit such that a sin-offering in its condition would be left to die, if it is a Paschal lamb in that condition it is sacrificed as a peace-offering. And what does he teach us with this statement beyond what was taught explicitly in the mishna? The Gemara answers that Shmuel's statement was meant to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said that a Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering only when it is found after slaughter, but if it is found before the slaughter, no, it is not sacrificed as a peace-offering. Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan held that the slaughter determines when a sacrifice is deferred; therefore, Shmuel teaches us that in his opinion midday determines whether it is considered deferred, even if the other animal has not yet been slaughtered, because midday is the time when it may be slaughtered. Consequently, if the Paschal lamb is still lost at midday, it may be offered as a peace-offering when it is found.**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that Shmuel must be holding a different position — one that diverges from Rabbi Yochanan. The divergence, the *Gemara* proposes, lies in the determinative moment. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *shechita* is the key act: it is *shechita* that establishes whether the original was *nidcheh*. Shmuel, by contrast, holds that *chatzot* — noon — is the key moment. The question is not what happened relative to *shechita* but what happened relative to midday.

### Chatzot Disagreement

This produces a genuine disagreement. Under Rabbi Yochanan's view, a *pesach* lost and found before *shechita* — even if found after *chatzot* — is *nidcheh* and only its proceeds may be used. Under Shmuel's view, the relevant question is whether the animal was found before or after *chatzot*. If it was found after *chatzot* but before *shechita*, Shmuel would say it is brought as a *shelamim*; Rabbi Yochanan would say it is *nidcheh* and only the proceeds are used. This is a substantive disagreement, and it justifies the *Gemara*'s presentation of the two as opposing positions.

### Rejected Interpretation

This interpretation is therefore rejected. Shmuel must be saying something that diverges from Rabbi Yochanan. And the only available divergence is the *chatzot* position.

## Rabbi Position

### No Roeh

The *Gemara* arrives at its final resolution. Shmuel holds like Rabbi: *avuda le-misa azla* — any lost *chatas*, once it qualifies as genuinely *avud*, is always left to die. There is no *ro'eh* case for Rabbi in a lost *chatas* scenario.

### Always Shelamim

Applied to *pesach*, this means: whenever a *pesach* qualifies as properly *avud* — lost at a relevant time — it is always brought as a *shelamim*. There is no scenario in the *avud* case where one uses only the proceeds.

### Time, Not Shechita

This means that what defines the *pesach* as *avud* is not whether *shechita* was performed while the animal was available, but whether the animal was lost at a time that is halachically relevant to the *pesach* service. The *shechita* act, for Shmuel, is not determinative. What matters is the temporal status of the *pesach* at the time of loss and recovery.

## Avud Definition

### Night Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֲבוּדָה לְמִיתָה אָזְלָא. וְהָא כֹּל אֲבוּדִין לְרַבִּי מֵתִין, וְאִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח, הֵיכָא דְּאָבַד קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וְנִמְצָא קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — רוֹעֶה! קָסָבַר: קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת לָאו אָבוּד הוּא, וְקָסָבַר חֲצוֹת קָבַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Shmuel's statement must be explained differently: Shmuel holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who said that a lost sin-offering always goes to its death. The Gemara asks: But according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi all lost sin-offerings are left in isolation to die, whereas with regard to the Paschal lamb, in a situation where it is lost before midday and found before midday it is left to graze and is not sacrificed as a peace-offering. The Gemara answers: He held that a Paschal lamb that is lost before midday is not considered lost because the time for slaughtering the Paschal lamb has not yet arrived, and he held that midday determines the status of a lost Paschal lamb, not the time of the actual slaughter.**

The *Gemara* now precisely defines what counts as *avud* for Shmuel's purposes. The analogous question for a *chatas* is whether a loss that occurred at night counts. The *Gemara* invokes a principle of Rava: *aveidas layla la shme aveda* — a loss at night is not called a loss. Why? Because *korbanos* cannot be slaughtered at night. The animal's absence during nighttime hours is completely irrelevant to its halachic function; nighttime is simply outside the operative window of the *korban*. A *chatas* lost at night and found by morning was never truly *avud* for halachic purposes.

### Rava's Principle

This principle — that a loss occurring entirely within an irrelevant time window does not constitute a halachic *avud* — is the structural key to understanding Shmuel's position.

### Pesach Analogy

The same logic applies to *pesach*. Before *chatzot*, the *pesach* cannot be slaughtered. The time before noon is simply outside the operative window of *pesach* service. Therefore, a *pesach* that was lost and found before *chatzot* was never truly *avud*; its absence was irrelevant to its function as a *korban pesach*.

### After Chatzot Rule

It follows that Shmuel's principle applies only to a *pesach* lost and found after *chatzot*. In that case, the loss occurred within the relevant window — the window when the *pesach* could and should have been slaughtered — and therefore the animal is genuinely *avud*. And in that scenario, following Rabbi's position that all *avudim* by a *chatas* lead to death, Shmuel holds that the *pesach* is always brought as a *shelamim*.

---

## Core Dispute

### Chatzot Determines

The *Gemara* now articulates the core of the disagreement. Shmuel's entire elaborate construction — the *chatas* analogy, the adoption of Rabbi's universal *meisa* position, the exclusion of pre-*chatzot* losses as non-*avud* — serves a single purpose: to establish that *chatzot* is the determinative moment. What defines a *pesach* as *nidcheh* or otherwise is whether it was properly *avud* at a relevant time, meaning after *chatzot*. Once that threshold is crossed, the *pesach* is always brought as a *shelamim*.

### Shechita Determines

Rabbi Yochanan's position is the direct contrary. For him, *shechita kava* — *shechita* is what establishes whether a *pesach* is *nidcheh*. The act of slaughtering the replacement, while the original is present and available, is the moment of rejection. Before that act, regardless of the time of day, the *pesach* is not yet definitively *nidcheh*. After that act, if the original was present, it is *nidcheh*.

### Avud Framework

Shmuel's invocation of the *chatas* framework implies that what matters is whether the *pesach* has the status of *avud* at a halachically relevant moment. For *chatas*, relevance is defined by the hours during which *korbanos* may be brought; loss at night is irrelevant. For *pesach*, relevance is defined by the hours during which *pesach* may be slaughtered, which begin at *chatzot*. Shmuel therefore holds that *chatzot*, not *shechita*, draws the critical line.

## Alternative Shechita

### Perfect Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: וְאִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח הֵיכָא דְּאָבַד וְנִמְצָא אַחַר חֲצוֹת, קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה יִקְרַב שְׁלָמִים! שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבָּה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה קָבַע. וְהָא מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן עֲלַהּ: אֵין הַפֶּסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא אַחַר שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה — לָא. אַלְמָא: שְׁחִיטָה קָבַע. מִכְּלָל דִּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר: חֲצוֹת קָבַע.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara presents another version of the discussion, beginning from the proof that the *halakhot* of a sin-offering cannot be equated to those of a Paschal lamb because with regard to a Paschal lamb, in a situation where it is lost and then found after midday but before the slaughter of its replacement, it is offered as a peace-offering, which is not consistent with Shmuel's principle. The Gemara answers: According to this version, Shmuel holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabba, who said that the slaughter of the replacement determines the status of a lost offering; therefore, if the original animal is found before the slaughter of the second animal, even after midday, it is not considered to have been lost. The Gemara asks: But from the fact that Rabbi Yoḥanan said about this *halakha* that a Paschal lamb is sacrificed as a peace-offering only when it was found after the slaughter of the replacement, but if it was found before the slaughter, no, it is not, apparently he held that the slaughter determines whether the offering is considered lost. Since there is a dispute about this point, this proves by inference that Shmuel holds that midday determines this status, so that any animal lost at midday is considered lost and is sacrificed as a peace-offering, even if it is found before the slaughter. That does not accord with this second version of Shmuel's opinion.**

The *Gemara* considers an alternative reading: perhaps Shmuel holds like Rava — *shechita kava* — rather than adopting a *chatzot*-based position. Under this reading, Shmuel would hold Rabbi's position that all *avudim* by *chatas* lead to *meisa*, and would map this onto *pesach* by saying that *shechita* determines what counts as *avud* for *pesach*. The result would be a clean parallel: *chatas* found before *shechita* grazes (under this reading of *chakhamim*, who have a *ro'eh* case), *pesach* found before *shechita* uses proceeds; *chatas* found after *shechita* dies, *pesach* found after *shechita* is brought as *shelamim*.

### Before/After Shechita

This reading would make the *chatas*-*pesach* parallel structurally exact: in both cases, the dividing line is *shechita*, and in both cases the before-*shechita* scenario yields the "lighter" disposition (grazing / proceeds) while the after-*shechita* scenario yields the "heavier" one (death / *shelamim*).

### Redundancy Again

But this interpretation cannot stand, for the same reason the *Gemara* identified earlier. Rabbi Yochanan already says precisely this: *ein pesach kareiv shelamim ella she-nimtza achar shechita*. If Shmuel's principle, resolved through the *chatas* analogy with *shechita* as the determinant, yields the same ruling as Rabbi Yochanan, then Shmuel is saying nothing new. He and Rabbi Yochanan would be in complete agreement, stated in very different idioms, and the *Gemara* would have no basis for presenting them as debating.

### Rejected Interpretation

This interpretation is therefore rejected. Shmuel must be saying something that diverges from Rabbi Yochanan. And the only available divergence is the *chatzot* position.

## Final Resolution

### Rabbi Position Final

The *Gemara* arrives at its final resolution. Shmuel holds like Rabbi: *avuda le-misa azla* — any lost *chatas*, once it qualifies as genuinely *avud*, is always left to die. There is no *ro'eh* case in the *avud* scenario according to Rabbi (and by extension, according to Rabbi Shimon, who likewise holds that all five cases of *chatas* lead to death without exception). Shmuel adopts this maximalist position.

### Before Chatzot Excluded

The apparent counterexample — that by *pesach*, a loss before *chatzot* yields only the proceeds (a "lighter" outcome) — is not actually a counterexample. A *pesach* lost and found before *chatzot* is not considered *avud* at all. Just as Rava teaches that *aveidas layla la shme aveda* — loss at night is not called a loss for a *chatas* — because nighttime is outside the halachically operative window, the same logic governs *pesach*: before *chatzot*, the *pesach* cannot be offered, and a loss within that window is entirely irrelevant to its *korban* status. It does not enter Shmuel's analysis at all.

### Chatzot Determines Status

When a *pesach* is genuinely *avud* — meaning it was lost at a time when it could have been slaughtered, i.e., after *chatzot* — then, following Rabbi's principle applied to *pesach*, it is always brought as a *shelamim*. The act of *shechita* plays no role. Whether the replacement was slaughtered before or after the original was found is irrelevant under Shmuel's framework. All that matters is whether the loss occurred during the operative window of *pesach* service.

### Time-Based Status

The conclusion the *Gemara* extracts is explicit. *Lema'afukei mi-Rabbi Yochanan* — Shmuel's entire principle is designed to show that Rabbi Yochanan is wrong. Rabbi Yochanan holds *shechita kava* — the act of *shechita* is what establishes *nidcheh* status. Shmuel, through his *chatas* analogy, teaches *chatzot kava* — time is what matters. By grounding the analysis in the concept of *avud* and by invoking the parallel to the irrelevance of nighttime loss by a *chatas*, Shmuel argues that the question is always one of temporal status: was the *pesach* lost during a time when it was halachically relevant? If yes, it is *avud* in the full sense and is brought as a *shelamim*. If no — because the loss was before *chatzot*, outside the operative window — it does not even enter the *avud* framework. Time, not the act of *shechita*, is the determinative criterion.
