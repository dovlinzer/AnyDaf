# Shevuot 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Review

### Daf 27 Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִשְׁבַּע לְבַטֵּל אֶת הַמִּצְוָה וְלֹא בִּיטֵּל – פָּטוּר. לְקַיֵּים וְלֹא קִיֵּים – פָּטוּר; שֶׁהָיָה בַּדִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב, כְּדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **takes an oath to refrain from** performing **a mitzva and he does not refrain,** he is **exempt** from bringing an offering for an oath on an utterance. If he takes an oath **to perform** a mitzva **and he does not perform** it, he is also **exempt, though it would have been fitting** to claim **that he is liable** to bring the offering, **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira.**

Daf 27 continues the systematic effort to define the parameters of a binding *shevuah* — specifically, in which cases one is liable to bring a *korban* and in which cases the *shevuah* does not take effect at all.

### Rabbi Akiva & Yishmael

The previous daf established the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael concerning a *shevuah* made about the past: whether one brings a *korban* for such an oath. All authorities agree that a willful violation of a *shevuat shav* incurs *makkot* (lashes), but the *korban* obligation is the contested issue between these two Tannaim.

### Positive & Negative

It was established that a *shevuah* capable of generating *korban* liability must be articulable in both the positive and the negative — the requirement known as *lehara o leheitiv*, that the oath must be expressible as either doing good or doing bad. An oath that cannot be reversed and restated in its opposite form lacks this essential quality.

### Shevuot vs Nedarim

The *mishnah* also addressed distinctions between *shevuot* and *nedarim*, and introduced the category of a *shevuah bishogeg* — an unintentional oath made about the past. One might have assumed this simply means taking an oath while not knowing the facts; in fact, the *gemara* frames the paradigm case as one of *ones* (compulsion) — where the person believed he was telling the truth. Tosafot extends this analysis beyond the *korban* context to *makkot* as well.

### Expression Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיּוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְבַטֵּא בִּשְׂפָתַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** Even after one **decided** to take an oath, he **needs to express** it **with his lips** for it to take effect, **as it is stated** in the verse: "Or if anyone take an oath **clearly with his lips** to do evil, or to do good" (Leviticus 5:4).

Finally, it was established that a *shevuah* is not binding unless it is articulated aloud — *levatay bisifatayim*, expressed through the lips. The *gemara* then raises related cases where silent acceptance does take effect, such as *terumah* and *kodshim*. The practically significant question this raises for contemporary halacha is whether accepting upon oneself a donation — say, to give a sum of money to a synagogue — requires verbal articulation to become binding. The general rule, as the *poskim* understand it, is that one must indeed speak the words aloud. Similarly, accepting a voluntary fast requires verbal expression to be effective.

## Negate/Fulfill Mitzvah

### Oath to Negate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל נִשְׁבַּע לְבַטֵּל אֶת הַמִּצְוָה וְלֹא בִּיטֵּל, יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מָה הֲטָבָה רְשׁוּת, אַף הֲרָעָה רְשׁוּת; אוֹצִיא נִשְׁבַּע לְבַטֵּל אֶת הַמִּצְוָה וְלֹא בִּיטֵּל, שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that when **one takes an oath to refrain** from performing **a mitzva and he does not refrain, he would be liable** to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance. To counter this, **the verse states: "To do evil, or to do good"** (Leviticus 5:4). **Just as doing good** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action, **so too, doing evil** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action. **I will** therefore **exclude** from liability one who **takes an oath to refrain from** performing **a mitzva and does not refrain,** so **that he is exempt** from bringing the offering.

The *mishnah* now on 27a states: *nishba levatel et hamitzvah v'lo biteil* — one who swore to negate a *mitzvah* and did not negate it is *patur*, exempt from bringing a *korban*. This case represents the clearest exclusion from the *korban* regime: a *shevuah* to violate a *mitzvah* cannot take effect at all. No authority holds that one can obligate oneself through oath to transgress a *mitzvah*. One might still incur *makkot* for *shevuat shav*, but the *shevuah* as such has no binding force.

### Oath to Fulfill

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל נִשְׁבַּע לְקַיֵּים אֶת הַמִּצְוָה וְלֹא קִיֵּים, שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מָה הֲרָעָה רְשׁוּת, אַף הֲטָבָה רְשׁוּת; אוֹצִיא נִשְׁבַּע לְקַיֵּים אֶת הַמִּצְוָה וְלֹא קִיֵּים, שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that when **one takes an oath to perform a mitzva and does not perform** it, **that he would be liable.** To counter this, **the verse states: "To do evil, or to do good." Just as doing evil** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action, **so too, doing good** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action. **I will** therefore **exclude** from liability one who **takes an oath to perform a mitzva and does not perform** it, so **that he is exempt** from bringing the offering.

The *mishnah* continues: *lekayem v'lo kiyem* — one who swore to fulfill a *mitzvah* and did not fulfill it is likewise *patur*. Here the *mishnah* itself notes that logic would seem to demand liability — *shehaya din sheyehei chayav* — and indeed this is the position of Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera.

### Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא: מָה אִם הָרְשׁוּת, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּשְׁבָּע עָלָיו מֵהַר סִינַי – הֲרֵי הוּא חַיָּיב עָלָיו; מִצְוָה, שֶׁהוּא מוּשְׁבָּע עָלֶיהָ מֵהַר סִינַי – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ?!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna explains: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira said: What? If,** with regard to an oath concerning **an optional** matter, **for which one is not under oath from Mount Sinai, he is liable for** breaking **it,** then with regard to an oath about **a mitzva, for which he is under oath from Mount Sinai, is it not logical that he would be liable for** breaking **it?**

Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera reasons as follows: if taking an oath in the realm of *reshut* — a discretionary act about which one is not already foresworn from Sinai — renders one liable if he violates it, then how much more so should an oath regarding a *mitzvah*, regarding which one is *already* obligated from Sinai, be binding? One is *mushba v'omed mehar sinai* to perform the *mitzvah*, and the additional personal oath should only reinforce that obligation. Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera therefore holds that such a *shevuah* is binding and its violation generates *korban* liability.

### Lav v'Hein Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁבוּעַת הָרְשׁוּת – שֶׁכֵּן עָשָׂה בָּהּ לָאו כְּהֵן, תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁבוּעַת מִצְוָה – שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהּ לָאו כְּהֵן; שֶׁאִם נִשְׁבַּע לְבַטֵּל וְלֹא בִּיטֵּל – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: No, if you said** that one is liable **for** breaking **an oath** concerning **an optional** action, **where** the Torah **rendered** one liable for **a negative** oath not to perform it **like** for **a positive** oath to perform it, **shall you** also **say** one is liable **with regard to** breaking **an oath** concerning **a mitzva, where** the Torah **did not render** one liable for **a negative** oath **like** for **a positive** oath, **since if one takes an oath to refrain** from performing **a mitzva and did not refrain,** he is **exempt.**

The Rabbis respond: the analogy does not hold. In the realm of *reshut*, an oath satisfies the requirement of *lehara o leheitiv* — it can be framed in the positive and in the negative, *lav v'hein*. The same action can be sworn to and sworn against. But in the realm of *mitzvah*, this symmetry collapses. We already agreed that one cannot take a binding *shevuah* to negate a *mitzvah* — for example, to refrain from sitting in a *sukkah*. Therefore, the oath to sit in the *sukkah* likewise cannot serve as a binding *shevuah*, because it lacks the *lav v'hein* quality: one cannot articulate its inverse as a valid oath. Without that reversibility, it does not generate *korban* liability.

It should be noted that earlier in the *gemara*, the reason given for why a *shevuah* does not take effect on an already-obligatory *mitzvah* was framed differently: *mushba v'omed mehar sinai* — *ein shevuah chala al shevuah*, the principle that one oath does not take effect upon another. Here, the framing is the *lav v'hein* argument. These are not necessarily contradictory; one could read the Rabbis as responding to Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera on his own terms — granting, for the sake of argument, that *mushba v'omed* strengthens rather than blocks the oath, yet still insisting that the absence of *lav v'hein* independently defeats the claim.

### Tosafot's Distinction

Tosafot offers a penetrating alternative reading of the *mishnah*, one that hinges on a textual variant. The key conceptual distinction Tosafot draws is between negative and positive *mitzvot*. The principle *ein issur chal al issur* — that a state of forbiddenness does not take effect upon an existing state of forbiddenness — is structurally parallel to *ein shevuah chala al shevuah*. Both principles address the impossibility of double *issur*, double forbiddenness.

### Tosafot's Girsa

Tosafot removes the *kaf* of comparison from the phrase *kidivrei Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera*, reading the passage not as the anonymous voice of the *mishnah* invoking Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera's position, but as Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera himself conceding a point. In Tosafot's reading, Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera is saying: I agree that taking a *shevuah* to fulfill a *negative mitzvah* — for instance, swearing not to eat *neveilah* — does not take effect. Why? Because *ein issur chal al issur* and *ein shevuah chala al shevuah*: the meat is already in a state of forbiddenness, and a *shevuah* cannot add a second layer of that same forbiddenness. But Rabbi Yehuda Ben Batera holds that taking a *shevuah* to perform a positive *mitzvah* — to sit in a *sukkah* — is entirely different and is indeed binding.

### Double Obligation

The conceptual core of Tosafot's distinction is this: one cannot be doubly *forbidden* — *ein issur chal al issur* — but one can be doubly *obligated*. The Torah obligates sitting in the *sukkah*, and a personal *shevuah* can add a further layer of obligation without any conceptual difficulty. There is no principle of *ein chiyuv chal al chiyuv*. According to Tosafot's reading, it is only in the case of negative *mitzvot* where the *shevuah* fails, because the *shevuah* would be attempting to add a second layer of forbiddenness to something already forbidden. This also illuminates why the Rabbis invoke the *lav v'hein* argument specifically for the case of an oath to sit in the *sukkah*: when dealing with a positive *mitzvah*, *ein issur chal al issur* and *ein shevuah chala al shevuah* do not apply, and the sole remaining reason the *shevuah* fails is the absence of *lav v'hein* — the inability to reverse it.

## Deriving Reshut Fram (II)

### Inclusion & Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל נִשְׁבַּע לְהָרַע לְעַצְמוֹ וְלֹא הָרַע – יָכוֹל יְהֵא פָּטוּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מָה הֲטָבָה רְשׁוּת, אַף הֲרָעָה רְשׁוּת; אָבִיא נִשְׁבַּע לְהָרַע לְעַצְמוֹ וְלֹא הֵרַע, שֶׁהָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that when **one takes an oath to harm himself and he does not harm himself, that he could be exempt** from liability. **The verse states: "To do evil, or to do good." Just as doing good** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action, **so too, doing evil** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action. **I include** as liable **one** who **takes an oath to harm himself and does not harm** himself, **since** it **is his prerogative** to harm himself or not.

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita*: *Tanu Rabbanan*. One might have thought that a *shevuah levatel et hamitzvah* — an oath to negate a *mitzvah* — should be binding, such that if one took such an oath and then did not follow through with the violation, one would be *chayav* a *korban*. The *baraita* rejects this: *talmud lomar lehara o leheitiv* — the verse specifies doing bad or doing good. From this we derive that both the bad and the good must refer to the same category of act.

### Good=Neutral

The *baraita* reasons: *mah hatavah reshut* — the same way doing good refers to a discretionary, neutral act, so too doing bad (*af haraah reshut*) refers to a neutral act, not to an act in the *mitzvah* realm. Negating a *mitzvah* would be "doing bad" in the *mitzvah* realm, not in the neutral realm. Therefore, an oath to negate a *mitzvah* is excluded: *hotzi nishba levatel et hamitzvah v'lo biteil sheyehei patur*.

### Bad=Neutral

The derivation then runs in the opposite direction. One might have thought that an oath to fulfill a *mitzvah* — doing good in the *mitzvah* realm — should also be binding. The *baraita* again invokes *lehara o leheitiv*: the same way doing bad refers to the neutral realm, so doing good (*af hatov reshut*) must also refer to a neutral, discretionary act, not to the fulfillment of a *mitzvah*. Hence, *otzi nishba lekayem et hamitzvah v'lo kiyem shepatur* — one who swears to fulfill a *mitzvah* and fails to do so is likewise exempt.

## Injury & Good Acts

### Self-Injury

The *baraita* continues with a striking principle: one might have thought that an oath to harm oneself would not be binding. The *baraita* rejects this: since harm to oneself falls within the realm of discretionary action — *shehareshut beyado*, one is permitted to do so — such an oath is binding, and if violated, liability follows.

This is a striking passage. Tosafot's brief comment notes that the primary function of including this case is to teach that self-injury is permitted (*adam rashai lachavel be'atzmo*). The *gemara* in Bava Kamma draws upon this *baraita* as evidence in the debate over whether self-injury is halachically permissible. It is important to flag that this *baraita* represents a position contrary to the normative ruling. The *Rambam* and the mainstream *poskim* rule that self-injury is forbidden, and the discussions around procedures such as elective surgery — what constitutes genuine injury versus an act ultimately aimed at benefit — are downstream of that ruling. But the *baraita* here assumes self-injury is permitted, and that assumption is what makes the *shevuah* binding.

### Harm Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל נִשְׁבַּע לְהָרַע לַאֲחֵרִים וְלֹא הֵרַע, שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״ – מָה הֲטָבָה רְשׁוּת, אַף הֲרָעָה רְשׁוּת; אוֹצִיא נִשְׁבַּע לְהָרַע לַאֲחֵרִים וְלֹא הֵרַע, שֶׁאֵין הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת הֲטָבַת אֲחֵרִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא הֲרָעַת אֲחֵרִים? ״אַכֶּה אֶת פְּלוֹנִי וַאֲפַצֵּעַ אֶת מוֹחוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that when **one takes an oath to harm others and does not harm** them, **that he would be liable.** To counter this, **the verse states: "To do evil, or to do good." Just as doing good** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action, **so too, doing evil** is referring to an oath about **an optional** action. **I will** therefore **exclude** from liability **one who takes an oath to harm others and he does not harm** them, **since** it **is not his prerogative** to do so. **From where** is it derived that taking an oath that concerns **doing good to others is included** among the oaths for which one may be liable? **The verse states: "Or to do good." And what is harming others?** An example is when one takes an oath saying: **I will strike so-and-so and injure his brain.**

The *baraita* then addresses: *yachol nishba lehara le'acherim v'lo hara sheyehei chayav* — one might have thought that taking an oath to harm others and then refraining from doing so would generate *korban* liability. The answer is again *lehara o leheitiv*, *mah hatov reshut af hara reshut*: the "bad" referred to in the verse must be in a neutral, discretionary domain, not in the domain of prohibited action. An oath to harm others is not binding because injuring another person is itself a violation of a *mitzvah* — it is *levatel et hamitzvah* — and therefore falls outside the operative range of the verse.

The *baraita* itself makes explicit what form of harm it has in mind: *akeh ploni va'eftza mocho* — I will strike so-and-so and crush his head. This explicit language confirms that the "bad" in question is physical injury, not merely an absence of generosity. This clarification is significant because there are forms of conduct toward others that are unfriendly or ungracious without constituting an *aveirah*. For instance, declining to lend one's lawnmower to a neighbor, or ignoring someone — these could be "bad" in a colloquial sense without violating any prohibition (though some such conduct might run afoul of *lo tikom*, the prohibition of revenge). Those forms of "bad," if one took a *shevuah* regarding them, could potentially be binding. It is specifically the category of physical harm — which constitutes *bitul mitzvah* — that is excluded from the *shevuah* framework.

### Do Good to Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִמַּאי דִּקְרָאֵי בִּדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת כְּתִיבִי? דִּלְמָא בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה כְּתִיבִי!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* assumes throughout that "to do evil, or to do good" is referring to optional actions. The Gemara asks: **But from where** do we know **that** these **verses are written** referring **to optional matters? Perhaps** they **are written** referring **to matters** involving **a mitzva.**

The *baraita* concludes: *minayin lerabot hatavat acherim talmud lomar o leheitiv* — how do we know that a *shevuah* to do good to another person is binding? From the word *o* ("or") in the phrase *lehara o leheitiv*. The inclusion of doing good to others requires a textual source.

One might ask: given that we have already established *lehara o leheitiv* as the operative framework, why should doing good to another person require a separate derivation? The answer lies in the *lav v'hein* principle. Since one cannot take a binding *shevuah* to harm another — the negative of "I will do good to Michael" might be thought to be "I will harm Michael" — one might have concluded that the oath to do good is also not binding, because its reverse falls into forbidden territory.

The *gemara* here implicitly introduces a crucial three-zone model.

### Three Zones

Between the zone of "good" and the zone of "bad" lies a neutral middle ground. The oath "I will do good to Michael" — for example, I will give him *tzedakah*, or I will help him — is reversible without entering the zone of prohibition. The negation of "I will help him" is simply "I will not help him," which is entirely permissible. Not doing a favor is not a *mitzvah* violation. Therefore this oath has a valid *lav v'hein* structure: its positive and negative formulations are both within the permitted range.

By contrast, the oath "I will not harm Michael" is paradoxically *not* binding in this framework — not because the oath itself is problematic, but because its logical opposite, "I will harm him," is forbidden. Since that reverse oath could not itself be binding, the forward oath cannot satisfy the *lav v'hein* requirement. It has the positive formulation, but lacks a valid negative.

The three-zone structure therefore operates as follows: an oath to act in the zone of "good" works, because its reverse is the neutral zone — the mere absence of doing good, which is permitted. An oath to act in the zone of "bad" (toward others, in the sense of physical injury) does not work, because it is itself *bitul mitzvah*. And an oath to stay out of the bad zone — "I will not harm" — also does not work, because its reverse falls into the bad zone, which is impermissible.

## Deriving Reshut Fram (II)

### Circularity Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דְּבָעֵינַן הֲטָבָה דּוּמְיָא דַּהֲרָעָה, וַהֲרָעָה דּוּמְיָא דַּהֲטָבָה; דְּאַקֵּישׁ הֲרָעָה לַהֲטָבָה – מָה הֲטָבָה אֵינָהּ בְּבִיטּוּל מִצְוָה, אַף הֲרָעָה אֵינָהּ בְּבִיטּוּל מִצְוָה. הֲרָעָה גּוּפָהּ – הֲטָבָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: This **should not enter your mind, since we require** that **doing good** be **similar to doing evil, and doing evil** be **similar to doing good, as doing evil is juxtaposed to doing good** in the verse. If one stipulates that the verse is referring to matters involving a mitzva, **then just as doing good does not** involve **refraining from** performing **a mitzva,** but must involve performing a mitzva, e.g., an oath to eat *matza* on Passover, **so too, doing evil does not** involve **refraining from** performing **a mitzva,** e.g., an oath not to eat leavened bread on Passover. The result of this reasoning is that **doing evil** in the verse **is itself doing good,** in that it will always involve taking oaths to keep mitzvot.

The *gemara* now raises what it frankly identifies as a problem of circularity in the *baraita*'s derivation. The *baraita* argued: since *hatavah* (good) is in the *reshut* realm, *haraah* (bad) must also be in the *reshut* realm — and therefore an oath to negate a *mitzvah* is excluded. Then it reversed and said: since *haraah* is in the *reshut* realm, *hatavah* must also be in the *reshut* realm — and therefore an oath to fulfill a *mitzvah* is excluded.

The *gemara* challenges: *umimai dekra d'b'milei d'reshut ketivi* — how do we know the verse is speaking of the *reshut* realm at all? *Dilma bidvar mitzvah ketivi* — perhaps the entire verse is framed in terms of *mitzvah* cases. Perhaps *lehara* means to negate a *mitzvah* and *leheitiv* means to fulfill one. If so, the whole derivation has read its conclusion into its premises.

The *gemara* acknowledges the circularity: *lo salka datach*. It cannot be dismissed, but the reason is not immediately obvious. The *gemara* invokes the principle that *hatavah* must be analogous to *haraah* and *haraah* to *hatavah* — *d'akish haraah l'hatavah*. If we were to situate both in the *mitzvah* realm, then good (*hatavah*) in that realm would be, say, eating *matzah* on *seder* night, and bad (*haraah*) in the *mitzvah* realm — while still being analogous to the good — would have to mean something that is bodily bad but a fulfillment of a *mitzvah*, like refraining from eating *chametz* on Pesach. But in that case, *hara gufa tova hi* — what presents as "bad" is fundamentally good in terms of *mitzvah*. The two categories collapse into one another, and the verse becomes redundant.

Running the argument in the opposite direction produces the same problem: *hatavah gufa ra'a hi*. If one insists on strict analogy between the two terms, whether in the *mitzvah* realm or in the *reshut* realm, one ends up with a redundancy. The *gemara* presses further: *ee hachi bidvar hareshut nami lo meshakchas lah* — if that is the objection, it applies equally in the *reshut* realm. If doing bad must be strictly analogous to doing good, then in the *reshut* realm, "bad" (say, fasting) would simply become another version of "good" (eating, just negated). The circularity cannot be resolved by pointing to the analogy between *hatavah* and *haraah* alone, whether in the *mitzvah* domain or in the *reshut* domain.

### Or Teaches Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בִּדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת נָמֵי לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ! אֶלָּא מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ ״אוֹ״ לְרַבּוֹת הֲטָבַת אֲחֵרִים – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that doing evil and doing good are compared in this manner, **you do not find** that the verse can be interpreted **even with regard to optional matters,** as the same sort of contradiction could be generated. **Rather,** one may derive that the verse is referring to optional matters **from** the fact **that it was necessary** for the verse to write **"or** to do good," in order **to include** liability for oaths that involve **doing good to others. Conclude from it** that these **verses are written** referring **to optional matters.

The *gemara* resolves the circularity with a different argument entirely: *mide'itztarich o l'rabos hatavat acheirim shma mina bidvar hareshut ketivi* — the fact that the word *o* ("or") was needed to include the case of doing good to others proves that the verse is operating in the *reshut* domain, not the *mitzvah* domain.

### Implicit Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה כְּתִיבִי; הַשְׁתָּא הֲרָעַת אֲחֵרִים אִיתְרַבַּי, הֲטָבַת אֲחֵרִים מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As, if it should enter your mind** that the **verses are written** referring **to matters** involving **a mitzva,** there is a difficulty: **Now** that **doing evil to others has been included,** i.e., when one takes an oath to refrain from performing a mitzva, **is** it **necessary** to mention **doing good to others?**

Why does the word *o* prove this? The *gemara* reasons: *de'ee salka dach'tach bidvar mitzvah ketivi* — if the verse were speaking in terms of *mitzvah* cases, then *lehara* would already include taking an oath to harm another person, even though doing so is *levatel et hamitzvah*. That would be the meaning of "bad" in the *mitzvah* realm. Once I knew the verse permitted an oath regarding something as severe as injuring another person, it would be obvious — *mibaya?* — that doing good to another person would also be covered. No separate word would be needed to include it.

But in fact the word *o* is needed to include doing good to others. Why would that inclusion be necessary? Only because we already know — from some other principle — that one cannot take a *shevuah levatel et hamitzvah*, i.e., one cannot take a binding oath to harm another person. Given that the "bad" case is already restricted in this way, one might have thought that the *lav v'hein* requirement blocks the "good" case as well (since the reverse of "doing good" might be thought to be "doing harm," which is forbidden). The word *o* therefore comes to teach that no, an oath to do good to another person is valid, because its reverse — not doing the favor — falls into the neutral zone, not the forbidden zone.

This means the verse presupposes that harming another is already excluded from the *shevuah* framework. That presupposition only makes sense if the verse is operating in the *reshut* domain, where the natural content of oaths is discretionary activity. The *mitzvah* exclusion (*levatel et hamitzvah*) is read as the one case the *Torah* by definition leaves outside the *shevuah* framework.

### Vav vs Or

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״אוֹ״ – מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְחַלֵּק! לְחַלֵּק לָא צְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But this "or" is necessary** in order **to separate** them, i.e., to indicate that one can be liable for either type of oath. Had the verse said: To do evil and to do good, one might assume that one is liable only for oaths that involve both. The Gemara answers: **A verse is unnecessary** in order **to separate,** as it is clear that either sort of oath is included.

The *gemara* then raises a complication: *vehai o mibaya leh lechalek* — perhaps the word *o* is not extra at all. It is needed simply to separate the two cases: the oath to do bad and the oath to do good are each independently binding, and the *o* teaches that one need not take an oath encompassing both simultaneously. Without *o*, one might have read the conjunctive *vav* in *lehara v'leheitiv* as requiring both elements present in the same oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יְקַלֵּל אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ; אָבִיו וְלֹא אִמּוֹ, אִמּוֹ וְלֹא אָבִיו – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ קִלֵּל״ – אָבִיו קִלֵּל, אִמּוֹ קִלֵּל. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מַשְׁמָע שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּאֶחָד וּמַשְׁמָע אֶחָד בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״יַחְדָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to Rabbi Yonatan's** opinion concerning the interpretation of conjunctions, **but according to Rabbi Yoshiya's** opinion, **what can be said?** The Gemara explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: From the verse: **"A man who curses his father and his mother** shall die" (Leviticus 20:9), **I have** derived **only** that one is liable if he curses both **his father and his mother. From where** do I derive that if one curses **his father but not his mother,** or **his mother but not his father,** he is liable? The continuation of **the verse states: "His father and his mother he has cursed;** his blood is upon him." In the first part of the verse, the word "curses" is in proximity to "his father," and in the last part of the verse, "cursed" is in proximity to "his mother." This teaches that the verse is referring to both a case where **he cursed** only **his father** and a case where **he cursed** only **his mother;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.** Rabbi Yoshiya maintains that conjunctions are interpreted strictly unless the verse indicates otherwise. **Rabbi Yonatan says:** There is no need for this derivation, because the phrase "his father and his mother" **indicates** that one is liable if he curses **both of them together, and it** also **indicates** that he is liable if he curses either **one** of them **on their own,** **unless the verse specifies** that one is liable only if he curses both **together.** An example of a verse where the Torah specifies that the *halakha* applies only to the two elements in conjunction is: "You shall not plow with an ox and a donkey together" (Deuteronomy 22:10).

The *gemara* then raises the view of Rabbi Yoshiya: *haniicha le'Rabbi Yochanan... ela le'Rabbi Yoshiya mai ika lemeimar?* Rabbi Yoshiya maintains that a *vav* would normally be read as "and" — requiring both elements together — and one needs an explicit *o* to read it as "or." The *baraita* brought in support reads: *ish asher yekallel et aviv ve'et imo* — a man who curses his father and his mother. Rabbi Yoshiya says: from this verse alone, I know only if he cursed them both together. The word *killel* appearing adjacent to *aviv* alone (at the beginning of the verse) and *killel* appearing adjacent to *imo* alone (at the end) teaches that cursing either one separately is also punishable — but Rabbi Yoshiya required that separate derivation, because for him a *vav* means "and" not "or." Rabbi Yonatan disagrees: a *vav* inherently encompasses both possibilities — together and apart — and the verse is simply confirming both readings. Only an explicit word like *yachdav* ("together"), as in the prohibition against plowing with an ox and a donkey, would restrict the case to the two elements being simultaneous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה; וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּדָרֵישׁ רִבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי, וְאִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ ״אוֹ״ לְחַלֵּק. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת כְּתִיב, מְמַעֵט דְּבַר מִצְוָה; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה כְּתִיב, מִמַּאי קָא מְמַעֵט?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **You** may **even say** that the verse is referring to optional matters according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya.** He **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who interprets** the Torah using the hermeneutical principle of **amplifications and restrictions** (see 26a), **and** the word **"or" is superfluous** and is used **to separate** "to do evil" from "to do good." The Gemara explains how one derives that the oaths referred to in the verse are oaths about optional matters in accordance with Rabbi Yoshiya, who interprets the verse in accordance with the principle of amplifications and restrictions: **Granted, if you say** that the verse about an oath on an utterance **is written with regard to optional matters,** the words "to do evil, or to do good" serve to **restrict** the meaning of the verse and exclude the application of an oath on an utterance to **a matter** involving **a mitzva.** The Gemara asks rhetorically: **But if you say** that the verse **is written** referring **to a matter** involving **a mitzva, what** do the words "to do evil, or to do good" serve to **restrict?**

The *gemara* concludes: even according to Rabbi Yoshiya — for whom the word *o* is not superfluous but structurally necessary — the exclusion of *levatel et hamitzvah* can still be derived. The source would instead be Rabbi Akiva's interpretive principle of *ribui umi'ut*. Rabbi Akiva taught that the phrase *lehara o leheitiv lechol asher yevata* functions as a *ribui* (expansion) followed by a *mi'ut* (exclusion). The expansion includes virtually everything; the exclusion removes one case. What would be the most natural single exclusion? An oath to contravene what the *Torah* itself commands. Under this reading, the *reshut* domain of the verse is established not from the word *o* but from Rabbi Akiva's interpretive framework, which identifies *levatel et hamitzvah* as the one case excluded. Once that exclusion is established, the conclusion that the verse operates in the *reshut* realm follows: *amar bishlama dvar hareshut ketiv, mimela dvar mitzvah* is excluded. If the verse covered the full *mitzvah* range — taking oaths about both fulfillment and negation of *mitzvot* — then everything in between would also be covered, and the *mi'ut* would exclude nothing meaningful.

## Multiple Oaths

### Three Formulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״; וַאֲכָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. זוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת בִּטּוּי – שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד. שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא – חַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: On my **oath I will not eat this loaf,** and he then says again: On my **oath I will not eat it,** and again: On my **oath I will not eat it, and he** then **ate it, he is liable only once.** Once the first oath had taken effect, the subsequent oaths could not, as a prohibition cannot take effect where another prohibition is already in place. **This is an oath on an utterance, for which** one is **liable** to receive **lashes for intentionally** breaking **it, and for unwittingly** breaking **it** one is liable to bring **a sliding-scale offering. For** an **oath** taken **in vain,** one is **liable** to receive **lashes when** it is taken **intentionally, and** one is **exempt when** it is taken **unwittingly.**

The *mishnah* presents the following scenario: a person takes a *shevuah* that he will not eat a certain loaf of bread, then takes another *shevuah* that he will not eat it, and a third that he will not eat it. He then eats it. He is liable for only one *korban*. This is the principle of *ein shevuah chala al shevuah*: a *shevuah* does not take effect upon an already-existing *shevuah* regarding the same object. The *mishnah* then identifies this as the paradigmatic case of *shevuat bitui* — the category of future-directed oath for which willful violation incurs *makkot* and unintentional violation incurs a *korban oleh v'yored* (of variable cost depending on one's means). By contrast, a *shevuat shav* — an oath for naught, such as swearing to something patently obvious or patently false — incurs *makkot* for willful transgression but no *korban* whatsoever.

### Ein Shevua Chala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵי ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ וַהֲדַר אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״ – דְּלָא מִיחַיַּיב אֶלָּא חֲדָא; אֲבָל אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״ וַהֲדַר אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ – מִיחַיַּיב תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **to teach** the mishna such that the wording of the first oath is: On my **oath I will not eat** this loaf, and then the wording of the second oath is: On my **oath I will not eat it?** The Gemara answers: **This teaches us** that **the reason that he is liable only once** is **that he said: I will not eat** this loaf, **and then said: I will not eat it. But** if he had **said: I will not eat it, and then had said: I will not eat** this loaf, **he would be liable twice.**

The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishnah* bother stating the oath in multiple formulations? Stating it once with different phrasing would suffice to establish the *korban* obligation. The *gemara* answers that the different formulations — *shelo ochel* and *shelo ochelnah* — are not identical in scope, and the *mishnah* teaches something from their juxtaposition and ordering.

### Rava's Wording Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָכַל מִמֶּנָּה כְּזַיִת – חַיָּיב. ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל אֶת כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava, as Rava says:** If one says: On my **oath I will not eat this loaf, once he has eaten an olive-bulk of it** he is **liable,** as this oath is understood to mean that it is prohibited for him to eat any of the loaf. If he says: On my oath **I will not eat it, he is not liable unless he eats the whole** loaf. The words: I will not eat it, indicate that his oath applies only to eating the entire loaf. Accordingly, when the first oath is: I will not eat it, and the second oath is: I will not eat this loaf, the second oath can take effect, as it generates a new prohibition that applies to each and every olive-bulk of the loaf.

Rava offers a crucial distinction: *shelo ochel kikar zo* — "I will not eat this loaf" — means I will not eat *from* it, and therefore even eating a *k'zayis* (an olive's volume) constitutes a transgression. *Shelo ochalnah* — "I will not eat it" — implies I will not eat *the entirety* of it, and therefore one is not liable until he has eaten the whole loaf.

### Order Matters

Given this distinction, order becomes determinative. If one first swore *shelo ochalnah* (not to eat the whole loaf) and then swore *shelo ochel* (not to eat any portion of it), the second oath takes effect because it is more stringent than the first: it creates a liability even for a *k'zayis*, something the first oath did not forbid. If, however, one first swore the stricter oath (*shelo ochel*, forbidding even a *k'zayis*) and then the broader formulation (*shelo ochalnah*, the entire loaf), the second oath is entirely subsumed by the first and does not take effect at all. The *mishnah* is constructed precisely to convey this: the order in which the formulations appear reflects which one was stated first.

### Hovering Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״, וַאֲכָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת כּוּ׳. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: חִיּוּבָא הוּא דְּלֵיכָּא – הָא שְׁבוּעָה אִיכָּא; דְּאִי מַשְׁכַּחַת רַוְוחָא – חָיְילָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one says: On my oath I will not eat this loaf, and he then says again: On my oath I will not eat it, and again: On my **oath I will not eat it, and he** then **ate it, he is liable only once.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to mention **this additional,** third, oath: I will not eat it? The Gemara answers: **This teaches us** that **there is no liability** where one states redundant oaths, **but there is an oath;** the final oath is not completely discounted, so **that if you find room,** i.e., an application, for the additional oath, **it goes into effect.**

Rava adds a further principle: even when the second oath does not take effect — because the first oath already covers the same ground — the second oath does not simply cease to exist. It remains present, hovering, unable to actualize only because of the blocking effect of the first. This has a practical consequence: if one seeks and obtains a *hetter* (annulment) from a *chacham* on the first oath, the *hetter* operates retroactively — the first oath is treated as though it never took effect. Looking back from that point, the second oath was never blocked, and it therefore takes retroactive effect in the first oath's place.

### Tosafot's Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לְכִדְרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁאִם נִשְׁאַל עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה – עָלְתָה לוֹ שְׁנִיָּה תַּחְתֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **For what** matter is this ***halakha*** relevant? It is relevant **for** the statement **of Rava, as Rava says that if one requested of** a halakhic authority **to** dissolve **the first oath,** and he did so, **the second one counts for him in its place** unless it, too, was dissolved.

Tosafot's version of the *mishnah* contains only two oath formulations, not three. Accordingly, Tosafot must derive the "hovering" principle from somewhere other than the extra third scenario. Tosafot finds it in the precise wording of the *mishnah* itself: *eino chayav ela achas* — "he is not liable except for one." Tosafot notes that the *mishnah* could have said simply *chayav achas* — "he is liable for one." The formulation *eino chayav ela achas* — "he is not liable except for one" — acknowledges that there were two oaths, both present, yet only one generates a *korban*. The phrasing *eino chayav ela achas* implies that both are acknowledged to exist; the second merely does not generate liability. *Chayav achas* would suggest the second is altogether ignored. The more elaborate phrasing therefore carries the implication that the second oath continues to hover — present and recognized, but inactive — which is precisely what enables it to take retroactive effect if the first is annulled.

### Nazir Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ: מִי שֶׁנָּדַר שְׁתֵּי נְזִירוֹת, וּמָנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְהִפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִשְׁאַל עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה – עָלְתָה לוֹ שְׁנִיָּה בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports his** opinion: With regard to **one who took two vows of naziriteship, and counted the first** term of naziriteship and **separated an offering for it, and afterward requested** and received dissolution **of the first** vow from a halakhic authority, **the second** term **was counted for him in** the observance of **the first** term, and he is not required to be a nazirite further. This indicates that the second vow went into effect retroactively once the first was dissolved, and the same would be true in the case of two oaths.

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof for Rava's hovering principle from the laws of *nezirut*. If someone accepted two separate terms of *nezirut* upon himself, completed the thirty days of the first, and separated a *korban* to conclude it — and then, before bringing the *korban*, obtained a *she'eilah* (halachic annulment) on the first *nezirut* — the ruling is: *alsa lo shniya barishona* — the second *nezirut* is treated as having filled the first slot. The thirty days he served count toward the second *nezirut*, and the *korban* he designated for the first is counted toward the second. He has now completed the second term as well.

This seems to support Rava: the second *nezirut* was always present and hovering, such that once the first was retroactively removed, the second stepped into its place.

### Challenge to Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם נְזִירוּת מִיהָא אִיתַאּ, דְּכִי מָנֵי לְרִאשׁוֹנָה, בָּעֵי מִיהְדָּר מִימְנָא לִשְׁנִיָּה בְּלָא שְׁאֵלָה. הָכָא, שְׁבוּעָה שְׁנִיָּה מִי אִיתַאּ כְּלָל?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** the second **naziriteship is there in any event, as once he counts the first** term, **he needs to return** and **count the second** if there is **no request** for dissolution. **Here,** if he does not request dissolution of the first oath, **is there a second oath at all?** Therefore one cannot find support for Rava's opinion from the *baraita*.

The *gemara* rejects the analogy: *hachi hashta? Hosam neziros mi isa* — in the *nazir* case, everybody agrees the second *nezirut* was always present and active, not merely hovering. How do we know this? Because even without a *she'eilah*, the practical consequence of the second *nezirut* was always clear: after completing the first term, the individual would simply proceed to observe the second. *Belo sheilah*, if no annulment had been sought, the second *nezirut* would have taken effect automatically upon completion of the first. Its continuous presence was never in doubt.

*Hacha shevuah shniya mi isa klal?* — here, by contrast, had the first *shevuah* not been annulled, the second would have had no practical effect whatsoever. There would be no moment at which the second oath visibly came into force. Because the second *shevuah* had no independent expression or consequence absent the annulment of the first, one cannot straightforwardly say it was always "present." The *nazir* proof therefore fails to establish Rava's principle, and the *gemara* leaves it as Rava's own assertion without an external proof.
