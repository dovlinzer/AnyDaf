# Pesachim 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mesorah vs. Interpretation

### Hillel/Bnei Beteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל הִלֵּל: מִיָּמָיו לֹא מָעַל אָדָם בְּעוֹלָתוֹ. אֶלָּא מְבִיאָהּ חוּלִּין לָעֲזָרָה וּמַקְדִּישָׁהּ, וְסוֹמֵךְ יָדוֹ עָלֶיהָ וְשׁוֹחֲטָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** They said about Hillel that no one ever misused his burnt-offering. Rather he would bring it unconsecrated to the Temple courtyard and consecrate it, and place his hand on its head and slaughter it.

We begin with a recapitulation of a theme that dominated the preceding daf, because it is too critical to pass over without emphasis. Much of the previous session was devoted to the *drasha* establishing that the *korban pesach* overrides *Shabbat*, introduced through the famous narrative of the *Bnei Beteira*. The story is set on an *erev Pesach* that fell on *Shabbat* — an occasion that, logically, should recur once every seven years, though the calendar may have been manipulated so that it had not been seen in some time. The *Bnei Beteira* did not know the *halacha*, and someone suggested summoning Hillel, who had studied under Shemaya ve-Abtalion and had come from Bavel. According to the *Bavli*, Hillel presented a series of *drashot* — *kal va-chomer*, *gzeirah shava*, and others — and conclusively demonstrated that *Pesach* overrides *Shabbat*, after which he spent the remainder of the day teaching *halacha*.

### Bavel vs. E.Y.

The *Yerushalmi*'s version of this story is, in many key respects, quite different, and the contrast is illuminating. The *Yerushalmi* opens with skepticism about whether Hillel, as someone from Bavel, would be of any use at all. When Hillel proceeds to derive the *halacha* through hermeneutic principles — *gzeirah shava*, *hekesh*, *kal va-chomer* — the *Bnei Beteira* counter every one of his arguments. They have a response to each derivation. Hillel does not spend the rest of the day in triumph; he spends it struggling to prove his point. Only when he gives up on argumentation entirely and declares, "This is the *mesorah* I received from Shemaya ve-Abtalion," do they accept.

The Netziv, in his extended introduction to the *She'iltos*, highlights precisely this contrast. He argues that the Torah of *Eretz Yisrael* was closer to the source and therefore primarily about transmission — *mesorah* to *mesorah*. The Torah of Bavel, more distant from that source, necessarily relied more heavily on creative intellectual engagement with the *pesukim*, on the hermeneutic process of interpretation and derivation. The clash with the *Bnei Beteira* was not merely personal or regional — it was a fundamental disagreement about the nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh* itself: is it a matter of what you received and pass on, or is it a matter of how you interpret and derive?

### Public Intuition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: הֲלָכָה זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְשָׁכַחְתִּי, אֶלָּא הַנִּיחוּ לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אִם אֵין נְבִיאִים הֵן — בְּנֵי נְבִיאִים הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** He said to them: I heard this *halakha* but I have forgotten it. But leave it to the Jewish people and rely on them to come up with a solution on their own, for if they are not prophets, they are the sons of prophets.

The end of the story reinforces this theme. When Hillel is asked about someone who forgot to bring a sheep or a knife on *erev Pesach*, and it is now *Shabbat*, he admits he does not remember the *halacha*. But rather than conceding defeat, he says: trust *Bnei Yisrael* — *inam nevi'im hem, bnei nevi'im hem* — if they are not themselves prophets, they are sons of prophets; their intuition can be trusted. The next day, people stuck their knives in the wool of their sheep and let the animals carry them to *Har ha-Bayit*. Hillel then recalled that this was indeed what Shemaya ve-Abtalion had told him, though he had forgotten.

The *Bavli* reads this conclusion as Hillel receiving a measure of comeuppance — after making himself so important, he had to admit he forgot something. But there is another dimension worth appreciating. The *Yerushalmi*'s account has the *Bnei Beteira* accept Hillel only when he invokes *mesorah*. The very next moment demonstrates that *mesorah* is fragile — it can be forgotten. If Hillel had not been present, no one would have remembered. What restores the *mesorah* is not another chain of transmission but the intuition of the people. Hillel, in this reading, does get the final word: he shows that trusting the intuition of *Bnei Yisrael*, and the ability to engage the *pesukim* creatively, is itself another legitimate path to accessing and reconstructing the *mesorah*. The process is not mechanical — there is an irreducible role for judgment, for intuition, for how one reads the *pesukim* in the first place.

### Rambam Model

One might ask whether this is a matter of "creating" *Torah she-ba'al peh* — and that framing requires nuance. The Rambam, in defending *Torah she-ba'al peh* against the Karaites, was confronted with the following problem: if the entire Oral Torah is simply a chain of transmission, then the existence of so many debates reveals a broken pipeline, undermining the claim to reliable tradition. The Rambam's response is that *mesorah* in the strict sense — *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — is actually a small portion of the whole. The overwhelming majority of *Torah she-ba'al peh* is not pure transmission; it is the empowerment given to *Chazal* to operate within hermeneutic principles, to interpret and derive within certain parameters. The process is one of finding the best understanding of what the text actually means — revelation through interpretation, not invention. Different *poskim* arriving at different conclusions through that process are not one right and others wrong; all are legitimate expressions of *Torah she-ba'al peh*. And that, ultimately, is exactly what the clash between Hillel and the *Bnei Beteira* is about.

## Three Impurity Categories

### Pesach/Tumah Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן תָּמִיד וּפֶסַח דְּדָחוּ שַׁבָּת, דְּדָחוּ טוּמְאָה מְנָא לַן? אָמְרִי: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּיָלֵיף פֶּסַח מִתָּמִיד לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, הָכִי נָמֵי יָלֵיף תָּמִיד מִפֶּסַח לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** We have found proofs that the daily offering and the Paschal lamb override Shabbat. From where do we derive that they also override ritual impurity? They say: Just as the law governing the Paschal lamb is derived from the law governing the daily offering in regard to the overriding of Shabbat, so too the law concerning the daily offering is derived from the law concerning the Paschal lamb in regard to ritual impurity.

We now pick up from the final lines of 66b. The *Gemara* states: *ashkhichan tamid u-pesach de-dachu Shabbat* — we know that the *tamid* and the *Pesach* override *Shabbat*. But *de-dachu tumah minalan* — where do we know that they override *tumah*? The *Gemara* answers by analogy: just as we derived that *Pesach* overrides *Shabbat* by learning from the *tamid*, we reverse the direction and derive that the *tamid* overrides *tumah* by learning from *Pesach*. But this presupposes we already know that *Pesach* itself overrides *tumah* — and for that we need a source.

### Ish Ish Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפֶסַח גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי יִהְיֶה טָמֵא לָנֶפֶשׁ״. אִישׁ נִדְחֶה לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, וְאֵין צִיבּוּר נִידְחִין לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, אֶלָּא עָבְדִי בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** And with regard to the Paschal lamb itself, from where do we derive that if most of the nation is ritually impure, the sacrifice is offered anyway? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: For the verse states: "Any man of you or your generations who shall be impure by reason of a corpse, or on a distant journey, he shall keep the Passover to the Lord. On the fourteenth day of the second month at evening they shall keep it, and eat it with *matzot* and bitter herbs." We can infer from here that an individual or a group of individuals are deferred to the second *Pesaḥ* if they are ritually impure, but the entire community or the majority thereof is not deferred to the second *Pesaḥ*; rather, they observe the first *Pesaḥ* in a state of ritual impurity.

Rabbi Yochanan provides the source: the *pasuk* states *ish ish ki yihyeh tameh l'nefesh* — if any person is *tamei* through corpse-*tumah* and cannot bring the *korban Pesach*, he may bring it on *Pesach Sheni*. From this, Rabbi Yochanan derives: *ish nidcha l'Pesach Sheni v'ein tzibur nidchin l'Pesach Sheni* — an individual is pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*, but the *tzibur* is not pushed off. If the entire community is *tamei meit*, they bring the *Pesach* even in a state of *tumah* — *tumah hutrah b'tzibur*.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵימָא, אִישׁ נִדְחֶה לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, צִיבּוּר לֵית לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא לָא בְּפֶסַח רִאשׁוֹן וְלָא בְּפֶסַח שֵׁנִי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: This verse cannot serve as proof, for you can say that it is to be understood as follows: An individual or a group of individuals is deferred to the second *Pesaḥ*, but the community has no remedy, neither on the first *Pesaḥ* nor on the second *Pesaḥ*.

Reish Lakish challenges this reading. He argues: perhaps you should read the *pasuk* *l'chumra* — an individual gets pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*, and the *tzibur* gets no accommodation at all, neither in *Pesach Rishon* nor in *Pesach Sheni*. On that reading, the *pasuk* about *ish ish* cannot be the basis for the *halacha* of *pesach habah b'tumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, מֵהָכָא: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה כׇּל צָרוּעַ וְכׇל זָב וְכֹל טָמֵא לָנָפֶשׁ״, יֵאָמֵר טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְאַל יֵאָמֵר זָבִין וּמְצוֹרָעִין, וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אִם טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים, מִשְׁתַּלְחִין, זָבִין וּמְצוֹרָעִין — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said that a different proof may be brought from here: "Command the children of Israel that they send out from the camp any leper and any *zav* and anyone impure by reason of a corpse." Let the verse say only that they are to send out those who are ritually impure due to a corpse, and not say anything about *zavin* and lepers, and I would say this law on my own through an *a fortiori* inference: If those ritually impure due to a corpse, whose impurity is not so severe as it is contracted from an external source, are sent out from the camp, with regard to *zavin* and lepers who are the source of their own impurity, all the more so is it not clear that they should be sent out?

Reish Lakish therefore proposes a different *pasuk* as the source: *v'yishalchu min hamachaneh kol tzarua v'chol zav v'chol tamei lanefesh* — send out of the camp everyone with *tzara'at*, everyone with a *zav* flow, and everyone who is *tamei* for a corpse. This *pasuk* identifies three categories. Now, the *Gemara* notes, the *pasuk* could have said only *tamei meit* — the most lenient category — and derived the others by *kal va-chomer*: if the lightest category is sent out, certainly the heavier ones are. The fact that the *pasuk* names all three separately implies there is something to learn from each. According to Reish Lakish, there are times when some categories are not sent out even though others are — and that time is *pesach habah b'tumah*, when *tamei meit* are permitted to remain inside while *zav* and *metzora* are not.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יֵשׁ לְךָ שָׁעָה שֶׁזָּבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין וְאֵין טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין, וְאֵיזֶה זֶה — פֶּסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, it teaches you that you have a time when *zavin* and lepers are sent out from the camp, but those who are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse are not sent out. And what is this time? When a Paschal lamb is brought in impurity, when those impure due to contact with a corpse are permitted to participate, but a *zav* and a leper may not.

Abaye objects to Reish Lakish's derivation. If the logic is that the *pasuk* divides categories to teach that sometimes some groups stay while others leave, then one might equally read the *pasuk* as teaching that *metzora* gets sent out while *zav* and *tamei meit* stay — or any other combination. There is no internal basis in Reish Lakish's reading for specifying precisely which groups gain access during *pesach habah b'tumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתְנַן: פֶּסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ זָבִים וְזָבוֹת נִדּוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת, וְאִם אָכְלוּ — פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** And if you say that it is indeed so that even a *zav* may participate when the Paschal lamb is brought in a state of impurity, there is a difficulty, for didn't we learn in a mishna: When a Paschal lamb is brought in a state of ritual impurity, *zavim* and *zavot*, menstruating women and women after childbirth, whose impurity is comparable to that of a *zav*, may not eat from it; but if they ate, they are exempt from *karet*.

More decisively, we already have a *mishna* that settles the question empirically: *pesach habah b'tumah lo yochlu mimenu zavim v'zavot niddot v'yoldot* — when the *Pesach* is brought in *tumah*, those with *zav*-type *tumah*, women who are *niddot*, and women who are *yoldot* may not eat from it. The license to eat extends only to those who are *tamei meit*. If Reish Lakish's reading implied that *zavim* might also receive license — since the *pasuk* could just as easily be read to allow them as well — and we know from the *mishna* that they do not, then Reish Lakish's derivation cannot be sustained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם מִקְּרָא קַמָּא. אִם כֵּן, נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי יִהְיֶה טָמֵא״, ״לָנֶפֶשׁ״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Abaye said: Actually, the law can be derived from the first verse quoted by Rabbi Yoḥanan: "Any man who shall be impure by reason of a corpse." And the derivation should be understood as follows: If so, that the verse comes to teach that only an individual can rectify his situation on the second *Pesaḥ*, but not the community, the Merciful One should have written: "Any man who shall be impure." Why do I need the words "by reason of a corpse"?

Therefore, Abaye concludes, we return to the original *pasuk* of Rabbi Yochanan: *ish ish*. The remaining difficulty with that *pasuk* — that the *tzibur* might simply receive no accommodation at all — is resolved as follows. Had the *Torah* intended only to address the individual case, it would have written simply *ish ish ki yihyeh tamei*, with no qualification. The specification *tamei lanefesh* — *tumah* of a corpse — is doing additional work. One might have thought to read it *l'chumra*: only if you are *tamei meit* do you get a *Pesach Sheni*; if you are *tamei* for other reasons such as *zav* or *metzora*, you do not. But that is contradicted by a *braita*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ עוֹשִׂים פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי אֶלָּא טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְשֶׁהָיָה בְּדֶרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה, זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִים וּבוֹעֲלֵי נִדּוֹת מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״, ״לָנֶפֶשׁ״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** For wasn't it taught otherwise in the following *baraita*: One might have thought that only those ritually impure due to contact with a corpse and those on a distant journey observe the second *Pesaḥ*. From where do we derive that even *zavin*, lepers and those who had relations with menstruating women may participate in the second *Pesaḥ*? Therefore, the verse states: "Any man," to include even people with these types of impurity. If so, why do I need the words "by reason of a corpse" that the Merciful One writes?

*yachol lo yi'u osim Pesach Sheni ella tmei meitim* — could it be that only *tamei meit* bring *Pesach Sheni*? *Zavin u'metzora'im u'vo'alei niddot minayin?* How do we know *zavim*, *metzoraim*, and men who have had relations with a *niddah* are also included? *Talmud lomar: ish ish* — the inclusive phrasing *ish ish* broadens the category to include anyone who is *tamei* for any reason.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִישׁ נִדְחֶה לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, וְאֵין צִיבּוּר נִדְחֶה לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, אֶלָּא עָבְדִי בְּטוּמְאָה. וְכִי עָבְדִי צִיבּוּר בְּטוּמְאָה — בִּטְמֵא מֵת, אֲבָל שְׁאָר טוּמְאוֹת — לָא עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, this is what the verse is saying: An individual or a group of individuals are deferred to the second *Pesaḥ*, but the entire community or the majority thereof is not deferred to the second *Pesaḥ*; rather, they observe the first *Pesaḥ* in a state of ritual impurity. And when we say that the community observes it in a state of impurity, that is only when they are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse, as indicated by the expression "by reason of a corpse," but when they are impure with other types of impurity, they do not observe it in a state of impurity, even if the majority of the community is impure.

Given this *braita*, the word *lanefesh* — corpse — cannot be functioning to limit the individual case. It must be functioning for the *tzibur* implication. The *Torah* says *tamei lanefesh* to teach: when an individual is *tamei meit*, he goes to *Pesach Sheni*; when the *tzibur* is *tamei meit*, they bring the *Pesach* now. And critically, the *heter* for the *tzibur* applies specifically to *tumat meit* — the lightest category — and not to *zav* or *metzora*.

### Pesach B'Tumah Limits

The practical scope of *pesach habah b'tumah* now emerges with precision. When the *Pesach* is brought in *tumah*, the *tzibur* that receives license is specifically *tamei meit* — those whose *tumah* was contracted from the outside, not generated from within their own bodies. The *Gemara* will elaborate at length on why *tumat meit* is categorically distinct from *zav* and *metzora* in this regard.

The *mishna* cited by Abaye had stated that *zavim*, *zavot*, *niddot*, and *yoldot* may not eat from the *pesach habah b'tumah*, but if they did eat, they are *pturim* — exempt from *karet*. Normally, a *tamei* person who eats from a *korban* is liable for *karet*; during a period of *pesach habah b'tumah*, even those who had no license to eat are not subject to that penalty *bedi'avad*. The same principle will emerge with respect to entering the *Mikdash* — *zavim* and *metzoraim* have no license to enter, but if they did during such a period, they are exempt from the usual liability.

It is worth noting, parenthetically, that the list appearing in the *braita* — *zavin*, *metzora'im*, and *vo'alei niddot* — is a list exclusively of men, whereas the *mishna*'s list included *zavot*, *niddot*, and *yoldot* as well. This is because the *braita* is addressing the *chiyuv* to bring *Pesach* and thus *Pesach Sheni*, and it is presumably assumed that the obligation falls upon men. Whether women are obligated in *korban Pesach* is a question the *Gemara* will address elsewhere.

What emerges from the full discussion is: the source for *pesach habah b'tumah* is the *pasuk* of *ish ish*, which teaches that an individual is pushed off while the *tzibur* is not. The license for the *tzibur* is limited to *tamei meit*, and neither *zav* nor *metzora* may eat or enter — though if they do, they bear no liability during that period.

## Three-Camp Division

### Camp Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מְצוֹרָע שֶׁנִּכְנַס לְפָנִים מִמְּחִיצָתוֹ פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּדָד יֵשֵׁב מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה מוֹשָׁבוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said: A leper, who must be sent out from all of the camps including the Israelite camp, who went in beyond his boundary, that is, he entered an area that is prohibited to him, is nonetheless exempt from the punishment of lashes. As it is stated: "All the days that the plague shall be in him he shall be impure; he is impure, he shall dwell in isolation, his dwelling shall be outside the camp."

Rav Chisda now introduces a ruling that occasions a broad discussion of the three-camp division and the respective exclusion zones that apply to each category of *tumah*. He states: *metzora shenichnas lifnim mimchitzato* — a *metzora* who enters the zone beyond his permitted boundary — *patur* — is exempt from lashes.

To understand this, it is necessary to lay out the tripartite spatial structure. In the time of the *Midbar*, there were three concentric zones. At the center was the *Mishkan* itself, the dwelling of the *Shechinah*. Surrounding it was the *Machaneh Leviyah* — the camp of Moshe, Aharon, and the *Levi'im*. Surrounding that was the *Machaneh Yisrael* — the camp of the rest of *Bnei Yisrael*. Each category of *tamei* person is excluded from a progressively inner zone. A *tamei meit* may not enter the *Mishkan* itself but may remain in the *Machaneh Leviyah*. A *zav* may not enter even the *Machaneh Leviyah* but may remain in the *Machaneh Yisrael*. A *metzora* is excluded even from the *Machaneh Yisrael* — he must sit in solitude outside all three camps.

### Yerushalayim Zones

When this framework is transposed onto the geography of *Yerushalayim*, the correspondences are as follows. *Machaneh Shechinah* becomes the *Beit HaMikdash* itself — specifically the *Azarah*. *Machaneh Leviyah* becomes *Har HaBayit*. *Machaneh Yisrael* becomes *Yerushalayim*. And *Chazal* extend this further, understanding that the status of *Yerushalayim* as *Machaneh Yisrael* applies to all walled cities — which is why the *metzora'im* in the *haftarah* are found sitting outside the city gate, even though the city is not *Yerushalayim*.

Practically speaking, a *tamei meit* may ascend *Har HaBayit* — which is the basis for those tours that take place on the *Har HaBayit* today. What such tours must be careful about is ensuring that participants do not walk on any ground that was once part of the *Azarah*, the *Ezrat Yisrael*, or the area near the *Mizbe'ach*, since a *tamei meit* is excluded from *Machaneh Shechinah*, which corresponds to that inner space. The *Gemara* also makes explicit that the category of *zav* includes a *ba'al keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission — as well as a *niddah* and a *yoledet*. These individuals may not ascend *Har HaBayit*. This has obvious contemporary implications: those who lead tours on *Har HaBayit* must ensure that participants are aware that a *ba'al keri* who has not immersed, or who has immersed but has not yet reached *ha'arev shemesh*, remains in a prohibited category for the *Machaneh Leviyah* zone. It is not clear that sufficient stringency is applied to this issue in practice.

### Metzora Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּדָד יֵשֵׁב״ — לְבַדּוֹ יֵשֵׁב. ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה מוֹשָׁבוֹ״, הַכָּתוּב נִתְּקוֹ לַעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** "He shall dwell in isolation" means he shall dwell alone, without even the company of others who are impure. "His dwelling shall be outside the camp" teaches that the verse has transmuted the negative precept into a positive mitzva.

Returning to Rav Chisda's ruling: the *metzora* who enters *Yerushalayim* is *patur* — exempt from lashes. This might seem paradoxical. If the *metzora* is the most severe category, why does he not receive lashes while presumably the others do? The answer lies in the *pasuk*: *badad yeshev michutz lamachaneh moshavo* — he shall sit alone outside the camp. This *pasuk* introduces what the *halacha* calls a *lav hanitak l'asei* — a negative prohibition that is connected to a remedying positive command.

Rashi reads this as follows: the *metzora* who enters *Yerushalayim* has violated the *lav* of *velo yitamu et machanehem*, but the transgression can be corrected by going back outside — the *asei* of *badad yeshev* provides the remedy. Because the *lav* is rectifiable through an *asei*, it does not carry lashes. Tosafot reads it differently: for the *metzora*, entering *Yerushalayim* is governed only by an *asei*, not a *lav* at all — the *pasuk* transformed the prohibition for the outermost zone into a positive command to remain outside. If the *metzora* were to enter a more interior prohibited zone — such as *Har HaBayit* or the *Azarah* itself — that would be a *lav* carrying lashes. But for the specific crossing of the *Yerushalayim* boundary, it is only an *asei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מְצוֹרָע שֶׁנִּכְנַס לִפְנִים מִמְּחִיצָתוֹ — בְּאַרְבָּעִים. זָבִים וְזָבוֹת שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לִפְנִים מִמְּחִיצָתָן — בְּאַרְבָּעִים. וּטְמֵא מֵת מוּתָּר לִיכָּנֵס לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** An objection was raised against Rav Ḥisda from a *baraita*: A leper, who may not even enter the Israelite camp, who went in beyond his boundary, is punished with forty lashes like one who violates a regular Torah prohibition. Similarly, *zavin* and *zavot*, who are prohibited from entering the Levite camp, who went in beyond their boundaries, are punished with forty lashes. And one who is ritually impure due to contact with a corpse is permitted to enter even the Levite camp.

A *braita* is then cited that seems to contradict Rav Chisda's ruling, stating that a *metzora* who enters beyond his *mechitza* does receive lashes. The *Gemara* resolves this as a *tannaitic* dispute, which it then elaborates through a *braita* that also establishes the three-camp structure itself.

### R. Yehuda Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״בָּדָד יֵשֵׁב״ — לְבַדּוֹ יֵשֵׁב, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ טְמֵאִין אֲחֵרִים יוֹשְׁבִין עִמּוֹ. יָכוֹל יְהוּ זָבִים וּטְמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יְטַמְּאוּ אֶת מַחֲנֵיהֶם״, לִיתֵּן מַחֲנֶה לָזֶה וּמַחֲנֶה לָזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is a matter that is subject to dispute between *tanna'im*, for it was taught in a different *baraita*: "He shall dwell in isolation" indicates that he shall dwell alone, meaning that other ritually impure people, such as *zavin* and those who are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse, should not dwell with him. One might have thought that *zavin* and those who are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse are sent to one camp, meaning that the laws governing which camps they may or may not enter are the same for both. Therefore, the verse states: "Both male and female shall you send out, outside the camp shall you send them out, and they shall defile not their camps in the midst of which I dwell." The plural term "camps" teaches that there are multiple camps for those who are ritually impure, so that we give a camp for this one and a camp for that one; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.

The *braita* presents two *tannaitic* positions on how to derive the three distinct exclusion zones. Rabbi Yehuda derives it from two *pesukim*. First, from *badad yeshev* — the *metzora* sits alone, meaning no other *tamei* people share his zone, establishing that the *metzora* has a distinct and more distant zone from all others. Second, from *machanehem* — "their camps," in the plural — which teaches that *zavim* and *tamei meitim* do not share a single zone either, but each occupies a different camp. The word *machanehem* rather than *machanam* is read as implying multiple distinct camps for multiple distinct categories.

### R. Shimon Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה כׇּל צָרוּעַ וְכׇל זָב וְכֹל טָמֵא לָנֶפֶשׁ״, יֵאָמֵר טְמֵאֵי מֵת וְאַל יֵאָמֵר טְמֵאֵי זָב, וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין, זָבִים — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר זָב — לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon says: It is not necessary to derive this law from the plural term "camps," for surely the verse says: "Command the children of Israel that they send out from the camp any leper and any *zav* and anyone impure by reason of a corpse." Let the verse say only that they are to send out those who are ritually impure due to a corpse, and not say anything about those impure as a *zav*, and I would say on my own that a *zav* is obviously included in this law: If those ritually impure due to a corpse are sent out from the camp, all the more so is it not clear that *zavin* should be sent out? If so, why is a *zav* stated? To give him a second camp.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵאָמֵר זָב וְאַל יֵאָמֵר מְצוֹרָע, וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: זָבִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין, מְצוֹרָעִין לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר מְצוֹרָע — לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁלִישִׁית, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״בָּדָד יֵשֵׁב״ — הַכָּתוּב נִתְּקוֹ לַעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** And furthermore: Let the verse say only that they are to send out a *zav*, and not say anything about a leper, and I would say on my own that a leper is obviously included in this law as well: If *zavin* are sent out, all the more so is it not clear that lepers should be sent out? Why then is a leper stated? To give him a third camp that he may not enter. When the verse says with regard to a leper: "He shall dwell alone," the verse has transmuted the negative precept into a positive command.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees not on the *halacha* but on the derivation. He argues that there is no need for the *pesukim* of *badad* and *machanehem* to establish the three camps. It emerges directly from the structure of the *pasuk*: *v'yishalchu min hamachaneh kol tzarua v'chol zav v'chol tamei lanefesh*. As the *Gemara* has already established, this *pasuk* could have stated only *tamei meit*, the most lenient category, and derived the others by *kal va-chomer*: if the lightest category is sent out, how much more so the heavier ones. The *Torah* stating *zav* in addition to *tamei meit* can only be to teach that a *zav* is sent even further — to a second camp. And stating *metzora* in addition to *zav* can only be to teach that a *metzora* is sent yet further still — to a third camp. The three-camp structure is embedded in the very necessity of mentioning all three categories when one would have logically sufficed.

According to Rabbi Shimon, then, *badad yeshev* is not needed for the *camp* derivation. What, then, does it teach? That is where *badad yeshev* does its work in the *halacha nitak l'asei*: it teaches that the *metzora* entering *Yerushalayim* does not receive lashes.

## Three Impurity Categories

### Severity Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חוּמְרֵיהּ דְּזָב מִטְּמֵא מֵת? שֶׁכֵּן טוּמְאָה יוֹצְאָה עָלָיו מִגּוּפוֹ. אַדְּרַבָּה: טְמֵא מֵת חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן הַזָּאָה שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** What is the stringency of a *zav* over one who is ritually impure due to a corpse? That the impurity of the *zav* issues out upon him from his own body, rather than coming from an external source, as is the case when impurity is contracted from a corpse. But on the contrary, it may be argued that the legal status of one who is ritually impure due to a corpse is more severe, as he requires sprinkling of the purifying waters on the third and seventh days of his purification process.

With the three-camp structure established, the *Gemara* now examines the hierarchy of severity that underlies it. The assumed order is: *tamei meit* is most lenient, then *zav*, then *metzora*. But the *Gemara* subjects each step of this hierarchy to challenge.

*Mai chumrei dezav mitmei meit?* — Why is a *zav* considered more severe than a *tamei meit*? The answer given is *tumah yotza alav migufo* — the *tumah* emerges from his own body. It is intrinsic to him, not contracted from an external source.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: perhaps *tamei meit* is more severe. After all, *tamei meit* requires *haza'ah* on the third and seventh days using the ashes of the *Parah Adumah* — a uniquely burdensome purification process that does not apply to a *zav*. Should that not indicate greater severity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״טָמֵא״ ״וְכֹל טָמֵא״, לְרַבּוֹת טְמֵא שֶׁרֶץ. וְזָב חָמוּר מִטְּמֵא שֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states not just "impure" but "and anyone impure." The additional words come to include one who is ritually impure due to contact with a creeping animal. He, too, is sent out from the camp like one who is impure due to contact with a corpse, and it is clear that the legal status of a *zav* is more severe than one who is ritually impure due to contact with a creeping animal.

The resolution lies in a close reading of the *pasuk*: *v'chol tamei lanefesh* — "and anyone *tamei* for a person" — interpreted as *kol tamei lanefesh*, anyone with a *tumah* similar to that of a corpse. This broadens the *tamei meit* category to include *tamei sheretz* — someone who touched a dead rodent or similar creature. *Tumah* contracted by contact from the outside is, in the *Gemara*'s framework, categorically less intrinsic than *tumah* that flows from within the body. Once the *tamei meit* category is understood to encompass even *tamei sheretz* — the most lenient form of *tumah* conceivable — the entire category is understood as the lightest tier. If even a *tamei sheretz* must leave the *Mikdash*, then certainly a *zav* must be sent even further, because the *Torah* still found it necessary to say so explicitly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חוּמְרֵיהּ דִּמְצוֹרָע מִזָּב? שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, וְאָסוּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה. אַדְּרַבָּה זָב חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וּמְטַמֵּא כְּלִי חֶרֶס בְּהֶיסֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** What is the stringency of a leper over a *zav*? That a confirmed leper is required to let his hair grow and rend his garments and he is prohibited from marital relations. But on the contrary, there is room to say that the legal status of a *zav* is more severe, for a *zav* imparts ritual impurity to that upon which he lies or sits even if he does not come into direct contact with it, and he imparts ritual impurity to an earthenware vessel through movement.

The *Gemara* then challenges the next step: *ve-zav chamur mitmei sheretz* — is a *zav* genuinely more severe than a *tamei sheretz*? The obvious case for severity is *tumah yotza migufo*, the internal origin of *zav*-*tumah*, and its seven-day duration. But the counterargument is: *shekein mitamei be'ones* — a *tamei sheretz* renders one *tamei* regardless of whether it was accidental. If one accidentally came into contact with a *sheretz*, one is nonetheless *tamei*. A *zav*, by contrast, is only classified as a full *zav* after a second flow; if the flow is attributable to external causes — something he ate, for instance — it may not count as a *zav* flow at all. This suggests a leniency in how the *zav* category is contracted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא — זָב נָמֵי טַמּוֹיֵי מְטַמֵּי בְּאוֹנֶס כִּדְרַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רְאִיָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב מְטַמְּאָה בְּאוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** In a case like this, meaning, if we compare the two cases in this way, a *zav* also becomes impure through an accident, in accordance with the opinion of Rav Huna, for Rav Huna said: The first sighting of a *zav* establishes ritual impurity even through an accident.

The *Gemara* responds by citing Rav Huna, who states on 69b that the *ra'iyah rishonah shel zav* — the first flow of a *zav* — renders him *tamei* even *be'ones*. On his first flow, a *zav* has the same one-day *tumah* status as a *ba'al keri*, and a *ba'al keri* is *tamei* regardless of how the emission occurred. So even by *ones*, the *zav* category is not entirely exempt. The counterargument from *ones* therefore fails to establish the superiority of *tamei sheretz*, and the hierarchy stands: *tamei meit* (including *tamei sheretz*) is the most lenient, and *zav* is more severe.

### Zav/Ba'al Keri/Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״זָב״, ״וְכׇל זָב״, לְרַבּוֹת בַּעַל קֶרִי. וּמְצוֹרָע חָמוּר מִבַּעַל קֶרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states not just "*zav*" but "and any *zav*," and this inclusive expression "any" comes to include a person who experienced a seminal emission, whose impurity is of the same type as a *zav*, and it is clear that the legal status of a leper is more severe than a person who experienced a seminal emission.

The category of *zav* is itself broader than the name implies. The *pasuk* reads *v'chol zav* — interpreted as encompassing anyone with a *zav*-like *tumah*: *lerabot ba'al keri* — this includes a man who has had a seminal emission. A *ba'al keri* is included within the *zav* category for purposes of the *machanot* exclusion, meaning a *ba'al keri* is likewise excluded from *Machaneh Leviyah* — from *Har HaBayit*. The same zone would apply to a *niddah* and a *yoledet*, who are parallel to the *ba'al keri* in this context.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חוּמְרֵיהּ — כְּדַאֲמַרַן. אַדְּרַבָּה: בַּעַל קֶרִי חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** And what is his stringency? In what way is the legal status of a leper more severe than one who experienced a seminal emission? As we said, that he must let his hair grow and rend his garments and he is prohibited to engage in marital relations. But on the contrary, there is room to say that the legal status of one who experienced a seminal emission is more severe, for he becomes ritually impure even with any amount.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai chumrei de'metzora mi'zav* — what makes the *metzora* more severe than the *zav*? Several *chumrot* are enumerated: the *metzora* must let his hair grow wild (*peri'ah*), rend his garments (*perimah*), and is prohibited from marital relations — derived from *vayeshev michutz le'ohalo*, "and he sat outside his tent," understood as separation from his wife. All of these indicate a uniquely severe personal status.

The *Gemara* challenges this too: perhaps *zav* is actually more severe in a critical dimension. *Shekein metamei mishkav u'moshav u'metamei kli cheres be'heseit* — a *zav* renders *tamei* whatever he sits or lies upon, and this *mishkav u'moshav* is an *av hatumah* — a primary source of *tumah* — not a derivative *tumah*. Even through ten cushions of separation, whatever constitutes a seat of the *zav* is *tamei* at the highest level. This category of *mishkav u'moshav* does not apply to a *metzora*. Additionally, a *zav* renders an earthen vessel (*kli cheres*) *tamei* through mere displacement (*hesheit*), without direct contact — another stringency absent by *metzora*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: זָב צָרִיךְ כַּחֲתִימַת פִּי הָאַמָּה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים. וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ לֵיהּ בַּעַל קֶרִי לְזָב.
>
> **Translation:** He holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Natan, as it was taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Natan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: In order for a *zav* to become ritually impure, he needs to experience a discharge substantial enough to cause a blockage of the tip of the male organ. But the Rabbis did not agree with him. And one who experienced a seminal emission is compared to a *zav* and therefore the same amount of discharge is required in order to cause ritual impurity.

The resolution again turns on the broader scope of *kol zav*. Once the *zav* category includes a *ba'al keri* — a one-day *tumah* of the most lenient kind — and if even a *ba'al keri* must leave *Machaneh Leviyah*, then by *kal va-chomer* a *metzora* must be sent even further. The *Torah* explicitly naming *metzora* is to say that it is sent to a third, still more distant zone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכׇל צָרוּעַ״ לְמָה לִי? אַיְּידֵי דִּכְתִיב ״כׇּל זָב״, כְּתִיב נָמֵי ״כׇּל צָרוּעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The expressions "any *zav*" and "anyone impure" teach that their respective categories are more inclusive than one would otherwise have thought. But why do I need the inclusive expression "and any leper"? What does the word "any" include? The Gemara explains: Since it is written "any *zav*," it is also written "any leper," so that the wording of the verse will be consistent.

One final step of the hierarchy is examined: *u'metzora chamur mi'ba'al keri* — and clearly a *metzora* is more severe than a *ba'al keri*. The counterargument offered is *shekein metamei be'mashehu* — a *ba'al keri* is *tamei* from even the smallest quantity of emission, while a *metzora*'s *tzara'at* must reach a minimum size of a *gris* (the area of a bean). The *Gemara* resolves this by citing the position of Rabbi Natan in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: a *zav*'s flow must be of sufficient volume to seal the opening of the reproductive organ. Since *ba'al keri* is connected to *zav* by analogy (*hekesh*), the same minimum applies. The *Gemara* acknowledges that Tosafot finds this comparison difficult — the opening in question seems smaller than a *gris* — but in any case the hierarchy is maintained: *tamei meit* at the base, then *zav*, then *metzora*.

At the conclusion of the analysis, the *Gemara* notes that *kol tzarua* — the corresponding expansion formula for the *metzora* category — does not in fact include any additional category. Having used *kol* with *tamei lanefesh* to include *tamei sheretz*, and *kol* with *zav* to include *ba'al keri*, the *Torah* wrote *kol tzarua* simply for stylistic parallelism — *aidi dechtav kol zav, uch'tav nami kol tzarua* — but no additional category is thereby included.

## Zav/Metzora in Pesach B'Tumah

### R. Eliezer Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל דָּחֲקוּ זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין וְנִכְנְסוּ לָעֲזָרָה בְּפֶסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה כׇּל צָרוּעַ וְכׇל זָב וְכֹל טָמֵא לָנֶפֶשׁ״. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁטְּמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין — זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין, אֵין טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין — אֵין זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** He needs that verse for that which was taught in a different *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer says: One might have thought that if *zavin* and lepers pushed and entered the Temple courtyard during the offering of a Paschal lamb that is brought in a state of impurity, when the majority of the nation are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse, that perhaps they would be liable for punishment for having violated the prohibition of entering the Temple while ritually impure. They are prohibited from entering even under such circumstances. Therefore the verse states: "That they send out from the camp any leper and any *zav* and anyone impure by reason of a corpse," which teaches us that at a time when those impure due to a corpse are sent out, *zavin* and lepers are sent out and are liable to *karet* for entering the Temple; but when those impure due to a corpse are not sent out, *zavin* and lepers are not sent out, meaning, they are not liable to receive *karet* for entering the Temple.

The *Gemara* now turns to a question raised by Rabbi Eliezer that returns us to the topic of *pesach habah b'tumah*. Rabbi Eliezer asks: suppose *zavim* and *metzoraim* pressed forward and actually entered the *Azarah* during a *Pesach* brought in *tumah* — would they be liable? The question is whether the general *heter* of *pesach habah b'tumah*, which suspends the usual prohibition for *tamei meit*, also extends in any way to *zavim* and *metzoraim*.

### Conditional Exemption

The resolution comes from the same *pasuk* that drove the earlier discussion: *v'yishalchu min hamachaneh kol tzarua v'chol zav v'chol tamei lanefesh*. The *Gemara* reads this as encoding a conditional relationship among the three categories. The principle is: *besha'ah she'tamei meitim mishtalchim, zavin u'metzoraim mishtalchim* — at the time when *tamei meitim* are subject to being sent out of their respective zones, then *zavin* and *metzoraim* are also subject to their respective exclusions. *Aval ein tamei meitim mishtalchim* — but at the time when the rule of exclusion is suspended for *tamei meitim*, as during *pesach habah b'tumah*, then *ein zavim u'metzoraim mishtalchim* — the *zavim* and *metzoraim* also do not transgress *bedi'avad* if they enter. They have no license to enter; they still may not do so *lechatchila*. But if they did enter during such a period, they are *patur*. The exemption is not an affirmative permission but a suspension of liability that follows logically from the exceptional status of the entire period. This is what Rabbi Yehuda, in his derivation, learns from the *pasuk* that lists all three categories — not the three-camp structure, which he derives elsewhere, but this conditional exemption that links all three categories together.

## Ba'al Keri Issues

### Ba'al Keri Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״זָב״, ״וְכׇל זָב״ — לְרַבּוֹת בַּעַל קֶרִי. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְחִילּוֹת לֹא נִתְקַדְּשׁוּ, וּבַעַל קֶרִי מִשְׁתַּלֵּחַ חוּץ לִשְׁתֵּי מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Master said above: The fact that the verse does not just say "*zav*" but rather the inclusive expression "and any *zav*" comes to include a person who experienced a seminal emission. This exposition supports the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, for Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The tunnels under the Temple were not sanctified with the sanctity of the Temple; and a person who experienced a seminal emission is sent outside the two camps just like a *zav*.

Having established that *v'chol zav* includes *ba'al keri*, the *Gemara* notes that this supports Rabbi Yochanan's position. A *braita* had stated *ba'al keri ke'maga sheretz* — a *ba'al keri* is like someone who touched a *sheretz*. One might read this as placing a *ba'al keri* in the most lenient category, equivalent to *tamei meit* (which includes *tamei sheretz*), and therefore permitting him on *Har HaBayit* just as a *tamei meit* is permitted. But the *Gemara* argues that the comparison is not about the *machanot* exclusion zones but about the nature of the *tumah* itself — specifically, that just as *tamei sheretz* applies even *be'ones* (regardless of intent or circumstance), so too a *ba'al keri* is *tamei be'ones*. The *pasuk* itself already specifies that each carries a one-day *tumah* (*tumat erev*), so the comparison is not needed for that purpose. The comparison must therefore be about the *be'ones* rule, not about the exclusion zones. This leaves Rabbi Yochanan's position intact: a *ba'al keri* is sent out from two camps — from *Machaneh Shechinah* (the *Mikdash*) and from *Machaneh Leviyah* (*Har HaBayit*).

### Tunnels Not Sanctified

Rabbi Yochanan himself states two related *halachot*: *mechilos lo niskadshu* — the underground tunnels beneath the *Beit HaMikdash* were not sanctified — and *u'ba'al keri mishtalei'ach chutz lishtei machanos* — a *ba'al keri* is sent out from both camps, from *Machaneh Shechinah* and from *Machaneh Leviyah*, that is, not only from the *Mikdash* itself but from *Har HaBayit* as well. The connection between these two statements may be that the first — the unsanctified tunnels — provides a practical avenue for *tamei* individuals to navigate beneath the *Har HaBayit* without violating the exclusion; and the second clarifies just how extensive the exclusion for *ba'al keri* is within the upper zones.

The ruling that underground tunnels were not sanctified has obvious relevance to contemporary discussions about *Har HaBayit*. Someone who is *tamei* could theoretically pass through tunnels beneath the walls without transgressing the *kedushah* of the *Machaneh Leviyah* zone.

### Priests' Ba'al Keri

This raises a pointed practical question about life in the *Beit HaMikdash* period. The *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* lived within or adjacent to *Machaneh Leviyah* — the zone corresponding to *Har HaBayit*. A man who has marital relations with his wife becomes a *ba'al keri* and is excluded from *Machaneh Leviyah*. How, then, did the *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* manage? Would a man have to leave his home in the middle of the night and travel outside the *machaneh* boundary after relations with his wife? The practical logistics seem deeply difficult, and the *Gemara* does not provide a resolution here. This is a question worth further investigation, but for now it is left open, and the *daf* concludes on this note.
