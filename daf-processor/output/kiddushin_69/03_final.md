# Kiddushin 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matrilineal Descent

### Non-Jewish Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נׇכְרִית מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תִתְחַתֵּן בָּם״. אַשְׁכַּחְנָא דְּלָא תָּפְסִי בַּהּ קִידּוּשֵׁי, וְלָדָהּ כְּמוֹתָהּ מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יָסִיר אֶת בִּנְךָ מֵאַחֲרַי״ – בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִיִּשְׂרְאֵלִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, וְאֵין בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן הַנׇּכְרִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, אֶלָּא בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that betrothal with **a gentile woman** is ineffective? **The verse states: "Neither shall you make marriages with them"** (Deuteronomy 7:3), which teaches that marrying gentile women is halakhically meaningless. The Gemara asks: **We have found that betrothal is ineffective with her; from where do we** derive that **her offspring is like her?** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: As the verse states** with regard to the same issue: "Your daughter you shall not give to his son…**for he will turn away your son from following Me"** (Deuteronomy 7:3–4). Since the verse is concerned that after one's daughter marries a gentile, the father will lead his children away from the service of God, this indicates that **your son,** i.e., your grandson, **from a Jewish woman is called "your son"** by the Torah, **but your son from a gentile woman is not called your son, but her son.**

The daf opens in the middle of an extremely important discussion rooted in the final point of the preceding *mishnah*: that the child of a *shifcha* — a slave woman — is also a slave, derived from the *pasuk* *ha'isha veyeladeha tihyeh le'adoneha* ("the woman and her children shall belong to her master"). From there, the *Gemara* moves to the *halacha* that the child of a non-Jewish woman and a Jewish man is not Jewish. The *mishnah* does not explicitly address the reverse — a Jewish mother and a non-Jewish father — but the *Gemara* here is the locus classicus for the entire principle of matrilineal descent.

The *Gemara* begins with the question: *nochrit minalan* — how do we know, in the case of a non-Jewish woman, that the child is not Jewish? The answer is *amar kra: lo titchaten bam* — the Torah commands not to intermarry with them, the context being the seven Canaanite nations. From this prohibition we derive that *kiddushin* does not take effect. But *vevalda kamota minalan* — how do we know that the child's status follows the mother?

### Rashi's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יָסִיר אֶת בִּנְךָ מֵאַחֲרַי״ – בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִיִּשְׂרְאֵלִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, וְאֵין בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן הַנׇּכְרִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, אֶלָּא בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: As the verse states:** with regard to the same issue: "Your daughter you shall not give to his son…**for he will turn away your son from following Me"** (Deuteronomy 7:3–4). Since the verse is concerned that after one's daughter marries a gentile, the father will lead his children away from the service of God, this indicates that **your son,** i.e., your grandson, **from a Jewish woman is called "your son"** by the Torah, **but your son from a gentile woman is not called your son, but her son.**

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*: the verse states *ki yasir et bincha me'acharai* — "for he will cause your son to stray from Me." From this we derive: *bincha haba miyisraelit karui bincha* — your child born from a Jewish woman is called your son, *ve'ein bincha haba min ha'avodat kochavim karui bincha ella binah* — but your child born from a non-Jewish woman is not called your son; rather, he is called her son.

The difficulty is obvious: how does one derive all this from that *pasuk*? The surrounding verses state *bitcha lo titein livno uvito lo tikach livnecha* — do not give your daughter to their son and do not take their daughter for your son — and the reason given is "for he will cause your son to stray from Me." But this is grammatically awkward. If we are talking about giving your daughter to their son, the verse should have said that he — the non-Jewish son-in-law — will cause *your daughter* to stray. And if we are talking about taking their daughter for your son, it should have warned that *she* will cause your son to stray. The verse, as written, says "he will cause your son to stray" — which fits neither case straightforwardly.

Rashi resolves this by reading the verse as follows: the prohibition against giving your daughter to their son is because *he* — the non-Jewish son-in-law — will cause *your grandson* to stray. In other words, the "son" referred to is not your own son but your daughter's son, your grandson. This is significant: it implies that the child of your Jewish daughter and her non-Jewish husband is still *your* grandson, still Jewish — and therefore the concern is that this non-Jewish husband will lead that Jewish grandchild away from God. The reverse inference is then drawn: if a Jewish man fathers a child with a non-Jewish woman, that child is called *her* child, not his, and is therefore not Jewish.

### Rabbeinu Tam's View

Rabbeinu Tam is troubled by the interpretive leap of reading "your son" as "your grandson," since the grandson has not been mentioned in the text. He therefore reads the verse differently. According to Rabbeinu Tam, "he" who will cause "your son" to stray refers not to any future child of the union but to the non-Jewish father-in-law himself. The case in view is your son marrying their daughter, and the warning is that *he* — the father-in-law — will pull your son away from Jewish practice. What the verse thereby teaches about the status of the offspring is that, under this reading, "turning your son away" implies that the children of that union will be non-Jewish — the entire family line is being drawn away. Rashi's reading remains somewhat simpler: the word *bincha* — your son — means your grandson through your daughter, which establishes that the child of a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father is still Jewish.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Returning to the *Gemara*'s derivation: the principle stated by Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai is that *bincha haba miyisrael karui bincha* — a child born of a Jewish woman is called your child — *ve'ein bincha haba min ha'ovedet kochavim karui bincha ella benah* — but the child of a non-Jewish woman is not your son but hers. However one parses the grammatical problem in *ki yasir et bincha*, it is from this difficulty that the *Gemara* derives the principle of matrilineal descent. It is worth noting that this is not the most airtight proof; the entire edifice rests on a subtle grammatical anomaly.

### Ravina's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בֶּן בִּתְּךָ הַבָּא מִן הַנׇּכְרִי – קָרוּי בִּנְךָ. נֵימָא קָסָבַר רָבִינָא נׇכְרִי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Learn from** it that **the son of your daughter, born to a gentile, is called your son** in all regards. The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **Ravina holds** that with regard to **a gentile or** a Canaanite **slave who engaged in sexual intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*?** One can infer from the fact that the offspring of this union is called "your son" that he is a Jew, and therefore the principle stated in the mishna should apply: If a woman cannot join in betrothal with someone, their child is a *mamzer*.

*Amar Ravina shema mina*: Ravina draws an explicit conclusion from this teaching — *ben bitcha haba mi'oved kochavim karui bincha* — the son of your daughter, even if born from a non-Jewish man, is still called your son, and thus is Jewish. This appears to be a complete restatement of what Rabbi Yochanan already said, and *Tosafos* attempts to distinguish them, but functionally both are making the same point: a child of a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father is Jewish. This principle, it bears repeating, was not stated in the *mishnah* itself, which addressed only the case of a non-Jewish mother.

### Mamzer Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּכָשֵׁר לָא הָוֵי, מַמְזֵר לָא הָוֵי, פָּסוּל מִיקְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Although he is not a fit** offspring, **he is** also **not a *mamzer*.** Rather, **he is** merely **called disqualified.** Since betrothal is inapplicable to a gentile, a gentile is not included in the category of someone with whom a Jewish woman cannot personally join in betrothal, as no Jewish women can be betrothed to him. Nevertheless, as their child's birth is the result of a transgression, he is considered disqualified.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a concern: *neima kasavar Ravina oved kochavim ve'eved haba al bat Yisrael havlad mamzer* — perhaps Ravina holds that the child of a non-Jewish man or slave who fathers a child with a Jewish woman is a *mamzer*? The reasoning is familiar: we know that when *kiddushin* cannot take effect between two parties, the child of their union is a *mamzer*. Since *kiddushin* is clearly not binding between a Jewish woman and a non-Jewish man, why isn't the child a *mamzer*?

This would actually create a strange dynamic: saying the child is non-Jewish is in one sense the more lenient outcome, because the child could simply convert and carry no stigma. Saying the child is Jewish immediately raises the specter of *mamzer* status.

### Psul Status

The *Gemara* resolves this: *nehi de'kasher lo havei mamzer lo havei* — Ravina said neither that the child is fully *kasher* nor that the child is a *mamzer*. As Rashi explains, *mamzer* status arises from a union where both parties could, in principle, marry someone else within *halakha* but are forbidden to each other — as in adultery or incest. In the case of a Jewish woman and a non-Jewish man, the non-Jewish man has no valid *halachic* marriage at all within our framework. This is a categorically different type of forbidden relationship. Therefore: the child is Jewish, but carries a *psul* — a defective status that falls short of full *kashrut* without rising to the severity of *mamzerut*.

What does this *psul* mean practically? Two areas are worth noting. First, there are contexts in the *Gemara* where the status of a person with a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father is considered alongside the status of a *geir*, not because such a person needs conversion — they do not — but because there is a partial analogy in that their Jewish identity has, as it were, a non-Jewish biographical component. Second, and perhaps more immediately practical, there is a significant debate among the *poskim* — addressed in another *Gemara* and not fully spelled out here — about whether a *kohen* may marry a woman born of a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father. This *psul* may give rise to at least a *de'rabbanan* concern in that context. The *Gemara* here does not spell out all the ramifications, but these are the two areas where this intermediate status carries genuine *halachic* weight.

## Seven Nations

### All Non-Jews Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בְּשִׁבְעָה גּוֹיִם כְּתִיב, שְׁאָר אוּמּוֹת מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יָסִיר אֶת בִּנְךָ״ – לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל הַמְּסִירִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement. **That** verse: "Neither shall you make marriages with them" (Deuteronomy 7:3), **is written with regard to the seven nations** of Canaan. **From where do we** derive that betrothal does not take effect with the **other nations?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** as a reason for prohibiting intermarriages: **"For he will turn away your son** from following Me," which serves **to include all those who might turn** a child **away,** no matter from which nation.

Having established the principle, the *Gemara* now asks: *hahu beshivah goyim ketiv, she'ar umos minalan* — that *pasuk* of *lo titchaten bam*, with its attendant consequences for the status of the child, is written in the context of the seven Canaanite nations. How do we know to apply the same rules to all non-Jews?

The *Gemara* answers: *amar kra ki yasir et bincha* — the verse explains the reason for the prohibition as "he will cause your son to stray," *l'rabos kol hamisirim* — which comes to include all those who would cause such straying. Since the rationale — that the non-Jewish spouse will lead the child away from Jewish identity — applies equally to any non-Jew, the verse's scope is not limited to the seven nations.

### Rabbi Shimon Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּדָרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִּקְרָא. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who expounds the reason** for the mitzvot of **the verse** and rules accordingly. Since the reason is that the gentile might turn away the son's heart, there should be no distinction between the Canaanite nations and other gentiles. **But according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who do not expound the reason for the mitzvot of the verse and rules accordingly, since the verse mentions only the Canaanite nations, **what is the reason,** the source for the prohibition, with regard to the other nations?

But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *hani l'Rabbi Shimon d'daresh ta'ama dikra* — this works well for Rabbi Shimon, who is willing to use the reason of a verse to redefine its parameters. Rabbi Shimon's method, *darshan ta'ama dikra*, allows the inferred rationale to expand or contract the application of a law. A classic illustration of this principle is the prohibition of *lo tachvol beged almanah* — one may not take a widow's garment as collateral. Rabbi Shimon reads this as applying only to cases where taking the garment would create a problematic situation — such as repeatedly entering a widow's home alone to return the garment each day, raising suspicion — but not to a rich widow, because the rationale does not apply there. Crucially, the rationale itself may vary: even when two authorities agree that *darshan ta'ama dikra* is legitimate, they may disagree entirely about what the *ta'ama dikra* is.

### Rabbanan Objection

The *Rabbanan*, however, do not use the reason of a verse to redefine its parameters. This does not mean they deny that *mitzvot* have reasons; it means they refuse to allow their own assumption of a reason to override or extend the plain scope of the verse. So *ella l'Rabbanan mai ta'ama* — how do the *Rabbanan* extend the law beyond the seven Canaanite nations?

### Rashi's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאַחַר כֵּן תָּבוֹא אֵלֶיהָ וּבְעַלְתָּהּ וְגוֹ׳״ – מִכְּלָל דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא תָּפְסִי בַּהּ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to a beautiful captive woman: **"And after that you may go in to her and be her husband,** and she shall be your wife" (Deuteronomy 21:13). One can derive from here **by inference that at the outset,** before she became a Jew, **betrothal would not take effect with her,** despite the fact that he had already brought her into his house, and according to some opinions, had even engaged in sexual intercourse with her.

Rashi's approach is that the very inclusion of the phrase *ki yasir et bincha* in the verse is what provides the extension — for *everyone*, including the *Rabbanan*. Had the verse not stated this reason, one might indeed have confined the prohibition and its consequences to the seven nations. But because the Torah itself explicitly provided the rationale, that explicit statement is itself teaching that wherever the rationale applies — any non-Jew who might lead the child astray — the law applies as well. This is not a case of the *Rabbanan* inferring an unstated reason and then using it to redefine the *mitzvah*; rather, the Torah stated the reason explicitly, and that explicit statement carries its own exegetical force as a *ribui* — an expansion. *Tosafos* takes a somewhat different approach, but the upshot is the same.

## Yefat Toar Source

### Yefat Toar Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּלָא תָּפְסִי בַּהּ קִידּוּשִׁין, וְלָדָהּ כְּמוֹתָהּ מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי תִהְיֶיןָ לְאִישׁ... וְיָלְדוּ לוֹ״ – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״כִּי תִּהְיֶינָה״, קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְיָלְדוּ לוֹ״. וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״כִּי תִּהְיֶינָה״ – לָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְיָלְדוּ לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **betrothal is ineffective with her; from where do we** derive that **her child is like her?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "If a man has** two wives, the one beloved and the one hated, **and they have borne him** children" (Deuteronomy 21:15), from which it is derived: **Anywhere that we read: "If he has,"** i.e., that a woman can be betrothed, **we** also **read: "And they have borne him,"** meaning that their children follow his lineage. **And anywhere that we do not read: "If he has," we** likewise **do not read: "And they have borne him,"** as the offspring inherit their mother's status.

The *Gemara* now introduces a second textual basis for the law of matrilineal descent, drawn from a completely different part of the Torah — the *yefat to'ar*, the captive woman taken in war. This case, by definition, involves non-Canaanite nations, since the laws of *yefat to'ar* apply specifically to wars waged against nations *outside* the land. The verse there states *v'acharei ken tavo eleha u'va'alta* — only after the prescribed mourning process does the marriage become valid.

### Kiddushin Bindable

From here the *Gemara* derives a general principle: *kiddushin* must be capable of taking binding effect for the child's status to follow the father. The mechanism is as follows: where *kiddushin* can bind, the children are "his"; where it cannot, they are not.

### Ki Tiheyena L'Ish

The textual peg for this is the verse *ki tiheyena l'ish shtei nashim v'yaldu lo* — if a man has two wives and they bear him children. The *Gemara* reads this as: *kol heicha d'karinan ki tiheyena* — anywhere that the woman can truly be said to "belong to a man" in the full legal sense of *kiddushin* — *karinan beih v'yaldu lo* — the children are considered born to him and follow his status.

### Non-Jewish Mother

Conversely, *v'kol heicha d'lo karinan beih ki tiheyena* — in any case where there is no Jewish man for whom this woman's *kiddushin* would be binding, *lo karinan beih v'yaldu lo* — the children are not attributed to the father. This is not merely a case where *kiddushin* is forbidden but where it is categorically incapable of taking effect with *any* Jewish man. In such a case, the children are not his and are not Jewish.

### Jewish Mother Inference

Notably, this derivation, like the one from *ki yasir et bincha*, establishes only the case of the non-Jewish mother: where *kiddushin* cannot bind, the child is not Jewish. The converse — that the child of a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father *is* Jewish — is not explicitly derived from this verse; that principle rests on the earlier *drasha* of Rabbi Yochanan.

## Servant Woman

### Redundancy Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שִׁפְחָה נָמֵי! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא ״הָאִשָּׁה וִילָדֶיהָ תִּהְיֶה לַאדֹנֶיהָ״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** one should learn from here with regard to a Canaanite **maidservant too,** that her child is like her, which means that the earlier proof from the verse: "The wife and her children" (Exodus 21:4), is not necessary. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so;** this source also teaches the *halakha* that the offspring of a maidservant is like her. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the verse **"The wife and her children shall be her master's"?** This verse apparently teaches nothing new with regard to the *halakhot* of lineage. The Gemara answers: It is required **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*:

One might ask: if we have already derived from *ki tiheyena l'ish* that where *kiddushin* cannot bind, the child follows the mother — this should cover the case of a *shifcha* as well, since no Jewish man can contract a valid *kiddushin* with a *shifcha*. If so, why do we need the separate verse *ha'isha veyeladeha tihyeh le'adoneha* — "the woman and her children shall belong to her master"? We have already established the principle from elsewhere.

### Braita Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לְשִׁפְחָתוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ בַּת חוֹרִין, וּוְלָדִךְ עֶבֶד״ – הַוָּלָד כְּמוֹתָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִים, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָאִשָּׁה וִילָדֶיהָ תִּהְיֶה לַאדֹנֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who says to his** pregnant Canaanite **maidservant: You are hereby a free woman but your offspring** shall remain **a slave, the offspring is** emancipated **like her.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. And the Rabbis say:** The master's **statement is upheld, because it is stated: "The wife and her children shall be her master's"** (Exodus 21:4).

The *Gemara* answers: *lichdetanya* — as was taught in a *braita* (on 67a). The verse is needed for a specific case: what if a master says to his female slave, *harei at bat chorin* — you are hereby a free woman — *u'vladech eved* — but your unborn child, still in the womb, will remain a slave? Can a master split the freedom of the mother from the slave-status of the fetus?

### Split Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רָבָא: אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this ruling: **What is the** biblical **derivation** here? How do the Rabbis learn from here that the child of an emancipated maidservant remains a slave in this case? **Rava said:** The proof from the verse beginning with: "The wife and her children," is not the source of the opinion of the Rabbis. Rather, this is referring **to** the statement **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** who claims that the children follow their mother, as indicated by this verse. Consequently, if she is emancipated, her offspring do not retain the status of slaves.

*Havlad kamota, divrei Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* — Rabbi Yossi HaGlili rules that you cannot split them: if the mother is freed, the child is automatically freed as well. *Va'chachamim omrim d'varav kayamin* — the *chachamim* say his words stand and the split is valid.

### Verse Unity

The verse is needed for a specific case: what if a master says to his female slave, *harei at bat chorin* — you are hereby a free woman — *u'vladech eved* — but your unborn child, still in the womb, will remain a slave? Can a master split the freedom of the mother from the slave-status of the fetus?

*Amar Rava aliba d'Rabbi Yossi HaGlili*: what is the textual basis for Rabbi Yossi HaGlili's position? The verse *ha'isha veyeladeha tihyeh le'adoneha* — the woman *and her children* shall belong to her master — teaches that mother and child are bound together: when the mother is the master's slave, the children are the master's slaves; but you cannot create a situation where the mother is no longer your slave while the unborn child is. The mother and child are a unified legal unit. This is what required the verse: the general principle of matrilineal descent was already derivable from *ki tiheyena l'ish*, but the specific rule that you cannot sever the legal bond between mother and fetus in this way required the verse *ha'isha veyeladeha* explicitly.

## Rabbi Tarfon

### Proposal Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יְכוֹלִין מַמְזֵרִין לִיטָּהֵר. כֵּיצַד? מַמְזֵר שֶׁנָּשָׂא שִׁפְחָה, הַוָּלָד – עֶבֶד, שִׁיחְרְרוֹ – נִמְצָא הַבֵּן בֶּן חוֹרִין. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה עֶבֶד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Tarfon says: *Mamzerim* can be purified,** so that their offspring will not be *mamzerim*. **How so?** With regard to **a *mamzer* who married** a Canaanite **maidservant,** their **offspring is a slave.** If his master subsequently **emancipates him,** that **son is found** to be **a freeman,** rather than a *mamzer*. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** This method is not effective, as **this** son **is a *mamzer* slave.**

The *mishnah* concludes the *perek* with a striking proposal: *Rabbi Tarfon omer yakhol ani letaher et hamamzerim* — Rabbi Tarfon says he can "purify" *mamzerim* — not retroactively remove the status, but arrange that their children will not be *mamzerim*. The mechanism is rooted in what was just established. Since the child of a *shifcha* is an *eved* and follows the mother, Rabbi Tarfon proposes: let the *mamzer* marry a *shifcha kena'anit*. Their children will be slaves — *vlad eved* — following the mother. As slaves, they are outside the category of *mamzerut*, which applies only within Jewish status. Then free the children. The freed children are Jewish and not *mamzerim*. Problem solved.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לוֹ״ – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר פְּסוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Eliezer says: This** son **is a *mamzer* slave. Rabbi Elazar said: What is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer? As the verse states** with regard to a *mamzer*: "Even to the tenth generation none **of his** shall enter the assembly of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:3), which indicates that in the case of the child of a *mamzer* and a Canaanite maidservant, one **follows his** parent with the **flawed** lineage, and the child is a *mamzer*.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer harei zeh eved mamzer* — Rabbi Eliezer disagrees sharply. The *mamzer* status passes through regardless; the child is worse off than before, now an *eved mamzer*. The reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer, as Rabbi Elazar explains, is derived from the verse *lo yavo lo bikhal Hashem* — the word *lo* (meaning "to him," the *mamzer*) teaches that the stigma always follows: *halach achar psulo*, any future generation traces back to the original *mamzer* regardless of the mother's status. The word *lo* is read as overriding the ordinary rules of lineage — just as normally we follow the father (*l'mishpechotam l'veit avotam*), yet the verse by *mamzer* says even where you would normally not transmit the *psul*, *lo* tells you the *psul* persists.

## Rabbi Tarfon…

### L'Chatchila Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לְכַתְּחִילָּה קָאָמַר, אוֹ דִיעֲבַד קָאָמַר? תָּא שְׁמַע: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: טִיהַרְתָּ אֶת הַזְּכָרִים, וְלֹא טִיהַרְתָּ אֶת הַנְּקֵיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Did **Rabbi Tarfon state** his *halakha **ab initio*****,** i.e., a *mamzer* is permitted to marry a maidservant, **or** did **he state** it only **after the fact,** but he does not permit a *mamzer* to marry a maidservant *ab initio*? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: The other Sages **said to Rabbi Tarfon: You have** thereby **purified the male** offspring of a *mamzer*, **but you have not purified the female** children of *mamzerim*, as your solution does not apply to them.

A question is raised: when Rabbi Tarfon proposes this solution, is he saying it is permitted *l'chatchila* — that a *mamzer* may proactively seek out a *shifcha* to marry — or only *b'di'eved*, meaning that if it happened, the children would not be *mamzerim*, but one may not initiate the arrangement? There is an underlying *issur* for a *Yisrael* to marry a *shifcha*. A *mamzer* may marry a *giyoret*, since there is no prohibition for a *Yisrael* to marry a *giyoret*, but marrying a *shifcha* is a different matter.

### Gender Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ לְכַתְּחִילָּה קָאָמַר, מַמְזֶרֶת נָמֵי תִּינְּסִיב לְעַבְדָּא! עֶבֶד אֵין לוֹ חַיִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the apparent proof from this *baraita*. **And if you say** that Rabbi Tarfon **stated** his *halakha **ab initio*** and permitted a *mamzer* to marry a Canaanite maidservant, **a *mamzeret* should also** be allowed to **marry** a Canaanite **slave** and her child can then be emancipated as well. The Gemara answers: **A slave has no lineage.** Even if she were to marry a slave, their child would not be considered his, but would be a Jewish *mamzer* like her. Consequently, this source provides no proof with regard to the Gemara's question.

The *Gemara* notes an asymmetry between the male and female *mamzer*. The *mishnah* notes that Rabbi Tarfon's interlocutors observed: *tiharta es hazacharim v'lo tiharta es hanekevos* — this solution works for males but not for females. A *mamzeret* who is a female cannot marry a male *eved* and achieve the same result, because the child of a *mamzeret* and a male *eved* would follow the mother — but the mother is Jewish and a *mamzeret*, so the child would be Jewish and a *mamzer*. The solution only works when the *mamzer* is male and the *shifcha* is female, because then the child follows the mother — the *shifcha* — and is an *eved*, breaking the Jewish status entirely, after which the children can be freed.

Rashi explains the practical reason why the solution was unavailable to a female *mamzeret*: she could not go off anonymously and enter into such an arrangement without her family — fathers, brothers — knowing and intervening. A male could, in principle, travel elsewhere and arrange a union with a *shifcha* without attracting attention. But this logic presupposes the arrangement is not entirely above-board, suggesting it might only work *b'di'eved* — after the fact.

### Rabbi Simlai Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּאוּשְׁפִּיזְכָּנֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי מַמְזֵר הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אַקְדֵּמְתָּךְ – טַהַרְתִּינְהוּ לִבְנָךְ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְכַתְּחִילָּה – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דִּיעֲבַד, מַאי נִיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Simlai's host was a *mamzer*, and** Rabbi Simlai **said to him: Had I** found out about your status **earlier,** before you married and had children, **I would have purified your sons** by advising you to marry a Canaanite maidservant, as suggested by Rabbi Tarfon. The Gemara explains the proof: **Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Tarfon spoke ***ab initio*,** it is **well** that Rabbi Simlai would suggest this. **But if you say** that he meant only that this method is effective **after the fact, what was** the advice that Rabbi Simlai would have given his host?

The *Gemara* brings a proof from a story: the host of Rabbi Simlai — *ushpizchanei de-Rabbi Simlai* — was a *mamzer*. Rabbi Simlai said to him, *ei akdamtach tihartinhu l'vanecha* — had I known you earlier, I could have advised you how to arrange that your children would not be *mamzerim*. The *Gemara* reasons: *ei amart bishlama l'chatchila shapir* — if it is permitted *l'chatchila*, Rabbi Simlai's statement is perfectly coherent; he would have given proactive advice. *Ela ei amart di'eved* — but if marrying a *shifcha* is forbidden and only works *b'di'eved*, how could Rabbi Simlai say he would have *advised* someone to do something prohibited?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמַנְסֵיב לֵיהּ עֵצָה וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל גְּנוֹב וְאִיזְדַּבַּן בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. וּבִשְׁנֵי דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי עֶבֶד עִבְרִי מִי הֲוָה? וְהָאָמַר מָר: אִין עֶבֶד עִבְרִי נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לְכַתְּחִילָּה קָאָמַר – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers **that** Rabbi Simlai would have **advised him by saying: Go steal, and be sold as a Hebrew slave,** which would mean you could marry a Canaanite maidservant and your offspring would be slaves. The Gemara asks: **But in the days of Rabbi Simlai, was** the *halakha* of **a Hebrew slave** observed in practice? **But didn't the Master say:** The *halakha* of **a Hebrew slave is practiced only when the Jubilee** Year **is practiced,** and Rabbi Simlai lived many years after the observance of the Jubilee Year ceased. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** it that **Rabbi Tarfon spoke *ab initio*,** i.e., it is permitted for a *mamzer* to marry a Canaanite maidservant? The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **conclude from** the *baraita* that this is the case. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon.**

The *Gemara* considers a workaround: Rabbi Simlai would have advised the man to go steal something, be sold as an *eved Ivri*, and since an *eved Ivri* is permitted to marry a *shifcha kena'anit*, the *issur* would be circumvented. Rashi notes the obvious problem — stealing is forbidden — but the *Gemara* rejects this solution on independent grounds: *uv'shnei de-Rabbi Simlai eved Ivri mi havai?* The laws of *eved Ivri* only apply when *yovel* is in force, which was not the case in Rabbi Simlai's time.

### Perpetuation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: כׇּל וָלָד בִּמְעֵי שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית כְּוָלָד בִּמְעֵי בְּהֵמָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** And** how do **the Rabbis,** Rabbi Tarfon, respond to this claim? They say: **Any offspring in the womb of a Canaanite maidservant is considered like the offspring in an animal's womb.** Consequently, her children do not inherit the father's status, even if his is the flawed lineage.

The disagreement between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan* thus comes into full relief. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *mamzer* status perpetuates through any union, even with a *shifcha*, based on *lo yavo lo bikhal Hashem* — *halach achar psulo*, the stigma always follows. The *Rabbanan* counter that *kol vlad shebimei shifcha kena'anit kevlad bimei behema dami* — the child of a non-Jewish slave woman is like the offspring of an animal, in the sense that it completely severs any prior lineage-based identity. Just as an animal's offspring has no tribal or family affiliation, the child of a *shifcha* exits the entire framework of Jewish lineage, including *mamzerut*. Once that break is complete, there is nothing for the *mamzer* stigma to attach to.

The distinction is important: we are not merely saying that the child follows the mother's status rather than the father's — which would be the normal matrilineal principle operating within a Jewish framework. Here, because the mother is entirely outside the Jewish legal order, the child's very connection to the father's status is severed at a more fundamental level. This is why the *Rabbanan*'s position is not simply "the child follows the mother's slave status" but the stronger claim that the bond to the father's Jewish identity — including its *psul* — is broken entirely.

### Halacha Conclusion

The *halacha* follows Rabbi Tarfon, and as established, it is *muttar l'chatchila*. A male *mamzer* is permitted, not merely *b'di'eved*, to marry a *shifcha* with the intention of freeing the children and resolving the *mamzerut*. Indeed, Rav Moshe Feinstein has a *teshuvah* in which he considers implementing this in practice as a solution for a *mamzer*, with all the *halachos* attending such an arrangement — the *kiddushin* would not be effected with the *shifcha*, she is *koneh* as a *shifcha*, the same *halachos* of *hilchos niddah* apply since a *shifcha kena'anit*'s obligations in *mitzvos* are equivalent to a woman's, and the children may be freed immediately upon birth without waiting for the mother to be freed.

## Ten Yuchasin

### Categories List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה יוּחֲסִים עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל: כָּהֲנֵי, לְוִיֵּי, יִשְׂרְאֵלֵי, חֲלָלֵי, גֵּירֵי, וַחֲרוֹרֵי, מַמְזֵירֵי, נְתִינֵי, שְׁתוּקֵי, וַאֲסוּפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There were **ten** categories of **lineage,** with varying restrictions on marriage, among the Jews who **ascended from Babylonia** to Eretz Yisrael with Ezra before the building of the Second Temple. They are as follows: **Priests; Levites; Israelites; priests disqualified due to flawed lineage [*ḥalalim*]; converts, and emancipated slaves; *mamzerim*; Gibeonites,** i.e., the descendants of the Gibeonites who converted in the time of Joshua; **children of unknown paternity [*shetuki*]; and foundlings.**

The next *mishnah* opens a new *perek* continuing the discussion of *yuchasin* — lineage categories — and their consequences for marriage eligibility. *Asara yuchasin alu miBavel* — ten categories of lineage came up from Babylon with Ezra. The ten are: *kohanei, leviyei, yisraeli* — Kohanim, Levi'im, Yisraelim — *challalim* (children of a Kohen from a forbidden union for *kehuna*, who lack *kedushat kehuna*), *geirim* (converts), *charurei* (freed slaves), *mamzeirim*, *nesinim* (Gibeonites, subject to David HaMelech's *gezera* prohibiting intermarriage within the Jewish nation), *shtuki*, and *asufi*. All of these categories were known and identified among those who made *aliyah* with Ezra.

### First Tier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּהֲנֵי לְוִיֵּי וְיִשְׂרְאֵלֵי – מוּתָּרִין לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna proceeds to detail their *halakhot*: With regard to **priests, Levites, and Israelites,** it is **permitted** for men and women in these categories **to marry one another.**

*Kohanei, leviyei, va'yisraeli mutarin lavo ze baze* — Kohanim, Levi'im, and Yisraelim may all intermarry freely with one another. This group constitutes the first tier.

### Second Tier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְוִיֵּי יִשְׂרְאֵלֵי חֲלָלֵי גֵּירֵי וַחֲרוֹרִי – מוּתָּרִין לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Levites** who are not priests, **Israelites, *ḥalalim*, converts, and emancipated slaves,** it is **permitted** for men and women in these categories **to marry one another.**

*Levi, yisrael, challali, geirei, ve'charurei mutarin lavo ze baze* — Levi'im, Yisraelim, *challalim*, *geirim*, and freed slaves may all intermarry with one another. The reason Kohanim are excluded: a Kohen may not marry a *chalalah*, and by extension is prohibited from marrying a *gerusha*, a *zonah*, and categories assimilated thereto. A *geir* and an *eved meshuchrar*, having presumably had a sexual life outside of marriage before their entry into full Jewish status, are placed by *halakha* into the category analogous to *zonah* for purposes of *kehuna* marriage prohibitions. Hence this second tier excludes Kohanim, but all others in it may intermarry freely.

### Third Tier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּירֵי וַחֲרוֹרֵי מַמְזֵירֵי וּנְתִינֵי שְׁתוּקֵי וַאֲסוּפֵי – כּוּלָּם מוּתָּרִין לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵם: שְׁתוּקִי כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מַכִּיר אֶת אִמּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מַכִּיר אֶת אָבִיו, אֲסוּפִי – כֹּל שֶׁנֶּאֱסָף מִן הַשּׁוּק, וְאֵינוֹ מַכִּיר לֹא אֶת אָבִיו וְלֹא אִמּוֹ. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל הָיָה קוֹרֵא לַשְּׁתוּקִי: ״בְּדוּקִי״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **converts, and emancipated slaves, *mamzerim*, and Gibeonites, children of unknown paternity [*shetuki*], and foundlings,** it is **permitted** for **all of** the men and women in these categories **to marry one another. And these are** the last two categories: **A *shetuki*** is **any** person **who knows** the identity of **his mother but does not know** the identity of **his father. A foundling** is **anyone who was collected from the marketplace and doesn't know** the identity of his parents, **neither** that of **his father nor** that of **his mother.** These two categories are people whose status is uncertain; they may be *mamzerim*. **Abba Shaul would call a *shetuki*** by the label of ***beduki*.**

*Geyri ve-charurei im mamzer ve-netini, shtuki va-asufi, kulam mutarin lavo ze baze* — converts and freed slaves may marry *mamzerim*, *nesinim*, *shtuki*, and *asufi*, and all of these may intermarry with one another. The rationale is rooted in the verse *lo yavo mamzer bikhal Hashem* (and similarly *lo yavo lo bikhal Hashem* by the descendants of *mamzerim*): the inner circle of *kehal Hashem* — as understood to exclude those who are not Jewish by birth — means that *geirim* and *meshuchrarim*, despite being fully Jewish, are considered outside the innermost *kehal Hashem* in this restricted sense, and therefore may marry *mamzerim*. This is a striking *halacha* and not without theological complexity, given the competing *pasuk* of *mishpat echad yihyeh lachem kager kaezrach ha'aretz*. But it is the operative *halakha*, and it actually functions as a *kula* in practice for these categories.

### Shtuki Definition

*Ve-eilu hem shtuki* — who is a *shtuki*? It is one who knows who his mother is but does not know his father. The etymology is from *shtak* — silence or "be quiet" — because when the child asks about his father, the mother says nothing, or perhaps tells the child not to ask, or redirects him from the man he might have identified as his father. Rashi notes an alternative reading: the mother may have said *shh* to the child when he pointed at the man currently married to her, indicating that person is not his father.

### Asufi Definition

*Asufi* is *kol sheneesaf min hashuk* — whoever was gathered in from the marketplace. This is the foundling, the child left in a basket and collected, who does not know either parent.

### Safek Mamzer

Both the *shtuki* and *asufi* are treated as *safek mamzerim*. One might wonder: in the case of a *shtuki*, the mother is not a married woman — there is no accusation of adultery. How could the child be a *mamzer* at all, since *mamzerut* requires a union forbidden under penalty of *karet* or death? The answer is that the father could have been a *mamzer* himself — and this is indeed the primary *choshesh*, not incest, which would be a far rarer concern.

The general principle of *yuchasin* operates differently from other areas of *halakha*. In other domains, we often rely on *rov* — the statistical majority — to resolve doubts. Here, we are far more demanding about certainty of lineage. Once a *safek mamzer* status is assigned, the individual is considered a *safek mamzer* and may not marry a *Yisrael* — but may marry a *vadai mamzer* or a *geir*. Interestingly, what appears to be a stringency — calling someone a *safek mamzer* — operates simultaneously as a *kula*: it opens the door to marriages with *mamzerim* and *geirim* that would otherwise be unavailable if the person were simply considered a full *Yisrael*.

The further asymmetry is worth noting: a *safek mamzer* cannot marry a full *Yisrael*, but nor can two *safek mamzerim* marry each other without difficulty, since one might be a *mamzer* and the other not. In practice, they may marry *geirim* and *mamzerim* of either sex.

### Abba Shaul

Abba Shaul is troubled by the label *shtuki* itself. *Abba Shaul haya korei l'shtuki beduki* — Abba Shaul would call the *shtuki* not *shtuki* but *beduki* — from *bedika*, investigation. Rather than accepting the silence as definitive, Abba Shaul's name for this person suggests that the proper response to uncertainty is inquiry and investigation. The *Gemara* will elaborate on what that means in practice.

## Babylonian Ascent

### Aliyah Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה יוּחֲסִין עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל. מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל״? נִתְנֵי ״הָלְכוּ לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל״! מִילְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְקַמְתָּ וְעָלִיתָ אֶל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִבְחַר ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ גָּבוֹהַּ מִכׇּל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל גְּבוֹהָה מִכׇּל הָאֲרָצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: There were **ten** categories of **lineage** among the Jews who **ascended from Babylonia.** The Gemara asks: **Why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** the phrase: **Ascended from Babylonia?** Why was it important for the *tanna* to specify their place of origin? **Let him teach** that they **went to Eretz Yisrael.** The Gemara answers: **He teaches us a matter in passing, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall arise and go up to the place that the Lord, your God, shall choose"** (Deuteronomy 17:8). This **teaches that the Temple is higher than all of Eretz Yisrael,** which is why the verse speaks of ascending from the cities of Eretz Yisrael to the Temple. **And** it teaches that **Eretz Yisrael is higher than all of the lands.**

The *mishnah* states *asara yuchasin alu miBavel* — ten categories of lineage *came up* from Babylon with Ezra. The *Gemara* asks: *mai irya d'tani alu miBavel* — why does the *tanna* use the language of "came up"? Why is traveling from Babylon to Eretz Yisrael described as going *up*?

### Eretz Yisrael Higher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ גָּבוֹהַּ מִכׇּל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״דִּבְרֵי רִיבֹת בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ וְקַמְתָּ וְעָלִיתָ״, אֶלָּא אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל גְּבוֹהָה מִכׇּל אֲרָצוֹת מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״לָכֵן הִנֵּה יָמִים בָּאִים נְאֻם ה׳ וְלֹא יֹאמְרוּ עוֹד חַי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלָה אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם. כִּי אִם חַי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלָה וַאֲשֶׁר הֵבִיא אֶת זֶרַע בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ צָפוֹנָה וּמִכֹּל הָאֲרָצוֹת אֲשֶׁר הִדַּחְתִּים שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, the Temple is higher than all of Eretz Yisrael. This is** derived from **that which is written:** **"Matters of controversy within your gates, and you shall arise and go up** to the place that the Lord, your God, shall choose" (Deuteronomy 17:8), indicating that the Temple, the place that God chose, is higher than all other cities in Eretz Yisrael. **But from where do we** derive the claim **that Eretz Yisrael is higher than all** other **lands?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "Therefore behold, the days are coming, says the Lord, when they shall no more say: As the Lord lives, Who brought up the children of Israel out of the land of Egypt, but: As the Lord lives, Who brought up and Who led the seed of the house of Israel out of the north country, and from all the countries where I had driven them"** (Jeremiah 23:7–8). The phrase "Who brought up" indicates that Eretz Yisrael is higher than all the other lands from where God will bring the Jewish people.

The answer: *milta gavita kamashma lan* — it is telling us something about the relative sanctity and elevation of Eretz Yisrael. As the *braita* derives from the verse *v'kamta v'alita el hamakom asher yivchar Hashem Elokecha* — "you shall rise and go up to the place that God has chosen" — we learn that the *Beit HaMikdash* is higher than all of Eretz Yisrael, and Eretz Yisrael is higher than all other lands. The verse from *Yirmiyahu* further confirms this: God says that people will no longer praise Him solely as the One who brought Israel up from Egypt, but as the One who brought them up from all the northern lands and all the places to which they had been scattered — *asher he'ela*, "who brought them up" — meaning all those other lands require going *up* to reach Eretz Yisrael.

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״עָלוּ מִבָּבֶל״? נִתְנֵי ״עָלוּ לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל״! מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא עָלָה עֶזְרָא מִבָּבֶל עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ כְּסוֹלֶת נְקִיָּה וְעָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If that is what the mishna wants to teach, **why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach: Ascended from Babylonia? Let him teach: Ascended to Eretz Yisrael.** The Gemara answers that the wording of the mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says: Ezra did not ascend from Babylonia until he made it like fine flour,** free of bran, i.e., he ensured that the lineage of those remaining was unsullied, and selected all of those in Babylonia who were of questionable lineage, **and** then **he ascended** with them to Eretz Yisrael.

But why specifically mention that they came from *Bavel*? *Mesayei lei l'Rebbi Elazar* — the *mishnah*'s phrasing supports the view of Rabbi Elazar: *lo ala Ezra miBavel ad she'asah k'solet nekiyah v'ala* — Ezra did not make *aliyah* from Babylon until he had made it like sifted flour — *solet nekiyah* — and then went up. That is, Ezra cleaned out Babylon of all those of questionable lineage before departing, so that what remained in Babylon was the refined, identified population, and what he brought to Eretz Yisrael was equally identifiable and categorized.

### Abbayyei Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: ״עָלוּ״ – מֵאֵילֵיהֶם, תְּנַן. וְרָבָא אָמַר: ״הֶעֱלוּם״ תְּנַן. וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא עָלָה עֶזְרָא מִבָּבֶל עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ כְּסוֹלֶת נְקִיָּה וְעָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that *amora'im* had a dispute with regard to this matter. **Abaye said: We learned** in the mishna that there were ten categories of lineage among the Jews who **ascended,** meaning that they ascended **of their own** accord. **And Rava said: We learned:** Ezra **brought them up,** against their will. The Gemara explains: **And they disagree about** the statement **of Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says: Ezra did not ascend from Babylonia until he made it like fine flour, and** only then **he ascended.**

*Abaye amar alu me-aleihem* — Abaye holds that they went up on their own initiative. According to Abaye, Ezra identified the lineage categories of the various families in Babylon; once identified as *challal*, *mamzer*, *nasin*, and so on, those individuals found their social position in Babylon untenable and chose on their own to join Ezra on his *aliyah*. Ezra did not force them out; he simply made their status known, and self-interest did the rest.

### Rava Position

*Rava amar he-alum tnan* — Rava holds that the *mishnah*'s language, *alu* (they went up), implies they were *brought up* — that Ezra actively compelled them to leave, against their will if necessary. Ezra controlled the entire process of identification and removal.

### Removal Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי – לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. רָבָא – אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: אַפְרוֹשֵׁי אַפְרוֹשִׁינְהוּ וּמִנַּפְשַׁיְיהוּ סְלִיקוּ, וּמָר סָבַר: בְּעַל כֻּרְחַיְיהוּ אַסּוֹקִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye does not** accept the statement **of Rabbi Elazar,** since he maintains that they ascended of their own free will, whereas **Rava does accept** the statement **of Rabbi Elazar.** Or, **if you wish, say that everyone accepts** the statement **of Rabbi Elazar, and here they disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage,** Abaye, **holds** that Ezra first **separated** the members of the community with flawed lineage, **and** they subsequently **ascended** to Eretz Yisrael out **of their own desire. And** one **Sage,** Rava, **holds** that **he brought them up against their will.**

A further layer of disagreement: *iy ba'is eima d'kulei alma is lehu d'Rabbi Elazar* — one can argue that everyone agrees with Rabbi Elazar's essential point, that Ezra's agenda was to clean out Babylon. The dispute is only about the *mechanism*: *mar savar afrushei afrashunihu, m'nafsheihu siliku* — one view is that Ezra merely separated and identified them, and they departed of their own accord; *u-mar savar b'al korcha asikinuhu* — the other view is that he physically compelled their departure.

### Sifted Flour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: ״עָלוּ״, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל אֲרָצוֹת עִיסָּה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עִיסָּה לְבָבֶל. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: ״הֶעֱלוּם״, מִידָּע יַדְעִינְהוּ! נְהִי דְּיָדְעִי לְהָהוּא דָּרָא, לְדָרָא אַחֲרִינֵי לָא יָדְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the mishna means that **they ascended** of their own accord, without any distinction between the types of people who came, **this is** the reason **that Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The lineage of residents of **all lands is muddled** compared **to** that of **Eretz Yisrael, and** the lineage of residents of **Eretz Yisrael is muddled** compared **to** that of **Babylonia.** As the people came of their own accord, there was a lack of oversight concerning who married whom. **But according to the one who says** that **he brought them up,** and Ezra identified and selected all those who came with him, the Sages of the time **knew** the lineage of all who came and would have been careful not to allow any forbidden marriages, so why is the lineage of residents of Eretz Yisrael considered to be muddled compared to that of Babylonia? The Gemara answers: **Though** the status of those who came **was known to that generation, it was not known to other** later **generations.**

*Kol ha'aratzot issah l'Eretz Yisrael, v'Eretz Yisrael issah l'Bavel* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel articulates the hierarchy: all lands are like *issah* (unkneaded dough — mixed, undifferentiated) relative to Eretz Yisrael, and Eretz Yisrael itself is like *issah* relative to Babylon. Babylon, having been purged of those of questionable lineage by Ezra, is the most refined — *solet nekiyah*, sifted flour. Eretz Yisrael, while more refined than other lands, still has admixture. All other lands are the most mixed.

This makes intuitive sense according to Abaye's view: since those who went up did so voluntarily after being identified, the process in Eretz Yisrael was less controlled. Ezra could not maintain the same oversight once they had gone up, and over time the clarity of lineage in Eretz Yisrael became diluted again. Babylon, from which they had been removed, remained clean.

### Later Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״עָלוּ״, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֶקְבְּצֵם אֶל הַנָּהָר הַבָּא עַל אַחֲוָה וַנַּחֲנֶה שָׁם יָמִים שְׁלֹשָׁה וָאָבִינָה בָעָם וּבַכֹּהֲנִים וּמִבְּנֵי לֵוִי לֹא מָצָאתִי שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: **Granted, according to the one who says** that **they ascended** of their own accord, **this is** the meaning of the verse **that is written** in the book of Ezra: **"And I gathered them together to the river that runs to Ahava, and we camped there for three days; and I viewed the people and the priests, and found there none of the sons of Levi"** (Ezra 8:15), as it was necessary for Ezra to clarify the identity of the people traveling to Eretz Yisrael.

The *Gemara* addresses the tension in Rava's view: if Ezra forcibly controlled the entire process, why isn't Eretz Yisrael also *solet nekiyah*? *Midei yad'inhu l'hahu dara* — in *that* generation, everyone knew everyone's identity. But *l'dara achrina lo yadi* — in subsequent generations, the clarity was lost. Over time, the categories blurred in Eretz Yisrael even though they had been crisply identified when those people arrived with Ezra. Babylon, having been emptied entirely of those categories, remained permanently clean — there was no one left there of questionable status to create confusion. Eretz Yisrael absorbed these people, and once the generation that knew them personally had passed, confusion set in again.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הֶעֱלוּם״, הָא מִיזְהָר זְהִירִי? נְהִי דְּאִיזְּהוּר בִּפְסוּלִים, בִּכְשֵׁירִים לָא אִיזְדְּהוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that **he brought them up, they were careful** to classify the lineage of the people before they left for Eretz Yisrael, so why was it necessary for him to clarify the matter by the riverside? The Gemara answers: **Though they were careful with regard to** people of **flawed** lineage before they left for Eretz Yisrael, **with regard to** people of **unflawed** lineage **they were not careful** to clarify the precise lineage of each of them earlier, and they did this by the riverside.

The *Gemara* also addresses a difficulty with Rava's view from the book of Ezra itself: when Ezra encamped at the river Ahava and investigated the people with him, he found that among the *kohanim* and *levi'im*, there were those who could not serve in the *Beit HaMikdash*. If Ezra had been in full control of identifying everyone from the outset, how did this come as a surprise? The answer: *nehi de-izhar bi-psulim* — Ezra had indeed identified who among the people bore stigmatized *yuchasin* statuses (*mamzer*, *nasin*, etc.), but what he discovered at the river was a different matter entirely — Kohanim and Levi'im who, while not bearing those general *psul* categories, were disqualified specifically from Temple service. That was a separate inquiry he had not yet completed. The two categories of disqualification are distinct, and discovering one does not mean the other had been addressed.
