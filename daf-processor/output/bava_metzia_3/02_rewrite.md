# Bava Metzia 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oath as Prevention

### Why Shvuah Required

The *gemara* on 3a opens not with a focus on why the disputed *tallis* is split between the two claimants, but rather with the prior question of why a *shvuah* is required at all. The groundwork laid in previous discussion established that a person tends to rationalize his conduct — what the *gemara* calls *moreh heter* — and that the *shvuah* serves to penetrate that rationalization and move the claimant toward truthfulness.

### Rationalizing & Ben Nanas

The *gemara* compared the *tallis* case to the position of Ben Nanas, who objected to any arrangement that would place one of the parties in the position of taking a demonstrably false *shvuah*. The concern operates on two levels: we do not want the individual to commit *shvuas sheker*, and we do not want the *beis din* to preside over a proceeding in which one party is clearly lying. However, Ben Nanas's case involved a situation where *vadai* — certainly — one party was making a false oath. The *gemara* noted that here, by contrast, it is not definitively established that either claimant is lying.

### Sumchos & Claims

The *gemara* then raised the position of Sumchos, who holds that in a case of disputed ownership one splits the disputed amount without requiring a *shvuah* at all. If Sumchos would not require a *shvuah* in his characteristic case — for example, when two parties dispute when a calf was born relative to a transaction — why should a *shvuah* be required here? The *gemara*'s initial answer was that Sumchos's case rests on an objective basis for each party's claim, giving each a legitimate standing that justifies splitting. But this objective basis, the *gemara* countered, explains why we split — it does not explain why we forgo the *shvuah*. If anything, the *shvuah* might be more compelling in Sumchos's case: since one party almost certainly does not deserve the award, the *shvuah* might force that party to admit. So the logic of "objective claim" cannot explain Sumchos's dispensing with a *shvuah*. The conclusion was that if Sumchos forgoes the *shvuah* in his case, he should also forgo it here — which would mean our *mishnah* does not follow Sumchos.

### Rabbinic Takanah

The *gemara* resolves this by introducing a new framework. Five lines from the top of 3a, the *gemara* states: *afilu teima Sumchos* — even Sumchos would agree that a *shvuah* is required in the *tallis* case, and the reason is that this *shvuah* is entirely rabbinic in character. The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yochanan: *shvuah zo takanas chachamim hi* — this oath is a rabbinic enactment. And its purpose is explicitly preventative: *shelo yehei kol echad ve'echad holech ve'tokef be'taliso shel chaveiro ve'omer sheli hi* — so that people will not simply go and seize another's garment and claim it as their own.

Without this *takanah*, a person of sufficient *chutzpah* need only grab hold of another's coat, assert ownership, and walk away with half of it. The system would be trivially exploitable. *Chazal* therefore instituted the *shvuah* not as a truth-finding mechanism — not as part of the *din* — but as a prophylactic social control, a deterrent against opportunistic seizure.

### Shlomo Analogy

This preventative character also explains why Sumchos's case differs. When two parties dispute the timing of a calf's birth relative to a commercial transaction, there is no realistic concern that people will manufacture such disputes for strategic advantage. The scenario cannot be engineered. There, the *shvuah* can only be justified as a tool of *din* — of reaching the truth — and Sumchos, who does not favor that use of the *shvuah*, dispenses with it. But the *tallis* case is different: it is precisely the kind of dispute that can be manufactured with minimal effort. The rabbinic *takanah* targets this vulnerability. Even Sumchos, who generally eschews the *shvuah* as a *din* instrument, would endorse it here as a *takanah* against exploitation — just as one might recall the lesson of *Shlomo Hamelech*: the legal system cannot simply invite such easy manipulation without consequence.

## Division vs Escrow

### Rabbi Yosi Problem

The *gemara* now shifts its focus from the *shvuah* to the other half of the *mishnah*'s ruling: the decision to split the *tallis*. Is *yehaloku* — division — really the appropriate resolution? Or should the disputed object be held in escrow? This question is introduced through the position of Rabbi Yosi, who raises a pointed objection to a split: *im kein mah hifsid ha'ramai* — if you simply divide, what does the deceiver lose?

### Deposit Case

The case that frames Rabbi Yosi's concern involves a *shomer* — a trustee — holding deposits from two people. One depositor left one hundred *zuz* and the other left two hundred *zuz*. When both come to retrieve their money, each claims to have deposited the larger amount. The trustee, having failed to keep adequate records, cannot remember who gave what. The *chachamim* rule that each party receives the hundred *zuz* that is certainly his, while the disputed third hundred is placed in escrow — *yehei munach ad she'yavo Eliyahu* — held until Elijah arrives, meaning until the matter can somehow be resolved or one party concedes.

### Escrow Leverage

Rabbi Yosi objects to this arrangement. The liar, in the *chachamim*'s scheme, loses nothing: he walked in claiming two hundred, he walks out with the hundred that was always his, and the disputed hundred sits in escrow. He has no incentive to admit. Rabbi Yosi's counter-proposal is therefore: *hakol yehei munach ad she'yavo Eliyahu* — place all three hundred in escrow, including the hundred that is certainly each party's. By doing so, you impose a real cost on the deceiver. The liar, who is motivated by greed, may crack first and admit the truth in order to retrieve his own money. The innocent party, who is telling the truth, will hold out. The escrow functions not as permanent resolution, but as coercive leverage.

It must be conceded that this strategy is imperfect. Placing undisputed funds in escrow also penalizes the truthful party. And, as with any system of imposed pressure, the guilty party will not always break first. But Rabbi Yosi judges this outcome preferable to one that costs the liar nothing at all.

### Testing Rabbanan

The *gemara* then asks: given that our *mishnah* rules to split the *tallis*, does this mean our *mishnah* cannot be reconciled with Rabbi Yosi? And further, can it even be reconciled with the *rabbanun*, who themselves rule that the disputed third hundred goes into escrow? The *gemara* poses a sharp challenge: the *tallis* is itself the disputed item. Just as the *rabbanun* hold the third hundred — the item in genuine dispute — in escrow, they should by the same logic hold the *tallis* in escrow. *Hani nami ke'shar dami de'sfeka hi* — this *tallis* is equivalent to that disputed hundred; it is the item about which there is genuine uncertainty.

### Split Possible Test

The *gemara* resolves this challenge on behalf of the *rabbanun*: *hai mai* — what kind of question is that? In the case of the three hundred *zuz*, it is certain that the third hundred belongs to exactly one of the two parties — *vadai hai mane de'chad minaihu*. A fifty-fifty split would be a patently false judgment, which is why the *rabbanun* resort to escrow. But in the *tallis* case, it is genuinely possible — *ika le'meimar de'tarvaihu* — that both parties actually picked it up simultaneously. Where the split is possibly true, the *rabbanun* say *yachaloku* with a *shvuah*. Where the split is patently false, the *rabbanun* say *yehei munach*. The distinction is whether the division corresponds to any possible truth on the ground.

This also explains the reasoning behind Rashi's alternative framing: in the version where each claimant says "I wove it" or "I inherited it from my grandfather," one of them is definitively lying in a way that cannot be reconciled with a fifty-fifty ownership split. That formulation would push toward escrow rather than division.

### Yosi & Tallis

The *gemara* now asks whether our *mishnah* can be reconciled with Rabbi Yosi himself. The challenge is powerful. If Rabbi Yosi is prepared to place even *undisputed* money — the hundred *zuz* that is certainly each man's — into escrow as a coercive strategy, then *a fortiori* he should be prepared to place disputed property into escrow. In the *tallis* case, the entire *tallis* is disputed. Rabbi Yosi's logic of *mah hifsid ha'ramai* — what does the liar lose? — applies here with full force. If you simply split the *tallis*, the liar walks away with half of something to which he had no claim whatsoever. He risked nothing and gained something. Under Rabbi Yosi's framework, that is untenable, and he should certainly demand escrow here.

### Not Definite Lying

The *gemara* answers: *afilu teima Rabbi Yosi* — even Rabbi Yosi can be reconciled with our *mishnah*. The reason Rabbi Yosi employs escrow in the deposit case is that there *vadai ika ramai* — it is certain that one party is lying. Someone definitely deposited only one hundred and is claiming two hundred. But in the *tallis* case, *mi yeimar de'ika ramai* — who says there is definitely a deceiver? *Eima tarvaihu bahadi hadadi agbuha* — perhaps both parties genuinely picked it up at the same time. If there is no definite liar, the coercive rationale for escrow does not apply.

### Liar Loses Test

The *gemara* adds a second, and arguably more decisive, answer: *kanis lei Rabbi Yosi le'ramai ki heihi de'lodei* — Rabbi Yosi's escrow strategy works because it imposes a real loss on the deceiver to coerce his admission. In the deposit case, placing all three hundred in escrow means the liar is cut off from the hundred that is genuinely his — he suffers. That deprivation is what creates the incentive to come clean. But in the *tallis* case, the liar has no pre-existing stake in the object. It was found, or it was grabbed. Putting it in escrow takes nothing away from him; there is nothing of his being withheld. *Mai pseda is'lei de'lodei* — what loss would he suffer that would prompt him to admit? The escrow mechanism, stripped of its coercive bite, accomplishes nothing.

Tosafot, it should be noted, understands these as two criteria that must both be met for escrow to be warranted: there must be a definite liar, *and* the escrow must impose a genuine cost on that liar. If either condition is absent, escrow is not the appropriate remedy.

There is a further wrinkle the *gemara* presses: what about the case in the *mishnah* involving a disputed purchase, where both parties handed money to a merchant? There, one might argue, escrow would impose a real cost: each party's payment to the merchant could be frozen along with the item in dispute. The *gemara* responds — *ela mechvarta ke'dishinan me'ikara* — the cleaner answer remains the original: the operative consideration is whether there is definitively a liar. That is the primary criterion.

## Shopkeeper Case

### Shopkeeper Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a new case to test these principles further, a case referenced already in the previous day's discussion. A *ba'al habayis* — an employer — instructs a *chanvani*, a shopkeeper who runs a nearby food establishment, to provide meals to his workers as part of their compensation, with the employer to pay the shopkeeper. At the end of the arrangement, the shopkeeper presents his bill and the workers claim they never received any food. The *halacha* in such a case is: *zeh nishba ve'noteil ve'zeh nishba ve'noteil* — each party takes a *shvuah* and collects from the employer. The shopkeeper swears he provided the meals and collects; the workers swear they received nothing and collect. The employer pays both.

### Why Not Escrow

This case demands explanation. As the *gemara* frames it, one of the two claiming parties is definitively lying — *vadai ika ramai*. Moreover, it is not possible for a split to be the correct judgment: either the shopkeeper fed them or he did not. Both conditions that the *gemara* identified as pushing toward escrow are present. Why, then, does the *gemara* not say: take the disputed wages, place them in escrow as *yehei munach ad she'yavo Eliyahu*, and let the parties sort it out?

### Employer's Fault

The *chanvani*'s position is as follows: he says to the employer — *shlichusa didach avdit*, I did exactly what you sent me to do. His dealings are with the employer, not with the workers. When the employer asks why the shopkeeper should be held responsible for a worker's false claim, the shopkeeper responds: *ant heimanteih* — you implicitly trusted him. You never instructed me to pay them in front of witnesses, *de'lo amarta li be'sahadei hav leih*. You set up an arrangement with no evidentiary protections whatsoever. If you wanted to guard against fraudulent claims, you should have required witnesses at the point of payment. By failing to do so, you effectively told me: feed them, I trust your word, and I trust theirs. The consequences of that unprotected system fall on you.

### Worker Defense

The worker makes a parallel argument. He says: *ana avdi avidta gabach* — I did a full day's work for you. You told me to go to this shopkeeper and receive food as part of my wages, and he gave me nothing. *Mai it li gabei chanvani* — what do I have to do with the shopkeeper? My employment relationship is with you. You have no evidence that you paid me. If you wanted to be able to prove payment, you should have structured the arrangement accordingly. His *shvuah* against a person with whom he has no direct dealings means little to him: *af al gav de'mishtatva li lo meheiman li* — even if he takes an oath, I have no reason to trust him; I have no relationship with him.

### Both Collect

The resolution follows directly: *hilchach shneihem nishba'in ve'notlin mi'ba'al habayis* — therefore both swear and collect from the employer. Since the employer structured a system that offered no protection against competing claims, and since neither the shopkeeper nor the worker can reasonably be expected to bear the cost of the employer's poor arrangement, the employer absorbs the loss on both ends. This is not standard escrow logic, nor standard *yehaloku* logic. It is a distinct category: where the party who bears responsibility for having created an unprotected system is the one who suffers the consequences.

## Decision Tree

### Muchzak Status

The *gemara*'s extended discussion through these cases makes it possible to articulate a decision framework for how *beis din* adjudicates when neither party's claim is proved and neither holds a clear presumption. Tosafot notes at the outset that our *gemara* deals specifically with cases where both parties enjoy a degree of *muchzak* — physical possession or the equivalent. The *tallis* case involves both parties holding the garment; in the deposit case, the trustee holds the funds on behalf of both parties, which Tosafot treats as a form of shared *muchzak*. Where neither party has any *muchzak* at all — such as a boat sitting in a harbor unclaimed — the *gemara* elsewhere rules *kol de'alim gevar*: let the stronger prevail, which may mean the court simply declines to adjudicate until one party establishes a better claim.

### Possibly True?

Assuming *muchzak* is present, the first question to ask is: is the proposed split *possibly* true? Can we conceive of a factual scenario in which both parties genuinely deserve half?

### Tallis: Possible

In the *tallis* case, the answer is yes. Both parties may have genuinely picked up the garment simultaneously. The split corresponds to a possible truth. In that case, the ruling is *yehaloku* — divide it — with a *shvuah*.

### Deposit: Not Possible

In the disputed deposit case, the answer is no. The third hundred *zuz* belongs, in reality, to exactly one of the two claimants. A fifty-fifty split of that hundred would be a patently false judgment. There is no factual scenario under which each party deserves half of that specific amount. The ruling is therefore *yehei munach* — place it in escrow.

### Equal Claims Test

The principle that emerges is: split only when both parties have an equal, legitimate claim that could conceivably be correct. Where the split would be patently wrong — where there is no possible truth in which it is the just outcome — do not split. Put the disputed property in escrow and wait. The *chanvani* case is sui generis: there, the employer's failure to protect his own interests generates an obligation to pay both parties in full, which is neither a split of the disputed amount nor a straightforward escrow, but rather the employer's self-imposed liability.

## Modeh BeHiktzat

### Rabbi Chiya Braita

Having worked through the logic of division and escrow, the *gemara* now shifts gears entirely and examines the *shvuah* from a different angle — not as a *takanah* against grabbing, and not as a preventative measure, but as a biblical *shvuah* of *modeh be'miktzat*, partial admission. Rabbi Chiya, understood to be the editor of the *Tosefta*, taught the following *braita*: if Reuven says to Shimon "you owe me one hundred *zuz*" and Shimon says "I owe you nothing," and then witnesses come and testify that Shimon owes at least fifty, Shimon must pay the fifty and take a *shvuah* on the remaining fifty — *noten lo chamishim ve'yishava al ha'she'ar*.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

Where does the obligation to take a *shvuah* come from in this witnesses case? Rabbi Chiya derives it through a *kal vachomer*: *shelo tehei hoda'as piv gedolah me'ha'adat eidim* — the force of one's own admission should not be greater than the force of witness testimony. The classic *shvuah* of *modeh be'miktzat* is biblical: if I claim you owe me a hundred and you admit to fifty, you take a *shvuah* on the remainder. The reasoning is that partial admission provides an objective basis for believing the rest of the claim may also be true, and the *shvuah* addresses the outstanding dispute. If that logic works when the partial truth is established by the debtor's own mouth, it should work at least as well — and arguably more forcefully — when the partial truth is established by external witnesses, whose testimony is weightier than self-admission in obligating monetary payment.

### Objection to Oath

One might object, however, that the *kal vachomer* is not obvious at all. The standard explanation for why *modeh be'miktzat* generates a *shvuah* obligation is not merely evidentiary — it is psychological. Rabba's principle, which the *gemara* develops at length, holds that a debtor partially admitting is presumed to be a rationalizer, not an outright liar. The *shvuah* is tailored to that psychology: it breaks through the rationalization and forces full admission. If that is the underlying logic, then witnesses who establish partial liability tell us nothing about the debtor's state of mind. He may be a complete *ganav* who never intended to pay anything, and a *shvuah* may be entirely ineffective in his case.

### Tallis & Modeh

The *gemara* adds that Rabbi Chiya's teaching is not unprecedented — our own *mishnah* can itself be read as a case of *modeh be'miktzat*, and thus as a biblical *shvuah*. This is the *gemara*'s basis for having noted earlier that the *shvuah* in our *mishnah* is rabbinic: the *gemara* anticipated that there was a possible framework in which it would be treated as biblical.

### Possession as Witness

The comparison is striking. The *gemara* says: *keivan de'tafsi, anan sa'adei de'mai de'tafsi hai didei hu u'mai de'tafsi hai didei hu* — since each party is physically holding the *tallis*, our collective witness-like observation is that what each one holds is his. Physical possession functions as testimony to partial ownership. Each party is therefore in the position of someone who has just been proven — via something akin to witnesses — to own half. But each party claims the whole. So each party is, in effect, someone against whom there is evidence of partial claim validity who continues to deny the full claim against him. That generates the *shvuah*.

The *gemara* itself acknowledges the bizarreness of this framing. The "testimony" here precedes the claims. The evidence cuts symmetrically in both directions — each party's holding is simultaneously "testimony" for his claim and against the other's. Whether this is truly analogous to *modeh be'miktzat* or is merely a loose structural parallel is a question the *gemara* leaves open. What it establishes is the conceptual path by which our *mishnah* could be read as instantiating a biblical *shvuah*, and Rabbi Chiya's *braita* extends that same logic to the case of actual witnesses.

## Rationalizer Psychology

### Rabba's Principle

The *gemara* now develops at length the psychological premise underlying *modeh be'miktzat*, which was already alluded to above. Rabba asks: *mipnei mah amrah Torah modeh be'miktzat ha'tana'ah yishava* — why does the Torah require an oath when one partially admits? The question is sharp. One might have supposed a *migo* argument: since the debtor could have denied everything and walked free, his voluntary partial admission should be credited, not punished with a *shvuah*.

Rabba answers: *chazakah ein adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — there is a presumption that a person will not act with brazen shamelessness before someone who has done him the favor of lending him money. The debtor cannot bring himself to look the creditor in the eye and say "I never borrowed a thing from you." That degree of *chutzpah* is beyond him.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi and Tosafot differ on the precise basis of this inhibition. Rashi attributes it to the *tovah* — the kindness — the creditor extended by lending in the first place. Because the creditor did me a favor, I cannot be so brazen as to deny it entirely. Tosafot disagrees: the inhibition applies even in cases such as *shvuas ha'shomrim*, where the creditor is not doing the debtor a *tovah* — if anything, the *shomer* is doing the owner the favor of watching his property. Tosafot therefore holds that the inhibition is rooted not in gratitude but in a more general reluctance to lie brazenly to someone's face about something that person knows you are liable for, regardless of the underlying relationship.

### Partial Admission

Given this inhibition, why does the debtor not admit to the full amount? Rabba explains: *be'kulei ba'ei di'nichferei* — his better self wants to fully admit. The reason he does not fully admit is *ishtamutei hu de'ka mishtamit minei* — he is simply putting the creditor off. He does not have the cash on hand. He borrowed the money, the creditor has been pressing him, and he says to himself: *ad de'havu li zuzei u'far'ana lei* — let me get the money together and then I'll pay him off. He is not trying to steal; he is trying to buy time. He rationalizes: "I'll eventually pay the full amount, so it's not really lying, I just need a bit longer."

### Oath Breaks Rationalizat…

This is where the *shvuah* becomes decisive. Because the debtor is fundamentally a rationalizer — not a brazen thief — the *shvuah* cuts through his psychological maneuvering. *Ve'amar Rachmana rami shvua aleih ki heihi de'lodei lei be'kulei* — the Torah therefore imposes a *shvuah* on him precisely in order to force full admission. Confronted with the gravity of an oath, the rationalizer can no longer hide behind his excuses. This makes the *shvuah* a remarkably targeted instrument of moral and legal pressure, one whose efficacy depends entirely on the debtor being a rationalizer rather than an outright liar. The *mussar* dimension of this insight is hard to miss: the mechanism by which *halacha* breaks through self-deception is directly analogous to the process of honest *cheshbon ha'nefesh* — taking stock of oneself without the cushion of rationalization.

## Witnesses & Oath

### Kal Vachomer Stated

The *gemara* now states the *kal vachomer* of Rabbi Chiya formally. *Umai piv* — when it comes to one's own admission — *she'ein mechayvo mamon* — it does not obligate in monetary payment in all cases — yet *mechayvo shvuah* — it does generate a *shvuah* obligation. *Eidin she'mechayvin oso mamon* — witnesses, who can obligate monetary payment — *eino din she'mechayvin oso shvuah* — how much more so should they generate a *shvuah* obligation?

### Admission vs Kenas

The *gemara* immediately objects: what do you mean self-admission does not obligate monetary payment? *Hoda'as ba'al din ki'me'ah eidin dami* — one's own admission is like the testimony of a hundred witnesses! Of course self-admission obligates payment. The *gemara* clarifies: *mai mamon — kenas*. The reference is not to ordinary monetary obligations but to court-imposed fines — *kenas*. If a person admits to having stolen and would therefore owe the double payment (*kefel*), his self-admission obligates him to pay the principal but not the fine. The additional payment is a punitive sanction, not a debt, and as such it belongs to the category of punishments — like lashes or execution — that can only be imposed on the basis of external witnesses, not self-incrimination.

### Kenas Definition

A *kenas* is thus distinguished from standard *mamon*: it is a court-imposed sanction rather than a restitutive obligation. Because it is punitive in character, it requires external proof. A person cannot, through his own admission alone, obligate himself in a *kenas*, just as no court could execute a defendant on the basis of his own confession alone. Self-admission, for purposes of the *kal vachomer*, is therefore weaker than witness testimony specifically with respect to *kenas* liability.

### Korban Exception

The *gemara* presses further: is it really true that witnesses are always weightier than self-admission? It raises a counterexample. If a person admits that he unknowingly ate *chelev* — forbidden fat — *be'shogeg*, he brings a *korban chatas*. But if witnesses testify that he ate it while he denies it, the consensus view is that he does not bring a *korban*. Here, self-admission generates an obligation — *korban* — that witnesses cannot generate. Self-admission is therefore, in this domain, *more* weighty than witnesses. This would undermine the *kal vachomer*'s foundational premise.

### Rabbi Meir Korban

Rabbi Meir disagrees with the consensus. He argues: *im heviu shnayim li'dei mitah chamurah, lo yaviuhu li'dei korban ha'kal* — if two witnesses can bring a person to the gravest of punishments, capital execution, can they not certainly bring him to the comparatively minor obligation of a *korban*? This *kal vachomer* mirrors the logic of Rabbi Chiya's *kal vachomer* about *shvuah*: witnesses are always weightier, therefore wherever self-admission generates an obligation, witnesses do so as well and to a greater degree.

### Viduy Requirement

The Rabbis reject Rabbi Meir's position. Their argument is that *korban* is categorically different from punishments: it is not imposed on the basis of objective facts alone. Bringing a *korban* presupposes *viduy* — confession and acceptance of guilt. *Ve'hitvadah asher chata aleha* — the verse itself conditions atonement on admission. Without the penitent's own acknowledgment of his act, the *korban* has no foundation. Tosafot sharpens this by noting that the Rabbis' argument is not merely technical. Rather, the *korban* is not about establishing facts; it is about taking responsibility. The entire *korban* process begins with the person's own *viduy*. Witnesses cannot supply what only the person's own moral reckoning can provide. This is in direct contrast to Rabbi Meir, who appears to view the *korban* as an objective metaphysical remedy — a response to the rupture caused by sin, irrespective of whether the sinner has internalized his guilt.

### Chiya & Meir Align

The *gemara* resolves the apparent difficulty for Rabbi Chiya: *Rabbi Chiya ke'Rabbi Meir svira lei* — Rabbi Chiya's position aligns with that of Rabbi Meir. Both maintain that witnesses are always weightier than self-admission across all domains. Just as Rabbi Meir holds that witnesses can obligate a *korban* even without *viduy*, Rabbi Chiya holds that witnesses can generate a *shvuah* obligation through the *kal vachomer*. The two positions are of a piece: a consistent jurisprudential stance that external witness testimony is the weightier form of evidence and always produces at least the legal consequences that self-admission produces. The *gemara* will continue in the next session to develop the further implications of this dispute.
