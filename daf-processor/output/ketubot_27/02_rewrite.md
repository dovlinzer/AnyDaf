# Ketubot 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Besieged City

### Context & Overview

Daf 27 continues the tractate's sustained focus on questions of personal status (*ishus*), and specifically on the status of women. The preceding daf addressed the case of a woman taken as collateral for a loan and under what circumstances she might be assumed to have been raped or to have consented willingly — a discussion with deep resonances in the stories of Esther and Achashveirosh. Today we turn to a more overtly violent scenario: a city under siege, and the status of the women found within it when the besieging army breaks through.

### Mishna Text

The *mishna* opens with the phrase *ir she'kevashuha karkom* — a city that has been besieged and finally breached, with the invading army now inside. The word *karkom* itself is a subject of some interest; Rashi traces it to an Aramaic root, and it connotes a siege in which no one can escape.

### Kohanic Status

*Kol kohanot shenimtzu betochah psulot* — all wives of *kohanim* found in the city are invalidated to their husbands, on the presumption that they have been raped. The relevant concern here is specific to women who are married to, or who might wish to marry, a *kohen*: a woman who has had intercourse with a non-Jew, whether willingly or by force, is forbidden to a *kohen*. (She would still be permitted to eat *terumah* from her father's household if she is a *kohenet* by birth, and an Israelite woman who is raped does not become forbidden to her Israelite husband — but she is permanently disqualified from marrying a *kohen*.) The *mishna* therefore addresses precisely this population.

### Witness Exception

The *mishna* continues: *ve'im yesh lahen eidim* — but if there are witnesses that they were not raped, even normally invalid witnesses are believed. *Afilu eved afilu shifchah harei elu ne'emanim* — even a slave or slave woman. This echoes leniencies we have seen elsewhere in *Yevamot* regarding testimony about a potentially deceased husband, where a single witness, or even a normally disqualified witness, suffices.

The *mishna* closes with the statement *ve'ein ne'eman adam al yedei atzmo* — a person is not believed about himself. The shift to the masculine form is notable; the *gemara* will show that this applies not only to the woman testifying about herself, but also to a husband testifying on behalf of his wife — since his testimony in her favor is effectively testimony in his own interest.

One earlier *mishna* had already gone further, permitting two women to testify on behalf of each other even where reciprocity might be suspected. The *chiddush* there was the basic acceptance of women as valid witnesses in captivity cases — and the rule that when one witness says she was raped and another says she was not, we always follow the lenient position, even if that witness would normally be disqualified.

## Wine Question

### Contradiction Raised

The *gemara* opens with a *rominhu* — a challenge from a contradiction. A *baraita* discusses a marauding troop (*baleshet*) that enters a city. *Bishat shalom*, in peacetime, open barrels of wine (*chaviyot ptuchot*) are forbidden — we fear the soldiers helped themselves and their contact rendered the wine *stam yeinam* — while sealed barrels are permitted. But *bishat milchama*, in wartime, *eilu va'eilu mutarot* — even the open barrels are permitted, because *ein pnai l'nasech* — the soldiers do not have time for libation. The concern about deliberate idolatrous contact is set aside when troops are in the midst of active combat.

The difficulty is apparent: if soldiers at war have no time to attend to wine, how can the *mishna* assume they do have time to rape the women of a conquered city? The two rulings seem to pull in opposite directions.

### Priority Answer

Rav Mari offers the first resolution: *l'iba'el yesh pnai, l'nasech ein pnai* — for sexual violence there is always time; for wine libation there is not. Rape is a priority that soldiers will pursue even under combat conditions; attending to wine is not. This answer, while blunt, has considerable pragmatic truth to it.

### Country Distinction

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Eleazar in the name of Chizkiya offers a second answer: *kan b'charkom shel otah malchut, kan b'charkom shel malchut acheret* — it depends whether the city belongs to the same kingdom or to a different one. The question is how to understand this distinction.

Rashi reads it as follows: when fighting within one's own country — say, suppressing a provincial rebellion — the army exercises greater restraint, since the city is ultimately their own. They will neither plunder the wine nor rape the women. Against a foreign enemy, no such restraint applies, and both concerns come into force.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot reads the distinction in precisely the opposite direction, and this reading integrates more naturally with the *gemara*'s subsequent development. According to Tosafot, when fighting in enemy territory, the army is operating under urgent pressure — surrounded by hostile forces, unable to linger — and so there is no time even for rape, let alone wine. But when the fighting is within one's own country, where the army is secure, unhurried, and in command of the surrounding territory, there is ample time for both. The *issur* on the women therefore applies specifically when the city is *shel otah malchut* — one's own country — while in enemy territory, neither concern is operative.

Tosafot's reading is to be preferred because it better fits the *gemara*'s broader thrust, which is to progressively narrow the circumstances under which the *mishna*'s prohibition actually applies — a point that will become increasingly clear as we proceed.

## Escape Routes

### All Women Raped?

Even accepting that the prohibition applies in one's own country, the *gemara* raises a further challenge: *iy efshar d'lo arak chada minayhu* — is it really impossible that even one woman escaped? Why should we assume that every single woman in the city was raped? Given any realistic scenario, some women must have found a way out.

### Guards Prevent Escape

The *gemara* responds with the answer of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *keshe'mishmarot ro'ot zo et zo* — when the conquering army has set up overlapping patrols within the city, each watching the others, so that there is truly no avenue of escape.

### Guards Must Sleep

But the *gemara* presses further: *efshar de'lo naima porta* — is it truly impossible that even one guard dozed off? Surely at least one person on patrol must sleep at some point. Perfect surveillance is not humanly achievable.

### Alarm System

Rabbi Levi provides the definitive answer: *kigun de'mahaderah le'mata shushilta ve'kalba ve'gaza ve'avza* — they have ringed the city with chains, dogs, loose debris that rattles underfoot, and geese. The combination creates an alarm system so comprehensive that no one can slip through undetected even when a guard is momentarily inattentive. It is only under such extreme circumstances — total physical enclosure reinforced by an active alarm network — that the *mishna*'s presumption of rape holds.

The *gemara* has now done something remarkable: it has essentially made the *mishna* moot in any ordinary scenario. We begin with a presumption that all the women have been violated, and then the *gemara* immediately begins dismantling the conditions under which that presumption could ever actually apply.

### Tannaim Debate

Rabbi Abba bar Zavda reports a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah and the Rabbis on precisely this question. One position resolves the wine-and-rape contradiction purely through the distinction between one's own country and a foreign one — and, having made that distinction, does not require any further narrowing. In that country where the prohibition applies, we simply assume all the women were raped, full stop, without entertaining further questions about possible escape. *Ve'lo kashya lei ve'lo midi* — he is not troubled by the objection at all.

The other position, however, is genuinely troubled by the possibility of escape and can only sustain the *mishna*'s presumption in the extremely narrow case where the city has been completely sealed with the alarm system described by Rabbi Levi — chains, dogs, debris, and geese. Short of that, the concern is always alive that a given woman may have managed to escape or hide, and this possibility is sufficient to suspend the blanket prohibition.

## One Tunnel Rule

### One Tunnel Permits

Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Ashian states: *im yesh sham machvoah achas, matzelas al ha'kohanot kulan* — if there is even a single tunnel or hiding place in the city, it permits all the *kohanot*, all the wives of *kohanim*, even if the city is otherwise perfectly sealed. The reasoning is that since any particular woman might have been the one who used that hiding place, no individual woman can be presumed with certainty to have been among those violated.

### One Hiding Place

Rabbi Yirmiya — who had a characteristic fondness for probing borderline cases, testing the precise limits of legal principles — poses the obvious follow-up question: *eina machzekes ela achas, mahu* — what if the hiding place can only conceal one person? Is it still true that all the women are permitted, on the theory that each one might have been that single individual?

### Two Paths Analogy

The *gemara* draws a comparison to the classic case of the two paths (*shtei shevilin*): one path has a corpse buried across its width, rendering anyone who walks it *tamei meis*, while the other is *tahor* — but no one remembers which is which. Two people each walked one of the paths and then handled *tahor* food. Rabbi Yehuda rules: if each comes to the *beis din* separately to ask his ruling, *tahorot* — each is declared *tahor* individually, because each has a *safek* and, in certain domains, *safek tumah birshut ha'rabbim* is always *tahor*. But *sheneihem ke'achas* — if both arrive together and present the question as a unit, they are *tmeim*, because we cannot simultaneously declare both *tahor* when we know with certainty that one of them is *tamei*. Rabbi Yossi rules *tmeim* in both cases.

Rava (and some say Rabbi Yochanan) clarifies: when they come *be'vat achas*, at the same time, everyone agrees they are *tmeim*; when they come *be'zeh achar zeh*, truly sequentially, everyone agrees they are *tahorim*. The dispute concerns only the intermediate case: someone who comes and asks about both himself and his companion in one presentation — is that treated as simultaneous or sequential?

Tosafot's reading of the dispute is sharper: the intermediate case is where a person came on one day and received a ruling for himself, then returned the next day to ask about his companion. Can the *beis din* issue a second, contradictory ruling knowing what they already told this person? That is the question the *tannaim* debate.

### Rava's Resolution

The parallel to our case is then stated: *keivan d'shara lehu le'chulhu, ke'vat achas dami* — if we issue a ruling permitting all the women at once, it is as if they all came before us simultaneously. Can we give a blanket ruling that every woman is permitted when we know that not all of them could physically have been in that one hiding place?

### Suspicion vs. Certainty

The *gemara* rejects the analogy: *hachi hashta* — how can you compare the cases? *Hatam vadai ika tumah* — in the paths case, there is definite *tumah*; it is certain that one of the two walked across the buried corpse. *Hacha mi yeimar de'itmei* — here, who says any woman was actually raped? It is only a concern, a *chashasha* — we do not know for certain that every woman, or even most women, was violated. And since it is only suspicion rather than certainty that grounds the *issur*, we can freely apply the *machvoah* as a basis for leniency across the board, even if it cannot physically accommodate everyone. This is the principle of *bishvuya hekilu* — in captivity cases, the rabbis were lenient — operating through the recognition that the entire prohibition rests on doubt, not established fact.

## Woman's Statement

### Rav Ashi's Question

Rav Ashi poses a further question: a woman says *lo nechbeisi ve'lo nitmeisi* — I did not hide in a cellar, and I was not raped. She is neither claiming to have used the one tunnel nor to have escaped the city. She simply says nothing happened to her. Do we believe her?

The issue is whether she has a valid *migo*: since she could have said she was the one who hid in the tunnel — a claim we would have accepted — she is instead saying something weaker (she was not raped, without any explanation), and we ask whether that weaker claim is believed because she could have made the stronger one.

### Donkey Case

The *gemara* draws a parallel to the case of a man who rented a donkey and was told explicitly not to travel by the road of Nahar Pekod because of the water there, but to go instead by the road of Narsh. He went by Nahar Pekod anyway, and the donkey died. When he came before Rava, he admitted the path he had taken but claimed there was no water that day. Rava applied *mah li leshaker* — he had no reason to lie, since he could have said he traveled the permitted route and no one would have known otherwise. Since he could have made the more advantageous false claim and did not, we believe the admission he did make along with the exculpatory detail. This is the operative logic of *migo*.

### Abaye's Limit

Abaye, however, challenges the application of *migo* in a famous limitation: *mah li leshaker be'makom eidim lo amrinan* — we do not apply *migo* against what is effectively established fact, something so widely known that it functions like witness testimony (*anan sahadei*). In the donkey case, everyone knows there is always water on the Nahar Pekod road; to claim otherwise is not simply an alternative version of events but a claim against general knowledge. Accordingly, the *migo* cannot save him in Abaye's view.

### Migo Applies

The *gemara* then applies this distinction to our case: *hatam vadai ika eidim de'ika maya* — in the Nahar Pekod case there is definite, established knowledge that water is present on that road; the claim to the contrary runs against certainty. *Hacha vadai itmei* — but here, is it certain that this woman was raped? It is not. It is only a *chashasha*. And *be'makom chashasha amrinan* — where there is only suspicion, not established fact, the *migo* does apply. The woman is believed when she says she was not raped, because she could have said she was in the cellar and we would have believed that too.

The progressive effect of the *gemara*'s reasoning is now fully visible. Not only does the entire *mishna* depend on extreme and rarely occurring physical conditions — total enclosure with chains, dogs, debris, and geese, and no hiding place anywhere in the city — but even in those rare conditions, a woman's own statement, backed by *migo*, is sufficient to permit her. The *mishna*'s prohibition is all but eliminated in practice.

## Witness Credibility

### Slaves Believed

Having established that the concern rarely applies and that women can free themselves from it easily, the *gemara* turns to the *mishna*'s provision that even a slave or slave woman (*eved* or *shifchah*) can serve as a valid witness. The *mishna* states: *afilu eved afilu shifchah harei elu ne'emanim*.

### Contradiction

The *gemara* immediately raises a contradiction (*u'rominhu*) from another *baraita* that limits the validity of a slave woman's testimony — and specifically singles out her own slave woman (*shifchasa*) as not believed. How are we to reconcile the two sources?

---

## Get & Seclusion

### Get Seclusion Rule

The *baraita* in question addresses a different context: a woman who has received a *get* (or whose *get* has been written but has not yet taken effect, or is to take effect at a later time — the precise scenario is the subject of a *machlokes* between Rashi and Tosafot). In any of these situations, if the woman enters into seclusion (*yichud*) with her soon-to-be or recently former husband, there is concern that they may have intercourse, which would invalidate or complicate the *get*. She will then circulate as a divorced woman when her status is in fact uncertain.

### Witness Requirement

To prevent this, witnesses are required who can attest that no *yichud* took place. The *baraita* rules that even a slave or a slave woman can serve in this capacity — by testifying, in effect, "I was present with her throughout," their presence eliminates the concern of seclusion.

### Her Own Slave Excluded

The one exception is *shifchasa* — her own slave woman. *Mipnei she'liba gas be'shifchasa* — because a woman feels completely uninhibited in the presence of her own slave woman, she will engage in intimate behavior even when the slave is present. The slave is treated as invisible, as furniture. This being so, the slave woman's presence does not constitute a real interruption of *yichud*, because the woman of the house does not modify her behavior on account of her slave's observation. From this it follows — or at least appears to follow — that we should not believe her own slave woman in captivity cases either.

---

## Witness Credibility (II)

### Whose Slave?

Rav Pappa resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between whose slave woman is testifying. When the *mishna* says slaves are believed in captivity cases, it refers to the husband's slave woman. The wife's own slave woman (*shifcha didah*) is the one who cannot be trusted.

The *gemara* challenges this: a *baraita* states explicitly that *ein adam meid al atzmo* — a person cannot testify about herself — but that everyone else can testify for her. Surely that "everyone else" includes even her own slave woman? The *gemara* answers: *shifchasa ke'atzma dami* — her own slave woman is treated as equivalent to herself and falls within the same prohibition. Just as the woman cannot testify on her own behalf, neither can her slave testify as if she were the woman herself.

### No Active Lies

Rav Ashi offers a more refined resolution that does not depend on *bishvuya hekilu* at all. He argues that the two sources address different types of conduct. A slave woman *meichazi chazya ve'shaski* — sees what she sees and stays silent. That is, in the *get* context, she was present while her mistress had relations with the husband and said nothing, because her mistress acts with complete freedom before her. This silence, or this withholding of information, is one thing. But when she comes to court and must make an affirmative statement — that too might involve simply saying "I was with her," without actively vouching for something she knows to be false.

### Silence vs. Testimony

The key distinction is as follows. In the *get* case, where her silence makes her mistress more permissible, the slave woman may simply not disclose damaging information. But in the captivity case, *de'shikuta osarta* — where silence would render her mistress forbidden, the only way to help her mistress is to actively testify that she was not raped. And that, Rav Ashi argues, she will do honestly. *Tartei lo avda* — she will not do two things wrong simultaneously. She may withhold; she will not lie outright. Therefore, when she does come forward and testify affirmatively that her mistress was not raped, she is believed — not as a special leniency for captivity cases, but because we are simply not concerned that she is fabricating testimony.

## Mari Bar Isak Case

### Brother Inheritance

The *gemara* now introduces a parallel case that illuminates the same principle. Mari bar Isak (some say Chana bar Isak) had a long-lost brother appear from Bei Chozai claiming a share of their late father's estate. Mari replied that he did not recognize this person as his brother. When the matter came before Rav Chisda, Rav Chisda initially sided with Mari: *shapir ka'amar lach* — he is saying well to you. As proof that non-recognition does not imply bad faith, the *gemara* cites the verse *vayakker Yosef et echav ve'heim lo hikkiruhu* — Yosef recognized his brothers but they did not recognize him — and explains that Yosef had left without a beard and returned with one, making recognition genuinely difficult after many years. Mari's failure to recognize a brother he had not seen for two decades cannot be taken as evidence of fraud.

### Fear Shifts Burden

Rav Chisda then told the claimant to bring witnesses to establish his identity. The claimant said he had witnesses, but *mistafu minei degavra alma hu* — they were afraid of Mari, who was a man of force, a powerful and intimidating figure. According to Tosafot, what actually happened is that the witnesses were brought and then refused to testify at all — neither confirming nor denying the claim. This conspicuous silence was itself suspicious; why would witnesses refuse to speak unless they were being intimidated into silence? Rav Chisda therefore took the striking step of reversing the burden of proof and instructing Mari to go bring witnesses that the claimant was *not* his brother — an essentially impossible evidentiary demand, but one that Rav Chisda justified by saying: *hachi danina lach u'le'chulhu amei chaverach* — this is how I deal with you and with all men of your kind. Where a party's intimidation of witnesses is evident, the ordinary rules of who bears the burden of proof can be adjusted accordingly.

### Fear & False Testimony

The *gemara* then asks: but if this man is so terrifying, will witnesses not be induced to testify *falsely* on his behalf as well? The answer is the same principle stated earlier: *tartei lo avdi* — people do not do two things. They may be intimidated into silence; they will not be frightened into outright false testimony. The case of Mari bar Isak and the case of the slave woman both illustrate this single principle from different angles. The degree of suborning required to induce active false testimony is categorically different from the pressure required to induce silence or omission.

## Many Witnesses

### All Can Testify

A *baraita* states that testimony about a woman's non-rape is accepted from the broadest possible range of witnesses: men, women, children, parents, siblings — virtually anyone.

### Own Children Excluded

The one exception noted in this *baraita* is her own son and daughter — presumably because children are so closely identified with their mother's interests that their testimony is not considered independent.

### Except Self & Husband

A second *baraita* takes a different approach: *hakol ne'emanim le'ha'id*, everyone is believed to testify, *chutz mi'heimeina u'va'alah* — except for herself and her husband. This *baraita* does not mention the exclusion of her children but does explicitly add the husband.

### Tannaim Views

The *gemara* observes that the positions of Rav Pappa and Rav Ashi — who disagree about her own slave woman — reflect this underlying *tannaitic* dispute. The first *baraita* (excluding her children and slaves) corresponds to one *tanna*'s position; the second *baraita* (excluding only herself and her husband) corresponds to another's. The debate among the *Amoraim* is anchored in a genuine disagreement among the *tannaim* about precisely which witnesses are too closely identified with the woman's interests to be credited.

## Innocent Speech

### Masiach Definition

*Masiach lefi tumo* — innocent speech, or testimony offered without awareness of its legal implications — is a recognized category in which we accord credibility even to normally disqualified witnesses or parties. When a person relates facts casually, as part of ordinary conversation, without realizing they are making a legally consequential claim, their account is treated as more reliable precisely because they have no strategic motive to shape it.

### Mother Captivity

Rav Dimi, upon returning from Eretz Yisrael, reported in the name of Rav Chanan of Carthage (*Kartagina*) a case that came before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi (some say before Rabbi himself): a certain man was relating his experience of captivity among non-Jews. He said — apparently as part of a general account of what captivity was like — *ani ve'imi nishveinu levein ovdei kochavim* — my mother and I were taken captive together. *Yatzati lishov mayim, da'ati al imi* — when I went to draw water, my thoughts were always on my mother. *Le'laket eitzim, da'ati al imi* — when I gathered wood, my thoughts were always on her. The implication of his story was that he was constantly watching over her, never leaving her unguarded.

### Mother Permitted

*Ve'hishiah Rabbi le'khunah al piv* — Rabbi ruled, on the basis of this account, that the mother was permitted to marry a *kohen*. The son had not come to offer testimony; he was simply telling the story of their captivity. Yet his innocent account contained within it all the elements needed to establish that his mother had not been violated. This is *masiach lefi tumo* at its most striking — credibility derived precisely from the absence of testimonial intent.

## Mishna Moot

### Leniency Summary

The *gemara* has now layered leniency upon leniency in a way that all but eliminates the practical reach of the *mishna*. The first leniency: the prohibition only applies in one's own country (per Tosafot's reading), not in enemy territory. The second: even in one's own country, it only applies if the city was sealed with a complete alarm system — chains, dogs, debris, geese. The third: even in that extreme case, if there is so much as one hiding place anywhere in the city, every woman may be presumed to have used it. The fourth: even if there is no hiding place, a woman who says she was not raped is believed by virtue of *migo*, since she could have claimed to have been in the hiding place. The fifth: even a slave woman's affirmative testimony is accepted, because *tartei lo avdi* — people do not both withhold information and testify falsely. The sixth: even casual, innocent speech (*masiach lefi tumo*) suffices to establish non-rape.

### Practical Rarity

The cumulative effect is that it is nearly impossible to construct a realistic scenario in which a woman would actually be rendered forbidden to her *kohen* husband. The *mishna*'s presumption of violation was always a *chashasha*, a concern rather than a certainty, and the *gemara* has systematically provided tools to overcome it at every stage. In practical terms, almost no actual case will result in forbiddance.

## Husband Testimony

### Rabbi Zechariah

The next *mishna* opens with a striking historical account. Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav — Rabbi Zechariah son of the butcher — invokes an oath by the *Beis HaMikdash* and declares that from the moment the enemy entered Jerusalem until they departed, his wife's hand never left his hand. He was with her constantly, and she was not harmed.

### Self-Testimony Banned

The response from the Sages is immediate: *ein adam meid al atzmo* — a person cannot testify about himself. The phrase is in the masculine because, as the *gemara* makes clear, a husband testifying on behalf of his wife is, in effect, testifying about himself — his ability to remain married to her depends on the outcome, and he is therefore a directly interested party whose testimony cannot be received.

### Husband Can't Permit

The practical implication is stark: even a *kohen* husband who was physically present with his wife throughout the siege, never separated from her for a moment, cannot by his own testimony render her permitted to him. His testimony, however honest and however corroborated by circumstance, is categorically excluded. This ruling reminds us that the *mishna*'s framework, for all the *gemara*'s leniencies, still has a floor: the husband's word alone will never suffice.

## Protective Arrangement

### Separate Housing

A *baraita* describes the practical arrangement that Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav put in place given his legal situation. Since he could not testify on his wife's behalf and therefore could not simply continue their marriage as before, he arranged for her to have her own house (*bayit*) within the family compound (*chatzeiro*). They remained married, but they would no longer share quarters.

### Seclusion Prevention

The challenge was to ensure that they were never in *yichud* together, which would itself raise concerns and complicate her status further. The solution was carefully choreographed around the movements of their children. *U'che'she'yotza, yotza be'rosh baneha* — when she left the compound, she would walk at the head of the group of children, so that the husband would never be left alone with her. *U'che'she'nichnesa, nichnesa be'sof baneha* — when they returned, she would enter last, after the children had already gone in.

### Marriage Maintained

In this way, Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav managed to maintain the marriage legally — keeping his wife within the family compound, within reach of the children, and part of the household — while ensuring that no opportunity for *yichud* ever arose that could further compromise her status or his. It is an arrangement that reflects both legal ingenuity and personal devotion: a *kohen* who could not testify for his wife found a way, through scrupulous arrangement of daily life, to keep the marriage intact within the law's constraints.
