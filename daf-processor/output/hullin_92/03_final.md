# Hullin 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Israel vs. Angels

### Shema vs. Kadosh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מַזְכִּירִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם אַחַר שְׁתֵּי תֵּיבוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אֵין מַזְכִּירִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ תֵּיבוֹת, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Jewish people mention the name of God after two words, as it is stated: "Hear, Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4). But the ministering angels mention the name of God only after three words, as it is written: "And one called unto another, and said: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of His glory" (Isaiah 6:3).**

The *aggadata* section of this *daf* concludes with a striking comparison between Israel and the angels in their praise of God. The *Gemara* had established that Israel surpasses the angels because Israel invokes God's name after only two words — *Shema Yisrael Hashem* — whereas the angels do so only after three, as in *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh*, the refrain from the vision in *Yeshayahu*. One might ask, however: after *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh*, the angels say *Baruch kevod Hashem mimkomo* — that too invokes God's name after only two words. How then does the original claim stand?

### Ophanim Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּרוּךְ״ – אוֹפַנִּים הוּא דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it is the ***ofanim* who say** the verse: **"Blessed be** the glory of the Lord from His place," as the next verse mentions "the noise of the *ofanim*" (Ezekiel 3:13), and Rav Ḥanina's statement citing Rav was referring not to *ofanim* but to ministering angels.

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between classes of heavenly beings: *baruch ophanim hu de-amri lei* — it is specifically the *ophanim*, the heavenly wheels described in the prophetic vision, who say it after two words, not the ordinary *malachim*. The ordinary angels require three words of *Kadosh* before they may invoke God's name. On this reading, Israel stands above the ordinary *malachim* and is on a par with the *ophanim*, the higher class. The *Tosafot* and *Maharsha* address this hierarchy at length for those who wish to explore it further.

### License & Holiness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִתְיְהִיב רְשׁוּתָא, אִתְיְהִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say that once permission has been given to them to mention the name of God after three words when they say: "Holy, holy, holy," permission is also given to them to mention the name of God again while praising Him even after fewer than three words.**

The *Gemara* offers an alternative answer as well: *kivan de-yehaiv reshut yehaiv* — once the angels have already begun with *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh* and have thereby been granted license to invoke God's name, the subsequent invocation may come more readily. But the initial permission requires three acknowledgments of God's holiness. This is deeply ironic, and that irony is precisely the *Gemara*'s point. One would expect the angels, who are perpetually and intimately aware of God's holiness, to require *less* preparation before invoking His name — yet Israel does so more readily. The *Gemara* seems to suggest that Israel's awareness of how God's presence is expressed within the actual world gives them a distinctive form of closeness, or perhaps that God wishes to grant Israel a special capacity to connect to Him that the angels, who have no need of it, do not possess. The element of free will and existential struggle that defines human religiosity — in contrast to the angels' unfree servitude — hovers behind this discussion, even if the *Gemara* does not spell it out explicitly.

## Jacob's Struggle

### Ambiguous Pronouns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּשַׂר אֶל מַלְאָךְ וַיֻּכָל בָּכָה וַיִּתְחַנֶּן לוֹ״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִי נַעֲשָׂה שַׂר לְמִי, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״כִּי שָׂרִיתָ עִם אֱלֹהִים״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר יַעֲקֹב נַעֲשָׂה שַׂר לַמַּלְאָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss Jacob wrestling with the angel. The prophet states: **"So he strove [*vayyasar*] with an angel, and prevailed; he wept, and made supplication to him; at Beth El he would find him, and there he would speak with us" (Hosea 12:5). From this verse I do not know who became master [*sar*], i.e., was victorious, over whom. When another verse states: "And he said: Your name shall no longer be called Jacob, but Israel; for you have striven with angels and with men, and have prevailed" (Genesis 32:29), you must say that Jacob became master over the angel.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *psukim* in *Hoshea* that poetically retell the story of Yaakov's wrestling with the angel: *vayasar el malach vayuchal, bacha vayitchanen lo* — he strove with the angel and prevailed; he wept and supplicated to him. The problem is that the antecedents of the pronouns are entirely ambiguous. Without knowing the backstory from *Bereishit*, one cannot determine from *Hoshea* alone who prevailed over whom, and who was weeping and supplicating to whom. *Eini yodea mi nasa sar l'mi* — I do not know who became master over whom.

### Jacob as Sar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּכָה וַיִּתְחַנֶּן לוֹ״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִי בָּכָה לְמִי, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וַיֹּאמֶר שַׁלְּחֵנִי״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: מַלְאָךְ בָּכָה לְיַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The verse in Hosea states: **"He wept, and made supplication to him." From this verse I do not know who cried to whom. When another verse states: "And he said: Let me go, for the dawn has risen" (Genesis 32:27), you must say that the angel cried to Jacob.**

The *Gemara* resolves the ambiguity through cross-reference. The verse in *Bereishit* — *ki sarita im Elokim* — says explicitly: *you*, Yaakov, strove with angels. *Hevei omer*: Yaakov was the one who became a *sar*, a prince, over the angel — he was the victor. And as for the weeping and supplication — *eini yodea mi bacha l'mi* — again the *Hoshea* text is unclear. But the verse *vayomer shalcheni* — "send me free" — makes clear who was in the position of begging to be released. The angel was crying and supplicating to Yaakov. This is not new exegesis so much as a demonstration that the poetic retelling in *Hoshea* cannot stand alone; it requires the narrative of *Bereishit* to be intelligible.

### Two Princes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי שָׂרִיתָ עִם אֱלֹהִים וְעִם אֲנָשִׁים״, אָמַר רַבָּה: רֶמֶז רָמַז לוֹ שֶׁעֲתִידִים שְׁנֵי שָׂרִים לָצֵאת מִמֶּנּוּ, רֹאשׁ גּוֹלָה שֶׁבְּבָבֶל וְנָשִׂיא שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִכָּאן רָמַז לוֹ גָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "And he said: Your name shall no longer be called Jacob, but Israel; **for you have striven with angels [*elohim*] and with men, and have prevailed" (Genesis 32:29). Rabba says: The angel **intimated to Jacob that in the future two princes would emerge from him: They are the Exilarch who is in Babylonia and the *Nasi* who is in Eretz Yisrael. And from here he also intimated to Jacob that there would be an exile.**

The *Gemara* now interprets the phrase *ki sarita im Elokim ve'im anashim* — you have striven with angels and with men. *Amar Rava*: the angel hinted to Yaakov that two future authorities would descend from him — *mimenu rosh golah she-beBavel*, the head of the exile in Bavel, and *veNasi she-beEretz Yisrael*, the *Nasi* in the land of Israel. The very fact that Yaakov would need two separate figures of authority — one in Bavel and one in Israel — was itself a hint at the coming exile. Two centers of authority imply a people divided between two lands.

### Religious Authority

Where is this encoded in the verse? *Sarita im Elokim* — you will have a prince among *Elokim*. The term *Elokim* in rabbinic reading denotes judges and courts vested with legal authority; indeed, the *Torah* uses *el haElokim yavo devar shneihem* when referring to bringing a case before a court. The *Nasi* in *Eretz Yisrael* — *Rebbi Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* — held both religious and political authority, and this corresponds to *sarita im Elokim*. *Ve'im anashim* — and with men — refers to secular authority. The *Resh Galuta*, the exilarch in Bavel, was often a figure whose household conduct was not fully consistent with *halacha*, making him a secular rather than religious authority. So the verse encodes both: a religious authority and a secular authority, both descendants of Yaakov. One may note, as was observed in the discussion, that this also provides a historical precedent for the existence of a Jewish political authority that is not itself a religious institution — a point that has not been lost on subsequent generations.

## Three Branches

### Sarei Ge'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבַגֶּפֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרִיגִים״, אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרֵי גֵּאִים הַיּוֹצְאִים מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל בְּכׇל דּוֹר וָדוֹר, פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנַיִם כָּאן וְאֶחָד בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנַיִם בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶחָד כָּאן. יְהִיבוּ רַבָּנַן עֵינַיְיהוּ בְּרַבָּנָא עוּקְבָא וְרַבָּנָא נְחֶמְיָה בְּנֵי בְרַתֵּיה דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the dream of Pharaoh's butler, the verse states: **"And in the vine were three branches [*sarigim*]; and as it was budding, its blossoms shot forth, and its clusters brought forth ripe grapes" (Genesis 40:10). Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba says that Rav says: These three branches refer to the three proud princes [*sarei ge'im*] who emerge from the Jewish people in each and every generation. There are times when two are here in Babylonia and one is in Eretz Yisrael, and there are times when two are in Eretz Yisrael and one is here in Babylonia. When this was stated in the study hall, the Sages present turned their eyes toward Rabbana Ukva and Rabbana Neḥemya, the sons of the daughter of Rav, who were from the family of the Exilarch and were two leaders of the generation who resided in Babylonia.**

The *Gemara* moves to interpret the vision of Pharaoh's wine steward, in which a vine bore three branches — *uvagefen shlosha sarigim*. *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rav*: the word *sarigim* evokes *sarei ge'im* — princes of great power and influence — who emerge from the Jewish people in every generation. Sometimes two such figures are found in Bavel and one in *Eretz Yisrael*, and sometimes the reverse — two in *Eretz Yisrael* and one in Bavel. The *Gemara* notes that the rabbis directed their gaze toward Rabana Ukva and Rabana Nechemya, the sons of Rav's daughter, apparently wealthy and prominent men of their day.

### Sarei Goyim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרֵי גוֹיִם, שֶׁמְלַמְּדִים זְכוּת עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּכׇל דּוֹר וָדוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says a different explanation of the verse: These three branches [*sarigim*] are the three ministering angels appointed to oversee the gentiles [*sarei goyim*], who plead in favor of the Jewish people in each and every generation.**

*Rava amar*: *sarigim* alludes to *sarei goyim* — princes of the nations, powerful non-Jewish figures *she-melamdin zechut al Yisrael bechol dor vador* — who argue merit on behalf of the Jewish people in every generation. *Rashi* interprets these as heavenly angels of the other nations who advocate on Israel's behalf before God — an interpretation reinforced by the phrase *melamdin zechut*, which typically connotes pleading a case before the divine tribunal. One might alternatively read the passage as acknowledging those human gentile figures who throughout history have occasionally come to the defense of the Jewish people, but *Rashi*'s reading of heavenly advocates carries its own poignant irony: it is the angels of the very nations that have often oppressed Israel who stand in their defense before God.

### Avos & Imahos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״גֶּפֶן״ – זֶה הָעוֹלָם, ״שְׁלֹשָׁה שְׂרִיגִים״ – זֶה אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, ״וְהִיא כְפֹרַחַת עָלְתָה נִצָּהּ״ – אֵלּוּ הָאִמָּהוֹת, ״הִבְשִׁילוּ אַשְׁכְּלֹתֶיהָ עֲנָבִים״ – אֵלּוּ הַשְּׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer says an alternate interpretation of the verse. "Vine"; this is a reference to the world. "Three branches"; this is a reference to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. "And as it was budding, its blossoms shot forth"; these are the matriarchs. "And its clusters brought forth ripe grapes"; these are the twelve tribes, i.e., the twelve sons of Jacob.**

A *braita* presents *Rabbi Eliezer*'s interpretation: *gefen zeh ha-olam* — the vine represents the world; *shelosha sarigim zeh Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov* — the three branches are the three *avos*. *Ve-hi ke-forachat alta nitzah* — the blossoming — *elu ha-imahos*, those are the matriarchs. *Hivshilu eshkeloteiha anavim* — the clusters that ripened to grapes — *elu ha-shevatim*, those are the tribes. Here at least the imagery is coherent: the vine of the world gives rise to the patriarchs, the matriarchs, and ultimately the tribes, the fruit of the whole.

### Future vs. Past

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וְכִי מַרְאִין לוֹ לְאָדָם מַה שֶּׁהָיָה? וַהֲלֹא אֵין מַרְאִין לוֹ לְאָדָם אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁעָתִיד לִהְיוֹת! אֶלָּא ״גֶּפֶן״ – זֶה תּוֹרָה, ״שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרִיגִים״ – אֵלּוּ מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם, ״וְהִיא כְפֹרַחַת עָלְתָה נִצָּהּ״ – אֵלּוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין, ״הִבְשִׁילוּ אַשְׁכְּלֹתֶיהָ עֲנָבִים״ – אֵלּוּ הַצַּדִּיקִים שֶׁבְּכָל דּוֹר וָדוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: But is a person shown in a dream what was in the past? Isn't it true that one is shown only what will be in the future? Since the patriarchs, matriarchs, and sons of Jacob were all born prior to this dream, the dream was not alluding to them. Rather, the verse should be interpreted as follows: "Vine"; this is a reference to the Torah. "Three branches"; these are Moses, Aaron, and Miriam. "And as it was budding, its blossoms shot forth"; these are the members of the Sanhedrin. "And its clusters brought forth ripe grapes"; these are the righteous people who live in each and every generation.**

*Amar lo Rabbi Yehoshua*: but what would be the point of showing someone a vision of things that had already occurred? The *avos*, the *imahos*, the tribes — all of these already existed in Yosef's time. *De-lo omerim le-adam ela mah she-atid li-heyos* — visions are shown only of what is yet to come, not of established historical facts. Therefore, *Rabbi Yehoshua* proposes: *ela gefen zeh Torah* — the vine is the *Torah*; *shelosha sarigim eilu Moshe Aharon u-Miriam* — the three branches are Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam; *elu Sanhedrin* — the blossoming is the *Sanhedrin*; and *eilu ha-tzaddikim she-bechol dor va-dor* — the ripened grapes are the righteous of every generation. It is worth noting that Miriam appears here among the primary three who emerge from the vine of *Torah* — a striking inclusion.

This interpretation, however, leaves something to be desired thematically. While *Moshe*, *Aharon*, and *Miriam* as the foundational leaders, the *Sanhedrin* as the institutional framework, and the *tzaddikim* of each generation as its fruit are all admirable Jewish ideals, they do not cohere around the specific motif of *Torah* in any obvious way — at least not without understanding *Torah* in a very broad sense as encompassing the full ideal of Jewish religious life. The *Maharsha* does not appear to resolve this tension.

### Jerusalem Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: עֲדַיִן צְרִיכִין אָנוּ לַמּוֹדָעִי, דְּמוֹקֵים לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ בְּחַד מָקוֹם. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: ״גֶּפֶן״ – זֶה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, ״שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרִיגִים״ – זֶה מִקְדָּשׁ, מֶלֶךְ, וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, ״וְהִיא כְפֹרַחַת עָלְתָה נִצָּהּ״ – אֵלּוּ פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה, ״הִבְשִׁילוּ אַשְׁכְּלֹתֶיהָ עֲנָבִים״ – אֵלּוּ נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel said: In order to understand this verse (Genesis 40:10) we still need the explanation of Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i, who is an expert in matters of *aggada*, as he interprets all of the phrases in the verse as referring to one location. Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says: "Vine"; this is a reference to Jerusalem. "Three branches"; this is a reference to the Temple, the king and the High Priest. "And as it was budding [*poraḥat*], its blossoms shot forth"; these are the young priests [*pirḥei khehunna*]. "And its clusters brought forth ripe grapes"; these are the wine libations.**

*Rabban Gamliel* then remarks: *adayin tzerichim anu la-Modai* — we still need the *Modai*, a phrase that both names *Rabbi Eliezer HaModai* specifically and signals more generally that someone with genuine skill in *aggadata* interpretation is needed here. The present interpretations are not fully satisfying.

*Rabbi Eliezer HaModai omer*: *gefen zu Yerushalayim* — the vine is Jerusalem. *Shelosha sarigim ze mikdash melech ve-chohen gadol* — the three branches are the Temple, the king, and the *kohen gadol*. At least these three belong together; they are all centered in Jerusalem and represent both the religious and political heart of the Jewish world. The blossoming — *elu pirchei kehuna* — are the young *kohanim* who serve in the *Beit HaMikdash*, a connection reinforced by the word *ke-forachat* evoking *lifroach*, to blossom, a rabbinic term for junior priests. The ripened clusters — *elu nesachim* — are the wine libations offered in the *Beit HaMikdash*, which creates at least a direct visual connection with grapes and the act of pressing them into wine. Everything here is geographically and institutionally centered on Jerusalem, even if the thematic coherence is not perfectly tight — the king, for instance, sits somewhat uneasily alongside the *kohen gadol* and the *nesachim*.

The broader question of why the *Gemara* dwells so extensively on interpreting the wine steward's dream is itself worth noting. The dream plays a limited narrative role in the *Yosef* story and would seem to have no lasting theological resonance. The *Gemara* appears determined to find in it an enduring message about the Jewish people — yet the interpreters themselves acknowledge, through *Rabban Gamliel*'s remark, that none of the available interpretations fully satisfies.

### Desert Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּמַתָּנוֹת, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״גֶּפֶן״ – זוֹ תּוֹרָה, ״שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרִיגִים״ – זֶה בְּאֵר, עַמּוּד עָנָן, וּמָן, ״וְהִיא כְפֹרַחַת עָלְתָה נִצָּהּ״ – אֵלּוּ הַבִּכּוּרִים, ״הִבְשִׁילוּ אַשְׁכְּלֹתֶיהָ עֲנָבִים״ – אֵלּוּ נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi interprets it with reference to the gifts that God gave the Jewish people, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: "Vine"; this is a reference to the Torah. "Three branches"; this is a reference to the miraculous items that accompanied the Jewish people in the wilderness and sustained and protected them: The well, the pillar of cloud, and the manna. "And as it was budding, its blossoms shot forth"; these are the first fruits that are brought to the Temple. "And its clusters brought forth ripe grapes"; these are the wine libations.**

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* interprets the passage as referring to gifts God gave the Jewish people: *gefen zu Torah* — the vine is the *Torah*; *shelosha sarigim ze be'er amud anan u-man* — the three branches are the three gifts of the desert: the miraculous well of water, the pillar of cloud, and the *manna*. The blossoming — *elu ha-bikkurim* — represents the first fruits; and the ripened clusters — *elu nesachim* — the wine libations. *Rashi* notes that in each case God provides the gift so that Israel may then return it to God. This interpretation carries perhaps the most satisfying internal logic: God gives, Israel receives, Israel returns. The *manna* was placed in a container by the *aron*; the well was acknowledged through song in the *Torah* — *be'er chafaruha sarim, ali be'er enu lah*; and the pillar of cloud, at least according to one opinion, is what we recognize and acknowledge on *Sukkot*. The *amud anan* fits somewhat less naturally into the pattern of return, but the overall imagery of the vine giving forth its bounty to be offered back does create a more coherent parallel to Pharaoh's steward pressing out the grapes into Pharaoh's cup.

### Egypt & Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: ״גֶּפֶן״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״גֶּפֶן מִמִּצְרַיִם תַּסִּיעַ״. ״שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׂרִיגִים״ – אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹלִין בָּהֶן בְּכׇל שָׁנָה וְשָׁנָה. ״וְהִיא כְפֹרַחַת עָלְתָה נִצָּהּ״ – הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְרוֹת וְלִרְבּוֹת, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל פָּרוּ וַיִּשְׁרְצוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: "Vine"; this is a reference to the Jewish people, and similarly another verse states: "You plucked up a vine out of Egypt; You drove out the nations and planted it" (Psalms 80:9). "Three branches"; these are the three pilgrimage Festivals, on which the Jewish people ascend to Jerusalem every year. "And as it was budding, its blossoms shot forth," means that the time has arrived for the Jewish people to be fruitful and multiply, and similarly another verse states: "And the children of Israel were fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and became exceeding mighty; and the land was filled with them" (Exodus 1:7).**

*Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba* reads the passage through the lens of the Exodus: *gefen elu Yisrael* — the vine is the Jewish people, as the verse says *gefen miMitzrayim tasi'a* — a vine from Egypt You lifted up. *Shelosha sarigim elu shlosha regalim* — the three branches are the three pilgrimage festivals, *she-Yisrael olin bahem bechol shanah va-shanah*. The connection to the Exodus is apt: *Pesach* and *Sukkot* both explicitly commemorate it, and *Shavuot* — understood by *Chazal* as the time of *Matan Torah* — is likewise bound up with that redemptive moment. The blossoming — *alta nitzah* — represents *higia zmanam shel Yisrael lifrot ve-lirbot*, the moment when the time came for Israel to be fruitful and multiply in Egypt. *Vayishretzu* — they swarmed — and then *alta nitzah*, the blossom rose up, signifying *higia zmanam shel Yisrael lehiga'el* — the time for Israel to be redeemed had arrived. The verse from *Yeshayahu* — *vayeiz nitzcham al bgadai* — is connected through the word *nitzcham*, linking the blossoming imagery to the blood of the Egyptians splattered on God's garments during the act of redemption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עָלְתָה נִצָּה״ – הִגִּיעַ זְמַן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל לִיגָּאֵל, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְיֵז נִצְחָם עַל בְּגָדַי וְכׇל מַלְבּוּשַׁי אֶגְאָלְתִּי״, ״הִבְשִׁילוּ אַשְׁכְּלֹתֶיהָ עֲנָבִים״ – הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם לִשְׁתּוֹת כּוֹס הַתַּרְעֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Its blossoms [*nitzah*] shot forth" means that the time has arrived for the Jewish people to be redeemed. And similarly another verse states: "And their eternity [*nitzḥam*] is dashed against My garments, and I have redeemed all My raiment" (Isaiah 63:3). "And its clusters brought forth ripe grapes" means that the time has arrived for Egypt to drink the cup of fury, i.e., to receive its punishment.**

### Three Cups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רָבָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה כּוֹסוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּמִצְרַיִם לָמָה? אֶחָד, שֶׁשָּׁתָת בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה, וְאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׁתָת בִּימֵי פַּרְעֹה נְכֹה, וְאֶחָד שֶׁעֲתִידָה לִשְׁתּוֹת עִם כׇּל הַגּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is as Rava said: Why are there three cups stated with regard to Egypt in the dream of Pharaoh's butler (see Genesis 40:11)? They are an allusion to three cups of misfortune that would later befall Egypt: One that Egypt drank in the days of Moses during the ten plagues and the Exodus; one that Egypt drank in the days of Pharaoh Nekho, the king of Egypt defeated by Nebuchadnezzar; and one that Egypt will drink in the future with all the other nations, when they are punished in the time of the Messiah.**

The ripened clusters — *hivshilu eshkeloteiha anavim* — represent the moment when *higia zmanam shel Mitzrayim lishtot et kos ha-tareilah* — the time came for Egypt to drink the cup of poison. *Ve-haynu de-amar Rava*: the three cups mentioned in this very dream — *kos Paroh* appears three times in the steward's vision — correspond to three moments of Egypt's punishment. The first is *kos she-shatah bi-mei Moshe*, the punishment Egypt suffered at the Exodus. The second is *she-shatah bi-mei Paroh Necho*, when *Nevuchadnetzar* swept through and Egypt was overrun. The third is *she-atid lishtot im kol ovdei chochavim* — the punishment Egypt will share with all the nations at the future time of reckoning, having already received particular punishment for subjugating Israel, they will nonetheless join in the universal judgment.

This interpretation achieves a coherence the earlier ones approached but did not quite reach. The connection to Pharaoh and his drinking is direct and visceral: the steward presses grapes into Pharaoh's cup in the dream, and the *Gemara* reads this as Pharaoh — Egypt — drinking the cup of punishment. The imagery of blossoming as redemption, of spring and new life, ties naturally to *Pesach*, and the punishment of Egypt follows organically from the Exodus narrative. *Rav Abba* confirms to *Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba* that when *Rav* himself interpreted these verses in *aggadata*, his interpretation matched this one exactly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: כִּי דָרֵישׁ לְהוּ רַב לְהָנֵי קְרָאֵי בְּאַגַּדְתָּא, כְּוָותָךְ דָּרֵישׁ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba: When Rav interpreted these verses homiletically he interpreted them according to the way in which you have interpreted them, and not according to any of the other opinions cited above.**

## Vine Metaphor

### Nation Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אוּמָּה זוֹ כְּגֶפֶן נִמְשְׁלָה. זְמוֹרוֹת שֶׁבָּהּ – אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים, אֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת שֶׁבָּהּ – אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, עָלִין שֶׁבָּהּ – אֵלּוּ עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ, קְנוֹקָנוֹת שֶׁבָּהּ – אֵלּוּ רֵיקָנִים שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Similar to Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba's interpretation of the word vine as an allusion to the Jewish people, **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: This nation is likened to a vine. The branches of the vine support the clusters of grapes, the leaves, and the tendrils; these are represented among the Jewish people by the homeowners, who provide financial support for the entire nation. The clusters of grapes on the vine, these are the Torah scholars. The leaves on the vine, which protect the grapes, these are the ignoramuses, who protect the Torah scholars. The tendrils of the vine, which do not directly serve the grapes themselves, these are the empty ones of the Jewish people.**

*Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish*: this nation has been compared to a vine — *uma zu ke-gefen nimshala*. Now the *Gemara* steps back from the specific vision of the wine steward's dream and reflects more broadly on the vine as an image of the Jewish people and its internal structure. The branches — *zemoros* — are the *ba'alei battim*, a term that in the *Gemara* connotes not merely homeowners but people of wealth and means: those whose resources sustain the community. The clusters — *eshkolos* — are the *talmidei chachamim*, the Torah sages, for the clusters are the true purpose of the vine, just as *Torah* scholarship is the highest fruit the nation produces. The leaves — *alim* — are the *amei ha'aretz*, the working people who farm, trade, and perform the practical labors of society; just as leaves protect the clusters, so these people provide the material conditions that allow *Torah* study to flourish. Finally, the *knoknot* — the small protrusions that branch off without bearing fruit — are the *reikanim she-b'Yisrael*, the empty ones who contribute little to the life of the community.

There is an unmistakable elitism in this hierarchy, though it is entirely consistent with the *Gemara*'s general orientation. The *talmidei chachamim* are the fruit — the ultimate purpose of the vine. The *Rambam* develops a parallel argument in his philosophical writings: the world requires enormous numbers of people performing mundane labors — planting, harvesting, weaving, building — precisely so that the philosophers who contemplate ultimate truths can be sustained. The *Gemara* here is less extreme, but the underlying logic is similar: a complex and productive society is necessary precisely so that its highest spiritual achievements become possible.

### Leaves Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּשְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: לִיבְעֵי רַחֲמִים אִיתְכָּלַיָּא עַל עָלַיָּא, דְּאִילְמָלֵא עָלַיָּא לָא מִתְקַיְּימָן אִיתְכָּלַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is the meaning of the instruction that they sent from there, i.e., from Eretz Yisrael: Let the clusters of grapes pray for the leaves, as were it not for the leaves, the clusters of grapes would not survive.**

*De-haynu de-shalchu mi-tam* — this is what they sent from the land of Israel as a message: *liba'ei rachamei itkala al ilaya* — the clusters should pray for the leaves, *de-imallei alaya lo miskayimei itkalya* — for without the leaves the clusters would not survive. This is a remarkable statement. The *talmidei chachamim*, highest in the hierarchy of value, are instructed to acknowledge a genuine dependence on those below them — not merely on the wealthy *ba'alei battim* who write the large checks, but on the *amei ha'aretz*, the ordinary working people who sustain the material fabric of society. The elitism of the hierarchy is real, but it is tempered by a requirement of genuine gratitude and recognition of mutual dependence.

## Righteous Survival

### 15th of Nisan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וָאֶכְּרֶהָ לִּי בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר כָּסֶף״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: אֵין ״כִּירָה״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן מְכִירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בְּקִבְרִי אֲשֶׁר כָּרִיתִי לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites homiletical interpretations of other verses that pertain to the leaders of the Jewish people in Eretz Yisrael and Babylonia. The verse states: **"So I bought her [*va'ekkereha*] to me for fifteen pieces of silver, and a *ḥomer* of barley, and a half-*ḥomer* of barley" (Hosea 3:2). Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: The term *kira*, which forms the basis of the verb *va'ekkereha*, is nothing other than language referring to a sale [*mekhira*], as it is stated that Joseph said: "My father made me swear, saying: Lo, I die; in my grave that I have acquired [*kariti*] for me in the land of Canaan, there shall you bury me" (Genesis 50:5).**

*Ve-achra li be-chamishah asar kasef* — and I purchased for me with fifteen pieces of silver. The *Gemara* pivots to a passage about redemption and the righteous, which connects to the preceding theme of the vine's fruit and those for whose sake the community survives. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yozadak*: *ein kirah ela lashon mechira* — the word *kirah* here denotes purchase, as in *be-kivri asher karisi li*, the grave that I purchased for myself. *Be-chamishah asar* — fifteen — is *zeh tet-vav be-Nisan*, the fifteenth of Nisan, *she-bo nigalu Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim* — the night Israel was redeemed from Egypt. *Kasef* — silver — *elu tzaddikim*, these are the righteous. The verse in *Mishlei* — *tzror ha-kesef lakach be-yado* — depicts God as having taken the bundle of silver, meaning the *tzaddikim*, with Him, leaving the community exposed. The meaning here: God redeemed Israel on the fifteenth, on *Pesach*, by virtue of the *chamishah asar kasef* — the fifteen righteous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ – זֶה חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּנִיסָן שֶׁבּוֹ נִגְאֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם, ״כָּסֶף״ – אֵלּוּ צַדִּיקִים, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״צְרוֹר הַכֶּסֶף לָקַח בְּיָדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan continues: **"For fifteen pieces of silver"; this is a reference to the fifteenth of Nisan, the date on which the Jewish people were redeemed from Egypt. "Silver"; these are the righteous people. And similarly, another verse states: "He has taken the bag of silver with him" (Proverbs 7:20), a reference to God taking the righteous away prior to the destruction of the First Temple (see *Sanhedrin* 96b).**

### 45 Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים וְלֶתֶךְ שְׂעוֹרִים״ – אֵלּוּ אַרְבָּעִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה צַדִּיקִים שֶׁהָעוֹלָם מִתְקַיֵּים בָּהֶם, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם שְׁלֹשִׁים כָּאן וַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאִם שְׁלֹשִׁים בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר כָּאן. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וָאֶקְחָה שְׁלֹשִׁים הַכֶּסֶף וָאַשְׁלִיךְ אֹתוֹ בֵּית ה׳ אֶל הַיּוֹצֵר״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשִׁים בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"A *ḥomer* of barley, and a half-*ḥomer* of barley." A *ḥomer* equals thirty *se'a*, and a half-*ḥomer* equals fifteen *se'a*, totaling forty-five *se'a*; these are the forty-five righteous individuals in whose merit the world continues to exist. And although the verse alludes to the fact that thirty of these righteous individuals are in one place and fifteen are elsewhere, I do not know if thirty are here in Babylonia and fifteen are in Eretz Yisrael, or if thirty are in Eretz Yisrael and fifteen are here in Babylonia. When it says in a different verse: "And I took the thirty pieces of silver and cast them into the treasury, in the house of the Lord" (Zechariah 11:13), you must say that thirty of the righteous individuals are in Eretz Yisrael and fifteen are here in Babylonia.**

The verse continues: *chomer se'orim ve-letech se'orim* — a *chomer* of barley and a *elkaar* of barley. A *chomer* is 30 *se'ah* and a *blevet* is 15 *se'ah*, for a total of 45 *se'ah*. *Elu mem-heh tzaddikim she-ha'olam mitkayem bahem* — these are the 45 righteous people in whose merit the world is sustained. This figure of 45 is not the one that has lodged most powerfully in the Jewish imagination — that would be the *lamed-vav tzaddikim*, the 36 righteous of other *Gemaras* — but this *Gemara* is explicit: 45. *Ve-eini yodea im sheloshim kan ve-tet-vav be-Eretz Yisrael* — I do not know whether 30 are here in Bavel and 15 in Israel, or the reverse. This is a notable bookend to the earlier discussion of the *sarei ge'im*, the figures of influence divided between Bavel and Israel in a two-to-one ratio. Here too there is a two-to-one ratio, and the *Bavli* itself concludes — from the verse *ve-ekcha sheloshim ha-kasef ve-ashlich oto beit Hashem* — that the 30 are in *Eretz Yisrael* and the 15 are in Bavel. The *Bavli* thus acknowledges that the greater number of righteous are found in the land of Israel, not in the diaspora community whose *Talmud* we are studying.

### Tuti Afsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְרוּבַּיְיהוּ מִשְׁתַּכְחִי בְּבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא דְּתוּתֵי אַפְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: And most of the fifteen righteous individuals in Babylonia are found in the synagogue under the upper room.**

*Amar Abaye*: *ve-rubaiyu mishtach'chi be-vei knishta de-Tuti Afsa* — most of the righteous of Bavel can be found in the synagogue of *Tuti Afsa*. *Rashi* explains that *Afsa* may denote a kind of alcove or recessed space within the synagogue. One sharp observation made in discussion is worth noting: the *tzaddikim* here are found not in the most prominent location but in an unassuming corner — which may itself be an implicit comment on what true righteousness looks like, as opposed to its surface appearances.

### Zechariah Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם טוֹב בְּעֵינֵיכֶם הָבוּ שְׂכָרִי וְאִם לֹא חֲדָלוּ וַיִּשְׁקְלוּ אֶת שְׂכָרִי שְׁלֹשִׁים כָּסֶף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And this is the meaning of that which is written:** in a vision in *Zechariah* in which God, figured as a shepherd, removes His protection from the other nations. **"And I said to them: If it is good in your eyes, give me my hire; and if not, refrain. And they weighed for my hire thirty pieces of silver" (Zechariah 11:12).**

*Ve-haynu de-chtiv* — and this is what is meant by the verse in *Zechariah*: *im tov be-eineichem havu schari ve-im lo chadalu va-yishklu et schari sheloshim kasef* — if it is good in your eyes, give me my fee; if not, cease; and they paid me thirty pieces of silver. This is a vision in *Zechariah* in which God, figured as a shepherd, removes His protection from the other nations. *Rashi* reads the passage as God reclaiming His thirty righteous, who had in some sense been present among the nations, and now placing them within the Jewish people — so that the nations, stripped of this protective merit, are exposed to punishment.

### Thirty Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים צַדִּיקֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מִתְקַיְּימִים עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: These are the thirty righteous individuals among the nations of the world, in whose merit the nations of the world continue to exist.**

*Rabbi Yehuda omer*: these thirty righteous are in fact *tzaddikei umot ha-olam* — righteous people among the non-Jewish nations themselves — *she-umot ha-olam mitkayimim aleihem*, in whose merit the nations survive. This reading keeps the thirty righteous *among* the nations rather than removing them.

### Bnei Noach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים מִצְוֹת שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בְּנֵי נֹחַ, וְאֵין מְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשָׁה. אַחַת שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין כְּתוּבָּה לִזְכָרִים, וְאַחַת שֶׁאֵין שׁוֹקְלִין בְּשַׂר הַמֵּת בְּמָקוֹלִין, וְאַחַת שֶׁמְּכַבְּדִין אֶת הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: These are the thirty mitzvot that the descendants of Noah initially accepted upon themselves; but they fulfill only three of them. One of these three mitzvot is that they do not write a marriage contract for a wedding between two males; although they violate the prohibition against engaging in homosexuality, they are not so brazen as to write a marriage contract as for a regular marriage. And one of the three mitzvot is that although they are suspected of eating human beings, they do not weigh the flesh of the dead in butcher shops [***bemakkulin***] and sell it publicly; and one is that they honor the Torah.**

*Ulla omer*: these thirty are not thirty righteous individuals but thirty *mitzvot* that the *bnei Noach* accepted upon themselves — *ve-ein mekayimim ela shelosha*, but they only keep three of them. The thirty *mitzvot* are not enumerated elsewhere in the *Gemara*, and *Rashi* acknowledges that they are not specified. The three they do keep are identified as follows. First: *she-ein kotvim ketubah le-zecharim* — they do not formalize male homosexual unions in the institution of marriage, even if the underlying prohibited behavior may occur. This connects to other *Gemaras* about *ma'aseh Mitzrayim* — the Egyptian practice of institutionalizing such unions — and remains a *sugyah* of ongoing relevance for contemporary discussions. Second: *she-ein shoklim besar ha-meit be-makolin* — they do not sell human flesh in the marketplace. Third: *she-mechab'dim et ha-Torah* — they honor the *Torah*, meaning they recognize and accord dignity to the Jewish people and their tradition, an influence presumably mediated through the spread of scriptural religion into the wider world.

The overall arc of this section moves from the 45 righteous who sustain Israel, to the 30 righteous in *Eretz Yisrael* and 15 in Bavel, to the role of righteous among the nations, to finally a somewhat deflating conclusion: the nations had thirty *mitzvot* and kept only three. Whatever merit the nations possessed — the protection figured in the *Zechariah* vision — has been substantially eroded by their failure to fulfill the obligations they accepted.

## Gid HaNasheh

### Birds Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בָּעוֹף. וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּאִית לֵיהּ? אִית לֵיהּ, וְלָא עֲגִיל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches (89b) that the prohibition of eating the sciatic nerve **does not apply to a bird due to the fact that the verse is referring to the sciatic nerve as being "upon the spoon of the thigh" (Genesis 32:33), and a bird has no spoon of the thigh. The Gemara challenges: But we see that it does have a protrusion of flesh on its thigh. The Gemara answers: It has a protrusion, but that protrusion is not rounded.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the *halacha* of *gid hanasheh*, the sciatic nerve. The *Mishnah* had established that *gid hanasheh* does not apply to birds — *de-ein noheig be-of*. This seems puzzling: *de-ha chazeinan de-it lei* — we observe that birds do have such a sinew. The *Gemara* explains: *it lei ve-lo agil* — they have it, but it is not situated in a rounded part of the thigh. The *Torah* specifies *asher al kaf ha-yerech* — the rounded, bowl-like hollow of the thigh — and birds lack this anatomical feature; their thighs are not structured in that curved, ladle-like form.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אִית לֵיהּ לְעוֹף וַעֲגִיל, אִית לֵיהּ לִבְהֵמָה וְלָא עֲגִיל, מַאי? בָּתַר דִּידֵיהּ אָזְלִינַן, אוֹ בָּתַר מִינֵיהּ אָזְלִינַן? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma: If a bird has a protrusion on its thigh bone and it is rounded, or an animal has a protrusion on its thigh bone but it is not rounded, what is the *halakha*? Do we follow it, i.e., does the status of the sciatic nerve depend upon the physical properties of each particular animal, or do we follow its species, so that the sciatic nerve of an animal is always forbidden and that of a bird is always permitted? The Gemara responds: The question shall stand unresolved.**

*Ba'ei Rabbi Yirmiyah*: what if you were to find a bird whose thigh happened to be rounded in the relevant way — or conversely, a *beheimah* whose thigh lacked the rounded shape? *Mai* — what is the law? Does one go by the anatomy of the individual animal — *batar didei azlinan* — or by the anatomical norm of the species — *batar minei azlinan*? One might expect this to be obvious: the *Torah* legislates for species, not individual anatomical variations. Nevertheless the *Gemara* concludes with *teiku*, leaving the question unresolved, which means one must be strict in both directions. A bird whose thigh is rounded would require attention to the laws of *gid hanasheh*.

### Fetus Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְחֶלְבּוֹ מוּתָּר לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that the prohibition of the sciatic nerve applies to a late-term animal fetus in the womb. Rabbi Yehuda says: It does not apply to a fetus; and its fat is permitted. **Shmuel says: When the mishna states: And its fat is permitted, that is according to everyone.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶלְבּוֹ דְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא דִּשְׁלִיל – וְהָא מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי בֵּיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל, וְחֶלְבּוֹ אָסוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלִיל, וְחֶלְבּוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The fat of what? If we say that it is referring to the fat of a fetus, don't the *tanna'im* disagree about it, as it is taught in a *baraita*: The prohibition of the sciatic nerve applies to a late-term fetus, and its fat is prohibited; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says that the prohibition of the sciatic nerve does not apply to a late-term fetus, and its fat is permitted.**

*Amar Shmuel*: when the *Mishnah* says *ve-chelbo mutar* — the fat is permissible — this applies universally. *Mutar le-divrei ha-kol*. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: which fat is Shmuel speaking of? If it is the fat of the fetus itself, surely that is disputed — the same debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* that applies to *gid hanasheh* in a fetus applies equally to its fat. *De-tanya*: we learned in a *braita* that *Rabbi Meir* says *gid hanasheh* applies to a fetus and its *chelev* is forbidden, while *Rabbi Yehuda* says *gid hanasheh* does not apply and its fat is permissible. *Ve-halach Rabbi Meir le-shitato ve-Rabbi Yehuda le-shitato* — each follows his own reasoning consistently. *Rabbi Meir* holds that a nine-month viable fetus is an independent legal entity, so it has its own *gid hanasheh* and its own *chelev*. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds it is legally part of its mother, and therefore the *shechita* of the mother covers the fetus and neither *gid hanasheh* nor *chelev* applies independently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּבֶן תִּשְׁעָה חַי, וְהָלַךְ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְשִׁיטָתוֹ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְשִׁיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says that Rabbi Oshaya says in explanation of the *baraita*: This dispute applies to a nine-month-old, i.e., a full-term, fetus that remains alive after its mother has been slaughtered. And Rabbi Meir followed his general opinion in this regard (see 74a), that this fetus is considered a live animal independent of its mother. Consequently, it must undergo ritual slaughter in order for its meat to be permitted for consumption, and its fats and sciatic nerve are forbidden like those of any other animal. And Rabbi Yehuda followed his general opinion that such a fetus is not considered a live animal but rather part of its mother. Consequently, it does not require ritual slaughter, and its fats and sciatic nerve are permitted.**

### Meir vs. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא חֶלְבּוֹ דְּגִיד? הָא מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי בָּהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה מְחַטֵּט אַחֲרָיו בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא, וְחוֹתֵךְ שׇׁמְנוֹ מֵעִיקָּרוֹ – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גּוֹמְמוֹ עִם הַשּׁוֹפִי.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather, perhaps Shmuel's statement was said with regard to the fat of the sciatic nerve. But the *tanna'im* disagree about this case as well, as it is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to the sciatic nerve, one scrapes around it to remove it entirely in any place that it is found in the thigh, and one cuts out its fat from its source, i.e., even the fat that is embedded in the flesh; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: One cuts out the nerve and fat so that they are level with the flesh [***hashufi***] of the thigh, but there is no need to scrape out the parts embedded in the flesh. Consequently, there is a dispute about the fat surrounding the sciatic nerve as well.**

Reframing the question: perhaps *Shmuel* is not speaking about the fetus at all, but rather about the fat surrounding the *gid hanasheh* itself. Does that fat have an independent prohibition? *Ve-ela chelbo de-gid? Ha-miflag paligei bah* — but they dispute that too. *De-tanya*: *Rabbi Meir* says *mechateit achrav be-chol makom she-hu* — you must dig after the *gid hanasheh* wherever it branches and spreads, and *ve-choteich shemano me-ikaro* — you must cut away the fat from its very root. Note the language carefully: the *braita* uses *shemano* — *shuman*, permitted fat — not *chelev*, which is the term for biblically forbidden fat. This is not accidental.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer*: *gummo im ha-shofeh* — cut it off at the surface. *Tosafot* understands *Rabbi Yehuda* as making two separate claims: first, one need not dig after the minor branches of the *gid* at all — only the main sinew at the surface requires removal; and second, the fat is entirely permissible, not even subject to *minhag*. *Rashi* reads *Rabbi Yehuda* as acknowledging some surface fat must be removed but not requiring one to dig deeply. The debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* therefore operates on two axes: the extent to which one must dig after the branches of the *gid*, and the status of the surrounding fat. On the fat, both agree that it is not biblically forbidden — they use the word *shuman*, not *chelev*. The question is whether there is a rabbinic prohibition or merely a *minhag*.

### Fat Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם חֶלְבּוֹ דְּגִיד, מוֹדֶה שְׁמוּאֵל דִּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִדְּרַבָּנַן אָסוּר, דְּתַנְיָא: וְשׇׁמְנוֹ מוּתָּר, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשִׁים נָהֲגוּ בּוֹ אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, Shmuel was referring to the fat of the sciatic nerve, and Shmuel concedes that according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir the fat surrounding the sciatic nerve is forbidden by rabbinic law but permitted by Torah law. As it is taught in a *baraita*: Its fat is permitted by Torah law, but the Jewish people are holy and treated it as forbidden.**

The *Gemara* resolves *Shmuel*'s statement accordingly: *l'olam chelbo de-gid* — Shmuel is indeed speaking of the fat surrounding the *gid hanasheh*, not the fetus. *U-modeh Shmuel d'mederabbanan assur* — Shmuel acknowledges that even though it is biblically permissible, there is a rabbinic prohibition. A *braita* supports this: *ve-shumano mutar, Yisrael kedoshim nahagu bo issur* — the *shuman* is permissible, but the holy people of Israel have adopted the practice of treating it as forbidden. Whether *nahagu bo issur* connotes a *minhag* or a full rabbinic enactment is itself a matter of discussion, but the bottom line is clear: the fat around the *gid hanasheh* is biblically permitted and at most rabbinically or customarily restricted.

The *Gemara* then confirms that this reading is tied to *Rabbi Meir*: for *Rabbi Yehuda*, according to *Tosafot*, there is not even a *minhag* — the fat is entirely permissible. For *Rabbi Meir*, there is at minimum a rabbinic concern, and the language of *Yisrael kedoshim nahagu bo issur* reflects that elevated practice.

### No Taste Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר מָרְתָּא, אָמַר רַב: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא קְנוֹקָנוֹת שֶׁבּוֹ. עוּלָּא אָמַר: עֵץ הוּא, וְהַתּוֹרָה חִיְּיבָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the sciatic nerve **Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel bar Marta says that Rav says: The Torah prohibited only its thin, tendril-like nerve fibers [*kenokanot*] that branch off and run alongside the sciatic nerve, under the flesh. By contrast, the sciatic nerve itself, which is inedible and has no flavor, is therefore considered like wood rather than food, and is not forbidden. Conversely, Ulla says: The sciatic nerve is inedible and has no flavor, like wood, but nevertheless the Torah rendered one liable for eating it.**

*Amar Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta amar Rav*: *lo asra Torah ela knoknot she-bo* — the *Torah* only forbade the branching extensions of the *gid hanasheh*, not the main sinew itself. Why? Because the main *gid* itself — *eitz hu* — is like a piece of wood; it has no taste whatsoever. Yet the *Torah* nonetheless obligated one concerning it — *ve-rachmana chayveih*. This is a striking position: it relocates the primary prohibition from the main sinew to its branches, precisely because the branches have some slight taste while the main sinew has none.

### Main Sinew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: חוּטִין שֶׁבַּחֵלֶב אֲסוּרִין, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן, אַלְמָא ״חֵלֶב״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא וְלֹא חוּטִין, הָכָא נָמֵי ״גִּיד״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא וְלֹא קְנוֹקָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It stands to reason that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Ulla, because Rav Sheshet said that Rav Asi said: The strands of veins that are in the forbidden fat are forbidden, but one is not liable for eating them. Apparently, when the Merciful One states in the Torah that it is prohibited to eat fat, the prohibition applies only to the fat itself but not to the strands of veins. Here also, the Merciful One states in the Torah that it is prohibited to eat the sciatic nerve, but that does not include the tendril-like nerve fibers.**

*Amar Abaye*: the position of *Ulla* is more compelling. *De-amar Rav Sheshet amar Rav Assi*: the sinews that run through *chelev* — forbidden fat — are themselves forbidden, but one does not incur *chiyuv* for them. Why not? *Alma chelev amar rachmana ve-lo chutin* — the *Torah* says *chelev*, meaning fat, not the stringy tissues running through it. So too here: *hachi nami gid amar rachmana ve-lo knoknot* — the *Torah* says *gid*, meaning the main sinew, not its branches. The fact that the *gid* has no taste does not justify relocating the prohibition to the *knoknot*; the *Torah* specified the *gid*, and the *gid* it means.

The resolution is thus the reverse of what the earlier position proposed. The main sinew is indeed what the *Torah* prohibits — even though it is like wood and has no taste — while the branches are precisely what may not be subject to the biblical prohibition at all. This is in fact what underlay the *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* debate about the branches: if the *knoknot* are not the primary locus of the prohibition, there is reason to debate how far the rabbinic extension reaches. And the question of whether the *gid* has taste or not connects to a later debate in the *Gemara* — one that *Tosafot* analyzes in relation to these positions. Unsurprisingly, those who are troubled by the tastelessness of the main *gid* and wish to relocate the prohibition to the branches have a greater stake in insisting that the *gid* does have taste. Those who accept that the *gid* has no taste are more naturally comfortable affirming that it is precisely the main sinew — taste or no taste — that the *Torah* forbids.
