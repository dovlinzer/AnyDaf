# Kiddushin 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Assur B'Hana'ah

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: שְׁחוֹט לִי – בְּשֶׁלִּי. וְשֶׁלָּךְ – בְּשֶׁלָּךְ. מַה שֶׁלִּי בְּשֶׁלָּךְ – אָסוּר, אַף שֶׁלָּךְ בְּשֶׁלִּי – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a man betroths a woman with the items it enumerated, **or** with **non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard,** she is not betrothed. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** i.e., that one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a non-sacred animal that was slaughtered in the Temple courtyard, derived? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Meir: The Torah said: Slaughter for Me,** i.e., for offerings to God, **in My** place, inside the Temple courtyard, **and your** non-sacred animals that are intended for eating should be slaughtered **in your** place, outside the Temple courtyard. **Just as** one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **My** consecrated animals if they were slaughtered **in your** place, as one is prohibited from deriving benefit from a consecrated animal slaughtered outside the Temple, **so too,** one is **prohibited** from deriving benefit from **your** non-sacred animals if they were slaughtered **in My** place.

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion about objects that are *assur b'hana'ah* — forbidden to derive benefit from — and whether a man who attempts to betroth a woman using such an object achieves a valid *kiddushin*. The Gemara has been working through the relevant cases systematically, and the primary focus at this point is *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah*.

### Chullin Ba'azarah

*Chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* refers to non-sanctified animals — ordinary *chullin* — that were slaughtered within the *Beis HaMikdash* courtyard. This act is prohibited, and the resulting meat is forbidden in benefit. The question is both how we know the act is prohibited and how we know the prohibition extends to *hana'ah*.

### Pasuk Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה שֶׁלִּי בְּשֶׁלָּךְ עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, אַף שֶׁלָּךְ בְּשֶׁלִּי עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ לְהַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּן לַה׳... וְנִכְרַת״, עַל קׇרְבָּן – עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, עַל חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה – אֵין עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** so, **just as** the act of slaughtering **My** consecrated animals **in your** place is **punishable by *karet*, so too,** to the act of slaughtering **your** non-sacred animals **in My** place should be **punishable by *karet*.** To counter this logic, **the verse states:** "If a man from the house of Israel slaughters an ox or lamb…**and has not brought it to the door of the Tent of Meeting to sacrifice an offering to the Lord…and that man shall be cut off"** (Leviticus 17:3–4). This teaches that it is only **for an offering** that one slaughtered outside the Tent of Meeting of the Tabernacle, or the Temple courtyard, that **he is punishable with *karet*,** but **for non-sacred** animals that one slaughtered **in the** Temple **courtyard he is not punishable with *karet*.**

The Gemara derives both dimensions from the *pasuk* that speaks of slaughtering and eating meat outside the *Beis HaMikdash*: *ki yirchak mimcha hamakom — vezavachta ve'achalta* — "when the place is distant from you, you shall slaughter and eat." The implication is that if one is *within* the *Beis HaMikdash*, one may not slaughter and one may not eat. Additional *psukim* extend the prohibition to all types of animals slaughtered there — even blemished ones, even species that could never be brought as a *korban*. From this web of verses the Gemara establishes both the prohibition of the act and the forbidden status of the meat that results.

## R. Shimon vs. Chachamim

### Braita: R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ מָר יְהוּדָה לְרַב יוֹסֵף וּלְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה דַּהֲווֹ קָיְימִי אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי רַבָּה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּנְיָא: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, בְּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וּבְחוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בָּעֲזָרָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אַלְמָא חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Mar Yehuda found Rav Yosef and Rav Shmuel, son of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, standing at the entrance of Rabba's study hall. He said to them:** It **is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a firstborn donkey, with meat** cooked **in milk, or with non-sacred** animals **slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard, Rabbi Shimon says she is betrothed, and the Rabbis say she is not betrothed. Apparently, according to Rabbi Shimon,** the prohibition against deriving benefit from **non-sacred** animals **slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard is not by Torah law,** which is why the betrothal takes effect.

The Gemara cites a *braisa* that introduces a dissenting voice. *Hamkadesh b'peter chamor, b'basar bechalav, uvchullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* — one who betroths a woman using a firstborn donkey that has not yet had its neck broken, or with meat and milk cooked together, or with *chullin* slaughtered in the *azarah* — *Rabbi Shimon omer mekudeshet*: Rabbi Shimon holds that the betrothal is valid in all three cases. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, say she is not betrothed.

### Logic of Positions

Rabbi Shimon's position in each case has a traceable basis. With respect to *peter chamor*, an earlier *braisa* already established that according to Rabbi Shimon, a firstborn donkey is not forbidden in benefit while still alive — only after its neck is broken does it take on a more restricted status, analogous to *eglah arufah*. The case here would concern the living donkey, where Rabbi Shimon is consistent with his own position. As for *basar bechalav*, a position attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda — that meat and milk are permitted in benefit — was cited the previous day. Rashi notes that *Rabbi Shimon le'shitaso* implies this is the standard Rabbi Shimon, namely Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, though some read his *girsa* as referring specifically to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda. Either way, there is a *tanna* who holds that *basar bechalav* is permitted in benefit.

### Not Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה – יִשָּׂרְפוּ, וְכֵן חַיָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה בַּעֲזָרָה. אִישְׁתִּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Mar Yehuda **raises a contradiction** against this inference from a different *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: Non-sacred,** domesticated, animals **slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard** must **be burned, as well as a** non-sacred **undomesticated animal slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard.** This indicates that it is prohibited to derive benefit from them. **They were silent** and had no answer.

The most striking element of the *braisa* is what it implies about *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah*: if Rabbi Shimon rules that a woman is betrothed through such meat, the Gemara draws the conclusion that *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah leRabbi Shimon lav de'oraisa* — for Rabbi Shimon, the prohibition is not biblical. As Rashi explains: *kasavar issuro lav de'oraisa, lo issur shechitah velo issur hana'ah* — Rabbi Shimon holds that neither the prohibition of the slaughter itself nor the prohibition of deriving benefit is of biblical force. Importantly, Tosafot notes in several places that saying the prohibition is not *de'oraisa* need not mean the act itself carries no biblical violation. It may mean specifically that the *status* of the meat produced — the consequence — is not biblically prohibited, even if the act of slaughtering it in the *azarah* might itself still be problematic on some level.

## Lav De-Oraisa Issue

### Rashi Explains

Rashi's comment establishes that when the Gemara says *lav de'oraisa*, it is making a claim about the resulting status of the meat: neither the prohibition against performing the slaughter nor the prohibition against deriving benefit from the outcome rises to a biblical level for Rabbi Shimon. The implication — that the prohibition is rabbinic — is what the Gemara acknowledges and what generates the next problem.

### Rabbinic Prohibition

This raises a pointed question: if the meat of *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* is only rabbinically forbidden in benefit according to Rabbi Shimon, can it nevertheless be used for *kiddushin*? The question has two competing frameworks. On one view, from the Torah's perspective the meat retains real value — it is edible flesh from which benefit can in principle be derived — and a woman who receives it has received something of value, so the marriage should take effect. The Rabbis' prohibition is a superimposed restriction, but it does not alter the Torah-level reality of the object. On the other hand, one might argue that the Rabbis have the power to affect ownership and can declare an object effectively worthless in order to enforce their enactment, or alternatively that within an observant community, an object that no one is permitted to purchase or use has no practical monetary value — *de facto* rather than *de jure*. The prohibition's practical effect collapses the object's market value to zero, regardless of its theoretical worth.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot addresses this directly. The Gemara in the chapter *Kol Sha'ah* quotes a teaching of Rav: one who betroths a woman from the sixth hour onward on *erev Pesach* — when *chametz* becomes forbidden in benefit — *ein chosheshin le'kiddushin*, the *kiddushin* does not take effect. This applies even with the type of hard wheat (*chitei de'kurdenayta*) whose *chametz* status is only rabbinic — *de'einam assurim ela mi'de'rabanan*. This appears to be a clear ruling that a rabbinically forbidden object cannot effect *kiddushin*, even when the prohibition is only rabbinic. The question then is how to reconcile this with Rabbi Shimon's ruling that *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* — which is only rabbinically prohibited according to him — can nevertheless be used for *kiddushin*.

---

## Tam vs. Rashi

### Tam Position

Tosafot resolves the tension by distinguishing between two types of rabbinic prohibition, following the view of Rabbeinu Tam. The case of the hard wheat on *erev Pesach* — in the sixth hour — is technically rabbinic, but Rabbeinu Tam explains that it sits within the immediate ambit of a biblical prohibition. The wheat itself may be genuine *chametz*; the only reason the prohibition at that specific moment is rabbinic is that the sixth hour has not yet fully elapsed. In a matter of minutes, the very same wheat will be forbidden *de'oraisa*. That proximity to, and structural position within, a biblical category gives the rabbinic prohibition a weight it would not otherwise carry. The Rabbis, in extending the prohibition to the sixth hour, are extending a biblically established category — and that extension participates in the stringencies of the biblical category, including the effect of rendering the object valueless for *kiddushin*.

### Rashi Position

Rashi, by contrast, reads the Gemara in *Pesachim* differently. On his reading, the wheat in question is not genuine *chametz de'oraisa* at all; the rabbinic prohibition there does not operate as an extension of the biblical category. Instead, the object itself is simply rabbinically prohibited — which might appear to make Rashi's position more permissive. Yet Tosafot acknowledges Rashi's reading and still must grapple with the question of how *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* differs.

### Extension Doctrine

Tosafot's resolution draws a structural distinction. When the Rabbis create a prohibition that is an *extension* of a biblical category — even if the particular case being prohibited is not itself biblical — the object is treated as carrying the full weight of the biblical prohibition, including being considered devoid of monetary value for *kiddushin*. By contrast, when the Rabbis create a prohibition that is entirely new, *whole cloth* — something that has no analogue or root in any biblical prohibition — the object is not stripped of its value, and a *kiddushin* effected with it would be binding. *Chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah*, on this analysis, is a standalone rabbinic category with no biblical counterpart, and therefore even if forbidden, it does not render the object worthless. The *chametz* of the sixth hour, however, is structurally part of the biblical *chametz* framework, and therefore carries biblical weight into the *kiddushin* analysis — even where the specific prohibition at that hour is only rabbinic.

## Burning Challenge

### Mar Yehuda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: פָּלְגָאָ[ה] אוֹקְמִינְכוּ, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They came before Rabba** and told him about this difficulty. **He said to them: A quarrelsome** person **has put you up** to asking this question. **With what are we dealing here** in the first *baraita*? We are dealing with a case **where it was slaughtered and found** to be **a *tereifa*, and Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning** that an act of slaughter which does not render the animal permitted to be eaten is not called slaughter, so the animal does not have the status of a non-sacred animal slaughtered in the Temple courtyard.

Mar Yehuda encountered Rav Yosef and Shmuel bar Rabba — the son of Rabba bar bar Chana — standing at the entrance of Rabba's academy and posed a sharp challenge. The *braisa* just discussed attributes to Rabbi Shimon the position that *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* allows *kiddushin* — implying that the prohibition is not biblical. Yet another source also attributed to Rabbi Shimon states: *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah yisarefu* — such meat must be burned. And not only that, but even a *chayah* — a deer or similar animal that could never be brought as a *korban* — must be burned if slaughtered in the *azarah*. Rashi explains the significance of the burning: ordinarily, things forbidden in benefit are buried, not burned. Burning is a special stringency reserved for disqualified *kodashim*. The reasoning is that when a *chullin* animal is slaughtered in the courtyard and cannot be eaten, observers will assume it is a disqualified sacrifice — and disqualified sacrifices are burned. The extension to a *chayah*, a species with no conceivable connection to any sacrifice, pushes this further: even there, the meat must be burned. This level of stringency suggests the prohibition is treated as fully biblical. How, then, can Rabbi Shimon simultaneously say the woman is betrothed when *kiddushin* is performed with such meat? Rav Yosef and Shmuel bar Rabba had no answer and were silenced.

### Rava Responds

Rava's response is dismissive — calling them troublemakers — suggesting he had already explained this. His resolution: *hacha b'mai askinan? kegon shenishchata venimtza trefa* — the case in which Rabbi Shimon permits *kiddushin* is where the animal was slaughtered and then discovered to be a *treifah*, or was known to be a *treifah* when slaughtered. This is not a standard case. In a standard case of *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah*, Rabbi Shimon would agree that the meat is *assur b'hana'ah*.

### Shechita She'eina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַטְּרֵיפָה וְכֵן הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה – זֶה וָזֶה חוּלִּין בַּעֲזָרָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר בַּהֲנָאָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ḥullin* 2:4): With regard to **one who slaughters** a known ***tereifa*, as well as one who slaughters** an animal **and it was found** to be **a *tereifa*,** and **this and that** were **non-sacred** animals slaughtered **in the** Temple **courtyard, Rabbi Shimon permits** one **to** derive **benefit** from them, as explained previously. **And the Rabbis prohibit** one from deriving benefit from them, since they do not distinguish between an act of slaughter that does render the animal permitted to be eaten and one that does not. It is only in a case where the man betrothed her with a *tereifa* that was slaughtered in the Temple courtyard that Rabbi Shimon says she is betrothed, but not when the animal was not a *tereifa*.

The key principle underlying Rava's resolution is *shechita she'eina re'uya* — a slaughter that does not render the meat permissible. This principle was introduced the previous day: if the slaughter does not achieve its purpose of permitting the meat for consumption — because the animal was a *treifah* — then halachically the act is not categorized as a valid *shechita*. It therefore does not trigger the prohibition of *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah*, because that prohibition is predicated on an act of *shechita* having occurred, and a *shechita she'eina re'uya* is not such an act. The resolution is not an artificial narrowing of the case but is directly grounded in an explicit *braisa*.

## Treifah Status

### Known/Discovered Treifah

A *braisa* addresses the case explicitly: *hashochet et hatrefa* — one who slaughters an animal known to be a *treifah* — and *vehen hashochet venimtza trefa* — one who slaughters and then finds the animal to be a *treifah* upon examining the organs — *vezeh vezeh chullin ba'azarah* — and both cases involve *chullin* slaughtered in the *azarah*.

### R. Shimon vs. Chachamim

*Rabbi Shimon matir b'hana'ah* — Rabbi Shimon permits benefit — because the act constitutes *shechita she'eina re'uya*: since the animal was a *treifah*, the *shechita* did not accomplish its purpose of rendering the meat permissible, and therefore halachically no act of *chullin shenishchatu ba'azarah* occurred. The *Chachamim*, however, forbid benefit: *osrim* — they hold that physically the act of slaughter was performed, the animal was indeed killed through *shechita* in the *azarah*, and it does not matter that the animal was a *treifah*. The physical act triggers the prohibition regardless. This *braisa* confirms that Rava's *oikimta* is not an ad hoc narrowing but rather reflects an explicit controversy that maps precisely onto the case — and this is why Rava referred to it as something he had already established.

## Money Transfer

### Status Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָן וְקִידֵּשׁ בִּדְמֵיהֶן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מְנָלַן? מִדְּגַלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: ״וְהָיִיתָ חֵרֶם כָּמֹהוּ״, כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה מְהַיֶּיה הֵימֶנָּה – הֲרֵי הוּא כָּמוֹהוּ, מִכְּלָל דְּכׇל אִיסּוּרִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **one sold** any of the items from which one is prohibited to derive benefit **and betrothed** a woman **with** the **money** received from **their** sale, **she is betrothed.** With regard to the possibility of deriving benefit from money received in exchange for items from which benefit is forbidden, the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that this is permitted? The Gemara answers: It is **from** the fact that **the Merciful One reveals** in the Torah **with regard to** an object of **idol worship: "And you shall be banned like it"** (Deuteronomy 7:26), which teaches that **anything that you** cause to **come from it,** i.e., in exchange for it, **is like it.** It can be understood **by inference that** with regard to **all** the other **prohibitions of the Torah,** monies received for items from which benefit is forbidden are **permitted.**

The Gemara now pivots away from the entire list of *issurei hana'ah* to address a new question. What happens when one takes a forbidden object and sells it, thereby obtaining money? The act of sale was itself a transgression — but what is the status of the proceeds? The answer the Gemara establishes is that the status does *not* transfer. The meat and milk was *assur b'hana'ah*; one transgressed by converting it to money; but the money itself is fully one's own, unrestricted by any prohibition. One may use these proceeds to betroth a woman, and the *kiddushin* takes effect.

### Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מִינַּהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּשְׁבִיעִית שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let us derive** a general principle concerning other prohibitions **from** the case of idolatry. The Gemara answers: This derivation is not applied, **because** an object of **idol worship and** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year **are two verses that come as one,** i.e., to teach the same matter, since both prohibitions also extend to the money obtained for them, **and any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common aspect to apply to other cases.

The Gemara asks: how do we know the status does not transfer? The answer: *midigallei rachmana b'avodat kochavim* — because the Torah explicitly states by the case of an idol: *vehayita cherem kamohu* — "you shall be under the ban like it." The phrase *kol she'ata mehaya mimenu* — anything that arises through it — acquires the status of the idol. So if one sells an idol, the money received carries the idol's forbidden status. This could suggest a general principle that selling any forbidden object transfers its *issur* to the proceeds. The Gemara rejects this: far from a general rule, the idol case is the exception.

### Two Verses Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. שְׁבִיעִית מַאי הִיא? ״יוֹבֵל הִיא קֹדֶשׁ תִּהְיֶה לָכֶם״ – מָה קוֹדֶשׁ תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו, אַף שְׁבִיעִית תּוֹפֶסֶת דָּמֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: The source indicating that this is true of an object of **idol worship** is **what we said. What is** the source indicating that this *halakha* applies to produce of the **Sabbatical** Year? The verse states: **"It is a Jubilee, it shall be holy to you"** (Leviticus 25:12), from which it is derived: **Just as consecrated property transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed, **so too,** produce of **the Sabbatical** Year, which has the same status as the Jubilee Year, **transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which it is redeemed. Consequently, money used to acquire the produce of the Sabbatical Year will be subject to the same *halakhot* as the produce itself.

The exception is proven by the principle of *shnei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad*: when the Torah teaches the same rule in two separate cases, it is signaling that these cases are the *exceptions*, not examples of a general rule. The two cases are *avodah zarah* and *shevi'is* — Sabbatical year produce — where the Torah separately establishes that the forbidden status transfers to whatever is exchanged for it. Because both cases are stated explicitly, we conclude that the *rule* is the opposite: for all other *issurei hana'ah*, the status does *not* transfer to the proceeds of a sale.

## Shemitah Transfers

### Redemption Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה קוֹדֶשׁ תּוֹפֵס אֶת דָּמָיו וְיוֹצֵא לְחוּלִּין, אַף שְׁבִיעִית תּוֹפֶסֶת דָּמֶיהָ וְיוֹצְאָה לְחוּלִּין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּהְיֶה״ – בַּהֲוָיָיתָהּ תְּהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **If** so, one could also say that **just as** with **consecrated** property, it **transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is redeemed **and becomes desacralized, so too,** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year should **transfer** its sanctity to the **money** with which it is redeemed and **becomes desacralized.** Nevertheless, **the verse states: "Shall be,"** meaning: **As it is,** so **it shall be,** indicating that the Sabbatical-Year produce does not become desacralized.

The basis for including *shevi'is* among the exceptions is a verse: *yovel hi, kodesh tihye lakhem* — "it is the Jubilee year, it shall be holy to you." Just as something that is sanctified (*kodesh*) transfers its status to whatever it is redeemed into — and the original object is thereby desanctified — so too *shemitah* produce transfers its *kedushah* to whatever one obtains in exchange for it. One might then think that, as with *pidyon*, once the status transfers to the money, the original produce loses its *kedushah*. Against this the Torah says *tihye* — "it shall be" — *b'havayatah tehei*: it retains its sanctity. The original *shemitah* produce never exits its status.

### Last Object Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? לָקַח בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית בָּשָׂר – אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מִתְבַּעֲרִים בַּשְּׁבִיעִית. בַּבָּשָׂר דָּגִים – יָצָא בָּשָׂר נִכְנְסוּ דָּגִים. בְּדָגִים יַיִן – יָצְאוּ דָּגִים נִכְנַס יַיִן. בְּיַיִן שֶׁמֶן – יָצָא יַיִן נִכְנַס שֶׁמֶן. הָא כֵּיצַד? אַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן נִתְפָּס בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, וּפְירִי עַצְמוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **How so?** If one **purchased meat with produce** of the **Sabbatical** Year, both **these and those,** i.e., the meat and the produce, **are disposed of in the Sabbatical** Year. The sanctity of the Sabbatical Year takes effect with regard to the meat as well. It is treated like the produce, and must be disposed of when the obligation to dispose of the Sabbatical-Year produce goes into effect. If he then purchases **fish with** this **meat,** the **meat loses** its consecrated status, and the **fish assumes** the consecrated state. If he then purchases **wine with** these **fish,** the **fish loses** its consecrated status and the **wine assumes** the consecrated state. If he then purchases **oil with** the **wine,** the **wine loses** has its consecrated status and the **oil assumes** the consecrated state. **How so? The final** item purchased has the sanctity **of** the **Sabbatical** Year **transferred** to it, **and the** Sabbatical-Year **produce itself** remains **forbidden.**

The Gemara illustrates through a chain of exchanges: if one takes *shemitah* fruit and exchanges it for meat, both the original fruit and the meat now carry *kedushat shevi'is* — meaning they must be consumed before the time of *biur* and cannot be used for other purposes. If one then exchanges the meat for fish, the meat exits the status and the fish take it on. Fish exchanged for wine: the fish exit, the wine enters. Wine exchanged for oil: the wine exits, the oil enters. The rule is *acharon acharon nitpas beshevi'is*: each successive exchange — the *last* object in the swap — carries the *shemitah* status. And *peri atzmo assur*: the original fruit itself always retains its status regardless.

### Exception Parallels

The Gemara's position that *shemitah* and *avodah zarah* are the two exceptions raises a certain conceptual question. *Shemitah* is not, at least at its core, an *issur hana'ah* in the standard sense — it is a *kedushah* status that governs how produce must be treated and consumed. It might seem more natural to include only *avodah zarah* as the single exception to the rule that status does not transfer, since *avodah zarah* is the paradigmatic case of an object forbidden in benefit. One could reasonably suggest that the analogy between *shemitah* and *avodah zarah* works because of the *biur* stage — after the time of *biur*, *shemitah* produce that has not been destroyed does become *assur b'hana'ah*, and perhaps it is from that angle that the Gemara makes the comparison. Regardless, the operative conclusion is clear: only by these two does the status transfer; for everything else, the proceeds of a sale are permitted.

## Mishnah: Terumot

### Objects Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמוֹת וּבַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת, וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, וּבְמֵי חַטָּאת, וּבְאֵפֶר חַטָּאת – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *terumot*, or with tithes, or with** the foreleg, cheeks, and stomach of an animal, which are given as **gifts** to priests, **or with the water of purification,** which is sprinkled on an impure person during the purification rite for impurity imparted by a corpse, **or with the ashes of purification,** which were mixed with the water sprinkled on an impure person during the purification rite for impurity imparted by a corpse, in all of these cases **she is betrothed, and** this is so **even if** the man betrothing her is **an Israelite,** not a priest or a Levite.

The final *mishnah* of the chapter takes up a new category of objects that can be used for *kiddushin*: *hamekadesh b'terumos u'b'maasros u'b'matnos b'mei chatas u'b'eifer parah harei zo mekudeshes v'afilu Yisrael* — one who betroths with *terumah*, with *ma'aser*, with priestly gifts, with water used in the *parah adumah* purification process, or with the ashes of the *parah adumah* — the woman is betrothed, and this applies even if the man is a non-kohen.

### Priest Gifts

The *matanot* referred to are the portions of a slaughtered animal that must be given to a *kohen* — the *zero'a*, the cheeks, and the maw — as obligated by the Torah. *Terumah* and *ma'aser* are the portions of one's grain that must be given to a *kohen* and *levi* respectively. The initial analysis suggests these cases are relatively straightforward: *ma'aser* and priestly gifts carry no inherent sanctity that would restrict their use, and *terumah*, even if the woman cannot eat it herself, could be resold to a *kohen*. On this surface reading, the *mishnah* seems obvious — if one possesses *terumah*, it has market value and can be given in *kiddushin*. The Gemara, however, will complicate this picture.

### Chatas/Parah

The more intriguing cases are the *mei chatas* — water that has been or is to be mixed with the ashes of the *parah adumah* — and the *eifer parah adumah* itself — the ashes of the red heifer used in the purification ritual. These raise genuine questions: are these objects *assur b'hana'ah*? Do the ashes of an animal used for an elaborate purification ritual carry any forbidden status if applied to a non-purification purpose? If one possesses this water or these ashes, can they be sold? And in what scenario does one use them to betroth a woman? The Gemara will address these questions directly.

## Tovas Hana'ah

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמוֹת, וּבְמַעַשְׂרוֹת, וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת בְּמֵי חַטָּאת, וּבְאֵפֶר פָּרָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ulla says:** The **benefit of discretion,** i.e., the benefit accrued from the option of giving *teruma* and tithes to whichever priest or Levite one chooses, **does not have monetary** value. **Rabbi Abba raised an objection to Ulla** from the mishna: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *terumot*, or with tithes, or with gifts, with the water of purification, or with the ashes** of the red **heifer, she is betrothed, and** this is so **even if** the man betrothing her is **an Israelite.** This indicates that although an Israelite cannot consume the priestly gifts, he may nevertheless betroth a woman with them, since he possesses the option to give them to the priest or Levite of his choice. That benefit has monetary value, and it is that value that he uses to betroth a woman, who can then give them to whichever priest or Levite she chooses.

Ulla offers a teaching that reframes how the entire *mishnah* is to be understood: *tovas hana'ah einah mamon* — the benefit one receives from the ability to choose which *kohen* or *levi* to give an obligatory gift to is not considered monetary value. This statement implies that Ulla understands the *mishnah* to be teaching precisely the case where the man does not own the *terumah* outright but has merely separated it and must give it away — the interest Ulla is addressing is not ownership of the object but the privilege of deciding its recipient.

### Separation Context

The scenario Ulla envisions is *hamafrish terumah mitvalo* — a man who has separated *terumah* from his grain, which he is now obligated to give to a *kohen*. He cannot keep it himself. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah*, on Ulla's reading, is about what exactly this man gives the woman when he betroths her with this *terumah*.

### Choice Benefit

What the woman receives, on this reading, is not the *terumah* in a full proprietary sense — she too must ultimately give it to a *kohen* — but rather the right to choose *which* kohen receives it. That privilege of selection — *tovas hana'ah* — is what Ulla characterizes as *einah mamon*, not genuine monetary ownership. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* would then be that despite the man's lack of full ownership in the object, the *kiddushin* takes effect. This makes the *mishnah* a genuinely novel ruling rather than an obvious case.

## Rav Abba Challenge

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ טְבָלִים מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן. וְקָא סָבַר: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to him:** You have misunderstood the case of the mishna, since **here** the case is **with an Israelite who came into** possession of **untithed produce** as an inheritance **from the household of his mother's father,** who was **a priest, and** the *tanna* of the mishna **holds** that **gifts that have not been separated are considered as though they have been separated.** The untithed produce is not viewed as one entity, but rather is viewed as a mixture of regular produce, *teruma*, and tithes. This *teruma* belonged to his grandfather, who was a priest. Since he has inherited this *teruma*, he has ownership rights to it in addition to the benefit of discretion. While he cannot consume this produce because he is an Israelite, he can sell it to a priest and keep the money. Since it has actual value, it can be used to betroth a woman.

Rav Abba challenges Ulla. If *tovas hana'ah einah mamon* — if the right to choose the recipient is not monetary — then how can the *mishnah* rule that the woman is betrothed? *Kiddushin* requires the man to give the woman something of genuine monetary value, at minimum a *perutah*'s worth. If the man has no real ownership stake in the *terumah*, what has he given her?

### Easy Answer

Ulla's response is that the *mishnah* need not be read in the case Rav Abba assumes. The easy reading — which Ulla rejects as too trivial — is that the man actually *owns* the *terumah*. A non-*kohen* can come to own *terumah* in several ways: he might have purchased it from *kohanim* as a commercial venture, he might have inherited it from a relative who was a *kohen*, or similar scenarios. If the man owns the *terumah* outright, the case is obvious — *terumah* has market value, it can be sold to *kohanim*, and using it for *kiddushin* is a straightforward application of giving the woman an object of value. This reading, Ulla argues, trivializes the *mishnah*.

### Rashi Critique

Rashi makes the point explicit: the reason we cannot be satisfied with the easy case is that it renders the *mishnah* unremarkable. Of course one can betroth with an object one owns, even if it is *terumah*. The *mishnah*'s ruling demands a harder case to be interesting and to constitute a genuine *chiddush*. Therefore the *mishnah* must be speaking of a more complex scenario — which is what the Gemara proceeds to establish.

## Inherited Tevel

### Tevel Inheritance

The harder case the Gemara identifies: a *Yisrael* who inherited *tevel* — grain from which *terumos u'maasros* have not yet been separated — from his maternal grandfather, who was a *kohen*. He himself is not a *kohen*, since *kehunah* follows the father's lineage. Nevertheless, because of his grandfather's status, there is a potential claim to the *terumah* embedded in the *tevel*.

### Gifts Present

The operative principle: *matanot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — gifts that have not yet been separated are considered as though they have been separated. The terumah exists in potential within the *tevel*, and a *kohen* who owns *tevel* is considered, on this view, to have already staked his claim to the *terumah* portion even before the physical act of separation.

### Grandfather's Claim

Because the grandfather was a *kohen*, his claim to the *terumah* portion of his *tevel* was already established during his lifetime, on the principle that *matanot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan*. When the grandson inherits this *tevel* and separates the *terumah*, he is not separating *terumah* that is owed to any other *kohen* — he is separating *terumah* that was already owned by his grandfather, which he has now inherited. He may keep it himself. This is the genuinely novel case of the *mishnah*: the grandson is considered an actual owner of the *terumah* — not through *tovas hana'ah*, but through the inherited status — and betrothing with it is therefore effective. Ulla's point, then, is preserved: *tovas hana'ah einah mamon*, and the *mishnah* is not speaking of a case of mere *tovas hana'ah* but of real ownership transmitted through the grandfather.

## Chiyya to Huna

### Direct Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין מֵרַב הוּנָא: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה מָמוֹן אוֹ אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵיתוּהָ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמוֹת וּבְמַעַשְׂרוֹת וּבְמַתָּנוֹת בְּמֵי חַטָּאת וּבְאֵפֶר פָּרָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָאו אוֹקֵימְנָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ טְבָלִים מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin inquired of Rav Huna:** Does the **benefit of discretion have monetary** value, **or does it not have monetary** value? Rav Huna **said to him: You learned it** in the mishna: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *terumot*, or with tithes, or with gifts,** or **with the water of purification, or with the ashes** of the red **heifer, she is betrothed, and** this is so **even if** the man betrothing her is **an Israelite.** This indicates that the benefit of discretion has monetary value. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin **said to him: But didn't we establish it,** in accordance with the opinion of Ulla, as referring **to an Israelite who came into** possession of **untithed produce** as an inheritance **from the household of his mother's father,** who was **a priest?**

Rav Chiyya bar Avin posed a direct question to Rav Huna: *tovas hana'ah mamon o eino mamon?* — is the benefit of choosing which recipient to give an obligatory gift to considered monetary value or not?

### Huna Answers

Rav Huna pointed to the *mishnah*: *tanitua* — you have learned it. *Hamekadesh b'terumah u'v'ma'aser u'v'matnot b'mei chatas u'v'eifer parah harei zo mekudeshes v'afilu b'Yisrael* — he understood the *mishnah* as Ulla had understood it, that the case involves a man who has separated *terumah* and must give it to a *kohen*, and whose right to choose the recipient constitutes the value given to the woman. From the fact that she is betrothed, Rav Huna concluded that *tovas hana'ah mammona*.

### Clarification Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוּצָאָה אַתְּ. אִיכְּסִיף, הוּא סָבַר: מִשְּׁמַעְתָּא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא: רַב אַסִּי דְּהוּצָל קָאֵי כְּוָתָיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said to him: You are out [*hotza'a*].** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin **was embarrassed,** as **he thought** Rav Huna **told him** he was out, i.e., wrong, **due to** the ***halakha* he** stated. Sensing Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin's embarrassment, Rav Huna **said to him: This is what I said:** You are a *Hutzla'a*, as **Rav Asi, from** the town of **Huzal, stands in accordance with your** opinion.

Rav Chiyya pressed back: but haven't you already explained the *mishnah* as the case of a *Yisrael* who inherited *tevalim* from his maternal grandfather the *kohen* — a case of genuine ownership, not mere *tovas hana'ah*? If the *mishnah* was explained away as a case of real ownership, it cannot serve as a proof that *tovas hana'ah* is *mamon*.

### Rav's Deflection

The response: *hutzat* — that explanation, the maternal grandfather case, is excluded from serious consideration, or alternatively, it designates the speaker as following the approach of a scholar from *Hutzal*. The exchange becomes uncomfortable: Rav Chiyya took *hutzat* as an insult, implying he was not fit to be in the *beis midrash*. The speaker clarified that he meant *hutzat* in reference to the approach of Rav Assi of *Hutzal* — a buffering of the offense. But the underlying point stands: Rav Huna's answer is that the *mishnah* should be read in its primary sense as a case of *tovas hana'ah*, and that reading demonstrates *tovas hana'ah mammona*.

## Theft Debate

### Theft Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַגּוֹנֵב טִבְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם לוֹ דְּמֵי טִבְלוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי חוּלִּין שֶׁבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that it is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** The *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ma'aser Sheni* 3:9) taught: **One who steals another's untithed produce pays him the** full **value of the other's untithed produce;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: He pays only the value of the non-sacred** produce **that it contains.**

The Gemara introduces a related dispute to probe the question of *tovas hana'ah* and monetary ownership further. If one steals *tevel* — grain from which *terumos u'maasros* have not been separated — from a non-*kohen*, how much must the thief repay? The owner would eventually be obligated to give away a portion: at minimum approximately 2% as *terumah* and 10% as *ma'aser*. Does the thief owe the full face value, or only the value of the portion the owner could actually retain?

### Positions

Rabbi holds: *meshalem lo d'mei tevelo* — the thief pays the full value of the *tevel*. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda holds: *ein meshalem lo ella d'mei chullin shebo* — the thief pays only the value of the *chulin* portion — approximately 88% of the total.

### Benefit = Ownership?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה מָמוֹן, וּמָר סָבַר: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** What, is it not** the case **that they disagree about this** issue: **That** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that the **benefit of discretion has monetary** value, so he must be compensated beyond the value of the non-sacred produce; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the **benefit of discretion does not have monetary** value, and only the non-sacred produce is of value to the owner.

The Gemara initially suggests this may reflect the debate about *tovas hana'ah*: Rabbi, who requires full payment, may hold that *tovas hana'ah mammona* — the owner's right to choose which *kohen* and *levi* receives the gifts constitutes genuine monetary ownership, and therefore the full value of the *tevel* belongs to him. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda, who requires payment only for the *chullin* portion, may hold that *tovas hana'ah eino mamon* — the owner has no monetary stake in the portions destined for *kohen* and *levi*, and the thief owes nothing for them. Rashi's reading of this position is striking: it would mean that *tovas hana'ah mammona* implies full ownership of the face value of the object — not merely that the owner has *some* stake, but that he is considered the complete owner of those portions, able to demand their full market value.

## Matanot Status

### Reframing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה אֵינָהּ מָמוֹן, וְהָכָא בִּטְבָלִים שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ מִבֵּית אֲבִי אִמּוֹ כֹּהֵן וּבְמַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין. וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone agrees** that the **benefit of discretion does not have monetary** value, **and here** the case is **with an Israelite who came into** possession of **untithed produce** as an inheritance **from the household of his mother's father,** who was **a priest. And they disagree with regard to** the question of whether or not **gifts that have not been separated are considered as though they have been separated.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that they **are considered as though they have been separated,** which means that the grandson inherited the *teruma* itself from his grandfather, so the thief must repay him the value of the *terumot* and tithes as well. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that such gifts **are not considered as though they have been separated;** and the produce is viewed as regular untithed produce, where the Israelite has only the benefit of discretion, which is of no monetary value.

The Gemara then offers an alternative framework in which the dispute between Rabbi and Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda does not actually turn on the question of *tovas hana'ah* at all. Rather, all parties could agree that *tovas hana'ah eino mamon*. The dispute instead concerns the application of the principle *matanot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — whether gifts that have not yet been separated are considered as though they already have been — to a specific factual scenario.

### Rabbi: Pre-owned

On this reading, Rabbi holds that *matanot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — the obligatory portions are considered already separated and already owned by the eventual recipient. The owner of the *tevel*, on this view, already fully owns the entire hundred bushels because the *terumah* and *ma'aser* portions within it are treated as though they had already been separated and allocated to him — that is, if his ancestor was a *kohen* who owned the grain, the *terumah* was already his. Hence the thief must pay the full value.

### R.Y.B.Y: Not Pre-owned

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda disagrees: *matanot shelo hurmu* are *not* like *shehurmu* — the obligatory gifts have no legal existence until physically separated, and until that moment the owner has no claim to those portions. Therefore the thief owes only for the portions the owner actually owns — the *chullin* part.

## Thief Penalty

### Penalty Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לֵית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי, דְּקַנְסוּהוּ רַבָּנַן לְגַנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is **not** in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel; and here, this is** the **reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: Although the benefit of discretion does not have monetary value, and by right should not lead to reimbursement, **the Sages penalized the thief.** If he did not need to pay for the *teruma* contained in the produce, he would have been able to keep it, as no priest has the legal ability to demand that the *teruma* be given specifically to him.

The Gemara ultimately rejects the interpretation that the dispute is about ownership — whether *tovas hana'ah* or *matanot* — and instead explains Rabbi's ruling as a rabbinic penalty. Even if technically the owner of the *tevel* has no monetary claim to the portions destined for *kohen* and *levi*, the Rabbis imposed a penalty on the thief: *kansuha rabbanan le'ganav* — they fined the thief — requiring payment of the full value.

### Escape Logic

The reasoning: if the thief only had to pay 88 dollars on 100 dollars' worth of stolen grain, he would escape any obligation to the *kohen* and *levi* whose portions he effectively consumed. Any *kohen* or *levi* who came to claim his share would be told: prove that you are the specific *kohen* or *levi* to whom this grain was going to be given. They cannot prove it, and the thief walks away without paying the full social debt his theft incurred. The Rabbis found it intolerable that a thief could steal a hundred dollars' worth of grain and walk away having paid only 88. Therefore, despite the owner's limited technical ownership of the obligatory portions, the thief is penalized and must pay the full value to the owner.

### Shmuel Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּקַנְסוּהוּ רַבָּנַן לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת, דְּלָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְשַׁהוֹיֵהּ לְטִיבְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel, and here, this is** the **reasoning of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda: The Sages penalized the owner, since he should not have left his untithed produce** in that state, and should have separated *teruma* without delay.

An additional resolution: one might say that everyone agrees with Shmuel's principle — *chitah achas poteres es hakari* — even a single grain of *terumah* exempts the entire pile, meaning the owner theoretically could separate only the smallest possible amount of *terumah* and retain nearly the full 90% *chullin* portion. Under this view, the dispute is not about ownership at all, but about the proper calculation of repayment given different assumptions about how much the owner would actually give away in practice versus what he was technically obligated to give.

## Chatas Service

### Payment Void

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּתְרוּמוֹת, וּבַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת, וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, בְּמֵי חַטָּאת, וּבְאֵפֶר פָּרָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת וְאַף עַל פִּי יִשְׂרָאֵל. וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַנּוֹטֵל שָׂכָר לָדוּן – דִּינָיו בְּטֵלִים, לְהָעִיד – עֵדוּתוֹ בְּטֵלָה, לְהַזּוֹת וּלְקַדֵּשׁ – מֵימָיו מֵי מְעָרָה, וְאֶפְרוֹ אֵפֶר מִקְלֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with *terumot*, or with tithes, or with** the **gifts** given to priests, or **with the water of purification, or with the ashes** of the red **heifer,** in all of these cases **she is betrothed, and** this is so **even if** the man betrothing her is **an Israelite. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this from the following *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bekhorot* 3:5): With regard to **one who accepts payment to judge, his judgments are nullified.** Similarly, with regard to one who accepts payment **to testify, his testimony is nullified.** With regard to one who accepts payment **to sprinkle** the purification water on one who was impure from impurity imparted by a corpse, **or to sanctify** the purification water by placing the ashes of the red heifer in them, **his water** is considered **cave water,** which is generally foul, **and his ashes are burnt ashes.** Using these items to betroth a woman is analogous to being paid for them, so they should be considered as having no monetary value, and the betrothal should not take effect.

The Gemara now turns to the final cases in the *mishnah*: *mei chatas* and *eifer parah adumah* — the water and ashes used in the purification ritual of the *parah adumah*. The Gemara challenges from a *braisa*: *ha'notel sachar ladun — dinav bateilin* — one who accepts payment for rendering judgment voids his rulings. Similarly, *l'ha'id — eidaso beteilah* — a witness who accepts payment voids his testimony. And then the relevant case: *l'hazos u'l'kadesh — memav mei mearah v'eifro eifer mikleh* — one who accepts payment for performing the *hazayah* (sprinkling of the purification water) or for the *kiddush* (mixing the ashes into the water) — his water is considered like water drawn from a swamp (not *mayim chayim* as required) and his ashes are like ordinary cooking ashes. The ritual is entirely voided. The reason, as Tosafot explains, is that one may not accept payment for performing a *mitzvah*; doing so invalidates the act, at least rabbinically. This creates an obvious problem: how can the *mishnah* say one may betroth a woman with *mei chatas* or *eifer parah*, if accepting benefit from performing these acts voids the ritual and is itself prohibited?

### Forbidden Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּשָׂכָר הֲבָאָה וּמִילּוּי, כָּאן בִּשְׂכַר הַזָּאָה וְקִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult,** since **here,** the mishna is referring to one who betroths a woman **with** the value of the **payment for bringing and filling** the vessel with the purification waters, for which one is permitted to accept payment. Performing this act for the woman is comparable to giving her an item of value, since she will not have to pay someone to bring and fill the vessel for her. **There,** the *baraita* dealing with one who accepts payment for sprinkling or sanctifying the water is referring **to payment for** the actual **sprinkling and sanctifying.**

Abaye resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan b'sachar hava'ah u'milui v'kan b'sachar haza'ah v'kiddush* — there is no difficulty. The *braisa* that voids the ritual refers to payment for the *mitzvah* acts themselves — *hazayah* (sprinkling) and *kiddush* (mixing the ashes with the water). These are the ritual acts that constitute the *mitzvah*, and one may not be compensated for performing them. Our *mishnah*, however, refers to something entirely different.

### Permitted Service

The permitted acts for which payment may be accepted — and with which *kiddushin* may be effected — are *hava'ah u'milui*: transporting the ashes and drawing the water. These are logistical services, not *mitzvah* acts. The *Beis HaMikdash* required people to carry ashes from one location to another and to draw water for use in the *parah adumah* process. These tasks are physical labor, not ritual acts, and one may legitimately charge for them. In whatever scenario the man provides this service to the woman rather than to the *Beis HaMikdash* directly — and she compensates him through *kiddushin* — the betrothal takes effect, because he has given her something of genuine value: his service.

### Language Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי הָכָא: ״בְּמֵי חַטָּאת וּבְאֵפֶר פָּרָה״, וְקָתָנֵי הָתָם: ״לְהַזּוֹת וּלְקַדֵּשׁ״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: According to this answer, the language of the mishna and the *baraita* **is also precise, as it teaches here,** in the mishna, that she is betrothed **with the water of purification or with the ashes of purification,** which indicates that the water and ashes have not yet been mixed together; **and it teaches there,** in the *baraita*: **To sprinkle or to sanctify,** indicating that he receives payment for the actual sprinkling and sanctification. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the correct explanation of the mishna and the *baraita*.

Abaye adds that this resolution is also *dika* from the language of the *mishnah* itself: *hacha b'mei chatas u'b'eifer parah* — the *mishnah* says "water" and "ashes" — referring to the objects transported and drawn, not to the acts of sprinkling or mixing. The contrasting *braisa* explicitly states *l'hazos u'l'kadesh* — "for sprinkling and for mixing" — highlighting that the prohibition targets the ritual acts themselves, while the *mishnah*'s permissive ruling concerns the preparatory physical services. *Shema minah* — this distinction is confirmed by the precise language each source chooses to employ.
