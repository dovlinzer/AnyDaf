# Menachot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Sprinkling Debate

### Intro & Contradiction

The present discussion begins as a continuation of 27b, where the Gemara had set up a contradiction between two *braitot*. One *braita* holds that the sprinkling of blood toward the *heichal* — even if not precisely directed — is valid (*kasher*); the other holds that while this imprecision is generally acceptable, by the *parah adumah* it renders the sprinkling invalid (*pasul*). The question the Gemara investigates is what this debate is actually about, and it unfolds in the larger framework of *me'akev* — the principle that certain component parts are indispensable to the validity of a ritual act.

### Kodesh Hakodashim Entry

To explain the debate over directional precision, the Gemara anchors the discussion in a *braita* about entry into the *kodesh hakodashim*. The *braita* opens with the laws of *mechuserai kaparah* — those whose *tahara* process is complete except for the final bringing of a *korban*, representing the lowest level of residual *tumah*. If such a person enters the *azarah* unintentionally (*bishogeg*), he is liable to bring a *chatat*; if willfully (*b'meizid*), he is liable to *kares*. This applies *kal vachomer* to those with more substantial *tumah*, including the *tvu'el yom* and certainly those who have not yet gone to the *mikveh* at all.

The *braita* then turns to those who are ritually pure (*tahorim*) but go beyond their permitted boundaries. One who enters the *heichal* — the roofed inner sanctum housing the *shulchan* and the golden *mizbeach* — is punishable by lashes, since the verse *v'lo yavo bechol et el hakodesh* constitutes a negative prohibition, applying even to a *kohen* who enters without a legitimate purpose (*bi'ah reikanit*). If, however, one enters *mibeis laparoches* — into the *kodesh hakodashim* behind the curtain — and stands *el pnei hakaporet*, directly in front of the *aron*, the punishment is divine death (*mita biyedei shamayim*). That is the first opinion.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disputes this. In his view, the entire *heichal* and the *kodesh hakodashim* alike are subject only to lashes. Divine death applies exclusively when one stands *el pnei hakaporet* — directly in front of the *aron*.

### Verse Parsing

The Gemara asks: *b'mai kamifligi* — what precisely are they debating? The answer is that they disagree over how to parse the verse in *Vayikra*: *daber el Aharon achicha v'al yavo bechol et el hakodesh mibeis laparoches al pnei hakaporet asher al ha'aron v'lo yamus* — "Speak to Aharon your brother, and he shall not come at any time into the sanctuary, behind the curtain, in front of the *kaporet* that is upon the *aron*, lest he die." Three locational phrases appear: *el hakodesh* (into the sanctuary), *mibeis laparoches* (behind the curtain), and *al pnei hakaporet* (in front of the *kaporet*). The outer phrases are not contested: *el hakodesh* is clearly the negative prohibition of entering, and *al pnei hakaporet* — when combined with *v'lo yamus* — is clearly a case of divine death. The dispute is about the middle phrase, *mibeis laparoches*: does it attach to the first half of the verse (the prohibition of entry), or to the second half (the death penalty)?

### Rabbanan Position

The *rabbanan* hold that *el hakodesh* — the prohibition of entry — refers to the *heichal*, and that both *mibeis laparoches* and *al pnei hakaporet* together trigger the death penalty. Their reasoning is textual: if *Rabbi Yehuda* were correct that one incurs *mita* only when standing directly in front of the *aron*, then the phrase *mibeis laparoches* would be entirely superfluous. The Torah could simply have written *el hakodesh* (negative prohibition) and *al pnei hakaporet* (death), and one would naturally have known that the *heichal* prohibits entry and that standing before the *aron* triggers death. Since the *heichal* is already prohibited, there is no need to separately prohibit the *kodesh hakodashim* — it is obviously included. The only reason to write *mibeis laparoches* is to teach that even entering the *kodesh hakodashim* alone, without confronting the *aron*, is sufficient to incur divine death. Furthermore, *d'heichal kulei ikrei kodesh* — the *heichal* is already called *kodesh*, as the verse states that the *paroches* separates *bein hakodesh u'vein kodesh hakodashim*, making clear that *kodesh* is the *heichal*. There is therefore no possibility of confusion requiring clarification.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* defends his reading by turning the same argument around. If we had only written *el hakodesh*, one might have thought this referred to the *kodesh hakodashim* — interpreting *kodesh* as the holiest place — and thereby excluded the *heichal* from any prohibition entirely. The Torah therefore had to include *mibeis laparoches* explicitly as a clarification that *kodesh* means the *heichal*, and that the *heichal* is prohibited. Once the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim* are both under a negative prohibition through this reading, the separate phrase *al pnei hakaporet* followed by *v'lo yamus* is unnecessary unless it is coming to tell us something new: only when one stands directly in front of the *aron* is the penalty death — otherwise it is merely a negative prohibition.

The Gemara then asks how the *rabbanan* account for the phrase *al pnei hakaporet* if they already derive *mita* from *mibeis laparoches*. The answer is *l'mivti derech meshupash* — the phrase is not superfluous but teaches that divine death applies only when entry is made facing the *aron* directly, not indirectly. A *braita* of *Beis Rav Eliezer ben Yaakov* is cited: *al pnei hakaporet keidma* — facing the *kaporet* toward the east — establishes a paradigm (*binyan av*) that wherever the Torah says "facing" (*pnei*), it means the eastern face.

## Directional Precision

### Eastern Facing

This paradigm is grounded in a broader structural observation about biblical orientation. The Torah consistently assumes that a person stands facing east: hence the word for south (*teimana*) derives from *yamin* — right — because if one faces east, the south is to the right. West is described as *acharei derech mivo hashemesh*, "behind toward the setting sun," since *achor* is opposite *kadim* (east). Avraham's offer to Lot — *im hasmol v'eimina v'im hayamin v'asme'ila* — is understood as referring to south and north respectively, since he assumes an eastward orientation.

### Rashi's Forward Entry

*Rashi* draws from this the precise implication for the *kodesh hakodashim*: the phrase *pnei hakaporet* teaches that one incurs *chiyuv mita* only if one enters facing the *aron* — through the front, as it were. If one digs a tunnel and enters from the side or from below, or enters walking backwards, or enters at an angle, one would not be liable, because one is not entering *facing* the *aron*. Without the phrase *pnei hakaporet*, we would have derived liability from simply entering the *kodesh hakodashim*; the phrase comes to qualify that: one must enter in a forward-facing manner. The question is purely physical — how one is oriented upon entry — not a matter of intent.

### El Means Exact

*Rabbi Yehuda* then pushes further. If all the Torah wished to convey was the requirement of forward-facing entry, it could have simply written *pnei*. Why does it write *el pnei*? *Shma mina: el davka* — the word *el* (toward, in front of) means precisely and directly in front, not merely in that general direction. This is the crux of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, and it carries implications that extend far beyond the *kodesh hakodashim*.

### Parah Adumah Link

The connection to the opening debate now becomes clear. *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that *el pnei hakaporet* means exactly in front, reads similarly the verse concerning the *parah adumah* — *v'hizah el nochach pnei ohel moed*, he shall sprinkle toward the face of the *ohel moed* — as meaning the sprinkling must be directed precisely in that direction. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold that *el* does not require exact precision, only general orientation, and so by the *parah adumah* as well, approximate direction suffices.

## Sprinkling Exceptions

### General Non-Precision

The Gemara now faces a secondary question: even granting the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabbanan* over the *parah adumah*, why do all opinions agree that by other *korbanot* — including the Yom Kippur inner service — imprecise direction is valid?

### Yom Kippur Challenge

*Rav Yosef* presses *Rav Yehuda*: if *el davka* means precise direction, consider the verse *al pnei hakaporet* used in the context of the Yom Kippur sprinkling. Would *Rabbi Yehuda* say the blood must literally strike the cover of the *aron*? And further — in the *beis hamikdash sheni*, where there was no *aron* and no *kaporet*, would *Rabbi Yehuda* say there could be no sprinkling at all?

### Ark Exception

*Rava bar Ulla* resolves this: the verse *v'chiper et mikdash hakodesh* — "he shall sanctify the sanctuary of the holy place" — teaches that atonement is achieved through the *makom hamekudash lakodesh*, the location sanctified for holiness, even when all the vessels are not present. So *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position accommodates the Yom Kippur service in the *beis hamikdash sheni* by means of this special verse. The general principle stands: *Rabbi Yehuda* reads *el* and *al* as precise, but the particular circumstance of the *beis hamikdash sheni* is addressed separately.

*Rava* then offers an alternative resolution to the whole contradiction between the *braitot* on sprinkling direction. He holds that both *braitot* represent the *rabbanan*'s position — that is, directional precision is generally not required. The distinction between them is not about who is right or wrong in principle, but about what angle of deviation is being discussed. When the celebrant is oriented east-west — generally facing toward the *mikdash* — slight imprecision is acceptable, and the *braita* says it is *kasher*. When oriented north-south — entirely off-axis — one is not sprinkling toward the *mikdash* at all, and the *braita* says it is *pasul*. The two *braitot* address different physical situations, not different legal opinions.

### Metzora Oil

The *braita* then addresses a related case: *v'she-bifnim v'she-bi-metzora she-lo lishmah psulot* — the inner *korbanot* and the sprinkling done in connection with the *metzora* are invalid if performed *shelo lishmah* (not for their designated purpose), but *shelo mechuvanos kesheros* — if not precisely directed, they remain valid. This is immediately challenged: does a contradicting *braita* not say that even *shelo lishmah* is valid by the *metzora*?

To understand the contradiction, the nature of the *metzora*'s ritual must be clarified. The verse from this week's *parasha* (Vayikra 14) describes how the *kohen* takes the blood of the *asham*, applies it to the *metzora*'s ear, and then dips his finger in the *log* of oil and sprinkles it seven times *lifnei Hashem* — toward the *heichal*. The question of *shelo lishmah* applies to this sprinkling of oil, whose status is linked to the *asham* that accompanies it.

### Asham Status

*Rav Yosef* identifies the contradiction as a dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan*. *Rabbi Eliezer*, as established at the beginning of Zevachim, holds that an *asham* is analogous to a *chatat* — both come to atone for sin — and therefore an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah*, like a *chatat* performed *shelo lishmah*, is invalid. Since *Rabbi Eliezer* links the *asham* to the *chatat*, he likewise links the *log* of oil to the *asham*: just as the *asham shelo lishmah* is invalid, so is the sprinkling of the oil *shelo lishmah*. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, do not equate *asham* with *chatat*, and so both the *asham* and its associated oil remain valid even *shelo lishmah*.

The Gemara then raises a further difficulty: can *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling about the oil rest on this reasoning? The rule that an *asham* is like a *chatat* is itself derived through a *hekesh* (analogy), and there is a general principle that a *davar halamad b'hekesh* cannot in turn serve as the basis of another *hekesh*. If the *asham's* invalidity *shelo lishmah* is itself only known through *hekesh*, it cannot be used to derive that the oil is also invalid.

### Rava's Resolution

*Rava* therefore resolves the contradiction differently: *ha v'ha rabbanan* — both *braitot* represent the position of the *rabbanan*, who agree that the *asham* and oil are valid even *shelo lishmah*. The key is the first *mishna* of Zevachim: a *korban* performed *shelo lishmah* is valid, but it does not *alah l'baalim l'shem chova* — it does not count toward the owner's obligation. Applying this to the *metzora*: the *braita* that says the sprinkling is valid means the *korban* retains its sanctity — the meat may be eaten, the remainder of the oil may be consumed — but the *metzora* does not thereby become *tahor*, since the ritual did not fulfill its designated purpose. The *braita* that says it is invalid means it does not achieve its goal of *tahara* for the *metzora*. One *braita* speaks to the validity of the object (*hechsher*), the other to whether it accomplishes what it set out to do (*hartzah*).

With this resolution, the Gemara concludes the extended digression into sprinkling direction — prompted by the *mishna*'s discussion of the number of sprinklings being *me'akev* — and pivots to the *mishna*'s next major theme: the interdependency of component parts in the *menorah*, *mezuzah*, *tefillin*, and *tzitzit*.

## Interdependent Parts

### Shift to Vessels

Having addressed the world of *korbanot* and their internal requirements, the Gemara now turns toward *klei hamikdash* and the practical *mitzvot* of everyday Jewish life. The structural framework of the earlier *baraita* had organized its cases numerically: two loaves, three elements of *parah adumah*, four *minim* of *lulav*, and seven sprinklings. The *mishna* now returns to a similar numerical ascent — two, four, four — but now speaks of objects rather than sacrificial acts, and specifically of objects with which Jews engage daily: the *menorah*, the *mezuzah*, the *tefillin*, and the *tzitzit*.

### Menorah Components

*Sheva kanei menorah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the seven branches of the *menorah* prevent one another from being valid. A *menorah* with fewer than seven branches is not a valid *menorah*; one cannot simply light what is present and fulfill the *mitzvah* on the remaining branches. Similarly, *sheva neroteha me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the seven lamps. The *kaneh* (branch) is the structural arm itself; the *ner* is the small vessel placed at the top of each branch to hold the oil and wick, which actually produces the flame. Both the branches and their lamps are mutually indispensable.

The verse undergirding this mutual indispensability is *kaftorehah u'kenotehah mimena yihyu* — "its spheres and its branches shall be from it" — and more broadly, the Gemara learns from *havaya*, "shall be," that the *menorah* must exist in precisely this form.

It is worth noting in this context the well-known dispute regarding the *menorah*'s physical shape. The familiar image of arching, curved branches is consistent with contemporary archaeological evidence — the depiction on Titus's arch, and the engravings found in the Burnt House near the *kotel*, which clearly show arched branches. The *Rambam*, however, drawing on the silence of the *Torah*'s descriptions regarding curves, understood the branches as extending at straight diagonal angles from the central shaft, and drew this accordingly in his own hand. While his diagram was reproduced and debated, the physical evidence clearly supports the arched form.

### Daily Mitzvot

*Shtei parshiot shebamezuzah me'akvin zu et zu* — the two *parshiot* of *Shema* and *V'hayah im shamo'a* are mutually indispensable in the *mezuzah*. Not only that, but *afilu ktav echad me'akev* — even a single defective letter invalidates the entire *mezuzah*.

*Arba parshiot shebatefillin me'akvin zu et zu* — the four *parshiot* of *tefillin* (*Shema*, *V'hayah im shamo'a*, *Kadesh li*, and *V'hayah ki yevi'acha*) are all required, and again *afilu ktav echad me'akvan* — a single letter in any of them invalidates all.

*Arba tzitzit me'akvot zu et zu* — all four *tzitziyot* at the four corners of the *beged* are interdependent. One cannot claim to have fulfilled three-quarters of the *mitzvah* by attaching *tzitzit* to three corners. This is because *she-hen mitzvah achat* — they constitute a single *mitzvah* of a garment with *tzitzit*. *Rabbi Yishmael*, however, says *arba'tan arba mitzvot* — they are four separate *mitzvot*. The Gemara will explore the implications of that position in due course.

## Menorah Material

### One Beaten Unit

The Gemara now addresses the *menorah*'s material requirements in detail. A *braita* states: *Menorah hayetah ba'ah min ha-ashet u'min ha-zahav* — the *menorah* was made from a single lump of ore, fashioned from gold. *As'ah min ha-gruta'ot, psulah* — if one assembled it from individual pieces, even if melded together, it is invalid. *Mishar minei matachot, kesherah* — if made from other metals besides gold, it is valid.

### Chashmonaim Evidence

The historical evidence confirms this ruling. When the *Chashmonaim* rededicated the *mikdash*, they initially constructed a *menorah* that was not of gold, and yet it was used for the service — demonstrating that gold is not *me'akev*, but the requirement of being fashioned from one beaten piece is.

### Mikshah Havaya

The Gemara derives this from the *psukim*. Why is assembling from pieces invalid? *D'chtiv mikshah* — the verse writes *mikshah* (beaten), *d'havaya* — and "it shall be," *yihyu*, meaning it must be exactly this way. But if the verse says both "gold" and "shall be," why not require gold precisely as well? The Gemara answers: *amar kra ta'aseh* — "shall be made" implies a certain latitude in how it is made. The word *ta'aseh* comes to include other metals. Moreover, *mikshah* is written twice in the *parashah* — once in *mikshah ta'aseh et ha-menorah* and again at the end, *kula mikshah achat zahav tahor* — indicating that the requirement of being beaten in one piece is uncompromisable.

### Reconciling Repetition

The Gemara then addresses the apparent redundancy of both *mikshah* and *zahav* each appearing twice. The question raised is: could the doubling of *zahav* similarly indicate that gold is required precisely and cannot be substituted? The Gemara's response is structural: *ha'inhu zahav zahav mikshah mikshah lidrasha* — the repetitions are there to generate a *drasha* that clarifies what is and is not essential. The position that other metals are valid but assembled pieces are not is the only reading that gives meaningful content to all the doubled terms.

The Gemara then identifies how this works. The verse *kikar zahav tahor ya'aseh otah et kol hakeilim ha'eileh* — "one *kikar* of pure gold shall he make it, all these vessels" — establishes a list of requirements that apply when gold is used. *Ba'ah zahav ba'ah kikar* — when gold is used, the *kikar* weight applies; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah kikar* — when other metals are used, the precise weight requirement does not apply. Similarly: *ba'ah zahav ba'ah gevi'im kaftorim u'ferachim* — when gold is used, all the ornamental cups, spheres, and flowers are required; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah gevi'im kaftorim u'ferachim* — if not gold, these ornaments are not mandatory.

The Gemara asks whether one might further extend this: perhaps when not gold, one need not even have seven branches? The answer is *hu pumit mikrei* — a single-branched lamp is simply not called a *menorah* at all; it is called a lamp. The seven-branch structure is definitional, not merely a gold-dependent enhancement.

*Ba'ah zahav ba'ah mikshah* — when gold is used, it must be one beaten piece; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah mikshah* — when other metals are used, this requirement does not apply. This confirms the overall framework: gold triggers a maximally demanding set of requirements, while other metals require only the seven-branched form itself. The doubled words of *mikshah* and *zahav* are therefore not redundant but structurally necessary, each pair working together to define a linked system of conditional requirements.

### Trumpets Inverse

The Gemara derives the second instance of *mikshah* — *mikshah ta'aseh* — as serving to exclude the *chatzotzrot* (trumpets) that Moshe fashioned. The *chatzotzrot* represent the precise inverse of the *menorah*: they must be silver, but they do not need to be fashioned from a single beaten piece. A *braita* states: *chatzotzrot hayu ba'ot min ha-ashet u'min ha-kesef* — the trumpets could come from a single piece or from silver; *asan min ha-gruta'ot, kesheirah* — if made from melted-together pieces, valid; *mishar minei matachot, psulim* — other metals, invalid. Why must they be silver? *D'chtiv kesef v'havaya* — the verse writes silver and "shall be" together, establishing silver as precise and non-negotiable. Why does the *Torah* not similarly insist on a single piece? Because the verse *hi v'lo chatzotzrot* — "it [the *menorah*] is one beaten piece, and not the *chatzotzrot*" — specifies the *menorah* as the single item requiring a unitary beaten form.

The Gemara then records a *braita* on the vessels of Moshe generally: *kol ha-keilim she'asah Moshe kesheirim lo u'kesheirim l'dorot* — all the vessels Moshe made were valid for his time and for all subsequent generations. The *chatzotzrot* are an exception: *kesheirot lo u'psulot l'dorot* — they were used in his generation but not in future ones, since their primary function was moving the camp. The verse *aseh lecha* — "make for yourself" — is read as *lecha v'lo l'dorot*, for your generation and not for future ones, precisely because both the context (mobilizing a moving camp) and the double use of *lecha* in the verse (*aseh lecha* and *v'hayu lach*) signal temporal limitation. By contrast, similar language by the *aron* (*v'asita lach aron etz*) does not limit it to Moshe's generation, since there the *lach* is read either as indicating Moshe's personal contribution of funds or as God expressing that ideally the *aron* would be Moshe's own — both readings that preclude the limiting interpretation.

## Permitted Materials

### Rav Pappa List

*Rav Pappa brei de-Rabbi Chanin* taught a *braita* before *Rav Yosef*: the *menorah* comes from a single beaten lump and from gold. If made of silver — valid. Of tin (*bedi'il*), lead (*oferet*), or *gisteron* (a metallic alloy) — *Rabbi* invalidates, *Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda* validates. If made of wood, bone, or glass — non-metallic materials — *divrei ha-kol psulah*, everyone agrees it is invalid.

### Rav Yosef Alt

*Rav Yosef* responded by presenting an alternative *braita*, which he considered more accurate: *klei sharet she-asahan shel eitz* — service vessels fashioned of wood — *Rabbi* invalidates, *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* validates. This formulation places the debate not over cheap metals, but over non-metals entirely: wood versus metal.

### Hermeneutics

*Rav Yosef* then asks *Rav Pappa*: according to your version, what interpretive method are both opinions using? *Rav Pappa* responds that both employ *klal u'prat u'klal*: *v'asita menorah* is the *klal* (general); *zahav tahor* is the *prat* (specific); *v'kikar et ha-menorah* is the return to *klal*. By the rules of *klal u'prat u'klal*, one includes anything like the specific — like gold. *Rav Yosef* finds this unsatisfying, since by his reading the debate is governed instead by *ribuy u'mi'ut*: *v'asita menorah* is the *ribuy* (broad inclusion); *zahav tahor* is the *mi'ut* (restriction); *v'kikar et ha-menorah* is the renewed *ribuy*. The rule of *ribuy mi'ut v'ribuy* is that one excludes only what is most dissimilar to the specific term.

### Material Positions

Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, as both sides agree in *Rav Pappa*'s version, the debate is about what "like gold" means. *Rebbi* holds: *mah prat meforash shel matechet, af kol shel matechet* — just as the specific (gold) is a metal, anything that is a metal qualifies. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* holds: *mah prat meforash davar chashuv, af kol davar chashuv* — just as gold is a distinguished material, anything distinguished qualifies, implying precious materials beyond metals might also be included, while inexpensive metals might be excluded.

Under *ribuy mi'ut v'ribuy*, as *Rav Yosef*'s version has it, almost everything is included by default; only the thing most dissimilar to gold is excluded. *Mi'et shel cheres* — pottery alone is excluded. Everything else — metals, wood, bone, glass short of pottery — would be included according to *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Evidence Consolidation

*Rav Pappa* pushes back: why should I abandon my *braita* in favor of yours? *Rav Yosef* replies that multiple *braitot* converge on the position that the debate concerns non-metals, not distinctions within metals. One *braita* states: *ein lo zahav mevi af shel kesef, shel nechoshet, shel barzel, shel bedil, shel oferet* — if one does not have gold, one may bring the *menorah* from any other metal: silver, copper, iron, tin, lead. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda machshir af be-shel etz* — *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* further validates even wood. This pattern repeats across several *braitot*, all of which show that *Rabbi* and *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* are not debating the rank of metals among themselves, but whether non-metallic materials such as wood are included at all.
