# Menachot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Sprinkling Debate

### Intro & Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁבַע הַזָּאוֹת שֶׁבַּפָּרָה, שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן – בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא מְכוָּּונוֹת אֶל נֹכַח פְּנֵי אוֹהֶל מוֹעֵד – פְּסוּלוֹת. וְשֶׁבִּפְנִים, וְשֶׁבִּמְצוֹרָע – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, פְּסוּלוֹת; שֶׁלֹּא מְכוָּּונוֹת, כְּשֵׁרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest **performed the seven sprinklings of** the blood of **the** red **heifer** improperly, **either** by performing them **not for their** own **sake or** performing them **not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting** of the Tabernacle (Numbers 19:4), which corresponds to the Sanctuary in the Temple, **they are not valid.** **But** with regard to the sprinkling of the blood **that** takes place **inside** the Sanctuary, of inner sin offerings, the blood of the bull and goat of Yom Kippur, the blood of the bull of the anointed priest, the blood of the bull for an unwitting communal sin, and the blood of the goats of idol worship, which are to be sprinkled "before the Lord, in front of the Curtain of the Sanctuary" (Leviticus 4:6), **and** the sprinkling of the oil **that** takes place during the purification **of the leper,** which is done "seven times before the Lord" (Leviticus 14:16), if these are performed **not for their** own **sake,** then **they are not valid.** But if they are performed **not precisely** toward the direction where they should be sprinkled, **they are valid.**

The present discussion begins as a continuation of 27b, where the Gemara had set up a contradiction between two *braitot*. One *braita* holds that the sprinkling of blood toward the *heichal* — even if not precisely directed — is valid (*kasher*); the other holds that while this imprecision is generally acceptable, by the *parah adumah* it renders the sprinkling invalid (*pasul*). The question the Gemara investigates is what this debate is actually about, and it unfolds in the larger framework of *me'akev* — the principle that certain component parts are indispensable to the validity of a ritual act.

### Kodesh Hakodashim Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מְחוּסְּרֵי כַפָּרָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָעֲזָרָה בְּשׁוֹגֵג – חַיָּיב חַטָּאת, בְּמֵזִיד – עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר טְבוּל יוֹם וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַטְּמֵאִים. וּטְהוֹרִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לִפְנִים מִמְּחִיצָתָן, לַהֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ – בְּאַרְבָּעִים, מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת – בְּמִיתָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ וּמִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת – בְּאַרְבָּעִים, וְאֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת – בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Kelim* 1:10): With regard to **those who have not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete the purification process, and therefore are not permitted to enter the Temple or partake of sacrificial meat, **who entered the** Temple **courtyard unwittingly,** they are **liable** to bring **a sin offering.** If they entered **intentionally,** then this is **punishable by *karet*. And needless to say,** the same applies to **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **and all the others who are ritually impure** and have not yet immersed. **And** with regard to those who are **pure who entered beyond their boundaries,** i.e., beyond where it is permitted for them to enter, such as a priest who enters the Sanctuary for a purpose other than performing the Temple service, if one entered **any** part **of the Sanctuary,** he is liable **to** receive **forty** lashes. If he entered **within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, i.e., into the Holy of Holies, or he entered the Holy of Holies all the way until he was **before the Ark Cover,** he is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he entered **any** part **of the Sanctuary or within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, he is liable **to** receive **forty** lashes; **but** if he entered the Holy of Holies all the way until he was **before the Ark Cover,** he is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven.

To explain the debate over directional precision, the Gemara anchors the discussion in a *braita* about entry into the *kodesh hakodashim*. The *braita* opens with the laws of *mechuserai kaparah* — those whose *tahara* process is complete except for the final bringing of a *korban*, representing the lowest level of residual *tumah*. If such a person enters the *azarah* unintentionally (*bishogeg*), he is liable to bring a *chatat*; if willfully (*b'meizid*), he is liable to *kares*. This applies *kal vachomer* to those with more substantial *tumah*, including the *tvu'el yom* and certainly those who have not yet gone to the *mikveh* at all.

The *braita* then turns to those who are ritually pure (*tahorim*) but go beyond their permitted boundaries. One who enters the *heichal* — the roofed inner sanctum housing the *shulchan* and the golden *mizbeach* — is punishable by lashes, since the verse *v'lo yavo bechol et el hakodesh* constitutes a negative prohibition, applying even to a *kohen* who enters without a legitimate purpose (*bi'ah reikanit*). If, however, one enters *mibeis laparoches* — into the *kodesh hakodashim* behind the curtain — and stands *el pnei hakaporet*, directly in front of the *aron*, the punishment is divine death (*mita biyedei shamayim*). That is the first opinion.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disputes this. In his view, the entire *heichal* and the *kodesh hakodashim* alike are subject only to lashes. Divine death applies exclusively when one stands *el pnei hakaporet* — directly in front of the *aron*.

### Verse Parsing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? בְּהַאי קְרָא: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן אָחִיךָ וְאַל יָבוֹא בְכׇל עֵת אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת אֲשֶׁר עַל הָאָרוֹן וְלֹא יָמוּת״. רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ – בְּ״לֹא יָבֹא״, ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״ וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָמוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** issue **do** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** the proper understanding of **this verse: "And the Lord said to Moses: Speak to Aaron your brother, that he not come at all times into the holy place, within the Curtain, before the Ark Cover which is upon the Ark, that he not die"** (Leviticus 16:2). **The Rabbis hold** that entering **into the holy place,** i.e., the Sanctuary, is **subject to** the prohibition of: **He shall not come,** and one who violates it is punished with lashes, whereas entering **within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies **and before the Ark Cover** is **subject to** the warning of: **He shall not die,** and entering there is punished by death at the hand of Heaven.

The Gemara asks: *b'mai kamifligi* — what precisely are they debating? The answer is that they disagree over how to parse the verse in *Vayikra*: *daber el Aharon achicha v'al yavo bechol et el hakodesh mibeis laparoches al pnei hakaporet asher al ha'aron v'lo yamus* — "Speak to Aharon your brother, and he shall not come at any time into the sanctuary, behind the curtain, in front of the *kaporet* that is upon the *aron*, lest he die." Three locational phrases appear: *el hakodesh* (into the sanctuary), *mibeis laparoches* (behind the curtain), and *al pnei hakaporet* (in front of the *kaporet*). The outer phrases are not contested: *el hakodesh* is clearly the negative prohibition of entering, and *al pnei hakaporet* — when combined with *v'lo yamus* — is clearly a case of divine death. The dispute is about the middle phrase, *mibeis laparoches*: does it attach to the first half of the verse (the prohibition of entry), or to the second half (the death penalty)?

### Rabbanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וּ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָבֹא״, וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָמוּת״. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״, וְלָא בָּעֵי ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: הֵיכָל מִיחַיַּיב, מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת מִבַּעְיָא? ״מִבֵּית הַפָּרֹכֶת״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בְּמִיתָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וְלָא כְּתַב ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי קוֹדֶשׁ – מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת, אֲבָל הֵיכָל לָאו נָמֵי לָא. וְרַבָּנַן: הָהוּא לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דְּהֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ אִיקְּרִי ״קוֹדֶשׁ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִבְדִּילָה הַפָּרֹכֶת לָכֶם בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that entering **into the holy place,** i.e., the Sanctuary, **and within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies is **subject to** the prohibition of: **He shall not come,** and one who violates it is punished with lashes, whereas entering **before the Ark Cover** is **subject to** the warning of: **He shall not die,** and entering there is punished by death at the hand of Heaven. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the interpretation **of the Rabbis?** The Gemara answers: **If** it should **enter your mind** to explain the verse **as Rabbi Yehuda says,** then **let the Merciful One write:** That he not come at all times **into the holy place and before the Ark Cover** that he not die, **and there is no need** to write **"within the Curtain," and I would say:** If one **becomes liable** to receive lashes for even entering the **Sanctuary, is** it **necessary** to teach that one incurs this punishment for entering **within the Curtain? Why do I** need the phrase **"within the Curtain" that the Merciful One wrote? Learn from** that seemingly extraneous term that entering the Holy of Holies is punishable **by death** at the hand of Heaven. **And Rabbi Yehuda** understands: **If the Merciful One had written** only that it is prohibited to come **"into the holy place" and did not write "within the Curtain," I would say: What** is the **holy place?** It is **within the Curtain,** i.e., the Holy of Holies, and one who enters it violates a prohibition, **but** if one enters the **Sanctuary** he does **not even** violate **a prohibition. And the Rabbis** respond to this claim: **You cannot say that, as the entire Sanctuary is called** "the **holy place," as it is stated: "And the Curtain shall divide for you between the holy place and the Holy of Holies"** (Exodus 26:33).

The *rabbanan* hold that *el hakodesh* — the prohibition of entry — refers to the *heichal*, and that both *mibeis laparoches* and *al pnei hakaporet* together trigger the death penalty. Their reasoning is textual: if *Rabbi Yehuda* were correct that one incurs *mita* only when standing directly in front of the *aron*, then the phrase *mibeis laparoches* would be entirely superfluous. The Torah could simply have written *el hakodesh* (negative prohibition) and *al pnei hakaporet* (death), and one would naturally have known that the *heichal* prohibits entry and that standing before the *aron* triggers death. Since the *heichal* is already prohibited, there is no need to separately prohibit the *kodesh hakodashim* — it is obviously included. The only reason to write *mibeis laparoches* is to teach that even entering the *kodesh hakodashim* alone, without confronting the *aron*, is sufficient to incur divine death. Furthermore, *d'heichal kulei ikrei kodesh* — the *heichal* is already called *kodesh*, as the verse states that the *paroches* separates *bein hakodesh u'vein kodesh hakodashim*, making clear that *kodesh* is the *heichal*. There is therefore no possibility of confusion requiring clarification.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לֵימָא קְרָא ״פְּנֵי״, מַאי ״אֶל״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״אֶל״ דַּוְקָא, וְרַבָּנַן – ״אֶל״ לָאו דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to this, as it is clear that the term "before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover" is necessary to exclude one who entered the Holy of Holies through a roundabout path? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Yehuda, if the purpose was for that reason, **let the verse say: Before [*penei*]** the Ark Cover. **What** is the purpose of the word ***el*? Learn from** that seemingly extraneous term that one is punished with death at the hand of Heaven **specifically** if he entered directly **before** the Ark, but not if he merely entered the Holy of Holies. **And the Rabbis** hold that the term **"*el*"** does **not** mean **specifically** one who enters directly before the Ark Cover.

*Rabbi Yehuda* defends his reading by turning the same argument around. If we had only written *el hakodesh*, one might have thought this referred to the *kodesh hakodashim* — interpreting *kodesh* as the holiest place — and thereby excluded the *heichal* from any prohibition entirely. The Torah therefore had to include *mibeis laparoches* explicitly as a clarification that *kodesh* means the *heichal*, and that the *heichal* is prohibited. Once the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim* are both under a negative prohibition through this reading, the separate phrase *al pnei hakaporet* followed by *v'lo yamus* is unnecessary unless it is coming to tell us something new: only when one stands directly in front of the *aron* is the penalty death — otherwise it is merely a negative prohibition.

The Gemara then asks how the *rabbanan* account for the phrase *al pnei hakaporet* if they already derive *mita* from *mibeis laparoches*. The answer is *l'mivti derech meshupash* — the phrase is not superfluous but teaches that divine death applies only when entry is made facing the *aron* directly, not indirectly. A *braita* of *Beis Rav Eliezer ben Yaakov* is cited: *al pnei hakaporet keidma* — facing the *kaporet* toward the east — establishes a paradigm (*binyan av*) that wherever the Torah says "facing" (*pnei*), it means the eastern face.

## Directional Precision

### Eastern Facing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת קֵדְמָה״ – זֶה בָּנָה אָב, כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״פְּנֵי״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פְּנֵי קָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught:** With regard to the verse: "And he shall sprinkle it with his finger **before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover to the east"** (Leviticus 16:14), **this established a paradigm** that **any place** in the Torah **where it is stated: "Before [*penei*],"** it is referring to **nothing other than before** the **eastern** side.

This paradigm is grounded in a broader structural observation about biblical orientation. The Torah consistently assumes that a person stands facing east: hence the word for south (*teimana*) derives from *yamin* — right — because if one faces east, the south is to the right. West is described as *acharei derech mivo hashemesh*, "behind toward the setting sun," since *achor* is opposite *kadim* (east). Avraham's offer to Lot — *im hasmol v'eimina v'im hayamin v'asme'ila* — is understood as referring to south and north respectively, since he assumes an eastward orientation.

### Rashi's Forward Entry

*Rashi* draws from this the precise implication for the *kodesh hakodashim*: the phrase *pnei hakaporet* teaches that one incurs *chiyuv mita* only if one enters facing the *aron* — through the front, as it were. If one digs a tunnel and enters from the side or from below, or enters walking backwards, or enters at an angle, one would not be liable, because one is not entering *facing* the *aron*. Without the phrase *pnei hakaporet*, we would have derived liability from simply entering the *kodesh hakodashim*; the phrase comes to qualify that: one must enter in a forward-facing manner. The question is purely physical — how one is oriented upon entry — not a matter of intent.

### El Means Exact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ דַּוְקָא, וְ״הִזָּה אֶל נֹכַח״ נָמֵי דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now returns to its suggestion that the contradiction between the two *baraitot* with regard to whether the sprinklings of the red heifer are valid or not when performed not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting can be resolved by explaining that one *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda and the other is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that the expression **"before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover"** teaches that the punishment is limited to one who **specifically** entered directly before the Ark Cover, holds that the expression: **"And sprinkle** of its blood **toward [*el*] the front"** (Numbers 19:4), **also** means that the sprinklings must be performed **specifically** toward the front of the Sanctuary.

*Rabbi Yehuda* then pushes further. If all the Torah wished to convey was the requirement of forward-facing entry, it could have simply written *pnei*. Why does it write *el pnei*? *Shma mina: el davka* — the word *el* (toward, in front of) means precisely and directly in front, not merely in that general direction. This is the crux of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, and it carries implications that extend far beyond the *kodesh hakodashim*.

### Parah Adumah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: מִדְּהָתָם לָאו דַּוְקָא, הָכָא נָמֵי לָאו דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** are of the opinion that **from** the fact **that there** the term *el* does **not** mean **specifically** that one is liable to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven only if he enters directly before the Ark Cover, **here too** they hold that it is **not** meant **specifically,** and therefore the sprinklings are valid even when performed not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.

The connection to the opening debate now becomes clear. *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that *el pnei hakaporet* means exactly in front, reads similarly the verse concerning the *parah adumah* — *v'hizah el nochach pnei ohel moed*, he shall sprinkle toward the face of the *ohel moed* — as meaning the sprinkling must be directed precisely in that direction. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold that *el* does not require exact precision, only general orientation, and so by the *parah adumah* as well, approximate direction suffices.

## Sprinkling Exceptions

### General Non-Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּ״אֶל״ דַּוְקָא, (אֶל נֹכַח) [עַל] נָמֵי דַּוְקָא? אֶלָּא דְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי דְּלָא הֲווֹ אָרוֹן וְכַפּוֹרֶת, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא עָבֵיד הַזָּאוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this** explanation: **According to Rabbi Yehuda, from** the fact **that** there the term ***el*** is used **specifically,** the verse: "And he shall sprinkle of the blood **before [*al*** *penei*] the Ark Cover" (Leviticus 16:14) should **also** mean that the sprinkling must be performed **specifically** upon the Ark Cover. **But** in the time **of the Second Temple, where there was no Ark or Ark Cover,** would Rabbi Yehuda then say that **indeed** the **sprinklings were not performed?** This is clearly not correct, as all agree that the sprinklings were performed in the Second Temple (see *Yoma* 53b).

The Gemara now faces a secondary question: even granting the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabbanan* over the *parah adumah*, why do all opinions agree that by other *korbanot* — including the Yom Kippur inner service — imprecise direction is valid?

*Rav Yosef* presses *Rav Yehuda*: if *el davka* means precise direction, consider the verse *al pnei hakaporet* used in the context of the Yom Kippur sprinkling. Would *Rabbi Yehuda* say the blood must literally strike the cover of the *aron*? And further — in the *beis hamikdash sheni*, where there was no *aron* and no *kaporet*, would *Rabbi Yehuda* say there could be no sprinkling at all?

### Ark Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִפֶּר אֶת מִקְדַּשׁ הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ – מְקוֹם הַמְקוּדָּשׁ לַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla said** in response: **The verse states** with regard to the Yom Kippur service: **"And he shall make atonement for the most holy place [*mikdash hakodesh*]"** (Leviticus 16:33), which is interpreted as follows: He will sprinkle the blood to make atonement not specifically on the Ark [*hakodesh*], but even on **the place that is dedicated [*hamkudash*] for the Ark [*lakodesh*].**

*Rava bar Ulla* resolves this: the verse *v'chiper et mikdash hakodesh* — "he shall sanctify the sanctuary of the holy place" — teaches that atonement is achieved through the *makom hamekudash lakodesh*, the location sanctified for holiness, even when all the vessels are not present. So *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position accommodates the Yom Kippur service in the *beis hamikdash sheni* by means of this special verse. The general principle stands: *Rabbi Yehuda* reads *el* and *al* as precise, but the particular circumstance of the *beis hamikdash sheni* is addressed separately.

### Metzora Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא רַבָּנַן, כָּאן לְהַכְשִׁיר הַקׇּרְבָּן, כָּאן לְהַרְצוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another resolution of the contradiction between the *baraitot* concerning whether the sprinklings of the red heifer are valid or invalid when performed not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. **Rava said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** **Here,** where the *baraita* teaches that the sprinklings are valid, it means that they were effective **in rendering the offering valid** and allowing the priests to partake of the remainder of the *log*, whereas **there,** where the *baraita* teaches that the sprinklings are not valid, it means that they do not **effect acceptance, as they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner,** and therefore the leper is still prohibited from partaking of sacrificial meat.

*Rava* then offers an alternative resolution to the whole contradiction between the *braitot* on sprinkling direction. He holds that both *braitot* represent the *rabbanan*'s position — that is, directional precision is generally not required. The distinction between them is not about who is right or wrong in principle, but about what angle of deviation is being discussed. When the celebrant is oriented east-west — generally facing toward the *mikdash* — slight imprecision is acceptable, and the *braita* says it is *kasher*. When oriented north-south — entirely off-axis — one is not sprinkling toward the *mikdash* at all, and the *braita* says it is *pasul*. The two *braitot* address different physical situations, not different legal opinions.

### Asham Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְהָא רַבָּנַן. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּמַקֵּישׁ אָשָׁם לְחַטָּאת – מַקֵּישׁ נָמֵי לוֹג לְאָשָׁם, רַבָּנַן לָא מַקְּשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult. This** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, and that** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** He explains: **Rabbi Eliezer** is the *tanna* **who juxtaposes** the **guilt offering to a sin offering,** teaching that just as a sin offering is disqualified when sacrificed not for its sake, so too, the guilt offering, such as the leper's guilt offering, is disqualified when sacrificed not for its sake, as it is written: "As is the sin offering, so is the guilt offering; there is one law for them" (Leviticus 7:7). He **also juxtaposes** the ***log*** of oil of the leper **to** the **guilt offering** of the leper in the same verse, teaching that if the sprinkling from the *log* of oil was performed not for its own sake, it is not valid. In contrast, **the Rabbis do not juxtapose** the guilt offering to the sin offering, and therefore they have no reason to invalidate the sprinkling from the leper's *log* of oil that is performed not for its own sake.

The *braita* then addresses a related case: *v'she-bifnim v'she-bi-metzora she-lo lishmah psulot* — the inner *korbanot* and the sprinkling done in connection with the *metzora* are invalid if performed *shelo lishmah* (not for their designated purpose), but *shelo mechuvanos kesheros* — if not precisely directed, they remain valid. This is immediately challenged: does a contradicting *braita* not say that even *shelo lishmah* is valid by the *metzora*?

To understand the contradiction, the nature of the *metzora*'s ritual must be clarified. The verse from this week's *parasha* (Vayikra 14) describes how the *kohen* takes the blood of the *asham*, applies it to the *metzora*'s ear, and then dips his finger in the *log* of oil and sprinkles it seven times *lifnei Hashem* — toward the *heichal*. The question of *shelo lishmah* applies to this sprinkling of oil, whose status is linked to the *asham* that accompanies it.

*Rav Yosef* identifies the contradiction as a dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and the *rabbanan*. *Rabbi Eliezer*, as established at the beginning of Zevachim, holds that an *asham* is analogous to a *chatat* — both come to atone for sin — and therefore an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah*, like a *chatat* performed *shelo lishmah*, is invalid. Since *Rabbi Eliezer* links the *asham* to the *chatat*, he likewise links the *log* of oil to the *asham*: just as the *asham shelo lishmah* is invalid, so is the sprinkling of the oil *shelo lishmah*. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, do not equate *asham* with *chatat*, and so both the *asham* and its associated oil remain valid even *shelo lishmah*.

The Gemara then raises a further difficulty: can *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling about the oil rest on this reasoning? The rule that an *asham* is like a *chatat* is itself derived through a *hekesh* (analogy), and there is a general principle that a *davar halamad b'hekesh* cannot in turn serve as the basis of another *hekesh*. If the *asham's* invalidity *shelo lishmah* is itself only known through *hekesh*, it cannot be used to derive that the oil is also invalid.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָא וְהָא רַבָּנַן, כָּאן לְהַכְשִׁיר הַקׇּרְבָּן, כָּאן לְהַרְצוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. Here,** where the *baraita* teaches that the sprinklings are valid, it means that they were effective **in rendering the offering valid** and allowing the priests to partake of the remainder of the *log*, whereas **there,** where the *baraita* teaches that the sprinklings are not valid, it means that they do not **effect acceptance, as they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner,** and therefore the leper is still prohibited from partaking of sacrificial meat.

*Rava* therefore resolves the contradiction differently: *ha v'ha rabbanan* — both *braitot* represent the position of the *rabbanan*, who agree that the *asham* and oil are valid even *shelo lishmah*. The key is the first *mishna* of Zevachim: a *korban* performed *shelo lishmah* is valid, but it does not *alah l'baalim l'shem chova* — it does not count toward the owner's obligation. Applying this to the *metzora*: the *braita* that says the sprinkling is valid means the *korban* retains its sanctity — the meat may be eaten, the remainder of the oil may be consumed — but the *metzora* does not thereby become *tahor*, since the ritual did not fulfill its designated purpose. The *braita* that says it is invalid means it does not achieve its goal of *tahara* for the *metzora*. One *braita* speaks to the validity of the object (*hechsher*), the other to whether it accomplishes what it set out to do (*hartzah*).

With this resolution, the Gemara concludes the extended digression into sprinkling direction — prompted by the *mishna*'s discussion of the number of sprinklings being *me'akev* — and pivots to the *mishna*'s next major theme: the interdependency of component parts in the *menorah*, *mezuzah*, *tefillin*, and *tzitzit*.

## Interdependent Parts

### Shift to Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׁבְעָה קְנֵי מְנוֹרָה מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, שִׁבְעָה נֵרוֹתֶיהָ מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, שְׁתֵּי פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁבַּמְּזוּזָה מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ כְּתָב אֶחָד מְעַכְּבָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the **seven branches of the Candelabrum** (see Exodus 25:32), the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** With regard to **its seven lamps** atop the branches, the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** With regard to the **two passages that are in the *mezuza*,** which are the first (Deuteronomy 6:1–9) and second (Deuteronomy 11:13–21) paragraphs of *Shema*, the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** Furthermore, the absence of **even one letter prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with** the rest of **them.**

Having addressed the world of *korbanot* and their internal requirements, the Gemara now turns toward *klei hamikdash* and the practical *mitzvot* of everyday Jewish life. The structural framework of the earlier *baraita* had organized its cases numerically: two loaves, three elements of *parah adumah*, four *minim* of *lulav*, and seven sprinklings. The *mishna* now returns to a similar numerical ascent — two, four, four — but now speaks of objects rather than sacrificial acts, and specifically of objects with which Jews engage daily: the *menorah*, the *mezuzah*, the *tefillin*, and the *tzitzit*.

### Menorah Components

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבַּע פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁבַּתְּפִילִּין מְעַכְּבִין זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ כְּתָב אֶחָד מְעַכְּבָן. אַרְבַּע צִיצִיּוֹת מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, שֶׁאַרְבַּעְתָּן מִצְוָה אַחַת. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אַרְבַּעְתָּן אַרְבַּע מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **four passages that are in the phylacteries,** which are the two passages in the *mezuza* and two additional passages (Exodus 13:1–10, 11–16), the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** Furthermore, the absence of **even one letter prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with** the rest of **them.** With regard to the **four ritual fringes** on a garment, the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, as the four of them** constitute **one mitzva. Rabbi Yishmael says: The four of them are four** discrete **mitzvot,** and the absence of one does not prevent fulfillment of the mitzva with the rest.

*Sheva kanei menorah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the seven branches of the *menorah* prevent one another from being valid. A *menorah* with fewer than seven branches is not a valid *menorah*; one cannot simply light what is present and fulfill the *mitzvah* on the remaining branches. Similarly, *sheva neroteha me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the seven lamps. The *kaneh* (branch) is the structural arm itself; the *ner* is the small vessel placed at the top of each branch to hold the oil and wick, which actually produces the flame. Both the branches and their lamps are mutually indispensable.

The verse undergirding this mutual indispensability is *kaftorehah u'kenotehah mimena yihyu* — "its spheres and its branches shall be from it" — and more broadly, the Gemara learns from *havaya*, "shall be," that the *menorah* must exist in precisely this form.

It is worth noting in this context the well-known dispute regarding the *menorah*'s physical shape. The familiar image of arching, curved branches is consistent with contemporary archaeological evidence — the depiction on Titus's arch, and the engravings found in the Burnt House near the *kotel*, which clearly show arched branches. The *Rambam*, however, drawing on the silence of the *Torah*'s descriptions regarding curves, understood the branches as extending at straight diagonal angles from the central shaft, and drew this accordingly in his own hand. While his diagram was reproduced and debated, the physical evidence clearly supports the arched form.

*Shtei parshiot shebamezuzah me'akvin zu et zu* — the two *parshiot* of *Shema* and *V'hayah im shamo'a* are mutually indispensable in the *mezuzah*. Not only that, but *afilu ktav echad me'akev* — even a single defective letter invalidates the entire *mezuzah*.

*Arba parshiot shebatefillin me'akvin zu et zu* — the four *parshiot* of *tefillin* (*Shema*, *V'hayah im shamo'a*, *Kadesh li*, and *V'hayah ki yevi'acha*) are all required, and again *afilu ktav echad me'akvan* — a single letter in any of them invalidates all.

*Arba tzitzit me'akvot zu et zu* — all four *tzitziyot* at the four corners of the *beged* are interdependent. One cannot claim to have fulfilled three-quarters of the *mitzvah* by attaching *tzitzit* to three corners. This is because *she-hen mitzvah achat* — they constitute a single *mitzvah* of a garment with *tzitzit*. *Rabbi Yishmael*, however, says *arba'tan arba mitzvot* — they are four separate *mitzvot*. The Gemara will explore the implications of that position in due course.

## Menorah Material

### One Beaten Unit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? הֲוָיָה כְּתִיב בְּהוּ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְנוֹרָה הָיְתָה בָּאָה מִן הָעֶשֶׁת וּמִן הַזָּהָב. עֲשָׂאָהּ מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת – פְּסוּלָה, מִשְּׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת – כְּשֵׁרָה. מַאי שְׁנָא מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת פְּסוּלָה? דִּכְתִיב ״מִקְשָׁה״ וַהֲוָיָה, שְׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת נָמֵי זָהָב וַהֲוָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** that the absence of any of the seven branches of the Candelabrum prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others? The Gemara answers: **It is written concerning them** a term of **being:** "Their knobs and their branches shall be of one piece with it" (Exodus 25:36), and a term of being indicates an indispensable requirement. **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta*, *Ḥullin* 1:18): The **Candelabrum was fashioned from** a complete **block [*ha'eshet*] and from gold.** If they **fashioned it from fragments [*hagerutaot*]** of gold then it is **unfit,** but if they fashioned it **from other types of metal** rather than gold, it is **fit.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **different** about a Candelabrum made **from fragments** of gold, that it is rendered **unfit? As it is written** with regard to it: "Their knobs and their branches shall be of one piece with it, the whole of it one beaten work of pure gold" (Exodus 25:36), employing the term **"beaten [*miksha*]" and** a term of **being,** indicating that it is an indispensable requirement. But accordingly, a Candelabrum fashioned from **other types of metal** should be rendered unfit **as well,** since the verse states that it is made from **gold and** uses a term of **being.**

The Gemara now addresses the *menorah*'s material requirements in detail. A *braita* states: *Menorah hayetah ba'ah min ha-ashet u'min ha-zahav* — the *menorah* was made from a single lump of ore, fashioned from gold. *As'ah min ha-gruta'ot, psulah* — if one assembled it from individual pieces, even if melded together, it is invalid. *Mishar minei matachot, kesherah* — if made from other metals besides gold, it is valid.

### Chashmonaim Evidence

The historical evidence confirms this ruling. When the *Chashmonaim* rededicated the *mikdash*, they initially constructed a *menorah* that was not of gold, and yet it was used for the service — demonstrating that gold is not *me'akev*, but the requirement of being fashioned from one beaten piece is.

### Mikshah Havaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״תֵּיעָשֶׂה״, לְרַבּוֹת שְׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת. וְאֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת גְּרוּטָאוֹת? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּאַ״מִּקְשָׁה״ כְּתִיבָה הֲוָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "And you shall make a Candelabrum of pure gold; of beaten work **will** the Candelabrum **be made"** (Exodus 25:31), **to include other types of metal.** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that the expression "will be made" serves **to include** a Candelabrum fashioned from **fragments** of gold? The Gemara answers: **It cannot enter your mind** to say this, **as** the term of **being,** which indicates an indispensable requirement, **is written with regard to** the command that the Candelabrum be **a beaten work,** i.e., fashioned from a single block and not from different fragments, as it is stated: "Shall be of one beaten work" (Exodus 25:36).

The Gemara derives this from the *psukim*. Why is assembling from pieces invalid? *D'chtiv mikshah* — the verse writes *mikshah* (beaten), *d'havaya* — and "it shall be," *yihyu*, meaning it must be exactly this way. But if the verse says both "gold" and "shall be," why not require gold precisely as well? The Gemara answers: *amar kra ta'aseh* — "shall be made" implies a certain latitude in how it is made. The word *ta'aseh* comes to include other metals. Moreover, *mikshah* is written twice in the *parashah* — once in *mikshah ta'aseh et ha-menorah* and again at the end, *kula mikshah achat zahav tahor* — indicating that the requirement of being beaten in one piece is uncompromisable.

### Reconciling Repetition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִקְשָׁה״ נָמֵי אַ״מִּקְשָׁה״ כְּתִיב, ״מִקְשָׁה״ ״מִקְשָׁה״ לְעַכֵּב. ״זָהָב״ ״זָהָב״ נָמֵי לְעַכֵּב! הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת פְּסוּלָה, מִשְּׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת כְּשֵׁרָה – הַיְינוּ ״זָהָב״ ״זָהָב״ ״מִקְשָׁה״ ״מִקְשָׁה״ לִדְרָשָׁא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת כְּשֵׁרָה, מִשְּׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת פְּסוּלָה – ״זָהָב״ ״זָהָב״ ״מִקְשָׁה״ ״מִקְשָׁה״ מַאי דָּרְשַׁתְּ בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But the term **"will be made" is also written with regard to** the command that the Candelabrum be **a beaten work,** as it is written: "Of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made" (Exodus 25:31). The Gemara answers: The term **"beaten work"** appears in Exodus 25:31, and the term **"beaten work"** appears again in Exodus 25:36, **to** demonstrate that this requirement is **indispensable.** The Gemara challenges: But the term **"gold"** appears in Exodus 25:31, and the term **"gold"** appears again in Exodus 25:36. Why not also say that this **as well** is **to** demonstrate that this requirement is **indispensable?** The Gemara answers: **What** is **this** comparison? **Granted, if you say** that when the Candelabrum is fashioned **from fragments** of gold it is **unfit** but when fashioned **from other types of metal** it is **fit,** then **this is** the reason that it was necessary for the verse to state **"gold," "gold"** twice, and **"a beaten work," "a beaten work"** twice, **to** teach **an interpretation,** which is explained shortly. **But if you say** that when the Candelabrum is fashioned **from fragments** of gold it is **fit** but when fashioned **from other types of metal** it is **unfit, what do you interpret from** the repeated terms **"gold," "gold"** and **"a beaten work," "a beaten work"?**

The Gemara then addresses the apparent redundancy of both *mikshah* and *zahav* each appearing twice. The question raised is: could the doubling of *zahav* similarly indicate that gold is required precisely and cannot be substituted? The Gemara's response is structural: *ha'inhu zahav zahav mikshah mikshah lidrasha* — the repetitions are there to generate a *drasha* that clarifies what is and is not essential. The position that other metals are valid but assembled pieces are not is the only reading that gives meaningful content to all the doubled terms.

### Material Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דְּרָשָׁא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּכָּר זָהָב טָהוֹר יַעֲשֶׂה אֹתָהּ אֵת כׇּל הַכֵּלִים הָאֵלֶּה״ – בָּאָה זָהָב, בָּאָה כִּכָּר; אֵינָהּ בָּאָה זָהָב, אֵינָהּ בָּאָה כִּכָּר. ״גְּבִיעֶיהָ כַּפְתּוֹרֶיהָ וּפְרָחֶיהָ״ – בָּאָה זָהָב, בָּאָה גְּבִיעִים כַּפְתּוֹרִים וּפְרָחִים; אֵינָהּ בָּאָה זָהָב, אֵינָהּ בָּאָה גְּבִיעִים כַּפְתּוֹרִים וּפְרָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What interpretation** is referenced above? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Of a talent of pure gold will it be made, with all these vessels"** (Exodus 25:39); this verse teaches that if the Candelabrum is **fashioned** of **gold,** it must be **fashioned** with the precise weight of **a talent;** and if it is **not fashioned** of **gold** but of other types of metal, then it does **not** need to be **fashioned** with the precise weight of **a talent.** Similarly, the verse: "And you will make a Candelabrum of pure gold; of beaten work shall the Candelabrum be made, even its base, its shaft, **its goblets, its knobs, and its flowers"** (Exodus 25:31), teaches that if the Candelabrum is **fashioned** of **gold** it must be **fashioned** with **goblets, knobs, and flowers,** and if it is **not fashioned** of **gold** but of other types of metal, then it does **not** need to be **fashioned** with **goblets, knobs, and flowers.**

The Gemara then identifies how this works. The verse *kikar zahav tahor ya'aseh otah et kol hakeilim ha'eileh* — "one *kikar* of pure gold shall he make it, all these vessels" — establishes a list of requirements that apply when gold is used. *Ba'ah zahav ba'ah kikar* — when gold is used, the *kikar* weight applies; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah kikar* — when other metals are used, the precise weight requirement does not apply. Similarly: *ba'ah zahav ba'ah gevi'im kaftorim u'ferachim* — when gold is used, all the ornamental cups, spheres, and flowers are required; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah gevi'im kaftorim u'ferachim* — if not gold, these ornaments are not mandatory.

The Gemara asks whether one might further extend this: perhaps when not gold, one need not even have seven branches? The answer is *hu pumit mikrei* — a single-branched lamp is simply not called a *menorah* at all; it is called a lamp. The seven-branch structure is definitional, not merely a gold-dependent enhancement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָ נָמֵי: בָּאָה זָהָב – בָּאָה קָנִים, אֵינָהּ בָּאָה זָהָב – אֵינָהּ בָּאָה קָנִים? הָהוּא פָּמוֹט מִיקְּרֵי. ״וְזֶה מַעֲשֵׂה הַמְּנֹרָה מִקְשָׁה זָהָב״ – בָּאָה זָהָב, בָּאָה מִקְשָׁה; אֵינָהּ בָּאָה זָהָב, אֵינָהּ בָּאָה מִקְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then why not **also say** with regard to the branches of the Candelabrum, which are described in Exodus 25:31 along with the term "gold," that if the Candelabrum is **fashioned** of **gold** it must be **fashioned** with **branches,** but if it is **not fashioned** of **gold** but of other types of metal, then it does **not** need to be **fashioned** with **branches?** The Gemara answers: A vessel like **that is called a candlestick [*pamot*],** not a candelabrum. With regard to the second derivation mentioned, the Gemara elaborates: The verse states: **"And this was the work of the Candelabrum, beaten work of gold,** to the base thereof, and to the flowers thereof, it was beaten work" (Numbers 8:4). This teaches that if the Candelabrum is **fashioned** of **gold** it must be **fashioned** as **a beaten work,** but if it is **not fashioned** of **gold** but of other types of metal, then it does **not** need to be **fashioned** as **a beaten work** and may be made from fragments.

*Ba'ah zahav ba'ah mikshah* — when gold is used, it must be one beaten piece; *eina ba'ah zahav eina ba'ah mikshah* — when other metals are used, this requirement does not apply. This confirms the overall framework: gold triggers a maximally demanding set of requirements, while other metals require only the seven-branched form itself. The doubled words of *mikshah* and *zahav* are therefore not redundant but structurally necessary, each pair working together to define a linked system of conditional requirements.

### Trumpets Inverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִקְשָׁה״ דְּסֵיפָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? לְמַעוֹטֵי חֲצוֹצְרוֹת, דְּתַנְיָא: חֲצוֹצְרוֹת הָיוּ בָּאִים מִן הָעֶשֶׁת, מִן הַכֶּסֶף. עֲשָׂאָם מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת – כְּשֵׁרִים, מִשְּׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת – פְּסוּלִים. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִשְּׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת פְּסוּלִים, דִּכְתִיב ״כֶּסֶף״ וַהֲוָיָה, מִן הַגְּרוּטָאוֹת נָמֵי ״מִקְשָׁה״ וַהֲוָיָה? מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְנוֹרָה ״מִקְשָׁה הִיא״ – ״הִיא״, וְלָא חֲצוֹצְרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose does the term **"beaten work"** that is repeated again **in the latter clause** of the verse **come?** The Gemara answers: It comes **to exclude** the **trumpets,** teaching that they are fit even if they were not fashioned from a single block. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The silver **trumpets** that Moses was commanded to fashion in the wilderness **were** to be **fashioned from** a complete **block** and **from silver.** If one **fashioned them from fragments they are fit,** but if he fashioned them **from other types of metal** then **they are unfit.** The Gemara asks: **And what** is **different** about trumpets made **from other types of metal** that **they are** rendered **unfit? As it is written** with regard to the trumpets: "Make for yourself two trumpets of silver; of beaten work you shall make them; and they shall be for you for the calling of the congregation" (Numbers 10:2). The verse employs the terms **silver and being,** indicating that it is an indispensable requirement. But accordingly, trumpets fashioned **from fragments** should be rendered unfit **as well,** since the verse employs the terms **beaten work and being.** The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One excludes** the trumpets when it states **with regard to** the **Candelabrum: "It was beaten work"** (Numbers 8:4), indicating that **it** alone, **but not** the **trumpets,** was beaten work.

The Gemara derives the second instance of *mikshah* — *mikshah ta'aseh* — as serving to exclude the *chatzotzrot* (trumpets) that Moshe fashioned. The *chatzotzrot* represent the precise inverse of the *menorah*: they must be silver, but they do not need to be fashioned from a single beaten piece. A *braita* states: *chatzotzrot hayu ba'ot min ha-ashet u'min ha-kesef* — the trumpets could come from a single piece or from silver; *asan min ha-gruta'ot, kesheirah* — if made from melted-together pieces, valid; *mishar minei matachot, psulim* — other metals, invalid. Why must they be silver? *D'chtiv kesef v'havaya* — the verse writes silver and "shall be" together, establishing silver as precise and non-negotiable. Why does the *Torah* not similarly insist on a single piece? Because the verse *hi v'lo chatzotzrot* — "it [the *menorah*] is one beaten piece, and not the *chatzotzrot*" — specifies the *menorah* as the single item requiring a unitary beaten form.

The Gemara then records a *braita* on the vessels of Moshe generally: *kol ha-keilim she'asah Moshe kesheirim lo u'kesheirim l'dorot* — all the vessels Moshe made were valid for his time and for all subsequent generations. The *chatzotzrot* are an exception: *kesheirot lo u'psulot l'dorot* — they were used in his generation but not in future ones, since their primary function was moving the camp. The verse *aseh lecha* — "make for yourself" — is read as *lecha v'lo l'dorot*, for your generation and not for future ones, precisely because both the context (mobilizing a moving camp) and the double use of *lecha* in the verse (*aseh lecha* and *v'hayu lach*) signal temporal limitation. By contrast, similar language by the *aron* (*v'asita lach aron etz*) does not limit it to Moshe's generation, since there the *lach* is read either as indicating Moshe's personal contribution of funds or as God expressing that ideally the *aron* would be Moshe's own — both readings that preclude the limiting interpretation.

### Rav Yosef Alt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי דַּעְתָּךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בֵּין מָר וּבֵין מָר כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי דָּרְשִׁי. מִיהוּ, מָר סָבַר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת – אַף כֹּל שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת, וּמָר סָבַר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ דָּבָר חָשׁוּב – אַף כׇּל דָּבָר חָשׁוּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סְמִי דִּידָךְ מִקַּמֵּי דִּידִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: What,** in **your opinion,** is the explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda? Rav Pappa, son of Rav Ḥanin, **said to him: Both** this **Sage and** that **Sage interpret** the verse: "And you will make a Candelabrum of pure gold; of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made" (Exodus 25:31), by means of the principle of **generalizations and details.** The verse begins with a generalization: "And you will make a Candelabrum," followed by a detail: "Of pure gold," which is then followed by a generalization: "Will the Candelabrum be made." According to the hermeneutic principle of generalizations and details, this teaches that any item that is similar to the detail is also deemed fit. **But** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **just as** the item mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined** as a type **of metal, so too, all** other types **of metal** may be used in fashioning the Candelabrum. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **just as** the item mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined** as **an item of substantial value, so too, all items of substantial value** may be used in fashioning the Candelabrum. Rav Yosef **said to him: Remove your** *baraita* **in light of my** *baraita*.

*Rav Yosef* responded by presenting an alternative *braita*, which he considered more accurate: *klei sharet she-asahan shel eitz* — service vessels fashioned of wood — *Rabbi* invalidates, *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* validates. This formulation places the debate not over cheap metals, but over non-metals entirely: wood versus metal.

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן שֶׁל עֵץ, רַבִּי פּוֹסֵל, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר. בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי דָּרֵישׁ כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דָּרֵישׁ רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continued: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to Temple **service vessels that** one **fashioned from wood, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** them **unfit and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems** them **fit.** According to this *baraita*, their dispute was with regard to a Candelabrum fashioned from wood, not from metal. Rav Yosef explains: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree? Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **interprets** verses by means of the principle of **generalizations and details, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, interprets** verses by means of the principle of **amplifications and restrictions.**

*Rav Yosef* then asks *Rav Pappa*: according to your version, what interpretive method are both opinions using? *Rav Pappa* responds that both employ *klal u'prat u'klal*: *v'asita menorah* is the *klal* (general); *zahav tahor* is the *prat* (specific); *v'kikar et ha-menorah* is the return to *klal*. By the rules of *klal u'prat u'klal*, one includes anything like the specific — like gold. *Rav Yosef* finds this unsatisfying, since by his reading the debate is governed instead by *ribuy u'mi'ut*: *v'asita menorah* is the *ribuy* (broad inclusion); *zahav tahor* is the *mi'ut* (restriction); *v'kikar et ha-menorah* is the renewed *ribuy*. The rule of *ribuy mi'ut v'ribuy* is that one excludes only what is most dissimilar to the specific term.

### Material Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דָּרֵישׁ כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי, ״וְעָשִׂיתָ מְנֹרַת״ – כָּלַל, ״זָהָב טָהוֹר״ – פָּרַט, ״מִקְשָׁה תֵּיעָשֶׂה הַמְּנוֹרָה״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל, כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל, אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת, אַף כֹּל שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דָּרֵישׁ רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ מְנֹרַת״ – רִיבָּה, ״זָהָב טָהוֹר״ – מִיעֵט, ״מִקְשָׁה תֵּיעָשֶׂה הַמְּנוֹרָה״ – חָזַר וְרִיבָּה. רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה – רִיבָּה הַכֹּל, וּמַאי רַבִּי? רַבִּי כֹּל מִילֵּי, וּמַאי מַיעֵט? מַיעֵט שֶׁל חֶרֶס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **interprets** the verse: "And you will make a Candelabrum of pure gold; of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made" (Exodus 25:31), by means of the principle of **generalizations and details. "And you will make a Candelabrum of"** is **a generalization,** as the material of the Candelabrum is not specified; **"pure gold"** is **a detail,** limiting the material exclusively to gold; and by then stating: **"Of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made,"** the verse **then makes a generalization.** The result is **a generalization and a detail and a generalization,** from which **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail,** leading to this conclusion: **Just as** the item mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined** as a type **of metal, so too, all** other types **of metal** may be used in fashioning the Candelabrum. By contrast, **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, interprets** the verse by means of the principle of **amplifications and restrictions. "And you will make a Candelabrum of"** is **an amplification,** as the material of the Candelabrum is not specified; **"pure gold"** is **a restriction,** limiting the material exclusively to gold; and by then stating: **"Of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made,"** the verse **repeated and amplified.** There is a hermeneutical principle that when a verse **amplified and** then **restricted and** then **amplified, it amplified** the relevant category to include **everything** except the specific matter excluded in the restriction. **And what did** the verse **include? It includes all materials,** even wood. **And what did** the verse **exclude** with this restriction? **It excluded** a Candelabrum fashioned **from earthenware,** which is furthest in quality from gold.

Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, as both sides agree in *Rav Pappa*'s version, the debate is about what "like gold" means. *Rebbi* holds: *mah prat meforash shel matechet, af kol shel matechet* — just as the specific (gold) is a metal, anything that is a metal qualifies. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* holds: *mah prat meforash davar chashuv, af kol davar chashuv* — just as gold is a distinguished material, anything distinguished qualifies, implying precious materials beyond metals might also be included, while inexpensive metals might be excluded.

Under *ribuy mi'ut v'ribuy*, as *Rav Yosef*'s version has it, almost everything is included by default; only the thing most dissimilar to gold is excluded. *Mi'et shel cheres* — pottery alone is excluded. Everything else — metals, wood, bone, glass short of pottery — would be included according to *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Evidence Consolidation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, סְמִי דִּידָךְ מִקַּמֵּי דִּידִי! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין לוֹ זָהָב מֵבִיא אַף שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, שֶׁל נְחֹשֶׁת, שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, וְשֶׁל בְּדִיל, וְשֶׁל עוֹפֶרֶת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר אַף בְּשֶׁל עֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa, son of Rav Ḥanin, said to him: **On the contrary, remove your** *baraita* **in light of my** *baraita*. Rav Yosef responded: That **cannot enter your mind, as it is taught** in another *baraita*: If the one who is fashioning the Candelabrum **has no gold, he may bring even** a Candelabrum made **of silver, of copper, of iron, of tin, or of lead. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems** it **fit even** if it was fashioned **from wood.** It is evident from this *baraita* that the dispute pertains only to a Candelabrum fashioned from wood, and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees that it may be fashioned from other types of metal.

*Rav Yosef* then asks *Rav Pappa*: why should I abandon my *braita* in favor of yours? *Rav Yosef* replies that multiple *braitot* converge on the position that the debate concerns non-metals, not distinctions within metals. One *braita* states: *ein lo zahav mevi af shel kesef, shel nechoshet, shel barzel, shel bedil, shel oferet* — if one does not have gold, one may bring the *menorah* from any other metal: silver, copper, iron, tin, lead. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda machshir af be-shel etz* — *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* further validates even wood. This pattern repeats across several *braitot*, all of which show that *Rabbi* and *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* are not debating the rank of metals among themselves, but whether non-metallic materials such as wood are included at all.
