# Shabbat 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three vs. Four Meals

### Dispute: 3 vs 4 Meals

The daf begins three lines from the bottom of the page, in the midst of a *gemara* discussing food on *shabbat* — a discussion that arose from a *mishna* dealing with saving *shalosh seudot* worth of food from a fire. After touching on *shetei halechem*, the *gemara* now turns to the fundamental question: how many meals is one obligated to eat on *shabbat*?

*Tanu rabanan*: how many meals is a person obligated (*chayav*) to eat on *shabbat*? The answer assumed by the *mishna* is three, and now the *gemara* makes that obligation explicit. Against this, Rabbi Chidqa asserts four. Rabbi Yochanan explains that both positions derive from a single *pasuk* — *vayomer Moshe ichluhu hayom*, Moses's instruction regarding the *man*, in which the word *hayom* ("today") appears three times: *ki Shabbat hayom l'Hashem* and *hayom lo timtza'uhu basadeh*. Rabbi Chidqa holds that these three instances of *hayom* refer exclusively to daytime, with the night meal additional and separate. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold that the nighttime meal is included within one of these three instances, yielding a total of three meals.

The precise mechanism by which *hayom* indicates the number of meals is not entirely transparent, and the position of four meals is itself worth pausing over. While we do not practice *arba seudot*, one can understand the logic: if the standard week involves roughly two major meals per day — a midday meal and an evening meal — then for *shabbat*, perhaps one adds a morning meal as well, yielding three. Rabbi Chidqa may be thinking along similar lines to *shetei halechem* — doubling the bread that fell on *erev shabbat* representing a kind of doubling of the standard — and extending that principle to meals as well. In any case, the *halacha* follows three meals.

### Hayom Derivation

The three instances of *hayom* in the account of the *man* serve as the hermeneutic anchor for both positions. Rabbi Chidqa's reading treats them as referring to the three daytime periods of *shabbat*, thereby placing the nighttime meal outside the count and arriving at four total. The Rabbis include the nighttime meal within the *hayom* derivation, reading one of the three instances as encompassing *leil shabbat*, and thus reaching three meals in total.

### Mishna Reconciliation

Having established this *tannaitic* dispute, the *gemara* examines whether the *mishna* that opened this discussion — *keitzad nafla deleika*, how does saving food work when a fire breaks out — can be reconciled with Rabbi Chidqa's position. The *mishna* states: if the fire broke out on *leil shabbat*, one may save food for three meals; if on *shabbat* morning, for two meals; if at *mincha* time, for one meal. On first reading, this seems to clearly presuppose only three *shabbat* meals in total, contradicting Rabbi Chidqa.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this. When the *mishna* says a fire broke out on *leil shabbat* and one may save three meals, perhaps it means the fire broke out *after* the Friday night meal had already been eaten — leaving three meals still to go, consistent with four total. Similarly, when the *mishna* says the fire broke out *shabbat* morning, perhaps it means after the morning meal had been eaten, leaving two — again consistent with four. And when the fire broke out at *mincha* and one may save one meal, perhaps that too was after the third meal, with one remaining. By this reading, the *mishna* is compatible with Rabbi Chidqa throughout.

### Rabbi Yosi's Rule

The *gemara* immediately recognizes that this reconciliation is strained, and it is undone by the *sof* of the very same *mishna*. Rabbi Yosi rules *leolam matsilin mazon gimmel seudot* — at any time a fire breaks out, one may always save the maximum of three meals. The debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosi concerns only whether one deducts meals already eaten or always saves the full three regardless. But the premise shared by both is that there are exactly three *shabbat* meals. *Michlal detanna kamma gimmel sevira leh* — it is implicit from the *tanna kamma*'s position that three meals is the operating assumption. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *makhvarta matnitin lo k'Rav Chisda* — the straightforward reading of the *mishna* is not in accordance with the four-meal position. Three meals on *shabbat* is the clear and simple sense of the *mishna*.

## Charity & Meals

### Tamchui & Kuppa

The *gemara* now pivots to related *mishnaic* material from *Peah*, using discussions about communal charity obligations to shed light on how many meals per day people were assumed to need. This is not a digression but rather a productive engagement with the *mishnayot* at the end of *Peah*, which constitute the *gemara*'s primary source for the institutionalized, communal dimension of *tzedakah*.

It is worth appreciating why these discussions appear at the end of *Peah*. *Peah*, along with *leket* and *shikhcha*, was the regular, recurring mechanism by which the poor were supported in an agricultural society — every harvest, the poor would gather the gleanings, the forgotten sheaves, and the corners of the field. It was both individual and communal in character, taking place simultaneously across farms, providing for the poor without requiring them to ask. It is at the end of those *mishnayot* that the Rabbis articulate what might be called a more urbanized and institutional expression of the same obligation: the *tamchui* and the *kuppa*. This communal responsibility precedes and stands apart from the later medieval articulation of *ma'aser kesafim*, which is more individualized, more oriented toward a commercial rather than agricultural economy, and while morally compelling, lacks the biblical grounding of *peah* itself. These *mishnayot* represent the Rabbis' effort to transpose the moral logic of agricultural *tzedakah* into a communal institutional framework.

The *mishna* states: *mi she'yesh lo mazon shtei seudot lo yitol min hatamchui* — one who has food for two meals may not take from the *tamchui*. The *tamchui* — literally the soup bowl — was a daily soup kitchen. Collectors would go from household to household, each home contributing a ladleful of whatever was being cooked, and the poor would come to receive a portion. The standard is two meals: if you have enough for two meals today, you are covered for the day and may not take from the daily distribution. This already tells us that the normative assumption for weekday eating is two meals per day.

The *mishna* then states: *mazon arba'asar* — if you have fourteen meals' worth of food, you may not take from the *kuppa*, the weekly charity box. The *kuppa* was a different institution: funds were collected, assessed according to people's means, and distributed to provide for a week's worth of food. Rashi notes that one would not typically draw from both simultaneously — the *tamchui* being for daily food, the *kuppa* for weekly provisions. Rashi suggests that the *kuppa* may have particularly served those who had known better circumstances and found the public line of the daily soup kitchen too degrading; taking from the *kuppa* would allow them to provision themselves privately across the week.

### Standard: 2 Meals/Day

The number fourteen is significant. A week has seven days. At two meals per day, that yields fourteen meals — exactly the threshold for the *kuppa*. But *shabbat* is part of that week, and *shabbat* has three meals, not two. So fourteen should actually be fifteen if *shabbat* is included, and sixteen according to Rabbi Chidqa with four *shabbat* meals. How then do we arrive at fourteen?

The *gemara* asks: *mani*? According to whom is this *mishna*? It fits neither the Rabbis (who would get fifteen) nor Rabbi Chidqa (who would get sixteen). The *gemara* answers: *le'olam Rabbanan* — it is the Rabbis — and we resolve the arithmetic by telling the recipient that the Saturday night meal he would otherwise eat as his Sunday evening allocation should instead be counted toward the third *shabbat* meal. In the summer especially, when *shabbat* ends late, one eats *shalosh seudot* in the afternoon and has no need for a full meal on *motzaei shabbat*. The Saturday night slot folds into the *shabbat* count, the Sunday morning meal covers one, and with that sleight of hand, the fourteen-meal count works.

The *gemara* then considers whether this can even be made to work according to Rabbi Chidqa, who requires four *shabbat* meals. The answer offered is that the Friday afternoon meal is simply moved into Friday night's slot — eat your Friday afternoon meal as your Friday night *shabbat* meal. But the *gemara* objects: *vekulei yoma de-me'alei shabsa be-ta'anisa mosivin leh*? Are we really asking him to fast all of *erev shabbat* so that he can fold that meal into *shabbat*? That is unreasonable.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty differently. *Ha mani Rabbi Akiva hi* — this *mishna* actually reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva, who says: *asei shabboscha chol ve-al titztarech le-briyos* — better to make your *shabbat* feel like a weekday than to become dependent on other people. On this view, the extra *shabbat* meal is not factored in at all — the poor person simply receives fourteen meals, like any other week, and does not receive additional funds to make *shabbat* special.

This raises an obvious tension: is this person not already *nitztrach labriyos*, already dependent on others? He is literally drawing from the *kuppa*. And there is a well-known *midrash* of *luvu alay va'ani poreia* — borrow money on my account and I will repay, meaning one should spend extra for *shabbat* even at the cost of debt. There are also *gemara*s that suggest taking *tzedakah* for *shabbat* is appropriate. And yet here is a *gemara* saying not to take extra.

The *Mishnah Berurah*, in the very opening discussion of *hilchos shabbat*, offers a resolution. The principle of borrowing and I will repay applies to someone who is not truly poor — he has a cash flow issue, not a poverty issue. For someone actually taking communal funds, if he is already receiving *tzedakah*, there is nothing wrong with taking a little extra to make *shabbat* nicer; the incremental dependence is not new. The statement of Rabbi Akiva — that it is better to make *shabbat* like a weekday — applies specifically to someone who has not yet entered the cycle of communal dependence. Better not to start than to take for *shabbat* as one's entry point into taking *tzedakah* at all.

Rashi, however, reads it somewhat differently in context. He suggests the obligation here runs not to the *ani* but to the givers and the communal fund. We are not obligated to provide extra funds beyond basic subsistence just so a poor person can fulfill the *mitzvah* of *oneg shabbat*. The *Pesach* parallel cuts the other way: *afilu ani hamitparnes min hatzedakah lo yifchesu lo me'arba kosos* — even a poor person supported by charity must be provided four cups at the Seder. But that is one day a year, the single occasion on which every Jew must experience the dignity of freedom. For *shabbat*, on a weekly recurring basis, it is not the communal fund's obligation to provide for *oneg*.

### Peah Evidence

The *gemara* now cites another *mishna* from *Peah*: *ein poskin le'ani ha'over mimakom lemakom mikikar befundyon me'arba sa'in besela* — a traveler passing through a town who is not even staying overnight is given a loaf of bread worth a *pundyon*, where four *se'ah* go to a *sela*. Working through the arithmetic: a *pundyon* is a 48th of a *sela*, and since a *sela* purchases four *se'ah*, a *pundyon* gets one a 12th of a *se'ah* of grain. That quantity, made into bread, provides two meals — enough for the day, consistent with the two-meal-per-day standard.

If the traveler stays overnight, *nosnin lo parnasas linah* — he is given provisions for his lodging. Rav Papa explains this as *purya* (a bed) and *uvei sadya* (a blanket or, according to Rashi, something to use as a pillow). This is a significant point: the communal obligation extended beyond food to basic physical comfort, though food remained the primary concern.

And critically for our discussion: *ve'im Shabbat* — if the traveler spends *shabbat* in town, *nosnin lo mazon gimmel seudot* — he is given three meals' worth of food. This is strong evidence from *Peah* that three meals is the standard for *shabbat*. The *gemara* asks whether this is conclusive proof for the Rabbis over Rabbi Chidqa. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps he arrives in town already holding one meal's worth of food, and we tell him to eat that one on *shabbat*, and we provide three more — giving him his four meals according to Rabbi Chidqa, with us only supplying three. But then, as the *gemara* notes, *vechi azil bereika azil*? Do we send him away on *motzaei shabbat* with nothing for his journey? No — we give him a meal to take with him. So it all ends up back at the same accounting. The simple sense of these *mishnayot* throughout is that *shabbat* entails three meals.

## Washing Dishes

### Dish-Washing Rules

Having completed the discussion of meals, the *gemara* addresses a practical *halachic* question that flows directly from the three-meal framework: when may dishes be washed on *shabbat*?

*Tanu rabbanan*: dishes used on *erev shabbat* may be washed in order to eat from them *shabbat* morning. Dishes used *shabbat* morning may be washed to eat from them at the midday meal. Dishes used at the midday meal may be washed to eat from them at *mincha* time. But from *mincha* onward — *shuv eino madiach* — one no longer washes dishes. The rationale is that there are no more *shabbat* meals ahead for which the washing would be preparation.

An exception applies to cups, glasses, pitchers, and other drinking vessels — *cosot, kityonot, v'tzluchiyot* — which may be washed *kol hayom kuloh*, throughout the entire day, *lefi she'ein keva lishtiya* — because drinking has no fixed time. One may want a drink at any point during the day, and therefore washing a cup is never preparatory in the problematic sense; it always serves a potential immediate need.

### Hachana Principle

Rashi articulates the underlying principle: *ein hachana ela lechol* — preparation for a weekday activity is prohibited on *shabbat*. The act of washing dishes after the last *shabbat* meal is not serving any *shabbat* purpose; it is preparing for *motzaei shabbat* or the following week, which is the prohibited *hachana*.

That said, this prohibition is not absolute. The *halacha* is that this rule applies only when the reason for washing is genuinely preparatory — for the next use. If one is washing dishes because one cannot tolerate having a dirty table or a sink full of plates on *shabbat* itself — because of the immediate aesthetic or domestic discomfort — that is entirely permitted. The issue is intent: washing for the sake of the present moment is allowed; washing for the sake of having things ready after *shabbat* is not.

## Rewards for Oneg

### Three Meals Salvation

The *gemara* now transitions to *aggadata* on *shalosh seudot* and *oneg shabbat*, and this material will occupy the remainder of the daf. Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi in the name of Bar Kappara: *kol hamkayem shalosh seudot beShabbat nitztal mishalosh puraniyot* — one who fulfills the obligation of three meals on *shabbat* is saved from three afflictions: *chevlei mashiach* (the birth pangs of the messianic era), *dina shel Gehinnom* (the judgment of *gehinnom*), and *milchemet Gog u-Magog* (the war of Armageddon).

The textual derivation works by verbal analogy (*gezeira shavah*) on the word *yom*. The three meals are derived from the three instances of *hayom* in the *man* passage. Each affliction is also associated with a *yom* in a separate verse: *chevlei mashiach* connects to the verse about the day of Elijah's coming before the great *yom Hashem*; the judgment of *gehinnom* connects to *yom evra*, the day of divine wrath; and *Gog u-Magog* connects to *beyom bo Gog*, the day Gog arrives.

The Maharsha explains the thematic logic: *shabbat* is *me'ein olam haba* — a foretaste of the world to come. All three of these afflictions — *chevlei mashiach*, *gehinnom*, *Gog u-Magog* — are themselves *me'ein olam haba* in a painful sense, the convulsions that precede or attend the ultimate reality. One who transforms *shabbat* into a genuine experience of *me'ein olam haba* through joy, through the three meals, through *oneg*, accesses that future reality directly — and bypasses the painful passages that otherwise lead to it.

### Oneg & Inheritance

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Yosi: *kol hime'aneg et haShabbat nosnin lo nachala bli meitzarim* — anyone who brings pleasure to *shabbat* is granted an inheritance without boundaries. The source is the verse in Isaiah: *vekarata laShabbat oneg* — if you call *shabbat* a pleasure — *az tisanag al Hashem, vehirkavticha al bamotei aretz, ve'he'achalticha nachalas Yaakov avicha* — then you will delight in God, be made to ride upon the heights of the land, and be given the inheritance of your father Yaakov.

Why is this described as *nachala bli meitzarim* — an inheritance without limits? The *gemara* contrasts it with the inheritances associated with Avraham and Yitzchak. Avraham was told *kum hithalech ba'aretz le'orkah u'lerachbah* — rise, walk the land in its length and breadth — but still, *ba'aretz*, this land, nothing more. Yitzchak was told *ki lecha u'lezaracha etein es kol ha'aratzos ha'eil* — to you and your descendants I give all these lands — but still *these* lands, a defined set. Yaakov, however, was told *u'faratzta* — you will burst forth — *yama va'keidma tzafona va'negba*, in all four directions, with no limit whatsoever. Hence *nachala bli meitzarim*.

There is another dimension here, drawn from a parallel *midrash*. In a different context, the nations say *le'asid lavo* that they will go up to the *beis Elokei Yaakov* — not the God of Avraham or Yitzchak. Why? *Lo ke'Avraham she'krao har* — Avraham called the place a mountain, *behar Hashem yeira'eh*. *Lo ke'Yitzchak she'krao sadeh* — Yitzchak called it a field, *vayeitzei Yitzchak lasuach basadeh*. *Ella ke'Yaakov she'krao bayis* — Yaakov called it a house, *ein zeh ki im beis Elokim*. The mountain and the field are vast and open, yet they have natural limits. The house has walls and boundaries — yet it is the house that becomes the *beis Elokei* for all nations. The intimacy and structure of the house, rather than undercutting expansiveness, enables it. One who provides that sense of bounded warmth, of structured intimacy, ultimately unleashes an inheritance without limit. This is what *oneg shabbat* represents: not wild, unbounded experience, but the disciplined, contained joy of the home — which paradoxically *u'faratzta*, bursts forth in every direction.

### Freedom from Exile

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a related teaching: *nitzol mishibud galiyot* — one who properly engages in *oneg shabbat* is saved from subjugation in exile. The verse reads *vehirkavticha al bamotei aretz* — I will make you ride upon the heights of the land — and a parallel verse says *ve'atah al bamoteimo tidrokh* — you will tread upon their heights — meaning you will have dominion and will not be trodden upon by other nations.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav adds: *kol hime'aneg et haShabbat nosnin lo mish'alot libo* — one who finds pleasure in *shabbat* receives all the desires of his heart. The proof-text: *vehit'aneg al Hashem veyiten lekha mish'alot libekha*. The *gemara* asks: the word *oneg* in that verse — why do we read it as referring to *shabbat* specifically? Because the verse in Isaiah says explicitly *vekarata laShabbat oneg* — you shall call *shabbat* a pleasure — connecting the *oneg* of Psalms to the *oneg* of *shabbat*.

The sense of all these teachings is a kind of expansiveness. The word *meitzar* carries both the meaning of boundary and the meaning of affliction (*tzar*). *Nachala bli meitzarim* is thus both inheritance without limit and inheritance without distress — the two being connected, since the experience of constraint and the experience of suffering are closely related. *Oneg*, pleasure, is precisely the release from that constriction.

### Examples of Oneg

The *gemara* now turns to the practical question: *bemah me'ang'o* — how does one actually experience *oneg shabbat*?

Rav Yehuda son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat in the name of Rav says: a special cooked dish of beets (*tavshil shel tared*), large fish (*dagim gedolim*), and heads of garlic (*roshei shumim*). Rashi comments that these were considered *chashuv* — prestigious, special fare. The point is not that these must be the specific foods, but that these were the special dishes of their time and place. *Oneg shabbat* means having something special, something you would not eat on an ordinary weekday.

Rav Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav goes further — or perhaps simply clarifies: *afilu davar mu'at, im lichvod Shabbat aso, harei zeh oneg* — even something small, if it is done specially for *shabbat*, that constitutes *oneg*. The formulation is striking: one might have expected *harei zeh kavod* — this is honor to *shabbat*. The point is that the pleasure, even if modest, is real *oneg* by virtue of the *kavanah* — the intention to make *shabbat* different. Rav Papa offers a concrete example: *kasa de-harsena* — a type of fish fried in batter, a small but special dish.

## Shabbat & Redemption

### Idolater Forgiven

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol ha-meshamer Shabbat ke-hilchato, afilu oved avodah zarah ke-dor Enosh, mochelon lo* — anyone who observes *shabbat* according to its laws, even if they have worshipped idols in the manner of the generation of Enosh — when, as the verse says, *az huchal likro be-shem Hashem*, *avodah zarah* first began — it is forgiven to him. The proof-text: *ashrei enosh ya'aseh zot, shomer Shabbat me-chalelo* — read not *me-chalelo* (from desecrating it) but *machul lo* — everything is forgiven to him.

This is, as the *gemara* presents it, a striking statement. The Maharal explains that *shabbat* is the unique gift of God to *Bnei Yisrael*, the most concentrated expression of belief in God's creation and sovereignty. Observing it fully demonstrates a total commitment that outweighs even the grave sin of *avodah zarah* — which, one might add, in a pervasive pagan social environment did not always represent the same wholesale rejection of God it might otherwise imply, but could reflect the pull of the *yetzer hara* rather than a principled rejection of the divine.

### First Shabbat Violation

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *ilmalei shamru Yisrael Shabbat rishonah, lo shalta bam ummah ve-lashon* — had Israel kept the very first *shabbat* in the desert, no nation or tongue would ever have had dominion over them. The source: the Torah records that on the seventh day, people went out to gather — violating the command to rest — and immediately following, *vayavo Amalek*. The connection is not coincidental; the violation made Israel vulnerable. The *gemara* acknowledges that *vayavo Amalek* is not immediately juxtaposed — several events intervene — but the *derasha* identifies the first *shabbat* violation as the moment that opened the breach.

This is the precise inverse of the promise: *shabbat* in its fullness connects Israel entirely to God and is the source of their protection. Its violation severs that connection and exposes them to attack.

### Two Shabbatot = Redempti…

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: *ilmalei meshamrin Yisrael shtei Shabbatot ke-hilchatan, miyad nig'alin* — if Israel were to properly keep just two *shabbatot*, they would be redeemed immediately. Two is the minimum to establish a pattern rather than an anomaly — two demonstrates commitment. The proof: *koh amar Hashem lasarisim asher yishmoru et shabbatotai* — the plural *shabbatotai* in Isaiah — and immediately following: *vahaviotim el har kodshi ve-simachtim be-veit tefilati* — I will bring them to My holy mountain and gladden them in My house of prayer.

The *shabbat*-redemption nexus thus runs in both directions: the failure to observe *shabbat* is what made exile possible; the genuine, committed observance of *shabbat* would bring the *geulah*.

## Yehai Chelki Sayings

### Three Meals Merit

Rav Yosef opens the series: *yehei chelki me-ochlei shalosh seudot be-Shabbat* — would that my portion be among those who eat three meals on *shabbat*. The very fact that this is articulated as a wish implies that it was not universally practiced. As with many of the *yehai chelki* statements, the form itself signals something significant: these are not simply praises of widely observed practices, but expressions of aspiration toward particular forms of piety that, while available to all, were not the norm.

The broader genre of *yehai chelki* is worth understanding. These are statements in which a *tanna* or *amora* says, in effect: of all the things one might aspire to, I most want my reward to be associated with those who do *X*. Some of the *meforshim* suggest that the underlying idea is not that these practices are more important than others, but that there is something particularly admirable about taking one *mitzvah* and making it one's own — specializing in it, excelling at it, giving it extraordinary care. The person who has one *mitzvah* they perform with complete devotion achieves a depth of connection to Torah that generalizes outward. It is, in some respects, an underemphasized ideal in our communities.

### Hallel Daily

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki migomrei hallel bechol yom* — would that my portion be among those who complete *hallel* every day. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: has it not been said that one who recites *hallel* every day is *mecharev umegadef* — as if cursing and blaspheming? Rabbi Yosi clarifies: he is not referring to what we call *Hallel Hamitzri* — the *hallel* of *chaggim* commemorating the miracles of Egypt — but to *psukei dezimra*.

Why is reciting the formal *hallel* daily considered blaspheming? Rashi explains that reciting it every day trivializes it, reducing what should be a profound religious expression to mere rote. One might add a deeper dimension: if every day is treated as equally miraculous — if we are always in the mode of *hallel* for *nisei Mitzrayim* — then nothing is special, and paradoxically God becomes less visible rather than more so. The lesson of *nisei Mitzrayim*, as the Ramban articulates, is to train us to see God in *everything*, including the natural order. But if we equate the natural and the supernatural by reciting *hallel* for both, we may actually desensitize ourselves rather than heighten our sensitivity.

*Psukei dezimra*, by contrast, is precisely the appropriate daily response: it is *hallel* for nature praising God, not for the supernatural. Rashi, in his definition of *psukei dezimra*, limits it to two of the *hallelu* psalms — the institution of *Baruch She'amar*, *Yishtabach*, and the fuller order of *psukei dezimra* familiar today is not laid out in the *gemara* as a formal institution, though this *gemara* does provide its foundational rationale. To praise God for the order of creation, every day, is not blaspheming — it is the ideal form of regular divine service.

### Sunrise/Sunset Prayer

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-hamitpalelim im dimdumei chamah* — would that my portion be among those who pray with the reddening of the sun, meaning at *vatikin* — exactly at sunrise. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: it is a *mitzvah* to pray at that moment. Rabbi Zeira derives this from the verse *yira'ucha im shemesh velifnei yareiach dor dorim* — they will see You with the sun, and before the moon, from generation to generation. Rashi notes that this is not limited to sunrise; the verse's mention of "before the moon" alludes to sunset as well, and there is something religiously evocative about praying precisely at the turning moments of the day, when the sun rises over the land or sets below the horizon, moments that naturally evoke a more heightened awareness of the divine.

### Righteous Death

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mimetai be-choli me'ayim* — would that my portion be among those who die from intestinal illness. The reason: it has been said that the majority of *tzaddikim* die this way. Why is that? Tosafot explains that it is a particularly painful form of death, and that suffering at the end of life serves as *kaparah*, atonement. Others suggest that such a death purges the body of its material waste, so that one enters *olam haba* in a state of minimal *gashmius*. Of these explanations, Tosafot's reading — focused on *kaparah* through suffering — is probably the most *peshat*.

### Death in Mitzvah

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-meisei be-derech mitzvah* — would that my portion be among those who die in the performance of a *mitzvah*. To die while engaged in a *mitzvah* is to depart this world at a moment of complete connection to God — it is the ultimate *zechus*, an incomparable merit. Unlike dying from intestinal disease, which one cannot choose, dying in the performance of a *mitzvah* speaks to a life so organized around *mitzvot* that even the final moment is defined by one.

### Tosefes Shabbat

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-machnisei Shabbat bi-Tveriya u-motzi'ei Shabbat be-Tzippori* — would that my portion be among those who bring *shabbat* in early in Tveriya and let *shabbat* out late in Zippori.

Tveriya sits in a valley, such that the surrounding mountains obscure the sun before it actually reaches the horizon; sunset appears to arrive earlier than it astronomically does. The residents of Tveriya therefore perceived *shabbat* as beginning earlier. Zippori sits on a mountain, and due to the curvature of the earth, the sun appears to still be above the horizon even after it has actually set; *shabbat* appeared to end later. In both cases, the communities' perception drove their practice, and what made their observance praiseworthy was not that they were simply following local conditions but that their local conditions happened to produce *tosefes shabbat* — the addition of time to *shabbat* from the weekday. Rather than finding ways to minimize *shabbat*, they naturally maximized it, accepting a longer *shabbat* in both directions. The aspiration is to be among those who welcome *shabbat* early and let it go late — who are not in a rush to start or end it.

### Beit Midrash Sitters

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-moshivei beis ha-medrash ve-lo mi-ma'amidei beis ha-medrash* — would that my portion be among those who sit in the *beis ha-medrash*, not among those who make it stand. Rashi explains: *moshivei beis ha-medrash* are those who convene the *seder*, who bring people to their seats to learn — the one who gathers others to the *beis midrash*. *Ma'amidei beis ha-medrash* are those who end the session — who get up and announce that it is time to go. To be the one who brings people to Torah rather than the one who brings the session to a close is the aspiration.

One might extend this reading: even without a formal position of authority, the person who arrives early and stays late, who is present and seated throughout — who never makes the *beis midrash* stand on his account — embodies this ideal as well.

### Collecting Tzedakah

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-gabai tzedakah ve-lo mi-mchalkei tzedakah* — would that my portion be among those who collect *tzedakah*, not those who distribute it. One might expect the opposite — the distributor is the one actually giving money to the poor. But Rashi offers a profound explanation. As the *gemara* in *Bava Batra* rules, *tzedakah nitveis bi-shnayim ve-mit-chaleket bi-shlosha* — charity is collected by two and distributed by three. The distributors must assess the needs of each recipient individually, and in doing so they are subject to the natural pull of partiality: they may feel more compassion for one person than another, assess one generously and another stringently, give more than is warranted to one and less to another. To be scrupulously impartial in distributing *tzedakah* — to assess every *ani* purely according to *halacha* without the distortion of personal feeling — is extraordinarily difficult. The collector's role, by comparison, is less fraught with this particular challenge. Rabbi Yosi wishes to be among those who do the work of sustaining others but are spared the near-impossible burden of perfectly impartial distribution.

### Innocent Suspicion

Rabbi Yosi says: *yehei chelki mi-ma she-choshkin oso ve-ein bo* — would that my portion be among those who are suspected of something they did not do. Rav Papa adds: *le-didi chashkin ve-lo havi bi* — I myself was once suspected of something I had not done.

Why would one want to be in this position? The Maharal offers a powerful explanation. To be suspected when innocent is a terrible ordeal — one is subjected to the judgment of the entire community, everyone assumes guilt, and there is no defense against a reputation that sticks. It is a profound form of suffering. If one did commit the act and is suspected, the suspicion functions as *kaparah* — it is proportionate. But to suffer suspicion for something one did not do is unearned pain, and unearned suffering generates extraordinary divine reward. The Maharal draws a comparison to the *sotah*: if she is innocent and undergoes the trial, the Torah promises *ve-niktah ve-nizra'ah zera* — she is cleansed and blessed with children. Innocent suffering of public suspicion functions in an analogous way. One would naturally rather avoid the suspicion altogether — *oy li ve-oy l'koni* — but among those things one cannot fully control, this is one that carries with it, for the innocent, remarkable reward.

## Rabbi Yosi's Testimonies

### Family Life

The *gemara* now shifts to a series of personal testimonies from Rabbi Yosi about his own conduct — not *yehai chelki* aspirations, but first-person accounts of how he lived. These statements stand somewhat apart from the earlier genre, and the transition into them through the *yehai chelki* series of Rabbi Yosi is a natural editorial connection.

Rabbi Yosi says: *chamesh be'ilot ba'alti, ve-natati chamisha arazim be-Yisrael* — I was intimate with my wife five times, and I planted five cedars in Israel. He then names his five sons: Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Chalafta bar Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Avtilos bar Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi.

The *gemara* asks: is there not a sixth son, ורדימס *Vardimas*? The *gemara* answers that Vardimas and Menachem are the same person — Vardimas was a nickname. Why was he called Vardimas? *She-panav domim le-vered* — his face resembled a rose. (The ArtScroll notes that Vardimas is a Greek word meaning "strong-hearted," though the etymological coincidence with *vered* — rose in Hebrew — is noted as well.)

The *gemara* then raises a more fundamental challenge: *le-meimra de-Rabbi Yosi mitzvat onah lo kiyem*? Are we to conclude that Rabbi Yosi did not fulfill his obligation of *onah* — marital intimacy — which recurs at regular intervals throughout one's life? Surely he was intimate with his wife more than five times in total. The *gemara* resolves: *ela eima chamesh be'ilot ba'alti ve-shaniti* — Rabbi Yosi means that on five occasions he was intimate twice in the same night — *bo'el ve-shoneh* — because according to the tradition, one who wishes to have intelligent male sons should be intimate and repeat within the same night.

### Wife as Home

Rabbi Yosi says: *miyamai lo kariti le-ishti ishti ve-leshori shori* — I never called my wife "my wife" or my ox "my ox." Instead, *le-ishti beiti* — I called my wife "my house," and *le-shori sadi* — my ox "my field." The reasoning is that the wife creates the home and the ox works the field; the reference to the purpose rather than the possession is meant to express the importance of each.

The *gemara*'s Aramaic itself reflects this sensibility — the Aramaic word for wife, *d'veiteihu*, means literally "the one of his house." And *beis Yaakov* in the Torah is interpreted by the *gemara* as referring to the women. That said, the formulation is not unproblematic: rendering one's wife in purely instrumental terms, even instrumentally honorable terms, is troubling. One can charitably read it as Rabbi Yosi emphasizing what he valued about her contribution — but the reduction of a person to their function is its own difficulty, and the *gemara* does not seem fully at ease with it either.

### Modesty

Rabbi Yosi says: *miyamai lo nistakalti be-milah sheli* — I never looked at my *brit milah*, my circumcision. The *gemara* then recounts that when people asked Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — Rabbeinu HaKadosh — why he merited the title "the Holy Rabbi," he gave the same answer: *miyamai lo nistakalti be-milah sheli*. If Rabbi Yosi made the same claim, why was he not called Rabbeinu HaKadosh?

### Extreme Modesty

The *gemara* answers: *be-Rebbi milta achrita havai bei* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi had an additional level beyond Rabbi Yosi. *Shelo hichnis yado tachat avneto* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi never placed his hand into his genital region at all. Not only did he avoid looking; his standard of bodily modesty was even more encompassing.

### No Strife

Rabbi Yosi says: *miyamai lo avarti al divrei chaveirai* — I never went against what my friends said. He then illustrates with a hyperbolic example: *yodeia ani be-atzmi she-eini kohen* — I know beyond any doubt that I am not a *kohen*. Yet *im omrim li chaveirai aleh laduchan, ani oleh* — if my friends were to tell me to go up to the *duchan* for *birkat kohanim*, I would go up.

The *halachic* dimension is set aside here as an exaggeration; the moral point is vivid. Even when he knew with certainty that he was right, Rabbi Yosi would not generate *machloket* by insisting on his position. The willingness to yield, when he knew he was correct, to avoid discord — this is the *midah* he is describing. It is a particularly difficult form of self-effacement, one that requires not only humility but a conviction that peace is worth more than being right.

### Careful Speech

Rabbi Yosi says: *miyamai lo amarti davar ve-chasarti le-achorai* — I never said something and then looked behind me to see who was listening. This is the final testimony, and in many ways the most immediately recognizable. The behavior it describes — saying something and then immediately checking the room to see whether anyone overheard — is a sign of speech that is calibrated not by truth or principle but by audience. If one never needs to check who is behind them, it is because one has nothing to fear from being overheard — because one says only what one would say in any company, to any listener. This consistency of speech, regardless of who is present, is the standard Rabbi Yosi held himself to throughout his life.
