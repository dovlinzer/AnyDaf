# Temurah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Se'ir HaMishtale'ach

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא מֵעִיקָּרָא לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי הַאי מִיקְּבַע לַשֵּׁם, אֲבָל הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִינְּכַר שֵׁם — לָא לָקֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Since it might **enter your mind to say** that **granted,** if one does this **initially,** before the lottery, **we do not know if this** blemished animal will **be designated** as the one sacrificed **to the Lord** or sent to the wilderness. Therefore, the one who consecrated the blemished animal is flogged. **But here, since it is** already **clear** that the other animal is to be sacrificed to the **Lord,** and the one he consecrates will be sent to the wilderness, perhaps he is **not flogged** for consecrating it. The verse therefore **teaches us** that this is also a violation of the prohibition and he is flogged.

The discussion on daf 7 continues a line of inquiry that is only somewhat related to the masechta's central theme of *temurah*. The unifying thread is the concept of sanctity one ought not to confer — and which, once conferred, takes effect regardless. Specifically, the *gemara* is occupied with the prohibition against sanctifying as a *korban* an animal that bears a blemish. The prohibition will, before long, explicitly invoke *temurah* as well. We pick up at the bottom of 6b.

### Lottery Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב קְרָא ״לַה׳״ הוּא דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, הָא לָא רַבִּי קְרָא ״לַה׳״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ בַּעַל מוּם שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. מִכְּדִי, אֵין הַגּוֹרָל קוֹבֵעַ אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר הָרָאוּי!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* indicates that **the reason** why a blemished animal **may not be brought** as the **scapegoat** is **that the verse writes: "Unto the Lord."** One may infer that **had the verse not included** the scapegoat by employing the term: **"Unto the Lord," I would say** that it is **permitted** to sacrifice **a blemished scapegoat. Now,** of the two goats of the Yom Kippur service, one is selected as the scapegoat and one as an offering by lottery. As a rule, **the lottery can establish** the animals in their respective roles **only** if each **is fit** for either role. Given that a blemished animal cannot be sacrificed as an offering, it cannot be designated as the scapegoat either. The term "unto the Lord" therefore seems unnecessary.

The *gemara* states: *ta'ama de-katav kra la-Hashem* — the reason one may not designate a *se'ir hamishtalea'ach* that is blemished is because the verse explicitly adds the word *la-Hashem*, which serves to include this case. The question immediately arises: had the verse not added *la-Hashem*, one might have supposed a blemished *se'ir hamishtalea'ach* would be permitted. But how could that be? The principle is *ein ha-goral kove'a ela be-davar ha-ra'uy* — the lottery over the two Yom Kippur goats is valid only if both animals are fit to serve in either capacity. Since one of the two goats may emerge from the lottery as the one designated *la-Hashem*, both must be unblemished at the outset. How then could we ever have entertained the thought that a blemished goat is acceptable?

The *gemara*'s answer is that without the explicit verse, one might have thought the blemished goat is technically ineligible but that no negative prohibition has been violated — perhaps the entire procedure would simply have been invalid from the start, and there would have been no occasion for transgression. The verse therefore comes to teach that not only is such a designation invalid, but that one actively transgresses in making it. Although the *gemara*'s precise reasoning on this point is somewhat elusive, the upshot is clear: without the verse, one might have thought that since a blemished animal is categorically excluded from the lottery, the prohibition never applies in the first place. The verse tells us otherwise.

### Chananya HaMitzri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מַנִּי? חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ דָּם בְּכוֹס — מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ וּמְזַוֵּוג לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Ḥanan the Egyptian, who said** that if the scapegoat dies before being sent to the wilderness, **even** if the other goat has already been slaughtered and its **blood** collected **in the cup,** the slaughtered goat is nevertheless a fit offering, and the priest **brings another** goat **and pairs it with** the slaughtered goat to serve as the scapegoat. This new scapegoat is thereby designated without a lottery, and one might therefore suppose it can be blemished. The term "unto the Lord" is therefore needed to teach that it must be unblemished.

Rav Yosef offers a different answer: this ruling follows the position of *Chananya HaMitzri*, who maintains that *afilu dam be-kos me'akev chaveiro mishtafek lo* — even if the blood of the *korban la-Hashem* has already been collected in the vessel, you may bring a replacement for the *se'ir la-Azazel* should it have died. According to *Chananya HaMitzri*, the collected blood remains valid and does not become *dachui* (rejected). If so, one might imagine a scenario in which the replacement goat is designated directly as *la-Azazel* without going through the lottery again, since we know from the outset that it will serve only in that capacity — in which case we might have thought that such a goat could bear a blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְחָנָן הַמִּצְרִי, דְּלָא הָוֵי דִּיחוּי, דְּלָא צְרִיךְ הַגְרָלָה — מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? דִּילְמָא מַיְיתֵי וּמַגְרִיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted that you learn from** the statement of **Ḥanan the Egyptian** that he holds **that there is no deferral** of the second goat once it has already been slaughtered. But **do you learn from him** that the new scapegoat **need not be** designated by **a lottery? Perhaps** the priest **brings** two potential scapegoats **and performs a lottery** to decide between them. Therefore, the question again arises: What need is there for the term "unto the Lord"?

The *gemara* pushes back: even granting *Chananya HaMitzri*'s position that the blood is not rejected, one cannot infer that no new lottery is required. *Dilma mayti u-magril* — perhaps you bring two new goats and conduct the lottery again. The one that falls *la-Hashem* is set aside and eventually redeemed when it acquires a blemish, while the blood from the original *korban* is used. Under this reading, even the replacement goat destined *la-Azazel* must be fit for the altar, since we do not know which will emerge from the lottery as *la-Hashem*. The original *hava amina* is thus not resolved by *Chananya HaMitzri*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן — מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ בְּלֹא הַגְרָלָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one of** the two goats **dies,** the priest **brings another** goat **without** another **lottery;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.** It is this scapegoat that, absent the term "unto the Lord," could have been blemished.

Rav Yosef therefore revises his answer: this ruling follows the view of Rabbi Shimon, who states explicitly in a *baraisa*: *met echad mehen mevi chaveiro belo hagrala divrei Rabbi Shimon* — if one of the two goats dies, you bring a replacement without a new lottery. According to Rabbi Shimon, there are scenarios in which the replacement goat is directly designated *la-Azazel*. One might therefore have thought that in such a case, a blemished animal would either be permitted or at least not constitute a punishable transgression. The extra word *la-Hashem* comes to teach that even a directly designated *se'ir la-Azazel* must be unblemished, and one who designates a blemished animal violates a prohibition.

### Rava's Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לֹא נִצְרְכָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּמַם בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם וְחִילְּלוֹ עַל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: No,** the term in the verse **is necessary** for a case **where** the scapegoat **became blemished on that day** of Yom Kippur, after it had been designated, **and** then the priest **desacralized it** by transferring its sanctity **onto another** blemished goat.

Rava offers yet another scenario that does not depend on any disputed position. *Kegon shehumam bo bayom* — suppose the lottery has already been conducted, designating one goat *la-Azazel*, and that goat then acquires a blemish on that same day. It cannot be used in its current state. The procedure is to transfer the *kedusha* off the blemished goat onto money, and then reassign that *kedusha* to a replacement. Everyone agrees no new lottery is required in this case, because the replacement's sanctity derives from the original designated animal rather than from a fresh designation.

In this scenario, one might have thought no prohibition is violated when the replacement itself bears a blemish, since the one designated *la-Hashem* is already clearly established. *Keivan d'itparesh l'shem lo laki* — once the other goat is explicitly set aside as *la-Hashem*, surely the *la-Azazel* replacement stands in no danger of being confused with it, and therefore perhaps no transgression occurs if it has a blemish. *Ka-mashma lan* — the verse teaches that you do nonetheless transgress when you transfer that *kedusha* onto a blemished replacement. Rava thus provides an independent scenario in which a new lottery is unnecessary, and where the prohibition against a blemished *se'ir la-Azazel* must be explicitly derived.

### Sanctity Transfer

It is worth noting that Rava's formulation assumes we already know, prior to the verse's teaching, that a blemished animal is objectionable for this purpose. The verse's contribution is specifically to establish that one incurs lashes — that the act constitutes a punishable violation — even in a case where the animal's role as *la-Azazel* has been unambiguously established. The broader principle established across all three scenarios is that transferring *kedusha* to a replacement for a designated *la-Azazel* animal does not require a new lottery, yet does not exempt one from the prohibition against conferring sanctity on a *ba'al mum*.

## Catching Blood

### Rabbi Yose Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַף קַבָּלַת הַדָּם. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּמָעוּךְ וְכָתוּת וְנָתוּק וְכָרוּת וְגוֹ׳״ — זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם, שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** above: The Sages said **in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda:** One who sacrifices a blemished animal violates a prohibition against **the collection of the blood as well.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "That which has its testicles bruised, or crushed, or torn, or cut,** you shall not offer unto the Lord" (Leviticus 22:24). The phrase "You shall not offer unto the Lord" is apparently superfluous, as the Torah already stated earlier: "But whatever has a blemish, you shall not offer" (Leviticus 22:20). Rather, **this** extra phrase is referring to **the collection of the blood,** for **which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said** that one is liable.

The *gemara* proceeds to an expansion of the prohibition's scope. We read: *mishum Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda af kabbalat hadam* — Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda adds that even the *kabbalat hadam*, the catching or receiving of the blood, constitutes a violation. Those familiar with Zevachim will recall the four principal acts of the sacrificial service: *shechita* (slaughter), *kabbala* (receiving the blood), *holacha* (conveying the blood to the altar), and *zerikat hadam* (sprinkling the blood). The preceding discussion had established that one transgresses by sanctifying, slaughtering, sprinkling, and burning a blemished animal. Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda now argues that catching the blood is equally prohibited.

### Rhetorical Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְתַנָּא קַמָּא, הַאי ״לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִזְרִיקַת דָּמִים. וְהָא נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״? אוֹרְחֵיהּ דִּקְרָא דְּמִשְׁתַּעֵי הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the first *tanna*,** who holds that one is not liable for collection of the blood per se, **why do I** need **this** phrase: **"You shall not offer,"** stated with regard to damaged testicles? The Gemara answers: **He requires** it **to** teach that one is liable for **sprinkling the blood.** The Gemara challenges: **But he derives this from** the phrase: **"Upon the altar"** (Leviticus 22:22), which indicates that one may not sacrifice any part of such an animal on the altar, even its blood. The Gemara answers: With regard to that phrase, the first *tanna* holds that it is simply **the** normal **manner of the verse that it speaks like this.** It does not teach any additional *halakha*.

The *gemara* asks: what is the source for this view? *Amar kra: u-ma'uach ve-chatut ve-natua'ch ve-karut lo takrivu elu la-Hashem* — the verse enumerates various categories of genital mutilation and declares: you shall not bring these to God. This *lo takrivu* could serve as the basis for prohibiting *kabbalat hadam*. The *tanna kamma*, who does not hold that catching the blood is prohibited, must use this verse for something else — specifically, as a source for the prohibition against sprinkling the blood. But the *gemara* points out that the prohibition against sprinkling was already derived from the phrase *al ha-mizbe'ach lo ta'alu*, "you shall not cause to ascend upon the altar." Why is the additional *lo takrivu* needed?

The *gemara* answers for the *tanna kamma*: *orcha dikra d'mishtaei hachi* — this is simply the rhetorical manner in which the verse speaks. The *lo takrivu* in this context is not establishing a new prohibition; rather, given that the verse is enumerating particularly repugnant physical mutilations, the phrase "you shall not bring these to God" functions as something closer to a moral declaration than a legal prohibition. The *Shita Mekubetzet* explains that the verse, dealing with mutilated genitalia, takes the tone of the verse in Malachi — *hakrivehu na le-pachatecha* ("present it to your governor") — expressing that this is simply not the sort of thing one brings before God. It is a statement of moral inappropriateness rather than a new *lav*.

This explanation is admittedly striking. Throughout the preceding *sugyot*, the *gemara* has derived separate prohibitions from each instance of *lo takrivu*. To now dismiss one instance as merely rhetorical requires explanation, and the *Shita Mekubetzet*'s answer — that the surrounding context of mutilation lends the phrase a different character — is the *gemara*'s considered response.

### Non-Jew Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, אוֹרְחֵיהּ דִּקְרָא הוּא? אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא קַבָּלַת הַדָּם מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָא: ״וּמִיַּד בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ — זוֹ הִיא קַבָּלַת הַדָּם שֶׁאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But also** according to **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** one may claim that **this is the** normal **manner of the verse.** Why does he derive a *halakha* from this phrase? The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** He does not derive liability for sprinkling of the blood from the phrase "upon the altar." **Rather, from where does he** derive the prohibition against **collection of the blood? He derives it from this** verse: **"Neither from the hand of a foreigner shall you offer** the bread of your God of any of these, because their corruption is in them; there is a blemish in them; they shall not be accepted for you" (Leviticus 22:25). **This** verse **is** referring to **the collection of the blood** when sacrificing a blemished animal, for **which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said** that one is liable.

The *gemara* continues: since even Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda cannot use the verse about mutilated animals (it too is merely rhetorical in its context), from where does he derive that *kabbalat hadam* is prohibited? *Nafka lei mei-ha: u-miyad ben nechar lo takrivu elu la-Hashem* — the verse states that you may not accept blemished animals even from non-Jews. This verse, as *Tosafos* notes, is itself revealing: its plain meaning presupposes that one may ordinarily accept *korbanos* from non-Jews (a principle derived elsewhere from the phrase *ish ish*), and only prohibits accepting blemished ones. The question then is why the verse needs to specify *lo takrivu* in this context. The answer, according to Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda, is that this phrase is superfluous — it would have been obvious that blemished animals cannot be accepted from non-Jews, since they cannot be accepted from anyone — and therefore the verse's additional *lo takrivu* must be teaching something new: that *kabbalat hadam*, the receiving of the blood, is also among the prohibited acts.

### Non-Jew Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְתַנָּא קַמָּא, הַאי ״לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְהַאי: סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נִצְטַוּוּ בְּנֵי נֹחַ אֶלָּא עַל מְחוּסַּר אֵבָרִים, לָא שְׁנָא בְּמִזְבֵּחַ דִּידְהוּ וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּמִזְבֵּחַ דִּידַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the first *tanna*,** who holds that one is not liable for collection of the blood, **why do I** need **this** phrase: **Neither** from the hand of a foreigner **shall you offer?** The Gemara answers: **He requires** it **to** teach **this** *halakha*: It may **enter your mind to say** that **since the descendants of Noah are commanded only with regard to** the sacrifice of an animal **lacking limbs** but they are permitted to sacrifice animals with minor blemishes, perhaps **there is no difference** if this is performed **on their altar** outside the Temple **or** if it is done **on our altar** in the Temple. Accordingly, one might claim that a gentile may sacrifice a blemished animal in the Temple in Jerusalem. The verse therefore **teaches us** that they may not sacrifice a blemished animal in such a manner.

The *tanna kamma* disagrees. For the *tanna kamma*, the verse prohibiting acceptance of blemished animals from non-Jews is not superfluous at all, because there is a genuine *hava amina* that non-Jews might be held to a different standard. The *gemara* explains: *ho'il ve-lo nitztavevu Bnei Noach ela mechusrei evarim* — the Noahide laws that govern sacrificial practice for non-Jews prohibit bringing animals that are lacking a limb, but do not restrict non-Jews to the same level of unblemished perfection required in the *Beit HaMikdash*. On their own altars, non-Jews sacrifice under a more lenient standard. One might therefore have thought: *lo shena be-mizbe'ach didu ve-lo shena be-mizbe'ach didan* — just as a non-Jew may bring a blemished (though not limb-deficient) animal on his own altar, perhaps he may bring it in the *Beit HaMikdash* by the same standard. *Ka-mashma lan* — the verse teaches that once an offering is brought in the *Beit HaMikdash*, our standards apply regardless of who is bringing it. The standards of the Temple are determined by the Temple's sanctity, not by the donor's national affiliation.

This principle invites a broader reflection. There is something significant in the idea that the *kedusha* of the *Beit HaMikdash* imposes its own normative standard irrespective of the donor. The *mikdash* is not merely a site of individual worship but a space with its own claims. A blemished animal that would be acceptable to God on a private altar becomes unacceptable when brought into that particular space.

One further consequence of this debate is worth noting: since the *tanna kamma* must use the verse about non-Jews to address the *hava amina* of differential standards, and Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda uses it instead for *kabbalat hadam*, the two are locked in a dispute about what the verse's *chiddush* is. For Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda, the differential standards problem is resolved from the verse about mutilated animals — though that verse's *lo takrivu* is admittedly rhetorical — and the verse about non-Jews is genuinely superfluous, teaching the prohibition on *kabbalat hadam* for Jews. For the *tanna kamma*, the mutilated animals verse teaches nothing new (it is rhetorical), and the non-Jew verse is needed to address the differential standards problem.

### Alternative Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל קַבָּלַת הַדָּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמָעוּךְ וְכָתוּת... לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ — זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם, וּזְרִיקָה נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of the discussion. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** One violates a prohibition **for the collection of the blood as well.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning** of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "That which has its testicles bruised, or crushed,** or torn, or cut, **you shall not offer** unto the Lord" (Leviticus 22:24). **This** verse is referring to **the collection of the blood. And he derives** the prohibition against **sprinkling** the blood **from** the phrase: **"Upon the altar"** (Leviticus 22:22).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי תִּיפּוֹק לְהוּ זְרִיקָה מִן ״עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״! הָכִי נָמֵי, וְאֶלָּא ״לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ דְּמָעוּךְ לְמַאי אֲתָא? מַפְּקִינַן [לֵיהּ] לְבָמַת יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And according to the Rabbis as well,** who hold that one is not liable for collection of the blood, **let them derive** liability for **sprinkling** the blood **from** the phrase **"upon the altar."** The Gemara explains that it is **indeed** so, that they derive it from that phrase. **But for what** purpose **comes** the phrase **"You shall not offer" with regard to bruised** testicles? **We learn** from this phrase the prohibition **of** the sacrifice of a blemished animal even on **a private altar,** where offerings were sacrificed before the construction of the Temple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְבָמַת יָחִיד? אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא ״תַּקְרִיבוּ״ דְּקַבָּלָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? ״מִיָּד בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ״ — זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** how can he then derive the prohibition against collection of the blood of a blemished animal from the phrase "You shall not offer"? After all, **he requires this** phrase **to** render prohibited sacrifice on **a private altar.** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** it **is indeed so. Rather, where does he** find another instance of the term **"shall offer" for** prohibiting **collection** of the blood? From the following verse: **"Neither from the hand of a foreigner shall you offer** the bread of your God of any of these, because their corruption is in them; there is a blemish in them; they shall not be accepted for you" (Leviticus 22:25). **This** verse is referring to **the collection of the blood.**

The *gemara* now presents *lishna achrina* — a second, parallel version of this debate. As noted in connection with Aramaic dialect patterns, these alternative versions appear to stem from an independent source tradition, likely of *Eretz Yisrael* provenance, and were preserved and incorporated together rather than one being selected over the other. The substantive positions are the same.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda states: one transgresses even through *kabbalat hadam*. His source: the verse *u-ma'uach ve-chatut... lo takrivu eleh* — from which he derives *kabbalat hadam*. The prohibition against sprinkling he derives from the verse *al ha-mizbe'ach*. The *rabbanan* use the *al ha-mizbe'ach* verse for sprinkling as well, and must therefore explain what they do with the *lo takrivu* from *u-ma'uach ve-chatut*.

### Private Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּבְנֵי נֹחַ אֵין מְצוִּּוין אֶלָּא עַל מְחוּסַּר אֵבֶר בְּבָמָה דִּלְהוֹן, דִּלְמָא אֲנַן נָמֵי נְקַבֵּיל מִינְּהוֹן? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״מִכׇּל אֵלֶּה״ דְּלָא מְקַבְּלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** maintain that this verse is **necessary** for a different *halakha*, as it **may enter your mind to say** that **since the descendants of Noah are commanded only with regard to** sacrificing an animal **lacking a limb on their** private **altar** but are permitted to sacrifice animals with minor blemishes, **perhaps we** should **accept** blemished offerings **from them** in the Temple **as well.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** by the use of the phrase **"of any of these" that we do not accept** them.

In this version, the *rabbanan* use the *lo takrivu* from the verse about mutilated animals not merely as a rhetorical flourish but to prohibit bringing a blemished animal even on a *bamat yachid*, a private altar, during the periods when such altars were permitted. This is the *chiddush* of that verse according to the *rabbanan*: even outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, even at a time when an Israelite was permitted to maintain a private altar, the prohibition against offering blemished animals applied.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda also needs this verse for *bamat yachid* — and indeed he uses it for that purpose as well. He derives *kabbalat hadam* from the verse about non-Jews, which in his view is superfluous since it would be obvious that blemished animals accepted from non-Jews in the *Beit HaMikdash* are prohibited. The *rabbanan* counter that the verse about non-Jews is not superfluous, since without it one might have reasoned that Noahides operate by their own lenient sacrificial standard (*ein metzuvin ela mechusrei evarim*) even within the *Beit HaMikdash*. The verse is needed to clarify that the *Beit HaMikdash* imposes a single standard. In the end both versions converge on the same fundamental debate, presented through slightly different angles.

## Reish Lakish

### Birth vs. Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שָׁנִינוּ אֶלָּא בְּתָם שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה בַּעַל מוּם, וְעוֹבֵר, דְּאִי בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרָא — דִּיקְלָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* cited earlier stated that one who consecrates a blemished animal violates several prohibitions, but it did not differentiate between animals born with blemishes and those that acquired them during their lives. **Reish Lakish objects to this: Perhaps we learned** the prohibition **only with regard to an unblemished** animal **that became blemished, and** only one who consecrates such an animal **transgresses** the prohibition. **As, if** he consecrated an animal that was **blemished from the outset, it is** akin to consecrating **a mere date palm,** and his presumed intention is to consecrate its value, with the proceeds from its sale being used to purchase an offering. This would be permitted.

With all of the above established — the prohibition extended to private altars, to non-Jews, and to multiple stages of the service — Reish Lakish poses a foundational challenge. *Shema lo shaninu ela be-tam she-nasa ba'al mum* — perhaps the entire prohibition applies only when an animal began its life unblemished and subsequently acquired a blemish. If the animal was a *ba'al mum* from birth, *dikla be-alma hu* — it is like a palm tree; why should its sanctification be treated any differently than the sanctification of an object that can never serve as a *korban*?

The question has genuine force. If an animal has borne a blemish since birth, it is, at the moment of sanctification, entirely outside the category of sacrificial animals. Sanctifying it for the altar would presumably produce only *kedushah* destined for *bedek ha-bayit* (the temple treasury), not *kedushat ha-mizbeach* at all. How then does the prohibition — which seems to presuppose that you are perverting something with genuine sacrificial potential — apply?

### Rashi's View

Rashi explains the distinction psychologically. Technically, one might acknowledge that by the time a person sanctifies the animal it has a blemish regardless of when the blemish appeared. But an animal born without a blemish carries a history of potential — it was once in the category of the fit, and therefore sanctifying it in its blemished state is experienced as a perversion of that potential, a wasted opportunity. By contrast, an animal born with a blemish was never in the category of the sacrificially relevant, and sanctifying it is no more a perversion than sanctifying a car or a palm tree. According to Rashi, then, the question of whether the prohibition applies is about how the act is perceived and evaluated, not about a formally different legal category.

### Tosafot's View

*Tosafos* is troubled by Rashi's approach. If the animal has a blemish at the moment of sanctification, it is hard to see — on Rashi's own terms — why the history of the blemish should matter. *Tosafos* therefore reads Reish Lakish's question as directed not at the prohibition against *sanctifying* a blemished animal, but at the prohibition against *offering* one. The distinction hinges on a well-known principle discussed in Bechorot: when an unblemished animal is sanctified and subsequently acquires a blemish, its *kedushah* retains the character of *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach* — altar sanctity — even though it cannot be brought. The sanctity was, at the moment it took hold, the sanctity of something fit for the altar. But when an animal already bearing a blemish is sanctified, the *kedushah* that takes hold is never *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach* to begin with, since the animal was unfit at the defining moment.

*Tosafos* therefore understands Reish Lakish's challenge thus: the prohibition against offering a *korban* with a blemish applies to an animal that genuinely had *kedushat korban*, which then became unfit. If the animal was sanctified while already blemished, it never had *kedushat korban* at all. Offering it is more akin to bringing *chullin la-azara* — bringing non-consecrated produce into the Temple precincts — than to offering a disqualified *korban*. That may be some form of prohibition, but not the specific prohibition against offering a blemished *korban*.

### Birth Defects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף: ״שָׂרוּעַ וְקָלוּט״ כְּתִיב בַּפָּרָשָׁה, וְהָנֵי בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין מֵעִיקָּרָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said to** Reish Lakish: The blemishes mentioned in the phrase in the verse that states: "Either a bull or a lamb **that has anything too long or too short"** (Leviticus 22:23), **are written in the passage** that prohibits the sacrifice of blemished animals, **and** animals such as **these are blemished from the outset.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Rav Yosef challenges Reish Lakish's proposed limitation directly from the scriptural text. The list of disqualifying conditions enumerated in the relevant passage includes *sarua* and *kalut* — a *sarua* is an animal with disproportionate limbs, and a *kalut* is one with undivided hooves. Both are birth defects, congenital conditions present from the animal's first moment of life. Yet they appear in the very same *parasha* from which we derive the prohibitions against sanctifying and offering blemished animals. This demonstrates that the prohibition explicitly encompasses animals that were *ba'alei mumim* from birth.

### Temurah Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שָׁנִינוּ אֶלָּא בִּתְמוּרָה, דִּתְנַן: חוֹמֶר בַּתְּמוּרָה מִבַּזֶּבַח, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עָלֶיהָ עַל בַּעַל מוּם קָבוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef: **Perhaps** in the case of animals born with blemishes, **we learned** the prohibition **only with regard to substitution, as we learned** in a mishna (16b): There is **greater stringency with regard to a substitute than** there is **with regard to** the initial consecration of **an offering, as,** if one substituted a non-sacred animal **with a permanent blemish** for a consecrated unblemished animal, the blemished animal **is imbued with sanctity.** But initial consecration of an animal with such a blemish is effective only with regard to its value, so perhaps one who does so is exempt from punishment.

Reish Lakish accepts this but shifts his position. *Shema lo shaninu ela be-temurah* — perhaps the prohibition includes congenitally blemished animals, but only in the context of *temurah*. The connection to *temurah* is significant and direct: a *mishna* in Bechorot establishes *chomer be-temurah mi-bezevach* — a stringency of *temurah* over ordinary sanctification — in that *kedushah* fully takes hold on a *temurah* animal even when it bears a permanent blemish. Normally, as discussed above, an animal with a blemish that is sanctified never acquires *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach*. The exception is *temurah*, where the Torah's own mechanism of sanctification transfers full *kedushah* from the original animal to its replacement regardless of whether the replacement is blemished — derived from the verse *ra be-tov o tov be-ra*, indicating that the exchange applies even when the substitute animal is inferior. In a *temurah* scenario involving a congenitally blemished animal, then, the full *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach* is genuinely at stake, and the prohibition would apply for that reason.

Reish Lakish therefore proposes: the cases where a congenitally blemished animal is included in the prohibition are those where real *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach* is conferred — namely, *temurah*. Outside of *temurah*, when you sanctify a congenitally blemished animal, no genuine altar sanctity is created, and perhaps no punishable prohibition applies.

### Rabbi Yannai Vote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי, בַּחֲבוּרָה נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּעַל מוּם לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה שֵׁמוֹת, וְאִי בִּתְמוּרָה נָמֵי — שִׁשָּׁה הָוְויָין, דְּאִיכָּא נָמֵי לָאו דִּתְמוּרָה! אֶלָּא מַאי — בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ? אַמַּאי לָקֵי? דִּיקְלָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Reish Lakish: **Didn't you hear that which Rabbi Yannai says,** that when the Sages sat **in a group,** their opinions **were counted and they concluded: One who consecrates blemished** animals **for** sacrifice **on the altar violates five** separate **categories** of prohibition. **But if** the *baraita* is **also** referring to a case of **substitution, there are six** categories, **as there is** also **the prohibition of** performing **substitution.** Reish Lakish responded: **Rather, what** can you say? Is the *baraita* speaking **of** one who consecrated an animal that is **blemished from the outset? Why** then **is he flogged? It is** akin to one who consecrates **a mere date palm.**

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *lo shemia lach ha de-amar Rabbi Yannai* — have you not heard what Rabbi Yannai taught? *Nimnu ve-gamru* — they convened, deliberated, and voted conclusively: *ha-makdish ba'al mum legabei mizbe'ach over mishum chamisha shemot* — one who sanctifies a blemished animal and offers it transgresses on account of five separate prohibitions. If Reish Lakish's limitation to *temurah* were correct, the count would rise to six, since *temurah* itself would add a sixth prohibition to the five. The fact that only five are enumerated — without *temurah* in the count — demonstrates that the prohibition applies even to animals with congenital blemishes outside the *temurah* context.

### Offensive Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּיקְלָא — לָאו זִילָא מִילְּתָא, מִין עֵצִים הוּא; בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרָא — זִילָא מִילְּתָא, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁבֵיק תְּמִימִים וְאַקְדֵּישׁ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין — מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: There is a difference between the cases. When one consecrates **a date palm, the matter is not disgraceful,** as **it is a type of wood** that one may burn on the altar. Therefore, he is not flogged. By contrast, when one consecrates an animal that is **blemished from the outset, the matter is disgraceful. Since he forsook unblemished** animals **and consecrated blemished** animals, **he is liable** to be flogged.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי בַּזְיָא מִילְּתָא, דְּדֶקֶל לֵיכָּא בְּמִינוֹ — לָא לָקֵי, לְאַפּוֹקֵי בַּעַל מוּם, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בְּמִינוֹ — לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of the previous point. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **Nevertheless, the matter** of consecrating an animal blemished from the outset **is disgraceful. For** in the case of **a date palm,** as **there is no** item **of its type** that can be sacrificed as an offering, **he is not flogged** for consecrating it. This is **to the exclusion of a blemished** animal; **since there are** items **of its type** that can be sacrificed as offerings, i.e., unblemished animals, **he is flogged** for consecrating it.

The *gemara* then addresses Reish Lakish's residual conceptual objection: granted that the prohibition applies, why should it? If the animal never acquires *kedushat ha-mizbe'ach*, in what sense is this categorically different from sanctifying a palm tree?

The answer is: *dikla lav zila milta, min eitzim hu* — a palm tree is not degrading; it is simply wood, categorically irrelevant to sacrifice. But an animal with a blemish is different. *Ve-keivan de-chazi le-tami ve-akdishei le-mumi mechayev* — since unblemished animals of the same species are eligible for sacrifice, the act of sanctifying one that is blemished stands in vivid contrast to what could and should have been done. The alternative is manifest. There is something *zila milta* — something that carries a sense of offense or desecration — about the act, precisely because the right choice was so evidently available.

According to Tosafos, the same logic extends to the act of offering: though the animal technically lacks *kedushat korban*, it was sanctified as if for sacrificial use and is being presented as an *olah*. Even if the *kedushah* never reached the level of *kedushat korban*, the act of bringing it retains a quality of offense that the prohibition captures. This represents a notably non-formalist dimension of the halachic analysis: the prohibition is not grounded solely in the animal's technical legal status but in the experiential and religious character of the act itself.

The *gemara* then presents *lishna achrina* of this resolution, which conveys the same point with slightly different phrasing: *de-dakla be-mina lo laki* — for a palm tree, since no specimen of its kind can serve as a sacrifice, there is nothing offensive about sanctifying it; but *l'afukei ba'al mum, keivan de-ika be-mina laki* — for a blemished animal, since unblemished animals of its species can and should be brought, the choice of a blemished one is inherently offensive and the prohibition applies. As with all instances of *lishna achrina* in this *sugya*, the two versions are substantially equivalent and reflect parallel textual traditions that the editors of the *gemara* chose to preserve in full.

## Nesachim & Treasury

### Rava's Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא דַּאֲמוּר טַעְמָא דְּבַעַל מוּם דְּלָקֵי — מִשּׁוּם דְּבַזְיָא מִילְּתַיְיהוּ, אֲפִילּוּ (למקדיש) [מַקְדֵּישׁ] לֵיהּ לִדְמֵי נְסָכִים — נָמֵי לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Now that it has been said** that **the reason that** one who consecrates **a blemished** animal **is flogged** is **due to** the fact that **the matter is disgraceful,** then **even one who consecrates it to** be sold and its **money** used **for** purchasing **libations** should be **flogged as well,** as consecrating a blemished animal is in and of itself a disgraceful act.

Rava now draws an important and somewhat surprising inference from the preceding conclusion. *Ha-shta de-amarta ta'ama de-ba'al mum de-laki mishum de-bziya milta hi* — now that the rationale for the prohibition has been established as the offensive character of the act rather than any technical quality of the *kedushah* conferred, Rava proposes: *afilu makdish lei li-dmei nesachim nami laki* — even if one sanctifies a blemished animal not for direct sacrificial use but in order to use its monetary proceeds for *nesachim* (wine offerings), one transgresses.

The logic is that once the prohibition is grounded in the offensive and unseemly nature of the act rather than in the formal legal quality of the *kedushah*, it extends to any use of a blemished animal within a sacrificial or temple context. Even if you are not attempting to bring the animal itself as a *korban* but only to benefit from its proceeds for sacrificial ends, there is something inherently degrading about employing a blemished animal in the orbit of the Temple service.

Rashi himself flags the difficulty in this inference. He notes in his commentary: *afilu akdesho li-dmei nesachim, de-hashta leika bizayon kol kach* — if you sanctify it for wine offerings, it is not so obviously a *bizayon*, since one does not bring sheep for wine offerings in the first place, and furthermore you are using only the proceeds rather than the animal itself. And yet the conclusion is that one still transgresses. This means the prohibition extends even where the *bizayon* rationale is, by Rava's own framing, considerably attenuated. The *gemara* appears to accept this despite the tension.

### Nedava Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״נְדָבָה תַּעֲשֶׂה אֹתוֹ״ — זֶה קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא נְדָבָה, נֶדֶר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּלְנֵדֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** A vow offering differs from a gift offering in that if it dies prematurely, one is liable to replace the former but not the latter. The verse states: "Either a bull or a lamb that has anything too long or too short, you may offer it for a gift, and for a vow it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 22:23). The phrase **"You may offer it for a gift" is** referring to a blemished animal **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance. And I have** derived **only** that one may consecrate it in the manner of **a gift; from where** is it derived that one may consecrate it in the manner of **a vow** as well? **The verse states:** "You may offer it for a gift **and for a vow."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וּלְנֵדֶר לֹא יֵרָצֶה״, זֶה קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא נֶדֶר, נְדָבָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נְדָבָה״. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ — בְּהַרְצָאַת גּוּפוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that one can consecrate blemished animals **even** as **offerings** to be sacrificed on **the altar.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And for a vow it shall not be accepted." This** is referring to **offerings** sacrificed on **the altar,** for which acceptance is a relevant term. **And I have** derived **only** that one may not consecrate such an animal as **a vow** offering. **From where** is it derived that one may not do so as **a gift** offering? **The verse states: "A gift**…and for a vow it shall not be accepted." **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** In employing the phrase **"It shall not be accepted," the verse is speaking of** consecration that depends upon **the acceptance of** the animal's **body,** i.e., consecration as an offering. Nevertheless, they may be consecrated for Temple maintenance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ אַקְדְּשֵׁיהּ לִדְמֵי נְסָכִים — נָמֵי לָקֵי, וְרַבִּי סָבַר: בְּהַרְצָאַת גּוּפוֹ — נָמֵי לָקֵי, אֲבָל בִּדְמֵי נְסָכִים — לָא לָקֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is** identical to the opinion of **the first *tanna*.** What is their dispute? The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this, that the first *tanna* holds** that **even** if one **consecrates it to** be sold and its **money** is used **for** purchasing **libations, he is flogged as well; and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **in** a case where there is **acceptance of** the animal's **body,** the one who consecrated it **is indeed flogged, but** if one consecrates it to be sold and its **money** is used **for** purchasing **libations, he is not flogged. Learn from** the *baraita* that the first *tanna* holds that consecration toward libations is equivalent to consecration as an offering, as Rava suggested above.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraisa* that teaches: *nedava ta'aseh oto u-le-neder lo yiratzeh* — the verse states that you may make the blemished animal a *nedava* (a free-will gift) but it shall not be accepted as a *neder* (a vow-offering). In the standard halachic vocabulary of *Chazal*, the distinction between *neder* and *nedava* turns on whether one says *harei alai* (imposing an obligation on oneself) or *harei zo* (directly designating the animal). The verse therefore must be read through a different lens in this context.

The *baraisa* reads the verse as distinguishing between two modes of temple dedication: giving the animal to *bedek ha-bayit* (the temple treasury) versus bringing it as a *korban* on the altar. *Nedava ta'aseh oto* — you may make it a gift, meaning you should dedicate it to the temple treasury. *Le-neder lo yiratzeh* — it shall not be accepted as a sacrifice, meaning *ritzuy* (divine acceptance) only applies when it is an offering on the altar, which is inappropriate for a blemished animal.

The *baraisa* then derives that this applies both to a *nedava* and to a *neder* in the conventional sense: whether one said *harei zo* or *harei alai*, a blemished animal may be dedicated to the temple treasury but may not be designated as a sacrifice. *Talmud lomar u-le-neder* — the verse includes the *neder* case explicitly. Conversely, *talmud lomar nedava* — the mention of *nedava* teaches that even a direct designation (*harei zo*) cannot be used for the altar. The verse is thus read to cover both directions: the blemished animal may and should go to the treasury, and cannot go to the altar regardless of how the dedication was framed.

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi

The *baraisa* records a dispute regarding a related but distinct question. *Tanna kamma* does not restrict *lo yiratzeh* to the animal itself but appears to extend the prohibition to using its proceeds in a sacrificial context — including, following Rava's extension, sanctifying a blemished animal for *nesachim*. *Rebbi*, by contrast, holds: *birtzos gufo ha-kasuv medaber* — the verse is speaking about the body of the animal itself; the prohibition applies only when one attempts to use the blemished animal itself as a sacrifice. *Aval bi-dmei nesachim lo laki* — but if one sanctifies it merely to use the proceeds for wine offerings, one does not transgress.

The *gemara* therefore understands the debate between *tanna kamma* and Rebbi as a dispute over the scope of Rava's extension: whether the prohibition encompasses the use of proceeds for sacrificial purposes, or whether it is limited to bringing the animal itself. Rava had argued for the broader reading based on the *bizayon* rationale; Rebbi's position restricts the prohibition to cases where the degrading act is direct — where the blemished animal is itself being presented as a sacrifice. *Tosafos* observes that in many other contexts, the *gemara* treats the sanctification of animals for their proceeds as unproblematic, which lends support to Rebbi's more restrictive reading.

### Unblemished for Treasury

The word *oto* in the verse (*nedava ta'aseh oto*) is now examined. A limiting word in the Torah typically carries a *chiddush* of exclusion. Here the *baraisa* derives: *oto ta'aseh nedava, ve-ee ata oseh temima nedava le-veit ha-bedek* — this blemished animal you may designate for the temple treasury, but you may not designate an unblemished (*temima*) animal for the treasury. Just as a blemished animal may only go to the treasury and not the altar, an unblemished animal may only go to the altar and not the treasury. Each category has its proper domain, and crossing those domains is itself a violation.

### Positive & Negative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״אוֹתוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״נְדָבָה תַּעֲשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ״ — ״אוֹתוֹ״ תַּעֲשֶׂה נְדָבָה, וְאִי אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה תָּמִים נְדָבָה לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת. מִיכָּן אָמְרוּ: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ תְּמִימִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת — עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the wording of Leviticus 22:23: **But why do I** need the word **"it,"** in the phrase: "You may offer it"? What does this serve to exclude? It is required **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"You may offer it for a gift,"** this teaches that **you may offer this** blemished animal **as a gift** for Temple maintenance, **but you may not offer an unblemished** animal **as a gift for Temple maintenance. From here** the Sages **said: One who consecrates unblemished** animals **for Temple maintenance transgresses a positive mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן אַף בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה לֵּאמֹר״ — לִימֵּד עַל הַפָּרָשָׁה שֶׁהִיא בְּלָאו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived that he has transgressed **a prohibition as well? As it is stated** at the beginning of that passage: **"And the Lord spoke to Moses, saying [*lemor*]"** (Leviticus 22:1). This **teaches that** every mitzva stated in **the passage is** considered **a prohibition.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְבַר קַפָּרָא: מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אֲמַר לֵיהּ דִּכְתִיב ״לֵאמֹר״ — ״לֹא״ נֶאֱמַר בַּדְּבָרִים. בֵּי רַבִּי אָמְרִי: ״לֹא אֱמוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to bar Kappara: From where may** it **be inferred** that every mitzva stated in the passage is considered a prohibition? Bar Kappara **said to him** that this is **as it is written:** As the verse states: **"*Lemor*,"** the term can be read as though it states: **No [***lo***],** an expression of prohibition, **is stated [*ne'emar*] with regard to** the subsequent **matters. In the school of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **they say:** The term should be understood to mean that God said to Moses: **No [*lo*] shall you say [*emor*]** to them these matters.

The *baraisa* continues: *ha-makdish temimim le-veit ha-bedek over be-aseh* — one who sanctifies an unblemished animal for the temple treasury transgresses a positive commandment, namely the positive implication of *nedava ta'aseh oto* — a blemished one you shall dedicate, implying that an unblemished one should not be dedicated this way.

The *baraisa* then asks: from where do we derive not only a positive violation but also a negative prohibition? *She-ne'emar: va-yidaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* — the verse introducing this passage says "God spoke to Moses *lemor*." The *gemara* records that Rebbi himself was asked by Bar Kappara: *mai mashma* — what is the implication of this? The answer: *le-mor* is read as *lav ne'emar* — a *lav* (prohibition) was stated about these matters. In the school of Rebbi they say: *lav emur* — a prohibition was said in connection with this passage.

The *Shita Mekubetzet* addresses the obvious question: does this mean that every *parasha* introduced with *va-yidaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* carries an implied negative prohibition? He explains that the principle applies specifically where a positive obligation already implies something should not be done — where, in other words, we know from a positive formulation that a certain act is wrong — and the introductory phrase *va-yidaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* serves to indicate that this implied prohibition carries the force of a formal *lav*, subject to lashes. Rashi emphasizes this as a very local *drasha*; how precisely its scope is limited to this context rather than applying universally remains a difficult question, though it is treated throughout as operative here.

## Five Transgressions

### Burning Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּעֲלֶה אֵבְרֵי בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אָמַר רָבָא: עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּקְטִירוּ כּוּלּוֹ״ וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּקְטִירוּ מִקְצָתוֹ״. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵין לוֹקִין עַל לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § A *baraita* above (6b) stated that one who sacrifices a blemished animal on the altar transgresses the prohibition "You may not burn all of it," and if he sacrificed part of it, he transgresses the prohibition "You may not burn part of it." With regard to this it **was stated:** In the case of **one who brings up the limbs of blemished** animals **onto the altar, Rava says** that **he violates** both the prohibition **of "You may not burn all of it;" and** the prohibition **of "You may not burn part of it,"** and he receives two sets of lashes. **Abaye says: One is not flogged** twice **for** violating **a general prohibition.** Since one verse serves as the source for both prohibitions, one is not flogged twice for its violation.

Rava raises the question of whether burning part of the limbs of a blemished *olah* and burning all of them constitute one transgression or two. The *gemara* had established five prohibitions for one who sanctifies and offers a blemished animal: sanctifying, slaughtering, sprinkling the blood, burning part, and burning all. The verse *ve-isheh lo takrivu mehem* — do not bring a fire-offering from them — forbids even a partial burning. The question is whether these final two — partial and complete burning — produce two separate sets of lashes.

### Abaye's Principle

Abaye holds: *ein lokin al lav she-be-chlelot* — a generic prohibition (*lav she-be-chlelot*), one that encompasses multiple distinct acts within a single formulation, does not produce multiple sets of lashes. A *lav she-be-chlelot* is a *lav* broad enough to include more than one type of prohibited act, and the halachic consequence is that one does not receive separate *malkot* for each included act. In the present case, the prohibition against offering even part of the limbs (*mehem*) is itself a subset of the prohibition against offering all of them; one is subsumed within the other. Abaye therefore maintains that only one set of lashes applies.

### Five or Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה שֵׁמוֹת, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the *Tosefta* (1:10): **One who consecrates blemished** animals **for** sacrifice **on the altar violates five** separate **categories** of prohibition. The aforementioned *baraita* enumerated these and included both the prohibition "You may not burn all of it" and the prohibition "You may not burn part of it," indicating that one is flogged for transgressing each of these prohibitions. This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Abaye.**

This leads to an apparent contradiction with the enumeration of five transgressions cited from Rabbi Yannai's ruling. If Abaye is correct that the last two prohibitions (burning part, burning all) collapse into one, then only four transgressions incur lashes, not five. The *gemara* tries several resolutions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּגַבְרֵי קָתָנֵי! אִי בְּגַבְרֵי — ״עוֹבֵר״?! ״עוֹבְרִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּחַד גַּבְרָא, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The *baraita* **is teaching** the *halakha* **with regard to** different **men,** one of whom burned an entire animal on the altar, and one of whom burned only part of it. One person cannot be liable for both prohibitions. The Gemara challenges: **If** it is teaching the *halakha* **of** different **men,** why does it say: **Violates,** in the third person singular? It **should have** stated: **Violate,** in the plural. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is referring **to one man, and** this is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Abaye.**

One suggestion is that the five apply to different people: the five prohibitions describe different ways in which different individuals might transgress, and no single person necessarily incurs all five. The *gemara* rejects this: the text says *over* (singular — one person transgresses), implying the count refers to a single individual.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַפֵּיק ״הַקְטָרַת מִקְצָתוֹ״, וְעַיֵּיל ״קַבָּלַת הַדָּם״. קַבָּלַת דָּם — לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִית לֵיהּ, לְתַנָּא קַמָּא לֵית לֵיהּ? קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** From the list of five prohibitions, **remove** that of **burning part of** the offering, **and insert** the **collection of the blood,** which means that there is only one prohibition for burning the offering, and the number of prohibitions still remains at five. The Gemara raises a difficulty: With regard to the **collection of the blood, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, is of** the opinion that one transgresses that prohibition, but **the first *tanna* is not of** that opinion. How, then, can Abaye contradict the first *tanna*? The Gemara concludes: It is **difficult.**

Abaye then proposes a textual revision: remove the prohibition of burning part from the count and substitute *kabbalat hadam* (catching the blood) as the fifth prohibition. But the *gemara* objects: *kabbalat hadam l'Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda it lei, le-tanna kamma leit lei* — catching the blood is only a prohibition according to Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda; the *tanna kamma* does not hold this view, and the count of five belongs to the *tanna kamma*. *Kashya* — this remains a difficulty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: וְהָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, רֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְאַבָּיֵי, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **an alternative version** of the previous point: **But** from the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** it may be inferred that **the first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Abaye** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The *lishna achrina* of this discussion preserves the same essential problem: even if one reads the *sof* of the passage as reflecting the view of Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda (who includes *kabbalat hadam*), the *reisha* reflects the *rabbanan* (who do not include it) — and yet the number five is stated. According to Abaye, the correct count for the *rabbanan* should be four, not five. The *gemara* concludes *tiyuvta de-Abaye tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of Abaye's position. The text establishes that burning part and burning all do produce separate transgressions, contrary to Abaye's principle as applied here.

## Kohanim Temurah

### Mishna Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֹּהֲנִים מְמִירִין בְּשֶׁלָּהֶן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְמִירִין בְּשֶׁלָּהֶן. אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים מְמִירִין לֹא בְּחַטָּאת וְלֹא בְּאָשָׁם וְלֹא בִּבְכוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מְמִירִין בִּבְכוֹר? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, וּבְכוֹר מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן. מָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם אֵין מְמִירִין בּוֹ, אַף בְּכוֹר אֵין מְמִירִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The priests substitute for their** own offerings **and Israelites substitute for their** own offerings. **The priests substitute neither for a sin offering, nor for a guilt offering, nor for a firstborn** offering that they received from an Israelite, as those animals are not their property, and one does not substitute an animal that is not his. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: For what** reason can priests **not substitute for a firstborn** offering that they received from an Israelite? Does it not belong to them? **Rabbi Akiva said to him: A sin offering and a guilt offering** are **a gift to the priest, and the firstborn** offering is likewise **a gift to the priest. Just as** in the cases of **a sin offering and a guilt offering,** priests that receive one of them from an Israelite **cannot substitute for it, so too** with regard to **a firstborn** offering, priests that receive it from an Israelite **cannot substitute for it.**

The *gemara* now introduces the opening *mishna* of the next section. *Ha-kohanim memirim be-shelahem ve-Yisrael memirim be-shelahem* — kohanim may perform *temurah* on their own animals and a Yisrael may perform *temurah* on his. The *mishna* then enumerates a set of important qualifications.

### Chatat & Asham

*Ein ha-kohanim memirim lo be-chatat ve-lo be-asham* — kohanim may not perform *temurah* on a *chatat* or *asham*, even though a kohen who brings such an offering on behalf of a Yisrael is entitled to eat its meat. One might have thought that since the kohen has a proprietary interest in the meat, he has sufficient standing to perform *temurah* on the animal. The *mishna* teaches otherwise: however significant the kohen's share in the *korban*, he is at all times offering the *korban* of the Yisrael, not his own. The Yisrael remains the *ba'al ha-korban*, and only a *ba'al ha-korban* may perform *temurah* on his own sacrifice. The kohen cannot perform *temurah* on what belongs to another.

### Bechor Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מְמִירִין בִּבְכוֹר? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ יִהְיֶה קּוֹדֶשׁ״, הֵיכָן קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עָלָיו? בְּבֵית הַבְּעָלִים, אַף תְּמוּרָה בְּבֵית הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: But isn't it already stated: "Then both it and its substitute shall be sacred"** (Leviticus 27:10), which juxtaposes the consecration of the consecrated animal with that of its non-sacred substitute? **Where is** the consecrated animal **imbued with sanctity?** It is **in the house of the owner. So too,** the **substitute** animal is consecrated **in the house of the owner.** Therefore, the priest cannot substitute for the firstborn that he received because he is not the owner that initially consecrated it.

The *mishna* adds a more striking ruling: *ve-lo ba-bechor* — a kohen may not perform *temurah* on a *bechor*, even though the *bechor* is given to him as an outright gift. This is a genuine *chiddush*. In the case of the *chatat* and *asham*, one can understand why the kohen is not the owner: he never held title to the animal. But a *bechor* is given to the kohen entirely — he brings it, he benefits from it, and there is no sense in which the Yisrael retains any ongoing claim. Yet the *mishna* holds that even here the kohen cannot perform *temurah*. The reason: the *kedushah* of the *bechor* took hold *be-veit be'alim* — in the house of the original owner, at the moment of birth, while the animal still belonged to the Yisrael. The location and moment of *kedushat korban* define the ownership context of the sacrifice for all subsequent purposes, including *temurah*.

### Akiva's Response

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri objects to this logic. *Mipnei mah ein memirim ba-bechor?* — why should a kohen not perform *temurah* on a *bechor*? He distinguishes: I understand the rule regarding *chatat* and *asham*, because *she-ein zochim bahen be-chayeihen* — the kohen has no legal entitlement to the animal while it is still alive. The *korban* is entirely the Yisrael's throughout the sacrificial process, and the kohen's benefit comes only after the offering. But *tomar ba-bechor she-zochim bo be-chayav* — in the case of the *bechor*, the kohen acquires the living animal itself; he receives it while it is alive and he is the one who brings it to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Surely that living animal is now *his* sacrifice?

Rabbi Akiva responds with a verse: *ve-lo kvar ne'emar, ve-haya hu u-temurato yihiyeh kodesh* — the Torah states that "it and its *temurah* shall be holy." The principle embedded in this verse is that *temurah* takes place wherever the original *kedushah* took hold. *Heichan kedushah chala alav?* — where did the *kedushah* of the *bechor* first apply? *Be-veit be'alim* — in the owner's house, at birth. Therefore *af temurah be-veit be'alim* — the act of *temurah* also belongs to the context of the owner's house, that is, to the original Yisrael owner. The source of the *kedushah* determines who may perform *temurah*, and since the *bechor*'s *kedushah* is rooted in the Yisrael's ownership, only the Yisrael can be the subject of a *temurah* act.
