# Shekalim 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meat Identity & Rav

### Intro: Money & Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נְחַת לְתַמָּן. חַמְתּוֹן מֵקִילוֹן וַחֲמַר עֲלֵיהוֹן. חַד בַּר נַשׁ אֲזַל בָּעֵי מְשִׁיגָּה אֵסְקוֹפְתֵּיהּ בְּגַו נַהֲרָא וְאִינְשִׁתָהּ וַאֲזַל לֵיהּ. חֲזַר בָּעֵי מִיסְבִינֵהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב. אָסִוּר לָךְ. דָּנָא אֲמַר. הַהִיא שְׁטַף נַהֲרָא וְאַיְיתִי חוּרִי דִנְבֵילָה תוֹחְתּוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav returned there and saw them taking things lightly and was restrictive with them. A man went to wash a piece of meat in the river. He forgot it and left. Then he went and wanted to sell it. Rav told him, it is forbidden to you, for I am saying the other one was carried away by the river and it brought another piece of carcass meat in its place.

The daf opens by picking up from the bottom of 19b, where the *mishna* deals with moneys that are found and the question of determining their identity. The *gemara* transitions from this into a parallel concern: finding pieces of meat and trying to determine which *korban* they came from — or whether the meat might be *nevela* rather than legitimately slaughtered. This latter issue, the identity and permissibility of found meat, becomes the primary focus as the *gemara* opens on 20a.

### Rav in Bavel

The *gemara* introduces the subject with a brief but significant biographical note: *Rav nachas l'saman* — Rav descended there, to Bavel. The phrase *ki asa Rav leBavel* appears in the *gemara* in Gittin as well, referring to Rav's famous transition from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel, where he was foundational in establishing the great *yeshivot*. Upon arriving, *chamzu meikilin* — he found that the Babylonian community was being lenient regarding meat, presumably meat found in the hands of non-Jews or meat that had not been continuously observed. And so, *v'chamir aleihon* — he was *machmir* upon them.

This is identified with the larger Talmudic category of *basar she-nisalem min ha-ayin* — meat that has passed out of one's sight. The *gemara*'s concern in that discussion is, on its face, quite far-fetched: the worry that if you look away from your meat for a moment, someone might come and substitute a different piece in its place. But the context here is important. Rav saw a community that was being broadly lenient — perhaps too quick to assume that found meat, or meat from an unobserved source, was permissible without proper scrutiny into whether it might have come from a *treif* animal. In response to that ambient leniency, he adopted an exaggerated stringency. It is worth noting, then, that some of what follows may represent *chumra* in the sense not merely of choosing the stricter of two standard *din* positions, but of an additional stringency exceeding normal halachic expectation.

### Bird Snatching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד בַּר נַשׁ הֲוָה מְהַלֵּךְ בְּשׁוּקָא טָעִין קוּפָּד. אֲתַא דַיְיתָא וַחֲטַפְתֵּיהּ מִינֵיהּ וּטְלַקְתֵּיהּ. חֲזַר בָּעֵי מִיסְבִינֵהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב. אֲסִיר לָךְ. דָּנָא אֲמַר. בָּשָׂר דִּנְבֵילָה הֲוָות טְעִינָה וּטְלַקְתֵּיהּ וְנָסְבָת הַהוּא אוֹחְרָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** A man was carrying meat while walking on the market. There came a vulture, seized it from his hand, and dropped it off. He came back and wanted to sell it. Rav told him, it is forbidden to you, for I am saying that it was carrying carcass meat, dropped it and took the other instead.

A second story follows in the same vein. A man, *chad bar nash*, was walking in the marketplace carrying some meat, when a bird swooped down — *chatafah minei* — and snatched it from him. He ran after it, watched it drop a piece of meat, and retrieved what he assumed to be his own. Rav again declared it forbidden: *assur lach*. The reasoning: *dilma basar d'neveila hava teina* — perhaps the bird was already carrying a piece of *nevela* when it grabbed his meat, dropped the *nevela*, and flew off with his kosher piece. What he retrieved, then, would not be his own meat at all.

Again, this is plainly fantastical as a realistic concern. The only way to make sense of it is as a deliberately overstated stringency, a response to the atmosphere of excessive leniency Rav encountered upon arriving in Bavel. Unless one watched the bird uninterruptedly every single second, one cannot be certain of the exchange — and under the rubric of *basar she-nisalem min ha-ayin*, even a brief lapse in observation opens the door, however theoretically, to this kind of substitution.

## Found Objects

### Wine Flasks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּינַאי שְׁטַף זִיקִין. אֲתַא עוֹבְדָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אֶלְעָזָר וְאָמַר. יַחְכְּמוֹן שְׁפַיָיא קִיטְרֵיהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Ginai swept away wineskins. The case came before Rebbi Isaac ben Eleazar who said, may the vintners recognize their knots.

The *gemara* now turns from meat to wine. The river known as Ginai flooded and washed away some flasks of wine — *shatif zikin*. The case came before Rabbi Yitzchak and Rabbi Elazar, and they ruled: *yachkmon shapaya kitreihon* — let the wine pourers or wine sellers identify their knots. That is, if the owners had made distinctive knots on their flasks, and they can recognize those identifying marks, they may reclaim their property.

This case represents a meaningful contrast to Rav. Whereas Rav was *machmir* even when the meat had seemingly been in sight the entire time, here at least some recovery is permitted — provided there is a *siman*, an identifying mark. The implication is that without a *siman*, a similar concern would presumably apply to wine as well; but with one, the owner may reclaim it.

### Cheese Wheels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוּקְנִיקָה אִישְׁתַּכַּח בִּכְנִישְׁתָּא דְבוּלֵי. אֲתַא עוֹבְדָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה. אָמַר. יַחְכְּמוֹן סִיקִיָירִיָּא עֲבִידְתֵּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** A sausage was found in the meeting room of the town council. The case came before Rebbi Jeremiah who said, may the butchers recognize their handiwork.

A parallel case: certain jugs — *nuknika* — were found in the *beit knesset* of *Buli*. The word *Buli* refers, as noted, to an administrative governing body, a Greek term — the *boule* — related, in fact, to the word that forms part of *pruzbul*. This particular *beit knesset* was presumably associated with a wealthy or civic community. The case came before Rabbi Yirmiyah, who ruled: *yachkmon sakuraya avidatehon* — let the craftsmen, those who made the identifying marks, recognize their handiwork. If they can identify their own marks on the vessels, they may assume the jugs are theirs and reclaim them.

The pattern is consistent: identification via *siman* is the key that unlocks the right to reclaim an otherwise found object.

### Roasted Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּדִי צְלִי אִישְׁתַּכַּח בְּאִיסְרָטָא דְגוּפְתָּא. וְהִתִּירוּהוּ מִשֵּׁם שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים. מִשֵּׁם מְצִיאָה וּמִשֵּׁם רוֹב מְהַלְּכֵי דְרָכִים. מִשֵּׁם מְצִיאָה. דְּתַנֵּי. הַמַּצִּיל מִיַּד הָאֲרִי מִיַּד הַגַּיִיס מִשּׁוּנַת הַיָּם וּמִשּׁוּנַת הַנָּהָר וּמֵאיסְרַטְיָא גְדוֹלָה וּמִפַּלַּטְיָא גְדוֹלָה הֲרֵי אֵילּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיַיאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן. מִשֵּׁם רוֹב מְהַלְּכֵי דְרָכִים. מִשֵׁם שְׁחִיטַת הַגּוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** A roast kid goat was found in the main street of Gufta. They permitted it because of two things, as a find and because of the majority of passers-by. As a find, as it was stated: If one saves from before a lion, from before an army, from a surge of the sea, or the surge of a river, from a wide road, or a wide plaza, it belongs to him, since the owners give up on them. Because of the majority of passers-by, because of a Gentile's slaughter.

A markedly different case now: a roasted goat — *gdi tzli* — was found in the main street of Gufta, a *stratya*, which is a major thoroughfare (a Greek term familiar from the Bavli, from which words like "street" and "estrada" derive). *Ve'hitirua* — they declared it permissible. This stands in striking contrast to Rav's extreme stringencies.

The permissibility rested on two distinct grounds — not two reasons for a single leniency, but two separate concerns that were each resolved. First, *mishum metziah*: the question of whether one is even permitted to keep a found object, or whether one must seek out its owner. Second, *mishum rov mahallchei drachim*: the question of whether the meat itself was permissible, resolved by the fact that the majority of those who pass through that thoroughfare are Jewish.

On the first point, a *baraita* is cited: one who saves an object from a lion, from marauding troops, from an ocean wave, from a river current, or who finds something on a major street or plaza — *harei elu shelo*, it belongs to the finder. The reason: *mipnei shehabe'alim mityashin mehen* — the owners have surely despaired of recovering it. If you drop your wallet in Times Square, you do not expect to see it again. In all such cases, *ye'ush* is immediate and automatic, and the finder acquires the object.

On the second point, *rov mahallchei drachim* — the majority of passersby — resolves the concern of *shechitat nochri*. One may assume the meat was slaughtered by a Jew and is therefore kosher.

This is quite striking. One might have imagined additional concerns: perhaps the reason someone dropped a roasted goat in the street is itself a signal that something was wrong with it; perhaps even if the *shechita* was done by a Jewish *shochet*, the animal might have been a *treifah*. But the *gemara*, drawing on the logic of the *mishna* and the earlier discussions, is willing to follow normal probabilities and declare the meat permissible — a position entirely at odds with the stringencies of Rav just discussed.

### Ye'ush Principle

The *gemara* elaborates on the *mishum metziah* principle. *Ye'ush* — the owner's loss of hope — operates as an automatic mechanism in certain defined circumstances. In contexts where recovery is objectively impossible or virtually so (an object about to be devoured by a lion, washed away by a river, or lost in the middle of a major public thoroughfare), the law does not require the owner to consciously articulate despair. The hopelessness is presumed, and the finder accordingly acquires the object.

It is worth noting the terminology here. *Rov mahallchei drachim* is not itself the category being resolved; rather, it is the *reason* to be lenient about the underlying category, which is the concern about *nevela* and non-Jewish meat. *Mishum metziah*, meanwhile, is itself the category being addressed. The phrasing is slightly unusual but precise.

### Goat Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִישְּׁתַּכַּח מִן דְּבֵית רִבִּי. עִיגוּל דְּגוּבְנָא אִישְׁתַּכַּח בְּפוּנְדָּקָא דְּלֵוִי. וְהִתִּירוּהוּ מִשֵּׁם שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים. מִשֵּׁם מְצִיאָה וּמִשֵּׁם רוֹב מְהַלְּכֵי דְרָכִים. מִשֵּׁם מְצִיאָה. דְּתַנֵּי. הַמַּצִּיל מִיַּד הַגַּיִיס מִיַּד הָאֲרִי מִשּׁוּנַת הַיָּם מִשּׁוּנַת הַנָּהָר מֵאִיסְטְרַטְיָה גְדוֹלָה וּמִפַּלַּטְיָא גְדוֹלָה הֲרֵי אֵילּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיַיאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן. מִשּׁוּם רוֹב מְהַלְּכֵי דְרָכִים. מִשּׁוּם גְּבִינַת גּוֹי. וְאִישְּׁתַּכַּח מִן דְּרִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֵּירִבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It turned out that it was from the House of Rebbi. A wheel of cheese was found in the hostelry of Levi. They permitted it because of two things, as a find, and because of the majority of passers-by. As a find, as it was stated: If one saves from before an army, from before a lion, from a surge of the sea, or the surge of a river, from a wide road, or a wide plaza, it belongs to him, since the owners give up on them. Because of the majority of passers-by, because of Gentile's cheese. It turned out that it was from Rebbi Eleazar ben Rebbi Yose.

The story of the roasted goat concludes with a remarkable postscript: *ve'ishtachach min d'vei Rabbi* — it was subsequently discovered that the meat had in fact originated from the household of Rabbi. The factual outcome thus confirmed the halachic reasoning. Not only did the *gemara* bracket Rav's stringencies and arrive at a more lenient position, but the empirical reality bore out that the lenient ruling was correct.

Similarly, a wheel of cheese — *igoola d'goofna*, literally a pressed circular wheel — was found in the inn of Levi, an establishment apparently doing brisk business with high foot traffic. Here too, permissibility was declared on two grounds: *mishum metziah*, since the inn's throughput made *ye'ush* immediate, and *mishum rov mahallchei drachim*, resolving the concern of *gvinat ovdei kochavim*, since the majority of patrons were Jewish. The same *baraita* about saving objects from lions and rivers and major thoroughfares is cited again in support. And again, the story concludes with a validating postscript: *ve'ishtachach mid'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosei* — the cheese turned out to have come from the household of Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yosei.

## Theory vs Practice

### Mani's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי מָנָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי יוֹסֵי. וַאֲנָן חָמְיָין רַבָּנִן מַכְרִזִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Mana said before Rebbi Yose, but do we not see that the rabbis publicize?

Rabbi Mani addressed Rabbi Yose with a challenging observation: *ve'anan chamian rabanan machrizin* — all of this is fine in theory, but in practice, we actually see that *rabanan* are more stringent, not about the *kashrut* question, but about *returning* the object. That is, even in cases where *ye'ush* has technically occurred and the finder is halachically entitled to keep the object, people nonetheless announce their find and attempt to return it to the owner.

This is a fascinating inversion of the expected trajectory. If you had predicted that the *gemara* would end with a *chumra*, you would have guessed it was reverting to Rav's position on *kashrut*. Instead, the *chumra* appears in the domain of *metziah* and *hashavas aveidah* — returning lost objects. Note too that if the objects had *simanim*, as implied by the cheese and goat stories, *ye'ush* alone does not fully settle the owner's claim — the *siman* remained a live avenue for identification and return, and apparently people acted on it.

### Yona's Wallet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ. [אִין אַתְּ] הֲוִויתָה מַשְׁכַּח לָא נָסְבָת. רִבִּי יוֹנָה אָבוּךְ לֹא אָמַר כֵּן אֶלָּא אָמַר. הַלְּוַאי כַּד נַשְׁכַּח נַשְׁכַּח מִן פִיּוֹסְרוֹס וּלְגֵיו. אֲפִילוּ כֵן אַשְׁכַּח וְלָא נְסַב.
>
> **Translation:** He told him, if you had been there, you would not have taken it. Your father, Rebbi Jonah, did not say so, but he said, if only I would find it inside from the circular road. Even so, he found and did not take.

Rabbi Yose responded to Rabbi Mani with a personal anecdote. He said: you, Rabbi Mani, I know you personally would not keep such a wallet even if you found it in Times Square. But *Rabbi Yona abuch* — your father Rabbi Yona — did not say that. What did he say? *Halevai kad nishkach min piyusa ulegav* — he said: I only wish that if I find a wallet, I find it in the middle of a major thoroughfare — meaning, I hope I find it in a place that allows me to keep it. In other words, Rabbi Yona expressed the quite natural sentiment of hoping to be in circumstances where the *halacha* permits him to keep what he finds, rather than being obligated to seek out the owner.

### Practice Reality

Rabbi Yose's statement continues: *ve'afilu kein, ashkach velo nasiv* — even so, when he actually found something, he did not keep it for himself. In practice, Rabbi Yona's behavior was more stringent than his stated theory.

There is something genuinely arresting about this passage. In the Bavli, the characteristic direction of such anecdotes is the opposite: "my father was greater than I — he would wait twenty-four hours between meat and milk; I, *nebach*, wait only until the next meal." The son is less rigorous than the father. Here, we have the son being more *frum* than the father — at least in terms of the stated theoretical position. And the *gemara* adds that even the father, in practice, returned what he found.

More strikingly still, consider the asymmetry in where stringency is applied. Most people's instinct, if they were going to be more *machmir* in one domain, would be the *kashrut* dimension rather than the *hashavas aveidah* dimension. And if one were going to identify a gap between theory and practice, the common expectation would be that the *yetzer hara* pushes a person toward leniency in practice relative to their theoretical *psak*. Here it is the exact reverse: the *psak* is lenient, but real people with real objects and real owners in front of them act with greater stringency in practice. The criterion for being more *frum* turns out to be returning the lost object, not holding the meat to a higher standard.

This discussion also effectively brackets Rav's stringencies. The Yerushalmi makes no attempt to rehabilitate Rav's position on *basar she-nisalem min ha-ayin*. Even the note that Rav was *machmir* because he found an unusually lenient community in Bavel — meaning the Eretz Yisrael tradition was different — does not salvage his rulings here. In the Bavli as well, of course, Rav's move to Bavel is what gave his stringencies their foothold; but the present daf makes clear that at least in the Palestinian tradition, his concerns were not the governing standard.

## Animals Jerusalem

### Mishna Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת מִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְעַד מִגְדַּל עֵדֶר וּכְמִידָּתָהּ לְכָל רוּחַ זְכָרִים עוֹלוֹת. נְקֵבוֹת זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים. רִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר הָרָאוּי לִפְסָחִים לִפְסָחִים קוֹדֶם לָרֶגֶל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: If it was found in the countryside26Palestine outside of Jerusalem and its surroundings., limbs are carrion27Since the butcher did not care to cut it into slices, one must assume that it was notkosher and thrown away for the dogs., cuts are permitted28If there are no sources of non-kosher meat in the neighborhood.. On holidays, when meat is abundant, even limbs are permitted29Since for big family meals one may buy whole limbs and cut them after cooking.

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which returns to the broader theme of identifying found objects and their halachic status. *Beheimah shenimtzeit miyerushalayim ve'ad migdal eder* — an animal found anywhere between Jerusalem and Migdal Eder. Migdal Eder is a nearby village, and *uchmidasah lechol ruach* — that radius applies in all directions around Jerusalem. Found animals in this zone are assumed to have originated in Jerusalem.

The extension of the radius is necessary because, unlike pieces of meat, live animals move on their own. An animal might have started in Jerusalem, been designated as a *korban*, and wandered out. We therefore apply the same presumption about Jerusalemite animals even at some distance from the city walls.

Given this presumption, the *mishna* rules: *zecharim olos* — male animals found in this zone are to be treated as *olos*, since males are eligible for *olas*. *Unekeivot zivchei shelamim* — females, which cannot be brought as *olos*, are presumed to be *shelamim*.

The logic of the *shelamim* presumption connects to the earlier discussion: the prevailing use of *ma'aser sheni* funds in Jerusalem generated a constant stream of *shelamim* offerings, making *shelamim* the statistically dominant *korban* in the city. The assumption that found females are *shelamim* follows naturally from this demographic reality.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a qualification: *hara'uy lapesachim kodem haregel shloshim yom* — if the animal is fit for a *pesach* offering (a male sheep or goat under one year old), and we are within thirty days before Pesach — from after Purim onward — then we presume it is a *pesach* offering. At that season, the most likely purpose for such an animal is the *korban pesach*.

### Status Problem

This raises the obvious question: if the prior *mishna* established that the dominant *korban* in Jerusalem is *shelamim* — so much so that even found meat is presumed to be *shelamim* — why do we not assume that male animals are also *shelamim*? After all, a *shelamim* may be male or female alike.

The *gemara* will engage with this difficulty in the discussions that follow. It is worth noting that the question has no fully satisfying answer at this stage. Perhaps the ruling is simply a *chumra* — treating the animal as an *olah* because that is the stricter of the two possibilities for a found male animal. But then one wonders whether it is consistent with the earlier tendency to follow likelihoods, which there led to *leniency* rather than to *stringency*. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension cleanly.

## Takanot Found Animal (II)

### Nesachim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אֶת מוֹצְאֶיהָ עַד שֶׁהוּא מֵבִיא נְסָכֶיהָ. חָזְרוּ לִהְיוֹת מַנִּיחִין אוֹתָהּ וּבוֹרְחִין. הִתְקִינוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁיְּהוּ נְסָכֶיהָ בָּאִין מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר:
>
> **Translation:** *Barishonah* — originally, the rule was *hayu memashkenin et motzah ad shehu mevi nesacheha* — they would take collateral from the person who found and turned in the wandering animal until he brought the *nesachim* — the libation offerings that accompany an *olah*. The finder, it seems, was expected to bear the cost of the *nesachim*, even though the animal was not his *korban* to begin with. The result was predictable: *chazru lihyot manichin osah uvorchin* — people began abandoning the animals and fleeing in the opposite direction whenever they saw a stray sanctified animal. No one wanted the financial and logistical burden of supplying *nesachim* for someone else's *korban*. In response, *hitkinu beit din shenesacheha ba'in mishal tzibbur* — the *beit din* enacted that the *nesachim* for such found animals would be supplied from communal funds. This enactment is one of the items that belongs to a broader list of seven.

### Seven Takanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים הִתְקִינוּ בֵּית דִּין וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן. גּוֹי שֶׁשִּׁילַּח עוֹלָתוֹ מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְשִׁילַּח עִמָּהּ נְסָכֶיהָ קְרֵיבִין מִשֶׁלּוֹ וְאִם לָאו קְרֵיבִין משֶּׁל צִיבּוּר. וְכֵן גֵּר שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ זְבָחִים אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ נְסָכִים קְרֵיבִין מִשֶׁלּוֹ וְאִם לָאו קְרֵיבִין מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon introduces these seven enactments of *beit din* — *shivah devarim hitkinu beit din*. The case of the found animal is one of them. Among the others: A non-Jew who sent a *korban* from overseas — *nochri she-sholach olato mimedinat ha-yam* — and included funds for *nesachim* will have the *nesachim* brought from his own money; if he did not send funds, the *nesachim* are brought from communal funds. We know the tragic story of what happened when this principle was violated at the end of the Second Temple period, but the operative rule is that a non-Jew's *korban* is not turned away for lack of *nesachim* funds when the donor is simply inaccessible. Similarly, *ve-chen ger she-met* — a convert who died without heirs. Because a convert who dies without children is considered to have no legal heirs, his estate is technically *hefker*. If he left behind animals that had been sanctified as *korbanos*, and his estate includes funds for *nesachim*, those funds are drawn upon; if not, communal funds are used. The case raises some interesting questions about how a lien on a seized estate would operate in practice, but the operative *takana* is clear: if there are no funds available, *nesachim* come from *mishal tzibbur*.

In both cases — the non-Jew overseas and the deceased convert — the common denominator is that the owner of the *korban* is not accessible to be asked for payment.

## Takanot Found Animal (II)

### Tenai Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַיי בֵּית דִּין הוּא עַל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת שֶׁתְּהֵא מִנְחָתוֹ קְרֵיבָה מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר. רִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשֶּׁל יוֹרְשִׁין. וּשְׁלֵימָה הָיְתָה קְרֵבָה: עַל הַמֶּלַח וְעַל הָעֵצִים שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים נֵיאוֹתִין בָּהֶן וְעַל הַפָּרָה שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מוֹעֲלִין בְּאֶפְרָהּ. עַל הַקִּינִּין הַפְּסוּלוֹת שֶׁיְּהוּ בָאוֹת מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר רִבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר הַמְסַפֵּק אֶת הַקִּינִּין הוא מְסַפֵּק אֶת הַפְּסוּלוֹת:
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* then introduces a distinct conceptual category: *u-tenai beit din hu* — and this is a *tenai beit din*. It is a stipulation through which *beit din* defines how *kedusha* itself operates — adjusting the rules by which sanctified property is governed. The first application: *al kohen gadol she-met she-tehe minchato kereva mishal tzibbur* — when the *kohen gadol* dies, his daily *mincha* offering (brought half in the morning and half in the afternoon) continues to be brought, funded from communal property. Rabbi Yehuda adds that it comes *mishal yorshim* — from the heirs of the deceased *kohen gadol*. The implicit assumption in Rabbi Yehuda's view is that the dead *kohen gadol* retains some continuing obligation even after his death, such that his heirs bear the cost. *U-shelemah hayetah kereva* — Rabbi Yehuda adds further that when it is brought under these circumstances, it is brought as a whole unit, not divided into two halves as the *kohen gadol* himself would have divided it. The subsequent *takanot* more clearly exemplify the *tenai beit din* mechanism in its technical sense. *Al ha-melach ve-al ha-etzim she-ha-kohanim ne'otim ba-hem* — a stipulation that the salt and wood in the *beit hamikdash*, which were designated for use in the *korbanos* and for salting meat per the verse *al kol korbancha takriv melach*, the *kohanim* may use for roasting and salting the *korban* meat that they themselves eat. This would ordinarily constitute *me'ilah* — misappropriation of consecrated property — since the salt and wood are *hekdesh*. The *tenai beit din* operates here not merely to redirect funds but to define the scope of the *kedusha* itself: by stipulating from the outset that these items may be used for this purpose, the *beit din* prevents the *me'ilah* category from applying. *Ve'al haparah shelo ye'u mo'alin be'afarah* — and a stipulation that there be no *me'ilah* with the ashes of the *parah adumah* after it has been burned. The question of why *me'ilah* would apply at all is itself worth examining — the fact that the ashes are used for a *mitzvah* does not by itself negate *me'ilah*, since *mitzvos lav lehanos nitnu*. *Ve'al hakinim hapesulot* — regarding invalid bird offerings. If birds purchased from the *shofer* fund turned out to be *pasul*, the first answer is that replacement birds are supplied from communal funds. Rabbi Yosi, however, holds that the supplier himself must replace *pasul* birds — *hamaspek et hakinim hu maspek et hapesulot* — as a condition of his supply contract. This is, once more, an application of *tenai beit din* in the sense of stipulating conditions that govern how the relationship between the consecrated objects and the parties involved will operate.

## Gemara Challenge

### Hoshaya Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָה רַבָּא אָמַר. רבא בִּדְמֵיהֶן שָׁנוּ. אָמַר לֵיהּ רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אוֹמְרִים לוֹ לְאָדָם. צֵא וּמְעוֹל בַקֳּדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Oshaya Rabba — *Rebbi Oshaya the great* — proposes: *lavo bidmeihem shanu* — the *mishna* is not speaking of bringing the animals themselves; it is speaking of bringing their monetary value. Rebbi Joḥanan objected: *omrin lo l'adam tze u-meal b'kodshim?* — do we tell a person to go and commit *me'ilah* with consecrated property?

The *gemara* now confronts the *mishna* about found animals directly. Rabbi Oshaya Rabba — *Rebbi Oshaya the great* — proposes: *lavo bidmeihem shanu* — the *mishna* is not speaking of bringing the animals themselves; it is speaking of bringing their monetary value. The procedure he envisions is one of transfer: the finder takes the found animal, transfers its *kedusha* to money, and with that money purchases the appropriate *korbanos*.

The reason for this maneuver is to cover multiple bases. We are not certain whether this animal is an *olah* or a *shelamim*. If we bring the animal itself as an *olah*, we may be wrong. But if we transfer the *kedusha* conditionally to money — if it is an *olah*, this money is sanctified as an *olah*; if it is a *shelamim*, this other money is sanctified as a *shelamim* — we can then bring both an *olah* and a *shelamim* with their respective funds, one representing the original animal and one as a new *korban*, and the bases are covered. The problem is that the *mishna* does not describe this procedure; it speaks of the animal itself.

Rabbi Yochanan objected: *omrin lo l'adam tze u-meal b'kodshim?* — do we tell a person to go and commit *me'ilah* with consecrated property? The *meforshim* note that Rabbi Yochanan does not literally mean the *me'ilah* prohibition in the technical sense here, but he is invoking the principle that one may not redeem a *korban* that has no *mum* — no blemish — transferring its *kedusha* to money. That is the operative prohibition: *podeh tmimim*, redeeming an unblemished animal, is not permitted under normal circumstances. How can Rabbi Oshaya Rabba suggest doing exactly this?

### Majority Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הִילְּכוּ בָהֶן אַחַר הָרוֹב. אִם רוֹב זְכָרִים. עוֹלוֹת. אִם רוֹב נְקֵיבוֹת. זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים. וְאֵין הַשְּׁלָמִים בָּאִים מִן הַזְּכָרִים וּמִן הַנְּקֵיבוֹת. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. מוֹצִיאָן לַחוּלִין וְחוֹזֵר וְעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתַן עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yochanan therefore proposes the simpler answer: *manach acharei harov* — you follow the majority. But this raises the persistent difficulty: why are males *olos* rather than *shelamim*? Rabbi Yochanan appears to address this by suggesting that if you found a flock of animals, you assess the makeup: *im rov zecharim olah, im rov nekeivot zivchei shelamim* — if the majority are male, the animals are *olos*; if the majority are female, they are *shelamim*. The problem with this reading, as the *gemara* immediately notes, is that *shelamim* can come from both males and females — *ein shelamim ba'in min hazecharim u-min hanekeivot* — so even a majority of males does not resolve whether they are *olos* or *shelamim*. And what happens if you find only one animal? The answer remains unclear, and the *meforshim* genuinely struggle here. The *gemara* then suggests: *motzi'an l'chullin v'chozer v'oseh osan olos* — take them out to *chullin* and then make them *olos*.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore proposes the simpler answer: *manach acharei harov* — you follow the majority. There is no need to cover bases or transfer *kedusha* to money. The animal itself is brought; you apply the rule of *rov* and treat it accordingly. Males are brought as *olos* because we follow the majority.

But this raises the persistent difficulty: why are males *olos* rather than *shelamim*? Rabbi Yochanan appears to address this by suggesting that if you found a flock of animals, you assess the makeup: *im rov zecharim olah, im rov nekeivot zivchei shelamim* — if the majority are male, the animals are *olos*; if the majority are female, they are *shelamim*. The *gemara* then suggests: *motzi'an l'chullin v'chozer v'oseh osan olos* — take them out to *chullin* and then make them *olos*. This would involve a conditional transfer: if the animal is an *olah*, transfer its *kedusha* to money; if it is a *shelamim*, transfer it to other money; then use that money to acquire an *olah* and a *shelamim*, and then retransfer the *kedusha* from the *olah* money back to the animal, restoring it as an *olah*. But as the *meforshim* note, this procedure is deeply problematic — we have never heard of redeeming an unblemished animal with the intent of re-sanctifying it, and the *mishna* nowhere describes anything like this. Some *meforshim* suggest this is not an answer at all, but rather a rhetorical continuation of the question: *do you want him* to perform this elaborate and questionable procedure? The discussion is genuinely unresolved.

### Zeira Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי זְעוּרָה. כְּמַה דְאַתְּ אָמַר תַּמָּן. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא עַל הַמּוֹתָרוֹת שֶׁיִּקָּרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת. כֵּן אַתְּ אָמַר אוֹף הָכָא. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא עַל הָאוֹבְדוֹת שֶׁיִּקָּרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה לְרִבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אָחָא. אֵין זֶה מֵזִיד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. מִכֵּיוָן שֶׁהוּא תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין אֵין זֶה מֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira offers a more coherent answer. *K'ma d'at amar taman* — the same logic applied in the previously discussed case applies here. We have been learning for the past two days that leftover money from a *chatat* or *asham* goes to the *nedava* fund — the *olah* fund. Whether someone dies after separating his *shekel*, or whether various ambiguities arise about sanctified funds, the operative rule is: *yiplu l'nedava*, it goes into the *olah* fund. This has been established as *tenai beit din* for those cases. Rabbi Zeira argues: *tenai beit din hu al avudot sheyikrevu olos* — *beit din* stipulates the same for lost animals. When you find a lost animal and do not know whether it is an *olah* or a *shelamim*, *beit din*'s standing stipulation determines that it becomes an *olah*. Just as the doubt about leftover money is resolved by the *nedava* rule, the doubt about a found animal's status is resolved by the same framework. Anyone who sanctifies an animal implicitly accepts all the conditions that *beit din* has stipulated, whether or not he is explicitly aware of them — a greatly expanded application of the principle of *kol hamikadesh ada'ata derabanan mikadesh*. Rabbi Yosi raised a pointed question to Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha: *ein zeh meizid?* — is this not *meizid*, willful misapplication? In all the other cases of *tenai beit din* involving leftover funds, the money was *passively* redirected. Here, one is *actively* and *knowingly* taking an animal that might be a *shelamim* and treating it as an *olah*. Does that not cross the line into intentional misapplication of *kedusha*? The response: *mideka tnai beit din ein zeh meizid* — since it is all part of the conditions of *beit din*, there is nothing improper about it. The stipulation is not a circumvention; it is the foundational framework within which all acts of *hekdesh* operate. The person who sanctified the animal did so within a system that has these rules built in from the start. Acting in accordance with those rules, even if one knows the animal might have been a *shelamim*, is not *meizid* — it is simply following the system as *beit din* defined it.

## Leftover Funds

### Shekel Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יָסָא. עַד דַּאֲנָא תַמָּן שְׁמָעִית קָל רַב יְהוּדָה שְׁאַל שְׁמוּאֵל. הִפְרִישׁ שִׁקְלוֹ [וָמֵת]. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yasa said, when I still was there, I heard the voice of Rav Jehudah asking Samuel, if somebody had set his *sheqel* apart and died? He told him, they shall fall to gift.

The *gemara* records that Rabbi Yosi heard, when he was still in Bavel, the sound of Rabbi Yehuda asking Shmuel: *hifrish shekalo u-meis* — someone separated his *shekel* but died before giving it; what is the *halacha*? Shmuel replied: *yiplu l'nedava* — it falls into the *nedava* fund. This is consistent with everything we have seen: when funds have been set aside for a communal purpose and circumstances prevent their use, they go to the *olah* fund.

### Yochanan Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתַר עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה שֶׁלּוֹ. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. יוֹלִיכֵם לְיַם הַמֶּלַח. רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר. יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The excess of his tenth of an ephah, Rebbi Joḥanan said, one shall bring them to the Dead Sea. Rebbi Eleazar said, it shall fall to gift.

A distinct question: *umosar asiris haeifa shelo* — what if the *kohen gadol* sanctified money to be used for his daily *asiris ha-eifa* and there are leftover funds? Here the rulings diverge in an interesting way. Rabbi Yochanan says: *yolichem l'Yam HaMelach* — those funds are cast into the Dead Sea, not placed into the *nedava* fund. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and says: *yiplu l'nedava* — they follow the same rule as everything else, and go into the *nedava* fund.

It is not immediately clear why Rabbi Yochanan treats this case differently. The *nedava* fund has absorbed leftovers from *chatat*, *asham*, the *shekel* of one who died, and found animals — but not, apparently, the surplus funds of the *kohen gadol*'s personal daily *mincha*. One possible distinction is that the *nedava* fund is traditionally associated with communal or quasi-communal *korbanos*, or with *korbanos* of the *chatat* and *asham* type, whereas the *kohen gadol*'s *asiris ha-eifa* is neither of those. But the *gemara* does not spell out the reason, and the ruling stands: Rabbi Yochanan says *Yam HaMelach*, Rabbi Eliezer says *nedava*.

## Daily Minha

### Sanctify Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה שֶׁלְכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. [רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר.] חוֹצֶה אוֹתָהּ וְאַחַרְכָּךְ מְקַדְּשָׁהּ. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר. מְקַדְּשָׁהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חוֹצֶה אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The tenth of an *ephah* from the High Priest71*Lev.* 6:12–16.. [Rebbi Joḥanan said,]72Correct addition by the corrector. he splits it and then dedicates73The requirement is a tenth of an *ephah* daily, half in the morning and half in the evening. Since the volume of the flour is prescribed, it must be measured. R. Joḥanan must hold that the measuring vessel is not a Temple vessel which automatically dedicates anything poured into it; otherwise there would be no possibility of dedicating the two parts separately.. Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish said, he dedicates and afterwards splits it.

The *gemara* now turns to a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish about the mechanics of the *kohen gadol*'s daily *asiris ha-eifa*. The Torah states that it is brought *machatzisah baboker u-machatzisah ba'erev* — half in the morning, half in the afternoon.

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *chotzah osah ve'achar kach mekadeshah* — first the whole *isaron* of flour is prepared in a non-*kli shares*, then it is divided into two halves, and only then is each half placed in a *kli shares* and thereby sanctified. Sanctification follows the division.

Reish Lakish holds the reverse: *mekadeshah ve'achar kach chotzah osah* — the entire *isaron* is first placed in a *kli shares* and sanctified as a whole, and only then is it divided into two halves. Sanctification precedes the division.

### Half Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתא פְלִיגָא עַל רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. מַקְרִיב מֶחֱצָה וּמֶחֱצָה אָבֵד. פָּתַר לָהּ. שֶׁכֵּן אֲפִילוּ מָעוֹת יֵלְכוּ לְיַם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** A Mishnah74Mishnah *Menaḥot*4:5. disagrees with Rebbi Joḥanan: "He brings half of it; the other half is lost.75If a High Priest died after he had brought his morning offering and another was appointed before the evening, the second part of the first's offering cannot be used anymore, but has to be kept as is until the time of its sacrificial use has elapsed and then disposed of by burning outside the sacred compound as disqualified sacrifice. The second one must bring a tenth of an *ephah*, but only the second half may be used." He explains it, because even the money has to be brought to the Dead Sea76Since R. Joḥanan holds that even money set aside for the Tenth but not used has to be destroyed (Note 70), he will agree that the second half cannot be used even if not dedicated..

The *gemara* challenges Rabbi Yochanan from a *baraita*: *makriv mechtzo u-mechtzo avad* — one half was brought in the morning and the other half was then lost or became *tamei*. In such a case, an entirely new whole *isaron* is made, divided in half, and the fresh half is brought in the afternoon; the remaining half of the new preparation goes to waste.

The question for Rabbi Yochanan is: if the half that was left over was not yet in a *kli shares* — since according to his view the second half would not have been sanctified yet — why should it go to waste? Simply do not sanctify it and there is no problem. The fact that it must be wasted implies it was already sanctified, which supports Reish Lakish.

Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *she-ken afilu ma'os yelchu l'Yam HaMelach* — even the money that was set aside for the *kohen gadol*'s *asiris ha-eifa* goes to the *Yam HaMelach* if it cannot be used, as Rabbi Yochanan himself ruled just now. This implies that even without being placed in a *kli shares*, the funds already bear a *kedusha* — what one might call *kedushas peh*, the *kedusha* of verbal designation. Once something is earmarked for the *asiris ha-eifa*, even without the formal sanctification of a *kli shares*, it acquires a status that requires disposal. The leftover half of the *isaron* is similarly governed by its designation, and must be wasted even according to Rabbi Yochanan's view. The contradiction from the *baraita* is thus resolved.

The question of why the *kohen gadol*'s leftover money cannot simply be used for the following day — which would seem the obvious solution — is addressed: if the *kohen gadol* died, there is no tomorrow; and if the *mincha* itself has been in a *kli shares* overnight, it is *pasul* because of *lina*.

### Two Priests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתָא פְלִיגָא עַל רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. נִמְצְאוּ שְׁנֵי חֲצָיִין קְרֵיבִין וּשְׁנֵי חֲצָיִין אֲבֵידִין. וְתַנֵּי עֲלָהּ. מֶחֱצָה זֶה רִאשׁוֹן מֶחֱצָה שֵׁינִי תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפַה.
>
> **Translation:** A Mishnah74Mishnah *Menaḥot*4:5. disagrees with Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: "It turns out that two halves are brought and two halves are lost." And it was stated on this77Tosephta *Menaḥot*7:10.: "If the first one's, the second half should lose its shape and be brought to the place of burning.78This text is difficult for R. Joḥanan (reading of B) and R. Simeon ben Laqish. For R. Joḥanan, why should the flour become unusable if it was not yet dedicated? The answer is given in Note 76. For R. Simeon ben Laqish, the entire flour is dedicated as sacrifice from the start; if it becomes disqualified in any way, it should be burned immediately and not need waiting for the next day.

A further *baraita*: *nimtze'u shnei chatza'in krevim u-shnei chatza'in avudin* — there is a scenario in which two halves are brought and two halves must be discarded. This occurs when one *kohen gadol* dies after bringing his morning half, and a new *kohen gadol* is appointed for the afternoon. The new appointee brings a whole, divides it, and brings his afternoon half. The morning half leftover from the first *kohen gadol* and the second half from the new *kohen gadol*'s preparation — both must be disposed of.

*V'sani ala* — a *baraita* teaches: *mechtza rishon u-mechtza sheni te'uber tzurasan v'yeitzu l'veis ha-sreifa* — both halves, the first priest's and the second's, must have their form allowed to pass — that is, one waits until they are *pasul b'lina* from being left overnight — and then they are burned. This is a stronger form of disposal than simply casting into the *Yam HaMelach*; it implies full *kedusha* requiring burning. And in particular, the second half of the new *kohen gadol*'s preparation — why would that necessarily have been placed in a *kli shares*? According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that sanctification comes after division, there would have been no need to sanctify the second half at all.

### Isaron Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַר לָהּ כְּרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. דְּרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר. עִשָּׁרוֹן מְקַדֵּשׁ. כְּשֶׁהַכֹּהֵן מִתְקָּרֵב תְּחִילָּה לָעֲבוֹדָה מֵבִיא עֲשִׁירִית שֶׁלּוֹ וְעוֹבְדָהּ בְיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** He explains it following Rebbi Ismael, for Rebbi Ismael said, the *issaron*79Since the dedication is automatic, not by his conscious act, it does not have the status of a directly dedicated animal and needs waiting for the next day. dedicates. "If a Cohen comes first to serve, he brings his Tenth and officiates himself.80*Sifra Ṣaw Parashah*3(3). He has to bring the tenth of an *ephah* once in his lifetime, when he officiates in the Temple the first time. *Lev.* 6:13."

The *gemara* resolves this by citing Rabbi Yishmael: *isaron mekadesh keshe-ha-kohen mitkarev techila* — the *isaron* is sanctified when the *kohen* first approaches to perform the service. The *Korban Ha-Eda* explains: when the *kohen* measures out the *isaron* of flour, he does so in a *kli shares*. Therefore, even according to Rabbi Yochanan, the flour is actually placed in a *kli shares* and thereby sanctified before it is divided. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling about dividing before sanctifying described only the sequence of the formal act of dividing relative to the formal act of *mekadesh*; but in practice, *l'chatchila*, the *isaron* is always measured in a *kli shares* from the very beginning of the preparation.

This effectively undermines the practical difference between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Since *l'chatchila* one always starts with a *kli shares*, the result is the same in practice. Rabbi Yochanan was speaking *b'diavad* — describing what happens if someone waited to place it in a *kli shares* until after dividing it; but normative practice would always sanctify the *isaron* first. The *baraita* about burning the leftover halves therefore poses no real challenge to Rabbi Yochanan, since it describes the standard procedure in which the *kli shares* is used from the beginning.

## Priest Initiation

### First Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וְאָחָד כֹּהֵן הֵדְיוֹט שֶׁעָבְדוּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאוּ עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה שֶׁלָּהֶם עֲבוֹדָתָם כְּשֵׁירָה. רִבִּי מָנָא בָעֵי מֵימַר. בּוֹ בַיּוֹם נִתְקָרֵב כַּתְּחִילָּה לָעֲבוֹדָה. בּוֹ בַיּוֹם נִתְמַנֶּה לִהְיוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. מֵבִיא שְׁתַּיִם. אַחַת לְחִינּוּכוֹ וְאָחַת לְחוֹבַת הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The officiating of both a High Priest and a common priest who officiated before they brought their tenth of an *ephah* is valid. Rebbi Mana wanted to say, if on that very day81A Cohen was appointed as High Priest on the day he first came to officiate in the Temple. Confirmed Babli *Menaḥot*78a. he was appointed High Priest he brings two, one for his initiation and one as daily obligation.

The *gemara* now introduces a rule about priestly initiation. *Keshe-kohen mitkarev techila la'avoda meivi asiris ha-eifa shelo* — when a *kohen* first enters the *avodah*, he brings his own *asiris ha-eifa* and himself performs the offering. This applies to any *kohen* beginning his service for the first time — not only the *kohen gadol* who brings it daily, but any *kohen* on his inaugural day.

*B'diavad*, the question of what happens if a *kohen* performed the *avodah* without having first brought his *asiris ha-eifa*: *echad kohen gadol ve'echad kohen hedyot she'avdu ad shelo heviu asiris ha-eifa shalahen* — whether a *kohen gadol* or an ordinary *kohen*, if they performed the *avodah* before bringing their initiatory *asiris ha-eifa* — *avodatan keshera b'diavad*, the service is valid after the fact, though the *Gra* has the reading *pesulah*, that it is invalid. The practical difference between these two readings is significant.

Rabbi Mani then poses a case: *meimar bo bayom shenitkarev techila la'avoda bo bayom nimna lihyot kohen gadol* — suppose a man's very first day of *avodah* coincides with the day he is appointed *kohen gadol*. He has two obligations: the *asiris ha-eifa* as an initiating *kohen* beginning his service, and the regular daily *asiris ha-eifa* as *kohen gadol*. Does he bring two separate *asiris ha-eifas* — *meivi shtayim, achat l'chinucho ve'achat l'chovat hayom* — one for his initiation and one for his daily obligation? Or can a single offering serve both purposes?

The case is framed as *ba'i* — a genuine inquiry — though it is formally stated as a ruling. The question of whether the initiatory *asiris ha-eifa* and the daily obligatory one are two conceptually distinct offerings, requiring two physical performances, or whether one can discharge both, is left somewhat open by the text.

### Baking Timing

Finally, the *gemara* addresses the word *tufini* in the verse *tufini minchat pitim takriv* — the *mincha* must be baked. *Besha'at hava'a tufini* — at the time of bringing it, it must be freshly baked; *ve'ein beshachris tufini* — it may not be baked the night before, and there is no *tufini* in the morning sense that it is prepared before morning. Reading the verse carefully, since it says *takriv* alongside *tufini*, the baking must coincide with the time of offering; it cannot have been done beforehand.

This creates an apparent tension with a *baraita* cited from tractate *Middot* that describes an official announcement made before daybreak: *he'emidu osei chavitin la'asot chavitin* — those who make the *chavitin* (the technical term for the *kohen gadol*'s *asiris ha-eifa*) rise and begin their work. This sounds like the baking begins well before dawn, in darkness, contradicting the rule that the baking must be contemporaneous with the *hakrava*.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba resolves this: *la'asot chamim lerivucha lirachva* — the pre-dawn announcement was not about beginning the actual baking. It was about heating the water and preparing the fire in advance, not the baking itself. The baking itself waited until the morning. The *gemara* continues with this discussion in the next session.
