# Shabbat 154 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Carrying Home Erev Shabb…

### Wallet Outside City

We pick up at the bottom of 153, with the continuing discussion of what a person should do when Shabbat overtakes him outside the city with his wallet. The *gemara* had concluded that one should simply run home without stopping, relying on the principle that continuous motion prevents one from inadvertently setting the object down in a prohibited domain. The *gemara* then asks: what happens when one finally arrives at one's house? The answer is that one sets it down *kil'achar yad* — in an irregular, backhanded manner — upon entering.

### Kil'achar Yad

The reason the *gemara* did not raise this question in the case of placing the object on an animal is that when the animal was already in motion, there was never a proper *akira* — a halakhically significant lifting — to begin with. Consequently, there is less concern about the *hanachah*, the act of setting down.

### Pachos Pachos Me'arba Am…

Tosafot, however, presses further: the same problem should arise in the case of walking *pachos pachos me'arba amos* — fewer than four cubits at a time — through the public domain. At some point the person must transition from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*, and the question of how to set the object down without a prohibited *hanachah* is equally acute. Tosafot notes that one could stop in a *karmelis* before entering one's house, which would reduce the concern to a rabbinic one. Regardless, Tosafot concludes that the same answer applies: one sets it down *kil'achar yad*.

### Chutz L'techum & Sakana

A further relevant question concerns the *techum* boundary. The *Shulchan Aruch* and the Rama rule on this with an interesting distinction. On one hand, the authorities permitted a range of measures to bring the wallet home — placing it on an animal, and so forth — yet they did not permit placing it on a friend who is in motion, because that would constitute *shnayim she'asu'u*, the paradigm of two people performing an act jointly, recalling the first *mishnah* about the poor man and the householder. So not every rabbinic leniency was granted.

Regarding the *techum* specifically: the Rama rules that if remaining outside the city poses a danger — *sakana* — one may violate *techum* concerns to return to safety. But absent such danger, one must stop at the boundary of one's *techum*, even if that boundary falls well short of the city. If, for example, one's *techum* ends two thousand feet before the city gate and one finds oneself five thousand feet outside the city, one travels two thousand feet and stops. No further movement is permitted unless *sakana* overrides the restriction.

One might ask whether the rabbinic prohibitions apply at all during *bein hashmashot*, the twilight period. There is certainly a position, associated with Rebbi, that rabbinic decrees do not apply during *bein hashmashot*, at least *bemakom mitzvah*, in the context of a mitzvah. That consideration would be relevant here. As for the status of *techum* itself: although Rabbi Akiva holds it to be *d'oraisa*, the accepted ruling is that it is *derabanan*. Yet it is not entirely clear why the authorities drew the line precisely where they did, permitting some rabbinic violations in this case but not others.

### Placing on Minor vs. Ani…

One final point before transitioning to today's *daf* proper concerns the case of placing the object on a minor. There is a debate between the Rambam and Tosafot about whether, in that case, one must also wait for the minor to be in motion before placing the object — analogous to the requirement in the donkey case. This question is closely tied to a fundamental distinction. The donkey case involves a *d'oraisa* concern: causing one's animal to perform labor on Shabbat. But if the child in question is not one's own, there is no *d'oraisa* concern at all, since the obligation of *shvisah* — ensuring rest — applies specifically to members of one's own household. One is commanded regarding one's own children and animals, not another's.

This leads to a conceptually important point. The Rambam apparently holds that the requirement to wait for the animal to be moving before placing the load applies only in the donkey case, precisely because one bears a *d'oraisa* responsibility for one's own animal. By contrast, the minor case — especially if the minor belongs to someone else — involves no such obligation. A question was raised: is one not, in such a case, causing another person to transgress the *shvisah* of their own child? The response is that the obligation of *shvisah* applies specifically when the minor is performing labor for the sake of the person commanded — meaning when the minor's activity is directed toward one's own purposes. When the minor is acting for someone else, that framing does not apply. In any event, the Ramban understands the animal case to be more stringent than the minor case, and that serves as the natural bridge into the *daf*'s opening *gemara*.

## Mechamer Achar Behemah

### Full Melachah: Chatas &…

The *gemara* begins with the statement of Rami bar Chama: *hamechamer achar behemah beShabbos beshogeg chayav chatas, meizid chayav sekilah* — one who drives his animal on Shabbat carrying a load is liable to bring a *chatas* if he did so inadvertently, and is liable to *sekilah* — death by stoning — if he did so deliberately. According to this position, *mechamer*, the act of leading one's laden animal, constitutes a full *melachah* in every sense: it is as though the person performed the act himself.

Before proceeding, it is worth noting that the *poskim* — as already developed by the *rishonim* — identify two distinct prohibitions relating to one's animal on Shabbat. The first is the positive commandment of *shvisas behemto*: *lemaan yanuach shorecha vachamorecha* — ensuring that one's ox and donkey rest. This applies even when one is not personally participating in the animal's activity. If one rents one's animal to a non-Jew who works it on Shabbat, one transgresses *shvisas behemto*, even without any direct involvement — but only with respect to one's own animal. This is a *mitzvas aseh*, a positive obligation, rooted in membership in one's household.

The second is the negative prohibition: *lo taaseh kol melacha atah uvincha uvitecha vashorecha vachamorecha* — do not perform any labor, you and your son and your daughter and your ox and your donkey. This verse is understood by one opinion to constitute a genuine prohibition of *chilul Shabbos* at the highest level — not merely ensuring the animal rests, but one actually performing the *melachah* together with the animal. When one directly drives the animal — *atah vechamorecha*, you together with your animal — this may be classified as performing the *melachah* oneself.

The *gemara* presents Rava's reasoning for this first position: *deamar kra lo taaseh kol melacha atah uvehemtecha* — the same verse in which the prohibition of *melacha* carries the penalty of *sekilah* says "you and your animal." *Behemtecha dumya dideich* — your animal is treated like you. Just as one who violates Shabbat through one's own action is liable to a *chatas* for inadvertence and *sekilah* for deliberateness, so too here. Rashi adds that *atah uvehemtecha* specifically connects to the *melachah* of leading — as if one had performed the *melachah* entirely oneself.

An important qualification: if one's animal sets out on its own initiative — say, if one placed food before it and it walked toward the food carrying a load, without one's direct participation in the act of carrying — that would not necessarily constitute *mechamer*, and certainly would not carry the status of *sekilah*. *Mechamer* requires direct, active participation. By way of illustration: if one's cat shreds toilet paper on Shabbat entirely on its own, not only is one not required to stop it, one may even use the shredded paper.

Regarding slaves: the *gemara* assumes that the *mitzvas aseh* of *shvisah* — ensuring rest — applies to minors and slaves. It is specifically with respect to animals that the *gemara* entertains the possibility of a genuine *melachah*, because one is using the animal as one performs the *melachah*, and that may be classified as one doing the *melachah* through it, at the level of *lo taaseh*.

The Ramban notes, however, that there are cases where one would indeed be liable even according to positions that do not accept full *mechamer* liability. If, for instance, one's cow is plowing and one is physically holding the plow and guiding it as the cow pulls, the Ramban says that constitutes performing the *melachah* oneself — because one is directly manipulating the implement of the *melachah* even as the animal provides the motive force.

### Rava's Responses

The *gemara* now records that Rava offers two responses (*shtei teshuvos ladavar*) to the first position — meaning he challenges the claim that *mechamer* is fully equivalent to performing the *melachah* oneself.

His first response: *chada dichsiv, Torah achas yihyeh lachem la'oseh bishgagah, vehanefesh asher taaseh beyad ramah* — there is one law for one who acts in error, and the one who acts presumptuously. This verse concerns *avodah zarah* but is applied to the entire Torah: *huksha kol haTorah kullah l'avodah zarah*. The whole Torah is analogized to *avodah zarah* for purposes of the *chatas* obligation. Just as *avodah zarah* requires a physical act performed by one's own body, so too a *chatas* for any Torah violation requires a physical, bodily act. Since in the case of *mechamer* it is the animal performing the act, not the person, there is no liability for a *chatas*. This is Rava's first answer.

His second response draws from a *mishnah*: *hamechalel es haShabbos bedavar shechayavin al shigagaso chatas ve'al zedonos sekilah* — one who violates Shabbat with something for which the inadvertent act incurs a *chatas* and the deliberate act incurs *sekilah*. The *gemara* infers: *michlal de'ika midi delo chayav al shigagaso chatas velo al zedonos sekilah* — there must be a category of Shabbat violation for which one does not bring a *chatas* and does not incur *sekilah*. What is that? The prohibition of *mechamer* — leading one's animal. So one can infer that *mechamer* is a form of *chilul Shabbos* but not of the same category that carries *sekilah*.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps the other categories referenced are *techum* according to Rabbi Akiva — who holds *techum* to be *d'oraisa* but not punishable by death — and *havarah* (kindling fire) according to Rabbi Yosi — who holds that the Torah's singling out of the prohibition of kindling indicates it carries lashes but not *sekilah*. If those are the categories intended by the *mishnah*, then *mechamer* might still be subject to *sekilah*. So the inference is inconclusive.

### No Chatas, Yes Sekilah

Rav Zevid teaches the discussion differently. In his version, Rami bar Chama says: *hamechamer achar behemah beShabbos beshogeg eino chayav chatas* — one who leads his animal on Shabbat inadvertently does not bring a *chatas* — *uvemeizid chayav sekilah* — but deliberately he is liable to *sekilah*. This is a striking intermediate position. The absence of a *chatas* for the inadvertent act is conceded — because, as Rava's first argument established, the *chatas* requires a bodily act which is absent here. But the deliberate act, Rami bar Chama argues, still warrants *sekilah*, because there are cases where one incurs *sekilah* without a corresponding *chatas* for inadvertence — as with one who blasphemes (*megadef*), for instance.

### Rava's Logic Challenge

Rava challenges this intermediate position. He cites the *mishnah*: *hamechalel es haShabbos bedavar shechayavin al shigagaso chatas chayavin al zedonos sekilah*. Rava argues that this implies the converse — *ha ein chayavin al shigagaso chatas, ein chayavin al zedonos sekilah*: if there is no *chatas* for the inadvertent act, there is no *sekilah* for the deliberate act. Rami bar Chama's position therefore cannot stand.

At this point the *gemara* itself intervenes with an important logical observation. The *mishnah*'s formulation — "if *chatas*, then *sekilah*" — is a conditional statement, not a biconditional. In elementary logical terms: if P then Q does not entail that if not-P then not-Q. "If P then Q" licenses only the contrapositive: if not-Q then not-P. The *mishnah* tells us that having a *chatas* guarantees *sekilah*. It does not tell us that lacking a *chatas* rules out *sekilah*.

The *gemara* makes this point explicitly: *mi kasni ha ein chayavin?* — did the *mishnah* say "if there is no *chatas* there is no *sekilah*"? No. What it said is: *davar shechayavin al shigagaso chatas chayavin al zedonos sekilah* — if there is a *chatas*, there is definitely *sekilah*. But there may well be something for which there is no *chatas* and yet there is *sekilah*. And what might that be? *Mechamer* — exactly. So the *mishnah* does not exclude *sekilah* for *mechamer*. Rami bar Chama's position, though it arrived at the same result, is not actually refuted by the *mishnah*.

This is thus a technically coherent position: *mechamer* incurs no *chatas* because no bodily act was performed, yet it may still incur *sekilah* because the prohibition is severe enough to warrant capital punishment even without the *chatas*-level requirements.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Exempt

The *gemara* then transmits the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *mechamer achar behemto beShabbos patur miklum* — one who leads his animal on Shabbat is completely exempt from punishment. This does not necessarily mean there is no biblical violation, but there is no punishment of any kind.

*Beshogeg lo mechayev chatas* — inadvertently, one does not bring a *chatas*, as established by the *huksha kol haTorah kullah l'avodah zarah* principle: a *chatas* requires a bodily act, and here one performed none.

*Uvemeizid nami lo mechayev misah* — deliberately, one does not incur capital punishment either. The *gemara* invokes the same *mishnah*: *hamechalel es haShabbos bedavar shechayavin al zedonos kares ve'al shegagaso chatas*. Rabbi Yochanan draws the inference that if there is no *chatas*, there is no *sekilah* — and we already noted the logical difficulty with this inference, yet the *gemara* here adopts it in Rabbi Yochanan's name as the operative conclusion.

*Belav nami lo mechayev* — one does not even receive lashes. Why? *D'havei lei lav shenitan le'azharat misas beis din* — the prohibition in question, *lo taaseh kol melacha*, is the very verse that serves as the basis for the capital penalty for deliberate *chilul Shabbos*. There is a general principle that a *lav* given to warn against a capital offense — *lav shenitan le'azharat misas beis din* — cannot simultaneously serve as the basis for lashes. Once the verse is "used" to ground the more severe punishment, it cannot be applied to impose the lesser one on someone not subject to the greater. Therefore, no *malkus* either.

The *gemara* continues on 154b: even according to the view that one does receive lashes for such a *lav*, here one would not. The verse could have read simply *lo taaseh kol melacha uvehemtecha* — do not perform any labor, and your animal. But it says *atah* — you — separately. *Hu nihu demichayev* — it is *you* who are the subject of liability for *melacha*. *Beheimah lo mechayev* — for the animal's actions alone, not only is there no *sekilah*, there is not even *malkus*. The explicit mention of *atah* signals that the animal is categorically distinct from the person for purposes of *melachah* liability.

### Atah vs. Behemtecha

This concluding derivation from the verse encapsulates the *gemara*'s final resolution. Two competing understandings of *atah uvehemtecha* were on the table throughout the discussion: one view read it as equating the animal's labor to the person's own act, grounding full *sekilah* liability; Rabbi Yochanan's view reads it as differentiating them — *atah* and *behemtecha* are listed together precisely because they are not the same, and the liability structure that applies to the person does not apply when it is the animal performing the act.

We *pasken* like Rabbi Yochanan: *mechamer* carries no punishment — no *chatas*, no *sekilah*, no *malkus*. It remains a *lav* in the view of most authorities, though a *lav shenitan le'azharat misas beis din*, and therefore not actionable. The Rambam, as the Ramban understands him, goes further and holds that it is not even a *lav* — only an *asei*, the positive obligation of *shvisas behemto*. That remains a question in the Rambam.

To summarize the two conceptual tracks that run through the *sugya*: there is a *mitzvas asei* — *lemaan yanuach shorecha vachamorecha* — to ensure one's animal rests, which applies even when one is entirely passive, and which applies only to one's own animals. And there is the *lo taaseh*, which applies when one is directly performing the *melachah* together with the animal. The whole discussion of *mechamer* concerns the *lo taaseh* dimension. When one is so directly involved — as the Ramban's example of holding the plow while the cow pulls — the Ramban holds one would be liable. But in the standard case of simply driving the animal, Rabbi Yochanan rules: *patur miklum*.

## Unloading at Courtyard

### Karni D'Umna & Pillows

Having resolved the theoretical question of *mechamer*, the *gemara* now turns to a practical scenario: one has arrived at the outer courtyard of the city — *higia l'chatzer hachitzonah* — a protected area where the leniencies granted for the journey cease to apply. Now one must unload the animal without handling *muktzah* items. Rav Huna addresses the case where one's animal is carrying glass vessels: *mevi kesasos u'maniach tachteiha, matir hachavalim, vehasakin noflin* — one brings pillows, places them beneath the vessels, unties the ropes, and the sacks fall without the glass breaking.

The *gemara* asks: but we have already established in the *mishnah* that *notlin kelim hanitlin* — one may remove non-*muktzah* items directly. Glass vessels are not *muktzah*. Why the need for pillows? The *gemara* answers: *ki ka'amar Rav Huna bekarni de'umna* — Rav Huna was speaking specifically about the horns of a bloodletter. These are glass instruments used in a medical procedure and, as Rashi explains, they are repulsive and also qualify as *klei umanut* — tools of a craft — and are therefore *muktzah*. They cannot be directly handled.

### Mevatel Kli Me'heichano

The *gemara* immediately raises a further difficulty: *v'ha kamvatel kli meheichano* — is one not invalidating a vessel from its use? By placing the pillow beneath the *muktzah* instrument, the pillow becomes unusable — one cannot move it without causing the glass to fall and break. And the Rabbis did not permit an act that renders a vessel functionally unusable on Shabbat. One might suggest that one can simply lift the pillow and the *muktzah* item along with it, as is often permitted with *basis* situations. But here, moving the pillow causes the glass to fall and shatter. Therefore, the pillow truly is rendered unusable.

## Unloading at Courtyard…

### Small Sacks Solution

The *gemara* resolves this: *bishlifi zutri* — with small sacks. Rashi explains that the vessels are light enough that one can remove the pillow from beneath them by sliding it out gradually — moving the vessel onto another pillow, and then another, until it is close enough to the ground that the final removal causes no breakage. The pillow is thus not truly rendered permanently unusable; it can be retrieved.

### Tevel & Glass Chunks

The *gemara* then raises a *beraisa*: if one's animal was carrying *tevel* — untithed grain, which is *muktzah* — and *ashashiyos*, which the *gemara* will clarify refers to chunks of raw glass rather than finished glass lanterns, one unties the ropes and lets the sacks fall, *af al pi shemishtabrin* — even though they will break. The implication is that one makes no accommodation and allows even breakable items to fall.

### Glass Chunks: Usable Bro…

Why does this not trigger concern for *mevatel kli meheichano*? Because *ashashiyos* here means a large chunk of glass — *kulsya* — which even after breaking remains usable. Rashi suggests that the broken pieces can still serve a purpose; one possibility is that colored glass shards can be used for mosaic work, or the pieces can be remelted. Therefore, it is not a genuine loss. The *gemara* confirms this reading: *dayka nami dekasni dumya detavla* — it can be inferred by close reading, since the *beraisa* juxtaposes these glass chunks with *tevel* — something completely unusable on Shabbat — indicating that the glass chunks too are not fit for Shabbat use (*lo chazi lei*), confirming we are dealing with raw industrial material rather than finished vessels.

The *gemara* asks: then what is the *chiddush* of *af al pi shemishtabrin* — why does the *beraisa* need to specify "even though they break"? One might have thought that even a minor loss — such as the small shards that cannot be salvaged — would give pause. The *beraisa* teaches that we are not concerned with such a minor loss.

## Gamliel's Donkey & Tzaa'r

### Honey & Dead Animal

A *beraisa* relates that Rabban Gamliel's donkey was carrying honey. He did not want to unload it until after Shabbat, and by the time Shabbat ended, the donkey had died from the burden. The *gemara* is evidently troubled by this outcome and frames the discussion around the prohibition of *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — causing suffering to animals.

### Spoiled Honey Use

The *gemara* first asks why the honey could not simply be removed, since honey is not *muktzah*. The answer is *k'shehikdish* — it had spoiled. The peculiar idiom here, *dvash hikdish* ("the honey became sanctified"), actually means the opposite — it soured. Spoiled honey is *muktzah* because it is unfit for normal use. The *gemara* asks: if it spoiled, for what use is it even being preserved? The answer: *likitita d'gamlei* — it is rubbed into the sores of camels as a medicinal salve, a use unsuitable for Shabbat. Hence it is *muktzah*.

### Alternative Solutions Fa…

The *gemara* then considers why other solutions were not employed. Why not untie the ropes and let the sacks fall? Because *mitzru ziki* — the barrels would break, spilling the honey. Why not place pillows beneath? Because *mitanfi* — the pillows would be soiled with honey and thereby rendered unusable, which again constitutes *mevatel kli meheichano*. Unlike the earlier case where the pillows could be retrieved by gradual removal, here they become permanently soiled and unusable. That solution was therefore off the table.

### Tzaa'r Overrides Rabbinic

The *gemara* points out, however, that the concern of *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — animal suffering — can override rabbinic prohibitions. A classic parallel is the case of an animal that has fallen into a well on Shabbat: one may lower pillows into the well for the animal to rest on, even though this renders the pillows unusable (*mevatel kli meheichano*), because preventing the animal's suffering takes precedence over rabbinic restrictions. If that precedent exists, why did Rabban Gamliel not simply place pillows to reduce the animal's load, notwithstanding the *mevatel kli* concern?

### Gamliel: Tzaa'r Only Rab…

The *gemara* resolves: *kasavar tza'ar ba'alei chayim derabbanan* — Rabban Gamliel holds that the prohibition against causing animal suffering is itself only rabbinic in origin. If that is the case, it is merely a rabbinic concern competing against another rabbinic concern (*mevatel kli meheichano*), and one does not simply override the other.

This is a halakhically decisive point. Whether *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* is *d'oraisa* or *derabanan* has immediate practical consequences: if it is biblical, it overrides rabbinic prohibitions; if it is only rabbinic, it does not. We rule that it is *d'oraisa*, and that ruling has direct application — for example, in the well-known issue of milking dairy cows on Shabbat. Because the cows suffer when not milked, various rabbinic leniencies (having a non-Jew do it, employing an automatic milking device set before Shabbat, and similar arrangements) are permitted, precisely because *tza'ar ba'alei chayim d'oraisa* overrides the relevant rabbinic concerns. Rabban Gamliel, holding it was only rabbinic, let the competing considerations stand — with the tragic result the *gemara* records.

## Sides of Animals & Trees

### Rubbing on Donkey Side

Abaye found Rabba, his *rebbe*, rubbing his son against the side of a donkey — *d'kameshafshef lei l'brei agav d'chamra*. Abaye challenged him: is the master using animals on Shabbat? This leads into a broad and practically significant discussion about the prohibition against using animals and trees on Shabbat. The baseline rule is that one may not ride an animal and may not climb a tree on Shabbat, and this has been extended to prohibit using an animal or tree in any manner. The question is what "using" encompasses: does it mean only the top — the riding surface of an animal, the branches and top of a tree — or does it extend to the sides as well?

### Sides Permitted

Rabba responded: *tzedadim heim, ve'tzedadim lo gazru bahem rabbanan* — it is the side of the animal, and the Rabbis did not extend their decree to the sides. The prohibition was against riding and using the animal in the manner of riding; it does not encompass leaning against the flank or using the side of the animal.

### Proof from Undoing Ropes

Where does this principle come from? The *gemara* derives it from our very *mishnah*: *matir hachavalim vehasakim noflim* — one unties the ropes and the sacks fall. The *gemara* asks: is this not referring to a case — *d'chavar gavalki* — where the sacks are combined as saddlebags on both sides of the donkey? When one unties them, one must lean against the donkey to reach both sides simultaneously, thereby using the donkey's flank for support. Since the *mishnah* permits untying the ropes in this configuration, it implicitly permits using the sides of the donkey.

The *gemara* offers an alternative: perhaps the *mishnah* refers to a different configuration — *d'lo havu tzedadim* — one where the bags are not truly saddlebags but are combined by a central loop or peg that one can release without leaning on the donkey at all. Or similarly, *einami b'lachta* — a rope configuration that allows one to free the sacks without any bodily contact with the animal's side. In that case the *mishnah* provides no proof about *tzedadim* at all.

### Sukkah Third Wall

The *gemara* then raises a case from *hilchos sukkah* to press the question. The *mishnah* in *Sukkah* states: if two walls of the *sukkah* are man-made and the third is a tree, the *sukkah* is valid, but one may not use it on *Yom Tov*. (Rashi notes that "going up" into the *sukkah* refers to the standard practice of building the *sukkah* on a roof, so that one ascends to enter it and descends to return to the house.) The question is: what configuration does this envision, and what precisely is the problem?

### Using Top vs. Trunk

One possibility: the tree's trunk serves as a wall, with a shelf inserted into it to support the *schach*. One cannot use the *sukkah* on *Yom Tov* because reaching for items stored atop the *schach* means making use of the *schach*, which is supported by the shelf in the tree's trunk, which means using the side of the tree. If *tzedadim* — the sides — were permitted, there would be no problem.

Another possibility: one has bent the tops of trees over to form the wall, using the treetops and branches rather than the trunk. In that case, the prohibition comes from using the top of the tree, not the side.

The *gemara* tests this by examining the next clause of the *mishnah*: if three walls are man-made and a tree forms the fourth, the *sukkah* is valid and one *may* use it on *Yom Tov*. This creates a difficulty regardless of how one explains the three-wall case. Whether the *schach* rests on the tree's top (bent-over branches) or on a shelf in its trunk, in either case the tree is still involved when three walls are man-made. Why does adding a third man-made wall resolve the prohibition?

The *gemara* answers: *ela hatam beguza deparsachna* — the case involves a pine-type tree whose branches naturally radiate outward along its trunk, forming a wall on their own without any bending or artificial support. The key point, as Rashi explains, is not the species per se but the structural implication: when three man-made walls support the *schach* entirely, the tree's role is purely that of a curtain-wall, bearing no weight. The rule, confirmed by the *mishnah*'s own summary — *zeh haklal*: if one were to remove the tree and the *sukkah* would remain standing, one may use it on *Yom Tov* — is that if the tree bears no structural load, using the *schach* does not constitute using the tree.

This resolves the four-wall case but leaves the question of *tzedadim versus* the top of the tree in the three-wall case unresolved.

### Sides of Sides Debate

The *gemara* considers whether this connects to a *tannaitic* dispute. The *mishnah* forbids using the three-wall *sukkah* on *Yom Tov*; Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the name of Rabbi Meir permits it. Perhaps they disagree about whether using the trunk — the *tzedadim* — is forbidden. Abaye says no: *dekuli alma tzedadim asurim* — most likely everyone agrees the trunk itself is forbidden. The dispute, he suggests, concerns *tziddei tzedadim* — things attached to the sides of the tree. If the *schach* rests not directly on the trunk but on a shelf that is inserted into the trunk, then one using the *schach* is making direct use of the shelf and only indirect use of the trunk. One opinion forbids this indirect use; the other permits it.

This distinction is practically decisive. The hammock example illustrates it clearly: if one ties a hammock rope directly to a tree trunk, that is *tzedadim* — using the side of the tree. If one inserts a peg into the trunk and ties the rope around the peg, the direct use is of the peg, and the trunk is engaged only indirectly — that is *tziddei tzedadim*.

### Peg in Tree Case

Rami bar Chama challenges Rava — who denied any meaningful distinction between *tzedadim* and *tziddei tzedadim* — from the following *mishnah*: *na'atz yated be'ilan ve'talah bah kalkalah* — one who drove a peg into a tree and hung a basket from it. The basket contains food intended to serve as an *eiruv* to extend one's *techum*. If the basket hangs above ten *tefachim*, the *eiruv* is invalid; below ten *tefachim*, it is valid. The *gemara* notes: *ta'ama d'na'atz yated be'ilan* — the reason it works is that a peg was driven into the tree. This implies that had the basket been hung directly on the tree without a peg, even below ten *tefachim* it would be invalid — because one would be using the tree to access the food. The *mishnah* specifies the peg because it makes the critical difference: with the peg, one is using *tziddei tzedadim*, which is permitted; without it, one is using the *tzedadim* themselves, which is forbidden.

This challenge shows that a distinction between *tzedadim* and *tziddei tzedadim* is implicit in the *mishnah*, contrary to Rava's position.

### Tree Shaking Solution

Rav Papa offers a different explanation of why the peg matters, one that does not require accepting a distinction between *tzedadim* and *tziddei tzedadim*. Even if one holds that the trunk itself may be used, the *mishnah* may specify the peg for a different reason: *hacha b'kalkalah d'chukah askinan* — the basket has a very narrow opening. If the basket were attached directly to the tree, retrieving the food would require inserting one's hand deeply into the basket, and the jostling — *d'shakil lei ati l'atuyei ilana* — would cause the tree to shake. Shaking the tree is the equivalent of using the treetop, which is unambiguously forbidden. With the peg, the basket hangs freely enough that one can retrieve the food without causing the tree to move.

### Halakha: Trunk vs. Attac…

*V'hilchesa*: the ruling is that *tzedadin asurin* — the trunk of the tree is forbidden — and *tziddei tzedadin mutarin* — things attached to the trunk are permitted. This follows Abaye's distinction rather than Rava's flattening of the categories.

### Ladder & Peg Application

Rav Ashi applies this ruling directly: *hashta d'amrat tzedadin asurin* — now that we have established that using the trunk is forbidden — *hai darga d'madla lo linchei inash a'dikla* — one should not lean a ladder against a palm tree, *d'havei lei tzedadin* — because the ladder rests directly on the trunk and one using the ladder is using the trunk.

How then does one climb such a tree when necessary? *Linchei a'guza l'var midikla* — one places a peg that protrudes from the side of the tree. And when climbing: *v'chi salik lo linach krei a'guza* — one should take care not to rest one's foot on the peg itself, *ella lintach a'kanim* — but rather on the rungs of the ladder. If the ladder rests on the peg and one's foot is on the rungs, one is using *tziddei tzedadim* — the ladder, which is supported by the peg, which is in the trunk — and that is permitted. But resting one's foot directly on the peg means using *tzedadim* — the side of the tree — which is forbidden.

The practical upshot is clear: a hammock hung from a nail or peg in a tree is permitted, since the person is in the hammock which hangs from the peg, not directly on the trunk. One simply must ensure the connection is via an intermediate object — peg, nail, or bracket — and not a rope tied directly to the bark.
