# Hullin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish & Hagrama

### Reish Lakish Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אַכְשַׁר בְּחוּדָּא דְּכוֹבָעָא, קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גִּיסָא גִּיסָא, אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: פְּגַע בְּחִיטֵּי – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish deemed** the slaughter **valid** in a case where one cut the windpipe **at the tip of the thyroid cartilage** that is above the large ring. **Rabbi Yoḥanan proclaimed about** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: He **greatly exaggerated** the limits of valid slaughter. **Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava:** If one cut the windpipe and **encountered the arytenoid cartilage** that is adjacent to the upper ring in the direction of the jaw and covered by the tip of the thyroid cartilage, the animal is **a *tereifa*,** i.e., forbidden. Since the arytenoid cartilage is outside the area of slaughter, the slaughter is invalid.

On Daf 19a the Gemara continues its discussion of the practical parameters of *correct*, with a particular focus on the upper boundary of the valid shechita zone and the problem of *hagrama* — moving the knife above the top cartilage ring of the trachea. The preceding sugya had established that Rav and Shmuel objected to shechita in the ring itself, preferring the spaces between the rings, while others disagreed, and the discussion concluded with the celebrated principle of נהרא נהרא ופשטיה — every river follows its own course — acknowledging that differing local practices all draw from the same halachic source.

The Gemara now introduces the position of רבי שמעון בן *lakish*. *reish lakish* ruled that the shechita is valid even if performed בחודא דכובעא — at the very tip of the hat. The anatomical image at work here is of the upper portion of the trachea, which, when viewed from the side, resembles a hat with a rising slope and a tip at the very top. *reish lakish* permitted shechita even at that uppermost point, well above the topmost ring. This position is framed against the background of רבי חנינא בן אנטיגנוס, whose opinion — as the Gemara will later develop — was that *hagrama* presents no problem, meaning not that there is no upper limit whatsoever, but that the valid zone extends considerably higher than the other tannaitic opinions allow. The question driving much of this portion of the sugya is precisely how far upward that zone extends, and whether the fleshy, muscular area just below the tip is included or excluded.

### R. Yochanan Objects


Rabbi Yochanan objected to Reish Lakish's position, dismissing it with the word גיסא גיסא — too much, too far. Even granting that shechita above the top ring is permissible, the very tip of the hat is excessive. Rav Papa in the name of Rava provides the technical explanation: *paga means* בחיטי *tereifah* — if one encounters the strings, the muscular or ligamentous strands in that upper zone, the shechita is a *tereifah*. The concern, in other words, is not merely elevation in a general sense, but specifically whether the knife reaches into or above this band of fibrous tissue that sits just beneath the tip of the hat.

### Pagah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פָּגַע וְנָגַע בָּהֶן, דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּפְגַּע בּוֹ וַיָּמֹת״, אוֹ דִלְמָא פָּגַע וְלֹא נָגַע, כְּדִכְתִיב ״וַיִּפְגְּעוּ בוֹ מַלְאֲכֵי אֱלֹהִים״?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does encountered mean **encountered and touched it, as it is written: "And he encountered him and he died"** (I Kings 2:25); **or perhaps** it means **encountered but did not touch, like that which is written: "And the angels of God encountered him"** (Genesis 32:2)?

The Gemara raises the question: what precisely does *paga means* mean in this context? Is it *paga means* ונגע בהן — that you actually touch and cut through the strings, as suggested by the verse וַיִּפְגַּע בּוֹ וַיָּמָת, which implies physical contact and impact? Or is it *paga means* ולא נגע — that you merely encounter the area without making contact, analogous to וַיִּפְגְּעוּ בוֹ מַלְאֲכֵי אֱלֹהִים, where the angels of God met Jacob without necessarily touching him? The halachic stakes of this question are whether the stringy area itself is included in the kosher zone or whether only cutting above that zone without touching it renders the shechita invalid.

### Shiyur B'Chite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: שַׁיַּיר בְּחִיטֵּי – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רַב אַמֵּימָר בַּר מָר יָנוֹקָא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא, וְאָמַר לִי: שַׁיַּיר בְּחִיטֵּי – כְּשֵׁרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמַר לִי רַב שֶׁמֶן מִסּוּבְרָא, אִיקְּלַע מָר זוּטְרָא לְאַתְרִין וּדְרַשׁ: שַׁיַּיר בְּחִיטֵּי – כְּשֵׁרָה. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: פְּגַע בְּחִיטֵּי – כְּשֵׁרָה, שַׁיַּיר בְּחִיטֵּי – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava:** If **one left** part **of the arytenoid cartilage,** i.e., if he cut it in the middle, the slaughter is **valid. Rav Ameimar bar Mar Yenuka said: I was standing before Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rav Avya, and he said to me:** If **one left** part **of the arytenoid cartilage,** the slaughter is **valid. Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Rav Shemen of Suvara said to me** that **Mar Zutra happened** to come **to our place and taught:** If **one left** part **of the arytenoid cartilage,** the slaughter is **valid. Mar bar Rav Ashi said:** If **one encountered the arytenoid cartilage,** the slaughter is **valid.** If **one left** part **of the arytenoid cartilage,** the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

An authoritative tradition is then transmitted in multiple chains: שיור בחיטי *that's exactly* — if one leaves some of the strings remaining, it is kosher. This means that cutting through that muscular zone is permissible as long as one does not cut above it entirely. The tradition is confirmed by Rav Papa in the name of Rava, corroborated by Rav Meimar bar Mar Yenuka who heard the same ruling from Rav Chiya the son of Rav Avya, and further attested in a report that Ravina told Rav Ashi: Rav Sheman mi-Suvra reported that Mar Zutra came to their locale and expounded publicly that שיור בחיטי *that's exactly*. This convergence of traditions reinforces the ruling. Mar bar Rav Ashi, however, states the matter in a formulation that Tosafot's reading presents as: *paga means* בחיטי *that's exactly* שיור בחיטי *tereifah* — if the knife encounters the strings and cuts through them, it is kosher; if one leaves the entire string area above, having cut above it, it is a *tereifah*. The version of Rashi reverses the girsa, but following Tosafot, the principle is that penetrating into that muscular zone is the lower threshold of acceptable practice, while remaining entirely above it crosses into invalidity.

### Hilchata Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא מִשִּׁיפּוּי כּוֹבַע וּלְמַטָּה – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְהַיְינוּ דְּשַׁיַּיר בְּחִיטֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is: If one slaughters **from the incline of the thyroid cartilage and below** in the direction of the windpipe, the slaughter is **valid, and that is in** accordance with the opinion **that one who left** part **of the arytenoid cartilage** has still performed a valid slaughter, as the arytenoids cartilage extends beyond this point.

The Gemara delivers its ruling: *ve-hilchata* משיפוע כובע ולמטה *that's exactly* — the halacha is that shechita is kosher only from the slanting of the cap downward, and this is consistent with the principle of שיור בחיטי — that one must at minimum cut through that stringy zone below the tip. The top of the cap, where *reish lakish* permitted shechita, is excluded. The practical upshot is that while the position of רבי חנינא בן אנטיגנוס permits a substantially larger zone than the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda who limit one to the top ring and below, even that expanded zone does not reach all the way to the very tip of the anatomical structure. Shechita begins to be valid only from the point where the hat begins to slope.

## Rav Nachman

### RN Allows Cap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אַכְשַׁר מִשִּׁיפּוּי כּוֹבַע וּלְמַטָּה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: כְּמַאן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman deemed** the slaughter **valid** in a case where one slaughtered **from the incline of the thyroid cartilage and below. Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ketina said to Rav Naḥman: In accordance with whose** opinion is that ruling?

The Gemara then records: *rav i only said my second position* נחמן אכשר משיפוע כובע ולמטה — Rav Nachman permitted shechita from the angling of the cap downward, which is the same ruling just delivered as the halacha. One might wonder why the editor placed the final halachic ruling before Rav Nachman's statement, but the substance of their positions is identical.

### Fitting Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא כְּרַבָּנַן וְלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis nor in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** who both hold that if one cuts the windpipe above the large upper ring, the cricoid cartilage, the slaughter is not valid.

Rav Chanan bar Rav Kitina challenged Rav Nachman: *ke-man* — like whom? This position, permitting the entire structure above the top ring, does not align with either of the two tannaitic positions in the Mishnah. Neither the Rabbis nor Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda would sanction shechita so high up, since both of their debates concern the top ring itself, and anything above that ring — which is this entire structure — they consider *hagrama*.

### Tradition Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לָא חִילָק יָדַעְנָא וְלָא בִּילָק יָדַעְנָא, אֲנָא שְׁמַעְתָּא יָדַעְנָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לָהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִשִּׁיפּוּי כּוֹבַע וּלְמַטָּה כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Neither do I know Ḥillek nor do I know Billek,** i.e., I know the reason neither for this one's opinion nor for that one's opinion. **I know the *halakha*, as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and some say** that **Rabbi Abba bar Zavda says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says, and some say** that **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: From the incline of the thyroid cartilage and below,** the slaughter is **valid.**

Rav Nachman responded with the celebrated declaration: אנא לא *chilek* ידענא ולא *bilek* ידענא אנא שמעתא ידענא — I know neither chilek nor bilek, I know my tradition. As Rashi explains, after entertaining the possibility that chilek referred to the Rabbis and bilek to Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda based on the connotations of those roots, he ultimately concludes that these are simply idiomatic expressions akin to "I don't know Joe, I don't know Schmoe" — I am not going to resolve the tannaitic dispute, I am simply transmitting what I received. And what he received, passed through Rabbi Chiya bar Zavda who said it in the name of Rabbi Chanina, and also transmitted as Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi said it in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, is: משיפוע כובע ולמטה *that's exactly* — from the slanting of the cap downward it is kosher.

### Fitting Amoraim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מוּגְרֶמֶת דְּרַבָּנַן כְּשֵׁרָה לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וּדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, כְּשֵׁרָה לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** With regard to a case where the knife is diverted from the place of slaughter above the ring, the slaughter is not valid. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus testified about a case where the knife is diverted from the place of slaughter above the ring that the slaughter is valid. **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** With regard to a case where the knife was **diverted** according to the opinion **of the Rabbis** cited in the mishna, the slaughter is **valid** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** who ruled that the slaughter is valid when the majority of the windpipe was cut within the large, upper ring. **And** with regard to a case where the knife was **diverted** according to the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** it is **valid** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus,** who ruled that even if a majority of the windpipe was cut outside the large upper ring, the slaughter is valid.

The Gemara then proceeds to integrate the amoraic tradition with the tannaitic framework through the voice of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself: מוגרמת דרבנן *that's exactly* לרבי יוסי ברבי יהודה — what the Rabbis define as invalid *hagrama* is kosher according to Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. And what even Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda considers invalid — *correct* conducted entirely above the top ring — is kosher according to Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus. The Gemara objects: *pishita*, this is obvious! The response is that one might have assumed that Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, who rejects *hagrama*, was only rejecting the Rabbis' definition of *hagrama*, not extending beyond Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position. The Gemara teaches that he goes further than all of them — his rejection of *hagrama* encompasses every definition of that disqualification that any tanna articulates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אַדְּרַבָּנַן קָאֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: That is **obvious.** The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that the statement of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus,** who ruled that in a case where the knife is diverted from the place of slaughter above the ring it is valid, **addresses** the statement **of the Rabbis** and he agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, that the slaughter is not valid when the majority of the windpipe was cut above the large upper ring, therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **teaches us** that this is not the case.

A further objection is raised: perhaps Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus indeed goes only as far as Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara answers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? אִם כֵּן, ״הֵעִיד עָלֶיהָ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס, דְּקָאֵי רַב נַחְמָן כְּוָותֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And say it is indeed so** that Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus agrees with Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara responds: **If so,** the formulation of the *baraita* **should have** been: Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus **testified about it.** Since the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus was introduced merely with the term: Testified, apparently he disagrees with all of the other opinions. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus, as Rav Naḥman holds in accordance with his** opinion.

if so, when the Mishnah reported that Rabbi Yose bar Chanina testified regarding *hagrama* and repeated that term, it would have said that he testified on "it" — meaning specifically the case of *hagrama* as defined by one particular opinion. The use of *hagrama* in a general sense implies that he dismissed every tanna's definition of *hagrama*, giving a far wider zone for valid shechita than anyone else. Thus the position from the slanting of the cap downward corresponds to following Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, who is the most lenient of the tannaim on this question.

## Majority Debate

### Majority Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁשָּׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ, דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כּוּלַּהּ שְׁחִיטָה בָּעֵינַן בְּטַבַּעַת גְּדוֹלָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: רוּבּוֹ כְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cited a dispute between the Rabbis, who hold that in a case where one slaughtered within the large upper ring and did not leave a thread breadth over the entire surface of the ring, the slaughter is not valid, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who hold that even if one left a thread breadth over a majority of the surface of the ring, the slaughter is valid. Apropos to that, **Rav Huna said** that **Rav Asi says:** The **dispute** is only **in** a case **where one cut two-thirds** of the windpipe within the ring **and** then **diverted** the knife upward toward the head of the animal and cut the remaining **one-third, as the Rabbis hold** that **we require the entire slaughter** to be performed **within the large ring, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds:** The halakhic status of **its majority is like** that of **its entirety.**

The Gemara now shifts from anatomical location to a more abstract conceptual question. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav Assi: מחלוקת בששחט שני *right* והגרים *right* — the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concerns the case where one has already shechted the first two-thirds of the trachea correctly and then, for the final third, moved the knife above the valid zone. The question is whether what occurs after the valid majority of the cut has been completed can invalidate the shechita retroactively.

It is important to remember that the measurement here is depth — how far through the hollow of the trachea the knife penetrates — not the linear length of the stroke. The *correct* is halachically defined as cutting through the majority of the inner hollow of the trachea, and so the debate is framed in terms of what portion of that hollow was cut correctly.

### Rabbis Whole Act


The Rabbis hold: כולי *correct* בעינן בטבעת גדולה — we require that the entire act of shechita be performed within the proper zone. Although it is true that performing the majority of the trachea's depth suffices to render the animal halachically slaughtered, if one is going to perform the entire act, every part of that act must be done correctly. Doing the majority correctly and then moving into the disqualified zone taints the shechita.

### RY Majority Stands


Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda applies the principle of *necessarily* ככולו — the majority is treated as the whole. Once the majority of the trachea has been cut through a valid act of shechita, the shechita is complete and whatever occurs afterward is legally irrelevant. The conceptual question underlying this debate, which has broader application across multiple פסולי *correct* such as *shehiya* and *drasa*, is whether the principle of "majority suffices" means that majority is all that is required — making everything after the majority legally invisible — or whether it means that majority serves as a stand-in for the whole, in which case if the whole thing is being done, the entirety of it must be performed correctly.

### Two-Thirds Good


This framing allows for the following resolution: in the case where the first two-thirds were done correctly and the final third was too high, that is the case of their debate. But if one considers the reverse scenario — beginning incorrectly — the analysis shifts entirely.

### First Third Bad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ, וְשָׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלָה, דְּכִי נָפְקָא חִיּוּתָא בָּעֵינַן רוּבָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **one diverted** the knife upward toward the head of the animal, cut **one-third** of the windpipe, **and** then **cut two-thirds** within the ring, **everyone agrees** that the slaughter is **not valid, as when the life left** the animal, i.e., when the majority of the windpipe was cut, **we require** that the entire **majority** be cut **by means of slaughter, and that is not** so in this case.

If the first third was too high and the final two-thirds were done correctly, דברי הכל *pasul* — everyone agrees it is invalid.

### Nafakat Chayuta


The reason is: דכי נפיק חיותא בעינן *necessarily* בשחיטה וליכא — when the life-force of the animal departs, meaning at the moment when the majority of the trachea has been severed, we require that the majority have been cut through a valid act of shechita, and here it was not. When the knife reached the fifty-one percent mark — the point at which the animal's *chiyut* leaves — half of the trachea had been cut through invalid *hagrama* and half through valid shechita. The majority of the trachea had not been cut correctly at that critical moment, and so the shechita is universally invalid. The entire dispute, on this reading, is confined to the case where the majority was already done correctly and only the final portion was deficient.

## Chisda Challenge

### Reverse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: אַדְּרַבָּה, לֵימָא מָר אִיפְּכָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ, וְשָׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ, דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַחֲצִי קָנֶה פָּגוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to** Rav Huna: **On the contrary, let the Master say the opposite.** The **dispute** is only **in** a case **when one diverted** the knife upward toward the head of the animal, cut **one-third** of the windpipe, **and** then **cut two-thirds** within the ring, **as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that the slaughter is valid **just as it is** in the case **where half the windpipe is deficient.** In that case, once the slaughterer cuts any additional part of the windpipe, the slaughter is valid because the cut that rendered a majority of the windpipe slaughtered was performed properly. So too in this case, since the cut of the second third was performed properly, the slaughter is valid.

Rav Chisda challenges this framing directly: *adaraba*, say the opposite. Perhaps the debate between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concerns precisely the case where the first third was incorrect and the last two-thirds were done correctly — and it is the other case, where the first two-thirds were correct and the last third was not, that is universally agreed upon.

### Hole Analogy


According to Rav Chisda's reversal, Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda would say that beginning incorrectly is valid, and his reasoning is: מידי דהוי אחצי **kaneh** פגום — it is analogous to a case where the trachea had a pre-existing natural injury occupying half its width. The halacha, as we will encounter, is that while small holes in the trachea generally render an animal a *tereifah*, the trachea is an organ that can sustain a natural cut of up to half its circumference without becoming a *tereifah*. If such a natural injury exists, and the shochet then cuts the remaining fraction needed to cross the fifty-one percent threshold, that constitutes a valid shechita — even though the shochet performed only a small portion of the cutting. The rationale is that the existing opening is incorporated into the act of shechita, redefining the trachea's remaining intact tissue as the relevant measure. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda applies the same logic to the case where the first third was cut above the valid zone: that invalid portion is comparable to the pre-existing hole, and the valid cutting of the remaining two-thirds, which does cross the majority threshold in the correct zone, suffices.

### Right Place Cut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הָתָם מָקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה, הָכָא לָאו מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** hold that **there,** in the case of the deficient windpipe, the deficiency was in **the place of** proper **slaughter,** and therefore most of the life of the animal left in the proper place. **Here,** the first third was cut while the knife was diverted, and therefore most of the life of the animal did **not** leave in **the place of** proper **slaughter.**

The Rabbis disagree and draw a crucial distinction: התם מקום *correct* — there, in the case of the natural pre-existing hole, the cut exists in the correct zone where shechita must take place. Even though the shochet only contributed the final increment, the majority of what was severed lies within the valid zone. הכא לאו מקום *correct* — here, however, when the first portion of the cut was made above the valid zone, the cut in the invalid zone does not count. When the knife reaches the fifty-one percent depth of the trachea, the right zone has not yet had the majority of its width severed — only half of the correct area has been cut. The principle the Rabbis assert is not merely that the majority of the trachea must be cut, but that the majority of the trachea must be cut correctly, meaning in the right place. Starting above the zone and finishing below it leaves the valid zone only partially incised even at the moment the fifty-one percent depth is crossed.

Under Rav Chisda's reversal, it would follow that if one did the first two-thirds correctly and the final third too high, everyone would agree it is valid — since the principle of רובו של אחד *means*, the majority of a **siman** counts as the whole, would mean we disregard entirely what happened after the majority was correctly performed.

## Rov Concept

### Part of Day


This conceptual divide — whether "majority suffices" means majority is the complete requirement or merely a substitute for the whole — has broad implications across halacha. An instructive parallel is the principle of מקצת היום ככולו, part of a day counts as a whole day. Consider the practical application in the laws of נידה: since women today are treated with the stringency of a *zavah*, who must count seven clean days, the beginning of the seventh day can halachically count as though the day is complete. A woman could therefore immerse in the mikvah at the beginning of the seventh day. Yet the practice is to wait until nightfall. The reason is that if she immerses during the day and then sees blood later that same day, the immersion is retroactively nullified — since the day did not in fact end cleanly, the beginning of the day never counted. This is a precise analogue to the question here: does the part-counting-as-whole work absolutely, rendering subsequent events irrelevant, or does it remain provisional and subject to invalidation by what follows?

### Majority Exclusive?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לָן, דְּהָהוּא רוּבָּא דְּהָתָם לָאו רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָתָנֵי לַהּ? דִּלְמָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָתָנֵי לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to** Rav Ḥisda: **Who will say to us that** with regard to the mishna **there** concerning **the majority of** one *siman*, **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, did not teach it? Perhaps Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, taught it,** and the Rabbis disagree.

Rav Yosef raises a pointed question regarding Rav Chisda's reversal and the conclusion that if the first two-thirds were done correctly everyone would agree it is valid based on the principle of רובו של אחד *means*. מאן לימא לן דהוא רובא דהתם לרבי יוסי בר יהודה קתני לה — who says that this ruling about majority is not exclusively the position of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda? Perhaps it is only Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda who says that once the majority is done we disregard what follows, and the Rabbis would say we continue to care about the entirety of the act.

### Shechita Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַטּוּ כֹּל רוּבֵּי דְּעָלְמָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָתָנֵי לְהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא רוּבָּא דִּשְׁחִיטָה קָאָמֵינָא, דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהוּ דִּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Is that to say** that with regard to **all** principles that address **majorities in general, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, taught them?** Rav Yosef **said to him: I am speaking of the** principle of **majority with regard to slaughter, as we heard that** the Rabbis **disagree** with him.

Abaye responds: אטו כל רובי דאמר רבי יוסי בר יהודה קתני להו — does every invocation of majority throughout Shas reflect only Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position? The principle of majority is a broad and fundamental principle of halachic reasoning applied across countless contexts. The rejoinder is: אנא רובי דשחיטה קאמינא — I am speaking specifically about majority within the domain of shechita. There we explicitly know that the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda debate this very question. Perhaps different areas of halacha treat the concept of majority differently, but within shechita, the dispute over how majority functions is between these two positions, and so the question of whether the Rabbis would accept the principle of disregarding post-majority deficiencies remains genuinely open.

## Lishna Achrina

### Begin Wrong

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ וְשָׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ, דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַחֲצִי קָנֶה פָּגוּם, וְרַבָּנַן – הָתָם מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה, הָכָא לָאו מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that there is **an alternative version** of this discussion: **Rav Huna said** that **Rav Asi says:** The **dispute** is only in a case **when one diverted** the knife upward toward the head of the animal, cut **one-third** of the windpipe, **and** then **cut two-thirds** within the ring, **as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that the slaughter is valid **just as it is** in the case **where half the windpipe is deficient, and the Rabbis** hold that **there,** in the case of the deficient windpipe, the deficiency was in **the place of** proper **slaughter,** but **here,** most of the life of the animal did **not** leave in **the place of** proper **slaughter.**

The Gemara presents a לישנא אחרינא, a second formulation of Rav Huna's statement in the name of Rav. In this version, the debate is framed from the outset as: מחלוקת שהגרים *right* ושחט שני *right* — the dispute concerns the case where the first third was performed incorrectly and the last two-thirds were performed correctly. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda says it is valid: מידי דהוה אחתיכה דפגא — analogously to a pre-existing cut in the trachea, the invalid opening in the beginning is treated as though it were a natural wound, and the valid cutting of the remainder suffices. The Rabbis say it is invalid: התם מקום *correct* הכא לאו מקום *correct* — there, the pre-existing wound is in the proper location; here, the initial cut was not in the proper location. In the end the valid zone was not cut through its majority even though the majority depth of the trachea was reached.

## Middle Third Case

### Good-Bad-Good

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שָׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ, וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרָה, דְּהָא תְּנַן: רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in a case where **one cut two-thirds** of the windpipe within the ring **and** then **diverted** the knife and cut the remaining **one-third, everyone agrees** that the slaughter is **valid, as didn't we learn** in a mishna (27a): The halakhic status of the slaughter of the **majority of one** *siman*, the windpipe or the gullet, **is like** that of the slaughter of the entire *siman* **itself?**

The second formulation then posits: if the first two-thirds were performed correctly and only the final third was above the zone — *aval *aval** *shechted even up* שני *right* והגרים *right*, דברי הכל *that's exactly* — everyone agrees it is valid, because the principle of רובו של אחד *means* applies and we do not concern ourselves with what occurred after the majority was correctly executed.

## Lishna Achrina…

### First Two OK


On this reading of the second formulation, the universal agreement on the validity of the case where the first two-thirds were correct is grounded in a precise reading of the phrase רובו של אחד *means*. One might note that the formulation says *means* rather than ככולו. Possibly the *diyuk* is that *means* means that the majority counts as itself — as sufficient — rather than as a stand-in for the whole, implying that once majority is achieved, the requirement is discharged and nothing thereafter can undermine it.

## Middle Third Case…

### Life at Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ, וְשָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ, וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ – רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כְּשֵׁרָה, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: טְרֵפָה. רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כְּשֵׁרָה – כִּי נָפְקָא חִיּוּתָא בִּשְׁחִיטָה קָא נָפְקָא. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: טְרֵפָה – בָּעֵינַן רוּבָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § If **one diverted** the knife and cut **one-third** of the windpipe, **and** then **cut one-third** properly, **and** then **diverted** and cut the final **one-third, Rav Huna** says that **Rav says:** The slaughter is **valid. Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says:** The animal is **a *tereifa*,** i.e., forbidden. **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says:** The slaughter is **valid,** as **when the life left** the animal, it was **in** the course of a valid **slaughter** that **it left. Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says:** The animal is **a *tereifa*, as we require a majority** of the windpipe to be cut **with** valid **slaughter,and** that **is not** the case.

A further case now presents itself: הגרים *right* ושחט *right* והגרים *right* — the first third was too high, the middle third was performed correctly, and the final third was again too high. Here only one third of the entire act of shechita was in the valid zone, and that one valid third was confined to the middle. The majority of the cutting was done incorrectly. Yet crucially, the middle of the trachea — the fifty-one percent line — was crossed during the valid portion of the cut. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: *that's exactly* — it is valid. The reasoning is: כי נפק חיותא בשחיטה קנפק — when the life-force of the animal departed, meaning when the knife passed the midpoint of the trachea, it was doing so through a valid act of shechita. The fact that the majority of the total cutting was performed in the wrong zone is subordinated to the fact that the critical moment — the crossing of the midpoint — occurred correctly.


Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *tereifah* — it is invalid. בעינן רובא בשחיטה וליכא — we require that the majority of the trachea be cut through a valid act of shechita, and here it was not. Only one-third of the trachea was cut correctly. The crossing of the midpoint alone is insufficient; what matters is not the midpoint but the majority of the trachea's depth, and most of that depth was severed in the wrong zone.

### Majority Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ, וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ, וְשָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ – רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **one cut one-third** of the windpipe properly, **and** then **diverted** the knife and cut **one-third, and** then **cut** the final **one-third** of the windpipe properly, **Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav said:** The slaughter is **valid.**

The Gemara then presents the mirror image of this case: *shechted even up* *right* והגרים *right* ושחט *right* — the first third was performed correctly, the middle third was above the zone, and the last third was performed correctly again. Here the majority of the cutting was done correctly but the middle third — where the fifty-one percent line sits — was crossed in the wrong zone.


Rav Yehuda now says: *that's exactly* — it is valid. He reverses his ruling from the previous case. Since for him what matters is the majority of the trachea being cut correctly, and here the majority of the cutting was indeed in the valid zone, the shechita is good. The fact that the fifty-one percent line was crossed during the invalid middle portion does not matter to him.

### Bad-Good-Bad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ שַׁיְילוּהּ לְרַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: טְרֵפָה. שְׁמַע רַב יְהוּדָה אִיקְּפַד, אֲמַר: טָרֵיפְנָא (ומכשר) [מַכְשַׁר], (ומכשרנא) [מַכְשַׁרְנָא] טָרֵיף! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שַׁפִּיר קָא מִיקְּפַד, חֲדָא – אִיהוּ שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וַאֲנָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, וְעוֹד – הָאִיכָּא רוּבָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** When the Sages **came and asked Rav Huna, he said to them:** It is **a *tereifa*. Rav Yehuda heard** the ruling of Rav Huna, and **he was angry. He said: I deem** it **a *tereifa* and he deems** the slaughter **valid, and I deem** the slaughter **valid** and **he deems** it **a *tereifa*. Rav Huna said:** It is **proper** that **he was angry. One** reason is that **he heard** it **from Rav and I did not hear** it from Rav; **and furthermore, isn't there the majority** of the *siman* that was cut **with** valid **slaughter?**

Rav Yehuda heard that Rav Huna ruled the opposite — *tereifah* for this mirror case — and was offended, saying: when I say *tereifah*, he says *kasher*; when I say *kasher*, he says *tereifah*. Rav Huna acknowledged that Rav Yehuda was right to be offended: שפיר קא מקפיד. He offered two reasons. First, חדא איהו שמיע ליה מיניה דרב ואנא לא שמיע לי — in one of these cases, Rav Yehuda transmitted the ruling in the name of Rav, while Rav Huna did not; Rav Yehuda had the authority of a direct tradition from Rav. Second, *ve'od* האיכא רובא בשחיטה — moreover, in the mirror case where Rav Huna declared *tereifah*, there is actually a majority of the trachea that was cut correctly, and Rav Huna should have acknowledged that. The implied criticism is that Rav Huna's position commits him to a standard inconsistent with his first ruling.

### RY Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא תֶּהְדַּר בָּךְ, דְּאִם כֵּן, מַפְסְדַתְּ לַהּ לְקַמַּיְיתָא. הָתָם מַאי טַעְמָא קָא מַכְשְׁרַתְּ? דְּכִי נָפְקָא חִיּוּתָא – בְּהֶכְשֵׁירַהּ קָא נָפְקָא. הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּי נָפְקָא חִיּוּתָא – בְּהַגְרָמָה קָא נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to** Rav Huna: **Do not retract** your statement, **as, if** you retract your statement, **you repudiate the first** ruling that you stated with regard to a case where one diverted the knife and cut one-third of the windpipe, and then cut one-third properly, and then diverted and cut the final one-third. **There, what is the reason** that **you deemed** the slaughter **valid?** The reason is **that when life left** the animal with the cutting of the second third of the windpipe, **it left** the animal **in** the course of **valid** slaughter. Based on that reasoning, **here too, when life left** the animal with the cutting of the second third of the windpipe, **it left** the animal **in** the course of **diverting** the knife, and the slaughter is invalid.

Rav Chisda then stepped in to warn Rav Huna: לא תהדר בך — do not reverse yourself. His caution is precise: דאם כן מסתרת לה לקמייתא — if you reverse yourself on this case, you implicitly undermine your first position. Rav Huna's first ruling, that the case with the valid middle third is *kasher*, was premised on the principle that כי נפיק חיותא בהכשר קנפיק — when the life-force departed, meaning when the fifty-one percent line was crossed, it was crossed through a valid act of shechita. That is what makes it valid, not the majority of the cutting. Now in the mirror case, where the first and last thirds are valid and the middle is not, כי נפיק חיותא באגרמה קנפיק — when the life-force departed, the fifty-one percent line was crossed during the invalid middle portion. If Rav Huna's criterion is whether the midpoint is crossed correctly, he must say this mirror case is invalid. To say otherwise would be to abandon his own rationale. Rav Chisda urges him: be consistent; do not let the pressure of Rav Yehuda's offense lead you to reverse a well-reasoned position.

## Comb Shechita

### RN Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיקְּלַע רַב נַחְמָן לְסוּרָא, בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ: שָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ וְשָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman happened** to come **to Sura,** where **they asked him:** If **one cut one-third** of the windpipe properly, **and** then **diverted** the knife and cut **one-third, and** then **cut** the final **one-third** of the windpipe properly, **what is** the *halakha*?

When Rav Nachman visited Sura, the scholars there posed to him the question of *shechted even up* *right* והגרים *right* ושחט *right* — the first and last thirds in the valid zone, the middle third above it.

### Don't Reverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי: שְׁחִיטָה הָעֲשׂוּיָה כְּמַסְרֵק כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: Isn't that** the *halakha* stated by **Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi, as Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi says: Slaughter that is performed like** the teeth of **a comb,** which are jagged, is **valid.**

Rav Nachman responded: לאו היינו דרבי אלעזר בר מניומי — is this not the case of Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi? Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi had said: *correct* העשויה כמסרק *that's exactly* — a shechita performed like a comb, meaning with an up-and-down jagged motion, is valid. Rav Nachman applies this to the case at hand, where the knife moved in and out of the valid zone in a comb-like pattern, and rules it kosher.

### Asked RN

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא בִּמְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה! בִּמְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: בָּעֵינַן שְׁחִיטָה מְפוֹרַעַת וְלֵיכָּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And perhaps** Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi stated his *halakha* only when the slaughter goes up and down **within** the proper **place of slaughter.** The Gemara asks in response: **Within** the proper **place of slaughter, what** is the purpose **of stating** it? Clearly the slaughter is valid in that case. The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **we require slaughter** that is **clear** and straight, **and** slaughter that is jagged **is not** straight, therefore, Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi **teaches us** that nevertheless, the slaughter is valid.

The Gemara challenges this analogy: ודילמא במקום *correct* — perhaps Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi's ruling about comb-like shechita refers only to a case where the jagged motion remained entirely within the kosher zone? That would not address a case where the knife moved outside the valid zone. The Gemara then asks: if comb-like shechita within the valid zone were the entire teaching, what would be the *chiddush*? One might have thought that בעינן *correct* מפורעת — a valid shechita requires an exposed, straight, unobstructed cut, such that a jagged line within the zone where portions of the cut are recessed and not directly visible in a straight line would be invalid. The ruling teaches that this is not required, and an irregular line within the kosher zone is acceptable. But this leaves open the question of a case where portions of the cut are outside the kosher zone entirely.

## Pre-Existing Cuts

### Masreik Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְיָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: שָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ וְהִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ וְשָׁחַט שְׁלִישׁ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba sat behind Rav Kahana and Rav Kahana sat before Rav Yehuda, and he sat and said** to Rav Yehuda: If **one cut one-third** of the windpipe properly, **and** then **diverted** the knife and cut **one-third, and** then **cut** the final **one-third** of the windpipe properly, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav Yehuda **said to** Rav Kahana: **His slaughter is valid.**

Rabbi Abba was sitting in the back row while Rav Kahana sat before Rav Yehuda in a shiur. Rav Kahana posed the following sequence of questions to Rav Yehuda regarding pre-existing cuts in the trachea.

### Kosher Zone Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם נֶקֶב, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. שָׁחַט וּפָגַע בּוֹ נֶקֶב, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana then asked: If **one cut in a place** where there was **a perforation** in the front of the windpipe and continued cutting, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav Yehuda **said to** him: **His slaughter is valid.** Rav Kahana further asked: If **one cut** the windpipe **and** after cutting half the windpipe **encountered a perforation,** after which point a majority of the windpipe had been cut, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav Yehuda **said to** him: **His slaughter is not valid.**

Rav Yehuda responded to Rav Kahana's questions as follows: *shechted even up* במקום נקב — if the shochet cut in the place where there was already a pre-existing hole in the front of the trachea, such that he simply extended what was already there and crossed the majority threshold — שחיטתו *that's exactly*, it is valid. *shechted even up* ופגע בו נקב — if, however, the shochet was cutting from the front and encountered a pre-existing hole in the back of the trachea, meaning the knife reached the midpoint by meeting the back hole rather than by cutting through the front wall to beyond the center — שחיטתו פסולה, it is invalid. Rabbi Abba then went and reported this distinction to Rabbi Elazar, who brought it before Rabbi Yochanan.

### RK Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל רַבִּי אַבָּא, אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אֲזַל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם נֶקֶב, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁשָּׁחַט גּוֹי וְגָמַר יִשְׂרָאֵל. שָׁחַט וּפָגַע בּוֹ נֶקֶב, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁשָּׁחַט יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגָמַר גּוֹי. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ: ״גּוֹי גּוֹי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba went** to Eretz Yisrael and **stated** these *halakhot* **in the presence of Rabbi Elazar,** and **Rabbi Elazar went** and **stated** these *halakhot* **in the presence of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: What is different** about a case where one cut in a place where there was a perforation relative to a case where he encountered a perforation in the middle of the slaughter? Rabbi Elazar **said to him:** In a case where **one cut in a place** where there was **a perforation, it becomes like** an animal **that a gentile** began to **slaughter and a Jew completed** its slaughter, in which case the slaughter is valid. In a case where **one cut** the windpipe **and encountered a perforation, it becomes like** an animal **that a Jew** began to **slaughter and a gentile completed** its slaughter, in which case the slaughter is not valid. Rabbi Yoḥanan mockingly **proclaimed about him: Gentile, gentile,** i.e., you merely repeat something about gentiles. Rabbi Yoḥanan did not accept the distinction.

Rabbi Elazar offered an explanation of why the front hole makes it valid while the back hole makes it invalid: *shechted even up* במקום נקב נעשה כמי ששחט עובד כוכבים וגמר ישראל — when one cuts where there is already a front hole, it is as if a non-Jew began the shechita and a Jew finished it. The first half of the opening was not the act of the shochet; he picked up from the midpoint and completed the majority correctly. So long as the critical crossing of the fifty-one percent line was done by the shochet in the valid zone, the shechita is valid. *shechted even up* ופגע בו נקב — if one encountered a back hole, נעשה כמי ששחט ישראל וגמר עובד כוכבים — it is as if a Jew began and a non-Jew finished. The shochet cut up to the midpoint, but the midpoint was crossed by connecting with the back hole rather than by a genuine cutting act through the valid tissue. The fifty-one percent line was not crossed by the shochet, and so the shechita is invalid.

### Front vs Back


Rabbi Yochanan was unimpressed: עובד כוכבים עובד כוכבים — goy, goy. As Rashi notes, this was a rhetorical dismissal rather than a personal insult to Rabbi Elazar; Rabbi Yochanan was mocking the analogy as inadequate, telling him not to explain it to him with this kind of reasoning.

### Goy Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שַׁפִּיר קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ ״גּוֹי גּוֹי״, בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל לְמִשְׁחַט רוּבָּא וְלָא שְׁחַט, כִּי נָפְקָא חִיּוּתָא – בִּידָא דְּגוֹי קָא נָפְקָא, אֶלָּא הָכָא מִכְּדֵי מִשְׁחָט שָׁחֵיט, מָה לִי בִּמְקוֹם נֶקֶב, מָה לִי פָּגַע בּוֹ נֶקֶב?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Rabbi Yoḥanan did **well** when he **proclaimed about him: Gentile, gentile. Granted, there,** in the case where the gentile completed the slaughter, **since a Jew was** supposed **to cut a majority** of the windpipe **and he did not cut** it, **when life left** the animal **it left by the hand of a gentile. But here, after all, he** was the one who **slaughtered** the entire animal, and the animal was not a *tereifa* until this point, and so **what** difference is there **to me** if one began to cut **in a place** where there was **a perforation** and **what** difference is there **to me** if he **encountered a perforation?**

Rava explains why the objection is well-founded: שפיר קרי ליה עובד כוכבים עובד כוכבים — it was correct to taunt him, because the analogy is genuinely flawed. In the actual case of a non-Jew and Jew, the invalidity is clear: מדאית ליה לישראל למשחט רובא ולא *shechted even up* — the Jew was obligated to shecht the majority and did not, and so when the life-force departed, כי נפקי חיותא בידא דעובד כוכבים קא נפקי — it departed through the act of the non-Jew, who cut the fifty-one percent line. The non-Jew's act brought the animal to its death.

But here, by contrast, מכדי שוחט שחית ליה — the shochet himself performed the entire act. There was no other actor. מה לי במקום נקב מה לי *paga means* בו נקב — what is the difference to me whether the shochet cut through a front hole or encountered a back hole? In both cases the shochet did everything, and there is no other actor whose intervention could be said to have taken over the act. The analogy to a non-Jew and a Jew breaks down precisely because in the pre-existing hole cases, a single person performs the complete act of shechita, meaning the existing hole should be incorporated into that actor's shechita regardless of whether it is in the front or the back. This creates a logical tension with Rav Yehuda's own earlier rulings, since if what matters is the majority of the trachea being cut correctly, and the shochet personally cut the majority of the trachea correctly in the front-hole case (even though he only added a small amount), the legal framework would need to treat the existing hole as part of his act of shechita — and that logic should apply equally to the back hole.

## Shechita vs Melikah

### Reject Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הַצְּדָדִין – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הַצְּדָדִין – מְלִיקָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who slaughters from the sides** of the throat, **his slaughter is valid. One who pinches** the neck of a bird offering **from the sides, his pinching is not valid.**

The Gemara closes this amud with a Mishnah that introduces a systematic contrast between *correct* and *melika*. השוחט מן הצדדים — one who shechts from the sides of the neck — שחיטתו *that's exactly*, the shechita is valid, because cutting from the side reaches the trachea and esophagus without severing the spine or otherwise creating an additional בסיס *pasul*. המולק מן הצדדים — one who performs *melika* from the sides — מליקתו פסולה, it is invalid. Melikah is the act performed by the *kohen does* with his thumbnail on bird sacrifices, and it specifically requires cutting from the back of the neck through the spine; the side is not the correct location for that act.

### Whole Act Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הָעוֹרֶף – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הָעוֹרֶף – מְלִיקָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who slaughters from the nape [*oref*]** of the neck, **his slaughter is not valid. One who pinches** a bird offering **from the nape** of the neck, **his pinching is valid.**

Correspondingly: השוחט מן העורף — one who shechts from the back of the neck, the עורף — שחיטתו פסולה, the shechita is invalid, because cutting from the back breaks through the spine, creating an independent *pasul* of a broken spine. המולק מן העורף — one who performs *melika* from the back of the neck — מליקתו *that's exactly*, it is valid, because that is precisely the required method.

### Side Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הַצַּוָּאר – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הַצַּוָּאר – מְלִיקָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹרֶף כָּשֵׁר לִמְלִיקָה, וְכׇל הַצַּוָּאר כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁחִיטָה. נִמְצָא, כָּשֵׁר בִּשְׁחִיטָה – פָּסוּל בִּמְלִיקָה, כָּשֵׁר בִּמְלִיקָה – פָּסוּל בִּשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who slaughters from the throat, his slaughter is valid. One who pinches** a bird offering **from the throat, his pinching is not valid, as the entire nape is valid for pinching and the entire throat is valid for slaughter. It is found that** that which is **valid for slaughter** is **not valid for pinching** and that which is **valid for pinching** is **not valid for slaughter.**

השוחט מן הצוואר — one who shechts from the front of the throat — שחיטתו *that's exactly*, obviously valid; this is the paradigmatic act of shechita. המולק מן הצוואר — one who performs *melika* from the front — מליקתו פסולה, it is invalid.

### Back Melikah


The Mishnah draws the governing principle: שכל העורף *kasher* למליקה — the entire back of the neck is valid for *melika*, וכל הצוואר *kasher* לשחיטה — the entire throat, which the Mishnah implies includes the sides as well, is valid for *correct*. The conclusion: *kasher* בשחיטה *pasul* במליקה — what is valid for *correct*, meaning the throat and sides, is invalid for *melika*; *kasher* במליקה *pasul* בשחיטה — what is valid for *melika*, meaning the back of the neck, is invalid for *correct*. This Mishnah opens a series of mutually exclusive categories that will be the subject of the discussion going forward.
