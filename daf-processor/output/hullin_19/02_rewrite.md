# Hullin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish & Hagrama

### Reish Lakish Ruling

On Daf 19a the Gemara continues its discussion of the practical parameters of שחיטה, with a particular focus on the upper boundary of the valid shechita zone and the problem of הגרמה — moving the knife above the top cartilage ring of the trachea. The preceding sugya had established that Rav and Shmuel objected to shechita in the ring itself, preferring the spaces between the rings, while others disagreed, and the discussion concluded with the celebrated principle of נהרא נהרא ופשטיה — every river follows its own course — acknowledging that differing local practices all draw from the same halachic source.

The Gemara now introduces the position of רבי שמעון בן לקיש. Reish Lakish ruled that the shechita is valid even if performed בחודא דכובעא — at the very tip of the hat. The anatomical image at work here is of the upper portion of the trachea, which, when viewed from the side, resembles a hat with a rising slope and a tip at the very top. Reish Lakish permitted shechita even at that uppermost point, well above the topmost ring. This position is framed against the background of רבי חנינא בן אנטיגנוס, whose opinion — as the Gemara will later develop — was that הגרמה presents no problem, meaning not that there is no upper limit whatsoever, but that the valid zone extends considerably higher than the other tannaitic opinions allow. The question driving much of this portion of the sugya is precisely how far upward that zone extends, and whether the fleshy, muscular area just below the tip is included or excluded.

### R. Yochanan Objects

Rabbi Yochanan objected to Reish Lakish's position, dismissing it with the word גיסא גיסא — too much, too far. Even granting that shechita above the top ring is permissible, the very tip of the hat is excessive. Rav Papa in the name of Rava provides the technical explanation: פגע בחיטי טרפה — if one encounters the strings, the muscular or ligamentous strands in that upper zone, the shechita is a טרפה. The concern, in other words, is not merely elevation in a general sense, but specifically whether the knife reaches into or above this band of fibrous tissue that sits just beneath the tip of the hat.

### Pagah Debate

The Gemara raises the question: what precisely does פגע mean in this context? Is it פגע ונגע בהן — that you actually touch and cut through the strings, as suggested by the verse וַיִּפְגַּע בּוֹ וַיָּמָת, which implies physical contact and impact? Or is it פגע ולא נגע — that you merely encounter the area without making contact, analogous to וַיִּפְגְּעוּ בוֹ מַלְאֲכֵי אֱלֹהִים, where the angels of God met Jacob without necessarily touching him? The halachic stakes of this question are whether the stringy area itself is included in the kosher zone or whether only cutting above that zone without touching it renders the shechita invalid.

### Shiyur B'Chite

An authoritative tradition is then transmitted in multiple chains: שיור בחיטי כשרה — if one leaves some of the strings remaining, it is kosher. This means that cutting through that muscular zone is permissible as long as one does not cut above it entirely. The tradition is confirmed by Rav Papa in the name of Rava, corroborated by Rav Meimar bar Mar Yenuka who heard the same ruling from Rav Chiya the son of Rav Avya, and further attested in a report that Ravina told Rav Ashi: Rav Sheman mi-Suvra reported that Mar Zutra came to their locale and expounded publicly that שיור בחיטי כשרה. This convergence of traditions reinforces the ruling. Mar bar Rav Ashi, however, states the matter in a formulation that Tosafot's reading presents as: פגע בחיטי כשרה שיור בחיטי טרפה — if the knife encounters the strings and cuts through them, it is kosher; if one leaves the entire string area above, having cut above it, it is a טרפה. The version of Rashi reverses the girsa, but following Tosafot, the principle is that penetrating into that muscular zone is the lower threshold of acceptable practice, while remaining entirely above it crosses into invalidity.

### Hilchata Ruling

The Gemara delivers its ruling: והלכתא משיפוע כובע ולמטה כשרה — the halacha is that shechita is kosher only from the slanting of the cap downward, and this is consistent with the principle of שיור בחיטי — that one must at minimum cut through that stringy zone below the tip. The top of the cap, where Reish Lakish permitted shechita, is excluded. The practical upshot is that while the position of רבי חנינא בן אנטיגנוס permits a substantially larger zone than the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda who limit one to the top ring and below, even that expanded zone does not reach all the way to the very tip of the anatomical structure. Shechita begins to be valid only from the point where the hat begins to slope.

## Rav Nachman

### RN Allows Cap

The Gemara then records: רב נחמן אכשר משיפוע כובע ולמטה — Rav Nachman permitted shechita from the angling of the cap downward, which is the same ruling just delivered as the halacha. One might wonder why the editor placed the final halachic ruling before Rav Nachman's statement, but the substance of their positions is identical.

### Fitting Opinions

Rav Chanan bar Rav Kitina challenged Rav Nachman: כמאן — like whom? This position, permitting the entire structure above the top ring, does not align with either of the two tannaitic positions in the Mishnah. Neither the Rabbis nor Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda would sanction shechita so high up, since both of their debates concern the top ring itself, and anything above that ring — which is this entire structure — they consider הגרמה.

### Tradition Defense

Rav Nachman responded with the celebrated declaration: אנא לא חילק ידענא ולא בילק ידענא אנא שמעתא ידענא — I know neither chilek nor bilek, I know my tradition. As Rashi explains, after entertaining the possibility that chilek referred to the Rabbis and bilek to Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda based on the connotations of those roots, he ultimately concludes that these are simply idiomatic expressions akin to "I don't know Joe, I don't know Schmoe" — I am not going to resolve the tannaitic dispute, I am simply transmitting what I received. And what he received, passed through Rabbi Chiya bar Zavda who said it in the name of Rabbi Chanina, and also transmitted as Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi said it in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, is: משיפוע כובע ולמטה כשרה — from the slanting of the cap downward it is kosher.

### Fitting Amoraim

The Gemara then proceeds to integrate the amoraic tradition with the tannaitic framework through the voice of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself: מוגרמת דרבנן כשרה לרבי יוסי ברבי יהודה — what the Rabbis define as invalid הגרמה is kosher according to Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. And what even Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda considers invalid — שחיטה conducted entirely above the top ring — is kosher according to Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus. The Gemara objects: פשיטא, this is obvious! The response is that one might have assumed that Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, who rejects הגרמה, was only rejecting the Rabbis' definition of הגרמה, not extending beyond Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position. The Gemara teaches that he goes further than all of them — his rejection of הגרמה encompasses every definition of that disqualification that any tanna articulates.

A further objection is raised: perhaps Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus indeed goes only as far as Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara answers: if so, when the Mishnah reported that Rabbi Yose bar Chanina testified regarding הגרמה and repeated that term, it would have said that he testified on "it" — meaning specifically the case of הגרמה as defined by one particular opinion. The use of הגרמה in a general sense implies that he dismissed every tanna's definition of הגרמה, giving a far wider zone for valid shechita than anyone else. Thus the position from the slanting of the cap downward corresponds to following Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, who is the most lenient of the tannaim on this question.

## Majority Debate

### Majority Principle

The Gemara now shifts from anatomical location to a more abstract conceptual question. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav Assi: מחלוקת בששחט שני שליש והגרים שליש — the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concerns the case where one has already shechted the first two-thirds of the trachea correctly and then, for the final third, moved the knife above the valid zone. The question is whether what occurs after the valid majority of the cut has been completed can invalidate the shechita retroactively.

It is important to remember that the measurement here is depth — how far through the hollow of the trachea the knife penetrates — not the linear length of the stroke. The שחיטה is halachically defined as cutting through the majority of the inner hollow of the trachea, and so the debate is framed in terms of what portion of that hollow was cut correctly.

### Rabbis Whole Act

The Rabbis hold: כולי שחיטה בעינן בטבעת גדולה — we require that the entire act of shechita be performed within the proper zone. Although it is true that performing the majority of the trachea's depth suffices to render the animal halachically slaughtered, if one is going to perform the entire act, every part of that act must be done correctly. Doing the majority correctly and then moving into the disqualified zone taints the shechita.

### RY Majority Stands

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda applies the principle of רוב ככולו — the majority is treated as the whole. Once the majority of the trachea has been cut through a valid act of shechita, the shechita is complete and whatever occurs afterward is legally irrelevant. The conceptual question underlying this debate, which has broader application across multiple פסולי שחיטה such as שהייה and דרסה, is whether the principle of "majority suffices" means that majority is all that is required — making everything after the majority legally invisible — or whether it means that majority serves as a stand-in for the whole, in which case if the whole thing is being done, the entirety of it must be performed correctly.

### Two-Thirds Good

This framing allows for the following resolution: in the case where the first two-thirds were done correctly and the final third was too high, that is the case of their debate. But if one considers the reverse scenario — beginning incorrectly — the analysis shifts entirely.

### First Third Bad

If the first third was too high and the final two-thirds were done correctly, דברי הכל פסול — everyone agrees it is invalid.

### Nafakat Chayuta

The reason is: דכי נפיק חיותא בעינן רוב בשחיטה וליכא — when the life-force of the animal departs, meaning at the moment when the majority of the trachea has been severed, we require that the majority have been cut through a valid act of shechita, and here it was not. When the knife reached the fifty-one percent mark — the point at which the animal's חיות leaves — half of the trachea had been cut through invalid הגרמה and half through valid shechita. The majority of the trachea had not been cut correctly at that critical moment, and so the shechita is universally invalid. The entire dispute, on this reading, is confined to the case where the majority was already done correctly and only the final portion was deficient.

## Chisda Challenge

### Reverse Case

Rav Chisda challenges this framing directly: אדרבה, say the opposite. Perhaps the debate between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concerns precisely the case where the first third was incorrect and the last two-thirds were done correctly — and it is the other case, where the first two-thirds were correct and the last third was not, that is universally agreed upon.

### Hole Analogy

According to Rav Chisda's reversal, Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda would say that beginning incorrectly is valid, and his reasoning is: מידי דהוי אחצי קנה פגום — it is analogous to a case where the trachea had a pre-existing natural injury occupying half its width. The halacha, as we will encounter, is that while small holes in the trachea generally render an animal a טרפה, the trachea is an organ that can sustain a natural cut of up to half its circumference without becoming a טרפה. If such a natural injury exists, and the shochet then cuts the remaining fraction needed to cross the fifty-one percent threshold, that constitutes a valid shechita — even though the shochet performed only a small portion of the cutting. The rationale is that the existing opening is incorporated into the act of shechita, redefining the trachea's remaining intact tissue as the relevant measure. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda applies the same logic to the case where the first third was cut above the valid zone: that invalid portion is comparable to the pre-existing hole, and the valid cutting of the remaining two-thirds, which does cross the majority threshold in the correct zone, suffices.

### Right Place Cut

The Rabbis disagree and draw a crucial distinction: התם מקום שחיטה — there, in the case of the natural pre-existing hole, the cut exists in the correct zone where shechita must take place. Even though the shochet only contributed the final increment, the majority of what was severed lies within the valid zone. הכא לאו מקום שחיטה — here, however, when the first portion of the cut was made above the valid zone, the cut in the invalid zone does not count. When the knife reaches the fifty-one percent depth of the trachea, the right zone has not yet had the majority of its width severed — only half of the correct area has been cut. The principle the Rabbis assert is not merely that the majority of the trachea must be cut, but that the majority of the trachea must be cut correctly, meaning in the right place. Starting above the zone and finishing below it leaves the valid zone only partially incised even at the moment the fifty-one percent depth is crossed.

Under Rav Chisda's reversal, it would follow that if one did the first two-thirds correctly and the final third too high, everyone would agree it is valid — since the principle of רובו של אחד כמוהו, the majority of a סימן counts as the whole, would mean we disregard entirely what happened after the majority was correctly performed.

## Rov Concept

### Part of Day

This conceptual divide — whether "majority suffices" means majority is the complete requirement or merely a substitute for the whole — has broad implications across halacha. An instructive parallel is the principle of מקצת היום ככולו, part of a day counts as a whole day. Consider the practical application in the laws of נידה: since women today are treated with the stringency of a זבה, who must count seven clean days, the beginning of the seventh day can halachically count as though the day is complete. A woman could therefore immerse in the mikvah at the beginning of the seventh day. Yet the practice is to wait until nightfall. The reason is that if she immerses during the day and then sees blood later that same day, the immersion is retroactively nullified — since the day did not in fact end cleanly, the beginning of the day never counted. This is a precise analogue to the question here: does the part-counting-as-whole work absolutely, rendering subsequent events irrelevant, or does it remain provisional and subject to invalidation by what follows?

### Majority Exclusive?

Rav Yosef raises a pointed question regarding Rav Chisda's reversal and the conclusion that if the first two-thirds were done correctly everyone would agree it is valid based on the principle of רובו של אחד כמוהו. מאן לימא לן דהוא רובא דהתם לרבי יוסי בר יהודה קתני לה — who says that this ruling about majority is not exclusively the position of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda? Perhaps it is only Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda who says that once the majority is done we disregard what follows, and the Rabbis would say we continue to care about the entirety of the act.

### Shechita Debate

Abaye responds: אטו כל רובי דאמר רבי יוסי בר יהודה קתני להו — does every invocation of majority throughout Shas reflect only Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position? The principle of majority is a broad and fundamental principle of halachic reasoning applied across countless contexts. The rejoinder is: אנא רובי דשחיטה קאמינא — I am speaking specifically about majority within the domain of shechita. There we explicitly know that the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda debate this very question. Perhaps different areas of halacha treat the concept of majority differently, but within shechita, the dispute over how majority functions is between these two positions, and so the question of whether the Rabbis would accept the principle of disregarding post-majority deficiencies remains genuinely open.

## Lishna Achrina

### Begin Wrong

The Gemara presents a לישנא אחרינא, a second formulation of Rav Huna's statement in the name of Rav. In this version, the debate is framed from the outset as: מחלוקת שהגרים שליש ושחט שני שליש — the dispute concerns the case where the first third was performed incorrectly and the last two-thirds were performed correctly. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda says it is valid: מידי דהוה אחתיכה דפגא — analogously to a pre-existing cut in the trachea, the invalid opening in the beginning is treated as though it were a natural wound, and the valid cutting of the remainder suffices. The Rabbis say it is invalid: התם מקום שחיטה הכא לאו מקום שחיטה — there, the pre-existing wound is in the proper location; here, the initial cut was not in the proper location. In the end the valid zone was not cut through its majority even though the majority depth of the trachea was reached.

## Middle Third Case

### Good-Bad-Good

The second formulation then posits: if the first two-thirds were performed correctly and only the final third was above the zone — אבל שחט שני שליש והגרים שליש, דברי הכל כשרה — everyone agrees it is valid, because the principle of רובו של אחד כמוהו applies and we do not concern ourselves with what occurred after the majority was correctly executed.

## Lishna Achrina (II)

### First Two OK

On this reading of the second formulation, the universal agreement on the validity of the case where the first two-thirds were correct is grounded in a precise reading of the phrase רובו של אחד כמוהו. One might note that the formulation says כמוהו rather than ככולו. Possibly the דיוק is that כמוהו means that the majority counts as itself — as sufficient — rather than as a stand-in for the whole, implying that once majority is achieved, the requirement is discharged and nothing thereafter can undermine it.

## Middle Third Case (II)

### Life at Middle

A further case now presents itself: הגרים שליש ושחט שליש והגרים שליש — the first third was too high, the middle third was performed correctly, and the final third was again too high. Here only one third of the entire act of shechita was in the valid zone, and that one valid third was confined to the middle. The majority of the cutting was done incorrectly. Yet crucially, the middle of the trachea — the fifty-one percent line — was crossed during the valid portion of the cut. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: כשרה — it is valid. The reasoning is: כי נפק חיותא בשחיטה קנפק — when the life-force of the animal departed, meaning when the knife passed the midpoint of the trachea, it was doing so through a valid act of shechita. The fact that the majority of the total cutting was performed in the wrong zone is subordinated to the fact that the critical moment — the crossing of the midpoint — occurred correctly.

### Majority Needed

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: טרפה — it is invalid. בעינן רובא בשחיטה וליכא — we require that the majority of the trachea be cut through a valid act of shechita, and here it was not. Only one-third of the trachea was cut correctly. The crossing of the midpoint alone is insufficient; what matters is not the midpoint but the majority of the trachea's depth, and most of that depth was severed in the wrong zone.

### Bad-Good-Bad

The Gemara then presents the mirror image of this case: שחט שליש והגרים שליש ושחט שליש — the first third was performed correctly, the middle third was above the zone, and the last third was performed correctly again. Here the majority of the cutting was done correctly but the middle third — where the fifty-one percent line sits — was crossed in the wrong zone.

### RY Reverses

Rav Yehuda now says: כשרה — it is valid. He reverses his ruling from the previous case. Since for him what matters is the majority of the trachea being cut correctly, and here the majority of the cutting was indeed in the valid zone, the shechita is good. The fact that the fifty-one percent line was crossed during the invalid middle portion does not matter to him.

### RN Objects

Rav Yehuda heard that Rav Huna ruled the opposite — טרפה for this mirror case — and was offended, saying: when I say טרפה, he says כשר; when I say כשר, he says טרפה. Rav Huna acknowledged that Rav Yehuda was right to be offended: שפיר קא מקפיד. He offered two reasons. First, חדא איהו שמיע ליה מיניה דרב ואנא לא שמיע לי — in one of these cases, Rav Yehuda transmitted the ruling in the name of Rav, while Rav Huna did not; Rav Yehuda had the authority of a direct tradition from Rav. Second, ועוד האיכא רובא בשחיטה — moreover, in the mirror case where Rav Huna declared טרפה, there is actually a majority of the trachea that was cut correctly, and Rav Huna should have acknowledged that. The implied criticism is that Rav Huna's position commits him to a standard inconsistent with his first ruling.

### Don't Reverse

Rav Chisda then stepped in to warn Rav Huna: לא תהדר בך — do not reverse yourself. His caution is precise: דאם כן מסתרת לה לקמייתא — if you reverse yourself on this case, you implicitly undermine your first position. Rav Huna's first ruling, that the case with the valid middle third is כשר, was premised on the principle that כי נפק חיותא בהכשר קנפיק — when the life-force departed, meaning when the fifty-one percent line was crossed, it was crossed through a valid act of shechita. That is what makes it valid, not the majority of the cutting. Now in the mirror case, where the first and last thirds are valid and the middle is not, כי נפק חיותא באגרמה קנפיק — when the life-force departed, the fifty-one percent line was crossed during the invalid middle portion. If Rav Huna's criterion is whether the midpoint is crossed correctly, he must say this mirror case is invalid. To say otherwise would be to abandon his own rationale. Rav Chisda urges him: be consistent; do not let the pressure of Rav Yehuda's offense lead you to reverse a well-reasoned position.

## Comb Shechita

### Asked RN

When Rav Nachman visited Sura, the scholars there posed to him the question of שחט שליש והגרים שליש ושחט שליש — the first and last thirds in the valid zone, the middle third above it.

### Masreik Position

Rav Nachman responded: לאו היינו דרבי אלעזר בר מניומי — is this not the case of Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi? Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi had said: שחיטה העשויה כמסרק כשרה — a shechita performed like a comb, meaning with an up-and-down jagged motion, is valid. Rav Nachman applies this to the case at hand, where the knife moved in and out of the valid zone in a comb-like pattern, and rules it kosher.

### Kosher Zone Only

The Gemara challenges this analogy: ודילמא במקום שחיטה — perhaps Rabbi Elazar bar Minyumi's ruling about comb-like shechita refers only to a case where the jagged motion remained entirely within the kosher zone? That would not address a case where the knife moved outside the valid zone. The Gemara then asks: if comb-like shechita within the valid zone were the entire teaching, what would be the חידוש? One might have thought that בעינן שחיטה מפורעת — a valid shechita requires an exposed, straight, unobstructed cut, such that a jagged line within the zone where portions of the cut are recessed and not directly visible in a straight line would be invalid. The ruling teaches that this is not required, and an irregular line within the kosher zone is acceptable. But this leaves open the question of a case where portions of the cut are outside the kosher zone entirely.

## Pre-Existing Cuts

### RK Questions

Rabbi Abba was sitting in the back row while Rav Kahana sat before Rav Yehuda in a shiur. Rav Kahana posed the following sequence of questions to Rav Yehuda regarding pre-existing cuts in the trachea.

### Front vs Back

Rav Yehuda responded to Rav Kahana's questions as follows: שחט במקום נקב — if the shochet cut in the place where there was already a pre-existing hole in the front of the trachea, such that he simply extended what was already there and crossed the majority threshold — שחיטתו כשרה, it is valid. שחט ופגע בו נקב — if, however, the shochet was cutting from the front and encountered a pre-existing hole in the back of the trachea, meaning the knife reached the midpoint by meeting the back hole rather than by cutting through the front wall to beyond the center — שחיטתו פסולה, it is invalid. Rabbi Abba then went and reported this distinction to Rabbi Elazar, who brought it before Rabbi Yochanan.

### Goy Analogy

Rabbi Elazar offered an explanation of why the front hole makes it valid while the back hole makes it invalid: שחט במקום נקב נעשה כמי ששחט עובד כוכבים וגמר ישראל — when one cuts where there is already a front hole, it is as if a non-Jew began the shechita and a Jew finished it. The first half of the opening was not the act of the shochet; he picked up from the midpoint and completed the majority correctly. So long as the critical crossing of the fifty-one percent line was done by the shochet in the valid zone, the shechita is valid. שחט ופגע בו נקב — if one encountered a back hole, נעשה כמי ששחט ישראל וגמר עובד כוכבים — it is as if a Jew began and a non-Jew finished. The shochet cut up to the midpoint, but the midpoint was crossed by connecting with the back hole rather than by a genuine cutting act through the valid tissue. The fifty-one percent line was not crossed by the shochet, and so the shechita is invalid.

### Reject Analogy

Rabbi Yochanan was unimpressed: עובד כוכבים עובד כוכבים — goy, goy. As Rashi notes, this was a rhetorical dismissal rather than a personal insult to Rabbi Elazar; Rabbi Yochanan was mocking the analogy as inadequate, telling him not to explain it to him with this kind of reasoning.

### Whole Act Defense

Rava explains why the objection is well-founded: שפיר קרי ליה עובד כוכבים עובד כוכבים — it was correct to taunt him, because the analogy is genuinely flawed. In the actual case of a non-Jew and Jew, the invalidity is clear: מדאית ליה לישראל למשחט רובא ולא שחט — the Jew was obligated to shecht the majority and did not, and so when the life-force departed, כי נפקי חיותא בידא דעובד כוכבים קא נפקי — it departed through the act of the non-Jew, who cut the fifty-one percent line. The non-Jew's act brought the animal to its death.

But here, by contrast, מכדי שוחט שחיט ליה — the shochet himself performed the entire act. There was no other actor. מה לי במקום נקב מה לי פגע בו נקב — what is the difference to me whether the shochet cut through a front hole or encountered a back hole? In both cases the shochet did everything, and there is no other actor whose intervention could be said to have taken over the act. The analogy to a non-Jew and a Jew breaks down precisely because in the pre-existing hole cases, a single person performs the complete act of shechita, meaning the existing hole should be incorporated into that actor's shechita regardless of whether it is in the front or the back. This creates a logical tension with Rav Yehuda's own earlier rulings, since if what matters is the majority of the trachea being cut correctly, and the shochet personally cut the majority of the trachea correctly in the front-hole case (even though he only added a small amount), the legal framework would need to treat the existing hole as part of his act of shechita — and that logic should apply equally to the back hole.

## Shechita vs Melikah

### Side Shechita

The Gemara closes this amud with a Mishnah that introduces a systematic contrast between שחיטה and מליקה. השוחט מן הצדדים — one who shechts from the sides of the neck — שחיטתו כשרה, the shechita is valid, because cutting from the side reaches the trachea and esophagus without severing the spine or otherwise creating an additional בסיס פסול. המולק מן הצדדים — one who performs מליקה from the sides — מליקתו פסולה, it is invalid. Melikah is the act performed by the כהן with his thumbnail on bird sacrifices, and it specifically requires cutting from the back of the neck through the spine; the side is not the correct location for that act.

### Back Melikah

Correspondingly: השוחט מן העורף — one who shechts from the back of the neck, the עורף — שחיטתו פסולה, the shechita is invalid, because cutting from the back breaks through the spine, creating an independent פסול of a broken spine. המולק מן העורף — one who performs מליקה from the back of the neck — מליקתו כשרה, it is valid, because that is precisely the required method.

### Front Shechita

השוחט מן הצוואר — one who shechts from the front of the throat — שחיטתו כשרה, obviously valid; this is the paradigmatic act of shechita. המולק מן הצוואר — one who performs מליקה from the front — מליקתו פסולה, it is invalid.

### Summary

The Mishnah draws the governing principle: שכל העורף כשר למליקה — the entire back of the neck is valid for מליקה, וכל הצוואר כשר לשחיטה — the entire throat, which the Mishnah implies includes the sides as well, is valid for שחיטה. The conclusion: כשר בשחיטה פסול במליקה — what is valid for שחיטה, meaning the throat and sides, is invalid for מליקה; כשר במליקה פסול בשחיטה — what is valid for מליקה, meaning the back of the neck, is invalid for שחיטה. This Mishnah opens a series of mutually exclusive categories that will be the subject of the discussion going forward.
