# Niddah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Checking Principle

### Kol Hayad Hamarbeh

The second *perek* of Niddah, known as *Kol Hayad*, opens on 13a with a striking apodictic statement: *kol hayad hamarbeh livdok b'nashim meshubachas, uv'anashim tikatzeitz* — every hand that checks more frequently among women is to be praised, while among men, it should be cut off. The parallel to the Passover Haggadah's formulation is unmistakable: just as *kol hamarbeh lesaper b'yitziat mitzrayim harei zeh meshubach* praises whoever elaborates more in recounting the Exodus, so too here *kol hamarbeh* in checking is deemed praiseworthy — but only for women. For men, the opposite extreme is declared: the hand should be severed, which is presumably hyperbolic, but expresses unambiguously that men should not check more than is strictly necessary.

This stands in an interesting contrast to Shmuel's position, encountered previously, that in an era when *taharos* are not in practice, even checking before and after marital relations is forbidden because it generates undue anxiety. Within the context of these *mishnayot*, however — where women were engaged with *taharos* and checked twice daily, morning and evening, as well as before and after intercourse — the principle is clear: the more a woman checks, the better.

### Hargasha Distinction

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mai shena nashim u-mai shena anashim* — what is the difference between women and men? The answer given is: *nashim lav bnot hargasha ninhu* — women are not "daughters of sensation," they do not possess *hargasha* — and therefore the more they check, the more praiseworthy. *Anashim debnei hargasha ninhu* — men, by contrast, are "children of sensation" — and therefore *tikatzeitz*, it should be cut off. The concern underlying the entire discussion, as the *Gemara* will make explicit, is *shichvat zera levatala* — the ejaculation of semen outside the context of marital intercourse, i.e., masturbation leading to emission. Men have *hargasha*; women, the *Gemara* claims, do not — and that asymmetry drives the entire ruling.

### Rashi on Hargasha

Rashi explains *hargasha* as follows: *she-mizdazin avarav keshe-mis-chamem v'ro'eh keri* — a man's limbs, and presumably his genitals specifically, tremble and become excited when he heats up and reaches the point of seminal emission. That physical arousal leading to ejaculation is what the *Gemara* calls *hargasha*. Rashi does not explain symmetrically what is absent in women, but the implication seems to be that women lack the same mechanism of seminal emission and therefore do not fall within the scope of concern.

### Female Mazria

This framing raises a significant question, however, because the *Gemara* elsewhere does acknowledge a concept of female *mazria* — the idea that women emit seed. The ancient "two-seed theory" of conception, which held that both male and female seed were necessary for fertilization, is reflected in a number of *Gemaras*. The well-known *Rashi* on the verse *isha ki tazria* — "when a woman emits seed" — quotes the *Gemara*'s ruling that *isha mazria techila yoledet zachar*, a woman who emits seed first gives birth to a male, placing female orgasm squarely at the center of the biological discussion. The *Gemara* indeed discusses the husband's responsibility to ensure that his wife achieves orgasm and is *mazria*, precisely because conception was understood to require it.

It is worth pausing on the historical irony here: although this biological framework reflects a male-centered worldview in many respects, it had the practical effect of making female sexual satisfaction a *halakhic* concern, since conception — and thereby the fulfillment of *pru u'rvu* — was thought to depend upon it. The subsequent discovery that female orgasm is biologically unnecessary for conception led, historically, to a sharp decline in cultural attention to female pleasure. Once the *Gemara* did acknowledge *isha mazria*, one would need to ask why it is nonetheless unconcerned with the possibility of women becoming aroused during checking.

### Women Off Radar

There are two possible ways to read the *Gemara*'s position here, even before arriving at *Tosafot*'s explanation. The first, most literal reading of the *hargasha* framework, is that the *Gemara* believed physical stimulation of the genital area during routine checking would not produce orgasm in women — or at least not as immediately or sustainably as it would in men. Perhaps the *Gemara* was simply not concerned that the brief act of checking would produce that effect in women in the same way it would in men. The second possibility, made more explicit in the *Rosh*'s reading of *Rashi*, is that even if arousal might occur, it would not lead to *zera* in the relevant sense — either because female *zera* is not the concern of *zera levatala*, or because *zera levatala* is understood as a prohibition applying specifically to male seminal emission and simply does not encompass whatever the female body emits.

Either way, the *Gemara* is not concerned with female arousal in this context, only with male arousal. One might observe — and this is a point worth sitting with — that women being "off the radar screen" in this discussion has a double edge. It results in fewer restrictions for women, but only because their sexual experience is not considered a subject of *halakhic* concern in the same way that men's is. The more permissive outcome arrives via a kind of obliviousness rather than through any positive recognition.

### Tosafot Pru Urvu

*Tosafot*, in the name of *Rabbeinu Tam*, offers a fundamentally different explanation that is not grounded in biology at all. *Tosafot* argues that women are simply not obligated in *zera levatala* because, in his view, the prohibition of *zera levatala* is a function of the *mitzvah* of *pru u'rvu* — procreation — which, as established in *Gemara Yevamos*, is an obligation incumbent upon men and not upon women. On this reading, *zera levatala* is prohibited because it represents the destruction of seed that could have contributed to procreation, or at least an act of emission entirely outside any procreative framework. Since women are not commanded in *pru u'rvu*, they are equally not included in the prohibition of *zera levatala*.

## Zera Levatala

### Women Destroy Seed

*Tosafot*'s framework has direct practical implications. If the prohibition of *zera levatala* is rooted in *pru u'rvu*, then a woman could potentially destroy male seed without violating any prohibition of her own — since the obligation being undermined is his, not hers. This raises the question of whether a woman using a *moch* or other contraceptive device that causes the male seed to be expelled from her body is considered *mashchis* — destroying — the male *zera*, and whether that constitutes a violation on her part.

### Moch Diaphragm

*Tosafot* raises this question explicitly in the context of the *moch* — a pad used internally, most analogous to a modern diaphragm — which would physically prevent the seed from leading to conception. If the woman uses it, the man has not performed an act of *hashchasas zera* himself, but she is doing so on his behalf or in a way that undermines the potential fruit of his seed. According to *Tosafot*'s framework, since she is not herself obligated in *pru u'rvu*, she would not be liable for *hashchasas zera*, and contraceptive use of this kind would therefore not constitute a prohibition on her part. This has significant practical implications for *halakhic* discussions of birth control.

### Nature Zera

Understanding *Tosafot*'s position also clarifies a broader conceptual question: what precisely is the problem with masturbation and seminal emission? If the concern is fundamentally about *pru u'rvu* — about the destruction of something that could have led to procreation — then the problem is not about self-pleasuring as such, but about the destruction of *zera* in a non-procreative context. On this understanding, the act of *hashchasas zera* is the violation, and a woman could theoretically commit that violation even without any act of masturbation — for example, by using a spermicide or diaphragm. Conversely, it also means that *Tosafot*'s approach would not in itself prohibit female masturbation, since there is no male *zera* being destroyed.

It must be said, however, that the *Gemara* is not monolithic on this point. As the sugya develops, there are clearly two distinct thematic concerns present: the destruction of seed (*zera*), and the self-pleasuring aspect — the concern about sexual indulgence outside of a relational, marital context. Some of the *aggadic* material that follows is clearly *zera*-focused; other formulations point more directly to the problem of self-indulgence, arousal, and sexual obsession as concerns in their own right.

### Female Masturbation

The question of female masturbation is not clearly addressed in the *Gemara*, and its *halakhic* status depends heavily on which of the above frameworks one adopts. If the entire concern is about *zera levatala* — the destruction of male seed — then there is no direct prohibition applicable to women, who neither emit seed in the relevant sense nor are obligated in *pru u'rvu*. If, on the other hand, the problem is understood to be self-pleasuring as such, the idea that it is a distinctly male concern would rely on the *Gemara*'s biological assumption that women do not experience *hargasha* through such stimulation — an assumption that, as noted, may reflect a kind of obliviousness to female sexual experience rather than any principled exclusion.

The situation is somewhat uncomfortable from a contemporary vantage point. A *she'elah* about whether a woman has any *halakhic* problem with masturbation is indeed asked in practice. The answer that it presents no problem, if grounded in the *Gemara*'s claim that women are not *bnot hargasha*, arrives through a premise that is arguably dismissive of female sexuality rather than respectful of it. A more lenient result through a less-than-flattering route. The *halakhic* outcome may be permissive, but the underlying reasoning does not reflect any positive recognition of the female experience.

## Checking Methods

### Forbidden Checking

The *Gemara* now turns to refine the *Mishnah*'s ruling. One might ask: if the problem with men checking is so severe, why does the *Mishnah* speak specifically of *kol hamarbeh* — doing it more than necessary? Even doing it at all should be a problem. The *Gemara* responds: the *Mishnah* is saying that even the first instance is not *meshubach* for men, and the additional qualifier *hamarbeh* is directed at women — the more women check, the more praiseworthy.

The *Gemara* then qualifies: *bameh devarim amurim l'inyan shichvat zera* — when is this ruling that men should not check applicable? When the concern is seminal emission. *Aval l'inyan zav* — but when the concern is a *zav*-type flow, a non-seminal penile discharge that renders one *tamei* — *af hu meshubach k'nashim*, even a man is praised for checking, just as women are.

### Ziva Exception

Why does the *ziva* case differ? The *Rosh* offers two complementary explanations. First, when a man is checking for seminal emission, there is a self-contradictory quality to doing so through genital manipulation, since the very act of checking risks producing the emission one is trying to detect. With *ziva*, by contrast, the checking does not contradict itself — the flow being examined is not caused or worsened by the act of examination. Second, the *Rosh* notes that seminal emission typically comes with a bodily sensation — a man generally knows when he has ejaculated — and therefore the necessity for physical examination is much reduced. *Ziva*, which flows silently and without sensation, requires more deliberate examination, and so the necessity overrides the incidental concern.

This also responds to the question one might raise: does the concern about *zera levatala* override all concerns about *tumah*? Not at all — it only overrides when the checking is either self-defeating or unnecessary. When there is genuine need and no self-contradiction, the checking is permitted and even praiseworthy.

### Cloth vs. Hand

Even when it comes to checking for *zera*, the *Gemara* notes that there are permissible means of doing so. One may check *betzror uvacheres* — with a stone or a shard of pottery — that is, by examination without direct manual contact. The question arises about checking with a cloth: a *baraita* permits checking *bematlit uvechol davar sherotzeh* — with a cloth or whatever one wishes, as long as one does not use one's hand directly. The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking a ruling of Abaye, stated elsewhere: the permissibility applies to *matlit avah* — a thick cloth — which does not produce the level of stimulation that a soft cloth would. The case of the soft cloth would be impermissible for checking even for *zera*.

### Abaye Rava Holding

The *Gemara* then returns to the context in which Abaye originally made this ruling. The *Mishnah* later discusses a man who is eating *trumah* and feels his limbs beginning to tremble — *hirgish shenizdaz'u avarav* — a sign that he may be on the verge of seminal emission. The *Mishnah* says he should hold his penis to suppress the emission and swallow the *trumah* already in his mouth so that he will remain *tahor* and not be handling *trumah* while becoming *tamei*.

The *Gemara* objects: does holding not make things worse, in light of Rabbi Eliezer's teaching that *kol ha'ochez b'amato u'mashtin ke'ilu meivi mabul le'olam* — anyone who holds his penis while urinating brings a flood to the world? Abaye says: the distinction lies in how one holds — with a thick cloth (*b'mapatz ava*), one applies pressure to suppress the emission without causing stimulation, and this is permissible.

Rava goes further: *afilu teima b'mapatz racha* — even with a soft cloth it would not be a problem here, because *keivan d'akar akar* — once the emission has already uprooted itself, it has uprooted. The concern about stimulation is that it causes semen to flow; here the semen is already in the process of flowing, and one cannot make the situation worse. Abaye disagrees: *chaishinan dilma ati l'oseifi* — we are concerned that continued stimulation will cause more semen to be emitted, even if some would have come out regardless. Rava is not concerned about this.

### Eye-Tears Analogy

A *baraita* challenges Rava's position directly, presenting an analogy: the act of stimulation leading to seminal emission is compared to placing a finger in one's eye — *kol zman she'etzba ba'ayin, ayin medama'at v'chozeret u'medama'at* — as long as the finger remains in the eye, tears flow and keep flowing. By the same principle, continued stimulation causes continued emission; therefore even at the point where some semen has already begun to flow, further stimulation risks increasing the total amount emitted.

Rava's response is that *achmumei v'hadar achmumei b'sha'ata lo shechei'ach* — repeated heating and re-heating at the same moment is not common. That is, the physiological dynamic at the precise moment of emission is not the same as the gradual escalation the eye-tears analogy describes. At that immediate instant, the body cannot meaningfully increase the emission through additional stimulation in the way the analogy suggests. The *Gemara* thus records a genuine dispute between Abaye and Rava as to whether even at the point of emission one must be concerned about worsening the situation.

## Rabbi Eliezer Urination

### Brings Flood

The *Gemara* now examines Rabbi Eliezer's statement directly. *Kol ha'ochez b'amato u'mashtin ke'ilu meivi mabul le'olam* — anyone who holds his penis while urinating is as though he brings a flood to the world. Rashi connects this to the verse *ki shicheit kol basar darko al ha'aretz* — all flesh corrupted its way upon the earth — which *Chazal* understood to refer to interspecies breeding and the emission of seed in improper contexts. The sin that brought about the *mabul* was precisely the corruption of the sexual order, and Rabbi Eliezer invokes that association to express the severity of this act.

### Urine Splatter

The *chachamim* push back: *v'halo nitfei nitfin al raglav v'nir'eh k'karut shofcha* — if a man urinates without holding, droplets will fall on his legs, and people will see the stains and think he is *karut shofcha*, that his genitals are deformed or defective. Moreover, *v'nimtza motzi la'az al banav shehem mamzerim* — this will cast suspicion on the legitimacy of his children, since onlookers will assume a man in such condition could not have fathered them, and his children will acquire a reputation as *mamzerim*, which would render them unmarriageable to ordinary Jews.

### Wickedness Priority

Rabbi Eliezer's response is extraordinary: *mutav sheyotzi la'az al banav shehem mamzerim, v'al ya'aseh atzmo rasha sha'ah achat lifnei HaMakom* — it is better that his children carry the stigma of *mamzerut* than that he be wicked in God's eyes for even one instant. Several things are striking here. First, Rabbi Eliezer regards the momentary sexual stimulation involved in this act — not even an act of *zera levatala*, simply the physical holding itself — as so severe that it outweighs the reputational destruction of one's own children. Second, he is not speaking of shame falling upon himself but upon others — his children bear the consequence of his moral scrupulousness. Rabbi Eliezer's position is extreme and, as the *Gemara* makes clear, is not how we rule in this particular case. But it has been sufficiently integrated into the discourse that its implications for other situations remain significant.

### Alternative Solutions

In a *baraita*, Rabbi Eliezer first offers a practical solution to the dilemma the *chachamim* presented: *efshar ya'amod adam b'makom gavo'ah v'yashtin* — one can stand on a high place and urinate so the urine does not splash back, *o yashtin b'afar tichuach* — or one can urinate on soft earth that absorbs the urine without causing splatter. Only when the *chachamim* press him — what if neither option is available? — does he give his stark final answer: even then, he would rather his children be considered *mamzerim* than that he be *rasha sha'ah achat lifnei HaMakom*. The *Gemara* frames the underlying reason explicitly: *mipnei shemotzi shichvas zera levatala* — because of the concern about wasteful seminal emission. Though it must be noted that in the urination case there is no *zera* at all — which suggests that for Rabbi Eliezer, even the momentary stimulation itself, entirely apart from actual emission, is the concern.

## Seed to Theology

### Death Penalty

The *Gemara* now connects Rabbi Eliezer's statement to that of Rabbi Yochanan, who is a first-generation *amora* and who sharpens the concern about *zera* into the harshest possible formulation. *Kol hamotzi shichvas zera levatala chayav misah* — anyone who emits semen wastefully is deserving of death. The proof-text is the verse regarding Onan: *vayeira be'einei Hashem asher asah, vayamas gam oso* — it was evil in the eyes of God what he did, and He killed him as well, just as He had killed Er.

### Onan Misreading

It is worth pausing here because the name "onanism" and its identification with *zera levatala* represents a widespread assumption that is not straightforwardly supported by the *pshat* of the *pesukim*. The plain meaning of the text is that Onan's sin was his refusal to fulfill *yibbum* — the levirate obligation to his sister-in-law — and that *mashchis artzah*, "spilling on the ground," was merely the mechanism by which he avoided that obligation, not necessarily the intrinsic sin deserving death. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* reads it as a proof-text for *zera levatala*, and this reading became the dominant framework in subsequent tradition, Christian and Jewish alike.

### Homunculus Murder

The *Gemara* continues: *Rabbi Yitzchak and Rabbi Ammi amar ke'ilu shofech damim* — it is as though he has committed murder. The proof-text is from the prophet Yeshayahu: *hannechamim ba'eilim tachat kol eitz ra'anan, shochtei hayeladim bannechalim* — those who warm themselves among the trees, slaughtering children in the wadies. The *Gemara* reads *shochtei* with a *sin* instead of a *shin*, yielding *sochtei* — squeezing out the children. Rashi also emphasizes the element of warming, connecting it to sexual arousal. The framing is unmistakable: emission of semen is treated as the destruction of potential — or even actual — life.

This reading is reinforced by what was an ancient biological assumption: that male semen contained miniature human beings — the homunculus — and that wasteful emission therefore literally destroyed nascent life. This theory, widespread in the ancient world, may explain why the *Gemara* treats male *zera* with special severity even while acknowledging female *zera*; the homunculus was located in the male seed, not the female.

### Idolatry Theme

Rav Assi then adds: *ke'ilu oved avodat kochavim* — it is as though one has worshipped idols. The verbal link is through the phrase *tachat kol eitz ra'anan*, which appears both in the context of Onan and in the context of idol worship on the high mountains and under every leafy tree. But beyond the verbal link, the *Gemara* here begins to articulate a second major theme: that giving in to sexual desires is a form of self-surrender to the *yetzer hara* that, once initiated, escalates. The connection to idolatry represents the idea that such self-indulgence ultimately leads to a complete casting off of the yoke of divine authority. The three cardinal sins — bloodshed, sexual immorality, and idolatry — are now all present in this discussion.

## Roof Story

### Shmuel Permits

The *Gemara* now recounts a narrative. Rav Yehuda and Shmuel were standing on the roof of the synagogue of *Shaf ve-Yativ* in Neharde'a — a synagogue said to have been built using stones transported from the *Beit HaMikdash* when the exiles were sent to Babylonia. Rav Yehuda said to Shmuel: *tzarich ani le-hashtin* — I need to urinate. Shmuel replied: *achoz be-amatcha ve-hashten la-chutz* — go ahead, hold yourself and urinate over the side of the roof. The *Gemara* immediately asks: how could he permit this? Has it not been taught that Rabbi Eliezer says it is like bringing a flood to the world?

### Fear Distraction

Abaye provides the resolution: *asa'uhu ke-voleshet* — they treated it like the case of the invading army (*boleshet*). The reference is to a *Mishnah* about wine left in a city occupied by soldiers: during peacetime encampment, open barrels are forbidden as *yayin nesech* because the soldiers had opportunity to pour libations; during active battle, even open barrels are permitted because *ein lahem pnai le-nasech* — they have no opportunity to attend to libations. The principle is that intense fear or preoccupation prevents the kind of focused intentional action that would be problematic. Applied here: *keivan de-ba'itei, lo atu le-hirhurei* — since a person urinating from the edge of a roof is afraid of falling, he will not enter into sexual thoughts. The physical stimulation does not occur in a mental vacuum; it is the combination of physical contact and a particular mental state that produces arousal. Fear disrupts that mental state entirely.

### Piety Context

The *Gemara* raises a further question: what precisely was the source of fear in this particular case? Several possibilities are offered. It was nighttime on a rooftop, making the fear of falling more acute. Alternatively, Rav Yehuda was in the presence of his teacher Shmuel, which itself was distracting. Or, the divine presence resting upon a synagogue precluded sexual thought altogether.

But the *Gemara* also notes that the case of Rav Yehuda is exceptional for an entirely different reason: Shmuel said of Rav Yehuda, *ein zeh yelud isha* — this person is as if not born of woman, meaning he was on a level of piety so extraordinary that sexual thoughts were essentially not a concern for him under any circumstances. What is permitted for Rav Yehuda in this particular case is not necessarily an indication of what is permitted generally.

### Married Status

Another possible reason for leniency in this case: *nasui haya* — he was married. Rav Nachman states: *im haya nasui mutar* — if the person was married, it is permissible. The reasoning appears to be not that a married man is incapable of improper desire, but that having a legitimate outlet means the concern about incidental arousal during a necessary act is reduced to a level that does not warrant prohibition. The *Gemara* is making a formal legal distinction here, acknowledging that context matters. This is not, it must be emphasized, a license for *hotza'at zera levatala*; the discussion remains confined to incidental physical contact during a necessary and legitimate act, and the question is whether we impose an additional stringency against even that.

### Below Crown

Finally, Shmuel's ruling to Rav Yehuda may also be explained by a specific teaching of Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *gvul yesh lo* — there is a defined limit. *Me-ha'atara u-lemata mutar* — from the crown of the penis downward, toward the tip, is permissible; *me-ha'atara u-lemala assur* — from the crown upward, toward the body, is forbidden. The distinction is anatomical: manual contact in the lower zone does not produce the level of stimulation that the upper zone does, and therefore it does not raise the same concern. Shmuel may have been directing Rav Yehuda to proceed within these parameters.

## Intentional Hardening

### Maksheh Atzmo

The *Gemara* records a statement of Rav: *ha-mekasheh atzmo lada'at yehei benidui* — one who intentionally causes himself to become erect should be placed in *nidui*. Significantly, this formulation does not speak of *zera levatala* at all; the act of deliberate arousal, independent of any emission, is condemned in the strongest terms. *Tosafot* notes that *yehei benidui* does not mean excommunication is automatically incurred, but rather that it is advice to the rabbinic authorities of the generation to impose *nidui* — a formal expression of how severe the act is considered.

### Evil Inclination

Why *nidui* rather than simply calling it *assur*? The *Gemara* explains: *de-ka magrei yetzer hara a-nafshei* — because he is inciting the *yetzer hara* against himself. This goes beyond ordinary transgression. When a person deliberately arouses himself, he is not merely giving in to desire; he is actively cultivating and amplifying that desire, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. Rabbi Ammi adds: *nikra avaryan, she-kach hu omanuto shel yetzer hara* — he is called a sinner, because this is precisely the craft of the *yetzer hara*: today it says give in on this small point, tomorrow it demands another concession, and day by day it draws the person further until *lemachar omer lo lech va-avod avodah zarah* — eventually it leads him to idol worship. The *yetzer hara* here is not just about sexuality; it is a metaphor for the complete surrender of the self to desire and the consequent abandonment of all restraint.

### Sexual Thought

Rav Ammi further states: *kol ha-mevi atzmo lidei hirhur, ein machnisim oto bi-mechitzato shel Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — anyone who intentionally brings himself to sexual thought is not admitted into God's inner circle in the world to come. The proof derives from a *gezerah shavah*: the phrase *vayeira be'einei Hashem* appears in the episode of Onan, and a parallel verse states *ki lo El chafetz rasha attah, lo yegurcha ra* — you are not a God who desires evil, evil shall not dwell with You. The condemnation of *hirhur* is notable because it does not require any physical act, let alone seminal emission. This may reflect the view, articulated with particular force by the *Rambam* in *Moreh Nevukhim*, that the intellectual and spiritual life is the highest dimension of human existence, and sexual obsession corrupts that dimension by directing the mind toward base concerns. One should note that *mevi atzmo* implies intentional cultivation of such thought, not an involuntary thought that passes through the mind.

## Adultery Hand

### Menahef Ba-Yad

Rabbi Elazar states: *eilu ha-mena'afim ba-yad* — these are those who commit adultery with the hand, explaining the verse *yedeichem damim maleu* — your hands are full of blood. The phrase *mena'ef ba-yad* is revealing. *Damim* again evokes the destruction-of-life theme; but the framing of the act as *ni'uf*, adultery, introduces a distinct dimension. The classical prohibition of adultery concerns sex with another man's wife. Here the *Gemara* deploys the language of *ni'uf* to characterize masturbation, conveying the idea that sexuality is by its nature relational — it is an act between persons — and that self-stimulation constitutes a kind of betrayal of that relational essence. Sex with oneself is, in this framing, a form of adultery against the legitimate relational context of sexuality.

### IVF Problem

This formulation carries significant contemporary *halakhic* weight. Rav Moshe Feinstein, more than many other *poskim*, placed great emphasis on the *mena'ef ba-yad* prohibition as an independent concern beyond *zera levatala*. In the context of IVF, the question arises whether a man may masturbate in order to produce semen for the purpose of impregnating his wife — a use that is manifestly not *levatala*, since conception is the explicit goal. Many *poskim* permit this given the *mitzvah* of *pru u'rvu* and the absence of the defining element of wastefulness. Rav Moshe Feinstein, however, maintained that the problem of *mena'ef ba-yad* applies independently of whether the semen is ultimately used productively: the act of procurement itself, through manual self-stimulation, remains prohibited. This position makes *halakhic* compliance in IVF situations extremely difficult. Institutions like *Puah* that seek to operate under *halakhic* guidance have had to grapple seriously with these constraints, and Rav Moshe's approach, emphasizing the *mena'ef ba-yad* theme, represents a distinctly rigorous position within the spectrum of *posekim*.

### Any Limb

The *tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael* concludes with a broader formulation: *lo tinaf* — you shall not commit adultery — means *lo tehei becha ni'uf, bein ba-yad bein ba-regel* — there should be no aspect of adultery within you, whether by hand or by foot, meaning through any act of self-stimulation by any part of the body. The relational conception of sexuality is here extended to its logical conclusion: any act that constitutes self-directed sexual stimulation, regardless of the means, falls within the ambit of *ni'uf*.

## Mashiah Held Back

### Converts Blemish

The *Gemara* presents a *baraita*: *hagvarim* — a category of people — *meakvin et hamashiach*, cause the delay of the *Mashiah*. Three groups are listed. The first is *geirim* — converts. This follows Rabbi Chelbo's position, *kashim geirim le-Yisrael ke-sapachat* — converts are as difficult for Israel as a scab — reflecting a view, by no means universally shared in the *Gemara*, that mass conversion weakens the national character of the Jewish people and delays redemption.

### Children Zera

The second group is *ha-misachakim be-tinokot* — those who engage in sexual play with children. The *Gemara* initially considers whether this refers to *mishkav zachar* with young boys — actual homosexual intercourse — but dismisses this because such an act is *benei sekila*, punishable by stoning, a far more severe category. The *Gemara* therefore reads it as *derech evarim* — sexual contact not constituting technical intercourse, oral or manual. The *Gemara*'s concern, notably, is framed entirely in terms of *zera levatala* — the seed is wasted in a non-procreative context — without engaging the dimension of child abuse that would be central to any contemporary discussion.

### Young Girls Souls

The third group: *denasivi katanot* — those who marry young girls. Again the *Gemara*'s concern is not framed in terms of harm to the child, but in terms of procreative consequence. *Delav benot olodei ninhu* — since these girls are not yet of childbearing age, the marital relations conducted with them cannot result in conception. It must be stated clearly, however, that the *Gemara* does not frame this as *zera levatala* in the technical sense. The prohibition of *zera levatala* is not understood to apply to marital intercourse even when conception is impossible — a postmenopausal wife, a pregnant wife — and nobody suggests that *onah*, the obligation of regular marital relations, is suspended in such cases. Even here, with an underage wife, the *Gemara* does not use the language of *zera levatala* to characterize the problem. Instead it frames the concern in eschatological terms.

### All Souls

Rabbi Yossi's teaching provides the eschatological key: *ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu kol haneshamot shebaguf* — the Mashiah will not come until all the souls in the *guf* have been discharged into the world. The word *guf* here does not mean a physical body but refers to a kind of celestial storehouse of souls awaiting incarnation. The *Mashiah* cannot arrive until all those souls have been placed into human beings. When a man marries a girl who cannot yet conceive, he foregoes procreative opportunities that could have drawn down those souls and thereby hastened the redemption. The verse invoked is *ki ruach milfanai ya'atof u-neshamot ani asiti* — the spirit is held back before Me, and the souls are those I have made. The souls are withheld, the *guf* is not emptied, and the *Mashiah* is delayed. The three groups that delay the *Mashiah* thus all share a common denominator, whether through demographic dilution, wasteful emission, or foregone fertility: the full complement of humanity's spiritual potential is not being realized, and redemption waits.
