# Zevachim 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parah Adumah Location

### Gitta Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי חוּץ מִגִּתָּהּ? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חוּץ מִמָּקוֹם הַבָּדוּק לָהּ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וַהֲלֹא כׇּל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּדוּקָה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who burns the red heifer outside its pit is not liable for sacrificing outside the Temple courtyard. The Gemara clarifies: **What** is the meaning of: **Outside its pit? Reish Lakish said:** It means **outside the place that was inspected** to ensure that it is not a gravesite, which would render it impure. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: But is not all of Eretz Yisrael inspected** for impurity? Therefore, there is no need for the site of the burning of the red heifer to be specially inspected.

The daf opens in the final chapter of the tractate, whose *mishnah* — spanning all the *mishnayot* of this last *perek* — is now subject to Gemara analysis. The discussion continues within the framework of *shechutei chutz*. The *mishnah* stated that one who slaughters the *parah adumah* outside its *gitta* — literally, outside its vat or its proper location — is not liable for *shechutei chutz*, because the *parah adumah* is not *ra'uy le'ohel moed*: it is not fit to be brought into the *mikdash*.

The Gemara opens by asking: *mai chutz migitta* — what exactly is meant by "outside its location"? The *parah adumah* can presumably be brought anywhere outside *Yerushalayim*, so what constitutes the wrong place? Resh Lakish answers: *chutz mimakom habaduk lah* — outside a place that has been specifically checked and verified to be free of buried bones and graves, i.e., a place confirmed as definitely *tahor*. If one slaughters it in an area that has not been so verified and may be *tamei*, that is not the proper location for the *parah adumah* — and yet one is still exempt from *shechutei chutz* because the *parah adumah* is not the kind of offering brought in the *mikdash*.

### Eretz Yisrael Tahur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לִפְנִים מִן חוֹמַת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The term: Outside its pit, is referring to a case **where** the priest **slaughtered** the red heifer **within the walls of Jerusalem** and not in the place outside the walls, as the Torah prescribes: "And it shall be brought outside the camp, and it shall be slaughtered before him" (Numbers 19:3).

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Resh Lakish directly: *vechol Eretz Yisrael bedukah hi* — is not all of *Eretz Yisrael* presumed to be free of unknown dead bodies? The default assumption is that any area in *Eretz Yisrael* not officially designated as a cemetery requires no special checking. Cemeteries and suspected burial sites were carefully marked, and absent such marking, the land is presumed *tahor*. Therefore, the definition of an improper location for the *parah adumah* cannot hinge on the unchecked-versus-checked distinction, since *me'ikar hadin* all of *Eretz Yisrael* is presumed acceptable.

If so, Rabbi Yochanan must offer an alternative account of what constitutes the wrong place for the *parah adumah*. He does: *kegon sheshachtah lifnim mechomat Yerushalayim* — for example, if one slaughtered it inside the walls of *Yerushalayim*. Once Israel enters the land, *Yerushalayim* defines *machaneh Yisrael*, the camp, and the *parah adumah* must be slaughtered outside the camp. If slaughtered within the walls, it is not in its proper place — yet there is still no liability for *shechutei chutz* because the *parah adumah* does not belong in the *mikdash* to begin with.

### Facing Petach Ohel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְמַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ חוּץ לַחוֹמָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח! דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שְׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחַט... וְהִזָּה״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **interpret it** to be a case **where** the priest **slaughtered it outside the wall** but **not opposite,** i.e., not in the direction of, **the entrance** to the Temple, **as Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** If **he slaughtered it** in a location **not opposite the entrance,** it is **disqualified, as it is stated** with regard to the red heifer: "And you shall give it to Elazar the priest, and it shall be brought outside the camp, **and it shall be slaughtered** before him. And Elazar the priest shall take of its blood with his finger, **and sprinkle** of its blood toward the front of the Tent of Meeting seven times" (Numbers 19:3–4).

One might ask: why not define the improper location more simply as anywhere outside *Yerushalayim* that does not face the entrance of the *beit hamikdash*? The *Torah* explicitly states regarding the *parah adumah* (Bamidbar 19): *unatatem otah el Elazar hakohen vehotzi otah el michutz lamachaneh* — take it outside the camp — *veshachat otah lefanav*, slaughter it before him, *velakach Elazar hakohen midamah be'etzba'o vehizah el nochach pnei Ohel Moed midamah sheva pe'amim* — and Elazar the kohen shall take some of the blood on his finger and sprinkle it toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting seven times. The sprinkling must face *Ohel Moed*; should not the slaughter as well?

### Shechitah Keneged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה הַזָּאָתָהּ כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח, אַף שְׁחִיטָתָהּ כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח. וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּלָא מַקֵּישׁ, וְהָא אִתְּמַר: שְׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: פְּסוּלָה – ״וְשָׁחַט... וְהִזָּה״. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כְּשֵׁרָה – ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה וְשָׁחַט״.
>
> **Translation:** The slaughter of the red heifer and the sprinkling of its blood are juxtaposed so that one will draw the following conclusion: **Just as its sprinkling** must be performed **opposite the entrance, so too, its slaughter** must be performed **opposite the entrance. And if you would say that** Rabbi Yoḥanan does **not juxtapose** the two verses for the purpose of this comparison, that is difficult: **But** it **was stated** with regard to a red heifer **slaughtered** in a location **not opposite the entrance** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **disqualified,** as **"and it shall be slaughtered"** is juxtaposed with **"and sprinkle." Reish Lakish says:** It is **fit,** since it is stated: "And it shall be brought **outside the camp, and it shall be slaughtered,"** indicating that it may be slaughtered in any location outside the camp.

This is precisely the subject of the next dispute. Rav Adda bar Ahava rules that if the *parah adumah* was slaughtered not facing the entrance of the *mikdash*, it is invalid — *shechtah shelo keneged hapetach psulah*. The derivation is from the juxtaposition in the verse: *veshachat vehizah* — just as the sprinkling must face the entrance, so too the *shechitah* must face the entrance. Rabbi Yochanan concurs with this *drasha*: *psulah*. Resh Lakish disagrees: *kesherah*. He reads the verse differently — *el michutz lamachaneh veshachat*, bring it outside the camp and slaughter. The requirement to face *Ohel Moed* applies only to the sprinkling of the blood, which is stage two. The *shechitah* itself need not be oriented toward the entrance.

Given this dispute, the Gemara could have argued that an improper location for the *parah adumah* is slaughter outside *Yerushalayim* but not facing the *mikdash*. Why, then, does Rabbi Yochanan choose the more dramatic example of slaughter inside the walls?

### Sreifa Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, שְׂרָפָהּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: פְּסוּלָה, וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: כְּשֵׁרָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר פְּסוּלָה – ״וְשָׂרַף... וְהִזָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it **was also stated** that *amora'im* disagree with regard to a red heifer that the priest **burned not opposite the entrance** to the Temple. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **disqualified, and Rabbi Oshaya says:** It is **fit.** The Gemara explains their reasoning: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is **disqualified** because of an additional juxtaposition. It is stated: **"And** the heifer **shall be burned** in his sight; its skin, and its flesh, and its blood, with its dung, shall be burned" (Numbers 19:5), while in the previous verse it is stated: **"And sprinkle** of its blood toward the front of the Tent of Meeting." This teaches that just as the sprinkling of the blood must be done opposite the entrance, so too must the burning be done opposite the entrance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר כְּשֵׁרָה – ״עַל פִּרְשָׁהּ יִשְׂרֹף״; מְקוֹם שֶׁפּוֹרֶשֶׁת לְמִיתָה, שָׁם תְּהֵא שְׂרֵיפָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Oshaya says** that a red heifer that was burned in a location not opposite the entrance is **fit,** as the verse states: **"With its dung [*pirshah*], shall be burned,"** which is interpreted homiletically to mean: In **the place that** its soul **departs [*poreshet*] for death, there shall be its burning.** Just as no specific location is given for the soul's departing, so too, the burning need not be performed in a specific location. Since Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that the red heifer must be slaughtered opposite the entrance to the Temple, why does he not understand the term: Outside its pit, to be referring to its slaughter in any location not opposite the Temple entrance?

The related dispute over the burning of the *parah adumah* is also recorded. If the *sreifa* — the burning — takes place not in the location facing the entrance of the *mikdash*, what is its status? Rabbi Yochanan rules *psulah*: invalid. He derives this from another juxtaposition — *vesharaf vehizah* — the burning is linked to the sprinkling, and just as the sprinkling must face *Ohel Moed*, so too the burning. Rabbi Oshaya rules *kesherah*: valid. He reads the relevant verse — *al pirsha yisrof* — as establishing an independent rule: the burning takes place where the animal has separated from life, i.e., where it actually died. One may slaughter it in one place, move with the blood to face the *mikdash* for the sprinkling, and then return to where the body lies — even a considerable distance away and not facing the *mikdash* — to perform the burning. The *sreifa* follows the location of death, not the location of the sprinkling.

### Lifnim Lechumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר; לָא מִיבַּעְיָא חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – דְּרַחוֹקֵי רַחֲקַהּ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה – דְּקָרוֹבֵי קָרְבַהּ, וְאֵימָא תִּתַּכְשַׁר – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** for the mishna to teach that in slaughtering a red heifer **outside the wall** in a location not opposite the entrance, one does not transgress the prohibition against slaughtering outside the Temple courtyard. In that case it is clearly disqualified, **as he has distanced it** from where it is meant to be slaughtered. **But even** if one slaughtered it **inside the wall** of Jerusalem, so **that he brings it closer** to the Temple, **and** one might **say** that **it is a valid** way of slaughtering the red heifer, Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us** that nevertheless it is disqualified.

Why does Rabbi Yochanan choose to illustrate the improper location with the case of *lifnim mechomat Yerushalayim* — inside the walls — rather than the seemingly more straightforward case of slaughter outside *Yerushalayim* in a direction not facing the *mikdash*? The Gemara explains with the principle of *lo mibaiya ka'amar*: it goes without saying. A location outside *Yerushalayim* that does not face the *mikdash* is obviously not the proper place for the *parah adumah*. That case involves an animal that is already a *parah adumah* — not brought inside the *mikdash* — combined with a location that is both further away and improperly oriented. Of course there is no liability for *shechutei chutz* there; no *hava amina* otherwise exists.

The genuine *chiddush* is the opposite case: slaughter inside *Yerushalayim*, closer to the *mikdash*. One might have supposed that proximity to the *mikdash* renders this a valid location — that only the remote, misdirected case is invalid, but the nearer case might actually qualify. The *mishnah* teaches that this is not so: even slaughter inside *Yerushalayim*, which brings the *parah adumah* closer to the *beit hamikdash*, is an improper location. And yet there remains no liability for *shechutei chutz*, since the *parah adumah* is not fit for the *mikdash* in the first place.

## Mabul Debate

### Yarad Lo Yarad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וַהֲלֹא כׇּל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּדוּקָה הִיא. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: יָרַד מַבּוּל לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּמָר סָבַר: לֹא יָרַד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the disagreement cited earlier: **The Master says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Reish Lakish: **But is not all of Eretz Yisrael inspected** for impurity? Since Reish Lakish's response to this question is not mentioned, the Gemara clarifies: **With regard to what do they disagree?** One **Sage,** Reish Lakish, **holds** that **the flood** in the time of Noah **descended upon Eretz Yisrael,** and its residents perished. It is therefore necessary to inspect the place where the red heifer is burned to ascertain whether it is a gravesite. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that the flood **did not descend** upon Eretz Yisrael, and there is no reason to suspect there are lost graves there.

Having explored the halachic dimension of *Eretz Yisrael*'s presumed *tahara*, the Gemara pivots to an extended *aggadic* discussion of the underlying premise. The Gemara asks: *bemai kamifligi* — on what do Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan actually disagree? What is the source of their divergent assumptions about the status of unmarked land in *Eretz Yisrael*?

The Gemara answers: *mar savar yarad mabul l'Eretz Yisrael, u'mar savar lo yarad* — one holds that the flood reached *Eretz Yisrael* and one holds it did not. The relevance is straightforward: wherever the *mabul*'s waters reached, the population perished and their bodies were buried undetected in the mud — unmarked, unknown, and therefore a potential source of *tumah* for anyone passing overhead. If the *mabul* reached *Eretz Yisrael*, then even the land of Israel contains such buried bodies and cannot be presumed *tahor*. If the waters never reached *Eretz Yisrael*, no such mass burial occurred, and the land retains its presumption of *tahara*.

This is a striking Gemara. The Torah states *tachat kol hashamayim* — under all of the heavens — and it seems inconceivable that floodwaters covering the entire world would somehow not have reached *Eretz Yisrael*. *Tosafot* even notes a *Bereishit Rabbah* that appears to treat such a notion as absurd. Nonetheless, the Gemara here entertains it as a genuine halachic position.

### Eretz Yisrael Lo Metoharah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ: ״בֶּן אָדָם אֱמׇר לָהּ אַתְּ אֶרֶץ לֹא מְטֹהָרָה הִיא לֹא גֻשְׁמָהּ בְּיוֹם זָעַם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: And both of them,** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, **interpreted the same verse,** stated by Ezekiel with regard to Eretz Yisrael, to derive their opinions. The verse states: **"Son of man, say to her: You are a land that is not cleansed, nor rained upon in the day of indignation"** (Ezekiel 22:24).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר, אַתְמוֹהֵי מַתְמַהּ קְרָא: אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִי לָא מְטוֹהָרָה אַתְּ?! כְּלוּם יָרְדוּ עָלַיִךְ גְּשָׁמִים בְּיוֹם זָעַם?! וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבַר: כִּפְשָׁטֵיהּ – אָרֶץ לֹא מְטוֹהָרָה אַתְּ; מִי לֹא יָרְדוּ עָלַיִךְ גְּשָׁמִים בְּיוֹם זָעַם?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that **the verse is asking** a rhetorical **question: Eretz Yisrael, are you not cleansed** from the impurity imparted by corpses? **Did** the **rains** of the flood **fall upon you on the day of indignation? And Reish Lakish holds** that this verse should be read **in accordance with its straightforward meaning,** i.e., as a statement, not a question: **You are a land that is not cleansed. Didn't rains fall upon you on the day of indignation?** Therefore, the bodies of all of those who perished in the flood are somewhere in the ground.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds: *u'shneihem mikra echad darshu* — remarkably, both opinions derive from the same verse, reading it in opposite directions.

The verse in question is from *Yechezkel* (22:24): *ben adam emor lah at eretz lo metoharah hi, lo gushmah beyom zaam* — Son of man, say to her: you are a land that is not pure; you have not been rained upon on the day of wrath. Rabbi Yochanan reads the verse as a rhetorical question addressed to *Eretz Yisrael*: *Eretz Yisrael mi lo metoharah at?* — Land of Israel, are you not a *tahor* land? Of course you are! *Lo gushmah beyom zaam* — the rains of the *mabul* did not fall upon you, and therefore the people of the *mabul* did not die and get buried in your soil. The verse is thus a declaration of *Eretz Yisrael*'s purity, grounded in the fact that the flood did not reach it.

Resh Lakish reads the verse simply, *kipshatei*: it is a plain statement — you are not a pure land. And the second clause is then the rhetorical element: *mi lo yardu alayich geshamim beyom zaam?* — Did the rains not fall upon you as they fell everywhere else on the day of wrath? They did, implying that the dead of the *mabul* are buried in *Eretz Yisrael* just as elsewhere.

### Chatzerot Al Hasela

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲצֵירוֹת הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם בְּנוּיוֹת עַל הַסֶּלַע, וְתַחְתֵּיהֶן חָלוּל מִפְּנֵי קֶבֶר הַתְּהוֹם, וּמְבִיאִין נָשִׁים מְעוּבָּרוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת, וּמְגַדְּלוֹת שָׁם בְּנֵיהֶם לַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raised an objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan** from a mishna (*Para* 3:2): **Courtyards were built in Jerusalem on stone, and beneath** these courtyards there was a hollow **space due to** the concern that there was a lost **grave** in **the depths.** The space served as a barrier preventing the impurity from reaching the courtyards above. **And they** would **bring pregnant women, and** those women would **give birth** in those courtyards. **And** those women would **raise their children there,** thereby ensuring that the children never became impure. This would enable the children **to** assist in the rite of **the** red **heifer.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְבִיאִין שְׁוָורִים, וְעַל גַּבֵּיהֶן דְּלָתוֹת, וְתִינוֹקוֹת יוֹשְׁבִין עֲלֵיהֶן, וְכוֹסוֹת שֶׁל אֶבֶן בְּיָדָן, וּמִלְּאוּ וְיָשְׁבוּ בִּמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** once the children reached the appropriate age, the priests would **bring oxen** there. **And on the backs of** these oxen, they would place **doors, and** the **children** would **sit upon** the doors, so that the doors would serve as a barrier between them and any impurity in the depths, **and** they would hold **cups of stone,** which are not susceptible to ritual impurity, **in their hands,** and they would ride upon the oxen to the Siloam pool. **And** they **filled** the cups with water **and would sit** back **in their places** upon the oxen and be taken to the Temple Mount. The water in the cups would be used for the rite of the red heifer. Apparently, there is concern that hidden sources of impurity exist in Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַעֲלָה עָשׂוּ בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** that Rabbi Yoḥanan would reply: The Sages **established a higher standard** for purity **in** the case of **the red heifer,** but generally speaking there is no concern for hidden sources of impurity in Eretz Yisrael caused by those who perished in the flood.

Resh Lakish now challenges Rabbi Yochanan from a *halachic* source. The Gemara cites: *chatzerot hayu biYerushalayim benuyot al ha-sela, ve-tachtehem chalul, mipnei kever ha-tehom* — there were courtyards in *Yerushalayim* built atop solid rock, with an airspace hollowed beneath the rock, because of concern for a *kever ha-tehom*: a grave of the deep, meaning an unknown and undetected burial somewhere below. The outcropping of rock, with its hollow air space beneath, served as a barrier: even if a body were buried many feet below in the earth, the *tumah* would rise, strike the underside of the rock, and be blocked from penetrating upward to those living or working above.

In these specially constructed courtyards, pregnant women would be brought and would give birth, and their children would be raised there from birth — never exposed to *tumat meit*. These children were then engaged to assist in aspects of the *parah adumah* process, specifically the sprinkling of the water mixed with the *parah*'s ashes upon the *kohen* who would perform the next *parah adumah* rite. When the time came to draw water from a spring for this purpose, the children were seated on broad wooden planks laid across the backs of oxen. As the oxen walked from the courtyard to the spring, any *tumah* rising from below would strike the underside of the plank, which served as an *ohel* — a roof — interposing between the ground and the child above. The children carried stone vessels, which cannot become *tamei*, filled them at the spring, and returned.

The existence of these elaborate precautions seems to contradict Rabbi Yochanan's position: if *Eretz Yisrael* is presumed *tahor* with no concern for buried bodies, why construct all this? The Gemara responds on Rabbi Yochanan's behalf: *ma'ala asu be-para* — these were extra-stringent measures adopted specifically for the *parah adumah*, due to the broader controversy involving the *Tzadokim*. They are not technically required *me'ikar hadin*. Resh Lakish, however, could counter that such extraordinary measures only make sense if there is some underlying concern — one does not go to such lengths if the worry is entirely without basis. The Gemara, however, resolves it as a simple *chumra* and moves on.

### Atzamot Bechazra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: פַּעַם אֶחָד מָצְאוּ עֲצָמוֹת בְּלִשְׁכַּת דִּיר הָעֵצִים, וּבִקְּשׁוּ לִגְזוֹר טוּמְאָה עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. עָמַד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: לֹא בּוּשָׁה וּכְלִימָּה הִיא לָנוּ, שֶׁנִּגְזוֹר טוּמְאָה עַל עִיר אֲבוֹתֵינוּ?! אַיֵּה מֵתֵי מַבּוּל? אַיֵּה מֵתֵי נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from a *baraita* (see *Tosefta*, *Eduyyot* 3:3): **Once,** human **bones were found in the Chamber of the Woodshed, and** the Sages **sought to decree impurity upon Jerusalem,** i.e., to proclaim all who go there to be impure, as if a corpse can be found in a chamber of the Temple there is reason to be concerned that there are lost graves in other places as well. **Rabbi Yehoshua stood upon his feet and said: Is it not a shame and disgrace for us to decree impurity upon the city of our fathers** because of this concern? Show me: **Where are the dead of the flood,** and **where are** all of **the dead** killed by **Nebuchadnezzar?**

Rabbi Yochanan now challenges Resh Lakish from the opposite direction. A *Tosefta* records: *pa'am achat matzu atzamot be-lishkat deir ha-etzim* — on one occasion, human bones were found in the chamber of the wood storage in the *beit hamikdash*. In response, *bikshu ligzor tumah al Yerushalayim* — the authorities sought to declare all of *Yerushalayim tamei* rabbinically. The discovery of bones even within the precincts of the *beit hamikdash* — in a place one would have assumed most pure — seemed to warrant the gravest concern.

### Ayin Metei Mabul

Rabbi Yehoshua rose and objected: *lo busha vechalima lanu shenigzar tumah al ir avoteinu* — would it not be a shame and embarrassment to decree *tumah* upon the holy city of our ancestors? He then posed the rhetorical challenge: *ayei metei mabul, ayei metei Nevuchadnezzar?* — Where are the dead of the *mabul*? Where are the dead of Nevuchadnezzar? The implication being: people have died in *Yerushalayim* before — many people — and yet the city remains *tahor*. Whatever arrangements were necessary to ensure that *Yerushalayim* remain pure have always been made. We need not now suddenly declare it *tamei*.

### Havu U'Pinnu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר הָכִי, לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּלָא הֲווֹ? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הֲרוּגֵי נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא הֲווֹ?! אֶלָּא הֲווֹ, וּפַנִּינְהוּ; הָכָא נָמֵי – הֲווֹ וּפַנִּינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan infers: **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehoshua **said this,** is this **not to say that there were no** lost graves in Jerusalem from the flood, because the flood did not take place there? Reish Lakish responds: **And according to your reasoning, so too were there not those killed by Nebuchadnezzar,** in and around Jerusalem, who were mentioned by Rabbi Yehoshua? Certainly there were, as Nebuchadnezzar killed many people in Jerusalem. **Rather,** there **were, and** others **removed** the bodies. **Here too,** with regard to the dead of the flood, **there were, and** others **removed** the bodies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִפַּנּוֹ, הָא אִיפְּנוֹ! נְהִי דְּאִיפַּנּוֹ מִירוּשָׁלַיִם, מִכּוּלַּהּ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא אִיפַּנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** as **they were** already **removed.** One can respond: This *baraita* deals exclusively with Jerusalem. **Granted that** the bones of those who perished in the flood and at the hands of Nebuchadnezzar **were removed from Jerusalem,** but they **were not removed from all** of **Eretz Yisrael.** Therefore, outside Jerusalem, the red heifer may be slaughtered only in a place that has been inspected.

Rabbi Yochanan reads this statement as a proof to his position: from the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua says "where are the dead of the *mabul*," is he not implying that they were never here — that the *mabul* never reached *Eretz Yisrael* and no one died here from it?

The Gemara immediately objects: *uleta'meich, harugei Nevuchadnezzar hacha nami delo havu?* — By that logic, you would have to say the victims of Nevuchadnezzar also never died in *Yerushalayim* — but Nevuchadnezzar unquestionably slaughtered Jews there. The Gemara therefore interprets Rabbi Yehoshua's statement differently: *ela havu upinnu, hacha nami havu upinnu* — both the dead of Nevuchadnezzar and the dead of the *mabul* died in *Yerushalayim*, but they were subsequently removed. The city was actively kept *tahor* through the removal of bodies.

This reading, however, seems to support Resh Lakish rather than Rabbi Yochanan — it concedes that people did die in *Yerushalayim* from the *mabul*. The Gemara presses: if, on Resh Lakish's view, bodies were cleaned out of *Yerushalayim*, why does Resh Lakish maintain a concern about *tumah* across *Eretz Yisrael*? The Gemara resolves this: *de'ihi de'ifni mi'Yerushalayim, mikol Eretz Yisrael lo ifni* — Rabbi Yehoshua was speaking only about *Yerushalayim* specifically, which was indeed cleared. But the rest of *Eretz Yisrael* was not subjected to the same comprehensive removal. *Yerushalayim* is *tahor*, but *Eretz Yisrael* outside *Yerushalayim* remains a concern on Resh Lakish's view. The Gemara then notes an alternative tradition (*ika de'amri*) in which the source is brought by Resh Lakish as a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan rather than the reverse — the logical reading of Rabbi Yehoshua's statement being that *Yerushalayim* was cleared but not all of Israel.

The Gemara ultimately deflects both readings, noting: *midei irya, ha kide'isa veha kide'isa* — perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua meant two different points simultaneously. The dead of the *mabul* never came to *Yerushalayim* at all (Rabbi Yochanan's position), and separately, the dead of Nevuchadnezzar died here but were removed (an independently valid point). This blended reading is admittedly strained, as the two clauses appear to be a single unified statement — but the Gemara records it. In any case, no decisive proof can be extracted from Rabbi Yehoshua's statement for either side of the dispute about whether the *mabul* reached *Eretz Yisrael*. And since the *parah adumah* must be taken outside *Yerushalayim* regardless, the question of the status of the rest of *Eretz Yisrael* is what remains practically at stake.

### Mikol Asher Bacharava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר בֶּחָרָבָה מֵתוּ״ – בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא יָרַד מַבּוּל לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מֵתוּ. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אַמַּאי מֵתוּ? מִשּׁוּם הַבְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **raised an objection to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: With regard to the flood, it is stated: "All in whose nostrils was the breath of the spirit of life, **whatsoever was on the dry land, died"** (Genesis 7:22). **Granted, according to my** opinion, **that I say** the **flood descended upon Eretz Yisrael, due to that** reason all living creatures on Earth **died,** even those in Eretz Yisrael. **But according to your** opinion that the flood did not descend on Eretz Yisrael, **why did they die** there? Rabbi Yoḥanan responds: They died **due to the heat** that accompanied the floodwaters, and that spread to Eretz Yisrael as well. Those corpses were then buried in known locations.

The Gemara now examines whether the *pesukim* of the *mabul* itself can resolve the dispute. The verse states: *mikol asher becharava meisu* — everything on dry land died. Resh Lakish observes: *bishlama ledidi* — according to me, who holds that the *mabul* reached *Eretz Yisrael*, this verse reads naturally. *Eretz Yisrael* is dry land; the waters came; everyone died. That is the straightforward reading of *pesukim*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds the *mabul* did not reach *Eretz Yisrael* — *amai meisu?* — why does the verse say that everything on dry land died? If the people of *Eretz Yisrael* were not flooded, why did they perish?

### Hevla Killing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּרוֹתְחִין קִלְקְלוּ, וּבְרוֹתְחִין נִידּוֹנוּ; דִּכְתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיָּשֹׁכּוּ הַמָּיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַחֲמַת הַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁכָכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda says:** The generation of the flood **sinned with boiling** heat, i.e., forbidden sexual intercourse, **and they were punished with** the **boiling** heat of the flood waters. **As it is written here,** with regard to the flood: "And God remembered Noah and every living creature and all the cattle that were with him in the ark; and God made a wind to pass over the earth **and the waters calmed [*vayashoku hamayim*]"** (Genesis 8:1); **and it is written there,** with regard to the execution of Haman: "So they hanged Haman on the gallows that he had prepared for Mordecai. **Then the king's boiling anger was assuaged [*shakhakha*]"** (Esther 7:10). This latter verse indicates that a matter is assuaged from heat; similarly, the flood waters were hot.

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is remarkable: *mishum hevla* — they died from the heat. Even though the waters of the *mabul* did not physically reach *Eretz Yisrael*, the catastrophic atmospheric conditions — the sulfurous heat and vapor generated by the worldwide flood — killed everyone in *Eretz Yisrael* as well. All who lived outside the ark perished, including those in *Eretz Yisrael*, but by heat rather than by water.

This answer creates an apparent difficulty for Rabbi Yochanan's entire thesis. His reason *Eretz Yisrael* is presumed *tahor* is that the *mabul* did not bury bodies in the mud there. But if everyone in *Eretz Yisrael* died nonetheless — from the *hevla* — then there are bodies buried in *Eretz Yisrael*. Why is it *tahor*? *Rashi* explains: those who died from the heat died on dry land, with their bodies remaining visible and accessible. *Noach* and his descendants could subsequently find them, bury them properly in marked graves, and the locations would be known. By contrast, those who perished in the flood itself were buried deep in the mud — covered by water, submerged, unmarked — making their graves permanently unknown. It is specifically those flood-buried bodies that create the *tumah* concern. Since *Eretz Yisrael* had no such flood, its bodies were properly buried and marked; hence the land remains *tahor*.

This approach to the verse — that *Eretz Yisrael* was spared the waters but not the heat — is a difficult reading, to say the least. The Gemara links Rabbi Yochanan's position to the teaching of Rav Chisda: *anshei dor hamabul beroschin kilkelu uveroschin nidonu* — the generation of the flood sinned through heated passion and were punished with boiling hot water. The word *vayashoku* (the waters abated) is read alongside *vachamas hamelech shachacha* (the king's wrath abated) to indicate that the waters of the *mabul* were boiling hot — and this intense heat would have killed even those not directly struck by the waters.

### Reima Survival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא יָרַד מַבּוּל לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, הַיְינוּ דְּקָם רֵימָא הָתָם. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יָרַד, רֵימָא הֵיכָא קָם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says the flood did not descend upon Eretz Yisrael,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, **this is** the explanation of the fact **that the *reima* remained there,** in Eretz Yisrael, and survived the flood. **But according to the one who says** the flood **descended** upon Eretz Yisrael, i.e., Reish Lakish, **how did the *reima* remain?** Given its large size, it clearly could not have fit into Noah's ark.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: גּוּרִיּוֹת הִכְנִיסוּ בַּתֵּיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai says:** They **brought** *reima* **cubs into the ark,** and they survived the flood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי אוּרְזִילָא דְּרֵימָא (בַּת) [בַּר] יוֹמֵאּ, וְהָוֵי כְּהַר תָּבוֹר. וְהַר תָּבוֹר כַּמָּה הָוֵיא – אַרְבְּעִין פַּרְסֵי; מְשָׁכָא דְּצַוְּארֵיהּ – תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי, מַרְבַּעְתָּא דְּרֵישָׁא – פַּרְסָא וּפַלְגָא, רְמָא כַּבָּא וּסְכַר יַרְדְּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say: I have seen a day-old offspring of the *reima*, and** it **was** as large **as Mount Tabor. And how** large **is Mount Tabor?** It is **forty parasangs.** And **the length of** the cub's **neck** was **three parasangs,** and **the place where its head rests,** i.e., its neck, was **a parasang and a half. It cast feces, and** thereby **dammed up the Jordan** river. Even the cub would have been too large for the ark.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רֹאשׁוֹ הִכְנִיסוּ לַתֵּיבָה. וְהָאָמַר מָר: מַרְבַּעְתָּא דְּרֵישָׁא פַּרְסָא וּפַלְגָא! אֶלָּא רֹאשׁ חוֹטְמוֹ הִכְנִיסוּ לַתֵּיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They brought** only **the head of** the cub **into the ark,** while its body remained outside. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't the Master,** i.e., Rabba bar bar Ḥana, **say** that the size of **the place where its head rests** was **a parasang and a half?** Consequently, even its head alone would not fit into the ark. **Rather, they brought the head,** i.e., edge, **of its nose into the ark,** so that it might breathe.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָסָגְיָא תֵּיבָה! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קַרְנָיו קָשְׁרוּ בַּתֵּיבָה. וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַנְשֵׁי דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל בְּרוֹתְחִין קִלְקְלוּ וּבְרוֹתְחִין נִידּוֹנוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But the ark was moving** upon the water. How was it was possible to keep the nose of the *reima* in the ark? **Reish Lakish says: They tied its horns to the ark,** so that the *reima* would move with it. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rav Ḥisda say** that **the people of the generation of the flood sinned with boiling** heat **and were punished with boiling** heat? How could the *reima* have survived the boiling water?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תֵּיבָה הֵיכִי סָגְיָא? וְעוֹד, עוֹג מֶלֶךְ הַבָּשָׁן הֵיכָא קָאֵי? אֶלָּא נֵס נַעֲשָׂה לָהֶם, שֶׁנִּצְטַנְּנוּ בְּצִידֵּי הַתֵּיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **And according to your reasoning,** that it was impossible to survive the boiling water, **how did** the **ark** itself **move?** It was covered with pitch, which melts in boiling water. **Moreover, how did Og, king of the Bashan** (see Numbers 21:33–35), who according to tradition was of the generation of the flood, **stand,** i.e., survive the boiling water? **Rather,** it must be that **a miracle was performed for them,** namely **that** the water **on the sides of the ark cooled,** allowing the ark, the *reima*, and Og to survive.

The Gemara now raises a different question rooted in the *mabul* debate: *bishlama leman de'amar lo yarad mabul le'Eretz Yisrael, hainu d'kam reima hasam* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds the *mabul* did not reach *Eretz Yisrael*, one can explain how the *reima* survived: it walked to *Eretz Yisrael*, where the waters did not come, and the *hevla* perhaps did not kill it. But *ela l'man de'amar yarad mabul* — according to Resh Lakish, who holds that the flood did reach *Eretz Yisrael* — *reima heichi kam?* — where could the *reima* have gone, and how did it survive? The *reima* is an animal of such extraordinary size that it clearly could not have fit inside the ark.

Before exploring the answer, the Gemara pauses to establish just how large this creature was. Rabba bar bar Chana reported: *l'didi chazi li orzila d'reima bas yoma* — I personally saw a newborn *reima*, one day old, and *havi k'tur tavor* — it was the size of Mount Tabor. How large is Mount Tabor? *Arba'in parsi* — forty parsangs, approximately 160 kilometers. The neck alone was three parsangs (twelve kilometers) in length. The area where it would lay its head was a parsang and a half — six kilometers. And, the Gemara adds, when it defecated, its refuse blocked the Jordan River. This is manifestly a mythic creature, yet the Gemara treats its survival of the *mabul* as a genuine question.

Rabbi Yannai's answer for Resh Lakish's position: *guriot hichnisu b'teiva* — they brought baby *reimas* into the ark. To this Rabba bar bar Chana's description responds: even a one-day-old *reima* is the size of Mount Tabor — no baby *reima* could have fit in the ark either.

Rabbi Yochanan then offers: *rosho hichnisu l'teiva* — its head was inserted into the ark. The *reima* perhaps walked through the flood or was borne on the water's surface, and only its head was kept inside the ark so it could breathe. But the Gemara objects: the area where the *reima* rests its head is a parsang and a half across — how could its head fit in the ark? The Gemara revises: *ella rosh chotamo hichnisu l'teiva* — the tip of its nose was inserted into the ark, just enough to allow it to breathe, while its body floated in the water below.

A further difficulty: *v'ha kasagya teiva* — the ark was tossing up and down on the waters. The *reima*'s nose would have slipped out, and it would have drowned. Resh Lakish resolves this: *karnav kishru b'teiva* — its horns were tied to the ark, so the *reima* rose and fell with the ark and could maintain its connection to breathe.

The Gemara then returns to Rav Chisda's teaching that the waters of the *mabul* were boiling hot. If so, how could the *reima* have survived immersion in boiling water? The Gemara asks a pointed question: *u'l'ta'ameich* — if you worry about the boiling water, consider also that the ark itself would have been destroyed: the pitch sealing the ark's planks would have melted. And how did *Og Melech Habashan* survive — he reportedly clung to the outside of the ark? The resolution offered is *nes na'aseh lahem, shenitztanenu betzidei hateiva* — a miracle occurred: the water immediately surrounding the ark cooled. It was within this protected sphere that Og clung, the pitch held, and the *reima*'s head — or nose — was kept viable.

*Tosafot* makes a practically important observation in this context. Noting that *reima* is identified by some with the *re'em* of the *pesukim* (as in *karnei re'em karnav*), and that *Targum Onkelos* on *Devarim* renders *dishon* as *reima*, *Tosafot* emphasizes that the *reima* is unquestionably a *chaya* — a wild animal — and not a *beheima*, a domesticated animal. This matters halachically: the *cheilev* (forbidden fat) of a *chaya* is permitted, while that of a *beheima* is forbidden. *Tosafot* warns that one should not identify the *reima* with the buffalo (*bafalos*) found in the author's time. Buffaloes are a *beheima* — their *cheilev* is forbidden. Several proofs: buffaloes are small enough to have fit easily in the ark, and thus do not exhibit the extraordinary dimensions associated with the *reima*; furthermore, and decisively for purposes of the *chaya/beheima* distinction, buffaloes can be domesticated and used for labor — they can pull wagons and plow fields. The *re'em*, by contrast, is not domesticatable. A *chaya*, in the relevant halachic sense, is not merely an animal that happens to be wild but one that is in principle incapable of domestication.

### Metzan Babylonia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״שִׁנְעָר״? שֶׁמְּנַעֶרֶת עֲשִׁירֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned some explanations for the names of Babylonia, the Gemara adds: **Rabbi Abbahu says: Why is it called Shinar? Because it shakes [*shemena'eret*] its wealthy** people, i.e., they do not remain wealthy. The Gemara asks: **But we see that there are** wealthy people in Babylonia who remain wealthy. The Gemara responds: Their wealth **does not extend** for **three generations.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: כָּל הָאוֹכֵל מֵעֲפָרָהּ שֶׁל בָּבֶל – כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל בְּשַׂר אֲבוֹתָיו. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כָּל הָאוֹכֵל מֵעֲפָרָהּ שֶׁל בָּבֶל – כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל בְּשַׂר אֲבוֹתָיו. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement that the corpses of those who perished in the flood came to Babylonia, **Rabbi Ami says:** Concerning **anyone who eats the dust of Babylonia,** it is **as if he eats the flesh of his ancestors,** since there is a great deal of dust from the dead there. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning **anyone who eats the dust of Babylonia,** it is **as if he eats the flesh of his ancestors. And some say:** It is **as if he eats repugnant creatures and crawling things,** which also died in the flood and were absorbed by the ground of Babylonia.

The Gemara concludes the *mabul* discussion with a question about Resh Lakish's own position. Since Resh Lakish holds the *mabul* reached *Eretz Yisrael*, why is he concerned about bodies buried there? The bodies of the flood's victims were not simply left in place: Resh Lakish himself taught that *Bavel* is called *Metzula* (swamp or depths) because *kol metei mabul nitztelelu l'sham* — all the dead of the *mabul* were swept there. *Bavel* was the lowest-lying land, and the bodies were carried there by the receding waters. Similarly, *Bavel* is called *Shinar* because *kol metei mabul ninaru l'sham* — all the dead were tumbled there. If the bodies were transported to *Bavel*, why should *Eretz Yisrael* remain suspect?

The Gemara responds: *ee efshar delo idbeku* — it is impossible that none were left behind. Even if the great mass of bodies was swept to *Bavel*, some surely remained stuck in the mud in *Eretz Yisrael*.

The Gemara appends further aggadic material on *Bavel*'s names. Rabbi Abbahu explains that *Shinar* alludes to *shemenaeret ashireha* — it empties its wealthy people, meaning sustained wealth does not endure there for more than three generations. And finally, Rabbi Ami states: *kol haochel me'afara shel Bavel, ke'ilu ochel besar avotav* — whoever eats of the soil of *Bavel* is as though eating the flesh of his ancestors, since all the bodies of the *mabul* decomposed there and mingled with the earth. Some say it is as though eating *shekatzim uremasim* — repugnant creatures — since those too perished and decomposed into the soil of *Bavel*. *Rashi* ties both formulations to the totality of death in the *mabul*.

## Se'ir Hamishtaleach

### Azazel Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one sacrificed **the scapegoat** of Yom Kippur outside the Temple he is exempt from the prohibition against sacrificing outside, since the Torah states: "And to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it" (Leviticus 17:3–4), and the scapegoat is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.

With the aggadic discussion concluded, the Gemara returns to the *mishnah*. Another case in which one is exempt from *shechutei chutz* is the *se'ir hamishtaleach* — the scapegoat sent off to *Azazel* on *Yom Kippur* — because it is not brought as an offering to the *Ohel Moed*.

### Korban L'Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: אוֹ ״קׇרְבָּן״ – שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ קׇרְבָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳״?
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: The verse states: "To present it as an offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 17:4), and it is derived from the word "offering" that one who slaughters non-sacred animals inside the Temple is not liable. The *baraita* asks: **Or** perhaps from the word **"offering" I** would **derive** that the prohibition against slaughtering outside the Temple applies **even** to items **consecrated for Temple maintenance, as** they too **are called offerings, as it is stated** with regard to the spoils of the war against Midian: **"And we have brought the Lord's offering,** what every man has gotten, of jewels of gold, armlets, and bracelets, signet rings, earrings, and girdles, to make atonement for our souls before the Lord" (Numbers 31:50). These were certainly not items consecrated for the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ״ – מִי שֶׁרָאוּי לָבֹא בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד; יָצְאוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת – שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it"** (Leviticus 17:4), which teaches that this *halakha* applies only to **that which is fit to come to** the entrance of the **Tent of Meeting,** i.e., is fit to be sacrificed. **Excluded** are items **consecrated for Temple maintenance, which are not fit** for sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֶת אֵלּוּ – שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן; וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ – שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לָבֹא אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לַה׳״ – לְהוֹצִיא שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְיוּחָד לַה׳.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Perhaps **I shall exclude these,** i.e., items consecrated for Temple maintenance, **which are not fit** to be sacrificed upon the altar, from the prohibition against slaughtering outside the Temple, **but I shall not exclude the scapegoat, which is fit to come to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "To present it as an offering **to the Lord,"** which serves **to exclude** from this prohibition **the scapegoat, which is not designated** as a sacrifice **to the Lord,** but is rather sent to Azazel. According to the *baraita*, the scapegoat is fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *midrash halacha*. The verse underlying *shechutei chutz* speaks of one who slaughters outside the *beit hamikdash* and fails to bring it as *lehakriv korban l'Hashem* — a *korban* to God. The *midrash halacha* raises the question: perhaps the word *korban* is broad enough to include even *kodshei bedek habayit* — animals sanctified not as sacrifices on the altar but as contributions to the Temple treasury — since the word *korban* can refer to gifts generally, as evidenced by the verse *vanakreiv et korban Hashem* referring to the gold and valuables brought from the spoils of Midian.

The *midrash halacha* rejects this expansion: *ve'el petach Ohel Moed lo hevi'o* — the verse adds that the animal was not brought to the *Ohel Moed*. This limits liability to animals that are *ra'uy lavo be'Ohel Moed* — fit to be brought into the Tent of Meeting. *Kodshei bedek habayit* are therefore excluded: an animal sanctified to the Temple treasury is never brought into the *Ohel Moed* as a sacrifice, and thus falls outside the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*.

### Kodem Achar Goral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן קוֹדֶם הַגְרָלָה, כָּאן לְאַחַר הַגְרָלָה. אַחַר הַגְרָלָה נָמֵי, הָאִיכָּא וִידּוּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* that states that the scapegoat is fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting is referring to **before** the **lottery,** wherein the two goats of the Day of Atonement are brought into the Temple courtyard, and the High Priest draws lots to determine which is to be sacrificed to the Lord, and which is for Azazel. **There,** the mishna that states that the scapegoat is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting is referring to **after** the **lottery,** at which point it is no longer fit for the Temple. The Gemara challenges: **After** the **lottery** it is **also** fit to be brought inside, as **there is** still an obligation for the High Priest to recite **confession** upon it in the Temple courtyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב מַנִּי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן קוֹדֶם וִידּוּי, כָּאן לְאַחַר וִידּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Mani said:** This is **not difficult,** as **here,** the *baraita* that states that the scapegoat may be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting is referring to **before** the **confession,** when it is still fit to enter the Temple. **There,** the mishna that states that it is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting is referring to **after** the **confession,** at which point it is no longer fit to be brought inside.

The *midrash halacha* then asks: having excluded *kodshei bedek habayit* on the ground that they are not fit for *Ohel Moed*, should one also exclude the *se'ir hamishtaleach*? The question is pointed: the *se'ir hamishtaleach* does begin its service in the *Ohel Moed* — both goats of *Yom Kippur* are brought to the entrance for the drawing of lots. So perhaps it is *ra'uy lavo b'Ohel Moed* and should be included in the prohibition.

The *midrash halacha* counters: *Talmud lomar l'Hashem* — the verse says the slaughter is to be a *korban l'Hashem*, a sacrifice to God. The *se'ir hamishtaleach* is not dedicated to God — it is sent *le'Azazel*. It is therefore excluded regardless of its connection to *Ohel Moed*.

This creates a tension with the *mishnah*, which says the *se'ir hamishtaleach* is excluded because it is not fit for *Ohel Moed*. But the *midrash halacha* implies the opposite — that it is fit for *Ohel Moed* (hence the need for the *l'Hashem* exclusion). The Gemara resolves this: *lo kashya, kan kodem hagoral, kan achar hagoral* — there is no contradiction. Before the lots are drawn, both goats are fit for *Ohel Moed* (and either could potentially be designated for any number of purposes), so the only basis for excluding the *se'ir hamishtaleach* from *shechutei chutz* liability is the *l'Hashem* verse. After the lots are drawn, the goat designated for *Azazel* is no longer fit for *Ohel Moed*, so it is excluded on that basis as well.

The Gemara refines this further: even after the lots, the *vidui* — the confession — over the *se'ir hamishtaleach* has not yet been performed, and the goat still has a connection to *Ohel Moed*. Rav Mani therefore corrects the dividing line: *kan kodem vidui, kan achar vidui* — it is the performance of the *vidui* that marks the transition. Until the *vidui*, the *se'ir hamishtaleach* retains a connection to *Ohel Moed*; only the *l'Hashem* exclusion applies. After the *vidui*, the goat is no longer fit for *Ohel Moed* either, and both exclusions apply simultaneously.

This connects to a discussion in *Yoma*: if one were to slaughter both goats outside the *beit hamikdash* before the lots are drawn, one would be liable for *shechutei chutz*. Before the lots, neither goat has been designated for *Azazel*, and both could theoretically be used for some other *korban*. Once the lots are drawn, the goat designated for God is *ra'uy l'ohel moed* and dedicated *l'Hashem*; the goat designated for *Azazel* is not *l'Hashem* and is thus exempt under the *midrash halacha*. After the *vidui*, the goat designated for *Azazel* is additionally no longer *ra'uy* for *Ohel Moed*.

### Rovei'a V'nirba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that with regard to **an animal that** actively **copulated with a person, or an animal that was the object of bestiality,** or another disqualified offering such as an animal that was designated for idol worship, or one that was worshipped: If one sacrificed it outside the Temple courtyard, he is exempt. This is because with regard to the prohibition against slaughtering outside, the Torah states: "He did not bring it, to present it as an offering to the Lord before the Tabernacle of the Lord" (Leviticus 17:4), which teaches that there is no liability for slaughtering outside the Temple courtyard an animal that is not fit to be sacrificed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא נָמֵי תִּיפּוֹק לִי מִ״פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **this too, derive from** the first part of that verse: **"To the entrance of the Tent of Meeting,"** that, as in the case of the red heifer and the scapegoat, if an animal is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, one is not liable for slaughtering it outside.

The *mishnah* also exempts an animal that engaged in or was subjected to bestiality (*rovei'a ve'nirba*) from *shechutei chutz* liability. Such an animal is disqualified from the altar — it is no longer *ra'uy* for *petach Ohel Moed* — and is thus excluded from liability. The Gemara's concern here is with the specific verse cited to exclude these animals: *lifnei mishkan Hashem*. Why is this additional phrase needed? Why not simply rely on the principle that they are not fit for *Ohel Moed*?

### Lifnei Mishkan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאַקְדְּשִׁינְהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, וַהֲדַר (רבעו) [אִירְבַעוּ].
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** with regard to **an animal that** actively **copulated with a person or an animal that was the object of bestiality, you find** circumstances in which the exemption for one who slaughters it outside the Temple courtyard cannot be based on the fact that it is not fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, e.g., a case **where one initially consecrated it,** at which point it was fit to be brought to the Temple courtyard, **and then engaged in bestiality with it.** Since it was initially fit to be brought to the Temple courtyard, another verse is needed to exclude it.

The Gemara explains: *bishlama rovei'a v'nirba maskachat lah de'akdishinhu me'ikara ve'hadar rav'o* — one can readily construct a scenario in which, at some earlier stage, such an animal was fit for *Ohel Moed*. One sanctified the animal as a *korban*, rendering it fit for *Ohel Moed*, and subsequently an act of bestiality occurred. At that earlier moment of sanctification, the animal was fit; one might therefore reason that the liability of *shechutei chutz* attaches at the point of sanctification and is not undone by the later disqualification. The verse *lifnei mishkan Hashem* is therefore needed to teach that what matters is the animal's current fitness, not its prior status: since it is now disqualified, there is no *shechutei chutz* liability, regardless of its history.

### Muktzeh V'ne'evad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד – אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ! בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – דְּאָמַר: קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to an animal that was **set aside** for idol worship **or** one that was **worshipped,** this explanation is not tenable, since an animal that was already consecrated would not become disqualified because **a person does not render forbidden an item that is not his.** The Gemara responds that it is possible to disqualify a consecrated item **in** the case of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** such as a peace offering, **and in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says: An offering of lesser sanctity is the property of the owner.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּמָעֲלָה מַעַל בַּה׳״ – לְרַבּוֹת קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹנוֹ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states with regard to the obligation to bring a guilt offering for robbery for taking a false oath concerning unlawful possession of the property of another: "If anyone sin, **and commit a trespass against the Lord,** and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, or of robbery, or have oppressed his neighbor" (Leviticus 5:21). The term "against the Lord" serves **to include** one who takes an oath with regard to another's **offerings of lesser sanctity, since they** are **the property of** their owner. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The same question arises for *muktzeh* (set aside for idolatry) and *ne'evad* (worshipped as an idol). Here the Gemara notes a crucial limiting principle: *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — one cannot impose a prohibition on something that is not his own. Once an animal has been sanctified as a *korban*, it is no longer the owner's property; he cannot then worship it and thereby impose the prohibition of *ne'evad* upon it. The only viable scenario of *muktzeh* or *ne'evad* that actually prohibits the animal is when the idolatry occurred before sanctification, meaning the animal was never *ra'uy* for *Ohel Moed* to begin with. If so, these cases seem to need no special verse — they are already excluded simply as not fit for *Ohel Moed*.

### Kodshim Kalim Mamon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע – דְּבַר עֶרְוָה. מוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד – עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara summarizes: **Therefore,** all of the cases listed in the mishna are cases in which the animal was initially fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting but was subsequently disqualified as an offering. **An animal that** actively **copulated with a person and an animal that was the object of bestiality** are disqualified after having been consecrated, due to **a matter of forbidden sexual intercourse.** An animal that was **set aside** for idol worship **or** one that **was worshipped** as an object of **idol worship** becomes forbidden after it was consecrated in the case **of an offering of lesser sanctity,** which according to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is the property of its owner.

The Gemara answers: the extra verse is required for the case of *kodshim kalim*, according to Rabbi Yosi HaGelili. Rabbi Yosi HaGelili holds that *kodshim kalim mamon be'alim hu* — *kodshim kalim* such as a *shelamim* remain, in a meaningful sense, the owner's property. Since the owner derives benefit from the meat and retains certain rights over the offering, Rabbi Yosi HaGelili considers the animal to still belong to him. Consequently, even after sanctification, if the owner worships the *shelamim* as an idol, he has the legal capacity to make it forbidden through that act of idolatry, since it remains "his." This creates a scenario where the animal was *ra'uy l'ohel moed* — it was a valid *korban shelamim* — and subsequently became disqualified through *ne'evad*. The verse *lifnei mishkan Hashem* is therefore needed to exclude such a case from *shechutei chutz* liability.

This is supported by the *baraita*: *u'maal maal b'Hashem* — regarding the violation of illicitly denying one's debt to a fellow Jew under oath — Rabbi Yosi HaGelili derives from the word *b'Hashem* that even *kodshim kalim* belong to the owner, such that stealing a friend's *shelamim* and denying it under oath constitutes theft and is actionable. The animal is sanctified, yet it is still considered the friend's property.

### Vladot Kodshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶתְנַן, וּמְחִיר, כִּלְאַיִם, יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן – בִּוְלָדוֹת קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as the **price** of a dog, or an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds,** or an animal **born by caesarean section,** none of which could have occured at a time that the animal was fit to be sacrificed, the mishna is referring **to the offspring of sacrificial** animals that were given as payment to a prostitute or as the price of a dog while in utero. These animals were fit to be brought as a sacrifice while they were still part of a consecrated animal, and only following birth are they considered to be unfit for sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר: וַלְדֵי קָדָשִׁים – בַּהֲוָיָיתָן הֵן קְדוֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Although these offspring are still part of a consecrated animal while in utero, and as such one might say that the status of payment to a prostitute or the price of a dog should not take effect with regard to them, the *tanna* of the mishna **holds** that with regard to **the offspring of sacrificial** animals, **they are sanctified** only **as they are** from the time of birth, but not in utero. Therefore, they can be disqualified by serving as payment to a prostitute or as the price of a dog.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין וְכוּ׳, אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cites a disagreement between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon with regard to temporarily **blemished** animals: Rabbi Shimon holds that one who sacrifices them outside the Temple courtyard violates a prohibition, as they will be fit for sacrifice after the passage of time, whereas the Rabbis hold that one is exempt. The mishna cites two similar disagreements: With regard to doves whose time of fitness has not yet arrived that are slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, and with regard to one who slaughters an animal **itself and its offspring** on one day, where the latter, which is not fit for being sacrificed until the next day, is slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard.

The *mishnah* also lists *etnan* (a gift given to a prostitute), *mechir* (exchange for a dog), *kilayim* (a hybrid animal), and *yotzei dofen* (an animal born by Caesarean section) as cases exempt from *shechutei chutz*. The Gemara asks: how can any of these cases involve an animal that was previously fit for *Ohel Moed*? The disqualifications of *kilayim* and *yotzei dofen* are present from birth — an animal born as a hybrid or born through Caesarean section has those disqualifications from the moment of birth. When was such an animal ever *ra'uy l'ohel moed*?

The Gemara answers: *b'vladot kodshim, v'kasavar vladot kodshimehavayatan hein kedoshim* — the cases involve offspring of sanctified animals, according to the opinion that animals born from sanctified mothers acquire their own sanctity only at the moment of birth, not while still in the womb. Under this view, a fetus in the womb of a sanctified animal is not yet independently sanctified. It is, however, already *ra'uy l'ohel moed* in a meaningful sense — it will be brought along with its mother, participating in the mother's sacrifice — even though it has not yet acquired its own *kedusha*. Because it lacks independent *kedusha*, the owner retains sufficient legal standing to affect it: he can give it (by deed or transaction) as *etnan* to a prostitute, exchange it for a dog, and a hybrid fetus would carry that status from conception. The fetus is simultaneously *ra'uy l'ohel moed* (in potentia, through the mother) and subject to all these disqualifications — because the absence of independent *kedusha* means the owner can still impose these prohibitions.

### Yotzei Dofen Cases

When the animal is subsequently born, it acquires its own *kedusha* at the moment of birth. Now it is independently sanctified — but it is also now manifestly *etnan*, *mechir*, *kilayim*, or *yotzei dofen*, and thus disqualified for the altar. We thus have a case that was at one stage *ra'uy l'ohel moed* — when it was in the womb — and is now disqualified. The verse *lifnei mishkan Hashem* is required to teach that despite the earlier period of fitness, the current disqualification removes any liability for *shechutei chutz*.

The case of *yotzei dofen* follows naturally: the fetus is *ra'uy l'ohel moed* while in the womb, and it only becomes a *yotzei dofen* upon being born through Caesarean section. At the moment of birth, it acquires its own *kedusha* and simultaneously its disqualification. Thus the verse is needed to clarify that even this case — where the transition from *ra'uy* to *pasul* is instantaneous — nonetheless removes *shechutei chutz* liability.
