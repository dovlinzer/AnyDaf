# Bava Batra 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wells & Structures

### Excluded Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא אֶת הַבּוֹר וְלֹא אֶת הַגַּת וְלֹא אֶת הַשּׁוֹבָךְ – בֵּין חֲרֵבִין, בֵּין יְשׁוּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In continuation of the previous mishna (68b) discussing one who sells a field, the mishna teaches that even if he says that he is selling it and everything that is in it, has sold **neither the cistern, nor the winepress, nor the dovecote, whether** it is **abandoned or utilized,** as these items are not part of the field itself.

The *daf* opens with a *Mishna* that will sound familiar, as it has been quoted before: when one sells a field, certain items within it are not included in the sale. The *Mishna* enumerates that one does not sell *velo et habor* — the wells in the field — *velo et hagat* — the oil press — and *velo et hashovach* — the pigeon coop — *bein charevin bein yushuvin*, whether these structures are currently in operation or lying desolate and empty. This applies even though such items are, presumably, essential components of a working field. The *bor* is perhaps the most striking example: one can imagine purchasing an entire wheat field and discovering that the wells necessary for irrigation are not included in the transaction.

A further question lurks here: are these excluded items in the same category as the *charuv hamurkav* and *sadan hashikma* — the grafted carob and the trimmed sycamore — which are treated as a conceptually independent field? In other words, does the exclusion of the well and pigeon coop apply only when the seller has not used the language *kol mah shebetochah* ("everything within it"), or does it apply even when such language was explicitly employed? The *Mishna* does not make this fully explicit, and the *Rashbam* will clarify the point below.

### Access Rights Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the seller **must purchase** for himself **a path** through the buyer's domain to reach whatever remains his. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** who holds that one who sells, sells generously; therefore, whatever is not explicitly excluded from the sale is assumed to be sold, and it is presumed that the seller did not retain for himself the right to the path that he requires to access his property. **And the Rabbis say:** The seller **need not** purchase a path through the buyer's domain, as it is assumed that since the seller withholds these items for himself, he also reserves a path to reach them.

The *Mishna* continues: *vetzarich likach lo derech* — the seller, having retained ownership of the well, must nonetheless acquire separate access rights in order to reach it. He owns the well but has no right of passage through the buyer's field to get to it. This, says the *Mishna*, is the position of Rabbi Akiva — *divrei Rabbi Akiva*. His reasoning, as the *Rashbam* makes clear, is not that generosity (*be'ayin yaffah*) compels one to give the well to the buyer. Rather, it is the opposite application of the same principle: what the seller *did* sell, he sold fully and generously. Having conveyed the field to the buyer, he does not also retain an access path through it to his retained well. The Sages, however — *vachachamim omrim einu tzarich* — hold that the seller need not separately acquire access; just as he withheld the well, he implicitly withheld the access path as well. The buyer has the land; the seller retains access.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״חוּץ מֵאֵלּוּ״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva concedes that when** the seller **says to** the buyer in the bill of sale that he is selling the field **apart from these** things, i.e., the cistern and the winepress, **he need not purchase for himself a path** through the buyer's domain.

The *Mishna* adds that Rabbi Akiva concedes one case: *umodeh Rabbi Akiva bizman she'amar lo chutz me'eilu* — if the seller explicitly stated "I am selling you everything *except* for the well," the use of that explicit exclusionary language indicates that he was also retaining access rights. Since he need not have said "except for the well" — the well would not have been sold by default — the fact that he chose to articulate the exclusion signals that he is retaining more than just the physical well; he is retaining the full complex of rights including access.

### Well Sold, Field Retained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָן לְאַחֵר – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: צָרִיךְ לִיקַּח לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** But if the seller kept the field but **sold** the cistern and winepress **to another** person, **Rabbi Akiva says:** The buyer **need not purchase for himself a path** through the seller's domain to reach what he has bought, since a seller sells generously. **But the Rabbis say: He must purchase for himself a path** through the seller's domain.

The *Mishna* then presents the mirror case: *macharan le'acher* — the seller retains the field and sells the well to someone else. Rabbi Akiva says *einu tzarich likach lo derech*: the buyer of the well does not need to separately acquire access rights, because *be'ayin yaffah hu mocher* — the seller sells generously. This case is necessary to state precisely because one might have supposed that the Sages' position reflected not a *be'ayin yaffah* versus *be'ayin ra'ah* (generous versus narrow eye) framework, but rather a rule that access always travels with the well — whether one keeps the well and keeps access, or sells the well and conveys access. The *Mishna* disabuses us of that reading. The Sages — *vachachamim omrim tzarich likach lo derech* — say that even when the well is sold, the buyer does not automatically receive access rights; he must acquire them separately. The structure, then, is symmetric: Rabbi Akiva consistently favors the buyer and the Sages consistently favor the seller.

### Bein Rah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמוֹכֵר, אֲבָל בְּנוֹתֵן מַתָּנָה – נוֹתֵן אֶת כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** that these items are excluded, **said?** It is said **with regard to one who sells** a field, **but with regard to one who gives** it away as **a gift,** it is assumed that **he gives all of it,** including everything found in the field.

The *Mishna* then delimits where all of the foregoing applies: *bameh devarim amurim bemocher* — this debate about withholding or granting rights operates in the context of a sale, where the assumption is *be'ayin ra'ah mocher*, the seller acts with a narrow eye, keeping as much as possible. *Aval benoten matanah* — but when one gives a gift, *noten et kullah* — one gives the whole thing, including, according to the Sages, the access rights as well.

### Gift Inclusion

The *Rashbam* broadens this principle considerably. He explains that the *bameh devarim amurim* qualification — that the restrictive rules apply only to a sale — extends beyond the question of access rights to the very question of which physical objects are included. The *Rashbam* identifies three categories of items in relation to a field sale. The first category is items that are routinely included in a sale by default: certain types of stones, wood, and harvested grain already present. The second category consists of items not normally included but that pass if the seller uses the phrase *kol mah shebetochah* — "everything within it." The third category — the most counterintuitive — contains items so conceptually distinct from the field that even the formula *kol mah shebetochah* does not capture them: the grafted carob, the trimmed sycamore, the well, the pigeon coop. These are treated as a virtually independent field and resist transfer even under the broadest conventional language.

And yet the *Rashbam* argues — noting that the point is borne out by the subsequent discussion of *hekdesh* — that even this third category is included when the conveyance takes the form of a *matanah*, a gift. The rationale, as he phrases it, is *denotayn be'ayin yafah notayn yotayr midai* — the giver of a gift gives excessively generously. Although the carob and sycamore are conceptually a separate field, they are nonetheless physically located within the field, and that physical connection is sufficient when the transfer is a gift.

The *Rashbam* adds one limitation: if there happens to be something in the field that has no conceptual connection to the field whatsoever — a dropped wallet, cut grain merely stored there temporarily and drawing nothing from the land — that would not be transferred even in a gift. Likewise, anything so obviously disconnected that the *Tanna* of our *Mishna* had no need to mention it as a case of "not sold" would not be included. The principle of *matanah* generosity, while broad, does not extend to items that are entirely adventitious to the field.

## Inheritance Division

### Complete Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ, זָכוּ בַּשָּׂדֶה – זָכוּ בְּכוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **brothers who divide** their father's estate among themselves, when they each **acquire** a **field** as part of their inheritance, **they acquire all of it,** including the items that would be excluded from a sale.

The *Mishna* proceeds to the case of *haachin shechileku* — brothers who divide a field inherited from their father. The ruling is *zakhu basadeh zakhu bekulah*: when a brother takes possession of the field, he takes possession of all of it, including the well, the pigeon coop, and every other item that would ordinarily be excluded from a sale.

### Why Different

One might ask why division of an inherited estate differs from a sale between strangers. The *Rashbam* explains: *keivan sheze natul sadeh achat veze achat kenegdo histalek ze mishel ze nistaken lo* — since each brother took one portion against the other's portion, each has fully separated himself from the other. The underlying logic of *chalukah* is not a commercial transaction in which each party carefully calibrates what he is conveying; rather, it is a clean break, a complete mutual withdrawal from the shared estate, so that each brother walks away fully independent of the other. The well falls into one brother's portion not by explicit agreement but as a consequence of the fundamental premise of the division: you take that half entirely, I take this half entirely, and we are done.

This may seem counterintuitive. In a commercial sale between strangers, one expects finality — the seller takes his money and the buyer takes his field and they have nothing further to do with each other, which would seem to argue for full inclusion. And when brothers divide a *yerushah*, one might expect that the bonds of family would leave more room for ongoing shared rights. But the logic runs the other way: a commercial sale is a transaction governed by *be'ayin ra'ah*, the seller holding back as much as possible, whereas an inheritance division is premised on total, clean separation. The *Rashbam* confirms: the point of splitting an estate is precisely that each party should be able to go his own way without any residual entanglement.

A subtler question arises with access. Suppose the well falls on one brother's side of the line while the other brother needs access to the public domain through the same plot. There the *Rashbam* acknowledges that an access right might survive the division, not because the well is jointly owned, but on the separate ground that when their father was alive the entire field had free internal access, and one brother may argue he has not relinquished that inherited right of passage. That, however, is a distinct question of access, not ownership, and it is not the focus of the present *Mishna*.

## Hefker Possession

### Chazaka Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּחֲזִיק בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר, הֶחְזִיק בַּשָּׂדֶה – הֶחְזִיק בְּכוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** So too, with regard to **one who takes possession of the property of a convert,** when **he takes possession of a field, he takes possession of all of it.**

The *Mishna* continues: *hamachzik benichsei hager* — one who takes possession of the estate of a convert who died without heirs, whose property becomes *hefker*. The rule is *hechzik basadeh hechzik bechulah*: taking possession of the field through an act of *chazakah* acquires everything in it. This includes the very items — the well, the pigeon coop, the conceptually distinct trees — that are not transferred even by the formula *kol mah shebetochah* in an ordinary sale.

### Chazaka vs. Sales

This result is striking. We have gone to considerable lengths to establish that the carob and sycamore trees, the well, and similar items are so independent from the field that even explicit all-inclusive language fails to convey them in a sale. Yet a simple act of *chazakah* — a man digging with a shovel in one corner of a *hefker* field — acquires all of these things simultaneously. How can that be?

### Rashbam's Explanation

The *Rashbam* resolves this by drawing on a distinction already established elsewhere in the *masechta*. If a *hefker* field has a *metzar* — a boundary line physically dividing it into two portions — then a *chazakah* on one portion does not acquire the other, because the boundary line renders them conceptually separate fields; acquiring one field does not acquire a different field. However, when two fields are conceptually distinct — as with the field and the sycamore tree — but there is no physical *metzar* separating them, an act of *chazakah* on one does extend to the other. The *Rashbam* is walking a carefully calibrated line: these items are distinct enough that a sale, which transfers only what was expressly or conventionally understood to be included, does not transfer them; but they are not separated by a physical boundary, and therefore a *chazakah*, which acquires whatever is physically encompassed by the act, does extend to them. In the case of *hefker* property, the absence of a formal separating line means that a single act of *chazakah* reaches everything.

## Hekdesh & Rav Shimon

### Consecration Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One who consecrates a field has consecrated all of it.**

The final clause of the *Mishna* states: *hamakdish et hasadeh hikdish et kulah* — one who consecrates his field to *hekdesh* has consecrated all of it. Just as giving a *matanah* draws on the *be'ayin yafah* principle and includes even the third category of conceptually distinct items, so too consecrating a field to the Temple treasury is treated with similar inclusiveness, on the premise that one who gives to *hekdesh* gives generously.

### Rav Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא אֶת הֶחָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב, וְאֶת סַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: One who consecrates a field has not consecrated** any of the items that are ordinarily excluded from a sale **except for the grafted carob tree and the sycamore trunk.**

Rabbi Shimon, however, disputes this. *Hamakdish et hasadeh lo hikdish ela hacharuv hamurkav vesadan hashikma* — one who consecrates a field has consecrated *only* the grafted carob and the trimmed sycamore trees, but not the well, the pigeon coop, or the other items in the analogous category. Rabbi Shimon thus takes an intermediate position: *hekdesh* is not as broad as a *matanah*. The field itself is consecrated, and the trees are included — but the well and similar structures are not.

This immediately raises a question: Rabbi Shimon does not seem to dispute the rule regarding *matanah*. By a *matanah*, everything goes. By *hekdesh*, most of the analogous items do not go — yet the trees specifically do. Why are the trees singled out? What makes them different from the well and the pigeon coop? The *Gemara* will address this question at length below.

## Sale vs. Gift Logic

### Yehuda's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא מֶכֶר וּמַאי שְׁנָא מַתָּנָה? פֵּירֵשׁ יְהוּדָה בֶּן נְקוֹסָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי: זֶה פֵּירֵשׁ וְזֶה לֹא פֵּירֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is a sale different from** a gift, **and** in **what** way **is a gift different from** a sale? Why does the mishna distinguish between the two with regard to what is retained by the prior owner? **Yehuda ben Nekosa explained before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: The difference between the cases is that **this** one, the seller, **specified** that certain items were not included in the sale, **and that** one, the donor, **did not specify.**

The *Gemara* now asks *mai shna mecher u'mai shna matanah* — what precisely is the difference between a sale and a gift that accounts for the dramatic difference in what is included? The obvious answer is *be'ayin yafah*, but as the *Rashbam* noted, generosity alone does not fully explain why items that are not even included under *kol mah shebetochah* should suddenly be included in a gift. The *Gemara* reports that *Yehuda bin Nakusa* explained this before *Rebbi* using the formula *zeh peireish vezeh lo peireish* — "this one explicated and this one did not explicate." This is cryptic at best. The *Gemara* immediately challenges it: *zeh lo peireish vezeh lo peireish* — in neither a sale nor a gift does either party actually spell out whether the well or the trees are included. Both cases involve an absence of explicit language. The formula explains nothing.

### Silence Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי "זֶה פֵּירֵשׁ וְזֶה לֹא פֵּירֵשׁ"?! זֶה לֹא פֵּירֵשׁ וְזֶה לֹא פֵּירֵשׁ הוּא! אֶלָּא זֶה הָיָה לוֹ לְפָרֵשׁ, וְזֶה לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can it be suggested that **this** one **specified and that** one **did not specify,** when in fact **this** one **did not specify, and that one did not specify,** as in neither case did the prior owner specify what items he was reserving for himself? **Rather,** the difference is that **this** one, the buyer, **should have specified** that those items that are not integral parts of the field are nevertheless included in the sale, and since he neglected to do so, he suffers the loss. **But** in the case of a gift, **that** one, the recipient, **should not have specified** what was included in the gift, as it would have been inappropriate for him to act in this manner.

The *Gemara* therefore offers the correct formulation: *ela zeh haya lo lefareish vezeh lo haya lo lefareish* — in one case the party *should have* explicated, and in the other case the party was *not in a position* to explicate. *Rabbeinu Gershom* and *Rabbeinu Chananel* explain as follows. In the case of a sale, the buyer is paying good money for the field. He has every right — indeed, every practical incentive — to turn to the seller and insist on clarity: does the transaction include the well? Does it include the trees? Does it include the pigeon coop? He could have demanded that these items be specified in the *shtar*. The fact that he did not do so is on him. He *haya lo lefareish* — he was in a position to demand explication and failed to do so. We therefore cannot assume he received what he did not ask for.

### Generosity & Silence

The recipient of a gift, however, is in an entirely different position. When someone announces a generous gift, the recipient cannot respond by saying, "Wait — does this include the trees? Does this include the well? I need you to be explicit." Such a demand would be the height of ingratitude. The receiver of a gift is simply not socially or halachically in a position to push back and require the giver to enumerate and specify every component. Since the recipient *lo haya lo lefareish* — was not in a position to demand explication — the absence of explicit language on his side cannot be taken as evidence that those items were not included. When this incapacity to demand clarification is combined with the underlying principle of *be'ayin yafah noten* — that one who gives a gift gives with a generous eye — the result is that we interpret the gift as including even those items that would not pass in a sale under *kol mah shebetochah*. The two principles reinforce each other: generosity of intent on the giver's side, and the impossibility of requesting clarification on the receiver's side, together produce the expansive rule of inclusion in the case of a *matanah*.

## House Gift Case

### Barrel Capacity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: "הַבוּ לֵיהּ לִפְלָנְיָא בֵּיתָא, דְּמַחְזִיק מְאָה גּוּלְפֵי". אִשְׁתְּכַח דַּהֲוָה מַחְזִיק מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִין, אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: מְאָה אֲמַר לֵיהּ, מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִין לָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that there was **a certain** person **who said to** others: **Give to so-and-so** my **house containing 100 barrels [*gulfei*]** as a gift. **It was found that there was** a house in his possession that **contained 120** barrels. **Mar Zutra said:** The owner **said to him** that he was giving him a house containing **100** barrels, **and** he **did not say to him** that he was giving him a house containing **120** barrels. Therefore, the recipient receives only the portion of the house that holds 100 barrels, and not the rest of the house.

The *Gemara* presents a practical application of these principles. A dying man instructed his heirs to give a particular person *beita d'machzik mea garvei* — "my house that holds a hundred barrels." When the heirs examined the one house known to belong to the deceased, they found it held not one hundred but one hundred and twenty barrels. The man apparently did not own a house of precisely one hundred barrels capacity. The question becomes: how do we interpret the instruction? Did he give nothing, since no such house exists? Did he give five-sixths of the house, corresponding to one hundred of the one hundred and twenty barrels? The case has the character of a deathbed gift, the paradigmatic context in which spoken words alone are binding.

### Generous Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, מִי לָא תְּנַן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמוֹכֵר, אֲבָל בְּנוֹתֵן מַתָּנָה – נוֹתֵן אֶת כּוּלָּן? אַלְמָא מַאן דְּיָהֵיב מַתָּנָה – בְּעַיִן יָפָה יָהֵיב; הָכָא נָמֵי, מַאן דְּיָהֵיב מַתָּנָה – בְּעַיִן יָפָה יָהֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Didn't we learn** in the mishna here: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to one who sells** a field, **but with regard to one who gives** it away as **a gift,** it is assumed that **he gives all of it. Apparently, one who gives a gift gives generously. Here too,** then, say that **one who gives a gift gives generously,** even if he is not always precise in his wording. Therefore, it should be assumed that the donor intended to give the recipient the entire house, which contains more than 100 barrels.

*Mar Zutra* initially observes that the man said one hundred, not one hundred and twenty, seemingly limiting the gift. But *Rav Ashi* responds by citing our *Mishna*: *mi lo tnan bim'deamran bimchar aval b'noten matanah noten et kulan* — did we not learn that in cases of sale the restrictive rules apply, but in the case of a gift one gives all of it? *Alma man deyahiv matanah be'ayin yafah yahiv; hacha nami man deyahiv matanah be'ayin yafah yahiv* — the principle is that whoever gives a gift gives with a generous eye, and so here too the gift should be interpreted generously. The figure of one hundred barrels was, on this reading, approximate — the man was characterizing the house, not specifying a fractional share — and the proper interpretation is that he gave the entire house. Rav Ashi thus establishes a broader hermeneutical principle: whenever a gift can be interpreted either narrowly or generously, we interpret it in the most generous way possible.

## Sale vs. Gift Logic (II)

### Gift Annulment

It is worth noting, however, that the expansive rules governing gifts are not without a counterbalance, though the subject is not fully developed in the *Gemara* here. The principle of *devarim shebelev einam devarim* — that unexpressed conditions or assumptions ordinarily have no legal force — applies quite differently in the context of gifts than in the context of sales.

The *Gemarot* elsewhere discuss cases in which a person gave away all his property believing, for instance, that his son had died, and the gift was subsequently voided when the son turned out to be alive. Some *Rishonim* attempt to explain such rulings by arguing that the assumption in question was *belev kol adam* — obvious to everyone, not merely a private unexpressed thought. But the *Rambam* and the *Tosafot Rid* articulate a more fundamental distinction. They hold that *devarim shebelev einam devarim* is primarily a rule governing commercial transactions: in a *mecher*, where Michael is giving money and receiving something in return, any condition must be made explicit, because one party cannot fairly be bound by unstated assumptions in the other's mind to which he had no access. But a gift is a unilateral act. The recipient contributes nothing; the entire transaction flows from the giver. Because it is entirely the giver's act, his subjective frame of mind — *da'at hanoten* — carries legal weight in a way it does not in a bilateral sale. As the *Rambam* formulates it: *le'olam omdin da'at hanoten* — we always seek to assess the subjective intent of the one who gave the gift.

The result is an interesting duality. On one side, gifts are interpreted expansively and generously in favor of the recipient: more is included, the language is read broadly, and the recipient's inability to demand clarification reinforces the presumption of inclusion. On the other side, because the gift is wholly the giver's act, there are circumstances — false assumptions, conditions that turned out to be unfounded — under which the entire gift can be voided, in ways that would never apply to a completed sale. The receiver gains more, but the gift is also more vulnerable to nullification.

## Hekdesh Trees

### Trees Retained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: הַקּוֹנֶה שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא קָנָה קַרְקַע, מָכַר קַרְקַע וְשִׁיֵּיר שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת לְפָנָיו – יֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Even though the Rabbis said:** If **one buys two trees in** the field **of another, he does not acquire** any of **the land** but acquires only the trees, and if **he sells land** to another, **and** he **retains two trees for himself, he** also **retains the land** around those trees,

The *Gemara* now turns to the position of *Rav Huna*, who articulates a principle that lies at the heart of the discussion of *hekdesh* and trees. The principle concerns what happens when a seller retains trees for himself while selling the surrounding land. *Rav Huna* states: *af al gav de'amur rabanan hakoneh shenei ilanot betoch shel chaveiro harei zeh lo kana karka* — even though the Sages ruled that one who *purchases* two trees within his neighbor's field does not acquire the land between or around them. Nevertheless, *machar karka veshayer shenei ilanot lefanav yesh lo karka* — if one sells his land while *retaining* ownership of two trees for himself, he does retain rights in the land. This asymmetry — buying trees does not convey land rights, but retaining trees while selling the surrounding land does — is *Rav Huna*'s core observation.

What is the scope of the land rights retained? Here the *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* diverge. The *Rashbam* understands *yesh lo karka* to mean that the seller retains full ownership of the land beneath the trees, such that if the tree dies he may replant. *Tosafot* reads the case more narrowly: the retained rights do not extend to full replanting rights, but they do include the right to keep the tree in place and to have it continue drawing nutrients from the surrounding soil, as well as rights to that plot that persist as long as the tree is standing — for instance, the right to plant a vegetable garden in the area of soil over which the tree's canopy extends. The analysis that follows in the *Gemara* fits somewhat better with *Tosafot*'s reading, and it will be taken as the operative framework here.

### Nutrients Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: מוֹכֵר בְּעַיִן יָפָה מוֹכֵר – הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבֵּי בּוֹר וָדוּת, דְּלָא קָא מַכְחֲשִׁי בְּאַרְעָא; אֲבָל אִילָנוֹת, דְּקָא מַכְחֲשִׁי בְּאַרְעָא – אִם אִיתָא דְּלָא שַׁיַּיר, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: "עֲקוֹר אִילָנָךְ, שְׁקוֹל וְזִיל".
>
> **Translation:** **And even according to Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **one who sells, sells generously** and does not retain anything for himself, **this does not always apply,** as **this statement** applies only **with regard to a pit and a cistern, which do not weaken the land,** and therefore the seller feels no need to protect himself from the potential claims of the buyer. **But** with regard to **trees, which do weaken the land,** as they draw water and nutrients from the soil, **if it is so that** the prior owner **did not retain** some of the land for himself, **let** the buyer **say to him: Uproot your trees, take** them, **and go,** as the trees draw water and nutrients from the soil, causing damage to the buyer's land. Therefore, the seller must have retained for himself the land needed for these trees.

The critical question is: why should retaining a tree confer land rights, given that under Rabbi Akiva's principle of *be'ayin yafah mocher* — the seller sells generously — the seller should be conveying all rights in the land fully to the buyer, retaining only the bare tree? The answer turns on the physical reality of how a tree exists. *Aval ilanot d'kamachshei b'ara* — trees draw nutrients from the land. Unlike a well or a cistern, which sits in the ground without ongoing interaction with the surrounding soil, a tree is in a perpetual relationship with the land: its roots draw moisture and nutrients continuously. This means that retaining the tree necessarily entails retaining something in the land — namely, the right for the tree to continue drawing from it.

The contrast with a well is instructive. *Hani milei gabei bor v'dos* — the rule that Rabbi Akiva's *be'ayin yafah* principle strips the retained well of access rights applies to a well or cistern. The reasoning is that the presence of a well in Michael's field does not damage Michael's field in any ongoing way. The structure is already there; it is not drawing on the land; Michael has no complaint that the retained well is injuring his property. Therefore, by selling generously, the seller conveys everything — including the access path — and retains only the well structure itself, with no access.

But a tree is different. *Im issa d'lo shiyar* — if I retain no rights in the land when I keep the tree, *leima leh akur ilanch v'zil* — Michael could demand that I uproot my tree, because my tree is actively drawing the nutrients from his land. Since ownership of the tree would be illusory without the right to have it remain in the ground and continue drawing nutrients, that right must be retained as well. Once we acknowledge that the seller necessarily retains some land rights when he keeps a tree, *Rav Huna* argues that those rights extend further — even to uses of the land under the tree's canopy, such as a vegetable garden — at least according to the *Rabbanan* who hold *be'ayin ra'ah mocher*. And as the *Gemara* will develop, the scope of those rights has implications for how we understand Rabbi Shimon's position on *hekdesh* as well.

## Rav Shimon Model

### Me'ilah Avoidance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא חָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב, וְסַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: "אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְיוֹנְקִין מִשְּׂדֵה הֶקְדֵּשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **We learned** in the mishna here that **Rabbi Shimon says: One who consecrates a field has not consecrated** any of the items that are ordinarily excluded from a sale **except for the grafted carob tree and the sycamore trunk. And it is taught with regard to** this in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** that it is specifically the carob tree and the sycamore trunk that are consecrated? **Since** they **draw** their **nutrients from a consecrated field,** the owner must have had in mind to consecrate them as well, as otherwise his trees would be nurtured from consecrated property.

We now return to Rabbi Shimon's ruling: *hamakdish et hasadeh lo hikdish ela hacharuv hamurkav vesadan hashikma* — one who consecrates his field has consecrated only the trees, not the well, the pigeon coop, and similar items. The *braita* cited by the *Gemara* supplies Rabbi Shimon's own reasoning: *ma ta'am — ho'il veyonkim misday hekdesh* — the reason the trees are included in the *hekdesh* is that they draw their nutrients from the *hekdesh* field. This is a decisive consideration. If I consecrate my field to *hekdesh* while retaining personal ownership of the trees, my trees would continue drawing nutrients from *hekdesh* property. That would constitute *me'ilah* — the prohibition against benefiting from *hekdesh* property — since the trees would be using the consecrated field's resources for private gain. To avoid this, we conclude that one who consecrates the field necessarily consecrates the trees along with it. One would not want to keep trees in a *hekdesh* field and thereby be continuously transgressing *me'ilah*.

The *Rashbam* also suggests an alternative formulation: since the trees draw from the *hekdesh* field, their growth is *gidulim* of *hekdesh* — produce of consecrated property — and belongs to *hekdesh*. The difficulty with this reading is that the main tree itself predates the consecration; only subsequent growth would be attributable to the *hekdesh* field. The stronger reading, then, is the first: the trees are included not because of a generosity principle, but as a practical necessity to avoid ongoing *me'ilah*.

### Retaining Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ שַׁיּוֹרֵי שַׁיַּיר, כִּי קָא יָנְקִי – מִדְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָא יָנְקִי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind,** as Rav Huna claims, that when the seller retains certain trees for himself, he also **retains** the land around them so that they will be nurtured from soil that belongs to him, what is the reason for Rabbi Shimon's ruling? **When** these trees **draw** their **nutrients, they draw** their **nutrients from** the ground that the consecrator had retained for himself that still **belongs to him,** not from consecrated property.

The *Gemara* now raises a sharp challenge to Rabbi Shimon's reasoning. If Rabbi Shimon treats *hekdesh* like *mecher* rather than like *matanah*, that means the consecrator retains ownership of things like the well and the pigeon coop. Fine. But why could he not also retain the trees? The *Gemara* proposes: let him retain the tree, and — since retaining a tree, as *Rav Huna* taught, grants him land rights in the surrounding soil — those land rights would include the right of the tree to be *yonek*, to draw nutrients from the field. This is precisely the classic case *Rav Huna* described: the seller retains the tree, therefore the tree retains the right to draw from the land. On that model, there would be no *me'ilah* problem, because the consecrator still legitimately owns the rights for his tree to draw from what is now the *hekdesh* field. Why, then, must the trees be consecrated along with the field?

### Rabbi Akiva Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר – כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר – כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The assumption that Rav Huna's statement is true according to everyone must be reconsidered. Rather, **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that one who consecrates his field does not retain for himself the land around the trees, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of his teacher, **Rabbi Akiva.** According to Rabbi Akiva, one who sells, sells generously, and there is no presumption that he retained some item or right for himself unless this was stated explicitly. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon rules that one who consecrates his field has also consecrated the carob trees, as otherwise they would draw nutrients from consecrated land. **And Rav Huna, who says** that when a seller retains trees for himself he also retains the land around them, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who say that one who sells, sells sparingly.

The *Gemara* resolves this by establishing that Rabbi Shimon operates according to the model of Rabbi Akiva. *Rabbi Shimon d'amar k'Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Shimon follows Rabbi Akiva's principle of *be'ayin yafah mocher*. And under Rabbi Akiva's framework, unlike the *Rabbanan*, the seller does *not* retain land rights when he keeps a tree. The well case makes this clear: Rabbi Akiva says that retaining the well does not preserve access rights, because *be'ayin yafah mocher* — whatever was actually sold was sold fully. By the same logic, when one sells the land and retains the tree, Rabbi Akiva would say the seller does not retain rights to the land under or around the tree. The *be'ayin yafah* principle conveys the land completely, and the retained tree is left without any accompanying land rights.

This means that according to Rabbi Akiva, retaining a tree while selling the surrounding field is a somewhat precarious arrangement: the buyer of the field could, it seems, demand that the tree be uprooted, since the tree-owner retains no rights to draw on the sold soil. Rabbi Shimon, working within Rabbi Akiva's framework, therefore concludes that when one consecrates a field, one necessarily consecrates the trees as well — because under Rabbi Akiva's model, there are no retained land rights that would permit the trees to lawfully continue drawing from the now-consecrated field. The trees must go to *hekdesh* in order to avoid the tree-owner being continuously in violation of *me'ilah*.

The result is that Rabbi Shimon's position is a hybrid: like Rabbi Akiva, he holds *be'ayin yafah mocher* (or in this case, *be'ayin yafah makdish*), and unlike the view that *hekdesh* is equivalent to *matanah*, he treats *hekdesh* as equivalent to *mecher*. The field is consecrated with the generosity appropriate to a formal transfer, but without the all-inclusive scope of a *matanah*. The well, the pigeon coop, and similar items are retained — but the trees cannot be, precisely because of the *yonek* problem under Rabbi Akiva's model.

### Land Rights Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבָּנַן?! פְּשִׁיטָא! נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ – דְּאִי נָפְלִי, הָדַר שָׁתֵיל לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If Rav Huna's statement is only **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** then isn't his statement **obvious?** What novel idea is he adding? The Gemara answers: The practical **difference** is **that** while one might have thought that the prior owner retains a right to the land only for the sake of trees that were there, this is not the case. Rather, he retains absolute ownership of the land, and therefore, **if** the trees **fall** or die **he can plant them again.**

Given this analysis, the *Gemara* revisits *Rav Huna*'s original ruling. *Rav Huna* stated that when one sells the land while retaining the trees, *yesh lo karka* — he retains land rights. The *Gemara* initially notes that this seems obvious according to the *Rabbanan*, who hold *be'ayin ra'ah mocher*: if the seller withholds access to his well and retains other prerogatives, it is no surprise that retaining a tree comes with land rights. *Kerabanan peshita* — it is self-evident under the *Rabbanan*'s framework.

The *Gemara* responds: *nafka mina denafala hadar shatil lehu* — the practical novelty of *Rav Huna*'s ruling is not about the rights that exist while the tree stands, but about what happens *if* the tree falls or is uprooted. On *Tosafot*'s reading, which the *Gemara* appears to favor, the first formulation of *Rav Huna*'s principle — that the retained tree grants rights sufficient for the tree to draw nutrients and perhaps even to use the surrounding plot — may have been obvious. The *chiddush* is that according to the *Rabbanan*, the rights retained are not merely functional rights tied to the existence of that particular tree; they are genuine, lasting rights in that plot of land, such that even if the tree dies the owner retains the right to replant. The seller does not merely have a license that expires with the tree; he holds real property rights in perpetuity over that area. That is *Rav Huna*'s true novelty, and it is what distinguishes his ruling from a straightforward application of *be'ayin ra'ah mocher*.
