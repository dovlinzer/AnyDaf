# Sanhedrin 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ben Sira & Apocrypha

### External Books Ban

Today's daf is 101. We pick up in the middle of 100b, where — if one looks at the left-hand column between Rashi and the Gemara — one finds a reference to Mishlei 12. The discussion concerns *ben Sira*, because one of the individuals listed as lacking a *chelek olam haba*, according to one opinion, is someone who is *kore besfarim chitzonim* — who reads from the apocrypha, from external books, presumably works that did not make it into the canon but were attributed with some degree of holy significance. To that category, a *tanna* adds even one who reads from *ben Sira*.

Now, this addition does not appear in the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah records only Rabbi Akiva's formulation of *kore besfarim hachitzonim*; it is in a *baraita* of Rav Yosef that *sefer Ben Sira* is included. The Gemara then asks: what exactly is so problematic about *ben Sira*?

### Gemara Quotes Ben Sira

Before proceeding to the Gemara's answer, one must appreciate a striking irony at the outset: the Gemara demonstrates complete fluency in *ben Sira* in the very course of discussing whether one is permitted to read it. It goes on to quote verse after verse from *ben Sira*, and at certain points even uses the word *dechtiv* — a term the Gemara ordinarily reserves for scriptural citation. The subtext is clear: either the Gemara does not rule like Rav Yosef, or — to the extent that it does — one has to understand Rav Yosef's concern in a much more limited sense.

### Interpretation Debate

The most natural reading of that limitation is that the prohibition applies only when one treats these non-canonical texts *as* scripture — as holy writ — or when one studies them in a manner that elevates them to the status of *Torah*, effectively blurring the line between the canon and what lies outside it. Reading *ben Sira* the way one might read Poor Richard's Almanac, or Confucius, for whatever wisdom one finds there — that is presumably not the concern. The problem is assuming the text is divinely inspired and therefore treating everything in it as authoritative and binding.

One student raised a subtle grammatical observation worth noting: the Mishnah uses the formulation *kore basfarim hachitzonim*, with a *bet*, rather than *kore et hasefer* or *kore sefer*. The *bet* may itself be suggestive — it implies a mode of engagement, a manner of reading, rather than the mere act of picking up the book. One might also suggest that the concern is not only about elevating the apocrypha to scriptural status in isolation, but about blurring lines more broadly: once one begins treating pseudo-biblical works as equivalent to the canon, the category of "what counts as scripture" becomes destabilized — and from there one cannot know where the erosion stops.

It was also noted, correctly, that immediately before Rav Yosef's statement about *ben Sira*, there is a *baraita* that concludes with a reference to *sifrei minim* — heretical books, which in this context likely refers to early Jewish-Christian writings. That juxtaposition is telling.

### Ben Sira Problems

Having framed the discussion, the Gemara turns to identifying which specific passages in *ben Sira* are problematic. The first verse offered is: *lo ta'il davya belibakh, dgavya gavrin ketila* — do not let anguish enter your heart, for anguish has slain the mightiest of men. The Gemara asks: what is wrong with that? It is essentially the same as what Shlomo says in Mishlei: *de'aga belev ish yis-chena* — anguish in the heart of a man lays him low. And indeed, Rav Ami and Rav Assi then offer two readings of that very Mishlei verse: one says *yesichenah mi-da'ato*, that one should try to suppress or set aside the worry; the other says *yesichenah le-acherim*, that one should speak about it to others — in effect, go find someone to talk to.

The Gemara proceeds to identify more genuinely problematic material. One verse reads: *mena rabbim mi-toch beitecha, velo ha-kol tavi el beitecha* — keep the masses out of your house, and do not bring everyone home. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi himself said the same thing — that a person should not crowd his house with too many guests — citing the verse *ish re'im lehitro'ea*, which is interpreted to mean that having too many acquaintances in one's home can lead to destruction of one's property or peace. So this verse from *ben Sira* is not problematic — it aligns perfectly with rabbinic teaching.

Then comes what the Gemara presents as genuinely foolish material. The verse reads: *zal dikan korteman* — a man with a thin beard is wise; *avdikan sachsachan* — a man with a thick beard is a fool. Then: someone who blows on his cup to cool it clearly cannot be very thirsty; someone who asks what would go nicely on his bread evidently cannot be very hungry — so take the bread away from him. And someone with a split down the middle of his beard — no one can stand up against him, presumably because he is always stroking it conspiratorially. Rashi offers the reading that such a man is sly and calculating.

So the problem with *ben Sira* comes into focus: the book contains material that is genuinely wise and parallel to received tradition, but it also contains material that is simply foolish — physiognomic speculation dressed up with the same literary gravity as serious moral teaching.

### Rav Yosef's Defense

Rav Yosef himself addresses this directly: *milei ma'alyata de-it bei darshinan le-hu* — the good material in it, we can still expound. Fine; some of it is foolish. But the worthy passages can still be treated with the weight of *derash*. And one student astutely noted that it is Rav Yosef himself — the very authority who declared *sefer Ben Sira* included in the ban — who now qualifies his own ruling. Perhaps his original statement was never meant to prohibit reading altogether, but rather to warn against treating the text as holy writ indiscriminately. By standing up and expounding its better passages, he is clarifying that the proper relationship with *ben Sira* is selective and critical, not reverential.

### Ben Sira on Women

The Gemara then proceeds to cite a series of passages from *ben Sira* that Rav Yosef commends as worthy of *derash*. A good woman is a good gift — *ishah tovah matanah tovah* — and should be given to a man who fears God. A wicked woman, by contrast, is like a plague of *tzara'at* to her husband. The Gemara interjects — *mai takan-teih?* what is his remedy? — and the answer comes: let him drive her from his house and he will be healed of his plague.

A beautiful woman makes her husband fortunate — *ishah yafah ashrei ba'alah* — and his days will be as though doubled. But — *ha'alem einecha me-eshet chen* — hide your eyes from a charming woman who belongs to your friend, lest you be caught in her net. Do not incline yourself toward your friend's home, and do not go there to drink with him wine and beer, *ki b'toar isha yafa rabim hushchatu* — because through the appearance of a beautiful woman many have been destroyed, and numerous are those she has brought down.

*Rabim hayu pitzei rochel* — many are the wounds of the perfume merchant, because he spends his time among women, and is either suspected of improper relations or actually falls into them — with the result that husbands come and beat him. *Hamargilim l'dvar erva* — those who habituate themselves to exposure to forbidden sexual scenarios are asking for catastrophe. *Ki nitzotz mav'ir gachelet* — it is like a spark landing on a smoldering ember. The image is apt: the ember of desire is always there, banked and controlled; a small provocation is all it takes to ignite a conflagration.

The Gemara then returns to show scriptural parallels to other *ben Sira* passages. The verse in Yirmiyah — *kichlov malei of, ken bateihem m'lei'im mirmah* — a cage full of birds, so their houses are full of deceit — parallels the *ben Sira* warning to keep the masses out of one's home. Then: *rabim yihiyu dorshei shlomecha* — let many seek your welfare; that is, have many friends. But *galei sodecha l'echad me'alef* — share your secret with only one in a thousand. And *mishochevet cheikecha shmor pitchi picha* — even from the one who sleeps in your bosom, your wife, guard the words of your mouth.

Finally: *al tatzar tzarot machar* — do not borrow tomorrow's anguish today, *ki lo teda ma yeled yom* — for you do not know what the day will bring. And perhaps even more starkly — *shema l'machar einenu* — perhaps by tomorrow you will no longer be alive, in which case you will have spent today worrying about a world that will not even be yours. The point is not fatalism, but the futility of unproductive anxiety. As one student observed, the line *v'nimtza mitzta'er al olam she'eino shelo* — you will have anguished over a world that is not yours — can be read not only to mean a day you will not live to see, but a reality that will never materialize. Rashi appears to support something of that reading, though the simpler meaning is the former.

## Poor Person Verse

### Rav's Comparison

The last citation from *ben Sira* bridges into the next topic. The verse *kol yemei ani ra'im* — all the days of a poor person are evil — appears in Mishlei. *Ben Sira* adds: *af leilot* — even the nights are bad, because a poor person cannot rest easily. His roof is lower than all his neighbors' roofs, so their rainwater runs down onto his. His vineyard is on the mountaintop where the soil is thin, so what little topsoil there is rolls down to everyone else's fields. Even sleep does not redeem him.

The Gemara then takes up the verse from Mishlei directly. Rabbi Zeira said in the name of Rav: what does it mean that all the days of a poor person are evil? *Elu ba'alei Talmud* — these are the people who learn Gemara, because the work of Talmud learning is genuinely hard. One strains the mind, the answers are not always clear, the process is exhausting. By contrast, *vetov lev mishteh tamid* — and a good heart is always a feast — *elu ba'alei Mishnah*, these are the students of Mishnah, because Mishnah is clear, apodictic, accessible. One has a good time learning it. Of course, the framing raises the obvious question: is being always at a feast the right goal? Is ease the correct measure of value? The contrast is not an endorsement of the easier path — it simply describes the experiential reality of the two modes of study.

### Rava Disputes

Rava says the opposite — *ipcha*. One must think not only about the difficulty of the process but about what it yields. Rav Mesharshiya said in the name of Rava: what does the verse mean — *masi'a avanim ye'atzev bahem* — one who carries stones is anguished by them? *Elu ba'alei Mishnah* — these are the Mishnah scholars. They labor under an enormous burden of material, but it is like hauling boulders: in the end, what do they have? A mass of memorized facts without deep understanding. By contrast, *ubokei'a etzim yisachen bahem* — one who chops wood provides warmth thereby. *Elu ba'alei Talmud* — the Gemara scholars, who break the material down into its component parts, who analyze, who get to the depth. The chopping is vigorous, productive work; the warmth it generates is real and lasting.

A student noted further that this image carries an additional dimension: a stone is inert and fixed, while wood is organic and alive. Mishnah is a fixed, closed text; Gemara is continuously growing and expanding. That resonance enriches the metaphor considerably.

### Multiple Readings

The Gemara then presents a series of additional readings of the verse, each interpreting "the poor person" differently.

Rabbi Chanina says: *ze mi sheyesh lo ishah ra'ah* — the one whose days are all evil is the man with a bad wife. Conversely, *vetov lev mishteh tamid* — the one whose heart is always at a feast — is the man with a good wife.

Rabbi Yannai says: *ze istanis* — the poor person is the fastidious individual, one who is forever troubled by germs, by the environment, by conditions that others do not even notice. Conversely, the one at the feast is *ze she-da'ato yafah* — the one who is, so to speak, easy with everything, for whom the world presents no particular irritants. The phrase *da'ato yafah* here does not mean refined — there is a Mishnah that uses *nefesh hayafah tochalena* to describe someone who has no problem eating something others might find repulsive, meaning someone who is simply unbothered. That is the sense here.

Rabbi Yochanan says: *ze rachmani* — the one whose days are all evil is the deeply empathetic person, one who feels everything around them, absorbs others' pain, has no emotional boundaries. *Vetov lev mishteh tamid ze achzari* — and the one at the feast is the hard-hearted person. This is not a normative endorsement; it is a description of a reality. The person without conscience, who is never troubled by others' suffering, moves through the world lightly. That may be the easiest way to live — it is not the right way.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: *ze she-da'ato ktzarah* — the poor person is one who is always importing trouble: borrowing others' anxieties, worrying about every possible future misfortune that might befall him. Rashi offers the additional reading that *da'ato ktzarah* means *ragzan* — someone who is short-tempered, who is easily angered and upset. Either way, *vetov lev mishteh tamid ze she-da'ato rechavah* — the one at the feast is the expansive-minded person, who neither carries borrowed anxieties nor reacts disproportionately to provocation.

### Shabbat Objection

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi then raises an apparent difficulty with the verse itself: *kol yemei ani ra'im* — how can one say *all* the days are evil? What about Shabbat and Yom Tov? Even a poor person ensures proper food for Shabbat — that is rather the entire point of Shabbat. The answer given is *ki deShmuel* — like the teaching of Shmuel: a change in one's normal diet can itself become a source of discomfort. If someone eats much better food on Shabbat than during the rest of the week, the change in routine may produce its own adverse effects by the following day. It was noted that Shmuel was a physician, which lends a particular weight to this medical observation.

## Scripture Misuse

### Shir HaShirim Song

The Gemara now transitions from books that lack canonical status — and the risk of treating them as scripture — to the opposite problem: taking canonical scripture and treating it in ways that are unworthy of it. The consequence here is not *ein lo chelek l'olam haba*, but the Gemara's language makes plain that the behavior is seriously objectionable.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: one who reads a verse of *Shir HaShirim* and makes it into a kind of song, or one who recites a verse in a drinking house *b'lo zmano* — not in its proper time, not in the right context — brings evil into the world. The phrase *b'lo zmano* resists precise definition, and the Gemara seems to leave it that way deliberately; some things cannot be fully quantified, but one knows the difference between singing *Shir HaShirim* in its proper context and turning it into a bar song. The issue is not melody per se — after all, much of *Tanakh* was composed to be sung — but the context in which it is done and the spirit in which it is treated.

### Torah's Complaint

The Gemara offers a powerful image for what is at stake. *Mipnei she-haTorah chogeret sak* — because the Torah puts on sackcloth. The personification is striking: by turning the Torah into something trivial, one strips it of its dignity, and the Torah itself dresses in mourning. It stands before God and cries: *Ribono shel Olam*, your children have made me like a flute played by wastrels — like a harmonica, like background noise in a tavern. That is what I have become.

And then God defends the people. *Besha'ah she-ochlim v'shotim bameh ya'asoku* — when they are eating and drinking, what would you have them do? At least they are engaging with Torah. The parallel is irresistible: it recalls the Chassidic story of the porter who changes a tire while davening Mincha — the Misnaged is scandalized, but the Chassid says to God: even with grease on their hands, Your children are thinking of You. God here is defending the Jewish people to the Torah itself: they could be singing something far worse; at least this is Torah.

But the Torah responds: *Ribono shel Olam*, if they are students of *mikra*, let them discuss Torah, Nevi'im, and Ketuvim; if they are students of Mishnah, let them discuss Mishnah, *halakhot*, and *aggadot*; if they are students of Talmud, let them discuss the laws of Pesach on Pesach, the laws of Shavuot on Shavuot, the laws of Sukkot on Sukkot. The key distinction is between discussing and singing. Talking about *psukim* at the table is not the same as converting them into entertainment. A *d'var Torah* at a meal is not the same as the *beit midrash* — no one requires that — but there is a difference between lighter Torah conversation and turning the Torah into a pop song. Notably, the Gemara links *ba'alei Talmud* with *halakhot* rather than with free-form analysis — though the assumption is presumably that one is *schmoozing* in learning, not issuing formal rulings, since one should not teach *halacha* in a state of inebriation.

### Context Matters

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Chananya offers the positive formulation: *kol hakoreh pasuk bizmano* — one who reads a verse in its proper time and context — *tovah le'olam*, it is good for the world. *Shene'emar vidavar be'ito mah tov* — how good is it to speak in its proper time. The problem is never intrinsic to the singing or the meal setting; the problem is the context and the spirit. The same act in the right setting is good; in the wrong setting it dishonors the Torah.

## Incantations Forbidden

### Lochesh Definition

The Mishnah lists among those who forfeit their share in the world to come the one who whispers an incantation — *halochesh al hamakah* — over a wound, using a verse from Torah. The example given is *kol hamachala asher samti beMitzrayim lo asim alecha* — all the illness that I placed upon Egypt I will not place upon you. Already it is somewhat striking that this should appear on the same list as one who denies the divine origin of the Torah or the resurrection of the dead. One wonders whether the Mishnah means something different by placing items on this list — perhaps indicating behavior that is condemned rather than technically prohibited, or that forfeits the *chelek olam haba* not through heresy but through disrespect of a different kind. The weight of being on this list alongside fundamental denials of faith is not easy to explain.

### Spitting Requirement

Rabbi Yochanan qualifies the ruling: the problem is specifically *uve-rokek bah* — if one spits while reciting the verse. Spitting and then uttering God's name — or a verse that invokes God's healing — is a form of disrespect to the divine name. That is what triggers the severe consequence. Even granting that reading, the question of whether *ein lo chelek l'olam haba* is truly the appropriate weight for this is an uncomfortable one.

### Without Name Still Bad

Rava extends the ruling further: even if one recites a verse that does not contain God's name — such as *nega tzara'at ki tihyeh ba'adam vehuva el hakohen* — it is still problematic. One might have thought that the absence of God's name makes it categorically different and less objectionable. Rava rules that it does not.

Rabbi Chanina goes further still: even *Vayikra el Moshe* — a verse that does contain God's name but has no direct healing content, no incantatory relevance to the wound — is nonetheless forbidden to recite over it in this manner. The act of deploying scripture as a magical incantation over a wound, regardless of which verse is chosen, remains in the category of that which the Gemara condemns here.

## Healing Practices

### Permitted Acts

The Gemara now turns to a series of permitted and forbidden practices, many relating to Shabbat, which provides the occasion for discussing the *lechishah* issue in its broader context.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: one may apply oil and rub it into the abdomen on Shabbat. The reason is that applying oil to the skin was a normal, everyday practice; doing so even for therapeutic purposes does not present the appearance of *refuah* in a way that would be prohibited. It does not look like medical treatment because it looks like ordinary grooming.

A subsequent *baraita* refines this: one may do so, provided one does not do it in precisely the same manner as one would during the week — a *shinui* is required. Rav Chama bar Chanina says the modification is to apply the oil first and rub afterward — rather than beginning to rub and then adding oil, which would more obviously suggest medicinal intent. Rabbi Yochanan says one may apply and rub simultaneously — but again, not begin with the rubbing before the oil is present.

### Snake Incantations OK

*Velochashim lechishat nechashim ve'akrabim beShabbat* — one may recite incantations to prevent snakes and scorpions from biting on Shabbat. This is permitted because it is not a physical act of trapping; one is simply reciting words. And since the concern is mortal danger, it is permitted even on Shabbat. One may also place a vessel over an inflamed eye on Shabbat to cool it. Eye disease was apparently extremely prevalent in the ancient world — infected hands touching the eyes caused serious problems — and applying a cold metal object to reduce inflammation was a recognized treatment. This is permitted on Shabbat, interestingly, and is not classified as *refuah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies: this applies only if the vessel is not *muktzeh*; if it is *muktzeh*, it is forbidden to move it for this purpose.

### Demon Inquiry Ban

*Ve'ein sho'alin bidvar shedim beShabbat* — one may not inquire of the *sheidim* on Shabbat. Rashi explains the nature of such inquiries: one might ask the *sheidim*, for instance, where one has lost something. The *sheidim*, apparently, may respond. The reason this is forbidden on Shabbat is that it is *dover davar* — weekday activity, the conduct of mundane commerce, which is inappropriate on Shabbat.

Rabbi Yossi goes further: *af bechol assur* — it is forbidden even on weekdays. The question is the basis: is there a formal prohibition, or is it purely *sakana* — danger? Rav Yosef says — and there is a variant in the text as to whether the ruling is like Rabbi Yossi or not — that even Rabbi Yossi's concern is not a formal *issur* but a concern for safety. Once one engages with *sheidim*, one may expose oneself to forces one cannot control and cannot safely navigate.

This raises the question of where this falls relative to *lo tenachashu* and the other prohibitions on magic and divination. The line between what is technically *assur* and what is merely dangerous — *darchei ha'Emori*, signs, inquiries — is genuinely complicated, and the Gemara's treatment here reflects that complexity without resolving it with precision.

### Dangerous Oil Story

A story illustrates the danger of incantations over oil. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta arrived at a certain inn. They brought him oil in a vessel — already a bad sign, as will become clear. He rubbed it on himself, and blisters or boils broke out on his face. He went out to the marketplace, and a certain woman saw him and said: I see here the spirit of *Cheimat* — apparently a known *sheid*. She performed some neutralizing ritual, and he was healed.

The lesson: incantations are performed over oil when it is in a vessel, not over oil held in the hand. Therefore, oil poured directly from someone's hand is safe, whereas oil poured from a vessel — if there is reason to suspect the one who handled it — may carry injurious incantations within it. One should not anoint oneself from such a vessel if the circumstances are suspicious.

The story of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef — who was swallowed by a cedar tree while being pursued by a *sheid*, and was miraculously saved when the tree burst open — is cited earlier in the same sugya as further illustration of why one should not casually involve oneself with *sheidim*.

## Healing Meaning

### Egypt Verse Paradox

Rav Abba raised a question to Rabba bar Mari about the verse that serves as the Mishnah's example of the forbidden incantation: *kol hamachala asher samti beMitzrayim lo asim alecha ki ani Hashem rofecha* — all the illness that I placed upon Egypt I will not place upon you, for I am the Lord your healer. The paradox is straightforward: if God is promising not to make one sick — *lo asim alecha* — then why does one need God as a healer? If the illness is never placed upon the person, what is there to heal?

### Two Conditions

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *mikra zeh me-atzmo nidrash* — the verse explains itself if one reads it carefully. The full verse reads: *vayomer im shamoa tishma le-kol Hashem Elokecha... kol hamachala asher samti beMitzrayim lo asim alecha ki ani Hashem rofecha*. There are two distinct parts. If you hearken carefully to the voice of God — *im shamoa tishma* — then *lo asim*: you will not be made sick at all. But even if you do not fully listen — *v'im lo tishma* — and the illness therefore does come upon you, even then: *ki ani Hashem rofecha* — know that I am still your healer. You may yet turn to Me and I will heal you. The promise of *refuah* is not rendered redundant by the promise of protection; rather, the verse is addressing two different situations depending on whether the condition of obedience is met.

## Rabbi Eliezer Death

### Fever & Tears

Rabba bar bar Chana said: when Rabbi Eliezer fell ill, his students entered to visit him. He said to them: *chama azah yesh ba'olam* — there is a powerful sun in the world. Rashi understands this as a reference to his own burning fever, expressing the intensity of his suffering. His students began to weep — *hitchilu hen bochin* — and Rabbi Akiva was laughing.

### Akiva Laughs

The other students asked Rabbi Akiva: why are you laughing? And he turned it back on them: why are you crying? They answered: how can we not weep? Here is a *sefer Torah* — Rabbi Eliezer had long been understood in those terms, as a repository who preserved and transmitted everything he had received without creative addition, not a *ma'ayan ha-mitgaber* but a *sefer Torah* — and this *sefer Torah* is in anguish. How could we not cry?

Rabbi Akiva said: that is precisely why I am laughing. As long as I watched my rabbi and saw that his wine did not spoil, his flax was undamaged, his oil did not go rancid, his honey did not congeal — I was worried. I feared that perhaps, *chas ve-shalom*, he had already received his full reward in this world, and there would be nothing left for him in the world to come. Now that I see him suffering, I am happy — he will receive his reward in the world to come.

This moment operates on multiple levels, not all of them comfortable. Rabbi Eliezer had been in *cherem*, cut off from his colleagues; one might ask whether material well-being fully measures whether someone has been *sharui be-tza'ar*. And there is an additional layer of irony: it is Rabbi Akiva who makes this argument, given the terrible end Rabbi Akiva himself would face. But the pastoral logic is distinct from the biographical one. Rather than weeping alongside the suffering man — which, however genuine, may only compound his misery — Rabbi Akiva offers a reframe: your suffering is not a punishment, it is a sign that your reward is being preserved for the world to come. Whether or not that always works as pastoral care, it is at least offering something that might help.

Rabbi Eliezer's response is telling: *amar lahem samchuni* — prop me up, support me, let me sit up. *D'eshma divrei Akiva talmidi* — I want to hear what Akiva my student is saying. He says something that is not what the others are saying — the others' words, however sincere and moving, are essentially the kind of tribute one finds in a eulogy. What is Akiva saying that is different?

Rabbi Akiva replied to his teacher's question — *minayin lach?* where do you derive that *yisurin* are beloved? — *mikra ani doreish*: from a verse, from Scripture.

### Four Elders Visit

A *baraita* records a second account: when Rabbi Eliezer fell ill, four elders came to visit him — Rabbi Tarfon, Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and Rabbi Akiva. Their mode of address is notable; they are called *zkenim*, elders, though Rabbi Yehoshua was a colleague and Rabbi Akiva was still a student.

Rabbi Tarfon spoke first: you are better to Israel than a drop of rain. Rain gives life — but only in this world. My teacher gives life to Israel in this world and in the world to come.

Rabbi Yehoshua spoke: you are better to Israel than the sun. The sun illuminates only in this world. My teacher illuminates both in this world and in the world to come.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya spoke: you are better to Israel than a father and mother. Parents — and it is notable that the text says *av v'em*, naming both — care for their children only in this world. My teacher cares for Israel in this world and in the world to come.

### Beloved Sufferings

Then Rabbi Akiva spoke: *chavivin yisurin* — sufferings are beloved. The effect on Rabbi Eliezer is immediate: *amar lahem samchuni* — prop me up, hold me up, I want to hear what my student Akiva is saying. *She-amar chavivin yisurin* — he has said that sufferings are beloved. The other tributes, however sincere, have the quality of things one says. Akiva has said something genuinely surprising, something with intellectual substance that catches attention.

Rabbi Eliezer asks: *minayin lach?* And Rabbi Akiva says: *mikra ani doreish* — I will derive it from a verse.

### Menashe Example

The verse is from Chronicles: *ben shtem esreh shanah Menashe b'molcho v'chamish v'chamishim shanah malach b'Yerushalayim vaya'as hara b'einei Hashem* — Menashe was twelve years old when he became king, he reigned fifty-five years in Jerusalem, and he did what was evil in the eyes of God. And elsewhere it is written that Chizkiyahu's men transcribed the proverbs of Shlomo.

The argument: Chizkiyahu taught *Mishlei* — with all its moral guidance on how to live a good life — to the entire world. Did he teach it to his own son Menashe? Obviously yes. And did all that moral education do Menashe any good? Clearly not. *Mikol torach shetarach bo, mikol amal she'amal bo* — no matter how much Chizkiyahu invested in giving Menashe a proper upbringing, *lo he'elu l'mutav* — it did not put him on a straight path. *Ela yisurin* — it was only *yisurin*, only suffering, that set Menashe straight.

As it says: God spoke to Menashe and his people, but they did not listen. And God brought against them the commanders of the Assyrian king's army; they captured Menashe with hooks and bound him in copper chains and led him away. He then humbled himself greatly before the God of his fathers, prayed to Him, and God listened to his supplication and restored him to Jerusalem and to his kingdom. And Menashe knew that the Lord is God.

*Halamadnu shekhavivin yisurin* — from this we learn how beloved sufferings are. How powerful their capacity to transform a person that all the moral instruction in the world could not achieve.

One must sit with the strangeness of this as a message to Rabbi Eliezer. Menashe's *yisurin* were the consequence of extraordinary wickedness — the suffering that woke him up to repentance. Rabbi Eliezer is not Menashe. How is this meant to be uplifting? One is left without a fully satisfying resolution. The parallel the Gemara draws elsewhere is instructive: *Vayasar hamelech et taba'ato* — Achashverosh handing his signet ring to Haman — accomplished more than all the words of all the prophets in spurring Israel to *teshuvah*. The *fear* of impending harm, the lived experience of suffering and consequence, reaches places that *mussar* cannot. That is the principle Rabbi Akiva is articulating. Whether it fully lands as consolation in this context is another question.

## Three Protesters

### Cain's Complaint

The Gemara records that three individuals came before God not merely in prayer but with something resembling a righteous complaint — *ba'alila*, with a claim. They were Kayin, Esav, and Menashe.

Kayin: *gadol avoni minaso* — is my sin too great to bear, or is my punishment too great to bear? He said before God: *Ribono shel Olam*, is my sin greater than the sin of the sixty myriads who will sin with the golden calf? You will forgive them — *ve'ata soleiach lahem* — and you cannot forgive me? This is not supplication; it is a challenge. A demand that justice be applied consistently.

### Esau's Challenge

Esav: *habracha achat hi lecha avi* — do you have only one blessing, Father? The complaint is directed to Yitzchak rather than to God, but it carries the same quality of pressing a claim. Is there only one blessing available? Give me the one that belongs to me.

### Menashe's Ultimatum

Menashe: *batkhila kara l'elohoot harbei u'levasof kara l'elohei avotav* — at first he called upon many other gods, and only at the end called upon the God of his fathers. Rashi explains the implicit challenge: having tried every other deity and found them unresponsive, Menashe's prayer to God carries an implicit ultimatum — if You are real, answer me as You answer others; prove yourself distinct from the gods who did not answer. The structure of the prayer, coming after all alternatives have been exhausted, constitutes its own form of pressure on the divine.

## God's Name Rules

### Name Pronunciation

The final ruling on this daf: Abba Shaul says — *af hahogeh et Hashem be'otiyotav* — one who pronounces the divine name as it is spelled is among those who forfeit their *chelek l'olam haba*. A *tanna* specifies: this applies in the *gevulin* — outside the Temple — and *b'lashon agah*, which is not entirely clear but appears to mean in the vernacular or in a casual register. The implication may be that pronouncing it in Hebrew — the sacred tongue — in a deliberate, reverent manner outside the Temple is at least somewhat less objectionable than doing so casually in the vernacular, though the precise line here is difficult to draw with confidence.
