# Kinnim 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamid Overview

### Tamid Scope

With the conclusion of *Kinnim*, we turn to *Masechet Tamid*, which begins on 28b. A brief orientation to the nature of this *masechet* is in order. In principle, *Masechet Tamid* is a discussion of how the *korban tamid* was brought every morning. But before reaching the actual offering of the *tamid*, the *masechet* describes what happened in the *Beit HaMikdash* during the early morning hours — essentially from the time of the night watches through the opening of the gates, the *terumat hadeshen*, and all the early preparations, up through the *korban tamid* itself. For most of us, this is a remarkable opportunity: after nearly seven and a half years through *Shas*, we finally get a direct, detailed account of what actually happened.

### Rambam: Why Last

The Rambam's *Peirush HaMishnah*, in his introduction written at the beginning of *Zeraim*, discusses the ordering of *masechtos* within each *seder*. At the end of that discussion, he explains why *Masechet Tamid* appears where it does. He writes: *ve'achar meilah masechet Tamid* — after *Meilah* comes *Tamid*. Why is *Tamid* placed last? *Lefi she'ein bah dinim velo issur veheter* — because it contains no *halacha* discussion, no analysis of what is forbidden or permitted. *Ella sippur* — it is purely narrative. *Heich hayah tamid karev* — how was the *tamid* brought? And why do we need to know this? *Kedei sheyehei nahug tamid* — so that when the next *Beit HaMikdash* is built, we will know how to bring the *korban tamid* properly.

The Rambam continues: *ve'achar Tamid Middos* — after *Tamid* comes *Middot*. And *ve'ein inyanah ella sippur middas HaMikdash vetzuratah* — its subject matter too is entirely descriptive, describing the dimensions and form of the *Beit HaMikdash* and how it was constructed. The reason for preserving this knowledge? *Shekesheyibaneh nishmor bo osah tzurah veoso hayachas* — so that when the Temple is rebuilt, we will maintain the same form and the same proportions. As the verse says, *ki hakol Hashem alai hiskil*.

One might ask whether the dimensions in *Masechet Middot* are rendered superfluous by the descriptions of the Temple found in *Tanakh* itself. The answer is that the *pesukim* in *Yechezkel* describe a different structure — the *Beit HaMikdash Rishon* and the prophetic vision differ from the *Beit HaMikdash Sheni*. There is even a broader *machlokes* about whether the Third Temple must replicate the Second or will come down from heaven in an entirely different form. These are real questions, and the Rambam's remark that we preserve these proportions *shekesheyibaneh* is not entirely free of ambiguity.

### Descriptive Nature

What emerges clearly, then, is that both *Tamid* and *Middot* are purely descriptive *masechtos*. Some *halachos* are alluded to or implicit within the descriptions, but there is no *halacha* as such — no debates about permissibility, no *machlokes* between *Tannaim* about legal conclusions. One striking consequence of this is the near-total absence of named *Tannaim* in both *masechtos*. It is almost entirely *stam* — anonymous — because there are no disputes to attribute to anyone. One might have expected that even descriptive accounts would generate differing recollections, but the text is remarkably uniform and unattributed throughout.

## Guard Stations

### Three Stations

The opening *mishnah* of *Masechet Tamid* states: *bishlosha mekomot hakohanim shomrim beveit hamikdash* — the *kohanim* would stand guard in three places in the *Beit HaMikdash*: *Beit Avtinas*, *Beit HaNitzotz*, and *Beit HaMoked*. Looking at a diagram of the *Beit HaMikdash*, these three locations can be identified. *Beit HaNitzotz* and *Beit HaMoked* appear in the north of the *azarah*, while *Beit Avtinas* — more precisely, *lishkat Beit Avtinas* — appears on the south. There is some question about the precise order and arrangement; some say *Beit HaMoked* was positioned further east. In any event, these were the three priestly guard stations.

### Priestly vs. Levite

The first *mishnah* of *Masechet Middot* opens with an identical formulation — *bishlosha mekomot hakohanim shomrim beveit hamikdash*, in *Beit Avtinas*, *Beit HaNitzotz*, and *Beit HaMoked* — but then adds: *vehaleviyim be'esrim ve'echad*, the *Leviyim* stood guard in twenty-one places. *Chamisha al chamisha shaarei har habayit* — five by the five gates of *Har HaBayit*, the outer wall where the *Kotel* stands today. *Arba'a al arba pinotav mitokho* — four at the corners of that wall, on the inside. *Chamisha al chamisha shaarei ha'azarah* — five by the five gates of the *azarah*, and four at its corners, and so on. So the *Leviyim* were stationed at the gates and outer perimeter of *Har HaBayit* and around the *azarah* itself — a total of twenty-one positions.

The roles of *kohanim* and *Leviyim* were spatially distinct. The *Leviyim* were at the gates and on the outside; the *kohanim* were on the inside, and specifically, as we will see shortly, on the rooftops of these structures. The *Leviyim* guarded below at the gates, while the *kohanim* watched from above.

### Sources: Guarding

The *Gemara* establishes that the *mitzva* to guard the *Mikdash* applies to both *kohanim* and *Leviyim*. The *Torah* says to *Aharon*: I have given you the *Leviyim* to be *shomer mishmeret ohel moed* — to guard the protection of the *Ohel Moed*. And the *Torah* speaks of *Moshe Aharon uvanav shomrim mishmeret hakodesh* — they would guard the *Ohel Moed*. Since *Moshe* was a *Levi* and not a *kohen*, we learn that both *kohanim* and *Leviyim* — in different but complementary roles — share in this obligation of *shemirat haMikdash*.

## Guard Purpose

### Mefaresh: Decree

What is the purpose of this *shemira*? The *mefaresh* — the commentator printed alongside the *mishnah*, whom nobody identifies as Rashi, but rather refers to consistently as the *mefaresh* — offers a surprising view. He says the guarding is a *gezeirat hakatuv*, a divine decree, because logically one might have reached the opposite conclusion: God can protect His own Temple, and stationing guards might actually undercut the *kavod* of the *Mikdash*, suggesting that something might be stolen. He writes: *de'ein aniyut bemakom ashirut* — we do not want to look poor in a place of wealth. If something were stolen from the *Mikdash*, a truly wealthy establishment might simply replace it: *ve'i ganvei klei mikdash kanya achrinei*. And perhaps *bechad shomer sagi* — one guard might suffice. The entire elaborate guarding system is therefore justified only by the explicit *pasuk*, since pure reason might have argued against it.

### Rambam: Honor

The *Rambam* takes the diametrically opposite position. In *Perek 8* of *Hilchot Beit HaBechirah*, he writes: *shemirat hamikdash mitzvat aseh* — guarding the Temple is a positive *mitzva*. And the reason? *Ve'af al pi she'ein sham pachad me'oyev velo milistim* — even though there is no fear of enemies or robbers — *she'ein shemirato ella kavod lo* — the guarding is entirely for the sake of honor to God. He explains: *eino domeh paltorin sheyesh alav shomrim lepaltorin she'ein alav shomrim* — a palace with guards is simply not the same as a palace without guards. The entire ceremony of changing guards, of armed sentinels marching about — this is precisely how one expresses majesty and respect. Nobody looks at the guards of a king's palace and says it signals weakness or poverty; they say it signals grandeur. The guarding of the *Mikdash* achieves this same expression of *kavod*.

The *Rambam* continues: *shemirah zu kol halaylah* — this guarding is performed all night. *Vehashomrim hem hakohanim vehaleviyim*, citing *vehachonim lifnei hamishkan lifnei ohel moed kedma* and *ushmartem et mishmeret hamishkan* — *Kehat*, *Moshe*, *Aharon*, and his sons. In *Halacha 3*, the *Rambam* adds that this is not only a positive *mitzva*: if it is not performed, there is also a violation of a negative prohibition. *Shene'emar ushmartem et mishmeret hakodesh — leshon shemirah azharah hi*. This is somewhat surprising, because ordinarily *leshon shemirah* denotes a negative prohibition in the sense of *hishamer lecha* — guard yourself against doing something — whereas here the *shemirah* is the literal act of guarding, not a prohibition against transgression. Nevertheless, the *Rambam* derives both a positive and a negative from the same formulation.

### Roles & Purposes

In *Halacha 4*, the *Rambam* states: *hakohanim shomrim mibifnim vehaleviyim mibachutz* — the *kohanim* guard from the inside, the *Leviyim* from the outside. This confirms what we see from the sources: the *Leviyim* are stationed at the gates and the outer perimeters; the *kohanim* are positioned more inward.

One might wonder: if the *Rambam*'s rationale for *shemirat haMikdash* is *kavod*, should it not apply during the day as well, when people can actually see the guards and appreciate the majesty they represent? The *Rambam* nevertheless limits the obligation to nighttime — *kol halaylah* — while the *mefaresh* and others maintain that the *mitzva* applies both day and night. This remains a genuine *machlokes*. Both the *mefaresh* and the *Rambam* agree, however, on one fundamental point: the *shemira* is not for any pragmatic protective purpose. The *Rambam*'s formulation — guards as an expression of honor, like the ceremonial guards of a royal palace — is the interpretation most widely adopted.

## Beit HaNitzotz

### HaNitzotz Role

The name *Beit HaNitzotz* means "the house of the spark." Some hold that there was a fire maintained there, from which one could kindle the fire of the *mizbe'ach* — since although the *mishnah* teaches that a divine fire descended upon the *mizbe'ach*, there is a *mitzva* nonetheless to add human fire, *mitzvah lehavi min hahedyot*. However, most authorities associate the perpetual fire more directly with *Beit HaMoked* — *moked* itself meaning "place of the bonfire." A separate view, cited in the name of *Tosafot*, is that *Beit HaNitzotz* takes its name from the first rays of sunlight that would strike it. The *Tiferet Yisrael*, however, raises an objection: *Beit HaNitzotz* was located in the north, and the sun comes from the other direction entirely, which makes this etymology difficult to sustain. He himself explains the name as referring to the constant sparks of fire there — *tamid hayu bo nitzotzot shel esh, mimenu yakhol lehadlik et esh hamizbe'ach* — from which the fire of the *mizbe'ach* could be kindled. The debate about the precise etymology and function of *Beit HaNitzotz* thus turns on both linguistic and geographical considerations.

### Archers on Roof

The *mishnah* states: *u'vBeit HaNitzotz hayu aliyot harovim shomrim sham* — in *Beit HaNitzotz* there were elevated areas, and the *rovim* would watch there. The word *rovim* almost certainly refers to archers. Anyone familiar with ancient fortifications knows the arrangement: archers would be positioned along the tops of walls, sheltered behind narrow slits, able to shoot downward while remaining protected. This fits precisely with the role described. Whether the *kohanim* stationed there actually carried bows and arrows is an interesting question — but they were at minimum functioning in an archer's capacity, watching from elevated positions on the rooftop.

Some commentators suggest, based on Aramaic, that *rovim* derives from the word *ravya*, meaning children or young men, i.e., the junior *kohanim*. This reading is far less convincing. The word *rovim* fits the context of guarding from an elevated position far more naturally, and Mishnaic Hebrew usage supports the meaning of archers. The *mefarshim* indicate that the same arrangement applied to *Beit Avtinas* as well — while the *mishnah* does not spell it out explicitly, the pattern is consistent: *Leviyim* guarded at the gates below, while *kohanim* watched from the roof above.

## Beit HaMoked

### Structure

The *mishnah* describes *Beit HaMoked*: *kipa* — it had a dome on top. This is worth noting architecturally: domed construction was characteristic of the Herodian period, and this is indeed Herod's *Beit HaMikdash*. A dome would also have had the practical benefit of allowing smoke to escape, which is relevant given the large fire maintained there. Looking at the diagram, one can see the dome indicated by a small circle at the center of *Beit HaMoked*. The structure was also *bayit gadol* — a large room — and as the diagram suggests, there were four smaller chambers opening off the main central space. *Mukaf rovadim shel avanim* — it was surrounded by stone steps, though the precise arrangement of these steps is not entirely clear: some understand them as interior steps rising toward a higher level within the room; others connect them to the smaller side-chambers.

### Sleeping Quarters

*Ziknei Beit Av* — the elders, or senior functionaries, of the *beit av* — would sleep there. The *beit av* was the subdivision of the *mishmar* serving on a given day, and the *ziknei beit av* were its senior members. The *mefaresh* explains that they would sleep in small niches in the walls of *Beit HaMoked*, reached by climbing the surrounding steps.

The *mishnah* then adds that *pirchei kehunah* — the young *kohanim* — had their garments on the ground beside them, indicating that they too slept there. It appears that both senior and junior *kohanim* slept in *Beit HaMoked*: presumably the *ziknei beit av* in the upper niches, and the *pirchei kehunah* below. This raises an obvious question: if everyone is sleeping, who is actually doing the guarding? Some suggest the *pirchei kehunah* were the *shomrim* and their sleep refers to rotational shifts. Others propose that the actual guarding was conducted from the top — perhaps from a walkway around the dome — since one cannot meaningfully guard from inside an enclosed room.

### Keys & Authority

The reason the *ziknei beit av* slept in *Beit HaMoked* is now made explicit: *u'maftechot ha'azarah beyadam* — they held the keys to the *azarah*. They needed to be present in order to open the gates at the start of the service each morning, and it was therefore practical and respectful that they sleep close by rather than travel from elsewhere. When the *mishnah* speaks of the keys to the *azarah*, this refers primarily to *Sha'ar Nicanor* and the entrance to *Ezrat Yisrael*.

There is also the structural question of how *kohanim* could sleep in *Beit HaMoked* at all, given that one is forbidden even to sit within the *azarah*. Looking at the diagram again, one can see a faint dotted line running horizontally through the building, indicating a division: the outer half of *Beit HaMoked* did not possess *kedushat azarah*, while the inner half did. The *kohanim* slept in the outer, non-sanctified half, thereby avoiding any violation.

### Garment Protocol

The *mishnah* specifies: *lo hayu yeshenim be'vigdei kodesh* — they did not sleep in the *bigdei kehunah*. Rather, *ella hayu poshtim* — they would remove their priestly garments — *u'manichim otan tachat rasheihem* — and place them beside their heads. This does not mean literally under the head, which would constitute deriving benefit from *bigdei kehunah*, but rather alongside. *U'mitkasim bi'kesut atzman* — they would cover themselves with their own ordinary garments and sleep in those. The *bigdei kehunah* were thus set aside properly for the morning service.

## Nocturnal Emission

### Exit Protocol

The *mishnah* now turns to a challenging situation: *ira keri leachad mehem* — if one of the *kohanim* experienced a nocturnal seminal emission during the night. He has now become *tamei* as a *ba'al keri* and is forbidden from remaining in, or passing through, the *azarah*. Fortunately, he is sleeping in the outer half of *Beit HaMoked*, which lacks *kedushat azarah*, so his immediate location is not the problem. The question is how he exits without crossing through the sanctified space.

The *mishnah* answers: *yotzei veholech lo bimsiba haholechet lo tachat habira* — he departs via a passage or tunnel that runs beneath the *bira*. The precise meaning of *bira* is debated — some understand it to refer to the entire *Beit HaMikdash*, others to a particular section or chamber — but in any event, the reference is to some form of underground passage that allowed egress without crossing the sanctified areas. Looking at the diagram of *Beit HaMoked*, one of the four corner chambers is labeled *yeridah lebeit hatvilah* — descent to the immersion chamber — which confirms this route.

### Tunnel System

The passage was not dark: *nerot dolkim mikan umikan* — candles were lit along both sides of the tunnel, illuminating the way. Whether these were maintained by someone who went through daily to relight them, or whether the *kohen* himself lit them as he proceeded, is not stated explicitly. The tunnel led *ad shehu magia lebeit hatvilah* — until he reached the *beit hatvilah*, the immersion house containing the *mikvah*.

### Mikvah & Toilet

*U'medura hayata sham* — there was a fire maintained by the *mikvah* to provide warmth, since a *kohen* might need to immerse in the early morning hours. One would not want practical discomfort to deter anyone from purifying himself promptly, so a fire was kept there for warmth.

*U'beit hakise shel kavod* — and there was an honorable bathroom facility. The rationale for its placement is not merely convenience: if a *kohen* still has residual semen within him and does not urinate before immersing, he risks becoming *tamei* again immediately after his *tevilah*. The bathroom therefore serves a ritual function, ensuring that he is fully purified before immersing. *Ze haya kevodo* — this was the honorable manner in which it operated.

The system of use was also dignified: *matzu naul yodea sheyesh sham adam* — if the door was found locked, one knew someone was inside; *patuach beyadua she'ein sham adam* — if it was open, it was unoccupied. One might think this is simply common sense, but the *mishnah*'s attention to this detail is itself significant. In an era and environment where privacy in personal hygiene was not universal — people often simply stepped outdoors — the *Beit HaMikdash* established a dignified convention, and the *kohanim* were careful to observe it. The locking system protected the privacy and honor of each individual, befitting the sanctity of the surroundings.
