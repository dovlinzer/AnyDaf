# Bekhorot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Precision Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: נִמְצָא מְכוָּּון בֵּין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּיהֶן מְבִיאוֹת שְׁתֵּי עֲגָלוֹת. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this **is subject** to a dispute between ***tanna'im,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: If the corpse of a slain person **was found precisely between two towns, they would not break the** heifer's **neck** at all. **Rabbi Eliezer says: The two** towns **bring two heifers** between them. **What, is it not** correct to say that **they disagree with regard to** this matter, in **that the first *tanna* holds** that it is **impossible** for two matters **to coincide precisely and Rabbi Eliezer holds** that it is **possible** for two matters **to coincide precisely?**

### Twins Simultaneity

The Gemara opens with the phrase *neima ketannai* — let us say that this is a dispute among *tanna'im*. The background is our *mishnah*, which discusses the case of two male twins born at precisely the same moment. Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds that both animals are *bechor*, while the Rabbis counter: *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* — it is impossible to be that precise. The Gemara now wishes to know whether the Rabbis would concede to Rabbi Yose HaGelili in a case of *bidei adam* — a humanly constructed or measured situation — as opposed to *bidei shamayim*, where things occur naturally without human control.

### Bidei Adam Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיסְבְּרַאּ? אִי קָסָבַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, אַמַּאי לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִים? יָבִיאוּ עֶגְלָה אַחַת בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת, וְיִתְּנוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **And** how can **you understand** that to be the dispute? **If the first *tanna* holds** that it is **impossible** for two matters **to coincide precisely, why** does he claim that **they would not break the** heifer's **neck?** Let **them bring one heifer in partnership and stipulate** that it is brought for the town that is actually nearest.

There are two possible forms of *bidei adam*. The first is a case where one constructs the situation from the outset — as in the *gemara* about a line placed at the precise midpoint of the *mizbeach*, where patience and deliberate craftsmanship make precision achievable. The second form is where one does not construct the situation but has the opportunity and patience to measure it carefully afterward.

### Dead Body Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וְהָכָא בִּ״קְרוֹבָה״ וְלֹא קְרוֹבוֹת קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: ״קְרוֹבָה״ וְלֹא קְרוֹבוֹת, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: ״קְרוֹבָה״ וַאֲפִילּוּ קְרוֹבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with regard to these *tanna'im*, everyone** agrees that it is **possible** for two matters **to coincide precisely. And here they disagree** over whether the singular form *kerova* in the phrase "the town which is **nearest [*kerova*]"** indicates that only the nearest town brings a heifer, **and not the** many **nearest [*kerovot*]** towns; **as the first *tanna* holds** that it is derived from the term **"*kerova*"** that only the nearest town brings a heifer, **and not** multiple ***kerovot*; and Rabbi Eliezer holds** that although the verse states **"*kerova*,"** nevertheless this includes **even** multiple towns that are ***kerovot*.

The second form — measurement after the fact — is precisely what is at issue in the well-known case of a murdered body found between two cities. There, the body is not placed equidistantly by design; rather, one measures after the fact to determine which city is closest. And the Gemara had already seen a position requiring two *eglot* — one from each city — when the body is found exactly equidistant. This is significant: if the law were simply that there is a doubt about which city is obligated, one would bring a single *egla* jointly from both cities, stipulating that it belongs to whichever city turns out to be closest. The fact that two *eglot* are brought demonstrates that we accept the measurement at face value: the body genuinely is equidistant, both cities are equally "closest," and both are obligated. This is strong proof that *bidei adam*, where precise measurement is possible, is a case of *efshar l'tzamtzum*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן? אִי קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וּקְרוֹבָה — וַאֲפִילּוּ קְרוֹבוֹת, לַיְיתֵי תַּרְתֵּי! וְאִי ״קְרוֹבָה״ וְלֹא קְרוֹבוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָא לָא לַיְיתֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן: אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּידֵי אָדָם. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What do the Rabbis hold** in this regard? **If the Rabbis hold that** is **possible** for two matters that are **in human hands to coincide precisely, and** the singular **"*kerova*"** includes **even** multiple towns, ***kerovot*,** then **they should bring two** heifers. **And if** they hold that **"*kerova*"** indicates: **But not *kerovot*,** then **they should not bring even one** heifer. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** it that **the Rabbis hold:** It is **impossible** for two matters **to coincide precisely, and** this is true **even** with regard to matters that are **in human hands?** Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

The Gemara initially proposes that the *egla arufa* dispute — between the *Tanna Kamma*, who says no *egla* is brought, and Rabbi Eliezer, who says both cities bring — maps directly onto the debate about *tzimtzum*. But the Gemara rejects this: if the *Tanna Kamma* truly held *iy efshar l'tzamtzem*, he would not say no *egla* at all; he would say bring one *egla* jointly, with a stipulation, since clearly one city must be closest, we simply do not know which. The fact that he says nothing is brought must mean he holds *efshar l'tzamtzem*, that the measurement can be precise — and his dispute with Rabbi Eliezer is purely about the verse. The Torah says *hakarov* — "the closest" city — in the singular. The *Tanna Kamma* reads this as excluding the case where two cities are equally close, since neither is singularly closest. Rabbi Eliezer reads *karov* as including *kerovot* — even two equally close cities qualify. Both *tanna'im*, then, agree that the measurement is reliable; they debate only the *din* when both cities are found to be equidistant.

### Physical Reality

One might ask what the Rabbis and Rabbi Yose HaGelili are really debating. One way to frame the question is whether, in physical reality, two things ever truly happen at the exact same moment. From a certain perspective, the answer is always no: one can always find a finer degree of measurement by which a difference will appear. As one participant noted, it is said that two objects never truly touch — their atoms only approach one another asymptotically. However refined the measuring instrument, there will always be some difference detectable in principle.

### Trust Measurement

But the more interesting and legally pertinent question — the one genuinely reflected in the distinction between *bidei adam* and *bidei shamayim* — is whether we can trust our measurements. The *chachamim*, on this reading, are saying: when you are simply observing, without having constructed or carefully measured the situation, you cannot say with confidence that two events were simultaneous. To call it simultaneous based on casual observation is to rely on something so rare as to be practically excluded from consideration. Rabbi Yose HaGelili disagrees: even a casual observation may be trusted. The further question is what happens when one does carefully measure. If the Rabbis say *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* even *bidei adam*, they appear to be saying that no measurement can ever be trusted to achieve true precision.

### Halacha Precision

This is a striking position, because one would think that *halacha* always operates on the basis of the best available measurement. When one measures a *kezayit* or a *tefach* or the ten *tefachim* required for various laws, we proceed on the basis of that measurement — not on the basis of some hypothetical more precise instrument that might reveal an error. So what would it mean to say that even *bidei adam*, even careful measurement, cannot be trusted to establish simultaneity or equidistance?

The only way to make sense of this, seemingly, is as follows: normally, we go by measurement because we are not trying to establish something practically impossible. Measuring a *kezayit* or a *tefach* is a realistic undertaking, and going by one's measurement is simply the *halacha*'s way of functioning. But claiming to have measured two events as perfectly simultaneous — or two distances as perfectly equal — is claiming to have achieved something so rare and so difficult that we must assume an error was made. It is not that measurements are generally distrusted; it is that this particular claim — perfect simultaneity or perfect equidistance — is so extraordinary that even after careful measurement the *chachamim* say we cannot accept it as established.

The Gemara's conclusion is that this itself is a *tannaitic* dispute. Our *mishnah* concerns *bidei shamayim*, but from the *egla arufa* case the Gemara draws evidence that there is a genuine dispute about *bidei adam* as well. The major *Tosafot* on the previous daf attempts to work through all the relevant *sugyot* in *Shas* to determine how we rule. The most logical position — that we follow the Rabbis in *bidei shamayim* cases but follow Rabbi Yose HaGelili in *bidei adam* cases — is treated by *Tosafot* as a serious possibility, though *Tosafot* also considers it possible that we rule like the Rabbis across the board, declining to accept any claim of perfect precision even when based on measurement.

## Two Animals

### Tarfon Picks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶת הַיָּפֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן? קָא סָבַר: הָהוּא דְּבָרִיא נָפֵק בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that in the case of a ewe that gave birth for the first time to two male offspring whose heads emerged as one, **Rabbi Tarfon says: The priest chooses the better** of the two. The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Tarfon? He holds** that it is presumed that **the healthier** and better of the two **emerged first,** and therefore it belongs to the priest.

The *mishnah* now turns to the practical question: given that the Rabbis hold we cannot determine which of two animals emerged first, how does one proceed? If we do not know which sheep is *bechor*, which belongs to the *kohen*?

Rabbi Tarfon says: *hakohen boreir lo hayafa* — the *kohen* picks the nicer one.

### Tarfon Logic

The Gemara explains the logic: *kasavar hahu d'barya nafik beresha* — Rabbi Tarfon reasons that the stronger, healthier, and presumably larger animal was most likely the one that forced its way out first. Since being *bechor* depends on being first, and the nicer animal is presumed to have been first, the *kohen* is entitled to select it. It is worth noting that the Gemara does not clarify whether Rabbi Tarfon regards this as definitive proof — sufficient to allow the *kohen* to bring the animal as a *korban* — or merely as probabilistic evidence sufficient to award monetary rights while still requiring that the animal develop a *mum* before use. This may be because, as the Gemara will soon note, Rabbi Tarfon ultimately reversed his position.

### Akiva Splits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״מְשַׁמְּנִין״ כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּהֵן נוֹטֵל כְּחוּשָׁה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהָא אֲנַן ״מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶן״ תְּנַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד דַּאֲכַלְתְּ כַּפְנְיָיתָא בְּבָבֶל, תַּרְגֵּימְנָא מִסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that **Rabbi Akiva says: They assess** the value of the lambs **between them. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** According to Rabbi Akiva, **the priest takes the leaner** of the two. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: But didn't we learn** that **they assess** the value of the offspring **between them,** which indicates that the priest and owner divide their value? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: While you were eating dates in Babylonia, we** in Eretz Yisrael **explained it** based **on the latter clause** in the mishna.

Rabbi Akiva says: *meshamnim beineihem* — one assesses the value between the two animals and, as the literal reading suggests, splits the difference.

### Yochanan Reads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶן״ דְּכִי הֲדָדֵי פְּלִיגִי, הָכִי נָמֵי (לִיפַּלְגִי) [לִיפְלוֹג] גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא מַאי ״מְשַׁמְּנִין״ — שׁוֹמֶן יְהֵא בֵּינֵיהֶם, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן: אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דִּבְכוֹר הוּא וּשְׁקֹיל.
>
> **Translation:** **As the latter clause teaches:** If **one of** the two born together **died, Rabbi Tarfon says:** The priest and the owner **divide** the remaining lamb, and **Rabbi Akiva says:** Since there is uncertainty to whom it belongs, it remains in the possession of the owner, as **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. And if it enters your mind** that when Rabbi Akiva said that **they assess** the value between them he meant **that they divide** the value **between them,** then in the latter clause **too, let them divide** the value of the remaining offspring **between them** instead of leaving it with the owner. **Rather, what does: They assess [*meshammenin*]** the value between them, mean? It means that the additional **fat [*shumman*]** of the better twin **will be** the subject of dispute **between them, as** the owner **says to the priest: Bring a proof that** the better one **is the firstborn, and take it.**

However, Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that this is not Rabbi Akiva's actual intent. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *kohen* simply takes the lesser animal — *hakohen d'ati kachusha* — because of the principle *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the one seeking to extract something from another's possession bears the burden of proof. Since the *kohen* cannot prove which is the *bechor*, he is entitled only to the lesser of the two.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba raised the obvious question: the *mishnah* says *meshamnim beineihem*, which appears to mean they split the difference in value, implying some form of joint ownership in both animals. How can this language be read to mean simply "take the lesser one"? Rabbi Yochanan's response — rendered colloquially in the Gemara as a gentle rebuke that in the Land of Israel they had already worked this out while the Babylonians were occupied with other pursuits — is that one should look to the *seifa*, the end of the *mishnah*, to understand what the *reisha* really means.

### Contradiction

The end of the *mishnah* states: if one of the animals dies, Rabbi Akiva says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the *kohen* is told to prove which one is his, and since he cannot, he receives the dead animal. Now, Rabbi Yochanan argues, if *meshamnim beineihem* truly meant equal joint ownership in both animals with the value split between them, then even when one dies, Rabbi Akiva should say the same thing: give me the lesser and half the difference. Why does Rabbi Akiva say the *kohen* gets nothing but the dead one? Clearly, Rabbi Akiva holds that the *kohen* has no rights in the more expensive animal at all — *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — and this must be his position in the *reisha* as well.

The phrase *meshamnim beineihem*, then, must be reread. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *shuman yehei beineihem* — what lies "between" the two animals, what differentiates them, is the *shuman*, the fat, the premium value. The *kohen* receives the lesser animal, and the differential — the degree to which one animal is worth more — is precisely what separates their respective portions. It is admittedly a forced reading of the phrase, and Rabbi Yochanan himself acknowledges as much, saying he was compelled toward this interpretation by the logic of the *seifa*.

What makes this passage particularly striking is that Rabbi Tarfon's position, too, appears inconsistent between the *reisha* and *seifa* of the *mishnah*: in the beginning he says the *kohen* takes the nicer animal, yet at the end he says *yachloku* — they split. The Gemara, as we will see shortly, addresses this by saying Rabbi Tarfon reversed himself. Whether Rabbi Akiva similarly shifted, or whether there is some internal logic that explains why different principles apply once one animal has died, remains a genuinely open question. The Vilna Gaon is among those who sometimes attempts to interpret a *mishnah* on its own terms, independent of the Gemara's reading, and this might be a case where such an exercise is warranted.

To complete the picture: the *kohen*'s animal must still develop a *mum* before it can be used, as must the animal retained by the *Yisrael* — since we cannot be certain which is truly the *bechor*, neither may be brought as a *korban* or consumed without a blemish.

### Guarding Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְחַיָּיב בְּמַתָּנוֹת, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי פּוֹטֵר. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וְכֹהֵן בָּא עָלָיו מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בְּכוֹר הוּא — כּוּלֵּיהּ דִּידִי הוּא, אִי לָא בְּכוֹר הוּא — הַב לִי מַתָּנוֹת מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And the second** lamb that remains in the possession of the owner **must graze until it becomes blemished,** at which point he may slaughter and eat it, **and** he is **obligated to** have **the gifts** of the priesthood, i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, taken from it; **and Rabbi Yosei deems** him **exempt** from giving those gifts. The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir,** whose opinion is the ruling cited first in the mishna? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The owner is obligated **since the priest comes upon him from two sides,** i.e., by force of two complementary claims, **as** the priest **says to him: If** the second lamb **is** in fact the **firstborn, it is mine** in its **entirety, and if it is not** the **firstborn,** at least **give me the** priestly **gifts from it.**

The *mishnah* states that the second animal — the one retained by the *Yisrael* — must be guarded until it develops a blemish, *yira'eh ad sheyista'ev*. Rashi notes that even the animal taken by the *kohen* requires a *mum*, though the *mishnah* treats this as understood. Furthermore, the *mishnah* rules *ve'chayav b'matanot* — one is obligated to give *matanot* from the animal one retains. Rabbi Yosei, however, exempts.

## Matanot Dispute

### Meir Both Sides

The Gemara asks: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir — identified as such because the parallel *mishnah* later on *amud bet* makes the attribution explicit — who holds one is obligated in *matanot*?

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *hava kohen ba lei mishnei tzdadim* — the *kohen* can come at the *Yisrael* from both sides. The *kohen* says: if the animal you retained is the *bechor*, *kuli didi hu* — give me the whole thing. And if it is not the *bechor*, then give me the *matanot* — the *zero'a, lechayayim, v'keiva*. Either way, whichever position the *Yisrael* adopts, the *kohen* is entitled to something from this animal. There is no escape from the *matanot* obligation.

### Yosei Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רָבָא: עָשׂוּ שֶׁאֵינוֹ זוֹכֶה כְּזוֹכֶה, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מְטָא לִידֵיהּ כְּמַאן דִּמְטָא לִידֵיהּ, וְזַבְּנֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמוּמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei,** who deems the owner exempt from giving the priestly gifts from the second animal? **Rava says:** In this particular case, the Sages **rendered one who did not acquire** the animal **like one who acquired** the animal. **And** therefore, **even though** the second lamb of the Israelite **did not** actually **enter** the priest's **possession,** it is considered **as** though **it entered** the priest's **possession and he** then **sold it to the Israelite in its blemished** state, in exchange for the lamb he received. The owner can then claim that perhaps it is the firstborn, and he already fulfilled his obligation of giving it to the priest.

### Rava Theory

Rava answers: *asu she'eino zocheh kezocheh* — they treated the one who did not actually acquire as if he had acquired. More precisely: *af al gav d'lo mati l'yadei k'man d'mati l'yadei, v'zavnei l'Yisrael b'mumei* — even though the larger, potentially more valuable animal never came into the *kohen*'s physical possession, it is as though it did come into his hand, and as though he then sold it to the *Yisrael* with a *mum*.

The logic requires unpacking. We have two animals. The *Yisrael* gives the *kohen* the lesser one, since *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the *kohen* cannot prove he deserves more. But the larger animal might in truth be the *kohen*'s. One cannot simply say the *Yisrael* has "paid off" the *kohen* with the lesser value as if it were a monetary claim, because the question is not only about money — it is about ownership of a specific object. The larger animal might be the wrong object entirely.

The resolution, on Rabbi Yosei's view, is as follows: since the *kohen* accepts the lesser animal and cannot extract more, we understand this as an implicit arrangement — the *kohen* is treated as having already taken possession of his potential ownership in the larger animal, and then as having sold that potential ownership back to the *Yisrael* in exchange for receiving the lesser one. Although no formal *kinyan* was performed, *chazal* constructed this as a legal fiction: *asu she'eino zocheh kezocheh* — they made it as though he had acquired and then sold. The result is that the larger animal, whatever its status, is now a *bechor* of a *kohen* that was sold to a *Yisrael* — and a *bechor* sold by a *kohen* to a *Yisrael* is exempt from *matanot*. Thus Rabbi Yosei exempts.

Importantly, Rabbi Yosei's exemption from *matanot* does not mean the *kohen* receives nothing. He receives the lesser animal. The question is solely whether *matanot* must also be given from the animal the *Yisrael* retains. On this, Rabbi Yosei says: no, because through the implicit exchange, the retained animal may be considered a *bechor* that passed through the *kohen*'s hands.

### Logic Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּסְפֵק בְּכוֹר, שֶׁאֵין חֲלִיפִין בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּמַתָּנוֹת. הַכֹּל מוֹדִים מַאן? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי? פְּשִׁיטָא! עַד כָּאן לָא קָא פָטַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הָתָם, אֶלָּא דַּחֲלִיפִין בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, דְּעָשׂוּ שֶׁאֵינוֹ זוֹכֶה כְּזוֹכֶה, אֲבָל אֵין חֲלִיפִין בְּיַד כֹּהֵן — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar claims that everyone — including Rabbi Yosei — would agree that if the *kohen* received nothing at all in exchange, *matanot* would be obligatory. What would such a scenario look like? Consider a case where the two births were a male and a female, and it is unknown which was born first. The *kohen* cannot claim the female as a *bechor* under any theory. If in such a case the *Yisrael* slaughters the male, must he give the *matanot*? **Rabbi Elazar says: Everyone concedes** in the case of an animal with **uncertain firstborn** status, the **replacement** for which **is not in the possession of the priest, that it is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it. In other words, in a case where the priest receives neither offspring, e.g., when a male and female are born together, all agree that the owner must give the gifts to the priest. The Gemara asks: When Rabbi Elazar said that **everyone concedes,** to **whom** was he referring? Was he referring to **Rabbi Yosei?** That seems **obvious.** After all, **Rabbi Yosei deemed** the owner **exempt** from giving the gifts **only there, where the replacement is in the possession of the priest, as** the Sages **rendered one who did not acquire** the animal **like one who acquired** the animal. **But** if **the replacement is not in the possession of the priest,** evidently Rabbi Yosei does **not** rule that one is exempt, as his reasoning does not apply. Why, then, is the statement of Rabbi Elazar necessary?

The Gemara now asks whether Rabbi Yosei's logic is indeed this *chalufin* reasoning, or whether something else is driving his position.

### Elazar Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּקָסָבַר דְּאִי מְחַיְּיבַתְּ לֵיהּ בְּמַתָּנוֹת — אָתֵי לֵיהּ לִידֵי גִּיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵין חֲלִיפִין בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Rabbi Elazar's statement is in fact necessary, **lest you say** that the **reason Rabbi Yosei** deems him exempt from giving the gifts is **that he holds that if you deem him obligated in the gifts, he** will assume that the animal is completely non-sacred and **come to** use it for **shearing and labor.** Consequently, **even though there is no replacement in the hands of the priest,** the Sages ruled that one is exempt from giving the gifts, in order to ensure that he does not violate the prohibition against shearing the animal or using it for labor. Rabbi Elazar therefore **teaches us** that this is not the *halakha*, as Rabbi Yosei agrees that one is obligated to give the priestly gifts in such circumstances.

Rabbi Elazar claims that everyone — including Rabbi Yosei — would agree that if the *kohen* received nothing at all in exchange, *matanot* would be obligatory. What would such a scenario look like? Consider a case where the two births were a male and a female, and it is unknown which was born first. The *kohen* cannot claim the female as a *bechor* under any theory. If in such a case the *Yisrael* slaughters the male, must he give the *matanot*? Rabbi Elazar says yes, and that Rabbi Yosei would agree — because Rabbi Yosei's entire exemption rests on the *kohen* having gone home with something in exchange. If the *kohen* received nothing, there is no implicit sale, and *ממה נפשך* — whichever way one looks at it — the *kohen* is entitled either to the whole animal if it is a *bechor*, or to the *matanot* if it is not.

### Yosei Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָתָנֵי סֵיפָא, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחֲלִיפָיו בְּיַד כֹּהֵן פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And how can you say this? But doesn't the latter clause** of the mishna (18b) **teach: As Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **any** animal **whose replacements** are **in the possession of the priest,** its owner **is exempt from** the mitzva of giving **the** priestly **gifts, and Rabbi Meir deems** him **obligated** to give the gifts. Evidently, in a case where the **replacement is in the possession of the priest, yes,** Rabbi Yosei rules that the owner is exempt from giving the gifts, but if the **replacement is not in the possession of the priest,** he is **not** exempt.

The Gemara accepts this framing but identifies what might have led one to think otherwise. One might have supposed that Rabbi Yosei's actual concern was a *gezeirah*: if one tells the *Yisrael* to give *matanot* from a *safek bechor*, he will conclude that this animal has been designated as a non-*bechor*, and will consequently treat it as fully his own — shearing it, working it, or even eating it without waiting for a *mum*. On this reading, Rabbi Yosei exempts from *matanot* to protect the *safek bechor* from misuse, not because of any *chalufin* theory.

If that were his reasoning, Rabbi Yosei would apply it even when the *kohen* went home empty-handed — in any case involving a *safek bechor*, he would say: do not give *matanot*, lest the *Yisrael* treat the animal as freely available. The Gemara rules out this reading: the explicit language of the later *mishnah*, where Rabbi Yosei says *kol shechalifin beyad kohen patur min hamatanot* — wherever the *kohen* has taken something in exchange, the *Yisrael* is exempt — makes unmistakably clear that the exemption is conditional on the *kohen* having received something. When the *kohen* goes home with nothing, Rabbi Yosei agrees that *matanot* are owed. His reason is purely the *chalufin* theory — the implicit sale — and not any protective *gezeirah*.

## Ma'aser Case

### Ma'aser Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּסְפֵק מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁפָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת. ״הַכֹּל מוֹדִים״ — מַאן? רַבִּי מֵאִיר? פְּשִׁיטָא! עַד כָּאן לָא קָמְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָתָם אֶלָּא בִּסְפֵק בְּכוֹר, הוֹאִיל וּבָא עָלָיו כֹּהֵן מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין, אֲבָל סָפֵק מַעֲשֵׂר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa says** with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei: **Everyone concedes** in the case **of** an animal whose status as **tithe** is **uncertain that** its owner **is exempt from** the mitzva of **the** priestly **gifts.** The Gemara asks: When Rav Pappa says that **everyone concedes,** to **whom** is he referring? Is he referring to **Rabbi Meir?** That would seem **obvious.** After all, **Rabbi Meir deemed** one **obligated** to give the gifts **only there,** in the case **of** an animal whose status as **firstborn** is **uncertain, since** the priest **comes upon** the Israelite **from two sides. But** with regard to an animal whose status as **tithe** is **uncertain** Rabbi Meir does **not** rule that one is obligated, as that reasoning does not apply. Why, then, is Rav Pappa's statement necessary?

Rav Pappa now introduces a comparative case. *Hakol modim besafek ma'aser shepatur min hamatanot* — everyone agrees that in a case of doubtful *ma'aser beheima*, the *kohen* receives no *matanot*. The scenario: one is counting out one's animals and the tenth one — the *ma'aser* — gets mixed up with the others. Now two animals are indistinguishable, one being *ma'aser* and one not. Both must wait to develop a *mum* and then be eaten by their owner. The *kohen* comes seeking *matanot* from one of them.

### Claim Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּלֹא תִּשְׁתַּכַּח תּוֹרַת מַתָּנוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ סְפֵק מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the statement of Rav Pappa is necessary **lest you say** that the **reason that Rabbi Meir** deems him obligated in the gifts is so **that the** halakhic **category of the gifts should not be forgotten;** if so, the same would apply **even** in the case of an animal whose status as **tithe** is **uncertain.** Rav Pappa therefore **teaches us** otherwise.

The difference from the *safek bechor* case is fundamental. With a *safek bechor*, the *kohen* can argue *mishnei tzdadim* — from both sides. If you call it a *bechor*, give me everything; if not, give me the *matanot*. Either way, he has a legitimate claim. With *safek ma'aser*, the *kohen* has no comparable argument. If this animal is the *ma'aser*, the *kohen* has no right to it at all — *ma'aser* is owned by its owner and eaten by the owner with a *mum*. So the *Yisrael* can simply say: perhaps this is the *ma'aser* one, and there is no *ממה נפשך* argument available. The *kohen* cannot prove entitlement to either animal individually, and therefore receives nothing.

### Institution Fear

This seems obvious, and the Gemara asks: what is the *chiddush* of Rav Pappa's statement? One might have thought, the Gemara answers, that Rabbi Meir's position — obligating the *Yisrael* to give *matanot* even for a *safek bechor* — is not based on the principled *din* that the *kohen* has a legitimate *mimah nafshach* claim, but rather on a concern *delo tishtakach torat matanot* — that the institution of *matanot* should not fall into desuetude. People might become lax about giving *matanot* if they see that in doubtful cases one is exempt. On this reading, Rabbi Meir would obligate even when there is no strict legal basis — including in cases of *safek ma'aser* — as a protective measure to ensure *matanot* remain a living practice.

### Principle Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחֲלִיפָיו בְּיַד כֹּהֵן — פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how can you say this? But doesn't the latter clause teach: As Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **any** animal **whose replacements are in the possession of a priest,** its owner **is exempt from** the mitzva of giving **the** priestly **gifts, and Rabbi Meir deems** him **obligated** to give the gifts. Evidently, the basis for the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei is not whether an obligation is imposed so that the halakhic category of the priestly gifts should not be forgotten, but whether the Sages rendered one who did not acquire the animal like one who acquired the animal, which means that an animal whose replacement is in the priest's possession is not subject to the mitzva of the gifts.

The Gemara rejects this reading of Rabbi Meir on the basis of the *seifa* of the later *mishnah*, which states explicitly that Rabbi Yosei grounds his exemption in the *chalufin* principle, and Rabbi Meir obligates. The structure of their debate presupposes that each is arguing from a principled *din* position. If Rabbi Meir's position were merely a protective ordinance, Rabbi Yosei's response — emphasizing the *chalufin* theory — would be non-responsive to Rabbi Meir's concern. One does not counter "we should protect the institution" with "but there was an implicit sale." The fact that Rabbi Yosei argues the *chalufin* point means Rabbi Meir's position must be a substantive claim about entitlement under the law.

Reciprocally, the Gemara also considers whether one might have thought Rabbi Yosei's *chalufin* argument was offered not as his genuine reasoning but as a rhetorical move aimed at convincing Rabbi Meir — and that Rabbi Yosei's own view, even without any *chalufin*, would exempt from *matanot* on *gezeirah* grounds. The explicit language of the later *mishnah* forecloses this as well: Rabbi Yosei's exemption is conditional on *chalifin beyad kohen*, and applies only when the *kohen* took something in exchange. Without exchange, Rabbi Yosei concedes *matanot* are owed.

The upshot, confirmed by the Gemara, is that the entire dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei — including its extension to *ma'aser* cases — is a debate *al pi din*, on the merits of the law itself. Rabbi Meir holds the *kohen* can claim from both sides and is therefore entitled to *matanot*. Rabbi Yosei holds that the implicit exchange constitutes a legal sale that severs the *matanot* obligation. Neither is animated by secondary protective concerns.

## Death Division

### Tarfon Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ. אַמַּאי יַחְלוֹקוּ? נִיחְזֵי: אִי שָׁמֵן מִית — דְּכֹהֵן הוּא, וְהַאי דְּאִיכָּא דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת; וְאִי כָּחוּשׁ מִית — דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת מִית, וְהַאי דְּאִיכָּא דְּכֹהֵן הוּא! אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If **one of** the two born together **died, Rabbi Tarfon says:** The priest and the owner **divide** the remaining lamb. The Gemara asks: **Why** should they **divide** it? **Let us see:** According to Rabbi Tarfon, who holds that it is presumed that the healthier and better of the two emerged first, as explained earlier, **if** the **fatter** and better of the two **died** it should be assumed **that it** was the one that belonged **to the priest, and this** one **that is** left belongs **to the owner. And if the leaner** of the two **died,** it should be assumed that the animal **of the owner died, and this** one **that is** left belongs **to the priest.** Rabbi **Ami said:** In fact, **Rabbi Tarfon retracted** his previous ruling that the priest takes the better of the two, and instead ruled that the priest and owner divide the value of the offspring in all cases. The mishna was not emended to reflect the updated ruling.

The *mishnah* now reaches the case already anticipated: one of the two animals dies. Rabbi Tarfon says *yachloku* — they split. This is the position that prompted Rabbi Yochanan's reinterpretation of the *reisha*, since it is flatly inconsistent with Rabbi Tarfon's earlier statement that the *kohen* picks the nicer animal. If Rabbi Tarfon truly held in the *reisha* that the nicer animal is presumptively the *bechor* and belongs to the *kohen*, then when one dies, the outcome should be determinable: if the nicer one died, it was the *kohen*'s and the remaining one is the *Yisrael*'s; if the lesser one died, it was the *Yisrael*'s and the remaining one is the *kohen*'s. There would be no basis for splitting.

Rabbi Ami therefore says: *chazar bo Rabbi Tarfon* — Rabbi Tarfon reversed his position. His final ruling is *yachloku*, and the *reisha* represents an earlier view he subsequently abandoned. Rabbi Tarfon's operative position is thus that the two parties split what remains.

### Reversed Position

### Burden Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל רוֹעֶה, שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ רוֹעֶה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: In a case where two animals were born together and one of them died, **Rabbi Akiva says:** The living offspring remains in the possession of the owner, as **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. Rabbi Ḥiyya stated a parable** that clarifies the opinion **of Rabbi Tarfon,** who says that the priest and the owner divide the remaining lamb: **To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to two** people **who** each **deposited** a sheep **with a shepherd** and one of the two sheep died, and it is unclear to whom it belonged. In **that** case, the **shepherd places** the remaining sheep **between them and withdraws,** leaving them to divide the value of the sheep between them. Likewise, the mishna is discussing a case where the live offspring is not in the possession of either the owner or the priest, and therefore it is divided between them.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof rests on the one seeking to extract from another's possession. The *kohen* cannot prove that the one that died was the *Yisrael*'s, so the *kohen* is told: prove it, and if you cannot, receive the dead one.

### Two Analogies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאֶחָד שֶׁהִפְקִיד אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, שֶׁהַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Ḥiyya also stated **a parable** that clarifies the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to one who deposited** a sheep **with a homeowner** who had sheep of his own, and it is unknown whose sheep died. In **that** case, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** Since the remaining sheep is currently in the possession of the homeowner, the one who deposited the animal must prove that the living sheep belongs to him. Likewise, the mishna is discussing a case where the live offspring is in the possession of the owner, and therefore the burden of proof rests upon the priest.

The Gemara introduces two analogies to map the logical terrain. Rabbi Tarfon's position resembles the case of two people who each deposited an animal with a shepherd, one of the animals died, and now neither owner knows which of their animals survived. In that case, the shepherd holds both animals as a neutral third party. He can only say: here is the surviving animal, sort it out yourselves. Since neither claimant is in possession, there is no *hamotzi mechavero* — no one is extracting from the other's *reshut* — and they split.

Rabbi Akiva's position resembles the case of a single person who deposited an animal with a *ba'al habayit*, it became mixed with the *ba'al habayit*'s animals, and one died. There, the *ba'al habayit* is in possession of the surviving animal, and the depositor is trying to extract from that possession. The depositor bears the burden of proof, and if he cannot establish which survived, he must accept the dead one.

The difficulty is that the actual case in our *mishnah* does not map neatly onto either analogy. The animals were born in the *Yisrael*'s possession and remain there — this looks like the *ba'al habayit* scenario, in which case everyone should agree on *hamotzi mechavero*. What, then, is Rabbi Tarfon's basis for *yachloku*?

### Kohen Shepherd

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא פְּלִיג בִּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל רוֹעֶה, שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ רוֹעֶה וּמִסְתַּלֵּק? וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן פְּלִיג בְּאֶחָד שֶׁהִפְקִיד אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת? אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל רוֹעֶה — שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ רוֹעֶה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, וּבְאֶחָד שֶׁהִפְקִיד אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת — שֶׁהַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva are addressing different circumstances, then **with regard to what** matter **do they disagree?** Does **Rabbi Akiva disagree** with the conclusion in the parable mentioned with regard to Rabbi Tarfon's opinion, **of two** people **who** each **deposited** a sheep **with a shepherd, that** the **shepherd places** the remaining sheep between them **and withdraws? And** similarly, does **Rabbi Tarfon disagree** with the conclusion in the case **of one who deposited** a sheep **with a homeowner** that the homeowner keeps the remaining sheep? Certainly not. Rather, **Rava says, and some say** it was **Rav Pappa** who says: **Everyone concedes** in the case **of two who deposited** sheep **with a shepherd that** the **shepherd places** it **between them and withdraws, and** everyone concedes in the case **of one who deposited** a sheep **with a homeowner that the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.**

Rava, and some say Rav Pappa, answer that everyone agrees on both basic models. In the pure shepherd case — neutral third party, no one has superior possession — everyone agrees on *yachloku*. In the pure *ba'al habayit* case — one party holds, the other seeks to extract — everyone agrees on *hamotzi mechavero*. The dispute arises only in a specific intermediate scenario: *bechatzer ba'al habayit ve'ro'eh kohen* — the animals are in the *Yisrael*'s yard, but the *kohen* is serving as the shepherd.

### Tarfon Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בַּחֲצַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְרוֹעֶה כֹּהֵן. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן סָבַר: אַקְנוֹיֵי קָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ בַּחֲצֵירוֹ, וְנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלִיתְעֲבִיד מִצְוָה בְּמָמוֹנֵיהּ, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל רוֹעֶה, שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ רוֹעֶה בֵּינֵיהֶן וּמִסְתַּלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon **disagree only** where the offspring were born **in the courtyard of the homeowner, and** the **shepherd is a priest** to whom the homeowner usually gives his firstborn animals. **Rabbi Tarfon holds:** The homeowner **transfers to** the priest a portion **of his courtyard** so that the priest should acquire the firstborn offspring located there, because it is **suitable for him that** the **mitzva** of giving the firstborn to a priest **should be performed.** Therefore, the priest is considered to hold partial jurisdiction of the courtyard. **And** consequently, **this is** comparable **to two** people **who** each **deposited** a sheep **with a shepherd** and one of the two sheep died, **where** the **shepherd places** the remaining sheep **between them and withdraws,** leaving them to divide the value of the sheep between themselves.

Rabbi Tarfon holds *aknoiye kamachni lei bechatzero* — by appointing this *kohen* as his shepherd, the *Yisrael* implicitly grants the *kohen* a degree of possession in the yard sufficient to acquire the *bechor* at the moment of birth. The reasoning: since it is understood that the *kohen*-shepherd will receive any *bechor* that is born, it is practically necessary and implicitly intended that the *kohen* be able to acquire it immediately even if he is not physically present. Thus the *Yisrael* effectively grants the *kohen* a partial right in the field for this purpose — *venicha lei deliavid mitzvah bemamonei* — the *Yisrael* wishes the *kohen* to perform a *mitzvah* through his property. Since both parties effectively have a stake in the animals, it resembles the shepherd case where neither has exclusive possession, and they must split.

### Akiva Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא — לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ מִידַּעַם, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּאֶחָד שֶׁהִפְקִיד אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, שֶׁהַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva holds: Since there is a loss** to the owner in this case of uncertainty, **he does not transfer anything** to the priest. **And** as the courtyard belongs exclusively to the Israelite, this case **is** comparable **to one who deposited** a sheep **with a homeowner** who had sheep of his own, and it is unknown whose sheep died. The *halakha* in **that** case is that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.**

Rabbi Akiva counters: *kivan de'is lei pseida* — precisely because the *Yisrael* knows that in cases of doubt he may lose the benefit of the doubt, he would never implicitly grant the *kohen* rights to the field. He may intend to give the *kohen* a *bechor* when one is born, but he does not grant the *kohen* acquisition rights in the yard. Since the *kohen* has no formal *kinyan* in the field, all the animals remain in the *Yisrael*'s possession, and the *kohen* who comes to claim one is the *hamotzi* — the one extracting from another's domain — and bears the burden of proof.

## Animal Variations

### Gender Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי רְחֵילָיו שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרוּ וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — שְׁנֵיהֶם לַכֹּהֵן. זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — הַזָּכָר לַכֹּהֵן. שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבָה — אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לַכֹּהֵן. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַכֹּהֵן בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶת הַיָּפֶה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶן, וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one's two ewes had not** previously **given birth and they gave birth to two males, both of them** are given **to the priest,** as each is its mother's firstborn. If one gave birth to **a male and** the other to **a female, the male** is given **to the priest,** as it is its mother's firstborn. If they gave birth to **two males and a female, one** of the males is kept **by him and one** is given **to the priest. Rabbi Tarfon says: The priest chooses the better** of the two. **Rabbi Akiva says: They assess** the value of the lambs **between them,** and the priest takes the leaner of the two. **And the second** lamb must **graze until it becomes blemished,** at which point the owner may slaughter and eat it.

The *mishnah* now repeats and elaborates the same *halachic* principles across a series of variations, with the underlying rules remaining constant throughout. If two sheep that have never previously given birth each produce a male, *shneihem lakohen* — the *kohen* receives both. If one produces a male and one a female, *hazachar lakohen* — the male goes to the *kohen*, straightforwardly.

### Multiple Births

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיב בְּמַתָּנוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי פּוֹטֵר. מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת וְזָכָר אוֹ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּשְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** when the owner slaughters the animal, he is **obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood, i.e., the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, taken from it. **Rabbi Yosei deems** him **exempt** from giving the gifts. If **one of** the two born together **died, Rabbi Tarfon says:** The priest and the owner **divide** the value of the remaining lamb. **Rabbi Akiva says:** Since there is uncertainty to whom it belongs, it remains in the possession of the owner, as **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** If they gave birth to **two females and a male or** to **two males and two females, the priest has nothing here,** as perhaps both ewes gave birth to females first.

If two sheep together produce two males and a female, one *bechor* certainly belongs to the *kohen* — since at least one of the animals bore a male first — but it is unknown which of the two males is the *bechor*. The positions of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva apply as before. If the births produce two females and a male, or two males and two females, it is possible in each scenario that the female emerged first from each of the two animals, meaning the *kohen* might receive nothing at all. Since the *kohen* cannot establish his claim, *ein kan lakohen klum* — the *kohen* gets nothing.

### Prior Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת בִּיכְּרָה, וְאַחַת לֹא בִּיכְּרָה, וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים, אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לַכֹּהֵן. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַכֹּהֵן בּוֹרֵר אֶת הַיָּפֶה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב. וְחַיָּיב בְּמַתָּנוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחֲלִיפָיו בְּיַד כֹּהֵן פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב. מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** of his ewes **had** previously **given birth and one had not** previously **given birth, and they gave birth to two males, one** of the males is kept **by him and one** is given **to the priest. Rabbi Tarfon says: The priest chooses the better** of the two. **Rabbi Akiva says: They assess** the value of the lambs **between them** and the priest takes the leaner of the two. **And the second** lamb must **graze until it becomes blemished,** at which point he may slaughter and eat it. **And** when he slaughters the animal he **is obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood taken from it. **Rabbi Yosei deems** him **exempt** him from giving those gifts, **as Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **any** animal **whose replacements are in the possession of a priest,** its owner **is exempt from** the mitzva of giving **the** priestly **gifts. And Rabbi Meir deems** him **obligated** to give the gifts. If **one of** the animals **died, Rabbi Tarfon says:** The priest and the owner **divide** the value of the remaining lamb. **Rabbi Akiva says:** Since there is uncertainty to whom it belongs, it remains in the possession of the owner, as **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** If **a male and a female** offspring were born together, everyone agrees that **the priest has nothing here,** as perhaps the one that had already given birth bore the male, and the one that had not given birth bore the female, in which case neither of the animals would have firstborn status.

Another scenario: one animal has previously given birth — meaning it has already had a *bechor* — and one has not. Together they produce two males. Since only the animal that has not yet had a *bechor* can produce a *bechor* now, one male certainly belongs to the *kohen* — but which one is unknown. Rabbi Tarfon says the *kohen* picks the nicer one; Rabbi Akiva says *meshamnim beineihem*, read as the *kohen* taking the lesser one. The *mishnah* obligates the retained animal in *matanot*; Rabbi Yosei exempts, for the reasons already analyzed — and it is here that the *mishnah* makes explicit what was earlier implicit, with Rabbi Yosei stating: *kol shechalifin beyad kohen patur min hamatanot*, wherever the *kohen* takes something in exchange, one is exempt. Rabbi Meir obligates. The same positions on the death of one animal follow: Rabbi Tarfon says *yachloku*, Rabbi Akiva says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*.

If the births yield a male and a female and one animal had previously given birth, the *kohen* gets nothing — since it is possible the male came from the animal that had already had a *bechor*, and the female from the one that had not.

### Repetition Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא, בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דִּתְרֵי מֵחֲדָא. אֲבָל רְחֵילָיו שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרוּ, דִּתְרֵי מֵחֲדָא וְחַד מֵחֲדָא — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, דְּהָךְ דִּילִידָא חַד שְׁבִיחַ טְפֵי. וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן הָא — בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ לֹא בִּיכְּרוּ, אֲבָל אַחַת בִּיכְּרָה וְאַחַת שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, דְּהָךְ דְּלָא בִּיכְּרָה שְׁבִיחַ טְפֵי צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: This mishna and the previous mishna (17a–b) listed three cases in which Rabbi Akiva rules that the priest receives the leaner of two potentially firstborn offspring, whereas Rabbi Tarfon maintains that he receives the better of the two. The Gemara notes: It was **necessary** to teach each of these three cases, **as, had** the mishna **taught** only **the first** case, that of a single ewe that gave birth to two male offspring, one might have thought that **Rabbi Akiva stated** his opinion only **in that** case, **as** the **two** offspring came **from one** womb, and there is no reason to assume that the better offspring was born first. **But** in the second case, that of **his two ewes** which **had not** previously **given birth** and which collectively gave birth to two males and a female, **where two** offspring came **from one** mother **and one** offspring came **from one** other mother, one might **say** that Rabbi Akiva **concedes to** the opinion **of Rabbi Tarfon,** as it can be claimed **that** the **superior** animal is **this** one that came from the mother **that gave birth to a single** offspring. **And had** the mishna **taught** only **that** second case, one might have thought that **Rabbi Akiva stated** his opinion only **in that** case, **as both** mothers **had not** previously **given birth. But** in a case where **one had** previously **given birth and** the other **one had not** previously **given birth, and** collectively they **gave birth to two male** offspring, one might **say** that Rabbi Akiva **concedes to** the opinion of Rabbi Tarfon

The Gemara asks the natural question: given that all these cases ultimately turn on the same principles, why does the *mishnah* bother to enumerate them all? Is it not simply a repetition of the same *halacha* applied to slightly different facts?

### Strength Proof

The Gemara answers: *tzericha* — each case is necessary. Without the full enumeration, one might think that in some configurations the physical qualities of the animals — their size, health, or vigor — could serve as evidence of birth order and thus resolve the doubt. For instance, in the case where one animal produced twins and the other a single birth, one might think the stronger male, presumably the single birth, could be identified as the *bechor* with enough confidence to award it to the *kohen* definitively. Rashi notes that this argument has some appeal: a single birth is typically more robust than a twin, and if one could identify the single-birth male as the healthier one, one might accept this as proof of its *bechor* status. Similarly, in the case where one animal had previously given birth and one had not, one might reason that the animal never before having given birth produces a healthier offspring, and therefore the healthier male is more likely to be the *bechor*.

Each scenario in the *mishnah* is therefore required to teach that Rabbi Akiva — and the *halacha* as a whole — does not employ such reasoning. No matter how suggestive the physical evidence, strength and healthiness cannot serve as probative evidence of birth order. The *tzericha* across all the scenarios collectively establishes this principle, ruling out in each configuration the argument that might have seemed most natural for using physical appearance to resolve the doubt.
