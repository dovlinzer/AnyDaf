# Bekhorot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Precision Debate

### Twins Simultaneity

The Gemara opens with the phrase *neima ketannai* — let us say that this is a dispute among *tanna'im*. The background is our *mishnah*, which discusses the case of two male twins born at precisely the same moment. Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds that both animals are *bechor*, while the Rabbis counter: *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* — it is impossible to be that precise. The Gemara now wishes to know whether the Rabbis would concede to Rabbi Yose HaGelili in a case of *bidei adam* — a humanly constructed or measured situation — as opposed to *bidei shamayim*, where things occur naturally without human control.

### Bidei Adam Types

There are two possible forms of *bidei adam*. The first is a case where one constructs the situation from the outset — as in the *gemara* about a line placed at the precise midpoint of the *mizbeach*, where patience and deliberate craftsmanship make precision achievable. The second form is where one does not construct the situation but has the opportunity and patience to measure it carefully afterward.

### Dead Body Case

The second form — measurement after the fact — is precisely what is at issue in the well-known case of a murdered body found between two cities. There, the body is not placed equidistantly by design; rather, one measures after the fact to determine which city is closest. And the Gemara had already seen a position requiring two *eglot* — one from each city — when the body is found exactly equidistant. This is significant: if the law were simply that there is a doubt about which city is obligated, one would bring a single *egla* jointly from both cities, stipulating that it belongs to whichever city turns out to be closest. The fact that two *eglot* are brought demonstrates that we accept the measurement at face value: the body genuinely is equidistant, both cities are equally "closest," and both are obligated. This is strong proof that *bidei adam*, where precise measurement is possible, is a case of *efshar l'tzamtzem*.

The Gemara initially proposes that the *egla arufa* dispute — between the *Tanna Kamma*, who says no *egla* is brought, and Rabbi Eliezer, who says both cities bring — maps directly onto the debate about *tzimtzum*. But the Gemara rejects this: if the *Tanna Kamma* truly held *iy efshar l'tzamtzem*, he would not say no *egla* at all; he would say bring one *egla* jointly, with a stipulation, since clearly one city must be closest, we simply do not know which. The fact that he says nothing is brought must mean he holds *efshar l'tzamtzem*, that the measurement can be precise — and his dispute with Rabbi Eliezer is purely about the verse. The Torah says *hakarov* — "the closest" city — in the singular. The *Tanna Kamma* reads this as excluding the case where two cities are equally close, since neither is singularly closest. Rabbi Eliezer reads *karov* as including *kerovot* — even two equally close cities qualify. Both *tanna'im*, then, agree that the measurement is reliable; they debate only the *din* when both cities are found to be equidistant.

### Physical Reality

One might ask what the Rabbis and Rabbi Yose HaGelili are really debating. One way to frame the question is whether, in physical reality, two things ever truly happen at the exact same moment. From a certain perspective, the answer is always no: one can always find a finer degree of measurement by which a difference will appear. As one participant noted, it is said that two objects never truly touch — their atoms only approach one another asymptotically. However refined the measuring instrument, there will always be some difference detectable in principle.

### Trust Measurement

But the more interesting and legally pertinent question — the one genuinely reflected in the distinction between *bidei adam* and *bidei shamayim* — is whether we can trust our measurements. The *chachamim*, on this reading, are saying: when you are simply observing, without having constructed or carefully measured the situation, you cannot say with confidence that two events were simultaneous. To call it simultaneous based on casual observation is to rely on something so rare as to be practically excluded from consideration. Rabbi Yose HaGelili disagrees: even a casual observation may be trusted. The further question is what happens when one does carefully measure. If the Rabbis say *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* even *bidei adam*, they appear to be saying that no measurement can ever be trusted to achieve true precision.

### Halacha Precision

This is a striking position, because one would think that *halacha* always operates on the basis of the best available measurement. When one measures a *kezayit* or a *tefach* or the ten *tefachim* required for various laws, we proceed on the basis of that measurement — not on the basis of some hypothetical more precise instrument that might reveal an error. So what would it mean to say that even *bidei adam*, even careful measurement, cannot be trusted to establish simultaneity or equidistance?

The only way to make sense of this, seemingly, is as follows: normally, we go by measurement because we are not trying to establish something practically impossible. Measuring a *kezayit* or a *tefach* is a realistic undertaking, and going by one's measurement is simply the *halacha*'s way of functioning. But claiming to have measured two events as perfectly simultaneous — or two distances as perfectly equal — is claiming to have achieved something so rare and so difficult that we must assume an error was made. It is not that measurements are generally distrusted; it is that this particular claim — perfect simultaneity or perfect equidistance — is so extraordinary that even after careful measurement the *chachamim* say we cannot accept it as established.

The Gemara's conclusion is that this itself is a *tannaitic* dispute. Our *mishnah* concerns *bidei shamayim*, but from the *egla arufa* case the Gemara draws evidence that there is a genuine dispute about *bidei adam* as well. The major *Tosafot* on the previous daf attempts to work through all the relevant *sugyot* in *Shas* to determine how we rule. The most logical position — that we follow the Rabbis in *bidei shamayim* cases but follow Rabbi Yose HaGelili in *bidei adam* cases — is treated by *Tosafot* as a serious possibility, though *Tosafot* also considers it possible that we rule like the Rabbis across the board, declining to accept any claim of perfect precision even when based on measurement.

## Two Animals

### Tarfon Picks

The *mishnah* now turns to the practical question: given that the Rabbis hold we cannot determine which of two animals emerged first, how does one proceed? If we do not know which sheep is *bechor*, which belongs to the *kohen*?

Rabbi Tarfon says: *hakohen boreir lo hayafa* — the *kohen* picks the nicer one.

### Tarfon Logic

The Gemara explains the logic: *kasavar hahu d'barya nafik beresha* — Rabbi Tarfon reasons that the stronger, healthier, and presumably larger animal was most likely the one that forced its way out first. Since being *bechor* depends on being first, and the nicer animal is presumed to have been first, the *kohen* is entitled to select it. It is worth noting that the Gemara does not clarify whether Rabbi Tarfon regards this as definitive proof — sufficient to allow the *kohen* to bring the animal as a *korban* — or merely as probabilistic evidence sufficient to award monetary rights while still requiring that the animal develop a *mum* before use. This may be because, as the Gemara will soon note, Rabbi Tarfon ultimately reversed his position.

### Akiva Splits

Rabbi Akiva says: *meshamnim beineihem* — one assesses the value between the two animals and, as the literal reading suggests, splits the difference.

### Yochanan Reads

However, Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that this is not Rabbi Akiva's actual intent. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *kohen* simply takes the lesser animal — *hakohen d'ati kachusha* — because of the principle *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the one seeking to extract something from another's possession bears the burden of proof. Since the *kohen* cannot prove which is the *bechor*, he is entitled only to the lesser of the two.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba raised the obvious question: the *mishnah* says *meshamnim beineihem*, which appears to mean they split the difference in value, implying some form of joint ownership in both animals. How can this language be read to mean simply "take the lesser one"? Rabbi Yochanan's response — rendered colloquially in the Gemara as a gentle rebuke that in the Land of Israel they had already worked this out while the Babylonians were occupied with other pursuits — is that one should look to the *seifa*, the end of the *mishnah*, to understand what the *reisha* really means.

### Contradiction

The end of the *mishnah* states: if one of the animals dies, Rabbi Akiva says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the *kohen* is told to prove which one is his, and since he cannot, he receives the dead animal. Now, Rabbi Yochanan argues, if *meshamnim beineihem* truly meant equal joint ownership in both animals with the value split between them, then even when one dies, Rabbi Akiva should say the same thing: give me the lesser and half the difference. Why does Rabbi Akiva say the *kohen* gets nothing but the dead one? Clearly, Rabbi Akiva holds that the *kohen* has no rights in the more expensive animal at all — *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — and this must be his position in the *reisha* as well.

The phrase *meshamnim beineihem*, then, must be reread. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *shuman yehei beineihem* — what lies "between" the two animals, what differentiates them, is the *shuman*, the fat, the premium value. The *kohen* receives the lesser animal, and the differential — the degree to which one animal is worth more — is precisely what separates their respective portions. It is admittedly a forced reading of the phrase, and Rabbi Yochanan himself acknowledges as much, saying he was compelled toward this interpretation by the logic of the *seifa*.

What makes this passage particularly striking is that Rabbi Tarfon's position, too, appears inconsistent between the *reisha* and *seifa* of the *mishnah*: in the beginning he says the *kohen* takes the nicer animal, yet at the end he says *yachloku* — they split. The Gemara, as we will see shortly, addresses this by saying Rabbi Tarfon reversed himself. Whether Rabbi Akiva similarly shifted, or whether there is some internal logic that explains why different principles apply once one animal has died, remains a genuinely open question. The Vilna Gaon is among those who sometimes attempts to interpret a *mishnah* on its own terms, independent of the Gemara's reading, and this might be a case where such an exercise is warranted.

To complete the picture: the *kohen*'s animal must still develop a *mum* before it can be used, as must the animal retained by the *Yisrael* — since we cannot be certain which is truly the *bechor*, neither may be brought as a *korban* or consumed without a blemish.

### Guarding Blemish

The *mishnah* states that the second animal — the one retained by the *Yisrael* — must be guarded until it develops a blemish, *yira'eh ad sheyista'ev*. Rashi notes that even the animal taken by the *kohen* requires a *mum*, though the *mishnah* treats this as understood. Furthermore, the *mishnah* rules *ve'chayav b'matanot* — one is obligated to give *matanot* from the animal one retains. Rabbi Yosei, however, exempts.

## Matanot Dispute

### Meir Both Sides

The Gemara asks: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir — identified as such because the parallel *mishnah* later on *amud bet* makes the attribution explicit — who holds one is obligated in *matanot*?

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *hava kohen ba lei mishnei tzdadim* — the *kohen* can come at the *Yisrael* from both sides. The *kohen* says: if the animal you retained is the *bechor*, *kuli didi hu* — give me the whole thing. And if it is not the *bechor*, then give me the *matanot* — the *zero'a, lechayayim, v'keiva*. Either way, whichever position the *Yisrael* adopts, the *kohen* is entitled to something from this animal. There is no escape from the *matanot* obligation.

### Yosei Sale

The Gemara then asks: what is Rabbi Yosei's reasoning for exempting?

### Rava Theory

Rava answers: *asu she'eino zocheh kezocheh* — they treated the one who did not actually acquire as if he had acquired. More precisely: *af al gav d'lo mati l'yadei k'man d'mati l'yadei, v'zavnei l'Yisrael b'mumei* — even though the larger, potentially more valuable animal never came into the *kohen*'s physical possession, it is as though it did come into his hand, and as though he then sold it to the *Yisrael* with a *mum*.

The logic requires unpacking. We have two animals. The *Yisrael* gives the *kohen* the lesser one, since *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the *kohen* cannot prove he deserves more. But the larger animal might in truth be the *kohen*'s. One cannot simply say the *Yisrael* has "paid off" the *kohen* with the lesser value as if it were a monetary claim, because the question is not only about money — it is about ownership of a specific object. The larger animal might be the wrong object entirely.

The resolution, on Rabbi Yosei's view, is as follows: since the *kohen* accepts the lesser animal and cannot extract more, we understand this as an implicit arrangement — the *kohen* is treated as having already taken possession of his potential ownership in the larger animal, and then as having sold that potential ownership back to the *Yisrael* in exchange for receiving the lesser one. Although no formal *kinyan* was performed, *chazal* constructed this as a legal fiction: *asu she'eino zocheh kezocheh* — they made it as though he had acquired and then sold. The result is that the larger animal, whatever its status, is now a *bechor* of a *kohen* that was sold to a *Yisrael* — and a *bechor* sold by a *kohen* to a *Yisrael* is exempt from *matanot*. Thus Rabbi Yosei exempts.

Importantly, Rabbi Yosei's exemption from *matanot* does not mean the *kohen* receives nothing. He receives the lesser animal. The question is solely whether *matanot* must also be given from the animal the *Yisrael* retains. On this, Rabbi Yosei says: no, because through the implicit exchange, the retained animal may be considered a *bechor* that passed through the *kohen*'s hands.

### Logic Question

The Gemara now asks whether Rabbi Yosei's logic is indeed this *chalufin* reasoning, or whether something else is driving his position.

### Elazar Agrees

Rabbi Elazar claims that everyone — including Rabbi Yosei — would agree that if the *kohen* received nothing at all in exchange, *matanot* would be obligatory. What would such a scenario look like? Consider a case where the two births were a male and a female, and it is unknown which was born first. The *kohen* cannot claim the female as a *bechor* under any theory. If in such a case the *Yisrael* slaughters the male, must he give the *matanot*? Rabbi Elazar says yes, and that Rabbi Yosei would agree — because Rabbi Yosei's entire exemption rests on the *kohen* having gone home with something in exchange. If the *kohen* received nothing, there is no implicit sale, and *ממה נפשך* — whichever way one looks at it — the *kohen* is entitled either to the whole animal if it is a *bechor*, or to the *matanot* if it is not.

### Yosei Exchange

The Gemara accepts this framing but identifies what might have led one to think otherwise. One might have supposed that Rabbi Yosei's actual concern was a *gezeirah*: if one tells the *Yisrael* to give *matanot* from a *safek bechor*, he will conclude that this animal has been designated as a non-*bechor*, and will consequently treat it as fully his own — shearing it, working it, or even eating it without waiting for a *mum*. On this reading, Rabbi Yosei exempts from *matanot* to protect the *safek bechor* from misuse, not because of any *chalufin* theory.

If that were his reasoning, Rabbi Yosei would apply it even when the *kohen* went home empty-handed — in any case involving a *safek bechor*, he would say: do not give *matanot*, lest the *Yisrael* treat the animal as freely available. The Gemara rules out this reading: the explicit language of the later *mishnah*, where Rabbi Yosei says *kol shechalifin beyad kohen patur min hamatanot* — wherever the *kohen* has taken something in exchange, the *Yisrael* is exempt — makes unmistakably clear that the exemption is conditional on the *kohen* having received something. When the *kohen* goes home with nothing, Rabbi Yosei agrees that *matanot* are owed. His reason is purely the *chalufin* theory — the implicit sale — and not any protective *gezeirah*.

## Ma'aser Case

### Ma'aser Nothing

Rav Pappa now introduces a comparative case. *Hakol modim besafek ma'aser shepatur min hamatanot* — everyone agrees that in a case of doubtful *ma'aser beheima*, the *kohen* receives no *matanot*. The scenario: one is counting out one's animals and the tenth one — the *ma'aser* — gets mixed up with the others. Now two animals are indistinguishable, one being *ma'aser* and one not. Both must wait to develop a *mum* and then be eaten by their owner. The *kohen* comes seeking *matanot* from one of them.

### Claim Difference

The difference from the *safek bechor* case is fundamental. With a *safek bechor*, the *kohen* can argue *mishnei tzdadim* — from both sides. If you call it a *bechor*, give me everything; if not, give me the *matanot*. Either way, he has a legitimate claim. With *safek ma'aser*, the *kohen* has no comparable argument. If this animal is the *ma'aser*, the *kohen* has no right to it at all — *ma'aser* is owned by its owner and eaten by the owner with a *mum*. So the *Yisrael* can simply say: perhaps this is the *ma'aser* one, and there is no *ממה נפשך* argument available. The *kohen* cannot prove entitlement to either animal individually, and therefore receives nothing.

### Institution Fear

This seems obvious, and the Gemara asks: what is the *chiddush* of Rav Pappa's statement? One might have thought, the Gemara answers, that Rabbi Meir's position — obligating the *Yisrael* to give *matanot* even for a *safek bechor* — is not based on the principled *din* that the *kohen* has a legitimate *mimah nafshach* claim, but rather on a concern *delo tishtakach torat matanot* — that the institution of *matanot* should not fall into desuetude. People might become lax about giving *matanot* if they see that in doubtful cases one is exempt. On this reading, Rabbi Meir would obligate even when there is no strict legal basis — including in cases of *safek ma'aser* — as a protective measure to ensure *matanot* remain a living practice.

### Principle Debate

The Gemara rejects this reading of Rabbi Meir on the basis of the *seifa* of the later *mishnah*, which states explicitly that Rabbi Yosei grounds his exemption in the *chalufin* principle, and Rabbi Meir obligates. The structure of their debate presupposes that each is arguing from a principled *din* position. If Rabbi Meir's position were merely a protective ordinance, Rabbi Yosei's response — emphasizing the *chalufin* theory — would be non-responsive to Rabbi Meir's concern. One does not counter "we should protect the institution" with "but there was an implicit sale." The fact that Rabbi Yosei argues the *chalufin* point means Rabbi Meir's position must be a substantive claim about entitlement under the law.

Reciprocally, the Gemara also considers whether one might have thought Rabbi Yosei's *chalufin* argument was offered not as his genuine reasoning but as a rhetorical move aimed at convincing Rabbi Meir — and that Rabbi Yosei's own view, even without any *chalufin*, would exempt from *matanot* on *gezeirah* grounds. The explicit language of the later *mishnah* forecloses this as well: Rabbi Yosei's exemption is conditional on *chalifin beyad kohen*, and applies only when the *kohen* took something in exchange. Without exchange, Rabbi Yosei concedes *matanot* are owed.

The upshot, confirmed by the Gemara, is that the entire dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei — including its extension to *ma'aser* cases — is a debate *al pi din*, on the merits of the law itself. Rabbi Meir holds the *kohen* can claim from both sides and is therefore entitled to *matanot*. Rabbi Yosei holds that the implicit exchange constitutes a legal sale that severs the *matanot* obligation. Neither is animated by secondary protective concerns.

## Death Division

### Tarfon Reversal

The *mishnah* now reaches the case already anticipated: one of the two animals dies. Rabbi Tarfon says *yachloku* — they split. This is the position that prompted Rabbi Yochanan's reinterpretation of the *reisha*, since it is flatly inconsistent with Rabbi Tarfon's earlier statement that the *kohen* picks the nicer animal. If Rabbi Tarfon truly held in the *reisha* that the nicer animal is presumptively the *bechor* and belongs to the *kohen*, then when one dies, the outcome should be determinable: if the nicer one died, it was the *kohen*'s and the remaining one is the *Yisrael*'s; if the lesser one died, it was the *Yisrael*'s and the remaining one is the *kohen*'s. There would be no basis for splitting.

### Reversed Position

Rabbi Ami therefore says: *chazar bo Rabbi Tarfon* — Rabbi Tarfon reversed his position. His final ruling is *yachloku*, and the *reisha* represents an earlier view he subsequently abandoned. Rabbi Tarfon's operative position is thus that the two parties split what remains.

### Burden Proof

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof rests on the one seeking to extract from another's possession. The *kohen* cannot prove that the one that died was the *Yisrael*'s, so the *kohen* is told: prove it, and if you cannot, receive the dead one.

### Two Analogies

The Gemara introduces two analogies to map the logical terrain. Rabbi Tarfon's position resembles the case of two people who each deposited an animal with a shepherd, one of the animals died, and now neither owner knows which of their animals survived. In that case, the shepherd holds both animals as a neutral third party. He can only say: here is the surviving animal, sort it out yourselves. Since neither claimant is in possession, there is no *hamotzi mechavero* — no one is extracting from the other's *reshut* — and they split.

Rabbi Akiva's position resembles the case of a single person who deposited an animal with a *ba'al habayit*, it became mixed with the *ba'al habayit*'s animals, and one died. There, the *ba'al habayit* is in possession of the surviving animal, and the depositor is trying to extract from that possession. The depositor bears the burden of proof, and if he cannot establish which survived, he must accept the dead one.

The difficulty is that the actual case in our *mishnah* does not map neatly onto either analogy. The animals were born in the *Yisrael*'s possession and remain there — this looks like the *ba'al habayit* scenario, in which case everyone should agree on *hamotzi mechavero*. What, then, is Rabbi Tarfon's basis for *yachloku*?

### Kohen Shepherd

Rava, and some say Rav Pappa, answer that everyone agrees on both basic models. In the pure shepherd case — neutral third party, no one has superior possession — everyone agrees on *yachloku*. In the pure *ba'al habayit* case — one party holds, the other seeks to extract — everyone agrees on *hamotzi mechavero*. The dispute arises only in a specific intermediate scenario: *bechatzer ba'al habayit ve'ro'eh kohen* — the animals are in the *Yisrael*'s yard, but the *kohen* is serving as the shepherd.

### Tarfon Theory

Rabbi Tarfon holds *aknoiye kamachni lei bechatzero* — by appointing this *kohen* as his shepherd, the *Yisrael* implicitly grants the *kohen* a degree of possession in the yard sufficient to acquire the *bechor* at the moment of birth. The reasoning: since it is understood that the *kohen*-shepherd will receive any *bechor* that is born, it is practically necessary and implicitly intended that the *kohen* be able to acquire it immediately even if he is not physically present. Thus the *Yisrael* effectively grants the *kohen* a partial right in the field for this purpose — *venicha lei deliavid mitzvah bemamonei* — the *Yisrael* wishes the *kohen* to perform a *mitzvah* through his property. Since both parties effectively have a stake in the animals, it resembles the shepherd case where neither has exclusive possession, and they must split.

### Akiva Counter

Rabbi Akiva counters: *kivan de'is lei pseida* — precisely because the *Yisrael* knows that in cases of doubt he may lose the benefit of the doubt, he would never implicitly grant the *kohen* rights to the field. He may intend to give the *kohen* a *bechor* when one is born, but he does not grant the *kohen* acquisition rights in the yard. Since the *kohen* has no formal *kinyan* in the field, all the animals remain in the *Yisrael*'s possession, and the *kohen* who comes to claim one is the *hamotzi* — the one extracting from another's domain — and bears the burden of proof.

## Animal Variations

### Gender Mix

The *mishnah* now repeats and elaborates the same *halachic* principles across a series of variations, with the underlying rules remaining constant throughout. If two sheep that have never previously given birth each produce a male, *shneihem lakohen* — the *kohen* receives both. If one produces a male and one a female, *hazachar lakohen* — the male goes to the *kohen*, straightforwardly.

### Multiple Births

If two sheep together produce two males and a female, one *bechor* certainly belongs to the *kohen* — since at least one of the animals bore a male first — but it is unknown which of the two males is the *bechor*. The positions of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva apply as before. If the births produce two females and a male, or two males and two females, it is possible in each scenario that the female emerged first from each of the two animals, meaning the *kohen* might receive nothing at all. Since the *kohen* cannot establish his claim, *ein kan lakohen klum* — the *kohen* gets nothing.

### Prior Birth

Another scenario: one animal has previously given birth — meaning it has already had a *bechor* — and one has not. Together they produce two males. Since only the animal that has not yet had a *bechor* can produce a *bechor* now, one male certainly belongs to the *kohen* — but which one is unknown. Rabbi Tarfon says the *kohen* picks the nicer one; Rabbi Akiva says *meshamnim beineihem*, read as the *kohen* taking the lesser one. The *mishnah* obligates the retained animal in *matanot*; Rabbi Yosei exempts, for the reasons already analyzed — and it is here that the *mishnah* makes explicit what was earlier implicit, with Rabbi Yosei stating: *kol shechalifin beyad kohen patur min hamatanot*, wherever the *kohen* takes something in exchange, one is exempt. Rabbi Meir obligates. The same positions on the death of one animal follow: Rabbi Tarfon says *yachloku*, Rabbi Akiva says *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*.

If the births yield a male and a female and one animal had previously given birth, the *kohen* gets nothing — since it is possible the male came from the animal that had already had a *bechor*, and the female from the one that had not.

### Repetition Reason

The Gemara asks the natural question: given that all these cases ultimately turn on the same principles, why does the *mishnah* bother to enumerate them all? Is it not simply a repetition of the same *halacha* applied to slightly different facts?

### Strength Proof

The Gemara answers: *tzericha* — each case is necessary. Without the full enumeration, one might think that in some configurations the physical qualities of the animals — their size, health, or vigor — could serve as evidence of birth order and thus resolve the doubt. For instance, in the case where one animal produced twins and the other a single birth, one might think the stronger male, presumably the single birth, could be identified as the *bechor* with enough confidence to award it to the *kohen* definitively. Rashi notes that this argument has some appeal: a single birth is typically more robust than a twin, and if one could identify the single-birth male as the healthier one, one might accept this as proof of its *bechor* status. Similarly, in the case where one animal had previously given birth and one had not, one might reason that the animal never before having given birth produces a healthier offspring, and therefore the healthier male is more likely to be the *bechor*.

Each scenario in the *mishnah* is therefore required to teach that Rabbi Akiva — and the *halacha* as a whole — does not employ such reasoning. No matter how suggestive the physical evidence, strength and healthiness cannot serve as probative evidence of birth order. The *tzericha* across all the scenarios collectively establishes this principle, ruling out in each configuration the argument that might have seemed most natural for using physical appearance to resolve the doubt.
