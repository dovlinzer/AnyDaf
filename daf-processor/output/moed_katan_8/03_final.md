# Moed Katan 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lesion Inspection Debate

### Kohen Deferral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to analyze the aforementioned *baraita*. **The Master said** that **as** the verse states: "But on the day it appears in him," it may be inferred that **there is a day when you examine** the symptoms found in **him and there is a day when you do not examine** those symptoms.

Daf 8 opens approximately seven lines from the top with the phrase *amar mar*, resuming a discussion about the circumstances under which a *kohen* may defer examining a person's *nega* — a spot that may constitute *tzara'at*. As we have already learned, a *kohen* may refuse to look at a *nega* during a person's wedding week, during the *regel*, and — remarkably — even for a *devar reshut*, something that is not even a *mitzvah*. The deferral may be to a more convenient time.

### Amar Mar vs Gufa

The phrase *amar mar* signals a return to a *braita* quoted earlier in order to analyze its law more deeply. This is worth distinguishing from the phrase *gufa*, which similarly looks back at a previously cited passage but does so in order to present the full body of the original source — that is, when an excerpt had been quoted, *gufa* restores the surrounding context. *Amar mar*, by contrast, takes the specific quoted line and goes deeper into its legal analysis. Both signal a return to a *tannaic* text, but they do so with different exegetical purposes.

### Abaye's Vav Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן לִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּיוֹם״, מַאי ״וּבְיוֹם״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** in this verse **may it be inferred? Abaye said: If** it were **so** that the priest must always examine the symptoms immediately, **the Merciful One should have written** simply: **On the day** it appears in him. **What is** implied by the actual formulation of the verse: **"But on the day"? Learn from here,** i.e., from the seemingly superfluous word "but" at the beginning of the phrase, that this is not an absolute *halakha*, but a conditional one, depending upon other factors: **There is a day when you examine** the leprous symptoms found in **him and there is a day when you do not examine** those symptoms.

The *braita* states that there are days on which one sees the *nega* and days on which one does not, derived from the phrase *uveyom hera'ot bo* — "and on the day that it appears to him." The *Gemara* asks: *mai mashma* — where exactly does one derive this selectivity from the verse? After all, the verse simply states that on the day you see it, you declare the person *tamei*. Where is the latitude to choose which days you will or will not look?

*Abaye* answers: if the Torah only meant to indicate timing, it would have written *beyom hera'ot bo* — "on the day." The fact that it writes *uveyom*, with the conjunctive *vav*, introduces a nuance — a kind of pause, a moment of deliberation — suggesting that there is some element of decision involved in selecting which day one will perform the examination. From this extra *vav*, *Abaye* derives that there are indeed days one sees and days one does not.

### Rava's Beyom Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ קְרָא יַתִּירָא הוּא, דְּאִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״וּבְהֵרָאוֹת״, מַאי ״וּבְיוֹם״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** offered a different explanation and **said: The entire phrase** "but on the day," **is superfluous. As, if** it were **so** that the priest must always examine the symptoms immediately, **the Merciful One should have written** simply: **And when it appears. What is** the meaning of the additional words **"but on the day"? Learn from here** that the issue is dependent upon other factors, as **there is a day when you examine** the symptoms of leprosy **in him and there is a day when you do not examine** them.

*Rava* takes a different approach. He argues that it is not merely the *vav* that is superfluous but the entire word *beyom* — "on the day." Had the Torah wished only to convey the act of seeing and its consequences, it would have written simply *uvehera'ot bo*, "when it appears to him," without specifying "on the day" at all. The full word *beyom* is therefore the exegetical anchor, and from its redundancy *Rava* likewise derives that there are days designated for examination and days that are not.

### Day & Vision Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ — בַּיּוֹם וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה. וְרָבָא, בַּיּוֹם וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״לְּכׇל מַרְאֵה עֵינֵי הַכֹּהֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** according to **Abaye,** who derives the *halakha* from the word "but," why does the verse include the phrase "on the day"? **That** phrase **is necessary** in order to teach that the priest examines the symptoms of leprosy only **during the day, but not at night. And** from where does **Rava** derive the *halakha* that the examination is performed **during the day but not at night?** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: **"As far as the priest's eyes can see"** (Leviticus 13:12), which indicates that the priest must be able to carry out a careful examination of the leprous symptoms; this cannot be done at night, when his vision is limited.

This divergence leads to a further question: if *Abaye* uses the extra *vav* to derive the selectivity of days, he still needs the word *beyom* itself for something. And indeed he does — *beyom velo balailah*, the examination may only be performed during the day, not at night. *Rava*, having used the entire word *beyom* to derive the discretionary principle, must find the daytime requirement elsewhere. The *Gemara* explains that *Rava* derives it from the phrase *kol marei einei hakohen* — "the full visual capacity of the *kohen*'s eyes" — which implies a condition of complete natural visibility, i.e., daytime, when one can see without artificial assistance.

### Vision Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו. וְרָבָא נָמֵי מִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְהָכִי? אִין, הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** could say that **this** verse **is necessary to exclude** a priest who is **blind in one eye** from inspecting symptoms of leprosy, as it says "eyes," in the plural. The Gemara asks: **But Rava also requires** this verse **to** teach **this** *halakha*, i.e., that a priest who is blind in one eye is unfit to examine symptoms of leprosy. The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so;** he agrees that this verse is the source of the *halakha* concerning a priest who is blind in one eye, and it is not the source of the *halakha* that the priest may only view leprous symptoms by day.

*Abaye*, in turn, uses that same phrase — *kol marei einav* — for a different ruling: to exclude a *kohen* who is blind in one eye (*suma b'achat mei'einav*) from performing the examination, since the verse requires the full visual capacity of both eyes. The *Gemara* notes that *Rava* presumably does not disagree with this ruling — *ein hachi nami*, indeed so — he too would derive it from *kol marei einav*. This creates the difficulty: if *Rava* also needs *kol marei einav* for the one-eyed *kohen* ruling, where does he derive the daytime-only requirement, since he has used *beyom* for the discretionary days? The *Gemara* therefore explains that *Rava* has yet another source.

### Natural Light Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם וְלֹא בַּלַּיְלָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״כְּנֶגַע נִרְאָה לִי בַּבָּיִת״. ״לִי״ — וְלֹא לְאוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **from where** does Rava derive the *halakha* that a priest examines leprous symptoms **during the day but not at night?** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse that quotes a homeowner saying to a priest: **"There seems to me as it were a plague in the house"** (Leviticus 14:35), which emphasizes that the leprous symptom is seen **by me, and not by** way of **my light,** i.e., that this takes place by day. When the next verse states that the priest comes to view the leprous symptom, it means that this also takes place by day.

That source is the phrase spoken by the homeowner to the *kohen*: *nega nira li babayit* — "a spot appeared to me in the house." From the word *li* — "to me" — the *Gemara* derives *li velo le'ori*, "to me and not by my [artificial] light." The appearance of the spot must be visible naturally, without the aid of torchlight or candles. This is what the *non-expert* householder declares, and one presumes that if even the layman reporting the spot must perceive it under natural light, certainly the *kohen* performing the formal examination must do so as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, אִי מֵהָתָם הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי טוּמְאָה דְּלָאו דְגוּפֵיהּ, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה דְגוּפֵיהּ אֲפִילּוּ לְאוֹרוֹ נָמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** why does **Abaye** require the proof that he adduces when it would seem that the verse that Rava brings offers sufficient proof? The Gemara explains: According to Abaye, **if** the source was derived **from there, I would have said** that **this** *halakha*, that one must wait until the light of day to examine the symptom, **applies** only to leprous symptoms on a house, which is **an impurity that is not** related to **the** individual's **body. However,** in a case of **impurity of the** individual's **body,** perhaps the priest may conduct his examination **even by** artificial **light,** as it is a more severe impurity. Therefore the verse **teaches us:** "But on the day," to indicate that even in the case of impurity of the body the priest may perform the examination only during the day by natural sunlight, and not by any artificial light.

One might then ask: is this not simply the plain reading of the verse — that the house must be examined by the natural light entering from outside during the day? *Abaye* anticipates this and responds: had we only this verse from the laws of *nega'im habayit* — lesions of the house — we might have thought the natural-light requirement applies specifically to *tuma* that is not *tuma degufei*, impurity not on one's own body. For *tuma* on the body itself, one might reason that since it is a weightier form of impurity, perhaps we are more prepared to declare it *tamei* even by candlelight — or alternatively, since it is more serious, perhaps we are more expansive in the means by which we arrive at a declaration of *tuma*. Therefore the teaching *li velo le'ori* comes to establish that the same natural-light requirement applies equally to bodily *nega'im*. *Kama'shma lan* — it teaches us that there is no difference.

### Amoraim Peshat Role

There is something striking about the entire discussion. The *Gemara* here is presenting *Abaye* and *Rava* engaged in direct biblical exegesis — analyzing which letter or word of a *pasuk* is superfluous and what each derives from the redundancy. Yet *Abaye* and *Rava* are among the latest of the *Amoraim*, and the standard assumption is that *Amoraim* interpret *Tannaim* but do not themselves engage in *drasha* of *pesukim* on their own authority. Even earlier in this passage, *Abaye* and *Rava* had been explaining the disagreement between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Tanna Kamma* regarding their respective readings of the *pesukim* — and then quite suddenly the discussion became *Abaye* and *Rava*'s own independent readings of those same verses.

This is a noteworthy shift and should not be taken entirely for granted. The *Gemara* is in some sense reconstructing or attributing these exegetical positions to *Abaye* and *Rava*, and the question of how much of this represents their own original formulations is genuinely open. Compounding this is a linguistic observation: even the initial statements attributed to *Abaye* and *Rava* in this passage — *amar Abaye im ken lichtov rachmana beyom mai uveyom*, *Rava amar kulla kra yeseira hi* — are entirely in Aramaic. This is itself unusual. When *Amoraim* issue statements in their own names, those statements are characteristically formulated in Hebrew, with Aramaic serving as the connective tissue — the narrative glue of the *Gemara* that weaves together the named rulings. The Aramaic of the *Gemara* is the *stam*; the named *Amoraim* speak Hebrew. When there is no Hebrew kernel at all — when even the attributed statement is in Aramaic — it raises the question of how much of this discussion is a *Gemara*-level reconstruction of what *Abaye* and *Rava* may have said, or even an imaginative elaboration of the debate they had. The elaboration — this letter versus that word, this derivation versus that one — is clearly the work of the *Gemara*. But it is nonetheless interesting to observe that this form of direct engagement with *pesukim*, which we might assume to be the province of *Tannaim*, is applied here to figures as late as *Abaye* and *Rava*.

## Festival Activities

### Bone Gathering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מְלַקֵּט אָדָם עַצְמוֹת אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׂמְחָה הִיא לוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵבֶל הוּא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Meir also stated** another leniency concerning the *halakhot* of the intermediate days of a Festival: **A person may gather the bones of his father and mother** from their temporary graves on the intermediate days of a Festival. In ancient times, it was customary to first bury a corpse in a temporary grave. After the flesh had decomposed, the bones would be collected, placed in a coffin, and buried in a vault together with the bones of the deceased individual's ancestors. This is permitted on the intermediate days of a Festival **because** the fact that one merited to bring the bones of his deceased parents to the graves of their ancestors **is** a source of **joy for him. Rabbi Yosei says:** One does not gather these bones on the intermediate days of a Festival, because **it is** a source of **mourning for him.** Even though he is happy to be able to bury his parents' bones in their ancestral graves, he is still pained by the memory of their death.

The *Mishnah* continues with another statement of *Rabbi Meir*: *melaket adam atzmos aviv ve'imo mipnei she-simcha hi lo* — a person may gather the bones of his father and mother during the *regel* because for him it is a *simcha*. *Rabbi Yose* disagrees: *avel hu lo* — it is mourning for him. The debate parallels the earlier one between these two *Tannaim*, with *Rabbi Meir* granting more latitude and *Rabbi Yose* adopting the stricter position. It is difficult to identify a clear logical link between the two debates, but they are structurally parallel.

### Ossuary Practice

The practice being described here is *likut atzamos* — the gathering of bones for reburial. In the land of Israel during the period of the *Tannaim*, burial was carried out in two stages. The body would first be interred until the flesh decomposed, after which the bones would be gathered and placed in a compact repository — a *kuch* in a family cave, a small niche in the wall of a burial chamber. This practice served multiple practical purposes: it conserved scarce land, and according to one view among the *Acharonim*, it also prevented the spread of *tumah* throughout *Eretz Yisrael*. By consolidating the remains of a family in a centralized location, the bones occupied far less space than a full grave.

This practice also illuminates certain laws of *tumas meis*. The *Gemara* elsewhere discusses *melo taryad rekev* — a certain quantity of decomposed bodily residue that conveys impurity. The context in which one would encounter such material is precisely this: opening a grave to remove the bones, one might also retrieve some decomposed matter still present in the burial site.

It has also been suggested that this is the rabbinic understanding of the biblical phrase *vayigva vayamos vayeisef el amav* — "he expired and died and was gathered to his people." The phrase *vayeisef el amav*, to be gathered to one's people, refers not merely to death but specifically to this act of *likut atzamos* — the gathering of one's remains into the family burial repository.

### Daytime Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמְלַקֵּט עַצְמוֹת אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה מִתְאַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶם כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְלָעֶרֶב אֵין מִתְאַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶן. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲפִילּוּ צְרוּרִין לוֹ בִּסְדִינוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to Rabbi Meir's opinion that gathering the bones of one's parents is considered an occasion of joy, based on what is taught in a *baraita*: **One who gathers the bones of his father or his mother mourns for them the entire day, but he does not mourn for them in the evening. And Rav Ḥisda said: And even** in a case where he does not gather the bones from a temporary grave, but where **he had them bundled up in his sheet,** when he buries them he mourns for them all day. This indicates that gathering one's parents' bones is an occasion of mourning rather than joy.

The *Gemara* raises a contradiction from a *braita*: one who gathers the bones of his father and mother mourns for them the entire day — *misabel aleihem kol hayom* — but does not mourn for them at night. This clearly implies that the act of *likut atzamos* carries an element of *aveilus*. How then does *Rabbi Meir* say it is a *simcha*?

*Rav Chisda* adds a further detail: the mourning of that day applies even if the bones are still wrapped in a cloth and have not yet been reinterred — *afilu tzrurin lo be-sadino*. *Tosafos* reads this as a leniency rather than a stringency: even if the bones remain bundled and awaiting burial, the mourning does not extend into the evening. The operative rule is that the *aveilus* is limited to the daytime of the gathering alone.

### Joy Overrides Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׂמְחַת הָרֶגֶל עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Say** that Rabbi Meir means that collecting and reinterring the bones of one's parents is permitted on the intermediate days of a Festival, not because it causes one joy, but rather **because the joy of the pilgrimage Festival is upon him.** The pain of gathering the bones of his parents is overpowered by the joy of the Festival.

*Abaye* offers a reinterpretation: *eima mipnei she-simchas ha-regel alav* — what *Rabbi Meir* meant was not that the act of bone-gathering is itself a *simcha*, but that because the joy of the *regel* overshadows everything else, the emotion of mourning that would otherwise attend the gathering does not take hold. The *aveilus* exists in principle, but the dominant experience of *simchas ha-regel* effectively suppresses it.

### Logical Tension

This reinterpretation is quite difficult. In the *Mishnah*, *Rabbi Meir*'s statement that it is a *simcha* is directly contrasted with *Rabbi Yose*'s statement that it is *avel* — mourning. If *Abaye* says that *Rabbi Meir* actually concedes there is an element of *aveilus*, he is substantially rewriting what the *Mishnah* says. But more fundamentally: if *Rabbi Meir* himself agrees that the person is *noheig minhagei aveilus* — that the behavioral norms of mourning apply during the day of the gathering — how can he then permit this on the *regel*? One's inner emotional state is one thing, but if he is formally observing the practices of mourning, allowing that on the *regel* seems very hard to justify under any emotional-override theory.

*Tosafos* notes, in fact, that the *braita* about mourning during *likut atzamos* — "he mourns for them the entire day" — is drawn from *Masechet Smachos*, a collection of *tannaic* material on *aveilus*, and that this *braita* reflects the position of *Rabbi Yose*, who holds it is *avel*. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees entirely: for him it is a *simcha* with no *aveilus* at all. Under this reading, there is no contradiction requiring *Abaye*'s resolution, since the two sources represent opposing *tannaic* views. Why the *Gemara* finds the contradiction so pressing and why it resolves it in such a strained manner is not entirely clear.

### Hesped Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יְעוֹרֵר אָדָם עַל מֵתוֹ, וְלֹא יַסְפִּידֶנּוּ קוֹדֶם לָרֶגֶל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** And all agree that **a person may not arouse [*ye'orer*]** lamentation **for his deceased** relative, **and he may not eulogize him** during the **thirty days before a pilgrimage Festival.**

The *Mishnah* adds: *lo yispod adam al meiso* — a person should not bestir himself in mourning over a relative who has died — *ve-lo yispedenu kodem l'regel shloshim yom* — and one should not eulogize him within thirty days before the *regel*. The word *hesped*, while often translated as "eulogy," more precisely denotes a form of public wailing and emotional stirring — closer to lamentation than to the formal addresses we associate with funerals today. *Le-haspid* carries connotations of beating one's chest and arousing communal grief. The *Mishnah* here is speaking of a highly public, emotionally charged phenomenon.

The phrase *lo ye'arer al meiso* — one should not "stir up" mourning over the deceased — is understood by the *Ritva* as referring to a somewhat different scenario: whereas *lo yispedenu* addresses a *hesped* held soon after the death, *lo ye'arer* refers to a case where the person died some time earlier and one now initiates a fresh round of public mourning — perhaps for the occasion of erecting a *matzeiva* or some other commemorative event. In both cases, the restriction applies for thirty days before the *regel*.

### Wailers Anecdote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁכִּינֵּס מָעוֹת לַעֲלוֹת לָרֶגֶל, וּבָא סַפְדָן וְעָמַד עַל פֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ, וּנְטָלָתַן אִשְׁתּוֹ וּנְתָנָתַן לוֹ, וְנִמְנַע וְלֹא עָלָה. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: לֹא יְעוֹרֵר עַל מֵתוֹ וְלֹא יַסְפִּידֶנּוּ קוֹדֶם לָרֶגֶל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said that Rav Yehuda said that Rav said:** There was an **incident** involving **a certain man who saved up money to ascend** to Jerusalem **for the pilgrimage Festival**. A professional **eulogizer came and stood at the opening to his house and** the man's **wife took** the money that he had saved **and gave it to** the eulogizer for his services. As a result, the man did not have enough money and **he refrained and did not ascend** to Jerusalem for the Festival. **At that time, they said: One may not arouse** lamentation **for his deceased** relative, **nor may** he **eulogize him** during the **thirty days before a pilgrimage Festival.**

The *Gemara* provides a historical illustration through *Rav Kahana* citing *Rav Yehuda* citing *Rav*: there was once a man who had gathered money to go up to Jerusalem for the *regel*. A professional wailer came and stood at the opening of his home, and the man's wife became so emotionally overwrought by the wailing and lamentation that she gave him the money. The man was left unable to make the pilgrimage. It was at that time, the *Gemara* says, that the rabbis enacted: one may not stir up mourning or eulogize within thirty days of the *regel*.

This episode captures the social dynamics of the professional wailing industry. These practitioners would circulate through the community, invoking the memory of the deceased in vivid and moving terms, drawing people to join in the grief. The communal pressure to demonstrate honor for the dead — often expressed by contributing money to the wailers — was real and substantial. In the period when pilgrimage to Jerusalem was still practiced, this created a direct financial conflict: the same funds needed for *aliyah la-regel* were being diverted by a socially coercive mourning culture. Even for those who were not themselves going up to Jerusalem, such emotional and financial pressure in the weeks before the *regel* ran contrary to the spirit of *simchat ha-regel*.

### Emotional Impact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַמֵּת מִשְׁתַּכֵּחַ מִן הַלֵּב שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּקָעָבֵיד בְּחִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** It is **because the dead is not forgotten from the heart** and put out of mind **for thirty days,** and therefore, when one laments the loss of a deceased relative within thirty days before a Festival, the pain is still remembered on the Festival. The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the two reasons? The practical difference **between them** is in a case **where** the eulogizer **performs** the eulogy **free** of charge. In that case there is no worry that money that had been set aside for the Festival will be spent, but there is still room for concern about the lingering pain that will be felt on the Festival.

*Shmuel* offers a different and more direct rationale: *she-ein ha-mes mishtakach min ha-lev shloshim yom* — the dead are not forgotten from the heart for thirty days. If a person undergoes an intense mourning experience, the emotional weight of it persists for up to thirty days. Even if the death occurred long before the *regel*, a major public *hesped* held thirty days before will leave the person still emotionally burdened at the time the *regel* arrives. That residual grief is incompatible with *simchat ha-regel*.

### Hesped Timing

The practical difference between these two rationales — the financial pressure concern versus the emotional persistence concern — is identified by the *Gemara*: *ika beinaihu de-avid bechinam*, where the wailers are performing for free. If no money is being exchanged, the financial drain on *oleh regel* funds is not at issue, but *Shmuel*'s concern still applies: the emotional experience of a major *hesped* will linger for thirty days regardless of whether anyone was paid.

This discussion also raises a question of broader contemporary application. The prohibition on *hespedim* thirty days before the *regel* — and analogous prohibitions during the month of *Nissan* or on *Chol Hamoed* itself — were formulated in a world where *hesped* meant something quite different from what a contemporary funeral address looks like. The ancient *hesped* was designed to generate mass grief: the wailing, the circulating through the community, the collective emotional stimulation. Contemporary eulogies, which tend toward affirmation and memory rather than raw lamentation, are a fundamentally different experience. This raises genuine halachic questions about whether all the parameters derived for *hesped* in the *Gemara* apply equally to what we do today — a question the *Gemara* itself, of course, does not address.

## Burial Restrictions

### Niches & Graves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין חוֹפְרִין כּוּכִין וּקְבָרוֹת בַּמּוֹעֵד, אֲבָל מְחַנְּכִין אֶת הַכּוּכִין בַּמּוֹעֵד. וְעוֹשִׂין נִבְרֶכֶת בַּמּוֹעֵד, וְאָרוֹן עִם הַמֵּת בֶּחָצֵר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹסֵר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ עִמּוֹ נְסָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not dig crypts or graves on** the intermediate days of **a Festival** in preparation for those who are still alive, **but one may adjust** previously dug **crypts on** the intermediate days of **a Festival** in order to receive a particular corpse. **And one may** also **construct** a laundry **pond on** the intermediate days of **a Festival** as it does not involve excessive effort. **And** one may also prepare **a coffin** if he is together **with the corpse in the** same **courtyard,** as in that case everyone knows that he is preparing the coffin for the deceased. **Rabbi Yehuda prohibits** making the coffin **unless** one **has** wooden **boards with him** that had already been cut before the Festival and that need only to be attached.

The *Mishnah* now addresses the practical management of burial on *Chol Hamoed*. One may not dig *kuchin* — burial niches — or *kvarot* — graves — on *Chol Hamoed*. These are acts of preparation for future burials, done in advance. However, one may *mechanech* existing niches — that is, one may arrange, repair, or otherwise work with niches that have already been dug. The restriction is on fresh digging, not on maintaining existing structures.

### Pool & Coffin

The *Mishnah* further states that one may construct a pool of water on *Chol Hamoed* — a *nivrechet* — and that one may build a coffin when the body is present in the courtyard. *Rabbeinu Gershom* understands the pool as being for laundry, though *Tosafos* objects that laundering itself is generally problematic on *Chol Hamoed* except in limited circumstances. *Tosafos* therefore prefers to read the pool in context: it is for the *tahara*, the ritual washing of the deceased, and this reading coheres better with the surrounding laws, which all concern the treatment of a dead body. Building a coffin is permitted, but only when the body is present in the courtyard — a contextual requirement whose significance will be explained shortly.

### Wood Restrictions

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds a further restriction: one may not plane fresh planks of wood. Making planks from raw timber involves too much labor *yesh meichlal*; one must use pre-prepared planks. The *tanna kamma* in the *Mishnah* stipulates that a coffin may be built from planks that were prepared before *Yom Tov* — *m'nesarim ha-menusarim me'erev Yom Tov*. This limits the permissible activity to assembly rather than production.

One might ask: what if there are no pre-made planks and no coffin, and the person has died on *Chol Hamoed*? Is one simply to leave the body unburied for the duration of the festival? Several *Rishonim* address this by reading the prohibition on digging niches and graves as applying only to preparation done in advance — that is, preparing burial sites before anyone has actually died. If a person has in fact died and there is no alternative, of course one digs the grave and builds what is needed. The *Gemara* itself does not explicitly address the case of genuine necessity with no alternatives, but the implication of the *Rishonim* is that such restrictions could not have been intended to prevent burial when there is no other option.

### Arranging Niches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי כּוּכִין וּמַאי קְבָרוֹת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כּוּכִין בַּחֲפִירָה, וּקְבָרוֹת בְּבִנְיָן. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵלּוּ הֵן כּוּכִין וְאֵלּוּ הֵן קְבָרוֹת, כּוּכִין בַּחֲפִירָה, וּקְבָרוֹת בְּבִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What are crypts and what are graves,** and what is the difference between them? **Rav Yehuda said: Crypts** are constructed **by digging and graves** are constructed **by building.** The Gemara adds: **That is also taught** in the following *baraita*: **These are crypts and these are graves: Crypts** are constructed **by digging and graves** are constructed **by building.**

The *Gemara* clarifies the distinction between *kuchin* and *kvarot*. *Rav Yehuda* states: *kuchin b'chafira u'kvaros b'vinyan* — *kuchin* are formed by digging and *kvarot* are formed by construction. This is somewhat counterintuitive: one might have assumed *kuchin* — the niches in a burial cave — to be built structures, and *kvarot* — graves — to be dug. A *tannaic* source corroborates *Rav Yehuda*'s formulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מְחַנְּכִין אֶת הַכּוּכִין. כֵּיצַד מְחַנְּכִין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שֶׁאִם הָיָה אָרוֹךְ — מְקַצְּרוֹ. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: מַאֲרִיךְ בּוֹ, וּמַרְחִיב בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna: **But one may adjust** previously dug **crypts** on the intermediate days of a Festival. The Gemara asks: **How does one adjust** a crypt? **Rav Yehuda said: If** the crypt **was long**er than the corpse, **he shortens it.** With regard to this issue it **was taught in a *baraita*: He lengthens** the crypt **and widens it** to make it the right size for the particular corpse.

As for the permissible act of *mechanech* — arranging the niches — *Rav Yehuda* explains: if a niche was too long, one may shorten it. A *masnisa* teaches the converse: one may lengthen or widen an existing niche. The principle is consistent with what we find in other contexts: one may modify what already exists but may not create anew. Regarding the *nivrechet*, *Rav Yehuda* identifies it as a *bika* — a pool — though a *braita* indicates that a *nivrechet* and a *bika* are two distinct things. *Abaye*, and some say *Rav Kahana*, reconciles this by explaining that there is a primary pool and a secondary runoff pool adjacent to it, both serving related functions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשִׂין נִבְרֶכֶת כּוּ׳. מַאי נִבְרֶכֶת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: זוֹ בָּקִיעַ. וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנִּבְרֶכֶת וְהַבָּקִיעַ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב כָּהֲנָא: גִּיהָא וּבַר גִּיהָא.
>
> **Translation:** It was further taught in the mishna: **And one may** also **construct a** laundry **pond** on the intermediate days of a Festival. The Gemara asks: **What is a** laundry **pond? Rav Yehuda said: This is a ditch** dug in the ground for washing clothes. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A** laundry **pond and a** laundry **ditch,** which indicates that they are different from one another? **Abaye said, and some say** that it was **Rav Kahana** who said: **A pond and a small pond,** i.e., the only difference between them is their size; a laundry pond is larger than a laundry ditch.

### Context Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָרוֹן עִם הַמֵּת בֶּחָצֵר. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: עוֹשִׂין כׇּל צוֹרְכֵי הַמֵּת. גּוֹזְזִין לוֹ שְׂעָרוֹ, וּמְכַבְּסִין לוֹ כְּסוּתוֹ, וְעוֹשִׂין לוֹ אָרוֹן מִנְּסָרִים הַמְנוּסָּרִים מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף מְבִיאִין עֵצִים וּמְנַסְּרָן בְּצִינְעָא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And** one may also prepare **a coffin** on the intermediate days of a Festival if he is together **with the corpse in the** same **courtyard.** The Gemara comments: **We** already **learned this, as the Sages taught** in the following *baraita*: **One may tend to all the needs of the deceased** on the intermediate days of a Festival: **One may cut** the deceased's **hair for him** if it was too long, **and one may wash his clothing,** i.e., his shrouds, **for him, and one may construct a coffin for him from boards that had** already **been cut from the eve of the Festival. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One may even bring timber and discreetly cut it** into boards **in** the privacy of **his house.**

The requirement that the coffin be built *im ha-mes be-chatzer* — with the body present in the courtyard — stems from a concern about public perception. If a craftsman is seen planing and hammering wood without any context, observers have no way of knowing what he is doing and may assume he is engaged in ordinary labor for profit. The presence of the body provides the necessary context that makes clear the work is being done for *tzorchei ha-mes* — the needs of the deceased.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* adds a further leniency: *af mevi'im etzim u'menasrim b'tzina b'toch beito* — one may even bring wood and plane planks privately, inside one's home. The *Gemara* implies that this dispensation applies even without the body being present, since the work is done *b'tzina* — in private — and therefore does not create any public impression. When work is done in public, the *mes* must be present to provide context. When done in private, the need for physical context is eliminated.

### Tosafos Leniencies

*Tosafos* draws an important inference from the passage in *Beitzah* 6a, where making new shrouds on *Chol Hamoed* is permitted. The reason given there is that obviously the person had no alternative — one does not typically prepare shrouds in advance. *Tosafos* therefore reasons: the restrictions of our *Mishnah* — not digging niches or graves — were only applicable because in the reality described, alternatives were available. People commonly prepared graves during their lifetimes, so there were usually other options. If one is restricted from digging a new niche, one can use a different one. But in a later reality where graves are not pre-dug and no alternatives exist, *Tosafos* argues that these restrictions would not apply, since the *Mishnah*'s entire premise was that alternatives were available.

*Tosafos* also invokes the *Yerushalmi*: *Rav Mana* states that the requirement to have the *mes* present in the *chatzer* applies only when the death is not publicly known. If the death is common knowledge throughout the community — *mes mefursam* — then the coffin may be built even in a public space, since everyone understands what is being done. The *Gemara* in the *Yerushalmi* illustrates this with the case of *Rabbi Chanina*, a colleague of the rabbis who died on *Chol Hamoed*, for whom a coffin was built in the marketplace — publicly — because his death was universally known and the context was self-evident. *Tosafos* concludes that in small Jewish communities, any death is immediately public knowledge, so the *mes mefursam* condition is nearly always met, making the requirement of physical presence in the *chatzer* largely moot.

### Yom Tov Hierarchy

*Tosafos* elsewhere raises a striking *hava amina* — a position it ultimately rejects but finds worth articulating. It notes that on the second day of *Yom Tov*, the *halacha* is *osin kol tzorchei ha-mes* — one may do anything necessary for the deceased, including weaving shrouds and other acts that go well beyond what is permitted on *Chol Hamoed*. Prima facie, this seems to give greater latitude on the second day of *Yom Tov* than on *Chol Hamoed* — which seems paradoxical. *Tosafos* entertains the possibility that this could be explained by the fact that *Chol Hamoed* is *d'Oraisa* — biblical — while the second day of *Yom Tov* is *d'Rabbanan*. Perhaps, then, leniencies for the dead are more expansive on the *rabbinically* established second day than on the biblically grounded *Chol Hamoed*.

This should register as a jolt. Throughout this tractate, we have been treating *Chol Hamoed* restrictions primarily as *rabbinic* in character — matters of *tircha* and communal atmosphere — and yet here *Tosafos* asserts that *Chol Hamoed* is *d'Oraisa*. The implication — that *Chol Hamoed* might in some ways be more stringent than the second day of *Yom Tov* — is remarkable. *Tosafos* himself rejects the reasoning on the grounds that *melacha* is always more prohibited on the second day of *Yom Tov* than on *Chol Hamoed*, and that the more expansive permissions for the dead in the *Yom Tov Sheni* context reflect the particular dispensation for *tzorchei ha-mes* rather than a categorical leniency of that day. The practical conclusion is that all such permissions are calibrated by the degree of necessity, and the restrictions of our *Mishnah* presuppose situations where alternatives exist.

## Marriage Prohibitions

### No Marriages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין נוֹשְׂאִין נָשִׁים בַּמּוֹעֵד, לֹא בְּתוּלוֹת וְלֹא אַלְמָנוֹת, וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׂמְחָה הִיא לוֹ. אֲבָל מַחְזִיר הוּא אֶת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not marry a woman on** the intermediate days of **a Festival, not virgins and not widows, and one may not perform levirate marriage** with his sister-in-law, if his brother died childless, **because it is** a **joyous** occasion **for him. However, one may remarry his divorced wife** on the intermediate days of a Festival, as this is not as great a joy for him.

The *Mishnah* states: *ein nosin nashim ba-moed* — one does not marry women on *Chol Hamoed*. This prohibition is perhaps initially surprising: we have seen that activities associated with *aveilus* are restricted during *Chol Hamoed*, but here we encounter the converse — activities of *simcha* are also restricted. Both extremes of the emotional register are curtailed, and the *Mishnah* applies this restriction broadly, encompassing virgins and widows alike.

### Kiddushin vs Chuppah

The word *nosin* refers specifically to *chuppah* — the actual bringing together of the couple in marriage, the beginning of shared life. This is to be distinguished from *kiddushin*, the formal betrothal with the ring, which contractually creates the marriage bond. *Kiddushin* alone does not trigger the full *simcha* of marriage; it is the *nisuin* — the *chuppah* — that does. Accordingly, *sheva brachos* are recited only at *nisuin*, not at *eirusin*, because *sheva brachos* express the joy of the *choson ve-kallah* coming together as a physical couple. The *seudas nisuin* is considered a *seudas mitzvah* in the full sense, while the *seudas eirusin* does not carry that character. It is therefore the *chuppah* — the *nisuin* event — that is prohibited on *Chol Hamoed*.

### Yibbum & Divorce

The prohibition extends to *yibbum* — levirate marriage — even though such a marriage is the product of obligation rather than courtship and desire. One might have thought that since the man is fulfilling a *mitzvah* imposed upon him by his brother's death rather than pursuing a freely chosen relationship, the *simcha* dimension is attenuated. The *Mishnah* rejects this: the marriage is still *simcha hi lo* — a joy for him — and is therefore prohibited. By contrast, a man is permitted to remarry a woman he previously divorced, since that reunion is not an occasion of unbounded new joy — it is, as one might say, a triumph of hope over experience, and does not carry the same weight of *simcha* as a fresh marriage.

### Women's Makeup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשָׂה אִשָּׁה תַּכְשִׁיטֶיהָ בַּמּוֹעֵד. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא תָּסוּד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּיוּוּל הוּא לָהּ. הַהֶדְיוֹט תּוֹפֵר כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וְהָאוּמָּן מַכְלִיב. וּמְסָרְגִין אֶת הַמִּטּוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְמַתְּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And a woman may engage in** all **her** usual **cosmetic treatments** to enhance her physical appearance **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival. Rabbi Yehuda says: She may not apply lime** to her skin **because it** is temporarily **a disgrace to her,** as she is unattractive before the lime is peeled off and will therefore be distressed during the Festival. The mishna continues: **A layman,** who is not a skilled tailor, **may sew in his** usual **manner** if necessary for the Festival, **whereas a craftsman may form** only **temporary stitches. And one may interweave** the cords attached to the frames of **beds** upon which a mattress is placed. **Rabbi Yosei says: One may** only **tighten** the cords but not interweave them.

The *Mishnah* permits a woman to attend to her personal adornment and toiletries on *Chol Hamoed* — *oseh isha tachshiteha be-moed*. *Rabbi Yehuda* adds a qualification: she may not undergo the facial plastering treatment (*lo sasud*) — a method of hair removal that, while producing a beautifying result, involves an unpleasant intermediate state. *Rabbi Yehuda* reasons that the process itself is a *nivvul* — disfiguring — and is therefore not appropriate to *Chol Hamoed*, even though the end result is beautification.

### Sewing Rules

The *Mishnah* distinguishes between a layperson and a craftsperson with respect to sewing on *Chol Hamoed*. A *hedyot* — an ordinary person — may sew in the normal manner, since his sewing does not rise to the level of professional craft labor. An *uman* — a craftsperson — must sew in an unusual manner. The *Gemara* will elaborate on the nature of this distinction.

### Bed Maintenance

The *Mishnah* concludes this section by permitting *mesargin et ha-miton* on *Chol Hamoed* — the weaving or stringing of beds. *Rabbi Yose* says one may *motihin* — stretch the ropes — which implies a more limited intervention. The *Gemara* will discuss these activities further. What is notable at this point in the *Mishnah* is the shift in the nature of the discussion: we are moving from cases of *davar ha-avad* or the special concerns of *aveilus* and *simcha*, to a more direct examination of what categories of labor are or are not inherently problematic on *Chol Hamoed* — a foundational question that has not yet been fully examined in this tractate.

## Simcha Reasons

### No Mixed Simchas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכִי שִׂמְחָה הִיא לוֹ, מַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְעָרְבִין שִׂמְחָה בְּשִׂמְחָה. רַבָּה בַּר [רַב] הוּנָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ שִׂמְחַת הָרֶגֶל וְעוֹסֵק בְּשִׂמְחַת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It was taught in the mishna that one may not marry a woman on the intermediate days of a Festival, because it is a source of joy for him. The Gemara asks: **And if it is a** source of **joy for him, what of it?** Isn't there is a mitzva to rejoice on a Festival? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said, and, so too, Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Oshaya said, and some say** that **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** The reason that one may not get married on the intermediate days of a Festival is **because one may not mix** one **joy with** another **joy,** as each requires its own celebration. **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** The reason is **because he forsakes the rejoicing of the pilgrimage Festival and occupies** himself **with rejoicing** with **his wife.**

Having established that marriages are prohibited on *Chol Hamoed*, the *Gemara* asks: *v'chi simcha hi lo mai hovei* — if a man has joy on *Chol Hamoed*, what of it? Why should his experiencing joy be a basis for prohibition? The *Gemara* offers multiple answers.

*Rav Yehuda* citing *Shmuel*, and similarly *Rabbi Elazar* citing *Rabbi Yoshia* (or according to some, *Rabbi Elazar* citing *Rabbi Chanina*): *lefi she-ein mearvin simcha b'simcha* — one does not mix one *simcha* with another. This is the celebrated principle familiar to many, though its precise meaning and scope deserve attention.

### Abandoning Festival

*Rabba bar bar Chana* formulates the concern differently: *mipnei she-maniach simchas ha-regel v'oseh b'simchas ishto* — because the person abandons the joy of the *regel* and instead engages himself in the joy of his wife. Here the concern is directional: it is specifically about the festival's *simcha* being sacrificed on the altar of personal marital joy. The *regel* is a communal, national *simcha*; the marriage is intensely personal. When the personal *simcha* draws one's focus and energy, it comes at the expense of the less personal but more obligatory *simchas ha-regel*.

The *Gemara* treats *Rabba bar bar Chana*'s formulation as closely related to *ein mearvin simcha b'simcha*, but there is a subtle difference. *Ein mearvin simcha b'simcha* is a more symmetric principle: it does not necessarily say you are neglecting one in favor of the other, but rather that you cannot do justice to both simultaneously. Even if you genuinely attempt to engage with both, neither receives its proper due. *Rabba bar bar Chana* speaks of consciously redirecting one's attention from the communal to the personal. *Tosafos* draws an analogy to the principle of not performing *mitzvos chavilos chavilos* — not discharging multiple *mitzvos* simultaneously — rooted in the requirement that one's heart and focus be fully given to each *mitzvah* in its own right. So too with *simcha*: it demands full engagement, and splitting one's attention between two simultaneous occasions of joy diminishes both.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא דְּרַבָּה בַּר [רַב] הוּנָא — דְּרַב הוּא. דְּאָמַר רַב דָּנִיאֵל בַּר קַטִּינָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין נוֹשְׂאִין נָשִׁים בַּמּוֹעֵד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׂמַחְתָּ בְּחַגֶּךָ״, ״בְּחַגֶּךָ״ — וְלֹא בְּאִשְׁתֶּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: This** statement **of Rabba bar Rav Huna is** actually a statement **of Rav, as Rav Daniel bar Ketina said that Rav said: From where** is it derived **that one may not marry a woman on** the intermediate days of **a Festival? As it is stated: "And you shall rejoice in your Festival"** (Deuteronomy 16:14). This verse emphasizes that you must rejoice **in your Festival and not in your wife.**

A biblical source is provided: *ve-samachta be-chagecha ve-lo be-ishtecha* — "you shall rejoice in your festival and not in your wife." The verse thus gives explicit textual grounding for the concern that marital joy competes with the festival, framing the two as alternatives rather than complements.

### Effort Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי הַטּוֹרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** The reason one may not marry on the intermediate days of a Festival is **due to the** excessive **exertion** that the wedding preparations demand, which is prohibited during the Festival.

*Ulla* offers an entirely different rationale: *mipnei ha-torach* — because of the effort. Weddings demand enormous preparation — catering, logistics, celebration — and this level of exertion is incompatible with the spirit of *Chol Hamoed*. The *simcha* element in this reading serves as the explanation for why the effort is so great: because it is so joyous an occasion, the investment of effort is correspondingly large. But the operative concern, *Ulla* argues, is really the *tircha*, not the *simcha* per se.

### Procreation Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי בִּיטּוּל פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa said:** The reason is **due to the neglect of** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply.** If it were permissible to get married during the intermediate days of a Festival, people would delay getting married until then in order to save money by avoiding the necessity of preparing separate feasts for the Festival and for the wedding. In the meantime they would neglect the mitzva of procreation.

*Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha* introduces yet a third rationale: *mipnei bittul piryah ve-rivyah* — because of the suspension of procreation. If a person knows he can marry on *Chol Hamoed*, he has an incentive to postpone a wedding he could have held months earlier in order to coincide it with the festival, when guests are already assembled and a large celebration is underway in any case. Delaying marriage means delaying the possibility of having children. The concern is not about the intrinsic competition between the *simchas* but about the perverse incentive structure that permission to marry on *Chol Hamoed* would create.

### Erev Regel Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֲסוּרִין לִישָּׂא בַּמּוֹעֵד — מוּתָּרִין לִישָּׂא עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל, קַשְׁיָא לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **All those whom** the Sages **said are prohibited to marry on** the intermediate days of **a Festival are permitted to marry on the eve of the pilgrimage Festival.** This poses **a difficulty to all of** the opinions, as a wedding celebration ordinarily extends for seven days, and the majority of the celebration will coincide with the Festival.

The *Gemara* raises an objection against all three rationales from a *braita*: *kol elu she-amru asur lisa be-moed, mutar lisa be-erev ha-regel* — all those who are prohibited from marrying on *Chol Hamoed* are permitted to marry on *erev ha-regel*, the day before the festival. This is problematic for each view. If the concern is competing *simchos*, the wedding joy will extend into the festival. If the concern is effort, the preparations will occupy the days of *Chol Hamoed*. If the concern is postponing procreation, a man who could have married six months earlier is still delaying — by one day is meaningless, but the logic of the concern should arguably still apply.

### Main Simcha Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם שִׂמְחָה — עִיקַּר שִׂמְחָה חַד יוֹמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as this *baraita* can be reconciled with all of the opinions. **According to the one who said** that one may not get married on the intermediate days of a Festival **because of joy,** i.e., because one must not mix one joy with another, or because one may not put aside the rejoicing of the Festival and occupy himself with rejoicing with his wife, this is not difficult, as **the primary joy** of a wedding **is** only **one day,** and after that, the joy of the wedding will not affect the joy of the Festival.

For the *simcha*-based rationale, the *Gemara* resolves: *ikar simcha chad yoma hu* — the primary, concentrated *simcha* of the wedding is the wedding day itself. If the wedding occurs on *erev ha-regel*, that day's joy stands on its own and does not genuinely compete with the festival's *simcha*, which begins the next day. Similarly, for *Ulla*'s rationale: *ikar tircha chad yoma hu* — the major exertion of preparation is concentrated on the first day. Once that day has passed, the ongoing celebration requires relatively little additional effort, and the *Chol Hamoed* days are not significantly burdened.

### One Day Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא — עִיקַּר טִירְחָא חַד יוֹמָא הוּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בִּיטּוּל פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, לְחַד יוֹמָא לָא מַשְׁהֵי אִינִישׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **according to the one who said** that one may not marry on the intermediate days of a Festival **due to** the excessive **exertion** that the wedding preparations demand, it is not difficult, as **the primary exertion is** only **one day.** After the wedding, excessive exertion is not required. And **according to the one who said** that the reason is **due to the neglect of** the mitzva to be **fruitful and multiply,** there is no room for concern: Since there is only **one day,** the eve of the Festival, when he can get married and save money on the feast, **a man will not delay** his wedding in order to get married then, lest some unforeseen circumstance arise that will prevent him from getting married on that day. Therefore, according to all the reasons offered, there is no need to prohibit weddings on the eve of a Festival.

For *Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha*'s rationale, the *Gemara* offers: *le-chad yoma lo mash-hei inish nafshih* — a person does not postpone his marriage for just one day. The incentive to delay that would be created by permission to marry on *Chol Hamoed* is real — it would lead people to delay by weeks or months. But if the only permissible window is *erev ha-regel*, no one would deliberately delay their marriage by several months just to shift it one day earlier to the eve of the festival. The structural incentive for delay disappears.

### Tosafos Specificity

*Tosafos* raises a significant point about the scope of *ein mearvin simcha b'simcha*. Looking at the passage, *Tosafos* notes that it is possible to read this principle not as a general halachic rule applicable across all contexts but as specific to the competition between the joy of marriage and the joy of the *regel*. The biblical verse *be-chagecha ve-lo be-ishtecha* is explicitly about this pairing. *Tosafos* identifies this as the position derivable from *Chagiga*, and cites the *Yerushalmi*'s application to the case of *Yaakov* and *Leah* — *malei shavua zos*, first complete the week of *Leah*'s marriage before beginning *Rachel*'s — as evidence that the principle applies to marriage specifically. This would be a striking limitation, given how widely the principle of *ein mearvin simcha b'simcha* is invoked.

### Simcha Threshold

*Tosafos* offers a second, less restrictive reading: the principle of *ein mearvin simcha b'simcha* is indeed general, but it applies only when the competing joy reaches a sufficient threshold. A *seudas bris milah*, for instance, does not reach that threshold, and therefore one may hold it on *Chol Hamoed*. *Tosafos* explains: *sheva brachos* — the blessing *she-ha-simcha bi-me'ono* — is not recited at a *bris milah* because the infant's pain (*tza'ar le-yanuka*) qualifies the joy, meaning it is not an unqualified *simcha*. An alternative reason offered is that since the *bris milah* is fixed to the eighth day and cannot be moved, the structure of competing incentives does not apply — the rabbis would not restrict it when it must occur on that specific day. A *seudas pidyon haben*, however, is more complex: its time is not rigidly fixed, so *Tosafos* expresses uncertainty (*tzarich iyun*) about whether it would be permitted on *Chol Hamoed*. The implication is that events which carry genuine and unqualified *simcha* of a wedding-like magnitude would reach the threshold, while those whose joy is qualified or whose timing is fixed for independent reasons fall below it.
