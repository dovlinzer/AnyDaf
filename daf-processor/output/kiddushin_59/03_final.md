# Kiddushin 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Agent Acts for Himse (II)

### Mishnah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״צֵא וְקַדֵּשׁ לִי אִשָּׁה פְּלוֹנִית״, וְהָלַךְ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ לְעַצְמוֹ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לַשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one** man **who says to another: Go and betroth so-and-so to me, and** the latter **went and betrothed her to himself, she is betrothed to the second** man.

The third *perek* opens with the following case: a man says to his friend, "Go and betroth a woman for me," and the friend goes and betroths her for himself. The *mishnah* rules that she is married — *mekudeshet* — to the second man, the one who acted as the emissary. The act works, whatever its ethical dimensions.

### Tosafot Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי, אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּהַג בּוֹ מִנְהַג רַמָּאוּת.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** concerning this issue: **What he did is done;** it is effective and the woman is betrothed to the second man, **but he has treated him,** i.e., the first man, **in a deceitful manner,** and it is prohibited to act in this fashion.

Tosafot immediately raises the obvious question: what is the *chiddush* here? Surely it is self-evident that if a man performs an act of *kiddushin* with his own money and in his own name, the marriage is valid. Tosafot answers by specifying the precise scenario the *mishnah* is addressing. At the moment of betrothal, the agent declared to the woman that so-and-so had sent him to betroth her to him — *Ploni shlachani l'kadeshach lo* — and then, in the very same breath, turned and said to her, *harei at mekudeshet li*, "You are betrothed to me." In such a case, one might have thought that when he said *li* — "to me" — she could not tell whether he meant himself or whether he was speaking in the voice of the *meshaleach*, representing the one who sent him. If she were genuinely confused and believed she was being betrothed to the one who sent him, the marriage would be a *kiddushei ta'ut*. Tosafot therefore adds that the *mishnah* is teaching a case where *hi yada'at* — she knew — that he meant himself. But the deeper *chiddush* is the rejection of a *hava amina* that we might read *li* as spoken in the principal's voice: we do not say that. When the agent says *li*, he speaks in his own voice, not that of the one who appointed him.

A natural question arises: why does the *mishnah* require that she subjectively understood him? Generally in *halacha* we assess matters objectively — *dvarim shebalev einam dvarim* — we do not ask what she thought he meant, but what the words or circumstances objectively signify. The answer seems to be that the language here is genuinely ambiguous in context, given that the agent had just been speaking as a representative of the *meshaleach*. That objective ambiguity is precisely why the *mishnah* must specify the scenario in which the meaning was clear.

## Rama'ut Discussion

### Braita Rama'ut

The *mishnah*'s ruling — that the marriage is valid — says nothing explicit about the moral dimension. A *braita* fills the gap: *mah she-asah asui*, what he did is done and stands, *ela she-nahag bah minhag rama'ut* — but he conducted himself with deceptive and faithless behavior toward the one who sent him.

This is worth pausing over, because the ethical weight of the critique depends heavily on the specific circumstances. The *mishnah* uses the phrase *isha plonit* — a specific, named woman — which means the principal had a particular woman in mind, not merely a general mandate to find someone suitable. That makes the betrayal considerably more concrete. The agent was not simply stumbling upon a woman while on an errand; he was dispatched with a particular mission regarding a particular woman and then diverted that mission to his own benefit.

Even so, the situation is not entirely simple. If the first man and this specific woman had no existing relationship beyond the principal's expressed interest, one might wonder how severe the *rama'ut* truly is. The *gemara* will also raise the principle of *shema yikdemenu acher* — the fear that someone else will seize the opportunity — which suggests that urgency can sometimes change the calculus. And the woman's own preference, while not foregrounded in the *gemara*, is not entirely absent from the moral picture.

As for the *mishnah* itself, the *gemara* notes that although it does not explicitly invoke the language of *rama'ut*, the word *halach* — "and he went" — carries a connotation of going off and doing something underhanded. The *tanna* of the *mishnah* encodes the moral critique in the choice of word, while the *braita* makes it explicit.

## Agent Acts for Himse (II)

### Friend vs. Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָתָנֵי ״הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks a question concerning the language of the mishna: **What is different here that** the mishna **teaches:** With regard to **one** man **who says to another,** **and what is different there,** in the previous chapter (50a), **that** the *tanna* **teaches:** With regard to **one who says to his agent,** go and betroth a certain woman for me in such and such a place, and he went and betrothed her in a different place, she is not betrothed? Why does the mishna there label him an agent, and here it labels him simply another?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא רְבוּתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, וְהָתָם רְבוּתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. הָכָא רְבוּתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּאִי תְּנָא ״שְׁלוּחוֹ״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שְׁלוּחוֹ הוּא דְּהָוֵי רַמַּאי, דְּסָמְכָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ, סָבַר: עָבֵד לִי שְׁלִיחוּתַי, אֲבָל חֲבֵירוֹ, דְּלָא סָמְכָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ, אֵימָא לָא לֶיהֱוֵי רַמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here** the mishna **teaches us a novel** element **and there** it **teaches us a novel** element, through its use of these terms. The Gemara elaborates: The mishna **here teaches us a novel** element, **as had it taught: His agent, I would say** that **it is his agent who is** considered **a scoundrel** in that case, **as** one who sends an agent **relies** upon him, **thinking:** He will **perform my agency for me,** since he was sent for that purpose. **But** with regard to the use of the term: **Another,** who was not appointed and **upon whom he does not rely,** as he is not his agent, you might **say** that he **should not** be considered **a scoundrel.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם רְבוּתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּאִי תְּנָא: ״הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא דְּכִי קִדְּשָׁהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, דְּסָבַר: לָא טָרַח, אֲבָל שְׁלוּחוֹ דְּטָרַח אֵימָא: מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הוּא לוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the mishna **there teaches us a novel** element, **as had it taught: One who says to another, I would say** that **it is** in the case of **another that she is not betrothed if he betrothed her in a different place, as he thinks** that this person would **not** go to the **trouble** of looking for her elsewhere, and therefore he authorized him to betroth the woman only in that particular place. **But** with regard to **his agent, who** is assumed to take special **trouble** to fulfill his agency, you might **say he is** merely **showing him** the **place** where she is likely to be found, and she is betrothed in any case. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that even when he appointed an agent she is not betrothed if he does not find her in the place the putative husband specified.

The *gemara* then asks: why does the *mishnah* describe the one sent as *chavero* — his friend — while an earlier *mishnah* in the previous *perek* described a similar figure as *shlucho*, his agent? The *gemara* answers that each formulation carries its own *chiddush*, and both were necessary.

In the present *mishnah*, which uses the language of *chavero*, the *chiddush* is this: one might have thought that only when you formally appoint an *agent* — someone you have explicitly commissioned, on whom you rely, from whom you expect full performance of the task — does his acting for himself constitute *rama'ut*. That is because *samcha da'atei*, you have placed full confidence in him; you feel you have discharged your responsibility. But if you merely ask a favor of a friend — if you said casually, "If you happen to be passing by and you get the chance, see if you can arrange this for me" — then perhaps there is less of a sense of binding obligation, and the friend who acts for himself would not be a *ramai*. The *mishnah* teaches that even in that case it is the wrong thing to do.

Rashi clarifies the distinction between *chavero* and *shaliach*: the friend was asked in an incidental way — if you happen to find yourself near her, perhaps you could arrange this on my behalf — not sent on a formal mission. Nevertheless, the *mishnah* tells us, even that informal request creates enough of a moral obligation that diverting it to one's own benefit is considered *rama'ut*.

The earlier *mishnah*, which uses *shlucho*, teaches a different *chiddush*: that if you send a formal agent to betroth a woman in a specific location, and he betroths her elsewhere, the *kiddushin* does not take effect. One might have thought that if you are sent on a mission, the specification of a location is merely *mareh makom* — a helpful pointer about where to find the woman — and that you retain latitude to act wherever you encounter her. The *mishnah* teaches otherwise: if a location was stipulated, only that location falls within the *shelichut*, and an act performed elsewhere is outside its scope entirely.

The broader point is that the nature of the relationship — whether the person is a paid agent on a formal commission or a friend asked a casual favor — can shape the moral weight of the betrayal and the scope of the *shelichut*, even if in all cases the person technically holds the legal power to perform the act.

## Rama'ut Discussion (II)

### Rabin Chasida Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִין חֲסִידָא אֲזַיל לְקַדּוֹשֵׁי לֵיהּ אִיתְּתָא לִבְרֵיהּ, קַידְּשַׁהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ. וְהָתַנְיָא: מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה – עָשׂוּי, אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּהַג בּוֹ מִנְהַג רַמָּאוּת! לָא יַהֲבוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאוֹדוֹעֵי! סָבַר: אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אֲתָא אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא מְקַדֵּשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabin the Pious** was appointed an agent and **went to betroth a woman to his son,** but in the end he **betrothed her to himself.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in the aforementioned *baraita*: **What he did is done, but he has treated him in a deceitful manner?** How could a pious individual act in this fashion? The Gemara answers: The woman's family **would not give her to** the son, and agreed only to let her marry the father. The Gemara further asks: Even so, before betrothing her **he should have** first **informed** his son that they refuse to let her marry him. The Gemara explains that Rabin **thought: In the meantime,** while I am busy reporting back to my son, **someone else** will **come** and **betroth her.**

### Duty to Inform

The *gemara* insists: *ibaya lei la-aduyei*, he should have informed his son before proceeding. The obligation to inform is more than a courtesy. It prevents the experience of betrayal that arises when someone discovers, without warning, that the person they trusted to act on their behalf has instead acted for himself. Even if the explanation that follows is entirely legitimate, the shock of the discovery is itself a form of harm. Moreover, informing the principal formally terminates the *shelichut*; the agent announces that the original mission cannot be fulfilled, and only then proceeds on his own behalf. Without that communication, the transition from acting as representative to acting for oneself happens invisibly, and that invisibility is its own form of *rama'ut*.

### Shma Yikdemenu Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר: אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אֲתָא אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא מְקַדֵּשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Rabin **thought: In the meantime, someone else will come** and **betroth her.**

The *gemara* explains why Rabin Chasida did not stop to inform his son: *savar ad hacha ve-hacha ati inish achrini mekadesh lah* — he calculated that by the time he could send word to his son and wait, another suitor would come and betroth her first. The principle of *shema yikdemenu acher* — the concern that delay will cost the opportunity entirely — justified acting without prior notification. This woman was evidently sought after, and the window was narrow. Under those circumstances, the *gemara* accepts the failure to inform as excusable.

## Land & Competition

### Rav & Land Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה יְהַיב לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְרַב, אֲמַר: זִבְנַהּ נִיהֲלִי לְהַאי אַרְעָא. אֲזַל זַבְנַהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ. וְהָתַנְיָא: מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה – עָשׂוּי, אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּהַג בּוֹ מִנְהַג רַמָּאוּת! בָּאגָא דְאַלִּימֵי הֲוָה (לֵיהּ), לְרַב – נָהֲגִי בֵּיהּ כָּבוֹד, לְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה – לָא נָהֲגִי בֵּיהּ כָּבוֹד. אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאוֹדוֹעֵי! סְבַר: אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אֲתָא אִינִישׁ אַחְרִינָא זַבֵּין לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar story: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana gave money to Rav** and **said: Purchase this land for me.** Rav **went and purchased it for himself.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* with regard to an agent who acts in this manner: **What he did is done, but he has treated him in a deceitful manner?** The Gemara answers: The land **was** located **in a valley** inhabited **by violent** men, who **treated Rav** with **respect** and were prepared to sell the land to him, but who **did not treat Rabba bar bar Ḥana** with **respect.** As in the case of Rabin, the Gemara questions this behavior: Nevertheless, Rav **should have informed him.** The Gemara responds that Rav **thought: In the meantime someone else will come** and **purchase** the land.

### Rabbi Abba Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב גִּידֵּל הֲוָה מְהַפֵּיךְ בְּהַהִיא אַרְעָא. אֲזַל רַבִּי אַבָּא זַבְנַהּ. אֲזַל רַב גִּידֵּל קַבְלֵיהּ לְרַבִּי זֵירָא. אֲזַל רַבִּי זֵירָא וְקַבְלֵיהּ לְרַב יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. אָמַר לֵיהּ: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה אֶצְלֵנוּ לָרֶגֶל. כִּי סְלֵיק, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עָנִי מְהַפֵּךְ בַּחֲרָרָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וּנְטָלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: **Rav Giddel was engaging in** the acquisition of **a certain** plot of **land.** In the meantime **Rabbi Abba went** and **purchased it. Rav Giddel went** and **complained about** Rabbi Abba **to Rabbi Zeira. Rabbi Zeira went and complained about** Rabbi Abba **to Rav Yitzḥak Nappaḥa.** Rav Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **said to him: Wait until** Rabbi Abba **ascends to** visit **us for the pilgrimage Festival,** when all come to hear the Festival sermon, on which occasion we can discuss this matter with him. **When** Rabbi Abba **ascended** Rav Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **found him** and **said to him:** If **a pauper is engaging in** the acquisition of **a loaf** of bread that he found, **and another came and took it from him, what** is the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִקְרָא רָשָׁע. וְאֶלָּא מָר מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא. הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי נִיתְּבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ מָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זַבּוֹנֵי לָא מְזַבֵּינְנָא לַהּ, דְּאַרְעָא קַמַּיְיתָא הִיא, וְלָא מְסַמְּנָא מִילְּתָא. אִי בָּעֵי בְּמַתָּנָה – נִישְׁקְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba **said to him:** The one who took it away **is called wicked.** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa replied: **But** if so, **what is the reason** that **the Master acted this way?** Rav Giddel was negotiating the purchase of this land and you purchased it. Rabbi Abba **said to him: I did not know** that Rav Giddel was trying to acquire the land. Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa retorted: **Now too, the Master should give it to him,** since you have been made aware that he submitted the first offer. Rabbi Abba **said to him:** As for **selling, I will not sell it, as it is** the **first land** I have ever purchased, **and** this **matter** of selling one's first acquisition **is not** a good **omen. If he wants** to accept it **as a gift, let him take it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב גִּידֵּל לָא נָחֵית לַהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשׂוֹנֵא מַתָּנֹת יִחְיֶה״. רַבִּי אַבָּא לָא נָחֵית לַהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּהַפֵּיךְ בַּהּ רַב גִּידֵּל. לָא מָר נָחֵית לַהּ וְלָא מָר נָחֵית לַהּ וּמִיתְקָרְיָא אַרְעָא דְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel did not descend to** claim this plot of land, **as it is written: "But he who hates gifts shall live"** (Proverbs 15:27), and therefore he did not wish to accept the land as a gift. **Rabbi Abba** also **did not descend to it, because Rav Giddel** was already **engaging in** the acquisition of **it** when he acquired the land. In this manner, **this Sage did not descend to** take the plot of land, **and that Sage did not descend to** take care of **it, and it was called the land of the Sages.**

A related but distinct case follows. Rav Giddel had been *mehapeich* — actively engaged in negotiating the purchase of — a certain plot of land. Rabbi Abba came along and purchased it. This is importantly different from the previous cases: Rabbi Abba was not a *shaliach* for Rav Giddel. He was simply another person, acting entirely for himself, with no prior relationship of agency or mission. Each man was, as it were, acting on his own behalf.

The *gemara* does not apply the *braita*'s critique of *rama'ut* to Rabbi Abba directly. The *gemara* reserves that critique specifically for situations where one was charged with acting on another's behalf. Here it is a case of open competition.

Rav Giddel complained to Rabbi Zeira, who brought the matter to Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha waited until Rabbi Abba came to the pre-*Yom Tov* lectures — the *pirka d'yarcha kalla* — and confronted him indirectly. He posed the case: what do we say about a poor person who is in the process of trying to seize a piece of cake — *ani mehapech becharara* — and someone comes and snatches it from under his nose? Rabbi Abba answered immediately: *nikra rasha*, the one who takes it is called a *rasha*. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha then said: so why did you do exactly that?

Rabbi Abba replied that he had not known Rav Giddel was in negotiations; the seller had not informed him. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha then said: in that case, now that you know, give the land to Rav Giddel. Rabbi Abba said he would not sell it — it was the first plot of land he had ever purchased, and divesting oneself of one's first property is a bad *siman*. But he offered to give it to Rav Giddel as a gift.

Rav Giddel would not take the gift, for it is written *ve-sonei matanot yichyeh* — one who hates gifts will live; he would not accept what he could not earn himself. Rabbi Abba would not use the land, because Rav Giddel had been engaged in trying to purchase it and he had unwittingly cut him out. The result: *lo mar nachis lah velo mar nachis lah* — neither this one nor that one would enter the land, and it came to be called *ar'a de-rabbanan*, the land of the rabbis, as it was given over to the use of the *benei yeshiva*. A story of everyone trying to do the right thing, at the conclusion of a discussion about people doing the wrong thing.

### Tosafot: Metziah

The case of *ani mehapech becharara* raises a deeper question about when it is and is not appropriate to seize an opportunity that someone else had their eye on — in a context where no *shelichut* exists at all. Tosafot notes the tension with the *halacha* of *metzia*: the law of found objects seems to be that even if you spotted a lost object first, if I physically seize it before you do, it belongs to me. Why would seizing a cake or a plot of land be any different?

Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Tam, offers an important distinction. The prohibition on *mehapech becharara* applies specifically in two types of cases: where a person is trying to profit from an ownerless or available thing and you compete with them for the same opportunity, as in the case of Rav Giddel and the land; or where a person is trying to purchase a particular item and you swoop in and buy it first. These are analogous to the case of Rav Giddel — *dumya de-Rav Giddel* — and are called *rasha* for the same reason: go and find another opportunity for yourself, rather than interfering with your fellow's engagement.

But if the object is a true *metziah* — a lost object on the street — then there is no equivalent alternative. There is only one such object, only one such opportunity. If you do not grab it, you will not find another. In that case, it is genuinely every man for himself, and there is no prohibition.

This maps onto an interesting parallel in the context of marriage. The very first *daf* of *Kiddushin* compared the act of finding a wife to finding a *metziah*. If a particular woman is unique — if there truly is no equivalent — then the zero-sum nature of the situation may change the moral analysis. By contrast, where the matter is purely commercial and similar opportunities exist, interfering with another person's active engagement is considered a form of *resha'a*.

Tosafot extends this reasoning to a further case: the prohibition of *yored le-umanuto shel chaveiro* — entering into another person's trade or livelihood. If a person has cast nets for fish, one may not begin casting nets in the same area, even though the fish are ownerless *hefker*. The reason is that this is not a case of grabbing an isolated windfall opportunity; the other person has established a business, and you are interfering with that business. The relevant category is not *metziah* but *umanuto* — the ongoing livelihood of the other.

### Employment Ethics

Tosafot draws an explicit practical application from this analysis, citing a ruling of Rabbeinu Yitzchak: it is forbidden for a tutor to approach a homeowner who already employs a different tutor and offer to take the position. The existing tutor is already engaged, and the one seeking to displace him should go find his own employment elsewhere rather than interfering with that *parnassah* arrangement.

However, if the homeowner himself indicates dissatisfaction and signals that he is looking for a replacement, then of course one may compete for the position. And, Tosafot adds, another homeowner may approach the already-employed tutor and try to hire him away — because the second homeowner can say: *eino ritzoni ela lazeh*, I want specifically this person, not just any tutor, since this one seems to me most likely to benefit my son. That rationale — I cannot simply find a functionally equivalent alternative — is precisely what removes the prohibition.

This parallels the logic applied to marriage: one cannot simply say to the person who wants a particular woman, "go find someone else," because in the world of persons, each individual is unique. The zero-sum nature of the situation justifies acting in one's own interest even at another's loss — provided one is not acting in violation of a prior commitment or an existing agency relationship.

## After 30 Days

### Thirty-Day Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״ וּבָא אַחֵר וְקִידְּשָׁה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִשְׁנֵי. בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן – תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** with regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me after thirty days, and another** man **came and betrothed her within** those **thirty days, she is betrothed to the second** man. This is a full-fledged betrothal, so that if she is **an Israelite woman** betrothed **to a priest, she may partake of *teruma*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו וּלְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״ וּבָא אַחֵר וְקִידְּשָׁה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן, אוֹ בַת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל – לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the first man said to the woman: You are hereby betrothed to me **from now, and** only **after thirty days** shall the betrothal take effect, **and another** man **came and betrothed her within** those **thirty days,** there is uncertainty whether **she is betrothed or** whether **she is not betrothed** to each of them. Consequently, if she was **the daughter of a non-priest** betrothed **to a priest, or the daughter of a priest** betrothed **to an Israelite, she may not partake of *teruma*.** Since her betrothal is uncertain, the daughter of a non-priest cannot be considered the wife of a priest, and similarly a priest's daughter who is doubtfully married to an Israelite loses her right to partake of *teruma* as the daughter of a priest.

The *mishnah* moves to a second case, which is joined by the word *ve-chen* — "and similarly." A man says to a woman: *harei at mekudeshet li le-achar shloshim yom* — you are betrothed to me as of thirty days from now. If another man came and betrothed her within that thirty-day window, she is betrothed to the second man. The *mishnah* further distinguishes the case where the first man said *me-achshav u-le-achar shloshim yom* — from now and taking effect in thirty days — in which case if another man betroths her within that period, her status is *mekudeshet ve-eina mekudeshet*, doubtfully betrothed, because it is unclear how much the formula "from now" advances the effect of the *kiddushin*. In that state of uncertainty, a *bat Yisrael* betrothed to a *kohen* or a *bat kohen* betrothed to an *Yisrael* may not eat *terumah*, as the respective marital status is unresolved.

### Money Consumed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּא אַחֵר וְקִידְּשָׁה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים, מַהוּ? רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּתְאַכְּלוּ הַמָּעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And similarly,** with regard to **one who says to a woman: Be betrothed to me** after thirty days, and another came and betrothed her within this period, she is betrothed to the second man. The Gemara asks: In a case where **another did not come and betroth her within** the **thirty** days, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rav and Shmuel both say: She is betrothed** after thirty days, **even if the money** he gave for her betrothal **has been used** before the end of this period, as the betrothal takes effect from when the money is given.

### Rav & Shmuel

The *gemara* asks: what is the law if no second man came and betrothed her within the thirty days, but the money — or ring — given at the time of the original *kiddushin* was consumed or spent before the thirty days elapsed? Does the *kiddushin* take effect at the end of the thirty-day period?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הָנֵי זוּזֵי לָא לְמִלְוָה דָּמוּ וְלָא לְפִקָּדוֹן דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** that she is betrothed despite the lack of money at the end of the thirty days? The Gemara explains that **these dinars** given for her betrothal are **not comparable to a loan, nor** are they **comparable to a deposit** that was transferred into her possession and subsequently lost. In both of these cases she would not be betrothed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִקָּדוֹן לָא דָּמוּ – פִּקָּדוֹן בִּרְשׁוּתָא דְמָרֵיהּ קָא מִתְאַכְּלִי, וְהָנֵי בִּרְשׁוּתָא דִידַהּ קָא מִתְאַכְּלִי. לְמַלְוֶה נָמֵי לָא דָּמוּ – מִלְוָה לְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה, הָנֵי בְּתוֹרַת קִידּוּשִׁין יַהֲבִינְהוּ נִיהֲלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **They are not comparable to a deposit,** as **a deposit is used in its owner's possession,** i.e., any loss is incurred by the owner, **whereas these are used in her own possession,** as he gave her the money to keep. These dinars **are also not comparable to a loan; a loan is given for spending** and therefore no money remains at the time of the betrothal, whereas **these** dinars **were given to her** from the outset **for the purpose of betrothal.**

Rav and Shmuel both rule: she is betrothed — *mekudeshet* — even though the money has been consumed. The *chiddush* of this ruling is striking, because ordinarily when a legal *kinyan* is structured to take effect after a period of time, some remnant of the original act of acquisition must still be present when that time arrives. If a person says "I will own your cow in thirty days" and draws the cow into his property, but on day thirty the cow is wandering in *reshut ha-rabbim*, the *kinyan* does not take effect. The original act must still have a surviving presence when the transaction is supposed to finalize.

The *gemara* explains why the thirty-day *kiddushin* is different. *Hanei zuzei lav l'milveh damu ve-lav l'pikadon damu* — this money is neither a loan nor a deposit.

It is not a *pikadon*, because if it were, the money would remain in the man's ownership, held in trust by the woman; loss of the money would be the man's loss, and no money remaining would mean nothing left with which to effect the *kiddushin*. But here the money was given into the woman's possession outright — if it is lost, it is her loss. That is not a *pikadon*.

Nor is it a *milveh*, a loan. A loan is money transferred for the borrower to spend, with an obligation to repay an equivalent sum. One cannot effect *kiddushin* with a loan — *ein mekadesh be-milveh* — because there is nothing concrete with which to betroth; you are merely forgiving a debt. That too does not apply here.

What was given was *kesef kiddushin* from the very first moment — money delivered to the woman specifically in the capacity of *kiddushin*, with the understanding that it would take effect later. Because it was originally given with the full force of *kesef kiddushin*, the memory of that act is not erased even if the physical money no longer exists. The woman received it as *kiddushin*, and if the *kiddushin* does not go through, she would be obligated to return it; that lingering reality of the transaction is sufficient to keep the original act "present" in the relevant sense. One is not marrying her with money that no longer exists at the moment the thirty days arrive; one is marrying her with the money that was originally given to her as *kesef kiddushin*, and the fact that it no longer physically exists does not erase the significance of that original act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּא אַחֵר וְקִידְּשָׁהּ, וְחָזְרָה בָּהּ, מַהוּ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת – אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר, וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת – לָא אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר.

Why? This **statement,** by which she changes her mind, **comes and nullifies** her previous **statement** when she agreed to the betrothal. **Reish Lakish said: She cannot retract** her consent, as her second **statement does not come and nullify** her previous **statement.**

And here it is** a case involving one **statement and** a second **statement,** as the owner of the produce appointed the agent and rescinded his appointment by speech. This shows that **a statement comes and nullifies** a previous **statement.** Reish Lakish replied: **Giving money to a woman is different, as it is considered like an action, and** a mere **statement does not come and nullify** the **action** of the transfer of money.

And giving a bill of divorce to an agent is considered** to be **like giving money to a woman, and** yet this *baraita* **teaches** that **it is nullified,** which indicates that speech can override even an action.

## Thought vs. Action

### Vessel Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַכֵּלִים יוֹרְדִים לִידֵי טוּמְאָתָן בְּמַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֵין עוֹלִים מִידֵּי טוּמְאָתָן אֶלָּא בְּשִׁינּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **All vessels descend into their** state of contracting **ritual impurity by means of thought.** Although an unfinished vessel cannot become ritually impure, if the craftsman decided not to work on it any further, it immediately assumes the status of a completed vessel and can become ritually impure. **But they ascend from their** state of **ritual impurity only by means of a change** resulting from **an action.** A ritually impure vessel, once it undergoes physical change, is no longer ritually impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה מוֹצִיא מִיַּד מַעֲשֶׂה וּמִיַּד מַחְשָׁבָה, מַחְשָׁבָה אֵין מוֹצִיאָה לֹא מִיַּד מַעֲשֶׂה וְלֹא מִיַּד מַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **An action** undertaken to improve the vessel **negates** both the status created by an earlier **action** that supposedly completed the vessel **and** the status created by the **thought** not to perform more work on the vessel. In that case the vessel cannot contract ritual impurity until the craftsman has finished working on it. By contrast, **a thought** to improve the vessel **negates neither** status created by **action nor** status created by **thought.**

Reish Lakish himself now raises a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan from the laws of *tumah* and vessels. The *mishnah* teaches: *kol ha-kelim yordim lidei tumatan be-machshava* — all vessels acquire susceptibility to *tumah* through thought. When a craftsman is fashioning a vessel, the question of when the vessel is "complete" — and therefore susceptible to *tumah* — is determined by the craftsman's intention. If he decides in his mind that the vessel is finished, it is finished and can become *tamei* from that moment, even without any further physical action.

However, *ve-ein olim mi-tumatan ela be-shinuy ma'aseh* — vessels do not lose their susceptibility to *tumah* except through a change in action — actually reworking the vessel. If a craftsman has already decided in his mind that the vessel is complete, he cannot simply think "I want to continue working on it" and thereby un-make the vessel's status as a complete vessel. He must actually go back and physically resume work on it.

The principle derived from this: *ma'aseh motzi miyad ma'aseh u-miyad machshava* — an act can reverse either a prior act or a prior thought. *Machshava eina motzi'ah lo miyad ma'aseh ve-lo miyad machshava* — thought reverses neither a prior act nor even a prior thought. This seems to directly contradict Rabbi Yochanan's position: even thought cannot reverse thought, so certainly words should not be able to reverse *kiddushin*.

### Maaseh Motzi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי מַחְשָׁבָה דְּטוּמְאָה, דְּכִי מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי, וְכִדְרַב פָּפָּא. דְּרַב פָּפָּא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב: ״כִּי יִתֵּן״, וְקָרֵינַן: ״כִּי יוּתַּן״. הָא כֵּיצַד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Thought** in the case **of ritual impurity is different, as** it **is considered like action, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa. As Rav Pappa raises a contradiction:** With regard to the *halakha* that produce must become wet in order for it to contract ritual impurity, the verse states: "But if water is placed [*vekhi yuttan*] upon the seed, and any part of a carcass falls upon it, it is impure" (Leviticus 11:38). The word *yuttan* is written defective, and **is written** as if it says ***ki yitten*.** Accordingly, this would mean that one must actively place the water on the produce. Yet **we read** it, based on the tradition as to its correct pronunciation, **"*ki yuttan*,"** which includes any situation where the produce becomes wet. **How so?** How can the way the verse is written and the way it is read be reconciled?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי יוּתַּן״ דֻּומְיָא ״דָּיִתֵּן״. מָה ״יִתֵּן״ דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ, אַף ״יוּתַּן״ נָמֵי דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa explains that the standard of **"if water is placed [*ki yuttan*]"** is **similar to: If one places [*ki yitten*]; just as** the term **places [*yitten*]** indicates **that** it is **satisfactory to** the owner for the produce to become wet, as he himself is placing the water, **so too,** the term **"is placed [*yuttan*]"** means **that** it is **satisfactory to** the owner that the produce becomes wet, despite the fact that he did not place the water himself. This is proof that thought is equivalent to action with regard to ritual impurity, as if one is satisfied with the produce becoming wet it is considered as though he actively placed the water himself. By the same reasoning, thought which renders a vessel susceptible to ritual impurity is powerful enough to require an action to counteract its effect.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: the domain of *tumah* is sui generis. *Shani machshava de-tuma de-chi ma'aseh damya* — thought regarding *tumah* is treated as equivalent to an act. This is derived from the *drasha* of Rav Pappa, who noted the tension between the written and read forms of the verse *ki yutan*: water placed passively on seeds renders them susceptible to *tumah* provided the owner was *nicha lei* — pleased about it. The owner's positive disposition toward the water having touched the seeds is treated as though he himself had placed the water on them. Thought and will, in the domain of *tumah*, have the legal force of action.

Because thought in the *tumah* context functions as an act, when the craftsman decides in his mind that the vessel is finished, that is legally equivalent to performing an act of completion. And since thought-as-act cannot be reversed by mere thought, it takes a physical act of resuming work on the vessel to undo that status. This is entirely specific to *tumah*; it does not establish a general principle that thought cannot reverse thought, and therefore has no bearing on the *kiddushin* question, where Rabbi Yochanan holds that words — in a domain where there is no such equivalence to acts — can indeed reverse prior words.

### Rav Zavid Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד מַתְנֵי לְהָא שְׁמַעְתְּתָא אַהָא: וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁנָּתְנָה רְשׁוּת לִשְׁלוּחָהּ לְקַדְּשָׁהּ, וְהָלְכָה הִיא וְקִדְּשָׁהּ אֶת עַצְמָהּ, אִם שֶׁלָּהּ קָדְמוּ – קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשִׁין, וְאִם שֶׁל שְׁלוּחָהּ קָדְמוּ – אֵין קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another version of the discussion: **Rav Zevid taught this** dispute **with regard to this** *halakha*: **And similarly,** in a case **where she gave permission to her agent to accept betrothal for her and she** subsequently **went and accepted betrothal herself** from someone else, **if her** betrothal **came first, her betrothal is** a valid **betrothal, but if** the betrothal **of her agent came first, her betrothal is not a betrothal.**

### Void Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא קִדְּשָׁהּ אֶת עַצְמָהּ וְחָזְרָה בָּהּ, מַהוּ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוֹזֶרֶת, אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, לָא אָתֵי דִּיבּוּר וּמְבַטֵּל דִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages discuss this *halakha*: If she did **not accept betrothal herself and retracted** her appointment of the agent, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: She can retract** her appointment; **and Reish Lakish says: She cannot retract** her appointment. The Gemara clarifies their respective opinions. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: She can retract** her appointment, as **a statement comes and nullifies** a previous **statement,** by which she appointed the agent. **Reish Lakish said: She cannot retract** her appointment, as **a statement does not come and nullify** a previous **statement.**

The *gemara* then asks: what if she did not betroth herself, but simply verbally retracted her appointment of the agent — *chazrah bah* — without performing any act? Can she void her agent with words?

Rabbi Yochanan says she can retract: *ati dibbur u-mevatel dibbur*. Reish Lakish says she cannot: *lo ati dibbur u-mevatel dibbur*. Reish Lakish's position here is, as noted, quite extreme: a woman could announce before witnesses that she is revoking her agent's authority, and yet the agent could theoretically proceed to marry her off. Tosafot grapples with this and attempts to limit the scope of Reish Lakish's ruling.

## Terumah & Get

### Terumah Negation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בִּיטֵּל, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא תָּרַם בִּיטֵּל – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from the aforementioned mishna (*Terumot* 3:4) with regard to one who appointed an agent to separate *teruma* on his behalf and subsequently **canceled** the agency: **If he canceled** the appointment **before** the agent **separated *teruma*, his *teruma* is not *teruma*.** This shows that a statement nullifies a previous statement.

### Rav Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּדַם בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְתָרַם אֶת כִּרְיוֹ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where the homeowner preceded** the agent **and separated *teruma*** from **his heap** himself, **which is an action,** and an action certainly nullifies a statement.

Rav responds on behalf of Reish Lakish: no — *hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon she-kadam ba'al ha-bayit ve-taram mi-kalav*, the case is where the householder himself went ahead and performed the act of taking *terumah* from his own vessels first. The revocation was not purely verbal; it was effected by an action — the householder taking *terumah* himself before the agent could act. This is a case of *ma'aseh mevatel shelichut*, an act terminating the agency, not words terminating the agency. Reish Lakish's principle — that words cannot void a prior arrangement — remains intact, because the effective revocation here was through action, not speech.

By extension, a woman wishing to void her agent for *kiddushin* would have to perform an act — such as betrothing herself to someone else — not merely declare verbally that she revokes the agency.

### Get Negation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ בַּשָּׁלִיחַ אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁלַח אַחֲרָיו שָׁלִיחַ וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״גֵּט שֶׁנָּתַתִּי לְךָ בָּטֵל הוּא״ – הֲרֵי זֶה בָּטֵל תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raised an objection to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: With regard to **one who sends a bill of divorce to his wife, and** later the husband **encountered the agent or sent** another **agent after him and** in this manner **said to him: The bill of divorce that I gave you is nullified, it is thereby nullified.** This indicates that the appointment of an agent can be nullified even by a statement. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation** of the opinion **of Reish Lakish is** in fact **a conclusive refutation.**

Rabbi Yochanan then raises the *get* case again: a man sends a messenger to deliver a *get* to his wife, and then sends a second messenger after the first saying "the *get* I gave you is void," it is indeed void — *harei ze batel*. Here, a physical *get* was placed in the agent's hand — an act was performed — yet a verbal declaration is sufficient to void the agency. This parallels the case of *kiddushin*, where money was placed in the woman's hand and she now verbally retracts.

Reish Lakish's earlier answer applies here too: *kol kamma de-lo mata gitta le-yadah, dibbur hu* — as long as the *get* has not reached the wife's hand, the entire arrangement is still in the realm of the abstract and contingent. The act of *gerushin* has not been performed. The delivery of the *get* to the agent was preparatory, not the completion of any legal change in status. Before any status has changed, words can still void words.
