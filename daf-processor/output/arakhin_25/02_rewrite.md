# Arakhin 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Arachin & Makdish…

### Masechet Overview

Today's *daf* is 25, though we pick up from the bottom of 24b, where we enter a new *perek* beginning with *ein makdishin*. It is worth stepping back to appreciate the full scope of *Masechet Arachin*. The tractate is not limited to the laws of *arachin* — the fixed valuations of persons — but rather addresses the entire latter half of *Parshat Bechukotai*, covering a range of subjects. The tractate begins with *erech*, but then moves into the second major topic of that parasha: the laws governing one who is *makdish* a *sadeh*, a field, during the *yovel* period — how such a field is redeemed, and what occurs when *yovel* arrives. Indeed, the very end of *Bechukotai* deals with *temura*, which is the subject of the next tractate. This is not a digression; rather, the full subject of *Masechet Arachin* is the range of different *hakdashot* — donations to the *beit hamikdash* — discussed at the close of *Parshat Bechukotai*.

### Hakdashot Structure

The tractate thus encompasses multiple forms of consecration and dedication to the *beit hamikdash*. What unifies them is their source in the concluding passages of *Bechukotai*, even if the tractate's name is drawn, as with many tractates, from the opening topic. With that context established, we turn to the *sugyot* at hand.

## Rav vs Shmuel: Yovel Year

### Mishna: Sanctify Fields

The *mishna* had stated that one may not be *makdish* a *sadeh* within two years of the *yovel*, because the *Torah* says *al pi hashanim hanotarot* — based on the remaining years. The Gemara, however, based on analysis of a *baraita* and the positions of Rav and Shmuel, revised that reading. Rather than a prohibition, what the *mishna* means is that it would be very unwise to be *makdish* within two years of *yovel*. The system works as follows: fifty *shekel* is the base value for a field planted with a *chomer* of barley seed — a *chomer seorim* — and this amount is divided proportionally over forty-nine years. That proportional deduction, *girua*, applies when there are more than two years remaining. Once fewer than two years remain, the *girua* no longer applies, and the full flat fifty *shekel* is charged. On this point, Rav and Shmuel agree. Their *machloket* concerns a different question: what is the law if someone is *makdish* a *sadeh* during the *yovel* year itself?

According to Shmuel, if one is *makdish* a *sadeh* during the *yovel* year, the sanctification does not take effect — it is not *kadosh*. According to Rav, the sanctification is valid and the person pays the full fifty *shekel*.

### Baraita: No Sanctify Yov…

The *gemara* now challenges Rav's position from a *baraita*. We read: *makdishin bein lifnei hayovel bein achar hayovel* — one may sanctify a field whether before *yovel* or after *yovel* — *uvesha'at hayovel atzmo lo yakdish* — but during the *yovel* year itself one shall not sanctify — *ve'im hikdish einah kedushah* — and if one did sanctify, it does not take effect. This appears to be a straightforward ruling like Shmuel's: just as *yovel* causes the field to revert to the *kohanim* and effects all manner of change, if one attempts to be *makdish* during *yovel* itself, it accomplishes nothing.

How does Rav respond? *Amar lecha Rav*: when the *baraita* says *einah kedushah*, it means *einah kedushah begirua* — it is not sanctified with a *girua* deduction — *aval kadosh venoten chamishim* — but it is indeed sanctified, and one pays the full fifty *shekel*. The reasoning is that the *yovel* year is the end of one cycle and the beginning of the next, so there is nothing to deduct — one pays full freight from the outset of a new cycle.

The *gemara* challenges this interpretation: if *einah kedushah* in the *baraita* really means "not sanctified with a *girua*," then the opening clause — *makdishin lifnei hayovel* — must mean *kedushah lege'al begirua*, that sanctification before *yovel* entails a *girua* deduction. But that cannot be, because both Rav and Shmuel agree — *de'amri tavaihu* — that *ein makdishin lege'al begirua pachat mishtei shanim*: within the last two years there is no *girua*, and yet the sanctification is still valid. So we are back where we started: the *mishna* says sanctification during *yovel* is invalid, and Rav says it is valid. What does Rav do with this *baraita*?

### Rav's Hermeneutic

Rav responds: *ha mani rabanan hi* — this *baraita* follows the position of the *Rabbanan* — *va'ana de'amri kirebbi* — and I follow *Rebbi*. For *Rebbi* holds the principle *de'amar rishon rishon bikhlal shevi'i shevi'i bikhlal*: when the *Torah* specifies a range from one point to another, both endpoints are included. This principle was discussed earlier in the tractate in the context of the verse *miyom harishon ad yom hashvi'i* — does "from the first until the seventh" include days one and seven? *Rebbi* says yes: endpoints are inclusive.

Applying this to our case: the verse says *ve'im mishnat hayovel yakdish sadehu* — from the year of *yovel*. According to Rav, following *Rebbi's* hermeneutic, this means including the *yovel* year itself — *ushnat hayovel bikhlal*. Therefore, the sanctification takes effect on the *yovel* year itself. The *baraita* that says otherwise follows a different hermeneutic, not *Rebbi's*. Rav is free to follow *Rebbi*, and on that basis holds that sanctification during *yovel* is valid and the full fifty *shekel* is paid.

### Years Calculation

The *gemara* now raises a fascinating arithmetic question. If, according to Rav, sanctification can begin during the *yovel* year itself — meaning the *yovel* year is the first year of the new cycle — then the total period over which the sanctity operates is fifty years: the *yovel* year itself plus the next forty-nine. If we are dividing fifty *shekel* proportionally over the number of remaining years, and sanctification on the *yovel* year itself means fifty years remain, then the rate should be exactly one *shekel* per year — fifty divided by fifty. Yet we know from the *mishna* we are about to read that *Rebbi* himself rules *noten sela upundyon leshana* — one *sela* and one *pundyon* per year — which reflects a division by forty-nine, not fifty.

*Rebbi* himself says it is one *sela* and a *pundyon* — a *pundyon* being one forty-eighth — so that forty-nine times one and one forty-eighth yields fifty plus a small remainder (for the money-changer). But according to Rav's reasoning, based on *Rebbi's* hermeneutic that *yovel* itself is included, should it not be a division by fifty, yielding exactly one *shekel* per year? The *gemara* asks: *pundyon mai avidteih* — why does the *pundyon* exist at all?

### Yovel as Year One

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a different *tannaitic* dispute: *Rebbi keRebbi Yehuda sevira leih* — *Rebbi* holds like *Rabbi Yehuda*, who says *bishnat chamishim ola lechan ulechan* — the fiftieth year, the *yovel* year, counts toward both cycles. It is not merely year fifty of the previous cycle; it is simultaneously year one of the next cycle. If the *yovel* year itself is year one of the next cycle, then even if sanctification begins at the start of *yovel*, the count runs from year one through year forty-nine of that same cycle — only forty-nine years — because *yovel* is one, the following year is two, and so on. The division is therefore by forty-nine, which yields *Rebbi's* figure of one *sela* and a *pundyon* per year.

There is even a conceptual elegance to this alignment: if the *yovel* year is also considered the first year of the following cycle, then it makes intuitive sense that the laws governing the next fifty years — including the capacity to be *makdish* — can already take hold during *yovel* itself. The *gemara* notes: *shnat hachamishim shana zu vezu* — the fiftieth year is simultaneously this and that.

The *gemara* then extends the analysis to Shmuel's position. According to Shmuel, who holds that one cannot be *makdish* during the *yovel* year, if we were to combine Shmuel with *Rabbi Yehuda's* position that *yovel* counts as year one of the next cycle, the result would be troubling: since *yovel* — year one — is off limits, only years two through forty-nine are available, meaning only forty-eight years remain. Dividing fifty by forty-eight yields one *sela* and two *pundyos*, not the one *sela* and one *pundyon* that *Rebbi* rules. Therefore, *al karchach d'Shmuel* — we must say that according to Shmuel, *Rebbi* holds like the *Rabbanan*, who say that the *yovel* year does not count as year one of the next cycle. Year one begins only after *yovel* concludes, leaving exactly forty-nine years — yielding the familiar *sela* and *pundyon*.

The net result of the discussion: everyone agrees the division is by forty-nine. According to Rav, one achieves this by holding that *yovel* is year one (so counting begins at *yovel* itself and runs forty-nine years). According to Shmuel, one achieves it by holding that *yovel* is year zero — the first usable year comes only after *yovel* — again leaving forty-nine years. In theory, one could combine positions to produce a division by fifty or a division by forty-eight, but neither of those is *Rebbi's* ruling.

## No Partial Years: Whole…

### Baraita: Full Year Units

The *gemara* continues. *Ta shema* — come and hear: *ve'lo goalin achar hayovel pachat mishana* — one cannot redeem after *yovel* in units less than a year. At first glance this is puzzling: why should there be any restriction on redeeming immediately? The *Torah* seems to permit redemption at any time from the outset. So what does it mean that one cannot redeem in less than a year after *yovel*?

The *gemara* explains that *b'shlama l'Shmuel* — according to Shmuel, this makes reasonable sense: since one cannot be *makdish* during the *yovel* year itself, the entire process of sanctifying and redeeming does not begin for a full year from the start of *yovel* — it begins only after *yovel* concludes. The statement "one cannot redeem less than a year after *yovel*" is thus an indirect way of saying the whole process is suspended during the *yovel* year itself. It is an unusual formulation, but it works.

*Ella l'Rav* — but according to Rav, who holds that sanctification during *yovel* is valid, why would one be unable to redeem within a year of *yovel*? *Mi savar achar yovel mamash?* The *gemara* rejects the literal reading. *Mai achar yovel? Be'emtza yovel* — it just means in the middle of any cycle, a normal case. *Achar yovel* simply denotes "sometime during a cycle," not specifically in the year immediately following *yovel*.

### Full Years Rounding

The real meaning of the *baraita*, according to Rav, is this: if one is *makdish* a field at some point during the cycle and then seeks to redeem it, one cannot calculate the remaining time in units smaller than a full year. *Dechol kamma delo malya lishana lo migra lei* — if a year has not yet been completed, one cannot deduct it. When counting the number of years that have passed since *yovel* in order to determine how much has been deducted from the fifty *shekel*, one counts only complete years. Conversely, when counting the number of remaining years, one rounds up: a fraction of a year counts as a full year remaining. In short, all calculations are done in whole-year units, always to the benefit of *hekdesh*.

### Verse Source

The *gemara* proceeds to ask: what does the *baraita* add that we did not already know? *She'ein mechashevin chodashim im hahekdesh* — that one does not count months when dealing with *hekdesh* — is already stated explicitly in the *mishna*. The *gemara* responds: *mah ta'am ka'amar* — the *baraita* is providing the rationale. Why may one not redeem in units less than a year? Because *she'ein mechashevin chodashim im hahekdesh* — when it comes to *hekdesh*, one reckons by years, not months. The two statements reinforce each other. The *tanna* derives this from the verse: *vechishev lo hakohen et kesef al pi hashanim hanotarot* — the *kohen* calculates the money based on the remaining *years* — *shanim ata mechashev ve'ei ata mechashev chodashim*: you count by year units, not month units.

### Months for Hekdesh Benef…

One might ask: is it ever possible to count months? The *tanna* continues: *minayin she'im ata rotze la'asot chodashim leshana ose?* How do we know that if you wish to count months toward a year, you may do so? The *gemara* asks what case this addresses. The answer: *kegon de'akdesha bepalga de'arba'in vetamnei* — suppose the field was sanctified when forty-eight and a half years had already elapsed in the cycle, meaning there is a year and a half remaining. Recall the rule: if fewer than two full years remain until *yovel*, there is no *girua* and the full fifty *shekel* is charged. Now, if one rounds up the remaining time to two years, the owner is still in the *girua* zone and pays less. If one rounds down to one year remaining, the owner pays the flat fifty *shekel* — which is more. Rounding down here benefits *hekdesh*. In such a case, the *tanna* teaches from the verse *vechishev lo hakohen* — the *kohen* will calculate for him in whatever manner — *mikol makom*, broadly — meaning the calculation can run either way so long as it serves *hekdesh's* interest.

The principle is thus: whole year units only; normally one rounds up when counting years remaining (benefiting *hekdesh*); but in cases where rounding down benefits *hekdesh* — as in the scenario with a year and a half remaining — that too is permitted.

## Field Measure & Rocks

### Chomer Barley Seed

Turning now to the *mishna* itself, the *Torah* states: *ve'im misedei achuzato yakdish ish laHashem vehaya erkecha lefi zaro zera chomer seorim bachamishim shekel kasef* — if one sanctifies a field of one's inheritance, the valuation is fifty *shekel* for a field planted with a *chomer* of barley seed. This is the standard: fifty *shekel* for the acreage corresponding to the planting of a *chomer seorim*, and everything is proportional from there. For the full forty-nine years until *yovel*, one pays fifty *shekel* for that acreage. If it is two and a half times that acreage, or a quarter of that acreage, the calculation is proportional.

### Crevices & Rocks

The *mishna* then introduces a complication. *Hayu sham neka'im amukim asara tefachim* — if there are deep crevices in the field, more than ten *tefachim* deep — a *tefach* being roughly three to three and a half inches, so ten *tefachim* is approximately three feet — *o sela'im gevohim asara tefachim* — or rocks more than ten *tefachim* tall — *ein nimdadim imah* — they are not counted toward the acreage of the field. One cannot plant in a deep narrow crevice or on top of a tall rock, so these areas are excluded from the calculation based on the fixed Torah rate of fifty *shekel* per *chomer seorim*. Instead, they are assessed separately at market value. The entire field is still sanctified; it is simply that the unplantable portions are not measured according to the Torah's fixed rate.

The *gemara* will also note the converse: if the crevice or rock is less than ten *tefachim*, it is counted with the field even if that specific spot cannot technically be planted. The ten-*tefachim* threshold is the standard measure distinguishing a separate domain from what is considered part of the surrounding area.

One might ask whether a drought or change in conditions affects the valuation — whether a parched and unusable field would receive a deduction. That question is not addressed in the *mishna* here; it concerns the intrinsic nature of the field, not temporary conditions.

The *mishna* continues: *hikdishah shtayim veshalosh shanim lifnei hayovel* — if one sanctified the field two or three years before *yovel* — *noten sela upundyon leshana* — one gives one *sela* and one *pundyon* per year. A *sela* is a *shekel*, and a *pundyon* is one forty-eighth of a *sela*, so forty-nine times one and one forty-eighth yields fifty *shekel* plus a small increment to cover the money-changer. This was already mentioned above in connection with *Rebbi's* ruling.

### Seed vs Harvest

A *baraita* clarifies the precise meaning of the *chomer seorim* measure. *Kor zera velo kor tevuah* — it is a *kor* (equivalent to a *chomer*) of seed, not a *kor* of produce. That is: the field must be large enough to plant a *kor* of barley seed in it, not large enough to produce a *kor* of barley. This is a crucial distinction, because one seed produces many times its weight in grain; a field large enough to produce a *kor* would be vastly smaller than one large enough to plant a *kor*. Since the *Torah's* measure is the planting amount, this results in a larger field — and since a larger field means a lower per-unit cost of redemption, this interpretation is more favorable.

*Mapolet yad velo mapolet shevarim* — the seed is spread by hand, not by ox. The practice of placing a bag of seed on an ox and making a small hole in it so that the seed scatters as the ox walks back and forth results in a denser distribution of seed than hand-spreading. Hand-spreading uses more seed per unit area — or equivalently, a given amount of seed covers more ground when scattered by hand. Again, this results in a larger field and therefore benefits the person redeeming.

*Tani Levi*: *lo me'aveh velo medak, ela beinoni* — neither overly thick nor overly thin seed, but average seed. This specification is notable in itself. One might have assumed that some fixed, well-known measure had long been standardized, like a foot of measurement. But the fact that the *gemara* must describe the seed in these terms — not this kind, not that kind, but average — suggests that in the era of the *gemara*, there was no single universally known standard, and the calculation would have required actual measurement in practice.

### Why Excluded

The *gemara* asks: why are rocks and deep crevices excluded from the field measurement at all? They were part of the field that was sanctified. Let them be sanctified and assessed using the same proportional system — fifty *shekel* per *chomer seorim* — as the rest of the field. If everything follows the same rate, why distinguish them at all?

One possible answer is that these areas might not reach the minimum measure of a *beit kor* on their own. The *gemara* rejects this, however, based on a *baraita*: the verse says *zera chomer seorim bachamishim shekel* — fifty *shekel* for a *chomer* — but one might think that only a full *chomer*-sized field is subject to this law. The *baraita* asks: *minayin lerabot letach, vechatzi letach, se'ah, vetarkav, vechatzi tarkav* — how do we know it applies to smaller measures such as a half-*chomer*, a *se'ah*, a *tarkav*, or even half a *tarkav*? The answer: *talmud lomar sadeh mikol makom* — the *Torah* says "a field," without restriction, meaning any size is subject to the proportional rate. So size alone cannot be the reason rocks and crevices are excluded.

*Amar Mar Ukva bar Chama*: *hacha benekikim melei mayim, dela benei zeri'ah ninhu* — we are speaking here of crevices that are filled with water and are simply not plantable. Since the entire measure of *zera chomer seorim* is about planting, an area that cannot be planted is categorically excluded from the calculation. The *gemara* notes that this interpretation is consistent with the parallel to rocks: *deika nami dekatani dumya desla'im* — the *mishna* puts crevices in the same category as rocks, and the issue with rocks is, of course, that nothing can be planted on them. *Shema mina*.

*Ei hachi* — if so, asks the *gemara*, why are smaller rocks and shallower crevices included? They also cannot be planted. The *gemara* answers: *hanu niganay de'ara mikeru* — those are considered mere "puddles of the ground" (in the case of shallow water-filled depressions) or *shidlay de'ara mikeru* — "backbones of the ground" (in the case of low rocky protrusions). In other words, below the ten-*tefachim* threshold, one takes a macro view: minor irregularities are considered part of the field even if this particular patch cannot technically be planted. At a certain scale, one does not measure every centimeter and ask whether that precise spot can hold a seed. Only features that are sufficiently large — ten *tefachim* tall or deep — are treated as genuinely separate from the surrounding field.

An interesting question, not resolved in this *sugya*, is what happens if a low-lying but unplantable feature takes up a very large percentage of the field. At what point does a feature that is technically below the ten-*tefachim* threshold nevertheless demand separate treatment because of its extent? Similarly, what is the minimum size for a field to be considered a "field" at all, such that the Torah's fixed valuation applies? The *gemara* does not address these questions here.

## Deduction & Installments

### Deduction Always

A *baraita* addresses a question left ambiguous in the *mishna*: when exactly does the deduction based on remaining years apply? Specifically, suppose the field was sanctified on year one of the cycle and has been sitting idle in the possession of *hekdesh* for many years — either because *hekdesh* has been working the land and collecting income from it, or because *hekdesh* has been lazy and nobody has been earning anything from it. When the owner now comes to redeem it, does the redemption price reflect the number of remaining years from the time of the original *hekdesh*, or from the present moment of redemption?

The *baraita* teaches: *venigra me'erkecha af min hahekdesh* — you deduct the years that have elapsed even if they elapsed while the field was in the possession of *hekdesh*. *She'im achla hekdesh shana o shtayim* — if *hekdesh* farmed it for a year or two — *inami lo achla ela shehayta lefanav* — or even if *hekdesh* did nothing with it and it simply sat there — *noten sela upundyon leshana* — you still pay one *sela* and a *pundyon* for each remaining year calculated from the moment of redemption. The logic is that the current value of the field is determined by the number of years remaining until *yovel*, and that is precisely the time of redemption. An earlier, higher valuation at the moment of *hekdesh* does not fix the redemption price.

### No Payment Plans

The *mishna* then rules: *ve'im amar hareini noten davar shana beshana* — if the owner says, "Let me work out an installment plan, paying a portion each year" — *ein shomin lo* — this is not permitted. The entire amount must be paid at once, *kulo ke'echad*.

The owner's incentive to redeem is acute: if the field is not redeemed before *yovel*, it is lost to the family entirely. If *hekdesh* has already sold it to a third party and the owner has not redeemed it, the *Torah* states *lo yiga'el od* — the owner is out of the picture — *vehayah hasadeh betzeito bayovel kodesh laHashem kisdeh hacherem lakohen tihye achuzato* — the field goes to the *kohanim* at *yovel*. Given this pressure, one can easily imagine an owner desperate to redeem but unable to marshal the full sum. Nevertheless, the *Torah's* language — *vechishev lo hakohen et kesef* — the *kohen* shall calculate the money — implies the full amount, *kulo ke'echad*, not in portions.

A *baraita* confirms this derivation: if the owners say *harenu notnim davar shana beshana* — we will pay a certain amount per year — *she'ein shomin lahem* — one does not listen to them. *Talmud lomar, vechishev lo hakohen et kesef* — the verse teaches that the *kohen* calculates the money as a single payment.

### Fifth for Owners

Both the owner and any other person are entitled to redeem the field. *Echad ba'alim ve'echad kol adam* — both the owner and any other person — may redeem it. The difference: *shehaba'alim notnim chomesh vekol adam eino noten chomesh* — the owner must add a *chomesh*, a fifth, on top of the redemption value, while any other person does not. This is somewhat counterintuitive — like the inverse of a pawnshop, where one buys back one's own item at a premium rather than a discount.

The logic appears to be this: when a person is *makdish* their own inherited field to the *beit hamikdash*, the act carries considerable spiritual and symbolic weight. If the very same person then turns around and buys it back, that somewhat undermines the power of the original donation. The *chomesh* is therefore not simply a financial surcharge; it is an acknowledgment that buying back what one gave is a step that diminishes the initial act of largesse, and the additional payment compensates for that diminishment. If a third party redeems it, no such concern applies — the *hekdesh* is simply liquidating an asset, and the third party pays the market equivalent with no surcharge.

It follows that the owner has a strong incentive to redeem the field before *yovel* — and to do so personally — but at a higher price. One might ask: could the owner engineer a workaround by having a third party redeem it and then transferring the field back? The *mishna* addresses precisely this question next.

## Redemption & Yovel

### Owner Redeems

The *Torah* spells out the case explicitly: *ve'im ga'ol yigal et hasadeh hamakdish oto veyasaf chamishit kesef erkecha alav vekam lo* — if the one who sanctified the field redeems it, adding a fifth to the valuation price, it remains his. The *mishna* states this as its first case: *hikdishah uge'alah* — if the owner sanctified and then redeemed it — *einah yotzah miyado bayovel* — it does not leave his possession at *yovel*. He paid the *chomesh*, he redeemed it, and he keeps it. That is the best outcome.

### Son Redeems

*Ge'alah bno* — if the owner's son redeemed it — *yotzah le'aviv bayovel* — it reverts to the father at *yovel*. Remarkably, this is an even better financial arrangement for the owner: the son pays the redemption price without the *chomesh* (since he is not the one who was *makdish*), and at *yovel* the field returns to the father. The son functions as a form of legal continuity of the father — not as a stranger, but as an extension of the father's own identity in the halakhic sense.

### Other Redeems

*Ge'alah acher o echad min hakrovim* — if a stranger, or even a relative who is not the son, redeemed it — *uge'alah miyado* — and even if after that person redeemed it the original owner purchased it from him, so that the field is now in the owner's hands — *yotzah lakohanim bayovel* — it still goes to the *kohanim* at *yovel*. The chain of redemption has been broken. The field was not redeemed from *hekdesh* by the owner or his son, and that is the operative fact. The subsequent transfer from the third-party redeemer back to the owner changes nothing.

The consequence is stark: if one wants to keep the field in one's family, one must either redeem it personally — paying the *chomesh* — or have one's son redeem it.

### Priest Redeems

*Ge'alah echad min hakohanim ve'harei hi tachat yado* — if one of the *kohanim* redeemed the field from the *beit hamikdash*, and then *yovel* arrives while it is still in his possession — *lo yomar ho'il vehia yotzei lakohanim bayovel veharei hi tachat yadi harei hi sheli* — that *kohen* may not simply say: since it is going to the *kohanim* anyway, and it is already in my possession, it is mine. *Ella yotzei mitachat yado umitchaleket lechol echav hakohanim* — rather, it must leave his possession and be divided equally among his fellow *kohanim*. The *mishna* does not specify which *kohanim* receive the division; the *gemara* will address this.

Left open by the current *mishna* is the case where nobody redeems the field and it remains sanctified in the hands of *hekdesh* when *yovel* arrives. The *Torah's* language — *ve'im lo yigal et hasadeh ve'im machar et hasadeh le'ish acher* — might suggest that going to the *kohanim* requires two conditions: the owner did not redeem it, and *hekdesh* sold it to someone else. What happens if neither condition is met — if it simply remained with *hekdesh*, unsold and unredeemed? That question will be the subject of a later *mishna*.

## Son vs Other: Baraita

### Not to Son Exception

The *gemara* now brings a *baraita* to clarify the precise scope of "other." *Im lo yigal et hasadeh ve'im machar et hasadeh le'ish acher* — if it is sold to another person — *le'acher velo laben* — to another, but not to the son. The *baraita* teaches that when the field passes out of *hekdesh* into the hands of someone other than the son, the owner's ability to reclaim the field through the *yovel* mechanism is lost. The son is an exception precisely because he represents continuity with the father.

### Brother Continuity

The *gemara* asks: perhaps the brother should enjoy the same status as the son? The word *ish* in the verse — "a man" redeems — is very broad and would include the brother. So the question is whether *acher* — "other" — excludes the brother as much as it excludes the son.

The concept underlying the son's special status is *continuity*: the son is not truly "other" relative to the father; he is a legal extension of the father's presence. The brother too, by the laws of *yibbum* — levirate marriage — is considered a continuation of the deceased brother. *Lehakim shem achiv* — he perpetuates his brother's name and inherits his brother's property. Does the brother's *yibbum*-based continuity earn him the same standing as the son?

### Son Higher Status

The *gemara* resolves the question: *klum yesh yibbum ela bemakom she'ein ben* — *yibbum* only applies in the absence of a son. The entire institution of *yibbum*, in which a brother stands in for a deceased man, is contingent on the absence of a son. If a son exists, there is no *yibbum*. This means the son's claim to continuity is categorically prior to and stronger than the brother's. *Ha'yesh ben, ein yibbum* — where there is a son, there is no *yibbum*.

The *gemara* adds: *vetipok leih dehacha tarti vehacha chada* — could one not have given the simpler answer of two against one? The son has two independent sources establishing his status as a continuation of the father: the case of *yi'ud* (where the *Torah* explicitly gives the son the right to convert a transaction involving an *amah ivriyah* into a *kiddushin*), and the case of *eved Ivri* (where the son-master continues the master's relationship with the slave). The brother has only one: *yibbum*.

The *gemara* responds that this quantitative argument, while valid, is actually circular: *mishum de'eved Ivri mehai pircha nami hu denafka* — the case of *eved Ivri* is itself established only through the very same logical argument about son versus brother that we are now making. We know the son, not the brother, maintains the master-slave relationship in *eved Ivri* only because we have already derived that the son has greater continuity status than the brother — based on the principle that *yibbum* only applies when there is no son. So the "two against one" argument cannot be invoked independently; both cases rest on the same underlying logic.

## Daughter & Wife Questions

### Daughter Preserves Field

*Ba'ei Rava bar Avuha*: if a daughter redeems the field from *hekdesh*, does it preserve the field for the father? That is, will the field revert to the father at *yovel*, as it does when the son redeems it?

### Daughter Yibbum Status

The question turns on which framework is more applicable to the daughter. On one hand, *keivan delinyan yibbum ben ubat kiyadadi patrei* — for the purpose of *yibbum*, a son and a daughter are treated identically: either one exempts the widow from *yibbum* because the father's memory and lineage are considered preserved. If that framework applies here, then the daughter should count as continuity just as the son does, and the field should revert to the father at *yovel*.

### Daughter as Other

On the other hand, *keivan delinyan nachalah, bat bimkom ben ke'acher damya* — when it comes to inheritance, a daughter's standing is lower than a son's; a daughter inherits only in the absence of sons. If the inheritance hierarchy is the relevant framework, then the daughter is categorically "other" vis-à-vis the son, and she does not represent continuity in the way the son does.

The *gemara* resolves the question from a *baraita*: *kol shehu acher bimkom ben* — whoever is considered "other" relative to a son — *veha nami bimkom ben ke'acher damya* — and the daughter too is considered "other" relative to the son. This reflects the underlying structure of the patrilineal inheritance system, in which legal continuity runs through the male line. The daughter, for all that she shares certain attributes of continuity with respect to *yibbum*, occupies a lower position in the inheritance hierarchy, and it is that hierarchy which is determinative here.

### Woman's Sanctification

*Ba'ei Rami bar Chama*: if a woman was *makdish* a field, who plays the role of the son — who represents continuity such that, if they redeem it, the field reverts to her at *yovel*? There are two candidates: her husband and her son.

The case for the husband: *ba'al mukim lah shekein yarshah* — the husband establishes continuity because he inherits from her. Moreover, he inherits first — before the son — in cases where both are present.

The case for the son: *shekein notel baraui kevemuchzak* — the son inherits what is "raui," what was owed to her but not yet in her possession at the time of her death. If she dies, is divorced, and then her father dies, the inheritance from her father passes down to her son. The husband, by contrast, inherits only what was already in her possession at the time of death. The son's inheritance is more expansive — it has a character of ongoing continuity rather than a one-time transfer.

One can frame the distinction conceptually: when a husband inherits from his wife, it has the quality of a transfer — the property passes from her domain to his. When a son inherits from his mother, it has the quality of continuity — the property flows through her to him as an extension of her line.

### Unresolved: Husband vs S…

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*. We do not know whether it is the husband or the son who functions as the operative "continuity" figure for a woman who was *makdish* a field.

## Within Two Years & Pries…

### Within Two Years

*Ba'ei mineih Rami bar Chama meRav Chisda*: if a field was sanctified less than two years before *yovel* — in which case we know the owner pays the full fifty *shekel* with no *girua* deduction — does that field still go to the *kohanim* if not redeemed by *yovel*? One might think not: the *Torah* juxtaposes the going-to-*kohanim* with *venigra me'erkecha*, the deduction mechanism. Perhaps only fields subject to *girua* — proportional deduction — are subject to the *yovel* transfer to *kohanim*. A field sanctified within two years, where no *girua* applies, might be a different category.

### Redeemable Goes to Pries…

*Amar leih mai da'atach* — Rav Chisda asks: what exactly are you thinking? The concern is that the verse *venigra me'erkecha* implies that fields subject to deduction go to the *kohanim*. *Debat girayon hi* — only something eligible for deduction goes to the *kohanim*; *delav bat girayon lo* — something not eligible for deduction does not.

Rav Chisda rejects this. *Adaraba* — on the contrary. The operative verse is *im lo yigal hasadeh vehayah hasadeh betzeito bayovel* — if the field is not redeemed, it goes to the *kohanim* at *yovel*. The criterion is not eligibility for deduction but eligibility for redemption. *Ve'hai nami bat geulah hi* — any field that is capable of being redeemed goes to the *kohanim* if it is not redeemed. A field sanctified within two years is perfectly capable of redemption — it simply costs the full fifty *shekel* with no discount. Therefore it absolutely goes to the *kohanim* if not redeemed.

### Priest Possession Rule

A *baraita* then addresses the case raised in the *mishna*: *ga'al echad min hakohanim* — one of the *kohanim* redeemed the field. Can he keep it? *Lo yomar ho'il veyotzei lakohanim bayovel veharei hi tachat yadi harei hi sheli* — he cannot argue: since it is going to the *kohanim* at *yovel* and it is already in my possession, it is mine. *Ella yotzei mitachat yado umitchaleket lechol echav hakohanim* — it must leave his hands and be divided among all his brother *kohanim*.

### Inheritance Principle

The *baraita* explains the underlying principle by asking: *minayin lesdeh sheyotzei lakohanim bayovel vega'al echad min hakohanim minayin shelo yomar* — how do we know the *kohen* who redeemed it cannot simply keep it? *V'din hu* — he might reason as follows: *b'shel acherim ani zoche* — I receive a portion in a field that belongs to others entirely; *b'shel atzmi lo kol shekein* — how much more so should I keep a field that is already in my possession? But *talmud lomar achuzato* — the verse says *achuzato*, his inheritance. The field must be like one's own ancestral inheritance to be retained. *Achuza shelo ve'ein zo shelo* — it must be truly his; and this field, which he purchased from *hekdesh*, is not his ancestral inheritance. Therefore he has no special claim to it, and it is divided equally among the *kohanim*.

### Next: Nobody Redeems

The one scenario not yet addressed — neither in the *Torah's* explicit language nor in the *mishnayot* discussed so far — is the case in which nobody redeems the field and it simply remains in the possession of *hekdesh*, still sanctified, when *yovel* arrives. The *Torah's* formulation — *ve'im lo yigal et hasadeh ve'im machar et hasadeh le'ish acher* — might be read as requiring two conditions: the owner did not redeem it, and *hekdesh* sold it to someone else. What happens if only the first condition is met — the owner did not redeem it, but *hekdesh* never sold it either, and the field remains in *hekdesh* in a sanctified state when *yovel* strikes? That question, and its resolution, will be the subject of the *mishna* taken up in the next session.
