# Rosh Hashanah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nissan/Bal Tacher Intro

### Nissan Rosh Hashanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה חַג הַמַּצּוֹת טָעוּן לִינָה — אַף חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת טָעוּן לִינָה. וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּפָנִיתָ בַבֹּקֶר וְהָלַכְתָּ לְאֹהָלֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as the festival of Passover requires remaining** overnight in Jerusalem, and only on the following day may one return home, **so too, the festival of *Sukkot* requires remaining overnight** in Jerusalem before returning home. The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to Passover, **from where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara explains: **As it is written** about the Paschal offering: "And you shall roast and eat it in the place which the Lord your God shall choose; **and you shall turn in the morning, and go to your tents"** (Deuteronomy 16:7).

Today's daf is 5, and the Gemara continues its discussion of the principle that Nissan is *Rosh Hashanah l'regalim* — it marks the beginning of the cycle for the *moadim* and for the prohibition of *bal tacher*, the obligation not to delay bringing one's sacrifices, vows, and other religious obligations. The Gemara had already surveyed, on the previous daf, a Baraita presenting a range of opinions about when exactly one transgresses *bal tacher*: whether it requires three *regalim* proceeding in order beginning from Pesach (the assumption of the Mishnah, which makes Nissan the *Rosh Hashanah*), or whether any two or any single *regel* suffices. Now the Gemara turns to working through the various *pesukim* that serve as the basis for these positions.

### Overnight in Jerusalem

One of the matters addressed in this context is the obligation to remain overnight in Jerusalem after bringing one's *olat re'iyah* and *shalmei chagigah*. As Rashi explains on this daf, the verse *ufanita vaboker ve-halachta le-ohalecha* — "you shall turn in the morning and go to your tents" — is not speaking of the morning of *Yom Tov* itself, for that is precisely the day one is obligated to appear in the *Azarah*. Rather, it speaks of the morning following *Yom Tov*, after one has brought the *olat re'iyah*. The pilgrim remains overnight and departs the next morning — which is the first day of *Chol Ha-moed*.

### Pesach/Sukkot Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן (בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר) — תַּשְׁלוּמִין לַעֲצֶרֶת מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִדְּתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה מְנֵה יָמִים וְקַדֵּשׁ חֹדֶשׁ, מְנֵה יָמִים וְקַדֵּשׁ עֲצֶרֶת. מָה חֹדֶשׁ לִמְנוּיָו — אַף עֲצֶרֶת לִמְנוּיָו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the first *tanna*** of the *baraita* **and Rabbi Shimon,** who learn from the verse: "On the festival of Passover, and on the festival of *Shavuot*, and on the festival of *Sukkot*" (Deuteronomy 16:16), that one transgresses the prohibition against delaying only after three Festivals have passed, **from where do they** derive the *halakha* that **the *Shavuot*** offering **has redress** for seven days? The Gemara answers: **They derive it from** that **which Rabba bar Shmuel taught, as Rabba bar Shmuel taught: The Torah states to count** the **days,** as it is stated: "A month of days" (Numbers 11:20), **and** then **sanctify** a new **month** with offerings. **And** the Torah also said to **count** the **days** from Passover, as it is stated: "You shall count fifty days" (Leviticus 23:16), **and** then **sanctify** the festival of ***Shavuot*** with offerings. **Just as the** new **month is** sanctified **for** the unit of time **by which it is counted,** i.e., for one day, **so too, *Shavuot* is** sanctified **for** the unit of time **by which it is counted,** i.e., for one full week, as it is stated: "Seven complete weeks shall there be" (Leviticus 23:15).

The Gemara draws a connection between Pesach and Sukkot to establish this overnight requirement: just as Pesach carries an obligation of *linah* — remaining overnight — so too does Sukkot: *mah Chag Hamatzot ta'un linah af Chag Hasukkot ta'un linah*. The derivation of the Sukkot obligation from the Pesach verse is achieved by linking the two festivals through their respective *pesukim*.

It is worth noting that the explicit verse about going home in the morning appears only in connection with Pesach and not with Sukkot. This is not coincidental. By Sukkot, the Torah already assumes one spends the entire week in Jerusalem — *ve-samachta lifnei Hashem Elokecha shivat yamim*, "you shall rejoice before the Lord your God for seven days." Sukkot falls at the end of the harvest season; one has gathered everything in, and taking a week in Jerusalem is entirely natural. Pesach, by contrast, falls in the middle of the agricultural year, with the wheat harvest still underway. The pilgrim must return quickly to his fields. Hence the explicit verse directing him to wait until morning before setting out — *ufanita vaboker ve-halachta le-ohalecha*. Tosafot raises the question whether there might not be a general obligation to remain overnight after bringing *korbanot* regardless of this specific verse, but the Gemara proceeds with the derivation as stated.

## Tashlumin/Shavuot

### Three Regalim Position

The Gemara then turns to a question about the positions of Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Shimon. Both of these opinions hold that *bal tacher* is transgressed only after three *regalim* — either any three in proper order or the three in sequence. The difficulty arises concerning *tashlumin* on Shavuot: the principle that if one failed to bring the required *korbanot* on the first day of a *regel*, one has the remaining days of that *regel* to make them up. For Shavuot, the accepted rule is that the full seven days following the first day serve as *tashlumin*. According to the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Shimon, the Torah's repetition of all three *chagim* is used to establish the requirement of three *regalim* for *bal tacher* — not to link the festivals to one another. So if the repetition is being used for that purpose, how do Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Shimon derive the seven-day *tashlumin* period for Shavuot?

### Rabba bar Shmuel

The Gemara answers: *nafka lehu midetani Rabba bar Shmuel* — they derive it from what Rabba bar Shmuel teaches. The Baraita states: *Rabba bar Shmuel amar Torah — manei yamim ve-kadesh chodesh*, count days and sanctify the new month. The underlying idea is that *Rosh Chodesh* is a single day, because the unit you count to arrive at it is the day — twenty-nine or thirty of them. By analogy: *manei yamim ve-kadesh Atzeret*, count days and sanctify Shavuot. Just as *Rosh Chodesh*, being counted in units of days, is itself one day, so too Shavuot should, by that logic, be one day.

### Shavuot Dual Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא עֲצֶרֶת חַד יוֹמָא? אָמַר רָבָא: אַטּוּ עֲצֶרֶת יוֹמֵי מָנִינַן, שָׁבוּעֵי לָא מָנִינַן? וְהָאָמַר מָר: מִצְוָה לְמִימְנֵי יוֹמֵי, וּמִצְוָה לְמִימְנֵי שָׁבוּעֵי. וְעוֹד: ״חַג שָׁבוּעוֹת״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if so, **say** that the ***Shavuot*** offering may be redressed for only **one day,** as *Shavuot* is determined by a count of fifty days from Passover. How, then, is it known that the *Shavuot* offering has seven days for redress? **Rava said: Is that to say** that **we count** only **days until *Shavuot*,** but **we do not** also **count weeks? But didn't the Master say:** It is **a mitzva to count** fifty **days, and** it is also **a mitzva to count** seven **weeks,** which teaches that the Festival peace-offering brought on ***Shavuot*** may be sacrificed for an entire week. **And further, it is written** in the verse: **"The festival of weeks [*Shavuot*],"** which teaches that it is a Festival that is established through a count of weeks.

But the Gemara immediately challenges this: surely when counting toward Shavuot, one counts not only days but weeks. The Torah emphasizes *sheva shabbatot temimot tihyena* — seven complete weeks — and indeed there is a *mitzvah* to count the weeks during *Sefirat Ha-omer* as well as the days. The verse *ve-od Chag Shavuot katuv* — "Chag Shavuot" is written — further suggests a concept of the *chag* extending across a week. The result is a fascinating dual identity for Shavuot: it is counted in units of both days and weeks. As a one-day *Yom Tov*, the festival lasts a single day; but as a *regel* for the purposes of bringing *korbanot*, it carries the weight of a full week. The week-long dimension does not express itself as *Yom Tov* throughout, but manifests in the context of *korbanot* brought as *tashlumin*. This is a striking way to think about Shavuot — that its very identity is simultaneously singular and weekly, and that the week is not merely a rabbinic extension but reflects something intrinsic to what Shavuot is.

## Bal Tacher List

### Baraita's List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפֶסַח בַּר מִיקְרַב בִּרְגָלִים הוּא? פֶּסַח זִימְנָא קְבִיעָא לֵיהּ, אִי אַקְרְבֵיהּ — אַקְרְבֵיהּ, וְאִי לָא אַקְרְבֵיהּ — אִידְּחִי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the *baraita* that one becomes liable for transgressing the prohibition against delaying if he delays bringing the Paschal lamb. The Gemara expresses its astonishment about this ruling: **But is the Paschal lamb fit to be sacrificed on the** other **Festivals? The Paschal lamb has a fixed time** to be brought, on the fourteenth of Nisan; **if one sacrificed it** then, **he has sacrificed it, but if he did not sacrifice it** then, **it is excluded** forever from any use.

The Gemara now turns to unpacking the full Baraita that enumerates the categories subject to *bal tacher*. The list includes: *chayvei damim, erchin, charamim, hekdeshot, chataot, ashamot, olot, shelamim, tzedakot, ma'asrot, bechor, ma'aser*, and *Pesach*. The Gemara jumps first to *Pesach* at the end of the list, though there is much to say about the items that precede it.

### Pesach Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פֶּסַח כְּדִי נַסְבֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: The Paschal lamb is cited** here **for no reason [*kedi*];** that is to say, the prohibition against delaying is not relevant to the Paschal lamb, and the latter was mentioned in the *baraita* only because firstborn offerings, animal tithes, and the Paschal lamb are often grouped together.

The Gemara asks: *u-Pesach bar mikra b'regalim hu?* Can the *korban Pesach* be brought on the other *regalim*, such that one could be *over* on *bal tacher* by not having brought it on Shavuot and then Sukkot? The answer is no: *Pesach zimna kvi'a lei* — Pesach has a fixed, immovable time. If you brought it on the fourteenth of Nissan, you brought it; if you did not, it is simply gone — *idchaya lei*. There is no opportunity on the other *regalim* to remedy the omission. Accordingly, Rav Chisda says: *Pesach k'dei nasba* — the mention of Pesach in the list was for nothing. It appears because it is a long enumeration of obligations, and Pesach was swept along with the rest — what the Gemara elsewhere calls *shigra d'lishna*, the momentum of speech. In substance, it does not belong in the list.

### Rav Sheshes Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: מַאי ״פֶּסַח״ — שַׁלְמֵי פֶסַח. אִי הָכִי — הַיְינוּ שְׁלָמִים! תְּנָא שְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח, וּתְנָא שְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָן. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח קָאָתוּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** a different explanation: **What is** meant here by **a Paschal lamb?** It is **the peace-offering** that is brought **in place of a Paschal lamb.** If a lamb that had been set aside to be sacrificed as a Paschal offering was lost, and its owner took another lamb and sacrificed that as his Paschal lamb, and afterward the first animal was found, it must now be brought as a peace-offering. This offering is subject to all the *halakhot* of the prohibition against delaying. The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as the **peace-offerings** listed earlier, and it is still redundant. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **taught** the *halakha* with regard to **peace-offerings brought in place of a Paschal lamb, and it** also **taught** the *halakha* with regard to **peace-offerings brought independently.** The reason for this repetition is that **it might enter your mind to say: Since** the peace-offerings **are brought in place of a Paschal lamb,**

Rav Sheshes offers an alternative reading. *Mai Pesach?* he asks — what does "Pesach" in the Baraita mean? He answers: *shalmei Pesach* — a *shelamim* that originated from a *Pesach* sacrifice. The scenario is as follows: one sanctified an animal as a *korban Pesach*, the animal was lost, another was brought in its place, and then the original was found. The found animal, which had been a *korban Pesach*, now carries the status of *shelamim*. That animal is subject to the obligation of being brought within three *regalim* and is therefore subject to *bal tacher*.

### Shelamim Pesach Vs Regul…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּפֶסַח דָּמוּ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **they are considered like the Paschal lamb** itself, and so one transgresses the prohibition against delaying as soon as one Festival has passed. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that this is not so, as even this type of peace-offering is treated like the other offerings, and there is no liability until three Festivals have passed.

But the Gemara challenges Rav Sheshes: if it is simply a *shelamim*, why was it necessary to specify? The Baraita has already listed *shelamim*. The answer is that there are two distinct cases: a *shelamim* that originates from a *Pesach* (*shelamim habaim machmat ha-Pesach*) and an ordinary *shelamim* (*shelamim habaim machmat atzman*). One might have thought — *salka daitach amina* — that since the animal originated as a *Pesach*, perhaps it retains a quasi-*Pesach* character: *ho'il u-machmat Pesach ka-atu, ke-Pesach dami*. Rashi explains this to mean that perhaps one would be *over* already at the first opportunity — at the very first *regel* — just as one transgresses by missing the fourteenth of Nissan. The Baraita therefore teaches that this is not so: once the animal has assumed the status of a *shelamim*, it follows the normal rules of *shelamim* and may be brought within three *regalim*.

### List Conceptual Issues

Having established what Pesach means in the Baraita, one must step back and confront some of the conceptual difficulties that run through the entire list of obligations subject to *bal tacher*. The foundational verse — *ki tidor neder la-Shem Elokecha lo te'acher leshalmo* — speaks of one who makes a *neder*, a vow, and delays fulfilling it. Yet several items on the list sit uneasily with that framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי תִדּוֹר נֶדֶר״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נֶדֶר. נְדָבָה מִנַּיִן? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״נֶדֶר״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אִם נֶדֶר אוֹ נְדָבָה״. מָה לְהַלָּן — נְדָבָה עִמּוֹ, אַף כָּאן — נְדָבָה עִמּוֹ. ״לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ — אֵלּוּ הַדָּמִין הָעֲרָכִין וְהַחֲרָמִין וְהַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת. ״לֹא תְאַחֵר לְשַׁלְּמוֹ״ — הוּא וְלֹא חִילּוּפָיו. ״כִּי דָרוֹשׁ יִדְרְשֶׁנּוּ״ — אֵלּוּ חַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת עוֹלוֹת וּשְׁלָמִים. ״ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ — אֵלּוּ צְדָקוֹת וּמַעַשְׂרוֹת וּבְכוֹר. ״מֵעִמָּךְ״ — זֶה לֶקֶט שִׁכְחָה וּפֵאָה. ״וְהָיָה בְךָ חֵטְא״ — וְלֹא בְּקׇרְבָּנְךָ חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters derived** that all the offerings and vows listed above in the *baraita* are subject to the prohibition against delaying? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"When you shall vow a vow** to the Lord your God, you shall not delay paying it; for the Lord your God will surely require it from you, and it would be sin in you" (Deuteronomy 23:22). From the words "when you shall vow a vow," **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* in the case of **a vow-**offering, where one says: I undertake to bring an offering, thereby assuming personal responsibility to bring an offering, no matter what happens to any particular animal. But as for the case of **a gift-offering,** one says: I undertake to bring this animal as an offering. He assumes responsibility only to bring that particular animal, without assuming a general responsibility to bring an offering. **From where** do I derive that this, too, is included in the prohibition against delaying? The Gemara now analyzes the words of Deuteronomy 23:22 cited above and looks at each component. **It is stated here: "Vow," and it is stated elsewhere: "But if** the sacrifice of his offering be **a vow or a gift-offering"** (Leviticus 7:16). **Just as there a gift-offering is** together **with** the vow and is governed by the same *halakha*, **so too, here, a gift-offering is** together with the vow and is governed by the same *halakha*. The verse continues: **"To the Lord your God." This** is referring to various types of consecrations that are allocated to Temple maintenance: **Assessments, valuations, dedications, and consecrations. "You shall not delay paying it"** teaches that one violates the prohibition against delaying if he is late in paying **it, but not** if he is late in paying **its substitute,** as will be explained below. **"For** the Lord your God **will surely require it** from you" comes to include all other things that one is required to bring; **these are sin-offerings, guilt-offerings, burnt-offerings, and peace-offerings.** The words in the verse: "For **the Lord your God"** are an apparently superfluous phrase that in fact comes to include additional things in the prohibition; **these are** vows of **charity, and tithes, and firstborn** offerings. **"From you"; this** comes to include other items that one gives of one's own for the sake of a mitzva, i.e., **gleanings, forgotten sheaves, and** produce of the **corner** of the field. **"And it would be sin in you";** this teaches that the sin of delaying would be in you, **but there would be no sin in your offering,** i.e., the offering is not disqualified due to the delay.

The first difficulty concerns *damim* and *erchin*: monetary obligations one accepts to pay to the Temple treasury. These at least fit the category of a vow — one undertakes to pay a sum, and delays. Slightly more surprising are *hekdeshot* — cases where one sanctifies an object, such as a piece of property, directly to the Temple. When one says *hikdashti et ha-mechonit* — "I sanctify my car to the Temple" — the ownership transfers immediately at the moment of speech. *Mi amirato legavoah kemesirato lehedyot* — one's verbal declaration to the Most High is equivalent to a physical transfer to an ordinary person. So one might ask: in what sense is the owner "delaying"? The Temple already owns the object. The delay is merely in delivery. Yet the Baraita includes *hekdeshot* under *bal tacher*. This is already a notable extension.

More striking still is the inclusion of *chataot* and *ashamot*. A *chatat* or *asham* arises from an obligation — one sinned *be-shogeg*, or incurred a specific liability — without any vow whatsoever. The verse speaks of *lo te'acher leshalmo*, of not delaying to fulfill one's *neder*. Yet no *neder* was made. The clock begins running and after three *regalim* one has transgressed *bal tacher* simply by not having brought a *korban* one was obligated in through circumstance, not through speech. Similarly with *olot u-shelamim* in their obligatory forms — *olat re'iyah* and *shalmei chagigah* — one did not undertake a vow to bring them, yet they fall under the same prohibition. This is a significant expansion of what *bal tacher* covers.

And then the list reaches *tzedakot, ma'asrot, bechor*, and — most surprisingly — *leket, shikcha, and pe'ah*, which have not yet appeared in the Baraita's formal derivations but which will be addressed. These obligations are not directed toward the Temple at all, and some of them arise not from any verbal act but simply from how one conducts one's harvest. The strangeness of this list should not be understated.

## Parashat Nedenim

### Neder/Nedavah

The Gemara now presents the Baraita's systematic derivation of each category from the verse in *parashat nedarim*. The verse reads *ki tidor neder la-Shem Elokecha lo te'acher leshalmo* — when you make a vow to the Lord your God, do not delay fulfilling it. The Baraita begins: *ein li ela neder* — I know only a *neder*, meaning a case where one says "I accept upon myself to bring a *korban*." But what about a *nedavah* — where one sanctifies the animal directly? The Baraita derives the inclusion of *nedavah* by a *gezerah shavah*: the word *neder* appears here, and the word *neder* also appears in another context alongside *nedavah* — *o neder o nedavah zevach korbano*. Just as there the *neder* encompasses the *nedavah*, so here too: *af kan nedavah imo*.

### Harei Zo Olah

This first extension — from *neder* to *nedavah* — is a genuine *chidush*, though perhaps not the most dramatic one. When one says *harei zo olah* and directly sanctifies an animal, one might argue that the vow is immediately and fully fulfilled the moment the words are spoken: the animal is sanctified. What remains to be done? Yet the Baraita teaches that the ultimate fulfillment of such a sanctification is the actual bringing of the *korban*, and until that occurs, the full purpose of the vow remains unrealized. The *chidush* is real but comprehensible: the *nedavah* is in essence a vow whose climax has not yet been reached.

### Money/Property Vows

The Baraita continues: *ki tidor neder la-Shem Elokecha* — the words *la-Shem Elokecha*, "to the Lord your God," teach us about things given entirely and directly to God. *Elu ha-damim, ha-erchin, ha-charamim, ve-ha-hekdeshot* — these are monetary evaluations, *erchin* (fixed Torah valuations), *charamim*, and *hekdeshot*. Rashi explains the derivation from *la-Shem Elokecha* specifically: unlike *korbanot* in which the *kohanim* receive a portion — even an *olah* yields the hide to the *kohen* — these categories go entirely to God, and are therefore learned from the phrase that emphasizes the divine destination.

As noted, the inclusion of *hekdeshot* carries a real conceptual *chidush*. Once one sanctifies an object and transfers its ownership to the Temple, the delay one is subject to under *bal tacher* concerns something one no longer owns — the Temple already holds title. Nevertheless, the Baraita teaches that the obligation to bring it, to actually deliver and realize the sanctification, remains and is subject to *lo te'acher leshalmo*.

### Obligations Not Vows

The next phrase — *ki darosh yidrashenu Hashem Elokecha me'imach*, "for God will seek it out from you" — extends *bal tacher* to obligations not grounded in a vow at all. *Elu chata'ot va-ashamot olot u-shelamim* — these are *chataot, ashamot, olot*, and *shelamim*. The word *yidrashenu*, "He will seek it," denotes a divine demand rather than a vow the person undertook. For *chataot* and *ashamot*, the obligation arose from circumstance or sin. For *olot u-shelamim*, Rashi explains that the reference here is to *olat re'iyah* and *shalmei chagigah*, which are obligatory festival offerings.

This raises a genuine difficulty. Tosafot notes that *olat re'iyah* and *shalmei chagigah* have a fixed time — *ze man kvi'a lehu* — and the general rule is that if one did not bring them on the *regel*, one is no longer liable: *avar ha-regel ve-lo chag eino chayav be-achrayuto*. If that is so, one would expect no *bal tacher* to apply, since the obligation has simply lapsed. Tosafot answers that if one actually sanctified an animal for this purpose and then did not bring it, the situation is different. Even if the personal obligation to bring an *olat re'iyah* has expired, the animal itself carries a *kedushah* as an *olah* and must be brought. It is not the personal *chiyuv* of the *regel* that generates the *bal tacher*, but the sanctity of the animal that was designated and not offered. This distinction — between the lapse of the personal obligation and the ongoing status of the consecrated animal — is Tosafot's resolution, and it is instructive.

### Divine Ownership Vows

The final clause — *Hashem Elokecha* — generates the inclusion of *tzedakot, ma'asrot*, and *bechor*. Rashi notes the connection: by *tzedakah* the verse says *ve-lecha tihyeh tzedakah lifnei Hashem Elokecha*, and by *ma'aser* the verse says *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ma'asar degancha tiroshcha ve-yitzharecha*. The phrase *Hashem Elokecha* links these categories to the same overarching principle.

## Bal Tacher List (II)

### Tzedakah/Maasrot/Leket

The inclusion of *tzedakah* is comprehensible, at least: charity can be given through a vow, and when one designates money as *tzedakah* money, it assumes a kind of *kedushah* or legal status, learned out from the world of *terumot, ma'asrot*, and *nedarim*. There is a verbal element — one obligates oneself, and the money's status changes through that speech act.

*Ma'aser* carries a similar structure. The *ma'aser* is verbally separated and thereby sanctified — *kol ma'asar ha-aretz mi-zera ha-aretz la-Shem hu*. What does it mean that it belongs to God? One gives it to the *Leviyim*, or to the *aniyim*, or fulfills it in other ways — but the foundational religious act is the separation of the tithe to God. When Yaakov says *ve-chol asher titein li aser asrenu lach* — all that You give me I will tithe to You — what does it mean to give the tithe to God? The forms of fulfillment vary, but the conceptual anchor is that this is God's portion, separated through one's word. At least *ma'aser* and *tzedakah* are tethered, however loosely, to the sphere of *hafla'ah* — the speech act that changes the legal status of a thing.

*Leket, shikcha*, and *pe'ah*, however, are another matter entirely. These are agricultural gifts to the poor — gleanings dropped during harvest, forgotten sheaves, and the corners of the field left unharvested. They are not sanctified through speech. They do not go to the Temple. They arise from the structure of how one harvests — from what one drops, from what one forgets, from what one leaves. How does one transgress *bal tacher* in connection with them? The verse uses the word *me'imach* — from you, or from among you — and the Baraita's derivation from *et he-ani imach*, "the poor person who is with you," will be examined next. But conceptually, this is the strangest inclusion in the entire list. One is not delaying a vow, not delaying the delivery of something whose ownership has transferred, not even failing to bring a fixed obligatory offering. One is simply not distributing leavings of one's field to the poor. And yet this falls under *lo te'acher leshalmo*.

## Parashat Nedenim (II)

### Community Obligations

The Baraita derives the inclusion of *leket, shikcha*, and *pe'ah* from the word *me'imach* in the verse — "from among you." The Torah's phrase *et he-ani imach*, "the poor who is with you," is read as identifying a category of obligations that arise not from one's relationship to God or the Temple but from one's relationship to *amecha* — those within one's community, one's society. This is a radically different conceptual foundation. Everything that came before was about one's obligations to the Most High, whether through vows, sanctifications, or imposed obligations. *Leket, shikcha*, and *pe'ah* begin with the horizontal dimension — one's responsibility to those around one.

Tosafot raises the practical puzzle: how does one actually transgress *bal tacher* with *leket, shikcha*, and *pe'ah*? The prohibition runs for three *regalim* — but what precisely is being delayed? The normal case is that one leaves the *leket* and *shikcha* behind in the field and the *pe'ah* at the corner. One does not "give" them; one leaves them. Tosafot offers two scenarios. In the first — involving a *be-aveirah* — one violated the obligation by going back and collecting what should have been left, and now must redistribute it to the poor. The *bal tacher* would apply to the failure to effect that redistribution. In the second scenario — which does not begin with a violation — there are no poor people nearby to collect the leavings. The owner gathers them indoors to preserve them, holding them on behalf of the poor. In this case, having taken possession of these goods for distribution, one must distribute them within three *regalim*, and failure to do so constitutes *bal tacher*.

This is also the moment to reflect on the logic of the *regel* as the unit of measurement for *bal tacher* across all these categories. The obvious reason the *regel* serves as this unit in the world of *korbanot* and *hekdesh* is that the pilgrimage is when one has the opportunity to go to Jerusalem and actually bring or deliver what one owes. Missing the first *regel* meant missing one's first opportunity; Rabbi Meir holds one is therefore *over* after just one *regel*. But does this logic extend to *leket, shikcha, pe'ah, ma'asrot*, and *tzedakot*? These have nothing to do with Jerusalem. Yet perhaps, as one might suggest, there is a secondary logic: the *regel* is when all of *Klal Yisrael* gathers, and if there is ever a natural moment to settle one's obligations to the poor and to society, it is when the community assembles together. The *regel* thus carries significance not only for one's relationship to God and the Temple but for one's relationship to the community — and the inclusion of these agricultural and charitable obligations in *bal tacher* forces us to recognize that dimension of the *regel* as well.

## Lo Tacher Leshalmo

### Offering Not Substitute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״לֹא תְאַחֵר לְשַׁלְּמוֹ״ — הוּא וְלֹא חִילּוּפָיו. חִילּוּפֵי מַאי? אִי חִילּוּפֵי עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים — מִקְרָב קָרְבִי. אִי חִילּוּפֵי חַטָּאת, לְמִיתָה אָזְלָא. אֶלָּא מַאי חִילּוּפָיו — חִילּוּפֵי תוֹדָה. דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: תּוֹדָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בִּתְמוּרָתָהּ, וּמֵתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן — חֲבֶרְתָּהּ אֵין לָהּ תַּקָּנָה. הֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לַיקְרְבַהּ וְלַיקְרֵיב לֶחֶם בַּהֲדַהּ — דִּלְמָא תְּמוּרָה הִיא. לַיקְרְבַהּ בְּלָא לֶחֶם — דִּלְמָא תּוֹדָה הִיא. וְהָא כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו בַּת הַקְרָבָה הִיא, קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְעוֹלָם לְמַעוֹטֵי חִילּוּפֵי עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָבְרוּ עָלָיו שְׁנֵי רְגָלִים וְהוּמַם, וְחִילְּלוֹ עַל אַחֵר, וְעָבַר עָלָיו רֶגֶל אֶחָד. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִכֹּחַ קַמָּא קָאָתֵי — כְּמַאן דְּעָבְרוּ עָלָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies certain points in the *baraita*. **The Master said,** citing the *baraita*: **"You shall not delay paying it"** teaches that one violates the prohibition if he is late in paying **it,** i.e., the original offering, **but not** if he is late in paying **its substitute,** i.e., an animal that substituted for his offering. The Gemara asks: **A substitute for what** offering? **If** you say that the *baraita* is speaking of **a substitute for a burnt-offering or a peace-offering,** i.e., if an animal was set aside to serve as a burnt-offering or a peace-offering and it was lost, and a substitute was set aside in its place, and then the original animal was found and sacrificed, in that case the substitute **is sacrificed** just like the first, and so it is certainly subject to the prohibition against delaying. **If** the *baraita* is referring to **a substitute for a sin-offering,** i.e., if an animal was set aside as a sin-offering and it was lost, and a substitute was set aside in its place, and then the original animal was found and sacrificed, in that case the substitute **is left to die,** as it has become disqualified and can no longer be sacrificed on the altar. This being the case, there is no reason to say that it is subject to the prohibition against delaying. **Rather, what** is the **substitute** referred to in the *baraita*? It is **the substitute for a thanks-offering. As Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a thanks-offering that became mixed up with its substitute,** i.e., one substituted an animal for one designated as a thanks-offering, in which case both animals are considered consecrated, and then the original animal and its substitute became mixed up with each other, **and one of them died, there is no remedy for the other one,** and so it must be left to graze until it becomes blemished. The Gemara explains: **What could he have done** with the remaining animal? If you say **that he may sacrifice it and sacrifice the bread with it,** i.e., the forty loaves of bread that are brought as a meal-offering together with the animal component of the thanks-offering, **perhaps this** animal **is** not the one that had originally been set aside but rather **the substitute,** and the rule is that the substitute is sacrificed like the thanks-offering itself, but without bread. If you say **that he should sacrifice it without bread, perhaps it is the** original **thanks-offering,** which must be brought with bread. This, then, is the substitute that the *baraita* says is not subject to the prohibition against delaying. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But since** the animal **is not fit to be sacrificed, why do I** need a special **verse to exclude it** from the prohibition against delaying? In any case it cannot be sacrificed on the altar, and so there is no need to state that it is not included in the prohibition. **Rav Sheshet said: Actually,** you can say that the verse comes **to exclude the substitute for a burnt-offering or a peace-offering. And here we are dealing with** a case **where two Festivals** already **passed** from the time that one had consecrated the original animal but did not bring it to the altar, **and it became blemished, and he redeemed it by** replacing it with **another** animal, as required. **And** then **another Festival passed** and he did not yet bring the substitute to the altar. In that case, **it might enter your mind to say** that **since** this second animal **comes in place of the first** one, as it was consecrated as a substitute for it, **it** should be **considered as one for which three Festivals have** already **passed;** therefore, the verse **teaches us** that this is not so. Rather, the three Festivals are counted from the time of the replacement animal's consecration.

The Baraita concludes its derivation with two additional principles. The first: *lo te'acher leshalmo — hu ve-lo chilufo* — "to fulfill it": the obligation applies to the offering itself, not to its substitute. One does not transgress *bal tacher* with respect to a substitute. The second: *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit* — "the sin will be in you and not in your *korban*." If one transgressed *bal tacher*, the *korban* itself remains valid. We will examine both principles in turn.

### Chilufim Definition

What is a *chilufo* — a substitute? The Gemara clarifies: it is an animal sanctified as a replacement for a lost or defective offering. One sanctifies animal A as an *olah*, it is lost, one sanctifies animal B in its place, and then animal A is found. Animal B was the substitute — the *chilufo* — of animal A.

### Olah/Shelamim Substitutes

If the original offering was an *olah* or *shelamim*, a substitute can be brought — the substitute animal is a valid *korban* that may be offered. One might ask: if the substitute can be brought, should one not be *over* on *bal tacher* for failing to bring it within three *regalim*? The Baraita seems to say one is not.

### Chatat Substitutes

If the original offering was a *chatat*, the situation is different: a *chatat* whose owner was atoned for through another offering must be left to die — *le-mitah azla*. If the substitute cannot be brought at all, there is obviously no *bal tacher* to transgress. The *bal tacher* presupposes a *korban* that ought to be brought and has not been.

### Gemara's Challenge

The Gemara presses: *ella mai chilufav* — so what exactly is the case where the verse needs to tell us there is no *bal tacher* on the substitute? If the substitute can be brought, one ought to be *over*; if it cannot be brought, of course one is not *over* — that is self-evident, just as with a *chatat* that dies. What is the scenario where a verse is required to exclude the substitute from *bal tacher*?

### Todah/Substitute Case

Rav Sheshes answers: *chilufei todah* — a substitute of a *todah* thanksgiving offering. The relevant teaching is from Rav Chiyya: if a *todah* became mixed with its *tmurah* (or its substitute, the animal sanctified in its place after the original was lost), and one of the two animals then died, the surviving animal has no remedy — *chavertah ein lah takkanah*. Why? A *todah* is brought together with forty loaves of bread; the substitute animal does not come with bread. If you do not know which animal is the *todah* and which is the substitute, you are in an impossible position: bring it with bread, and perhaps it is the substitute — *chametz* would be brought in the *Azarah* without authorization; bring it without bread, and perhaps it is the *todah* — which requires the bread as part of its offering. With both animals present, one could bring both along with the forty loaves and declare: whichever is the *todah*, these loaves are connected to it. But when one animal has died and only the other remains, this solution is no longer available. The surviving animal cannot be brought.

So here is the case: the surviving animal is theoretically a valid *korban* — it is either a *todah* or a *tmurah/chilufo* — but in practice it cannot be offered. This is not the same as a *chatat she-metah* where the animal dies and there is nothing left. Here there *is* an animal, and one might think that because the animal exists and could theoretically be a *todah* requiring bringing, one might be *over* on *bal tacher* for not bringing it. The verse therefore teaches: *lo te'acher leshalmo — hu ve-lo chilufo* — the substitute is excluded. Since the animal standing before you might be the *chilufo*, and the *chilufo* is excluded, you are not *over* on *bal tacher*.

## Chilufim/Defects

### Mum Transfer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָבַר עָלָיו רֶגֶל אֶחָד עוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּמַם בְּתוֹךְ הָרֶגֶל, וְחִילְּלוֹ, וְעָבַר עָלָיו הָרֶגֶל. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִכֹּחַ קַמָּא קָאָתֵי — כְּמַאן דְּעָבַר עֲלֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ רֶגֶל דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This answers the question of which substitute the *baraita* is referring to according to the opinion of the Rabbis, **but** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: Once** even **the first Festival has passed one transgresses** the mitzva: **You shall not delay, what is there to say** here? **Rava said: Here, we are dealing with** a case **where** the original animal **became blemished during the Festival, and one redeemed it** by replacing it with another animal, **and the Festival passed** without that animal being sacrificed. In that case, **it might enter your mind to say** that **since** this second animal **comes in place of the first** one, and the first one had already been consecrated before the Festival, **it** should be **considered as one for which an entire Festival has** already **passed,** so that he transgresses the prohibition against delaying; therefore, the verse **teaches us** that this is not the case. Rather, an entire Festival must pass for the replacement animal.

The Gemara now examines a different scenario involving substitutes and defective offerings. Suppose an animal sanctified as an *olah* or *shelamim* develops a *mum* — a disqualifying blemish — during the period between the *regalim*. A blemished animal cannot be offered as a *korban*, but one is permitted to transfer the sanctity: one redeems the blemished animal through money and uses that money to purchase and sanctify a new animal. The question is how this transfer of sanctity affects the *bal tacher* clock.

### Rava on Mum Mid-Holiday

According to the majority view, the scenario is readily analyzed: the original animal had passed through two *regalim*, developed a *mum*, and the sanctity was transferred to a new animal before the third *regel*. The new animal then passes through one *regel* without being brought. Has one transgressed *bal tacher*? The question is whether the new animal should be treated as a continuation of the same *kedushah* — in which case the three *regalim* have effectively passed — or whether the clock resets with the new animal.

Rava explains the case as it applies according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that *bal tacher* is transgressed after even a single *regel*. According to Rabbi Meir, there would seem to be no scenario where the transfer of sanctity matters: if the *regel* has already passed, one is already *over* on *bal tacher* on the original animal; if the *regel* has not yet passed, then transferring the sanctity mid-interval simply means the new animal will face whatever *regel* comes next, just like any other newly sanctified *korban*. What difference does the exchange make?

Rava's answer: the case involves a *mum* that developed in the middle of a *regel* — say, on the third day of Pesach — with the sanctity transferred to a new animal before Pesach ends. The first animal did not pass through the complete *regel*; the transfer occurred mid-*regel*. The new animal likewise did not receive its sanctity at the start of the *regel* but only mid-way through. The question is: if the new animal fails to be brought on that same Pesach, is it as though a complete *regel* passed over it — since taken together with the first animal the full *regel* was spanned — or does the clock reset to zero with the new animal's sanctification?

### Clock Resets

The Gemara teaches: *ka mashma lan* — the verse comes to tell us that the clock resets. The new animal is treated as freshly sanctified from the moment the *kedushah* was transferred to it. Even though the *kedushah* derives from the original animal (*mikach kama ka-ati*), one does not say the new animal is treated as though the entire *regel* passed over it. *Bal tacher* requires a complete *regel* to pass over *this* animal, and this animal only received its sanctity partway through.

### Neder Vs Nedavah

This scenario also illustrates a conceptual distinction between *neder* and *nedavah*. If one said *harei alai olah* — "I accept upon myself to bring an *olah*" — then the *bal tacher* question centers on the person: has the vow-maker fulfilled his obligation? The focus is on the individual's unrealized undertaking. But if one said *harei zo olah* — "this animal is an *olah*" — the focus shifts entirely to the animal: has this specific animal been offered within three *regalim*? When the *kedushah* is transferred to a new animal, that new animal has not yet had three *regalim* pass over it, and therefore there is no *bal tacher*. This contrast between *neder* and *nedavah* — between the obligation resting on the person versus the sanctity resting on the object — is essential to understanding how *bal tacher* operates across different categories.

## Ve-hayah Becha Cheit

### Sin in Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיָה בְּךָ חֵטְא״, וְלֹא בְּקׇרְבָּנְךָ חֵטְא. וְהָא מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מִדַּאֲחֵרִים נָפְקָא, דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: יָכוֹל יְהֵא בְּכוֹר שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתוֹ כִּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, וְיִפָּסֵל — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ תִּירוֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכוֹרוֹת בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ״, מַקִּישׁ בְּכוֹר לְמַעֲשֵׂר: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ, אַף בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the *baraita*: The verse states: **"And it would be sin in you,"** which teaches that the sin of delaying would be sin in you, **but there would be no sin in your offering,** i.e., the offering would not become disqualified due to the delay. The Gemara asks: **Is it from here that this is learned?** But **isn't it derived** from the statement **of *Aḥerim*? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say** that one **might** have thought **that a firstborn** animal **after its** first **year passed,** during which time it was not sacrificed, **should be like consecrated things that have become disqualified** due to a blemish, **and** so **it is disqualified** from being brought to the altar. Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God,** in the place which He shall choose to place His name there, **the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborns of your herds, and of your flocks"** (Deuteronomy 14:23), thereby **juxtaposing a firstborn** animal **to the tithe** of grain. **Just as tithe is not disqualified** by being kept over **from one year to the next,** as it is explicitly stated that tithes may be eaten until the end of three years, **so too, a firstborn** animal **is not disqualified** by being kept over **from one year to the next,** despite the delay in being brought to the altar. Therefore, there is another source for the *halakha* that the offering itself does not become disqualified even if it is brought late.

The Baraita's second concluding principle: *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit* — the sin of *bal tacher* inheres in the owner, but the *korban* itself is not rendered invalid. Even if one transgressed by delaying too long, the offering may still be brought and is still accepted.

### Bechor/Maaser Parallel

The Gemara notes that this principle is also derived from a Baraita attributed to Rabbi Meir — referenced as *Acherim*, others, a name used for Rabbi Meir in certain contexts. The Baraita asks: perhaps a *bechor* that was not brought within its first year should be treated like disqualified *hekdesh* and become invalid? The verse answers: *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ma'asar degancha tiroshcha ve-yitzharecha u-vechorot bekarcha ve-tzonacha* — you shall eat before the Lord your God your *ma'aser* of grain, wine, and oil, and the *bechorot* of your cattle and flocks. The verse links *bechor* to *ma'aser* — *makish bechor le-ma'aser*. Just as *ma'aser* can be given in a later year (the Torah explicitly contemplates a three-year clearing cycle, demonstrating that *ma'aser* from one year can be distributed more than a year late), so too *bechor* is not invalidated by late bringing. This implies that the *korban*, more generally, is not rendered *pasul* by the transgression of *bal tacher*.

### Deriving from Bechor

The Gemara raises the question: given this derivation from *bechor* and *ma'aser*, is the verse *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit* needed at all? Could we not derive the validity of a late-brought *korban* from the *bechor/ma'aser* analogy alone?

### Bechor Not Ratzon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּכוֹר, דְּלָאו בַּר הַרְצָאָה הוּא, אֲבָל קׇדָשִׁים, דִּבְנֵי הַרְצָאָה נִינְהוּ — אֵימָא לָא לִירַצּוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The first derivation cited **was necessary.** Had this *halakha* been learned only from the case of a firstborn animal, **it might enter your mind to say** that **this** *halakha* that the offering is not disqualified **applies** only to **a firstborn, which is not for appeasement,** i.e., it does not come to atone for any sin, not even for the neglect of a positive mitzva, but is merely a gift for the priest. **But** as for other **consecrated** animals, **which appease,** their role being to atone for the sins of their owners, one might **say** that **they do not appease** when brought late. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that this is not so. Rather, the other offerings are also not disqualified when brought late.

The Gemara answers: *itzterich* — both derivations are necessary. One might have argued that the *bechor/ma'aser* analogy proves the point only for *bechor* itself. Why? Because *bechor* is fundamentally a gift — *lav bar hartza'ah hu*, it is not oriented toward *hartza'ah*, the seeking of divine favor. Rashi explains that *bechor* is something one gives as a donation; its bringing is not primarily about achieving *ratzon*, divine acceptance and favor.

### Sanctification Vs Connec…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּכוֹר, דְּלָאו בַּר הַרְצָאָה הוּא, אֲבָל קׇדָשִׁים, דִּבְנֵי הַרְצָאָה נִינְהוּ — אֵימָא לָא לִירַצּוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאַכַּתִּי, מִדְּבֶן עַזַּאי נָפְקָא, דְּתַנְיָא: בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: ״אוֹתוֹ״ לֹא יֵרָצֶה, וְאֵין מְאַחֵר נִדְרוֹ בְּ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The first derivation cited **was necessary.** Had this *halakha* been learned only from the case of a firstborn animal, **it might enter your mind to say** that **this** *halakha* that the offering is not disqualified **applies** only to **a firstborn, which is not for appeasement,** i.e., it does not come to atone for any sin, not even for the neglect of a positive mitzva, but is merely a gift for the priest. **But** as for other **consecrated** animals, **which appease,** their role being to atone for the sins of their owners, one might **say** that **they do not appease** when brought late. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that this is not so. Rather, the other offerings are also not disqualified when brought late. The Gemara asks further: **But still,** it may be argued that this derivation is unnecessary, as this *halakha* **is derived from** that **which ben Azzai** said, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Azzai says:** The verse states: "And if any of the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace-offering be eaten at all on the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be imputed to him who offers **it,** it shall be *piggul*" (Leviticus 7:18). For **what** purpose does **the verse state** the word "it"? **Since** elsewhere **it states:** "When you shall vow a vow to the Lord your God, **you shall not delay paying it;** for the Lord your God will surely require it from you, and it would be sin in you" (Deuteronomy 23:22), **I might have interpreted** from this verse that **even one who is late** in paying **his vow** is included in: **It shall not be accepted.** Therefore, **the verse states "it." It,** an offering disqualified by improper intention [*piggul*], **is** included in the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted," but** the animal of **one who is late** in paying **his vow is not** included **in** the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted."**

Other *kodashim* — *olah*, *shelamim*, *chatat* — are fundamentally different in character. These *korbanot* are oriented toward connection with God; there is an inherent element of *le-ratzon*, of being desired and accepted. The Torah frequently describes these offerings in terms of *hartza'ah* — the *korban* "comes before God" in a way that involves divine reception and favor. *Bechor* and *ma'aser*, by contrast, are fundamentally sanctifications: one separates what belongs to God and gives it over. What does one do with the *ma'aser*? Give it to the *Leviyim*, give it to the *aniyim*. The primary religious act is the separation, the acknowledgment that this portion belongs to God. It is not the vehicle through which one achieves *ratzon* or connection. Therefore, even if one grants that *bechor* is not disqualified by late bringing, one might argue that *olah* and *shelamim* — *korbanot* oriented toward *ratzon* and divine acceptance — perhaps should be: perhaps a *korban* brought after *bal tacher* is transgressed cannot achieve *ratzon*, and therefore cannot be *oleh le-shem chovah* for the owner.

There is an additional consideration that pushes toward this concern. The concept of *mitzvah haba'ah be-aveirah* — a *mitzvah* fulfilled through a transgression — might suggest that the delayed *korban* itself is tainted. If the very act of bringing it now involves, in some sense, the *aveirah* of having waited, perhaps the *ratzon* dimension of the *korban* is compromised. This is precisely the worry the verse *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit* comes to address.

### Ben Azzai Pigul

The Gemara adds a teaching of Ben Azzai that makes this necessity even clearer. The Gemara notes that one might think the verse *lo te'acher leshalmo* is also needed to establish that a *bal tacher* offering is not *ratzon*. Ben Azzai teaches: the verse by *pigul* — an offering invalidated through improper intent during the *avodah* — states *ha-makriv oto lo yeichashev lo, pigul yiheyeh*: it will not be desired. One might reason: just as *pigul* involves a thought that pervades the act of offering and renders it *lo yeiratzeh*, not desired, perhaps *bal tacher* similarly renders the eventual *korban lo yeiratzeh*? The verse therefore states *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit*: the *cheit* — the wrongdoing — remains in the person, but the *korban* itself is entirely unblemished. Not only is it *kasher* in the technical sense; it retains the full dimension of *ratzon*. *Oto lo yeratzeh* — only *that* (*pigul*) is not desired; *ve-ein me'acher nidro b'lo yeiratzeh* — one who delays his vow does not thereby make his *korban* undesired.

The question of what *ve-hayah becha cheit ve-lo be-korbancha cheit* comes to add, given that these principles might seem derivable from the *bechor/ma'aser* analogy and from Ben Azzai's teaching, will carry us into the next daf.
