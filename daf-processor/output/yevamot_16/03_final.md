# Yevamot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tarmod & Converts

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְקַבְּלִים גֵּרִים מִן הַקַּרְדּוֹיִים וְהַתַּרְמוֹדִים.
>
> **Translation:** Lastly, Haggai testified: **And one accepts converts from the Karduyin and the Tarmodim,** without concern that there might be Jews mingled among them, which could render them *mamzerim* and prohibited from entering the community.

Masechet Yevamot is not exclusively about *yibum*; it is equally concerned with questions of personal status in the broadest sense. Daf 16 plunges directly into some of the most central and consequential of those questions — who is Jewish, what is the status of children born of mixed unions, and what communities carry with them a taint of *mamzerut* or compromised lineage. These themes, introduced here in a relatively compressed passage, serve as a genuine precursor to major discussions that will recur throughout the tractate.

### Haggai's Testimony

The Gemara had already, in the preceding amud, introduced the testimony of Haggai haNavi on two matters: first, that *tzarat habat* is *muteret*, in accordance with the position of Beit Hillel; and second, that one may accept *geirim* from the peoples of Tarmod and Karduyim. This second ruling implies that the residents of those places are presumed to be non-Jews — which is itself significant, because the alternative fear was that they might be Jews who are *mamzerim*. If they were *mamzerim*, the problem would be irresolvable: they would not need *geirus*, yet they could not marry into the community. The testimony of Haggai thus served to relieve that concern and permit the acceptance of converts from those locales.

### R. Yochanan Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָפָסֵיק וְתָנֵי: מִבֵּין הַגּוֹיִם, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִן תַּרְמוֹד. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת מְקַבְּלִין גֵּרִים מִתַּרְמוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* **concluded and taught:** All stains from **among the gentiles** are pure, from which it may be inferred: **And even from Tarmod.** This indicates that in all places inhabited by gentiles there is no need to be concerned about stains. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That is to say, one accepts converts from Tarmod** without concern that they might be Jewish, albeit *mamzerim*. This contradicts the previous opinion attributed to Rabbi Yoḥanan that converts from the Tarmodim are not accepted.

The Gemara had noted an apparent contradiction in the statements of Rabbi Yochanan regarding whether one may or may not accept converts from Tarmod. That tension sets the stage for the present analysis, which now investigates what the underlying problems with Tarmod actually were.

## Mamzeirut Issue

### Tarmod Problems

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתַּרְמוֹד מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְסָבַיָּא, חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בְּנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that the Sages do **not** accept converts **from Tarmod? Rabbi Yoḥanan and the Elders disagree** about **this** matter. Although they concur that converts from Tarmod are not accepted, they disagree with regard to the reason. **One** of them **said** that it is **due to the servants of** King **Solomon.** Solomon built a city in Tarmod (see I Kings 9:18), and his gentile servants, taking advantage of their status and power, married Jewish women unlawfully. Therefore, it is possible that the inhabitants of Tarmod and their descendants are *mamzerim*. **And** the other **one said** that it is **due to the daughters of Jerusalem,** who were taken captive and raped and gave birth to children among the gentiles.

The Gemara asks: *MiTarmod mai ta'ama lo?* — what is the concern that would prevent accepting converts from Tarmod? Rabbi Yochanan and Savya, a rare *amora*, dispute the matter. One says the problem stems from *avdei Shlomo* — the servants of Solomon — who apparently intermarried with women from Tarmod. The other says the issue is *benot Yerushalayim* — the young women of Jerusalem, whose fate during the destruction of the Beit HaMikdash we will examine shortly.

The Gemara's initial framing already seems puzzling: if these women had Jewish mothers, why should there be any problem? The children of a Jewish mother are Jewish regardless of the father's lineage — or so we would ordinarily say. The nature of the problem only becomes clear once the Gemara introduces a startlingly radical opinion.

### Non-Jew = Mamzer?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, קָסָבַר: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** that it is **due to the servants of Solomon,** this is logical, as **he holds** that in the case of **a gentile or a slave who had intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.** Accordingly, as the servants of Solomon were slaves and they engaged in intercourse with Jewish women, their children are considered *mamzerim*.

The Gemara explains the approach of those troubled by *avdei Shlomo* as follows: *kasavar oved kochavim ve'eved haba al bat Yisrael havlad mamzer* — the child born of a non-Jew or a slave who has relations with a Jewish woman is a *mamzer*. This is a profoundly counterintuitive position. We are accustomed to saying that matrilineal descent settles the matter: if the mother is Jewish, the child is Jewish, full stop. Yet here is an opinion that this child is not merely Jewish, but Jewish and a *mamzer*.

It should be emphasized at the outset that we do not rule this way. But the opinion exists in the Gemara, and its implications are striking.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot, in the *dibur hamatchil* *kasavar*, takes up the challenge of explaining this opinion and raises a pointed difficulty. The first possible explanation is that this position follows Rabbi Akiva's view that *mamzerut* results from any violation of a negative prohibition — *mamzer mechayavei lavin*. A union with a slave carries a biblical *lav*, and thus the child would be a *mamzer* according to Rabbi Akiva. But the case of a non-Jewish man is more complicated. On the one hand, such a union is treated with the utmost gravity — communities would sit *shiva* for a child who intermarried — yet the technical biblical prohibition of sexual relations with a non-Jew may in fact be only rabbinic, at least when the non-Jew is not from one of the *shiva amamim*, the seven Canaanite nations. Relations in public invoke *kana'im pog'in bo*, as in the story of Pinchas — but that applies specifically to the public act. In private, if there is no biblical *lav*, why would the child be a *mamzer*?

Tosafot offers one answer: perhaps the *mamzerut* is only *miderabbanan*. But he then develops a second, more penetrating answer, which reframes the entire question. Moving to the passage beginning *ve'im tomar*, Tosafot writes: granted that for the case of a slave there is a *lav*, making sense of *mamzerut* along Rabbi Akiva's lines; but for a non-Jew, where relations may be *bias heter* from a biblical standpoint in private — *mikol makom*, since *kiddushin* cannot take effect with a non-Jew (*keivan delo tafsi bah kiddushin havay mamzer, kemo be'eved*) — the child is a *mamzer* in a sense that has nothing to do with the weight of the transgression at all.

This is a fundamental reconceptualization of *mamzerut*. The standard understanding is that *mamzerut* results from a prohibited union — a *lav* or a *karet* relationship. But Tosafot here articulates an alternative framework: *mamzerut* may be a function not of the severity of the transgression but of the impossibility of *kiddushin* taking effect. In all cases of *issurei karet*, *kiddushin* does not take effect. Here too, with a non-Jew, *kiddushin* does not take effect — not because of a weighty transgression, but because the Torah does not recognize such a *kiddushin*. And in this sense, the union with a non-Jew resembles the *karet* category structurally, even if not in terms of the gravity of the sin.

This framework also resolves an apparent paradox: a union that is *issur karet*, namely *nidda*, does not produce a *mamzer* — because *kiddushin* could take effect in that relationship and indeed may even be present. Conversely, a union with no biblical prohibition whatsoever can, according to this view, produce a *mamzer* — because *kiddushin* is categorically impossible. The determining factor is not transgression but the impossibility of marital bond.

Again, we do not rule this way. The *halakhah* is that the child of a Jewish mother and a non-Jewish father is simply Jewish, without any taint of *mamzerut*. The practical stakes are significant: *mamzerut* cannot be solved, whereas questions of Jewish identity — say, where one parent was not Jewish — can be resolved through conversion. That said, it is worth noting that even according to our normative position, the question of *pagum le'kehunah* — whether such a child is *pasul* to marry a *kohen* — is not entirely without discussion.

### Patrilineal Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: גּוֹי שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְקִדּוּשִׁין, שֶׁמָּא מֵעֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים הוּא. וְהָא כֹּל דְּפָרֵישׁ — מֵרוּבָּא פָּרֵישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said:** With regard to **a gentile who betrothed** a Jewish woman **nowadays, we are concerned that the betrothal** might be valid, despite the fact that a betrothal of a gentile is meaningless, **lest he be from the ten tribes** of Israel who intermingled with the gentiles. The Gemara raises an objection: **But** there is an important principle in *halakha* that **any** item **separated,** i.e., not fixed in its place, is presumed to have been **separated from the majority.** In this case, it can be assumed that any individual singled out from the gentiles belongs to the majority of gentiles and has no Jewish roots at all.

An even more shocking possibility now emerges. The Gemara shifts to discussing the case of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav Assi: *oved kochavim shekidesh b'zman hazeh chosheshin l'kiddushin shema me'aseret shevatim hu* — a non-Jew who betroths a Jewish woman nowadays, we are concerned that the *kiddushin* may be binding, lest this person turns out to be a descendant of the ten tribes and is therefore Jewish. The Gemara asks: surely we follow the principle that anyone who separates from a group is assumed to have come from the majority — and the majority of non-Jews are genuinely non-Jews? The Gemara answers that the case is specifically in places where we know the ten tribes were exiled, citing Rabbi Abba bar Kahana's identification of the locations — *Chalach* is *Chalazon*, *Chavor* is *Chadyav*, *Nehar Gozan* is *Ginzak*, and *arei Madai* is *Chemdan*.

One might assume that the concern about these people being descendants of the ten tribes is a concern about matrilineal descent — perhaps a great-great-great-grandmother all the way back through the maternal line was Jewish, and therefore the concern is that there is an unbroken chain of Jewish mothers. That is indeed one way of reading the Gemara, and it is ultimately what Tosafot concludes.

But Rashi reads the Gemara differently, and his reading is genuinely startling. In the passage beginning *measeret shevatim hu* (toward the bottom of 17b), Rashi explains: *shenasu ovdot kochavim* — the Jewish men of the ten tribes married non-Jewish women, and Rashi then states: *dekasavar ovedet kochavim sheyalda mi'Yisrael havlad mamzer vechoshshin lekiddushei mamzer* — there is a view that the child born of a non-Jewish woman from a Jewish father is a *mamzer*, and we are concerned about the *kiddushin* of a *mamzer*. That is: Rashi understands the Gemara to be entertaining the possibility that patrilineal descent — Jewish father, non-Jewish mother — produces not a non-Jew, but a Jew who is a *mamzer*.

Tosafot is understandably troubled. Multiple *mishnayot* establish clearly that Jewishness follows the mother, not the father. How can Rashi be reading this Gemara as suggesting patrilineal descent with *mamzerut*? Tosafot offers two resolutions. The first is that if one accepts the earlier principle — that a Jewish mother and non-Jewish father produce a *mamzer* because *kiddushin* cannot take effect — then perhaps by the same logic the mirror image holds: a Jewish father and non-Jewish mother would also produce a *mamzer*, because that union too admits no possibility of *kiddushin*. In that case, Rashi's reading is coherent, but only within the framework of that radical earlier opinion. Since we do not rule that way in the first case — we say the child is simply Jewish — we certainly do not say so in this direction either; we say the child is simply not Jewish. Tosafot's second resolution, which he treats as more straightforward, is that the concern is simply about matrilineal descent: perhaps these people are descendants of Jewish women from the ten tribes, and therefore the concern is an unbroken maternal line.

The result of this discussion is that two radical positions have now been articulated, both rejected in normative *halakhah*: first, that any union between a Jew and a non-Jew — regardless of which parent is Jewish — produces a child who is both Jewish and a *mamzer*; and second, that there is something akin to patrilineal descent, at least in the sense that the child retains Jewish identity from the father. We do not rule either way. We rule that a Jewish mother produces a Jewish child without any *mamzerut* issue, and a Jewish father with a non-Jewish mother produces a non-Jewish child — also without any *mamzerut* issue.

### Benot Yerushalayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ גּוֹיִם לַהֵיכָל, הַכֹּל נִפְנוּ עַל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב, וְהֵם נִפְנוּ עַל בְּנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָשִׁים בְּצִיּוֹן עִנּוּ בְּתוּלוֹת בְּעָרֵי יְהוּדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** When the gentiles entered the Sanctuary** during the conquest of Jerusalem, they **all turned to** plunder the **silver and the gold** they saw there, **but** the warriors of Tarmod **turned to the daughters of Jerusalem, as it is stated: "They have ravished the women in Zion, the maidens in the cities of Judah"** (Lamentations 5:11). According to the opinion that children born of relations between gentiles and Jewish women are *mamzerim*, all the children born to these women are *mamzerim*.

The Gemara now turns to the second explanation for the concern about Tarmod — *benot Yerushalayim*. The Gemara asks: *mai hi?* What is this about? Rav Yosef and the Rabbis debate, both in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chana. One says 12,000 men and 6,000 archers; the other says 18,000. The substance: when the non-Jews entered the Beit HaMikdash to destroy it at the time of the *churban*, everyone else went for the gold and silver, but this group — whether 12,000 or 18,000 men — *nitnu al benot Yerushalayim*, they went to seize the young women of Jerusalem. As the verse in Eichah says: *nashim be-Tziyon inu, betulot be-arei Yehudah* — the women of Jerusalem were subjected to assault, which is understood as referring to rape.

The *halakhic* question is thus the same one we have already been examining: what is the status of children born from these unions of non-Jewish men and Jewish women? According to one view, entirely unproblematic — the children are Jewish. According to the radical opinion we have discussed, the children would be *mamzerim*. The concern about the descendants of these events thus explains why there was a question about accepting *geirim* from Tarmod, or indeed about the status of people from that region generally.

## Enemy Drasha

### Metatron Passage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, פָּסוּק זֶה שַׂר הָעוֹלָם אֲמָרוֹ: ״נַעַר הָיִיתִי גַּם זָקַנְתִּי״. מַאן אַמְרֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא — מִי אִיכָּא זִקְנָה קַמֵּיהּ? וְאֶלָּא דָּוִד אַמְרֵיהּ — מִי קַשִּׁישׁ כּוּלֵּי הַאי? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שַׂר הָעוֹלָם אֲמָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: This verse was stated by the ministering angel** appointed over **the world: "I have been young, and now am old;** yet I have not seen the righteous forsaken, nor his seed begging bread" (Psalms 37:25). **Who said this? If we say** that it was **the Holy One, Blessed be He, is there old age before Him?** Could God possibly say: "I have been young, and now am old"? **And rather,** one could say that **David** himself **said it,** from his own experience; but **was he indeed so old?** After all, David died at the age of seventy. **Rather, conclude from this** that **the ministering angel** appointed over **the world said it,** as he can speak both of youth and old age, and it is he who reported his observations from around the world.

The Gemara interpolates a *derasha* from Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: the verse *naar hayiti gam zakanti* — "I was a youth and now have grown old" — was said by the *sar ha'olam*, the steward or prince of the world. The strong inclination among commentators is to identify this figure with Metatron, understood as a supreme angelic being or perhaps something beyond that. The Gemara's reasoning is: if it were God who said *gam zakanti*, that is impossible — God would not say He has grown old. And if it were David who said it, David was not so old that the phrase *gam zakanti* would be appropriate. The conclusion is therefore *shema mina sar ha'olam amro*. The precise theological implications of this passage are a matter for deeper exploration; the Gemara itself moves quickly past it to the next and more legally significant *derasha*.

### Sefer Torah Burned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יָדוֹ פָּרַשׂ צָר עַל כׇּל מַחֲמַדֶּיהָ״ — זֶה עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ גּוֹיִם לַהֵיכָל, הַכֹּל נִפְנוּ עַל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב, וְהֵם נִפְנוּ עַל סִפְרֵי תוֹרָה, אָמְרוּ: זֶה שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ ״לֹא יָבֹא עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי בִּקְהַל ה׳״ — יִשָּׂרֵף בָּאֵשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** further **said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The adversary has spread out his hand upon all her treasures;** for she has seen that the heathens have entered into her Temple, concerning which You commanded that they should not enter into Your congregation" (Lamentations 1:10)? **This** is referring to **Ammon and Moab.** How so? **When the gentiles entered the Sanctuary, all turned to** plunder **the silver and the gold, and** the soldiers from Ammon and Moab **turned to the scrolls of Torah** to destroy them. **They said:** Is **this** the scroll **in which it is written: "An Ammonite and a Moabite shall not enter into the assembly of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 23:4)? **Let it be burnt by fire.**

The Gemara then states, again from Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: what is meant by the verse *yado paras tzar al kol machmadeha* — the hand of the enemy stretched out over all its precious things? *Zeh Amon u'Moav* — this refers to Ammon and Moab. When the non-Jews entered the *heichal* to destroy it, everyone else went for the gold and silver, but *heim nitnu al sefer Torah* — they, Ammon and Moab, turned to the Sefer Torah, for the Sefer Torah is *machmadeha*, the most precious and desirous thing of all. Why did they attack the Sefer Torah specifically? *Amru*: because it is written there, *lo yavo Amoni u'Moavi b'kehal Hashem* — Ammon and Moab may not enter the congregation of God. So *Amon u'Moav yisrofu ba'eish* — they burned it. The Maharsha points out a play on words: the word *paras* read backwards spells *saraf* — burned. There is also a verse in Eichah that reads *asher tzivita lo yavo'u b'kahalecha* — those whom You commanded shall not enter Your congregation — which in its plain sense means that enemies came into the Temple, the very ones excluded from the community. The *drasha* sharpens this: the specific enemy who burned the Sefer Torah was Ammon and Moab, precisely those excluded by the text they destroyed.

### Ammon & Moab

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צִוָּה ה׳ לְיַעֲקֹב סְבִיבָיו צָרָיו״, אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן הוּמַנְיָא לְפוּם נַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the Ammonites, the Gemara cites another verse: **"The Lord has commanded against Jacob that they who are round about him should be his adversaries"** (Lamentations 1:17). **Rav said: For an example** of this, there is the city **Homanya** in relation **to** the city **Pum Nahara,** as the descendants of the Ammonites live in Homanya, and they harass the Jews of Pum Nahara.

The verse in Eichah, *tzivah Hashem l'Yaakov sevivav tzarav* — God commanded for Jacob that enemies would surround it — is connected by Rav to cities such as Humanya and Pum Nahara. Rashi notes that Humanya was populated by Greeks and Pum Nahara by Jews, and the Greeks of that city were oppressive toward the Jewish residents. The broader theme is the same: the enemies of Israel and the problem of mixed ancestry and the intertwining of peoples at the time of the *churban* — Ammon and Moab being paradigmatic cases of peoples whose lineage was itself the product of illicit unions, and who were therefore excluded from the *kahal*, and who then sought to destroy the very text that excluded them.

## Matrilineal Descent

### Ten Tribes Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּקְבִיעִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: ״וַיַּנְחֵם בַּחְלַח וּבְחָבוֹר נְהַר גּוֹזָן וְעָרֵי מָדָי״, חֲלַח — זֶה חִלָּזוֹן, וְחָבוֹר — זוֹ חַדְיָיב, ״נְהַר גּוֹזָן״ — זוֹ גִּינְזַק, ״וְעָרֵי מָדַי״ — זוֹ חַמְדָּן וְחַבְרוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav Yehuda means that there is a concern only with regard to those who came from **the permanent** dwelling **places** of the ten tribes. **As Rabbi Abba bar Kahana said** that the verse states about those exiled from Samaria: **"And he put them in Halah, and in Habor, on the river of Gozan, and in the cities of the Medes"** (II Kings 18:11). Rabbi Abba bar Kahana proceeded to identify these places. **Halah; this is** the place called **Ḥalzon. And Habor; this is Ḥadyab. The river of Gozan; this is Ginzak. And the cities of the Medes; this is Ḥamadan and its surroundings.**

The Gemara now returns explicitly to the issue of descendants of the ten tribes. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav Assi: *oved kochavim shekidesh b'zman hazeh chosheshin l'kiddushin shema me'aseret shevatim hu* — if a non-Jew betroths a Jewish woman in our time, we are concerned that his *kiddushin* might be binding, lest he is in fact a descendant of the ten tribes and therefore Jewish. The Gemara objects: do we not ordinarily say that anyone who separates from a group separates from the majority, and since most non-Jews are genuinely non-Jewish, there is no concern? The Gemara answers: *b'duchta d'kviei* — the concern applies specifically in places where the ten tribes are known to have been settled. The locations are identified by Rabbi Abba bar Kahana through a verse: *vayanaichem b'Chalach u'vChavor nehar Gozan v'arei Madai* — *Chalach* is *Chalazon*, *Chavor* is *Chadyav*, *Nehar Gozan* is *Ginzak*, and *arei Madai* is *Chemdan*. An alternative tradition identifies the area as Nehar and its surrounding cities, which Shmuel specifies as Moshchei, Chidkei, and Dumkaya.

At this point the question becomes more nuanced. If we are worried that a person might be a descendant of the ten tribes, what exactly are we picturing? Are we assuming that men and women of the ten tribes married only amongst themselves and descended in a pure line? Or are we allowing for intermarriage, in which case the concern would be about someone whose maternal great-great-great-grandmother was Jewish?

Tosafot ultimately explains that the concern tracks matrilineal descent: perhaps there is an unbroken chain of Jewish mothers, generation after generation, from the exiled Jewish women of the ten tribes through to the present day. That is the straightforward reading.

But as noted above, Rashi reads the Gemara differently — understanding the case as involving Jewish fathers with non-Jewish mothers, with the concern that such a child is Jewish and a *mamzer*. That reading is, as Tosafot observes, deeply difficult to reconcile with the standard *halakhah* established in numerous *mishnayot* that Jewish identity follows the mother. The resolution in Tosafot's first approach is that if one accepts the framework in which any Jew-plus-non-Jew union produces *mamzerut* (in either direction), then Rashi's reading is internally coherent — but since we reject that framework entirely, we are left with the simpler reading that the concern is about matrilineal descent.

### Shmuel's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לִי: בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן יִשְׂרְאֵלִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, וְאֵין בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן הַגּוֹיָה קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, אֶלָּא בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda continued his recounting: **When I said this** *halakha*, that there is a concern about the betrothal of gentiles nowadays, **before Shmuel, he said to me:** One need not worry about this, as **your son from a Jewish woman is called your son,** i.e., he inherits his lineage from you, **and your son from a gentile woman is not called your son,** but **rather her son.** Consequently, all children born to Jews from gentile women are not considered Jews, as their lineage is determined by their gentile mothers.

The tradition continues: *ki amritah kamei d'Shmuel amar li* — when Rav Yehuda reported Rav Assi's ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel responded in the clearest possible terms. *Bincha haba min hayisraelit karuy bincha* — your son who comes from a Jewish woman is called your son. *Ve'ein bincha haba min ha'ovedet kochavim karuy bincha ella b'nah* — but your son who comes from a non-Jewish woman is not called your son; he is called her son. This is the definitive articulation of matrilineal descent. The child of a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman is not Jewish — he is her son, not yours, in the sense that Jewish identity does not pass through you in that case.

This response also clarifies why the earlier Gemara was concerned about non-Jews in those places: if the concern had been about Jewish fathers (as Rashi read), Shmuel now rules that the children of those Jewish fathers are simply not Jewish, and there is therefore no concern at all. If the non-Jews there are descended from Jewish men who married non-Jewish women, their children are not Jewish, period.

### Scripture & Inheritance

The Gemara proceeds to identify the scriptural basis for this principle. The relevant verse is in Devarim 7: *lo titchaten bam, bitcha lo titen livno u'vito lo tikach livnecha* — do not intermarry with them; do not give your daughter to his son, and do not take his daughter for your son. The structure of the verse is telling: the daughter always follows the man she is with. The reason given is *ki yasir et bincha me'achai* — because he will turn your son away from Me.

The grammatical problem here is significant. *Ki yasir* is in the masculine — *he* will turn away. Who is the male who will turn your son away? If it were the non-Jewish daughter who leads your son astray, the verse should read *ki tasir et bincha*, using the feminine. If it were the non-Jewish man leading your daughter astray, it should read *ki yasir et bitcha*. But *ki yasir et bincha* — a male turning away your *son* — what does that mean?

The Rabbis read it as follows: *bincha* here does not mean your son but your grandson. When a non-Jewish man marries a Jewish woman, that non-Jew will lead your grandson — who will be Jewish through the Jewish mother — away from God. That grandson is called *bincha* because he is indeed Jewish through his mother. By contrast, when a Jewish man's daughter marries a non-Jewish man's son, there is no such concern — because the child of that union (Jewish father, non-Jewish mother) follows the mother and is not Jewish, so there is no Jewish grandson to be led astray. The Torah therefore does not say *ki yasir et bincha* in that direction, because there is no *bincha* — no Jewish grandson — at stake.

This *pasuk* is the textual peg upon which the principle of matrilineal descent is hung. As the Gemara notes, that is a great deal to derive from a single *yud* — the difference between the masculine *yasir* and the feminine *tasir* — but that is indeed what the *drashah* is built upon.

### Ravina's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא בָּנוֹת, וְאָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בֶּן בִּתְּךָ הַבָּא מִן הַגּוֹי קָרוּי בִּנְךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Aren't there** Jewish **girls** who were captured by gentiles, whose children are considered to be Jews? **And Ravina said: Learn from this** that **the son of your daughter from a gentile is called your son.**

Ravina adds: *shema mina ben bitcha haba min oved kochavim karuy bincha* — we learn from this that the son of your daughter who was born from a non-Jewish man is still called your son. In other words, a Jewish daughter who has a child with a non-Jewish man produces a child who is Jewish — and that child is considered your descendant (*bincha*) through the maternal line. This is implicit in the Shmuel formula already stated, but Ravina makes it explicit: matrilineal descent means that your daughter's child is Jewish regardless of the father, and that child is in a real sense your son.

### Infertile Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמִירִי דִּבְנָתָא דְּהָהוּא דָּרָא אִיצְטְרוֹיֵי אִצְטְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is no concern, as it **is learned** as a tradition **that the girls** from the ten tribes **of that generation became barren** and did not give birth to any offspring, whereas some of the exiled men of the ten tribes married gentile women. Consequently, all of the children born there were gentiles.

If the concern about the ten tribes could still be answered by saying: well, perhaps these people are descendants through an unbroken maternal line from Jewish women of the ten tribes — the Gemara closes that avenue with a tradition. *Gemiri* — we have received a tradition — *debanshei dehahu dara itztaruy itztaru* — the women of the generation exiled with the ten tribes became infertile; their wombs were somehow rendered incapable of bearing children. If that tradition is correct, then no descendants trace back to the Jewish women of the ten tribes. Any descendants of those exiled communities trace only through Jewish men, and since Jewish men with non-Jewish women produce non-Jewish children, all of those descendants are simply non-Jews. According to this Gemara, there are no remaining Jewish descendants of the ten tribes at all — a remarkable and sobering conclusion that stands in some tension with both popular imagination and certain *responsa* literature on communities like the Ethiopian Jews.

### Assimilation View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם, עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאוּם גּוֹיִם גְּמוּרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּה׳ בָּגָדוּ כִּי בָנִים זָרִים יָלָדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that Rav Yehuda actually related the following: **When I said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said to me: They did not move from there,** the place where they deliberated on this matter, **until they rendered** all of **them,** including those who intermingled with the ten tribes in different locations, **full-fledged gentiles.** Consequently, there is no concern that their betrothals might be of any effect, **as it is stated: "They have dealt treacherously against the Lord, for they have begotten strange children"** (Hosea 5:7).

An alternative version of the tradition is also recorded. Some say that when Rav Yehuda reported Rav Assi's ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel responded differently: *lo zazu misham ad she-asa'um ovdei kochavim gemurim* — they did not leave those places until they had been made into complete non-Jews. The verse cited is from Hoshea: *ba-Hashem bagadu ki banim zarim yaldu* — they betrayed God, for they bore foreign, non-Jewish children. Whether this verse refers to deliberate assimilation that caused the ten tribes to lose their Jewish status as a matter of communal or rabbinic ruling, or whether it is simply another way of expressing that all children born of those communities were non-Jewish by matrilineal reckoning, is not entirely clear. The listener who noted that the verse in Hoshea is prophetic rather than descriptive — that it depicts intermarriage before the exile rather than after — raises an interesting point about the original context of the verse, though its application here in the Gemara is as described.

Either way, the upshot is the same: there is no viable concern that a non-Jew from those regions today is actually a Jewish descendant of the ten tribes. The door is closed.

## Yichus Cities

### Rav Hamnuna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּעוּלָּא וְקָא הָוֵי בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא, אָמַר: מָה גַּבְרָא וּמָה גַּבְרָא! אִי לָאו דְּהַרְפַּנְיָא מָאתֵיהּ! אִכְּסִיף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּסַף גֻּלְגֻּלְתָּא, לְהֵיכָא יָהֲבַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְפוּם נַהֲרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, מִפּוּם נַהֲרָא אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Hamnuna sat before Ulla and was engaged in** the study of ***halakha*.** Ulla **said** about him: **What a man; what a man.** In other words: What a great man this Rav Hamnuna is. **If only Harpanya were not his city,** as the inhabitants of that place are all of flawed lineage, which indicates that the lineage of Rav Hamnuna is likewise impaired. Rav Hamnuna **was ashamed.** Ulla **said to him: Where do you give the money for** payment of the **poll** tax? **He said to him:** I pay it **to** the city of **Pum Nahara,** as my city is subject to taxation by that city. **He said to him: If so, you are from Pum Nahara,** not Harpanya, and your lineage is evidently not flawed.

The Gemara transitions to a series of vivid vignettes about cities of questionable lineage. Rav Hamnuna was sitting before Ulla, and Ulla was posing challenging questions in the course of their learning. Rav Hamnuna, impressed, exclaimed: *ma gavra* — what a man! And then, in what he presumably intended as a compliment with an unfortunate addendum: *u'ma gavra ilav deHarfanya ma'aseh* — and how much greater this man would be if he were not from Harfanya. Apparently, Harfanya was a city known for its questionable *yichus* — not necessarily outright *mamzerut*, but lineage that was questioned enough that others would not want to intermarry with its residents.

The remark caused Ulla to be *ichsif* — embarrassed. Ulla then asked, with a play on the words *ichsif* and *kesef*: *kesef gulgulta le'heika yahavas* — where do you pay your poll tax? Rav Hamnuna replied that he paid it to Pum Nahara. Ulla immediately said: *im ken mi-Pum Nahara at* — in that case, you're from Pum Nahara. Since Pum Nahara was a Jewish city of unquestioned lineage — indeed, we encountered it earlier as a Jewish city oppressed by the neighboring Greek city of Humanya — Rav Hamnuna's payment of taxes there indicated that he was affiliated with Pum Nahara, not truly of Harfanya. The embarrassment was thereby mitigated: Rav Hamnuna was not casting aspersions on a resident of Harfanya; he was speaking about a city without reference to his interlocutor's actual community.

### Harfanya City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הַרְפַּנְיָא? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַר שֶׁהַכֹּל פּוֹנִים בּוֹ. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: כׇּל שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ וְשִׁבְטוֹ נִפְנֶה לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the name **Harpanya? Rabbi Zeira said:** It means **the mountain to which all turn [*har shehakol ponin bo*].** In other words, it is the destination for all those who could not find wives anywhere else, as most of its population is of flawed lineage. **It is taught in a *baraita*: All those who do not know their family or tribe turn there.**

The Gemara asks: *mai Harfanya?* What does this city's name mean? Rav Zeira offers a *drash*: *har she-kol ponim bo* — a mountain, or city, that everyone turns toward, in the sense that all *psulim*, all those with questionable parentage, migrate there. When no one else will marry you, when your lineage is in doubt, you gravitate toward a community of similarly situated people. A *Beraisa* reinforces this: *kol she-ein makir mishpachto ve-shivto nifneh le-sham* — anyone who does not know his family or his tribe ends up there.

This is a striking social portrait. Even outside the specific legal category of *mamzerut*, the intense focus on *yichus* and known ancestry meant that people with uncertain lineage were effectively shut out of normal communal life and marriage. They congregated in cities like Harfanya, creating communities defined by their exclusion from others.

### Hierarchy of Defect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְהִיא עֲמוּקָּה מִשְּׁאוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִיַּד שְׁאוֹל אֶפְדֵּם מִמָּוֶת אֶגְאָלֵם״, וְאִילּוּ פְּסוּל דִּידְהוּ לֵית לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא. פְּסוּלֵי דְהַרְפַּנְיָא מִשּׁוּם פְּסוּלֵי דְּמֵישׁוֹן, וּפְסוּלֵי דְּמֵישׁוֹן מִשּׁוּם פְּסוּלֵי דְתַרְמוֹד, פְּסוּלֵי דְתַרְמוֹד מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: This** type of flaw is appalling and **is deeper than the netherworld, as is stated: "Shall I ransom them from the power of the netherworld? Shall I redeem them from death?"** (Hosea 13:14). This verse indicates that it is possible to be ransomed and released from the netherworld, **whereas their disqualification cannot be rectified. The** Gemara comments: **Those** who are **disqualified from Harpanya** are unfit **due to the disqualification** of the inhabitants **of** nearby **Meishon,** who were unfit and intermarried with the residents of Harpanya. **Those disqualified from Meishon** are unfit **due to those disqualified from Tarmod, and those disqualified from Tarmod** are disqualified **due to the servants of Solomon.**

Rava adds a grim metaphor: *ve-hi amuka mi-she'ol* — Harfanya is deeper than the netherworld. For, as the verse says, *mi-yad she'ol efdem mi-mavet eg'alem* — from the hand of *she'ol*, from death itself, God can redeem. But the invalidity of *Harfanya* — meaning the *mamzerut* that ultimately characterizes its residents — has no remedy. The Gemara then traces the genealogy of the problem. The *psulim* of Harfanya came from those of Meshang; the *psulim* of Meshang came from those of Tarmod; and the *psulim* of Tarmod came from the servants of Solomon who had intermarried with Jewish women. And as the folk saying goes: *kava raba vekava zuta, minda ve'azla lish'ol* — a large measure and a small measure, rolling and going down to *she'ol*. From *she'ol* to Tarmod, from Tarmod to Meshang, from Meshang to Harfanya — everything rolls downhill, and each stop is further from any possibility of redemption.

The image is deliberately bleak, and yet one should perhaps hear in it something more than mere legal taxonomy. These are communities of human beings whose condition the Gemara regards with the gravity it deserves — a gravity that comes precisely from the irreversibility of their situation.

### Mamzeirut Irredeemable

The Gemara makes this explicit: *leit lei takanta* — there is no remedy. Unlike someone who is merely from a city of unknown *yichus*, or even someone caught in *she'ol*, the *mamzer* has no path out. The law of *mamzerut* is absolute and hereditary — a *mamzer* cannot marry into the congregation, and the status passes to subsequent generations. The Gemara acknowledges what the earlier discussion made clear: the Rabbis were deeply aware of this irreversibility and worked to construct frameworks — such as the rule that *safek mamzerim mutar* (those of doubtful *mamzer* status are permitted) — that could offer some mitigation. But those efforts did not change the fundamental reality that genuine *mamzerut* carried with it a permanent exclusion that no remedy could undo.

This passage, taken together with everything that has preceded it on this *daf*, represents what the Gemara presents as a distillation of the major concerns of Masechet Yevamot beyond the specific laws of *yibum*: the definition of Jewish identity, the catastrophic consequences of *mamzerut*, the fierce communal concern with *yichus*, and the question of what happens to communities that have lived at the intersection of Jewish and non-Jewish worlds for generations.

## Eshet Achivu

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ? שְׁנֵי אַחִים, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְנוֹלַד לָהֶן אָח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִיבֵּם הַשֵּׁנִי אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וּמֵת. הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה מִשּׁוּם צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What** is the case of **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist?** For example: If there were **two brothers, and one of them died** childless, **and** subsequently **a brother of theirs was born, after which the second brother,** the elder, **took his** deceased **brother's wife in levirate marriage, and** then **died** as well. Consequently, two women require levirate marriage: The widow of the first brother who had been taken in levirate marriage by the second brother, and the widow of the second brother, the first widow's rival wife. **The first** widow, who had been the wife of the first brother to die, **goes out** without any obligation to be taken in levirate marriage by the youngest brother who was born later, **since** she is **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.** The first deceased brother never lived at the same time as the newly born brother. **The second** widow, who had been married to the second brother, is exempt **due to her rival wife.**

The Gemara now opens the next *perek* with the *mishna* that asks: *keitzad eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo?* — what is the case of the wife of a brother who was not yet alive at the same time? The scenario requires careful tracking. Reuven and Shimon are brothers. Reuven marries Rachel. Reuven then dies. Subsequently, Levi is born. At this point, Shimon — who is still alive — performs *yibum* with Rachel: that is stage three. Then Shimon dies. Now Levi is the surviving brother. What is his relationship to Rachel?

Rachel is *erva* to Levi — specifically, *eshet ach shelo haya be'olamo*, the wife of a brother who was not in existence at the same time as that brother. Because Levi was born after Reuven died, Levi and Reuven were never alive simultaneously, which means Levi has no *mitzvah* of *yibum* with Rachel; she is simply a brother's wife, and therefore *erva*. Shimon was married to Sarah, making Sarah *tzarat erva* — the co-wife of a woman who is *erva* — and therefore Sarah is also exempt from *yibum* and forbidden to Levi.

### Rachel & Sarah Status

The *mishna* therefore rules that Rachel — the first wife in this scenario — is excluded from *yibum* on the grounds of *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo*, and Sarah is excluded as her *tzarat erva*. Rachel stands as *erva* and Sarah as *tzarat erva*. Both are forbidden to Levi: Rachel by direct prohibition, and Sarah derivatively by virtue of being the co-wife of a woman who is *erva*.

### Maamar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּמֵת, שְׁנִיָּה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses an additional situation: If the second brother had **performed** only **levirate betrothal with her,** meaning that he had not yet consummated the marriage, **and** then **died,** both the wife betrothed by a levirate betrothal to the second brother and the wife of the second brother fall before the youngest brother born after the death of the first brother. In that case, the first wife certainly goes out and is exempt from both *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage, since she is to him the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist. **The second,** however, was never effectively the rival wife of the first brother's wife, as the first brother's wife had only been betrothed by levirate betrothal and was not fully married to the second brother. Therefore, **she performs *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna* adds a further variation: what if Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel — a quasi-betrothal, a rabbinic *kiddushin* intended as a precursor to full *yibum* — and then Shimon died before actually completing *yibum*? In this case, Rachel is not fully his wife even by rabbinic standard, and certainly not biblically. Biblically, Shimon was married only to Sarah, and the full obligation of *yibum* rests upon Sarah alone. Yet because Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel, there is a quasi-marital bond to Rachel at a rabbinic level. Rachel therefore stands as something like a quasi-*erva* in the context of *yibum*, and Sarah becomes something like a quasi-*tzarat erva*. The practical consequence: Levi may not perform *yibum* with Sarah because of the quasi-*tzarat erva* concern, but he also cannot simply walk away, because his real biblical obligation is to Sarah. The resolution is *chalitza*: Sarah requires *chalitza* from Levi, but not *yibum*. Levi discharges his obligation through *chalitza* rather than marriage.

### Textual Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַאן דְּתָנֵי רִאשׁוֹנָה לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, מַאן דְּתָנֵי שְׁנִיָּה לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Naḥman said: He who taught** the version of the mishna which reads: **The first** widow goes out, **is not mistaken** in his version, and **he who taught** a variant version of the mishna which reads: **The second** widow goes out, **is not mistaken** either, since it is possible to understand the mishna both ways.

The Gemara notes a textual discrepancy. Some versions of the *mishna* refer to Rachel as *rishona* — the first — while others refer to her as *shniya* — the second. Rav Nachman rules that neither version is an error: *man de'tani rishona lo mishtabesh, man de'tani shniya lo mishtabesh*. What does *rishona* mean? *Rishona l'nefilah* — she was the first to fall to *yibum*, in the sense that from the very beginning of this chain of events, the first person involved in the *yibum* process was Rachel (when Reuven died). And what does *shniya* mean? *Shniya b'nisuim* — not "second" in the ordinal sense, but the one who has been married twice: Rachel was married first to Reuven and subsequently taken into the household of Shimon through *yibum*. Sarah, by contrast, was married only once. So while *shniya* seems counterintuitive as a label for Rachel — she was involved from the beginning — it refers to the fact that she is the doubly-married woman in this scenario.

## Ki Yeshvu Achim

### Simultaneous Living

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַאן דְּתָנֵי רִאשׁוֹנָה לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, מַאן דְּתָנֵי שְׁנִיָּה לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ. מַאן דְּתָנֵי
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Naḥman said: He who taught** the version of the mishna which reads: **The first** widow goes out, **is not mistaken** in his version, and **he who taught** a variant version of the mishna which reads: **The second** widow goes out, **is not mistaken** either, since it is possible to understand the mishna both ways. Both versions of the text can refer to the same woman, i.e., the wife of the first brother, by different titles. The Gemara explains that **he who taught:**

The Gemara now asks: *heicha ketiva?* — where in the Torah is the principle of *eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* derived? Rav Yehuda answers: from the verse *ki yeshvu achim yachdav* — "if brothers dwell together." The phrase *yeshvu yachdav* — dwell together — requires *shehayta lahem yeshiva achat ba'olam*: they must have had a period of simultaneous coexistence in this world. *Prat le'eshet achiv shelo haya be'olamo* — this excludes the case where one brother was born only after the other had already died. The word *yachdav* is doing real halakhic work: it mandates contemporaneous existence as a precondition for the *mitzvah* of *yibum* to apply.

### Paternal Brothers

Paternal brothers — not maternal brothers — are subject to *yibum*. The Gemara derives this from *yachdav* as well: *meyuchadim benachala* — they are unified by inheritance. Brothers who share a father inherit from one another; maternal half-brothers do not share inheritance. Therefore, *yachdav* excludes *achiv min ha'em* — a maternal half-brother — from the obligation of *yibum*.

Rabba adds a parallel derivation: we learn the meaning of *achim* — brothers — in the context of *yibum* from the sons of Jacob, not from the case of Lot. Joseph's brothers say *shneim asar achim anachnu* — we are twelve brothers — and those brothers are all paternal brothers. Therefore, *achim* in *ki yeshvu achim yachdav* means paternal brothers. This derivation tracks: *ma lehalan min ha'av velo min ha'em, af kan min ha'av velo min ha'em* — just as there (by Jacob's sons) it means paternal brothers only, so here too.

### Yachdav Meaning

The Gemara asks: why not learn *achim* from the case of *arayot*, where the Torah uses the word *achicha* — your brother — and where that term is understood to include even a maternal half-brother? If *achicha* in the context of *arayot* includes maternal brothers, perhaps *achim* in the context of *yibum* should also include them?

The Gemara responds: *danin achim me'achim ve'ein danin achim me'achicha* — we derive a rule using *achim* from another context that also uses *achim*, not from a context that uses *achicha*. The exact same word is a closer linguistic match.

The Gemara pushes back: what difference does it make whether the word is *achim* or *achicha*? They are close enough. The Gemara answers with the principle of Rabbi Yishmael regarding *tzara'at*: where one verse says *veshav hakohen* and another says *uva hakohen*, the Rabbis link them despite the different words — *zo hi shiva, zo hi via* — because essentially the same act is being described. So too here, *achim* and *achicha* might be close enough to learn from one another. The Gemara's reply is: that principle applies only where there is no equally close match available. Here, we have *achim* appearing both in the context of Jacob's sons and in the context of *yibum*, which is a closer and more direct match. *Midi dedami lei yalpinan* — we derive from the closer analogue.

### Both Words Needed

The Gemara then asks: given all this, why was it necessary for the verse to write both *achim* and *yachdav*? Could not one word have done the work of both? The Gemara responds that each is necessary to prevent a misreading. If the Torah had written only *achim*, we might have derived the meaning of *achim* from the case of Lot — where *ki anashim achim anachnu* refers not to brothers but to relatives or even a nephew-uncle relationship. Perhaps *yibum* therefore applies even to a nephew? In that case, the problem of *erva* could theoretically be "solved" by limiting *yibum* to cases where there is no *erva* prohibition — i.e., only to a nephew, not a brother. The Gemara notes that this was not merely a hypothetical concern; certain non-rabbinic sects of the Second Temple period actually interpreted the Torah's law of *yibum* this way, holding that *achim* means relatives-but-not-brothers, and that *yibum* applies specifically where there is no *issur erva*. The Gemara therefore requires *yachdav* — which indicates shared inheritance — to clarify that *achim* here means brothers in the strict paternal sense, not relatives more broadly.

And if the Torah had written only *yachdav*, we might have thought that the *mitzvah* requires brothers who share *both* a father and a mother — i.e., full brothers only. The word *achim* (interpreted in light of Jacob's sons, all of whom were paternal brothers but had different mothers) establishes that maternal relationship is irrelevant; paternal brothers suffice even without a shared mother.

### Yibum & Inheritance

The Gemara's explanation for why *yachdav* signals inheritance — *yibum banachala tala Rachmana* — is genuinely striking. The ordinarily cited rationale for *yibum* is *yakum al shem achiv hamet* — to establish the name of the deceased brother. Yet the Gemara here understands that part of the essential structure of *yibum* is tied to inheritance: the *yavam* takes over the brother's *nachala* and, by implication, is responsible for perpetuating the brother's line through inheritance. Since inheritance flows through the father, the relevant fraternal relationship for *yibum* is similarly paternal. Maternal brothers have no shared *nachala* and therefore fall outside the *yibum* framework. This is not a peripheral point; it reveals something about the Gemara's underlying understanding of what *yibum* is for.

### Closest Relationship

A final *hava amina* rounds out the discussion. One might have thought to limit the application of *yibum* — which does override the ordinary *erva* prohibition of a brother's wife — to only the very closest possible fraternal relationship: brothers who share both a father and a mother. The logic would be: *yibum* is a *chiddush*, an exceptional override of an *erva* prohibition. Since it is exceptional, perhaps we should require the absolute closest relationship — full brothers — before we invoke this exceptional permission. Therefore, we might have required *yachdav* to mean sharing both father and mother. The verse's use of *achim* (from Jacob's sons, who were paternal but not all maternal brothers) teaches us that this is not required — paternal alone is sufficient for *yibum*.

Ironically, the Gemara also notes that one sect resolved the tension between *yibum* and *erva* by going in the opposite direction — applying *yibum* only to a nephew rather than a brother, thereby eliminating the *erva* issue entirely. The Gemara rejects this reading entirely, but it is worth registering that the tension the Gemara is grappling with — the apparent collision between the obligation of *yibum* and the prohibition of *eshet ach* — was recognized as a genuine puzzle that attracted competing solutions in Jewish antiquity.
