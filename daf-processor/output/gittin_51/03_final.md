# Gittin 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Third-Party Protection

### Rule: בני חורין First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִים מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. בָּעֵי רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: בְּמַתָּנָה הֵיאַךְ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Payment of a debt or other obligation **is not collected from liened property** that has been sold to a third party **when** the debtor still **has unsold property, even** when this unsold property is inferior-quality land. **Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* **with regard to** liened property that the debtor gave to another person as **a gift?** Is it the *halakha* that the debt is not collected from liened property that has been given as a gift to a third party when the debtor still has property?

The daf opens in the middle of a series of *tikkun olam* enactments concerning the collection of debts. One such enactment establishes that a creditor cannot collect from *meshubadim* — property encumbered by a lien that is now held by a third-party purchaser — so long as the original debtor still possesses available *bnei chorin*, that is, unencumbered assets. This principle applies even where the property in the hands of the third party is of a superior grade. If the debtor retains even inferior-grade land, the creditor must exhaust that option first before turning to the purchaser. The rationale is straightforward: purchasers of land must be protected from the prospect of creditors bypassing the original debtor and proceeding directly against them. Without such protection, the entire market for land transactions would be chilled.

The question the Gemara now raises is whether this protection extends beyond purchasers to recipients of *matana* — outright gifts. If someone received land as a gift, can a creditor be forced to first collect from the debtor's remaining assets before seizing the gifted property?

### Braita: Distribution Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר: "תְּנוּ מָאתַיִם זוּז לִפְלוֹנִי, וּשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת לִפְלוֹנִי" – אֵין אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בִּשְׁטָר זָכָה; לְפִיכָךְ, יָצָא עָלָיו שְׁטַר חוֹב – גּוֹבֶה מִכּוּלָּן. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר: "תְּנוּ מָאתַיִם זוּז לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַחֲרָיו לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַחֲרָיו לִפְלוֹנִי" – אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בִּשְׁטָר זָכָה. לְפִיכָךְ, יָצָא עָלָיו שְׁטַר חוֹב – גּוֹבֶה מִן הָאַחֲרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Come and hear what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Batra* 9:6): If **a person on his deathbed said: Give two hundred dinars to so-and-so, and three hundred to so-and-so, and four hundred to so-and-so,** in this case one **does not say** that **whoever appears first in the deed acquires** his money first. **Therefore,** if **a promissory note emerged against** the one who gave the gifts, and it becomes clear that the money given was pledged to a creditor, then the creditor **collects from all of them.** **But** if the person on his deathbed **said: Give two hundred dinars to so-and-so, and after him to so-and-so, and after him to so-and-so,** then one **says: Anyone who appears first in the deed acquires** his money first. **Therefore,** if **a promissory note emerged against him,** the creditor first **collects from the last** one of the recipients. If **he does not have** enough to repay the debt, **he collects from the previous** person.

To explore this question, the Gemara cites a *braita* concerning a *shechiv meira* — a person on his deathbed — who distributes his estate to three recipients in amounts of 200, 300, and 400. If he simply declares that each person should receive these amounts without using the word *acharav* ("after him"), the distribution is understood as simultaneous and equal. Should the estate prove insufficient to cover the full 900, each recipient receives a proportional share. Similarly, if a creditor subsequently appears with a *shtar chov*, the debt is collected proportionally from all three recipients.

If, however, the dying man uses the word *acharav* — "and after him" — establishing a sequential order of priority, then in the case of a shortfall, the first recipient receives the full allotted amount before anything is distributed to the second, and the second receives in full before anything reaches the third. When a creditor appears with a lien, the Gemara explains that he collects first from the last recipient — the one with the weakest claim — and only if that is insufficient does he proceed to the second, and then the first.

### Gift Protection Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקַמָּא בֵּינוֹנִית וּבָתְרָא זִיבּוּרִית – מִזִּיבּוּרִית גָּבֵי, מִבֵּינוֹנִית לָא גָּבֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בְּמַתָּנָה נָמֵי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **And** this is the *halakha* **even if** the property given **to the first** recipient is **intermediate-quality land and** the property given to **the last** recipient is **inferior-quality land.** Although a creditor is entitled to collect his debt from intermediate-quality land, the creditor in this case **collects from inferior-quality land,** as he collects from the last person to receive his gift, and **he does not collect from the intermediate-quality land** given to the first recipient. **Conclude from** the *baraita* that **the Sages also instituted** this **ordinance in** the case of **a gift,** and that payment of a debt is not collected from liened property that has been given as a gift to a third party when the debtor still has property that has not been sold or given away as a gift. The reason is that the second gift is considered to be unsold property in relation to the first gift because, when the first gift was given, the second gift was still in the donor's possession.

Here the Gemara's analysis becomes sharply focused. Consider a scenario in which the first recipient received grade-B property and the later recipients received grade-C property. The creditor, who held a lien on the grade-B property predating the distribution, should ostensibly be able to proceed directly against the first recipient and demand his grade-B property. Why is he instead directed to collect from the last recipient, who holds only grade-C land?

The implicit answer — and the proof for our case — is that once property is given away, whether by sale or by gift, the creditor is confined to collecting from whatever the debtor still held at each successive moment. The fact that the Gemara does not permit the creditor to seize the grade-B property gifted to the first recipient, even though his liens attached to it, demonstrates that even a *matana* entitles its recipient to the same protection extended to purchasers. The creditor is relegated to the last person in the distribution chain, working backward only if earlier assets are insufficient.

### Debts vs. Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּבַעַל חוֹב. וְהָא "תְּנוּ" קָאָמַר! "תְּנוּ" בְּחוֹבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **With what are we dealing here?** We are not dealing here with a person on his deathbed who is distributing monetary gifts, but rather **with** a debtor who is dividing his money between his various **creditors.** The Gemara raises an objection: **But didn't** the person on his deathbed **say: Give** the money to so-and-so, a formulation that indicates that he is granting a gift? The Gemara answers that what this means is: **Give** this money as payment **for my debt.**

The Gemara declines to accept this as a definitive proof. It offers an alternative reading: perhaps the *braita* is not discussing gifts at all. Perhaps the *shechiv meira* was not distributing gifts but rather acknowledging debts — declaring that he owed these amounts to each of the three recipients. Under this reading, *tnu* ("give") means "pay off," and the protection afforded to the recipients follows naturally from their status as creditors being repaid, not from any extension of the gift-protection principle.

### Declaration Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְזֵי שְׁטָרָא דְּמַאן קָדֵים! דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁטָרָא. וְהָא "כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בִּשְׁטָר" קָאָמַר! בִּשְׁטָר פְּקַדְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, why should the recovery of the debts depend upon the wording of the debtor's instructions? **But let us see whose** promissory **note** was written **first,** as it is he who collects first. The Gemara answers: We are dealing with a case **where there is no** promissory **note,** e.g., where the creditors lost their deeds. The Gemara raises an objection: **But didn't it say** in the *baraita*: **Whoever appears first in the deed?** This indicates that there is in fact a deed. The Gemara answers: The reference here is not to the promissory notes but to **the testamentary deed** drawn up by the person on his deathbed.

The Gemara immediately challenges this reinterpretation. If all three are creditors, priority among them should follow the dates of their respective *shtarot*. The earlier-dated document should prevail. So why does the *braita* establish priority based on the sequential language of the declaration? The Gemara responds: *v'leika shtar* — they have no documentary proof of their debts. The dying man is simply admitting that he owes them money, but since there is no written *shtar*, the order of the declaration document itself determines priority. The creditor with a formal *shtar* meanwhile has priority over all of them because his lien is documented, even though he is directed to collect from the last recipient first.

The Gemara then addresses the *braita*'s phrase *kol hakodam bashtar zachah* — "whoever precedes in the document acquires." On the creditor-repayment reading, this refers to whoever appears first in the deathbed declaration document, not to whoever holds the earlier-dated debt instrument. The phrase *bishtar pekadata* — "in the declaration document" — clarifies that it is the order of names in the dying man's statement that governs, not the chronology of independent debt records.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ מַתָּנָה נָמֵי. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – מַאי "גּוֹבֶה מִן הָאַחֲרוֹן"? אֵין נִפְסָד אֶלָּא אַחֲרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say:** The *baraita* is referring **also** to a case of **a gift, and** even if the mishna's ordinance does not apply to liened property that had been given away as a gift, the *halakha* that the creditor collects from the last recipient is **not difficult,** as **what** is the meaning of the words: **He collects from the last** recipient? This means that it is **only the last** recipient who really **loses,** as the creditor can collect from intermediate-quality land wherever it is, even if it is in the possession of the first recipient. But after the creditor collects what is due him, the recipients of the gifts distribute the remaining property, in accordance with the order set down in the donor's testament. Therefore, it will always be the last recipient who loses and no one else.

The Gemara ultimately offers two additional resolutions. First, it suggests that perhaps *goveh min ha'acharon* — "he collects from the last" — does not mean the creditor physically takes the last recipient's property. Rather, the creditor takes the grade-B property to which his lien attaches, the estate is then redistributed, and it is the last recipient who bears the loss in the reallocation. The recipient of the gift is not physically dispossessed; the mathematical burden falls on whoever is last in line. Second, the Gemara suggests that perhaps all recipients received property of equal grade, and the question of differential protection simply does not arise on the facts of the *braita*. On this reading, the question of whether gift recipients enjoy the same protection as purchasers remains unresolved. As is often the case with *tikkun olam* enactments, protecting one party necessarily comes at the expense of another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּשָׁווּ כּוּלְּהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where all** of the properties **are** of **equal** quality.

## Indeterminate Debts

### Daughters & Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹצִיאִין לַאֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כְּתוּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The court **does not appropriate** liened property that has been sold to a third party **for the consumption of produce.** If one appropriated a field and sold it, and the buyer worked the land, improved it, and grew produce on it, and then the owner came and took back his field together with the produce, the buyer cannot collect the value of the produce from property that the robber sold to another person. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **Ulla says** that **Reish Lakish says:** This is **because** the produce **is not written** in the deed of sale. The obligation of a seller to reimburse the buyer if the field he sells him is repossessed by a prior owner or creditor is dependent upon the obligation recorded in the deed of sale. His obligation of reimbursement with regard to the produce is therefore treated like a loan by oral agreement, which is not a matter of public knowledge, and those who subsequently purchased land from the robber are not obligated to pay for debt incurred in a loan by oral agreement, as they had no way to know about it at the time of their purchase.

The Gemara moves to the next cluster of *tikkun olam* cases: *ein motzin le'achilat peirot* — we do not allow collection from *meshubadim* for certain categories of ongoing obligations. The common thread is that the amount of the liability is not yet determinable at the time a third party purchases the property.

Two paradigmatic examples illustrate the problem. First, a man's daughters are entitled to be supported from his estate after his death until they marry. But the number of daughters he will ultimately have, and the duration of their support, cannot be known at the time he sells property to a purchaser. If daughters could collect their ongoing *mezonos* from third parties, purchasers would have no way of assessing the risk they were assuming. Second, when a person sells land that subsequently turns out to have been stolen, the purchaser is entitled to compensation — including for improvements — from the seller's remaining assets. But if the purchaser has made substantial capital improvements, the amount owed to him vastly exceeds what could have been anticipated at the time any subsequent purchaser acquired those assets.

### Stolen Land Risk

The second scenario deserves elaboration because it involves a chain of transactions. A purchases land from B, who in turn purchased it from C. It subsequently emerges that C never had valid title. A is dispossessed and turns to B for compensation. B has no money but has sold other properties to third parties. A holds an implicit lien on B's estate — the standard guarantee embedded in any land transaction — but the amount of that lien is unknown. It includes the purchase price and potentially the full value of capital improvements A made. The people who purchased B's other properties cannot know, at the time of their purchase, how much liability they are implicitly assuming from A's claim against B. For this reason, they are protected: A may only collect from property B still holds directly, not from what B has sold to others.

### Ulla vs. Not Written

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: וְהָא מְזוֹן הָאִשָּׁה וְהַבָּנוֹת, דִּכְמַאן דִּכְתִיבִי דָּמוּ, וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין מוֹצִיאִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Ulla: But isn't the sustenance of** a man's **wife and daughters considered as if it were written,** as even if it is not explicitly recorded in the marriage contract it is one of the fixed stipulations of a marriage contract that are imposed by the court? It is known that a man is obligated to provide for the sustenance of his wife and daughters, **and** yet the mishna **teaches:** Payment for the sustenance of a man's wife and daughters **cannot** be **collected** from the husband's liened property.

Rashi notes a difficulty with this formulation: in the case of the stolen land and the right to compensation, the obligation is actually written into the standard *shtar* of sale. Yet Ulla and Reish Lakish still say the reason is that it is "not written." Rashi resolves this by explaining that even when it appears in the document, the indeterminate nature of the obligation means that no one pays serious attention to it — it is effectively unknown.

### Kesubah Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם, מֵעִיקָּרָא הָכִי אִתַּקּוּן – כְּתוּבִין הֵן אֵצֶל בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְאֵין כְּתוּבִין הֵן אֵצֶל מְשׁוּעְבָּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to him: There,** with regard to sustenance of a man's wife and daughters, the Sages **instituted** it **like this from the outset: It is** considered as if it were **written with regard to unsold** property that is still in the man's possession, so that sustenance can be collected from such property, **but it is not** considered as if it were **written with regard to liened** property that has been sold to another party.

Rabbi Abba challenges Ulla directly: the obligation of a husband to support his wife and daughters is not written in any document, yet *Chazal* established that it carries the full legal weight of a written *shtar*. If the rationale for refusing collection from third parties is simply that the debt is not written, why does the wife's and daughters' *mezonos* — which is treated *k'man dichtivei*, as if it were written — still not entitle them to collect from *meshubadim*?

Rabbi Zeira poses the same question to Rabbi Assi, who had transmitted the same principle in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. The answer in both cases is identical: *meikara hachi itkin* — this is precisely how *Chazal* structured the obligation from the outset. The wife's and daughters' *mezonos* is *ketuvim etzel bnei chorin* — it has the force of a written document with respect to the estate itself — but *ein ketuvim etzel meshubadim* — it does not carry that force against third-party purchasers. The protection of purchasers was built into the very structure of the rabbinic enactment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כְּתוּבִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַב אַסִּי: וְהָא מְזוֹן הָאִשָּׁה וְהַבָּנוֹת, דִּכְמַאן דִּכְתִיבִי דָּמוּ, וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין מוֹצִיאִין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵעִיקָּרָא הָכִי אִתַּקּוּן – כְּתוּבִין הֵן אֵצֶל בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְאֵין כְּתוּבִין הֵן אֵצֶל מְשׁוּעְבָּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** with regard to the mishna: Why can't the payment for the consumption of produce be collected from property that the robber sold to another person? It is **because** the produce **is not written** in the purchaser's deed of sale. **Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Asi: But isn't the sustenance of** a man's **wife and daughters considered as if it were written** in the marriage contract, **and** yet the mishna **teaches:** Payment for the sustenance of a man's wife and daughters **cannot** be **collected** from the husband's liened property? Rav Asi **said to him:** The Sages **instituted** it **like this from the outset:** Sustenance **is** considered as if it were **written with regard to unsold** property that is still in the man's possession, so that sustenance can be collected from such property, **but it is not** considered as if it were **written with regard to liened** property that has been sold to another party. This concludes the discussion of one reason for the *halakha* in the mishna.

### Rabbi Chanina: Not Fixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קְצוּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that it is for a different reason that payment for the consumption of produce cannot be recovered from property that the robber sold to another person: It is **because** the produce **is not** of **a fixed** amount, i.e., it could not be known at the outset how much produce would grow on the field or what its value would be. The Sages instituted that any obligation that is not of a fixed amount cannot be collected from property sold to another person, because the purchaser of the liened property cannot assess the risk he is assuming of having another person come to collect compensation from that property for a loss he has suffered.

Rabbi Chanina offers a cleaner and more intuitive rationale: *mipnei she'ein ketzuvim* — not because the debts are unwritten, but because the amounts are not fixed. When a purchaser acquires property and assumes an indeterminate liability, the unfairness lies not in his ignorance that some risk exists, but in the impossibility of knowing how large that risk might be. An open-ended obligation cannot fairly bind third parties. This explanation, the Gemara implies, is more satisfying than the "not written" formulation, and the subsequent discussion will largely revolve around Rabbi Chanina's position.

## Fixed Not Written

### Both or One?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – קְצוּבִין וּכְתוּבִין בָּעֵי, אוֹ דִּלְמָא קְצוּבִין, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן כְּתוּבִים?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina,** in order to collect from liened property, is it **necessary** that the obligation be both of **a fixed** amount **and** also **written?** If so, this would indicate that Rabbi Ḥanina adds another requirement in addition to that of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **Or perhaps,** in order to collect from liened property, it suffices that the obligation be of **a fixed** amount, **even if it is not written?**

The Gemara now poses the question directly: *ibaya lehu, Rabbi Chanina ketzuvim vektuvim ba'i?* Does Rabbi Chanina require both conditions — that the amount be fixed *and* that it be written — for a lien to be enforceable against third parties? Or is a fixed amount alone sufficient, even without a written document?

Rashi suggests that the case of a fixed but unwritten debt is a *milve al peh* — an oral loan. Tosafot rejects this, noting that an oral loan has independent reasons for not creating liens against third parties. Tosafot instead understands the question as concerning other categories of debt — for instance, *mazik*, tortious damage — where the amount might be fixed and determined, but where no formal document was ever drawn up. The question is whether such debts, fixed in amount but lacking documentary form, can give rise to liens on property in the hands of third parties.

### Two Daughters Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאִיתְּמַר: מִי שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ שְׁתֵּי בָּנוֹת וּבֵן, וְקָדְמָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְנָטְלָה עִישּׂוּר נְכָסִים, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיקָה שְׁנִיָּה לִגְבּוֹת עַד שֶׁמֵּת הַבֵּן. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנִיָּה וִיתְּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer to this question from what **was stated,** that the *amora'im* disagree about the following issue: There is a case of **one who died and left two daughters and a son, and the first** daughter **went ahead and took one-tenth of the estate** as her dowry, as sons are obligated to sustain their deceased father's daughters until they reach majority or become betrothed and to give them part of his estate as a dowry, as daughters do not inherit when there are sons; **but the second** daughter **did not manage to collect** her tenth of the estate for her dowry **before the son died.** Therefore, the entire estate fell to the two daughters, who then divide it between themselves, and there is a dispute as to how they divide the estate. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The second** daughter **forfeited** her one-tenth of the estate, and therefore she cannot demand that she should first receive one-tenth of the estate as her dowry, as did her sister, and that only afterward they divide what remains of the estate equally between themselves.

The Gemara introduces a *tana* case to sharpen the question. A man dies leaving two daughters and a son. The son inherits the estate. Each daughter is entitled, upon marriage, to a dowry — *parnasa* — equal to one-tenth of the estate, a fixed percentage established by rabbinic enactment. The first daughter marries and collects her one-tenth. Before the second daughter can collect her one-tenth, the son dies without children. The daughters now inherit the estate from the son. The second daughter argues: I have a lien on the estate for my one-tenth, just as my sister had hers. My sister already collected from the top. I want my one-tenth off the top before we split the remainder equally.

This is precisely the structure of a debt that is *katzuv* — fixed in amount — but not written in any document. The *parnasa* obligation arises automatically from the father's death; no *shtar* is drawn up memorializing it. Rabbi Yochanan rules that the second daughter loses out — *shniya vitrah*. She cannot collect her *parnasa* from the property now held by her sister.

### Parnasa Collectible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, גְּדוֹלָה מִזּוֹ אָמְרוּ: מוֹצִיאִין לְפַרְנָסָה וְאֵין מוֹצִיאִין לִמְזוֹנוֹת; וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ שְׁנִיָּה וִיתְּרָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥanina said to him:** Did **they** not **say** even **more than this,** that if the brother sold off property belonging to his father's estate, payment for the daughter's **dowry can be appropriated** from the buyer, **but** payment for her **sustenance cannot be appropriated** from him? If the father's estate is liened to his daughter's dowry, so that she can collect her dowry even from a third party who bought the property from the son, she should be able to collect it from her father's estate before it is divided up between the daughters. **And you say** that the **second** daughter **forfeited** her one-tenth of the estate?

Rabbi Chanina is sharply critical of this ruling. He invokes an even stronger case: *motzin l'parnasa v'ein motzin l'mezonos* — we allow collection of *parnasa* from *meshubadim*, even though we do not allow collection of *mezonos* from *meshubadim*. The distinction is that *mezonos* is indeterminate in amount, while *parnasa* — the one-tenth dowry — is a fixed figure. If the father sold property to third parties during his lifetime and the daughter's *parnasa* was not paid before he died, she can seize that property from the third-party purchasers to satisfy her fixed claim.

If that is so, argues Rabbi Chanina, then certainly she can collect from her sister — who is not even a third-party purchaser but a co-heir sitting on the same estate. He expresses astonishment that Rabbi Yochanan would say the second daughter loses out entirely. This case of *parnasa* — *demikat katz, michtav lo ktivan* — a fixed amount that is not written — is a direct proof that Rabbi Chanina's position is correct: a fixed amount, even without a written document, creates an enforceable lien.

### Parnasa Well-Known

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא פַּרְנָסָה – דְּמִיקָץ קַיְיצָא, מִיכְתָּב לָא כְּתִיבָא; וְקָא מוֹצִיאָה! שָׁאנֵי פַּרְנָסָה, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לַהּ קָלָא, כְּמַאן דִּכְתִיבָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara tries to draw a conclusion with regard to the question that was raised previously: **But isn't** the **dowry** mentioned by Rabbi Ḥanina, i.e., the dowry to which an orphan daughter is entitled from her father's estate, of **a fixed** amount, i.e., one-tenth of the estate, and **it is not written? And** nevertheless, according to Rabbi Ḥanina, **it can be appropriated** from liened property that has been sold to another party. The Gemara rejects this argument: **A dowry is different, since it** generates **publicity.** If one dies and is survived by daughters, everyone knows that a portion of his estate is pledged for their dowries. Therefore, the obligation **is considered** to be **as if it were written.** In other situations, it might be necessary according to Rabbi Ḥanina that the obligation be both of a fixed amount and written.

The Gemara responds on behalf of those who require the debt to be written: *shani parnasa, keivan d'is lei kala, k'man dichtiva dami* — *parnasa* is different, because it is so universally known and publicized that it is treated as if it were written. Everyone knows that daughters receive a one-tenth dowry from their father's estate; no one can claim ignorance of this obligation. Therefore, those who purchase land from a father are on constructive notice of the daughters' *parnasa* claim, and the daughters may accordingly collect from *meshubadim*.

This response, however, threatens to swallow the distinction entirely. If every fixed and well-known obligation is treated as if it were written, then any debt sufficiently established by common knowledge would qualify. The Gemara nonetheless proceeds with this resolution and turns to further cases.

### Stepdaughter Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ: מֵתוּ – בְּנוֹתֵיהֶן נִזּוֹנוֹת מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְהִיא נִזּוֹנֶת מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כְּבַעֲלַת חוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ raised an objection** from a mishna (*Ketubot* 101b) that addresses the case of a woman who was married to a man with whom she had stipulated that he would sustain her daughter from a previous marriage. After receiving a divorce from him, she married a different man with whom she made the same stipulation, so that the stepdaughter receives sustenance from the two husbands. That mishna states: If the husbands **died,** then **their** own **daughters,** even from that same woman, **are sustained** only **from** the **unsold property** in their estate. This accords with the *halakha* taught in the mishna above (48b). The mishna in *Ketubot* continues: **But** the stepdaughter **is sustained** even **from liened property** that had been sold to a third party. This is **due to** the fact **that her** legal status is **like** that of **a creditor,** and therefore she has the right to collect her debt from property formerly owned by her stepfather, her debtor. This is difficult according to both opinions, as the stepdaughter's sustenance is appropriated from liened property that has been sold to another person, despite it being neither a fixed amount nor written.

*Mativ Rav Huna bar Manoach*: A *braita* presents a case involving a stepdaughter. A woman remarries, and as a condition of the marriage her new husband commits to supporting her daughter — his stepdaughter — for a fixed period of five years. This is a fixed-duration, fixed-amount obligation that is not embodied in any formal *shtar* of sale. The husband subsequently divorces the mother, but the stepdaughter's right to five years of support survives the divorce. The mother then remarries again, and her second husband makes the same commitment. The stepdaughter thus holds two parallel five-year support obligations from two different men.

When both men die, the *braita* draws a stark contrast. Their own daughters — *benoseihem* — can only collect their *mezonos* from *bnei chorin*, from the estate property directly held. But the stepdaughter — *vehi nizonos minechasim meshubadim* — can collect from *meshubadim*, from property the stepfather sold to third parties. She is treated *k'ba'alas chov* — like a creditor holding a documented lien. This appears to be a direct proof that a fixed but unwritten obligation creates enforceable liens against third parties.

### Kinyan Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the mother **acquired** the right to the daughter's sustenance **from his possession,** i.e., where they performed an act of acquisition confirming the stipulation. Consequently, it is considered as though the stipulation had been written and publicized, and so the property of the two husbands is liened for the stepdaughter's sustenance.

The Gemara deflects this proof as well. *Hacha b'mai askinan b'shekinu miyado* — the case involves an obligation that was formalized through a *kinyan*, a formal act of acquisition such as *chalipin*, the exchange of a *sudar*. Because the husband performed a *kinyan*, the stepdaughter's claim has the force of a documented obligation even without a written *shtar*. This is what entitles her to collect from *meshubadim*; it is not proof that a merely verbal fixed-amount obligation would carry the same force.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בָּנוֹת נָמֵי! בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ לָזוֹ, וְלֹא קָנוּ לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that the mishna is referring to a case where they performed an act acquisition, then the deceased's own **daughters as well** should be entitled to collect their sustenance from liened property that has been sold to a third party. The Gemara rejects this argument: The mishna is referring to a case **where** the mother **acquired** the right to sustenance **on behalf of this** one, the stepdaughter, **but did not acquire** the right to sustenance **on behalf of** the other daughters.

The Gemara pushes back: if the *kinyan* is the distinguishing factor, then why not say the same for the husband's own daughters? If he performed a *kinyan* committing to support his stepdaughter, surely one could equally say he performed one for his daughters. The Gemara responds: *b'shekinu l'zo v'lo konu l'zo* — the *kinyan* was performed for the stepdaughter, not for the daughters. The *braita* is specifically constructed to contrast these two situations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי פַּסְקָא? בַּת אִשְׁתּוֹ, דַּהֲוַאי בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן – מַהֲנֵי לַהּ קִנְיָן; בִּתּוֹ, דְּלָא הֲוַאי בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן – לָא מַהֲנֵי לָהּ קִנְיַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for **the decision** of the *tanna* to record the *halakha* in a case where the mother acquired the right for this one but not for that one? The Gemara explains: With regard to **his wife's daughter** from her previous marriage, **who was** alive **at the time of the** act of **acquisition,** i.e., when he gave the mother her marriage contract, **the** act of **acquisition is effective for her.** With regard to **his** own **daughter** from this mother, **who was not** alive **at the time of the** act of **acquisition, the** act of **acquisition is not effective for her.**

The Gemara objects again: *mai pseeka* — why is this so definitive? The *braita*'s entire point seems to be the contrast between the stepdaughter and the daughters. Reading into it a specific *kinyan* for one and not the other seems forced. The Gemara responds: *bas ishto dehavya b'sha'as kinyan mahni lah kinyan* — the stepdaughter was already in existence at the time the *kinyan* was performed, so the *kinyan* was effective for her. *Beto d'lo havya b'sha'as kinyan* — his own daughters, who did not yet exist when he married their mother and undertook his obligations via *kinyan*, cannot benefit from that *kinyan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דַּהֲווֹ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בִּשְׁעַת קִנְיָן – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי, דְּגָרְשַׁהּ וְאַהְדְּרַהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Are we not dealing** here even with a case **where both of them were** alive **at the time of the** act of **acquisition? And what are the circumstances?** It is a case **where** after she was married to him and had a daughter from him, **he divorced her and** later **remarried her,** at which time an act of acquisition was performed to confirm the stipulation with regard to sustenance. As his own daughter was alive at the time, why is the act of acquisition not effective for his own daughter's sustenance as it is for that of his stepdaughter?

The Gemara anticipates an objection: one can construct a case where his daughters *were* present at the time of a *kinyan* — for instance, if he divorced his wife and then remarried her, at which point his existing daughters were alive when he renewed his obligations. In that scenario, *dehavu tarye b'sha'as kinyan*, both the stepdaughter and the daughters were present. The Gemara's final answer is dispositive:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בִּתּוֹ – כֵּיוָן דְּבִתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין קָאָכְלָה, לָא מַהֲנֵי לַהּ קִנְיַן; בַּת אִשְׁתּוֹ – דְּלָאו בִּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין קָאָכְלָה, מַהֲנֵי לַהּ קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the difference between them is as follows: With regard to **his** own **daughter, who eats,** i.e., is sustained, from his estate **based on a stipulation of the court,** as the daughter's right to sustenance from her father's estate is an inseparable part of her mother's marriage contract, **the** act of **acquisition** that was performed **is not effective for her,** because her entitlement is derived from a different source, the stipulation of the court. With regard to **his wife's daughter,** who eats from his estate **not** based **on a stipulation of the court,** as her entitlement is based on the explicit stipulation made between the husband and the wife, **the** act of **acquisition is effective for her** and enables her to collect her sustenance even from liened property that has been sold to a third party.

*beto keivan d'bitnai beis din ka'achlah, lo mahni lah kinyan* — because his daughter's right to support derives from a rabbinic obligation, an independent *kinyan* is superfluous and adds nothing. It cannot elevate her rights beyond what *Chazal* already prescribed, which is collection limited to *bnei chorin*. The stepdaughter, by contrast, has no rabbinic entitlement at all; her entire claim rests on the *kinyan* the stepfather voluntarily performed, and it is precisely that *kinyan* which grants her the status of a *ba'alas chov* entitled to collect from *meshubadim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מִיגְרָע גָּרְעָה?! אֶלָּא בִּתּוֹ – אֵימַר צְרָרֵי אַתְפְּסַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **But is** a right that is rooted in two sources, both a stipulation of the court and an act of acquisition, **inferior** to a right that is rooted in an act of acquisition alone? The act of acquisition is in addition to the stipulation of the court, and should be effective for her as well. **Rather,** this is the difference between them: With regard to **his** own **daughter, say** that perhaps **he** already **gave her money** during his lifetime for her sustenance.

The Gemara raises one final difficulty: *v'chi migra gara* — surely the daughter, who began with a stronger foundational right, should not end up in a worse position simply because that right derives from *beis din* rather than from a voluntary act. The Gemara's answer is that since the daughter's obligation is so thoroughly rooted in *beis din*, there is a concern that the father may have already prepaid it by setting aside funds in escrow. Because of this possibility, *beis din* will not permit her to collect from third parties even if a *kinyan* were theoretically relevant. This is an admittedly complex resolution, but the essential upshot is that the stepdaughter case cannot serve as straightforward proof that a fixed, unwritten obligation entitles a claimant to collect from *meshubadim*. The *kinyan* is doing all the work.

## Capital Improvements

### Improvements Before Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁקָּדַם מִקָּחוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי לְשִׁבְחוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן; אֲבָל קָדַם שִׁבְחוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן לְמִקָּחוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion with regard to collection from liened property that has been sold to a third party: **Come** and **hear** what **Rabbi Natan said: When** do we say that if one appropriated a field and sold it, and the buyer worked the land and enhanced it, and then the initial owner from whom the field had been appropriated reclaimed the property, the buyer can recover the increase in value of the field resulting from his enhancements only from unsold property in the robber's possession but not from liened property that has been sold to another party? This is said **when the purchase of the second** buyer, i.e., the party who purchased the property that rightfully belonged to the robber, **preceded the enhancements** made **by the first** buyer to the appropriated property he purchased from the robber. **But** if **the enhancements** made **by the first** buyer **preceded the acquisition of the second** buyer, then **he can collect** the increase in value of the field resulting from his enhancements even **from liened property** that has been sold to the second buyer.

The Gemara cites one further *braita* in pursuit of the question of whether a fixed, unwritten obligation creates enforceable liens. *Amar Rabbi Natan: eimasai* — when is it that the person whose stolen land was improved cannot collect the value of those improvements from third parties? *Bizman shekodam mekach sheni lishvacho shel rishon* — when the third party purchased the property before the improvements were made. In that case, the third-party purchaser did not know and could not have known the extent of the liability he was assuming. He acquired the property without any way to assess the risk.

*Aval kodam shvacho shel rishon lemekach sheni* — but if the improvements were already made before the third party purchased, then when he purchased he knew, or could have known, that there was a specific, determinable claim against the seller's estate. In that case, *goveh minechasim hameshubadim* — the claimant may collect even from *meshubadim*.

### Tosafot: Amount Fixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָדֵים הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently,** he cannot collect the increase in value from liened property **because** the enhancements **did not precede** the sale of the other field, not because the increase in value is not of a fixed amount or written in a deed.

Tosafot reads this *braita* as a direct vindication of Rabbi Chanina's position. Here is a case where the obligation is plainly not written in any document — it is an implicit warranty embedded in every land transaction — yet once the amount is fixed and determinable, the claimant may collect from third parties. The critical variable is not documentation but knowability. Once Michael acquired the property after the improvements were already made, the risk was ascertainable, and David's right to collect is enforceable. This demonstrates that it is the indeterminacy of amount, not the absence of a written instrument, that is the operative concern.

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין מוֹצִיאִין לַאֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת, וְלִשְׁבַח קַרְקָעוֹת, וְלִמְזוֹן אִשָּׁה וְהַבָּנוֹת – מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם; לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כְּתוּבִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי מָה תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם יֵשׁ בָּזוֹ? וַהֲלֹא אֵין קְצוּבִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Proof cannot be brought one way or the other from this *baraita*, since the issue **is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in another *baraita*: The court **does not appropriate** payment **from liened property** that has been sold to a third party **for the consumption of produce or for the enhanced value of land or for the sustenance of** a man's **wife and daughters.** The reason why one cannot collect these debts from liened property is **for the betterment of the world, because** all these obligations **are not written** in any deed. If purchasers are at risk of losing the land they purchased to pay debts of the seller that had not been written, they will have no way to protect themselves, and no one will purchase land. The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yosei said: And what betterment of the world is there in this,** in stating that the reason the land is not appropriated is only because the debt was not written? **But isn't** the real reason that these obligations cannot be collected from liened property is that **they are not** of **a fixed** amount? No one would be willing to purchase land if the land were liened to an unlimited debt.

The Gemara draws its conclusion: *tannai hi* — this dispute between the "not written" rationale and the "not fixed" rationale reflects a genuine disagreement between *tannaim*. A *braita* explicitly states that collection from *meshubadim* is prohibited for *achilat peirot*, improvement of land, and *mezon ha'ishah vehabanot* — *mipnei tikkun ha'olam*, for the sake of the public good — *lefi she'ein ketuvim*, because these obligations are not written. This *braita* explicitly frames the issue as one of documentation.

Rabbi Yosei pushes back within the *braita* itself: *vechi mah tikkun ha'olam yesh bazeh?* — what kind of *tikkun olam* is achieved by pointing to the absence of a written document? The real issue, he argues, is *halo ein katzuv* — that the amount is not fixed. It is the open-ended, indeterminate nature of the liability that makes it impossible for purchasers to assess what they are assuming, and it is that impossibility which justifies the rabbinic protection. By the end of the discussion, the Gemara's position is that if an obligation is fixed in amount and the purchaser knew of the risk at the time of acquisition, collection from third parties is permitted — even without a formal *shtar*.

## Finder Dispute

### Finder Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמּוֹצֵא מְצִיאָה לֹא יִשָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** it was further instituted that **one who finds a lost item** and returns it to its rightful owner is **not** required to **take an oath** that he did not keep any part of the lost item for himself. This ordinance was also instituted for the betterment of the world.

The Gemara turns to another *tikkun olam* enactment: *ha-motzi metziah* — the finder of lost property is exempt from taking an oath *mipnei tikkun olam*. The scenario is familiar: a person returns a lost object to its owner, who then claims that the original contents were more than what was returned. Under the normal rules of *modeh b'miktzat* — partial admission — the finder, by acknowledging the object as the owner's while contesting the amount, would ordinarily be required to take a *shvua*. But if finders were routinely subjected to oaths every time an owner disputed the recovered contents, no one would bother returning lost objects. *Tikkun olam* therefore exempts the finder from this obligation.

### Wallets vs. Oxen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״שְׁנֵי כִּיסִין קְשׁוּרִין מָצָאתָ לִי״; וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא מָצָאתִי אֶלָּא אֶחָד״ – נִשְׁבָּע. ״שְׁנֵי שְׁוָורִים קְשׁוּרִין מָצָאתָ לִי״; וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא הָיָה אֶלָּא אֶחָד״ – אֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** If the owner of the lost item brings a claim against the finder, saying: **You found two** money **pouches tied** together that belong **to me, and the other** person **says: I found only one** pouch, then the finder **takes an oath,** similar to anyone who admits to part of a claim. If the owner claims: **You found two oxen tied** together that belong **to me, and the other** person **says: There was only one** ox, the finder is **not** required to **take an oath.**

Rav Yitzchak, however, rules in a manner that appears to entirely disregard this principle. He states: if a person says to another, "You found two wallets of mine that were tied together," and the finder replies, "I found only one," the finder must take a *shvua* — it is a case of *modeh b'miktzat*. But if the claim is "You found two of my oxen bound together" and the finder responds "There was only one," the finder need not take a *shvua*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? שְׁווֹרִים מִנַּתְחִי מֵהֲדָדֵי, כִּיסִין לָא מִנַּתְחִי מֵהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for the difference between the two cases? It is that **oxen become detached from each other.** Consequently, it is possible that when the oxen were lost, there had been two that were tied together, but afterward they became separated and the finder found only one. By contrast, **pouches do not become detached from each other.** Since the finder admits that he found one of them, it stands to reason that he actually found both of them.

The distinction rests on the natural behavior of the objects. *Shvarim mitnachu hadadai* — oxen, even when tied together, will naturally separate. Therefore the claimant's assertion that both oxen were found together cannot be stated with certainty; it is a *ta'anat shema*, a claim based on conjecture. Without a definitive claim, there is no *modeh b'miktzat*. Wallets, by contrast, *kisim lo mitnachu hadadai* — do not naturally come apart. If you found one, you found both. The claimant's assertion is therefore a *ta'anat barai*, a certain claim, and the finder's partial denial constitutes a genuine *modeh b'miktzat* requiring a *shvua*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁנֵי שְׁוָורִים קְשׁוּרִין מָצָאתָ״; וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״מָצָאתִי וְהֶחְזַרְתִּי לָךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶן״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak also says: If the owner of the lost item says to the finder: **You found two tied oxen, and the other** person **says: I found** two oxen, **but I** already **returned one of them to you,** then the finder **takes an oath.**

A *braita* adds a variation: if the finder says "I found both oxen and I have already returned one to you," and the owner denies receiving any return, the finder must take a *shvua* in that case as well.

### R.E.b.Y. Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק – לֵית לֵיהּ ״הַמּוֹצֵא מְצִיאָה לֹא יִשָּׁבַע – מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם״?! הוּא דְּאָמַר, כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב
>
> **Translation:** A question may be raised against these rulings of Rabbi Yitzḥak: **But does Rabbi Yitzḥak not accept** the *halakha* stated in the mishna that **one who finds a lost item is not** required to **take an oath,** this being an enactment instituted **for the betterment of the world?** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yitzḥak **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.**

The Gemara notes that Rav Yitzchak's rulings appear to contradict the *mishna*'s *tikkun olam* exemption. His answer: *hu d'amar k'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — Rav Yitzchak follows the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who holds that even a finder of lost property may be required to take a *shvua*. This is a *machloket tannaim*, and Rav Yitzchak has ruled in accordance with the minority position.

### Admission Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם נִשְׁבָּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ. כֵּיצַד? ״מָנֶה לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי, וְהֶאֱכַלְתִּיו פְּרָס״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** — as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shevuot* 5:10) that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** There are **times when a person takes an oath about his own claim. How so?** One says to another: **One hundred dinars of your** deceased **father's was in my possession,** as I had borrowed that sum from him. **And I** already **paid him part [*peras*]** of it, but I still owe you fifty dinars. In this case, he is not believed unless **he takes an oath** that he repaid the half, like anyone who admits to part of a claim.

The Gemara now elaborates on Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position. *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer: pe'amim she'adam nishba al ta'anat atzmo* — there are circumstances in which a person may be required to take a *shvua* based on their own admission, even in the absence of a formal legal claim by the opposing party. This captures the very situation of the *motzi metziah*: the finder voluntarily came forward and is now subjected to a *shvua* not because the owner prosecuted a winning claim, but because the finder's own admission creates the structure of *modeh b'miktzat*.

### Half-Debt Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה הוּא שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** an example of a case **where** one **takes an oath about his own claim.** Although nobody has claimed anything from him, he still takes an oath on the basis of his own statement.

The Gemara specifies the case: *maneh l'avicha b'yadi* — a person approaches the son of a deceased and says, "Your father deposited one hundred *zuz* with me. I must tell you that I spent half of it, so I owe you fifty." He has spontaneously admitted to a debt, declared that half has been satisfied, and thereby placed himself in the position of one who admits to part of a claim. *V'harei zeh nishba* — he must take a *shvua* on the fifty he claims to have paid. This is the paradigm case of what Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov calls an oath on one's own admission.

## Migo Analysis

### Migo Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה, וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But the Rabbis say:** In such a case, the borrower **is** regarded **only as someone who is returning a lost item, and** therefore **he is exempt** from an oath. Rabbi Yitzḥak's position is similar to that of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. Since the finder says that he found only half of what the owner claims was lost, he is treated like someone who admits to part of a claim and therefore takes an oath.

The obvious difficulty with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position is the principle of *migo*. *Migo d'i ba'i shatik* — I could have simply remained silent. Had this person said nothing, the heir would have had no claim against him at all. The fact that he voluntarily came forward and disclosed the debt should render him credible when he says he repaid half. Why, if I had the option of total silence, should my partial admission subject me to a *shvua*? This is a powerful *migo* argument, and the Chachamim employ it to exempt the finder: he could have simply not disclosed that he found the object, so his voluntary disclosure entitles him to be believed when he gives an account of the contents.

### Ignorant Claimant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב – לֵית לֵיהּ: ״מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה – פָּטוּר״?! אָמַר רַב: בְּטוֹעֲנוֹ קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov not maintain** that **someone who returns a lost item is exempt** from an oath? **Rav says:** The case in dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov and the Rabbis is not one where nobody has claimed anything from the borrower, as in such a case all agree that the borrower is exempt from taking an oath. Rather, it is a case **where** the creditor has died, leaving a child as his heir, and this **minor** confronts the borrower and **claims** a hundred dinars **from him,** which he alleges was lent by his late father. The other admits to having borrowed the money but claims that he already repaid half the sum. Since he admits to part of the claim, he takes an oath that he did repay the other part.

Rav resolves the difficulty: *b'ta'anat katan* — the case involves a *katan*. The heir confronting the debtor is a child who is pressing him for money. The debtor therefore cannot say "I could have kept quiet"; he was, after a fashion, being challenged. The *migo* of "I could have remained silent" is inapplicable when there is some form of pressure, even if that pressure lacks full *halachic* force. The term *ta'anat atzmo* — "his own claim" — therefore means that while there was technically a claim against him, it was not a legally operative one, and his response is effectively self-generated.

### Katan Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָן, מִידֵּי מְשָׁשָׁא אִית בֵּיהּ?! וְהָתְנַן: אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל טַעֲנַת חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does the claim of **a minor have any substance? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 38b): **One does not take an oath** in response **to the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** as the claim of one who lacks halakhic competence has no significance whatsoever. According to this, if a minor brought a claim against the borrower, it is as though there were no claim at all but only the borrower's admission, and so the borrower should be exempt from taking an oath.

The Gemara objects: a *katan*'s claim has no *halachic* standing at all. *D'tnan: ein nishba'in al ta'anat cheresh, shoteh, v'katan* — one does not take an oath in response to the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor. If the *katan*'s challenge carries no legal weight, why does it neutralize the debtor's *migo*? The *migo* should still apply: even facing a *katan*, I could simply have denied everything, so my partial admission should be credited.

### Ignorant = Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קָטָן״ – גָּדוֹל; וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״קָטָן״? דִּלְגַבֵּי מִילֵּי דְאָבִיו, קָטָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: To **which** kind of **minor** was Rav referring? It was to **an adult** son of the creditor. **And why does** Rav **call him a minor,** if he is in fact an adult? It is **as with regard to his father's affairs he is** like **a minor.** He does not know with certainty how much money the borrower repaid but merely says that he thinks he owes his father more.

The Gemara reframes the case: *mai katan? gadol* — when we say *katan*, we do not mean a literal minor. We mean an adult who is ignorant of his father's financial affairs — one who does not know what debts his father held or was owed. *D'lgabei milei d'avuh katan hu* — with respect to his father's dealings, he is like a minor. This adult heir is pressing the debtor on a matter he does not fully understand. The debtor therefore cannot comfortably say he could have remained completely silent; the heir was making some kind of claim. Yet because the heir is effectively ignorant, his claim lacks the force of a genuine legal *ta'anah*. This is precisely why the Chachamim characterize the debtor's conduct as *meshiv aveida* — returning lost property — and exempt him from a *shvua*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, by contrast, holds that the debtor's partial admission constitutes *modeh b'miktzat* regardless, and he must swear.

### Claim vs. Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ?! טַעֲנַת אֲחֵרִים הוּא! טַעֲנַת אֲחֵרִים, וְהוֹדָאַת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that we are dealing with a case where the deceased creditor's adult son made a claim against the debtor, how can Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov say about this claim that it is his own claim? Is this an oath taken about **his own claim? It is** an oath taken about **the claim of others,** i.e., the son. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov calls it an oath taken about one's own claim, although it is really **the claim of others,** because it is **his own admission** that obligates him to take the oath.

The Gemara closes with an unresolved aporia. When Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says the person swears *al ta'anat atzmo* — on his own claim — the Gemara notes that this description is paradoxical. If there was a genuine claim by the other side, then calling it "his own claim" is misleading — it is the other side's claim. And if there was no genuine claim by the other side, then *modeh b'miktzat* should not apply, because the *migo* of silence was available. But if we say the heir's claim *was* legally meaningful, then describing the admission as self-generated seems wrong. *Kullehu ta'anata nami ta'anas acherim vehoda'as atzmo ninhu* — every case of *modeh b'miktzat* involves a claim by one side and an admission by the other. What is unique about this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ טַעֲנָתָא נָמֵי, טַעֲנַת אֲחֵרִים וְהוֹדָאַת עַצְמוֹ נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **All claims** that lead to the oath of one who admits to part of the claim **are also** a combination of **the claim of others and** the defendant's **own admission.**

The Gemara leaves this question open. Tomorrow's session will return to clarify precisely what Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the Chachamim are debating, and why one side holds the debtor obligated while the other exempts him entirely.
