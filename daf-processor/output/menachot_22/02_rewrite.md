# Menachot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Salt & Priests

### Intro & Salting

Today's daf properly begins at the bottom — or perhaps middle — of 21b, treating the practice of salting everything that would be burned in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The salt applied to *korbanot* was provided by the Temple, and this raises a series of related questions about who is entitled to use it and on what basis.

The established position is that *kohanim* are given salt both for the purpose of salting the portions they burn on the *mizbeach* and, by a special stipulation, for salting the *korban* meat they eat. Crucially, this privilege does not extend to salting non-*korban* food they happen to eat alongside their *korban* meal.

### Ravina's Q

Ravina poses a question to Rav Ashi: *pshita* — is it not obvious what the stipulation must have been referring to? If one were to suppose that *Beit Din*'s stipulation was needed to permit *kohanim* to use the salt for burning their *korbanot* on the *mizbeach*, that would be strange. The logic Ravina presses is: *hashta l'Yisrael yahvina le-Kohanim lo yahvina* — if we give salt to an ordinary Israelite who brings a *korban*, why would we imagine that a *kohen* would require a special dispensation to do the same? It follows that the stipulation must have addressed something less obvious: the use of communal salt for eating *korban* meat. But to establish this, the *Gemara* must first confirm that the *Beit HaMikdash* indeed provides salt to everyone who brings a *korban*.

### Baraita Proof

The *Gemara* cites a *Baraita* to address who bears responsibility for providing the salt. One might have thought: *yakhol ha-omer harei alai mincha yavi melach mi-toch beito kederech she-mevi levona mi-toch beito* — perhaps one who vows to bring a *mincha* must also supply the salt from his own home, just as he must provide the *levona* (frankincense). The parallel is apt: when a person vows to bring a *mincha*, the Torah independently mandates that *levona* accompany it; he never explicitly committed to the frankincense, yet it is required of him. So perhaps the salt, equally mandated by the Torah alongside any *mincha*, should similarly be the donor's responsibility.

The *Baraita* frames this as a formal *binyan av*: it says bring a *mincha* and bring salt, and it also says bring a *mincha* and bring *levona*. Perhaps just as *levona* must come from one's own home, so too must salt. On the other hand, one could construct a parallel comparison: it says bring a *mincha* and bring salt, and it also says bring a *mincha* and burn it on wood. If wood is provided by the *Beit HaMikdash*, perhaps salt should be as well.

### Salt Communal

The *Gemara* now weighs these two analogies against each other. Which comparison is more apt — salt to *levona*, or salt to wood?

The argument for comparing salt to wood runs as follows: *danin davar ha-noheg be-khol ha-zevachim mi-davar ha-noheg be-khol ha-zevachim* — we derive the rule for something that applies across all *korbanot* from another thing that applies across all *korbanot*. Salt, like wood, is required for every *korban* that meets certain conditions. *Levona*, by contrast, does not apply universally — it accompanies a *mincha* but not animal sacrifices. So the more natural comparison is salt to wood, and since wood is provided communally, so too salt.

The counterargument is: *danin davar ha-ba imo bi-khli echad mi-davar ha-ba imo bi-khli echad* — we derive the rule for something placed in the same vessel from another thing placed in the same vessel. Both the salt and the *levona* are placed together with the *korban* in its vessel before the *kemitza*, making them more integral to the *korban* itself. Wood, by contrast, is never placed in the same vessel; it sits on the fire beneath. Perhaps this vessel-based integration means the salt, like *levona*, is the owner's responsibility.

The *Baraita* resolves this by citing a verse: *berit melach olam hi* — an eternal covenant of salt — which the Torah links to the phrase *me-et benei Yisrael berit olam*, appearing in connection with the *lechem ha-panim*. Just as *lechem ha-panim* is provided from communal funds, so too the salt. The salt is provided *mishel tzibur*, from the public treasury, not by the individual who brings the *korban*.

### Salt Approaches

This raises the question of what conceptual difference exists between the two approaches. On one reading, salt is analogous to the *kometz* — something physically integrated with the *korban* and placed in the same vessel — which might suggest it is the donor's obligation. On another reading, salt is like wood: a universal operating expense of the *Beit HaMikdash* applicable to every *korban*, and therefore logically a communal expense. The *Baraita*'s resolution via the *berit melach olam* verse settles the matter in favor of communal provision, but the underlying conceptual tension between these two ways of understanding salt's status remains instructive.

### Ben Buchri

This brings the *Gemara* back to the original question: given that the *Beit HaMikdash* provides salt for everyone who brings a *korban*, why would *kohanim* need any special stipulation to use it for burning their own *korbanot*? Every Israelite gets that salt automatically — why would *kohanim* be different?

Rav Mordechai explains in the name of Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi: *lo nitzrecha ela le-Ben Buchri* — the stipulation is only necessary according to the position of Ben Buchri. To understand this requires a detour into a famous *Talmudic* dispute.

### Ben Buchri Testify

The *Gemara* cites a *Mishna*: Rabbi Yehuda reports that Ben Buchri testified at Yavneh — *kol Kohen she-shokel eino chotei* — any *kohen* who contributes the *shekel* does not sin. The implication is that *kohanim* were not obligated to contribute to the communal *shekalim* fund; contributing was optional and praiseworthy, but the obligation did not apply to them.

### RYbZ Counter

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai disagreed sharply: *lo ki — ela kol Kohen she-eino shokel chotei* — on the contrary, any *kohen* who does not give the *shekel* sins. According to him, *kohanim* are fully obligated to contribute to communal funds just like any other Israelite.

### Priests' Drasha

The *Gemara* then explains the *kohanim*'s own rationale. They interpreted a verse to their advantage: *ve-khol minchat Kohen kalil tihiyeh lo techachel* — every *mincha* of a *kohen* must be completely burned; it shall not be eaten. Their reasoning: if *kohanim* were to contribute to communal funds, they would thereby acquire a partial ownership stake in all communal *korbanot* — the *shtei ha-lechem*, the *lechem ha-panim*, the *omer*. But a *mincha* that is partly owned by *kohanim* must be entirely burned. Since these communal offerings are not, in fact, burned entirely, the *kohanim* concluded they must have no share in them — meaning they should not contribute to communal funds in the first place. Indeed, they went further: it is not merely that they are exempt; they are positively prohibited from contributing, lest they acquire an ownership interest that would render those offerings invalid.

Returning now to the *Gemara*'s point: according to Ben Buchri, *kohanim* have no stake in communal funds and therefore no inherent right to the communal salt. The salt belongs to those who contributed to the *lishkah*. One might therefore have thought — *salka daitach amina ki zachu lehu rachmana be-Yisrael de-it lehu lishkah* — the Torah granted rights to the salt only to those Israelites who contributed to the treasury. *Ha-Kohanim de-leit lehu lishkah* — the *kohanim*, who have no share in the communal fund, might not be entitled to the salt even for burning their own *korbanot*. Hence, a stipulation was necessary. The *Gemara* does note that even according to Ben Buchri, when *kohanim* do choose to contribute voluntarily, those funds are genuinely transferred to the *tzibur* — *demayiti u-masar lehu la-tzibur* — and so there is no concern about *chulin* entering the *azarah*. But absent that contribution, their claim on communal resources required explicit authorization.

### RYbZ Counter

*(See above — Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's position is treated in context of the Ben Buchri discussion.)*

### Entitlement

This dispute is, on reflection, quite striking. A *Tosafot* notes that since the *kohanim* interpreted this verse *le-atzmanam* — for their own benefit — it implies Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was not himself a *kohen*. A citation in the *Sifrei* regarding the *Parah Adumah* where Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai refers to what "my hands served" is addressed by *Tosafot* as well: this language does not mean he personally performed the service, but rather that it was carried out under his rulings and teaching, and he had forgotten the precise details.

Beyond the textual point, there is something worth dwelling on in the psychology of the *kohanim*'s position. The instinct to say "I work for this institution; I should not have to pay into it" has an obvious surface logic. A *kohen* devotes himself to the *avodah*; why should he also be required to fund it? But Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's response — insisting on the obligation to give even a half-*shekel* — captures something important. Being served by an institution and working for it are not mutually exclusive. To exempt oneself from contribution on the grounds of one's role is to establish a posture of pure entitlement, positioning oneself as only a provider and never a recipient. What Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai insists upon is that even those who labor at the center of the institution are also served by it and must participate in sustaining it. This dynamic feels all the more pointed coming from the figure who would lead the transition from a Temple-centered Judaism to a *Torah*-centered one. The critique of *kohen* exemption is, in a subtle way, a critique of a certain priestly self-understanding that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai spent his life pushing against.

There is also a deeper question lurking here: according to the *kohanim*'s position, does that mean all *korbanot tzibur* were brought exclusively on behalf of *Klal Yisrael* excluding *kohanim*? Or is it that the *kohanim* assumed they would of course receive the credit — simply by virtue of being the ones who performed the service — without needing to formally contribute? That kind of assumed entitlement, where one expects the benefits of collective endeavor without formal participation, is precisely what the insistence on giving the *shekel* is designed to correct.

## Wood & Fire

### Wood Baraita

The *Gemara* now addresses the question of wood, which had been assumed in the earlier discussion to be provided by communal funds. The basis for this assumption requires its own derivation. A *Baraita* asks: might someone who declares "I hereby take upon myself an *olah*" be required to bring the wood from his own home, just as he must bring *nesachim* from his own home? After all, the *nesachim* — the wine, flour, and water that accompany an animal *korban* — are not something he separately vowed; the Torah simply requires them as an accompaniment. Yet he must provide them. Perhaps the wood is analogous.

The *Baraita* rejects this by citing the verse: *al ha-etzim asher al ha-eish asher ha-mizbeach* — the *kohen* is to arrange the *olah* on the wood that is on the fire that is on the *mizbeach*. The inference: *ma mizbeach mishel tzibur* — just as the altar itself is provided by communal funds as part of the institutional infrastructure of the *Beit HaMikdash*, so too *af etzim va-eish mishel tzibur* — even the wood and fire are communally provided.

### Elazar Fresh

Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua offers a different formulation: *ma mizbeach shelo nishtamesh bo hedyot* — just as the altar was never used by a layperson, *af etzim va-eish shelo nishtamesh bahen hedyot* — so too the wood and fire must not be items that a layperson has used. The concern here is not merely the source of funding but the status of the materials themselves: the wood and fire for the *mizbeach* must be fresh, unused by any private individual.

This raises a practical question about fire, since in an era without matches, fire required active effort to produce. The principle would mean one cannot take a burning log from a private barbecue and use it to light the *mizbeach*; one would need to kindle a genuinely new fire for the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Fresh vs Source

The *Gemara* notes: *mai beinayhu* — what is the practical difference between these two positions? *Ika beinayhu chadta* — the difference lies in new wood. According to the first opinion (wood must come from communal funds), the *Beit HaMikdash* could theoretically purchase secondhand wood — previously used by others — so long as it is now owned and provided by the *mikdash*. The key criterion is the source of provision, not the physical history of the material. According to Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, what matters is whether the wood itself is new, regardless of who provides it.

Conversely, one could imagine wood that is brand new yet donated directly by a private individual. These two positions — communal provision versus newness of material — are therefore genuinely distinct, and both criteria could, in principle, apply or fail independently.

The *Gemara* then asks: does Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua mean that old, used wood simply cannot be brought at all? Does not the verse in connection with David and Aravnah indicate that Aravnah offered the threshing implements — *ha-morigim* — and the cattle implements as wood for the altar? This appears to be a case of used items serving as wood for the *mizbeach*.

### Morag

The *Gemara* resolves this: *hacha nami be-chadatin* — in that case, the implements were brand new; they had been fashioned but not yet actually used for threshing. Despite having been manufactured into instruments, since they had never been put to use, they retained the status of new materials.

This prompts the *Gemara* to clarify the meaning of *morigim*. Ulla explains: *mita shel tarbal* — a spiked board. Rav Yehuda elaborates: *iza de-korkasa* — a thick plank fitted with spikes. Rashi explains that *korkasa* denotes a thick plank and that the device consisted of a heavy spiked board drawn by cattle across the harvested grain, functioning as a threshing implement to separate the kernel from its husk. Rav Yosef adduces the verse: *hinei samticha le-morag charutz chadash ba'al pipiyot tadush harim* — "Behold I have made you like a new sharp threshing sledge with many teeth; you shall thresh mountains." The word *morag* in that verse clearly refers to a threshing instrument, confirming Ulla's identification.

## Kometz Mixing

### Kometz Mixed

The *Mishna* now addresses what happens when *korban* portions become mixed together — a topic familiar from parallel discussions in Zevachim concerning blood and animals. The *Mishna* states: *nitarv kometz be-kometz chaveiro* — if a *kometz* becomes mixed with another *kometz* of a different *mincha*, or with the *minchat kohanim*, or with the *minchat kohen mashiach*, or with a *minchat nesachim* — all of these are *korbanot* whose portions are entirely burned — the mixture is *kasher*. Since everything in the mixture must in any case be burned on the *mizbeach*, one simply burns the entire mixture, and every component has received its proper treatment. No portion is lost and no invalid material has been introduced.

The scenario is a practical one: two *kohanim* each take their *kometz*, they collide, the two portions mix in the same vessel. The solution is straightforward: burn everything.

### RY Thick/Thin

Rabbi Yehuda, however, rules differently in two specific cases: if a *kometz* becomes mixed with a *minchat kohen mashiach* or with a *minchat nesachim*, the mixture is *pesulah*. His reasoning: *she-zo bililatah avah ve-zo bililatah rakkah ve-hen bole'ot zo mi-zo* — one has a thick consistency and the other a thin consistency, and they absorb from one another.

The proportions are as follows. A standard *mincha* uses one *issaron* of flour — approximately two and a half liters — combined with one *log* of oil, roughly half a liter: a one-to-five ratio, producing a dough-like consistency. The *minchat nesachim*, by contrast, uses the same quantity of flour but three *log* of oil — a *revi'it* of a *hin*, which is a quarter of twelve *log* — producing a much looser, batter-like consistency. The same higher oil ratio applies to the *minchat kohen mashiach*. When a dense dough-*kometz* and a thin batter-*mincha* come into contact, each absorbs something of the other. The dough becomes somewhat thinner; the batter becomes somewhat thicker. The result is that you no longer have a proper *kometz* of the standard proportions, nor a proper *minchat nesachim* of its proper proportions. Rabbi Yehuda holds that this physical alteration invalidates the mixture.

## Nullification

### Blood Water

The *Gemara* now turns to a *Mishna* from Zevachim dealing with mixtures and the principle of *bitul*. If blood of a *korban* falls into water, the rule is: *im yesh bo marit dam kasher* — if the mixture still has the appearance of blood, it is *kasher* and may be sprinkled on the *mizbeach*.

This standard is notably different from the *kashrut* context. In food law, the key criterion for nullification is taste — even a trace detectable amount of a forbidden substance is enough to render something *treif*. Here, however, we are not dealing with food, and the dominant criterion shifts to visual appearance. *Tosafot* clarifies that "the appearance of blood" means something precise: *lo be-admumit me'at makhshir* — a slight reddish tinge is not sufficient. *Ela mar'it dam gamur ka-amar* — it must genuinely look like blood itself, not merely like a pinkish liquid. *Li-mi'utei heikha de-deiha mar'iso* — a pale, watered-down appearance does not qualify.

There is an interesting asymmetry here. In *kashrut*, even a trace amount of the forbidden substance — sufficient to impart flavor — renders the mixture *treif*; a tiny contaminant disqualifies. But here, to establish that the blood remains *kasher* and usable, it is not enough that there be a little blood present; it must truly look like blood. For disqualification, a little goes a long way; for qualification, a strong positive presence is required.

### Blood Wine

If blood becomes mixed with wine, the rule is: *ro'in oto ke-ilu hu mayim* — we treat the wine as though it were water, and then assess whether the mixture would still look like blood in water. If yes, it is *kasher*. *Tosafot* finds this surprising: this is actually a *leniency*, since one would have thought to simply say the red wine overwhelms the blood's appearance and renders it invalid. Instead, we imaginatively substitute water for the wine, which is a less visually dominant medium, and assess the blood's visibility in that hypothetical. *Tosafot* acknowledges some difficulty in identifying the source for this principle, since treating a present substance as something else is not a standard rule of *bitul*.

### Blood Blood

Similarly, if *korban* blood becomes mixed with non-*korban* blood — blood of an animal that was not a sacrifice — the rule is again: *ro'in oto ke-ilu hu mayim* — we treat the non-*korban* blood as if it were water. If the *korban* blood would still appear as blood in that quantity of water, the mixture is *kasher*.

### RY No Null

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood does not nullify blood. According to him, when blood of a *korban* mixes with non-*korban* blood, no *bitul* occurs regardless of proportions, and one can always proceed to *zerikat ha-dam*. The mixture is never invalidated through quantity, because like-substances do not nullify one another.

### YK Blood

Rabbi Yochanan states that both the Sages and Rabbi Yehuda derive their respective positions from the same verse. On Yom Kippur, the *Kohen Gadol* took blood from the bull and blood from the goat — *ve-lakach mi-dam ha-par u-mi-dam ha-sa'ir* — and it is understood that he mixed these two bloods together before applying them to the *karnot* of the *mizbeach*. Yet the Torah still refers to them as both present: the blood of the bull and the blood of the goat remain distinct. This is the shared proof-text. The question is what principle each derives from it. *Ha-davar yadua* — it is well known that the blood of an ox is considerably greater in volume than that of a goat, yet the mixture is still described as containing both. Why?

### Olim

The Rabbis derive: *mikan le-olin she-ein mevatlin zeh et zeh* — from here we learn that things which go up on the *mizbeach* — *korbanot* — do not effect *bitul* upon one another. The reason the mixture is described as still containing both bloods is not because of any inherent property of blood, but because both are *korban* bloods destined for the *mizbeach*. This principle resolves a recurring puzzle from Zevachim, where *mishna* after *mishna* treated cases of mixed *korbanot* — a *chatat* mixed with other animals, various blood mixtures — without invoking *bitul*. The operative rule underlying all those cases is *ein olin mevatlin zeh et zeh*: when the mixed substances are both *korbanot*, they do not nullify each other. Non-*korban* blood, however, can effect *bitul* on *korban* blood.

### Min Bemin

Rabbi Yehuda derives something entirely different from the same verse: *mikan le-min be-mino she-eino batel* — from here we learn that like nullifies not like. According to him, the reason both bloods remain present after mixing is not their *korban* status but the simple fact that they are both blood. The principle is general and has nothing to do with *korbanot* at all. In the realm of *kashrut*, according to Rabbi Yehuda, a drop of *treif* chicken soup falling into kosher chicken soup — however large the quantity of kosher soup — would not be nullified, because the substances are identical: *min be-mino lo batel*. This is a sweeping and practically consequential position. Indeed, *Rashi* rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda on this point, though the accepted *halakha* does not follow *Rashi*.

### Feature Debate

The *Gemara* now probes why each side learns its respective principle rather than the other. The Yom Kippur verse has two features simultaneously: the bloods are *olin* (offered on the *mizbeach*), and they are *min be-mino* (like and like — both blood). Each feature could independently explain why *bitul* is absent. What drives the Rabbis to emphasize the *olin* feature, and what drives Rabbi Yehuda to emphasize the *min be-mino* feature?

The *Gemara* argues: had the Torah provided an example of *olin* with substances that are not *min be-mino* — say, blood and wine — the Rabbis would have a clean proof for the *olin* principle without ambiguity. Since the example happens to also be *min be-mino*, one might counter that it is the *min be-mino* feature that matters. The *Gemara* replies: *hashta de-ashmi'inan olin* — since the Torah chose an example within the world of *olin*, we assume it is the *olin* feature that is determinative. But then: *ve-dilma ad de-ika min be-mino ve-olin* — perhaps you need both features simultaneously: only when substances are both *olin* and *min be-mino* is there no *bitul*? *Kashya* — this remains difficult.

The symmetrical challenge applies to Rabbi Yehuda: had the Torah given an example of *olin* of different types — not blood and blood but two distinct substances — we would have a clean proof for the *olin* principle. *Hashta de-ashmi'inan min be-mino* — since the Torah's example happens to be blood and blood, Rabbi Yehuda concludes that the *min be-mino* feature is salient. But again: perhaps both features are required together? *Kashya*.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that it cannot resolve which single feature — or whether the combination of features — is the true determinant. In the end, the Rabbis intuitively read the verse as establishing a *korban* exception, while Rabbi Yehuda intuitively reads it as establishing the logical principle that like does not nullify like. There is a certain coherent rationale available for each position: *bitul* depends on loss of identity through difference, which is why identical substances retain their identity and resist nullification (Rabbi Yehuda's logic); and there is equally good reason to see this as one of many special rules that govern the world of *korbanot* specifically (the Rabbis' logic). The *Gemara* is candid that neither derivation can be fully grounded from the text alone.

### Liquid Solid

*Tosafot* addresses a problem: throughout Zevachim and elsewhere in *Shas*, there appear to be cases where Rabbi Yehuda seems to admit *bitul* even in cases of *min be-mino*, which would contradict his principle. *Tosafot* works through these cases and arrives at what it considers its most satisfying resolution: a distinction between *lach be-lach* and *yavesh be-yavesh*. Rabbi Yehuda's principle that *min be-mino lo batel* applies specifically to liquid mixtures. When two liquids of the same type combine, they form a single homogeneous mass with no distinguishable identity for the minority component — and Rabbi Yehuda holds that a substance that retains the same identity as the majority cannot be said to lose itself in it. In the case of solids — *yavesh be-yavesh* — the situation is different. Each discrete item retains its separate physical identity, and one can pick up each piece individually and ask: is this the *kasher* one or the *treif* one? The determination is then made by statistical majority (*rov*) rather than by the logic of identity-loss. Rabbi Yehuda may well admit *bitul* in that context. *Tosafot* acknowledges that this distinction does not perfectly resolve every case but regards it as the clearest available answer.

## Mencha Mixture

### Similar Mix Q

The *Gemara* now returns to our *Mishna*'s case: the *kometz* mixed with a *minchat nesachim* or *minchat kohen mashiach*, which Rabbi Yehuda holds is *pesulah* because one has a thick consistency and the other thin, and they absorb from one another. The *Gemara* asks a sharp question: *ve-chi balu ze mi-ze mai havei* — so they absorbed from each other; why does that matter? *Min be-mino hu* — it is all *min be-mino*! The oil from the batter-*mincha* that seeps into the dough-*kometz* is mixing with oil that is already there. Both are oil. The flour is mixing with flour. According to Rabbi Yehuda himself, *min be-mino lo batel* — like does not nullify like. So the oil from the *minchat nesachim* that enters the *kometz* should not be *batel* at all, and vice versa. What is the *pesul*?

*Tosafot* is candid that he finds the *Gemara*'s framing puzzling from the outset. The problem with the mixture seems straightforwardly physical: by absorbing from each other, the two *menachot* change their actual oil-to-flour ratios. You no longer have a proper dough-*kometz* and a proper batter-*minchat nesachim*; you have a dough that has become somewhat looser and a batter that has become somewhat denser. Neither one conforms any longer to its required specification. This has nothing to do with *bitul* — it is simply a question of physical alteration of the *korban*. Yet the *Gemara* insists on framing the issue as one of *bitul*, which is what necessitates the answer that follows.

### Flour Nulls

Rava answers: *kasavar Rabbi Yehuda kol she-hu min be-mino ve-davar acher* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that when you have a mixture of *min be-mino* but also *davar acher* — a third, different substance — the rule changes. *Salek et mino ke-mi she-eino* — remove the like-substance from the equation; treat it as if it were not there. *Ve-she-eino mino raba alav u-mevatlo* — the unlike substance then predominates over and nullifies the minority component.

Applied to our case: the oil from the *minchat nesachim* seeps into the *kometz*. Now, there is already oil in the *kometz* — that oil is *min be-mino* and will not create *bitul*. But there is also flour in the *kometz* that is a *davar acher* relative to the absorbed oil. So: ignore the oil-to-oil aspect (it does nothing either way), and what you are left with is: absorbed oil surrounded by flour. The flour is a *davar acher*, and it will *mevatel* the absorbed oil. The absorbed oil loses its identity, meaning the *kometz* is now missing whatever oil was nullified out of it — and the receiving *mincha* is correspondingly deficient. Hence *pesulah*.

### Sages View

This resolution, while addressing Rabbi Yehuda's position, creates a difficulty for the Sages, who rule that such a mixture is *kasher*. If Rava's analysis is correct and there is a genuine *bitul* problem — the flour nullifying the absorbed oil — why do the Sages not agree? The Sages certainly accept the principle of *bitul* in general. If the oil is truly being nullified by the flour, the Sages should also see a problem here. The answer must be that the Sages do not see the physical mixing of these *korbanot* as generating a *bitul* issue at all — perhaps because, as *Tosafot* initially suggested, the problem is one of changed physical proportions rather than *bitul*, and the Sages are not concerned with that degree of incidental absorption. The tension between these positions — and between the *Gemara*'s *bitul*-framing and *Tosafot*'s alternative physical account — is left without full resolution.
