# Bava Batra 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Payment Amount

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי מַה שֶּׁגָּדַר. חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the following point. **Rav Huna says** that when Rabbi Yosei said the court obligates the owner of the inner field to pay his share for **all** of the partitions, he pays **in accordance with what** the other person actually spent when he **built the partitions.** That is, the owner of the inner field must contribute his share according to the cost of the partition his neighbor built. **Ḥiyya bar Rav says:** He must pay his share for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds,** and no more. The owner of the surrounded field can claim he had no desire for a more substantial partition.

The daf opens in the middle of interpreting the mishna's case of one who builds walls in a valley where the inner resident cannot be compelled to participate. The mishna established that three walls built without the inner resident's agreement impose no payment obligation on him. Rabbi Yose, however, holds that once a fourth wall is built — thereby completing the enclosure — the inner resident (*nikaf*) can be made to pay. The Gemara has already cited Shmuel to the effect that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda and like Rabbi Yose, and further that this obligation holds even if it was the outer party (*makif*) alone, not the *nikaf*, who built the walls: since the enclosure confers clear benefit, the *nikaf* must pay.

The question that now arises is: how much must he pay? On this point the Gemara records a dispute. *Itmar*: it was stated. Rav Huna holds *hakol lepi mah she-gadar* — the inner resident pays according to the actual building materials used in the wall and his proportional share of their cost. The payment is benchmarked to the real wall as constructed.

### Chiya bar Rav

Chiya bar Rav disagrees. He holds *hakol lefi d'mei kanim be-zol* — the inner resident pays only the equivalent cost of cheap reed or stick fencing, regardless of how expensive the actual wall was. Since the *nikaf* never agreed to any construction, and it is not clear at this stage of the discussion whether he even affirmatively demonstrated desire for the enclosure, he cannot be compelled to pay as though he had commissioned a stone wall. He pays only for the benefit he actually received, and that benefit — having his field protected — could have been achieved even with a cheap wooden fence. So the assessment is pegged to that minimal benchmark.

### Benefit vs. Cost

This debate calls to mind a principle from Bava Kamma: *meshalem mah she-nehenis* — one who indirectly receives benefit from another pays according to the benefit received, not necessarily according to the cost incurred by the other party or the actual market value of the thing. The canonical illustration is a case of *shein* occurring on public ground (*reshus ha-rabbim*): if my sheep wanders and eats your prize produce, I do not pay you the market price of that produce; I pay only what I would have spent to feed my sheep otherwise — the measure of my benefit, assessed at its most minimal level. This is precisely the framework Chiya bar Rav is applying. The wall was not constructed at the *nikaf*'s request, so the benefit is assessed in the most minimal terms possible.

### Reconciling Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמַּקִּיף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ רוּחוֹתָיו, וְגָדַר אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה וְאֶת הַשְּׁלִישִׁית – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. הָא רְבִיעִית – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אִם עָמַד וְגָדַר אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof from what **we learned** in the mishna: With regard to **one who surrounds another on three sides,** that is, he owns parcels of land on three sides of the other person's field, **and he built a partition** on **the first, the second, and the third** sides, the court **does not obligate** the neighbor who owns the inner field to contribute to the construction of the partition. By inference, if he also built a partition on the **fourth** side of the field, the court **does obligate** the owner of the inner field to contribute to the building of the partition. The Gemara continues with its proof: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **Rabbi Yosei says: If he arose and built a partition** on **the fourth** side of the field, the court **imposes upon** the owner of the inner field the responsibility to pay his share for **all** of the partitions. The first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei seem to be stating the same ruling.

The Gemara now attempts to read this debate into the language of the mishna itself. *Tnan*: the mishna states that once three walls are built, *ein mechayevin oso* — the inner resident pays nothing. The implication of the *Tanna Kamma* is that if the fourth wall is added, *mechayevin oso* — he does owe something. And Rabbi Yose uses the words *megalgelin alav et hakol* — "we roll upon him everything." The Gemara proposes that according to Rav Huna's framework — where payment reflects the actual wall — the debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yose can be understood as a dispute about how much is paid: the *Tanna Kamma* implicitly sanctions only minimal payment (*hakol lefi d'mei kanim be-zol*), while Rabbi Yose's emphatic *et hakol* signals payment according to the full cost of the wall as built (*hakol lefi mah she-gadar*). This is a coherent and natural reading of Rabbi Yose's language.

### Wood Fence Cost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי מַה שֶּׁגָּדַר בָּהּ, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל – אִין, וּמַה שֶּׁגָּדַר – לָא; וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי מַה שֶּׁגָּדַר. אֶלָּא לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב, דְּאָמַר: הַכֹּל לְפִי דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? אִי דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל לָא קָיָהֵיב לֵיהּ, מַאי קָיָהֵיב לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Rav Huna, who says** that the owner of the inner field pays for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with what** the other person actually spent when he **built the partitions, this is** the difference **between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei: The first *tanna* maintains** that **yes,** he pays his share for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds, but not** a larger sum in accordance with **what** the other person actually spent when he **built the partitions. And Rabbi Yosei maintains** that he pays his share for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with what** the other person actually spent when he **built the partitions.** **But according to Ḥiyya bar Rav, who says** he pays his share for **all** of the partitions **in accordance with the value of** a partition fashioned from **inexpensive reeds, what** difference **is there between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei? If** according to the first *tanna* **he does not give him** his share for all of the partitions in accordance with **a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds, what does he give him?**

But if we adopt Chiya bar Rav's position — that even Rabbi Yose means only the cost of a cheap wooden fence — the Gemara asks: *mai ika bein Tanna Kamma le-Rabbi Yose*? What distinguishes them? Both seem to say the *nikaf* owes something once the fourth wall goes up; if Rabbi Yose's *et hakol* means only cheap sticks, what could the *Tanna Kamma* possibly be saying that is still less? If even the *Tanna Kamma* requires payment of something, and the floor is the cost of a cheap fence, there is no room beneath Rabbi Yose for the *Tanna Kamma* to occupy.

### Watchman Wages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲגַר נְטִירָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֲגַר נְטִירָא – אִין, דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל – לָא; וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: דְּמֵי קָנִים בְּזוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say there is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **the wage of a watchman. The first *tanna* maintains** that **yes,** since his field is safeguarded by the partition which surrounds it, the one who built the partition can demand payment of **the wage of a watchman.** He cannot, however, demand the cost of building the partition, **not** even the other's share for all of the partitions in accordance with **a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds. And Rabbi Yosei maintains** that he can demand the other's share for all of the partitions in accordance with **a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds.**

The Gemara answers: *ibayeit eima agar netira ika beinaihu* — there is in fact something less than a cheap wood fence: the wages one would pay a watchman (*agar netira*) to guard the field. Rather than paying for any physical structure at all, the *nikaf* pays only what it would cost to hire someone to watch over the field. In an economy without minimum wage, that might well be less than even rudimentary fencing materials, though the calculation over time obviously raises questions. The point conceptually is that protection of the field is the operative benefit, and that benefit is achievable without any wall at all — through human oversight. So the *Tanna Kamma* holds that the *nikaf* pays only *agar netira*, the cost equivalent of a watchman, and no more; while Rabbi Yose holds he pays at least the cost of a cheap wood fence, a somewhat higher floor. Note too that in a field, the relevant benefit is protection from intruders and animals — not privacy, which is not a concern in an open agricultural setting. So *agar netira* captures that benefit directly and at minimal cost.

## Which Walls Paid

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: רִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: רְבִיעִית הוּא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ, אֲבָל רִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית – לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ; וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: רִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁנִיָּיה וּשְׁלִישִׁית נָמֵי יָהֵיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say there is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the partitions built on the **first, second, and third** sides of the inner field. **The first *tanna* maintains** that the owner of the inner field must **give to** his neighbor money for the partition built on the **fourth** side, **but** he is **not** required to **give him** money for the partitions built on the **first, second, and third** sides. **And Rabbi Yosei maintains** that **he** must **also give him** money **for** the partitions built on **the first, the second, and the third** sides.

The Gemara now proposes a third way to distinguish the *Tanna Kamma* from Rabbi Yose, bracketing the question of how much per wall one pays, and instead asking: for which walls does the *nikaf* owe anything? *Ibayeit eima rishona sheniya ve-shlishit ika beinaihu*. Perhaps the dispute is not about the cost metric but about the scope of payment. On this reading, we assume in both positions that the assessment is based on a cheap wood fence — that much is agreed — but the *Tanna Kamma* holds that once the *makif* builds the fourth wall and triggers the payment obligation, the *nikaf* pays only for the fourth wall: *revi'it hu de-yahiv lei aval rishona sheniya ve-shlishit lo yahiv lei*. The first three walls were already built and standing; the *nikaf* is not made to pay retroactively for them. Only the fourth wall, which completed the enclosure and created the triggering condition, is charged to the *nikaf*.

### Rabbi Yose

Rabbi Yose, by contrast, holds *rishona sheniya ve-shlishit nami yahiv lei* — the *nikaf* pays for all four walls, because he benefits from all four. The word *et hakol* in Rabbi Yose's formulation is taken to mean the entirety: all the walls that together constitute the enclosure from which the *nikaf* benefits. Since the enclosure as a whole is what provides the protection, and the *nikaf* benefits from the whole, there is no principled reason to exclude the first three walls from the accounting.

### Rashi Explanation

Why would the *Tanna Kamma* exempt the *nikaf* from the first three walls? Rashi explains it in terms of time and *status quo*. The first three walls were built, and for some period — a month, a year, an indeterminate time — that was simply how things stood. Three walls, one side open: animals and thieves could still enter freely. During that period, the *nikaf* received no real benefit. That was the accepted state of affairs, a historical *status quo*. When the fourth wall was finally built, we treat only that final wall as the one that created the benefit and therefore the obligation. The three prior walls have already been absorbed into the background. Rashi's approach is intuitive, though it raises the question of what length of time is required to establish that *status quo* — a day? a week? a year? — and the Gemara does not specify.

### Tosafot Explanation

Tosafot offers a conceptually different account. *Omer Ri dehainu ta'ama desheni ve-shlishi d'ein lecha adam shelo haya mesaye'a la-revi'it al m'nat sheyigdor me'arba ruchoteha*: in principle, someone who builds without another's agreement or participation cannot compel payment for anything. But every normal person would agree to chip in for a fourth wall if it ensured their property was fully enclosed. There is an implicit presumption that any reasonable person in the *nikaf*'s position would have desired the fourth wall. This is a conceptual shift: the *Tanna Kamma* is not simply exempting the first three walls on historical grounds but is saying that the basis of any obligation at all is implicit consent — and implicit consent extends only to the completing fourth wall, not to the prior three. Rabbi Yose, by contrast, focuses on actual benefit received: if you benefit from four walls, you pay for four walls. Tosafot's approach is somewhat more abstract than Rashi's, though Rashi's is more straightforwardly grounded in the language and facts of the mishna.

## Who Built Wall

### First Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַקִּיף וְנִיקָּף אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סוֹבֵר: טַעְמָא דְּעָמַד נִיקָּף דִּמְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל, אֲבָל עָמַד מַקִּיף – אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶלָּא דְּמֵי רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another difference between the two opinions: **If you wish, say there is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the issue of whether the partition on the fourth side was built by the owner of the **surrounding** field **or** by the owner of the **surrounded** field. This is **because the first *tanna* maintains** that **the reason** the owner of the **surrounded** field must contribute his share of the entire expense is **that he arose** and built a partition on the fourth side of his field. Therefore, the court **imposes upon him** the responsibility to pay his share for **all** of the partitions, because his actions demonstrate that he wants the partition between their fields. **But** if the owner of the **surrounding** field **arose** and built a partition on the fourth side of the field, the owner of the surrounded field **must give him only** his share of **the value of** the partition of the **fourth** side.

The Gemara now turns to a fourth possible way to read the dispute. *Ibayeit eima makif ve-nikaf ika beinaihu* — the debate may turn on who actually built the fourth wall: the *makif* (the outer party who built the first three) or the *nikaf* (the inner resident). The Gemara has been reading the mishna in one particular way throughout, and now introduces an alternative reading that bears directly on the phrase *im amad ve-gadar* in Rabbi Yose's formulation. At this point in the discussion, the Gemara is still treating the question of payment amount and the question of who builds the fourth wall as interconnected, and working through the implications of each combination.

### Second Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא נִיקָּף וְלָא שְׁנָא מַקִּיף, אִם עָמַד וְגָדַר – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: מַקִּיף וְנִיקָּף אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אִם גָּדַר מַקִּיף אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – נָמֵי יָהֵיב לֵיהּ; וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: אִם עָמַד נִיקָּף וְגָדַר אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – הוּא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ, דְּגַלִּי דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ; אֲבָל אִם גָּדַר מַקִּיף – לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei maintains** that it is **no different** if it was the owner of the **surrounded** field who arose and built a partition on the fourth side, **and** it is **no different** if it was the owner of the **surrounding** field who arose and built a partition on the fourth side. **If** either one **arose and built a partition** on the fourth side, the court **imposes upon** the owner of the inner field the obligation to pay his share for **all** of the partitions. The Gemara reports **another version** of this last answer: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the issue of whether the partition on the fourth side was built by the owner of the **surrounding** field **or** by the owner of the **surrounded** field. **The first *tanna* maintains** that even **if** the owner of the **surrounding** field **built a partition** on **the fourth** side, the owner of the surrounded field **also gives him** his share of the cost of the partitions. **And Rabbi Yosei maintains** that only **if** the owner of the **surrounded** field **arose** and **built a partition** on **the fourth** side **does he give** the owner of the surrounding field his share of the cost of the partitions. Why is that? **Because he reveals that he is pleased** with the partitions. **But if** the owner of the **surrounding** field **built a partition** on the fourth side, the owner of the surrounded field **does not give him anything,** as he can continue to claim that he has no interest in the partitions.

*Lishna achrina* — an alternative version of the reading: the Gemara presents a cleaner, more textually consistent account of the *makif/nikaf* dimension of the dispute. This second reading is framed as more faithful to the plain sense of the mishna, which opens with the *makif* building walls one through three. The implication of the *Tanna Kamma* in the mishna's own framing is that the *makif* builds wall four as well, and the *nikaf* pays.

### Tanna Kamma View

According to this second reading, the *Tanna Kamma* holds: *im gadar makif et ha-revi'it nami yahiv lei* — if the outer party goes ahead and builds the fourth wall, the inner resident pays. This is the plain reading of the mishna: it is the *makif* who has been building throughout, and once he completes the fourth wall, the obligation kicks in. The *Tanna Kamma* does not require the *nikaf* to have done anything; the *makif*'s completion of the enclosure is sufficient to trigger payment.

### Rabbi Yose View

Rabbi Yose on this reading holds differently: *im amad nikaf ve-gadar et ha-revi'it hu de-yahiv lei — degali da'atei de-nicha lei*. Only if the *nikaf* himself gets up and builds the fourth wall does the full payment obligation arise, because only then has he clearly revealed his preference (*gala da'ato*) for the enclosure. *Aval im gadar makif lo yahiv lei midi* — but if the *makif* builds the fourth wall, the *nikaf* pays nothing at all. On this reading, Rabbi Yose is actually the more lenient position: he requires the *nikaf*'s own affirmative act before any obligation attaches, whereas the *Tanna Kamma* is willing to impose payment even when the *makif* alone built everything.

This is surprising, and the Gemara acknowledges the tension. Rabbi Yose's language — *megalgelin alav et hakol*, "we roll upon him everything" — sounds like the more demanding position. Yet on this reading it is the *Tanna Kamma* who imposes payment when the *makif* builds the wall, while Rabbi Yose actually requires a higher showing before the *nikaf* can be held liable. The Gemara is consistent with its treatment of Rabbi Yose throughout as the one more inclined to impose liability on the *nikaf*, but the second reading of this last alternative creates a real tension with those earlier assumptions. These different dimensions — how much is paid, for which walls, and conditioned on who builds the fourth wall — are all potentially interconnected. One could naturally link them: if the *nikaf* himself builds the fourth wall and thereby signals desire for the entire enclosure, perhaps he pays the higher rate based on the actual cost of the walls; if the *makif* builds it and the *nikaf* never participated, perhaps he pays only a minimal amount, or perhaps nothing at all. These possibilities remain open.

## Ravina-Runya Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּנְיָא, אַקְּפֵיהּ רָבִינָא מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹתָיו. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי כַּמָּה דִּגְדַרִי״, לָא יְהֵיב לֵיהּ. ״הַב לִי לְפִי קָנִים בְּזוֹל״, לָא יְהֵיב לֵיהּ. ״הַב לִי אֲגַר נְטִירוּתָא״, לָא יְהֵיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that a man named **Ronya** had a field that **was surrounded by** fields belonging to **Ravina on** all **four sides.** Ravina built partitions around his fields and **said to him: Give me** your share of the expense **in accordance with what I** actually spent when I **built the partitions,** i.e., half the cost of the partitions. Ronya **did not give** it **to him.** Ravina said to him: **Give me** then at least your share of the expense **in accordance with a reduced** assessment of the **price of reeds.** Ronya **did not give** it **to him.** Ravina said to him: **Give me** then at least **the wage of a watchman.** But **he did not give** even this **to him.**

The Gemara now moves from abstract debate to a concrete legal incident involving two real figures: Ravina and Runya, neighbors with neighboring fields. *Akfei Ravina me'arba ruchotav* — Ravina surrounded Runya's field with walls from all four sides. His own fields surrounded Runya's on all sides, and he built walls throughout. This is thus precisely the scenario of our mishna, with Ravina as the *makif* and Runya as the *nikaf* — except that here the *makif* did everything, all four walls, without the *nikaf*'s participation.

### Payment Dispute

Ravina came to Runya and said: *hav li kamma de-gadrat* — pay me according to the cost of the wall, invoking the position of Rav Huna (*hakol lefi mah she-gadar*). Runya refused — he had never requested a stone wall and would not pay for one.

### Compromise Offers

Ravina then descended to lesser demands. Fine, pay me at least according to the cost of a cheap wood fence — *lefi d'mei kanim be-zol*, in the manner of Chiya bar Rav. *Lo yahiv lei* — Runya still refused. Fine, pay me at least *agar netira*, watchman's wages, the most minimal possible measure of the benefit you received. *Lo yahiv lei* — Runya refused even that.

### Date-Stealing Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָא גָדֵר דִּיקְלֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲרִיסֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל מִנֵּיהּ קִיבּוּרָא דַאֲהִינֵי. אֲזַל לְאֵתוֹיֵי, רְמָא בֵּיהּ קָלָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּלִּית דַּעְתָּךְ דְּמִינַּח נִיחָא לָךְ; לָא יְהֵא אֶלָּא עִיזָּא בְּעָלְמָא, מִי לָא בָּעֵי נְטִירוּתָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עִיזָּא בְּעָלְמָא, לָאו לְאַכְלוֹיֵי בָּעֲיָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא גַּבְרָא בָּעֵית דְּמַיכְלֵי לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **One day,** Ronya **was harvesting dates.** Ravina **said to his sharecropper: Go take a cluster [*kibbura*] of dates from him.** The sharecropper **went to bring them,** but Ronya **raised his voice at him** in protest, whereupon Ravina **said to him: You have revealed that you are pleased** with the partitions and the protection that they provide you. Even **if it were only a goat** that entered your field, **wouldn't** the field **need safeguarding,** to prevent the goat from eating the dates? Ronya **said to him:** If it were **only a goat, doesn't** one **need** merely **to chase it away [*le'akhluyei*]?** No partition is required. Ravina **said to him: But wouldn't you need a man to chase** the goat **away?** Pay me then at least the wage of a watchman.

At this point, Ravina took a different approach. One day he noticed Runya was out harvesting dates from his palm trees — the verb *gadar* carrying the sense of gathering or harvesting, not only of building. Ravina told his sharecropper: *zil shkol minei kivura d'ahini* — go and take a cluster of dates from him. The sharecropper went to grab the dates. *Rama bei kala* — Runya immediately cried out: stop, get away from my dates! Ravina responded: *galui ishtachach de-minaich nicha lach* — you have now shown that you want your field protected. You yourself just demonstrated that you care about securing what is inside these walls.

The argument is admittedly somewhat specious — Runya objected because Ravina's own agent was attempting to take the dates, not because of any general risk. The walls would not have prevented Ravina's agent from entering in the first place. But Ravina's point was the general one: any person wants their field to be protected.

### Goat Protection

Ravina pressed further: *lo yehei ela iza b'alma mi lo ba'i netiruta* — even if you are unconcerned about thieves, surely you need protection from goats. Any normal person wants their property secured, if not from human intruders then from stray animals. Your field clearly benefits from these walls.

### Runya's Defense

Runya responded: *iza b'alma lachluyei ba'i* — if all I needed was protection from goats, I could deal with that by simply yelling at them, as I just yelled at your agent. I do not need a wall for that; I would merely have hired someone to shout at any stray animal that wandered near. Ravina came back: *ve-lo gavra ba'it de-machlei lach?* — but you are not in the field every day. If you were serious about that protection, you would have hired a watchman to be there regularly to yell. So at minimum you are getting the benefit of what it would have cost you to employ such a watchman — *agar netira*.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל פַּיְּיסֵיהּ בְּמַאי דְּאִיפַּיַּיס; וְאִי לָא, דָּאֵינְנָא לָךְ דִּינָא כְּרַב הוּנָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **came before Rava** to adjudicate the matter. Rava **said to** Ronya: **Go appease** Ravina **with what he** expressed his willingness to be **appeased with,** namely, the wage of a watchman. **And if not, I will judge you in accordance with** the ruling of **Rav Huna in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and you will be required to pay half the cost of the partition based on what Ravina actually spent on it.

The case was brought before Rava. Rava told Runya: *zil payisei be-mai de-mifayis* — go and settle with Ravina on whatever he will accept. Ravina had already shown he was willing to take less than the full wall cost — he would accept the wood-fence equivalent or even watchman's wages. So go and pay him something within that range. *Ve-im lo* — and if you refuse to make even that minimal payment, *dayinan lach dina ke-Rav Huna aliba de-Rabbi Yose* — I will rule against you according to Rav Huna's reading of Rabbi Yose, which means payment based on the full cost of the actual stone wall. The implication is that this is the most stringent reading: Rabbi Yose's *et hakol* means full payment, and Rav Huna applies that even in a case where it was the *makif* who built all four walls.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot raises the question of whether Rava's threat was genuine or merely rhetorical — a device to pressure Runya into accepting the compromise. The Gemara elsewhere employs recognizable hyperbolic phrases when rabbis threaten litigants: "I will seize your home," "I will not let you be," and so on. These phrases signal that the actual *halacha* may be less severe and that the threat is persuasive rather than substantive. Here, however, Rava used none of those standard formulas. Tosafot concludes that the absence of hyperbolic language suggests that Rava's ruling was the genuine *psak*: the actual *halacha* is full payment according to the cost of the wall, and because Ravina was willing to accept less, Rava urged Runya to take advantage of that generosity. But if Runya remained intransigent, the full amount would indeed be imposed.

## Bar Metzra Case

### Bar Metzra Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּנְיָא זְבֵן אַרְעָא אַמִּיצְרָא דְּרָבִינָא. סְבַר רָבִינָא לְסַלּוֹקֵי מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא; אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יֵיבָא לְרָבִינָא, אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אַרְבְּעָה לְצַלָּא, אַרְבְּעָה לְצַלָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** Incidental to that episode, the Gemara relates another encounter between Ravina and Ronya. **Ronya** once **bought land adjoining** property belonging to **Ravina. Ravina considered removing him due to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. When land is up for sale, the owners of the adjoining fields have the right of first refusal. If one of the neighbors is willing to match the highest price being offered to the seller, that neighbor has the preemptive right to purchase the property, and if somebody else buys it, that buyer can be removed. Since Ravina owned the adjacent property, he thought that he enjoyed the right of first refusal. **Rav Safra, son of Rav Yeiva, said to Ravina: People say: Four** dinars **for a large hide [*tzalla*], four for a small hide [*tzelala*].** Since Ronya also owned land bordering the desired parcel, you cannot remove him even though his plot of land is smaller than yours.

The Gemara now introduces a separate incident between the same two parties. *Runia zaven ar'a ametzra de-Ravina* — Runya purchased a field that was on the boundary (*ametzra*) of Ravina's field. *Savar Ravina le-silukei mishum dina de-bar metzra* — Ravina thought he could force Runya off and take possession of the purchased field, invoking the law of *dina de-bar metzra*, the right of first refusal that belongs to an adjacent neighbor. The logic of *dina de-bar metzra* is that contiguous land is worth considerably more to a neighbor than to a stranger: a neighbor who already farms the surrounding land can work a larger, unified plot more efficiently — plowing it all at once, for instance — so the adjacent field has greater value to him than to any outside buyer. The neighbor is therefore entitled to priority when adjacent land comes up for sale.

Importantly, it appears from the Gemara that even after the sale has been completed, the neighbor with first-refusal rights may have the ability to negate the transaction entirely — not merely a claim for compensation against the seller, but a right to step into the buyer's shoes and annul the sale.

Rav Safra, son of Rav Yeiva, said to Ravina: people say — *amri inshi* — *arba letzalla arba letzlalla*. This cryptic phrase requires unpacking, and Rashi offers three readings of its meaning and relevance.

### Principle

The foundation of *dina de-bar metzra* is the verse *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — "and you shall do what is right and good" (Devarim 6:18). One could argue that a property owner has an absolute right to sell to whomever he chooses. The answer of the tradition is: be a *mensch*. If you will receive the same price regardless, why sell to a stranger rather than to your neighbor who will benefit far more? *Ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* demands that the neighbor be offered first refusal. Crucially, however, this ethical imperative was not left as a moral aspiration — it was codified as *dina*, law. The very term *dina de-bar metzra* signals that this has become a legal obligation enforceable in court.

### Rashi Reading 1

Rashi's first reading of *arba letzalla arba letzlalla* understands it as a contrast between a poor person and a wealthy one — *tzalla* suggesting a poor figure and *tzlalla* a wealthy one. In this reading, Rav Safra is invoking a different dimension of *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov*: yes, Ravina has a legal right as adjacent neighbor, but Runya is poor and Ravina is rich. The *mensch*-like thing to do is to let the poor man keep his small plot. You, Ravina, would expand from a thousand contiguous acres to a thousand and two; Runya may have no other ready option. The saying — four for the poor man, four for the wealthy man — captures the idea that both need to eat, both need to sustain themselves, and the powerful should not simply use legal entitlement to crowd out the vulnerable.

### Rashi Reading 3

Rashi's third reading is the most analytically compelling. On this reading, Runya himself had a field within or adjacent to the contested area, such that Runya might actually have had his own *bar metzra* claim — perhaps on a different boundary. Ravina could have had a stronger claim as a neighbor on three sides, while Runya was a neighbor on one side only, which might normally give Ravina greater priority. But the phrase *arba letzalla arba letzlalla* is interpreted through an analogy of a poor tanner versus a wealthy tanner. A poor tanner spends four units of expense to process a small piece of leather; a rich tanner spends the same four units to process a large piece. The wealthy tanner already enjoys economy of scale — his fixed costs are distributed across a much larger output, so each unit costs him proportionally less. The poor tanner, with a small operation, bears the full weight of those four units against far less production. Giving the poor tanner the adjacent piece of leather — or the adjacent plot of land — reduces his per-unit cost dramatically; giving it to the rich tanner produces a negligible marginal benefit because he already benefits from scale. On this reading, Runya actually gains more from acquiring the adjacent field than Ravina does, even if Ravina's formal *bar metzra* priority might appear stronger. This third reading has the advantage of remaining within legal rather than purely moral territory.

## Bar Metzra Case — Continued

### Tosafot Challenge

Tosafot, following Rabbeinu Tam, challenges Rashi's first reading sharply: *u-ferush ha-kuntres maksha Rabbeinu Tam — hai merachaminan be-din?* How can we say, within a legal ruling, that we simply take pity on the poor man? *Dina de-bar metzra* was elevated from an ethical aspiration into a *din*. Once it is a *din*, you cannot simply override one person's legal right by appeal to the other party's poverty. It is not one *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* against another *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — it is a codified *din* versus a moral sentiment, and a *din* should prevail.

This is a striking challenge, and the question deserves to be taken seriously. There are cases in the Talmud — notably in Bava Metzia — where a rabbi rules that a wealthy litigant should forgive a debt or pay workers their wages even when not legally required, invoking *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* or *lema'an telech be-derech tovim*. And the language of the Gemara here — *amri inshi*, "people say" — does not necessarily signal a technical legal holding; it could be read as invoking a norm of decent conduct rather than a strict rule. On that reading, it may well be that Rava Safra was not telling Ravina he had no legal claim, but that the right thing to do — *lifnim mishurat ha-din* — was to stand aside. The tension between these readings is not fully resolved here and will receive fuller treatment when *dina de-bar metzra* is addressed directly later in the tractate.

## Fallen Wall

### Shared Wall Falls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כּוֹתֶל חָצֵר שֶׁנָּפַל – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹתוֹ עַד אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁנָּתַן, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a dividing **wall** in a jointly owned **courtyard that fell,** if one of the owners wishes to rebuild the wall, the court **obligates** the other owner **to build** the wall with him **up to** a height of **four cubits.** If after the wall was built one of the neighbors claims he alone constructed it and the other did not participate in its building, the latter is nevertheless **presumed to have given** his share of the money, **unless** the claimant **brings proof that** the other **did not give** his part.

The Gemara now cites the next mishna: *kotel chatzer she-nafal mechayvin oto livnot* — if a shared courtyard wall falls, both parties can be compelled to rebuild it, *ad arba amot* — up to four cubits in height. The rationale, developed earlier in the Gemara, is that even a party who originally did not believe in the claim of *hezek re'iyah* (visual damage) becomes habituated over time to conducting private activities in his yard with the expectation of a wall. Once that wall is gone, real damage results, and both parties share the obligation to rebuild up to the height necessary to restore privacy — four cubits.

### Payment Presumption

The mishna continues: *bechezkat she-natan ad she-yavi re'ayah shelo natan* — once the obligation to rebuild jointly is established, the party who urges rebuilding can compel the other to participate, and the presumption is that the reluctant party *has* paid his share unless the other can bring proof that he has not. Since both are understood to be equally obligated, societal norms create a presumption of payment; and since proving a negative — that payment was never made — is extremely difficult, the burden effectively falls on the one seeking to collect.

### Higher Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וּלְמַעְלָן – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. סָמַךְ לוֹ כּוֹתֶל אַחֵר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן עָלָיו אֶת הַתִּקְרָה – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל. בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁנָּתַן.
>
> **Translation:** The court **does not obligate** the reluctant neighbor to contribute to the building of the wall **higher than four cubits.** But if the reluctant neighbor **built another wall close** to the wall that had been built higher than four cubits, in order to set a roof over the room that was thereby created, the court **imposes upon him** the responsibility to pay his share for **all** of the rebuilt wall, **even though he has not** yet **set a roof over it.** Since he has demonstrated his desire to make use of what his neighbor built, he must participate in the cost of its construction. If the builder of the first wall later claims that he did not receive payment from his neighbor, the neighbor **is presumed not to have given** his share of the money, **unless he brings proof that he did** in fact **give** money for the building of the wall.

The mishna adds: *samach lo kotel acher af al pi she-lo natan alav et ha-tikra megalgelin alav et ha-kol* — if one party built the shared wall to a height greater than four cubits, and the neighbor subsequently built a parallel wall on his own side and appears to be preparing to roof the space between them, the Gemara treats this as a demonstration of the neighbor's desire for the greater height. Just as building the fourth enclosing wall reveals that the *nikaf* wanted the enclosure, building a parallel structure reveals that the neighbor desired and took advantage of the higher wall. In that case, *megalgelin alav et ha-kol* — the neighbor is made to pay his proportionate share of the full height, not just the four-cubit minimum.

### Tosafot Benefit

Tosafot raises a question regarding this case that connects back to the foundational principle of *zeh nehene ve-zeh lo chaser* — one benefits while the other loses nothing. If I have already built a tall wall, and my neighbor simply benefits from its greater height at no additional cost to me, why should he have to pay? The principle from Bava Kamma is that when benefit is received at no cost to the provider, no payment is owed. Tosafot answers in two ways. First, once the neighbor reveals through his actions that he desired the height (*gala da'ato de-nicha lei*), that demonstration of preference changes the analysis — the consent-derived obligation arises independently. Second — and Tosafot's preferred answer — there actually *is* a *chaser* here: the neighbor's presence is what caused the original builder to construct a wall higher than four cubits. Had there been no neighboring field, there would have been no need to build beyond the standard privacy height. The additional height was incurred *because* of the neighbor, even if the neighbor did not directly cause a monetary loss in the moment. That causal connection is sufficient to establish that the benefit was not entirely free; it came at an expense the builder would not otherwise have borne.

## Payment Claims

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַקּוֹבֵעַ זְמַן לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנִּי״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן; וּלְוַאי שֶׁיִּפְרַע בִּזְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish says:** If a lender **set a time for another** to repay the loan that he had extended to him **and** when the debt came due the borrower **said to** the lender: **I** already **repaid you within the time, he is not deemed credible,** as people do not ordinarily repay their debts before they are due. The lender would be happy **if** the borrower **would only repay** his debt **on time.**

The Gemara now turns to a broader principle triggered by the mishna's presumptions of payment. Reish Lakish states: *ha-kove'a zman le-chavero ve-amar lo paraticha be-toch zmani* — if one explicitly fixes a due date for a loan and the borrower later claims "I paid you before the date arrived," *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. The reasoning: *delavay she-yifra be-zmano* — it would be enough of an achievement if people simply paid on time. The presumption runs strongly against early payment.

### Abaye/Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּפָרַע בְּגוֹ זִימְנֵיהּ – זִימְנִין דְּמִתְרְמוּ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, אָמַר: אֵיזִיל אֶיפְרְעֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיטְרְדַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye and Rava** disagree with Reish Lakish, **as they both say: A person is apt to repay** his debt **within its time,** i.e., before it is due. This is because **sometimes he happens to have money** and the borrower **says** to himself: **I will go** and **repay** my debt **so that he will not trouble me** later by constantly demanding the money.

Abaye and Rava both disagree: *avid inish de-para be-go zamneh* — it does happen that a person pays within the loan period. *De-zimnin de-mitramu lei zuzei amar eizil ve-efreih ki heichi de-lo litridin* — sometimes money becomes available unexpectedly, and the borrower thinks: let me pay now so the creditor won't pester me later. That is a recognizable human motivation, and since it does occur, the borrower is believed to claim early payment.

### Shared Wall Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: – בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁנָּתַן, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵימָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּזְמַנִּי״, פְּשִׁיטָא בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁנָּתַן! אֶלָּא לָאו דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךְ בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנִּי״? אַלְמָא עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּפָרְעֵיהּ בְּתוֹךְ זִמְנֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּכׇל שָׂפָא וְשָׂפָא זִימְנֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof in support of the opinion of Abaye and Rava from what **we learned** in the mishna (5a): If after the wall was built one of the neighbors claims he alone constructed it and the other did not participate in its building, the latter is nevertheless **presumed to have given** his share of the money, **unless** the claimant **brings proof that** the other **did not give** his part. The Gemara clarifies the matter: **What are the circumstances** of the case under discussion? **If we say** that **he said to him: I paid you at the time** when the payment became due, when the wall was completed, it is **obvious** that **he is presumed to have given** his part. **Rather, is it not that he said to him: I paid you within the time** before the payment became due, while the wall was still under construction? And with regard to such a case the mishna states that he is presumed to have given his share. **Apparently, a person is apt to repay** his debt **within its time,** in accordance with the opinion of Abaye and Rava. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Here** it **is different, because the time** to pay is upon the completion of **each and every row.** Payment does not become due specifically at the completion of the entire wall.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this dispute from the mishna's presumption. The case of the shared courtyard wall — *bechezkat she-natan* — seems to credit the claim of payment. What is the scenario? If the claim is that payment was made after the wall was complete, that is trivial: of course a debtor is believed to say he paid once the debt was due, at least absent a *shtar*. The *chiddush* of the mishna must therefore be that the neighbor is believed to claim payment *during* the construction — before the wall was finished — suggesting that *avid inish de-para be-go zamneh*, in support of Abaye and Rava.

### Ongoing Construction

The Gemara deflects: *shani hacha d-chol shafa ve-shafa zimneh hu* — this case is different, because construction is an ongoing process. Each course of stones laid is its own expense and its own due date. There is no single lump-sum due date that the payment preceded; each installment is payable as incurred. So the mishna's presumption of payment tells us nothing about whether someone is believed to claim early payment on a conventional fixed-term loan.

### Extra Height Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁנָּתַן. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵימָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּזְמַנִּי״, אַמַּאי לָא? אֶלָּא לָאו דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פְּרַעְתִּיךְ בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנִּי״? אַלְמָא לָא עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּפָרַע בְּגוֹ זִימְנֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר: מִי יֵימַר דִּמְחַיְּיבוּ לִי רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof in support of the opinion of Reish Lakish from the continuation of the mishna (5a): The court does not obligate the reluctant neighbor to contribute to the building of the wall higher than four cubits. But if the reluctant neighbor built another wall close to the wall that had been built higher than four cubits, in order to set a roof over the room that was thereby created, the court imposes upon him the responsibility to pay his share for all of the rebuilt wall, even though he has not yet set a roof over it. If the builder of the first wall later claims he did not receive payment from his neighbor, the neighbor **is presumed not to have given** his share of the money, **unless he brings proof that he did** in fact **give** money for the building of the wall. The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of the case under discussion? **If we say** that **he demanded** payment of the money **after the time** that the payment became due had passed, **and** the borrower **said to him: I** already **repaid you within the time** before the payment became due? **Why** is he **not** deemed credible? **Rather,** is it **not that he said to him: I paid you within the time,** before the payment became due, and therefore he is not deemed credible? **Apparently, a person is not apt to repay** his debt **within its time,** in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Here** it **is different, since** the reluctant neighbor **says: Who says that the Rabbis will obligate me** to pay for this wall? In such a case he certainly does not pay before the payment becomes due.

The Gemara turns to the mishna's second case: *bechezkat she-lo natan ad she-yavi re'ayah she-natan* — the neighbor who built the parallel wall and thereby incurred an obligation for the extra height is presumed *not* to have paid unless he can prove otherwise. The Gemara asks: what is the scenario here? If the claim is that payment was made after the obligation became clear and the debt was fully due, why should the neighbor not be believed — as any debtor is believed to say he paid once the time came?

### Skepticism Argument

The Gemara answers: *shani hacha d-amar mi yeymar de-mechayivei li Rabbanan* — this case is different because the neighbor was genuinely skeptical that *beit din* would impose an obligation on her at all. She built her own parallel wall and did not necessarily accept that she thereby obligated herself for the original builder's extra height. Because she doubted whether the ruling would go against her, she had no motivation to pay preemptively. The presumption is therefore that she did not pay even once the obligation was formally established — and she must prove otherwise. Again, no inference can be drawn from this case about early payment of fixed-term loans.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עָבְדִי כְּאַבַּיֵּי וְרָבָא. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי עָבֵד כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי; וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר מָר: הַבָּא לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים – לֹא יִפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה; חֲזָקָה: לָא עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דְּפָרַע בְּגוֹ זִימְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, acted** in such a case **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye and Rava. Mar bar Rav Ashi acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** that one is not deemed credible when he says that he repaid a loan before it became due. **And** if the debtor dies, the court collects payment **even from** his **orphans** based on this assumption. **And even though the Master said** that **one who comes to collect** money **from the property of orphans cannot collect unless** he first takes **an oath** that he did not already collect the debt from the deceased, here he can collect without taking an oath because there is **a presumption** that **a person is not apt to repay** his debt **within its time.**

The Gemara concludes by stating the law. Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua ruled like Abaye and Rava — a person is believed to say he paid within the loan period. Mar bar Ashi, however, acted like Reish Lakish, holding that early payment claims are not credited. *Ve-hilchata ke-Reish Lakish* — the final ruling follows Reish Lakish. If there was a fixed due date and the borrower claims to have paid before it arrived, he is not believed. *Ve-afilu mi-yasmei* — this applies even when collecting from orphans. Normally, one who seeks to collect from an estate of orphans must take an oath (*shevuah*) that the debt has not been repaid, even if a *shtar* exists, since the orphans cannot know what their father may have arranged. But if the debt is being collected before the due date has arrived, no *shevuah* is required: *chazakah lo avid inish de-para be-go zimneh* — there is an established presumption that people do not pay before the due date, so no realistic possibility of prior payment need be entertained, and the collector proceeds without oath. This presumption is especially strong in a halakhic framework where no interest is charged and where mechanisms like *shemitah* can cancel debts — there is simply no incentive to pay early, making the *chazakah* particularly robust.
