# Gittin 88b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Me'useh

### Mishna Get Me'useh

The penultimate daf of *Gittin* opens with a *mishna* whose legal and moral weight belies its brevity. The *mishna* states: *get me'useh biYisrael kasher* — a *get* that is compelled by a Jewish *beis din* is valid. *Uv'ovdei kochavim pasul* — if non-Jews are the ones compelling it, the *get* is invalid. However, *uv'ovdei kochavim chovtin oto v'omrim lo asei mah sheYisrael omrim lach* — non-Jews may beat the husband and tell him to do what the Jews instruct him. The final word *ve-kasher* appears in the text in parentheses, reflecting a certain ambiguity. Tosafot raises the possibility that even when non-Jews are doing the physical coercion on behalf of a Jewish *beis din*, the *get* might still be problematic. Tosafot ultimately rejects that reading, reasoning that the *dayanim* themselves need not personally carry out the coercive act — they may delegate the unpleasant work. The simple reading, and the one Tosafot endorses, is that if the non-Jewish court is merely doing the dirty work under the supervision of a Jewish *beis din*, the *get* is valid.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

The *gemara* then introduces the ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *ha-get me'useh biYisrael kadin kasher* — a *get* compelled by a Jewish *beis din* where there is a genuine legal mandate, meaning where the law actually obligates the husband to give a *get*, is valid. *Shelo kadin pasul u'posel* — if the court compelled it without a legitimate legal mandate, it is an invalid *get*, but it creates a *re'ach haget* problem: if the husband subsequently dies, the wife would be prohibited from marrying a *kohen*, as there is enough of a "whiff of a *get*" to affect her status for *kehunah*. Turning to the non-Jewish court scenario — again, assuming the non-Jewish court is acting on its own authority, not merely as the arm of a Jewish *beis din* — *kadin pasul u'posel*: if there is a legal mandate, the *get* is invalid because it was not done under Jewish auspices, but it still creates a *re'ach haget* problem. *Shelo kadin* — if it is doubly removed, both non-Jewish and without legal mandate, *afilyu re'ach haget ein bo*: there is not even the faintest whiff of a *get*, and there is no *kehunah* consequence whatsoever.

Notably, the *gemara* throughout this entire discussion does not raise what might seem like the most obvious question: how can a forced *get* be valid at all, given that a *get* must be given *mirtsono*, of the husband's own free will, and a coerced transaction is ordinarily void? The *gemara* elsewhere does address forced transactions — *talu v'zavin*, where a person was strung up until he agreed to sell his property, and the ruling is *zvinei zvin*, the sale is valid — because even under compulsion, at some level the person is choosing to act. Similarly, *talu v'kideish kiddushei kiddushin*: a shotgun *kiddushin* is valid *b'diavad*. One could simply have placed this case in that same category.

### Rambam Yetzer Hara

The Rambam, however, offers an additional reason specific to the *get*. He explains that deep down, every Jew wishes to do what is right. The husband's *yetzer hara* is simply getting in the way. When the *beis din* coerces him, it is not overriding his will — it is wearing down the *yetzer hara* and liberating his true, inner will to do the right thing. In that sense, he is ultimately acting *mirtsono*.

The question arises: why did the Rambam need this additional reason rather than simply relying on the general rule that coerced transactions are valid? The answer may lie in the Rambam's own formulation at the beginning of *Hilchos Gittin*, where he lists ten criteria for a valid *get*, one of which is that it must be *mirtsono*. The *rishonim* and *acharonim* ask: isn't *mirtsono* simply the standard requirement for any valid legal act? The answer seems to be that there is a distinction between *da'as* — consenting to something — and actually *wanting* it in the deeper sense that the Torah describes when it says *ki matza bah ervat davar v'chatav lah sefer kiritut*: the husband is motivated, not merely consenting. It is not enough that he agreed; he must on some level want to give the *get*. It is for this reason that the Rambam required his special explanation: the beating does not merely produce *da'as*, it reveals the husband's genuine, latent will to fulfill his *halachic* obligation.

This explanation also illuminates why a *get* compelled without a legal mandate is invalid. The entire justification for treating the forced *get* as truly voluntary is anchored in the existence of a *halachic* mandate. Without that mandate, there is no basis to say that the husband's "true will" is to give a *get*, and so the *get* is not *mirtsono* in any meaningful sense.

### Four-Case Matrix

The structure of valid and invalid *get me'useh* cases can be mapped as a matrix of two variables: the source of compulsion (Jewish *beis din* or non-Jewish court) and whether there is a legal mandate (*kadin* or *shelo kadin*). Only one of the four cells produces a genuinely valid *get*: a Jewish *beis din* acting with a legal mandate. The two cells that combine one condition with its absence — a Jewish *beis din* without legal mandate, or a non-Jewish court with legal mandate — each produce a *re'ach haget* problem: close enough to the real thing to affect *kehunah* status, but not valid as a *get*. The fourth cell — a non-Jewish court acting without a legal mandate — is so far removed from any legitimate *get* that *afilu re'ach haget ein bo*: it carries no halakhic weight whatsoever.

### Rav Mesharshaya Rejected

The *gemara* probes the logic of this framework by introducing a position of Rav Mesharshaya, which it will ultimately reject. Rav Mesharshaya proposes: *dvar Torah*, as a biblical matter, *get me'useh b'ovdei kochavim kasher* — if there is a genuine legal mandate, a *get* compelled by a non-Jewish court is in principle valid. The reason *chazal* declared it invalid is merely rabbinic: *shelo t'hei kol achas v'achas holches v'tolah atzma b'ovdei kochavim u'mafka'as atzma miyad ba'alah* — so that women would not hire non-Jewish courts to pressure their husbands into giving a *get* without a legitimate *halachic* basis, particularly since non-Jewish courts may be more susceptible to being paid off.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this position: if in principle a *get* compelled by a non-Jewish court is biblically valid, then the combination of a non-Jewish court and no legal mandate should still generate a *re'ach haget* problem, since *shelo kadin* under a valid Jewish *beis din* also creates *re'ach haget*. But the *mishna* says in that fourth case there is not even a *re'ach haget*. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ella hadar Rav Mesharshaya b'dusiah hi* — this position of Rav Mesharshaya is sheer foolishness; it is rejected entirely. A *get* compelled by a non-Jewish court acting on its own authority is completely invalid on any level.

The *gemara* then asks: given that a non-Jewish court has no authority, what explains the *re'ach haget* problem even when there is a legal mandate? The answer is *k'din b'k'din d'Yisrael mechalaf*: when there is a legal mandate, the non-Jewish scenario resembles closely enough the one case that genuinely works — a Jewish *beis din* with a legal mandate — that it creates concern for *kehunah* status. *Shelo k'din b'k'din d'Yisrael lo mechalaf*: when there is neither a Jewish *beis din* nor a legal mandate, the case is too far removed to create any concern at all.

## Semicha & Shlichus

### Abaye Challenge

The *gemara* next records that Abaye found Rav Yosef sitting and compelling husbands who were obligated to give *gittin*, using various forms of pressure and leverage. Abaye challenged him: *vaanen hedyotot anan* — we are mere commoners. We do not possess the *semicha* that traces back to Moshe Rabbeinu.

### Rabbi Tarfon

In support of this concern, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Tarfon: wherever you find gatherings of non-Jews — *Rashi* understands this as referring to non-Jewish courts — even if their substantive laws are identical to Jewish law, *i atah rashai lehizakek lahem*: you are forbidden to submit to their authority. The verse states *ve'eleh hamishpatim asher tasim lifneihem* — before *them*, the Jewish judges — *velo lifnei ovdei kochavim*. This is not merely a question of substantive law. Even if the law itself is the same, the critical question is who is recognized as the empowered legal authority. It is the Jewish court system that is divinely empowered, not merely correct in its rulings.

The *baraita* adds a second clause: *davar acher*, before them and not before *hedyotot* — commoners. To exercise coercive authority as a *beis din*, one requires *semicha*. This seemingly creates a grave problem for Rav Yosef's practice. One might think that if the requirement of *semicha* applies to ordinary legal proceedings, it applies with even greater force to cases of compelling a *get*, where the court authorizes actual physical force over a transaction that on its surface appears involuntary.

### Common Case Exception

Rav Yosef responds: *anan shelichutaihu ka avdinan* — we act as agents of the court that does possess *semicha*. The *gemara* supports this by analogy to ordinary cases of loans and admissions of debt, which courts without *semicha* handle regularly, on the grounds that they represent the authority of the courts that did have *semicha*.

### Theft Pushback

The *gemara* immediately presses: *i hachi gezeilos vachavalos nami* — if that is so, should courts today not also handle cases of theft and personal injury as agents of the *semicha* court? The *gemara* answers: *ki avdinan shelichutaihu bemilsa deshchicha* — we only function as agents of the *semicha* court in matters that are common occurrences. *Bemilsa d'lo shchicha lo avdinan shelichutaihu* — in uncommon cases, we do not claim that agency.

This generates an extensive body of *poskim* literature about the scope of contemporary *beis din* authority. The *gemara* here implies that theft and personal injury cases fall outside this agency. This is in tension with sources elsewhere in the *gemara* that suggest courts did handle such cases. Tosafot addresses the apparent contradiction and suggests that perhaps the agency applies only when certain conditions are met jointly. Additionally, some note a distinction between *knas* — punitive fines, which represent the coercive power of a court of full authority — and pure restitution in cases of loans and debts, which is more a matter of compensating actual loss and restoring the status quo. The concept that *shlichus* applies only in matters of *chisaron kis*, where someone has actually suffered a monetary loss, is raised elsewhere, though it is not made explicit in this *gemara*.

What is striking, as the *gemara* implicitly acknowledges, is that forcing a *get* — which involves actual physical coercion to produce a document that on its face looks involuntary — would seem to be the most demanding case requiring formal authority. Yet the *gemara* concludes that this too falls within the common-case *shlichus*. That forcing a *get* is classified as *milsa deshchicha*, a common occurrence, is itself a painful commentary on the frequency with which husbands refuse to give *gittin* — a reality that has not changed since the time of the *gemara*.

### Tosafot Semicha

Tosafot raises two further questions in this context. The first concerns the acceptance of converts: accepting a *ger* requires three expert *mumchin* with *semicha*, as derived from the word *mishpat* written in connection with *chalitza* in *Hacholetz*. Yet we accept converts today, without any court possessing *semicha*. Tosafot answers, again invoking the agency principle, *d'avdinan shlichuseihu*, and adding that acceptance of converts is considered sufficiently common — *d'chashiv k'shchicha* — to fall within the scope of that agency. Tosafot notes that the *gemara* in *Hacholetz* itself implies that they would accept converts in *Bavel* even in the absence of *semicha*.

The second and more fundamental question is this: to say we are agents of a court with *semicha* presupposes there is such a court to represent. In the era of the *gemara*, courts with *semicha* existed in Eretz Yisrael and the later Babylonian courts could claim to represent them. But today, *v'hashta ein mumchin b'Eretz Yisrael* — there are no ordained judges in Eretz Yisrael at all. Of whom are we agents? Tosafot answers: *d'shlichus d'kama'i avdinan* — we act as agents of the earlier courts, the ancient courts that no longer exist. This is a remarkable conceptual claim: contemporary *batei din* derive their authority from their status as the continuing institutional expression of an ancient court whose *semicha* has long since lapsed, yet whose transmitted authority is still, in some sense, operative. It is on that basis that contemporary *batei din* are empowered to address the full range of *halachic* cases they handle.

## Kol & Rumors

### Mishna Kol

Following the discussion of *get me'useh*, the *gemara* turns to a new *mishna* dealing with a categorically different problem: the halakhic weight of rumors about a woman's marital status. The *mishna* states: *yatza shemah ba'ir mekudeshes harei zo mekudeshes* — if a rumor has spread in the town that a woman is married, she must be treated as married and cannot marry another man without receiving a *get*. *Megureshes harei zo megureshes* — if a rumor spread that she is divorced, she is treated as divorced; meaning, at minimum, if she is married to a *kohen*, the rumor that her husband divorced her would preclude her from remaining with him. The *mishna* adds the crucial qualification: *uvilvad shelo yehei sham amatla* — all of this applies only if there is no contextualizing explanation that neutralizes the rumor.

The *mishna* provides two examples of an *amatla*. If the rumor was that so-and-so divorced his wife, but it was said that he did so *al tenai*, on a condition, that does not constitute a rumor of divorce because one may simply assume the condition was not met. Alternatively, if the rumor was of a *kiddushin* in which it was unclear whether the object of value reached her — *safeik karov lo safeik karov lah* — the ambiguity built into the rumor itself constitutes an *amatla* that neutralizes it.

What immediately becomes apparent is that the *gemara* will work systematically and relentlessly to narrow the scope of this *mishna* until the cases to which it actually applies are extraordinarily limited. The stakes are high: how much disruption to a woman's life can be justified on the basis of rumors alone?

### Rav Assi Single

The *gemara* begins its narrowing. *V'amar Rav Assi kol kal dibatar nisuin lo chashinan lei* — any rumor about a woman that arises after she has already been married, we do not concern ourselves with it. The second case in the *mishna* — a woman married to a *kohen* and a rumor spreads that she was divorced — falls entirely under this principle. Rav Assi's position means we do not force a married woman to leave her husband on account of a rumor that she was divorced. The *mishna*, Rav Assi therefore clarifies, applies in full only to the first case: a single woman about whom a rumor spreads that she got married. In that case alone, we treat her as potentially married, and she may not marry another man without a *get*. The case of *mekudeshes u'megureshes* — a rumor that she both married and was divorced — cancels itself out as *kol v'shovro imo*, a rumor that carries its own refutation.

### Frivolous Gossip

The *gemara* continues further limiting the *mishna*. Rava rules: *yatza lah shem mezanah ba'ir ein chosheshin lah* — if a rumor circulates that a woman is promiscuous or sexually loose, we are not concerned with it. This would be relevant, for example, to a *kohen* who might otherwise have to avoid marrying her, as intercourse with a non-Jew would render her prohibited to *kehunah*. Rava's reason: *pritzuta b'alma hu d'chazu* — people have simply observed some minor behavioral impropriety and extrapolated wildly from it. *Rashi* illustrates the range: the "pritzuta" might have been something as minor as joking with men in the marketplace, or at the other end, some form of physical contact. The point is the same — once people observe behavior that slightly transgresses communal norms, they will embroider and amplify, and such rumors carry no halakhic weight.

### Tannaim Adultery

The *gemara* notes that the question of how much evidentiary weight to assign to behavior-based inferences was debated among the *Tannaim*. If a woman was observed eating in the marketplace, walking with an outstretched neck, or nursing her child in public, Rabbi Meir says *tetzei* — she must be divorced, as we infer adultery from such conduct. Rabbi Akiva is more lenient, requiring that the town gossips — those who knit in the moonlight, *mezasos balevana* — have already begun talking about her before we take any such action. Both, however, give significant halakhic weight to behavioral rumors.

### Ben Nuri Witnesses

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri objects sharply to both positions, and his is the voice of reason in this discussion. *Im kein lo hinachta bas l'Avinu sheyosheves tachas ba'alah* — with this approach, no woman will be allowed to remain married to her husband. The Torah explicitly requires *ki matza bah ervat davar*, a concrete matter of nakedness, and invokes the general evidentiary principle that *al pi shenayim eidim o pi shlosha eidim yakum davar* — it takes two or three witnesses to establish a fact. *Davar* appears in both: evidence must be clear and concrete, not inferential gossip.

Tosafot draws out the implication for our *mishna*: if we won't force a married woman to leave her husband based on behavioral rumors, then *a fortiori* we should not impose halakhic consequences on a single woman based on such rumors either — even though the threshold for consequences to a single woman (e.g., *kehunah* eligibility) might seem lower than forcing divorce. The *gemara*'s thrust is consistent: it is not the identity of the rumored act but the evidentiary quality of the rumor itself that determines whether it carries weight.

## Limiting Kol

### No Concern List

A *baraita* catalogs a sweeping series of cases in which we are categorically not concerned about a rumor: a rumor of sexual intercourse (*be'ula*), a rumor of marriage (*nesu'ah*), a rumor of *kiddushin* only (*arusa*), a rumor that identifies no specific person (*shelo l'ploni*), a rumor about an event in another city (*d'ir acheret*), a rumor that she is a *mamzeret*, and a rumor that she is a *shifcha*. The list extends to property-status rumors as well. In short, standing alone, none of these rumors are halakhically actionable. *Rashi* summarizes the operative principle: only a rumor that claims she married a specific, named person on this very day in this very city carries any weight at all. A rumor about the past, about an unidentified party, or about a distant location is simply discarded.

### Circumstantial Evidence

Ulla radically narrows the operative case of the *mishna* further still. The *mishna* is not concerned with a mere *kol havarah* — a disembodied echo, a rumor of uncertain origin that simply passed from mouth to mouth. Rather, the concern arises only when there is robust circumstantial evidence: candles are lit, couches are spread out, there is a catering van at the hall, and *uvnei adam nichnasim v'yotzim* — people are streaming in and out. It is in that context, where the entire town has witnessed what looks unmistakably like a wedding, that we take a rumor seriously. Levi's teaching parallels Ulla's, adding that women are knitting by candlelight and rejoicing.

### Nitkadshah Not Mitkad

Even with all of that circumstantial evidence, the *gemara* further demands precision in what is actually being said. If the townspeople say *plonit mitkadeshet hayom* — so-and-so is getting married today — *v'dilma lo ikadshah*: maybe it got called off at the last moment, and we have only evidence of a planned wedding, not an actual one. The concern arises only when people say *plonit nitkadshah hayom* — so-and-so *got* married today, an established fact and not merely an anticipated event. This distinction between *mitkadeshet* (getting married) and *nitkadshah* (got married) is determinative. Rav Papa and Bar Rav Papa citing Rabbi Yochanan both make this same point, with slight variations in the accompanying details.

The *gemara* then addresses a cryptic statement: if the people did say *nitkadshah*, that is a *kol*; if they did not say it, it is an *amatla* — an explanation that neutralizes the *kol*. This is puzzling: if they said nothing, there is no *kol* to neutralize in the first place. The *gemara* resolves that it means when an explanation emerges immediately alongside the rumor, it functions as though the rumor was never established. This stands in contrast to the position of Rav Abba bar Rav Huna, who holds that an *amatla* can neutralize an established *kol* even if it is offered as much as ten days later.

### Witness Tracing

Rav Papa and Rav Huna bar Minyomi citing Rav Abba citing Rav Huna citing Rav offer a second track for establishing a *kol*: even without the full battery of circumstantial evidence described by Ulla and Levi, if one can trace the rumor back through a chain of people — A heard it from B, B heard it from C — and at the end of the chain identify specific individuals who are claimed to have witnessed the event directly, that too constitutes a *kol* worthy of concern. The final version of this tradition adds the crucial detail: *v'halchu lahem l'medinat hayam* — those identified witnesses have gone overseas and cannot be questioned. If the tracing leads to a *davar barur*, a clearly established fact through identifiable witnesses who can actually testify, it is no longer a *kol* at all but direct testimony. It is specifically the case where the original witnesses have been identified but are unavailable that creates a *kol*. Tosafot observes that this and Ulla's approach represent two distinct tracks: circumstantial evidence from a community-wide wedding scene, or a traceable chain ending in named but absent witnesses.

## Bittul Kol

### Abaye Question

Having established the extremely narrow circumstances in which a *kol* is halakhically significant, the *gemara* turns to a related but distinct question: once a *kol* has been established, should the *beis din* proactively work to dispel it? Abaye poses this to Rav Yosef: *mevatlinan kola o lo mevatlinan?* One might assume that since *kol* is given relatively little weight, the *beis din* should have every interest in stamping out harmful rumors. On the other hand, one might argue that attempting to dispel a rumor could be counterproductive — if people believe the rumor, a *beis din* denying it will simply discredit itself.

### Witnesses Debate

The answer turns on a dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Sheshet regarding the standard for establishing a *kol* in the first place. Rav Chisda maintains that a *kol* is only significant if it is spread by *kesherim* — valid witnesses, or at least those who heard directly from valid witnesses. Since this is such a stringent standard, the *gemara* infers *shema mina mevatlinan kala*: because we are concerned about rumors only in very narrow circumstances, we would obviously not hesitate to proactively dispel them. Rav Sheshet disagrees and holds that *afilu mipi nashim havei kala* — even if the rumor is spread by women, who are not valid witnesses for such purposes, it constitutes a *kol* of concern, given that the other factual prerequisites are in place. From his broader standard, *shema mina lo mevatlinan kala*: since the circumstances creating concern are somewhat more expansive, we are more cautious about the underlying facts and do not rush to dispel rumors, lest there be some fire behind the smoke.

### Regional Practice

Rav Yosef resolves the tension practically: this is a matter of regional custom. *Be-Sura mevatli kala* — in Sura they proactively nullify rumors. *Be-Nahardea lo mevatli kala* — in Nehardea they do not. This reflects the respective positions of Rav (Sura) and Shmuel (Nehardea).

### Conditional Marriage

The *gemara* now presents a series of cases that test when nullification is effective. In the first case, a rumor spread that a woman had been betrothed to a *bar bei Rav*, a yeshiva student. Rav Chama brought the woman's father before him and asked what had happened. The father explained that the *kiddushin* had been performed *al tenai* — on the condition that the student would not travel to the region of *Bei Chuzai* — but the student subsequently went there, voiding the condition and thus the *kiddushin*. Rav Chama rejected this: *keivan de-be-idna de-havei kala lo havei amatla* — since this explanation was not offered at the time the rumor was established, *lav kol kaminach de-mechazket amatla* — the father cannot retroactively establish it as an *amatla* to neutralize the rumor. This directly applies the earlier dispute: can an explanation that comes after the *kol* was established neutralize it? Here the answer is no — once the *kol* is established, a later explanation does not suffice.

### Dates Case

The next case involves a woman about whom a rumor circulated that she had been betrothed at a specific wellspring, using what *Rashi* identifies as remnants of unripe dates — an object presumably worth less than a *shaveh perutah*, the minimum value required for valid *kiddushin*. The question was sent to Abaye: must we be concerned, or may we nullify this rumor? Abaye rules: *afilu le-man de-amar lo mevatlinan kola, be-ha mevatlinan kola* — even according to the position that in general we do not nullify rumors, here we would. Why? Because *meimar amri inhu be-derabbanan be-kidushin ve-lo havei bah shaveh perutah* — people will reason on their own that the object used was not worth a *shaveh perutah*, and so the *kiddushin* was never valid. The key principle is effectiveness: a *beis din* will only act to nullify a rumor when it can expect to be effective. If the *beis din*'s nullification directly contradicts public perception, it will discredit the *beis din* and accomplish nothing. But when the rumor itself contains internal elements — here, the specific detail of the unripe dates — that people will naturally use to explain away the *kiddushin*, then working with that internal logic can be effective, and the *beis din* will act.

### Unnamed Son

A parallel case: a rumor spread that a woman had become betrothed to one of the sons of a certain family, without specifying which son. Rava rules: *afilu le-man de-amar lo mevatlinan kola, be-ha mevatlinan kola* — even for those who hold we generally do not nullify, here we do. The reasoning: *meimar amri inhu be-rabbanan be-kiddushei u-vi-kiddushei katan havu* — people will reason that since the rumors don't specify which son, it was probably the minor son, and a minor cannot effect valid *kiddushin*. Again, the internal logic of the rumor provides the explanation that allows for effective dispelling.

### Mature Minor

A harder case: a rumor that a woman was betrothed to a minor who appeared physically mature — tall, husky, looking every bit like an adult. Here there is hesitation to work toward nullification. The difficulty is that public perception directly contradicts the halakhic reality. The *beis din* might say he was a minor, but people saw what appeared to be an adult. Rav Mordechai told Rav Ashi, however, that in a case like this, there was a precedent and people said *adayin lo higia le-phalgut Reuven* — he had not yet reached the stage of "the divisions of Reuven." This is a reference to the verse in *Shirat Devorah*, *li-flogos Reuven gedolim chikrei lev*, describing how mature adults engage in deep deliberation. The point is that even if someone looks physically adult, people can perceive from his behavior and intellectual immaturity that he is a minor. Therefore, if the *beis din* points out that the young man in question was clearly behaving in childish, immature ways — *gedolim chikrei lev* being the marker of adult capacity — people will understand and accept that he was a minor, making the nullification effective. The underlying principle throughout these cases is consistent: we will dispel a rumor when we think we can be effective in doing so; we will not if it will merely entrench it.

## Amatla Neutralizing

### Ten Days Later

Returning to the question of when an *amatla* can neutralize an established *kol*, the *gemara* cites Rabba bar Rav Huna who holds the lenient position: *amatla she-amru afilu mikan ve-ad asarah yamim* — an explanation, even if it comes as late as ten days afterward, is sufficient to neutralize the *kol*.

### Possible Amatla

Rav Zvid goes even further: *be-makom amatla chosheshin le-amatla* — in a place where an *amatla* is possible, meaning wherever an explanation is conceivable even hypothetically, that theoretical possibility alone is sufficient to neutralize the *kol*. One need not even produce an actual explanation; the mere availability of one neutralizes the rumor. The *gemara* at this stage does not present the more stringent view — that an *amatla* must come on the very day of the *kol* — but instead consistently cites the lenient positions, reflecting the overall *gemara*'s sustained effort to limit the halakhic consequences of rumors.

Rav Papa challenges Rav Zvid from the *mishna* itself, which says *uvilvad shelo tehe sham amatla* — the *kol* is only neutralized when there is an *amatla* present. This implies an actual *amatla* is needed, not merely a theoretical one. Rav Zvid responds: *bimkom amatla ka-amar* — the *mishna* means in a place where an *amatla* is conceivable. Rav Kahana then presses Rav Papa: surely you agree that a possible explanation suffices?

### Conditional Kiddushin

The question is adjudicated with a case from *Yevamos*. A woman remarried on the basis of a single witness's testimony that her first husband had died; her first husband then reappeared alive. If she had only received *kiddushin* from the second man and had not yet cohabited with him, *muteres lachzor* — she may return to her first husband. Why? The *gemara* suggests that people will naturally assume the *kiddushin* was done *al tenai*, on a condition, that proved not to be fulfilled. This reflects the rule that *kiddushin* without subsequent *nissu'in* is assumed to be conditional when circumstances call for it, while *kiddushin* that has led to *nissu'in* is not so easily unwound. No one explicitly stated the *tenai* — yet we read one into the situation because a plausible explanation exists. This appears to support Rav Zvid's position that the mere possibility of an *amatla* suffices.

### Husband Contest

The *gemara* deflects: *shani hasam de-asi ba'al ve-ka me'ar'er* — that case is different, because the first husband has appeared and is actively objecting, loudly contesting the claim that he ever died or that she was ever free to remarry. It is the husband's own presence and insistence that drives the *gemara* to accept that the *kiddushin* must have been conditional, not merely the theoretical availability of an explanation. The *gemara* then asks: if the husband's objection is the operative factor, why not allow her to return to him even if she had already cohabited with the second man? The answer leads to the final principle.

### Nisuin Issur

*Nisseis de-avdi issura knasu rabbanan, nitkadsha de-lo avdi issura lo knasu rabbanan* — once the woman has cohabited with the second man, she has unwittingly committed a violation, and the Rabbis imposed a consequence to give the initial *kiddushin* legal permanence and prevent a situation that would look like *machzir gerushaso*, a man taking back a woman he had divorced after she had been with another. Before cohabitation, there is no violation — she had only *kiddushin* — and therefore no rabbinic penalty applies, and she may return to her first husband. The husband's contestation provides the warrant for assuming the explanation, but that warrant operates differently depending on whether *nissu'in* has occurred.

The arc of this entire discussion is clear and consistent. The *mishna*'s seemingly expansive rule — that a *kol* creates halakhic consequences — has been narrowed at every stage. The circumstances that establish a valid *kol* are exceptional: all the hallmarks of a public wedding, or a traceable chain to named, absent witnesses. Even then, the *kol* may be dispelled when the *beis din* can do so effectively. And when the *kol* has been established, an *amatla* — actual or merely possible — can neutralize it. The *gemara*'s consistent movement is toward protecting women from the halakhic consequences of unverified communal rumor, a theme that will continue as the *masechet* concludes.
