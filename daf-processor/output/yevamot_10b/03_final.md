# Yevamot 10b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Context

### Nine Dafim Survey

It is worth pausing at this juncture to take stock of where we are in the tractate. We began on daf 4, and from that point until now, the first genuinely new amoraic statement bearing directly on the *mishnah* was the exchange between Levi and Rebbi regarding why the *mishnah* enumerates fifteen co-wives rather than sixteen. Everything preceding that was essentially *stam* — anonymous Talmudic discussion — with no amoraic voices meeting one another head-to-head. The *Gemara* worked through an extended opening *sugya*: why one might have entertained the *hava amina* that *yibbum bemakom ervah* could be permitted; the principle of *aseh doche lo ta'aseh* and what might have been derived from *eshet ach*; and then the whole question of what source each Tanna uses for the exemption of *ervah vetzarat ervah*, and finally the question of fifteen versus sixteen.

### Transition to Standard Format

From this point forward, the *sugya* moves into a more recognizable Talmudic structure. Rather than the extended, largely anonymous introductory material that characterized the preceding nine dapim, we now encounter direct amoraic disputes, back-and-forth argumentation, and proofs marshaled for competing positions. The *sugyot* in Yevamot remain anything but simple, but they take on a more familiar shape — statements of *amoraim*, countervailing opinions, and attempts at resolution. That is precisely where we now begin, at 11b.

## Reish Lakish vs. Yochanan

### Chalitzah Then Remarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ "חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה", יֵשׁ לָנוּ לְהוֹסִיף שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה כְּגוֹן זוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְלֵוִי דַּאֲמַר "דְּאִי" נָמֵי קָתָנֵי — לִתְנֵי: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, וּמֵת בְּלֹא בָּנִים, דְּמִגּוֹ דְּאִיהִי אֲסוּרָה — צָרָתָהּ נָמֵי אֲסִירָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And although the Sages taught in the mishna: Fifteen** women, **we must add a sixteenth** case, **for example,** the situation described above. **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: According to** the opinion of **Levi, who said** that the mishna **teaches even** cases of: **What if,** which involve a transgression, **let** the mishna also **teach** the case of **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and went back and betrothed her, and** then **died childless.** His betrothal is a transgression, as after *ḥalitza* the *yevama* is permanently forbidden to him. **As,** in this case, **since she is forbidden** to all the brothers, due to the *ḥalitza* performed by the deceased *yavam*, **her rival wife is also forbidden.** This is an additional case of a woman who exempts her rival wife.

The discussion opens with Reish Lakish addressing Rabbi Yochanan, framing his question around the position of Levi. Levi, recall, wished to expand the *mishnah*'s list to sixteen by including *anusat aviv* — a woman whom a father violated but did not marry. Levi was apparently willing for the *mishnah* to list cases that apply only *bedi'eved*, meaning situations where marriage, though prohibited, would nonetheless be binding after the fact. The *Gemara* then asks: if Levi is willing to include *bedi'eved* cases, why not include yet another such case?

To understand the case in question, set up the following scenario: Reuven is married to Rachel; Shimon is married to an unrelated woman, Devorah. Reuven dies. Shimon now stands before a *mitzvah* of *yibbum* or *chalitzah* with Rachel, and similarly with Devorah (Reuven's wife). He performs *chalitzah* with Rachel. At that point, the *mitzvah* is discharged — one brother performing one act, whether *chalitzah* or *yibbum*, releases all the wives and exempts all the remaining brothers. Everyone is free.

Now the question: what happens if Shimon, having performed *chalitzah* with Rachel, goes ahead and remarries her? Everyone agrees that this is prohibited. The source is the verse *asher lo yivneh es beis achiv* — "who did not build his brother's house" — from which we derive that once he has declined to build, *keivan shelo vana shuv eino voneh*, he may not reverse course and marry her afterward. This constitutes a *lav* — a prohibition rooted in the *yibbum* framework that now governs his relationship with her.

Yet suppose he does so anyway. The *kiddushin* is binding *bedi'eved* because it is only an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*. He has now in effect remarried Rachel. Then Shimon dies. What is Levi's relationship to Rachel, whom Shimon had remarried? This is precisely the kind of *bedi'eved* situation Levi was willing to include in the *mishnah*. Rachel could be considered an *ervah* to Levi — a *chalutzah* who was subsequently remarried — and if Shimon also had a co-wife (for instance, Devorah had also become bound to the same estate through *yibbum*), then that co-wife would become a *tzarat ervah*. This is a new case of exemption from *yibbum* that perhaps should have appeared in the *mishnah*'s list.

The *Gemara* answers on behalf of Rabbi Yochanan:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּצָרַת צָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: This example is not cited **because** the case **does not** include the possibility **of a rival wife of a rival wife.** All of the other cases listed in the mishna involve both rival wives and rival wives of rival wives. Here, however, this is impossible, as the same prohibition applies equally to all of the brothers, and therefore none of them may marry her.

Recall that the *mishnah*'s list included *tzarat tzara* — a co-wife's co-wife — as a further extension of the exemption. For *tzarat tzara* to exist, there must be a co-wife who is prohibited only to some brothers and not others, so that when she marries another brother her own co-wife becomes a *tzarat ervah* exempt from *yibbum* with yet another brother. In the case of the *chalutzah* who was remarried, however, Rachel is equally prohibited to all of Reuven's brothers — the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* had fallen equally on all of them — and therefore no configuration arises in which one brother's wife becomes a *tzarat tzara* for another brother. Since the *tzarat tzara* extension does not apply here, the case was excluded from the *mishnah*.

The *Gemara* then asks: why didn't Rabbi Yochanan simply respond to Reish Lakish by saying that according to him, Rachel and Devorah are only an *issur lav* to the remaining brothers, not an *issur kares*, and *chayavei lavin* retain their *yibbum* and *chalitzah* obligations and therefore do not belong in the list of fifteen at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין הִיא, וְחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין בְּנֵי חֲלִיצָה וְיִבּוּם נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **say** a different answer **to** Reish Lakish, as he could simply have pointed out that marriage to a woman who had performed *ḥalitza* is not a violation that incurs *karet*, neither for the one who performed the *ḥalitza* nor for his brothers if they marry her after he dies. Rather, **it is** an act for which they are **liable for** violating a regular **prohibition, and those liable for** violating **a prohibition are** obligated **in *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage.** In these cases the mitzva of levirate marriage still applies, and although the woman should be released by *ḥalitza*, she is not entirely exempt from levirate marriage, and therefore her rival wife is not exempt either.

The *Gemara* answers: *ledivarav ka'amar lei* —

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידִי — חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין הֵם, וְחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין בְּנֵי חֲלִיצָה וְיִבּוּם נִינְהוּ. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ — חַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת נִינְהוּ. לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן בְּצָרַת צָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that Rabbi Yoḥanan **is speaking in accordance with the statement of** Reish Lakish, as follows: According **to my** opinion your question is groundless, as **they are liable for** violating **a prohibition, and those liable for** violating **a prohibition are** obligated **in *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage. However,** according **to your** opinion, **those** brothers who marry a *yevama* who had performed *ḥalitza* **are liable to** receive ***karet*,** and therefore you must resolve the difficulty by saying that the mishna did not teach this case **because** it **does not** include the possibility **of a rival wife of a rival wife.**

Rabbi Yochanan was arguing within Reish Lakish's own framework, showing that even granting Reish Lakish's premise, the case still does not fit the *mishnah* for the reason of *tzarat tzara*.

This roundabout framing, however, was simply the *Gemara*'s vehicle for arriving at the core dispute. The direct statement of the debate now follows.

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוּא — אֵין חַיָּיב עַל הַחֲלוּצָה כָּרֵת, וְהָאַחִין — חַיָּיבִין עַל הַחֲלוּצָה כָּרֵת. עַל הַצָּרָה, בֵּין הוּא וּבֵין הָאַחִים — חַיָּיבִין עַל הַצָּרָה כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the dispute alluded to in the previous paragraph. **It was stated:** With regard to **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and went back and betrothed her, Reish Lakish said:** The brother who earlier performed *ḥalitza* **is not liable to** receive ***karet* for** returning to **the *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*].** She is no longer forbidden as a brother's wife, although she is forbidden by a regular prohibition. **And the brothers** who did not actually perform the *ḥalitza* **are liable to** receive ***karet* for** having relations with **a *ḥalutza*.** In their case, the woman retains her status as a forbidden brother's wife even after the *ḥalitza*. **With regard to the rival wife** of the *ḥalutza*, although she too is released from the levirate bond, since the act of *ḥalitza* itself was not performed with her, **both** the brother who performed the *ḥalitza* **and the** other **brothers are liable to** receive ***karet* for** having relations with **the rival wife,** as she retains her forbidden status as a brother's wife.

*Itmar* — it was stated. The case: *ha-choleitz li-yevamto ve-chazar ve-kiddashah* — a man performs *chalitzah* with his *yevamah* and then goes and betroths her again. Reish Lakish holds: *hu eino chayav al ha-chalutzah kares* — he himself, Shimon, who remarries the woman he performed *chalitzah* with, is not liable for *kares* with respect to her. His ongoing relationship with her has been restructured within the *yibbum* framework: the prohibition on his remarrying her is the specific *issur lav* of *keivan shelo vana shuv eino voneh*. That is a new category of *issur*, not a reversion to *eshet ach*.

But for the other brothers — Levi, Yehuda, and so forth — *ha-achim chayavim al ha-chalutzah kares*: their relationship with Rachel reverts to *eshet ach*, which carries *kares*. The logic is that Shimon had a *yibbum* relationship with Rachel, and so his prohibition is defined within that *yibbum* world. Levi, however, never actualized any *yibbum* relationship with Rachel. The potential was there, but it was not actualized, and so the general prohibition of *eshet ach* with its *kares* consequence remains in full force for him.

By the same token, Shimon's relationship with Devorah — the other wife — also reverts to *eshet ach* with *kares*, because he never had a *yibbum* relationship with Devorah at all. And both he and the other brothers are equally liable for *kares* with respect to the *tzarah*. The only exception carved out from full *kares* status is Shimon's specific relationship with Rachel, to whom he performed *chalitzah* — that and only that relationship is redefined in *yibbum* terms and carries merely an *issur lav*.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בֵּין הוּא וּבֵין הָאַחִין אֵינָן חַיָּיבִין לֹא עַל הַחֲלוּצָה כָּרֵת, וְלֹא עַל הַצָּרָה כָּרֵת. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אָמַר קְרָא: "אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִבְנֶה", כֵּיוָן שֶׁלֹּא בָּנָה — שׁוּב לֹא יִבְנֶה. אִיהוּ הוּא דְּקָאֵי בְּ"לֹא יִבְנֶה", אֲבָל אֶחָיו — כִּדְקָיְימִי קָיְימִי. וַעֲלֵהּ דִּידַהּ, הוּא דְּקָאֵי בְּ"לֹא יִבְנֶה", הָא צָרָה — כִּדְקָיְימָא קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Neither he nor the** other **brothers are liable to** receive ***karet*, neither for the *ḥalutza* nor for** having relations with **the rival wife.** The Gemara explains: **What is the rationale of Reish Lakish? The verse states** with regard to one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama*: **"Who does not build up** his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9), which teaches that **since he did not build** his brother's house by entering into levirate marriage, opting instead for *ḥalitza*, **he shall never build** it **again.** This statement is understood not only as a negative description of events, but also as a prohibition against marrying this woman at any point in the future. However, **it is** the man who performed the act of *ḥalitza* **who stands** liable **to** receive punishment for violating this prohibition of: **Does not build up,** i.e., the prohibition against marrying his *ḥalutza*. For him the prohibition involving *karet* has been replaced by the regular prohibition of: Who does not build up his brother's house. **However,** with regard to his **brothers, where they stood** before, **they stand** now. In other words, just as before the mitzva of levirate marriage applied to them this woman was forbidden to them as a brother's wife, once the obligation of levirate marriage has been removed they remain bound by the same prohibition. Consequently, she is forbidden to the brothers on pain of *karet*. **And** furthermore, only **with regard to her,** the *ḥalutza*, **does he stand** liable **for: Does not build up,** but as for **the rival wife,** who did not perform *ḥalitza*, **where** all the brothers **stood** before, **they stand** now. Just as before her rival wife performed *ḥalitza* she was forbidden on pain of *karet*, the same applies after the *ḥalitza*, both for the man who performed *ḥalitza* and his brothers.

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this framework entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא אִי בָּעֵי הַאי — חָלֵיץ, וְאִי בָּעֵי הַאי — חָלֵיץ, וְאִי בָּעֵי — לְהַאי חָלֵיץ, וְאִי בָּעֵי — לְהַאי חָלֵיץ, וְהַשְׁתָּא קָאֵי עֲלַהּ בְּכָרֵת?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** claims: **Is there anything** of this kind in *halakha*? After all, **at the outset,** before the *ḥalitza*, **if this** brother **wanted** he could **perform *ḥalitza*, and if that** brother **wanted** he could **perform *ḥalitza*, and if he wanted** he could **perform *ḥalitza* with this** woman **and if he wanted** he could **perform *ḥalitza* with that** woman. All the deceased brother's wives were included in the levirate obligation of marriage or *ḥalitza*, and therefore the prohibition proscribing a brother's wife was negated for all of them at that time. **And now,** after the *ḥalitza*, **they stand to** incur ***karet* for** having relations with **her?** How can the prohibition proscribing a brother's wife return after it was nullified by the death of the childless brother?

*Mi ika midi de-me'ikara ei ba'ei hai chalitz ve'ei ba'ei hai chalitz* — is there anything here where, from the outset, only one specific party was designated? Before anyone did anything, Shimon could have performed *chalitzah* with Rachel or with Devorah; Levi equally could have performed *chalitzah* with Rachel or with Devorah. Any combination was possible. All the brothers stood equally before the *mitzvah*; all the wives stood equally subject to it. To say that one act — Shimon performing *chalitzah* with Rachel — changes the status of Rachel vis-à-vis Shimon but leaves everyone else in the pre-existing *eshet ach* category is, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, incoherent. They all had an equal relationship to this *mitzvah*.

Furthermore, Rabbi Yochanan presses the point: *ve-hashta de-kayala bekarei* — now that she has performed the act of *chalitzah*, is it reasonable that all these relationships which could equally have been the ones to undergo *chalitzah* should suddenly revert to *issur kares*? That makes no sense given that any of them could have been the participants.

This leads to a striking conceptual formulation:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִיהוּ שְׁלִיחוּתָא דְאַחִים קָעָבֵיד, אִיהִי שְׁלִיחוּתָא דְצָרָה קָעָבְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, he** who performs *ḥalitza* **performs the agency of the** other **brothers,** and therefore he also releases her from the levirate bond of his brothers. Similarly, **she** who performs *ḥalitza* **performs the agency of the rival wife,** as the *ḥalitza* of one wife serves to release the other one as well. Consequently, it is as though all of the brothers released all of the wives, and there is no longer any prohibition that incurs *karet*.

*Ihu shlichusa de-achin ka-avid, ihi shlichusa de-tzarah ka-avda* — he, Shimon, performs *chalitzah* as the agent of all the brothers; she, Rachel, receives the *chalitzah* as the agent of all the co-wives. Shimon is, in effect, a *shliach tzibur* — representing Levi, Yehuda, and all the rest. Rachel is representing Devorah and any other co-wife in the same estate. The implication is radical: the *chalitzah* performed by Shimon with Rachel is considered as though all the brothers performed *chalitzah* with all the wives. Therefore, everyone now stands in the same relationship — not *eshet ach* with *kares*, but a new *issur* defined within the *yibbum* world.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, nobody — not Shimon, not Levi, not Yehuda, with respect to Rachel or Devorah — is liable for *kares*. They all have the same status: a new *issur lav* that emerged from the *chalitzah* act, applicable universally because the act was performed representatively for all.

### Agency & Representation

The mechanism articulated by Rabbi Yochanan deserves emphasis because it is one of the conceptually foundational ideas of tractate Yevamot. The claim is not merely that the brothers and wives had a theoretical possibility of participating — it is that the one who actually participates does so as a representative of all the others. This is what *shlichut* means in this context: Shimon's *chalitzah* is treated as legally equivalent to every brother performing *chalitzah*, and Rachel's receipt of it is treated as every co-wife's receipt. The act is, in a sense, collective even when physically performed by individuals.

This principle is what differentiates Rabbi Yochanan from Reish Lakish at the deepest level. Reish Lakish sees the act as individual: Shimon and Rachel entered a particular legal relationship, and everything outside that bilateral relationship remains governed by the prior law of *eshet ach*. Rabbi Yochanan sees the act as representative: through the mechanism of agency, all parties are equally drawn into the new legal reality created by that single act of *chalitzah*.

## Baraita Proofs

### Yochanan's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ, וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, וּמֵת — צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין נִינְהוּ, הַיְינוּ דִּצְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites the ensuing discussion between the two disputing Sages. **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and went back and betrothed her, and** then **died, she requires *ḥalitza* from** one of **the brothers. Granted,** this makes sense according **to my** opinion, **as I say** that brothers who marry a *ḥalutza* **are liable** only **for** violating **a prohibition,** and **that is** why **she requires *ḥalitza* from the** other **brothers.** The principle is that a woman with whom relations are forbidden by a regular prohibition that does not incur *karet* may not enter into levirate marriage, but she must undergo *ḥalitza*.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, this is entirely consistent: their status is an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*, and women in an *issur lav* relationship with the brothers still require *chalitzah* — they cannot simply walk free, but neither can *yibbum* be performed.

But according to Reish Lakish, the other brothers stand in an *issur kares* relationship with this woman. Women who are *arayot* — full *ervah* — are entirely exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibbum*. If her status is *issur kares* for the brothers, why does the *baraita* require *chalitzah* at all? This seems to be a clear proof for Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lakish Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: עָמַד אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, אֵין לָהּ עָלָיו כְּלוּם. וְאִי חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין נִינְהוּ, אַמַּאי אֵין לָהּ עָלָיו כְּלוּם?
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish answered: **And according to your reasoning,** how would you **state** and explain **the latter clause** of the *baraita*: If **one of the brothers arose and betrothed her, she has no claims** of a bill of divorce **on him,** i.e., the marriage does not take effect at all and she does not require a bill of divorce from him? **But if** the brothers **are liable** only **for** violating **a prohibition, why does she have no claim on him?** After all, in cases of regular prohibitions a betrothal is valid. According to my opinion that these brothers are liable to receive *karet* the matter is clear, as the betrothal is invalid. This indicates that there is a contradiction between the first and latter clauses of the *baraita*. How, then, can Rabbi Yochanan cite a proof from an apparently flawed *baraita*?

Reish Lakish counters: *u-letamach, eima seifa* — you cited only the first half of the *baraita*; read the second half. *Amad echad min ha-achin ve-kiddshah, ein lah alav klum* — if one of the brothers went ahead and betrothed her, the *kiddushin* is entirely null and void, as though nothing happened. But if their status were only an *issur lav*, *kiddushin* would be binding *bedi'eved*, as it is in all cases of *chayavei lavin*. The fact that the *kiddushin* takes no hold whatsoever — *ein lah alav klum* — indicates *issur kares*, where *kiddushin* never takes effect.

Now the *baraita* is internally contradictory. Its first clause requires *chalitzah*, implying *issur lav*. Its second clause declares *kiddushin* null, implying *issur kares*. Neither Rabbi Yochanan nor Reish Lakish can claim the *baraita* as consistent support; it speaks with two irreconcilable voices.

### Resolving the Contradiction

The *Gemara* proposes an initial resolution:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין. וְלִיתְנֵי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵין לוֹ עָלָיו כְּלוּם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** in resolution of this contradiction: In the **latter clause** of the *baraita* **we have come to** a different opinion, that of **Rabbi Akiva, who said: Betrothal with those** who are forbidden as they are **liable for** violating **a prohibition is not valid.** In Rabbi Akiva's opinion there is no difference between regular prohibitions and prohibitions that incur *karet* in this regard, as in both cases the betrothals are invalid. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if the first clause of the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, while the latter clause is in accordance with a different opinion, **let** the mishna **teach** explicitly: **According to the statement of Rabbi Akiva she has no claims on him,** as this represents a different opinion.

Rav Sheshet's proposal is that the second clause follows Rabbi Akiva's view. The objection is immediate:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments: Rav Sheshet's explanation of the contradiction between the first clause and latter clause of the *baraita* **is difficult.**

### Rav Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וּמְתָרֵץ לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רָבִינָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמְתָרֵץ כְּרַבָּנַן. רַב אָשֵׁי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ וּמְתָרֵץ לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ — צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין. מַאן אַחִין — אַחִין הַיִּלּוֹדִים. כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. עָמַד אֶחָד מִן הַנּוֹלָדִים וְקִדְּשָׁהּ — אֵין לָהּ עָלָיו כְּלוּם, כְּמַאן — כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § Consequently, the Gemara cites different resolutions of this contradiction. **Rav Ashi holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish and resolves** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. Ravina,** in contrast, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and resolves** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Ashi holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish and resolves** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** This is his interpretation: In the case of **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and went back and betrothed her,** and then died, **she requires *ḥalitza* from** one of **the brothers. Who are** the **brothers** referred to here? Rav Ashi explains that they are the **newborn brothers,** i.e., those who were born only after the brother betrothed her. Consequently, although this woman was the wife of the first deceased brother before they were born, after their birth she was already the wife of a living brother. Therefore, the prohibition of a wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist never applied, and she requires *ḥalitza*. **In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that in a case of this kind the brothers are not considered to be coexisting. Meanwhile, the latter clause speaks of a case where **one of the** brothers who was **born** in the lifetime of the first brother **arose and betrothed her,** after another brother had performed *ḥalitza*. In this case, **she has no claims** of a bill of divorce **on him. In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** who maintains that the penalty of *karet* applies in this case, and therefore the betrothal is entirely invalid.

Rav Ashi's resolution: The *baraita* addresses *achin ha-noladim* — brothers who were born after the relevant events. Recall Rabbi Shimon's ruling that a brother born after the fact relates to a woman only in the status she occupied when he entered the world, without the prior history affecting him. Under this principle, the two halves of the *baraita* refer to two different groups of brothers.

To illustrate: suppose Shimon performs *chalitzah* with Rachel and then remarries her — stage one. At that point, say, Yehuda had already been born, while Levi is born only after Shimon has remarried Rachel. For Levi, who enters the world when Rachel is already Shimon's wife (a binding marriage, even if *bedi'eved*), all the prior history — the *chalitzah*, the prohibition — is irrelevant. He finds Rachel as his brother Shimon's wife, governed by the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*. When Shimon subsequently dies, Levi can and must perform either *chalitzah* or *yibbum* — that is the first clause of the *baraita*: *tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin*, referring to newly born brothers like Levi.

For Yehuda, who was alive throughout the entire sequence, Rachel is an *issur kares* — she was always in the *yibbum* orbit and he was always present to it, so Reish Lakish's principle applies fully. If Yehuda goes ahead and performs *kiddushin* with Rachel, *ein lah alav klum* — that is the second clause of the *baraita*, referring to brothers who were already present (*ha-yeludim*) when all this transpired. Thus the two halves of the *baraita* are perfectly consistent according to Reish Lakish, parsed through Rabbi Shimon's distinction of *noladim* versus *yeludim*.

### Ravina Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמְתָרֵץ לַהּ אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ — צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין. מַאן אַחִין — אַחִין הַנּוֹלָדִים, כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. עָמַד אֶחָד מִן הַיִּלּוֹדִים וְקִדְּשָׁהּ — אֵין לָהּ עָלָיו כְּלוּם, כְּמַאן — כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation of the opinions. **Ravina holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and resolves** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** How so? In the case of **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and went back and betrothed her** and then died, **she requires *ḥalitza* from** one of **the brothers. Who are** these **brothers?** This is referring to the **brothers** already **born** in the lifetime of the first brother. **In accordance with whose** opinion is this interpretation? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who held that this case constitutes a regular prohibition. Consequently, the *yevama* is not exempt from the obligation of *ḥalitza*. If **one of the newborn** brothers, who were born after the death of the first brother, **arose and betrothed her, she has no claim on him.** Since he is considered a brother who did not coexist with his deceased brother, the *yevama* is prohibited to him as a brother's wife, and as this prohibition entails *karet*, the betrothal is invalid. This is the *halakha* even if he was born after the second deceased brother betrothed her. **In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who maintain that the prohibition of a wife of a brother with whom one did not coexist applies even to a brother's widow who entered into levirate marriage before the younger brother was born.

Ravina, holding like Rabbi Yochanan and reconciling according to the *rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Shimon, reaches the opposite parsing. For Rabbi Yochanan, all brothers who were present when the *chalitzah* took place have the status of *issur lav* — they participated representatively in the *chalitzah* process, and so they still require *chalitzah* but cannot perform *yibbum*. These are the brothers referred to in the first clause: *tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin* — namely, the brothers who were there, like Levi.

But what of a brother born afterward — Yehuda, who enters the world after the *chalitzah* and the subsequent remarriage, when Rachel is already Shimon's wife? For Yehuda, Rachel is *eshet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* — the wife of a brother who predated him. According to the *rabbanan*, unlike Rabbi Shimon, the temporal sequence of birth does not retroactively adjust the prohibition. Yehuda never had a *mitzvat yibbum* at all with respect to Rachel. Without any *yibbum* relationship having ever attached to him, there is no *yibbum*-framework *issur lav* — Rachel simply remains an *eshet ach* and carries *issur kares* for him. Therefore, if Yehuda attempts *kiddushin*, *ein lah alav klum* — the *kiddushin* does not take hold because it is *issur kares*. That is the second clause.

The two resolutions thus mirror each other structurally while reflecting the underlying dispute: for Rav Ashi following Reish Lakish, it is the *previously born* brothers who are *issur kares* and the *newly born* who still have *chalitzah* requirements; for Ravina following Rabbi Yochanan, it is precisely the reverse — the previously present brothers are *issur lav* with *chalitzah* obligations, while the newly born brother, who never had a *yibbum* connection, is *issur kares*.

## Yibbum Application

### Yibbum Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַבָּא עַל יְבָמָה, וּבָא אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין עַל צָרָתָהּ, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא. חַד אָמַר: בְּכָרֵת. וְחַד אָמַר: בַּעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a similar dispute between *amora'im* with regard to **one** who **had relations with** his ***yevama*** and thereby performed the mitzva of levirate marriage as required, **and one of the** other **brothers had relations with her rival wife. Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree** with regard to this matter. **One said:** The second brother is liable **to** receive ***karet*. And** the other **one said** that he is in violation **of** only **a positive mitzva.** The verse: "Who does not build up his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9), is a positive mitzva, as it teaches that one house may be built up, but not two houses, i.e., no more than one wife of a deceased brother may be married by one of his brothers. Any mitzva formulated as a positive injunction has the status of a positive mitzva, even if it is violated by the performance of an action, in the manner of a prohibition.

The *Gemara* now introduces a parallel dispute involving *yibbum* rather than *chalitzah*. The case: Reuven is married to Rachel and Devorah. He dies. Levi performs *yibbum* with Rachel — that is, he has marital relations with her in fulfillment of the *mitzvah*. Subsequently, Yehuda comes and has relations with Devorah. What is Devorah's status with respect to Yehuda?

The debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish applied to *chalitzah*; one can now pose the same question for *yibbum*. Once one brother has performed *yibbum* with one wife, what is every other brother's relationship to the remaining co-wife? Reish Lakish's logic would say: Levi actualized a *yibbum* relationship with Rachel specifically, and everything else reverts to *eshet ach* with *kares*. Rabbi Yochanan's logic of representation would say: everyone participated representatively, so all relationships are now reconfigured within the *yibbum* framework, and no one reverts to full *eshet ach*.

### Rav Acha vs. Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּכָרֵת — כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בַּעֲשֵׂה — כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The one who said that he** is liable **to** receive ***karet*** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** who maintains that after the mitzva is performed with one *yevama* who required levirate marriage, the prohibition of a brother's wife is once again fully applicable to her rival wife. **And the one who said** that he is in violation **of** only **a positive mitzva** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

Why *issur asei* rather than *issur lav* as in the *chalitzah* case? The source differs. In the *chalitzah* case, the *lav* came from *asher lo yivneh es beis achiv* — the negative formulation of not building. In the *yibbum* case, the relevant verse yields a positive commandment: *bayit echad hu voneh ve-eino voneh shenei batim* — he builds one household, not two. Levi performed *yibbum* with Rachel; the verse teaches that he may not perform a second *yibbum* with Devorah. This is understood as an *issur asei*. By Rabbi Yochanan's representational logic, the same *issur asei* applies equally to all brothers and all wives, since all participated representatively in the *yibbum* act. Hence one who holds like Rabbi Yochanan says *be-asei*; one who holds like Reish Lakish says *be-kares*, since for all parties other than the specific Levi-Rachel *yibbum* pair, the relationship reverts to *eshet ach*.

We know from the earlier discussion that Ravina holds like Rabbi Yochanan; it follows that Ravina is the one who says *be-asei*, and Rav Acha takes the Reish Lakish position of *be-kares*.

## Sotah Question

### Rav on Sotah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: צָרַת סוֹטָה אֲסוּרָה. טוּמְאָה כְּתִיב בָּהּ, כָּעֲרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further analyzes the list of women exempt from levirate marriage. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The rival wife of a *sota* is forbidden.** In this context, *sota* refers to an unfaithful wife. If the husband died, the unfaithful wife and her rival wife are exempt from both *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage. What is the reason for this? The term **defilement is written in that** passage dealing with an unfaithful wife: "She being defiled secretly" (Numbers 5:13), just **as** defilement is written with regard to **those with whom relations are forbidden** in the verse: "Defile not yourselves in any of these things, for in all these the nations are defiled, which I cast out from before you" (Leviticus 18:24). This teaches that the *halakha* of an unfaithful wife is like that of a woman with whom relations are forbidden; both she and her rival wife are exempt from levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

Having completed the central *sugya* of the Reish Lakish–Rabbi Yochanan dispute and its application, the *Gemara* pivots to a different question: are there women who perhaps should have been added to the *mishnah*'s list of fifteen but were not? One such case is now introduced.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: tzarat sotah asurah* — the co-wife of a *sotah* is prohibited from *yibbum*. The reasoning: *tumah ketiv bah* — the *Torah* describes the *sotah* who committed adultery using the language of *tumah*. The verse states *ve-nistara ve-hi nitma'ah*, and separately, *ve-nistara ve-hi lo nitma'ah*. When she has in fact committed adultery, the act is described as *tumah*. Since the *issur arayot* is likewise described in the *Torah* in terms of *tumah* — *lo titam'u be-chol eleh* — a *sotah* who definitively committed adultery is treated as though she carries the status of *arayot*. There is therefore no *mitzvat yibbum* with respect to her, and by extension, no *mitzvat yibbum* with respect to her *tzarah* either.

One might note the irony: the status of *tumah* that makes her forbidden was, in the first instance, her prohibition to her own husband, Reuven. It was not a prohibition extending to anyone else. Yet when Reuven dies and Shimon faces the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*, we say that because she is termed *temeiah* — carrying the character of *arayot* — Shimon is exempt from *yibbum* entirely. The *Gemara* itself does not make this observation, but it sharpens what is at stake in Rav's ruling.

### Chisda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חִסְדָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּיאָתָהּ אוֹ חֲלִיצָתָהּ מֵאָחִיו שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן — פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raised an objection** to this from the case of a woman who heard that her husband had died and remarried, only afterward to discover that he had still been alive at that time before dying later. What is the *halakha* in this case? **Rabbi Shimon says: Her relations** with **or *ḥalitza* from the brother of the first** husband **exempts her rival wife.** This shows that the obligation of levirate marriage applies to this woman. Although she lived as a married woman with someone else during her husband's lifetime, Rabbi Shimon nevertheless maintains that her sexual intercourse or *ḥalitza* fulfills the mitzva of levirate marriage and exempts her rival wife. This suggests that the rival wife of an unfaithful wife requires levirate marriage.

Rav Chisda challenges this from a *baraita*: a woman's husband travels overseas and is reported dead by a single witness. Relying on that testimony, she remarries. Then the first husband returns alive. She is now in the anomalous position of being bound to two men, and the law imposes severe consequences — *asurah la-zeh ula-zeh, tetze mi-zeh u-mi-zeh*. Yet the *baraita* indicates that if the first husband subsequently dies before these matters are resolved, the levir still performs *chalitzah* or *yibbum* — and that act exempts her co-wife. This implies that even a woman in the quasi-*sotah* category, having effectively cohabited with two men, still generates *yibbum* obligations. How, then, can Rav say a *sotah*'s co-wife is entirely exempt?

### Sotah Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [אָמַר לְךָ רַב:] אָמֵינָא לָךְ אֲנָא סוֹטָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לִי סוֹטָה דְּרַבָּנַן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav** could have **said to you** in response: **I spoke to you** about the *halakha* of **a *sota* by Torah** law, i.e., a wife who was intentionally unfaithful, **and you speak to me** of **a *sota* by rabbinic** law? The case you mentioned is an unavoidable accident, as the wife married another man only because she received testimony that her husband was deceased. When the Sages instituted that she was forbidden to both her first and second husbands, this was a penalty designed to ensure that women would examine testimony of this kind very thoroughly. However, this is not a case of a *sota* by Torah law, as she was not intentionally unfaithful, and therefore the mitzva of levirate marriage certainly applies to her.

The answer is immediate: *amar lach Rav, amina lach ana sotah de-oraita* — Rav was speaking of a *sotah de-oraita*, a woman who willfully and definitively committed adultery, a true biblical *sotah*. The case in the *baraita* is a *sotah de-rabbanan* — the woman acted entirely in good faith, relying on valid testimony; her second marriage was entered unwittingly. She is not a *sotah* in the biblical sense at all. The various stringencies imposed upon her — that she cannot return to either husband — are rabbinic measures. At the biblical level she is an *ones*, her act was entirely unwitting, and Rav's ruling about true *sotah* does not apply to her.

### Ashi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אָשֵׁי: נִכְנְסָה עִמּוֹ לְסֵתֶר, וְשָׁהֲתָה עִמּוֹ כְּדֵי טוּמְאָה — אֲסוּרָה לְבֵיתָהּ, וַאֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה. וְאִם מֵת, חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raised an objection** to Rav's opinion from a different source: The *halakha* is that a wife who was warned by her husband not to be secluded with a specific man and yet **entered** alone **with him in private, and remained with him** long enough **to be defiled,** i.e., sufficient time to have sexual relations, she **is forbidden to her house,** i.e., her husband, from that moment until she undergoes the *sota* ritual. **And** likewise, if she was the wife of a priest **she is prohibited from partaking of *teruma*.** **And if he dies, she performs *ḥalitza* and does not enter into levirate marriage.**

Rav Ashi then raises a challenge from another *baraita*: *nichnesah immo le-seter ve-shafah immo ke-dei tumah, asurah le-beitah ve-asurah le-echol bi-trumah* — a woman who secluded herself with the suspected man for a duration sufficient for intercourse to have occurred is forbidden to resume marital relations with her husband and, if she is married to a *kohen*, is forbidden to eat *terumah*. *Im meis — choletzet ve-lo mityabemet* — if the husband then dies, the *yevamah* performs *chalitzah* but not *yibbum*. 

The *baraita* thus shows that even a woman suspected of adultery — not one who definitively committed it — still requires *chalitzah*. This seems to undercut Rav's position: if even the *sotah safek* requires *chalitzah*, how can Rav hold that a *sotah vaday* is treated as *arayot* with no *chalitzah* requirement?

### Definite vs. Suspected

The *Gemara* must answer Rav's position again:

We know from Sefaria 11a (the following daf) that there is more discussion here. Based on the essay's narrative and the structure of the Talmud, the answer would explain the distinction between *sotah vaday* (certain adultery) and *sotah safek* (suspected adultery), but this specific resolution is not present in the three Sefaria text blocks provided. The essay text states: "The answer, again, is Rav's own distinction: *amina lach ana sotah vaday* — I was speaking of a woman who definitely committed adultery. A *sotah safek* is precisely that — uncertain. Perhaps she is entirely innocent. Because of that doubt, the law cannot treat her as a full *ervah*, and so *chalitzah* remains required. But a *sotah vaday*, whose adultery is established, is described in the *Torah* as *temeiah*, and that *tumah* language assimilates her to the category of *arayot*, where there is no *yibbum* and no *chalitzah*."

Since this specific Talmudic formulation does not appear in the provided text blocks, this section continues without a blockquote.

The *Gemara* clarifies that Rav distinguishes between *sotah vaday* and *sotah safek*. A *sotah vaday* is a woman whose adultery is definitively established, described in the Torah using the language of *tumah* in a way that assimilates her to the category of *arayot*, where there is no requirement for either *yibbum* or *chalitzah*. A *sotah safek*, by contrast, is a woman suspected of adultery — perhaps innocent. Because of this doubt, she cannot be treated as a full *ervah*, and therefore *chalitzah* remains required.

### Pasuk Source

The *Gemara* then probes the source of the *lav* that applies to a *sotah vaday*. The word *tumah* appears in the *sotah* passage (*ve-hi nitma'ah*), which establishes her status. But what is the source of the prohibition itself — the *lo ta'aseh* — that makes it forbidden for her husband to continue with her? The *Gemara* traces this to the verse in *Devarim* dealing with *machzir gerushato*: *lo yuchal ba'alah ha-rishon asher shilcheha lashuv lekachtah acharei asher hutama'ah*. The word *hutama'ah* at the end of that verse, which would seem redundant given that the entire passage describes a woman who legitimately remarried, is interpreted as extending the prohibition to a *sotah* who committed adultery — teaching that a woman declared *temeiah* may not return to her husband. And since the verse imposes a *lo ta'aseh*, this establishes the *lav* for *sotah vaday*.

### Yosi ben Kippur

Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar, citing Rabbi Eliezer, reads the verse entirely differently. He holds that *ha-machzir gerushato min ha-nisuin asurah, min ha-eirusin muteret* — a man who divorced his wife and she subsequently married another man may not remarry her if she had full *nisuin* with the second husband, but if she had only *eirusin* — betrothal without cohabitation — she is permitted to return to him. The word *hutama'ah* in the verse is therefore not extending the prohibition to *sotah* cases; it is limiting the prohibition of *machzir gerushato* to cases where the second marriage involved *nisuin*, meaning there was an opportunity for actual relations, so that the *tumah* language is meaningful. Without *nisuin*, there was no realistic *tumah* and the *lav* does not apply.

According to Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar, then, the verse is entirely occupied with *machzir gerushato* and does not extend to *sotah* at all. As a result, *lav be-sotah leisa lei* — he has no *lav* for the *sotah* who committed adultery. The *Gemara* notes the striking implication: *ve-afilu zenai nami* — even a woman who definitively committed adultery does not carry a biblical *lav* prohibiting her from returning to her husband, according to Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar. The *lav* of *machzir gerushato* is generated by *havayah ve-ishus* — the legitimate marriage and marital bond with a second man, formalized through *nisuin*. A case of adultery, however severe, does not fulfill that description.

The irony is unmistakable, and the *Gemara* plainly intends it to register: the very prohibition that would seem most obviously to apply in the case of adultery in fact, according to this view, applies only in the fully legitimate case — where a woman was properly divorced, properly remarried, and the second marriage was consummated. The worse violation carries no *lav*; the entirely proper sequence does. This is where the *sugya* rests for now, and the discussion of *machzir gerushato* as a potential addition to the *mishnah*'s list will continue in the *daf* that follows.
