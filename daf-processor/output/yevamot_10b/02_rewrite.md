# Yevamot 10b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Context

### Nine Dafim Survey

It is worth pausing at this juncture to take stock of where we are in the tractate. We began on daf 4, and from that point until now, the first genuinely new amoraic statement bearing directly on the *mishnah* was the exchange between Levi and Rebbi regarding why the *mishnah* enumerates fifteen co-wives rather than sixteen. Everything preceding that was essentially *stam* — anonymous Talmudic discussion — with no amoraic voices meeting one another head-to-head. The *Gemara* worked through an extended opening *sugya*: why one might have entertained the *hava amina* that *yibbum bemakom ervah* could be permitted; the principle of *aseh doche lo ta'aseh* and what might have been derived from *eshet ach*; and then the whole question of what source each Tanna uses for the exemption of *ervah vetzarat ervah*, and finally the question of fifteen versus sixteen.

### Transition to Standard Format

From this point forward, the *sugya* moves into a more recognizable Talmudic structure. Rather than the extended, largely anonymous introductory material that characterized the preceding nine dapim, we now encounter direct amoraic disputes, back-and-forth argumentation, and proofs marshaled for competing positions. The *sugyot* in Yevamot remain anything but simple, but they take on a more familiar shape — statements of *amoraim*, countervailing opinions, and attempts at resolution. That is precisely where we now begin, at 11b.

## Reish Lakish vs. Yochanan

### Chalitzah Then Remarriage

The discussion opens with Reish Lakish addressing Rabbi Yochanan, framing his question around the position of Levi. Levi, recall, wished to expand the *mishnah*'s list to sixteen by including *anusat aviv* — a woman whom a father violated but did not marry. Levi was apparently willing for the *mishnah* to list cases that apply only *bedi'eved*, meaning situations where marriage, though prohibited, would nonetheless be binding after the fact. The *Gemara* then asks: if Levi is willing to include *bedi'eved* cases, why not include yet another such case?

To understand the case in question, set up the following scenario: Reuven is married to Rachel; Shimon is married to an unrelated woman, Devorah. Reuven dies. Shimon now stands before a *mitzvah* of *yibbum* or *chalitzah* with Rachel, and similarly with Devorah (Reuven's wife). He performs *chalitzah* with Rachel. At that point, the *mitzvah* is discharged — one brother performing one act, whether *chalitzah* or *yibbum*, releases all the wives and exempts all the remaining brothers. Everyone is free.

Now the question: what happens if Shimon, having performed *chalitzah* with Rachel, goes ahead and remarries her? Everyone agrees that this is prohibited. The source is the verse *asher lo yivneh es beis achiv* — "who did not build his brother's house" — from which we derive that once he has declined to build, *keivan shelo vana shuv eino voneh*, he may not reverse course and marry her afterward. This constitutes a *lav* — a prohibition rooted in the *yibbum* framework that now governs his relationship with her.

Yet suppose he does so anyway. The *kiddushin* is binding *bedi'eved* because it is only an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*. He has now in effect remarried Rachel. Then Shimon dies. What is Levi's relationship to Rachel, whom Shimon had remarried? This is precisely the kind of *bedi'eved* situation Levi was willing to include in the *mishnah*. Rachel could be considered an *ervah* to Levi — a *chalutzah* who was subsequently remarried — and if Shimon also had a co-wife (for instance, Devorah had also become bound to the same estate through *yibbum*), then that co-wife would become a *tzarat ervah*. This is a new case of exemption from *yibbum* that perhaps should have appeared in the *mishnah*'s list.

The *Gemara* answers on behalf of Rabbi Yochanan: *lefi she-einah betzoras tzora* — the reason this case was not included is that it does not generate a *tzarat tzara*. Recall that the *mishnah*'s list included *tzarat tzara* — a co-wife's co-wife — as a further extension of the exemption. For *tzarat tzara* to exist, there must be a co-wife who is prohibited only to some brothers and not others, so that when she marries another brother her own co-wife becomes a *tzarat ervah* exempt from *yibbum* with yet another brother. In the case of the *chalutzah* who was remarried, however, Rachel is equally prohibited to all of Reuven's brothers — the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* had fallen equally on all of them — and therefore no configuration arises in which one brother's wife becomes a *tzarat tzara* for another brother. Since the *tzarat tzara* extension does not apply here, the case was excluded from the *mishnah*.

The *Gemara* then asks: why didn't Rabbi Yochanan simply respond to Reish Lakish by saying that according to him, Rachel and Devorah are only an *issur lav* to the remaining brothers, not an *issur kares*, and *chayavei lavin* retain their *yibbum* and *chalitzah* obligations and therefore do not belong in the list of fifteen at all? The *Gemara* answers: *ledivarav ka'amar lei* — Rabbi Yochanan was arguing within Reish Lakish's own framework, showing that even granting Reish Lakish's premise, the case still does not fit the *mishnah* for the reason of *tzarat tzara*.

This roundabout framing, however, was simply the *Gemara*'s vehicle for arriving at the core dispute. The direct statement of the debate now follows.

### Reish Lakish Position

*Itmar* — it was stated. The case: *ha-choleitz li-yevamto ve-chazar ve-kiddashah* — a man performs *chalitzah* with his *yevamah* and then goes and betroths her again. Reish Lakish holds: *hu eino chayav al ha-chalutzah kares* — he himself, Shimon, who remarries the woman he performed *chalitzah* with, is not liable for *kares* with respect to her. His ongoing relationship with her has been restructured within the *yibbum* framework: the prohibition on his remarrying her is the specific *issur lav* of *keivan shelo vana shuv eino voneh*. That is a new category of *issur*, not a reversion to *eshet ach*.

But for the other brothers — Levi, Yehuda, and so forth — *ha-achim chayavim al ha-chalutzah kares*: their relationship with Rachel reverts to *eshet ach*, which carries *kares*. The logic is that Shimon had a *yibbum* relationship with Rachel, and so his prohibition is defined within that *yibbum* world. Levi, however, never actualized any *yibbum* relationship with Rachel. The potential was there, but it was not actualized, and so the general prohibition of *eshet ach* with its *kares* consequence remains in full force for him.

By the same token, Shimon's relationship with Devorah — the other wife — also reverts to *eshet ach* with *kares*, because he never had a *yibbum* relationship with Devorah at all. And both he and the other brothers are equally liable for *kares* with respect to the *tzarah*. The only exception carved out from full *kares* status is Shimon's specific relationship with Rachel, to whom he performed *chalitzah* — that and only that relationship is redefined in *yibbum* terms and carries merely an *issur lav*.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this framework entirely. *Mi ika midi de-me'ikara ei ba'ei hai chalitz ve'ei ba'ei hai chalitz* — is there anything here where, from the outset, only one specific party was designated? Before anyone did anything, Shimon could have performed *chalitzah* with Rachel or with Devorah; Levi equally could have performed *chalitzah* with Rachel or with Devorah. Any combination was possible. All the brothers stood equally before the *mitzvah*; all the wives stood equally subject to it. To say that one act — Shimon performing *chalitzah* with Rachel — changes the status of Rachel vis-à-vis Shimon but leaves everyone else in the pre-existing *eshet ach* category is, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, incoherent. They all had an equal relationship to this *mitzvah*.

Furthermore, Rabbi Yochanan presses the point: *ve-hashta de-kayala bekarei* — now that she has performed the act of *chalitzah*, is it reasonable that all these relationships which could equally have been the ones to undergo *chalitzah* should suddenly revert to *issur kares*? That makes no sense given that any of them could have been the participants.

This leads to a striking conceptual formulation: *ihu shlichusa de-achin ka-avid, ihi shlichusa de-tzarah ka-avda* — he, Shimon, performs *chalitzah* as the agent of all the brothers; she, Rachel, receives the *chalitzah* as the agent of all the co-wives. Shimon is, in effect, a *shliach tzibur* — representing Levi, Yehuda, and all the rest. Rachel is representing Devorah and any other co-wife in the same estate. The implication is radical: the *chalitzah* performed by Shimon with Rachel is considered as though all the brothers performed *chalitzah* with all the wives. Therefore, everyone now stands in the same relationship — not *eshet ach* with *kares*, but a new *issur* defined within the *yibbum* world.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, nobody — not Shimon, not Levi, not Yehuda, with respect to Rachel or Devorah — is liable for *kares*. They all have the same status: a new *issur lav* that emerged from the *chalitzah* act, applicable universally because the act was performed representatively for all.

### Agency & Representation

The mechanism articulated by Rabbi Yochanan deserves emphasis because it is one of the conceptually foundational ideas of tractate Yevamot. The claim is not merely that the brothers and wives had a theoretical possibility of participating — it is that the one who actually participates does so as a representative of all the others. This is what *shlichut* means in this context: Shimon's *chalitzah* is treated as legally equivalent to every brother performing *chalitzah*, and Rachel's receipt of it is treated as every co-wife's receipt. The act is, in a sense, collective even when physically performed by individuals.

This principle is what differentiates Rabbi Yochanan from Reish Lakish at the deepest level. Reish Lakish sees the act as individual: Shimon and Rachel entered a particular legal relationship, and everything outside that bilateral relationship remains governed by the prior law of *eshet ach*. Rabbi Yochanan sees the act as representative: through the mechanism of agency, all parties are equally drawn into the new legal reality created by that single act of *chalitzah*.

## Baraita Proofs

### Yochanan's Proof

With the two positions established, the *Gemara* turns to evidence. Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: *ha-choleitz li-yevamto ve-chazar ve-kiddashah u-meis, tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin* — if a man performs *chalitzah* with his *yevamah*, then remarries her, and then dies, the remaining brothers are required to perform *chalitzah* with her.

According to Rabbi Yochanan, this is entirely consistent: their status is an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*, and women in an *issur lav* relationship with the brothers still require *chalitzah* — they cannot simply walk free, but neither can *yibbum* be performed. *Bishlama lidi, amina chayavei lavin ninhu, hainu de-tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin* — this is exactly what my position predicts.

But according to Reish Lakish, the other brothers stand in an *issur kares* relationship with this woman. Women who are *arayot* — full *ervah* — are entirely exempt from both *chalitzah* and *yibbum*. If her status is *issur kares* for the brothers, why does the *baraita* require *chalitzah* at all? This seems to be a clear proof for Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lakish Counter

Reish Lakish counters: *u-letamach, eima seifa* — you cited only the first half of the *baraita*; read the second half. *Amad echad min ha-achin ve-kiddshah, ein lah alav klum* — if one of the brothers went ahead and betrothed her, the *kiddushin* is entirely null and void, as though nothing happened. But if their status were only an *issur lav*, *kiddushin* would be binding *bedi'eved*, as it is in all cases of *chayavei lavin*. The fact that the *kiddushin* takes no hold whatsoever — *ein lah alav klum* — indicates *issur kares*, where *kiddushin* never takes effect.

Now the *baraita* is internally contradictory. Its first clause requires *chalitzah*, implying *issur lav*. Its second clause declares *kiddushin* null, implying *issur kares*. Neither Rabbi Yochanan nor Reish Lakish can claim the *baraita* as consistent support; it speaks with two irreconcilable voices.

The *Gemara* proposes an initial resolution attributed to Rav Sheshet: *seifa asya ke-Rabbi Akiva de-amar ein kiddushin tofsin be-chayavei lavin* — the second clause follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *chayavei lavin* are treated like *chayavei kares* and *kiddushin* does not take effect with them either. The first clause follows the normative view that *lav* differs from *kares* and *chalitzah* is still required. The *Gemara* immediately objects: if the second clause represents only Rabbi Akiva's view, it should have been introduced with explicit attribution — *le-divrei Rabbi Akiva*. Since it was not, this resolution is unsatisfying. *Kashya.*

### Rav Ashi Resolution

The *Gemara* now records two amoraic positions on how to reconcile the *baraita*: *Rav Ashi savar lah ke-Reish Lakish u-metaretz lah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — Rav Ashi holds like Reish Lakish and reconciles the *baraita* through Rabbi Shimon's principle. *Ravina savar lah ke-Rabbi Yochanan u-metaretz ke-rabbanan* — Ravina holds like Rabbi Yochanan and reconciles the *baraita* according to the rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Shimon.

Rav Ashi's resolution: The *baraita* addresses *achin ha-noladim* — brothers who were born after the relevant events. Recall Rabbi Shimon's ruling that a brother born after the fact relates to a woman only in the status she occupied when he entered the world, without the prior history affecting him. Under this principle, the two halves of the *baraita* refer to two different groups of brothers.

To illustrate: suppose Shimon performs *chalitzah* with Rachel and then remarries her — stage one. At that point, say, Yehuda had already been born, while Levi is born only after Shimon has remarried Rachel. For Levi, who enters the world when Rachel is already Shimon's wife (a binding marriage, even if *bedi'eved*), all the prior history — the *chalitzah*, the prohibition — is irrelevant. He finds Rachel as his brother Shimon's wife, governed by the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*. When Shimon subsequently dies, Levi can and must perform either *chalitzah* or *yibbum* — that is the first clause of the *baraita*: *tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin*, referring to newly born brothers like Levi.

For Yehuda, who was alive throughout the entire sequence, Rachel is an *issur kares* — she was always in the *yibbum* orbit and he was always present to it, so Reish Lakish's principle applies fully. If Yehuda goes ahead and performs *kiddushin* with Rachel, *ein lah alav klum* — that is the second clause of the *baraita*, referring to brothers who were already present (*ha-yeludim*) when all this transpired. Thus the two halves of the *baraita* are perfectly consistent according to Reish Lakish, parsed through Rabbi Shimon's distinction of *noladim* versus *yeludim*.

### Ravina Resolution

Ravina, holding like Rabbi Yochanan and reconciling according to the *rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Shimon, reaches the opposite parsing. For Rabbi Yochanan, all brothers who were present when the *chalitzah* took place have the status of *issur lav* — they participated representatively in the *chalitzah* process, and so they still require *chalitzah* but cannot perform *yibbum*. These are the brothers referred to in the first clause: *tzrichah chalitzah min ha-achin* — namely, the brothers who were there, like Levi.

But what of a brother born afterward — Yehuda, who enters the world after the *chalitzah* and the subsequent remarriage, when Rachel is already Shimon's wife? For Yehuda, Rachel is *eshet achiv she-lo haya be-olamo* — the wife of a brother who predated him. According to the *rabbanan*, unlike Rabbi Shimon, the temporal sequence of birth does not retroactively adjust the prohibition. Yehuda never had a *mitzvat yibbum* at all with respect to Rachel. Without any *yibbum* relationship having ever attached to him, there is no *yibbum*-framework *issur lav* — Rachel simply remains an *eshet ach* and carries *issur kares* for him. Therefore, if Yehuda attempts *kiddushin*, *ein lah alav klum* — the *kiddushin* does not take hold because it is *issur kares*. That is the second clause.

The two resolutions thus mirror each other structurally while reflecting the underlying dispute: for Rav Ashi following Reish Lakish, it is the *previously born* brothers who are *issur kares* and the *newly born* who still have *chalitzah* requirements; for Ravina following Rabbi Yochanan, it is precisely the reverse — the previously present brothers are *issur lav* with *chalitzah* obligations, while the newly born brother, who never had a *yibbum* connection, is *issur kares*.

## Yibbum Application

### Yibbum Case Setup

The *Gemara* now introduces a parallel dispute involving *yibbum* rather than *chalitzah*. The case: Reuven is married to Rachel and Devorah. He dies. Levi performs *yibbum* with Rachel — that is, he has marital relations with her in fulfillment of the *mitzvah*. Subsequently, Yehuda comes and has relations with Devorah. What is Devorah's status with respect to Yehuda?

The debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish applied to *chalitzah*; one can now pose the same question for *yibbum*. Once one brother has performed *yibbum* with one wife, what is every other brother's relationship to the remaining co-wife? Reish Lakish's logic would say: Levi actualized a *yibbum* relationship with Rachel specifically, and everything else reverts to *eshet ach* with *kares*. Rabbi Yochanan's logic of representation would say: everyone participated representatively, so all relationships are now reconfigured within the *yibbum* framework, and no one reverts to full *eshet ach*.

### Rav Acha vs. Ravina

*Peligi bah Rav Acha ve-Ravina* — Rav Acha and Ravina disagree. *Chad amar be-kares, ve-chad amar be-asei* — one holds the prohibition is *issur kares*, the other holds it is an *issur asei*.

Why *issur asei* rather than *issur lav* as in the *chalitzah* case? The source differs. In the *chalitzah* case, the *lav* came from *asher lo yivneh es beis achiv* — the negative formulation of not building. In the *yibbum* case, the relevant verse yields a positive commandment: *bayit echad hu voneh ve-eino voneh shenei batim* — he builds one household, not two. Levi performed *yibbum* with Rachel; the verse teaches that he may not perform a second *yibbum* with Devorah. This is understood as an *issur asei*. By Rabbi Yochanan's representational logic, the same *issur asei* applies equally to all brothers and all wives, since all participated representatively in the *yibbum* act. Hence one who holds like Rabbi Yochanan says *be-asei*; one who holds like Reish Lakish says *be-kares*, since for all parties other than the specific Levi-Rachel *yibbum* pair, the relationship reverts to *eshet ach*.

We know from the earlier discussion that Ravina holds like Rabbi Yochanan; it follows that Ravina is the one who says *be-asei*, and Rav Acha takes the Reish Lakish position of *be-kares*.

## Sotah Question

### Rav on Sotah

Having completed the central *sugya* of the Reish Lakish–Rabbi Yochanan dispute and its application, the *Gemara* pivots to a different question: are there women who perhaps should have been added to the *mishnah*'s list of fifteen but were not? One such case is now introduced.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: tzarat sotah asurah* — the co-wife of a *sotah* is prohibited from *yibbum*. The reasoning: *tumah ketiv bah* — the *Torah* describes the *sotah* who committed adultery using the language of *tumah*. The verse states *ve-nistara ve-hi nitma'ah*, and separately, *ve-nistara ve-hi lo nitma'ah*. When she has in fact committed adultery, the act is described as *tumah*. Since the *issur arayot* is likewise described in the *Torah* in terms of *tumah* — *lo titam'u be-chol eleh* — a *sotah* who definitively committed adultery is treated as though she carries the status of *arayot*. There is therefore no *mitzvat yibbum* with respect to her, and by extension, no *mitzvat yibbum* with respect to her *tzarah* either.

One might note the irony: the status of *tumah* that makes her forbidden was, in the first instance, her prohibition to her own husband, Reuven. It was not a prohibition extending to anyone else. Yet when Reuven dies and Shimon faces the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*, we say that because she is termed *temeiah* — carrying the character of *arayot* — Shimon is exempt from *yibbum* entirely. The *Gemara* itself does not make this observation, but it sharpens what is at stake in Rav's ruling.

### Chisda Challenge

Rav Chisda challenges this from a *baraita*: *Rav Shimon omer, biah o chalitzatah me-achiv ha-rishon poteres tzaratah* — Rabbi Shimon rules that *yibbum* or *chalitzah* performed with her by the first husband's brother exempts her co-wife.

The case behind this citation: a woman's husband travels overseas and is reported dead by a single witness. Relying on that testimony, she remarries. Then the first husband returns alive. She is now in the anomalous position of being bound to two men, and the law imposes severe consequences — *asurah la-zeh ula-zeh, tetze mi-zeh u-mi-zeh*. Yet the *baraita* indicates that if the first husband subsequently dies before these matters are resolved, the levir still performs *chalitzah* or *yibbum* — and that act exempts her co-wife. This implies that even a woman in the quasi-*sotah* category, having effectively cohabited with two men, still generates *yibbum* obligations. How, then, can Rav say a *sotah*'s co-wife is entirely exempt?

### Sotah Categories

The answer is immediate: *amar lach Rav, amina lach ana sotah de-oraita* — Rav was speaking of a *sotah de-oraita*, a woman who willfully and definitively committed adultery, a true biblical *sotah*. The case in the *baraita* is a *sotah de-rabbanan* — the woman acted entirely in good faith, relying on valid testimony; her second marriage was entered unwittingly. She is not a *sotah* in the biblical sense at all. The various stringencies imposed upon her — that she cannot return to either husband — are rabbinic measures. At the biblical level she is an *ones*, her act was entirely unwitting, and Rav's ruling about true *sotah* does not apply to her.

### Ashi's Proof

Rav Ashi then raises a challenge from another *baraita*: *nichnesah immo le-seter ve-shafah immo ke-dei tumah, asurah le-beitah ve-asurah le-echol bi-trumah* — a woman who secluded herself with the suspected man for a duration sufficient for intercourse to have occurred is forbidden to resume marital relations with her husband and, if she is married to a *kohen*, is forbidden to eat *terumah*. *Im meis — choletzet ve-lo mityabemet* — if the husband then dies, the *yevamah* performs *chalitzah* but not *yibbum*. 

The *baraita* thus shows that even a woman suspected of adultery — not one who definitively committed it — still requires *chalitzah*. This seems to undercut Rav's position: if even the *sotah safek* requires *chalitzah*, how can Rav hold that a *sotah vaday* is treated as *arayot* with no *chalitzah* requirement?

### Definite vs. Suspected

The answer, again, is Rav's own distinction: *amina lach ana sotah vaday* — I was speaking of a woman who definitely committed adultery. A *sotah safek* is precisely that — uncertain. Perhaps she is entirely innocent. Because of that doubt, the law cannot treat her as a full *ervah*, and so *chalitzah* remains required. But a *sotah vaday*, whose adultery is established, is described in the *Torah* as *temeiah*, and that *tumah* language assimilates her to the category of *arayot*, where there is no *yibbum* and no *chalitzah*.

### Pasuk Source

The *Gemara* then probes the source of the *lav* that applies to a *sotah vaday*. The word *tumah* appears in the *sotah* passage (*ve-hi nitma'ah*), which establishes her status. But what is the source of the prohibition itself — the *lo ta'aseh* — that makes it forbidden for her husband to continue with her? The *Gemara* traces this to the verse in *Devarim* dealing with *machzir gerushato*: *lo yuchal ba'alah ha-rishon asher shilcheha lashuv lekachtah acharei asher hutama'ah*. The word *hutama'ah* at the end of that verse, which would seem redundant given that the entire passage describes a woman who legitimately remarried, is interpreted as extending the prohibition to a *sotah* who committed adultery — teaching that a woman declared *temeiah* may not return to her husband. And since the verse imposes a *lo ta'aseh*, this establishes the *lav* for *sotah vaday*.

### Yosi ben Kippur

Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar, citing Rabbi Eliezer, reads the verse entirely differently. He holds that *ha-machzir gerushato min ha-nisuin asurah, min ha-eirusin muteret* — a man who divorced his wife and she subsequently married another man may not remarry her if she had full *nisuin* with the second husband, but if she had only *eirusin* — betrothal without cohabitation — she is permitted to return to him. The word *hutama'ah* in the verse is therefore not extending the prohibition to *sotah* cases; it is limiting the prohibition of *machzir gerushato* to cases where the second marriage involved *nisuin*, meaning there was an opportunity for actual relations, so that the *tumah* language is meaningful. Without *nisuin*, there was no realistic *tumah* and the *lav* does not apply.

According to Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar, then, the verse is entirely occupied with *machzir gerushato* and does not extend to *sotah* at all. As a result, *lav be-sotah leisa lei* — he has no *lav* for the *sotah* who committed adultery. The *Gemara* notes the striking implication: *ve-afilu zenai nami* — even a woman who definitively committed adultery does not carry a biblical *lav* prohibiting her from returning to her husband, according to Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar. The *lav* of *machzir gerushato* is generated by *havayah ve-ishus* — the legitimate marriage and marital bond with a second man, formalized through *nisuin*. A case of adultery, however severe, does not fulfill that description.

The irony is unmistakable, and the *Gemara* plainly intends it to register: the very prohibition that would seem most obviously to apply in the case of adultery in fact, according to this view, applies only in the fully legitimate case — where a woman was properly divorced, properly remarried, and the second marriage was consummated. The worse violation carries no *lav*; the entirely proper sequence does. This is where the *sugya* rests for now, and the discussion of *machzir gerushato* as a potential addition to the *mishnah*'s list will continue in the *daf* that follows.
