# Bava Metzia 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Butcher's Knife Seizure

### Case Setup

The daf opens at the midpoint of 116a, concluding the discussion of *kli she'osim bo ochel nefesh* — implements used in the production of food — which the Torah prohibits taking as collateral. The *Gemara* had been exploring the scope of this prohibition beyond the explicit biblical example of *recheim varechev* (millstones and the upper millstone), asking whether the verse's concluding phrase *ki nefesh hu chovel* extends the prohibition to other implements of the same category. In the course of that discussion, the *Gemara* offered examples: scissors and a team of oxen. *Tosafot* narrows those examples considerably, explaining that the scissors are vegetable-trimming scissors used directly in food preparation, and that the "team of oxen" refers not to plowing oxen but to threshing oxen — and specifically to the yoke itself, not to the living animals. This keeps the examples tightly within the category of implements that directly interface with food production.

It should also be noted, as *Tosafot* observes in a brief but pointed comment, that this prohibition applies specifically to *keilim* — implements — rather than to food itself. One might have expected the opposite: surely taking someone's food is more harmful than taking their tools. But the logic is the inverse of the familiar teaching about teaching a person to fish: take the food and you deprive someone of one meal; take the implement and you deprive them of an ongoing livelihood. *Tosafot* notes explicitly: *nireh le'Ri dedavka keilim aval ochlim atsmam mutar lachvol* — it appears to Rabbi Yitzchak that specifically implements are prohibited, but actual food may lawfully be taken as collateral.

The *Gemara* now introduces a concrete case: *hahu gavra dechaval sakina de'ashkavta mechavreih* — a man who seized a butcher's knife from his fellow as collateral. The term *ashkavta* appears to specify a butcher's knife. The seizure was apparently in violation, since such a knife qualifies as a *kli she'osim bo ochel nefesh*.

### Abaye's Ruling

The case was brought before *Abaye*, who ruled: *zil ahadreih* — go return it. You violated the prohibition by taking it, since it is a *kli she'osim bo ochel nefesh*. And *vetekum bedina aleih* — if you have a financial claim against him, take him to court. But the improperly seized implement must be returned first.

### Rava's Migo Strategy

*Rava* disagreed. He held that before returning the knife, the creditor need not go to court at all — because he possesses a legal lever that spares him the need. *Veyachol lit'on ad kedei demeihen*: since the creditor is currently holding the object and there are no witnesses to the seizure, he can credibly claim either that the knife was always his or that it had been sold to him. Say the butcher's knife was worth a hundred dollars and the underlying loan was five hundred. *Rava*'s advice: before returning the knife, assert that the debtor owes you at least the value of the knife and that you hold it as collateral for that amount — because you could have claimed all along that the knife was simply yours. This leverage, *Rava* holds, allows the creditor to secure payment of at least the knife's value without litigation.

This is a striking position: the creditor violated a biblical prohibition by seizing this implement, yet *Rava* is prepared to allow him to use possession of that very object as a legal lever before returning it.

### Migo Principle

The mechanism *Rava* invokes is *migo*: I am believed in my weaker true claim because I could have asserted a stronger false claim. In the classic *migo*, the stronger alternative claim is the lie, and the weaker claim is the truth — and the fact that one could have lied more effectively lends credibility to the more modest true assertion.

One might object that a *migo* typically does not involve a lie. But in fact, the stronger alternative claim in a *migo* is always a lie. The entire structure of *migo* is: believe me when I say X, because if I wanted to deceive you, I could have said Y — a more advantageous false claim. That I refrained from the bolder lie lends credibility to my more modest true assertion.

The *Gemara* then steps back from the question of the violation entirely and asks, on its own terms: why did *Abaye* not allow this *migo*? Is the butcher's knife case not identical in structure to a well-known ruling of the father of *Shmuel*?

## Lent vs. Rented Items

### Goats Case

The *Gemara* raises the case of goats that ate *chushla* — some type of vegetation — in Nehardea, destroying someone's field. The owner of the damaged field seized the goats and claimed compensation, asserting that the damage amounted to a large sum. There were no witnesses who could precisely corroborate how much the field had been worth before the goats ate from it. Nevertheless, *avuh de'Shmuel* — the father of *Shmuel* — ruled: *yachol lit'on ad kedei demeihen* — the field owner is believed as to the extent of the damage, up to the value of the goats he is holding. The logic is the same *migo*: since he is holding the goats and could credibly claim they are his own, he is believed when he makes the more modest assertion that the goats caused this much damage and he holds them as security.

Why then did *Abaye* not permit the same reasoning in the butcher's knife case?

### Chazaka Principle

The *Gemara* explains by invoking the principle of *chazaka*: *chazaka mah she'tachas yad adam harei hu shelo* — the presumption is that whatever a person is holding belongs to him. For chattels (as opposed to land), possession carries with it a presumption of ownership. This is the foundation of the *migo* in the goats case: because holding the goats creates a presumption that they are yours, you are believed to make a lesser claim about what you are owed.

### Rented Items Exception

This presumption, however, is not absolute. It does not apply to items that are *de'avidan le'ahshil ule'haskir* — things that are normally lent out or rented out. If I am seen driving a Hertz rental car, no one presumes it is mine. Similarly, certain items — a lawnmower, for instance — are so commonly borrowed from neighbors that possession alone does not establish ownership. *Rav Huna bar Avin* sent a ruling formalizing this principle: *devarim ha'asuyim le'ahshil ule'haskir* — things that are made to be lent or rented — and the possessor claims *lekuchim hem beyadi*, that he purchased them, *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. This is true even in the ordinary case where we knew yesterday that the object belonged to someone else; normally one would be believed to say "I purchased it just now," since people buy and sell things. But when the object is of the type normally lent or rented, that presumption is overridden.

Goats are not normally lent or rented; people do not lend their livestock. Hence, holding goats carries the full presumption of ownership and the *migo* operates. A butcher's knife, by contrast, the *Gemara* treats as something like a lawnmower — an implement that people sometimes borrow — and therefore the *migo* does not apply. That is *Abaye*'s position.

*Rava*'s own practice confirms that he accepts this principle regarding items normally lent or rented. The *Gemara* records that *Rava* himself once recovered *zugga d'sarba v'safra d'agadata* — a pair of wool-shearing scissors and a book of *aggadic* teachings — from orphans who held them as part of their father's estate. *Rava* proved these had once been his, claimed he had lent them to the father, and took them back, reasoning that since these items are normally lent or rented, the orphans' possession did not generate a presumption that the father had purchased them.

So *Rava* unambiguously accepts the principle that items normally lent or rented do not create an ownership presumption. How then could he have permitted the butcher's knife *migo*?

### Knife Depreciation

The *Gemara* resolves this by recording *Rava*'s own explanation: *amar lach Rava, hai nami keivan d'meiphagma* — *Rava* would tell you, this case too is different, because the butcher's knife depreciates significantly through use. People do not lend out items that wear down in value — just as one does not lend a fine, expensive kitchen knife that loses its edge or its finish with each use. Since butcher's knives of the kind in question depreciate and lose value, they are not the sort of item normally lent out, and therefore the full presumption of ownership applies, and the *migo* works.

### Tosafot Analysis

The distinction between items that are and are not normally lent raises a further question about methodology: does one determine the category of an object by reference to a generic statistical norm — do people in general lend out this type of item? — or does one examine the specific circumstances of the parties involved? A lawnmower may be commonly borrowed among neighbors generally, but what if the particular person claiming ownership is known never to lend his tools, or the particular neighbor is not the type who borrows? How much does the specific context matter?

*Tosafot*, in the final comment on the daf, engages this tension by referencing a *Gemara* in *perek kol hanishba'in* that handles a case where someone was seen removing vessels from beneath his garment and claimed he had purchased them. That *Gemara* limits the rule of non-belief to a *ba'al habayis she'eino asuy limkor keilav* — a householder who is not the type to sell his vessels — and further to vessels that are not normally concealed. These are concrete, situational factors layered on top of the generic category analysis. *Tosafot* thus indicates that one does not simply ask what type of object this is in the abstract, but also considers the specific parties and circumstances to assess whether the presumption of ownership holds.

## Violation & Legal Effect

### Return Reasons

*Abaye*'s ruling that the knife must be returned seems, on first reading, like a straightforward moral point: you violated a prohibition, so make it right and give it back. But *Tosafot* will not accept a purely moral reading. He identifies three distinct legal reasons why *Abaye* might have required the return, and carefully evaluates each.

### Mitzvah Rectification

The first possibility: the prohibition of taking *rechayim varechev* is *nitak la'aseh* — connected to a positive rectifying *mitzvah* of return. *Tosafot* sees this as one way to read *Abaye*'s ruling: since *Abaye* said to return it, perhaps there is a *mitzvas aseh* obligating that return, which is what constitutes *nitak la'aseh* and would also explain why lashes apply to the violation.

*Tosafot* raises this possibility by first asking how lashes can apply to taking *rechayim varechev* if it is *nitak la'aseh*, since a prohibition connected to a rectifying positive commandment ordinarily exempts from lashes. The answer would be that *Abaye*'s instruction to return implies precisely such a positive commandment.

However, *Tosafot* then rejects this reading. He points out that the *Torah* nowhere states an explicit positive commandment to return implements taken in violation of this prohibition. The case of *hashev tashiv et ha'avot k'vo hashemesh* — returning a garment taken as collateral at nightfall — involves an explicit *aseh* written in the same passage as the prohibition of entering the home to seize collateral. But taking *rechayim varechev* has no parallel explicit positive commandment of rectification attached to it. Therefore, one cannot read *Abaye*'s instruction as evidence of *nitak la'aseh*.

### Ei Avid Lo Mahani

The second possibility: even setting aside any positive commandment, the act of seizing the knife was simply not legally effective — because whenever the *Torah* says do not do something and one does it anyway, the act has no legal standing. This is the principle discussed in *perek kama* of *Temurah*: *ei avid lo mahani* — if one acted in violation, the act does not take effect.

If this principle applies here, then the creditor never acquired any rights over the knife, and *Abaye* required return simply because the seizure was null from the outset.

*Tosafot* rejects this interpretation as well — not on general grounds, but because *Abaye* himself, in *perek kama* of *Temurah*, holds the opposite: *ei avid mahani* — *Abaye*'s own position is that acts done in violation of a *Torah* prohibition are nevertheless legally effective. It would be contradictory to attribute to *Abaye* an instruction premised on the very principle he explicitly rejects.

### Error & Lack Effect

The third possibility — which *Tosafot* accepts — is more subtle. *Abaye*'s reason for requiring return is not a positive commandment and not that violation renders an act legally void. Rather: *keivan deHashta shogeg de'ei hava yada dehava assur lo hava avid* — since at the time of the seizure the man was acting unknowingly (*shogeg*), had he realized that seizing this implement was biblically prohibited he would never have seized it. Therefore, his act of taking possession was a *zechiya be'ta'ut* — an acquisition made in error — and does not confer legal rights.

The important implication *Tosafot* draws is that this reasoning is limited to the case of *shogeg*. If a creditor knowingly and deliberately seizes a prohibited implement — fully aware of the *issur* — then he does acquire legal rights through the seizure, because one cannot say he would not have acted had he known it was forbidden. *Tosafot* explicitly notes this: the unscrupulous lender who acts with full awareness is not helped by this third rationale.

This yields a clear and legally coherent account of *Abaye*'s ruling: not a moral admonition, not *nitak la'aseh*, not the general principle of *ei avid lo mahani*, but a targeted rule that an unknowing violator's seizure is an act of acquisition done in error and therefore carries no legal force.

## Collapsed Building

### Mishnah Setup

The *Gemara* now begins the final chapter of *Bava Metzia*, which, as the lecturer notes, serves in many respects as a bridge into the opening themes of *Bava Batra*: shared ownership of property, division of jointly held assets, and the allocation of rights when claims are uncertain. The chapter opens with the *Mishnah*: *habbayis v'ha'aliyah shel shnayim* — a house and an upper story owned by two different people, one owning the ground floor and one owning the upper story. They collapse. *Shneihem cholkim b'etzim u'v'avanim u'v'afar* — both parties divide equally the wood, stones, and plaster-grade earth that constitute the rubble.

### Proportional Division

*Rashi* clarifies that the division is not a simple fifty-fifty split but is proportional to the amount of building material that originally constituted each level. If the ground floor accounted for two-thirds of the total building material and the upper story accounted for one-third, the rubble is divided in those proportions. The two parties share not just in quantity but in quality — they take a proportional mix of whole stones and broken stones.

### Damage Assessment

The *Mishnah* also directs: *v'roim elu avanim hara'uyot lishaber* — one assesses which stones were more likely to have broken and attributes them accordingly. The *Gemara* elaborates on this assessment with two scenarios of collapse.

If the building fell from external impact — *chavta* — such that the upper structure came crashing down and spread outward, then the stones found at greater distance from the foundation belong to the upper story and are the ones most likely to be broken, having traveled the furthest and struck with the greatest force. The stones closest to the foundation, belonging to the lower story, are more likely to be whole.

If the building collapsed from internal pressure or foundation failure — *chafsa* — then the structure fell more or less in place, and the pile of rubble reflects the original vertical arrangement: the material from the upper story lies on top of the rubble heap and the material from the lower story lies beneath. In this case, *elayata itbar* — the upper story material is the more broken — or conversely, if the foundation shattered, *tata'ata itbar*, the lower story material bears the greater damage.

The *Mishnah*'s directive to divide equally applies precisely when such an assessment is impossible. The *Gemara* asks: given that one can often determine the manner of collapse, when would assessment be impossible?

### Assessment Impossible

The *Gemara* answers: when the building fell at night and no one witnessed it. One might think that the rubble itself, examined in the morning, would reveal how it fell — the spread and pattern of the debris. But the *Gemara* posits a case where the rubble was cleared away before daylight by passersby from the public domain, acting collectively, without any of them paying close attention to the pattern. Those individuals then dispersed, leaving no witnesses available to describe what the scene looked like.

The *Gemara*'s extended effort to construct a scenario of true epistemic impossibility is whimsical in tone, but the legal point is real: the *Mishnah*'s proportional equal division is the fallback rule for when no assessment can be made.

## Chazaka & Joint Ownership

### Location & Ownership

The *Gemara* raises a sharper question. When the rubble has settled, why not simply ask where the stones are physically located? If all the stones are sitting in the front yard, which is legally mine, I should be *muchzak* — the presumed possessor — and the other party, as *hamotzi mechavero*, bears the burden of proof.

### Shared Yard Rights

The *Gemara* dismisses this: the case posits that the rubble fell into a yard jointly owned by both parties, or into the public domain. But the *Gemara* adds a third scenario that is conceptually the most striking: *shutfim bechai hai gavna lo kapdi ahadadi* — in a partnership of this kind, the parties do not stand on strict legal rights against each other. Suppose the ground floor and its yard are legally mine, but you as the tenant of the upper story walk through that yard every day on your way home, and I allow your children to play there and permit you to set out a lawn chair. Technically the yard is mine. But since practically you have an equal presence and I have effectively granted you equal practical use, neither of us is considered more *muchzak* than the other when the rubble falls there. Legal title does not automatically produce *chazaka* when the practical reality is one of equal access and use.

This is a significant *chiddush*: *muchzak* is not purely a matter of legal ownership but reflects actual practical control. Where practical presence is equal — even if legal title is not — neither party can invoke *chazaka* against the other.

### Knowledge Chazaka

The *Gemara* then moves to a still more abstract application of *chazaka*. The *Mishnah* states that if one party recognizes some of his stones, *notlan* — he takes them — and *va'olu lo min hacheshbon*, they are deducted from his share. The *Gemara* asks: what is the other party claiming? If he concedes that the stones belong to the recognizing party, the ruling is obvious. If he contests it, why does the recognizing party win at all? We just established that neither party is *muchzak* with respect to the rubble.

The *Gemara* answers: the case is one where the other party says *eini yodea* — I don't know. And the question then becomes whether this is a refutation of *Rav Nachman*, who holds that *bari* (a definite claim) does not override *chazaka* in favor of the claimant when the respondent says *shema* (I'm uncertain) — the money stays with the *muchzak*. If neither party is *muchzak*, why shouldn't *bari* simply win?

*Tosafot* takes up this question directly. He acknowledges that *Rav Nachman*'s reason for not allowing *bari* to prevail is precisely the *chazaka* in favor of the respondent. Here, where the rubble is in neither party's yard or in equally shared space, there seems to be no *chazaka* for *bari* to overcome.

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* resolves the difficulty with a conceptual reformulation: *mi-kol makom be-hachi chashiv muchzak she-anu yodim she-chatzi shelo* — nevertheless, each party is considered *muchzak* because we know that half of all the rubble belongs to each of them. This is a *chazaka* grounded not in physical possession of any particular stone but in knowledge of equal ownership of the whole. When I assert that a specific stone is mine, I am effectively trying to extract that stone from your share of a pile of which you are a known fifty-percent owner. Even in a shared yard or public domain, each stone in the pile is presumptively half yours — and my *bari* claim regarding a particular stone is, structurally, an attempt to take something out of your *chazaka*. *Rav Nachman*'s principle therefore applies even here: *bari* alone cannot override this *chazaka* grounded in knowledge of equal ownership.

*Tosafot* finds support for this formulation in a passage from *perek ha-sho'el*: *kol mamon ha-muttal be-safek chashiv ke-ilu hem muchzakim bah* — any property that is in doubt is treated as though both claimants are *muchzakim* in it. This echoes the logic of the opening *perek* of the tractate, *shnayim ochazin be-talit*, where two parties jointly holding an object are each considered *muchzak* in half.

## Stone Recognition

### Shevua Solution

If *Rav Nachman*'s principle blocks the recognizing party from simply taking the stones on the strength of a *bari* claim against an *eini yodea*, how does the *Mishnah*'s ruling that he *does* take the stones stand? The *Gemara* resolves this by analogy to *Rav Nachman*'s own mechanism in a parallel case: *keivan she-yesh esek shevua beineihem* — there is an oath-obligation between the parties. In the paradigm case established by *Rabba*: I claim you owe me a hundred dollars; you admit to fifty but say you don't know about the other fifty. The partial admission creates an obligation of *shevua*, but since you cannot swear about what you don't know, *mitoch she-eino yachol lishava meshalem* — your inability to take the oath requires you to pay. The *bari* party wins not because *bari* overrides *chazaka* directly, but because the respondent's *shema* generates a *shevua* he cannot discharge.

The same applies here: the respondent's *eini yodea* — with respect to stones he has partially admitted are the other's — creates an oath-obligation that cannot be fulfilled, and the recognizing party therefore prevails through that mechanism, not through the raw force of *bari* over *shema*.

### Deduction Method

The *Mishnah*'s instruction that the recognized stones *va'olu lo min hacheshbon* — count toward the recognizing party's share — follows naturally. If the ground-floor owner is entitled to two-thirds of the rubble and he identifies and takes a certain stone, that stone is counted within his two-thirds, not in addition to it.

### Rava's Position

The *Gemara* then turns to a subtler question: when the recognizing party takes his stones and a corresponding weight of rubble is allocated to the other party, what quality of stones does the other party receive?

*Rava* holds: *lefi cheshbon shevuros* — the other party receives a proportional share of whatever remains, which will include broken stones. The recognizing party identified and extracted specific whole stones as definitively his; the other party gets the corresponding weight from the remaining mixture of whole and broken material. The recognizing party's knowledge gives him the advantage of selecting known whole stones; the other party, having said *eini yodea*, is in a disadvantaged position and receives the mix.

### Abaye Quality Variance

*Abaye* disagrees, and interestingly frames the matter in the recognizing party's disadvantage. He argues: *mi-de-hani yadai ve-tfei lo yadai* — the recognizing party knows that these particular stones are his, but does not know the others. This could be read as an implicit concession that the remaining whole stones belong to the other party. Taken to its extreme, one might say the recognizing party is signaling by his act of recognition that everything else is not his. *Abaye* ultimately rejects that extreme — it would perversely discourage honest identification — but instead arrives at a symmetrical ruling: *lefi cheshbon shelmos* — the other party receives an equivalent weight of whole stones. If I take whole stones that I recognize as mine, you receive whole stones from the remaining pile. The distribution of whole stones remains proportionally equal; I simply identify which specific whole stones are mine from within my share.

The *Gemara* then asks: if both parties receive an equal share of whole stones in any case, what practical difference does recognition make? The answer is *lemivna ravcha* — perhaps one stone is larger or wider — or *tina de-ma'avda* — perhaps a particular stone has superior plaster. Within the category of whole stones, quality varies, and recognition allows the party to select the superior specimens within his share. The ultimate disposition remains equitable in quantity; recognition affects quality within that quantity.

This resolution also diffuses the *bari v'shema* problem: the recognizing party does not actually extract a greater *monetary* value from the other's *chazaka* — both still receive equal shares of whole stones — but simply identifies, from within the whole-stone allotment, which particular ones are definitively his. The assertion of *bari* in this context is of relatively modest legal consequence, and does not constitute the kind of taking-from-the-other's-pocket that *Rav Nachman*'s principle was designed to prevent.

## Upper Story Rental

### Tenancy Setup

The final *Mishnah* of the tractate shifts from divided ownership to a rental situation, as *Rashi* makes clear. One party owns the entire building — both the ground floor and the upper story — and has rented the upper story to a tenant. *Nifcheta ha-aliyah* — the upper story is damaged, specifically its floor. The landlord refuses to repair it. The *Mishnah* rules: *harei ba'al ha-aliyah yored ve-dar lemata ad she-yitaken lo et ha-aliyah* — the upper-story tenant descends and lives downstairs in the landlord's space until the repair is made. The incentive structure is elegant: refuse to fix the floor, and you share your apartment.

### Yossi Division

*Rabbi Yossi* introduces a refinement regarding the allocation of repair responsibilities between the two parties: *hatachton noten et hatikra vehaelyon et hamaziva* — the lower-story owner (landlord) provides the structural beams (*tikra*), while the upper-story tenant provides the plaster (*maziva*). The floor between the two units is simultaneously the ceiling of the lower story and the floor of the upper story, and the *Mishnah* divides responsibility along structural lines: the landlord bears responsibility for the load-bearing beams; the tenant bears responsibility for the plaster finish of his floor. This parallels modern co-op distinctions between what lies within the walls (owner's responsibility) and the structural elements themselves (building's responsibility).

### Damage Threshold

How significant must the damage be before the tenant has the right to move downstairs? Even minor damage may create an obligation to repair, but what is the threshold of uninhabitability that entitles the tenant to exercise the right of moving into the landlord's space?

### Rava vs. Abaye

*Rava* holds: *beruba* — a majority of the floor must be compromised. Until then, the tenant must make do. *Abaye* (and *Shmuel*) hold: *bearbaa* — four *tefachim* suffices. Four *tefachim* is a very small measurement — roughly a foot by a foot — and one might think such a small breach is tolerable. *Rava* says it is: *adam dar chetzyo lemata vechetzyo lemala* — a person can live partly upstairs and partly downstairs. If a foot of floor is damaged, one can leave that space unused or store items downstairs. *Abaye* and *Shmuel* disagree: *arbaa* already constitutes a usable space, and if I cannot use it upstairs and must use it downstairs, that is an unreasonable imposition. The arrangement of living split between two floors is not a tolerable condition.

### Explicit Conditions

The *Gemara* examines when the obligation to provide alternate lodging applies. If the landlord said explicitly *aliyah zu* — I am renting you this specific upper story — then *azda* — what's done is done. If it becomes uninhabitable through no one's fault, the tenant's misfortune (*mazlacha gram*) is the tenant's problem. The *Gemara* notes the underlying premise: without a specific stipulation, if something happens to the specific rented item, it falls on the renter. This applies even when neither party is at fault — say, a tree fell in a storm and damaged the roof — and is indeed a surprising principle. The landlord need not find alternative lodging.

If, on the other hand, the landlord said simply *aliyah stam* — I am renting you an upper story, generically — then the tenant's right is to a habitable upper story, not to this particular one in its damaged condition. In that case, the landlord should provide another upper story elsewhere. But then why does the *Mishnah* speak of moving downstairs rather than renting an alternative elsewhere?

### Rav Ashi Rule

*Rav Ashi* provides the operative reading of the *Mishnah*: *de-amar lei aliyah zu she-al gabi bayis zeh ani maskir lecha* — I am renting you this upper story that sits atop this house. By explicitly naming the house below, the landlord has implicitly subjected the house to the upper story: *de-ashabeid bayis l'aliyah* — the house is encumbered in favor of the upper-story tenant. The tenant does not merely have rights to an abstract unit; he has rights tied to this house, which is obligated to support his habitability. If the upper story fails, the house below is where he belongs until it is fixed.

*Rav Ashi* supports this by citing a ruling of *Rabbi Chiyya*: a person sold a vine growing around a particular tree. The tree was subsequently uprooted. *Rabbi Chiyya* ruled that the seller must replant the tree for as long as the vine exists, because by describing the vine as growing around *this tree*, he implicitly guaranteed that the tree — the support structure — would continue to exist. The principle is the same: mentioning a supporting structure in the terms of a transaction creates an obligation regarding that structure.

### Tosafot Conclusion

*Tosafot* draws the practical conclusion regarding the *stam* case — a generic rental without mention of the house below. If the upper story collapses and no special language was used: *ei de-amar lei aliyah zu ve-nafla azda leh* — it's simply gone. *Ve-yiten lo secharo she-kevar dar* — the landlord refunds the rent for the period the tenant was unable to live there, proportionally. If the tenant lived there for half the rental period before the collapse, he pays half the rent and owes nothing more. But equally, the landlord owes him nothing more: no alternative lodging, no replacement unit. The tenant exits the lease, the landlord refunds the unused portion of rent, and the matter is closed.

This outcome is equitable in a narrow sense — the tenant does not pay for what he cannot use — but it also means that the landlord bears no obligation to find alternate accommodations. Unless the language of the rental agreement explicitly encumbers the supporting structure, a collapsed rental leaves the tenant without remedy beyond the rent refund.
