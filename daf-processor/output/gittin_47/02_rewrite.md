# Gittin 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Self-Sale to Non-Jews

### Mishna Intro

The daf opens near the bottom of 46b with the *mishna*: *hamocher atzmo u'vanav l'ovdei kochavim* — one who sells himself and his children to non-Jews. The broader sugya has been preoccupied with slaves, freeing slaves, and various forms of *tikkun olam*, and the theme of redeeming captives has run throughout. Now those threads converge: a Jew, presumably driven by severe poverty, sells himself — and his children — into some form of servitude to non-Jews. Whether this is outright slavery or a form of indentured servitude, the person cannot simply leave; he must be redeemed by others paying to release him.

### Logic of Prohibition

The *mishna*'s ruling is stark: *ein podin oto* — we do not redeem him. This seems harsh, but the logic is coherent. Unlike the case of captives seized by enemies — where redeeming them risks encouraging future raids — here there is only one behavior we wish to discourage: voluntarily selling oneself. If a person knows the community will feel compelled to redeem him through *pidyon shvuyim*, the decision to sell himself becomes far easier to make. Removing that safety net imposes real consequences on the choice.

One might protest that the result is terrible: the person will violate *Shabbat*, eat *treifot*, and be cut off from Jewish communal life. But *tikkun olam* is not always a gentle enterprise. Sometimes it means accepting the immediate suffering of individuals in order to prevent a deeper, systemic deterioration. A comparable logic appears in the *gemara* about *Shema*: some hold that if one recited *Shema* without *kavana*, one does not repeat it — not because repetition is categorically forbidden, but because the permission to repeat would make lack of *kavana* far more common. Hard rules sometimes prevent worse outcomes.

One might further ask whether the community bears some responsibility for allowing a member to reach such a state of poverty. This is a genuine question, and in cases where the community's own negligence contributed to someone's predicament, there may be a stronger immediate obligation to act. The case is not unlike contemporary situations where individuals travel to dangerous places against advice and are subsequently seized — the degree of personal responsibility affects how strongly the obligation of rescue falls on others. Here, however, the concern is not a one-time lapse of judgment but a broader societal problem: if people know they will be redeemed, they will keep selling themselves, and the community will be perpetually burdened by rescuing people from consequences of their own choices.

### Children Redeemed

The *mishna* continues: *aval podin et habanim le'achar mitat avihem* — but we do redeem the children after the father's death. The children did not choose this situation; they were brought into it. Still, the question arises: why wait until the father dies? One reading in the *gemara* is that as long as the father lives, he can at least protect them to some degree. Another reading preserves the deterrent logic: even if we will eventually redeem the children, knowing that it will not happen in your own lifetime makes the decision to sell them that much more consequential and harder to rationalize.

### Rav Assi Limit

The *gemara* immediately softens the ruling. *Amar Rav Assi: v'hu shemachar v'shana v'shilesh* — this applies only to one who sold himself once, twice, and three times. Only after the third instance, establishing a *chazakah* of repeated behavior, does the community withhold redemption. The push-and-pull is striking: the *mishna* states the rule in its starkest form, and the *gemara* at once pulls it back, confining the harshness to truly habitual offenders.

## Debt Seizure Case

### Michsi Story

The *gemara* now presents a case. There were people from the city of *Michsi* who had borrowed money from non-Jews and could not repay them. The creditors came and seized the borrowers themselves as human collateral — effectively enslaving them as payment of the debt. These people retained enough freedom of movement to come before Rav Huna and ask the community to redeem them.

### Rav Huna Refuses

Rav Huna responded: *mai a'avid lachem* — what can I do for you? He cited the *mishna*'s rule: *hamocher atzmo u'vanav l'ovdei kochavim ein podin oto*. The case is not identical to voluntary sale — being seized as debt collateral involves at least some degree of negligence rather than pure intention — but Rav Huna treated it as falling within the *mishna*'s scope. One might note the distinction: these people were not deliberately selling themselves; they simply borrowed money they could not repay, and if there was culpability it was more negligence than design. Then again, if one borrows from creditors who are known to seize people as debt payment, that negligence carries its own moral weight.

### Rav Abba Objects

Rav Abba challenged Rav Huna directly: *limadtenu Rabbeinu* — but our Master, you yourself taught us *v'hu shemachar v'shana v'shilesh*, that the rule applies only to one who has done this repeatedly. Why are you applying the *mishna*'s blanket prohibition here?

### Habitual Behavior

Rav Huna replied: *hani marga regili d'avdi hachi* — these people are accustomed to doing this. They have a pattern. Whether this means they had been seized by creditors two or three times previously, or simply that their pattern of borrowing beyond their means from such creditors had become habitual, is not entirely resolved by the *gemara*. If they had been physically seized and redeemed multiple times already, the case is merely an illustration of the *Rav Assi* limit. But perhaps the pattern of negligent borrowing itself — even if it had not previously resulted in seizure — was sufficient to classify them as *regila* in this behavior and thus withold redemption. The *gemara* leaves this somewhat open.

## Ludian Gladiators

### Who Are Ludians

Another case. *Hahu gavra d'zavin nafshei l'Luda'ei* — a certain person sold himself to the *Luda'ei*. Who are these *Luda'ei*? Rashi identifies them as *umah she'ochlin benei adam* — a nation that eats human beings, i.e., cannibals. The Soncino translation, drawing on Krauss and Gretz, identifies them as *Ludi* — those who organize gladiatorial contests, specifically the men hired to kill off enraged beasts in the Roman games, or to deliver the finishing stroke. Jastrow similarly associates the term with those involved in Roman games, and notes an additional identification with a group that practiced human sacrifice in their religion. In any case, it is clear that one's life is in acute danger in the hands of the *Luda'ei*.

### Rabbi Ami Rules

People who had sold themselves to the *Luda'ei* came before Rabbi Ami and said: *pirkin* — redeem us. Rabbi Ami responded initially by citing the *mishna*: *hamocher et atzmo v'et banav l'Ludim ein podin oto aval podin et habanim* — we don't redeem him, but we do redeem the children. His reasoning for why we redeem the children is telling. Rather than saying simply that the children are not at fault, he frames it in terms of *kilkula* — the risk of moral or personal deterioration, including the possibility of abuse, when the father is no longer alive to protect them. When there is a heightened risk to the person's safety, we redeem regardless.

From this he argues *kol shekein*: all the more so in the present case, where there is *katla* — the threat of actual killing. Whether we are speaking of gladiatorial combat, human sacrifice, or cannibalism, these people's lives are in immediate danger. The obligation to redeem is overwhelming.

### Mumarim Objection

The rabbis objected to Rabbi Ami: *v'hai Yisrael mumar hu d'chazi lei d'kachil neveilot u'treifot* — this Jew is a *mumar*, an apostate, because we have seen that he eats *neveilot* and *treifot*. We have no obligation toward such a person. The context raises the obvious question of where and when they were observed eating *treifot* — presumably while in the hands of the *Luda'ei*, where their food options were not their own to determine.

### Appetite vs Apostasy

Rabbi Ami responded: *eima l'teiavon hu d'kachil* — perhaps they eat it out of appetite, out of the pressure of circumstance, not out of genuine rejection of Jewish identity. Being given meat and eating it rather than subsisting on vegetables — especially in an era when vegetarianism was not a realistic option — does not make one a *mumar*. It reflects *l'teiavon*, eating because the food is there and one is not pious enough to refuse it under such extreme conditions. That is very different from *mumar l'hachis*, actively choosing transgression as a statement of defiance.

### Gemara Counter

The rabbis pushed back: *v'zimnin d'ika heteira v'issura kamei v'shavik heteira v'achil issura* — there are occasions when both kosher and non-kosher food are placed before them simultaneously, and they abandon the kosher food and eat the non-kosher. This is not appetite-driven eating under constraint; this is a deliberate choice. The scenario the *gemara* envisions is not choosing ham over beef for reasons of taste — it is being presented with two identical cuts of meat, one properly slaughtered and one not, and choosing the *treifa* when the kosher option is equally available. That, the *gemara* suggests, is *mumar l'hachis* — or at minimum a category of complete indifference to *mitzvot* that places one outside communal obligation. It is not that one actively seeks to spite God; one simply no longer feels bound by *kashrut* at all. In the period of the *gemara*, that level of indifference — choosing an *aveirah* when the *mitzvah* is just as easy — was sufficient to classify someone as *chutz l'kahal*.

### Rabbi Ami Surrenders

Rabbi Ami's response is plaintive: *zil lo shavku li d'afrokinach* — go, they are not letting me redeem you. The other rabbis have made it halachically impossible for him to act, even though he clearly wishes to. The *gemara* does not record him invoking *pikuach nefesh* as an override — perhaps because the categorization of such a person as approaching the status of a *min* removes even that obligation in this context, given other *gemarot* that treat a *min* differently with respect to life-saving. The story ends on a somber note.

## Reish Lakish Story

### RL Context

The *gemara* now introduces a famous biographical episode. *Reish Lakish zavin nafshei l'Luda'ei* — Reish Lakish sold himself to the *Luda'ei*. Tosafot is quick to clarify: *kodem shechazar l'mutav* — this was before he became a *frum* Jew, in his earlier days as a bandit. Once he had joined the rabbinic class, one cannot imagine him selling himself to gladiators. This was the younger, rougher Reish Lakish.

### Preparation

Reish Lakish came prepared. *Shakal bahadei chiyusa v'gilgla* — he took with him a sack and a stone inside the sack. He knew — *gmiri*, it is a known tradition — that on the final day, *yoma batra*, the day appointed for one's death in the arena, the *Ludim* would grant any last request. Why? *Ki heichi de'leichlu adamei* — so that his blood would not be on their hands, so they could say they had been generous to him at the end, that they bore him no ill will. This custom of a final request was his opportunity.

### Final Request

On his last day, they asked him: *mai nicha lach* — what would you like? He answered: *be'ina akmitinchu* — I want to bind you all, *v'osvinchu* — and have you sit down, *v'chol chad minaikhu amchei chiyusa u'palga* — and I want to strike each of you one and a half times with my sack. His captors agreed readily. What harm could it do? This is a man who wants to feel powerful for a moment before he dies — let him slap us around. They did not reckon with the stone inside the sack.

### Execution

*Kamtinehu v'osvinhu* — he bound them and had them sit down. Then, *kad machyei chad chiyusa* — when he struck each one with the sack, *nafak nishmatei* — either they died outright or were rendered unconscious, stunned or paralyzed. Soncino translates this as fainting; either way, they were incapacitated and could not reverse the situation since they were already bound.

One of them, however, gritted his teeth at Reish Lakish. He demanded: *achucha kamachaychat bi* — are you laughing at me? And he announced: *akati pash lach gabai palga dechiyusa* — I still have half a strike coming to you. And with that, *katlinhu kulhu* — whether he had merely stunned them initially, his rage at what he perceived as mockery led him to finish the job and kill them all.

### Aftermath

*Nafak v'ata yasiv ke'achel veshasei* — he escaped, returned home, and sat down to eat and drink. His daughter asked him: *lo ba'yat midi lemizgal lei* — don't you need something to recline upon? He replied: *bitti, kareisi kari* — my daughter, my stomach is my pillow. (Other stories in the *gemara* similarly describe Reish Lakish as a man of considerable physical bulk.)

### Self-Quotation

When Reish Lakish died, *shavak kava demurika* — all he left behind was a measure of saffron. It should be noted that saffron is extraordinarily valuable, more so by weight than gold even in antiquity. He applied to himself the verse from Tehillim: *ki yireh chachamim yamutu yachad kesil uva'ar yovedu ve'azvu le'acherim cheilam* — for one sees that even the wise die, that the fool and the brute alike perish and leave their wealth to others. The *pasuk* is recited at a *beit avel* and carries a tone of futility: what good does accumulated wealth do you when you die and others inherit it? In context, Reish Lakish seems to be reflecting on the lesson of his life — eat, drink, live fully now, and don't accumulate wealth for the benefit of others. Whether the measure of saffron was all he had to his name — consistent with the portrait of a man who spent freely and saved nothing — or whether it was itself a treasure, the *pasuk* he chose speaks to the philosophy of a man who never hoarded.

## Land to Non-Jews

### Mishna Land Sale

The *gemara* now pivots from the sale of persons to the sale of land. The *mishna*: *hamocher et sadehu l'ovdei kochavim* — one who sells his field to non-Jews. We are speaking of a field in *Eretz Yisrael*. The concern is twofold: there is the *mitzvah* dimension — selling to a non-Jew threatens to remove the land from its obligations of *terumot u'ma'asrot* — and there is the societal dimension, the concern for maintaining a Jewish presence in the land. Masechet Avodah Zarah makes clear that selling land, slaves, and livestock to non-Jews is deeply disfavored precisely because those are the elements that establish the Jewish character of the land.

### Two Readings

*Lokeiach u'meivi mimenu bikkurim mipnei tikkun ha'olam* — the seller is to purchase and bring *bikkurim* from it, for the sake of *tikkun ha'olam*. There are at least two ways to read this *mishna*. Rashi reads *lokeiach* as part of the *takana* itself: we require the seller to go back each year and purchase the first fruits from the non-Jew so that he can bring them as *bikkurim*. The repurchase is mandatory. The alternative reading is *ha'lokeiach meivi mimenu bikkurim*: there is no obligation to purchase, but if you did purchase the fruit from the non-Jew, you must still treat it as carrying a *bikkurim* obligation. The *tikkun ha'olam* consists in maintaining the land's *kedusha* by ensuring *bikkurim* continues to be brought from it, not necessarily in compelling a buyback.

### Rava Position

The *gemara* now enters one of the central debates of *Shas*. Rava states: *af al pi she'ein kinyan l'oved kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael l'hafki'a mi'yedei ma'aser* — even though a non-Jew owns land in *Eretz Yisrael*, this does not alter the land's *kedusha* status. Grain grown in that field remains obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, even if the non-Jew will not in practice separate them. The source: *ki li ha'aretz* — for the land is Mine. God's *kedusha* inheres in the land regardless of who holds legal title. *Li kedushat ha'aretz* — the sanctity of the land remains His.

Yet at the same time, Rava holds, *yesh kinyan l'oved kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael lachpor bah borot shichin u'me'arot* — a non-Jew does acquire full property rights in *Eretz Yisrael* for purposes of digging pits, cisterns, and caves, i.e., making structural changes to the land. He has absolute *dinei mamonot* ownership. The source: *hashamayim shamayim l'Hashem v'ha'aretz natan livnei adam* — the heavens belong to God, but the earth He gave to human beings. Ownership as a legal matter is fully transferable; *kedusha*, however, is not.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer holds the precise inverse. *Af al pi she'yesh kinyan l'ovdei kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael l'hafki'a mi'yedei ma'aser* — non-Jewish ownership does affect the land's status, and grain grown while the land is in a non-Jew's possession is exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot*. The source: *degancha v'lo degan ovdei kochavim* — your grain, not the grain of non-Jews. But Rabbi Eliezer simultaneously holds that *ein kinyan l'ovdei kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael lachpor borot shichin u'me'arot* — a non-Jew cannot make structural changes to the land, because the land belongs to God and therefore a non-Jew never acquires full title. His *kinyan* is more like a *kinyan peirot* — a right of use — without the deeper property ownership. The source for this limitation: *l'Hashem ha'aretz*.

### Conceptual Framework

An important question lurks beneath Rabbi Eliezer's position. When non-Jewish ownership exempts the grain from *terumot u'ma'asrot*, does this reflect a fundamental claim that the land loses its *kedusha* under non-Jewish ownership? Or is it a more technical point: the *pasuk* defines *chiyuv* in terms of *degancha*, your grain, and if the grain grew while owned by a non-Jew, it fails that condition — just as grain that grew while the field was *hefker* or *hekdesh* might similarly be exempt for technical reasons without implying any diminishment of the land's metaphysical holiness?

The same ambiguity applies by analogy to *hefker* and *hekdesh*: if grain grew when the field was declared ownerless or consecrated, it may be exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot*, but no one claims those categories remove the land's *kedusha*. So the exemption under non-Jewish ownership need not be conceptualized as a loss of *kedusha* — it could simply be a technical matter of who holds ownership at the moment of critical halachic obligation. The *gemara* will continue to press this question.

## TuM Debate

### Rava's Proof

The *gemara* attempts to identify the pivot of the dispute and then to marshal proof. *B'mai kamifligi?* What is at stake between the two positions? One position learns from *degancha v'lo degan ovdei kochavim* that the critical moment is when the grain reaches *shlish*, one-third ripening. At that point, if a non-Jew owns the field, the grain is exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot* for the entire subsequent growth, regardless of who later owns it. The other position reads *degancha* as referring not to the moment of *shlish* but to the moment of *digun* — the smoothing and piling of the winnowed grain, the *miru'ach* — which is the final critical stage, after which the grain is fully *tevel* and eating from it without separating *terumot u'ma'asrot* carries severe consequences. On this reading, what matters is not who owned the field when it became a third ripe, but who performed the final smoothing-out. If a non-Jew did the *digun*, the grain is exempt; otherwise, it is not. Under this view, the grain could grow and ripen entirely in a non-Jew's hands, but if a Jew does the final *miru'ach*, it would still be fully obligated.

With this framework established, Rava declares: *mina amina lah* — where do I derive my position? From a *mishna*: *leket shikcha u'pe'ah shel oved kochavim chayavim b'ma'aser ela im ken hefkir* — if a non-Jew leaves *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah* in his field, the grain is still obligated in *ma'aser*, unless he truly declared it *hefker*.

### Two Interpretations

The *gemara* immediately asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? There are two possible readings. The first: the field belongs to a Jew, and the non-Jew is the impoverished collector — the *ani* — who gathers the *leket shikcha u'pe'ah* left by the Jewish owner. But the *gemara* challenges this reading: the Jew has already been *mafkir* the grain by leaving it as *pe'ah*; the grain is already *hefker*. Why should the fact that a non-Jew picked it up make it obligated in *ma'aser*? And what would it mean to say the Jew can be *mafkir* it again? He already was *mafkir* it. This reading collapses.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela lav*, the case must be that the non-Jew owns the field and leaves *leket shikcha u'pe'ah*, and the Jew comes and collects it. Now the *mishna*'s logic becomes clear: the non-Jew declared what he thought was *pe'ah*, but halachically, grain growing in a non-Jew's field does not have the status of *pe'ah* — there is no *mitzvah* of *pe'ah* incumbent upon a non-Jew. So when the non-Jew left the corners of his field unharvested and invited poor Jews to collect, he was not in fact rendering the grain *hefker* through the mechanism of *pe'ah*, because *pe'ah* by a non-Jew carries no such legal effect. The grain remained in his ownership and was transferred to the Jew as something closer to a *matana* — a gift. Since it was never truly *hefker*, it retains its obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot*. And the *mishna* says it is only exempt if the non-Jew explicitly declared full *hefker* — renouncing ownership entirely to anyone, Jew or non-Jew alike. This would support Rava's position that non-Jewish ownership does not remove the land's obligation.

### Intent Reading

At this point the *gemara* considers a more nuanced reading of the case where the field belongs to a Jew and the non-Jew is the collector. On this reading, when the Jewish farmer left *pe'ah*, he declared it *hefker* with a certain intent: *adaita d'Yisrael* — specifically with the understanding that Jews would come to collect it. The *pe'ah* was meant for Jewish *aniyim*. When a non-Jew comes and takes it, the *hefker* did not apply to him — he was not within the class of recipients for whom the *hefker* was declared. Therefore, vis-à-vis the non-Jew, the grain was never truly *hefker*; it is as if he took the Jew's property, and if he now sells it to a Jew, that Jew must separate *terumot u'ma'asrot* because the grain was *tevel* all along.

The *mishna*'s caveat — *ela im ken hefkir*, unless he declared *hefker* — means: unless the Jew explicitly declared unconditional *hefker*, open to all comers regardless of identity. In that case, even if a non-Jew takes it, it genuinely was *hefker* for him as well, and the grain is exempt. But a *pe'ah* declaration, which by its nature is directed toward the Jewish poor, does not accomplish universal *hefker*.

This is connected to a broader question about the *halacha* of *pe'ah* vis-à-vis non-Jews. The *pasuk* of *v'achlu evyonei amecha* — the poor of your people shall eat — and the narrative in Ruth where *Boaz* arranges for Ruth to glean despite her being a foreigner (*l'hakireni v'anochi nochriya*) raise the question of whether a *ger toshav* or an outright *nochri* is legally entitled to *leket shikcha u'pe'ah*, or whether the *halacha* is that *pe'ah* goes to Jews and non-Jews collect only by way of *darkhei shalom*. If the latter, then the non-Jew's taking of *pe'ah* is not grounded in *hefker* regardless of the farmer's subjective intent — the *halacha* itself defines the class of recipients, and vis-à-vis anyone outside that class the grain is not *hefker*.

The practical consequence: if a non-Jew collected what a Jew left as *pe'ah* and ate it himself, nothing further is required — it is as if some of the farmer's grain was lost. But if that non-Jew sells the grain in the marketplace and a Jew purchases it, the Jew must separate *terumot u'ma'asrot*, because the grain was *tevel* when the non-Jew acquired it and remains so.

### Suria Limitation

Having explored these readings, the *gemara* then attempts to use other *mishnayot* as proof for Rabbi Eliezer's position that non-Jewish ownership does exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot*. One such source: a Jew who bought a field from a non-Jew before the grain reached *shlish*, then sold it back after *shlish* — the grain is obligated because it hit the critical moment of *shlish* in Jewish hands. The implication is clear: had it reached *shlish* while still in the non-Jew's possession, it would be exempt.

The *gemara* deflects: *hacha b'mai askinan b'Suryia* — we are not speaking of *Eretz Yisrael* proper, but of *Suria*, the lands conquered by King David. Those lands carry only *kedusha mi'derabbanan* — rabbinic-level holiness — because *kibush yachid lo shmei kibush*, the conquest by an individual monarch without the full national participation that characterized the original conquest of Canaan does not confer the same *kedusha d'Oraita* as *Eretz Yisrael* proper. In *Suria*, therefore, non-Jewish ownership does exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot* even under Rava's framework, because the *kedusha* there is rabbinic and susceptible to such qualification.

### Mixed Ownership

A further proof is attempted from the case of a Jew and non-Jew who own a field jointly, *b'shutafut*. Rebbi holds: *tevel v'chullin me'uravim zeh ba'zeh* — each kernel of grain is simultaneously half *tevel* (obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*) and half *chullin* (exempt), since each owner holds a half-share. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: the grain that the Jew takes home is fully *chayav*, and the grain the non-Jew takes home is fully exempt — based on the principle of *yeish breirah*, the retroactive clarification that what each party takes at division was always theirs from the moment of growth.

The *gemara* notes: *aval di'chulei alma yesh kinyan l'oved kochavim b'Eretz Yisrael l'hafki'a mi'yad ma'aser* — regardless of the dispute about *yeish breirah*, it is taken for granted by all parties that non-Jewish ownership exempts from *terumot u'ma'asrot*. This is strong proof for Rabbi Eliezer's position. The *gemara* again deflects: *hacha nami b'Suryia v'kasavar kibush yachid lo shmei kibush*. Any case that seems to prove non-Jewish ownership exempts from *terumot u'ma'asrot* can be limited to *Suria*, where such exemption is uncontested. This retreat to *Suria* is the *gemara*'s consistent escape hatch — though it should be noted that *pshat* strongly favors the exemption as applying in *Eretz Yisrael* proper as well.

## Takkanot Reversal

### Original Obligation

Rav Chiya bar Avin now invokes our *mishna* itself as evidence: *hamocher sadeh l'oved kochavim lokeiach u'meivi bikkurim mipnei tikkun ha'olam* — one who sells his field to a non-Jew purchases and brings *bikkurim* from it because of *tikkun ha'olam*. The language *mipnei tikkun ha'olam* implies that *me'ikar ha'din* — as a matter of strict *d'Oraita* law — there would be no obligation to bring *bikkurim*. The *tikkun ha'olam* is what creates or restores the obligation. This supports the view that non-Jewish ownership does in principle exempt from such obligations, and the Rabbis are overriding that exemption through *tikkun ha'olam*.

The *gemara* accepts this framing: *mid'Oraita lo* — biblically it is not obligated; *mipnei tikkun ha'olam in* — it is the *takana* that mandates it.

### First Takkana

But Rav Ashi complicates the picture by introducing a two-stage history: *shtei takanos havu* — there were actually two distinct *takkanot*. Originally, the law was in accordance with Rava: the land retained its *kedusha* even when sold to a non-Jew, and grain grown on it was *d'Oraita* obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot* and *bikkurim*. Sellers would buy back the first fruits and bring *bikkurim*, and this was entirely *d'Oraita*. But the Rabbis observed that this very continuity of *kedusha* — the fact that one could still bring *bikkurim* — made it too easy to sell the land. People felt the sale was not so consequential: the *kedusha* would remain, they could always buy back the fruit and bring *bikkurim*, and everything would be fine. So the Rabbis enacted a *takana*: do not bring *bikkurim* from such land. Remove that comfort. Make the sale feel irreversible in practice.

### Stringency Failed

The problem is that *takkanot* intended to deter a behavior sometimes fail to do so. *Kivun de'hazei de'man de'lo sagi lei mazvin v'kamishtakei b'yad ovdei kochavim* — when the Rabbis saw that people who had no financial alternative were selling their land anyway, regardless of the consequences, the *takana* had not achieved its goal. The land was still passing into non-Jewish hands, but now *bikkurim* was not being brought from it either. This is the worst of both worlds: the problem the *takana* was meant to solve was not solved, and an additional loss — the cessation of *bikkurim* — had been created as a collateral consequence.

### Reversal

*Hadaru v'takinu lehu de'ayisu* — the Rabbis therefore reversed the first *takana* and reinstated the obligation to bring *bikkurim*. The result is what the *mishna* records as *tikkun ha'olam*: the obligation to purchase the fruit and bring *bikkurim* is not a new rabbinic creation but rather the restoration of the *d'Oraita* situation, now enacted as a *takana* because the intermediate prohibition had been in force. On this reading, the *mishna*'s language of *tikkun ha'olam* is somewhat ironic — it is *tikkun ha'olam* in the sense of reversing a failed rabbinic intervention and returning to the original state.

### Tikkun Olam

This episode illuminates the broader theme of *tikkun ha'olam* that has run throughout this chapter of Gittin. *Tikkun olam* is not a one-directional enterprise. It sometimes means imposing harsh consequences — withholding redemption, prohibiting *bikkurim*, refusing to readmit a divorced wife — in order to address deep systemic problems. But systemic interventions do not always succeed. Economic and social realities can overwhelm even well-designed deterrents. When the Rabbis saw that their stringency was producing only harm without any corresponding benefit, they reversed course. The willingness to reverse a *takana* is itself an act of *tikkun olam* — recognizing that a societal remedy that does not remedy must be abandoned, even at the cost of acknowledging that the initial intervention failed.

## Kinyan Peirot Debate

### Core Dispute

The *gemara* now transitions to another fundamental debate in *Shas*, one that will continue into the next *daf*. *Itmar: hamocher sadehu l'peirot* — if one sells his field *l'peirot*, granting the purchaser *kinyan peirot* — usufruct ownership, the right to benefit from the land's produce. The buyer owns the land for the purpose of its produce; he cannot make structural changes, and underlying title remains with the seller. The question: can such a buyer bring *bikkurim* from that land? Can he recite the declaration of *al ha'adamah asher natata li* — upon the land which You gave me?

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan says: *meivi u'korei* — he brings *bikkurim* and recites the declaration. *Kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf dami* — ownership of the produce is legally equivalent to ownership of the body of the land itself. Since the buyer has the right to all the produce, he has sufficient standing to declare the land as his own for purposes of *bikkurim*.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish disagrees: *meivi v'eino korei* — he brings *bikkurim* but does not recite the declaration. *Kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf dami* — usufruct ownership is not legally equivalent to full ownership. The buyer has real rights to the produce, sufficient to create a *bikkurim* obligation — the fruit grew in *Eretz Yisrael* under Jewish ownership of some kind — but he cannot say *al ha'adamah asher natata li*, because he does not hold title to the land itself.

### Wife's Produce

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish: *u'lveisecha melamed she'adam meivi bikkurei ishto v'korei*. The *pasuk* regarding *bikkurim* reads: *v'samachta b'chol hatov asher nasan lecha Hashem Elokecha u'lveisecha* — you shall rejoice in all the good that God has given you and to your household. The phrase *u'lveisecha*, to your household, is understood to include a man's wife — meaning a husband brings *bikkurim* from his wife's fields and recites the declaration. But the husband's right to his wife's land is precisely a *kinyan peirot*: the wife retains title to her fields, and the husband's entitlement to their produce is a rabbinic institution — perhaps reflecting a pre-existing social reality that the *Torah* then incorporated — by which he benefits from those fields without owning them outright. If the husband can say *al ha'adamah asher natata li* over his wife's land despite holding only *kinyan peirot*, this proves that *kinyan peirot* is sufficient for the full *bikkurim* declaration.

### Proof Debate

Reish Lakish responds: *shani hasam d'chsiv u'lveisecha* — that case is different because the *Torah* specifically wrote *u'lveisecha*, creating a special rule for the husband. It is not a general principle about *kinyan peirot*; it is an exception carved out by the *pasuk*. Accordingly, it cannot be used to establish that *kinyan peirot* generally suffices for *meivi v'korei*.

The *gemara* then records an alternate version of the exchange: *ika d'amri*, some say the proof ran in the opposite direction — Reish Lakish cited *u'lveisecha* against Rabbi Yochanan, as evidence that the husband can bring his wife's *bikkurim* only because of the special verse, implying that without such a verse *kinyan peirot* alone would not suffice. To this Rabbi Yochanan replies: *ta'ama d'idi nami hacha kamina* — you think that is an exception? That is my paradigm. The *Torah* said *u'lveisecha* and thereby established the rule: one who has *kinyan peirot* in a field brings *bikkurim* and recites the declaration. The verse does not create an exception; it enunciates the principle. This debate will continue on the following *daf*.
