# Bava Batra 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Husband's Chazakah

### Husband No Chazakah

The *mishnah* established that a husband cannot acquire *chazakah* on his wife's property, since his right to consume the *peirot* — the produce — explains his presence on the land without implying ownership. The *gemara* adds a further layer: even if the husband explicitly waives his right to the *peirot*, he still cannot establish *chazakah*, because we assume the wife is not particular (*makpid*) about his use and would forgive any benefit he derives. The *mishnah*'s ruling thus rests on two independent pillars: the legitimacy of his presence due to *kinyan peirot*, and the assumption of the wife's ongoing acquiescence.

### Wife's Sale Valid

The implication of the *mishnah*, however, is that if the husband produces a *shtar* — a formal document — showing that the wife sold the property to him, this would be valid evidence of ownership. One might think otherwise based on a *mishnah* elsewhere that invalidates a wife's act of releasing her liens on property the husband has sold, on the grounds that she may have done so merely to please her husband (*mishum ikva d'ba'lah*). If that logic applies to *nichsei milog* as well, perhaps even a formal sale from wife to husband should be suspect.

The *gemara* rejects this concern. The case of releasing liens is qualitatively different for two reasons. First, the wife is not actually surrendering her own property — she is waiving a future collection right, while the underlying debt remains. Second, those liens exist precisely to protect her in the event of his death or divorce, and to hold onto them aggressively would generate marital tension; hence we suspect she released them under social pressure. By contrast, to actually sell her own *nichsei milog* — the property over which she retains title — is a substantial and independent act. We do not assume she would make such a sale merely to accommodate her husband. A *shtar* documenting such a sale is therefore valid.

### Amemar Nichsei Milog

The *gemara* now raises a challenge from a teaching of Amemar: *ish v'ishah she-machru be-nichsei milog lo asu v'lo klum* — if both husband and wife sold the *nichsei milog*, they have accomplished nothing. At first glance, this seems to describe a case where husband and wife jointly approached a third party, the husband selling his *kinyan peirot* and the wife selling her title (*kinyan haguf*), and together they conveyed the property — yet the sale is invalid. This would suggest that the concern of *mishum ikva d'ba'lah* does apply to *nichsei milog* after all.

The *gemara*, however, reinterprets Amemar's statement. Rather than "husband *and* wife," the word should be read as "husband *or* wife" — that is, when only one of them acts alone, the sale does not fully succeed. This resolves the apparent contradiction: Amemar is not speaking of a joint sale at all, but of the inherent limitations of each party's independent authority.

### Sale Not Permanent

This reinterpretation now requires elaboration. When only one party acts, *lo asu v'lo klum* does not mean the transaction is entirely void — it means it is not permanent. If the husband sells his *kinyan peirot* to a third party, the buyer does acquire those rights for the duration of the marriage; however, since the husband can only convey what he himself holds, the *peirot* rights revert to the wife upon his death or their divorce.

The wife's case is more complex. If she sells her *kinyan haguf* — her title to the *nichsei milog* — one might ask why that is not fully permanent. She owns title outright; if she sells it to a third party and then dies, why should the husband be able to reclaim it? Ordinarily, when any seller dies after conveying property, the heirs cannot retrieve it. The answer is that this is the subject of a special *takanah* enacted at Usha, which is addressed in the next section. The reinterpretation also clarifies that a sale *to one another* is fully valid: if wife sells to husband, or they act in concert to transfer to a third party, these cases are distinct from the problematic one-sided conveyance. A *shtar* from wife to husband conveying her *nichsei milog* is therefore entirely effective.

## Takanah Usha

### Joint vs Individual

Rav Nachman draws the lines clearly. If both husband and wife jointly agree to divest themselves of the *nichsei milog* to a third party, or if the wife sells her title directly to her husband, these transactions are binding and permanent. The rule of *lo asu v'lo klum* applies only when one party acts unilaterally and without the participation of the other.

### Husband Lokeach

The *gemara* turns to the case that the *mishnah* itself could not have resolved: if the wife sells her *kinyan haguf* and then dies, why should the husband be able to reclaim the property from the buyer? The *gemara* answers: *be-takanta de-rabbanan* — this is by force of a rabbinic ordinance. As Rabbi Yose bar Chanina taught: *be-Usha takinu* — at Usha, the Sages enacted that if a woman sells her *nichsei milog* and subsequently dies, the husband may retrieve the property from the purchasers.

The logical basis for this ruling is striking, since ordinarily a purchaser acquires the property definitively and the seller's heirs have no claim. The *gemara* explains that the Sages gave the husband the status of *lokeach rishon* — a first purchaser. Because the wife's *nichsei milog* enters the marriage as property in which the husband has ongoing rights, the Sages treated him as though he had a prior purchase on it. He anticipated inheriting her, and accordingly is given priority over any subsequent purchaser. This is a deliberately constructed protective *takanah* designed to encourage marriage by ensuring the husband's investment in the marital estate is not undermined by his wife's unilateral sale.

The practical upshot, then, is this: a wife may sell her *kinyan haguf* to a third party — it is a real transaction during her lifetime. But if she predeceases her husband, the *takanah* of Usha allows him to step in and reclaim it. If, however, the husband and wife together sold the property, or if the wife sold her title directly to her husband, no such retrieval is possible; the sale stands absolutely.

## Kinyan Peirot Debate

### Four Ownership Types

The *gemara* now widens its lens considerably. Amemar's statement that a unilateral sale of *nichsei milog* accomplishes nothing, the *gemara* suggests alternatively, may reflect the position of Rabbi Eliezer in a broader and fundamental dispute: when ownership of an object is split between two parties — one holding *kinyan peirot* (the right to the produce or benefit) and the other holding *kinyan haguf* (title to the object itself) — who is considered the legal owner?

This question is not merely theoretical. Numerous *halachos* turn on identifying who is the legal "owner" of an object: to whom do we pay double payment (*kefel*) when the object is stolen? Who may consecrate (*makdish*) the object? Who bears responsibility for it? In all these cases, we need to know to whom the title of "owner" belongs.

The *gemara* identifies four logical possibilities. Either the one with *kinyan peirot* is the owner, or the one with *kinyan haguf* is the owner, or both are considered owners, or neither is a full owner. It is worth pausing to distinguish this situation from ordinary *shutfus* — partnership. In a partnership, each party owns fifty percent of the object, but each party's ownership is complete in the sense that each holds both *kinyan haguf* and *kinyan peirot* over his portion. Here, by contrast, the division runs across the entire object: one person has the totality of its benefit-rights and the other holds the entire title, yet neither has both dimensions of ownership over the whole.

### Slave Case

The *gemara* introduces a *baraisa* to illuminate the debate: *ha-mocher avdo u-fasak imo she-yishameshenu sheloshim yom* — Reuven sells his slave to Shimon, but stipulates that Reuven will retain the right to the slave's services for the next thirty days. The sale is immediate and genuine — Shimon acquires title now — but Reuven retains *kinyan peirot* in the slave for that month.

The *baraisa* then asks which of the two holds the *halachic* status of "master" for the purposes of the verse: *lo yukam ki chaspo hu* — "he shall not be avenged, for he is his money" (Shemot 21:21). This refers to the exemption granted to a master whose slave dies one or two days after a beating. The underlying premise of *yom o yomayim* is that if the slave survived at least a full day, the master is exempt from execution, since the very fact of the slave's survival suggests the blow was not intended to kill. Only the "master" receives this exemption; an unrelated person who beat the slave would be liable even after two days. The question is thus: which party — Reuven with *kinyan peirot* or Shimon with *kinyan haguf* — is considered the "master" for this purpose?

It is worth noting that the case of a seller retaining *kinyan peirot* might differ from a buyer who acquires only *kinyan peirot*: one could argue that Reuven, who began as full owner and merely kept the *peirot* rights, has a stronger claim to the title of owner. The *gemara*, however, does not draw this distinction, and the debate is treated as a general one about the nature of split ownership.

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir rules: *ha-rishon yeshno be-din yom o yomayim* — the original owner, Reuven, who retains *kinyan peirot*, has the status of master and receives the exemption of *yom o yomayim*. His reasoning is *mipnei she-hu tachtav* — because the slave is "under him," under his actual control and authority. The *pasuk* in question employs the phrase *u-met tachat yado*, "and he dies under his hand," which Rabbi Meir reads as pointing to the one who actually controls the slave's labor and daily existence.

The *gemara* frames Rabbi Meir's position as *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — having *kinyan peirot* is legally equivalent to having *kinyan haguf*. The one who controls the benefit of the object is considered its owner in the full legal sense. This is not merely a local ruling about the *halachah* of *yom o yomayim*; the *gemara* understands Rabbi Meir's position as a global claim about ownership: wherever the law asks "who is the owner?" the answer is whoever holds *kinyan peirot*.

### Rabbi Yehudah

Rabbi Yehudah rules the opposite: *ha-sheni yeshno be-din yom o yomayim* — it is Shimon, the buyer, who holds title (*kinyan haguf*), who is considered the master and receives the exemption, even though during this thirty-day period he has no right to the slave's services. His reasoning follows from the end of the verse: *lo yukam ki chaspo hu* — the slave is exempted because he is the master's "money," his property. Shimon is the one who purchased the slave; the slave is legally Shimon's asset. Reuven, despite retaining practical control, does not own the slave and so cannot claim the master's exemption.

The *gemara* frames Rabbi Yehudah's position as *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* — having *kinyan peirot* does not render you the owner. Ownership in the full legal sense belongs exclusively to the one who holds title, regardless of who currently enjoys the benefit.

### Rabbi Yose

Rabbi Yose stakes out a third position: *shneihem yesh bahem din yom o yomayim* — both Reuven and Shimon have the status of master, and the exemption applies to each of them. For Reuven: *mipnei she-hu tachtav*, the slave is under his control. For Shimon: *mipnei she-hu chaspo*, the slave is his purchased property. Each party satisfies one of the two grounds for the master's exemption, so both qualify.

One might ask: how can there be two masters simultaneously? In ordinary *shutfus*, two partners each own fifty percent, and each might indeed share the title of "master" for his proportion. Rabbi Yose seems to say something similar here — that both parties hold a genuine ownership status, each through his own dimension of ownership, such that the law assigns both the title of owner for the purpose of this *halachah*.

The *gemara* notes, however, that Rabbi Yose's position may not be one of certainty. The phrase *u-mistaflei* — he is uncertain — suggests that Rabbi Yose may be saying not that he affirmatively assigns both the status of master, but that he does not know which one to assign it to. The underlying question — *im kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami im lav* — is unresolved, and since the case involves potential capital punishment, the principle of *safek nefashot le-hakeil* applies: when there is doubt about liability for execution, we are lenient, and neither party is executed.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer dissents from all three previous positions: *shneihem einam be-din yom o yomayim* — neither Reuven nor Shimon has the status of master, and neither receives the exemption. The *pasuk* demands *chaspo ha-meyuchad lo* — the slave must be one's property in a complete and undivided sense, exclusively yours. That means a single person must possess both *tachtav* (the slave is under his control) and *chaspo* (the slave is his titled property). When these two dimensions of ownership are split between two people, neither satisfies the full criterion.

The practical implication, as Rava explains, is stark: not only does neither have the master's exemption, but neither is sufficiently in the position of "owner" to act unilaterally with respect to the object. This is why, according to the *gemara*, Amemar's statement that a unilateral sale by one of the parties is *lo asu v'lo klum* may reflect Rabbi Eliezer's view. Since the husband has only *kinyan peirot* and the wife has only *kinyan haguf*, neither is a complete owner, and therefore neither can independently convey the property to a third party. For such a transfer to be valid, both must act in concert — because only then are the full dimensions of ownership operating together.

It is a genuinely strange position. The slave exists; someone must bear responsibility for him. Yet according to Rabbi Eliezer, no single person holds the unified legal title of "master." Each party owns what he owns, but the integrated legal identity of ownership — with its attendant rights and duties — belongs to no one in isolation. Rava's formulation that *chaspo ha-meyuchad lo* is required reinforces that this is not a case of fifty percent ownership as in ordinary partnership, but of a fragmented ownership in which neither half is sufficient to constitute full legal dominion.

It would seem, however, that both Rabbi Eliezer and every other opinion would agree on one point: each party may convey his own rights *to the other*. When the wife sells her *kinyan haguf* to her husband, she is not acting unilaterally against someone who holds a competing title — she is investing the other party with the totality of ownership. That, by any account, is a valid transaction.

## Third Party Chazakah

### Third Party No Chazakah

Having concluded the discussion of whether a husband has *chazakah* on his wife's property, the *gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s ruling — *v'lo l'ish chazakah b'nichsei ishto* — and approaches it from a new angle. Rav states: *ein machzikin b'nichsei eshet ish* — a third party cannot establish *chazakah* on the *nichsei milog* of a married woman. This is a significant and independent ruling, not derived from anything in our *mishnah*.

To understand why, consider the dynamics at play. If a third party — call him Michael — is occupying the wife's land and consuming its *peirot*, he is encroaching on the husband's immediate benefit. The husband has *kinyan peirot*, so it is his produce that is being taken. One might therefore think the husband should be the one to protest, and indeed he should. But the question for *chazakah* purposes is different: whose silence constitutes an implicit concession of ownership? The answer is: only the silence of the owner. The wife holds title; it is her acquiescence that would communicate that Michael is the legitimate owner. And the wife can always explain her silence by saying: *aba'ali samachti* — I relied on my husband to handle it; he is the one who deals with the fields, whose *peirot* are being taken, and I assumed he would protest.

### Wife Relies Husband

This is how the Rashbam explains the ruling. The wife's failure to protest is not an implicit admission that the third party owns the land. Rather, it reflects a reasonable and natural division of domestic responsibility — the husband manages the practical affairs of the land, and she expected him to raise the objection. Her silence therefore carries no evidentiary weight.

### Owner's Silence

The conceptual point deserves emphasis. In ordinary *chazakah*, it is the *silence of the owner* that serves as the implicit admission: if this person were truly occupying property that belongs to me, I would have protested; my silence suggests I must have sold or given it. This logic is powerful only when the silent party is the owner. When the silent party is someone *other* than the owner — in this case, the husband — his silence cannot be used against the owner. The husband's failure to protest may be circumstantial evidence, but in *halachah* it does not constitute the kind of owner-admission on which *chazakah* is built. The wife retains her ability to say: I was not responsible for his silence, and my own silence was explained by my reliance on him.

The consequence is a striking one: according to Rav, *no* third party can ever establish *chazakah* on a married woman's *nichsei milog* during the marriage. If you purchase land from a married woman without a *shtar*, hold onto that document carefully — because without it, you are legally vulnerable.

## Husband Chazakah Cases

### Principal Changes

If a third party cannot establish *chazakah* on the wife's *nichsei milog*, and the husband has no *chazakah* by virtue of simply consuming the *peirot*, who can ever have *chazakah* on a married woman's property? The *gemara* earlier asked: since Rav rules that a third party cannot have *chazakah* on a married woman's land, what did Rav mean when he said that a married woman *must* protest (*eshet ish tzricha limchos*)? If third parties cannot establish *chazakah* regardless of her silence, and her husband has no *chazakah* by virtue of his *kinyan peirot*, what case is left where her protest is necessary?

Rava resolves this: *le'olam be-va'al* — the case where she must protest is precisely when her *husband* takes possession, but in a specific way. The *mishnah*'s ruling that a husband has no *chazakah* applies to his ordinary use of the land: eating the fruit, working the field, living on the property. All of that is entirely consistent with his rights as husband. But if he makes structural changes to the principal of the property itself — *kegon she-chafar bah boros, shichin u-me'aros*, such as digging cisterns, pits, and caverns — this is a different category entirely.

Such acts are not the consumption of *peirot*; they are alterations to the land's essential character, changes to the *keren*, the principal, that go beyond anything sanctioned by his marital rights. The Rashbam notes that the case must involve a type of field where such constructions would be abnormal — the husband has, say, decided to excavate water cisterns in a wheat field, convinced this is an improvement, while a reasonable owner would regard it as a damaging alteration. Since this falls outside his legitimate authority, his doing so is an *adverse act*, and the wife's silence in the face of it becomes meaningful. Her failure to object to something she was clearly not obligated to accept suggests, by the logic of *chazakah*, that she must have conveyed ownership to him.

Interestingly, even here the third-party rule appears to remain intact: even if a third party were to make such changes to the wife's land, she could still say she was relying on her husband to object, and no *chazakah* would result.

### Damage Acts Immediate

This raises a further question: does the husband's *chazakah* through principal changes require the standard three-year period, or is it immediate? The *gemara* invokes the principle *ein chazakah le-nezikin*, which on first reading seems to say: when damaging acts are involved, there is no *chazakah* — and which, as the Rashbam reinterprets, means the very opposite: *ein din chazakah le-hatzrich shalosh shanim le-nezikin* — there is no requirement of three years when the act causing *chazakah* is itself a damaging one. If someone observes another person damaging his own land and remains silent, the silence is immediately significant, because no owner tolerates visible destruction of his property without objection. The *chazakah* therefore takes effect at once.

### Nezikin Clarified

The *gemara*, however, reconsiders the meaning of *ein chazakah le-nezikin* altogether. Rav Mari cites *kutra* — smoke — and Rav Zvid cites *beis ha-kisei* — an outhouse — as the true referents of this phrase. The line *ein chazakah le-nezikin* does not speak about establishing ownership of a field through damaging acts at all. Rather, it addresses the laws of neighbors from the second *perek*: if I have been generating smoke or noxious odors that drift into your property, and you have tolerated it for years without protest, can I claim *chazakah* — that your silence has waived your right to complain permanently? The answer is *ein chazakah le-nezikin*: for ongoing damaging intrusions, silence never constitutes a waiver. You may always protest, no matter how long you have tolerated the nuisance.

This reinterpretation leaves the question of immediate *chazakah* through damaging physical alterations somewhat open. The phrase has been reassigned to neighbor-damage cases, and what the *halachah* requires when a husband makes principal changes — whether immediately or after three years — may depend on further analysis of the *sugia*.

## Post-Death Chazakah

### Widowed Exception

Rav Yosef introduces a case where a third party *can* establish *chazakah* on what was a married woman's property. The scenario is: the third party began occupying the land while the woman was married — a period during which, as established, her silence carried no evidentiary weight, since she could claim reliance on her husband. But then the husband died, and the third party continued in possession for three full years as a widow.

The question is whether the initial period of possession, begun during the marriage, can combine with the post-mortem years to create a valid *chazakah*. Can she say: since the whole thing started while my husband was alive, I was never truly paying attention, even after he died? Or do we say that once she became a widow, she stands on her own, and three years of uncontested possession by a third party during that period creates full *chazakah*?

Rav Yosef rules that *chazakah* applies in this case. Once the husband dies, the wife is treated like any other property owner. Three years of uncontested possession after his death — regardless of how the occupation began — is sufficient. The *gemara* further notes the interesting *migo* that this creates: the possessor could have claimed "I bought it from you after your husband died," and since that claim would have been believed, he is also believed when he says "you sold it to your husband while he was alive and he sold it to me" — *meheman*, he is believed. The more fundamental point, however, is simply that the widow no longer has the same excuse; she cannot defer responsibility for protest to a husband who no longer exists.

## Dayanei Golah

### Dayanei Golah

The *gemara* returns to the original statement of Rav — *ein machzikin be-nichsei eshet ish* — and notes an opposing ruling. The *dayanei golah*, the judges of the exile, whom the Rashbam identifies as Shmuel and Karna, ruled: *machzikin* — a third party *can* establish *chazakah* on a married woman's property. They reject Rav's position outright, holding that the wife's reliance on her husband does not excuse her silence indefinitely, and that the normal rules of *chazakah* apply.

### Rav's Reversal

The *gemara* then records a remarkable development: Rav himself stated, *halacha ke-dayanei golah* — the *halachah* follows the judges of the exile. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged him: have you reversed your own ruling? You were the one who said that no one can establish *chazakah* on a married woman's property!

Rav's response: *mistavra ami ke-deRav Yosef* — I prefer to understand the ruling of the *dayanei golah* in accordance with what Rav Yosef said. That is, when the *dayanei golah* say that *chazakah* applies to a married woman's property, they mean the case where the husband has already died and three years of possession followed — which is the scenario Rav Yosef described.

The *gemara* itself acknowledges, through the Rashbam, that this is a strained reading: the *dayanei golah* appear to have genuinely disputed Rav and held that even during the marriage, a third party can establish *chazakah* on a wife's property. Rav's attempt to harmonize their position with his own is a *diplomatic* reconciliation rather than a straightforward one. And so the *daf* concludes with this unresolved tension: Rav maintaining, at least nominally, that his original position stands, while the *dayanei golah* — Shmuel and Karna — articulate a substantially more permissive view of third-party *chazakah* on a married woman's property.
