# Ketubot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Petach Patuach & Ketubah…

### Petach Patuach Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: חֲכָמִים תִּקְּנוּ לָהֶם לִבְנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל, לִבְתוּלָה מָאתַיִם, וּלְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה. וְהֵם הֶאֱמִינוּהוּ, שֶׁאִם אָמַר "פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי" — נֶאֱמָן. אִם כֵּן — מָה הוֹעִילוּ חֲכָמִים בְּתַקָּנָתָם?
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated: Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: The Sages instituted** the marriage contract **for Jewish women: For a virgin two hundred** dinars **and for a widow one hundred dinars.** And they **deemed** the groom **credible** in **that if** he **says** with regard to his virgin bride: **I encountered an unobstructed orifice** and she is not a virgin, **he is deemed credible,** causing her to lose her marriage contract. The Gemara asks: **If so,** and the Sages deemed him credible, **what did the Sages accomplish in their ordinance** that the marriage contract of a virgin is two hundred dinars, if his claim that she is not a virgin is effective?

The *gemara* opens with a case that had been discussed at length: whether a husband is believed when he claims *petach patuach* — that upon consummating the marriage, he experienced what felt like an already-open entrance, as opposed to finding no *dam betulim* whatsoever. The stakes of this claim are significant. One consequence is that, in certain configurations — such as where the wife is the wife of a *kohen* — the claim effectively translates into an allegation of prior adultery, rendering her forbidden to him by operation of the principle *shavya anafshei ḥaticha de-issura*: he has bound himself by his own words. But the more immediately surprising consequence is that he is believed to cause her to forfeit her *kesuva*. The *gemara* now addresses both the mechanism by which he is believed and the deeper question of what the *kesuva* itself actually is.

### Rabbinic Institution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: חֲזָקָה, אֵין אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ בַּסְּעוּדָה וּמַפְסִידָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ordinance is effective due to the **presumption** that **a person does not exert** himself to prepare a wedding **feast and** then **cause it to be lost.** Investing in the wedding preparations clearly indicates that the groom's intention is to marry the bride and rejoice with her. If, nevertheless, he claims that she is not a virgin, apparently he is telling the truth.

*Itmar* — it was taught. Rav Naḥman said in the name of Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *ḥachamim tiknu lahem livnot yisrael masayim u-le-almanah maneh* — the rabbis instituted that a virgin receives a *kesuva* of 200 *zuz*, and an *almanah* — a widow, or more broadly, a woman who is not a virgin — receives 100 *zuz*. The entire institution, in other words, is rabbinic in origin. And since it is rabbinic, *ve-hem hem eminu'hu*: the very same rabbinic authority that created the institution chose to believe the husband when he claims *petach patuach matzasi* and to rule accordingly that she does not receive her *kesuva*.

The *gemara* immediately raises the obvious challenge: *im kein mah ho'ilu ḥachamim betakkanatam*? If the rabbis created an obligation and simultaneously gave the husband an easy exit from it, what exactly did their enactment accomplish? A man need only say *petach patuach* and he avoids payment entirely. The *gemara* responds with Rava's principle: *ḥazakah ein adam tore'aḥ bise'udah u-mafsidah* — we may assume that a person does not go to the trouble of making a wedding feast only to destroy it. The expense and effort of a wedding creates a strong presumption that the husband is not fabricating this claim, because making it up would mean throwing away everything he invested. This presumption is sufficient to render the enactment functionally effective: cases of false claims will be rare enough that the *takkanah* retains its protective force.

### Tosafot Qualification

*Tosafot* raises a pointed objection. The logic of *ein adam tore'aḥ bise'udah u-mafsidah* presupposes that the husband is sacrificing something by making the claim. But what if he is not? *Tosafot* notes that there is a later dispute in the *gemara* about the consequences of a husband discovering that his wife was not a virgin as he had expected: is this a *mekaḥ ta'os* — a transaction conducted on false premises — in which case the marriage is annulled and she receives no *kesuva* at all? Or does it merely warrant an adjustment of the *kesuva* downward from 200 to 100 *zuz*, leaving the marriage intact?

This distinction is critical. If the claim of *petach patuach* results in something drastic — she is forbidden to him because adultery is implied, or the entire marriage is void as a *mekaḥ ta'os* — then indeed one would not fabricate such a claim after investing in a wedding, because the consequence is losing one's wife entirely. *Tosafot* states this explicitly: *tinaḥ be-eshes kohen o bifḥut mibas gimmel* — in a case involving a *kohen*'s wife or a girl married under the age of three, where the claim implies forbidden relations, *keivan d'mifsada*, since he loses his wife, we believe him. Similarly, according to the view that the result is *mekaḥ ta'os* and she gets nothing, the drastic nature of the consequences again supports his credibility.

But *Tosafot* continues: *aval le-man d'amar yeish lah kesuva maneh* — according to the opinion that the only result is reducing her *kesuva* from 200 to 100, *lo mafsid midei* — he loses nothing. He remains married, keeps the wedding gifts, and simply pays less. In that scenario, *amai meheiman*? Why should he be believed at all? *Ve-yeish lomar de-lo meheiman* — and one may answer that indeed he would not be believed. This is an important qualification on the *gemara*'s blanket statement: the husband's credibility when claiming *petach patuach* applies only in contexts where his claim carries sufficiently drastic personal consequences for him.

## Mohar to Kesubah

### Rabbinic Fine & Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הוֹאִיל וּקְנַס חֲכָמִים הוּא — לֹא תִּגְבֶּה אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית. קְנָסָא? מַאי קְנָסָא?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְתַקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הוּא — לֹא תִּגְבֶּה אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: **Since** payment of the marriage contract **is a penalty** instituted by the **Sages, she may collect only from the** husband's **land of the most inferior quality.** The Gemara asks: **A penalty? What penalty** is there in a marriage contract? **Rather,** emend the *baraita* and **say: Since it is a rabbinic ordinance** and not a Torah obligation, **she may collect only from the** husband's **land of the most inferior quality.**

Having established that the *kesuva* is a rabbinic institution, the *gemara* now cites a *beraita*: *havi knas ḥachamim hu lo tigbeh ella min ha-ziburis* — since the *kesuva* is a rabbinic fine, she may collect only from *ziburis*. Land in rabbinic law is categorized into three grades: *idis* is Grade A, *beinonis* is Grade B, and *ziburis* is Grade C — the lowest quality. Ordinarily, a creditor collecting a debt is entitled to land of equivalent value, but the location and quality of that land matters enormously. The *beraita* reasons that since the *kesuva* is not a conventional debt arising from a prior obligation but rather a rabbinic creation, collection is limited to the least desirable category of land.

The *gemara* notes that the word *knas* — fine — requires explanation: what wrong did the husband commit that we are fining him? The *gemara* emends the framing: *ho'il ve-takkanat ḥachamim hu* — since it is a rabbinic institution, it is simply of lesser legal weight than a biblical or naturally arising obligation, and therefore collection is restricted accordingly. Underlying this ruling is also the rabbis' persistent concern for social equilibrium: the protective obligations upon the husband must be calibrated so as not to be so burdensome that men become reluctant to marry. The limitation to *ziburis* reflects that balance.

### Biblical Mohar Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה מִן הַתּוֹרָה. וּמִי אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: "כֶּסֶף יִשְׁקֹל כְּמֹהַר הַבְּתוּלוֹת": שֶׁיְּהֵא זֶה, כְּמוֹהַר הַבְּתוּלוֹת, וּמוֹהַר הַבְּתוּלוֹת כָּזֶה. מִכָּאן סָמְכוּ חֲכָמִים לִכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה מִן הַתּוֹרָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה אֵינָהּ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The marriage contract of a woman is** an obligation **by Torah** law. **And did Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel say that? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that it is written with regard to a seducer: **"He shall pay money according to the dowry of virgins"** (Exodus 22:16)? The Torah establishes that **this** fine **will be like the dowry of a virgin,** and that **the dowry of a virgin** will be **like this** fine, i.e., fifty silver *sela*, or two hundred dinars. **From here the Sages based** their determination **that a woman's marriage contract is** an obligation **by Torah** law. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The marriage contract of a woman is not** an obligation **by Torah law, but** is **by rabbinic law.**

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: kesubas isha min ha-Torah* — the *kesuva* is biblical in origin. The *gemara* challenges this immediately: did Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel actually say this? A *beraita* is cited in which he appears to say the opposite — that the *kesuva* is purely rabbinic.

The *gemara* first discusses the biblical foundation itself. The verse *kesef yishkol ke-mohar ha-betulos* — he shall weigh out silver according to the *mohar* of virgins — appears in the context of a man who seduces a virgin. Because she is no longer a virgin, she loses her *mohar*, the traditional payment given by the groom to the bride's father when contracting a marriage. Accordingly, the seducer must compensate the father for that loss. The *pshat* of the Torah, and the position of biblical scholars as well, is that the *mohar* was a genuine bridal price, paid up front and given to the father — not a symbolic token like a ring or a *perutah*, but a substantial sum.

By combining this passage with the case of the rapist in *Devarim*, who pays fifty units of silver, and identifying the currency as *shekel* — itself derived from the word *yishkol* — the Sages establish that the standard *mohar ha-betulos* was 50 *shekel*, which equals 200 *zuz*. *Mikan samchu ḥachamim le-kesubas isha min ha-Torah* — from here the rabbis found a biblical basis for the institution of a woman's *kesuva*. The 200 *zuz* that was once the *mohar* paid at the beginning of a marriage became the 200 *zuz* of the *kesuva* payable at the end, in cases of death or divorce. The rabbis transformed an upfront biblical payment into a deferred rabbinic institution.

### Reconciling Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ. וּמַאי חָזֵית דְּאָפְכַתְּ בָּתְרָיְיתָא, אֵיפוֹךְ קַמַּיְיתָא? הָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. דִּתְנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר — נוֹתֵן לָהּ מִמְּעוֹת קַפּוֹטְקְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the contradiction between the statements of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: **Reverse** the attribution of opinions in this *baraita*. The Gemara asks: **And what did you see that** led **you** to **reverse** the attribution of opinions **in the latter** *baraita*? **Reverse** the attribution of opinions in **the former,** in the *baraita*, and say that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is the one who holds that the marriage contract is a rabbinic ordinance. **But we learned that** it is **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel who said** elsewhere **that the marriage contract of a woman is** an obligation **by Torah** law, **as we learned** in a mishna (110b) that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** that if a man marries a woman in Cappadocia, where the currency is more valuable, and he divorces her in Eretz Yisrael, **he gives her** payment for the marriage contract **from the money of Cappadocia.** From the fact that he is obligated to pay the marriage contract in the currency of the place where he undertook the obligation, apparently the marriage contract of a woman is an obligation by Torah law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: כּוּלָּהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: מִכָּאן סָמְכוּ חֲכָמִים לִכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה מִן הַתּוֹרָה. כְּתוּבַּת אַלְמָנָה אֵינָהּ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, שֶׁרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּתוּבַּת אַלְמָנָה אֵינָהּ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the **entire** latter *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, but** the *baraita* **is incomplete and it is teaching the following: From here, the Sages based** their determination **that a woman's marriage contract** in the case of a virgin **is** an obligation **by Torah** law. However, the **marriage contract of a widow is not** an obligation **by Torah law but** is an ordinance **by rabbinic law, as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The marriage contract of a widow is not** an obligation **by Torah law but** is an ordinance **by rabbinic law.**

The *gemara* now faces the contradiction: in one source Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says the *kesuva* is biblical, in another he says it is rabbinic. The *gemara*'s first solution is *ipoch* — reverse the attribution in the *beraita* — assigning the position of biblical origin to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the rabbinic position to the *ḥachamim*. But why reverse the *beraita* rather than the earlier statement? Because we have independent evidence that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel treats the *kesuva* as a serious, binding obligation: *de-tenan, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer noten lah mi-me'os Kapotkia*.

A later *mishnah* addresses a case where a man married in Kapotkia and divorced in Eretz Yisrael, or vice versa. The *shekel* of Kapotkia was worth more than the *shekel* of Eretz Yisrael — comparable to the relationship between US and Canadian dollars. The *ḥachamim* say one always pays in the local Israeli currency, the lesser amount, reasoning that since the *kesuva* is rabbinic we go easy on the husband. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: if you contracted the obligation in Kapotkia, you pay in Kapotkia currency. This position — that the obligation is binding according to where and how it was contracted — reflects treating the *kesuva* as a weighty, serious obligation rather than a lenient rabbinic creation. The *gemara* therefore finds it consistent to attribute to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel the position that the *kesuva* is biblical.

The *gemara* then offers a second resolution: *ve-iba'it eima, kulei Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel hi* — perhaps the entire *beraita* reflects only Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view, and it is missing a crucial distinction. *Ve-ḥisurei meḥsera ve-hachi tanni*: the *beraita* is incomplete and must be read as follows. The *kesuva* of a *betulah* — a virgin — has a biblical basis in the *mohar ha-betulos*. The *kesuva* of an *almanah*, however, *eina mi-divrei Torah ela mi-divrei Sofrim* — is purely rabbinic — because the Torah never assigns any *mohar* for non-virgins. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's statement that the *kesuva* is rabbinic referred only to the *kesuva* of a widow, not to the *kesuva* of a virgin. The contradiction is resolved.

### Dual Nature Analysis

It is worth pausing to note something striking even in the position that the *kesuva* is *de-Oraita*: the language is *samchu ḥachamim*, they found support — not that the obligation flows directly from biblical law. The Torah's *mohar* was paid up front, at the initiation of the marriage; the *kesuva* is paid at the end. The rabbis took a biblical phenomenon and transformed it into a different institution. So even according to the *de-Oraita* position, the *kesuva* as we have it is a rabbinic re-application of a biblical prototype, not a direct biblical mandate.

This dual nature resolves a well-known difficulty raised by *Tosafot* in their first comment on the *daf*. The anonymous *stam* of the *gemara* throughout *Shas* assumes the *kesuva* is *de-rabbanan* — the *gemara* in *Isha Rabbah*, for instance, gives the reason for the institution as *she-lo te'hei kalla be-einav le-hotzi'ah*, so that divorce should not be easy, which is a classic *de-rabbanan* rationale. Yet Ashkenazic *ketubot* include the language *mohar besulayichi de-ḥazi lichi mi-de-Oraita* — the 200 *zuz* of your virginal *mohar* that is fitting to you biblically. Sephardic *ketubot* use *de-ḥazi lichi* without the word *mi-de-Oraita*, but still employ the term *mohar*. How can we write *mi-de-Oraita* if the accepted ruling is *de-rabbanan*?

The *Rosh* offers one answer: the obligation is rabbinic, but the method of calculating the amount — the coinage, the specific figure of 200 *zuz* — is derived from biblical standards. *Tosafot* offers another approach, arguing that we do in fact rule it is *de-Oraita*. But perhaps the clearest answer is simply that both are true simultaneously. The *kesuva* is *de-rabbanan* in the form we have it — a deferred payment at the end of marriage — and it is *de-ḥazi lichi mi-de-Oraita* in that it ultimately originates in, and draws its weight from, the biblical obligation of *mohar ha-betulos*. The *gemara*'s phrase *mikan samchu ḥachamim le-kesubas isha min ha-Torah* captures precisely this: it originates in the Torah but its current form is a rabbinic institution.

## Testing Non-Virginity

### Rav Naḥman's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: אַסְּבוּהוּ כּוּפְרֵי, מְבָרַכְתָּא חֲבִיטָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who** had never been married **came before Rav Naḥman** and **said to him: I encountered an unobstructed orifice** when I consummated the marriage. Rav Naḥman **said in his** regard: **Flog him** with **palm branches [*kufrei*]; prostitutes [*mevarakhta*] are common** around **him.** As he was never previously married, how was he able to determine whether or not the orifice was unobstructed, if he did not gain experience with prostitutes?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב נַחְמָן הוּא דְּאָמַר מְהֵימַן! מְהֵימַן, וּמַסְּבִינַן לֵיהּ כּוּפְרֵי. רַב אַחַאי מְשַׁנֵּי: כָּאן בְּבָחוּר, כָּאן בְּנָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't Rav Naḥman he who said that he is deemed credible** when he claims that he encountered an unobstructed orifice? The Gemara answers: Yes, **he is deemed credible, and** nevertheless, **we flog him** with **palm branches. Rav Aḥai answered: Here,** in the case where he is flogged, it is **with regard to a bachelor,** who is not accorded credibility, because he lacks experience. **There,** in the case where he is accorded credibility, it is **with regard to** one who has been **married.**

The *gemara* returns to practical cases of *petach patuach*. A man came before Rav Naḥman and said *petach patuach matzasi* — he claimed that his experience of consummation indicated his wife had not been a virgin. Rav Naḥman's response was striking: *asvuhi kufrei* — go beat him with palm branches, the barbed kind that cut as well as strike.

The *gemara* explains: *mavrachta ḥaviva lei* — he must have had prostitutes available to him. In other words, the only way he would know the difference between a virgin and a non-virgin from physical experience alone is if he had been sexually active beforehand. Two interpretations follow. Rashi reads Rav Naḥman as ordering the beating on account of the man's prior promiscuity — he has no standing to complain about his wife when he himself has been involved with prostitutes. *Tosafot* reads it in the opposite direction: precisely because only a promiscuous man would know the difference, and we cannot assume he is promiscuous, we must conclude he is lying — and so he is beaten for *motzi shem ra*, for slandering his wife.

The *gemara* then raises the obvious tension: but Rav Naḥman himself is the authority who said the husband is believed in such a case! The resolution is that he is indeed believed — *meheiman* — and we also beat him. The belief and the beating coexist: his *petach patuach* claim is accepted as evidence regarding his wife's status, but the claim simultaneously serves as evidence of his own prior sexual activity, for which lashes are appropriate. The practical effect of this approach is that very few men will come to *beit din* with such a claim once word spreads that making it results in a beating.

One might ask whether the man could simply say that he never experienced *petach patuach* but found no blood — *lo matzati dam betulim*. That would indeed be a different matter, and the answer is yes, that would be a different claim.

### First vs. Remarriage

Rav Aḥai draws a further distinction: *kan bevachur kan benasui* — it matters whether this is the man's first marriage or whether he has been married before. If he has been previously married, he has legitimate basis for comparison and would be believed when he claims *petach patuach*. If this is his first marriage, he would not be believed — and in that case, rather than lashes for promiscuity, he would receive lashes for *motzi shem ra*, for slandering his wife without any possible basis for the claim. This distinction significantly qualifies the earlier principle: the husband's general credibility regarding *petach patuach* applies only when he has had prior marital experience from which to draw a legitimate comparison.

### Door in Darkness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ מָצָאתִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁמָּא הִטֵּיתָהּ? אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בְּאִישׁוֹן לַיְלָה וַאֲפֵילָה, הִיטָּה — מְצָאוֹ פָּתוּחַ, לֹא הִיטָּה — מְצָאוֹ נָעוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who came before Rabban Gamliel said to him: I encountered an unobstructed orifice.** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: Perhaps you diverted** your approach and therefore, you encountered no obstruction? **I will tell you a parable to which this is similar.** It is similar **to a man who was walking in the blackness of night and darkness** and he arrived at the entrance to the house; if **he diverts** the object preventing the door from opening, **he finds it open;** if **he does not divert** it, **he finds it locked.** Perhaps you too diverted your approach and entered from a different angle and that is why you did not encounter an obstruction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, הָכִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁמָּא בְּמֵזִיד הִטֵּיתָהּ, וַעֲקַרְתְּ לְדַשָּׁא וְעָבְרָא? אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁהוּא מְהַלֵּךְ בְּאִישׁוֹן לַיְלָה וַאֲפֵילָה, הִיטָּה בְּמֵזִיד — מְצָאוֹ פָּתוּחַ, לֹא הִיטָּה בְּמֵזִיד — מְצָאוֹ נָעוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say this is what** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: Maybe you diverted** your approach **intentionally and you displaced the door and the bolt. I will tell you a parable to which this is similar.** It is similar **to a man who is walking in the blackness of night and darkness** and he arrives at his entrance. **If he diverts intentionally, he finds it open; if he does not divert intentionally, he finds it locked.**

A man came before Rabban Gamliel with the claim *petach patuach matzasi*. Rabban Gamliel responded: *shema hitita* — perhaps you approached at an angle. He then offered a *mashal*: a man walking in the dead of night and in total darkness approaches the door of his house. If he comes in at an angle, he may find it appears open — he slips through a crack without realizing that the door had in fact been closed. But had he come straight on, at a perpendicular, he would have discovered that the door was indeed locked. Because he did not know what he was doing, he came in at an angle and experienced it as open when it was in fact closed.

The parable maps onto the situation of a first-time husband who genuinely does not know what he is doing in consummating a marriage. His experience of *petach patuach* may be the result of his own inexperience rather than any prior breach on his wife's part.

A second version of the story — *ika d'amri* — offers a related but distinct explanation: *shema be-mazid hitatso va'akarta le-dasha ve-avra* — perhaps you pushed through so forcefully that you ripped out the door and its bolt. That is, if the husband came with such force that he broke through without feeling the obstruction, he would falsely conclude that there had been nothing there. This version maps onto a different kind of inexperience — not going in at the wrong angle, but going in with such force that the presence of an intact barrier was not registered. In either case, the conclusion is the same: a man who has not been married before has no reliable basis for such a claim.

## Blood Evidence Cases

### Dirty Sheet Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בַּר רַבִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, בָּעַלְתִּי וְלֹא מָצָאתִי דָּם. אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, בְּתוּלָה הָיִיתִי. אָמַר לָהֶם: הָבִיאוּ לִי אוֹתוֹ סוּדָר. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ הַסּוּדָר, וּשְׁרָאוֹ בְּמַיִם וְכִבְּסוֹ, וּמָצָא עָלָיו כַּמָּה טִיפֵּי דָמִים. אָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ זְכֵה בְּמִקָּחֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who came before Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: My teacher, I engaged in intercourse and did not find blood.** The bride **said to him: My teacher, I was a virgin.** Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: Bring me that cloth [*sudar*]** on which you consummated the marriage. **They brought him the cloth, and he soaked it in water and laundered it and found upon it several drops of blood** from the rupture of the hymen. Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** the groom: **Go take possession of your acquisition,** as she was a virgin and there is no need for concern.

A man came before Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi — the son of Rabbi — and said *ba'alti ve-lo matzati dam*: I had relations with my wife on the wedding night and found no blood. This claim — the absence of blood on the sheet — is more objective than *petach patuach*; the couple had been supervised and the sheet was present for inspection. His wife, speaking for the first time in these cases, said: *Rabbi, betulah hayiti* — I was a virgin. She does not deny that no blood was found; she asserts nonetheless that she had been a virgin.

Rabban Gamliel told them to bring him the sheet. He immersed it in water, cleaned it, and found several drops of blood. The sheet had been so grimy and dusty — conditions of the time — that the blood had been invisible against the general dirtiness. Once cleaned sufficiently to remove the surface dirt, the drops of blood became apparent. He told the husband: *lech zechei be-mikḥacha* — go, take good possession of your purchase. The wife was vindicated. One cannot help but notice the language throughout these cases: the woman is consistently framed as an object of purchase — *mekach*, *mikchacha* — which relates directly to the legal framework being applied. The *kesuva* of 200 *zuz* reflects what he expected to be acquiring; if she is not a virgin, it is analogous to *mekaḥ ta'os*, a transaction on false premises.

### Cleaning Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הוּנָא מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּרְזִקְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲנַן נָמֵי נַעֲבֵיד הָכִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גִּיהוּץ שֶׁלָּנוּ, כְּכִבּוּס שֶׁלָּהֶם. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ נֶיעְבַּד גִּיהוּץ — מְעַבְּרָא לֵיהּ חוּמַרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Huna Mar, son of Rava, from Parzakya, said to Rav Ashi: Let us do so as well** in similar cases and examine whether there is blood that is obscured by semen or another substance. Rav Ashi **said to him:** **Our calendering** in Babylonia, which includes passing an abrasive stone over the garments to scrape off dirt, **is like their laundering** in Eretz Israel, and only in that manner do the garments in Babylonia reach that level of cleanliness. **And if you say: Let us perform** the process of **calendering** on cloths brought as proof that she was not a virgin, **the stone removes** any trace of blood. Therefore, the process would be ineffective.

Huna the son of Rava of Parzika raised a question to Rav Ashi: why can we not simply employ this same technique today — clean the sheet when there is a claim of *lo matzati dam* and look for blood?

Rav Ashi replied: *gihutz shelanu ke-kibus shelahem* — what counts as a simple laundering for them required only immersion in water, which was sufficient to remove the surface dirt without disturbing the blood. For us, basic laundering already involves the level of scrubbing and soap that they would call *gihutz* — a deep cleanse involving rubbing against a stone and the use of cleansing agents. And if we were to perform *gihutz* in order to remove the dirt, *ma'aver lei ḥumra* — the stone used in *gihutz* would also remove the blood. There is no method available to us that removes the dirt without also removing the blood, and therefore the test cannot be replicated today.

### Wine Barrel Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בַּר רַבִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, בָּעַלְתִּי וְלֹא מָצָאתִי דָּם. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, עֲדַיִין בְּתוּלָה אֲנִי. אָמַר לָהֶן: הָבִיאוּ לִי שְׁתֵּי שְׁפָחוֹת, אַחַת בְּתוּלָה וְאַחַת בְּעוּלָה. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ, וְהוֹשִׁיבָן עַל פִּי חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן, בְּעוּלָה — רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף, בְּתוּלָה — אֵין רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף. אַף זוֹ הוֹשִׁיבָה וְלֹא הָיָה רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף. אָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ זְכֵה בְּמִקָּחֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who came before Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: My teacher, I engaged in intercourse and did not find blood.** The bride **said to him: My teacher, I am still a virgin.** Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: Bring me two maidservants, one a virgin and one a non-virgin,** to conduct a trial. **They brought him** the two maidservants, **and he seated them on the opening of a barrel of wine.** From **the non-virgin,** he discovered that the **scent** of the wine in the barrel **diffuses** from her mouth; from **the virgin** he discovered that the **scent does not diffuse** from her mouth. Then, **he also seated that** bride on the barrel, **and** the scent of the wine **did not diffuse** from her mouth. Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** the groom: **Go take possession of your acquisition,** as she is a virgin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִבְדּוֹק מֵעִיקָּרָא בְּגַוַּוהּ? גְּמָרָא הֲוָה שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, מַעֲשֶׂה לָא הֲוָה חָזֵי, וְסָבַר דִּלְמָא לָא קִים לֵיהּ בְּגַוַּוהּ דְּמִלְּתָא שַׁפִּיר, וְלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בִּבְנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since Rabban Gamliel was familiar with this method of examination, **let him** use it to **examine her initially.** Why was the trial with the maidservants necessary? The Gemara answers: **He learned** that it was effective through **tradition;** however, **he had never seen it in action, and he thought perhaps he was not sufficiently expert in** that **manner** of examination, **and** it is **improper conduct to demean Jewish women** by subjecting them to that indignity for naught. Once he established the effectiveness of that method, he proceeded to examine the bride to resolve the matter.

Another man came before Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi with the same claim: *ba'alti ve-lo matzati dam*. His wife responded differently this time: *Rabbi, adayin betulah ani* — I am still a virgin. Her claim is not that she was a virgin who produced no blood, but that the consummation never actually succeeded; the husband had not achieved full penetration and was falsely attributing the absence of blood to her prior non-virginity.

Rabban Gamliel said: bring me two maidservants, *achat betulah ve-achat be'ulah* — one who is a virgin and one who is not. They were brought. He had each of them sit on top of an open barrel of wine. *Be'ulah reicha nodef* — from the non-virgin, a smell of wine became detectable; *betulah ein reicha nodef* — from the virgin, it did not. Rashi explains: for a woman who is no longer a virgin, the fumes rising from the wine barrel travel upward through her body and emerge through her mouth, where the scent is detectable. For a virgin, the physical barrier prevents this from occurring.

Having confirmed through the controlled experiment that the test worked as he understood it should — the virgin and non-virgin responding exactly as his tradition described — Rabban Gamliel then applied the same test to the woman in question. *Ve-lo haya reicha nodef* — no smell was detectable from her. She was indeed still a virgin, and her husband had not achieved penetration. *Amar lo lech zechei be-mikḥacha* — go, your wife is still yours, and still a virgin; go try again.

The *gemara* asks: why did he first test the maidservants before applying the test to the woman in question? The answer: *gemara hava shemia lei, ma'aseh lo hava chazi* — he had received the tradition that this test works, but he had never actually carried it out. One can know a technique in principle without knowing whether one is able to execute it correctly in practice. He therefore first ran the experiment on women whose status he knew, to confirm that he was administering the test competently. Only once that was established did he apply it where it mattered. Additionally, *ve-lav oraḥ ar'a le-zalzulei bivnot Yisrael* — it would not be respectful to subject a Jewish woman to this test if there were any doubt that it would yield reliable results. As to why we do not use this test today: we simply no longer know how to administer it correctly.

## Special Cases

### Durkani Family Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן, אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, בָּעַלְתִּי וְלֹא מָצָאתִי דָּם, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת דּוֹרְקְטִי אֲנִי, שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן לֹא דַּם נִדָּה וְלֹא דַּם בְּתוּלִים. בָּדַק רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, וּמָצָא כִּדְבָרֶיהָ. אָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ זְכֵה בְּמִקָּחֶךָ, אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁזָּכִיתָ לְמִשְׁפַּחַת דּוֹרְקְטִי.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who came before Rabban Gamliel the Elder said to him: My teacher, I engaged in intercourse and did not find blood.** The bride **said to him: My teacher, I am from the family of Dorketi, who have neither menstrual blood nor blood** from the rupture of **the hymen. Rabban Gamliel investigated among her relatives** to determine whether the claim with regard to her family was true, **and discovered** that the truth was **in accordance with her statement. He said to him: Go take possession of your acquisition. Happy are you that you were privileged** to marry a member of **the Dorketi family,** as those forms of blood will never pose a problem for you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "דּוֹרְקְטִי" — דּוֹר קָטוּעַ. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: תַּנְחוּמִים שֶׁל הֶבֶל נִיחֲמוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ. דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַשְּׂאוֹר יָפֶה לְעִיסָּה, כָּךְ דָּמִים יָפִים לָאִשָּׁה. וְתָנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁדָּמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין — בָּנֶיהָ מְרוּבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the meaning of **Dorketi?** It means **truncated generation [*dor katua*]. Rabbi Ḥanina said: Rabban Gamliel consoled that man with vain** words of **consolation,** because the absence of blood in this woman is a drawback. **As Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Just as leaven is fortuitous for dough, so** too, **blood is fortuitous for a woman. And** it was **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir: Any woman whose blood is plentiful, her children are plentiful.** This bride, who lacks blood, will not produce many children.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר. רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: "זְכֵה בְּמִקָּחֶךָ" אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר: "נִתְחַיַּיבְ[תָּ] בְּמִקָּחֶךָ" אֲמַר לֵיהּ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר "נִתְחַיַּיבְ[תָּ]" — הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר "זְכֵה", מַאי זְכוּתָא? דְּלָא אָתֵי לִידֵי סְפֵק נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a dispute with regard to Rabban Gamliel's reply. **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba said** that Rabban Gamliel **said to** the groom: **Exercise your privilege** and take possession **of your acquisition. And Rabbi Yosei bar Avin said** that Rabban Gamliel **said to him:** It is your **misfortune** to take possession **of your acquisition. Granted, according to the one who says:** It is your **misfortune, that is** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥanina,** who said the consolation was vain. **However, according to the one who says: Exercise your privilege, what is the privilege** to which he is referring? The Gemara answers: The privilege is **that** thanks to the condition of the women of this family, **he will not come to a situation of uncertainty** whether she has the halakhic status of a **menstruating woman.**

A man came before Rabban Gamliel ha-Zaken — the elder Rabban Gamliel, at the time of the *Beis Hamikdash* — with the claim: *ba'alti ve-lo matzati dam*. His wife responded: *Rabbi, mi-mishpaḥat Durkani ani* — I am from the Durkani family, *she-ein lahem lo dam niddah ve-lo dam besulim* — who have neither menstrual blood nor hymenal blood. She is asserting a hereditary physiological condition.

Rabban Gamliel investigated her relatives — *badak Rabban Gamliel bi-krovoseha* — and found that her account was accurate. He told the husband: *lech zaḥei be-mikḥacha* — go, take good possession of your purchase. He added: *ashrecha she-zaḥita le-mishpaḥat Durkani* — praised are you that you merited a woman from the Durkani family.

This last remark is challenged by Rav Ḥanina: *tanḥumin shel hevel niḥamo Rabban Gamliel le-oso ha'ish* — Rabban Gamliel offered that man empty consolation. The reason is stated by Rav Ḥiyya: *keshem she-has'or yafeh la-isah, kach damim yafim le-ishah* — just as yeast is beneficial for dough, blood is beneficial for a woman. Menstrual blood is a sign of fertility. A woman without such blood is, in a meaningful sense, like unleavened dough — the capacity for growth and new life is impaired. *Ve-harei zeh ke-nodos* — it is like an empty wineskin.

Furthermore, *tana mishum Rabbi Meir: kol ishah she-dameha merubim, baneha merubim* — any woman whose menstrual flow is abundant will have many children; she is highly fertile. The Durkani woman's condition thus raises serious questions about childbearing, making *ashrecha* a hollow blessing. Whether the phrase the *gemara* attributes to Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba — *zachei be-mikḥacha* — or the one attributed to Rabbi Yosef and Rabbi Avin — *niṣ-ḥayeiv be-mikḥacha* — you are stuck with your purchase — the tone differs markedly. The *gemara* resolves this by noting one genuine advantage: *de-lo asi lidei sefek niddah* — since she has no menstrual blood, there is never any question of *niddah* or doubt-*niddah* status. That is the grain of truth in Rabban Gamliel's blessing, though it is a thin consolation.

### Famine Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי בָּעַלְתִּי וְלֹא מָצָאתִי דָּם, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי עֲדַיִין בְּתוּלָה אֲנִי. וּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת הֲוָה, רָאָה רַבִּי שֶׁפְּנֵיהֶם שְׁחוֹרִים. צִוָּה עֲלֵיהֶן וְהִכְנִיסוּם לַמֶּרְחָץ, וְהֶאֱכִילוּם וְהִשְׁקוּם, וְהִכְנִיסוּם לַחֶדֶר. בָּעַל, וּמָצָא דָּם. אָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ זְכֵה בְּמִקָּחֶךָ. קָרֵי רַבִּי עֲלֵיהֶם: "צָפַד עוֹרָם עַל עַצְמָם יָבֵשׁ הָיָה כָעֵץ".
>
> **Translation:** **A certain** man **who came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: My teacher, I engaged in intercourse and did not find blood.** The bride **said to him: My teacher, I was still a virgin. And** the Gemara comments that this incident **was** during **years of drought. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **saw that their faces were black** due to hunger. **He instructed** his attendants to tend **to them and they took them into the bathhouse** and bathed them **and they fed them and gave them drink.** Then **they took them into a room,** and the groom **engaged in intercourse** with her **and found blood,** as it was due to the famine that there was no blood. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Go take possession of your acquisition. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **read** this verse **in their regard: "Their skin is shriveled upon their bones, it is withered, it has become like a stick"** (Lamentations 4:8), in the sense that no blood flows from them.

A man came before Rabbi — Rabban Gamliel's era having passed — with the same claim. The wife said she was still a virgin. Rabbi, however, observed that *sheni batzorot havei* — these were years of famine, and *ra'ah Rabbi she-pneihem sheḥorim* — he saw that their faces were blackened from malnutrition. He had them bathe, and then fed and gave them drink. Once they had recovered somewhat, he had them return to the bedroom, and this time *matzu dam* — blood was found. The verse Rabbi applied to them was *tzafad oram al atzmam, yavesh haya ka-etz* — their skin cleaved to their bones, it was as dry as wood.

Whether the absence of blood in the original consummation was due to the hymen failing to bleed because of extreme dehydration and malnourishment, or due to insufficient physical capacity for full penetration under conditions of severe physical debilitation, the *gemara* does not specify precisely. What is clear is the pastoral lesson: *lo matzati dam* in such circumstances reflects bodily conditions, not prior unchastity.

This sequence of cases — *petach patuach* dismissed as inexperience or promiscuity, the dirty sheet revealing hidden blood, the failed penetration exposed by the wine barrel test, and now famine undermining the very physical evidence — creates a cumulative picture. The theoretical framework giving the husband credibility is maintained in the books, but in practice rabbis worked carefully and creatively to arrive at just outcomes, and the *halacha*'s bark turns out to be considerably worse than its bite.

## Mishnah Categories

### Betulah vs. Almanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּתוּלָה — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מָאתַיִם, וְאַלְמָנָה — מָנֶה. בְּתוּלָה, אַלְמָנָה, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה מִן הָאֵירוּסִין — כְּתוּבָּתָן מָאתַיִם, וְיֵשׁ לָהֶן טַעֲנַת בְּתוּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a virgin, her marriage contract is two hundred dinars, and** with regard to **a widow,** her marriage contract **is one hundred** dinars. With regard to **a virgin** who is **a widow, a divorcée, or a *ḥalutza*** who achieved that status **from** a state of **betrothal,** before marriage and before consummation of the marriage, for all of these **their marriage contract is two hundred** dinars, **and they are subject** to **a claim concerning** their **virginity,** as their presumptive status of virginity is intact.

The *mishnah* now states the foundational categories: *betulah kesuvatah masayim* — a virgin receives a *kesuva* of 200 *zuz*; *ve-almanah maneh* — a widow, or more broadly a woman who is not a virgin, receives only 100 *zuz*. The biblical basis for the figure of 200 *zuz* has already been laid out in the preceding *gemara*: it derives from the *mohar ha-betulos*, the standard bridal payment for virgins in the Torah, which was 50 *shekel* or 200 *zuz*. For the *almanah*, as established above, there is no biblical *mohar* equivalent, and the 100 *zuz* is purely a rabbinic institution.

### Widow from Erusin

The *mishnah* adds that a woman who is an *almanah*, *gerushah*, or *ḥalutzah* — widow, divorcée, or one who has undergone *ḥalitza* — but whose prior marriage consisted only of *eirusin* (betrothal) and never reached *nisuin* (the consummated marriage stage), receives a *kesuva* of 200 *zuz* for her next marriage, not 100. This produces a seeming paradox: a woman who has been married before yet whose *kesuva* is 200. The rule is that *eirusin* alone, absent *nisuin*, does not change the presumption of virginity. Since *eirusin* in standard practice did not involve cohabitation — unlike in Yehuda where couples sometimes had relations after *kiddushin* — a woman who was betrothed and then separated before *nisuin* is still treated as a *betulah* and receives the full *kesuva* of 200 *zuz*.

### Ta'anas Betulim

The same logic that entitles her to 200 *zuz* also exposes her to *ta'anat betulim*: the husband's claim that she was not a virgin. The two sides of this coin are inseparable. Because the presumption is that she is a virgin — sufficient to grant her the full 200 *zuz kesuva* — the husband has a legitimate expectation of virginity. If on the morning after he claims either *petach patuach* or *lo matzati dam*, he cannot be told that he should have expected otherwise given her prior marital history, because *eirusin* alone does not change that presumption. He is therefore entitled to raise *ta'anat betulim* in such a case, with all the consequences that entails.

## Almanah Etymology

### Maneh Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי "אַלְמָנָה"? אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: "אַלְמָנָה" — עַל שֵׁם מָנֶה. אַלְמָנָה מִן הָאֵירוּסִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר! אַיְּידֵי דְּהָא קָרֵי לַהּ אַלְמָנָה, הָא נָמֵי קָרֵי לַהּ אַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is** the relationship between the term ***almana*** and its meaning, widow? **Rav Ḥana of Baghdad said:** A widow is called **an *almana* after** the ***maneh*,** one hundred dinars, which is the sum of her marriage contract. The Gemara asks: With regard to **a widow from betrothal,** whose marriage contract is two hundred dinars and not a *maneh*, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: **Since they called this** widow from marriage ***almana*, this** widow from betrothal **they also called *almana*.**

*Mai almanah?* — What is the origin of the word *almanah*? Rav Ḥana Bagdata'ah — possibly from Baghdad, though that city is typically dated somewhat later — offers an etymology: *almanah al shem maneh* — the word *almanah* derives from *maneh*, the hundred-*zuz* sum she receives. It is a functional etymology: she is called *almanah* because her *kesuva* is a *maneh*.

### Biblical Anticipation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה דִּכְתִיבָא בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דַּעֲתִידִין רַבָּנַן דִּמְתַקְּנִי לַהּ מָנֶה. וּמִי כָּתֵב קְרָא לְעָתִיד? אִין, דִּכְתִיב: "וְשֵׁם הַנָּהָר הַשְּׁלִישִׁי חִדֶּקֶל הוּא הַהוֹלֵךְ קִדְמַת אַשּׁוּר", וְתָנָא רַב יוֹסֵף: אַשּׁוּר זוֹ סְלֵיקָא. וּמִי הֲוַאי? אֶלָּא דַּעֲתִידָה. הָכָא נָמֵי דַּעֲתִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **That explains the use of *almana* in the terminology of the Sages. However, with regard to the term ***almana* that is written in the Torah, what is there to say?** The rabbinic ordinance that the marriage contract of a widow is a *maneh* was not yet instituted. The Gemara answers: The Torah employs the term *almana* **because the Sages are destined to institute** the sum of a ***maneh* for her** in her marriage contract. The Gemara asks: **And is a verse written for the future?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** indeed it is, **as it is written: "And the name of the third river is Tigris; that is it which goes toward the east of Asshur"** (Genesis 2:14). **And Rav Yosef taught: Asshur, that is Seleucia. And did** that city **exist** when the Torah was written? **Rather,** the Torah is referring to that city **because** it was **destined** to exist in the future. **Here too,** the Torah employs the term *almana* **because** a widow was **destined** to have a marriage contract of a *maneh* instituted for her.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: a woman who is an *almanah* from *eirusin* receives 200 *zuz*, not 100 — so the etymology cannot apply to her. The response is that because the standard *almanah* from *nisuin* is called *almanah* on account of her *maneh*, the same term is extended to the *almanah* from *eirusin* by association, even though her *kesuva* is 200.

But a deeper objection follows: *almanah di-chtiv be-Oraita mai ika lemeimar?* The word *almanah* appears throughout the Torah — long before any rabbinic institution of a 100-*zuz kesuva* existed. How can the Torah's use of the word be explained by a rabbinic institution that would not be created for a thousand years? The *gemara* answers: *de-atidin rabbanan de-matkenei lah maneh* — the Torah used the term *almanah* in anticipation of the future rabbinic enactment. And yes, the Torah does write with reference to future events: *ve-shem ha-nahar ha-shlishi Ḥidekel hu ha-holech kidmat Ashur* — the third river is the Ḥidekel, going east of Ashur. Rav Yosef teaches that *Ashur* here refers to Selika, a city that did not yet exist at the time of *Gan Eden*, when this verse was set. Thus the Torah names a future reality. Here too, the Torah called the widow *almanah* in anticipation of a future rabbinic institution.

## Aggadic Wordplay

### Rain Drashah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: "מָטָר" — מַשְׁקֶה, מַרְוֶה, וּמְזַבֵּל, וּמְעַדֵּן, וּמַמְשִׁיךְ. אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יֵימַר בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא, מַאי קְרָא: "תְּלָמֶיהָ רַוֵּה נַחֵת גְּדוּדֶיהָ בִּרְבִיבִים תְּמֹגְגֶנָּה צִמְחָהּ תְּבָרֵךְ".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥana of Baghdad said: Rain irrigates, saturates, and fertilizes** the land, **and refines** the fruit **and** causes it to **proliferate. Rava bar Rabbi Yishmael, and some say** it was **Rav Yeimar bar Shelamya** who **said: What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"Watering its ridges abundantly, settling its furrows, You make it soft with showers, You bless its growth"** (Psalms 65:11). "Watering its ridges abundantly" indicates that the rain irrigates and saturates the land, "You make it soft with showers" indicates that it fertilizes the land, and "You bless its growth" indicates that it refines the fruit and causes it to proliferate.

Since Rav Ḥana Bagdata'ah has been cited, the *gemara* brings several additional teachings in his name. On the word *matar* — rain — he offers an alliterative *drasha* on the letter *mem*: rain *mashkeh* — waters the ground; *meraveh* — sates it; *mezabel* — fertilizes it; *me'aden* — moistens it; and *mamshich* — draws out the fruit from the trees. Rava bar Yishmael, and some say Rav Yeimar bar Shelamya, identifies the scriptural basis in the verse *telameha raveh, naḥat gedudeha bi-rvivim, temogegenah bi-rviv, tzimḥah tevarech* — you will sate its furrows, bring down its banks with raindrops, make it fertile with drops, bless its sprouting.

### Altar Drashah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: "מִזְבֵּחַ" — מֵזִיחַ, וּמֵזִין, מְחַבֵּב, מְכַפֵּר. הַיְינוּ מְכַפֵּר, הַיְינוּ מֵזִיחַ! מֵזִיחַ גְּזֵירוֹת, וּמְכַפֵּר עֲוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** The term ***mizbe'aḥ*,** altar, is a rough acrostic representing its qualities. **It moves [*meziaḥ*]** sins **and sustains [*mezin*],** because as a result of the offerings sacrificed on the altar, sustenance is provided to all. It **endears [*meḥabev*],** and **atones [*mekhapper*].** *Mizbe'aḥ* evokes the letters *mem* and *zayin* from the first two qualities, *bet* from *meḥabev* and the *kaf* from *mekhapper*. The Gemara asks: **This** quality, that the altar **atones, is** the same as **that** quality, that it **moves** sins. Why are they listed separately? The Gemara answers: The altar **moves** evil **decrees, and atones** for **sins.**

Rabbi Elazar offers a similar alliterative treatment of the word *mizbe'aḥ* — altar. It *mazi'aḥ* — repels; *mazin* — sustains; *meḥabev* — makes Israel dear; and *meḥaper* — atones. The *gemara* asks whether *mazi'aḥ* and *meḥaper* are not simply the same thing — both suggesting the removal of sin. The *gemara* distinguishes them: *mazi'aḥ* refers to *mazi'aḥ gezeirot* — casting off evil decrees — while *meḥaper avonot* refers to atoning for sins themselves. Two distinct functions of the *mizbe'aḥ* are thus identified.

### Dates Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: תַּמְרֵי מְשַׁחֲנָן, מַשְׂבְּעָן, מְשַׁלְשְׁלָן, מְאַשְּׁרָן וְלָא מְפַנְּקָן. אָמַר רַב: אָכַל תְּמָרִים אַל יוֹרֶה. מֵיתִיבִי: תְּמָרִים, שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית — יָפוֹת, בְּמִנְחָה — רָעוֹת. בַּצׇּהֳרַיִם — אֵין כְּמוֹתָן, וּמְבַטְּלוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים: מַחְשָׁבָה רָעָה, וְחוֹלִי מֵעַיִם, וְתַחְתּוֹנִיּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥana of Baghdad said: Dates warm and satiate, loosen the bowels, strengthen, but do not pamper. Rav said:** If **one ate dates he should not issue** halakhic **rulings,** as dates are intoxicating. The Gemara **raises an objection:** With regard to **dates,** in the **morning and evening** they have a **positive** effect on one who eats them; **in the afternoon,** they have a **negative** effect on one who eats them. **At noon, their** positive effect is **unparalleled, and they negate three matters: A troubling thought, intestinal illness, and hemorrhoids.** Apparently, the effect of dates is primarily a positive one.

Rav Ḥana Bagdata'ah then expounds on *tamrei* — dates. They *meshachnan* — warm the body; *mesav'an* — sate it; *meshalshelan* — act as a laxative; *me'ashran* — cause contentment; and *lo mefantan* — do not make one overly fastidious or spoiled.

Rav rules: *ekhol tamrei al yoreh* — if one has eaten dates, one should not issue *halachic* rulings, since they were thought to cloud thinking somewhat. The *gemara* challenges this from a teaching that dates in the morning and evening are beneficial, at midday they are unparalleled, and only at *Mincha* time — on an empty stomach in the afternoon — are they inadvisable. But clearly dates have many benefits; why should they impair judgment?

The resolution is that the goodness of dates for the body — *ilu'ei ma'alu* — is not in question, but *le-fi sha'ata tarda* — at the moment of eating, they temporarily cloud one's thinking. They are analogous to wine in this respect: wine is beneficial, yet *ha-shoteh revi'it yayin al yoreh* — one who has drunk even three fluid ounces of wine should not issue rulings. A further resolution notes that the timing relative to a meal is decisive: *ha mikamai nahama, ha batar nahama* — the concern applies before bread, not after. Abaye's nursemaid taught him: *tamrei mikamai nahama ke-narga le-dikula* — dates before a meal are like a hatchet to a palm tree, destructive; *batar nahama ke-avra le-dasha* — after a meal they are like a bolt on a door, strengthening and beneficial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן דְּלָא מְעַלּוּ? עַלּוֹיֵי מְעַלּוּ, וּלְפִי שַׁעְתָּא טָרְדָא. מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַחַמְרָא. דְּאָמַר מָר הַשּׁוֹתֶה רְבִיעִית יַיִן — אַל יוֹרֶה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — מִקַּמֵּי נַהֲמָא. הָא — לְבָתַר נַהֲמָא. דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אֲמַרָה לִי אֵם: תַּמְרֵי מִקַּמֵּי נַהֲמָא — כִּי נַרְגָּא לְדִיקּוּלָא. בָּתַר נַהֲמָא — כִּי עָבְרָא לְדַשָּׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that there is no contradiction. **Did we say that they are not exemplary? They are exemplary, and** at the same time **cause temporary distraction** and **intoxication, just as it is** in the case **of wine, as the Master said: One who drinks a quarter***-log* **of wine should not issue** halakhic **rulings. And if you wish, say** instead: This apparent contradiction is **not difficult. This** statement, which prohibits issuing a ruling under the influence of dates, is referring to one eating dates **before** he eats **bread,** when eating them can lead to intoxication. **That** statement, which enumerates the salutary effects of dates, is referring **to** one eating dates **after** he eats **bread. As Abaye said: My mother told me** that **dates** eaten **before** eating **bread** are destructive **like an ax to a palm tree;** dates eaten **after** eating **bread** are beneficial **like a bolt to a door,** which provides support.

### Final Wordplay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "דַּשָּׁא", אָמַר רָבָא: דֶּרֶךְ שָׁם. "דַּרְגָּא" — אָמַר רָבָא: דֶּרֶךְ גַּג. "פּוּרְיָא" — אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁפָּרִין וְרָבִין עָלֶיהָ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַף אָנוּ נֹאמַר: "אַיְלוֹנִית" — דּוּכְרָנִית, דְּלָא יָלְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to the word ***dasha*,** door, **Rava said:** It is an acrostic for ***derekh sham*,** meaning through there. **With regard to the word ***darga*,** ladder or stair, **Rava said:** It is an acrostic for ***derekh gag*,** meaning way to the roof. **With regard to the word ***purya*,** bed, **Rav Pappa said:** It is an acrostic for ***parin veravin aleha*,** meaning one procreates upon it. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: We too will say: *Ailonit*,** a sexually underdeveloped woman, is a term meaning: Like a **ram [*dukhranit*], because** like a male sheep [*ayyil*] **she does not bear children.**

The *gemara* concludes with a series of wordplays. *Dasha* — a door — Rava explains as *derekh sham*, the path in. *Darga* — a step — is *derekh gag*, the way to the roof. *Puraiya* — a bed — Rav Papa explains as the place where *parin ve-ravin*, one procreates and multiplies. Finally, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak adds: *af anan nami neima* — let us similarly expound — *ilonit*, the term for a woman who has not physically matured and cannot bear children, is *dukhranita de-lo yadla* — like a male ram that cannot give birth. The *gemara* thus concludes this aggadic interlude with a series of playful but pointed observations about language, physiology, and the vocabulary through which the Sages described human life and its conditions.
