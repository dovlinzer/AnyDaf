# Zevachim 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Altar Dimensions

### Blood vs. Ketores

We pick up four lines into daf 60a, at the words *u'le'Rabbi Yehuda*. We are in the middle of a debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose, which was brought into the discussion in connection with the ruling that a chipped *mizbeach* prevents *shechita* of a *korban*. The question was raised: if the inner golden altar is absent, can the *ketoret* still be offered? The answer is that blood-related *avodah* is categorically different. The blood requires the *mizbeach*; the burning of incense does not. This distinction will now be connected to a parallel debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose regarding what happened in the time of Shlomo.

### R. Yehuda: Wide Altar

The debate concerns the relative size of Shlomo's altar compared to the copper *mizbeach* of the *mishkan*. Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse describing the *mishkan*'s copper altar — which states dimensions of five by five by three — as meaning ten by ten, with the five measured from the center outward, and only three *amot* in height. This makes the altar low and very wide. According to this reading, even the new altar built by Shlomo was not proportionally much larger than Moshe's, so when an enormous volume of sacrifices was required on a particular day, they had to sanctify the floor of the *azarah* to burn the *emurim* there. The burning of the *emurim* requires a large surface area, and that is precisely why the dimensions of the altar's top were the focus: you need room to burn a great many entrails. The blood work, by contrast, does not require the same expanse — you perform a *zerika*, step aside, and proceed. The *mizbeach* structure is therefore always sufficient for blood, even when it is insufficient for the *emurim*.

### R. Yose: Tall Altar

Rabbi Yose reads the very same verse differently. He holds that the altar was five by five, but ten *amot* tall — the "three" in the verse refers to the height of the latticed grating in the middle of the structure. On this reading the *mishkan* altar was narrow and tall, and Shlomo's new altar was therefore a substantial improvement in size. According to Rabbi Yose, the ground of the *azarah* was never sanctified to serve as an altar; the new altar was fully adequate.

This creates an immediate difficulty. If the altar was ten *amot* tall and a *kohen* is approximately three *amot* to his shoulders, the *kohen* standing atop the altar would have his shoulders at six *amot* off the ground — yet the surrounding curtains were only five *amot* high. The *kohen* would be visible above the curtains, and this was considered deeply inappropriate, a violation of the intimacy and sanctity proper to the *avodah*. Why would curtains be erected at all if they scarcely obscured anything?

### Privacy Resolution

Rabbi Yose's answer requires a rereading of the verse about the curtain height. Since the verse compares the courtyard curtains to those of the *ohel moed*, and the *ohel moed*'s own curtains reached ten *amot* to the height of its structure with an additional five *amot* extending beyond, these curtains too were not five but fifteen *amot* — ten up to the top of the altar and five beyond. This is how the *kohen* performed the *avodah* in privacy.

What then of Rabbi Yehuda's reading, where the curtains are taken literally as five *amot* and the altar is only three *amot* high? A *kohen* standing on a three-*amot* altar would have his shoulders at six *amot*, still above the five-*amot* curtains. The *Gemara* addresses this directly: *nihi dikohan mit'chazi, avoda d'beyado lo mit'chazi*. It is true that the *kohen* himself was visible from the chest up, but the actual *avodah* — the work of his hands, the arrangement of the limbs — could not be seen. The visibility of the person does not undermine the sanctity; it is the visibility of the sacred acts that matters, and those remained hidden.

### Solomon Context

Having resolved the internal difficulties of each position, the *Gemara* now returns to the event in Shlomo's time. The verse states that on that day the king sanctified the courtyard because the copper *mizbeach* was too small. The problem is that by Shlomo's time a new altar had already been built; why does the verse speak of the copper *mizbeach* as if it were still the relevant altar?

### Sanctification Interpret…

Two readings emerge, each reflecting the underlying position on altar dimensions.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, even the new altar that Shlomo built was insufficient for the scale of the sacrifices brought on that day. The verse refers to the new altar as "the copper *mizbeach*" because it was the copper altar's replacement — it stood in for it. But since even this substitute was too small, they sanctified the actual floor of the *azarah* to function as an altar. On Rabbi Yehuda's reading, then, *kidesh* means that the courtyard ground itself was consecrated to serve as a sacrificial surface, and *mizbeach hanechoshet* refers not to the original copper altar but to its stone successor.

According to Rabbi Yose, the courtyard was sanctified not to function as an altar but to provide a site for the construction of a new altar — *l'ha'amid bo mizbeach*. The old copper altar was too small, and Shlomo consecrated the courtyard space in order to erect something adequate. The word *kidesh* therefore means he prepared the space for the new structure, not that the floor itself became an altar.

The *Gemara* then works through the textual consequence of each reading. According to Rabbi Yose, the text's mention that the old altar was "too small" (*katan*) makes sense: the old altar was inadequate, so a new one was needed. But according to Rabbi Yehuda, the same word *katan* is puzzling, because even the new altar was too small — so the *Gemara* explains: *mizbeach avanim she'asa Shlomo tachat mizbeach hanechoshet katan hu*, the stone altar that Shlomo built as a substitute for the copper altar was itself too small, and that is why the courtyard had to be sanctified.

### Hermeneutical Basis

What underlies the disagreement about the altar's shape? The debate turns on which analogical connection one prefers for the word *ravua* (square) as it applies to the copper *mizbeach* of the *mishkan*.

Rabbi Yehuda holds *danim chutz michutz v'ein danim chutz mipnim*: we learn an outer altar from an outer altar. He links the *mishkan*'s copper altar to the altar described in Yechezkel, where *ravua* means measured from the center outward — yielding a ten-by-ten structure, low and wide.

Rabbi Yose holds *danim kli mikli*: we prefer to connect one vessel to another. The copper altar of the *mishkan* was movable and thus had the status of a vessel; it is therefore better compared to the golden incense altar, which was also a vessel. For the golden altar, *ravua* implied that the height was double the width. Applied to the copper altar, this yields a structure that is tall and narrow. Rabbi Yose adds *v'ein danim kli mibinyan*: one should not compare a vessel to a permanent structure, and therefore linking the copper altar to the altar of Yechezkel — which was a fixed architectural structure — is the less appropriate inference.

## Blood Requires Altar

### Rava: Blood Necessity

Having established the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose, the *Gemara* now addresses how far Rabbi Yehuda's principle extends. If the floor of the *azarah* can function as an altar for burning *emurim*, does the same apply to blood work? Rava states clearly: *modeh Reb Yehuda b'damim*. Whatever flexibility Rabbi Yehuda grants for the burning of entrails and incense, he concedes that the blood work — the *zerika* that gives the *korban* its validity — requires an actual *mizbeach*.

### Pesach Blood Proof

The proof comes from a *braita*. Rabbi Yehuda himself taught that on *Pesach*, when the volume of sacrifices was so great that blood inevitably spilled onto the floor, one would fill a vessel with blood gathered from the mixture on the floor — a mixture that might include blood from a *korban* whose vessel had been properly filled but then spilled before reaching the altar — and pour that collected blood onto the *mizbeach*. The reasoning: if someone's blood had been caught properly in a vessel but spilled before being thrown, this mixture, thrown on the altar, would validate it. *V'shofcho gabei mizbe'ach* — they would pour it on the base of the altar.

### Floor Theory Refuted

This teaching proves that the floor does not have altar-status for blood. The *Gemara* presses: *v'ei salka daitach savar Reb Yehuda kula Azara mikdasha* — if Rabbi Yehuda truly held that the entire *azarah* functions as an altar, why bother gathering the spilled blood and carrying it to the *mizbeach*? For the *korban Pesach* in particular, whose blood merely needs to be "spilled" (*v'damam shel zevachecha yishpach*), one who tripped and spilled the blood onto the *azarah* floor should have fulfilled the obligation right there, if the floor had altar-status. The fact that the *braita* prescribes gathering the blood and bringing it to the altar demonstrates that the floor does not function as a *mizbeach* for blood.

### Koach Adam Question

The *Gemara* attempts to deflect this proof: *v'dilma mishum d'kasvar shfichah mikoach adam beinan* — perhaps the floor does have altar-status for blood, but the reason one cannot simply leave the spilled blood on the ground is that the act of sprinkling must be performed through deliberate human agency. An unintentional spill, caused by tripping, does not constitute a valid *zerika* regardless of where the blood lands.

This raises a genuinely fascinating question about what constitutes *koach adam*. Even in other contexts — such as *netilat yadayim* — there is debate about whether water flowing through a faucet, where one's agency is indirect, qualifies as *koach gavra*. Here too: if one trips and the vessel tilts over, is that not still the *kohen*'s energy, even if unintentional? Perhaps the requirement is not intentionality per se but directed agency — the act must in some meaningful sense be *his* act, not the floor's.

The *Gemara* then presses further: if the only issue with the spilled blood was the absence of *koach adam*, why does the *braita* instruct one to gather the blood and pour it on the *mizbeach*? Why not simply gather it and re-pour it right back onto the *azarah* floor — this time intentionally? The *Gemara* responds: *v'dilma mishum d'bainan mitzvah min hamuvchar* — perhaps one simply prefers the better form of the *mitzvah*. If the floor had altar-status for blood, one could theoretically throw blood directly on the floor and satisfy the requirement, yet one chooses the altar for the superior manner of fulfillment.

### Tosafot: Blood Obvious

The *Gemara*'s willingness to entertain these alternative explanations is notable, because *Tosafot* register sharp surprise at the premise of the discussion. On the phrase *modeh Reb Yehuda b'damim*, *Tosafot* ask: *teima mai kasalka daiteich me'ikara* — how could one have imagined for even a moment that blood work does not require an actual *mizbeach*? The entire *masechet* has been replete with requirements of physical altar structure: *keren*, the horn of the altar; *arbuah*, its square shape; *yesod*, its base. All of these structural requirements — and the *Gemara* has repeatedly made clear that they are indispensable — make it inconceivable that blood could simply be thrown on the ground. The novelty of Rava's statement, says *Tosafot*, lies entirely in the other half of the principle: that the *emurim* and the *ketoret* do *not* require an altar. That is the *chiddush*; the blood's dependence on the *mizbeach* was never in question.

### Blood Theology

This principle — that blood is the exclusive focus of what makes a *korban* valid, while the burning of entrails and incense does not require a *mizbeach* — is a striking theological claim. The stories of *korbanot* throughout the Torah emphasize the burning, the ascending smoke, the *rei'ach nicho'ach*. Think of Noach building an altar, of Kayin and Hevel — it is what is placed on the fire and ascends that seems to carry the weight of the offering. Even in *Vayikra*, the Torah describes what is done with the blood, what is done with the *emurim*, what is done with the various parts, without obviously privileging one over the other.

Yet *Chazal* throughout this *masechet* have consistently placed blood at the absolute center. The conceptual foundation is a single *pasuk*: *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper, va'ani netativ lachem al hamizbeach lechaper al nafshoteichem* — for the blood, through the life-force it carries, effects atonement, and it is for this reason that I have given it to you upon the altar to atone for your souls. Even this verse does not unambiguously establish blood as the *sole* vehicle of atonement; it could be read as identifying blood as one important component. Yet it is this verse that animates the entire *masechet*'s governing principle: it is the blood, and the blood alone, that is *me'akev*. One could, in theory, have burned the entrails on the ground of the *azarah*. All that elaborate burning — and the *Gemara* is prepared to say that none of it needed a structure. Only the blood required the altar.

## Chipped Altar

### Chipped Invalidates

The *Gemara* returns to the ruling of Rabbi Elazar: a *mizbeach* that has become chipped (*nifgam*) not only prevents valid *zerika* but also retroactively affects the *shechita*. This is derived from the verse *vezavachta alav* — the slaughtering must be performed while the *korban* is "upon" a valid altar, meaning the altar must be whole at the time of *shechita*. If the *mizbeach* was intact during *shechita* but became chipped before *zerika*, and one then repaired the altar, the *korban* remains invalid because the *shechita* and the altar's integrity must coexist.

Given that we have established blood work as the defining requirement of the altar, one might ask about related acts that are not themselves blood work but are connected to it. What, for instance, about eating the *shiyarei mincha* — the remnant of the *mincha* consumed by the *kohanim*? And beyond that, what about eating the meat of various categories of *korbanot*?

### Mincha Remnant

The *Gemara* rules: *mizbeach shenifgam ein ochlin bigino shi'arei mincha* — when the *mizbeach* is chipped, one may not eat the remnant of the *mincha*. This is not immediately about blood; it is about the consumption of the *mincha* by the *kohanim* after the *kometz* has been offered. That this eating should be connected to the altar's wholeness requires explanation.

### Mincha Verse Link

The source is a verse from the *milu'im*, where Moshe instructs Aharon and his sons: *ve'achlu hamatzot etzel hamizbeiach ki chen tzuveiti* — eat the unleavened bread beside the altar, for so have I commanded. Does this mean they literally had to stand next to the altar? Of course not — they had the entire *azarah* in which to eat. Rather, the verse's invocation of the altar is interpreted as establishing a conceptual and legal connection: the eating of the *mincha* remnant is contingent upon the altar's integrity, just as the *shechita* is contingent upon the altar's integrity. *Ella bizman shehu shaleim velo bizman shehu chaser* — only when the altar is whole, not when it is lacking.

This has an important implication for the taking of the *kometz* as well. If the eating of the remnant is tied to the altar's wholeness, then certainly the *kemitzah* — the act of taking the handful, which is the *mincha*'s parallel to *shechita* — is similarly dependent on an intact *mizbeach*. And note the striking scope: not only is the preparatory act (*kemitzah*) and the core act (burning the *kometz*) connected to the altar, but even the *kohanim*'s eating afterward is tied to it. Once the *korban* has been offered, one might assume the eating is simply a benefit unrelated to the altar's status. The *Gemara* says otherwise.

### Kodshei Kodashim

From the *mincha* the *Gemara* extends the principle to *kodshei kodashim* — offerings of the highest sanctity such as the *chatat* and the *asham*, whose meat is eaten by the *kohanim* within the *azarah*. The source is the continuation of the same verse: *ki kodesh kodashim hi* — for it is holy of holies. Anything classified as *kodesh kodashim* stands in this same relationship to the altar.

This is a significant claim about the nature of the *kohanim*'s eating. One might have understood their consumption of *kodshei kodashim* meat as simply their form of material support — God's generosity toward those who serve in the *Mikdash*, an instance of *mishulchan gavoah kazchu*, receiving from the divine table. But the *Gemara* draws on a deeper principle: *kohanim ochlim uba'alim mitkaprim* — the *kohanim* eat and the owners achieve atonement. The *kohanim*'s eating is not merely a benefit; it is an extension of the atonement process itself, a continuation of what the *mizbeach* accomplishes. Their consumption is, in a meaningful sense, the *mizbeach*'s consumption. It follows that just as the *mizbeach* must be whole for the blood and the burning, it must be whole for the eating.

This produces a striking and even counterintuitive result within Rabbi Yehuda's own framework: according to Rabbi Yehuda, the burning of *emurim* does not require an intact *mizbeach* — it can be performed on the *azarah* floor. Yet the eating of the meat does require an intact *mizbeach*. The eating is, on this analysis, more tightly bound to the *mizbeach* than the burning of entrails. That is genuinely surprising.

## Kodshim Kalim Need Altar

### Kodshim Kalim Scenario

If the above holds for *kodshei kodashim*, whose consumption takes place within the *azarah* in close proximity to the altar and involves the *kohanim* in their sacral role, one might consider it intuitive. But the *Gemara* now extends the principle to *kodshim kalim* — offerings of lesser sanctity eaten by the owners themselves anywhere within *Yerushalayim*. Here the connection to the *mizbeach* is far less obvious. Once the blood has been properly sprinkled and the *korban* validly brought, and one is sitting at home somewhere in *Yerushalayim* eating the meat — what possible relevance does the *mizbeach*'s physical integrity have?

The scenario the *Gemara* contemplates: the *korban* was brought properly, the blood was thrown, and subsequently the *mizbeach* was chipped. Must one stop eating the *kodshim kalim* meat? The *Gemara*'s answer is yes — even this eating requires the *mizbeach* to be whole.

### Abaye Source

Abaye derives this ruling from the same *drashah* of Rabbi Yose that transmits a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Yose is cited as having taught three things in the name of three different sages; the relevant teaching here was said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael.

### Ishmael Ma'aser

Rabbi Yishmael poses the following question: perhaps one should be permitted to bring *ma'aser sheni* to *Yerushalayim* and eat it there in the present era — that is, after the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The question arises because *ma'aser sheni* is not brought to the *Beit HaMikdash* itself; it is eaten in *Yerushalayim*. If the sanctity of the land of Israel for agricultural obligations (*terumot u-ma'asrot*) remains in force today — a position that itself requires a separate determination — then the sanctity of *Yerushalayim* might similarly persist, and one could theoretically bring *ma'aser sheni* to the city and eat it there even without a standing *Beit HaMikdash*.

Rabbi Yishmael proceeds to reject this possibility. The governing principle: *bekhor ta'un hava'at makom u-ma'aser ta'un hava'at makom* — both *bechor* and *ma'aser* require being brought to a specific place, namely *Yerushalayim*. Just as *bechor* can only be brought when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands — since it is a *korban* requiring *matan damim* and *emurim* on the *mizbeach* — so too *ma'aser* can only be eaten when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands.

### Bechor-Ma'aser Link

The obvious objection: *mah l'bechor shekein ta'un matan damim v'imurim legabei mizbeach* — the *bechor* obviously requires a *Beit HaMikdash* because it is a *korban*. Blood is thrown on the altar and *emurim* are burned there. *Ma'aser sheni* is not a *korban* at all; it is simply food eaten in *Yerushalayim*. Why should it require a *Beit HaMikdash*?

The *Gemara* attempts to rescue the analogy by invoking *bikurim*, which also requires the *Beit HaMikdash* — one brings the first fruits to the *Mikdash* and places them before the altar, as the verse states *v'hinachto lifnei mizbach Hashem Elokecha*. However, this too fails: *mah l'bikurim shekein te'unim hanacha* — *bikurim* requires placement before the altar, and *ma'aser* does not. And the attempt to learn from a *mah hatzad*, a common denominator of *bechor* and *bikurim*, is also rejected: *mah l'tzad hashaveh shebahen shekein yesh bahen tzad mizbeach* — both *bechor* and *bikurim* involve an altar-related act, so naturally both require a *Beit HaMikdash*. *Ma'aser sheni*, which has no altar-related act, cannot be included in the inference.

The only available source is therefore a direct verse. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* groups all of these together: *v'havasem shama olosaychem v'zivchaychem v'eis ma'asrosaychem v'eis trumas yedchem u-vchorot bkarchem v'tzonchem v'achaltem sham lifnei Hashem Elokechem* — bring there your burnt offerings, your sacrifices, your *ma'asrot*, your *terumot*, your *bechorot*, and eat them there before the Lord your God. The verse packages *ma'aser* together with *bechor* in the same directive to come to *Yerushalayim*. From this shared context, *mekish ma'aser l'bechor*: *ma'aser* is linked to *bechor*, and just as *bechor* requires a *Beit HaMikdash*, so does *ma'aser*.

Conceptually, the connection makes sense. *Ma'aser sheni* must be eaten *lifnei Hashem* — in the presence of God. This is not merely a geographic requirement of being within *Yerushalayim*'s walls; rather, *Yerushalayim* derives its character of being "before God" from the presence of the *Beit HaMikdash* at its center. Being anywhere in the city is a way of being in the divine presence because the city's entire identity is defined by the *Beit HaMikdash* within it. Without the *Beit HaMikdash*, the designation *lifnei Hashem* loses its grounding.

### Kedusha vs. Structure

This raises a question that the *Gemara* addresses but does not fully resolve. What exactly is the obstacle to eating *ma'aser sheni* nowadays — is it the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash*'s physical structure, or is it the absence of the *kedushah* of *Makom HaMikdash*?

The *Gemara* frames this as a question about what Rabbi Yishmael assumes: *ei kasavar kedushah rishonah kidsah le-sha'atah ve-kidsah le-atid lavo* — if he holds that the original sanctification of *Yerushalayim* and the *Mikdash* was for all future times, then even today the place retains its sanctity and one could theoretically bring a *bechor*, since *makrivin af al pi she-ein bayit* — offerings may be brought even without a standing structure, as long as the place retains its sanctity (along with a *mizbeach*, since we have established that blood requires one). In that case the analogy between *ma'aser* and *bechor* would be undermined, because *bechor* can still be brought today.

The alternative is that Rabbi Yishmael holds the sanctity did not endure — *lo kidsah le-atid lavo*. In that case the question about *bechor* is not whether it requires a physical structure but whether the *kedushah* of the place exists at all.

*Ravina* clarifies: *le'olam kasavar lo kidsah le-atid lavo* — Rabbi Yishmael consistently holds that the *kedushah* of *Makom HaMikdash* does not persist after the *churban*. The question about *bechor* that the *Gemara* raises is therefore the following: the *Gemara* then focuses the discussion on a case where the *bechor*'s blood was sprinkled before the *churban* but the meat remained to be eaten afterward.

## Bechor Post-Churban

### Blood-Meat Timeline

The *Gemara* now investigates a hypothetical that is conceptually precise even if historically strained: *bebechor shenizrak damo kodem churban habayit vecharav habayit* — a *bechor* whose blood was sprinkled before the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, after which the Temple was destroyed, and the meat still remained to be eaten. The *bechor* is eaten over two days and a night; imagine the blood was thrown on the eighth of *Av*, and on the ninth of *Av* the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed. The *kohen* is now somewhere in *Yerushalayim* with the remaining meat. May he eat it?

This case is parallel in structure to the scenario of a chipped *mizbeach*: the *korban* was validly initiated — the blood was properly sprinkled — but the condition that enables eating has subsequently been compromised. The question is whether the eating of the meat is so tightly bound to the altar's presence that its destruction terminates permission to eat, or whether valid *zerika* having already occurred, the eating may continue.

---

## Bechor Post-Churban (II)

### Bechor Linkage

The resolution comes from a verse. The Torah states regarding the *bechor*: *ve'es damam tizrok al hamizbeach ve'es chelbam taktir u-vsaram yiheyeh lach* — throw their blood on the altar, burn their fat, and the flesh shall be yours. The verse links blood, *emurim*, and flesh in a single sentence. From this, *ve'itkash besaro ledamo* — the flesh is made legally equivalent to the blood through *hekesh*.

### Meat Requires Altar

*Mah dam bemizbeach af basar bemizbeach* — just as the blood requires a *mizbeach*, so does the flesh. The eating of the *bechor*'s meat is not merely a benefit that flows from valid *shechita* and *zerika*; it is a continuation of the same *avodah* complex that is tied to the *mizbeach*'s presence. If the *Beit HaMikdash* is destroyed and the *mizbeach* is gone, the meat may not be eaten even though the blood was already validly thrown.

---

## Chipped Altar (II)

### Meat-Blood Link

This same verse — linking *emurim*, blood, and flesh together in the context of *bechor* — provides the exegetical foundation for the broader principle: the eating of meat parallels the blood work. The flesh's dependence on the *mizbeach* is not derived independently or by mere analogy; it is derived directly from the same verse that establishes the blood's requirement of the altar. Blood and flesh are thus presented as a unified legal unit, not as two separate requirements that happen to converge on the same object.

This point will become significant in the discussion that follows, where the *Gemara* confronts a structural problem in the chain of reasoning.

---

## Hekesh Resolution

### Hekesh Limitation

Having established that the flesh of the *bechor* is linked to the blood (*itkash besaro ledamo*), the *Gemara* proceeds to apply this to *ma'aser sheni*: the flesh requires a *mizbeach*, and therefore by analogy so does *ma'aser*. But the *Gemara* raises the classic objection: *v'chi davar halamed behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh* — a matter derived by *hekesh* cannot itself serve as the source for a further *hekesh*. If the *bechor*'s flesh-requirement is itself derived from the blood by *hekesh*, can that derived rule then teach us something about *ma'aser sheni* through a second *hekesh*? The principle prohibiting double *hekesh* inference would seem to block this.

### Unity Resolution

The *Gemara* answers: *dam u-vasar chad milsa hi* — the blood and the flesh of a *korban* are not two separate matters that happen to be linked; they are a single legal entity. The verse does not derive the flesh-requirement from the blood-requirement by *hekesh* in the traditional sense. Rather, it reveals that flesh and blood together constitute one unit, and it is this unified blood-and-flesh unit that stands in relation to *ma'aser sheni* through a single *hekesh*. There is accordingly no "double" inference: the connection from the blood-flesh unit to *ma'aser* is a single step, and the principle against chained *hekesh* does not apply.

## Moving Camp

### Yirmiya Critique

When Ravin went up from *Bavel* to *Eretz Yisrael* and presented this entire discussion — the *bechor* scenario, the *ma'aser sheni* derivation, the link between flesh and blood — before Rabbi Yirmiyah, Rabbi Yirmiyah responded with his characteristic phrase: *Bavla'ei tipsha'ei, deyasvi be'ara dechashocha, amri shemaysa demachshacha* — those foolish Babylonians, who dwell in a dark land, say murky teachings. He maintained that the Babylonian scholars had worked through an elaborate derivation to establish something that could have been learned directly from a straightforward *baraita*.

### Camp Sanctity

The *baraita* Rabbi Yirmiyah cites: *besha'at siluk masaot* — at the time the camp was in motion and the *mishkan* was being dismantled for travel — *kodshim nitzolim*, one could not eat the *korbanot*. Yet *vezavim u-metzoraim mishtalchim chutz lemechitzah* — the *zavim* and *metzoraim* were still expelled beyond the camp boundary. This last detail is critical: the expulsion of *zavim* and *metzoraim* outside the camp demonstrates that the camp retained its sanctity and its camp-status even while moving. The *machaneh shechinah*, the *machaneh leviyah*, and the *machaneh Yisrael* all remained legally constituted; a *zav* who wandered into *machaneh Yisrael* was still in violation, a *metzora* had to remain outside, and so on.

Yet *kodshim nitzolim* — the *korbanot* could not be eaten. The *kedushah* of the camp persisted, but eating was prohibited. The reason can only be that the eating of *korbanot* requires not merely the *kedushah* of the camp but the standing of the *mizbeach* in a fixed location. While the altar was in transit — disassembled, being carried — it was as though the *mizbeach* were absent, even though the camp retained its sanctity. This maps precisely onto the cases we have been discussing: it is the structure, not merely the *kedushah* of the place, that governs the eating of *kodshim*.

### Kodshei vs. Kodshim

However, the *Gemara* then raises a second *baraita* that states *beshnei mekomot kodshim ne'echalin* — that *kodshim* may be eaten in two distinct situations, which the *Gemara* interprets as including both when the camp is stationary and when it is in motion. This seems to directly contradict the first *baraita*.

The resolution: *mai lav hai bekodshei kodashim hai bekodshim kalim* — the first *baraita* refers to *kodshei kodashim* and the second to *kodshim kalim*. *Kodshei kodashim*, eaten within the *azarah* by the *kohanim* in the context of the *mizbeach*'s service, requires the *mizbeach* to be standing in place. When the camp moves and the *mizbeach* is in transit, the eating of *kodshei kodashim* is prohibited. But *kodshim kalim*, eaten by the owners throughout the camp (parallel to eating in *Yerushalayim*), does not require the *mizbeach* to be fixed in place. The owners may continue eating even as the camp moves.

### Ma'aser Parallel

This distinction maps naturally onto *ma'aser sheni*: eating *ma'aser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* does not require the *mizbeach* to be standing, in the same way that eating *kodshim kalim* does not require the *mizbeach* to be standing. What *ma'aser sheni* requires is not the physical presence of the *mizbeach* but the *kedushah* of *Makom HaMikdash* — the sanctity of the place as expressed through the verse *lifnei Hashem Elokecha*. On this analysis, the question of whether one may eat *ma'aser sheni* nowadays turns not on whether the *mizbeach* is standing but on whether *Makom HaMikdash* retains its *kedushah*. This is precisely the question that Charlie raised at the outset of the *ma'aser sheni* discussion, and which the *Gemara* here implicitly addresses by distinguishing between the altar-presence requirement applicable to *kodshei kodashim* and the *kedushah*-of-place requirement applicable to *kodshim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni*.

## Blood Spill Dispute

### Rav Papa Setup

The *Gemara* now presents an alternative version of the entire dispute. Rav Papa reframes where exactly the disagreement lies. According to this version, when the *mizbeach* itself is destroyed, there is no dispute: all authorities agree that one may not eat the meat. The eating is dependent on the *mizbeach*'s existence, and when it is gone, the permission to eat is terminated. The disagreement, says Rav Papa, arises only in a case where the blood was spilled — that is, the *shechita* was performed but the blood was not successfully sprinkled on the altar.

### Bavel Position

The scholars of *Bavel* hold that eating the meat is dependent on the blood having been sprinkled on the *mizbeach*. If the blood was spilled and never reached the altar, the meat is forbidden regardless of whether the *shechita* itself was valid. The act of valid *zerika* is a condition for the permissibility of eating, not merely a requirement of the *avodah* process.

### EY Position

The scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* hold otherwise: eating the meat depends on the validity of the *shechita*, not on whether the blood was ultimately sprinkled. Once the animal has been slaughtered in a valid manner, the meat is in principle permitted to eat, and the failure of the *zerika* — whether through spillage or some other cause — does not retroactively prohibit it.

This is the second version of the debate between *Bavel* and *Eretz Yisrael*. Where the first version concerned the presence or absence of the *mizbeach* as a structure, this version concerns the relationship between *zerika* and eating: is the *kohen*'s act of throwing the blood a prerequisite for the *bechor*'s meat, or is valid *shechita* alone sufficient to permit it?
