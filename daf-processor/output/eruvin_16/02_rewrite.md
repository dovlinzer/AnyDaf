# Eruvin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Paratz K'Omed Debate

### Rav Papa vs Rav Huna

The daf begins by picking up from the bottom of 15b, where the *mishna* described a caravan that encamped just before *Shabbos* and constructed walls from the baggage and equipment loaded on their animals. The *mishna* established that such an arrangement is valid provided the gaps in the improvised walls do not exceed the standing portions. Since the caravan is assumed to have enclosed all four sides, no *kora* is required. This led the *Gemara* into a sustained discussion of what the law is when the gaps and the standing portions are exactly equal — a case known as *paratz k'omed*.

Two Amoraim dispute this question. Rav Papa holds that a fifty-fifty split is acceptable, while Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua holds it is not. The *Gemara* frames the issue with the striking phrase *hachi agmurei machr'na l'Moshe* — "this is what God taught Moshe" — reflecting the notion that the underlying principle is a *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### Criteria Definition

The dispute between Rav Papa and Rav Huna turns on a fundamental question of legal formulation: is the criterion stated in terms of what is required for validity, or in terms of what renders something invalid? If one says that for the arrangement to be *kosher* the majority must be walled, then fifty-fifty fails to meet that positive threshold — that is Rav Huna's position. If, on the other hand, one says the arrangement is invalid only when the majority is exposed, then fifty-fifty does not trigger invalidity — that is Rav Papa's position.

This tension between positive and negative formulations recurs across *halacha*. The positive formulation assumes the default is invalid and requires proof of validity; the negative formulation assumes the default is acceptable and requires proof of invalidity. The two framings sound equivalent, but they diverge precisely at the boundary case of exact equality.

### Etrog Analogy

A useful parallel: an *etrog* must be *hadar* — beautiful and aesthetically pleasing. One could say the *etrog* is *kosher* only if it is *hadar*, or alternatively, the *etrog* is invalid if it is not *hadar*. These formulations sound identical, yet they diverge in the middle ground between clearly beautiful and clearly not beautiful. The positive formulation demands that beauty be demonstrated; the negative formulation demands that its absence be demonstrated. That gap between the two is precisely the contested territory in the *paratz k'omed* debate.

### Mishna Language

The *Gemara* begins its attempts to resolve the debate by turning to the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* states: *v'lo yehu pirtzot yiterot al habinyan* — the gaps should not exceed the structured portion. The implication of this formulation, *ha k'binyan mutar*, is that if the gaps are exactly equal to the standing part, it is permissible — a straightforward proof for Rav Papa. The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: do not read the *mishna* as saying that equal is permissible; rather, read it as saying that it is only permissible when the standing portion exceeds the gaps. If so, what is the law when they are equal? Forbidden. But then, the *Gemara* objects, the *mishna* should have said that the gaps may not equal the standing portion, not that they may not exceed it. The *Gemara* acknowledges this as a genuine difficulty — *kashya* — and concedes that the plain sense of the *mishna* supports Rav Papa. The deeper question that runs through the entire discussion is whether the various *mishnayot* and *braitot* are precise enough in their language to resolve a case of exact equality, or whether they were simply not concerned with that edge case.

## Sukkah S'chach Proof

### S'chach Equal Gaps

The *Gemara* turns to a *mishna* in *Masechet Sukkah* for a further proof. The case there involves alternating strips of valid *s'chach* with invalid material — such as metal bars or bed frames, which are either a *kli* or not grown from the ground. The *mishna* rules: *im yesh revach beinehem kemosan, kesherah* — if the gap between the valid *s'chach* is equal to the width of the valid *s'chach*, the *sukkah* is valid. This appears to be a direct proof for Rav Papa: when equal, it is acceptable.

The *Gemara* is not entirely comfortable applying reasoning about roofs to reasoning about walls, particularly since the law governing *s'chach* is ordinarily framed in terms of *tzilaso merubah mechamaso* — that the shade must exceed the sunlight. Whether the proportions of valid to invalid material on top necessarily map to shade below is not straightforward. Yet the *Gemara* proceeds to engage with the proof on its own terms.

### Rav Ami Response

The *Gemara* deflects the proof by explaining: *keshenichnas veyotze* — what the *mishna* means when it says "equal" is not literally equal, but rather that a strip of valid *s'chach* of the same width can be slid easily into the gap. If it slides in easily, the gap must in fact be slightly larger than the *s'chach* being inserted. The "equality" is therefore only apparent; the gap is truly a bit wider. One might ask: doesn't a slightly larger gap make things worse, since there is now less valid *s'chach*? The answer is that the invalid material is fixed in place; the gap is the space where the valid *s'chach* will be inserted. The point is simply that "equal" is being read as "just slightly more than equal" — and thus the *mishna* does not establish that exact equality is permissible.

The *Gemara* then asks: but it is possible to measure exactly, so why would the *mishna* speak in terms of easy insertion rather than precise equality? Rav Ami answers: *bema'adif* — the intention is that the craftsman deliberately makes the gap slightly larger in order to have room to insert an equal width of valid *s'chach*. The *mishna* is not describing a precisely equal case; it is describing the practical outcome of intentionally enlarging the gap.

### Rava Direction

Rava offers an alternative explanation. *Im hayu nesunim erev, nosen shesi; shesi nosen erev* — if the invalid material was laid widthwise, the valid *s'chach* is inserted lengthwise, and vice versa. By inserting the valid *s'chach* perpendicular to the invalid material, the valid strip rests upon and overlaps the edges of the invalid strips, which means it covers slightly more width than the gap itself. The proof for Rav Papa therefore fails: the *mishna* in *Sukkah* is not actually describing a case of precise equality. The broader observation stands that the sukkah discussion is somewhat puzzling, since the *Gemara* seems to bypass the particular concerns about shade that usually govern *s'chach*, but that is the substance of the *Gemara*'s resolution here.

## Caravan Gaps

### Camel Measurements

The *Gemara* brings yet another source — a *braita* closely related to the *mishna* itself. The case: *shayara shechantah bevikah* — a caravan encamped in a valley — and the travelers surrounded themselves using camels, saddles (*uchafos*), and the cloth or pillow inserts that sit beneath the saddles (*avitim*, which Rashi explains as cushioned padding rather than the chamber pots that word can elsewhere denote), as well as saddlebags and luggage. The conditions specified are: *shelo yihiyeh bein gamal legamal kimlo gamal* — between one camel and the next, the gap may not equal the width of a camel; similarly between one saddle and the next, and between one pillow and the next. This formulation sounds like a direct proof for Rav Huna: when the gap equals the standing portion, it is invalid.

### Easy Insertion

The *Gemara* defends Rav Papa with the same answer deployed above: *hacha nami keshnichnas veyotze* — here too, the *braita* means not literally equal, but a gap big enough that an object of the same width can be easily inserted. The stated criterion of "equal to a camel's width" therefore implies the gap is actually somewhat larger. The *Gemara* is thus consistently reinterpreting all of these sources as dealing not with the precise fifty-fifty case but with a gap that is functionally a bit beyond equal, all in service of keeping the debate alive and protecting Rav Papa's position from being definitively refuted.

## Kilayim Categories

### Category 1: <3

The *Gemara* now introduces an extensive *Tosefta* discussing *kilayim* — the prohibition against planting different species together. A *mechitza* can permit adjacent planting of different species, so the *Tosefta* defines three categories of *mechitzot* with different legal outcomes.

Category one: *kol shehu pachot mishlosha* — where the sections of the wall are each less than three *tefachim*. In this case the requirement is that the gaps between sections also be less than three *tefachim* — *shelo yihiyeh bein zeh lezeh shlosha, kedei shelo yizdaker hagadi bevat rosh*, so that a kid goat cannot pass through easily, which is the operative definition of *lavud*. The key insight here is that if the individual wall sections are less than three *tefachim*, they can in principle be very small indeed — even a sliver — because the principle of *lavud* treats the gap as closed when it is less than three. The section itself need not be large; as long as each gap is less than three *tefachim*, the entire structure is treated as continuous.

### Category 2: 3-4

Category two: *migimmel v'ad daled* — where the sections of the wall are between three and four *tefachim*. The rule here is *tzarich shelo yihiyeh bein zeh lezeh kimlo'o* — the gap may not equal the size of the section — and the *Tosefta* explicitly uses the phrase *kedei shelo yihiyeh parutz ke'omed*, stating that it is in order that the gap not equal the standing portion. This formulation appears to label *parutz ke'omed* as invalid and seems to directly support Rav Huna.

But then the *Tosefta* continues: *im hayah parutz merubeh al ha'omed, af keneged ha'omed assur* — if the gap exceeds the standing portion, then even the area directly opposite the standing partition itself is forbidden. This creates a significant tension: three formulations of the *Tosefta* say the problem begins when the gap is equal, but this line says the problem arises when the gap is greater. It also raises the question of what distinguishes category two from category one beyond the size of the sections. Here, when the gaps are too large, nothing is permissible — not even the area directly opposite the partition — because each wall section is less than four *tefachim* and therefore does not have independent halachic significance. A partition that is less than four *tefachim* wide does not stand on its own; when the overall structure fails, it all fails.

### Category 3: 4-10

Category three: *me'arba tefachim ad eser amot* — where the partition sections are from four *tefachim* up to ten *amot*. Again the *Tosefta* states that the gap may not equal the section — *shelo yehei parutz k'omed* — and that if it does equal the partition, the area directly opposite the gap is forbidden. But here it adds: *k'neged ha'omed muttar* — opposite the standing partition, it remains permissible. This is the crucial distinction from category two. A section of four *tefachim* or more has independent significance; even when isolated from adjacent sections by an oversized gap, it stands on its own and permits planting opposite it. This mirrors the general principle that a space of four *tefachim* constitutes a *makom chashuv* — a defined, significant location — in contrast to anything smaller.

### Equal Rejected

Tallying the language of the *Tosefta*, three times it phrases the invalidity in terms of the gap equaling the partition (*parutz k'omed*), while once it phrases it in terms of the gap exceeding the partition (*parutz merubeh al ha'omed*). The *Gemara* observes: *tani miat* — the primary point is the formulation found in the case of sections between three and four *tefachim*, where the gap cannot equal the section. This seems a compelling proof against Rav Papa. The *Gemara* declares: *tiyuvta d'Rav Pappa* — a definitive refutation of Rav Papa.

### Rav Papa Defense

Rav Papa responds: *mai lav nichnas veyotze* — once again, when the *Tosefta* says "equal is forbidden," it does not mean precisely equal, it means a gap large enough to easily accommodate an object of equal size, which implies the gap is actually slightly larger than equal. The *Gemara* then notes that this reading is itself supported by an internal tension in the *Tosefta*: one of the four formulations says the problem arises when the gap is greater, not equal. *Hachi nami mistavra* — this actually supports Rav Papa, because that one line uses *parutz merubeh* rather than *parutz k'omed*, suggesting the real threshold is greater-than-equal, and the repeated use of "equal" in the other formulations is somewhat imprecise. Every side can thus point to at least one line in the *Tosefta* that is inconvenient for the other position.

The *Gemara* then considers whether the line supporting Rav Papa should be read as a decisive refutation of Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua. He in turn deflects: *u'l'ta'amecha, eima seifa* — even according to you, look at the final line of the *braita*: *im hayah omed merubeh al haparutz, af k'neged haparutz mutar* — if the standing part is greater than the gap, then even opposite the gap is permissible. The implication, *ha k'farutz assur*, is that when equal, it is forbidden. So almost all lines of this *Tosefta* support Rav Huna. The one line that uses *merubeh* is an imprecision, a consequence of the *Tosefta*'s wanting to phrase the favorable outcome (*omed merubeh... mutar*) symmetrically with the unfavorable one stated earlier (*parutz merubeh... assur*). The *Gemara* acknowledges that the difficulty goes both ways: *seifa kashya l'Rav Pappa, reisha kashya l'Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua*. Neither reading of the *Tosefta* is perfectly consistent.

### Categories Not Combined

The *Gemara* raises an additional internal question about the *Tosefta* itself: why are categories one and two kept separate? Under Rav Papa's reading, the case of sections exactly three *tefachim* wide belongs in category two, not category one, because when the section is exactly three, *lavud* cannot be invoked (that requires less than three), and the gap may nonetheless be exactly three *tefachim* wide (since for Rav Papa equal is permissible). That is a genuine distinction from the case of sections less than three *tefachim*, where the gap must be less than three for *lavud* to apply. So Rav Papa can explain the separate categories: at exactly three *tefachim*, the gap can be as large as three, whereas at less than three *tefachim*, the gap must be under three. *Bishlema l'Rav Pappa, mishum hachi lo arev lehu* — this is precisely why the *Tosefta* keeps them separate.

But according to Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua, the gap always has to be strictly less than the standing portion, so whether the section is exactly three or slightly under three, the gap must be under three in both cases. Why then does the *Tosefta* treat them as distinct categories? The *Gemara* answers: *mishum delo dami pesula d'reisha l'pesula d'seifa* — because the rationale for invalidity differs. In category one, the gap must be less than three in order to invoke *lavud*, the principle that treats a gap as closed — *kedei shelo yizdaker hagedi b'vat achas*. In category two, the gap must be less than the partition size for the entirely different reason that the gap may not equal the standing portion — *shelo yehei parutz k'omed*. Thus even under Rav Huna's view, the categories are legitimately distinct, since the underlying principle shifts from *lavud* to *parutz k'omed*.

### Abaye Significance

A further internal question: the *Tosefta* treats sections of three *tefachim* differently from sections of four or more, giving independent significance only to the latter. This distinction seems to align with the view of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that *lavud* applies to gaps of less than four *tefachim* rather than the standard three, thereby making four the threshold of significance. Does the *Tosefta* therefore follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rather than the Rabbis?

Abaye resolves the difficulty: *mide'reisha Rabbanan, seifa nami Rabbanan* — since the first category manifestly follows the Rabbis (who use three *tefachim* as the threshold for *lavud*), the later categories also follow the Rabbis. The two questions — when does *lavud* apply, and when does a partition have independent significance — are governed by different standards. The Rabbis say *lavud* applies when the gap is less than three *tefachim*. Separately, they hold that for a partition to be halachically significant when standing alone — *keneged ha'omed muttar* — it must cover at least four *tefachim* of space. This is consistent with the general rule that a space of less than four *tefachim* is a *makom p'tor*, a halachically neutral zone; conversely, a partition covering four *tefachim* creates a defined significant space. The two thresholds operate independently: three *tefachim* for *lavud*, four *tefachim* for independent significance.

### Rava Attribution

Rava offers an alternative resolution. He attributes the *Tosefta* in its entirety to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — not to the Rabbis. The end of the *Tosefta*, which gives significance to four *tefachim*, is indeed Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position since he holds *lavud* applies within four *tefachim*, making four the threshold of significance. As for the beginning, where less-than-three gaps must apply even though Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *lavud* extends to four — Rava explains: *hani mili le'malah*, this is above, where one invokes *lavud* to connect a beam to a wall or similar elevated constructions. But *aval lemata*, when dealing with ground-level *mechitzot*, the concern is *mechitzah shehagedi'im bok'in bah* — a partition through which kid-goats can pass. Even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would agree that a gap of three *tefachim* at ground level cannot be called a partition since goats can freely pass through it. Thus the three-*tefach* standard in category one reflects the goat-passage criterion, not the *lavud* principle, and the four-*tefach* threshold in categories two and three reflects Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's measure of significance.

The *Gemara* notes that this approach has difficulties with the precise language of the *Tosefta*, and the resolution is left somewhat open. The discussion of the *kilayim* case thus concludes without fully settling the question of *parutz k'omed*.

## Courtyard Openings

### Equal Forbidden

One final attempt. The *Gemara* cites a ruling about a courtyard whose walls are mostly openings and windows: *sheruban pesachim vachalonos, mutar, uvilvad she'omed merubeh al haparutz* — if most of the wall is gaps and openings, it may nonetheless serve as a valid enclosure provided the standing portions exceed the gaps. The *Gemara* quickly clarifies the opening phrase: one cannot literally say that most of the wall is gaps and then conclude it is permissible; rather, the case is that there are many openings distributed across multiple walls, yet the overall standing portions exceed the openings. The implication is clear: *ha k'farutz, assur* — if equal, it is forbidden. The *Gemara* treats this as a definitive refutation: *tiyuvta d'Rav Pappa tiyuvta*.

### Mishna Supports Pappa

Immediately following this declaration, the *Gemara* states: *vehilchasa kevassia d'Rav Pappa* — the ruling follows Rav Papa. The *Gemara* then raises the obvious objection: *tiyuvta vehilchasa?* — you just contradicted him and now you rule like him? The answer: *in, mishum dedayka masnisin kevassia* — yes, because our own *mishna* strongly implies his position. The *mishna* that opened this entire discussion stated *lo yihyu p'ratzos yeseiros al habinyan* — the gaps may not exceed the standing portion. The implication, *ha k'binyan mutar*, is that equal is permissible. This is precisely Rav Papa's view. The evidence from the various *braitot* is contradictory, and the *Tosefta* is imprecise in its formulations; the *Gemara* concludes that neither the *mishnayot* nor the *braitot* were focused on the precise fifty-fifty case. Given the contradictory state of the evidence, the *Gemara* returns to the *mishna* from which the discussion began and rules accordingly: when gaps equal the standing portion, it is permissible.

It is worth noting a comment from *Tosafot* at this point. *Tosafot* observes the apparent tension: here we rule that *parutz ke'omed muttar*, while in the second chapter of *Chullin* the *Gemara* concludes that all opinions agree that *mechtza al mechtza eino ke-rubo* — fifty-fifty is not equivalent to a majority. *Tosafot* suggests that one may distinguish between *mechitzot* and the domains of *issur* and *tumah*. The general principle of *rubo ke-kulo* — treating the majority as if it were the whole — requires an actual majority of fifty-one percent or more when the claim is that an act is fully accomplished (as in the case of *shechita* of the trachea and esophagus). By contrast, the question regarding *mechitzot* is not whether we treat the entire perimeter as walled, but whether enough of it is walled to confer the status of an enclosure. For that purpose, fifty percent may suffice without invoking the *rubo ke-kulo* principle at all.

## Three-String Walls

### String Construction

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which addresses a more minimalist form of *mechitza* made from strings or posts — constructions that rely entirely on *lavud* for their validity. The *mishna* states: *makifin sheloshah chavalim zeh lema'ala mizeh* — one may create an enclosure using three ropes strung one above another. The condition is that the gap between one rope and the next may not be three *tefachim*, so that *lavud* can treat each gap as closed.

### String Width Requirement

Additionally, the *mishna* specifies: *shiur chavalim ve-ovyan yeter al tefach* — the combined width of all three ropes together must exceed one *tefach*. The practical purpose of this requirement is to ensure that the total height reaches ten *tefachim*. If each of the three gaps is slightly under three *tefachim* — say 2.9 *tefachim* — and there are three gaps, the total gap distance is 8.7 *tefachim*. The combined thickness of the ropes must therefore account for the remaining 1.3 *tefachim* or more to bring the total to ten *tefachim*. This is achieved by using thick straps or ropes whose widths add up to over one *tefach*, as the *mishna* requires.

The *mishna* similarly allows posts — *makifin b'kanim* — provided the gaps between posts are less than three *tefachim*, applying *lavud* in the horizontal rather than the vertical direction.

### Caravan Only

The *mishna* then records a dispute over the scope of this leniency. *Beshayara dibru, divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *mishna*'s permission to use strings or posts in place of proper walls was spoken only in the context of a caravan. Normally, Rabbi Yehuda requires that a *mechitza* run in both directions — *sheti va'erev*, the warp and the woof — and he permits this minimalist approach only for travelers stranded in the wilderness without building materials, as a special leniency born of necessity.

### Sages Always OK

The Sages disagree: *lo dibru beshayara ela behoveh* — they described the caravan case because it was a common situation, but the principle is general. One may always create a valid *mechitza* using strings or posts arranged in a single direction, relying on *lavud*, without needing both vertical and horizontal elements. This is what permits the practice in the context of a *sukkah* or other enclosures as well. A clear window into the history of the teaching emerges here: an early *mishna* recorded the *halakha* in the context of a caravan, and later *tannaim* debated whether the caravan was a limitation or merely a commonly described case.

The *mishna* also records a further position attributed to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yehuda's son, who states: *kol mechitza she'eina shel sheti v'shel erev eina mechitza* — any *mechitza* that does not run in both the warp and woof directions is not a valid *mechitza*. He goes further than his father: Rabbi Yehuda permitted single-direction walls for caravans; Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda denies their validity in all circumstances. The Sages conclude: *echad mishnei dvarim* — either direction, vertical or horizontal, is sufficient; one need not employ both.

## Horizontal Gaps Question

### Rav Hamnuna Question

The *Gemara* now takes up a question posed by Rav Hamnuna in the name of Rav, connecting the earlier discussion of *parutz k'omed* to the new *mishna*'s material. The Sages stated that the principle of *omed merubeh al haparutz* — the standing portion exceeding the gaps — applies in the vertical direction (*besheti*, the warp). Rav Hamnuna asks: *ba'erev mahu* — what is the law when the majority principle is applied in the horizontal direction, the woof? In other words, if a wall has gaps running not vertically but horizontally — planks on the bottom and top with a gap in the middle — is it sufficient that the combined height of the planks exceeds the height of the gap, even without invoking *lavud*?

### Three-String Proof

Abaye attempts to resolve this question from the *mishna* about three strings. The *mishna* requires that the combined thickness of the ropes exceed one *tefach* — *shiur chavalim ve-ovyan yeter al tefach*. If one could simply rely on horizontal majority (*omed merubeh al haparutz* in the woof direction), why would additional rope thickness be necessary at all?

### String Width Necessity

The reasoning: suppose one argued that it is enough to have a majority of solid material in the vertical plane even when the gaps are not individually subject to *lavud*. One could then construct three ropes with gaps of, say, 2.9 each — totaling 8.7 *tefachim* of gap — and with the ropes themselves providing a smaller combined measurement, provided the solid ropes outweigh the gaps. Why does the *mishna* insist on the ropes being thick enough to contribute more than one *tefach* to the total height? The *Gemara* explores whether this *mishna* can actually be made into a workable proof for the majority-in-the-woof question.

The difficulty is that the three possible scenarios for placing a large horizontal gap are all problematic. If the large gap is at the bottom — *ei mokimt leh tata'ei* — then we have *mechitzah shehagedi'im bok'in bah*, a partition through which goats can pass at ground level, which is invalid. If the large gap is at the top — *ei mokimt leh ila'ei* — then *ati avira d'hai gisa u'd'hai gisa u'mevatel leh*: the airspace on either side of the rope nullifies its effect. A rope surrounded by open space above and below is treated as nonexistent; unlike a case where *lavud* has been invoked and the gap is treated as closed, an actual open gap means the rope floats in void and counts for nothing.

### Mat Scenario

Since the scenario cannot be constructed using strings, the *Gemara* reformulates Rav Hamnuna's question around a different case. Take a mat (*machtzelet*) that is seven *tefachim* and a bit in height. Cut an opening of three *tefachim* in the middle of it, leaving a solid portion of four *tefachim* and a bit above the gap. Stand the mat with its bottom edge within less than three *tefachim* of the ground, so that *lavud* closes the space between the mat's base and the ground.

The resulting structure: *lavud* gives you a virtual solid from the ground up to the bottom of the mat, roughly 2.9 *tefachim*; the mat itself has 4.1 *tefachim* of solid above the gap. The gap of three *tefachim* in the middle is surrounded by solid portions above and below. The total is just over ten *tefachim*. The solid portions — 2.9 plus 4.1 — equal seven *tefachim*, which exceeds the gap of three *tefachim*. So the *omed* is indeed *merubeh al haparutz*. Does this constitute a valid *mechitza*?

The concern about *avira d'hai gisa u'd'hai gisa u'mevatlei* — airspace on either side nullifying the rope — does not apply here, since the opening is cut into a substantial physical mat rather than being a thin rope suspended in space. Yet the question remains whether a partition with a central gap qualifies as a *mechitza* even when the solid portions together form a majority.

The *Gemara* attempts to work through the arithmetic of this case and notes that the scenario only makes sense if one already assumes that the solid portions on two sides can be combined — *omed merubeh al haparutz mishtei ruchot* — which is precisely the point under debate. The *Gemara* concludes that the premise of the question has not been established independently of the conclusion.

### Air Space Problem

The crux of the concern with suspended walls is that a *mechitza* through which goats can pass is not a *mechitza*, and a rope or thin material suspended between two airspaces — below and above — suffers from the structural problem that the surrounding void effectively eliminates it. This is *mechitzah tluya* — a suspended partition — and the *Gemara* identifies this as the true form of Rav Hamnuna's question: can a suspended partition function as a valid enclosure in a case involving a *churva*, an open ruined area of dry land?

### Rav Ashi Water Rule

Rav Ashi provides a clear answer to that specific question: *ein mechitzah tluya materes ela bemayim* — a suspended *mechitza* is effective only over water. *Kal hu shehikilu chachamim bemayim* — it is a specific leniency that the Sages granted with respect to water. Over dry land, a partition that is suspended above the ground — not reaching down to within three *tefachim* of the surface — cannot function as a *mechitza*, since animals and people can simply pass beneath it. The broader question of whether one can rely on horizontal majority in the woof direction is noted but left without a definitive answer by the *Gemara*.

## Ropes Caravans

### Yehuda Restriction

The *Gemara* returns to the *mishna*'s dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages regarding the scope of the rope-and-post leniency. Rabbi Yehuda said it was spoken only in the context of a caravan — *beshayara, v'ein beyachid* — implying it does not work for individuals at all. But a *braita* seems to show that Rabbi Yehuda did permit it for individuals, at least up to a certain size.

### Braitot Limits

The *braita* states: *kol mechitzos shabbos lo hetiru leyachid yoser mibeis sasayim* — for a *Shabbos mechitza* of this minimal type, an individual is granted no more than a *beis sasayim*, five thousand square *amos*. The *braita* further distinguishes: a single individual (*yachid*) may use up to a *beis sasayim*; two people also get a *beis sasayim*; once there are three people they become a *shayara*, a caravan, and receive *beis shesh* — six *se'ah* of space, essentially fifteen thousand square *amos*.

### Nachman Interpretation

Rav Nachman (and some attribute this to Rav Bibi bar Abaye) reconciles the apparent contradiction. When the *mishna* records Rabbi Yehuda as saying this works only for a caravan, it means unlimited usage. For a caravan, there is no upper limit on the enclosed space. For an individual, there is a limit — the *beis sasayim* — but the minimal rope-and-post construction does work within that limit. So Rabbi Yehuda's restriction in the *mishna* is not an absolute prohibition for individuals; it is a restriction on unlimited usage.

### Yose Warp-Woof

The same reconciling principle is then applied to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda. His statement in the *mishna* — *kol mechitza she'eina shel sheti v'shel erev eina mechitza* — sounds like an absolute rejection of single-direction walls. But a *braita* reveals that Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda actually permits them, only with limits: *echad yachid ve'echad shayara, lechavalim* — whether an individual or a caravan, ropes may be used. The difference is that for a *yachid* the space is capped at a *beis sasayim*, and for a *shayara* of three or more it is capped at *beis shesh*. When Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda said in the *mishna* that a single-direction *mechitza* is invalid, he meant it is invalid for unlimited use, not that it is categorically unusable.

### Sages Unlimited

The Sages take the permissive position: *echad yachid ve'echad shayara, nosnin lahem kol tzarchan* — whether for an individual or a caravan, they may enclose as much space as they need.

### Empty Space Rule

The one qualification the Sages add is: *uvilvad shelo yehei beis sasayim panui* — the enclosed area may not contain an unused empty space exceeding *beis sasayim*. If the people and their equipment actually occupy the enclosed area, the enclosure may be as large as needed. What is prohibited is creating an enormous enclosure with a large portion of it — more than a *beis sasayim* — lying entirely empty and unused. As long as the space serves the needs of the people who erected it, the minimal rope-and-post *mechitza* is fully valid according to the Sages, without any upper limit, and that is the ruling we follow.
