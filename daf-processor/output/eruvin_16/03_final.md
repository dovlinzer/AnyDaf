# Eruvin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Paratz K'Omed Debate

### Rav Papa vs Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, פָּרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד — רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מוּתָּר, רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is stated** that the *amora'im* disagree about the case where the **breached** segment of the partition **equals the standing** portion. **Rav Pappa said: It is permitted** to carry within that enclosure. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: It is prohibited.**

The daf begins by picking up from the bottom of 15b, where the *mishna* described a caravan that encamped just before *Shabbos* and constructed walls from the baggage and equipment loaded on their animals. The *mishna* established that such an arrangement is valid provided the gaps in the improvised walls do not exceed the standing portions. Since the caravan is assumed to have enclosed all four sides, no *kora* is required. This led the *Gemara* into a sustained discussion of what the law is when the gaps and the standing portions are exactly equal — a case known as *paratz k'omed*.

Two Amoraim dispute this question. Rav Papa holds that a fifty-fifty split is acceptable, while Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua holds it is not. The *Gemara* frames the issue with the striking phrase *hachi agmurei machr'na l'Moshe* — "this is what God taught Moshe" — reflecting the notion that the underlying principle is a *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### Criteria Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר מוּתָּר: הָכִי אַגְמְרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמֹשֶׁה — לָא תִּפְרוֹץ רוּבָּה. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר אָסוּר: הָכִי אַגְמְרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמֹשֶׁה — גְּדוֹר רוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Pappa said: It is permitted. This is what the Merciful One taught Moses: Do not breach the majority** of the partition; as long as the greater part is not breached, it is considered a partition. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: It is prohibited. This is what the Merciful One taught Moses: Circumscribe the greater** part; if the greater part is not enclosed, it is not a partition.

The dispute between Rav Papa and Rav Huna turns on a fundamental question of legal formulation: is the criterion stated in terms of what is required for validity, or in terms of what renders something invalid? If one says that for the arrangement to be *kosher* the majority must be walled, then fifty-fifty fails to meet that positive threshold — that is Rav Huna's position. If, on the other hand, one says the arrangement is invalid only when the majority is exposed, then fifty-fifty does not trigger invalidity — that is Rav Papa's position.

This tension between positive and negative formulations recurs across *halacha*. The positive formulation assumes the default is invalid and requires proof of validity; the negative formulation assumes the default is acceptable and requires proof of invalidity. The two framings sound equivalent, but they diverge precisely at the boundary case of exact equality.

### Etrog Analogy

A useful parallel: an *etrog* must be *hadar* — beautiful and aesthetically pleasing. One could say the *etrog* is *kosher* only if it is *hadar*, or alternatively, the *etrog* is invalid if it is not *hadar*. These formulations sound identical, yet they diverge in the middle ground between clearly beautiful and clearly not beautiful. The positive formulation demands that beauty be demonstrated; the negative formulation demands that its absence be demonstrated. That gap between the two is precisely the contested territory in the *paratz k'omed* debate.

### Mishna Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְלֹא יְהוּ פִּירְצוֹת יְתֵרוֹת עַל הַבִּנְיָן. הָא כַּבִּנְיָן — מוּתָּר?! לָא תֵּימָא הָא כַּבִּנְיָן מוּתָּר, אֶלָּא אִם בִּנְיָן יָתֵר עַל הַפִּירְצָה — מוּתָּר. אֲבָל כַּבִּנְיָן, מַאי — אָסוּר? אִי הָכִי לִיתְנֵי ״לֹא יְהוּ פִּירְצוֹת כַּבִּנְיָן״! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **And there will not be breaches** in the partition **greater** than **the built** segment. Only then would carrying be permitted in the enclosed area. By inference, **if** the breaches **equal the built** segment, **it is permitted.** This presents a difficulty for Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua. The Gemara responds: **Do not say:** By inference if they **equal the built** segment, **it is permitted; rather, say: If the built** segment **is greater than the breach, it is permitted** to carry in the enclosed area. The Gemara continues: **However,** according to that way of understanding the mishna, if the breach **equals the built** segment, **what** is the *halakha*? Is carrying **prohibited? If so, let** the mishna **teach** that carrying is permitted, provided that the **breaches do not equal the built** segment. It can be inferred from this that if the breaches are greater than the built segment, it is certainly prohibited. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this poses **a difficulty** to the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.

The *Gemara* begins its attempts to resolve the debate by turning to the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* states: *v'lo yehu pirtzot yiterot al habinyan* — the gaps should not exceed the structured portion. The implication of this formulation, *ha k'binyan mutar*, is that if the gaps are exactly equal to the standing part, it is permissible — a straightforward proof for Rav Papa. The *Gemara* immediately pushes back: do not read the *mishna* as saying that equal is permissible; rather, read it as saying that it is only permissible when the standing portion exceeds the gaps. If so, what is the law when they are equal? Forbidden. But then, the *Gemara* objects, the *mishna* should have said that the gaps may not equal the standing portion, not that they may not exceed it. The *Gemara* acknowledges this as a genuine difficulty — *kashya* — and concedes that the plain sense of the *mishna* supports Rav Papa. The deeper question that runs through the entire discussion is whether the various *mishnayot* and *braitot* are precise enough in their language to resolve a case of exact equality, or whether they were simply not concerned with that edge case.

## Sukkah S'chach Proof

### S'chach Equal Gaps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְקָרֶה סוּכָּתוֹ בְּשַׁפּוּדִין אוֹ בַּאֲרוּכּוֹת הַמִּטָּה, אִם יֵשׁ רֶיוַח בֵּינֵיהֶן כְּמוֹתָן — כְּשֵׁירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof to support Rav Pappa's opinion. **Come** and **hear** that which the mishna taught about the *halakhot* of *sukka*: With regard to **one who roofed his *sukka* with** metal **skewers or with bed posts,** both of which are unfit for *sukka* roofing because they are susceptible to ritual impurity, **if there is space between them, equal to their** width, filled with materials valid for *sukka* roofing, the *sukka* **is valid.** Apparently, with regard to roofing, if the valid materials equal the invalid, the *sukka* is valid. Similarly, if the built segment of an enclosure equals the breached segment, it is a valid enclosure for the purpose of carrying on Shabbat. This supports Rav Pappa's opinion against that of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.

The *Gemara* turns to a *mishna* in *Masechet Sukkah* for a further proof. The case there involves alternating strips of valid *s'chach* with invalid material — such as metal bars or bed frames, which are either a *kli* or not grown from the ground. The *mishna* rules: *im yesh revach beinehem kemosan, kesherah* — if the gap between the valid *s'chach* is equal to the width of the valid *s'chach*, the *sukkah* is valid. This appears to be a direct proof for Rav Papa: when equal, it is acceptable.

The *Gemara* is not entirely comfortable applying reasoning about roofs to reasoning about walls, particularly since the law governing *s'chach* is ordinarily framed in terms of *tzilaso merubah mechamaso* — that the shade must exceed the sunlight. Whether the proportions of valid to invalid material on top necessarily map to shade below is not straightforward. Yet the *Gemara* proceeds to engage with the proof on its own terms.

### Rav Ami Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן: כְּשֶׁנִּכְנָס וְיוֹצֵא. וְהָא אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בְּמַעֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara contests this conclusion. **With what are we dealing here?** It is with a case **where** the skewers can be **inserted and extracted** easily. In other words, the case of the mishna in *Sukka* is not one where there are equal amounts of valid and invalid roofing. It is referring to a case where there is additional space between the skewers, which allows for their easy insertion and removal. Consequently, the space filled by the valid roofing is greater than that filled by the skewers. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it possible to be precise?** Couldn't the mishna in *Sukka* be understood as describing a case where the gaps between the skewers equal the width of the skewers? That understanding supports the opinion of Rav Pappa, who maintains that when the valid segment precisely equals the invalid segment, the whole is valid. **Rabbi Ami said:** This mishna is referring to a case **where one adds** roofing, so that the area of the valid roofing is greater than that of the skewers.

The *Gemara* defects the proof by explaining: *keshenichnas veyotze* — what the *mishna* means when it says "equal" is not literally equal, but rather that a strip of valid *s'chach* of the same width can be slid easily into the gap. If it slides in easily, the gap must in fact be slightly larger than the *s'chach* being inserted. The "equality" is therefore only apparent; the gap is truly a bit wider. One might ask: doesn't a slightly larger gap make things worse, since there is now less valid *s'chach*? The answer is that the invalid material is fixed in place; the gap is the space where the valid *s'chach* will be inserted. The point is simply that "equal" is being read as "just slightly more than equal" — and thus the *mishna* does not establish that exact equality is permissible.

The *Gemara* then asks: but it is possible to measure exactly, so why would the *mishna* speak in terms of easy insertion rather than precise equality? Rav Ami answers: *bema'adif* — the intention is that the craftsman deliberately makes the gap slightly larger in order to have room to insert an equal width of valid *s'chach*. The *mishna* is not describing a precisely equal case; it is describing the practical outcome of intentionally enlarging the gap.

### Rava Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִם הָיוּ נְתוּנִין עֵרֶב — נוֹתְנוֹ שְׁתִי, שְׁתִי — נוֹתְנוֹ עֵרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is referring to a case where **if** the skewers **were placed crosswise** to the *sukka*, **he should place** the valid roofing **lengthwise,** and similarly, if the skewers were placed **lengthwise, he should place** the valid roofing **crosswise,** ensuring that there is more valid than invalid roofing.

Rava offers an alternative explanation. *Im hayu nesunim erev, nosen shesi; shesi nosen erev* — if the invalid material was laid widthwise, the valid *s'chach* is inserted lengthwise, and vice versa. By inserting the valid *s'chach* perpendicular to the invalid material, the valid strip rests upon and overlaps the edges of the invalid strips, which means it covers slightly more width than the gap itself. The proof for Rav Papa therefore fails: the *mishna* in *Sukkah* is not actually describing a case of precise equality. The broader observation stands that the sukkah discussion is somewhat puzzling, since the *Gemara* seems to bypass the particular concerns about shade that usually govern *s'chach*, but that is the substance of the *Gemara*'s resolution here.

## Caravan Gaps

### Camel Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁיָירָא שֶׁחָנְתָה בְּבִקְעָה וְהִקִּיפוּהָ בִּגְמַלִּין, בְּאוּכָּפוֹת, בַּעֲבִיטִין, בִּשְׁלִיפִין, בְּקָנִים, בְּקוֹלָחוֹת — מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין גָּמָל לְגָמָל כִּמְלֹא גָּמָל, וּבֵין אוּכָּף לְאוּכָּף כִּמְלֹא אוּכָּף, וּבֵין עָבִיט לְעָבִיט כִּמְלֹא עָבִיט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to adduce a proof in support of the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in a *baraita*: If **a caravan camped in a field, and** the travelers **surrounded** their camp **with camels** that were made to crouch down, or **with** their **saddles,** or **with** saddle **cushions [*avitin*],** or **with wheat sheaves,** or **with boards,** or **with stalks [*kolaḥot*], one may carry within** the enclosed area, **provided that there is no camel-length** gap **between one camel and another, or a saddle-length** gap **between one saddle and another, or a cushion-length** gap **between one cushion and another.** Apparently, from this *baraita* it can be understood that if the breach is equal to the standing segment, it is not a valid enclosure.

The *Gemara* brings yet another source — a *braita* closely related to the *mishna* itself. The case: *shayara shechantah bevikah* — a caravan encamped in a valley — and the travelers surrounded themselves using camels, saddles (*uchafos*), and the cloth or pillow inserts that sit beneath the saddles (*avitim*, which Rashi explains as cushioned padding rather than the chamber pots that word can elsewhere denote), as well as saddlebags and luggage. The conditions specified are: *shelo yihiyeh bein gamal legamal kimlo gamal* — between one camel and the next, the gap may not equal the width of a camel; similarly between one saddle and the next, and between one pillow and the next. This formulation sounds like a direct proof for Rav Huna: when the gap equals the standing portion, it is invalid.

### Easy Insertion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי כְּשֶׁנִּכְנָס וְיוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion. **Here, too,** it is referring to gaps through which the various objects can easily **be inserted and extracted,** so that the breached segment is in fact slightly greater than that of the standing segment.

The *Gemara* defends Rav Papa with the same answer deployed above: *hacha nami keshnichnas veyotze* — here too, the *braita* means not literally equal, but a gap big enough that an object of the same width can be easily inserted. The stated criterion of "equal to a camel's width" therefore implies the gap is actually somewhat larger. The *Gemara* is thus consistently reinterpreting all of these sources as dealing not with the precise fifty-fifty case but with a gap that is functionally a bit beyond equal, all in service of keeping the debate alive and protecting Rav Papa's position from being definitively refuted.

## Kilayim Categories

### Category 1: <3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה אוֹמֵר, שָׁלֹשׁ מִדּוֹת בִּמְחִיצוֹת: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה, צָרִיךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִזְדַּקֵּר הַגְּדִי בְּבַת רֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites yet another proof: **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in the *Tosefta* in tractate *Kilayim*: **Ultimately, you say that there are three measures for partitions.** These partitions form a barrier that demarcates between vines and seeds. They are needed to render permitted the sowing of a field with diverse kinds of seeds. In the case of **any** partition consisting of boards that are each **less than three** handbreadths wide, **it is necessary that there will not be** a gap of **three** handbreadths **between this** board **and that, so that a goat would not** be able to **leap headlong** through it unimpeded. If the gap is wider than three handbreadths, i.e., wide enough that a goat can leap through it, the boards are not considered joined and it is not considered a partition.

The *Gemara* now introduces an extensive *Tosefta* discussing *kilayim* — the prohibition against planting different species together. A *mechitza* can permit adjacent planting of different species, so the *Tosefta* defines three categories of *mechitzot* with different legal outcomes.

Category one: *kol shehu pachot mishlosha* — where the sections of the wall are each less than three *tefachim*. In this case the requirement is that the gaps between sections also be less than three *tefachim* — *shelo yihiyeh bein zeh lezeh shlosha, kedei shelo yizdaker hagadi bevat rosh*, so that a kid goat cannot pass through easily, which is the operative definition of *lavud*. The key insight here is that if the individual wall sections are less than three *tefachim*, they can in principle be very small indeed — even a sliver — because the principle of *lavud* treats the gap as closed when it is less than three. The section itself need not be large; as long as each gap is less than three *tefachim*, the entire structure is treated as continuous.

### Category 2: 3-4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁהוּא שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּמִשְׁלֹשָׁה עַד אַרְבָּעָה, צָרִיךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה כִּמְלוֹאוֹ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא פָּרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד. וְאִם הָיָה פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד — אַף כְּנֶגֶד הָעוֹמֵד אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **any** partition that consists of boards that are **three** handbreadths wide, **as well as** boards **from three to four** handbreadths wide, the gap between the boards may be greater than three handbreadths. Nonetheless, **it is necessary that there will not be** a gap equal to **the full width** of a board **between one** board **and the next, so that the breached** segment **will not equal the standing** segment. **And if the breached** segment **is greater than the standing** segment, **it is prohibited** to sow another species, **even** in the area **opposite the standing** segment, as the breached segment invalidates the entire partition.

Category two: *migimmel v'ad daled* — where the sections of the wall are between three and four *tefachim*. The rule here is *tzarich shelo yihiyeh bein zeh lezeh kimlo'o* — the gap may not equal the size of the section — and the *Tosefta* explicitly uses the phrase *kedei shelo yihiyeh parutz ke'omed*, stating that it is in order that the gap not equal the standing portion. This formulation appears to label *parutz ke'omed* as invalid and seems to directly support Rav Huna.

But then the *Tosefta* continues: *im hayah parutz merubeh al ha'omed, af keneged ha'omed assur* — if the gap exceeds the standing portion, then even the area directly opposite the standing partition itself is forbidden. This creates a significant tension: three formulations of the *Tosefta* say the problem begins when the gap is equal, but this line says the problem arises when the gap is greater. It also raises the question of what distinguishes category two from category one beyond the size of the sections. Here, when the gaps are too large, nothing is permissible — not even the area directly opposite the partition — because each wall section is less than four *tefachim* and therefore does not have independent halachic significance. A partition that is less than four *tefachim* wide does not stand on its own; when the overall structure fails, it all fails.

### Category 3: 4-10

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁהוּא אַרְבָּעָה, וּמֵאַרְבָּעָה עַד עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, צָרִיךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה כִּמְלוֹאוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא פָּרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד. וְאִם הָיָה פָּרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד, כְּנֶגֶד הָעוֹמֵד — מוּתָּר, כְּנֶגֶד הַפָּרוּץ — אָסוּר. וְאִם הָיָה עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ — אַף כְּנֶגֶד הַפָּרוּץ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **any** partition that consists of boards that are **four** handbreadths wide, **as well as** boards **from four** handbreadths **to ten cubits** wide, **it is necessary that there not be** a gap **the full** width of a board **between one** board **and the next, so that the breached** segment **will not equal the standing** segment. **And if the breached** segment **equals the standing** segment, then in the area **opposite the standing** segment, **it is permitted** to sow other species, as there is a partition there; however, in the area **facing the breached** segment **it is prohibited. And if the** sum of the **standing** segments **is greater than the** sum of **the breached** segments, sowing other species **is permitted, even** in the area **opposite the breached** part.

Category three: *me'arba tefachim ad eser amot* — where the partition sections are from four *tefachim* up to ten *amot*. Again the *Tosefta* states that the gap may not equal the section — *shelo yehei parutz k'omed* — and that if it does equal the partition, the area directly opposite the gap is forbidden. But here it adds: *k'neged ha'omed muttar* — opposite the standing partition, it remains permissible. This is the crucial distinction from category two. A section of four *tefachim* or more has independent significance; even when isolated from adjacent sections by an oversized gap, it stands on its own and permits planting opposite it. This mirrors the general principle that a space of four *tefachim* constitutes a *makom chashuv* — a defined, significant location — in contrast to anything smaller.

### Equal Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת רֵישָׁא מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה עַד אַרְבָּעָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה כִּמְלוֹאוֹ, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב פָּפָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how the passage from the *Tosefta* of tractate *Kilayim* supports the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, contrary to that of Rav Pappa. **In any case, the first clause** of the *Tosefta* **teaches** that if each of the boards that make up the partition is **from three to four** handbreadths wide, sowing other species is permitted, **provided that there is not** a gap **the full** width of a board **between one** board **and the next.** This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Rav Pappa,** who permits carrying when the breach equals the standing segment of the partition.

Tallying the language of the *Tosefta*, three times it phrases the invalidity in terms of the gap equaling the partition (*parutz k'omed*), while once it phrases it in terms of the gap exceeding the partition (*parutz merubeh al ha'omed*). The *Gemara* observes: *tani miat* — the primary point is the formulation found in the case of sections between three and four *tefachim*, where the gap cannot equal the section. This seems a compelling proof against Rav Papa. The *Gemara* declares: *tiyuvta d'Rav Pappa* — a definitive refutation of Rav Papa.

### Rav Papa Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב פָּפָּא: מַאי מְלוֹאוֹ — נִכְנָס וְיוֹצֵא. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי: אִם הָיָה פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד — אַף כְּנֶגֶד הָעוֹמֵד אָסוּר. הָא כְּעוֹמֵד — מוּתָּר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** could have **said to you: What** does the *baraita* mean by a gap **the full** width of a board? It means a gap through which the board could easily be **inserted and extracted,** which is a gap slightly wider than the board itself. The Gemara comments: **So too, it stands to reason, from** the fact that the *Tosefta* **teaches** later: **If the breached** segment **is greater than the standing** segment, **it is prohibited** to sow another species, **even** in the area **opposite the standing** portion. By inference, **if** the breached segment **equals the standing** segment, sowing other species **is** in fact **permitted.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** proof for the opinion of Rav Pappa.

Rav Papa responds: *mai lav nichnas veyotze* — once again, when the *Tosefta* says "equal is forbidden," it does not mean precisely equal, it means a gap large enough to easily accommodate an object of equal size, which implies the gap is actually slightly larger than equal. The *Gemara* then notes that this reading is itself supported by an internal tension in the *Tosefta*: one of the four formulations says the problem arises when the gap is greater, not equal. *Hachi nami mistavra* — this actually supports Rav Papa, because that one line uses *parutz merubeh* rather than *parutz k'omed*, suggesting the real threshold is greater-than-equal, and the repeated use of "equal" in the other formulations is somewhat imprecise. Every side can thus point to at least one line in the *Tosefta* that is inconvenient for the other position.

The *Gemara* then considers whether the line supporting Rav Papa should be read as a decisive refutation of Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua. He in turn deflects: *u'l'ta'amecha, eima seifa* — even according to you, look at the final line of the *braita*: *im hayah omed merubeh al haparutz, af k'neged haparutz mutar* — if the standing part is greater than the gap, then even opposite the gap is permissible. The implication, *ha k'farutz assur*, is that when equal, it is forbidden. So almost all lines of this *Tosefta* support Rav Huna. The one line that uses *merubeh* is an imprecision, a consequence of the *Tosefta*'s wanting to phrase the favorable outcome (*omed merubeh... mutar*) symmetrically with the unfavorable one stated earlier (*parutz merubeh... assur*). The *Gemara* acknowledges that the difficulty goes both ways: *seifa kashya l'Rav Pappa, reisha kashya l'Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua*. Neither reading of the *Tosefta* is perfectly consistent.

### Categories Not Combined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב פָּפָּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא עָרֵיב לְהוּ וְתָנֵי לְהוּ. אֶלָּא לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — לִיעָרְבִינְהוּ וְלִיתְנִנְהוּ: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה — צָרִיךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה! מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא דָּמֵי פְּסוּלָא דְרֵישָׁא לִפְסוּלָא דְסֵיפָא. פְּסוּלָא דְרֵישָׁא — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִזְדַּקֵּר הַגְּדִי בְּבַת אַחַת, פִּסּוּלָא דְסֵיפָא — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא פָּרוּץ כָּעוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted, according to Rav Pappa,** who permits carrying in the case where the breaches equal the standing segments, the *baraita* makes sense, as **for that** reason the *tanna* **did not combine** the case of boards less than three handbreadths wide with the case of boards three handbreadths wide **and teach them** in a single clause. According to Rav Pappa, there is a significant difference between the two situations. In a case where the boards are less than three handbreadths wide, the partition is invalid if there is a gap of three handbreadths between one bar and the next. However, if the boards are precisely three handbreadths wide, the partition is valid unless there is a gap of more than three handbreadths between them. **However, according to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** who considers a partition invalid when its breached segments are equal to its standing segments, the *baraita* **should have combined** the case of boards less than three handbreadths wide with the case of boards exactly three handbreadths wide **and taught them** in the following single clause: **Any** partition made of boards **less than three** handbreadths wide **or** exactly **three** handbreadths wide, **it is necessary that there not be** a gap of **three** handbreadths **between one** board **and another.** According to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, if a partition with boards three handbreadths wide is to be valid, the gap must be less than three handbreadths. **From the fact** that the *baraita* **distinguishes between the two cases, because the disqualification in the first clause is not similar to the disqualification in the latter clause.** The reason for **the disqualification in the first clause** is because a valid partition must be constructed **so that a goat would not** be able **to jump** through the gap **in one** leap; the reason for **the disqualification in the latter clause,** where the boards are three handbreadths wide, is **so that the breached** segments **will equal the** combined **standing** segments. In practice, just as in the case of boards less than three handbreadths wide, the gap must be less than three handbreadths, so too, in the case of boards three handbreadths wide, the gap must also be less than three handbreadths. However, in terms of underlying reasoning, the case of boards three handbreadths wide must be categorized in the second grouping in the *Tosefta*, not the first. Therefore, no proof can be cited from here, neither in support of the opinion of Rav Pappa nor in support of the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.

The *Gemara* raises an additional internal question about the *Tosefta* itself: why are categories one and two kept separate? Under Rav Papa's reading, the case of sections exactly three *tefachim* wide belongs in category two, not category one, because when the section is exactly three, *lavud* cannot be invoked (that requires less than three), and the gap may nonetheless be exactly three *tefachim* wide (since for Rav Papa equal is permissible). That is a genuine distinction from the case of sections less than three *tefachim*, where the gap must be less than three for *lavud* to apply. So Rav Papa can explain the separate categories: at exactly three *tefachim*, the gap can be as large as three, whereas at less than three *tefachim*, the gap must be under three. *Bishlema l'Rav Pappa, mishum hachi lo arev lehu* — this is precisely why the *Tosefta* keeps them separate.

But according to Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua, the gap always has to be strictly less than the standing portion, so whether the section is exactly three or slightly under three, the gap must be under three in both cases. Why then does the *Tosefta* treat them as distinct categories? The *Gemara* answers: *mishum delo dami pesula d'reisha l'pesula d'seifa* — because the rationale for invalidity differs. In category one, the gap must be less than three in order to invoke *lavud*, the principle that treats a gap as closed — *kedei shelo yizdaker hagedi b'vat achas*. In category two, the gap must be less than the partition size for the entirely different reason that the gap may not equal the standing portion — *shelo yehei parutz k'omed*. Thus even under Rav Huna's view, the categories are legitimately distinct, since the underlying principle shifts from *lavud* to *parutz k'omed*.

### Abaye Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה מַנִּי — רַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד, שְׁלֹשָׁה לָא אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּמִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד אַרְבָּעָה — אֲתָאן לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר: פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה לָבוּד. דְּאִי רַבָּנַן: מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד אַרְבָּעָה? שְׁלֹשָׁה וְאַרְבָּעָה חַד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara briefly discusses the ruling cited in the *Tosefta* that a breach of **less than three** handbreadths does not invalidate a partition. In accordance with **whose** opinion is that ruling? It is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who **say:** In the case of a gap **less than three** handbreadths, **we say,** i.e., we apply, the principle of ***lavud*,** and the partitions are considered joined; however, if the gap is **three** handbreadths, **we do not say *lavud*.** **Say the latter clause** with regard to a partition of boards as follows: In the case of any partition whose boards are **three** handbreadths wide, **and** any partition whose boards are **from three to four** handbreadths wide, **we have arrived at** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who said:** Concerning any gap **less than four** handbreadths wide, the principle of ***lavud*** is applied. **As had** it been taught in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** why does the *baraita* list **from three to four** handbreadths as a separate category? In the case of both **three and four** handbreadths, the *halakha* **is one** and the same: The principle of *lavud* does not apply from three handbreadths upward.

A further internal question: the *Tosefta* treats sections of three *tefachim* differently from sections of four or more, giving independent significance only to the latter. This distinction seems to align with the view of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that *lavud* applies to gaps of less than four *tefachim* rather than the standard three, thereby making four the threshold of significance. Does the *Tosefta* therefore follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rather than the Rabbis?

Abaye resolves the difficulty: *mide'reisha Rabbanan, seifa nami Rabbanan* — since the first category manifestly follows the Rabbis (who use three *tefachim* as the threshold for *lavud*), the later categories also follow the Rabbis. The two questions — when does *lavud* apply, and when does a partition have independent significance — are governed by different standards. The Rabbis say *lavud* applies when the gap is less than three *tefachim*. Separately, they hold that for a partition to be halachically significant when standing alone — *keneged ha'omed muttar* — it must cover at least four *tefachim* of space. This is consistent with the general rule that a space of less than four *tefachim* is a *makom p'tor*, a halachically neutral zone; conversely, a partition covering four *tefachim* creates a defined significant space. The two thresholds operate independently: three *tefachim* for *lavud*, four *tefachim* for independent significance.

### Rava Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּרֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן, סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבָּנַן, וּמוֹדוּ רַבָּנַן דְּכׇל לְמִישְׁרֵא כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, אִי אִיכָּא מָקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה — חֲשִׁיב, וְאִי לָא — לָא חֲשִׁיב. רָבָא אָמַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. וְכִי אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אָמְרִינַן לָבוּד — הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל לְמַטָּה, הָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְחִיצָה שֶׁהַגְּדָיִים בּוֹקְעִין בָּהּ — לָא אָמְרִינַן: לָבוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From** the fact **that the first clause is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, the latter clause** must **also be** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. And the Rabbis concede that** with regard to **any case** where the *halakha* **permits** sowing other species in the area **opposite** the standing portion, **if there is an area of four** handbreadths, **it is** considered **a significant** partition, which permits sowing; **and if not, it is not** considered **a significant** partition and does not permit sowing. Accordingly, there is a difference between a fence of three handbreadths and one of four handbreadths, as even the Rabbis concede that a fence of four handbreadths is more significant. **Rava said: From** the fact **that the latter clause is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the first clause** must **also be** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. And when Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** that **we say** the principle of ***lavud*** in the case of a gap up to four handbreadths wide, **this applies above,** off the ground, e.g., in the case of a cross beam suspended at a distance from the wall. **However, below,** near the ground, **it is like a partition through which goats can pass,** and therefore he too agrees that **we do not say** the principle of ***lavud*** in that case.

Rava offers an alternative resolution. He attributes the *Tosefta* in its entirety to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — not to the Rabbis. The end of the *Tosefta*, which gives significance to four *tefachim*, is indeed Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position since he holds *lavud* applies within four *tefachim*, making four the threshold of significance. As for the beginning, where less-than-three gaps must apply even though Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *lavud* extends to four — Rava explains: *hani mili le'malah*, this is above, where one invokes *lavud* to connect a beam to a wall or similar elevated constructions. But *aval lemata*, when dealing with ground-level *mechitzot*, the concern is *mechitzah shehagedi'im bok'in bah* — a partition through which kid-goats can pass. Even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would agree that a gap of three *tefachim* at ground level cannot be called a partition since goats can freely pass through it. Thus the three-*tefach* standard in category one reflects the goat-passage criterion, not the *lavud* principle, and the four-*tefach* threshold in categories two and three reflects Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's measure of significance.

The *Gemara* notes that this approach has difficulties with the precise language of the *Tosefta*, and the resolution is left somewhat open. The discussion of the *kilayim* case thus concludes without fully settling the question of *parutz k'omed*.

## Courtyard Openings

### Equal Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְפָנוֹת הַלָּלוּ שֶׁרוּבָּן פְּתָחִים וְחַלּוֹנוֹת מוּתָּר, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ. ״שֶׁרוּבָּן״, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא: שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶן פְּתָחִים וְחַלּוֹנוֹת מוּתָּר, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ. הָא כַּפָּרוּץ אָסוּר, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב פָּפָּא תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the dispute with regard to a breach equal to the standing segments of a partition and cites another proof. **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in the following *baraita*: With regard to an area enclosed by **these walls,** in a case **where most of them** consist of **entrance and windows,** it is **permitted** to carry on Shabbat within the area, **provided that the standing** segments **are greater than the breached** segments. The Gemara analyzes the formulation of the *baraita*: **Can it enter your mind that** the *baraita* is referring to a case where **most of** the walls are entrances and windows? If so, the standing segments are certainly not greater than the breached segments. **Rather,** emend the *baraita* as follows: Carrying in the area enclosed by these walls, **to which one added** many **entrances and windows, is permitted, provided that the standing** segments **are greater than the breached** segments. The Gemara draws an inference: **If** the standing segments **equal the breached** segments, carrying **is prohibited** in that enclosure. This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Pappa.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, it is **a conclusive refutation.**

One final attempt. The *Gemara* cites a ruling about a courtyard whose walls are mostly openings and windows: *sheruban pesachim vachalonos, mutar, uvilvad she'omed merubeh al haparutz* — if most of the wall is gaps and openings, it may nonetheless serve as a valid enclosure provided the standing portions exceed the gaps. The *Gemara* quickly clarifies the opening phrase: one cannot literally say that most of the wall is gaps and then conclude it is permissible; rather, the case is that there are many openings distributed across multiple walls, yet the overall standing portions exceed the openings. The implication is clear: *ha k'farutz, assur* — if equal, it is forbidden. The *Gemara* treats this as a definitive refutation: *tiyuvta d'Rav Pappa tiyuvta*.

### Mishna Supports Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא. תְּיוּבְתָּא וְהִילְכְתָא?! אִין, מִשּׁוּם דְּדַיְיקָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ. דִּתְנַן: לֹא יִהְיוּ פְּרָצוֹת יְתֵירוֹת עַל הַבִּנְיָן. הָא כַּבִּנְיָן מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Pappa.** The Gemara wonders: **A conclusive refutation and the *halakha*?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** that is the case, **because the precise reading of the mishna is in accordance with** the opinion of Rav Pappa. **As we learned** in the mishna the following phrase: Provided…**there will not be breaches** in the partition **greater** than **the built** segment. This is clearly indicating that if the breached segments are **equal to the built** segments, carrying **is permitted,** as maintained by Rav Pappa.

Immediately following this declaration, the *Gemara* states: *vehilchasa kevassia d'Rav Pappa* — the ruling follows Rav Papa. The *Gemara* then raises the obvious objection: *tiyuvta vehilchasa?* — you just contradicted him and now you rule like him? The answer: *in, mishum dedayka masnisin kevassia* — yes, because our own *mishna* strongly implies his position. The *mishna* that opened this entire discussion stated *lo yihyu p'ratzos yeseiros al habinyan* — the gaps may not exceed the standing portion. The implication, *ha k'binyan mutar*, is that equal is permissible. This is precisely Rav Papa's view. The evidence from the various *braitot* is contradictory, and the *Tosefta* is imprecise in its formulations; the *Gemara* concludes that neither the *mishnayot* nor the *braitot* were focused on the precise fifty-fifty case. Given the contradictory state of the evidence, the *Gemara* returns to the *mishna* from which the discussion began and rules accordingly: when gaps equal the standing portion, it is permissible.

It is worth noting a comment from *Tosafot* at this point. *Tosafot* observes the apparent tension: here we rule that *parutz ke'omed muttar*, while in the second chapter of *Chullin* the *Gemara* concludes that all opinions agree that *mechtza al mechtza eino ke-rubo* — fifty-fifty is not equivalent to a majority. *Tosafot* suggests that one may distinguish between *mechitzot* and the domains of *issur* and *tumah*. The general principle of *rubo ke-kulo* — treating the majority as if it were the whole — requires an actual majority of fifty-one percent or more when the claim is that an act is fully accomplished (as in the case of *shechita* of the trachea and esophagus). By contrast, the question regarding *mechitzot* is not whether we treat the entire perimeter as walled, but whether enough of it is walled to confer the status of an enclosure. For that purpose, fifty percent may suffice without invoking the *rubo ke-kulo* principle at all.

## Three-String Walls

### String Construction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַקִּיפִין שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבָלִים זֶה לְמַעְלָה מִזֶּה, וָזֶה לְמַעְלָה מִזֶּה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בֵּין חֶבֶל לַחֲבֵירוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a caravan is camped in a field, and the travelers seek to construct partitions to render the area fit for one to carry within it on Shabbat, **one surrounds** the area with **three ropes, one above another, and** a third **one above** the **other** two. One is permitted to carry within the circumscribed area **provided that there will not be** a gap of **three handbreadths between** one **rope and the next.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which addresses a more minimalist form of *mechitza* made from strings or posts — constructions that rely entirely on *lavud* for their validity. The *mishna* states: *makifin shelosha chavalim zeh lema'ala mizeh* — one may create an enclosure using three ropes strung one above another. The condition is that the gap between one rope and the next may not be three *tefachim*, so that *lavud* can treat each gap as closed.

### String Width Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיעוּר חֲבָלִים וְעוֹבְיָין יָתֵר עַל טֶפַח, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא הַכֹּל עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The measure of the ropes and their** combined **thickness** must be **greater than a handbreadth, so that the entire** partition, consisting of three ropes and the empty spaces between them, **will be ten handbreadths** high.

Additionally, the *mishna* specifies: *shiur chavalim ve-ovyan yeter al tefach* — the combined width of all three ropes together must exceed one *tefach*. The practical purpose of this requirement is to ensure that the total height reaches ten *tefachim*. If each of the three gaps is slightly under three *tefachim* — say 2.9 *tefachim* — and there are three gaps, the total gap distance is 8.7 *tefachim*. The combined thickness of the ropes must therefore account for the remaining 1.3 *tefachim* or more to bring the total to ten *tefachim*. This is achieved by using thick straps or ropes whose widths add up to over one *tefach*, as the *mishna* requires.

The *mishna* similarly allows posts — *makifin b'kanim* — provided the gaps between posts are less than three *tefachim*, applying *lavud* in the horizontal rather than the vertical direction.

### Caravan Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁיָירָא דִּבְּרוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא דִּבְּרוּ בִּשְׁיָירָא, אֶלָּא בַּהוֹוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** When the Sages issued this ruling, **they spoke** exclusively **of a caravan;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that a partition of this kind, which consists of only horizontal or vertical elements, is permitted exclusively in exigent circumstances. Otherwise, full-fledged partitions are required. **However, the Rabbis say: They spoke of a caravan** in the mishna **only** because they spoke **in the present,** citing the most typical case. Those traveling in caravans were typically unable to erect full-fledged partitions, so they would surround their camps with ropes or boards. However, the *halakha* in the mishna applies in all cases.

The *mishna* then records a dispute over the scope of this leniency. *Beshayara dibru, divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *mishna*'s permission to use strings or posts in place of proper walls was spoken only in the context of a caravan. Normally, Rabbi Yehuda requires that a *mechitza* run in both directions — *sheti va'erev*, the warp and the woof — and he permits this minimalist approach only for travelers stranded in the wilderness without building materials, as a special leniency born of necessity.

### Sages Always OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מְחִיצָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁתִי וְשֶׁל עֵרֶב — אֵינָהּ מְחִיצָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד מִשְּׁנֵי דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites an additional dispute: **Any partition that is not** constructed **of** both **warp and woof,** i.e., vertical and horizontal elements, **is not a partition;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.** He holds that the vertical boards and the horizontal ropes are not considered a partition, even in the exigent circumstances of a caravan. **However, the Rabbis say: One of the two elements,** either vertical or horizontal, is sufficient.

The Sages disagree: *lo dibru beshayara ela behoveh* — they described the caravan case because it was a common situation, but the principle is general. One may always create a valid *mechitza* using strings or posts arranged in a single direction, relying on *lavud*, without needing both vertical and horizontal elements. This is what permits the practice in the context of a *sukkah* or other enclosures as well. A clear window into the history of the teaching emerges here: an early *mishna* recorded the *halakha* in the context of a caravan, and later *tannaim* debated whether the caravan was a limitation or merely a commonly described case.

The *mishna* also records a further position attributed to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yehuda's son, who states: *kol mechitza she'eina shel sheti v'shel erev eina mechitza* — any *mechitza* that does not run in both the warp and woof directions is not a valid *mechitza*. He goes further than his father: Rabbi Yehuda permitted single-direction walls for caravans; Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda denies their validity in all circumstances. The Sages conclude: *echad mishnei dvarim* — either direction, vertical or horizontal, is sufficient; one need not employ both.

## Horizontal Gaps Question

### Rav Hamnuna Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ, עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ בִּשְׁתִי — הָוֵי עוֹמֵד. בָּעֵי רַב הַמְנוּנָא: בְּעֵרֶב מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Hamnuna said** that **Rav said:** It was concluded in the previous mishna that the Rabbis **said** that **in** the case of a partition that consists only of **warp,** i.e., vertical, elements, if **the standing** segment of the partition **is greater than the breached** segment, the fence **is** considered **standing. Rav Hamnuna raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* in the case of a partition that consists only of **woof,** i.e., horizontal, elements? Is it also considered standing if the standing segment is greater than the breached segment, or not?

The *Gemara* now takes up a question posed by Rav Hamnuna in the name of Rav, connecting the earlier discussion of *parutz k'omed* to the new *mishna*'s material. The Sages stated that the principle of *omed merubeh al haparutz* — the standing portion exceeding the gaps — applies in the vertical direction (*besheti*, the warp). Rav Hamnuna asks: *ba'erev mahu* — what is the law when the majority principle is applied in the horizontal direction, the woof? In other words, if a wall has gaps running not vertically but horizontally — planks on the bottom and top with a gap in the middle — is it sufficient that the combined height of the planks exceeds the height of the gap, even without invoking *lavud*?

### Three-String Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: שִׁיעוּר חֲבָלִים וְעוֹבְיָין יָתֵר עַל טֶפַח, שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּל עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. וְאִי אִיתָא — לְמָה לִי יָתֵר עַל טֶפַח?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from the mishna: **The measure of the ropes and their** combined **thickness** must be **greater than a handbreadth, so that the entire** partition **will be ten handbreadths** high. **And if it is** so that, in a case where the standing segment is greater than the breached segment, the partition is considered standing even in the case of a fence that consists of horizontal elements, **why do I need** ropes with a combined thickness of **greater than a handbreadth?**

Abaye attempts to resolve this question from the *mishna* about three strings. The *mishna* requires that the combined thickness of the ropes exceed one *tefach* — *shiur chavalim ve-ovyan yeter al tefach*. If one could simply rely on horizontal majority (*omed merubeh al haparutz* in the woof direction), why would additional rope thickness be necessary at all?

### String Width Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶיעְבֵּיד פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְחֶבֶל מַשֶּׁהוּ, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְחֶבֶל מַשֶּׁהוּ, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה וְחֶבֶל מַשֶּׁהוּ. וְתִיסְבְּרָא? הַאי פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבָּעָה הֵיכָא מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ? אִי מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ תַּתַּאי, הָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְחִיצָה שֶׁהַגְּדָיִם בּוֹקְעִין בָּהּ. אִי מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ עִילַּאי, אָתֵי אַוֵּירָא דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וּדְהַאי גִּיסָא וּמְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ. אִי מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ בְּמִיצְעֵי, הָוֵה לֵיהּ עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת הָוֵי עוֹמֵד!
>
> **Translation:** Instead, **let one leave** a space slightly **less than three** handbreadths, **and** place **a rope of any size,** leave another space slightly **less than three** handbreadths, **and** place another **rope of any size,** leave a third space slightly **less than four** handbreadths, **and** place **a** third **rope** of **any size.** The ropes between which there is a space less than three handbreadths should be considered joined, based on the principle of *lavud*. The entire partition should be considered standing because the standing segment, measuring six handbreadths, is greater than the breached segment, which measures four handbreadths. The Gemara presents a difficulty: **And** how can **you understand** that this would be effective? **Where does he position** the space of slightly **less than four** handbreadths? **If he positions it at the bottom, its** legal status **is like** that of **a partition through which goats pass,** which is not a valid partition. **If he positions it at the top,** then **the air on this side, above** the uppermost rope, **and on that side,** below that rope, **come and negate it.** As there are more than three handbreadths between them the upper and lower ropes, they are not joined together based on the principle of *lavud*. The four handbreadths below the uppermost rope and the airspace above it combine to negate the connection. **If he positions it in the middle,** then **the standing** segment of the partition **is greater than the breached** segment, provided that the standing portions **on the two sides** of the breach are combined. However, if each side is considered separately, the breach is greater than the standing portion. If it is nevertheless deemed a partition, **conclude from it** that even if **the standing** segment **is greater than the breached** segment only when the standing segments **on the two sides** of the breach are combined, the partition is considered **standing.** However, that circumstance was raised as a dilemma and remained unresolved.

The reasoning: suppose one argued that it is enough to have a majority of solid material in the vertical plane even when the gaps are not individually subject to *lavud*. One could then construct three ropes with gaps of, say, 2.9 each — totaling 8.7 *tefachim* of gap — and with the ropes themselves providing a smaller combined measurement, provided the solid ropes outweigh the gaps. Why does the *mishna* insist on the ropes being thick enough to contribute more than one *tefach* to the total height? The *Gemara* explores whether this *mishna* can actually be made into a workable proof for the majority-in-the-woof question.

The difficulty is that the three possible scenarios for placing a large horizontal gap are all problematic. If the large gap is at the bottom — *ei mokimt leh tata'ei* — then we have *mechitzah shehagedi'im bok'in bah*, a partition through which goats can pass at ground level, which is invalid. If the large gap is at the top — *ei mokimt leh ila'ei* — then *ati avira d'hai gisa u'd'hai gisa u'mevalet leh*: the airspace on either side of the rope nullifies its effect. A rope surrounded by open space above and below is treated as nonexistent; unlike a case where *lavud* has been invoked and the gap is treated as closed, an actual open gap means the rope floats in void and counts for nothing.

### Mat Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַב הַמְנוּנָא הָכִי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן דְּאַיְיתִי מַחְצֶלֶת דְּהָוֵי שִׁבְעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וַחֲקַק בַּהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּשְׁבַק בַּהּ אַרְבָּעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Hamnuna raised the following dilemma:** What is the *halakha* in a case **where one brought a mat that is seven** handbreadths **and any** additional amount, **and carved in it** a hole **three** handbreadths wide, **and left four** handbreadths above the hole **and any** additional amount below it, **and positioned** the mat **less than three** handbreadths off the ground?

Since the scenario cannot be constructed using strings, the *Gemara* reformulates Rav Hamnuna's question around a different case. Take a mat (*machtzelet*) that is seven *tefachim* and a bit in height. Cut an opening of three *tefachim* in the middle of it, leaving a solid portion of four *tefachim* and a bit above the gap. Stand the mat with its bottom edge within less than three *tefachim* of the ground, so that *lavud* closes the space between the mat's base and the ground.

The resulting structure: *lavud* gives you a virtual solid from the ground up to the bottom of the mat, roughly 2.9 *tefachim*; the mat itself has 4.1 *tefachim* of solid above the gap. The gap of three *tefachim* in the middle is surrounded by solid portions above and below. The total is just over ten *tefachim*. The solid portions — 2.9 plus 4.1 — equal seven *tefachim*, which exceeds the gap of three *tefachim*. So the *omed* is indeed *merubeh al haparutz*. Does this constitute a valid *mechitza*?

The concern about *avira d'hai gisa u'd'hai gisa u'mevatlei* — airspace on either side nullifying the rope — does not apply here, since the opening is cut into a substantial physical mat rather than being a thin rope suspended in space. Yet the question remains whether a partition with a central gap qualifies as a *mechitza* even when the solid portions together form a majority.

The *Gemara* attempts to work through the arithmetic of this case and notes that the scenario only makes sense if one already assumes that the solid portions on two sides can be combined — *omed merubeh al haparutz mishtei ruchot* — which is precisely the point under debate. The *Gemara* concludes that the premise of the question has not been established independently of the conclusion.

### Air Space Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה אִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ. כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי טַבְלָא מֵרַב: מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה מַהוּ שֶׁתַּתִּיר בְּחוּרְבָּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מְחִיצָה תְּלוּיָה מַתֶּרֶת אֶלָּא בַּמַּיִם, קַל הוּא שֶׁהֵקֵלּוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: The dilemma he raised** is with regard to the legal status of a ten-handbreadth **partition suspended** off the ground. That dilemma is **similar to that which Rabbi Tavla raised** as **a dilemma before Rav: Does a suspended partition** act as if it were a partition that reaches the ground and **render it permitted** for one to carry **in a ruin?** Rav **said to him: A suspended partition renders it permitted** for one to carry **only** when it is suspended over **water,** as there is **a leniency** introduced **by the Sages with regard to water.**

The crux of the concern with suspended walls is that a *mechitza* through which goats can pass is not a *mechitza*, and a rope or thin material suspended between two airspaces — below and above — suffers from the structural problem that the surrounding void effectively eliminates it. This is *mechitzah tluya* — a suspended partition — and the *Gemara* identifies this as the true form of Rav Hamnuna's question: can a suspended partition function as a valid enclosure in a case involving a *churva*, an open ruined area of dry land?

Rav Ashi provides a clear answer to that specific question: *ein mechitzah tluya materes ela bemayim* — a suspended *mechitza* is effective only over water. *Kal hu shehikilu chachamim bemayim* — it is a specific leniency that the Sages granted with respect to water. Over dry land, a partition that is suspended above the ground — not reaching down to within three *tefachim* of the surface — cannot function as a *mechitza*, since animals and people can simply pass beneath it. The broader question of whether one can rely on horizontal majority in the woof direction is noted but left without a definitive answer by the *Gemara*.

## Ropes Caravans

### Yehuda Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקִּיפִין בְּקָנִים וְכוּ׳. בִּשְׁיָרָא אִין, בְּיָחִיד לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מְחִיצּוֹת שַׁבָּת לֹא הִתִּירוּ לְיָחִיד יוֹתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One** may **surround** the area **with boards** that stand upright, provided there will not be a gap of three handbreadths between one board and the next. Rabbi Yehuda said that this leniency, which allows the establishment of a partition consisting exclusively of horizontal or vertical elements, was stated only with regard to a caravan. The Gemara infers: **With regard to a caravan, yes,** it is permitted; **with regard to an individual, no,** it is not permitted. **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **all** unsteady **partitions of Shabbat,** e.g., those consisting exclusively of horizontal or vertical elements, the Sages **did not permit** their use **for an individual** if the space that they enclose is **greater than two *beit se'a*?** This indicates that, for an area of up to two *beit se'a*, Rabbi Yehuda permits these partitions even for an individual.

The *Gemara* returns to the *mishna*'s dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages regarding the scope of the rope-and-post leniency. Rabbi Yehuda said it was spoken only in the context of a caravan — *beshayara, v'ein beyachid* — implying it does not work for individuals at all. But a *braita* seems to show that Rabbi Yehuda did permit it for individuals, at least up to a certain size.

### Braitot Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: לָא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִיתֵּן לָהֶן כׇּל צָרְכָּן, הָכָא נָמֵי לִיתֵּן לָהֶן כׇּל צָרְכָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda's statement in the mishna can be understood **in accordance with that which Rav Naḥman, and some say** it was **Rav Beivai bar Abaye, said** with regard to a different statement: This *halakha* **was necessary only** in order **to provide** those traveling in the caravan with space to satisfy **all their needs. Here, too,** in the mishna, Rabbi Yehuda's statement can be understood as coming **to provide** those traveling in the caravan with space to satisfy **all their needs.** In other words, Rabbi Yehuda does not dispute the fundamental effectiveness of a partition of this kind, even for an individual. When he says that the *halakha* applies solely to a caravan, he means that it applies only in the case of a caravan, regardless of the size of the area in question. However, in the case of an individual, a partition of that kind is effective only if it encloses an area up to two *beit se'a*.

The *braita* further distinguishes: a single individual (*yachid*) may use up to a *beis sasayim*, five thousand square *amos*; two people also get a *beis sasayim*; once there are three people they become a *shayara*, a caravan, and receive *beis shesh* — six *se'ah* of space, essentially fifteen thousand square *amos*.

Rav Nachman (and some attribute this to Rav Bibi bar Abaye) reconciles the apparent contradiction. When the *mishna* records Rabbi Yehuda as saying this works only for a caravan, it means unlimited usage. For a caravan, there is no upper limit on the enclosed space. For an individual, there is a limit — the *beis sasayim* — but the minimal rope-and-post construction does work within that limit. So Rabbi Yehuda's restriction in the *mishna* is not an absolute prohibition for individuals; it is a restriction on unlimited usage.

### Yose Warp-Woof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב נַחְמָן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי? אַהָא דִּתְנַן: כׇּל מְחִיצָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁתִי וָעֵרֶב אֵינָהּ מְחִיצָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֶחָד יָחִיד וְאֶחָד שְׁיָירָא לַחֲבָלִים. וּמָה בֵּין יָחִיד לִשְׁיָירָא? יָחִיד נוֹתְנִין לוֹ בֵּית סָאתַיִם, שְׁנַיִם נוֹתְנִין לָהֶם בֵּית סָאתַיִם, שְׁלֹשָׁה נַעֲשׂוּ שְׁיָירָא וְנוֹתְנִין לָהֶן בֵּית שֵׁשׁ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where was** this statement **of Rav Naḥman, and some say** of **Rav Beivai bar Abaye, stated?** It was stated with regard **to this** ruling at the end of the mishna: **Any partition that is not** made of both **vertical and horizontal** elements **is not a partition;** this is the statement of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara asks: **Did Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** actually **say this? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: For **both an individual and a caravan,** partitions made of **ropes** are effective? **And what,** then, **is** the difference **between an individual and a caravan?** With regard to an **individual,** the *halakha* **provides him** with an area of **two *beit se'a*,** in which he may carry by virtue of partitions of this kind. With regard to **two** individuals as well, the *halakha* **provides them** with an area of **two *beit se'a*. Three** individuals **assume the** legal **status of a caravan, and** the *halakha* **provides** each of **them** with an area of two *beit se'a*, for a total of **six *beit*** *se'a*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The same reconciling principle is then applied to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda. His statement in the *mishna* — *kol mechitza she'eina shel sheti v'shel erev eina mechitza* — sounds like an absolute rejection of single-direction walls. But a *braita* reveals that Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda actually permits them, only with limits: *echad yachid ve'echad shayara, lechavalim* — whether an individual or a caravan, ropes may be used. The difference is that for a *yachid* the space is capped at a *beis sasayim*, and for a *shayara* of three or more it is capped at *beis shesh*. When Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda said in the *mishna* that a single-direction *mechitza* is invalid, he meant it is invalid for unlimited use, not that it is categorically unusable.

### Sages Unlimited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד יָחִיד וְאֶחָד שְׁיָירָא נוֹתְנִין לָהֶן כׇּל צָרְכָּן, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בֵּית סָאתַיִם פָּנוּי!
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** With regard to **both an individual and** those traveling in **a caravan, one provides them** with space to satisfy **all their needs, provided that there will not be an unoccupied space of two *beit se'a*.** They may not enclose an area that is two *beit se'a* larger than the space that they require. Apparently, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, relies on the ruling that ropes render an area fit for one to carry within it, even for an individual.

The Sages take the permissive position: *echad yachid ve'echad shayara, nosnin lahem kol tzarchan* — whether for an individual or a caravan, they may enclose as much space as they need.

### Empty Space Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: לָא נִצְרְכָא, אֶלָּא לִיתֵּן לָהֶן כׇּל צָרְכָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman, and some say** it was **Rav Beivai bar Abaye, said:** The opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, in the mishna **was necessary only to provide them** with the space to satisfy **all their needs** in the case of a properly constructed partition consisting of both horizontal and vertical elements. A partition consisting of exclusively horizontal or vertical elements renders an area of six *beit se'a* fit for one to carry within it, only in the case of a caravan.

The one qualification the Sages add is: *uvilvad shelo yehei beis sasayim panui* — the enclosed area may not contain an unused empty space exceeding *beis sasayim*. If the people and their equipment actually occupy the enclosed area, the enclosure may be as large as needed. What is prohibited is creating an enormous enclosure with a large portion of it — more than a *beis sasayim* — lying entirely empty and unused. As long as the space serves the needs of the people who erected it, the minimal rope-and-post *mechitza* is fully valid according to the Sages, without any upper limit, and that is the ruling we follow.
