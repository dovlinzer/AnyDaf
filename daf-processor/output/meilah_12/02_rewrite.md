# Meilah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dishun Mizbeach HaPnimi

### Intro & Na'asah Mitzvaso

The Mishnah at the bottom of 11b reads: *dishun mizbeach hapnimi v'hamenorah lo nehenin v'lo moalin* — the ashes of the inner altar and the Menorah: one may not derive benefit from them, and there is no *meilah*. To situate this properly, recall what we have been learning: the earlier Gemara discussed the *dishun* — the removal of ashes — from the outer *mizbeach*, and explored at what stage the ashes remaining in the *tapuach*, the great ash-heap, cease to carry *meilah*. The question there was whether *meilah* ends after the *trumas hadeshen* or only after the ashes are taken outside. Here, by contrast, we are dealing with ashes generated inside the *heichal* — from the Menorah and from the inner golden *mizbeach* upon which the *ketores* was burned. For these, the ruling is *lo nehenin v'lo moalin*: rabbinic benefit is forbidden (*lo nehenin* is always rabbinic), but there is no *meilah* at the Torah level.

The reason is *na'asah mitzvaso* — once the lights of the Menorah have burned or the *ketores* has been offered, the *mitzvah* is already accomplished. The Gemara notably does not analyze precisely when this threshold is crossed for the inner *mizbeach* and Menorah, though a Gemara elsewhere, cited by Tosafot, clarifies that by the *ketores* the *mitzvah* is fulfilled once the column of smoke begins to rise — parallel to what the Gemara said regarding the *emurim* of *kodshim kalim*, where *na'asah mitzvaso* occurs essentially as soon as the burning begins.

### Tosafot on Outer Altar

This raises the question: why is the threshold different here than by the outer *mizbeach*, where the Gemara had suggested that *meilah* might persist until the ashes are actually removed outside? Tosafot addresses this directly. The *mitzvah* of *trumas hadeshen* — the formal, daily removal of ashes described at the beginning of *Parashat Tzav* — applies only to the outer *mizbeach*, and even there, only in connection with the *olah* or perhaps *kodshei kodashim* generally. Where there is no specific *mitzvah* of ash-removal, the *mitzvah* cannot be considered to reside in the ashes themselves; rather, the *mitzvah* attaches to the object being burned, and it is completed as soon as that object begins to burn. Thus, the prolonged *meilah* status that the Gemara discussed — requiring *trumas hadeshen* or actual removal — is unique to the outer *mizbeach* in the context of *kodshei kodashim*, where an independent *mitzvah* of ash-removal exists. For the Menorah and the inner *mizbeach*, where no such *mitzvah* exists, *na'asah mitzvaso* occurs at the earliest stage, and *meilah* falls away.

Interestingly, although there is no *mitzvah* of *trumas hadeshen* for the inner *mizbeach* and Menorah — a fact that causes the *meilah* status to lapse earlier — the Gemara nonetheless establishes through derivation that when these ashes are physically cleared away, they are placed at the same location as the ashes of the outer *mizbeach*, beside the ramp. This is, in fact, the question the Gemara pursues, and Tosafot explains that the Gemara's inquiry is not about the *meilah* question at all, but rather about where the ashes go, since the Mishnah does not specify this. It is assumed that there is some practice of placing them beside the outer *mizbeach*, and the Gemara asks for the source.

## Hamakdish Dishun

### Rambam's Reading

The Mishnah then states: *hamakdish dishun batchila moalin bo* — if one sanctifies ashes directly, there is *meilah*. Rashi and Tosafot feel compelled to read this in context, linking it to the ashes-removal procedure and arguing that it addresses a case of re-sanctifying ashes after they have been burned. The Rambam, however, reads the Mishnah in the most straightforward way: it simply establishes that no matter how trivial the object one sanctifies, even a heap of ashes, *meilah* applies. The subject here happens to be ashes, so the Mishnah teaches: if you sanctify ashes, there is *meilah*. Nothing more complicated is required.

## Torin & Bnei Yonah

### Mishnah Statement

The Mishnah then introduces the case of birds of improper age: *torin shelo higia zmanan u-vnei yona she-avar zmanan lo nehenin v'lo moalin*. Turtle-doves (*torin*) must be adults to serve as offerings, and *bnei yonah* — young pigeons — must be young. If a *tor* is consecrated before it reaches adulthood, or a *ben yonah* is consecrated but has already aged past the valid stage, neither benefit may be derived nor is there *meilah*. We will discuss in the Gemara why this is so — since normally a *pasul korban* retains *meilah* — but that analysis belongs to the continuation of this discussion.

## Dishun Mizbeach HaPnim…

### Gemara: Ash Placement

The Gemara asks: *bishlama mizbeach hachitzon* — for the outer *mizbeach* I understand, since the Torah says *vesamo eitzel hamizbeach*, "and he shall place it beside the altar." But *mizbeach hapnimi minalan* — from where do I derive that the same placement rule applies to the inner *mizbeach*? Rabbi Elazar responds: the verse concerning the *olat ha'of* states *ve-hesir mur'aso benotsaso ve-hishlich oto eitzel hamizbeach kedma el mekom hadeshen* — the crop and its feathers are cast beside the altar to the east, to the place of the ashes. So for the bird offering, the verse explicitly directs the innards to *mekom hadeshen*, the ash-place.

The Gemara then applies this through a principle of *im eino inyan*: since this placement instruction was not needed for the outer *mizbeach* — we would already know it — the verse is superfluous there and is redirected to teach about the inner *mizbeach*, establishing that the ash-place beside the outer *mizbeach* is the proper destination for the ashes of the inner *mizbeach* as well.

### Gezerah Shavah

The Gemara resists this immediately. Perhaps the verse about the bird offering is not superfluous; perhaps it is necessary to specify for the outer *mizbeach* exactly where the bird's innards go — telling us not just *eitzel hamizbeach* but the specific location of *mekom hadeshen*. In that case, the verse has its own work to do and cannot be redirected.

The Gemara responds: if that were so, the verse need not have said *el mekom hadeshen* at all. It could have said simply *eitzel hamizbeach* — "beside the altar" — and we would have derived the precise location by means of a *gezerah shavah*, since *eitzel hamizbeach* appears both in the *trumas hadeshen* passage at the beginning of *Tzav* and here. Given that the *gezerah shavah* would have sufficed to identify the location, the additional phrase *el mekom hadeshen* is genuinely redundant. *Mai hadeshen* — why emphasize "place of ashes"? *De-afillu mizbeach hapnimi* — to teach that this location serves as the ash-place even for the inner *mizbeach*.

The Gemara then asks: *menorah minalan* — and from where do we derive the same for the Menorah? The answer: *deshen hadeshen* — the doubled language (*hadeshen*, with the definite article emphasizing the word) indicates that this is the ash-place even beyond the outer *mizbeach*, encompassing the Menorah as well.

What is striking in all of this is the conceptual implication. The Gemara has shown that although there is no *mitzvah* of *trumas hadeshen* for the inner *mizbeach* and the Menorah — the very fact that causes *meilah* to lapse earlier — there is nonetheless a designated ash-place for those ashes as well, and they go to the same location as the outer *mizbeach*'s ashes. This is conceptually distinct from the outer *mizbeach* situation, where the formal *trumas hadeshen* involves placing the ashes ceremonially beside the ramp, while a full cleaning-out sends them outside the *Beit Hamikdash* entirely. For the inner *mizbeach* and Menorah, even a routine cleaning places the ashes at that same ceremonial spot — which might seem to confer a *mitzvah*-like character on the act, a point that the Gemara here leaves without further elaboration.

## Torin & Bnei Yonah…

### Rabbi Shimon

Returning now to the question raised by the Mishnah about birds of improper age: the Mishnah records a dissent. *Rabbi Shimon omer, turin shelo higia zmanan mo'alin bahen* — Rabbi Shimon says that birds which are too young do carry *meilah*, because they will grow older and eventually become fit for use as a *korban*. Simply wait. By contrast, *bnei yona she-avar zmanan*, birds that are too old — Rabbi Shimon agrees with the anonymous first position: *lo nehenin v'lo moalin bahen*, there is no *meilah*, because these birds can no longer be used under any circumstances.

It bears noting that everyone agrees about the case of birds that are too old: since there is no possibility of *pidyon* — redemption — for bird offerings, the *kedushah* is stuck and there is nowhere for it to go. That situation resembles *chatas hamaitah* — a sin-offering that must be left to die — or *holech l'ibud* more generally. But Rabbi Shimon correctly observes that the case of birds that are too young is different, because they will become fit.

Rabbi Shimon's reasoning has a broader foundation. The Gemara cites a Mishnah in which Rabbi Shimon addresses a related principle: *kol hara'ui l'achar zman vehikdisho betoch zmano harei hu b'lo ta'aseh v'ein bo kares* — anything that will be fit after some time, if it was sanctified while not yet fit, and one then slaughtered it outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, one transgresses the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* but without *karet*. The case there involves *oto v'et beno* — a scenario where a parent animal was slaughtered and the offspring was then sanctified and slaughtered outside the Temple on the same day. Because of the prohibition of *oto v'et beno*, this offspring cannot be brought as a *korban* today; but Rabbi Shimon holds that since it will be fit tomorrow, it already has enough significance to trigger the prohibition, even if insufficient for *karet*. The same principle animates his position here: future fitness endows present significance.

### Pidyon Concept

The Gemara asks: *bishlama Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Shimon's position is well understood. But what is the Rabbis' reasoning? Their position, implicitly rejecting Rabbi Shimon's logic, requires explanation, especially since we have no difficulty with *meilah* by animals that are *mechusar zman* — too young to be brought as a *korban* at all. A sheep less than eight days old, for example, clearly has *meilah*. What is the difference between that and a bird that is too young?

The Gemara responds: *mechusar zman mideihavei abaal mum* — an underage animal has *meilah* for the same reason as a blemished animal: *davar pidyon hu*, it is something capable of redemption. Even though a blemished animal cannot be brought as a *korban*, its *kedushah* can be transferred through *pidyon* — it is redeemed, and the proceeds are used to purchase another offering. The *kedushah* is not trapped; it can move forward. For that reason, even while the animal cannot currently be used, the *kedushah* is considered purposeful and *meilah* applies.

But these birds — *kivan de'ein mum poseil be'of*, since the concept of blemish does not apply to bird offerings in the same way — *ein le'ofot pidyon*, bird offerings cannot be redeemed. Tosafot clarifies that it is not precisely the absence of the *mum* category that matters; rather, the point is that nothing can ever allow a bird offering to be redeemed. Whatever deficiency a bird has, *pidyon* is never available. And therefore, because the *kedushah* of these birds cannot go anywhere if they are unfit, even the currently-too-young birds — which are not yet fit — lack *meilah*.

### Pidyon & Meilah

The Gemara's reasoning here, it must be said, is not entirely intuitive. One can understand saying that if something is permanently unusable, like a bird that is too old, the *kedushah* is going nowhere and therefore there is no *meilah*. The case of *pidyon* clarifying matters for blemished animals is also comprehensible: the option of redemption means the *kedushah* is never truly stranded. But when a bird is merely too young, the *kedushah* is not stranded at all — just wait a week or a month, and the bird will be fit. The *kedushah* is very much going somewhere. Why should the theoretical unavailability of *pidyon*, in the event something went wrong, affect the current status of the *kedushah*?

The Gemara's answer seems to be operating on a more abstract level: since there exists a category of cases in which the *kedushah* of these birds would become entirely trapped — any impediment to their use, however caused, produces *holech l'ibud* with no exit — the insufficiency of their current state already raises doubt about the purposefulness of the *kedushah*. For animals, no such trap exists, because *pidyon* is always available. For birds, the trap is always latent. Rabbi Shimon's position, which focuses simply on the fact that the bird will be fit later, remains the more intuitively compelling one. Nevertheless, the Gemara's answer stands, and that is the accepted ruling.

## Kodshim Shemesu

### Rabbi Yochanan

The Gemara now introduces a ruling of Rav Yochanan, transmitted by Ulla: *kodshim shemeisu yatze'u midei me'ilah de'oraissa* — consecrated animals that died have exited the category of *meilah* at the Torah level.

To understand what is striking about this, recall the framework we have been developing. If a *korban* becomes *pasul*, it ordinarily retains *meilah* — the *kedushah* is not diminished by *psul*. Even after slaughter, if it is *psulei mukdashim*, there is a *mitzvah* of *sreifah* — ritual burning — and the animal remains in the orbit of *kedushah*. What removes something from *meilah* is when the *kedushah* is entirely stranded, going nowhere: *holech l'ibud*, as with *chatas hamaitah* or too-old birds. Now Rav Yochanan addresses the case where a consecrated animal simply died — not through a *pasul shechita*, not through any Temple-related disqualification, but died of its own accord. In that case, there is no *mitzvas sreifah* and the animal is simply gone. At the far end, as it were, this too is *ibud* — the *kedushah* is no longer going anywhere — and there is no *meilah* at the Torah level.

One question raised: what if the *shechita* itself was performed incorrectly? That would not be a case of *hekdesh pasul* in the ordinary sense and would not generate a *mitzvas sreifah*. That too would fall into the category of *kodshim shemeisu* — effectively treated as a death — and there would be no *meilah*.

### Rav Chisda Challenge

When Ulla transmitted this ruling, Rav Chisda responded sharply: *man shama lach Rav Yochanan rabach* — who will accept this ruling from you and your teacher Rav Yochanan? *L'chi kedushah she-bahem hechan halcha* — where did the *kedushah* go? The animal was fully *kadosh*. There was nothing wrong with the *kedushah* when it was alive. How does dying cause the *kedushah* to vanish?

The implicit distinction underlying Rav Chisda's challenge is important: in the cases we discussed earlier where there is no *meilah* — *chatas hamaitah*, too-old birds — the *kedushah* was already problematic from the outset or became *holech l'ibud* through some recognized mechanism. But a perfectly fit *korban* that happened to drop dead — its *kedushah* had been entirely valid and functioning. How can it simply disappear?

Ulla responds by citing the Mishnah itself: *turin shelo higia zmanam u-vnei yona she-avar zmanam lo nehenin v'lo moalin* — and the Gemara asks the same question back: where did the *kedushah* go? Tosafot points out, importantly, that the Mishnah does not specify when the consecration took place. If the birds were consecrated at the right age and only subsequently became too old, then the *kedushah* started perfectly good and later became unusable. The Mishnah still rules no *meilah*. Similarly, *chatas hamaitah* need not arise from a defective consecration; a perfectly valid *chatas* can become a *chatas hamaitah* when the owner achieves atonement through a different offering. The entire opening chapter of *Meilah* is full of cases where *kedushah* began in good standing, became unusable, and the ruling is no *meilah*.

### Meilah D'Rabbanan

Ulla concedes the force of the argument. He acknowledges: *modina lach de-ika me'ilah mi-drabbanan* — I admit that at minimum there should be rabbinic *meilah*. Rav Yochanan's statement that they have "exited *meilah*" referred to the Torah level — *de'oraissa*. A rabbinic *meilah* continues to apply even to *kodshim shemeisu* that had begun as valid *kedushah*, as well as to birds that were initially fit and became too old, and to a *chatas* that became a *chatas hamaitah*. The concession is that the *kedushah* does not entirely disappear when it started properly; it diminishes to a rabbinic level.

A terminological distinction is worth noting here. The Mishnah formulates the rule for birds as *lo nehenin v'lo moalin* — no benefit and no *meilah*. *Lo nehenin* is not the same as rabbinic *meilah*. The essential difference: an *issur hana'ah* — a prohibition of benefit, like *chametz b'Pesach* — means one may not derive benefit, but if one does, there is no payment to be made to the *Beit Hamikdash*. Rabbinic *meilah*, by contrast, carries the sense that the *kedushah* is still operative at some level and that one who impermissibly used the object must pay back the value of the benefit. The Rabbis' concession of *meilah d'rabbanan* therefore represents a stronger claim than *lo nehenin* — it implies some residual, actionable *kedushah*.

### Difficulty Raised

There is, however, a further difficulty: *mi ika midi demeikara lo is bei meila ulesof is bei meila* — is there anything that initially had no *meilah* and subsequently acquired *meilah*? The issue is that Rav Yochanan's statement — *kodshim shemeisu yatze'u midei me'ilah de'oraissa* — implies *meilah d'rabbanan* exists afterward. If *kodshim shemeisu* includes *kodshim kalim* that died, the problem becomes acute: while a *korban* of *kodshim kalim* is alive, there is no *meilah* on its meat. Once it dies, would one really say *meilah d'rabbanan* now applies? That would mean the animal's death makes things stricter — it moved further from the *mizbeach* and yet acquired a category of *meilah* it never had before. This is perplexing.

The difficulty is not resolved symmetrically. The Gemara will bring the case of blood — *dam* — as a precedent for something that initially lacks *meilah* and later acquires it. But as will emerge, the blood case may be structurally different.

### Blood Bloodletting

The Gemara cites: *veha dam, demeikara leis bei meila ulesof is bei meila* — and what of blood? Originally there is no *meilah* on blood: *dam betchila ein moalin bo*. Once the blood has been used — gone out to the stream of *Nachal Kidron*, its purpose accomplished — the Gemara considers whether rabbinic *meilah* applies at that later stage. So here too, it seems, we have something that starts without *meilah* and ends with it.

The Gemara responds: *hasam nami ika meila meikara* — that does not bother me, because there is a context in which blood carries *meilah* from the very beginning. As Rav Huna stated (and we will explore this more fully in the next segment): *hamakiz dam levehemas kodashim assur behana'ah umoalin bo* — if one lets blood from a sanctified animal for therapeutic purposes, that blood is forbidden for benefit and carries *meilah*. The blood drawn by bloodletting has never been used for *kaparah* and was never released from *meilah* by the verse's dispensation of *lachem*. It is therefore *meilah* from the outset.

The point is that the concept of *meilah* is not foreign to blood. It is only blood being used on the *mizbeach* for *kaparah* that is released from *meilah* by the verse. Other blood — blood from a wound, blood from bloodletting — remains subject to *meilah* since it is part of the animal itself. Therefore, the later rabbinic application is not creating a category of *meilah* entirely de novo; it is extending an existing concept.

The challenge about *kodshim kalim* dying and then acquiring *meilah d'rabbanan*, however, is left without a fully satisfying answer. The case of blood is structurally different: *meilah* on blood is not a foreign concept, just a limited one. For *kodshim kalim*, *meilah* on the meat was never applicable at any stage. Rav Chisda's difficulty — how can something become stricter after death — remains pressing, and the Gemara leaves it unresolved.

What emerges from the discussion overall: *psulei mukdashim* that became *pasul bakodesh*, with a *mitzvas sreifah* remaining, retain full *meilah*. If the *korban* was *holech l'ibud* — whether through *chatas hamaitah*, too-old birds, or actual death — the Torah-level *meilah* falls away, though rabbinic *meilah* likely persists when the *kedushah* had been valid at some point. These are the poles within which the Gemara is operating.

## Rav Huna & Blood

### Bloodletting Meilah

The Gemara now states explicitly: *amar Rav Huna amar Rav, hamakiz dam levehemas kodashim assur behana'ah umoalin bo* — Rav Huna in the name of Rav rules that if one lets blood from a sanctified animal, that blood is forbidden for benefit and is subject to *meilah*. The reasoning, as we noted, is that this blood was never used for *kaparah* on the *mizbeach*, and therefore the Torah's dispensation — by which blood was excluded from *meilah* because "I have given it to you on the altar to atone" — does not apply. Blood drawn by bloodletting remains part of the animal, intrinsically.

### Milk & Eggs

Rav Hamnuna raises a challenge from the very next Mishnah: *chalav hamukdashin u-veitzei torin lo nehenin v'lo moalin* — milk from sanctified animals and eggs from turtle-doves have no *meilah*. If blood drawn by bloodletting has *meilah* because it is intrinsic to the animal, why should milk and eggs be different? They too come from the animal's body.

### Intrinsic vs Product

The distinction the Gemara draws in response is this: *de-lo mitkayyemat belo dam* — an animal cannot live without blood. Blood is not a product of the animal; it is the animal's very life-force, constitutive of its existence. Therefore, blood drawn from the body of a *korban* is to be treated like the animal's flesh — it is part of the *guf* and carries *meilah* in the same way.

Milk and eggs are different: *de-kamkayyema belo chalav* — an animal can exist without producing milk. These are byproducts, outputs the animal generates, not constituents of the animal itself. Since they are not intrinsic parts of the *korban*, they do not carry *meilah*.

### Dung Question

Rav Mesharshaya then challenges from another case: *ha-zevel v'ha-peresh she-be-chatzer ein nehenin v'ein moalin bahen* — dung and undigested stomach contents found in the area where *kodashim* animals were kept: no benefit may be derived and there is no *meilah*. Now *peresh* — undigested food in the animal's digestive tract — is surely something an animal always contains. If the Gemara's reasoning is that anything the animal cannot exist without is intrinsic and therefore *meilah*, why does *peresh* carry no *meilah*?

The Gemara's response: *mai irya* — what kind of comparison is this? *Ha-dein peresh de-me-alma ka ati lah* — this undigested food comes from the outside. *Azil hai ati achrina* — one portion is processed through and exits, another portion enters from outside to replace it. The undigested food is never truly part of the animal; it is always incoming material passing through the system. Unlike blood, which the body itself produces and in which one is born — *de-migufeh hu* — the food in the digestive tract is external matter in transit. Therefore blood is intrinsic, while *peresh* is not, and the distinction holds.

The observation stands: the ruling about dung and *peresh* is that one may not derive benefit from them — *ein nehenin* — but there is no *meilah*, and their proceeds, if sold, go to the Temple treasury per the principle of Rabbi Eliezer: *kol makom she-ameru chachamim kadosh v'eino kadosh damav yiplu lalishka* — wherever the Rabbis said something is quasi-*kadosh* (with *issur hana'ah* but no *meilah*), the proceeds belong to the Temple. Tosafot, notably, objects to reading *damav yiplu lalishka* as a consequence of impermissibly using such items — which would effectively constitute *meilah d'rabbanan* — and instead reads it as practical guidance to the Temple: if you have such objects where there is *issur hana'ah* but no *meilah*, sell them and use the proceeds for Temple purposes. If someone does impermissibly derive benefit, they have violated *issur hana'ah* — analogous to deriving benefit from *chametz b'Pesach* — but there is no payment owed, no *meilah* even rabbinically.

## Chalav & Beytzim

### No Benefit, No Meilah

The Mishnah states: *chalav hamukdashin u-veitzei torin lo nehenin v'lo moalin* — milk of consecrated animals and eggs of turtle-doves: no benefit may be derived and there is no *meilah*. The formula *lo nehenin v'lo moalin* indicates that these are byproducts of the *korban* itself, not the *korban*; they are not going on the *mizbeach*, so there is no *meilah*. A rabbinic prohibition of benefit still applies.

### Bedek Habayit Case

The Mishnah then draws a sharp contrast: *bameh devarim amurim, bekodshei mizbeach* — when is this so? When the animal was sanctified for the altar. *Aval bekodshei bedek habayis* — but if it was sanctified for the Temple treasury: *hikdish tarnegolet, moalin bah u've'beitzatah* — if one consecrated a chicken, there is *meilah* on the chicken and on its eggs. *Chamora, moalin bah u've'chalvah* — if one consecrated a female donkey, there is *meilah* on it and on its milk.

Why the choice of chicken and donkey as examples? Because one is generally not supposed to consecrate for *bedek habayit* something that could serve as a *korban*. Pigeons and cows are eligible as offerings; to give an undiluted illustration of *bedek habayit* consecration, the Mishnah uses animals that could not be *korbanot*. The ruling is clear: by *bedek habayit*, the eggs and milk carry *meilah*, whereas by the altar they do not.

### Kedushat Types

Why does this difference exist? The answer requires attention to a fundamental conceptual distinction. The *kedushah* of a *korban* is not primarily about the *Beit Hamikdash* acquiring ownership; it is about the animal's identity being directed toward the *mizbeach*. This animal is going to the altar. With that in mind, the milk the animal produces and the eggs the bird lays are not going to the *mizbeach*. They are not the *korban* itself, merely its byproducts. Since they are neither the identity-defined object nor destined for the altar, the *kedushah* does not extend to them, and there is no *meilah*.

*Bedek habayit* consecration, by contrast, is fundamentally about ownership: one transfers ownership of one's asset to the *Beit Hamikdash*. When I transfer my chickens or my donkey to the Temple treasury, the Temple owns them. What does ownership entail? If the Temple owns the chicken, it owns the eggs. If it owns the donkey, it owns the milk. The *kedushah* in this mode is about value and ownership, and value increases — the animals produce — so the *kedushah* extends to all of it. Hence *meilah* on the eggs and the milk.

### Gemara Analysis

The Gemara, however, notices that the Mishnah formulates the *bedek habayit* case specifically. This raises a question: does the distinction depend on *bedek habayit* versus the *mizbeach*, or on a different axis entirely? The Gemara probes whether sanctifying a donkey with *kedushat damim* for the *mizbeach* — that is, sanctifying it so that it will be redeemed and the proceeds used to purchase an *olah* — would yield the same result as *bedek habayit*.

### Kedushat Damim

Rava responds: the Mishnah is incomplete as stated, and must be understood as follows — *hachi katani*: *bameh devarim amurim, keshehikdish kedushat haguf ligabei mizbeach* — the rule that eggs and milk have no *meilah* applies specifically when one has sanctified an animal with *kedushat haguf* for the altar, that is, the animal itself is to be the *korban*. *Aval hikdish kedushat damim ligabei mizbeach* — but if one sanctified the animal with *kedushat damim* for the *mizbeach*, meaning its monetary value will ultimately be used to purchase an offering, *naaseh k'mi shehikdisho liBedek Habayit* — it is treated like *bedek habayit* consecration, and *meilah* applies to the eggs and the milk.

This is a clarifying Gemara: the operative distinction is not *mizbeach* versus *bedek habayit*, but *kedushat haguf* versus *kedushat damim*. Where the *kedushah* is about the identity and function of the thing itself — *kedushat haguf* — byproducts are not drawn in. Where the *kedushah* is about value and cost — *kedushat damim* — the *kedushah* extends to whatever increases that value, and so the eggs and milk carry *meilah*.

It is worth noting an additional nuance. If one sanctifies a donkey for an *olah*, it is not that the *Beit Hamikdash* now owns the donkey in the sense that they can come to collect it. The owner is still going to sell it himself and bring the proceeds to purchase an *olah*. Yet the *kedushah* is still considered to be about value — not about identity. The Gemara is teaching that the defining feature of *kedushat damim* is that what matters is the cost and the monetary representation of the object, not the object's own physical trajectory toward the *mizbeach*. When *kedushah* is about value, value increases are encompassed; when *kedushah* is about identity, only the thing itself carries it.

### Summary & Conclusion

Several key conceptual points emerge from today's discussion and deserve to be drawn together.

First, the categories of things that exit *meilah*: *pasulei mukdashim* that became *pasul bakodesh*, with a *mitzvas sreifah* remaining, retain full *meilah*. When something becomes *holech l'ibud* — whether through *chatas hamaitah*, birds too old to be used, or actual death without *psul bakodesh* — *meilah* at the Torah level falls away, though rabbinic *meilah* likely persists wherever the *kedushah* had been valid at its inception.

Second, the distinction between *issur hana'ah* and *meilah d'rabbanan*: an *issur hana'ah* means benefit is forbidden but there is no payment if one transgresses; *meilah d'rabbanan* carries the notion of a residual, actionable *kedushah* such that benefit improperly taken must be repaid to the Temple. Tosafot is careful to insist that most of the cases formulated as *lo nehenin v'lo moalin* are *issur hana'ah* without *meilah d'rabbanan*.

Third, on byproducts: milk and eggs are products of the animal, not parts of it, and carry no *meilah* when the animal was sanctified as a *korban*. Blood, by contrast, is intrinsic to the animal — *de-migufeh hu* — and does carry *meilah* wherever it has not been used for *kaparah* on the *mizbeach*.

Fourth, and most broadly: the central conceptual axis for both *kedushah* and *meilah* is whether the *kedushah* concerns the identity of the object or its value. *Kedushat haguf* — where the thing itself is directed toward the altar — extends only to the thing itself; byproducts are not drawn in. *Kedushat damim* — where the *kedushah* is about the monetary value of the object, whether for *bedek habayit* or for ultimately purchasing a *korban* — extends to everything that increases that value. The *Beit Hamikdash* need not be the direct owner for *kedushat damim* to apply; what matters is that the concept of *kedushah* operative in the case is one of value, not of identity. When it is, the eggs, the milk, and everything that increases the monetary worth is swept in, and *meilah* applies.
