# Meilah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dishun Mizbeach & Menorah

### Mishnah: No Hana'ah, No…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דִּישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, וְהַמְּנוֹרָה – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. הַמַּקְדִּישׁ דִּישּׁוּן בַּתְּחִלָּה – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ. תּוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן, וּבְנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּן – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the removal of ash** from **the inner altar** to the place where the ashes lifted from the outer altar are deposited, **and** similarly with regard to the wicks of **the Candelabrum,** one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*; **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for their **misuse.** In the case of **one who consecrates anew** the **ash** that has been **removed,** he is liable for **misusing it.** With regard to **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not arrived,** as they are too young, **and pigeons whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has passed,** as they are too old, one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*; **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for their **misuse.**

The daf opens by continuing with the theme of cases in which there is no *me'ilah*. The *mishnah* at the bottom of 11b states: *dishun mizbeach hapnimi v'hamenorah lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* — the ashes of the inner altar and the *menorah* carry no *me'ilah* and one may not derive benefit from them. The Gemara preceding this had addressed the daily *terumat hadeshen*, the removal of a handful of ashes from the outer *mizbeach*, along with the larger question of at what stage the ashes in the *tapuach* — the great ash heap — lose their *me'ilah* status. Here the subject shifts to the ashes generated inside the *heichal*: the *menorah*, whose lamps burn, and the inner gold *mizbeach*, upon which the *ketores* is offered daily, both producing ash that must periodically be cleaned away.

### Na'asah Mitzvaso

The governing principle here is *na'asah mitzvaso* — once the *mitzvah* has been performed, the object's sacred purpose is complete and *me'ilah* no longer applies. Interestingly, the *Gemara* does not explore precisely when that moment arrives for these ashes. *Tosafos* supplies the answer by reference to another *Gemara*: in the case of the *ketores*, it is when the column of aromatic smoke begins to rise. This is structurally similar to the rule derived earlier for the *emurim* of *kodshim kalim*, where *me'ilah* ceases essentially as soon as burning begins.

### Trumas HaDeshen Distinct…

One might ask why the inner *mizbeach* and *menorah* ashes follow a different timeline than the outer *mizbeach* ashes, where the question of when *me'ilah* ends required far more analysis. *Tosafos* explains that the *mitzvah* of *terumat hadeshen* — the formal, daily removal of ashes described at the beginning of *Parashat Tzav* — applies only to the outer *mizbeach*, and even there only to the *olah* or perhaps to *kodshei kodshim* more broadly. Where there is no such discrete *mitzvah* of ash-removal, the object's *mitzvah* is not performed by means of the ashes at all; the *mitzvah* is performed by the object that burns. Therefore *na'asah mitzvaso* occurs as soon as the burning begins, even before the ash stage. This explains why the extended analysis required for the outer *mizbeach* — whether *me'ilah* persists until *terumat hadeshen* or until the ashes are fully carried outside — simply does not apply here.

### Hamakdish Dishun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקְדִּישׁ דִּישּׁוּן בַּתְּחִלָּה – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who consecrates anew** the **ash** that has been **removed,** he is liable for **misusing it.**

The *mishnah* then adds: *hamakdish dishun batchilah mo'alin bo* — if one consecrates ashes at the outset, there is *me'ilah* upon them. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* seek to read this in the specific context of the ash-removal process, understanding it as a case of re-consecrating already-burned ashes, which involves some complexity. The *Rambam*, however, reads the statement with elegant simplicity: it is simply establishing the principle that no matter how trivial the object being consecrated — even a heap of ashes, even dung — the act of *hekdesh* still produces *me'ilah*. Since the *mishnah* is discussing ashes, it naturally uses ashes as its example.

## Turin & Bnei Yona Meilah

### Mishnah: Young/Old Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן, וּבְנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּן – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not arrived,** as they are too young, **and pigeons whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has passed,** as they are too old, one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*; **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for their **misuse.**

The *mishnah* turns to a case involving birds used as *korbanot*: *torin shelo higia zmanam u'vnei yona she'avar zmanam lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin*. A *tor* (turtledove) must be an adult to be brought as a *korban*, and a *ben yona* (young pigeon) must be young. If one consecrated a *tor* before it has matured, or a *ben yona* that has already passed its valid age, the *mishnah* rules: one may not derive benefit from them, but there is no *me'ilah*. The question that will animate the *Gemara* discussion — returned to in a later passage — is why this is different from a standard *pasul* animal, in which case *me'ilah* certainly does apply.

## Ashes Placement Sources

### Where Inner Ashes Go

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״וְשָׂמוֹ אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהֵסִיר אֶת מֻרְאָתוֹ בְּנֹצָתָהּ״ – אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְמִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְמִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי.
>
> **Translation:** that the ashes of **the external altar** are subject to misuse, **as it is written in** the context of those ashes: **"And he shall put it beside the altar"** (Leviticus 6:3). This teaches that these ashes must be interred, despite the fact that their mitzva has been performed by its removal, and therefore they are subject to misuse. But **from where do we** derive that the ashes of **the inner altar** are also subject to misuse? **Rabbi Elazar said** in response: It is derived from the fact **that the verse states** with regard to a bird sacrificed as a burnt offering: **"And he shall take away its crop with its feathers,** and cast it beside the altar on the east part, in the place of the ashes" (Leviticus 1:16), referring to the location for the placement of the removed ashes. **If** this verse **is not needed for** the **matter of the external altar,** as that *halakha* is already derived from the phrase: "And he shall put them beside the altar" (Leviticus 6:3), **apply it** to the **matter of** the ashes of **the inner altar,** teaching that these ashes must also be placed there.

The *Gemara* now turns to a different question raised by the *mishnah* about the ashes of the inner *mizbeach* and *menorah*. The *mishnah* mentions the removal of these ashes but does not specify where they go. The *Gemara* presupposes — and *Tosafos* explains this as the *Gemara*'s underlying assumption — that one places those ashes in the same location as the ashes of the outer *mizbeach*: at the side of the ramp. The *Gemara* then asks: while this placement is explicit for the outer *mizbeach*, where is the source for the inner *mizbeach*?

The derivation begins by noting the verse in the daily *terumat hadeshen* passage: *vesamo eitzel hamizbeach* — he places it beside the *mizbeach*. That is the source for the outer *mizbeach*. For the inner *mizbeach*, Rabbi Elazar points to a verse in the law of the *olat ha'of* (the bird burnt-offering): *ve'hishlich oto eitzel hamizbeach kedma el mekom hadashen* — and he shall cast it beside the *mizbeach* eastward, to the place of the ashes. When one brings this bird *korban*, the innards are cast to the ash-heap beside the ramp. This verse is read as providing the derivation for the inner *mizbeach* as well.

### Hermeneutics of Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּמִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, וְלִקְבּוֹעַ לוֹ מָקוֹם! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. מַאי ״הַדָּשֶׁן״? דַּאֲפִילּוּ מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this conclusion: One can **say** that **this and that,** i.e., both verses cited above, are stated **with regard to** the ashes that are on **the external altar, and** the additional verse is necessary **to fix its place,** i.e., that it should be put on the east part, which is mentioned only in Leviticus 1:16. If so, there is no source for the placement of the ashes of the inner altar. The Gemara answers: **If so,** that both verses are referring to the external altar, and the sole purpose of Leviticus 1:16 is to fix its place, **let** that **verse** merely **state:** "And he shall take away its crop with its feathers and cast it **beside the altar,"** and it would be understood that the two verses are referring to the same place, as the identical phrase "beside the altar" appears in the other verse. **What** is the reason for the additional phrase "in the place of **the ashes"?** It teaches **that even** the ashes of the **inner altar** are placed there.

The *Gemara* employs a *gezeirah shava* to establish this. It notes that both the *terumat hadeshen* passage and the bird-offering passage use the phrase *eitzel hamizbeach*. One might therefore suppose that the bird verse did not need to add *el mekom hadashen* — "to the place of the ashes" — since a *gezeirah shava* on the shared phrase *eitzel hamizbeach* would have been sufficient to tell us where the bird's innards go. The additional phrase *el mekom hadashen* is therefore read as extra language, carrying an independent teaching.

### Pesuk Redundancy Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוֹרָה מְנָלַן? ״דָּשֶׁן״, ״הַדָּשֶׁן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the ashes of the **Candelabrum** are also placed to the east of the altar? The Gemara answers: It is derived from the definite article in: "The ashes," as the verse could have said **"ashes"** and instead it said **"the ashes."** This addition serves to include the ashes of the Candelabrum.

The *Gemara* challenges this reasoning: perhaps the phrase *el mekom hadashen* is not redundant at all. Perhaps it is needed in its plain sense — to specify, within the bird-offering law itself, precisely which location beside the *mizbeach* the innards are cast, namely the same spot as the ash deposit. On that reading the verse is not extra and cannot be redirected toward the inner *mizbeach*. The *Gemara* responds: if the verse's only intent were to specify the exact spot for the outer *mizbeach*, it could have done so implicitly through the *gezeirah shava* on *eitzel hamizbeach* alone. The deliberate addition of *el mekom hadashen* — "the place of the ashes" — must therefore carry extra significance. *Mai hadashen* — what does "the ashes-place" add? It teaches that this designated location is a place of ashes even for the ashes of the inner *mizbeach*.

The *Gemara* then asks the same question about the *menorah*: *menorah minalan*? This is derived from a slight textual emphasis — *deshen hadeshen* — the doubled root that singles out this spot as a place of ashes even for the *menorah*'s ash. 

This discussion raises a notable conceptual tension. Throughout, the *Gemara* has treated the absence of a formal *mitzvah* of *terumat hadeshen* as the reason *me'ilah* ends so early for the inner *mizbeach* and *menorah*. Yet by establishing that even their ashes go to the designated ash-spot beside the outer *mizbeach*, there is now a kind of procedural echo of *terumat hadeshen* in the handling of those ashes — even though the *mitzvah* itself does not apply. The practical placement is the same; the *mitzvah*-status is not. These are conceptually distinct, and the *Gemara* leaves that distinction intact without harmonizing it further.

## Turin & Bnei Yona Meil…

### Rabbi Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר תּוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, בְּנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּם – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן. גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְאַחַר זְמַן, וְהִקְדִּישׁוֹ בְּתוֹךְ זְמַנּוֹ – הֲרֵי הוּא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna teaches that one may not derive benefit from doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived and from pigeons whose time of fitness for sacrifice has passed, but one who derived benefit from them is not liable for their misuse. **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees with this ruling and **says:** With regard to **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not arrived,** one is liable for **misusing them.** With regard to **pigeons whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has passed,** one **may not derive benefit** *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for their **misuse.** GEMARA: This and the preceding mishna indicate that the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon disagree as to whether or not doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived are subject to misuse. The Gemara clarifies their opinions: **Granted,** one can understand the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as** he himself **teaches** his **reason** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 112b). **As Rabbi Shimon would say:** In the case of **any** sacrificial animal **that is fit to** be sacrificed **after** the passage of **time,** e.g., doves that will be fit for sacrifice when they mature, if **one consecrated it before its time** of fitness and slaughtered it outside the Temple courtyard, **that** person is **in** violation of **a prohibition but there is no** liability to receive ***karet* for it.**

Returning to the birds, *Rabbi Shimon* dissents from the *mishnah*'s blanket ruling. He holds: *torin shelo higia zmanam mo'alin bahen* — if you consecrated *torin* (turtledoves) before they reached maturity, there *is* *me'ilah*, because they will simply grow older and become fit for a *korban*. However, *bnei yona she'avar zmanam* — if the *bnei yona* have already passed their valid age — then he agrees: *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin bahen*. Once they are permanently unfit, there is no *me'ilah*.

The *Gemara* explains that *Rabbi Shimon*'s position follows from a broader principle he articulates elsewhere, most prominently in the *mishnah* in Chullin regarding *oto v'et beno* applied to *kodshim*: *kol hara'ui l'achar zman vehikdisho betoch zmano harei hu b'lo ta'aseh v'ein bo kares*. Anything that will become fit later, even if it cannot be offered today, still carries the force of the prohibition against slaughtering a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. It transgresses the *lav* — though without *karet*, since it is not presently fit. The underlying principle is that future fitness confers present significance. It is therefore entirely coherent that *Rabbi Shimon* would hold that birds too young to be offered still carry *me'ilah*, since they will be fit in time.

### No Pidyon, No Meilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבָּנַן: מַאי שְׁנָא מִמְּחוּסַּר זְמַן? אָמְרִי: מְחוּסַּר זְמַן – מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַבַּעַל מוּם, דְּבַר פִּדְיוֹן הוּא. אֲבָל הָנֵי עוֹפוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּאֵין מוּם פּוֹסֵל בְּעוֹפוֹת – אֵין לָעוֹפוֹת פִּדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** according to the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived are not subject to misuse, in **what** way **is** this case **different from** an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived, and yet it can be consecrated? An animal whose time has not yet arrived enters the pen to be tithed together with the other animals (see *Bekhorot* 56a). Why is the case of the young doves any different? The Rabbis would **say** in response that the cases are not comparable. An animal **whose time has not yet** arrived can indeed be consecrated, **just as is** the *halakha* **with regard to a blemished** animal, **which** can be consecrated, although only to the degree **that** it **is subject to redemption. But** in the case of **these birds, since a blemish does not render birds unfit, there is no** possibility of **redemption for** blemished **birds.** Therefore, one cannot compare the case of animal, which is subject to redemption, to the case of a bird whose time has not yet arrived.

The *Gemara* then asks: the Rabbis who disagree with *Rabbi Shimon* on the birds — why do they not follow the parallel reasoning applied to an animal that is *mechusar zman*, less than eight days old? If you consecrate such an animal, certainly there is *me'ilah* even though it cannot yet be offered. The birds are in the same position — wait a while and they will be fit. Why should the ruling differ?

The *Gemara* answers: *mechusar zman* carries *me'ilah* because it is *midei d'havei abaal mum* — it is like an animal with a blemish. And why does a blemished animal retain *me'ilah* even though it cannot be offered? Because it is *davar pidyon hu* — it is something that can be redeemed. The availability of *pidyon* (redemption) keeps the *kedushah* purposeful even when the animal is presently unfit. The *kedushah* is not stuck; it can move forward, to another animal, through redemption. But these birds — *kivan de'ein mum poseil be'of* — since there is no operative concept of *mum* (blemish) for birds that triggers *pidyon*, birds simply cannot be redeemed. *Tosafos* clarifies that the *Gemara*'s formulation is not precisely accurate: it is not that birds are never afflicted with disqualifying conditions, but that no condition affecting birds ever permits *pidyon*. The result is the same: *ein le'ofot pidyon*.

Since birds cannot be redeemed, their *kedushah*, whenever they are unfit, has nowhere to go. This is structurally identical to the *holech l'ibud* category — the *kedushah* is headed toward loss with no remedy. Everyone therefore agrees that once the birds are past their usable age, there is no *me'ilah*.

The puzzle remains, however, and it is worth acknowledging: if the birds are presently too young and will become fit without any intervention — simply by waiting — why should the theoretical impossibility of *pidyon* matter? The *kedushah* is not stuck right now; it will find its outlet in a matter of days. *Rabbi Shimon*'s position, that future fitness grants present significance, seems more compelling on this point. The *Gemara*'s answer is that because the species of bird is one in which the *kedushah* can become permanently stuck with no recourse, even the present phase — where things are actually fine — is viewed through that lens of vulnerability. *Rabbi Shimon*'s reasoning is nonetheless acknowledged as the more intuitively attractive.

### Birds & Blemishes

The *Gemara* continues, noting that to say birds are entirely immune to disqualifying physical conditions is an overstatement. There are conditions — *mechusar eiver*, for example, the absence of a limb — that do disqualify a bird. The precise point is not the absence of any *halakhic* category of disqualification, but the categorical absence of *pidyon* as a mechanism. No condition affecting a bird, whatever its nature, ever opens the door to redemption. That, and not the technical language of *mum*, is what distinguishes birds from animals and closes off the *me'ilah* status for birds that cannot presently be offered.

## Kodshim Shemesu Debate

### Kodshim Shemesu No Meilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָדָשִׁים שֶׁמֵּתוּ, יָצְאוּ מִידֵי מְעִילָה דְּבַר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Sacrificial** animals **that died** without being sacrificed **are excluded from** the *halakhot* of **misuse by Torah law.** This is because they are no longer fit to be sacrificed, and therefore are no longer in the category of: "The sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). They cannot be redeemed either, since one may not redeem sacrificial animals merely in order to feed them to the dogs.

The *Gemara* introduces a ruling from *Ulla* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *kodshim shemeisu yatze'u midei me'ilah de'oraisa* — consecrated animals that have died exit the category of biblical *me'ilah*. This is a striking *Gemara*. We have established that a *pasul* animal, one that became unfit in the context of the *avodah*, retains *me'ilah* — indeed, there is a *mitzvat sreifah* (obligation to burn it), and the *me'ilah* persists. What removes *me'ilah* is *holech l'ibud*: being headed toward loss with no usable role, as with the *chatat hamaisa* or the birds past their age. Now we reach the far end: an animal that was perfectly fine, never subjected to any *pesul*, and then simply died. It was never *pasul bakodesh* in the technical sense, there is no *mitzvat sreifah*, it has simply become useless. *Rabbi Yochanan* rules that in such a case, *me'ilah de'oraisa* falls away.

### Kedushah's Whereabouts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב עוּלָּא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאן שָׁמַע לָךְ וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רַבָּךְ?! וְכִי קְדוּשָּׁה שֶׁבָּהֶן לְהֵיכָן הָלְכָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Ulla was sitting** in the study hall **and he recited this *halakha*** in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **Rav Ḥisda said to him: Who will listen to you and Rabbi Yoḥanan, your teacher,** with regard to this opinion, that such offerings are not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse by Torah law? After all, **to where has** the **sanctity that was** inherent **in them** until they died **gone?**

*Rav Chisda* challenges this forcefully: *l'chi kedushah she'bahem hechan halcha?* — where, then, did the *kedushah* go? His challenge is conceptually different from the cases discussed earlier. If the *kedushah* started as problematic — like a *chatat hamaisa* or an overage bird — one might accept that it never rose to the level required for *me'ilah*. But here we have an animal whose *kedushah* was entirely valid and purposeful, and then it dropped dead. How does the *kedushah* simply vanish?

### Derabbanan Meilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תִּיקְשֵׁי מַתְנִיתִין: ״תּוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן וּבְנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּם – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין״, הָכָא נָמֵי נֵימָא: קְדוּשָּׁה שֶׁבָּהֶן לְהֵיכָן הָלְכָה?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ דְּאִיכָּא מְעִילָה מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to** Rav Ḥisda: According to your reasoning, **the mishna** itself **should present a difficulty,** as it teaches: With regard to **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not arrived,** as they are too young, **and pigeons whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has passed,** as they are too old, one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for their **misuse. Here too,** one can **say: To where has** the **sanctity that was** inherent **in** the pigeons whose time of fitness for sacrifice has passed **gone?** Why are they no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? Rav Ḥisda **said to** Ulla in response: I agree that both the sacrificial animals that died and the pigeons whose time of fitness for sacrifice has passed are not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse by Torah law. **I** also **concede to you that** in the case of sacrificial animals that died and doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived **there is** a prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property **by rabbinic law.**

*Ulla* responds by pointing to our *mishnah*: *torin shelo higia zmanam u'vnei yona she'avar zmanam lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin*. Does *Rav Chisda* not ask the same question there — where did the *kedushah* go? *Tosafos* notes that *Rav Chisda*'s challenge would apply there too, since the *mishnah*'s case of overage birds encompasses the scenario in which the birds were originally consecrated at a valid age and subsequently grew too old. The *kedushah* thus started perfectly good and later became unusable. Similarly, the very first *mishnah* of the *perek* — the *chatat hamaisa* — can arise from an animal that was validly consecrated and then became a *chatat hamaisa* because the owner secured atonement through another *korban*. The entire *perek* is replete with cases in which the *kedushah* began well, became unusable, and *me'ilah* nonetheless fell away.

*Rav Chisda* concedes the point — *modina lach* — but makes a critical distinction: *de'ika me'ilah mi'derabbanan*. He accepts that biblical *me'ilah* falls away, as *Rabbi Yochanan* ruled, but insists that at minimum there should be rabbinic *me'ilah* when the *kedushah* started good. *Tosafos* reads the text to indicate that *Ulla* is conceding this point as well: *de'oraisa* no, *de'rabbanan* there remains something.

This matters practically. *Lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* — as the *mishnah* phrases it — is not the same as rabbinic *me'ilah*. The former is an *issur hana'ah* (prohibition against deriving benefit), analogous to *chametz be'Pesach*: one transgresses if one derives benefit, but there is no concept of repayment. *Me'ilah de'rabbanan*, by contrast, carries a sense that the object remains in some fashion owned by or tied to the *hekdesh*, such that misappropriating it generates an obligation to restore value — to pay back what one took. *Rav Chisda* argues that when the *kedushah* had a proper start, that residual connection should survive even the animal's death at the level of *de'rabbanan*.

### Status Change Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא לִי: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא אִית בֵּיהּ מְעִילָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף אִית בֵּיהּ מְעִילָה? וְלָא? וְהָא אִיכָּא דָּם, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לֵית בַּהּ מְעִילָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף אִית בַּהּ מְעִילָה, דִּתְנַן: דָּם, בַּתְּחִלָּה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, יָצָא לְנַחַל קִדְרוֹן – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ! אָמְרִי: הָתָם נָמֵי אִיכָּא מְעִילָה מֵעִיקָּרָא,
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, I am no longer troubled by the question of where the sanctity has gone. **But** there is another matter that is **difficult for me: Is there anything where initially,** when it was consecrated, it is **not subject to** the *halakhot* of **misuse, and ultimately** it is **subject to** the *halakhot* of **misuse** by rabbinic law, such as these doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived? The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that there is **no** instance of an item that is initially not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, and in the end is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? **But there is** the case of **blood, which initially** is **not subject** to the *halakhot* of **misuse, and ultimately** it is **subject** to the *halakhot* of **misuse. As we learned** in the mishna (11a): With regard to **blood, at** its **outset,** before it is sprinkled on the altar, one is **not** liable for **misusing it,** but once it **emerges** via the canal that runs through the Temple **to the Kidron Valley** at the foot of the Temple Mount, one is liable for **misusing it.** The Sages **say** in response: This is not a proof, as **there too,** in the case of blood **it is** subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse initially.**

A further difficulty is raised: *mi ika midi de'meikara lo is bei me'ilah u'lesof is bei me'ilah?* — is there any case in which something begins without *me'ilah* and ends up with *me'ilah* — even *de'rabbanan*? The concern is directed specifically at the implicit corollary of *Rav Chisda*'s position: if *kodshim shemeisu* includes *kodshim kalim* — animals of lesser sanctity for which there is no *me'ilah* while alive — are we to say that once they die, they acquire rabbinic *me'ilah*? That would mean dying makes the *halakha* stricter, an outcome that is conceptually jarring. The *Gemara* raises this as a serious challenge to the *de'rabbanan* formulation and the discussion continues with this unresolved tension.

### Blood Drawn from Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב: הַמַּקִּיז דָּם לְבֶהֱמַת קָדָשִׁים, אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav said:** In a case of **one who lets blood from** a live **sacrificial animal,** deriving **benefit** from that blood **is prohibited and** one is liable for **misusing it** by Torah law. Since there is a stage when there is a prohibition of misuse by Torah law, one can understand the *halakha* that one is liable by rabbinic law for misusing the blood ultimately, when it descended to the Kidron Valley. This is not comparable to doves whose time of fitness for sacrifice has not arrived, as they are initially not subject to misuse by Torah law.

The *Gemara* introduces the case of blood to address the parallel structure. *Amar Rav: hamakiz dam livehemas kodshim assur be'hana'ah u'mo'alin bo* — if one performs bloodletting on a consecrated animal, that blood is prohibited for benefit and is subject to *me'ilah*. This is because blood is intrinsic to the animal, not a by-product: *de'lo mitkayemet belo dam* — the animal cannot survive without its blood. Since blood is the lifeforce of the animal, the blood of a *korban* is treated as part of the *korban* itself, and all *me'ilah* that applies to the animal applies to its blood — unless and until that blood is the specific blood being used on the *mizbeach* for *kapparah*.

This is somewhat ironic. The earlier discussion in the *Gemara* established that there is no *me'ilah* on the blood of a *korban* after *zerikah* (sprinkling), based on the verse *lachem n'tateihu* and related *pesukim* indicating the blood was "given" for *kapparah* and not for *me'ilah*. But that exclusion applies only to blood designated and used for *kapparah*. Blood that exits the animal through bloodletting — blood that has nothing to do with *kapparah* — remains part of the animal and carries full *me'ilah*.

The *Gemara* raises blood in the context of the status-change puzzle: does blood not represent a case where *me'ilah* applies first (*de'oraisa*), then falls away (after *zerikah*), and then returns (*de'rabbanan* once it flows to the *nachal kidron*)? The *Gemara* suggests that this is not a pure example of something that begins without *me'ilah* and acquires it, since blood always had *me'ilah* in principle — the post-*zerikah* rabbinic *me'ilah* is not foreign to the category. The question of whether one can legitimately impose rabbinic *me'ilah* on something that *never* had *me'ilah* — like the meat of *kodshim kalim* that died — is left as a genuine difficulty without full resolution.

## Parts of Korban & Meilah

### Blood as Animal Part

The next *mishnah* takes up the question of which parts and products of a consecrated animal carry *me'ilah*. The discussion of the bloodletting case from the preceding *Gemara* serves as a pivot: since *de'lo mitkayemet belo dam* — an animal cannot survive without its blood — blood is considered intrinsically part of the animal. The *me'ilah* that applies to the animal's body extends to its blood in any context other than the blood used on the *mizbeach* for *kapparah*. This stands as a baseline: if it is truly part of the animal, it shares the animal's status.

### Milk & Eggs No Meilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַב: הַמַּקִּיז דָּם לְבֶהֱמַת קָדָשִׁים – אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא חֲלֵב הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, וּבֵיצֵי תוֹרִין – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** In a case of **one who lets blood from a sacrificial animal, deriving benefit** from that blood **is prohibited and** one is liable for **misusing it. Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav from the mishna below: With regard to **the milk of** animals **consecrated** to be sacrificed **and the eggs of doves** consecrated to be sacrificed, one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them after the fact he is **not** liable for their **misuse,** despite the fact that one is liable for misuse of the animals and doves themselves. Apparently, the products of a consecrated item do not share its status with regard to the *halakha* of misuse. Why doesn't this principle apply to blood as well?

*Chalav hamukdashin u'veitzei torin lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* — milk from consecrated animals and eggs from consecrated turtledoves carry no *me'ilah* and one may not derive benefit from them. *Rav Hamnuna* raises a challenge from this *mishnah* to the principle that blood carries *me'ilah*: if bloodletting blood has *me'ilah* because the animal cannot live without blood, should not the same apply to milk or eggs, since presumably some physiological continuity exists there as well?

### Dung Not Part of Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן, לְגַבֵּי דָּם, דְּלֹא מִתְקַיֶּימֶת בְּלָא דָּם. אֲבָל חֵלֶב, דְּקָא מִקַּיְימָא בְּלֹא חֵלֶב – לָא. מֵתִיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הַזֶּבֶל וְהַפֶּרֶשׁ שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – אֵין נֶהֱנִין וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין. וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמֶיהָ לַלִּשְׁכָּה. אַמַּאי? הָכָא נָמֵי, לָא מְקַיֵּים בְּלֹא פֶּרֶשׁ! אָמְרִי: מַאי אִירְיָא, הָדֵין פֶּרֶשׁ דְּמִן עָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לַהּ, אָזֵיל הַאי – אָתֵי אַחֲרִינָא. לְאַפּוֹקֵי דָּם, מִגּוּפַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to** Rav Hamnuna in response: **When we said** the products of a consecrated item are also subject to the *halakha* of misuse that was only **with regard to blood, as** the animal **cannot exist without blood** and therefore the blood is considered like the animal itself. **But** in the case of **milk, since** the animal can **exist without milk,** the milk is **not** considered like the animal itself. **Rav Mesharshiyya raises an objection** to this suggestion from a *baraita*: One **may not derive benefit** from **the** dried **manure and the** fresh **dung** of offerings of the most sacred order found **in the** Temple **courtyard** *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for **misusing** them; **and** the **money** received from **their** sale **will** be **allocated for the** treasury **chamber** of the Temple. In light of the suggested distinction between blood and milk, the Gemara asks: **Why** is the dung not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? **Here too,** the animal **cannot exist without dung,** and therefore the dung should be subject to the *halakhot* of misuse like blood. The Sages **say** in response: **How** can you **compare** the two cases? In the case of **this dung that comes to** the animal **from an external** source, i.e., the food that it ate, **this** food **goes** out of the body in the form of dung and **that other** food **comes** into the body and takes its place. This description serves **to exclude blood, which is** part **of** the animal's **body** and is not replaced from an external source.

The response draws a sharp distinction: milk and eggs are products of the animal — the animal can exist *without* producing them continuously. They are something the animal generates and separates from itself; they are not the constitutive substance of the animal. Blood is categorically different. The animal is, at every moment, sustained by its blood. Blood does not merely pass through the animal — it is the animal's vital substance. Milk and eggs, however, are generated and expelled, and the animal persists without them. They are by-products; blood is the animal itself.

*Rav Mesharshaya* raises a further challenge from a *beraita*: *hazevel v'haferesh she'bechatzer ein nehenin v'ein mo'alin bahen v'yinatenu lalishkah* — the dung and the *peresh* (undigested stomach contents) found in the courtyard of consecrated animals carry no *me'ilah*, and their proceeds go to the *lishkah* (temple treasury). If even these must be handled with some care, does that not imply that the animal cannot exist without them either?

The *Gemara* dismisses the comparison: *ha'dein peresh de'me'alma ka'ati lah* — this material comes from the outside world. Food enters, is processed, and passes through; what is always present in the digestive tract at any given moment is simply the most recent intake, not something intrinsic to the animal's constitution. *Azil hai ati achrina* — one portion exits, the next intake replaces it. This is fundamentally unlike blood, which is *de'migufeh hu* — it is of and from the animal's own body. The *Gemara* also adds, per the *mefaresh*, that we are born with blood already within us; it is constitutive from the outset. Food, dung, and undigested contents are always passing through and never become part of the animal in any meaningful sense.

### Kedushah Framework in Dung

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָתָנֵי לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין וְדָמָיו לַלִּשְׁכָּה. מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ – דָּמָיו יִפְּלוּ לַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The *baraita* **teaches** that one **may not derive benefit** from the manure and dung *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for **misuse, and** the **money** received from **their** sale **will** be **allocated for the** treasury **chamber** of the Temple. This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said: Wherever the Sages said** an item is **consecrated and not consecrated,** as in this case where one may not derive benefit but he is not liable for misuse either, the **money** received from **its** sale is **allocated for the** treasury **chamber** of the Temple.

The *Gemara* then notes that the law of *Rabbi Eliezer* applies to cases like these: *kol makom she'amru chachamim kadosh v'eino kadosh damav yiplu lalishkah* — wherever the Rabbis characterize something as quasi-sacred, the proceeds go to the temple treasury. *Tosafos* objects strenuously to reading this as a consequence triggered by one who improperly benefits from these items (which would amount to *me'ilah de'rabbanan*). *Tosafos* insists that *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* means there is no *me'ilah* even rabbinically — it is simply an *issur hana'ah*, analogous to *chametz be'Pesach*, without any payment obligation. The instruction that *damav yiplu lalishkah* is not a penalty imposed on the transgressor but rather practical guidance to the temple: since there is no *issur me'ilah* but there is *issur hana'ah*, the temple should not let these items go to waste; it should sell them and channel the proceeds to the treasury. No payment arises from the act of improper benefit itself.

## Guf vs. Damim Kedushah

### Milk/Eggs by Mizbeach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֲלֵב הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין וּבֵיצֵי תוֹרִין – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the milk of sacrificial** animals **and the eggs of** sacrificial **doves,** one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them after the fact he is **not** liable for their **misuse.** **In what** case **is this statement,** that if one derived benefit from the eggs or milk of sacrificial animals, he is not liable for their misuse, **said?** It is stated **in** the case of **sacrificial** animals offered on the **altar,** as their eggs and milk are not brought to the altar and therefore they are considered distinct from the offerings themselves.

The *mishnah* states: milk and eggs from animals or birds consecrated for the *mizbeach* carry no *me'ilah* and may not be used for benefit. This applies specifically to animals whose *kedushah* is directed toward the altar — *kodshei mizbeach*.

### Milk/Eggs by Bedek Habayit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, הִקְדִּישׁ תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ וּבְבֵיצָתָהּ, חֲמוֹרָה – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ וּבַחֲלָבָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** this is not the *halakha* **in** the case of animals that are not sacrificed and are **consecrated** only **for Temple maintenance.** For example, if one **consecrated a hen** he is liable for **misusing it and for** misusing **its egg;** if one consecrated **a donkey** he is liable for **misusing it and for** misusing **its milk,** as the animal and its milk, and likewise the hen and its eggs, are both consecrated for Temple maintenance and are deemed a single unit.

The *mishnah* then draws a crucial distinction: *aval bekodshei bedek habayis hikdish tarnegolet mo'alin bah u've'beitzatah chamor mo'alin bo u've'chalbo* — if one consecrated a chicken or a donkey for the temple treasury (*bedek habayit*), *me'ilah* applies to the animal and to its eggs or milk. The examples of a chicken and a donkey are deliberately chosen: animals that *could* serve as *korbanot* should not normally be consecrated for *bedek habayit*, so the *mishnah* uses examples of animals that cannot serve on the *mizbeach* at all.

Why the difference? The underlying principle is that *kedushah* oriented toward the *mizbeach* is fundamentally about the identity and destination of the animal: this animal is going to the altar, it is defined by that role. The eggs and the milk are not going to the altar; they are not the animal itself. Therefore, the *kedushah* of a *korban* does not extend to its by-products. *Kedushah* of *bedek habayit*, by contrast, is essentially about ownership and value: the *Beit HaMikdash* now owns this animal. And as the owner, whatever the animal produces — eggs, milk — belongs to the owner. Hence *me'ilah* applies to those as well.

### Identity vs. Value Kedushah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, כִּי אַקְדְּשַׁהּ קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים, לָא אִית בַּהּ מְעִילָה? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – כְּשֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל הִקְדִּישׁוֹ קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁוֹ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת. הִקְדִּישׁ תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ וּבְבֵיצָתָהּ, חֲמוֹרָה – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ וּבַחֲלָבָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one derived benefit from the eggs or milk of consecrated animals sacrificed on the altar he is not liable for their misuse. The Gemara asks: **But** does that mean that in a case of an item that is suitable to be sacrificed **on the altar, if he consecrated it** with **a sanctity** that inheres in its **value,** i.e., to sell it and use the money to buy an offering rather than sacrifice the animal itself, then its eggs or milk are **not subject to** the *halakhot* of **misuse?** Since he does not intend to sacrifice the animal itself, why shouldn't the prohibition of misuse apply to its milk or its eggs? **Rav Pappa said:** The wording of the mishna **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: In what** case **is this statement,** that the milk and eggs of a consecrated animal sacrificed on the altar are not subject to misuse, **said?** It is said **when he consecrated** the animal with **inherent sanctity** to be sacrificed **on the altar. But** if **he consecrated it** with **a sanctity** that inheres in its **value,** i.e., to sell it and use the money to buy an offering to be sacrificed **on the altar,** then **it is** considered **as though he consecrated it for** the **Temple maintenance** and it is subject to misuse. Therefore, if **one consecrated a hen** to sell it and use the money to buy an offering he is liable for **misusing it and for** misusing **its egg;** if one consecrated **a donkey** he is liable for **misusing it and for** misusing **its milk.**

The *Gemara* introduces a refinement that is, as the *Gemara* itself signals, conceptually significant. It notes: *ela gabi mizbeach, ki akdishei kedushat damim lo is bei me'ilah?* — does this mean that if one consecrated a donkey or chicken to the *mizbeach* through *kedushat damim* (value-based consecration destined for the *mizbeach*), there would be no *me'ilah*? *Rava* responds with *chisorei mechasra*: the *mishnah* is incomplete as stated and must be filled in. The correct formulation is: *bameh devarim amurim? Keshehikdish kedushat haguf ligavei mizbeach* — the rule that eggs and milk carry no *me'ilah* applies only when one consecrated the animal with *kedushat haguf* for the *mizbeach*, meaning the animal itself is to go on the altar.

But if one consecrated the donkey or chicken as *kedushat damim ligavei mizbeach* — meaning the animal's *value* is earmarked for the *mizbeach*, to be transferred and used to purchase an *olah*, for example — then *na'aseh ke'mi shehikdisho livdek habayit*, it functions like *bedek habayit* consecration. In that case, *me'ilah* applies to the eggs and the milk just as it would if it had been given directly to the temple treasury.

This is a remarkable *Gemara*, because it draws the operative distinction not between *mizbeach* and *bedek habayit* — which is the surface-level contrast in the *mishnah* — but between *kedushat haguf* and *kedushat damim*. It is the underlying conceptual character of the *kedushah* that determines the *halakha*, and that character does not map perfectly onto the *mizbeach*/*bedek habayit* divide.

### Value Extends to Increase

The principle that follows is: when *kedushah* is about value and cost rather than about identity and destination, that *kedushah* extends naturally to any increase in value. If the *Beit HaMikdash* owns the animal — or if the animal's *kedushah* is at bottom about its monetary worth — then when that worth increases through eggs or milk, those products carry the *kedushah* along with them. Ownership-*kedushah* and value-*kedushah* both extend to increase; identity-*kedushah* does not.

This is so even though *kedushat damim lemizbeach* — consecrating one's donkey so that its proceeds will fund an *olah* — does not make the *Beit HaMikdash* the literal owner. The temple cannot send a collector to demand delivery. The person will himself sell the animal and use the proceeds to buy a *korban*. Nevertheless, since the concept of the *kedushah* is entirely about *price* and *value* rather than about the identity of the thing, the *kedushah* follows the value wherever it goes — including to the milk and eggs that the animal produces in the interim.

### Kedushah Framework Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳, כׇּל הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **any** consecrated item **that is fit for** sacrifice on **the altar**

It is worth drawing together the several conceptual threads that have run through the entire daf. The first concerns the range of *me'ilah* categories. An animal that became *pasul bakodesh* — unfit through some technical disqualification in the *avodah* — retains *me'ilah*, because there remains a *mitzvat sreifah* (the obligation to burn it), and the *kedushah* is still engaged. What removes *me'ilah* is *holech l'ibud*: the *kedushah* being headed toward total loss with no usable role. This covers the *chatat hamaisa*, the overage birds, and — as *Rabbi Yochanan* teaches — animals that simply died without any *pesul bakodesh* and without any *mitzvat sreifah* being triggered. In those last cases, *me'ilah de'oraisa* falls away, and a question remains whether *me'ilah de'rabbanan* persists when the *kedushah* had started good.

The second thread concerns the distinction between *issur hana'ah* and *me'ilah de'rabbanan*. The former, like *chametz be'Pesach*, prohibits benefit but carries no repayment obligation. The latter implies that the *hekdesh* retains a claim on the object, such that misappropriating it generates an obligation to restore value. Most of the cases of *lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin* in the *mishnah* represent *issur hana'ah* without even rabbinic *me'ilah*.

The third and most fundamental thread is the distinction between *kedushat haguf* and *kedushat damim* — between *kedushah* of identity and *kedushah* of value. *Kedushat haguf* says: this thing itself is going somewhere sacred, it is defined by its destination. *Kedushat damim* says: the worth of this thing is earmarked for something sacred, it is defined by its price. These two forms of *kedushah* normally align with the *mizbeach*/*bedek habayit* distinction, but not always. One can have *kedushat haguf* for *bedek habayit* — as when the temple itself will use the beam, not sell it. One can have *kedushat damim lemizbeach* — as when one earmarks the proceeds of a non-kosher animal toward an *olah*. And when *kedushah* is of the value-type, the *kedushah* naturally follows any increase in value: the eggs, the milk, the offspring, all carry it. When *kedushah* is of the identity-type, it is limited to the thing itself — what is not that thing, and not going where that thing is going, lies outside the *kedushah*'s reach.
