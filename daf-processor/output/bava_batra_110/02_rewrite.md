# Bava Batra 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Family Structure

### Inheritance Order

The daf begins at the bottom of 109b, picking up from the previous session's discussion of the order of inheritance. The Gemara had worked through various *drashot* to establish that the sequence runs: son, then daughter, then father, then brother. It had also introduced the important principle that a man inherits from his wife, derived from the phrase *she'er hakarev elav* — "his close relative" — where one reading identifies *she'er* as the wife, and another as the father, with the latter insertion placing the father ahead of the brothers.

The Gemara then raises the question: from where do we know that *she'er* refers to the father? It quotes *she'er avicha* — "the flesh of your father" — though as noted, that is not really the *pshat* of those *psukim* either. This leads to the central principle that anchors the entire discussion: *mishpachat ha'av kruya mishpacha, mishpachat ha'em einah kruya mishpacha* — the father's family is called a family; the mother's family is not called a family.

### Patriarchal Principle

This statement is, in effect, the Talmud's articulation of patriarchal family structure. All children of the same father — even if born of different mothers, like Rachel and Leah — are of one family. Children of the same mother but different fathers are of different families entirely. The family is defined by the father. This is why when a person dies without children, the inheritance does not pass to the mother: doing so would mean leaving the family, since he belongs to his father's family, not his mother's. The daughter, at least until she marries out, remains part of her father's family, and so the inheritance still stays, conceptually, within that family unit.

### Bnot Tzelofchad

This principle makes the ruling of *Bnot Tzelofchad* all the more striking. What the daughters of Tzelofchad accomplished was not merely a matter of girls receiving a portion — it was a radical disruption of the entire patriarchal and tribal structure that kept ancestral land within the family estate.

Consider the following scenario: a man dies without sons, leaving only a married daughter whose husband is from a different tribe. The daughter herself inherits — but now suppose she subsequently dies, leaving behind a son. That son, a member of a different tribe entirely, directly receives the inheritance. So the land passes not gradually but immediately to a member of another tribe. The *chiddush* of *Bnot Tzelofchad* was truly disruptive in this sense.

### Practical Consequences

The Gemara notes, of course, that practically speaking such cross-tribal inheritance would have been rare, since people generally remained within their tribal territories — much as, before the age of modern travel, one simply did not frequently encounter someone from a distant region. But in principle, the possibility was entirely open.

This was precisely what the uncles of *Bnot Tzelofchad* argued: if these daughters marry men from other tribes, sections of our tribal inheritance will pass to strangers. Moshe's response — that they must marry within their tribe — was acknowledged by the Gemara to apply only to that generation. In subsequent generations, there was no technical restriction preventing the land of Yehudah, for instance, from having portions belonging to Shimon, or Yissachar, or Zevulun. Yet the Gemara insists that the fundamental patriarchal structure remains intact: the inheritance does not travel up to the mother, because the deceased belongs to his father's family, not his mother's.

## Micha Idol Story

### Tolin Kalkala

The Gemara transitions to a striking aggadic principle arising from the narrative of the *pesel shel Micha*. It had been established in the preceding discussion that the Levite priest of Micha's idol was identified as a grandson of Moshe — and yet the text also associates him with the tribe of Yehudah and (through the Gemara's *drasha*) with the name Menashe rather than Moshe, so as to link him to the later idolater Menashe. Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: *mikan she-tolin et ha-kalkala ba-mekulkal* — from here we learn that one associates corruption with the corrupt. Even when the association is not technically accurate, the Torah goes out of its way to make the thematic link, because such people share a moral character.

### Adoniyah Case

Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Chanina offers a parallel example from the story of Adoniyah, of whom the verse says: *vegam hu tov to'ar me'od ve'oto yaldah acharei Avshalom* — "he too was very handsome, and she bore him after Avshalom." The Gemara asks the obvious question: was Adoniyah not the son of Chagit, while Avshalom was the son of Maachah? What does it mean that she bore him after Avshalom? The Gemara answers: *mitoch she-asa ma'aseh Avshalom de-marad be-malchut* — since Adoniyah acted like Avshalom in rebelling against his father David and against the monarchy, the verse deliberately associates him with Avshalom. The text forges a connection that is morally apt even if genealogically inapposite. This is the principle of *tolin kalkala ba-mekulkal* in operation.

### Cleave to Good

Rabbi Elazar draws the positive corollary: *le'olam yidbak adam be-tovim* — a person should always cleave to good people. The proof is that Moshe married the daughter of Yitro, and from that union descended Yehonatan, who became a priest to the *pesel shel Micha*. Aharon, by contrast, married the daughter of Aminadav, and from him descended Pinchas. The implication, taken at face value, carries an irony worth noting: Aharon himself made the Golden Calf, yet because he married into a worthy family, his grandson came out righteous. Moshe Rabbeinu, who spoke *panim el panim* with God, yet married into a family associated with *avodah zarah*, and his grandson became a priest to an idol. The lesson is presented without softening.

## Moshe's Grandson

### Pinchas & Yitro

The Gemara immediately notes a complication: if the principle is that Moshe's lineage was compromised because Yitro was an idolater — for Yitro, whatever his later recognition of God, was a *kohen Midyan* and presumably served foreign gods — then what about Pinchas? The verse in Shemot states: *Elazar ben Aharon lakach lo mi-bnot Putiel le-isha* — Elazar married from the daughters of Putiel. The classic *drasha* identifies Putiel as Yitro, *she-pitem agalim la-avodah zarah* — who fattened calves for idol worship. So Pinchas was also a descendant of an idolater on his mother's side. How then can we contrast Moshe's descendant unfavorably with Aharon's?

### Women Invisible

This is where the discussion takes a revealing turn. The Gemara's entire framing — *Moshe who married the daughter of Yitro* and *Aharon who married the daughter of Aminadav* — places the women almost entirely out of the picture. The interest is in the men: whose daughter did he marry? What kind of man was her father? What kind of man was her brother? And then: what will your grandson turn out to be? The women function as conduits between male lineages; the men on each side of the marriage are the primary subjects of scrutiny. This is, in a sense, a striking reinstatement of the patriarchal logic after *Bnot Tzelofchad* had disrupted it — women become nearly invisible in the transmission of character and family standing, while the focus falls entirely on fathers and brothers.

### Putiel Descent

The Gemara resolves the Pinchas difficulty as follows. Rather than read *Putiel* as referring solely to Yitro, the Gemara proposes a different *drasha*: *lo de-asi mi-Yosef she-pitpeit be-yitzro* — Putiel refers to Yosef, who combated his *yetzer ha-ra* when tempted by Potiphar's wife. Elazar thus married a woman who descended from Yosef, not Yitro. But the Gemara does not want to abandon the well-known *midrash* that the tribes mocked Pinchas on account of his Yitro connection: *re'isem ben Puti ze she-pitem avi imo agalim le-avodah zarah yahrog nasi shevet mi-Yisrael* — "Have you seen this son of Puti, whose mother's father fattened calves for idolatry, and he goes and kills a prince of Israel?" This *midrash* is too important to set aside.

The resolution offered is therefore: *avuah de-imei mi-Yosef, imei de-imei mi-Yitro* — either the father of his mother was from Yosef and the mother of his mother was from Yitro, or vice versa. Either arrangement is possible. The word *Putiel* itself, notably written in a form that can be read as dual, hints at this double descent: *tarti* — two lineages, one from Yosef and one from Yitro.

Tosafot and the Rashbam both acknowledge that this resolution is not entirely clean: Pinchas also had an idolatrous grandfather, and yet he turned out righteous, while Moshe's grandson — despite Moshe's own greatness — did not. Perhaps the Yosef lineage counterbalances; perhaps the degree of removal over generations matters. The argument is not as tidy as it first appears. Nevertheless, the overall thrust — that *yidbak adam be-tovim*, that one must deliberately align oneself and one's family with good people — is maintained.

## Marriage & Brothers

### Check Brothers

Rava introduces a related but distinct principle: *ha-nosei ishah tzarich she-yivdok be-acheiha* — when a man marries a woman, he must investigate her brothers. The proof-text is the verse describing Aharon's marriage: *vayikach Aharon et Elisheva bat Aminadav achot Nachshon* — Aharon took Elisheva the daughter of Aminadav, the sister of Nachshon. The Gemara asks: if she is the daughter of Aminadav, is it not obvious that she is the sister of Nachshon? Why does the verse add the detail? From here we learn that one should check the brothers when marrying a woman.

### Rov Banim Domim

The reason is stated explicitly: *rov banim domim le-achei ha-em* — most sons resemble their mother's brothers. If you want to know what kind of sons you will have, look at your wife's brothers. The logic may be genetic or it may be environmental — one could argue that a woman raises her sons the way her mother raised her brothers, so the brothers serve as a preview of the type of boys likely to emerge. Either way, the practical advice is to attend to the brothers.

### Patriarchal Focus

And yet here too the patriarchal framing is striking. The woman one is marrying — her own qualities, her own *middos*, her own upbringing — might seem the most natural source of information about the children she will raise. But the Gemara's focus falls on the men in her life: her father and her brothers. The concern is specifically about sons and how they will turn out, not daughters. The girls, it seems, do not feature in this calculus. One is marrying a woman, but the inquiry is directed toward the male relatives — father and brothers — and the outcome of interest is the male offspring.

This connects directly to the earlier structural principle: the family is defined by the father and the men. The women, including the wife herself, become almost invisible in this framework of looking forward to the next generation. Counterexamples, of course, exist — Ruth, whose father's family were *ovdei avodah zarah*, and whose great-grandchild was David HaMelech; Devorah; the pairs of siblings throughout Bereishit who did not resemble one another. The Gemara does not address these, but they hang in the background as complicating data.

## Self-Sufficiency Teaching

### Danites Question

The Gemara returns to the narrative of *pesel Micha*. When the Danites came to Micha's house and found the Levite — who, according to the *midrash*, was Moshe's grandson Yehonatan — they questioned him. The verse reads: *vayasuru sham vayomeru mi hevi'acha halom* — "they turned aside and said, who brought you here? What are you doing? What business do you have here?" The Gemara reads these phrases as echoing language associated with Moshe himself — *al tikrav halom*, *ma zeh be-yadecha*, *ve-ata poh amod imadi* — so as to heighten the irony and confrontation. They were essentially saying: are you not a descendant of Moshe, of whom God said "do not draw near" at the *sneh*? And you are here serving as a priest to *avodah zarah*?

### Yaskir Adam

His response is extraordinary. He said to them: *kach mekubalni mi-beit avi aba* — this is the tradition I received from my grandfather's house, meaning from Moshe: *le'olam yaskir adam atzmo le-avodah zarah ve-al yitztarech la-briyot* — a person should sooner hire himself out to *avodah zarah* than need to depend on others.

The principle itself is entirely sound as a teaching about self-sufficiency and the dignity of earning one's own living. It is better to take any work, even work that feels foreign or beneath you, than to become a dependent on other people's charity. The verse in the narrative makes clear that Micha attracted Yehonatan with a salary — it was indeed a matter of earning a living.

One might also note that *yaskir adam atzmo le-avodah zarah* need not even imply actual *avodah zarah*: hiring yourself out to work in such a place — chopping wood, maintaining the grounds — is categorically different from serving as a *kohen* in that place. The phrase, taken carefully, need not have led to the conclusion he drew.

### David Intervenes

The Gemara delivers the punchline: *v'hu savar le-avodah zarah mamash* — he understood it to mean literally idolatry. *V'lo hi* — but that is not what was meant. *Ela avodah zarah* means *avodah she-zarah lo* — work that is strange or foreign to him, completely outside his natural sphere, but not literally idol worship. It is a pointed observation — and a cautionary one — about how a teaching can be profoundly misunderstood when taken literally rather than figuratively. There is also, as one participant noted, a lesson about what one says around children or descendants: if a teaching can be heard in a dangerous literal sense, one must be careful in how it is transmitted, especially in a home already adjacent to *avodah zarah*.

### Rav's Teaching

The Gemara brings a parallel teaching. Rav said to Rav Kahana: *nitosh nevielta be-shuka u-shkol agra ve-lo teima gavra raba ana ve-zeila bei milta* — go and flay a carcass in the marketplace and take your pay, and do not say "I am an important person and this is beneath my dignity." It is more self-respecting to work and earn one's keep, even in repulsive or demeaning labor, than to depend on others.

Some have the variant reading *ve-lo teima kahana ana* — do not say "I am a *kohen*." This connects to the story directly, since Yehonatan was a *levi* serving as a *kohen*. Tosafot raises the objection that Rav Kahana may not actually have been a *kohen*, and suggests that just as the Gemara earlier noted that the Levite's name was "Levi" rather than actually being from that tribe, so too here the statement may refer to the *name* Kahana rather than to priestly status.

The Rashbam elaborates: there is no *chillul Hashem* in a *talmid chacham* doing hard physical labor to earn a living. The *chillul Hashem* condemned elsewhere — as in the teaching from Masechet Shabbat that a *talmid chacham* who wears stained clothing causes people to degrade the Torah — applies to one's presentation. But performing *melacha le-hitparnes*, working to support oneself, carries no shame whatsoever. The distinction between a one-time crisis requiring temporary support and a permanent condition of dependence on others is also important here: the Gemara's concern is with the latter, the general posture of self-sufficiency rather than any specific emergency.

### Shevuel Repents

The Gemara closes this narrative thread. David saw that Moshe's grandson — referred to here as Shevuel ben Gershom ben Moshe, with the Gemara noting that this is the same individual previously called Menashe (Moshe) — was extremely devoted to money. Rather than condemning him outright, David appointed him *nagid al ha-otzarot*, supervisor over the royal storehouses. It is, admittedly, a somewhat surprising appointment given that his money-hunger was what led him to the *avodah zarah* in the first place.

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *sheshav le-El be-chol libo* — he returned to God wholeheartedly. Hence the name *Shevuel*, from *shuv* — return. The Rashbam notes a difficulty: the *psukim* in Shmuel indicate that his descendants continued to serve as priests to the *pesel Micha* until the exile. He resolves it via the Yerushalmi: when Shlomo HaMelech came to power, he dismissed the entire royal staff and appointed a new one — at which point Shevuel returned to Micha's temple. His *teshuvah*, apparently, did not survive the regime change.

## Sons vs. Daughters

### Sons Inherit

The Gemara now returns to the *mishna* and the formal derivation of the inheritance order. The *mishna* teaches: *ve-ha-banim et ha-av* — sons inherit from father. This seems obvious, and yet the Gemara asks: *minalan* — from where do we derive it? The verse states: *ish ki yamut u-ven ein lo ve-ha'avartem et nachalato le-vito* — if a man dies and has no son, you shall transfer his inheritance to his daughter. The implication is clear: *ta'ama de-ein lo ben* — the reason the daughter gets it is that there is no son. If there is a son, the son comes first.

### Rav Pappa's Reading

Rav Pappa challenges Abaye with an alternative reading. Perhaps the verse is saying something more nuanced: if there is a son and no daughter, the son inherits; if there is a daughter and no son, the daughter inherits. But when there is both a son and a daughter, perhaps *lo hai leyaras ve-lo hai leyaras* — neither inherits exclusively — meaning perhaps the son and daughter share equally. On this reading, the verse would not establish that sons precede daughters when both are present; it would only tell us what happens when only one type of heir exists.

The Gemara dispatches the absurdist version of this reading immediately — the suggestion that if neither inherits it goes to the *moshel ha-ir*, some civic authority, is obviously not a reasonable reading of the *pasuk*. So Rav Pappa clarifies: what he means is that perhaps sons and daughters inherit equally when both are present.

### Abaye's Response

Abaye responds: if sons and daughters are fundamentally equal heirs, the verse would not need to say anything special. *Ve-itzrecha kra le-ashmiunan* — why would the verse need to tell me separately what happens when there is only a son or only a daughter? If they are equal, the general rule would cover all cases without requiring the specific statement that in the absence of a son the daughter gets it.

But the Gemara then asks: perhaps the verse *does* need to tell us explicitly about daughters — because without it, one might have assumed that *banim* means only sons, and daughters do not inherit at all. On that reading, the verse's mention of the daughter is necessary simply to establish that daughters can inherit, and once that is established, maybe they are equal to sons.

### Bnot Tzelofchad Claim

The Gemara responds: that information — that daughters can inherit — is already derived from the separate verse *kol bat yoreshes nachalah* — every daughter who receives an inheritance. Once that verse has introduced the principle of female inheritance following the *Bnot Tzelofchad* episode, the verse at hand (*im ein lo ben ve-ha'avartem nachalato le-vito*) no longer needs to do that work. Its only function is therefore to establish priority: only in the *absence* of sons does the daughter receive it. The verse's formulation is fully explicit in this structure.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov proposes yet another approach: we can derive the same point from the *claim* of *Bnot Tzelofchad* themselves. They said: *lama yigara shem avinu mi-toch mishpachto ki ein lo ben* — why should our father's name be diminished because he has no son? Give us a *nachalah* among our father's brothers. The very framing of their plea presupposes that if there had been a son, the son would have gotten it and they would not be making any claim. They were explicitly accepting the subordination of daughters to sons; they were only asking to fill the vacuum left by the absence of sons.

### HaKarov Eilav

The Gemara objects: one cannot prove from what *Bnot Tzelofchad* asked what the law actually is. Perhaps God responded to their request more expansively than they had asked — *nitnah Torah ve-nis'chadshah halachah* — the Torah was given and a new ruling emerged, one that placed sons and daughters on equal footing when both are present. The daughters were willing to accept the secondary position, but God may have granted more than they sought. Therefore one cannot use their *claim* to derive the *psak*.

The Gemara concludes: *ela michvarta ki-deshanin me-ikara* — the clearest proof remains the one stated earlier, from the verse itself, which plainly says *im ein lo ben* — only in the absence of a son does the daughter inherit.

Ravina then adds a separate proof. The verse says *uve-nesatem et nachalato li-sh'eiro ha-karov eilav mi-mishpachto* — you shall give his inheritance to his close relative from his family. The principle is *ha-karov karov kodem* — the closest relative comes first. From this one might infer that sons precede daughters on the grounds that sons are closer relatives to the father than daughters are.

## Son's Closeness

### Son Closer Logic

The question is immediately raised: *u-mai kurva de-ven mi-bas* — on what basis do we actually know that a son is closer to the father than a daughter? This is not as self-evident as it might appear. The inquiry is not merely reaffirming the known *halacha* but probing whether there is an independent source in *halacha* or Scripture that reflects this structural closeness — a source from which we could then derive the inheritance order through *ha-karov karov kodem*.

### Failed Proofs

The Gemara suggests two areas where a son is treated as an extension of the father while a daughter is not. First: *ben kam tachas aviv le-ye'adah* — a son can step into his father's role with respect to the *yi'ud* (betrothal) of a Hebrew maidservant whom the father acquired but did not himself marry. Second: with regard to *sedei achuzah* — a sanctified ancestral field — if the son redeems it, it is treated as if the father redeemed it, and therefore does not pass to the *kohanim* at *yovel*; but if a daughter redeems it, this benefit does not apply.

Both proofs fail, however. With respect to *yi'ud*: a daughter is simply not eligible to perform *yi'ud* at all — the institution applies only to a male master. The inapplicability of the rule to the daughter does not show that she is a more distant relative; it shows only that the institution is sex-specific. With respect to *sedei achuzah*: we know the daughter cannot redeem it with the same effect as the father only because we already assume from elsewhere that she is a more distant heir. The proof is circular — it presupposes the very hierarchy it is trying to establish.

### Closeness by Default

The Gemara concludes: *ela michvarta ki-deshanin me-ikara* — there is no independent source establishing that a son is closer than a daughter. The only place we actually derive this is from the verse *im ein lo ven ve-ha'avartem nachalato le-vito* itself. The son's superior position in the family hierarchy is established by the inheritance order, not the other way around. One cannot use *ha-karov karov kodem* as an independent proof that sons precede daughters, because the very fact that sons are considered "closer" is derived from that same inheritance order. The argument from Ravina's verse is therefore not an independent proof.

### Banim Definition

The Gemara then examines another potential source: *ve-hisnachaltem otam li-vneichem achareichem* — you shall bequeath them (slaves) to your sons after you. Does *livneichem* here mean sons specifically, to the exclusion of daughters? The Gemara asks: *bneichem ve-lo bnoseichem* — sons and not daughters?

But this cannot be the implication in all contexts. The verse *le-ma'an yirbu yemeichem vi-mei vneichem* — so that your days and the days of your children be increased — surely extends the blessing to daughters as well. *Bracha shani* — the context of a blessing is different; there, the inclusive meaning is obviously intended.

This raises a genuine question about the default interpretation of *banim* and *bneichem*. The Gemara's apparent position is that the default is *sons* unless context pushes toward the more inclusive meaning of *children*. This is directly relevant to the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah* as well: *ve-limadetem otam et bneichem ledaber bam* — *bneichem ve-lo bnoseichem* — and there the exclusion of daughters from the paternal obligation of *talmud Torah* is derived from the same default reading. The modern instinct may run the other way — to read *bneichem* as *children* by default — but the Gemara's interpretive assumption appears to run in the direction of *sons* as the baseline meaning, requiring an affirmative reason to extend the term.

## Paternal Brothers

### Paternal Brothers Only

The *mishna* states: *ve-achei min ha-av nochalin u-manchilim* — brothers of the same father inherit from one another and transmit inheritance to one another. The restriction to paternal brothers — *achi min ha-av* — needs derivation. The *mishna* specifies that only brothers through a common father are in the inheritance relationship; maternal half-brothers, who share a mother but not a father, are not.

### Yaakov Sons Proof

Rava derives this from the sons of Yaakov. The verse in Bereishit records them saying: *shneim asar avadecha achim anachnu bnei ish echad* — "we your servants are twelve brothers, sons of one man." They call themselves *achim* — brothers — and immediately qualify it as *bnei ish echad*, sons of one man. This is the biblical paradigm of brotherhood: defined by a shared father. *Ma le-halan min ha-av ve-lo min ha-em, af kan min ha-av ve-lo min ha-em* — just as in that paradigm brotherhood is defined through the father and not through the mother, so too throughout the laws of inheritance, brothers are those who share a father, not merely a mother.

### Family via Father

The Gemara then notes that one need not go all the way to the sons of Yaakov for this derivation. The general rule already tells us everything we need: *uve-nesatem nachalato le-mishpachto, mishpachat ha-av kruya mishpacha, mishpachat ha-em einah kruya mishpacha* — the family is defined by the father. If family is patrilineally defined, then obviously brothers — as members of the same family — are brothers through the father, not through the mother alone.

Why, then, did Rava need to go to the sons of Yaakov? The answer is: Rava's derivation was actually stated in the context of *yibum*, not inheritance. For *yibum* — where the obligation falls on a *brother* of the deceased — the patrilineal definition of brotherhood is equally essential, but there the verse (*ki yeshvu achim yachdav*) does not occur in the explicit context of the patriarchal family structure, so an independent derivation from the precedent of the sons of Yaakov is needed. For inheritance, by contrast, the principle of *mishpachat av kruya mishpacha* suffices on its own. The family is the father's family, and brotherhood within that family is necessarily paternal brotherhood. This closing rule thus returns us to the foundational axiom with which the daf began.
