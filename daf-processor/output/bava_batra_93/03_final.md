# Bava Batra 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Goring Ox Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: הֲרֵי זֶה מִקָּח טָעוּת – בָּתַר רוּבָּא אָזְלִינַן, וְרוּבָּא לְרִדְיָא זָבְנִי. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר לָךְ: כִּי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוּבָּא – בְּאִיסּוּרָא, בְּמָמוֹנָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: This is a mistaken transaction,** as in cases of uncertainty **we follow the majority, and** since **the majority** of people **purchase** oxen **for plowing,** it is presumed that this buyer also purchased the ox for plowing. Accordingly, since the ox he received was not suitable for plowing, the sale is void. **And Shmuel** could have **said to you: When we follow the majority,** that is only **with regard to ritual matters,** but **with regard to monetary matters,** such as this, we do **not** follow the majority. Accordingly, there is no basis for voiding the sale.

The daf begins at the very bottom of 92b, where the Gemara presents a classic debate between Rav and Shmuel. The case: someone purchases an ox, and the standard assumption is that the buyer intends it for plowing. It turns out the ox was a goring ox. Rav holds this constitutes a *mekach taus* — a mistaken transaction — while Shmuel disagrees, arguing that since a minority of buyers purchase oxen for meat, there is no sufficient basis to claim *mekach taus*. The sale stands.

### Holchin B'Mamon

Rather than confining this to the narrow domain of *mekach umemkar* — commercial transaction law — the Gemara generalizes the dispute into the broader question of *holchin bemamon achar harov*: when there is a doubt in a monetary dispute, and one party seeks to extract money from another who is *muchzak* (in possession), does majority (*rov*) constitute sufficient proof to do so? The governing default principle is *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof lies on the one seeking to take money from another's possession. The question Rav and Shmuel are debating is whether *rov* satisfies that burden.

### Rav's Position

According to Rav, *rov* does carry that weight. Since the majority of buyers purchase oxen for plowing, that majority constitutes a presumption — or proof — sufficient to establish that the purchase was made for that purpose, thus grounding a claim of *mekach taus*.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel disagrees. In monetary disputes, *rov* does not carry sufficient weight to overcome the *chazaka* of possession. Even if the majority of buyers had a particular purpose in mind, that statistical majority does not create the legal presumption needed to justify extracting money from the seller. Without that presumption, the claim of *mekach taus* fails.

## Slave Defects

### Slave Flaws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹכֵר עֶבֶד לַחֲבֵרוֹ וְנִמְצָא גַּנָּב אוֹ קוּבְיוּסְטוּס, הִגִּיעוֹ. לִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין אוֹ מוּכְתָּב לַמַּלְכוּת – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a challenge to Shmuel's opinion from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who sells a slave to another, and** the slave **is found to be a thief or a gambler [*kuvyustus*],** and the buyer does not wish to have such a slave, nevertheless **it has come to him,** i.e., the slave is acquired by the one who purchased him and the transaction is nonrefundable. By contrast, if the slave is found to be **an armed bandit [*listim*] or** has been judged and **written** to be executed **by the government,** then the buyer can return the slave to the seller and **say to him: That which is yours is before you;** return the money I paid for him, as the sale is void.

The Gemara now surveys a range of cases to test this debate. The first *tasha* (come and hear): if someone sells a slave to another and the slave is found to be a burglar (*ganav*) or a *kubyustos* — which Rashi and the Rashbam understand as a murderer, though Tosafot marshals considerable evidence that it means a dice player, a gambler — the buyer has no recourse. *Higio* — tough luck. These are undesirable traits in a slave, and would have constituted a *mum* had they been known, but the buyer is stuck.

### Buyer Beware

However, if the slave turns out to be an armed robber (*listis*), or already has a death sentence pending from the government (*muchtav lemalchus*), that is out of proportion to any reasonable expectation. In that case, the buyer may say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — here, take back what you sold me — and demand a refund. The precise distinction between the two categories is worth examining: is it about whether the defect falls within a tolerable range of expectation? Or does the phrase *harei shelcha lefanecha* invoke its technical resonance in cases of whether a defect is *nikar* — visible and apparent? That question remains open. The immediate issue, however, is why in the first category the buyer cannot claim *mekach taus*.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּרוּבָּא הָכִי אִיתַנְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **what is the reason** that the sale is not considered to be a mistaken transaction? Is it **not because a majority** of slaves **are like this,** i.e., either thieves or gamblers? Apparently, the majority is followed in monetary matters.

The Gemara asks: *mai tama* — what is the reason in the first case that the buyer is stuck? *Lav mishum derova hachi isnu* — is it not because the majority of slaves are burglars or gamblers? This would appear to support Rav's position: we follow *rov*, and since most slaves have these characteristics, you should have anticipated this, and *rov* defeats your *mekach taus* claim.

### Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כּוּלְּהוּ הָכִי אִיתַנְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** it is because **all** slaves **are like this.** Accordingly, no proof can be drawn as to whether we follow the majority in monetary matters.

The Gemara deflects this proof: *lo, kulu hachi isnu* — it is not that the majority of slaves are like this; all slaves are like that. This is, of course, a morally troubling statement on its own terms, but analytically the Gemara's point is clear: the rule here does not depend on *rov* at all. If it were one hundred percent, the concept of *rov* is not even in play.

There is a further structural difficulty with this attempted proof that deserves attention. The debate between Rav and Shmuel concerns whether *rov* can overcome *chazaka* — specifically the *chezkat mamon*, the presumption of continued possession that protects a party who holds the money. But in the slave case, the *rov* would actually be reinforcing the *chazaka* — helping the seller keep the money by arguing the buyer should have anticipated the defect. That is a different application altogether, not the head-on clash between *rov* and *chazaka* that Rav and Shmuel are debating. The Rashbam attempts to reconcile this, but it is genuinely difficult to see the case as directly relevant. The Gemara ultimately sets it aside.

## Pregnant Cow Case

### Goring Pregnant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח אֶת הַפָּרָה, וְנִמְצָא עוּבָּרָהּ בְּצִדָּהּ, וְאֵינוֹ יָדוּעַ אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נְגָחָהּ יָלְדָה, אוֹ אִם מִשֶּׁנְּגָחָהּ יָלְדָה – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק לַפָּרָה, וּרְבִיעַ לַוָּלָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rav's opinion from a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 46a): In the case of an innocuous **ox that gored** and killed **a cow, and** the cow's **fetus was found** dead **at its side, and it is not known whether** the cow **calved before** the ox **gored it** and the fetus' death was unrelated to the goring, **or whether it calved after** the ox **gored it** and the fetus died on account of the goring, the ox's owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage for the cow,** as is the *halakha* for an innocuous ox (see Exodus 21:35), **and one-quarter** of the cost of the damage **for the offspring.** Since it is uncertain whether the ox caused the death of the fetus, its owner pays for half of the standard liability of half the cost of the damage.

The Gemara offers a more promising test case: *shor shenagach es hapar* — an ox gored a cow. The cow was pregnant, and the fetus is found lying beside it. The question is *veino yadua* — it is not known — whether the cow had already given birth before the goring (*ad shelo nagcha yaldah*), or whether the goring caused the miscarriage (*mishenagcha yaldah*). The practical legal stakes are significant: a cow that had already given birth before being gored is worth less than a pregnant cow. If the fetus was lost due to the goring, the owner of the ox bears liability for the additional loss; if the cow had already miscarried on its own, there is no such liability. The ox in question is a *shor tam* — first offender — and thus pays only half-damages. The question is whether the fetus contributes to that calculation.

### Sumchus Splits

The Gemara in Bava Kamma attributes to Sumchus the ruling *mamon hamutal besafek cholkin* — when money rests in doubt, we divide it. Accordingly, in this case: the owner pays *chatzi nezek* (half-damages) for the cow, and *revia lavlad* — a quarter, that is, half of the half — for the fetus. The logic is that if he were fully liable for the fetus he would pay half, if not liable at all he would pay zero, and since there is genuine doubt, he pays half of the half. This is the position of Sumchus.

It is worth noting parenthetically that Sumchus's framework — *mamon hamutal besafek cholkin* — is generally understood as minimizing the weight given to *chazaka*. Normally, we would say: I hold the money, you must prove your claim, and doubt alone does not suffice to extract money from me — *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*. Sumchus disrupts that default and says: in cases of genuine doubt, we split. His approach therefore operates on a different axis than the Rav-Shmuel debate about whether *rov* overcomes *chazaka*, and it is not straightforward to map this case directly onto that debate. We bracket that complication and proceed.

### Rov Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב פָּרוֹת, וְרוֹב פָּרוֹת מִתְעַבְּרוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת; וְהָא וַדַּאי מֵחֲמַת נְגִיחָה הִפִּילָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof from the *baraita*: **But why,** according to Rav, should he only pay for one-quarter of the damage to the fetus? Since there is an uncertainty, **let us say: Follow** the **majority of cows, and** since the **majority of cows become pregnant and calve** live offspring, one should conclude that **this** cow, which did not, **certainly miscarried due to** the ox **goring** it, and the ox's owner should be liable for half the cost of the fetus. Since that line of reasoning is not applied here, it is apparent that the majority is not followed in monetary matters.

The Gemara now asks: if we truly follow *rov* in monetary matters, why should there be a *safek* here at all? *Leima halach achar rov parot* — let us follow the majority of cows. Most cows do not miscarry; they carry to live birth. The occurrence of a miscarriage adjacent to a goring event cries out for an explanation, and the most plausible one — by *rov* — is that the goring caused it. *Vadai machmat negicha hipila* — almost certainly it miscarried on account of the goring. If we follow *rov*, the liability for the fetus should be clear, and there should be no *safek* requiring a split. The fact that Sumchus nevertheless splits suggests that *rov* is not operative — which would be evidence against Rav's position.

### Fear vs. Goring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם דִּמְסַפְּקָא לַן, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: מִקַּמַּהּ אֲתָא, וּמִבִּיעֲתוּתָא הִפִּילָה; וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: מֵאֲחוֹרַהּ אֲתָא, וּמִינְגָּח נַגְחַהּ וְהִפִּילָה; הָוֵי מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק, וְכׇל מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק – חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **There,** the reason that the ox's owner pays for only one-quarter of the damage for the fetus is **due** to the fact **that we are uncertain** about how it died, **as it is** possible **to say** that the ox **approached** the cow **from its front and** it was **due to** the cow's **fright,** not due to the goring, that **it miscarried,** meaning that the ox's owner would not be liable; **and it is** also possible **to say that** the ox **approached** the cow **from behind it and gored it, and** that is why the cow **miscarried.** Accordingly, the payment for such damage constitutes **property of uncertain ownership, and** the *halakha* is that **all property of uncertain ownership is divided** equally between the two parties.

The Gemara responds: *hatam mishum d'mesapka lan* — there, the reason is that a genuine doubt undermines the *rov* before it can do its work. Even granting that in most cases the miscarriage would be attributable to the goring, there is a further question within that causal chain: *mikama ata umibiyuta hepila* — did the ox charge from the front, and the cow miscarried from fright? Or *meachura ata, umengach nagcha, vehepila* — did it come from behind and gore her directly, causing the miscarriage by direct contact? This is not a trivial distinction. If the cow miscarried from fear of the approaching ox, that is *grama* — indirect causation — and the owner of the ox is exempt from liability for the fetus. Only if the direct physical goring caused the miscarriage is there full liability.

### Tosafot's Analysis

Tosafot strengthens this analysis by framing it as a decision tree. At the first branch: *rov* says the miscarriage was goring-related; a *miut* says it was a natural miscarriage. Moving down the branch where *rov* points — goring-related — there is a further split between fear (which produces no liability) and direct goring (which produces liability). Even if the direct-goring branch is more probable than the fear branch within that category, combining the small probability of a natural miscarriage with the probability of a fear-induced miscarriage creates sufficient cumulative doubt that the case cannot be treated as a clean *rov*. For Sumchus, that combined uncertainty is enough to justify *cholkin*. The case is not, therefore, a case where a clear *rov* goes unheeded — it is a case where the *rov* is itself undermined by internal complexity.

### No Proof Here

The conclusion is that this case cannot serve as proof that *rov* is disregarded in monetary matters. It is equally consistent with the view that we do follow *rov* in such matters — and that here, we simply lack a sufficiently clear *rov* to apply. The Rav-Shmuel debate remains unresolved.

## Chazaka Tannaim

### Ox Propensity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: שׁוֹר שֶׁהָיָה רוֹעֶה, וְנִמְצָא שׁוֹר הָרוּג בְּצִדּוֹ; אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁזֶּה מְנוּגָּח וְזֶה מוּעָד לִיגַּח, זֶה מְנוּשָּׁךְ וְזֶה מוּעָד לִישּׁוֹךְ; אֵין אוֹמְרִים: בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁזֶּה נְגָחוֹ וְזֶה נְשָׁכוֹ. רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: גָּמָל הָאוֹחֵר בֵּין הַגְּמַלִּים, וְנִמְצָא גָּמָל הָרוּג בְּצִדּוֹ – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁזֶּה הֲרָגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this dispute between Rav and Shmuel **is parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **an ox that was grazing and** another **ox** that **was found killed at its side, even though this** dead ox **has been gored and that** grazing ox **is forewarned with regard to goring,** or **this** dead ox **has been bitten and that** grazing ox **is forewarned with regard to biting,** nevertheless **one does not say** that **it is evident that this** grazing ox **gored** the dead ox **or that** grazing ox **bit it,** despite the fact such behavior is typical for the ox; rather, one cannot draw any definite conclusions. **Rabbi Aḥa says** that in the case of a rutting male **camel that is rampaging among** other **camels and** another **camel** that **was found killed at its side, it is evident that this** rampaging camel **killed it,** as such behavior is typical for a rutting camel. Therefore, the owner of that camel is liable.

The Gemara now proposes that the debate between Rav and Shmuel parallels a *tannaitic* dispute: *leima k'tanai*. The case is as follows: *shor shehaya ro'eh* — an ox was grazing peacefully. Nearby, another ox is found dead — *nimtza shor harug betzido*. The grazing ox is known to be a *muad ligach*, an ox with an established propensity to gore (having gored three times before). Nevertheless, there is no direct evidence that this particular ox gored the dead one — no one witnessed it, and the ox shows no obvious signs. *Af al pi shezeh menugach vezeh muad ligach* — even though this one has been gored and that one has a propensity to gore — *ein omrim beyadua shezeh nagach* — we do not say it is established that this was the one that gored. The same applies to a bitten animal found beside an ox with a propensity to bite. The *tanna kamma* insists on direct evidence; circumstantial reasoning, even where a *chazaka* of propensity exists, is insufficient to establish monetary liability.

### Rabbi Acha

Rabbi Acha disagrees, and goes further. Not only would he permit using the propensity of a *muad* ox as evidence — he extends the principle to a case without any prior propensity. *Gamal ha'ocher bein hagamalim* — a camel that was behaving wildly among other camels, whether because it was copulating or because it was in heat and thrashing about, and a dead camel is found nearby: *beyadua shezeh harago* — we may connect the dots and treat it as established that this camel killed the other. The Rashbam understands Rabbi Acha as arguing with the *tanna kamma*: even in the case of the *muad* ox, circumstantial evidence of propensity is sufficient for monetary liability. One might ask whether Rabbi Acha would extend this reasoning to accusations against persons — that a known burglar found near a burglarized home could be held civilly liable on that basis alone. The sugya does not resolve this, but it is a natural question.

### Connection Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ דְּרוּבָּא וַחֲזָקָה כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ; לֵימָא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי אַחָא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא?
>
> **Translation:** Those who suggested the parallel between the tannaitic dispute and the dispute between Rav and Shmuel **assumed that a majority and** a logical **presumption** about whether an event will happen **are equivalent** in their capacity to determine the facts of a case. Consequently, **let us say** that **Rav, who says** that one follows the majority in monetary matters, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa,** who follows a presumption to determine the facts of a case, **and** that **Shmuel, who says** that one does not follow the majority in monetary matters, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** who does not follow a presumption.

The Gemara now attempts to link these *tannaitic* positions directly to the Rav-Shmuel debate. The assumption (*savruha*) is that following *rov* and following *chazaka* (propensity) are equivalent — that whatever one says about *rov* in monetary disputes, one would say the same about *chazaka*. On this reading: Rav, who says we follow *rov*, would align with Rabbi Acha, who uses propensity (*chazaka*) as the basis for liability. Shmuel, who says we do not follow *rov*, would align with the *tanna kamma*, who demands direct evidence. The parallel seems clean.

### Rav's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְתַנָּא קַמָּא; עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא הָתָם – אֶלָּא דְּלָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר חֲזָקָה, אֲבָל בָּתַר רוּבָּא – אָזְלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Rav** could have **said to you: I am stating** my ruling **even in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** as **the first *tanna* says** that one cannot draw a definite conclusion **only there,** in the case of the grazing ox, **as we do not follow a presumption** in monetary matters, **but** he concedes that **we follow the majority** in monetary matters.

The Gemara complicates this alignment. Rav pushes back: *ana de'amri afilu leTanna Kamma* — when I say we follow *rov*, I could agree even to the *tanna kamma*. The *tanna kamma* is not making a general claim about *rov*; he is specifically saying we do not follow *chazaka* of propensity. But *aval batar ruba azlinan* — we do follow a general statistical majority. Rov and *chazaka* of propensity are not the same thing, and Rav is prepared to follow *rov* while conceding the *tanna kamma*'s position on *chazaka*.

### Shmuel's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר לָךְ: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי אַחָא; עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא הָתָם – אֶלָּא דְּאָזְלִינַן בָּתַר חֲזָקָה, דְּהוּא גּוּפֵיהּ מוּחְזָק; אֲבָל בָּתַר רוּבָּא – לָא אָזְלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could have **said to you: I am stating** my ruling **even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa,** as **Rabbi Aḥa says** that one can draw a definite conclusion **only there,** in the case of the camels, **since we follow a presumption** in monetary matters, **as** this camel **itself is presumed,** based on its behavior, to be the killer. **But** he concedes that **we do not follow the majority** in deciding monetary matters.

Shmuel responds symmetrically: *ana de'amri afilu Rabbi Acha* — when I say we do not follow *rov*, I could agree even with Rabbi Acha. Rabbi Acha permits using propensity as evidence because *d'hu gufa machazik* — what we know is specific to this particular animal. We know something concrete and individualized about this ox or this camel. Even without witnessing the act, we know its nature, we know it was in the vicinity, and we apply what we know about the individual. That is sufficient, according to Shmuel, as a basis for monetary liability. But *aval batar ruba lo azlinan* — a general statistical majority, which tells us nothing specific about the individual before us, does not suffice.

### Rov vs. Chazaka

This distinction is the conceptual heart of the sugya and deserves careful attention. *Chazaka* of propensity is knowledge about a specific individual — this ox, this camel — and we are applying that knowledge to an event involving that individual. *Rov* is an inference from general population statistics: most people who buy oxen buy them for plowing; most cows that miscarry next to a goring ox miscarried because of the goring. We know nothing specific about the buyer or the cow; we know something about the general distribution of cases.

This generates a real conceptual tension. One might argue that *chazaka* of propensity is only circumstantial — yes, this ox likes to gore, but does that mean it gored *today*? A known burglar found near a burgled house is still not proven guilty by his nature alone. By contrast, *rov* tells us that in ninety percent of cases, the outcome follows the statistical pattern, giving us a high degree of confidence about this particular case even without individualized knowledge. That is why the Gemara earlier in *Chullin* concluded *ruba adif* — *rov* is weightier than *chazaka*. Yet Shmuel holds the reverse: individualized propensity (*chazaka*) is weightier than general statistics (*rov*) for purposes of monetary adjudication. Both positions are defensible, and the Gemara acknowledges that the question genuinely runs in both directions.

## Flaxseed Debate

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵרוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע טִנּוֹפֶת לִסְאָה. תְּאֵנִים – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר מְתוּלָּעוֹת לְמֵאָה. מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה. קַנְקַנִּים בַּשָּׁרוֹן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר פִּיטָסוֹת לְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** When selling a significant quantity of produce or a number of items, there is a possibility that there will be a certain proportion of impurities in it or that some of the product will be of substandard quality. The mishna delineates what proportion is considered acceptable, for which a buyer may not demand compensation. With regard to **one who sells produce,** i.e., grain, **to another,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **a quarter-***kav* **of impurities** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** of produce purchased. When purchasing **figs,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten infested** figs may be present **in** each **hundred** figs purchased. When purchasing **a cellar** containing barrels **of wine,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten** barrels of **souring** wine may be present **in** each **hundred** barrels purchased. When purchasing **jugs** of wine **in the Sharon** region, the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten inferior-quality jugs [*pitasot*]** of wine may be present **in** each **hundred** jugs purchased.

The Gemara now produces what appears to be a direct proof from a *mishnah*. *Hamocher peiros l'chavero l'zoran v'lo tzamchu* — someone who sells fruit (including grain or seeds) for the purpose of planting, and they did not grow. *Va'afilu zera pishtan* — even flaxseed, where the majority of buyers purchase it specifically for planting. The seller bears no liability — *eino chayav b'acharayutan*. The Gemara reads the emphasis on flaxseed as deliberate: even though a majority of flaxseed is bought for planting, that *rov* does not establish a presumption of intended purpose sufficient to generate liability. This sounds exactly like Shmuel.

### Baraita Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵרוֹ וּזְרָעָן וְלֹא צִמֵּחוּ; זֵרְעוֹנֵי גִינָּה שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִין – חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן, זֶרַע פִּשְׁתָּן – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי זֶרַע. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַרְבֵּה לוֹקְחִין אוֹתוֹ לִדְבָרִים אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concedes that the mishna cannot be reconciled with Rav's opinion, but suggests that there are other *tanna'im* who hold in accordance with his opinion. It **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who sells produce to another, and** the buyer **planted it and it did not sprout,** if the produce was **seeds** for **garden** plants, **which are not eaten** at all, then the seller **bears** financial **responsibility for them.** If the produce was **flaxseeds,** which are only occasionally eaten, then the seller **does not bear** financial **responsibility for them. Rabbi Yosei says:** The seller **gives** back **to** the buyer **the value of the seeds.** Since the majority of flaxseeds are sold are for planting, it is a mistaken transaction. **They said to him,** i.e., to Rabbi Yosei: **Many,** i.e., a majority of people, **purchase** flaxseeds **for purposes other** than planting. Consequently, the sale stands.

The Gemara then produces a *baraita* that parallels but diverges from the *mishnah*. The *tanna kamma* of the *baraita* distinguishes: *zir'onei ginah she'ein ne'echalin* — vegetable seeds that nobody eats, purchased only for planting — *chayav b'acharayutan*, the seller is liable. *Zera pishtan* — flaxseed, where the majority but not all buy it for planting — *eino chayav*, no liability. So the *tanna kamma* of the *baraita* does not apply *rov* even in the flaxseed case. But then Rabbi Yossi says: *nosein lo d'mei zera* — for flaxseed, you must reimburse the buyer for the cost of the seed. Rabbi Yossi, in other words, treats the majority-for-planting as sufficient to establish the purpose of the purchase, generating at least some liability. This sounds like Rav.

### Amru Lo

A further voice responds to Rabbi Yossi: *harbe lokchin oso l'davar acher* — many people buy flaxseed for other purposes. At face value, this seems to dispute whether there actually is a *rov* in favor of planting — perhaps the numbers are not as Rabbi Yossi claims. But the Gemara will examine this more carefully.

### Two Rovs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּאֵי? אִילֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְ״אָמְרוּ לוֹ״; תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בָּתַר רוּבָּא אָזְלִי – מָר אָזֵיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דְאִינָשֵׁי, וּמָר אָזֵיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דִזְרִיעָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** are the ***tanna'im*** in this *baraita* who have a dispute that parallels the dispute between Rav and Shmuel? **If we say** that they are **Rabbi Yosei and** the opinion cited as: **They said to him,** that is incorrect, as **both of them** hold that one **follows the majority** in monetary matters. Their dispute concerns only which majority to follow: One **Sage,** i.e., the opinion cited as: They said to him, **follows the majority of people** making purchases, **and** one **Sage,** i.e., Rabbi Yosei, **follows the majority** of the volume **of seeds** that are sold overall. The disparity arises because the majority of sales are each made with a relatively small quantity of seeds that are purchased for purposes other than planting. The minority of sales involve large quantities of seeds that are purchased for planting. This means that the majority of the seeds sold overall are purchased for planting, but the majority of people purchasing seeds do so for purposes other than planting.

The Gemara reframes the *amru lo* objection in a more interesting way. Both Rabbi Yossi and the *amru lo* agree that *rov* is operative in monetary matters — they disagree about how to measure the *rov*. *Mar azil basar ruba d'inshei* — one follows the majority of *people* who buy flaxseed; *umar azil basar ruba d'zri'ah* — the other follows the majority of the *seed* sold. Concretely: a single farmer may buy a hundred pounds of flaxseed for planting, while ten individuals buy one pound each for culinary or other purposes. By headcount, the majority of buyers buy for other purposes. By volume, the majority of flaxseed is sold for planting. The Gemara is suggesting that the *amru lo* is not denying that *rov* matters — it is asserting a different *rov* that cuts in the opposite direction.

### Positions Aligned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי תַּנָּא קַמָּא וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אִי תַּנָּא קַמָּא וְ״אָמְרוּ לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the dispute that parallels the dispute between Rav and Shmuel is **either** the dispute between **the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei, or the** dispute between **the first *tanna* and** the opinion cited as: **They said to him.**

With this framing, the alignment of positions becomes clear. The *mishnah*, which ignores the flaxseed *rov* entirely and denies liability, reflects Shmuel's position. The *tanna kamma* of the *baraita* similarly declines to apply *rov* in the flaxseed case and therefore also aligns with Shmuel. Rabbi Yossi, who does apply *rov* (by volume of seed) and holds the seller liable, reflects Rav. The *amru lo*, who also applies *rov* but by headcount of buyers, similarly reflects Rav — just with a different computation of where the *rov* falls. The debate of Rav and Shmuel is thus the debate of the *tannaim*, though the Gemara concedes that the broader question of *holchin bemamon achar harov* has not been definitively resolved by the sugya as a whole.

## Professional Liability

### What to Reimburse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַהוּ נוֹתֵן לוֹ? דְּמֵי זֶרַע, וְלֹא הוֹצָאָה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הוֹצָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the seller bears financial responsibility for selling seeds that did not sprout, **what is** he liable to **give to** the buyer? He is liable to give him only the **value of the seeds** themselves, **but not the expenses** that the buyer incurred in planting them, e.g., the hire of laborers. **And some say:** He is liable to give him **even the expenses** that the buyer incurred.

The Gemara now turns from whether there is liability to what exactly is owed when a *mekach taus* is established. In the clearest case — vegetable seeds that are purchased only for planting — everyone agrees there is *mekach taus*. The question is: *mah hu noten lo?* What must the seller reimburse?

### Expense Debate

The *tanna kamma* (here aligned with Rabbi Yossi's earlier statement) says: *d'mei zera bilvad* — the cost of the seed only, not any further expenses the buyer incurred in planting. *Ve'yesh omrim af hotza'ah* — some say the seller must reimburse even the expenses. The analogy that makes this feel intuitive is the backup tape: a seller who says "I'll replace the tape if it fails" is not really addressing the buyer's loss, which is all the data that was on it. The seed is a small fraction of the true cost to the farmer, who plowed, irrigated, and tended a field that produced nothing. Yet extending liability to cover all those expenses raises serious questions: must negligence be proven? How does one establish that the failure was due to the defective seed and not defective farming? These evidentiary hurdles are real, and Tosafot addresses them directly.

### Rabban Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן יֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** the *tanna* whose opinion is cited as: **Some say? Rav Ḥisda said** that **it is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

*Man yesh omrim?* — who holds the position of *yesh omrim*? Rav Chisda attributes it to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The Gemara then investigates whether this can be demonstrated from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position in the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* reads: *hamocher peiros l'chavero* — even flaxseed the seller is not liable — *chutz mizironei ginah she'einan ne'echalin chayav b'acharayutan*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then states explicitly that for vegetable seeds one is liable. The Gemara asks: is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adding anything beyond what is already implicit in the *tanna kamma*? The *tanna kamma*'s exemption of flaxseed already implies, by negative inference, that vegetable seeds do create liability. So what is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's chiddush?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא אִיפְּכָא! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא; כׇּל תַּנָּא בָּתְרָא, לְטַפּוֹיֵי מִילְּתָא קָא אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it apparent that it is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel who holds that the seller is liable for the expenses? **Perhaps** it is **the opposite,** i.e., it is the first *tanna* who holds that the seller is liable for the expenses, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that he is liable only for the value of the seeds. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **This** is **not difficult,** because **the last *tanna*** cited **always comes to add something** to the ruling of the first *tanna*, not to detract from it.

The Gemara concludes that the *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel may actually be debating the *scope* of liability: the *tanna kamma* holds you liable only for the seed itself, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel extends liability to include the farmer's expenses. The principle is *kol tanna batar letafuei milta ka'atei* — a second opinion in a *mishnah* generally comes to extend or intensify the first, not merely restate it. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, appearing after the *tanna kamma*, must be adding something — and that something is the broader, expense-inclusive liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹלִיךְ חִטִּין לַטָּחוֹן, וְלֹא לְתָתָן – וַעֲשָׂאָן סוּבִּין אוֹ מוּרְסָן; קֶמַח לַנַּחְתּוֹם, וַאֲפָאוֹ פַּת נִיפּוּלִין; בְּהֵמָה לַטַּבָּח, וְנִיבְּלָהּ – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the statement in question is **this** statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 10:9) In a case of **one who brought wheat** to a miller **to grind, and** the miller **did not wet** the grains sufficiently for the grinding to be performed effectively, **and** as a result **he converted** the grain **into bran or coarse bran [*mursan*],** or in a case of one who gave **flour to the baker and** the baker **made it** into **bread** that is **underbaked** and tends to crumble, or if one gave **an animal to a butcher and** the butcher killed it in a way that **rendered it** an unslaughtered **animal carcass,** in all these cases the worker is **liable, because he is like a paid bailee.**

The Gemara then raises an alternative: perhaps Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *tanna kamma* are not debating at all, and the attribution of *yesh omrim* to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel must be grounded elsewhere. *Ella ha Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel d'tanya* — the Gemara points to a *baraita* that makes the attribution explicit.

### Tradespeople

The *baraita*: *hamolich chitin litachon* — you bring your wheat to the miller, and he fails to process it properly (failing to dehusk the grain before grinding), producing bran or coarse bran rather than fine flour. *Kemach lenachtom* — you bring flour to the baker and he produces bread that is falling apart. *O behemah letabach, venivla* — or you bring your animal to the butcher and he performs the *shechita* improperly, rendering it a *neveilah*. In each case, *chayav* — the tradesperson is liable, *mipnei shehu k'nosei sachar* — because it is as if he is being paid.

This phrase — "as if he is being paid" — is worth unpacking. The Gemara elsewhere establishes that these craftspeople are in fact paid. The *shomer* framework informs the liability analysis: a *shomer chinam* is liable only for *peshiya* (gross negligence), a *shomer sachar* for *geneivah va'aveidah* as well, and a *shoel* even for *ones* (unavoidable accident). A paid tradesperson occupies a status analogous to a *shomer sachar*, and therefore bears liability for anything short of a complete unavoidable accident. The grinding failure or the defective *shechita* is not gross negligence exactly, but neither is it a pure accident — it is a lapse that a paid professional should have avoided.

### Catering Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי בוֹשְׁתּוֹ, וּדְמֵי בּוֹשֶׁת אוֹרְחָיו. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִנְהָג גָּדוֹל הָיָה בִּירוּשָׁלָיִם – הַמּוֹסֵר סְעוּדָה לַחֲבֵרוֹ, וְקִלְקְלָהּ; נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי בׇשְׁתּוֹ וּדְמֵי בּוֹשֶׁת אוֹרְחָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If the owner had invited guests to eat the food and due to the worker's actions he was unable to serve them, then the worker must **give him compensation for his humiliation and compensation for the humiliation of his guests. And similarly, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say: There was a great custom in Jerusalem** that if **one gives** raw materials for **a meal to another** to prepare the meal for him, **and** that person **spoils it,** that person **gives** the former **compensation for his humiliation and compensation for the humiliation of his guests.**

Then comes Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *notsei lo d'mei boshto v'dmei boshet orchav* — the liable party must pay not only for the damaged product but for the embarrassment suffered by the owner and by all of his guests. If you bring your meat to the butcher for a wedding feast and he renders it *neveilah*, he does not discharge his obligation simply by reimbursing you for the cow. He owes you for the ruined event.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then generalizes this to a *minhag* — a practice in Jerusalem: *hamoseir seudah l'chavero v'kilkela* — if you engage a caterer and the caterer ruins the meal, *nosein lo d'mei boshto v'dmei boshet orchav* — he is liable for your embarrassment and that of all your guests. The fact that this is described as a *minhag* is significant: it cannot be derived straightforwardly from the standard categories of *nizikin*. The embarrassment is indirect — *grama b'nizikin* — and direct *boshet* in the usual sense requires deliberate intent (*kavanah*). This is better understood as a *takkanah* or *knas*, a rabbinic imposition of expanded liability, or alternatively as a form of *garmi* — a recognized category of indirect causation that does generate liability in certain contexts. The Gemara assumes that this broader liability extends by analogy to the seed case: a seller of defective seeds, when their defectiveness was foreseeable to produce significant consequential losses, may be liable even for the farmer's expenses.

### Tablecloth Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹד מִנְהָג גָּדוֹל הָיָה בִּירוּשָׁלָיִם – מַפָּה פְּרוּסָה עַל גַּבֵּי הַפֶּתַח; כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּפָּה פְּרוּסָה – אוֹרְחִין נִכְנָסִין. נִסְתַּלְּקָה הַמַּפָּה – אֵין הָאוֹרְחִין נִכְנָסִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Another great custom** that **was** followed **in Jerusalem** was that when one made a feast, there would be **a cloth [*mappa*] spread over the entrance** to the hall. **As long as the cloth was spread,** the **guests would enter,** as the presence of the cloth indicated that there was food for more guests. When **the cloth was removed, the guests would not enter** any more.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appends a second *minhag* from Jerusalem, not directly connected to the liability analysis but preserved here: *mappa prusa al gabi hapetach* — if a tablecloth was hung across the doorway, it signaled that a meal was in progress and all who wished to eat were welcome to enter. *Nistalka mappa* — once the tablecloth was removed, the invitation closed. This was a genuine, visible gesture of *hachnasat orchim* — a real-time public announcement of hospitality, unlike the ceremonial *kol dichfin yeitei v'yeichol* of the Seder, which is recited after the doors are shut.

### Foreseeability

The conceptual thread unifying Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's rulings is what we might call professional liability tied to foreseeability. He is not saying that any indirect cause of loss generates unlimited liability. The common denominator is that the tradesperson — miller, baker, butcher, caterer, seed seller — is operating in a professional capacity in which the consequences of failure are predictably significant to those who rely on them. That heightened professional responsibility is what justifies extending liability beyond the immediate product to the consequential damages that flow from the failure. An incidental stranger who crashes into a catering truck is not subject to this expanded liability; the caterer who deliberately undertook to provide the meal is. As one of the *talmidim* noted, foreseeability of the consequential damage — knowing that the meat is for a wedding, knowing that the seeds are for a whole season's planting — would seem to play a role in calibrating the precise scope of liability, though the Gemara does not fully articulate where that line falls.

## Shomer Liability

### Payment Status

A question arises from the *baraita*: why is the liability of these craftspeople expressly linked to whether they are being paid? The tradesperson who grinds wheat improperly or performs a defective *shechita* has directly caused damage to an object in his hands. One would expect this to trigger the principle of *adam muad l'olam* — a person is always liable for direct damage he causes, regardless of intent or fault. If so, liability should follow regardless of payment status, even in a case of complete *ones*.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot seizes on this difficulty as evidence for a broader proposition: when the Gemara says *adam muad l'olam*, it does not mean absolute strict liability in all cases. Even an *adam hamazik* is only liable when there is some minimal degree of negligence. This explains why payment status matters: without payment, the tradesperson's inadvertent mistake may be treated as close enough to pure accident that he bears no liability. Tosafot extends this reading to other *sugyot*, including the case where a sleeping person breaks a vessel placed beside him — Tosafot argues that exemption demonstrates that a person is not always unconditionally liable.

### Ramban View

The Ramban takes a different approach. He maintains that *adam muad l'olam* means what it says: a person bears strict liability for direct damage, no qualifications. The key move for the Ramban is categorical: we do not classify the craftsperson as an *adam hamazik* at all. The *mazik* paradigm applies to a stranger who imposes himself on someone else's property — you slip on a puddle and break a vase sitting in the *reshut harabbim*, and though you had no intent and no negligence, you are liable because you were an outsider whose contact with the object was uninvited. By contrast, the miller and the butcher were given license to handle the object. The owner asked them to process it. They are not strangers; they are *umanim* — craftspeople operating with explicit authorization. The legal category that governs them is not *hilchos mazik* but *hilchos umanim*, and within that framework different rules apply. The liability analysis proceeds through the *shomer* analogy because that is the correct conceptual rubric, not because persons are exempt from strict liability generally.

### Ramban Alternative

The Ramban deploys a related but distinct argument when addressing Tosafot's proof from the sleeping person. When someone places a vessel beside a sleeping person and the sleeper inadvertently breaks it, the Ramban does not concede that this demonstrates the limits of *adam muad l'olam*. Rather, he argues *peshias hanizak* — the damaged party bears responsibility for his own loss by placing his vessel in a position where damage was foreseeable. Whether this constitutes something akin to assumption of risk or is a distinct principle of self-created vulnerability is a question worth considering, though the Ramban treats it as its own category.

## Produce Standards

### Defect Rates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע טִנּוֹפֶת לִסְאָה. תְּאֵנִים – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר מְתוּלָּעוֹת לְמֵאָה. מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה. קַנְקַנִּים בַּשָּׁרוֹן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר פִּיטָסוֹת לְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When selling a significant quantity of produce or a number of items, there is a possibility that there will be a certain proportion of impurities in it or that some of the product will be of substandard quality. The mishna delineates what proportion is considered acceptable, for which a buyer may not demand compensation. With regard to **one who sells produce,** i.e., grain, **to another,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **a quarter-***kav* of **impurities** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** of produce purchased. When purchasing **figs,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten infested** figs may be present **in** each **hundred** figs purchased. When purchasing **a cellar** containing barrels **of wine,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten** barrels of **souring** wine may be present **in** each **hundred** barrels purchased. When purchasing **jugs** of wine **in the Sharon** region, the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten inferior-quality jugs [*pitasot*]** of wine may be present **in** each **hundred** jugs purchased.

The daf concludes with a new *mishnah* establishing acceptable tolerance ranges for extraneous material and defects in agricultural products sold in bulk. The framework is straightforward: a purchaser of produce must expect some degree of imperfection, and provided that imperfection falls within the established range, no claim arises. The *mekach* is presumed to have been made with awareness of these customary tolerances.

### Wheat Standard

The standard for wheat is: *hamocher peiros l'chavero harei ze mekabel alav rova tinofes l'sa'ah* — one who sells grain to another, the buyer accepts up to a *rova* (a quarter of a *kav*) of extraneous material per *sa'ah*. Since a *sa'ah* contains six *kavin*, this works out to roughly four percent. Below that threshold, no complaint may be made.

### Figs Standard

*Te'einim* — figs. The buyer accepts up to *eser mesulaos l'me'ah* — ten percent wormy figs. This is a notably high threshold by modern standards, but it reflects the reality that wormy figs could not easily be detected without opening each one, and producers could not realistically guarantee below that rate.

### Wine Standard

*Martef shel yayin* — a wine cellar. The buyer accepts up to *eser kosesos l'me'ah* — ten percent of the barrels containing spoiled wine. Again, ten percent is a high allowance, reflecting the difficulty of inspecting sealed barrels without compromising the airtight seal. One of the *talmidim* raised the question of whether ancient producers had methods for checking barrels without opening them fully — apparently there were techniques for inserting instruments to assess the contents — which would affect how the tolerance interacts with the buyer's inspection obligations. The question of whether a buyer who could have inspected and did not is in a different position than one who genuinely could not is not resolved here.

### Jugs Standard

*Kankanim besharon* — jugs purchased in the Sharon Valley. The buyer accepts up to *eser pitzetzos l'me'ah* — ten percent imperfect or cracked jugs. The *mishnah* specifies the Sharon Valley specifically; the Gemara will elaborate on why *davka* there, but that discussion belongs to the next amud.

### Legumes vs. Dirt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּאנֵי רַב קַטִּינָא: רוֹבַע קִטְנִית לִסְאָה. וְעַפְרוּרִית לָא?! וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִיָּיא קְטוֹסְפָאָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה: בּוֹרֵר צְרוֹר מִגׇּרְנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי חִטִּין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ketina taught:** When the mishna states that a buyer accepts upon himself that a quarter-*kav* of impurities per *se'a* may be present, that means only that he accepts upon himself the presence of **a quarter-***kav* **of legumes,** but he does not accept upon himself the presence of a quarter-*kav* of dirt. The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that the buyer does **not** also accept upon himself that some quantity of **dirt** might be present in the produce? **But doesn't Rabba bar Ḥiyya Ketosfa'a say in the name of Rabba:** One who **picks out a pebble from** the wheat on **another's threshing floor** must **give him the value of wheat** of equal volume to the pebble that he removed. Had he not removed the pebble, the owner would have sold his wheat together with the pebble, all for the price of wheat. Accordingly, the removal of the pebble effectively caused the owner a small loss. It is apparent from this ruling that when selling produce, a buyer accepts upon himself that a quantity of dirt may be mixed in.

*Tani Rav Katina* — Rav Katina taught: *rova kitnis l'sa'ah* — a quarter of a *kav* of legumes per *sa'ah*. This clarification specifies the nature of the tolerated extraneous material in the grain case. The four-percent tolerance established by the *mishnah* refers to mixed legumes and wild vegetation that naturally grow alongside grain in the field and get harvested together with it — not to inert dirt or foreign matter.

### Mixed Vegetation

The distinction is principled. Mixed vegetation — other legumes, wild plants, the stray wild garlic that grows alongside wheat — is a natural byproduct of agricultural cultivation. It is the kind of incidental co-harvest that any buyer of grain must anticipate. Dirt, by contrast, is not an anticipated component of grain; it is a sign of careless processing or storage. A buyer is not expected to absorb the cost of dirt in his grain, and any significant quantity of dirt exceeds what is included in the *mekach*. The buyer may claim reimbursement for the weight of dirt, even when the legume mixture falls within tolerance. The Gemara will continue to examine whether this is in fact what the *mishnah* means in the next session.
