# Bava Metzia 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gold vs. Silver Status

### Intro: Silver to Gold

The daf begins, picking up from the bottom of 44b, with a debate concerning the various coinages of precious metals—gold, silver, and copper—and which is understood as the standard by which the others are defined. The immediate context is a dispute between *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* regarding *maaser sheni*: if one has already redeemed *maaser sheni* onto silver coins, may one now transfer those silver coins for gold coins, moving the *kedushah* from silver to gold? *Beit Shammai* held that one may not, and *Beit Hillel* held that one may.

The *Gemara* initially assumes that this debate mirrors the broader question found in two versions of the *mishnah*—whether gold, relative to silver, is classified as a coin (*matbeia*) or as produce (*peirot*). If gold relative to silver is considered a coin, one may transfer *maaser sheni* onto it. If it is considered produce—that is, non-coin—one cannot transfer *maaser sheni* from coin to produce. Under this framing, *Beit Shammai*, who disallows the transfer from silver to gold, must hold that gold is not coin relative to silver, while *Beit Hillel* holds that it is.

### Yochanan vs. Lakish

Within the position of *Beit Shammai*, there is a further debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish. The question is whether *Beit Shammai* would permit a direct transfer from fruit to gold coin—that is, whether *Beit Shammai* regards gold as coin for no purpose whatsoever, even absent a comparison to silver, or whether *Beit Shammai* only objects to gold's status *relative to silver*.

One view (*vachad amar*) holds that the debate would extend even to the case of *hapeiros al dinarim*—transferring directly from fruit to gold coins. On this reading, *Beit Shammai* does not consider gold to be a coin in any context; it is always categorized as produce. The *Gemara* then asks: if that is so, why do *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* debate the case of silver to gold? Why not instead debate the more striking case of fruit to gold, which would demonstrate more clearly that *Beit Shammai* denies gold the status of coin altogether?

The answer is that if only the fruit-to-gold case were debated, one might have thought that *Beit Hillel* objects only there—because at least relative to fruit, gold counts as a coin—but that even *Beit Hillel* would concede that relative to silver, gold is considered produce and the transfer would fail. Therefore, the debate is cast in terms of silver to gold, to teach us the strength of *Beit Hillel*'s position: even relative to silver, *Beit Hillel* considers gold to be a coin, and the transfer is valid.

The question of how *Beit Shammai* would treat gold relative to fruit—as always being non-coin, or only as non-coin relative to silver—remains the crux of the Yochanan-Lakish dispute.

### Borrowing Gold Issue

The *Gemara* now attempts to determine which of the two—Rabbi Yochanan or Resh Lakish—holds which position. It proposes: *tistayem d'Rabbi Yochanan hu d'amar ain michalelin*—let us conclude that it is Rabbi Yochanan who holds that according to *Beit Shammai*, one cannot even transfer from fruit to gold.

The evidence is a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan himself, cited at the top of 45a: *asur lihalvos dinar bidinar*—it is forbidden to borrow a gold *dinar* and pay back a gold *dinar*. This is a striking ruling. The *Gemara* immediately clarifies: surely it cannot refer to silver coins borrowed against silver coins, for no one would argue that silver relative to itself is not currency. The concern is clearly the case of gold *dinarei* borrowed and paid back in gold *dinarei*.

The force of this ruling lies in what was established on the previous daf. When assessing the relative value of currencies, gold is treated as the commodity being purchased, while silver functions as the reference currency. If I borrow a gold *dinar* and the gold has shifted in value relative to silver by the time I repay, the repayment in gold may reflect a different silver equivalent than what was originally borrowed—and this could constitute *ribbis*. The implication of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is therefore that gold, even on its own terms and not merely relative to silver, is always considered produce (*peira*) and never true currency. It is as though one borrowed an egg and returned an egg, where the relative value of that object may have changed.

The *Gemara* then notes that *Tosafot* raises the obvious question: since when does Rabbi Yochanan rule according to *Beit Shammai*? *Tosafot* answers that the ruling should be understood as expressing the view *aliba d'Beit Shammai*, even if the phrasing does not immediately indicate this. *Tosafot* adds—and this is important—that this ultimately connects back to the different versions of the *mishnah*. Our version reads *hazahav koneh et hakesef*: gold is the product, and by taking the gold, the other party becomes obligated to pay the silver. This framing is consistent with the *Beit Shammai* position: gold is product, not currency, perhaps even purely on its own terms.

### Exception Model

The *Gemara* responds to this proof with a qualification: *le'olam eima lach*—one can still maintain that Rabbi Yochanan is the opinion that allows transfer from fruit to gold (i.e., holds gold is coin for *maaser sheni* purposes). The ruling prohibiting gold-for-gold borrowing is different, and the two domains need not be governed by the same logic.

The explanation is as follows: *v'shani halva'ah keivan del'inyan mikach umimkar shav'yuha rabanan kepeirei*—when it comes to commercial exchange, the Rabbis have treated gold as produce relative to silver, meaning that the gold is understood as the fluctuating item whose value is measured in silver terms. Since gold is recognized in the context of commerce as a thing of fluctuating value, this recognition carries over into the law of borrowing as well. One who borrows gold and repays in gold is borrowing something whose silver-equivalent value may have changed, and that creates the potential for *ribbis*. Hence the borrowing is forbidden.

But *maaser sheni* redemption is a distinct matter. There, the act of transferring *kedushah* to money is not a commercial exchange in which silver sets the reference price. It is a one-time act of sanctification onto coin, and perhaps for that purpose gold functions adequately as coin even under the view of Rabbi Yochanan. So the prohibition on gold borrowing does not preclude using gold for *maaser sheni* redemption; rather, *maaser sheni* is the exception.

### Three Scenarios

This creates three distinct scenarios that must be distinguished. The first is the straightforward commercial exchange: a pile of gold is exchanged for silver. In this context, our *mishnah* establishes that gold is treated as produce (*peiros*) and silver as currency. The second scenario is *maaser sheni* redemption: fruit is transferred onto gold coin. In this context, even those who treat gold as produce for commercial purposes may acknowledge that gold functions adequately as coin for the purpose of *kedushah* transfer. The third scenario is a loan: one borrows gold *dinarei* and repays in gold *dinarei*. Here, because commerce has already established gold as a thing of fluctuating value relative to silver, this characterization follows gold into the borrowing context as well.

The *Gemara* supports the conclusion that *maaser sheni* is an exception by citing a tradition: *d'ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan*—when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: even though borrowing a gold *dinar* and repaying in gold is forbidden, *aval mechallelin maaser sheni alav*—one may nonetheless redeem *maaser sheni* onto gold coin. *Shema mina*—this is a clear proof that *maaser sheni* operates as an exception. In commercial settings, gold is produce; for the sanctification of *maaser sheni*, gold retains sufficient coin-status to accept the *kedushah*.

One might ask whether the same prohibition on gold-for-gold borrowing would apply to copper coins. The *Gemara* itself addresses a closely related question in the passage immediately following.

A *baraita* is now introduced: *haporeis sela mema'ot maaser sheni*—one is exchanging a *sela* for *ma'ot* out of *maaser sheni* money. Rashi explains—and *Tosafot* does not disagree—that despite the natural reading of the language, what is actually being described is the reverse: a person who has accumulated *maaser sheni* funds in copper coins (*perutot*) and wishes to avoid carrying heavy sacks of them to Jerusalem. The person therefore wishes to exchange the copper *maaser sheni* coins for silver. *Beit Shammai* says one must exchange the entire amount into silver (*bechol hasela ma'os*—meaning *bechol hama'os sela'im*). *Beit Hillel* says one exchanges half—a *shekel*'s worth of silver and a *shekel*'s worth of copper coins.

The reason for the debate, according to Rashi, is a concern about what happens upon arrival in Jerusalem. If everyone arrives with only silver coins, there will be a run on the copper market as all the pilgrims simultaneously try to exchange their silver for copper to make small purchases, driving down the exchange rate and thereby reducing the purchasing power of *maaser sheni* funds—a loss to *maaser sheni*. To prevent this, *Beit Hillel* requires retaining half the funds in copper for ready use. *Tosafot* offers the alternative explanation for *Beit Shammai*'s position: copper can become damaged over time, and therefore *Beit Shammai* insists on converting everything to silver to avoid that loss.

The *Gemara* now applies this *baraita* to the gold question. *Hashta l'Beit Shammai l'gabe priti mechallelin*—if according to *Beit Shammai* one may transfer *maaser sheni* *kedushah* onto copper coins, then surely there should be no question that gold—a more substantial form of value—should work. The *Gemara* responds: *shani priti*—copper is different. *B'asra d'sagyin charifi*—in the places where copper circulates broadly, it is actually more current and accepted than gold in everyday commerce, even if it carries no status in broader transactions. This is a striking point: the very ubiquity of copper may make it more functional as currency for *maaser sheni* purposes than the rarer gold, even though gold is intrinsically more valuable.

This brings out exactly the asymmetry one might have intuited: it is possible that for *maaser sheni*, gold does not qualify as coin while copper does, precisely because currency status is determined by widespread practical use rather than intrinsic worth.

### Money-to-Money Theory

The *Gemara* now introduces *lishna achrina*—an alternative formulation of the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, which reframes the entire debate of *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* along entirely different lines.

On this reading, *Beit Shammai*'s position is not about gold's status as coin or produce at all. Instead, *Beit Shammai* holds *ha-kesef, kesef rishon v'lo kesef sheni*: the verse *v'tzarta ha-kesef b'yadcha* teaches that the money upon which *maaser sheni* resides must be the *first* money—the initial coins onto which the *kedushah* was transferred. Transferring from that first money to any second money, regardless of what it is, is not permitted. *Beit Hillel* counters that the doubled language of the verse—*ha-kesef, kesef*—means to include any money, even a second transfer. And on this reading, the fruit-to-gold case is uncontested: *divrei hakol mechallelin*—everyone agrees one may redeem fruit directly onto gold *dinarei*, since the gold in that scenario constitutes the first money.

Within this *lishna achrina*, the sub-dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish concerns whether the debate extends even to the fruit-to-gold case. One view holds that the debate is purely about coin-to-coin transfers, making the gold-to-silver case the locus of disagreement. The other view holds that even fruit-to-gold can be contested, because if one is concerned about postponement (as will be elaborated shortly), the same concern applies even when starting from fruit.

### Refutation: Copper Case

The *Gemara* challenges the coin-to-coin theory directly. If *Beit Shammai*'s objection is purely about the prohibition of transferring from one *kesef* to a second *kesef*, then *Beit Shammai* should have debated the clearest possible instance of that principle: silver to silver. Why frame the debate around silver to gold, which introduces the gold question as an apparent confound? The *Gemara* responds: had the debate been silver to silver, one might have concluded that *Beit Hillel* objects only there—permitting silver-to-silver—but would agree with *Beit Shammai* that silver to gold is problematic, since gold relative to silver might be considered *peira*. By debating silver to gold, the *mishnah* teaches that *Beit Hillel* accepts the transfer even in that more questionable case.

But the more powerful refutation comes from the *baraita* discussed just above. The *baraita* describes the case of *haporeis sela shel maaser sheni b'Yerushalayim*—breaking a silver coin into copper *perutot* once already inside Jerusalem. *Beit Shammai* allows this, even permitting the full exchange of silver for copper inside Jerusalem. This is manifestly a case of transferring *kedushah* from one coin to another—from silver to copper. If *Beit Shammai* categorically prohibited coin-to-coin transfers, this case should be forbidden.

The *Gemara* reports that Rava defends the coin-to-coin position by distinguishing between these two scenarios: *Yerushalayim ka movat*—you are raising a question about exchanges *within* Jerusalem, which operate under a different framework entirely. Once one is in Jerusalem, the money is no longer in a holding pattern; it is at the stage of active use. The verse *v'nasata ha-kesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshcha* gives broad license to spend the money on anything. The exchanges that happen within Jerusalem—breaking coins into smaller denominations for immediate purchase—are part of the spending process and not abstract transfers of *kedushah*. They cannot serve as a model for understanding the rules of transfer *outside* Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* then presses further and produces the *baraita* cited earlier regarding the exchange of copper coins for silver *before* arriving in Jerusalem. That *baraita*—*haporeis sela mema'ot maaser sheni*—describes an exchange that takes place outside Jerusalem, and both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* accept that it may be done (they debate only the extent). This conclusively demonstrates that according to *Beit Shammai*, coin-to-coin transfers are not categorically forbidden. *Ella*—the coin-to-coin theory is therefore rejected. *Dechulei alma kesef kesef ribba*—everyone agrees the verse includes even second-generation money.

### Gezeirah Theory

With the coin-to-coin theory rejected, the *Gemara* presents a second *lishna achrina*, an entirely new way of reading the dispute. On this reading, gold is unambiguously a coin from the Torah's perspective. The debate has nothing to do with gold's status as coin or produce. Instead, *Beit Shammai*'s prohibition on transferring silver to gold is a *gezeirah*—a rabbinic safeguard.

The concern *Beit Shammai* anticipates is *shema yish'a aliyotav*—perhaps a person will delay bringing his *maaser sheni* funds to Jerusalem. If the law permits transferring silver coins to gold *dinarei* before making the journey, a person who has accumulated silver but not yet enough to exchange for a full gold coin may say: I will wait until I accumulate the full amount needed before making the transfer. Since gold coins come in large denominations, someone with slightly fewer silver coins than required will postpone the conversion—and consequently postpone the pilgrimage—until he can fill the denomination. *Beit Shammai* therefore enacts a *gezeirah* against allowing such transfers, to prevent this postponement.

*Beit Hillel* responds: *lo gazrinan shema yish'a aliyotav*—we do not enact this safeguard, because even if a person's silver does not fill a full gold *dinar*, he will still bring it to Jerusalem. As Rashi explains: *d'chi lo malu nami b'dinara asukei masik lehu she'ein masan kaved she'ma'akev*—whatever remains after converting the bulk to gold is only a modest weight of silver, easily carried. One will simply take the balance up in silver and convert the rest; the partial remainder creates no real obstacle.

### Fruit Rots Exception

On the *gezeirah* reading, both *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* agree that the fruit-to-gold transfer is permitted according to everyone—*divrei hakol mechallelin*. The question is why the concern about postponement does not apply there as well. If one has only a small amount of *maaser sheni* fruit—not enough to fill the value of a single gold *dinar*—why would one not similarly delay the transfer until enough produce accumulates?

The answer is simple: *d'keivan d'markevi*—because fruit rots. Since *maaser sheni* produce may not be consumed outside Jerusalem, leaving it in a state of *kedushah* means one cannot eat it at home. If a person waits too long, the fruit will decay and the *maaser sheni* will be lost entirely. No one will deliberately allow that to happen. Therefore, *lo mash'ei lehu*—one will not let the fruit sit around; the urgency created by the perishability of produce removes any temptation to postpone, and so there is no need for any *gezeirah* in that case.

The second opinion within this *lishna achrina* argues that even the fruit-to-gold case is contested. If the concern is that insufficient denomination will cause delay, fruit is arguably even more susceptible: if a person waits until accumulating enough fruit to fill the value of a gold coin before doing the transfer, and then the fruit rots in the meantime, the loss would be even more consequential. According to this view, the very severity of the potential loss makes the *gezeirah* more pressing, not less—and therefore even fruit-to-gold is debated.

The *Gemara* closes this section by noting a textual consideration bearing on which version of the debate is correct. *Bishlama lehach lishna*—according to the *gezeirah* reading, where the gold is certainly a valid coin from the Torah and the entire dispute is rabbinic in nature, the language of the *mishnah* fits naturally: *lo ya'ase*—one should not do this. This is the language of a *lechatchila* restriction, which implies that if done it would nonetheless be effective *bedi'avad*. But according to the reading where gold is not genuinely a coin relative to silver and the transfer therefore simply does not work, the *mishnah* should have used the language *ein mechallelin*—it cannot be done—implying that the transfer is ineffective even after the fact. The *Gemara* registers this as a *kashya*—a real difficulty—against the earlier readings.

This concludes the first major *sugya* of *hazahav koneh et hakesef*: the question of whether gold is coin or produce relative to silver, explored through the lens of *maaser sheni*, commercial exchange, and the law of borrowing.

## Coins in Chalipin

### Chalipin Concept

The *Gemara* now opens a new and conceptually rich topic, still centering on gold but from a completely different angle. A new teaching is cited: *itmar Rav v'Levi*—there is a dispute between Rav and Levi. *Chad amar matbe'a na'aseh chalipin, v'chad amar ein matbe'a na'aseh chalipin*—one holds that a coin (*matbeia*) may serve as the object of *chalipin*, and the other holds that it may not.

To understand what is at stake, it is necessary to clarify what *chalipin* means as a mode of acquisition. The classic form of *chalipin* is a barter exchange—a cow for a donkey, for instance—in which taking possession of one item finalizes the acquisition of the other. But *chalipin* also operates in a more abstract register: the famous paradigm from the book of Ruth, *shalaf ish naalo v'natan lerei'ahu*, describes handing over one's shoe or sandal to formalize a transaction. This is the basis of *kinyan sudar*—the handkerchief exchange familiar from the *choson's tish* at a wedding, where the groom takes the rabbi's handkerchief and thereby obligates himself to the *kesubah*. In this mode of *chalipin*, the object transferred is not exchanged as equivalent value for something received in return; rather, the physical act of taking an object serves as the formal gesture that expresses *gemirat da'at*—the finalization of intent—and thereby effects the *kinyan*.

The question now is whether a coin—a *matbeia*—may serve as the object handed over in such a transaction. One person wishes to acquire another's cow; rather than paying for it, the parties agree to effect the acquisition through *chalipin*. Instead of handing over a handkerchief, the buyer offers a coin. The recipient takes the coin, and the question is whether this constitutes valid *chalipin* such that the buyer now acquires the cow.

### Rav vs. Levi

Rav and Levi dispute this directly: *chad amar matbe'a na'aseh chalipin*—one holds that a coin may indeed be used for *chalipin*—*v'chad amar ein matbe'a na'aseh chalipin*—and the other holds that it may not.

It is worth noting that there is a separate debate, which the *Gemara* will address, between Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet regarding whether *peiros*—produce—may be used for *chalipin*. Those who require a *kli* (a vessel or implement) for *chalipin* would not allow either produce or a coin to serve as the object. But even those who take the broader view and allow *peiros* might still exclude a coin on particular grounds. *Tosafot* suggests that coin might even qualify as a *kli*—since money is an instrument used to accomplish transactions—but in any case the Rav-Levi dispute is specifically about whether the status of coin as currency creates a categorical exclusion from *chalipin*, regardless of one's position on *peiros*.

### Stamp Theory

Rav Pappa offers an explanation for why a coin cannot serve as the object of *chalipin*: *mishum d'da'atei atzurata, v'tzurata avida d'batela*—because one's mindset regarding a coin focuses on its image, its stamp, and that stamp is subject to being nullified.

Rashi explains the underlying logic: a coin is not conceptualized in terms of its intrinsic material—the gold or silver it is made of—but in terms of its purchasing power, which is represented by the governmental stamp it bears. Since that stamp can be invalidated—a government may demonetize a currency—the coin is not a sufficiently stable *object* to anchor a *chalipin* acquisition. The *chalipin* object needs to be conceived as a discrete, usable thing; a coin is conceived instead as an abstraction of value.

One might object that many objects are fragile or easily destroyed, and the probability of governmental demonetization may be lower than the probability of many ordinary objects breaking. But perhaps the deeper point is not about statistical likelihood: a coin's very nature is relational and representational rather than intrinsic. Even a pencil or a piece of fruit is grasped as itself; a coin is always grasped as what it can *obtain*. This abstraction from physical objecthood is what disqualifies it from serving as the object of *chalipin*. By contrast, an unstamped slug of metal—pure gold or silver not yet coined—would presumably qualify for *chalipin*, even under this theory, since it would be treated simply as metal.

### Mishnah Analysis

The *Gemara* now turns to our *mishnah*—*hazahav koneh et hakesef*—and asks whether this teaching can be read as a case of *chalipin* with coins.

*Tenan: hazahav koneh et hakesef*—the *mishnah* states that gold *koneh* (acquires) the silver. The *Gemara* asks: *mai lav d'chalipin*—is this not a case of *chalipin*? On this reading, one party takes the gold and in exchange the other party owns (*koneh*) the pile of silver sitting in the first party's possession. This would constitute a *chalipin* of coin for coin, and would support the view that *matbeia na'aseh chalipin*.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading: *lo, bedamim*—no, the *mishnah* describes a sale with payment, not a swap. The gold is the product being purchased, and the silver is the payment. When the buyer takes the gold, the seller becomes entitled to receive payment—that is, to be paid the silver—not that the seller suddenly *owns* a specific pile of silver coins sitting elsewhere.

### Gold-Silver Asymmetry

The *Gemara* presses further on the *chalipin* reading. If the *mishnah* were indeed describing a *chalipin* transaction with coins, and if coins may in principle be used for *chalipin*, then the mechanism should work symmetrically: whether the gold is taken or the silver is taken, the other party should acquire the remaining pile of coins through the *chalipin* act. Yet the *mishnah* explicitly states that gold acquires the silver—*hazahav koneh et hakesef*—but the silver does not acquire the gold—*hakesef eino koneh et hazahav*. This asymmetry is inexplicable on the *chalipin* reading: if both gold and silver are coins and coins may be used for *chalipin*, there is no reason why taking the silver coin should work any differently than taking the gold coin.

The asymmetry is fully explicable, however, on the sale-with-payment reading. In that model, gold is the product and silver is the currency. Only taking possession of the product finalizes the sale; taking the payment does not. If the buyer takes the gold (the product), the sale is complete. If the buyer instead tries to hand over silver (the payment), that is merely like handing over money, which does not effect the *kinyan*—for it is the *meshichah* on the product, not the tender of payment, that completes the transaction.

### Language of Ownership

A further challenge arises from the language of the *baraita* cited alongside the *mishnah*: *keivan shemashach et hazahav, niknah hakesef b'chol makom shehu*—once the gold is taken, the silver is acquired (*niknah*) wherever it is. This language strongly implies that the silver is actually *owned* by the other party the moment the gold is taken, not merely that payment is now owed. *B'chol makom shehu*—wherever it happens to be sitting—suggests that the silver has changed hands conceptually even before it is physically transferred, as one would expect in a swap arrangement where both piles belong to their respective new owners from the moment of the exchange. This sounds precisely like *chalipin*: you take my gold, I own your silver pile.

### Sale Not Barter

Rav Ashi resolves the difficulty: *le'olam bedamim*—the *mishnah* and *baraita* are indeed describing a sale with payment, not a *chalipin* swap. The phrase *b'chol makom shehu* does not mean that the seller literally owns a specific pile of silver. Rather, *kemot shehu, k'di'amar lei*—it means the buyer is obligated to pay according to whatever was stipulated in the agreement. The phrase is a statement about the terms of the debt, not about proprietary ownership of a pile of coins. For example, if the parties agreed to payment in freshly minted coins (*me'anki chadta yahavina lach*), the buyer cannot discharge the obligation with old coins (*me'anki atika*)—even if those older coins are arguably of higher quality—because the seller specified new coins, presumably intending to hold them in reserve and wanting them in pristine condition. This illustrates that *b'chol makom shehu* is about the precise terms of the obligation rather than about ownership of a specific deposit.

The conclusion of the *Gemara* is therefore that the *mishnah*—*hazahav koneh et hakesef*—describes a straightforward sale in which gold is the product and silver is the means of payment. When the buyer takes the gold, the seller becomes owed the silver. The asymmetry of the *mishnah* is fully explained by the product/payment distinction. The *mishnah* is not evidence that *matbeia na'aseh chalipin*; the discussion of whether a coin may serve as the object of *chalipin* remains unresolved by this *mishnah* and will continue in the *sugya* to follow.
