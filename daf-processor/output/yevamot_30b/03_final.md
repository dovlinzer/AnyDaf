# Yevamot 30b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Brothers & Zika

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְכָנַס נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וּמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה, וּשְׁנִיָּה מִשּׁוּם צָרָתָהּ. עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּמֵת — נׇכְרִית חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: **The husband of one of the sisters died** childless, **and** the brother **who was married to the unrelated woman married,** i.e., performed levirate marriage with, the deceased brother's **wife and** later **died** himself, childless. In this situation, both women happen for levirate marriage before the other, remaining, brother. **The first** woman **is dismissed due to** the prohibition proscribing **the sister of one's wife,** as she is the sister of this brother's wife, **and the second** woman is dismissed **due to** her status as the first woman's **rival wife.** Following the first levirate marriage, this second woman became the rival wife of the sister, and is therefore exempt from levirate marriage as well. If, however, the brother married to the unrelated woman **performed** only **levirate betrothal,** but had not yet consummated the levirate marriage with the sister, **and he died, the unrelated woman,** whose halakhic status with regard to *yibbum* is similar to that of a sister's rival wife, must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna* under discussion presents a case involving three brothers: two are married to two sisters, and one is married to a third, unrelated woman. Concretely, Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah respectively, while Levi is married to Devorah.

### Sequence Analysis

The *mishna* then describes a sequence of events. First, Shimon divorces Leah — she is out of the picture from the very outset. Second, Levi dies, at which point either Reuven or Shimon could perform *yibum* with Devorah. Third, Shimon does in fact perform *yibum* with Devorah, bringing her into his household. Finally, Shimon dies.

The question is: what is Reuven's status vis-à-vis Devorah? Since Leah was already divorced before Levi died — before Devorah became relevant to the conversation at all — she was fully out of the picture by the time Shimon died. Devorah is therefore permitted to Reuven. This is the principle the *mishna* establishes: *zo hi she'amru, v'chulan shemetu o nisgareshu* — when the *ervah* has died or been divorced, her *tzarot* are permitted. In this case, since Leah was removed from the equation at the very first stage, Devorah is permitted to Reuven. The *mishna* deliberately chose an extreme case where Leah was out of the picture well before anything else occurred.

A more nuanced scenario — one the *mishna* does not directly address — would be if Shimon had first married Devorah and then divorced Leah afterward. The question in that scenario would be whether the period during which both women were simultaneously in Shimon's household created a permanent problem for Devorah vis-à-vis Reuven. But the *mishna*'s chosen case avoids that complication entirely.

### Yesh Zika Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דַּעֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר, הָא לָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר — נׇכְרִית יַבּוֹמֵי נָמֵי מְיַיבְּמָה. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת אֵין זִיקָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּחַד אַחָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara deduces the following *halakha* from the second clause of the mishna: **The reason** that the mishna requires *ḥalitza* is specifically **because he,** the brother who was married to the unrelated woman, **performed levirate betrothal with** the sister. **Consequently, had he not performed levirate betrothal with her, the unrelated woman** would be permitted to **enter into levirate marriage as well.** This is true despite the fact that the levirate bond could potentially render her the rival wife of his wife's sister. **Rav Naḥman said: That is to say, the** levirate **bond is not substantial;** the woman requiring levirate marriage is not considered married to the *yavam*. **And** this is true **even** if the levirate bond was **with a single brother,** as this widowed sister happened for levirate marriage only before the brother who was married to the unrelated woman; her levirate bond was with him alone.

The *gemara* now probes the *mishna*'s implicit logic. The *mishna* specifies that the reason the case works is *d'girash v'achar kach met* — Shimon first divorced Leah and only then did Levi die and *yibum* unfold. But what would have happened had Levi died first, creating a *zika* — a juridical connection — between Shimon and Devorah, before Leah was divorced? The *gemara* suggests that if Levi died first and Shimon thereby acquired a *zika* to Devorah, Devorah might immediately become a *tzarat ervah* relative to Reuven, even before any *yibum* had taken place. This would be problematic regardless of what followed.

Rav Ashi draws an explicit inference: *amar Rav Ashi, zos omeret, yesh zikah afilu b'trei achei* — from this *mishna* we can infer that *zika* applies even when there are two brothers. When Levi died, Reuven also had a claim on Devorah, not just Shimon. Even so, Shimon's *zika* is strong enough to render Devorah a quasi-*tzarat ervah*. This is a robust statement of *yesh zika*.

### Ein Zika Challenge

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: Rav Nechman held *ein zika afilu b'chad* — that *zika* is not a legally operative connection, even when there is only a single surviving brother. He derived this from an earlier *mishna* in which Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah, and Levi is married to Devorah. That *mishna* stated that if Reuven dies and Levi subsequently performs *ma'amar* with Rachel and then dies, Shimon cannot perform *yibum* with Devorah because she has become a *tzarat ervah*. Rav Nechman's reading is that Rachel only became problematic because of the *ma'amar* — the *zika* alone, without *ma'amar*, was insufficient to pull Rachel into the equation. Yet the *mishna* before us seems to imply that even *zika* alone — without *ma'amar* — creates a problem with Devorah. How are these two *mishnayot* to be reconciled?

## Zika & Tzarat Erva

### Rav Ashi's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַב אָשֵׁי: הוּא הַדִּין דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר — נׇכְרִית מִיחְלָץ חָלְצָה, יַבּוֹמֵי לָא מִיַּיבְּמָה. וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי מַאֲמָר — לְאַפּוֹקֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, דְּאָמְרִי: מַאֲמָר קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Ashi** could have **said to you:** Rav Naḥman's deduction in the first mishna was not logically necessary. With regard to that mishna, one could have said that when the mishna requires *ḥalitza* in the case of levirate betrothal, **the same is true even** in the case **where he** who was married to the unrelated woman **did not perform levirate betrothal with her.** In that case as well, **the unrelated woman must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage** since she was the rival wife of his wife's sister by levirate bond. **And** the reason **that it teaches** the ruling in the case of **levirate betrothal** was not in order to inform us that she was forbidden due to levirate betrothal, but rather **to exclude** the statement of **Beit Shammai, who say** that through the act of **levirate betrothal** one **acquires** the *yevama* **as a full-fledged acquisition.** According to that opinion, the unrelated woman does not even require *ḥalitza*, since she is considered to be a rival wife of a forbidden relative. This mishna **teaches us that** the *halakha* **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.**

Rav Ashi responds to Rav Nechman's challenge directly. He concedes the point: even without *ma'amar*, the *zika* itself would have made Rachel a problem. *Af al gav d'lo avad bah ma'amar, nachis michlal chalitzah, yibumi lo miyabma* — even without *ma'amar*, Devorah would have required *chalitza* and *yibum* would have been forbidden.

### Ma'amar's Role

If so, why did the *mishna* specifically mention that Levi performed *ma'amar* with Rachel? The *ma'amar* did not create the problem — *zika* alone was sufficient to pull Rachel in and render Devorah a *tzarat ervah*. The *ma'amar* is mentioned not to establish the prohibition but to address a different question entirely.

### Beit Shammai Debate

The *mishna*'s mention of *ma'amar* is *l'apuki mi'Beit Shammai d'amri ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur* — to exclude the position of Beit Shammai, who hold that *ma'amar* constitutes a full acquisition, equivalent to *nisuin*. According to Beit Shammai, one might have thought that if Levi performed *ma'amar* with Rachel, Rachel was now fully his wife. In that case, when Shimon subsequently became the surviving brother, Rachel would be a complete *tzarat ervah* — not merely a quasi-*tzarat ervah* on the strength of *zika* — and Devorah could theoretically walk free without *chalitza*, since she is the *tzarah* of a genuine *ervah*. The *mishna* teaches that we do not rule like Beit Shammai: even with *ma'amar*, Rachel is not fully acquired, *chalitza* remains necessary, and *yibum* with Devorah is forbidden.

So the *mishna*'s structure is this: *zika* itself pulls Rachel in and creates the problem; *ma'amar* does not solve it by making Rachel a complete *nisuin*-level wife. Doing *chalitza* with Devorah is required, and doing *yibum* with Devorah is forbidden.

### Rav Nechman's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב נַחְמָן — קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ מֵת וְאַחַר כָּךְ גֵּירַשׁ — צָרָתָהּ מוּתֶּרֶת, אֶלָּא ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי כָּנַס וְאַחַר כָּךְ גֵּירַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question from the opposite perspective: **And according to Rav Nechman,** who deduced from the earlier mishna that the levirate bond is not substantial, the deduction **of Rav Ashi** that the levirate bond is substantial is **difficult. And if you would** try to resolve this in a similar manner and **say:** With regard to the ruling in the present mishna, which states that the sister's **rival wife,** i.e., the unrelated woman, **is permitted, the same is true even if** Levi had **died and** then **afterward** the brother married to the other sister **divorced** his wife, there is a difficulty. If that is the case, then **what does** the phrase **this is,** cited at the end of the mishna, come **to exclude?** If we follow this explanation then the same ruling would hold true in all cases. The Gemara responds: **It excludes** the case where Shimon first **married** the unrelated woman **and** only **afterward divorced** the sister, as in such circumstances the unrelated woman is most certainly a rival wife of Reuven's wife's sister and is therefore not permitted.

Having explained Rav Ashi's reading, the *gemara* now turns the tables. *U'l'Rav Nachman kashya d'Rav Ashi* — according to Rav Nechman, who holds *ein zika* and maintained that Rachel was only a problem because of *ma'amar*, how does our *mishna* work? If *zika* is legally inert, why does the *mishna* appear to exclude cases where Levi dies before Leah is divorced, implying that the *zika* at that moment creates a problem?

Rav Nechman's answer, as the *gemara* reconstructs it, is that the *mishna* does not necessarily exclude that scenario. One might say: *hu hadin d'afilu metat ach v'achar kach gerush, tzarata muteret* — even if Levi died first and only then Leah was divorced, the case would still be permissible, since *ein zika* and the *zika* creates no legal problem. If so, what is the *mishna* actually excluding?

## Three Scenarios

### Scenario A

The simplest scenario — Scenario A — is precisely the one the *mishna* describes: Leah is divorced before Levi dies, before anything else unfolds. Leah is out of the picture from the very beginning. This is the easiest case and clearly presents no problem.

### Scenario B

Scenario B is that Levi dies first and then Leah is divorced. According to those who hold *yesh zika*, this might immediately create a problem at the moment Levi dies, since Shimon acquires a *zika* to Devorah while Leah is still in the picture. According to those who hold *ein zika*, however, Scenario B is just as permissible as Scenario A — the *zika* is meaningless, and as long as Leah is out of the picture before Shimon dies, there is no issue.

### Scenario C

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. גֵּירַשׁ אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמֵת נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית, וּכְנָסָהּ הַמְגָרֵשׁ וָמֵת — זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁמֵּתוּ אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשׁוּ — צָרוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: Shimon, **the husband of one of the sisters, divorced his wife, and** then Levi, **who was married to the unrelated woman, died, and** Shimon, **the man who divorced** his wife, **married,** i.e., performed levirate marriage with, **her,** i.e., this unrelated woman. **And** then Shimon himself later **died,** so that the unrelated woman happened for levirate marriage before Reuven, the third brother, who is married to the second sister. In this scenario, Reuven is allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with the unrelated woman. **This is** the case that was referred to when **they said: And** with regard to **all those** fifteen forbidden relatives **who died or were divorced, their rival wives are permitted** to enter into levirate marriage. This is because at the time that they happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage they were no longer the rival wives of a forbidden relative.

Scenario C is more complex: Levi dies, Shimon performs *yibum* with Devorah and thereby marries her, and then — while married to both Devorah and Leah — he divorces Leah before he himself dies. In this scenario, there was a period when Shimon was simultaneously married to both women. Even though Leah was out of the picture before Shimon died, the question is whether that matters. This scenario is what the *gemara* designates as *kanas v'achar kach geireish* — he married Devorah and only afterward divorced Leah.

### Nisuin vs. Mita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, דְּאָמַר: תַּבְרָא, מִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה זוֹ לֹא שָׁנָה זוֹ. וְהַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר מִיתָה מַפֶּלֶת, וְהַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר נִישּׂוּאִין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים מַפִּילִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **This works out well if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yirmeya, who said** with regard to a seeming contradiction between this mishna and an earlier mishna (13a): The *mishnayot* are **disjointed; he who taught this** mishna **did not teach that** mishna. The earlier mishna established the principle that if a man was married to two women, one of whom was a forbidden relative, and he divorced the forbidden relative before he died, then the rival wife is no longer prohibited to the brothers. This dispute is based upon the following: **This *tanna*** from the earlier mishna **holds that death determines** her status when **she happens** before the brothers, i.e., the crucial moment for determining whether the prohibition relating to rival wives applies is the moment at which the brother dies. In other words, whether the *yevama* is permitted to the *yavam* is determined by the status of the *yevama* at that given moment. Therefore, in the case where he had married a forbidden relative and later divorced her, the rival wife would be permitted. **And this *tanna*** of our present mishna **holds that the original marriage determines** her status when **she happens** before the brothers. If, at the time the woman was married to the deceased brother she was forbidden as a close relative, and her rival wife was likewise forbidden as the rival wife of a forbidden relative, then even though the status of the relative had changed at the time of the death of the brother, both she and her rival wife remain forbidden.

This brings the *gemara* to a fundamental dispute about when *tzarat ervah* is determined. One opinion holds *nisuin rishonim mappilin* — the relevant moment is the time of the original marriage; the question is whether the two women were ever simultaneously married to the same man. Another opinion holds *mita mapelet* — everything is determined at the moment of death; if the *ervah* is out of the picture when the husband dies, there is no problem regardless of the marital history. The *gemara* notes that this dispute was articulated by earlier *amoraim*: one position is associated with Rabbi Yirmiyah's reading (*mi sheshana zu lo shana zu*, that the different *mishnayot* reflect different *tannaim*), and the other with Rava's position that it is all one *tanna* and everything goes by the moment of death.

## Rabbi Yirmiyah & Rava

### Rabbi Yirmiyah View

If one follows Rabbi Yirmiyah's approach — that *tzarat ervah* is determined by the time of marriage, i.e., *nisuin rishonim mappilin* — then the criterion is whether the two women were ever simultaneously married to the same man. Under this view, Scenario A and Scenario B are both permitted: in neither case was Shimon simultaneously married to both Leah and Devorah. Scenario C, however, is problematic, because Shimon was indeed married to both women at the same time, even though Leah was eventually divorced before he died.

### What Mishna Excludes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי כָּנַס וּלְבַסּוֹף גֵּירַשׁ. אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: לְעוֹלָם חַד תַּנָּא הוּא, וְ״זוֹ וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר זוֹ״ קָתָנֵי, ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? עַל כׇּרְחָךְ כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** According to this opinion, it is indeed possible to state that the phrase **this is** comes **to exclude** the case where **he married** one woman **and ultimately divorced** the other. According to this mishna, in that case, the rival wives would be prohibited. **However, if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava, who said: Actually,** understand **this** to be the opinion of **a single *tanna*, and he teaches** the mishna employing the style: **This, and it is unnecessary to say that,** a difficulty remains. According to Rava, both *mishnayot* maintain the position that in the case where the *yavam* married one woman and ultimately divorced the other, the rival wife would be permitted. If that is the case, **what** does the phrase **this is** come **to exclude?** In what case would the rival wife be prohibited to the *yavam*? The Gemara responds: **Perforce** Rav Naḥman **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yirmeya** with regard to his interpretation of the *mishnayot*.

This gives the *gemara* a clear answer to the question of what the *mishna* is excluding. Since, under *ein zika*, Scenario B is permissible — the *zika* creates no problem — what remains to be excluded is Scenario C, the case of *kanas v'achar kach geireish*: Shimon married Devorah and only later divorced Leah. That case is forbidden because they were simultaneously married at some point, and *nisuin rishonim* determines the status. The *mishna*'s emphasis that Leah was divorced first is not merely incidental — it is precisely excluding the scenario in which Shimon was ever married to both women at once.

The *gemara* makes this explicit: *al kurchak k'Rabbi Yirmiyah s'vira lei* — if one holds *ein zika* and reads the *mishna* consistently, one is compelled to follow Rabbi Yirmiyah's view that the critical moment is the time of marriage, not the time of death. Only then is there a meaningful case to exclude.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא, הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב אָשֵׁי — ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מֵת בְּלֹא גֵּירֵשׁ. אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב נַחְמָן, ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? עַל כׇּרְחָךְ כְּרַב אָשֵׁי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question from a different perspective: **And according to Rava,** who maintains that in both cases the rival wife is permitted, **this works out well if he holds in accordance** with the opinion **of Rav Ashi** that the levirate bond is substantial. In that case the phrase **this is** comes **to exclude** the case where **he died without divorcing his wife.** In this case the rival wife is forbidden because the entire time she was the rival wife of a forbidden relative by levirate bond. **If, however, he holds in accordance** with the opinion of **Rav Nechman** in this matter, **what** does the phrase **this is** come **to exclude?** The Gemara answers: **Perforce** Rava **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ashi.** Accordingly, with regard to this *halakha* there is a connection between the various opinions as to how to interpret the *mishnayot* and the dispute.

Rava, however, holds that everything goes by the moment of death — *mita mapelet*. Under Rava's view, any divorce before the husband's death is sufficient, regardless of the marital history. If Leah was divorced at any point before Shimon died, Devorah is permitted. This means Scenario A, Scenario B, and even Scenario C are all permissible, because in all three cases Leah was out of the picture at the moment of Shimon's death.

### Rava's Conclusion

If Rava holds that all three scenarios of divorce work, the *gemara* asks: *zu hi l'me'utei mai?* — what does the *mishna*'s formulation actually exclude? There must be some case that does not work when *geirushin* is in play. The *gemara* answers: *meis b'lo geireish* — the case being excluded is one where Levi died and Shimon acquired a *zika* to Devorah, and then Shimon himself died without ever divorcing Leah. In that situation, if one holds *yesh zika* as Rav Ashi does, the *zika* alone was sufficient to render Devorah a *tzarat ervah* at the moment of Levi's death, and since Leah was never divorced, the problem was never resolved.

This is a striking conclusion. The one case excluded under Rava's view is precisely the case where Shimon's connection to Devorah was at its weakest — a mere *zika*, without *yibum*, without *ma'amar*. Yet that weak *zika*, on the view of *yesh zika*, is enough to create a lasting problem. All scenarios in which an actual divorce occurred at any point before Shimon's death are fine under Rava; the only failure case is the *zika* without any subsequent divorce whatsoever.

The *gemara* then asks: *ella i savra leh k'Rav Nachman* — but if one holds like Rav Nechman that *ein zika*, then under Rava's framework there is nothing left to exclude, because every scenario of divorce before death works and the *zika* is meaningless. *Al kurchak k'Rav Ashi s'vira lei* — therefore Rava must hold like Rav Ashi, that *yesh zika*. Only then can Rava's framework exclude anything at all, and the excluded case is the *zika* without any divorce.

## Safek & Status Quo

### Safek Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְכוּלָּן שֶׁהָיוּ בָּהֶן קִדּוּשִׁין אוֹ גֵרוּשִׁין בְּסָפֵק — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ הַצָּרוֹת חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. כֵּיצַד סְפֵק קִדּוּשִׁין? זָרַק לָהּ קִדּוּשִׁין, סָפֵק קָרוֹב לוֹ סָפֵק קָרוֹב לָהּ — זֶהוּ סְפֵק קִדּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** if **any of these** fifteen women who are prohibited as forbidden relatives **had undergone a betrothal or divorce** whose status **is uncertain** with the deceased brother, **then those** women who were their **rival wives** must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage** since they are possibly the rival wives of forbidden relatives. The mishna elaborates: **How** could there be a situation of **uncertainty** with regard to **betrothal?** If in the public domain **he threw her** an item for the purpose of **betrothal** and there were eight cubits between them, and the item **was possibly closer to him** and did not enter into her domain, and **possibly closer to her,** i.e., within four cubits of her, whereby she could acquire the object, **this is** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **betrothal.**

The *mishna* now turns to a different category of cases. Up to this point, the discussion concerned *erva* who was definitively married or definitively divorced, and the question was about the sequence and timing of those events. The *mishna* now addresses situations where the *erva*'s status is itself uncertain — she may be married, or she may not be. The two categories of uncertainty are *safek kiddushin* (uncertain betrothal) and *safek gerushin* (uncertain divorce).

### Safek Solution

In all such cases, the *mishna* rules: *tzarotehen choltzot v'lo misyabmot* — the co-wife performs *chalitza* and does not do *yibum*. She cannot perform *yibum* because perhaps the *erva* is in the picture and that would create a forbidden situation. But she cannot simply walk away either, because perhaps the *erva* is not in the picture and some form of resolution is required. The solution is *chalitza* without *yibum*.

### Safek Kiddushin

The *mishna* defines *safek kiddushin* with the following scenario: a man declares betrothal to a woman and throws the ring between them, and it is unclear whether it fell closer to his domain or to hers — *safek karov lo safek karov lah*. As Rashi explains, the relevant *kinyan* here is that of *arba amos* — within a person's four cubits, objects can be acquired. If the couple were standing eight cubits apart, with their respective *arba amos* meeting at the midpoint, and the ring landed precisely at that midpoint, it is genuinely unclear whether the woman's *kinyan* of *arba amos* was effected. That is *safek kiddushin* — we do not know if she was ever betrothed to him.

### Safek Gerushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפֵק גֵּרוּשִׁין — כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן, יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד, זֶהוּ סְפֵק גֵּרוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Uncertainty** with regard to **divorce** occurs when, for instance, **he wrote a** bill of divorce **in his handwriting but there are no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document, or **there are** the signatures of **witnesses** on the document **but there is no date** written **in it,** or **the date is** written **in it but there is only** the signature of **a single witness.** Since there is doubt as to whether these three kinds of bills of divorce are valid, a woman who was divorced through them is only possibly divorced, and so **this** case **is** called **uncertainty** with regard to **divorce.**

*Safek gerushin* is defined differently. Rather than a parallel scenario of a *get* that fell ambiguously between the two parties, the *mishna* instead presents three cases of a *get* that is valid *d'oraisa* but invalidated *m'd'rabbanan*, and which is therefore treated as a quasi-*gerushin* of uncertain status. The three cases are: a *get* written in the husband's own handwriting but lacking witnesses (*kasav b'ksav yado v'ein alav edim*); a *get* bearing witnesses but lacking a date (*yesh alav edim v'ein bo zman*); and a *get* with a date but bearing only one witness rather than two (*yesh bo zman v'ein bo ela ed echad*). The *mishna*'s phrasing is worth noting: the language flows from one case into the next, with each statement introducing an element that the next case then takes as its starting point — a mnemonic elegance in the oral formulation.

## Gerushin vs. Kiddushin

### Missing Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאִילּוּ בְּגֵרוּשִׁין, סָפֵק קָרוֹב לוֹ סָפֵק קָרוֹב לָהּ לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara remarks: **But yet when** discussing **divorce, it does not teach** the case where it is **uncertain** if the bill of divorce **is closer to him, and uncertain if it is closer to her.** It would have been appropriate to describe this case, as it parallels the case involving the object of betrothal. He could have tossed it in such a way that it was not clear to whom the bill was closer.

The *gemara* observes an asymmetry. By *kiddushin*, the *mishna* gave the parallel case of a ring thrown between them — a genuine *safek* about whether the *kinyan* was effected. By *gerushin*, however, the *mishna* did not give the parallel case of a *get* thrown toward the woman, where it is unclear whether it entered her domain or remained in his. Why not? *V'ilu b'gerushin safek karov lo safek karov lah lo katani* — this case is simply not mentioned.

### Rabba's Status Quo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: אִשָּׁה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת הֶיתֵּר לַשּׁוּק עוֹמֶדֶת, וּמִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְאוֹסְרָהּ — אַל תַּאַסְרֶנָּה מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** the mishna did not present this situation as well? **Rabba said:** When this type of uncertainty exists, the performance of *ḥalitza* is not mandatory, as **this woman,** the rival wife, **has the presumptive status of being permitted** to marry a man **from the** general **public.** At the time of her marriage she was rendered a rival wife of a forbidden relative. **And due to an uncertainty** alone **would you render her forbidden** to the general public until she performs *ḥalitza*, simply because it is unclear to us whether or not the forbidden relative had indeed been divorced? **Do not render her forbidden due to an uncertainty.** This is not, however, the case with the various bills of divorce mentioned in the mishna, for they are all certainly considered effective bills of divorce, even if the circumstances involved raise some questions or doubts.

Rava explains the omission on the basis of *chazakah* — the principle of status quo. In the case of a *get* thrown ambiguously between them, the woman was definitively married before the act. We do not know if that status changed. Since her status quo is that she remains married, we do not treat her as a *safek gerusha* — she retains the *chazakah* of a married woman, and there is no *safek* to speak of. The *get* that may or may not have reached her does not displace that presumption.

### Kiddushin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, בְּקִדּוּשִׁין נָמֵי נֵימָא: אִשָּׁה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת הֶיתֵּר לַיָּבָם עוֹמֶדֶת, וּמִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְאוֹסְרָהּ — אַל תַּאַסְרֶנָּה מִסָּפֵק!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If that is so, let us say** in the case of **betrothal as well** that **this woman,** the rival wife, **has the presumptive status of being permitted to the *yavam*** before he betrothed the forbidden relative, **and due to the uncertainty** whether she is the rival wife of a forbidden relative **would you come and render her forbidden? Do not render her forbidden due to an uncertainty.** Consequently, you should permit her to enter into levirate marriage.

One might apply the same logic in reverse to *safek kiddushin*: before the ring was thrown, the woman was definitively unmarried. Her status quo is that she is single. If we keep everyone at their status quo, she should simply remain unmarried, and no *safek kiddushin* should be recognized at all. If that is so, her co-wife Devorah's status quo — that she was permitted to do *yibum* with the *yavam* — should equally be preserved, and there should be no reason to require *chalitza*.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* presses this logic: *i hachi, b'kiddushin nami neima, isha zo b'chezkat heter l'yavam omedes* — apply the same status quo principle to Devorah in the *safek kiddushin* case. Before the questionable *kiddushin* took place, Devorah was permitted for *yibum*. *Umisafek ata ba u'osra* — why should the doubt created by the building collapse change that status? *Al tosrena misafek* — keep her at her status quo and let her go free without *chalitza*.

## Cost of Chumra

### Message Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, לְחוּמְרָא. הַאי — חוּמְרָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי קוּלָּא הוּא. זִימְנִין דְּאָזֵיל הוּא, וּמְקַדֵּשׁ לַהּ לַאֲחוֹתַהּ קִדּוּשֵׁי וַדַּאי. וְאִי נָמֵי, זִימְנִין דְּאָתֵא אַחֵר וּמְקַדֵּשׁ לַהּ לְדִידַהּ קִדּוּשֵׁי וַדַּאי, וְכֵיוָן דְּאָסַר לַהּ מָר לְצָרָה לְיַיבּוֹמֵי, אָמְרִי: דְּקַמָּא — קִדּוּשִׁין, וּדְבָתְרָא — לָאו קִדּוּשִׁין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **There,** with regard to betrothal, the *halakha* follows **the stringent ruling,** because the rival wife is certainly his wife and requires levirate marriage. As there is uncertainty with regard to the betrothal with the forbidden relative, the ruling is stringent; she may not enter into levirate marriage and must only perform *ḥalitza*. The Gemara objects: Yet **this is a stringency that might bring about a leniency** in another scenario. How so? **Sometimes** that same man who betrothed the forbidden relative might **go and betroth her sister** with **a betrothal** whose status is **certain.** If one says that due to a stringency the rival wife may not enter into levirate marriage due to her possible status as the rival wife of a forbidden relative, people might come to assume that the betrothal with the forbidden relative was a valid betrothal, and that the subsequent betrothal with her sister was not valid, since she is his wife's sister. This would be assumed because they would not know that the status of the first betrothal was itself uncertain and that only due to a stringent ruling is the rival wife not allowed to enter into levirate marriage. In fact, the status of the betrothal with the sister of the forbidden relative is also uncertain. As a result of this ruling, however, people might be led to think that a man's wife, i.e., the sister of the forbidden relative, is in fact permitted. **Alternatively, sometimes another man might come and betroth** the forbidden relative **herself** with **a betrothal** whose status is **certain, and since the Master** rendered it **prohibited** for **the rival wife to enter into levirate marriage,** people would **say** that the betrothal **of the first** man, i.e., the deceased brother, was a fully effective **betrothal, and** that the betrothal **of the latter** man **was not** a valid **betrothal.** If she was married to the first man, then she is forbidden to the second as the man's wife, and betrothal cannot take effect with her. However, since the status of her betrothal to the first man was uncertain, then she is also considered possibly betrothed to the second man and would require a divorce from him as well. As a result, one can find a situation that would lead people to think that a man's wife is in fact permitted.

The *gemara* responds: even though the status quo argument is valid in principle, in the case of *safek kiddushin* we are nonetheless stringent and require *chalitza* rather than permitting *yibum*. The concern is the message conveyed to the public. If Reuven declares his intent to perform *yibum* with Devorah and is told he may not, this prohibition sends a signal: people will infer that Rachel must in fact be married to Shimon, since that is the only reason *yibum* would be denied. If people conclude that Rachel is married to Shimon, they will draw consequences from that: any subsequent *kiddushin* that Rachel or someone close to her entered into will be viewed as having failed. *Ud'batar lav kiddushin* — those subsequent acts are actually valid, but the public message will lead people to treat them as void.

Specifically, the *gemara* identifies two problematic consequences: if Shimon subsequently performs a definite *kiddushin* to Leah (*kiddushei vadai l'achota*), people will assume it was ineffective because Rachel was already his wife; and if another man — let us say Barak — performs a definite *kiddushin* to Rachel herself after the ambiguous first *kiddushin*, people will assume that too was ineffective. Both of these real marriages will be undermined by the false inference that the first *safek kiddushin* was real.

### Chalitza Consistency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מַצְרְכַתְּ חֲלִיצָה — מִידָּע יָדְעִי דְּחוּמְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. אִי הָכִי, גֵּירוּשִׁין נָמֵי לִיתְנֵי, וְלַיצְרְכַהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּמִידַּע יָדְעִי דְּחוּמְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba answered: **Since you require *ḥalitza*** and you do not exempt her completely, all will **know that this is merely a stringency** and that the Sages did not decide with certitude that the first betrothal was fully valid. Consequently, they would not come to disregard the other betrothal. Abaye raised a challenge: **If so, let** the mishna **teach** the case where it is uncertain whether the item is closer to him or closer to her with regard to **divorce, and** stipulate **that she requires *ḥalitza*. And they would know that this is merely a stringency** and not make a mistake.

Here is the critical insight: the *gemara* notes that the relevant public signal is not the absence of *yibum* but the performance of *chalitza*. And *chalitza*, in the case of *safek kiddushin*, is entirely consistent with the true legal reality. What is the status quo? Rachel was not married to Shimon — that is the *chazakah*, since we go by the status quo of being unmarried. *Chalitza* with Devorah sends precisely that message: the reason *chalitza* was performed is that Rachel is not in the picture, that there was no operative *kiddushin*. People who see *chalitza* will understand that Rachel is out of the equation, and the subsequent *kiddushin* to Leah and to Rachel herself will be understood as valid.

This is what makes the *chumra* here cost-free. The *halacha* is that Rachel, by status quo, is not married to Shimon after a *safek kiddushin*. Demanding *chalitza* from Devorah conveys the accurate message. The only restriction is that Reuven is not permitted to do *yibum* — but the public does not see the absence of *yibum*; they see the performance of *chalitza*, which accurately reflects the underlying legal reality. *Meida yedaya d'chumra b'alma hu* — people will understand that this is simply a stringency. The *chumra* costs nothing because it sends no misleading signal. *V'kevan d'kamatba'ei chalitzah* — since *chalitza* is demanded, the right message is broadcast.

### Wrong Message

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת, מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. הָכָא נָמֵי, אִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת, מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת! וְתִתְיַיבֵּם, וְאֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם — אַחֲזָקָה קָא קָיְימָא. אִי נָמֵי: גֵּירוּשִׁין דְּקָיְימָא עֶרְוָה דְּקָא מוֹכְחָ[א], וְצָרָתָהּ קָמַצְרְכַתְּ לַהּ חֲלִיצָה, אָמְרִי: קָמוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן בְּגִיטָּא דְּגִיטָּא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְאָתוּ לְיַיבּוֹמֵי לְצָרָה. מַפּוֹלֶת, מִי קָמוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן בְּמַפּוֹלֶת?
>
> **Translation:** He answered: A mistake could in fact be made here, as, **if you say that she** must **perform *ḥalitza*** then **she** may also **enter into levirate marriage.** People might mistakenly think that if she is suitable for *ḥalitza* then she is also suitable for levirate marriage, and as a result the woman might enter into levirate marriage, despite the fact that it is forbidden for her to do so. Abaye objected: **Here too,** in the case of uncertain betrothal, the concern exists that **if you say that she performs *ḥalitza*** then **she** might also **enter into levirate marriage.** Rabba answered: **So let her enter into levirate marriage, and** there is **no** problem **with that.** In this instance **she remains with** her **presumptive status** as permitted because she was originally assumed to be permitted and was rendered forbidden only due to our concern. However, there would be no actual transgression involved even if she were to enter into levirate marriage. **Alternatively,** there is another reason to differentiate between the cases. In the case of **divorce where there is a forbidden relative who indicates** that the rival wife is forbidden due to her status as the rival wife of a forbidden relative, **and you require that her rival wife perform *ḥalitza*,** people **will say: The Sages determined that this bill of divorce is a full-fledged bill of divorce.** Consequently, they required her rival wife to perform *ḥalitza*, **and** people **may come to consummate the levirate marriage with the rival wife** based on this mistaken assumption. In cases of **collapse,** however, **could the Sages have determined** who died first **in the collapse?** As it is known to all that there was a doubt that could not be clarified, it is clear that the Sages required the rival wife to perform *ḥalitza* only due to this uncertainty. Therefore, there is no concern that she would come to enter into levirate marriage because of this *ḥalitza*.

In the case of *safek gerushin*, however, the situation is reversed. Here Rachel was definitively married to Shimon, and the question is whether she was divorced. Her status quo is that she remains married. If Devorah were required to perform *chalitza*, people would ask: why is *chalitza* being done? Isn't Rachel still in the picture as a *tzarat ervah*? The answer they would draw is: the Rabbis must have investigated and determined that Rachel was in fact divorced — otherwise, why would *chalitza* be occurring at all? *Kamu bah rabbanan b'gita d'gita maalya hu* — they will assume that the Rabbis validated the *get* and established that Rachel is genuinely divorced.

That inference leads directly to harmful consequences. If people conclude that Rachel is divorced, they will then reason that *yibum* with Devorah is possible — since Rachel the *ervah* is out of the picture. Reuven or someone like him will come to perform *yibum* when Rachel is in fact not divorced and Devorah remains a genuine *tzarat ervah*. *V'asu l'yibumei l'tzarah* — this is a serious *issur*, potentially implicating *karet*. Performing *chalitza* in the case of *safek gerushin* is therefore not a no-cost *chumra* — it actively generates the most dangerous leniency imaginable.

### No Chalitza Demanded

Therefore, the *gemara* concludes, the Rabbis did not impose *chalitza* as a *chumra* in the case of *safek gerushin*. When the status quo says Rachel is married, Devorah retains her prior status of *tzarat ervah* and is simply permitted to go free — *muteret lashuk* — without any requirement of *chalitza*. The logic of status quo is preserved, and the dangerous message that *chalitza* would send is avoided entirely.

## Fallen House Case

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: נָפַל הַבַּיִת עָלָיו וְעַל בַּת אָחִיו, וְאֵין יָדוּעַ אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם מֵת רִאשׁוֹן, צָרָתָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba by citing a case where even in places of doubt, the woman requires *ḥalitza*. As we learned in a mishna (67b): **A house fell on him,** on a certain man, **and on his brother's daughter** to whom this man was married, and he was childless, **and it is unknown which of them died first.** If the deceased wife had a rival wife, then **her rival wife** must **perform *ḥalitza* but may not enter into levirate marriage.** If the man had died first, then at the time of his death the rival wife was forbidden to the *yavam* as the rival wife of his daughter and exempt from levirate marriage. If, however, the daughter had died first, then at the time of the husband's death the second wife was not the rival wife of a forbidden relative, and requires levirate marriage. It is due to this doubt that she must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.

The *gemara* now introduces a challenge from Abaye based on the case of a fallen house. The scenario is this: Shimon is married to both Rachel and Devorah. Rachel is Reuven's daughter, making her an *ervah* to Reuven. Shimon and Rachel go together and a house falls upon them — *nafal habayit alav v'al bat achiv* — and it is unknown which of them died first. *V'ein yadua eizeh meihem meis rishon.* The *mishna* rules: *tzarata choletzet v'lo misyabemes* — Devorah performs *chalitza* and does not do *yibum* with Reuven.

The *safek* is this: if Rachel died first, then when Shimon subsequently died, only Devorah was his wife and Reuven could perform *yibum* normally. But if Shimon died first, then at the moment of his death both Rachel and Devorah were his wives, making Devorah a *tzarat ervah* to Reuven and forbidding *yibum* entirely. Since we do not know the order of death, the *mishna* requires *chalitza*.

### Devorah's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? הָכָא נָמֵי, נֵימָא: אִשָּׁה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת הֶיתֵּר לַשּׁוּק עוֹמֶדֶת, וּמִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְאוֹסְרָהּ — אַל תַּאַסְרֶנָּה מִסָּפֵק!
>
> **Translation:** And according to Rabba's opinion, **why** is that so? **Here too, let us say: This woman,** the rival wife, **has the presumptive status of** being **permitted** to marry a man **from the** general **public.** This is because she was exempt from levirate marriage for the entire period of her marriage as the rival wife of a forbidden relative. **And due to the uncertainty** whether her rival wife was the first to die **you come to render her forbidden** and require that she perform *ḥalitza*. **Do not render her forbidden due to an uncertainty.**

Abaye's challenge to Rava's status quo principle is immediate: before the house fell, what was Devorah's status quo? She was *muteret lashuk* — she was a *tzarat ervah* and therefore not subject to *yibum* at all, fully permitted to the outside world. That was her *chazakah*. *Umisafek ata ba l'osra* — why should the doubt created by the building collapse change that status? *Al tosrena misafek* — keep her at her status quo and let her go free without *chalitza*.

### Rare Event

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי לְחוּמְרָא — חוּמְרָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי קוּלָּא הוּא: שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת, מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת! גֵּירוּשִׁין דִּשְׁכִיחִי — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, מַפּוֹלוֹת דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Here too,** we rule **more stringently** due to the uncertainty. Nevertheless, **this would be a stringency that brings about a leniency, for if you say that she** must **perform *ḥalitza*, she may** also **enter into levirate marriage.** However, it is forbidden for her to enter into levirate marriage, because she is possibly forbidden to the *yavam* as the rival wife of his daughter and therefore forbidden just like the daughter herself. Rabba replied: In cases of **divorce, which are common, the Sages issued a rabbinic decree** preventing her from performing *ḥalitza* due to a concern that if she were required to perform *ḥalitza* then she may enter into levirate marriage as well. In cases of **collapse, which are not common, the Sages did not issue a rabbinic decree,** because they did not introduce decrees with regard to uncommon matters.

The *gemara* acknowledges the force of this logic but answers on the basis of frequency. In this case, requiring *chalitza* is indeed a *chumra*, but it is a *chumra* whose cost is negligible because the underlying event is extraordinarily rare. How often does a house fall on a husband and his relative such that the order of death is unknown? Since the scenario almost never occurs, even if demanding *chalitza* sends a potentially misleading message, the real-world cost of that message is minimal. The Rabbis had no difficulty being stringent in a case that barely ever arises. *Mapoles d'lo shechiachi lo gazru behu rabbanan* — for a case of a building collapse, which is uncommon, the Rabbis did not issue the same decree as for a more common situation.

### Common vs. Rare

Divorce, by contrast, is unfortunately common. Cases of *safek gerushin* — where the validity of a *get* is uncertain — arise with sufficient regularity that the Rabbis had to think carefully about what message demanding *chalitza* would send to the public at large. The rarity of the *mapoles* case and the frequency of divorce cases thus lead to opposite outcomes.

### Rabbinic Verification Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַבֵּי גֵירוּשִׁין מִי לָא תְּנַן? וְהָתְנַן: הָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ, קָרוֹב לָהּ — מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, קָרוֹב לוֹ — אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. וְאָמְרִינַן: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? דְּאִי כֹּהֵן הוּא — אֲסִורָה לֵיהּ, וְאִי עֶרְוָה הִיא — צָרָתָהּ בָּעֲיָא חֲלִיצָה. וְלָא אָמְרִינַן: שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת, מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did we not learn** in a mishna about the case where it is uncertain whether the bill of divorce is closer to him or closer to her **with regard to** situations of **divorce** whose status is uncertain? **And didn't we learn** in a mishna: In a case where his wife **was standing in the public domain and he threw her** the bill of divorce, if the bill landed **closer to her, she is divorced.** If it was **closer to him, she is not divorced.** If it was **half and half,** i.e., if the bill of divorce landed midway between the man and the woman, there is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced. And we say: With regard to what *halakha*** was the ruling said that she is both divorced and not divorced? The Gemara explains that this affects two areas of *halakha*. The first is **that if** the man divorcing his wife **is a priest,** then his wife **is forbidden to him** due to the uncertainty that she may in fact be divorced through that bill of divorce. Consequently, he would then be unable to remarry her. The second ramification is that **if** the woman being divorced **was a forbidden relative** to her husband's brother, and her husband died childless, then **her rival wife would require *ḥalitza*.** The mishna indicates that in this type of divorce whose status is uncertain as well, the Sages require the rival wife to perform *ḥalitza*, and **we do not say that if you say** that **she** must **perform *ḥalitza*,** she may **enter into levirate marriage.** Here there is no such concern.

The *gemara* makes this distinction explicit. In the case of *safek gerushin*, people understand that the Rabbis have the capacity and authority to investigate the validity of a *get*. If they see *chalitza* being performed in a case where Rachel is apparently still in the picture as a potential *ervah*, they will naturally assume that the Rabbis looked into it and determined that the *get* was in fact valid. *Kamu bah rabbanan b'gita* — they will conclude the Rabbis verified the divorce. And from that conclusion they will infer that *yibum* is permissible, when in truth Rachel remains married and Devorah is still a genuine *tzarat ervah*. The cost of demanding *chalitza* in the *safek gerushin* case is therefore real and substantial.

In the case of the fallen house, no such inference is available. Determining the order in which two people died when a building collapsed is not something people associate with Rabbinic investigation and verification. Nobody will assume the Rabbis conducted an inquiry into which death came first. *Mi kamu bei Rabbanan b'mapoles?* — the Rabbis are not understood as having the tools to determine that. Therefore, if *chalitza* is performed, people will accept that it is simply a precautionary measure under uncertainty, and no harmful inference will be drawn. The *chumra* of demanding *chalitza* in the *mapoles* case carries no risk of leading to the forbidden *yibum*.
