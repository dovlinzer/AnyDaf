# Yevamot 30b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Brothers & Zika

### Mishna Setup

The *mishna* under discussion presents a case involving three brothers: two are married to two sisters, and one is married to a third, unrelated woman. Concretely, Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah respectively, while Levi is married to Devorah.

### Sequence Analysis

The *mishna* then describes a sequence of events. First, Shimon divorces Leah — she is out of the picture from the very outset. Second, Levi dies, at which point either Reuven or Shimon could perform *yibum* with Devorah. Third, Shimon does in fact perform *yibum* with Devorah, bringing her into his household. Finally, Shimon dies.

The question is: what is Reuven's status vis-à-vis Devorah? Since Leah was already divorced before Levi died — before Devorah became relevant to the conversation at all — she was fully out of the picture by the time Shimon died. Devorah is therefore permitted to Reuven. This is the principle the *mishna* establishes: *zo hi she'amru, v'chulan shemetu o nisgareshu* — when the *ervah* has died or been divorced, her *tzarot* are permitted. In this case, since Leah was removed from the equation at the very first stage, Devorah is permitted to Reuven. The *mishna* deliberately chose an extreme case where Leah was out of the picture well before anything else occurred.

A more nuanced scenario — one the *mishna* does not directly address — would be if Shimon had first married Devorah and then divorced Leah afterward. The question in that scenario would be whether the period during which both women were simultaneously in Shimon's household created a permanent problem for Devorah vis-à-vis Reuven. But the *mishna*'s chosen case avoids that complication entirely.

### Yesh Zika Inference

The *gemara* now probes the *mishna*'s implicit logic. The *mishna* specifies that the reason the case works is *d'girash v'achar kach met* — Shimon first divorced Leah and only then did Levi die and *yibum* unfold. But what would have happened had Levi died first, creating a *zika* — a juridical connection — between Shimon and Devorah, before Leah was divorced? The *gemara* suggests that if Levi died first and Shimon thereby acquired a *zika* to Devorah, Devorah might immediately become a *tzarat ervah* relative to Reuven, even before any *yibum* had taken place. This would be problematic regardless of what followed.

Rav Ashi draws an explicit inference: *amar Rav Ashi, zos omeret, yesh zikah afilu b'trei achei* — from this *mishna* we can infer that *zika* applies even when there are two brothers. When Levi died, Reuven also had a claim on Devorah, not just Shimon. Even so, Shimon's *zika* is strong enough to render Devorah a quasi-*tzarat ervah*. This is a robust statement of *yesh zika*.

### Ein Zika Challenge

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: Rav Nechman held *ein zika afilu b'chad* — that *zika* is not a legally operative connection, even when there is only a single surviving brother. He derived this from an earlier *mishna* in which Reuven and Shimon are married to Rachel and Leah, and Levi is married to Devorah. That *mishna* stated that if Reuven dies and Levi subsequently performs *ma'amar* with Rachel and then dies, Shimon cannot perform *yibum* with Devorah because she has become a *tzarat ervah*. Rav Nechman's reading is that Rachel only became problematic because of the *ma'amar* — the *zika* alone, without *ma'amar*, was insufficient to pull Rachel into the equation. Yet the *mishna* before us seems to imply that even *zika* alone — without *ma'amar* — creates a problem with Devorah. How are these two *mishnayot* to be reconciled?

---

## Zika & Tzarat Erva

### Rav Ashi's Response

Rav Ashi responds to Rav Nechman's challenge directly. He concedes the point: even without *ma'amar*, the *zika* itself would have made Rachel a problem. *Af al gav d'lo avad bah ma'amar, nachis michlal chalitzah, yibumi lo miyabma* — even without *ma'amar*, Devorah would have required *chalitza* and *yibum* would have been forbidden.

### Ma'amar's Role

If so, why did the *mishna* specifically mention that Levi performed *ma'amar* with Rachel? The *ma'amar* did not create the problem — *zika* alone was sufficient to pull Rachel in and render Devorah a *tzarat ervah*. The *ma'amar* is mentioned not to establish the prohibition but to address a different question entirely.

### Beit Shammai Debate

The *mishna*'s mention of *ma'amar* is *l'apuki mi'Beit Shammai d'amri ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur* — to exclude the position of Beit Shammai, who hold that *ma'amar* constitutes a full acquisition, equivalent to *nisuin*. According to Beit Shammai, one might have thought that if Levi performed *ma'amar* with Rachel, Rachel was now fully his wife. In that case, when Shimon subsequently became the surviving brother, Rachel would be a complete *tzarat ervah* — not merely a quasi-*tzarat ervah* on the strength of *zika* — and Devorah could theoretically walk free without *chalitza*, since she is the *tzarah* of a genuine *ervah*. The *mishna* teaches that we do not rule like Beit Shammai: even with *ma'amar*, Rachel is not fully acquired, *chalitza* remains necessary, and *yibum* with Devorah is forbidden.

So the *mishna*'s structure is this: *zika* itself pulls Rachel in and creates the problem; *ma'amar* does not solve it by making Rachel a complete *nisuin*-level wife. Doing *chalitza* with Devorah is required, and doing *yibum* with Devorah is forbidden.

### Rav Nechman's Logic

Having explained Rav Ashi's reading, the *gemara* now turns the tables. *U'l'Rav Nachman kashya d'Rav Ashi* — according to Rav Nechman, who holds *ein zika* and maintained that Rachel was only a problem because of *ma'amar*, how does our *mishna* work? If *zika* is legally inert, why does the *mishna* appear to exclude cases where Levi dies before Leah is divorced, implying that the *zika* at that moment creates a problem?

Rav Nechman's answer, as the *gemara* reconstructs it, is that the *mishna* does not necessarily exclude that scenario. One might say: *hu hadin d'afilu metat ach v'achar kach gerush, tzarata muteret* — even if Levi died first and only then Leah was divorced, the case would still be permissible, since *ein zika* and the *zika* creates no legal problem. If so, what is the *mishna* actually excluding?

## Three Scenarios

### Scenario A

The simplest scenario — Scenario A — is precisely the one the *mishna* describes: Leah is divorced before Levi dies, before anything else unfolds. Leah is out of the picture from the very beginning. This is the easiest case and clearly presents no problem.

### Scenario B

Scenario B is that Levi dies first and then Leah is divorced. According to those who hold *yesh zika*, this might immediately create a problem at the moment Levi dies, since Shimon acquires a *zika* to Devorah while Leah is still in the picture. According to those who hold *ein zika*, however, Scenario B is just as permissible as Scenario A — the *zika* is meaningless, and as long as Leah is out of the picture before Shimon dies, there is no issue.

### Scenario C

Scenario C is more complex: Levi dies, Shimon performs *yibum* with Devorah and thereby marries her, and then — while married to both Devorah and Leah — he divorces Leah before he himself dies. In this scenario, there was a period when Shimon was simultaneously married to both women. Even though Leah was out of the picture before Shimon died, the question is whether that matters. This scenario is what the *gemara* designates as *kanas v'achar kach geireish* — he married Devorah and only afterward divorced Leah.

### Nisuin vs. Mita

This brings the *gemara* to a fundamental dispute about when *tzarat ervah* is determined. One opinion holds *nisuin rishonim mappilin* — the relevant moment is the time of the original marriage; the question is whether the two women were ever simultaneously married to the same man. Another opinion holds *mita mapelet* — everything is determined at the moment of death; if the *ervah* is out of the picture when the husband dies, there is no problem regardless of the marital history. The *gemara* notes that this dispute was articulated by earlier *amoraim*: one position is associated with Rabbi Yirmiyah's reading (*mi sheshana zu lo shana zu*, that the different *mishnayot* reflect different *tannaim*), and the other with Rava's position that it is all one *tanna* and everything goes by the moment of death.

---

## Rabbi Yirmiyah & Rava

### Rabbi Yirmiyah View

If one follows Rabbi Yirmiyah's approach — that *tzarat ervah* is determined by the time of marriage, i.e., *nisuin rishonim mappilin* — then the criterion is whether the two women were ever simultaneously married to the same man. Under this view, Scenario A and Scenario B are both permitted: in neither case was Shimon simultaneously married to both Leah and Devorah. Scenario C, however, is problematic, because Shimon was indeed married to both women at the same time, even though Leah was eventually divorced before he died.

### What Mishna Excludes

This gives the *gemara* a clear answer to the question of what the *mishna* is excluding. Since, under *ein zika*, Scenario B is permissible — the *zika* creates no problem — what remains to be excluded is Scenario C, the case of *kanas v'achar kach geireish*: Shimon married Devorah and only later divorced Leah. That case is forbidden because they were simultaneously married at some point, and *nisuin rishonim* determines the status. The *mishna*'s emphasis that Leah was divorced first is not merely incidental — it is precisely excluding the scenario in which Shimon was ever married to both women at once.

The *gemara* makes this explicit: *al kurchak k'Rabbi Yirmiyah s'vira lei* — if one holds *ein zika* and reads the *mishna* consistently, one is compelled to follow Rabbi Yirmiyah's view that the critical moment is the time of marriage, not the time of death. Only then is there a meaningful case to exclude.

### Rava's View

Rava, however, holds that everything goes by the moment of death — *mita mapelet*. Under Rava's view, any divorce before the husband's death is sufficient, regardless of the marital history. If Leah was divorced at any point before Shimon died, Devorah is permitted. This means Scenario A, Scenario B, and even Scenario C are all permissible, because in all three cases Leah was out of the picture at the moment of Shimon's death.

### Rava's Conclusion

If Rava holds that all three scenarios of divorce work, the *gemara* asks: *zu hi l'me'utei mai?* — what does the *mishna*'s formulation actually exclude? There must be some case that does not work when *geirushin* is in play. The *gemara* answers: *meis b'lo geireish* — the case being excluded is one where Levi died and Shimon acquired a *zika* to Devorah, and then Shimon himself died without ever divorcing Leah. In that situation, if one holds *yesh zika* as Rav Ashi does, the *zika* alone was sufficient to render Devorah a *tzarat ervah* at the moment of Levi's death, and since Leah was never divorced, the problem was never resolved.

This is a striking conclusion. The one case excluded under Rava's view is precisely the case where Shimon's connection to Devorah was at its weakest — a mere *zika*, without *yibum*, without *ma'amar*. Yet that weak *zika*, on the view of *yesh zika*, is enough to create a lasting problem. All scenarios in which an actual divorce occurred at any point before Shimon's death are fine under Rava; the only failure case is the *zika* without any subsequent divorce whatsoever.

The *gemara* then asks: *ella i savra leh k'Rav Nachman* — but if one holds like Rav Nechman that *ein zika*, then under Rava's framework there is nothing left to exclude, because every scenario of divorce before death works and the *zika* is meaningless. *Al kurchak k'Rav Ashi s'vira lei* — therefore Rava must hold like Rav Ashi, that *yesh zika*. Only then can Rava's framework exclude anything at all, and the excluded case is the *zika* without any divorce.

## Safek & Status Quo

### Safek Introduction

The *mishna* now turns to a different category of cases. Up to this point, the discussion concerned *erva* who was definitively married or definitively divorced, and the question was about the sequence and timing of those events. The *mishna* now addresses situations where the *erva*'s status is itself uncertain — she may be married, or she may not be. The two categories of uncertainty are *safek kiddushin* (uncertain betrothal) and *safek gerushin* (uncertain divorce).

### Safek Solution

In all such cases, the *mishna* rules: *tzarotehen choltzot v'lo misyabmot* — the co-wife performs *chalitza* and does not do *yibum*. She cannot perform *yibum* because perhaps the *erva* is in the picture and that would create a forbidden situation. But she cannot simply walk away either, because perhaps the *erva* is not in the picture and some form of resolution is required. The solution is *chalitza* without *yibum*.

### Safek Kiddushin

The *mishna* defines *safek kiddushin* with the following scenario: a man declares betrothal to a woman and throws the ring between them, and it is unclear whether it fell closer to his domain or to hers — *safek karov lo safek karov lah*. As Rashi explains, the relevant *kinyan* here is that of *arba amos* — within a person's four cubits, objects can be acquired. If the couple were standing eight cubits apart, with their respective *arba amos* meeting at the midpoint, and the ring landed precisely at that midpoint, it is genuinely unclear whether the woman's *kinyan* of *arba amos* was effected. That is *safek kiddushin* — we do not know if she was ever betrothed to him.

### Safek Gerushin

*Safek gerushin* is defined differently. Rather than a parallel scenario of a *get* that fell ambiguously between the two parties, the *mishna* instead presents three cases of a *get* that is valid *d'oraisa* but invalidated *m'd'rabbanan*, and which is therefore treated as a quasi-*gerushin* of uncertain status. The three cases are: a *get* written in the husband's own handwriting but lacking witnesses (*kasav b'ksav yado v'ein alav edim*); a *get* bearing witnesses but lacking a date (*yesh alav edim v'ein bo zman*); and a *get* with a date but bearing only one witness rather than two (*yesh bo zman v'ein bo ela ed echad*). The *mishna*'s phrasing is worth noting: the language flows from one case into the next, with each statement introducing an element that the next case then takes as its starting point — a mnemonic elegance in the oral formulation.

---

## Gerushin vs. Kiddushin

### Missing Case

The *gemara* observes an asymmetry. By *kiddushin*, the *mishna* gave the parallel case of a ring thrown between them — a genuine *safek* about whether the *kinyan* was effected. By *gerushin*, however, the *mishna* did not give the parallel case of a *get* thrown toward the woman, where it is unclear whether it entered her domain or remained in his. Why not? *V'ilu b'gerushin safek karov lo safek karov lah lo katani* — this case is simply not mentioned.

### Rabba's Status Quo

Rava explains the omission on the basis of *chazakah* — the principle of status quo. In the case of a *get* thrown ambiguously between them, the woman was definitively married before the act. We do not know if that status changed. Since her status quo is that she remains married, we do not treat her as a *safek gerusha* — she retains the *chazakah* of a married woman, and there is no *safek* to speak of. The *get* that may or may not have reached her does not displace that presumption.

### Kiddushin Parallel

One might apply the same logic in reverse to *safek kiddushin*: before the ring was thrown, the woman was definitively unmarried. Her status quo is that she is single. If we keep everyone at their status quo, she should simply remain unmarried, and no *safek kiddushin* should be recognized at all. If that is so, her co-wife Devorah's status quo — that she was permitted to do *yibum* with the *yavam* — should equally be preserved, and there should be no reason to require *chalitza*.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* presses this logic: *i hachi, b'kiddushin nami neima, isha zo b'chezkat heter l'yavam omedes* — apply the same status quo principle to Devorah in the *safek kiddushin* case. Before the questionable *kiddushin* took place, Devorah was permitted for *yibum*. *Umisafek ata ba u'osra* — why should a doubt change that? *Al tosrena misafek* — do not impose a prohibition on account of doubt. Let Devorah retain her status quo and allow *yibum* without restriction.

---

## Cost of Chumra

### Message Problem

The *gemara* responds: even though the status quo argument is valid in principle, in the case of *safek kiddushin* we are nonetheless stringent and require *chalitza* rather than permitting *yibum*. The concern is the message conveyed to the public. If Reuven declares his intent to perform *yibum* with Devorah and is told he may not, this prohibition sends a signal: people will infer that Rachel must in fact be married to Shimon, since that is the only reason *yibum* would be denied. If people conclude that Rachel is married to Shimon, they will draw consequences from that: any subsequent *kiddushin* that Rachel or someone close to her entered into will be viewed as having failed. *Ud'batar lav kiddushin* — those subsequent acts are actually valid, but the public message will lead people to treat them as void.

Specifically, the *gemara* identifies two problematic consequences: if Shimon subsequently performs a definite *kiddushin* to Leah (*kiddushei vadai l'achota*), people will assume it was ineffective because Rachel was already his wife; and if another man — let us say Barak — performs a definite *kiddushin* to Rachel herself after the ambiguous first *kiddushin*, people will assume that too was ineffective. Both of these real marriages will be undermined by the false inference that the first *safek kiddushin* was real.

### Chalitza Consistency

Here is the critical insight: the *gemara* notes that the relevant public signal is not the absence of *yibum* but the performance of *chalitza*. And *chalitza*, in the case of *safek kiddushin*, is entirely consistent with the true legal reality. What is the status quo? Rachel was not married to Shimon — that is the *chazakah*, since we go by the status quo of being unmarried. *Chalitza* with Devorah sends precisely that message: the reason *chalitza* was performed is that Rachel is not in the picture, that there was no operative *kiddushin*. People who see *chalitza* will understand that Rachel is out of the equation, and the subsequent *kiddushin* to Leah and to Rachel herself will be understood as valid.

This is what makes the *chumra* here cost-free. The *halacha* is that Rachel, by status quo, is not married to Shimon after a *safek kiddushin*. Demanding *chalitza* from Devorah conveys the accurate message. The only restriction is that Reuven is not permitted to do *yibum* — but the public does not see the absence of *yibum*; they see the performance of *chalitza*, which accurately reflects the underlying legal reality. *Meida yedaya d'chumra b'alma hu* — people will understand that this is simply a stringency. The *chumra* costs nothing because it sends no misleading signal. *V'kevan d'kamatba'ei chalitzah* — since *chalitza* is demanded, the right message is broadcast.

### Wrong Message

In the case of *safek gerushin*, however, the situation is reversed. Here Rachel was definitively married to Shimon, and the question is whether she was divorced. Her status quo is that she remains married. If Devorah were required to perform *chalitza*, people would ask: why is *chalitza* being done? Isn't Rachel still in the picture as a *tzarat ervah*? The answer they would draw is: the Rabbis must have investigated and determined that Rachel was in fact divorced — otherwise, why would *chalitza* be occurring at all? *Kamu bah rabbanan b'gita d'gita maalya hu* — they will assume that the Rabbis validated the *get* and established that Rachel is genuinely divorced.

### No Chalitza Demanded

That inference leads directly to harmful consequences. If people conclude that Rachel is divorced, they will then reason that *yibum* with Devorah is possible — since Rachel the *ervah* is out of the picture. Reuven or someone like him will come to perform *yibum* when Rachel is in fact not divorced and Devorah remains a genuine *tzarat ervah*. *V'asu l'yibumei l'tzarah* — this is a serious *issur*, potentially implicating *karet*. Performing *chalitza* in the case of *safek gerushin* is therefore not a no-cost *chumra* — it actively generates the most dangerous leniency imaginable.

Therefore, the *gemara* concludes, the Rabbis did not impose *chalitza* as a *chumra* in the case of *safek gerushin*. When the status quo says Rachel is married, Devorah retains her prior status of *tzarat ervah* and is simply permitted to go free — *muteret lashuk* — without any requirement of *chalitza*. The logic of status quo is preserved, and the dangerous message that *chalitza* would send is avoided entirely.

## Fallen House Case

### Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a challenge from Abaye based on the case of a fallen house. The scenario is this: Shimon is married to both Rachel and Devorah. Rachel is Reuven's daughter, making her an *ervah* to Reuven. Shimon and Rachel go together and a house falls upon them — *nafal habayit alav v'al bat achiv* — and it is unknown which of them died first. *V'ein yadua eizeh meihem meis rishon.* The *mishna* rules: *tzarata choletzet v'lo misyabemes* — Devorah performs *chalitza* and does not do *yibum* with Reuven.

The *safek* is this: if Rachel died first, then when Shimon subsequently died, only Devorah was his wife and Reuven could perform *yibum* normally. But if Shimon died first, then at the moment of his death both Rachel and Devorah were his wives, making Devorah a *tzarat ervah* to Reuven and forbidding *yibum* entirely. Since we do not know the order of death, the *mishna* requires *chalitza*.

### Devorah's Status

Abaye's challenge to Rava's status quo principle is immediate: before the house fell, what was Devorah's status quo? She was *muteret lashuk* — she was a *tzarat ervah* and therefore not subject to *yibum* at all, fully permitted to the outside world. That was her *chazakah*. *Umisafek ata ba l'osra* — why should the doubt created by the building collapse change that status? *Al tosrena misafek* — keep her at her status quo and let her go free without *chalitza*.

### Rare Event

The *gemara* acknowledges the force of this logic but answers on the basis of frequency. In this case, requiring *chalitza* is indeed a *chumra*, but it is a *chumra* whose cost is negligible because the underlying event is extraordinarily rare. How often does a house fall on a husband and his relative such that the order of death is unknown? Since the scenario almost never occurs, even if demanding *chalitza* sends a potentially misleading message, the real-world cost of that message is minimal. The Rabbis had no difficulty being stringent in a case that barely ever arises. *Mapoles d'lo shechiachi lo gazru behu rabbanan* — for a case of a building collapse, which is uncommon, the Rabbis did not issue the same decree as for a more common situation.

### Common vs. Rare

Divorce, by contrast, is unfortunately common. Cases of *safek gerushin* — where the validity of a *get* is uncertain — arise with sufficient regularity that the Rabbis had to think carefully about what message demanding *chalitza* would send to the public at large. The rarity of the *mapoles* case and the frequency of divorce cases thus lead to opposite outcomes.

### Rabbinic Verification Concern

The *gemara* makes this distinction explicit. In the case of *safek gerushin*, people understand that the Rabbis have the capacity and authority to investigate the validity of a *get*. If they see *chalitza* being performed in a case where Rachel is apparently still in the picture as a potential *ervah*, they will naturally assume that the Rabbis looked into it and determined that the *get* was in fact valid. *Kamu bah rabbanan b'gita* — they will conclude the Rabbis verified the divorce. And from that conclusion they will infer that *yibum* is permissible, when in truth Rachel remains married and Devorah is still a genuine *tzarat ervah*. The cost of demanding *chalitza* in the *safek gerushin* case is therefore real and substantial.

In the case of the fallen house, no such inference is available. Determining the order in which two people died when a building collapsed is not something people associate with Rabbinic investigation and verification. Nobody will assume the Rabbis conducted an inquiry into which death came first. *Mi kamu bei Rabbanan b'mapoles?* — the Rabbis are not understood as having the tools to determine that. Therefore, if *chalitza* is performed, people will accept that it is simply a precautionary measure under uncertainty, and no harmful inference will be drawn. The *chumra* of demanding *chalitza* in the *mapoles* case carries no risk of leading to the forbidden *yibum*.
