# Nazir 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish vs. R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּמֵי חַטָּאת מְעוֹרָבִין בָּהֶן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״לְכׇל נִדְרֵיהֶם וּלְכׇל נִדְבוֹתָם״, הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: מוֹתַר נֶדֶר יְהֵא לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But the money for the sin-offering is mingled with these** unallocated funds. How can it all be used for gift burnt-offerings? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is** an accepted ***halakha* with regard to a nazirite** that his unallocated funds are used for gift offerings, despite the fact that they include the value of a sin-offering. **Reish Lakish said:** The verse states: "Whether **for any of their vows or for any of their gift offerings,** which are brought to the Lord as a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 22:18). In this verse, **the Torah states** that **the surplus of a vow shall be for a gift offering.** Here too, if the vow of naziriteship cannot be fulfilled, due to the death of the one who took the vow, the money is for a gift offering.

The daf opens with a debate concerning a foundational halacha of *nazir*: when a *nazir* dies or a husband annuls his wife's vow of *nezirut*, and money has been sanctified and set aside for the *korbanot* without specification as to which *korban* each sum was designated for, the entire pile is assigned to an *olah*. The Gemara understands this case to involve a pile that would have contributed to all of the *nazir*'s *korbanot*, including a *chatat* — making the ruling a genuine *chiddush*. It is not as if the entire pile could have gone to an *olah* in the ordinary course of events; some portion of it was destined for a *chatat*. And yet, the halacha is that once the funds can no longer be used for their *nazir* purpose, the entire pile is directed toward an *olah*.

### Halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי סְתוּמִין — אִין, מְפוֹרָשִׁין — לָא. אֶלָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ ״לְכׇל נִדְרֵיהֶם וּלְכׇל נִדְבוֹתָם״, מַאי אִירְיָא סְתוּמִין? אֲפִילּוּ מְפוֹרָשִׁין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that **it is a *halakha* with regard to a nazirite,** it is **for this reason** that if the funds **were unallocated, yes,** the money is used for gift offerings, but if they were **allocated, no,** they may not be used for this purpose, as this is the *halakha* passed down by tradition. **However, according to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** who derives the *halakha* from the verse: **"For any of their vows or for any of their gift offerings," why** state **specifically unallocated** funds; **even allocated** money should be used for gift offerings after the death of their owner.

Rabbi Yochanan holds that this ruling is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — a tradition received from Sinai with no textual derivation. On his view, the halacha distinguishes between *stumim* (undesignated funds) and *meforashim* (designated funds): if the pile was non-designated, this special dispensation applies; if individual sums were explicitly designated — this pile for the *chatat*, this for the *olah*, this for the *shelamim* — then the halacha was not said, and each sum follows its own designation. Since it is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, no logical questions can be pressed against it. It operates as an arbitrary rule confined to its precise parameters, and one cannot ask why similar scenarios are treated differently.

### Reish Lakish's Pasuk

Reish Lakish, by contrast, derives this halacha from a *pasuk*: *le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvosem* — "for all their vows and for all their free-will offerings." He understands *nezirut* as something that begins as a *neder* (a vow), and leftover or unusable funds therefrom can be directed toward a *nedava*, which paradigmatically refers to an *olah* — the quintessential free-will offering that is entirely given over. The Gemara, however, presses Reish Lakish: if the derivation is from this *pasuk*, why should the halacha be limited to undesignated funds? Even if the money was explicitly designated, it originated from a *neder* and now becomes a *nedava* — so let it go to an *olah* regardless.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְפוֹרָשִׁין לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, כְּבָר פַּסְקַהּ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״רַק קׇדָשֶׁיךָ אֲשֶׁר יִהְיוּ לְךָ וּנְדָרֶיךָ״ — בְּוַלְדֵי קָדָשִׁים וּבִתְמוּרָתָם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: You cannot say** that this *halakha* should apply to **allocated** funds, as **the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael has already ruled** in **this** regard. The *tanna* explained the following verse that deals with the obligation to bring consecrated animals to the Temple: **"Only your sacred things that you have, and your vows"** (Deuteronomy 12:26). **The verse is speaking of the offspring of consecrated** female peace-offerings **and their substitutes,** i.e., when one says a non-sacred animal should substitute for a sacred animal.

Rava responds: designated funds cannot be repurposed. This principle is already established from the *braita* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael. Once funds carry a concrete designation, they remain within that category — just as a *tmura* or *vlad* (derivative sanctities) retain the identity of their parent *kedusha*. Accordingly, since we already know that anything with a designated *kedusha* cannot be redirected, the *pasuk* learned by Reish Lakish can only be speaking to a pile of undesignated funds. That is precisely where the *chiddush* lies: even though it was clear that *chatat* money was present in that pile, since it had not been formally designated, the *pasuk* permits the entire pile to be used as an *olah*.

## Vlad & Tmura

### Rak Kodashecha Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה תַּקָּנָתָן — ״תִּשָּׂא וּבָאתָ אֶל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִבְחַר ה׳״. יָכוֹל יַעֲלֵם לְבֵית הַבְּחִירָה, וְיִמְנַע מֵהֶם מַיִם וּמָזוֹן בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עֹלֹתֶיךָ הַבָּשָׂר וְהַדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these animals, **what is their** means of **remedy? "You shall take and go to the place that the Lord shall choose"** (Deuteronomy 12:26). One **might** have thought that this means that **he must bring them up to the Temple, and** yet he does not sacrifice them but **withholds water and food from them so that they should die.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall sacrifice your burnt-offerings, the flesh and the blood,** upon the altar of the Lord your God; and the blood of your offerings shall be poured out against the altar of the Lord your God, and you shall eat the flesh" (Deuteronomy 12:27).

The Gemara now turns to unpack the *braita* of Rabbi Yishmael, which grounds the broader principle about derivative sanctities. The *pasuk* reads: *rak kodashecha asher yihyu lecha u-nedarecha tisa u-vata el hamakom asher yivchar Hashem* — "Only your holy things that you possess, and your vows, shall you lift up and come to the place that God shall choose." The *pasuk* concludes: *ve-asita olotecha habasar vehadam al mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha vedam zevachecha yishapech al yesod mizbe'ach Hashem v'habasar tochel* — bring your *olot*, and the blood and flesh of your *shelamim* upon the altar, and eat the meat. Since there are ample other *pesukim* instructing one to bring *korbanot* to the *Beit Hamikdash*, this *pasuk* must be communicating something additional. The *midrash halacha* identifies that additional teaching: *bevladei kodashim u-vitmurasam hakatuv medaber* — the *pasuk* is speaking of the offspring of consecrated animals (*vlad*) and substituted animals (*tmura*), both of which represent derivative *kedusha*.

### Bringing Derivatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹמַר לָךְ: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹהֵג בָּעוֹלָה — נְהוֹג בִּתְמוּרָתָהּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלָמִים — נְהוֹג בְּוַלְדֵיהֶם. יָכוֹל אַף וְלַד חַטָּאת וּתְמוּרַת אָשָׁם כֵּן, — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״רַק״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* explains that this verse comes **to say to you: In the manner that you treat a burnt-offering you must treat its substitute,** i.e., it should be sacrificed in its entirety; **in the manner that you treat a peace-offering you must treat their offspring,** as its inner parts must be sacrificed on the altar, with the rest eaten by the owners. One **might** have thought that **the offspring of a sin-offering and the substitute of a guilt-offering** should **also** be treated **so,** i.e., they too should be sacrificed on the altar like sin-offerings and guilt-offerings. Therefore **the verse states: "Only"** (Deuteronomy 12:26), which excludes these cases. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

One might have supposed that such derivative sanctities — since they are not the original consecration — should be left to graze until they develop a blemish and are redeemed. The *pasuk* of *tisa u-vata* instructs that they be brought to the *Beit Hamikdash*. But perhaps the intent is merely to bring them there and withhold food and water until they die — *yimna mehem mayim u-mazon bi-shvil she-yamusu*. Therefore the *pasuk* continues: *ve-asita olotecha habasar vehadam* — you shall actually bring them as sacrifices. The principle derived is: *ke-derech she-ata noheg ba-olah noheg bi-tmurata* — treat the *tmura* of an *olah* as an *olah* is treated; and *ke-derech she-ata noheg bi-shlamim noheg bi-vladeihem* — treat the offspring of a *shelamim* as a *shelamim* is treated.

A subtle point underlies the pairing of examples. An *olah* must be male — *im olah korbano min ha-zachar* — and therefore cannot produce a *vlad*. Accordingly, the example of a derivative *olah* is a *tmura*, not a *vlad*. A *shelamim*, by contrast, may be female, so it can produce offspring; hence the example of a derivative *shelamim* is a *vlad*. The *pasuk* thus instructs that each type of derivative is brought in the manner appropriate to its original *kedusha*.

One might further ask: *yachol af vlad chatat u-tmurat asham kein* — perhaps the same applies to the offspring of a *chatat* or the *tmura* of an *asham*, and those too should be brought as their respective *korbanot*? To this, the *pasuk* responds with the word *rak* — "only" — which excludes the *chatat* and *asham*. This is the position of Rabbi Yishmael. The rationale is well-established: a *chatat* that cannot be brought for its original purpose — whether it is the offspring of a *chatat*, the *chatat* whose owner died, the *chatat* whose owner was already atoned for by another offering, the *chatat* that passed its year, or a *tmurat chatat* — must be let to die. This rule encompasses five cases, recalled by the mnemonic *vitmanah* (ו-ת-מ-נ-ע): *vlad chatat*, *tmurat chatat*, *metu ba'aleha*, *nitkapru be-acher*, and *avrah shnatah*. For all five, the animal is starved until death, and this is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אָשָׁם הוּא״ — בַּהֲוָיָיתוֹ יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** In the case of a guilt-offering this exposition is **not necessary, as it says: "It is a guilt-offering"** (Leviticus 5:19), from which it may be inferred: **It** alone **shall be** sacrificed **in its** current **state,** but not its substitute. This *baraita* explicitly teaches that the surplus money of a sin-offering cannot be used for another offering. Consequently, the money set aside for a nazirite's sin-offering must be cast into the Dead Sea upon the death of its owner rather than used for a gift offering, even according to the opinion of Reish Lakish.

Rabbi Akiva holds that one need not derive the exclusion of the *chatat* and *asham* from the word *rak*. Rather, a separate *pasuk* states *asham hu* — "it is a guilt-offering" — from which the Gemara derives *ba-havayato yehei*: the *asham* retains its identity and cannot be redirected to serve any other function. Implicitly, the same applies to the *chatat*.

The distinction between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is not immediately transparent, since both are learning from ancillary textual indicators. Rabbi Akiva's preference for *asham hu* over *rak* may stem from the fact that *rak* is a generic exclusion whose precise scope is uncertain, whereas *asham hu* speaks specifically to the *asham* and communicates directly that its identity is fixed and cannot be overridden.

### Why Learn from Pasuk?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֵם לְבֵית הַבְּחִירָה וְיִמְנַע מֵהֶם מַיִם וּמָזוֹן בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עֹלֹתֶיךָ״. אַמַּאי? הָא וְלַד חַטָּאת הוּא דִּגְמִירִין בָּהּ מִיתָה? אִי לָאו קְרָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא וְלַד חַטָּאת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם,
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara has cited the above *baraita*, it analyzes it in detail. The *baraita* **teaches:** One **might** have thought that **one must bring them up to the Temple and withhold water and food from them so that they should die. The verse states: "And you shall sacrifice your burnt-offerings."** The Gemara asks: **Why** would one think he should act in this manner? After all, **it is** only with regard to **the offspring of a sin-offering that we learned** that it must be left to **die,** but not other offerings. Why, then, is it necessary to derive the exclusion of the offspring of other offerings and the substitution of other offerings from a verse? The Gemara answers: **If** it were **not** for the **verse, I would say** that **the offspring of a sin-offering** may be killed **anywhere,** i.e., it need not be brought to the Temple and left to die there,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּוַלְדֵי קָדָשִׁים בְּבֵית הַבְּחִירָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **but the offspring of** other **sacred** animals, for peace-offerings, must be brought **to the Temple** and left to die there. The verse therefore **teaches us that** one does **not** leave the offspring of peace-offerings to die. Rather, they are sacrificed upon the altar.

This leads to a pressing question that the Gemara raises at multiple junctures: *lama li kra, hilchata g'miri lah* — why do we need a *pasuk* at all? We already have the *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* that the five *chatot* die and that whatever would require a *chatat* to die by an *asham* instead requires it to graze (*kol she-be-chatat metah be-asham ro'eh*). Why, then, does the *braita* appear to be deriving these very rulings from *pesukim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: יָכוֹל אַף וְלַד חַטָּאת וּתְמוּרַת אָשָׁם כֵּן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״רַק״. לְמָה לִי קְרָא? הִילְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ: וְלַד חַטָּאת לְמִיתָה אָזֵיל! הָכִי נָמֵי, וּקְרָא לְאָשָׁם הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the *baraita*. The *baraita* **teaches:** One **might** have thought that **the offspring of a sin-offering and the substitute of a guilt-offering** should **also** be treated **so,** i.e., they too should be sacrificed on the altar like sin-offerings and guilt-offerings. Therefore, **the verse states: "Only"** (Deuteronomy 12:26). The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a verse** for this *halakha*? After all, **this *halakha* is learned** as a tradition that **the offspring of a sin-offering goes to** its **death.** The Gemara answers: **So too,** this is correct as far as the offspring of a sin-offering is concerned, **and the verse comes** to teach that this *halakha* applies **to a guilt-offering** as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם נָמֵי הִילְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לָהּ: כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ בְּחַטָּאת מֵתָה, בְּאָשָׁם רוֹעֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a guilt-offering as well, this *halakha* is learned** as a tradition: **Any** situation **that if it were** to occur **with a sin-offering** it would be left to **die,** i.e., any offering that is invalid in such a manner that it would have to be left to die if it were a sin-offering, which includes a substitute, if **it** occurs **with a guilt-offering it** is left to **graze** until it develops a blemish so that it is no longer fit for the altar. Why, then, is a verse required to teach this *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אִי מֵהִילְכְתָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הִילְכְתָא, וְאִי אַקְרְיבֵיהּ — לָא לִיחַיַּיב עֲלֵיהּ וְלֹא כְּלוּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן קְרָא דְּאִי מַקְרֵיב לֵיהּ — קָאֵים עֲלֵיהּ בַּעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the argument is as follows: **If** one would have learned this ruling only **from the** aforementioned ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **I would say:** It is admittedly **the *halakha*** that the offspring of a sin-offering must die and the substitute of a guilt-offering is left to graze, **but if he sacrificed it** regardless, **he would not be liable to** receive **any** punishment **for it. The verse** therefore **teaches us that if he sacrifices** the offspring or substitute of a guilt-offering, **he transgresses a positive mitzva by it,** as derived from the verse: "Only your sacred things which you have, and your vows, you shall take and go to the place which the Lord shall choose" (Deuteronomy 12:26).

The Gemara's resolution differs for Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. For Rabbi Yishmael, the *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* establishes that the animal cannot be validly brought (*lo kasher*), but the *pasuk* of *rak kodashecha* adds a further dimension: if one were to bring such a *chatat* in these circumstances, one would violate a *lav ha-ba mi-klal aseh* — a prohibition implicit in a positive command. The positive command *ve-asita olotecha* covers only *olot* and *shelamim* in their derivative forms, implicitly excluding the *chatat*. Bringing the *chatat* in this state thus constitutes a transgression of an *aseh*. The *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* tells us the act is invalid; the *pasuk* tells us it is additionally a transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אָשָׁם הוּא״ — בַּהֲוָיָיתוֹ יְהֵא. לְמָה לִי קְרָא? גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִין לַהּ: כׇּל שֶׁבְּחַטָּאת מֵתָה — בְּאָשָׁם רוֹעֶה!
>
> **Translation:** § The same *baraita* teaches that **Rabbi Akiva says:** In the case of a guilt-offering this exposition is **not necessary, as it says: "It is a guilt-offering"** (Leviticus 5:19), which indicates: **It** alone **shall be** sacrificed **in its** current **state,** but not its substitute. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a verse** for this *halakha*? **We learned it** as a ***halakha*: Any** situation **that if it were** to occur **with a sin-offering** it would be left to **die,** if **it** occurs **with a guilt-offering it** is left to **graze.** If so, it is clear that this guilt-offering cannot be sacrificed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי, וְכִי אֲתָא קְרָא — לִדְרַב. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּיה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **So too,** this is correct, **and when the verse comes** to teach a *halakha*, it comes **for** that which was said **by Rav. As Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **a guilt-offering that was consigned to grazing,** i.e., it had been ruled that the animal must be left to graze until it develops a blemish as it could not be sacrificed as a guilt-offering, at which point it was sold so that the proceeds could be used for voluntary burnt-offerings, **and** one transgressed and **slaughtered** the guilt-offering itself **for the sake of a burnt-offering** rather than using an animal purchased with the money of its sale, **it is valid.**

For Rabbi Akiva, who does not connect the matter to the *pasuk* of *rak kodashecha*, this resolution is unavailable. The Gemara therefore says the *pasuk* of *asham hu* comes to teach the ruling articulated by Rav Huna in the name of Rav — a ruling about the *asham*'s capacity for transformation, discussed in the following section.

The Gemara notes, moreover, the broader methodological point that this entire *midrash halacha* presupposes the *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. Tosafot observes that the plain sense of the *braita* suggests Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva are both deriving the basic rule about the *chatat* dying — but the Gemara refuses to read it that way. Since it is committed to the view that this halacha is already known from *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, it assigns each *tanna* a distinct teaching derived from the *pasuk*, even at the cost of departing from the surface reading of the *braita*. Tosafot candidly acknowledges this tension.

## Hava Amina: Olah/Shelamim

### Hava Amina Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֵם לְבֵית הַבְּחִירָה וְיִמְנַע מֵהֶם מַיִם וּמָזוֹן בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עֹלֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara has cited the above *baraita*, it analyzes it in detail. The *baraita* **teaches:** One **might** have thought that **one must bring them up to the Temple and withhold water and food from them so that they should die. The verse states: "And you shall sacrifice your burnt-offerings."**

Before reaching the question of why the *pasuk* is needed, the Gemara addresses the *hava amina* — the initial assumption — that the *pasuk* must overcome in the first place. The initial assumption is not that one would starve derivative *olot* and *shelamim* everywhere; rather, it is something more peculiar: *yachol ya'aleh le-veit habechira ve-yimna mehem mayim u-mazon bi-shvil she-yamusu* — one might have brought them up to the *Beit Hamikdash* and let them die there.

This *hava amina* arises from the structure of the *pasuk* itself. The first part, *tisa u-vata*, instructs bringing these animals to the *Beit Hamikdash*. One might read this as: bring them there, and then — since they are not eligible to be brought as original consecrations — leave them there to die. The *pasuk* then adds *ve-asita olotecha habasar vehadam*, teaching that they must actually be offered as sacrifices. The *hava amina* is therefore a perfectly logical reading of the sequence of the *pasuk*, and the conclusion (*ka mashma lan d'lo*) dispels it.

### Bring as Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹמַר לָךְ: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹהֵג בָּעוֹלָה — נְהוֹג בִּתְמוּרָתָהּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹהֵג בַּשְּׁלָמִים — נְהוֹג בְּוַלְדֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* explains that this verse comes **to say to you: In the manner that you treat a burnt-offering you must treat its substitute,** i.e., it should be sacrificed in its entirety; **in the manner that you treat a peace-offering you must treat their offspring,** as its inner parts must be sacrificed on the altar, with the rest eaten by the owners.

The *pasuk* thus teaches: bring derivative *olot* and *shelamim* — whether *tmura* or *vlad* — and offer them as the *korban* that their original *kedusha* represents. The derivative *olah* is brought as an *olah*; the derivative *shelamim* is brought as a *shelamim*. The word *rak* then comes to limit this dispensation.

### Chatat Must Die

The word *rak* excludes the *vlad chatat* and *tmurat asham* from this permission. The *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* of the five *chatot* that die is well established, and the Gemara is resolute that it not be displaced by a *pasuk*-derived rule. This is why, for Rabbi Yishmael, the *pasuk*'s purpose is re-understood: rather than establishing that these animals cannot be brought (which is known from *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*), the *pasuk* establishes the additional *issur aseh* one incurs by bringing them improperly. For Rabbi Akiva, the *pasuk* of *asham hu* teaches the more nuanced halacha of Rav Huna — that the *asham*'s identity can transform, but only once it has been formally handed over to pasture.

## Asham's Identity Transformation

### Asham Given to Pasture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּיה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה — כָּשֵׁר. טַעְמָא דְּנִיתַּק, הָא לֹא נִיתַּק — לָא. דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״הוּא״ — בַּהֲוָיָיתוֹ יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **So too,** this is correct, **and when the verse comes** to teach a *halakha*, it comes **for** that which was said **by Rav. As Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **a guilt-offering that was consigned to grazing,** i.e., it had been ruled that the animal must be left to graze until it develops a blemish as it could not be sacrificed as a guilt-offering, at which point it was sold so that the proceeds could be used for voluntary burnt-offerings, **and** one transgressed and **slaughtered** the guilt-offering itself **for the sake of a burnt-offering** rather than using an animal purchased with the money of its sale, **it is valid.** The Gemara infers from Rav's statement: **The reason** for this ruling is **that** it is a guilt-offering that was **consigned** to grazing, from which it may be inferred that **if** it were **not consigned** in this manner it is **not** valid as a burnt-offering. This is because **the verse states: "It** is a guilt-offering," which indicates that **it shall be in its** current **state,** and if it were sacrificed as a different offering it is invalid.

The Gemara resolves the question of why Rabbi Akiva needs the *pasuk* of *asham hu* by invoking the teaching of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *asham she-nitak le-re'iya u-shechato le-shem ola kasher* — an *asham* that has been handed over to graze, if one then *shechts* it with the intent that it be an *olah*, it is valid as an *olah*. Once the *asham* has been set into the process of grazing — the process by which it will eventually develop a blemish, be redeemed, and have its funds applied to an *olah* — its *kedusha* has already functionally transformed. Even though the animal itself has not yet been redeemed, the act of handing it over to the shepherd initiates a shift in its identity such that a *shechita* performed *le-shem olah* at that point is effective.

This halacha applies specifically to an *asham* that cannot be used for its original purpose — a *tmurat asham*, an *asham* whose owner has died, or whose owner has already been atoned for by another offering. For such an *asham*, the *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* prescribes that it graze until it develops a blemish, at which point the animal is redeemed for its monetary value and that money is brought as an *olah*. The transformation is not of the money to another *asham* or *chatat* — since the original purpose can no longer be served — but specifically to an *olah*. The *pasuk* of *asham hu* comes to teach, according to Rabbi Akiva, that this process of identity transformation is real and operative — and that it is only once the *asham* is handed over to the shepherd that its *kedusha* begins to shift.

### Transformation Conditions

The condition is precise: *taima d'nitak* — the reason for the transformation is that the *asham* was actually handed over for pasture. *Ha lo nitak lo* — if it was not yet handed over, even if the owner knows full well that this *asham* can no longer serve its original purpose, the animal's identity has not yet shifted. *D'amar kra hu, asham hu, ba-havayato yehei* — the *pasuk* affirms that it retains its status as an *asham* unless and until something actually initiates the transformative process.

There is a significant Tosafot on this sugya, since Rabbeinu Tam takes the position that the requirement of handing over to pasture is only *derabbanan*, not *de'oraisa*. On his reading, what truly shifts the animal's identity at the *de'oraisa* level is the *kavana* at the time of *shechita*: if one *shechts* it *le-shem olah*, it is valid as an *olah*, even without having first handed it to a shepherd; if one *shechts* it *le-shem asham*, it does not work. The plain sense of the Gemara, however, appears to establish the handing-over-to-pasture as the operative *de'oraisa* criterion, and Rabbeinu Tam's reinterpretation departs significantly from that reading in order to reconcile it with another *sugya* that seems to undercut the *de'oraisa* status of the pasturing requirement.

## Beyond Nazir

### Single Obligation, Multiple Korbanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר. וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: וּשְׁאָר חַיָּיבֵי קִינִּין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of a nazirite's offerings: **The Master,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **said** earlier: **It is a *halakha* with regard to a nazirite** that his unallocated funds, including the value of his sin-offering, are used for the purchase of gift offerings. The Gemara asks: This formulation indicates that this *halakha* pertains only to a nazirite, **but is there no other** case where the surplus goes toward the purchase of gift offerings? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **And the rest of those liable to** bring **nests by Torah law,** i.e., those paupers who are obligated to bring only a bird-offering, e.g., a poor leper, who must bring two turtledoves, one for a burnt-offering and one for a sin-offering, instead of a sheep or a ewe,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהִפְרִישׁוּ מָעוֹת לְקִינֵּיהֶם, רָצָה לְהָבִיא בָּהֶן חַטַּאת בְּהֵמָה — יָבִיא, עוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה — יָבִיא. מֵת וְהָיוּ לוֹ מָעוֹת סְתוּמִין — יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה!
>
> **Translation:** **who separated money for their nests** and then became wealthy, if the owner **wishes** to change their designation and **to bring an animal sin-offering with them, he may bring** a sin-offering with them. If he wishes to use them to buy **an animal burnt-offering he may bring** it, supplementing the required amount with other money. If the owner **died and he had unallocated funds,** they all **will be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings, including the value of the sin-offering. This shows that the *halakha* that unallocated funds are used for gift offerings applies in cases other than that of a nazirite.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yochanan's formulation, which described the rule as a *halacha* specifically in *nazir*. The Gemara asks: is this truly unique to *nazir*? Surely the same halacha applies in other analogous cases. The Gemara answers: *tana nazir ve-chayvei kinim d'damu lei* — the *tanna* taught *nazir* because it is the paradigm, but the halacha extends to all cases that resemble *nazir* — namely, any situation in which a single obligation generates multiple *korbanot*.

### Yoledes Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא נָזִיר וְחַיָּיבֵי קִינִּין דְּדָמוּ לֵיהּ, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: מִי שֶׁהָיָה מְחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה״, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לְחוֹבָתִי״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He taught** the case of **a nazirite and** also the case of **those obligated to** bring **nests, which is similar to** that of a nazirite and is therefore treated identically with regard to its *halakha*. This serves **to exclude that** case **which is taught** in a *baraita*. The situation discussed in the *baraita* involves **one who was obligated to** bring **a sin-offering** for a transgression he committed, **and he** also **said: It is** incumbent **upon me** to bring a gift **burnt-offering, and he separated money and said: These are hereby for my obligatory** offering. Since he might have meant either his obligation of the sin-offering or his burnt-offering for the new vow, the question arises as to what should be done with the money.

A *braita* establishes the parallel case: those obligated to bring *kinim* (pairs of bird-offerings) — of whom the *yoledes* is a classic example — who set aside money for their birds, and subsequently come into greater wealth such that they are now obligated to bring animal sacrifices rather than birds. Since their pile was set aside for an open-ended *chovah* (obligation), without specifying how much goes to the *chatat*-bird and how much to the *olah*-bird, the same principles apply: *rotzeh le-havi bahen chatat behemah yavi, olat behemah yavi* — one may direct the whole pile toward the animal *chatat* or toward the animal *olah*.

Tosafot engages carefully with the language used in setting aside such a pile. If one says "this is for my *korbanot*," Tosafot understands this to mean that both *korbanot* must come from this pile, even though it remains unspecified which sum is for which. If one says merely "this is for my obligation," the language is open-ended enough that the whole pile could go toward a single *korban*. This distinction matters because the *chiddush* of the halacha — that after the person's death the pile can go entirely to an *olah* — is greatest when the pile was understood to contain both *chatat* and *olah* money, yet the halacha redirects the whole to an *olah*. If the pile was open-ended enough that even while the person lived it could be assigned entirely to one *korban*, there is less novelty in the post-death ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָצָה לְהָבִיא בָּהֶן חַטַּאת בְּהֵמָה — לֹא יָבִיא. עוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה — לֹא יָבִיא. מֵת וְהָיוּ לוֹ מָעוֹת סְתוּמִים — יֵלְכוּ לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains that if he **wishes to bring an animal sin-offering with it, he may not bring** one; if he wishes to use it to purchase **an animal burnt-offering, he may not bring** it either. If he **died and had unallocated funds,** one must **take** them and cast them into **the Dead Sea.** Since the two offerings are not part of the same obligation, the unallocated funds may not be used for gift offerings.

In the *yoledes* case: if she dies and the pile is *stumim* (unspecified), *yiplu le-nedavah* — it falls to an *olah*. This is precisely the same ruling as in *nazir*, confirming that the halacha is not limited to *nazir* but extends to any single obligation that generates multiple *korbanot*.

### Two Independent Obligations

The contrast case is introduced to delineate the scope of this principle precisely. The *braita* describes a person who is obligated in a *chatat* (arising from one cause) and who separately accepts upon himself an obligation of an *olah* (a completely unrelated *neder*). He sets aside money and says *harei eilu le-chovotai* — this is for my obligations. Since he has two entirely independent obligations, the pile contains both *chatat* money and *olah* money: *rotzeh le-havi bahen chatat behemah lo yavi, olat behemah lo yavi* — he may not use the entire pile for either the animal *chatat* or the animal *olah* alone. Each obligation's funds must be distinguished and directed appropriately.

When such a person dies with this unspecified pile, *yelchu le-yam ha-melach* — the money must be thrown into the Dead Sea. The reason is that this is not a case of one obligation generating multiple *korbanot*; it is two separate, independent obligations that happen to share a single pool of funds. Since *chatat* money is present and cannot be repurposed, and since the *chatat* money cannot be separated out with precision, the entire pile is lost. One cannot extract the *olah* money from the mixture because no individual coin can be identified as specifically belonging to one obligation or the other. The sanctification of a coin is concrete — it is not merely a symbolic representation of general monetary value that can be reassigned — and thus the intermingling of *chatat* funds with *olah* funds, where the former cannot be redirected, disqualifies the entire pile from any further use.

This stands in contrast to the *nazir* or *yoledes* case, where the single origin of the obligation means that even a pile containing identifiable *chatat* and *olah* funds can, upon the person's death, be directed entirely toward an *olah* through the special halacha derived either from *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* or from the *pasuk* of *le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvosem*.

## Unspecified vs. Designated Funds

### Rav Ashi's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ מְפוֹרָשִׁין לָא — לָא תֵּימָא דְּאָמַר ״אֵלּוּ לְחַטָּאתִי, וְאֵלּוּ לְעוֹלָתִי, וְאֵלּוּ לִשְׁלָמַי״. אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״אֵלּוּ לְחַטָּאתִי וּלְעוֹלָתִי וְלִשְׁלָמַי״ — מְפוֹרָשִׁין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ashi said: That** which **you said** with regard to a nazirite who had **allocated** money, that he may **not** use it all for gift offerings because the value of the sin-offering must be taken and cast into the Dead Sea, **do not say that** this is referring only to a case where he explicitly **said: These are for my sin-offering, and these are for my burnt-offering, and these are for my peace-offering,** each one separately. **Rather, even** if **he said: These are for my sin-offering and for my burnt-offering and for my peace-offering, they are** considered **allocated** for the purposes of this *halakha*, despite the fact that he did not designate the money for particular offerings.

Rav Ashi clarifies the precise meaning of *meforashim* — explicitly designated — which serves as the cutoff beyond which the special halacha of redirecting funds to an *olah* no longer applies. His teaching refines what "unspecified" must mean in order for the halacha to operate.

### Naming Korbanos

One might assume that "designated" refers only to the clearest case: where the *nazir* said explicitly *elu le-chatasi ve-elu le-olasi ve-elu le-shalmi* — "these coins are for my *chatat*, these for my *olah*, these for my *shelamim*." Rav Ashi teaches that this is not the only case. Even if the person said *elu le-chatasi u-le-olasi u-le-shalmi* — "this pile is for my *chatat*, my *olah*, and my *shelamim*" — without further specifying which sum is for which, this is already considered *meforashim*. The explicit naming of the *chatat* as one of the intended recipients of this pile sufficiently concretizes the *chatat*'s identity within that pile, and therefore the pile can no longer be redirected to an *olah* after the *nazirut* is annulled or the *nazir* dies.

### Obligation vs. Korbanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא תֵּימָא עַד דְּאָמַר ״אֵלּוּ לְחַטָּאתִי וּלְעוֹלָתִי וְלִשְׁלָמַי״, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר: ״אֵלּוּ לְחוֹבָתִי״ — הֲרֵי הֵן כִּמְפוֹרָשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** a different version of this statement. **Rav Ashi said: Do not say** they are deemed allocated **only if he says: These are for my sin-offering and for my burnt-offering and for my peace-offering; rather, even if he said** it in broader terms: **These are for my obligation, they are** considered **as allocated.**

A second formulation attributed to Rav Ashi extends the principle further: *lo teima ad d'amar elu le-chatasi u-le-olasi u-le-shalmi* — do not say that only when one names the specific *korbanot* is it considered designated. Rather, *afilu amar elu le-chovati harei hein ke-meforashim* — even if one says merely "this pile is for my obligation," that too is treated as designated, and the *chatat* money it contains cannot be redirected.

This leads to a subtle question: if even saying "for my obligation" is considered sufficiently explicit to constitute *meforashim*, what formulation remains genuinely *stumim* and qualifies for the special halacha? Tosafot wrestles with this directly. He argues that saying *le-korbanot neziruti* — "for my *nazir*-offerings" — is marginally less concrete than *le-chovati*, because while both imply that all the *korbanot* are to come from the pile, the formulation *le-korbanot neziruti* does not explicitly invoke a *chatat* by name or category. Nevertheless, Tosafot acknowledges that even this formulation refers to all the *korbanot*, and therefore he is not entirely satisfied that this represents a true functional difference. The Gemara's *chiddush* — that a *stumim* pile containing *chatat* funds can go entirely to an *olah* — thus operates in an increasingly narrow band of cases, and the exact dividing line between *stumim* and *meforashim* remains genuinely difficult to pin down.

## Separating Chatat Money

### Pile Becomes Concrete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דַּאֲמַרַן מָעוֹת סְתוּמִין יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה, אִם נָפְלָה דְּמֵי חַטָּאת מִבֵּינֵיהֶן — הֲרֵי הֵן כִּמְפוֹרָשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said: That** which **we said,** that if one had **unallocated funds** they **will be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings, applies only if the money for all of the offerings was mixed together. However, **if the money for the sin-offering fell** and was separated **from** the others, all the remaining money **is** now considered **as allocated.** This means that instead of the entire sum being used for a gift burnt-offering, part of it is used for a peace-offering, which is eaten for one day and does not require bread.

Rava teaches a further principle that extends the concretization logic in a striking direction: *im hifrish d'mei chatat mibeineihem harei hen ke-meforashim* — if one separates out the *chatat* funds from an otherwise unspecified pile, then even the remaining funds — which consist only of *olah* and *shelamim* money, with no *chatat* money remaining — are treated as though the entire pile has become designated.

The intuition one might bring to this case would be exactly the opposite: now that the *chatat* money has been cleanly removed, there is no longer any admixture of *chatat* funds. Surely the remaining pile should now qualify for the special halacha and go entirely to an *olah*? Rava rules otherwise. The act of separating out the *chatat* funds draws the entire pile out of the indeterminate, amorphous state in which the special halacha operates. Once something about the pile has been made concrete and real — even if it is only the *chatat* portion being segregated — the pile as a whole crosses from that indeterminate state into a more determinate one. Its identity has begun to solidify, and the special mechanism by which an unspecified pile is redirected to an *olah* is no longer available.

### Must Divide Remaining Money

The practical consequence is that after the separation, the remaining *olah* and *shelamim* funds must be divided between an *olah* and a *shelamim* in the appropriate proportions; the entire remainder cannot simply be assigned to an *olah*. The pile has, in Rava's framing, entered the real world of determinate identity. Just as the Gemara's broader analysis draws on the image of sanctified funds as concretely attached to specific purposes — each coin, once designated, is not merely symbolic monetary value but carries a fixed *kedusha* — so too the act of beginning to apportion that pile triggers a kind of irreversible concretization. From that point forward, the remaining funds must be treated according to their actual proportional designations, and the special halacha of *stumim* no longer applies.
