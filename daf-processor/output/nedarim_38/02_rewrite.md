# Nedarim 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Torah Teaching & Fees

### Torah Teaching Rules

The daf begins at the bottom of 37b, in the middle of a *mishnah* discussing the laws of a *mudar hana'ah*: a situation in which Reuven is prohibited from deriving *hana'ah* from Shimon. The *mishnah* establishes that Shimon may nonetheless teach Reuven *Torah*, because *mitzvos lav lehenos nitnu* — the commandments were not given for physical or monetary pleasure, and imparting Torah knowledge is therefore not technically classified as *hana'ah*. One exception, however, is teaching *Torah she-bichtav*. The reason offered is that teaching written Torah — particularly to children — typically carries a fee. One cannot charge for teaching *Torah she-be'al peh*, but for *Torah she-bichtav* a fee is permitted, either on account of the babysitting function involved in teaching children, or because the instruction is assumed to include *pisuk te'amim* — the *trop* — which, while necessary for understanding the *pesukim*, is not considered *Torah* proper. It is somewhat analogous to biblical grammar: a subject one might classify under *limudei kodesh* or *limudei chol* depending on one's framework. Accordingly, if Shimon teaches Reuven *Torah she-bichtav* without charge, he is effectively waiving the fee he could have collected, thereby conferring a monetary benefit — which would be prohibited under the *neder*.

### Women's Torah Study

A striking corollary emerges from the *mishnah*'s ruling that Shimon may teach Reuven's sons and daughters, even though this saves Reuven the cost of hiring a teacher elsewhere. This is permitted because the benefit reaches Reuven only indirectly — analogous to paying off a creditor — and is not something given directly to him.

More significant, however, is what this ruling implies about the Torah education of daughters. The fact that daughters are included in this permission indicates that women were in fact taught *Torah she-bichtav*, and according to this reading of the *mishnah*, they were taught the *trop* as well. This need not be because they were expected to perform a ritual reading in the *beis knesses* — although a *braisa* does state in theory that women may receive *aliyos*, the practice being set aside only on account of *kevod hatzibur* — but at minimum they were being taught to grammatically parse the verse through its punctuation. That women received such instruction is, as noted in the course of discussion, a genuinely fascinating datum.

### Mikra & Pisuk Te'amim

The *Gemara* now turns to the question of whether *pisuk te'amim* — the *trop* — has the status of *Torah* itself, that is, whether it is *de'oraisa*. The term *de'oraisa* can be understood either in the historical sense of going back to Har Sinai, or in the definitional sense of whether it counts as *Torah* proper for purposes of obligations such as *birchas haTorah* or the restrictions on where one may study. This same question arises regarding *Targum*, which some hold also traces back to Sinai. The practical consequence here is that according to the view permitting a fee for teaching *pisuk te'amim*, such instruction is not considered *Torah* per se; whereas the opposing view would prohibit charging, treating it as fully Torah. Both positions remain in play.

## Torah Teaching & Fees…

### Sofrim Traditions

The *Gemara* continues with a statement of Rabbi Yitzchak. It draws on the verse in Nechemia, *vayikre'u basefer toras elokim meforash vesum sechel vayavinu bamikra* — "they understood in the *mikra*" — which the *Gemara* had earlier applied to *pisuk te'amim* or the *masoret*. Rabbi Yitzchak now introduces a different teaching: *mikra sofrim*, *ve'itur sofrim*, *vekaryan velo kesivan*, *vekesivan velo karyan* — the reading tradition of the scribes, the embellishments of the scribes, words that are read but not written, and words that are written but not read — all of these are *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*. They trace all the way back to Moshe at Sinai. The *Gemara* now proceeds to unpack each category in turn.

## Scribal Traditions

### Vowel Shifts

*Mikra Sofrim* refers to the tradition of how certain words are vocalized differently depending on their position in the verse — specifically, whether they fall at a major pause such as an *esnachta* or at the end of a *pasuk*. The *Gemara* cites the examples of *Eretz*, *Shamayim*, and *Mitzrayim*. The Ran explains this as the phenomenon of vowel shift: a word such as *Eretz* may be read with a *segol* in the middle of a verse but shift to a *kamatz* at an *esnachta* or end of verse, yielding *Aretz*. Similarly, *Shamayim* and *Mitzrayim* shift from a *patach* to a *kamatz* at these junctures. The Ran also offers an alternative explanation rooted in the written text: words like *Mitzrayim* or *Shamayim* could in theory be read differently without the stabilizing vowel letters — the scribal tradition of how such words are pronounced fills this gap. Whether this reflects a contrast with Judeo-Arabic conventions or points to the significance of the *kamatz*-*patach* alternation, the underlying principle remains the same: there is an ancient tradition, going back to Sinai, of how to vocalize these words in their varying syntactic positions.

### Embellishment Words

*Ittur Sofrim* — the "adornment" or "embellishment" of the scribes — refers to extra words appearing in certain verses whose presence requires explanation. The examples given are occurrences of the word *achar* in several contexts: *achar ta'avoru* (Genesis 18:5), *achar teleich* (Numbers 12:14), and *achar te'asef* — as well as *keharerei el* (Psalms 36:7).

The Ran offers two readings of this category. The first is a matter of punctuation and sense: the word *achar* divides a verse in a way that could easily be misread. For instance, in the verse *sa'adu libchem achar ta'avoru*, the question is whether to read "fortify your hearts, and then after that you shall pass," or "fortify your hearts after you pass" — a reading that would make no sense in context. The *ittur sofrim* tradition tells us where to place the pause, saving the meaning of the verse from confusion. Understood this way, the category seems functionally similar to *pisuk te'amim*, and the distinction between them is not immediately clear.

The second reading the Ran offers is that these words were simply not grammatically necessary. The verse could have read *sa'adu libchem vetaavoru*, or *teshev hana'arah yamim itanu vetelech*, or *Miriam tisager shivat yamim veteaseif* — the word *achar* in each case is an additional embellishment that makes the verse sound more natural or melodious. Similarly, *keharerei el* in Psalms 36:7 has a *kaf* of comparison where the verse's parallel structure — *mishpatecha tehom rabah* — would have suggested simply *harerei El* without the *kaf*. The Ran identifies this as *tiferet halashon*, an aesthetic enhancement. The claim that such embellishments are *halakha l'Moshe miSinai* would then mean that these words were always part of the original text, against any position that they represent later additions. Neither explanation, it must be said, is entirely satisfying: the first merges uncomfortably with *pisuk te'amim*, while the second raises the question of what the *hava amina* would have been — why would anyone have thought these words were later additions?

### Read Not Written

*Keri velo ketiv* — words read in the biblical text but not actually written there — constitutes the third category. The *Gemara* enumerates several instances:

*Prat debelachto*: In a verse in Samuel, the word *Prat* — the Euphrates River — is read but absent from the written text.

*Ish dekaasher yishal ish*: The word *ish* is read in the verse but not written.

*Ba'im denivnetah*: In Yirmiyahu, the verse *hineh yamim neum Hashem venivnetah hair* has *ba'im* read but not written — the text would simply say *hineh yamim neum Hashem*.

*La depleitah*: In the final chapters of Yirmiyahu, the word *la* — "for it" — appears in the reading of a verse about *pleitah* but is absent from the written text.

*Et dehaged hugad*: In Ruth, Boaz speaks to Ruth — *haged hugad li kol asher asit chamotech* — and the word *et* is read before *kol asher* but is not written.

*Eilai dehagoren*: Also in Ruth, the word *eilai* — "to me" — is read in the verse but not written, as in *asher tomri eilai eeseh*.

*Alai de-has'orim*: Again in Ruth, in the phrase *shesh has'orim ha-eleh natan li ki amar alai*, the word *alai* is read but absent from the written text.

### Written Not Read

*Ketiv velo keri* — words written in the biblical text but not read aloud — is the fourth category. The *Gemara*'s examples are as follows:

*Na de-yislach*: In the request of Na'aman — *yislach na Hashem le-avdecha* — the word *na* is written but not read aloud; we read simply *yislach Hashem le-avdecha*.

*Zot de-hamitzvah*: The *Gemara* claims a word *zot* is written but not read in *parashas va'etchanan*. Here the Ran notes an important discrepancy: he found this cited in his sources, but writes *kach matzati katuv velo nimtza be-sfarim she-lanu* — it appears in the tradition but is absent from the manuscripts he possessed. He tentatively identifies it with a verse in Yirmiyahu — *chai Hashem asher asah lanu et hanefesh hazot* — where *hazot* may be the word written but not read.

*Yidroch deha-dorech*: In Yirmiyahu, the verse *yidroch ha-dorech et kashto* (or *el ha-dorech kashto*) has the word *yidroch* written but not read.

*Chamesh de-pa'at negev*: In Yechezkel, the verse *ve-elu midoteha chamesh chamesh me'ot ve-arba alafim* has the word *chamesh* written twice but read only once.

*Im de-ki goel anochi*: In Ruth, the verse *ve-atah ki omnam ki im goel anochi* has the word *im* written but not read; we read simply *ki goel anochi*.

The *Gemara* also takes care to demarcate the two lists clearly. After listing the *keri velo ketiv* examples, it states *hani keryan velo ktivan* — these are the ones read and not written — before introducing the *ketiv velo keri* list, and then restates the identification at the end of the second list with *halin ktivan velo keryan*. This redundancy is deliberate: with two parallel lists flowing into each other, a reader could easily lose track of which category is under discussion, and the bracketing language prevents the confusion.

### Gemara vs. Mesora

A significant methodological observation emerges from the Ran's discussion of these lists. The Ran notes that in the *mesora* — the systematic tradition codified by the school of Ben Asher, probably in the seventh or eighth century, shortly after the close of the *Gemara* — the list of *keri u-ketiv* examples is a superset of the *Gemara*'s list: *chashiv kol hani ve-tapya lehu achrinei* — it records all of the *Gemara*'s cases and adds others. Yet more striking is that the *mesora* does not record *et ha-mitzvah* or *zot ha-mitzvah* among the *ketiv velo keri* cases, indicating that the lists do not perfectly align. The *Gemara* has some items that are not corroborated by the *mesora* as we have it.

This points toward a broader and important structural observation. According to scholars of the biblical text, the phenomenon of *keri u-ketiv* likely reflects the existence of two distinct schools of tradition operating in partial independence from one another. One school preserved the written text — the scribes (*sofrim*) who specialized in the craft of writing, producing parchment and copying letters with exactitude according to their received tradition, and who were expert in matters such as *malei* and *chaseir* (full and deficient spellings). A second school preserved the oral tradition of how the text was read: vocalization, grammatical parsing, *pisuk te'amim* — the entire apparatus of reading, which is a distinct skill from scribal copying. A scribe who knows the letters of *Shamayim* does not thereby know whether to read it with a *kamatz* or a *patach* at the end of a verse; that belongs to the reader's tradition.

These two schools did not always match perfectly. The reading tradition might include a word the writing tradition did not record, or vice versa — not because either school was in error, but because they preserved different layers of transmission. The *Gemara*, by attributing all of these categories to *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*, resolves the apparent tension: both traditions are equally ancient and authoritative, and their occasional divergence reflects not a contradiction but a dual transmission going back to Sinai itself. The *Gemara*'s framing is itself noteworthy in how it attributes the reading tradition to *sofrim* — *mikra sofrim*. Rather than presenting the scribes and the readers as separate or competing schools, the *Gemara* converges them: the tradition of *how to read* is itself attributed to the scribal class, synthesizing what might otherwise appear as two opposed streams.

A separate but related category is *tikkun sofrim* — the scribal practice of substituting a softer word for a harsh or explicit one, as in *yishkavenah* for *yishgalenah* in the *kelalos*. This is not the same phenomenon as *keri u-ketiv*, because it does not represent an ancient transmission of two parallel textual layers; rather, it is a later ritual adjustment for the sake of propriety in public reading, comparable to the practice of not reading *ma'aseh Dinah* in a congregational setting. There is, in fact, a well-known Rashi in the *Torah* — the precise location requires looking up — that explicitly characterizes *tikkun sofrim* as a later scribal intervention for the sake of *tikkun ha-lashon*, distinct from any *halakha l'Moshe miSinai*.

## Pasuk Divisions

### Three-Pasuk Division

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Abba reports that in *Eretz Yisrael* — *b'ma'arava* — a verse that we divide as one *pasuk* was divided into three *pesukim*. The verse in question is the one beginning *Vayomer Hashem el Moshe hinne Anochi ba elecha ba'av he'anan ba'avur yishma ha'am b'daberi imach v'gam b'cha ya'aminu l'olam vayyashev Moshe et divrei ha'am el Hashem*. The final clause, *vayyashev Moshe et divrei ha'am el Hashem*, could readily stand as an independent *pasuk*, and the opening phrase through *ba'av he'anan* could similarly be separated. The intermediate phrase beginning *ba'avur yishma ha'am* is a somewhat awkward way to open a *pasuk*, but the point stands: there existed a regional tradition of breaking this verse into three separate units, acknowledging that the boundaries between *pesukim* were not universally fixed.

## Moshe's Attributes

### Moshe's Wealth Source

The *Gemara* now transitions into a series of *drashot* that, while occasioned by the theme of scribal activity, constitute a rich midrashic exploration of Moshe's character and the nature of his relationship to Torah. Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: *lo he'eshir Moshe ela mi-psoletan shel luchot* — Moshe became wealthy from nothing other than the leftover fragments of the *luchot*. The tradition holds that the *luchot* were made of precious stone, and the verse *pesol lecha shnei luchot avanim karishonim* — "carve for yourself two tablets of stone like the first" — is read not only as *pesol lecha* ("carve for yourself") but as *psolatan shelcha* ("the cuttings are yours to keep"). Moshe carved the stone, and the sapphire dust or the fragments that fell away were his to keep. As one student astutely observed, this reading works even better according to the understanding that *pesol lecha* means Moshe himself did the cutting and shaping from a larger stone — the *pesolas* being the material carved away in forming the tablets.

### Torah Given to Moshe

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina teaches a remarkable *aggadah*: *lo nittna Torah ela l'Moshe u'zaro* — the Torah was given only to Moshe and his descendants, not to the rest of Israel. The derivation is from the same verse: just as *pesol lecha* is read as "the cuttings are for you," so too *ketov lecha et hadvarim ha-eleh* — "write for yourself these words" — is read as *katvanvan shelcha*, meaning what is written down belongs to you, to Moshe and his offspring alone. Nevertheless, *Moshe nahag ba tovat ayin v'nattna l'Yisrael* — Moshe acted with a generous spirit and gave it to the Jewish people as a whole. The verse applied to him is *tov ayin hu yevorach* — the one with a generous eye shall be blessed.

### Teaching Obligation

Rav Chisda raises a powerful challenge to this position. If the Torah was given only to Moshe, what of the verse *v'oti tziva Hashem ba'et ha-hi l'lammed etchem* — "God commanded me at that time to teach you"? This appears to be an explicit divine command to transmit Torah to the entire people, not merely a personal act of generosity. Similarly, *va'ani limadeti etchem chukim umishpatim ka'asher tzivani Hashem Elokai* — "I have taught you statutes and laws as God commanded me" — indicates that the teaching itself was commanded, not merely voluntary.

### Pilpula & Generosity

The *Gemara* responds to Rav Chisda's challenge by offering a forced but creative re-reading: *v'oti tziva Hashem... va'ani lachem* — God commanded me, and I decided to teach you. On this reading, God commanded Moshe for himself, and the teaching to Israel was Moshe's own initiative. The *Gemara* attempts similar readings of the other verses. In the end, however, the *Gemara* retreats: *ella pilpula be'alma* — what was uniquely given to Moshe and what he generously shared was not the *Torah* itself (which was always commanded to be transmitted to Israel) but *pilpula be'alma*, the capacity for incisive analytical engagement with the text. The word *pilpul*, from the root meaning pepper, connotes the kind of penetrating, nuanced interpretive analysis that is the hallmark of deep Torah learning. This is what was uniquely Moshe's gift, and what he shared with all of Israel.

### Torah she-bichtav

The Maharsha offers an alternative and, in this context, more contextually fitting reading. He notes that virtually all the *pesukim* cited in this passage concern written dimensions of Torah: *pesol lecha*, *ketov lecha*, *kitvu lachem et hashira hazot*. The *hava amina* is therefore not about who was commanded to observe the *mitzvos* — clearly all of Israel was — but about who possessed *Torah she-bichtav*, the written text itself. The claim of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina is that in principle, the sacred written scripture was intended for Moshe and his descendants alone, to be protected and transmitted within that line, while Israel would have had the *mitzvos* and the *halacha* without necessarily possessing access to the derivational process rooted in the written word.

This reading resonates with the broader history of religious tradition: ancient religions often maintained a protected class of priests who guarded access to sacred texts, while the people received only the transmitted message. The medieval church famously resisted translating scripture into the vernacular for analogous reasons. The Maharsha suggests the *Gemara* is taking seriously — not dismissing — the idea that there is something appropriate about keeping the written Torah in a protected sacred domain, close to its divine source. What is theologically significant is that in the end the *Gemara* abandons this position entirely, insisting that *Torah she-bichtav* belongs to all of Israel. And the closing move is revealing: what was uniquely Moshe's, what he gave as an act of generosity, was *pilpula* — precisely the tool of *Torah she-be'al peh*, the analytical engagement that has nothing to do with protecting the written text. The *Gemara* has thus dramatically inverted the *hava amina*: the written Torah was always meant for everyone, and what distinguished Moshe's unique contribution was something entirely in the oral-analytical realm.

### Five Attributes

Rabbi Yochanan teaches that the divine presence — *shechinas* — rests only upon one who possesses four qualities: *gibor* (physical might), *ashir* (wealth), *chacham* (wisdom), and *anav* (humility). The Talmud then states *v'chulam mi-Moshe* — Moshe embodied all of these — and proceeds to prove each one from scripture.

### Physical Strength

The proof of Moshe's *gevurah* begins with the verse *vayifros es ha-ohel al ha-mishkan* — he spread the tent covering over the *Mishkan* — with the tradition that Moshe did this personally, without assistance. Given that the *Mishkan* stood ten *amos* — approximately fifteen feet — high, spreading the covering unassisted would have required extraordinary physical stature and strength. The *Gemara* entertains the objection that perhaps Moshe was simply very tall but physically weak — *arich v'katin* — but then advances a more compelling proof: the verse *va-etpos b'shnei ha-luchos va-ashlichem me-al shtei yadai va-ashabrem*, in which Moshe physically gripped the two tablets and hurled them. A *braisa* teaches that each tablet was six *tefachim* long, six *tefachim* wide, and three *tefachim* thick — approximately a foot and a half by a foot and a half by nine inches of solid stone. Holding and throwing such a mass with bare hands bespeaks enormous physical power.

### Wealth Proof

For wealth: *psal lecha psoltan shelcha* — as established above, Moshe retained the precious stone fragments from carving the tablets, and from this he became wealthy. One might note that this enrichment came relatively late in Moshe's career, at the second giving of the tablets, which raises the interesting question of whether Moshe possessed all four qualities earlier, when the *shechinah* first rested on him. This chronological difficulty is acknowledged but left unresolved.

### Wisdom Gates

For wisdom: Rav and Shmuel both teach that fifty gates of understanding — *chamishim sha'arei binah* — were created in the world, and all were given to Moshe save one, as the verse states *vatachasreihu m'at me'elokim* — "You have made him but little less than God." Moshe's wisdom was thus as close to divine perfection as a human being can attain. Notably, the *Gemara* says these gates were *given* to Moshe — which raises the same question as before: is this about an innate capacity or a divine gift? The Rambam's position in his discussion of *nevuah* is relevant here: prophecy rests on one whose own intellect has been developed to apprehend the divine, not merely on one passively chosen by God. From that perspective, the gift and the developed capacity are not in tension.

### Humility Proof

For humility: *ve-ha'ish Moshe anav me'od* — "the man Moshe was exceedingly humble." This is the most straightforward of the four proofs.

## Prophets' Wealth

### General Principle

Rabbi Yochanan teaches further that all of the prophets were wealthy. Since prophecy rests upon one who possesses all four attributes including wealth, it follows as a general principle. The *Gemara* grounds this claim in four specific cases — Moshe, Samuel, Amos, and Jonah — from which we extrapolate to all the prophets.

### Moshe's Refusal

For Moshe, the proof text is *lo chamor echad mehem nasati* — "I have not taken even one donkey of theirs." The *Gemara* asks: does this just mean he didn't steal? Surely that goes without saying. The *Gemara* therefore reads it more specifically: Moshe is declaring that he never even rented a donkey from them, even with payment. To which the *Gemara* objects: perhaps he refrained from renting not because he was wealthy but because he was poor. The *Gemara* therefore falls back on the earlier source: the proof that Moshe was wealthy comes from *psal lecha* — the retention of the precious stone fragments — rather than from this verse.

### Samuel's Wealth

For Samuel, the proof text is the famous passage from his farewell address: *hineni anu bi neged Hashem v'neged meshicho et shor mi lakachti v'chamor mi lakachti* — "here I am, testify against me: whose ox have I taken, whose donkey have I taken?" Parallel to the discussion of Moshe, the *Gemara* asks whether Samuel is merely denying theft. The answer is the same: he is denying even having rented an animal from them, even for fair compensation. But again, this could reflect poverty rather than wealth. The actual proof of Samuel's wealth comes from the verse *utshuvato haRamatah ki sham beito* — "his return was to Ramah, for his home was there" — and Rava's interpretation: *kol makom shehalach beito imo* — wherever Samuel traveled, his household accompanied him. This does not mean he was homeless; it means he never required anyone's hospitality because he traveled with all his servants, provisions, and resources. He was wealthy enough to be entirely self-sufficient wherever he went.

### Samuel's Refusal

Rava draws a comparison between Moshe and Samuel that favors Samuel. By Moshe, the verse says *nasati* — "I did not take" — implying that Moshe never initiated a request to borrow or rent an animal, since even asking would have been an abuse of his authority over people who could not comfortably refuse him. But by Samuel, the verse says *lo ashatanu velo ritzotanu* — "you have not oppressed us and have not exploited us" — and the word *ritzotanu*, read as deriving from *ratzon*, indicates that even when people offered Samuel the use of their animals of their own free will, he refused. Moshe held back from asking; Samuel refused even when offered. In this specific dimension of scrupulous avoidance of benefit from one's position, Samuel's standard exceeded even that of Moshe.

### Amos's Possession

For Amos: *lo navi anochi velo ben navi anochi ki boker ani uboles shikmim* — "I am neither a prophet nor a son of a prophet; I am a *boker* and a *boles* of sycamores." As Rav Yosef translates: *arei marei getei ana* — I am an owner of cattle (*marei*, meaning master or owner, not merely a hired herder), and I have sycamore groves in the valley. The term *marei* confirms that Amos was not a laborer but a proprietor of his flocks and lands.

### Jonah's Payment

For Jonah: *vayiten sechara vayered bah* — "he gave its fare and went down into it." Rabbi Yochanan explains that Jonah paid the fare for the entire ship, not just his own passage. Rav Romanus adds that the fare for the whole ship amounted to four thousand gold *dinari*. The explanation offered is that Jonah wished to depart immediately and could not wait for the ship to fill with other passengers; he therefore purchased all the remaining tickets himself in order to set sail at once. That he was able to do so speaks to his considerable wealth.

## Torah as Gift

### Forgetting & Gift

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *betchila haya Moshe lomed Torah umeshakecha* — at first, Moshe would learn Torah and immediately forget it, until it was given to him as a gift. The verse cited is *vayiten el Moshe ke-chaloto ledaber ito* — "God gave it to Moshe upon finishing speaking with him" — read as a divine gifting of the Torah to Moshe rather than merely a conclusion to a conversation.

### Gift Theology

This passage stands in a provocative relationship to the earlier claim that Moshe was a *chacham* who possessed fifty gates of understanding. If Moshe's wisdom was so extraordinary, why did he initially forget? One way to read this is that the intellectual apprehension and the stable possession of Torah are two different things. Moshe could receive, process, and analyze — but to actually *hold* the Torah, to retain it as an enduring possession, required an act of divine gifting.

There is also a direct connection back to the opening discussion of this daf: the prohibition against charging for teaching Torah. If even Moshe received the Torah not through his own efforts but as a *matanah*, a divine gift, then Torah at its root is not a commodity to be earned or purchased. *Ma ani be-chinam af ata be-chinam* — just as I gave it freely, so too you shall transmit it freely. The entire Torah that any person acquires is ultimately not the fruit of their own efforts alone but a gift from God. To charge for it, then, is to treat as merchandise something that was always in the domain of divine generosity.

The passage also closes a circle opened earlier by Rabbi Yose bar Chanina's *aggadah* about Torah belonging to Moshe and his descendants. That *aggadah* explored the question of ownership — could the written Torah be the protected possession of one family? The *Gemara* rejected it. And here, at the conclusion of the sugya, the *Gemara* quietly makes the deepest point of all: even Moshe did not fully *own* the Torah. He could not even retain it on his own. It had to be given to him as a gift. The Torah belongs to God, and is given freely — to Moshe, through Moshe, and ultimately to all of Israel.

## Mudar Hana'ah Rules

### Feeding Family

The *Gemara* returns to the laws of *nedarim* and the case of a *mudar hana'ah*. If Reuven may not receive benefit from Shimon, Shimon is nonetheless permitted to provide food for Reuven's wife and children. Although Reuven has a legal obligation to provide his wife's sustenance under the terms of the *ketubah*, Shimon's feeding her is categorized not as a direct benefit to Reuven but as the indirect benefit of paying off a creditor. The money or food goes to someone else who has a claim against Reuven; it does not come directly to Reuven himself. The rule applies *af al pi shehu chayav bimzonotan* — even though Reuven is legally obligated to support them, the indirectness of the benefit means it does not constitute prohibited *hana'ah*.

### Feeding Animals

The same permission does not extend to Reuven's animals. *Velo yazon et behemto bein tmeiah bein tehorah* — Shimon may not feed Reuven's animals, whether they are kosher or non-kosher. The distinction is clear: feeding someone's livestock directly increases the value of their property. A fatter or stronger animal is worth more. This is not analogous to paying off a creditor or providing for a dependent; it is directly enriching the *mudar's* estate. The benefit passes through no intermediary — it is an immediate enhancement of Reuven's asset, which is precisely the kind of *hana'ah* the prohibition targets.

### Kosher vs. Non-Kosher

Rabbi Eliezer, however, draws a distinction within the category of animals: *zan et hatmeiah ve'eino zan et hatehorah* — he may feed the non-kosher animal but not the kosher one. His reasoning is striking: *shehatehorah nafsha lashamayim vegufa shelo* — a kosher animal's soul goes to heaven, but its body belongs to the owner. Therefore, making the body of a kosher animal heavier or stronger does increase its owner's value. *Utmeiah nafsha vegufa lashamayim* — but a non-kosher animal, both its soul and its body are, in a sense, destined for heaven; the owner cannot eat it. So fattening a non-kosher animal confers no real benefit to its owner.

### Value vs. Loss

The underlying conceptual framework here is the distinction between *increasing value* and *preventing loss*. A useful analogy: if you give someone's car a new paint job or repair its dents, you have actively increased its market value — that is a direct benefit. But if you take the car for an oil change it was due for, you have performed maintenance that staves off future damage without increasing present value. The maintenance prevents a loss; it does not generate a gain. Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning about non-kosher animals follows this logic: feeding a donkey prevents it from weakening, but it does not actively increase the owner's wealth, since the owner has no way to monetize the animal's physical condition through consumption.

### Ran's Distinction

The Ran reads the case differently. In his interpretation, the discussion concerns a situation where the animal has already received its basic daily portion. Giving it that basic portion would be a genuine benefit to the owner — maintaining the animal's value — and would presumably be prohibited. What the discussion concerns is whether one may provide *additional* food beyond what the animal requires. Extra feeding, on the Ran's reading, does not increase the animal's value and is therefore permitted. However, this reading may not align with the plain sense of the *sugya*, where the simpler reading appears to be that any feeding of an animal is prohibited because it maintains or increases value, and the question is only about the conceptual category — active enhancement versus passive maintenance — rather than about the quantity of food.

### Alternative Values

The Sages respond to Rabbi Eliezer's position: *mah bein tmeiah letehorah* — what is the real difference? Even a non-kosher animal has market value. *She'im yirtzeh harei hu mochrah le'ovdei chochavim uma'achilah leklavim* — if the owner wishes, he can sell the animal to non-Jews for consumption, or use it as dog food. Accordingly, a larger, healthier non-kosher animal is worth more than a weaker one, and feeding it does increase the owner's wealth. Rabbi Eliezer's distinction between kosher and non-kosher animals is therefore rejected: since both types can be monetized, feeding either one constitutes a direct benefit to the *mudar*.
