# Sanhedrin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Qualifying Conditions

### Previous Restrictions

The daf begins in the middle of the laws of *ben sorer u'moreh*, the rebellious son. The Gemara has already established a series of limiting conditions: the son must be within the narrow window of thirteen years and three months of age, with two pubic hairs; the eating must involve specific quantities of meat and wine; and the meal must occur in a neutral setting — neither in the context of a *mitzvah* nor of an *aveira* — with all participants at the meal being, as it were, dissolute characters.

### Maaser Sheni

The Gemara continues, at approximately ten lines from the bottom of 71b, with the case of *achal maaser sheni b'Yerushalayim* — one who ate *maaser sheni* in Jerusalem. This is in principle possible to constitute meat and wine, since the money of *maaser sheni* may be used to purchase meat, and wine may be brought directly. Nevertheless, the Gemara rules: *kivan d'orchai hu ka achil lei lo mamshech* — since he is eating in a natural, regulated manner, within the structure of those laws and parameters, the behavior will not lead to the habituating, addictive pattern that defines the *ben sorer u'moreh*. Notably, the Gemara does not simply say it falls under the category of *bemitzvah ka asik* — being engaged in a *mitzvah* — even though the Gemara had previously invoked that reasoning. The phrasing here seems to emphasize the structured, lawful nature of the eating rather than the *mitzvah* dimension per se.

### Chicken Meat Debate

The Gemara then addresses *achal neveilot u'treifot shkatzim u'remasim* — eating various non-kosher meats. Rava rules: *achal basar of eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — eating chicken does not qualify, since chicken is not red meat, and it is the red meat that carries the gluttonous character relevant to this *din*. The Gemara raises an objection: the Mishnah explicitly includes *shkatzim u'remasim* — rodents and insects — which are not red meat. The Gemara resolves this: *hatam hu d'naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — in that case it qualifies because these are forbidden foods, not because of the species of meat. The Mishnah is cited to teach that one may have a small proportion of chicken alongside the red meat: *ki t'nan nami matnitin l'hashlim* — the Mishnah is addressing a case where the overwhelming majority of the *maneh* or half-*maneh* consists of red meat, and a small remainder may be other things. That remainder must still be kosher; *treifa* would not qualify even in that supplemental role.

### Red Meat Proportion

The precise proportion is not given — the Gemara does not specify whether 51% suffices or a larger share is required — but the principle stands: the bulk must be red meat, and the allowable remainder is a minor portion.

### Mitzvah vs. Aveira

The Gemara then generalizes: if one ate in the context of a *mitzvah* or in the context of an *aveira*, the *din* of *ben sorer u'moreh* does not apply.

### Meal Contexts

The Gemara provides illustrations. An example of *dvar mitzvah* is *tanchumei avelim* — the meal eaten with mourners to provide comfort. An example of *dvar aveira* is a *taanit tzibur* — a communal fast day — when eating is prohibited. Both of these examples are striking, as neither is explicitly biblical: *tanchumei avelim* falls for the Rambam under the general principle of *v'ahavta l'reiacha kamocha*, while *taanit tzibur* is rabbinic in origin. The Gemara's discussion clarifies that *dvar mitzvah* and *dvar aveira* refer to the context of the meal, not necessarily to the food itself — as was clear from the earlier case of *ibur hachodesh*, which was certainly not biblical. In the case of *shkatzim u'remasim*, the food itself was the *dvar aveira*; in the case of a *chaburat mitzvah*, the context sanctifies it — but the underlying principle is the same.

### Biblical Defiance

The Gemara then provides the verse-based reason for these exclusions: *amar kra einenu shomeia b'koleinu* — the verse says, "he does not listen to our voice," *b'koleinu v'lo b'kolo shel Makom* — meaning he must be defying specifically the parents' authority, not God's. If the act also constitutes defiance of divine command, or if it fulfills a divine command, the singular character of parental defiance is altered. Until this point, the Gemara had primarily grounded these exclusions in the concern of *lo mamshech* — that the behavior would not become habitual or addictive. Here the Gemara shifts to a more formal textual ruling, invoking the language of the *pasuk* itself. Rashi notes explicitly: *af al gav d'lo avar ella al de'rabbanan* — even when the violation is only rabbinic, as in *taanit tzibur*, it still falls under *kolo shel Makom* and thus takes the case outside the parameters of *ben sorer u'moreh*. There is reason to believe the Yerushalmi applies this same logic to explain why a *bar mitzvah* who performs a *mitzvah* cannot be a *ben sorer u'moreh*: *einenu shomeia b'koleinu* means the issue at stake is the defiance of parental authority, and anything that implicates divine authority — whether as compliance or as additional defiance — alters that equation. The clearest reading remains that the setting must be neutral: neither *mitzvah* nor *aveira*.

## Meat & Wine Specificity

### Meat & Wine Only

The Mishnah states: *achal kol machal v'lo achal basar, shasa kol mashke v'lo shasa yayin, eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — no matter how much one eats, if it is not meat, or no matter how much one drinks, if it is not wine, one does not become a *ben sorer u'moreh*. The requirement is derived from the verse *zolel v'sove*, glutton and drunkard, which is read specifically as referring to meat and wine.

### Intoxicants Excluded

The Gemara then specifies what is excluded from the category of qualifying drink. *Achal kol machal v'lo achal basar l'atuyei d'veila kilit* — even pressed figs, which according to the Gemara do produce a mild intoxicating effect, do not qualify as "meat." *Shasa kol mashke v'lo shasa yayin l'atuyei devash v'chalav* — similarly, honey and milk are excluded from the category of qualifying drink, even though they too can produce a mild intoxicating effect. Notably, *shechar* — liquor — is not mentioned, which is itself somewhat surprising. A *baraita* confirms this: *achal d'veila kilit v'shasa devash v'chalav v'nichnas l'mikdash chayav* — one who consumes those substances and enters the *Beis Hamikdash* is liable, because the prohibition of entering while intoxicated covers *kol midi d'meshaker* — anything that causes intoxication, based on *yayin v'shechar al tesht*. But that prohibition is grounded in the state of intoxication, while *ben sorer u'moreh* is grounded in the specific language of *zolel v'sove*, which is read as referring only to meat and wine.

### Temple vs. Ben Sorer

The distinction is therefore between two different legislative contexts: the law of intoxication in the *Beis Hamikdash*, which is broad enough to encompass any intoxicant, and the law of *ben sorer u'moreh*, which requires the specific substances of meat and wine by virtue of the verse's wording.

### Schnapps Tangent

This distinction surfaces naturally in connection with a *teshuvah* concerning whether a *kohen* who made *kiddush* on schnapps on *Yom Tov* may subsequently perform *birchas kohanim*. Since the prohibition on *avodah* applies to any intoxicating substance — not only wine — the question turns on how much one must consume and precisely what constitutes intoxication for this purpose. Tosafos notes an apparent exception in the practice of *birchas kohanim* on *Shemini Atzeret* following *shacharis*, suggesting a more lenient reading in that particular context, but the underlying *machlokes* is whether even a small quantity — such as one ounce for *kiddush* — is sufficient to disqualify.

## Theft from Parents

### Theft Component

The Mishnah introduces a further requirement that the son must have stolen money. This is striking, because the *psukim* make no explicit mention of theft. The verse reads: *ki yihyeh l'ish ben sorer u'moreh einenu shomeia b'kol aviv u'v'kol imo v'yisru oso v'lo yishma aleihem v'safsu vo aviv v'imo* — and then the parents declare *bneinu zeh sorer u'moreh einenu shomeia b'koleinu zolel v'sove*. The only explicit characterization is *zolel v'sove*, glutton and drunkard. Where, then, does the requirement of theft originate?

The answer likely lies in the word *sorer u'moreh* itself — or in *einenu shomeia b'koleinu* — suggesting that the act must constitute a direct affront to parental authority, not merely irresponsible behavior. It is worth noting in this context that *kibbud av va'em*, as understood by the *acharonim*, does not technically require obedience to parental commands, particularly once a child has reached adulthood. *Kibbud* refers to acts of service — providing food, clothing, attending to needs. *Yira* prohibits publicly defying or embarrassing parents. But simply declining to follow instructions, when done respectfully, is generally not a violation of *kibbud av va'em*. If so, then mere gluttony and drinking — even in defiance of the parents' wishes — might not constitute the kind of direct assault on parental authority that the *Torah* envisions. *Chazal* therefore understood that the offense must involve something that more directly injures the parents — specifically, stealing from them. Compellingly, simply taking the food itself from the house would not rise to this level. Taking money, purchasing meat and wine, and consuming them elsewhere involves the son behaving as an outsider — violating property in a way that explicitly treats the family home and parental authority as foreign. This distinction between taking food at hand and actively stealing to fund the behavior seems to underlie the entire construct.

### Eating Locations

The Mishnah then enumerates the possible combinations of theft source and eating location. *Ganav mishel aviv v'achal birshus aviv* — stole from the father and ate in the father's domain: not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. *Mishel acherim v'achal birshus acherim* — stole from others and ate elsewhere: not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. *Mishel acherim v'achal birshus aviv* — stole from others and ate in the father's home: not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. Only *ad sheyignov mishel aviv v'yochal birshus acherim* — until he steals from the father and eats elsewhere — does the *din* of *ben sorer u'moreh* apply.

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *ad sheyignov mishel aviv u'mishel imo* — one must steal from both father and mother, derived from *einenu shomeia b'koleinu*, since both parents are named in the verse.

### Accessibility Logic

The Gemara explains the underlying logic of the Mishnah's ruling with remarkable clarity. *Ganav mishel aviv v'achal birshus aviv*: *af al gav d'shachiach lei bias* — even though the money is readily available at home, he is afraid of being caught while eating there. *Mishel acherim v'achal birshus acherim*: *af al gav d'lo bias* — even though he is not afraid of being caught while eating, *lo shachiach lei* — the money is not readily available, since stealing from others is not repeatable in the same way. *V'chol shekein mishel acherim v'achal birshus aviv*: both deficiencies are present — the money is not available and he fears being caught. Only *mishel aviv v'yochal birshus acherim* — *d'shachiach lei v'lo bias* — combines readily available money with freedom from fear of detection, and this combination is what gives rise to the concern about habitual, escalating behavior. The analogy is instructive: someone with a drug habit will typically steal from their parents' wallet, where money is always accessible, and consume elsewhere, where they will not be caught. That precise pattern is what the Torah identifies as the dangerous behavioral trajectory.

One might ask whether stealing repeatedly from others — demonstrating rebelliousness even more boldly — should equally qualify. The Gemara's answer is that the parameters of *ben sorer u'moreh* are defined not by maximum rebelliousness but by the specific configuration that gives rise to a predictable, repeatable pattern. Stealing from others is episodic; stealing from one's own home provides ongoing, easy access. The system requires that particular combination.

### Both Parents' Money

Rabbi Yehuda requires that the theft be from both father and mother, derived from the verse's mention of *b'koleinu* — our voice — encompassing both. The Gemara immediately raises the difficulty: *imo mana lah* — from where does the mother have money? The principle *mah shekansah isha kanah ba'alah* establishes that whatever a wife acquires, her husband acquires. How can theft from the mother be a meaningful category?

### Mother's Ownership

Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina answers: *misuda hamuchenes l'aviv u'l'imo* — from the meal being prepared for the father and mother. Even if she does not technically own the food, it is designated for her consumption, and stealing it constitutes stealing from her in a meaningful sense. However, the Gemara notes that *geneivah* implies clandestine taking, and if one is merely taking food from the kitchen, the character of the act may differ. The Gemara ultimately revises this: *ela eima midmei suda hamuchenes l'aviv u'l'imo* — the son stole money that had been set aside to purchase a meal for the parents. Even though the mother does not hold legal title to that money, it is regarded as theft from her. The Gemara further suggests: *d'akni lei echad v'amar la al menas she'ein l'baalech reshus bahen* — alternatively, someone could have transferred property to her on the explicit condition that her husband has no rights to it, in which case she would hold independent title. The question of whether *nichsei milog* — property in which the husband holds the right to *peiros* but the wife retains title — would satisfy this requirement is left somewhat open.

### Purchased Goods

Rav Huna, cited by Rabbi Chana bar Molda, states: *eino chayav ad sheyikneh basar b'zol v'yochal v'yayin b'zol v'yishteh* — he is not liable unless he purchases the meat and wine cheaply and eats and drinks them. The critical word is *ad sheyikneh* — he must steal money and *purchase* the goods. If he merely takes food directly from the home, the *din* does not apply. The reason connects to the underlying concern with habitual behavior: a person willing to steal money, sell or exchange it, and use the proceeds to purchase wine and meat has demonstrated a level of initiative and commitment to the addictive pattern that mere opportunistic eating at home does not. There is also a dimension of rebelliousness: taking the family's cash and engaging in commerce with it treats the son as an outsider to the household, violating the parents' authority in a more thoroughgoing way.

## Parental Authority

### Both Parents Required

The Mishnah states: *haya aviv rotzeh v'imo einah rotzah* — if the father wished to prosecute and the mother did not, or vice versa, *eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — he is not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. Both parents must equally desire to bring him to *beis din*: *eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh ad sheyihyu shneihem rotzim*.

### Mother's Fitness

Rabbi Yehuda adds: *im lo haysa imo re'uyah l'aviv eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — if his mother was not a fitting wife for the father, he is not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. This formulation raises an immediate question, since the Torah speaks of mother and father, not of husband and wife. Why should the permissibility of the union between the parents be relevant? The Torah's language speaks only in terms of the parental relationship — *einenu shomeia b'kol aviv u'v'kol imo* — not of the marital status of the parents.

### Same Attributes

The Gemara asks: *mai einah reuyah*? What does "not fitting" mean? If it means a woman forbidden to the father — say, his sister or some other *arayos* prohibition — that should not matter: *sof sof avuh avuh ninhu v'imei imei ninhu* — ultimately he is still the father and she is still the mother, and the Torah never requires marriage. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ella b'shaveh l'aviv ka'amar* — Rabbi Yehuda means she must be equal to the father. A *baraita* confirms and elaborates: *Rabbi Yehuda omer im lo hayta imo shavah l'aviv b'kol u'v'mareh u'v'komah eino naaseh ben sorer u'moreh* — if she does not share the same voice, the same appearance, and the same height as the father, he is not a *ben sorer u'moreh*. The Gemara then derives: since the verse requires *b'koleinu* — our voice — meaning a shared voice, and the voice must be the same, so too *mareh* and *komah* must be the same. This seems reminiscent of the requirement that the two goats of *shnei se'irim* on *Yom Kippur* be identical in appearance and height.

### Unified Voice Logic

The Maharsha offers a penetrating explanation of both the requirement and its logic. If the parents had different voices, different appearances, or different heights, the *beis din* would assume that the son was effectively only defying one parent — since no child, the Maharsha reasons, would willingly defy two parents who were truly united and presenting a genuinely unified front. If both parents were visually and aurally indistinguishable in their authority — *kol echad* — we could believe the son defied both. If they were distinguishable, we would assume he really only defied one of them, and therefore the claim fails.

Read more conceptually, the requirement is not literally about physical resemblance but about the unity of parental voice. Parents who present a divided front — where one quietly concedes while the other insists, or where one says "listen to your mother" while privately sympathizing with the child — cannot truly claim the son defied *both* of them. *Einenu shomeia b'koleinu* demands that the son have genuinely defied a unified authority. When parents operate in unison — speaking in a single voice, presenting a single front — they can credibly bring this claim. When they do not, even if one parent comes to *beis din*, the defiance was at most of one parent's authority, not a rebellion against the unified household. The literal reading of the Gemara — same voice, same appearance, same height — expresses this conceptual requirement in concrete, if stringent, terms.

### Parent Discord

The Mishnah's requirement that both parents must desire prosecution follows naturally: if only one parent brings the son to *beis din*, the son has not truly defied joint parental authority. The verse *v'safsu vo aviv v'imo* — the father and mother shall seize him — demands their joint action. Notably, even the mother's refusal is sufficient to halt the proceedings entirely, notwithstanding that within much of the *halachic* framework regarding women's authority, the mother's power is often subordinate. Here, her unwillingness alone is an absolute bar.

## Never Occurred

### Never Was/Will Be

The Gemara now cites the famous *baraita*: *ben sorer u'moreh lo haya v'lo asid lihyot* — the *ben sorer u'moreh* never occurred and never will occur. *V'lama nechtav? Derosh v'kabel sachar* — and why was it written? Interpret it and receive reward. The Gemara asks: *k'man*? Like whose opinion does this go? *K'Rabbi Yehuda* — like Rabbi Yehuda, who requires the parents to share voice, appearance, and height. The practical impossibility of finding two parents meeting all of those criteria means the *din* will never be applied.

### Moral Objection

But the Gemara then offers an alternative: *ibayit eima Rabbi Shimon hi* — alternatively, it goes like Rabbi Shimon. A *baraita* records Rabbi Shimon's position: *vechi mipnei she'achal zeh tartimar basar v'shata chatzi log yayin italki harei aviv v'imo motzi'in oto l'saklo*? Just because a boy ate a certain portion of meat and drank half a *log* of Italian wine, are his father and mother going to bring him to be stoned? *Ella lo haya v'lo asid lihyot.* It cannot be, and it will never be.

### Parental Mercy

One might ask: Rabbi Shimon is not claiming it is technically impossible; he is expressing moral incredulity. Why does that mean it will never happen? The Maharsha reads the passage carefully: Rabbi Shimon is not arguing that the Torah's requirement is unjust. He is arguing that no parents would ever actually do such a thing. *Hen vadai lo yagia l'sof da'ato v'yachos alav* — what parents would conclude that this child is truly lost and have him executed? They will have *rachmanus* on their child. That is the Maharsha's reading of *lo haya v'lo asid lihyot* — not a metaphysical impossibility, but a certainty rooted in the nature of parental love.

### Parental Lesson

What, then, is the *derosh v'kabel sachar*? The Maharsha explains: the Torah was not unaware that parents would never carry through on this. The *parsha* was written precisely so that parents would read it and internalize its lesson. The Torah is telling you: we are declaring that this child is fit to be executed — because if you do not intervene now, he is heading toward a life of crime, addiction, and ruin. We know you will have *rachmanus* on him. But take heed. Start placing limits. Show some firmness. *Aval al yedei zeh yochichu v'yisru es bneihem v'yekablu al zeh sachar* — by rebuking and disciplining their children, parents fulfill the Torah's purpose and receive *sachar* for it.

The Maharsha adds a striking personal observation: *aval bazman hazeh einu sam leiv l'zeh* — in his own day, no parents were truly taking this lesson to heart. *D'chol echad mechapeh al bno* — every parent was covering for their child. And even when children deserved rebuke, parents stood by. *Hana'arim holchim rov yameihem b'vittul Talmud Torah* — the children were spending most of their days in idleness from Torah study. *Lo charvah Yerushalayim ella bishvil shebitlu tinokos shel beis rabban* — and the Maharsha cites the principle that Jerusalem was destroyed only because the schoolchildren ceased their learning. Nothing, it seems, is new under the sun.

### Yonatan Witness

Against the position of *lo haya v'lo asid lihyot*, Rabbi Yonatan states: *ani ra'isi v'yashavti al kivro* — I myself witnessed a *ben sorer u'moreh*, and I sat upon his grave. His testimony is simply registered, without further resolution.

## Parallel Cases

### City Apostasy

The Gemara continues: *k'man azla ha d'tanya — Ir Ha'nidachas lo haysah v'lo asidah lihyos* — the *ir ha'nidachas*, the city gone astray into idolatry, likewise never occurred and never will occur. *V'lama nichtavah? Derosh v'kabel sachar.* The Gemara does not explain precisely why it would never occur, though one might observe that in the period of the *Beis Rishon*, when idolatry was pervasive, the very conditions for convening a *beis din* prepared to prosecute a whole city would be absent — the verse says *ish hayashar b'einav ya'aseh, ein melech b'Yisrael*, meaning there was no central authority capable of enforcing such a ruling. The combination of a city willing to engage in idolatry and a *beis din* capable and willing to prosecute it to this degree may simply never converge.

### Mezuzah Exception

The Gemara asks whose position this goes like: *k'Rabbi Eliezer*. A *baraita* records: *kol sheyesh bah afilu mezuzah achas einah na'asis Ir Ha'nidachas* — any city that has even one *mezuzah* cannot become an *ir ha'nidachas*. The reason: the verse requires gathering all the property of the city to the *rechovah* and burning it — *v'es kol shelalah tikbotz el toch rechovah v'sarafta ba'eish*. But a *mezuzah* cannot be included in that burning, for *lo ta'asun ken l'Hashem Elokeichem* — one may not destroy holy objects. If any *mezuzah* is present, the full procedure cannot be carried out, and the city cannot be declared an *ir ha'nidachas*.

A natural question arises: does the presence of a *mezuzah* only prevent the burning of the city's property, or does it also prevent the execution of the inhabitants? For those individuals who personally worshipped idolatry, one might argue they remain individually liable. The larger controversy — touched upon here — concerns the Rambam's ruling that the *ir ha'nidachas* procedure includes the execution of the families of the perpetrators, including wives and children. The Ramah finds this position shocking. But assuming that only actual perpetrators are liable, the *mezuzah* may affect only the property-burning component.

### City Witness

Again, Rabbi Yonatan counters: *ani re'itiha v'yashavti al tilah* — I myself saw a city that had been destroyed, and I sat upon its ruins. He is a witness against the position of *lo haya v'lo asid lihyot* in this case as well.

### Tzaraat House

The Gemara then turns to the third parallel case: *bayit hamenuga lo haya v'lo asid lihyos* — a house afflicted with *tzara'at* never occurred and never will occur. *V'lama nichtav? Derosh v'kabel sachar.* This case lacks the moral dimension of the first two. The Gemara presents it as a further instance of the general pattern, going like the position of Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon.

### Lentil Bean Measure

Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon rules: *le'olam ein habayit tamei bayit hamenuga* until the affliction appears as two *gerisin* in size — the minimum *nega tzara'at* being one *geris*, equivalent to a lentil bean (or, according to some, a *pul hashelomi*, a fava bean). The affliction must appear on two different stones, on two different walls, in a corner. That is, the spot must span the corner: each stone bearing a patch of one *geris*, for a combined area of two *gerisin* long by one *geris* wide. The derivation: the verse sometimes reads *kir* (singular wall) and sometimes *kirot* (plural walls) — *eizu kir shehu kekirot? hevei omer zeh keren zavit* — the wall that is like multiple walls is the corner wall, which belongs to two faces of the structure. This technical requirement — two afflicted stones at a corner, each bearing a full *geris* of *nega* — may be why, as a practical matter, the *din* never arose.

### Tzaraat Dump Site

A *baraita* records Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Tzadok: *makom haya bitchum Azza v'hayu korin oto churbata sagirta* — there was a place in the region of Gaza called the "ruin of *tzara'at*," which was apparently a dump site for stones removed from afflicted houses. Rabbi Shimon of Kfar Akko adds that he once traveled to the Galilee and saw a place marked as containing stones with *tzara'at*, which people were warned to avoid so as not to contract impurity. These testimonies indicate that the destruction or removal of stones from afflicted houses did in fact occur, even if the full formal procedure may have been rare.

## Parental Qualifications

### Arm Requirement

The Mishnah enumerates a series of physical qualifications required of the parents. *Hayah echad mehem gidam* — if one of the parents was without arms, the *din* does not apply, for the verse states *v'safsu vo aviv v'imo* — the father and mother shall seize him — *v'lo gidmin*, which they cannot do without arms.

### Mobility Requirement

*O chiger* — if one was lame, *v'hotziu oso* — and they shall bring him out — *v'lo chigrin* — which they cannot do without the mobility to bring him themselves.

### Speech Requirement

*O ilem* — if one was mute, they cannot say *bneinu zeh* — this our son — *v'lo ilmin.*

### Vision Requirement

*O suma* — if one was blind, the declaration *bneinu zeh* also involves identification, pointing to the son — *v'lo sumin*, which they cannot do without sight.

### Hearing Requirement

*O cheresh* — if one was deaf, *einenu shomeia b'koleinu* — he does not listen to our voice — cannot be established, because without hearing, one cannot know whether the son was truly defying the parental voice rather than simply not hearing it. *V'lo chirshin.*

## Court Procedure

### Warning & Lashing

The Mishnah states: *matrin bo bifnei shlosha u'malkin oto* — one warns him and lashes him in front of three. The term *matrin* here is *veyisru oto* — "they afflicted him" — from the verse, which encompasses both the physical lashing and the formal warning. The initial process proceeds as follows: the parents bring their son before *beis din*, present witnesses testifying to the theft and the eating and drinking, the son is warned, and he receives lashes. The nature of this *hatra'ah* requires attention, as will be discussed below.

### Three-Judge Court

The court for this initial stage — administering lashes — is a court of three judges, *bifnei shlosha*, as is standard for *malkes*.

### Twenty-Three Judges

*Chazar v'kilkel nidon b'esrim u'shlosha* — if he repeated the offense, he is judged by a court of twenty-three, since it has now become a capital case.

### Same Judges Required

*V'eino niskal ad she'yihyu sham shlosha rishonim* — he cannot be stoned unless the three judges from the original proceeding are present in this court of twenty-three. The basis is the verse *bneinu zeh sorer u'moreh* — *ze* — this one, who was lashed before you. The identification of the son as the same individual who was previously lashed requires the presence of those who witnessed the lashing, rendering the three original judges an indispensable component of the capital court.

### Witness Testimony

The Rambam's account of the full procedure is illuminating. In the first stage, two witnesses testify that the son stole from the father and ate and drank the required quantities — that is his initial offense. He receives lashes and a warning. If he then repeats the behavior — *chazar v'ganav* — the parents bring him to *beis din* again with witnesses who testify to the renewed eating and drinking, *achar she'hitru vo*, following the warning that was given. Only then does the capital proceeding before twenty-three judges commence.

## Lashes Source

### Veyisru Etymology

The Gemara asks: *malkut b'ven sorer u'moreh heicha ktiva* — where in the *pasuk* of *ben sorer u'moreh* is the requirement of lashes actually derived? Rabbi Abbahu answers: *lamadnu veyisru min veyisru* — we derive the *veyisru* of *ben sorer u'moreh* from the *veyisru* of *motzi shem ra*, the man who falsely accuses his wife. In the passage of *motzi shem ra* the verse says the elders should afflict him — and we know there that it means lashes. How do we know it means lashes in the case of *motzi shem ra*? From the word *ben* — derived from *vehayah im bin hakot harasha*, the classic verse establishing the institution of lashes, where the word *bin* (or *ben*) grounds the lashing procedure.

### Derivation Chain

The chain is thus: the *veyisru* of *ben sorer u'moreh* is learned from the *veyisru* of *motzi shem ra*, which is itself derived from the lashing verse *vehayah im bin hakot harasha*, whose use of *bin* connects it to the *ben* of *ben sorer u'moreh*. This interlocking derivation establishes lashes as the penalty for the initial offense of the *ben sorer u'moreh*.

### Judges Continuity

The Gemara then reaffirms: *chazar v'kilkel nidon b'esrim u'shlosha* — and the reason we need the *ze* of *bneinu ze* to include the original three judges in the court of twenty-three has now been established. Were it only to teach *v'lo sumim* — that the parents cannot be blind — the verse could have said *bneinu hu*. The fact that it says *ze* — this one — carries the additional implication that the court must recognize the son as the same individual who was previously before them, requiring the presence of those original three judges.

## Warning Nature

### Parental Hatra'ah

Rashi's comment on the Mishnah's phrase *matrin bo* is essential for understanding the nature of the warning. Rashi clarifies that this *matrin* is not the standard *hatra'ah* — the judicial warning given by witnesses immediately before an act, enabling subsequent punishment. In the normal structure of *hatra'ah*, witnesses warn the perpetrator at the moment before the transgression: "do not do this, or you will be liable to such-and-such punishment." Here, no such warning precedes the initial theft and eating.

### Authority Assertion

Rather, this *hatra'ah* is a parental one — *einenu shomeia b'koleinu* — an assertion of parental authority. The parents are saying: we have told this son to stop, and he has defied us. The warning is an expression of their authority over him and his rebellious refusal to accept it, not a procedural trigger for the subsequent lashing.

### Warning Uncertainty

There is also a *safek* in the structure: the warning is uncertain in its sequence. *V'od zu safek hatra'ah hu im over hu im lav, shelo miyad bishneihem over* — the warning is given, and only later, not immediately, does the son go out and repeat the offense. This further distinguishes it from the classic *hatra'ah*, which must immediately precede the act. The entire punitive structure of *ben sorer u'moreh* departs from ordinary rules.

### Parental Mussar

Rashi explains: *ela mochiachin oso. D'ba'inan v'yisru oso, shelo yargil* — they rebuke him so that he does not become habituated. It is *mussar*, parental discipline and exhortation: do not do this again. *V'im lo shama, malkin oso b'veis din* — and if he does not listen, he is then brought to *beis din* and given lashes. Rashi's formulation here suggests that the warning preceded the lashing — consistent with the reading of the verse's sequence — so that the lashes are administered as a consequence of having been warned and having persisted nonetheless. Whether this warning occurs at the first offense or only at the second, and whether it must occur in the presence of witnesses, remains somewhat open, but the essential character of the *hatra'ah* here is parental rather than judicial: an expression of authority, an act of *mussar*, and a demand for obedience — not the technical mechanism for enabling punishment.
