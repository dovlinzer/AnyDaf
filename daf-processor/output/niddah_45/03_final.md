# Niddah 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Years and a Day

### Meir vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִיאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A girl** who is **three years** old **is betrothed through intercourse;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** She must be **three years and one day old.**

The daf begins near the bottom of 44b, in the midst of a series of *mishnayot* that carry significance not only for *Niddah* but throughout *Shas*, addressing the halakhic significance of various stages of physical development for boys and girls. The *mishna* discussed previously established that once a girl reaches three years and a day, an act of intercourse with her is considered halakhically meaningful — less than that, it is not — and enumerated the practical consequences of this threshold.

The *Gemara* now picks up with a *beraita*: *bat shalosh shanim mitkadeshet be-vi'ah, divrei Rabbi Meir* — a three-year-old girl can acquire *kiddushin* through an act of intercourse, in the view of Rabbi Meir. By way of background: *kiddushin* can be effected through *kesef*, *shtar*, or *bi'ah*. When the girl is a minor, it is the father who provides the necessary consent, and the act of *bi'ah* — with the father's acquiescence — can constitute *kiddushin* once she has reached the relevant age. The *chachamim*, however, say *bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad* — three years and a day, not merely three years. The *Gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what precisely is the point of dispute?

### Yochanan: Mitzas Hashana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: עֶרֶב רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai said:** There is a difference **between** their opinions in the case of a girl on **the eve of the first day of** the fourth **year** of her life.

*Amar debei Rabbi Yannai*: the debate concerns *erev hashana* — the eve of the new year of her birth. To illustrate with a concrete case, suppose she was born on Rosh Hashana. Rabbi Meir holds that the final day of her third year — the day before her birthday, still within her third year — suffices. The *chachamim* hold that only the following day, when her fourth year begins, is valid. On this reading, the dispute is literally about a single day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם בַּשָּׁנָה חֲשׁוּבִין שָׁנָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** There is a difference **between** their opinions with regard to the issue of whether **thirty days in a year are considered** equivalent to **a year.**

Rabbi Yochanan, however, understands the disagreement to be far more substantial. He invokes the principle of *mitzas hashana ke-kulah* — part of a year counts as a full year. According to Rabbi Yochanan, when Rabbi Meir says "three years," he does not mean three complete years. Rather, once she is in her third year — even one month into it, at two years and thirty days — that partial year is treated as a full year, and she is considered to have reached the threshold. The *chachamim*, by contrast, require three complete years, and the phrase "and a day" means the day of her actual third birthday, that is, the day her fourth year commences.

### Beraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִיאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against the explanation of Rabbi Yannai from a *baraita*: A girl who is **three years old, and even** one who is **two years and one day old, is betrothed through intercourse;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** She must be **three years and one day old.**

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a *beraita*: *bat shalosh shanim va-afilu bat shetei shanim ve-yom echad mitkadeshet be-vi'ah, divrei Rabbi Meir* — three years, or even two years and a day, is sufficient for *kiddushin* through *bi'ah*, according to Rabbi Meir. The *chachamim* in this *beraita* likewise say *bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad*. The *beraita* thus implies that Rabbi Meir's position allows for as little as two years and one day — even less than two years and a month. The *Gemara* notes that according to Rabbi Yochanan's framework, this is not difficult: there is already a dispute elsewhere about how much of a year must pass for the principle of *mitzas hashana ke-kulah* to apply — some hold that even a single day into a year counts as the whole year, while others require a full month. Both positions share the same conceptual foundation; they differ only in how minimal the partial year must be for it to count as complete.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּקָאָמַר יוֹם אֶחָד בַּשָּׁנָה חָשׁוּב שָׁנָה, הָכִי נָמֵי אִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם בַּשָּׁנָה חֲשׁוּבִין שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, just as there is a *tanna* who says** that **one day in a year is considered** equivalent to **a year, so too, there is a *tanna* who says** that **thirty days in a year are considered** equivalent to **a year.**

The *Gemara* then considers the difficulty this *beraita* creates for the view of *debei Rabbi Yannai*, which had reduced the dispute to a single day. If the *chachamim* understood Rabbi Meir to require three full years, how can the *beraita* attribute to Rabbi Meir a position that allows two years and a day? The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela, mitzas hashana ke-kulah* — Rabbi Meir, in both the *mishna* and the *beraita*, operates with the principle that a partial year counts as a full year. Whether one applies it as a day or as a month, the conceptual position is the same. The difficulty of reconciling the exact figures with the *chachamim's* counter-position, however, remains — and the *Gemara* leaves it with *kashya*.

## Hymenal Formation

### Rupture vs. Formation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת מִכֵּן — כְּנוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הָנֵי בְּתוּלִין — מֵיזָל אָזְלִי וְאָתוּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא אִתְּצוֹדֵי הוּא דְּלָא מִתַּצְדִּי עַד לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** If the girl is **less than that** age, younger than three years and one day old, the status of intercourse with her is not that of intercourse in all halakhic senses; rather, it is **like placing a finger into the eye. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What happens to **this hymen,** i.e., to the hymen of a girl under three with whom a man engaged in intercourse? Does it **disappear and come** back again later, **or perhaps it is not removed at all until after** she reaches the age of **three?**

The *mishna* had employed the phrase *pachos mi-kan ke-noten etzba ba-ayin* — less than three years is like placing a finger in the eye — to characterize why intercourse with a girl under three is not considered a meaningful act of sex. The implication, drawn by juxtaposition, is that it does not permanently rupture the hymen, or that whatever occurs is reversible. The *Gemara* now asks a precise anatomical question about this: *hanei besulim* — the hymen, when ruptured before the age of three, what exactly is its status? *Meizil azli ve-atu* — does it rupture and then grow back? Or, alternatively, *itzulyei hu d'lo mitzadei* — has it not yet sufficiently formed, so that the reason intercourse under three has no lasting effect is that there was no fully developed hymen to rupture in the first place?

### Nafka Mina: Bleeding Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּעַל בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ, וּמָצָא דָּם, וּבָעַל לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ וְלֹא מָצָא דָּם. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מֵיזָל אָזְלִי וְאָתוּ — שְׁהוּת הוּא דְּלָא הָוְיָא לְהוּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: אִתְּצוֹדֵי הוּא דְּלָא מִתַּצְדִּי עַד לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ, הָא אַחֵר בָּא עָלֶיהָ — מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What difference is** there in *halakha* between these two suggestions? The Gemara answers that there is a practical ramification in a case **where** a priest **engaged in intercourse** with a girl to whom he is married **within** her first **three** years, **and found blood** on her due to that intercourse, **and** again **engaged in intercourse** with her many times, including **after** she turned **three, but** on that occasion **he did not find blood. If you say** that after engaging in intercourse when the girl is younger than three, the hymen **disappears and comes** back again, here one can maintain that it disappeared due to the first time they engaged in intercourse and did not grow back because **there was not enough time** without intercourse **for it** to grow back. **But if you say** that the hymen **is not removed** at all **until after** she reaches the age of **three,** the fact that **this** girl did not emit blood after three years must be because **another** man **engaged in intercourse with her** after she turned three, in which case she is classified as a *zona*, a woman who has engaged in sexual intercourse with a man forbidden to her by the Torah, and is forbidden to her husband the priest. The Gemara reiterates: **What,** then, is the resolution of the dilemma?

The *Gemara* asks: *l'mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does this theoretical question make? Either way, the halakhic assumption after the age of three is that the hymen is present. The *Gemara* then constructs a case: a man has intercourse with a girl under three — in a scenario where, for instance, the father had effected a *kiddushin* through *kesef* when she was under three — and there was bleeding. Subsequently, after she turns three, he has intercourse again and there is no bleeding. How do we explain the absence of blood the second time?

If we say the hymen ruptures and grows back — *meizil azli ve-atu* — then we can explain the absence of blood on the second occasion by saying *shehussa lo hava lah*: there was not sufficient time for the hymen to regenerate between the two acts. The lack of blood proves nothing sinister.

If, on the other hand, we say the hymen had not yet formed under three and there was therefore no rupture to begin with, then the blood from the first encounter requires a separate explanation — likely *dam niddah*, menstrual blood. But now the absence of blood after the age of three becomes difficult to explain. The hymen had never been ruptured before, since it did not yet exist. Now that she is three and it has formed, there should be blood. If there is none, the implication is that someone else — *acher ba aleha* — must have had intercourse with her in the interim, rupturing the hymen before this second act. And that conclusion matters: if the husband is a *kohen*, and a man of *pasul* status had intercourse with her in that interval, she becomes *asura* to him.

This discussion proceeds with full awareness — shared by several in the room — of the profound moral horror that underlies it. The *Gemara* deals with these scenarios in an entirely dispassionate, technical register, asking about hymenal formation and the *issur kehunah* consequences without pausing to comment on the acts themselves. The disconnect between the clinical halakhic analysis and the reality of what is being described — sexual acts with girls of two and three years old — is striking, and it bears noting explicitly even if the *Gemara* does not.

### Two Bleeding Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: וּמַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּמַכָּה שֶׁבְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת לְאַלְתַּר? שֶׁמָּא חוֹזֶרֶת לְאַלְתַּר, וְהָא [וַדַּאי] אַחֵר בָּא עָלֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya, son of Rav Ika, objects to this** explanation of the practical ramifications of the dilemma: **But** even if one maintains that the hymen of a girl younger than three disappears and grows back, one can still contend that this girl engaged in intercourse with another man, as **who will say to us that a wound that** was inflicted **within three years** of a girl's birth **is not restored** and healed **immediately? Perhaps it is restored immediately, and this** girl did not emit blood because **another** man **engaged in intercourse with her** previously, and she is therefore a *zona* who is forbidden to a priest.

Rav Chiya the son of Rav Ika objects: this *nafka mina* does not actually distinguish between the two positions. *U-man leima lan d'maka she-betoch gimmel einah chozeret l'altar* — who says that a rupture before the age of three does not grow back immediately? Perhaps the hymen regenerates at once, in which case the absence of blood after three cannot be attributed to insufficient time for regrowth. Under either theory — rupture and regrowth, or non-formation — the absence of blood after three would suggest that someone else had relations with her, and there would be no *nafka mina* between the two positions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ, כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּעַל בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ וּמָצָא דָּם, וּבָעַל לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ וּמָצָא דָּם. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מֵיזָל אָזְלִי וְאָתוּ — הַאי דַּם בְּתוּלִין הוּא, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אִתְּצוֹדֵי הוּא דְּלָא מִתַּצְדִּי אֶלָּא עַד לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ — הַאי דַּם נִדָּה הוּא. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the practical **difference** between the two suggestions relates to a case **where** the husband **engaged in intercourse** with this girl **within** her first **three** years, **and found blood, and engaged in intercourse** with her again **after** she turned **three, and** again **found blood. If you say** that the hymen **disappears and comes** back again, **this** blood emitted when she is less than three years old **is blood** from the tearing of **the hymen,** which does not render her impure. **But if you say** that the hymen **is not removed** at all **until after** she reaches the age of **three,** then **this** blood she emitted when she was younger than three **is menstrual blood,** which renders her impure. **What,** then, is the resolution of the dilemma?

The *Gemara* therefore reframes the distinguishing case: suppose there was intercourse under three and there was bleeding, and then intercourse after three and there was bleeding again. The question now is what to make of the bleeding *under* three. If we say the hymen ruptures and grows back, then the blood under three is *dam betulin* — hymenal blood, since there was indeed something there to rupture. If we say the hymen had not yet formed by under three, then there was nothing to rupture, and the blood under three must be *dam niddah* — with all the attendant *tumah* implications that follow from that.

### Eye Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: תָּא שְׁמַע, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן כְּנוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן. לְמָה לִי לְמִתְנֵי ״כְּנוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן״? לִתְנֵי: פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן וְלֹא כְלוּם! מַאי לַָאו הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: מָה עַיִן מַדְמַעַת, וְחוֹזֶרֶת וּמַדְמַעַת, אַף בְּתוּלִין מֵיזָל אָזְלִי וְאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Come** and **hear** the mishna: If the girl is **less than that** age of three years and one day, intercourse with her is **like placing a finger into the eye. Why do I** need the mishna **to teach: Like placing a finger into the eye? Let** it **teach** simply: If she is **less than that** age, intercourse with her is **nothing. What, is it not** correct that **this** is what the mishna **teaches us,** by its comparison to an eye: **Just as** placing a finger in **an eye** causes it to **tear and tear again,** when another finger is placed in it, **so too** after the intercourse of a girl under three the **hymen disappears and comes** back again?

Rav Chisda resolves the underlying question by directing attention back to the language of the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* compared intercourse with a girl under three to *ke-noten etzba ba-ayin* — like placing a finger in an eye. The *Gemara* asks: why deploy that metaphor at all? Simply say "under three it is nothing" — *pachos mi-kan ve-lo klum*. The elaborate comparison must be teaching something specific. *Mah ayin m'dama'as ve-chozeres u-m'dama'as* — just as an eye, when a finger is placed in it, produces tears, and if the finger is withdrawn and reinserted it will produce tears again, so too the *betulin* — *meizal azli ve-atu* — rupture and grow back. The hymen is present from the outset; under three it ruptures but regenerates, just as the eye continues to produce tears each time it is irritated. This resolves the question: the blood under three is *dam betulin*, and the hymen's regrowth, not its absence, is what the *mishna* is describing.

## Justinian Story (II)

### Pregnancy Age Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּיוֹסְטָנִי, בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל אַסְוִירוּס בֶּן אַנְטֹנִינוּס, שֶׁבָּאתָ לִפְנֵי רַבִּי. אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, אִשָּׁה בְּכַמָּה נִיסֵּת? אָמַר לָהּ: בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving** a gentile woman called **Yusteni, the daughter of Asveirus, son of Antoninus,** a Roman emperor, **who came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **She said to him: My teacher, at what** age is **a woman** fit to **marry,** i.e., at what age is it appropriate for a woman to engage in intercourse, which would therefore be the appropriate time to marry? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to her:** She must be at least **three years and one day old.**

The *Gemara* introduces a narrative: *ma'aseh be-Yostani bito shel Asaverus ben Antoninos* — a story involving the daughter of Severus the son of Antoninus, likely the Roman emperor Severus, though the exact identification was not confirmed. In any case, this is a non-Jewish woman of high standing — perhaps Justina — who came before Rebbe. She posed two questions. First, *isha be-chama nisnis* — at what age does a woman become capable of an actualized marriage and consummation? Rebbe replied: three years and a day. This response is notable because she was not asking a strictly halakhic question about when *kiddushin* takes effect; she was asking something closer to a biological or universal question. That Rebbe answers with the halakhic threshold underscores the extent to which, in the *Gemara's* framework, the halakhic definition of a meaningful act of intercourse is grounded in an understanding of physical reality — specifically, the rupture of the hymen.

### Six-Year Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכַמָּה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת? אָמַר לָהּ: בַּת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד. אָמְרָה לוֹ: אֲנִי נִשֵּׂאתִי בְּשֵׁשׁ, וְיָלַדְתִּי בְּשֶׁבַע. אוֹי לְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים שֶׁאִבַּדְתִּי בְּבֵית אַבָּא!
>
> **Translation:** Yusteni further inquired: **And at what** age is **she** fit to **become pregnant?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to her:** When she is at least **twelve years and one day old. She said to him: I married when** I was **six, and gave birth** a year later, **when** I was **seven. Woe for** those **three years,** between the age of three, when I was fit for intercourse, and the age of six, when I married, **as I wasted** those years **in my father's house** by not engaging in intercourse.

She then asked: *u-ve-chama mitaberet* — at what age can she become pregnant? Rebbe replied: twelve years and a day. To this she responded: *ani nisa'ti be-shesh ve-yaladti be-sheva* — I was married at six and gave birth at seven. I became pregnant at the age of six. *Oi le-shalosh shanim she-ibad'ti be-veis aba* — woe to the three years I wasted in my father's house. In other words, if pregnancy is possible from the age of three — from the moment you have declared that intercourse is meaningful — then she lost three years of potential childbearing by not marrying until she was six.

The precise intent of her remark invites scrutiny. One reading is that she is being subversive — challenging the entire rabbinic framework by pointing to her own experience as evidence that pregnancy is possible well before twelve. On this reading, the closing line about wasted years is laced with irony: if what you told me is true, I could have been pregnant three years sooner. Another reading is that the *Gemara* is placing in the mouth of a woman an embrace of early marriage and early childbirth — presenting her as mourning lost years. Either way, the episode is striking. The narrative puts this statement in a woman's voice without any authorial commentary on its content, and it is difficult to read without discomfort — particularly following the earlier discussion in which men's intercourse with girls as young as two was treated as a technical halakhic category.

## Moch & Birth Control

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מִעַבְּרָה? וְהָתָנֵי רַב בִּיבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת בְּמוֹךְ — קְטַנָּה, מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וּמְנִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And can** a minor of that age **become pregnant? But didn't Rav Beivai teach** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman: Three women** may **engage in intercourse** while **using** a contraceptive absorbent **cloth,** a soft fabric placed at the entrance to the womb to prevent conception, despite the fact that this practice generally is prohibited. They are **a minor; a pregnant woman; and a nursing woman.**

The *Gemara* here introduces a *beraita* taught by Rabbi Bibi before Rav Nachman: *gimel nashim meshameshot be-moch* — three categories of women use a *moch*, a contraceptive barrier device. The *moch* is best understood as something akin to a cervical barrier — closer to what might today be called a female condom or a diaphragm positioned at the cervix — rather than a diaphragm in the modern sense, which sits higher internally and raises fewer concerns about *zera le-vatala*. There is a well-known dispute among the *rishonim* as to whether the *beraita* is prescriptive — these three women *must* use the *moch* — or permissive — these three *may* use it, while all others are forbidden. That dispute carries significant implications for the broader *halacha* of *hashchatat zera* and certain forms of contraception, but this *sugya* is treated at greater length in Yevamot and is mentioned here only in passing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּתְעַבֵּר וְתָמוּת; מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲשֶׂה עוּבָּרָהּ סַנְדָּל; מְנִיקָה — שֶׁמָּא תִּגְמוֹל אֶת בְּנָהּ וְיָמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* specifies the reason for allowing these women to use contraceptive absorbent cloths: **A minor, lest she become pregnant and** perhaps **die** from this pregnancy; **a pregnant woman, lest** she be impregnated a second time and **her** older **fetus become** deformed into the shape of **a sandal** fish, by being squashed by the pressure of the second fetus; and **a nursing woman, lest** she become pregnant and her milk dry up, in which case **she weans her son** too early, thereby endangering him, **and he dies.**

The three women are: *ketana* — a minor, who uses the *moch* because she might otherwise become pregnant and die; *me'uberet* — a pregnant woman, because a second conception could produce a *sandal*, the flattened, compressed fetus the *Gemara* had discussed earlier; and *menika* — a nursing woman, because a new pregnancy might force premature weaning, endangering the child already born.

### Ages 11-12

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹהִי קְטַנָּה? מִבַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד וְעַד שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן, אוֹ יָתֵר עַל כֵּן — מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת [כְּדַרְכָּהּ] וְהוֹלֶכֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And who is** considered **a minor?** It is a girl **from the age of eleven years and one day until** the age of **twelve years and one day.** If she was **younger than that or older than that, she may go ahead and engage in intercourse** in her usual manner, i.e., without contraception. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** Since it is assumed that a minor who is less than eleven years old cannot become pregnant, she is considered to be in no danger.

The *beraita* then specifies what it means by *ketana* in this context: *mi-bat yod-alef shana ve-yom echad ve-ad yod-bet shana ve-yom echad* — from eleven years and a day until twelve years and a day. This is the year immediately before *bat mitzvah*, and according to the *beraita* it is the window during which pregnancy is physiologically possible but physically dangerous. Below eleven, the concern is that she will not become pregnant at all; above twelve, she has passed the *bat mitzvah* threshold — *meshameshet ve-holekhet*, she may have relations without the device, because the danger of a life-threatening pregnancy no longer applies. The risk of pregnancy combined with mortal danger is concentrated precisely in that single intermediate year.

### Meir vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהוֹלֶכֶת, וּמִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יְרַחֲמוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שׁוֹמֵר פְּתָאיִם ה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Both** in **this** case of a minor girl who can become pregnant **and** in **that** case of a minor girl who cannot become pregnant, she **may go ahead and engage in intercourse in her usual manner, and Heaven will have mercy** upon her and prevent any mishap, **as it is stated: "The Lord preserves the simple"** (Psalms 116:6).

This position — that the *moch* must or may be used during this window — is attributed to Rabbi Meir. *Ve-chachamim omrim: achat zo ve-achat zo meshameshet ke-darkah ve-holekhet* — the *chachamim* say that all women, regardless of category, may have relations in their ordinary manner without using the *moch*. Their justification: *u-min ha-shamayim yerachamu*, heaven will have mercy; *she-ne'emar shomer peta'im Hashem*, as it is written, God guards the simple.

This invocation of *shomer peta'im Hashem* raises a serious underlying question about the extent to which *halacha* mandates proactive self-protection. The principle *chamira sakanta me-isura* would suggest that danger to life should be treated with greater stringency than mere prohibitions. Yet at the same time, there are relatively few areas in which *halacha* actively legislates prophylactic health measures when the risk is not absolute. Rav Moshe Feinstein invoked a similar logic when discussing smoking — ruling that it is forbidden to begin but stopping short of obligating those already addicted to quit, implicitly allowing *shomer peta'im Hashem* to do some work. In that context the question is one of legislating individual self-protection; it is more tractable. Here, however, it is not solely the woman's own risk that is at stake — the *chachamim's* position extends to cases involving the nursing infant, and arguably to the minor girl herself, whose life is endangered by the very acts being permitted. The claim that *shomer peta'im* can justify exposing an eleven-year-old girl to mortal danger from pregnancy is, at minimum, a position that demands careful scrutiny, and the *Gemara* does not provide it.

## Justinian Story (II)

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״אֲשֶׁר בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹרִים בְּשָׂרָם״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״אֲשֶׁר פִּיהֶם דִּבֶּר שָׁוְא וִימִינָם יְמִין שָׁקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that Yusteni was able to become pregnant at such a young age because she was a gentile, and the verse states with regard to gentiles: **"Their flesh is the flesh of donkeys"** (Ezekiel 23:20). **And if you wish, say** instead that Yusteni was lying when she said she became pregnant at age seven, as it is stated with regard to gentiles: **"Whose mouth speaks falsehood, and their right hand is a right hand of lying"** (Psalms 144:8).

Having established that under eleven, pregnancy does not occur, the *Gemara* returns to the story of Justina and asks: *u-mi me'abra* — could she really have become pregnant at six? The *Gemara* offers two answers. *Iba'it eima*: *asher besar chamorim besaram* — their flesh is the flesh of donkeys, a reference to Ezekiel's characterization of non-Jews as constitutionally different from Jews. On this reading, non-Jews develop differently and may become capable of pregnancy at younger ages; thus no proof can be drawn from Justina's account to challenge the Jewish halakhic standard. *Ve-iba'it eima*: *asher pihem diber shav ve-yiminam yemin shaker* — their mouths speak falsehood and their right hand is a right hand of lying. She was simply not telling the truth.

These two answers sit uncomfortably. The *Gemara* is, in effect, rejecting an empirical claim by saying either that non-Jews are biologically akin to animals or that the woman is lying. This reflects a broader feature of ancient intellectual culture — not unique to the rabbis — in which authoritative texts determined reality, and observed phenomena that conflicted with received wisdom were explained away rather than taken as grounds to revise the theory. Just as a physician trained on Galen would interpret an anomalous body as a deviation from the norm rather than as evidence against Galen, the *Gemara* here subordinates the empirical testimony to the halakhic framework. The specific language used to dismiss her account, however — *besar chamorim besaram* or flat-out lying — carries implications that go well beyond methodological conservatism, and they do not reflect well when read without the filter of apologetics.

## Woman & Rabbi Akiva

### Kohen Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת שֶׁבָּאת לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, נִבְעַלְתִּי בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, מָה אֲנִי לִכְהוּנָּה? אָמַר לָהּ: כְּשֵׁרָה אַתְּ לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving a certain woman who came before Rabbi Akiva** and **said to him: My teacher, I engaged in intercourse within three years** of my birth; **what** is **my** status with regard to marrying **into the priesthood?** Rabbi Akiva **said to her: You are fit** to marry **into the priesthood.**

The *Gemara* presents another narrative: *ma'aseh be-isha achat she-ba'as lifnei Rabbi Akiva* — a woman came before Rabbi Akiva. *Nivalti be-toch shalosh shanim* — I had intercourse with a man when I was under three years old. *Mah ani le-kehunah* — what is my status with respect to marrying a *kohen*? The halakhic background: a woman does not become *pesulah le-kehunah* simply by virtue of premarital intercourse. She becomes *pesulah* only if the man involved is someone with whom *kiddushin* cannot take effect — such as a non-Jew — or if he is personally of *pasul* status, such as a *mamzer* or *chalal*. Apparently, the man in question was of such a category. Rabbi Akiva told her: *ke-sheira le-kehunah* — you are fit to marry a *kohen*, because the act occurred before the age of three and is therefore not considered a halakhically meaningful act of intercourse.

### Honey Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְתִינוֹק שֶׁטָּמְנוּ לוֹ אֶצְבָּעוֹ בִּדְבַשׁ, פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה גּוֹעֵר בָּהּ, שְׁלִישִׁית מֹצְצָהּ. אָמַר לָהּ: אִם כֵּן, פְּסוּלָה אַתְּ לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him: My teacher, I will tell you a parable; to what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a baby whose finger** one forcibly **dipped in honey.** On the **first time and** the **second** time, **he moans at** his mother for doing so, but on the **third** occasion, once he is used to the taste of honey, he willingly **sucks** the finger dipped in honey. She was insinuating to Rabbi Akiva that she engaged in intercourse several times, and although the first couple of times were against her will, the third incident was with her consent. Rabbi Akiva **said to her: If so, you are disqualified** from marrying **into the priesthood.**

The woman then offered a *mashal*: *l'tinok she-tamnu lo etzba'o be-dvash* — consider an infant whose finger is placed in honey. *Pa'am rishonah u-shniyah go'al bah* — the first and second times, it is repulsive to him. *Shlishit matzetzah* — by the third time, he sucks it and enjoys it. Her point was that although the first encounters were distressing, eventually the experience became pleasurable. How, she asked, can you say it was not an act of sex if it ultimately became a pleasurable and meaningful experience for me, even when I was under three?

The metaphor maps onto something known about the psychology of abuse — that victims can, over time, develop responses that complicate the straightforward characterization of early experiences as simply nothing. The *Gemara* records this without comment. Rabbi Akiva's initial response, however, was sharp: *im kein, pesulah le-kehunah* — if that is your position, then you are indeed *pesulah*, because you are asking that we treat it as an act of sex.

### Halacha Lmoshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאָה הַתַּלְמִידִים מִסְתַּכְּלִים זֶה בָּזֶה, אָמַר לָהֶם: לָמָה הַדָּבָר קָשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיכֶם? [אָמְרוּ לוֹ]: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁכׇּל הַתּוֹרָה הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי, כָּךְ פְּחוּתָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים כְּשֵׁרָה לַכְּהוּנָּה הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. וְאַף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לֹא אֲמָרָהּ אֶלָּא לְחַדֵּד בָּהּ אֶת הַתַּלְמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **saw** his **students looking at each other,** puzzling over this ruling. **He said to them: Why is this matter difficult in your eyes? They said to him: Just as the entire Torah is a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai, so too** this *halakha* of a girl who engaged in intercourse when she was **less than three years old,** i.e., that she is **fit** to marry **into the priesthood,** is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai,** and it applies whether she engaged intercourse against her will or with her consent. The Gemara notes: **And even Rabbi Akiva did not say** to the woman that she was unfit to marry into the priesthood because that is the *halakha*; rather, he did so **only to sharpen** the minds of his **students with** his statement, to see how they would respond.

The students were visibly astonished — *talmidim mistaklim zeh ba-zeh* — apparently troubled by the reversal. There then follows a statement — whether from the students back to Rabbi Akiva or from Rabbi Akiva to the students is somewhat unclear from the text, though reading it as a student response better resolves the flow — *ke-sheim she-kol ha-Torah kulah halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai, kach pe-chutah mi-bat shalosh shanim ke-sheira le-kehunah halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: just as the entire Torah is a tradition from Moses at Sinai, so too the rule that intercourse with a girl under three is not considered an act of sex is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. The halakhic threshold is a formal, objective category — a *shiur* — and it does not depend on the subjective experience of the parties involved. What the woman felt, or eventually came to feel, is simply not the relevant criterion. As with all such quantitative thresholds in *halacha*, the line is fixed and not negotiable through personal testimony about inner states.

*Ve-af Rabbi Akiva lo amar ela le-chaded bah et ha-talmidim* — the *Gemara* concludes that even Rabbi Akiva's sharp reversal was intended only to sharpen the students' thinking, not to rule that she was genuinely *pesulah*. The episode thus ends by reasserting the original ruling — she is *ke-sheira le-kehunah* — while using the rhetorical challenge as a pedagogical tool.

## Boys: Nine Years

### Nine-Year-Old Boy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, שֶׁבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ — קְנָאָהּ, וְאֵין נוֹתֵן גֵּט עַד שֶׁיַּגְדִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a boy, **nine years and one day old,** whose brother had died childless, **who engaged in intercourse with his *yevama*,** his brother's widow, the status of the intercourse is that of halakhic intercourse and **he acquires her** as his wife; **but he cannot give** her **a bill of divorce,** if he chooses to end the marriage, **until he reaches majority.**

The *Gemara* now shifts to boys. *Ben tisha shanim ve-yom echad* — nine years and a day — is the threshold at which a boy's act of intercourse carries halakhic weight. This applies in several distinct domains.

### Kiddushin vs. Yibbum

One area is *yibbum* — levirate marriage. If the boy's brother dies leaving a childless widow, the obligation of *yibbum* applies, and it can be actualized through the act of intercourse. The reason a minor can accomplish *yibbum* through *bi'ah*, even though he cannot effect *kiddushin* until he is an adult at thirteen, lies in the fundamental nature of each institution. *Kiddushin* requires a conscious, deliberate act to create a new halakhic status — and for that, the actor must be a full adult with the legal capacity to perform binding acts. *Yibbum*, by contrast, is not the creation of a new status *ex nihilo*. It is understood as a continuation of the deceased brother's marriage: the surviving brother has an existing, legally recognized *zika* — connection — to the widow, and the act of *bi'ah* merely actualizes that pre-existing relationship. Since it is the actualization of something already present rather than an originating act, the minor's *bi'ah*, once it is considered a meaningful act of sex at nine years and a day, is sufficient to accomplish it.

### Yibbum Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְטַמֵּא בְּנִדָּה לְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב תַּחְתּוֹן כָּעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And he becomes ritually impure** after engaging in intercourse **with a menstruating woman to** the degree that he **renders impure** all the layers of **bedding beneath** him, such that they become impure **like the upper** bedding covering a *zav*. Accordingly, the bedding assumes first-degree ritual impurity status and does not become a primary source of ritual impurity, and it renders impure food and drink and does not render impure people and vessels.

Several halakhic consequences follow from the nine-year-old's act of *yibbum*. *Ve-eino noten get ad she-yagdil* — he cannot issue a *get* until he reaches adulthood, because giving a *get* is an independent legal act requiring full majority. *U-metame be-niddah* — if he has intercourse with a *niddah*, he acquires the status of *boel niddah*: he generates *tumah* such that his *mishkav* — the surface he lies on — is a *rishon le-tumah* rather than an *av ha-tumah*, comparable to what a *zav* would produce rather than a *niddah* herself. This *tumah* status applies once he is nine and a day; younger than that, the act is not considered intercourse and the relevant *tumah* does not attach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵל, וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל בַּתְּרוּמָה, וּפוֹסֵל אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְנִסְקֶלֶת עַל יָדוֹ, וְאִם בָּא עַל אַחַת מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה — מוּמָתִין עַל יָדוֹ, וְהוּא פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if he is disqualified from the priesthood and the woman with whom he engages in intercourse is the daughter of a priest, **he disqualifies** her from partaking of *teruma*; **but** if he is a priest who marries an Israelite woman, **he does not enable** her **to partake of *teruma*. And** if he engages in bestiality, **he disqualifies the animal from** being sacrificed **upon the altar, and** the animal **is stoned due to his** act. **And if he engaged in intercourse with one of all those with whom relations are forbidden,** as **stated in the Torah,** e.g., his aunt or his mother, **they are executed** by the court **due to** having engaged in intercourse with **him,** because they are adults; **but he is exempt,** as he is a minor.

*U-fosel ve-eino machil be-trumah* — if the boy is himself of *pasul* status, such as a *chalal*, then his act of intercourse at nine and a day *fosel* the woman, rendering her *pesulah le-kehunah*. But even after the valid act of *yibbum* has been performed, the *yevamah* — the sister-in-law — cannot yet eat *trumah* by virtue of her marriage to him. There is an important dispute between Rashi and Tosafot about why this is so: the marriage has been effected through *bi'ah*, yet because he is a minor, the marriage is in some sense incomplete. Although *kiddushin* of a girl under three through *bi'ah* is fully valid even for a girl of that age, the question here is whether the minority of the *husband* prevents the marriage from attaining its full legal force — and the inability to eat *trumah* suggests that it does not yet constitute the complete actualized state of marriage.

Similarly, *posel et ha-behemah* — if at nine years and a day the boy has intercourse with an animal, it is considered an act of bestiality. The animal becomes disqualified for use as a *korban* and is subject to *sekilah* — stoning — even though the boy himself is exempt from punishment as a minor.

If a boy of nine years and a day has intercourse with any of the *arayot* enumerated in the Torah — a married woman, his mother, his sister, and the like — *mumassim al yado* — the woman is subject to the death penalty, *ve-hu patur* — and he is exempt, being a minor. This is the precise mirror of the rule established in the prior *mishna*: when a man has intercourse with an underage girl and it is an act of *arayot*, the man is liable and the girl is not. In both cases, only the adult is punishable; the minor is exempt.

## Get & Chalitzah

### Maamar Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' וְלִכְשֶׁיַּגְדִּיל. בְּגֵט סַגִּי לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע כְּמַאֲמָר בַּגָּדוֹל. מָה מַאֲמָר בַּגָּדוֹל — צָרִיךְ גֵּט לְמַאֲמָרוֹ וַחֲלִיצָה לְזִיקָּתוֹ, אַף בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע — צָרִיךְ גֵּט לְמַאֲמָרוֹ וַחֲלִיצָה לְזִיקָּתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a boy aged nine years and one day cannot give his *yevama* a bill of divorce until he reaches majority. The Gemara asks: **And** even **when he reaches majority, is a bill of divorce enough to** enable **her** to marry any man? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the Sages **rendered the** halakhic status of the act of **intercourse of** a boy **nine** years and one day **old like** that of **levirate betrothal** by means of money or a document performed **by an adult man,** which is an acquisition by rabbinic law? Accordingly, she is not his full-fledged wife. Therefore, one can assert as follows: **Just as** after **a levirate betrothal** performed **by an adult man,** the *yavam* **must** give the *yevama* **a bill of divorce to** release her from **his levirate betrothal and** perform ***ḥalitza* to** release her from **his** levirate **bond, so too** with regard to **the intercourse of** a boy **nine** years and one day **old,** the *halakha* should be that he **must** give her **a bill of divorce for his levirate betrothal and** perform ***ḥalitza* to** release her from **his** levirate **bond.**

The *Gemara* now addresses what happens when the nine-year-old, having performed *yibbum*, reaches adulthood. *Get sagi lah* — is a *get* alone sufficient? To understand the question, the *Gemara* introduces the parallel institution of *maamar* — the quasi-*kiddushin* that one of the *yevamim* can perform with the widow as a way of locking in his intention to do *yibbum* before the actual act takes place. A *maamar* is not binding in the full sense — only *bi'ah* can fully actualize *yibbum* — but it has sufficient halakhic weight that reversing it requires both a *get*, to undo the *maamar* itself, and *chalitzah*, to sever the underlying biblical *zika*. The *get* addresses the rabbinic act of *maamar*; the *chalitzah* addresses the real biblical obligation that has not yet been actualized through intercourse.

The *Gemara* then draws the analogy: *af bi'as ben tes ke-maamar be-gadol* — the intercourse of a nine-year-old is halakhically comparable to a *maamar* performed by an adult. Just as *maamar* requires both a *get* and *chalitzah* to unwind, so too the *bi'ah* of the minor *yavam* — if he wishes to dissolve the marriage before reaching adulthood — requires both a *get* for the *bi'ah* itself and *chalitzah* for the residual *zika* that the *bi'ah* has not fully resolved.

### Adult Actualization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הָכִי קָאָמַר, לִכְשֶׁיַּגְדִּיל יִבְעוֹל וְיִתֵּן גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said** in response that **this** is what the *tanna* of the mishna **is saying: When he reaches majority he may engage in intercourse with her,** and thereby acquire her as his full-fledged wife, **and** if he wished to divorce her **he** can then **give her a bill of divorce** without having to perform *ḥalitza*.

Rav explains the *mishna's* statement that a *get* is sufficient: *le-cheshe-yagdil yavo ve-yiten get* — the meaning is that once he grows up, he should first have relations again as an adult, thereby fully actualizing the *yibbum*, and only then give a *get*. Once the adult act has occurred, the *yibbum* is complete in every respect, the *zika* has been fully actualized, and a *get* alone is sufficient to dissolve the marriage. If, however, he seeks to dissolve the marriage while still relying only on the act performed as a minor, the *chalitzah* requirement remains, because the minority of the actor left something incomplete in the actualization of the *yibbum*. The inability to eat *trumah*, noted earlier, is itself evidence that the marriage was not fully actualized — which in turn explains why the *zika* is not fully discharged and *chalitzah* is still needed alongside the *get*.

## Mufla & Adulthood

### Ages 11-12-13

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — נְדָרֶיהָ נִבְדָּקִין. בַּת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — נְדָרֶיהָ קַיָּימִין, וּבוֹדְקִין כׇּל שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה. בֶּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — נְדָרָיו נִבְדָּקִין, בֶּן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — נְדָרָיו קַיָּימִין, וּבוֹדְקִין כׇּל שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a girl who is **eleven years and one day old, her vows are examined** to ascertain whether she is aware of the meaning of her vow and in Whose name she vowed. Once she is **twelve years and one day old** and has grown two pubic hairs, which is a sign of adulthood, even without examination **her vows are in effect. And one examines** her vows throughout **the entire twelfth** year until her twelfth birthday. With regard to a boy who is **twelve years and one day old, his vows are examined** to ascertain whether he is aware of the meaning of his vow and in Whose name he vowed. Once he is **thirteen years and one day old** and has grown two pubic hairs, even without examination **his vows are in effect. And one examines** his vows throughout **the entire thirteenth** year until his thirteenth birthday.

The *Gemara* now turns to the age of majority as it pertains to vows and sanctifications. The *mishna* establishes: *bat achas esreh shanah ve-yom echad* — eleven years and a day — *nedareha nivdakim*: if a girl of this age makes a *neder*, her vow is examined to determine whether she understood what she was doing, and if she did, the vow is binding even though she has not yet reached *bat mitzvah*. *Bat sheteim esreh shanah ve-yom echad* — twelve years and a day — *nedareha kayamim*: her vows are binding without examination, because she is now a full adult. The *mishna* adds that throughout the twelfth year — *uvechol sheteim esreh* — one remains prepared to examine her vows at any point, since her understanding may develop over the course of that year. The parallel structure applies to boys: examination at twelve years and a day, full binding force at thirteen years and a day, with the entire thirteenth year treated as a period of ongoing examination.

### Mufla Year

The halakhic status of the year before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* has a formal name: *mufla samuch le-ish* — one who is *mufla*, capable of *hafla'ah* (verbal acts of sanctification or vowing), in close proximity to adulthood. The term *mufla* derives from *hafla'ah*, the class of verbal acts — vows, dedications, sanctifications — that this transitional status governs. There is a tradition, treated as *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, that precisely in this year, if the minor demonstrates genuine understanding of what he or she is doing, such acts take effect. Outside of this context, the acts of a minor are simply null.

### Pre-Mufla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹדֶם לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: ״יוֹדְעִין אָנוּ לְשֵׁם מִי נָדַרְנוּ״, ״לְשֵׁם מִי הִקְדַּשְׁנוּ״ — אֵין נִדְרֵיהֶם נֶדֶר וְאֵין הֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Prior to that time,** eleven years and one day for a girl and twelve years and one day for a boy, **even if they said: We know in Whose name we vowed** and **in Whose name we consecrated, their vow is not** a valid **vow and their consecration is not** a valid **consecration.**

*Kodem la-zman ha-zeh* — prior to the *mufla* year, more than a year before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — even if the minor explicitly claims full knowledge and awareness (*yodim anu le-shem mi nadarnu u-le-shem mi hikdashnu*), the claim is disregarded. *Ein nidrehem neder, ve-ein hekdesham hekdesh* — their vows are not vows and their *hekdesh* is not *hekdesh*. The acts of a minor in this period carry no legal weight, regardless of the minor's claimed level of understanding.

### Post-Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר הַזְּמַן הַזֶּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְשֵׁם מִי נָדַרְנוּ״, ״לְשֵׁם מִי הִקְדַּשְׁנוּ״ — נִדְרָן נֶדֶר וְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **After that time,** twelve years and one day for a girl and thirteen years and one day for a boy, **even if they said: We do not know in Whose name we vowed** and **in Whose name we consecrated, their vow is** a valid **vow and their consecration is** a valid **consecration.**

Conversely, *le-achar zman ha-zeh* — after *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — even if the adult explicitly states *ein anu yodim le-shem mi nadarnu u-le-shem mi hikdashnu*, disclaiming any knowledge of what they were doing when they made the vow or the *hekdesh*, that claim is ineffective. *Nidrehem neder ve-hekdesham hekdesh* — the acts are fully binding. Once one has reached the age of majority, one cannot escape the legal consequences of one's verbal acts by claiming ignorance.

### Rashi Response

Rashi addresses what might seem puzzling about the post-*mitzvah* rule. Why should an adult's claim of ignorance be dismissed entirely? Rashi explains: *gedolim heim, u-vi-chlal shoteh nami lo machzikin lehu* — they are adults, and we do not categorize them as a *shoteh*. The term *shoteh* in its technical halakhic sense refers not to someone with a cognitive disability or limited intelligence, but to someone whose grip on reality is fundamentally impaired — the one who tears his clothing, sleeps in a cemetery, or wanders alone at night. *Desofa lavo li-chlal da'at* — eventually, these young adults will fully develop their mature understanding. They are not permanently without *da'at*; they are simply still developing. The claim of ignorance at thirteen is therefore not credited, because the law presumes that the normal developmental trajectory will produce adult *da'at*, and that trajectory is already underway.

This raises a genuinely challenging question with which the *Gemara* leaves us. The general principle of *devarim she-be-lev einam devarim* — unexpressed subjective intent does not override the formal content of an act — applies in contractual and halakhic contexts broadly. Yet there is a recognized exception for *devarim she-be-lev kol adam* — assumptions so self-evidently shared that they may be read into any transaction. One might then ask: if a thirteen-year-old genuinely and demonstrably lacked the requisite *da'at* when making a vow or *hekdesh*, should that not matter? The *mishna* seems to say no — once the threshold is crossed, the status is fixed. Rashi's explanation — we presume *da'at* will come and is already forming — is a presumption, and like all presumptions, one might wonder what becomes of it when the facts are unambiguously to the contrary. The *mishna* may be suggesting that the threshold is absolute, and that a demonstrated lack of understanding in a particular case is simply not a recognized category once majority is reached. Whether that holds even when the absence of *da'at* is manifest, rather than merely claimed, remains an open question that the *Gemara* will continue to explore.
