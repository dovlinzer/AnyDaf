# Hullin 134 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Female Kohanim & Gifts

### Female Status

Before turning to the new daf, two points from yesterday's discussion deserve brief summary. The first concerns the twenty-four *matnot kehuna* and the surprising inclusion of the skins of the *korbanot* among the gifts eaten in Yerushalayim. Even the skin of an *olah* is given to the *kohanim*, yet we have never heard that this is limited to Yerushalayim. When the Rambam addresses this, he describes it as a gift *ba min hamikdash* — it comes from the Temple — but does not say it may only be used in Yerushalayim. One might suggest that ideally the *kohanim* would keep it there as a matter of respect, though the Rambam stops short of requiring it.

The second point concerns the status of female *kohanim* — *kohanos* — and female *leviyot* in receiving priestly gifts. The Gemara had emphasized, following Rashi, that *zroa lechayim vekeiva* carries no *kedusha*, and therefore the restrictions that apply to *kedusha*-bearing gifts — which belong specifically to male *kohanim* and their immediate households — do not govern it. A *kohenet* is entitled to *zroa lechayim vekeiva* and *reishit hagez* by virtue of her own personal status, not merely as a member of her father's household. This holds even if she is married to a non-*kohen*, even to a *Yisrael* — as the Gemara made explicit — since it is her own priestly identity, not her family affiliation, that creates the entitlement.

The Rambam extends this principle to a *leviyah* for *maaser*, since *maaser* likewise carries no *kedusha*, and a *leviyah* is independently entitled to it. The same logic applies to the question raised about *pidyon bechor*: since the Rambam groups all *bechor*-related gifts together, and since the *bechor* of an animal is restricted to male *kohanim* on account of its *korban* status, the *pidyon bechor* — both of a firstborn son and of a *peter chamor* — follows the same classification, even though those latter gifts carry no independent *kedusha*. The Rambam confirms this explicitly. The organizing principle is clear: gifts bearing *kedusha* follow the Torah's emphasis on male *kohanim* and their households; gifts without *kedusha* open the door to the independent status of *kohanos* and *leviyot*.

### Rambam on Terumah

The Rambam's position becomes considerably more striking when it comes to gifts that do carry *kedusha*. One might have expected that even if a *kohenet* is entitled to non-*kedusha* gifts in her own right, gifts like *terumah*, *terumas maaser*, and *challah* would remain the exclusive domain of male *kohanim* and their families. The Rambam, however, rules that a person may give *terumah* and *challah* directly to a *kohenet* — provided she is unmarried, since a *kohenet* married to a *Yisrael* cannot eat *terumah*. This has left commentators astounded, as it appears to run against numerous passages elsewhere in the Talmud.

The way the later authorities reconcile this is to note that the Rambam must be speaking of an unmarried *kohenet*, one who has no impediment to eating *terumah*. But even granting that qualification, it remains a remarkable extension: the Rambam is saying that even where the gift is a *kedusha*-bearing item like *terumah* or *challah*, a *kohenet* is entitled to receive it directly on the strength of her own status, not merely as part of her father's priestly household. Concretely, a person could, according to the Rambam, give his *terumah* to an unmarried *kohenet* as a direct fulfillment of the obligation. The same principle applies to a *leviyah* for *maaser*, which the Rambam states definitively, though there the absence of *kedusha* makes the case less surprising.

### Kedusha & Recipients

The underlying framework that ties these rulings together is the distinction between gifts that carry *kedusha* and those that do not. Gifts with *kedusha* — *korbanot*, *bechor* of animals, *terumah* when the question of eating is implicated, and the like — place particular weight on the category of male *kohanim* and their immediate families, as the Torah's formulations emphasize. Gifts without *kedusha*, including *zroa lechayim vekeiva*, *reishit hagez*, *pidyon bechor*, *pidyon peter chamor*, and *maaser* for *leviyot*, carry no such restriction, and the personal status of a *kohenet* or *leviyah* is sufficient to establish entitlement in her own right.

One further nuance emerges from the connection the Rambam draws between *zroa lechayim vekeiva* and *terumah* appearing in the same *pasuk*. The Rambam seems to read the juxtaposition as supporting the principle that wherever personal status suffices — wherever there is no overriding reason to restrict the gift to the male priestly household — the *kohenet* is entitled directly. It remains a daring ruling, and the commentators continue to wrestle with it, but the Rambam's internal logic is coherent.

## Partial Ownership

### Head vs. Body Split

The Gemara now poses the following question at the very bottom of 133b. A *kohen* who owns an animal says to a *Yisrael*: "The head is yours and the rest is mine." The *Yisrael* thus acquires only the head of the animal, and it is precisely in the head that the *lechayayim* — the jaw — is found. Does the *Yisrael* have to give that *matana* to a *kohen*? He owns only the part of the animal that contains the *matana*, but the main body of the animal belongs to the *kohen*.

This is the mirror image of the case discussed the previous day, where a *kohen* owning even a partial share of the *matana* portion was sufficient to exempt the *Yisrael*. Here the question runs in the opposite direction: does the *Yisrael*'s ownership of the specific portion that contains the *matana* — and only that portion — create an obligation to give it?

### Batar Chiyuva vs. Ikar

The Gemara frames the question as a dispute between two principles. *Batar chiyuva azlinan*: we follow where the obligation is located — that is, where the *matana* itself resides, which is in the *Yisrael*'s portion. If so, the *Yisrael* who owns that portion is obligated. Or *batar ikar behema azlinan*: we follow the main animal — the principal part of the *behema* belongs to the *kohen*, and therefore there is no obligation on the *Yisrael* who holds only a secondary portion.

The question is not merely technical. It turns on how we conceptualize the nature of the obligation: does it attach to whoever holds the specific anatomical gift, or does it require a relationship to the animal as a whole?

### Reishit Hagez Proof

The Gemara brings a proof from *reishit hagez*. If a *kohen* or a non-Jew gives his sheep to a *Yisrael* to shear, the *Yisrael* is exempt from giving *reishit hagez*, because the animal is not his — the obligation follows the owner of the sheep. And if a *Yisrael* purchases only the shearings — only the wool — without owning the sheep itself, he is likewise exempt. This establishes that by *reishit hagez*, the obligation depends on ownership of the whole animal, not merely ownership of the product. The principle operating there is *batar ikar behema*: you must own the animal, not just the gift that comes from it.

### Zroa Proof

But the Gemara then cites a *ברייתא* that teaches a contrast: *veze chamur bizroa uvelchayim uvekeiva yoter mireshit hagez* — there is a greater stringency by *zroa lechayim vekeiva* than by *reishit hagez*. The implication is that by *zroa lechayim vekeiva*, owning just the portion that contains the gifts is sufficient to create an obligation, even without owning the main animal.

### Zroa Resolution

The Gemara concludes: *shema mina batar chiyuva azlinan* — we learn from this that by *zroa lechayim vekeiva*, the operative principle is *batar chiyuva*, we follow where the obligation is located. The *Yisrael* who owns only the head — the portion that contains the *lechayayim* — is therefore obligated to give that *matana*, even though the main body of the animal belongs to the *kohen*. By *reishit hagez* the rule is different; there one must own the full animal. The two obligations are thus governed by distinct principles.

## Conditional Sale

### Chutz Exclusion

The Gemara now turns to a set of cases involving conditional language in the sale of an animal. The *Mishnah* states: if a *kohen* sells his animal to a *Yisrael* and explicitly says *chutz min hamatanot* — "except for the gifts" — meaning he is transferring ownership of the animal but retaining ownership of the *zroa lechayim vekeiva* — then the *Yisrael* does not have to give those gifts. He never acquired them.

### Al Menat Condition

Against this, the Gemara cites a *baraita* that appears to rule otherwise. If a *kohen* sells an animal to a *Yisrael* and stipulates *al menat shehamatnot sheli* — "on the condition that the gifts are mine" — the buyer may disregard the stipulation and give the gifts to whichever *kohen* he wishes. The *Yisrael* is not bound by the condition.

### Chutz vs. Al Menat

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing the legal structure of the two formulations. *Chutz* operates as a *shiyura* — a reservation. When the *kohen* says "except for the gifts," he is not transferring ownership of that portion to begin with. He is selling most of the animal while leaving part of it in his own possession, which is a perfectly valid mode of partial sale. The *Yisrael* never owns the *matanot*, so there is nothing to give.

*Al menat*, by contrast, is not a reservation. The *kohen* using *al menat* is selling the entire animal — lock, stock, and barrel — but attaching a condition to the complete sale. The animal changes hands entirely; the *kohen* is then attempting to impose a subsequent obligation on the buyer.

### Rabbinic Prevention

Why doesn't that condition hold? After all, contractual conditions are generally enforceable, and *Tosafot* notes that if a sale is made *al menat* and the condition is not fulfilled, the entire sale should arguably be voided. Some *Rishonim* accept that in principle the *Yisrael* is bound by the condition — but the Rabbis will not enforce it. The reason is the norm established earlier in the tractate: a *kohen* is not permitted to walk around demanding the gifts or positioning himself as entitled to receive them from a specific slaughter. The whole point of the obligation is that it is the *Yisrael*'s prerogative to decide which *kohen* to give the gifts to. A *kohen* who stipulates *al menat* is playing an entitlement game — using the language of contractual conditions to effectively demand what he should only be able to receive — and the Rabbis will not allow the machinery of contract law to be used toward that end.

### Ritva's View

The Ritva offers a different and more technical explanation. He argues that the outcome depends on the precise formulation. If the *kohen* had said *al menat shetitten li* — "on the condition that you give them to me" — that would be a real condition demanding a specific act, and the buyer would be obligated to fulfill it. But the formulation in our case is *al menat shehamatnot sheli* — "on the condition that they are mine." This is not a condition demanding action; it is a claim of fact. It is, in effect, a declaration that the *matanot* already belong to the *kohen* — which is either meaningless or factually incorrect, since the *matanot* do not yet belong to him. At most, he might receive them; they are not his as a matter of right. The Ritva therefore reads the stipulation as self-defeating: it purports to establish a factual state of ownership that does not exist, and therefore cannot be treated as a valid condition.

One might push back and say the statement sounds like an assertion of entitlement — a claim that the *kohen* retains control over them. But that is precisely the problem: the *kohen* has no such control, and if the stipulation were taken to its logical conclusion, it would mean the condition was never satisfied and the sale never went through. The cleaner explanation, and arguably the more satisfying one halachically, remains the first: the Rabbis simply will not permit this form of entitlement-claiming.

## Tannaitic Debate

### Opinion 1: Retention

The Gemara notes that there is in fact a Tannaitic dispute about the *al menat* formulation. The first opinion holds that *al menat* in this context means: I am not selling the *matanot*. Under this reading, the phrase functions similarly to *chutz* — it expresses the *kohen*'s intent not to transfer ownership of the gifts. Since he is understood to be retaining them, the *Yisrael* acquires the animal without the *matanot*, and the *kohen* keeps them.

### Opinion 2: Stipulation

The second opinion holds that *al menat* means precisely what it says: I am selling the whole animal but imposing a condition. Under this reading, the sale is complete and includes the *matanot*, but the *kohen* is attempting to attach an obligation to the buyer. As discussed, this stipulation is not enforced — the Rabbis decline to allow it to operate as a binding condition.

## Intestines Case

### Price Determination

The *Mishnah* concludes with the case of someone who buys the intestines from a butcher. If the buyer purchased all the intestines at a flat price, the *keivah* — the stomach — is included in what he acquired, and he must give it to a *kohen*. He receives no discount from the butcher, since he agreed to a fixed price and the stomach was part of the package; he should have known. If, however, he bought by weight, he still gives the stomach to the *kohen*, but he is entitled to a discount from the butcher equivalent to the weight of the *keivah*, since the butcher charged him for something he would never be able to keep.

### Rav: Butcher Liability

Rav qualifies this rule: *lo shanu ela sheshakal l'atzmo* — the distinction between flat price and by weight applies only if the buyer himself weighed out the intestines. But if the butcher did the weighing, then the *kohen* may deal with the butcher directly — *hadin im hatabach*. In that case, the butcher placed the stomach on the scale and charged for it, effectively appropriating something that belonged to the class of *kohanim*.

## Theft of Matanot

### Ramban: Benefit

This raises an immediate difficulty. Throughout the preceding discussion, the Gemara established that one cannot collect compensation for damaging *matnot kehuna* because they are *mamon she'ein lo tov'im* — money with no specific claimant who can sue. How then can a *kohen* go to the butcher?

The Ramban draws a crucial distinction. There was a case discussed earlier where the king's officers seized the gifts to satisfy debts, and there the ruling was that the owner must compensate the *kohen*. The reason it differs from ordinary damage is that the person who appropriated the *matanot* derived direct financial benefit from them. The benefit is still sitting, so to speak, in his account. The butcher did not merely destroy the stomach — he sold it and collected payment for it. That monetary gain belongs to the *kohanim*, and because it represents concrete, present benefit that was extracted from something not his to sell, he must give it back. It is not the same category as *mazik matnot kehuna*, where one simply diminishes or destroys something with no standing owner. Here there is ill-gotten gain, and that gain can be demanded.

The question of who exactly goes to the butcher remains somewhat open. The Ramban's framing suggests it is not a conventional lawsuit — *mamon she'ein lo tov'im* still means no specific *kohen* can compel the butcher in court. But the butcher bears the moral and legal responsibility. One possibility is that the case involves *makirei kehuna* — a known *kohen* to whom the gifts were designated — who would then have standing to demand compensation. Alternatively, a *kohen* might be able to demand it without being able to formally litigate it. The phrase *hadin im hatabach* — the law is with the butcher — sounds as though there is actionable liability, even if the Ramban's analysis moderates what that means in practice.

## Intestines Case…

### Rav Ashi: Buyer Only

Rav Ashi disagrees with Rav. Even if it was the butcher who weighed out the intestines, the sole responsibility rests with the buyer — *hadin im ba'al habayit*. The buyer is the one who now possesses the *keivah*, and it is he alone who must give it to the *kohen* or bear accountability for it. The butcher's role in the transaction does not shift the obligation to him.

## Theft of Matanot…

### Rav Chisda Rule

The Gemara now attempts to frame the dispute between Rav and Rav Ashi as a debate about Rav Chisda's principle. Rav Chisda rules: if someone stole an object and the original owners have not yet despaired of recovering it, and a second party then came and consumed or destroyed it — *ratza mizeh goveh ratza mizeh goveh* — the owners may collect from either the thief or the consumer, since each one committed a distinct wrong against them. The thief removed the object from their possession; the consumer destroyed it. The Gemara initially suggests that Rav follows Rav Chisda — the butcher who appropriated the stomach can be pursued, just as a thief can — while Rav Ashi rejects Rav Chisda and says only the person currently holding the object can be pursued.

### Abstract Ownership

The Gemara then complicates this picture by raising a more fundamental question: can *matnot kehuna* be stolen at all? This question has two distinct layers. First, does a *kohen* even own the *matanot* before they are formally separated and given? The *Yisrael* owns the animal until the moment of giving; the *matanot* are not yet the possession of any specific *kohen*, only of the abstract class of *kohanim*. If they are not yet owned by any individual, there may be nothing to steal in the classical sense. Second, even granting that they belong in some sense to the class of *kohanim*, the act of theft classically involves removing an object from someone's control and possession. But the *matanot* are not under the control of any particular *kohen* prior to being given — they are, as it were, owned by a category rather than by a person. If so, what does it mean to steal them? Moving the stomach from one place to another, or transferring it from one person to another, does not seem to dispossess any particular *kohen* who was holding it.

### Rav: Can Steal

Rav holds that *matnot kehuna* can be stolen. This is consistent with the earlier principle of *mamon she'ein lo tov'im* — a principle that, paradoxically, seems to acknowledge that the *kohanim* do own the *matanot* even before receiving them (otherwise the concept of "money with no claimant" would not apply). If there is ownership — even abstract, collective ownership — it is conceptually possible to steal. The butcher who intentionally gave the stomach to someone else rather than the *kohen* effectively took it away from the class of *kohanim*, and can therefore be pursued.

### Rav Assi: Cannot Steal

Rav Assi holds that *matnot kehuna* cannot be stolen. Since there is no specific possessor — no individual *kohen* from whose grip the object was removed — the act of appropriation does not constitute *geneivah* in the technical sense. Under this view, one can only pursue the person who physically has the object; there is no additional claim against the intermediary who set the process in motion.

### Principle Agreement

The Gemara resolves the framing: *de-chulei alma it lehu de-Rav Chisda* — everyone in principle accepts Rav Chisda's rule that one may collect from either the thief or the consumer when both committed wrongs. The debate between Rav and Rav Ashi is not about Rav Chisda's general principle at all. The real dispute is whether *matnot kehuna* constitute a stealable entity in the first place. Rav holds that they do, and therefore the butcher who appropriated them falls into Rav Chisda's category of a thief who can be pursued. Rav Assi holds that they do not, and therefore only the person with the object in hand can be held to account.

## Convert's Animal

### Shechita Timing

The next *Mishnah* takes up the case of a convert who owned an animal. If the animal was slaughtered before he converted, no *matnot* need be given — it is treated as the animal of a non-Jew, which is exempt. If it was slaughtered after his conversion, he is obligated. If the timing is uncertain — say, someone on his farm slaughtered the animal while he was in the process of converting — he is exempt: *safek patur*.

### Burden of Proof

The governing principle is *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-re'aya* — the burden of proof rests with whoever seeks to collect. A *kohen* who wishes to claim the gifts from the convert must prove that the animal was slaughtered after the conversion took place. Without that proof, the convert need not give anything.

This raises a tension that runs throughout the *perek*. Should we not approach this through the lens of *mitzvah* rather than civil liability — and say that since there is a possibility I owe a *mitzvah*, I should perform it? The Gemara's answer, as we will see, is that when the obligation is purely monetary, the civil standard of proof controls; it is only when there is an *issur* dimension that we say to be strict in cases of doubt. Here, failing to give *zroa lechayim vekeiva* does not render any food forbidden, so the civil lens governs, and the *kohen* who claims the gifts has the burden of proof.

## Doubt & Obligation

### Reish Lakish Proof

The Gemara reports that when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he conveyed the following exchange: Reish Lakish challenged Rabbi Yochanan from our *Mishnah*. Our *Mishnah* says *safek patur* — in cases of doubt, the convert is exempt, and we rule leniently when there is uncertainty about the *matnot*. But, Reish Lakish argued, this appears to conflict with how we treat doubtful gifts to the poor.

The case Reish Lakish cites is that of the holes made by burrowing animals in a wheat field. Wheat found in holes dug within the standing grain — before harvesting — belongs to the owner, since it clearly fell there before the harvest began. Wheat found in holes in the already-harvested area could have fallen there during harvesting, in which case it would be *leket*, belonging to the poor. The top layer of grain in such holes is given to the poor — *ha-elyonim la-aniyim* — as a safeguard, since it may have been dropped during the harvest. The bottom layer goes to the owner, since it must have accumulated earlier. And Rabbi Meir says: *hakol la-aniyim* — everything goes to the poor, for *safeik leket leket* — any doubt as to whether something is *leket* is resolved in favor of the poor.

Moreover, *Tosafot* notes that the question is not limited to Rabbi Meir's position: even the *Tanna Kamma* seems to agree that wherever there is genuine doubt, the grain belongs to the poor. The debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Meir appears to concern only whether the very bottom of the hole is actually in a state of doubt or not. On the underlying principle — that doubt about *leket* resolves in favor of the poor — everyone seems to agree. If so, how can our *Mishnah* say the convert is exempt when there is a doubt?

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan responded: *al taknitani* — "do not press me on this." He claims that the *Mishnah* which says doubtful *leket* goes to the poor reflects only a single, non-authoritative opinion — specifically that of Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra in the name of Rabbi Meir. Since it is a *da'at yachid*, we do not rule like it. The *baraita* records: *safeik leket leket, safeik shikcha shikcha, safeik peah peah* — each category of doubt about gifts to the poor is resolved in favor of the poor. But this, Rabbi Yochanan insists, represents only Rabbi Meir's minority view, not normative *halacha*.

### Reish Lakish: Tzedek Mishelcha

Reish Lakish was unmoved. He replied: even if it is an obscure opinion — even if it is attributed to someone as unknown as Ben Tada, as Rashi identifies him, essentially a nobody — I don't care. The opinion gives a compelling reason, and the reason should be examined on its merits. *Ve-ha-ta'ama ka'amar* — the reasoning stands on its own.

Reish Lakish then articulates his own independent derivation: what does the verse mean when it says *ani va-rash hatzdiku* — "give justice to the poor and the impoverished"? One cannot read it as a command to favor the poor in judicial proceedings, since the Torah explicitly says *vedal lo tehadar be-rivo* — do not show favor to a poor person in his lawsuit. Courts must be impartial. Rather, the verse means: *tzedek mishelcha veten lo* — if there is any doubt as to whether you owe something to the poor, tip the scales from your own resources and give it to them. Outside of formal adjudication, where you are weighing your personal obligations, the default should be generosity toward the poor. If you think you might owe them, give it to them.

This is reminiscent of the famous case in Bava Metzia where an *Amora*'s workers broke barrels and the judge, while technically ruling in the employer's favor, urged him to waive the payment and even pay their wages — an instance of *lifnim mishurat hadin*. The court must be objective, but your personal obligations, outside the courtroom, may pull you toward a higher standard of generosity toward those in need.

### Issur vs. Mamona

The Gemara ultimately provides the principled distinction. Rava formulates an initial answer: in the case of the convert, the cow began its life in a status of exemption — it belonged to a non-Jew, and we have a *chazakah* that it was not subject to *matnot*. We therefore presume it remains exempt unless proven otherwise. In the wheat field, by contrast, wheat owned by a Jew starts with an obligation to give the gifts to the poor, so we lean toward obligation when in doubt. Rava is thus reading our *Mishnah* as a narrow exception based on *chazakah*, while accepting Reish Lakish's general principle that doubt about gifts to the poor favors the poor.

But Abaye challenges this: the same *chazakah* logic would apply to the case of *challah*. A convert's dough, made before conversion, would also start with an exempt status. Yet we rule that if the timing is doubtful, the convert is *chayav* for *challah*. How does Rava explain that?

Rava's response — which the Gemara accepts as the authoritative resolution — is: *safek issura lechumra, safek mamona lekula*. When uncertainty involves an *issur*, we are strict; when it involves only money, we are lenient. The *challah* case involves an *issur*: if the dough was obligated in *challah* and none was separated, the remaining dough is *tevel* — forbidden to eat. You cannot eat from it. That is an *issur*, and so we rule strictly in cases of doubt. But *zroa lechayim vekeiva* and *reishit hagez* involve no *issur*. If you fail to give them, you do not thereby make any food forbidden; the only question is who owns the money. When it is purely monetary, *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-re'aya* controls: the claimant must prove his case.

### Eight Cases

This principle is illustrated and confirmed by Rav Chisda — and cited also by Rabbi Hiyya — who enumerates eight cases involving converts where uncertainty about the timing of conversion arises, four of which result in obligation and four in exemption.

The four cases of obligation all involve *issur* dimensions: the case of a convert's wife whose childbirth *korban* is uncertain (if she might need to bring a *korban* for a birth that occurred after conversion, and she doesn't, she may be eating *kodshim* while ritually impure); *challah* (where the dough would be *tevel* without separation); the firstborn of a pure animal (*bechor behema tehorah*, which carries *kedusha*); and the firstborn of a donkey (*peter chamor*, which remains forbidden in benefit until redeemed). In all four, failing to act when obligated leads to a prohibited state, so we rule strictly in cases of doubt.

The four cases of exemption are all purely monetary: *reishit hagez*, *zroa lechayim vekeiva*, *pidyon haben* (the redemption of a firstborn son, which is simply money paid to a *kohen* with no *issur* if not paid), and *pidyon peter chamor* — not the question of the donkey's forbidden status, which would be *issur*, but the question of the sheep used to redeem it, which now simply concerns whether the *kohen* gets the sheep as a monetary gift. In all four, no prohibition results from withholding, so the civil principle applies and the *kohen* has the burden of proof.

This resolves the apparent tension with the gifts to the poor. The case of the poor may be an exception — tipping the scales in their favor based on Reish Lakish's reading of *ani va-rash hatzdiku* — but that question remains a matter of debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. The main principle is settled: *safek issur lechumra, safek mamona lekula*. And under that framework, our *Mishnah*'s ruling that a convert in doubtful circumstances is exempt from *matnot* stands on solid ground.

## Poor vs. Kohanim

### Levi's Field

The Gemara now presents a case that sharpens the distinction between the two modes of giving. Levi planted his field in a place called Kishar. Grain was dropped during harvesting — potential *leket* — but there were no poor people in the area. The field was in a remote location, with no *aniyim* anywhere nearby.

### Rav Sheshet

Levi came before Rav Sheshet. Rav Sheshet ruled: *le'ani velager ta'azov otam* — "you shall leave it for the poor and for the stranger." The purpose of leaving *leket* is not to feed ravens and bats. If no poor person can actually reach the field and take the grain, you are not obligated to preserve it for them — and you may keep it for yourself. The obligation to give gifts to the poor is satisfied by leaving; if no one can come and take, there is nothing to leave and no obligation that remains unfulfilled.

### Terumah Transport

The Gemara challenges Rav Sheshet's ruling from the laws of *terumah*. There is a principle that one is not obligated to transport *terumah* from the threshing floor to the city, or from the wilderness to a settled area — the *kohen* comes to take it. But if there is no *kohen* available and the *terumah* would otherwise rot, one is required to hire an animal and bring it to the city — *socher parah umevia mipnei hefsed terumah* — because you may not allow *terumah* to go to waste. Here, then, you are obligated to actively transport the *terumah* even when no *kohen* is nearby. Why should the case of *leket* be different?

### Tevul Distinction

The Gemara answers: *shani terumah detavla* — *terumah* is different because it has *tevul* status. If you do not separate *terumah*, your produce is *tevel* and you cannot eat it. You are therefore obligated to separate *terumah* regardless of whether a *kohen* is available to receive it, and once separated, you must preserve it — even transport it — so that it does not rot. The obligation to preserve *terumah* is thus driven not by a duty to the *kohen* per se, but by the issur-dimension: you need to render your produce permissible, and the separated *terumah* must be given to a *kohen* eventually. Gifts to the poor carry no such *tevul* status; failing to give *leket* does not make any of your produce forbidden. Therefore the principle of *ta'azov* — leave it — controls entirely, and if the poor cannot come to take it, the obligation is not triggered.

The Gemara adds that the same reasoning now explains why *matanot kehuna* would seem to require active preservation: a *baraita* rules that if there is no *kohen* present when you separate the *zroa lechayim vekeiva*, you estimate its value, set that amount aside, and eat the gifts yourself — *ma'alin oto bedamim v'ochlan mipnei hefsed kohen* — so that the *kohen* does not lose out on the monetary value. Yet *matanot kehuna* also carry no *tevul* status. How is this different from *leket*?

### Nittina vs. Ta'azov

The Gemara provides the answer: *shani matnot kehuna denetina ketiva behu* — *matanot kehuna* are different because the Torah uses the language of giving. *Venatan* — you shall give it to the *kohen*. By the gifts to the poor, the Torah says *ta'azov* — you shall leave it. The language is not incidental; it defines the structure of the obligation. *Nittina* — giving — means the obligation is on you, the owner, to ensure the *kohen* receives it or its value. *Ta'azov* — leaving — means your job is only to make it accessible; the poor come and take it themselves. If they cannot come, your obligation is discharged by not interfering.

The same logic explains why *terumah* requires transport: the Torah there also uses the language of giving — *titen lo terumah* — and therefore it is your responsibility to ensure it reaches the *kohen*.

### Honor & Dignity

There is a deeper principle underlying this distinction. The entire discussion of *matanot kehuna* throughout this *perek* has emphasized that the gifts are given in a manner that reflects the honor of the *kohanim*: you give, they receive. It is an active gesture of acknowledgment. With the poor, the dynamic is deliberately reversed. The poor come and take; you do not hand it to them directly. This is not a lesser form of generosity — it is a more sensitive one. Giving directly to a poor person highlights their poverty and makes the interaction feel like a handout. Leaving it for them to take preserves their dignity; they exercise their own agency in coming to claim what belongs to them. Thus the Torah's choice of *ta'azov* versus *nettina* is not merely technical — it reflects two distinct conceptions of how one gives with respect. For the *kohen*, honor is shown through the act of giving. For the poor, dignity is preserved through the act of leaving.

## Sack of Dinari

### Rabbi Ami's Action

A concluding narrative ties together many of these themes. A sack filled with *dinari* — gold coins — was sent to the *beis medrash* as a gift to the *yeshiva*. Rabbi Ami, who served as *rosh yeshiva*, ran ahead and seized possession of the coins for himself — *kadim Rabbi Ami vezachi bahen*.

### Grabbing Gifts

The Gemara is puzzled. The entire preceding discussion has emphasized that gifts — and *matanot kehuna* in particular — should not be grabbed. The proper mode of receiving is that the gift is given to you; you do not run and snatch. The obligation involves *venatan* — giving — not grabbing. How could Rabbi Ami behave this way?

### First Answer

The first answer: *Rabbi Ami le'aniyim zachah bahen* — Rabbi Ami seized the coins on behalf of the poor, intending to distribute them as charity rather than keeping them for the *yeshiva*. Once the coins are understood as a gift for the poor rather than for a *kohen*-analog, the rules change. The applicable principle is *ta'azov* — the poor may take, they are supposed to come and claim what belongs to them. Rabbi Ami, acting as their agent, did precisely that.

### Adam Chashuv

The second answer: *adam chashuv shani* — a person of exceptional stature operates by different rules. The Gemara cites a *baraita*: the *kohen gadol* must be greater than his fellow *kohanim* in beauty, wisdom, and wealth. If he lacks wealth, *acherim omrim* — other authorities say — his fellow *kohanim* are obligated to contribute and make him wealthy, as the verse says *vehakohen hagadol me'echav* — *gadlehu mishel echav*, let his brothers make him great from their own resources. Since people have a responsibility to make the *kohen gadol* wealthy, he is in a sense entitled to claim resources that are being sent for that purpose. The *rosh yeshiva* — or the *adam chashuv* he represents — occupies an analogous position of institutional status, and the normal prohibition on grabbing does not apply to him.

This second answer is admittedly somewhat difficult to fully understand. There is a difference between whether money should go to a particular person and how one takes it. The whole point of the norm against grabbing is that even the person who is entitled to a gift should receive it graciously rather than seizing it. The first answer — that Rabbi Ami was acting for the poor, invoking *ta'azov* — is far more elegant and plays directly off the contrast developed throughout this sugya between how one gives to a *kohen* and how one gives to the poor. The second answer is noted by the Gemara as an alternative, but the first captures the deeper logic of the preceding discussion.

## Anatomy Lesson

### Zroa Definition

The final *Mishnah* of the *perek* defines the anatomical scope of each of the three gifts. The *zroa* — the arm — runs *miferek shel arkuva* — from the joint of the *arkuva*, the lower joint of the foreleg — *ad kaf shel hayad*, up to the shoulder. This comprises the two upper arm bones and all the meat on them. It is the same *zroa* that the Torah specifies when commanding that the arm of the ram be cooked for the *nazir*, and it corresponds anatomically to the *shok* — the thigh — given from a *korban shelamim*: the *shok* is the analogous pair of upper bones in the hind leg.

### Right Arm

The *Mishnah* and *baraita* establish that the *zroa* given as a *matana* is specifically the right arm — *hazroa zo zroa yamin*. This parallels the right thigh of the *shelamim*, and is derived through the same exegetical principle Rava applied to Rabbi Yehuda's position on the *gid hanasheh*: the "important" limb, *hamiyumenet*, is the right one — *hazroa hamiyumenet shebazroa*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Regarding the *shok* of a *korban*, Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the majority about what the term encompasses. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *shok* consists of only the single middle bone, not the two upper bones of the thigh. The *Mishnah* records his opinion: *shok miferek shel arkuva ad sof haregel* — the *shok* runs from the lower joint to the end of the leg, i.e., only the central bone, not the upper two. This is a minority view; the standard position includes the full upper pair.

### Lechayayim

The *lechayayim* — the jaws — are defined as the area *miferek shel lechi* — from the joint of the jaw — *ad pika shel gargoret* — until the covering of the esophagus at the back of the throat. This includes the tongue.

### Wool & Hair

A *baraita* teaches that the definite article *heh* of *v'halechayayim* in the verse extends the gift beyond the jaw itself. *Lehavi tsemer sheberosh kevasim* — it includes the wool on the head of sheep. Even though the *kohen* is only receiving the jaw as meat, he also receives the wool around the head of the sheep along with it. Similarly, *ve-sa'ar shebizkan tayashim* — the hair of the beard of goats is included as well.

### Keiva

The *heh* of *v'hakeivah* likewise extends the gift. *Lehavi chelev shel gabei hakeivah* — it includes not just the stomach itself, but the fat around the outside of the stomach. Although this fat is prohibited for eating, it may be used for benefit — for making leather, for burning, and similar purposes — and therefore belongs to the *kohen*. *Ve-chelev shebetoch hakeivah* — the fat inside the stomach is also included.

### Kohanim's Generosity

Rabbi Yehoshua adds: *kohanim nahagu bahen ayin yafah* — the *kohanim* practiced exceptional generosity when it came to the *keivah*, and *venatnu l'ba'alim* — they returned the fat to the owners. The Gemara notes that this was a voluntary practice, not a requirement: *ta'ama de-nahagu* — it is because of their customary generosity, but *ha lo nahagu, didehu* — had they not adopted this practice, they would have been entitled to the fat both inside and outside the stomach.

## Pinchas Derash

### Zroa Symbol

A section of *aggadata* closes the *perek*, introduced with the phrase *dorshei chamurot hayu omrim* — those who offer symbolic or *midrashic* interpretations of commandments said the following. The three *matanot* — *zroa*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* — are understood as commemorating the act of Pinchas. The *zroa* represents the arm — specifically the right arm — with which Pinchas took hold of his spear: *vayikach romach beyado* — and he took a spear in his hand.

### Lechayayim Symbol

The *lechayayim* corresponds to prayer — *tefillah*. The verse in Tehillim says *vaya'amod Pinchas vayifallel*, which is read as *vayitpallel* — and Pinchas stood and prayed. The *lechayayim*, the site of speech and prayer, thus symbolizes his turning to God for strength before performing his act.

### Keiva Symbol

The *keivah* — the stomach — is *kemashma'ah*, its plain meaning: *ve-et ha-ishah el kuvatah* — and he thrust through the woman into her stomach. The spear passed through the bodies of Zimri and Cozbi at precisely that anatomical location. The Rambam in *Moreh Nevukhim* offers a different type of reason for these gifts — he sees them as representing something primary or first about the animal, the *zroa* as the first appendage, the *lechayayim* on the head, and so on — but the Gemara here clearly prefers the Pinchas parallel, which is a remarkably tight fit for two of the three gifts and a compelling homiletical framework for all three.

## Right Side Derivation

### Thigh Source

Having derived from the *aggadic* material that the *zroa* is the right arm, the Gemara now traces the technical textual basis for this. The *baraita* begins with the *shok* of a *korban shelamim*. The verse explicitly states *hayamin* — the right — in the context of the thigh given from a *shelamim*. From that case it is clear: the *kohen* receives the right thigh.

### Nazir Derivation

From the *shelamim* the Gemara then derives the *zroa* of a *nazir*'s ram. The verse there uses the term *terumah* — an extra word, or a word whose inclusion teaches something beyond its immediate context. The appearance of *terumah* implies that other items also characterized as elevated gifts to the *kohen* follow the same right-side rule. Therefore the arm of the *nazir*'s ram is the right arm.

### Regular Shechita

Finally, from the *nazir*'s ram the rule is extended to the *zroa* given in ordinary *shechita*. The verse uses the word *titnu* — you shall give — and this additional formulation is read as extending the right-side requirement to all priestly gifts of this type. Thus at every stage — the *shelamim*, the *nazir*, and regular slaughter — the gift is from the right side.

## Lechayayim Boundary

### Beit Shechita

The *Mishnah* defined the *lechayayim* as running from the jaw joint to the covering of the esophagus. A *baraita* complicates this: it says the jaw is taken along with the *beit shechita* — the place of slaughter. If the *shechita* takes place lower on the trachea or esophagus than the top, then the phrase "with the *beit shechita*" would seem to imply the jaw includes area that extends down to — or even includes — the site of slaughter, which would be lower than the top of the esophagus.

### Mugremet Debate

This leads to a Tannaitic dispute about whether *shechita* may be performed at the very top of the esophagus. *Mugremet pesulah* — slaughter performed in a *mugremet* position, meaning too high up on the esophagus, is invalid according to the standard view. Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus, however, validates *shechita* performed at the *mugremet*, i.e., at the very top. The two opinions thus understand the *beit shechita* differently, and consequently have different views about where exactly the *lechayayim* ends.

### Resolution

The Gemara offers a resolution that sidesteps the Tannaitic dispute about *shechita* entirely. *Iba'it eima ha v'ha Rabbanan* — both opinions can actually be those of the Rabbis, who agree that *mugremet* is invalid. The phrase "with the *beit shechita*" does not mean the jaw is taken together with the place of slaughter. Rather, it means the jaw is separated *above* the place of slaughter — *imah d'behema*, the *beit shechita* remains with the animal. You take the jaw until the top of the esophagus; from there downward, where the actual *shechita* occurs, remains part of the *behema*. The *lechayayim* does not include the *beit shechita*; it ends just above it.

*Hadran alach zroa ulechayayim.*
