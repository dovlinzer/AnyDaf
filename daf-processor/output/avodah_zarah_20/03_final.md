# Avodah Zarah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Jewelry Prohibition

### Mishnah & R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תַּכְשִׁיטִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, קַטְלָאוֹת וּנְזָמִים וְטַבָּעוֹת. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בְּשָׂכָר מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And one may not fashion jewelry for** an object of **idol worship,** and this applies to jewelry such as **necklaces [*katla'ot*], nose rings, and rings. Rabbi Eliezer says:** If one fashions them in exchange **for payment,** it is **permitted.**

The daf begins at the bottom of 19b, continuing a series of *mishnayot* governing the permitted scope of interaction with the pagan non-Jewish world. The previous *mishnah* had addressed participation in construction projects associated with pagan courts and public spectacles, and had opened a rich digression on *moshav letzim* and the primacy of *talmud Torah*. The underlying theme was that the true line of division drawn by the Rabbis was not merely the formal boundary between Jewish and pagan, but the contrast between the seductions of the broader world and a life anchored in *Torah u-mitzvot*.

The *mishnah* now under discussion opens with what appears to be a prohibition: *ve-ein osin tachshitin le-avodat kochavim* — one may not fashion jewelry to adorn an idol. The specific items listed are a *katla* (likely a necklace), a *nezem* (likely a nose ring), and a *taba'at* (a ring). Rabbi Eliezer then adds: *be-sachar mutar* — if one is being compensated, it is permitted. This position is striking. If manufacturing the niche of a bathhouse designated for idolatrous use is forbidden, how could fashioning actual ornaments for an idol be permitted under any circumstances? And even if one were to say that *bedi'avad* the craftsman may retain his fee — a position the *gemara* had already countenanced in a previous discussion about crafting idols — to say it is *mutar le-chatchilah* seems deeply at odds with the general tenor of the tractate.

### Manuscript Evidence

Tosafot raises a fundamental question about whether this line belongs in the *mishnah* at all. The text does not appear in *Ktav Yad Kaufmann*, which is widely regarded as the most reliable extant manuscript of the *mishnah*. There is no parallel in the *Tosefta*, and no *gemara* engages with it as such.

Tosafot frames the textual difficulty as follows: *ve-ikka de-garsinan* — there are those who have this reading — but he regards it as an unlikely *girsa*, for *de-lo mistavar she-yattir shum tanna la-asot le-chatchilah tachshitei Avodah Zarah* — it is implausible that any *tanna* would permit the crafting of idolatrous ornaments in the first instance. Furthermore, one cannot invoke the argument of *mishum eivah* — that refusal would generate hostility — as one might in the case discussed later of a Jewish midwife declining to assist in childbirth. There, one might plausibly say the non-Jew will attribute the refusal to malice. But here, *de-ha vadai ika eivah* — even if there is some hostility, the non-Jew would understand why a Jew would decline to make jewelry for their idol. The *mishum eivah* defense simply does not apply.

### Rashi & Textual Issues

Tosafot adds a final and notably elegant argument from silence: *ve-nireh she-lo haya katuv be-sefer Rabbeinu Shlomo al perusho* — it appears that Rashi did not have this line in his text of the *mishnah*, and the proof is that Rashi offers no commentary on the word *katla'ot*. The word is unusual enough that had Rashi possessed this *girsa*, he would surely have explained it. This is a compelling instance of evidence derived from the absence of commentary — and, one might note, it is the same type of reasoning Tosafot deploys when Rashi's silence signals a textual problem rather than mere indifference.

The question of how such a line entered certain manuscripts, complete with an attributed position for Rabbi Eliezer, remains genuinely puzzling. One might speculate that it originated as a marginal gloss, perhaps written by someone seeking to justify commercial activity that had become commonplace. In any case, since there is no *gemara* engaging with this line, and the manuscript evidence is so weak, the *mishnah* as our *gemara* understands it begins elsewhere.

## Land Sales Ban

### Mishnah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶם בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע, אֲבָל מוֹכֵר הוּא מִשֶּׁיִּקָּצֵץ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מוֹכֵר הוּא עַל מְנָת לָקוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not sell to** a gentile any item **that is attached to the ground, but one may sell** such an item **once it is severed** from the ground. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is not necessary to sever the item from the ground; rather, **one may sell it on the condition that it be severed.**

The *mishnah* now turns to a different subject, one that connects back to the earlier discussion about selling large and small animals. There, the *gemara* had suggested various rationales — concerns about *Shabbat* violations or bestiality — but a more straightforward reading is that many of these prohibitions reflect an underlying concern about maintaining Jewish presence and control in the Land of Israel, a concern made explicit by the *gemara*'s earlier discussion about purchasing animals, land, and slaves at market in order to reinforce that presence.

The *mishnah* states: *ein mochrin lahem bi-mechubar la-karka* — one may not sell to them anything that is attached to the ground. This effectively encompasses land itself, though land is so obvious a case it goes without saying. Trees, standing grain, and other attached property are included. However, *aval mocher mi-she-yikatzetz* — once the item has been cut, it may be sold, which is self-evident. Rabbi Yehuda adds a nuance: *mocher lahem al menat liktoz* — one may sell them standing grain on condition that it will be cut. The Rabbis reject even this, because while the grain remains standing the buyer effectively holds a temporary claim over the land itself.

### Lo Techonem Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״, לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם חֲנָיָיה בַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** that it is prohibited to sell to a gentile anything that is attached to the ground, **derived? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** The source is **that the verse states: "You should not show them mercy [*lo teḥonnem*]"** (Deuteronomy 7:2), which is understood as meaning: **You should not give them** a chance to **encamp [*ḥanayah*] in,** i.e., to acquire **land** in, Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* asks *minah hanei milei* — what is the biblical source for this rule? Rav Yosef bar Chanina answers that the verse states *lo tichrot lahem berit ve-lo techonem* — do not make a covenant with them and do not *techonem* them. The word *lo techonem* receives multiple interpretations in the *gemara*, the first being *lo titein lahem chanayah ba-karka* — do not grant them a dwelling or foothold in the land.

The *peshat* of the surrounding verses clearly addresses the seven Canaanite nations. The passage also contains the prohibition *lo titchaten bam* — do not intermarry with them — which, notably, the dominant *gemara* position limits to those same seven nations. Even the technical biblical intermarriage prohibition may not extend beyond the seven Canaanite peoples, though this is a startling conclusion that *poskim* have had occasion to address.

### Scope & Extensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״לָא תְחׇנֵּם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, דְּהָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם חֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** phrase: **"You should not show them mercy";** isn't **it necessary** to teach **that this** is what **the Merciful one is saying: You should not give them favor [*ḥen*]** by praising them?

Tosafot addresses the obvious scope question: since the verse is speaking about the seven Canaanite nations, why does the *gemara* treat *lo techonem* as a general prohibition applicable to all non-Jews? Tosafot's position is that the *gemara* assumes — without extended argument — that the concerns expressed in *lo techonem* are of a nature that applies more broadly. Whether this broader application is *de-oraita* or merely an *asmachta* for a rabbinic prohibition is itself debated. It is also possible, the *gemara* allows, that we limit the scope to those actually engaged in *avodah zarah*, rather than to all non-Jews as such.

### Policy vs. Mitzvah

A closer look reveals a significant framing question. The most natural reading of these restrictions is that they are communal policy measures — responses to the precarious position of the Jewish minority under Roman rule, designed to prevent the erosion of the Jewish land base. In a context where Jews were politically vulnerable, selling land to the dominant power would have been deeply destabilizing for the community. The analogy is not difficult to find: in other communities under occupation or demographic pressure, there have been comparable norms against selling land to outsiders, understood as acts of communal solidarity and survival rather than religious injunction.

The *gemara*, however, consistently reframes these practical concerns as *mitzvah* categories — the same move it made earlier when it read the prohibition on selling large animals through the lens of *Shabbat*. The concern is no longer a communal policy responsive to historical circumstances; it becomes a timeless religious obligation derived from a biblical verse. This shift has significant implications. A policy-based norm can be revisited as circumstances change; a *mitzvah* framing tends toward absolute application regardless of context. It is precisely this tension that has given rise to contemporary halakhic debates about whether these rules apply when Jews are not a vulnerable minority but the dominant power — a question that falls well beyond the scope of this *shiur*, but whose roots lie in exactly this point.

## Lo Techonem: Favor

### Three Meanings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״לֹא תְחוּנֵּם״, מַאי ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי. וּמַתְּנַת חִנָּם גּוּפַהּ תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** that were **so, let the verse say: *Lo teḥunnem*,** with the letter *vav*, as then it would be evident that this is a form of the root *ḥet*, *vav*, *nun*, which means favor. **What** is the reason that the verse instead states: ***Lo teḥonnem*,** without the letter *vav*? **Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** that one may not praise them and also that one may not allow them to acquire land. The Gemara notes: **And** this issue of **an undeserved gift** to a gentile **is itself** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.

The *gemara* continues to derive multiple meanings from the single word *lo techonem*. The first derivation, *lo titein lahem chanayah ba-karka*, has already been discussed. But the *gemara* now objects: the word *lo techonem* is needed to teach something else — *lo titein lahem chein* — do not give them favor, do not express admiration for them. If the word was meant only to convey the land-dwelling prohibition, it would have been written differently. And if it was meant only to convey the favor prohibition, a different vocalization would have sufficed. The *gemara* therefore concludes *shema mina tartei* — we derive both meanings — and adds a third: *lo titein lahem matnat chinam* — do not give them a free gift.

The *lo titein lahem chein* prohibition does not mean simply "don't be kind to them." It means: do not go about publicly praising and extolling the non-Jewish world — its beauty, its achievements, its religious institutions. The concern is not merely social warmth, but the cultural and spiritual pull exerted by enthusiastic admiration. If one is constantly declaring how magnificent pagan temples are, or how brilliant non-Jewish cultural productions are, one feeds the seductive appeal of that world and implicitly denigrates one's own. This is especially acute when the objects of praise are directly associated with *avodah zarah*: extolling the grandeur of the Temple of Zeus is not a neutral aesthetic observation; it actively rehabilitates what Jewish law demands one treat as repugnant.

That said, this is a genuinely challenging *gemara* from the perspective of a more pluralistic religious sensibility. We are apparently prohibited not only from giving non-Jews land, but from complimenting them, and from giving them gifts. The most generous limiting principle available is to confine all three derivations to the context of genuine idolaters — which, in many contemporary contexts, would dramatically narrow the practical scope of the prohibition.

## Matnat Chinam

### R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״ — לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם חֲנָיָיה בַּקַּרְקַע, דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״ — לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם חֵן, דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״ — לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם מַתְּנַת חִנָּם. וּמַתְּנַת חִנָּם גּוּפַהּ תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇּל נְבֵלָה לַגֵּר אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תִּתְּנֶנָּה וַאֲכָלָהּ אוֹ מָכֹר לְנׇכְרִי״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְגֵר בִּנְתִינָה וּלְגוֹי בִּמְכִירָה, לְגֵר בִּמְכִירָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּתְּנֶנָּה... אוֹ מָכֹר״. לְגוֹי בִּנְתִינָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּתְּנֶנָּה וַאֲכָלָהּ אוֹ מָכֹר לְנׇכְרִי״, נִמְצָא אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד גֵּר וְאֶחָד גּוֹי בֵּין בִּנְתִינָה בֵּין בִּמְכִירָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן, לְגֵר בִּנְתִינָה, וּלְגוֹי בִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"You should not show them mercy";** this teaches that **you should not give them** a chance to **encamp in** the **land** of Eretz Yisrael. **Another matter: "You should not show them mercy";** this indicates that **you should not give them favor. Another matter: "You should not show them mercy";** this teaches that **you should not give them an undeserved gift.** The Gemara notes: **And** this issue of **an undeserved gift** to a gentile **is itself** a dispute between ***tanna'im. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"You shall not eat of any unslaughtered animal carcass; you may give it to the resident alien who is within your gates, that he may eat it; or you may sell it to a foreigner;** for you are a sacred people to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 14:21). **I have** derived **only** that it is permitted **to a resident alien through giving and to a gentile through selling. From where** do I derive that it is permitted to transfer an unslaughtered animal **to a resident alien through selling? The verse states: "You may give it**…**or you may sell it,"** meaning that one has the option to do either of these. **From where** is it derived that it is permitted **to a gentile through giving** and one is not required to sell it to him? **The verse states: "You may give it**…**that he may eat it; or you may sell it to a foreigner."** Therefore, **you may say** that he may transfer it to **both a resident alien and a gentile, both through giving and through selling.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** These **matters are** to be understood **as they are written;** one may transfer an unslaughtered animal carcass **to a resident alien** only **through giving, and to a gentile** only **through selling,** as it is prohibited to give an undeserved gift to a gentile.

The *gemara* notes that the prohibition of *matnat chinam* — giving a free gift to a non-Jew — is itself the subject of a *tannaitic* dispute. The *gemara* cites a *beraita* based on the verse in Deuteronomy: *lo tochlu kol neveila la-ger asher bi-sh'arecha titnenah va-achala o machor la-nochri* — do not eat any *neveila*; give it to the *ger* in your gates, or sell it to the foreigner. The *ger* in rabbinic reading is the *ger toshav*, the resident alien, while the *nochri* is the visiting foreigner — a stranger from another land.

The *beraita* then asks: I know only that giving applies to the *ger* and selling applies to the *nochri*; how do I derive that selling to the *ger* and giving to the non-Jew are also permitted? Through creative rearrangement of the verse's clauses, the *beraita* constructs an argument that both *netinah* and *mechirah* apply to both categories. Rabbi Meir takes this position: both giving and selling are permitted to both *ger* and non-Jew alike, the verse's framing being a matter of priority rather than exclusion. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, takes the verse literally — *devarim kichtavam* — *ger* receives, *nochri* is sold to. Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning: since you have an affirmative obligation to sustain the *ger toshav* (*ve-chai achicha imach* includes the *ger toshav*), you would have known to prioritize him without the verse's guidance. The fact that the verse uses the disjunctive *o* rather than a simple *vav* therefore conveys an actual prohibition: you may not give the non-Jew a free gift; you may only sell.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר לָךְ: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״תִּתְּנֶנָּה וַאֲכָלָהּ ומָכֹר״, ״אוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִדְבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן הוּא דַּאֲתָא. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר? הָהוּא לְאַקְדּוֹמֵי נְתִינָה דְּגֵר לִמְכִירָה דְּגוֹי. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כֵּיוָן דְּגֵר אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ, וְגוֹי אִי אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה לְהַחֲיוֹתוֹ — לְהַקְדִּים לָא צְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Meir is saying well,** as the verse indicates that either method is acceptable. The Gemara explains: **And Rabbi Yehuda** could have **said to you: If it enters your mind** to understand the verse **in accordance with** that which **Rabbi Meir says,** then **let the Merciful One write: You may give it** to the resident alien who is within your gates **that he may eat it, and** also **you may sell it** to a foreigner. **Why do I** need the word **"or"** between these two options? **Learn from it that it comes to** teach that **the matters are** to be understood **as they are written.** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Meir** explain the wording of the verse? The Gemara answers: **That** word, "or," teaches **that** one should **give precedence to giving to a resident alien over selling to a gentile. And Rabbi Yehuda** holds that **since you are commanded to sustain a resident alien,** as it is stated: "And he shall live with you" (Leviticus 25:35), **and you are not commanded to sustain a gentile, there is no need for a verse to** teach that one should **give precedence** to a resident alien.

The *gemara* validates Rabbi Yehuda's reading as the more compelling analysis and identifies him as the *tanna* who holds the prohibition of *matnat chinam*.

### Tosafot Qualifications

Tosafot significantly narrows the operative scope of *matnat chinam*. He cites a *Tosefta* on *neveila*: Rabbi Yehuda's prohibition applies to non-Jews who are strangers — but if one *knows* the non-Jew, *hamakiro mutar*, it is permitted, *mipnei she-hu ke-mochro lo* — because in the context of an ongoing relationship, what appears to be a gift functions more like a transaction with implicit reciprocity. You give now, they give later; there is no pure gratuitousness.

Tosafot further cites the *gemara* in *Perek Hadar*: even where one does not know the non-Jew, if one happens to be traveling together with him on the road, giving is permitted — *shehayah mislaveh imo ba-derech* — the context of shared journeying creates a momentary relational bond that removes the act from the category of *matnat chinam*.

And finally: *darchei shalom* — the rabbinic policy of supporting non-Jewish poor together with Jewish poor, of maintaining civil societal relations — is explicitly not *matnat chinam*. Tosafot states: *darchei shalom eino matnat chinam*, giving in the context of cultivating good relations is definitionally excluded from the prohibition.

### Chinam Meaning

The Koren translation renders *chinam* not as "free" but as "undeserved." The distinction is worth examining. Tosafot's framework suggests the operative concept is not deserving per se, but rather the presence or absence of any relational or purposive context. If there is a prior relationship, a shared journey, or a policy of *darchei shalom*, the giving has a context — it is not *chinam*. What Tosafot's qualifications effectively eliminate is the case of pure, contextless largesse directed at a random non-Jew with no relationship, no shared circumstances, and no communal rationale. In that narrow case, one's charitable impulses should be directed toward the Jewish community rather than to outsiders.

This reading, it should be noted, makes the prohibition far more about internal communal solidarity than about any hostility toward non-Jews. It does not forbid maintaining good relations — on the contrary, it explicitly permits and even encourages giving in any relational context. What it discourages is indiscriminate generosity extended to strangers who happen to be non-Jewish when that same generosity could be directed toward one's own community.

Tosafot raises a practical question about the *neveila* case specifically: the verse seems to say one must *give* to the *ger* rather than *sell* to him, even though one is normally permitted to sell one's property at fair market value. Tosafot resolves this by noting the particular economics of *neveila*: within a Jewish community where *neveila* is worthless to Jews, the commercial option would require exporting it to foreign buyers at considerable difficulty, whereas the *ger* living in your midst can receive it at the minimal local value. The verse is not insisting on pure charity at commercial cost; it is aligning practical reality with the *mitzvah* to sustain the *ger toshav*.

### Textual Censorship

A philological observation is worth making here. The original text of this *masechet*, like much of the *Talmud*, used the words *goy* or *nochri* when referring to non-Jews. Due to the attentions of church censors — and, more significantly, to internal Jewish self-censorship driven by fear that these passages would be reported as anti-Christian — the standard texts were systematically altered to read *ovdei kochavim*, "star-worshipers," or sometimes even *kena'ani*. The implication was that all such discussions referred only to ancient idolaters, not to contemporary Christians or Muslims.

The self-censorship becomes particularly visible at a telling moment in this *gemara*. Throughout the discussion of *matnat chinam*, the text consistently uses *ovdei kochavim*. But when the *gemara* reaches the statement that one is not obligated to sustain the non-Jew's life — *ve-kena'ani i ata metzuveh le-hachayoto* — the language shifts from *ovdei kochavim* to *kena'ani*. The rationale is transparent: a statement that one need not be concerned with a non-Jew's survival was apparently too sensitive to be left even with the already-censored *ovdei kochavim* designation, and required further distance through the term *kena'ani*.

The same phenomenon appears elsewhere in the Talmud. The famous *gemara* in *Bava Kamma* about whether *gezel* from a non-Jew is permitted reads, in censored manuscripts, *gezel kena'ani mutar* — as though it were speaking exclusively about Canaanites, not about any non-Jew. It was evidently not sufficient to replace *goy* with *akum*; the statement had to be attributed to an entirely different, historically extinct category of people.

What is remarkable is that over time, for many *poskim* who were more tolerantly minded, *halakha* eventually caught up to the censorship. What began as a face presented outward for self-protection gradually became internalized through genuine halakhic reasoning — whether by limiting the scope to actual idolaters, by arguing that contemporary non-Jews are not *ovdei avodah zarah*, or through other mechanisms. For many *poskim*, the internal halakhic position eventually converged with what the texts had been forced to say under external pressure.

## Lo Techonem: Favor (continued)

### Complimenting Non-Jews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״לֹא תְחׇנֵּם״ — לֹא תִּתֵּן לָהֶם חֵן. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לָאָדָם שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״כַּמָּה נָאָה גּוֹיָה זוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the *baraita* cited earlier: **Another matter: "You should not show them favor";** this teaches that **you should not give them favor** by praising them. The Gemara notes that this **supports** the opinion of **Rav. As Rav says: It is prohibited for a person to say: How beautiful is this gentile woman!**

The *gemara* now turns to the second derivation from *lo techonem*: *lo titein lahem chein* — do not give them favor or express admiration. Rav states explicitly: *assur le-adam she-yomar kama na'eh avodat kochavim zu* — it is forbidden to say how beautiful this *avodah zarah* is. The original unedited text apparently reads *kama na'eh goy zeh*, *kama na'eh goyah zu* — how handsome is this non-Jewish man, how beautiful is this non-Jewish woman. The prohibition thus applies to statements *about* non-Jews, not only statements made *to* them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁהָיָה עַל גַּבֵּי מַעֲלָה בְּהַר הַבַּיִת, וְרָאָה גּוֹיָה אַחַת נָאָה בְּיוֹתֵר, אָמַר: ״מַה רַבּוּ מַעֲשֶׂיךָ ה׳״. וְאַף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רָאָה אֵשֶׁת טוֹרָנוּסְרוּפוּס הָרָשָׁע, רָק, שָׂחַק וּבָכָה. רָק — שֶׁהָיְתָה בָּאָה מִטִּיפָּה סְרוּחָה, שָׂחַק — דַּעֲתִידָה דְּמִגַּיְירָא וְנָסֵיב לַהּ, בָּכָה — דְּהַאי שׁוּפְרָא בָּלֵי עַפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who was on a step on the Temple mount, and he saw a certain gentile woman who was exceptionally beautiful** and **said: "How great are Your works, O Lord!"** (Psalms 104:24). **And Rabbi Akiva too,** when he **saw the wife of the wicked Turnus Rufus he spat, laughed, and cried. He spat, as she was created from a putrid drop; he laughed, as** he foresaw that **she was destined to convert and he would marry her; he cried, as this beauty** would ultimately **be consumed by dirt.**

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge from a story about Rabban Shimon Gamliel. He was standing on the Temple Mount — itself a setting saturated with Jewish religious meaning — when he saw a strikingly beautiful non-Jewish woman, and he declared: *mah rabu ma'asecha Hashem* — how abundant are Your works, O God. Is this not a violation of Rav's ruling?

The *gemara* adds a parallel story: Rabbi Akiva saw the wife of the wicked Turnusrufus — presumably also a woman of exceptional beauty — and his reaction was threefold. He spat (*rak*), he laughed (*shachak*), and he cried (*vacha*). The *gemara* explains each response. He spat because she came from *tipah sruchah* — a putrid drop, that is, from semen; all human beauty ultimately originates from something base and transient. He laughed because he foresaw that she would eventually convert to Judaism and he would marry her — a rather striking biographical note, given his celebrated marriage to Rachel. He cried because *hai shufra bali afra* — this beauty will ultimately dissolve in the earth; it is all passing.

There is a certain irony in Rabbi Akiva's laughing, consistent with his characteristic disposition. Just as he once laughed at the ruins of the Temple because he saw in its destruction the guarantee of ultimate restoration, here he laughs at what might seem like loss — the beauty that will perish, the woman who belongs to an enemy — because he perceives the irony of the situation, the hidden trajectory toward something different. The spitting and crying form the frame of transience; the laughing is the recognition that even within transience, things can turn.

### Rav's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, אוֹדוֹיֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹדֵה, דְּאָמַר מָר: הָרוֹאֶה בְּרִיּוֹת טוֹבוֹת אוֹמֵר ״בָּרוּךְ שֶׁכָּכָה בָּרָא בְּעוֹלָמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how would **Rav** explain the incident involving Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who praised the beauty of a gentile? The Gemara answers: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **was giving thanks to** God for creating such beautiful people rather than praising the gentile herself. **As the Master said: One who sees beautiful** or otherwise outstanding **creatures recites: Blessed** be He, **Who has created such in His world.**

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between Rabban Shimon Gamliel's statement and Rav's prohibition by explaining that Rabban Shimon Gamliel was engaged in *aduye hu de-kamode* — an act of thanksgiving to God, not an expression of admiration for the non-Jewish woman herself. The supporting principle: *ha-ro'eh beriyot tovot omer baruch she-kacha bara be-olamo* — one who sees beautiful creatures may recite the blessing acknowledging that God created such things in His world.

The distinction Rav draws is therefore not simply about whether one says something beautiful to a non-Jew versus about a non-Jew. It is about the direction of the admiration. If one says, "How magnificent these non-Jews are," or "How beautiful that church is" — that makes the non-Jewish world itself the object of esteem, and implicitly elevates its attractions. But if one redirects the aesthetic experience toward God — "How abundant are Your works, O God" — then the beauty of the created world, wherever it appears, becomes a vehicle for recognizing the Creator. The non-Jewish world is not thereby elevated; it is subsumed. It is not that those people or those institutions are remarkable; it is that God has created a world of remarkable beauty, some of which is visible here. This reframing deflects the concern that one is feeding the seductive pull of the non-Jewish world by celebrating it.

## Sexual Thoughts Ban

### Davar Ra Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאִסְתַּכּוֹלֵי מִי שְׁרֵי? מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְנִשְׁמַרְתָּ מִכֹּל דָּבָר רָע״ — שֶׁלֹּא יִסְתַּכֵּל אָדָם בְּאִשָּׁה נָאָה וַאֲפִילּוּ פְּנוּיָה, בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ וַאֲפִילּוּ מְכוֹעֶרֶת, וְלֹא בְּבִגְדֵי צֶבַע [שֶׁל] אִשָּׁה, וְלֹא בַּחֲמוֹר וְלֹא בַּחֲמוֹרָה, וְלֹא בַּחֲזִיר וְלֹא בַּחֲזִירָה, וְלֹא בְּעוֹפוֹת בִּזְמַן שֶׁנִּזְקָקִין זֶה לָזֶה, וַאֲפִילּוּ מָלֵא עֵינַיִם כְּמַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **But is it permitted to gaze** upon a woman? The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*. The verse states: **"And you shall keep yourself from every evil thing"** (Deuteronomy 23:10); this teaches **that a person should not gaze upon a beautiful woman, even** if she is **unmarried; and** a person should not gaze upon **a married woman, even** if she is **ugly;** **and** a person should **not** gaze upon **the colored garments of a woman; and** a person should **not** gaze **at a male donkey, at a female donkey, at a pig, at a sow, or at fowl, when they are mating; and even** if one were **full of eyes like the Angel of Death** and saw from every direction, it is not permitted to look.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge to the story about Rabban Shimon Gamliel and Rabbi Akiva: *ve-le-istakulei mi sharei* — but were they even permitted to be *looking* at this woman? The *gemara* cites the verse *ve-nishmarta mi-kol davar ra* — you shall protect yourself from every evil thing — and understands it as a prohibition on the very act of gazing. The later part of the verse, which addresses *davar mikreh layla* — seminal emission at night — indicates that the *davar ra* is understood here as sexual thought that leads to involuntary emission. Rashi reads the verse's internal logic as: the way to protect oneself against seminal emission at night is not to indulge in sexual thoughts during the day.

### Looking Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת, שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ מָלֵא עֵינַיִם, בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתוֹ שֶׁל חוֹלֶה עוֹמֵד מֵעַל מְרַאֲשׁוֹתָיו, וְחַרְבּוֹ שְׁלוּפָה בְּיָדוֹ, וְטִיפָּה שֶׁל מָרָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁחוֹלֶה רוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ מִזְדַּעְזֵעַ וּפוֹתֵחַ פִּיו, וְזוֹרְקָהּ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו, מִמֶּנָּה מֵת, מִמֶּנָּה מַסְרִיחַ, מִמֶּנָּה פָּנָיו מוֹרִיקוֹת! קֶרֶן זָוִית הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **They said about the Angel of Death that he is entirely full of eyes. When a sick person is** about to **die,** the Angel of Death **stands above his head, with his sword drawn in his hand, and a drop of poison hanging on** the edge of the sword. **Once the sick person sees him, he trembles and** thereby **opens his mouth; and** the Angel of Death **throws** the drop of poison **into his mouth. From** this drop of poison the sick person **dies, from it he putrefies, from it his face becomes green.** The Gemara answers: Rabban Gamliel did not intentionally look at the woman; rather, he **was** walking around **a corner** and he saw her unexpectedly as they each turned.

The *gemara* elaborates a series of prohibitions on *histaklut* — intentional looking: one may not gaze at a beautiful woman, even if she is unmarried (*penuya*); nor at a married woman, even if she is unattractive; nor at women's colored garments; nor at donkeys, donkey-mares, pigs, sows, or birds when they are copulating. The concern in all cases is that such visual experiences will evoke sexual thoughts.

The question of how Rabban Shimon Gamliel and Rabbi Akiva could have found themselves in this situation is resolved by the *gemara* elsewhere: *keren zavit havai* — they came upon it at a corner, unexpectedly. The encounter was not a deliberate gaze but an inadvertent sighting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּבִגְדֵי צֶבַע [שֶׁל] אִשָּׁה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲפִילּוּ שְׁטוּחִין עַל גַּבֵּי כּוֹתֶל. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּבְמַכִּיר בַּעֲלֵיהֶן. אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״וְלֹא בְּבִגְדֵי צֶבַע אִשָּׁה״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״וְלֹא בְּבִגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִין״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעַתִּיקֵי, אֲבָל בְּחַדְתֵי לֵית לַן בַּהּ, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, אֲנַן מָנָא לְאַשְׁפּוֹרֵי הֵיכִי יָהֲבִינַן? הָא קָא מִסְתַּכֵּל! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מִין בְּמִינוֹ מוּתָּר לְהַכְנִיס כְּמִכְחוֹל בִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת, הָא קָא מִסְתַּכַּל! אֶלָּא בַּעֲבִידְתֵּיהּ טְרִיד, הָכִי נָמֵי בַּעֲבִידְתֵּיהּ טְרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement in the *baraita*: **Nor** may one gaze **at the colored garments of a woman, Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** This is the *halakha* **even** if **they are spread on a wall,** not only when they are being worn. **Rav Pappa says: And** the prohibition applies only **when one knows their owner. Rava said:** The language of the *baraita* **is also precise, as it teaches: Nor** may one gaze **at the colored garments of a woman, and it does not teach: Nor** may one gaze **at colored garments. Learn from it** that the prohibition applies only to the garments of one he knows. **Rav Ḥisda said: That statement** applies only **in** the case of **old** garments, i.e., garments that have been worn; **but in** the case of **new** garments, **we have no** problem **with it.** The reason is **that if you do not say so, how can we give** a woman's **garment** before it is worn **to a launderer,** i.e., one who prepares new garments for use, knowing that the launderer must **look** at the garments? The Gemara refutes this proof: **But according to your reasoning,** i.e., your assumption that a launderer is no different from all other men, there is a similar difficulty with **that which Rav Yehuda says:** If one wishes to mate an animal of one **species with** an animal of **its own species,** it is **permitted to insert** the male organ into the female **like a brush into a tube.** One could ask here as well: **But isn't he looking** at the animals as they mate? **Rather, he is occupied with his work,** and therefore his mind will not entertain sinful thoughts. **So too** with regard to a launderer, **he is occupied with his work,** and therefore a launderer differs from other men.

The *gemara* goes on to discuss women's colored garments. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states that the prohibition applies even to garments laid out on a wall — i.e., not being worn by anyone. Rav Pappa qualifies this: it applies only when *makir ba'aleihen* — when one recognizes their owner, because in that case seeing the garment evokes the mental image of the woman who wears it. Rava confirms that the *gemara*'s precise wording — *bigdei tzeva shel isha*, "colored garments of a woman," rather than simply "colored garments" — supports Rav Pappa's reading: the prohibition is not about colored clothing as such, but about colored clothing associated with a specific woman in one's mind. Rav Chisda adds that even this applies only to garments that have been worn (*atikin*); new garments not yet worn (*chadatei*) are not subject to the prohibition.

The *gemara* proves this last point from a practical *reductio*: if new garments were also forbidden, how could we ever bring clothing to the *shapurya* (tailor or dry cleaner) for adjustments? The *gemara* responds that professional engagement constitutes a distinct category: *be-avideiteih tarid* — he is absorbed in his work — and this applies to the farmer who must handle copulating animals as well. However, this raises nuanced questions for *Shulchan Aruch* and later *poskim* about which professional roles may be assumed by men given the potential for *hirhur*; not every professional context suppresses arousal equally.

### Critique of Obsession

It is worth pausing here to note something that the *gemara*'s own framework suggests, even if it does not say it directly: *adam nitzal mehem be-chol yom* — a person cannot be saved from *hirhur aveira* every single day. Sexual thoughts are, as the *gemara* essentially concedes, an inescapable feature of human experience. The appropriate response would seem to be acknowledgment and equanimity, not obsessive suppression.

The intense religious focus in certain communities on the elimination of sexual thoughts — the elaborate *viduyim*, the *tefila zaka*, the architectural and sartorial measures designed to prevent male arousal — is, by any reasonable assessment, counterproductive even on its own terms. The effort to eliminate what cannot be eliminated tends to intensify the focus on precisely what one wishes to move past. If *lashon hara*, which violates multiple biblical prohibitions and causes tangible communal harm, received a fraction of the religious energy devoted to the policing of sexual thought, the community would be better served.

A related point: the *gemara*'s framing consistently locates the responsibility with the male observer, not with the woman observed. Rava's careful reading of *bigdei tzeva shel isha* emphasizes that the problem is what happens in the man's mind, not what the woman is wearing. Women are entitled to wear their colored garments; the obligation to manage one's response belongs to the one who sees. The widespread practice of imposing *tzniut* obligations on women as a mechanism for controlling men's sexual responses inverts this responsibility in a way the *gemara* itself does not support.

### Angel of Death

The *gemara* briefly digresses to discuss the *malach ha-mavet* (Angel of Death), prompted by the phrase *ve-afilu malei einayim ke-malach ha-mavet* — even if one is filled with eyes like the Angel of Death, one must not look. The *gemara* elaborates: the *malach ha-mavet* is described as entirely filled with eyes (*she-kulo malei einayim*). At the moment of a sick person's death, he stands above the person's head, his sword drawn, with a drop of poison (*tipah shel marah*) hanging from it. When the dying person sees him, he shudders, opens his mouth, and the drop falls in. That drop causes death, causes the body to rot afterward, and causes the face to change color.

The father of Shmuel is quoted as having been told by the Angel of Death himself: were it not for *kavod ha-beriyot* — respect for human dignity — he would slit the throat as an animal is slaughtered at the *beit ha-shchitah*. Instead, he cuts internally. The *gemara* reconciles this with the poison account by suggesting that the poison acts as an acid, effectively cutting through the *simanim* from the inside — it is both a drop and a type of cutting.

The thematic connection between the Angel of Death and the preceding discussion of sexual thoughts is not incidental. The *gemara* has been moving between the beauty of the created world — which evokes desire — and the fragility of that same world, which is subject to decay and death. Rabbi Akiva's three reactions to Turnusrufus's wife traverse exactly this terrain: the beauty, its transience, and the ironic consolation available to one who sees through the surface. The Angel of Death interweaving reinforces the point: the same physical beauty that evokes *hirhur* is also the body that will rot. The corrective to excessive investment in the world's physical attractions is not mere willpower, but a sustained awareness of their ultimate nature.

### Colored Garments & Professional Engagement

The *gemara* returns to the looking prohibitions with a discussion of the farmer engaged in animal husbandry who must physically assist in animal copulation. Rav Yehuda states that *min be-mino mutar le-hachnis ke-michol ba-shoferet* — one may insert like an applicator into a tube — performing the act of insemination professionally. This would seem to contradict the prohibition on looking at copulating animals. The *gemara* resolves this, as noted above, through *be-avideiteih tarid* — professional preoccupation overrides the concern. The same principle explains why a tailor handling a woman's garments is not in violation: the professional context is determinative. But the *gemara*'s caveat is implicit and noted by later authorities: *be-avideiteih tarid* applies only where the professional engagement genuinely absorbs attention in a way incompatible with arousal. Professions that are themselves sexually charged, or that create extended intimate contact, require separate analysis.

## Rabbi Pinchas' Ladder

### Ten Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר: תּוֹרָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי זְהִירוּת, זְהִירוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי זְרִיזוּת, זְרִיזוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי נְקִיּוּת, נְקִיּוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי פְּרִישׁוּת, פְּרִישׁוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי טׇהֳרָה, טׇהֳרָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי חֲסִידוּת, חֲסִידוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי עֲנָוָה, עֲנָוָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי יִרְאַת חֵטְא, יִרְאַת חֵטְא מְבִיאָה לִידֵי קְדוּשָּׁה, קְדוּשָּׁה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מְבִיאָה לִידֵי תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, וַחֲסִידוּת גְּדוֹלָה מִכּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז דִּבַּרְתָּ בְחָזוֹן לַחֲסִידֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **From here Rabbi Pineḥas ben Ya'ir** would **say: Torah** study **leads to care** in the performance of mitzvot. **Care** in the performance of mitzvot **leads to diligence** in their observance. **Diligence leads to cleanliness** of the soul. **Cleanliness** of the soul **leads to abstention** from all evil. **Abstention** from evil **leads to purity** and the elimination of all base desires. **Purity leads to piety. Piety leads to humility. Humility leads to fear of sin. Fear of sin leads to holiness. Holiness leads to the Divine Spirit. The Divine Spirit leads to the resurrection of the dead. And piety is greater than all of them, as it is stated: "Then You did speak in a vision to Your pious ones"** (Psalms 89:20).

The *gemara* transitions to the famous statement of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair, introduced as *mikan amar Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* — from here Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair taught. The passage is the foundation upon which the *Mesillat Yesharim* is structured:

*Torah* leads to *zehirut* (carefulness against sin); *zehirut* leads to *zerizut* (alacrity in performing *mitzvot*); *zerizut* leads to *nekiut* (cleanliness from sin); *nekiut* leads to *perishut* (separation, ascetic withdrawal even from the permitted); *perishut* leads to *taharah* (purity); *taharah* leads to *kedushah* (holiness); *kedushah* leads to *anavah* (humility); *anavah* leads to *yir'at chet* (fear of sin); *yir'at chet* leads to *chasidut* (piety); *chasidut* leads to *ruach ha-kodesh* (divine spirit); and *ruach ha-kodesh* leads to *techiyat ha-meitim* (resurrection of the dead).

It is notable that *yir'at chet* — which one might expect to appear near the beginning as a foundational motivation — appears near the top of the ladder. At this level, it is clearly not rudimentary fear of punishment, but a refined, internalized aversion to sin that emerges from a life already deeply shaped by Torah, purity, and holiness. Similarly, *chasidut* here may not refer primarily to acts of *chesed*, but to a higher form of *prishut* — a quality of separation and dedication that transcends ordinary observance.

### Maharsha's Daily Schedule

The Maharsha offers a reading that is at once creative and practically grounding: these levels correspond not to a lifetime progression alone but to the stages of a single day's spiritual schedule. *Zehirut* — general carefulness — is the background disposition one carries always. *Zerizut* is awakening early in the morning, getting out of bed with alacrity to serve God. That early rising allows time for *nekiut* — washing and physical cleanliness before prayer. *Perishut* is the act of *davening* itself, in which one's hands are spread out (*perushos la-shamayim*) and one withdraws momentarily from the physical world. *Taharah* — a higher purity — represents one's *Torah* study undertaken after *davening* and the morning meal. *Kedushah* is the state achieved through immersion in *Torah* study, an entry into a kind of sanctified space analogous to the *Beit ha-Mikdash*. *Anavah* is the humility one carries into the workday that follows, tempered by the morning's spiritual activity. And so on through the ladder, until *ruach ha-kodesh* attends one's final waking hours and *techiyat ha-meitim* is, in a gentle metaphor, the re-ensoulment of the body each morning upon waking.

It is a charming and instructive reading, locating these elevated spiritual categories not only in the lives of great mystics and scholars but in the structured rhythm of daily Jewish life.

### Chasidus vs. Humility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עֲנָוָה גְּדוֹלָה מִכּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רוּחַ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים עָלָי יַעַן מָשַׁח ה׳ אֹתִי לְבַשֵּׂר עֲנָוִים״, ״חֲסִידִים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״עֲנָוִים״, הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁעֲנָוָה גְּדוֹלָה מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this statement **disagrees** with the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Humility is greater than all of them, as it is stated: "The spirit of the Lord God is upon me; because the Lord has anointed me to bring good tidings to the humble"** (Isaiah 61:1). Since **the pious is not stated,** but **rather "the humble," you learn that humility is greater than all of them.**

The *gemara* records a dispute about which quality is the highest rung before *ruach ha-kodesh*. The text as cited by Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair places *chasidut* at the top, supported by the verse *az dibarta be-chazon la-chasidecha* — then You spoke in a vision to Your *chasidim* — implying that *chasidut* immediately precedes *ruach ha-kodesh*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagrees: *anavah gedolah mi-kulam* — humility is the greatest of all. His proof is from the verse *ruach Hashem Elokim alai ya'an mashach Hashem oti levasser anavim* — the spirit of God is upon me, for God has anointed me to bring good tidings to the *anavim*, the humble. Since *anavim* rather than *chasidim* is the quality invoked in association with *ruach ha-kodesh*, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi concludes that *anavah* is the higher rung. The word *ya'an* — "because" — signals that *anavah* is the *reason* for the bestowing of *ruach ha-kodesh*, making it causally, and therefore hierarchically, more fundamental.
