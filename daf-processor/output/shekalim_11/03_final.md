# Shekalim 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumat HaLishka Uses

### Omer & Shabbat

Before turning to the daf proper, it is worth clarifying a point from the previous day's discussion about the cutting of the *omer* and its relationship to *Shabbat*. The question arose whether the *omer* could be cut before the appointed day — and if so, whether it would be *bedi'eved kasher* to do so in advance. According to the Bavli, both propositions cannot coexist: if cutting the *omer* early is valid *bedi'eved*, then it would never be necessary to override *Shabbat*, since one could simply cut it beforehand. There is some question regarding the *psak* of the Rambam on this point, but the basic tension between the two positions is clear.

### Terumat HaLishka Overview

We also ended the previous daf with a long list of permitted uses for *terumat ha-lishka* — the funds removed from the Temple treasury chamber. This requires some framing. An earlier *mishnah* had stated that *terumat ha-lishka* is designated exclusively for *korban tzibur*, the communal sacrifices. It did include the ancillary cost of stationing guards over the grain for the *omer*, but the emphasis was squarely on the *korban tzibur* itself. By contrast, the *mishnah* under current discussion (Mishnah 2) demonstrates just how narrowly defined that principle really is: one may spend *terumat ha-lishka* on the *parah adumah* itself, but not on the ramp constructed for its ritual — only what pertains directly to the *korban tzibur* qualifies.

Yet the list at the bottom of 10b appears to contradict this by enumerating a host of non-sacrificial expenditures funded by *terumat ha-lishka*: rabbinic students who taught the *kohanim* the laws of *korbanot*, the *shnei dayanei gzeirot* (or according to some, *gzeilot*) — judges in Yerushalayim who maintained fixed salaries to adjudicate ongoing cases — and even the women who wove the *parochet*. What any of this has to do with *korban tzibur* is not immediately apparent.

The *Mishnat Eliyahu*, one of the commentaries on the Vilna printing based on the *Gra*, addresses this. It cites a *Rashi* in Kesubos who explains that *beit din* was empowered to stipulate from the outset that certain funds would be directed toward these purposes, and that such funds would therefore never have been considered sanctified for the standard purpose of *korban tzibur*. This is the principle of *lev beit din matneh* — the rabbis can condition a sanctification at its very inception, such that designated sums are retroactively deemed never to have held the ordinary *kedushah*. The *Mishnat Eliyahu* adds further: the verse prohibiting the maintenance of an unchecked *sefer Torah* — *al tishkan be-oholecha avla* — obligated individuals to have their scrolls examined, and when *beit din* observed that the public was neglecting this duty, they directed *terumat ha-lishka* toward paying professional proofreaders to do the checking.

It is worth pausing to appreciate the broader structure of these funds. The *terumat ha-lishka* was the primary fiscal mechanism for collecting revenue from the entire Jewish population — not only those in the Land of Israel — for the needs of the *beit ha-mikdash*. From the outset, the authorities knew that far more money would be collected than was needed for the *korbanot tzibur* alone. The *shiyarei ha-lishka* — the funds remaining after the three baskets were drawn — was in a very real sense a planned operating surplus, not an accidental one. This explains why the *mishnah* could allocate the *shiyarei ha-lishka* even toward projects as expansive as repairing the city walls of Yerushalayim (*chomat ha-ir u-migdaloteha mi-shiyarei ha-lishka*). What rested under the tarp — the funds already processed through the *terumah* procedure — constituted the *shiyarei ha-lishka* and could be applied to a wide range of purposes. The two subsequent rounds of *terumah* drawn from newly arriving provincial contributions were then placed on top of the tarp. The whole system appears to have been structured with this intentional surplus in mind.

### Magihei Sifrei

One of the items in that list merits special attention: the *magihei sifrei ha-azarah* — those who would proofread and correct the *sifrei Torah* kept in the Temple courtyard, or perhaps the communal scrolls used there. The precise scope of their work is itself a matter of discussion. One interpretation holds that they circulated in Yerushalayim checking individuals' *sifrei Torah* against the authoritative temple copy; another holds that the reference is to the *sefer Torah* read by the *kohen gadol* in the *azarah* itself.

### Three Torah Scrolls

This brings us to a famous and striking source in *Avot de-Rabbi Natan* (paralleled also in the *Sifrei* on the *pasuk* in *Vezot ha-Berachah*, *me'onah Elohei kedem*), which illuminates what the proofreading work of the *magihei sifrei ha-azarah* may have been responding to.

*Gimel sefarim nimtze'u ba-azarah* — three *sifrei Torah* were found in the Temple courtyard. The *baraita* does not specify exactly when, but the episode appears to describe a one-time authoritative adjudication of textual variants. The three scrolls diverged from one another in several places, and the procedure adopted was to follow the reading attested by two of the three scrolls against the one.

### Textual Variants

The three variant pairs the *baraita* records are as follows. The first scroll was called *sefer me'onah*, because it read *me'on Elohei kedem*, while the other two read *me'onah* — the form we have today. The majority prevailed and *me'onah* was retained. The second scroll was called *sefer za'atuti*, because it read *za'atutei bnei Yisrael* in a passage where the other two read *vayishlach et na'arei bnei Yisrael* — an entirely unfamiliar word in place of *na'arei*. Again the two-scroll majority was followed. The third scroll was called *sefer hi*, because it recorded eleven instances where *hi* was spelled *heh-yud-aleph* rather than *heh-vav-aleph*; the other two agreed on a different count, and again the majority ruled.

This is a remarkable source for questions surrounding the text of the *Torah* and the existence of early textual variants. The connection to the *magihei sifrei ha-azarah* becomes clear: once the authoritative text was established, the temple scrolls served as the standard against which all other *sifrei Torah* were checked. The proofreaders' function was thus to preserve and propagate the canonically settled text. This episode also stands as an early precursor to the later work of the Masoretes — who developed a comprehensive system of counting every letter to ensure the text would never again be subject to such uncertainty — though that enterprise came many centuries later.

## Braita: Fund Sources

### Ketoret & Korbanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה. תַּנָּא רִבִּי יְהוּדָה גְרוֹגְרוֹת. הַקְּטוֹרֶת וְכָל קָרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּיבּוּר בָּאִין מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizqiah said that Rebbi Jehudah Dried Figs stated: The incense and all public sacrifices come from the disbursement from the lodge.

We now pick up at the opening of 11a. The *amora* Rabbi Chizkiyah cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Yehudah, which enumerates in organized fashion what each fund is used for. The *baraita* begins precisely where one would expect: *ha-ketoret ve-chol korbanot ha-tzibur ba'in mi-terumat ha-lishka* — the *ketoret* and all communal sacrifices are purchased from *terumat ha-lishka*. This is entirely consistent with the purpose for which the half-shekel was collected.

### Altars & Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב וְכָל כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת בָּאִין מִמּוֹתָרֵי נְסָכִים. מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה וְהַהֵיכָל וְהָעֲזָרוֹת בְּאִין מִשְּׁיֵּרֵי לִשְׁכָּה. חוּץ לָעֲזָרוֹת בָּאִין מִלִּשְׁכַּת בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The golden altar and vessels for the Service come from the excess of libations. The altar for elevation sacrifices, the building, and the courtyards come from the leftover in the lodge. The outside of the courtyards come from the lodge for maintenance of the House.

The *baraita* then turns to the gold altar and all the sacred vessels: *mizbe'ach ha-zahav ve-chol klei shareit ba'in mi-motar nesachim* — these come from the surplus funds of the *nesachim*, the wine and flour libations accompanying the *korbanot*. We will return later to the precise mechanism by which a surplus arose from the *nesachim*. For now, the logic is that since the *nesachim* are burned entirely on the altar with no human benefit, any surplus from them is appropriately directed toward other Temple objects that are similarly wholly devoted to the *beit ha-mikdash* — either the *mizbe'ach* or the *klei shareit*. How large a surplus the *motar nesachim* actually generated, given the expense of maintaining the golden altar and all the sacred vessels, will become clearer when we examine the *nesachim* system in detail.

The *mizbe'ach ha-olah* — the outer altar for burnt offerings — along with the *heichal* and the *azarot*, any repairs needed for these spaces, would come from *shiyarei ha-lishka*. As already noted, the *shiyarei ha-lishka* could be applied to an extraordinarily broad range of purposes.

### Interior vs. Exterior

The *baraita* then distinguishes between the interior Temple spaces and those beyond the *azarah*: repairs needed *chutz la-azarot* — outside the *azarah* proper, in external areas — would come from *lishkat bedek ha-bayit*, a dedicated fund supported by voluntary donations specifically for Temple repair. This creates an apparent anomaly: one would naturally assume that funds donated to *bedek ha-bayit* would be used for the interior and most sacred parts of the structure, yet the *baraita* allocates those funds to the exterior while directing *shiyarei ha-lishka* — the less sacred surplus — toward the inner spaces.

The *Gra* resolves this by pointing out that the Bavli's version of this *tosefta* reverses the order: in the Bavli's *girsa*, the *mizbe'ach ha-olah*, the *heichal*, and the *azarot* are funded from *bedek ha-bayit*, while repairs to areas outside the *azarah* come from *shiyarei ha-lishka*. This is far more intuitive: the sanctified donations of *bedek ha-bayit* fund the sanctified interior, while the desanctified *shiyarei ha-lishka* — which could even go toward city walls — covers the outer perimeter. The *Gra* accordingly emends the *baraita* in our text accordingly.

### Me'ilah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַנֵּי. אַבְנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַהֵיכָל וְהָעֲזָרוֹת מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן. וְכִי יֵשׁ מְעִילָה בִּשְׁיֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** But was it not stated: One commits *me`ilah* with stones of the Temple or the courtyards. How can one commit *me`ilah* with leftovers?

The *gemara* then raises a pointed difficulty. A separate *baraita* teaches that *avnei ha-mizbe'ach ha-heichal ve-ha-azarah mo'alin bahem* — one can transgress *me'ilah* with respect to the stones of the *mizbe'ach*, the *heichal*, and the *azarah*. According to the Yerushalmi's *girsa*, these structures were funded from *shiyarei ha-lishka*. But *shiyarei ha-lishka*, as we learned, was desanctified once the *terumah* was drawn. The *gemara* therefore asks: *v'chi yesh me'ilah bi-shiyarayim?* — can there be *me'ilah* with respect to desanctified funds and the objects purchased with them?

### Rabbi Meir Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְרִבִּי מֵאִיר. דְּרִבִּי מֵאִיר אָמַר. מוֹעֲלִין בִּשְׁיֵרִיִים. אָמַר רִבִּי חִינְנָא. כְּלוּם אָמַר רִבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא בְתוֹךְ שְׁנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** But it must follow Rebbi Meïr, for Rebbi Meïr said, one commits *me`ilah* with leftovers. Rebbi Ḥinena said, Rebbi Meïr said this only during its year.

The *gemara* answers: the *baraita* follows the position of Rabbi Meir, who held that *mo'alin bi-shiyarayim* — the *shiyarayim* do retain *kedushah* even after the *terumah* is drawn. Rabbi Meir's reasoning was that those funds might still be needed during the year for *korbanot tzibur* should the primary funds run short, and therefore their *kedushah* remains operative.

Rabbi Chiya sharpens this: Rabbi Meir's position that the *shiyarayim* retain *kedushah* applies only *betoch shnato* — within the year of that *shekel* — because that is the year in which one might still draw on those funds for *korbanot*. At year's end, once those funds can no longer be used for that year's *korbanot*, their *kedushah* lapses. This creates a further difficulty: according to Rabbi Meir, you cannot draw on the *shiyarei ha-lishka* during the year for construction purposes — those funds must remain available for *korbanot tzibur*. But once the year ends and you can finally use them for construction, they have already become desanctified. The *me'ilah* question thus reasserts itself.

This is precisely why the Bavli's reversed *girsa* is superior: if the interior structures are funded from *bedek ha-bayit* — sanctified voluntary donations — then *me'ilah* applies straightforwardly. The external areas, funded from *shiyarei ha-lishka*, would not carry *me'ilah*, and no contradiction arises. The Yerushalmi's *girsa*, which reverses this, is left in genuine difficulty.

It is worth noting that the Rambam rules in accordance with the Yerushalmi here, a decision that the *Kesef Mishneh* finds puzzling given the Rambam's general stated principle of following the Bavli. One resolution sometimes offered is that in tractate Shekalim — where the Yerushalmi constitutes the primary and natural locus of discussion — the Rambam felt justified in preferring the Yerushalmi over the Bavli. More broadly, careful scholarship has shown that the Rambam did not uniformly subordinate the Yerushalmi; in cases where he deemed it appropriate, he would rule according to Tannaitic sources, the Yerushalmi, or the *Tosefta* even against the Bavli.

## Korban Requirements

### Four Items Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשֻּׁלְחָן וְהַמְּנוֹרָה וְהַמִּזְבְּחוֹת וְהַפְּרָכוֹת מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַקָּרְבָּן. דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְ‌ְרִין אֵין לָךְ מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַקָּרְבָּן אֶלָּא הַכֵּיּוֹר וְהַכָּן בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The table, the candelabrum, the altars, and the gobelins obstruct the sacrifice, the words of Rebbi Meïr. But the Sages say, the sacrifice is only obstructed by the wash basin and its base.

Still within Rabbi Chizkiyah's citation of Rabbi Yehudah's *baraita*, the discussion shifts from questions of funding to the minimum ritual prerequisites for offering *korbanot*. The first position states: *ha-shulchan ve-ha-menorah ve-ha-mizbechot ve-ha-parochet me'akvin et ha-korbanot* — the table (*shulchan*), the candelabrum (*menorah*), the altars (*mizbechot*), and the curtain (*parochet*) are all prerequisites that impede the bringing of *korbanot* if absent. This is a striking claim: these are all features of the *heichal*, the inner sanctum, yet *korbanot* are offered in the outer courtyard. One of the *mefarshim* suggests reading this functionally — each of these is required for its particular associated service: the *parochet* for the *Yom Kippur* service, the *menorah* for the lighting, the *shulchan* for the *lechem ha-panim*. In their absence, the corresponding service cannot be performed.

### Chakhamim Opinion

The *chakhamim* disagree sharply: *ein lecha me'akev et ha-korban ella ha-kiyor ve-chano bilvad* — the sole prerequisite for bringing *korbanot* is the laver and its base, the *kiyor* and the *kan*. None of the interior furnishings are indispensable. The *Gra* is dissatisfied with this *girsa* and emends it to read *ha-karkov ve-ha-keren* — features of the outer *mizbe'ach* — arguing that what is logically indispensable is a properly constituted altar, not the laver. His emendation is based on a parallel Bavli discussion elsewhere. Regardless, neither the first opinion nor the *chakhamim* here cite their *pesukim*, which is unusual.

The broader context of this debate is the well-known principle in the Bavli, cited in Megilla and elsewhere, that *makrivin af al pi she-ein bayit* — one may offer *korbanot* even without a standing *beit ha-mikdash*. This becomes practically significant in contemporary discussions about ascending to the Temple Mount and offering a *korban pesach*, and the question naturally arises: what is the minimum institutional infrastructure required? This *baraita* addresses exactly that question.

### Interior vs. Exterior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה. כָּאן בָּעֲבוֹדוֹת שֶׁבִּפְנִים וְכָאן בָּעֲבוֹדוֹת שֶׁבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥananiah said, here for service inside, there for service outside.

Rabbi Chanina provides the resolution: *kan ba-avodas she-bifnim, kan ba-avodas she-ba-chutz* — the two positions are not actually in conflict; they address different domains. For the outer service — the standard *korbanot* on the outer altar — the only prerequisite is the *kiyor* and the *kan*; that is the only required element in the outer space. For the inner service — the burning of *ketoret*, the *Yom Kippur avodah*, and related rituals — one requires the *shulchan*, the *menorah*, and the other interior furnishings.

The *gemara* had also noted positions by Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yose bar Chanina, both of whom derive these requirements from the word *nochach* in the *Torah* — wherever a Temple object is described as positioned *nochach* (opposite/facing) something else, that object is indispensable; where the text uses *tzela* (side) or *simah* (placement), various other views disagree about whether *me'akeiv* status applies. The difficulty that all of these positions raise is that in *beit sheni* there was no *aron* — yet the inner *avodot* were performed. How this was managed according to any opinion that treats the *aron* as *me'akeiv* remains unresolved.

## Parah Adumah

### High Priests Arrogance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. שַׁחַצִית גְּדוֹלָה הָ‍יְתָה בִּבְנֵי כֹהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים. שֶׁיּוֹתֵר מִשִּׁשִּׁים כִּכְּרֵי זָהָב הָיוּ מוֹצִיאִין בָּהּ. הָיָה כִבְשָׁהּ שֶׁלְפָּרָה עוֹמֵד וְלֹא הָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן מוֹצִיא פָּרָתוֹ בְכִבְשׁוֹ שֶׁלְחֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא סוֹתְרוֹ וּבוֹנֶה אוֹתוֹ מִשֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥanina said, there was much arrogance among the sons of the High Priests, for more than 60 talents of gold did they spend on it. If the ramp for the Cow was still standing, no one was leading out his cow on the other's ramp, but he tore it down and was building it anew.

Rabbi Chanina opens a new topic: *shachatzit gedolah hayetah bi-vnei kohanim gedolim* — there was a great haughtiness among the *kohanim gedolim*. The *mishnah* had taught that the *kohen gadol* would spend his own personal funds to build the ramp over which the *parah adumah* was led to the place of burning. Rabbi Chanina characterizes this practice as an expression of arrogance: they would spend in excess of sixty talents of gold on such a ramp, and *shehayah kivsho shel parah omed* — even though a ramp from the previous *parah* ceremony already existed, *velo hayah echad mehen motzi parato be-kivsho shel chavero* — no *kohen gadol* would use the ramp built by his predecessor. Each would demolish the existing ramp and construct a new one at his own expense.

### Shimon HaTzadik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִיב רִבִּי עוּלָּא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי מָנָא. וְהָא תַנֵּי. שִׁמְעוֹן הַצַּדִּיק שְׁתֵּי פָרוֹת עָשָׂה. לֹא בְכֶבֶשׁ שֶׁהוֹצִיא אֶת זוֹ הוֹצִיא אֶת זוֹ. אִית לָךְ מֵימַר. שִׁמְעוֹן הַצַּדִּיק שִׁיחֵץ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ulla objected before Rebbi Mana: Was it not stated that Simeon the Just made two Cows; on the ramp on which he took out one he did not take out the other. Could you say that Simeon the Just was arrogant?

Rebbi Ulla challenges this characterization before Rebbi Mana from a *baraita*: Shimon HaTzadik, during his long tenure as *kohen gadol*, presided over two *parah adumah* ceremonies, and the *baraita* explicitly states *lo be-kivesh she-hotzi et zo hotzi et zo* — the ramp used for the first ceremony was not the one used for the second. The objection is pointed: *is lach le-meimar Shimon HaTzadik shachatz hayah?* — would you say that Shimon HaTzadik was motivated by arrogance? Clearly there is a substantive requirement to use a freshly dedicated ramp for each ceremony, independent of any personal vanity. The individual *kohanim gedolim* may have been guilty of excessive expenditure, but the practice of constructing a new ramp was not itself rooted in mere arrogance.

### Parah Ornamentation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַיי כְדוֹן. עַל שֶׁם מַעֲלָה הִיא בְפָרָה סִילְסוּל הוּא בְפָרָה. תַּנֵּי. זִיזִין וּכְתָלִין הָיוּ יוֹצְאִין מִיכָּן וּמִיכָּן כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָצִיצוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים וְיִטַּמּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** What about it? Because they gave eminence to the Cow, ornaments they made for the Cow. It was stated: Ledges and walls were on both sides so that the priests could not look down and become impure.

The resolution offered is that the special treatment accorded to the *parah* ceremony reflects *silsul* — a kind of elevation and ornamentation deliberately cultivated around this ritual. Numerous special stringencies surrounded the *parah adumah*, some of them also linked to the ongoing debates with the *Tzeddukim*, who interpreted its laws differently. In certain areas the rabbis were more lenient specifically to undercut the *Tzeddukim*'s position; in others they were exceptionally stringent, and the distinctive treatment of the ramp belongs to this larger pattern.

A *baraita* adds: *zizin u-ktalim hayu yotzin mikan u-mikan* — protrusions and walls jutted out from both sides of the ramp, *kedei she-lo yeitzu ve-yatzitzu ha-kohanim ve-yitame'u* — so that the *kohanim* would not lean over the sides, lose their footing, or otherwise risk becoming *tamei* through inadvertent contact with something below. The ramp was therefore ornamented and reinforced not merely for aesthetic reasons but to maintain the ritual purity of everyone involved in the ceremony.

## Year-End Surplus

### Yishmael Business

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקְחִין בָּהֶן יֵינוֹת שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת וְהַשָּׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל
>
> **Translation:** One buys with it wine, oil, and fine flour, and the gain is the Temple's, the words of Rebbi Ismael.

The discussion now turns to what is done with leftover *shiyarei ha-lishka* — the desanctified surplus — at the end of the year. Rabbi Yishmael holds that one may purchase oil, wine, and flour with those funds and sell them to pilgrims and visitors in Yerushalayim, pocketing the difference as profit for *hekdesh*.

### Akiva Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה אוֹמֵר אֵין מִשְׁתַּכְּרִין בְּשֶּׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ אַף לֹא מִשֶּׁל עֲנִיִּים
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aqiba says, one gains neither for the Temple nor funds for the poor.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *ein mishtakrin be-shel hekdesh* — one does not engage in business ventures with *hekdesh* funds. He applies the same prohibition to funds held in trust for the poor. The question underlying the dispute is whether the objection is one of propriety — it looks unseemly for *hekdesh* to be operating commercial enterprises — or whether it is a concern about the risk inherent in any business venture. One does not expose funds that belong to others, whether to *hekdesh* or to the poor, to the possibility of loss.

### Risk & Investment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִם רָצָה יְהֵא הַהֶפְסֵד שֶׁלּוֹ. וְהַשָּׂכָר לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** But only if he wants that the loss be his and the gain for the endowment it is permitted.

The *gemara* resolves this tension by means of a conditional arrangement: *im bayit di-yiheh hefseida didach ve-agra de-tarveichon* — if the investor is willing to absorb any loss personally while splitting any profit with *hekdesh*, then *shari*, it is permitted. Under such terms the *hekdesh* funds face no risk; the investor functions as a guarantor of the principal, and whatever commercial gain results is shared. Even Rabbi Akiva would agree to this structure, since the concern about exposing trust funds to risk is entirely neutralized.

### Entrusted Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּהָדָא בַּר זְמִינָא אִת פְקִיד גַּבֵּיהּ מֵדַל דְּיַתְמִין. אֲתַא וּשְׁאַל לְרִבִּי מָנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. אִין בְּעִית דִּי הֶפְסֵדְה דִידָךְ וְאַגְרָה דִתְּרַוֵיכוֹן שָׁרֵי. רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר אָדָא אִתְפְקִיד גַּבֵּיהּ מֵדַל דְּיַתְמִין וַעֲבַד כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** As the following: Bar Zemina was appointed for property of orphans. He came and asked Rebbi Mana, who told him, if you want that the loss be yours and the gain be split among you it is permitted. Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Ada was appointed for property of orphans and acted in this way.

Two parallel cases illustrate this principle. Rabbi Chiya bar Adda was entrusted with the estate funds (*midal de-yatmi*) of orphans and wished to invest them. He approached Rabbi Imi, who ruled: if you are willing to bear any losses yourself and to share the profits equally, it is permitted. Similarly, Rabbi Chiya bar Adda himself acted on the same terms when similarly entrusted. The *gemara* acknowledges that this arrangement has some of the structural characteristics of a loan with interest (*ribbit*): the investor guarantees return of principal (making the relationship resemble a loan) while also receiving a share of profits. Ordinarily such an arrangement would be rabbinically problematic, but the authorities permitted it here for the benefit of orphans.

The conceptual boldness of permitting even *hekdesh* to benefit from this arrangement deserves emphasis. The plain meaning of *ein mishtakrin* suggests something intrinsically problematic about *hekdesh* engaging in commerce — not merely a concern about risk, but a dignity concern: one does not want *hekdesh*, which is sustained by sacred collections, to be seen operating as a merchant. Yet the *gemara* frames the entire prohibition as purely a risk-management question and permits the arrangement as soon as the risk is removed.

## Motar Peirot

### Yishmael Profits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתַר שְׁיֵרֵי לִשְׁכָּה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן. לוֹקְחִין בָּהֶן יֵינוֹת שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת וְהַשָּׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה אוֹמֵר אֵין מִשְׁתַּכְּרִין בְּשֶּׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ אַף לֹא מִשֶּׁל עֲנִיִּים. מוֹתַר הַתְּרוּמָה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ. רִקּוּעֵי זָהָב צִיפּוּי לְבֵית קָדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר מוֹתַר הַפֵּירוֹת קַיָיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** What did one do with the leftover in the lodge? One buys with it wine, oil, and fine flour, and the gain is the Temple's, the words of Rebbi Ismael. Rebbi Aqiba says, one gains neither for the Temple nor funds for the poor. What did they use the leftover of the disbursement for? Gold sheets coating for the Holiest of Holies. Rebbi Ismael said, the leftover of produce is for adornment of the altar.

The *mishnah* contains an ambiguous reference to *motar peirot* — the leftover of the fruits — and states that Rabbi Yishmael directed these funds toward *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach*, the voluntary *olot* brought when the outer altar would otherwise stand idle.

### Rejection Motar

Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Chanina Sgan ha-Kohanim are recorded as not having accepted the category of *motar peirot* at all. The *gemara* now works to determine what *motar peirot* actually means, and why Rabbi Akiva rejected it.

### Fourth Se'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר יוֹסֵף פָּתַר מַתְנִיתָא. מוֹתַר פֵּירוֹת שָׂכָר לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ. מוֹתַר נְסָכִים זוֹ סְאָה רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Joseph explains the Mishnah: The leftover of produce is the gain of the Temple; the leftover of libations is the fourth *seah*.

Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef explains the *mishnah* as follows: *motar peirot* refers to *sechar la-hekdesh* — the profit the Temple made by going into business with the *shiyarei ha-lishka* funds, in the manner Rabbi Yishmael described. *Motar nesachim*, by contrast, is identified as *zu se'ah revi'it* — "the fourth *se'ah*." To understand this, one must appreciate the Temple's commercial arrangements with its suppliers of *nesachim*. A supplier would contract at the beginning of the year to provide, say, four *se'ah* of wine per *shekel*. If prices rose during the year so that the market rate fell to three *se'ah* per *shekel*, the supplier was still contractually obligated to deliver four. The Temple always captured the benefit of price fluctuations in its favor while bearing none of the downside. The surplus that accumulated through this systematic advantage is the *motar nesachim* — what the *gemara* here calls *se'ah revi'it*, the "fourth *se'ah*" that the market no longer required the supplier to provide but the contract still did.

On this reading, Rabbi Akiva's rejection of *motar peirot* is entirely consistent: since *motar peirot* means the profit from commercial activity, and Rabbi Akiva prohibits *hekdesh* from engaging in commerce, he naturally refused to acknowledge this category.

### Heaping Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן פָּתַר מַתְנִיתָא. מוֹתַר פֵּירוֹת זוֹ סְאָה רְבִיעית. מוֹתַר נְסָכִים לְבֵירוּצִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan explains the Mishnah, The leftover of produce is the fourth *seah;* the leftover of libations is the overflow.

Rabbi Yochanan interprets the *mishnah* differently. According to him, *motar peirot* is the *se'ah revi'it* — the price-fluctuation surplus — and *motar nispacham* is *le-beirutzin*, the profit from heaping measures. When the Temple purchased liquid commodities, the supplier was required to fill the measuring vessel without smoothing off the top; any amount that heaped above the rim belonged to the Temple. When the Temple subsequently sold that commodity, it would measure out precise, level quantities. The cumulative difference between heaped purchases and level sales generated a consistent and not insignificant profit stream, particularly given the extraordinary volume of *nesachim* consumed daily.

### Certification System

The *gemara* then asks: does this not constitute the Temple engaging in commerce — precisely what Rabbi Akiva prohibited? The answer lies in understanding the actual mechanism by which *nesachim* were supplied. Worshippers did not purchase their *nesachim* directly from Temple officials. Instead, upon arriving, a worshipper would pay a fixed price to a Temple administrator and receive receipts (*pitkin*) specifying the type and quantity of *nesachim* required. The worshipper then proceeded to an independent contractor who would exchange the receipts for the actual *nesachim*. At the end of the day, the contractor returned to the Temple with the receipts and was reimbursed. Officially, the Temple was never selling anything directly; it was merely reimbursing a contractor. Yet since the Temple was the ultimate buyer of all the commodities, it was positioned to capture the benefit of heaping measures at the point of purchase, while the contractor measured out level quantities to the worshippers. The net surplus accumulated to the Temple through this intermediated structure.

Even Rabbi Akiva concedes the legitimacy of this arrangement, since the Temple never directly engaged in buying and selling with the public. The certified-contractor system insulated *hekdesh* from direct commerce. Given the volume of *korbanot* brought daily, even a fractional percentage of surplus per transaction compounded into substantial *motar nispacham*.

### Business Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל דַּעְתִֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן קַשְׁיָא. תַּנִּינָן עָ‍מְדוּ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ יְסַפֵּק מֵאַרְבַּע. וְתַנִּינָן. וְזֶה וָזֶה לֹא הָיוּ מוֹדִין בַּפֵּירוֹת לֹא הָיוּ מוֹדִין בַפֵּירוֹת בְּקַיִיץ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ. אֲבָל מוֹדִין הָיוּ בִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The opinion of Rebbi Joḥanan is difficult. We have stated: "if the going rate was three, he has to deliver for four" and we have stated, "neither of them did agree about produce." They did not agree about produce to adorn the altar; they did agree for Service vessels.

The *gemara* then asks: given that Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef identifies *motar peirot* as the profit from business ventures, it is clear why Rabbi Akiva rejected it — he prohibits *hekdesh* from engaging in business. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who identifies *motar peirot* with the price-fluctuation surplus (*se'ah revi'it*), why did Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Chanina Sgan ha-Kohanim decline to endorse *motar peirot*? The price-fluctuation mechanism is a contractual advantage, not a commercial venture — everyone agrees to it. The *gemara* answers: they did not reject the *phenomenon* of *motar peirot*; they rejected only what Rabbi Yishmael said you do with it. Rabbi Yishmael directed these funds to *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach*; Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Chanina held that such funds should go instead toward *klei shareit*, because *motar nesachim* funds are designated for *klei shareit*, and *motar peirot* is essentially a form of *motar nesachim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כְּדוֹן בֵּירוּצֵי צִיבּוּר. וַאֲפִילוּ בֵירוּצֵי יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** So far the overflow of public {sacrifices}. Even overflow for private {sacrifices}.

The *gemara* then raises the question of *beirutzei yachid* — profits from heaping in the context of individual, private *korbanot*. Most of the *nesachim* purchased through the contractor system were funded by private individuals, not communal funds. If the heaping surplus derives from individual expenditure, how can it be channeled toward communal purposes like *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach* or *klei shareit*? The *gemara* invokes a principle: as long as an individual fully transfers what he has given over to the public domain — as the woman who wove a priestly garment and donated it to the *tzibur* renders it fully communal — individually sourced funds that are entirely surrendered can be used for communal purposes. The private source of origin does not permanently characterize them. One further question is raised concerning *beirutzei yavesh* — heaping of dry measures, as distinct from liquid. The distinction may rest on whether the heaping material ever occupied the interior of the sanctified vessel: liquid that fills a vessel touches its walls and airspace and is thereby sanctified; dry goods that heap above the rim were never within the vessel's contained airspace. Whether the Temple could claim that surplus as well, and on what grounds, is left without a fully satisfying resolution.

## Motar HaKetoret

### Ketoret Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: מוֹתַר הַקְּטוֹרֶת מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ מַפְרִישִׁין מִמֶּנָּה שְׂכַר הָאוּמָּנִים וּמְחַלְּלִין אוֹתָהּ עַל מָעוֹת הָאוּמָּנִים וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָהּ לָאוּמָּנִים בִּשְׁכָרָן וְחוֹזְרִין וְלוֹקְחִין אוֹתָהּ מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה. וְאִם בָּא חָדָשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ לוֹקְחִין אוֹתָהּ מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה וְאִם לָאו מִן הַיְּשָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: What did one do with the excess of incense? One sets apart the artisans' wages and redeems them on the artisan's money; one gives them to the artisans as their wages and then buys them back from them from the new disbursement. If the new money is available at its time one takes them from the new disbursement, otherwise from the old.

The *mishnah* now turns to *motar ha-ketoret* — the handling of year-end surplus *ketoret*. The practice was to purchase, at the start of each year, 365 portions (*manot*) of *ketoret* — one for each day of the solar year — plus three additional portions for the *Yom Kippur* service, totaling 368. The difficulty is structural: the *shekalim* cycle and the *ketoret* purchase were calibrated on a lunar year basis, which is shorter than the solar year. Consequently, there was a regular annual surplus. Over several years this surplus could theoretically be absorbed in a leap year, but this created a serious problem: *korban tzibur* must be purchased with that year's consecrated funds. The leftover *ketoret* from the previous year retained its old year's *kedushah* and could not simply be carried over and used as though it were newly sanctified. How, then, was the surplus *ketoret* reintegrated into the next year's service?

### Desanctification

The *mishnah* describes the solution: *mafrishin mimenu sechar ha-umnin* — one designates a portion of the surplus *ketoret* as the wage owed to the craftsmen (*umnin*) who produced it. One then performs *challalah* — a transfer of *kedushah* — from the *ketoret* onto the coins that represent the workers' payment: *u-mechalelalin oto al me'ot ha-umnin*. The surplus *ketoret* is thereby desanctified. The *ketoret* is then repurchased with newly consecrated *shekalim* of the new year, and through the purchase it is re-sanctified under this year's *kedushah*. If the new funds are already available, the repurchase is made from them; if not, it is made from the old funds.

### Multi-Step Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: וְלֹא נִמְצָא הַהקֲדֵּשׁ מִתָחַלֵּל עַל הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. אָמַר רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר כַּרְסָנָה. מֵבִיא מָעוֹת וּמְחַלְלָן עַל הַבִּנְיָין וּמֵבִיא קְטוֹרֶת וּמְחַלְלָן עֲלֵיהֶן וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָן לָאוּמָנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: What is gained by redeeming incense with Temple money, which is not profane? What does he do? Rebbi Simeon bar Carsana said, he brings coins and redeems them for the building; brings incense and redeems it for them, and gives it to the artisans as their wages.

The *gemara* immediately asks: this transfer of *kedushah* onto the workers' wages — what exactly does it accomplish? The *umnin* cannot use sanctified funds; *kedushah* cannot simply land on an abstraction. *Velo nimtza hekdesh mitchalel al ha-melachah* — one cannot transfer *kedushah* onto labor, which is not a tangible object.

Rabbi Simon ben Bisna describes the precise multi-step procedure that resolves this. The administrator brings a sum of desanctified money — the amount owed to the *umnin*. He then transfers the *kedushah* of that money onto some physical object in the Temple, say the masonry of the building (*ha-binyan*), thereby stripping the money of its *kedushah* and leaving it as ordinary currency. He then takes the *ketoret* that the *umnin* have delivered — which the *umnin* own, having supplied their own raw materials and labor — and transfers the *kedushah* of the *ketoret* onto that money. The *ketoret* is thereby desanctified.

### Umnin Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנִין אוֹתָן לָאוּמָנִין בִּשְׂכָרָן.
>
> **Translation:** and gives it to the artisans as their wages.

Now the *umnin* are paid: *ve-notnin otah le-umnin be-scharan* — the desanctified *ketoret* is given to them as their wages. The craftsmen, who had delivered the finished *ketoret* and are owed payment, receive the *ketoret* itself back as compensation in lieu of cash. The analogy is apt: they come and deliver a finished product worth a certain amount, and instead of cash, they are paid with the equivalent value of the very product they delivered.

### Repurchasing

But now the Temple needs that *ketoret* for the coming year. So it immediately repurchases the *ketoret* from the *umnin* using newly consecrated *shekel* funds. Through this purchase, the *kedushah* of the new year's *shekalim* transfers onto the *ketoret*, and the money passes to the *umnin* as fully desanctified currency they can use freely. The entire sequence achieves what was needed: the old *kedushah* is removed from the *ketoret*, and new *kedushah* of the current year is applied to it through purchase with this year's consecrated funds.

### Remnant Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתָן הַמָּעוֹת מַה יֵעָשֶׂה בָהֶן. רִבִּי אוֹמֵר. אוֹמֵר אֲנִי. יִינָתְנוּ לְבֵית גַרְמוּ וּלְבֵית אַבְטִינָס שֶׁהָיוּ בְקִיאִים בְּפִיטּוּם הַקְּטוֹרֶת וּבְמַעֲשֶׂה לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים. אָמַר רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב. וְהֵן שֶׁיְּהוּ חַיָיבִין לָהֶן מָעוֹת מִשָּׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** What should be done with those coins? Rebbi says, I am saying that they should be given to the family Garmu and the family Eutinos who were experts in compounding the incense and in making the shew-bread. Rebbi Samuel bar Rav said, only if they were due to them beforehand.

This leaves a further question: after the *kedushah* of the *ketoret* was transferred onto the intermediate sum of money in the first step of the process, what becomes of that money? It now carries the *kedushah* of last year's *ketoret*.

*Rebbi* suggests it be given to the house of Garmo and the house of Avtinas — the families who held hereditary expertise in the preparation of the *ketoret* and the *lechem ha-panim* respectively — as payment for services rendered. Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak qualifies: this is appropriate only if the Temple in fact owed those families money from the prior year, since this money carries last year's *kedushah*. The *mefarshim* explain that using last year's *terumat ha-lishka*-derived funds for payment of services is consistent with what we established at the outset — that *lev beit din matneh* permits directing such funds toward certain non-*korban* Temple services.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר בָּא בָעֵי. לֹא הָיוּ חַיָיבִין לָהֶן מָעוֹת מִשָּׁעָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אָתָא רִבִּי בָּא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חִיָיה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחְנָן. מְקַיְּיצִין בָּהֶן אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Abba asked, what if they were not due beforehand? There came Rebbi Abba in the name of Rebbi Ḥiyya in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: One uses them to adorn the altar.

Rabbi Ba, citing Rabbi Chiya in the name of Rabbi Yosi, offers an alternative: use those funds for *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach* — the voluntary *olot* that fill idle time at the outer altar. This serves as the general fallback use for surplus sanctified funds.

### Ketoret Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי בָּא בַּר כֹּהֶן בְּעָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי יוֹסֶה. מִחְלְפָא שִׁיטָּתֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי חִיָיא בַּר בָּא. תַּמָּן צְרִיכָה לֵיהּ וְהָכָא פְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ. הֵן דִּצְרִיכָה לֵיהּ. בִּכְלֵי שָׂרֵת. הֵן דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ. בְּקַיָיץ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abba bar Cohen asked before Rebbi Yose: The opinion of Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Abba seems inverted. He told him, where it is questionable for him, for Service vessels; where it is obvious for him, to adorn the altar.

Rabbi Ba bar Kahana then raises what appears to be a contradiction in Rabbi Chiya bar Ba's statements, noting that in one place he seems uncertain about the proper use of these funds while in another he states it clearly. The *gemara* resolves this: when Rabbi Chiya bar Ba expressed uncertainty, he already knew the answer regarding *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach* — that is obvious. What he was uncertain about was whether the funds could also be directed toward *klei shareit*. That remained the open question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְפַּלְּגוֹן. פִיטְּמָהּ בַחוּלִין. רִבִּי יוֹסֶה בֵּירִבִּי חֲנִינָה אָמַר. פְּסוּלָה. רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לִֵוי אָמַר. כְּשֵׁירָה. מַה טַעֲמָא דְרִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֵּירִבִּי חֲנִינָה. קוֹדֶשׁ הִיא. שֶׁתְּהֵא הֲוָיָיתָהּ בַּקוֹדֶשׁ. מַה טַעֲמָא דְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. קוֹדֶשׁ הִיא. שֶׁתְּהֵא בָאָה מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּישְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** As they disagreed: If it was compounded as profane, Rebbi Yose ben Ḥanina said, it is disqualified; Rebbi Joshua ben Levi said, it is qualified. What is Rebbi Yose ben Ḥanina's reason? *It is holy*, that it shall be brought into the Sanctuary. What is Rebbi Joshua ben Levi's reason? *It is holy*, that it shall be brought from the disbursement from the lodge.

The daf closes with a debate on a related matter concerning the *ketoret* itself. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina rules: *pitma be-chulin — pesulah* — if the *ketoret* was ground in a non-sanctified vessel, the *ketoret* is invalid. His reasoning is the verse *kodesh hi* — it is holy — which he interprets as requiring that the very production of the *ketoret* occur within a sanctified vessel: *she-teheh havayatah ba-kodesh*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagrees and rules it valid: *kodesh hi* means only that the *ketoret* must be purchased from sanctified funds (*mi-terumat ha-lishka*); it does not require that the grinding itself take place in a *keli shareit*. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Bun notes that these two positions parallel the broader dispute between Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan on related questions of *kedushah* and production, and the discussion of their respective supports is left for the following daf.
