# Shabbat 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Forgetting Shabbat vs Me…

### Rav Safra's Explanation

The *gemara* opens *daf ayin* with a deceptively simple question: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what is the distinction between the first case of the *mishna* and the latter? In the opening case, one who forgot that it was *shabbat* brings a single *korban* regardless of how many *melachot* he performed; in the latter case, one who forgot the individual *melachot* brings a separate *korban* for each category violated. The difference seems self-evident, yet the *gemara* proceeds to articulate it with precision.

Rav Safra states: *kan yedi'at Shabbat hu poresh v'kan yedi'at melacha hu poresh* — in one case it is knowledge of *shabbat* that would have caused him to desist, and in the other it is knowledge of the *melacha*. The logic is straightforward: if the person had been told "it is *shabbat*," he would have stopped everything he was doing, because his single error was forgetting that the day was *shabbat*. All the various *melachot* he performed flow from that one mistake. In the second case, however, had he been reminded that a particular act was a *melacha*, he would have stopped only that act — and each separate *melacha* would have required its own reminder, its own correction, and therefore constitutes its own category of error.

### Rav Nachman's Challenge

Rav Nachman challenges this formulation, raising what initially appears to be a compelling objection: *klum poresh miShabbat ela mishum melachot?* — when one stops because he is told it is *shabbat*, does he not also stop because he now recognizes the act as a *melacha*? The two pieces of knowledge — that it is *shabbat* and that the act is forbidden — operate together in every case. Likewise, *v'klum poresh mi'melacha ela mishum Shabbat?* — when told his act is a *melacha*, he stops because both the identity of the day and the forbidden nature of the act are now clear to him.

His point seems to be that in both scenarios the person ultimately needs both pieces of information to desist, so how can Rav Safra's distinction hold?

Rav Nachman then offers his own formulation. The *korban* is brought *al shgaga* — on account of an error. *Hatam chada shgaga*: when one forgets it is *shabbat*, he has made a single error. *Hacha tuvah shgagot havinan*: when one forgets individual *melachot*, he has made multiple distinct errors — one about cooking, one about planting, and so on.

### Common Ground Between Vi…

On reflection, the two positions are expressing essentially the same point through slightly different language. Both agree that forgetting *shabbat* constitutes one mistake and forgetting individual *melachot* constitutes multiple mistakes. One framing emphasizes what would have caused the person to desist; the other emphasizes the number of distinct errors made. The practical significance of this subtle difference in articulation will become clearer later in the *daf*, where the case of forgetting *both* *shabbat* and the *melachot* simultaneously forces us to confront precisely how we define and count the nature of one's error.

## Chiluk Melachot - Source

### Question on Chiluk Melac…

The *gemara* then moves to a foundational question: *chiluk melachot minalan* — from where do we derive the principle that separate *melachot* generate separate *korbanot* altogether? This requires a source, because the assumption otherwise might be that all violations of *shabbat* constitute a single prohibition — much as there is one *lav* of *shabbat* — and therefore one *korban* regardless of how many categories were violated.

### Pesach Analogy

The force of this question is illustrated by analogy. Consider a person who, throughout the day of *Pesach*, forgets entirely that it is *Pesach* and eats bread, then pasta, then cookies. How many *chataot* does he bring? One — because it is a single sin. He violated one prohibition (*lo tochal chametz*) multiple times without any intervening moment of awareness that would divide the acts into separate obligations.

The same logic would seem to apply to *shabbat*: there is one prohibition of *melacha*, and even though we identify thirty-nine categories, perhaps they are all one large category of *chilul Shabbat*. Why should violating *katzir* and then *bishul* in a single state of forgetfulness be any different from eating bread and then pasta on *Pesach* — still just one *korban* for one prohibition? This is precisely the question the *gemara* is pressing: what gives the thirty-nine *melachot* the status of conceptually separate types of *chilul Shabbat*, each generating independent liability?

### Shmuel's Derivation

Shmuel answers: *amar kra*, the *pasuk* states *mechaleleha mos yumas* — those who desecrate it shall surely be put to death. The double language of *mos yumas* suggests, in Shmuel's reading, that the *Torah* has expressed a multiplicity of deaths for violations of *shabbat*, implying that a single act of *chilul Shabbat* can carry manifold liability. Since obviously a person cannot die more than once, this doubling cannot be referring to the *mezid* case — and indeed, *Tosafot* adds that the verse *kol ose melacha yumas* already establishes death for willful violation. The principle of *im eino inyan lemezid tenehu inyan leshogeg* therefore redirects this doubling to the inadvertent case, teaching that there can be multiple *korbanos* for a single period of *shabbat* — one for each *melacha* category violated.

The *gemara* clarifies that when speaking of the *shogeg* case, *yumas bemamon* — "death" is understood as monetary cost, that is, the obligation to bring a *korban*. Playful as that phrase sounds, it captures the *gemara*'s point precisely.

### Gemara Questions Shmuel

The *gemara* presses Shmuel: why resort to this derivation from *mechaleleha mos yumas* when there is a much more elegant Tannaitic source? *V'teipokei chiluk melachos meheicha d'nafka lei l'Rabbi Noson* — why not learn it out from where Rabbi Natan derived it?

## Rabbi Natan - Fire as Pa…

### Fire as Specific Melacha

The *braisa* records Rabbi Natan's position: *lo tiva'aru eish bechol moshvoseichem beyom haShabbos* — you shall not kindle fire in any of your dwelling places on *shabbat*. This is the one act of *melacha* that the *Torah* singles out by name in the context of *shabbat* prohibition. The question Rabbi Natan asks is: *mah talmud lomar* — what is the point of specifying fire? We already know fire is one of the thirty-nine *melachot*. Why does the *Torah* need to articulate it separately?

### Eileh Hadvarim - 39 Cate…

To understand Rabbi Natan's answer, the *gemara* traces the derivation of the thirty-nine categories themselves. The *Torah* prefaces the *shabbat* laws in *Vayakhel* with the phrase *eileh hadvarim* — these are the things. The *gemara* derives thirty-nine from this formulation: *dvarim*, *hadvarim*, *eileh hadvarim* — the word *dvarim* in its plural form combined with the *heh* of *hadvarim* and the *gematria* of *eileh* (36) yields thirty-nine. It is worth noting that Rav Yoel Bin-Nun proposed an alternative reading: rather than relying on *gematria*, he observed that if one counts the distinct items listed in the accounts of the *Mishkan* in *Vayakhel* and *Pikudei*, one arrives at precisely thirty-nine. In his reading, the *Torah* is indicating that the activities involved in building the *Mishkan* define the categories of *melacha*, and the phrase *eileh hadvarim* — these are the things — makes that explicit.

Either way, the number thirty-nine is established. What is *not* yet established from this derivation is that violating multiple categories generates multiple *korbanos*. Knowing there are thirty-nine forbidden acts does not by itself tell us that they function as thirty-nine independent prohibitions for purposes of *korban* liability.

### Davar Shehaya Baklal

This is where fire enters. The principle invoked here is *davar shehayah baklal v'yatza min haklal lilamed* — a law that was part of a general category and was singled out from that category was not singled out merely to teach something about itself alone, but to teach something about the entire category. Fire was already included among the thirty-nine. The *Torah* chose to single it out and state an explicit prohibition. That singling-out teaches us two things about fire: that it is an *av melacha*, one of the thirty-nine chief categories, and *v'chayavin aleha bifne atzma* — that one bears separate liability for it as a distinct act. And since this is established for fire through the principle of *davar shehayah baklal*, it applies to every one of the thirty-nine: *af kol she'hu av melacha chayavin alav bifne atzmo* — for any *av melacha*, one bears separate liability.

The logic thus proceeds in stages. First, we establish thirty-nine categories from *eileh hadvarim*. Second, we establish that violating even one of them — not necessarily all thirty-nine — already constitutes a violation of *shabbat*, which we learn from the verse *b'charish uv'katzir tishbot*, where the *Torah* specifically names *charish* (plowing) and *katzir* (harvesting). Third, we now ask: if one violates multiple categories, does that generate multiple *korbanos*, or is it still just one? And for that, we invoke the singling-out of fire — each category is conceptually distinct and independently liable.

### Charish and Katzir Objec…

An obvious question arises: if *charish* and *katzir* are also singled out, why couldn't we have derived *chiluk melachot* from them, without needing fire at all?

Rashi offers two answers. First, *charish* and *katzir* are prohibited through a positive commandment — *b'charish uv'katzir tishbot* is a *mitzvas aseh* (you shall rest), not a *lav*. A positive obligation to rest does not generate *korban* liability the same way a *lav* does; one cannot use it as a paradigm for separate *korban* liability. Second, there is a general principle that where the *Torah* singles out two items rather than one — *shnei ketuvim* — we do not derive a general rule from them; they are treated as exceptions. Rashi prefers this second answer, as does *Tosafot*.

*Tosafot* additionally notes a Mechilta that adds a prior stage to the argument: before we can even ask whether multiple categories generate multiple *korbanos*, we must first establish that violating a single category is already a full violation of *shabbat* — perhaps one must violate all thirty-nine to have "desecrated" it in any meaningful sense. It is from *charish* and *katzir* that we learn even one category suffices. Only after that do we proceed to the question of separate liability, which is answered by fire.

### Shmuel and Rabbi Yossi

Given this compelling Tannaitic derivation, why did Shmuel not use it? The *gemara* answers: *Shmuel savar lah k'Rabbi Yossi* — Shmuel holds like Rabbi Yossi. And Rabbi Yossi maintains *havarah l'lav yatzat* — fire was singled out not to teach the rule of separate liability across all thirty-nine, but rather to downgrade fire specifically. According to Rabbi Yossi, fire carries only the status of a simple *lav* — like going beyond the *techum* or having one's animal work on *shabbat* — and is not subject to *karet* or *misas beis din*. This is a striking position. The very singling-out of fire, which Rabbi Natan reads as elevating all thirty-nine to independent liability, Rabbi Yossi reads as removing fire from the full severity of *melacha*. Since Shmuel follows Rabbi Yossi's reading of the fire verse, he cannot use it as a source for *chiluk melachot*, and must resort instead to his derivation from *mechaleleha mos yumas*.

## Rabbi Yossi - Achas (II)

### Achas Meihena Juxtaposit…

The *gemara* then asks: if Shmuel holds like Rabbi Yossi, where does Rabbi Yossi himself derive *chiluk melachot*? If fire is unavailable as a source, Rabbi Yossi must have another derivation.

A *braisa* provides the answer. Rabbi Yossi cites the verse in *Vayikra* dealing with the *chatat*: *v'asah me'achas meihena* — and he does [one] from one of them. This verse is not about *shabbat* per se — it speaks of violating one of the commandments and bringing a *chatat* — but it is read through the lens of *shabbat* and its *korban* liability.

### Deriving Overlapping Err…

The *drasha* turns on the juxtaposition of *achas* (one) and *hena* (they, many), along with the additional *mem* prefix in *me'achas* and *meihena*. This juxtaposition teaches that there are circumstances under which one brings a single *korban* and circumstances under which one brings multiple *korbanos*, even for violations occurring within a single *shabbat*.

### One That Is Many, Many T…

The derivation proceeds as follows. *Achas she'hi hena* — one which is many: this is the case of one who forgot it was *shabbat* yet performed many *melachot*. The core mistake was singular — forgetting *shabbat* — but it expressed itself in many acts. Therefore one *korban*. *Hena she'hi achas* — many which are one: this is the case of one who forgot individual *melachot*, performing multiple acts each representing a separate error, yet all on one *shabbat*. Here one brings multiple *chataot*.

From *me'achas* — from one — we learn that even a partial act can constitute a violation. The example is one who intended to write the name *Shimon* and wrote only *shem*: even though he did not complete his original intention, he wrote two letters (the minimum *shiur* for the *melacha* of *ketiva*) and is liable. The *mem* of *me'achas* signals that even doing less than what one intended, so long as the act itself is complete and reaches the minimum measure, is a violation.

From *meihena* — from them — we derive the relationship between *avos* and *toldos*: the main categories (*hena* = the *avos*) generate subsidiary categories (*me'hena* = the *toldos* that derive from them).

Spelling it out fully: *achas she'hi hena* corresponds to the case of *zadon Shabbat v'shiggat melachot* — one knew it was *shabbat* but forgot the individual *melachot*, thus multiple *korbanos*. *Hena she'hi achas* corresponds to *shiggas Shabbat v'zadon melachot* — one forgot it was *shabbat* but knew the acts were *melachot*; since the single error was forgetting *shabbat*, it is one *korban*.

## Rabbi Yossi - Achas (II)

### Shmuel's Disagreement

Why, then, did Shmuel not adopt Rabbi Yossi's derivation from *achas meihena*? The *gemara* states simply: *achas she'hi hena v'hena she'hi achas lo mashma lei* — Shmuel is not persuaded that the juxtaposition of *achas* and *hena* teaches this lesson. Combining two words and reading into their conjunction an entire structure of overlapping and distinct liability is not, in Shmuel's view, a compelling *drasha*. He therefore goes to his own source in *mechaleleha mos yumas*, which, although Amoraic and arguably the weakest of the three derivations, is the one he finds compelling.

We are left, then, with three derivations of *chiluk melachot*: fire (Rabbi Natan), *achas meihena* (Rabbi Yossi), and *mechaleleha mos yumas* (Shmuel). *Tosafot* raises the interesting point that Shmuel, as an Amora, appears to be disagreeing with *Tannaim* — though perhaps he had a *mesorah* supporting his position.

## Both Forgotten - Korban…

### He'lem Zeh V'zeh

The *gemara* now introduces Rava's question to Rav Nachman: *he'lem zeh v'zeh biyado mahu* — what is the law when someone forgot both? He forgot that it was *shabbat* and he also forgot, for each *melacha* he performed, that those acts were forbidden. He watered his plants thinking it was Wednesday, but even had he known it was *shabbat*, he would have continued — because he had forgotten that watering plants is a *melacha*. He also baked, under the same double forgetting. How many *korbanos* does he bring — one, because he forgot *shabbat*, or two, because he also forgot each individual *melacha* separately?

### Shabbat as Primary Error

Rav Nachman answers: *harei he'lem Shabbat biyado* — the forgetting of *shabbat* is in his possession, and that would have led to all of his actions regardless. Therefore it is one *korban*. The forgetting of *shabbat* is the encompassing mistake that subsumes all else. *V'eino chayav ela achat*.

One might argue the reverse: *adrabba*, say the opposite — *harei he'lem melachot biyado* — the forgetting of individual *melachot* is also in his possession, and those were the independent errors that drove each act. Perhaps those should govern, yielding a separate *korban* for each. The logic is coherent: the *melacha* errors are distinct from one another, and had he even known it was *shabbat*, each *melacha* error would have persisted independently. Rav Nachman, however, holds that *shabbat* is the larger, more encompassing mistake, and when both are present, the *shabbat* forgetting governs.

### Rashi and Tosafot Debate

Rav Ashi reformulates the question in terms of the language from the opening of the *daf*: *im mishum Shabbat kaparish* — if the person would have stopped because of *shabbat* (i.e., being reminded it was *shabbat* would have been sufficient to stop him), then *harei he'lem Shabbat biyado v'eino chayav ela achat* — it is the *shabbat* forgetting that is primary and he brings only one. *V'im mishum melacha kaparish* — if he would have stopped because of the *melacha* — *harei he'lem melachot biyado v'chayav al kol achas v'achas* — it is the *melacha* forgetting that is primary and he is liable for each one.

This formulation raises the question of what "would have stopped him" means when he forgot both. Rashi explains: if someone called out "it is *shabbat*!" and that alone — without any specific mention of the *melacha* — would have caused the person to stop, then it is because hearing "*shabbat*" triggered the realization that the act was forbidden. The reminder of *shabbat* implied the *melacha* prohibition, and therefore *shabbat* was the key piece of information he had forgotten.

*Tosafot* is dissatisfied with this reading. Why should the order of inference from a third party's reminder determine anything? *Tosafot* reframes the criterion as a matter of self-reporting: we ask the person himself which piece of information was more fundamental to his error. If he reports that had he remembered it was *shabbat*, he would have paused, hesitated, and checked whether his actions were permitted — even if he was somewhat ambivalent about the *melacha* question — then *shabbat* was the more foundational forgetting. Conversely, if the *melacha* was the deeper error and *shabbat* was at most an ambivalent uncertainty, then the *melacha* is primary. The criterion is not what an observer would infer, but which error the person identifies as having been the core of his mistake.

### Gemara's Pushback

The *gemara* is not satisfied even with Rav Ashi's reformulation. Ravina responds: *klum parash miShabbos ela mishum melachos?* — the same objection that appeared on *amud alef* resurfaces. When one stops because of *shabbat*, it is because *both* pieces of knowledge — that it is *shabbat* and that the act is a *melacha* — have been engaged. And *klum piresh mi'melachos ela mishum Shabbos?* — when one stops because of the *melacha*, it is again because both factors are now operative. No matter which piece of information triggers awareness, both are necessarily present in the moment of stopping. So if both were forgotten, one cannot locate the "more fundamental" forgetting simply by asking what would have made the person stop.

According to *Tosafot*, what Ravina is really saying is that Rav Ashi has not addressed the true case — where both were genuinely and equally forgotten. Perhaps Rav Ashi handles the case where one forgetting was dominant and the other ambivalent, but where both are real and complete, the question remains open.

### Gemara's Pushback

The *gemara* resolves the impasse — at least at this stage of the discussion.

### Rav Ashi's Criterion

Rav Ashi's criterion (*im mishum Shabbat kaparish / im mishum melacha kaparish*) reflects the same logical structure as the debate at the opening of the *daf*. This is worth pausing on. What seemed earlier like two merely verbal formulations of the same point — "what would have made you stop" versus "what mistake did you make" — may carry genuine practical weight precisely in the case of *he'lem zeh v'zeh*. When you have only forgotten *shabbat*, the two formulations converge: one mistake, one *korban*. When you have only forgotten the *melachot*, they again converge: multiple mistakes, multiple *korbanos*. But when both are forgotten simultaneously, the two framings can point in different directions. "What would have made you stop" requires identifying which piece of information was the decisive trigger. "Where was your mistake" requires counting the errors. These can come apart, and the case of *he'lem zeh v'zeh* is precisely where that divergence becomes visible.

### Ravina's Position

Ravina's ultimate position — *ela lo shena* — is that it makes no difference which information would have caused the person to desist; in all configurations of *he'lem zeh v'zeh*, *shabbat* is the more encompassing category. The forgetting of *shabbat* implies and includes all the individual *melacha* forgettings; the reverse is not true. Therefore, when both are forgotten, we follow the larger category and it is one *korban*.

## 39 Melachot - Source (II)

### Rabbi Yochanan's Question

The *gemara* now invokes a *mishna* we have already encountered: *avos melachos arba'im chaser achas* — the primary categories of *melacha* are forty minus one. The *gemara* had asked, *minyana lama li* — why does the *mishna* need to state the number? Surely one can count.

### 39 Korbanot Scenario

Rabbi Yochanan answered: to teach that it is possible to bring thirty-nine *korbanos* in a single state of forgetfulness — *she'im asaan kulam b'he'elam echad chayav al kol achas v'achas* — that one who forgot all thirty-nine categories and violated each one within a single *he'lem* brings a separate *korban* for each. The number thirty-nine is stated to tell us the theoretical maximum: thirty-nine *korbanos* for a single *shabbat*.

## 39 Melachot - Source (II)

### Forgetting All vs. Shabb…

This scenario immediately raises a difficulty: if one forgot all thirty-nine *melachot*, one has effectively forgotten everything that *shabbat* prohibits. Doesn't that amount to forgetting *shabbat* itself — in which case, by our earlier analysis, there should be only one *korban*?

### Karetand Lav Framework

The *gemara* resolves this elegantly through Rabbi Yochanan's own position — *kivan d'shagog b'kares af al pi d'mezid b'lav* — that even if one knew the act was prohibited at the level of a *lav*, if he forgot that it was punishable by *karet*, he is considered *shogeg* and liable for a *korban*. Accordingly, one can know it is *shabbat* and know that certain acts are forbidden, yet forget the severity — specifically, that each of these categories is subject to *karet*. In such a case, he brings thirty-nine *korbanos* for the thirty-nine categories he violated while still, in some sense, "knowing" it was *shabbat* (in that he knew the acts were at least prohibitied at the level of a *lav*).

This resolution is specific to Rabbi Yochanan's view. Reish Lakish, who holds that *shogeg* liability requires forgetting even the basic *lav* prohibition — *ad d'yishgog b'lav u'vkares* — faces the difficulty more acutely: if one forgot that all thirty-nine things are forbidden, hasn't he simply forgotten *shabbat*?

### Techumin as Shabbat Know…

For Reish Lakish's position, the *gemara* employs the same resolution offered two days prior: *d'yadah lei b'tchumin v'aliba d'Rabbi Akiva* — one retains knowledge that it is *shabbat* because he knows, at minimum, that it is biblically prohibited to go beyond the *techum* (city limits), which Rabbi Akiva holds is a biblical prohibition. This residual knowledge of *some* *shabbat* prohibition — even if not *melacha* — suffices to establish that he "knew" it was *shabbat*. He then made thirty-nine independent errors about the individual *melachot*, and thus brings thirty-nine *korbanos*. If no such residual *shabbat* knowledge exists and he forgot even *techum*, then his forgetting of all *melachot* collapses into a forgetting of *shabbat* itself, and only one *korban* is brought.

## Order of Realization - R…

### Katzar V'Tachan Case

Rava introduces a case of considerable complexity. A person harvested a *k'gogeret*'s worth of grain and ground it — performing two *melachot* — while in a state of *shiggas Shabbat* (having forgotten it was *shabbat*). He then, without any intervening realization that he had sinned, performed the same two acts again — harvesting and grinding — this time in a state of *zadon Shabbat v'shiggas melacha*: he knew it was *shabbat* but had forgotten that these acts were forbidden.

To simplify: he performed two acts under error-type one (forgetting *shabbat*) and then two acts under error-type two (forgetting the *melachot*). Call these "error-one acts" and "error-two acts" respectively.

### Realizing Shabbat First

Now the order in which he realizes his mistakes: first, he becomes aware of his error-one acts — the acts he committed while forgetting it was *shabbat*. At that moment, he becomes obligated in a *korban*, and that obligation encompasses both *ketzira* and *techina* done under error-type one.

### Ktzira Goreret Ktzira

Then he becomes aware of his error-two acts — those committed while knowing it was *shabbat* but forgetting the individual *melachot*. What is his obligation? Rava states: *ktzira goreret ktzira v'techina goreret techina* — harvesting drags harvesting, and grinding drags grinding. Since his first realized obligation already includes an obligation relating to *ketzira*, the subsequent *ketzira* obligation (from the error-two acts) is subsumed within the earlier one; he need not bring an additional *korban* for it. The same applies to *techina*. Thus, having realized mistake-one first, both error-two *korbanos* are absorbed by the first obligation, and he brings only one *korban* total.

### Realizing Melacha First

But reverse the order of realization: first he becomes aware of his error-two acts — those committed while knowing it was *shabbat* but forgetting the *melachot*. This immediately generates two obligations: one *korban* for *ketzira* and one *korban* for *techina*. Then he becomes aware of his error-one acts. The error-one acts, because committed under *shiggas Shabbat*, would normally generate one *korban* (since forgetting *shabbat* is a single error). But in this reversed scenario, since his *ketzira* obligation from the error-two realization already exists, and the error-one *korban* would overlap with it (it too involves *ketzira*), the error-one *korban* is absorbed — *ktzira goreret ktzira*. He need not bring a separate *korban* for the error-one *ketzira*. However, the error-one *korban* also included *techina*, and since a *techina* obligation already exists from the error-two realization, no additional *techina korban* is needed either.

The practical result depends on which realization came first — and the order of recognition determines which obligation "drags" the other into its ambit.

### Abaye on Overlaps

Abaye pushes back on this analysis, particularly regarding the second scenario. He argues: *techina nami goreret techina shem techina achas hi* — the grinding also drags the grinding, by a chain of inclusion. Even if one never actually brings the *korban* generated by the error-one realization (because it was absorbed by the prior error-two obligation), the fact that the error-one *korban* conceptually *included* a *techina* obligation means that when the remaining *techina* obligation from error-two surfaces, it too is subsumed by that chain.

In other words, Abaye holds that inclusion works across links: the error-two *ketzira* obligation absorbed the error-one *korban* (which included *techina*), and therefore the remaining *techina* obligation is also absorbed — even though no actual *korban* was ever brought for the intermediate step. This is a striking claim: an obligation that was itself absorbed, never actualized into a *korban*, nevertheless transmits its power of inclusion to further obligations downstream. The *gemara* will continue this debate on the following *daf*.
