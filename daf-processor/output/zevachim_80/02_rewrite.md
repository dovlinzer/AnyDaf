# Zevachim 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blemished vs. Unblemished

### Limbs & Mixed Blood

The Gemara on Daf 80 opens by revisiting the debates that appeared in the Mishnah. The first component of the Mishnah recapitulated an earlier controversy: if a limb from a *baal mum* — a blemished animal — becomes mixed together with limbs of unblemished animals, what is the law? Rabbi Eliezer rules leniently: once one limb has already been brought up on the altar, one may bring the rest, attributing the *baal mum* limb to the one already offered. The *chachamim* disagree and prohibit this. Critically, this dispute operates even though each act of bringing a limb is a distinct act — the blood has not all been blended together. Yet Rabbi Eliezer permits it nonetheless.

The Mishnah then extends the same framework to a different case: bowls of blood from different sacrifices that have become intermingled. Here too Rabbi Eliezer rules that if one application was already performed, the rest may proceed; the *chachamim* counter that even if all applications were performed save one, the final bowl must be poured out, for we remain concerned that it contains *baal mum* blood.

### Pairs Only

The Gemara refines Rabbi Eliezer's position: *amar Rabbi Elazar lo hechshir Rabbi Eliezer ela lishnayim lishnayim* — Rabbi Eliezer's leniency applies only when one performs the applications in pairs, not individually. When proceeding two at a time, one can reason: first, the bowl already offered was the *baal mum*, and second, since two are being offered simultaneously and at least one of them must be valid, the other may be assumed valid as well. But if each bowl is offered individually, one cannot make that assumption.

### Rav Dimi's Challenge

Rav Dimi challenged this restriction. The Mishnah states that the *chachamim* say: *afilu karvu kulan chutz me'echad mehen yishapech la'amah* — even if all the bloods were applied except one, that last one must be poured out. This phrasing implies that for Rabbi Eliezer, even the single remaining bowl may be offered — suggesting his leniency extends even to individual applications, not merely to pairs. Rabbi Yaakov answered Rav Dimi by explaining: *mai echad — zug echad* — "one" here means one pair, not a single bowl. This is precisely the same resolution that was reached by the earlier case of the limbs.

### Two Cases Necessary

The Gemara then asks why both cases — limbs and blood — need to be stated at all. *Vetzricha* — both are necessary. Had the Mishnah taught only the case of the limbs, one might have thought Rabbi Eliezer permits it specifically because the main act of *kapparah* — atonement — was already performed through the *zerikat ha-dam*, and bringing up the remaining limbs is only a secondary completion. In that case, Rabbi Eliezer would allow one to fictively attribute the *baal mum* limb to the already-offered portion. But one might say that when it comes to the blood itself — the central act of offering — even Rabbi Eliezer would concede to the Rabbis and prohibit applying *baal mum* blood. Therefore the Mishnah must also state the blood case explicitly. Conversely, had only the blood case been stated, one might have thought the Rabbis' stringency is limited to that case, where the concern of applying *baal mum* blood is most acute, but that in the case of the limbs they would concede to Rabbi Eliezer. *Vetzricha* — both cases must be taught independently.

## Above & Below Blood

### Mixed Blood Cases

The Mishnah continues with a second distinct scenario: blood of sacrifices whose application belongs *above* the *chut ha-sikra* — the red line that divides the altar — becomes mixed with blood whose application belongs *below*. Rabbi Eliezer rules that one sprinkles above and also below, and the blood that does not belong in each location is to be viewed as water — *ro'in* — so that the appropriate blood in each location is what counts. The *chachamim* reject this principle entirely: one may not fictively "view" the inappropriate blood as water, and the entire mixture must be poured out.

The Gemara had also introduced the related concerns of *bal tosif* and *bal tigra* — the prohibitions against adding to or subtracting from the commanded rites — which arise in certain configurations of mixed blood. These concerns will be returned to later.

### Parah Adumah Case

To illuminate the underlying principles governing the mixed blood debate, the Gemara invokes a parallel case from *Masechet Parah*. The Mishnah there (*tnan hatam*) states: *tzluchit shenaflu l'tocha mayim kol shehu* — if a container holding the purification water of the *parah adumah* had ordinary tap water fall into it, what is the law? This water is used to sprinkle upon individuals who are *tamei* from *tumat met*, and the mixture raises the question of whether the purifying water remains valid. Rabbi Eliezer says: *yaze shtei hazayot* — one should sprinkle twice. The *chachamim* say: *psulin* — it is entirely invalid.

## Parah Adumah Analysis

### Chachamim Position

*Bishlama rabbanan* — the *chachamim*'s position is straightforward once their assumptions are identified. The *rabbanan* hold *yesh bilah* — that when two liquids of similar substance are combined, we assume perfectly equal distribution throughout the mixture. They further hold that *hazah tzrichah shiur* — a *haza'ah*, a sprinkling, requires a minimum amount of the *parah adumah* water to be valid. And they hold *ein mitztarfin lehaza'ot* — one cannot combine two partial amounts from two separate sprinklings to meet that minimum. Therefore, since any single sprinkling from the mixture will contain only a fraction of the required *parah adumah* water — the remainder being ordinary water, equally distributed — that sprinkling will never satisfy the minimum. The water is thus invalid regardless of how many times one sprinkles.

### Yesh Bilah Principle

At the heart of this analysis is the principle of *yesh bilah*, which requires some elaboration. The question is this: when a drop of ordinary water falls into *parah adumah* water, do we assume that the liquids fully and equally disperse throughout the mixture — *yesh bilah* — or do we assume that they remain non-uniformly concentrated — *ein bilah*? The Gemara treats this as a binary, without considering factors such as passage of time or stirring. *Yesh bilah* means that every spoonful of the mixture contains the same proportion of each liquid; *ein bilah* means that concentrations may be uneven and one cannot assume a given portion reflects the overall ratio.

This raises a related question: why is there no *bittul* — nullification — of the ordinary water when only a small amount falls into a large volume of *parah adumah* water? The answer lies in a fundamental distinction in how *bittul* functions. The classical operation of *bittul* removes a problematic status from a substance. But the question here is the inverse: can *bittul* confer a positive halachic status — can it make something that lacked validity now valid? This is a deeper question that the Gemara does not resolve here, and in any event the framework of the discussion proceeds without invoking *bittul* as a mechanism.

It should also be noted that the Gemara assumes a sprinkling is always performed in a fixed quantity of one unit. Why one cannot simply sprinkle a double amount is not fully explained, but the Gemara operates consistently on that assumption.

### Rabbi Eliezer Deduction

The more challenging question is Rabbi Eliezer's position. How does sprinkling twice help? The Gemara proceeds deductively. *Ei kasavar ein bilah* — if Rabbi Eliezer held there is no equal distribution, then even two sprinklings might both have come entirely from the ordinary water, and *ki maza shtei haza'ot mai hava, dilma tartaihu mayya kamazi* — perhaps both sprinklings were entirely ordinary water. It would therefore be impossible to rely on two sprinklings. Thus Rabbi Eliezer must hold *yesh bilah* — that the liquids are equally distributed.

Having established that Rabbi Eliezer holds *yesh bilah*, the Gemara continues: *ei kasavar ein hazah tzrichah shiur* — if Rabbi Eliezer held there is no minimum amount required for a valid *haza'ah*, then even one sprinkling should suffice, since even the tiniest amount of *parah adumah* water would validate it. But Rabbi Eliezer requires two sprinklings. This proves he holds *hazah tzrichah shiur* — there is a minimum amount. And by extension, since he allows the two sprinklings to work together, it follows that *mitztarfin lehaza'ot* — two partial amounts can combine to reach the minimum. This is a remarkably tight deductive argument: Rabbi Eliezer's three positions — *yesh bilah*, minimum amount required, and the ability to combine two sprinklings — are each proven from the internal logic of his ruling alone.

One further consequence follows: given *yesh bilah* and the requirement of a minimum amount, for two sprinklings to guarantee that the combined *parah adumah* water meets the minimum, the mixture must contain at least fifty percent *parah adumah* water. If it were less — say forty-nine percent — then each individual sprinkling would contain only forty-nine hundredths of a unit, and two such sprinklings would total only ninety-eight hundredths, falling short of the full unit required. This is the first answer proposed, and it sets the stage for the Amoraim who follow.

### Reish Lakish View

Reish Lakish states: *le'olam yesh bilah, vehazah tzrichah shiur* — the mixture is always equally distributed, and a minimum amount is required. The case under discussion is *kegon shenitarvu achat be'achat* — one where the two liquids are mixed in at least equal halves, so that two sprinklings will together yield a full unit of *parah adumah* water. This is precisely the framework laid out in the first answer above.

### Rava's Position

Rava offers a different reading: *le'olam yesh bilah*, the distribution is equal, but *hazah ein tzrichah shiur* — there is no minimum amount required at all. If so, even one sprinkling should be sufficient. Why then does Rabbi Eliezer require two? *Uknasa dekenasu rabbanan ki heichi delo lishtarshelu* — the rabbis imposed the requirement of two sprinklings as a penalty, so that one who is careless enough to allow foreign water to fall into the *parah adumah* water should not simply proceed as if nothing happened. The double sprinkling is a rabbinic deterrent against negligence, not a logical necessity.

### Rav Ashi's Approach

Rav Ashi takes a different approach entirely: *ein bilah* — the distribution is not equal. This immediately raises the difficulty already posed: if the liquids are not equally distributed, perhaps both sprinklings come entirely from the ordinary water. Rashi preserves a slightly different textual reading for Rav Ashi. On this reading, the reason the Mishnah specified that only a small amount — *kol shehu* — of ordinary water fell in is precisely because of this problem. If it is such a tiny quantity, then even under the assumption of unequal distribution, after one sprinkling that tiny amount is entirely used up. In the worst case, if the first sprinkling contained only ordinary water, that sprinkling exhausted the entire supply of ordinary water. The second sprinkling will then consist exclusively of *parah adumah* water. So Rav Ashi's reading turns on the specificity of the Mishnah's language: the case is deliberately limited to a minimal admixture.

### Braita on Small Amount

A *baraita* is then cited that addresses this directly. *Rebbi omer, ledivrei Rabbi Eliezer, hazah kol shehu metaheres* — according to Rabbi Eliezer, even the smallest amount of *parah adumah* water effects purification. The *baraita* states this in three formulations: first, that the smallest amount works; second, *hazah ein tzrichah shiur* — no minimum amount is required; and third, *hazah mechtzah kesherah umechtzah pesulah* — even a mixture that is half valid and half invalid suffices, as long as some small amount of the *parah adumah* water is present.

This *baraita* clearly supports Rava's and Rav Ashi's approaches — both of which deny a minimum requirement — and decisively rejects Reish Lakish's position. Reish Lakish had held that a minimum amount is required and that two half-units can combine to meet it. But the *baraita* teaches that for Rabbi Eliezer the tiniest amount suffices. Therefore, the reason two sprinklings are required must be either the rabbinic penalty described by Rava or the unequal-distribution concern described by Rav Ashi, not the combining of two partial units as Reish Lakish proposed.

## Bilah in Mixed Blood

### Braita Mixed Blood

A second *baraita* is then cited, explicitly connecting the *parah adumah* discussion back to the sacrificial blood scenario of our Mishnah: *hanitnin lema'alah shenitarvu banitnin lemata* — blood of sacrifices that belong above became mixed with blood of those that belong below. Rabbi Eliezer says: *yiten lema'alah vehatachtonim alu lo* — apply above, and the blood that belonged below will also have been discharged through that act.

At this stage, the discussion of *parah adumah* has served its purpose. The question now is focused specifically on our Mishnah and the case of mixed sacrificial bloods: does Rabbi Eliezer operate with *yesh bilah* — equal distribution — or *ein bilah*? The simple reading of the Mishnah and all the *baraitot* surrounding it clearly assumes *yesh bilah*: by putting blood above and blood below, both *korbanot* are satisfied, because the mixture is equally distributed and every sprinkling contains blood from both sacrifices. But the Gemara attempts to maintain the possibility that Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein bilah*, and it is worth tracking how it does so.

### Majority Blood Case

The Gemara says: *ve'i amrat ein bilah — amai alu lo?* If we assume unequal distribution, why would the below-blood discharge be considered accomplished? *Dilma ki yaheiv elyonim lemata vetachtonim lema'alah* — perhaps when he applied blood above, it was all the below-blood, and the above-blood went to the wrong location. The Gemara answers: *hacha bemai askinan kegon de'ika rubei elyonim* — the case involves a majority of above-blood. More specifically, six units of *chatat* blood (which belongs above) are mixed with four units of *olah* blood (which belongs below), and when applying above, one puts four and a bit more than four units. Given this configuration, even under *ein bilah*, one can guarantee that at least some *chatat* blood was applied above, and when the remainder is poured below, at least some *chatat* blood goes there as well.

Critically, however, this reading concedes that the *olah* is not necessarily completed. The claim *vehatachtonim alu lo katani* — that the blood for the below sacrifices was discharged — is interpreted not as meaning the *olah* was properly performed, but *leshem shirayim* — that the remainder was properly disposed of as *shirayim*, the remnant that is to be poured at the base of the altar. It is a forced reading, but it is the price of maintaining *ein bilah*.

This case also introduces a subtle wrinkle. When the Gemara describes a second *baraita* — *natan lemata velo nimlach, Rabbi Eliezer omer yachzor veyiten lema'ala vehatachtonim alu lo* — blood was placed below first without authorization; Rabbi Eliezer says go back and apply above, and the bottom application counts — the same forced answer is offered: the majority is above-blood, a precise quantity is applied above, and the bottom application counts only as *shirayim*, not as a valid application of the *olah* blood.

### Below Then Above

This second *baraita* — where the blood was first applied below, and then Rabbi Eliezer instructs it to be applied above — introduces a fascinating wrinkle noted by the *rishonim*. If what was done below counts only as *shirayim*, one must ask: can *shirayim* be poured out before the primary blood application has even been performed? Normally, *shirayim* refers to the remainder after the primary sprinkling. Rashi appears to read the Gemara as accepting precisely this: the application below is retroactively treated as *shirayim* once the primary application above is completed. Tosafot disagrees and reads the Gemara as requiring that after the above application is performed, the blood is brought below again. In Tosafot's reading, the first below-application does not itself serve as *shirayim*; rather, a new below-application is performed after completing the above. This is not merely a textual nuance — it reflects a substantive question about whether *shirayim* can precede the primary act it is the remnant of.

### Above First Agreement

A third *baraita* brings a further case: *natan lema'ala velo nimlach* — blood was applied above without authorization. *Eilu ve'eilu modim sheyachzor veyiten lemata* — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* agree that one should proceed and apply below. This is a striking point: the *chachamim*, who generally oppose Rabbi Eliezer's leniency, agree here. Why? Because once the blood has been applied above, the remaining blood must go somewhere. For the *chatat* blood, it should properly be poured *leshem shirayim* at the base below. For the *olah* blood mixed in, applying it below is the correct thing to do. Therefore the *chachamim* have no objection — regardless of whether *yesh bilah* or *ein bilah*, placing the remainder below is correct and necessary.

The *baraita* then says: *eilu ve'eilu alu lo* — both sacrifices were discharged. This phrase is what the Gemara seizes upon. If both sacrifices were properly completed, this seems to require *yesh bilah* — for without equal distribution, one could not know that both bloods reached their proper locations. The Gemara attempts the same answer as before: the case involves a majority of above-blood, applied in a precise quantity. But the *baraita*'s explicit language — *eilu ve'eilu alu lo* — both counted — is difficult to reconcile with *ein bilah* and only the *chatat* working.

The Gemara's resolution is elegant: *seifa atan lerabbanan de'amri yesh bilah* — the final clause, stating that both counted, reflects the *rabbanan*'s position, not Rabbi Eliezer's. The *rabbanan* hold *yesh bilah*, and for them, of course, both sacrifices were completed. The agreement that one proceeds below is shared by both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim*, but the statement that "both counted" is the *chachamim*'s conclusion based on their holding of *yesh bilah*. For Rabbi Eliezer, we can still maintain *ein bilah*, with the forced answers given earlier — only the *chatat* necessarily worked, and the *olah* may not have.

Throughout all of this, the Gemara acknowledges that the plain and natural reading of every source is *yesh bilah* — equal distribution. The forced arguments are precisely that: the Gemara is stretching to preserve the possibility of *ein bilah* for Rabbi Eliezer, even as every case seems to point in the opposite direction.

## Single Application Proof

### Bechor & Maaser

The Gemara brings a further proof. The Mishnah discusses a case where *bechor* blood — the firstborn animal offering, which requires a single application — becomes mixed with *maaser* blood — tithe blood, which also requires a single application. In such a case, the Mishnah rules that one application is performed. The Gemara asks: if we assumed *ein bilah*, how would one application work? *Dilma mehai kahiv umehai lo kahiv* — perhaps the application consisted entirely of *bechor* blood and none of the *maaser*, leaving the *maaser* offering incomplete.

### One Application Question

The Gemara answers: *kegon shenitarvu achat be'achat* — the case is that equal amounts of each blood were mixed. But the Gemara presses further: even with equal amounts, if distribution is unequal, perhaps all the blood applied came from one and not the other. The answer cannot simply be that the amounts are equal; one still needs to explain how a single application can satisfy both.

### Rashi Four & Four

Rashi addresses this with a reading of the next case in the Mishnah as well: *matan arba b'matan arba* — blood requiring four applications becomes mixed with blood requiring four applications. The Mishnah rules that four applications are performed. Rashi explains that when the Mishnah says to perform four applications, it means to perform four for one sacrifice and then four for the other — in other words, use all of the blood, performing the prescribed applications for each. The mixture is used in full, and whatever the distribution, both bloods will reach the altar. While this is admittedly a strained reading of the Mishnah's simple language, it is the reading required to maintain consistency with *ein bilah*.

## Bal Tosif Debate

### Four & One Mixed

The most vexing case is *matan arba b'matanah achat* — blood requiring four applications becomes mixed with blood requiring only one application. Here the *bal tosif* and *bal tigra* debate emerges in full. Does one perform four applications? Then the blood requiring only one application may have been applied four times, violating *bal tosif*. Does one perform one application? Then the blood requiring four applications may not have received the full rite, violating *bal tigra*. The Mishnah indicates that Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua debate this, and the very existence of the *bal tosif* concern demonstrates that the applications are in some way being performed with knowledge of what blood is going where — for otherwise, why would there be a *bal tosif* concern at all?

### Rashi Division

The Gemara asks: *ve'i amrat ein bilah* — if distribution is unequal — *amai alu lo?* How could any of it work? And the Gemara suggests the case is, once again, that exactly one unit of each blood was mixed. Rashi's reading of the Mishnah in this context is that when one performs four applications, each application contains one and a quarter units — the five total units divided across four applications. On this reading, the entire blood ends up on the altar and the *korban* requiring one application is discharged because all of its blood eventually reached the *mizbeach*. But the question then arises: why should there be any *bal tosif* concern? Rashi's understanding appears to be that placing more than the required quantity on the altar is the *bal tosif* issue. Yet the simpler understanding of *bal tosif* here — which the Gemara itself seems to favor — is that a blood type that was supposed to be applied only to one corner ends up being placed on four corners. The tension in Rashi's reading is acknowledged.

### Tosafot Minimum

Tosafot offers a different explanation. Tosafot assumes that there is a minimum visible amount required for each application to be halachically significant. With this in mind, Tosafot analyzes the *bal tosif* concern under *ein bilah* as follows. If the bloods are not equally distributed and we are dealing with precise amounts, the scenario where the single-application blood ends up spread across two or more corners with the minimum amount in each is extremely unlikely. In order for there to be a *bal tosif* problem — where the single-application blood has been applied to an additional corner with enough to count — one would need exactly equal distribution. Under *ein bilah*, any other distribution will result in the single-application blood failing to appear in a minimum amount in the extra corner, meaning there is no actual *bal tosif* violation. Therefore, says Tosafot, under *ein bilah* there is no realistic *bal tosif* concern in the constrained scenarios the Gemara has been working with. The *bal tosif* concern only makes genuine sense if one assumes *yesh bilah*, because it is only under equal distribution that one can be sure that the single-application blood appears in sufficient quantity in each of the multiple applications. This is, Tosafot argues, the Gemara's precise question: given the reading that limits the mixture to precise quantities and treats the distribution as unequal, why does the *bal tosif* concern arise at all?

## Vessels vs. Liquids

### Rava Distinction

After all the attempts to maintain *ein bilah* for Rabbi Eliezer, the Gemara proposes a clean resolution through Rava: *ella amar Rava, be-velulim lo peligi* — regarding liquids that have actually been blended together, there is no debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* about *bilah*. *Ki peligi — be-khosot* — their debate concerns separate vessels whose contents have become intermingled, not liquids that have been physically mixed together.

This is a decisive clarification. All the scenarios in the Mishnah and the *baraitot* — putting above, putting below, one counted, both counted — are not cases where the bloods were literally poured into one bowl and mixed. They are cases where the bowls themselves were confused, so that one does not know which bowl belongs to which sacrifice. When the Gemara says to apply the blood above from both and below from both, it means: take from each bowl and apply it to each location. In that framework, one need not worry about *bilah* at all — one is deliberately applying from each bowl to each location, and therefore the question of equal versus unequal distribution of liquids never arises.

The practical implications of *yesh bilah* and *ein bilah* for mixed liquids are not resolved here. If liquids were actually blended, then for Rabbi Eliezer — who by this reading takes no position on *bilah* — the various forced answers the Gemara offered earlier would be necessary. But the Mishnah itself does not concern mixed liquids. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* in the Mishnah is entirely about mixed vessels, and it turns not on *bilah* at all, but on *ro'in*: whether one may halachically "view" the inappropriate blood as if it were water. Rabbi Eliezer holds of *ro'in* and the *Rabbanim* do not.

### Rabbi Yehuda Braita

The Gemara challenges this reading by citing a *baraita*: *amar Rabbi Yehuda lo nechelku Rabbi Eliezer vachachamim al dam chatat shenitarev bedam olah sheyikarev* — Rabbi Yehuda says that Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* do not debate the case of *chatat* blood mixed with *olah* blood at all. Where there is no *baal mum* concern and both sacrifices are equally valid, everyone agrees the blood may be offered. Furthermore, everyone agrees that blood of an animal that was used in an act of bestiality (*rova venirva*) may not be offered. The actual debate, says Rabbi Yehuda, is limited to blood of an unblemished animal mixed with blood of a *baal mum*: *she-Rabbi Eliezer omer yikarev bein be-valla bein be-khosot* — Rabbi Eliezer says to offer it whether the liquids were blended or the vessels were mixed, and the *chachamim* say not to offer it in either case.

This *baraita* directly contradicts Rava's resolution. Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states that Rabbi Eliezer's ruling applies *bein be-valla bein be-khosot* — both to blended liquids and to mixed vessels. This would restore all the difficulties: if Rabbi Eliezer rules even for blended liquids, we are back to needing to determine his position on *yesh bilah*, and all the strained answers to the various *baraitot* are required.

### Rabbanim Vessels

The Gemara's response is that Rabbi Yehuda's formulation reflects his own understanding of Rabbi Eliezer's position — *Rabbi Yehuda aliba de-Rabbi Eliezer masik bein be-valla bein be-khosot* — Rabbi Yehuda attributes to Rabbi Eliezer a ruling that covers both scenarios. For Rabbi Yehuda, one must indeed work with the forced answers given earlier for the blended-liquid cases. But the *rabbanan* — the *chachamim* — understand their own dispute with Rabbi Eliezer as concerning only mixed vessels: *ve-rabbanan be-khosot peligi*. On this reading, the *chachamim* and Rabbi Eliezer agree about the case of blended liquids — perhaps because, without *ro'in*, blended blood can never be rendered valid regardless of *bilah* — and their actual disagreement is confined to the vessel-confusion scenario, where the question of *ro'in* is operative.

The upshot of the entire sugya is thus as follows. The simple and plain reading of the Mishnah and all the related *baraitot* assumes *yesh bilah* — equal distribution of mixed liquids. The Gemara labored to show that it is possible to read all the sources as consistent with *ein bilah* for Rabbi Eliezer, but those readings were uniformly forced and concede that results are partial at best. Rava's elegant resolution — that the debate concerns vessels, not blended liquids, and turns on *ro'in* rather than *bilah* — bypasses the *bilah* question entirely for the primary dispute in the Mishnah. And where Rabbi Yehuda's *baraita* insists that Rabbi Eliezer addresses blended liquids too, the Gemara confines that to Rabbi Yehuda's personal reading, while the *rabbanan* maintain that even their own controversy with Rabbi Eliezer never extended to that case.
