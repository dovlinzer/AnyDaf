# Zevachim 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Altar Sanctifies

### Principle Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ אִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ: הַלָּן, וְהַיּוֹצֵא, וְהַטָּמֵא, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וְשֶׁקִּבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These** are the items that even if they were disqualified, **if they ascended** the altar **they shall not descend:** Blood, sacrificial portions, or limbs of a burnt offering, any of **which were left overnight** off the altar, **or that emerge** from the Temple courtyard, **or that** become **ritually impure, or** that came from an animal **that was slaughtered** with the intent to sacrifice it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, or** an offering **that** people **unfit** to perform the Temple service **collected and** then **sprinkled its blood.**

The focus of this *perek* — as the name of the *perek* itself announces and as the first *mishna* introduced — is the principle that the altar sanctifies things placed upon it. Even if an offering is invalid, once it has been placed on the altar it may remain there and be consumed by the fire. The central question animating the chapter is not merely that such a rule exists, but which invalidity is so severe that the item must be removed despite having reached the altar.

### Types of Items

Before arriving at our *mishna*, the previous *mishna* addressed a prior question: what *types* of things are subject to this rule? The obvious cases are anything meant to be burned on the altar. But what of liquids — blood or wine? That was a matter of debate. What of items not yet fully designated, such as the *komets* of a *mincha* that had not yet been placed in the *kli*? One opinion held that only animals qualify; another perhaps excluded even birds. Having established the scope of objects to which the rule applies, the *mishna* now turns to the *nature* of the invalidity — which failures are tolerable and which are not.

## Elu Im Alu Lo Yardu

### Cases Staying Up

The *mishna* opens with the phrase *elu im alu lo yardu* — the following things, if they were placed on the altar, do not come down.

### Notar, Yotzei, Tamei

The first group encompasses items that once had a valid moment — a *sha'at hakosher* — but subsequently became *pasul*. *Halan*: something that slept overnight, i.e., *notar*, whether meat, *emurim*, or blood. If blood slept overnight, the sacrifice was never legitimately completed — yet it stays. *Ve'hayotzei*: the item was taken outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and became *pasul*, again whether meat, blood, or *emurim*. One might wonder: if it is already *yotzei*, how can one speak of putting it on the altar? The case is where an absent-minded *kohen*, still holding the bowl of blood or the *emurim* in hand, inadvertently stepped into the *Ezrat Nashim* and then returned and placed the item on the altar. Do not remove it. *Ve'hatamei*: something that became *tamei*.

### Improper Slaughter Cases

The *mishna* then turns to a different category: cases where the invalidity was present from the very instant of *shechita* — there was never a valid moment at all. *Ve'shenishchat chutz lizmano ve'chutz limekomo*: if a *chutz lizmano* thought — or the quasi-*piggul* variant of *chutz limekomo* — attended the *shechita*, the offering was *pasul* from that instant. Similarly, *ve'shekiblu psulim ve'zarku et damo*: a *pasul kohen* caught the blood or threw it on the altar. Even in a case where a *pasul kohen* threw the blood — meaning the blood never legitimately reached the altar — the remaining meat or *emurim* placed on the altar nonetheless stay there.

### Piggul Concept

The *piggul* case deserves brief mention: an item that is *piggul* is also included in this list and stays on the altar. It does not achieve atonement, but it is not removed.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁנִּשְׁחַט בַּלַּיְלָה וְנִשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ וְיָצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים – אִם עָלְתָה תֵּרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In the case of a sacrificial animal **that was slaughtered at night, or** one **whose blood was spilled** on the floor of the Temple without its being collected in a vessel, **or** one whose **blood emerged outside the curtains,** i.e., outside the Temple courtyard: Even **if it ascended** upon the altar **it shall descend.**

*Rabbi Yehuda* agrees with the foregoing but carves out three additional exceptions. He holds that three cases must come down if they were placed up: *shenishchat balayla* — slaughtered at night; *venishpach damah* — the blood was spilled; and *veyatza damah chutz lakla'im* — the blood exited beyond the curtains, i.e., outside the *Azara*. In those three scenarios, *im alta tered* — if it went up, it must come down.

The distinction between these three and the other *psulim* requires explanation. One might ask: if the blood was spilled or the blood was taken outside, the blood is never going to legitimately reach the altar — but the same is true when a *pasul* person catches the blood. What makes these three worse? And *shenishchat balayla* is indeed different from *chutz lizmano*: in the case of *chutz lizmano*, at least the *shechita* was performed during the day and the problem entered only with a disqualifying thought. When the *shechita* itself was performed at night, the entire starting point is off. The question of why *nishpach damo* and *yatza damo chutz lakla'im* are worse than *kibel psulim* — given that the latter case also means the blood never legitimately arrived — is something the *gemara* and the *Rishonim* will need to address.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא תֵּרֵד, שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ. שֶׁרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – הַקֹּדֶשׁ מְקַבְּלוֹ, לֹא הָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – אֵין הַקֹּדֶשׁ מְקַבְּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** In all these cases, if it ascended **it shall not descend, because its disqualification** occurred **in sanctity. As Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **any** unfit offering **whose disqualification** occurred **in sanctity,** i.e., in the course of the Temple service, **the sacred** area **renders** the offering **acceptable,** and if it ascended onto the altar it shall not descend. But with regard to any offering **whose disqualification** did **not** occur **in sanctity** but rather was unfit initially, **the sacred** area **does not render** the offering **acceptable.**

*Rabbi Shimon* says: even those three cases do not come down. What then is his own limiting principle — where would he, and where would the *Tanna Kamma*, draw the line? That is the subject of the next section.

## Psuluto Bakodesh

### Kodesh Accepts

*Rabbi Shimon* formulates the principle as follows: *kol she-psulo ba-kodesh, ha-kodesh mekabelo* — anything whose invalidity occurred once it was already within the *kodesh* context, the *kodesh* receives it. The item got in the door, so to speak, and will not be ejected. Conversely, *lo hayah psulo ba-kodesh, ein ha-kodesh mekabelo* — if the invalidity preceded the *kodesh*, it is not accepted.

### Invalid Before Kodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה פְּסוּלָן בַּקֹּדֶשׁ: הָרוֹבֵעַ, וְהַנִּרְבָּע, וְהַמּוּקְצֶה, וְהַנֶּעֱבָד, וְהָאֶתְנַן, וְהַמְּחִיר, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְהַטְּרֵפָה, וְהַיּוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the offerings **whose disqualification** did **not** occur **in sanctity: An animal that copulated with a person, and an animal that was the object of bestiality, and** an animal that was **set aside** for idol worship, **and** an animal that was **worshipped** as a deity, **and** an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as **the price** of a dog, **and** an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds, and an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], and** an animal **born by caesarean section, and blemished** animals.

The *mishna* then enumerates the items that fall under this latter category — *ve'eilu she-lo hayu psulan ba-kodesh*: *ha-rove'ah ve-ha-nirba*, an animal used in or subjected to an act of bestiality — its disqualification preceded its very sanctification. *Ve-ha-muktzeh ve-ha-ne'evad*, something designated for or actually worshipped as *avoda zara*. *Ve-ha-etnan ve-ha-mechir*, an animal given as payment to a prostitute or exchanged for a dog. *Ve-ha-kilayim*, a crossbreed — for instance, between a sheep and a goat. *Ve-ha-treifa*, an animal with an internal injury that would disqualify it even for non-*kodesh* use. *Ve-ha-yotze dofen*, an animal born by C-section, since only a vaginal birth satisfies the requirement of *ki yivaled*. And *u-va'alei mumin*, blemished animals.

### Ritual vs. Defect

A crucial conceptual point must be made here. While it is true that the *psul* of these items typically *precedes* the *kodesh*, the distinction is not merely chronological. The *gemara* learns elsewhere that all of these items must come down from the *mizbe'ach* even if their disqualification arose *after* the animal was sanctified — even if it occurred within the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. One might sanctify a perfect animal and then have it subjected to bestiality, or sanctify it and then watch it acquire a *mum*, or sanctify it in the *Beit HaMikdash* only for someone to accidentally make it a *treifa* with a misplaced knife.

What, then, is the meaning of *lo hayah psulo ba-kodesh*? The key is conceptual rather than temporal. All the *psulim* that are accepted — *notar*, *yotzei*, *tamei*, *chutz lizmano*, *chutz limekomo*, *kibel psulim* — are cases where what went wrong was a *mikdash ritual performed incorrectly*. The violation was embedded in the handling of the sanctified animal: it was kept too long, removed from the wrong area, rendered impure, or processed by an invalid officiant. These are failures *within* the ritual system of *korbanot*. By contrast, *treifa*, *yotzei dofen*, *rove'ah*, and their companions represent a different type of problem — not a ritual done wrong, but an intrinsic defect or an external circumstance that has nothing to do with *mikdash* ritual. Even if such a thing happened in the *Beit HaMikdash* after sanctification, it is conceptually unrelated to the *mikdash* process, and that is what is called *lo hayah psulo ba-kodesh*. 

There is also a simpler and practical way to draw this line: the *psulim* that are accepted as *psuluto ba-kodesh* all occur at the moment of *shechita* or afterwards — when the animal is already dead. Every single item in the *lo hayah psulo ba-kodesh* list is disqualified while the animal is still alive. That is an easy and accurate way to see where the line falls.

### Rabbi Akiva on Mumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַכְשִׁיר בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר: דּוֹחֶה הָיָה אַבָּא אֶת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva deems blemished** animals **fit** in the sense that if they ascended they shall not descend. **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, says: My father would reject blemished** animals **from upon the altar.**

*Rabbi Akiva machshir be-va'alei mumin* — *Rabbi Akiva* rules that a blemished animal may remain on the altar. The question is immediately pressing: why should a *ba'al mum* be any better than, say, a *yotzei dofen* or a *treifa*? The *gemara* will limit *Rabbi Akiva*'s position to a very narrow case: a *mum* that has a comparable situation in which it would *not* be a *mum* — specifically in a bird, where the comparable type of blemish is not disqualifying. Because such a case exists in which it is not problematic, it has enough of a connection to the world of *kasher* to be treated more leniently. This limiting interpretation is not the simple reading of the *mishna*, but it is how the *gemara* constrains *Rabbi Akiva*'s ruling.

### Practice Testimony

*Rabbi Chanina sgan ha-kohanim* provides testimony from actual Temple practice that stands as a striking counterpoint to *Rabbi Akiva*. It is worth noting — something remarked upon in the discussion — that this is one of the very rare moments across nine chapters of *Zevachim* in which anyone introduces a firsthand memory of what was actually done in the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Rabbi Chanina* reports that his father — a *kohen* who served in the Temple — would physically push *ba'alei mumin* off the altar if they made it up there. He was insistent about it. This testimony directly contradicts *Rabbi Akiva*'s leniency and reflects a practice entirely consistent with the majority position that *ba'alei mumin* must come down.

### Reciprocity Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ, כָּךְ אִם יֵרְדוּ לֹא יַעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning those animals that, if they ascended, do not descend, **just as if they ascended** the altar **they shall not descend, so too, if they descended they shall not** then **ascend.**

The *mishna* then states the reciprocity principle: *keshem she'alu lo yerdu, kach im yerdu lo ya'alu* — just as invalid things that went up do not come down, so too if they were brought down by accident, they may not go back up. This is in contrast to the ordinary rule for valid offerings, where *emurim* accidentally removed from the altar are replaced. Once these invalid items have been brought down, they remain down.

### Alive Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעָלוּ חַיִּים לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יֵרְדוּ. עוֹלָה שֶׁעָלְתָה חַיָּה לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – תֵּרֵד. שְׁחָטָהּ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יַפְשִׁיט וִינַתְּחֶהָ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And all of them that** if they ascend they do not descend, if **they ascended to the top of the altar alive they descend,** as an animal is fit for the altar only after it is slaughtered. **A burnt offering that ascended to the top of the altar alive shall descend,** as one does not slaughter an animal atop the altar *ab initio*. But if **one slaughtered** the animal **at the top of the altar, he should flay** it **and cut it into pieces in its place,** and it is not removed from the altar.

Finally, the *mishna* addresses a distinct case: *ve'kulan she'alu chayim le'rosh ha-mizbe'ach yerdu* — all of these, if they walked up alive to the top of the altar, must come down. The altar's sanctifying power does not extend to a living animal atop the altar — one cannot simply leave it there indefinitely. The cases to which this applies are largely those that could still be *ba'alei chayim* at the point of potential disqualification, such as *rove'a* and *nirba*. This observation reinforces the easier formulation of the *psuluto ba-kodesh* principle: things that are *pasul* while still alive fall in the *lo hayah psulo ba-kodesh* category. If, however, an inattentive *kohen* slaughtered an animal at the top of the altar — a question previously raised as to whether the top of the altar might constitute "the north" — the rule is *yafshit ve'yenatach bi'mkomah*: flay it and complete all the processing right there at the top, without bringing the carcass down.

## Textual Basis

### Zot Hi Ha'olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת״, ״הִיא״, ״הָעֹלָה״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה מִיעוּטִין; פְּרָט לְשֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה בַּלַּיְלָה, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, וְשֶׁיָּצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים – שֶׁאִם עָלְתָה, תֵּרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The verse from which is derived the *halakha* that items that ascended upon the altar shall not descend, states: **"This** is the law of the burnt offering: **It is the burnt offering** on the pyre upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:2). **These are three** terms of **exclusion** used in the verse: "This," "it," and "the," from which it is derived that three instances are **excluded** from this *halakha*: A sacrificial animal **that was slaughtered at night, and** one **whose blood was spilled, and** one **whose blood emerged outside the curtains,** i.e., outside the Temple courtyard. With regard to these cases, the *halakha* is **that if** one of them **ascended** upon the altar **it shall descend.**

A *braita* establishes the textual basis for the rule that things placed on the altar remain there. The *pasuk* cited is *zot torat ha'olah, hi ha'olah al mokdah al ha-mizbe'ach kol ha-layla ad ha-boker* — this is the *torah* of the *olah*, it is the *olah* upon its pyre on the altar all night until morning. The words *hi ha'olah* — that is the *olah* which is put up — teaches that things which ascend remain. There is also the *pasuk* of *kol hanogea ba-mizbe'ach yikdash*, which is more directly evoked by the language of *ha-mizbe'ach mekadesh* in the *mishna*, but the primary textual basis for the *im alu lo yerdu* rule is the verse just quoted.

### Three Limiting Words

*Rabbi Yehuda* derives his three exclusions from three limiting words in this *pasuk*. *Zot* — only this and not another. *Hi* — this and not another. *Ha'olah* — the *olah* and not something else. These are *gimel miyutim* — three exclusionary terms. They correspond to his three exceptional cases: *prat le-shenishchata ba-layla*, slaughtered at night; *ve-shenishpach dama*, the blood was spilled; *ve-sheyatza dama chutz lakla'im*, the blood was taken outside the curtains. Those three, if placed on the altar, must come down: *she'im alu yerdu*. Why these three correspond to those three limiting words is, at this stage, not yet fully explained.

### Torat as Inclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״עֹלָה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹלָה כְּשֵׁרָה; מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה בַּלַּיְלָה, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, וְשֶׁיָּצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים, וְהַלָּן, וְהַיּוֹצֵא, וְהַטָּמֵא, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וְשֶׁקִּבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמוֹ, הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה, וּלְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה, וְהַנִּיתָּנִין בַּחוּץ שֶׁנְּתָנָן בִּפְנִים, בִּפְנִים שֶׁנְּתָנָן בַּחוּץ, וּפֶסַח וְחַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תּוֹרַת הָעֹלָה״ – רִיבָּה תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הָעוֹלִין, שֶׁאִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** From the usage of the term **"burnt offering" I have** derived **only** with regard to **a fit burnt offering** that it shall not descend. **From where** is it derived that the verse also **includes** a sacrificial animal that was disqualified, such as one **that was slaughtered at night; or whose blood was spilled; or whose blood emerged outside the curtains,** i.e., outside the Temple courtyard; **or that was left overnight; or that emerged** from the Temple courtyard, **or that** became **ritually impure; or** that came from an animal **that was slaughtered** with the intent to sacrifice it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area; or** an offering **that** people **unfit** to perform the Temple service **collected and** then **sprinkled its blood?** In addition, with regard to **Those** offerings whose blood is to be **placed below** the red line that divided between the upper and lower halves of the external altar, i.e., a burnt offering, a guilt offering, or a peace offering, but it **was placed above** the red line; **and** a sin offering, whose blood is to be placed **above** the red line, **that** had its blood **placed below** the red line; **and those** offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed outside,** on the external altar, **that** had their blood **placed inside,** in the Sanctuary; and those offerings whose blood is to be placed **inside that** had their blood **placed outside?** **And** in addition, with regard to **a Paschal offering or sin offering that were slaughtered not for their sake, from where** is it derived that if they ascended upon the altar they shall not descend? **The verse states: "The law of the burnt offering,"** which **included** in **one law all** items **that ascend** upon the altar, establishing the principle **that if they ascended** the altar **they shall not descend.**

*Rabbi Shimon* takes a different approach. Rather than multiplying exclusions from the three limiting words, he focuses on the word *torat*. *Ein li ela olah kashira* — from the word *olah* alone I would know only a fully *kasher olah* stays up. The word *torat* functions as an inclusive umbrella: *torat* implies a general rule encompassing a broad category, bringing in all the cases about which both *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* agree — *halan*, *ha-yotzei*, *ha-tamei*, *chutz lizmano*, *chutz limekomo*, *kibel psulim ve-zarku et damo* — and even cases not mentioned explicitly in the *mishna*, such as blood applied to the wrong place or blood of an inner *korban* applied outside.

### Shimon's Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע, וְהַמּוּקְצֶה וְהַנֶּעֱבָד, וְאֶתְנַן וּמְחִיר, וְכִלְאַיִם וּטְרֵפָה וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought **that I** should also **include an animal that copulated with a person, and an animal that was the object of bestiality, and** an animal that was **set aside** for idol worship, **and** an animal that was **worshipped** as a deity, **and** an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as the **price** of a dog, **and** an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds, and** an animal that is **a *tereifa*, and** an animal **born by caesarean section.** Therefore, **the verse states: "This,"** to exclude these types of disqualifications, which descend even after they have ascended the altar.

*Rabbi Shimon* then asks: should I perhaps include also *rove'a*, *nirba*, *muktzeh*, *ne'evad*, *etnan*, *mechir*, *kilayim*, *treifa*, and *yotzei dofen*? The answer comes from the word *zot*: this and not those. But what determines the line between what *zot* excludes and what *torat* includes? Here the *gemara* presents *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle as a case of the *Torah* providing an inclusive and an exclusive term — *ribah* and *mi'et* — and then delegating to human wisdom the task of drawing the meaningful line. *Rabbi Shimon* draws it at *psuluto ba-kodesh*: items whose invalidity arose within the *kodesh* context are included; items whose invalidity was not the result of a *kodesh* ritual done wrong are excluded. The *Torah* tells us there is a line; the *Sages* determine where the line falls.

## Rabbi Yehuda Analogy

### Methodology Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ לָן בַּדָּם כָּשֵׁר – שֶׁהֲרֵי לָן כָּשֵׁר בָּאֵימוּרִין. לָן בָּאֵימוּרִין כָּשֵׁר – שֶׁהֲרֵי לָן כָּשֵׁר בַּבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Yehuda,** who disagrees with Rabbi Shimon and does not deem it permitted for items whose disqualification occurred in sanctity to remain on the altar, yet agrees that those items listed in the beginning of the mishna, such as sacrificial portions left overnight, shall not descend, **he derives it from here,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **For what** reason **did** the Sages **say to us** that **in** the case of **blood** left overnight it is **fit,** i.e., if blood of an offering had been left overnight and was then placed on the altar it is not removed? This is **as** the *halakha* is **in** the case of **sacrificial portions,** which if they are **left overnight** are **fit.** From where is it derived that **in** the case of **sacrificial portions** that are **left overnight,** they are **fit?** This is **as** the *halakha* is **in** the case of **meat,** which if it is **left overnight** is **fit,** because the meat of a peace offering may be eaten for two days and one night.

Having explained *Rabbi Shimon*'s approach, the *gemara* now turns to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s methodology, which operates from a fundamentally different orientation. *Rabbi Shimon* begins with a presumption of inclusion — everything is in unless excluded — and then identifies a category to exclude. *Rabbi Yehuda* begins from the opposite direction: the text gives me exclusions, and I need to be persuaded why anything should be *included* in the category of *psulim* that stay on the altar. Do not tell me why something should come down; rather, convince me why it should be allowed to stay up. From this starting point, his list of three additional exclusions follows naturally: those are items for which he cannot find a persuasive justification for inclusion.

### Notar Analogy

The *braita* then presents *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning for each inclusion. *Mipnei mah amru lan be-dam kasher* — why is blood that slept overnight in the category that stays? *Mipnei she-harei lan kasher be-imurim* — because *emurim* that are *notar* and placed on the *mizbe'ach* get to stay. And why is *lan* tolerable for *emurim*? *She-harei lan kasher be-vasar* — because there is meat, namely *shlamim* meat, that is perfectly *kasher* the next day. Of course, the analog is imperfect: *shlamim* meat is *kasher* overnight because it is supposed to be, not because it has slept inappropriately. The *gemara* will raise this objection shortly.

### Yotzei Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא – שֶׁהַיּוֹצֵא כָּשֵׁר בְּבָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering that **emerges** from the Temple courtyard is then placed on the altar it is not removed? This is derived by comparison, **since** an offering that **leaves** its area is **fit in** the case of an offering brought on a private **altar,** as the entire notion of sacrifice on such an altar is that it may be performed anywhere.

*Yotzei* — an item removed from the *Beit HaMikdash* — may stay because *she-ha-yotzei kasher be-vamah*: outside the *mikdash*, at a *bamah*, items brought outside are perfectly valid. This too presents difficulties: if we invoke the *bamah* precedent, it is not obvious why blood taken outside would not receive the same treatment, which would undercut *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own exclusion of *yatza dama chutz lakla'im*. *Rashi* is troubled by this, and the answer is not fully clear.

### Tamei Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא – הוֹאִיל וְהוּתַּר לַעֲבוֹדַת צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering that has become **ritually impure** is placed on the altar it is not removed? This is derived by comparison, **since** it is **permitted to** offer an impure offering in **the** case of **communal rites,** i.e., communal offerings. In cases of necessity, the communal offerings may be sacrificed even if they are ritually impure.

*Tamei* — an impure item — may stay *ho'il ve-hutar la'avodat tzibbur*, since a *korban tzibbur* that is *tamei* may be offered. This is actually the strongest of the analogies, because unlike the others it reflects a genuine and specific *halacha* that validates the *tamei* state in a comparable context.

### Chutz Lizmano Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – הוֹאִיל וּמְרַצֶּה לְפִיגּוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering that was disqualified due to the intention of the priest who slaughtered it to consume it **beyond its** designated **time** [*piggul*] was placed on the altar, it is not removed? This is derived by comparison, **since** the sprinkling of its blood **effects acceptance** with regard to **its** status as ***piggul*.** The status of *piggul* takes effect only if the sacrificial rites involving that offering were otherwise performed properly. This indicates that it still has the status of an offering, so it is not removed from the altar.

For *chutz lizmano*, the reasoning is *ho'il u-meratzeh le-piggulo* — the curious principle that in the context of *piggul*, the offering is viewed as *meratzeh*, as though it were properly received, precisely in order to render the act one of *piggul*. The *chutz lizmano* thought thereby participates in a suspended legal reality in which the offering is still treated as valid — and that suspended validity is enough to make it tolerable under *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework.

### Chutz Limekomo Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – הוֹאִיל וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering that was disqualified due to the intention of the priest who slaughtered it to consume it **outside its** designated **area** was placed on the altar, it is not removed? This is derived by comparison, **since** it is **juxtaposed to** an offering that was slaughtered with intent to consume it **beyond its** designated **time.**

*Chutz limekomo* is carried along by *chutz lizmano*, the two being conceptually paired and in certain respects equated by the *halacha*. One might also have noted that *chutz limekomo* independently satisfies the *meratzeh* criterion, but the *braita* connects it to the *chutz lizmano* reasoning.

### Kibel Psulim Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמוֹ – בְּהָנָךְ פְּסוּלֵי דַּחֲזוֹ לַעֲבוֹדַת צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering **that** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **collected and** then **sprinkled its blood** was placed on the altar, it is not removed? This is derived **from** the *halakha* of **these** priests who are generally **disqualified** because they are impure, yet **who are fit to** perform **the communal rites,** i.e., to sacrifice communal offerings, in a case when all the priests or the majority of the Jewish people are impure.

The case of *kibel psulim* — a *pasul kohen* receiving and throwing the blood — is the most difficult. When would a *pasul kohen*'s act ever be *kasher*? The *gemara* answers: *be-hanech psulei de-chazu la'avodat tzibbur* — the *mishna* is referring specifically to those *psulim* who are valid for a *korban tzibbur*, namely a *tamei* person. This, however, is emphatically not the plain reading of the *mishna*, which uses the word *psulim* broadly. If the *mishna* meant only a *tamei kohen*, it should have said *tamei*. So the explanation, while it resolves the logical problem, sits uneasily with the text.

### Gemara Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁרוֹ, מִדָּבָר שֶׁבְּהֶכְשֵׁרוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the derivations of the *baraita*: **But can one deduce** the *halakha* of **a matter that is not fit,** i.e., sacrificial portions that are disqualified due to having been left overnight, **from** the *halakha* of **a matter that is fit,** i.e., the peace offering, which is permitted for eating for two days and one night? Similarly, how can the *baraita* derive the *halakha* of flesh that was removed from the Temple courtyard from the *halakha* of a private altar, which has no halakhic area surrounding it?

The *gemara* raises its formal objection to the entire methodology: *ve-chi danim davar she-lo be-hechshero mi-davar she-be-hechshero* — how can one draw an analogy from something valid to something invalid? The comparison between *notar* blood and overnight *shlamim* meat is not between two comparable cases of the same law; one is done correctly, the other is done incorrectly. You cannot bootstrap the validity of *shlamim* meat overnight into an argument that invalid blood which slept overnight should stay on the altar.

### Resolution via Torat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָּא אַ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָעֹלָה״ רִיבָּה סְמִיךְ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The ***tanna* relied on** the verse: "Command Aaron and his sons, saying: **This is the law of the burnt offering:** It is the burnt offering on the pyre upon the altar all night until the morning; and the fire of the altar shall be kept burning there" (Leviticus 6:2), which **amplified** the application of the *halakha* stated in the verse, teaching that many types of disqualified offerings may be left upon the altar. The derivations written in the *baraita* are mere supports for those two *halakhot*. The explanations cited in the *baraita* for including these disqualifications are mentioned only to clarify why Rabbi Yehuda does not exclude them based on the terms "this," "it," and "that."

The *gemara*'s resolution is that *tanna azot torat ha'olah riba samich* — *Rabbi Yehuda* is not, in fact, making the analogical argument from a blank slate. He is not saying: because *shlamim* meat is valid overnight, *notar* blood or *emurim* should get to stay on the altar. That would indeed be a non-sequitur. Rather, he begins from *zot torat ha'olah* — the *Torah* has already told him that there is a category of *psulim* that get to stay on the altar. Given that this category exists, it is reasonable to identify its members as those *psulim* that bear some analogy to a world in which things can be *kasher*. The analogy does not provide the weight of the argument; the *Torah* does. The analogy merely helps identify *which* *psulim* belong to the category the *Torah* established. With this framing, even a weak analogy suffices — it need not be perfect, only meaningful enough to place the *psul* in the vicinity of a recognizable *kasher* context. Several of the analogies remain difficult even within this framework, particularly the *yotzei* case and the *shenishchat balayla* case when one considers the *bamah* precedent, and the *gemara* does not fully resolve every tension.

## Outside Mikdash

### Night Inside, Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּהֵמָה בַּלַּיְלָה בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, לֹא תְּהֵא פְּחוּתָה מִשּׁוֹחֵט בַּחוּץ וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an offering that was slaughtered at night, which Rabbi Yehuda holds shall descend from the altar even if it ascended, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who slaughters** a sacrificial **animal at night inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** then **offers** it **up** on an altar **outside** the Temple courtyard, is **liable** to receive *karet*, which is the punishment for one who sacrifices an offering outside the Temple courtyard. Although one is normally liable for sacrificing an offering outside the Temple courtyard only if it was fit to be offered on the altar within the Temple, and an animal slaughtered at night is disqualified and shall descend from the altar according to Rabbi Yehuda, nevertheless, the *halakha* with regard to one who slaughters an animal at night **should not be less** stringent **than** that of one who **slaughters** an animal **outside** the Temple **and offers** it **up outside.** With regard to that case, the mishna (106a) states that such a person is liable, even though an animal slaughtered outside the Temple is not fit to be offered on the altar inside the Temple and shall descend from the altar even if it has ascended.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: one who slaughters an animal — a *behema* — at night inside the *Beit HaMikdash* and then takes the meat and places it on a private altar outside, is *chayav* for *ha'alat chutz*. This is a statement about the laws of *shchutei chutz*, the prohibition against bringing offerings outside the *mikdash*. *Rabbi Yochanan* is speaking even according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that an animal slaughtered at night must come *down* from the altar if placed upon it. Even though from *Rabbi Yehuda*'s perspective this is so severe an invalidity that the offering would be removed from the altar, *Rabbi Yochanan* nonetheless says that one who brings the meat to a private altar outside is *chayav*.

### Comparative Logic

The reasoning: *lo tehei pechuta mi-shochet ba-chutz u-ma'aleh ba-chutz* — let it not be treated as less severe than one who slaughters outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and then places the meat on an external altar. In that classic case of *shchutei chutz*, both acts are fully prohibited and one is *chayav* for each. Now, critically, when someone slaughters outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and a second person then places the meat on an external altar, that second person is *chayav* — even though from the moment of the outside *shechita*, the meat could *never* have legitimately gone onto the altar inside. So too here: the night-*shechita* inside is certainly a *pasul shechita*, and the meat would have to come off the altar. Nevertheless, the act of *shechita* was a genuine act of *shechita*, and bringing the meat to an external altar makes one *chayav*, no less than in the paradigmatic outside case.

### Bird Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹף בִּפְנִים וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. שֹׁחֵט בַּחוּץ וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. נֵימָא: לֹא תְּהֵא פְּחוּתָה מִשּׁוֹחֵט וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin raises an objection** to the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan from a mishna (111a): **One who slaughters a bird inside** the Temple courtyard **and** then **offers** it **up** on an altar **outside** the Temple is **exempt,** as a bird offering is supposed to be pinched at the nape of the neck and not slaughtered with a knife. But if he **slaughtered** the bird **outside** the Temple courtyard **and offered** it **up outside** the Temple courtyard, he is **liable** to receive *karet*. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, **let us say** that the *halakha* with regard to one who slaughters an animal inside the Temple **should not be less** stringent **than** that of one who **slaughters** an animal **and offers** it **up outside** the Temple. Why, then, is such a person exempt?

*Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* raised a *tiyufta* — a contradiction — against *Rabbi Yochanan* from a *mishna*: *ha-shochet of bifnim u-ma'aleh ba-chutz, patur* — one who slaughters a bird *inside* the *Beit HaMikdash* and then places it on an external altar is *exempt*. Whereas *shachat ba-chutz u-ma'aleh ba-chutz chayav* — one who both slaughters and offers outside is *chayav*. This is precisely the paradox *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle would seem to produce for birds. If one is *chayav* when doing everything outside, one should by parity of reasoning be no less *chayav* when the *shechita* was done inside and the bringing-up was done outside. Yet the *mishna* rules *patur*. This appears to flatly contradict *Rabbi Yochanan*.

### Melikah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּיוּבְתָּא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שְׁחִיטַת הָעוֹף בִּפְנִים – מִיקְטָל קַטְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is indeed **a conclusive refutation,** and the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan is rejected. The Gemara adds a possible explanation: **If you wish, say** that one cannot derive the *halakha* of a bird slaughtered inside the Temple from that of a bird slaughtered outside of it, because in the case of **the slaughter of a bird inside** the Temple, it is considered as if **he killed it.** By contrast, in the case of an animal slaughtered at night within the Temple, although it is disqualified, it is still considered a slaughtered animal, and its status may therefore be derived from that of an animal that is slaughtered outside the Temple.

The *gemara* does not simply accept the *tiyufta*. After stating it, the *gemara* offers a resolution: *shechitat of bifnim meta katla* — the *shechita* of a bird performed *inside* the *Beit HaMikdash* is categorically different from ordinary *shechita*. Inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the proper act for a bird is *melikah* — pinching the back of the neck. *Shechita* as performed on a bird inside the *Beit HaMikdash* is not simply a *pasul* act of *shechita*; it is essentially no act of *shechita* at all. It is as though someone shot the bird in the head. There is no recognized *halachic* content to the act whatsoever — it does not even rise to the level of a *pasul shechita*, because *shechita* is simply not the applicable process for a bird in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Mammal Distinction

This is why the *mishna*'s ruling of *patur* applies specifically to the bird case and does not contradict *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position regarding mammals. When a *behema* is slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* at night, the act is a genuine act of *shechita* — *pasul*, yes, but recognizable as *shechita*. The night invalidates the offering; it does not nullify the act. Therefore, when the meat is subsequently placed on an external altar, the person is *chayav*, because the original act was a meaningful *shechita* and the bringing-outside completes a prohibited sequence. By contrast, when a bird is slaughtered inside by *shechita* rather than *melikah*, the original act has no *halachic* standing as a legitimate killing of the bird for *korban* purposes, and the subsequent bringing-outside therefore produces no *chiyuv*. The *gemara* thus vindicates *Rabbi Yochanan*: his rule applies to mammals, and the *mishna*'s exception applies uniquely to birds by virtue of the *melikah* requirement.
