# Zevachim 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Sacrifices

### Mishna Setup

The daf opens with the *mishna*, which pivots from the foregoing discussion of *piggul* to a broader set of related topics — *nosar*, *tamei*, and the question of what *halachos* apply to a distinctive category of *korban*. The *mishna* asks: what is the legal status of *kodshei ovdei kochavim* — an animal sanctified by a non-Jew for the purpose of bringing a *korban*? It is an established principle that the *Beit HaMikdash* accepts *korbanos* from non-Jews, and indeed the verse supports this. But does such a *korban* carry the full halachic status of a Jewish sacrifice?

The *mishna* states: *ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, ve-tamei* — one does not transgress the prohibitions of *piggul*, *nosar*, or *tamei* in connection with such offerings. Furthermore, one who slaughters such an animal outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is exempt (*patur*). This is presented as *divrei Rabbi Shimon* — the position of Rabbi Shimon.

### Kedusha Concept

The *mishna*'s ruling raises a fundamental conceptual question. On one hand, if we permit the animal to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*, we might expect it to carry the full array of halachic statuses that apply to any *korban*. On the other hand, one might frame the question differently: to what degree does a non-Jew's act of *hakdasha* generate genuine *kedusha* — the kind that produces consequences such as *piggul*, *nosar*, *tamei*, and *me'ilah*?

Two distinct framings emerge. The first is territorial or relational: how much does a non-Jew's *korban* truly *belong* in our *Beit HaMikdash*? The verse *beiti yikarei le-chol ha-amim* suggests universal access, and historically non-Jewish authorities sent sacrifices to be offered in the Temple. But one might still ask whether such an offering is an accommodation — a kind of *hutra* — rather than a full expression of the *korban*'s essential function. This framing connects naturally to the rule that *chutz la-mikdash* liability attaches only to something that fundamentally belongs inside, which is why exemption from that prohibition serves as an indicator of diminished status.

The second framing concerns *kedusha* as a halachic construct. The various statuses that attend a *korban* — *piggul*, *me'ilah*, *nosar*, *tamei* — are not simply the natural human experience of bringing an offering. They are products of a legal system that defines sanctity from within a Jewish framework. The question then becomes: can someone outside that system create this halachic status? This is not a question about spiritual worth but about legal capacity. A fascinating edge case illuminates the issue: if a Jew were to be *makdish* an animal as an *olah* on behalf of a non-Jewish friend, has a Jew created the *kedusha*, or is it the non-Jew's *korban* regardless? The *gemara* does not pose this precise question, but it sharpens the underlying conceptual tension.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

According to Rabbi Shimon, the *korban* of a non-Jew is accepted but carries a diminished status. All the prohibitions enumerated — *piggul*, *nosar*, *tamei*, the liability for *shechita* outside the Temple — do not apply. This position implicitly treats the non-Jew's offering as something permitted but not fully integrated into the *halachic* structure governing Jewish sacrifices.

---

## Braita Exclusions

### Hana'ah & Me'ilah

The *gemara* proceeds to a *braita* — a *tosefta* — that expands on the *mishna*'s ruling while following the position of Rabbi Shimon. The *braita* states: *kodshei ovdei kochavim lo nehenin ve-lo mo'alin* — one may not derive *hana'ah* from them, but if one does, there is no violation of *me'ilah*. Rabbinically, deriving benefit (*hana'ah*) is prohibited; at the Torah level, however, the formal *me'ilah* prohibition does not apply.

### Pigul, Nosar, Tamei

The *braita* continues: *ve-ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, ve-tamei* — the prohibitions of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* do not apply. Adding to what the *mishna* stated, the *braita* specifies further: *ve-ein osin temurah* — the laws of *temurah* do not apply, so that if one attempts to exchange the consecrated animal for another, the second animal does not become sanctified. The *braita* then addresses *nesachim*: non-Jews may not bring *nesachim* independently, though when their *korban* is offered, it must be accompanied by *nesachim*, just as any *korban* brought on the *mizbe'ach* would be. If the non-Jew provided funds for the *nesachim*, those are used; if not, public funds cover the requirement. These distinctions — some things excluded, others required — will each be linked to *pesukim* by the *gemara*.

---

## Non-Jewish Sacrifices…

### Rabbi Yose Position

Rabbi Yose takes the opposing view: *roni be-chulam le-hachmir* — in all of these matters, I would rule strictly that the full halachic status applies. His reasoning derives from the very verse that establishes non-Jewish participation. The *pasuk* in *Vayikra* reads: *ish ish mi-beis Yisrael u-min ha-ger be-Yisrael asher yakriv korbano le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvosam asher yakrivu la-Hashem le-olah*. The phrase *ish ish* — "any person" — is taken to include non-Jews. Strikingly, the very *pasuk* that one might least expect to extend participation to non-Jews — one that begins with *mi-beis Yisrael* — serves as the textual source for their inclusion, by virtue of the doubled *ish ish*.

Rabbi Yose then reasons: since this *pasuk* ends with *asher yakrivu la-Shem* — that they bring it "to God" — the offering is fully consecrated *la-Shem*. If the *pasuk* itself declares the offering fully dedicated to God, Rabbi Yose argues it cannot simultaneously be treated as a second-class sacrifice. The logic is straightforward: if you include them, you include them fully.

## Braita Exclusions…

### Me'ilah Distinction

The *braita* then introduces a qualification: *bameh devarim amorim — be-kodshei mizbe'ach. Aval be-kodshei bedek ha-bayis mo'alin bahen* — this applies to sacrificial *korbanos*, but when it comes to property sanctified to the Temple treasury (*kedushas bedek ha-bayis*), the laws of *me'ilah* do apply even according to Rabbi Shimon.

Rashi reads this as a continuation of Rabbi Shimon's position — not Rabbi Yose's — and it is a logically compelling distinction. The *gemara* explains: *kasavar ki gamar me'ilah cheit cheit mi-terumah dumya de-terumah de-kadish kedushas ha-guf*. The *me'ilah* prohibition is derived from *terumah* via a *gezeira shava* on the word *cheit*, and *terumah* involves *kedushas ha-guf* — an intrinsic sanctity defined by the object's designated purpose. A *korban* is sanctified for the *mizbe'ach*; *terumah* is sanctified for the *kohanim*. In both cases, the sanctity is a function of what the object *is*, not who owns it. It is in this domain — sanctity of purpose — that Rabbi Shimon argues a non-Jew cannot generate the full legal status, because the *halachic* system defining that sanctity is specifically Jewish.

But *kedushas bedek ha-bayis* is different. This is *kedushas damim* — monetary sanctity, sanctity of value and ownership. When a non-Jew transfers ownership of his property to the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *kedusha* derives from the Temple's *ownership* of that asset, not from the non-Jew's ability to confer *kedushas ha-guf*. Once the Temple holds legal title, the *me'ilah* prohibition follows automatically, regardless of who originally owned the item. Thus even Rabbi Shimon, who limits the halachic status of non-Jewish sacrificial offerings, agrees that *me'ilah* applies to *kodshei bedek ha-bayis* of a non-Jew.

## Torah Derivations

### Hana'ah & Me'ilah Gezeira

The *gemara* now provides the textual underpinnings for each of these exclusions. Regarding *hana'ah*: the prohibition on deriving benefit is *mi-de-rabbanan* — rabbinic in origin. There is no Torah-level prohibition violated by deriving *hana'ah* from a non-Jewish *korban*; the Sages enacted the restriction. Regarding *me'ilah*: it is excluded by a *gezeira shava* from *terumah*. The *me'ilah* violation is described using the word *cheit*, and the same word *cheit* appears in the context of *terumah* — specifically in the phrase *velo yisu alav cheit*. Furthermore, *terumah* explicitly limits itself: *velo yechalelu et kodshei bnei Yisrael*. Since *terumah* is demonstrably the domain of *Bnei Yisrael*, and *me'ilah* is learned from *terumah*, it too is limited to *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Pigul Gezeira Shavah

The exclusion of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* follows a chain of *gezeiros shavos*. The *pasuk* governing *tumah* states: *vayinazru mi-kodshei Bnei Yisrael* — separate yourselves from the sanctified items of *Bnei Yisrael*. This *pasuk* thus limits *tumah* to the *korbanos* of *Bnei Yisrael*. From *tumah*, *nosar* is derived via the shared keyword *chilul* (both violations are described as *chilul* in their respective *pesukim*). And from *nosar*, *piggul* is derived via the shared keyword *avon* (*piggul avon avon mi-notar*). The irony is notable: the same verse that uses the phrase *Bnei Yisrael* to include non-Jews in *korban*-bringing (*ish ish*) is also the source tradition that ultimately works to limit their inclusion in these various prohibitions. Rabbi Shimon reads the inclusion as partial and governed by the many verses that specify *Bnei Yisrael* explicitly.

### Temurah Hekesh

For *temurah*, the derivation is more complex. The *parsha* of *temurah* in the Torah opens with *maaser behema*, and the *gemara* notes that *maaser behema* and *temurah* are thus linked through a *hekesh* — a juxtaposition. Furthermore, the verse *aser te-aser* is read as linking *maaser behema* to *maaser dagan*, grain tithes, and *maaser dagan* explicitly states *Bnei Yisrael*. The apparent conclusion is: *maaser dagan* establishes *Bnei Yisrael* → *maaser behema* → *temurah*, and therefore *temurah* applies only to *Bnei Yisrael*.

However, the *gemara* pauses to raise a significant methodological problem: *vechi davar ha-lamed be-hekesh chozer u-melamed be-hekesh?* — can something derived through a *hekesh* itself turn around and teach through another *hekesh*? Unlike a *gezeira shava*, which carries a special hermeneutic power allowing multi-step derivations, a *hekesh* — which works through natural textual juxtaposition rather than a shared keyword — does not permit this kind of double jump in the domain of *kodshim*. The *gemara* answers that ordinarily the rule against double *hekesh* applies when the intermediate category is *kodshim*, but *maaser dagan* is *chulin* — non-sacred produce — which permits the double step. This, however, raises a further complication: does one determine the category by *the source* of the teaching (*batar melamed azlinan*), or by *the subject* of the teaching (*batar lamed azlinan*)? If the latter, then since *maaser behema* is *kodshim*, the double jump may again be impermissible for those who hold that view.

### Maaser Behemah

The *gemara* resolves this difficulty by sidestepping the *maaser dagan* link entirely. There is a direct and simpler reason to exclude non-Jews from *maaser behema*: it is an obligatory sacrifice (*chovah*), and non-Jews were only ever included in *nedarim* and *nedavos* — vows and free-will offerings. They were never included in obligatory sacrifices. Therefore, non-Jews are self-evidently excluded from *maaser behema* without any need for a chain of derivation. And since *maaser behema* and *temurah* are linked by *hekesh* within the same *parsha*, and non-Jews are definitionally excluded from *maaser behema*, they are likewise excluded from *temurah*. *Ve-chovah she-ein la zman kavua Yisrael maasiru, ovdei kochavim lo maasiru.*

## Nesachim Rules

### Ezrach vs. Non-Jew

The *gemara* now turns to the *nesachim* requirement, citing the relevant *parsha* in *Bamidbar* 15 — in *Parshat Shelach*, following the *chet ha-miraglim*. The *Torah* states that when one brings a *korban* — *olah*, *zavach*, *neder*, or *nedavah* — one brings the *nesachim* along with it. The *pasuk* continues: *ve-chi yagur itchem ger* — if a *ger* dwells among you and brings a fire-offering, he does likewise. And then: *Torah achat u-mishpat echad yihyeh lachem ve-lagger ha-gar*. Before that, the *pasuk* states: *kol ha-ezrach ya'aseh kachah et elleh* — the *ezrach* brings like this. The *braita* derives: *ezrach mevi nesachim ve-ein oved kochavim mevi nesachim* — the *ezrach* brings *nesachim*, but a non-Jew does not bring *nesachim* independently.

### Nesachim Attached

One might have inferred from this that even a *korban* brought by a non-Jew is exempt from *nesachim* requirements. The *braita* answers: *talmud lomar kachah* — the word *kachah* instructs that every *korban* on the *mizbe'ach* must come with *nesachim*. Rashi notes that *kachah* is parallel to the usage *kachah ye'aseh la-par ha-echad*, specifying the manner in which a *korban* is brought. The upshot is clear: a non-Jew cannot bring *nesachim* as a standalone offering, but when he brings an animal *korban*, the *korban* itself demands *nesachim* — whether funded by the non-Jew or by public Temple resources.

## Ritzuy Tzitz

### Blood Tamei Case

The *gemara* introduces a *braita* concerning a different topic with a parallel Jew–non-Jew distinction: the *ritzuy tzitz*, the capacity of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *tzitz* to render acceptable a *korban* whose blood became *tamei*. The *braita* states: *dam she-nitma ve-zarko be-shogeg — hurtzah; be-meizid — lo hurtzah*. If blood that had become *tamei* was sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach* unknowingly, the *korban* is accepted — that is the principle of *ritzuy tzitz*, rooted in the verse that the *tzitz* atones for *tumot Bnei Yisrael*. But if it was done knowingly, the *gemara* explains that while *mi-ikar ha-din* the *tzitz* might technically render the *korban* acceptable, the Sages ruled as a *kenas* that in the case of knowing transgression, the owner may not eat the meat. Importantly, it does not mean another *korban* must be brought — that would raise the issue of *chulin ba-azara* if the first in fact worked — but the practical consequence is that the meat is forbidden *mi-de-rabbanan*.

By contrast, for a communal *korban*, the *tzitz* is *meratze* both *be-shogeg* and *be-meizid* without restriction.

### Non-Jew Ritzuy

The *braita* then states: for a *korban* of a non-Jew — *bein be-shogeg bein be-meizid lo hurtzah* — neither knowingly nor unknowingly does the *tzitz* render it acceptable. The Rabbis raised this before Rav Pappa: is this ruling consistent only with Rabbi Shimon, who holds that non-Jewish *korbanos* lack full status? For Rabbi Yose, who grants them full status and rules strictly on all counts, the *ritzuy tzitz* should presumably apply. Rav Pappa responded: *afilu teima Rabbi Yose* — even according to Rabbi Yose the *tzitz* does not work here, because a distinct verse limits it: *le-ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — the *tzitz* creates *ratzon* for *them*, meaning *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Rav Ashi Resolution

Rav Huna son of Rav Nassan challenged Rav Pappa: if we use the word *lahem* to exclude non-Jews, we face a problem. The very *pasuk* that permits non-Jews to bring *korbanos* contains the phrase *asher hem makdishim li* — "that *they* sanctify to Me." If *hem* there does not exclude non-Jews from bringing *korbanos* in the first place, how can *lahem* elsewhere exclude them from *ritzuy tzitz*? One cannot simply invoke a pronoun as an exclusionary force in one context when the same grammatical structure is used inclusively elsewhere.

Rav Ashi provides the resolution: the exclusion of non-Jews from *ritzuy tzitz* does not rest mechanically on the word *lahem* as a pronoun. Rather, *le-ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* expresses a substantive idea: the *ritzuy* achieved by the *tzitz* is grounded in a particular relationship — *lahem* — between *Bnei Yisrael* and God. The *gemara* articulates this as: *ve-ovdei kochavim lav bnei hartzah ninhu*. The concept of *ritzuy* — of the sacrifice being favorably received — is tied to the unique covenantal relationship expressed through the *avodah* of *Bnei Yisrael*. The *tzitz*, as part of the *bigdei kehunah*, and the entire apparatus of *ritzuy* it achieves, is anchored in that relationship.

It should be noted that one need not read this as a sweeping theological statement that God does not accept prayer or sacrifice from non-Jews — after all, *Noach* brings *korbanos* and they are clearly accepted. The more precise reading is that this specific mechanism of *ritzuy* — the formal *ritzuy* achieved by the *kohen gadol*'s *tzitz* as part of the Temple's *avodah* system — operates within the framework of *Bnei Yisrael*'s covenantal service and is not extended to non-Jewish *korbanos*. Nevertheless, the *gemara*'s language — *lav bnei hartzah ninhu* — is broader than this narrow reading strictly requires.

One additional conceptual observation is worth noting in this context. A student raised the question of whether the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yose might be understood as focusing on different axes: Rabbi Shimon focusing on the *gavra* — the person behind the offering — and Rabbi Yose focusing on the *cheftza* — the object, the animal itself. If Rabbi Yose treats the animal as simply an object that has been sanctified and placed on the *mizbe'ach*, then distinctions based on the offerer's identity become less operative. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, may be more attentive to who stands behind the act of *hakdasha* and whether that person's relationship to the *Beit HaMikdash* is such that they can generate the full legal status. This reading resonates with a broader observation about the halachic literature on *korbanos*: the Talmud tends to focus on the technical acts of the *kohanim* rather than on the identity and intent of the offerer, and the dispute here may partly reflect that tension.

## Piggul Mishna

### Piggul Exclusions

The *mishna* now addresses a different question: which items are excluded from *piggul*? The *mishna* states: *devarim she-ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, chayavin aleihem mishum nosar mishum tamei*. Things that are themselves *matirim* — such as the *kometz*, *levonah*, and *dam* — are not subject to *piggul* (this was established earlier). But one who eats them in a state of *tumah* or leaves them past their time (*nosar*) does transgress those prohibitions. The one exception is *dam*: *chutz min ha-dam* — blood is excluded from *nosar* and *tamei* as well, for reasons to be worked out in the *gemara*.

### Rabbi Shimon Edible

Rabbi Shimon adds a further qualification: *Rabbi Shimon mechayev be-davar she-darko le-echol* — Rabbi Shimon holds one liable only for things that are customarily eaten. He does not dispute the exclusion of *dam*, but he does dispute whether *tamei* and *nosar* apply to *levonah*, *ketoret*, and wood, which are not eaten. The *mishna* thus records his view: *aval etzim ve-levonah ve-ketoret ein chayavin aleihem mishum tumah* — one who is *tamei* and comes into contact with these non-edible sanctified items, or who retains them past their time, does not thereby transgress. The violations of *tamei* and *nosar* require that the object be of the kind normally consumed.

## Tamei Scope

### Kal VaChomer

The *gemara* now interrogates the scope of *tumah*. The *mishna* has implied that *tumah* applies even to items that are not subject to *piggul* — specifically, to *matirim* such as *kometz* and *levonah*. But the *gemara* asks: why not confine *tumah* to the same scope as *piggul*? A *kal va-chomer* is constructed:

*Piggul* is stringent in several respects: it requires a *korban chatas kevuah* (a fixed-value sin-offering), only one act of awareness is required (*yedia achas*), and it has no exceptions — it is never *hutar mi-kelalo*. Despite all this stringency, *piggul* applies only to something that has a *matir* — both a *matir* for human consumption and a *matir* for the *mizbe'ach* (*bein le-adam bein le-mizbe'ach*). If *tumah* is lighter — it requires a variable-cost offering (*oleh ve-yored*), demands prior and subsequent awareness (*shtei yedios*), and is sometimes suspended in public circumstances (*tumah hutrah be-tzibur*) — then certainly *tumah* should have no broader scope than *piggul*. By a *kal va-chomer*, *tumah* should likewise apply only to items with a *matir*.

### Asher Hem Makdishim

The *gemara* answers from a verse in the *parsha* of *tumah*: *vayinazru mi-kodshei Bnei Yisrael asher hem makdishim li* — separate from the sanctified items that they dedicate to Me. The phrase *asher hem makdishim li* is deliberately broad — "all things that they sanctify to Me." This overrides the *kal va-chomer* and expands the scope of *tumah* to include any sanctified item, regardless of whether it has a *matir* in the manner required for *piggul*.

### Yikrav Requirement

One might then think *tumah* applies from the moment of *hakdasha* — from the instant an item is sanctified. The *gemara* corrects this by citing a further verse: *kol ish asher yikrav mi-kol zarachem el ha-kodashim asher yakdishu Bnei Yisrael la-Hashem ve-tumaso alav* — the word *yikrav* indicates that liability attaches only when the item is *fit* to be offered, not from the mere moment of sanctification. Rabbi Elazar clarifies: *yikrav* cannot be read literally as "touching" (there is no known *kares* liability for merely touching a sanctified item while *tamei*). Rather, *be-muchshar basar li-krav ha-katuv medaber* — the verse speaks of when the item is fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach* or consumed by those entitled to eat it.

### Matirim Threshold

For items that have a *matir*, the threshold is straightforward: *mi-she-yikrevu matiraiv*. The *korban*'s meat and entrails become fit for eating or the *mizbe'ach* only once the blood has been sprinkled. Correspondingly, one who is *tamei* and eats the meat is not liable until after the *zrikat ha-dam* has occurred. Before that point, the meat is not yet in the state of *muchshar li-krav*, and there is no *kares* liability for consuming it while *tamei*.

### Kli Sharet Threshold

For items that have no *matir* — such as *kometz*, *levonah*, *ketoret*, or *minchat kohanim* — there is no analogous act of *zrika* to render them fit. What makes these items fit for the *mizbe'ach* or for their proper use? The *gemara* answers: *mi-she-yikdash be-kli* — once they are placed in a *kli sharet*, a sacred service vessel. It is this act that renders them fully fit to be offered. Therefore, one who eats such an item while *tamei* is liable only once it has been sanctified through a *kli sharet*. This is the *yikrav* threshold for this category.

## Nosar Scope

### Chilul vs. Avon

The *gemara* now turns to *nosar* and asks whether it follows the broad scope of *tumah* or the narrow scope of *piggul*. The chain of derivation that excluded *piggul* and *nosar* from non-Jewish *korbanos* (discussed earlier) also implied that *nosar* and *piggul* are related — *nosar* was linked to *piggul* via the *gezeira shava* of *avon-avon*. Now the *gemara* raises the competing possibility: *nosar* appears in its *parsha* with the word *chilul*, and *tumah* appears in its *parsha* with the word *chilul*. Should we therefore link *nosar* to *tumah* via *chilul-chilul*, giving it the broad scope? Or should we link *nosar* to *piggul* via *avon-avon*, giving it the narrow scope?

This is a genuine *gezeira shava* conflict: *nosar* shares *chilul* with *tumah* and *avon* with *piggul*. How do we resolve which linkage governs? The *gemara* raises the possibility that there are more points of similarity between *nosar* and *piggul* than between *nosar* and *tumah*, which would argue for linking *nosar* to *piggul*. The common features of *nosar* and *piggul* include: neither is *hutar be-tzibur*; the *tzitz* works for *tumah* but not for either *nosar* or *piggul*; both *nosar* and *piggul* are time-related (one conceptually, one factually); and both apply to the *object* consumed, whereas *tumah* is a status of the *person*. On this count, the similarities tilt toward *piggul*. If so, *nosar* should have *piggul*'s narrow scope and not apply to *matirim*.

### Double Lamed

The *gemara* resolves the tension by appealing not to the *gezeira shava* comparison but to an internal reading of the *pasuk* governing *tumah* itself. Levi teaches: *minayin she-af be-psul zman ha-katuv medaber? Talmud lomar ve-lo yechalelu* — the doubled *lamed* in *ve-lo yechalelu* signals two forms of *chilul*. The *pasuk* does not merely govern *tumah*; it is formulated to encompass another category of *chilul* as well. That second *chilul* is *nosar* — *psul zman*, the disqualification through time. Accordingly, *nosar* is included within the same *pasuk* that establishes *tumah*'s broad scope, and derives its scope from there directly — not via the *chilul-chilul gezeira shava* from *tumah*, which would face the competing *avon-avon* derivation from *piggul*, but via the doubled *lamed* in the *pasuk* itself, which independently includes *psul zman*.

### Nosar Conclusion

The upshot is that *nosar* does not derive its scope by comparison to *piggul* or by a contested *gezeira shava*. Instead, the *pasuk* that establishes *tumah*'s broad scope — *velo yechalelu* — simultaneously establishes through its doubled *lamed* that *nosar* shares that same broad scope. Both *nosar* and *tumah* therefore apply to a wider range of items than *piggul*, including those without a *matir* in the *piggul* sense, subject to the *yikrav* threshold: the item must be fit for offering, whether through the completion of its *matirim* or through placement in a *kli sharet*.
