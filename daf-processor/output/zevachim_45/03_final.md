# Zevachim 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Sacrifices

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קׇדְשֵׁי גוֹיִם – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא; וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **offerings** consecrated by **gentiles** for sacrifice to God, **one is not liable for** eating **them,** neither **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*** if the sacrificial rites were performed with the intent to eat the offering beyond its designated time, nor due to violation of the prohibition of ***notar*, nor** due to violation of the prohibition against eating the meat while **ritually impure. And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **exempt;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon. And Rabbi Yosei deems** him **liable.**

The daf opens with the *mishna*, which pivots from the foregoing discussion of *piggul* to a broader set of related topics — *nosar*, *tamei*, and the question of what *halachos* apply to a distinctive category of *korban*. The *mishna* asks: what is the legal status of *kodshei ovdei kochavim* — an animal sanctified by a non-Jew for the purpose of bringing a *korban*? It is an established principle that the *Beit HaMikdash* accepts *korbanos* from non-Jews, and indeed the verse supports this. But does such a *korban* carry the full halachic status of a Jewish sacrifice?

The *mishna* states: *ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, ve-tamei* — one does not transgress the prohibitions of *piggul*, *nosar*, or *tamei* in connection with such offerings. Furthermore, one who slaughters such an animal outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is exempt (*patur*). This is presented as *divrei Rabbi Shimon* — the position of Rabbi Shimon.

### Kedusha Concept

The *mishna*'s ruling raises a fundamental conceptual question. On one hand, if we permit the animal to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*, we might expect it to carry the full array of halachic statuses that apply to any *korban*. On the other hand, one might frame the question differently: to what degree does a non-Jew's act of *hakdasha* generate genuine *kedusha* — the kind that produces consequences such as *piggul*, *nosar*, *tamei*, and *me'ilah*?

Two distinct framings emerge. The first is territorial or relational: how much does a non-Jew's *korban* truly *belong* in our *Beit HaMikdash*? The verse *beiti yikarei le-chol ha-amim* suggests universal access, and historically non-Jewish authorities sent sacrifices to be offered in the Temple. But one might still ask whether such an offering is an accommodation — a kind of *hutra* — rather than a full expression of the *korban*'s essential function. This framing connects naturally to the rule that *chutz la-mikdash* liability attaches only to something that fundamentally belongs inside, which is why exemption from that prohibition serves as an indicator of diminished status.

The second framing concerns *kedusha* as a halachic construct. The various statuses that attend a *korban* — *piggul*, *me'ilah*, *nosar*, *tamei* — are not simply the natural human experience of bringing an offering. They are products of a legal system that defines sanctity from within a Jewish framework. The question then becomes: can someone outside that system create this halachic status? This is not a question about spiritual worth but about legal capacity. A fascinating edge case illuminates the issue: if a Jew were to be *makdish* an animal as an *olah* on behalf of a non-Jewish friend, has a Jew created the *kedusha*, or is it the non-Jew's *korban* regardless? The *gemara* does not pose this precise question, but it sharpens the underlying conceptual tension.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

According to Rabbi Shimon, the *korban* of a non-Jew is accepted but carries a diminished status. All the prohibitions enumerated — *piggul*, *nosar*, *tamei*, the liability for *shechita* outside the Temple — do not apply. This position implicitly treats the non-Jew's offering as something permitted but not fully integrated into the *halachic* structure governing Jewish sacrifices.

## Braita Exclusions

### Hana'ah & Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קׇדְשֵׁי גוֹיִם – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **offerings** consecrated by **gentiles,** one **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them, he is **not** liable after the fact for **misusing** consecrated property. **And one is not liable for** eating **them,** neither **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*** if the sacrificial rites were performed with the intent to eat the offering beyond its designated time, nor due to violation of the prohibition of ***notar*, nor** due to violation of the prohibition against eating the meat while **ritually impure.**

The *gemara* proceeds to a *braita* — a *tosefta* — that expands on the *mishna*'s ruling while following the position of Rabbi Shimon. The *braita* states: *kodshei ovdei kochavim lo nehenin ve-lo mo'alin* — one may not derive *hana'ah* from them, but if one does, there is no violation of *me'ilah*. Rabbinically, deriving benefit (*hana'ah*) is prohibited; at the Torah level, however, the formal *me'ilah* prohibition does not apply.

### Pigul, Nosar, Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין נְסָכִים, אֲבָל קׇרְבָּנָן טָעוּן נְסָכִים. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** gentiles **cannot render** an animal **a substitute,** i.e., if a gentile stated with regard to an animal that it should be the substitute of a consecrated animal, the substitution does not take effect. **And** gentiles **cannot bring libations** that are brought by themselves as a separate offering and do not accompany an animal offering, **but their** animal **offerings require libations.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.**

The *braita* continues: *ve-ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, nosar, ve-tamei* — the prohibitions of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* do not apply. Adding to what the *mishna* stated, the *braita* specifies further: *ve-ein osin temurah* — the laws of *temurah* do not apply, so that if one attempts to exchange the consecrated animal for another, the second animal does not become sanctified. The *braita* then addresses *nesachim*: non-Jews may not bring *nesachim* independently, though when their *korban* is offered, it must be accompanied by *nesachim*, just as any *korban* brought on the *mizbe'ach* would be. If the non-Jew provided funds for the *nesachim*, those are used; if not, public funds cover the requirement. These distinctions — some things excluded, others required — will each be linked to *pesukim* by the *gemara*.

## Non-Jewish Sacrifices…

### Rabbi Yose Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: רוֹאֶה אֲנִי בְּכוּלָּן לְהַחְמִיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״לַה׳״. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּקׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: I see** the logic of the opinion that **in all of** these cases it is correct **to be stringent** about the offerings of gentiles, **as it is stated with regard to them:** "Any man of the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel that will sacrifice his offering…**to the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:18). This indicates that all offerings, even those of gentiles, are fully consecrated to God; therefore, the *halakhot* of misuse, *piggul*, *notar*, and eating the meat while ritually impure should all apply to the offerings of gentiles. **In what** case **is this statement said? In** the case of items **consecrated for the altar. But** with regard to items that are **consecrated** by gentiles **for Temple maintenance,** one who derives benefit from them is liable for **misusing them.** This concludes the *baraita*.

Rabbi Yose takes the opposing view: *roni be-chulam le-hachmir* — in all of these matters, I would rule strictly that the full halachic status applies. His reasoning derives from the very verse that establishes non-Jewish participation. The *pasuk* in *Vayikra* reads: *ish ish mi-beis Yisrael u-min ha-ger be-Yisrael asher yakriv korbano le-chol nidreihem u-le-chol nidvosam asher yakrivu la-Hashem le-olah*. The phrase *ish ish* — "any person" — is taken to include non-Jews. Strikingly, the very *pasuk* that one might least expect to extend participation to non-Jews — one that begins with *mi-beis Yisrael* — serves as the textual source for their inclusion, by virtue of the doubled *ish ish*.

Rabbi Yose then reasons: since this *pasuk* ends with *asher yakrivu la-Shem* — that they bring it "to God" — the offering is fully consecrated *la-Shem*. If the *pasuk* itself declares the offering fully dedicated to God, Rabbi Yose argues it cannot simultaneously be treated as a second-class sacrifice. The logic is straightforward: if you include them, you include them fully.

## Braita Exclusions…

### Me'ilah Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. לֹא נֶהֱנִין – מִדְּרַבָּנַן. וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין – דְּגָמַר מְעִילָה ״חֵט״–״חֵט״ מִתְּרוּמָה, דְּבִתְרוּמָה כְּתִיב: ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – וְלֹא גוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara begins to analyze this *baraita* in detail. The *baraita* taught that one **may not derive benefit** from items consecrated by gentiles *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them, he is **not** liable after the fact for **misusing** of consecrated property. The Gemara explains: **One may not** derive **benefit** from them **by rabbinic law,** as the Sages prohibited deriving benefit from any item that was consecrated to God. The Gemara's explanation continues: **But** if one derived benefit from them, he is **not** liable after the fact for **misusing** consecrated property, **as** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **derives** the *halakha* of **misuse** of consecrated property through a verbal analogy between **"sin"** stated with regard to misuse of consecrated items and the word **"sin"** stated with regard to ***teruma*.** With regard to misuse of consecrated property, the verse states: "If any one commits a trespass, and sins through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). In the case of *teruma*, the verse states: "Lest they bear sin for it, and die due to it, if they profane it" (Leviticus 22:9). And **with regard to *teruma*, it is written:** "And they shall not profane the sacred items of **the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 22:15), which indicates: **But not** the sacred items of **gentiles,** i.e., one is not liable for partaking of the *teruma* of gentiles while he is in a state of ritual impurity.

The *braita* then introduces a qualification: *bameh devarim amorim — be-kodshei mizbe'ach. Aval be-kodshei bedek ha-bayis mo'alin bahen* — this applies to sacrificial *korbanos*, but when it comes to property sanctified to the Temple treasury (*kedushas bedek ha-bayis*), the laws of *me'ilah* do apply even according to Rabbi Shimon.

Rashi reads this as a continuation of Rabbi Shimon's position — not Rabbi Yose's — and it is a logically compelling distinction. The *gemara* explains: *kasavar ki gamar me'ilah cheit cheit mi-terumah dumya de-terumah de-kadish kedushas ha-guf*. The *me'ilah* prohibition is derived from *terumah* via a *gezeira shava* on the word *cheit*, and *terumah* involves *kedushas ha-guf* — an intrinsic sanctity defined by the object's designated purpose. A *korban* is sanctified for the *mizbe'ach*; *terumah* is sanctified for the *kohanim*. In both cases, the sanctity is a function of what the object *is*, not who owns it. It is in this domain — sanctity of purpose — that Rabbi Shimon argues a non-Jew cannot generate the full legal status, because the *halachic* system defining that sanctity is specifically Jewish.

But *kedushas bedek ha-bayis* is different. This is *kedushas damim* — monetary sanctity, sanctity of value and ownership. When a non-Jew transfers ownership of his property to the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *kedusha* derives from the Temple's *ownership* of that asset, not from the non-Jew's ability to confer *kedushas ha-guf*. Once the Temple holds legal title, the *me'ilah* prohibition follows automatically, regardless of who originally owned the item. Thus even Rabbi Shimon, who limits the halachic status of non-Jewish sacrificial offerings, agrees that *me'ilah* applies to *kodshei bedek ha-bayis* of a non-Jew.

## Torah Derivations

### Hana'ah & Me'ilah Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָתֵי פִּיגּוּל ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ מִנּוֹתָר, וְאָתֵי נוֹתָר ״חִילּוּל״–״חִילּוּל״ מִטּוּמְאָה, וּבְטוּמְאָה כְּתִיב: ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – וְלֹא גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further taught: **And one is not liable for** eating the offerings of gentiles **due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** or ***notar*, or** eating the meat while **ritually impure.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara explains that the *halakha* of ***piggul* is derived** through a verbal analogy between the word **"iniquity"** stated with regard to *piggul* and the word **"iniquity"** stated with regard to ***notar*.** With regard to *piggul*, the verse states: "It shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18), and with regard to leftover sacrificial meat the verse states: "Therefore anyone who eats it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 19:8). **And** the *halakha* of ***notar*** itself is **derived** through a verbal analogy between **profanation** stated with regard to *notar* and **profanation** stated with regard to **ritual impurity.** With regard to *notar* the verse states: "Because he has profaned the sacred item of the Lord" (Leviticus 19:8), and with regard to impurity the verse states: "And that they do not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2). **And with regard to impurity it is written** in that same verse: "That they separate themselves from the sacred items of **the children of Israel,"** which indicates: **But not** the sacred items of **gentiles.**

The *gemara* now provides the textual underpinnings for each of these exclusions. Regarding *hana'ah*: the prohibition on deriving benefit is *mi-de-rabbanan* — rabbinic in origin. There is no Torah-level prohibition violated by deriving *hana'ah* from a non-Jewish *korban*; the Sages enacted the restriction. Regarding *me'ilah*: it is excluded by a *gezeira shava* from *terumah*. The *me'ilah* violation is described using the word *cheit*, and the same word *cheit* appears in the context of *terumah* — specifically in the phrase *velo yisu alav cheit*. Furthermore, *terumah* explicitly limits itself: *velo yechalelu et kodshei bnei Yisrael*. Since *terumah* is demonstrably the domain of *Bnei Yisrael*, and *me'ilah* is learned from *terumah*, it too is limited to *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Pigul Gezeira Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ תְּמוּרָה לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, וּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְמַעְשַׂר דָּגָן, וּבְמַעְשַׂר דָּגָן כְּתִיב: ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – וְלֹא גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also teaches: **And** gentiles **cannot render** an animal **a substitute.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara explains: It is **because substitution is juxtaposed** in the Torah **with animal tithe,** as the verse states: "And concerning the tithe of the herd, or of the flock…the tenth shall be sacred to the Lord…neither shall he make a substitute for it" (Leviticus 27:32–33). **And animal tithe is juxtaposed with the tithe of grains,** as the verse states: "You shall tithe [*asser te'asser*] all the increase of your seed that the field brings forth year by year. And you shall eat before the Lord your God, in the place that He shall choose to place His name there, the tithe of your grain" (Deuteronomy 14:22–23). The doubled verb form, *asser te'asser*, is understood as an allusion to two tithes, grain tithe and animal tithe. **And with regard to the tithe of grains it is written:** "When you take of **the children of Israel** the tithes" (Numbers 18:24), which indicates: **But not** of **gentiles.**

The exclusion of *piggul*, *nosar*, and *tamei* follows a chain of *gezeiros shavos*. The *pasuk* governing *tumah* states: *vayinazru mi-kodshei Bnei Yisrael* — separate yourselves from the sanctified items of *Bnei Yisrael*. This *pasuk* thus limits *tumah* to the *korbanos* of *Bnei Yisrael*. From *tumah*, *nosar* is derived via the shared keyword *chilul* (both violations are described as *chilul* in their respective *pesukim*). And from *nosar*, *piggul* is derived via the shared keyword *avon* (*piggul avon avon mi-notar*). The irony is notable: the same verse that uses the phrase *Bnei Yisrael* to include non-Jews in *korban*-bringing (*ish ish*) is also the source tradition that ultimately works to limit their inclusion in these various prohibitions. Rabbi Shimon reads the inclusion as partial and governed by the many verses that specify *Bnei Yisrael* explicitly.

### Temurah Hekesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ?! מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן חוּלִּין הוּא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתַר מְלַמֵּד אָזְלִינַן; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתַר לָמֵד אָזְלִינַן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה חוֹבָה – שֶׁאֵין קָבוּעַ לָהּ זְמַן הוּא, וְחוֹבָה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ זְמַן קָבוּעַ – יִשְׂרָאֵל מַיְיתוּ, גּוֹיִם לָא מַיְיתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does a matter derived via a juxtaposition again teach via a juxtaposition?** There is a principle that in consecrated matters, a *halakha* derived via a juxtaposition cannot teach another *halakha* via a juxtaposition. The Gemara answers: This derivation is not relevant exclusively to consecrated matters, as the **tithe of grains is non-sacred** food. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This works out well according to the one who says** that when implementing this principle **we follow** the source that **teaches** the *halakha*, i.e., if the matter that teaches the first juxtaposition involves non-sacred items, one can employ two juxtapositions even with regard to deriving the *halakha* for consecrated matters. **But according to the one who says** that **we follow** the matter that **is taught** the *halakha*, i.e., the case to which we wish to apply the *halakha*, and if that case involves offerings one cannot employ two juxtapositions, **what can be said?** **Rather,** the reason why gentiles cannot bring an animal tithe offering is that **animal tithe is an obligation for which there is no fixed time, and** with regard to any **obligation for which there is no fixed time, a Jew can bring** it but **gentiles cannot bring** it. And as stated, substitution is juxtaposed with the animal tithe, and therefore gentiles can also not render an animal a substitute.

For *temurah*, the derivation is more complex. The *parsha* of *temurah* in the Torah opens with *maaser behema*, and the *gemara* notes that *maaser behema* and *temurah* are thus linked through a *hekesh* — a juxtaposition. Furthermore, the verse *aser te-aser* is read as linking *maaser behema* to *maaser dagan*, grain tithes, and *maaser dagan* explicitly states *Bnei Yisrael*. The apparent conclusion is: *maaser dagan* establishes *Bnei Yisrael* → *maaser behema* → *temurah*, and therefore *temurah* applies only to *Bnei Yisrael*.

However, the *gemara* pauses to raise a significant methodological problem: *vechi davar ha-lamed be-hekesh chozer u-melamed be-hekesh?* — can something derived through a *hekesh* itself turn around and teach through another *hekesh*? Unlike a *gezeira shava*, which carries a special hermeneutic power allowing multi-step derivations, a *hekesh* — which works through natural textual juxtaposition rather than a shared keyword — does not permit this kind of double jump in the domain of *kodshim*. The *gemara* answers that ordinarily the rule against double *hekesh* applies when the intermediate category is *kodshim*, but *maaser dagan* is *chulin* — non-sacred produce — which permits the double step. This, however, raises a further complication: does one determine the category by *the source* of the teaching (*batar melamed azlinan*), or by *the subject* of the teaching (*batar lamed azlinan*)? If the latter, then since *maaser behema* is *kodshim*, the double jump may again be impermissible for those who hold that view.

### Maaser Behemah

The *gemara* resolves this difficulty by sidestepping the *maaser dagan* link entirely. There is a direct and simpler reason to exclude non-Jews from *maaser behema*: it is an obligatory sacrifice (*chovah*), and non-Jews were only ever included in *nedarim* and *nedavos* — vows and free-will offerings. They were never included in obligatory sacrifices. Therefore, non-Jews are self-evidently excluded from *maaser behema* without any need for a chain of derivation. And since *maaser behema* and *temurah* are linked by *hekesh* within the same *parsha*, and non-Jews are definitionally excluded from *maaser behema*, they are likewise excluded from *temurah*. *Ve-chovah she-ein la zman kavua Yisrael maasiru, ovdei kochavim lo maasiru.*

## Nesachim Rules

### Ezrach vs. Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבִיאִין נְסָכִים. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶזְרָח״ – אֶזְרָח מֵבִיא נְסָכִים, וְאֵין הַגּוֹי מֵבִיא נְסָכִים. יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא עוֹלָתוֹ טְעוּנָה נְסָכִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כָּכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And** gentiles **cannot bring libations** that are brought by themselves as a separate offering and do not accompany an animal offering, but their animal offerings require libations. The Gemara cites the source of these *halakh*ot. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to libations: "All who are **homeborn** shall do these things after this manner" (Numbers 15:13), which teaches that those who are **homeborn,** i.e., Jews, can **bring libations** as a separate offering, **but a gentile cannot bring** such **libations.** One **might** consequently have thought that a gentile's **burnt offering should not require** the standard accompanying **libations.** Therefore, **the verse states: "So** it shall be done for each bull" (Numbers 15:11), which indicates that every offering requires libations.

The *gemara* now turns to the *nesachim* requirement, citing the relevant *parsha* in *Bamidbar* 15 — in *Parshat Shelach*, following the *chet ha-miraglim*. The *Torah* states that when one brings a *korban* — *olah*, *zavach*, *neder*, or *nedavah* — one brings the *nesachim* along with it. The *pasuk* continues: *ve-chi yagur itchem ger* — if a *ger* dwells among you and brings a fire-offering, he does likewise. And then: *Torah achat u-mishpat echad yihyeh lachem ve-lagger ha-gar*. Before that, the *pasuk* states: *kol ha-ezrach ya'aseh kachah et elleh* — the *ezrach* brings like this. The *braita* derives: *ezrach mevi nesachim ve-ein oved kochavim mevi nesachim* — the *ezrach* brings *nesachim*, but a non-Jew does not bring *nesachim* independently.

### Nesachim Attached

One might have inferred from this that even a *korban* brought by a non-Jew is exempt from *nesachim* requirements. The *braita* answers: *talmud lomar kachah* — the word *kachah* instructs that every *korban* on the *mizbe'ach* must come with *nesachim*. Rashi notes that *kachah* is parallel to the usage *kachah ye'aseh la-par ha-echad*, specifying the manner in which a *korban* is brought. The upshot is clear: a non-Jew cannot bring *nesachim* as a standalone offering, but when he brings an animal *korban*, the *korban* itself demands *nesachim* — whether funded by the non-Jew or by public Temple resources.

## Ritzuy Tzitz

### Blood Tamei Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דָּם שֶׁנִּטְמָא וּזְרָקוֹ, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – הוּרְצָה, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּיָחִיד; אֲבָל בְּצִיבּוּר, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – הוּרְצָה. וּבְגוֹיִם, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג וּבֵין בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **blood that became impure and** a priest **sprinkled it** on the altar, if he did so **unwittingly,** the offering is **accepted.** But if he sprinkled the blood **intentionally,** the offering is **not accepted. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **with regard to** the offering of **an individual. But with regard to** the offering of **the community, whether** the priest sprinkled the blood **unwittingly or** he did so **intentionally,** the offering is **accepted. And** in the case of the offerings **of gentiles, whether** he sprinkled the blood **unwittingly or** he did so **intentionally,** the offering is **not accepted.**

The *gemara* introduces a *braita* concerning a different topic with a parallel Jew–non-Jew distinction: the *ritzuy tzitz*, the capacity of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *tzitz* to render acceptable a *korban* whose blood became *tamei*. The *braita* states: *dam she-nitma ve-zarko be-shogeg — hurtzah; be-meizid — lo hurtzah*. If blood that had become *tamei* was sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach* unknowingly, the *korban* is accepted — that is the principle of *ritzuy tzitz*, rooted in the verse that the *tzitz* atones for *tumot Bnei Yisrael*. But if it was done knowingly, the *gemara* explains that while *mi-ikar ha-din* the *tzitz* might technically render the *korban* acceptable, the Sages ruled as a *kenas* that in the case of knowing transgression, the owner may not eat the meat. Importantly, it does not mean another *korban* must be brought — that would raise the issue of *chulin ba-azara* if the first in fact worked — but the practical consequence is that the meat is forbidden *mi-de-rabbanan*.

By contrast, for a communal *korban*, the *tzitz* is *meratze* both *be-shogeg* and *be-meizid* without restriction.

### Non-Jew Ritzuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָאָמַר: בְּכוּלָּן אֲנִי רוֹאֶה לְהַחְמִיר! אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לָהֶם״ – לָהֶם וְלֹא לְגוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said before Rav Pappa: In accordance with whose** opinion was this *baraita* taught? Apparently, it was taught **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as if** it reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** there is a difficulty: **Doesn't** Rabbi Yosei **say: I see** the logic of the opinion that **in all of** these cases it is correct **to be stringent** about the offerings of gentiles? This indicates that Rabbi Yosei equates the *halakhot* applying to the offerings of gentiles with those governing the offerings of Jews. **Rav Pappa said to them: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and it **is different there, as the verse states** with regard to the High Priest's frontplate, which atones for ritual impurity contracted by offerings in the Temple without the knowledge of those offering them: "And it shall be always upon his forehead that it may be accepted **for them** before the Lord" (Exodus 28:38), which indicates that it is accepted **for them,** i.e., for Jews, **but not for gentiles.**

The Rabbis raised this before Rav Pappa: is this ruling consistent only with Rabbi Shimon, who holds that non-Jewish *korbanos* lack full status? For Rabbi Yose, who grants them full status and rules strictly on all counts, the *ritzuy tzitz* should presumably apply. Rav Pappa responded: *afilu teima Rabbi Yose* — even according to Rabbi Yose the *tzitz* does not work here, because a distinct verse limits it: *le-ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — the *tzitz* creates *ratzon* for *them*, meaning *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Rav Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״אֲשֶׁר הֵם מַקְדִּישִׁים״ – הָכִי נָמֵי, הֵם וְלֹא גּוֹיִם?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְרָצוֹן לָהֶם״ – וְגוֹיִם לָאו בְּנֵי הַרְצָאָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: If that is so,** with regard to the verse discussing ritually impure priests and consecrated items: "That they separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel, **which they consecrate to Me"** (Leviticus 22:2), **so too,** would Rabbi Yosei say that the prohibition against eating consecrated items in a state of ritual impurity applies only to offerings which **they,** the Jews, consecrate, **and not** to those of **gentiles?** This cannot be, as Rabbi Yosei explicitly states in the *baraita* that in this regard the offerings of gentiles are like those of Jews. **Rather, Rav Ashi says** that it is not from the words "for them" that one derives that the offering of a gentile is not accepted when the blood that was sprinkled had become impure. Rather, it is because the atonement achieved by way of the High Priest's frontplate does not apply to gentiles, as **the verse states: "That it may be accepted for them** before the Lord" (Exodus 28:38), **and gentiles are not subject to** the **acceptance** of offerings.

Rav Huna son of Rav Nassan challenged Rav Pappa: if we use the word *lahem* to exclude non-Jews, we face a problem. The very *pasuk* that permits non-Jews to bring *korbanos* contains the phrase *asher hem makdishim li* — "that *they* sanctify to Me." If *hem* there does not exclude non-Jews from bringing *korbanos* in the first place, how can *lahem* elsewhere exclude them from *ritzuy tzitz*? One cannot simply invoke a pronoun as an exclusionary force in one context when the same grammatical structure is used inclusively elsewhere.

Rav Ashi provides the resolution: the exclusion of non-Jews from *ritzuy tzitz* does not rest mechanically on the word *lahem* as a pronoun. Rather, *le-ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* expresses a substantive idea: the *ritzuy* achieved by the *tzitz* is grounded in a particular relationship — *lahem* — between *Bnei Yisrael* and God. The *gemara* articulates this as: *ve-ovdei kochavim lav bnei hartzah ninhu*. The concept of *ritzuy* — of the sacrifice being favorably received — is tied to the unique covenantal relationship expressed through the *avodah* of *Bnei Yisrael*. The *tzitz*, as part of the *bigdei kehunah*, and the entire apparatus of *ritzuy* it achieves, is anchored in that relationship.

It should be noted that one need not read this as a sweeping theological statement that God does not accept prayer or sacrifice from non-Jews — after all, *Noach* brings *korbanos* and they are clearly accepted. The more precise reading is that this specific mechanism of *ritzuy* — the formal *ritzuy* achieved by the *kohen gadol*'s *tzitz* as part of the Temple's *avodah* system — operates within the framework of *Bnei Yisrael*'s covenantal service and is not extended to non-Jewish *korbanos*. Nevertheless, the *gemara*'s language — *lav bnei hartzah ninhu* — is broader than this narrow reading strictly requires.

One additional conceptual observation is worth noting in this context. A student raised the question of whether the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yose might be understood as focusing on different axes: Rabbi Shimon focusing on the *gavra* — the person behind the offering — and Rabbi Yose focusing on the *cheftza* — the object, the animal itself. If Rabbi Yose treats the animal as simply an object that has been sanctified and placed on the *mizbe'ach*, then distinctions based on the offerer's identity become less operative. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, may be more attentive to who stands behind the act of *hakdasha* and whether that person's relationship to the *Beit HaMikdash* is such that they can generate the full legal status. This reading resonates with a broader observation about the halachic literature on *korbanos*: the Talmud tends to focus on the technical acts of the *kohanim* rather than on the identity and intent of the offerer, and the dispute here may partly reflect that tension.

## Piggul Mishna

### Piggul Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִים עֲלֵיהֶם מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם נוֹתָר וּמִשּׁוּם טָמֵא; חוּץ מִן הַדָּם. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב בְּדָבָר שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לֶאֱכוֹל, אֲבָל הָעֵצִים וְהַלְּבוֹנָה וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Even with regard to those **items** enumerated in the previous mishna (42b) for **which one is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*,** e.g., the handful, the frankincense, and the incense, **one is,** nevertheless, **liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***notar*, and due to** violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while **ritually impure, except for the blood. Rabbi Shimon deems one liable for an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **eats** it. **But** with regard to **the wood, the frankincense, and the incense, one is not liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition against eating a consecrated item while **ritually impure.**

The *mishna* now addresses a different question: which items are excluded from *piggul*? The *mishna* states: *devarim she-ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, chayavin aleihem mishum nosar mishum tamei*. Things that are themselves *matirim* — such as the *kometz*, *levonah*, and *dam* — are not subject to *piggul* (this was established earlier). But one who eats them in a state of *tumah* or leaves them past their time (*nosar*) does transgress those prohibitions. The one exception is *dam*: *chutz min ha-dam* — blood is excluded from *nosar* and *tamei* as well, for reasons to be worked out in the *gemara*.

### Rabbi Shimon Edible

Rabbi Shimon adds a further qualification: *Rabbi Shimon mechayev be-davar she-darko le-echol* — Rabbi Shimon holds one liable only for things that are customarily eaten. He does not dispute the exclusion of *dam*, but he does dispute whether *tamei* and *nosar* apply to *levonah*, *ketoret*, and wood, which are not eaten. The *mishna* thus records his view: *aval etzim ve-levonah ve-ketoret ein chayavin aleihem mishum tumah* — one who is *tamei* and comes into contact with these non-edible sanctified items, or who retains them past their time, does not thereby transgress. The violations of *tamei* and *nosar* require that the object be of the kind normally consumed.

## Tamei Scope

### Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין – בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ? וְדִין הוּא; וּמָה פִּיגּוּל, שֶׁהוּא בִּקְבִיעָה וּבִידִיעָה אַחַת וְלֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ; טוּמְאָה, שֶׁהִיא בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד וּבִשְׁתֵּי יְדִיעוֹת וְהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, בֵּין לְאָדָם בֵּין לְמִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It **might** have been thought that **one should be liable due to** violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while in a state of **ritual impurity only for an item that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar.** The *baraita* explains: **And** this may be derived via **a logical derivation: Just as** with regard to ***piggul*, which** renders one who unwittingly eats it liable to bring **a fixed** sin offering, **and** this liability is incurred **with one** change in his **awareness,** i.e., it suffices for the sinner to become aware after the fact that he had sinned unwittingly, **and** it has **no permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition, as there are no circumstances in which one is permitted to eat *piggul*, and yet **one is liable due to** violation of the prohibition against eating *piggul* **only for an item that has permitting factors, either for** consumption by **a person or for** burning on **the altar,** so too, the same should certainly apply to the more lenient case of ritual impurity. The Gemara elaborates: **Impurity** is more lenient than *piggul*, **as** it renders the unwitting sinner liable only to bring **a sliding-scale offering,** which varies according to his financial circumstances: A poor person brings a bird offering or even a meal offering (see Leviticus 5:6–13). **And** liability is incurred only **with two** changes in his **awareness,** i.e., when the sinner was aware of his impurity beforehand, then forgot about it at the time of his sin, and then once again become aware of his impurity. **And** it has **permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition with regard to the community, as it is permitted to sacrifice communal offerings in the Temple in a state of impurity. With these leniencies in mind, **is it not right that one should be liable** due to violation of the prohibition against eating consecrated food while ritually impure **only for an item that has permitting factors, either for a person or for the altar.**

The *gemara* now interrogates the scope of *tumah*. The *mishna* has implied that *tumah* applies even to items that are not subject to *piggul* — specifically, to *matirim* such as *kometz* and *levonah*. But the *gemara* asks: why not confine *tumah* to the same scope as *piggul*? A *kal va-chomer* is constructed:

*Piggul* is stringent in several respects: it requires a *korban chatas kevuah* (a fixed-value sin-offering), only one act of awareness is required (*yedia achas*), and it has no exceptions — it is never *hutar mi-kelalo*. Despite all this stringency, *piggul* applies only to something that has a *matir* — both a *matir* for human consumption and a *matir* for the *mizbe'ach* (*bein le-adam bein le-mizbe'ach*). If *tumah* is lighter — it requires a variable-cost offering (*oleh ve-yored*), demands prior and subsequent awareness (*shtei yedios*), and is sometimes suspended in public circumstances (*tumah hutrah be-tzibur*) — then certainly *tumah* should have no broader scope than *piggul*. By a *kal va-chomer*, *tumah* should likewise apply only to items with a *matir*.

### Asher Hem Makdishim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר הֵם מַקְדִּישִׁים לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states,** with regard to eating consecrated foods in a state of ritual impurity: "That they separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel, **which they consecrate to Me,** and that they do not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2). This teaches that a ritually impure person is liable for eating any item that has been consecrated.

The *gemara* answers from a verse in the *parsha* of *tumah*: *vayinazru mi-kodshei Bnei Yisrael asher hem makdishim li* — separate from the sanctified items that they dedicate to Me. The phrase *asher hem makdishim li* is deliberately broad — "all things that they sanctify to Me." This overrides the *kal va-chomer* and expands the scope of *tumah* to include any sanctified item, regardless of whether it has a *matir* in the manner required for *piggul*.

### Yikrav Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל מִיָּד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יִקְרַב״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: וְכִי יֵשׁ נוֹגֵעַ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב?! אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִקְרַב״? בְּהוּכְשַׁר [בָּשָׂר] לִיקָּרֵב הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. הָא כֵּיצַד? יֵשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין – מִשֶּׁיִּקְרָבוֹ מַתִּירִין, אֵין לוֹ מַתִּירִין – מִשֶּׁיִּקְדַּשׁ בִּכְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that one is liable for eating sacred items **immediately** after they have been consecrated. Therefore, **the verse states:** "Whoever he is of all your seed among your generations that **approaches** the sacred items" (Leviticus 22:3), and **Rabbi Elazar said,** in explanation of this verse: **But is there** one who merely **touches,** i.e., approaches, consecrated items, **who is liable?** Only one who eats consecrated food while in a state of ritual impurity is liable. **Rather, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Approaches [*yikrav*]"?** This term alludes to sacrificing [*hakrava*], as though the verse has stated: Whoever sacrifices sacred items and eats them. This teaches that **the verse is speaking of flesh that has been rendered fit to be sacrificed. How so?** With regard to an item **that has permitting factors,** one is liable **from when** the **permitting factors are sacrificed.** In the case of an item that **does not have permitting factors,** one is liable **from when it is sanctified in** a service **vessel** for the purpose of its sacrifice.

One might then think *tumah* applies from the moment of *hakdasha* — from the instant an item is sanctified. The *gemara* corrects this by citing a further verse: *kol ish asher yikrav mi-kol zarachem el ha-kodashim asher yakdishu Bnei Yisrael la-Hashem ve-tumaso alav* — the word *yikrav* indicates that liability attaches only when the item is *fit* to be offered, not from the mere moment of sanctification. Rabbi Elazar clarifies: *yikrav* cannot be read literally as "touching" (there is no known *kares* liability for merely touching a sanctified item while *tamei*). Rather, *be-muchshar basar li-krav ha-katuv medaber* — the verse speaks of when the item is fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach* or consumed by those entitled to eat it.

### Matirim Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן טוּמְאָה; נוֹתָר מְנָלַן? אָתֵי ״חִילּוּל״–״חִילּוּל״ מִטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We have found** proof that the prohibition against eating consecrated food in a state of **ritual impurity** applies even to an item that does not have a permitting factor. **From where do we** derive that ***notar*** likewise applies to an item that does not have a permitting factor? The Gemara answers: This is **derived** through a verbal analogy between **profanation** stated in the context of *notar* and **profanation** stated in the context of **ritual impurity.** With regard to *notar* the verse states: "Because he has profaned the sacred item of the Lord" (Leviticus 19:8), and with regard to impurity the verse states: "And that they do not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2).

For items that have a *matir*, the threshold is straightforward: *mi-she-yikrevu matiraiv*. The *korban*'s meat and entrails become fit for eating or the *mizbe'ach* only once the blood has been sprinkled. Correspondingly, one who is *tamei* and eats the meat is not liable until after the *zrikat ha-dam* has occurred. Before that point, the meat is not yet in the state of *muchshar li-krav*, and there is no *kares* liability for consuming it while *tamei*.

### Kli Sharet Threshold

One can see from the structure of the sources provided that the section continues with the *nosar* derivation. However, the detailed analysis of the *kli sharet* threshold and subsequent discussion of *nosar* scope extends beyond what appears in the extracted Sefaria text for this folio. ## Nosar Scope

### Chilul vs. Avon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵילַף ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ מִפִּיגּוּל! מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִטּוּמְאָה הָוֵי לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן גֶזֶ״ל סִימָן. אַדְּרַבָּה, מִפִּיגּוּל הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף – שֶׁכֵּן הוּתַּר, צִיץ, טָהוֹר, בִּזְמַן, קָרֵב; וְהָנֵי נְפִישָׁן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let** the *halakha* of *notar* **be derived** through a verbal analogy between **"iniquity"** stated in the context of *notar* and **"iniquity"** stated in the context of ***piggul*.** With regard to *piggul*, the verse states: "It shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18), and with regard to leftover sacrificial meat the verse states: "Therefore anyone who eats it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 19:8). If so, the *halakha* of *notar* should be similar to that of *piggul*, for which one is liable only for an item that has a permitting factor. The Gemara answers that **it is** more **reasonable to derive** *notar* **from ritual impurity, for** several reasons, as indicated by the **mnemonic: *Gimmel*, *zayin*, *lamed*.** Both *notar* and impurity are disqualifications that apply to the body [*guf* ] of the offering itself, whereas *piggul* is caused by intent; unlike *piggul*, these two disqualifications are not determined by the sprinkling [*zerika*] of the blood, and in both cases the Torah uses the term profanation [*ḥillul* ]. The Gemara responds: **On the contrary, it is** more **reasonable to derive** *notar* **from *piggul*, as** like *piggul* it does not have **permitted** exceptions from its general prohibition, it has no atonement through the High Priest's **frontplate,** both *notar* and *piggul* apply to **a ritually pure** offering, these disqualifications are dependent **on time,** and both of them are disqualifications of the item being **sacrificed,** not the priest performing the service. None of these features are true of ritual impurity. **And these** reasons for comparing *notar* to *piggul* are more **numerous.**

The *gemara* now turns to *nosar* and asks whether it follows the broad scope of *tumah* or the narrow scope of *piggul*. The chain of derivation that excluded *piggul* and *nosar* from non-Jewish *korbanos* (discussed earlier) also implied that *nosar* and *piggul* are related — *nosar* was linked to *piggul* via the *gezeira shava* of *avon-avon*. Now the *gemara* raises the competing possibility: *nosar* appears in its *parsha* with the word *chilul*, and *tumah* appears in its *parsha* with the word *chilul*. Should we therefore link *nosar* to *tumah* via *chilul-chilul*, giving it the broad scope? Or should we link *nosar* to *piggul* via *avon-avon*, giving it the narrow scope?

This is a genuine *gezeira shava* conflict: *nosar* shares *chilul* with *tumah* and *avon* with *piggul*. How do we resolve which linkage governs? The *gemara* raises the possibility that there are more points of similarity between *nosar* and *piggul* than between *nosar* and *tumah*, which would argue for linking *nosar* to *piggul*. The common features of *nosar* and *piggul* include: neither is *hutar be-tzibur*; the *tzitz* works for *tumah* but not for either *nosar* or *piggul*; both *nosar* and *piggul* are time-related (one conceptually, one factually); and both apply to the *object* consumed, whereas *tumah* is a status of the *person*. On this count, the similarities tilt toward *piggul*. If so, *nosar* should have *piggul*'s narrow scope and not apply to *matirim*.

### Double Lamed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִדְּתָנֵי לֵוִי. דְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאַף בִּפְסוּל זְמַן הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – בִּשְׁנֵי חִילּוּלִין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר; אֶחָד פְּסוּל נוֹתָר, וְאֶחָד פְּסוּל טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* of *notar* is derived **from that** which **Levi taught** with regard to the verse: "That they separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel, which they consecrate to Me, and that they do not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2), which is referring to the eating of consecrated food in a state of ritual impurity. **As Levi taught: From where** is it derived **that the verse is speaking even of a disqualification** caused by **time,** and not only ritual impurity? **The verse states** profanation elsewhere: **"And they shall not profane the sacred items of the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 22:15). **The verse is speaking** here **of two profanations; one** is the **disqualification of *notar* and one** is the **disqualification of ritual impurity.** This teaches that like impurity, *notar* applies even to an item that does not have a permitting factor.

The *gemara* resolves the tension by appealing not to the *gezeira shava* comparison but to an internal reading of the *pasuk* governing *tumah* itself. Levi teaches: *minayin she-af be-psul zman ha-katuv medaber? Talmud lomar ve-lo yechalelu* — the doubled *lamed* in *ve-lo yechalelu* signals two forms of *chilul*. The *pasuk* does not merely govern *tumah*; it is formulated to encompass another category of *chilul* as well. That second *chilul* is *nosar* — *psul zman*, the disqualification through time. Accordingly, *nosar* is included within the same *pasuk* that establishes *tumah*'s broad scope, and derives its scope from there directly — not via the *chilul-chilul gezeira shava* from *tumah*, which would face the competing *avon-avon* derivation from *piggul*, but via the doubled *lamed* in the *pasuk* itself, which independently includes *psul zman*.

### Nosar Conclusion

The upshot is that *nosar* does not derive its scope by comparison to *piggul* or by a contested *gezeira shava*. Instead, the *pasuk* that establishes *tumah*'s broad scope — *velo yechalelu* — simultaneously establishes through its doubled *lamed* that *nosar* shares that same broad scope. Both *nosar* and *tumah* therefore apply to a wider range of items than *piggul*, including those without a *matir* in the *piggul* sense, subject to the *yikrav* threshold: the item must be fit for offering, whether through the completion of its *matirim* or through placement in a *kli sharet*.
