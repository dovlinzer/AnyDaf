# Hullin 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mayim Stories

### Abaye's Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא, הַאי דְּשָׁדֵי מַיָּא מִפּוּמָּא דְּחַצְבָּא – מִשּׁוּם צִיבְתָּא. אֲמַר לִי מָר: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מַיִם הָרָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said: At first I would say** that **this** practice **that** people **pour out** a little **water from the mouth of a pitcher** before drinking from it is followed **because of twigs** it might contain. But **the Master said to me** that it is followed **because there are foul waters** in the pitcher.

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Abaye: *meireisha hava amina*, I used to assume that a certain widespread practice was motivated by purely practical concerns. The practice in question is *hai dishadi maya mipuma dechatzba* — the custom of pouring off the top layer of water from a barrel or jar before drinking. Abaye had assumed this was done *mishum tzip tza*, because of small impurities — splinters of wood and the like — that would float to the surface and needed to be removed. Rava, however, corrected him: no, it is done *mishum zika demaya hara'im*, because of "bad water" — evil spirits were believed to drink from the top layer, leaving a harmful spiritual residue there, which is why one pours it off before use. This is a striking juxtaposition: the same observable act carries either a prosaic hygienic explanation or a spiritual one, and Rava insists the latter is the real reason.

### Bar Shida Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בַּר שֵׁידָא דַּהֲוָה בֵּי רַב פָּפָּא, אֲזַל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַיָּא מִנַּהֲרָא, אִיעַכַּב. כִּי אֲתָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי אִיעַכַּבְתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: עַד דְּחָלְפִי מַיִם הָרָעִים. אַדְּהָכִי חֲזָנְהוּ דְּקָא שָׁדוּ מַיָּא מִפּוּמָּא דְּחַצְבָּא, אָמַר: אִי הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא דִּרְגִילִיתוּ לְמִיעְבַּד הָכִי, לָא אִיעַכַּבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain son of a demon that was in Rav Pappa's house** as a servant. It **went to bring water from the river,** and **it delayed** in returning. **When it came,** the members of Rav Pappa's household **said to it: Why did you delay? It said to them:** I waited **until the foul waters passed. In the meantime,** the demon **saw** the members of Rav Pappa's household **pouring water from the mouth of the pitcher** before drinking from it. The demon **said** to them: **If I had known that you regularly do this, I would not have delayed.** I would have brought the water straight from the river, knowing you would pour out the foul waters.

A brief but vivid narrative follows. There was a certain *bar shida* — a minor shade or spirit — who was employed in Rav Pappa's household, apparently due to a shortage of human workers. Rav Pappa sent him to fetch water from the river, *azal le'atuyei maya minhara*, and the spirit took an unusually long time. When he returned, the members of the household asked him why he had delayed. He replied: *ad dechalfei maya hara'im* — I had to wait for the bad water to pass, so that I could bring you clean water.

Upon his return, however, he observed the household members doing *dekishadu maya mipuma dechatzba* — pouring off the top layer from the pitchers themselves. He then said: had I known that this was your regular practice, I would not have delayed. I would have brought the water immediately, knowing you would handle the purification yourselves at home. *Tosafot* notes the natural question of why the spirit had not simply told them about this practice: the answer given is that he did not want to alert other spirits and provoke their hostility.

### Rav Dimi Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: מַיִם הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הֶאֱכִילוּ בְּשַׂר חֲזִיר, אַחֲרוֹנִים הוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה מִבַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said:** Due to the failure to wash with **the first waters,** they ultimately **fed** a Jew **pig meat.** This case involved a storekeeper who would sell different meat to his Jewish and gentile customers. When a Jew who came to eat with him neglected to wash before eating, the storekeeper assumed he was a gentile and fed him pig meat. And due to the failure to wash with **final** waters **a woman** was ultimately **divorced from her husband.** In this incident, a host who had stolen his guests' money had lentils on his mustache from a previous meal because he had not washed his hands and mouth after eating. Realizing he had eaten lentils that day, his victims approached the man's wife and said that her husband had instructed them to tell her to return their money. They then claimed that the man told them to tell her that he had eaten lentils that day as proof that they were telling the truth. They thereby fooled his wife into thinking he wanted her to give their money back. Out of anger, the host divorced his wife.

The *gemara* now transitions away from the world of *shedim* and toward a series of cautionary reports about the practical consequences of neglecting *mayim rishonim* and *mayim acharonim*. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported two cases: *mayim rishonim he'echilu basar chazir* — failure to wash before the meal caused someone to be fed pork; and *mayim acharonim hotziu et ha'ishah miba'alah* — failure to wash after the meal caused a woman to be needlessly divorced.

### Ravin's Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר: רִאשׁוֹנִים הֶאֱכִילוּ בְּשַׂר נְבֵלָה, אַחֲרוֹנִים הָרְגוּ אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: וְסִימָנָיךְ – אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אַפְּקַהּ, אֲתָא רָבִין קַטְלַהּ. רַבִּי אַבָּא מַתְנֵי חֲדָא מֵהָנֵי, וַחֲדָא מֵהָנֵי, לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said** the statement slightly differently: Due to the failure to wash with **first** waters, they **fed** a Jew meat from **an animal carcass,** and the failure to wash with **final** waters **killed a person,** as in the second incident the host was so angry with his wife that he killed her. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: And your mnemonic** to remember which Sage said which version is: **Rav Dimi came** and **divorced her** from her husband, i.e., according to his version she was divorced, and **Ravin came** and **killed her,** since in his version the husband killed his wife. **Rabbi Abba would teach one of these** versions involving first waters **and one of them** with regard to final waters, and in both cases he taught **the more severe** version, i.e., he specified the meat of a pig and that the husband killed his wife.

When Ravin arrived from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported versions of both cases that were worse: *rishonim he'echilu basar neveilah* — the first case involved *neveilah* rather than *chazir*; and *acharonim hergu et hanefesh* — the second case resulted not in divorce but in death.

The *gemara* provides a mnemonic for keeping the versions straight: Rav Dimi came first, so in his version the woman was divorced — she could still be divorced at that point. Ravin came later, so in his version she was killed — one cannot divorce a woman who is already dead. Rabbi Abba, for his part, combined the worst elements of both accounts: *chazir* in the first case and death in the second.

The question naturally arises: why is *chazir* worse than *neveilah*? Both carry a *lav*. *Rashi* notes that *chazir* involves two prohibitions, though he does not specify which two. One possibility is that unkosher meat, including *chazir*, would typically also carry the prohibition of *dam*, since there would be no reason to salt non-*shechted* meat — though one could construct a scenario involving *neveilah* from an animal that had been salted before the *shechita* was discovered to be invalid, in which case *dam* would not apply to the *neveilah*. Beyond the technical analysis, however, it is simply true historically that *chazir* has always carried an outsized visceral weight relative to other prohibitions: archaeologists excavating ancient sites in *Eretz Yisrael* can identify a Jewish settlement by the absence of pig bones alone, even when no other marker of Jewish identity survives.

### Stories Analysis

The question of what exactly happened in each case requires some reconstruction. *Rashi* fills in the narrative: someone arrived at an inn and ordered meat but did not wash *mayim rishonim* before eating. The innkeeper — a Jewish innkeeper who served both kosher and non-kosher food to Jewish and non-Jewish customers respectively — concluded from the absence of *mayim rishonim* that the guest was not Jewish and therefore served him *treif* meat. This is the mechanism behind the first case.

The *mayim acharonim* story is traced to a fuller narrative found in *Yoma*, involving Rabbi Meir and the innkeeper named *Kidor*. Rabbi Meir, who was attentive to the meaning of names, did not trust this man — deriving from the verse *ki dor tahapuchot heima* that the name *Kidor* bespoke an untrustworthy character — and so did not entrust his wallet to him. The other Sages were less cautious. When Kidor denied having taken their wallets, they noticed lentils caught in his beard, indicating what he had eaten for breakfast. They went to his wife, gave the detail of the lentils as a *siman*, and retrieved the wallets. When the husband learned of this, he either divorced or killed his wife. The connection to *mayim acharonim* is that had he washed properly after the meal — which in the time of the *gemara* constituted a thorough washing that would have included cleaning the beard — there would have been no lentils caught there, and the *siman* would not have worked.

This observation has an important implication: *mayim acharonim* in the *gemara*'s era was not the highly ritualized, minimal act familiar today — a small silver vessel, a few drops of water, performed almost as a formality. It was a genuine washing-up after the meal, and apparently included the face and beard. The reason, as the *gemara* has made clear, is *melach Sedomit* — the concern about Sodomite salt, which could blind if left on the fingers and brought to the eyes. That concern no longer obtains in the same practical sense today, which explains why the ritual has contracted into something more ceremonial.

Both stories together speak to something beyond their immediate subject: they raise questions of *identity*. In the first case, the person was identified — or misidentified — as non-Jewish precisely because he did not wash *mayim rishonim*. In the second, the entire catastrophe hinged on a question of identity and whether one could be trusted. It is striking that the absence of *mayim rishonim* was sufficient to mark someone as non-Jewish — this implies that there was *no* other visible marker of Jewish identity that would have given the innkeeper a different impression. The person was not wearing a distinctive Jewish garment; he carried no other immediately visible sign. From this, several late *rishonim* and early *acharonim* derived that there was no binding requirement for Jews to dress in a manner that distinguished them from their non-Jewish surroundings — since if there had been, the question of *mayim rishonim* would have been superfluous as an identifier.

The *psukim* the *gemara* elsewhere associates with these practices — *vehiskadishtem viheyitem kedoshim*, "you shall sanctify yourselves and be holy" — assign *vehiskadishtem* to *mayim rishonim* and *viheyitem kedoshim* to *mayim acharonim*. This framing suggests that beyond whatever specific physical reasons motivated these practices, there is also a dimension of identity and sanctity: the way one conducts oneself at the table is a statement about who one is and what kind of life one aspires to.

## Halachic Basis

### Heated Water Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, חַמֵּי הָאוּר: חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר אֵין נוֹטְלִים מֵהֶן לַיָּדַיִם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר נוֹטְלִין מֵהֶם לַיָּדַיִם. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁאַלְתִּי אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי, וְאוֹכֵל טְהָרוֹת, וְאָמַר לִי כׇּל גְּדוֹלֵי גָּלִיל עוֹשִׂין כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** A disagreement **was stated** with regard to **water heated by fire: Ḥizkiyya says** that **one may not wash** his **hands with** such water, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **one may wash** his **hands with it. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: I asked Rabban Gamliel, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, about this *halakha*, **and** he was one who would **eat** only in a state of **ritual purity** and was therefore careful about washing his hands; **and he said to me** that **all the great men of the Galilee would do so,** i.e., wash their hands in heated water.

The *gemara* now turns to the technical question of which types of water may be used for *mayim rishonim*. The case under discussion is *chamei ha'ur* — water that has been heated by fire. Chizkiyah says: *eino notlin leyadayim* — one may not use it for *netilat yadayim*. Rabbi Yochanan says: *notlin leyadayim* — one may use it.

Rabbi Yochanan then relates: *sha'alti et Rabban Gamliel bno shel Rabbi* — I asked Rabban Gamliel, the son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who was known as someone who ate his *chullin betahara*, and he told me that *kol gedolei Galil osim ken* — all the great personages of the Galilee do this; they use heated water for *mayim rishonim*.

The significance of the reference to *ochlei taharos* — those who ate their non-sanctified food with purity — will be unpacked shortly. But it is worth pausing here to ask why the *gemara* does not specify the reason hot water is problematic. *Rashi* gestures toward an explanation rooted in the distinction between water in its natural state and water that has been altered. The previous *gemara* had already established that one cannot use water that is *yad soledet bo* — hot enough to scald — for *mayim rishonim*, with two opinions as to whether the prohibition applies to any hot water or only water at that threshold of heat. The present *gemara* seems to address a question about *how* the water was heated rather than *how hot* it is, though the exact contours remain somewhat unclear.

### Hot Springs Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַמֵּי טְבֶרְיָא, חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: אֵין נוֹטְלִין מֵהֶם לַיָּדַיִם, אֲבָל מַטְבִּילִין בָּהֶם הַיָּדַיִם. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כׇּל גּוּפוֹ טוֹבֵל בָּהֶן, אֲבָל לֹא פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו. הַשְׁתָּא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ טוֹבֵל בָּהֶם, פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בִּמְקוֹמָן – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי, מִשְׁקַל מִינַּיְיהוּ בְּמָנָא – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דְּפַסְקִינְהוּ בְּבַת בִּירְתָּא: מָר סָבַר גָּזְרִינַן בַּת בִּירְתָּא אַטּוּ מָנָא, וּמָר סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, with regard to **the hot springs of Tiberias, Ḥizkiyya says** that **one may not wash** his **hands with** water from them before eating, **but** if there are forty *se'a*, the requisite size of a ritual bath, then **one may immerse the hands** directly **in them,** and this is effective for the ritual of washing the hands before a meal. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that an impure person **may immerse his entire body in** such water to become pure, **but** one may still **not** use it for the immersion of part of his body, such as **his face, hands, and feet,** as this immersion is not considered equivalent to washing the hands. The Gemara asks: **Now** that it has been said that one **may immerse his entire body in** the hot springs of Tiberias, is it **not all the more so** permitted for **his face, hands, and feet? Rav Pappa said:** When the water in the hot springs stands **in place, everyone,** both Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan, **agrees that it is permitted** to immerse one's hands in it. Likewise, **everyone agrees that** to **take from** these waters **in a vessel** and wash one's hands from it **is prohibited. They disagree when one draws** the waters **through a ditch.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **we decree** against the use of **ditch** water **due to** the concern that one might come to use water in **a vessel, and** one **Sage,** Ḥizkiyya, **holds** that **we do not decree** against it.

The *gemara* then turns to *chamei Tveriah* — the natural hot springs of Tiberias, water that is hot not because of fire but by virtue of the geological heat of the earth. Here the analysis becomes more nuanced.

Chizkiyah maintains: *eino notlin leyadayim* — one cannot use this water for *netilat yadayim*. Yet he concedes that *matbilin bahen et yadayim* — one may immerse one's hands in it and fulfill *tevilat yadayim*. This distinction is significant. *Tevilat yadayim* is the alternative to *netilat yadayim* available when one finds oneself without a convenient place to pour water — near a river, a pond, or some other body of water — where one may dip one's hands in lieu of pouring. It requires that the water be in its natural location, analogous to a *mikvah*, and contain at least *arbaim se'ah*. Since the hot springs of Tiberias are in their natural state — the heat is intrinsic to them, not an external alteration — they function as a valid *mikvah* for this purpose.

Rabbi Yochanan takes a different and at first seemingly contradictory position: *kol gufo tovel bahen* — you may immerse your whole body in them — *aval lo panav yadav veraglav* — but you may not do just the hands. *Tosafot* notes that the mention of face and feet alongside hands is *lav davka*; in practice, when the *gemara* discusses partial body washing, it conventionally lists hands, face, and feet as the standard units of washing for cleanliness, but the ritual concern here is specifically about the hands. So Rabbi Yochanan is saying: the springs are a valid *mikvah* for full immersion, but *tevilat yadayim* specifically — immersing only the hands — is not permitted.

The *gemara* immediately raises the obvious objection: *hashtah kol gufo tovel bahen panav yadav veraglav lo kol sheken* — if one can use these waters for full-body immersion, how much more so should one be able to dip just the hands? Rav Pappa resolves this by reframing the entire dispute. When the water is *bimkoman* — in its original location, still flowing from the spring — *dechulei alma lo pligi desharei*: all opinions agree it is permitted for *tevilat yadayim*. When water has been removed with a vessel — *mishku mineihu bimana* — all agree it is forbidden. What, then, is the dispute? *Depaskinehu be'vatz biertza* — a small channel or eddy that has been cut off from the main flow, a little sidepool fed by the springs but no longer fully part of the main body of water. One opinion holds that we apply a safeguard — *gazrinan vatz biertza atu mana* — and treat this sidepool as if it were water removed in a vessel, lest people confuse the two situations. The other holds that no such safeguard is imposed — *lo gazrinan*.

*Rashi* makes an interesting observation about the logic of Rabbi Yochanan's position as it relates to heated water: Rabbi Yochanan, who was lenient about water heated by fire for *netilat yadayim*, is apparently strict about naturally hot water used in a vessel for *tevilat yadayim*. *Rashi* suggests this is because water heated by fire began its existence as normal water and its prior identity as ordinary water continues to obtain. Water that has been hot from its very origin — like the springs of Tiberias — is in a different category: it has never had *sha'at hachosher*, a moment of being ordinary water, and so lacks that prior identity.

### Purpose Serach Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְחוּלִּין, מִפְּנֵי סֶרֶךְ תְּרוּמָה. וְעוֹד מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה. מַאי מִצְוָה? אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִצְוָה לִשְׁמוֹעַ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Washing hands** before eating **non-sacred** food is **due to an ancillary** decree on account of ***teruma*,** the portion of produce designated for the priest, which must be consumed in a state of ritual purity. By rabbinic decree, one's hands are considered impure with second-degree ritual impurity, as they may have touched impure items. Therefore, they render *teruma* impure. Consequently, priests who partake of *teruma* are obligated to wash their hands first. The Sages therefore decreed that all must wash their hands even before eating non-sacred food, so that people not become accustomed to eating without washing their hands, which would in turn lead the priests to partake of *teruma* without washing their hands. **And** the obligation is **further due to** its being **a mitzva.** The Gemara asks: **What mitzva** does it involve? **Abaye says:** It is **a mitzva to listen to** and obey **the statements of the Sages,** who instituted this washing of the hands.

The *gemara* then provides the key statement about the purpose of *mayim rishonim* for *chulin*: *netilat yadayim l'chulin mishum serach terumah* — we wash our hands before non-sanctified bread because of a *serach*, a vestige or semblance, of the concern for *terumah*. The background: eating *terumah* required *kohanim* to have ritually pure hands. Unlike full-body *tumah*, which required full immersion in a *mikvah*, the rabbinically decreed *tumah* that applies specifically to hands could be remedied by *netilat yadayim* alone. Over time, those who ate their *chulin betahara* — who maintained purity standards even for non-sanctified food — adopted the same practice. The ritual washing before bread that we observe today is a carry-through from that world.

### Mitzvah to Listen

The *gemara* adds: *ve'od mishum mitzvah* — and also because it is a *mitzvah*. The immediate question is: what *mitzvah*? Abaye answers: *mitzvah lishmo'a divrei chachamim* — it is a *mitzvah* to heed the words of the Sages.

### Cleanliness Holiness

*Tosafot* probes this further: if the whole institution goes back to the concern of *terumah*, what does the *ve'od* add? *Tosafot* proposes that the additional dimension is *mishum nekiyut* — cleanliness. The rabbis also simply wanted people to have clean hands before sitting down to eat. This is a striking observation. *Mayim acharonim*, as noted, has an explicit cleanliness rationale in the concern about *melach Sedomit*. If *Tosafot* is correct, *mayim rishonim* shares this dimension as well.

This connects to the *pasuk* the *gemara* elsewhere cites for both practices: *vehiskadishtem viheyitem kedoshim* — sanctify yourselves and be holy. According to that *gemara*, *vehiskadishtem* refers to *mayim rishonim* and *viheyitem kedoshim* to *mayim acharonim*. There is a sense in which both practices are about maintaining a kind of holiness at the table — treating oneself with the dignity appropriate to someone who eats in the awareness that food comes from God and that the act of eating carries religious weight.

This connects to a broader theme: the table as *mizbeiach*, the act of eating as sustained within a framework of purity and sanctity. It is no accident that the same *gemara* that discusses *netilat yadayim* invokes the concept that deriving benefit from this world without a *bracha* is like *me'ilah* — misappropriating property of *hekdesh*. The deliberate choice of the *Beis HaMikdash* term *me'ilah* rather than simply *gezel* from God is telling: the framework is one in which eating takes place within the conceptual world of sanctified property, and *netilat yadayim* is one of the practices that anchors the ordinary meal within that world. The *ochlei chullin betahara* were those who took this aspiration most seriously and lived it most completely; the retention of *netilat yadayim* in the absence of the *Beis HaMikdash* is a way of sustaining at least a trace of that orientation — accessible to everyone, not requiring the full rigor of *chulin betahara* — and affirming that something of the world of *tumah vetahara* persists even now.

## Rabbi Eliezer Derivation

### Zav Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִצְוָה לִשְׁמוֹעַ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: "וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בּוֹ הַזָּב וְיָדָיו לֹא שָׁטַף בַּמָּיִם". אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ: מִכָּאן סָמְכוּ חֲכָמִים לִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** It is **a mitzva to listen to the statement of Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh, as it is written** with regard to a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]: **"And whomever he that has the issue touches, without having rinsed his hands in water,"** he contracts ritual impurity (Leviticus 15:11), and **Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh says: From here the Sages based washing of the hands** upon a verse **from the Torah.**

Rava adds his own formulation: *mitzvah lishmo'a divrei Rabbi Eliezer ben Arach* — it is a *mitzvah* specifically to heed the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer ben Arach. What teaching? The verse states: *v'chol asher yiga bo hazav, v'yadav lo shataf bamayim* — and whatever the *zav* touches, and his hands have not been washed with water, becomes *tamei*. At first glance, this is puzzling: a *zav* is *tamei* in his entirety and must immerse his whole body in a *mikvah* to become *tahor*. Why does the verse single out his hands and the rinsing of water, as if rinsing alone would suffice? The answer normally given is that this is a synecdoche — the verse mentions hands as a part representing the whole, meaning he has not immersed.

### Literal Reading

Rabbi Eliezer ben Arach, however, takes the verse more literally and derives from it: *mikan samchu chachamim l'netilat yadayim min hatorah* — here is the Scriptural basis, the *asmachta*, for the rabbinic institution of washing hands.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מַאי מַשְׁמַע? דִּכְתִיב "וְיָדָיו לֹא שָׁטַף בְּמַיִם", הָא שָׁטַף – טָהוֹר? הָא טְבִילָה בָּעֵי! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאַחֵר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁטַף – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: From where** is this **inferred?** How can this verse, which concerns a *zav*, be interpreted as referring to washing the hands before a meal? Rava explains: **As it is written: "Without having rinsed his hands in water." Consequently,** one could infer that if he **rinsed** his hands the *zav* becomes **ritually pure. But** this cannot be correct, as verses elsewhere prove that a *zav* **requires** the **immersion** of his entire body. **Rather, this** is what the verse **is saying: And** there is **another** type of person **who,** if he **has not rinsed** his hands in water, is considered like one who is **impure.** The verse thereby serves as the basis for washing the hands.

Rava presses Rav Nachman on the precise reading: *mai mashma* — how do you derive it? *D'ksiv yadav lo shataf bamayim* — the verse writes that his hands were not washed with water. Implication: *hashataf*, once you rinse them, *tahor* — he becomes *tahor*. But that cannot be correct — *ha'tvilah bei*, a *zav* is required to immerse his whole body. Rinsing the hands alone would not make him *tahor*.

### Unwashed Hands Tumah

The resolution is a different reading of the verse. *Ela hachi kamar* — this is what the verse actually means: *v'chol asher yiga bo hazav* does not refer to the *zav* himself but to a person whom the *zav* has touched. Such a person becomes *tamei* at one degree of remove — a *rishon l'tumah*. And *v'yadav lo shataf bamayim* — a different person, one whose hands have simply not been washed — is also *tamei* and conveys *tumah* to *terumah*. Both such individuals — the person touched by a *zav* and the ordinary person with unwashed hands — produce the same result when they contact food. From here, the rabbis derived that unwashed hands transmit *tumah*, lending Scriptural support to the institution of *netilat yadayim*. The support is an *asmachta*, a reading into the verse, but it is the *peg* on which the institution is hung.

## Bread vs Fruit

### No Fruit Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לֹא אָמְרוּ נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְפֵירוֹת אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם נְקִיּוּת. סְבוּר מִינָּה: חוֹבָה הוּא דְּלֵיכָּא, הָא מִצְוָה אִיכָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא: לֹא חוֹבָה וְלֹא מִצְוָה, אֶלָּא רְשׁוּת. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַנּוֹטֵל יָדָיו לְפֵירוֹת אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִגַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says:** The Sages **said** that **washing of the hands** before eating **fruit** is mandatory **only due to cleanliness.** The Gemara comments: **They understood from this** statement that **there is no** true **obligation** to wash the hands before eating fruit, **but there is a mitzva** to do so. **Rava said to them:** This practice is **not an obligation nor a mitzva, but** merely **optional. And** the Gemara notes that Rava **disagrees with Rav Naḥman** in this regard, **as Rav Naḥman said: One who washes his hands** before eating **fruit is nothing other than** one **of the arrogant,** i.e., it is actually prohibited to do so.

Rabbi Eleazar citing Rabbi Yehoshua teaches: *lo amru netilat yadayim l'peirot* — they did not require *netilat yadayim* before fruit. *Ela mishum nekiyut* — the only reason to wash one's hands before eating fruit is if one wishes to be clean; there is no rabbinic ritual obligation. The reason is structural: *terumah* was drawn primarily from grain, and the purity obligation pertained principally to bread. The *serach terumah* rationale does not extend to fruit.

The students initially understood Rabbi Yehoshua to mean that while there is no *chovah* — no strict obligation — there remains a *mitzvah*, a religious value, in washing before fruit. Rava corrects this: *lo chovah v'lo mitzvah ela reshut* — there is neither obligation nor *mitzvah*; it is entirely optional. If one wishes to be clean, one may wash, but there is no religious dimension to the act.

### Rava Arrogance

Rav Nachman goes further still. *Hanoteil yadav l'peirot einu ela migasei haruach* — one who washes before eating fruit is displaying arrogance: "I am so refined that I must wash even before fruit." Washing before fruit is not merely neutral — it signals a kind of haughtiness that is inappropriate. The progression is notable: from a possible *mitzvah* on the grounds of cleanliness, to a merely optional act, to something verging on spiritual vanity.

## Bread vs Fruit…

### Rabba Bar Chana Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ כַּלְכַּלָּה דְּפֵירֵי, וַאֲכַלוּ וְלָא מָשׁוּ יְדַיְיהוּ, וְלָא יְהַבוּ לִי מִידֵּי, וּבָרֵיךְ חַד חַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְפֵירוֹת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין מְזַמְּנִין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁאָכְלוּ – מִצְוָה לֵיחָלֵק. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁאָכְלוּ – מִצְוָה לֵיחָלֵק. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם סוֹפְרִים, אֲבָל אֶחָד סוֹפֵר וְאֶחָד בּוּר – סוֹפֵר מְבָרֵךְ וּבוּר יוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: I was standing before Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** when attendants **brought a basket of fruit before them, and they ate and did not wash their hands. And they did not give me anything** to eat, to enable me to join the *zimmun*, the quorum required for communal Grace after Meals, **and they each recited a blessing** after eating, **separately.** One may **learn three** *halakhot* **from** this incident. **Learn from it** that **there is no washing of the hands** before **fruit. And learn from it** that **one does not issue a *zimmun* on fruit,** i.e., the *halakha* that when three people eat together, one leads the Grace after Meals does not apply when they ate fruit. **And** finally, **learn from it** that if only **two** people **ate,** it is **a mitzva** for them **to separate,** i.e., each should recite the blessing after eating for himself. The Gemara notes: **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: If only **two** individuals **ate,** it is **a mitzva** for them **to separate. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **when they were both scribes,** i.e., Torah scholars, who know how to recite Grace after Meals properly. **But** if **one** of them was **a scribe and one** was **an ignoramus,** the **scribe recites Grace** after Meals **and** the **ignoramus fulfills** his obligation by listening to the scribe.

Rabba bar bar Chana reports: I was in the presence of Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi. They had a basket of fruit brought before them, *achlu v'lo mishu yadayhu* — they ate without washing their hands. Furthermore, *lo yahavu li midi* — they did not offer him any fruit. And *varich chad chad l'chudei* — each of them recited *birkat acharona* separately rather than together.

The *gemara* derives three conclusions: first, *ein ta'un netilat yadayim l'peirot* — one does not require *netilat yadayim* before fruit. Second, *ein mezamnin al hapeirot* — one does not do *zimun* after eating fruit, since if *zimun* applied, they would have offered him some so as to have a quorum of three. Third, *shnayim she'achlu mitzvah l'chaleik* — when two people eat, it is preferable for each to bench separately rather than together. The last inference is derived from the fact that Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi, even as two people, chose not to bench jointly. *Tosafot* raises a difficulty with this inference: the rule about two people benching separately is a rule about bread, where *zimun* applies at three; but fruit does not generate a *zimun* obligation even for three, so of course two would not bench together on fruit. The *gemara* is making a jump from fruit to bread, and *Tosafot* goes through considerable difficulty trying to justify it. The question is in many ways more compelling than the answer.

A supporting *baraita* confirms: *shnayim she'achlu mitzvah l'chaleik* — when two people eat, the proper practice is to bench separately. *Bamei devarim amurim* — when does this apply? When both are *sofrim* — when both are literate and capable of benching for themselves. But if one is literate and the other is *bur* — unlettered and unable to bench independently — then the one who knows benches aloud and the other fulfills his obligation thereby: *sofer mevarech v'bur yotzei*. The *zimun* obligation creates a communal structure; absent that structure, individuality in expression is preferred, unless dependence makes joint recitation necessary.

## How Much to Wash

### Baraita Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְחוּלִּין – עַד הַפֶּרֶק, לִתְרוּמָה – עַד הַפֶּרֶק. קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ – עַד הַפֶּרֶק, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁחוֹצֵץ בִּטְבִילָה בַּגּוּף – חוֹצֵץ בִּנְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְחוּלִּין, וּבְקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In **washing of the hands for** consumption of **non-sacred** food, one must pour the water on the area that extends **until the joint** of the fingers. In washing hands **for** consumption of ***teruma*,** he must pour on the area extending **until the joint.** In **sanctifying the hands and feet in the Temple** before the service, he must pour the water **until** another **joint,** where the palm meets the wrist. **And any item that** is considered to **interpose** between one's skin and the water **with regard to immersion of the body** in a ritual bath, disqualifying the immersion, likewise **interposes with regard to washing the hands for** eating **non-sacred** food **and with regard to sanctification of the hands and feet in the Temple.**

A *baraita* lays out a tiered structure: *netilat yadayim lechulin* requires washing *ad haperek* — up to one joint; for *terumah*, up to a higher joint; and *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim bamikdash* — the washing of hands and feet by the *kohanim* in the *Beis HaMikdash* — extends to yet a higher joint still. *Rashi* specifies: for *chulin*, up to the middle knuckle; for *terumah*, up to the joint where the fingers meet the palm; for the *Mikdash*, up to the wrist.

### Rav Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: עַד כָּאן לְחוּלִּין, עַד כָּאן לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said** this *halakha* to his students while indicating with his hand the joints to which the *baraita* is referring: One washes **until here,** the second joint of the fingers, **for non-sacred** food, and **until there,** the third joint, where the fingers join the palm, **for *teruma*.**

Rav rules: *ad kan l'chulin, ad kan l'trumah* — here for *chulin*, here for *terumah* — consistent with *Rashi*'s reading of distinct requirements for each.

### Shmuel Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד כָּאן בֵּין לְחוּלִּין בֵּין לִתְרוּמָה, לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** disagreed and **said:** One washes **until here both for** eating **non-sacred** food **and for *teruma*,** indicating **the more stringent** location, the third joint where the fingers join the palm.

Shmuel rules: *ad kan bein l'chulin bein l'trumah l'chumra* — the same level for both *chulin* and *terumah*, but on the strict side. According to *Rashi*, this means the joint where the fingers meet the palm for both.

### Rav Sheshes Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: עַד כָּאן בֵּין לְחוּלִּין בֵּין לִתְרוּמָה, לְקוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet said: Until here both for** the consumption of **non-sacred** food **and for *teruma*,** indicating **the more lenient** location, the second joint.

Rav Sheshes rules: *ad kan bein l'chulin bein l'trumah l'kula* — the same level for both, but on the lenient side, meaning the middle knuckle applies even for *terumah*.

### Wrist Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר בַּר הֶדְיָא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, וְאָמַר: עַד כָּאן בֵּין לְחוּלִּין בֵּין לִתְרוּמָה לְחוּמְרָא, וְלָא תֵּימָא רַבִּי אַמֵּי מִשּׁוּם דְּכֹהֵן הוּא, דְּהָא רַבִּי מְיָישָׁא בַּר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הוּא לֵיוַאי, וְאָמַר: עַד כָּאן בֵּין לְחוּלִּין בֵּין לִתְרוּמָה לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Hedya said: I was standing before Rabbi Ami, and he said:** One washes **until here both for non-sacred** food **and for *teruma*,** indicating **the more stringent** location, the third joint. **And do not say** that **Rabbi Ami** acted this way only **because he is a priest** and acted stringently to avoid confusing *teruma* and non-sacred food. This cannot be, **as Rabbi Meyasha, son of the son of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, is a Levite, and** yet **he** too **said: Until here both for non-sacred** food **and for *teruma*,** indicating **the more stringent** location.

Bar Hadaya reports that he observed Rav Ami rule strictly — the same joint for both *chulin* and *terumah*. The *gemara* cautions against attributing this stringency solely to the fact that Rav Ami was a *kohen* who might have felt personally bound to the higher standard. The proof: Rav Meisha bar bei d'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was a *Levi*, not a *kohen*, and he too ruled strictly.

The practical *halacha* that emerges: *Rashi*'s reading, combined with the dominant stringent opinions, would require washing up to the joint where the fingers meet the palm. *Tosafot*, however, reads the *gemara* as requiring washing until the wrist. The *Shulchan Aruch* brings both opinions and recommends doing the full wrist. One may rely on the more lenient position if necessary, but *lechatchila* the accepted practice is to wash the entire hand up to and including the wrist.

## All Day Coverage

### Piksa Valley

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: נוֹטֵל אָדָם אֶת שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו שַׁחֲרִית, וּמַתְנֶה עֲלֵיהֶן כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי אֲבִינָא לִבְנֵי פַּקְתָּא דַּעֲרָבוֹת: כְּגוֹן אַתּוּן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי לְכוּ מַיָּא, מְשׁוּ יְדַיְיכוּ מִצַּפְרָא וְאַתְנוֹ עֲלַיְיהוּ לְכוּלֵּאּ יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: A person may wash both** of **his hands in the morning and stipulate with regard to them** that he may eat on the basis of that washing throughout **the entire day,** provided he guards his hands from dirt and ritual impurity. It is likewise related that **Rabbi Avina said to the inhabitants of the valley of Aravot [*pakta da'aravot*],** where there was a shortage of water: People **such as you, for whom water is scarce,** should **wash your hands in the morning and stipulate with regard to them for the entire day.**

Rav says: *notel adam shtei yadav shachrit u'matneh aleihem kol hayom kulo* — a person may wash their hands at *shachrit*, in the morning, and rely on that washing for the entire day, provided they maintain attention to their hands and do not allow them to become dirty or *tamei* in the interim. In principle, a single morning washing can cover all subsequent meals throughout the day.

Rav Avina applied this rule concretely: he instructed the people of the valley of Piksa that since they had limited access to water — no plumbing, no nearby wells — *m'shu yadaychu mi'tzafra v'atnu aleihu l'chulei yoma* — wash your hands in the morning and rely on that washing for the whole day.

### Distress Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק – אִין, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק – לָא, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק נָמֵי, וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** A disagreement **was stated** over the scope of Rabbi Avina's ruling. **Some say** that Rabbi Avina maintains that **in exigent circumstances, yes,** one should act in this manner, but when one is **not in exigent circumstances,** he should **not** do so. **And** according to this explanation, Rabbi Avina **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rav,** who permitted this practice to all. **And some say** that Rabbi Avina ruled that one may do so **even** when **not in exigent circumstances, and** Rabbi Avina's opinion **is** identical to that **of Rav.**

A dispute arises over the scope of Rav Avina's ruling. Some say he only permitted this because of the particular circumstances — *b'sha'at hadechak*, a time of pressing need — but without such need the all-day extension would not be permitted. This reading would place Rav Avina's ruling in tension with Rav's more general statement.

### Morning Washing

Others say Rav Avina's ruling was not conditioned on *sha'at hadechak* at all — it is permitted under any circumstances — and this is consistent with Rav. The practical *halacha* is that one may rely on morning washing for the entire day, provided one maintains care with one's hands. The key mechanism is *hesech hada'at* — once one ceases to pay attention to one's hands, the principle of *yadayim askaniyot hen* reasserts itself: hands are "busy" and always potentially touching something, and as soon as they fall out of one's attentive awareness, a new *netilah* is required. Conversely, if one maintains mindful attention throughout the day, the morning washing remains in effect.

## Koach Gavra

### Irrigation Channel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי אֲרִיתָּא דְּדַלָּאֵי, אֵין נוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנּוּ לַיָּדַיִם, דְּלָא אָתוּ מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא. וְאִי מְקָרַב לְגַבֵּי דַּוְלָא, דְּקָאָתוּ מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא – נוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנּוּ לַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** With regard to **this irrigation channel [*arita dedalla'ei*],** into which water is poured from a river using buckets, and which then transports the water to the fields, **one may not wash** his **hands in** it. The reason is that this water **does not come from a person's force,** i.e., it is not poured on the hands by a direct act, as it moves by force of the current in the channel. **But if one draws his hands near the bucket** itself, in such a manner **that** the water poured on his hands **comes from a person's force** before it begins to flow in the channel, then **one may wash** his **hands with it.**

Rav Pappa introduces one of the defining structural requirements for *netilat yadayim*: *koach gavra* — human agency. The case is *arisa d'dilu'ei* — an irrigation channel. Water is drawn from a river by a person operating a bucket, poured into a channel, and flows down that channel for some distance. If one places one's hands into that flowing water at a point far enough downstream, *d'lo atu mikoach gavra* — the water is no longer reaching one's hands through direct human force — it does not qualify for *netilat yadayim*. The water is moving of its own momentum, not by virtue of the human act of pouring.

### Bucket Proximity

However, *v'i mikrav legabei dula* — if one places one's hands right at the point where the bucket empties — *d'ka'atu mikoach gavra* — where the water is directly flowing from the act of human pouring — *notlin mimenu l'yadayim*: this does qualify for *netilat yadayim*. At that proximate point, the human force is still operative and the requirement of *koach gavra* is satisfied.

Two distinct requirements govern *netilat yadayim*: *koach gavra* — that the water arrives at the hands through direct human agency — and the use of a *kli* — a vessel. These are separate and both must be met. If one opens a faucet and places one's hands directly underneath the initial flow, there is a basis to argue that the opening of the faucet constitutes *koach gavra*; the energy of the water pressure released by the initial act of turning the tap might be attributed to the human agent. However, leaving the faucet running continuously removes this connection — the ongoing flow is no longer attributable to the original human act. The question of the *kli* requirement in that context remains a separate discussion.

### Bucket Hole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בְּזִיעַ דַּוְלָא, בְּכוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה, מֵילָף לָיְיפִי, וּמְטַבֵּיל בָּהּ אֶת הַיָּדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the bucket** in which the water is drawn from the river **is perforated** with a hole large enough **to enable liquid to enter** the vessel when it is placed in the river, the presence of this hole **connects** the water in the channel to the water in the river, as they touch through that hole. **And** therefore, **one may immerse** his **hands in** that channel as he would in the river itself.

A final case: *u'vazya dula b'choness mashkeh* — if the bucket has a hole large enough for water to enter (*kones mashkeh* is the halachic measure for an aperture through which liquid can pass into a vessel). In this scenario, the bucket is drawn from the river and poured into the channel immediately adjacent, but there is a hole in the bucket through which water streams back into the river, creating a continuous connection between the river and the contents of the bucket and channel. *Milef layef u'matev beita yadeya* — through that connection, the water in the channel retains its link to the river, and one may perform *tevilat yadayim* by dipping one's hands in it, because it is treated as still connected to the natural body of water — functionally equivalent to dipping one's hands in the river itself.

The overarching principle anchoring this entire discussion is thus: for water to function in *netilat yadayim* — as opposed to *tevilat yadayim* in a connected natural body — it must arrive at the hands through direct human force. Without *koach gavra*, the *netilah* is invalid.
