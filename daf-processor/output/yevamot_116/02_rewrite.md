# Yevamot 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witness Testimony Review

### Impressions vs Facts

Yesterday's discussion centered on a fundamental problem in the law of witness testimony regarding a husband's death: when a woman reports facts that would permit her to remarry, we must ask whether she is conveying concrete knowledge or merely drawing conclusions from impressions. The Gemara uses the phrase *amar bedami* to capture this concern — she may be stating as fact something she perceived or inferred, perhaps without even being fully aware that she has done so. At the same time, the Gemara acknowledges that in many cases we simply will not have all the concrete facts available to us, and that we may nonetheless be entitled to draw certain conclusions from what we do know.

### Death Cases Overview

That tension — between requiring concrete facts and permitting reasoned inference — was the backdrop for the Gemara's discussion of cases such as a drowned man pulled from the water, a charred body, and reports of death delivered by messenger. The Gemara now turns to a case that sits precisely at the intersection of these two concerns: a message was sent that Yitzchak Reish Galuta, the son of the sister of Rav Bibi, had died. The question is whether we can act on that report — and underlying it is a question about whether knowing a person's name, and knowing someone who fits that name, is sufficient context for us to conclude that the report refers to the person we know.

## Yitzchak Reish Galuta

### Identity from Name

The case itself raises an immediate puzzle. The term *reish galuta* ordinarily refers to the Exilarch, a singular political office. How, then, could there be any question about which person named Yitzchak Reish Galuta died? It seems the term may have been used more broadly to refer to regional officials of some standing, or alternatively, the title here is secondary and the identifying detail is really "the son of the sister of Rav Bibi" — yet even that description raises the question of how many individuals might fit it. The Gemara's point is precisely this: we have a name and a identifying detail, and we know someone who matches. Is that enough to allow us to act?

### Abaye vs Rava

The Gemara introduces here the foundational debate between Abaye and Rava about what level of verification is required when we have a name — and possibly identifying details — that matches a known individual. Abaye holds that we must be concerned that another person might share the same name, and we are therefore obligated to investigate. Rava holds that if we are going to take that concern seriously, we would need to search the entire world, since a person who once lived in a given city might now reside elsewhere and still match the description. That standard would be impossible to meet, and so Rava concludes there is in practice no such requirement. As a gentle gesture toward Rav Bibi's father, who had asked the question, Rava suggests that when Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah told him to investigate all of Nehardea, he did not mean it as a formal legal requirement but as a courtesy response to an honorable inquiry.

## Found Get Case

### Neharda'a Get

Abaye cites his own proof-text for his position. He says: where do I derive that we are concerned about another person with the same name? From a *get* found in Nehardea. The *get* identified the husband as Andulinay of Nehardea and named his wife. The father of Shmuel sent this *get* to Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah, the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, asking whether we must investigate to confirm there is no one else matching that description, or whether the person we already know who fits it is sufficient. Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah ruled: *tivdu Neharda'a kula* — you must check all of Nehardea. Despite knowing someone who matched the name, city, and wife's name, that is not enough; you must confirm that no one else fits the same profile.

### Abaye: Check & Eliminate

Abaye's position is thus that while verification is required, it is not an infinite regress. If one checks and finds no other candidate, the investigation has done its job. The standard is not to canvass the whole world, but to make a reasonable and thorough inquiry within the relevant community. If after that inquiry there is no competing candidate, we may proceed.

## Rava: Common Names

### Mechuza Writs

Rava brings his own proof from practice. He reports that in Mechuza, his home city, two writs of debt circulated bearing the names *Chavi bar Nanai* and *Nanai bar Chavi* — clearly very common names in that locale. It is important to clarify a point about these cases: the common names belong to the *creditors*, not to both parties. The debtor's identity was presumably unambiguous. The question was whether the individuals presenting the writs and bearing those common names could be assumed to be the genuine creditors, given that many others shared those names. Rabba bar Avua — presumably a court authority in Mechuza — allowed collection on these documents. He was not deterred by the fact that many others in the city shared the creditors' names.

## Found Get Case (II)

### Rashi: Money vs Issur

Rashi draws an explicit analogy from this *dinei mamonot* case to the domain of *issur*. His reasoning runs as follows: here we have a monetary case where we concretely know that other people share the creditor's name — *shechichi tuva*, they are very common — and yet we do not require further verification beyond the fact that the person is presenting the document. If we do not demand exhaustive verification even under those conditions in money matters, then certainly in a case of *issur* — permitting a woman to remarry — where we do not actually know of anyone else with the same name, we should not be required to investigate the entire city. The analogy is admittedly imperfect, since one could distinguish in multiple directions, but the underlying principle is that not every circumstance of a shared name triggers an obligation to search and verify.

## Rava: Common Names (II)

### Document as Testimony

Abaye, however, does not simply concede. He distinguishes the *Mechuza* case by pointing to the supporting evidence inherent in the act of *presentation*. In a case where a person presents a document bearing their name, that act of presentation is itself meaningful. The Gemara considers and eliminates several alternative explanations for how the writ might have come into the wrong hands. If we worry that the original creditor dropped it, the Gemara responds that people are careful with their financial documents. If we worry that the real creditor deposited it with someone of the same name for safekeeping, the Gemara responds that no one would be foolish enough to entrust such a document to a person who shares their own name. If we worry that the sale of the debt to this person was not legally completed, the Gemara responds: *otiyot niknot bimesirah* — documents are acquired through physical transfer, so the moment it was handed over, the sale was complete.

### Possession vs Authority

This leads to a subtle but important conceptual point, one that Tosafot makes explicit. We have a general principle in *dinei mamonot* that *chazakah mah shetachas yad adam harei hu shelo* — what a person holds is presumed to be theirs. Why, then, do we need all of these elaborate arguments to justify Abaye's position? Tosafot answers: the presumption of physical ownership covers the piece of paper itself. But a document's legal force — its capacity to function as testimony, to speak as a writ — depends on something more. For a *shtar chov* or a *get* to operate as a legal instrument, it must not merely be owned but must be *presented by the right person*, by the one to whom it testifies. The document, in this sense, does not merely sit passively in one's possession; it actively testifies on behalf of the person presenting it. That is precisely why Abaye argues that a *get* found on the street raises questions that a *get* being actively presented does not. When someone produces a writ and their name matches it, the document is engaged in an act of testimony on their behalf, and the concern about mistaken identity is substantially diminished.

## Anan bar Chiya Get

### Get from Sura

The Gemara now presents a concrete case that brings the Abaye-Rava debate into sharp relief. A *get* was found in Sura. It read: "In the city of Sura, I, Anan bar Chiya of Nehardea, have divorced my wife" — and named her. The man identified himself as Anan bar Chiya, from Nehardea, and the *get* was physically written in Sura.

### Alternative Candidate

The Rabbis conducted an investigation from Sura to Nehardea and found only one other individual named Anan bar Chiya — a man originally from a place called Chagra, but who had been residing in Nehardea. His wife's name presumably also matched. So there did exist a second person who fit the description in the *get*: his name matched, his city of residence matched (Nehardea), and his wife's name matched. However, witnesses testified that on the very day the *get* was written in Sura, this second Anan bar Chiya — the one from Chagra — had been with them in Nehardea. He was demonstrably not in Sura on the day the *get* was executed.

### Abaye: Elimination

Abaye responds: even according to my view, which requires investigation, this case presents no problem. I said you must check — and you checked. You found one other candidate, and you were able to eliminate him. Witnesses confirm he was in Nehardea on the day in question. *Ma ba'ei b'Sura?* — what would he be doing in Sura on that day? The investigation was done properly, a potential competing candidate was identified and eliminated, and therefore we may proceed.

### Rava: Concrete Doubt

Rava, characteristically, takes the opposite position. Even according to his general view that we are not concerned about other people with the same name, here — where a concrete second candidate has actually been identified — the concern becomes real and cannot simply be waved away. The very fact of investigation has introduced a concrete doubt where none existed before. And as for the evidence that the second candidate was in Nehardea that day: *dilma begamla parcha azal* — perhaps he traveled by some extraordinarily fast means and arrived in Sura in time. The point seems almost deliberately absurd, and indeed Rashi and Tosafot note that this likely refers to some form of miraculous transport or *sheid*. The argument appears to be that once a concrete alternative exists, even logically implausible explanations cannot fully dispel the doubt.

### Mili Masar

The Gemara then offers a third and considerably more reasonable possibility for Rava's concern: *mili masar* — it is not necessary for the husband to be physically present in the place where the *get* is written. The *get* names the city in which it is being executed, not the city where the husband issued his instructions. Rav Safra and Rav Huna both taught this explicitly to scribes: if you are sitting in Shili writing a *get* but the husband gave you instructions while in Hini, you write Shili as the place of the *get*. And conversely, if you are in Hini when the husband spoke to you in Shili, you write Hini. The language of the *get* is in the first person — "I, so-and-so, divorce my wife" — but the place recorded is the scribe's location, not the husband's. Therefore, the fact that the *get* says it was written in Sura does not tell us anything about where Anan bar Chiya himself actually was. The second candidate need not have traveled to Sura at all — his instructions could have been transmitted to a scribe who happened to be writing in Sura. This makes Rava's concern not only plausible but quite rational.

## Sesame Seeds Recap

### Apply Standards

The Gemara then returns to a case that had been set aside — the case involving sesame seeds. Rav Yeimar rules *lo chaishinan* — we are not concerned. Rav Ashi rules *chaishinan* — we are concerned. And the *halachah* follows Rav Ashi: we are concerned. This conclusion is reached in what is effectively a *mammonot* context, one with perhaps fewer identifying marks than some of the other cases. The broader point is that all of these cases — the *get*, the death report, the drowned body, the writ of debt — are variations on a common set of questions about what level of identification is required, what kinds of evidence are sufficient, and at what point we may conclude that a report or document refers to a specific known individual. The cases are not necessarily governed by a single rule, and the standards likely vary depending on numerous factors: the number and specificity of identifying marks, whether there is supporting evidence, whether we concretely know of another candidate, and whether the domain is *mamonot* or *issur*.

### Sesame Seeds

One correction to what was said previously: the case at issue involves sesame seeds, not sunflower seeds. Sesame seeds are in any case likely the more valuable commodity.

## Ketata Quarreling

### Ketata Definition

The Mishnah had mentioned two scenarios in which a woman's report of her husband's death is not believed: when there is a state of war (*milchama*) in the world, and when there is *ketata* — fighting — between husband and wife. The war scenario raises concerns about the fog of war and misperception; the *ketata* scenario raises a different set of concerns. The Gemara now asks: *heichi dami ketata*? What constitutes *ketata* in practice? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says: she tells her husband "divorce me" — *garsheni*. The Gemara objects: *kulan nami amru hachi* — all women say this in the heat of argument. Rashi explains that this is a common expression of frustration. The Gemara therefore clarifies: the operative case is not that she says "divorce me" but that she goes about telling people "*gerashtani*" — "my husband has already divorced me." She is publicly claiming to be divorced even though she has no *get* and even though we know it is not true.

At that point, the Gemara raises a further question: if she is publicly claiming to be divorced, why not simply believe her about the divorce itself, based on the ruling of Rav Hamnuna? Rav Hamnuna held that a woman who tells her husband to his face that he has already divorced her is believed, on the principle that *ein isha me'iza paneiha bifnei baalah* — a woman would not be so brazen as to say this to her husband's face unless it were true. The Gemara resolves this by establishing a more specific case: she said she was divorced in front of named witnesses — Reuven and Shimon — and when those witnesses were asked, they said it never happened. She is thus a confirmed fabricator of the divorce claim, yet she does not have a *get*. This very fact — that she is publicly asserting a fictional divorce — demonstrates that she has emotionally and psychologically exited the marriage, and it is this posture that disqualifies her subsequent report of his death from being credited.

### Lying vs Misinterpretati…

The Gemara presents two distinct rationales for why *ketata* undermines credibility. Rav Chanina says: *mishum de'meshakra* — because she will intentionally lie. Rav Shimi bar Ashi says: *mishum de'amra bedidami* — not because she is deliberately deceptive, but because her emotional state colors her perception. She would like her husband to be dead, and so she interprets ambiguous or incomplete evidence in that direction, unconsciously constructing a narrative of his death from vague impressions. Tosafot raises the obvious question: if the *ketata* case involves a woman who has already been proven to lie — she claimed a divorce that witnesses denied — why does the Gemara need a separate explanation about unconscious misinterpretation? She is already a confirmed liar.

### Blame Shifts Concern

The distinction between the two rationales becomes practically significant at the top of 27b, where the Gemara introduces the case in which *he* is to blame for the fighting — *adal hu ketata*. If it is clear that the husband's behavior is driving the conflict, that she is not seeking to leave the marriage but is being mistreated within it, then the concern about intentional lying is substantially reduced. She demonstrably wants to remain married and has no motive to fabricate his death. However, the concern about unconscious misinterpretation — *bedidami* — remains. Even if she would not deliberately lie, the ongoing difficulty and pain of the marriage might still unconsciously influence how she reads ambiguous information about his fate.

Tosafot addresses the apparent contradiction: if we know she has already lied — she made a false divorce claim — why don't we conclude she is a confirmed liar and disbelieve her regardless of who is to blame? Tosafot answers: lying about a divorce is one thing, but falsely claiming her husband is dead is another matter entirely. An earlier *perek* catalogued the severe consequences that fall upon a woman who claims her husband is dead, remarries, and is then confronted with his return. A woman would not be willing to expose herself to those consequences — losing both the first and second husband, and bearing children of uncertain status — merely to escape a marriage in which the husband is at fault. She would not go that far in her deception when she knows he bears the blame. So the concern narrows to unconscious misinterpretation, not intentional lying, in the case where he is responsible.

## Ed Echad Ketata

### Third Party Credibility

The Gemara now asks the parallel question it had asked regarding *milchama*: what if the report of the husband's death comes not from the wife herself but from an objective third-party witness — an *ed echad*? In the *milchama* context, the value of an outside witness was not obvious, since the fog of war affects everyone's perceptions. Here, by contrast, the concern about *ketata* is specifically tied to the wife's emotional state and her relationship with her husband. A third party has none of that emotional involvement. Should an *ed echad* be believed straightforwardly when there is a known *ketata*?

### Two Theories

The answer depends on which underlying theory explains why an *ed echad* is believed in the first place when testifying to a husband's death. The first theory: *milta de'avida le-igluya lo meshaker* — a person will not lie about something that will eventually come to light. If the husband is alive, he will show up, and the false testimony will be exposed. Under this theory, an outside witness in a *ketata* situation should indeed be believed; his concern about being found out is entirely independent of the state of the marriage. The second theory: *hi dayka u'minsava* — the reason we trust an *ed echad* is that we rely on the wife's own careful investigation before she acts on the testimony. She knows the consequences of remarrying incorrectly, and so she will rigorously verify the report before proceeding. The *ed echad*'s testimony is thus only valid in combination with the wife's backup diligence.

### Teiku Unresolved

Under the second theory, the case of *ketata* presents a real problem. *Ve-hacha keivan de-is lah ketata lo dayka* — since the marriage is in turmoil, she is not going to exercise careful, sober investigation. She is motivated, consciously or not, to accept the report and act on it without the diligent scrutiny that ordinarily backs up the *ed echad*'s testimony. The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Modern Applications

### War & Tragedy

The practical stakes of this unresolved question are significant. The concepts of *ketata* and *milchama* have very broad potential application. Many of the most devastating human catastrophes — wars, terrorist attacks, building collapses, plane disasters — fall into the category of *milchama*-type scenarios. And marriages, particularly under conditions of stress and tragedy, are not always in a state of tranquility. The *teiku* on *ed echad* in a *ketata* situation means that in precisely those circumstances where testimony is most urgently needed, its admissibility may be in doubt.

### Agunah Solutions

This directly affects the scope of the rabbinic solutions to the *agunah* problem. The leniencies that the Sages developed over centuries to permit women to remarry when their husbands' deaths cannot be formally established depend heavily on the credibility of *ed echad* testimony and on the woman's own report. If *ketata* limits even third-party testimony, and if *milchama* scenarios are broadly defined, then the practical availability of these solutions is substantially curtailed. There is an extensive literature of responsa dealing with precisely these questions — following the Holocaust, after 9/11, and in other mass-casualty events — and the *poskim* have had to navigate all of these considerations with enormous care.

## Rabbi Yehuda Stringent

### Mourning Signs

Against this backdrop, the Mishnah records the view of Rabbi Yehuda, who applies a more stringent standard across the board: *le'olam ein ne'emenet* — she is never believed without additional corroboration, regardless of whether it is wartime or peacetime and regardless of the state of the marriage. Specifically, Rabbi Yehuda requires that the woman exhibit visible signs of mourning — torn garments, weeping, and other outward expressions of grief — before her testimony about her husband's death will be credited.

### Smart vs Foolish

The Sages respond with a straightforward objection: *de'varecha pike'achas tinasei, shotah lo tinasei* — according to your position, a clever woman who knows how to perform grief convincingly will be permitted to remarry, while a simple woman who does not know to stage a display will not. That is an arbitrary and unjust standard. The Sages therefore conclude: *achas zu ve'achas zu tinasei* — whether she is shrewd or not, if the facts support her claim, she may remarry.

### Woman Coached Act

The Gemara then records an illustrative incident. A woman came before Rabbi Yehuda's court — which required these outward signs of mourning — and the people around her coached her in advance. They told her: before you walk in, eulogize your husband, rend your garments, loosen your hair, and then enter and tell Rabbi Yehuda what happened.

### Coaching Falsification

The Gemara asks: did they thereby teach her to falsify evidence — *afu'ei shikra*? The Gemara answers no. Those who coached her held like the Sages, not like Rabbi Yehuda: in their view, she was fully believable even without the dramatic display. Rabbi Yehuda's requirement was, in their opinion, an artificial condition not demanded by law. When they coached her to perform the display, they were not teaching her to lie but rather helping her satisfy what they considered to be an illegitimate technical hurdle in order to reach what they believed was the correct halachic outcome. The discomfort remains — there is something troubling about the entire episode, a kind of *limdu leshonam davar sheker* — but the Gemara accepts the explanation as technically exculpatory.

## Beis Hillel Principle

### Major Innovation

We now reach the foundational principle that underlies this entire *perek*: the rule that a woman is believed to report her husband's death and may on that basis remarry. This is a striking *chiddush* on multiple levels. Ordinarily, a woman's testimony is not accepted in the same way as a man's, and certainly one would not expect a *nogea badavar* — a party with a personal interest in the outcome — to be credited. Yet here the Sages introduced exactly that rule: the wife's own testimony about her husband's death suffices to permit remarriage. This principle had already been assumed in earlier *perakim* of the tractate, which discussed the consequences when a woman remarried on the basis of such testimony and the husband later appeared. But the principle itself is introduced and established here.

### Beis Hillel Limited

Interestingly, it is Beis Hillel who initially takes the more restrictive position. They rule: *lo shama'nu ela be-va min ha-katzir u've-otah medinah ke-ma'aseh she-hayah* — we have only heard this leniency as applying to the specific type of case that served as the original precedent. That precedent involved a woman returning from the grain harvest, reporting that her husband died, and the death occurring in the same locale — not in a distant country. Beis Hillel holds that the leniency cannot be extended beyond those parameters: the same type of agricultural work, the same local setting.

### Beis Shammai Object

Beis Shammai responds that this level of specificity is untenable. They ask: does it matter whether she came from the wheat harvest or the olive harvest or the grape harvest? Is the type of agricultural work actually relevant? And does it matter whether the death occurred locally or in a distant country? The principle behind the leniency, they argue, has nothing to do with those particulars; they appeared in the original case by historical accident. *Lo dibru chachamim ba-katzir ela ba-hoveh* — the Sages spoke of the harvest because that was the circumstance that happened, not because the harvest is legally significant.

## Generalize Case

### Reductio Ad Absurdum

Beis Shammai's argument is a *reductio ad absurdum*. If Beis Hillel insists on replicating the original case's details, then the leniency should apply only to wheat — *katzir chittim* — and not even to barley harvest. And should it extend only to grain and not to olive-picking, date-gathering, grape-harvesting, or any other form of agricultural work? Since Beis Hillel would surely agree that the type of crop is irrelevant, they must concede that at least *some* abstraction beyond the literal original case is required. And once that concession is made, Beis Shammai asks, why stop at the type of harvest? Why not also generalize with respect to geography?

### Local vs Distant

Beis Hillel, however, does not concede equally on both points. They hold that while the type of agricultural work is indeed irrelevant — that much they grant — the distinction between a local death and a distant one is substantive. When the death occurs locally — *be-otah medinah de-shchichi inshi* — people are present, people who know the husband and wife, and if the husband is alive, the lie will quickly be exposed. That social visibility is a real deterrent to false testimony. When the death is reported from a distant country — *de-la shchichi inshi* — no one there knows them, and the woman could plausibly lie without discovery. *Mirtata* — she is afraid to lie when she is locally known; *la mirtata* — she is not similarly afraid when the event occurred far away where no one recognizes them.

### Caravans Counter

Beis Shammai counters: *hachi nami shchichi shayarta* — there are caravans, there is regular travel and commerce between cities and countries, and the facts will surface regardless of distance. Word travels. The threat of exposure is not meaningfully diminished merely because the death occurred in a distant place.

### Hillel Concedes

Ultimately, Beis Hillel concedes and rules like Beis Shammai, accepting the broader generalization. This concession is significant — it is one of the relatively rare instances where Beis Hillel acknowledges that Beis Shammai's reasoning is compelling and reverses course.

## Original Case

### Snake Bite Case

The Gemara then identifies the original case that gave rise to this entire body of law. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel reports: it was the end of the wheat harvest season, and ten men went out to harvest wheat. A snake bit one of them, and he died.

### Investigation Confirmed

His wife came and reported the death to the *beis din*. The court did not simply accept her word; *veshalchu umatzu kidvareha* — they investigated and found her account to be accurate. There was independent corroboration. What is notable, given that ten people were present at the scene, is that it was specifically the wife who came to report to the *beis din*. And it is equally notable that despite the availability of corroborating witnesses, the case led the Sages to establish a principle based on the wife's testimony.

### Remarriage Principle

One possible reading of the Gemara's thinking: the Sages found themselves asking what would have happened had the corroboration not been available. What if there had been no surviving witnesses, or they were inaccessible? Would the woman have been left without remedy? That hypothetical seems to have prompted the more general principle. Having confirmed through investigation that this woman told the truth, the Sages extrapolated to establish the rule: *ha-isha she-amrah meis ba'ali tinasei* — a woman who says her husband has died may remarry. And similarly, she may enter into *yibum*. The principle was crystallized from a concrete, verified case and then extended as a general rule.

## Parah Adumah

### Prohibition Rules

The Gemara introduces a parallel case from a seemingly unrelated area of law to analyze the question of when we generalize from a concrete case and when we confine a ruling to its original circumstances. The *Braisa* teaches a series of prohibitions relating to the transport of the ashes and water of the *parah adumah*: one may not carry *mei chatas* and *efer chatas* — the purification water and ashes — and take them across the Jordan River, whether by boat, by throwing them from one bank to the other, by floating them on a raft, or by carrying them on the back of an animal or a person who is not standing with feet on the ground. However, it is permitted to carry them over a bridge, since the bridge is connected to the earth. And this applies to the Jordan and to all other rivers alike.

### Narrow View

Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva disagrees. He rules: *lo amru ela Yarden u'vesfinah* — the prohibition was stated only regarding the Jordan River, and only in a boat. Not other rivers, and not other means of crossing. His principle is *ke-ma'aseh she-hayah* — like the case in which it occurred. The ruling is limited to the precise parameters of the original incident.

### Broad View

The *Rabbanan* take the broader view: once the prohibition is established for the Jordan and for boats, it extends to all rivers and all methods of crossing that share the relevant characteristics.

## Generalize vs Limit

### Logic Purpose

The Gemara now analyzes whether the debate of Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva and the *Rabbanan* maps onto the debate of Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai, and what principles govern when to generalize and when to limit. The *Rabbanan* could say: even we, who generalize here, would not necessarily generalize in all cases. In the case of the woman's testimony about her husband's death, Beis Hillel initially did limit the ruling. But there was a clear reason Beis Hillel ultimately conceded and expanded: the rationale for believing the woman is *mirtata* — she fears exposure, she fears the consequences — and that rationale applies equally in all circumstances. There is an identifiable logic underlying the leniency, and because that logic applies broadly, the rule can be extended broadly.

### No Logic Limits

Here, with the *parah adumah* transfer prohibition, there is no such clear logic. Why should carrying *mei chatas* over a river on a boat be prohibited? There is no obvious reason that such transport is inherently problematic for the sanctity or purity of the ashes. The prohibition exists because something went wrong once — it is a *gezerah*, a rabbinic response to an event. And since there is no clear underlying logic, there is no principled basis for extending it beyond the specific circumstances of that event. Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva's view that we confine it to *ke-ma'aseh she-hayah* reflects exactly this principle: without a rationale to drive abstraction, we stay with the concrete.

### Corpse Incident

The original incident was this: someone was transporting *mei chatas* and *efer chatas* across the Jordan in a boat, and *nimtza kezayis min ha-mes tachuv b'karkisa shel sefinah* — a piece of a human corpse, the size of an olive, was found lodged in the hull of the boat. That corpse piece rendered the contents of the boat *tamei*, contaminating the ashes. The Sages responded immediately — *b'otah sha'ah amru* — and prohibited this practice. But the prohibition was a reaction to a specific accident, not derived from a general principle.

### Woman vs Parah

Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva, conversely, could explain why he does not apply Beis Shammai's logic in the *parah adumah* case even while Beis Shammai generalizes in the woman's case. The reason Beis Shammai generalizes there is *dayka u'minsava* — there is always a structural logic at work: the woman will investigate carefully before acting, and the prospect of exposure will deter false testimony across all circumstances. That logic provides a principled basis for abstraction. In the *parah adumah* case, there is simply no equivalent logic. *Mishum ma'aseh she-hayah b'Yarden u'vesfinah d'hava ma'aseh gazru rabbanan* — the Rabbanan issued their prohibition because of that specific event. *B'she'ar naharos d'lo hava ma'aseh lo gazru rabbanan* — for other rivers, where no such event occurred, there is no basis for the *gezerah* to apply. The rule should therefore remain as narrow as its origin. In the case of the woman's testimony, there is a reason we want to extend the leniency broadly: we want her to be able to remarry, there is a rationale supporting the rule's application in all circumstances, and therefore we generalize. In the case of *parah adumah*, we have no such driving rationale and no logic compelling extension, and so we limit.
